# Community & Ecosystem

## "My Dream Is to Create 10 Black Millionaires Right Here in Seattle"

Sir Mix-A-Lot told the Seattle Times: "My dream is to create
10 black millionaires right here in Seattle." The quote is
not casual. It is a mission statement -- specific in its
number (10), specific in its geography (Seattle), specific
in its ambition (millionaires, not just successful artists),
and specific in its community (Black).

In a city where the Black population was less than 10% and
shrinking, where the music industry had no infrastructure,
where the nearest major hip-hop market was 800 miles south,
this was not idle dreaming. It was an engineering problem.
And Mix-A-Lot approached it the same way he approached music
production: by trying to build every layer of the stack
himself.

The dream did not fully materialize. The infrastructure he
built -- NastyMix Records, Rhyme Cartel Records, the
compilation projects, the roster of local artists -- did not
produce 10 Black millionaires in Seattle. It did not produce
one, beyond Mix himself. The reasons for this failure are
structural, not personal, and they illuminate the difference
between an artist's ambition and an ecosystem's capacity.

## NastyMix Records: Seattle's First Hip-Hop Ecosystem

### The Roster

NastyMix Records was more than Sir Mix-A-Lot's label. It was
an attempt to build Seattle's hip-hop ecosystem -- a
constellation of artists, producers, and releases that could
sustain itself beyond any single act. The roster included:

**Kid Sensation** -- Born Roldan Anderson, Kid Sensation was
the most prominent artist on the NastyMix roster after
Mix-A-Lot himself. He appeared on "Posse on Broadway" and
released solo material on the label. His track "Back 2 Boom"
appeared on the Juice soundtrack (1992). Kid Sensation
represented the possibility that NastyMix could develop
multiple commercially viable artists, not just serve as a
vehicle for Mix's solo career.

**E-Dawg** -- A Seattle rapper who recorded for NastyMix and
appeared on various label compilations. E-Dawg's career
illustrates the challenge of the regional hip-hop artist in
the pre-internet era: talent existed, but the infrastructure
to convert talent into sustained commercial success was
fragile and dependent on a single label's fortunes.

**Funk Daddy** -- Another NastyMix artist who contributed to
the label's attempt to build a roster. Funk Daddy's presence
on the label demonstrated that Seattle had multiple hip-hop
voices, not just Mix-A-Lot, but the national market's
attention was focused on Mix as the region's representative,
leaving little oxygen for supporting acts.

**Criminal Nation** -- A group whose "Trouble in the Hood"
(1992) was NastyMix Records' final release. The timing is
significant: Criminal Nation's album came out as Mix-A-Lot
was departing for Rhyme Cartel and Def American, and NastyMix
could not sustain operations without its flagship artist's
commercial draw. Criminal Nation's album was a casualty of
the label's collapse -- a release that entered the market
without the promotional support or distribution network that
Mix's presence had underwritten.

**Jazz Lee Aston** -- A vocalist who appeared on Mix-A-Lot's
albums and on the "Seattle... The Dark Side" compilation.
Aston represented the label's attempt to include R&B and
vocal-driven hip-hop in its offerings, broadening the sonic
palette beyond pure rap.

### The Label as Ecosystem

NastyMix Records functioned as a miniature ecosystem -- a
self-contained entity that handled artist development,
recording, manufacturing, and local distribution. The
parallels to other artist-founded labels are instructive:

| Label | City | Founder | Key Artists | Outcome |
|-------|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Motown | Detroit | Berry Gordy | Supremes, Temptations, Stevie Wonder | Multi-decade empire |
| Sugar Hill | New Jersey | Sylvia Robinson | Sugarhill Gang, Grandmaster Flash | Financial collapse |
| Def Jam | New York | Simmons, Rubin | LL Cool J, Beastie Boys, Public Enemy | Major-label status |
| NastyMix | Seattle | Mix, Nes, Locke | Sir Mix-A-Lot, Kid Sensation | Collapsed after Mix left |
| Death Row | LA | Suge Knight | Dr. Dre, Snoop Dogg, Tupac | Legal/criminal collapse |
| Rhyme Cartel | Seattle | Sir Mix-A-Lot | Sir Mix-A-Lot | Distribution via Def American |

The comparison reveals NastyMix's structural vulnerability:
unlike Motown, Def Jam, or Death Row, NastyMix never
developed a second commercially viable artist. Mix-A-Lot's
platinum success was the label's entire commercial foundation.
When he left, there was nothing to sustain operations.

The label had artists, but it did not have a pipeline -- a
mechanism for developing raw talent into marketable acts that
could generate revenue independently of the founder.

This is not a criticism of NastyMix's ambition. Building a
hip-hop label pipeline in 1980s Seattle was orders of
magnitude harder than building one in New York, LA, or even
Detroit. The city had no hip-hop radio (KUBE's format change
did not happen until 1991), no hip-hop venues, no hip-hop
press, no A&R scouts visiting from major labels, no tour
circuit that included Seattle as a regular stop.

Everything had to be built from scratch, and everything
depended on one artist's commercial momentum to survive.

## "Seattle... The Dark Side" (1993)

### The Compilation

"Seattle... The Dark Side" was released in 1993 on Rhyme
Cartel/Def American. It was Sir Mix-A-Lot's most explicit
attempt at platform-sharing -- using his commercial success
and his distribution deal with Rick Rubin to create a
showcase for Seattle's hip-hop talent.

The timing was deliberate. In 1993, "Seattle" meant grunge
to the rest of the world. Nirvana's Nevermind (1991) and
Pearl Jam's Ten (1991) had made the city synonymous with
flannel, distortion, and white male angst. The Seattle music
story, as told by Rolling Stone, Spin, and MTV, was a rock
story.

"The Dark Side" was a corrective: here is the Seattle you are
not seeing. Here is the city's Black music scene, its hip-hop
tradition, its other voice.

The title itself -- "The Dark Side" -- carries multiple
meanings. It references the literal dark side of the Seattle
music scene (the hip-hop that existed in grunge's shadow).
It references the racial dimension (the Black musical
tradition in a city whose public image was overwhelmingly
white). And it references the physical landscape of the
Pacific Northwest -- the dark, rainy winters, the overcast
skies, the long nights that distinguish the region from the
sun-soaked imagery of LA hip-hop.

### The Featured Artists

The compilation featured Mix-A-Lot alongside local artists
including Kid Sensation, Jazz Lee Aston, and others from the
NastyMix/Rhyme Cartel roster. The tracks represented a
cross-section of Seattle's hip-hop styles -- not just Mix's
own bass-heavy party rap, but the range of approaches that
the city's artists were developing.

### Commercial Reception

"The Dark Side" did not achieve significant commercial
success. The compilation did not produce a breakout single.
The featured artists did not parlay their appearance into
sustained careers. The reasons were structural:

1. **Radio support was limited.** KUBE 93.3 provided local
   airplay, but national hip-hop radio was focused on the
   LA-driven G-funk explosion (Snoop Dogg's "Doggystyle" was
   the dominant album of late 1993) and the emerging East
   Coast response (Wu-Tang Clan's "Enter the Wu-Tang").

2. **Press coverage was nonexistent.** Music journalists
   covering Seattle in 1993 were writing about grunge. The
   Source, Vibe, and other hip-hop publications had no
   Seattle correspondents and no institutional interest in
   the city's hip-hop scene.

3. **Tour infrastructure was absent.** Getting a Seattle
   hip-hop act in front of audiences outside the Pacific
   Northwest required tour support that an independent label
   could not finance. The featured artists had no managers,
   no booking agents, no road crews.

4. **The compilation format has inherent limits.** Compilations
   introduce artists but do not develop them. A listener who
   hears Kid Sensation on "The Dark Side" has no follow-up
   product to purchase, no tour to attend, no media narrative
   to follow. The compilation is a showcase, not a career
   launch.

### The Lesson

"The Dark Side" taught a lesson that every artist-driven label
venture eventually learns: platform-sharing is necessary but
not sufficient. Mix-A-Lot could put local artists on a
nationally distributed album, but he could not build the
management infrastructure, the touring support, the press
relationships, and the sustained promotional campaigns that
would be required to turn exposure into careers.

One person cannot be the artist, the label head, the A&R
executive, the publicist, the tour promoter, and the career
counselor simultaneously. The stack is too deep.

## The Central District as Geographic Anchor

### The Neighborhood

Seattle's Central District was the physical anchor of the
city's Black community and, by extension, its hip-hop scene.
The CD's boundaries are roughly defined by Madison Street to
the north, Rainier Avenue to the south, 12th Avenue to the
west, and Martin Luther King Jr. Way to the east.

Within these boundaries, the neighborhood's institutions --
churches, barbershops, community centers, small businesses,
parks -- constituted the social infrastructure that supported
cultural production.

The CD's musical history predates hip-hop by decades. Jackson
Street, running through the southern edge of the neighborhood,
was Seattle's jazz corridor from the 1930s through the 1960s.
Clubs like the Black and Tan (the first officially desegregated
club in Seattle), the Rocking Chair, and the Royal Esquire
hosted Ray Charles, Quincy Jones, Ernestine Anderson, and
dozens of other artists who shaped American music.

Jimi Hendrix grew up at 26th and Yesler, twelve blocks from
the Bryant Manor apartments where Anthony Ray was raised.

This lineage matters. Mix-A-Lot's hip-hop did not emerge from
a vacuum. It emerged from a neighborhood with a seven-decade
tradition of Black musical expression -- a tradition that
produced world-class artists despite (or because of) the
city's geographic isolation and small Black population.

### Gentrification

The Central District's gentrification is one of the most
dramatic demographic shifts in American urban history. In
1970, the CD's population was approximately 73% Black. By
2010, it was approximately 20% Black. By 2020, the figure
had dropped further. The neighborhood that had anchored
Seattle's Black community for three-quarters of a century
was transformed within a single generation.

The gentrification was driven by multiple factors: rising
property values in an increasingly expensive city, the
absence of protective zoning or community land trusts, the
displacement of renters by homebuyers, the arrival of
tech-industry workers drawn to nearby employers (Amazon's
headquarters is just north of the CD), and the citywide
expansion of development pressure.

For the hip-hop ecosystem, the loss of the Central District
was catastrophic. The physical spaces where the culture had
been incubated -- the community centers, the informal
gathering spots, the apartments where artists lived and
worked -- were replaced by condominiums, craft breweries,
and coworking spaces. The people who had populated those
spaces were displaced to South King County, the suburbs,
and in some cases out of the metropolitan area entirely.

Mix-A-Lot's dream of creating 10 Black millionaires in
Seattle becomes even more poignant in this context. The
city's Black community was not just small -- it was
shrinking. The geographic anchor was being pulled up. The
infrastructure of proximity -- people living near each
other, running into each other at the barbershop and the
Boys & Girls Club and the corner store -- was being
dissolved by economics.

## The 206 Zulu Nonprofit

### Origin and Mission

206 Zulu is a Seattle-based nonprofit organization dedicated
to preserving and promoting the city's hip-hop culture. Named
after Seattle's area code (206), the organization connects to
the broader Zulu Nation tradition in hip-hop while grounding
itself in Pacific Northwest specifics.

206 Zulu's activities include:

- **Events:** Regular hip-hop events, showcases, and community
  gatherings that provide spaces for local artists to perform
  and audiences to engage.

- **Oral histories:** Documentation of Seattle hip-hop history
  through first-person accounts, interviews, and archival
  work. This includes the "Rap Attack Lives: The Legacy of
  Nasty Nes" project and the "OurStory" series, which capture
  the memories and perspectives of the people who built the
  scene.

- **Education:** Youth programs that teach the elements of
  hip-hop culture (MCing, DJing, breaking, graffiti) and
  connect young people to the city's musical heritage.

- **Community building:** Networking and mentorship that
  connects established artists with emerging ones, maintaining
  the intergenerational continuity that the loss of physical
  spaces (the CD's clubs and community centers) has threatened.

### Significance

206 Zulu represents the institutional survival of the ecosystem
that Mix-A-Lot tried to build through commercial means. Where
NastyMix and Rhyme Cartel attempted to create a sustainable
scene through the music industry's mechanisms (labels,
distribution, radio), 206 Zulu operates through nonprofit
channels -- grants, donations, volunteer labor, community
partnerships. The approach is different, but the goal is the
same: keep the culture alive, develop new talent, and ensure
that Seattle's hip-hop story does not end when the last
original practitioner retires.

## MOHAI: "Legacy of Seattle Hip Hop" (2015-2016)

### The Exhibit

The Museum of History & Industry (MOHAI), Seattle's primary
history museum, hosted the "Legacy of Seattle Hip Hop"
exhibit from 2015 to 2016. The exhibit was curated by Jazmyn
Scott and Aaron Walker-Loud and represented the first major
institutional recognition of hip-hop as a significant part
of Seattle's cultural history.

### Exhibit Contents

The exhibit featured:

- **Massive Monkees breakdance crew:** The Seattle-based
  b-boy crew, known for winning the international R16
  competition, demonstrated the physical dimension of
  hip-hop culture -- breaking as a discipline with its own
  history, technique, and competitive tradition.

- **Production stations:** Interactive stations featuring
  beats by Vitamin D and Jake One, two of Seattle's most
  respected hip-hop producers. These stations allowed
  visitors to experience the production side of hip-hop --
  the beat-making, sampling, and arrangement that underlie
  the genre's sonic identity.

- **Artifacts:** Macklemore's "Thrift Shop" fur coat (from
  the video for his 2012 #1 hit) was displayed alongside
  Nasty Nes Rodriguez's NastyMix bomber jacket. The
  juxtaposition was powerful: two eras of Seattle hip-hop,
  two artists who achieved national success from the same
  city, connected by a shared jacket display in a museum
  case.

  The fur coat represented Seattle hip-hop's 2010s commercial
  peak. The bomber jacket represented its 1980s origins.
  Together, they told a 30-year story.

- **Photography and memorabilia:** Images and objects from
  Seattle hip-hop's history, including flyers from community
  center shows, album artwork, and personal items donated by
  artists and community members.

### Significance

The MOHAI exhibit was a watershed moment for Seattle hip-hop's
institutional legitimacy. Museums confer a specific kind of
recognition: they declare that something is historically
significant, that it belongs in the permanent record, that
future generations should know about it.

For a scene that had been marginalized by the city's
grunge-centric music narrative, the exhibit was an official
correction. Seattle's music story includes hip-hop. Sir
Mix-A-Lot is part of that story. Nasty Nes is part of that
story. The Central District is part of that story.

The exhibit also served an educational function, introducing
younger Seattleites -- many of whom had no direct experience
of the CD's pre-gentrification culture -- to a history that
was rapidly becoming invisible in the physical landscape. The
community centers were closing. The apartments were being
demolished. The barbershops were becoming coffee shops. MOHAI
was preserving what the real estate market was erasing.

## Ben Camp's "The Birth of Seattle Rap" (2025)

### The Book

Ben Camp (known in the Seattle hip-hop community as
Novocaine132) published "The Birth of Seattle Rap" through
Arcadia Publishing in 2025. The book is the first
comprehensive written history of Seattle's hip-hop scene,
documenting the period from the early 1980s through the
scene's commercial and cultural peak.

### Significance

The book represents the maturation of a historical project
that began with oral histories (206 Zulu's "OurStory" series),
continued through museum exhibits (MOHAI's "Legacy" show),
and now exists in published form. The progression -- from
oral tradition to institutional exhibit to published book --
follows the standard pattern by which cultural histories are
preserved.

The people who lived it tell their stories. The institutions
archive the artifacts. Eventually someone writes it down.

Camp's book is particularly important because it provides a
narrative framework that connects the individual stories into
a coherent history. Oral histories are invaluable but
fragmented. Museum exhibits are curated but temporally
bounded. A book persists. It can be cited, referenced,
taught from, and debated.

"The Birth of Seattle Rap" gives the scene's history a
permanence that flyers, recordings, and memories alone
cannot provide.

## The Question of Ecosystem Sustainability

Mix-A-Lot's community-building efforts raise a fundamental
question: can one artist build a scene?

### The Argument For

Mix-A-Lot did build a scene, at least temporarily. NastyMix
Records created a label infrastructure. "Square Dance Rap,"
Swass, and Seminar proved that Seattle hip-hop could sell.
KUBE's format change was driven in part by Mix's commercial
success. The roster of NastyMix artists -- Kid Sensation,
E-Dawg, Funk Daddy, Criminal Nation -- demonstrated that
other talent existed.

"Seattle... The Dark Side" attempted to showcase that talent
nationally. For approximately a decade (1985-1995), Seattle
had a functioning hip-hop ecosystem, and Mix-A-Lot was its
center of gravity.

### The Argument Against

The ecosystem did not survive its founder's commercial
decline. When Mix left NastyMix, the label collapsed. When
"Baby Got Back's" momentum faded, the national attention
moved on. When KUBE lost its hip-hop format (in 2022, though
the decline began earlier), the PNW lost its most powerful
broadcast platform.

The infrastructure Mix built was dependent on his personal
commercial success -- it was not self-sustaining, not
diversified, not resilient to the departure or decline of
any single component.

### The Structural Reality

The truth is somewhere between the two arguments. Mix-A-Lot
built an ecosystem that was functional but fragile. Its
fragility was not a function of his effort or talent. It was
a function of Seattle's structural conditions:

1. **Small Black population:** With less than 10% of the
   city's residents identifying as Black, the potential
   artist pool and audience base were both small.

2. **No industry infrastructure:** Seattle had no major-label
   offices, no hip-hop A&R presence, no established studio
   system, no tour circuit, no music press covering the scene.

3. **Single-artist dependency:** Because Mix was the only
   commercially successful artist, the ecosystem's financial
   model was built on his revenue alone.

4. **Competing narrative:** The grunge explosion consumed all
   of the media's attention to Seattle music. There was no
   bandwidth left for a second Seattle narrative.

5. **Gentrification:** The physical displacement of the
   Central District's Black community removed the geographic
   anchor that had sustained the scene's social infrastructure.

## The Legacy Carriers

Despite the ecosystem's fragility, its legacy persists through
multiple channels:

**Institutional:** 206 Zulu, MOHAI's exhibit, Ben Camp's book,
the Seattle Symphony's recognition, and various academic and
journalistic accounts have preserved the history.

**Artistic:** Subsequent Seattle hip-hop artists -- Macklemore,
Blue Scholars, Shabazz Palaces, Sol, Prometheus Brown, Ishmael
Butler -- carry forward the tradition that Mix-A-Lot
established. Macklemore's "Thrift Shop" (#1, 2012) was the
second Seattle-based hip-hop song to top the Billboard Hot
100, twenty years after "Baby Got Back."

The Macklemore phenomenon -- an independent artist achieving
massive commercial success from Seattle without major-label
support -- is a direct echo of Mix-A-Lot's career arc.

**Cultural:** The "Wheedle's Groove" documentary (2009), the
MOHAI exhibit (2015-2016), and ongoing community events
maintain awareness of Seattle's hip-hop heritage among younger
generations who did not experience the original scene.

**Radio:** While KUBE's loss in 2022 was devastating, other
stations and platforms (streaming, podcasts, internet radio)
have partially filled the gap. The transmission mechanism has
changed, but the music continues to reach audiences.

## The Broader Pattern

Mix-A-Lot's community-building efforts are part of a broader
pattern in hip-hop: successful artists attempting to use their
platforms to elevate their local scenes. Jay-Z did it with
Roc-A-Fella (Harlem/Brooklyn). Master P did it with No Limit
(New Orleans). T.I. did it with Grand Hustle (Atlanta). Nipsey
Hussle did it with All Money In (Crenshaw/South LA).

The pattern is consistent: artist achieves success, artist
invests in local ecosystem, ecosystem produces some additional
artists, ecosystem's sustainability depends on the founder's
continued success and engagement.

The artists who succeeded most fully in scene-building -- Berry
Gordy, Russell Simmons, Dr. Dre -- did so by transitioning
from artist to executive, dedicating their primary energy to
label management rather than personal artistry. Mix-A-Lot
never made that transition. He remained, first and foremost,
an artist -- a person who wanted to make music, not manage a
roster.

This is not a failure of character. It is a structural
limitation of the artist-as-ecosystem-builder model. Building
an ecosystem requires different skills, different temperaments,
and different time allocations than making art. The rare
individuals who excel at both are the exceptions.

Mix-A-Lot was an extraordinary artist and a sincere community
advocate. He was not an extraordinary executive. That is not
a criticism. It is a recognition that ecosystem-building
requires a different kind of greatness.

## The Dream's Afterlife

"My dream is to create 10 black millionaires right here in
Seattle."

The dream did not produce 10 Black millionaires through the
music industry. But it produced something else: a proof of
concept.

Mix-A-Lot proved that world-class hip-hop could come from
Seattle. He proved that an independent artist could compete
with major-label machinery. He proved that creative
self-sufficiency -- writing, producing, engineering,
performing everything yourself -- was a viable model. He
proved that genre boundaries were imaginary. And he proved
that one person, with the right tools and the right
determination, could build infrastructure where none existed.

The people who followed -- Macklemore proving it again with
"Thrift Shop," Shabazz Palaces proving it with experimental
hip-hop on Sub Pop (the grunge label!), Blue Scholars proving
it with community-rooted conscious rap -- all stand on ground
that Anthony Ray broke from a dining room in Auburn.

The ecosystem he built did not survive in the form he
imagined. But the principles he demonstrated -- independence,
self-sufficiency, genre freedom, community investment, the
refusal to relocate to someone else's city -- are still
operating. They are still producing artists. They are still
proving that hip-hop does not require New York or Los Angeles
to be real.

The dream is still alive. It just looks different than he
expected.
