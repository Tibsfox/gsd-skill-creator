# The Discography

## Overview: Seven Albums, Four Labels, One Producer

Sir Mix-A-Lot's discography spans six studio albums and one
compilation, released between 1988 and 2003 across four
different label configurations. The label trajectory --
NastyMix Records to Rhyme Cartel Records to Def American /
American Recordings to Artist Direct -- is itself a narrative
about creative control, independence, and the economic
realities of hip-hop in the late 20th century.

What makes this discography unusual is the production credit.
On every album, Mix-A-Lot is the primary (and usually sole)
producer. He wrote, arranged, programmed, performed, produced,
and engineered his own material. In an era when hip-hop
production was increasingly specialized -- when artists like
Dr. Dre, DJ Premier, and Pete Rock were establishing the
producer as a distinct creative role -- Mix-A-Lot insisted on
handling everything himself.

This was not ego. It was the logical extension of a career
that began in a dining-room studio in Auburn, Washington,
where there was simply nobody else to do the work.

## Swass (1988) -- The Platinum Debut

"Swass" was Sir Mix-A-Lot's debut full-length album, released
on NastyMix Records in 1988. The title is Seattle slang --
"swass" meaning cool, smooth, or impressive. The album was
certified platinum by the RIAA in 1990, selling over one
million copies.

For context, consider what platinum meant in 1988 for a
hip-hop album from Seattle. The genre's commercial center
was still New York. Run-DMC's "Raising Hell" (1986) had been
the first rap album to go platinum. The Beastie Boys'
"Licensed to Ill" (1986) had been the first rap album to
debut at #1. Public Enemy's "It Takes a Nation of Millions
to Hold Us Back" was released the same year as Swass. NWA's
"Straight Outta Compton" came out the same year.

These were the albums that were defining hip-hop's commercial
and artistic trajectory -- and they were all backed by major
labels (Def Jam, Columbia, Ruthless/Priority) with national
distribution and marketing budgets.

Swass had none of that. NastyMix Records was a local Seattle
label with no national distribution infrastructure. The album
received virtually no radio exposure outside the Pacific
Northwest. It was not reviewed by the major music publications.
It had no music videos in rotation on MTV or BET.

It went platinum anyway.

### "Posse on Broadway"

The album's signature track was "Posse on Broadway," a
narrative about cruising Broadway Avenue in Seattle's Capitol
Hill neighborhood. The song is geographically specific in a
way that was unusual for hip-hop at the time -- it references
Broadway, the CD (Central District), Rainier Avenue, the
corner of 23rd and Union, and other Seattle landmarks that
would have been meaningless to listeners in New York or
Los Angeles. This specificity was the point.

Mix-A-Lot was not trying to make music that sounded like it
could have come from anywhere. He was making music that could
only have come from Seattle.

"Posse on Broadway" became a regional anthem. It established
Sir Mix-A-Lot as Seattle's premier hip-hop artist and
demonstrated that local geographic references -- the same
strategy that would later define artists from Houston
(Scarface, UGK) and Atlanta (OutKast, Gucci Mane) -- could
resonate with audiences who had never set foot in the city.

The song's appeal was not the specific geography but the
universal experience it described: young people driving
around their city, music loud, claiming their space. Every
city has a Broadway. The details were Seattle's. The feeling
was everyone's.

### "Iron Man" with Metal Church

The album also included "Iron Man," a collaboration with
Seattle thrash metal band Metal Church. This was a remake
of Black Sabbath's classic, reimagined as a rap-metal hybrid.
The collaboration was extraordinary for 1988 -- years before
Run-DMC's "Walk This Way" with Aerosmith became the template
for rap-rock crossovers (though "Walk This Way" was actually
released in 1986, the broader genre fusion did not become a
movement until the early 1990s).

The Metal Church collaboration was not a novelty. It was an
expression of Seattle's unique musical ecosystem, where
hip-hop and metal coexisted in a way they did not in other
cities. Mix-A-Lot and Metal Church shared a city, shared
audiences, and shared a DIY ethos. The collaboration made
sense locally even if it confused the national audience.

This pattern -- crossing genre boundaries that the industry
treated as walls -- would recur throughout Mix-A-Lot's career.

## Seminar (1989) -- The Gold Follow-Up

"Seminar" was released in 1989 on NastyMix Records and was
certified gold, selling over 700,000 copies. The album
consolidated Mix-A-Lot's position as a commercially viable
artist while demonstrating the range of his production skills.

### "My Hooptie"

The album's most enduring track was "My Hooptie," a comedic
ode to a broken-down car. The song catalogs every possible
automotive malfunction -- no muffler, cracked windshield,
broken heater, bald tires -- over a sparse, bass-heavy beat.
Like "Posse on Broadway," the song's appeal was its
specificity and humor.

Mix-A-Lot was not posturing as wealthy or glamorous. He was
describing the reality of his audience's daily life with
affection and wit. The broken-down car was not a symbol of
failure. It was a shared experience, something to laugh
about rather than be ashamed of. The humor was inclusive --
he was laughing with, not at.

"My Hooptie" anticipated a strain of hip-hop humor that
would later flourish in artists like Ludacris and Lil
Dicky -- comedy as a legitimate mode of rap expression,
not a gimmick but a genuine artistic choice.

### "Beepers"

"Beepers" captured a specific technological moment -- the
era when pagers (beepers) were the primary mobile communication
device, particularly in communities that could not afford or
access early cellular phones. The song is a time capsule,
documenting a technology that would be obsolete within a
decade.

Mix-A-Lot's ability to find narrative material in everyday
technology -- cars, pagers, the detritus of daily life --
distinguished him from both the conscious rap of Public
Enemy and the gangsta posturing of NWA. He occupied a
middle ground: observational, funny, grounded in the
material culture of his audience.

The production on Seminar also showed growth. While Swass
had been raw and energetic, Seminar was more polished
without losing its bass-heavy punch. Mix was refining his
studio skills, learning from each project, pushing the
limits of what he could produce from his Auburn home
studio.

## The NastyMix Split (1991)

After two commercially successful albums on NastyMix Records,
Mix-A-Lot left the label in 1991. The split was about
creative control. He told Spin magazine:

> "My main reason for leaving Nastymix was to get creative
> control over my product. I have a difference of opinion
> with how most labels promote rap. People don't take it
> seriously. What's happening with rap is it's being all
> lumped together, and that's scary. Rap is more informative
> than any music ever was."

This quote reveals several things. First, the departure was
not primarily financial (though there were financial disputes).
It was about control over "my product" -- a phrase that
reveals Mix's understanding of himself not just as an artist
but as a manufacturer of intellectual property.

Second, his complaint about rap being "all lumped together"
reflects a sophisticated understanding of genre politics --
the tendency of the industry to treat all hip-hop as
interchangeable, to deny its internal diversity and artistic
seriousness.

Third, the assertion that "rap is more informative than any
music ever was" positions hip-hop not as entertainment but
as a knowledge medium. This is a remarkable claim from an
artist best known for humorous party tracks, and it reveals
a depth of conviction about the genre's potential that his
public persona did not always advertise.

Mix founded Rhyme Cartel Records as his own label. He named
his publishing company "Where's My Publishing Inc." -- a wry
reference to the financial disputes with NastyMix. The joke
embedded in the company name tells its own story: the artist
who did all the creative work asking, with dark humor, where
the money went.

Publishing rights in the music industry are the mechanism
through which songwriters earn ongoing royalties. Controlling
your publishing means controlling the long-term revenue from
your creative output. "Where's My Publishing Inc." is both a
punchline and a business strategy.

NastyMix Records' final release was Criminal Nation's "Trouble
in the Hood" (1992). Without Mix-A-Lot, the label had lost
its commercially viable artist and had no path to sustainability.
The label's collapse after Mix's departure illustrates a
structural vulnerability in artist-founded labels: when the
label's commercial success depends on a single artist, the
artist's departure is an extinction event.

## Mack Daddy (1992) -- The #1 Album

Mix-A-Lot signed a distribution deal with Rick Rubin's Def
American Recordings. The arrangement was significant: Mix
retained creative control through Rhyme Cartel while gaining
access to Def American's national distribution network and
Rubin's promotional expertise. This was the model he had been
seeking -- independence with infrastructure.

"Mack Daddy" was released in 1992 and became Mix-A-Lot's
most commercially successful album, achieving multi-platinum
certification.

### "Baby Got Back" (1992)

"Baby Got Back" was released as the lead single on February
4, 1992. It spent five weeks at #1 on the Billboard Hot 100.
It was certified double-platinum as a single. It won the 1993
Grammy Award for Best Rap Solo Performance and the 1993
American Music Award for Favorite Rap/Hip-Hop Artist.

The song was recorded in Mix's home studio in Auburn,
Washington. This fact bears repeating: the #1 song in
America, the Grammy-winning single, the double-platinum
hit, was produced in a studio off the dining room of a house
in a suburb of Seattle. Not in a professional studio in New
York or Los Angeles. Not with a team of engineers and
producers. In Auburn. By one person.

MTV aired the video only after 9 PM due to complaints about
its content. The controversy -- a celebration of Black female
body types that diverged from the fashion-industry thin ideal --
anticipated by decades the body-positivity movement that would
later become mainstream. Mix-A-Lot's defense of the song was
characteristically straightforward: he was describing what he
actually found attractive, not performing a role.

Mix later estimated he earned over $100 million in royalties
from "Baby Got Back" over the song's lifetime. The song's
cultural persistence has been extraordinary -- it remains
instantly recognizable more than three decades after its
release, has been referenced, sampled, parodied, and covered
hundreds of times, and continues to generate streaming revenue.

### The 1992 Context

"Baby Got Back" hit #1 in the same year that Dr. Dre released
"The Chronic," the album that defined G-funk and established
the West Coast as hip-hop's dominant commercial force. But
here is the chronological detail that matters:

"Baby Got Back" reached #1 in May 1992. "The Chronic" was
released in December 1992.

Mix-A-Lot's Seattle-produced hit preceded the G-funk
revolution. He was not riding a wave. He was ahead of it.

The standard narrative of West Coast hip-hop runs: NWA (1988)
to Death Row (1991) to The Chronic (1992) to Snoop Dogg's
"Doggystyle" (1993). In this narrative, Mix-A-Lot is a
footnote -- a novelty act whose biggest hit happened to occur
during the West Coast's rise.

But the timeline tells a different story. Mix-A-Lot was
selling platinum records before The Chronic existed. He had
a #1 hit before Snoop Dogg had a record deal. His success
was not a byproduct of the West Coast movement. It was
independent evidence that hip-hop had transcended geography
entirely -- that an artist from a city with no hip-hop
infrastructure could compete with, and outsell, artists from
the genre's established centers.

## "Seattle... The Dark Side" (1993) -- The Compilation

"Seattle... The Dark Side" was released in 1993 on Rhyme
Cartel/Def American. It was not a Sir Mix-A-Lot solo album
but a compilation featuring local Seattle artists, including
Kid Sensation and Jazz Lee Aston. The album was Mix's attempt
to use his commercial platform to elevate the Seattle hip-hop
scene -- to share the spotlight with artists who did not have
his national visibility.

The compilation was an act of ecosystem building. Mix had
proven that platinum records could come from Seattle. "The
Dark Side" was an attempt to answer the question: if one
artist can break through, can he bring others with him?

The title itself -- "The Dark Side" -- positioned Seattle
hip-hop as the city's underground counterpart to the grunge
explosion that was dominating mainstream attention. In 1993,
Seattle meant Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, and Alice in
Chains to most of the world. "The Dark Side" asserted that
the city had another story to tell.

The commercial impact was limited. The compilation did not
produce a breakout hit for any of the featured artists. The
challenge Mix faced was structural: national distribution
could get the record into stores, but without radio support,
press coverage, and tour infrastructure for the individual
artists, exposure alone was not enough to launch careers.

## Chief Boot Knocka (1994)

"Chief Boot Knocka" was released in 1994 on Rhyme Cartel /
American Recordings (the label had dropped "Def" from its
name). It peaked at #69 on the Billboard 200.

The album featured a guest appearance by Flea, the bassist
from Red Hot Chili Peppers -- another cross-genre collaboration
that reflected Mix-A-Lot's refusal to stay within hip-hop's
assumed boundaries. "Just Da Pimpin' in Me" received a Grammy
nomination, confirming that Mix-A-Lot's critical credibility
extended beyond "Baby Got Back."

"Chief Boot Knocka" was a more ambitious and darker album
than its predecessors. Mix was responding to the success of
"Baby Got Back" by demonstrating range -- proving that he was
not a one-hit wonder or a novelty act but a serious artist
with a body of work.

The album's relative commercial underperformance compared to
"Mack Daddy" reflected a broader industry shift: by 1994, the
market's attention had moved to G-funk (Snoop Dogg, Warren G,
Dr. Dre's productions) and the emerging East Coast/West Coast
rivalry.

## Return of the Bumpasaurus (1996) -- The Quiet Album

"Return of the Bumpasaurus" was released in 1996 on American
Recordings. It received minimal promotional support from the
label, and Mix-A-Lot subsequently left American Recordings.

The timing was brutal. 1996 was the year Tupac Shakur was
murdered in Las Vegas (September 13). The East Coast/West
Coast feud was at its peak. Hip-hop was consumed by the
rivalry between Bad Boy Records and Death Row Records,
between Biggie and Pac, between New York and Los Angeles.
A Seattle rapper releasing a bass-heavy party album in this
environment was commercially invisible -- not because the
music was bad, but because the cultural moment had no space
for it.

Mix-A-Lot's departure from American Recordings marked the
end of his major-label distribution era. From this point
forward, he would operate as a fully independent artist.

## Daddy's Home (2003) -- The Independent Return

After a seven-year recording hiatus, Mix-A-Lot released
"Daddy's Home" in 2003 through Artist Direct, a digital-era
distribution platform. The album featured "Big Johnson" as
its lead single.

By 2003, the music industry had been transformed by Napster,
peer-to-peer file sharing, and the early stages of the
digital distribution revolution. Artist Direct represented a
new model: internet-enabled distribution that gave artists
direct access to their audience without traditional label
intermediation.

For Mix-A-Lot, this was the logical endpoint of a career-long
pursuit of independence. He had left NastyMix for creative
control. He had left American for lack of promotional support.
Now he was releasing music on a platform that eliminated the
label entirely.

## The Label Trajectory as Narrative

The progression from NastyMix to Rhyme Cartel to Def American
to American to Artist Direct tells a story about the economics
of creative independence in the music industry:

**NastyMix (1985-1991):** The bootstrap phase. Local label,
no distribution, total creative output from one artist.
Commercial success beyond anyone's expectations. But the
artist does not control the business.

**Rhyme Cartel / Def American (1991-1994):** The optimal
configuration. Artist-owned label for creative control,
major-label partner for distribution and promotion. Rick
Rubin's infrastructure provides the reach that NastyMix
could not. This is the period of maximum commercial success.

**American (1994-1996):** The decline. Label support
evaporates. Promotional budgets shrink. The artist is still
producing the same quality of work, but the infrastructure
has withdrawn its investment.

**Artist Direct (2003):** The fully independent endgame.
No label, no distribution partner, no promotional
infrastructure. Just the artist and the audience, connected
by the internet.

Each phase represents a different answer to the same
question: what is the relationship between creative control
and commercial reach? The career arc suggests that the
optimal configuration -- control plus reach -- is inherently
unstable, achievable only when an artist's commercial
momentum is strong enough to compel a label partner to
respect their autonomy.

## Legacy of the Discography

Six studio albums, one compilation, two platinum certifications,
one gold, a Grammy, and over $100 million in estimated
royalties from a single song. All of it produced by one
person in a home studio in Auburn, Washington.

The discography is not just a body of work. It is a proof of
concept -- evidence that vertically integrated creative
production, operating from outside the industry's established
centers, can produce results that compete with anything the
major-label system generates.

The music industry did not learn this lesson from Mix-A-Lot.
It would have to relearn it from Chance the Rapper, from
Macklemore (another Seattle artist, not coincidentally), from
the entire wave of independent artists who used the internet
to circumvent the label system. Mix-A-Lot was 15 years ahead
of that wave. He was independent before independent was a
marketing strategy.
