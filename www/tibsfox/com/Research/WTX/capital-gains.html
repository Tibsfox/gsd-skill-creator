Capital Gains Excise Tax

SB 5096 (2021), the Quinn v. State constitutional challenge, the 2025 SB 5813 surcharge, and the IRC Section 1202 QSBS interaction

Washington taxes what you sell, not what you earn. The capital gains excise tax is imposed on the act of selling or exchanging a capital asset — a specific, discrete event — not on the accumulation of income over time. That framing is the constitutional key to the entire statute.

1. The Constitutional Backdrop — Culliton v. Chase

Any discussion of Washington's capital gains tax must begin with Culliton v. Chase, 185 Wash. 476 (1933). The Washington Supreme Court held that income is “property” under Article VII of the state constitution, and therefore any tax on income must satisfy the uniformity clause — uniform rate, no graduation. This ruling, never overturned, forecloses graduated income taxation in Washington.

The capital gains excise tax was designed to navigate around Culliton. The legislative strategy: frame the tax not as a tax on income (which would be a property tax under Culliton), but as an excise tax on the specific transaction of selling or exchanging a capital asset. The gain is the measure of the tax; the subject of the tax is the act of transfer.

This distinction — between the subject of a tax and the measure of a tax — is the constitutional linchpin of the entire statute. The legislature bet that the Washington Supreme Court would accept it. In 2023, the court did.

2. SB 5096 (2021) — The Enactment

Senate Bill 5096 was passed by the Washington Legislature and signed by Governor Jay Inslee on May 4, 2021. The tax became effective January 1, 2022.

Structure

  • Rate: 7% on long-term capital gains.
  • Threshold: $250,000 standard deduction (the first $250,000 of net long-term capital gains is exempt). Indexed for inflation.
  • Taxpayers: Individuals only. C-Corporations are not subject to the Washington capital gains tax. (C-Corps pay federal corporate income tax on capital gains; Washington does not impose a state-level equivalent.)
  • Tax base: “Adjusted capital gain” as reported for federal income tax purposes, with Washington-specific modifications.

Exclusions

SB 5096 excluded several categories of assets from the capital gains tax base:

  • Real estate. All gains from the sale of real property are excluded. (Real property sales are subject to the Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) instead.)
  • Retirement accounts. Gains within 401(k)s, IRAs, and other qualified retirement accounts are excluded.
  • Livestock and agricultural products. Gains from the sale of livestock and agricultural land are excluded — a concession to Washington's agricultural sector.
  • Timber. Gains from the sale of standing timber are excluded — a concession to the timber industry.
  • Depreciable assets used in a trade or business. These are generally treated as ordinary income federally (IRC Section 1245/1250 recapture), not capital gains.

Filing and Administration

The capital gains tax is administered by the Washington Department of Revenue (DOR), not the IRS. The filing deadline aligns with the federal return (April 15, with extensions available). Taxpayers file a separate Washington capital gains return through the DOR portal.

3. The “Excise Tax” Framing

The legislature labelled the capital gains tax as an excise tax in the statute itself (RCW 82.87). The statutory language imposes the tax on “the sale or exchange of long-term capital assets” — on the transaction, not on the income.

This framing was deliberate. The constitutional logic:

  1. An excise tax is a tax on an activity, privilege, or transaction — not on property.
  2. The uniformity clause of Article VII applies to property taxes, not to excise taxes.
  3. Because the capital gains tax is an excise (on the sale), not a property tax (on income-as-property), Culliton's uniformity requirement does not apply.
  4. Therefore, the tax may be levied at a non-uniform rate (applying only above a threshold) without violating Article VII.

Critics argued this was formalism — that the tax is economically indistinguishable from an income tax on capital gains, regardless of the statutory label. Supporters argued that the distinction between a tax on a discrete event (a sale) and a tax on accumulated income is analytically meaningful and well-grounded in excise tax jurisprudence.

4. Quinn v. State (2023)

The defining constitutional test for Washington's capital gains tax.

The Lower Court

The tax was challenged in Douglas County Superior Court by a group of taxpayers who argued that the capital gains tax was, in substance, an income tax on property — and therefore unconstitutional under Culliton. The Superior Court agreed and struck down the tax.

The Washington Supreme Court Reversal

The Washington Supreme Court reversed in a 7–2 decision in March 2023. The majority held:

  • The capital gains tax is an excise tax on the sale or exchange of capital assets.
  • The tax is triggered by a specific, discrete event — the act of selling or exchanging a capital asset — not by the mere accumulation of income over time.
  • The gain is the measure of the tax, not the subject. The tax is measured by gain but imposed on the act of transfer.
  • Culliton v. Chase addresses taxes on income as property. The capital gains tax is not a tax on income-as-property; it is an excise on a transaction.
  • Therefore, the uniformity clause of Article VII does not apply, and the tax does not violate the Washington Constitution.

Certiorari Denied

The plaintiffs petitioned the US Supreme Court for certiorari. In January 2024, the US Supreme Court denied the petition, declining to review the Washington Supreme Court's decision. The capital gains excise tax is now settled law.

The Analytical Question

Whether the Quinn majority's distinction between an excise on a transaction and an income tax on accumulated gain is analytically rigorous or analytically sufficient-by-stipulation is a question that tax scholars will debate. What is not debatable is that the Washington Supreme Court adopted it, and the US Supreme Court declined to disturb it. As a practical matter, the framing is settled.

5. SB 5813 (2025) — The Capital Gains Surcharge

As part of the 2025 Ferguson reform package, Governor Bob Ferguson signed SB 5813 on May 20, 2025. The bill added a 2.9% surcharge on long-term capital gains exceeding $1 million, creating a two-tier rate structure.

Effective Date and Retroactivity

SB 5813 was retroactive to January 1, 2025. Taxpayers who realized capital gains in the first five months of 2025 (before the bill was signed) are subject to the new surcharge rate. Retroactive tax legislation raises due process questions, though retroactive tax changes within the same tax year have generally been upheld by federal courts.

Revenue Estimates

The 2025 surcharge was projected to generate substantial additional revenue, though capital gains tax revenue is inherently volatile — it depends on asset prices, market timing, and taxpayer behavior (deferral, realization timing, relocation).

6. QSBS — IRC Section 1202

Critical planning point for C-Corp equity holders. The interaction between the federal QSBS exclusion and the Washington capital gains tax is one of the most important planning considerations for founders and early-stage investors in Washington.

Federal QSBS Exclusion

IRC Section 1202 provides a federal exclusion for gain from the sale of Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS). The requirements:

  1. C-Corporation. The issuing entity must be a domestic C-Corporation (not an S-Corp, LLC, or partnership).
  2. Gross assets under $50 million. At the time of stock issuance, the corporation's aggregate gross assets must not exceed $50 million.
  3. Original issuance. The stock must have been acquired at original issuance (directly from the corporation), not on the secondary market.
  4. Five-year holding period. The stock must be held for more than five years.
  5. Active business requirement. At least 80% of the corporation's assets must be used in the active conduct of one or more qualified trades or businesses. Certain businesses are excluded (professional services, banking, insurance, farming, mining, hospitality).

Exclusion Amount

For QSBS acquired after September 27, 2010, the exclusion is 100% of the gain, up to the greater of:

  • $10 million, or
  • 10 times the taxpayer's adjusted basis in the stock.

For a founder who invested $100,000 in a qualifying C-Corp and sells after five years for $5 million, the entire $4.9 million gain could be excluded from federal taxation.

The Washington Interaction

This is the critical point. Washington's capital gains tax is imposed on the taxpayer's “adjusted capital gain” — which is derived from the taxpayer's federal adjusted capital gain. If gain from the sale of QSBS is excluded from federal adjusted capital gain under Section 1202, that gain arguably does not appear in the starting figure used to compute Washington's capital gains tax.

The implication: for taxpayers holding qualifying QSBS, the Section 1202 exclusion may eliminate both the federal and Washington capital gains tax liability on the same gain. This is a powerful outcome for founders of qualifying C-Corps who meet the five-year hold requirement.

Planning note: The QSBS exclusion is a federal provision. Washington has not enacted its own QSBS exclusion, but the flow-through effect of the federal exclusion on Washington's tax base computation may achieve the same result. This area requires careful analysis by a qualified tax advisor, as the DOR has not issued formal guidance on the treatment of Section 1202-excluded gain for Washington capital gains purposes.

Entity Structure Implications

The QSBS exclusion applies only to C-Corporation stock. For founders choosing between a C-Corp and an S-Corp or LLC, the potential QSBS benefit is a significant factor. The trade-off:

  • C-Corp with QSBS: Corporate-level federal income tax at 21%, but if Section 1202 requirements are met, gain on sale of stock may be excluded from both federal and Washington capital gains tax.
  • S-Corp or LLC: Pass-through taxation (no entity-level federal tax), but no QSBS exclusion available. Capital gains on exit flow through to the individual level and are subject to Washington's capital gains tax above the deduction threshold.

7. Distributional Analysis

The capital gains tax is, by design, a tax on the wealthiest residents. Capital gains are overwhelmingly concentrated among high-income taxpayers:

  • Nationally, the top 1% of taxpayers by income account for approximately 70–80% of all realized capital gains in a typical year.
  • The top 0.1% account for a large share of gains above $1 million — the threshold for the 2025 surcharge.
  • The $278,000 standard deduction (2025) means that taxpayers with relatively modest capital gains — including most retirees selling appreciated stock gradually — owe no Washington capital gains tax.

Revenue Volatility

Capital gains tax revenue is highly volatile. It depends on:

  • Stock market performance
  • Real estate market cycles (though real estate gains are excluded in Washington)
  • Taxpayer timing decisions (deferral in down markets, realization in up markets)
  • Federal tax law changes (changes to federal capital gains rates affect realization behavior)
  • Migration behavior (see below)

States that rely heavily on capital gains revenue experience significant year-to-year revenue swings. Washington's reliance on the B&O tax and sales tax as primary revenue sources means that capital gains revenue is supplemental rather than foundational, partially mitigating the volatility risk.

8. The “Tax Flight” Debate

A recurring question in capital gains taxation is whether the tax drives wealthy residents to relocate to no-income-tax states — Nevada, Texas, Florida, Wyoming, South Dakota, Tennessee, Alaska, or New Hampshire.

The Case That Flight Occurs

  • High-net-worth individuals with significant unrealized gains have a financial incentive to establish domicile in a state with no capital gains tax before realizing gains.
  • Remote work makes it easier for knowledge workers and entrepreneurs to relocate without changing employers or business operations.
  • Anecdotal reports of founders and investors relocating from Washington to Nevada or Texas increased after SB 5096 (2021) and intensified after SB 5813 (2025).
  • The 9.9% top rate (after the 2025 surcharge) is now among the highest state-level capital gains rates in the country.

The Case That Flight Is Overstated

  • Academic research on interstate migration in response to state taxes generally finds modest effects. Most taxpayers do not relocate based on tax considerations alone.
  • Washington's quality of life, technology sector ecosystem, proximity to family and professional networks, and other non-tax factors are strong retention forces.
  • The capital gains tax affects a small number of taxpayers (those with gains above $278,000 in a single year). Most Washington residents owe nothing.
  • Domicile changes for tax purposes require genuine relocation — not just a Nevada mailing address. DOR audit of domicile claims is a compliance mechanism.

The Analytical Reality

Tax migration almost certainly occurs at the margins. Some high-net-worth individuals will relocate. The revenue impact of that migration is uncertain and difficult to isolate from other factors (job changes, retirement, personal preferences). The policy question is whether the revenue gained from the tax exceeds the revenue lost through migration and behavioral responses (deferral, restructuring, avoidance). Definitive answers require several years of data collection and analysis.
