The Starting Fact

Washington State has no corporate income tax and no personal income tax. Instead, Washington operates one of the oldest and most comprehensive gross receipts tax systems in the United States — the Business & Occupation (B&O) tax, layered with a 6.5% state retail sales tax, local sales tax add-ons that can push combined rates above 10%, a 2021-enacted capital gains excise tax that survived a constitutional challenge in 2023, and a 2025 tax reform package that represents the largest tax change in Washington State history.

This research series treats Washington's tax system as an object of study. It traces the constitutional and historical origins back to Culliton v. Chase (1933) and the Revenue Act of 1935, walks through the mechanics of the B&O tax (including the new three-tier Service rate introduced by HB 2081 in 2025), maps the retail sales tax before and after the SB 5814 service expansion, unpacks the Capital Gains Excise Tax upheld in Quinn v. State (2023) and surcharged by SB 5813 (2025), and surveys compliance, incentives, and comparative frameworks.

Washington's tax architecture is radically different from the income-tax-based systems of forty-one other US states. It was built in the 1930s, shaped by the Washington Supreme Court's 1933 Culliton v. Chase decision holding that income is “property” under the state constitution's uniformity clause, and has survived nine decades of repeated attempts at constitutional reform. The result is a system that taxes revenue flows rather than accumulated income — a gross receipts excise layered with a sales tax and, since 2021, a stand-alone capital gains excise.

The 2025 Ferguson reforms (HB 2081, SB 5813, SB 5814), signed by Governor Bob Ferguson on May 20, 2025, represent the largest tax change in Washington State history. They introduced a progressive three-tier Service B&O rate, added a 2.9% surcharge to capital gains above $1 million, and dramatically expanded the sales tax base to cover IT services, custom software development, advertising, staffing, and live presentations starting October 1, 2025.

The Tax Architecture — At a Glance

  1. No state corporate income tax. No Washington analog to Form 1120. No net-income computation, no NOL carryforward, no deduction mechanics on a state income tax return. Federal corporate income tax still applies at 21%.
  2. No state personal income tax. No Form 1040 state equivalent. W-2 wages are not taxed at the state level.
  3. Business & Occupation (B&O) Tax — RCW 82.04. The primary Washington business tax. Gross receipts excise on all revenue, before any deductions. Rate depends on activity classification. Service tier structure as of October 1, 2025: 1.5% / 1.75% / 2.1% based on prior-year income.
  4. Retail Sales Tax — RCW 82.08. 6.5% state base + local rates (2–4% typical) = ~8.5%–10.6% combined. After SB 5814 (effective October 1, 2025), the base expanded to include IT services, custom software development, advertising services, staffing, and live presentations.
  5. Use Tax — RCW 82.12. Complement to the sales tax. Due on purchases made without paying Washington sales tax.
  6. Capital Gains Excise Tax — RCW 82.87. Enacted 2021 as SB 5096, upheld as an excise tax in Quinn v. State (2023), surcharged by SB 5813 (2025). 7% on long-term gains above $278,000 (2025 deduction); additional 2.9% tier above $1M for total 9.9% on the highest bracket. Applies to individuals only, not to C-Corporations.
  7. Real Estate Excise Tax (REET). Washington's transfer tax on real property sales, tiered from 0.75% to 1.28% at the state level plus local REET additions.
  8. Property Tax. Administered at the county level, subject to the 1% annual levy growth cap from Initiative 747 (2001).
  9. Estate Tax. Washington estate tax on estates above $2.193 million (2025), rates from 10% to 20%.

Why Washington's Tax System Is Interesting to Study

The short version: Washington is the leading example in the United States of a state that taxes business activity through a gross receipts excise rather than a net income tax. It is also the only state that has attempted to tax capital gains without a broad-based income tax — a maneuver upheld by the state Supreme Court in 2023 and dramatically expanded in 2025. These are not just tax-nerd curiosities. They are case studies in how a state's fiscal architecture shapes its economy, its political culture, and the strategies its businesses adopt.

The longer version:

  1. Constitutional path dependence. Culliton v. Chase (1933) foreclosed graduated income taxation in Washington. The result was not a simple no-tax state — it was a state that had to invent alternative revenue mechanisms. The B&O tax (1935) was that invention. Ninety years later, the invention still shapes every conversation about Washington tax policy.
  2. Gross receipts taxation as a model. Ohio's Commercial Activity Tax (2005), Oregon's Corporate Activity Tax (2019), Delaware's gross receipts tax, and Nevada's Commerce Tax all owe intellectual debts to Washington's B&O. Understanding Washington is the key to understanding this family of taxes.
  3. The 2023 Quinn decision. A state supreme court holding that a tax imposed on “the sale or exchange of capital assets” is an excise on the transaction rather than a property tax on income is either analytically elegant or analytically defensible-by-stipulation, depending on your priors. Either way, it is a major recent case in US state tax law.
  4. The 2025 reforms as fiscal history. May 20, 2025 is a date that Washington tax practitioners will remember for a generation. The simultaneous enactment of a progressive B&O tier structure, a capital gains surcharge, and the biggest sales tax expansion in decades represents a coordinated policy shift of the kind that rarely happens at the state level.
  5. Regressivity critique. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) has for years rated Washington as having one of the most regressive state and local tax systems in the country, primarily because of the heavy reliance on sales tax. The 2025 reforms partially address this critique by shifting burden toward larger businesses and higher-gain individuals.

The punchline: Washington's tax system is not an accident. It is the direct product of constitutional interpretation, Depression-era policy choices, and ninety years of political resistance to income taxation. It is also, after 2025, in the middle of its most significant structural reshaping in a generation. That is worth studying carefully.
