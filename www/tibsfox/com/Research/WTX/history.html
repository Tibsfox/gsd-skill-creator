History & Origins

From territorial property taxes through Culliton v. Chase (1933), the Revenue Act of 1935, ninety years of anti-income-tax politics, and the 2025 Ferguson reforms

Washington's tax system was not designed from a blank page. It was built in the wreckage of a fiscal crisis, shaped by a single court decision, and then frozen in place by nine decades of constitutional inertia. Every feature of the current system — the B&O tax, the sales tax, the absence of an income tax, the capital gains excise — traces back to the 1930s.

1. Territorial-Era Property Taxes (1853–1889)

Washington Territory was created by Congress in 1853, carved from the northern portion of Oregon Territory. From its founding, the territorial government relied on property taxation as its primary revenue source — a standard 19th-century approach shared by virtually every US state and territory.

The property tax was a general levy on real and personal property, administered at the county level, with assessed valuations set by county assessors and reviewed by boards of equalization. The system was simple by modern standards: there were no income taxes, no sales taxes, no excise taxes of any significance. Government was small; its revenue needs were modest; and the property tax, supplemented by federal transfers and fees, was sufficient.

Statehood and Article VII

Washington achieved statehood on November 11, 1889, entering the Union as the 42nd state. The 1889 Washington Constitution included Article VII, which established the framework for state and local taxation. The critical provision was the uniformity clause: all taxes on property must be uniform in rate upon the same class of property within the jurisdiction levying the tax.

This clause was not unusual for its era — many state constitutions adopted uniformity requirements. But in Washington, the interaction between the uniformity clause and the later judicial interpretation of what constituted “property” would prove decisive. Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution would become the constitutional foundation for every subsequent tax policy debate in the state.

2. The Great Depression Fiscal Crisis

The Depression that began in 1929 devastated Washington's property tax base. Property values collapsed. Delinquency rates soared. County governments, which depended almost entirely on property tax revenue, faced fiscal emergencies. The state government, which relied on property tax revenue supplemented by modest license fees and federal grants, was in similar straits.

The crisis produced a political insurgency. The Washington State Grange, a powerful agrarian organization, led a populist movement for tax reform. Farmers, whose land was the most visible and easily assessed form of property, bore a disproportionate share of the property tax burden. The Grange’s solution was twofold: limit the property tax and replace the lost revenue with an income tax.

Initiative 69 (1932) — The Income Tax Attempt

In 1932, Washington voters approved Initiative 69, which enacted a graduated net income tax. The initiative was a direct product of the Depression-era fiscal crisis and the Grange movement's push for a progressive alternative to property taxation. The income tax was intended to shift the tax burden from property owners (primarily farmers and landowners) to higher-income earners.

The income tax was short-lived. It was immediately challenged in court.

3. Culliton v. Chase (1933)

Culliton v. Chase, 185 Wash. 476, 56 P.2d 81 (1933) — the single most consequential tax decision in Washington State history.

The Washington Supreme Court struck down the graduated income tax enacted by Initiative 69. The court's holding was built on two steps:

  1. Income is “property” within the meaning of Article VII of the Washington Constitution.
  2. Because income is property, any tax on income is a property tax and must satisfy the uniformity clause — meaning it must be levied at a uniform rate, not a graduated rate.

The key holding:

“Income, from whatever source derived, is property, and a tax on income is a tax on the property from which it is derived. A graduated net income tax is a property tax, and as such, must conform to the constitutional requirement that all taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property.”

The practical consequence: a graduated income tax is unconstitutional in Washington unless the constitution is amended. A flat-rate income tax would theoretically satisfy the uniformity requirement, but the political appetite for a flat income tax has never been sufficient to enact one.

The Shadow That Extends Nine Decades

Culliton has never been overruled. Multiple subsequent challenges and legislative attempts to work around it have failed. Constitutional amendment proposals to modify Article VII have been introduced repeatedly and have never achieved the supermajority needed for a ballot referral, or have failed at the ballot. As of 2026, Culliton remains controlling law — income is property, graduated income taxes violate the uniformity clause, and Washington cannot enact an income tax without amending its constitution.

This single decision, rendered ninety-three years ago, is the reason Washington has a B&O tax, a sales tax, and no income tax. Every feature of Washington's current tax architecture traces back to Culliton.

4. The Revenue Act of 1935

With income taxation foreclosed by Culliton, the state legislature turned to alternatives. The result was the Revenue Act of 1935, signed by Governor Clarence D. Martin, which created two new taxes that would become the permanent pillars of Washington's tax system:

  1. The Business & Occupation (B&O) Tax — a gross receipts excise tax on the privilege of engaging in business in Washington, levied on gross income without deductions for costs, wages, or expenses. This was not an income tax (because it was not measured by net income) and not a property tax (because it was imposed on the activity of doing business, not on the ownership of property). It was a privilege tax, framed as an excise.
  2. The Retail Sales Tax — a tax on the sale of tangible personal property at retail, collected by the seller and remitted to the state. Initial rate: 2%.

These two taxes were the alternative to income taxation. They were designed to be legally defensible under Culliton by avoiding the property-tax classification. The B&O tax was an excise on the activity of doing business; the sales tax was an excise on the transaction of sale. Neither was a tax on “property” within the meaning of Article VII.

The Two-Pillar Architecture

The 1935 Revenue Act established the fundamental architecture of Washington's tax system: a gross receipts tax on business revenue combined with a retail sales tax on consumer purchases. Nine decades later, this two-pillar architecture still stands. Every subsequent reform, expansion, and controversy in Washington tax law operates within the framework created in 1935.

5. Post-War Expansion (1945–1980)

The post-war period brought steady expansion of Washington's tax system within the 1935 framework:

  • Sales tax rate increases. The state sales tax rate rose incrementally: 2% (1935) to 3% (1941), 3.33% (1959), 4% (1965), 4.5% (1971), 4.6% (1976), and eventually to 6.5% (permanent in 1983). Local add-on rates were authorized starting in the 1970s.
  • B&O classification proliferation. The legislature created an increasing number of B&O tax classifications with different rates tailored to different industries. The major classifications that emerged included retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, and services (initially called “service and other activities”). Each classification had its own rate.
  • Industry-specific preferences. As specific industries became politically significant (aerospace, timber, agriculture), the legislature enacted targeted B&O rate reductions and exemptions. The Boeing aerospace preferences would become the largest single category of industry-specific tax preferences.
  • Base broadening. The sales tax base was expanded to cover additional categories of goods, and the use tax was strengthened to capture out-of-state purchases made by Washington residents and businesses.

Throughout this period, repeated attempts to enact an income tax — either by constitutional amendment or by legislative action testing the Culliton framework — failed. The anti-income-tax consensus that solidified after 1935 proved remarkably durable.

6. The Initiative Era (1980s–2000s)

Washington's initiative process became the dominant vehicle for tax policy change starting in the 1980s. A series of ballot initiatives and referenda shaped the tax landscape:

Initiative 747 (2001) — The Property Tax Levy Cap

Initiative 747, sponsored by anti-tax activist Tim Eyman, limited annual property tax levy growth to 1% per year (absent a voter-approved lid lift). This replaced the previous 6% annual growth limit and dramatically constrained local government revenue growth. The initiative was initially struck down by the Washington Supreme Court in Eyman v. Department of Revenue (2007) on technical grounds, but the legislature promptly re-enacted the 1% cap legislatively.

The practical effect: local governments that rely on property tax revenue (cities, counties, fire districts, library districts, school districts) can increase their levy by only 1% per year plus new construction, regardless of inflation or population growth. Over two decades, the compounding effect has significantly constrained local government budgets.

Failed Income Tax Initiatives

Multiple income tax initiatives appeared on Washington ballots during this period:

  • Initiative 1098 (2010) — Proposed by Bill Gates Sr., this initiative would have imposed a state income tax on high-income earners (above $200,000 individual / $400,000 joint). It was defeated by a wide margin, approximately 64–36%. The defeat reinforced the perception that Washington voters will not accept an income tax regardless of its structure.
  • Other income tax proposals in the 1990s and 2000s similarly failed at the ballot or in the legislature.

The consistent pattern: income tax proposals fail in Washington, regardless of whether they are pitched as progressive (tax the wealthy), modest (start small), or revenue-neutral (replace another tax). The anti-income-tax consensus appears cultural as much as constitutional.

7. The Progressive Tax Wave (2017–2022)

Starting around 2017, a new progressive tax movement emerged in Washington, driven by concerns about income inequality, the regressivity of the existing tax system (documented by ITEP's “Who Pays?” reports), and the political energy of the Seattle-area progressive coalition.

Seattle Income Tax Attempt (2017)

In 2017, the Seattle City Council enacted a city income tax on high-income earners. The ordinance was immediately challenged and struck down by King County Superior Court, which held that state law preempts city-level income taxes. The case highlighted the continuing constitutional and statutory barriers to income taxation at any level in Washington.

Seattle JumpStart Payroll Tax (2020)

In 2020, Seattle enacted the JumpStart Payroll Expense Tax, a payroll tax on large employers (those with annual payroll expense above $7 million in Seattle). The tax is structured as a business privilege tax on the act of employing workers in Seattle, measured by payroll expense and tiered by employee compensation levels. The highest rate (2.4%) applies to businesses with payroll above $1 billion for employees earning $400,000 or more.

JumpStart was designed to avoid the constitutional barriers that blocked the 2017 income tax. It is technically a business privilege tax (like the B&O tax) rather than a tax on individual income. It survived initial legal challenges.

SB 5096 — Capital Gains Excise Tax (2021)

In 2021, the Washington Legislature enacted SB 5096, imposing a 7% tax on long-term capital gains above $250,000 (indexed) for individuals. Governor Jay Inslee signed the bill on May 4, 2021. The legislature framed the tax as an excise tax on the transaction of selling or exchanging capital assets, not as an income tax on accumulated gain. This framing was designed to survive a Culliton challenge.

Quinn v. State (2023)

The capital gains tax was immediately challenged. Douglas County Superior Court struck it down as an unconstitutional income tax. The Washington Supreme Court reversed in March 2023 in Quinn v. State, holding that the tax was an excise on the specific act of selling or exchanging capital assets — triggered by a discrete event (the sale), not by the mere accumulation of income. Because it was an excise tax, not a property tax, Culliton's uniformity requirement did not apply.

The US Supreme Court denied certiorari in January 2024, leaving the Washington Supreme Court's decision undisturbed. The capital gains excise tax was locked in.

8. The 2025 Ferguson Reforms

May 20, 2025 — the largest tax change in Washington State history.

On May 20, 2025, Governor Bob Ferguson signed three bills that together represent the most significant restructuring of Washington's tax system since the Revenue Act of 1935:

HB 2081 — B&O Rate Restructure

Replaced the flat Service B&O rate with a three-tier progressive structure based on prior-year gross income:

Prior-year gross incomeService B&O rate
Under $1 million1.5% (unchanged)
$1 million to $5 million1.75%
Over $5 million2.1%

Also provided for manufacturing and wholesaling rates to increase to 0.5% effective January 1, 2027.

SB 5813 — Capital Gains Surcharge

Added a 2.9% surcharge on long-term capital gains exceeding $1 million, bringing the top rate to 9.9%. Retroactive to January 1, 2025. The standard deduction was set at $278,000 for 2025 (indexed for inflation).

SB 5814 — Sales Tax Service Expansion

Expanded the retail sales tax base to include: IT services, custom software development, advertising services, temporary staffing, live presentations, and investigation/security services. Effective October 1, 2025. The largest single expansion of the sales tax base in the tax's ninety-year history.

The Political Context

The 2025 reforms were enacted by a Democratic-majority legislature and signed by Governor Ferguson, who took office in January 2025. The legislative rationale combined revenue need (a projected state budget shortfall), policy logic (modernizing the tax base for a services economy), and equity arguments (shifting burden toward larger businesses and higher-income individuals). The business community's response was divided: large technology companies and service firms opposed the new taxes, while some small business organizations supported the progressive rate structure of HB 2081 that shielded smaller businesses from rate increases.
