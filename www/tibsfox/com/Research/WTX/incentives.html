Washington's tax system operates primarily through a gross receipts tax with no deductions for costs. Tax incentives in this system take the form of rate reductions, credits against the B&O tax, sales tax exemptions, and deductions from the gross receipts base. Understanding these incentives is critical for any business planning operations in Washington.

1. High Technology B&O Tax Credit

Washington provides a B&O tax credit for qualifying research and development (R&D) expenditures in specified high-technology categories. The credit is designed to encourage technology-sector investment in Washington and to partially offset the B&O tax burden on R&D-intensive businesses that may generate substantial revenue but reinvest heavily in research.

Qualifying Technology Categories

The credit applies to R&D expenditures in the following categories (RCW 82.04.4452):

  • Advanced computing — hardware, software, networking, and data processing technologies
  • Biotechnology — molecular biology, genetic engineering, bioinformatics, pharmaceutical development
  • Electronic device technology — semiconductors, microelectronics, optoelectronics, display technology
  • Environmental technology — pollution prevention, waste treatment, environmental monitoring, renewable energy technology
  • Advanced materials — composites, ceramics, polymers, nanomaterials, and other materials with novel properties

Credit Calculation

The credit is equal to 1.5% of qualifying R&D expenditures in the eligible categories. Qualifying expenditures generally follow the federal definition of research expenditures under IRC Section 41 (the federal R&D tax credit), adapted for Washington purposes. The credit is taken against B&O tax liability.

Annual Tax Performance Reporting

Businesses claiming the high-tech credit must file an annual tax performance report with DOR. The report includes:

  • Total R&D expenditures by qualifying category
  • Number of employees engaged in qualifying R&D
  • Gross revenue attributable to qualifying activities
  • Certification that the expenditures meet the statutory requirements

Failure to file the annual report can result in loss of the credit and potential claw-back of previously claimed credits.

2. Rural County B&O Credit

Washington provides a B&O tax credit for businesses that create manufacturing or R&D jobs in designated rural counties. The credit is designed to encourage economic development outside the Puget Sound metropolitan area.

Qualifying Counties

A county qualifies as “rural” for purposes of this credit if it meets population density and employment criteria established by statute. Typical qualifying counties include:

  • Eastern Washington counties: Adams, Asotin, Benton, Chelan, Columbia, Douglas, Ferry, Franklin, Garfield, Grant, Kittitas, Klickitat, Lincoln, Okanogan, Pend Oreille, Stevens, Walla Walla, Whitman, Yakima
  • Rural peninsular and coastal counties: Clallam, Grays Harbor, Jefferson, Mason, Pacific, Wahkiakum
  • Other qualifying counties based on population thresholds: Cowlitz, Lewis, Skamania, San Juan

The specific list is updated periodically by DOR based on Census data and statutory criteria. Not all rural counties qualify in every year.

Credit Structure

The credit is a fixed dollar amount per qualified employee hired in a manufacturing or R&D position in a qualifying county. The credit amount has been set at approximately $2,000–$3,000 per employee per year (verify current amount with DOR). The credit is taken against B&O tax liability and cannot exceed the total B&O tax due.

Requirements

  • The employee must work at a facility located in a qualifying rural county
  • The position must be in manufacturing or qualified R&D
  • The position must be a net new position (not a transfer from another location)
  • Annual tax performance reporting is required

3. Investment Income Deduction

Under RCW 82.04.4281, businesses may deduct incidental investment income from their B&O tax base. This deduction recognizes that many businesses earn interest, dividends, and other investment returns on cash reserves that are ancillary to their primary business operations.

The Antio LLC Decision (2024)

In 2024, the Washington Supreme Court decided Antio LLC, a case that narrowly interpreted the investment income deduction. The court held that the deduction was available only for investment income that was truly “incidental” to the taxpayer's primary business — not for businesses whose primary activity was investment management or for investment income that constituted a substantial portion of total revenue.

The Antio decision created uncertainty for businesses with significant investment portfolios. The practical question: at what point does investment income cease to be “incidental”?

2025 HB 2081 Clarification

The 2025 HB 2081 reform package included a legislative clarification of the investment income deduction, partially in response to the Antio decision. The clarification provided more specific criteria for determining when investment income qualifies as “incidental” and is eligible for the deduction. The legislative history suggests the intent was to maintain the deduction for operating businesses with treasury investment programs while limiting it for businesses that are primarily investment vehicles.

4. Aerospace Industry Preferences

Washington has the most extensive aerospace tax preference program of any US state, reflecting the historical dominance of aerospace manufacturing in the state's economy. The preferences include:

B&O Rate Preferences

Aerospace manufacturers have historically received a reduced B&O tax rate on manufacturing activities — lower than the standard manufacturing rate. The specific rate and eligibility criteria have been modified multiple times by the legislature, most recently in connection with the 2025 reforms.

Machinery & Equipment (M&E) Exemptions

Sales tax exemptions for machinery and equipment used directly in the manufacturing process. This includes production equipment, testing equipment, and tooling. The M&E exemption is significant for capital-intensive aerospace manufacturing facilities.

Workforce Training

Tax credits or deductions for aerospace workforce training programs. These incentives are designed to ensure a skilled labor force for aerospace manufacturing and to subsidize the significant training costs associated with aerospace production.

Historical Context

The aerospace preferences trace back to the mid-20th century when Washington's economy was heavily dependent on a single major aerospace employer in the Puget Sound region. The preferences were expanded in subsequent decades as the legislature sought to retain and attract aerospace manufacturing capacity. The preferences have been controversial: critics argue they represent corporate welfare for a profitable industry; supporters argue they are essential to maintaining Washington's competitive position against states aggressively courting aerospace relocation.

Accountability and Reporting

Businesses claiming aerospace preferences must file annual reports documenting employment levels, wages, capital investment, and tax benefits received. The Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee (JLARC) periodically reviews aerospace tax preferences for effectiveness.

5. Semiconductor Manufacturing Preferences

In 2003, the Washington Legislature enacted a tax preference package for semiconductor manufacturing, including:

  • B&O rate reduction for semiconductor fabrication and assembly
  • Sales tax exemptions for equipment used in semiconductor manufacturing (clean-room equipment, lithography tools, wafer processing systems)
  • Workforce development incentives for semiconductor-related training programs

The semiconductor preferences were designed to attract and retain fabrication facilities in Washington. The preferences are subject to periodic review and JLARC audit. Annual tax performance reporting is required.

The 2020s semiconductor supply chain reshoring trend (driven by the federal CHIPS Act and geopolitical concerns) has renewed interest in state-level semiconductor incentives, and Washington's existing preference program positions the state as a potential location for new fabrication investment.

6. QSBS — IRC Section 1202 (Federal)

Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) under IRC Section 1202 is a federal provision, not a Washington State incentive. However, its interaction with Washington's capital gains excise tax makes it one of the most important planning tools for Washington-based founders and investors.

Summary

  • What: 100% exclusion of gain on sale of qualifying C-Corporation stock held for 5+ years, up to $10 million or 10x basis
  • Requirements: C-Corp, under $50M gross assets at issuance, original issuance, active business, 5-year hold
  • Washington interaction: If gain is excluded from federal adjusted capital gain under Section 1202, it arguably does not appear in the starting figure for Washington's capital gains tax computation

For a detailed treatment of QSBS and its Washington implications, see the Capital Gains Excise Tax module, Section 6.

7. Multi-State Apportionment

For businesses operating in multiple states, Washington's B&O tax applies only to Washington-sourced revenue. The apportionment rules determine which revenue is attributed to Washington.

Single-Factor Receipts for Services

Washington uses a single-factor receipts apportionment method for services. Only the revenue attributable to Washington customers is subject to Washington B&O tax. This differs from the traditional three-factor formula (property, payroll, receipts) used by many states for income tax apportionment.

Market-Based Sourcing

Since 2010, Washington has used market-based sourcing for services: revenue is sourced to the state where the customer receives the benefit of the service, not where the service is performed. For a Washington-based service company with customers nationwide:

  • Revenue from Washington customers: subject to Washington B&O tax
  • Revenue from customers in other states: not subject to Washington B&O tax

The practical benefit is significant for technology companies and service firms headquartered in Washington with a national or global customer base. A company with $10 million in total revenue but only $3 million from Washington customers owes B&O tax only on the $3 million.

Goods: Origin-Based vs. Destination-Based

For sales of tangible goods, the sourcing rules differ. Sales tax uses destination-based sourcing (where the goods are delivered). B&O tax for manufacturing and wholesaling uses rules based on where the goods are manufactured and where they are delivered, with credits to avoid double taxation when both manufacturing and selling occur in Washington.

8. Data Center Incentives

Washington offers sales tax exemptions for qualifying data centers, targeting the construction, equipment, and ongoing operations of large-scale computing facilities.

Qualifying Criteria

  • Minimum investment threshold: The data center must meet a minimum capital investment level (typically in the hundreds of millions of dollars)
  • Location: Some incentives are targeted at rural counties to encourage economic development outside the Puget Sound region
  • Job creation: Minimum employment requirements during construction and ongoing operations
  • Power consumption: Qualifying facilities must meet minimum power capacity thresholds

Exemption Scope

  • Server equipment: Sales tax exemption on purchases of servers, storage systems, networking equipment, and related hardware
  • Cooling and power infrastructure: Exemptions for HVAC systems, uninterruptible power supplies, generators, and power distribution equipment
  • Construction materials: In some programs, exemptions for construction materials used in building the data center facility

Competitive Context

Washington's data center incentives compete with programs in Oregon (no sales tax), Nevada, Virginia, Texas, and other states seeking to attract hyperscale data center investment. Washington's natural advantages include abundant and relatively inexpensive hydroelectric power, cool climate (reducing cooling costs), proximity to submarine cable landing points for trans-Pacific connectivity, and an existing technology ecosystem.

9. Small Business B&O Credit

Washington provides a small business B&O tax credit that reduces the effective B&O tax rate for businesses with lower gross revenue. The credit phases in below a specified revenue threshold and phases out as revenue increases.

Structure

  • Businesses with gross income below approximately $125,000 per month (verify current threshold with DOR) receive a credit that offsets some or all of the B&O tax due.
  • The credit is calculated automatically on the Combined Excise Tax Return.
  • Above the phase-out threshold, the credit reduces to zero and the full B&O rate applies.

The small business credit effectively creates a de minimis exemption for very small businesses, reducing the compliance burden and tax liability for sole proprietors, micro-businesses, and early-stage companies.
