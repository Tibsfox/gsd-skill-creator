Washington's tax compliance system is built on the Combined Excise Tax Return — a single form that captures B&O tax, retail sales tax, and use tax in one filing. Learn the form, learn the frequencies, learn the penalties. Then file on time, every time.

1. The My DOR Portal

The Washington Department of Revenue (DOR) operates the My DOR online portal as the primary interface for tax administration. My DOR handles:

  • Business registration — new business applications and UBI number issuance
  • Tax return filing — electronic submission of the Combined Excise Tax Return
  • Payment processing — ACH debit, credit card, and electronic check payments
  • Account management — address changes, contact updates, filing frequency changes
  • Reseller permit management — application, renewal, and verification
  • Rate lookup — sales tax rate determination by address (destination-based sourcing)
  • Correspondence — DOR notices, audit communications, and appeal filings

Paper filing is still available but is discouraged. The DOR has steadily migrated toward electronic-only communication for most interactions. Businesses filing electronically receive a confirmation number that serves as proof of timely filing.

2. Registration

The UBI Number

Every business operating in Washington must obtain a Unified Business Identifier (UBI) number. The UBI is assigned by the Washington Secretary of State as part of business registration and is used by multiple state agencies (DOR, Employment Security, Labor & Industries) as the common business identifier.

Washington Business License

A Washington State Business License is required for most business activities. The license is issued through the Business Licensing Service (BLS), which is integrated with the Secretary of State's registration process. Many cities have their own business license requirements in addition to the state license.

My DOR Account Creation

After obtaining a UBI number and business license, the business creates a My DOR account to manage tax filings. The account creation process involves:

  1. Registering at the DOR website with the UBI number
  2. Selecting applicable tax types (B&O, sales tax, use tax, etc.)
  3. Providing business activity details for classification assignment
  4. Receiving a filing frequency assignment based on estimated revenue

New businesses can complete registration, licensing, and DOR account setup in a single online session through the state's integrated business portal.

3. The Combined Excise Tax Return

The Combined Excise Tax Return (CETR) is the single form used to report and pay Washington's three primary business taxes:

  1. Business & Occupation (B&O) Tax — reported by classification (retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, service, etc.) with the applicable rate for each classification
  2. Retail Sales Tax — state and local portions, reported by location code for destination-based sourcing
  3. Use Tax — on purchases made without paying Washington sales tax

Line Items

The CETR includes line items for:

  • Gross income by B&O classification
  • Deductions and exemptions (with supporting schedules)
  • Taxable income by classification
  • B&O tax due by classification
  • Small business B&O tax credit (if applicable)
  • Gross retail sales
  • Exempt sales (reseller, government, nonprofit, other)
  • Taxable retail sales by location code
  • Sales tax collected (state + local)
  • Use tax on business purchases
  • Credits and adjustments
  • Total tax due
  • Penalties and interest (if late)

The CETR is filed electronically through My DOR. The form auto-populates certain fields based on prior filings and the business's registered classifications.

4. Filing Frequencies

DOR assigns a filing frequency based on the business's prior-year tax liability:

Filing frequencyTypical thresholdReturns per year
MonthlyTax liability > $4,800/month (roughly > $50K/month in revenue)12
QuarterlyTax liability $1,050–$4,800/month (roughly $10K–$50K/month)4
AnnualTax liability < $1,050/month (roughly < $10K/month)1

DOR reviews and may adjust filing frequency annually based on actual prior-year activity. Businesses that grow significantly may be moved from annual to quarterly or quarterly to monthly. Businesses that shrink may be moved down.

Businesses may request a more frequent filing schedule than assigned (for example, a quarterly filer may request monthly filing to smooth cash flow), but may not request a less frequent schedule than assigned.

5. Due Dates

AnnualCalendar yearApril 15 of the following year

If a due date falls on a weekend or state holiday, the return is due the next business day. Extensions may be available in limited circumstances but are not automatic — unlike federal income tax extensions, Washington does not routinely grant excise tax filing extensions.

6. Penalty Schedule

Washington's penalty structure is aggressive by state tax standards. Late payment penalties escalate rapidly, and the maximum penalty for willful non-compliance is among the highest in the country.
ViolationPenalty rateNotes
Late payment, 1–30 days9%Applies to the tax amount due
Late payment, 31–60 days19%Replaces the 9% penalty (not cumulative)
Late payment, 61+ days29%Replaces the 19% penalty
Late filing (return not submitted)5%Per month, up to a maximum; separate from late payment
Substantial underpayment5%If underpayment exceeds threshold, additional accuracy penalty
Willful non-payment / fraudUp to 39%Maximum penalty for intentional evasion or fraud
Interest on underpaymentsVariableSet quarterly by DOR based on federal short-term rate + statutory margin. Compounds on unpaid tax.

Penalties are calculated on the tax amount due, not on the total return. A business that files on time but underpays faces the late payment penalty on the underpaid portion. A business that files late and underpays faces both the late filing penalty and the late payment penalty.

Penalty Mitigation

DOR has authority to waive or reduce penalties in certain circumstances:

  • First-time penalty waiver — available for businesses with a clean compliance history
  • Reasonable cause — natural disaster, serious illness, DOR system outage, or other circumstances beyond the taxpayer's control
  • Voluntary disclosure — reduced penalties for businesses that self-report prior non-compliance (see Section 9 below)

7. Audit Procedures

Statute of Limitations

DOR has a four-year statute of limitations to assess additional tax. The four-year period runs from the later of: the due date of the return, or the date the return was actually filed. For fraud or willful evasion, there is no statute of limitations.

Field Audits vs. Desk Audits

  • Field audit: A DOR auditor visits the business location (or, increasingly, conducts a remote review of records). Field audits typically cover multiple tax periods and examine books, records, invoices, contracts, bank statements, and supporting documentation. Duration: weeks to months, depending on complexity.
  • Desk audit: DOR reviews the filed return and supporting data without visiting the business. Desk audits are faster and less invasive but may be followed by a field audit if discrepancies are found.

Common Audit Triggers

  • Significant changes in reported revenue between periods
  • High ratio of exempt sales to total sales (reseller permit abuse screening)
  • Industry-specific audit programs (DOR periodically targets specific industries)
  • Information from other agencies (IRS data sharing, Employment Security payroll data)
  • Third-party tips or complaints
  • Inconsistencies between B&O classifications and reported activity
  • Random selection (a percentage of audits are random)

Audit Rights

During an audit, the taxpayer has the right to:

  • Representation by a CPA, tax attorney, or other authorized representative
  • Access to audit workpapers and the auditor's preliminary findings
  • A closing conference to discuss findings before final assessment
  • Appeal of the assessment (see Section 8 below)

8. Appeals

Washington's tax appeal process follows a three-stage structure:

Stage 1: DOR Administrative Review

The taxpayer files a petition for review with DOR's Appeals Division within 30 days of receiving the assessment. The Appeals Division conducts an independent review of the assessment — the reviewer is separate from the auditor who made the original determination. The review may result in full abatement, partial abatement, or denial.

Stage 2: Board of Tax Appeals (BTA)

If the DOR review is unsatisfactory, the taxpayer may appeal to the Board of Tax Appeals — an independent quasi-judicial body. The BTA conducts a formal hearing with testimony, exhibits, and legal argument. The BTA's decision is a formal order that can be appealed to court.

Stage 3: Superior Court

Either party (the taxpayer or DOR) may appeal the BTA's decision to Superior Court in the county where the taxpayer resides or does business. The Superior Court reviews the BTA's decision under the Administrative Procedure Act, typically deferring to factual findings but reviewing legal conclusions de novo.

From Superior Court, further appeal lies to the Washington Court of Appeals and ultimately the Washington Supreme Court through the standard appellate process.

Practical Notes

  • Most disputes are resolved at the DOR administrative review stage.
  • BTA proceedings can take months to over a year.
  • The taxpayer must generally pay the assessed tax before appealing (pay-to-play rule), though payment under protest preserves the right to a refund if the appeal succeeds.
  • Interest continues to accrue during the appeal process.

9. Voluntary Disclosure

DOR operates a Voluntary Disclosure Agreement (VDA) program for businesses that discover they have been operating in Washington without proper registration, filing, or payment. The VDA process:

  1. The business (typically through a representative) contacts DOR to initiate voluntary disclosure, often through a multistate voluntary disclosure clearinghouse.
  2. DOR agrees to limit the lookback period (typically to four years, matching the statute of limitations) rather than assessing for all prior years of activity.
  3. The business files back returns, pays the tax due plus interest, and registers for ongoing compliance.
  4. In exchange, DOR waives penalties (but not interest). This is the primary incentive: voluntary disclosure avoids the 9%–29% late payment penalties that would otherwise apply.

VDAs are particularly relevant for out-of-state businesses that discover they have economic nexus in Washington (post-Wayfair) and need to come into compliance without triggering the full penalty structure.
