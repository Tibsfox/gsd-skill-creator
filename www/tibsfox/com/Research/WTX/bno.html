Business & Occupation Tax

Washington's gross receipts tax — applied to the flow of revenue, not the accumulation of profit

Most states tax what a business keeps. Washington taxes what a business takes in. The Business & Occupation tax is a gross receipts tax — applied to total revenue before any deductions for costs, wages, rent, materials, or other expenses. This fundamental design choice, made in 1935, defines the character of Washington's entire business tax system.

The Gross Receipts Concept

The B&O tax is levied on the gross income of a business — the total amount received from all activities, without any deduction for the cost of goods sold, operating expenses, compensation, rent, interest, taxes paid, or any other cost of doing business. The tax base is revenue, not profit.

This is the defining structural distinction. In an income tax system, a business earning $10 million in revenue with $9 million in costs pays tax on $1 million. In Washington's B&O system, the same business pays tax on the full $10 million. The tax is indifferent to whether the business is profitable, breaking even, or losing money.

The fundamental insight: Washington taxes the flow, not the accumulation. A dollar of revenue is taxed the moment it enters the business, regardless of what happens to it afterward. This makes the B&O structurally different from every income tax, franchise tax, and margins tax in use elsewhere in the United States.

The consequence is that the B&O's effective burden varies enormously by business model. A high-margin software company and a low-margin grocery distributor may pay identical B&O on identical revenue, but the tax consumes a radically different share of their actual earnings. This asymmetry is the central structural feature — and the central structural criticism — of the gross receipts model.

Historical Origins — The Revenue Act of 1935

The B&O tax was enacted as part of the Revenue Act of 1935, a comprehensive fiscal reform driven by two converging forces: the Great Depression's collapse of property tax revenue (the state's primary funding mechanism) and the Washington Supreme Court's 1933 decision in Culliton v. Chase, which held that a graduated net income tax violated the state constitution's uniformity clause.

Culliton foreclosed the option most states used to diversify revenue. Unable to tax net income, the legislature turned to alternatives that could survive constitutional scrutiny. The B&O tax — a flat-rate tax on gross receipts, applied uniformly within each classification — satisfied the uniformity requirement because it did not graduate rates based on the amount of income.

The Revenue Act of 1935 also created the retail sales tax, and together these two taxes replaced the state's dependence on property taxation. Ninety-one years later, the B&O and the retail sales tax remain the two pillars of Washington's revenue system. The income tax remains constitutionally foreclosed, though the 2021 capital gains excise tax (upheld in Quinn v. State, 2023) opened a narrow additional channel.

Why "Business and Occupation"?

The name reflects the original statutory language: the tax is imposed on the privilege of engaging in business activities or occupations within Washington State. It is not a property tax, not an income tax, and not a sales tax. It is an excise tax on the act of doing business, measured by gross receipts.

The Classification System

The B&O tax is activity-based, not entity-based. The rate a business pays depends on what it does, not what it is. A single business can have multiple B&O classifications simultaneously if it engages in multiple types of activity.

Consider a technology company that sells software licenses (Retailing), provides consulting services (Service & Other Activities), and manufactures hardware components (Manufacturing). Each revenue stream is classified separately. The company files a single Combined Excise Tax Return but reports gross income under each applicable classification at the rate for that classification.

Example: Multiple Classifications

A Washington C-Corporation earns:

  • $2,000,000 from consulting services → Service & Other Activities
  • $500,000 from product sales to end users → Retailing
  • $300,000 from wholesale distribution → Wholesaling

Each stream is taxed at its own classification rate. The entity type (C-Corp, LLC, sole proprietor) is irrelevant to the rate determination.

The major B&O classifications include: Retailing, Wholesaling, Manufacturing, Service & Other Activities, Extracting, Printing and Publishing, and several specialized categories (e.g., Financial Institutions, Public Utilities, Advanced Computing). The Service & Other Activities classification is the catch-all — any business activity not specifically assigned to another classification defaults to Service.

Rate Table — 2026, Post-HB 2081

The following rates reflect the B&O structure as of January 1, 2026, incorporating the changes enacted by HB 2081 (signed May 20, 2025). The Service & Other Activities classification now uses a three-tier structure based on prior-year gross income.

Classification Prior-Year Gross Income Rate Notes
Service & Other Activities Under $1,000,000 1.5% Unchanged from prior law
Service & Other Activities $1,000,000 – $4,999,999 1.75% New tier, effective Oct 1, 2025

The Advanced Computing surcharge applies to a defined set of large technology companies meeting specific revenue and activity thresholds. The Financial Institutions surcharge applies to banks, savings institutions, and other entities classified under RCW 82.04.29004. Both surcharges are additive — they stack on top of the base classification rate.

The 2025 HB 2081 Reform

House Bill 2081 was signed by Governor Bob Ferguson on May 20, 2025, as part of the three-bill tax reform package (alongside SB 5814 expanding the sales tax base and SB 5813 adding a capital gains surcharge). HB 2081 restructured the B&O tax by introducing tiered rates for the Service & Other Activities classification and adjusting surcharges and base rates for several other classifications.

Implementation Timeline

Effective Date Change
October 1, 2025 Service $5M+ tier increases to 2.1%; Advanced Computing surcharge increased; three-tier Service structure takes effect
January 1, 2026 $250M gross income surcharge (0.5%) takes effect for the largest businesses
January 1, 2027 Manufacturing and Wholesaling base rates rise from 0.484% to 0.5%

The phased timeline was designed to give businesses time to adjust. The Service tier changes hit first (October 2025), the large-business surcharge follows three months later (January 2026), and the goods-sector rate increases arrive a full year after that (January 2027).

How the Tiers Work

The three-tier Service & Other Activities structure is determined by prior-year gross income. The prior calendar year's total gross income from all B&O-taxable activities determines which tier applies for the entire following calendar year.

Critical: This Is Not Marginal

The tier system is not marginal. It does not work like federal income tax brackets, where only the income above the threshold is taxed at the higher rate. Instead, crossing a threshold changes the rate on the entire amount.

A company with $999,999 in prior-year gross income pays 1.5% on all Service income in the current year. A company with $1,000,001 in prior-year gross income pays 1.75% on all Service income in the current year — not just the $2 above the threshold.

This creates a cliff effect at each threshold boundary. A business approaching $1M or $5M in annual gross income faces a discontinuous jump in its effective tax rate for the following year.

The tier is locked for the entire calendar year once the prior-year determination is made. A company that earned $5.2M in 2025 pays the 2.1% rate on all Service income throughout 2026, regardless of whether its 2026 revenue drops below $5M. Conversely, a company that earned $800K in 2025 pays the 1.5% rate throughout 2026, even if its 2026 revenue surges past $5M. The adjustment catches up in the following year.

For new businesses without a prior calendar year of operations, the DOR assigns a tier based on estimated activity, with reconciliation after the first full year of operations.

The Pyramiding Problem

The structural concern with any gross receipts tax is pyramiding — the compounding of tax as a product or service passes through multiple stages of the production chain. Because the B&O tax is applied to the full sale price at each stage, the effective tax rate on the final consumer price is higher than the nominal B&O rate.

Example: Four-Stage Production Chain

Stage Sale Price B&O Classification B&O Paid
Raw material extractor $100 Extracting (0.484%) $0.48
Manufacturer $250 Manufacturing (0.484%) $1.21
Wholesaler $400 Wholesaling (0.484%) $1.94
Retailer $600 Retailing (0.471%) $2.83
Total B&O embedded in the $600 product $6.46

The effective B&O rate on the final $600 product is approximately 1.08% — more than double the nominal Retailing rate of 0.471%. Each stage in the chain added its own layer of B&O, and none of those layers was deductible by the next stage.

Compare this with a Value Added Tax (VAT), which taxes only the value added at each stage. In a VAT system, the manufacturer deducts the tax paid by the extractor, the wholesaler deducts the tax paid by the manufacturer, and so on. The total tax burden equals the VAT rate applied to the final retail price, regardless of how many production stages are involved. A VAT does not pyramid.

Compare also with a net income tax, which taxes only profit at each stage. A net income tax also avoids pyramiding because costs (including the purchase price from the prior stage) are deductible.

The B&O tax does neither. It taxes gross receipts at every stage, with no credit or deduction for prior-stage taxes. This is the fundamental structural critique. Industries with long production chains bear a disproportionately high effective tax rate compared to industries that sell directly to the end consumer.

Effective Tax Rate by Business Margin

Because the B&O tax is applied to gross receipts rather than profit, the effective income-equivalent tax rate — the B&O expressed as a percentage of actual profit — varies dramatically depending on the business's profit margin. A 1.5% gross receipts tax translates to very different income-equivalent burdens at different margins.

At the 1.5% Service Rate (Under $1M Tier)

5% $500,000 $25,000 $7,500 30.0%
2% $500,000 $10,000 $7,500 75.0%
1% $500,000 $5,000 $7,500 150.0%

Profit Margin Revenue Profit B&O Paid (2.1%) Effective Income-Equivalent Rate
50% $10,000,000 $5,000,000 $210,000 4.2%
25% $10,000,000 $2,500,000 $210,000 8.4%
10% $10,000,000 $1,000,000 $210,000 21.0%

2% $10,000,000 $200,000 $210,000 105.0%

At a 2% profit margin, the 2.1% B&O rate produces an effective income-equivalent rate of 105% — the B&O tax alone exceeds the business's entire profit. At a 1% margin, it is 210%. This is not a theoretical concern. Grocery distribution, commodity wholesaling, and construction subcontracting routinely operate at single-digit margins. For these industries, the B&O tax is the dominant tax burden, exceeding what they would pay under any plausible income tax regime.

Nexus Rules

A business must register and pay B&O tax if it has sufficient connection — nexus — with Washington State. Nexus can be established through either physical presence or economic activity.

Physical Nexus

Physical nexus exists when a business has:

  • Employees working in Washington (including remote employees)
  • An office, warehouse, or other place of business in the state
  • Inventory stored in Washington (including goods held by a third-party fulfillment center)
  • Agents or representatives conducting business on the company's behalf in Washington
  • Real or tangible personal property in the state

Economic Nexus

Following the U.S. Supreme Court's decision in South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc., 138 S. Ct. 2080 (2018), Washington implemented an economic nexus standard for B&O tax. A business has economic nexus if it exceeds:

  • $100,000 in annual gross receipts from Washington-sourced activities

Once the threshold is crossed, the out-of-state business must register with the Washington Department of Revenue, file the Combined Excise Tax Return, and pay B&O tax on its Washington-sourced income. The threshold is evaluated on a calendar-year basis.

Filing Frequency

The DOR assigns a filing frequency to each registered business based on prior-year tax liability. The assignment determines how often the business must file the Combined Excise Tax Return.

Average Monthly Tax Liability Filing Frequency Due Date
Over $4,800/month (over ~$57,600/year) Monthly 25th of the following month
$1,050 – $4,800/month Quarterly Last day of the month following the quarter end
Under $1,050/month Annual April 15 of the following year

The DOR periodically reassesses filing frequency. A business that grows past the quarterly threshold will be moved to monthly filing. New businesses are typically assigned quarterly filing and adjusted after the first full year of operation.

Late filing incurs penalties: 5% of the tax due if filed by the end of the month following the due date, 15% if filed later, plus interest on the unpaid balance. Repeated late filing can result in the DOR revoking the business's tax registration certificate.

The Small Business B&O Credit

Washington provides a graduated B&O tax credit for small businesses to reduce the tax burden on the smallest enterprises. The credit is calculated based on annual taxable gross income (after any deductions but before the credit itself).

Credit Structure

As of 2026, the Small Business B&O Credit provides:

  • Full credit (100% of tax owed) for businesses with annual taxable gross income under approximately $56,000
  • Graduated phase-out between ~$56,000 and ~$115,000, reducing the credit incrementally
  • No credit for businesses with annual taxable gross income above the phase-out ceiling

The exact thresholds are adjusted periodically by the DOR. The credit applies per reporting period (monthly, quarterly, or annually) with the thresholds prorated accordingly.

The practical effect is that a newly formed business with minimal initial revenue — a sole proprietor in the first year of operations, a startup in pre-revenue or early-revenue phase — may owe zero B&O tax until its gross income exceeds the credit threshold. This provides meaningful relief for micro-businesses and early-stage companies, though the credit becomes irrelevant quickly as revenue grows.

The credit is applied on the Combined Excise Tax Return. The business must still register, file returns, and report its gross income — the credit simply reduces the amount owed to zero or near-zero for qualifying periods.

The Investment Income Deduction

Under RCW 82.04.4281, a business may deduct investment income that is not derived from the business's primary activity. The deduction applies to interest, dividends, and capital gains from investments that are incidental to the main business operations.

The logic is straightforward: if a manufacturing company earns interest on its cash reserves or dividends from an investment portfolio, that passive income should not be taxed as Manufacturing or Service income under the B&O. The deduction removes incidental investment income from the B&O base.

The Antio LLC Decision (2024)

The scope of the investment income deduction was litigated in Antio LLC v. Department of Revenue, decided by the Washington Supreme Court in 2024. The case addressed the question of when investment income is "incidental" to the main business versus when the investment activity itself constitutes a separate business activity subject to B&O.

The court's ruling narrowed the application of the deduction in certain circumstances, particularly for entities whose investment activities were substantial relative to their other operations. The decision created uncertainty about the boundary between incidental investment income (deductible) and investment-as-business-activity (taxable).

HB 2081's Clarifying Amendment (2025)

HB 2081 included a clarifying amendment to RCW 82.04.4281 intended to address the ambiguity created by the Antio decision. The amendment refined the definition of incidental investment income and provided clearer criteria for distinguishing deductible investment income from taxable business activity. Businesses relying on the investment income deduction should review the amended statute and any subsequent DOR guidance to confirm eligibility.

Apportionment for Multi-State Businesses

Businesses operating in multiple states do not owe Washington B&O tax on their entire worldwide revenue. Only income sourced to Washington is subject to the tax. The method for determining how much income is sourced to Washington depends on the B&O classification.

Service & Other Activities: Single-Factor Receipts Apportionment

For Service businesses, Washington uses single-factor receipts apportionment with market-based sourcing (adopted post-2010). This means:

  • The apportionment factor is based solely on the ratio of Washington receipts to total receipts (single-factor)
  • Receipts are sourced to the location of the customer or the market for the service, not the location of the service provider (market-based)
  • A consulting firm headquartered in Washington with 60% of its clients in Washington and 40% out-of-state pays B&O on approximately 60% of its total Service revenue

Example: Multi-State Service Business

A technology company headquartered in Washington earns $8,000,000 in annual Service revenue. Its customer base is:

  • 50% Washington customers: $4,000,000
  • 30% California customers: $2,400,000
  • 20% other states: $1,600,000

Under market-based sourcing, only the $4,000,000 in Washington-sourced revenue is subject to Washington B&O tax. The California and other-state revenue is not taxable in Washington (though it may be taxable in those other states under their own tax regimes).

Retailing and Wholesaling: Destination Sourcing

For Retailing and Wholesaling, income is sourced to Washington when the goods are delivered to a Washington location. This is consistent with the destination-based sourcing rules used for the retail sales tax.

Manufacturing: Washington Situs

Manufacturing B&O is generally owed on goods manufactured in Washington, regardless of where they are sold. This creates a potential for double taxation (B&O on manufacturing and B&O on the subsequent sale), which the Multiple Activities Tax Credit (MATC) is designed to mitigate.

Comparative Notes — Other Gross Receipts Taxes

Washington's B&O is not the only gross receipts tax (GRT) in the United States, but it is among the oldest and — post-HB 2081 — among the highest-rate for service businesses. Several other states use GRT structures:

State Tax Name Rate Notable Features
Ohio Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) 0.26% Flat rate, broad base, $1M exemption threshold
Oregon Corporate Activity Tax (CAT) 0.57% above $1M Enacted 2019; allows 35% subtraction for cost of goods sold or labor
Hawaii General Excise Tax (GET) 4.0% retail; 0.5% wholesale Broadest base in the U.S.; pyramiding permitted; functionally a GRT
Nevada Commerce Tax 0.051% – 0.331% Industry-variable rates; $4M exemption threshold
Texas Franchise Tax (Margin Tax) 0.375% – 0.75% Modified GRT; allows deductions for cost of goods sold OR compensation

Washington's Service rate — 1.5% to 2.1% post-HB 2081 — is the highest pure GRT rate applied to service businesses among all U.S. states. Ohio's CAT is one-sixth the rate. Oregon's CAT is roughly one-third, and Oregon allows a cost subtraction that Washington does not. Only Hawaii's GET approaches Washington's effective burden, and Hawaii's tax applies to a much broader base (including categories Washington exempts).

The comparison highlights Washington's unusual position: a high-rate gross receipts tax with no income tax alternative. Most other GRT states either have low rates (Ohio, Nevada) or also levy an income tax. Washington stands alone in combining high GRT rates with zero income tax.

The Revenue Stability Trade-Off

The structural advantage of a gross receipts tax is revenue stability. Because the tax is applied to total revenue rather than profit, the tax base does not collapse during economic downturns the way an income tax base does.

In a recession, businesses may see profits fall to zero or go negative, but revenue typically declines less sharply. A company that earned $10M and made $1M in profit during a boom year might earn $7M and lose $500K during a recession. An income tax state collects nothing from that company in the downturn (and may even issue a refund from the loss carryback). Washington still collects B&O on the $7M in revenue.

This was the original 1935 design rationale. The Great Depression demonstrated that property taxes and income taxes were pro-cyclical — they amplified fiscal crises by collapsing revenue exactly when government spending needs were greatest. The B&O was explicitly chosen as a counter-cyclical alternative: a tax that would produce relatively stable revenue regardless of the business cycle.

The empirical evidence supports the design. Washington's general fund revenue has historically been more stable year-over-year than that of income-tax-dependent states. During the 2008–2009 Great Recession, Washington's B&O collections declined, but less dramatically than income tax collections in states like California and New York. The 2020 pandemic recession showed a similar pattern — B&O revenue dipped but recovered rapidly as commerce resumed, even while many businesses remained unprofitable.

The trade-off is equity. Revenue stability comes at the cost of taxing businesses regardless of their ability to pay. A business losing money still owes B&O. A low-margin business pays the same B&O rate as a high-margin competitor on identical revenue. The stability benefit accrues to the state treasury; the equity cost falls on businesses that are struggling.

Additional Deductions and Credits

The Multiple Activities Tax Credit (MATC)

When a single product or service is subject to B&O under more than one classification — for example, a product that is both manufactured and sold at retail in Washington — the MATC provides a credit to prevent double taxation. The credit is equal to the lower of the two tax amounts, effectively ensuring the business pays B&O at the higher rate only once rather than paying both rates on the same revenue.

Interstate Sales Deduction

Revenue from sales delivered outside Washington is generally deductible from Retailing and Wholesaling B&O under the interstate commerce deduction. This prevents Washington from taxing sales that are properly sourced to other states.

Specific Industry Deductions

Washington provides targeted B&O deductions for specific activities, including:

  • International services (RCW 82.04.4286)
  • Amounts received from the federal government for public health services
  • Certain agricultural products
  • Newspaper publishing revenue (partial deduction)
  • Royalty income from intellectual property developed in Washington (under certain conditions)

Each deduction has specific qualification criteria defined in statute. Businesses claiming deductions should confirm eligibility with DOR guidance or professional tax counsel.
