Washington is simultaneously one of nine no-income-tax states and the leading example of a gross receipts tax state. This combination is rare, and it produces a tax burden profile that is meaningfully different from both its neighbors and its fellow no-income-tax states.

Washington is unique among these states in three ways: (1) it is the only one with a comprehensive gross receipts tax (B&O); (2) it is the only one with a stand-alone capital gains tax; and (3) it has one of the highest combined sales tax rates (pushed up by local add-ons in the Puget Sound region).

The Gross Receipts Tax Family

Gross receipts taxes (GRTs) are a small but growing category of state business taxes. They impose tax on total revenue before any deductions for costs, wages, or other business expenses. Washington's B&O is the oldest and most comprehensive.

Why Gross Receipts Taxes Are Different

A net income tax is imposed on profit — revenue minus costs. A gross receipts tax is imposed on revenue, full stop. For a business with a 50% net margin, a 1.5% GRT is effectively 3% of net income. For a business with a 5% net margin (say, a grocery store or a staffing agency), the same 1.5% GRT is effectively 30% of net income. This is the structural regressivity of gross receipts taxation with respect to business profit margins.

Gross receipts taxes also exhibit pyramiding: when a product moves through multiple stages of production (manufacturer → wholesaler → retailer → consumer), GRT is paid at each stage on the full sale price, compounding the effective tax. A net income tax does not pyramid because each stage only pays on its own margin.

The trade-off is simplicity and revenue stability. GRTs are harder to avoid than income taxes (no NOL games, no aggressive deductions), and their revenue is more stable across business cycles because it does not depend on whether businesses are profitable.

The Regressivity Rankings

The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) publishes “Who Pays? A Distributional Analysis of the Tax Systems in All 50 States.” Over multiple editions (2013, 2015, 2018, 2024), ITEP has consistently rated Washington as having one of the most regressive state and local tax systems in the United States — typically in the bottom five. The 2024 edition ranked Washington as the 2nd most regressive.

The drivers of this ranking:

  • No income tax eliminates the primary progressive element of most state tax systems
  • Heavy reliance on sales tax — regressive because lower-income households spend a larger share of income on taxable goods and services
  • Property tax limitation — while Initiative 747's 1% levy growth cap moderates property tax growth, the remaining property tax burden falls more heavily on middle-class homeowners
  • The B&O tax is passed through to consumers in many sectors, further concentrating burden on low- and middle-income households

The 2025 Ferguson reforms (HB 2081, SB 5813, SB 5814) were explicitly framed in part as a response to this regressivity critique. The three-tier Service B&O rate shifts burden toward larger businesses. The capital gains 2.9% surcharge above $1M is highly targeted at the top of the income distribution. The sales tax expansion to services is broader-based but also hits higher-income consumption categories (advertising, IT services, professional services) more than low-income consumption.

Whether the 2025 reforms materially change Washington's regressivity ranking will not be known until ITEP's next edition, but the direction of change is clear.

The Hawaii GET — Washington's Oldest Sibling

Hawaii's General Excise Tax and Washington's B&O Tax were enacted in the same year — 1935. Both were products of the same Depression-era fiscal reform impulse. But they developed differently:

  • Hawaii's GET is economically equivalent to a broad sales tax that is legally imposed on the seller but universally passed through to consumers (visibly, as a line item on receipts). The rate is low (4% state, 4.5% with Oahu surcharge) but the base is extraordinarily broad — it taxes essentially all business activity including services, rents, professional fees, and construction.
  • Washington's B&O is legally imposed on the seller and is not conventionally passed through as a line item on invoices (though the economic incidence is similar). Rates are higher (1.5%–2.1% for services) but the base has historically been narrower — carving out professional services, education, and other categories that are taxed in Hawaii. The 2025 SB 5814 expansion partially closed this gap by pulling IT services, custom software, advertising, and live presentations into the sales tax base.

The two systems are two siblings of the same 1935 fiscal reform era, diverging in emphasis but sharing the same DNA. Washington studied Hawaii's GET closely in the 1935 drafting process; the B&O is in part a response to the GET's design choices.
