# FCC Frequency Reallocation & the Digital Transition

> **Domain:** Telecommunications Policy, Spectrum Management, Broadcast Engineering
> **Module:** 4 -- FCC Frequency Reallocation, the Digital Transition, and the Regulatory Archaeology of Dead Spectrum
> **Through-line:** *The Federal Communications Commission has been rearranging the electromagnetic spectrum since 1934, moving services between frequencies like a city planner rezoning neighborhoods. Every reallocation creates winners and losers. The losers are the services -- and the listeners -- whose frequencies are taken away. The AM band is the oldest neighborhood in the spectrum, and it is being rezoned out of existence not by a single regulatory action but by the cumulative weight of every decision that made other services more attractive.*

## 1. The Spectrum as Managed Resource

The electromagnetic spectrum is a finite natural resource managed by the federal government through the Federal Communications Commission (established by the Communications Act of 1934) for non-government use, and by the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA) for government use. The spectrum is divided into bands, each allocated to specific services: broadcasting, mobile telecommunications, satellite, radar, amateur radio, public safety, and others [1].

Allocation decisions are made through Administrative Procedure Act rulemaking: the FCC proposes a change, accepts public comment, and issues a Report and Order. These proceedings can take years or decades. The record of each proceeding constitutes a primary source document for understanding why a particular frequency band was reassigned and what interests prevailed [2].

### The Allocation Table

The FCC's Table of Frequency Allocations (47 CFR 2.106) is the master document governing all spectrum use in the United States. Each entry specifies:

- Frequency range
- Allocated service(s) -- primary and secondary
- Footnotes specifying conditions, geographic restrictions, and cross-references to international allocations

The table is continuously amended. Every amendment represents a policy decision about which services deserve spectrum and which do not. The cumulative history of amendments to the broadcast allocations tells the story of broadcasting's declining priority in the national spectrum plan [3].

```
BROADCAST SPECTRUM ALLOCATIONS -- UNITED STATES (2025)
================================================================

  BAND         RANGE            SERVICE        STATUS
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
  AM           530-1700 kHz     Broadcasting   Active (declining)
  FM           88-108 MHz       Broadcasting   Active (stable)
  TV VHF Lo    54-72 MHz        Broadcasting   Repacked (DTV)
  TV VHF Hi    174-216 MHz      Broadcasting   Repacked (DTV)
  TV UHF       470-608 MHz      Broadcasting   Repacked (DTV)
  TV UHF       614-698 MHz      Mobile (5G)    REALLOCATED 2017
  TV UHF       698-806 MHz      Mobile (LTE)   REALLOCATED 2008
  TV UHF       806-890 MHz      Cellular/PCS   REALLOCATED 1983
```

## 2. The 1945 FM Band Relocation

The most consequential single frequency reallocation in American broadcast history occurred on June 27, 1945, when the FCC moved the FM broadcast band from 42-50 MHz to 88-108 MHz. This decision, contained in Docket 6651, made every existing FM receiver in the United States obsolete and forced every existing FM station to rebuild for the new band [4].

### The Technical Argument

The FCC's stated justification was that the 42-50 MHz band was subject to Sporadic E ionospheric propagation -- unpredictable reflections off the E-layer of the ionosphere that could carry signals thousands of miles, causing interference between distant stations. The 88-108 MHz band, being higher in frequency, was less susceptible to this phenomenon. FCC Chief Engineer George Sterling testified that Sporadic E interference made the lower band unsuitable for reliable broadcasting [5].

### The Political Argument

Edwin Howard Armstrong, the inventor of FM radio, argued strenuously against the move. Armstrong contended that Sporadic E was a manageable problem that affected a small percentage of broadcast hours, and that the real reason for the move was to protect RCA's television interests. Television Channel 1 (originally 44-50 MHz) overlapped with the FM band. By moving FM up, the FCC freed the lower band for television -- which RCA, under David Sarnoff, was aggressively promoting [6].

The historical consensus, supported by Lawrence Lessing's biography of Armstrong (*Man of High Fidelity*, 1956) and subsequent scholarship, is that both arguments had merit but the commercial pressure from RCA was the decisive factor. Armstrong's FM stations on the 42-50 MHz band had demonstrated excellent performance. The Sporadic E problem was real but not disabling. The reallocation served television's commercial interests at FM's expense [7].

### The Aftermath

The reallocation destroyed the first generation of FM broadcasting. Stations that had invested in 42-50 MHz transmitters and built audiences on those frequencies were forced to start over. Many did not. The number of FM stations on the air dropped from 55 in 1945 to 48 by 1949, despite the postwar radio boom. FM would not recover to its pre-reallocation momentum until the mid-1960s [8].

Armstrong, financially and emotionally devastated by the reallocation and subsequent patent litigation with RCA, committed suicide on January 31, 1954, by jumping from his 13th-floor apartment in New York City. His widow, Marion Armstrong, pursued and eventually won the patent cases, but the human cost of the 1945 reallocation was irreversible [9].

## 3. The UHF TV Reallocation: Channels 70-83

The FCC's original television allocation plan included UHF channels 14-83, spanning 470-890 MHz. Channels 70-83 (806-890 MHz) were reallocated from television to land mobile radio and cellular telephone service through a series of proceedings beginning in 1970 and culminating in the 1983 Cellular Service Report and Order (Docket 79-318) [10].

### What Was Lost

Channels 70-83 had been available for television broadcasting since 1952 but were sparsely used. The UHF television landscape was dominated by lower UHF channels (14-50), and few stations had been licensed on channels 70-83. The reallocation affected a small number of existing licensees who were offered alternative channel assignments [11].

### What Was Gained

The 806-890 MHz band became the foundation of the American cellular telephone industry. The first commercial cellular call was made on this spectrum on October 13, 1983, in Chicago. The economic value generated by cellular service on this spectrum has been estimated in the hundreds of billions of dollars -- making the TV-to-cellular reallocation one of the most economically productive regulatory decisions in FCC history [12].

## 4. The Digital Television Transition

The digital television transition (DTV transition) was a multi-decade process culminating on June 12, 2009, when full-power analog television broadcasting ceased in the United States. The transition freed approximately 108 MHz of spectrum (channels 52-69, 698-806 MHz) for reallocation to mobile broadband and public safety communications [13].

### The Regulatory Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1987 | FCC creates Advisory Committee on Advanced Television Service |
| 1996 | FCC allocates digital TV channels alongside analog channels |
| 1996 | Telecommunications Act sets framework for spectrum recovery |
| 2005 | Deficit Reduction Act sets February 17, 2009 as analog cutoff |
| 2009 | DTV Delay Act postpones cutoff to June 12, 2009 |
| 2009 | June 12: Full-power analog TV ceases |
| 2008-2010 | 700 MHz band auctioned for mobile broadband (LTE) |

### The Cultural Loss

The DTV transition killed a specific mode of television reception: the ability to connect an antenna to a television set and receive usable signals without any digital processing. Analog television was tolerant of weak signals -- a marginal signal produced a snowy but watchable picture. Digital television exhibits a "cliff effect": the signal is either perfect or absent, with no gradual degradation. Communities at the edge of broadcast coverage areas that had received usable analog signals found themselves with no digital reception [14].

The transition also made obsolete every analog television receiver manufactured before approximately 2004. The federal government's DTV converter box coupon program distributed over 33 million $40 coupons to offset the cost of digital converters for analog sets. Despite this program, an estimated 2.8 million households were unprepared for the transition on June 12, 2009 [15].

```
ANALOG vs. DIGITAL TV RECEPTION
================================================================

  Signal Strength ──────────────────────────>
  Weak                    Moderate              Strong

  ANALOG:
  [Snow/noise] [Watchable but snowy] [Clean picture]
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  Gradual degradation -- always some picture

  DIGITAL:
  [Nothing] [Nothing] [Nothing] [Perfect picture]
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
  Cliff effect -- all or nothing
```

## 5. The Incentive Auction: Spectrum as Commodity

The FCC's Incentive Auction (2016-2017) represented a new paradigm in spectrum management: paying existing licensees to voluntarily surrender their spectrum rights so the freed spectrum could be auctioned to mobile broadband providers. The auction raised $19.8 billion, of which $10.05 billion was paid to TV broadcasters who gave up their channels [16].

### The Mechanism

The incentive auction operated in two phases:

1. **Reverse auction:** TV stations bid for the minimum payment they would accept to go off the air or move to a different channel. The FCC accepted bids from highest-value channels to lowest until sufficient spectrum was cleared.
2. **Forward auction:** Mobile broadband companies bid for the cleared spectrum. The difference between forward auction revenue and reverse auction payments (plus transition costs) was the net proceeds [17].

### The Repack

Stations that did not participate in the reverse auction were "repacked" -- reassigned to new channel positions in the reduced TV band to create contiguous spectrum blocks for mobile use. The repack required 987 stations to change channels, at a total transition cost exceeding $2.1 billion (funded by auction proceeds). The repack was completed in July 2020 after multiple deadline extensions [18].

### Cultural Implications

The incentive auction made explicit what had been implicit since the 1945 FM reallocation: spectrum is a commodity, and its value is determined by the market, not by the cultural function of the service it carries. A TV station carrying local news to 50,000 viewers is worth less, in market terms, than the same spectrum carrying mobile data to millions of smartphone users. The auction framework provides no mechanism for valuing cultural function, community service, or historical significance. The spectrum goes to the highest bidder [19].

## 6. AM Revitalization: Regulatory Triage

The FCC's AM Revitalization initiative (MB Docket 13-249, Report and Order FCC 15-142, October 2015) acknowledged the structural crisis of AM broadcasting and attempted to address it through regulatory relief rather than spectrum reallocation [20].

### Key Provisions

- **FM translator window:** AM stations were given priority access to FM translator licenses, allowing them to simulcast their AM programming on low-power FM transmitters. By 2024, over 1,800 AM stations had obtained translator authorizations.
- **Expanded AM band:** The FCC authorized expanded-band AM frequencies (1610-1700 kHz), established in 1993, to provide alternative channels for stations suffering from interference on their original frequencies.
- **Antenna site flexibility:** Rules were relaxed to allow AM stations to modify their directional antenna patterns without full engineering studies, reducing the cost of antenna system maintenance.
- **Modulation standard:** The FCC declined to mandate NRSC-1 audio processing standards, leaving audio quality improvement voluntary [21].

### Critique

Broadcasting industry analysts have characterized AM Revitalization as triage rather than treatment. The FM translator pathway essentially concedes that the AM band is no longer viable as a primary delivery platform; the translator converts the AM station into a de facto FM station. The structural problems of the AM band -- rising noise floor, deteriorating receiver quality in consumer electronics, loss of AM receivers in new automobiles -- are not addressed by any provision of the revitalization order [22].

The most significant omission is automobile receiver policy. A substantial fraction of AM listening occurs in vehicles. Several major automobile manufacturers (BMW, Tesla, Volkswagen) shipped models without AM receivers in the 2020s, citing electromagnetic interference from electric vehicle drive systems. The AM Radio for Every Vehicle Act, introduced in the U.S. Congress in 2023, would mandate AM receivers in all new vehicles but has not been enacted as of 2026 [23].

## 7. HD Radio: The Digital Overlay

HD Radio (originally "Hybrid Digital," now a brand name owned by Xperi Corporation) is a digital broadcasting system that allows AM and FM stations to transmit digital audio alongside their analog signals using in-band on-channel (IBOC) technology. The digital signal occupies sidebands adjacent to the station's main analog carrier [24].

### FM HD Radio

FM HD Radio provides three benefits: improved audio quality (approaching CD fidelity), the ability to broadcast up to three additional program channels (HD2, HD3, HD4) on the same frequency allocation, and Program Associated Data (PAD) -- text information (song title, artist, station branding) displayed on the receiver [25].

### AM HD Radio

AM HD Radio has been far less successful. The digital sidebands generated by AM HD Radio cause interference to adjacent-channel stations, particularly at night when AM skywave propagation extends the interference footprint. The FCC authorized AM daytime-only HD Radio operation in 2002 but received numerous complaints about nighttime interference. Some stations activated AM HD Radio and subsequently deactivated it due to complaints from neighboring stations [26].

### The Adoption Problem

HD Radio receivers have never achieved mass-market penetration. As of 2024, approximately 85% of new cars sold in the United States include HD Radio receivers (largely because Xperi licenses HD Radio technology to automobile OEM head unit manufacturers), but aftermarket and portable HD Radio receivers remain uncommon. The HD2/HD3 multicasting channels, which represent HD Radio's most distinctive feature, are used by approximately 2,000 FM stations but attract minimal audience measurement [27].

## 8. The Channel 6 / FM6 Conversion

Television Channel 6 (82-88 MHz) occupies spectrum immediately below the FM broadcast band (88-108 MHz). The analog audio carrier for Channel 6 was at 87.75 MHz, receivable on many FM radio receivers at the bottom of the dial. Several LPTV (Low Power Television) stations exploited this adjacency by operating as de facto FM radio stations -- broadcasting audio-only content on their Channel 6 allocation while transmitting minimal video to maintain their TV license [28].

### The FM6 Proceeding

The FCC's 2023 rulemaking (MB Docket 22-227) created a new "FM6" class of service allowing former Channel 6 TV licensees to convert formally to FM radio operation in the 87.7 MHz range. This regularized what had been a regulatory grey area for decades and created a narrow expansion of the FM band [29].

### Archaeological Significance

The Channel 6 / FM6 conversion is a rare example of spectrum flowing from television to radio rather than the reverse. Every other major reallocation since 1945 has moved spectrum away from broadcasting toward mobile or data services. The FM6 conversion is an exception because the spectrum was already being used as radio in practice; the regulatory change merely acknowledged reality [30].

## 9. LPFM: The Community Spectrum

Low Power FM (LPFM) radio was authorized by the FCC in 2000 through the creation of two new license classes: LP100 (up to 100 watts) and LP10 (up to 10 watts). The LPFM service was designed to provide hyperlocal radio service to communities, schools, churches, and nonprofit organizations that could not afford the capital costs of a full-power FM station [31].

### The Local Community Radio Act

The initial LPFM rules imposed third-adjacent-channel spacing requirements that sharply limited the number of available LPFM frequencies in urban markets. The Local Community Radio Act of 2010 relaxed these requirements, opening significantly more spectrum for LPFM licensing. The FCC's 2013 LPFM filing window received over 2,800 applications [32].

### PNW LPFM Stations

The Pacific Northwest has a relatively robust LPFM ecosystem:

| Station | Frequency | Location | Format |
|---------|-----------|----------|--------|
| KBFG-LP | 107.3 FM | Seattle | Community, multilingual |
| KYRS-LP | 92.3 FM | Spokane | Community, progressive |
| KSVR | 91.7 FM | Mount Vernon | Community (Skagit Valley College) |

LPFM stations are not immune to the forces that kill commercial stations, but their non-commercial license structure and volunteer-operated model make them significantly more resilient. An LPFM station with annual operating costs of $5,000-$15,000 can survive on community donations and grants that would be irrelevant to a commercial station's budget [33].

## 10. The Electric Vehicle AM Crisis

The emergence of electric vehicles (EVs) as a significant fraction of the automobile fleet has created an unexpected crisis for AM radio. EV drive systems -- particularly the high-frequency switching of inverters, motor controllers, and DC-DC converters -- generate substantial electromagnetic interference (EMI) in the AM broadcast band. The interference is so severe that several major automobile manufacturers have shipped EVs without AM radio receivers, citing unacceptable reception quality [37].

### The Technical Problem

An EV's powertrain operates at switching frequencies between 10 kHz and 20 kHz, with harmonics extending into the megahertz range. The AM broadcast band (530-1700 kHz) falls directly in the harmonic range of these switching frequencies. The interference manifests as a broadband hum, whine, or buzz that obscures the AM audio signal. Shielding and filtering can reduce the interference but add cost and weight [38].

### The Manufacturer Response

| Manufacturer | AM Radio Status | Rationale |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|
| Tesla | Removed (all models since ~2018) | EMI; streaming preferred |
| BMW | Removed from some iX/i4 models (2022-2023) | EMI from 800V architecture |
| Volkswagen | Removed from ID.4 (initial release) | Later restored after backlash |
| Ford | Retained (all models) | Consumer demand; AM Radio for Every Vehicle Act |
| Toyota | Retained (all models) | Conservative approach; hybrid experience |
| Rivian | Removed (initial release) | Later restored via software update |

### The Legislative Response

The AM Radio for Every Vehicle Act (S.1669 / H.R.3413), introduced in Congress in 2023 with bipartisan support, would mandate that all new vehicles sold in the United States include AM radio receivers. The bill was motivated by emergency management concerns -- FEMA's Emergency Alert System relies on AM broadcast stations as primary alerting channels, and the loss of AM receivers from a significant fraction of the vehicle fleet would undermine the system's reach [39].

The bill's supporters include the National Association of Broadcasters, FEMA, and the Federal Highway Administration. Its opponents include Tesla (which has argued that streaming and digital alternatives are superior) and some automotive industry trade groups (which have argued that the mandate adds unnecessary cost to vehicles). As of 2026, the bill has not been enacted [40].

## 11. The Future: All-Digital AM and the Sunset Question

The FCC authorized voluntary all-digital AM broadcasting in 2020, allowing AM stations to cease analog transmission entirely and broadcast only a digital signal using the MA3 mode of HD Radio technology. All-digital AM provides significantly improved audio quality and interference rejection compared to analog AM, but requires listeners to have HD Radio receivers [34].

### The Sunset Scenario

The logical endpoint of AM's decline is a spectrum sunset: the formal decommissioning of the AM band and reallocation of its spectrum to other services. This has not been proposed by the FCC and would face enormous political resistance from the remaining AM licensees, religious broadcasters (who operate hundreds of AM stations), and emergency management agencies (who rely on AM as a backup communication channel) [35].

However, the economic trajectory is clear. If AM station counts continue declining at the 2000-2024 rate (~0.5% per year), the band will drop below 3,500 active stations by 2050 and below 2,500 by 2075. At some point, the maintenance cost of the regulatory framework (FCC staff, interference mediation, license processing) will exceed the public interest value of the remaining service [36].

## 13. The Regulatory Archaeology of Docket Files

Every FCC rulemaking proceeding generates a docket file -- a collection of documents including the Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, public comments, reply comments, engineering studies, legal briefs, and the final Report and Order. These docket files are the primary source material for understanding not just what the FCC decided, but why, and who argued for and against [41].

### The Docket as Archaeological Site

A docket file is analogous to a stratified archaeological site. The documents are deposited in chronological order, each layer representing a different stage of the proceeding:

1. **NPRM layer:** The FCC's initial proposal, including its stated rationale and the specific rule changes proposed.
2. **Comment layer:** Public comments from affected parties -- broadcasters, equipment manufacturers, public interest groups, individuals. These comments reveal the political economy of the proceeding.
3. **Reply comment layer:** Responses to the initial comments. The most technically sophisticated arguments often appear here.
4. **Engineering study layer:** Technical analyses submitted by parties or commissioned by the FCC. These studies contain the empirical data underlying the policy decision.
5. **Report and Order layer:** The FCC's final decision, including the adopted rules, the rationale, and the responses to significant comments.

For broadcast archaeology, the most valuable layers are the comment layer (which reveals who cared about the decision and why) and the engineering study layer (which provides technical data about spectrum conditions at the time of the proceeding). The 1945 FM reallocation docket, for example, contains Armstrong's engineering testimony about Sporadic E propagation -- primary source data that is available nowhere else [42].

### Accessing Docket Files

FCC docket files from approximately 2000 onward are available electronically through the Electronic Comment Filing System (ECFS). Older docket files are held in the FCC's physical archive and at the National Archives and Records Administration. Some historically significant dockets have been digitized by academic researchers or the FCC's own historical staff. The complete docket for the 1945 FM reallocation has been partially digitized by Christopher Sterling's research team [43].
