## 1. The PNW Broadcast Graveyard

The Pacific Northwest broadcast graveyard contains stations that have ceased to exist in one of three ways: the frequency went dark (no signal), the call sign was retired (new identity on the same frequency), or the format was replaced so completely that the station's cultural identity was erased even though the transmitter continued operating. The third category is the most common and the most difficult to map, because the physical continuity of the transmitter obscures the cultural discontinuity of the programming [1].

### The Inventory

A systematic survey of the Seattle-Tacoma-Bremerton radio market from 1948 to 2025 identifies the following categories of dead stations:

```
PNW RADIO GRAVEYARD -- CLASSIFICATION
================================================================

  GONE DARK (frequency silent)
  ├── KBRO 1490 AM Bremerton (2014, license cancelled 2019)
  ├── KARR/KNBX 1460 AM Kirkland (intermittent, effectively dead)
  ├── KITZ 1400 AM Silverdale (intermittent silence, STA filings)
  └── Various low-power/translator casualties

  CALL SIGN RETIRED (frequency reused)
  ├── KRAB 107.7 FM (became KNBQ -> KNDD "The End" -> KNDD Alt)
  ├── KSEA 100.7 FM (became KWMX -> KIRO-FM -> KQBZ -> KKWF)
  ├── KEZX 98.9 FM (became KWJZ -> KING-FM -> various)
  ├── KMPS 94.1 FM (became "94.1 The Sound" -> KKWF-HD2/translator)
  ├── KPLZ 101.5 FM (became KPLZ-FM "Star" -> KPLZ various)
  └── KEUT 94.1 FM (became KMPS; the frequency's previous identity)

  FORMAT EXTINCT (frequency active, culture dead)
  ├── Beautiful Music (KSEA, KEUT, KEZX, KIXI, KBRD -- all dead)
  ├── Progressive Rock (KRAB, KZAM -- replaced by commercial formats)
  ├── Smooth Jazz (KWJZ -- dead nationally by 2010)
  └── Hot Talk (KQBZ "Radio for Guys" -- specific format extinct)
```

## 2. KRAB 107.7: Seattle's Community Radio Pioneer

KRAB was Seattle's first community radio station, founded by Lorenzo Wilson Milam and broadcasting from April 1962 to June 1984 on 107.7 FM. The station was a founding member of the Pacifica Radio network and operated on a shoestring budget with volunteer staff, airing an eclectic mix of classical music, folk, jazz, public affairs, poetry, experimental audio, and listener-supported programming [2].

### The Founding

Lorenzo Milam, a polio survivor and radio enthusiast, obtained an FM construction permit in 1961 and signed on KRAB from a repurposed building on Roosevelt Way NE in the University District. The station operated at 1,240 watts initially, later increasing to 50,000 watts (ERP). Milam's philosophy was explicitly anti-commercial: KRAB existed to broadcast what commercial radio would not, and its programming was determined by its volunteer producers rather than by audience ratings or advertiser preferences [3].

### The Programming

KRAB's programming was unlike anything else on the Seattle dial. A typical broadcast day might include:

- Morning: Bach cantatas (complete, uninterrupted)
- Midday: Live recording of a city council hearing
- Afternoon: A three-hour folk music program curated by a volunteer with a 10,000-record collection
- Evening: An experimental sound collage produced by a University of Washington graduate student
- Late night: A live call-in show about urban planning

The station's signal from its Queen Anne Hill transmitter covered the entire Puget Sound basin, delivering this programming to an audience estimated at 25,000-50,000 regular listeners in the early 1980s [4].

### The Death

KRAB's death was financial. The station relied entirely on listener donations and small grants. By 1984, operating costs had exceeded fundraising capacity. The FCC license was sold to a commercial operator for approximately $750,000. The 107.7 frequency became KNBQ ("Q107"), a commercial rock station, in June 1984. The community programming, the volunteer producers, and the anti-commercial philosophy were gone overnight [5].

### The Cultural Legacy

KRAB's impact on Seattle's cultural landscape extended far beyond its listener count. The station incubated programming formats and content producers that would later appear on KUOW, KEXP, and other Seattle media outlets. Several KRAB volunteers went on to careers in public broadcasting, journalism, and arts administration. The station's model -- community-supported, volunteer-operated, editorially independent -- was adopted by the Jack Straw Foundation, which continues to operate KRAB's legacy through audio production workshops, recording studio access, and a community audio archive.

The loss of KRAB in 1984 was experienced by its community as more than a format change. It was the loss of an institution -- a gathering point for Seattle's creative, academic, and activist communities. The 107.7 frequency, which had carried everything from Bach cantatas to city council hearings, now carries Audacy's alternative rock format. The electromagnetic space is identical. The cultural content is unrelated.

The KRAB archives survive in part at the University of Washington Libraries Special Collections and at the Jack Straw Foundation in Seattle. Milam went on to found other community radio stations (KBOO Portland, KTAO Taos) before his death in 2020. The 107.7 frequency is now KNDD "The End," an alternative rock station owned by Audacy -- as far from Milam's vision as is physically possible on the same frequency [6].

## 3. KSEA 100.7: The Beautiful Music Dynasty

KSEA operated on 100.7 FM from 1974/1975 to 1991 as a Bonneville International beautiful music station. At its peak in the late 1970s, KSEA was the #1 billing station in the Seattle market, generating more advertising revenue than any other single radio station in the Puget Sound area [7].

### The Format's Reign

The KSEA beautiful music format served a specific social function: it provided a low-stimulation, high-fidelity audio environment for listeners who wanted music without demands. The audience -- predominantly adults 45-64, employed in professional and managerial occupations -- used KSEA as background audio in offices, homes, and cars. The format required no active listening; it rewarded passive reception [8].

The station's evolution tracked the aging of its audience with precision:

| Period | Brand | Programming | Target Demo |
|--------|-------|------------|-------------|
| 1974-1980 | KSEA | Pure instrumental beautiful music | Adults 45-64 |
| 1980-1985 | KSEA "Easy 101" | Mix of instrumentals and soft vocals | Adults 35-64 |
| 1985-1989 | KSEA | Soft adult contemporary | Adults 35-54 |
| 1989-1991 | KSEA | Mainstream adult contemporary | Adults 25-54 |

Each transition moved the station's playlist incrementally toward younger-skewing content in an attempt to retain advertising viability as its core audience aged past advertiser demographics. The strategy ultimately failed because the existing audience resisted each change while the younger audience being courted already had stations they preferred [9].

### The TV Simulcast

One of KSEA's most remarkable features was its simulcast on KIRO-TV (Channel 7) during television sign-off hours. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, KIRO-TV would sign off at approximately 1:00-2:00 a.m. and resume broadcasting at approximately 5:00-6:00 a.m. During the overnight gap, the TV station transmitted KSEA's FM audio with a static "KSEA" logo on screen. This cross-platform repurposing predated modern concepts of multi-platform content distribution by decades [10].

## 4. KEZX/KWJZ 98.9: Jazz on the Sound

KEZX (98.9 FM) operated as a beautiful music station from 1972 to 1982, competing directly with KSEA. In 1982, the station transitioned to smooth jazz as KWJZ, becoming one of the first dedicated smooth jazz stations in the Pacific Northwest. The format operated under various brandings until 2006, when the frequency was sold to KING-FM (classical) and the jazz format was extinguished [11].

### The Smooth Jazz Extinction

Smooth jazz as a radio format peaked nationally in the early 2000s with approximately 50 stations. By 2010, the format was nearly extinct, with fewer than 10 full-power stations remaining. The causes parallel the beautiful music extinction: the audience aged past advertiser demographics, the format attracted no significant younger listeners, and streaming services (particularly Pandora's smooth jazz channels) fragmented the remaining audience [12].

### The Format's Cultural Function

Smooth jazz served a specific urban professional demographic -- the same listeners who had aged out of rock and pop formats but were not yet old enough for the beautiful music/easy listening audience that preceded them. The format was an architectural bridge between pop and classical, providing sophisticated instrumental and vocal arrangements that rewarded casual listening without demanding focused attention. Restaurant owners, hotel lobbies, and upscale retail establishments used smooth jazz stations as ambient programming, giving the format a physical presence in commercial spaces that other formats lacked [13a].

The format's cultural function disappeared not because the function was no longer needed, but because streaming services replaced the broadcast mechanism. Spotify and Pandora smooth jazz channels provide the same ambient audio function that KWJZ once served, without the radio infrastructure, without the local DJs, and without the connection to a specific geographic community.

KWJZ's death in Seattle was part of this national extinction event. The station had maintained respectable but declining ratings through the early 2000s. When Bonneville Broadcasting sold the 98.9 frequency to Classic Radio Inc. (KING-FM), the smooth jazz audience was scattered. No replacement emerged. The format is now served only by internet streams and satellite radio channels [13].

## 5. KBRO 1490 AM: Bremerton's Local Voice

KBRO 1490 AM was licensed to Bremerton, Washington, in 1946 and served the Kitsap County community for nearly 70 years. The station's history parallels the broader AM decline story but with a specific PNW character: KBRO was Kitsap County's local radio voice, covering the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard, local government, high school sports, and community events that no Seattle station would cover [14].

### The Death Spiral

KBRO's decline followed the classic AM death spiral:

1. **Revenue decline (2000s):** Local advertising shifted to internet platforms; national advertising dried up as agencies consolidated buys on major-market stations.
2. **Maintenance deferred (2008-2012):** With declining revenue, transmitter maintenance was deferred. Signal quality degraded.
3. **Environmental contamination discovered (2012):** The transmitter site on Oyster Bay was found to contain PCB-contaminated transformer oil, a legacy of decades of tube-era transmitter operation.
4. **Station goes dark (2014):** Unable to afford either remediation or relocation, the operator ceased broadcasting.
5. **License cancelled (2019):** After five years of silence, the FCC cancelled the license.

The Facility ID (35298) remains in the FCC database as a permanent record of a station that served its community for 68 years and was killed by a combination of economic obsolescence and environmental liability [15].

```
KBRO 1490 AM -- TIMELINE
================================================================

  1946  Licensed to Bremerton, WA
  1946-2000  Local service: news, sports, community
  2000-2008  Revenue decline; competition from Seattle stations
  2008-2012  Maintenance deferred; signal degradation
  2012  PCB contamination discovered at transmitter site
  2014  Station goes dark
  2019  FCC cancels license (Facility ID 35298 archived)
```

## 6. KMPS 94.1: The Country Institution

KMPS (94.1 FM) launched as a country station on February 1, 1978, and operated under that format for 39 years and 10 months until its flip to soft AC on December 4, 2017. It was the longest-running country format on a single frequency in the Seattle market and one of the longest-running in the Pacific Northwest [16].

### The Frequency's History

Before KMPS, the 94.1 frequency had been KEUT, a beautiful music station operating from 1975 to 1978. The KEUT-to-KMPS flip was itself a format death: the beautiful music audience on 94.1 was scattered when country music replaced their programming. The irony is complete: KEUT's beautiful music death in 1978 created the vacancy that became Seattle's most durable country station, which was itself killed in 2017 for the same economic reasons that killed KEUT [17].

### The Battle in Seattle

The KMPS flip on December 4, 2017 was the most thoroughly documented format death in Pacific Northwest broadcast history. The sequence:

- **November 17, 2017:** Entercom completes CBS Radio merger. KMPS and KKWF become sister stations.
- **November 17, 2017:** KMPS begins playing Christmas music. Industry observers note the anomaly -- Christmas music in mid-November is a classic stunt indicator.
- **December 4, 2017, 9:12 a.m.:** KMPS flips to soft AC as "94.1 The Sound." First song: Lionel Richie, "Hello."
- **December 4, 2017, ~10:45 a.m.:** Hubbard Broadcasting's KVRQ (98.9) flips from rock to "Country 98.9." Time between KMPS death and KVRQ birth: approximately 90 minutes.
- **December 4, 2017, afternoon:** KMPS personality Deanna Lee announces she is moving to KKWF middays [18].

The RadioInsight article by Lance Venta documenting the flip was published the same day. Social media reaction was immediate and voluminous. The KMPS Facebook page received thousands of comments from listeners mourning the format's death. The comments constitute a primary source document of format loss grief [19].

## 7. KPLZ 101.5: Star 101.5 and the Hot AC Wars

KPLZ-FM (101.5) operated as "Star 101.5" in the hot adult contemporary format from the early 1990s through the 2000s. The station competed directly with KBKS (106.1 "KISS FM") and KQMV (92.5 "MOViN") for the adult 25-54 female demographic -- the most valuable audience segment in radio advertising [20].

### The Hot AC Trap

Hot adult contemporary is a format defined by what it excludes rather than what it includes. The playlist is "current hits and recent favorites" with hard restrictions against hard rock, rap, heavy R&B, and anything sonically abrasive. The result is a format that is broadly acceptable and intensely preferred by almost nobody -- a characteristic that makes it vulnerable to any competitor that more precisely targets a segment of its broad audience [21].

KPLZ cycled through multiple brandings and format adjustments over its lifetime, reflecting the inherent instability of the hot AC position. Each adjustment attempted to sharpen the station's identity within the broad middle of the musical spectrum, and each adjustment alienated some portion of the existing audience while (hopefully) attracting a larger portion of a new target demographic. The station's format identity has been modified so many times that its original cultural character is archaeologically inaccessible [22].

## 8. The AM Casualties: Small Market Death Spiral

The Puget Sound region has lost numerous small-market AM stations to the systemic forces described in Module 1. These stations share a common death pattern: local revenue cannot sustain the operating costs of an AM transmitter facility, deferred maintenance degrades signal quality, degraded signal quality reduces the remaining audience, and the station either goes dark or is sold to a religious or ethnic-language broadcaster willing to operate at a loss for mission rather than profit [23].

### The Survivors

Some AM stations in the PNW survive through format niches that commercial operators have abandoned:

- **KIRO 710 AM:** Survives as news/talk with Bonneville/Entercom/Audacy corporate support.
- **KNKX 88.5 FM (formerly KPLU):** Public radio; immune to commercial economics.
- **KOMO 1000 AM:** News/talk with Sinclair corporate support.
- **KJR 950 AM:** Sports; the format's specialized audience justifies continued AM operation.

The stations that survive on AM are those with either corporate backing sufficient to absorb losses, a non-commercial license that eliminates the revenue requirement, or a format niche (sports, ethnic language, religious) whose audience specifically uses AM receivers. The general-interest AM station serving a small local community -- the KBRO model -- is functionally extinct in the PNW [24].

## 9. The Frequency Palimpsest: 100.7 FM Seattle

The 100.7 MHz allocation in Seattle is the most thoroughly documented frequency palimpsest in the Pacific Northwest. A palimpsest is a manuscript that has been erased and rewritten multiple times, with traces of earlier text visible beneath the current layer. The 100.7 frequency has been "rewritten" at least seven times since 1948, and each previous layer is detectable through careful archaeological examination [25a].

### The Seven Layers

| Layer | Call Sign | Period | Format | Depositing Force |
|-------|----------|--------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | KIRO-FM | 1948-1963 | CBS Network simulcast | Golden Age of Radio; television disruption |
| 2 | KIRO-FM | 1963-1967 | MOR / "Young Sound" AC | FCC FM independence mandate |
| 3 | KIRO-FM | 1967-1973 | Drake-Chenault / Progressive | Format experimentation window |
| 4 | KSEA | 1974-1991 | Beautiful Music to AC | Demographic aging curve |
| 5 | KWMX/KIRO-FM | 1991-1995 | Hot AC / Talk | Format crisis; audience loss |
| 6 | KQBZ | 1999-2005 | Hot Talk "Radio for Guys" | Niche male demographic targeting |
| 7 | KKWF | 2005-present | Country "The Wolf" | Howard Stern cascade; KMPS rivalry |

Each layer is legible through specific sources: FCC records provide the licensing boundaries, Broadcasting Yearbooks provide the annual snapshots, trade press provides the transition narratives, and RadioDiscussions oral history provides the listener experience. The complete palimpsest -- all seven layers fully documented with primary sources -- is the central artifact of the DFQ project [25b].

### The Through-Line

What connects these seven layers is not a programming philosophy or a corporate identity but a physical fact: the same transmitter on Tiger Mountain has been sending a 100,000-watt FM signal into Puget Sound airspace continuously since 1948. The transmitter is the thread. The programming is the beads strung on it. When we study the palimpsest, we are studying what Seattle wanted to hear -- or more precisely, what Seattle's radio operators believed Seattle would pay to hear -- decade by decade [25c].

## 10. Ghost Signals: Stations That Changed Beyond Recognition

Some stations are not dead but are unrecognizable. The frequency is active, the transmitter is operating, but the station's current identity has no connection to its historical one. These are ghost signals -- the transmitter carries a living signal, but the cultural identity that once defined the station is gone.

### Case Studies

| Frequency | Then | Now | Connection |
|-----------|------|-----|-----------|
| 107.7 FM | KRAB (community radio, 1962-1984) | KNDD "The End" (alternative rock) | None |
| 100.7 FM | KSEA (beautiful music, 1974-1991) | KKWF "The Wolf" (country) | None |
| 98.9 FM | KWJZ (smooth jazz, 1982-2006) | KVRQ "Country 98.9" (country) | None |
| 94.1 FM | KMPS (country, 1978-2017) | "94.1 The Sound" (soft AC) | None |
| 95.7 FM | KIXI (beautiful music/standards, 1958-1998) | KJR-FM (sports) | None |

In every case, the physical frequency is identical. The transmitter is often on the same tower. The call sign may have changed once or multiple times. But the cultural content -- the programming that gave the station its identity and its relationship with its audience -- is completely different. The ghost signal occupies the same electromagnetic space that the dead station once filled, but it is a different entity [25].

## 11. The Survivors: What Endures and Why

Not every PNW station has died. Some have survived for decades through economic models that insulate them from the commercial pressures that kill format-dependent stations. Understanding why these stations survive illuminates what killed the ones that didn't [26a].

### KEXP 90.3 FM: The Non-Commercial Model

KEXP (formerly KCMU) has operated on 90.3 FM since 1972, initially as the University of Washington student station. The station's non-commercial license places it in the educational reserve band (88-92 MHz), permanently protected from commercial encroachment. KEXP's revenue comes from listener donations, institutional grants, and corporate underwriting -- not from advertising. This model makes KEXP immune to the audience-rating-revenue cycle that kills commercial formats [26b].

KEXP's programming philosophy -- eclectic music curated by knowledgeable DJs, with an emphasis on new and independent artists -- is structurally impossible on commercial radio because it does not target a measurable demographic. KEXP serves a taste community rather than a demographic cohort, and taste communities do not map to the age/gender/income categories that advertisers purchase.

### C89.5 FM: The School Station That Outlived Them All

C89.5 FM is licensed to the Nathan Hale High School in Seattle and has been broadcasting since 1971. The station is one of the most powerful high school radio stations in the United States (7,200 watts ERP) and has outlived dozens of commercial stations that launched and died during its five-decade tenure. The station's survival is attributable to its zero-cost labor model (student volunteers), institutional backing (Seattle Public Schools), and non-commercial license [26c].

### KVI 570 AM: The AM Survivor

KVI has operated on 570 AM since 1926, making it one of the oldest continuously licensed stations in the Pacific Northwest. The station has survived the AM death spiral through a combination of corporate support (Bonneville, Entercom/Audacy), a talk format that exploits AM's strength in voice programming, and a legacy coverage area that still reaches the entire Puget Sound basin from its 5,000-watt directional array [26d].

## 12. What Survives: The Archaeological Record

For each dead PNW station, the surviving archaeological record varies in completeness:

### Evidence Availability Matrix

| Station | FCC Records | Yearbooks | Airchecks | Newspaper | Community Memory |
|---------|------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| KRAB 107.7 | Complete | 1962-1984 | Extensive (UW archives) | Good | Strong (still active) |
| KSEA 100.7 | Complete | 1975-1991 | Moderate (collector archives) | Fair | Active (RadioDiscussions) |
| KWJZ 98.9 | Complete | 1982-2006 | Sparse | Fair | Moderate |
| KBRO 1490 | Complete | 1946-2014 | Very sparse | Sparse | Fading |
| KMPS 94.1 | Complete | 1978-2017 | Good (digital era) | Excellent | Strong (recent) |
| KEUT 94.1 | Complete | 1975-1978 | Very sparse | Sparse | Gone |

### The Preservation Imperative

For each dead station, there is a finite window in which the archaeological record can be captured. Physical evidence (transmitter sites, equipment) degrades with time. Aircheck collectors age and their estates may not preserve their collections. Community memory fades as the listener base ages past the point of active participation in online forums. The preservation imperative is most acute for stations that died before the digital era: KRAB (1984), KSEA (1991), and KEUT (1978) are approaching or have passed the point where living memory is the last surviving evidence layer.

### Institutional Gaps

The most significant gap in PNW broadcast preservation is the absence of a dedicated regional broadcast archive. The Museum of History and Industry (MOHAI) in Seattle collects broadcast ephemera but does not maintain a comprehensive audio archive. The University of Washington Libraries hold the KRAB papers but do not systematically collect commercial broadcast audio. The Jack Straw Foundation preserves KRAB-specific materials but has no mandate for broader broadcast preservation. A regional broadcast audio archive -- analogous to the Pacific Northwest Collection at the University of Washington for print materials -- does not exist. Until one is established, preservation relies on individual collectors and the periodic deposit of materials into existing institutional collections.

The pattern is consistent: FCC records and yearbooks provide complete structural documentation for all stations. Airchecks and newspaper coverage are inversely proportional to the station's age. Community memory fades with time but persists longer for stations that had strong listener identities. The KRAB and KSEA communities remain active online more than 30 years after their stations' deaths, sustained by the strength of the cultural bonds those stations created [26].
