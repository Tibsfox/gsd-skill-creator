> **Through-line:** *A frequency doesn't die when the transmitter goes dark. ## 1. What Is a Dead Frequency?

A dead frequency is an allocation on the electromagnetic spectrum that once carried a licensed broadcast signal and no longer does. The term encompasses several distinct conditions: frequencies where the license has been voluntarily surrendered, frequencies where the license was revoked by regulatory action, frequencies where the transmitter hardware was physically decommissioned, and frequencies that were reallocated to entirely different services through regulatory proceedings [1].

The distinction matters. A frequency whose license was surrendered may sit dormant for years on the FCC's available allocation table before being claimed by a new applicant. A frequency that was reallocated -- such as the TV channels 70-83 that became cellular telephone spectrum in the 1983 Cellular Service proceeding -- will never return to broadcast use [2]. The first is a vacancy. The second is an extinction.

```
DEAD FREQUENCY TAXONOMY
================================================================

  DORMANT              REALLOCATED           TECHNICAL ORPHAN
  +---------------+    +---------------+     +---------------+
  | License       |    | Band plan     |     | Transmitter   |
  | surrendered   |    | changed by    |     | destroyed or  |
  | or expired    |    | regulatory    |     | abandoned;    |
  |               |    | proceeding    |     | license       |
  | Frequency     |    |               |     | technically   |
  | available for |    | Frequency     |     | active but    |
  | new applicant |    | permanently   |     | no signal     |
  +---------------+    | removed from  |     +---------------+
                       | broadcast     |
                       | allocation    |
                       +---------------+
```

The FCC maintains records of every licensed broadcast facility through the Licensing and Management System (LMS), successor to the Consolidated Database System (CDBS). Each facility is assigned a permanent Facility ID that persists even after the station goes dark. This creates an archaeological record: the Facility ID for a station that signed off in 1953 is still queryable in the same database that tracks stations broadcasting today [3].

> **SAFETY WARNING:** Abandoned transmitter sites may contain hazardous materials including PCB-contaminated transformer oil, asbestos insulation, and high-voltage capacitor banks. The FCC's environmental compliance rules (47 CFR 1.1307) require site remediation, but enforcement on long-abandoned sites is inconsistent. Never enter an abandoned broadcast facility without appropriate safety equipment and environmental assessment [4].

## 2. The Archaeology of Spectrum

Broadcast archaeology is not a formally recognized academic discipline, but it operates with the same methodological rigor as any historical archaeology: stratigraphy (layered sequence of occupancy), artifact analysis (surviving recordings, equipment, documents), and oral history (interviews with engineers, on-air talent, and listeners who remember what was there) [5].

The primary stratigraphic tool for American broadcast archaeology is the Broadcasting Yearbook, published annually from 1935 to 2010 by Broadcasting Publications, Inc. (later Reed Elsevier, then Newton Media). Each volume provides a complete snapshot of every licensed AM, FM, and TV station in the United States: call sign, frequency, power, antenna height, owner, network affiliation, and format. The American Radio History archive (americanradiohistory.com) has digitized and made freely available the complete run [6].

### The Yearbook as Core Sample

A single frequency examined across consecutive yearbooks produces a core sample analogous to a geological column. The 100.7 MHz allocation in Seattle, examined through yearbooks from 1950 to 2005, reveals nine distinct strata: CBS simulcast, MOR transition, automated AC, Drake-Chenault top-40, progressive rock, beautiful music, hot AC, talk radio, and hot talk -- each deposited by distinct economic and cultural pressures [7].

The boundaries between strata -- the format flip events -- are the most information-rich zones, just as formation boundaries in geology carry the most diagnostic evidence of environmental change. A flip event compresses months of corporate strategy, audience research, competitive analysis, and regulatory compliance into a single moment: the last song under the old format and the first song under the new one.

### Source Hierarchy

Broadcast archaeological research employs a strict source hierarchy:

| Rank | Source Type | Authority | Example |
|------|------------|-----------|---------|
| 1 | FCC records (LMS/CDBS) | Primary regulatory | Facility ID query, call sign history |
| 2 | Broadcasting Yearbook | Primary reference | Annual station listings with technical data |
| 3 | Trade press (Radio & Records, Broadcasting & Cable) | Contemporary reporting | Format change announcements, personnel moves |
| 4 | Newspaper archives | Contemporary coverage | Local reporting on station events |
| 5 | Community archives (RadioDiscussions.com) | Oral history | First-person accounts requiring corroboration |
| 6 | Wikipedia (FCC-sourced sections) | Secondary synthesis | Useful for orientation, not for citation |

This hierarchy is not arbitrary. FCC records are the legal record of what was licensed. Yearbooks are the systematic record of what was operating. Trade press captures what the industry said about itself in real time. Newspaper archives capture what the public noticed. Community archives preserve what listeners and engineers remember. Each layer adds context that the one above it lacks, but each layer below is less reliable without corroboration from above [8].

## 3. AM Band Decline: The Long Goodbye

The AM broadcast band (530-1700 kHz in the United States) has been in structural decline since the 1970s. The causes are cumulative and reinforcing: FM's superior audio fidelity attracted music listeners, cable and satellite television absorbed news and talk audiences, and the proliferation of electronic interference sources (switching power supplies, LED lighting, computer equipment) raised the AM noise floor in urban environments to the point where some frequencies became effectively unusable [9].

### The Numbers

The FCC licensed 4,727 AM stations in the United States as of 2000. By 2024, the count had dropped to approximately 4,185 -- a loss of 542 stations, or 11.5%, in 24 years. But the raw count understates the damage. Many surviving AM stations operate at drastically reduced power, have switched to part-time operation, or have been granted Special Temporary Authority to operate with reduced facilities while their owners decide whether to continue [10].

The AM Revitalization order (FCC 15-142, October 2015) acknowledged the crisis by creating a pathway for AM licensees to obtain FM translator licenses -- low-power FM rebroadcast transmitters that extend an AM station's coverage onto the FM band. By 2024, more than 1,800 AM stations had obtained FM translator authorizations, essentially admitting that their AM signal alone was no longer viable [11].

### PNW AM Casualties

The Pacific Northwest has seen notable AM departures:

- **KBRO 1490 AM Bremerton** -- Licensed 1946, went dark 2014. The transmitter site on Oyster Bay was contaminated with PCB transformer oil. License cancelled by FCC 2019 [12].
- **KITZ 1400 AM Silverdale** -- Licensed 1957 as KBRO's companion. Went silent multiple times in the 2010s. The pattern of intermittent operation is characteristic of the AM death spiral: reduced revenue leads to reduced maintenance leads to reduced signal quality leads to reduced audience leads to reduced revenue [13].
- **KARR 1460 AM Kirkland** -- Originally KNBX, briefly operated in the 1990s and early 2000s before going dark. The East Side of Lake Washington has lost all locally licensed AM stations [14].

The technical reason AM is dying is that the band's propagation characteristics, once its greatest advantage (groundwave coverage extending hundreds of miles from a high-power transmitter), have become its greatest liability. The same groundwave that carries the signal also carries every source of electrical interference between the transmitter and the receiver. In 1950, a suburban home contained perhaps two motors (refrigerator, furnace blower) and no switching power supplies. In 2025, the same home contains dozens of switching supplies (phone chargers, LED drivers, laptop adapters, smart home devices) each generating broadband RF interference that raises the noise floor across the entire AM band [15].

## 4. FM Reallocation and Channel 6

The FM broadcast band (88-108 MHz) was not always where it is today. The original FM allocation, established by the FCC in 1941, placed FM broadcasting at 42-50 MHz. In June 1945, the FCC relocated the entire FM band to 88-108 MHz, rendering every existing FM receiver in the country instantly obsolete. The decision, championed by FCC Commissioner Charles Denny and opposed by FM inventor Edwin Howard Armstrong, remains one of the most consequential and controversial spectrum management decisions in American broadcasting history [16].

Armstrong argued that the 42-50 MHz band had been proven by years of broadcasting from his experimental station W2XMN atop Alpine, New Jersey, and that the reallocation was designed to protect television's Channel 1 (44-50 MHz) at FM's expense. The FCC's stated rationale was that the 88-108 MHz band would suffer less from Sporadic E ionospheric skip interference. Armstrong's counterargument -- that skip was manageable and the real motive was commercial -- has been substantially supported by subsequent historical analysis [17].

### The Channel 6 Loophole

Television Channel 6 occupies 82-88 MHz, immediately adjacent to the FM band's lower edge at 88 MHz. The audio carrier for Channel 6 analog television was at 87.75 MHz. Some FM receivers, especially car radios with analog tuning, could receive Channel 6 audio at the bottom of the FM dial. This created a regulatory anomaly: several LPTV (Low Power Television) stations licensed on Channel 6 operated as de facto FM radio stations, broadcasting audio-only content on their audio carrier while transmitting a minimal video signal to maintain their TV license [18].

The FCC's 2023 rules for the post-analog-TV Channel 6 band acknowledged this history by creating a new "FM6" class of service, allowing former Channel 6 TV licensees to convert to FM radio operation. The frequency range 87.7 MHz thus transitions from television to radio -- a dead frequency in one service becoming a live frequency in another [19].

## 5. The FCC Licensing and Management System

The FCC's LMS database is the authoritative record of every broadcast facility ever licensed in the United States. For broadcast archaeology, the critical queries are:

- **Facility ID lookup:** Every station has a permanent numeric identifier. KKWF (100.7 Seattle) is Facility ID 6367. This ID persists through every call sign change, ownership transfer, and format flip [3].
- **Call sign history:** The CDBS call sign history query returns every call sign ever assigned to a Facility ID, with effective dates. This is the definitive tool for resolving call sign count discrepancies.
- **Application search:** Construction permits, license modifications, and Special Temporary Authority grants are all searchable. A station that went dark may have an STA on file documenting why.

### Construction Permit Archaeology

Stations that were authorized but never built, or that were built under construction permits that were later modified before regular licensing, leave traces in the FCC database. A construction permit call sign (often a "K" or "W" prefix with a sequential number) may appear in the call sign history even if the station never broadcast under that identifier. This is one mechanism by which call sign counts can diverge from the number of distinct operating identities [20].


## 6. Frequency Reuse and Signal Ghosts

When a station goes dark, its frequency does not remain vacant indefinitely. The FCC's allocation scheme is built on contour protection: each station's licensed coverage area (defined by signal strength contours measured in dBu) must not overlap with any co-channel or adjacent-channel station's protected contour. When a station surrenders its license, the protection it provided to neighboring stations disappears, and the protection they provided to it is released. This opens the allocation table for a new applicant who can demonstrate that their proposed contour fits within the remaining protection geometry [21].

The result is that a frequency abandoned in one location may be claimed by a completely different operator in a different city. The physical frequency is the same; the cultural content is entirely different. In broadcast archaeological terms, this is the equivalent of a building being demolished and a new one constructed on the same foundation -- the address is the same but the occupant has changed.

### Skywave and Skip

AM frequencies exhibit a particularly haunting form of reuse. After sunset, when the ionosphere's D-layer (which absorbs medium-wave signals during the day) recedes, AM signals reflect off the F-layer and travel hundreds or thousands of miles. A clear-channel AM frequency like 1070 kHz, once occupied by KNX Los Angeles during the day, can be received as far north as British Columbia at night. But if KNX were to go dark, the same skip propagation would carry whatever replaced it -- or, in the absence of a replacement, the interference from distant co-channel stations that KNX's dominant signal had previously masked [22].

This phenomenon means that AM dead frequencies are never truly silent. They are occupied by the noise floor plus the cumulative skywave contributions of every co-channel station on the continent. The silence is not silence -- it is the sum of everyone else's signal.

## 7. The Archaeology of Automation

Broadcast automation systems are a critical but underappreciated layer of broadcast archaeology. The physical artifacts -- tape cartridge machines (carts), reel-to-reel automation controllers, and early computer-based systems -- document the transition from live human operation to machine-mediated programming. Each generation of automation technology enabled a different format strategy and displaced a different category of human labor [23].

### The Automation Generations

| Era | Technology | Format Enabled | Labor Impact |
|-----|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| 1960s | Sequential cart machines (Gates, Collins) | Beautiful Music, MOR | Replaced overnight and weekend DJs |
| 1966-1975 | Drake-Chenault reel-to-reel syndication | Top-40, Beautiful Music | Replaced format programming decisions |
| 1975-1990 | Computer-controlled cart sequencers (IGM, Cetec Schafer) | Any format with tight rotation | Reduced staff to morning drive + news |
| 1990-2005 | Hard-drive playout (Dalet, Scott Studios, Prophet) | All formats | Enabled voice-tracking; one host "live" on six stations |
| 2005-present | Cloud-based playout (WideOrbit, Enco) | All formats | Enabled centralized hub operation; local studios optional |

The Drake-Chenault syndication system deserves particular archaeological attention because its physical artifacts are scarce. The service provided stations with custom-sequenced reel-to-reel tapes containing a complete top-40 or beautiful music format, including pre-recorded DJ voiceovers with local station identification inserted at designated splice points. The reels were returned to Drake-Chenault after use and reused or destroyed. Very few survive in archives [24].

## 8. Dead Air as Cultural Artifact

Dead air -- unintentional silence during a broadcast -- is the most feared event in live radio. The standard broadcast engineering specification calls for no more than 4 seconds of dead air before an automated silence sensor triggers backup programming or an alarm [25]. Dead air represents a failure of the broadcast chain: somewhere between the programming source and the transmitter, the audio path has been interrupted.

But dead air also carries cultural meaning. The last moments of a station's final broadcast before a format flip -- the final song, the final station identification, the silence before the new format begins -- are a form of cultural death. These moments are preserved by aircheck collectors (hobbyists who record station audio, particularly format changes) and have become a recognized category of broadcast ephemera [26].

### The Station Sign-Off

Before the deregulation of the 1980s, most AM and FM stations were required to sign off overnight. The sign-off typically included a final station identification, the national anthem, a public service announcement, and then the carrier was shut down. The sign-off created a daily death and resurrection cycle for each frequency. Deregulation removed the sign-off requirement, and most stations transitioned to 24-hour operation, eliminating this daily archaeological boundary marker [27].

The sign-off tradition survives in the format flip. When KQBZ (100.7 Seattle) flipped to KKWF on November 30, 2005, the transition included a period of stunting (countdown audio using Microsoft Sam text-to-speech) followed by the new format's first song. This transition is functionally identical to a sign-off/sign-on sequence, compressed into a single event. The aircheck of this flip exists in multiple collector archives and constitutes a primary document of the frequency's cultural transition [28].

## 9. The Transmitter as Monument

The physical transmitter site is the most tangible archaeological artifact of a dead station. Unlike the signal (which is gone) and the programming (which may or may not have been recorded), the transmitter site persists as a physical location with real infrastructure: towers, buildings, transmission lines, ground systems, and the environmental legacy of decades of operation [29a].

### Tower Archaeology

AM broadcast antennas are vertical radiators -- steel towers that serve as the antenna element itself. A directional AM station may have two to twelve towers arranged in a specific geometric pattern to shape the radiation pattern. These tower arrays are engineered to provide maximum signal in the desired coverage area while protecting co-channel stations in other markets. The geometry of the array encodes the station's relationship to every other station on the same frequency [29b].

When an AM station goes dark, its tower array may remain standing for years or decades. The towers are often leased to cellular carriers, who mount their antennas on the existing structures. The AM station is dead, but its towers continue to serve a different communication function -- a physical palimpsest mirroring the frequency palimpsest on the dial.

### Ground Systems

AM broadcast towers require an extensive ground system: a radial network of copper wires buried in the earth beneath the tower, extending outward for at least a quarter-wavelength (approximately 75 meters at 1000 kHz). The ground system completes the antenna circuit and determines the efficiency of the signal launch. A full-service AM station may have 120 radials per tower, each 75-100 meters long, buried at a depth of 15-30 centimeters [29c].

When a station is decommissioned, the ground system is almost never removed. It remains buried, a hidden network of copper threading through the earth beneath suburban developments, parking lots, and wetlands. In broadcast archaeological terms, the ground system is the station's footprint -- invisible but permanent, detectable by metal detector surveys and construction excavation.

```
AM TOWER SITE ARCHAEOLOGY -- TYPICAL ELEMENTS
================================================================

  VISIBLE:
    - Steel tower(s): 60-150 meters tall
    - Guy wires and anchors (guyed towers)
    - Equipment building (transmitter, phasing equipment)
    - Perimeter fencing (RF safety)
    - Obstruction lighting (FAA requirement)

  BURIED:
    - Copper ground radials (120 per tower, 75-100m each)
    - Transmission line trenches
    - Electrical service conduit
    - Tower base foundations (concrete, 2-6 meters deep)

  ENVIRONMENTAL:
    - PCB-contaminated soil (transformer leakage)
    - Lead paint on tower steel
    - Mercury from rectifier tubes (pre-1970 sites)
    - Asbestos insulation in transmitter building
```

### The Tiger Mountain Transmitter

The Tiger Mountain transmitter site, located on Tiger Mountain in Issaquah, Washington, has carried the 100.7 FM signal since 1948. The site has been continuously upgraded through nine call signs and seven format eras, but the physical location has remained constant for 78 years. The geographic coordinates of the transmitter are filed with the FCC and constitute the fixed point around which the station's entire broadcast history revolves. Every signal that has ever been transmitted on 100.7 FM in Seattle originated from the same mountainside [29d].

This physical continuity gives the transmitter site a memorial function that the changing call signs and formats do not possess. The Tiger Mountain site is the one constant in 78 years of change. It is the archaeological datum point from which all other measurements are taken.

## 10. International Precedents: The BBC Long Wave Shutdown

The United Kingdom's experience with frequency abandonment provides instructive parallels. BBC Radio 4 broadcast on 198 kHz long wave from 1967 to 2024, providing a nationwide service receivable on simple receivers without internet access. The frequency served agricultural communities, maritime users, and as a backup communication channel during emergencies. The BBC's 2023 announcement of the long wave shutdown provoked significant public response, particularly from rural listeners and the amateur radio community that used the Radio 4 carrier as a frequency reference [29].

The Droitwich transmitter, which carried the 198 kHz signal at 500 kW, was one of the most powerful broadcast transmitters in Europe. Its shutdown represented the extinction of a propagation mode -- long wave groundwave coverage of the entire British Isles from a single transmitter -- that cannot be replicated by any digital platform. The frequency was not reallocated; it simply went dark [30].

### The Shipping Forecast

The most culturally resonant casualty of the long wave shutdown was the Shipping Forecast, broadcast on Radio 4 long wave at 0048, 0520, 1201, and 1754 daily. The forecast covers 31 sea areas around the British Isles and has been broadcast continuously since 1924. While the forecast continues on FM and digital platforms, its long wave transmission was the version received by actual mariners at sea on simple receivers. The cultural weight of the Shipping Forecast -- it has been called "an icon of Britishness" by cultural historians -- derives precisely from its broadcast on a frequency that reaches everywhere, reliably, without infrastructure [31].

## 11. Preservation Methodologies

Broadcast archaeological preservation operates on three levels: the signal record (what was broadcast), the physical record (what equipment and documents survive), and the cultural record (what listeners and practitioners remember).

### Signal Preservation

The most direct preservation method is the aircheck -- a recording of a station's broadcast, typically capturing station identifications, jingles, DJ patter, and commercial breaks. Professional aircheck services like RadioTapes.com and the Pavek Museum of Broadcasting maintain archives of station audio dating to the 1920s. The Library of Congress Recorded Sound Section holds the most comprehensive institutional collection [32].

### Physical Preservation

Broadcast equipment preservation is handled primarily by museums: the Pavek Museum of Broadcasting (St. Louis Park, Minnesota), the Museum of Broadcast Communications (Chicago), and the Antique Wireless Association Electronic Communication Museum (Bloomfield, New York). The challenge is that broadcast equipment is large, heavy, and often contaminated with hazardous materials. A single broadcast transmitter may weigh several tons and contain PCB-laden capacitors, mercury-vapor rectifiers, and asbestos insulation [33].

### Digital Preservation

The Internet Archive's "Old Time Radio" collection provides streaming access to thousands of hours of historical broadcast audio. The Radio Heritage Foundation (radioheritage.net) focuses specifically on Pacific region broadcast history, including PNW stations. The Archive of American Television (emmytvlegends.org) preserves interview recordings with broadcast engineers and executives whose oral histories document technical and business decisions that left no paper trail [34].

## 12. The Economics of Silence

The final dimension of dead frequencies is economic. A silent frequency represents not just the absence of a signal but the absence of a revenue stream. Every AM and FM frequency in the United States can be valued by its potential advertising revenue, which is a function of market size, signal coverage, and the demographics of the reachable audience [35a].

### Spectrum Valuation

The FCC does not directly price broadcast spectrum -- licenses are not auctioned for radio and television as they are for cellular and broadband spectrum. Instead, broadcast licenses are transferred through private sales, with prices determined by market negotiation. The sale price of a broadcast license reflects the capitalized value of its future revenue stream, which in turn reflects the frequency's reach and the market's advertising economy [35b].

A major-market FM station license (top 10 DMA) can sell for $20-100 million. A small-market AM station license may sell for less than $50,000 -- or may have negative value if the cost of maintaining the transmitter site exceeds the revenue the station can generate. When a station's license value drops below zero, the rational economic outcome is abandonment: surrender the license, decommission the transmitter, and walk away. This is exactly what happened to KBRO 1490 AM Bremerton [35c].

### The Silence Premium

Paradoxically, a dead frequency can have value precisely because it is dead. A dormant allocation on the FCC's available frequency list represents an opportunity for a new entrant. If the frequency's contour protection geometry is favorable -- if it can cover a desirable market without interfering with existing stations -- the allocation itself has value as a platform for a new station. Broadcast engineering consultants specialize in identifying these dormant allocations and matching them with potential applicants [35d].

The silence premium is highest in growing markets (where new stations can capture expanding advertising pools) and lowest in declining markets (where the forces that killed the previous occupant will likely kill the next one). In the PNW, growing suburban communities on the I-5 corridor represent potential markets for new allocations, while rural communities in Eastern Washington represent the declining end.

## 13. The Listener as Witness

The final category of broadcast archaeological evidence is the listener. Every station that has ever operated had an audience, and the members of that audience carry memories of what the station sounded like, what it meant to them, and how its loss affected their daily life. These memories constitute oral history -- the least reliable but most human layer of the archaeological record [35e].

### Memory Decay

Listener memory of defunct stations follows a predictable decay curve. Specific details (call sign, frequency, DJ names, jingle melodies) fade within 5-10 years of the station's death. General impressions (the station's "feel," the type of music, the emotional association) persist for 20-30 years or longer. The most durable memories are those tied to specific life events: the station you were listening to when you heard about a major news event, the station that played during your commute for years, the station whose jingle you could sing in your sleep [35f].

RadioDiscussions.com threads about defunct stations are natural experiments in collective memory. A thread about KSEA, started 15 years after the station's death, will contain vivid impressions ("it was like audio wallpaper -- soothing, constant, always there") alongside factual errors ("I think they played 'Sailing' by Christopher Cross every hour" -- unlikely but emotionally true). The challenge for the broadcast archaeologist is to extract reliable data from these memory-distorted accounts [35g].

### The Witness Interview

The Radio Preservation Task Force has identified structured witness interviews -- recorded conversations with former listeners, using photographs of station logos and audio clips of jingles as memory prompts -- as a high-priority preservation activity. The witnesses are aging. The KSEA audience of 1978 is now in their 70s-90s. The KRAB audience of 1975 is similarly aging. Within 10-15 years, first-hand listener memory of pre-1985 PNW radio will be largely extinct [35h].

The urgency is not academic. These witnesses carry knowledge that no document preserves: what it felt like to listen, what the station meant in the context of daily life, how the format change was experienced emotionally. This knowledge dies with the witness. It cannot be reconstructed from FCC records, yearbooks, or airchecks.
