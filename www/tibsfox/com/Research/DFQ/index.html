Dead Frequencies & Living History

Broadcast Archaeology · AM Decline · Format Changes · PNW Radio Graveyard · FCC Spectrum · Audio Preservation
Overview Broadcast Archaeology Format Changes PNW Graveyard FCC Reallocation Audio Preservation Mission Pack
A frequency doesn't die when the transmitter goes dark. It dies when nobody remembers what it carried. The AM band is a graveyard of call signs, and the FM dial is a palimpsest — every allocation written over someone else's signal. KRAB 107.7 was community radio before the term existed. KSEA 100.7 was audio Valium for a generation. KMPS 94.1 was country music for 39 years. They are gone. The frequencies remain, carrying someone else's programming, and the silence between the old signal and the new one is where the history lives.

Research Modules

M1: Abandoned Radio Frequencies & Broadcast Archaeology

Archaeology Track A · Foundation

Dead frequency taxonomy: dormant, reallocated, technical orphans. The FCC Licensing and Management System as archaeological record. AM band decline: station counts, noise floor, PNW casualties. FM reallocation history. The 1945 band relocation. Frequency reuse and signal ghosts.

M2: Format Changes & Cultural Loss

Culture Track A · Economics

Anatomy of a format flip: stunting, flip point, post-flip blanket. The economics of format death. Beautiful music as first mass extinction. Telecommunications Act consolidation. Voice-tracking and ghost DJs. Sign-offs and dead air. The Seattle format war cycle.

M3: PNW Radio Graveyard

PNW Track B · Regional survey

Station-by-station profiles of dead PNW broadcasters: KRAB 107.7 (community pioneer), KSEA 100.7 (beautiful music dynasty), KWJZ 98.9 (smooth jazz), KBRO 1490 AM (Bremerton local), KMPS 94.1 (country institution). AM casualties, ghost signals, archaeological evidence matrix.

M4: FCC Frequency Reallocation & Digital Transition

Regulatory Track B · Policy

Spectrum as managed resource. The 1945 FM band relocation and Armstrong's fight. UHF TV reallocation to cellular. DTV transition and the cliff effect. Incentive auction: spectrum as commodity. AM Revitalization triage. HD Radio. LPFM. The all-digital AM sunset question.

M5: Historical Audio Preservation & Dead Air

Preservation Synthesis

The preservation crisis: less than 5% of pre-1970 radio survives. Aircheck methodology and collector community. Institutional archives: LOC, Paley Center, Pavek Museum. Digital format migration and bit rot. The cultural weight of dead air. Sign-off as monument. PNW audio survival inventory.
