# Corporate Era and Physical Development (1993--Present)

## MCA Concerts Acquisition (1993)

According to a Seattle Times archive report (August 4, 2002), MCA Concerts -- the concert arm of entertainment giant MCA, which would later become Universal Concerts -- purchased the amphitheatre and approximately 100 acres from the Bryan family for **$3.9 million** in 1993. MCA subsequently invested $2 million in site improvements during their initial ownership period.

The 1993 purchase renamed the facility from "Champs de Brionne Music Theatre" to "The Gorge Amphitheatre" and shifted it from a family operation to commercial concert programming under a major entertainment conglomerate. The name change was more than cosmetic -- it signaled the venue's transition from a regional winery attraction to a nationally marketed concert destination.

### Financial Impact on Grant County

In 1994, Grant County began collecting a 5% fee on all tickets sold at the Gorge. That year MCA paid nearly $275,000 in fees to the county. This revenue stream established the amphitheatre as a significant economic driver for the surrounding rural community -- a role it continues to play under Live Nation management.

## House of Blues Ownership (1999)

According to the Seattle Times (2002), House of Blues purchased the venue in 1999. Under House of Blues management, the Gorge continued to expand its programming and audience reach. By 2001 (the last year referenced in the 2002 Seattle Times article), annual attendance had approached 300,000, generating over $700,000 in county ticket fees -- more than double the 1994 figure under MCA.

The House of Blues era represents a transitional period in the venue's corporate history. House of Blues brought experience in music venue management and brand development, but the company itself was undergoing changes that would ultimately lead to its absorption into the Live Nation empire.

## Live Nation Acquisition (2006)

According to Wikipedia and multiple venue sources, Live Nation Entertainment acquired the Gorge Amphitheatre in 2006 and has managed it since. Under Live Nation's management, capacity expanded to its current range of 20,000--27,500 depending on event configuration. Live Nation is described in venue documentation as "the global concert promoter that has since managed expansions, bookings, and operations" (Grokipedia, 2026).

## Mountain Productions Stage Installation

According to Mountain Productions' own portfolio documentation, the company "played a critical role in the Gorge's history and provided the stage installation that remains standing today." Mountain Productions constructed the permanent stage structure overlooking the canyon with what they describe as "skilled engineering and experienced construction."

The permanent stage -- which gives spectators unobstructed views of the Columbia River Gorge canyon -- replaced earlier temporary configurations that had been used since the Bryan era. The stage structure is engineered to withstand the high winds that can occur in the exposed canyon setting while maintaining clear sightlines to the natural backdrop. This installation was a turning point in the venue's physical infrastructure, transforming it from a site with temporary seasonal staging to a purpose-built concert facility.

## White River Amphitheatre Competition (2003)

According to Wikipedia, the White River Amphitheatre opened in 2003 on the Muckleshoot Indian Reservation near Auburn, Washington, offering direct competition to the Gorge for the Seattle market. White River is approximately 40 minutes from Seattle versus 2 hours 20 minutes for the Gorge. The proximity advantage is significant for casual concertgoers who do not want a multi-hour drive for a weeknight show.

However, according to Wikipedia, a Seattle Times columnist noted that the Gorge offers a preferable view and "experience," and the Gorge has maintained its dominant position as the premier Pacific Northwest outdoor concert destination. The competitive dynamic between the two venues illustrates the Gorge's unique value proposition: it is not merely a concert venue but a landscape experience. Artists and fans choose the Gorge specifically because of the setting, not despite the distance.
