# Indigenous Deep History

## The Wanapum People

The land on which the Gorge Amphitheatre sits is part of the ancestral territory of the Wanapum people. According to Wikipedia and the Confluence Project, the Wanapum (Sahaptin: Wanapam) are a Sahaptin-speaking people whose name means "river people" -- from *wana* (river) + *-pam* (people). They inhabited the Columbia River from present-day Pasco, Washington, upstream to the Priest Rapids area, approximately 20 miles south of the Gorge Amphitheatre. Their principal village, P'na, was located approximately two miles downstream of what is now the Priest Rapids Dam.

The Wanapum are not a federally recognized tribe. They never signed a treaty with the United States government, never ceded their land, and never fought a war against settlers. They have maintained continuous presence along the Columbia River for millennia. Their relationship to the land surrounding the Gorge Amphitheatre is not historical -- it is ongoing.

### Petroglyphs and the Columbia Basalt

Over 300 petroglyphs were carved by Wanapum ancestors into the same Columbia Basalt cliffs that form the amphitheatre's backdrop. According to Wikipedia, approximately 60 of these petroglyphs were blasted from the rock before being flooded by dam construction between 1953 and 1966. Surviving petroglyphs were relocated to Ginkgo Petrified Forest State Park, where they can be viewed today.

## Lewis and Clark Contact (1805)

According to HistoryLink.org, Captain Clark's journals from October 1805 describe the Wanapum people, led by chief Cutssahnem, greeting the Corps of Discovery and sharing food and entertainment. The journals document the Wanapum sharing food, providing hospitality, and engaging in cultural exchange with the expedition. This represents the first European written record of the Wanapum people, though the Wanapum had inhabited the Columbia River corridor for thousands of years prior.

The Lewis and Clark expedition passed through the Columbia River Gorge region during their westward journey to the Pacific Ocean. Their journals provide the earliest written European documentation of the indigenous peoples, landscape, and natural resources of the area that would later become the amphitheatre site.

## David Thompson (1811)

Canadian explorer David Thompson provided the first detailed written description of Wanapum life on July 8--9, 1811. According to HistoryLink.org (File 9010), Thompson documented their use of seine nets, diet of pounded roots and salmon, and the singing and dancing of women, described as containing "something of a religious nature." Thompson's account is significant because it captures Wanapum cultural practices that predate sustained European contact and the disruptions that followed.

## Smohalla and the Washani Religion

According to HistoryLink.org and Wikipedia, in the 1850s a Wanapum spiritual leader named Smohalla (c. 1815--1895) created the Washani religion (also called "Dreamer Religion"). The Washani faith held that the land could not be owned and that traditional lifeways should be maintained rather than taking up arms. Smohalla taught that the Earth was the mother of all people, that it could not be sold or given away, and that indigenous peoples should refuse to adopt Euro-American agricultural practices.

The Wanapum never fought white settlers, did not sign the Yakama Treaty of 1855 (their leaders were not invited to negotiations according to HistoryLink.org), and as a result retained no federally recognized land rights. Governor Isaac Stevens was reportedly unaware of their existence as a distinct people. This absence from treaty negotiations is not a minor historical footnote -- it defines the Wanapum's legal status to the present day. They are a sovereign people who never surrendered sovereignty through treaty, yet have no federal recognition of that sovereignty.

### The Yakama Treaty Exclusion

The 1855 Yakama Treaty consolidated multiple Columbia Plateau nations onto reservations. The Wanapum were not included in the negotiations because their leaders were simply not invited. According to HistoryLink.org, this was not a deliberate exclusion but a consequence of Governor Stevens's incomplete knowledge of the region's indigenous peoples. The result was that the Wanapum fell through the cracks of the treaty system entirely -- they were not party to any treaty, did not cede any land, and received no reservation allocation.

## Displacement and Continuity

### Manhattan Project (1942)

According to Wikipedia and Grant County PUD records, the 1942 Manhattan Project (Hanford Engineer Works) forced temporary relocation of approximately 30 Wanapum from their winter camp. They were allowed to remain near ancestral fishing grounds, but the establishment of the Hanford Nuclear Reservation fundamentally altered the landscape and restricted access to traditional areas along the Columbia River.

### Dam Construction (1953--1966)

Construction of Priest Rapids Dam (1953) and Wanapum Dam (1966) flooded the riverbanks where the Wanapum had lived in traditional tule houses. The dams inundated village sites, fishing stations, and the locations of many petroglyphs. This was not ancient history -- it occurred within living memory of Wanapum elders who are still alive today.

### Wanapum Heritage Center

Today, approximately 60 Wanapum live near the Priest Rapids Dam site under a cooperative arrangement with Grant County PUD. The Wanapum Heritage Center, completed in 2015, occupies 50,000 square feet and documents Wanapum culture through the permanent exhibition "Life as a Wanapum." The center is located approximately 20 miles south of the Gorge Amphitheatre and serves as the primary public-facing institution for Wanapum cultural preservation.

## The Land and the Venue

The connection between Wanapum history and the Gorge Amphitheatre is geographical and material. The basalt cliffs into which the Wanapum carved petroglyphs are the same geological formations that create the venue's acoustic properties. The Columbia River that defined Wanapum life for millennia is the same river whose canyon frames the stage. The terraced hillside where 27,500 concertgoers sit was shaped by the same Missoula Floods that the Wanapum's ancestors witnessed.

This is not a metaphorical connection. The land itself is continuous. The amphitheatre site sits within the ancestral territory of a people who remain present, who never signed away their relationship to this place, and who maintain cultural sites within 20 miles of the venue. Every concert at the Gorge takes place on Wanapum ancestral land. This history is systematically omitted from entertainment media coverage of the venue.
