## 1. The Classic Rock Format

KISM broadcasts a classic rock format, branded as "NW Washington's Classic Rock." The format is a programming category that originated in the mid-1980s as album-oriented rock (AOR) stations began aging with their audience. Rather than following listeners into softer adult contemporary territory, classic rock stations maintained the rock catalog while shifting the playlist toward established artists and familiar tracks [1][2].

The classic rock format at KISM centers on the canonical rock catalog: Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd, The Rolling Stones, AC/DC, Tom Petty, Fleetwood Mac, and their contemporaries. The playlist extends from the late 1960s through the early 2000s, with occasional inclusions of newer artists who fit the format's sonic profile. The format does not chase trends -- it provides familiarity. For listeners aged 35--54, the core classic rock demographic, these are the songs that formed their musical identity. The station does not need to introduce them to new music; it needs to play the music they already know at the moments they want to hear it [1][2].

In the streaming era, the classic rock format faces a specific competitive challenge. Every song KISM plays is available on Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube, and a dozen other platforms, on demand, without commercials. The format's competitive advantage is not access to music -- that battle was lost to streaming a decade ago. The competitive advantage is curation, locality, and companionship. A streaming service provides songs. A radio station provides a voice, a community connection, and the shared experience of knowing that the person driving next to you on the I-5 is hearing the same thing you are.

## 2. Audience Demographics

KISM's audience profile, as published by PNW Media Group in their advertising materials, shows the expected classic rock demographic skew [2]:

| Demographic | Share |
|-------------|-------|
| Male | 65% |
| Female | 35% |
| Age 18--24 | 10% |
| Age 25--34 | 16% |
| Age 35--54 | 59% |
| Age 55--64 | 11% |
| Age 65+ | 4% |

The 35--54 core represents approximately 59% of listeners -- a concentration that is typical of the classic rock format nationwide. This demographic is attractive to advertisers because it represents peak earning years, homeownership, vehicle purchases, and discretionary spending. A Bellingham car dealer advertising on KISM reaches male buyers aged 35--54 who are statistically the most likely to purchase a new or used vehicle [2].

The 65% male skew is standard for classic rock. The format's historical catalog was predominantly recorded by male artists for a male audience during an era when rock radio was explicitly gender-coded. This demographic reality shapes the station's advertising mix: the advertiser roster skews toward automotive, home improvement, sports, outdoor recreation, and similar male-targeted categories.

The 10% share in the 18--24 demographic is notable. Classic rock stations historically struggle with younger listeners, who have no nostalgic connection to the catalog. KISM's 10% share suggests that either the format has some crossover appeal with younger listeners (perhaps through parents' musical influence) or that KISM is the default rock station in a market with limited alternatives.

## 4. The Brad and John Morning Show

The Brad & John morning show is KISM's anchor program -- the daily broadcast that defines the station's personality and drives its community engagement. Brad Cash and John Reynolds host the morning drive slot, combining classic rock music with local conversation, community event promotion, and the civic engagement that makes local radio different from nationally syndicated programs [1][3].

Morning drive is the highest-rated daypart in radio. More people listen to radio between 6am and 10am than at any other time of day, because morning commutes are when car radios are on and listening is a passive activity compatible with driving. For advertisers, morning drive represents the largest available audience and commands the highest rates. For the station, the morning show is the face of the brand [1].

Brad & John's community engagement extends beyond on-air conversation. The show is the primary vehicle for KISM's signature programs -- "Vehicle for a Vet" is a Brad & John initiative, and the morning show promotes community events, fundraisers, and local causes throughout the broadcast year. In a small market, the morning show hosts become civic figures. They emcee events, appear at festivals, and are recognized at restaurants and grocery stores. The relationship between the hosts and the community is personal in a way that is impossible in large-market broadcasting.

The show is distributed as a podcast -- "Brad and John -- Mornings on KISM" -- available on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, Player FM, and other platforms. This podcast distribution extends the show's reach beyond the FM coverage area and creates an archive that over-the-air broadcasting inherently lacks. A listener who misses the live morning show can catch up via podcast, and former Bellingham residents who have moved away can maintain their connection to the community through the hosts they grew up listening to [4].

## 5. Vehicle for a Vet

"Vehicle for a Vet" is KISM's signature community program, running since at least 2017 -- eight consecutive years as of the 2024 cycle. The program pairs donated and refurbished vehicles with deserving military veterans, selected through a community nomination and review process [5][6].

### How It Works

The program cycle runs annually:

1. **Vehicle donation:** Community members donate vehicles -- often veterans themselves donating to fellow veterans. The 2024 vehicle was donated by an anonymous Whatcom County couple, the wife being a veteran herself [5][6]
2. **Vehicle refurbishment:** Local automotive shops volunteer labor and parts to restore the donated vehicle to reliable condition. Participating shops have included Bellingham Automotive, Dr. John's Auto Clinic, and Angler Automotive [5][6]
3. **Nomination:** The community submits nomination letters for deserving veterans. The program receives over 100 nominations annually [5][6]
4. **Selection:** A volunteer Veterans Committee reviews nominations and selects the recipient. The 2024 committee was led by Dan Johnson, a former Marine [5]
5. **Presentation:** The vehicle is presented on-air and at a community event, with the Brad & John morning show hosting the reveal [5][6]

### Documented Recipients

- **Jason Thayer (2024):** 12-year US Army Infantry veteran with two Afghanistan tours. Received a refurbished 2002 GMC Envoy [5][6]
- **USMC Gunnery Sergeant Jake Freeman (previous year):** Marine veteran, details of vehicle received documented in station archives [5]

### Program Significance

Vehicle for a Vet is not a corporate sponsorship exercise or a promotional gimmick. It is a community program that leverages the station's broadcasting platform to connect donors, mechanics, and veterans in a direct, tangible act of support. The program creates multiple points of community engagement: the veteran who receives the vehicle, the donor who gives it, the mechanics who restore it, the committee members who review nominations, and the listeners who follow the story on-air.

The program has been covered by Radio Ink (a national broadcasting trade publication) and MyBellinghamNow.com, indicating that its significance extends beyond the local market into the broadcasting industry's recognition of community-engaged programming [5][6].

Eight years of continuous operation (2017--2024) demonstrates institutional commitment. The program has survived personnel changes, ownership transitions, and the rebrand from Cascade Radio Group to PNW Media Group. It is embedded in the station's identity, not dependent on any individual's involvement.

## 6. Community Engagement Programs

Beyond Vehicle for a Vet, KISM and its sister stations in the PNW Media Group cluster operate several community programs:

### Show Us Your Roll (Toilet Paper Drive)

In 2023, KISM organized "Show Us Your Roll," a toilet paper drive that collected over 14,000 rolls valued at approximately $20,000. The collected supplies were donated to the Skagit Friendship House, a social services organization serving people experiencing homelessness and poverty in Skagit County [7].

The program's name is characteristic of classic rock radio's irreverent personality -- turning a mundane item into a community challenge. The 14,000-roll result demonstrates the station's mobilization capacity: listeners responded to an on-air call to action by physically bringing toilet paper to collection points, converting broadcast reach into tangible community resources.

### Christmas Wish (KAFE 104.1)

Sister station KAFE operates "Christmas Wish," an annual holiday program that fulfills wishes for families in need during the holiday season. While KAFE-branded rather than KISM, the program operates within the same PNW Media Group cluster and shares the community engagement infrastructure [7].

### Airwaves for Animals

A radiothon partnering with the Whatcom Humane Society, "Airwaves for Animals" raises funds for animal welfare services in Whatcom County. The partnership connects the station's audience with a local nonprofit, using broadcasting time as a fundraising platform [7].

### Knucklehead of the Year

An annual audience-voted humorous award, the "Knucklehead of the Year" invites listeners to nominate and vote for the year's most notable instance of poor judgment or comedic misfortune. The award is not mean-spirited -- it is participatory humor that engages the audience and generates on-air content during the holiday season [7].

## 7. Sister Station Programming

KISM's community role is amplified by its position within the five-station PNW Media Group cluster. Each station serves a different audience segment, and the combined programming creates a local media ecosystem:

- **KGMI (790 AM / 96.5 FM translator / KISM-HD2):** News/Talk format. Provides the local news and public affairs programming that complements KISM's entertainment focus. KGMI's 2219 Yew Street Road newsroom generates local coverage -- city council, school board, county government, breaking news -- that informs the community KISM entertains [1]

- **KAFE (104.1 FM):** Soft Adult Contemporary. Serves the female 25--54 demographic that KISM's male-skewing classic rock does not reach. KAFE's "Christmas Wish" program extends the cluster's community engagement to a different audience segment [1]

- **KPUG (1170 AM):** Sports. Covers Whatcom County high school sports, Western Washington University athletics, and regional sports content. For a sports-focused listener, KPUG provides local coverage that national sports media ignores [1]

- **K-Bay (98.9 FM translator / KISM-HD3):** Classic Hits. A lighter format than KISM's rock focus, classic hits includes pop, soft rock, and mainstream catalog tracks from the same era. K-Bay serves listeners who want familiar music without the hard rock edge [1]

The five stations collectively cover the major commercial radio format categories for a mid-sized market: rock, soft AC, news/talk, sports, and classic hits. A Bellingham resident can move through their day with PNW Media Group stations: KGMI for morning news, KISM for the commute, KPUG for evening sports, KAFE for weekend errands. The cluster is designed to be the listener's radio companion throughout the day, regardless of mood or activity.

The sister station ecosystem also enables cross-promotion that individual stations cannot achieve. A community event promoted on KISM reaches the classic rock audience; the same event promoted on KAFE reaches a different demographic. Vehicle for a Vet can be announced across all five stations, reaching the full spectrum of Bellingham radio listeners. This cross-pollination multiplies the impact of every community initiative, turning a single-station program into a cluster-wide campaign.

The cluster model also provides resilience. If one format declines in popularity -- if classic rock loses its audience share to streaming, for example -- the cluster's revenue is not dependent on that single format. KGMI's news/talk format serves a different need, KPUG's sports coverage has a dedicated audience, and KAFE's soft AC format reaches a demographic that classic rock does not. Format diversity is the cluster's insurance policy against changing listener preferences.

## 8. Digital Engagement Channels

KISM maintains listener engagement through multiple digital platforms that extend the broadcast relationship beyond the FM signal:

- **kism.com:** Station website with programming schedule, personality profiles, contest information, and live streaming. The website serves as the station's digital home base and primary point of contact for listeners seeking information [4]
- **MyBellinghamNow.com:** Free local news site operated by PNW Media Group. Provides text-based local news coverage that complements KGMI's on-air reporting [8]
- **Facebook:** Community page for listener interaction, contest promotion, and event announcements. Social media engagement supplements the one-to-many broadcast model with many-to-many conversation [4]
- **Text messaging:** SMS-based listener interaction for contests, requests, and real-time feedback during broadcasts [4]
- **TuneIn:** Live streaming through the TuneIn platform, enabling internet-based listening for audiences outside the FM coverage area [4]
- **PNW Perks:** Half-price deal platform offering discounted local products and services. Converts the station's advertiser relationships into a direct e-commerce channel [8]

The digital strategy follows the small-market radio playbook: use digital platforms to extend the reach and depth of relationships that originate on the broadcast side. The station does not compete with pure digital media companies on their terms. Instead, it uses digital tools to reinforce the local connections that are its core competitive advantage.

## 9. Podcast Distribution

KISM distributes two podcast programs:

### Brad and John -- Mornings on KISM

The flagship morning show podcast captures highlights and segments from the daily broadcast. Distribution includes Apple Podcasts, Spotify, Player FM, and other major podcast platforms. The podcast serves multiple audience segments [4]:

- **Time-shifted local listeners:** Bellingham residents who cannot listen live during morning drive
- **Expatriate listeners:** Former Bellingham residents who maintain community connection through the morning show
- **On-demand listeners:** Audience members who prefer to consume specific segments rather than the full broadcast

### Time Warp Radio

A specialty podcast focusing on music history, deep cuts, and behind-the-scenes stories from the classic rock era. The format aligns with KISM's core audience interest in the history and culture behind the music, not just the music itself [4].

Podcast distribution is significant for KISM because it addresses the fundamental limitation of over-the-air broadcasting: temporal constraint. A radio broadcast exists only in the moment of transmission. A podcast exists indefinitely, discoverable by new listeners and replayable by existing ones. For a station whose competitive advantage is local personality and community content, podcasting converts ephemeral broadcasts into persistent assets.

## 10. The Classic Rockforce

The "Classic Rockforce" is KISM's email club -- a direct-to-listener communication channel that bypasses social media algorithms and third-party platform dependencies. Members receive updates on station events, contests, promotions, and community programs directly in their email inbox [4].

Email clubs are a foundational digital engagement tool for radio stations. Unlike social media followers (whose reach is subject to platform algorithm changes) or app users (who must download and maintain a separate application), email subscribers represent a direct, owned communication channel. The station controls the message, the timing, and the format without intermediary interference.

The "Classic Rockforce" branding reinforces the station's format identity -- "Rockforce" combines the classic rock positioning with a sense of community membership. Listeners who join are not passive recipients; they are "Rockforce" members, part of a named community with a shared interest. This naming strategy is small but effective: it transforms a transactional relationship (station broadcasts, listener receives) into a membership relationship (listener belongs to a named group).

## 11. Studios at 2219 Yew Street Road

All five PNW Media Group stations share a studio facility at 2219 Yew Street Road, Bellingham, WA 98229. The location is accessible from I-5 via the Lakeway Drive exit, situated in a commercial area south of downtown Bellingham [1][9].

The shared facility model is standard for radio clusters. Multiple studios share common infrastructure -- engineering rooms, server/IT systems, sales offices, conference rooms, reception -- while each station maintains its own broadcast studio with dedicated audio equipment. The physical proximity allows engineering staff to service all stations from a single location, sales teams to coordinate multi-station advertising packages face-to-face, and management to oversee the cluster without traveling between sites.

The Yew Street Road address is the physical anchor of KISM's community identity. When listeners are invited to "come by the station," this is where they go. When advertisers visit to discuss campaigns, this is where they meet the sales team. When community organizations partner with the station for events, this is the coordination center. The studio is not just a broadcasting facility -- it is a community touchpoint, a physical place where the relationship between station and audience becomes tangible.

The location's significance extends beyond operations. Being physically present in Bellingham -- not in a corporate office in Michigan, not in a content hub in a larger market -- signals that the station is part of the community, not just a signal that happens to be licensed there. The studio is where Brad and John arrive each morning, where Scott Less programs the music, where Heidi Persson manages the operation. The work happens here, in Bellingham, on Yew Street Road.

## 12. The Role of Local Radio

KISM's community programming and engagement represent a case study in what local radio provides that streaming services, satellite radio, and podcasts cannot:

**Locality.** When Brad and John discuss a Bellingham city council decision, a road closure on Lakeway Drive, or a fundraiser at a local restaurant, they are providing information that has zero value to anyone outside the community and immense value to everyone within it. Streaming algorithms do not know about Lakeway Drive. Local radio does.

**Simultaneity.** When KISM plays "Stairway to Heaven" at 4:45pm on a Thursday, every listener hears it at the same moment. This shared temporal experience -- knowing that the person in the next car is hearing the same song -- creates a communal connection that on-demand listening cannot replicate. Radio is a shared experience; streaming is a private one.

**Civic function.** Programs like Vehicle for a Vet, the toilet paper drive, and Christmas Wish convert broadcasting infrastructure into civic infrastructure. The station's transmitter, audience, and airtime become tools for community mobilization. No streaming service offers this function because no streaming service has a local community to mobilize.

**Personality.** The relationship between a listener and a local radio host is qualitatively different from the relationship between a listener and a playlist algorithm. Brad Cash and John Reynolds are real people who live in Bellingham, who are seen at community events, who know the callers by name. This personal relationship generates trust, loyalty, and engagement that technology-mediated relationships cannot match.

**Discovery.** A radio station introduces listeners to content they did not seek out. A streaming service responds to demand; a radio station creates it. Nick Michaels' "The Deep End" music history features expose listeners to stories and deep cuts they would never have found through algorithmic recommendations. This curatorial function -- a human being selecting content for a community based on knowledge and judgment -- is radio's original value proposition, and it remains unmatched.

**Emergency information.** When a winter storm closes I-5, when a wildfire threatens communities in Skagit County, when an earthquake requires immediate public communication, local radio is the fastest and most reliable mass communication channel available. FM radio requires no internet connection, no cellular service, no app updates. A battery-powered FM receiver works when the power is out, when cell towers are down, and when streaming services are unreachable. KISM's 50,000-watt signal from Mount Constitution can reach communities across northwest Washington and into British Columbia during emergencies when other communication infrastructure has failed. This is not a theoretical capability -- it is a tested reality during every major winter storm that disrupts power and communications in the region.

The Emergency Alert System (EAS) integration adds a formal role to KISM's emergency function. As part of the national EAS infrastructure, the station is required to broadcast emergency alerts -- weather warnings, AMBER alerts, civil emergency messages -- as part of its license obligations. But the informal emergency role -- the morning show hosts staying on air during a snowstorm, providing real-time road condition updates based on listener reports -- is equally important and entirely voluntary. This is civic function expressed through broadcasting infrastructure.

## 13. Safety and Sensitivity Protocols

The KISM research project observes the following protocols for community and programming documentation:

| Consideration | Category | Protocol |
|---------------|----------|----------|
| Veterans' personal details | Privacy | Use only publicly shared stories from KISM broadcasts and official sources; no unpublished details |
| Indigenous territory context | Cultural | Acknowledge Lummi and Nooksack territories by nation-specific name; never use generic "Native American" or "Indian" without specific nation identification |
| Cross-border regulatory details | Legal | Present factual FCC/CRTC framework; no legal advice or regulatory advocacy |
| Living personalities | Privacy | Use only information from public broadcasts, official bios, and station-published materials |

These protocols ensure that the research documents factual information that is already in the public domain without exposing private details, offering legal guidance, or making cultural generalizations. The Whatcom County region includes the traditional territories of the Lummi Nation and the Nooksack Tribe -- any reference to these territories uses the specific nation names, never generic labels [10].
