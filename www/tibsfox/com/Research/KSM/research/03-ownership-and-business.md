## 1. Edward K. Christian and Saga Communications

Saga Communications, Inc. (NASDAQ: SGA) was founded in May 1986 by Edward K. Christian in Grosse Pointe Farms, Michigan. Christian's founding vision was specific: acquire radio stations in mid-sized American markets where large broadcast groups had not yet consolidated, and operate them with local management autonomy and community engagement that national chains could not match [1][2].

Christian frequently cited the Nordic etymology of "Saga" -- "an ongoing adventure" -- in corporate communications. The name was not accidental. It reflected a philosophy that broadcasting is a continuous narrative, not a quarterly earnings exercise. Each market was a chapter, each station a character, and the company's role was to provide the resources and framework that let local operators tell their communities' stories [1].

Christian's background was in broadcasting operations, not finance. His approach to acquisitions prioritized operational quality and community fit over pure financial engineering. While larger broadcast groups were assembling portfolios through leveraged buyouts and cost-cutting consolidation, Saga was building relationships with local management teams and preserving the operational independence that made small-market stations valuable to their communities [1][2].

He served as Chairman, President, and CEO simultaneously -- a concentration of authority that reflected the company's entrepreneurial culture rather than corporate governance trends. Christian was the company, and the company's strategy was his strategy. This integration of vision and execution persisted until his death in July 2022, when leadership passed to Christopher Forgy [3].

Christian's management philosophy was articulated in a quote that appears in Saga's corporate communications: "We began with a few stations, great management, and a vision. Building upon that foundation, we continue to grow our broadcast company. Each year we shape and mold the properties we acquire into our vision." The language is revealing -- "shape and mold" implies craftsmanship, not extraction. Saga's approach to acquisitions was to invest in the properties and develop them, not to strip assets and reduce costs. This philosophy is reflected in the Bellingham cluster, which has expanded its digital presence, maintained local programming, and invested in community engagement rather than cutting staff to meet quarterly targets [1].

The contrast with the "financial engineering" approach to broadcasting is instructive. In the 2000s and 2010s, several large radio companies -- most notably iHeartMedia (formerly Clear Channel) -- accumulated debt through leveraged acquisitions, then cut costs aggressively to service that debt. The result was hollowed-out stations: syndicated programming, voice-tracked shifts, reduced news staff, and minimal community engagement. These stations generated cash flow but lost the community connection that makes radio valuable. Saga's approach -- lower leverage, local management, community investment -- produced lower growth rates but sustainable operations. When iHeartMedia filed for bankruptcy in 2018, Saga was profitable and debt-free.

## 2. The Goldilocks Market Strategy

The "Goldilocks market" concept is the strategic framework that defines Saga Communications. The principle is simple: target markets that are "just right" -- large enough to generate meaningful advertising revenue, but small enough to be ignored by the consolidation machines of iHeartMedia, Cumulus, and Audacy [1][2].

These markets share characteristics:

- **Population:** Typically 50,000--250,000 in the metro area
- **Competition:** Enough local businesses to support multiple ad-supported stations, but not enough to attract national chain attention
- **Community identity:** Strong local identity where listeners prefer local voices over syndicated programming
- **Operational leverage:** A well-run cluster of 3--5 stations can dominate the advertising market and achieve margins that justify the investment

Bellingham exemplifies the Goldilocks profile. With a metro population of roughly 230,000 (Whatcom County), it is large enough to support a five-station radio cluster but small enough that iHeartMedia, Cumulus, and Audacy have not invested heavily. Saga's acquisition of the Bellingham cluster in 1998 gave the company a dominant position in a market where dominance is achievable and sustainable [4][5].

The Goldilocks strategy is a broadcasting application of a broader business principle: serve the markets that larger competitors neglect. In technology, this is the "long tail" strategy. In retail, it is the independent bookstore or hardware store that survives by knowing its customers personally. In broadcasting, it is the station where the morning host is the person you see at the grocery store on Saturday.

The strategic wisdom becomes visible through contrast. When iHeartMedia consolidated hundreds of stations under centralized programming, the result was operational efficiency but cultural flattening. A classic rock station in Bellingham sounded identical to a classic rock station in Boise -- same syndicated morning show, same nationally programmed playlist, same voice-tracked afternoon host who had never visited the city. Saga's approach was the opposite: local programming, local personalities, local decision-making. The cost was higher per station. The result was listener loyalty that centralized programming could not achieve.

The mathematics of the Goldilocks strategy are instructive. In a market of 230,000 people, a five-station radio cluster with dominant market share might generate $5--8 million in annual advertising revenue. This is invisible to a company like iHeartMedia, which needs individual market revenue in the tens of millions to justify the overhead of corporate management. But for Saga, operating with lean corporate overhead and autonomous local management, a $5--8 million market with high margins and low competition is excellent business. The strategy works because it operates at a scale that larger competitors cannot profitably address.

## 3. Corporate Structure

Saga Communications is structured as a publicly traded company (NASDAQ: SGA) with a dual-class share structure that concentrates voting control among insiders. As of late 2025, the company's ownership profile shows approximately 97% of shares held by institutional investors and insiders [2][6].

The Edward K. Christian Trust holds 886,709 shares as of October 2025, making it the single largest shareholder. This concentration of ownership means that despite being publicly traded, Saga operates with the decision-making agility of a private company. Major strategic decisions -- acquisitions, divestitures, market expansions -- do not require the consensus-building that diluted ownership structures demand [6].

The dual-class structure is common among media companies that want the capital access of public markets without surrendering operational control. The Sulzberger family uses a similar structure at The New York Times. The practical effect for Saga's market managers is stability: the company is not vulnerable to hostile takeovers, activist investors, or the short-term pressure that drives publicly traded companies toward cost-cutting that undermines local operations.

The 97% insider/institutional ownership figure deserves scrutiny. "Institutional investors" in this context includes mutual funds, pension funds, and investment firms that hold shares as long-term positions. These are not day traders or activist funds -- they are investors who have evaluated Saga's strategy, accepted its Goldilocks thesis, and purchased shares as a stable, dividend-paying investment. The thin public float means that Saga's stock price is not a meaningful indicator of the company's health -- volume is too low for efficient price discovery, and the price is more influenced by individual block trades than by market sentiment.

For the Bellingham operation, this ownership structure is invisible in daily operations. The morning show goes on the air, the sales team calls on advertisers, the engineer monitors the transmitter -- none of these activities are affected by the corporate share structure. But the share structure affects the decisions that are not made: the decision not to sell the cluster to a larger buyer, the decision not to cut staff to improve margins, the decision not to replace local programming with cheaper syndicated content. These negative decisions -- the things Saga chooses not to do -- are protected by the ownership structure, and they are what make the Bellingham cluster operationally distinctive.

## 4. Going Public

Saga went public in December 1992. The initial public offering was completed in August 1993, with 685,000 shares sold at $17.75 per share through First Boston Corporation. The IPO raised approximately $12.2 million in gross proceeds [2].

The timing was significant. The 1990s saw rapid consolidation in the radio industry following the Telecommunications Act of 1996, which eliminated most restrictions on the number of radio stations a single entity could own. Large companies -- Clear Channel (now iHeartMedia), Cumulus, Infinity (now Audacy) -- went on acquisition sprees, buying hundreds of stations and consolidating them into nationally programmed networks. Saga's IPO positioned the company to participate in this consolidation wave, but Christian's strategy was selective rather than aggressive [2].

The initial funding of approximately $38.5 million (combining IPO proceeds with initial capitalization) had acquired the radio division of Josephson International, providing the foundation for Saga's station portfolio. From this base, Christian built the company through targeted acquisitions in Goldilocks markets, using the public company's access to capital while maintaining the private company's operational philosophy [2].

## 5. The Bellingham Acquisition

In 1998, Saga Communications acquired KISM (92.9 FM) and KGMI (790 AM) for $9.8 million. This acquisition was the entry point for Saga's Bellingham market presence and established the nucleus of what would become the five-station Cascade Radio Group cluster [4][5].

The $9.8 million price for two stations in the approximately 200th-ranked US radio market reflects the geographic leverage premium. Saga was not buying two stations in a small market. It was buying two stations with coverage that extended far beyond the market boundaries -- including cross-border reach into Canada and southward coverage into the northern Seattle suburbs. The Mount Constitution transmitter site was part of the value proposition, even if the purchase price was negotiated based on conventional market metrics [4].

The seller and intermediate ownership chain between Jones' death in 1972 and Saga's acquisition in 1998 involved multiple transactions. The Broadcasting & Cable Yearbook 2000 (p. D-477) documents the Saga ownership, and Wikipedia's KISM article provides additional context on the intermediate owners. The complete chain of title from Jones to Saga has not been fully documented in a single source -- a gap that reflects the fragmented nature of small-market broadcasting history [5].

The $9.8 million price is worth contextualizing in the 1998 broadcasting market. The Telecommunications Act of 1996 had triggered a buying frenzy in radio. Clear Channel was paying hundreds of millions for major-market stations. Infinity Broadcasting (later Audacy) was consolidating at similar price points. In this environment, $9.8 million for two stations in the 200th market was a modest transaction -- barely a rounding error in the national consolidation wave. But for Saga, whose entire strategy was to avoid bidding wars in large markets, $9.8 million for a dominant position in a Goldilocks market was exactly the right investment at the right price.

The acquisition aligned with Saga's Goldilocks strategy perfectly. Bellingham was large enough to support a multi-station cluster, small enough to be overlooked by national chains, and positioned geographically to reach audiences far exceeding the license city's population. Every element of the Goldilocks thesis was confirmed in the Bellingham market.

## 6. The Five-Station Cluster

Saga's Bellingham cluster expanded from the initial KISM/KGMI pair to five stations (plus translators and HD subchannels), creating a format portfolio that covers the major listener demographics:

| Station | Frequency | Format | Notes |
|---------|-----------|--------|-------|
| KISM | 92.9 FM | Classic Rock | Flagship; Mt. Constitution transmitter |
| KAFE | 104.1 FM | Soft AC | "Christmas Wish" community program |
| KGMI | 790 AM / 96.5 FM | News/Talk | Also on KISM-HD2; founded 1926 as KVOS |
| KPUG | 1170 AM | Sports | Whatcom County sports coverage |
| K-Bay | 98.9 FM (translator) | Classic Hits | KISM-HD3; formerly KBAI 930 AM |

All five stations share studios at 2219 Yew Street Road, Bellingham, WA 98229 -- accessible from I-5 via the Lakeway Drive exit. The shared studio facility is a key efficiency of cluster operations: engineering staff, sales teams, traffic/billing systems, and management are co-located, allowing resources to be shared across stations while each maintains its own programming identity [4][5].

The format diversity is deliberate. Classic Rock (KISM), Soft AC (KAFE), News/Talk (KGMI), Sports (KPUG), and Classic Hits (K-Bay) target different demographic segments without directly competing with each other. A Bellingham business wanting to advertise can buy time on the station that best matches their customer profile, or buy a package across multiple stations for broader reach. The sales team can offer multi-station deals that no single-station competitor can match [7].

## 7. Cascade Radio Group Operations

From the Saga acquisition through January 2025, the Bellingham stations operated under the "Cascade Radio Group" brand. This local brand name -- rather than the Saga Communications corporate name -- reinforced the community identity strategy. Listeners and advertisers interacted with the Cascade Radio Group, not with a Michigan-based holding company. The local brand provided the operational autonomy and community trust that Saga's strategy depends on [4][7].

The Cascade Radio Group had its own general manager, local sales team, and programming staff. National advertising was coordinated through Saga's corporate infrastructure, but local ad sales -- the primary revenue source for small-market stations -- were handled by a Bellingham-based team that knew the local business community personally. This structure mirrors the Goldilocks thesis: corporate resources supporting local execution [7].

The operational structure allowed the Bellingham cluster to function as a self-contained media operation. News coverage for KGMI was generated locally. Morning shows (Brad & John on KISM, for example) featured local hosts discussing local issues. Sports coverage on KPUG focused on Whatcom County teams. The programming was not syndicated national content with local bumpers -- it was genuinely local content produced by people who lived in the community.

The Cascade Radio Group brand served a specific psychological function in the market. Bellingham has a complicated relationship with outside corporate ownership. The city's identity is rooted in independence -- independent businesses, local food systems, community-owned cooperatives. A station group identified as "Saga Communications, Grosse Pointe Farms, Michigan" would have been received differently than one identified as "Cascade Radio Group, Bellingham." The local brand was not deceptive -- anyone who looked up the ownership could find Saga's name -- but it framed the listener's experience in local terms. This is standard practice in small-market broadcasting, but it is also a genuine reflection of operational reality: the Bellingham stations are managed locally, programmed locally, and sold locally. The Cascade (later PNW Media) brand accurately represents the listener's experience even if the corporate ownership is national.

## 8. The 2025 Rebrand: Pacific Northwest Media Group

Effective January 2025, the Cascade Radio Group rebranded as Pacific Northwest Media Group. The rebrand was led by General Manager and Market President Heidi Persson and reflected the cluster's expansion beyond traditional radio into digital properties [7][8][9][10].

The name change signals strategic intent:

- **"Pacific Northwest"** replaces "Cascade," broadening the geographic identity beyond the mountain range reference to encompass the full PNW region the stations serve
- **"Media Group"** replaces "Radio Group," acknowledging that the operation now includes digital properties, websites, podcasts, and marketing services alongside broadcast radio
- **The rebrand does not affect individual station brands** -- KISM is still KISM, KAFE is still KAFE. Station websites, email addresses, and apps remain unchanged

The rebrand was covered by Radio Ink, Inside Radio, and Barrett Media -- three of the industry's primary trade publications -- indicating that the trade press viewed the rebrand as significant within the small-market broadcasting community. The Bellingham Chamber of Commerce also announced the rebrand, confirming its local significance [8][9][10].

The timing (January 2025) positions the rebrand as a response to the streaming era's impact on traditional radio. By defining itself as a "media group" rather than a "radio group," PNW Media Group signals that its future includes digital content distribution, not just broadcast transmission. The five AM/FM stations remain the core business, but the branding creates space for digital expansion without requiring a second rebrand.

## 9. Digital Expansion

PNW Media Group's digital properties extend the cluster's reach beyond over-the-air broadcasting:

- **MyBellinghamNow.com:** Free online news covering Whatcom County. The site provides local news coverage that complements KGMI's on-air news/talk programming, creating a multi-platform local journalism operation [7]
- **PNW Perks:** A half-price deal platform offering discounted local products and services. This digital marketplace connects the station's advertiser relationships with listener spending, creating a direct commerce channel that traditional radio advertising cannot match [7]
- **Digital marketing services:** Design, social media management, and digital advertising services offered to local businesses. This diversifies revenue beyond on-air advertising and positions the media group as a full-service marketing partner for small businesses [7]
- **Podcasts:** "Brad and John -- Mornings on KISM" and "Time Warp Radio" distributed via Apple Podcasts, Spotify, Player FM, and other platforms. Podcast distribution extends the reach of on-air content beyond the FM coverage area and creates an archive of programming that over-the-air broadcasting does not [11]
- **Streaming:** Live audio streaming via TuneIn and station websites, enabling internet-based listening worldwide [11]

The digital expansion follows a pattern common across small-market radio: leverage the existing content creation capability (local hosts, news staff, community connections) into digital platforms that reach audiences beyond the FM signal and generate revenue from sources beyond traditional advertising.

The PNW Perks platform deserves particular attention because it represents a structural innovation, not just a digital extension. Traditional radio advertising is a broadcast model: the station airs a message to its entire audience, and some fraction of listeners respond. PNW Perks inverts this: the station offers a specific product at a specific price to listeners who actively choose to purchase. The transaction is measurable, the revenue is direct, and the advertiser pays only for actual sales, not for impressions. This model converts the station's audience relationship from a one-to-many broadcast into a many-to-one marketplace. For small businesses that find traditional advertising ROI difficult to measure, PNW Perks provides a directly attributable revenue channel.

MyBellinghamNow.com fills a gap left by the contraction of local newspaper journalism. As the Bellingham Herald and other local print outlets reduced their newsrooms, the demand for local news coverage did not diminish -- only the supply did. PNW Media Group's investment in a free local news website leverages the journalism capability that already exists in the KGMI newsroom, extending it to a text-based platform that serves readers who prefer to read rather than listen. The site also generates digital advertising revenue that is incremental to on-air radio advertising.

## 10. Leadership Transition

Edward K. Christian's death in July 2022 marked the end of the founder era at Saga Communications. Christian had been Chairman, President, and CEO since the company's founding in 1986 -- a 36-year tenure that defined the company's culture, strategy, and market position [3].

Leadership passed to Christopher Forgy, continuing the company's operational philosophy without the founder's direct involvement. The transition tested the durability of Saga's decentralized strategy: if the company's success depended on Christian's personal relationships and judgment, the transition would be disruptive. If the strategy was embedded in the company's structure and culture, it would survive the founder's departure [3].

The evidence through 2025 suggests the strategy has survived. Saga continues to operate in its Goldilocks markets, the Bellingham cluster rebranded and expanded, and no major divestitures or strategic shifts have been announced. The company's concentrated insider ownership -- with the Christian Trust as the largest shareholder -- provides continuity of ownership intent even after the founder's death [6].

At the Bellingham level, Heidi Persson's role as General Manager and Market President provides local leadership continuity. The PNW Media Group rebrand was executed under Persson's leadership, suggesting that the local management team has the autonomy and confidence to make strategic decisions without requiring corporate-level approval for every initiative [7][8].

## 11. Small-Market Broadcasting Economics

KISM's business context is the economics of small-market radio in the streaming era. Understanding these economics explains both Saga's strategy and the operational decisions that shape the station's programming and community engagement:

**Revenue structure:** Small-market radio stations derive the vast majority of their revenue from local advertising. National advertising, which flows through agencies and rep firms, favors large-market stations with measurable audiences in the millions. Bellingham's approximately 230,000 metro population is too small for most national ad budgets. This means the sales team must build and maintain direct relationships with hundreds of local businesses -- car dealers, restaurants, law firms, medical practices, home improvement contractors -- and renew those relationships annually [7].

**Cost structure:** The primary costs are personnel (on-air talent, sales, engineering, administrative), transmitter site fees (Mount Constitution Sites, Inc.), music licensing (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC), and studio facility costs. A five-station cluster sharing a single studio facility and engineering staff achieves significant per-station cost reductions compared to five independently operated stations.

**Format strategy:** Each station in the cluster targets a different demographic to avoid cannibalization. KISM (Classic Rock, male 35--54) and KAFE (Soft AC, female 25--54) serve different audiences, so a local business can buy time on both without redundancy. The format diversity maximizes the cluster's addressable advertising market.

**Community engagement as business strategy:** Programs like "Vehicle for a Vet" and "Christmas Wish" are not philanthropic expenses -- they are audience-building investments. Listeners who feel emotionally connected to a station's community programs are more loyal, listen longer, and respond more favorably to advertising. In a small market where listener relationships are personal, community engagement is not a cost center; it is the core product.

**Streaming era competition:** The existential challenge for small-market radio is that every song in KISM's playlist is available on Spotify for $11.99/month without commercials. The station cannot compete on music access -- that battle was lost a decade ago. What the station can compete on is locality, companionship, and civic function. Brad and John discussing the Bellingham High School football game is content that Spotify will never provide. The "Classic Rockforce" email club creates a sense of membership that an algorithm cannot replicate. Vehicle for a Vet mobilizes community action in a way that no digital platform has demonstrated. These are not features that a streaming service could add if it wanted to -- they emerge from the station's physical presence in Bellingham, its decades of community relationships, and its employment of people who live in the city and care about it. The streaming era does not threaten small-market radio's core value proposition; it threatens only its music delivery function, which is the least valuable thing a local radio station offers.

**Talent economics:** In a small market, on-air talent is not compensated at large-market rates. This creates both a challenge (attracting and retaining skilled broadcasters) and an advantage (the talent that stays in Bellingham is there by choice, not by contract). Morning hosts who have lived in the community for years have a depth of local knowledge and personal connection that no amount of talent coaching can replicate. The economic model works because the talent values quality of life -- living in a beautiful Pacific Northwest city with mountains, water, and a close-knit community -- alongside compensation that is modest by industry standards but comfortable by Bellingham standards.

## 12. Ownership Structure and Insider Control

Saga's ownership structure as of late 2025 reflects the company's private-company-within-a-public-shell philosophy:

- **Edward K. Christian Trust:** 886,709 shares (largest single holder) [6]
- **Institutional investors + insiders:** Approximately 97% of total shares [6]
- **Public float:** Approximately 3% of shares

This ownership concentration means that Saga's stock is thinly traded -- daily volume is minimal, and the stock price is not subject to the volatility that characterizes broadly held media companies. For the company's market managers, this translates to stability: there is no risk of an activist investor forcing cost-cutting, no risk of a hostile takeover driving a break-up, and no pressure to hit quarterly earnings targets at the expense of long-term community relationships.

The dual-class share structure ensures that voting control remains with insiders even if the ownership percentages were to shift. This is a deliberately anti-corporate governance structure -- it prioritizes operational continuity and strategic consistency over shareholder democracy. For a company whose strategy depends on long-term relationships and community trust, this structure is functional, even if it would not survive the governance scrutiny applied to Fortune 500 companies.

## 13. The Saga Portfolio Context

As of late 2025, Saga Communications owns or operates approximately 79 FM stations, 35 AM stations, and 80 translator stations across 27 markets in the United States. The Bellingham cluster (PNW Media Group) is one of these 27 market operations [2][6].

The portfolio spans small and mid-sized markets, from coastal communities to college towns to agricultural centers. Each market operates with local management, local programming, and local sales -- connected to the corporate parent through financial reporting, national advertising coordination, and shared best practices, but operationally autonomous in daily decisions.

Bellingham sits at the upper end of Saga's market size range. The cross-border signal reach and the Mount Constitution infrastructure give the KISM cluster a coverage footprint that is disproportionate to its market ranking. Within Saga's portfolio, the Bellingham operation likely generates revenue and audience metrics that would be typical of a larger-ranked market -- a direct consequence of the geographic leverage that Rogan Jones first exploited seventy years ago.

The portfolio context also illuminates why Saga has maintained the Bellingham cluster through multiple industry downturns. Each Saga market is operationally independent -- a bad quarter in one market does not trigger cost-cutting in another. The Bellingham cluster's revenue and audience metrics are evaluated on their own terms, not averaged into a national portfolio where they would be statistically insignificant. This per-market evaluation means that Bellingham's success or failure is judged by Bellingham's standards, not by comparison to larger or smaller markets elsewhere in the portfolio.

For KISM specifically, the Saga portfolio context provides stability. The station has been continuously operated under Saga ownership since 1998 -- twenty-seven years of consistent ownership in an industry where stations change hands frequently. This stability enables the kind of long-term community relationships that make programs like Vehicle for a Vet possible. An owner who might sell the station next year has no incentive to invest in eight-year community programs. An owner who has held the station for twenty-seven years and shows no indication of selling can build programs that compound in community value over decades.
