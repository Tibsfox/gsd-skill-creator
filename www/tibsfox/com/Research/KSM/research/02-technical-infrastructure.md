## 1. Mount Constitution: The Site

Mount Constitution is the highest point in the San Juan Islands at 2,409 feet (734 meters). Located on the eastern lobe of Orcas Island, it rises above the Strait of Georgia with line-of-sight views extending from the Olympic Peninsula to the south, Mount Baker to the east, Vancouver Island to the west, and the Coast Mountains of British Columbia to the north. On a clear day, the visible horizon encompasses nearly the entire Salish Sea basin [1][2].

The mountain sits within Moran State Park, a 5,424-acre preserve donated to Washington State by shipbuilder Robert Moran in 1921. The park's roads, trails, and structures were substantially built by Civilian Conservation Corps crews during the 1930s, making the entire site a layered artifact of Depression-era public works, conservation philosophy, and the industrial infrastructure of broadcasting [1].

For a broadcaster, Mount Constitution's value is expressed in a single technical metric: height above average terrain (HAAT). The FCC calculates HAAT by averaging the terrain elevation in a circle around the transmitter site. For KISM, the HAAT is 744 meters (2,441 feet) -- a figure that reflects the mountain's dramatic rise above the surrounding sea-level waters of the San Juan Islands. This HAAT, combined with 50,000 watts of effective radiated power, produces a coverage footprint that extends far beyond what the station's Bellingham license would suggest [3].

The mountain is accessible by road -- a winding, narrow route through Moran State Park that was originally built by CCC crews. Equipment installation and maintenance require scheduling around park operations, seasonal road closures, and the Washington State Ferry system. Every transmitter component that has ever been installed on Mount Constitution arrived by ferry, then by truck up the mountain road. The logistical challenge is part of the site's history -- and part of the reason that transmitter equipment decisions are made carefully, since replacement is never convenient.

## 2. The CCC Tower

The observation tower at the summit of Mount Constitution was constructed in 1935--36 by Civilian Conservation Corps workers. Over 700 tons of local sandstone were quarried on Orcas Island's north shore, transported to the summit, and assembled into a 53-foot stone tower designed by Roland Koepf under architect Ellsworth Storey [1].

The tower was built as a fire lookout -- one of hundreds constructed across the Pacific Northwest during the CCC era. Its design references medieval European watchtowers, a deliberate aesthetic choice by Storey that distinguished it from the utilitarian wooden lookouts common elsewhere in the region. The sandstone construction gave it a permanence that wooden structures lacked, which would prove significant when the tower found its second life as a broadcasting platform [1].

During World War II, the tower served as a military observation and radio post. Its elevation and line-of-sight to shipping lanes in the Strait of Georgia made it strategically valuable for coastal defense. The military use introduced radio equipment to the summit for the first time, establishing the precedent of the mountain as a communications site [1].

After the war, the tower reverted to park use, but the infrastructure question had been answered: Mount Constitution could support radio transmission. When Rogan Jones was looking for a transmitter site for KVOS-TV in the early 1950s, the mountain's proven capability as a communications platform -- combined with its unobstructed view toward Vancouver -- made it the obvious choice.

The tower still stands. Visitors to Moran State Park can climb its stairs to the observation deck, where they stand surrounded by broadcasting antennas that Jones first placed there seven decades ago. The CCC workers who quarried sandstone from the north shore could not have imagined that their fire lookout would become one of the most significant broadcasting sites in the Pacific Northwest. But they built it to last, and it has.

The CCC construction context is worth dwelling on. These were Depression-era workers -- young men from across the country, enrolled in a federal program that provided food, shelter, and a small wage in exchange for physical labor on public infrastructure projects. The CCC built trails, fire lookouts, lodges, and bridges across the national park and forest system. Most of these structures were utilitarian -- functional enough for their intended purpose but not designed for permanence. The Mount Constitution tower is an exception. Storey and Koepf designed a structure that aspired to beauty as well as function, using local materials that tied the tower to its site. The sandstone came from the island itself, quarried and hauled by hand. The result is a structure that looks like it grew from the mountain rather than being placed upon it.

This aesthetic durability proved practically significant. When broadcasting companies needed a mountaintop facility, the stone tower provided a visible, durable, and iconic structure that anchored the site's identity. Had the CCC built a wooden lookout -- as they did at hundreds of other sites -- it would have rotted within decades, requiring replacement or removal. The stone tower's permanence created continuity: the same structure that housed wartime radio operators in the 1940s supported television antennas in the 1950s and FM radio equipment in the 1960s. Architecture intended for one purpose served purposes its designers never imagined, because they built it well enough to outlast its original function.

## 3. Broadcasting Comes to the Mountain

The first broadcasting installation on Mount Constitution was KVOS-TV's transmitter in September 1954. Rogan Jones moved the television transmitter from its original Bellingham location to the mountaintop specifically to reach the Vancouver, BC market. The elevation gain was transformative: from Bellingham's near-sea-level studios, the signal barely reached beyond Whatcom County. From Mount Constitution's 2,409 feet, the signal cleared the San Juan Islands, crossed the Strait of Georgia, and reached Greater Vancouver [4][5].

The FM radio installation followed in 1960 when Jones launched KGMI-FM on 92.9 MHz. The FM transmitter joined the television transmitter on the summit, establishing the multi-tenant broadcasting facility that continues to operate today. Over the following decades, additional stations installed transmitters on the mountain, creating a shared-site broadcasting infrastructure that serves the entire northwest Washington and southwest British Columbia region [3][6].

The logistics of the 1954 television installation were significant. All equipment traveled by Washington State Ferry from Anacortes to Orcas Island, then by truck up the park road to the summit. Installation crews worked on a mountaintop with no commercial power -- generator-supplied electricity was the only option until permanent power lines were extended to the site. The isolation that made the mountain valuable for broadcasting also made it expensive and difficult to maintain.

Subsequent installations benefited from the infrastructure improvements each predecessor made. Power lines, access road improvements, equipment shelters, and the organizational framework of Mount Constitution Sites, Inc. all reduced the friction for new broadcasters joining the site. This is a pattern common to shared transmitter sites: the first tenant bears the highest infrastructure cost, and each subsequent tenant benefits from the investment of those who came before.

(Source: FCC Licensing and Management System, Facility ID 34469; radio-locator.com) [3]

The Class C designation is the highest power classification for FM stations in the United States. Class C stations are authorized for effective radiated power up to 100,000 watts with antenna heights up to 600 meters HAAT. KISM operates at 50,000 watts -- half the maximum -- but the 744-meter HAAT exceeds the standard maximum, a grandfathered condition reflecting the station's long tenure at the Mount Constitution site [3].

The combination of high power and extreme HAAT produces a coverage footprint that is disproportionate to the station's market designation. Bellingham is ranked approximately 200th among US radio markets. But KISM's signal reaches populations in the top 20 markets (Greater Vancouver) and top 15 markets (Seattle metro northern suburbs). The station's own promotional materials claim a coverage population of approximately 4,000,000 people -- a 42:1 ratio of coverage population to license city population [7].

The auxiliary transmitter authorization at 680 watts provides backup capability during main transmitter outages. When the station operates at auxiliary power, the signal footprint contracts dramatically. Radio enthusiasts have documented that during auxiliary operations, Canadian stations on adjacent frequencies -- normally masked by KISM's full-power signal -- become receivable in northwest Washington [8].

## 5. Transmitter Equipment

The KISM transmitter is a Nautel NV30 -- a solid-state FM transmitter capable of 30,000 watts of continuous output power with HD Radio capability. Nautel (originally "Nautical Electronic Laboratories") is a Nova Scotia-based manufacturer that has become the dominant supplier of FM broadcast transmitters in North America. The NV30 uses multiple hot-swappable power amplifier modules, allowing individual modules to fail without taking the transmitter off the air [9].

Solid-state transmitter technology replaced the tube-based transmitters that served broadcasting for decades. The advantages are significant: reduced power consumption, lower heat generation, modular maintenance (swap a failed module instead of replacing an entire tube), and digital control systems that allow remote monitoring and adjustment. For a transmitter site as remote as Mount Constitution -- accessible only by ferry and mountain road -- the reduced maintenance requirements of solid-state equipment are operationally critical [9].

The NV30 is located in a broadcast facility on Mount Constitution that is separate from the facility housing the co-located KAFE (NV20) and KWPZ (GV40) transmitters. Site engineer "Erling" (identified in Nautel case studies) has commented on the layout differences between the two facilities, confirming that multiple broadcast buildings exist on the summit [9].

The transmitter feeds a directional antenna system mounted on the tower structure at the summit. The antenna pattern is designed to maximize coverage toward population centers -- Bellingham to the east, the I-5 corridor to the south, and the cross-border areas to the north -- while the natural terrain shielding of the mountain shapes the coverage contour in other directions.

## 6. The Nautel GV40 Story

In April 2014, a co-located station on Mount Constitution -- KWPZ (Star 101.5) -- installed the first Nautel GV40 transmitter ever manufactured. The unit bore serial number H0101 and was shipped directly from the NAB (National Association of Broadcasters) 2014 exhibition floor in Las Vegas to Orcas Island [9].

The installation logistics illustrate the challenges of the Mount Constitution site. The transmitter was trucked from Las Vegas to Anacortes, then loaded onto a Washington State Ferry for the crossing to Orcas Island, then driven up the mountain road to the summit facility. Installation crews worked 15-hour days to complete the changeover, minimizing the station's off-air time [9].

The GV40 represented a generational advance over the previous transmitter. Its exciter can output 500 watts of RF power directly, eliminating the traditional intermediate power amplifier (IPA) stage that older transmitters required. This simplification reduces the number of components that can fail and improves the signal path from exciter to antenna. Engineers at the site noted reduced power consumption compared to the previous transmitter even while increasing HD Radio levels -- a significant advantage at a site where electrical power must be delivered up a mountain [9].

The GV40 installation is documented in Nautel's customer stories as a showcase case study. The fact that the first production unit of a new transmitter model was installed on a remote island mountaintop -- accessible only by ferry -- speaks to both the confidence the site engineers had in Nautel equipment and the priority they placed on upgrading the Mount Constitution infrastructure.

## 7. HD Radio Implementation

KISM operates HD Radio, the digital broadcasting standard developed by iBiquity Digital (now Xperi). HD Radio transmits a digital signal alongside the analog FM signal on the same frequency -- a hybrid approach that allows existing analog radios to continue receiving the station while HD Radio receivers access higher-quality audio and additional subchannels [3].

The HD Radio implementation adds digital sidebands to the 92.9 MHz carrier. These sidebands extend approximately 100 kHz on either side of the center frequency, occupying spectrum from roughly 92.8 to 93.0 MHz. At full power, these digital sidebands can cause "splatter" -- interference to stations on adjacent frequencies. This is particularly relevant for KISM because Vancouver's CKYE "Red FM" at 93.1 MHz is only 200 kHz away, and the HD sidebands extend into the guard band between the two stations [8].

The HD Radio system allows KISM to carry three simultaneous program streams:

- **HD1:** The main classic rock format (identical to the analog signal, but at higher audio quality)
- **HD2:** A simulcast of KGMI (790 AM News/Talk), providing FM-quality reception of the AM station
- **HD3:** "98.9 K-Bay" classic hits format

Each subchannel operates at lower audio quality than the HD1 main channel, reflecting the finite bandwidth available for the digital signal. But for the subchannels, the bandwidth trade-off is acceptable -- the alternative for KGMI would be AM-only reception, and for K-Bay, the alternative was an AM station (KBAI 930) that closed in 2024 [10].

## 8. HD Radio Subchannels

### HD2: KGMI Simulcast

KISM-HD2 carries a simulcast of KGMI 790 AM, the news/talk station in the PNW Media Group cluster. The HD2 signal is also available on FM translator K243BX at 96.5 MHz, giving KGMI listeners an FM option in addition to the AM signal. This dual-path availability -- AM 790 plus FM 96.5 via HD2 translator -- is a common strategy for AM stations in an era of declining AM listenership and increasing FM-only vehicles [3][10].

### HD3: 98.9 K-Bay

KISM-HD3 carries "98.9 K-Bay," a classic hits format. K-Bay was previously broadcast on KBAI 930 AM, which operated from 2017 until its closure in 2024. When the AM station went silent, the programming migrated to the HD3 subchannel and its associated FM translator K255DC at 98.9 MHz, 250 watts, Class D (FCC Facility ID 142903) [10].

The K-Bay transition illustrates a broader trend in broadcasting: AM stations migrating to FM translators and HD subchannels as AM infrastructure becomes economically unsustainable. The AM transmitter, tower, and land lease costs exceed the advertising revenue that a small-market AM station can generate. An HD subchannel on an existing FM transmitter costs essentially nothing in additional infrastructure -- the digital bandwidth is already there. The FM translator adds reach for listeners without HD Radio receivers, at minimal power (250 watts) and minimal cost [10].

## 9. FM Translators

KISM's cluster operates two FM translators that extend the reach of its HD subchannels:

| Translator | Frequency | Power | Class | Source | FCC ID |
|------------|-----------|-------|-------|--------|--------|
| K243BX | 96.5 MHz | -- | -- | KISM-HD2 (KGMI simulcast) | -- |
| K255DC | 98.9 MHz | 250W | D | KISM-HD3 (K-Bay) | 142903 |

FM translators are low-power rebroadcast stations that receive a signal and retransmit it on a different frequency. They require no separate programming -- they are purely infrastructure, extending the coverage of existing stations. For the PNW Media Group cluster, translators convert the digital HD Radio subchannels into analog FM signals that any radio can receive, bridging the gap between the digital capability of HD Radio and the analog reality of most car and home radios [3].

The translator model is increasingly common in broadcasting. It allows station groups to offer multiple format options from a single transmitter site, using subchannels for programming diversity and translators for delivery. KISM's three-channel HD Radio setup effectively provides three FM stations from one license and one transmitter location.

## 10. Co-Located Stations

Mount Constitution hosts transmitters for multiple broadcasters. Confirmed stations at the site include:

- **KISM** (92.9 FM) -- Nautel NV30, Classic Rock
- **KAFE** (104.1 FM) -- Nautel NV20, Soft AC
- **KWPZ** (101.5 FM) -- Nautel GV40 (serial H0101, first production unit), Contemporary Christian

The presence of multiple Nautel transmitters at the site -- NV20, NV30, and GV40 -- reflects a standardization on Nautel equipment that simplifies maintenance. A site engineer familiar with one Nautel platform can service all three, and spare parts are compatible across models. For a remote site that requires ferry access, this standardization is operationally valuable [9].

Additional stations may be co-located at the site. Mount Constitution Sites, Inc. manages access and facility operations for all tenants. The shared-site model distributes infrastructure costs (power, access road maintenance, security, generator backup) across multiple licensees, reducing the per-station cost of maintaining a mountaintop transmitter facility.

## 11. Mount Constitution Sites, Inc.

Mount Constitution Sites, Inc. is the facility management company that operates the broadcasting infrastructure on the mountain summit. The company describes itself as "the premier broadcast site company serving the Greater Puget Sound area" with reach extending from Hope, British Columbia to Vancouver, Washington [11].

The company's role is infrastructure management: maintaining the physical facilities (buildings, towers, power systems, backup generators), managing tenant relationships, coordinating maintenance access, and ensuring that the shared site operates reliably for all co-located broadcasters. This is a specialized niche in the broadcasting industry -- tower and site management companies serve as the landlords of the airwaves, providing the physical platform from which multiple stations transmit [11].

The "Hope, BC to Vancouver, WA" coverage claim in their marketing reflects the geographic reality of Mount Constitution's signal reach. From the summit, transmitters can reach the entire I-5 corridor from the Canadian border to the Portland metro area -- a north-south span of approximately 300 miles. The claim also underscores the mountain's significance: it is not merely a local broadcasting site but a regional infrastructure asset serving a multi-state, binational coverage area.

## 12. Signal Coverage Geometry

KISM's coverage geometry is defined by the interaction of three factors: transmitter power (50,000 watts ERP), antenna height (744 meters HAAT), and terrain.

```
GREATER KISM COVERAGE AREA (92.9 FM, 50kW, Mt. Constitution)

                  BRITISH COLUMBIA, CANADA
        Hope .....Victoria.....Vancouver.....Abbotsford
              \         |          |         /
               \   Vancouver Is.  |        /
                \        |        |       /
    .....SAN JUAN.ISLANDS.........|....../......
         Orcas Island             |    /
     *** MT. CONSTITUTION ***     |   /
         (Transmitter, 2409 ft)   |  /
                  |               | /
    Olympic   BELLINGHAM [Studios: 2219 Yew St Rd]
    Peninsula     |               |
         \    Skagit County   I-5 Corridor
          \       |               |
           \  Mt. Vernon          |
            \     |               |
             Everett............../
              \       |
               Seattle (northern suburbs)

    Approx. coverage: ~4,000,000 population basin
    Licensed city: Bellingham, WA (pop. ~95,000)
    Leverage ratio: ~42:1 (coverage pop : license city pop)
```

The 42:1 leverage ratio -- 4,000,000 potential listeners from a station licensed to a city of 95,000 -- is the quantitative expression of the geographic leverage principle. No amount of power on flat ground in Bellingham could produce this coverage. The mountain is the multiplier [7].

The coverage extends asymmetrically. To the north and west, across the open water of the Strait of Georgia, the signal reaches Vancouver and Victoria with minimal terrain obstruction. To the south, down the I-5 corridor, terrain features (Chuckanut Mountain, the Skagit Valley lowlands, the foothills east of I-5) create a more complex propagation environment. To the east, the Cascade Range presents a wall that limits inland penetration.

The result is a coverage contour that follows the water -- north through the San Juans to Canada, south through the Puget Sound lowlands toward Seattle, and west toward Vancouver Island. This water-following pattern is characteristic of mountaintop FM transmitters in the Puget Sound region: water is flat, and flat terrain means unobstructed signal paths.

The coverage geometry also explains why KISM's actual listener base is more concentrated than the raw coverage map suggests. The four-million-person population estimate includes everyone within the signal footprint -- but not everyone within the footprint listens, and not all reception is equal. The strongest, most reliable signal is in the immediate Bellingham area, the San Juan Islands, and the open-water paths toward Vancouver. The southern reaches (Everett, northern Seattle suburbs) receive a weaker signal that competes with Seattle-market stations operating at similar or higher power levels. The coverage population is a theoretical maximum, not an actual audience. The practical audience is concentrated in Whatcom and Skagit counties, where KISM is the dominant classic rock station, and in the San Juan Islands, where the Mount Constitution transmitter provides the strongest FM signal available.

Understanding this distinction between coverage population and actual audience is critical for evaluating KISM's market position. The 42:1 leverage ratio captures the geographic reach, but the station's advertising rates, programming decisions, and community engagement are calibrated to the actual listening audience -- which is centered on the Bellingham market, not on the theoretical four-million-person footprint. The leverage ratio is a measure of the mountain's capability, not a measure of the station's commercial reach.

## 13. The Infrastructure Leverage Principle

KISM's technical story is a case study in what happens when the right infrastructure is placed in the right location and maintained across decades. The components are not extraordinary individually: a 50,000-watt transmitter is common; a solid-state Nautel transmitter is industry standard; HD Radio is available on thousands of stations. What is extraordinary is the combination with the Mount Constitution site [7].

The principle generalizes beyond broadcasting:

- **Position over power:** 50,000 watts on a mountain outperforms 100,000 watts on flat ground
- **Shared infrastructure reduces costs:** Multiple tenants on one mountaintop split the fixed costs of access, power, and maintenance
- **Technology upgrades compound the advantage:** Each transmitter generation (tube to solid-state to HD Radio) adds capability to the same geographic position
- **The site is the asset:** Licenses can change, formats can change, owners can change -- but the mountain does not move

This is the principle that Rogan Jones understood in 1954 when he moved the KVOS-TV transmitter to the summit. It is the principle that Saga Communications valued in 1998 when they acquired the Bellingham cluster. And it is the principle that Mount Constitution Sites, Inc. manages on behalf of every broadcaster who transmits from the highest point in the San Juan Islands.
