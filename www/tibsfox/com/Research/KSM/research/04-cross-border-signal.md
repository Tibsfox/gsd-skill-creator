# The Cross-Border Signal

> **Domain:** International Broadcasting / RF Propagation
> **Module:** 4 -- Cross-Border Signal Dynamics
> **Through-line:** *A CRTC official once compared KVOS-TV to "smallpox" for its effect on the Vancouver advertising market. That was the television signal. The FM signal from the same mountain has been reaching across the same border for sixty-five years, and the dynamics have only gotten more complex. KISM at 92.9 MHz sits 200 kHz from Vancouver's CKYE "Red FM" at 93.1 -- close enough that HD Radio digital sidebands from the American station can mask reception of the Canadian one. This is not a technical accident. It is the physics of geography meeting the politics of spectrum.*

## 1. The Binational Signal

KISM's signal does not stop at the 49th parallel. Physics does not respect political boundaries, and a 50,000-watt FM signal from 2,409 feet above sea level propagates according to electromagnetic principles, not treaty obligations. The Mount Constitution transmitter has line-of-sight to the Greater Vancouver metropolitan area (population approximately 2.6 million), the city of Victoria on Vancouver Island, and communities throughout the lower Fraser Valley and the Gulf Islands of British Columbia [1][2].

This cross-border reach is not incidental. It is the same geographic leverage that Rogan Jones exploited when he moved the KVOS-TV transmitter to Mount Constitution in 1954. The mountain's elevation and position in the San Juan Islands provide an unobstructed signal path across the Strait of Georgia -- open water with no terrain barriers between transmitter and receiver. For FM radio, which propagates as line-of-sight waves, the combination of elevation and water path creates coverage that extends far beyond what the station's Bellingham license and market designation would suggest [1][2].

The station's own promotional materials claim a coverage population of approximately 4,000,000 people. This figure is credible when the Canadian audience is included: Bellingham and Whatcom County (~230,000), Skagit and Island counties (~150,000), the northern Seattle suburbs (~500,000), and the southwestern British Columbia population basin (~3,000,000+). The Canadian component represents the majority of the coverage population -- a situation that is unique among Bellingham-licensed stations and that carries regulatory, commercial, and cultural implications [2].

For a classic rock FM station, the Canadian audience is simultaneously an opportunity and a complication. The audience is there, listening to a signal that reaches them clearly. But advertising to them requires navigating cross-border commercial regulations, and measuring them requires audience research that spans two countries. KISM exists in a regulatory middle ground: licensed as a US station for a US market, but audible to millions of Canadians who may or may not factor into the station's business model.

The binational signal is not unique to KISM. Along the entire US-Canada border, radio and television signals cross the line routinely. Detroit stations are heard in Windsor. Buffalo stations reach southern Ontario. Spokane signals penetrate into British Columbia's interior. But KISM's situation is distinctive because of the population ratio: the Canadian audience within the signal footprint (Greater Vancouver, ~2.6 million) vastly exceeds the American market the station is licensed to serve (Bellingham, ~95,000). In most border situations, the cross-border audience is a secondary market. For KISM, the cross-border audience is the largest single population center within the coverage area. This inversion of primary and secondary markets creates dynamics that are unusual even by border-broadcasting standards.

## 2. US-Canada Frequency Coordination

The United States and Canada share the world's longest undefended border, and they also share the electromagnetic spectrum. Radio waves transmitted near the border inevitably cross it, creating the need for bilateral coordination to prevent interference between American and Canadian stations [3].

Frequency coordination between the two countries is governed by a series of bilateral agreements administered by the FCC (Federal Communications Commission) on the US side and the CRTC (Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission) and ISED (Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada) on the Canadian side. These agreements establish rules for:

- **Frequency assignment:** Coordinating which frequencies are assigned to stations near the border to minimize adjacent-channel and co-channel interference
- **Power limits:** Restricting the effective radiated power and antenna patterns of border-area stations to limit signal penetration into the other country
- **Protection requirements:** Defining the interference protection that each country's stations must provide to the other country's stations
- **Notification procedures:** Requiring advance notice of new station applications, power changes, or technical modifications that could affect cross-border reception [3]

The agreements are technical documents, not policy statements. They do not address the commercial implications of cross-border signals or the audience dynamics that result from border-area broadcasting. They address only the electromagnetic reality: stations near the border will create signals in the other country, and those signals must be managed to prevent destructive interference.

The coordination process is not abstract. When KISM was licensed for HD Radio, the FCC would have evaluated the application against the bilateral agreement's provisions. The HD Radio digital sidebands occupy additional spectrum compared to the analog-only signal, and this additional spectrum use has potential implications for Canadian stations operating on adjacent frequencies. Whether the FCC formally consulted with Canadian regulators on the KISM HD Radio authorization -- or whether it was processed as a domestic modification within existing parameters -- is not documented in public records. But the bilateral framework requires that cross-border impacts be considered, even if the process is administrative rather than diplomatic.

The coordination mechanism also affects Canadian station planning. If a Canadian broadcaster wanted to launch a new station near 92.9 MHz in the Vancouver market, they would need to account for KISM's existing signal from Mount Constitution. The American station's coverage into Canada is a physical fact that constrains Canadian frequency planning -- a constraint that exists regardless of whether anyone in Canada listens to KISM. The electromagnetic environment does not care about nationality; it only cares about power, frequency, and distance.

## 3. The Bilateral Agreement Framework

Under the bilateral broadcasting agreement, the core principle for cross-border FM stations is reciprocal protection. Each country's stations must protect reception of the other country's stations on their territory. Specifically [3]:

- **A station may accept interference** from existing cross-border stations on the other country's territory. This means KISM accepts whatever interference Canadian stations create in the Bellingham area, and Canadian stations accept whatever interference KISM creates in the Vancouver area.
- **A station may not cause new interference** to existing cross-border stations. This means that any increase in KISM's power, change in antenna pattern, or modification of its HD Radio levels must be evaluated for its impact on Canadian stations within range.

The "existing stations" qualifier is critical. The agreement grandfathers the current interference environment. KISM has been transmitting at 50,000 watts from Mount Constitution for decades, and Canadian stations have been assigned frequencies with that signal as part of the interference baseline. Any new Canadian station near 92.9 MHz would need to accept KISM's existing signal. Conversely, any new KISM modification would need to avoid increasing interference to existing Canadian stations.

This framework creates a kind of electromagnetic détente. Both countries' stations operate within a negotiated interference environment, and neither can unilaterally change the terms without the other's agreement. For KISM, this means the station's current operating parameters are protected -- but so are the operating parameters of Canadian stations near 92.9 MHz.

## 4. KISM's Canadian Footprint

KISM's signal into Canada follows the geography of the Strait of Georgia. From Mount Constitution, the signal propagates:

- **North:** Across the San Juan Channel to the southern Gulf Islands (Saturna, Mayne, Galiano, Pender), then across the Strait of Georgia to Vancouver and the lower mainland
- **Northwest:** Across Haro Strait to the southern tip of Vancouver Island, reaching the Greater Victoria area
- **Northeast:** Up the Fraser Valley toward Abbotsford, Chilliwack, and potentially as far as Hope under favorable conditions

The signal quality degrades with distance, but the nature of FM propagation from an elevated site means that the degradation is relatively gradual over water paths. An FM signal crossing open water loses strength primarily through free-space path loss -- a predictable, distance-dependent reduction. The water surface provides a reflective path that can actually enhance signal strength compared to overland propagation, where terrain features create multipath interference and shadowing [1].

The practical result is that KISM is a receivable, listenable station in Greater Vancouver. The signal is not a fringe curiosity picked up by DX enthusiasts with rotatable antennas -- it is a full-quieting FM signal that comes in on car radios during the daily commute across the Lions Gate Bridge. This makes KISM part of the Vancouver radio landscape in a way that the station's US licensing and market designation do not capture.

How many Canadians listen to KISM regularly is unknown. Canadian audience measurement (through Numeris, formerly BBM Canada) does not typically include US border stations in its ratings. American audience measurement (through Nielsen) does not survey Canadian listeners. KISM's four-million-person coverage claim presumably includes the Canadian population within the signal footprint, but the actual listening audience across the border is unmeasured and commercially unmonetized.

This measurement gap is a defining feature of KISM's market position. The station may have a significant Canadian audience -- classic rock has no language barrier, and the format appeals to the same demographic on both sides of the border -- but that audience is invisible to the measurement systems that determine advertising rates. KISM's advertising rates are based on American audience data from Nielsen, which captures only the Bellingham-area listening audience. If the Canadian audience could be measured and monetized, the station's commercial value would be substantially higher. But the regulatory and measurement barriers to cross-border audience monetization have never been overcome, and the Canadian audience remains a geographic fact without a commercial consequence.

The unmeasured Canadian audience also creates an interesting cultural dynamic. KISM's programming decisions are driven by American listener data. If the station were aware that, hypothetically, 50,000 Canadians listen regularly, programming decisions might shift to acknowledge that audience -- Canadian content, cross-border event promotions, advertisers that serve both markets. Without measurement, the Canadian audience is like dark matter in astrophysics: its presence is inferred from the coverage geometry but it cannot be directly observed or acted upon.

## 5. The CKYE Interaction

KISM at 92.9 MHz and CKYE "Red FM" at 93.1 MHz in Vancouver represent the most significant adjacent-channel interaction in the KISM cross-border story. The two stations are separated by only 200 kHz -- one FM channel width -- and both serve the same geographic area (the Strait of Georgia basin) from elevated transmitter sites [3][4].

CKYE "Red FM" is a South Asian-format station broadcasting primarily in Punjabi, Hindi, and Urdu, with weekend block programming in Fijian and Hungarian. It serves Greater Vancouver's large South Asian community, one of the most significant diaspora populations in North America. The station occupies a culturally important position in the Vancouver media landscape that has no equivalent in the Bellingham market [3][4].

The 200 kHz separation between 92.9 and 93.1 MHz is technically sufficient under normal FM broadcasting conditions. FM channel spacing in North America is 200 kHz, and adjacent-channel stations are designed to coexist through the inherent selectivity of FM receivers. A well-designed FM tuner can receive 92.9 MHz without interference from a 93.1 MHz signal, provided both signals are within the receiver's capture range and neither generates excessive out-of-band energy [3].

The problem arises with HD Radio.

## 6. HD Radio Splatter Effect

HD Radio adds digital sidebands to the analog FM carrier. These sidebands extend approximately 100 kHz on either side of the center frequency, occupying spectrum from roughly 92.8 to 93.0 MHz for KISM. At the upper edge, the HD digital energy reaches to 93.0 MHz -- only 100 kHz from CKYE's center frequency of 93.1 MHz [3][4].

In normal FM reception, the 200 kHz guard band between adjacent channels provides adequate isolation. But HD Radio digital sidebands are broadband noise from the perspective of an analog FM receiver. When KISM's HD Radio is operating at full power, the digital energy in the 92.9--93.0 MHz range can "splatter" into the lower sideband of CKYE's 93.1 MHz signal. The effect is most pronounced in areas where both signals are strong -- which, given the overlapping coverage areas of Mount Constitution and CKYE's transmitter, includes a significant portion of the shared Strait of Georgia basin [3][4].

Radio enthusiasts on the RadioDiscussions forum have documented this effect. They report that when KISM operates at full power with HD Radio enabled, reception of CKYE in the Bellingham and Skagit County area is degraded or impossible. The digital hash from KISM's HD sidebands masks the analog signal from CKYE, effectively silencing the Canadian station for listeners in northwest Washington [4].

This is not a problem unique to KISM and CKYE. HD Radio splatter is a recognized issue across North America. The iBiquity (now Xperi) HD Radio system was designed to coexist with analog FM, but the digital sidebands inevitably occupy spectrum that was previously empty guard band. When adjacent-channel stations are both operating at high power -- as is the case with KISM (50,000W) and CKYE -- the splatter effect is most pronounced.

The regulatory implications are complex. The bilateral agreement requires KISM to not cause new interference to Canadian stations. HD Radio implementation arguably creates new interference that did not exist when both stations were analog-only. Whether this constitutes a violation of the bilateral agreement depends on how "interference" is defined in the technical annexes and whether HD Radio sidebands are classified differently from the main carrier.

## 7. Auxiliary Power Observations

When KISM operates at its authorized auxiliary power of 680 watts -- a 73.5:1 reduction from full power -- the station's signal footprint contracts dramatically. Radio enthusiasts have documented that during auxiliary operations, the HD Radio splatter effect disappears and Canadian stations on adjacent frequencies become clearly receivable in northwest Washington [4].

The observations from the RadioDiscussions forum (February 2025) provide empirical evidence of the relationship between KISM's power level and cross-border signal dynamics. At 680 watts, KISM's coverage contracts to essentially the immediate Bellingham area. The Mount Constitution HAAT still provides some elevation advantage, but 680 watts is a fraction of the energy needed to maintain the full coverage footprint. The Strait of Georgia is no longer bridged, the Canadian audience is no longer reached, and the frequency environment around 92.9--93.1 MHz opens up for other signals [4].

These observations are valuable because they demonstrate the sensitivity of the cross-border signal environment to power levels. The difference between 50,000 watts and 680 watts is not merely a proportional reduction in coverage -- it is a qualitative change in the electromagnetic environment across the entire Strait of Georgia basin. At full power, KISM dominates the 92.9 MHz environment for a 100-mile radius. At auxiliary power, it is a local Bellingham station with minimal cross-border impact.

The auxiliary transmitter is authorized for use during main transmitter maintenance or failure. It is not a permanent operating condition. But the periodic use of auxiliary power creates windows during which the cross-border frequency environment changes, and these windows have been documented by listeners and DX enthusiasts who monitor the border-area spectrum.

## 8. The KVOS-TV Precedent

KISM's cross-border signal dynamics are the FM continuation of a story that began with KVOS-TV Channel 12 in 1954. The television precedent is essential context for understanding the current FM situation, because the regulatory, commercial, and political responses to cross-border broadcasting were first articulated in the KVOS-TV era [5][6].

When Rogan Jones moved the KVOS-TV transmitter to Mount Constitution, he immediately reached Greater Vancouver -- a metropolitan area with only one local television station (CBUT, the CBC affiliate). The American programming on KVOS-TV was an instant hit with Vancouver audiences starved for content choice. Jones recognized the commercial opportunity and incorporated KVOS-TV Limited in Vancouver in 1955 specifically to sell advertising to Canadian businesses targeting the cross-border audience [5][6].

The CRTC's response was hostile. Canadian broadcasting regulators viewed KVOS-TV as a commercial threat to the developing Canadian television industry. The "smallpox" comparison attributed to CRTC officials captures the intensity of the regulatory sentiment: KVOS-TV was not merely a competitor, it was characterized as a contagion threatening the health of Canadian broadcasting [6].

The Canadian audience's response was different. Viewers wanted the programming. Vancouver residents adjusted their antennas toward Mount Constitution and watched American programming that was not available through Canadian channels. The audience was exercising choice, and the choice was clear: content diversity trumped national broadcasting policy.

The tension between regulator hostility and audience enthusiasm is the enduring dynamic of cross-border broadcasting. Regulators view cross-border signals through the lens of sovereignty: spectrum is a national resource, broadcasting is a regulated industry, and foreign signals undermine both. Audiences view cross-border signals through the lens of utility: the signal provides content they want, and the fact that it originates in another country is irrelevant to their listening experience. This tension has never been resolved -- not for KVOS-TV in the 1950s, not for KISM in the 2020s, and not for the countless border-area stations worldwide that face similar dynamics.

The KVOS-TV story also established a practical precedent for how governments respond to cross-border broadcasting. Rather than jamming the signal (an option available to authoritarian regimes but not to democracies) or negotiating its reduction (technically possible under the bilateral agreement but diplomatically costly), Canada chose an economic instrument: tax policy. Bill C-58 did not affect the signal -- it affected the economics of advertising on the signal. This approach targeted the commercial incentive for cross-border broadcasting without touching the physical transmission. For KISM, this precedent means that any future Canadian concern about the station's cross-border impact would likely be addressed through economic or regulatory instruments rather than through signal suppression.

## 9. Bill C-58 and Regulatory Response

Canada's most direct regulatory response to cross-border broadcasting was the Income Tax Act amendment known as Bill C-58, enacted in 1976. The bill removed the tax deduction that Canadian businesses had previously claimed for advertising purchased on American border television stations. This meant that a Vancouver car dealer who bought advertising time on KVOS-TV could no longer deduct that expense from their Canadian taxes, while the same advertising purchased on a Canadian station remained deductible [5][6].

Bill C-58 was specifically aimed at KVOS-TV and similar border operations. By making American advertising non-deductible, the legislation effectively imposed a tax penalty on Canadian businesses that advertised across the border. The financial impact was significant: advertising on KVOS-TV became approximately 30-50% more expensive (on an after-tax basis) compared to equivalent advertising on Canadian stations.

The legislation achieved its primary goal of redirecting Canadian advertising revenue toward Canadian stations. KVOS-TV's Canadian advertising revenue declined substantially after 1976, and the station eventually shifted its strategy away from the Canadian market. But the signal continued to cross the border -- physics did not change with the tax code -- and Canadian viewers continued to watch, just without the Canadian advertising revenue that had made the cross-border business model profitable [5][6].

The KVOS-TV story eventually ended in 2016 when the station went off the air after years of declining viability. The transmitter on Mount Constitution that Jones installed in 1954 fell silent, though the site continued to host FM radio transmitters including KISM. The television story is a cautionary tale about the limits of cross-border broadcasting when the regulatory environment turns hostile. But it is also a testament to the durability of the infrastructure: the Mount Constitution site outlived the specific use that created it. The FM transmitters continue to benefit from the access roads, power infrastructure, and facility management that the television operation established decades earlier.

For KISM, Bill C-58 is historical context rather than a direct operational constraint. FM radio advertising is primarily local (Bellingham-area businesses advertising to local listeners), not cross-border. Few if any Canadian businesses advertise on KISM. But the regulatory precedent is relevant: Canada has demonstrated a willingness to use economic tools to manage the impact of American border broadcasting, and that willingness could be applied to FM radio if the cross-border signal dynamics became commercially or politically significant.

## 10. The Binational Audience Profile

KISM's audience is binational by geography, even if it is measured and monetized as a US-only operation. The station's programming -- classic rock, local morning show, community events -- is inherently American in its cultural orientation. But the listeners in Greater Vancouver, the Gulf Islands, and the Fraser Valley experience the station differently than Bellingham listeners do [2].

For a Vancouver listener, KISM is:

- **Background noise from across the border:** A station that happens to come in on the radio, offering a different format from the Vancouver FM dial
- **A cultural window into small-city American life:** The Brad & John morning show discussing Bellingham city council issues, Whatcom County events, and local sports is a view into a community that is geographically close but culturally distinct
- **An alternative to Canadian radio:** Canada's CANCON (Canadian Content) regulations require Canadian stations to broadcast a minimum percentage of Canadian music. KISM is not bound by these regulations, and its playlist may include tracks that Canadian stations cannot program in sufficient rotation to meet CANCON requirements

For a Bellingham listener, the Canadian audience is largely invisible. KISM's programming is not designed for Canadian listeners, its advertising is not sold to Canadian businesses, and its community engagement is focused on Whatcom and Skagit counties. The binational coverage is a geographic fact, not a programming strategy.

This asymmetry -- a station that is designed for one audience but heard by another -- is characteristic of border-area broadcasting worldwide. Radio Free Europe broadcasts were designed for Eastern European audiences but heard (and jammed) by Soviet listeners. Mexican border radio stations (the "border blasters") were designed for American audiences but broadcast from Mexican soil. KISM's situation is less dramatic but structurally similar: the signal serves a purpose in one country while being an uninvited guest in another.

The cultural dimension of the binational audience deserves attention. Vancouver is a multicultural metropolitan area with strong ties to Asian, South Asian, and Indigenous communities. Bellingham is a smaller, less diverse city with a college-town character (Western Washington University). When KISM's classic rock signal reaches Vancouver, it carries Bellingham's cultural perspective into a very different cultural environment. The morning show's discussion of Whatcom County politics, the classic rock playlist that reflects a particular generational and demographic cohort, the advertising for Bellingham businesses -- all of this is contextually foreign to a Vancouver listener in a way that is qualitatively different from how it is received in Bellingham.

This cultural mismatch may actually be part of the appeal for some Canadian listeners. Just as Americans who listen to the BBC appreciate hearing a perspective from outside their own media ecosystem, Canadians who tune to KISM may appreciate the small-city American perspective as a counterpoint to Vancouver's metropolitan media landscape. The cross-border signal provides cultural diversity that no Vancouver station can offer -- not because KISM is trying to serve Canadian listeners, but precisely because it is not.

## 11. Signal Propagation Across Water

The physics of FM propagation across the Strait of Georgia deserves specific attention because it explains why KISM's cross-border coverage is so effective. FM radio (88--108 MHz) propagates primarily as line-of-sight waves. At these frequencies, the radio horizon is determined by the height of the transmitting and receiving antennas, not by the curvature of the earth (as with lower frequencies) or by ionospheric reflection (as with shortwave) [1].

From Mount Constitution at 2,409 feet, the radio horizon extends approximately 60 miles in every direction -- assuming flat terrain at sea level. The Strait of Georgia is flat: it is open water, with no terrain features to block or deflect the signal. This means the signal path from Mount Constitution to Vancouver is essentially free-space propagation with a water-surface reflection component.

Water surfaces are excellent RF reflectors. The reflection coefficient of seawater at FM frequencies approaches -1 (perfect reflection with phase inversion), meaning that the reflected signal nearly equals the direct signal in amplitude. The combination of direct and reflected signals creates a multipath environment, but over open water with a high transmitter, the geometry typically produces constructive interference (signal reinforcement) rather than destructive interference (signal cancellation) [1].

The result is that KISM's signal arrives in Greater Vancouver at a higher signal level than it would if the same distance were covered over land. The water path is not merely a passive gap between transmitter and receiver -- it is an active participant in the signal propagation, reflecting and reinforcing the signal in ways that overland paths cannot match.

Temperature inversions over the Strait of Georgia can further enhance propagation. When a layer of warm air sits above cooler marine air -- common during stable summer weather patterns -- the temperature boundary acts as a waveguide, trapping FM signals and bending them beyond the normal radio horizon. During strong temperature inversions, KISM's signal may reach further than its calculated coverage area, potentially extending into the interior of British Columbia or down the coast of Vancouver Island. These propagation enhancements are temporary and weather-dependent, but they demonstrate that the coverage footprint is not static -- it fluctuates with atmospheric conditions, sometimes exceeding and sometimes falling short of the theoretical calculations.

The marine environment also introduces seasonal variation. Summer months, with calmer seas and more stable atmospheric conditions, tend to produce the most consistent over-water propagation. Winter storms, with their turbulent atmosphere and rough seas, can degrade the water-surface reflection and create more variable reception. Listeners in the Gulf Islands and southern Vancouver Island may notice that KISM reception quality varies with the weather -- a direct physical connection between the Strait of Georgia's conditions and the quality of the radio signal crossing it.

This explains a counterintuitive observation: KISM may be received more clearly in some parts of Vancouver (55 miles northwest across water) than in some parts of the Skagit Valley (30 miles south over terrain). The signal path to Vancouver is unobstructed water. The signal path to Mount Vernon involves terrain features -- Chuckanut Mountain, the Skagit flats, foothills -- that create shadow zones and multipath interference. Geography is not just about distance; it is about what lies between.

## 12. Regulatory Implications for HD Radio

The introduction of HD Radio to KISM's signal creates regulatory considerations that did not exist in the analog-only era. The bilateral agreement between the US and Canada was negotiated based on analog FM characteristics -- 200 kHz channel spacing, well-defined bandwidth limits, and predictable interference patterns. HD Radio changes these parameters [3][4].

The regulatory questions include:

- **Is HD Radio splatter "new interference"?** If KISM adds HD Radio capability to an existing analog license, does the resulting sideband energy constitute "new interference" under the bilateral agreement? The FCC has generally treated HD Radio as a modification within the existing license, not a new service. But the bilateral agreement may classify the additional sideband energy differently [3]

- **Power level coordination:** HD Radio digital power levels are adjustable. The FCC has progressively allowed stations to increase their HD Radio power levels relative to their analog carriers. Each increase extends the digital sidebands further and increases the potential for adjacent-channel interference. For border-area stations like KISM, these power increases have cross-border implications that domestic-only stations do not face [3]

- **Receiver sensitivity:** The HD Radio splatter effect depends partly on receiver quality. High-quality FM receivers with narrow filters can separate 92.9 and 93.1 MHz even with HD Radio sidebands present. Budget receivers with wider filters are more susceptible to splatter. The bilateral agreement does not specify receiver performance standards, so the definition of "interference" is partly a function of equipment quality [3]

- **Asymmetric technology adoption:** Canada has not adopted HD Radio. Canadian stations broadcast analog FM only (or, in some cases, use the DAB+ standard in limited markets). This means the interference is one-directional: KISM's HD Radio creates splatter into Canadian channels, but Canadian stations do not create corresponding splatter into KISM's channel. This asymmetry could become a negotiating point if the bilateral agreement is revisited.

These questions do not have clear answers under the current regulatory framework. The bilateral agreement predates HD Radio, and the technical annexes may not address digital broadcasting modalities explicitly. The practical resolution, as of 2025, appears to be tolerance: both countries accept the current interference environment without formal complaints, and stations on both sides of the border continue operating within their existing authorizations.

The asymmetric technology adoption issue may resolve itself over time. If Canada eventually adopts a digital radio standard (whether HD Radio, DAB+, or a future technology), the interference equation would become bilateral rather than unilateral. Canadian digital sidebands would create splatter into American channels just as American HD Radio sidebands create splatter into Canadian channels. At that point, the bilateral agreement would need to address digital interference in both directions, creating a mutual incentive to establish clear technical standards.

Until then, the practical situation is that KISM's HD Radio implementation creates a measurable impact on the reception of at least one Canadian station (CKYE at 93.1 MHz) in the border area, and this impact is tolerated rather than formally addressed. The tolerance may reflect a pragmatic judgment by both regulatory agencies: the interference is localized, the affected stations can still be received in their primary coverage areas, and the cost of renegotiating the bilateral agreement to address HD Radio specifically would exceed the benefit of resolving a localized interference issue.

## 13. The Geography of Spectrum

KISM's cross-border signal dynamics are ultimately a story about the relationship between geography and spectrum. The electromagnetic spectrum is a shared resource that does not recognize political boundaries. The bilateral agreements between the US and Canada are attempts to impose political order on a physical phenomenon that is inherently anarchic [1][3].

Mount Constitution is the geographic fact that makes this complexity unavoidable. A different transmitter site -- lower, further from the border, with terrain blocking the northward signal path -- would eliminate the cross-border issues entirely. But it would also eliminate the coverage advantage that makes KISM disproportionately valuable. The cross-border complications are inseparable from the cross-border benefits. You cannot have the four-million-person coverage footprint without also having the CKYE interaction, the bilateral agreement constraints, and the regulatory ambiguity of HD Radio in a binational signal environment.

This is the fundamental trade-off of geographic leverage: the same positioning that amplifies your signal also amplifies your entanglements. Rogan Jones understood this when he moved KVOS-TV to the mountaintop and attracted both a Canadian audience and Canadian regulatory hostility. Saga Communications accepted this trade-off when they paid $9.8 million for a Bellingham cluster whose coverage statistics were inflated by cross-border reach they could not commercially exploit.

The signal from Mount Constitution will continue to cross the border regardless of what regulators, advertisers, or station managers decide. The 2,409-foot elevation, the open water path, and the 50,000 watts of ERP create a physical reality that no treaty can alter. The only question is how that physical reality is managed -- through bilateral agreements, technical standards, commercial relationships, and the pragmatic tolerance that characterizes most border-area broadcasting worldwide.
