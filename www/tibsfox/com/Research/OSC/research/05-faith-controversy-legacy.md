## 1. Mormon Theology as Structural Architecture

Card's LDS faith does not merely influence his fiction thematically -- it operates as structural architecture, shaping the deep logic of how his stories begin, develop, and resolve. Understanding this architecture is essential for reading Card with full comprehension, though it is invisible to most non-LDS readers [1].

The key theological frameworks that appear in Card's fiction:

| Theological Concept | LDS Doctrine | Fictional Expression |
|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|
| Plan of Salvation | Pre-mortal existence, mortal testing, celestial reward | Life-stage progressions in *Speaker for the Dead*, Worthing stories |
| Book of Mormon narrative | Chosen family flees corrupt city, wanders, builds new civilization | Homecoming Saga, echoes in Alvin Maker |
| Restoration | Prophetic figure restores lost truth | Alvin as Maker, Ender as Speaker |
| Communitarian sacrifice | Individual sacrifice for community is the highest calling | Throughout all major works |
| Agency (free will) | Central LDS doctrine: God does not compel | Pathfinder, Ender's choice, Alvin's Maker power |
| Temple ordinances | Sacred rituals that bind communities across generations | The Speaking vocation, Crystal City building |

Science fiction scholar Susanne Reid observed that Card's religious background is evident in his frequent messiah protagonists and "moral seriousness." Card himself has stated that speculative fiction is "the genre best suited to exploring theological and moral issues" [2].

## 2. The Plan of Salvation in Fiction

The LDS Plan of Salvation -- pre-mortal existence, mortal testing, and celestial progression -- appears as structural substrate in multiple Card works:

**Speaker for the Dead:** Literary critic Michael R. Collings identified the pequenino lifecycle as a parallel to the Plan of Salvation. The grub stage corresponds to pre-mortal existence, the humanoid stage to mortal life, and the tree stage to celestial glory. The parallel is not decorative but structural -- it determines the plot's central moral question (is the transformation ritual murder or sacred passage?) [3].

**The Worthing Stories:** The somec-mediated immortality creates a parallel to the pre-mortal/mortal transition. Characters who choose somec exist in a state of suspended time; those who refuse it face mortality's full weight. The theological question embedded in the narrative is whether suffering is necessary for moral development -- a question to which LDS doctrine answers affirmatively [4].

**The Homecoming Saga:** The Oversoul's plan for humanity on Harmony parallels the divine plan: a guiding intelligence creates conditions for human development, permits suffering and conflict as necessary for growth, and works through chosen individuals to accomplish purposes that transcend individual understanding [5].

## 3. Book of Mormon Transpositions

The Book of Mormon's core narrative -- a righteous family flees a corrupt city, wanders in wilderness, and builds a new civilization in a promised land -- is the structural template for the Homecoming Saga. The transposition is explicit and systematic [6]:

| Book of Mormon | Homecoming Saga |
|---------------|----------------|
| Lehi receives visions from God | Volemak receives visions from the Oversoul |
| Nephi, the faithful youngest son | Nafai, the faithful youngest son |
| Laman and Lemuel, the rebellious brothers | Elemak and Mebbekew |
| Jerusalem, the corrupt city | Basilica |
| The promised land | Earth (the original human homeworld) |
| The Liahona (divine compass) | The Oversoul's direct guidance |

The transposition raises a genuine literary question: does the story work if you do not recognize its source? Card seems to believe it does -- the Homecoming Saga is published as science fiction, not as Mormon allegory. But Collings and other LDS scholars argue that recognizing the source material significantly enriches the reading [7].

## 4. Joseph Smith Reimagined

The Tales of Alvin Maker present Card's most sustained engagement with LDS founding mythology. Alvin Miller is Joseph Smith reimagined as a frontier folk hero whose supernatural abilities are literally real [8].

The parallel is multilayered:

- **Alvin's seventh-son-of-a-seventh-son status** maps to Smith's prophetic calling
- **The Crystal City** maps to Nauvoo/New Jerusalem
- **The Unmaker** (the cosmic antagonist) maps to Satan/entropy
- **Taleswapper** (Benjamin Franklin figure) maps to the broader American Enlightenment context that shaped early Mormonism
- **The frontier setting** maps to the historical American frontier where Mormonism emerged

Card's treatment of Smith through the Alvin character is neither hagiographic nor critical -- it is transpositional. He takes the essential narrative pattern (gifted individual with genuine power faces persecution, builds community, confronts cosmic evil) and tests what it would look like if the supernatural claims were literally true. The result is one of the most interesting literary engagements with LDS history ever produced [9].

## 5. Communitarian Values

Across all genres and series, Card's fiction consistently valorizes communitarian sacrifice over individual achievement. His heroes do not triumph alone; they build, serve, and sacrifice for communities [10]:

- Ender destroys the Formics but spends the rest of his life serving the pequeninos
- Alvin's Maker power exists to build the Crystal City, not for personal gain
- The Homecoming Saga's entire plot is about a family's service to a larger purpose
- Bean's genius in the Shadow series is redirected from survival to geopolitical service
- The Speaker vocation is defined as service to the community's need for truth

This communitarian emphasis is a direct expression of LDS theology, which emphasizes that individual salvation is inseparable from communal obligation. The eternal family, temple ordinances performed on behalf of the dead, and the communal structure of the ward system all express the same principle: no one progresses alone [11].

## 7. The 1990 Sunstone Essay

"The Hypocrites of Homosexuality," published in *Sunstone* (an independent Mormon intellectual journal) in February 1990, is the foundational document of the controversy. In it, Card argued that laws against homosexual behavior should be maintained "but not enforced" -- kept on the books as a statement of society's values while not being used to prosecute individuals. He argued that the LDS Church's treatment of homosexual members was hypocritical because it demanded celibacy without providing the communal support structures that would make celibacy bearable [12].

The essay's position was complex enough to anger people on multiple sides. Traditional LDS readers objected to the suggestion that church discipline was hypocritical. LGBTQ readers and allies objected to the fundamental premise that homosexuality required any form of legal or social regulation. The essay established Card's pattern of engaging with the topic at length and with evident personal conviction, in terms that became progressively more alienating to liberal and progressive audiences over the following decades [22].

---

## 8. NOM and Institutional Advocacy

Card's appointment to the board of the National Organization for Marriage in 2009 represented a shift from personal opinion to institutional advocacy. NOM was the leading organizational opponent of same-sex marriage in the United States, spending millions on ballot initiatives and lobbying against marriage equality legislation [16].

Board membership conveyed organizational authority and fiduciary responsibility. Critics argued that Card was no longer merely expressing personal opinions but actively participating in an organization whose stated purpose was to prevent legal recognition of same-sex relationships. Defenders noted that NOM's position was consistent with mainstream LDS church teaching and that Card's involvement reflected sincere religious conviction rather than personal animus [23].

Card left the NOM board in 2013, coinciding with the Supreme Court's ruling in *United States v. Windsor*, which struck down the Defense of Marriage Act.

## 9. DC Comics and the Superman Cancellation

In February 2013, DC Comics announced that Card would write a story for *Adventures of Superman*, a new digital-first anthology series. The announcement triggered immediate backlash. A Change.org petition demanding Card's removal gathered over 16,000 signatures. Illustrator Chris Sprouse, assigned to Card's story, withdrew from the project, stating that the controversy had made it impossible for him to do his best work [18].

DC Comics placed the story on indefinite hold, never publishing it. The incident was significant as one of the first major cases in which a creator was effectively removed from a project due to public pressure over personal political positions, predating many similar controversies in subsequent years [24].

Card's response was to note that his *Ender's Game* novels had been praised for their empathic treatment of diverse characters and that he was being judged for his public statements rather than his creative work. Critics countered that financial support (through purchasing his work) enabled continued advocacy [25].

## 10. The Ender's Game Film Boycott

The October 2013 release of the *Ender's Game* film became a flashpoint for the art-vs-artist debate. Geeks OUT organized the "Skip Ender's Game" boycott campaign, arguing that ticket purchases would financially benefit Card and, by extension, organizations opposing LGBTQ rights [20].

Lionsgate, the studio, issued a public statement distancing itself from Card's personal views while promoting the film. Card issued his own statement calling for "tolerance" and arguing that the Supreme Court's marriage equality rulings had made his advocacy "moot" [19].

The boycott's actual impact on box office is debated. The film grossed approximately $125 million worldwide against a $110 million production budget, generally considered a commercial disappointment. However, the film's underperformance had multiple causes (marketing, competition, adaptation quality) and cannot be attributed solely to the boycott [26].

The cumulative professional consequences of the controversy include:

| Domain | Consequence | Year |

| Publishing | Lionsgate public distancing | 2013 |

| Convention invitations | Reduced con Guest of Honor invitations | 2010s |
| Online reception | Wikipedia and fan wiki controversies | Ongoing |

It is important to note what did *not* happen: Card's publisher (Tor Books) continued to publish his work. His major series continued to appear on schedule. His SVU position was maintained. The professional consequences were significant but not career-ending, reflecting the complex dynamics of a case where the creator's work and public positions are in evident tension [27].

## 12. The Art-vs-Artist Question

Card is one of the most prominent test cases for the broader cultural question of whether consumers should financially support creators whose public positions they find harmful. The question has no settled answer, but the arguments on both sides can be stated clearly [28].

**Arguments for separating art from artist:**

- Card's fiction itself often treats marginalized characters sympathetically. The gay character Zdorab in the Homecoming Saga is depicted positively and with genuine empathy [29].
- The empathy thesis of *Speaker for the Dead* -- that understanding another person's full truth is the highest moral achievement -- directly contradicts the dismissiveness of Card's public statements about LGBTQ people.
- Refusing to engage with art because of the artist's personal views would eliminate much of the Western canon (Ezra Pound, Richard Wagner, Roald Dahl, etc.).
- Card's craft contributions (MICE quotient, Boot Camp, IGMS) have value independent of his personal positions.

**Arguments against separation:**

- Financial support for Card's work generates income that has been directed to anti-LGBTQ organizations (specifically NOM).
- The art itself, while generally empathetic, does not extend that empathy to LGBTQ people. Card's fictional universe treats homosexuality as, at best, unremarkable and, at worst, pathological (*Hamlet's Father*).
- The "separate art from artist" argument has historically been used to excuse creators from accountability for real-world harm.
- Readers who are LGBTQ or who have LGBTQ family members may find it impossible to enjoy fiction by an author who has actively worked against their civil rights, regardless of the fiction's quality.

The question remains genuinely unresolved in American cultural discourse. Card is neither the first nor the last creator to generate this debate, but his case is particularly sharp because the tension between his art's empathy and his public positions' exclusion is so stark [30].

## 13. Card's Political Self-Identification

Card's political positioning resists simple categorization. He has described himself as a "Moynihan Democrat" and a "Blue Dog Democrat," positioning himself in the tradition of centrist, culturally conservative Democrats who supported strong national defense and traditional social structures [36].

His actual voting record, as publicly disclosed, complicates this:

| Year | Supported | Notes |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-2008 | Various Democrats | Self-identified as Democrat |
| 2008 | John McCain (R) | Broke from Democratic Party over social issues |
| 2012 | Newt Gingrich (R) | Supported during Republican primary |
| Various | Local candidates | Mixed party support at state/local level |

The self-identification as Democrat while supporting Republican candidates reflects a broader pattern in American politics: the migration of culturally conservative voters from the Democratic to the Republican coalition over the past fifty years, particularly on social issues related to LGBTQ rights and abortion [37].

Card's political columns (primarily in the *Rhinoceros Times* of Greensboro and on "The Ornery American" blog) addressed a wide range of topics beyond social issues, including foreign policy, education, immigration, and media criticism. His positions were generally hawkish on foreign policy, skeptical of public education bureaucracy, and hostile to what he perceived as liberal media bias -- positions broadly consistent with the centrist Democratic tradition he claimed, though increasingly aligned with Republican messaging [38].

## 14. Card in LDS Literary Tradition

Card occupies a unique position in the literary tradition of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. He is simultaneously:

- **The most commercially successful LDS author of literary fiction** (excluding genre-agnostic bestsellers like Meyer)
- **The most theologically engaged LDS novelist**, embedding church doctrine into fiction at structural levels
- **The most controversial LDS public figure in the arts**, whose positions have generated both pride and discomfort within the LDS community

The Association for Mormon Letters (AML) has recognized Card's contributions extensively. His *Saints* won the AML Award for Best Novel in 1985. The AML community has debated Card's work with the same intensity as the broader literary community, but with additional layers: LDS critics must navigate the tension between appreciating Card's theological sophistication and grappling with the public relations damage his controversy has caused to the church's image [39].

Card's relationship to the institutional LDS church is not uncritical. His 1990 *Sunstone* essay criticized church leaders for hypocrisy in their treatment of homosexual members. His fiction frequently features religious institutions that are well-intentioned but flawed -- the Battle School administrators in *Ender's Game*, the Oversoul in the Homecoming Saga, the church in *Saints*. This pattern of loyal criticism from within is characteristic of the *Sunstone* tradition of Mormon intellectual discourse [40].

### Card vs. Other LDS Authors

| Author | Genre | LDS Engagement | Public Profile |
|--------|-------|---------------|---------------|
| Orson Scott Card | SF/Fantasy/Historical | Theological architecture in fiction | Controversial public intellectual |
| Brandon Sanderson | Epic Fantasy | LDS influences subtle; world-building focus | Low-profile; avoids public controversy |
| Stephenie Meyer | Paranormal Romance | LDS chastity themes embedded in romance | Minimal public political engagement |
| Shannon Hale | YA Fantasy/Contemporary | LDS family values in YA context | Moderate public engagement |
| Dave Wolverton/Farland | SF/Fantasy | LDS themes present but not dominant | Primarily known as craft teacher |

The comparison reveals Card's unusual combination: theological depth plus public controversy. Sanderson, who learned from Card, has deliberately maintained a lower public profile on social issues, perhaps learning from his mentor's example [41].

## 15. Legacy Assessment

Card's legacy is multi-dimensional and cannot be reduced to either his achievements or his controversies:

### Literary Legacy

- One of science fiction's most decorated authors (consecutive Hugo/Nebula wins)
- Creator of enduring works that have entered the cultural vocabulary (*Ender's Game* is shorthand for "the game was real all along")
- Pioneer of character-driven science fiction in an era dominated by hard SF and space opera
- Demonstrator of extreme cross-genre range, with significant works in SF, fantasy, horror, historical fiction, and biblical fiction [31]

### Pedagogical Legacy

- The MICE quotient has become a standard framework in writing workshops worldwide
- Literary Boot Camp produced some of the most commercially successful authors of the 21st century
- His craft books remain in print and widely recommended decades after publication
- IGMS provided a professional market for emerging writers for fourteen years [32]

### Theological-Literary Legacy

- Card is the most significant literary artist to emerge from the LDS tradition
- His fiction provides the most sustained literary engagement with Mormon theology in American letters
- The AML (Association for Mormon Letters) has recognized his contributions extensively
- His work demonstrates that religious conviction and literary ambition are not mutually exclusive [33]

### Controversial Legacy

- Card's public statements have made him a symbol of the art-vs-artist debate
- His case has influenced how publishers, studios, and conventions navigate creator controversies
- The controversy has overshadowed his later creative work in public discourse
- His example has been cited in discussions about accountability, boycotts, and the boundaries of tolerance in creative industries [34]

### The Through-Line

Card wrote that in the moment of truly understanding an enemy, you also love them. This is the paradox of research itself -- and the paradox at the heart of this study. To speak Card's life truthfully, as a Speaker would, requires holding the empathy of his fiction alongside the exclusion of his public positions, the brilliance of his craft alongside the damage of his advocacy, the generosity of his teaching alongside the rigidity of his pronouncements. A Speaker does not resolve contradictions; a Speaker presents them. The community decides what they mean [35].

## 16. The Empathy Paradox

The central paradox of Card's legacy deserves its own analysis. The writer who created the most powerful fictional architecture of empathy in modern science fiction -- the Speaker vocation, the Hierarchy of Foreignness, the Battle Room's lesson that understanding your enemy is the prerequisite for defeating them -- is also the public figure who has been widely criticized for failing to extend that empathy to LGBTQ people [42].

This paradox has several dimensions:

**The fictional empathy is genuine.** Card's fiction consistently and convincingly portrays the experience of understanding someone radically different from yourself. The pequeninos, the Formics, Bean's genetically engineered consciousness, Alvin's Maker perception -- these are not cardboard aliens but richly imagined other minds. The emotional power of *Speaker for the Dead* depends on the reader believing that Ender truly comprehends the piggies' perspective, and Card delivers that conviction with full literary force [43].

**The public positions are also genuine.** Card's statements on homosexuality are not off-the-cuff remarks but sustained, considered arguments published over decades. They reflect deeply held convictions rooted in his Mormon theology. The 1990 *Sunstone* essay is not a casual opinion but a carefully structured argument. The NOM board membership was a deliberate institutional commitment [44].

**The contradiction may be structural, not personal.** One interpretation is that Card's empathic capacity is genuine but bounded by his theological framework. He can imagine alien minds with stunning facility because his theology encourages it -- LDS doctrine emphasizes that all beings are children of God, that the Plan of Salvation encompasses all creation. But that same theology provides a specific moral framework for human sexual behavior that excludes homosexuality. The empathy operates within doctrinal boundaries [45].

**The fiction may be more honest than the public statements.** Several critics have noted that Card's fiction is more nuanced than his essays. The gay character Zdorab in the Homecoming Saga is portrayed with genuine sympathy. The themes of tolerance and understanding that pervade the Ender novels are not qualified or constrained. It is possible that Card the novelist accesses a moral imagination that Card the essayist constrains -- that fiction, by its nature, resists the simplifications that advocacy requires [46].

This paradox is not unique to Card. Wagner was an anti-Semite who composed music of extraordinary emotional depth. Ezra Pound broadcast fascist propaganda while writing some of the most innovative poetry of the 20th century. Flannery O'Connor wrote with profound Catholic compassion while holding racist views. In each case, the creative work accesses something the public person does not. The question is whether this discrepancy excuses, explains, or compounds the failure [47].
