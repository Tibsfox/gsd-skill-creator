## 1. The Salish Sea

The Salish Sea is the name formally adopted in 2009 (by the Washington State Board on Geographic Names) and 2010 (by the British Columbia Geographical Names Board) for the inland marine waters of the Pacific Northwest -- encompassing Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the Strait of Georgia, and the San Juan Islands archipelago. The name honors the Coast Salish peoples who have inhabited the shores and islands of these waters for at least 10,000 years [1][2].

The Salish Sea is one of the most complex estuarine systems in the world. It receives freshwater input from major rivers -- the Fraser, Skagit, Snohomish, Duwamish/Green, Puyallup, and Nisqually among them -- and exchanges saltwater with the Pacific Ocean through the Strait of Juan de Fuca. The mixing of fresh and salt water creates a layered water column with distinct temperature, salinity, and oxygen profiles that support extraordinarily productive marine ecosystems [1][2].

The sea's significance to the Resonant Landscapes project is as a unifying element. Every landscape documented in the preceding modules drains into the Salish Sea or connects to it through maritime routes. The Sound Garden sits on its shore (Lake Washington connects to the sea via the Ship Canal and Ballard Locks). Glacier Peak's meltwater reaches the sea via the Suiattle, Sauk, Skagit, and Columbia river systems. The Olympic coast borders the Strait of Juan de Fuca. The sea is the medium through which all the resonant frequencies converge [1][2].

## 2. Mount Constitution -- The Highest Point

Mount Constitution rises to 2,409 feet (734 m) on Orcas Island, making it the highest point in the San Juan Islands. The mountain is located within Moran State Park and is accessible by road (a winding, paved mountain road from the park entrance) as well as by several hiking trails that climb through old-growth forest to the summit [3][4].

The summit view is one of the most comprehensive panoramas in the Pacific Northwest. On clear days, the 360-degree view encompasses Mount Baker (10,781 feet) to the east, the Olympic Mountains to the south, Vancouver Island and the Coast Mountains of British Columbia to the north, and the San Juan Islands archipelago spread across the Salish Sea in every direction. The view captures the entire geographic scope of the Resonant Landscapes project -- from the Cascade volcanoes to the Pacific coast, from the Canadian border to the Seattle skyline [3][4].

The mountain's name is one of the few surviving designations from the United States Exploring Expedition of 1838-42, commanded by Lieutenant Charles Wilkes. Wilkes named the peak after the USS Constitution -- "Old Ironsides" -- the famous frigate of the War of 1812. Most of Wilkes's place names in the San Juan Islands were later overwritten by British Admiralty charts, but "Mount Constitution" persisted [3][5].

## 3. The CCC Tower

The stone observation tower at the summit of Mount Constitution was designed by Seattle architect Ellsworth Storey (1879-1960) and built by 28 men of the 4768th Company of the Civilian Conservation Corps, primarily from Fort Snelling, Minnesota. Construction ran from August 1935 to summer 1936 [3][4].

The tower is built of native sandstone quarried from the northern shore of Orcas Island. The architectural inspiration was the ancient watchtowers of the Caucasus Mountains -- a reference that is simultaneously scholarly and romantic. Storey, who was known for his residential designs in the Pacific Northwest Craftsman style, created a structure that combines medieval European fortress architecture with the materials and setting of the Pacific Northwest [3][4].

The CCC was one of the most successful New Deal programs, employing over 3 million young men between 1933 and 1942 in conservation and public works projects across the United States. The CCC built trails, shelters, bridges, and structures in national and state parks throughout the country. The Mount Constitution tower is one of the finest surviving examples of CCC craftsmanship in Washington State [3][4].

The tower connects architecturally to Yamasaki's Pacific Science Center (Module 1) -- both are structures where an architect's vision transforms utilitarian purpose (observation, exhibition) into something approaching art. Storey's Caucasian watchtower and Yamasaki's Gothic arches both draw on distant architectural traditions to create structures that feel both ancient and modern, both local and universal. The stone tower on a mountain summit and the steel arches on a World's Fair campus are different expressions of the same aspiration -- to mark a place as significant through the quality of what is built upon it [3][4].

## 4. Moran State Park

Moran State Park encompasses over 5,252 acres -- the largest public recreation area in the San Juan Islands and one of the largest state parks in Washington. The park was dedicated in 1921 and has been continuously operated by Washington State Parks since that date. The park contains several freshwater lakes (Cascade Lake, Mountain Lake, Summit Lake, Twin Lakes), old-growth western hemlock forest, and over 30 miles of trails [3][4].

The ecological significance of Moran State Park lies in its preservation of old-growth forest in a region where most original forest was logged in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The park's western hemlock and Douglas fir stands include trees exceeding 500 years in age, with the structural complexity -- snags, nurse logs, canopy gaps, multi-layered canopy -- that defines old-growth conditions. These old-growth stands connect directly to the forest ecology documented in the LFR (Living Forest) research module [3][4].

The park's freshwater lakes provide habitat for resident and migratory waterfowl, river otter, beaver, and several fish species including cutthroat and rainbow trout. Cascade Lake, the park's largest lake at 172 acres, is accessible by road and popular for swimming, fishing, and non-motorized boating. Mountain Lake, higher on the mountain, offers a more secluded experience with backcountry camping along its shore [3][4].

## 5. Robert Moran -- The Shipbuilder Mayor

Robert Moran (1857-1943) was a shipbuilder, industrialist, and politician who served as mayor of Seattle from 1888 to 1890 -- a period that included the Great Seattle Fire of June 6, 1889. Moran's leadership during and after the fire, when he organized the rebuilding of the city's commercial district in brick and stone, established his reputation as a decisive public figure [3][5].

Moran's shipbuilding company, the Moran Brothers Company, operated from a yard on the Seattle waterfront and built vessels for commercial, military, and government use. The yard's most famous product was the sternwheeler steamship fleet that served the Yukon Gold Rush in 1897-98. Moran prospered during the Gold Rush boom but subsequently faced health problems that led his doctors to predict he had only a few years to live [3][5].

In 1906, Moran retired to Orcas Island, eventually acquiring 7,800 acres of mountain and shoreline property. He built a grand estate -- Rosario, now a resort -- overlooking East Sound. As his health improved (he lived to age 86, outliving his doctors' predictions by decades), he donated the bulk of his Orcas Island property to the state of Washington for use as a public park. The donation of over 5,000 acres in 1921 created Moran State Park [3][5].

Moran's story connects to the broader theme of industrial wealth and landscape preservation that runs through PNW history. Like the Weyerhaeuser timberlands documented in the LFR module, Moran's land represented the conversion of industrial capital into preserved landscape. The difference is that Moran chose public donation rather than continued private exploitation -- a decision that preserved old-growth forest and public access on Orcas Island for the subsequent century [3][5].

## 6. San Juan Islands -- Naming Layers

The San Juan Islands carry three layers of European naming -- Spanish, British, and American -- overlaid on the Coast Salish names that preceded all of them. These naming layers document the successive waves of colonial encounter that shaped the region's political geography [5][6].

Spanish exploration under Francisco de Eliza in 1791, financed by Viceroy Juan Vicente de Guemes Padilla Horcasitas y Aguayo, produced the first European charts of the islands. The name "San Juan" was applied by the Spanish to the entire archipelago. "Orcas" derives from a shortened form of "Horcasitas" -- the viceroy's family name -- not from orca whales, despite the common assumption. Lopez Island was named for Gonzales Lopez de Haro, Eliza's pilot [5][6].

The British Admiralty's Henry Kellett reorganized the charts in 1847, replacing many American names that had been assigned by Lieutenant Charles Wilkes during the United States Exploring Expedition of 1838-42. Wilkes had named Orcas Island "Hull Island" after Commodore Isaac Hull; this name did not survive the British revision. However, several of Wilkes's names persisted, including Mount Constitution (after the USS Constitution) and Mount Baker (named earlier by George Vancouver in 1792 after his third lieutenant, Joseph Baker) [5][6].

The naming layers create a palimpsest -- a surface that has been written on multiple times, with earlier inscriptions partially visible beneath later ones. The Coast Salish names, where they survive in the historical record, describe functional relationships with the land (fishing grounds, canoe passages, camping sites). The European names commemorate distant dignitaries who never visited the islands. The contrast between these naming traditions reveals fundamentally different relationships with landscape -- one based on use and knowledge, the other on political claim and cartographic convention [5][6].

## 7. The Pig War

The Pig War of 1859 is one of the more improbable episodes in Pacific Northwest history -- a military confrontation between the United States and Great Britain triggered by the shooting of a pig owned by the Hudson's Bay Company on San Juan Island by an American settler named Lyman Cutlar [5][6].

The underlying dispute was not about the pig but about the international boundary. The Oregon Treaty of 1846 established the border at the 49th parallel but left ambiguous the boundary through the San Juan Islands, where the 49th parallel runs through the archipelago. Both nations claimed the islands. When Cutlar shot the pig (which had been eating his potatoes), the British threatened arrest; the Americans responded by landing troops under Captain George Pickett (later of Civil War fame). Both sides escalated, eventually deploying significant naval and land forces -- 461 Americans with 14 cannons and five British warships with over 2,000 men [5][6].

Cooler heads prevailed. No shots were fired beyond the initial pig incident. The two sides established a joint military occupation that lasted from 1859 to 1872, when Kaiser Wilhelm I of Germany, serving as arbitrator, ruled in favor of the United States. The American Camp and British Camp on San Juan Island are now preserved as San Juan Island National Historical Park [5][6].

The Pig War's relevance to the Resonant Landscapes project lies in its illustration of the jurisdictional complexity that characterizes the entire PNW landscape. National borders, state lines, county boundaries, reservation limits, park boundaries, wilderness boundaries -- the PNW is a landscape where overlapping jurisdictions create a complex governance mosaic that shapes access, management, and interpretation of the land. The Pig War was an extreme case of jurisdictional ambiguity, but the principle extends to every landscape in the project [5][6].

## 8. Coast Salish Peoples

The Coast Salish peoples -- notably the Lummi and Samish tribes -- are the original inhabitants of the San Juan Islands and the surrounding Salish Sea shores. The Coast Salish cultural area extends from the northern Puget Sound region through the Strait of Georgia to the central coast of British Columbia, encompassing dozens of distinct nations that share related languages, cultural practices, and material traditions [6][7].

The Lummi Nation, whose traditional territory includes the San Juan Islands and the adjacent mainland, maintains a reservation near Bellingham, Washington. The Samish Indian Nation, which was not federally recognized until 1996 (after decades of legal struggle), has traditional ties to the San Juan Islands and the Samish Bay area. The Straits Salish peoples -- including the Klallam (S'Klallam), Songhees, and Saanich -- occupied the shores of the Strait of Juan de Fuca and southern Vancouver Island [6][7].

Coast Salish culture is characterized by cedar longhouse architecture, reef net fishing technology (a method of salmon harvesting unique to the Straits Salish), potlatch ceremonies, and a rich artistic tradition distinct from the better-known Northwest Coast art of the Tlingit, Haida, and Kwakwaka'wakw peoples to the north. Coast Salish art emphasizes geometric forms, woven textiles, and carved house posts rather than the formline design system of the northern nations [6][7].

The relationship between Coast Salish peoples and the Salish Sea is not merely geographic -- it is ecological, economic, spiritual, and political. The sea provided salmon, shellfish, marine mammals, and waterfowl that formed the foundation of Coast Salish economies. Canoe travel across the sea connected communities in a network of trade, kinship, and ceremony that functioned as a maritime civilization as sophisticated as any in the pre-contact Americas [6][7].

## 9. Madrona Point -- A Burial Ground Returned

Madrona Point on Orcas Island is a traditional Lummi burial ground that was controversially sold out of tribal hands in 1890 during a period when federal and state policies actively undermined indigenous land tenure. The site was developed for private use, with the burial ground's cultural significance unacknowledged in property records [6][7].

In 1989, a coalition of supporters raised $2.2 million to purchase Madrona Point out of private ownership and return it to the Lummi Nation. The transfer represented one of the early successful efforts in the Pacific Northwest to restore culturally significant sites to indigenous stewardship through market-based acquisition rather than litigation [6][7].

The Madrona Point story connects to the broader themes of land ownership, cultural sovereignty, and the relationship between economic value and cultural value that run through the Resonant Landscapes project. The Ozette archaeological site (Module 3) was protected through a negotiated agreement between the Makah Nation and a university. The Sheldon Jackson artifacts (Module 5) were dispersed across institutions without tribal consent. Madrona Point was recovered through financial purchase. Each case illustrates a different mechanism by which indigenous cultural heritage is preserved, lost, or restored in the context of American property law [6][7].

## 10. Reef Net Fishing -- A Unique Technology

The Straits Salish peoples (S'Klallam, Lummi, Samish, Saanich) developed reef net fishing -- a method of salmon harvesting that is unique in the world and represents one of the most sophisticated fishing technologies ever developed by any culture [6][7].

Reef nets are stationary nets suspended between two anchored canoes at traditional fishing locations where salmon pass in predictable patterns. The nets are set at specific sites that were identified and refined over generations -- locations where underwater topography channeled migrating salmon into narrow passages. Spotter platforms allowed fishers to see approaching schools and time the net haul precisely [6][7].

The reef net sites were owned by specific families and passed down through generations. They were among the most valuable properties in Coast Salish society -- more valuable than longhouses, canoes, or ceremonial regalia. The knowledge of where to set the nets, when the salmon would pass, how the currents and tides affected net position, and how to read the underwater topography was as specialized and carefully guarded as any technological knowledge in human history [6][7].

The Lummi Nation operates one of the last remaining reef net fisheries in the world, maintaining the traditional technology while adapting it to contemporary fisheries management requirements. The fishery is both a cultural practice and a practical salmon harvesting method that is notable for its selectivity -- reef nets allow non-target species and runs to be released alive, making them one of the most sustainable fishing technologies ever developed [6][7].

Reef net fishing connects the Islands and Salish Sea module to the broader salmon heritage documented in the SAL cross-reference. The salmon that pass through the reef net sites are the same fish that spawn in the rivers draining the Cascade Range (Module 2's hinterland), the Olympic Peninsula (Module 3), and the broader Puget Sound watershed. The reef net sites are nodes in a continental-scale migration network that connects alpine snowfields to the Pacific Ocean [6][7].

## 11. Geological Origins

The San Juan Islands are geologically distinct from the surrounding Puget Sound lowland and the Cascade Range. The islands are composed primarily of pre-Tertiary rocks -- metamorphosed volcanic and sedimentary formations that predate the current Cascade volcanic arc. The geology includes pillow basalts, chert, limestone, and metamorphic schists that record a complex history of oceanic plate accretion and tectonic transport [3][8].

The islands' topography was shaped by the Cordilleran Ice Sheet during the Pleistocene glaciations. The ice sheet, which covered the entire Puget Sound lowland to a depth of over 3,000 feet approximately 16,000 years ago, carved the channels between islands, rounded the bedrock summits, and deposited glacial till across the landscape. The characteristic form of the islands -- rounded hilltops, steep shorelines, sheltered bays -- is primarily a glacial landform [3][8].

The sandstone quarried for the Mount Constitution tower came from formations exposed on the northern shore of Orcas Island -- rocks that were deposited as marine sediment, lithified, uplifted by tectonic forces, glacially polished, and finally shaped by human hands into the watchtower that crowns the island's highest point. The stone carries the geological history of the island in its mineral composition, a physical record readable to anyone who knows the language of stratigraphy [3][8].

## 12. Orca Whales and the Marine Ecosystem

The Salish Sea is home to the Southern Resident Killer Whale (SRKW) population -- one of the most studied and most endangered marine mammal populations in the world. The three pods (J, K, and L pods) are a genetically distinct community of orcas that have inhabited the Salish Sea for thousands of years, specializing in Chinook salmon as their primary food source [1][2].

The SRKW population was listed as endangered under the Endangered Species Act in 2005. The population has declined from an estimated 98 individuals in 1995 to approximately 73 as of the most recent census. The primary threats are prey depletion (Chinook salmon runs have declined dramatically), vessel noise and disturbance (the whales' echolocation and communication are disrupted by boat traffic), and persistent organic pollutants (PCBs, PBDEs) that accumulate in the food chain [1][2].

The orcas connect the marine ecosystem of Module 4 to the salmon heritage documented in the SAL cross-reference. The SRKW depend on Chinook salmon; the salmon depend on intact river habitat; the river habitat depends on the forest canopy documented in COL, CAS, and LFR. The orca-salmon-forest connection is one of the most powerful examples of ecological interconnection in the PNW -- a single thread that runs from the canopy to the canyon to the coast to the open sea [1][2].

The San Juan Islands are a primary habitat for J pod during summer months. Lime Kiln Point State Park on San Juan Island is the premier land-based whale-watching site in the Pacific Northwest, where orcas regularly pass within a few hundred meters of shore as they travel through Haro Strait. The experience of watching orcas from shore -- hearing the explosive exhalation as a whale surfaces, seeing the six-foot dorsal fin of an adult male cut through the current -- is one of the defining wildlife encounters of the region [1][2].

## 13. Victoria and Vancouver -- Northern Anchors

The Salish Sea extends north from the San Juan Islands through the Strait of Georgia to the cities of Victoria (British Columbia, on Vancouver Island) and Vancouver (British Columbia, on the mainland). These two cities serve as the northern cultural anchors of the Salish Sea system, completing the maritime arc that begins at Seattle's waterfront and extends through the San Juan Islands to British Columbia [1][2].

Victoria, the capital of British Columbia, sits at the southern tip of Vancouver Island across the Strait of Juan de Fuca from the Olympic Peninsula. The city's relationship to the Salish Sea is similar to Seattle's -- an urban center built on indigenous land at the edge of a maritime landscape that extends in every direction. The Royal BC Museum in Victoria houses one of the finest collections of Northwest Coast indigenous art in the world [1][2].

Vancouver, on Burrard Inlet at the mouth of the Fraser River, is the largest city on the Salish Sea and the cultural capital of the Pacific Northwest's Canadian side. The Museum of Anthropology at the University of British Columbia, designed by Arthur Erickson, holds the Bill Reid collection and other major Northwest Coast art assemblages. The city's relationship to the Fraser River -- the largest salmon-producing river system on the Pacific coast -- connects it to the salmon heritage that underlies Coast Salish culture across the entire Salish Sea [1][2].

The international border that divides the Salish Sea between Canada and the United States is a political artifact that the marine ecosystem ignores. Orcas, salmon, seabirds, and tidal currents cross the border continuously. The Coast Salish peoples whose territories straddle the border have advocated for a more integrated approach to Salish Sea management that recognizes the ecological unity of the system -- a unity that the name "Salish Sea" itself was intended to express [1][2].

## 16. Deception Pass -- The Threshold

Deception Pass State Park, located at the northern tip of Whidbey Island where it nearly touches Fidalgo Island, provides a dramatic illustration of the tidal forces that animate the Salish Sea. The pass is a narrow channel through which enormous volumes of water flow with each tidal cycle, creating currents that can exceed 8 knots during peak flow [1][3].

The name "Deception Pass" was given by George Vancouver in 1792, who initially mistook Whidbey Island for a peninsula connected to the mainland. When he discovered the narrow passage that proved Whidbey was an island, he named it for the deception. The pass is spanned by a bridge (completed in 1935, also a CCC project) that is one of the most photographed structures in Washington State [1][3].

The CCC connection links Deception Pass to the Mount Constitution tower (Section 3) -- both are products of the same New Deal conservation program, both were built by young men from across the country who came to the Pacific Northwest during the Depression, and both have become iconic landmarks that define the experience of their respective locations. The CCC's legacy in Washington State's parks system is profound: dozens of trails, shelters, bridges, and structures built in the 1930s remain in active use today, a testament to the quality of the craftsmanship and the durability of the materials [1][3][4].

Deception Pass sits at the boundary between the Salish Sea proper and the Strait of Juan de Fuca -- the transition zone where the inland waters meet the open Pacific. The ecological communities on either side of the pass differ significantly: the protected waters east of the pass support eelgrass meadows, oyster beds, and calm-water species, while the exposed waters to the west support kelp forests and species adapted to stronger currents and wave action. The pass itself is a mixing zone where these two ecosystems overlap [1][3].

The tidal flows through Deception Pass produce visible phenomena -- standing waves, whirlpools, tide rips, and boils -- that make the otherwise invisible force of tidal current dramatic and tangible. Like Hollis's sculpture, which makes wind visible through sound, the pass makes tidal current visible through water motion. Both are places where a natural force that is normally imperceptible becomes manifest to the senses [1][3].

## 17. The Salish Sea as Connective Medium

The Salish Sea is not merely a body of water between landmasses. It is the connective medium of the entire Pacific Northwest -- the shared element that links the urban waterfront of Seattle to the rural islands of the San Juans, the industrial ports of Tacoma and Vancouver to the fishing villages of the San Juan Islands and the Olympic coast. Every river in western Washington and southwestern British Columbia drains into the Salish Sea, making it the terminal repository for the runoff from every landscape documented in this project [1][2].

The sea carries more than water. It carries salmon, nutrients, pollution, sediment, sound, and cultural meaning. Coast Salish canoe journeys -- revived in the modern era through the annual Tribal Canoe Journeys that bring nations from across the Pacific Northwest together for multi-day paddling events -- use the sea as a transportation network, a gathering space, and a ceremonial medium. The canoe journeys are one of the most significant contemporary expressions of Coast Salish cultural vitality [6][7].

The sea also carries the acoustic environment documented throughout the Resonant Landscapes project. The underwater soundscape of the Salish Sea includes orca vocalizations (echolocation clicks and social calls), ship noise (the Port of Seattle and Port of Vancouver are among the busiest on the Pacific coast), the clicking of snapping shrimp, and the low-frequency rumble of tidal currents. This underwater acoustics connects to the aeolian acoustics of Module 1 and the coastal acoustics of Module 3 -- different frequency ranges of the same resonant system [1][2].

## 18. The San Juan Islands Archipelago Ecology

The San Juan Islands archipelago comprises over 450 islands, rocks, and reefs (depending on the definition of "island" -- the count varies between 172 and 768 depending on whether tidal rocks are included). Only about 60 are named, and approximately 20 are inhabited year-round. The islands represent one of the most ecologically diverse marine environments in the Pacific Northwest [1][3].

The island ecosystems are characterized by a rain shadow effect -- the Olympic Mountains to the southwest intercept most of the moisture-laden Pacific air masses, leaving the San Juan Islands with significantly less rainfall than the western slopes of the Olympics. The town of Friday Harbor on San Juan Island receives approximately 29 inches of annual precipitation, compared to 140+ inches in the Hoh Rain Forest just 100 miles to the southwest. This drier climate supports plant communities that differ dramatically from the western Washington norm: Garry oak (Oregon white oak) savannas, prickly pear cactus (the northernmost occurrence in the Pacific Northwest), and Mediterranean-like grasslands that are unique in Washington State [1][3].

The marine environment is equally distinctive. The San Juan Islands lie at the confluence of multiple marine currents, creating upwelling zones that bring nutrient-rich deep water to the surface. These upwelling zones support dense populations of invertebrates, fish, seabirds, and marine mammals. The kelp forests around the islands -- dominated by bull kelp (*Nereocystis luetkeana*) -- are among the most productive marine ecosystems in the temperate world [1][3].

Bald eagle density in the San Juan Islands is among the highest in the lower 48 states, a recovery from near-extirpation during the DDT era. Great blue heron rookeries occupy old-growth stands on several islands. Tufted puffins, rhinoceros auklets, and pigeon guillemots nest on the outer islands and rocks. Harbor seals haul out on rocky islets throughout the archipelago. The marine bird and mammal communities connect the islands to the broader PNW wildlife documented in the AVI and MAM cross-references [1][3].

The San Juan Islands National Wildlife Refuge, established in 1914, encompasses 83 rocks, reefs, and islands set aside for nesting seabirds and marine mammals. The refuge is managed by the US Fish and Wildlife Service and is closed to public access to protect nesting habitat. These protected rocks represent the smallest and most specific jurisdictional unit in the PNW parks hierarchy -- individual rocks set aside for individual species by federal mandate [1][3].

## 19. The Observation Tower as Cultural Form

The stone observation tower on Mount Constitution belongs to a tradition of summit structures that extends from ancient watchtowers to modern fire lookouts. The function is always the same -- to elevate the observer above the surrounding terrain for a broader view -- but the cultural meaning changes with each iteration [3][4][10].

Storey's choice to model the tower on Caucasian watchtowers was an act of architectural imagination that transported a defensive military form from the mountains of Georgia and Azerbaijan to the mountains of a Pacific island. The original watchtowers served a practical purpose -- surveillance against invading armies in mountain terrain -- but Storey transformed the form into a purely contemplative structure. The Mount Constitution tower has no defensive function; it exists solely to frame the view [3][4][10].

This transformation from practical to contemplative mirrors the transformation of the NOAA campus from military (Naval Air Station) to scientific (NOAA research center) to artistic (Art Walk). In both cases, structures built for one purpose are repurposed for another -- the watchtower becomes a viewing platform, the naval base becomes a sculpture park. The landscape accumulates meaning through these transformations, each layer adding to the richness of the place [3][4].

The tower also connects to the fire lookout tradition documented in Module 2 (Miners Ridge lookout near Image Lake). Fire lookouts were the PNW's version of watchtowers -- elevated structures built for surveillance, in this case surveillance against fire rather than invading armies. Both the CCC tower on Mount Constitution and the fire lookout on Miners Ridge serve the same fundamental function: they place a human observer at a high point where the landscape becomes visible as a whole, where the relationships between parts become apparent, where the system reveals itself [3][4][6].

From the top of the Mount Constitution tower, the Resonant Landscapes system becomes visible as a geographic unity. The Cascade Range, the Olympic Mountains, the Salish Sea, the San Juan Islands, the urban skyline of Seattle -- all are present in a single panoramic view. The tower is the observation point from which the project's thesis can be verified empirically: these are not separate landscapes but a single resonant system, connected by wind, water, and the attention of anyone who climbs high enough to see the whole.

## 20. The Archipelago as Network

An archipelago is not merely a collection of individual islands -- it is a network, a system of discrete nodes connected by the medium that separates them. The San Juan Islands function as a network in which the water between islands is not a barrier but a connective medium. Canoe routes, ferry lines, tidal currents, wildlife corridors, and acoustic pathways all use the water between islands as their channel [1][2][3].

This network structure maps precisely onto the architecture of the Resonant Landscapes project. Each module is an island -- a discrete body of research with its own internal coherence -- connected to the other modules by the themes (wind, water, sound, silence, attention) that flow between them. The spaces between modules, like the spaces between islands, are not empty -- they are the medium through which resonance travels [1][2].

The archipelago metaphor also applies to the PNW research series as a whole. Each research project (COL, CAS, ECO, GDN, AVI, MAM, RLS, and the others) is an island in a larger archipelago of knowledge. The series.js navigation bar that appears at the top of every project page is the ferry system connecting the islands -- the mechanism that allows a reader to travel from one body of research to another across the thematic water that separates them [1][2].

The Salish Sea, seen from Mount Constitution, is the visual proof of this network principle. The islands are not isolated peaks -- they are the visible summits of a single submarine ridge, connected beneath the surface by the same geological formation that creates them all. The research modules of the Resonant Landscapes project are similarly connected beneath their surfaces by the geological, ecological, and cultural substrates that they share.

## 21. Ferry System as Cultural Infrastructure

The Washington State Ferries system, the largest ferry system in the United States, connects the San Juan Islands to the mainland at Anacortes and to Sidney, British Columbia. The ferries are not merely transportation -- they are cultural infrastructure that defines the island experience. The ferry schedule structures the rhythm of island life: when you can arrive, when you must leave, how long you have between sailings [1][3].

The ferry ride from Anacortes to Friday Harbor (San Juan Island) or to Orcas Island traverses the heart of the archipelago, passing between islands, through channels, and across tidal currents. The passage takes 60-90 minutes and provides a transition from mainland tempo to island tempo -- a deliberate slowing that mirrors the transition from highway to trail documented in Modules 2 and 3. The ferry is the maritime equivalent of the forest road that narrows from pavement to gravel as it approaches the wilderness trailhead [1][3].

The inter-island ferries that connect the four main islands (San Juan, Orcas, Lopez, and Shaw) create a secondary network within the archipelago. Islanders use these ferries for daily commerce, medical appointments, school activities, and social connections. The ferry system makes the islands accessible while preserving their separateness -- a balance between connection and isolation that defines island culture worldwide [1][3].
