## 1. A Sound Garden -- The Sculpture

Douglas R. Hollis designed and built "A Sound Garden" from 1982 to 1983 on the NOAA Western Regional Center campus adjacent to Warren G. Magnuson Park, on the northwestern shore of Lake Washington in Seattle. The sculpture consists of twelve 21-foot (6.4 m) steel tower structures, each topped with an organ pipe attached to a weathervane. When wind rotates the vanes and pushes through the pipes, the installation produces what observers describe as high-pitched hollow whistles and wails -- an aeolian instrument, referencing Aeolus, the Greek god of wind [1][2].

The sculpture is not a musical instrument in any conventional sense. It does not play melodies or harmonies. It responds to atmospheric conditions -- wind speed, direction, temperature, humidity -- producing sounds that change continuously, never repeating. On calm days the sculpture is silent. On windy days the twelve pipes produce overlapping tones that shift as the wind changes direction, creating what Hollis described as a sonic landscape that makes the invisible force of wind audible [1].

The organ pipes are tuned to produce specific pitches, but because the wind activates them unpredictably, the resulting sound is neither random nor composed. It occupies a space between music and weather, between intention and accident. This liminal quality -- the space between -- is the central metaphor of the entire Resonant Landscapes project [1][2].

The installation sits on a grassy slope overlooking Lake Washington, oriented to catch the prevailing winds that blow across the lake. The twelve towers are arranged in a roughly linear configuration, each tower consisting of a steel pole supporting a horizontal arm with a weathervane and organ pipe assembly at the top. The materials are utilitarian -- galvanized steel, standard organ pipe construction -- but the effect is sculptural and atmospheric [1].

## 2. Douglas Hollis and Aeolian Art

Douglas R. Hollis (born 1948) is a San Francisco-based artist whose career has centered on creating site-specific works that make natural forces perceptible through sound and movement. His work belongs to the tradition of aeolian art -- art activated by wind -- which has ancient roots in the Aeolian harp (a stringed instrument played by wind) and modern expression in sound sculpture and environmental art [1].

Hollis studied at the University of Michigan and the California College of Arts and Crafts. His approach combines engineering, acoustics, and environmental awareness. Each installation is designed for its specific site, responding to local wind patterns, topography, and acoustic conditions. A Sound Garden was his breakthrough work -- the commission that established his national reputation and demonstrated that wind-activated sculpture could function as both public art and acoustic landscape [1].

The name "A Sound Garden" plays on multiple meanings: a garden of sounds, a garden that sounds, a sound garden (as in a healthy or thriving garden). Hollis intended the work to create a space where visitors could experience the wind as sound -- to make audible a force that is normally felt but not heard in this way. The sculpture transforms the atmospheric conditions of its site into a continuously evolving sonic environment [1][2].

Hollis's other major works include "Listening Vessel" in San Jose and wind-activated installations across the United States. But A Sound Garden remains his most culturally significant work, not only for its artistic merit but because of its unexpected role in naming one of the most important bands in rock history [1].

## 3. The NOAA Campus at Sand Point

The NOAA Western Regional Center occupies the former Naval Air Station at Sand Point on the northeastern shore of Lake Washington in Seattle. The naval facility operated from 1920 until its decommissioning, after which the land was transferred to NOAA and the surrounding area developed into Warren G. Magnuson Park. Construction of the NOAA Western Regional Center began in 1977 [1][2].

The campus includes an Art Walk featuring six commissioned artworks by nationally recognized artists. In addition to Hollis's A Sound Garden, the Art Walk includes "Berth Haven" (1983) by George Trakas, and works by Martin Puryear, Scott Burton, and Siah Armajani. The Art Walk was part of a broader initiative to integrate public art into federal facilities [1].

Access to the sculpture has been restricted since September 11, 2001, when the NOAA campus moved to heightened security protocols. Visitors must present photo identification at the guard house on NE NOAA Drive. This restriction has paradoxically enhanced the sculpture's mystique -- it is simultaneously one of Seattle's most culturally significant artworks and one of its least accessible [1][2].

The NOAA campus itself is significant as a center for oceanic and atmospheric research. The proximity of a wind-activated sculpture to an institution dedicated to studying atmospheric phenomena creates an unintentional but resonant connection between art and science -- the sculpture literally performs the atmospheric conditions that NOAA studies [1].

## 4. Soundgarden -- The Band

Soundgarden was formed in Seattle in 1984 by Chris Cornell (vocals/drums), Kim Thayil (guitar), and Hiro Yamamoto (bass). They named themselves after the Hollis sculpture at Sand Point. Cornell initially played drums while singing; drummer Scott Sundquist joined in 1985, followed by Matt Cameron in 1986. Yamamoto departed in 1989, replaced first by Jason Everman, then permanently by Ben Shepherd in 1990 [3][4].

The band's trajectory traces the arc of grunge from underground to mainstream and back: Deep Six compilation (1986, C/Z Records) -- the compilation widely considered the first grunge record; Screaming Life EP (1987, Sub Pop); Ultramega OK (1988, SST Records -- the first grunge band to generate major-label interest); signed to A&M Records (1988 -- the first grunge act on a major label); Louder Than Love (1989); Badmotorfinger (1991); Superunknown (1994, debuting at No. 1 on the Billboard 200); Down on the Upside (1996) [3][4].

Soundgarden disbanded in 1997 amid internal tensions. The band reunited in 2010 and released King Animal in 2012. Chris Cornell died on May 18, 2017, in Detroit, following a performance with the band. His death was ruled a suicide [3].

Soundgarden was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame on November 8, 2025. The ceremony featured performances by Taylor Momsen, Brandi Carlile, Mike McCready, and Jerry Cantrell. Cornell's daughter Toni performed "Fell on Black Days" -- a song about depression and the imperceptible shift from light to dark. The induction brought the story full circle: a sculpture named for the wind produced a band named for the sculpture, and the band's legacy was consecrated in the same year its singer's memorial was formalized at the sculpture site [3][4].

## 5. The Memorial Circle

Following Chris Cornell's death on May 18, 2017, the sculpture at Sand Point became a spontaneous memorial. Fans left flowers, candles, handwritten notes, and mementos at the base of the steel towers. The NOAA campus, which had restricted public access since 2001, became an unlikely pilgrimage site [2][3].

The circle closes with poetic precision: Douglas Hollis created a sculpture that makes wind audible. A young musician heard it and named his band for the place. The band became one of the defining voices of a generation. The singer died. And the sculpture -- the original Sound Garden -- became his memorial. The wind that activated the sculpture's pipes now plays over a site marked by grief and remembrance. The sound that named the band continues after the voice that carried the name has gone silent [2][3].

This is the first instance of the "spaces between" theme: the space between the sculptor's intention and the band's appropriation, between the wind's indifferent physics and the human meaning projected onto it, between sound and silence, between presence and absence. The same wind that plays through the organ pipes at Sand Point plays through Spider Meadow and through the Crystal Monastery at Shey Gompa. The Resonant Landscapes project traces this wind across every scale [2].

## 6. Pacific Science Center -- Yamasaki's Vision

The Pacific Science Center occupies the former United States Science Pavilion, designed by Seattle-born architect Minoru Yamasaki (1912-1986) for the Century 21 Exposition (1962 Seattle World's Fair). Yamasaki drew inspiration from Gothic cathedrals, Islamic temples, and Japanese gardens. The complex features five interconnected buildings enclosing a courtyard with reflecting pools on approximately 7 acres, with five soaring Gothic-styled steel arches rising to 110 feet [5][6].

Structural engineering was by Worthington and Skilling -- the firm of John Skilling, Jack Christiansen, and Leslie Robertson. This is the same team that later engineered the World Trade Center in New York. The connection is not incidental: the Pacific Science Center commission brought Yamasaki international recognition, which led directly to the World Trade Center commission. The Gothic arches of the Science Pavilion and the structural tubes of the Twin Towers share an architectural DNA -- Yamasaki's lifelong pursuit of structures that combine massive scale with the appearance of lightness and aspiration [5][6].

Landscape architecture for the Science Pavilion was by Lawrence Halprin, the landscape architect who would later design the FDR Memorial in Washington, D.C. and the Ira Keller Fountain in Portland, Oregon. Halprin's reflecting pools at the Pacific Science Center create a mirroring effect that doubles the visual height of Yamasaki's arches -- a design principle echoed by Image Lake's reflection of Glacier Peak in Module 3 [5].

## 7. The 1962 World's Fair and Seattle's Transformation

The Century 21 Exposition opened on April 21, 1962, and ran for six months, attracting nearly 10 million visitors. The fair was conceived as a showcase for space-age technology and science during the Cold War. Its legacy includes the Space Needle (designed by John Graham Jr. and Victor Steinbrueck), the Monorail, the Coliseum (now Climate Pledge Arena), and the US Science Pavilion that became the Pacific Science Center [5][6].

The fair transformed Seattle from a regional city into an internationally recognized cultural center. The Seattle Center campus that remains from the fair grounds is one of the most significant clusters of mid-century modern architecture in the Pacific Northwest. Yamasaki's Science Pavilion was the architectural crown of the exposition -- the building that appeared on postcards, posters, and magazine covers [5].

The design brought Yamasaki international fame and a Time magazine cover. He subsequently designed the IBM Building in Seattle (1962-64), the World Trade Center Towers I and II in New York (1966-73), and the Rainier Bank Tower in Seattle (1972-77). The trajectory from the Science Pavilion's Gothic arches to the Twin Towers' structural innovation represents one of the most consequential architectural careers of the twentieth century [5][6].

## 8. Yamasaki's Gothic Modernism

Minoru Yamasaki was born in Seattle in 1912 to Japanese immigrant parents. He attended the University of Washington, studying architecture under Lionel Pries. Despite facing anti-Japanese prejudice -- including the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II, which affected his family -- Yamasaki built a career that made him one of the most prominent architects of his generation [5][6].

His architectural philosophy centered on what he called "humanistic modernism" -- a rejection of the brutalist concrete boxes favored by many mid-century modernists in favor of structures that evoked the vertical aspiration of Gothic cathedrals, the geometric refinement of Islamic architecture, and the contemplative stillness of Japanese gardens. The Pacific Science Center embodies all three influences: the Gothic arches reaching skyward, the geometric patterns of the structural elements, and the reflecting pools creating a Japanese-garden stillness at the center of the complex [5][6].

The Pacific Science Center was designated a Seattle Landmark in 2010, meeting all six designation criteria -- a rare achievement indicating the building's significance across historical, architectural, and cultural dimensions. In 2025, the financially challenged Pacific Science Center signed a letter of intent to integrate its campus with Seattle Center as one unified public space. The center was the nation's first science and technology center and continues to serve over 500,000 visitors annually [5][6].

## 9. Warren G. Magnuson Park

Warren G. Magnuson Park connects the NOAA campus to the broader urban landscape of northeast Seattle. The park occupies 350 acres of former naval land on the shore of Lake Washington, making it one of the largest parks in Seattle. Named for Senator Warren G. Magnuson (1905-1989), who represented Washington State in the US Senate for 36 years, the park was developed in phases following the decommissioning of Naval Air Station Sand Point [1][2].

The park provides the pedestrian context for experiencing A Sound Garden. Visitors walk through open meadows and along shoreline paths before reaching the NOAA campus and the sculpture. The transition from urban park to federal installation to artwork creates a layered experience of jurisdictional boundaries -- a theme that runs through the entire PNW parks hierarchy documented in the mission package [1].

Magnuson Park also connects to the broader Seattle parks network -- Carkeek Park, Discovery Park, Lincoln Park -- that provides urban residents with access to the PNW landscape. These municipal parks represent the innermost ring of the jurisdictional landscape that expands outward through county, state, national, and wilderness scales [1].

The park's history mirrors the broader pattern of military land conversion that characterizes much of the Puget Sound region. Naval Air Station Sand Point operated from 1920 through the post-World War II era, training pilots and servicing aircraft. When the base was decommissioned, the land -- like Fort Lawton (now Discovery Park) and the Bremerton Naval Shipyard -- transitioned from military to civilian use. The former hangars, barracks, and administrative buildings have been repurposed for community organizations, arts groups, and recreational facilities. The runway became open parkland; the shoreline was restored for public access [1][2].

The Burke-Gilman Trail, one of the most heavily used multi-use trails in Washington State, passes through Magnuson Park on its route from the Fremont district to Bothell. The trail follows the former bed of the Seattle, Lake Shore and Eastern Railway, creating a linear park that connects neighborhoods along the north shore of Lake Washington. Cyclists, runners, and walkers on the Burke-Gilman pass within a few hundred yards of A Sound Garden -- many without knowing the sculpture is there, hidden behind the NOAA security perimeter [1][2].

The layered history of Sand Point -- Duwamish land, naval air station, NOAA research center, public park, sculpture garden, memorial site -- encapsulates the accumulation of meaning that characterizes significant PNW places. Each layer does not erase the previous one; it overlays it, creating a palimpsest of use and memory that rewards sustained attention. This is the urban analog of the geological layering visible in the Olympic coast's rock formations and the cultural layering documented in the San Juan Islands' naming history [1][2].

## 10. The NOAA Art Walk

A Sound Garden is one of six commissioned artworks on the NOAA Western Regional Center Art Walk. The other five works represent a cross-section of major American public art from the early 1980s [1][2].

George Trakas's "Berth Haven" (1983) is a site-specific sculptural intervention at the waterfront, using industrial materials -- steel, concrete, wood -- to create walkways and platforms that frame views of Lake Washington and the Olympic Mountains. Trakas, a Canadian-American sculptor known for site works that engage with water and industrial landscapes, designed Berth Haven to mediate between the built campus and the natural shoreline [1].

Martin Puryear, who would later win the MacArthur Fellowship (1989) and represent the United States at the Venice Biennale (1989), contributed a work that exemplifies his approach to organic form and natural materials. Scott Burton's functional sculpture -- work that blurs the boundary between art object and furniture -- reflects the 1980s movement toward public art that invites use rather than merely contemplation. Siah Armajani's contribution extends his career-long engagement with architectural elements as sculptural forms [1].

The Art Walk program reflects a federal initiative, the General Services Administration's Art in Architecture program, which allocates a percentage of construction budgets for federal buildings to commissioned artworks. The program has produced some of the most significant public art installations in the United States, and the NOAA campus collection represents one of the strongest concentrations of Art Walk commissions on the West Coast [1].

The ensemble of six artworks creates a curatorial context for A Sound Garden that is not available when the sculpture is experienced in isolation. The Art Walk positions Hollis's aeolian work alongside sculptural practices that engage different senses and different aspects of the site -- visual, tactile, spatial, and temporal. The Sound Garden is the only work that engages hearing, making it unique within the ensemble and establishing its significance as the artwork that makes the invisible (wind) audible [1].

## 11. Smithsonian Connections

The institutional web connecting the NOAA campus, the Pacific Science Center, and the Smithsonian Institution reveals the depth of the cultural infrastructure that underlies Seattle's identity as a center for science and the arts [5][6].

The Pacific Science Center's origins in the US Science Pavilion place it within the Smithsonian orbit -- the federal government's primary institution for science education and exhibition. The 1962 World's Fair was conceived in part as a Smithsonian-adjacent showcase, with federal sponsorship and institutional support that connected Seattle to the national network of science museums and research institutions [5].

This same institutional network connects to Sheldon Jackson's artifact collections documented in Module 5. Jackson deposited significant portions of his Alaska Native collections with the Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History, creating a direct line from Tlingit material culture in Sitka to the national collections in Washington, D.C. The 1909 Alaska-Yukon-Pacific Exposition in Seattle -- another world's fair, held 47 years before Century 21 -- served as the conduit for some of these transfers [4][5].

The Smithsonian thread weaves through the Resonant Landscapes project as an institutional through-line: the same federal cultural infrastructure that built the Science Pavilion, supported the NOAA Art Walk, and received Jackson's collections also manages the national parks, forests, and wilderness areas documented in Modules 2 through 4. The wind that plays through Hollis's pipes plays across a landscape managed, interpreted, and -- for better and worse -- collected by the same institutional apparatus [5][6].

## 12. The Resonance Thread

Sound and Sculpture is the starting point of the resonant landscape system. The aeolian sculpture at Sand Point establishes the foundational metaphor: wind as the animating force that connects disparate landscapes through a shared medium. The same atmospheric dynamics that push air through Hollis's organ pipes also shape the weather systems that determine snowpack on Glacier Peak, wave patterns on the Olympic coast, and the prevailing winds of the Salish Sea [1][2].

The Pacific Science Center extends the thread through architecture -- Yamasaki's Gothic arches aspire upward in the same vertical gesture as the old-growth Douglas firs documented in the COL and CAS modules. The reflecting pools mirror the sky just as Image Lake mirrors Glacier Peak. The World's Fair ambition to showcase human achievement connects to the Ozette archaeological site, where 55,000 Makah artifacts document a different kind of technological sophistication -- maritime culture adapted to the most dangerous coast on the continent [5][6].

Soundgarden's music occupies the space between the sculpture's ambient tones and the crash of surf through Hole in the Wall. Cornell's voice -- equally capable of whispered contemplation and full-throated power -- mapped the same dynamic range as the PNW soundscape itself. The band's induction into the Rock Hall in 2025 formalized a cultural legacy that began with wind through steel pipes in 1982 [3][4].

The grunge movement that Soundgarden helped define was itself a PNW soundscape -- a musical genre shaped by the same rain, isolation, and blue-collar industrial landscape that characterizes the region. The distorted guitars and heavy rhythms of grunge encoded the sonic texture of the Pacific Northwest just as Hollis's organ pipes encode the wind patterns of Lake Washington. The music was not imported from elsewhere; it grew from the same soil, in the same weather, in the same spaces between the sounds [3][4].

The connection between sound art, architecture, and popular music at Sand Point creates a nexus of cultural meaning that is unique in the Pacific Northwest. No other site carries this density of artistic, institutional, and ecological significance in a single location. The sculpture, the band, the memorial, the NOAA campus, the Art Walk, Magnuson Park -- together they form the urban frequency that the remaining modules modulate across alpine, coastal, maritime, and contemplative landscapes [1][2][3].

The spaces between these sounds -- between the sculpture's whistles and the meadow's silence, between the surf's crash and the monastery's stillness -- are where the Resonant Landscapes project finds its meaning. Module 1 establishes the frequency. The remaining modules trace it across every scale the Pacific Northwest contains.

## 13. Cross-References

### PNW Parks Hierarchy Context

The Sound and Sculpture module sits at the municipal level of the PNW parks hierarchy -- the innermost ring of a jurisdictional system that expands outward through county, state, national park, national forest/wilderness, and tribal lands. Understanding this hierarchy is essential context for the Resonant Landscapes system [1][2].

**Municipal parks** (Seattle Parks): Magnuson Park, Discovery Park, Carkeek Park, Lincoln Park. These provide urban residents with direct access to PNW landscape -- shoreline, forest, wetland -- within the city limits. Magnuson Park, at 350 acres, is the gateway to A Sound Garden [1].

**County parks** (King County Parks): Regional trail systems, natural areas, and community parks that connect municipal parks to larger landscape corridors. The Burke-Gilman Trail, which passes through Magnuson Park, is a King County / City of Seattle partnership that connects the university district to the northern suburbs along the former railroad right-of-way.

**State parks** (Washington State Parks): Moran State Park (Module 4), Deception Pass State Park, and over 100 others. State parks often preserve landscape features of regional significance that fall outside national park boundaries [1].

**National parks** (NPS): Olympic National Park (Module 3), Mount Rainier National Park, North Cascades National Park. Federal designation provides the highest level of landscape protection in the American system [1].

**National forests and wilderness** (USFS): Mt. Baker-Snoqualmie, Okanogan-Wenatchee, Olympic National Forests. Glacier Peak Wilderness (Module 2), Alpine Lakes Wilderness, The Enchantments. Wilderness designation prohibits motorized access, roads, and commercial resource extraction [1].

**Tribal lands** (sovereign): Makah Reservation (Module 3), Quileute Reservation, Lummi Reservation (Module 4), and dozens of others. Tribal lands operate under sovereign governance distinct from federal, state, or local jurisdiction [1].

This six-level hierarchy creates the jurisdictional landscape through which the Resonant Landscapes project moves -- from the NOAA campus (federal) through a city park (municipal) to a state park (state) to a national park (NPS) to a wilderness area (USFS) to tribal land (sovereign). Each level carries different access rules, management priorities, and cultural meanings [1][2].

## 14. The Grunge Sound and Seattle's Musical Geography

The naming of Soundgarden after Hollis's sculpture is not an isolated cultural moment but part of a broader pattern in which Seattle's physical geography shaped its musical identity. The grunge movement that emerged from Seattle in the late 1980s and early 1990s was inseparable from the city's landscape -- its rain, its isolation from other major music centers, its blue-collar industrial character, and its specific sonic textures [3][4].

The music rehearsal spaces that incubated grunge were often repurposed industrial buildings -- warehouses in the Ballard and Georgetown neighborhoods, basements in the University District, converted storefronts in Capitol Hill. The acoustics of these spaces -- concrete floors, high ceilings, reverberant walls -- shaped the heavy, resonant guitar tones that defined the genre. The sound of grunge was literally the sound of Seattle's built environment, filtered through amplifiers and returned to the air [3][4].

Sub Pop Records, the label that first recorded Soundgarden, Nirvana, and Mudhoney, operated from a tiny office in the Terminal Sales Building on First Avenue. The label's aesthetic -- lo-fi production, black-and-white photography, ironic humor -- reflected the scrappy, rain-soaked, slightly defiant character of Seattle's music scene in the pre-Nevermind era. Sub Pop's decision to release the Deep Six compilation in 1986, featuring Soundgarden alongside Green River, Melvins, Malfunkshun, Skin Yard, and the U-Men, is widely considered the moment when grunge became recognizable as a genre [3][4].

The musical geography extends beyond rehearsal spaces and record labels to the venues that hosted performances: the Crocodile Cafe on Second Avenue, the Off Ramp on Eastlake Avenue, the Central Saloon in Pioneer Square, the Moore Theatre and the Paramount. These venues created the live performance ecosystem that allowed bands to develop from basement projects to national acts. The acoustic characteristics of each venue -- the Crocodile's low ceiling and intimate stage, the Moore's ornate Beaux-Arts proscenium -- contributed to the sonic identity of the bands that played there [3][4].

Chris Cornell's vocal range -- four octaves from baritone to soprano -- was uniquely suited to filling these spaces. His ability to shift from a whispered intimacy to a full-power scream within a single phrase mapped the dynamic range of the venues themselves, from the Crocodile's close quarters to the arena-scale performances of the Superunknown era. The voice that named itself after a wind sculpture became one of the most distinctive instruments in rock history [3][4].

The connection between A Sound Garden, Soundgarden the band, and Seattle's musical geography creates a web of cultural meaning that is unique to this place. The sculpture responded to wind; the band responded to the sculpture; the city's music scene responded to its own geography. Each layer amplified the resonance of the layers beneath it, creating a cultural landscape as rich and layered as the geological landscape documented in the other modules [3][4].

## 15. Yamasaki's Later Career and the World Trade Center

The trajectory from the Pacific Science Center to the World Trade Center is one of the most consequential architectural narratives of the twentieth century. Yamasaki's success with the Science Pavilion in 1962 brought him to national prominence and directly led to his selection for the World Trade Center commission in 1966 [5][6].

The World Trade Center (completed 1973) applied the same principles Yamasaki had explored at the Science Pavilion -- soaring verticality, structural innovation, the interplay of light and space -- at a radically different scale. The Twin Towers rose to 1,368 and 1,362 feet, making them the tallest buildings in the world at their completion. The structural engineering was by the same firm -- Worthington, Skilling, Helle & Jackson (the partnership having reorganized since the Science Pavilion days) -- with Leslie Robertson as the lead structural engineer for the towers [5][6].

The connection between the Gothic arches of the Pacific Science Center and the structural tubes of the World Trade Center is visible in the detailing. Both use narrow, closely spaced vertical elements that create a screen-like effect -- at the Science Center, the effect is decorative and aspirational; at the World Trade Center, it was structural, with the closely spaced perimeter columns bearing the building's gravity and wind loads. Yamasaki's distinctive aesthetic -- the pursuit of lightness and elegance at massive scale -- is recognizable in both structures [5][6].

The IBM Building in Seattle (1962-64), designed by Yamasaki concurrently with the early World Trade Center planning, represents the intermediate step between the two major commissions. The Rainier Bank Tower (1972-77), also in Seattle, with its distinctive inverted pedestal form, completed Yamasaki's quartet of Seattle-area works. Together, these four structures -- Science Center, IBM Building, Rainier Bank Tower, and World Trade Center -- constitute a body of work that placed Seattle at the center of one of the most important architectural careers of the modern era [5][6].

Yamasaki died in 1986. The World Trade Center towers were destroyed on September 11, 2001. The Pacific Science Center remains as the surviving masterwork of an architect whose career spanned from Gothic arches in Seattle to the towers that defined the New York skyline for three decades. The wind that plays through Hollis's organ pipes on the NOAA campus plays across a city whose architectural identity was shaped by the same hand that designed the buildings at the other end of the Art Walk trail [5][6].

## 16. Sound, Silence, and the Urban Frequency

The Sound and Sculpture module establishes the urban frequency of the Resonant Landscapes system -- the innermost ring of the concentric landscape that expands outward from Hollis's twelve steel towers through alpine wilderness, coastal trails, island waterways, and contemplative journey. The urban frequency is not the beginning of the system in any hierarchical sense -- it is simply the first frequency encountered, the one closest to the place where most visitors start [1][2].

The urban frequency is characterized by its density of human-generated sound. The acoustic environment of the NOAA campus at Sand Point includes traffic noise from Sand Point Way, aircraft overhead from Boeing Field and SeaTac, marine vessels on Lake Washington, the mechanical systems of the NOAA buildings, and the ambient electronic hum that pervades all modern urban environments. Against this background, the aeolian tones of A Sound Garden are subtle -- easily missed by a visitor who is not listening for them [1][2].

This is precisely the point. The sculpture does not compete with urban noise -- it reveals what is already present but inaudible. The wind is always blowing. The atmospheric conditions that Hollis's pipes translate into sound are always active. The sculpture makes the invisible visible (or, more precisely, makes the inaudible audible) by providing a resonant structure that converts atmospheric energy into acoustic energy at frequencies the human ear can perceive [1].

The remaining modules trace what happens as this urban frequency modulates outward into landscapes where the ratio of natural sound to human-generated sound shifts progressively. In the alpine wilderness of Module 2, natural sound dominates and human-generated sound is absent. On the Olympic coast of Module 3, the surf overwhelms everything. In the islands of Module 4, the maritime environment creates its own acoustic character -- wind, water, and wildlife in shifting proportions. In Module 5, the contemplative tradition seeks silence as a medium for insight -- and finds it not in the absence of all sound but in the absence of the specific sounds that prevent attention [1][2].

The resonant landscape is a single instrument played by the same wind. The modules are the stops on the organ. The spaces between the sounds are where the meaning lives.

## 17. Aeolus and the Wind Tradition

The name "aeolian" connects Hollis's sculpture to a tradition of wind-activated art and instrument making that extends back to antiquity. Aeolus, the keeper of the winds in Greek mythology, gave his name to the Aeolian harp -- a stringed instrument placed in a window or outdoor location where wind vibrates the strings to produce haunting, ethereal tones [1][7].

The Aeolian harp was popular in the Romantic period of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Samuel Taylor Coleridge wrote "The Eolian Harp" in 1795, using the instrument as a metaphor for the responsive human mind -- a consciousness that does not generate its own content but resonates with external forces, producing beauty from the interaction between self and environment. Percy Bysshe Shelley, in "Ode to the West Wind" (1820), extended the metaphor: "Make me thy lyre, even as the forest is" -- asking the wind to play him as it plays the trees [7].

Hollis's contribution to this tradition was to translate the principle from strings to pipes, from the intimate scale of a window instrument to the monumental scale of a twelve-tower installation on a lakeside campus. The shift from string to pipe changes the acoustic character -- from the singing overtones of a harp to the hollow, organ-like tones of tuned pipes -- but the underlying principle is identical. The wind provides the energy; the instrument provides the resonant structure; the result is a sound that belongs to neither the wind nor the instrument but to the space between them [1][7].

This is the Resonant Landscapes project in miniature. The wind is the constant. The landscapes -- sculpture, meadow, coast, island, monastery -- are the resonant structures. The meaning is in the spaces between.

## 18. Lake Washington -- The Resonant Surface

Lake Washington provides the physical setting for A Sound Garden and serves as a resonant surface in its own right. The lake extends 20 miles from Renton to Kenmore, with a maximum depth of 214 feet and a surface area of 21,500 acres. It is the second-largest natural lake in Washington State (after Lake Chelan) and the largest in the Puget Sound lowland [1][2].

The lake's acoustic properties are significant for the sculpture. Sound travels efficiently across water -- the smooth, reflective surface of the lake amplifies and carries the organ pipe tones farther than they would travel over rough terrain. On still evenings, the sculpture's sounds can be heard from considerable distances across the water. The lake acts as a resonant chamber, extending the sculpture's acoustic reach beyond its immediate physical footprint [1][2].

Lake Washington also connects the Sound Garden to the broader PNW hydrological system through the Lake Washington Ship Canal and the Hiram M. Chittenden Locks (Ballard Locks), which link the freshwater lake to the saltwater Puget Sound. This connection makes Lake Washington part of the Salish Sea watershed documented in Module 4 -- the same water system that supports orcas, salmon, and Coast Salish maritime culture. The wind that plays through the organ pipes at Sand Point carries moisture from the same Pacific Ocean that feeds the Salish Sea [1][2].
