## 1. The Corridor Paradox

The Hwy 99 corridor from Everett south to Lynnwood carries the full social stack of a regional economy: low-income neighborhoods cycling up through workforce training, established businesses anchored at Alderwood Mall, aerospace manufacturing at Paine Field, and ferry logistics connecting to the Olympic Peninsula and beyond. The paradox is that these layers exist in proximity without connection. The wealth generated at Paine Field and distributed through Alderwood does not circulate through the neighborhoods on either side of SR 99 between those anchors.

The creative commons model addresses this by building the missing middle: a structured pathway that connects the creative talent already present in the corridor's lower-income neighborhoods to the economic infrastructure that surrounds them.

```
HWY 99 CORRIDOR -- SOCIAL STACK (NORTH TO SOUTH)
================================================================

  EVERETT (north)
  |
  |-- Port of Everett (cargo, marina, naval station)
  |-- Downtown Everett (government, services)
  |-- Everett Mall / Hub @ Everett (TRANSITION ZONE)
  |       |-- Topgolf anchor (68K sq ft, 2026)
  |       |-- Trader Joe's / Ulta (opened 2024)
  |       |-- Multifamily housing (final phase planning)
  |       |-- Creative commons opportunity (rear parking area)
  |
  |-- South Everett (lower-income, youth population)
  |-- SR 99 / I-5 / SR 526 / SR 527 Broadway interchange
  |
  |-- (mid-corridor gap -- small businesses, restaurants, services)
  |
  |-- Alderwood Mall (ESTABLISHED BUSINESS HUB)
  |       |-- National brands
  |       |-- Professional services
  |       |-- Higher disposable income catchment
  |
  LYNNWOOD (south)
```

## 2. Hwy 99 -- The Social Stack

State Route 99 through Snohomish County is not a highway in the interstate sense. It is a commercial arterial -- a street lined with businesses, restaurants, services, and housing that serves as the daily circulation path for thousands of corridor residents. Unlike I-5, which bypasses communities, SR 99 passes through them.

### Corridor Characteristics

- **Length:** Approximately 12 miles from Everett south to Lynnwood
- **Character:** Mixed commercial/residential, predominantly strip-mall and single-story commercial
- **Transit:** Everett Transit, Community Transit, future Swift BRT alignment
- **Demographics:** Diverse, with significant lower-income populations in South Everett
- **Business density:** High restaurant and food service concentration, automotive services, small retail
- **Redevelopment pressure:** Hub @ Everett represents the largest active redevelopment on the corridor

The social stack means that a person living in South Everett can walk or take a short bus ride to every layer of the corridor economy: food service jobs, retail positions, training at Sno-Isle TECH (via Airport Road), creative space at Hub @ Everett (once developed), and eventually business ownership at Alderwood Mall for enterprises that outgrow the incubation phase.

## 3. Everett Mall / Hub @ Everett

Everett Mall occupies 62 acres at the intersection of SR 99 and Everett Mall Way in the Twin Creeks neighborhood of southern Everett [1]. The property is mid-redevelopment as "The Hub @ Everett" by Brixton Capital, with MG2 architecture and Bayley Construction managing the transformation.

### Current Status (March 2026)

- Interior mall closed July 1, 2025
- Exterior redevelopment ongoing
- Topgolf confirmed as 68,000 sq ft anchor (replacing Burlington/LA Fitness building) [1]
- Trader Joe's and Ulta Beauty opened in former Sears building (2024)
- Everett Transit Mall Station relocated (December 2025, $2M project) to accommodate redevelopment [2]
- Full Hub @ Everett opening expected 2026
- Location: less than 1 mile south of the SR 99/I-5/SR 526/SR 527 Broadway interchange

### The Creative Commons Opportunity

The large rear parking area (near former LA Fitness / Regal Cinemas) is in "final phase" planning with multifamily housing, hotel, and mixed-use options on the table [1]. This space represents the corridor's primary creative commons opportunity: a significant land area, on transit, in a redeveloping commercial zone, adjacent to the population that would most benefit from creative programming.

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2022 | Brixton Capital acquires Everett Mall | Private redevelopment begins |
| 2023 | MG2 / Bayley Construction engaged | Architecture and construction team |

| 2026 | Hub @ Everett full opening (expected) | New commercial identity launches |

The timeline matters because the creative commons opportunity exists in the "final phase" planning window. The anchor tenants (Topgolf, Trader Joe's, Ulta) are set. The question is what fills the remaining development parcels. A creative commons model -- maker spaces, arts studios, music practice rooms, small business incubation -- is a viable mixed-use program for the final phase, particularly given the adjacent population demographics and the corridor's workforce training infrastructure.

## 5. Creative Commons Model

The creative commons model for Hub @ Everett follows the IOOF hall pattern (see M1) adapted for contemporary needs:

### Architecture

```
CREATIVE COMMONS -- BUILDING PROGRAM
================================================================

  GROUND FLOOR (revenue-generating)
  +-----------------------------------------------+
  | Co-op retail | Food hall | Pop-up | Gallery    |
  | (small biz)  | (culinary | shops  | (rotating |
  |              | graduates)|        | exhibits) |
  +-----------------------------------------------+

  SECOND FLOOR (community programming)
  +-----------------------------------------------+
  | Maker lab    | Music     | Art     | Meeting  |
  | (tools,      | practice  | studio  | rooms    |
  | 3D print,    | rooms     | (paint, | (co-op   |
  | electronics) | (sound-   | draw,   | governance|
  |              | proofed)  | digital)| classes) |
  +-----------------------------------------------+

  THIRD FLOOR (incubation / office)
  +-----------------------------------------------+
  | Small biz    | Co-working | Design | Admin    |
  | offices      | (hot desk, | studio | (co-op   |
  | (graduate    | dedicated) | (web,  | mgmt,    |
  | from maker)  |            | print) | booking) |
  +-----------------------------------------------+
```

### Operating Principles

1. **Below-market entry:** First-floor retail space offered at below-market rates for co-op members, subsidized by upper-floor co-working revenue
2. **Progressive pricing:** Rates increase as businesses grow; successful enterprises graduate to Alderwood or other market-rate locations
3. **Culinary connection:** Sno-Isle TECH Culinary Arts graduates operate the food hall, gaining real-world experience in a supported environment
4. **Arts-to-business pathway:** Artists begin with studio access, develop products/services, transition to ground-floor retail
5. **Co-op governance:** The creative commons is itself a cooperative under RCW 23.86; all members participate in governance

## 6. Arts and Music as Business Pathway

The arts-to-business pathway is not charity. It is economic development with a different entry point. Instead of requiring capital to start a business, it requires talent and effort -- resources that exist in abundance in the corridor's lower-income neighborhoods but have no structured on-ramp to entrepreneurship.

### The Pathway

1. **Discovery** (free): Open studio hours, community music jams, gallery visits
2. **Training** (subsidized): Arts and music classes, digital design workshops, business basics
3. **Creation** (studio access): Maker lab membership, practice room time, materials budget
4. **Exhibition** (gallery): Rotating shows, pop-up markets, online portfolio
5. **Sales** (retail): Ground-floor co-op retail space, food hall station, online store
6. **Business formation** (co-op): Formal cooperative entity under RCW 23.86
7. **Graduation** (market-rate): Successful enterprise moves to Alderwood or independent location

### Cultural Production as Economic Output

The creative commons is not adjacent to the corridor economy -- it is part of it. Cultural production generates economic output: art sales, music performance revenue, design services, culinary products, handcrafted goods. The corridor's JIT manufacturing layer (M3) provides the production infrastructure. The knowledge network (M5) provides the global distribution channel. The creative commons provides the creative input.

## 7. Alderwood Mall -- The Graduation Destination

Alderwood Mall in Lynnwood serves as the established business anchor further south on the corridor [4]. National brands, professional services, and a higher disposable income catchment make Alderwood the natural destination for businesses that outgrow the creative commons incubation phase.

### The Strategic Distinction

The relationship between Hub @ Everett and Alderwood Mall is intentional and complementary:

| Attribute | Hub @ Everett | Alderwood Mall |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Role | Creative commons / incubation | Established business hub |
| Pricing | Below-market (co-op subsidized) | Market-rate |
| Target | Emerging businesses, artists, makers | Scaled businesses, national brands |
| Demographic | Lower-income, diverse, younger | Higher-income, established |
| Transit | Everett Transit Mall Station | Community Transit, I-5 access |
| Character | Experimental, flexible, maker-friendly | Professional, polished, consumer-focused |

A business born in the Hub @ Everett creative commons, nurtured through the co-op incubation process, and ready for market-rate operations has a natural next step: Alderwood Mall, 6 miles south on the same corridor. The graduation is geographic as well as economic -- moving south along SR 99 from incubation to market.

### Graduation Metrics

How does a business know it is ready to graduate? The co-op provides structured checkpoints:

1. **Revenue threshold** -- consistent monthly revenue exceeding creative commons operating costs
2. **Customer base** -- repeat customers and demand that exceeds the food hall or maker lab format
3. **Staffing** -- the business employs co-op members beyond the founder
4. **Product maturity** -- product or service has been refined through customer feedback cycles
5. **Financial literacy** -- the founder has completed co-op business management training
6. **Market validation** -- the concept has been tested at the creative commons for a minimum of 6 months

Graduation is not expulsion. The co-op network continues to support graduated businesses through wholesale purchasing, shared marketing, and governance participation. The business remains a co-op member even after leaving the physical creative commons space.

## 8. Funding Mechanisms

The creative commons model requires initial subsidy to reach economic self-sufficiency. The following funding mechanisms are applicable to the corridor [3][5]:

| Program | Application | Approx. Available |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| HUD CDBG | Low/moderate income housing, economic development | $869K/yr (Everett) |
| WA AHTF (2060) | Affordable housing trust fund | State allocation |
| HUD HOME | Rental housing production | Federal formula |
| EDA (Economic Development Administration) | Creative district, distressed community | Competitive |
| WA Career Connect | Workforce-to-arts pipeline | State |
| NEA Creative Placemaking | Arts infrastructure, community spaces | Competitive |
| BEAD / IIJA | Broadband infrastructure (co-op eligible) | WA: $1.2B allocation |

### Funding Strategy

The funding strategy is layered, not dependent on any single source:

1. **Infrastructure phase:** CDBG + AHTF for building acquisition/renovation
2. **Programming phase:** NEA Creative Placemaking + WA Career Connect for arts programs
3. **Technology phase:** BEAD / IIJA for broadband and mesh infrastructure
4. **Sustainability phase:** Co-op revenue from retail, co-working, events replaces grant funding

The goal is self-sufficiency within 5-7 years. The IOOF model demonstrates this is achievable: ground-floor retail revenue funds upper-floor community programming without ongoing subsidy, once the building is acquired and equipped.

## 9. Youth Safety Context

Everett Mayor Rob Franklin issued Mayoral Directive 2025-01, identifying youth safety as a priority for the city [6]. Any creative commons programming touching youth or low-income populations must acknowledge this context.

### Design Implications

- All youth programming includes supervised access during posted hours
- Background checks for adult mentors and instructors
- Collaboration with Everett Parks & Recreation Department for existing youth programming alignment
- No unsupervised after-hours access for minors
- Partnership with school districts (the same 15 districts that operate Sno-Isle TECH)
- Program design reviewed against Directive 2025-01 requirements

> **SAFETY NOTE:** Creative commons programming involving youth requires structured supervision. The Everett Mayoral Directive 2025-01 on youth safety establishes the policy context. All program design in this module acknowledges this requirement. Content does not suggest or imply unsupervised youth engagement.

## 11. Housing and Demographics

### Demographic Context

South Everett, the area immediately surrounding Hub @ Everett, has the following characteristics [3][5]:

- Lower median household income than Snohomish County average
- Higher percentage of households receiving public assistance
- Younger median age
- Higher racial and ethnic diversity
- Higher percentage of renter-occupied housing
- Lower vehicle ownership rates (transit-dependent population)

### Housing Pipeline

The City of Everett issued permits for nearly 600 housing units in 2024 -- a 340% increase from 2023 [3]. This explosive growth in the housing pipeline means the corridor's residential density is increasing rapidly. More residents means more demand for:

- **Food service** -- the restaurant density on SR 99 serves this growing population
- **Arts and culture** -- the creative commons fills a programming gap for new residents
- **Small business** -- the co-op incubation model provides entrepreneurship access
- **Transit** -- the Everett Transit Mall Station relocation ensures continued service

The housing growth is the demand signal. The creative commons is the supply response.

## 12. The Culinary Corridor

Sno-Isle TECH's three-track Culinary Arts program (see M1) makes the food service industry a first-class pathway on the corridor, not a fallback career. The Hwy 99 spine has one of the highest restaurant densities in Snohomish County, and culinary graduates have a natural market waiting for them.

### Three Tracks, Three Destinations

| Track | Training Focus | Corridor Destination |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| Restaurant Management | Front-of-house, menu design, business operations | Hub @ Everett food hall, independent restaurants |
| Institutional Food Service | High-volume prep, nutrition, HACCP compliance | Boeing cafeteria supply, school district food service |
| Specialized Preparation | Pastry, ethnic cuisine, farm-to-table technique | Pop-up kitchens, catering co-ops, farmers market stands |

### The Food Hall Model

The creative commons food hall follows the same IOOF-pattern logic as the rest of the building: shared infrastructure (commercial kitchen, cold storage, health department certification) supporting individual operators who would struggle to afford their own fully permitted kitchen. A culinary arts graduate can begin operating in the food hall with minimal capital investment, building a customer base and refining their concept before graduating to an independent restaurant on the corridor.

### Connection to Daily Beat

The food service layer connects directly to the daily beat protocol (M4). Restaurant prep windows, supplier delivery schedules, and demand spikes from ferry arrivals and shift changes are all signals that the daily beat provides to food operators in real time. A taco truck on Evergreen Way that knows the 3:30 PM Boeing shift change is coming can pre-prep accordingly. Without the daily beat, the same truck estimates demand from yesterday's traffic. The difference is waste reduction, margin improvement, and customer satisfaction.

### Culinary Co-op Structure

A culinary co-op under RCW 23.86 allows food operators to:

- **Pool purchasing power** -- co-op wholesale from Costco, US Foods, or Sysco at volume pricing
- **Share kitchen infrastructure** -- commercial kitchen, walk-in coolers, dishwashing, HVAC
- **Coordinate delivery windows** -- single receiving dock for multiple operators, reducing truck traffic
- **Joint marketing** -- "Hub @ Everett Food Hall" brand carries more weight than individual vendors
- **Shared compliance** -- food safety certification, health inspections, insurance as co-op overhead

The culinary corridor is not a food court. It is a co-operative enterprise where each operator builds equity while the shared infrastructure reduces the capital barrier to entry.

## 13. Transit-Oriented Development

The creative commons model is inseparable from transit access. The Everett Transit Mall Station relocation ($2M, December 2025) ensures that Hub @ Everett remains on the transit network despite the physical redevelopment of the surrounding property [2].

### Transit Access Matrix

| Transit Service | Hub @ Everett Access | Frequency | Corridor Connection |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Everett Transit Route 7 | Mall Station (direct) | 30 min | SR 99 spine, Everett Station |
| Everett Transit Route 29 | Mall Station (transfer) | 30 min | Casino Rd, South Everett |
| Community Transit | Mall Station (stop) | 15-30 min | Lynnwood, Alderwood, regional |
| Future Swift BRT | Planned SR 99 alignment | 10-15 min | High-frequency spine service |

### Why Transit Matters for Creative Commons

The creative commons serves a lower-income population. Lower-income populations are disproportionately transit-dependent. If the creative commons is not accessible by transit, it fails its primary constituency. The Mall Station relocation ensures this connection survives the redevelopment transition.

Transit access also creates the corridor's spatial logic: a person at Alderwood Mall can reach Hub @ Everett by bus. A person at Hub @ Everett can reach Sno-Isle TECH by bus. A person at Sno-Isle TECH can walk to Paine Field. The corridor is connected not by highways but by the transit system that serves people who do not have cars.
