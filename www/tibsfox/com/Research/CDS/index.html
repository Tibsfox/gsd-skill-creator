Central District

Cultural Heritage · Jazz History · Cassette Economy · Hip-Hop Radio · Gentrification · Community Resilience
Overview Neighborhood Musical Lineage Cassette Economy Radio Ecosystem Community Spaces Displacement Mission Pack
There is a neighborhood four square miles wide at the geographic center of Seattle that spent most of the twentieth century holding a Black community together against forces that wanted it dispersed. What the policy-makers intended as confinement, the community converted into infrastructure. They built their own banks, their own newspapers, their own performance venues, their own cassette distribution systems. The geography is the argument.

Research Modules

M1: Neighborhood Foundation

History Track A · Geographic formation

Redlining and restrictive covenants compressed Black Seattle into four square miles. William Grose's 1882 land purchase, WWII migration surge, FHA disinvestment, the demographic peak, and the displacement arc to the present. The structural causes that made the Central District.

M2: Musical Lineage

Music Track A · Jackson Street to hip-hop

Jackson Street jazz clubs where Ray Charles and Quincy Jones played. Garfield High School as cultural crucible. Jimi Hendrix at Washington Hall. The busing program as sonic vector. The Emerald Street Boys and the birth of Seattle hip-hop.

M3: Cassette Economy

Economy Track A · Distribution infrastructure

Sir Mix-A-Lot's bedroom studio: Commodore 64, Roland 808, cassette tapes sold for $10 from an apartment window. Trunk sales, record store adoption, Boys & Girls Club parties, military base markets. The pre-digital distribution system that made Nastymix possible.

M4: Radio Ecosystem

Radio Track B · Station genealogy

KYAC to KFOX to KCMU to KUBE — not four separate stations but one continuous social network. Nasty Nes Rodriguez's FreshTracks, the first all-rap show west of the Mississippi. Ed Locke, B-Mello, and the connective tissue that held the network together.

M5: Community Spaces & Artists

Community Track B · Venues and crews

The Emerald Street Boys at Lateef's All City Rap Off (1981). Boys & Girls Club parties at a dollar a head. Washington Hall from Duke Ellington to B-boy events. The military pipeline from Fort Lewis and Whidbey Island. Geographic isolation as creative freedom.

M6: Displacement & Preservation

Preservation Track B · Memory and continuity

From 70% Black to under 20%. The crack epidemic, the tech boom, and the erasure of physical markers. Where the community went: Skyway, Kent, Federal Way. The organizations fighting to hold the memory: NAAM, 206 Zulu, Africatown Community Land Trust.
