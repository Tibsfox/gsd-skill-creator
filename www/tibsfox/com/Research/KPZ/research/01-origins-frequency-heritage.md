## 1. The FM Frontier: Seattle in 1959

FM radio in 1959 was the frontier nobody was watching. The medium had been commercially available since the early 1940s, but AM dominated -- the receivers were cheaper, the signals stronger, and the programming established. Most American homes in 1959 did not contain an FM-capable receiver. The FCC had allocated the FM band between 88 and 108 megahertz in 1945, but two decades later, the band remained sparsely populated in most markets. Seattle was no exception.

The Puget Sound radio market in the late 1950s was defined by its AM powerhouses. KJR (950 AM) carried Top 40 with the kind of signal that covered the entire region. KOMO (1000 AM) provided news and general entertainment as the NBC affiliate. KVI (570 AM) ran a middle-of-the-road format that attracted adult listeners. KING (1090 AM) mixed talk and music. These were the stations that mattered -- the ones with audience, advertisers, and institutional weight.

FM stations existed in Seattle, but they occupied a marginal position. KING-FM offered classical music. KISW would not arrive until 1968. KZOK was years away. The FM band was a place for experimentation, niche formats, and operators willing to broadcast to an audience measured in hundreds rather than thousands. Into this landscape, on September 1, 1959, KETO-FM signed on at 101.5 megahertz.

> The FM band in 1959 was like a vast empty highway -- the infrastructure existed, the lanes were paved, but almost nobody was driving on it yet. The stations that signed on during this era were not competing for audience so much as staking claims on frequencies whose value would only become apparent decades later.

## 2. KETO-FM: The Pelican Station

KETO-FM was licensed to Chem-Air, Inc., a company whose primary business was not broadcasting. The station signed on September 1, 1959 with an easy listening format -- the default choice for FM stations in this era, since the medium's superior fidelity made it a natural home for instrumental music, orchestral arrangements, and the kind of background audio that restaurants and dental offices piped through their overhead speakers.

The station operated at 10,000 watts effective radiated power (ERP), a modest signal by FM standards but sufficient to cover the immediate Seattle metropolitan area. The transmitter location would later prove critical -- by the time the station reached full power in the 1970s, the Cougar Mountain transmitter site would become one of the most valuable pieces of broadcasting real estate in the Pacific Northwest.

A 1960 Broadcasting Yearbook advertisement provides the most vivid image of the station's early identity: a pelican wearing headphones and holding a key, a visual pun on "KEY-to" (KETO). The logo was whimsical in a way that commercial broadcasting rarely permitted -- a reminder that FM stations in this era operated with the creative freedom that comes from having almost no audience to offend and almost no advertisers to satisfy.

The easy listening format that KETO-FM carried was not a creative choice so much as a market default. FM's technical superiority -- stereo capability, wider frequency response, lower noise floor -- made it ideal for music that rewarded fidelity. Beautiful music, as the format was industry-labeled, required minimal DJ presence, minimal production, and minimal staffing. For a small operator like Chem-Air, it was the most economically efficient way to keep a signal on the air while the FM audience slowly grew.

> The pelican logo tells you everything about what FM radio was in 1960: playful, marginal, and completely unaware that the frequency it occupied would one day be worth millions. KETO-FM was not building a brand -- it was keeping a light on.

## 3. The Slow Build: FM in the 1960s

The 1960s were FM's decade of slow accumulation. The FCC's 1964 non-duplication rule -- requiring FM stations in major markets to program at least 50% original content rather than simply simulcasting their AM siblings -- created the first regulatory pressure toward FM as an independent medium. Before this rule, many FM stations existed solely as simulcast mirrors of AM stations, adding no unique programming to the dial. The non-duplication rule forced FM operators to develop distinct identities.

For KETO-FM, the 1960s were a decade of quiet persistence. The easy listening format continued. FM listenership in Seattle grew incrementally as stereo FM receivers became more affordable and automobile manufacturers began offering FM as a standard or optional feature. The demographic shift was generational: younger listeners, raised on rock and roll but increasingly interested in audio quality, began to discover that FM offered something AM could not -- stereo sound, wider dynamic range, and freedom from the static and interference that plagued AM signals, particularly in the electrically noisy Pacific Northwest with its power lines, industrial equipment, and frequent atmospheric disturbances.

By the late 1960s, KETO-FM was no longer alone on the FM band. KISW signed on in 1968 with an album rock format that would define it for decades. KZOK would arrive in 1974. The FM landscape was filling in, and each new station validated the medium while simultaneously increasing competition for the growing but still limited FM audience.

The significance of KETO-FM's 1960s tenure is not what it accomplished but what it preserved: continuous occupation of 101.5 megahertz. In broadcasting, a frequency is a finite resource allocated by the FCC. Maintaining a license requires continuous operation. KETO-FM's decade of easy listening kept the 101.5 frequency in active use, preventing its reallocation and ensuring that the signal would be available when the market finally caught up with the medium.

## 4. Power Increase and the Country Pivot

By 1970, KETO-FM had increased its power to 100,000 watts ERP -- a tenfold increase from its original 10,000-watt signal. At 100,000 watts from Cougar Mountain, 101.5 FM became one of the strongest FM signals in the Seattle-Tacoma market. The power increase transformed the station from a local Seattle signal into a regional broadcaster capable of covering the entire Puget Sound basin, from Olympia to Bellingham, from the Cascades to the Olympic Peninsula.

The timing of the power increase was significant. FM listenership was accelerating in the early 1970s, driven by the counterculture's embrace of album-oriented rock (which found its natural home on FM) and by the continued improvement and cost reduction of FM receivers. A 100,000-watt FM signal in 1970 was positioning for a future that was now clearly arriving.

In April 1974, KETO-FM made its first format change, abandoning easy listening for country music. The move reflected a market calculation: country music was experiencing a crossover boom in the early-to-mid 1970s, with artists like Glen Campbell, Johnny Cash, and Charley Pride attracting listeners who might not have identified as country fans. More importantly, country had limited FM competition in Seattle -- KAYO on 1150 AM was the dominant country station, but it operated on the AM band with all its attendant limitations. An FM country station could offer the format's growing audience the stereo fidelity that AM could not deliver.

The country experiment at KETO-FM lasted only two years, but it established a pattern that would define the station's entire history: format changes driven by market opportunity rather than organic audience evolution. The frequency was not building a community; it was seeking one.

| Year | Format | Power | Market Position |
|------|--------|-------|-----------------|
| 1959 | Easy Listening | 10 kW | Marginal FM pioneer |
| 1970 | Easy Listening | 100 kW | Full-power regional signal |
| 1974 | Country | 100 kW | Competing with AM-based KAYO |
| 1976 | Pop Rock Top 40 | 100 kW | New ownership, new identity |

## 5. Gene Autry and Golden West Broadcasters

In 1976, KETO-FM was purchased by Golden West Broadcasters, a media company owned by Gene Autry -- the singing cowboy of 1930s and 1940s Hollywood fame. Autry had built Golden West into a significant broadcasting portfolio that included television stations (KTLA in Los Angeles), radio stations, and the California Angels baseball team. Golden West already owned KVI (570 AM) in Seattle, which carried a popular middle-of-the-road/adult contemporary format.

The acquisition of KETO-FM gave Golden West a paired AM-FM operation in Seattle -- a common strategy in this era, as the FCC's ownership rules allowed a single entity to own one AM and one FM station in the same market. The AM-FM pairing provided operational efficiencies (shared studios, engineering staff, and sales teams) and strategic flexibility (the ability to differentiate formats across the two signals).

Golden West's purchase of 101.5 FM was part of a broader pattern of entertainment industry figures investing in broadcasting. Autry understood that radio stations were not just businesses but community institutions -- assets whose value derived from the relationship between a signal and its audience. His investment in Seattle radio reflected a belief that the Pacific Northwest market was undervalued relative to its growth potential.

> Gene Autry's path from singing cowboy to broadcasting magnate is one of the more unlikely trajectories in American media history. The man who recorded "Rudolph the Red-Nosed Reindeer" in 1949 would, through Golden West Broadcasters, shape the identity of Seattle FM radio for nearly two decades. The cowboy bought the frequency that would ride the disco wave, fight the radio wars, and become Star 101.5.

The significance of the Golden West acquisition extends beyond ownership. It brought professional broadcasting management to a frequency that had operated as a marginal FM signal for seventeen years. Golden West had the resources, the experience, and the strategic vision to transform 101.5 from a frequency into a brand.

## 6. The FM KVI: Pop Rock Top 40

Golden West's first move was to rebrand KETO-FM as "The FM KVI," linking the FM station to the established identity of its AM sibling KVI (570 AM). The new call sign was KVI-FM, and the format shifted to a younger pop-rock Top 40 approach. The first song broadcast under the new format was "Beginnings" by Chicago -- a choice that carried its own symbolism, whether intentional or not.

The decision to brand the FM station as "The FM KVI" was both logical and problematic. On the positive side, it leveraged KVI-AM's established brand recognition among Seattle listeners. KVI had been a fixture on the Seattle AM dial for decades, and associating the FM station with that brand gave it instant credibility. On the negative side, it created confusion between the two signals -- a confusion that would contribute to another rebranding just two years later.

The pop-rock Top 40 format represented a dramatic departure from KETO-FM's easy listening and country heritage. Golden West was positioning the FM station for the audience that was now migrating to the FM band in force: younger listeners who wanted contemporary music in stereo. The format was adult-leaning compared to the pure Top 40 formats on AM stations like KJR, but it was unmistakably aimed at a younger demographic than anything 101.5 had previously targeted.

KVI-FM entered a competitive landscape that was rapidly evolving. KISW had established itself as Seattle's album rock station. KZOK, launched in 1974, was developing its own rock identity. KJR still dominated Top 40 on AM, but its audience was beginning to fragment as FM alternatives multiplied. The FM KVI carved out a position between these competitors -- not as hard-rocking as KISW, not as album-deep as KZOK, and more contemporary than KVI-AM's adult format.

The format's significance in the broader 101.5 story is that it established the station's competitive orientation for the first time. Under Chem-Air, KETO-FM had been content to occupy a frequency. Under Golden West, KVI-FM was competing for audience share. This shift from occupation to competition would define everything that followed.

## 7. Frank Colbourn and the Gold Record Era

The first program director of the rebranded KVI-FM was Frank Colbourn, who relocated from Monterey, California to take the position. Colbourn brought experience from smaller-market California radio and a programming philosophy that emphasized artist relationships and listener connection over pure playlist mechanics.

Under Colbourn's direction, KVI-FM earned twelve gold records from artists including Stevie Wonder, Exile, and Donna Summer. The gold records were not sales certifications for the station itself but acknowledgments from record labels and artists of the station's role in breaking and promoting their music -- a practice common in the 1970s when radio airplay was the primary mechanism through which new music reached listeners. Each gold record represented a relationship between the station, the label, and the artist that was worth maintaining.

Colbourn's tenure at KVI-FM established several precedents that would persist through the station's later incarnations. The emphasis on music discovery -- playing tracks that listeners might not hear elsewhere -- created an identity distinct from the more conservative playlist approaches of AM Top 40. The artist relationships brought promotional appearances, exclusive previews, and a sense that the station was plugged into the music industry rather than simply receiving playlists from a national syndicator.

The gold record count is significant as a measure of industry respect. Twelve gold records in approximately two years (1976--1978) indicates that KVI-FM was punching well above its weight in terms of national music industry attention. A mid-market FM station accumulating gold records at that rate was generating the kind of airplay that labels noticed -- which in turn attracted more promotional attention, creating a virtuous cycle that amplified the station's influence beyond what its audience size alone would have warranted.

> Twelve gold records from a Seattle FM station in two years. Stevie Wonder, Donna Summer, Exile -- artists whose careers were in full acceleration. Colbourn understood that a radio station's relationship with the music industry was a competitive advantage that could not be replicated by simply copying a playlist. The relationships were the format.

## 8. 104.9: The Translator Years

While 101.5 FM was building its identity through KETO-FM and KVI-FM, the 104.9 frequency existed in a fundamentally different state. In the late 1980s, 104.9 appeared on the Seattle-area dial as FM translator K285AE, a low-power repeater that rebroadcast the adult contemporary format of KLTX (now KJEB-FM). An FM translator is not a radio station in the conventional sense -- it has no studios, no staff, no programming of its own. It simply receives a signal from a primary station and retransmits it on a different frequency to extend coverage.

K285AE operated at minimal power, serving a limited geographic area. It was invisible to most Seattle listeners -- a ghost frequency carrying someone else's voice. The translator went dark in the early 1990s when KLTX increased its own transmitter power, rendering the relay unnecessary.

The 104.9 frequency would not return to active broadcasting until July 1995, when KJUN-FM signed on as a full-power station with a country music format. Licensed to Eatonville, Washington -- a small town at the base of Mount Rainier -- the station operated at 17,000 watts ERP, significantly less than the 100,000-watt signal that 101.5 enjoyed from Cougar Mountain. The transmitter location on Alder Cutoff Road East in Eatonville meant that 104.9 was fundamentally a South Sound/Tacoma frequency, not a Seattle signal.

This geographic limitation would define everything about the 104.9 frequency's subsequent history. Where 101.5 commanded the entire Puget Sound basin from its Cougar Mountain perch, 104.9 was perpetually trying to punch above its weight class -- a 17,000-watt signal attempting to compete in a market dominated by 100,000-watt powerhouses. Every format that occupied 104.9 would have to contend with this fundamental asymmetry.

## 9. The Seattle FM Landscape of the 1970s

Understanding the origins of KPLZ requires understanding the competitive landscape into which it was born. By the late 1970s, Seattle's FM dial had evolved from a sparse collection of marginal signals into a vibrant ecosystem of distinct format identities.

The battle lines of the 1980s radio wars were being drawn in the late 1970s, with multiple stations positioning for the Top 40 audience that had traditionally belonged to AM powerhouses.

The significance of this landscape for the KPLZ story is that 101.5's transition from KVI-FM's pop rock to KPLZ's disco-inflected format did not happen in a vacuum. It happened in a market where KISW owned rock, KZOK was building its identity, and the Top 40 space on FM was wide open. The station that occupied 101.5 would be among the first to claim FM Top 40 in Seattle -- a strategic position with enormous potential upside.

## 10. The AM-FM Transition: Why It Mattered

The transition from AM to FM as the dominant medium for music radio is one of the most significant technological shifts in American broadcasting history, and KETO-FM's origins place it at the leading edge of that shift. Understanding why FM eventually won the music audience is essential context for understanding why 101.5 became the valuable property it did.

AM radio operates on the amplitude modulation principle: the signal's amplitude (strength) carries the audio information. This approach is simple, robust, and capable of covering large geographic areas -- a single AM transmitter can serve an entire metropolitan region. But AM has fundamental audio quality limitations. The bandwidth allocated to each AM channel (10 kHz) constrains the frequency response to approximately 50-5,000 Hz -- adequate for voice but inadequate for music reproduction. AM is also susceptible to electrical interference from power lines, motors, lightning, and other sources that produce amplitude variations indistinguishable from the intended signal.

FM radio operates on the frequency modulation principle: the signal's frequency (rather than amplitude) carries the audio information. FM channels have 200 kHz of bandwidth -- twenty times wider than AM -- enabling frequency response from 30 to 15,000 Hz, which captures the full range of musical instruments and human vocals. FM is inherently resistant to amplitude noise because the information is encoded in frequency variations rather than amplitude variations. And FM supports stereo broadcasting, which AM does not in any practical sense.

These technical advantages made FM the inevitable winner for music programming once receiver costs dropped to consumer-affordable levels. The transition was slow -- it took approximately two decades from FM's commercial availability in the 1940s to the point in the late 1960s where FM listening began to grow significantly. But once it began, it was unstoppable. By 1980, FM had surpassed AM in total listenership nationally. By 1990, the transition was essentially complete for music formats.

KETO-FM's 1959 sign-on placed it at the beginning of this transition. The station's easy listening format was perfectly suited to FM's technical strengths: orchestral music, with its wide dynamic range and rich harmonic content, sounded dramatically better on FM than on AM. The station was not competing with AM for the same audience; it was serving a different audience -- one that valued audio quality over programming variety -- through a medium that could deliver what that audience wanted.

| Parameter | AM Radio | FM Radio | Advantage Factor |
|-----------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| Bandwidth | 10 kHz | 200 kHz | 20x |
| Frequency response | 50-5,000 Hz | 30-15,000 Hz | Full audio range |
| Stereo | No (practical) | Yes | Spatial imaging |
| Noise resistance | Low | High | Cleaner signal |
| Coverage per watt | Higher | Lower | AM covers more area |
| Receiver cost (1960) | Lower | Higher | AM more accessible |

The irony of KETO-FM's early years is that its greatest asset -- the FM frequency -- was also its greatest liability. FM's technical superiority was irrelevant if nobody owned an FM receiver. The station was broadcasting superior audio quality to an audience that, in 1959, was too small to measure. The bet was that FM's advantages would eventually attract listeners. The bet was correct, but it took fifteen years to pay off.

## 11. Cougar Mountain: The Strategic Transmitter Site

The transmitter location for 101.5 FM -- Cougar Mountain, in the Issaquah Alps east of Bellevue -- deserves specific attention because it is one of the most important factors in the station's commercial viability. Transmitter placement in FM broadcasting is not merely a technical decision; it is a strategic one that determines coverage area, signal quality, and competitive position.

Cougar Mountain rises to 1,595 feet (486 meters) above sea level, providing excellent line-of-sight to the Seattle metropolitan area, the Eastside communities (Bellevue, Redmond, Kirkland), and the southern Puget Sound. The mountain hosts transmitters for multiple FM and TV stations, making it one of the premier broadcast sites in the Pacific Northwest.

A 100,000-watt FM signal from Cougar Mountain covers approximately 3,500 square miles of the Puget Sound basin, reaching from Olympia to Marysville and from the Cascades to the Olympic Peninsula's eastern edge. This coverage encompasses the entire Seattle-Tacoma metropolitan statistical area (MSA), which is the unit of measurement for Arbitron/Nielsen radio ratings. A station that covers the entire MSA can compete for the full measured audience; a station that covers only part of the MSA is structurally disadvantaged.

The contrast with 104.9's Eatonville transmitter site is stark. Where Cougar Mountain sits in the geographic center of the Seattle metro area, Eatonville is at the southern edge -- 55 miles from downtown Seattle, at the base of Mount Rainier. A signal from Eatonville must travel through terrain, over hills, and across distance to reach Seattle listeners. At 17,000 watts, much of that signal is attenuated before it arrives. The result is a coverage pattern centered on Tacoma and the South Sound, with marginal reception in Seattle proper.

> In FM broadcasting, the transmitter site IS the competitive advantage. Two stations with identical programming, identical talent, and identical promotion budgets will produce vastly different results if one broadcasts from Cougar Mountain and the other from Eatonville. The mountain is the multiplier. Everything else -- format, talent, promotion -- is multiplication applied to the base that geography provides.

## 12. Frequency as Cultural Geography

The origins of KPLZ and the 104.9 frequency invite a way of thinking about radio that the TEX source document calls "frequency-indexed cultural stratigraphy." Each frequency is a core sample. Drill down through the layers at 101.5 and you find easy listening (1959), country (1974), pop rock (1976), and the beginning of the disco pivot that would give birth to K-Plus in 1978. Each layer is a sediment record of ownership economics, audience demographics, and regional identity.

This stratigraphic metaphor is more than literary convenience. Radio frequencies are finite, non-renewable resources allocated by the FCC. Unlike a website or a podcast, a radio station cannot simply be created -- it requires a license to use a specific frequency in a specific market. The history of that frequency is inseparable from the history of the station, because the frequency persists even as call letters, formats, owners, and personalities change.

101.5 megahertz oscillates regardless of whether anyone is broadcasting on it. The electromagnetic wave does not care about format changes, ownership transfers, or cultural significance. But when a broadcaster activates a signal on that frequency, they create a space between -- between the transmitter and the receiver, between the voice and the ear, between the institution and the community. The origins of KPLZ are the origins of that space.

The contrast between 101.5 and 104.9 is instructive from the first moment of their respective histories. 101.5 began as a full-power FM station with the resources (if not the audience) to compete. 104.9 began as a translator -- a signal without a voice of its own. This asymmetry, established at origin, would persist through every subsequent era: 101.5 as the well-funded, full-power competitor; 104.9 as the scrappy underdog searching for identity from its Eatonville perch.

> The geology of a radio frequency: easy listening is the sedimentary base, country music is the first volcanic disruption, and pop rock is the tectonic shift that transforms the landscape entirely. By 1978, the bedrock layer was buried deep, and what rose from the surface would be unrecognizable to anyone who had tuned in at the beginning.

## 11. The First Song: "Beginnings" by Chicago

The choice of "Beginnings" by Chicago as the first song on the newly rebranded FM KVI in 1976 deserves attention because first songs in format launches are never accidental. Program directors select launch tracks with care, understanding that the first song sets the emotional and musical tone for everything that follows. The first song tells the audience what kind of station this is going to be.

"Beginnings" was released by Chicago in 1969 on their debut album Chicago Transit Authority, then re-released as a single in 1971, reaching number 7 on the Billboard Hot 100. The song is a mid-tempo jazz-rock hybrid -- brass-driven, melodic, and sophisticated in a way that distinguishes it from both the bubblegum pop of AM Top 40 and the harder rock of album-oriented FM stations. It is a song that says: this station plays music for adults who care about musicianship.

The title itself carried symbolic weight, whether the program director intended it or not. "Beginnings" -- the station was beginning a new identity, a new format, a new era. The song's opening line could be read as a promise to the listener: wherever you tune in, we will be there. These readings may be over-interpretive, but the practice of choosing symbolically resonant launch tracks is well-documented in radio history. When KPLZ launched the Star era with "Waiting for a Star to Fall," the title connection was unmistakably deliberate. "Beginnings" may have been chosen with similar intentionality.

The first-song tradition connects KVI-FM's 1976 launch to a broader pattern that runs through the entire KPLZ story. Every major format change at the station can be indexed by its first song: "Beginnings" for KVI-FM's pop rock, the lost-to-history first disco track for K-Plus, the Hot Hits launch track, "Waiting for a Star to Fall" for Star 101.5, and "Good Riddance (Time of Your Life)" as the final Star 101.5 song before Hank FM. Each song is a bookmark in the station's cultural stratigraphy -- a marker separating one sediment layer from the next.

The gold records that followed under Frank Colbourn's direction -- Stevie Wonder, Donna Summer, Exile -- trace the musical trajectory from "Beginnings" forward. Chicago's jazz-rock sophistication gave way to the funk and soul of Stevie Wonder, the disco energy of Donna Summer, and the pop-rock crossover of Exile. The musical arc from "Beginnings" to "Hot Stuff" (Donna Summer, 1979) spans the entire sonic territory that KVI-FM and early KPLZ would occupy.

## Appendix: Format Classification Glossary

Understanding the KPLZ origin story requires familiarity with the radio format terminology used throughout this module and the broader research series.

| Format | Full Name | Description |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| Easy Listening | Easy Listening / Beautiful Music | Instrumental arrangements, orchestral versions of popular songs, minimal DJ presence. The default FM format of the 1960s. |
| Country | Country Music | Broadly defined genre including traditional, contemporary, and crossover country. KETO-FM's 1974 experiment targeted this audience. |
| Pop Rock Top 40 | Pop Rock / Top 40 | Contemporary hit music with adult-leaning selection. KVI-FM's 1976 format under Golden West Broadcasters. |
| CHR | Contemporary Hit Radio | The formal industry term for Top 40 radio. Emphasizes current hits across all pop genres. |
| AC | Adult Contemporary | Softer current and recent pop/rock. Less aggressive than CHR, targeting adults 25-54. |
| Hot AC | Hot Adult Contemporary | A subset of AC that leans more current and energetic. Star 101.5's format from 1994-2024. |
| AOR | Album-Oriented Rock | Deep-cut rock format emphasizing album tracks rather than singles. KISW's approach. |
| MOR | Middle of the Road | Adult-oriented mix of easy listening, pop standards, and soft rock. KVI-AM's format. |

These format classifications are not absolute categories but industry shorthand that program directors, consultants, and trade publications use to describe a station's competitive positioning. A station's actual playlist may span multiple format categories; the classification indicates the station's primary audience target and competitive identity.

The distinction between CHR and Hot AC is particularly important for understanding the KPLZ story. CHR plays current hits across all genres -- hip-hop, rock, pop, dance, country crossovers -- without filtering for audience sensibility. Hot AC plays current and recent pop and soft rock while filtering out genres that might cause adult listeners to change the station. The 1994 flip from CHR to Hot AC at KPLZ was a shift from "play everything that is popular" to "play the popular things that adults enjoy." It sounds like a subtle distinction, but it defined two completely different stations with two completely different audience relationships.

The format terminology also reflects the industry's relationship with audience measurement. Arbitron (now Nielsen Audio) surveys measure listening by demographic groups -- the "25-54" demo, the "18-34" demo, the "Women 25-44" target. Each format is designed to maximize listenership within a specific demographic target. CHR targets younger listeners (12-34). Hot AC targets adult women (25-44). The format classification system is, at its core, a system for matching programming strategies to measurable audience segments.

Understanding these classifications is essential because KPLZ's entire history -- from KETO-FM's easy listening through K-Plus disco, Hot Hits CHR, Star Hot AC, and Hank FM country -- is a journey through this taxonomy. Each format change was a recalculation of which audience segment the 101.5 frequency could most profitably serve. The frequency stayed constant at 101.5 megahertz. The target audience rotated through the format classification system like a dial searching for the right setting. The origins documented in this module established the foundation upon which all subsequent rotations would turn.

The parallel story of 104.9 adds another dimension. While 101.5 moved deliberately through format categories with years-long tenures, 104.9 would cycle through nine format identities in three decades -- country, classic country, urban contemporary, active rock, adult hits, active rock again, rhythmic CHR, and finally Contemporary Worship. The same format taxonomy, applied to a weaker signal, produced a fundamentally different history: not the slow stratigraphy of 101.5 but the rapid turbulence of a frequency that could never find its stable layer.

The foundation is laid. The pelican has worn the headphones, Gene Autry has bought the frequency, Frank Colbourn has earned the gold records, and "Beginnings" by Chicago has played its opening notes. What follows is the era when it all mattered most.
