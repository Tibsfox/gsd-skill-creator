Prosocial Persuasion

Prosocial Persuasion at Scale: LLMs Outperform Humans

Paper: arXiv:2604.03202
Categories: cs.CL, cs.CY · Date: April 2026

Key Finding: LLMs generate more effective prosocial persuasion messages than human writers for donation appeals and behavioral change campaigns. The study compares LLM-generated and human-written appeals across multiple charitable causes, measuring actual donation rates and behavioral follow-through (not just self-reported intent). LLM-generated messages achieve 14–23% higher conversion rates depending on the cause category.

Mechanism: The advantage appears to come from three sources: (1) LLMs can rapidly A/B test message variants and converge on effective framings, (2) they naturally adopt empathetic framing without the “compassion fatigue” that affects human copywriters working on difficult causes, and (3) they can personalize messages at scale based on audience demographic signals that would be impractical for human writers to process individually.

Ethical Dimensions: The paper explicitly addresses the dual-use concern: the same capabilities that make LLMs effective at prosocial persuasion could be applied to manipulation. The authors propose a “prosocial certification” framework where persuasion systems are audited for: (1) truthfulness of claims, (2) respect for autonomy (no dark patterns), (3) alignment between the persuader’s stated and actual objectives, and (4) equitable treatment across demographic groups.

Implication: LLM persuasion capability is already beyond human baseline for prosocial applications. The question is no longer whether to deploy it but how to ensure it remains prosocial. The certification framework provides a starting point but requires adversarial testing to verify compliance.

DAG Scheduling

C.A.T. Engine: Deterministic Frame Stability via DAG Scheduling

Paper: zenodo:19427346 — Rawson & Chrzanowski

Key Finding: The C.A.T. (Concurrent Action Topology) engine provides deterministic frame stability for real-time pipelines through DAG-based scheduling. Unlike traditional frame-based systems that drop frames under load, C.A.T. guarantees frame completion by scheduling tasks as a directed acyclic graph with explicit dependency tracking and deadline-aware priority assignment.

Architecture: The engine decomposes each frame into a DAG of micro-tasks with declared dependencies, then schedules them across available cores using a work-stealing algorithm constrained by deadline monotonic priority. Tasks that miss their sub-deadline trigger adaptive quality reduction (lower LOD, reduced particle count) rather than frame drops, maintaining perceptual continuity.

Connection to Agentic Systems: The DAG scheduling pattern maps directly to multi-agent orchestration: agents are nodes, data dependencies are edges, and the scheduling constraint is total context budget rather than frame time. C.A.T.’s adaptive quality reduction is analogous to progressive context compression when an agent pipeline exceeds its token budget.

Implication: Deterministic scheduling for real-time systems provides patterns directly applicable to agentic pipeline orchestration. The key insight — schedule by dependency graph with deadline-aware degradation rather than fixed-order execution — solves the same class of problems in both domains.

Synthesis: Scale Requires Infrastructure

These two entries pair content (what to communicate) with infrastructure (how to schedule it):

  • Persuasion as capability (2604.03202) — LLMs have crossed the human-parity threshold for prosocial persuasion. The 14–23% improvement in conversion rates is significant enough to make LLM-generated appeals the default for charitable organizations with limited marketing budgets.
  • Scheduling as enabler (zenodo:19427346) — Operating persuasion at scale requires deterministic pipeline infrastructure. C.A.T.’s DAG scheduling pattern provides the execution model: dependency-aware, deadline-constrained, gracefully degrading.

The connection to the broader APD corpus: prosocial persuasion must be verifiable (hallucination cluster), its claims must be grounded (context cluster), and its deployment must be secured against misuse (security cluster). No cluster operates in isolation.
