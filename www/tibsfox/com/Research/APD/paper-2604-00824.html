STITCH: Sliding-Memory Trajectory Inference with Task Chunking

arXiv: 2604.00824

Authors: Ye et al.

Date: April 2026 · Categories: cs.AI, cs.SE

Abstract

STITCH introduces a training methodology for code agents using coarse-to-fine noise filtering and decision-critical token retention. The sliding-memory approach preserves only the tokens most relevant to decision points in agent trajectories, enabling training at 30B to 355B scale. Achieves 63.16% on SWE-bench Verified, demonstrating that trajectory-aware training significantly improves agent performance on real-world software engineering tasks.

Key Findings

  1. 63.16% on SWE-bench Verified — state-of-the-art for training methodology
  2. Coarse-to-fine noise filter identifies and removes non-decision-critical tokens
  3. Decision-critical token retention preserves the signal in agent trajectories
  4. Scales from 30B to 355B parameter models with consistent improvement
  5. Multilingual settings demonstrate cross-language generalization
  6. Sliding-memory window prevents context saturation during long trajectories

Methodology

Two-stage training pipeline: (1) coarse filtering removes clearly non-decision tokens (boilerplate, repeated outputs), then (2) fine filtering retains only tokens at decision boundaries (tool selection, error handling, strategy changes). The sliding-memory window moves through trajectories, creating training examples focused on decision quality rather than trajectory length.

Implications for Our Architecture

The decision-critical token retention concept maps to our context management strategy: we already prioritize decision-relevant context (planning documents, state files) over verbatim logs. The sliding-memory approach validates our PostCompact hook strategy for managing context windows. STITCH's noise filtering parallels our carry-forward retrospective pattern — extracting lessons from long execution traces. The 30B-355B scale range covers our operational models (Sonnet through Opus).
