Overthinking Loops: Structural Risk in MCP Tool Interactions

arXiv: 2602.14798

Authors: Lee et al.

Date: February 2026 · Categories: cs.AI, cs.SE

Abstract

Agents interacting with MCP tools enter cyclic tool-call trajectories that amplify token consumption by up to 142.4x. The paper analyzes 14 malicious tools across 3 servers, demonstrating that decoding-time controls are ineffective against structural overthinking. The root cause is the absence of progress detection in agent-tool interaction loops. Tool-call structure analysis is required to identify and break cyclic patterns.

Key Findings

  1. 142.4× token amplification from cyclic tool-call trajectories
  2. 658× maximum cost inflation from overthinking loops (cluster-wide finding)
  3. 12–18% of complex multi-tool tasks trigger overthinking
  4. Three loop patterns: verification spirals (median 340x), exploration loops, plan oscillation (58% most frequent)
  5. Decoding-time controls are ineffective — the problem is structural, not generative
  6. 14 malicious tools across 3 servers tested; all trigger cyclic patterns

Proposed Defenses

Four circuit breaker patterns proposed:

  1. Max-iteration limits per tool — hard cap on repeated invocations
  2. Diminishing-returns detection — flag when new tool calls produce <5% new information
  3. Cost budget enforcement with graceful degradation — stop before economic damage
  4. Progress heuristics based on output entropy change — detect stagnation

Implications for Our Architecture

Current Coverage

Our GUPP propulsion system and polecat-worker pattern already implement progress detection via diminishing-returns heuristics. The token-budget skill enforces cost limits. This paper quantifies the remaining risk: 12-18% of complex tasks still trigger overthinking.

Action Items

  • Formalize all four circuit breaker patterns in the token-budget skill
  • Add output entropy tracking to detect verification spirals (the most costly pattern at 340x)
  • The 58% plan oscillation rate validates our “planning is the hard part” principle — plan once, then execute deterministically
  • Overthinking is structural, not generative — decoding-time controls won't help
