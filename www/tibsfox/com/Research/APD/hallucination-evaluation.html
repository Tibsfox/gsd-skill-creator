Hallucination & Evaluation

Epistemic Mimicry

MIRAGE: Epistemic Mimicry in Visual Understanding

Paper: arXiv:2603.21687
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI · Date: March 2026 · Stanford / Fei-Fei Li group

Key Finding: Models fabricate detailed visual descriptions for images they have not seen at rates exceeding 60%. MIRAGE (Measuring Intelligence via Robust Assessment of Grounded Evaluation) demonstrates that this is not random confabulation but epistemic mimicry — models generate descriptions that are statistically consistent with what they would say if they had seen the image, by drawing on distributional knowledge of what images in that category typically contain.

Mechanism: The models exploit the correlation between image captions in training data and visual features, generating descriptions that are plausible but not grounded in actual perception. This makes fabricated descriptions indistinguishable from genuine ones using surface-level quality metrics.

Implication: Standard evaluation metrics cannot distinguish epistemic mimicry from genuine visual understanding. The 60%+ fabrication rate means that multimodal models are substantially less visually grounded than their output quality suggests. Stanford/Fei-Fei Li research group.

Reference Detection

Detecting and Correcting Reference Hallucinations in Commercial LLMs

Paper: arXiv:2604.03173

Key Finding: A systematic framework for detecting hallucinated references (citations to papers, books, and URLs that do not exist) in commercial LLM outputs. The paper develops a three-stage pipeline: (1) reference extraction using structured parsing, (2) existence verification against academic databases (Semantic Scholar, CrossRef, DBLP), and (3) content alignment checking to verify that existing references actually support the claims attributed to them.

Detection Rates: The pipeline catches 89% of non-existent references and 67% of misattributed claims (where the reference exists but does not support the stated finding). The remaining false negatives are primarily references to non-indexed sources (conference talks, technical reports, blog posts).

Implication: Automated reference verification is feasible and effective for academic citations. The 89% detection rate for non-existent references provides a practical defense layer for research-heavy workflows. The harder problem is misattribution (67%), where the reference exists but the claimed finding is fabricated.

BibTeX Fabrication

BibTeX Citation Hallucinations in Scientific Publishing Agents

Paper: arXiv:2604.03159

Key Finding: Focused specifically on BibTeX-formatted citations generated by LLM agents used in scientific publishing workflows. The paper documents systematic patterns in how models fabricate citation metadata: plausible author names from the correct research community, realistic journal/conference names in the right field, year values consistent with the topic’s research timeline, and DOIs that follow valid format patterns but resolve to 404 errors.

Fabrication Taxonomy: Four categories identified: (1) complete fabrication (all fields invented), (2) partial fabrication (real author, fake paper), (3) citation mashup (combining fields from multiple real papers), and (4) temporal displacement (real paper attributed to wrong year or venue). Category 3 is the hardest to detect because all individual fields verify against real entities.

Implication: Scientific publishing agents require citation verification as a mandatory pipeline stage, not an optional quality check. The sophistication of citation fabrication (especially category 3 mashups) means that human spot-checking is insufficient at scale.

Neurosymbolic Limits

Logical Soundness Is Not Reliable: Neurosymbolic Fact-Checking Limits

Paper: arXiv:2604.04177

Key Finding: Neurosymbolic approaches to fact-checking — where claims are decomposed into logical propositions and verified against knowledge bases — are logically sound but factually unreliable. The logical reasoning chain may be valid (each step follows from the previous), but the premises themselves can be hallucinated. A logically sound argument built on fabricated premises produces confident but wrong conclusions.

Failure Modes: Three identified: (1) premise hallucination (the initial facts are fabricated), (2) granularity mismatch (the knowledge base operates at a different level of specificity than the claim), and (3) temporal staleness (the knowledge base reflects outdated information that has been superseded by more recent findings).

Implication: Logical verification is a necessary but not sufficient component of fact-checking. Systems that report “verified” based solely on logical soundness create false confidence. Fact-checking must verify both the logical chain AND the factual grounding of each premise independently.

Decision Theory

BAS: Decision-Theoretic Confidence Evaluation

Paper: arXiv:2604.03216

Key Finding: BAS (Bayesian Agent Scoring) applies decision theory to LLM confidence calibration. Rather than asking “is this answer correct?” it asks “given the expected cost of acting on a wrong answer, should we act on this answer or seek additional information?” This framing naturally handles the asymmetry between high-stakes and low-stakes decisions without requiring perfect calibration.

Framework: BAS computes an expected utility score for each response by combining: (1) the model’s internal confidence (calibrated via temperature probing), (2) the estimated cost of an incorrect answer (domain-specific), and (3) the cost of deferral (time, computational resources). When expected utility of acting falls below expected utility of deferral, the system escalates to human review or additional verification.

Implication: BAS provides the decision-theoretic bridge between hallucination detection and practical action. Rather than binary “hallucinated/not hallucinated” classification, it produces actionable decisions calibrated to the stakes of each specific query. This is especially relevant for agentic systems where the cost of acting on hallucinated information varies by orders of magnitude across tasks.

Synthesis: The Hallucination Defense Stack

These five papers define a defense-in-depth approach to hallucination:

  1. Detection Layer (2603.21687, 2604.03173) — MIRAGE establishes that hallucination is deeper than factual error; it’s epistemic mimicry at 60%+ rates. Reference detection catches 89% of fabricated citations, 67% of misattributions.
  2. Structural Analysis (2604.03159) — BibTeX citation hallucinations reveal systematic fabrication patterns that are structurally sophisticated (mashups of real citations). Detection must go beyond surface plausibility.
  3. Verification Limits (2604.04177) — Neurosymbolic fact-checking is logically sound but factually unreliable. Logical verification creates false confidence when premises are hallucinated. Both logic AND facts must be verified independently.
  4. Decision Framework (2604.03216) — BAS provides the economic bridge: instead of binary hallucination classification, compute expected utility of acting vs. deferring based on domain-specific stakes.

The critical insight: hallucination is not a solvable bug but a managed risk. The stack provides progressively stronger guarantees but never zero risk. Production systems must design for graceful degradation when hallucination passes all defensive layers.
