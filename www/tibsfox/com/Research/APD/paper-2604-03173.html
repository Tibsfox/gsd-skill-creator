Detecting & Correcting Reference Hallucinations in Commercial LLMs

arXiv: 2604.03173

Date: April 2026 · Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI

Abstract

A detection methodology for reference hallucinations in commercial LLMs with precision/recall analysis. Compares hallucination rates across commercial LLMs including deep research modes, identifying that extended reasoning often increases rather than decreases hallucination rates for citations. Proposes correction strategies and a citation verification pipeline for research automation workflows.

Key Findings

  1. Detection methodology achieves measurable precision and recall for reference hallucinations
  2. Deep research modes often increase hallucination rates for citations, not decrease them
  3. Commercial LLM comparison reveals significant variance in hallucination profiles
  4. Correction strategies reduce but do not eliminate fabricated references
  5. Citation verification pipeline can be automated but requires external API access
  6. Reference hallucinations follow predictable patterns amenable to rule-based detection

Methodology

Automated reference extraction from LLM outputs, cross-referenced against bibliographic databases (CrossRef, Semantic Scholar, Google Scholar). Precision/recall measured at the individual citation level. Commercial LLMs compared under standard and deep research modes. Correction strategies tested iteratively with feedback loops.

Implications for Our Architecture

Directly relevant to our research-engine and data-fidelity skills. The finding that deep research modes increase citation hallucination validates our sources-first methodology: we fetch and verify sources before generating content, rather than generating and verifying afterward. Our publish-pipeline should include automated citation verification against CrossRef/Semantic Scholar. The counterintuitive deep-research finding warns against assuming more reasoning = more accuracy.
