Logical Soundness Is Not Reliable for Neurosymbolic Fact-Checking

arXiv: 2604.04177

Date: April 2026 · Categories: cs.AI, cs.LO

Abstract

Demonstrates that logical soundness as an evaluation criterion fails for neurosymbolic fact-checking systems. The paper presents experimental evidence that current approaches produce logically sound but factually wrong conclusions, because the symbolic reasoning component inherits errors from the neural component's knowledge extraction. Proposes alternative evaluation criteria and discusses implications for automated verification systems.

Key Findings

  1. Logical soundness and factual correctness are decoupled in neurosymbolic systems
  2. Sound reasoning over wrong premises produces wrong conclusions that look verified
  3. Current neurosymbolic approaches inherit neural extraction errors into symbolic reasoning
  4. Alternative evaluation criteria needed: factual grounding, premise verification, source tracing
  5. Automated verification creates false confidence when the verification itself is compromised
  6. Experimental evidence demonstrates systematic failure modes across multiple benchmarks

Implications for Our Architecture

Validates our distrust of purely automated verification. Our GSD verifier uses human-in-the-loop confirmation precisely because automated verification can produce “sound but wrong” results. The premise verification requirement maps to our sources-first research methodology: verify the inputs, not just the reasoning chain. This paper argues against the neurosymbolic fact-checking approach some systems use for automated truth claims. Our data-fidelity skill should prioritize source tracing over logical analysis.
