MIRAGE: Epistemic Mimicry in Visual Understanding

arXiv: 2603.21687

Authors: Asadi et al. (Stanford, Fei-Fei Li)

Date: March 2026 · Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI

Abstract

MIRAGE demonstrates that multimodal LLMs fabricate visual descriptions for absent images at 60%+ rates. Models achieve 70-80% benchmark accuracy without actually processing images, relying on textual cues and priors. A 3B text-only model beats radiologists on chest X-ray benchmarks, revealing that 99% of medical vision benchmarks are vulnerable to epistemic mimicry. Introduces B-Clean evaluation framework to distinguish genuine visual understanding from fabrication.

Key Findings

  1. 60%+ fabrication rate when images are absent
  2. 70-80% benchmark accuracy achieved without processing images
  3. A 3B text-only model outperforms radiologists on chest X-ray benchmarks
  4. 99% of medical vision benchmarks vulnerable to epistemic mimicry
  5. B-Clean framework separates genuine visual understanding from text-prior exploitation
  6. Models learn benchmark patterns, not visual reasoning — Goodhart’s Law at scale

Methodology

Controlled experiments presenting multimodal LLMs with questions about images that are absent, corrupted, or replaced. Models are scored on whether they admit image absence or fabricate descriptions. B-Clean framework provides three evaluation modes: with-image (baseline), without-image (hallucination detection), and text-only-model (upper bound on text-prior exploitation). Medical benchmarks specifically tested with radiological images removed.

Implications for Our Architecture

Verification Impact

Critical validation for our data-fidelity skill and verification-first philosophy. If models fabricate 60%+ of visual descriptions, they fabricate in all modalities when not grounded. MIRAGE quantifies why benchmarks are compromised by Goodhart's Law.

Concrete Actions

  • B-Clean evaluation methodology should inform how we validate image-to-mission and pic2html outputs
  • Never trust model claims about image content without cross-verification
  • Our research-engine pipeline already requires source verification — this paper quantifies the stakes
  • The 3B text-only finding demonstrates that benchmark scores are not capability measures
