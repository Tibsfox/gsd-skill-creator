Mathematics of Tides

Harmonic analysis, sinusoidal decomposition, and the 37 constituents that govern Puget Sound
Dashboard Weather Science Convergence Zone Tides Math Weather Math Research
The tides are among the most predictable phenomena in nature. Their regularity arises from the clockwork of orbital mechanics — the Moon's orbit around Earth, Earth's orbit around the Sun, the tilt of Earth's axis, the ellipticity of each orbit. Each gravitational influence contributes a sinusoidal component. The tide at any location is the sum of these components. This is harmonic analysis, and it is one of the oldest applications of Fourier mathematics.

The Fundamental Idea

Tides as a Sum of Sinusoids

The central insight of tidal theory is that the complex, seemingly irregular rise and fall of water level at any coastal location can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoidal (cosine) waves, each with a known period determined by the astronomy of the Earth-Moon-Sun system.

Tidal Height Prediction h(t) = H0 + Σ fi · Hi · cos(ait + V0i + ui − κi)

Where:

  • h(t) — predicted tide height at time t
  • H0 — mean water level above the reference datum
  • Hi — mean amplitude of constituent i at this location
  • fi — node factor, adjusting amplitude for the 18.6-year lunar nodal cycle
  • ai — angular speed of constituent i (degrees per hour)
  • V0i + ui — astronomical argument at the reference time
  • κi — epoch (phase lag) of constituent i at this location

The amplitude Hi and epoch κi are the harmonic constants — they are specific to each location and must be determined from observed tidal data. Everything else is computed from astronomical theory.

The M2 constituent dominates: At most locations worldwide, M2 accounts for roughly 45% of the total tidal range. Its 12.42-hour period (half the lunar day of 24 hours 50 minutes) explains why high tides come about 50 minutes later each day — the Moon advances in its orbit, and Earth must rotate a bit further to catch up.

Why 37?

Each constituent represents a specific astronomical cycle: the Moon's orbital period, the Sun's apparent annual motion, the Moon's nodal regression (18.6 years), the eccentricity of orbits, and their various combinations. The 37 standard constituents capture the dominant gravitational influences. Some specialized analyses use up to 60 or even 100+ constituents for higher precision, but the 37 standard ones predict tides to within a few centimeters at most stations.

To determine all 37 constituents at a new location, NOAA requires a minimum of one full year of continuous water level measurements. A 30-day record can resolve most of the major constituents, but the longer record is needed to separate closely-spaced frequencies (like S2 at 12.00 hours and K2 at 11.97 hours).

Mixed Semidiurnal Tides in Puget Sound

The Diurnal Inequality

Puget Sound experiences mixed semidiurnal tides — two high tides and two low tides per day, but of unequal height. This creates four distinct daily levels:

  • Higher High Water (HHW) — the taller of the two daily highs
  • Lower High Water (LHW) — the shorter of the two daily highs
  • Higher Low Water (HLW) — the shallower of the two daily lows
  • Lower Low Water (LLW) — the deeper of the two daily lows

The inequality arises from the interaction of diurnal constituents (K1, O1, P1 — one cycle per day) with semidiurnal constituents (M2, S2, N2 — two cycles per day). When the diurnal signal is in phase with one of the semidiurnal peaks, that peak is enhanced while the other is suppressed.

What this means at Mukilteo: On the Mukilteo Observatory dashboard, you see four tide events per day (from NOAA Station 9447659, Everett). The heights of the two highs and two lows are typically different. The Lower Low Water (LLW) is the most important for beach access, crabbing, and tidal flat exposure — it determines when the maximum shoreline is accessible at Lighthouse Park, Picnic Point, and Kayak Point.

Connection to the Unit Circle

Tides and Trigonometry

The tidal prediction formula is built entirely from cosine functions — the same cosine that lives on the unit circle. Each tidal constituent traces a point around the unit circle at its own angular speed:

Unit Circle Connection cos(θ) where θ = a · t + φ

As time advances, the angle θ increases at rate a (the angular speed of the constituent). The cosine of this angle gives the vertical displacement. Multiple cosines at different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases sum to produce the complex tidal curve.

This is the core insight of Fourier analysis: any periodic signal can be decomposed into a sum of sinusoids. Tides are one of the purest natural examples because the driving frequencies are known exactly from orbital mechanics.

TSPB Layer 1 (Unit Circle): The unit circle provides the geometric foundation for understanding how cosine waves generate the tidal signal. Each constituent's amplitude determines how far from center its circle extends; its phase determines where on the circle it starts.

TSPB Layer 3 (Trigonometry): Tidal harmonic analysis is applied trigonometry. The sum-of-cosines formula, phase relationships, and beat frequencies between constituents all rely on trigonometric identities taught in this layer.

Beat Frequencies: Spring and Neap Tides

When two cosine waves of slightly different frequency are added, they produce a beat pattern — the envelope oscillates at the difference frequency. The most important tidal beat is between M2 (lunar, 12.42 hours) and S2 (solar, 12.00 hours):

Spring-Neap Beat Period Tbeat = 1 / |fM2 − fS2| = 1 / |1/12.42 − 1/12.00| ≈ 14.77 days

This is the spring-neap cycle: roughly every 14.8 days, the lunar and solar tidal forces align (spring tide, maximum range) or oppose (neap tide, minimum range). At Mukilteo, the difference between spring and neap tidal ranges can be over 1.5 meters.

Historical Tide Prediction

1872–1873 — Lord Kelvin's Tide-Predicting Machine

Sir William Thomson (later Lord Kelvin) designed and built the first mechanical tide-predicting machine in collaboration with Edward Roberts and Alexander Légé. The machine was a special-purpose mechanical analog computer — one of the earliest purpose-built computing devices.

Pairs of toothed disks rotated at rates corresponding to each tidal constituent's period. Wire linkages summed the displacements, and a pen traced the predicted tidal curve on a moving strip of paper. Turning a crank advanced time. A year's tidal predictions for any location could be plotted in about four hours.

1876–1879 — Improved Machines

Kelvin refined his design, building machines of increasing complexity. His final version summed 24 tidal constituents. The British Association for the Advancement of Science funded construction, recognizing the machine's importance for navigation and harbor operations.

1882–1910 — U.S. Coast and Geodetic Survey Machines

The United States built its own tide-predicting machines, starting with a 19-constituent machine in 1882 designed by William Ferrel. A larger 37-constituent machine (Tide Predicting Machine No. 2, or "Old Brass Brains") entered service in 1910 and remained in use until 1965 — over half a century of continuous service. It produced official NOAA tide predictions for every U.S. port.

1965–Present — Digital Computation

Electronic computers replaced mechanical machines in the 1960s. The mathematics remains identical — the sum-of-cosines formula has not changed since Kelvin's time. What changed is the speed and precision: a modern computer evaluates the 37-constituent sum millions of times per second, enabling real-time predictions with 6-minute resolution for every NOAA tide station simultaneously.

Modern NOAA Tide Prediction

The CO-OPS Process

NOAA's Center for Operational Oceanographic Products and Services (CO-OPS) maintains the national tide prediction system. The process:

  • Data collection: Continuous water level measurements at primary tide stations (6-minute intervals, 24/7)
  • Harmonic analysis: Least-squares fitting of the 37-constituent model to observed data, extracting amplitude and phase for each constituent at each station
  • Quality control: Comparison with previous analyses, identification of secular trends (sea level rise), and datum adjustments
  • Prediction generation: Application of the harmonic formula to produce 6-minute predicted water levels for the upcoming year
  • Real-time correction: Comparison of predictions with observations to detect storm surge, wind setup, or other non-tidal influences

Our Mukilteo Observatory uses NOAA Station 9447659 (Everett) predictions via the CO-OPS API. The high/low predictions shown on the dashboard are extracted from the full 6-minute prediction time series.

Oscillator Synchronization

Tides and the Kuramoto Model

The tidal system is a natural example of coupled oscillators — multiple periodic signals interacting to produce emergent behavior. The Kuramoto model, which describes synchronization in systems of coupled oscillators, provides a mathematical framework for understanding how tidal constituents interact.

In the Kuramoto model, each oscillator has a natural frequency and couples to other oscillators through a coupling strength. In tidal analysis, the "oscillators" are the gravitational forcing terms, and the "coupling" occurs through the response of the ocean basin (its natural resonant frequencies, friction, and geometry).

The beat frequencies between tidal constituents (spring-neap cycle, monthly inequality, the 4.4-year perigean cycle) are direct analogs of the beat patterns seen in coupled oscillator systems.
