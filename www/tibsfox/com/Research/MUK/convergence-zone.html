Puget Sound Convergence Zone

How the Olympics split the wind and Mukilteo catches the seam
Dashboard Weather Science Convergence Zone Tides Math Weather Math Research
The Puget Sound Convergence Zone is one of the most studied mesoscale meteorological phenomena in the Pacific Northwest. It is also the single largest source of weather forecast error for Mukilteo. Understanding it is understanding why weather models disagree here.

How the PSCZ Forms

Step 1: The Olympic Split

When Pacific air flows eastward toward western Washington, it encounters the Olympic Mountains — a massive barrier rising to nearly 8,000 feet (Mt. Olympus, 7,980 ft). The low-level airflow cannot go over the range. Instead, it splits into two branches:

  • Northern branch: Flows eastward through the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the 100-mile-long channel between the Olympic Peninsula and Vancouver Island
  • Southern branch: Flows around the southern flank of the Olympics through the Chehalis Gap, the low-elevation pass between the Olympics and the Willapa Hills

This split typically occurs when low-level winds are from the west to northwest, at speeds sufficient to create dynamic flow separation — usually 15-25 knots at 850 mb (roughly 5,000 feet).

Step 2: The Convergence

East of the Olympics, the two branches re-converge over the Puget Sound lowlands. The northern branch curves southward after exiting the Strait of Juan de Fuca, while the southern branch curves northward after passing through the Chehalis Gap. Where they meet, the air has nowhere to go but up.

This forced ascent creates a narrow band of enhanced cloudiness and precipitation, typically 10-20 miles wide. The band usually sets up somewhere between north Seattle (around Shoreline/Mountlake Terrace) and south Everett — precisely over the Mukilteo area.

The Cascade Range to the east acts as a backstop, preventing the converging air from continuing eastward and intensifying the upward forcing.

Step 3: Enhanced Precipitation

The convergence-driven uplift triggers convection. Moist Pacific air, already near saturation from its ocean crossing, quickly condenses as it rises. The result can range from persistent drizzle to intense thunderstorms, depending on the instability of the atmosphere.

In winter, when the air aloft is cold enough, the PSCZ produces snow — sometimes heavy snow — in locations that would otherwise receive rain at sea level. The convergence zone's uplift cools the air sufficiently to cross the rain/snow threshold.

The Mathematics of Convergence

Fluid Dynamics of Flow Splitting

The Olympic Mountains' interaction with low-level flow can be characterized by the Froude number, a dimensionless ratio that determines whether air flows over or around a barrier:

Froude Number Fr = U / (N · h)

Where U is the upstream wind speed, N is the Brunt-Väisälä frequency (a measure of atmospheric stability), and h is the mountain height. When Fr < 1 (subcritical flow), the air is blocked and must flow around the barrier. When Fr > 1 (supercritical flow), the air flows over the top.

For typical PSCZ conditions (U = 10-15 m/s, N = 0.01 s−1, h = 2400 m for the Olympics), Fr ranges from 0.4 to 0.6 — solidly in the blocked regime. This is why the flow splits rather than climbing over.

The resolution problem: The PSCZ band is typically 10-20 km wide. The GFS global model has a grid spacing of approximately 13 km, meaning the entire convergence zone fits within 1-2 grid cells. The model literally cannot resolve the spatial structure of the event. Higher-resolution models like the HRRR (3 km grid) and the UW WRF (1.3 km grid) perform significantly better, but even they struggle with the exact position of the convergence band.

Convergence and Vertical Velocity

The vertical velocity generated by convergence can be estimated from the horizontal convergence of the wind field:

Continuity Equation (simplified) w = −∫(∂u/∂x + ∂v/∂y) dz

Where w is vertical velocity, u and v are horizontal wind components, and the integral is from the surface to the top of the convergent layer. Typical PSCZ events produce vertical velocities of 0.5-2 m/s — modest by thunderstorm standards, but sustained over hours, enough to produce significant precipitation totals.

History of PSCZ Research

University of Washington Atmospheric Sciences

The Puget Sound Convergence Zone has been a central research topic at the UW Department of Atmospheric Sciences for decades. Professor Cliff Mass and his students have systematically studied the phenomenon, completing numerous papers on its dynamics, prediction, and interaction with the broader Pacific Northwest weather regime.

Mass's research group runs the most extensive local high-resolution prediction system in the United States through the Northwest Modeling Consortium. Their WRF (Weather Research and Forecasting) model runs at 1.3 km grid spacing over the Puget Sound region — fine enough to begin resolving convergence zone structure.

Key research milestones include:

  • Early characterizations of PSCZ dynamics in the 1980s-1990s, establishing the flow-splitting mechanism
  • A 1997 paper in the Monthly Weather Review examining the interaction of warm-season frontal systems with the coastal mountains, including detailed analysis of PSCZ formation
  • Numerical simulation studies using increasingly high-resolution models to capture the mesoscale structure
  • Development of ensemble forecasting techniques tailored to PSCZ prediction uncertainty

Notable PSCZ Events

December 18, 1990 — "The Mother of All Convergence Zones"

A powerful PSCZ stalled over the Seattle area around noon and produced 12 inches of snow by evening. This remains the benchmark PSCZ snowfall event. The snow stayed on the ground for more than a week, paralyzing the region. The convergence zone's precise positioning determined which neighborhoods were buried and which received only rain — a distance of a few miles.

January 2012 — PSCZ in Satellite Imagery

A well-documented convergence zone was captured in GOES satellite imagery, clearly showing the narrow band of enhanced cloud development stretching from the Strait of Juan de Fuca across the Puget Sound lowlands. The event was analyzed by Colorado State University's VISIT meteorological interpretation blog as a teaching case.

March 2025 — "Super Convergence Zone"

An unusually intense PSCZ event produced heavy precipitation across the convergence band. Professor Cliff Mass documented the event on his widely read weather blog, noting the exceptional strength of the convergence and the challenges it posed for operational forecasting.

Recurring: PSCZ Snow Events

PSCZ-driven snowfall events occur multiple times per winter, typically delivering localized totals of 3-8 inches in narrow bands. Notable locations that frequently fall under the convergence band include Shoreline, Mountlake Terrace, Lynnwood, Mukilteo, and Woodinville. One event dumped 3.5 inches in Shoreline while depositing 6.5 inches just a few miles northeast in Woodinville.

PSCZ Frequency and Seasonality

The convergence zone is not a rare event. Typically 10-20 convergence zones form per year over the Puget Sound lowlands. However, their frequency varies seasonally:

SeasonFrequencyCharacter
March – JuneHighestMost frequent; post-frontal northwest flow common in spring. Often produces showers or thunderstorms.
October – FebruaryModerateLess frequent but more impactful when they occur; winter events can produce heavy snow.
July – SeptemberLowestPacific high suppresses onshore flow; convergence zone events rare but possible.

Why Regional Models Struggle

The Resolution Problem

Weather models divide the atmosphere into a three-dimensional grid of cells. The model solves the equations of fluid dynamics at each grid point. Features smaller than approximately 4-6 grid cells cannot be properly resolved.

ModelGrid SpacingEffective ResolutionCan Resolve PSCZ?
GFS (global)~13 km~52-78 kmNo — PSCZ is 10-20 km wide
NAM (regional)~12 km~48-72 kmNo
HRRR (high-res)3 km~12-18 kmMarginally — detects presence but not exact position
UW WRF1.3 km~5-8 kmYes — resolves structure reasonably well
ICON (DWD)~13 km~52-78 kmNo
What this means for our scoring: When Open-Meteo (which uses ICON, GFS, or ERA5 backend models) reports "overcast" while KPAE observes "clear," it may be correctly identifying the presence of a convergence zone cloudband — just placing it at the wrong coordinates. A 10 km position error in the PSCZ band is the difference between heavy rain and clear skies at any given point.

The Sensitivity Problem

Even high-resolution models struggle with the exact position of the convergence band because it is exquisitely sensitive to initial conditions. Small changes in the upstream wind speed, direction, or stability can shift the convergence line by 10-20 km north or south. This is a classic example of mesoscale predictability limits — the atmosphere's chaotic nature amplified by topographic forcing.

Ensemble forecasting (running 20-50 model simulations with slightly perturbed initial conditions) helps quantify this uncertainty. When ensemble members disagree about the PSCZ position, forecasters know to use language like "chance of showers" rather than definitive predictions.

Connection to Weather Scoring

The PSCZ is the single largest contributor to forecast error in our Mukilteo scoring system:

  • Temperature MAE: Convergence zone cloud cover can suppress daytime highs by 3-5°C compared to clear areas just miles away
  • Precipitation F1: Binary rain prediction fails when the model places the PSCZ band 5 km from its actual position
  • Timing accuracy: Convergence zone onset and dissipation are difficult to time; the band can develop or collapse in under an hour
  • Confidence calibration: Models that correctly identify PSCZ uncertainty (wide ensemble spread) score better on Brier score than models that confidently place the band in the wrong location
