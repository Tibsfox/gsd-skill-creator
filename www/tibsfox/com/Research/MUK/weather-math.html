Mathematics of Weather Scoring

MAE, F1, Brier score — the statistical foundations behind our accuracy rankings
Dashboard Weather Science Convergence Zone Tides Math Weather Math Research
Every number on the Mukilteo Observatory dashboard has a mathematical definition. When we say a weather source has a temperature MAE of 0.23°C, or a precipitation F1 of 0.82, or a Brier score of 0.14 — each of those numbers encodes a specific relationship between prediction and reality. This page derives and explains each one.

1. Mean Absolute Error (MAE)

Definition

Mean Absolute Error measures the average magnitude of errors between paired predictions and observations, without regard to their direction. It answers: on average, how far off was the forecast?

Mean Absolute Error MAE = (1/n) · Σ|ŷi − yi|

Where ŷi is the predicted value (forecast temperature), yi is the observed value (KPAE actual temperature), and n is the number of observations in the scoring window.

Why MAE for Temperature?

MAE is preferred over RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) for weather scoring because it treats all errors equally. RMSE amplifies large outliers — a single 10°C miss would dominate the score even if every other prediction was within 0.5°C. MAE gives a more intuitive "typical error" for daily use.

For our Mukilteo scoring, MAE is calculated over a rolling 30-day window. This means the score responds to recent model performance changes while smoothing out single-day anomalies.

Microclimate note: KPAE (Paine Field) sits 2 miles inland from Possession Sound. Waterfront temperatures can differ by 1-3°C due to marine influence. An MAE of 1.5°C against KPAE actuals might not mean the forecast is wrong for the waterfront — it might mean the station itself doesn't represent shoreline conditions. This is why we track multiple sources.

Interpreting MAE Scores

MAE RangeRatingInterpretation
0.0 – 0.5°CExcellentForecast is within instrument precision for most readings
0.5 – 1.5°CGoodReliable for planning; within the microclimate variation range
1.5 – 3.0°CFairNoticeable bias; source may be reporting for a different microclimate
> 3.0°CPoorSource is not tracking local conditions; likely a different climate zone

2. Wind Accuracy Scoring

Combined Direction and Speed Score

Wind accuracy is harder than temperature because it has two components: direction (a circular quantity) and speed (a linear quantity). We combine them into a single score.

Direction Error (circular) Δθ = min(|θpred − θobs|, 360 − |θpred − θobs|)

Direction error must account for the circular nature of compass headings. A forecast of 350° when the actual is 10° is a 20° error, not a 340° error. The formula above handles the wrap-around at 0°/360°.

Speed Ratio Rspeed = min(vpred, vobs) / max(vpred, vobs)

Speed ratio ranges from 0 (completely wrong) to 1 (perfect match). Using a ratio rather than absolute difference means a 5 km/h error matters more in light winds (5 vs 10 km/h) than in strong winds (35 vs 40 km/h).

Possession Sound channeling: Marine wind funnels up the sound from the northwest. Models that use coarser grid spacing miss this channeling effect, reporting winds from the west when actual flow is from the north-northwest. This is why wind direction error is often the most revealing metric for distinguishing local-aware models from regional ones.

3. Precipitation F1 Score

Binary Classification of Rain

Precipitation scoring is fundamentally a classification problem: did it rain or not? Each forecast-observation pair falls into one of four categories:

Actually RainedActually Dry
Forecast: RainTrue Positive (TP)False Positive (FP)
Forecast: DryFalse Negative (FN)True Negative (TN)

Precision, Recall, and F1

Precision Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

Precision answers: when the forecast said rain, how often did it actually rain? A cautious model that only predicts rain when very confident will have high precision but may miss many rain events.

Recall Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

Recall answers: of all the times it rained, how often did the forecast predict it? An aggressive model that always predicts rain will have perfect recall but terrible precision.

F1 Score F1 = 2 · (Precision · Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall. It ranges from 0 (worst) to 1 (perfect). The harmonic mean punishes imbalance — a model needs both good precision and good recall to score well. You cannot game F1 by being aggressive or cautious.

Why F1 matters at Mukilteo: The Puget Sound Convergence Zone (PSCZ) makes binary rain prediction the hardest metric. Rain can fall on Mukilteo while Everett stays dry, or vice versa. A model that predicts rain for the entire region will have high recall but poor precision at any specific point. F1 rewards models that correctly locate the precipitation band.

4. Fog Prediction

The Dewpoint Spread Threshold

Fog prediction is another binary classification problem, but instead of directly forecasting "fog" or "no fog," we use the dewpoint spread as a proxy:

Dewpoint Spread S = Tair − Tdew

When the spread drops below approximately 2.5°C, the air is near saturation and fog formation becomes likely. Two mechanisms produce fog at Mukilteo:

Fog TypeMechanismTypical Conditions
Radiation fogGround cools overnight, air temperature drops to dewpointClear, calm nights; autumn/winter
Advection fogCool marine air from Possession Sound meets warmer landSummer mornings; onshore flow

We score fog prediction using the same F1 framework as precipitation: was fog predicted when it actually formed? The dewpoint spread threshold translates a continuous measurement into a binary classification.

5. Confidence Calibration — The Brier Score

What Is Calibration?

When a forecast says "80% chance of rain," does it actually rain 80% of the time? A well-calibrated forecast has this property at every probability level. The Brier score, introduced by American meteorologist Glenn W. Brier in 1950, measures exactly this.

Brier Score BS = (1/n) · Σ(pi − oi)²

Where pi is the predicted probability (e.g., 0.80 for "80% chance of rain") and oi is the actual outcome (1 if it rained, 0 if it didn't). The Brier score ranges from 0 (perfect) to 1 (worst possible).

Brier Score Decomposition

The power of the Brier score lies in its decomposability into three components, each revealing a different aspect of forecast quality:

Decomposition BS = Reliability − Resolution + Uncertainty
ComponentWhat It MeasuresGoal
ReliabilityHow close predicted probabilities are to observed frequenciesMinimize (0 = perfectly calibrated)
ResolutionHow much forecasts vary from the base rateMaximize (forecasts should discriminate)
UncertaintyInherent variability of the event being predictedNot controllable (property of the climate)

A forecast can have a good Brier score in two very different ways: by being well-calibrated (low reliability term) or by being highly discriminating (high resolution term). The decomposition lets us diagnose which aspect a model excels at.

Calibration vs. accuracy: A forecast that says "50% chance of rain every day" in Seattle would be roughly calibrated (it rains about half the days) but have zero resolution — it never distinguishes rainy days from dry days. A good forecast needs both calibration AND resolution.

Interpreting Brier Scores

Brier ScoreQualityContext
0.00 – 0.10ExcellentWorld-class probabilistic forecasting
0.10 – 0.20GoodWell-calibrated; typical of NWS probabilistic forecasts
0.20 – 0.30FairSome calibration issues; still better than climatology
> 0.25PoorClimatological baseline (always predicting the base rate) scores ~0.25 for Seattle rain

6. Timing Accuracy

Front Arrival Error

Timing accuracy measures how well a forecast predicts when weather changes will occur. If the forecast says rain starts at 2 PM and it actually starts at 5 PM, the timing error is 3 hours.

Timing Error Δt = |tpredicted − tobserved|

This metric is especially important for convergence zone events, which are notoriously difficult to time. The PSCZ can develop rapidly as wind patterns shift, and models that correctly predict the onset of convergence zone precipitation score significantly higher.

We measure timing accuracy for three event types:

EventDetection MethodTypical Error Range
Precipitation onsetFirst non-zero precipitation in METAR1–6 hours
Wind shiftDirection change > 45° within 1 hour2–8 hours
Temperature frontRate of change > 2°C/hour1–4 hours

Statistical Foundations

Mean, Variance, Standard Deviation

All weather scoring rests on these three pillars of descriptive statistics:

Arithmetic Mean μ = (1/n) · Σxi
Variance σ² = (1/n) · Σ(xi − μ)²
Standard Deviation σ = √(σ²)

The mean tells us the central tendency of a set of observations. Variance measures the average squared deviation from the mean — how spread out the data is. Standard deviation is the square root of variance, returning the spread to the original units (degrees Celsius, km/h, etc.).

In weather scoring, standard deviation matters because it defines the natural variability of conditions. A temperature MAE of 1°C is excellent when the standard deviation of temperature is 8°C, but mediocre when standard deviation is 2°C.

Bayesian Reasoning in Weather Prediction

Prior, Likelihood, Posterior

Modern weather forecasting is fundamentally Bayesian. Each model run begins with a prior (the current best estimate of atmospheric state), incorporates new evidence (observations from weather stations, satellites, radiosondes), and produces a posterior (the updated forecast).

Bayes' Theorem P(H|E) = P(E|H) · P(H) / P(E)

In weather forecasting terms:

TermWeather MeaningExample
P(H)Prior probabilityClimatological rain frequency for March in Mukilteo (~48%)
P(E|H)LikelihoodGiven rain, what's the probability of seeing this pressure pattern?
P(H|E)Posterior probabilityGiven this pressure pattern, what's the updated rain probability?
P(E)EvidenceHow common is this pressure pattern overall?

Ensemble forecasting (running multiple model simulations with slightly different initial conditions) is an applied form of Bayesian reasoning. The spread of the ensemble represents the posterior uncertainty. When ensemble members agree, confidence is high; when they diverge, we're in a regime where small perturbations lead to large forecast differences — exactly what happens during PSCZ events.
