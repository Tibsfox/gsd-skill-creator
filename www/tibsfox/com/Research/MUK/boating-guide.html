Boating Guide — Puget Sound & Lake Washington

Boat launches, marinas, the Ballard Locks, and freshwater access

The waters around Mukilteo connect to an extraordinary network: from the open reaches of Possession Sound and Puget Sound, through the Ballard Locks, and into the fresh waters of Lake Union and Lake Washington. Understanding the launches, the locks, and the navigation hazards is essential for any boater in the region — whether you are crabbing off Hat Island, transiting to the San Juans, or spending a summer afternoon on Lake Washington.

1. Puget Sound Boat Launches

Puget Sound offers hundreds of miles of navigable saltwater, from the industrial port facilities of Everett to the recreational marinas of Shilshole Bay. The following launches provide the primary access points relevant to Mukilteo-area boaters.

Port of Everett — Jetty Landing

Address: 1720 W Marine View Dr, Everett, WA 98201
Lanes: 13 · Fee: $15/day · Annual passes available
Tide-dependent: No — deep-water launch with floats at all tide levels

The largest public boat launch on the central Puget Sound coast. Thirteen lanes with boarding floats make this the go-to launch for serious boaters. Direct access to Possession Sound, the Snohomish River delta, and north Puget Sound. Popular staging point for Dungeness crab fishing off Hat Island and Possession Point, as well as salmon fishing runs to the Everett Flats and Tulalip Bay.

The Port of Everett Marina complex includes guest moorage, fuel docks, pump-out stations, and the Waterfront Place shops and restaurants. Ample trailer parking in the adjacent lots, though weekends in summer fill quickly by mid-morning.

Mukilteo Lighthouse Launch

Address: Adjacent to Mukilteo Lighthouse Park, 609 Front St, Mukilteo, WA 98275
Lanes: 1 · Fee: Free · Public access
Tide-dependent: Yes — usable only at mid to high tide

A single-lane concrete ramp at the south end of Mukilteo Lighthouse Park. The ramp is exposed at low tide and becomes unusable below approximately +4 ft MLLW. Best suited for small boats, kayaks, and trailer boats that can be hand-launched. Provides direct access to Possession Sound and the Mukilteo waterfront.

The location is immediately adjacent to the Mukilteo-Clinton ferry terminal. Boaters must exercise extreme caution around ferry traffic — the WSF Tokitae and other ferries make approximately 24 crossings daily and have right-of-way in the channel.

Tide planning: Check current tide tables before launching. See Tides Math for tide prediction models and harmonic analysis at the Mukilteo reference station.

Edmonds Marina — Port of Edmonds

Address: 336 Admiral Way, Edmonds, WA 98020
Lanes: 4 · Fee: $10/day · Annual permits from Port of Edmonds
Tide-dependent: Minimal — protected basin with adequate depth at most tides

Located in the Port of Edmonds harbor, this four-lane ramp sits in a protected breakwater basin. The marina is a full-service facility with 700+ slips, fuel dock, haul-out, and guest moorage. Access to central Puget Sound is straightforward via the harbor entrance channel.

Edmonds is the departure point for the Edmonds-Kingston ferry (Washington State Ferries), so boaters exiting the harbor must be alert to ferry traffic in the approach lanes. The underwater park just south of the marina (Brackett's Landing) is a popular dive site — watch for dive flags near shore.

Shilshole Bay Marina — Port of Seattle

Address: 7001 Seaview Ave NW, Seattle, WA 98117
Lanes: 4 · Fee: $12/day · King County residents eligible for annual passes
Tide-dependent: No — deep-water facility

Shilshole Bay Marina is the gateway to two worlds: westward to Puget Sound and the San Juan Islands, or eastward through the Hiram M. Chittenden Locks into the Lake Washington Ship Canal and the fresh waters of Lake Union and Lake Washington. The marina has 1,400 slips and is the largest recreational marina on Puget Sound.

The four-lane launch ramp is in excellent condition with boarding floats. From Shilshole, it is roughly 5 nautical miles south to Elliott Bay and downtown Seattle, or 8 nautical miles north to Edmonds. The Ballard Locks entrance is approximately 0.5 nautical miles east of the marina via the Ship Canal approach channel.

2. Hiram M. Chittenden Locks (Ballard Locks)

The Ballard Locks are one of the great engineering achievements of the Pacific Northwest. They connect the salt water of Puget Sound to the fresh water of the Lake Washington Ship Canal, Lake Union, and Lake Washington — a transition that every vessel must navigate to move between the two systems.

History

Built between 1911 and 1917 by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, the locks were named after Major Hiram Martin Chittenden, the district engineer who championed the project. Chittenden recognized that Seattle's growth demanded a navigable connection between Puget Sound and the freshwater lakes to the east. The project also involved lowering Lake Washington by approximately 9 feet to match the level of Lake Union, creating the present-day ship canal.

The locks opened on July 4, 1917, and have operated continuously for over a century. They were designated a National Historic Landmark in 1978. Today, over 40,000 vessels transit the locks annually, and the grounds receive more than one million visitors per year.

The Lock Chambers

Two chambers accommodate vessels of different sizes:

  • Large lock: 825 feet long by 80 feet wide — handles commercial vessels, barges, and large recreational boats
  • Small lock: 150 feet long by 28 feet wide — used by most recreational boats, kayaks, and small craft

Water level differential between Puget Sound and the Ship Canal varies with the tide but can reach up to 26 feet at extreme low tides. The fill/drain cycle takes approximately 10–15 minutes, during which massive valves in the lock floor allow water to flow by gravity — no pumps are used.

Transit Procedures

The locks are open daily from 7:00 AM to 9:00 PM and transit is free for all vessels. Boats approaching from Puget Sound enter through the guide pier on the south side. Lockmaster staff direct traffic via horn signals and radio (VHF Channel 13).

  • Approach slowly — 4 knot speed limit in the approach channel
  • Have fenders and lines ready on both sides
  • Listen for horn signals: one long blast = enter; two long = standby
  • Secure to floating bollards inside the chamber (they rise/fall with water level)
  • Do not tie off to fixed structures — your boat must be free to rise or descend
  • Typical transit time: 20–30 minutes from approach to exit

Fish Ladder

Adjacent to the locks, a 21-step fish ladder allows salmon and steelhead to migrate between Puget Sound and the freshwater spawning grounds of the Lake Washington watershed. Underground viewing windows let visitors observe fish passage from June through November. Peak sockeye runs occur in July; Chinook and coho follow in August through October.

The fish ladder includes a counting station operated by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife. Daily fish counts are published online and contribute to the long-term monitoring of Puget Sound salmon populations.

Carl S. English Jr. Botanical Garden

The grounds surrounding the locks include the Carl S. English Jr. Botanical Garden, established in 1931 and named after the Army Corps horticulturist who spent 43 years cultivating the site. The garden contains over 1,500 species from around the world, arranged in a naturalistic English garden style. It is one of the most botanically diverse public gardens in the Pacific Northwest, with specimens from every continent except Antarctica.

3. Lake Washington Access

Once through the Ballard Locks and across Lake Union, the Montlake Cut delivers you into Lake Washington — the second-largest natural lake in Washington State at 21.5 miles long and 215 feet deep. The lake is entirely fresh water, maintained at a constant level by the locks system. The following launches and parks provide primary access.

Magnuson Park (Sand Point)

Address: 7400 Sand Point Way NE, Seattle, WA 98115
Fee: Free · Public launch ramp, beach, fishing pier

Located on the former Naval Air Station Seattle (NAS Seattle, decommissioned 1970), Magnuson Park is one of Seattle's largest parks at 350 acres. The boat launch on the north shore provides free access to the central-north portion of Lake Washington. The park also features a swimming beach, a public fishing pier, and extensive walking trails through the former military grounds.

Sand Point was a seaplane base during World War II and a major naval aviation facility through the Cold War. The NOAA Western Regional Center still occupies the southern portion of the former base.

Gene Coulon Memorial Beach Park (Renton)

Address: 1201 Lake Washington Blvd N, Renton, WA 98056
Fee: $7/day · Boat launch, swimming beach, fishing pier

At the south end of Lake Washington where the Cedar River enters, Gene Coulon Park offers a two-lane boat launch, a large swimming area, and a 1,500-foot fishing pier and boardwalk. The park sits at the mouth of the Cedar River, which is the primary spawning route for Lake Washington sockeye salmon — the same fish that transit the Ballard Locks fish ladder.

Renton's waterfront has been extensively redeveloped. The adjacent Boeing Renton Factory (737 production) is visible across the water, making for an unusual industrial-meets-nature landscape.

Luther Burbank Park (Mercer Island)

Address: 2040 84th Ave SE, Mercer Island, WA 98040
Fee: Free · Dock access, swimming, trails

On the north end of Mercer Island, Luther Burbank Park occupies the grounds of a former school for troubled youth (Parental School, 1904–1956). The park has a public dock suitable for small boat tie-up, a designated swimming area, and trails through second-growth forest. No formal launch ramp — best for kayaks, canoes, and boats arriving by water.

Kenmore — North End Launch

Address: 5815 NE 180th St, Kenmore, WA 98028 (Log Boom Park area)
Fee: $5/day · Launch ramp, Sammamish River access

At the north tip of Lake Washington, the Kenmore launch provides access to both the open lake and the mouth of the Sammamish River, which connects to Lake Sammamish 8 miles to the east. The Burke-Gilman Trail passes through here, and Kenmore Air's seaplane base operates from the adjacent waterfront — watch for floatplane traffic on the water.

Kirkland Waterfront

Marina Park: 25 Lakeshore Plaza, Kirkland, WA 98033
Fee: Varies · Guest moorage, fishing piers, swimming

Kirkland's downtown waterfront stretches along the east shore of Lake Washington with Marina Park, Waverly Park, and Houghton Beach Park providing public access. Marina Park has a public dock and guest moorage; Waverly Park includes a fishing pier popular with families. No formal trailer launch — Kirkland is best approached by water or used for hand-carry craft (kayaks, paddleboards).

Lake Washington note: Because the lake level is controlled by the Ballard Locks, it does not experience tidal fluctuation. The maintained level is approximately 20–22 feet above MLLW (Mean Lower Low Water). Seasonal variation is typically less than 2 feet, managed by the Army Corps of Engineers through the locks spillway.

4. Navigation & Safety

Boating in the greater Puget Sound region requires awareness of commercial traffic, ferry routes, tidal currents, and rapidly changing weather. The following resources and considerations are essential for safe navigation.

Charts & Publications

  • NOAA Chart 18443 — Puget Sound, northern part. Covers the waters from Admiralty Inlet north through Possession Sound, Saratoga Passage, and Deception Pass. Essential for Mukilteo-area boating.
  • NOAA Chart 18447 — Lake Washington Ship Canal and Lake Washington. Required for transiting the locks and navigating the lake.
  • U.S. Coast Pilot 7 — Pacific Coast: California, Oregon, Washington, Hawaii. Detailed narrative descriptions of harbors, currents, and anchorages.
  • NOAA Tide Tables — Station 9447659 (Everett), Station 9447130 (Seattle). Published annually with daily predictions.

Ferry Traffic

Two major Washington State Ferries routes operate in the immediate area:

  • Mukilteo–Clinton: Crosses Possession Sound between the mainland and Whidbey Island. Approximately 24 daily crossings. Ferries have absolute right-of-way — recreational vessels must stay clear of the approach lanes.
  • Edmonds–Kingston: Crosses central Puget Sound between Edmonds and the Kitsap Peninsula. Approximately 20 daily crossings. The route intersects with north-south recreational traffic lanes.
Rule of the road: Ferries operate on fixed routes and schedules. They cannot maneuver to avoid small craft. Give ferries at least 400 yards of clearance and never attempt to cross ahead of an approaching ferry. Monitor VHF Channel 16 (distress) and Channel 13 (bridge-to-bridge) when near ferry routes.

Commercial Shipping

The Port of Everett handles cargo vessels, military ships (Naval Station Everett is the home port for several guided-missile destroyers), and occasional cruise ships. Deep-draft vessels use the main shipping channel through Possession Sound. Recreational boaters should monitor AIS traffic and maintain safe distance from commercial vessels, which may have limited maneuverability in the channel.

Tidal Currents

Two areas demand particular respect for their tidal currents:

  • Admiralty Inlet: The main connection between the Strait of Juan de Fuca and Puget Sound. Currents can exceed 4 knots during spring tides, with standing waves and rip currents forming at the inlet's constrictions near Point Wilson and Marrowstone Island.
  • Deception Pass: The narrow channel between Whidbey Island and Fidalgo Island. Currents regularly reach 6–8 knots and can exceed 9 knots during extreme spring tides. Transit should only be attempted at slack water. Consult current tables and plan your passage within 30 minutes of predicted slack.
Tidal analysis: See Tides Math for harmonic constituent analysis and predictions. Understanding the M2, S2, and K1 tidal components helps predict current strength and timing.

Weather Monitoring

Puget Sound weather can change rapidly. Marine forecasts, wind observations, and visibility reports are critical for safe boating:

  • NWS Marine Forecast: Puget Sound and Hood Canal — updated four times daily
  • KPAE (Paine Field): METAR observations for wind speed, direction, visibility, and ceiling. Critical for assessing conditions at Mukilteo and north Puget Sound.
  • NOAA Weather Radio: WX4 (162.475 MHz) from Tiger Mountain covers the Puget Sound basin. Continuous marine forecasts and warnings.
Observatory resources: Weather Station Map for real-time observations from 8 METAR stations and 7 NWS/CWOP/NDBC sources. See also PNW Weather for broader Pacific Northwest forecasting and the Convergence Zone page for understanding the Puget Sound Convergence Zone that can bring sudden wind shifts and squalls.
Essential safety equipment: Washington State requires life jackets for all passengers under 12. All vessels should carry: PFDs for every passenger, visual distress signals (flares), sound-producing device (horn or whistle), fire extinguisher, anchor with adequate rode, VHF radio, and current NOAA charts. For Puget Sound open-water crossings, add a GPS chartplotter and radar reflector.
