Atmospheric Entry Physics

Orion returns from the Moon at 11.0 km/s (Mach 32) — faster than any human spacecraft since Apollo. The kinetic energy that must be dissipated is enormous: for a 10,700 kg capsule, that's about 650 gigajoules, equivalent to 155 tons of TNT.

Chapman's heating equation gives the convective heat flux: q = C * √(ρ/r_n) * V³, where ρ is atmospheric density, r_n is the nose radius, and V is velocity. The heat flux scales as the cube of velocity, making the initial moments of entry the most intense.

Skip Entry vs Direct Entry

Skip entry (used by Artemis I and planned for Artemis II): The capsule dips into the atmosphere, decelerates partially, then skips back up above the atmosphere like a stone on water. After coasting briefly, it re-enters for the final descent. This spreads the heat load over two passes, reducing peak heat flux and g-loads. It also improves landing accuracy (targeting Baja California waters).

Direct entry: A single continuous pass through the atmosphere. Peak heat flux is higher but the total entry time is shorter. Apollo used this approach.

On Artemis I, the AVCOAT heat shield experienced unexpected charring patterns during the skip, with some ablator material lost in chunks rather than eroding smoothly. NASA investigated this extensively before certifying the shield for Artemis II crew flight.

AVCOAT Heat Shield

Orion's heat shield is 5 meters in diameter, the largest ever built for human spaceflight. It uses AVCOAT, an ablative material that chars and vaporizes, carrying heat away from the structure. The material is injected into 186 individual honeycomb cells. During entry, surface temperatures exceed 2,760 °C (5,000 °F), while the crew cabin stays at a comfortable 21 °C.
