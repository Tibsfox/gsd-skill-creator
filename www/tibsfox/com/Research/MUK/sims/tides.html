How Tidal Harmonics Work

Ocean tides are the sum of many sinusoidal components, each driven by a specific gravitational interaction. The four largest constituents are:

M2 (Principal Lunar Semidiurnal) — Period: 12.42 hours. The Moon's gravity creates two bulges as Earth rotates. This is the dominant component almost everywhere, including Everett/Mukilteo (~1.1m amplitude).

S2 (Principal Solar Semidiurnal) — Period: 12.00 hours. The Sun creates its own pair of tidal bulges. When M2 and S2 align (new/full Moon), you get spring tides (larger range). When they oppose (quarter Moon), you get neap tides (smaller range).

K1 (Lunisolar Diurnal) — Period: 23.93 hours. Created by the declination of both Moon and Sun relative to Earth's equator. This makes consecutive high tides unequal — the "mixed semidiurnal" signature of Puget Sound.

O1 (Principal Lunar Diurnal) — Period: 25.82 hours. A purely lunar diurnal component. Combined with K1, it determines how unequal the two daily highs/lows are.

The spring-neap cycle (~14.8 days) is visible as the beat frequency between M2 and S2. Drag the S2 amplitude to zero to see it vanish.

Reading the Chart

The X-axis spans 30 days. Each thin colored line is a single harmonic constituent. The thick white line is the composite tide — the sum of all components, which is what you'd actually measure at the Everett waterfront.

The dashed gray line shows approximate real Everett tidal data reconstructed from NOAA Station 9447130 harmonic constants. Try matching the composite to it by adjusting amplitudes and phases.
