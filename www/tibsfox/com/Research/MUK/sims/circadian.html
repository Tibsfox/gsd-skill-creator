Circadian Desynchronization in Space

The human circadian clock has an intrinsic period of about 24.2 hours (the "free-running" period), slightly longer than Earth's 24-hour day. On Earth, sunlight resets ("entrains") this clock every morning. In space, without strong light cues, the biological rhythm drifts relative to the mission schedule.

The drift per day = (bio period - 24.0h) = ~12 minutes. Over a 10-day Artemis II mission, that accumulates to ~2 hours. The beat frequency between the two oscillators creates an envelope where phase alignment oscillates — meaning some days the crew feels alert at mission-scheduled wake time, and other days they feel groggy.

Alertness prediction combines two factors: the circadian phase (peak alertness near biological noon, lowest near biological 4 AM) and a sleep homeostatic pressure that increases with time awake. When the circadian trough coincides with the mission's active period, performance drops significantly.

NASA manages this with light therapy (blue-enriched LEDs) and carefully scheduled sleep-wake cycles. On ISS, crew are exposed to 11,000 lux blue-enriched light during "morning" to keep the circadian clock entrained.

Why This Matters for Artemis II

Critical mission events (lunar flyby, re-entry, skip maneuver) may fall at times when circadian phase is sub-optimal. Understanding the drift allows mission planners to schedule critical tasks during predicted alertness peaks and avoid the worst desynchronization windows.
