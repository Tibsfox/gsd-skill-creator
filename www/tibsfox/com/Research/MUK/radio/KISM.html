Timeline

  • 1960 First signed on in March as KGMI-FM, the FM companion to KGMI 790 AM. The "GMI" stood for International Good Music, Inc., the parent company. Simulcast AM programming.
  • 1960s Operated as a simulcast of KGMI-AM, carrying easy listening and middle-of-the-road music to Bellingham and Whatcom County.
  • 1970 Call letters changed to KVGM. Station began establishing its own identity separate from the AM counterpart.
  • 1973 Renamed KISM. Adopted an automated Top 40 format, reflecting the rise of FM as the primary music medium.
  • 1970s-90s Evolved through various rock and pop formats as the Bellingham market developed. The Mount Constitution transmitter site on Orcas Island gave the station extraordinary coverage across the San Juan Islands, Whatcom and Skagit counties, and into British Columbia.
  • 1998 Saga Communications purchased KISM and KGMI for $9.8 million, consolidating Bellingham's major radio properties under the Cascade Radio Group banner.
  • 2000s Settled into the classic rock format that defines the station today. The 50,000-watt Class C signal from Mount Constitution ensures coverage from the Canadian border to north Snohomish County.

Format History

KISM's format journey tracks the evolution of FM radio itself. Born as a simulcast appendage to an AM station, it spent its first decade simply repeating what KGMI was already broadcasting. The 1970 rebranding to KVGM and then 1973 rename to KISM marked the station's emergence as an independent voice.

The automated Top 40 format of the 1973 era was typical of small-market FM stations finding their footing. As the Bellingham market matured and FM listenership overtook AM, KISM gravitated toward rock formats that served the college-town demographic (Western Washington University is in Bellingham).

Today's classic rock format serves a broad audience across northwest Washington. The station's signal, powered by its remarkable Mount Constitution transmitter location at 2,409 feet elevation in Moran State Park, reaches well beyond its Bellingham license city. Listeners in the San Juan Islands, Skagit Valley, north Snohomish County, and across the Canadian border in Greater Vancouver and Victoria can receive the signal clearly.

The Mount Constitution Transmitter

KISM's transmitter location is one of the most scenic in American broadcasting. Mount Constitution is the highest point in the San Juan Islands at 2,409 feet, located within Moran State Park on Orcas Island. The stone observation tower at the summit (built by the Civilian Conservation Corps in the 1930s) offers 360-degree views of the San Juan archipelago, Mount Baker, the Cascades, Vancouver Island, and the Olympic Mountains. The radio transmitter site shares this elevation advantage, giving the 50,000-watt signal line-of-sight coverage across an enormous geographic area.

Connection to PNW Research

KISM connects the MUK radio guide to the broader geography of the PNW Research Series. While most stations in the guide transmit from the Seattle metro area, KISM reaches Mukilteo and south Snohomish County from the north, its signal traveling across the water from an island transmitter in the San Juans. This is radio as geography: the same Salish Sea waters documented in the MUK research carry KISM's signal southward. The Mount Constitution transmitter site in Moran State Park connects to the ecological research tradition (state parks, old-growth forest, island biogeography), while the Bellingham license city ties to the northern extent of the Puget Sound corridor that the S36 engine documents. KISM's coverage into British Columbia also makes it a cross-border cultural bridge, carrying PNW rock music to Canadian listeners.
