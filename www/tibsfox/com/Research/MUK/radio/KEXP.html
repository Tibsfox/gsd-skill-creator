Timeline

  • 1972 Founded May 10 as KCMU by four University of Washington undergraduates: Vic Kean, Rich Noonan, Pete Fiedler, and John Wilcox. Started at 10 watts on 90.5 MHz. Signal "barely reached the Ave" (University Way NE). Name derived from the Communications Building campus abbreviation.
  • 1970s-80s Grew into a freeform college radio station. DJs had full freedom to program their own shows.
  • 1986 Local band hourly requirement established, ensuring PNW artists received consistent airplay during the emerging alternative scene.
  • 1988 Kurt Cobain walked into the station and handed DJ Jonathan Poneman a copy of "Love Buzz," the first Nirvana single. Poneman and Bruce Pavitt, both KCMU DJs, had already co-founded Sub Pop Records. Kim Thayil of Soundgarden was also a station DJ.
  • 2000 Became the first radio station in the world to stream at 128 kbps, pioneering high-quality internet radio.
  • 2001 Renamed KEXP in March, partnering with Experience Music Project (Paul Allen's museum, now MoPOP). Moved to 113 Dexter Ave N. Frequency changed to 90.3 MHz. Power increased to 720 watts.
  • 2014 Friends of KEXP purchased the broadcast license from the University of Washington, completing full independence from the university. Listener-supported model fully in place.
  • 2015 Relocated to purpose-built facility at Seattle Center in December. $15 million capital campaign chaired by Mike McCready of Pearl Jam. The Gathering Space: a public performance stage and viewing gallery open to all.
  • 2018 Anonymous $10 million bequest from a listener identified only as "Suzanne," one of the largest single gifts in public radio history.

Format History

KEXP has maintained a freeform, DJ-curated format throughout its history. Unlike commercial radio, there is no playlist algorithm. Individual DJs research, select, and sequence their own music, creating a listening experience that prizes discovery and depth over repetition.

The station's programming spans genres without boundaries: indie rock, hip-hop, electronic, world music, jazz, folk, metal, and experimental. Specialty shows focus on specific traditions (Audioasis for Northwest music, Expansions for jazz, El Sonido for Latin music, Street Sounds for hip-hop).

Live in-studio sessions are a signature format element. Artists perform and interview in the station's purpose-built live room, with sessions filmed for YouTube. These sessions have become a global phenomenon, with KEXP's YouTube channel reaching over 3 million subscribers. Seventy-five percent of views come from outside the United States, making KEXP one of Seattle's most significant cultural exports.

The Grunge Connection

KCMU/KEXP was the incubator of the Seattle sound. The station's local band hourly requirement meant that Mudhoney, Soundgarden, Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Alice in Chains, and dozens of other bands received regular airplay before any commercial station would touch them. Two KCMU DJs founded Sub Pop Records. The grunge explosion was not spontaneous — it was cultivated over years on this frequency.

Connection to PNW Research

KEXP is the single most important broadcast node in the Seattle 360 Engine's network. Nearly every artist documented in the S36 engine received formative airplay on 90.3. The station's role goes beyond broadcast: it is a cultural institution that actively shapes which music gets heard, which artists get discovered, and which sounds define the Pacific Northwest. The Gathering Space at Seattle Center has become a venue in its own right. KEXP's global streaming reach means the PNW music tradition documented in the 360 Engine travels worldwide through this single frequency. The $10M "Suzanne" bequest and the McCready-led capital campaign demonstrate the depth of community investment in this broadcast infrastructure.
