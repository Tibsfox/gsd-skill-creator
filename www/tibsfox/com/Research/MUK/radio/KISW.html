Timeline

  • 1950 Founded January 16 by Ellwood Lippincott, a Weyerhaeuser engineer. Original format: classical music. One of the earliest FM stations in the Seattle market.
  • 1950s-60s Operated as a classical music station, serving a niche FM audience in an era when AM dominated commercial radio.
  • 1969 Purchased by Kaye-Smith Enterprises, a partnership between entertainer Danny Kaye and broadcast executive Lester Smith.
  • 1971 Format switched to progressive rock on February 15, following the Tom Donahue freeform model that had transformed San Francisco radio. This was the pivotal moment: a classical station became a rock station.
  • 1970s-80s Established itself as Seattle's premier rock station. Progressive rock format evolved into album-oriented rock (AOR) and eventually active rock.
  • 1987 Nationwide Communications acquired KISW for $13 million.
  • 1989-94 The grunge era. KISW broadcast the sound that was emerging from Seattle's clubs and rehearsal spaces. Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains, and dozens of other bands received heavy rotation. The station was both a mirror and an amplifier of the movement.
  • 1996 Entercom (now Audacy) acquired the station as part of post-Telecommunications Act consolidation.
  • 2006 BJ Shea Morning Experience launched, becoming a long-running fixture of Seattle morning radio.
  • 2023 BJ Shea Morning Experience ended after 17 years.

Format History

KISW's format journey from classical music to "The Rock of Seattle" spans the entire history of FM radio in the Pacific Northwest. When Ellwood Lippincott signed on the station in 1950, FM was a curiosity — most listeners were on AM. Classical music was a natural fit for the higher-fidelity medium.

The 1969 sale to Kaye-Smith set the stage for transformation. By 1971, the progressive rock revolution that Tom Donahue had pioneered at KMPX in San Francisco reached Seattle. The February 15 format flip was decisive: KISW would be a rock station from that day forward.

Through the 1970s and 1980s, KISW refined its identity around harder rock. When grunge exploded in the late 1980s, the station was positioned perfectly. Unlike KCMU/KEXP, which had cultivated the underground, KISW brought the Seattle sound to a mass commercial audience. The two stations were complementary forces: KEXP for discovery, KISW for amplification.

Today KISW maintains an active rock format with emphasis on new hard rock and metal alongside classic rock staples. The "Rock of Seattle" brand is one of the longest-running in Pacific Northwest radio.

Connection to PNW Research

KISW occupies the commercial rock pillar of the Seattle 360 Engine's broadcast infrastructure. Where KEXP documents the indie and underground tradition, KISW represents the moment when Seattle music crossed from subculture to mainstream. The grunge era degrees in the S36 engine (Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains) all had heavy KISW rotation. The station's Tiger Mountain transmitter covers the entire Puget Sound basin, making it the dominant rock signal from Olympia to Bellingham. Its 1950 founding makes it one of the oldest FM stations in the region, and its format evolution from classical to progressive rock to active rock mirrors the broader transformation of PNW music culture over seven decades.
