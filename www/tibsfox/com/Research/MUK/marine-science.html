Possession Sound Marine Science

Physical oceanography, estuarine mixing, and marine biology where the Snohomish meets the sea
Dashboard Salish Sea Convergence Zone Tides Math Marine Science Research
Possession Sound is an inlet of the Puget Sound located between Whidbey Island and the mainland coast at Everett and Mukilteo. It is where the Snohomish River — the third largest freshwater source to Puget Sound — enters the marine environment. This mixing zone creates one of the most biologically productive estuaries in the Salish Sea.

Physical Oceanography

Geography and Bathymetry

Possession Sound occupies the central portion of the Whidbey Basin, one of the four major sub-basins of Puget Sound. It is bounded by Whidbey Island to the west and the mainland (Everett, Mukilteo) to the east. To the north, it connects with Port Susan and Saratoga Passage; to the south, it opens into the main body of Puget Sound.

The sound is classified as a fjord-type estuary — carved by glacial action during the Pleistocene, with steep walls and a relatively deep channel. Depths reach 180 meters (590 feet) in the deeper portions of the Whidbey Basin. The cross-section narrows at Possession Point (southern tip of Whidbey Island), creating a constriction that affects tidal flow and mixing.

Water Circulation

The circulation in Possession Sound is driven by three primary forces:

  • Tidal forcing: Mixed semidiurnal tides (two unequal highs and lows per day) drive water in and out of the sound. Tidal currents can reach 1-2 knots in the narrows near Possession Point. See Tides Math for harmonic details.
  • River discharge: The Snohomish River delivers freshwater at its northern end. Average flow is approximately 280 m³/s (9,900 ft³/s), but spring freshet (snowmelt) flows can exceed 1,000 m³/s. This freshwater input drives an estuarine circulation pattern.
  • Wind forcing: Prevailing winds channel up the sound from the south-southwest in summer and from the north-northwest in winter. Wind-driven mixing is especially important during storm events.

Salinity and Stratification

The Freshwater-Saltwater Interface

Where the Snohomish River enters Possession Sound, a sharp boundary forms between lighter freshwater (flowing seaward at the surface) and denser saltwater (intruding landward along the bottom). This is the halocline — a zone of rapid salinity change over a few meters of depth.

Stratification and mixing: The strength of stratification determines how much vertical mixing occurs. Strong stratification (high river flow + weak tides) traps nutrients in the deep layer and limits oxygen replenishment from the surface. Weak stratification (low river flow + strong spring tides) allows deep mixing, bringing nutrients to the surface and oxygen to the depths. This cycle drives the seasonal rhythm of biological productivity.

Estuarine Circulation

Possession Sound exhibits classic two-layer estuarine circulation:

  • Surface layer: Lower-salinity water flows seaward (southward), driven by river discharge. This outflow carries sediment, nutrients, and juvenile salmon toward the open Sound.
  • Deep layer: Higher-salinity ocean water flows landward (northward) along the bottom, drawn in by the surface outflow. This intrusion brings marine nutrients, phytoplankton, and the larvae of marine invertebrates into the estuary.
Estuarine Richardson Number Rie = g · Δρ · h / (ρ · U²)

The estuarine Richardson number describes the competition between stabilizing buoyancy forces (freshwater input) and destabilizing mixing forces (tidal currents). When Rie > 1, stratification dominates; when Rie < 1, mixing dominates. In Possession Sound, Rie varies from < 0.5 during strong spring tides with low river flow (well-mixed) to > 5 during weak neap tides with high river flow (strongly stratified).

Upwelling and Nutrient Cycling

Tidal Mixing and Productivity

Tidal mixing at the constriction near Possession Point and the narrows between Whidbey and Camano Islands generates localized upwelling, bringing nutrient-rich deep water to the surface. This process fuels phytoplankton blooms that form the base of the food web.

The seasonal nutrient cycle follows a predictable pattern:

SeasonProcessBiological Response
Winter (Dec–Feb)Deep mixing, low lightNutrients accumulate in surface waters; phytoplankton limited by light
Spring (Mar–May)Increasing light, stratification beginsSpring bloom — explosive phytoplankton growth consumes nutrients
Summer (Jun–Aug)Strong stratification, low river flowNutrients depleted in surface; deep water may become hypoxic
Autumn (Sep–Nov)Stratification breaks down, storms mix waterSecondary bloom possible; nutrients replenished from depth

Marine Biology

Salmon Runs

The Snohomish River system supports all five species of Pacific salmon, plus steelhead and bull trout. The estuary is critical habitat for juvenile salmon transitioning from freshwater to marine environments — a process called smoltification that requires the mixing zone.

Chinook Salmon
Oncorhynchus tshawytscha

The largest Pacific salmon, listed as Threatened under the Endangered Species Act throughout Puget Sound. Chinook spend weeks to months rearing in the Snohomish estuary before moving to the open ocean. They are the primary prey species for Southern Resident orcas — Chinook make up approximately 80% of the orcas' diet. All Puget Sound Chinook populations are below recovery escapement levels.

Coho Salmon
Oncorhynchus kisutch

Coho return to the Snohomish system from September through December. Juvenile coho spend 12-18 months in freshwater before migrating to the estuary. They are a popular sport fish at the Mukilteo fishing pier and Everett Jetty Landing during fall runs.

Pink Salmon
Oncorhynchus gorbuscha

Pink salmon return in odd years (2025, 2027, etc.) in massive numbers. Unlike other salmon, pinks migrate to the ocean almost immediately after emerging from gravel, making the estuary a brief but important waypoint. Odd-year returns to the Snohomish system can exceed 100,000 fish.

Chum Salmon
Oncorhynchus keta

Chum return from October through January, often the last salmon to arrive. They are important prey for orcas during winter months when Chinook are less available. Chum use the estuary extensively during their juvenile migration.

Sockeye Salmon
Oncorhynchus nerka

Less common in the Snohomish system than in Lake Washington or the Skagit River, but present. Sockeye require a lake for juvenile rearing and use the estuary during both juvenile outmigration and adult return.

Orca Presence

Two ecotypes of orcas use Possession Sound and the surrounding waters of the Salish Sea:

Southern Resident Orcas
Orcinus orca (fish-eating ecotype)

The endangered Southern Resident killer whales — J, K, and L pods — have been listed under the Endangered Species Act since 2005. Fewer than 80 individuals remain. They feed primarily on Chinook salmon and historically spent much of the summer and fall in the inland waters of the Salish Sea, including Possession Sound, following salmon runs. In recent years, declining Chinook populations have driven the orcas to spend more time on the outer coast, making their appearance in Puget Sound an increasingly rare event.

Bigg's (Transient) Orcas
Orcinus orca (mammal-eating ecotype)

Bigg's orcas feed exclusively on marine mammals — harbor seals, sea lions, porpoises, and occasionally gray whale calves. Their population has grown significantly as marine mammal populations have recovered under the Marine Mammal Protection Act. Bigg's orcas are now seen in Puget Sound year-round, often in small groups of 2-6 animals. They are regularly spotted in Possession Sound, particularly near seal haul-out sites.

The orca-salmon connection: The fate of Southern Resident orcas is directly tied to Chinook salmon recovery. The Snohomish River estuary restoration projects (Blue Heron Slough, Spencer Island, Smith Island) aim to rebuild the estuarine habitat that juvenile Chinook need to survive their freshwater-to-saltwater transition. More estuary habitat means more surviving Chinook, which means more food for orcas. The connection runs through the mixing zone.

Seabird Colonies

The productive waters of Possession Sound support diverse seabird populations:

  • Pigeon Guillemots — nest in burrows along the bluffs from Mukilteo to Edmonds; feed on small fish in nearshore waters
  • Great Blue Herons — colonies (rookeries) in the Snohomish River estuary; feed in tidal mudflats and shallow waters
  • Bald Eagles — common year-round along the Possession Sound shoreline; nest in tall conifers near the water
  • Rhinoceros Auklets — forage in deeper waters of the sound; breed on Protection Island in the Strait of Juan de Fuca
  • Marbled Murrelets — Threatened species that nests in old-growth forests and forages in nearshore waters; present in Possession Sound year-round
  • Western Grebes — winter in large rafts on the open water of Possession Sound; spectacular courtship displays
  • Surf Scoters and Barrow's Goldeneyes — winter residents that dive for shellfish and invertebrates along the shoreline

The Snohomish River Estuary

Anatomy of a Mixing Zone

The Snohomish River estuary is the second largest river discharge into Puget Sound (after the Skagit) and accounts for approximately 30% of the freshwater discharge to the Whidbey Basin. The lower Snohomish consists of a network of braided sloughs — Steamboat Slough, Union Slough, Ebey Slough — that create a complex mosaic of channels, mudflats, and tidal marshes.

The freshwater-saltwater mixing zone shifts location with the tides and river flow. During high tide with low river flow, the salt front can intrude several miles upstream. During low tide with high river flow, the mixing zone pushes seaward past Jetty Island.

Estuary Restoration

The Snohomish River estuary has lost over 75% of its historical tidal marsh and blind channel habitat due to diking, drainage, and development since the 1850s. Major restoration projects are rebuilding this critical habitat:

Why restoration matters: Juvenile Chinook salmon use estuary habitats as transitional rearing areas, gradually acclimating to saltwater while feeding on the rich invertebrate communities of tidal marshes. Studies show that juvenile Chinook that spend more time in functional estuary habitat have higher marine survival rates. Every acre of restored tidal marsh contributes to salmon recovery — and therefore to orca recovery.

Water Quality Monitoring

Ongoing Monitoring Programs

Multiple agencies and institutions monitor water quality in Possession Sound:

  • NOAA (CO-OPS): Continuous water level and meteorological data at Station 9447659 (Everett). Temperature, conductivity, and water level at 6-minute intervals.
  • USGS: River flow and gage height at Station 12150800 (Snohomish River near Monroe). Real-time discharge data used to estimate freshwater input to the estuary.
  • Washington Department of Ecology: Monthly water quality sampling including nutrients, dissolved oxygen, pH, temperature, and salinity at fixed stations throughout the Whidbey Basin.
  • Washington Department of Health: Shellfish growing area classification. Monitors bacterial contamination (fecal coliform) to determine whether shellfish harvest is safe.
  • Ocean Research College Academy (ORCA): Everett Community College program where students conduct oceanographic research in Possession Sound. Studies have covered ocean acidification, bacterial presence, temperature-salinity profiles, and phytoplankton ecology.

Shellfish Safety

Commercial and recreational shellfish harvest in Possession Sound is regulated by the Washington Department of Health based on water quality classification:

ClassificationMeaningPossession Sound Status
ApprovedMeets all water quality standards; harvest permittedSome areas in outer sound
Conditionally ApprovedMeets standards under specific conditions (e.g., dry weather)Most recreational harvest areas
RestrictedElevated contamination; harvest limited to special permitsAreas near urban stormwater outfalls
ProhibitedContamination exceeds safe limits; no harvest permittedNear Everett marina, industrial areas

The primary contaminant of concern is fecal coliform bacteria, which enters the Sound via stormwater runoff, combined sewer overflows, and agricultural drainage from the Snohomish River watershed. Biotoxins from harmful algal blooms (HABs) — particularly paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) from Alexandrium dinoflagellates — can also close shellfish harvest areas regardless of water quality classification.

Always check the WDOH Shellfish Safety Map before harvesting.

Ocean-Atmosphere Connection

How the Sound Affects Weather

Possession Sound is not just affected by weather — it affects it. The ocean-atmosphere coupling in the sound influences the microclimate measured by our Observatory:

  • Thermal moderation: Water temperature (8-12°C year-round) moderates air temperature along the shoreline. Winter days are warmer near the water; summer days are cooler. This creates the 1-3°C difference between KPAE (2 miles inland) and the waterfront that challenges our weather scoring.
  • Fog generation: When warm, moist air flows over the cool water surface, advection fog forms. This is most common in summer when air-water temperature difference is greatest. The fog burns off as morning sun heats the air above the dewpoint.
  • Wind channeling: The north-south orientation of Possession Sound channels wind along its axis. Models that assume free-flowing wind from the west miss this channeling effect, which produces northerly or southerly surface winds even when the synoptic flow is westerly.
  • Tidal influence on air temperature: Large tidal exchanges expose or cover mudflats that have different heat capacity than open water. Low tide exposes dark mud that absorbs solar radiation and warms the overlying air; incoming tide replaces this with cooler water. The 12.4-hour tidal cycle overlays the 24-hour solar cycle, creating beat frequencies in shoreline temperature.
