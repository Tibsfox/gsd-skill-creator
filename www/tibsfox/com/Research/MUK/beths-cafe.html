Origins — From Nickel Slots to Omelets

Beth and Harold Eisenstadt opened the place in 1954 as a nickel-slots gambling parlor at 7311 Aurora Avenue North, in Seattle's Green Lake neighborhood. The gambling drew people in; the food kept them around. Over time the slots disappeared and the griddle took over.

Aurora Avenue was a different road in 1954. Before Interstate 5 opened in 1962, Aurora (US Route 99) was the region's primary north-south highway — a stream of motels, diners, taverns, and gas stations stretching from the Aurora Bridge to Shoreline. Twin Teepees, a kitschy roadside landmark, stood one block south at 7201. Beth's opened into this world of passing motorists and all-night truck stops.

When I-5 diverted traffic, Aurora declined. Motels became weekly rentals. The corridor earned a reputation for drugs, prostitution, and strip clubs. Most of the original roadside businesses closed. Twin Teepees was bulldozed in 2001. Beth's stayed.

The 12-Egg Omelet

The signature dish is a 12-egg omelet served on a pizza tray with all-you-can-eat hash browns (dine-in only) and your choice of toast — white, wheat, rye, sourdough, egg bread, English muffin, or biscuit. Six varieties are available. The "Triple Bypass" — bacon, sausage, ham, and two cheeses — is the most popular.

The omelet is designed for sharing. No competitive prizes for finishing solo, though roughly one in ten manages it. Completers earn a spot on the Wall of Fame and a bumper sticker. The restaurant's motto: "Diet Schmiet."

Adam Richman brought the Man v. Food cameras here in 2009 (Season 1, Episode 12: "Seattle"). He attempted the Southwestern Exposure variant — beef brisket chili, salsa, sour cream, and cheddar, roughly five pounds total — racing previous champion Adam Houghton. Richman led throughout but with two bites remaining, found himself unable to finish. Neither competitor completed it. The episode created hour-and-a-half wait times for weeks.

450,000 eggs per year. Breakfast accounts for 80% of business. The 12-egg omelet put Beth's in the Travel Channel's World's Best Places to Pig Out (#4), the Food Network's "Top 5 Big Breakfasts," and 1,000 Places to See in the USA and Canada Before You Die.

The Wall Art

Every table at Beth's has paper and crayons alongside the menus. You draw. You leave it. It goes on the wall.

This tradition has been running for decades. No one knows exactly when it started — it grew organically, like the cafe itself. Over the years, tens of thousands of crayon drawings have accumulated on every available surface: walls, ceiling, counter panels, support columns. The art ranges from Kurt Cobain and Jimi Hendrix portraits to Pokemon characters to declarations like "I drove 3,000 miles to come to Beth's" and "I lost my virginity at Beth's" and scrawled phone numbers and inside jokes that meant everything at 4 AM.

The wall art is a living, breathing project spanning generations of Seattle residents and visitors. It's folk art in the truest sense — made by the people who were there, in the moment, usually at an hour when the rest of the city was asleep.

A Personal Connection

In the late 1990s, the walls of Beth's held a drawing that caught someone's eye — a piece by a furry artist, a student at the University of Washington. A post to alt.fan.furry on Usenet about the artwork led to a response from the artist themselves. That exchange became a connection to Seattle's local furry community — one of those threads that only forms because someone left their art in a public place, and someone else recognized it.

Seattle was an early hub of the furry fandom. Conifur Northwest, the Pacific Northwest's first furry convention, launched in 1998 (running through 2005, peaking at 525 attendees). Many early furry fans found each other through Usenet groups and FurryMUCK (established 1990, 2,000+ users by 1996). Beth's, open all night, full of crayons and paper, was exactly the kind of place where those worlds overlapped.

The Aurora Corridor

Beth's sits in context. Aurora Avenue North was named in 1901 by Dr. Edward Corliss Kilbourne after his hometown of Aurora, Illinois. The road's story tracks Seattle's growth:

1880s

Rough wagon road through forest

1925

Widened and paved with concrete — Seattle's north-south spine

1932

Aurora Bridge opens. Six-lane highway to Green Lake. Motels and diners flourish.

1937

Twin Teepees opens at 7201 — one block from Beth's future home

1950

Canlis Restaurant opens on Aurora — fine dining amid the motels

1954

Beth's Cafe opens — nickel slots and breakfast

1962

Interstate 5 opens. Aurora traffic collapses. Highway downgraded from US 99 to SR 99.

1970s–90s

Economic decline. Drug trade, sex work, weekly-rate motels. Most original businesses close.

2001

Twin Teepees demolished. Beth's is now one of the last originals standing.

Today about 20 motels still operate on Aurora between N 40th and N 145th. The stretch accounts for 17% of Seattle's traffic deaths despite being just one corridor. Thirty of Aurora's city blocks lack sidewalks. Beth's persists through all of it.

Ownership — Six Hands on the Griddle

Beth's has changed hands roughly six times since 1954. The Eisenstadts founded it. The intervening owners are largely unnamed in the historical record, though the 1990s-era owner was connected to Seattle's grunge scene — "in the midst of Nirvana," as one account puts it.

Chris Dalton (2002–2020)

A Port Angeles native with a degree in hotel and restaurant management. After working in tech during the dot-com bust, Dalton was looking for "a nice and easy breakfast place to manage." He answered a classified ad selling a breakfast eatery. When he called, he realized the place was Beth's.

Dalton transformed the operation: fresh ingredients, home-baked goods, vegetarian options. He also owned Duck Island Ale House and Grinders Hot Sands. He listed Beth's for sale at $6 million in February 2019 after being diagnosed with aggressive stage-4 pancreatic cancer.

My hope would be that a new owner would respect the history of Beth's. — Chris Dalton, 2019

Chris Dalton died in April 2020, having owned Beth's for nearly 20 years.

Hazel Dalton (2020–2023)

Chris's widow kept the cafe alive through COVID, closures, and reopenings.

This was my husband's baby. He passed a couple of years ago from pancreatic cancer. I'm doing this for his legacy and for my family's legacy. It's part of Seattle. I don't want it to die on my watch. — Hazel Dalton

Mason Reed (January 2024–present)

Musician (9 years touring, ~250 shows/year, 550,000 miles driven), chef (Tacoma Dome, The Gorge, cooking for touring artists like Harry Styles), and co-owner of Tim's Tavern in White Center (800–1,000 bands booked annually). He first fell in love with Beth's decades ago during touring stops in Seattle. He was approached with a stark message: "It's either going to be you or we're going to let Beth's die."

Reed installed a jukebox, expanded the vegan and vegetarian options, and planned a 70th anniversary celebration with 1950s pricing. He describes Beth's as "something akin to a punk rock Waffle House."

It's a special, special place. I don't have to tell people that. They know. — Mason Reed

The Late-Night Democracy

Beth's was 24 hours for most of its life. The crowd between 2 AM and 7 AM was its own ecosystem: taxi drivers, fishermen, light rail operators finishing shifts, bar crawlers winding down, punks coming off shows, college students from UW with nowhere else that was open and cheap.

Someone said a long time ago, Beth's is one of the only places in the world where you can see a guy getting done with his night, having drunk biscuits and gravy at 4 or 5 a.m., and a guy who is suited up on his way to work having biscuits and gravy and coffee, sitting next to each other and having a conversation. — Mason Reed
My fondest memory is coming here after a show, usually punk or metal, at 3 a.m. for the all-you-can-eat hash browns. — anonymous patron

The cafe has a game room with pinball machines and 1980s arcade games. The eclectic jukebox runs from Eminem to the Grateful Dead. Current hours: Mon–Thu 7 AM–3 PM, Fri–Sat 7 AM–4 AM, Sun 7 AM–5 PM. The 24-hour schedule may not return, but the Friday/Saturday late-night window preserves the tradition.

COVID — The Longest Close

April 2020

Chris Dalton dies of pancreatic cancer

October 2020

Beth's closes. Operating at 50% occupancy under Phase 2 mandates was "not economically feasible." Delivery and takeout "not adequately suited for both our product and our clientele."

July 2021

Closes again after two months. Delta variant, supply chain, staff shortages. "The shutdown sapped most of our bank account."

Usenet, FurryMUCK, and the 1990s Seattle Internet

Before the web took over, Seattle had a thriving BBS scene and active Usenet culture. alt.fan.furry was created on December 18, 1990, by a user called "Tzoq" — one of the earliest online gathering spaces for the furry fandom. FurryMUCK, established in November 1990, had grown to 2,000+ users by 1996 with 500 simultaneous users by 1998.

Seattle was a natural node in this network. The city's tech infrastructure (Microsoft, early ISPs, university network access at UW) combined with its countercultural streak made it a hub for online communities that other cities didn't yet understand. By the mid-1990s, local furry fans organized through Usenet and MUCKs. Portland's PDXFURS community began around 1994–95 via FurryMUCK.

Conifur Northwest, the PNW's first furry convention, was founded in 1998 by James "Tibo" Birdsall and ran through 2005, peaking at 525 attendees at SeaTac-area hotels. It was cancelled in 2006 when its hotel was demolished for Link Light Rail construction. RainFurrest (2007–2015) succeeded it, growing to 2,704 attendees before controversies ended its run.

Beth's, open 24 hours, full of crayons, on the bus line, cheap enough for college students — was a natural crossroads for these communities. The wall art made it a canvas. The late-night hours made it a gathering point. A furry artist at UW could draw something at 3 AM, pin it to the wall, and someone from alt.fan.furry could find it months later and recognize the style. These accidental connections are what Beth's has always been about.
