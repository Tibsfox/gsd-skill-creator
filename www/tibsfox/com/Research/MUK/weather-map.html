Why These Stations Matter for Mukilteo

KPAE (Paine Field) is our primary ground-truth station — 2.5 miles from Mukilteo's waterfront. But at 607 ft elevation on a bluff, it sees different conditions than the shoreline. When the Puget Sound Convergence Zone is active, KPAE can report overcast/rain while KSEA (25 mi south) reports clear. Comparing KPAE vs KAWO (25 mi north) reveals the PSCZ's north-south oscillation. KBFI at 21 ft elevation captures sea-level conditions that hilltop KPAE misses.

CO-OPS API Access

All NOAA tide stations are accessible via the CO-OPS API at https://api.tidesandcurrents.noaa.gov/api/prod/datagetter. Parameters: station, product (water_level, predictions, air_temperature, wind, air_pressure, water_temperature), datum (MLLW), units (english/metric), time_zone (lst_ldt), format (json). No API key required. Rate limit: reasonable use.

NWS Forecast Grid Points

In addition to observation stations, the NWS provides gridded forecasts at ~2.5 km resolution. Key grid points for our coverage area:

  • Mukilteo: SEW/129,82 — hourly forecast
  • Clinton (Whidbey): SEW/128,85 — hourly forecast
  • Marine zone PZZ135: Puget Sound & Hood Canal forecast

These give us Clinton-specific forecasts even without an observation station on south Whidbey. The grid forecast can be scored against KPAE observations to measure cross-water accuracy.

GOES-18 for Convergence Zone Tracking

GOES-18 visible and infrared imagery at 5-minute intervals can show the Puget Sound Convergence Zone forming in real time — the cloud band appears as air splits around the Olympics and reconverges over Snohomish County. The mesoscale sector can scan our area at 1-minute intervals during active weather. GeoColor composite provides natural-color daytime imagery and IR-based nighttime imagery, ideal for tracking marine layer advection into Possession Sound.

Direct S3 access: https://noaa-goes18.s3.amazonaws.com/index.html. Python: goes2go package for automated download and visualization.

Sentinel-2 for Land Surface Monitoring

At 10-meter resolution with 5-day revisit, Sentinel-2 can track vegetation health across the Mukilteo watershed, monitor snow extent on the western Cascades, and detect land-use changes affecting drainage patterns. Cloud-filtered composites reveal seasonal patterns in the convergence zone's impact on vegetation — the PSCZ rain band creates a visible greening gradient from north to south across Snohomish County.

Coverage Gaps

Where the monitoring network has blind spots. These gaps affect our ability to validate forecasts, detect local phenomena, and understand Mukilteo's microclimate.

Gap 1: No marine buoys in Possession Sound
The nearest NOAA NDBC buoys are in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the open Pacific. Possession Sound — the body of water directly west of Mukilteo — has no instrumented marine buoy. This means we have no real-time sea surface temperature, wave height, or marine-level wind data for the water that directly influences Mukilteo's marine layer and fog formation. The Everett tide gauge (9447659) provides water level and water temperature at the port, but not open-water conditions mid-Sound.
Gap 2: No mountain weather stations nearby
The nearest mountain weather station is at Stevens Pass (elevation 4,061 ft, ~50 miles east). There are no instrumented stations on the western Cascade foothills between the lowland ASOS network and the pass. This gap matters because orographic precipitation — rain and snow generated as air rises over the foothills — is a major contributor to Snohomish County weather, and we cannot directly observe the transition zone where rain turns to snow.
Gap 3: Limited south Whidbey Island coverage
Whidbey Island sits in the Olympic rain shadow and channels marine air into Possession Sound. NAS Whidbey (KNUW) is at the island's north end, 30+ miles from Mukilteo. South Whidbey — Clinton, Langley, Freeland — has no ASOS station. The closest data is from the Mukilteo-Clinton ferry's onboard sensors and occasional Weather Underground personal stations. This gap means we cannot directly measure the marine air mass that crosses the Sound to reach Mukilteo.
Gap 4: No real-time air quality in Mukilteo proper
The Puget Sound Clean Air Agency (PSCAA) operates regulatory-grade monitors, but the nearest to Mukilteo are in Marysville (north) and Lynnwood (south). PurpleAir community sensors provide PM2.5 data, but coverage in Mukilteo itself is sparse and sensor quality varies. During wildfire smoke events (increasingly common in August-September), the nearest reliable AQ data comes from stations 5-10 miles away, missing local terrain effects on smoke pooling and dispersal.

Network Topology

Data Flow Architecture

All 35+ data sources follow a common pattern: instrument → telemetry → federal database → public API → our collection. The key difference is latency. ASOS stations report in near-real-time (seconds). NOAA tide gauges batch at 6-minute intervals. USGS stream gauges transmit via satellite every 15 minutes. GOES-18 imagery arrives on AWS S3 within ~2 minutes of acquisition. Model grids (Open-Meteo, NWS) are compute-bound — a new HRRR run takes ~45 minutes to process after the observation window closes.

Observation vs. Forecast

A critical distinction: METAR, tide gauges, and stream gauges report what happened. Open-Meteo and NWS grids report what the model thinks will happen. Our scoring system at the MUK Dashboard compares forecasts against KPAE observations to measure which model knows this place best. The data sources on this page are the raw ingredients for that comparison.

Spatial Resolution

The monitoring network is not uniformly distributed. Aviation stations cluster at airports (lowland, flat terrain). Tide gauges cluster at ports (sheltered harbors). Stream gauges cluster at bridge crossings. The result: excellent coverage of lowland river valleys and harbors, poor coverage of hillsides, foothills, and open water. The Puget Sound Convergence Zone — a 10-mile-wide band of enhanced precipitation — can pass between stations without being directly observed by any of them.
