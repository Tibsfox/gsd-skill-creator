The ferries are essential transportation infrastructure, not tourist attractions — though tourists love them. For the communities of Whidbey Island, the San Juans, and the Kitsap Peninsula, the ferry is the road.

Washington State Ferries

WSF is the largest ferry system in the United States and the third-largest in the world. The system operates 10 routes connecting 21 terminals with a fleet of 21 vessels, serving approximately 24 million riders per year. The ferries carry both passengers and vehicles, functioning as floating extensions of the state highway system.

The system was founded in 1951 when Washington state purchased the failing Puget Sound Navigation Company, better known as the Black Ball Line. Captain Alexander Peabody had operated the private ferry system since the 1930s, but a bitter labor dispute and financial decline forced the state's hand. The purchase price was $4.9 million for 21 vessels and terminal facilities — an investment that created the backbone of cross-Sound transportation that still operates today.

Research connection: Ferry operations are directly affected by weather conditions we monitor at the Mukilteo Observatory. Wind speeds above 40 knots sustained or 55 knots gusting trigger service suspensions. Our weather data source network tracks the exact conditions that determine GO/NO-GO for ferry sailings.

Mukilteo — Clinton Route

Route Overview

Crossing time: 20 minutes across Possession Sound

Connects: Mukilteo (Snohomish County mainland) to Clinton (south Whidbey Island)

Vessels: Tokitae and Suquamish (144-car, Kwa-di Tabil class)

Frequency: Every 30 minutes during peak, hourly late evening

Peak wait: 1-2 sailings on summer weekends, especially Friday evenings northbound

This route has connected Mukilteo to Whidbey Island since the 1920s, when private operators first ran vessels across Possession Sound. It is one of the original state ferry routes and carries approximately 4 million riders annually. From Clinton, SR-525 runs north through Langley and Greenbank to connect with SR-20, providing access to Oak Harbor, Deception Pass, and the Anacortes ferry terminal for the San Juan Islands.

The new Mukilteo multimodal terminal opened in November 2020 after a $187 million construction project — the first new WSF terminal built in over 40 years. The old terminal (built 1981) was demolished. The new facility is designed to reduce vehicle queuing on SR-525 and integrates with the Sounder commuter rail station and Community Transit bus stops, creating a true multimodal transportation hub.

Historical Note — Point Elliott Treaty, 1855

The Mukilteo ferry terminal sits near the site where the Point Elliott Treaty was signed on January 22, 1855, between Territorial Governor Isaac Stevens and leaders of the Duwamish, Snoqualmie, Snohomish, and other Puget Sound tribes. The treaty ceded tribal lands in exchange for reservation lands, fishing rights, and payments. The Tulalip Tribes of Washington are the successors to the signatories. Mukilteo Lighthouse Park, adjacent to the terminal, commemorates this history.

Edmonds — Kingston Route

Route Overview

Crossing time: 30 minutes across central Puget Sound

Connects: Edmonds (south Snohomish County) to Kingston (north Kitsap Peninsula)

Vessels: Various, 144-188 car capacity

Frequency: Every 30-60 minutes depending on season and day

Operating since the 1960s, the Edmonds-Kingston route is a critical commuter corridor connecting the Kitsap Peninsula to the Seattle metropolitan area. The Edmonds terminal sits at the downtown waterfront, adjacent to the historic railroad depot and Brackett's Landing underwater marine sanctuary. From Kingston, travelers access Poulsbo, the Hood Canal Bridge, and connections to the Olympic Peninsula.

Edmonds is 8 miles south of Mukilteo along the waterfront. The two ferry routes together provide comprehensive cross-Sound access from the north Sound region: Mukilteo for Whidbey Island and the northern islands, Edmonds for the Kitsap Peninsula and Olympic Peninsula.

Other WSF Routes

Seattle — Bainbridge Island

35-minute crossing from Colman Dock (downtown Seattle Pier 52) to Winslow. The most-ridden WSF route. Walk-on passengers can reach the terminal via Link light rail to Pioneer Square. Bainbridge has restaurants, galleries, and the Bloedel Reserve.

Seattle — Bremerton

60-minute crossing from Colman Dock to Bremerton. Passes through Rich Passage with views of the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard. Home port of decommissioned aircraft carriers and submarines. Longer crossing but scenic.

Anacortes — San Juan Islands

Multiple stops: Lopez Island (45 min), Shaw Island (60 min), Orcas Island (75 min), Friday Harbor/San Juan Island (90 min). The scenic route through the San Juan archipelago. Whale watching season May-October (Southern Resident orcas). Reservations strongly recommended in summer.

Coupeville — Port Townsend

30-minute crossing from Keystone Harbor (central Whidbey Island) to Port Townsend (Olympic Peninsula). The only direct link between Whidbey and the Olympic Peninsula. Small vessel (64 cars), frequently full in summer. Port Townsend is a Victorian seaport with a thriving arts community.

Weather Impact on Ferry Operations

Ferry operations are one of the most weather-sensitive transportation systems in the Pacific Northwest. WSF monitors conditions continuously and makes GO/NO-GO decisions based on wind, visibility, and sea state.

Sustained wind>40 knotsService suspension on exposed routes
Wind gusts>55 knotsSystem-wide suspension possible

Sea stateSignificant wave height >6 ftRoute-dependent suspension

The Mukilteo-Clinton route is relatively sheltered within Possession Sound, but strong southerly winds can create challenging conditions. The Edmonds-Kingston route crosses more open water and is more susceptible to wind and wave delays. The Coupeville-Port Townsend route through Admiralty Inlet is the most exposed and most frequently disrupted by weather.

The Black Ball Line — History

Before the state ferry system, Puget Sound's cross-water transportation was operated by private companies, the most prominent being the Puget Sound Navigation Company, known as the Black Ball Line after the black ball painted on its vessel funnels. Captain Alexander Peabody built the company into the dominant ferry operator in the region during the 1930s and 1940s.

The system's decline began with the 1935 ferry workers' strike, one of the largest maritime labor actions on the West Coast. Workers demanded better wages and working conditions. The strike paralyzed cross-Sound transportation for weeks and highlighted the region's dependence on ferry service. Subsequent labor disputes, rising costs, and the inability to raise fares (regulated by the state) pushed the company toward insolvency.

By 1951, the state legislature authorized the purchase of the Black Ball Line's Puget Sound operations for $4.9 million. The transaction included 21 vessels and terminal facilities across the Sound. Washington State Ferries began operations on June 1, 1951. The private Black Ball Line continued operating a single international route between Port Angeles and Victoria, BC, which still runs today as Black Ball Ferry Line (MV Coho).

The MV Kalakala — Art Deco Icon

The most famous vessel of the Black Ball era was the MV Kalakala ("flying bird" in Chinook), an art deco streamliner launched in 1935. Built on the hull of a burned ferry, its aerodynamic silver design made it a Seattle icon and reportedly the most photographed vessel in the world during the 1930s-40s. The Kalakala served until 1967, was briefly an Alaska fish processing plant, returned to Seattle in 1998 amid preservation hopes, but was ultimately scrapped in 2015 after fundraising efforts failed.
