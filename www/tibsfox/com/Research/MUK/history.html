The Point Elliott Treaty was signed on the beach at Mukilteo in 1855. One hundred and thirteen years later, the largest building in the world rose from the same county's forests. The distance between those two events is measured in displacement, extraction, and reinvention — and the land remembers all of it.

Indigenous History time immemorial – 1855

The original peoples of this land shaped it for thousands of years before European contact.

The Snohomish People

Sdoh-doh-hohbsh — "a style of crossing a stream" or "sleeping waters"
Coast Salish

The Snohomish were a Southern Coast Salish people whose territory spanned the Snohomish River drainage, the shores of Possession Sound, and the lowlands of what is now Snohomish County. Their language, Lushootseed, connected them to a network of Salish Sea peoples stretching from the Skagit to the Puyallup.

They built cedar longhouses along riverbanks and shorelines — structures that could house multiple families and last for generations. Cedar was the foundation of material culture: canoes, clothing, baskets, and the great bent-wood boxes used for cooking and storage.

Salmon was the center of the food economy. Five species of Pacific salmon — Chinook, Coho, Sockeye, Pink, and Chum — returned to the rivers each year in overlapping runs from spring through fall. The First Salmon Ceremony honored the returning fish and ensured continued abundance.

~8000 BCE Post-glacial habitation along Puget Sound shorelines begins
~3000 BCE Established village sites along the Snohomish and Stillaguamish rivers
1792 George Vancouver charts Possession Sound, names Whidbey Island
1841 Wilkes Expedition maps the region in detail
1855 Point Elliott Treaty signed at Mukilteo — tribes cede most of their land
Point Elliott Treaty (January 22, 1855): Signed on the beach at Mukilteo between Governor Isaac Stevens and leaders of the Duwamish, Suquamish, Snoqualmie, Snohomish, Lummi, Skagit, Swinomish, and allied bands. The tribes ceded most of their territory in exchange for reservations, fishing rights, and cash payments. Chief Seattle (Si'ahl) was among the signers.

The Tulalip Reservation was established under the treaty, consolidating the Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, and other bands onto a single reservation on the shores of Tulalip Bay. The Tulalip Tribes today operate as a federally recognized tribal government and are the largest employer in Marysville.

Mukilteo 1855 – present

Named from the Snohomish word Buk-wil-tee-oh — "good camping ground" or "narrow passage."

Treaty Ground to Ferry Town

Treaty Site Ferry Boeing

Mukilteo sits on the Snohomish word Buk-wil-tee-oh, meaning "good camping ground" or "narrow passage" — a reference to the narrowing of Possession Sound between the mainland and Whidbey Island. The Point Elliott Treaty was signed on this beach on January 22, 1855, making it one of the most historically significant sites in the Pacific Northwest.

1855 Point Elliott Treaty signed on the beach at Mukilteo

1909 Ferry landing food service begins (see Food Heritage)

Mukilteo Lighthouse (1906): Built by the U.S. Lighthouse Service on the point where the treaty was signed. The wood-frame lighthouse guided ships through the narrow passage between Whidbey Island and the mainland. Now a city park and museum, it remains one of the most photographed lighthouses in Washington State.
Paine Field & Boeing: The Boeing Everett Factory, built starting in 1967 on the edge of Paine Field, is the largest building by volume in the world — 472 million cubic feet. It produces the 747, 767, 777, and 787 widebody aircraft. Paine Field itself dates to the 1930s and became a major military airfield during World War II before transitioning to commercial and industrial aviation use.

Everett 1890 – present

Founded on eastern capital, built on timber, tested by labor conflict, transformed by aerospace.

The County Seat

Timber Labor Naval Station Boeing

Everett was founded in 1890 by Henry Hewitt Jr. and a group of eastern investors, including John D. Rockefeller associates, who envisioned a Pacific Northwest industrial hub. The city was named after Everett Colby, son of investor Charles L. Colby, president of the Wisconsin Central Railroad.

The early economy centered on Weyerhaeuser timber, paper mills, and shipyards. The deep water of Port Gardner Bay made Everett a natural port, and the arrival of the Great Northern Railway in 1893 connected the city to eastern markets.

The Everett Massacre (November 5, 1916): Members of the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW, "Wobblies") sailed from Seattle on the steamer Verona to support striking shingle weavers in Everett. When the ship docked at the city wharf, armed vigilantes and deputies opened fire. Five IWW members were killed, with two deputies also dead — likely from crossfire. The event became a defining moment in Pacific Northwest labor history.
Naval Station Everett (1992): The Navy's most modern homeport, built on the former Scott Paper mill site on Port Gardner Bay. Home to a carrier strike group, guided-missile destroyers, and Coast Guard cutters. The base brought approximately 6,000 military personnel and their families to Snohomish County.

Silicon Forest 1917 – present

From Boeing's first factory to the semiconductor corridor — the tech roots of the Pacific Northwest.

The Tech Corridor

Tech Aerospace Semiconductors

The Pacific Northwest tech ecosystem is often dated to Boeing's founding in Seattle in 1916, but the broader "Silicon Forest" label emerged from the Portland-area cluster of Tektronix, Intel, and Hewlett-Packard in the 1970s. In Snohomish County, the story threads differently — through precision instruments, aerospace, and semiconductor fabrication.

1917 First Boeing factory on the Duwamish River

1948 Fluke Corporation founded — test and measurement instruments
1953 Hewlett-Packard opens first PNW operations
1968 Boeing Everett Factory — world's largest building, widebody jet production
1975 Microsoft founded in Albuquerque, moves to Bellevue 1979
1980s Philips Semiconductor establishes Bothell/Mill Creek corridor
1990s NXP (Philips spin-off) continues semiconductor fabrication in the corridor
2000s Microsoft influence spreads north from Redmond through south Snohomish County
2010s Tech workforce increasingly distributed across Snohomish County
Fluke Corporation: John Fluke Sr. started the company in his Springdale, Connecticut garage in 1948, building precision electrical measurement instruments. The company relocated to Seattle and eventually to Everett, where it became a major employer. Fluke's multimeters and calibration instruments are standard equipment in electronics labs worldwide. The company is now a Fortive subsidiary but maintains its Everett presence.
Philips/NXP Semiconductor Corridor: The Bothell-Mill Creek area became a node in the global semiconductor supply chain through Philips Semiconductor (later NXP) fabrication facilities. Combined with the Fluke instrumentation tradition and Boeing's avionics demands, Snohomish County developed a quiet but persistent electronics manufacturing base that predates the software-dominated tech narrative.

The Convergence Zone atmospheric

Where Pacific air splits around the Olympics, then crashes back together over south Snohomish County.

Puget Sound Convergence Zone

Microclimate PSCZ

The Puget Sound Convergence Zone (PSCZ) is one of the most distinctive and locally frustrating weather phenomena in the Pacific Northwest. When prevailing westerly or northwesterly winds encounter the Olympic Mountains, the airflow splits — one branch goes north through the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the other south through the Chehalis Gap. These two branches reconverge over the central Puget Sound lowlands, typically across a band from Everett to Lynnwood.

The convergence creates a narrow zone of enhanced precipitation, cloud formation, and sometimes thunderstorms that can be entirely absent just a few miles north or south. A day that is sunny in Seattle and sunny in Stanwood can deliver steady rain in Mukilteo.

The PSCZ is the reason weather forecasts for Snohomish County are notoriously unreliable. The zone shifts north and south depending on wind direction and speed, and it can activate or collapse within hours. This is the phenomenon that the Mukilteo Microclimate Observatory was built to track.

Why models disagree here: The PSCZ exists at the resolution limit of most weather models. A 12km grid cell cannot resolve a 5-mile-wide convergence band. Higher-resolution models (HRRR at 3km, NAM Nest at 3km) perform better, but the zone's rapid shifts still defeat deterministic prediction. This is why multiple source comparison — the core of the MUK dashboard — matters most in this specific geography.

Communities most affected by the PSCZ: Mukilteo, south Everett, Lynnwood, Edmonds, Mountlake Terrace, Bothell, and Mill Creek. The zone's northern boundary roughly follows the Snohomish River; its southern boundary varies but rarely extends past the King County line.

Transportation 1893 – 2037

Railroads, interurbans, highways, ferries, and the light rail yet to come.

Connecting the Corridor

Rail Ferry Aviation Light Rail

Snohomish County's transportation history tracks the broader arc of American mobility: from rail to trolley to automobile to air, with water crossings woven through every era. The county sits on the I-5 corridor between Seattle and the Canadian border, and its ferry connection to Whidbey Island remains one of the busiest routes in the Washington State Ferries system.

Interurban Trolley (1910–1939): The Everett-Seattle interurban electric railway ran 33 miles along a dedicated right-of-way, connecting Everett, Edmonds, Ballard, and downtown Seattle. At its peak it carried over a million passengers a year. The Interurban Trail, a paved multi-use path through Shoreline and Edmonds, follows parts of the original route.
Mukilteo-Clinton Ferry: The crossing between Mukilteo and Clinton on Whidbey Island is approximately 20 minutes and is operated by Washington State Ferries. The route is the primary vehicle connection between Whidbey Island and the mainland, carrying approximately 4 million passengers per year. The Mukilteo terminal was rebuilt and relocated in 2020 to a new multimodal facility.
Link Light Rail to Everett (2037): Sound Transit's Everett Link Extension will connect downtown Everett to the regional light rail network, running through south Everett, the SR 99 corridor, and connecting to the existing Lynnwood Link station. When complete, the trip from Everett to downtown Seattle will take approximately 55 minutes by rail — closing a loop that the interurban trolley opened over a century earlier.
