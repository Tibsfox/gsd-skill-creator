Sound Transit Light Rail

From streetcars to Link — 140 years of Seattle mass transit, station by station
Dashboard Trip Planner Light Rail Radio Research
Seattle tore up its streetcar tracks in 1941. Voters rejected a subway in 1968 and 1970. The region waited until 1996 to approve light rail, and the first trains didn't roll until 2009. Now, in 2026, Link crosses Lake Washington on a floating bridge and connects two counties. This is the story of how it happened, and a guide to every station.

The Streetcar Era (1884–1941)

Electric Rails Across the City

Seattle's first streetcars were horsecars operated by Frank Osgood as the Seattle Street Railway, beginning service on September 23, 1884. Electric traction arrived on March 30, 1889, making Seattle one of the earliest cities in the country to electrify its transit system.

Growth was explosive: by 1891, Seattle had 78 miles of street railway tracks, 70 miles of which had been built in just two years. Starting in 1898, banker Jacob Furth began quietly buying up the city's 22 independent streetcar lines on behalf of the national utility holding company Stone & Webster, which consolidated them under the Seattle Electric Company by 1900 with a 40-year operating franchise.

The Interurban Railway

Electric interurban railways defined early twentieth-century transportation in the Puget Sound region. By 1912, private interurban lines connected Tacoma, Seattle, and Everett:

  • Seattle–Tacoma Interurban (1902): Opened with stops in Rainier Beach, Renton, and Kent. Operated until 1928 when Highway 99 made the route uncompetitive.
  • Seattle–Everett Interurban (1910): Stone & Webster inaugurated service on April 30, 1910, connecting Seattle to Everett along a waterfront route through Ballard, Edmonds, and Mukilteo. Service ended in 1939.

The interurban railway's Mukilteo stop was near the current ferry terminal — the same corridor now served by the Sounder commuter rail. History echoes in transit routes.

The End of Streetcars

Seattle ripped up its streetcar tracks in 1941, joining the nationwide trend of replacing fixed-rail transit with buses. The decision was driven by declining ridership, competition from automobiles, and the expiration of Stone & Webster's 40-year franchise. For the next 68 years, Seattle would have no fixed-rail transit.

The Failed Attempts (1968–1970)

February 1968 — Forward Thrust I

A proposal for a 47-mile rapid transit system (subway with suburban extensions to Bellevue and Renton) appeared on the ballot as part of the Forward Thrust series of civic measures. The measure was approved by 51% of voters but failed to reach the 60% supermajority required by Washington state law for municipal bond issuance. The federal government had committed to covering roughly two-thirds of the cost. The money eventually went to Atlanta's MARTA system instead.

May 1970 — Forward Thrust II

A second attempt received only 46% approval. The "Boeing Bust" — a devastating local recession caused by massive layoffs at Boeing, the region's largest employer — soured voters on expensive infrastructure projects. A famous billboard read: "Will the last person leaving Seattle — turn out the lights." Mass transit was dead for a generation.

Sound Transit Is Born (1993–1996)

1993 — Regional Transit Authority Created

The Washington State Legislature authorized the creation of a Regional Transit Authority (RTA) encompassing King, Pierce, and Snohomish counties. The RTA would plan, build, and operate a regional transit system combining light rail, commuter rail, and express bus service.

November 5, 1996 — Sound Move Approved

After a failed first attempt in 1995, the ballot measure known as "Sound Move" was approved by 56.5% of voters across all three counties. The $3.9 billion package funded the initial segment of Link light rail, Sounder commuter rail, and ST Express bus service. The RTA renamed itself "Sound Transit" in August 1997 and adopted "Link" as the name for the light rail system.

Opening Timeline

July 18, 2009 — Central Link Opens

After 13 years of planning and construction, the first Link light rail trains carry passengers between Tukwila International Boulevard and Westlake Station in downtown Seattle. Initial ridership exceeded projections.

December 2009 — Airport Extension

Link extends south to Sea-Tac Airport, connecting the region's major airport to downtown for the first time by rail.

March 2016 — University Link

Two new stations open: Capitol Hill and University of Washington. Ridership jumps significantly as Link connects the university and one of Seattle's densest neighborhoods.

October 2021 — Northgate Link

Three new stations: U District, Roosevelt, and Northgate. The extension connects to the Northgate Transit Center and provides the north end's first direct rail connection to downtown.

August 2024 — Lynnwood Link

Four new stations extend the 1 Line north into Snohomish County: Shoreline North/185th, Shoreline South/148th, Mountlake Terrace, and Lynnwood City Center. This is the first Link station accessible by bus from Mukilteo (Community Transit Route 113 to Lynnwood Transit Center).

April 2024 — East Link (Bellevue segment)

The 2 Line begins service between South Bellevue and Redmond Technology Station with eight new stations on the Eastside.

December 2025 — Federal Way Link

Three new stations extend the 1 Line south: Kent Des Moines, Star Lake, and Federal Way Downtown.

March 28, 2026 — Crosslake Connection (2 Line Complete)

The 2 Line is completed with the opening of the cross-lake segment between Seattle and Bellevue via Mercer Island. The line runs across the I-90 floating bridge — the world's first light rail service on a floating bridge. From Lynnwood, passengers can now ride continuously to Downtown Redmond, a 33-mile journey across Lake Washington.

1 Line Station Guide

Lynnwood City Center to Federal Way Downtown — 26 stations, 41 miles

Lynnwood City Center

1 Line · 2 Line · Snohomish County · Opened Aug 2024

Northern terminus shared by both lines. Major park-and-ride (1,200 spaces). Community Transit hub connecting to Mukilteo, Everett, and north Snohomish County. Adjacent to the Lynnwood Convention Center and city center redevelopment.

Mountlake Terrace

1 Line · 2 Line · Snohomish County · Opened Aug 2024

Freeway station adjacent to I-5. Park-and-ride facility. The city of Mountlake Terrace was founded around its lake (originally called "Lake Ballinger") and has invested in transit-oriented development around the station.

Shoreline North/185th

1 Line · 2 Line · King County · Opened Aug 2024

Elevated station at NE 185th St. The area is rapidly densifying with new apartment and mixed-use construction. Bus connections to Aurora Village and points west.

Shoreline South/148th

1 Line · 2 Line · King County · Opened Aug 2024

At NE 148th St. Near the Shoreline Community College campus. The station area is transitioning from single-family homes to dense mixed-use development.

Northgate

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Oct 2021

Major transfer point. Adjacent to Northgate Mall (now redeveloped), the NHL Seattle Kraken practice facility, and North Seattle College. The pedestrian bridge over I-5 connects to the east side of the freeway and residential neighborhoods.

Roosevelt

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Oct 2021

Underground station in the Roosevelt neighborhood. Named for Roosevelt Way, named for President Theodore Roosevelt. Near the Roosevelt Whole Foods and a vibrant neighborhood commercial district. Bus connections to Green Lake and Wallingford.

U District

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Oct 2021

Underground station on Brooklyn Ave NE, in the heart of the University District. One block from "The Ave" (University Way NE), the main commercial strip. Home to college radio station KCMU (now KEXP's predecessor) and decades of UW student culture. Book and record stores, international restaurants, and the University Book Store.

College radio roots: KCMU broadcast from the UW campus for decades before becoming KEXP.

University of Washington

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Mar 2016

Deep underground station near Husky Stadium, with its distinctive curved roof canopy. Gateway to the UW campus — home to the Department of Atmospheric Sciences where Cliff Mass studies the convergence zone. The Burke Museum (natural history) and Henry Art Gallery are nearby.

Capitol Hill

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Mar 2016

Underground station at Broadway and John. Capitol Hill is Seattle's densest neighborhood and the historic heart of its LGBTQ+ community. Home to an extraordinary concentration of music venues, bars, and cultural institutions. The neighborhood was ground zero for Seattle's grunge scene in the late 1980s and early 1990s.

Music connections: KEXP's studios are nearby. Neumos, Barboza, and the recently closed Chop Suey defined the live music scene. Sub Pop Records' spiritual home.

Westlake

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Downtown Seattle's primary transit hub. Connection to the Seattle Streetcar (South Lake Union line), King County Metro bus tunnel, and monorail to Seattle Center. Westlake Center mall sits above. Pike Place Market is four blocks west.

Symphony

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Named for Benaroya Hall, home of the Seattle Symphony. Located on 3rd Avenue in the downtown retail core. Originally named "University Street" station, renamed in 2024. The Seattle Art Museum (SAM) is one block away.

Pioneer Square

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Seattle's oldest neighborhood, founded after the 1889 Great Fire. The original "Skid Road" (Yesler Way) ran through here — logs were skidded down the hill to Henry Yesler's sawmill, giving the world the term "skid row." Pioneer Square's art galleries, the Underground Tour, and Occidental Park anchor a neighborhood that was also the birthplace of Seattle's jazz scene.

Jazz heritage: Pioneer Square clubs hosted the Pacific Northwest jazz scene from the 1930s-1960s. The neighborhood's music legacy predates grunge by decades.

International District/Chinatown

1 Line · 2 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Transfer point between the 1 Line and 2 Line. Heart of Seattle's Asian-American community — the Chinatown-International District encompasses Chinatown, Japantown, and Little Saigon. Uwajimaya Village, Wing Luke Museum, and Hing Hay Park. King Street Station (Amtrak, Sounder) is adjacent.

Stadium

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Adjacent to T-Mobile Park (Mariners) and Lumen Field (Seahawks, Sounders). On game days, this is one of the busiest stations in the system. The station sits at the edge of the SODO industrial district.

SODO

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

South of Downtown — Seattle's industrial spine. Starbucks corporate headquarters (the "Siren" campus) is nearby. The SODO district houses warehouses, manufacturing, and an emerging arts scene in converted industrial spaces. Busway connections to south-end routes.

The industrial character has attracted rehearsal spaces and DIY music venues to the area's warehouse buildings.

Beacon Hill

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Deep tunnel station (160 feet below ground) serving one of Seattle's most diverse neighborhoods. The station required boring through Beacon Hill — the deepest station in the system. The neighborhood is home to a thriving Vietnamese, Chinese, and Filipino community with excellent restaurants along Beacon Avenue.

Mount Baker

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Elevated station in the Mount Baker neighborhood, with views of the Cascades. Named for the volcanic peak visible to the north. Near the Mount Baker rowing course on Lake Washington and Franklin High School.

Columbia City

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

At-grade station in Columbia City, one of Seattle's most diverse and vibrant neighborhoods. The Rainier Avenue commercial strip features restaurants, galleries, and the historic Columbia City Cinema (1921). Named one of America's best neighborhoods by multiple publications.

Othello

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Named for Othello Street. The surrounding Rainier Valley neighborhood is one of the most linguistically diverse ZIP codes in the United States, with over 60 languages spoken. East African, Southeast Asian, and Latin American communities give the area its distinctive character.

Rainier Beach

1 Line · Seattle · Opened Jul 2009

Southernmost Seattle station on the original Central Link alignment. The Rainier Beach neighborhood connects to the south end of Lake Washington. The area has deep roots in Seattle's African-American and hip-hop communities. The Rainier Beach Urban Farm and wetlands are nearby.

Hip-hop heritage: Rainier Valley is the cradle of Seattle hip-hop. Sir Mix-a-Lot, Macklemore, and others have roots here. The "Rainier" in local culture is as much about this valley as the mountain.

Tukwila International Boulevard

1 Line · Tukwila · Opened Jul 2009

Near Westfield Southcenter mall, the largest shopping center in the Pacific Northwest. International Boulevard (formerly Pacific Highway South) is home to immigrant communities from around the world. The Tukwila Urban Center redevelopment surrounds the station.

SeaTac/Airport

1 Line · SeaTac · Opened Dec 2009

Serves Seattle-Tacoma International Airport via a covered walkway to the terminal. The airport handled over 50 million passengers pre-pandemic and is the primary air gateway to the Pacific Northwest. Direct rail-to-air connection that took decades to achieve.

Angle Lake

1 Line · SeaTac · Opened Sep 2016

Named for the adjacent Angle Lake, a small urban lake with a park and swimming beach. The station includes a 1,100-space parking garage. Served as the southern terminus of the 1 Line from 2016 to 2025.

Kent Des Moines

1 Line · Kent/Des Moines · Opened Dec 2025

At the intersection of Kent-Des Moines Road and SR 99. Serves the Highline College campus and the surrounding communities of Kent and Des Moines.

Star Lake

1 Line · Federal Way · Opened Dec 2025

Park-and-ride facility near Star Lake. Serves north Federal Way neighborhoods and provides connections to local bus routes.

Federal Way Downtown

1 Line · Federal Way · Opened Dec 2025

Current southern terminus of the 1 Line. Major transit center with bus connections throughout south King County. The Federal Way Transit Center serves as a hub for routes to Tacoma, Auburn, and surrounding communities.

2 Line Station Guide (Eastside Segment)

The 2 Line shares stations with the 1 Line from Lynnwood to International District, then crosses Lake Washington to the Eastside.

Judkins Park

2 Line · Seattle · Opened Mar 2026

First station east of the downtown tunnel. Named for the adjacent Judkins Park in the Central District. The station provides access to the I-90 Trail, a cycling and pedestrian path that extends across the lake.

Mercer Island

2 Line · Mercer Island · Opened Mar 2026

On the I-90 corridor in the center of Mercer Island, a residential island community in Lake Washington. The station sits in the freeway lid park. The world's first light rail station on a floating bridge approach — the train runs on the fixed spans adjacent to the Homer M. Hadley Memorial Bridge.

South Bellevue

2 Line · Bellevue · Opened Apr 2024

1,500-space park-and-ride. Gateway to Bellevue from the south. Near Mercer Slough Nature Park, a 320-acre urban wetland — one of the largest remaining wetlands on Lake Washington.

Bellevue Downtown

2 Line · Bellevue · Opened Apr 2024

Bellevue's central station, in the heart of the city's urban core. Bellevue has transformed from a bedroom community to a major tech hub. Amazon, Meta, T-Mobile, and numerous other tech companies maintain significant campuses here. The Bellevue Collection (retail) and Bellevue Arts Museum are adjacent.

Bellevue's tech corridor proximity: Microsoft's main campus is accessible via bus connections from nearby stations. The Eastside's tech culture contrasts with Seattle's music heritage.

Wilburton

2 Line · Bellevue · Opened Apr 2024

Near the Bellevue Botanical Garden and the Spring District development. The Eastrail (former BNSF rail corridor) passes nearby, providing a regional cycling and walking path.

Spring District / 120th

2 Line · Bellevue · Opened Apr 2024

Serves the Spring District, a 36-acre mixed-use development built on former BNSF rail yard land. Facebook (Meta) and REI have offices here. A model of transit-oriented development — the neighborhood was designed around the station from the beginning.

Bel-Red / 130th

2 Line · Bellevue · Opened Apr 2024

In the Bel-Red corridor between Bellevue and Redmond. The area is transitioning from light industrial to mixed-use development. Near the planned Bel-Red Arts District.

Overlake Village

2 Line · Redmond · Opened Apr 2024

Near Microsoft's main campus in Redmond. The Connector shuttle links the station to Microsoft buildings. Overlake Village is developing as a dense urban node around the station. The area was formerly dominated by parking lots and low-rise office parks.

Redmond Technology

2 Line · Redmond · Opened Apr 2024

In Redmond's Overlake technology corridor. Serves the eastern portion of the Microsoft campus and other tech employers. Adjacent to SR 520.

Marymoor Village

2 Line · Redmond · Opened May 2025

Near Marymoor Park, King County's largest park at 640 acres. The park includes a velodrome, off-leash dog area, climbing wall, model airplane field, and the Sammamish River Trail. Concert venue hosts outdoor music events in summer.

Downtown Redmond

2 Line · Redmond · Opened May 2025

Eastern terminus of the 2 Line. In the heart of Redmond's old town center, near the Saturday Market and Bear Creek. Redmond was originally a logging town before becoming "the bicycle capital of the Northwest" and then a tech hub. The Redmond Town Center and numerous restaurants surround the station.

Ballard and the Music Connection

The Ballard extension is particularly significant for Seattle's music geography. Ballard is the historic center of Seattle's Nordic community (founded by Scandinavian fishermen and loggers) and home to the Hiram M. Chittenden Locks (Ballard Locks). It has become one of the city's premier live music neighborhoods, with venues like Tractor Tavern, Sunset Tavern, and the Conor Byrne Pub.

When the Ballard Link opens, it will connect Capitol Hill's music scene to Ballard's venues via a single train line, with South Lake Union's tech campus in between — a transit corridor that mirrors the evolution of Seattle's cultural geography.
