## 1. Historical Arc: Newton to Digital

Color theory -- the study of rules and guidelines for color use in art and design -- was first formally referenced by Italian humanist Leon Battista Alberti in 1435 [1][2].

| Year | Figure | Contribution |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1435 | Leon Battista Alberti | First formal reference to color theory in art |
| 1704 | Isaac Newton | *Opticks*: demonstrated white light contains all spectral colors; established the color wheel |
| 1810 | Johann Wolfgang von Goethe | *Theory of Colors*: linked color directly to emotional response |
| 1905 | Albert Munsell | Systematic three-dimensional color space (hue, value, chroma) |
| 1919 | Johannes Itten | Bauhaus color theory; subjective color experience in art education |
| 1940s | Kurt Goldstein | Physiological evidence for color-emotion links |
| 1960s+ | CIE, RGB, sRGB | Digital color standards; additive light-based color systems |

### 1.1 Newton's Foundation

Newton's prism experiments demonstrated that white light is composed of a continuous spectrum of colors. By arranging the spectrum in a circle, he created the color wheel -- the organizational framework that still underlies color theory. The wheel revealed complementary relationships: colors opposite each other on the wheel produce the strongest visual contrast.

### 1.2 Goethe's Emotional Color

Goethe associated "plus colors" (yellow, red-yellow, yellow-red) with warmth, excitement, and positive affect, and "minus colors" (blue, blue-violet) with coolness, calm, and contemplation. While some of Goethe's specific claims have not held up to empirical scrutiny, his fundamental insight -- that color has systematic emotional effects independent of the object it colors -- was confirmed by subsequent research.

### 1.3 Munsell's Three Dimensions

The Munsell color system provided the first systematic three-dimensional color space based on psychological principles: **hue** (color identity), **value** (brightness), and **chroma** (saturation). This framework remains the standard reference for controlled color psychology research because it separates the three dimensions that independently influence perception.

## 2. Color Psychology: The Measured Effects

A peer-reviewed study in BMC Psychology (2025) investigated emotional valence responses to color across hue, brightness, and saturation dimensions [3]:

| Hue | Arousal Level | Common Associations |
|-----|---------------|---------------------|
| Red | Highest | Energy, urgency, warmth, passion |
| Yellow | High | Brightness, cheer, energy (Western); jealousy (French) |
| Orange | High | Warmth, social connection |
| Green | Intermediate | Freshness, calm, nature, health |
| Blue | Low | Trust, calm, coolness, sadness |
| Purple | Intermediate | Royalty, creativity, imagination |

### 2.1 Key Findings

- Warm hues (yellow, orange, red, red-purple) consistently produce higher emotional arousal and intensity
- Cool hues (blue, blue-green, green) evoke calm and relaxation
- High saturation correlates with stronger emotional response across all hue categories
- High brightness links to positive valence (happier associations)
- Art-trained observers show systematic differences in color-emotion perception, suggesting both training and innate sensitivity modulate the response

### 2.2 Goldstein's Physiological Evidence

Neuropsychologist Kurt Goldstein (1940s) found that red worsened motor tremors and balance while green improved motor function -- physiological evidence that color affects the nervous system beyond subjective experience [4].

### 2.3 Cultural Variation

Color associations vary significantly across cultures. Yellow connotes brightness and cheer in the United States but jealousy in France. White signals mourning in Eastern contexts but purity in Western ones. Any analysis of color psychology must note these variations rather than presenting Western associations as universal.

## 3. Hue, Saturation, and Brightness as Independent Variables

A comprehensive review (Elliot, *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2015) confirmed that the three basic color properties each independently influence psychological functioning [4]:

- **Hue** -- The most studied dimension. Red produces the most emotional arousal; blue the least; green intermediate.
- **Saturation (chroma)** -- Higher saturation = stronger emotional response. A vivid red produces more arousal than a muted red.
- **Brightness (value)** -- Higher brightness = more positive valence. A bright yellow feels cheerful; a dark yellow feels sickly.

Most research has focused on hue, leaving saturation and brightness understudied. This is a gap the ARC research module helps fill by examining how artists like Phil Lewis manipulate all three dimensions simultaneously.

## 4. Art Movements as Color Laboratories

Several art movements treated color as their primary experimental variable [2][5]:

### 4.1 Fauvism

The Fauvist movement (early 20th century, Matisse central) treated color as autonomous from descriptive function. Henri Matisse: "When I put down green it doesn't mean grass, and when I put down blue it doesn't mean sky." The Fauvists used complementary colors and high saturation to maximize emotional impact. The name "les fauves" (wild beasts) was originally a critique of their "unfinished" appearance.

### 4.2 Picasso's Blue Period

Picasso's Blue Period (1901-1904) demonstrated monochromatic color's capacity for sustained emotional register. Using only blue and green hues after a friend's death, he produced paintings of profound melancholy -- benchmarks of color-emotion research. The constraint of a single hue family forced all emotional variation onto the saturation and brightness axes.

### 4.3 Rothko's Color Fields

Mark Rothko's monochromatic color field paintings eliminated hue variation to focus emotional response on subtle within-color variation. A Rothko painting with two large rectangles of similar but not identical red forces the viewer to attend to saturation and brightness differences that would be invisible in a more complex composition.

## 5. RGB vs. Pigment: The Digital-Analog Divide

Phil Lewis and other contemporary artists who combine traditional and digital media navigate two fundamentally different color systems [2][6]:

| Property | RGB (Digital) | Pigment (Traditional) |
|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| Color mixing | Additive (light + light = brighter) | Subtractive (pigment + pigment = darker) |
| White | All channels maximum | Absence of pigment (paper/canvas) |
| Black | All channels zero | All pigments combined (or black pigment) |
| Luminosity | Can exceed reflection; colors "glow" | Limited to reflected light; no self-luminous colors |
| Gamut | sRGB: ~35% of visible spectrum | Depends on pigment set; typically narrower |
| Precision | Exact numerical control (R:127, G:45, B:200) | Skill-dependent mixing; inherently approximate |

### 5.1 The Luminosity Advantage

Digital color enables effects impossible in traditional media: colors that appear to glow, emit light, or float above the surface. When Phil Lewis places an electric teal (high saturation, high brightness) against a warm amber, the digital medium produces a luminosity contrast that pigment cannot achieve. The teal appears to radiate light.

This is not a trick or an illusion -- the screen literally emits light at those wavelengths. The digital artist's palette includes self-luminous colors that no painter in history had access to before the screen.

### 5.2 Hybrid Practice

Lewis's practice -- pen-and-ink as the raw organic base, digital color as the expansion medium -- navigates both systems. The ink work provides the organic structure (subtractive: black marks on white paper). The digital color provides the luminous expansion (additive: light-based color applied to scanned linework). The synthesis produces compositions that are rooted in traditional craft but expressed with a palette that only digital tools provide.
