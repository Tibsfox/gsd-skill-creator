## 1. Current PNW Grid Architecture

### 1.1 The Hydroelectric Foundation

The Pacific Northwest electric grid is unlike any other in the contiguous United States. No other region derives the majority of its electricity from a single renewable resource at this scale. The Columbia River and its tributaries, dammed by a chain of federal and public utility facilities built between the 1930s and 1970s, produce more hydroelectric power than any other river system in North America. This infrastructure gave the PNW some of the lowest electricity rates and lowest carbon intensity in the nation -- and simultaneously created a deep structural dependency on precipitation patterns that are now shifting.

**Regional Generation Summary (2024):**

| Metric | Washington | Oregon | PNW Combined |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Total net generation (GWh) | ~102,400 | ~47,000 | ~149,400 |
| Hydroelectric share | 63.2% | ~42% | ~56% |
| Wind share | ~8% | ~15% | ~10% |
| Natural gas share | ~12% | ~38% | ~21% |
| Nuclear share | ~8% | 0% | ~5% |
| Solar share | ~1% | ~3% | ~2% |
| Geothermal share | 0% | ~0.4% | <0.3% |

*Sources: EIA-WA, EIA-OR. Oregon 2024 figures estimated from 2023 data. Percentages approximate.*

The disparity between the two states is striking. Washington generates 63.2% of its electricity from hydropower and only 12% from natural gas (EIA-WA). Oregon generates roughly 42% from hydropower but still relies on natural gas for 38.1% of its electricity -- nearly three times Washington's gas share (EIA-OR). This means the decarbonization challenge is fundamentally different in each state. Washington's path to 100% clean by 2045 under CETA requires displacing a relatively small gas wedge. Oregon's path to 100% clean by 2040 under HB 2021 requires eliminating a gas dependency that currently provides more than a third of all generation -- and doing so on a timeline five years shorter than Washington's.

### 1.2 Wind: The Columbia Gorge Corridor

Wind energy is the PNW's second-largest renewable resource after hydropower. The Columbia River Gorge, where the river cuts through the Cascade Range, funnels prevailing westerlies into one of North America's premier onshore wind corridors, with average wind speeds of 7-9 m/s. Installed wind capacity across Oregon and Washington exceeds 5 GW, with capacity factors of 30-40% in the Gorge corridor (Module E, E-PNW; Module F, F-GRID).

Wind's seasonal profile in the PNW is a critical grid planning factor. Gorge wind is strongest during the spring, precisely when hydroelectric generation is also at its peak due to snowmelt runoff. This creates a dual surplus condition: both wind and hydro produce more power than the region can consume. During these periods, BPA has historically curtailed wind generation to manage grid frequency and prevent involuntary spill at dams (Module E, E-PNW). This curtailment represents lost economic value and a barrier to meeting clean energy targets.

Wind generation also runs at night, unlike solar, giving it 24-hour production capability. This characteristic makes wind particularly valuable for pairing with electrolyzers (Module E) and for meeting overnight heating demand from heat pumps (Module A).

### 1.3 Solar: Growing but Still Small

Solar generation in the PNW remains a small fraction of total output -- approximately 3.2% in Oregon and roughly 1% in Washington (EIA-OR, EIA-WA). The PNW's cloudy winters limit solar's contribution during the season when electricity demand is highest. However, solar resources improve significantly east of the Cascades, where central and eastern Oregon receive solar irradiance comparable to parts of the Southwest.

Solar's primary grid integration value in the PNW is as a summer peak resource, complementing the spring peak of hydroelectric and wind. Its seasonal anti-correlation with winter heating demand means solar cannot be the sole replacement for natural gas generation during the months when gas plants run hardest.

### 1.4 Natural Gas: The Gap to Close

Natural gas generation is the carbon problem the PNW must solve. Oregon's 38.1% gas share is the single largest obstacle to its 2040 clean electricity mandate (EIA-OR). The closure of the Boardman coal plant in 2020 was a significant milestone, but much of that capacity was replaced by gas -- an improvement in carbon intensity but not a path to zero.

Washington's 12% gas share is smaller but still represents roughly 12,300 GWh of generation that must be displaced by 2045 (EIA-WA). In both states, gas plants serve two functions that are difficult for variable renewables to replicate:

1. **Dispatchable capacity:** Gas plants generate on demand, regardless of weather or season. Grid operators can call on gas during cold snaps, heat waves, or drought years when hydro is low.
2. **Winter peak coverage:** The PNW's winter heating demand coincides with the period of lowest solar output and potentially low wind. Gas plants currently fill this gap.

Any credible 100% clean electricity architecture must replace both of these functions -- not just the energy (annual GWh) but the on-demand dispatchability and the seasonal reliability.

### 1.5 The Institutional Landscape

The PNW grid is not managed by a single entity. Its institutional complexity shapes every aspect of generation planning and dispatch.

**Bonneville Power Administration (BPA):** The federal power marketing administration sells electricity from 31 federal dams, operates approximately 15,000 circuit miles of high-voltage transmission, and functions as the balancing authority for its service area. BPA is entirely self-funded from power and transmission sales -- it receives no congressional appropriations. This financial structure creates strong incentives to maximize generation revenue while simultaneously funding $500-700 million per year in fish and wildlife mitigation (Module F, Section 4.2).

**Public Utility Districts (PUDs):** Washington's voter-created PUDs -- particularly Grant County PUD (Wanapum and Priest Rapids dams, ~2,048 MW) and Douglas County PUD (Wells Dam, 840 MW) -- produce far more electricity than their local ratepayers consume. The surplus is sold on wholesale markets, often at very low prices during spring runoff (Module E, E-PNW).

**Investor-Owned Utilities:** PacifiCorp, Portland General Electric, Puget Sound Energy, and Avista serve large portions of both states and are subject to state utility commission regulation. These utilities face the most direct pressure from clean energy mandates because their generation portfolios include significant gas capacity.

**Western Interconnection:** Washington and Oregon operate within the Western Interconnection, one of three major AC grids in North America. All generators synchronize at 60 Hz across 14 states, two Canadian provinces, and a portion of Baja California. The Western Electricity Coordinating Council (WECC) sets reliability standards (Module F, Section 10.1).

### 1.6 Seasonal Patterns

The PNW grid follows a pronounced seasonal rhythm dictated by Columbia Basin snowmelt hydrology:

| Season | Grid Condition | Key Dynamic |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Spring (Mar-Jun) | Hydro surplus + strong wind | Peak generation; export to CA; curtailment risk; BPA may spill |
| Summer (Jul-Sep) | Declining hydro; rising demand | Bidirectional flows with CA; solar supplements; gas ramps up |
| Fall (Oct-Nov) | Lowest hydro; mild demand | Both PNW and CA may be tight; market prices spike |
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | Moderate hydro; heating peak | Storage draw-down; gas covers peaks; limited export capacity |

*Source: Module F, Section 10.7*

This seasonal cycle defines the integration challenge. Spring surplus and winter deficit are the two poles around which the entire clean energy architecture must be designed.

## 2. The Climate Variability Challenge

> **Sensitivity classification: GATE (SC-CLI)**
> Climate projections in this section cite PNNL and associated agency sources. All projections include uncertainty language and scenario specification. No projection is presented as certainty.

### 2.1 The 2023 Drought: A Stress Test

In 2023, Oregon and Washington experienced hydroelectric production approximately 20% below 2021 levels -- the lowest output in roughly two decades (EIA-WA, EIA-OR, PNNL-HYDRO). Oregon's hydroelectric share dropped to 42.1%, well below its historical average of over 50% (EIA-OR). The production shortfall was driven by drought conditions across the Columbia River Basin: reduced snowpack, earlier snowmelt timing, and below-average precipitation in key headwater regions.

The cascading effects were immediate:

- **Gas backfill:** Natural gas generation increased to cover the hydroelectric deficit, raising Oregon's grid carbon intensity and complicating progress toward HB 2021 targets (EIA-OR)
- **Market purchases:** Oregon utilities purchased power from out-of-state sources, including California gas plants
- **Revenue pressure:** Lower hydroelectric generation reduced BPA's revenue base
- **Price spikes:** Wholesale power prices increased during deficit periods

Recovery in 2024, when improved precipitation restored much of the deficit, demonstrated the high year-to-year variability that makes planning so difficult. A single drought year is manageable. A sequence of low-water years -- which climate projections suggest may become more frequent -- would stress the grid beyond its current planning margins.

### 2.2 What PNNL Projects

The PNNL 2024 Hydropower Climate Study, drawing on data from more than 1,400 hydropower facilities nationwide, provides the most comprehensive assessment available of how climate change may affect U.S. hydropower generation (PNNL-HYDRO).

**National findings:**
- U.S. hydropower generation is projected to increase by approximately 5% by 2039 and approximately 10% by 2059 relative to recent baselines (PNNL-HYDRO)
- These national averages mask significant regional variability (PNNL-HYDRO)
- The Southwest is the only U.S. region projected to see average generation decline (PNNL-HYDRO)

**PNW-specific findings (with uncertainty):**

The PNNL study suggests the PNW may see a modest long-term increase in average annual precipitation, supporting slightly higher average generation. However, several critical caveats apply (PNNL-HYDRO):

1. **Snowpack decline:** Warmer temperatures shift precipitation from snow to rain, reducing the natural mountain snowpack reservoir and compressing the runoff season
2. **Timing shift:** Earlier snowmelt moves the spring freshet forward by 2-4 weeks in many model runs, potentially misaligning peak generation with peak demand
3. **Increased drought frequency:** Even with higher average precipitation, drought years may become more frequent and severe due to increased evapotranspiration
4. **Amplified extremes:** Both flood and drought extremes are projected to intensify, widening the range of possible generation outcomes in any given year

**The planning implication:** The long-term average may be roughly stable or slightly improving, but the year-to-year variance is growing. A grid planner who designs for the average will be caught short in drought years and unable to capture value in surplus years. The 100% clean electricity mandates require a system that works in the worst water years, not just the average ones.

### 2.3 The Reliability Gap

The climate variability projections create a specific engineering problem. During a drought year, the PNW could lose 15-25% of its hydroelectric generation -- roughly 12,000-20,000 GWh relative to an average year (calculated from Module F generation data and PNNL-HYDRO variability ranges). Under current conditions, natural gas fills this gap. Under a 100% clean electricity mandate, gas is not available.

What fills the gap?

This is the central question that the four-pillar model addresses. No single resource can fill a 15,000+ GWh annual shortfall that arrives unpredictably in drought years. The answer requires a portfolio: a baseload resource that does not depend on weather (geothermal), a flexible dispatch resource with seasonal storage (pumped hydro), a long-duration energy storage medium (hydrogen), and a demand-side resource that can shift load (grid-interactive heat pumps).

## 3. The Four-Pillar Integration Model

The four pillars described here are not independent solutions. They form an interlocking system where each resource compensates for the weaknesses of the others. Geothermal provides the baseload that hydro can no longer guarantee in every year. Pumped storage and hydrogen provide the time-shifting that variable renewables require. Heat pumps provide the demand flexibility that allows supply-side variability to be absorbed without blackouts.

### 3.1 Pillar 1: Geothermal Baseload

**Role:** 24/7 dispatchable generation that does not depend on precipitation, wind, or sunlight.

Geothermal energy is the renewable resource operationally closest to natural gas: it generates power continuously, on demand, regardless of weather or season. At 90-95% capacity factor, geothermal outperforms every other renewable resource and matches nuclear (Module F, Section 11.1). Neal Hot Springs in Malheur County, Oregon -- the PNW's only commercial geothermal power plant at 22 MW -- has demonstrated this reliability since 2012 with capacity factors at or above 90% (Module F, Section 7.3, GEO-RISE).

**Why the PNW has been slow to develop geothermal:**

The answer is simple: cheap hydropower. When Columbia River dams provided electricity at some of the lowest rates in the nation, there was no economic incentive to drill expensive geothermal wells. This created an institutional knowledge gap -- the PNW has far less geothermal exploration data than California or Nevada (Module F, Section 7.1, GEO-RISE).

**What has changed:**

Three shifts have altered the calculus. First, climate variability has introduced uncertainty into hydroelectric generation forecasts, eroding the reliability premium that hydro once provided. Second, Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) technology has advanced from concept to commercial demonstration -- Fervo Energy's Cape Station project in Nevada delivered 3.5 MW from a two-well EGS system to the grid in 2024-2025 (Module F, Section 8.5). Third, both Washington (Geothermal Resources Act, 2024) and Oregon (HB 2021's clean energy mandate) have created policy frameworks that favor geothermal development (Module F, Sections 7.6, 3.4).

**PNW geothermal resources:**

The Cascade Range contains the PNW's highest-temperature geothermal resources, with Newberry Crater in central Oregon measuring temperatures exceeding 300 degrees C at 3,000 meters depth and predicted temperatures above 400 degrees C at greater depths (ALTAROCK). However, most Cascade volcanic centers are protected as national parks, monuments, or wilderness areas -- Mount Rainier, Mount St. Helens, Crater Lake, and others are effectively off-limits to energy development. Newberry is the exception: it sits on Bureau of Land Management land with a geothermal lease area (Module F, Section 11.2).

Eastern Oregon's Basin and Range province offers a different resource type: moderate-temperature (120-180 degrees C) hydrothermal systems along extensional faults, proven commercially at Neal Hot Springs. These represent the path of least resistance for near-term expansion because they do not require EGS technology and sit on private or BLM land with simpler permitting (Module F, Section 11.3).

**Grid integration value:**

Geothermal's value to the PNW grid is not measured solely in megawatts. Its primary contribution is reliability during exactly the conditions when the rest of the grid is stressed: drought years when hydro is low, calm nights when wind and solar produce nothing, and winter cold snaps when heating demand peaks. One megawatt of geothermal displaces one megawatt of gas capacity that would otherwise be needed as drought insurance.

| Resource | Typical PNW Capacity Factor | Dispatchable? | Climate-Variable? |
|----------|---------------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Geothermal | 90-95% | Yes | No |
| Nuclear | 90-93% | Yes (limited ramp) | No |
| Hydroelectric (storage) | 30-50% | Yes | Yes (precipitation) |
| Wind (onshore) | 25-35% | No | Yes (wind patterns) |
| Solar PV | 15-25% | No | Yes (clouds, season) |

*Sources: EIA-WA, EIA-OR, GEO-RISE. Module F, Section 11.1.*

### 3.2 Pillar 2: Hydroelectric Dispatch and Pumped Storage

**Role:** Flexible dispatch, seasonal storage, and grid balancing -- the system's shock absorber.

Hydroelectric power is not going away. Even with climate variability, it will remain the PNW's dominant generation resource for decades. The question is how to optimize it for a grid that no longer has gas backup.

**Storage dams vs. run-of-river:**

The distinction between storage dams and run-of-river dams is fundamental to understanding hydroelectric flexibility (Module F, Section 4.4). Storage dams like Grand Coulee (6,809 MW) can hold water and release it on schedule, ramping generation up or down within minutes. Run-of-river dams like Chief Joseph and Bonneville must pass water as it arrives, with minimal ability to time-shift generation. The entire FCRPS relies on a few upstream storage dams -- primarily Grand Coulee, Libby, and Dworshak -- to regulate flows through the downstream run-of-river chain. When Grand Coulee releases stored water, every downstream dam generates more power (Module F, Section 4.4).

**Pumped storage -- the missing link:**

Pumped storage hydropower (PSH) is the most mature large-scale energy storage technology in the world, with round-trip efficiency of 70-85%, discharge duration of 6-20 hours, service life of 50-100 years, and effectively unlimited cycle life (Module F, Section 9.1). Two major PSH projects are in development in the PNW:

| Project | Location | Capacity | Storage Duration | Status |
|---------|----------|----------|-----------------|--------|
| Goldendale Energy Storage | Klickitat County, WA | 1,200 MW | 12 hours (14,400 MWh) | FERC licensing in progress |
| Swan Lake North | Klamath County, OR | 393 MW | 10 hours (3,930 MWh) | FERC license issued 2019 |

*Source: Module F, Section 9.3.*

Goldendale's 1,200 MW capacity would make it one of the largest PSH facilities in the United States. Its location near the Columbia Gorge wind corridor and multiple transmission lines positions it to absorb surplus wind and hydro generation during spring and dispatch during winter evening peaks.

**How pumped storage complements variable hydro:**

PSH addresses the seasonal mismatch at the heart of the PNW grid problem. During spring surplus, when hydroelectric and wind generation exceed demand and BPA faces curtailment decisions, PSH can absorb excess energy by pumping water uphill. During summer and fall, when hydro declines and demand rises, PSH releases that stored energy. This is not seasonal storage in the full sense -- PSH provides hours to days of shifting, not months -- but it smooths the daily and weekly variability that makes grid management difficult during transition seasons.

**Dam modernization:**

Turbine upgrade programs at existing federal dams can increase generation efficiency by 5-10% without building new dams or raising reservoir levels. Modern Kaplan and Francis turbine designs extract more energy per unit of water flow, which means more generation from the same environmental flow releases. These upgrades also improve operational flexibility, allowing faster ramp rates and wider operating ranges that better complement variable wind and solar (Module F, Section 4).

### 3.3 Pillar 3: Hydrogen Storage

**Role:** Seasonal-scale energy storage and inter-regional energy transport -- the bridge between spring surplus and winter deficit.

Batteries store energy for hours. Pumped storage stores energy for hours to days. Hydrogen stores energy for days to seasons. This timescale hierarchy is the key to understanding hydrogen's role in the PNW grid -- it is not competing with batteries for 4-hour duration; it is providing the weeks-to-months buffer that no other storage technology can economically deliver (Module E, E-PNW; Module F, Section 9.5).

**The surplus-to-storage pathway:**

The PNW regularly generates more electricity than it can consume or export during spring snowmelt. BPA has historically curtailed wind and, when transmission is saturated, spilled water at dams without generating power. Both represent wasted energy. Electrolyzers offer a productive alternative: converting surplus hydroelectric and wind electricity into hydrogen that can be stored and reconverted during deficit periods.

The economics of this pathway depend on electricity cost, electrolyzer capital cost, and the value of the stored hydrogen. Module E provides a PNW-specific LCOH estimate:

| Parameter | Value | Basis |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Electricity cost | $25/MWh | BPA industrial rate |
| Electrolyzer CAPEX | $600/kW | DOE-H2COST (PEM, 2024) |
| System efficiency | 55 kWh/kg | Conservative PEM estimate |
| Capacity factor | 85% | Hydroelectric baseload minus maintenance |
| **Estimated LCOH** | **$2.10-2.60/kg** | NREL-LCOH methodology |

*Source: Module E, E-PNW. This is a current-technology estimate; projected cost reductions may bring LCOH below $2/kg by 2028-2030 (SC-MED: projection, not demonstrated).*

With the IRA Section 45V production tax credit of $3.00/kg for the lowest-emission tier, PNW green hydrogen production becomes immediately cost-competitive -- the credit exceeds the production cost, creating a net-negative cost that incentivizes market development (Module E, E-ECON).

**Round-trip efficiency -- the honest assessment:**

Power-to-hydrogen-to-power has a round-trip efficiency of approximately 33-44%, accounting for electrolysis (75-80%), compression (88-93%), storage (~99%), and fuel cell reconversion (50-60%) (Module E). This is substantially worse than batteries (85-95%) or pumped hydro (70-85%). For short-duration storage, hydrogen loses on efficiency. For seasonal storage, hydrogen wins on cost per unit of stored energy: $0.50-2.00/kWh in compressed gas or geological caverns, compared to $150-300/kWh for battery capacity (Module E, OIES-ET48).

The crossover point is approximately 100-200 hours of storage duration. Below that, batteries and pumped storage are more efficient and more economic. Above that, hydrogen is the only viable option at grid scale.

**PNW Hydrogen Hub:**

The DOE selected the Pacific Northwest Hydrogen Hub (PNWH2) as one of seven Regional Clean Hydrogen Hubs funded through the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law. The hub concept centers on large-scale electrolyzers co-located with hydroelectric facilities and wind farms along the Columbia River corridor, serving industrial hydrogen consumers, heavy-duty transportation, and potentially ammonia production (Module E, E-PNW, DOE-MYPP).

Two public utility districts merit specific attention. Douglas County PUD (Wells Dam, 840 MW) and Grant County PUD (Wanapum and Priest Rapids dams, ~2,048 MW) produce far more electricity than their local ratepayers consume. During spring surplus, these PUDs sell power at wholesale prices that can drop to $15-25/MWh -- or even go negative. Hydrogen electrolysis at these sites would capture value from surplus generation that currently has no productive outlet (Module E, E-PNW).

**Seasonal production profile:**

| Season | Hydro Availability | Wind Resource | Hydrogen Strategy |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Spring (Apr-Jun) | Peak (snowmelt) | Strong (Gorge) | Maximum production; storage buildup |
| Summer (Jul-Sep) | Declining | Moderate | Steady production; begin drawing stored H2 |
| Fall (Oct-Dec) | Low to moderate | Variable | Reduced production; rely on stored H2 |
| Winter (Jan-Mar) | Moderate (rain) | Strong | Moderate production; balance with heating demand |

*Source: Module E, E-PNW.*

This profile aligns hydrogen production with the PNW's natural energy surplus and hydrogen consumption with the deficit periods -- exactly the seasonal bridging function that the 100% clean grid requires.

### 3.4 Pillar 4: Heat Pump Demand Response

**Role:** Flexible load that can shift electricity consumption to match available supply -- the building envelope as grid infrastructure.

The first three pillars are supply-side resources: they generate or store electricity. The fourth pillar operates on the demand side. Grid-interactive heat pumps can increase or decrease electricity consumption in response to grid signals, turning millions of buildings into distributed thermal batteries.

**How demand response works with heat pumps:**

Buildings have thermal mass. A well-insulated home can coast for 1-4 hours without active heating before occupants notice discomfort (Module A, Section 8.3). This thermal inertia creates a window for demand flexibility:

- **Pre-heating (thermal charging):** Before a grid peak event, the heat pump raises the building temperature 2-4 degrees F above setpoint. The thermal mass stores this energy. During the peak, the heat pump reduces output or shuts off. The building coasts to normal setpoint. Net energy consumption is roughly the same, but peak demand is reduced (Module A, Section 8.3).
- **Price-responsive operation:** Time-of-use rates incentivize heat pump operation during off-peak hours. Variable-speed inverter-driven heat pumps modulate output to maximize operation during cheap or clean electricity periods (Module A, Section 8.3).
- **Frequency regulation:** Fast-responding inverter-driven heat pumps can adjust compressor speed within seconds to support grid frequency. Each unit contributes 0.5-3 kW of flexibility; aggregated across thousands of units, the resource is significant (Module A, Section 8.3).

**CTA-2045 -- the enabling standard:**

CTA-2045 is a modular communication interface standard that allows utility demand-response signals to reach appliances directly. Washington state requires CTA-2045 ports on all electric water heaters sold in the state since 2021. Heat pump manufacturers including Mitsubishi, Daikin, and Carrier are integrating CTA-2045 or equivalent interfaces (Module A, Section 8.6).

The practical significance: a heat pump with CTA-2045 can receive utility signals to reduce output during peaks, pre-heat before demand events, or shift to higher-efficiency operating points. The occupant sets comfort boundaries; the utility optimizes within them. This transforms heat pumps from passive loads into grid assets.

**The building envelope multiplier:**

Demand response effectiveness scales with building envelope quality. A well-insulated home (R-21+ walls, R-49+ ceiling, 3-5 ACH50 air infiltration) has a peak heating demand of 12-20 BTU/h per square foot, needs a smaller heat pump (2-3 tons for a 2,000 sq ft home), and can coast longer during demand response events. A poorly insulated home (R-11 walls, 8-15 ACH50) has peak demand of 25-45 BTU/h per square foot, needs 5+ tons, and cannot coast as long (Module A, Section 9.1).

At the scale of millions of homes, this difference determines whether the existing distribution grid can absorb electrified heating or requires billions in upgrades. Envelope improvements are grid investments, not just homeowner efficiency measures (Module A, Section 8.2).

**Grid impact of mass adoption:**

BPA and regional utility modeling suggests that electrifying 50% of PNW residential heating with heat pumps would add approximately 3,000-5,000 MW of winter peak demand (Module A, Section 10.4). BPA's current firm winter peak capability is approximately 28,000-30,000 MW. The additional demand is significant but manageable if:

1. Heat pumps are grid-interactive (CTA-2045 or equivalent)
2. Building envelopes are improved to reduce peak loads
3. Demand response programs are operational at scale
4. Other supply-side resources (geothermal, stored hydrogen) cover the net peak increase

Without demand response, mass heat pump adoption turns the winter peak problem into a crisis. With demand response, it turns heat pumps into grid stabilizers that absorb surplus generation during off-peak hours and shed load during peaks.

## 4. Salmon and Grid Integration

> **Sensitivity classification: ANNOTATE (SC-ADV) + GATE (SC-IND)**
> This section presents ecological constraints on dam operations as engineering parameters affecting grid flexibility. No advocacy for dam removal or retention. All tribal references name specific nations per SC-IND protocol.

### 4.1 The Engineering Constraint

Salmon are not a sidebar in PNW grid planning -- they are an operating constraint that directly reduces the flexibility of the largest generation fleet in the region. Thirteen distinct salmon and steelhead populations in the Columbia River Basin are listed under the Endangered Species Act (Module F, Section 6.1). Federal dam operations are governed by Biological Opinions issued by NOAA Fisheries under the ESA, Columbia Basin Fish Accords, and the *United States v. Oregon* management agreement.

These legal frameworks impose specific operational requirements:

- **Spill operations:** During the spring juvenile migration season (typically April through June), federal dams must spill water over spillways rather than routing it through turbines, because spill passage provides higher juvenile survival rates than turbine passage. Spill reduces generation at exactly the time when the hydroelectric system produces its most surplus -- the same surplus that could power electrolyzers for hydrogen production (Module F, Section 6.3).
- **Flow augmentation:** Stored water from upstream reservoirs (Dworshak, Grand Coulee) is released to increase river velocity through reservoir reaches, accelerating juvenile downstream migration. These releases are timed for biological benefit, not generation optimization (Module F, Section 6.3).
- **Fish passage infrastructure:** Fish ladders, juvenile bypass systems, and barging/trucking programs at federal dams require continuous operation during migration seasons, consuming staff resources and occasionally requiring generation curtailment for maintenance (Module F, Section 6.3).

### 4.2 Treaty Rights as Grid Parameters

Several Columbia Basin tribal nations hold treaty-reserved fishing rights that are not grants from the federal government but pre-existing rights retained when tribes ceded lands. Federal courts, including the U.S. Supreme Court in *United States v. Winans* (1905), have consistently upheld these reserved rights (Module F, Section 6.2).

The specific nations with treaty-reserved Columbia Basin fishing rights relevant to grid operations include:

- **Yakama Nation** -- Treaty of 1855; fishing rights throughout the Columbia Basin
- **Nez Perce Tribe** -- Treaty of 1855; Snake River salmon of particular cultural and subsistence significance
- **Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation** -- Treaty of 1855; member tribes include Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla
- **Confederated Tribes of the Warm Springs Reservation** -- Treaty of 1855; member tribes include Wasco, Warm Springs, and Paiute

These four tribes, along with the Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Colville Reservation and the Shoshone-Bannock Tribes, participate in Columbia Basin fish management through government-to-government consultation with federal dam operators (Module F, Section 6.2).

### 4.3 What This Means for the Four-Pillar Architecture

The salmon constraint has three concrete effects on grid integration planning:

**Reduced spring surplus available for electrolysis.** Spring spill operations at federal dams reduce the generation that would otherwise be available for hydrogen production. During peak migration months, spill can reduce generation at individual dams by 20-40% below what unrestricted operations would produce. This does not eliminate the spring surplus -- BPA still curtails wind and occasionally spills beyond biological requirements when transmission is saturated -- but it narrows the window of surplus generation available for productive use.

**Increased value of geothermal baseload.** Salmon constraints reduce the dispatchability of hydroelectric dams by limiting how operators can time generation. Geothermal energy, which generates independently of river conditions, becomes more valuable precisely because it fills the flexibility gap that fish operations create.

**Dam modernization must be fish-compatible.** Turbine upgrades at federal dams must incorporate fish-friendly designs -- minimum gap runners, reduced-speed zones, and optimized flow patterns that improve both generation efficiency and juvenile passage survival. The engineering objectives of more power and better fish passage are not necessarily in conflict, but they add design complexity and cost to modernization programs.

## 5. Transmission and Market Integration

### 5.1 BPA Transmission: The Backbone and Its Limits

BPA operates approximately 15,000 circuit miles of high-voltage transmission, including the 500 kV backbone connecting Columbia River dams to Portland and Seattle, the Pacific DC Intertie (3,100 MW HVDC to Los Angeles), and the Pacific AC Intertie (~4,800 MW to California). Total PNW-to-California transmission capacity is approximately 7,900 MW on the combined interties (Module F, Section 10.2).

**Constraints that affect grid integration:**

**North-south congestion.** The Pacific Interties are frequently congested during peak export periods (spring hydro surplus) and peak import periods (California summer solar exports). Proposed transmission expansions have faced decades of siting, permitting, and cost-allocation challenges (Module F, Section 10.3).

**East-west bottleneck.** The PNW's best wind, solar, and geothermal resources are east of the Cascades. The best geothermal sites in Oregon (Newberry, Basin and Range) are in central and eastern parts of the state, far from Willamette Valley load centers. Cross-Cascades transmission capacity is limited, and reinforcement projects extend into the 2030s (Module F, Section 10.3).

**Curtailment consequences.** When transmission is insufficient to deliver generation to load, generators must curtail. BPA has experienced increasing wind curtailment during high-wind/high-hydro periods when California transmission is congested. This wasted energy is the same surplus that electrolyzers could convert to hydrogen (Module F, Section 10.3).

### 5.2 Market Integration: EIM and EDAM

**Energy Imbalance Market (EIM).** Operated by CAISO since 2014, the EIM provides real-time energy trading across the Western Interconnection in 5-minute and 15-minute intervals. BPA, PacifiCorp, Puget Sound Energy, Portland General Electric, and others participate. Since launch, cumulative benefits have exceeded $5 billion across all participants (Module F, Section 10.4).

EIM benefits the four-pillar architecture by:
- Finding real-time buyers for PNW surplus, reducing curtailment
- Leveraging geographic diversity -- when PNW wind is calm, California solar may be strong, and vice versa
- Providing price signals that indicate where new generation or storage has highest value

**Extended Day-Ahead Market (EDAM).** Building on EIM, CAISO is developing EDAM for day-ahead energy scheduling. BPA and PacifiCorp have signaled interest. EDAM would enable more efficient scheduling of storage dam releases, cross-regional renewable transfers, pumped storage cycles, and geothermal baseload contracts (Module F, Section 10.5).

### 5.3 Western Resource Adequacy Program (WRAP)

The Western Power Pool's WRAP is a binding reliability framework requiring utilities to demonstrate adequate firm capacity to meet peak demand plus reserves. WRAP addresses the PNW-specific concern that over-reliance on hydropower creates resource adequacy risk during drought years (Module F, Section 10.6).

WRAP participation incentivizes utilities to develop the dispatchable resources at the core of the four-pillar model: geothermal, pumped storage, and hydrogen reconversion capacity. Utilities that invest in these resources can count them toward WRAP obligations; utilities that rely solely on variable renewables cannot.

### 5.4 Hydrogen Pipeline as Transmission Alternative

An often-overlooked dimension of hydrogen infrastructure is its potential as an alternative to electrical transmission. Moving energy as hydrogen through pipelines avoids the siting, permitting, and cost-allocation challenges of new high-voltage transmission lines.

A hydrogen pipeline connecting eastern Oregon production sites (co-located with geothermal and wind resources) to Willamette Valley demand centers could serve dual purposes: delivering hydrogen as an energy commodity and providing a parallel energy transport pathway that reduces pressure on east-west electrical transmission. The PNWH2 hub proposal includes pipeline infrastructure leveraging existing natural gas pipeline rights-of-way, though hydrogen embrittlement of steel pipelines requires new materials or internal lining (Module E, E-PNW).

This is not a near-term solution -- pipeline construction requires years of planning, permitting, and construction. But in the 2035-2045 timeframe, hydrogen pipelines could become an integral part of the PNW's energy transport infrastructure, complementing rather than competing with electrical transmission.

## 6. The 2030-2045 Architecture

The four pillars do not arrive simultaneously. They phase in over two decades along a trajectory shaped by technology maturation, construction timelines, policy milestones, and market development.

### 6.1 Phase 1: Foundation (2025-2030)

**Focus:** Heat pump adoption, pilot hydrogen, pumped storage construction, geothermal exploration.

| Resource | 2025-2030 Milestones | Capacity Addition |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Heat pumps | Mass residential adoption accelerates; CTA-2045 grid-interactive units become standard; demand response programs scale to cover 500,000+ units | 1,000-2,000 MW of flexible demand |
| Hydrogen | Pilot electrolyzers at Douglas County PUD and Grant County PUD sites; PNWH2 hub initial infrastructure; first hydrogen storage demonstrations | 50-200 MW electrolyzer capacity |
| Pumped storage | Swan Lake North construction; Goldendale permitting and construction begin | 393 MW (Swan Lake North operational by ~2030) |
| Geothermal | 2-4 additional conventional hydrothermal plants in Oregon Basin and Range; exploration drilling at 3-5 new sites; Washington Geothermal Favorability Model guides development | ~50-100 MW (from current ~24 MW) |

**Grid role of natural gas in this phase:** Gas plants remain essential for winter peak coverage and drought backup. Gas generation declines as a share of total output but is not yet eliminated. Both states are on track for their interim milestones -- Washington's 2030 carbon-neutral target and Oregon's 2030 80% emission reduction target -- but gas remains as a declining backstop.

### 6.2 Phase 2: First Commercial Scale (2030-2035)

**Focus:** First commercial geothermal, hydrogen infrastructure reaches critical mass, pumped storage operational.

| Resource | 2030-2035 Milestones | Capacity Addition |
|----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Heat pumps | Demand response programs mature; aggregated heat pump fleets bid into EIM/EDAM as virtual power plants; building envelope retrofit programs reduce peak loads | 2,000-3,500 MW flexible demand |
| Hydrogen | Commercial-scale electrolyzers operational along Columbia corridor; hydrogen pipeline segments connect production to demand centers; seasonal storage in geological formations | 500-1,000 MW electrolyzer capacity |
| Pumped storage | Goldendale operational; additional PSH projects advance through FERC licensing | 1,200 MW (Goldendale) + potential additional projects |
| Geothermal | Newberry Crater EGS first commercial phase (50-100 MW); 1-2 additional EGS demonstrations; mmWave drilling technology matures | ~200-500 MW cumulative |

**Grid role of natural gas in this phase:** Gas generation falls significantly. Oregon faces the steeper challenge -- its 2035 target is 90% emission reduction. Geothermal baseload and hydrogen reconversion begin to replace gas for drought-year backup. Gas plants may be retained for extreme contingencies but run very few hours per year.

### 6.3 Phase 3: Integrated Dispatch (2035-2040)

**Focus:** Coordinated dispatch system operational across all four pillars.

By this phase, the PNW grid operates with a fundamentally different dispatch logic than today:

- **Geothermal provides firm baseload** independent of weather and hydrology, reducing the minimum gas requirement toward zero
- **Hydroelectric dams optimize for flexibility** rather than baseload, using storage capacity to ramp generation up and down in coordination with wind, solar, and demand response signals
- **Hydrogen electrolyzers absorb surplus** during spring and high-wind periods, producing hydrogen that flows into pipeline networks and storage caverns
- **Hydrogen fuel cells and turbines dispatch** during deficit periods -- winter peaks, drought years, low-wind nights -- reconverting stored hydrogen to electricity
- **Heat pump aggregations participate in markets** via EIM/EDAM, shedding load during peaks and absorbing load during surplus, managed through CTA-2045 and utility coordination
- **Pumped storage provides the hourly balancing** that smooths the transitions between these operating modes

Oregon reaches its 100% clean electricity target in 2040 under HB 2021. By this point, the 38.1% natural gas share that defined Oregon's grid in 2023 must be zero. The four-pillar architecture provides the replacement: geothermal for baseload, hydrogen for seasonal storage and reconversion, pumped storage for daily/weekly balancing, and demand response for peak management.

### 6.4 Phase 4: Full Architecture (2040-2045)

**Focus:** Washington achieves 100% clean electricity under CETA (2045); system optimization continues.

| Resource | 2040-2045 Status | Capacity |
|----------|-----------------|----------|
| Heat pumps | Standard equipment in all new construction; retrofit programs have reached majority of existing housing stock; demand response fully integrated into grid operations | 4,000-6,000 MW flexible demand |
| Hydrogen | Mature production and storage infrastructure; seasonal buffer sized for worst-case drought + cold-snap scenarios; potential hydrogen exports to California | 1,000-2,000+ MW electrolyzer capacity |
| Pumped storage | Multiple facilities operational; integrated with hydro dispatch optimization | 2,000-3,000+ MW |
| Geothermal | EGS technology mature; multiple Cascade Range and Basin and Range projects; geothermal provides 5-15% of PNW generation | ~1,000-3,000 MW |

*Source: Geothermal development scenarios from Module F, Section 11.5.*

**What "100% clean" means operationally:** Both states will have achieved their statutory mandates. The grid runs on hydroelectric dispatch, geothermal baseload, wind, solar, pumped storage, and hydrogen reconversion. No fossil fuel generation operates within state boundaries for retail electricity supply. In drought years, the seasonal hydrogen buffer and geothermal baseload cover the hydroelectric deficit. In surplus years, excess energy flows to hydrogen production for storage and potential export.

## 7. Key Findings

### Finding 1: The four pillars form a time-hierarchy of grid services

Each pillar operates at a different timescale, and together they cover the full range from seconds to seasons:

| Timescale | Grid Service | Primary Resource |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Seconds to minutes | Frequency regulation | Heat pump demand response; pumped storage fast ramp |
| Minutes to hours | Load following; peak shaving | Pumped storage dispatch; heat pump pre-heating/shedding |
| Hours to days | Daily energy shifting | Pumped storage; hydroelectric storage dam dispatch |
| Days to weeks | Weather-event buffer | Hydrogen reconversion; hydroelectric reservoir management |
| Weeks to seasons | Drought/surplus management | Hydrogen seasonal storage; geothermal baseload |
| Year-round continuous | Baseload reliability | Geothermal (90-95% capacity factor) |

No single resource covers all timescales. Batteries (not a named pillar but present in the architecture) fill the seconds-to-hours range with higher efficiency than hydrogen. The four pillars together provide the full spectrum without fossil fuel.

### Finding 2: Geothermal fills the specific gap that climate variability creates

The PNNL 2024 study projects amplified year-to-year variability in PNW hydroelectric generation, even if long-term averages remain stable or slightly increase (PNNL-HYDRO). This variability is the direct threat to grid reliability under 100% clean mandates. Geothermal is the only renewable resource that is both dispatchable and immune to precipitation, wind, and solar variability. Its 90-95% capacity factor directly replaces the drought-insurance function currently served by natural gas.

Connection: Module F (F-CLI climate variability) identifies the problem; Module F (F-GEO geothermal resources) and Module F (F-EGS Enhanced Geothermal Systems) identify the solution; this synthesis maps how geothermal plugs into the broader architecture.

### Finding 3: Hydrogen converts the spring surplus problem into a winter solution

The PNW's spring surplus -- when hydro and wind generation exceed demand and transmission export capacity -- is currently managed through curtailment (wasted wind) and spill (wasted water). Hydrogen electrolysis converts this surplus into a storable commodity. The seasonal production profile (Module E, E-PNW) aligns hydrogen production with the surplus season and hydrogen consumption with the deficit season.

Connection: Module E (E-PNW surplus power analysis) quantifies the opportunity; Module F (F-PUMP pumped storage) provides the medium-duration complement; Module A (A-GRID heat pump demand response) provides the demand-side complement that reduces the deficit hydrogen must cover.

### Finding 4: Heat pump demand response reduces the peak capacity problem that otherwise makes 100% clean electricity more expensive

Electrifying heating with heat pumps adds 3,000-5,000 MW of winter peak demand to the PNW grid (Module A, Section 10.4). Without demand response, this peak must be met by building new dispatchable generation -- the very gas capacity the clean mandates are eliminating. With demand response, heat pump fleets shift load to off-peak hours, reducing the net new peak capacity requirement.

The building envelope is the multiplier: a well-insulated home with a CTA-2045-equipped heat pump adds 3-5 kW of peak demand and can shed that load for 1-4 hours during grid events. A poorly insulated home adds 8-15 kW and cannot coast as long (Module A, Sections 8.2, 9.1). Envelope retrofit programs are grid infrastructure investments.

Connection: Module A (heat pump grid interaction and building envelope coupling) provides the technical basis; Module F (F-GRID grid context) provides the system-level capacity numbers; this synthesis shows how demand response scales the 100% clean target from engineering challenge to achievable engineering plan.

### Finding 5: Salmon constraints reduce but do not eliminate the hydrogen opportunity from spring surplus

Spring spill operations for juvenile salmon migration reduce generation at federal dams during precisely the season when surplus is highest. This narrows the window of surplus energy available for electrolysis but does not close it -- BPA still curtails wind and spills beyond biological requirements when transmission is saturated. The net effect is that hydrogen production from spring surplus must be sized to the post-spill surplus, not the unrestricted generation potential.

Connection: Module F (F-SAL salmon considerations) documents the biological and legal framework; Module E (E-PNW seasonal production profile) quantifies the surplus available for electrolysis; this synthesis identifies the interaction between the two.

### Finding 6: Transmission constraints make hydrogen pipelines a potential alternative energy transport pathway

The PNW's best renewable resources (wind, solar, geothermal) are east of the Cascades. The load centers (Portland, Seattle) are west. Cross-Cascades electrical transmission is limited and expansion faces long permitting timelines. Hydrogen produced east of the Cascades and transported by pipeline to western demand centers provides an alternative energy transport mechanism that avoids the electrical transmission bottleneck.

Connection: Module F (F-GRID transmission constraints) documents the bottleneck; Module E (E-PNW hydrogen hub infrastructure) describes the pipeline concept; this synthesis maps the integration.

### Finding 7: The 2040-2045 architecture is achievable but depends on parallel progress across all four pillars

No single pillar achieves 100% clean electricity alone. Geothermal without hydrogen cannot manage seasonal storage. Hydrogen without geothermal cannot provide baseload. Heat pump demand response without supply-side resources cannot generate electricity. Pumped storage without variable resources has nothing to time-shift. The architecture works as a system or not at all.

The critical path items are:
- Geothermal: EGS must move from demonstration (Fervo Cape Station, 3.5 MW) to commercial scale (50-100+ MW) at Newberry and other PNW sites by 2030-2035
- Hydrogen: Electrolyzer and storage infrastructure must reach critical mass by 2030-2035 to provide seasonal buffer capacity by the mandate deadlines
- Pumped storage: Goldendale (1,200 MW) must be constructed and operational by the early 2030s
- Heat pumps: CTA-2045 demand response must reach hundreds of thousands of units by 2030

Delay in any one pillar increases the burden on the others or forces continued gas reliance past mandate deadlines.
