Accidents are not caused by component failures. They emerge from inadequate control of system-level interactions. This paradigm shift — from linear causality chains to systems-theoretic control structures — is the single most important development in root cause analysis since the Five Whys.

The Evolution from Linear to Systemic Causality

Classical RCA methods (Five Whys, FTA, FMEA) assume that accidents result from component failures propagating through chains. This works for simple, tightly coupled systems. But modern systems — software-intensive, sociotechnical, with complex feedback loops — exhibit emergent failures that no single component “caused.”

The systems-theoretic revolution, led by Nancy Leveson at MIT, reframes accidents as control problems: the system’s safety constraints were violated because the control structure failed to enforce them. This shift has profound implications for how we investigate and prevent failures.

STAMP — Systems-Theoretic Accident Model and Processes

Developed by Nancy Leveson at MIT, STAMP models safety as a control problem rather than a reliability problem. Key concepts:

  • Safety constraints: System-level requirements that must be enforced at every level
  • Hierarchical control structure: Controllers (human and automated) enforcing constraints on controlled processes
  • Process models: Each controller’s mental/software model of the controlled process — accidents occur when process models become inconsistent with reality
  • Control actions: Commands from controllers that can be unsafe (wrong, too late, out of sequence, stopped too soon)
  • Feedback: Information from the controlled process back to the controller

STAMP provides the theoretical foundation for two analysis techniques: STPA (proactive) and CAST (retrospective).

STPA — Systems-Theoretic Process Analysis

STPA is the proactive hazard analysis technique derived from STAMP. Unlike FMEA (which enumerates component failures) or FTA (which decomposes from a top event), STPA identifies unsafe control actions and the causal scenarios that lead to them.

STPA four-step process:

  1. Define purpose: Identify system-level losses, hazards, and safety constraints
  2. Model the control structure: Map all controllers, control actions, and feedback channels
  3. Identify unsafe control actions: For each control action, determine how it can be unsafe (provided when not needed, not provided when needed, wrong timing, wrong duration)
  4. Identify loss scenarios: Determine why each unsafe control action could occur (controller failures, inadequate feedback, conflicting control, process model flaws)

STPA has been adopted by the FAA, NASA, the automotive industry (ISO 21448 SOTIF), and increasingly in software safety analysis.

CAST — Causal Analysis using Systems Theory

CAST is the retrospective accident investigation counterpart to STPA. Given an accident that has occurred, CAST works backward through the control structure to identify:

  • Which safety constraints were violated
  • Which control actions were unsafe and why
  • How each controller’s process model became incorrect
  • What interactions between controllers contributed to the failure
  • How the overall safety control structure degraded over time

Unlike traditional investigation methods that terminate at a “root cause,” CAST traces the systemic conditions that made the accident possible — organizational, managerial, and technical.

Swiss Cheese Model — Reason’s Framework Revisited

While introduced in the Classical Methods module as barrier analysis, James Reason’s Swiss Cheese Model (1990) also embodies systems thinking. The key insight is the distinction between:

  • Active failures: Errors and violations at the sharp end (operators, front-line staff)
  • Latent conditions: Organizational decisions, management trade-offs, and design flaws that create the holes in the cheese slices long before an accident occurs

Reason’s model shifted investigation from blaming the last person who touched the system to examining the organizational conditions that made the error inevitable.

Safety-III — Beyond Linear Causality

The evolution of safety paradigms:

ParadigmCore QuestionView of Failure
Safety-IWhat went wrong?Failure is the absence of success; eliminate failures
Safety-II (Hollnagel)What goes right?Success and failure have the same sources; learn from both
Safety-IIIHow does the system adapt?Safety emerges from system-level adaptation; focus on resilience, not compliance

Safety-III moves beyond even systems-theoretic models to embrace complexity science: systems are non-linear, adaptive, and irreducible to component interactions. RCA in this paradigm is less about finding “the cause” and more about understanding the system’s adaptive capacity and how it was overwhelmed.

Safety-III Critique (Leveson, 2020)

Leveson argues that Hollnagel’s “Safety-I” does not resemble actual safety engineering practice of the past 70 years. System Safety already incorporates most of Safety-II’s proposals. Safety-II focuses too narrowly on human operator adaptability; system-level redesign creates durable safety. Safety-III = a systems approach subsuming both within a control-theoretic framework.
