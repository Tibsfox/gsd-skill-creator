The classical methods form the bedrock of all root cause analysis. Every modern framework — from STAMP to AgentRx — can trace its intellectual lineage back to these five foundational techniques developed between the 1930s and the 1960s.

Five Whys

Developed by Sakichi Toyoda in the 1930s and formalized within the Toyota Production System (TPS), the Five Whys technique is iterative interrogation at its simplest. Starting from the observable problem, the investigator asks “why?” repeatedly (typically five times, though the number is not fixed) until the root cause is exposed.

Example chain:

1. Why did the machine stop? — The fuse blew due to overload.

2. Why was there an overload? — The bearing was not lubricated.

3. Why was the bearing not lubricated? — The lubrication pump was not working.

4. Why was the pump not working? — The shaft was worn out.

5. Why was the shaft worn out? — There was no filtration on the intake, allowing metal particles in.

Root cause: Missing intake filter. Corrective action: Install filter on lubrication pump intake.

Strengths: Simple, requires no tools, accessible to any team member. Weaknesses: Can converge on a single causal chain when multiple root causes exist; susceptible to investigator bias; lacks structural rigor for complex systems.

Ishikawa Fishbone Diagram

Created by Kaoru Ishikawa at the University of Tokyo in the 1960s, the fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagram provides structured brainstorming for potential causes organized into categories. The standard 6M framework classifies causes into:

CategoryScopeExample Causes
Man (People)Human factors, training, fatigueOperator error, insufficient training, communication failure
MachineEquipment, tools, technologyWear, miscalibration, software bug
MaterialRaw materials, consumables, dataContaminated input, wrong specification, corrupt data
MethodProcedures, processes, workflowsMissing step, wrong sequence, outdated SOP
MeasurementMetrics, instrumentation, inspectionUncalibrated gauge, wrong metric, sampling bias
Mother NatureEnvironment, external conditionsTemperature, humidity, seismic event, regulatory change

The diagram’s visual structure prevents tunnel vision by forcing consideration of all six categories before converging on a root cause.

Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)

Developed at Bell Labs in 1961 for the Minuteman missile program, FTA is a top-down, deductive analysis technique. Starting from an undesired top event, the analyst constructs a Boolean logic tree using AND gates (all inputs must occur) and OR gates (any input suffices) to decompose the failure into basic events.

Key concepts:

  • Top event: The undesired system-level failure being investigated
  • Intermediate events: Failures that contribute to the top event through logical gates
  • Basic events: Leaf nodes — the initiating failures with known probability
  • Minimal cut sets: The smallest combination of basic events that causes the top event
  • Quantitative FTA: Assigns probabilities to basic events, calculates top event probability

FTA remains the standard in aerospace, nuclear, and defense for safety-critical system analysis. Its mathematical foundation connects directly to Boolean algebra and probability theory (see Mathematical Foundations).

Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA)

Originating in US military procedure MIL-P-1629 (1949) and adopted widely after the Apollo program, FMEA systematically catalogs every potential failure mode for each component, its effects, and a risk score.

RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection

Each factor is rated 1–10. The Risk Priority Number (RPN) ranges from 1 to 1,000. High RPNs drive corrective action priority. FMEA variants include Design FMEA (DFMEA), Process FMEA (PFMEA), and Software FMEA.

Limitation: RPN conflates severity and likelihood — an RPN of 100 could be 10×10×1 (catastrophic but detectable) or 2×5×10 (minor and undetectable). Modern approaches supplement RPN with action priority tables.

Barrier Analysis & Swiss Cheese Model

Based on the energy transfer model (Haddon, 1973), barrier analysis asks: what barriers existed between the hazard and the target, and which barriers failed? James Reason’s Swiss Cheese Model (1990) visualizes defenses as slices of cheese — each slice has holes (latent conditions), and an accident occurs when holes in successive slices align, allowing hazard energy to pass through all barriers.

Barrier types: Physical (guards, containment), Procedural (checklists, SOPs), Administrative (training, supervision), Active (alarms, shutoffs), Passive (structural design, redundancy)

Key insight: Accidents are rarely caused by a single barrier failure. They require the alignment of multiple latent conditions across organizational layers — from management decisions to front-line actions.
