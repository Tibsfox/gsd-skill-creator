Healthcare and aviation are the two domains where root cause analysis carries the highest stakes — failures cost lives. Both have developed mature investigation cultures, mandatory reporting systems, and regulatory frameworks that the software industry is only beginning to approach. Their decades of experience provide essential lessons for any RCA practice.

Joint Commission Sentinel Events

The Joint Commission (formerly JCAHO) requires accredited healthcare organizations to perform RCA for all sentinel events — unexpected occurrences involving death or serious physical or psychological injury. The Joint Commission’s RCA framework mandates:

  • Team composition: Multidisciplinary team including front-line staff, not just managers
  • Timeline reconstruction: Detailed chronology of events leading to the sentinel event
  • Causal analysis: Identify contributing factors at the individual, team, equipment, environment, and organizational levels
  • Root cause identification: Drill down to systemic factors, not individual blame
  • Action plan: Specific, measurable corrective actions with responsible parties and timelines
  • Monitoring: Follow-up to verify effectiveness of corrective actions

Common sentinel event types: wrong-site surgery, medication errors, patient falls, treatment delays, and hospital-acquired infections. The Joint Commission publishes anonymized data on sentinel event patterns to enable system-wide learning.

NASA Aviation Safety Reporting System (ASRS)

Established in 1976, ASRS is a voluntary, confidential reporting system for aviation safety incidents. Administered by NASA (not the FAA) to maintain independence from the regulatory body, ASRS receives approximately 100,000 reports annually.

Key design principles that make ASRS effective:

  • Confidentiality: Reporter identity is protected; reports cannot be used for enforcement
  • Immunity incentive: Good-faith reporting provides limited immunity from FAA enforcement action
  • De-identification: All published data is stripped of identifying information
  • Analysis and publication: NASA analysts identify trends and publish ASRS Callback newsletters
  • Alert bulletins: Urgent safety issues trigger immediate notifications to FAA and industry

ASRS demonstrates that voluntary reporting with confidentiality protections captures far more safety data than mandatory punitive systems. This principle directly informs Google’s blameless postmortem culture (see SRE).

NTSB Investigation Methodology

The National Transportation Safety Board conducts independent investigations of aviation accidents (and major surface transportation accidents). The NTSB methodology is the gold standard for accident investigation:

PhaseActivitiesDuration
Field InvestigationGo-team deployment, wreckage mapping, CVR/FDR recovery, witness interviews, perishable evidenceDays–weeks
Factual InvestigationSystems group, structures group, operations group, human performance group — each produces factual reportsMonths
AnalysisIntegrate factual group findings, apply causal frameworks, identify probable cause and contributing factorsMonths
Board MeetingPublic meeting where board members deliberate, amend analysis, vote on probable cause determination12–24 months after accident
RecommendationsSafety recommendations to FAA, manufacturers, operators — tracked for implementationOngoing

The NTSB deliberately uses “probable cause” rather than “root cause” — acknowledging that complex accidents have multiple contributing factors and that certainty about the single “root” cause is often impossible.

STAMP vs Traditional RCA in Healthcare

Research comparing STAMP-based analysis to traditional RCA methods in healthcare settings reveals significant differences:

The evidence strongly favors systems-theoretic approaches for complex healthcare incidents, though traditional methods remain appropriate for simpler failure modes.

Human Factors Engineering & Cognitive Bias in RCA

Both healthcare and aviation have learned that human error is rarely the root cause — it is the starting point for investigation. Cognitive biases that affect RCA practitioners themselves:

  • Hindsight bias: Knowing the outcome makes the failure seem more predictable than it was
  • Outcome bias: Judging decisions by their outcomes rather than the information available at the time
  • Fundamental attribution error: Attributing failures to individuals rather than situational factors
  • Counterfactual thinking: “If only they had...” — creating a narrative of a single decision point that “caused” the accident

High-Reliability Organizations (HRO)

Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe identified five principles that characterize organizations operating successfully in high-hazard environments:

  1. Preoccupation with failure: Treating near-misses as windows into the system’s vulnerabilities
  2. Reluctance to simplify: Resisting easy explanations and single root causes
  3. Sensitivity to operations: Maintaining awareness of current system state at all levels
  4. Commitment to resilience: Building capacity to recover from unexpected situations, not just prevent anticipated ones
  5. Deference to expertise: Decision-making migrates to whoever has the most relevant knowledge, regardless of hierarchy

HRO principles provide the cultural foundation for effective RCA — without these organizational characteristics, even the best analytical methods will be undermined by blame culture, oversimplification, and authority bias.
