## 1. Overview

California Historical Stations covers a critical dimension of West Coast AM broadcasting history, regulation, and future trajectory. This module examines the technical foundations, institutional structures, and practical pathways that define AM radio's role in the Pacific Northwest and California media landscape.

AM broadcasting on the West Coast began with experimental transmissions in the early 1920s and evolved through network affiliation, format wars, consolidation, and digital transition to its current state in 2026.

---

## 2. Historical Context

The historical arc of this domain spans over a century:

- **1920s** -- Experimental licenses, first commercial stations, Federal Radio Commission
- **1930s-40s** -- Golden Age of network radio, NBC/CBS/Mutual affiliations
- **1950s-60s** -- Television's impact, Top 40 format revolution, demographic targeting
- **1970s-80s** -- News-talk pivot, AM stereo experiments, FM ascendance
- **1990s-present** -- Telecommunications Act consolidation, ownership concentration, digital transition

---

## 3. Technical Details

The technical substrate of AM broadcasting includes:

- **Frequency range** -- 535-1705 kHz (standard broadcast band)
- **Modulation** -- amplitude modulation of carrier wave
- **Propagation** -- ground wave (daytime, reliable) and sky wave (nighttime, via ionospheric refraction)
- **Station classes** -- Class A (clear channel, 50 kW), Class B (regional, up to 50 kW), Class C (local, up to 1 kW), Class D (daytimer)
- **Channel bandwidth** -- 10 kHz spacing, 20 kHz occupied bandwidth

## 5. Current State (2026)

The current state of AM broadcasting reflects both decline and adaptation:

- AM listening share has fallen but remains significant in specific demographics
- AM Revitalization proceeding (FCC Docket 13-249) has enabled FM translator access
- HD Radio (IBOC) deployed on approximately 2,000 AM stations nationwide
- FCC approved all-digital AM in MA3 mode (FCC 20-154, 2020)
- 95 million vehicles equipped with HD Radio receivers
