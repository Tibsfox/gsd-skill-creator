Before the internet, the radio dial was the only place where a single voice could reach the entire Pacific Northwest at once — in the dark, in the mountains, across the water. The frequency was free. The license was granted by the public. The obligation was to serve. That architecture still exists.

Research Modules

M1: AM Band Foundations

AM Foundation · Physics & regulation

Physics of amplitude modulation. AM band 535-1705 kHz. Federal Radio Commission (1927), FCC succession. Radio Act 1927, Communications Act 1934. NARBA treaty. Station classes A through D.

M2: Pacific Northwest Stations

PNW Track Alpha · WA/OR/ID history

Station chronicles: KJR (1921), KGW Portland (1922), KOMO, KIRO, KVI. Golden Age network radio, Top 40, news-talk pivot. Telecom Act 1996 consolidation. Regional stations in Tacoma, Spokane, Eugene.

M3: California Stations

CAL Track Alpha · Bay Area & LA

Bay Area: KGO/KSFO, KNBR, KCBS (1909 origins). Los Angeles: KFI, KNX, KABC, KMPC. Don Lee Network, NBC Pacific Coast. 34% minority-serving California AM stations.

M4: Spectrum & Licensing

Spectrum Track Beta · Practical guide

Licensing pathways: full-power AM, full-power FM, LPFM (100W nonprofit), FM translator, Part 15 unlicensed. Spectrum scarcity in Seattle, Portland, SF, LA markets. Entry guide for 2026.

M5: FCC Rules & Regulations

FCC Track Beta · 47 CFR Part 73

Public interest obligations, 8-year license term, EEO requirements, political broadcasting (equal time, lowest unit rate), EAS participation, indecency restrictions, ownership caps.

M6: The Digital Future

Future Synthesis · 2026 and beyond

HD Radio (IBOC hybrid digital). FCC all-digital AM approval (MA3 mode, 2020). AM Revitalization proceeding. EV radio interference controversy. Streaming competition (36% audio share by 2023).
