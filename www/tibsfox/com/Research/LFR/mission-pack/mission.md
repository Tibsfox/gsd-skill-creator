## 1. Old-Growth Forest Ecology

Old-growth forest in the Pacific Northwest is not defined by age alone. A 300-year-old tree plantation is not old-growth. A 200-year-old forest with snags, nurse logs, canopy gaps, and seven structural layers is. The distinction matters because old-growth is an ecosystem state -- a description of complexity, not a description of years since the last disturbance [1][2].

The formal ecological definition requires multiple criteria operating simultaneously: trees exceeding 200 years in age (often 500-1,000 years for Douglas fir and western red cedar); a multi-layered canopy with emergent, dominant, co-dominant, and suppressed crown classes; standing dead trees (snags) at various stages of decay providing nesting habitat for woodpeckers, owls, and dozens of other cavity-dependent species; large fallen logs (nurse logs) serving as germination substrate for hemlock and spruce seedlings; canopy gaps created by windthrow or disease that allow light penetration and drive successional dynamics; and a deep, structurally complex forest floor with moss, lichen, and fungal mats [1][3].

The PNW old-growth forest is dominated by four conifer species often called "the big four": Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*), Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), and western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*). Douglas fir is the defining species of the region -- the tallest native conifer in North America, reaching heights of 75-100 meters (250-330 feet) and diameters of 1.5-2 meters (5-6.5 feet). Individual trees commonly exceed 500 years of age, with documented specimens reaching 1,200 years [1][4].

Western red cedar -- the "tree of life" to many Coast Salish peoples -- can reach 1,500 years of age and is the most culturally significant tree in the PNW. Its decay-resistant heartwood provided the material culture of Northwest Coast peoples: longhouses, canoes, bentwood boxes, ceremonial masks, and clothing [5][6]. Sitka spruce dominates the coastal fog zone, reaching enormous size in the Olympic Peninsula and Oregon Coast Range. Western hemlock, despite being shade-tolerant and often subordinate in the canopy, is the climax species of many PNW forest types -- the tree that would eventually dominate if all disturbance ceased [1][7].

How much old-growth remains is a question with political weight. Before European settlement, approximately 15-19 million acres of old-growth forest covered the PNW (western Washington and Oregon). By the late 20th century, an estimated 10-15% remained on federal lands, and far less on private land. The Northwest Forest Plan of 1994 -- enacted after the spotted owl crisis -- protected approximately 7.4 million acres of federal forest, including most remaining old-growth, from commercial harvest. On private timberlands, virtually all old-growth was logged during the 19th and 20th centuries [8][9].

The ecological importance of old-growth extends far beyond the trees themselves. Old-growth forests store more carbon per hectare than any other terrestrial ecosystem. They regulate stream temperature for salmon by providing canopy shade over spawning streams. They produce the large woody debris (fallen trees in streams) that creates the pool-riffle habitat structure salmon require. They support species that exist nowhere else -- the northern spotted owl, the marbled murrelet, the red tree vole, the Oregon slender salamander. They are, in the most literal sense, irreplaceable: a logged old-growth forest cannot be regrown in any human lifetime. The structural complexity that defines old-growth requires centuries of undisturbed succession [1][3][10].

## 2. The Mother Tree / Mycorrhizal Networks

In 1997, a young forest ecologist named Suzanne Simard published a paper in *Nature* that changed how the world understood forests. Working in the mixed forests of British Columbia, Simard used radioactive carbon isotopes (carbon-14 and carbon-13) to demonstrate that Douglas fir and paper birch were exchanging carbon through underground fungal networks. The carbon was not moving randomly -- it was flowing preferentially from trees in full sunlight to trees growing in shade. The forest was not a collection of individuals competing for resources. It was a cooperative network sharing them [11][12].

The mechanism is mycorrhizal association -- the symbiotic partnership between tree roots and soil fungi that has existed for at least 400 million years. In PNW forests, the dominant form is ectomycorrhizal fungi (EMF): species of *Rhizopogon*, *Suillus*, *Laccaria*, *Cortinarius*, and hundreds of others that form dense sheaths around tree root tips and extend hyphal networks outward into the soil. A single tree can be colonized by dozens of fungal species simultaneously, and a single fungal individual can connect multiple trees of different species. This network -- the common mycorrhizal network (CMN) -- creates a physical pathway through which carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, water, and chemical defense signals can flow between trees [11][13][14].

Simard's subsequent research at the University of British Columbia identified what she called "mother trees" or "hub trees" -- the largest, oldest trees in a forest that serve as the most-connected nodes in the mycorrhizal network. A single mother tree can be connected to hundreds of neighboring trees through the CMN. Simard's work showed that mother trees provision their offspring: when seedlings germinate near a mother tree, carbon transfer through the mycorrhizal network to those seedlings increases significantly. Mother trees appear to recognize their own kin -- genetically related seedlings receive more carbon than unrelated seedlings of the same species [12][15].

The "Wood Wide Web" metaphor, coined by *Nature* in 1997, captured public imagination and became one of the most widely discussed concepts in modern ecology. But the metaphor has also drawn scrutiny. In 2023, a meta-analysis by Justine Karst and colleagues at the University of Alberta, published in *New Phytologist*, challenged several of Simard's broader claims. The Karst review found that while mycorrhizal networks clearly exist and facilitate some resource transfer, the evidence for ecologically significant carbon transfer between adult trees was weaker than often presented. The quantities of carbon transferred may be too small to meaningfully affect recipient tree growth. The review questioned whether the network truly functions as a cooperative system or whether the fungi are simply pursuing their own metabolic interests [16][17].

Simard responded by noting that the meta-analysis conflated studies across very different forest types and methodologies, and that her isotope tracing work specifically demonstrated net carbon flow from resource-rich to resource-poor trees in amounts sufficient to affect seedling survival. The scientific debate continues, and it is exactly the kind of productive disagreement that advances understanding [12][18].

What is not in dispute: ectomycorrhizal fungi are essential to PNW forest function. Trees deprived of their mycorrhizal partners grow poorly or die. The fungal network facilitates nutrient uptake, particularly of phosphorus and nitrogen. It provides drought resistance by extending the effective root zone. It may transmit chemical signals that trigger defense responses in neighboring trees when one tree is attacked by insects. The network is real. The debate is about the magnitude and ecological significance of the transfers, not their existence [13][14].

Western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) holds a special place in this system. As the longest-lived species in PNW old-growth forests (specimens exceeding 1,500 years), cedars function as the deepest anchor points in the mycorrhizal network. Grandmother Cedar is not a metaphor -- she is a functional description of the oldest, most connected nodes in the forest's underground economy. She has been provisioning seedlings through the common mycorrhizal network for centuries before any living human was born [5][6][12].

## 3. Terrain Genesis

The Pacific Northwest landscape is the product of three geological forces operating on vastly different timescales: tectonic collision (millions of years), volcanic eruption (thousands of years), and catastrophic flooding (days). Understanding the terrain is essential to understanding the forest, because geology determines soil, soil determines hydrology, and hydrology determines what grows where [19][20].

**The Cascadia Subduction Zone.** The Juan de Fuca plate is sliding beneath the North American plate at approximately 40 millimeters per year along a 1,000-kilometer boundary from Cape Mendocino, California to Vancouver Island, British Columbia. This subduction zone built the Cascade Range -- a volcanic arc that runs from Mount Lassen in California to Mount Garibaldi in British Columbia. The Cascades are not ancient mountains; they are geologically young, with most major peaks less than 2 million years old. Mount St. Helens is approximately 40,000 years old. The subduction zone also generates the most powerful earthquakes in North America: magnitude 9.0 events that occur roughly every 200-600 years. The last full-rupture Cascadia earthquake occurred on January 26, 1700, generating a tsunami that reached Japan [19][21].

**The Volcanic Arc.** The Cascade volcanoes define the skyline of the PNW: Tahoma/Mount Rainier (4,392 m), Mount St. Helens (2,549 m after the 1980 eruption; formerly 2,950 m), Mount Hood (3,429 m), Mount Baker (3,286 m), Glacier Peak (3,213 m), Mount Adams (3,742 m). The 1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens was the most significant volcanic event in the contiguous United States since Mount Lassen in 1915 -- it removed 400 meters from the summit, generated a lateral blast that destroyed 230 square miles of forest (including 150 square miles of Weyerhaeuser timberlands), killed 57 people, and deposited ash across 11 states. The blast zone has since become one of the most intensively studied ecological recovery sites in the world [20][22].

Volcanic soils (andisols) are among the most fertile on earth, and they underlie many PNW forests. The Jory soils of the Willamette Valley -- red, iron-rich, derived from Columbia River Basalt weathering -- support the world-class Pinot Noir vineyards of Oregon. The volcanic ash soils of the eastern Cascades support the ponderosa pine forests and Douglas fir stands that define the dry side of the range [19][23].

**Glaciation.** During the last ice age (Pleistocene, peaking approximately 20,000 years ago), the Cordilleran Ice Sheet covered most of British Columbia and extended south into Washington. The Puget Lobe of the ice sheet advanced as far south as Olympia, carving the Puget Sound basin, depositing glacial till across the lowlands, and creating the island-dotted inland sea that defines the region's geography. Glacial valleys in the Cascades and Olympics -- U-shaped, steep-walled, with cirque lakes at their heads -- were carved by alpine glaciers that persisted until the early Holocene. Many Cascade peaks still carry active glaciers, though all are in rapid retreat [19][24].

**The Missoula Floods.** Between approximately 18,000 and 13,000 years ago, an ice dam at the mouth of glacial Lake Missoula in Montana repeatedly formed, impounded water, and catastrophically failed. Each failure released approximately 500 cubic miles of water -- a volume roughly equal to Lake Erie and Lake Ontario combined -- across eastern Washington, through the Columbia River Gorge, and into the Willamette Valley. These floods, occurring 40-80 times over 2,000-3,000 years, carved the Channeled Scablands of eastern Washington, deposited the deep alluvial soils of the Columbia Basin and Willamette Valley, and created the geological foundation for the agricultural empire that now feeds the region. The floods are among the largest documented flood events in Earth's history [25][26].

The terrain these forces created determines the forest. Basalt produces thin, rocky soils that support drought-adapted species (ponderosa pine, Oregon white oak). Glacial till produces deep, well-drained soils that support Douglas fir. Alluvial deposits produce rich bottomland soils that support western red cedar, Sitka spruce, and black cottonwood. The geology is the first author of the ecology [19][20].

## 4. Forest Canopy Ecology

A PNW old-growth forest is not one ecosystem -- it is at least eight, stacked vertically from the forest floor to the emergent crown. Each layer receives a different amount of light, experiences different temperature and humidity, and supports a different community of organisms. The canopy is not the top of the forest; it is a separate world suspended in the air [27][28].

**Emergent layer (>60 m / 200 ft).** The tallest Douglas firs and Sitka spruces that break through the general canopy surface. These trees are fully exposed to wind, direct sunlight, and temperature extremes. They serve as perch sites for bald eagles and peregrine falcons, and their structural prominence makes them lightning targets -- a source of ignition that links canopy ecology to fire ecology [27].

**Upper canopy (40-60 m / 130-200 ft).** The continuous crown layer that intercepts 80-95% of incoming sunlight. This is the primary photosynthetic zone of the forest and the habitat of the marbled murrelet, a seabird that nests exclusively on the large, moss-covered limbs of old-growth conifers, sometimes flying 50+ miles inland from the ocean to reach nesting sites. The upper canopy also supports arboreal lichens -- particularly the genus *Lobaria* -- that fix atmospheric nitrogen and contribute it to the forest nutrient cycle when they fall [27][28][29].

**Lower canopy (25-40 m / 80-130 ft).** The domain of shade-tolerant species: western hemlock, Pacific silver fir, Pacific yew. This layer receives 5-20% of full sunlight and is characterized by lower wind speed, higher humidity, and more stable temperatures than the upper canopy. Pacific yew (*Taxus brevifolia*) gained international attention in the 1990s when the anti-cancer compound taxol (paclitaxel) was isolated from its bark [27][30].

**Understory (10-25 m / 30-80 ft).** Dominated by shade-tolerant hardwoods -- vine maple (*Acer circinatum*), bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) -- and regenerating conifers. Bigleaf maples in the PNW carry extraordinary epiphyte loads: a single tree may support 35 kg (77 lb) or more of mosses, liverworts, lichens, and ferns growing on its branches. These epiphyte mats create canopy soils -- suspended soil ecosystems in the tree crowns that support their own invertebrate communities [27][28].

**Shrub layer (2-10 m / 6-30 ft).** Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*), huckleberry (*Vaccinium* spp.), devil's club (*Oplopanax horridus*). This layer provides browse for deer and elk, berry production for bears and birds, and the structural complexity that shelters small mammals. Devil's club, with its toxic spines and medicinal properties, is a culturally significant plant for many PNW Indigenous nations [27][31].

**Herb layer (0-2 m / 0-6 ft).** Sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*), oxalis (*Oxalis oregana*), bunchberry (*Cornus canadensis*), vanilla leaf (*Achlys triphylla*). In deep old-growth, the herb layer receives less than 2% of full sunlight. These species have evolved extreme shade tolerance, with some capable of maintaining positive carbon balance at light levels that would kill most plants [27].

**Ground layer.** Mosses, liverworts, and lichens forming continuous mats on the forest floor. Nurse logs -- fallen trees in advanced decay -- serve as elevated germination platforms for hemlock and spruce seedlings, which often grow in perfectly straight lines along the length of a decomposing log. A single nurse log can persist as a recognizable structure for 200-400 years [1][27].

**Subterranean layer.** The mycorrhizal network. Fungal hyphae penetrate the first 30 cm of soil at densities of up to 200 meters of hyphal length per gram of soil. This layer connects the roots of all canopy layers into a single, continuous network. The subterranean layer is the foundation of everything above it [13][14].

Canopy science in the PNW was pioneered by researchers like Nalini Nadkarni, who began climbing old-growth trees in the 1980s and documented the extraordinary epiphyte communities of the Olympic rainforest. Her work revealed that old-growth canopies are not simply the tops of trees -- they are distinct habitats with species found nowhere else, microhabitats that require centuries of structural development to form, and nutrient cycling pathways that operate independently of the forest floor [28].

## 5. Fire Ecology

The PNW forest evolved with fire. Fire is not a catastrophe that befalls the forest -- it is a process the forest requires. The question is not whether fire will come, but what kind of fire, how often, and what the forest does afterward [32][33].

**Fire regimes.** PNW forests operate under two fundamentally different fire regimes, distributed roughly by precipitation and elevation. East of the Cascade crest and at lower elevations, fire historically burned at low severity every 10-35 years, clearing understory brush and small trees while leaving large, thick-barked Douglas firs and ponderosa pines standing. These are the fire-adapted forests -- open, parklike, with widely spaced large trees and a grassy or shrubby understory maintained by frequent burning. West of the Cascade crest, in the wet maritime forests, fire is rarer but more catastrophic: high-severity, stand-replacing events occurring every 200-500 years that kill all or most canopy trees and reset succession. Both regimes are natural. Both are necessary. The forests evolved under their influence over thousands of years [32][34].

**Fire suppression.** After the Great Fire of 1910 -- which burned 3 million acres across Idaho, Montana, and Washington in two days, killing 85 people, mostly firefighters -- the United States adopted a policy of total fire suppression. For more than a century, the US Forest Service and state agencies fought every fire as quickly as possible. The policy was extraordinarily effective at preventing fire. It was also extraordinarily effective at storing fuel. In forests adapted to burn every 15-30 years, a century of suppression produced forests packed with small trees, understory brush, dead wood, and ladder fuels (vegetation that carries fire from the ground into the canopy). The forests were not protected by suppression -- they were loaded [32][35].

**The 2020 Oregon Labor Day Fires.** On September 7-8, 2020, a historic east wind event -- dry, hot, high-velocity winds from the interior blowing west through the Cascade passes -- drove multiple fires simultaneously across western Oregon. In approximately 72 hours, over 1 million acres burned. The Beachie Creek, Lionshead, Holiday Farm, Archie Creek, and Almeda fires collectively destroyed more than 4,000 homes and killed 11 people. It was the most destructive fire event in Oregon's recorded history and a direct consequence of a century of fuel accumulation ignited by extreme weather [36][37].

**Indigenous fire management.** The forests that European settlers encountered in the PNW were not "wilderness" -- they were managed landscapes. For thousands of years, Indigenous peoples including the Kalapuya, Yakama, Chinook, Salish, and Klamath practiced cultural burning: the deliberate, targeted application of fire to manage vegetation, maintain prairies for camas harvest, clear travel corridors, improve hunting grounds, reduce pest populations, and stimulate the growth of basket materials and food plants. The Kalapuya burned the Willamette Valley so regularly that early European explorers described it as "parkland" -- open grassland with scattered oaks -- not realizing it was a managed agricultural landscape. When burning ceased after colonization, Douglas fir rapidly invaded, converting the open valley into dense forest within decades [38][39].

The reintroduction of Indigenous fire management is one of the most important ecological restoration movements in the PNW. Tribal nations including the Yurok, Karuk, and Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde have revived cultural burning programs, often in partnership with federal and state agencies. Prescribed fire -- the controlled application of fire under specific weather conditions -- is increasingly recognized as essential to reducing fuel loads and restoring fire-adapted ecosystems. The forest doesn't need to be saved from fire. It needs fire to be saved from fuel [33][38][39].

## 6. Climate and the Forest

Climate change is restructuring the PNW forest in real time. The effects are not projections -- they are observations. The Douglas fir that defines the region is under stress, and the stress is measurable [40][41].

**Douglas fir at its southern and low-elevation limits.** Douglas fir is adapted to the PNW's historic climate: cool, wet winters and warm, dry summers with adequate soil moisture from winter precipitation and snowmelt. As temperatures rise and summer drought intensifies, Douglas fir is experiencing increased mortality at the southern and low-elevation margins of its range. In parts of southwestern Oregon and the Willamette Valley, Douglas fir seedling establishment is declining, and regeneration after disturbance increasingly favors hardwoods (Oregon white oak, bigleaf maple) over conifers. The species' range is shifting upslope and northward [40][42].

**Bark beetle outbreaks.** Mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*) and Douglas fir beetle (*Dendroctonus pseudotsugae*) populations are controlled primarily by cold winter temperatures that kill overwintering larvae. As winters warm, beetle populations expand into previously inhospitable elevations and latitudes. Major bark beetle outbreaks in the 2000s-2020s have killed millions of trees across the western United States, and the PNW is increasingly affected. Beetle-killed trees add to fuel loads, amplifying fire risk [41][43].

**Drought stress.** PNW forests depend on a specific precipitation pattern: wet winters that saturate soils and build snowpack, followed by gradual snowmelt through spring and early summer that sustains trees through the dry months. As snowpack declines (Washington Cascades snowpack has decreased by approximately 25% since the 1950s), summer soil moisture deficits begin earlier and last longer. Trees under drought stress are more susceptible to beetle attack, fungal disease, and fire mortality [40][44].

**Carbon storage.** Old-growth forests store far more carbon than young forests or plantations. A hectare of PNW old-growth may contain 500-1,100 tonnes of carbon in living biomass, dead wood, and soil organic matter -- among the highest carbon densities of any ecosystem on Earth. Logging old-growth and replacing it with plantation forestry releases a large fraction of this stored carbon and requires decades to centuries to recover. The carbon argument for preserving old-growth is straightforward: these forests are irreplaceable carbon sinks, and their destruction accelerates climate change [45][46].

**Assisted migration.** A controversial adaptation strategy: planting tree seedlings sourced from warmer, drier locations (further south or lower elevation) at sites where the local seed source may be maladapted to future climate conditions. Proponents argue that natural migration rates (typically 100-200 meters per century for tree species) are far too slow to track the rate of climate change. Critics argue that assisted migration risks introducing maladapted genotypes, disrupting local adaptation, and ignoring the genetic diversity already present in native populations. The debate reflects a fundamental tension between active intervention and ecological humility [41][47].

## 7. Mesh Telemetry / Forest Monitoring

The mycorrhizal network is the forest's own sensing system -- an information network evolved over 400 million years. Modern forest monitoring is our attempt to build a parallel system, using technology to map what the forest already knows about itself [48][49].

**Weather stations and soil moisture sensors.** Networks of automated weather stations (RAWS -- Remote Automated Weather Stations) across PNW forests record temperature, humidity, wind speed and direction, solar radiation, and precipitation at 10-minute to hourly intervals. Soil moisture sensors placed at multiple depths track the water availability that determines tree stress, fire risk, and seedling survival. The MesoWest system aggregates data from over 40,000 surface weather stations across the US, providing near-real-time conditions for any forested region [48][50].

**Dendrometer bands.** Precision instruments wrapped around tree trunks that measure radial growth at sub-millimeter resolution. Continuous dendrometer data reveals the daily and seasonal patterns of tree growth: expansion during daylight photosynthesis, contraction during nighttime transpiration, growth spurts after rain events, cessation during summer drought. When connected to data loggers with wireless transmission, dendrometers provide real-time measurement of how individual trees respond to environmental conditions [48].

**Camera traps.** Motion-triggered cameras deployed throughout forests capture images of wildlife that are otherwise invisible to human observers. Modern camera trap networks use AI-assisted image classification to identify species, estimate population density, and track movement patterns. The Snapshot USA project coordinates camera trap surveys across 1,500+ sites nationwide. In PNW forests, camera traps document everything from black bears and cougars to red tree voles and northern flying squirrels [51].

**Passive acoustic monitoring (PAM).** Microphone arrays deployed in forest canopies record the soundscape continuously. Machine learning algorithms trained on known species vocalizations can identify birds, frogs, and mammals from audio recordings, providing species presence/absence data without human observation. PAM is particularly valuable for monitoring spotted owls, marbled murrelets, and other rare or cryptic species whose vocalizations are distinctive but whose visual detection is difficult [49][52].

**Satellite imagery.** Landsat (30-meter resolution, since 1972) and Sentinel-2 (10-meter resolution, since 2015) provide regular multispectral imagery that reveals forest health, canopy cover change, post-fire recovery, and phenological patterns. The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) derived from satellite imagery quantifies photosynthetic activity across entire landscapes. Time series analysis of satellite data can detect forest die-off, insect outbreaks, and drought stress months before they are visible on the ground [49][53].

**LiDAR.** Light Detection and Ranging -- laser scanning from aircraft or satellites -- produces three-dimensional point clouds that map canopy structure with extraordinary precision. LiDAR data distinguishes ground elevation from canopy height, enabling generation of Canopy Height Models (CHM) that show the three-dimensional structure of the forest. The USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) is systematically collecting LiDAR data across the United States, with extensive coverage in the PNW. LiDAR data at 1-meter resolution reveals individual tree crowns, canopy gaps, snags, and structural complexity that no other remote sensing technology can capture [49][54].

**IoT sensor networks and digital twins.** Emerging forest monitoring systems deploy networks of low-power sensors (temperature, humidity, soil moisture, CO2 concentration, light levels) connected via mesh networking protocols (LoRaWAN, Zigbee) to create continuous environmental monitoring at scales from individual plots to entire watersheds. The concept of a "digital twin" -- a computational model of a living forest that ingests real-time sensor data and models forest dynamics -- is moving from theory to practice. Such systems could predict fire risk, drought stress, and growth trajectories days to weeks before they become visible [48][55].

The parallel between the mycorrhizal network and the sensor mesh is not metaphorical -- it is structural. Both are distributed networks that carry information between nodes. Both respond to environmental signals. Both enable system-level coordination that no individual node could achieve alone. The mycorrhizal network evolved over millions of years. The sensor mesh we are building in decades. Both map the same territory [13][48].

## 8. Connection to This Project

The Living Forest is the center of the Research series. Every other project maps territory around this forest. This project maps the forest itself -- the living system that connects them all.

**COL** (Columbia Valley Rainforest) maps the temperate rainforest of the Olympic Peninsula and Columbia Valley -- old-growth Sitka spruce and western hemlock at their wettest and most dramatic. COL is the rainforest face of the living forest: 100+ inches of annual precipitation, the Hoh and Quinault river valleys, the most biomass-dense forests on Earth.

**CAS** (Cascade Range) maps the mountain range that defines the PNW bioregion. The Cascades create the rain shadow, carry the glaciers, harbor the volcanoes, and stratify the forest into elevation bands from oak savanna to alpine krummholz. CAS is the geological backbone of the living forest -- terrain genesis made vertical.

**ECO** (Living Systems) synthesizes the taxonomy that organizes everything. Eight elevation bands, six chips, the Rosetta Stone that translates between projects. ECO is the classification system; the Living Forest is what it classifies.

**GDN** (Gardening) brings the forest to personal scale. The gardener who plants native species, builds soil, manages water, and creates habitat in their own yard is practicing the same ecology the old-growth forest practices at landscape scale. GDN is the living forest in a backyard.

**AVI** (Wings of the Pacific Northwest) maps the birds in the canopy. The marbled murrelet nesting on old-growth limbs, the spotted owl hunting in the understory, the pileated woodpecker excavating snags, the winter wren singing from nurse logs. AVI is the avian layer of the living forest -- 250+ species mapped to canopy layers and habitat types.

**MAM** (Fur, Fin & Fang) maps the mammals on the forest floor and in the canopy. Black bear, cougar, elk, northern flying squirrel, red tree vole, mountain beaver. MAM is the mammalian layer -- the trophic structure that shapes forest dynamics through herbivory, predation, and seed dispersal.

**SAL** (Salmon Heritage) maps the salmon in the streams. Pacific salmon carry marine-derived nitrogen from the ocean into riparian forests -- the nutrient pathway that fertilizes streamside trees, which in turn shade and cool the spawning streams the salmon require. SAL is the living forest's circulatory system -- nutrients flowing from ocean to canopy via the salmon's body.

**WYR** (Weyerhaeuser) maps the timber industry that logged the old-growth. Frederick Weyerhaeuser's 900,000-acre purchase in 1900, the clear-cutting of old-growth through the 20th century, the Timber Wars of the 1990s, and the modern sustainable forestry practices of the largest private timberland owner in America. WYR is the human impact story -- what happened when the living forest met the industrial economy.

**AGR** (PNW Agriculture) maps the agriculture that replaced forest on the valley floors. The Missoula Flood soils that grow the wheat, the Willamette Valley that grows the Pinot Noir, the Columbia Basin that grows the apples. And the smoke taint -- wildfire smoke from the forest that threatens the wine in the glass. AGR is where fire ecology meets viticulture.

**TIBS** (Traditions, Indigenous Knowledge & Bridge Studies) maps the knowledge systems that understood the forest long before western science arrived. Grandmother Cedar in Coast Salish tradition. Cultural burning as landscape management. The relationship between the human community and the forest community that sustained both for thousands of years. TIBS is the cultural layer of the living forest -- the knowledge that lives in practice, not in publications.

**BPS** (Bio-Physics Sensing) maps the physics of biological sensing -- how organisms detect and respond to their environment. Photosynthesis, chemoreception, acoustic communication, magnetic navigation. BPS is how the forest senses itself -- the biophysics beneath the ecology.

This is the center because the forest is the integrator. Every other project maps a part of the system. The living forest is the system itself -- mycorrhizal networks connecting roots to roots, canopy layers stacking light gradients from sun to soil, fire resetting succession, climate shifting ranges, terrain determining what grows where, salmon bringing nutrients from the sea, and mesh telemetry mapping it all in real time.

Grandmother Cedar is the mother tree. The mycorrhizal network is the Rosetta Stone made biological -- one network translating nutrients between species, between generations, between the living and the dead.
