The Forest

What Old-Growth Means

Old-Growth

200+ years, multi-layered canopy, snags, nurse logs, structural complexity. The big four: Douglas fir, western red cedar, Sitka spruce, western hemlock. Not a description of age — a description of complexity.

Grandmother Cedar

Mycorrhizal

Suzanne Simard’s mother tree research. The common mycorrhizal network: ectomycorrhizal fungi connecting roots underground. Carbon transfer between trees. Hub trees provisioning seedlings. The Wood Wide Web — and the controversy.

Terrain & Canopy

How the Land Was Made

Terrain

Cascadia Subduction Zone, volcanic arc, Cordilleran Ice Sheet, the Missoula Floods. How the rock beneath determines the forest above. Geology is the first author of ecology.

Seven Layers of Light

Canopy

Emergent to subterranean: eight layers, each a distinct habitat. Epiphytes carrying 35 kg per tree. Marbled murrelets nesting 50 miles from the ocean. The canopy as its own ecosystem.

Fire & Climate

The Forest That Needs to Burn

Fire

A century of suppression didn’t prevent fire — it stored it. Indigenous fire management. The 2020 Oregon Labor Day fires. When the debt came due, a million acres burned in three days.

The Range Is Shifting

Climate

Douglas fir moving upslope. Bark beetles amplified by warming. Snowpack declining. Old-growth as irreplaceable carbon sink. The question: can the forest move fast enough to follow its climate?

Sensing & Verification

Sensing the Forest

Mesh

The mycorrhizal network is the forest’s sensing system. Mesh telemetry is ours. Weather stations, LiDAR, PAM, camera traps, satellite imagery, IoT sensor networks. Both map the same territory.

The Growth Ring

8/8 PASS

Named for the annual growth ring — the tree’s own verification of each year lived. Module completion, source registry, cross-link coverage, and cultural sensitivity review.
