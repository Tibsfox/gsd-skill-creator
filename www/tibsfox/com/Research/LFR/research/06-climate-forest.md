## 1. The Climate That Built the Forest

The PNW forest exists because of a specific climate pattern: cool, wet winters that saturate soils and build snowpack, followed by warm, dry summers during which stored moisture sustains tree growth. This "Mediterranean" precipitation pattern -- reversed from the summer-rain pattern that characterizes much of the eastern United States -- is the foundation of PNW forest ecology. Douglas fir, western hemlock, western red cedar, and Sitka spruce all evolved under this regime and are adapted to its specific stresses: winter waterlogging, summer drought, and the transition seasons that connect them [1][2].

The maritime influence moderates the climate. Proximity to the Pacific Ocean keeps winter temperatures above freezing at low elevations (except during rare Arctic outflow events), provides reliable moisture from October through June, and limits summer heat to 10-15 degrees Celsius above winter means. The ocean also supplies the fog that is essential to coastal Sitka spruce forests -- fog drip on leaves and branches provides moisture during the dry summer months that precipitation does not [1][2][3].

This climate is changing. PNW annual temperatures have increased by approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius (2 degrees Fahrenheit) since 1900. Summer temperatures have increased more than winter temperatures. Precipitation patterns are shifting: more rain, less snow (due to warmer winter temperatures), and earlier snowmelt. The dry season is lengthening -- summer drought now begins 2-3 weeks earlier than mid-20th century averages and extends later into fall [3][4][5].

## 2. Douglas Fir Under Stress

Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) -- the signature tree of the PNW -- is adapted to moderate summer drought but not to the intensified drought conditions that climate change is producing. Research across the species' range has documented increased drought-related mortality, reduced growth, and declining regeneration success at the southern and low-elevation margins of its distribution [6][7][8].

**Growth decline.** A comprehensive study by Restaino et al. (2016), published in *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, analyzed tree ring data from over 11,000 Douglas fir trees across the western United States and found that increased water deficit (the gap between atmospheric demand for water and soil supply) was the primary driver of growth decline. Trees at lower elevations and at the southern edge of the range showed the most pronounced growth reductions -- declining 10-30% compared to mid-20th century growth rates [6].

**Regeneration failure.** After wildfire, the ability of Douglas fir to regenerate depends on seed availability, soil moisture, and climatic conditions during the first several years after germination. Davis et al. (2019) documented that post-fire regeneration of Douglas fir and other conifers is failing at many low-elevation sites in the western US -- sites where climate conditions have shifted beyond the species' regeneration niche even though mature trees can still survive. The post-fire landscape is converting from conifer forest to shrubland or grassland, a state change that may be irreversible on human timescales [7].

**Range shift.** Douglas fir's optimal elevation band is shifting upslope by approximately 100-200 meters per century, tracking the temperature increase. At the lower edge of its range (particularly in southwestern Oregon, the Willamette Valley margins, and the Rogue River basin), Douglas fir is being replaced by more drought-tolerant species -- Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*), madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), and in the driest sites, chaparral shrubs. At the upper edge, Douglas fir is expanding into subalpine zones formerly dominated by mountain hemlock and Pacific silver fir [3][6][8].

## 3. Bark Beetle Amplification

Mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*) and Douglas fir beetle (*Dendroctonus pseudotsugae*) are native insects that have coexisted with PNW forests for millions of years. Under normal conditions, beetle populations are controlled by cold winter temperatures (which kill overwintering larvae) and by the resinous defenses of healthy, vigorous trees (which "pitch out" attacking beetles by flooding entry holes with resin). Climate change has weakened both control mechanisms [9][10].

**Winter temperature thresholds.** Mountain pine beetle larvae are killed by sustained cold: temperatures below -35 to -40 degrees Celsius for several days kill the majority of overwintering larvae. As winter minimum temperatures increase, the geographic and elevational range where beetles can survive expands. Forests at higher elevations and latitudes -- previously protected by cold -- are now within the beetle's thermal range. The epidemic that killed millions of hectares of lodgepole pine in British Columbia beginning in the late 1990s was enabled by a sequence of mild winters that allowed beetle populations to explode [9][10].

**Drought stress reduces tree defenses.** Trees under drought stress produce less resin and have lower carbon reserves, reducing their ability to resist beetle attack. A beetle that would be pitched out by a well-watered tree can successfully colonize a drought-stressed tree. The interaction between drought and beetles is synergistic: drought weakens the tree, beetles kill it, and the dead tree adds to the fuel load that increases fire risk. Drought, beetles, and fire form a positive feedback loop that amplifies the impact of each individual stressor [9][10][11].

**Beetle-killed trees and fire risk.** Standing beetle-killed trees initially retain their red needles (the "red phase"), which are highly flammable. After 2-5 years, the needles fall and the snags enter the "gray phase," which is less flammable but provides long-lasting fuel on the ground as the trees eventually fall. The fuel loading from beetle-killed forests compounds the fuel debt from fire suppression -- two independently created fuel sources converging on the same landscape [9][10][11].

## 4. Snowpack: The Forest's Water Bank

PNW forests depend on snowpack as a water bank. Winter precipitation falls as snow at higher elevations (above approximately 1,200-1,500 meters in the Cascades), accumulates over the wet season, and melts gradually through spring and early summer, providing a steady supply of soil moisture during the months when precipitation is minimal. This snowmelt-sustained moisture is what allows the forest to maintain photosynthesis and growth through the PNW's characteristically dry summers [3][4][12].

Snowpack in the western United States is declining. Mote et al. (2018) documented that April 1 snow water equivalent (SWE) -- the standard measure of snowpack -- declined at 90% of monitored sites in the western US between 1955 and 2016. In the Washington Cascades, April 1 SWE has decreased by approximately 25-30% since the mid-20th century. The decline is driven primarily by warming temperatures: more winter precipitation falls as rain rather than snow, and snowmelt begins earlier in the spring [4][12].

The consequences for the forest are direct. Earlier snowmelt means that soil moisture begins to decline earlier in the spring. By late summer -- August and September, when temperatures are highest and atmospheric demand for water is greatest -- soil moisture deficits are deeper and more prolonged than in the past. Trees that depended on snowmelt-sustained moisture through July now experience drought stress beginning in June [3][4][12].

The snowpack decline also affects stream flow, which affects salmon habitat, which affects the nutrient cycling that connects the marine and terrestrial ecosystems. Reduced summer streamflow means warmer stream temperatures, lower dissolved oxygen, and less habitat for cold-water species like salmon and trout. The snowpack is not just the forest's water bank -- it is the hydrological connection between the living forest and the living river [4][12][13].

## 5. Drought and the Summer Gap

The defining feature of PNW climate is the summer drought -- the 2-4 month period from June/July through September/October when precipitation is minimal and the forest depends on stored moisture. PNW forests are adapted to this summer gap: deciduous understory species drop leaves, evergreen conifers reduce transpiration through stomatal closure, and the deep root systems of old-growth trees access subsoil moisture that shallow-rooted species cannot reach [1][2][3].

Climate change is widening the summer gap. The dry season is beginning earlier (snowmelt timing) and ending later (delayed onset of fall rains). A summer gap that historically lasted 8-12 weeks is now lasting 12-16 weeks in many areas. Each additional week of drought stress reduces tree growth, increases fire risk, and pushes vulnerable species closer to their physiological limits [3][4][6].

Vapor pressure deficit (VPD) -- the difference between the atmosphere's capacity to hold water and the actual water content -- is increasing across the PNW. Higher VPD means the atmosphere is drawing more water out of trees, even when soil moisture is still available. Trees must close their stomata to prevent water loss, which simultaneously prevents CO2 uptake and halts photosynthesis. The result is a growth reduction that occurs even when soil moisture has not yet reached critical levels -- the atmosphere itself is drying the trees [6][14].

Old-growth trees have an advantage in drought tolerance: their large root systems access deeper soil moisture, and their massive trunks store water in sapwood that can buffer short-term deficits. Young trees and seedlings, with shallow roots and small water reserves, are more vulnerable. This differential vulnerability means that climate change may selectively favor the survival of existing old-growth while preventing the establishment of the next generation -- a temporal gap that could break the continuity of the old-growth system [6][7][8].

## 6. The Carbon Argument

The relationship between PNW forests and atmospheric carbon is among the most consequential ecological questions of the 21st century. Old-growth PNW forests are the densest carbon pools in the terrestrial biosphere, storing 500-1,100 tonnes of carbon per hectare in living biomass, dead wood, and soil organic matter. The question is: what happens to that carbon, and what should we do about it? [15][16][17].

**Old-growth as carbon sink.** Contrary to the historical assumption that old forests are "carbon neutral" (growth balanced by decomposition), Luyssaert et al. (2008) demonstrated in *Nature* that old-growth forests worldwide continue to accumulate carbon, with forests >200 years old serving as net carbon sinks. PNW old-growth exemplifies this: large trees continue to add wood volume throughout their lives, and the carbon in dead wood and soil accumulates over centuries [15].

**Logging releases carbon.** When old-growth is logged, a significant fraction of the stored carbon is released. Slash (branches, tops, roots left on site) decomposes within decades. Soil carbon is released by disturbance. Even wood products (lumber, plywood) have finite lifetimes -- most wood products decompose or are burned within 50-100 years. The net effect of logging old-growth and replacing it with plantation is a large pulse of carbon release followed by decades of net carbon emission before the new plantation begins to accumulate carbon at rates comparable to the old-growth it replaced [15][16][17].

**The carbon debt of logging.** Harmon et al. (1990) calculated that converting Pacific Northwest old-growth to plantation forestry on 40-80 year rotations reduces landscape-level carbon storage by 40-60%. The "carbon payback period" -- the time required for the replacement plantation to accumulate as much carbon as was stored in the old-growth -- is estimated at 200-500 years, depending on site productivity and rotation length. In carbon terms, old-growth logging creates a debt that requires centuries to repay [16].

**Large trees are disproportionate.** Mildrexler et al. (2020) found that large trees (>53 cm diameter) account for approximately 42% of aboveground live carbon storage in forests east of the Cascade crest, despite representing only 3% of stems. The relationship between tree size and carbon storage is nonlinear -- a single large tree may store more carbon than hundreds of small trees combined. This finding has direct implications for forest management: selective removal of the largest trees has a disproportionate impact on carbon storage [17].

## 7. Assisted Migration: Moving Trees to Follow Climate

Assisted migration -- the deliberate movement of tree populations from locations where they are currently well-adapted to locations where they may be better adapted to projected future climate -- is one of the most debated adaptation strategies in forestry [18][19][20].

The argument for assisted migration is based on a mismatch between the rate of climate change and the rate of natural tree migration. Tree species expand their range through seed dispersal, which for most PNW conifers is limited to 100-200 meters per generation (30-100 years). Climate zones are shifting northward and upslope at rates of 100-500 meters per decade -- far faster than trees can naturally migrate. Without assistance, the trees currently growing at any given location may be maladapted to the climate that location will experience in 50-100 years [18][19].

The argument against assisted migration includes:

- **Genetic disruption.** Moving seed sources from one location to another may introduce genotypes that are poorly adapted to local soil, photoperiod, frost timing, or other non-climatic factors. A tree adapted to the climate of Ashland, Oregon may fail in the soils and day-length of Tacoma, Washington [19][20].

- **Unknown interactions.** Transplanted populations may carry diseases, insects, or mycorrhizal associates that disrupt the receiving ecosystem. The history of species introductions in other contexts (chestnut blight, Dutch elm disease, emerald ash borer) suggests that moving organisms between populations carries risks that cannot be fully anticipated [19][20].

- **Existing genetic diversity.** Natural populations contain genetic variation that may include individuals adapted to warmer, drier conditions. Rather than importing genetics from elsewhere, management could focus on maintaining large, genetically diverse populations that contain the adaptive potential to respond to climate change through natural selection [19][20].

- **Philosophical concerns.** Assisted migration represents a shift from conservation (preserving what exists) to design (creating what we think should exist). Some ecologists argue that the proper response to climate change is to protect large, connected landscapes that allow natural migration, rather than assuming we can predict which genotypes will be successful under future conditions [19][20].

Operational trial programs exist: the British Columbia Ministry of Forests has conducted seed transfer experiments since the 1960s, and several US programs are testing climate-adapted seed sources. The results are mixed and the experiments are still young -- definitive answers are decades away [18][19].

## 8. What the Models Project

Climate models for the PNW project a future that is warmer and drier in summer, with consequences for every aspect of forest ecology [3][4][5]:

- **Temperature:** 2-5 degrees Celsius increase in annual mean temperature by 2100 (depending on emissions scenario). Summer temperatures increase more than winter temperatures. The number of days above 35 degrees Celsius (95 degrees F) at low elevations may increase 2-5 fold [3][4].

- **Precipitation:** Total annual precipitation may remain roughly stable or increase slightly, but the seasonal distribution shifts: more winter rain (less snow), drier summers. The proportion of precipitation falling as snow decreases by 30-70% by 2100 [4][12].

- **Fire season:** Fire season lengthens by 2-4 weeks by mid-century. The number of high fire danger days increases by 50-100%. The probability of extreme fire weather events (like the 2020 Labor Day fires) increases substantially [3][5].

- **Forest type:** Models project a shift in forest types from current conditions: Douglas fir retreating from lower elevations, replaced by Oregon white oak and hardwood forests or grassland at the driest sites. Subalpine forests shrinking as treeline shifts upward. The "western hemlock zone" -- the most productive forest zone in the PNW -- becoming more like the current "Douglas fir zone," while the current Douglas fir zone becomes more like the current Oregon white oak zone [3][8].

- **Species composition:** Some models project that by 2100, Douglas fir will be absent from or severely reduced in areas where it currently dominates at low elevations in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, and southwestern Oregon. Western red cedar may experience similar range contraction. The species that expand are those adapted to warmer, drier conditions: Oregon white oak, madrone, ponderosa pine [3][8].

## 9. The Pace Problem

The fundamental problem at the intersection of climate change and forest ecology is pace. Climate is changing at rates measured in decades. Forests respond at rates measured in centuries. The gap between these timescales creates a period of disequilibrium during which the existing forest is maladapted to the climate it occupies but cannot be replaced by a forest adapted to the new climate [3][6][8].

This disequilibrium is already visible. Douglas fir seedlings are failing to establish after fire at low-elevation sites that will still support mature Douglas fir for another 100-200 years. The old trees survive because they are large, deep-rooted, and resilient. The young trees die because the climate has already shifted past their establishment threshold. The result is a forest of aging giants with no successors -- a demographic dead end playing out over centuries [7][8].

Old-growth is both the most resilient forest condition (large trees with deep roots, stored resources, mycorrhizal networks) and the most vulnerable to permanent loss (structural complexity that requires centuries to develop cannot be regrown in a changed climate). Protecting existing old-growth is simultaneously a carbon strategy, a biodiversity strategy, and a climate adaptation strategy -- the single most effective action available [15][16][17].

The forest has survived climate change before -- multiple ice ages, warm periods, volcanic winters. But past climate changes occurred over millennia, allowing gradual range shifts and evolutionary adaptation. The current rate of change is 10-100 times faster than any climate transition in the forest's evolutionary history. Whether the forest can adapt at this rate is not a question that science can answer in advance. It is an experiment we are conducting on the only forest we have [3][6][8].
