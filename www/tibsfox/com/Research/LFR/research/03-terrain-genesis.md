## 1. Three Forces

The Pacific Northwest landscape was built by three geological processes operating on vastly different timescales. Tectonic collision has been building the Cascade Range for millions of years. Volcanic eruptions have been reshaping peaks and depositing soils for tens of thousands of years. Catastrophic flooding carved the terrain in a matter of days. Understanding the forest requires understanding the rock beneath it, because geology determines soil, soil determines hydrology, and hydrology determines what grows where [1][2].

No part of the PNW landscape is geologically old in the continental sense. The Cascade Range is largely less than 2 million years old. The Puget Sound basin was carved by glaciers less than 20,000 years ago. The Channeled Scablands of eastern Washington were created by floods approximately 15,000 years ago. The soils that support PNW agriculture in the Willamette Valley were deposited by those same floods. The terrain is young, dynamic, and still being shaped by the same forces that created it [1][2][3].

This youth matters ecologically. PNW forests are growing on geologically young substrates that are still actively weathering, eroding, and accumulating. The deep, productive soils of western Oregon are mere adolescents by geological standards. The volcanic soils of the Cascades are still being modified by ongoing volcanic gas emissions, ash falls from minor eruptions, and hydrothermal alteration. The forest is not growing on a stable platform -- it is growing on a surface that is still being made [1][2].

## 2. The Cascadia Subduction Zone

The fundamental tectonic feature of the PNW is the Cascadia Subduction Zone -- a 1,000-kilometer boundary where the Juan de Fuca plate slides beneath the North American plate at approximately 40 millimeters per year. This subduction zone extends from Cape Mendocino in northern California to the northern tip of Vancouver Island in British Columbia. It is the engine that built the Cascade Range, generates the region's volcanism, and produces the most powerful earthquakes in North America [3][4].

The subduction process works by driving oceanic crust downward into the Earth's mantle. As the descending plate reaches depths of 80-120 kilometers, the intense heat and pressure cause water trapped in the oceanic crust to be released into the overlying mantle wedge. This water lowers the melting point of the mantle rock, generating magma that rises through the continental crust to feed the volcanic arc above. The Cascade volcanoes are the surface expression of this deep process -- each peak marks a location where magma has found a pathway to the surface [3][4].

The subduction zone also stores enormous tectonic strain, which is released periodically in megathrust earthquakes of magnitude 8.7-9.2. Geological evidence preserved in coastal marshes, ocean floor turbidite deposits, and Japanese tsunami records indicates that these full-rupture earthquakes have occurred approximately 40 times in the last 10,000 years, with an average recurrence interval of 200-600 years. The last full Cascadia rupture occurred on January 26, 1700, generating a tsunami that crossed the Pacific and was recorded in Japan. Three hundred and twenty-six years have elapsed since the last event [4][5].

The implications for the living forest are practical: when the next Cascadia earthquake occurs (and it will), landslides triggered by the shaking will destroy forest across hundreds of square kilometers of steep terrain. Coastal forests will be inundated by the resulting tsunami. Stream channels will be reorganized by debris flows. The forest has experienced these events before -- it is adapted to them on evolutionary timescales -- but no living forest in the PNW has experienced a full Cascadia rupture. The last one predates all standing old-growth [4][5].

## 3. The Volcanic Arc

The Cascade Range is a volcanic arc -- a chain of volcanoes aligned above the subduction zone. The major peaks from south to north include Mount Lassen (3,187 m), Mount Shasta (4,322 m), Crater Lake/Mount Mazama (2,487 m, caldera rim), Mount Hood (3,429 m), Mount Adams (3,742 m), Mount St. Helens (2,549 m), Mount Rainier/Tahoma (4,392 m), Glacier Peak (3,213 m), and Mount Baker (3,286 m). All are considered active or potentially active volcanoes by the USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory [3][6].

**Tahoma/Mount Rainier** (4,392 m / 14,411 ft) is the highest peak in the Cascade Range and the most glaciated mountain in the contiguous United States, carrying 25 named glaciers with a total ice volume of approximately 4.4 cubic kilometers. It is also the single most dangerous volcano in the Cascades: its steep, glacier-covered flanks are prone to enormous lahars (volcanic mudflows) that have repeatedly traveled down river valleys to the Puget Sound lowlands. The Osceola Mudflow, approximately 5,600 years ago, was one of the largest known lahars on Earth, traveling 70 miles to Puget Sound and depositing debris that underlies the present-day cities of Enumclaw, Auburn, Kent, and portions of the Port of Tacoma [6][7].

**Mount Hood** (3,429 m / 11,249 ft) is the most climbed glaciated peak in North America, with an estimated 10,000 summit attempts per year. Its last significant eruptive period was approximately 220-170 years ago (Old Maid eruptive period, c. 1781-1810), producing pyroclastic flows and lahars that traveled down the Sandy River drainage. Mount Hood's volcanic hazard zone directly overlaps with heavily populated areas east of Portland [3][6].

**Mount Baker** (3,286 m / 10,781 ft), in the North Cascades of Washington, last erupted in 1843 and experienced significant thermal activity in 1975 (increased fumarole output, meltwater lake formation in the summit crater). Baker's glaciers feed the Nooksack and Baker river systems, both critical salmon habitat. The mountain's forest flanks transition from low-elevation Douglas fir and western hemlock through Pacific silver fir to mountain hemlock and subalpine meadows -- a vertical transect through the entire PNW forest typology [3][6].

## 4. Mount St. Helens: The Living Laboratory

The eruption of Mount St. Helens on May 18, 1980 was the most significant volcanic event in the contiguous United States since Lassen Peak in 1915. The eruption removed 400 meters (1,300 feet) from the summit, produced a lateral blast that traveled at speeds up to 1,000 km/h, and devastated 230 square miles (600 km2) of forest, including 150 square miles (390 km2) of Weyerhaeuser's St. Helens Tree Farm. Fifty-seven people were killed, along with an estimated 11,000 hares, 6,000 deer, 5,200 elk, 1,400 coyotes, 300 bobcats, 200 black bears, and 15 mountain lions [8][9].

The blast zone has since become the most intensively studied ecological recovery site in the world. Forty-five years of continuous research have documented the process by which life recolonizes terrain sterilized by volcanic eruption -- a natural experiment in primary succession that no laboratory could replicate [8][10].

Key findings include:

- **Biological legacies matter.** Organisms that survived the eruption in protected microsites (snowfields, lakebeds, deep root systems, underground burrows) provided the biological capital for recovery. Pocket gophers (*Thomomys talpoides*) that survived beneath the snow mixed buried organic soil with volcanic deposit, creating patches of fertility that other species colonized. Lupines (*Lupinus lepidus*) fixed atmospheric nitrogen and built soil on the pumice surface. These biological legacies accelerated succession by decades compared to bare substrate [8][10].

- **Recovery is patchy, not uniform.** The blast zone recovery follows a landscape mosaic pattern, with some areas developing dense forest (where seed sources were nearby and soils accumulated quickly) and others remaining open meadow or shrubland after 45 years. This heterogeneity is itself ecologically valuable, creating the kind of habitat diversity that uniform plantation forestry cannot produce [8][10].

- **The eruption created habitat.** Spirit Lake, dammed by a massive debris avalanche, contains a floating log mat of approximately 20,000 logs that has persisted for 45 years and now supports its own plant communities. The pumice plains support populations of elk and deer that graze the recovering vegetation. New wetlands, ponds, and streams created by the eruption support amphibian communities that did not exist before 1980 [8][10].

The St. Helens blast zone directly connects the Living Forest project to the Weyerhaeuser (WYR) project: Weyerhaeuser replanted its devastated timberlands within years of the eruption and is now harvesting the second generation of trees grown on those sites. The contrast between the managed reforestation on Weyerhaeuser land and the natural recovery inside the Mount St. Helens National Volcanic Monument boundary is one of the most powerful demonstrations in the world of the difference between plantation forestry and ecological succession [8][9][10].

## 5. The Cordilleran Ice Sheet

During the last glacial maximum (approximately 20,000 years ago), the Cordilleran Ice Sheet covered most of British Columbia and extended south into Washington. The Puget Lobe of the ice sheet advanced as far south as Olympia, Washington, filling the Puget Sound basin with ice up to 1,000 meters (3,300 feet) thick. The ice was so massive that it depressed the Earth's crust by as much as 300 meters -- a depression that is still rebounding (isostatic adjustment) at a rate of approximately 1-2 mm per year [1][11][12].

The glaciers carved the landscape. The Puget Sound -- the inland sea that defines western Washington's geography -- was carved by the Puget Lobe. The San Juan Islands are the tops of ridges that the ice flowed over, sculpting them into the streamlined forms visible today. The deep, U-shaped valleys of the Cascades and Olympics (Elwha Valley, Skagit Valley, Snoqualmie Valley) were carved by alpine glaciers that ground rock into the fine glacial till that now covers valley floors [1][11][12].

Glacial deposits dominate the soils of the Puget Sound lowlands. Till (unsorted rock and clay deposited directly by ice), outwash (sorted sand and gravel deposited by meltwater streams), and lacustrine clay (deposited in glacial lakes dammed by ice) create the complex, layered soil profiles that determine hydrology, drainage, and forest type throughout the region. The dense clay layers (hardpan) in Puget lowland soils often create perched water tables that force trees to develop shallow root systems, making them vulnerable to windthrow -- a process that creates the canopy gaps central to old-growth dynamics [1][11].

Many Cascade peaks still carry active glaciers: Mount Rainier (25 named glaciers), Mount Baker (8 glaciers), Mount Adams (12 glaciers), Glacier Peak (7 glaciers). These glaciers are in rapid retreat. Mount Rainier's glaciers have lost approximately 25% of their volume since 1970. The retreat has implications for the living forest: glacial meltwater feeds rivers and streams during the critical late-summer dry period. As glacial volume declines, late-summer streamflow decreases, reducing water availability for trees, salmon, and the entire riparian ecosystem [7][12][13].

## 6. The Missoula Floods

Between approximately 18,000 and 13,000 years ago, the largest floods in Earth's recent geological history reshaped the PNW landscape. The Missoula Floods -- also called the Spokane Floods or the Bretz Floods (after J Harlen Bretz, the geologist who first proposed the hypothesis in the 1920s) -- were caused by the repeated formation and catastrophic failure of an ice dam at the mouth of glacial Lake Missoula in present-day western Montana [14][15].

At its maximum extent, Lake Missoula held approximately 500 cubic miles (2,100 km3) of water -- roughly equal to the combined volume of Lake Erie and Lake Ontario. When the ice dam failed (as ice dams inevitably do, because water is denser than ice and the rising lake level eventually lifts the ice dam from its bed), the entire lake emptied in a matter of days. The resulting flood discharged at a rate estimated at 10-17 cubic kilometers per hour -- a flow rate ten times greater than the combined flow of all rivers on Earth [14][15][16].

The floodwaters traveled southwest across the Columbia Plateau, carving the Channeled Scablands of eastern Washington -- a landscape of dry coulees, cataracts, and scoured basalt that Bretz recognized as the product of catastrophic flooding despite decades of opposition from geologists who rejected the idea of sudden, violent geological processes. The floods were eventually confirmed by aerial photography, satellite imagery, and detailed sedimentological analysis. Bretz received the Penrose Medal -- geology's highest honor -- in 1979, at the age of 96 [14][15].

The floods converged on the Columbia River Gorge, backing up behind the narrow passage and creating temporary lakes hundreds of feet deep. The floodwaters then spilled into the Willamette Valley through gaps in the Columbia Gorge walls, depositing thick layers of fine-grained alluvial sediment that now form the agricultural soils of the valley. The floods occurred 40-80 times over approximately 2,000-3,000 years, each time depositing a new layer of sediment [14][15][16].

The ecological significance is profound. The deep alluvial soils of the Willamette Valley -- the soils that grow Oregon's Pinot Noir grapes, hazelnuts, grass seed, and berries -- are Missoula Flood deposits. The Columbia Basin soils that produce Washington's wheat, potatoes, and wine grapes were deposited or exposed by the floods. The channeled landscape of eastern Washington, where basalt cliffs create microclimates in their lee, supports habitat diversity that would not exist on unscoured terrain. The floods did not merely carve the landscape -- they distributed the substrates that determine what grows where 15,000 years later [14][15][16].

## 7. Soil from Rock: The Substrate Determines the Forest

The connection between geology and forest is mediated by soil. Rock weathers into mineral particles; those particles combine with organic matter, water, and organisms to form soil; soil determines what grows. Different parent rocks produce different soils, which support different forests [1][2][17].

**Columbia River Basalt.** The basalt flows that cover most of eastern Washington and parts of western Oregon (erupted 17-6 million years ago from fissures in eastern Oregon/Washington) weather into soils with moderate fertility, good drainage, and a distinctive red-brown color (iron oxidation). On the wet side of the Cascades, deeply weathered basalt produces the Jory and Nekia soils -- the red, clay-rich volcanic soils that support Oregon's premier Pinot Noir vineyards. On the dry side, basalt soils support dryland wheat farming and irrigated agriculture [2][17][18].

**Glacial till.** The unsorted deposits left by retreating ice sheets produce soils of extremely variable texture -- boulders, gravel, sand, silt, and clay in unpredictable combinations. In the Puget Sound lowlands, glacial till soils support Douglas fir forests where drainage is adequate and western red cedar-bigleaf maple forests where drainage is impeded by clay layers. The perched water tables created by till clay layers are a primary determinant of tree species composition in lowland western Washington [1][11].

**Alluvial deposits.** The flood-deposited and river-deposited soils of valley bottoms are typically deep, well-drained, and fertile -- the most productive forest sites in the region. Western red cedar, Sitka spruce, black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), and red alder (*Alnus rubra*) dominate alluvial sites. These are also the sites most commonly converted to agriculture, because the same soil properties that produce big trees also produce big crops [1][2].

**Volcanic ash (andisol).** Deposits of volcanic ash from Cascade eruptions produce andisol soils -- light, porous, highly permeable, and among the most fertile soils on Earth. Andisols have unusual properties: they retain moisture well despite being well-drained, and they have high cation exchange capacity despite their coarse texture. Douglas fir growing on andisol soils in the Cascades achieves some of the fastest growth rates in the species' range [2][17].

## 8. Volcanic Soils and Terroir

The wine industry provides an unexpected but informative lens on the geology-soil-plant connection. In the Willamette Valley, three distinct soil types derived from different geological processes produce measurably different wines from the same grape variety (Pinot Noir) grown within miles of each other [18][19].

**Jory soils** (derived from basalt weathering) produce wines described as having bright cherry fruit, earthy tones, and structured tannins. **Willakenzie soils** (derived from marine sedimentary rock) produce wines with darker fruit, spice, and softer texture. **Laurelwood soils** (loess -- wind-deposited silt over basalt) produce wines with floral notes and elegant structure. The same viticultural practices, the same clone of Pinot Noir, the same climate -- different soil, different wine [18][19].

This is terroir in its most literal sense: the taste of the ground. It is also a precise demonstration of the principle that governs the entire living forest. The rock determines the soil. The soil determines the water chemistry. The water chemistry determines the nutrient availability. The nutrient availability determines the plant physiology. The plant physiology determines the forest. From basalt to canopy, geology writes the first draft [18][19].

## 9. The Rain Shadow

The Cascade Range is not only a volcanic arc -- it is the primary climatic feature of the PNW. By intercepting Pacific moisture, the Cascades create one of the most dramatic rain shadows in North America [1][2][20].

West of the Cascade crest: 60-120+ inches (150-300+ cm) of annual precipitation. Maritime climate: mild winters (rarely below freezing at low elevations), cool summers (70-80 F / 21-27 C typical), persistent cloud cover, and reliable moisture from October through June. This is the climate that grew the PNW old-growth -- the Douglas fir, western red cedar, Sitka spruce, and western hemlock forests that define the region [1][20].

East of the Cascade crest: 6-20 inches (15-50 cm) of annual precipitation. Continental climate: cold winters (well below freezing), hot summers (90-100+ F / 32-38+ C), 300+ days of sunshine, and a pronounced summer drought. This is the climate of the ponderosa pine forest, the sagebrush steppe, and the irrigated agricultural empire that Missoula Flood soils and Grand Coulee Dam water made possible [1][20].

The rain shadow is not a line -- it is a gradient that plays out over approximately 20-40 miles across the Cascade crest. On the western slope, precipitation decreases with elevation above the snowline (precipitation maxima occur at 3,000-5,000 feet, where orographic lifting is strongest). On the eastern slope, precipitation drops precipitously. The forest changes accordingly: wet Douglas fir-hemlock forest on the west slope gives way to dry Douglas fir-grand fir forest on the eastern flank, then to ponderosa pine savanna, then to sagebrush. This gradient -- visible from any east-west highway crossing the Cascades -- is the single most powerful demonstration in the PNW of how climate determines forest [1][2][20].

The Cascade rain shadow created two of the four big old-growth conifers (western hemlock and Sitka spruce are wet-side specialists), shaped the fire ecology of the region (east side burns frequently and at low severity; west side burns rarely but catastrophically), and determined the agricultural geography of the PNW (wet-side rainfed vs. east-side irrigated). Every project in the Research series maps a system whose fundamental character is determined by which side of the Cascades it sits on [1][2][20].
