## 1. The Vertical Forest

A PNW old-growth forest is not one habitat. It is at least eight habitats stacked vertically in a column of air 60-90 meters tall, from emergent crown to bedrock. Each layer receives a different quantity and quality of light. Each maintains a different temperature and humidity regime. Each supports a different community of organisms. Some species spend their entire lives in a single layer and never encounter species living ten meters above or below them [1][2][3].

This vertical zonation is one of the most important structural features of old-growth forest. Plantation forests -- which consist of a single canopy layer of even-aged trees -- contain one or two of these eight layers. Young second-growth forests may contain three or four. Only old-growth forests, developed over 200-500+ years of undisturbed succession, contain the full vertical complement. The loss of any layer reduces the habitat available to the species specialized for that layer's conditions [1][2].

Canopy science as a formal discipline is surprisingly recent. Before the 1980s, the forest canopy was essentially unstudied -- a "last biotic frontier" that ecologists could see from below but could not access. The development of canopy access techniques (climbing ropes, walkways, construction cranes) in the 1980s and 1990s opened the canopy to direct study for the first time. What researchers found was startling: the canopy was not simply the tops of trees but a distinct ecosystem with its own soil, its own nutrient cycling, its own hydrology, and species found nowhere else on Earth [2][3].

## 2. Emergent Layer

**Height:** 60-90+ meters (200-300+ feet)
**Light:** 100% of incident solar radiation
**Species:** The tallest Douglas firs and Sitka spruces

The emergent layer consists of individual trees that extend above the general canopy surface, fully exposed to wind, direct sunlight, and temperature extremes on all sides. These are the largest trees in the forest -- typically Douglas firs exceeding 60 meters (200 feet) in height and 1.5 meters (5 feet) in diameter, or Sitka spruces of similar dimensions in the coastal zone [1][4].

Emergent trees serve as perch and nest sites for bald eagles (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*), which require exposed treetops for their massive platform nests (up to 2 meters wide, 3 meters deep, weighing over 1,000 kg in long-used nests). Peregrine falcons (*Falco peregrinus*) use emergent snags as hunting perches, surveying the canopy below for prey. Vaux's swifts (*Chaetura vauxi*) nest in the hollow trunks of emergent snags, entering through the broken top [1][5].

The structural prominence of emergent trees makes them lightning targets. Lightning strikes are a natural ignition source for forest fires in the PNW, and emergent trees -- extending 10-20 meters above the general canopy -- are the most frequently struck. A lightning-killed emergent tree becomes a snag, which may stand for decades providing cavity nesting habitat before falling to become a nurse log. The emergent layer is where fire ecology meets canopy ecology [1][4][6].

## 3. Upper Canopy

**Height:** 40-60 meters (130-200 feet)
**Light:** 60-95% of incident solar radiation
**Species:** Douglas fir, western hemlock (dominant crown class), Sitka spruce

The upper canopy is the continuous crown layer -- the surface visible from above or from satellite imagery. It intercepts 80-95% of incoming sunlight, making it the primary photosynthetic zone of the forest. The energy budget of the entire ecosystem is largely determined by what happens in this layer [1][2][4].

The upper canopy is the habitat of two of the PNW's most iconic old-growth-dependent species:

**The marbled murrelet** (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) is a small seabird in the family Alcidae (puffins, murres, auklets) that nests exclusively on the large, moss-covered limbs of old-growth conifers. Unlike all its relatives, which nest on cliffs, burrows, or the ground, the murrelet nests on horizontal limbs typically 20-60 meters above the ground, often 30-80 kilometers from the ocean. The nesting female flies from the sea to the forest at dawn or dusk, navigating at speeds of up to 160 km/h through the forest canopy in near-darkness. The species was not confirmed to nest in trees until 1974, when a logger in Big Basin Redwoods State Park in California found a chick on a mossy limb. It is listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act, primarily due to loss of old-growth nesting habitat [5][7].

**The northern spotted owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) roosts and nests in the upper and lower canopy of old-growth forests, using the dense, multi-layered canopy structure for thermal cover and predator protection. Spotted owls are the species whose listing under the Endangered Species Act in 1990 triggered the Northwest Forest Plan and the effective end of old-growth logging on federal lands. The species requires large territories (2,000-10,000 acres of suitable habitat per pair) and cannot persist in simplified, young forests [5][8].

## 4. Lower Canopy

**Height:** 25-40 meters (80-130 feet)
**Light:** 5-20% of incident solar radiation
**Species:** Western hemlock, Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*), Pacific yew (*Taxus brevifolia*)

The lower canopy is the domain of shade-tolerant species -- trees that can grow, reproduce, and maintain positive carbon balance under the deep shade of the upper canopy. Western hemlock, the most shade-tolerant of the big four conifers, is often the dominant species in this layer, slowly growing upward over decades or centuries, waiting for a canopy gap to release it into the upper canopy [1][4].

Pacific yew (*Taxus brevifolia*) deserves special attention. This small, shade-tolerant conifer, typically 5-15 meters tall in old-growth forests, was considered a "trash tree" by the timber industry -- too small for lumber, too slow-growing for commercial forestry. In 1971, researchers at the National Cancer Institute isolated a compound from Pacific yew bark called taxol (paclitaxel), which proved to be one of the most effective anticancer drugs ever discovered. The initial production method -- stripping bark from wild trees -- raised conservation concerns, as it required approximately 6 trees to produce enough taxol for one patient's treatment. Synthetic and semi-synthetic production methods have since replaced wild harvest, but the Pacific yew story remains a powerful illustration of the potential value of species in old-growth forests that were never studied because they had no commercial timber value [9][10].

The lower canopy is also the primary hunting zone for northern spotted owls, which glide silently between trunks, using their asymmetric ear placement to triangulate the sounds of prey (primarily northern flying squirrels, red-backed voles, and woodrats) on the forest floor below [5][8].

## 5. Understory

**Height:** 10-25 meters (30-80 feet)
**Light:** 2-10% of incident solar radiation
**Species:** Vine maple (*Acer circinatum*), bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*), young conifers

The understory layer is dominated by shade-tolerant hardwoods and young conifers awaiting their turn in the canopy. Vine maple -- a small, multi-stemmed tree that arches and bends across the understory, sometimes rooting where its branches touch the ground -- is one of the most characteristic understory species of PNW forests. Its brilliant red-orange fall color provides the only deciduous color display in otherwise evergreen forests [1][4][11].

Bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) is the understory giant -- reaching 20-30 meters in height with trunks up to 1 meter in diameter. Bigleaf maples in PNW old-growth carry extraordinary epiphyte loads that distinguish them from the same species in younger forests. A single old bigleaf maple can support 35 kg (77 lb) or more of epiphytic mosses, liverworts, lichens, and ferns growing on its branches and trunk. These epiphyte mats create "canopy soils" -- suspended organic matter accumulations that retain water, cycle nutrients, and support their own communities of invertebrates, including mites, springtails, and nematodes [2][3][11].

The canopy soils of bigleaf maples have been shown to contain their own adventitious roots -- roots growing from the maple's branches into the epiphyte mat, absorbing nutrients that would otherwise be washed away by rain. The tree is essentially farming its own epiphytes, extracting nutrition from the organisms growing on its surface. This phenomenon, documented by Nalini Nadkarni and others, demonstrates that the canopy is not simply a structure for supporting leaves but an active nutrient-cycling system [2][3].

## 6. Shrub Layer

**Height:** 2-10 meters (6-30 feet)
**Light:** 1-5% of incident solar radiation
**Species:** Salal, Oregon grape, huckleberry, devil's club, elderberry

The shrub layer provides the structural complexity at human height that defines the experience of walking through an old-growth forest. Dense salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) thickets, waist-high sword ferns, and the spiny stalks of devil's club create the "jungle" quality of wet PNW forest undergrowth [1][11][12].

**Salal** -- an evergreen shrub with thick, leathery leaves -- is so abundant in PNW forests that it supports a commercial harvest industry. Salal branches are exported to European floral markets, where they are valued as greenery in arrangements. The berries are edible and were a significant food source for Coast Salish peoples [11][12].

**Oregon grape** (*Mahonia aquifolium*) is the state flower of Oregon (technically it is a shrub, not a wildflower). Its hollylike leaves, yellow flowers, and blue berries provide year-round visual interest in the forest understory. The roots contain berberine, an antimicrobial compound used in traditional medicine [11][12].

**Huckleberry** (*Vaccinium* spp.) -- including red huckleberry (*V. parvifolium*), which grows preferentially on nurse logs, and evergreen huckleberry (*V. ovatum*) -- produces the berries that are among the most culturally and ecologically important fruits in the PNW. Black bears consume enormous quantities of huckleberries in late summer, gaining the fat reserves needed for winter hibernation. Indigenous peoples have managed huckleberry fields through cultural burning for thousands of years [11][12][13].

**Devil's club** (*Oplopanax horridus*) is unmistakable: a large-leaved, woody shrub armed with dense sharp spines on its stems, leaf stalks, and even leaf undersides. Contact with the spines produces painful, slow-healing wounds. Despite (or because of) its formidable defenses, devil's club is one of the most culturally significant medicinal plants for PNW Indigenous nations, used for treating diabetes, respiratory ailments, and as a spiritual medicine. It grows in wet, shady ravines and stream bottoms -- the darkest, wettest microsites in the forest [11][12][14].

## 7. Herb and Ground Layers

**Height:** 0-2 meters (0-6 feet)
**Light:** <1-2% of incident solar radiation

The herb layer and ground layer of old-growth forest exist in near-permanent twilight. In the deepest old-growth, less than 2% of the sunlight that strikes the canopy reaches the forest floor. Species growing here have evolved extreme shade tolerance -- some can maintain positive carbon balance at light levels that would kill any crop plant [1][4][11].

**Sword fern** (*Polystichum munitum*) is the dominant herb in many PNW forests -- a large, evergreen fern forming dense stands under the canopy. Individual sword fern clumps can persist for decades, with fronds reaching 1.5 meters in length. The species is so characteristic of PNW forests that it has become an icon of the region's identity [11][12].

**Oxalis** (*Oxalis oregana*) -- wood sorrel -- carpets the forest floor in deep shade. Its clover-shaped leaves are phototropic, folding closed in bright light (such as a sunfleck penetrating the canopy) and opening in shade. This behavior protects the leaves from photodamage in conditions brighter than they are adapted to -- a response calibrated to an organism that has evolved to live in less than 2% light [11].

**Mosses and liverworts** form continuous mats on the forest floor, on rocks, on logs, and on the lower trunks of trees. In wet PNW forests, moss biomass on the forest floor can exceed 1,000 kg per hectare. Mosses play a critical role in moisture retention, soil formation, and nutrient cycling. They are also the germination substrate for many tree seedlings -- hemlock and spruce seeds germinate more successfully on moss-covered surfaces than on bare mineral soil [1][11][15].

**Nurse logs** -- fallen trees in advanced decay -- are the elevated germination platforms discussed in Module 01. The ground layer is where the nurse log cycle operates: the intersection of dead wood, moss, seedling establishment, and the mycorrhizal network [1][15].

## 8. The Subterranean Layer

**Depth:** 0-30+ cm below surface
**Light:** Zero
**Species:** Mycorrhizal fungi, decomposer fungi, bacteria, archaea, soil invertebrates

The subterranean layer is the foundation of everything above. Module 02 covers the mycorrhizal network in detail. Here, the key points are structural: fungal hyphae penetrate the first 30 cm of old-growth forest soil at densities of up to 200 meters of hyphal length per gram of soil. The total hyphal length in a hectare of old-growth forest soil is estimated at thousands of kilometers -- an underground network of extraordinary extent and connectivity [16][17].

The subterranean layer connects the root systems of all canopy layers into a single, continuous network. A seedling germinating on a nurse log two meters above the forest floor extends roots downward through the decaying wood into the soil, where mycorrhizal fungi connect it to the root systems of the surrounding canopy trees. The physical separation of the canopy layers -- eight distinct habitats from emergent crown to subterranean soil -- is bridged by the mycorrhizal network that links them all [16][17].

Old-growth soils are themselves a product of centuries of biological activity. The deep organic horizon (O-horizon) of old-growth soils -- typically 5-20 cm thick -- consists of decomposed leaves, needles, bark, wood fragments, fungal residues, and invertebrate castings accumulated over hundreds of years. This organic layer retains moisture, buffers soil temperature, stores nutrients, and provides the physical substrate in which mycorrhizal fungi operate. Logging removes the trees, but soil compaction from heavy equipment damages the soil structure and mycorrhizal network that the next forest depends upon [1][16][17].

## 9. Epiphytes: The Canopy Garden

Epiphytes -- plants that grow on other plants, using them as substrate but not as nutrient sources -- are among the most remarkable features of PNW old-growth canopies. In the wettest forests (Olympic Peninsula, Oregon Coast Range), epiphyte biomass can exceed 35 kg (77 lb) per tree, with some bigleaf maples carrying over 50 kg of mosses, lichens, liverworts, and ferns on their branches [2][3][18].

The dominant canopy mosses include *Isothecium myosuroides* (cat-tail moss), *Antitrichia curtipendula* (hanging moss), and *Hylocomium splendens* (stair-step moss). These species form thick mats on branches that retain water like sponges, creating moist microhabitats in the canopy even during summer drought. Epiphytic mosses can hold 15-20 times their dry weight in water, effectively creating a suspended reservoir that moderates canopy humidity and provides moisture to other epiphytes and to the trees themselves [2][3][18].

Epiphytic lichens -- particularly *Lobaria oregana*, *Lobaria pulmonaria*, and species of *Usnea* (old man's beard) -- are ecologically significant because many fix atmospheric nitrogen through their cyanobacterial symbionts. This "free" nitrogen input, delivered to the forest via rainfall washing through the canopy (throughfall), is a meaningful component of the nitrogen budget in nitrogen-limited PNW forests. Studies have estimated that epiphytic lichen nitrogen fixation contributes 2-9 kg N per hectare per year in old-growth forests -- a significant fraction of the total nitrogen input [2][3][19].

Epiphytic ferns -- particularly licorice fern (*Polypodium glycyrrhiza*) -- grow on mossy trunks and branches, their fronds emerging in fall and dying back in summer (opposite to most ferns). The licorice fern's rhizome was chewed by Indigenous peoples as a cough remedy and sweetener [11][18].

The canopy epiphyte community requires decades to centuries to develop. Old-growth trees carry far more epiphytes than young trees, not simply because they are larger but because epiphyte colonization is a slow process that requires stable, long-lived substrate. Logging resets the epiphyte community to zero, and recovery takes the full 200+ years required for new old-growth conditions to develop [2][3].

## 10. Canopy Gaps: Windows to the Floor

Canopy gaps are the primary mechanism of change in old-growth forests between fire events. When a large canopy tree dies -- from root rot, bark beetle attack, windthrow, or simply old age -- the gap it creates in the canopy allows a pulse of sunlight to reach the forest floor. This pulse triggers a cascade of ecological responses [1][4][20].

In the gap itself: light levels increase 5-10 fold. Air temperature rises. Soil temperature rises. Soil moisture may initially increase (no canopy interception of rainfall) and then decrease (increased evapotranspiration from released understory vegetation). Seeds of shade-intolerant species (Douglas fir, red alder) that have been dormant in the soil or have blown in from nearby sources germinate and grow rapidly. Shade-tolerant species (western hemlock, Pacific silver fir) that have been growing slowly in the understory experience a "release" -- their growth rates increase dramatically as light becomes available [1][4][20].

The spatial scale of gaps matters. Small gaps (20-50 m2, single tree) favor shade-tolerant species that are already present in the understory. Medium gaps (50-200 m2, 2-5 trees) allow shade-intolerant species to establish if seed sources are nearby. Large gaps (200-1,000+ m2, from windthrow or localized fire) function as small-scale disturbance events that reset succession in a patch. This continuum of gap sizes creates the mosaic of age classes and species compositions that characterizes old-growth forests [1][20].

Canopy gaps also affect the subterranean layer. The death of a large tree eliminates a major node in the mycorrhizal network, but the network typically persists through connections to surviving trees. The gap-creating tree's root system and mycorrhizal connections decay over years to decades, while new seedlings establishing in the gap form new mycorrhizal associations. Gap dynamics and network dynamics are coupled systems -- each responds to and influences the other [1][16][20].

## 11. The Light Gradient

The single most important physical gradient in the forest is light. From 100% at the emergent crown to less than 1% at the deepest forest floor, light intensity decreases by two orders of magnitude over a vertical distance of 60-90 meters. This gradient determines which species can survive at each height and creates the selective pressures that have driven the evolution of shade tolerance in PNW forest species [1][4].

The light gradient is not smooth. Sunflecks -- brief patches of direct sunlight that penetrate through gaps in the canopy as the sun moves across the sky -- provide intermittent pulses of high-intensity light to the forest floor. In some old-growth forests, sunflecks contribute 50-80% of the total light energy received by understory plants, despite representing only 5-10% of the daylight hours. Understory plants have evolved rapid photosynthetic responses to exploit these brief windows -- ramping up photosynthesis within seconds of a sunfleck's arrival and ramping down within seconds of its departure [1][4][21].

The quality of light also changes with depth. The canopy selectively absorbs red and blue wavelengths (used by chlorophyll for photosynthesis) and transmits green and far-red wavelengths. The forest floor is bathed in light enriched in far-red relative to red -- a signal that plants use (via the phytochrome pigment system) to detect shade. Shade-tolerant species have evolved to function under this altered spectrum; shade-intolerant species cannot [1][4][21].
