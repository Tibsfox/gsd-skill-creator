# Threshold Records and Artistic Independence

## Formation and Rationale

Threshold Records was founded in 1969 by the Moody Blues, following the precedent of the Beatles' Apple Records (1968). The name came from their UK #1 album *On the Threshold of a Dream*. The stated motivation, per Justin Hayward speaking to *Melody Maker*: "After we'd completed each of our albums we found that we had so many ideas left over...we felt that we weren't able to exercise sufficient control over our material."

### The Structural Innovation

The Threshold model anticipated a structural shift in the music industry that would not become widespread until the 1970s: artist-owned labels operating within the distribution infrastructure of major corporations. The key insight was that creative control and business infrastructure could be separated -- you did not need to own pressing plants and distribution trucks to control your artistic output.

## The Decca Distribution Partnership

The label operated under a distribution and facilities agreement with Decca Records, giving the band access to Decca's recording studios and distribution network while retaining creative and business control over their releases, gatefold packaging, and artist roster decisions. This hybrid model -- artist ownership with corporate distribution -- was revolutionary for its time.

### What Threshold Controlled

| Domain | Control | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| Recording decisions | Threshold (band) | Choice of studio, producer, engineer |
| Album sequencing | Threshold (band) | Track order, concept structure |
| Cover art / packaging | Threshold (band) | Gatefold design as artistic statement |
| Release timing | Threshold (band) | When albums shipped |
| Artist roster | Threshold (band) | Which other acts to sign and produce |
| Manufacturing | Decca | Pressing, printing |
| Distribution | Decca/London | Retail, wholesale, international |
| Marketing support | Shared | Promotional strategy coordinated |

## Gatefold Cover Design as Artistic Statement

The Threshold releases consistently used gatefold covers -- double-fold sleeves that opened like a book, providing interior space for artwork, lyrics, and liner notes. This was not merely packaging; it was an assertion that the album was a complete artistic object, not a commodity container for singles. The gatefold format aligned with the band's concept-album approach: the physical object should reward extended engagement, just as the music did.

## Label Activity and Legacy

### First Releases

The first Threshold release was *To Our Children's Children's Children* (THS-1, 1969). All subsequent Moody Blues albums through 1999's *Strange Times* bore the Threshold logo. The catalog number system (THS-) became synonymous with the band's output.

### Other Artists

The band also produced other artists: bassist John Lodge produced the rock sextet Trapeze; Providence also recorded for the label. The roster remained small -- Threshold was primarily a vehicle for the Moody Blues' own work rather than a full-service record company.

## The Threshold Record Shop

The Threshold record shop in Cobham, Surrey operated from the early 1970s through February 2011. A second shop operated in Swindon in the late 1970s. These retail outlets were unusual for an artist-owned label -- most artist labels existed purely as contractual entities. The Threshold shop was a physical manifestation of the band's commitment to direct connection with their audience.

The Cobham shop operated for nearly four decades, outlasting the band's active recording career by over a decade. It served as both a retail outlet and a pilgrimage site for Moody Blues fans -- a rare case of a band maintaining a continuous physical presence in a community independent of touring.

## The Pinder Dispute

The most significant internal conflict related to Threshold concerned Mike Pinder's relationship with the remaining members after his departure following *Octave* (1978). The legal dispute over the *Long Distance Voyager* album (1981) -- the first Moody Blues album recorded without Pinder, with Patrick Moraz of Yes replacing him -- centered on album name rights and the use of the Moody Blues name on Threshold releases.

The dispute highlighted a structural tension inherent in artist-owned labels: when the artists disagree, who controls the label? The Moody Blues' solution was not elegant -- it involved litigation -- but the label survived the conflict and continued to function as the band's primary release vehicle.

## The Threshold Model's Influence

The Threshold model anticipated and influenced subsequent artist-owned labels:

| Label | Artist | Year | Threshold Parallel |
|-------|--------|------|--------------------|
| Rolling Stones Records | Rolling Stones | 1971 | Major-label distribution; artist creative control |
| Purple Records | Deep Purple | 1971 | Birmingham connection; rock act asserting independence |
| Swan Song Records | Led Zeppelin | 1974 | Gatefold aesthetics; artist-owned with Atlantic distribution |
| Manticore Records | Emerson, Lake & Palmer | 1973 | Prog rock act; concept-album packaging focus |

The Moody Blues were among the earliest British rock acts to pursue this model. The specific combination -- artist ownership, major-label distribution, gatefold packaging as artistic statement, and retail presence -- was more comprehensive than most subsequent imitators achieved.

## The Label as Architecture

The Threshold model connects to the band's broader philosophical framework: the threshold is the space between dependence and independence, between corporate infrastructure and artistic autonomy. They did not reject the industry -- they found the membrane between industry and art and built a sustainable structure there. The label is an expression of the same principle that drove their music: the most powerful creative territory is in the spaces between established forms.
