# The Great Transformation (1966--1967)

## The 1966 Pivot

The transformation of the Moody Blues from an R&B singles act into the architects of symphonic progressive rock is one of the most dramatic creative reinventions in rock history. It happened in a compressed six-month window between June and November 1966, driven by departures, arrivals, a corporate commission, and a decisive act of creative insubordination.

### Warwick's Departure (June 1966)

Clint Warwick left the band in June 1966, citing that touring kept him from his wife and two young children. Rod Clark replaced him as bassist for approximately three months. Clark's tenure was brief but it maintained the band as a performing unit during a period of instability.

### Laine's Departure (October 1966)

Denny Laine departed in October 1966 to pursue a solo career. He later joined Paul McCartney's Wings, becoming one of the most commercially successful sidemen in rock history. Laine's departure removed the last link to the band's hit-making R&B identity -- without his distinctive voice on "Go Now," the band had no commercial anchor and was free to become something entirely different.

### Hayward and Lodge Arrive

In the same month as Laine's departure, guitarist Justin Hayward (aged 19, formerly of Marty Wilde's backing band) and bassist John Lodge both joined. Hayward had previously sent demo tapes to Eric Burdon of the Animals; Burdon passed them to Mike Pinder, who invited Hayward to London.

Lodge's arrival closed a circle -- he had been an original member of El Riot & the Rebels with Thomas and Pinder but had declined to join the Moody Blues in 1964 because he was still in college. Now qualified as an engineer, he returned to music. The classic-era lineup was complete:

| Member | Role | Age at Joining | Background |
|--------|------|----------------|------------|
| Justin Hayward | Guitar / vocals | 19 | Marty Wilde's band; singer-songwriter |
| John Lodge | Bass / vocals | 21 | El Riot & the Rebels; engineering degree |
| Mike Pinder | Keys / Mellotron | 24 | El Riot & the Rebels; Streetly Electronics |
| Ray Thomas | Flute / vocals | 24 | El Riot & the Rebels |
| Graeme Edge | Drums | 24 | Gerry Levine & the Avengers |

## The Decca Commission and Its Subversion

By autumn 1967, the Moody Blues owed Decca Records several thousand pounds in unrecouped advances. Decca's Deram subsidiary was promoting a new "Deramic Stereo" recording format and needed a demonstration LP. The band was assigned to record a rock adaptation of Dvorak's *New World Symphony*.

### The Original Assignment

The Deramic Stereo format was Decca's answer to the emerging stereo market. They needed content that would showcase the format's wide-band reproduction capabilities -- orchestral music adapted by a rock band seemed ideal for demonstrating the dynamic range and spatial separation that Deramic Stereo could achieve.

### The Subversion

The band attempted the assignment but convinced producer Tony Clarke and engineer Derek Varnals that the Dvorak adaptation was misguided. Clarke, impressed by the band's original compositions depicting an archetypal day from morning to night, agreed to record original material instead. Decca executives were not informed until the album was completed.

Justin Hayward stated in 1990: "We all thought we were making some sort of limited-appeal art record" and expected an invitation to "a few sort of champagne parties."

This act of creative insubordination -- taking a corporate commission and delivering something completely different -- is the pivotal moment in the Moody Blues' history. Without the Deramic Stereo assignment, there would have been no budget for orchestral recording. Without the subversion, there would have been no *Days of Future Passed*.

## The Mellotron: History and Engineering

### Pinder's Background at Streetly Electronics

Mike Pinder had worked at Streetly Electronics, the Birmingham company that manufactured the Mellotron, before the band's formation. He could not afford to purchase one until 1967. The Mellotron is an electro-mechanical polyphonic tape-replay keyboard: pressing a key causes a pre-recorded magnetic tape to play. Each key has its own tape strip, approximately eight seconds long, returning to its starting position when released.

### Pinder's Modifications

Pinder's modification involved removing the instrument's left-hand rhythm/sound-effects tapes and replacing them with additional right-hand orchestral instrument recordings, effectively creating two banks of orchestral sounds. This was not a minor adjustment -- it fundamentally changed the instrument's capability from a novelty home organ into a proto-orchestral keyboard.

### The "Wave Sound" Engineering

Engineer Derek Varnals, working with Pinder and Clarke, further modified the playback to produce an overlapping "wave" sound rather than the instrument's characteristic sharp cutoff. This engineering decision -- removing the harsh tape-splice transient -- created the "orchestral wave" sound that defined the Moody Blues' sonic signature.

The Mellotron's inherent imperfection was key to its musical power. Each tape strip was a recording of a real musician, so every note carried the slight variations of human performance. The overlap technique turned individual notes into washes of sound that were neither keyboard nor orchestra -- they existed in a space between both. This "in-between" quality became the band's defining sonic characteristic.

### The Beatles Connection

Pinder also introduced the Mellotron to the Beatles. He called the group and sent four Mellotrons to their studio; the instrument's influence is audible on "Strawberry Fields Forever." Pinder and Thomas played harmonica on "I Am the Walrus" and "The Fool on the Hill." Pinder later contributed tambourine to John Lennon's *Imagine* sessions (on "I Don't Wanna Be a Soldier Mama").

## Days of Future Passed: Production and Reception

### The Album

*Days of Future Passed* was released on November 17, 1967 on Deram Records. The concept: a full day in the life of an ordinary person, from dawn to night, used as an allegory for the phases of human life itself. Each of the five songwriting members contributed original compositions. Edge contributed spoken-word poetry.

### Peter Knight and the London Festival Orchestra

Peter Knight, a staff composer at Decca, wrote and conducted the London Festival Orchestra interludes. Crucially, the London Festival Orchestra and the Moody Blues never played together simultaneously: the band recorded their tracks, Peter Knight wrote orchestral arrangements for the gaps, and the orchestra recorded its parts separately. This is a frequently misunderstood detail -- the "rock band with orchestra" sound was an illusion of production, not a live collaboration.

### Critical Reception

Initial critical reception was hostile:

| Source | Year | Assessment |
|--------|------|-----------|
| Rolling Stone | 1967 | "constantly marred by one of the most startlingly saccharine conceptions of beauty and mysticism" |
| New York magazine | 1967 | "a ponderous mound of thought-jello" |
| Rolling Stone | 2007 | Included in Essential Albums of 1967 list |
| Spin | 2007 | Cited as a classic of progressive rock |
| Rock and Roll Hall of Fame | 2018 | "one of the very first progressive rock albums" |

The album reached the UK Top 30 and performed modestly on initial release. Its rehabilitation took decades -- from "thought-jello" (1967) to essential album status (2007) to Rock Hall of Fame citation (2018). The critical arc of *Days of Future Passed* mirrors the band's own trajectory: dismissed, then slowly recognized as foundational.

### Graeme Edge's "Late Lament"

Edge's spoken-word poem "Late Lament" closes *Nights in White Satin* and the album. It explicitly asks what it means to exist -- working philosophically years before philosophy became a standard prog rock posture. The poem positions the day-as-allegory structure as cosmological: morning as birth, evening as age, night as death. This was not accident but design.

> **SAFETY: The "thought-jello" and "saccharine" quotes are attributed to their specific publications and years. They represent the critical consensus of 1967, not a current assessment. The critical rehabilitation documented here (Rolling Stone 2007, RRHOF 2018) represents the current scholarly position.**
