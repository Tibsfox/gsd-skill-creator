Ellensburg — 100 Miles Over the Cascades

The Screaming Trees are the engine's most geographically displaced major band. Ellensburg, Washington: population 20,000, seat of Central Washington University, high desert in the Kittitas Valley, east of the Cascades on Interstate 90. The drive crosses Snoqualmie Pass at 3,022 feet — the mountains that divide the wet west from the dry east, the Puget Sound climate from the Columbia Basin. Ellensburg is sagebrush and basalt, hot summers and cold winters, a landscape nothing like the rain-soaked neighborhoods where most of the engine's Seattle bands formed.

A Seattle band making grunge was responding to an immediate cultural environment. An Ellensburg band making music with the same weight and ambition chose to reach across the Cascades. The Screaming Trees chose to be in conversation with the Seattle scene from the outside.

"He's a young man / And he's nearly lost you." — "Nearly Lost You," 1992. Singles soundtrack. #1 Mainstream Rock.

Mark Lanegan (1964–2022)

Mark Lanegan was born November 25, 1964, in Ellensburg, Washington. He died February 22, 2022, in Killarney, Ireland, at age 57. His baritone voice — deep, worn, emotionally direct — is the defining instrument of the Screaming Trees and one of the most distinctive in Pacific Northwest music history. His lineage was not metal or punk but the blues, Johnny Cash, Roy Orbison, Neil Young. He brought a different American tradition into the Seattle grunge moment.

After the Screaming Trees, Lanegan built a solo catalog of extraordinary depth (beginning with The Winding Sheet, 1990, Sub Pop), collaborated with Queens of the Stone Age, Greg Dulli (The Gutter Twins), Isobel Campbell, and many others. He sang for Layne Staley at the 2011 Mad Season reunion shows — his voice was one of the few that could inhabit Staley's material without imitation. He wrote two memoirs: Sing Backwards and Weep (2020) and Devil in a Coma (2021).

"Nearly Lost You" and Sweet Oblivion

Sweet Oblivion (1992)

Epic Records

Produced by Don Fleming. The breakthrough album. Major-label resources applied to the Screaming Trees' core aesthetic. Lanegan at maximum expressiveness. The album that made the band known outside the underground.

"Nearly Lost You"

#1 Mainstream Rock

On the Singles (1992) soundtrack alongside Soundgarden, Pearl Jam, Alice in Chains, Mudhoney. Billboard Hot 100 #34. Mainstream Rock Tracks #1. Lee Conner's climbing riff + Lanegan's voice at full cry.

Dust (1996)

Epic Records

More atmospheric, more psychedelic. Jack Endino in the credits. The album the band wanted to make after Sweet Oblivion's success. "Gospel Plow" and "Sworn and Broken": Screaming Trees at full maturity.

SST Years (1987–1989)

Underground

Even If and Especially When, Invisible Lantern, Buzz Factory. SST = Black Flag's label, Hüsker Dü, Dinosaur Jr., Sonic Youth. The national underground context before the grunge moment.

Barrett Martin — The Human Bridge

Barrett Martin plays in both the Screaming Trees (degrees 305-307 context) and Mad Season (degrees 224 and 305). This makes him the human infrastructure connecting the two bands in the engine's current zone. The bridge is concrete: the same drummer, two bands, separated by two degrees (305 Mad Season, 307 Screaming Trees). Martin's jazz-adjacent approach to rock drumming — rhythms that breathe, dynamic control wide enough to serve both Lanegan's blues-rooted approach and Staley's grunge weight — is identifiable across both bands.
