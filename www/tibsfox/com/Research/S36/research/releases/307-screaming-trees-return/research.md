## ELLENSBURG -- THE GEOGRAPHIC ANOMALY

### 100 Miles East of Seattle, Over the Cascades

The Screaming Trees are the engine's most geographically displaced major band:

- **Ellensburg, Washington.** Population approximately 20,000. Located in the Kittitas Valley, east of the Cascades on Interstate 90. The drive from Ellensburg to Seattle crosses Snoqualmie Pass at 3,022 feet elevation -- the same pass that closes in winter, the same mountains that define the weather divide between the wet west and the dry east. Ellensburg is high desert: hot summers, cold winters, sagebrush and basalt, a landscape nothing like the rain-soaked Puget Sound lowlands.
- **Central Washington University.** Ellensburg is a college town -- the seat of Central Washington University. The Screaming Trees formed in the context of a small college town with a music scene large enough for ambition but small enough that serious musicians quickly outgrew it. The combination of CWU's student population and Ellensburg's geographic isolation produced a creative pressure that the Seattle bands, living in the center of the action, did not experience in the same way.
- **The Conner brothers.** Lee Conner (guitar) and Van Conner (bass) were the founding axis of the Screaming Trees. Both grew up in Ellensburg. Their guitar approach -- heavy, psychedelic-inflected, with roots in proto-punk and noise rock -- was formed in the Kittitas Valley, not on Capitol Hill or in the U-District.
- **Why Ellensburg matters.** A Seattle band making grunge was responding to an immediate cultural environment. An Ellensburg band making music with the same weight and ambition was making a deliberate decision to reach across the Cascades. The Screaming Trees chose to be in conversation with the Seattle scene from the outside -- and that position gave them a different perspective.

(Sources: Ellensburg WA geography; Kittitas Valley; Central Washington University; Snoqualmie Pass 3,022 feet; Conner brothers formation; geographic isolation as creative pressure; Cascades as cultural divide)

## THE BAND -- FORMATION AND PERSONNEL

### Four Instruments, One Voice Above Them All

The Screaming Trees lineup:

- **Mark Lanegan (vocals).** Born November 25, 1964, in Ellensburg, Washington. Died February 22, 2022, in Killarney, Ireland, at age 57. Lanegan's voice was the defining instrument of the Screaming Trees: a deep baritone with extraordinary emotional range, capable of bluesy intimacy and full-throated intensity in the same performance. His vocal approach was rooted in the blues and in classic American rock (Johnny Cash, Roy Orbison, Neil Young) -- sources that gave him a different lineage than the metal and punk influences that shaped most Seattle vocalists. The comparison to Jim Morrison was made often and was not entirely wrong; the comparison to Robert Plant was made occasionally and was partially right. But Lanegan was ultimately his own instrument.
- **Lee Conner (guitar).** The primary songwriter alongside Lanegan. Conner's guitar work draws from garage rock, proto-punk, psychedelia, and heavy rock -- a wide palette that gave the Screaming Trees their genre-straddling quality. He is the architect of the band's sound.
- **Van Conner (bass).** Lee's brother. Heavy, melodic bass playing that grounds the Screaming Trees' more exploratory guitar work. Van Conner died in January 2023 at age 55 -- one year after Lanegan.
- **Barrett Martin (drums).** The same Barrett Martin who played in Mad Season (degrees 224 and 305). Martin joined the Screaming Trees for *Sweet Oblivion* (1992) and *Dust* (1996), replacing previous drummer Mark Pickerel. His jazz-adjacent approach to rock drumming is identifiable across both bands. Martin is the human connection between Mad Season and the Screaming Trees in the engine.

(Sources: Mark Lanegan biography; born November 25 1964 Ellensburg; died February 22 2022 Killarney Ireland; age 57; Lee Conner guitar; Van Conner bass; Barrett Martin drums; Van Conner death January 2023 age 55; Barrett Martin Mad Season connection)

## "NEARLY LOST YOU" (1992) -- THE DEFINING SONG

### The Song That Almost Wasn't

"Nearly Lost You" is the Screaming Trees' most commercially successful and critically recognized single:

- **The recording context.** From *Sweet Oblivion* (1992, Epic Records). The album was produced by Don Fleming and produced a cleaner, more radio-ready sound than the band's SST-era records while retaining the core Screaming Trees aesthetic. Epic signed the band in the grunge moment -- post-Nirvana, labels were reaching for Pacific Northwest bands -- and the signing gave the Screaming Trees major-label production resources for the first time.
- **The song's structure.** "Nearly Lost You" is built on a climbing guitar riff that Lee Conner wrote at a moment of personal crisis. The song opens with a simple, repeated riff, adds Lanegan's voice at maximum expressiveness -- somewhere between a shout and a plea -- and builds through multiple repetitions to a cathartic release. The structure is simple; the performance is everything.
- **The Cameron Crowe connection.** "Nearly Lost You" appeared on the *Singles* (1992) soundtrack -- Cameron Crowe's film about Seattle's music scene. This placement was significant: the soundtrack included Soundgarden, Pearl Jam, Alice in Chains, Mudhoney, and the Screaming Trees. "Nearly Lost You" held its place in that company. The song reached a much wider audience through the *Singles* soundtrack than it would have through album release alone.
- **Lanegan's vocal.** The performance on "Nearly Lost You" is Lanegan at peak expressiveness. He is not shouting in the grunge mode; he is not singing in the pop mode. He is occupying the specific space between blues-influenced soul and rock power that defines his approach at its best. The vocal is the song.
- **Commercial performance.** "Nearly Lost You" peaked at #34 on the Billboard Hot 100. It reached #1 on the Mainstream Rock Tracks chart. For a band from Ellensburg, this was an extraordinary commercial achievement.

(Sources: Nearly Lost You; Sweet Oblivion 1992 Epic Records; Don Fleming production; Singles 1992 soundtrack; Cameron Crowe; Billboard Hot 100 #34; Mainstream Rock Tracks #1; Screaming Trees Singles soundtrack placement alongside Soundgarden Pearl Jam Alice in Chains Mudhoney)

## THE DISCOGRAPHY -- FROM SST TO EPIC

### A Career Across the Full Grunge Arc

The Screaming Trees' discography spans the complete arc from pre-grunge underground to post-grunge twilight:

- ***Other Worlds* (1985, Velvetone).** The debut. Recorded when the band members were teenagers. Raw, experimental, lo-fi. Not the Screaming Trees of *Sweet Oblivion*, but already the same restless energy.
- ***Even If and Especially When* (1987, SST Records).** The first SST album. SST was the most important American indie label of the 1980s -- Black Flag's label, home to Hüsker Dü, Dinosaur Jr., Sonic Youth. Being on SST in 1987 placed the Screaming Trees in conversation with the entire American underground. The album is heavier and more focused than the debut.
- ***Invisible Lantern* (1988, SST).** The second SST album. Growing confidence, more complex arrangements, Lanegan's voice beginning to reach its full expression.
- ***Buzz Factory* (1989, SST).** The final SST album. The band at maximum underground intensity before the Epic signing.
- ***Sweet Oblivion* (1992, Epic).** The breakthrough. Don Fleming production. "Nearly Lost You." The album that defined the Screaming Trees in the mainstream consciousness. Critical and commercial success by the standards of the scene.
- ***Dust* (1996, Epic).** The last studio album. More atmospheric, more psychedelic, less commercially oriented. Jack Endino in the production credits. The album that the band wanted to make after *Sweet Oblivion*'s success had opened the budget. Lanegan's voice at full maturity. "Gospel Plow" and "Sworn and Broken" are among the finest Screaming Trees recordings.
- ***Last Words: The Final Recordings* (2011).** Recorded in 2000, released 2011. The final studio sessions before the band's dissolution.

(Sources: Screaming Trees full discography; SST Records history; Other Worlds 1985; Even If and Especially When 1987; Invisible Lantern 1988; Buzz Factory 1989; Sweet Oblivion 1992; Dust 1996; Jack Endino; Last Words 2011)

## MARK LANEGAN -- THE SOLO CAREER AND LATER LIFE

### Beyond the Screaming Trees

Lanegan's post-Screaming Trees career was as substantial as his work with the band:

- **Mark Lanegan Band.** Beginning with *The Winding Sheet* (1990, Sub Pop) -- recorded while still in the Screaming Trees -- Lanegan built a solo catalog of extraordinary depth. *Whiskey for the Holy Ghost* (1994), *Scraps at Midnight* (1998), *I'll Take Care of You* (1999), and many subsequent albums. The solo work is darker, more intimate, more blues-rooted than the Screaming Trees recordings.
- **Queens of the Stone Age.** Lanegan contributed vocals to multiple Queens of the Stone Age albums -- most significantly *Songs for the Deaf* (2002) and *...Like Clockwork* (2013). His voice on "The Sky is Falling" and other QOTSA tracks demonstrates his range beyond the PNW scene into the broader landscape of heavy rock.
- **Mad Season 2011 reunion.** As noted at degrees 224 and 305, Lanegan sang for Layne Staley at the 2011 Mad Season reunion shows. His voice was one of the few that could inhabit Staley's material with authority. This speaks to the range and depth of Lanegan's instrument.
- **Collaborations.** Lanegan collaborated with Greg Dulli (The Afghan Whigs) as The Gutter Twins, with Isobel Campbell (Belle and Sebastian) on multiple albums, and with many other artists across genres. His collaborative range is wider than any other musician from the Seattle scene.
- **Death.** Mark Lanegan died February 22, 2022, in Killarney, Ireland, at age 57. He had been living in Ireland with his wife Shelley Brien. The cause of death was initially undisclosed; later revealed as COVID-19 complications and related illness. He had written extensively about his struggles with addiction and health in his memoirs *Sing Backwards and Weep* (2020) and *Devil in a Coma* (2021).

(Sources: Mark Lanegan Band discography; The Winding Sheet 1990 Sub Pop; Whiskey for the Holy Ghost 1994; Queens of the Stone Age collaborations; Songs for the Deaf 2002; Mad Season 2011 reunion; The Gutter Twins with Greg Dulli; Isobel Campbell collaborations; death February 22 2022 Killarney Ireland age 57; Sing Backwards and Weep 2020; Devil in a Coma 2021)

## THE SCREAMING TREES IN THE ENGINE

### Connecting Ellensburg to the Grunge Cluster

- **Geographic principle.** The Screaming Trees demonstrate that grunge was not exclusively a Seattle phenomenon -- it was a Pacific Northwest phenomenon with Seattle as its center of gravity. Ellensburg is 100 miles from Seattle, across the Cascades, in a different climate and a different cultural context. The band formed there and chose to engage with the Seattle scene from that position.
- **Barrett Martin bridge.** Martin plays in both the Screaming Trees (307) and Mad Season (305, 224). This makes him the human infrastructure connecting the two bands within the engine's current zone. The bridge is concrete: the same drummer, two bands, degrees 305 and 307.
- **The SST connection.** SST Records (Greg Ginn/Black Flag's label) is the American underground's most important label of the 1980s. The Screaming Trees' SST years connect the Pacific Northwest scene to the national American underground -- a connection that predates and contextualizes the grunge moment.

(Sources: Screaming Trees geographic principle; Ellensburg 100 miles from Seattle; grunge as PNW phenomenon; Barrett Martin bridge degrees 305-307; SST Records as national underground connection; Black Flag / Greg Ginn)
