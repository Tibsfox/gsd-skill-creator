## THE SONICS IN PROTO-PUNK GENEALOGY

### The Ancestry of Punk

The Sonics belong to the tradition of bands that pre-figured punk before punk had a name or a cultural identity:

- **What proto-punk means.** Proto-punk refers to bands active in the 1960s whose music shares essential characteristics with 1970s punk: fast tempos, raw distorted guitar, aggressive vocals, short songs, an attitude of confrontational energy rather than technical refinement. The Sonics are among the defining examples. Others include the early Kinks, the Stooges (Iggy Pop), the MC5, the New York Dolls, and the Velvet Underground.
- **The Sonics' specific proto-punk attributes.** What makes The Sonics proto-punk rather than simply "loud garage rock":
  - The deliberate embrace of distortion at a time when distortion was considered poor technique or equipment failure. The Sonics heard distortion as a sound, not a problem.
  - Gerry Roslie's vocals -- screaming, raw, at the edge of control. He is not singing; he is attacking the microphone. This vocal approach is the direct ancestor of punk screaming.
  - The tempo. Songs like "Psycho" and "Have Love Will Travel" move at tempos that are closer to punk than to most contemporary pop or rock.
  - The attitude. The Sonics were not attempting to make pleasant music. They were attempting to make the loudest, most aggressive sound they could produce. This is punk's core aesthetic principle, expressed six to ten years before punk.
- **The Stooges and the MC5.** The Sonics pre-dated both. Iggy Pop has cited early 1960s garage rock as a formative influence. The raw-energy tradition that The Sonics embodied in 1965 flowed through the Stooges into the New York punk scene (Ramones, Television, Patti Smith) of the 1970s. The Sonics are not isolated phenomena -- they are part of a continuum.
- **The British connection.** When British punk bands of the 1977 generation began to identify their American predecessors, the 1960s garage tradition -- including The Sonics -- was in the mix. The Ramones are often cited as the first punk band, but the Ramones themselves had predecessors. The Sonics are upstream of the Ramones in the genealogy.

## THE MUSIC: WHAT THEY ACTUALLY PLAYED

### The Songs That Define the Proto-Punk Argument

The Sonics' catalog is the evidence for the proto-punk designation:

- **"Have Love Will Travel" (1965).** One of the most covered songs in the history of rock and roll. The song has been covered by hundreds of bands across six decades -- from the Troggs in the 1960s to the Black Keys in the 21st century. The song structure is simple: a riff, a verse, a riff, a verse. The simplicity is not a limitation -- it is the point. The riff is everything. The urgency is everything. This is proto-punk in 1965.
- **"Psycho" (1965).** Gerry Roslie screaming over a churning distorted guitar. The lyrics are an ominous first-person catalog of antisocial behavior. "Psycho" was too intense for most radio stations in 1965. It is a proto-punk standard. When punk bands needed a cover to demonstrate their aggression to audiences, "Psycho" was in the repertoire.
- **"The Witch" (1965).** Another garage punk standard. The organ riff is eerie, the vocals aggressive, the production deliberately raw. The organ is not being played to add atmospheric texture; it is being played as a confrontational instrument.
- **"Strychnine" (1965).** More deliberate weirdness. The lyrics are about toxic substances. The song is fast, distorted, wrong-sounding by contemporary standards -- and exactly right for what it was trying to be.
- **"Boss Hoss" (1965).** Pure aggression on the American muscle car mythology. The Sonics were not romanticizing car culture; they were weaponizing it.
- **The overall catalog.** The Sonics released two studio albums (Here Are the Sonics, 1965; Boom, 1966) and various singles through Etiquette Records. The catalog is consistent: raw, distorted, fast, aggressive, slightly unhinged.

(Sources: The Sonics discography; "Have Love Will Travel" cover history; "Psycho" analysis; Here Are the Sonics 1965; Boom 1966; Etiquette Records)

## ETIQUETTE RECORDS AND THE TACOMA CONTEXT

### An Independent Label in Tacoma

Etiquette Records was a small independent label based in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Etiquette Records.** Founded by Buck Ormsby and Rockin' Robin Roberts, Etiquette Records was the home of several Tacoma-area bands in the early to mid-1960s. The label's catalog documents a specific moment in Pacific Northwest music: the pre-grunge, pre-Sub Pop era when Tacoma was producing some of the most aggressive rock and roll in America.
- **Tacoma vs. Seattle.** Tacoma and Seattle are 30 miles apart but have historically had distinct music scenes. Tacoma's music culture in the 1960s was more working-class, more physically aggressive, less attached to cultural pretension than Seattle's. The Sonics' music reflects this: there is nothing fashionable about "Psycho." It is just as loud and aggressive as they could make it.
- **The Puget Sound garage tradition.** The Sonics were part of a broader Northwest garage rock tradition that included the Wailers (also on Etiquette), the Kingsmen (Portland), and later the Ventures (Tacoma). These bands defined a Northwest sound in the 1960s that was simultaneously American and regionally specific. The engine has documented this tradition across multiple degrees (241-244 in particular).
- **Limited national distribution.** Etiquette Records had limited distribution outside the Pacific Northwest. The Sonics were influential regionally and eventually achieved national recognition retrospectively, but during their original run they were primarily a regional band with a cult following in the Northwest.

(Sources: Etiquette Records history; Buck Ormsby biography; Rockin' Robin Roberts; Tacoma music culture 1960s; Wailers and Sonics connection; Northwest garage tradition; national vs. regional distribution)

## THE RETROSPECTIVE RECOGNITION

### When the World Caught Up

The Sonics were not appreciated nationally during their original run (1960-1968), but the subsequent six decades have been kind to their reputation:

- **The punk generation's discovery.** When the punk movement of the 1970s began identifying its American predecessors, the 1960s garage tradition received reappraisal. The Sonics were among the bands that punk musicians named as formative influences. This retrospective recognition confirmed what the band had always been: too far ahead of their time to be commercially successful in 1965, but historically correct.
- **Compilation appearances.** The Sonics appeared on garage rock and proto-punk compilations through the 1970s, 1980s, and 1990s, consistently introduced to new generations of listeners who recognized what the band had been doing decades earlier.
- **The 2015 reformation.** The Sonics reformed in 2015 for the first time in decades and released new material, demonstrating that the band's approach had not aged out of relevance. A band playing essentially the same music they played in 1965 was still capable of producing material that felt vital.
- **Critical reassessment.** Music critics have increasingly placed The Sonics in the front rank of 1960s American rock -- not as a footnote to the British Invasion or a regional curiosity, but as originators of an approach to rock music that became the foundation for punk. This reassessment is correct.

(Sources: Proto-punk compilation appearances; punk generation discovery of garage rock; The Sonics 2015 reformation; This Is the Sonics 2015; critical reassessment sources; Allmusic and Rolling Stone retrospective reviews)

### The Tacoma Forefathers

- **Proto-punk before the term existed.** The Sonics deserve recognition as proto-punk originators precisely because they had no cultural framework to tell them what they were doing. They were not self-consciously making punk -- they were making the loudest, most aggressive music they could, following their own aesthetic instincts. The retroactive recognition validates those instincts.
- **"Have Love Will Travel" as cultural object.** The song's 60-year history as a cover standard is evidence of its compositional quality. Songs that get covered for six consecutive decades have achieved something real. The simplicity of the formula is the genius: riff, urgency, velocity.
