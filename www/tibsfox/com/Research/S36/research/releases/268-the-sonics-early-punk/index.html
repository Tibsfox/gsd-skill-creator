Proto-Punk Before the Term Existed

In 1965, The Sonics were playing music that would not have a cultural home for another decade. Deliberate distortion (a sound, not an equipment failure). Gerry Roslie screaming (not singing). Fast tempos. Short songs. The attitude of confrontational energy over technical refinement. This is punk’s core aesthetic principle — in Tacoma, Washington, in 1965.

“Have Love Will Travel.” “Psycho.” “The Witch.” “Strychnine.” The catalog is the argument.

The Proto-Punk Genealogy

The Sonics belong to the tradition of bands that pre-figured punk before punk had a name. The others: the early Kinks, the Stooges (Iggy Pop), the MC5, the New York Dolls, the Velvet Underground. The Sonics are among the earliest in this tradition — 1965, not 1967 or 1969. They predated the bands that would predated the Ramones.

Iggy Pop has cited early 1960s garage rock as formative. The raw-energy tradition that The Sonics embodied in 1965 flowed through the Stooges into the New York punk scene of the early 1970s, into the Ramones, into British punk of 1977. The line is traceable. The Sonics are upstream.

"They were not attempting to make pleasant music. They were attempting to make the loudest, most aggressive sound they could produce. This is punk's core aesthetic principle, expressed six to ten years before punk."

The Songs: The Evidence

"Have Love Will Travel" (1965)

Etiquette Records

One of the most covered songs in rock history. Hundreds of covers across six decades. Riff, urgency, velocity. The formula is simple because the formula is everything. Proto-punk in 1965.

"Psycho" (1965)

Etiquette Records

Gerry Roslie screaming over churning distorted guitar. Too intense for most radio in 1965. A proto-punk standard for every aggressive band that needed a cover. The song that makes the argument for E=8.

"The Witch" (1965)

Etiquette Records

Eerie organ riff, aggressive vocals, deliberately raw production. The organ is a confrontational instrument here. Proto-punk's use of keyboard as aggression rather than decoration.

"Strychnine" (1965)

Etiquette Records

Fast, distorted, lyrically weird. Toxic substance as lyrical subject. Wrong-sounding by contemporary standards; exactly right for what it was trying to be.

Etiquette Records and the Tacoma Scene

Etiquette Records, founded by Buck Ormsby and Rockin’ Robin Roberts, documented a specific moment in Pacific Northwest music: the pre-grunge, pre-Sub Pop era when Tacoma was producing some of the most aggressive rock and roll in America. The Sonics, the Wailers, and the broader Northwest garage tradition were all rooted in Tacoma’s more working-class, more physically direct musical culture.

The national distribution was limited during the original run. The Sonics were primarily a regional band during their 1960-1968 active period. The world caught up later.
