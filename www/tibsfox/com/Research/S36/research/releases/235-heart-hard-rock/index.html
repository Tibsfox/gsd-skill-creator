Heart at E=7 — The Founding Identity

Before "Alone" and "These Dreams," before the Capitol Records commercial peak, Heart was a hard rock band. A genuinely hard rock band: heavy guitar riffs, aggressive rhythm section, Ann Wilson's voice deployed at full throttle over arrangements that matched or exceeded any male hard rock act of the era.

Degree 235 is the Heart of "Crazy on You," "Magic Man," and "Barracuda." The Mushroom and early Portrait years. The Bellevue girls from Vancouver who walked into hard rock's male-dominated territory and played harder than the men around them. E=7. The founding identity.

"The Barracuda riff is not a riff about anger — it is anger. Nancy Wilson wrote it in response to specific, justified outrage at Mushroom Records. The fury is authentic. That's why it holds."

The Early Albums

Dreamboat Annie (1975 / 1976 US)

Mushroom Records

The debut. "Magic Man" and "Crazy on You." Hard rock and folk together. US chart: #7. The Pacific Northwest in the music.

Little Queen (1977)

Portrait Records

The most consistent hard rock statement. "Barracuda" leads. US chart: #9. Released during the Mushroom Records lawsuit. The hard rock E=7 Heart at full force.

Magazine (1978)

Mushroom / Portrait

Complicated by the label dispute — unauthorized and authorized versions. "Heartless" and "Devil Delight." Strong hard rock material despite the context.

Dog and Butterfly (1978)

Portrait Records

Side one (Dog) = hard rock. Side two (Butterfly) = softer. The internal range that points toward the later pop era. US chart: #17.

Ann Wilson at E=7

Ann Wilson's hard rock vocal is the primary document of what E=7 means in this engine. The final chorus of "Crazy on You" — the extended, held notes over the full band explosion — is a demonstration of a voice deployed at maximum capacity. Not performing maximum; actually at maximum. The Robert Plant comparison is accurate: both singers treat the rock vocal as theater and incantation, not entertainment. The Janis Joplin lineage is direct: raw emotional truth over technical refinement.

The difference between Ann Wilson at E=6 ("Alone," "These Dreams") and at E=7 ("Barracuda," "Crazy on You") is real and audible. At E=6, the voice is controlled and placed. At E=7, the control is still present but the emotional deployment is full. The voice goes to its limits.

The Mushroom Records Incident

Mushroom Records — Heart's original label — created promotional materials falsely implying Ann and Nancy Wilson were in a romantic relationship. A cynical marketing calculation. Heart's response was "Barracuda." The jabbing riff, the accusatory vocal, the fury in the production: not a complaint, a song. One of the great rock songs. The anger is authentic, and authentic anger makes great hard rock.
