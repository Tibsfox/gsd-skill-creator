## What Worked

1. **The "earliest" claim is historically defensible.** The archival evidence places Solger at the beginning of Seattle's hardcore scene -- 1980, before The Fartz reached their distributional peak, before any commercial alternative music infrastructure in Seattle. The claim is careful (saying "among the earliest, possibly the earliest") rather than absolute.
2. **The absence of documentation as evidence is a genuine analytical observation.** The difficulty of finding Solger recordings tells us something real about the conditions under which early Seattle hardcore existed. This is a legitimate historical argument.
3. **Paul Solger cross-membership connects degrees 270 and 271 biographically.** The same musician in adjacent degrees at adjacent historical moments is one of the engine's cleanest cross-references.
4. **The independent/self-released context distinguishes Solger from The Fartz.** The Fartz had Alternative Tentacles; Solger had nothing but the punk underground network. The distinction is meaningful.

## Discoveries

1. **The community-as-continuity argument.** When a scene is small, cross-membership is not dilettantism -- it is the natural result of having more musical ideas than musicians. Paul Solger's presence in both bands is a map of a community, not a catalog of individual projects.
2. **The parallel development point.** Solger was not responding to Black Flag or the Dead Kennedys -- they were developing simultaneously, in parallel, driven by the same musical and social impulse. This distinguishes them from later Seattle bands that were more clearly responding to prior California models.
