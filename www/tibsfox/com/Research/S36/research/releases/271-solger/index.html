Earliest Seattle Hardcore — 1980

Solger are among the earliest — possibly the earliest — Seattle hardcore band. Active 1980-1981, before The Fartz reached their peak, before Alternative Tentacles, before any commercial Seattle punk infrastructure existed.

Paul Solger (guitar) gives the band its name — and also appears as a member of The Fartz (degree 270). The same musician in both bands. The Seattle hardcore scene of 1980-1981 was small enough that this is not surprising: it is a community, not a roster.

The Raw Beginning

Solger’s recordings are harder to access than The Fartz’s. This is meaningful. The absence of documentation — no major label, no national distribution, recordings on cheap tape — is not failure. It is the condition of being first. The most raw artifacts of any scene are those produced before the infrastructure exists to distribute them.

The recordings that survive are lo-fi even by hardcore standards: minimal production, maximum velocity, confrontational vocals. This is philosophically appropriate. Hardcore was in part a rejection of the expensive, polished production of arena rock. Solger’s rough recordings are not accidental; they are the point.

“Being first matters in rock history. Solger being among the first Seattle hardcore bands means the entire subsequent history of Seattle’s alternative music scene has a line — however indirect — that runs back through them.”

The Paul Solger Connection

Paul Solger’s presence in both Solger (guitar) and The Fartz (bass) is the engine’s most direct biographical link between degrees 270 and 271. Adjacent engine degrees, adjacent historical moments, the same musician in both bands. The cross-membership is a map of the community: when a scene is small, musicians appear in multiple configurations. This is not dilettantism — it is what happens when there are more musical ideas than available musicians.

The cross-membership also shows how the early Seattle hardcore scene was structured: Paul Solger was a founding figure in both the earliest band (Solger) and the slightly later, more distributed band (The Fartz). The scene grew through the actions of a small number of committed people with overlapping memberships and shared commitments.

1980 in American Hardcore

Solger’s formation places them in the first wave of American hardcore. In 1980, Black Flag was touring the country with Nervous Breakdown, the Dead Kennedys had just released Fresh Fruit for Rotting Vegetables, and Minor Threat was forming in Washington D.C. The American hardcore explosion was simultaneous and geographically distributed: Los Angeles, San Francisco, Washington D.C., Boston, Portland, Seattle. Solger emerged in parallel with these developments — not as followers, but as independent generators of the same musical impulse.

The Independent Context

Self-Released (1980)

No Label

No Alternative Tentacles, no Subterranean, no Dischord. Independent, self-released, distributed through the punk underground by hand. Maximum DIY.

Pre-Infrastructure Seattle

Before Sub Pop

Sub Pop not founded until 1986. No commercial Seattle alt-music infrastructure in 1980. The scene was building itself from nothing.

West Coast Network

Maximum Rocknroll

The distribution network: Maximum Rocknroll fanzine, mail order, touring, hand-to-hand exchange. A band in Seattle could reach San Francisco and New York through these channels.
