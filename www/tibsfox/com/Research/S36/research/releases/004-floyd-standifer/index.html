Biography & Central District Roots

Floyd Standifer was born in 1929 in Wilmington, North Carolina. After World War II, he moved to Seattle and settled in the Central District — the same neighborhood that had shaped Quincy Jones (degree 0°) and Ernestine Anderson (degree 3°). He became a fixture on the Jackson Street jazz corridor — not a transit player using the scene as a launching pad, but a permanent resident who made the corridor his home base for decades.

Standifer was a true multi-instrumentalist: trumpet, saxophone, and vocals. His trumpet tone was warm and full, rooted in swing but capable of the precision bebop demanded. He played every section of a small ensemble — leading from the front (trumpet), filling from the middle (saxophone), or communicating directly with audiences through lyrics (voice). The first multi-instrumentalist in the S36 engine.

Sources: HistoryLink.org, Essay #20023; Paul de Barros, Jackson Street After Hours (1993); Seattle Times obituary, January 2007; UW Music Department archives

The Teacher: University of Washington & “Jazz by Standifer”

Standifer joined the University of Washington music faculty, teaching jazz history, jazz theory, and performance. His courses were not academic abstraction — when he taught swing rhythm, he was describing something he had played thousands of times in clubs where the rhythm section was ten feet away and the audience was dancing. When he taught bebop vocabulary, he was teaching patterns learned on the bandstand, not transcribed from recordings in a practice room.

His “Jazz by Standifer” sessions blurred the line between concert and class: Standifer would play, explain what he was playing, demonstrate the technique, trace the lineage of a phrase back to its source, and play again. The audience heard the music and understood it simultaneously. By the 1980s and 1990s, with the Jackson Street corridor demolished by urban renewal, these sessions kept the corridor’s musical language alive — not as a museum piece but as a living practice demonstrated by someone who had been there.

Career Arc

Late 1940s — Arrival & Jackson Street

Moved to Seattle post-WWII. Settled in the Central District. Joined the Jackson Street jazz corridor as a working musician — trumpet, saxophone, vocals. Became a permanent fixture on the scene.

1950s–1970s — Central District Scene

Regular performer on the Central District club circuit. Played with Quincy Jones during Jones’s Seattle years, Ernestine Anderson, Overton Berry, and the core of Seattle’s jazz community. Held his own in cutting sessions with visiting national artists. Chose Seattle over a touring career.

UW Faculty — Jazz Educator

Joined the University of Washington music faculty. Taught jazz history, theory, and performance. Built curriculum bridging the oral tradition of Jackson Street with academic music study. Created the “Jazz by Standifer” performance-education format.

1980s–2000s — Elder Statesman & Radio

Performances broadcast on KPLU (now KNKX). Provided commentary for local television and documentary segments on Seattle jazz history. Became the city’s primary jazz interpreter — the person media producers called when they needed Seattle jazz explained.

January 2007

Died in Seattle. More than fifty years in the Central District. One of the last direct links to the Jackson Street corridor’s living tradition.

Music Theory Mapping

Four theory nodes — two extending existing branches (swing rhythm, chromaticism) and two opening new branches (brass technique, jazz pedagogy). Degree 4 is the first to introduce pedagogy itself as a theory contribution.

Swing Rhythm & Triplet Feel

Intermediate

Asymmetric eighth-note subdivision (approximately 2:1 long-short). The triplet model is theoretical; the reality is a fluid range navigated by ear. Standifer made implicit swing knowledge explicit — articulating the mechanics that most musicians learn only through immersion.

Jazz by Standifer sessions, Central District club recordings
From 3°: Anderson = how swing feels. Standifer = how swing works. Complementary.

Brass Articulation & Embouchure

Intermediate

Embouchure (lip/jaw/muscle configuration) determines tone, range, endurance. Articulation (tonguing technique) determines note onset. Standifer’s warm, lyrical trumpet tone prioritized melodic clarity over pyrotechnics. Body as sound source — trumpet lips vibrate like vocal cords.

Jazz by Standifer trumpet demonstrations, UW faculty recitals
New at 4°: First brass theory node. Body-as-sound-source shared with vocal theory (3°).

Bebop Vocabulary

Advanced

Chromatic approach notes (half-step above/below target chord tone) and enclosure patterns (target approached from both sides). Creates bebop’s characteristic zigzag contour. Standifer taught these as explicit, repeatable method — deconstructing patterns over ii-V-I progressions. Chromaticism as curriculum.

Standifer trumpet solos, Jazz by Standifer bebop demonstrations
Thread: Jones (bass) → Frisell (upper) → Anderson (voice) → Standifer (codified method)

Jazz Educator as Bridge

Advanced

Translation of implicit musical knowledge into explicit, teachable form. Fifth position on the composition-improvisation spectrum: pedagogy. The educator’s output is not performance or composition but understanding — the student’s ability to generate their own music because they possess the theoretical framework.

Jazz by Standifer format (performance + explanation), UW curricula
Spectrum: Jones (compose) → Horvitz (compose+improv) → Frisell (improv) → Anderson (interpret) → Standifer (teach)

The Teacher Pattern

The S36 engine has tracked four artist-city relationships through degrees 0–3. Standifer introduces the fifth: The Teacher. This is not the same as The Stay. Anderson stayed and performed. Standifer stayed and taught. The distinction is output: the performer’s output is the performance itself, experienced in real time. The teacher’s output is the student — a living carrier of knowledge who will produce their own performances, teach their own students, and extend the chain.

Carry-Forward from Degrees 0°–3°

  • Central District thread: Fifth degree, fourth artist directly connected. Standifer is the third primary Jackson Street participant (after Jones and Anderson). The geographic concentration is now five degrees deep.
  • Chromaticism thread: Four deployments — Jones (bass-line), Frisell (upper-register), Anderson (embodied/vocal), Standifer (codified/melodic). Two intuitive, two systematic.
  • Composition-improvisation spectrum: Five positions — composition (Jones), composed improvisation (Horvitz), free improvisation (Frisell), interpretation (Anderson), pedagogy (Standifer).
  • Artist-as-institution spectrum: Five positions — industry (Jones), community (Horvitz), performer (Frisell), presence (Anderson), educator (Standifer).
  • Body-as-sound-source: New thread connecting brass (trumpet lips) and vocal (vocal cords). Degrees 3 and 4 share the embodied-instrument principle.
