The Modified Instruments

The Presidents of the United States of America play modified instruments. This is not a gimmick — it is the engineering solution to a specific creative problem.

The Basitar — Chris Ballew

Two bass strings on a guitar neck and body. Plays like bass, like guitar, like both simultaneously. The low-mid frequency presence that defines the Presidents' sound. Built by Ballew because the instrument he needed didn't exist.

The Guitbass — Dave Dederer

Three-string guitar. Melody and upper-mid range, complementing the basitar without competing with it. Together, basitar and guitbass create a midrange-forward sonic landscape that is immediately recognizable.

Drums — Jason Finn

Already in the engine at degree 221 (Love Battery). Finn co-founded PUSA while still in Love Battery. Punchy, precise, propulsive. The Love Battery-PUSA cross-reference closes here at degree 238.

The Debut — The Million-Seller

The self-titled debut (1995, Columbia Records) sold over one million copies. Songs about fruit, cats, and a woman floating in a bog. At the peak of grunge’s commercial dominance, a band with fewer-than-standard-string instruments and a commitment to songs about peaches went platinum.

Lump

Radio Hit

A woman atop a floating mound in a swampy bog. Surreal, catchy, inexplicable. Three minutes. The album opener and one of the defining alt-rock songs of 1995.

Peaches

Cultural Artifact

About eating peaches. Moving to the country. No irony. Genuinely enthusiastic about peaches. One of the catchiest choruses of the decade.

Kitty

Alt Radio Hit

About a cat. The cat is on the computer. A man’s affection for his cat, three minutes, alternative radio. During grunge’s peak. A hit.

II (1996)

Columbia

“Mach 5” as standout. Second album under commercial pressure. Consistent aesthetic. Slightly more varied. “Volcano.”

Eastlake — New Neighborhood in the Engine

Eastlake runs along Lake Union’s east shore, between Capitol Hill and Fremont. Working-class, water-adjacent, practical. Not the bohemian density of Capitol Hill, not the collegiate energy of the U-District. Ballew built the basitar in Eastlake. The neighborhood’s practical inventiveness is embodied in that instrument: a working musician solving a problem with available materials.

PUSA brings Eastlake into the engine for the first time. Adjacent to Capitol Hill but a different character entirely — and a very different band.
