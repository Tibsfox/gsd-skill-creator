## THE BAND -- ORIGIN AND UNUSUAL INSTRUMENTS

### Chris Ballew's Vision and the Modified Instrument Philosophy

The Presidents of the United States of America have a founding philosophy that is immediately audible in their music:

- **Chris Ballew (bass/vocals).** Ballew is the primary creative force and the band's most unusual musician. He plays a "basitar" -- a modified instrument with two bass strings and a standard guitar neck/body. The basitar is strung with two bass strings, played with a pick, and functions as a rhythmic-melodic hybrid instrument. The result sounds like bass and guitar simultaneously, with a specific low-mid frequency characteristic that became the band's sonic signature. Ballew built and modified the instrument himself.
- **Dave Dederer (guitar/vocals).** Dederer plays a "guitbass" -- a three-string guitar, standard guitar body but with three strings (typically tuned to handle melody and low end simultaneously). The guitbass gives the Presidents their guitar sound: slightly detuned, slightly unusual, never quite conventional. Like the basitar, it's a modification that creates a specific sonic world.
- **Jason Finn (drums).** Finn was already in the engine at degree 221 (Love Battery), where the text noted he was drumming for Love Battery while simultaneously co-founding PUSA. This is that PUSA entry. Finn's drumming in PUSA is punchy, precise, and rhythm-forward -- he locks the melodic chaos of Ballew and Dederer into propulsive songs.
- **The three-piece logic.** A standard rock trio (guitar-bass-drums) typically divides roles cleanly: guitar handles melody and chords, bass handles low end, drums handle rhythm. The Presidents' modified instruments collapse and blur these roles -- the basitar does things a guitar would do; the guitbass does things a bass would do. The result is a sonic inversion: the three-piece sounds strangely full because the instruments are functioning outside their standard roles.

(Sources: Chris Ballew basitar construction; Dave Dederer guitbass; Jason Finn biography; modified instrument philosophy; PUSA three-piece configuration; sound analysis)

## THE EASTLAKE NEIGHBORHOOD

### Between Capitol Hill and Lake Union

- **Eastlake.** Eastlake is a Seattle neighborhood running along the eastern shore of Lake Union, roughly between Capitol Hill to the south/east and Fremont to the north. It is a residential neighborhood with water access, a working-class feel (houseboats, boat yards, small businesses along Eastlake Avenue East) and proximity to the University of Washington Medical Center. It is quieter than Capitol Hill, less obviously bohemian, but has historically housed working musicians and artists who wanted proximity to Seattle's music scenes without the Capitol Hill density.
- **The PUSA-Eastlake connection.** Ballew lived and worked in the Eastlake area during the band's formation. The neighborhood's working-class water-adjacent character is consistent with the band's aesthetic: not precious, not self-consciously arty, just inventive and direct. The basitar was not invented in a Capitol Hill art loft -- it was built by a working musician who wanted the sound he heard in his head and couldn't get from a standard instrument.
- **Eastlake's position in the engine.** The engine has covered Capitol Hill extensively (degrees 213-221 and now 237). Eastlake is adjacent but distinct. The Presidents bring Eastlake into the engine for the first time: a neighborhood that produces unusual, self-invented music that doesn't fit the dominant scene's narrative.

## THE DEBUT ALBUM -- THE MILLION-SELLER

### "Lump," "Peaches," "Kitty"

The Presidents of the United States of America self-titled debut (1995, Columbia Records) is one of the most unusual platinum albums in the history of alternative rock:

- ***Presidents of the United States of America* (1995, Columbia).** The album was recorded and released at the height of the post-Nirvana commercial boom for Seattle alternative rock. A record label executive (Dan Perlman at PopLlama, then a Columbia deal) recognized that the band's combination of extreme catchiness, genuine weirdness, and family-friendly humor could reach a wide audience. The album sold over 1 million copies in the US -- going platinum.
- **"Lump."** The album's opening track and one of the band's signature songs. "Lump" is three minutes of melodic alt-rock about a woman who sits atop a floating mound in a swampy bog. The lyrics are surreal, the tune is inescapably catchy, the basitar-guitbass-drums arrangement is deceptively simple. The meaning of "Lump" has been discussed and debated -- Ballew has suggested it is about a feeling of inertia, about not wanting to move. The song became a radio hit without trying to be one.
- **"Peaches."** A song about eating peaches and moving to the country. Musically simple, instrumentally odd, lyrically committed to its absurdist premise. The chorus is one of the catchiest in 1990s alternative rock. It was written quickly; it became a defining song of the decade. There is no irony in "Peaches" -- it is genuinely enthusiastic about peaches.
- **"Kitty."** A song about a cat. The cat is on the computer. The cat wants to be a kitty all day long. The song is three minutes of melodic guitar pop about a man's affection for his cat. It became a genuine alternative radio hit. In the middle of the grunge era, when the dominant mode was angst and the dominant topic was alienation, the Presidents wrote a hit song about their cat.
- **"Boll Weevil," "Naked and Famous," "Feather Pluckin'."** Other highlights of the debut that demonstrate the band's consistent aesthetic: melodic, rhythmically propulsive, lyrically committed to unusual topics, never quite normal.
- **The debut's context.** The debut sold because it was different from everything around it. The grunge era had produced enormous commercial success but also enormous seriousness -- Pearl Jam was anguished, Soundgarden was metal-heavy, Alice in Chains was dark. The Presidents were none of these things. They were fun. In the mid-1990s alt-rock landscape, fun was a counterculture move.

(Sources: PUSA self-titled debut 1995; Columbia Records; "Lump" lyrical analysis; "Peaches" writing history; "Kitty" radio play; debut commercial context; grunge era contrast)

## THE SECOND ALBUM AND COMMERCIAL PEAK

### "Mach 5," "Volcano," "Naked and Famous"

- ***II* (1996, Columbia).** The second album appeared one year after the debut's success. It was recorded under pressure -- the band had to follow a million-selling debut. *II* is slightly more varied, slightly more polished, but consistent in aesthetic. "Mach 5" is the standout track: a propulsive, relentless alt-rock song that was one of the better singles of the mid-1990s. "Volcano" is a meditation on geological forces; "Lunatic to Love" is a return to the unusual-topic comedy of the debut.
- **The commercial plateau.** *II* sold well but not as dramatically as the debut. This is typical: a debut that achieves platinum status from a standing start is followed by a second album with a built-in audience but also built-in expectations. The Presidents didn't change the formula, but the initial surprise effect was spent.
- **The hiatus and reunions.** The original run of the band ended in 1998 when Ballew and Dederer decided to pursue other projects. The hiatus was not acrimonious. The band reunited in 2004, recorded *Love Everybody* (2004), and has continued to record and tour intermittently since. The reunion albums are less commercially significant than the original run but demonstrate that the creative core of the band -- Ballew's modified instrument philosophy, the three-piece melodic weirdness -- remains intact.

(Sources: PUSA *II* 1996; "Mach 5"; PUSA 1998 hiatus; 2004 reunion; *Love Everybody* 2004; ongoing activity)

## THE MODIFIED INSTRUMENT PHILOSOPHY IN DEPTH

### Building the Sound You Hear in Your Head

The basitar and guitbass are not gimmicks -- they are the result of a specific creative problem:

- **The problem Ballew was solving.** Ballew wanted to play in a band with only guitar and drums -- no separate bass player. He wanted the low end of bass with the melodic capability of guitar, in a single instrument. Standard bass is too melodically constrained; standard guitar lacks low-end authority. The solution was to combine them: take a guitar neck and body, string it with two bass strings, and play it as both simultaneously. The basitar is the engineering solution to a specific sonic requirement.
- **The guitbass solves a related problem.** With the basitar handling the low-mid frequency range, Dederer's guitar needed to fill different frequency territory. A standard guitar would clash with the basitar's low-mid presence. The guitbass -- strung with three strings, tuned to cover melody and upper-mid range -- complements the basitar rather than competing with it. The instruments were designed together, in relationship with each other.
- **The DIY tradition.** The Presidents' instrument modifications place them in a long tradition of DIY instrument construction: Les Paul modifying guitars in his New Jersey garage; Bo Diddley building his rectangular guitar; the experimental instrument builders of the avant-garde tradition. Ballew's basitar is a working musician's engineering project -- built to solve a specific problem, not to be conceptual.
- **The sonic consequences.** The basitar's two bass strings produce a specific frequency response: lower than a guitar, higher than a standard bass, with a midrange presence that sits differently in a mix than either. The guitbass produces a slightly thin, slightly bright guitar sound (three strings versus six) that has more space in the upper registers. Combined, the two instruments create a midrange-forward sonic landscape that is unusual, immediately recognizable, and distinctive.

(Sources: Chris Ballew basitar construction; guitbass engineering; DIY instrument modification tradition; frequency analysis; recording and live sound implications)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### The Anti-Grunge That Conquered Grunge Radio

- **Comedy as resistance.** In a Seattle scene dominated by angst, the Presidents' comedy was a genuine aesthetic choice, not a commercial calculation. Ballew's modified instruments, the songs about fruit and cats, the lack of any identifiable existential crisis -- these were countercultural moves in a grunge landscape. The success proved that the audience was broader than grunge's self-serious default.
- **The modified instrument legacy.** The basitar and guitbass are not widely imitated (they're hard to play well), but they demonstrate a principle: the instrument you need may not exist yet, so build it. This DIY philosophy is one of the most important threads in Seattle's music history, running from Kurt Cobain's cobbled-together guitars through to Ballew's bass-string guitar hybrid.
- **"Lump" as a cultural artifact.** "Lump" is one of those songs that entered the cultural atmosphere and stayed there. It turns up in commercials, in retrospectives, in "songs of the 90s" lists. For a song whose lyrics are entirely surreal and whose topic (a woman floating in a bog) is genuinely strange, its cultural longevity is remarkable.
- **Jason Finn's career.** Finn's dual membership in Love Battery and PUSA, and his long tenure with PUSA through reunions and new records, makes him one of the more interesting Seattle musicians in the engine's scope -- a drummer who has played across a remarkable range of sonic contexts without losing his distinctive style.

(Sources: PUSA cultural legacy; "Lump" chart longevity; modified instrument DIY legacy; Jason Finn career arc; comedy as aesthetic resistance)
