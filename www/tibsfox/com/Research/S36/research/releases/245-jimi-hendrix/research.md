## THE CENTRAL DISTRICT -- WHERE JIMI HENDRIX CAME FROM

### Seattle's Black Neighborhood and the Making of a Genius

James Marshall Hendrix was born November 27, 1942, in Seattle, Washington, at Harborview Hospital. He grew up in the Central District -- the historically Black neighborhood on the hill east of downtown Seattle. Understanding the Central District is essential to understanding Hendrix:

- **The Central District in the 1940s-1950s.** Seattle's Central District (CD) was, by the mid-twentieth century, the primary residential area for Seattle's African American community. Racial covenants in housing deeds legally restricted where Black families could live in Seattle; the CD became the de facto Black neighborhood by exclusion as much as by community. This concentration created a dense, vibrant cultural environment: Black-owned businesses, churches, music venues, and community organizations. The social solidarity of the CD was forged in part by the same discriminatory forces that had confined people to it.
- **Jimi's early life.** His mother, Lucille (née Jeter), was of Cherokee and African American descent. His father, James Allen "Al" Hendrix, was African American and served in the US Army during World War II. Jimi's early childhood was unstable -- his parents' marriage was troubled, his mother died in 1958 when Jimi was fifteen, and he was raised largely by his paternal grandmother Nora Hendrix. The instability of Jimi's childhood is documented in detail; what is less often noted is how the Central District's community structures provided partial stability even when family structures did not.
- **Music in the CD.** The Central District's churches, particularly the COGIC (Church of God in Christ) congregations, were significant musical environments. Gospel music -- call-and-response, emotional intensity, the blues as secular gospel -- permeated the CD. Jimi absorbed this tradition before he had a guitar.
- **Garfield High School.** Jimi attended Garfield High School in the Central District -- the same school that would later produce Quincy Jones, the same institution at the center of Seattle's Black cultural life. Jimi was not a distinguished academic student, but Garfield's musical environment mattered.
- **First guitar.** Jimi received his first acoustic guitar in 1958, around the time of his mother's death. He taught himself to play -- there was no money for lessons, no formal instruction. He learned by ear, by watching, by obsessive practice. The left-handed Jimi played a right-handed guitar restrung upside-down -- a physical adaptation that would become one of the defining characteristics of his technique.

(Sources: Jimi Hendrix birthplace; Harborview Hospital; Central District history; racial covenants; Lucille Hendrix; Al Hendrix; Nora Hendrix; Garfield High School; first guitar 1958; left-handed technique)

## SEATTLE 1958-1963 -- THE FORMATION YEARS

### Before London, Before the Experience, Before the World Knew

The Seattle years are often treated as prologue -- as if Hendrix's story doesn't truly begin until he arrived in London in 1966. This is wrong. The formation happened in Seattle:

- **The first bands.** Jimi played in his first band, the Velvetones, around 1958-1959 while still in high school. The early Seattle bands were playing R&B and early rock and roll -- the sounds on the radio, the sounds in the clubs. These were apprentice years, playing covers, learning the repertoire, developing the physical dexterity that would later become supernatural.
- **The Rocking Kings.** His most significant early Seattle band. Playing high school dances and local venues. The repertoire was R&B -- Little Richard, Ray Charles, B.B. King, Chuck Berry. Hendrix was absorbing the full tradition of Black American popular music from the inside, as a practicing musician.
- **Left-handed in a right-handed world.** Playing a right-handed guitar upside-down forced Hendrix to develop unconventional technique. He could not use standard chord shapes; he developed his own. He played bass lines with his thumb on the bottom strings while playing melody on top -- a technique that would become his signature. The physical constraint produced the innovation.
- **The Army and the departure.** After being arrested twice, Jimi joined the US Army in 1961 and was assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky, where he met bassist Billy Cox, his future bandmate in the Band of Gypsys. He received a discharge in 1962 and spent the next several years on the Chitlin' Circuit -- the network of Black-owned venues across the American South and East that provided the infrastructure for Black musicians to work before and during desegregation.
- **What Seattle gave him.** The CD gave Hendrix his musical vocabulary: gospel, blues, R&B, early rock. It gave him left-handedness as unconventional technique. It gave him Garfield High School and the early bands. Seattle did not make Hendrix famous -- New York, London, and the world did that. But Seattle made Hendrix.

(Sources: Velvetones 1958-1959; Rocking Kings; left-handed upside-down guitar; US Army 101st Airborne; Fort Campbell; Billy Cox; Chitlin' Circuit history; Garfield High School musical context)

## THE CHITLIN' CIRCUIT -- 1963-1966

### Playing Behind Giants, Learning the Trade

The years between Seattle and London are the least-documented period of Hendrix's career -- and among the most formative:

- **The circuit.** The Chitlin' Circuit ran from the South through the Northeast, covering Black-owned or Black-patronized venues in cities across America. It was the working musician's school -- you learned by playing night after night, watching how the masters handled a room, how a song built and released tension, how a band held a crowd.
- **Playing behind Little Richard.** Jimi joined Little Richard's band in 1964. Little Richard's stage show was one of the most spectacular in American popular music -- extreme physical performance, gospel-derived intensity, absolute command of an audience. Hendrix was a sideman here, expected to play in support without drawing attention to himself. He often couldn't contain himself: he would improvise, add flourishes, play too long. Little Richard reportedly fired him multiple times for upstaging him.
- **The Isley Brothers.** Jimi played with the Isley Brothers in 1964. The Isley Brothers had a string of major hits and a professional touring operation. Playing with them exposed Hendrix to major-label recording and the upper tier of the R&B touring circuit.
- **Curtis Knight and the Squires.** In New York, Jimi played with Curtis Knight and the Squires from 1965 to 1966. This is where some of the pre-fame recordings exist -- recordings that would be repackaged and released (controversially) after Hendrix became famous.
- **King Casuals.** With Billy Cox on bass, briefly in Nashville.
- **The lesson of the circuit.** The Chitlin' Circuit years taught Hendrix everything about stagecraft that he would later apply with unprecedented intensity. The physical performance -- playing guitar behind his head, with his teeth, between his legs -- was not gimmickry. It was learned showmanship from watching Little Richard and others, refined and pushed to an extreme that no one had imagined.

(Sources: Chitlin' Circuit; Little Richard band 1964; Isley Brothers 1964; Curtis Knight and the Squires 1965-1966; King Casuals; Billy Cox; sideman years; stagecraft tradition)

## LONDON 1966 -- THE JIMI HENDRIX EXPERIENCE

### Chas Chandler, Mitch Mitchell, Noel Redding, and the Explosion

The transformation from sideman to superstar happened in London, with remarkable speed:

- **Chas Chandler discovers Hendrix.** Chas Chandler, bassist for the Animals, saw Hendrix playing at the Cafe Wha? in Greenwich Village in July 1966. Chandler was about to retire from performing and transition to managing. He immediately recognized what he was seeing -- something he had never seen before -- and convinced Hendrix to come to London, where the British blues scene would receive him differently than the American market.
- **London's British blues scene.** Britain in 1966 had Cream (Eric Clapton, Jack Bruce, Ginger Baker), the Rolling Stones, the Yardbirds, John Mayall's Bluesbreakers, Jeff Beck. These were the best blues-influenced rock musicians in the world, and they knew it. Hendrix arrived and immediately demonstrated that something different was possible -- a more physical, more intense, more adventurous approach to the electric guitar than anyone in London had imagined.
- **The Experience.** Chandler assembled the Jimi Hendrix Experience: Jimi on guitar and vocals, Noel Redding on bass, Mitch Mitchell on drums. Mitchell was a jazz-influenced drummer with a facility for improvisation; Redding was a guitarist converted to bass who provided the straight anchor Hendrix needed. The power trio format -- guitar, bass, drums -- gave Hendrix maximum freedom to fill sonic space.
- **"Hey Joe" (1966).** The debut single. A traditional folk song arranged as a slow blues ballad. Hendrix's guitar tone -- rich, compressed, with controlled feedback -- immediately announced something new. "Hey Joe" reached #6 in the UK.
- **"Purple Haze" (1967).** The second single. Built on the "devil's interval" tritone (E and B-flat), associated in medieval music theory with evil -- a coincidence that Hendrix appreciated. The opening riff is one of the most recognizable sounds in rock history. "Purple Haze" introduced Hendrix to the US market.
- ***Are You Experienced* (1967).** The debut album. Twelve tracks that ranged from R&B ("Foxy Lady") to blues ("Red House") to psychedelic freakout ("Third Stone from the Sun") to pop songwriting ("The Wind Cries Mary"). The album reached #5 on the US Billboard 200. Hendrix had arrived.

(Sources: Chas Chandler; Cafe Wha?; British blues scene 1966; Noel Redding; Mitch Mitchell; "Hey Joe" 1966; "Purple Haze" 1967; tritone "devil's interval"; *Are You Experienced* 1967)

## THE RECORDINGS -- THE COMPLETE JIMI HENDRIX EXPERIENCE

### *Are You Experienced*, *Axis: Bold as Love*, *Electric Ladyland*

In three years, Hendrix recorded three studio albums that together constitute one of the most concentrated bodies of creative achievement in rock history:

- ***Are You Experienced* (1967).** The debut. US release had different track listing than UK. "Purple Haze," "Manic Depression," "Hey Joe," "The Wind Cries Mary," "Foxy Lady," "Fire," "Red House." Every track distinctive. The album established Hendrix's range -- he was not just a blues guitarist; he was a songwriter, a producer in all but name, a sonic architect.
- ***Axis: Bold as Love* (1967).** Released the same year as the debut -- Hendrix was working at an extraordinary pace. "Little Wing," "Spanish Castle Magic," "Castles Made of Sand," "Bold as Love." The production is more adventurous: phasing, panning, layered overdubs. "Little Wing" in particular is one of the most beautiful guitar compositions in the history of the instrument -- a chord melody where the guitar voice-leads through a progression using open strings, hammer-ons, and subtle harmonic additions that no one else had imagined.
- ***Electric Ladyland* (1968).** The double album. Hendrix's most ambitious statement. "...And the Gods Made Love," "Have You Ever Been (To Electric Ladyland)," "Crosstown Traffic," "Voodoo Chile," "Still Raining, Still Dreaming," "House Burning Down," "All Along the Watchtower," "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)." Produced by Hendrix himself. The Bob Dylan cover of "All Along the Watchtower" became so definitive that Dylan himself acknowledged he has never played it better than Hendrix's version. "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)" -- recorded in one take -- is perhaps the single greatest display of electric guitar technique ever committed to tape.
- **The Woodstock performance (August 18, 1969).** The last act to play Woodstock, performing at dawn for the crowd of half a million. The set included the solo electric rendition of "The Star-Spangled Banner" that became the defining political act in popular music history -- Hendrix playing the national anthem through controlled feedback, dive bombs, and whammy-bar manipulation that evoked bombs falling, rockets, the sonic texture of American violence. No political statement was made explicitly. None was needed. The music spoke.

(Sources: *Are You Experienced* 1967; *Axis: Bold as Love* 1967; *Electric Ladyland* 1968; "Little Wing" chord-melody analysis; Bob Dylan *All Along the Watchtower*; Woodstock August 18 1969; "Star-Spangled Banner" political interpretation)

## THE BAND OF GYPSYS

### Buddy Miles, Billy Cox, and the Black Power Pivot

After the Experience disbanded in 1969, Hendrix assembled the Band of Gypsys with Buddy Miles (drums) and Billy Cox (bass -- his Army friend from the Fort Campbell years):

- **The political context.** The Band of Gypsys was an all-Black band -- a deliberate choice by Hendrix in a politically charged period. 1969-1970 was the height of the Black Power movement. Hendrix had been criticized in some circles for his predominantly white audiences and his white British Experience bandmates. The Band of Gypsys was a response.
- **Fillmore East NYE 1969-1970.** The Band of Gypsys' four performances at the Fillmore East on New Year's Eve 1969 and New Year's Day 1970 were recorded and released as the *Band of Gypsys* live album. The performances are rawer, bluesier, more politically direct than the Experience recordings. "Machine Gun" -- a 12-minute improvised blues about the Vietnam War -- is one of the most powerful anti-war statements in rock history.
- **The dissolution.** The Band of Gypsys ended after a troubled performance at Madison Square Garden in January 1970. Hendrix reassembled with Mitch Mitchell and Billy Cox for the final touring period.
- **Electric Lady Studios.** Hendrix used his earnings to build Electric Lady Studios in Greenwich Village, New York City -- his own recording facility. Opened in August 1970. Hendrix recorded there through the summer of 1970. He died in London on September 18, 1970, before the studio's official opening party.

(Sources: Band of Gypsys formation; Buddy Miles; Billy Cox; Fillmore East NYE 1969; "Machine Gun"; Band of Gypsys album; Madison Square Garden 1970; Electric Lady Studios; death September 18 1970)

## THE TECHNIQUE -- WHAT MADE HENDRIX DIFFERENT

### Left Hand, Right-Handed Guitar, and the Architecture of a New Sound

Hendrix's technical innovations were so profound and numerous that they are still being catalogued by musicians and academics:

- **Thumb-over bass notes.** Hendrix wrapped his left thumb over the top of the fretboard to fret the low E string -- a technique most classical guitar pedagogues would consider incorrect. This allowed him to play bass notes with his thumb while using his remaining four fingers to play chords and melody simultaneously. He was, in effect, playing two parts at once.
- **Chord voicings.** Because Hendrix played a right-handed guitar upside-down and restrung, some standard chord shapes were available to him; others were not. He developed new voicings -- particularly for dominant 7th and 9th chords -- that became the harmonic vocabulary of funk and soul guitar. Prince, Stevie Ray Vaughan, and virtually every guitarist who came after him uses chord voicings that Hendrix either invented or popularized.
- **Whammy bar integration.** The whammy bar (tremolo arm) on a Fender Stratocaster was used before Hendrix -- but used decoratively, for vibrato on sustained notes. Hendrix integrated the bar into the melody itself: dive bombs, pitch raises, controlled flutter. The bar became a melodic instrument.
- **Feedback control.** Hendrix used guitar-to-amplifier feedback as a musical element -- not as accident or noise but as a controlled, pitchable addition to the sonic palette. He could sustain notes through feedback, shape feedback into harmonics, use feedback as a texture under a chord.
- **Tone controls.** Hendrix was a master of the guitar's onboard tone controls -- rolling the volume and tone knobs during a performance to shape the attack and character of each note. This level of nuanced control, combined with foot pedals (Fuzz Face, Uni-Vibe, Octavia, Wah-Wah), created a tonal vocabulary that no one has fully replicated.
- **The Stratocaster.** Hendrix's primary instrument was the Fender Stratocaster -- particularly left-handed Strats he played right-side-up (string-upside-down) and right-handed Strats he played upside-down. The Strat's single-coil pickups and floating tremolo system were essential to his sound. The relationship between Hendrix and the Stratocaster is as important to guitar history as any other pairing.

(Sources: Thumb-over bass technique; chord voicing innovations; whammy bar integration; feedback control technique; Fuzz Face pedal; Uni-Vibe; Octavia; Wah-Wah; Fender Stratocaster; left-handed playing technique)

## LEGACY -- THE GREATEST OF ALL TIME

### What the World Inherited from the Central District

Jimi Hendrix died at 27 years old. In seven years of professional performance -- four years of global fame -- he transformed the electric guitar, invented entire genres, and left a body of recorded work that musicians have spent fifty years trying to understand:

- **Guitar rankings.** In virtually every poll of musicians, critics, and historians, Hendrix ranks first or first-equal among electric guitarists. Rolling Stone's "100 Greatest Guitarists" placed him first. Guitar Player magazine, various musician polls -- Hendrix is consistently at the top.
- **Influence on every guitarist who followed.** There is essentially no major rock, funk, or blues guitarist who came after Hendrix who was not influenced by him. Eddie Van Halen, Stevie Ray Vaughan, Prince, John Mayer, Tom Morello -- the genealogy runs directly from Hendrix.
- **Woodstock as cultural event.** The "Star-Spangled Banner" performance at Woodstock is one of the defining moments of the 1960s -- a piece of political art that required no words, only sound.
- **The Central District's contribution.** Hendrix's origin in Seattle's Central District is the engine's most significant geographical data point. The CD -- a neighborhood formed in part by discriminatory housing policies -- produced the greatest guitarist in rock history. The neighborhood is inseparable from the artist.
- **27 Club.** Hendrix died at 27, in the same year as Janis Joplin and Jim Morrison, beginning the cultural pattern later associated with the "27 Club" (also including Kurt Cobain, Amy Winehouse, and others). His death robbed the world of what might have come next -- there were plans for further recording, for a more jazz-influenced direction, for collaboration with Miles Davis.
- **Seattle's debt.** Seattle has not always fully claimed Hendrix. In the years immediately after his death, Seattle's rock culture was still in formation. By the time Seattle became a globally recognized music city in the 1990s grunge era, the Hendrix legacy had become central to Seattle's musical identity. The Jimi Hendrix Park in the Central District, the First Avenue apartment complex where his grandmother lived (a National Historic Landmark), and the ongoing presence of Experience Hendrix LLC in Seattle all anchor Hendrix to the city.

(Sources: Rolling Stone 100 Greatest Guitarists; guitar influence genealogy; Woodstock Star-Spangled Banner; 27 Club; Jimi Hendrix Park Seattle; Central District National Historic Landmark; Experience Hendrix LLC)
