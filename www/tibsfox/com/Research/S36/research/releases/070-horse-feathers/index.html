Kill Rock Stars: Olympia Enters the Engine

Kill Rock Stars is one of the most significant independent record labels in the history of Pacific Northwest music. Founded in Olympia, Washington in 1991 by Slim Moon, KRS was born out of the same riot grrrl and punk scenes that produced Bikini Kill, Sleater-Kinney, and Heavens to Betsy. The label later signed Elliott Smith, whose albums on KRS became landmarks of indie folk and singer-songwriter music. Kill Rock Stars is not merely a label — it is an institution of Pacific Northwest independent music culture, a philosophical statement about artistic autonomy, and a geographic anchor in Olympia.

With Horse Feathers, KRS enters the Seattle 360 engine for the first time. This is a structural event of the first order. The engine has tracked Sub Pop (Seattle), Barsuk (Seattle), Tender Loving Empire (Portland), and various self-released artists. Kill Rock Stars adds Olympia to the engine’s label geography — the state capital, the Evergreen State College town, the birthplace of riot grrrl. Olympia becomes the sixth I-5 corridor node: Seattle, Portland, Bellingham, Everett, Tacoma, and now Olympia. The corridor thickens. The engine’s map of Pacific Northwest music infrastructure gains another anchor point.

The significance of KRS extends beyond geography. Sub Pop defined the grunge era and evolved into a national tastemaker. Barsuk nurtured the Death Cab ecosystem. Tender Loving Empire represents Portland’s community-retail model. Kill Rock Stars represents something else: the political independent label — the label founded on principles of artistic freedom, DIY ethics, and resistance to commercial co-optation. KRS did not chase trends. It created the conditions for artists to work outside the commercial system entirely. That Horse Feathers landed on KRS rather than Sub Pop or Barsuk tells us something about the band’s relationship to the commercial music ecosystem: they are outside it, deliberately, by choice and by aesthetic.

Kill Rock Stars at degree 70 is the engine’s first encounter with the Olympia node — the state capital, the college town, the riot grrrl birthplace. KRS adds a political dimension to the engine’s label ecology: not just geography and genre, but ideology. The label as philosophical statement. Artistic autonomy as organizing principle. Olympia as the conscience of the corridor.

The Fiddle Line: Violin and Banjo Enter the Engine

Horse Feathers’ sound is built on delicate acoustic arrangements where violin and banjo carry as much melodic and emotional weight as the voice itself. This is structurally significant: through seventy degrees, the engine has tracked guitar-centric arrangements almost exclusively. Acoustic guitar, electric guitar, piano, synthesizer, percussion — these have been the engine’s primary instrumental voices. Violin and banjo have appeared in arrangements (Fleet Foxes’ layered instrumentation, for instance) but never as primary voices defining a band’s sonic identity.

Horse Feathers changes this. The violin in Thistled Spring (2008) and Broken Birthday (2016) is not an ornamental layer — it is a lead voice, carrying melodies, providing counterpoint to Ringle’s hushed vocals, and creating the emotional atmosphere that defines the band’s sound. The banjo functions similarly: not the driving percussive banjo of bluegrass, but a fingerpicked, melodic instrument that interlocks with the guitar and violin to create a woven texture — three string instruments braiding around each other, none dominating, all essential.

The fiddle line is the seventy-first acoustic element: the melodic line played on violin that weaves through the arrangement like a thread through fabric, appearing and disappearing, carrying the emotional subtext that the lyrics cannot hold. In Horse Feathers’ music, the fiddle line does what the voice cannot — it sustains emotion across the spaces between words, fills the silences that Ringle’s hushed delivery creates, and provides a second narrative that runs parallel to the lyrical narrative. The thread beneath the words.

The Corridor Wanderer: Portland, Seattle, Olympia

Horse Feathers occupies the I-5 corridor as a zone rather than a fixed point. Justin Ringle formed the band in Portland but moved between Portland and Seattle over the course of the band’s career. The label is based in Olympia. The music draws from the folk traditions of the entire Pacific Northwest corridor — not anchored to one city’s specific scene, but existing in the spaces between cities, in the corridor itself.

The engine has seen I-5 corridor artists before. Y La Bamba (degree 61) moved from Portland to Seattle. Laura Gibson (degree 57) is Portland-based on a Seattle label (Barsuk). But Horse Feathers is the first artist whose identity is genuinely multi-nodal — Portland origin, Seattle presence, Olympia label. Three cities on the corridor, all contributing to the band’s identity. The corridor wanderer is the seventy-first artist-city pattern: the artist who does not belong to one city but to the corridor itself, whose movement between cities is not a departure or a migration but a way of inhabiting the region as a connected whole.

The corridor wanderer is distinct from the Export (leaves permanently), the visiting artist (connects institutionally without geographic presence), and the working musician (commits to one city). The corridor wanderer treats the I-5 corridor as a single ecosystem — Portland, Seattle, and Olympia as neighborhoods of one extended metropolitan region of independent music. The freeway as neighborhood. The corridor as city.

Blue Note: The Thread

Intermediate — 70th Deployment

The blue note that appears in the space between two interlocking string lines — the moment where violin and guitar almost meet but do not quite resolve, creating a delicate tension. The thread is the blue note of counterpoint: not a single note but the relationship between two voices. The open chord (69) rang in sustained simplicity; the thread shimmers in woven complexity.

Indie Folk at Genre Stage 52

Advanced — Genre Stage 52

Indie Folk returns at the fifty-second genre stage. The genre appeared at degree 53 (Fleet Foxes, Genre Stage 34) as communal, multi-voice folk. At degree 70, Indie Folk is intimate, string-driven, hushed — the chamber-folk end of the spectrum. Same genre, different architecture: Fleet Foxes’ wall of voices versus Horse Feathers’ woven strings. Genre Stage 52 follows Singer-Songwriter (51, degree 69).

Key Discography

House with No Home

2006 · Self-Released

The debut. Justin Ringle’s earliest recordings — sparse, hushed, intimate. Guitar and voice as foundation, with early hints of the string arrangements that would define the band. Portland beginnings. The seed before the fiddle line grew.

Thistled Spring

2008 · Kill Rock Stars

The KRS debut. Violin and banjo fully integrated into the arrangements. Delicate, woven textures — three string instruments braiding around Ringle’s hushed vocals. The album that defined the Horse Feathers sound: chamber folk as indie music.

Broken Birthday

2016 · Kill Rock Stars

Matured arrangements, deeper emotional territory. The string interplay reaches its most sophisticated form. The fiddle line as a fully developed second narrative voice. Songs of loss, time, and the persistence of feeling.

Appreciation

2012 · Kill Rock Stars

Mid-career expansion. The arrangements grow more confident without losing the delicate core. Piano textures appear alongside the string instruments. The woven quality deepens. KRS stewardship continues.
