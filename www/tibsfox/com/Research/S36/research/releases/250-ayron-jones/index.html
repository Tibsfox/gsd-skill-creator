DEGREE 250 — CENTRAL DISTRICT BLUES — HENDRIX LINEAGE CONTINUED. Same neighborhood, same tradition, same instrument — five decades later. Ayron Jones grew up in the Central District of Seattle, the same neighborhood where Jimi Hendrix (degrees 245-246) grew up. The blues runs through both.

Central District, Seattle. Guitar and Voice. Hollywood Records.

Ayron Jones is Seattle's current blues rock champion — a guitarist and vocalist with Central District roots who has built a national audience on Hollywood Records through raw, emotionally direct blues rock. "Mercy" brought him to national rock radio. He carries the tradition that Jimi Hendrix synthesized from the same streets, applied to the present moment.

The Hendrix Thread -- Two Central District Guitarists

The Central District of Seattle is the entry point. Jimi Hendrix grew up there — attended Garfield High School, learned guitar in the neighborhood's African American community, absorbed blues and R&B from the culture that surrounded him. He left in 1961 and transformed into something global, but the roots stayed in the Central District. Ayron Jones grew up in the same neighborhood, in the same tradition, on the same instrument.

The engine documented Hendrix at degrees 245-246. Five degrees later, the Central District blues returns at degree 250 in the form of its current practitioner. This is not tribute or imitation. It is continuation — the same geographic origin, the same instrument, the same tradition's ongoing life.

"The blues is not a historical artifact. It is a living form, and Ayron Jones makes the case for it from the same neighborhood where Hendrix made his version of the same case."

The Music -- E=7, Blues Rock, National Reach

Jones's guitar playing is the defining fact about his music. The electric blues tradition — Muddy Waters' amplified Delta, Buddy Guy's emotional intensity, Hendrix's fusion with rock dynamics — runs through his playing. His vocals carry the same directness: powerful, soulful, without affectation.

Hollywood Records provides national distribution and production resources. The tension between the blues's raw tradition and major label polish is the creative space Jones inhabits. "Mercy" demonstrated that this tension can produce music that reaches a national rock audience without losing the tradition's essential character.

The 249-250 Blues Rock Pair

Degrees 249 and 250 form a micro-arc: The Black Tones (249, Sub Pop, Rainier Valley, two-piece, raw) and Ayron Jones (250, Hollywood Records, Central District, solo artist, major label). Two blues rock musicians, both active, both rooted in Seattle's African American community, adjacent degrees. The contrast in approach — indie vs. major, stripped-down duo vs. produced solo artist — represents the blues tradition's own internal range, from raw to polished, from small to large.
