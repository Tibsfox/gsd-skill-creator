## What Worked

1. **The "covers more sonic territory than any rock album of the era" framing is the essential claim.** The internal range of *Superunknown* -- from the tuned-percussion world-music groove of "Spoonman" to the psychedelic dread of "Black Hole Sun" to the hard riff power of "My Wave" to the devastating directness of "Fell on Black Days" to the 9-minute orchestral metal of "Like Suicide" -- is genuinely exceptional. This range, maintained at consistent excellence across 70 minutes, is the evidence for E=10.
2. **The four-octave vocal range deployed with compositional intelligence is the correct characterization.** The analysis should not simply say "Cornell had a great voice." It should say what he did with it: upper register for intensity and pleading, lower register for dread and inevitability, midrange for rhythmic riff-vocal interplay. Each register serves the song's architecture.
3. **The "Let Me Drown to Like Suicide" arc framing captures the album's structural achievement.** The sequencing -- one of the strongest of the decade -- moves the listener through a 70-minute experience with consistent intensity while varying mood and tempo. Documenting the specific opener and closer anchors the structural observation.
4. **The March 1994 temporal context is relevant but not decisive.** Superunknown was released on March 8, 1994; Kurt Cobain died on April 5, 1994. The coincidence is historically significant -- the album entered the public consciousness as the grunge era's last major statement before its defining tragedy. This context enriches the entry without overwhelming it.
5. **Kim Thayil's guitar vocabulary analysis is essential and often underemphasized.** Critical coverage of Soundgarden tends to focus on Cornell's voice. Thayil's widened guitar vocabulary on Superunknown -- dropped tunings, sitar influences (Spoonman), dissonant harmonics, plus conventional alt metal crunch -- is equally responsible for the album's range.

## Respect Note

Chris Cornell died on May 18, 2017, at age 52. The engine documents the music with factual accuracy and genuine respect. *Superunknown* is an artistic achievement of the first order. Cornell's voice is one of the most extraordinary instruments in rock history. The entry focuses on what the music is and what the album achieved. Nothing else is warranted.

## Discoveries

1. **The album's 1994 release date situates it at the exact apex of grunge's commercial moment and the exact edge of its imminent dissolution.** This is not merely contextual detail -- it means the album was received and heard in a specific cultural moment that shaped its legacy. The album is both a product and a document of that moment.
2. **Ben Shepherd's bass on Superunknown is more melodically active than most accounts acknowledge.** Shepherd (who had replaced Hiro Yamamoto, then Jason Everman) brought a more compositionally ambitious bass approach to the band. The bass movement on tracks like "Mailman" and "Fell on Black Days" contributes materially to the album's emotional range.
3. **Michael Beinhorn's production achieves density without sacrificing detail.** The album sounds enormous (Cornell's vocals at maximum presence, Thayil's guitar tones huge and varied, Cameron's drums authoritative) without any loss of compositional clarity. This is a production achievement of genuine difficulty that Beinhorn executes throughout 70 minutes.
