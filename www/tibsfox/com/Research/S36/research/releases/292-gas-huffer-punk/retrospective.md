## What Worked

1. **The E=8 streak (290-292) is a genuine cluster of related energy.** Three consecutive degrees at E=8 document the punk tier of Seattle's heavy music scene in three distinct characters: post-hardcore (TAAS, Capitol Hill), punk/grunge (Alcohol Funnycar, Seattle), punk/garage (Gas Huffer, Fremont). The cluster is not coincidental -- it is a real grouping of bands that share energy while differing in tradition, neighborhood, and label.
2. **Amphetamine Reptile's first engine appearance is correctly placed.** AmRep is one of the most significant American noise-punk labels. Its entry through Gas Huffer at degree 292 is historically accurate: Gas Huffer is the AmRep catalog's Seattle representative, and their return brings the label into the engine at the correct moment.
3. **Fremont enters the engine with the right band.** Gas Huffer is a Fremont band in character, not just address. The neighborhood's bohemian-humorous identity maps directly onto Gas Huffer's approach: capable, committed, funny, anti-pretentious. The first Fremont entry is a precise fit.
4. **Tom Price as central figure is correctly documented.** Price is Gas Huffer's creative engine across the full thirteen-year run. His guitar approach -- choppy, propulsive, blues-rooted punk -- is the band's signature sound. The return at 292 solidifies his position in the engine's guitarist catalog.
5. **The thirteen-year run is unusual and significant.** Most punk-garage bands do not last thirteen years. Gas Huffer's longevity is evidence of genuine community embeddedness: the Fremont punk-garage scene sustained them far beyond the commercial cycle.

## Discoveries

1. **The Melvins connection through AmRep is the Pacific Northwest-Minneapolis axis.** Melvins (Aberdeen, Washington) and Gas Huffer (Fremont, Seattle) shared the AmRep catalog. Both Pacific Northwest bands on a Minneapolis label. The label relationship reveals cross-regional independent music community connections that are not visible in the purely local Seattle scene narrative.
2. **Gas Huffer's multi-label career documents the evolution of Seattle's independent infrastructure.** AmRep → Empty Records → Estrus. The three-label progression maps onto the stages of Seattle's independent music scene: the AmRep era (early 1990s, national noise-punk label connection), the Empty era (mid-to-late 1990s, local punk infrastructure), the Estrus era (early 2000s, PNW garage tradition). The label transitions are a chronological map.
