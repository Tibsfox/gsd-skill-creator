## The Weight of Succession: Why This Degree Matters

Degree 37 follows degree 36 -- Jimi Hendrix. There is no avoiding what that means structurally. The engine placed a living, working, currently-touring Seattle blues-rock guitarist directly after the most famous guitarist in the history of American music. This is not accidental. The CSV knows what it is doing. The question this degree must answer is not whether Ayron Jones is Jimi Hendrix's equal -- nobody is, and Jones himself would never claim otherwise. The question is: what does it mean to carry a tradition forward? What does the *continuation* of a musical lineage look like when the originator burned at E=6 and collapsed, and the inheritor is still alive, still playing, still building?

Jones has addressed this directly, repeatedly, and with clarity. In interviews with Guitar World, Louder, and others, he has said: "That's gonna be my guitar idol because we came from the same place, carry the same spirit and I'm only continuing the work that he laid down." He does not claim equivalence. He claims continuity. The engine validates this: Blues Rock at degree 36, Blues Rock at degree 37. Same genre, consecutive degrees, different eras -- 1962-1970 and 2010-present. Forty years of Seattle blues-rock history compressed into a two-degree gap.

The succession is also biographical. Jones attended Washington Middle School -- the same school Hendrix attended. He grew up in the Central District -- the same neighborhood. He was friends with Hendrix's nephew, a connection that led to his first professional break. He didn't just study Hendrix from a distance; he grew up in the same streets, walked the same hallways, heard the same stories -- not stories of a legend, but stories of a neighbor, a friend, a babysitter. "The Hendrix stories I grew up hearing weren't of torched guitars or legendary jams," Jones has explained, "but of Jimi Hendrix the friend, the neighbor, the babysitter."

This is the thirty-eighth artist-city pattern and the thirty-eighth acoustic element. It is E=5 after E=6 -- the energy drops back, not because Jones is less intense than Hendrix but because nobody in the engine is at E=6 except Hendrix. The engine recalibrates.

## Biography: A Child of the State

### Born in Seattle (August 23, 1986)

Ayron Jones was born on August 23, 1986, at the University of Washington Medical Center in Seattle, Washington. His mother was nineteen years old. His father "was never around." Both parents struggled with severe drug addiction. This is a different origin story than Hendrix's -- Hendrix's parents were poor and unstable but present, at least intermittently, through his childhood. Jones's parents were absent from the beginning. The city that produced both artists operated on the same neighborhood, the same schools, the same streets -- but the family structures that failed both children failed in different ways.

Jones's early childhood was defined by his parents' addictions and their inability to provide stable care. By the age of four, he had been placed under state supervision -- foster care, the system that catches children when families break. At four, his aunt took custody. By age seven, the state formalized the arrangement: his aunt and uncle were named his official adoptive parents. The album title *Child of the State* (2021) is not a metaphor. It is a literal description of Jones's legal status as a minor. He was a ward of the state of Washington.

"A lot of people have faced adoption and abandonment, but it's not really talked about as to how that affects people," Jones told Loudwire. "I wanted to be a beacon of hope for those people, to prove not everyone has to be a stereotype or statistic." His aunt, he has said, was the crucial guide without whom he would have become "a statistic that we read about."

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Ayron Jones; Loudwire -- "Rising Rocker Ayron Jones: Our Beginnings Don't Have to Define Us"; Consequence of Sound -- "Heavy Culture: Ayron Jones on His Upbringing, Fusing Music Genres, and Parenthood"; Facebook -- Ayron Jones adoption anniversary post)

### The Central District, Again

Jones was raised in Seattle's Central District -- the same neighborhood that produced Quincy Jones (degree 0), Ray Charles (degree 30), Luther Rabb (degree 32), Larry English (degree 33), The Tempos (degree 34), and Jimi Hendrix (degree 36). The CD now holds ten of thirty-eight degrees in the engine -- more than one-quarter of all artists. Jones is the tenth Central District degree, and the first to be born after 1960. He is the contemporary inheritor of a neighborhood tradition that stretches back to the 1940s.

The neighborhood Jones grew up in was different from the one that produced Hendrix. The Jackson Street corridor clubs were gone. The dense Black cultural ecosystem that had nurtured generations of musicians had been severely disrupted by decades of redlining, urban renewal, displacement, and gentrification. By the time Jones was a teenager in the early 2000s, the Central District was in the middle of a dramatic demographic shift -- the historically Black neighborhood was becoming increasingly white and affluent. Jones lived in the CD until 2017. The neighborhood that made him was disappearing around him.

But the Hendrix stories survived the neighborhood's transformation. Jones attended Washington Middle School, where a mural of Hendrix decorated the wall. He didn't initially realize who the man in the mural was. When he found out -- when he learned that the most famous guitarist in history had walked these same hallways -- the connection was immediate and permanent. "That's gonna be my guitar idol because we came from the same place, carry the same spirit."

(Sources: The Seattle Times -- "50 years after his death, Jimi Hendrix continues shaping Seattle music"; King5 -- "Seattle rocker Ayron Jones says it's finally his time"; Wikipedia -- Ayron Jones)

### Gospel, Soul, and the First Guitar

Jones's adoptive aunt was a religious figure in the family. The household was steeped in gospel and soul music -- not the blues that filled Al Hendrix's turntable, but a parallel tradition rooted in the Black church. Jones learned piano first, then drums, absorbing the rhythmic and harmonic vocabulary of gospel before he ever picked up a guitar.

The guitar came as a birthday gift from his mother -- his biological mother, the one who had been unable to raise him. This detail carries emotional weight that Jones has addressed in interviews: the instrument that would define his life came from the person whose absence defined his childhood. He was self-taught, like Hendrix. He learned by listening, by watching, by playing along -- the same oral tradition that had trained every blues musician before him, transmitted through the same city but a different generation.

The influences Jones absorbed were broader than Hendrix's initial blues diet. Where Hendrix grew up on Muddy Waters, B.B. King, and Albert King, Jones grew up on Hendrix, Nirvana, Soundgarden, Michael Jackson, gospel, and hip-hop. Seattle in the 1990s and 2000s was not the same city as Seattle in the 1950s. The grunge explosion had happened. Hip-hop was ascendant. Jones's musical DNA is a blend of everything Seattle produced across multiple generations -- the blues tradition through Hendrix, the grunge tradition through Cobain and Cornell, the hip-hop tradition through Sir Mix-a-Lot, the soul and gospel tradition through the Black church.

Jones himself has described his sound with a formula: "like if Michael Jackson played guitar like Jimi Hendrix in Kurt Cobain's band." This is not a casual analogy. It maps three of his primary influences (Jackson's vocal precision, Hendrix's guitar vocabulary, Cobain's raw structural approach) onto a single artistic identity. It also implicitly claims all three traditions as legitimate ingredients in a Black artist's toolkit -- a point Jones has made explicitly in interviews about the racial politics of rock music.

## The Apprenticeship: Playing Every Stage in Seattle

### Ayron Jones and the Way (2010-2018)

In 2010, Jones formed the trio Ayron Jones and the Way -- a name that deliberately invokes the power-trio format of Hendrix's Experience, Stevie Ray Vaughan's Double Trouble, and Cream. The original lineup featured DeAndre Enrico on bass and Kai Van De Pitte on drums. The trio format is not incidental; it is a conscious choice to occupy the same structural space as the blues-rock trios that defined the genre.

Jones played hundreds of shows in Seattle through the 2010s. Every venue, every neighborhood showcase, every dive bar. The city functioned as what Jones later described as "a living, breathing master class for the aspiring artist" -- a proving ground with stages in every neighborhood and mentors like Sir Mix-a-Lot, Nancy Wilson, Ben Gibbard, and Mike McCready offering guidance. This is the apprenticeship model that the chitlin' circuit provided for Hendrix -- except Jones's circuit was a single city, not a national network of segregated venues.

The apprenticeship was long. Jones spent a decade building a local reputation before his national breakthrough. This patience is structurally significant in the engine: Hendrix spent four years on the chitlin' circuit (1962-1966) before London. Jones spent more than a decade in Seattle's club scene (2010-2020) before national radio. The timescales are different, but the model is the same -- learn the trade by performing it, night after night, in front of audiences who don't care about your potential, only about whether you can deliver right now.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Ayron Jones; Flood Magazine -- "Ayron Jones Talks Joining the City's Musical Lineage in Exclusive Excerpt from New Book 'The Sound of Seattle'"; The Seattle Times -- "Seattle rocker Ayron Jones is having his national breakout in the strangest of times")

### Sir Mix-a-Lot: The Discovery

In 2012, while performing at a dive bar in Seattle's University District, Jones was discovered by Sir Mix-a-Lot -- Anthony Ray, the Seattle hip-hop legend whose "Baby Got Back" (1992) had defined a different dimension of Seattle's musical output. The discovery story has its own texture: a friend insisted that Mix-a-Lot come hear this band. Mix was reluctant. He showed up on a sleepy Wednesday night. He heard the music from outside the venue and assumed he was about to see "three 50-year-old white dudes" based on the sound. Instead, he found himself staring at three young Black men playing what he described as "a wicked mix of blues and rock."

Mix-a-Lot became Jones's first significant patron. He produced the trio's debut album, *Dream* (2013), and used his connections and credibility to amplify Jones's visibility. The cross-pollination is remarkable: Seattle's most famous hip-hop artist sponsoring Seattle's most promising blues-rock guitarist. The connection illustrates the city's internal musical ecosystem -- genres that might seem siloed in other cities are connected through personal relationships in Seattle.

(Sources: Seattle Times Soundposts -- "Sir Mix-a-Lot on Ayron Jones Q&A"; Seattle Times -- "Ayron Jones and his 'dream' come true"; Wikipedia -- Ayron Jones; Last.fm -- Ayron Jones biography)

### The Hendrix Family Connection

A friendship with Hendrix's nephew led to Jones's first real professional break -- playing with the opening band on a Janelle Monae tour [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- the specific tour and date vary across sources]. The Hendrix connection is not institutional (no estate sponsorship, no formal endorsement) but personal and familial. Jones knew Hendrix's family as neighbors and community members, not as custodians of a legend. This personal proximity to the Hendrix legacy gave Jones both an intimate understanding of the man behind the myth and an inescapable comparison that followed him through his career.

In 2024, Jones was invited to participate in the Experience Hendrix Tour, a traveling tribute concert featuring guitarists performing Hendrix's music. During the tour's kickoff in Seattle -- Hendrix's hometown -- Jones was one of the artists who played Jimi's actual Fender Stratocaster from the 1969 Woodstock performance. The symbolic weight of this moment should not be understated: the living Seattle blues-rock guitarist, from the same neighborhood, playing the same instrument that Hendrix used for the most famous guitar performance in American history.

## The Breakthrough: Child of the State (2021)

### Big Machine / John Varvatos Records

After a decade of independent work in Seattle, Jones signed with Big Machine / John Varvatos Records -- a partnership between Big Machine Label Group (Nashville-based, known primarily for country and pop-rock acts) and fashion designer John Varvatos, whose record label has focused on rock artists. The signing represented a fundamental shift: from local Seattle artist to nationally distributed rock act.

*Child of the State* was released on May 21, 2021. The album title, as noted above, refers literally to Jones's childhood as a ward of the state. The twelve tracks map Jones's emotional landscape -- abandonment, resilience, identity, and the specific textures of growing up Black in Seattle. The production is heavy and guitar-forward, built on the blues-rock foundation but incorporating grunge dynamics, hip-hop rhythms, and soul vocal sensibilities.

### "Rock and Roll Is a Creation of Black America"

Jones has been direct about the racial politics of his genre. "Rock and roll is a creation of Black America," he told Consequence of Sound. "Even though it may be less talked about and may be forgotten by some of the newer generations, it's our sound."

This statement carries special weight at degree 37, immediately following Hendrix. The engine has documented the blues-to-rock genealogy across multiple degrees: Ray Charles's R&B (degree 30), Luther Rabb's funk-rock (degree 32), Robert Cray's blues (degree 35), Hendrix's blues rock (degree 36). Jones at degree 37 extends this lineage into the present day. He is a living reminder that the tradition is not over, not a museum exhibit, not a tribute act. It is an ongoing creative practice, rooted in Black American musical culture, performed by artists who are still fighting for recognition within a genre that their predecessors invented.

Jones has also spoken about the constant racial profiling he faces as a Black rock musician. In interviews, he has described being asked "Are you a rapper?" and "Do you rap?" with enough frequency that it became a recurring frustration. The assumption that a Black musician must be a rapper, not a guitarist, is the same assumption that excluded "Red House" from the American release of *Are You Experienced* in 1967 -- the blues was the part of Hendrix that his label thought wouldn't sell to American audiences. Fifty-four years later, Jones faces the same cultural gatekeeping.

## The Guitar: Equipment, Tone, and Approach

### The Stratocaster Continues

Jones plays Fender Stratocasters -- the same instrument that Hendrix wielded and that Robert Cray (degree 35) plays. The Stratocaster thread now spans three consecutive degrees (35-36-37), an unprecedented run of a single instrument defining the engine's sonic signature. Jones's primary guitars include a Fender American Professional Stratocaster and several HH (humbucker-humbucker) Strat configurations.

He was selected for Fender's 2021 "Fender Next" class of rising artists -- an endorsement program that positions him within the Fender lineage alongside other emerging players. The corporate lineage mirrors the artistic lineage: Fender -> Hendrix -> Cray -> Jones.

### Tone and Dynamics

Jones's approach to tone is rooted in a philosophy of dynamics -- the contrast between quiet and loud, restrained and explosive. "Music should have very specific dynamics -- that's how you create drama," he told Guitar Player. He credits the influence of Seattle grunge bands -- Nirvana, Mudhoney, Soundgarden -- for teaching him the soft/loud dynamic that structures his songs.

His effects chain includes a Boss DS-1 distortion pedal, a TS9 Tube Screamer, an FX Engineering Moab Boost pedal, and a Boss PS-6 Harmonist, run through a Fender Hot Rod Deluxe amplifier. The rig is notably different from Hendrix's Marshall stacks -- smaller, more controlled, less physically overwhelming. This is not E=6. This is E=5: high energy channeled through dynamics and contrast rather than through raw volume and physical intensity.

Jones has articulated his playing philosophy in explicitly physical terms: "I like to attack the instrument... I'm almost like Bruce Lee when it comes to playing... Whatever it takes to get my point across, I'll do it." The martial-arts metaphor is telling: precision and power combined, economy of motion, maximum impact from controlled technique. This is closer to Robert Cray's stinging clean than to Hendrix's controlled scream -- but played through a dirtier signal chain and with grunge-informed dynamics.

## The Forty-Year Gap: 1970-2010

### What Happened Between Hendrix and Jones?

The engine jumps from Hendrix's era (1962-1970) to Jones's era (2010-present). That is a forty-year gap -- the largest era jump between consecutive degrees in the engine's history. This gap is not a failure of the engine's coverage; it is a structural statement about the blues-rock tradition in Seattle.

What happened in Seattle blues rock between 1970 and 2010? The short answer is: grunge happened. The Seattle sound that emerged in the late 1980s and exploded globally with Nirvana's *Nevermind* (1991) was built on the same blues-rock foundation that Hendrix had established -- Jerry Cantrell (Alice in Chains), Kim Thayil (Soundgarden), and Stone Gossard / Mike McCready (Pearl Jam) all absorbed Hendrix's vocabulary. But grunge was categorized as alternative rock, not blues rock. The blues DNA was present but rebranded.

Jones exists on the other side of that gap. He absorbed both traditions -- the original blues-rock through Hendrix and the grunge reprocessing through Cobain and Cornell -- and synthesized them into something that is explicitly, self-consciously blues rock again. He is the artist who closes the loop: blues -> Hendrix -> grunge -> Jones -> blues rock again. The forty-year gap is the engine acknowledging that the tradition went underground (into grunge) and re-emerged (in Jones) rather than disappearing.

This is not a gap to be filled. It is a compression to be understood. Degrees 36-37 are not claiming that nothing happened in Seattle between 1970 and 2010. They are claiming that the explicit blues-rock tradition -- the self-identified lineage, the artist who says "I am a blues-rock musician carrying Hendrix's legacy" -- skipped a generation and returned in Jones.

## Shared Stages and Musical Context

Jones's live performance history maps a remarkable breadth of musical context. As an opening act or festival co-participant, he has shared stages with:

- **B.B. King** -- direct blues lineage (Hendrix's primary influence)

- **Judas Priest, Alice Cooper** -- classic rock and metal

The breadth is striking. Jones has performed across blues, rock, metal, hip-hop, and art-pop -- a range that no other artist in the engine has achieved in live contexts. This breadth reflects both the genre-crossing nature of his music and the practical reality of being a Black blues-rock guitarist in the 2020s: you play whatever stages will have you, because the genre boxes don't have a comfortable slot for your specific combination.

The Living Colour connection deserves special mention. Vernon Reid's band was the most visible Black rock act of the late 1980s and early 1990s -- the generation between Hendrix and Jones. Jones sharing a stage with Living Colour completes a three-generation chain of Black rock visibility: Hendrix (1960s-70s) -> Living Colour (1980s-90s) -> Jones (2010s-present).

## "Our Generation Doesn't Have a Guitar Player -- I Want to Be That Guy"

Jones has stated his ambition explicitly, in a Louder interview: "Our generation doesn't have a guitar player -- I want to be that guy." This is not a claim to be the next Hendrix. It is a claim to a structural vacancy -- the absence of a guitar hero in the current generation comparable to Hendrix, Page, Clapton, Van Halen, or Slash in theirs.

The ambition is significant because it is culturally specific. Jones is not claiming to be the best technical guitarist alive. He is claiming a cultural role: the guitarist who defines a generation's relationship to the electric guitar, the way Hendrix defined the 1960s and Van Halen defined the 1980s. In the 2020s, when electronic production and hip-hop dominate popular music, this role is structurally unfilled. Jones is positioning himself to fill it.

Whether he succeeds is beyond the engine's scope. The engine documents what IS, not what might be. What IS: a Seattle-born, Central District-raised, self-taught blues-rock guitarist who has achieved two No. 1 Mainstream Rock singles, played Hendrix's Woodstock Stratocaster, been endorsed by Fender, and explicitly claimed the mantle of the guitar-hero tradition. The engine places him at degree 37, directly after Hendrix, because the CSV recognizes the lineage. The rest is up to Jones.

## Mixed Heritage and Identity

Jones's family background is multiracial. His mother is from Seattle; her mother is from Oklahoma, where the family is Black. Jones has stated in interviews that, based on DNA evidence, his family believes they were enslaved by the Chickasaw Nation, and that his three-times-great-grandmother spoke fluent Muskogee (Creek) and did not speak English. This indigenous and Black American heritage adds another layer to Jones's identity within the Seattle music scene and within the engine's broader mapping of race, place, and music.

Jones has been explicit about the racial dimensions of performing in a genre -- rock -- that was created by Black Americans but is now perceived as white. "Rock and roll is a creation of Black America," he has said. His experience of being constantly asked whether he raps, of having police called on his band for being too loud, of navigating a music industry that struggles to categorize a Black blues-rock guitarist -- all of this echoes Hendrix's experience of having "Red House" excluded from the American release of *Are You Experienced* because the blues didn't fit the commercial image.

The difference is generational. Hendrix navigated the segregated chitlin' circuit and the racial dynamics of 1960s London and America. Jones navigates the post-grunge, post-hip-hop, algorithmically sorted landscape of 2020s music, where genre categories are policed by streaming platforms, radio formats, and audience expectations more than by explicit racial segregation. The structures are different; the underlying dynamic -- a Black artist fighting for space in a genre that Black artists invented -- is the same.

## Engine Analysis: Patterns, Elements, and Accumulation

### The Inheritor (thirty-eighth artist-city pattern)

Jones produces the engine's thirty-eighth artist-city pattern: **The Inheritor**. The Inheritor is an artist who grows up in the same city, the same neighborhood, the same schools as a prior artist of transformative significance, and who explicitly claims continuity with that predecessor's tradition. The Inheritor does not replicate the predecessor's art; they extend it into a new era with new materials. The Inheritor carries the weight of comparison without being crushed by it, by defining success as continuation rather than equivalence.

The Inheritor is the structural complement to The Supernova (degree 36). The Supernova burned intensely and collapsed. The Inheritor arrives in the aftermath -- same neighborhood, same instrument, same genre -- and says: the tradition didn't die. It went underground for a generation. I'm bringing it back.

| Pattern | Degree | Artist | Mode |
|---------|--------|--------|------|
| The Export | 0 | Quincy Jones | Leave for long career |
| The Incubator | 30 | Ray Charles | Pass through briefly, then leave |
| The Deep Root | 35 | Robert Cray | Stay regional, then break through |
| The Supernova | 36 | Jimi Hendrix | Form intensely, leave totally, burn briefly, die young |
| **The Inheritor** | **37** | **Ayron Jones** | **Grow up in the same place, claim the same tradition, extend it forward** |

The PNW-to-global taxonomy now has five modes. The Inheritor adds a temporal dimension that the other four lack: it describes not just spatial relationship (where the artist goes) but temporal relationship (when the artist arrives relative to a predecessor).

### The dynamic grit (thirty-eighth acoustic element)

Jones produces the engine's thirty-eighth acoustic element: **the dynamic grit**. The dynamic grit is the sound of a Fender Stratocaster played through overdriven but contained amplification (Fender Hot Rod Deluxe + DS-1 + Tube Screamer), with deliberate soft/loud dynamics inherited from Seattle's grunge tradition. It is neither the stinging clean of Cray (degree 35) nor the controlled scream of Hendrix (degree 36). It occupies a middle ground: grittier than Cray, more controlled than Hendrix. The distortion is present but shaped; the dynamics create drama through contrast rather than through sustained maximum volume.

The dynamic grit extends the Stratocaster thread to three consecutive degrees (35-36-37) -- the longest single-instrument run in the engine's history.

| Element | Degree | Timbral Quality | Philosophy |
|---------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Stinging clean | 35 | Clean, articulate, precise | Blues is powerful without being loud |
| Controlled scream | 36 | Overdriven, saturated, physically intense | Blues was always this loud |
| **Dynamic grit** | **37** | **Overdriven with soft/loud dynamics** | **Blues is powerful through contrast** |

The three-element Stratocaster arc maps the instrument's full expressive range in blues performance: maximum restraint (35), maximum force (36), and dynamic contrast (37). Three philosophies of the same instrument through the same genre lineage.

### Blue note -- the grunge bend (thirty-seventh deployment)

The blue note at degree 37 is **the grunge bend** -- the pitch bend played through overdriven amplification at moderate volume, shaped by the soft/loud dynamics of grunge. It is not the screaming bend of Hendrix (maximum saturation) or the articulate sting of Cray (maximum clarity). It is a hybrid: the bend begins in relative restraint and swells into distortion, or begins loud and pulls back. The micro-tonal blue-note zone is traversed through dynamic change rather than through either pure clarity or pure saturation.
