The Nasty Nes Lineage

Nasty Nes Rodriguez (degree 126, E=4) brought hip-hop to KCMU in the late 1980s. DJ B-Mello (degree 134, E=5) carries that lineage forward into the KEXP era — eight degrees later, one energy level higher. The genre that Nasty Nes planted on community radio continues to grow.

B-Mello hosts KEXP’s hip-hop programming blocks, bringing the genre’s full breadth to the station: classic golden-age hip-hop, underground and independent releases, Pacific Northwest hip-hop (Sir Mix-A-Lot, Shabazz Palaces, Blue Scholars, Gifted Gab), and contemporary work. The programming is curated with the same depth and respect that characterizes all KEXP shows — applying the station’s curatorial ethos to hip-hop specifically.

The connection to Seattle’s hip-hop scene is essential. In a city whose musical identity is dominated by grunge and indie rock, KEXP’s hip-hop programming is a correction — a reminder that Seattle’s sound includes hip-hop as a major component. B-Mello is the on-air voice of that correction.

The DJ Dimension

B-Mello brings a DJ’s mixing sensibility to KEXP that the other hosts in the streak don’t share. Beat-matching, blending, seamless transitions between tracks. A B-Mello set is constructed as a narrative arc — building energy, creating peaks and valleys, telling a story through the sequence of tracks. This is the DJ art applied to radio programming.

The pacing is faster, the talk breaks are more rhythmically engaged, the energy between tracks is higher. Where a Midday Show segment breathes slowly between songs, a B-Mello hip-hop block moves with kinetic urgency. The host doesn’t just introduce the music — the host is part of the music’s energy. Performance as curation.
