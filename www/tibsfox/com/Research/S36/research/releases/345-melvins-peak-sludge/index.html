Bullhead (1991, Boner Records) — The Foundation of E=9

Bullhead is the Melvins' fourth studio album and their most widely recognized masterwork. The E=9 calibration for degree 345 anchors here first: this is the record that defined sludge metal as a genre and established the maximum that the genre would subsequently try to approach but not exceed.

The album's defining track, "Boris," runs 14 minutes at approximately 50-60 BPM. Buzz Osborne's guitar operates in the lowest tunings available — B standard and below — through heavy fuzz distortion, producing fundamentals in the 30-62 Hz range that are felt as much as heard. Dale Crover's drums maintain absolute rhythmic authority at slow tempo, each stroke isolated and deliberate. The composition uses a single primary riff developed across 14 minutes through minor dynamic variation: no verse-chorus structure, no conventional climax, no resolution in the classical sense. The piece ends as a cessation rather than an arrival. "Boris" is a fully realized compositional statement at the absolute edge of what rock music can be.

"Your Blessened" opens the record, announcing the sonic palette immediately: enormous guitar, slow tempo, production that gives everything a raw, slightly degraded quality that suits the material exactly. "It's Shoved" deploys crushing mid-tempo heaviness in a more structured format. "Zodiac" uses extreme tempo variation as a compositional tool, moving from near-punk velocity to full sludge stop multiple times. The transitions are jarring and deliberate. The listener's sense of temporal stability is actively undermined — this is not incompetence but design.

Lysol (1992, Boner Records / C/Z) — The Extreme Document

Lysol is the Melvins' most extreme commercial release. A single 30-minute composition, continuous on vinyl, released months before Atlantic Records signed the band. It is the last pre-commercial statement and the most extreme one: the record that precedes the accommodation of any audience whatsoever.

The structure is monolithic: no verse-chorus architecture, no conventional song boundaries, no resolution. The piece develops through a series of massive riff cycles and textural passages, incorporating drone-metal elements that anticipate the genre that would emerge in the 2000s. Extended passages where the harmonic content is static and the interest is purely in the texture and volume of sustained sound: this is Lysol's contribution to the drone lineage that runs through Sunn O))), Earth, and — most directly — the Japanese band Boris, who named themselves after the Bullhead track and whose catalog explores the drone territory that Lysol first mapped.

Within the composition, an arrangement of "The Rose" (written by Amanda McBroom, recorded most famously by Bette Midler in 1979) appears without irony or commentary. The Melvins include a gentle, sentimental ballad in the middle of a 30-minute sludge/drone composition because they choose to, and they deploy it with perfect deadpan sincerity. The "Rose" passage is not commentary on the surrounding material; it simply exists within it. This is the Melvins' humor at its most characteristic: unexpected, sincere, and structurally startling.

The Lysol brand threatened legal action over the name; subsequent pressings are officially untitled. The band was indifferent to the legal situation. The album is universally known as Lysol.

The Influence Network — Who the Melvins Built

The E=9 calibration for the Melvins in 1991-1992 acknowledges not only what these records are but what they produced. The influence network from Bullhead and Lysol runs in multiple directions simultaneously:

Nirvana (E=10, degree 348): Kurt Cobain's musical vocabulary was built on Buzz Osborne's teaching. The Melvins were the first band Cobain ever saw live. *Bleach* (1989) is a Melvins-influenced document. The approach to distortion, tempo variation, and riff-based composition on *Nevermind* was learned from watching the Melvins. The E=10 ceiling at degree 348 is downstream from the E=9 foundation at degree 345.

Dave Grohl (Nirvana drummer): Grohl's drumming on *Nevermind* — the explosive, precision-power approach that made the album's rhythm section so distinctive — was shaped in part by years of watching and listening to Dale Crover. The genealogy: Crover's *Bullhead* drumming → Grohl's *Nevermind* drumming.

Sleep, Electric Wizard, Kyuss, Sunn O))): The sludge/drone genre that developed in the 1990s and 2000s traces directly to the Melvins' *Bullhead*/*Lysol* approach. Sleep's *Dopesmoker* (recorded 1996, released 2003) is the next major document in the lineage. Earth's drone work throughout the 1990s is the other direct branch. The Melvins established the maximum; the genre has been exploring what falls below it ever since.

Boris (the band): Named directly after the *Bullhead* track. The Tokyo trio became one of the most important heavy bands of the 2000s and 2010s, exploring the drone territory that the Melvins mapped in *Lysol*. The naming is tribute and genealogy simultaneously.

Aberdeen and the Pacific Infrasound

The pairing is geographic and acoustic simultaneously: Aberdeen sits at Grays Harbor, where the Chehalis and Wishkah Rivers meet near the Pacific coast. The infrasound of the open Pacific — continuous, low-frequency, below the threshold of human hearing but felt in the body — is the ambient soundscape of the town that produced the Melvins.

The Melvins' guitar fundamentals in the *Bullhead* era (30-62 Hz) overlap with the upper range of infrasound (below 20 Hz) and the lower range of human hearing. The band's sonic territory is the frequency range where the Pacific Ocean's continuous low-frequency output meets the lower edge of what human music can produce. The E=18 pairing at degree 345 — E=9 + E=9 — is acoustically coherent: both tracks operate at the low-frequency extreme of their respective ranges, and both originate in the same coastal geography.
