## "LOUIE LOUIE" -- THE MOST FAMOUS GARAGE ROCK SONG

### A Recording That Changed Everything

"Louie Louie" is not merely the Kingsmen's best-known song -- it is the defining cultural artifact of American garage rock. Understanding "Louie Louie" is understanding the Kingsmen:

- **The original.** "Louie Louie" was written by Richard Berry, a Los Angeles rhythm and blues musician, in 1955. Berry wrote the song as a Jamaican-style calypso ballad, told from the perspective of a sailor describing his longing for his girl back home on the island while sailing back to her. Berry recorded it in 1956 for Flip Records. It was a minor regional hit on the West Coast and in the Pacific Northwest, where it became a dance staple in the early 1960s.
- **The Northwest connection.** Before the Kingsmen, "Louie Louie" was already part of the Pacific Northwest musical vocabulary. The Wailers (degree 242) had recorded a version. Rockin' Robin Roberts and other Northwest musicians had performed it. The song circulated in the PNW dance hall circuit and was a known quantity to Northwest rock audiences. When the Kingsmen recorded it in 1963, they were not discovering an unknown song -- they were recording the song that every Northwest band already knew.
- **The Kingsmen recording (1963).** The Kingsmen's version was recorded at Northwestern Inc. in Portland in April 1963 -- a recording that famously cost $50 and took less than an hour. The recording has a specific quality that is responsible for its enduring fame: the microphone was positioned above the band rather than in the conventional positions, creating a distance and room-sound that is unique. Lead singer Jack Ely sang too close to the boom microphone and pitched his voice at an angle that created the slurred, impossible-to-decipher vocal delivery that would make the song notorious.
- **The $50 recording.** The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" was made on a budget so small that most professional recordings of the era wouldn't round it up. The rough, barely-produced quality of the recording -- the room sound, the indistinct vocals, the guitar that occasionally feels like it's about to fall apart -- is exactly what made it immortal. The constraints produced the aesthetic. The limitations were the art.
- **Jack Ely's vocal.** The famous incomprehensibility of the Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" lyrics is not accidental. Jack Ely was wearing braces at the time, singing at an upward angle to a microphone mounted too high, and pushing his voice past the point of clear articulation. The result is a vocal performance that sounds dirty, urgent, and deliberately obscured -- even though the lyrics themselves are perfectly innocent.

(Sources: Richard Berry biography; "Louie Louie" 1955 original; Jamaican calypso influence; Wailers prior version; Northwestern Inc. Portland studio; $50 recording cost; Jack Ely braces and microphone position; recording quality as aesthetic)

## THE FBI INVESTIGATION

### Two Years, $100,000, and No Conclusion

The "Louie Louie" FBI investigation is one of the most absurd episodes in American cultural history:

- **The complaint.** In 1963-64, a parent in Indiana filed a complaint with the FBI claiming that "Louie Louie" contained obscene lyrics. The complaint alleged that the unintelligible portions of Ely's vocal contained sexual content that was deliberately obscured to avoid censorship. The parent claimed to have played the record at 33 1/3 rpm, at 45 rpm, and at 78 rpm to decode the alleged obscenities.
- **The investigation begins.** The FBI under J. Edgar Hoover opened a formal investigation. FBI agents across the country were assigned to analyze the recording, transcribe the lyrics, and determine whether the song violated federal obscenity laws. This investigation was not small or brief -- it involved multiple field offices, numerous agents, and extended for approximately two years (1964-1966).
- **The transcription problem.** FBI analysts could not agree on what the lyrics said. Different agents transcribed different words. Some thought they detected obscenities; others found the same passages completely innocent. The sound quality of the recording -- the room reverb, Ely's slurred delivery -- made definitive transcription impossible.
- **The conclusion.** In May 1965, the FBI published its conclusion: "The lyrics of the recording could not be interpreted" and "therefore it was not possible to determine whether this recording was obscene." In other words: after two years and approximately $100,000 in federal resources, the FBI could not determine what the Kingsmen were saying in "Louie Louie." The investigation was closed without charges.
- **The legacy of the investigation.** The FBI investigation made "Louie Louie" more famous, not less. By investigating the song, the federal government confirmed to American teenagers that it was worth listening to. Sales, already strong, accelerated. The notoriety generated by the FBI investigation is arguably the most effective free advertising campaign in the history of American popular music.
- **The actual lyrics.** The lyrics of "Louie Louie" (as written by Richard Berry in 1955) are entirely innocent: a sailor telling the bartender "Louie" about his girlfriend on the island and his longing to return to her. There is nothing obscene in them. The scandal was entirely a product of Jack Ely's incomprehensible delivery and the conspiracy-minded interpretation of that incomprehensibility.
- **The Hoover era context.** The "Louie Louie" investigation was not an isolated aberration -- it was consistent with the FBI's investigation of popular culture for communist influence, sexual content, and "subversive" material throughout the Cold War era. The investigation of a $50 garage rock recording for obscenity fits perfectly into the Hoover FBI's pattern of treating popular culture as a national security threat.

(Sources: Indiana parent complaint; FBI investigation opening 1964; field office assignments; transcription failures; "lyrics could not be interpreted" conclusion May 1965; investigation cost estimates; free advertising effect; Richard Berry's actual innocent lyrics; Jack Ely braces explanation; Hoover era FBI popular culture investigations)

## THE BAND

### The Kingsmen's History

- **Formation.** The Kingsmen formed in Portland, Oregon in 1959 -- the same year as the Wailers and the Frantics, the founding year of the Northwest Sound. The founding lineup included Jack Ely (vocals, guitar) and Lynn Easton (drums), who would become the two central figures of the band's early history.
- **Jack Ely (vocals).** The voice of "Louie Louie" -- one of the most recognizable voices in the history of American rock, entirely by accident. Ely's vocal delivery on "Louie Louie" is the direct result of singing at the wrong angle to a microphone positioned incorrectly. He left the band in 1964, before the song's full commercial peak, in a dispute over management and royalties.
- **Lynn Easton (drums).** The band's business and organizational center. After Ely's departure, Easton maintained the Kingsmen as a going concern, cycling through various lineups while continuing to release records and tour. The legal and commercial continuity of the Kingsmen from 1964 onward is largely Easton's achievement.
- **Don Gallucci (keyboards).** The organist whose keyboard work is one of the defining sounds of the "Louie Louie" recording. Gallucci was reportedly 14 or 15 years old when the song was recorded -- which adds another layer of improbability to the FBI's investigation of obscene content (the keyboard player was a child).
- **Norm Sundholm (bass), Mike Mitchell (guitar).** The rhythm section and lead guitar in the core lineup.

(Sources: Kingsmen formation Portland 1959; Jack Ely biography; Lynn Easton; Don Gallucci age (14-15); "Louie Louie" recording lineup; Ely departure 1964; Easton's organizational role)

## THE WAND RECORDS STORY

### From Portland to New York to National Hit

The commercial trajectory of "Louie Louie" illustrates how the national pop music business worked in 1963:

- **The regional success.** The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" was initially released locally in Portland. It was a moderate regional hit -- performing well in the Pacific Northwest and parts of the Midwest where the song already had some recognition from earlier versions.
- **Wand Records.** Wand Records was a subsidiary of Scepter Records, a New York independent with significant experience distributing R&B and pop singles nationally. When Wand picked up "Louie Louie" for national distribution in 1963, the song was positioned for national attention.
- **The chart trajectory.** "Louie Louie" reached #2 on the Billboard Hot 100 in December 1963 -- the second-highest position in the chart, blocked from #1 by the Beatles' early American releases. The song spent multiple weeks in the Top 10. Its national sales made it the most commercially successful Pacific Northwest rock single of the era -- surpassing even the Wailers' "#36 with Tall Cool One" (1959) by a significant margin.
- **The radio play problem.** Many radio stations refused to play "Louie Louie" due to the alleged obscenity controversy, which paradoxically made the song even more desirable to young listeners. The refusal to play a song is the most effective advertisement a radio station can give it.
- **The cover version proliferation.** "Louie Louie" has been covered more times than almost any other rock song in history. The Kingsmen's version spawned hundreds of covers -- from the Beach Boys to the Kinks to Black Flag to Motley Crue to Iggy Pop. The song's simple, memorably riff (just three chords: A, D, Em) and its three-minute structure make it accessible to virtually any band. "Louie Louie" is the most democratic rock song ever written: any band can play it, and many bands have recorded it as their first original-quality recording.

(Sources: "Louie Louie" regional success; Wand Records Scepter subsidiary; national distribution mechanism; Billboard Hot 100 #2 December 1963; Beatles blocking from #1; radio play refusals; cover version history; three-chord structure accessibility)

## RICHARD BERRY AND THE SONG'S FULL HISTORY

### The Author Who Got Nothing

- **Richard Berry (1935-1997).** The Los Angeles R&B musician who wrote "Louie Louie" in 1955. Berry sold the rights to the song for a small sum in the late 1950s when he needed money. By the time the Kingsmen's version made "Louie Louie" one of the most famous rock songs in history, Berry had no financial stake in it -- he had sold his rights. He received nothing from the song's enormous commercial success.
- **The partial correction.** In the 1980s and 1990s, a campaign led by Berry and his supporters resulted in some financial recognition for his authorship, though the legal situation was complex. Berry received some compensation before his death in 1997, but the injustice of an artist receiving nothing from the song that made other people famous and wealthy is one of the less flattering chapters in American music industry history.
- **The Sonics-Kingsmen connection.** Berry also wrote "Have Love Will Travel," which was covered by the Sonics at degree 241. The Berry connection links The Sonics and The Kingsmen -- the two E=7-8 bookends of the garage era in the engine -- through a single songwriter.
- **"Louie Louie" as cultural property.** The song has become so embedded in American popular culture that it functions as something beyond a pop song. It is the unofficial anthem of the Pacific Northwest. It is the song played at countless bars, sports events, and parties. Washington State legislators have periodically introduced bills to name "Louie Louie" the official state song of Washington (despite the song being from Oregon). The song belongs to everyone and to no one -- and Richard Berry, its author, owned it for the briefest period.

(Sources: Richard Berry biography 1935-1997; rights sale for small sum; received nothing from Kingsmen version; 1980s-90s partial compensation campaign; "Have Love Will Travel" Berry connection to Sonics; "Louie Louie" as unofficial PNW anthem; Washington State official song campaigns)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### The Most Famous Song in PNW Rock History

- **"Louie Louie" as cultural monument.** There are more famous rock songs than "Louie Louie" -- but there is no rock song more culturally embedded in the American experience as an artifact of pure teenage energy, rough production, and deliberate (or accidental) incomprehensibility. "Louie Louie" is not merely a song. It is a cultural symbol of what rock and roll could be when produced with no resources, no polish, and no expectation of national fame.
- **The accessibility thesis.** "Louie Louie" is three chords. It has a simple, driving rhythm. It is in a key playable on basic guitar. It is -- by design or accident -- the most accessible great rock song ever recorded. Every generation rediscovers it. Every band eventually plays it. It is the lowest common denominator of rock and roll in the best possible sense.
- **The FBI as inadvertent promoter.** The FBI's investigation did more to immortalize "Louie Louie" than any promotional campaign could have. By treating the song as potentially obscene, the government confirmed to every teenager in America that the song was exciting, dangerous, and worth listening to. The Hoover FBI was the best publicist the Kingsmen ever had.
- **Portland's permanent contribution.** "Louie Louie" is Portland's permanent contribution to American musical culture. Where Tacoma contributed the proto-punk prototype (Sonics) and the national chart breakthrough (Wailers), and Seattle contributed the surf-garage synthesis (Frantics), Portland contributed the song that will be played at bars and parties until the end of rock and roll. The Pacific Northwest garage rock era is complete.

(Sources: "Louie Louie" cultural monument; accessibility thesis three chords; FBI inadvertent promotion; Portland's permanent contribution; Pacific Northwest garage rock era complete at 244)
