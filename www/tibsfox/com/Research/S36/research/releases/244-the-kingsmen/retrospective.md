## The Two Hundred Forty-Fourth Degree: The Most Famous Song

### "Louie Louie." #2 Billboard Hot 100. Three chords. $50. Portland, 1963. Jack Ely's braces and a mispositioned microphone. The FBI's two-year investigation. The most famous garage rock recording ever made. The unofficial anthem of the Pacific Northwest. Richard Berry's song, the Kingsmen's immortality. The PNW garage rock era is complete.

---

## What Worked

1. **The FBI investigation story is the entry's most culturally important element.** Two years, $100,000, and a conclusion that the lyrics could not be determined -- by investigating "Louie Louie," the federal government confirmed to every teenager in America that the song was exciting, dangerous, and worth listening to. The investigation is the most inadvertently effective marketing campaign in American music history.
2. **The $50 recording cost is the entry's most important production note.** The limitations produced the aesthetic. Microphone too high, braces affecting diction, room reverb instead of studio control -- every "flaw" in the recording is the reason it works. This is the most concentrated example of the garage rock aesthetic in the engine: the rough production is the art.
3. **The Richard Berry connection to degree 241 (The Sonics, "Have Love Will Travel") is the entry's most important cross-reference.** One songwriter links the proto-punk maximum and the most famous garage recording. Both bands covered Berry material. Both songs are central to the Northwest Sound. Berry sold his rights and received little from either.
4. **The garage rock era completion table (241-244) is the entry's most useful structural element.** Tacoma (2 bands) + Seattle (1 band) + Portland (1 band) = the complete geographic map of the Northwest Sound.

## Discoveries

1. **Don Gallucci was reportedly 14-15 years old when "Louie Louie" was recorded.** The keyboard player on the recording the FBI investigated for obscene content was a child. This adds a layer of absurdity to the already absurd investigation that is difficult to overstate.
2. **Paul Revere and the Raiders may have recorded their version of "Louie Louie" at the same Portland studio, possibly the same day.** The Kingsmen's version became definitive; the Raiders' version was recorded by musicians who would soon move to Los Angeles and achieve national fame through conventional means. Two bands, one song, one studio day, dramatically different outcomes.
3. **"Louie Louie" was already a Pacific Northwest standard before the Kingsmen recorded it.** The Wailers (242) had already recorded it. The song circulated in the PNW dance hall circuit for years before April 1963. The Kingsmen weren't discovering the song -- they were recording the song everyone already knew in a way that accidentally made it eternal.
