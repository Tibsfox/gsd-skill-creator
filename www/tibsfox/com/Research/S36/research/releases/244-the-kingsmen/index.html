The Song That Never Dies

The Kingsmen did not invent "Louie Louie" — Richard Berry wrote it in Los Angeles in 1955, as a Jamaican-style calypso ballad from a sailor's perspective. The Wailers (degree 242) had already recorded it. But when the Kingsmen recorded it in April 1963 at Northwestern Inc. in Portland for $50, they created something that Richard Berry's original and every prior cover had not: a recording so perfectly rough, so exquisitely uncertain in its vocal delivery, that it would become the most covered rock song in American history and the subject of a two-year federal investigation.

"The lyrics of the recording could not be interpreted." — FBI conclusion, May 1965, after two years and approximately $100,000 investigating "Louie Louie" for obscene content.

Jack Ely was wearing braces. The microphone was mounted too high. Ely sang at an upward angle that blurred every word. The result is one of the most intelligible-yet-unintelligible vocal performances in the history of recorded music: you know he's saying words; you're not sure which ones.

THE FBI INVESTIGATION (1964–1965)

A parent in Indiana filed a complaint alleging obscene lyrics. J. Edgar Hoover's FBI opened a formal investigation. Field offices across the country transcribed the lyrics. Agents disagreed on what the words were. Different playback speeds produced different interpretations. After two years and an estimated $100,000 in federal resources, the conclusion was published in May 1965: the lyrics could not be determined. No charges. The investigation made "Louie Louie" immortal. The FBI was the best publicity campaign the Kingsmen ever had.

Richard Berry & The Song's Full History

Richard Berry (1935-1997) wrote "Louie Louie" in 1955 and sold the rights for a small sum when he needed money. By the time the Kingsmen's version became a #2 national hit and one of the most covered songs in history, Berry had no financial stake. He received little from the song that made other people famous. Berry also wrote "Have Love Will Travel," covered by The Sonics at degree 241. One songwriter connects both ends of the Northwest Sound's range: the proto-punk maximum (Sonics) and the most famous garage single (Kingsmen).

The $50 Recording

Northwestern Inc., Portland, 1963

The most famous garage rock recording was made for $50 at a small Portland studio. Microphone positioned too high. Jack Ely sang at an angle. The limitations were the art.

Don Gallucci, Keyboard (age 14)

The Youngest Obscenity Suspect

The organ player on "Louie Louie" was reportedly 14 or 15 years old when recorded. The FBI investigated a recording on which the keyboard player was a child.

Three Chords: A-D-Em

The Democratic Song

Any band can play "Louie Louie." The three-chord structure is accessible to every skill level. It has been covered hundreds of times by artists from the Beach Boys to Black Flag to Motley Crue.

Wand Records / Scepter

National Distribution

Wand (Scepter subsidiary) distributed nationally, getting "Louie Louie" to #2. The same mechanism that got The Wailers' "Tall Cool One" to #36 four years earlier.
