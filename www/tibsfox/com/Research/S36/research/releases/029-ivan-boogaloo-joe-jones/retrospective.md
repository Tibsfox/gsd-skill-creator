## Prestige Records: The Deepest Label Halo in the Engine

### What a Major Label Means for a Minor Artist

The most significant structural finding at degree 29 is not about Jones himself but about **Prestige Records** and the label halo effect. Prestige's catalog includes Miles Davis (1951-1956), John Coltrane (1957-1958), Thelonious Monk (1952-1954), and Sonny Rollins (1951-1956) -- four of the most important jazz musicians in history. Jones' seven albums sit in this same catalog. On streaming platforms, the algorithm treats all Prestige releases as related content, meaning a listener who discovers Coltrane may be recommended Jones.

This is the first time the engine has documented how **label infrastructure amplifies marginal artists**. Jones is not in the jazz canon. He does not appear in most jazz histories. But he is on Prestige, and Prestige is canonical. The label gives him a discoverability that his individual reputation cannot.

- **Situation:** Jones recorded for Prestige Records, the most historically significant jazz label to appear in the engine. His albums sit in the same catalog as Davis, Coltrane, Monk, and Rollins.
- **Root Cause:** Prestige's business model in the late 1960s-1970s was to produce affordable soul-jazz and funk albums for the jukebox and budget market. Jones was part of this assembly line, not the label's canonical era (1949-1960).
- **Recommendation:** Document the label halo effect as a new theory node. Track whether other major-label artists appear in the engine and whether the halo effect shapes their discoverability.
- **Carry Item:** PATTERN -- label halo effect. Prestige = deepest halo in the engine. Track.

## The Boogaloo: A Dance Craze as Genre Name

### Functional Naming in Jazz

Jones' performing name "Boogaloo Joe" introduces the concept of **functional naming** -- a name that describes what the music does to the listener's body rather than who the musician is. The boogaloo was a 1966-1969 dance craze centered in New York and Newark. By adopting it as a performing name, Jones declared his guitar's function: this music is for dancing.

No prior artist in the engine has a functional name. All prior names are identity labels (birth names, band names, stage names with personal significance). "Boogaloo Joe" is the first name that serves as a kinetic instruction.

- **Situation:** Jones' performing name is "Boogaloo Joe" -- a compound of a dance (boogaloo) and a personal name (Joe). The name describes the music's physical effect on the audience.
- **Root Cause:** The soul-jazz market of the late 1960s was a dance-music market. Recordings were sold to jukeboxes and nightclubs. A name that promises dancing is functional marketing. Album titles follow the same pattern: "Right On Brother," "What It Is," "Snake Rhythm Rock."
- **Recommendation:** Document boogaloo as functional naming. Track whether other functional names appear in the engine.
- **Carry Item:** PATTERN -- functional naming. First in engine. Track.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **Prestige Records discography is well-documented.** AllMusic, Discogs, and jazz discography databases provide complete session details (personnel, dates, catalog numbers) for all seven Jones leader dates. The assembly-line nature of Prestige's production means the documentation is systematic rather than sporadic.

2. **The boogaloo dance craze is well-documented in Latin music scholarship.** The cultural context for Jones' performing name is available through multiple sources. The connection between the dance craze and the performing name is self-evident (the name literally contains the word "boogaloo").

3. **The Grant Green / George Benson lineage provides sonic context.** Even without listening to Jones' recordings, the lineage placement (between Green's purity and Benson's commercialism) provides a reliable description of the guitar style.

4. **The sideman cross-references are revealing.** Bernard Purdie and Idris Muhammad on Jones sessions connect him to the broader R&B and soul recording infrastructure of the era. These are not obscure sidemen -- they are among the most documented session musicians in American music.

5. **The geographic transparency standard (established at degree 20) provided a framework** for handling the Seattle discrepancy. Rather than fabricating a connection, the engine documents the gap and introduces a new pattern (The Catalog) to explain the relationship between a recording artist and a city they may never have visited.

### Source Quality

6. **Discographic sources are strong.** Album details, personnel, dates, and catalog numbers are confirmed across multiple independent databases.

7. **Biographical sources are weak.** No interview, profile, obituary, or first-person account from Jones has been located. Birth year is estimated. Post-1974 career is blank.
