## THE SUPERGROUP -- THE LINEUP

### Four Musicians from Three Legendary Bands

Mad Season's lineup is the engine's most significant cross-band assembly to date:

- **Layne Staley (vocals).** Alice in Chains vocalist. One of the most distinctive voices in rock history -- a dark baritone capable of extraordinary emotional range, from whispering vulnerability to full-throated anguish. Staley co-wrote the Alice in Chains catalog alongside Jerry Cantrell: *Facelift* (1990), *Dirt* (1992), *Jar of Flies* (1994), *Alice in Chains* (1995). His vocal approach influenced an entire generation of rock singers. By the time of Mad Season (1994), Staley was already in the grip of severe heroin addiction -- the addiction that would eventually kill him in April 2002.
- **Mike McCready (guitar).** Pearl Jam lead guitarist. McCready's guitar work is the emotional spine of Pearl Jam's sound -- blues-rooted, technically accomplished, capable of both delicate melodic phrasing and explosive lead work. On *Above*, McCready's guitar is the architectural element: it structures the space within which Staley's voice moves. McCready was himself in recovery from alcohol and drug addiction during the Mad Season period.
- **Barrett Martin (drums).** Screaming Trees drummer (degrees 219 adjacent -- the Trees had connections to the Seattle scene). Martin is also a multi-instrumentalist, composer, and music researcher with academic training. He brought a different rhythmic vocabulary to Mad Season -- more jazz-adjacent, more dynamic, less straightforwardly rock than typical grunge drumming. Martin's drumming on *Above* gives the album its meditative quality.
- **John Baker Saunders (bass).** The least commercially known member, but crucial to *Above*'s sound. Saunders was a Seattle-based bassist who had worked with various acts. He brought a heavy, melodic bass approach that grounds the album's more atmospheric passages. Saunders died in January 1999 of a drug overdose -- the first member of Mad Season to die.

(Sources: Layne Staley biography; Mike McCready biography; Barrett Martin; John Baker Saunders; Alice in Chains discography; Pearl Jam discography; Screaming Trees)

## *ABOVE* (1995, COLUMBIA RECORDS) -- THE ALBUM

### Born Out of Addiction and Recovery

*Above* is one of the most emotionally heavy albums in Seattle's catalog:

- **Context.** The album was conceived when McCready was in residence at a rehabilitation center in Minnesota (Hazelden) in late 1994. Staley, also dealing with severe addiction, was simultaneously in treatment at a different facility. The two musicians connected through their shared experience of addiction and the Seattle scene's losses (Andrew Wood in 1990, Kurt Cobain in April 1994 -- just months before *Above* was recorded).
- **The title.** *Above* has multiple readings: above the addiction, above the noise of the commercial moment, above the ground (a counterpoint to the underworld of heroin's grip). Staley's lyrics are drenched in aquatic and atmospheric imagery -- water, sky, suspended states. The title is both aspirational and descriptive.
- **Key tracks:**
  - *Wake Up* -- The opening track. A mid-tempo meditation on consciousness and numbness. Staley's vocal enters over McCready's guitar in a way that feels like surfacing.
  - *I Don't Know Anything* -- The admission of uncertainty that is one of the album's most honest moments. Staley's voice stripped of pretense.
  - *Long Gone Day* -- The album's most melodic and accessible track. A horn section (an unusual choice for Seattle rock) broadens the sonic palette.
  - *River of Deceit* -- The signature track. Saunders' bass opens the song alone. Staley's lyric about self-deception and the river that carries it. McCready's guitar builds through the verse into a chorus that feels like resignation. The most-played Mad Season song.
  - *Lifeless Dead* -- The darkest track. The addiction speaking directly, without the mediation of recovery's language.
  - *I'm Above* -- The album closes with the title statement. Aspirational, fragile, uncertain whether it is true.
- **"River of Deceit" as capstone.** The lyric "my pain is self-chosen / at least, so the prophet says" is one of the most honest articulations of addiction's psychology in rock music. The word "self-chosen" carries enormous weight -- the moral judgment of addiction, the counter-argument to that judgment, and the exhaustion of the debate, all in three words.

(Sources: Above 1995; Columbia Records; McCready Hazelden; Staley addiction timeline; track analysis; River of Deceit lyric; aquatic imagery; Kurt Cobain death April 1994)

## STALEY'S LYRIC -- AQUATIC IMAGERY

### Water, Sky, and the Suspended State

The instruction to pair Mad Season with Osprey notes that "Staley's lyrics are drenched in aquatic imagery." This is accurate and worth examining:

- **Water in the lyrics.** *River of Deceit* (the river), *Above* (implied sky/water boundary), *Long Gone Day* (light and water), *Wake Up* (submersion and surfacing). The aquatic imagery pervades the album. Water is the medium of both addiction (submersion, drowning, loss of agency in current) and recovery (surfacing, clarity, flow).
- **The Pacific Northwest context.** Seattle is a water city -- Elliott Bay, Lake Washington, the Puget Sound, the rivers of the Cascade foothills. Staley's aquatic imagery is geographically grounded. The water he is imaging is the water of his home environment.
- **Sky and elevation.** *Above* implies height. McCready's guitar sounds like it is in the air. Staley's voice aspires toward lightness even when the lyrics are heaviest. The Osprey -- a bird of the water's surface, hunting from the air -- is an apt pair: it lives in the zone between water and sky, exactly where Staley's lyrics position their narrator.
- **Suspense.** The album's emotional register is frequently suspended -- neither underwater nor above, but in the moment between submersion and surfacing. This is addiction's experiential quality: the suspended present, the inability to be fully in one state or the other.

(Sources: Above lyrics; aquatic imagery analysis; Seattle water geography; Osprey ecology; suspension and addiction; Staley vocal performance)

## THE SEATTLE SCENE LOSSES -- THE CONTEXT OF ABOVE

### 1990-1994: Four Years of Grief

*Above* was recorded in a Seattle already deep in mourning:

- **Andrew Wood (March 1990).** Mother Love Bone vocalist. Heroin overdose. Wood's death is the first major tragedy of the Seattle rock community -- the before/after moment. Pearl Jam formed from Mother Love Bone's ashes. Wood's death haunts the entire subsequent decade.
- **Kurt Cobain (April 1994).** Nirvana vocalist. Death by suicide. The most commercially visible loss of the Seattle scene. Cobain's death brought global attention to the Seattle scene's relationship with depression, addiction, and the pressures of sudden commercial success.
- **Mad Season forms (late 1994).** In the months immediately following Cobain's death. The timing is not coincidental -- the album is partly a response to the grief that had accumulated in the Seattle community, an attempt to find something worth saying after that level of loss.
- **What Above means in 1995.** By the time *Above* was released in March 1995, Seattle had lost Wood and Cobain. The music press was already writing about the "Seattle jinx" and the dark underside of grunge's commercial triumph. *Above* is an honest document of that darkness -- not exploitative, not commercial, but truthful about what addiction and loss feel like from the inside.

(Sources: Andrew Wood death March 1990; Kurt Cobain death April 1994; Seattle grief timeline; Above release March 1995; scene context)

## THE COLUMBIA RECORDS DEAL

### Major-Label Context for an Unusual Album

- **Columbia Records.** One of the oldest and most prestigious major labels in the world (founded 1889). Columbia's rock roster in the mid-1990s included Soundgarden (who had moved to A&M), Pearl Jam (on Epic, a Columbia subsidiary), and others. Columbia signing Mad Season gave the band full major-label resources.
- **Pearl Jam's label connection.** Epic Records (Pearl Jam's label) is a Columbia subsidiary. Mike McCready's involvement with Mad Season while active with Pearl Jam created a label adjacency -- Epic/Columbia had aligned interests in the project.
- **Commercial performance.** *Above* sold approximately 800,000 copies in the US. This is significant but well below the commercial peaks of the constituent bands (AIC, Pearl Jam). *Above* is a cult record -- known and loved by the fans who found it, less broadly known than the bands it came from.
- **One album, one run.** Mad Season's Columbia deal was for the one album. The band's dissolution after the album was partly due to Staley's deteriorating health (his addiction was worsening throughout 1994-1995) and the members' commitments to their primary bands.

(Sources: Columbia Records history; Epic Records Columbia relationship; Above sales figures; Staley health decline; one-album structure)

## JOHN BAKER SAUNDERS AND BARRETT MARTIN

### The Rhythm Section That Made Above

- **Saunders' bass.** John Baker Saunders brought a heavy, melodic bass approach that is the unsung foundation of *Above*. His bass on "River of Deceit" -- the song opens with his instrument alone -- is one of the more memorable bass intros in Seattle rock. Saunders died in January 1999 of a heroin overdose. He was 34.
- **Martin's drumming.** Barrett Martin's academic music training is audible in his drumming -- there is a jazz-inflected sophistication in his rhythmic choices, a willingness to drop into unusual patterns, to let the kit breathe. This is not typical grunge drumming. It is more patient, more compositionally considered.
- **Martin post-Mad Season.** Barrett Martin went on to record and perform across multiple genres, including a focus on music from the Amazon basin and indigenous traditions of the Americas. He is an unusually broad musician. His scholarship on the relationship between music and ecology resonates with the engine's SPS (Sound of Puget Sound) mission.

(Sources: John Baker Saunders biography; Saunders death January 1999; Barrett Martin biography; Martin post-Mad Season career; Martin ethnomusicology)
