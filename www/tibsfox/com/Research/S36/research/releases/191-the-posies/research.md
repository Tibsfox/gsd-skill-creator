## FIRST POWER POP -- GENRE STAGE 103

### The Third Genre in Three Degrees

Degree 191 introduces power pop to the engine:

- **FIRST POWER POP.** The Posies are the first power pop band in the S36 engine. Genre Stage 103. Three genres in three degrees: grunge (189), indie pop (190), power pop (191). The post-hip-hop era is a genre explosion.
- **Power pop defined.** Melodic rock with strong harmonies, jangly guitars, and song structures inherited from the Beatles, Big Star, and the Raspberries. Power pop is pop songwriting with rock instrumentation -- hooks as architecture.
- **The Posies as exemplars.** Jon Auer and Ken Stringfellow are among the finest practitioners of power pop ever produced. Their harmonies, guitar interplay, and songwriting craft place them in the lineage of Lennon/McCartney, Chilton/Bell (Big Star), and Westerberg (Replacements).
- **Three genres, three neighborhoods/cities.** Grunge from Central District (189). Indie pop from Capitol Hill (190). Power pop from Bellingham (191). The geographic diversification mirrors the genre diversification.

## E=5 -- GORGEOUS PRECISION

### Why E=5

The Posies register at E=5:

- **Songwriting craft.** Auer and Stringfellow write songs of extraordinary melodic sophistication. The chord progressions are complex but never showy. The melodies are immediate but reveal new layers on repeated listening. This is craft at its highest level.
- **Harmonies.** The Auer/Stringfellow vocal harmonies are among the finest in rock music. Two voices that interlock with effortless precision. The harmonies are not decoration -- they are structural.
- **Frosting on the Beater (1993).** The masterpiece. Twelve tracks of perfectly constructed power pop. "Dream All Day," "Solar Sister," "Flavor of the Month" -- every track is a potential single. One of the best power pop albums ever made.
- **Dear 23 (1990).** The debut on DGC. Already fully formed. "Golden Blunders," "Apology" -- the songwriting was mature from the start.
- **Big Star connection.** Auer and Stringfellow became members of the reformed Big Star (Alex Chilton and Jude Cola's legendary band), replacing the deceased Chris Bell. The Posies literally joined the lineage of power pop's greatest band.
- **PopLlama.** The Posies' early work was on PopLlama, a Seattle indie label that specialized in pop-oriented guitar music. PopLlama enters the engine with The Posies.
- **Why E=5?** Extraordinary craft, beautiful harmonies, essential albums. But power pop is not a high-energy genre -- it is a precision genre. The Posies never had a massive commercial breakthrough. The audience is devoted but not massive. E=5 reflects brilliance within a genre that prizes beauty over intensity.

(Sources: The Posies discography; *Frosting on the Beater* reception; Big Star connection; PopLlama label; engine internal analysis)

## BELLINGHAM ENTERS

### A New City

Degree 191 introduces Bellingham to the engine:

- **Bellingham, Washington.** College town at the northern tip of the I-5 corridor, 90 miles north of Seattle, 20 miles south of the Canadian border. Home to Western Washington University.
- **The Posies formed in Bellingham.** Auer and Stringfellow met in high school in Bellingham and formed The Posies there. Their early recordings and first performances were Bellingham-based before they relocated to Seattle.
- **Bellingham music scene.** A small but dedicated music scene built around college radio (KUGS), house shows, and local venues. The Posies, Death Cab for Cutie (Ben Gibbard attended WWU), and other acts have roots in Bellingham.
- **First city outside Seattle.** The engine has been Seattle-specific until now. Bellingham's entry expands the geographic scope to the broader Puget Sound region. The engine is no longer just the Sound of Seattle -- it is the Sound of Puget Sound.

## THE BIG STAR CONNECTION

### Joining the Lineage

The Posies' connection to Big Star is the most significant genealogical link in power pop:

- **Big Star.** Alex Chilton and Chris Bell formed Big Star in Memphis in 1971. Three albums (*#1 Record*, *Radio City*, *Third/Sister Lovers*) that defined power pop and influenced generations. Commercial failure during their active years. Posthumous recognition as one of the most important bands in rock history.
- **The reformation.** When Big Star reformed in the 1990s (after Chris Bell's death in 1978), Alex Chilton invited Jon Auer and Ken Stringfellow to fill out the lineup. The Posies became Big Star.
- **The passing of the torch.** Auer and Stringfellow playing in Big Star was not just a tribute -- it was a continuation. The genealogy is direct: Big Star invented power pop; The Posies inherited it.
- **After Chilton.** Alex Chilton died in 2010. The Big Star chapter closed. But The Posies continue, carrying the lineage forward.

(Sources: Big Star history; reformation lineup; Auer/Stringfellow participation; power pop genealogy; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of The Posies

The Posies' sonic profile is power pop at its purest:

- **Guitars.** Jangly, ringing, interlocking. Two guitars (Auer and Stringfellow) that weave around each other. Clean to slightly overdriven. Rickenbacker-influenced tone. The guitars serve the melodies.
- **Vocals.** Two-part harmonies that define the sound. Auer and Stringfellow's voices blend seamlessly. Lead vocals alternate between them. The harmonies are tight, precise, and emotionally resonant.
- **Song structure.** Verse-chorus-verse with bridges, pre-choruses, key changes, and unexpected harmonic turns. The structures are sophisticated but feel natural. Nothing forced.
- **BPM range.** 90-140 BPM. Mid-tempo to uptempo. The tempos serve the melodies -- never rushed, never dragging.
- **Production.** Varied across albums. *Dear 23* is lush and full. *Frosting on the Beater* has more edge. Later work experiments. But always: clarity, melody, harmony.
- **Dynamics.** Moderate. Power pop dynamics are about tension and release within song structures, not loud/quiet extremes. The Posies build intensity through harmonic and melodic development rather than volume.

(Sources: The Posies discography; production analysis; live recordings; engine internal analysis)
