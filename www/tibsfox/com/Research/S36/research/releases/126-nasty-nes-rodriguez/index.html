First Radio Personality — A New Category in the Engine

Nasty Nes Rodriguez at degree 126° is the first radio personality in the engine — not a musician, but the DJ who brought hip-hop to Seattle radio. “Fresh Tracks” on KCMU (now KEXP) was the sole point of contact between New York hip-hop and Seattle in the early 1980s. Rainier Valley enters the engine. The KCMU–to–KEXP pipeline begins. Without Nasty Nes, the line from early Seattle hip-hop to Sir Mix-a-Lot to Blue Scholars to Macklemore does not exist in the same form.

The Bridge from New York to Seattle

In the early 1980s, Seattle had no hip-hop scene. No radio support, no promoters, no clubs, no infrastructure. Hip-hop was happening in New York (Grandmaster Flash, Afrika Bambaataa, Run-DMC), but it had not reached the Pacific Northwest. Into this void stepped Nasty Nes Rodriguez, who launched “Fresh Tracks” on KCMU — the first hip-hop radio show in Seattle and one of the first on the West Coast.

Fresh Tracks was the sole access point. No hip-hop in record stores (yet). No internet. If you wanted to hear the latest from New York, you tuned in to KCMU and listened to Nasty Nes. He was not just playing records — he was importing a culture. Young listeners who heard Fresh Tracks were inspired to start their own projects. The show created demand that did not previously exist, which created supply: local artists, local promoters, local events. The root node.

KCMU — The Freeform Foundation

KCMU (90.3 FM) was the University of Washington’s freeform college radio station, broadcasting from the basement of Kane Hall. No playlists, no format restrictions, no advertisers. If Nasty Nes wanted to play hip-hop for three hours on a Friday night, he could. Commercial radio would never have aired hip-hop in 1982 Seattle. KCMU’s freeform ethos made it the only station that could. The station became KEXP in 2001 — one of the most influential independent radio stations in the world. The DNA of that evolution includes Fresh Tracks.

Rainier Valley — A New Neighborhood

Degree 126 introduces Rainier Valley to the engine. Southeast Seattle, running along Rainier Avenue South through Columbia City, Hillman City, and Rainier Beach. One of the most ethnically diverse corridors in the United States. While Capitol Hill was the base for punk and indie rock, Rainier Valley and the Central District were the base for Seattle hip-hop. The geography of genre maps to neighborhoods, to demographics, to lived experience.

The Seattle Hip-Hop Foundation

The chain of influence runs directly: Nasty Nes Rodriguez (early 1980s, Fresh Tracks) → Sir Mix-a-Lot (mid-1980s, “Posse on Broadway” 1988, “Baby Got Back” 1992) → the 1990s-2000s explosion (Shabazz Palaces, Blue Scholars, Common Market, Macklemore & Ryan Lewis, Sol, Prometheus Brown). Nasty Nes also managed early Seattle hip-hop acts — controlling both the airwaves (which records got played) and the business (which artists got supported). In a nascent scene with no infrastructure, this dual role was essential. He is the root node of Seattle hip-hop.
