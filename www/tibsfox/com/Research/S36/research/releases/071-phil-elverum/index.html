Anacortes: The Seventh Node

Anacortes, Washington is a small town on Fidalgo Island in the San Juan archipelago, approximately eighty miles north of Seattle. Population roughly 18,000. It is not on the I-5 corridor itself but connected via State Route 20 — psychologically remote, maritime in orientation, facing the water rather than the freeway. The San Juan Islands ferries depart from here. The town is an island between islands.

Anacortes becomes the engine’s seventh geographic node and the first non-city node. Every previous node has been a city on or near the I-5 corridor: Seattle, Portland, Bellingham, Everett, Tacoma, Olympia. Anacortes is neither a city nor on the corridor. It is a town on an island — the engine’s geography shifts from urban corridors to island remoteness. The hermit’s geography.

Despite its size, Anacortes has produced a disproportionate amount of significant independent music. Phil Elverum is the most prominent figure, but the town has supported a community of artists connected to the K Records and International Pop Underground orbits. Elverum’s annual What the Heck Fest brings musicians to the island. A town of 18,000 producing one of the most critically acclaimed artists in American independent music is a geographic anomaly that the engine must register.

Anacortes at degree 71 is the engine’s first encounter with the island town — a place where the natural world is closer than the music industry, where the mountain is visible from the studio window, where the crow outside is more real than the review in Pitchfork. The hermit’s geography. The island as studio. The mountain as name.

The Room Tone: The World Bleeding Into the Music

Phil Elverum’s recordings are defined by their lo-fi directness. The room in which the music is recorded is audible: tape hiss, wind against the walls, the hum of the electrical system, the creak of the floor, the distant sound of rain or birds. These ambient sounds are not noise to be eliminated. They are the sonic context that makes the music real — that places the listener in the same space as the artist, that insists on the physical reality of the recording act.

The room tone is the seventy-second acoustic element: the ambient sound of the recording space, made audible and intentional rather than eliminated through production. In Elverum’s music, the room is not a neutral container. The room is an instrument. The silence between notes is not silent — it is filled with the sound of the space: hiss, hum, breath, the world continuing its business while the musician pauses.

The room tone is the first acoustic element in the engine describing ambient, found sound rather than composed or performed quality. Previous elements — the fiddle line (70), the earnest voice (69), the hush (68) — describe intentional musical gestures. The room tone describes the sound that surrounds those gestures: the world that the music cannot escape, because the music happens in the world.

The Microphones / Mount Eerie: The Mountain Names the Voice

Phil Elverum has recorded under two project names: The Microphones (approximately 1996–2003) and Mount Eerie (2003–present). The transition is not a rebrand. The Microphones’ final album was titled Mount Eerie (2003), and the project name changed to match — the music named the artist rather than the artist naming the music. The mountain named the voice.

Mount Eerie is a real peak on Orcas Island in the San Juan archipelago, visible from Anacortes. To name yourself after a mountain is to acknowledge that the landscape will outlast the artist. The name change enacts the central theme of Elverum’s music: the relationship between the human self and the natural world, the dissolution of the boundary between person and landscape.

The Glow Pt. 2 (2001) is the masterpiece of the Microphones era — a sprawling, genre-defying exploration of lo-fi texture, distortion, silence, and natural sound. As Mount Eerie, Elverum has released a prolific catalog including Wind’s Poem (2009), Sauna (2015), and the devastating A Crow Looked at Me (2017).

A Crow Looked at Me

A Crow Looked at Me (2017) was written and recorded in the months following the death of Elverum’s wife, the artist and musician Geneviève Castrée, from pancreatic cancer in July 2016. She was thirty-five years old. They had a young daughter.

The album is one of the most emotionally direct documents in the history of recorded music. Elverum recorded it in the room where Castrée died, using some of her instruments. The songs do not aestheticize grief or transform loss into art in the conventional sense. They document the daily reality of loss: the tasks of caring for a child alone, the objects left behind, the impossibility of language in the face of death. The album’s title comes from a moment of terrible simplicity: a crow looked at him. The natural world continues. The mountain does not mourn.

The recording is characteristically lo-fi — voice and guitar, room tone audible, no production between the listener and the grief. The rawness is not an aesthetic choice. It is the sound of a person who does not have the energy or the interest to polish, because polishing would be a lie. The room where she died. Her instruments. His voice. The crow outside.

P.W. Elverum & Sun: The Artist as Label

P.W. Elverum & Sun is Phil Elverum’s own record label — the fifth label category in the engine. Elverum writes, records, mixes, masters, presses vinyl, prints packaging, and ships orders from Anacortes. The entire supply chain from creation to consumption runs through one person in one small island town. A one-person music industry.

The artist-owned label is distinct from self-released (no label infrastructure) and from signing to an existing independent label. P.W. Elverum & Sun creates formal infrastructure: a name, a catalog, a philosophy, a physical operation. The engine’s label ecology now includes five categories: commercial tastemaker (Sub Pop), band ecosystem (Barsuk), community retail (Tender Loving Empire), political independent (Kill Rock Stars), and now artist-owned (P.W. Elverum & Sun).

Music Theory Contributions

The Room Tone

Advanced — 72nd Acoustic Element

The ambient sound of the recording space, made audible and intentional rather than eliminated through production. Tape hiss, wind, electrical hum, environmental sound — the room as instrument. First acoustic element describing ambient/found sound rather than composed or performed quality. Distinct from the fiddle line (70, composed) — the room tone is found. The world bleeding into the music.

The Anacortes Hermit

Advanced — 72nd Artist-City Pattern

The artist who operates entirely outside industry infrastructure, from a tiny island town. Complete artistic ecosystem from a single point: writing, recording, manufacturing, distributing. Not the corridor wanderer (multi-nodal), not the working musician (one city), not the visiting artist (institutional). The hermit who needs no city, no label, no scene. The scene that is one person.

Blue Note: The Fog

Intermediate — 71st Deployment

The blue note that appears when the room tone and the music become indistinguishable — the dissolution of the boundary between signal and noise, between music and environment. The thread (70) maintained separateness between two voices; the fog dissolves separateness entirely. The acoustic phenomenon of Pacific Northwest coastal fog made musical.

Indie/Folk at Genre Stage 53

Advanced — Genre Stage 53

Third folk-rooted genre. Three architectures: Fleet Foxes’ cathedral (many voices, layered), Horse Feathers’ chamber (few instruments, woven), Elverum’s hermitage (one voice, room tone, elemental). Back-to-back folk (70–71). Genre Stage 53 follows Indie Folk (52, degree 70). Elemental folk — music stripped to its most basic components.

Key Discography

The Glow Pt. 2

2001 · The Microphones · K / PWE&S

The masterpiece of the Microphones era. A sprawling, genre-defying exploration of lo-fi texture, distortion, silence, and natural sound. Widely regarded as one of the greatest indie/lo-fi albums ever made.

Wind’s Poem

2009 · Mount Eerie · PWE&S

The Pacific Northwest coast as musical material. Wind as compositional element. Black metal textures alongside folk guitar. All sounds — distortion, wind, acoustic guitar, voice — treated as equal elements in a landscape.

A Crow Looked at Me

2017 · Mount Eerie · PWE&S

Written after the death of his wife Geneviève Castrée. Recorded in the room where she died. One of the most emotionally direct and devastating albums in the history of recorded music. Voice, guitar, room tone. The world continuing outside.

Sauna

2015 · Mount Eerie · PWE&S

Nature-as-music extended: heat and cold, interior and exterior, the body in relationship to the elements. Recorded in a sauna that Elverum built in Anacortes. The recording space as concept.

Mount Eerie

2003 · The Microphones · PWE&S

The transitional album. The Microphones’ final recording, named after a peak on Orcas Island. The mountain named the project. The album that ended one name and began another.

Now Only

2018 · Mount Eerie · PWE&S

The aftermath of A Crow Looked at Me. Processing grief while grappling with the paradox of performing grief publicly. The private made public. The strange recursion of art about loss.
