K Records: The Heart of the International Pop Underground

Degree 73 introduces K Records into the engine. This is a landmark event. K Records is arguably the most culturally significant independent label in Pacific Northwest history — more foundational than Sub Pop to the philosophy of independent music, more radical than Kill Rock Stars in its vision of what music could be. Founded in 1982 by Calvin Johnson of Beat Happening in Olympia, Washington, K Records was built as more than a label — it was a philosophy: music belongs to everyone.

K Records organized the International Pop Underground Convention (1991) — a five-day festival in Olympia, one week before Nirvana’s Nevermind dropped. The convention’s opening night, “Love Rock Revolution Girl Style Now,” crystallized riot grrrl as a movement. While Sub Pop built a commercial empire on loud guitars, K Records declared that pop music belonged to everyone. The label’s roster — Beat Happening, The Microphones (Phil Elverum, degree 71), Beck (early work), Built to Spill, Modest Mouse (early singles), Tiger Trap, The Blow, Mirah — is a map of the Pacific Northwest underground.

K Records is the second Olympia label after Kill Rock Stars (degree 70, Horse Feathers). The two labels are the twin pillars of Olympia’s music culture: KRS is the political/punk pillar — riot grrrl, Sleater-Kinney, political independence as aesthetic. K Records is the pop/communal pillar — lo-fi sweetness, democratic music-making, the conviction that the pop song is a democratic form. Same city. Different philosophies. Complementary visions.

K Records at degree 73 is the engine’s encounter with the label that proved anyone can make music — that the pop song belongs to everyone, that lo-fi production is a choice, not a limitation, and that independence is not just a business model but a way of life. The International Pop Underground enters.

Indie Pop: Genre Stage 55

Degree 73 introduces Indie Pop into the engine for the first time — the fifty-fifth genre stage and the first pure Indie Pop classification. Indie Pop is defined by jangly guitars, melodic accessibility, lo-fi production values, and the communal spirit of shared vocals. The genre treats the pop song not as a commercial product but as a democratic form — accessible to anyone who can strum a guitar and sing.

LAKE embodies all four defining qualities: jangly guitars that shimmer and chime, sweet melodies that lodge in the listener’s mind, lo-fi warmth that rejects studio polish in favor of handmade charm, and dual vocals that create a conversational quality — two people singing together, not performing at an audience.

The genre progression from degree 72: the engine moves from ethereal art pop (Marela, 72) to jangly indie pop (LAKE, 73). Both are pop-adjacent. Both depart from the folk/indie folk quadrant. But the construction methods differ. Art pop adds through technology — one voice multiplied by loop pedals into dense textures. Indie pop adds through warmth — friends playing instruments together in a room, the jangle arising from human interaction, not electronic processing.

Dual Vocals and the Adventure Time Connection

LAKE features genuine dual vocals — Eli Moore and Ashley Eriksson sharing vocal duties as co-vocalists, trading lines, harmonizing, answering each other. The effect is conversational: the pop song as dialogue rather than monologue. Neither voice dominates. Both are present, distinct, and essential.

Ashley Eriksson brings an extraordinary connection: she wrote “Island Song” — the closing credits theme for Adventure Time (Cartoon Network, 2010–2018). “Come along with me / And the butterflies and bees...” — a jangly, sweet, lo-fi pop song heard by millions through animation. The K Records aesthetic — warm, simple, heartfelt — reaching a global audience not through the music industry but through a cartoon. The International Pop Underground went mainstream in the most unexpected way.

Music Theory Contributions

The Jangle

Advanced — 74th Acoustic Element

The bright, chiming, treble-rich guitar sound that defines indie pop — open chords, clean tone, rhythmic strumming creating a shimmering quality. The K Records jangle is warm, sweet, democratic. Distinct from the layered loop (72, technology-mediated) and the fiddle line (70, melodic thread) — the jangle is spatial shimmer, not linear melody. Connected to the fraternal jangle (56, Ivan & Alyosha) but broader: the genre’s defining texture.

The K Records Collective

Advanced — 74th Artist-City Pattern

The artist whose relationship to the city is mediated through a label that functions as community, philosophy, and way of life. LAKE’s connection to Olympia is inseparable from K Records. Not the corridor wanderer (moves between cities) or the international student (trains abroad). The K Records collective is rooted and communal — the label as home.

Blue Note: The Sweet Imperfection

Intermediate — 73rd Deployment

The blue note that appears in the slight roughness of lo-fi indie pop — vocal cracks, fret buzz, timing wobbles that make music warm and human. The K Records blue note. Not errors but the source of charm. The echo chamber (72) shimmered between voice and loop. The sweet imperfection warms between intention and execution.

Indie Pop at Genre Stage 55

Advanced — Genre Stage 55

First pure Indie Pop classification in the engine. Jangly guitars, sweet melodies, lo-fi production, dual vocals. The K Records genre — pop as democratic form. Two consecutive pop-adjacent genres (Art Pop 72, Indie Pop 73) mark a genuine departure from the folk/indie folk quadrant.
