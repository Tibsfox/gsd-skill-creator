A shy Army brat discovers Muddy Waters in his father’s record collection. Albert Collins tells him to “keep it up” at a Tacoma graduation party. He moves to Eugene, plays bass in Animal House, forms the Robert Cray Band, and spends a decade on the Pacific Northwest bar circuit. Then Strong Persuader goes triple-platinum, “Smoking Gun” hits No. 1, and the blues is alive again. Five Grammys. Blues Hall of Fame. A Fender signature Stratocaster. Fifty years and counting. The root stayed planted in the Pacific Northwest. The branches reached the world.

Career Timeline

1953 — Born

August 1, Columbus, Georgia. Father stationed at Fort Benning. Military family moves through Virginia, California, Germany.

1968 — Tacoma

Family settles in Tacoma, WA. Cray attends Lakes High School in Lakewood. Discovers Muddy Waters and Howlin’ Wolf in his father’s record collection.

1969 — Richard Cousins

Meets bassist Richard Cousins in Tacoma. They study the blues together, play Tacoma bars, form a musical partnership lasting over 20 years.

1971 — Albert Collins

Attends high school graduation party where Albert Collins performs. Collins tells the young guitarist to “keep it up.” The lineage is seeded.

1974 — Eugene, Oregon

Cray and Cousins move to Eugene. The Robert Cray Band forms with Tom Murphy on drums. Bar-circuit years begin. Curtis Salgado joins as singer/harp player; they merge into the Cray-Hawks.

1977–78 — Animal House

Cray plays bass (uncredited) in Otis Day and the Knights for National Lampoon’s Animal House, filmed at the University of Oregon. Belushi’s exposure to PNW blues helps inspire The Blues Brothers.

1980 — Debut

Who’s Been Talkin’ recorded in 1978, released on Tomato Records. Label goes bankrupt. Album is functionally invisible.

1983–85 — Hightone

Bad Influence (1983) and False Accusations (1985) on Hightone Records. “Phone Booth” later covered by Albert King. Critical breakthrough.

1985 — Showdown!

Records Showdown! with Albert Collins and Johnny Copeland. Wins Grammy for Best Traditional Blues Recording. The graduation-party lineage bears fruit.

1986 — Strong Persuader

Mercury Records debut. 3x Platinum. No. 13 Billboard 200. “Smoking Gun” hits No. 1 Rock Tracks, No. 22 Hot 100. Grammy for Best Contemporary Blues Recording. The best-selling blues album since the 1960s revival.

1988–99 — Mercury Era

Ten Billboard 200 albums. Tours with Eric Clapton. Five total Grammy Awards. Fender designs signature Robert Cray Stratocaster (1990).

2000s–Present

Continues recording and touring. Blues Hall of Fame inductee. That’s What I Heard (2020). “Out Driving Around” tour (2025). Over 50 years as a working musician.

Theory Contributions

The Deep Root

Advanced · Artist-City #36

The artist raised in a PNW city during formative years who stays regional during the transition from amateur to professional (Tacoma → Eugene) before achieving national/global recognition — while the root stays planted in Pacific Northwest soil. Unlike The Export (degree 0), the Deep Root never leaves the region during formation.

Cray: Tacoma upbringing → Eugene bar circuit → national via Strong Persuader. PNW-rooted throughout.
Ancestor: The Export (degree 0, Quincy Jones). The Export leaves entirely; the Deep Root stays regional, then goes global.

The Stinging Clean

Advanced · Acoustic Element #36

The timbral quality of a Fender Stratocaster played clean with pickups lowered flush to the pickguard — bright, articulate, with a sharp leading edge and clear decay. The sound of restraint: dynamic range and touch over gain and wattage. Defined the 1980s blues revival’s tonal palette.

Cray’s “Smoking Gun” opening riff: clean Strat, every note placed precisely. Contrast with Vaughan’s overdriven approach.
Follows: The ensemble blend (34). From collective composite sound to individual voice cutting through with precision.

Blue Note: The Articulate Sting

Intermediate · Blue Note #35

The blue note on a clean-toned Stratocaster where every micro-tonal increment of the bend is audible. No distortion to smear the pitch transition. The departure, the passage through the blue-note zone, the arrival — all exposed under a magnifying glass of clarity.

35th deployment. Follows the unison bend (34, collective). Returns to solo voice at higher precision. Clean tone = nothing hidden.
Opposite: Ghost bend (33, unrecorded). The articulate sting is maximally audible; the ghost bend was never captured.
