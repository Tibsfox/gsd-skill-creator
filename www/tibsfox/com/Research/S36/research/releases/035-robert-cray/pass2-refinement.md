Mariner 4 flew past Mars on July 15, 1965, and transmitted 22 photographs — the first close-up images of another planet's surface. The images showed a cratered, barren, Moon-like landscape with no canals, no vegetation, no signs of the civilization that a century of popular culture had imagined. Mars was dead. The romantic notion of Mars — the canals of Schiaparelli, the civilization of H.G. Wells, the princess of Burroughs — was destroyed by 22 photographs. The truth was more interesting and more bleak: a world of craters, dust, and thin atmosphere. Reality replaced fantasy.

Robert Cray is the Blues — the first artist in the engine with "Blues" as the primary genre label. Thirty-five degrees of jazz-to-soul-to-R&B, and now the blues arrives. Cray is from Tacoma — the first Tacoma artist, extending the engine's geography beyond Seattle proper for the first time in the PNW metro area. Tacoma is 34 miles south of Seattle, a separate city with its own musical culture, connected by I-5 but distinct in character. The engine's geographic range expands.

The blues is the genre that everything else in the engine has been built on — jazz drew from it, R&B formalized it, soul sanctified it, funk electrified it. Cray at degree 35 is the root that arrives after the branches, the same way Lanphere's bebop root (degree 12) arrived after hard bop (degree 9) and cool jazz (degree 10). The foundational genre appears after its descendants. The archaeology digs down to bedrock.

Mariner 4 destroyed Mars's romantic image and replaced it with scientific reality. Cray's blues destroyed the sentimental notion that the blues is a museum genre — his playing is contemporary, sharp, emotionally precise, technically virtuosic. The blues is not nostalgia. It is alive. The crater-pocked Mars is not romantic, but it is real.
