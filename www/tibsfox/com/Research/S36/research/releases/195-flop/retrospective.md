## The One Hundred Ninety-Fifth Degree: The Post-Nirvana Frenzy

### Indie Rock, E=5, Seattle, Frontier to Epic Records. The post-Nirvana major-label frenzy personified. Power-pop band signed to Frontier (indie), then Epic (Sony), then dropped. 1990-1996. Rusty Willoughby. The cautionary tale. v1.49.336.

---

## What Worked

1. **E=5 matches the power-pop energy.** Buzzing guitars, vocal hooks, driving rhythms. Not overwhelming, not quiet. The power-pop sweet spot, consistent with Fastbacks (193).
2. **Post-Nirvana frenzy narrative is the entry's strength.** The story is bigger than the band -- it's the story of what the industry did to dozens of Seattle bands. Flop is the representative case.
3. **Frontier-to-Epic arc is a clean narrative.** Indie label (natural fit) to major label (scale mismatch) to dropped. The three-act structure writes itself.
4. **The economic trap is specific and grounded.** $100K advance, $2 royalty, 50,000 albums to recoup. The math is impossible. This isn't hand-waving -- it's industry economics.
5. **Connection to Fastbacks and Young Fresh Fellows is natural.** Same power-pop zone. Different label strategies. The indie path preserved those bands; the major path destroyed Flop.

*Degree 195 retrospective complete. FLOP at the one hundred ninety-fifth degree -- THE POST-NIRVANA FRENZY. Formed 1990. Frontier Records: power-pop with punk energy, rough edges, authentic. The Nirvana earthquake hit in 1991-92 and the gold rush began. Epic Records (Sony) signed Flop. The polished album missed what made them appealing. Modest sales. Dropped. Dissolved 1996. Six years. The story repeated across dozens of Seattle bands -- signed for geography, not sound. Advances as loans, impossible recoupment math, creative pressure to smooth the edges for radio. The bands that survived (Mudhoney on Sub Pop, Fastbacks on PopLlama, Young Fresh Fellows on PopLlama) stayed indie. The bands that took the major-label deal mostly disappeared. Flop is the representative case. Rusty Willoughby continued solo. The music was good. The industry was indifferent.
