Flop

E = 5 Indie Rock Frontier → Epic

The Post-Nirvana Frenzy

After Nirvana went diamond, major labels descended on Seattle. Flop was one of dozens caught in the gold rush: Frontier Records indie power-pop, then an Epic Records (Sony) signing, then a polished album that didn’t sell in Nirvana numbers, then dropped. Six years. 1990–1996. The story of what the frenzy did to the bands it was supposed to help.

Theory Contributions

The Major-Label Trap

Milestone — Industry Theory

Advances are loans. $100K advance against $2 royalty = 50,000 albums before earning a dollar. For a band selling 5,000 per release, the math was impossible. The trap consumed dozens of Seattle bands.

Frontier to Epic — Indie to Major

Advanced — Label Theory

Frontier Records: respected indie, natural fit. Epic Records: global major, scale mismatch. The polished Epic album missed what made Flop appealing: the rough edges.

Power-Pop in the Grunge Shadow

Intermediate — Genre Theory

Flop played power-pop, not grunge. Closer to Fastbacks (193) and Young Fresh Fellows (192). But the major-label frenzy didn’t distinguish — it signed everything from Seattle.
