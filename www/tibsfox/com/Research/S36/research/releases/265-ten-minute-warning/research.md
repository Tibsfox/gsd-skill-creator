## THE ORIGIN STORY: DUFF McKAGAN IN SEATTLE

### Before Los Angeles, Before Guns N' Roses

Michael Andrew McKagan -- known throughout his entire career as Duff McKagan -- was born on February 5, 1964, in Seattle, Washington. He grew up in Seattle's Capitol Hill neighborhood, which in the early 1980s was becoming the epicenter of the city's nascent punk and post-punk scene.

- **The Capitol Hill years.** McKagan was a teenage musician in Seattle before most of the world had heard of Seattle as a music city. This was not the grunge era -- grunge did not yet exist. This was punk, post-punk, and proto-punk emerging from the same mix of DIY energy and Pacific Northwest restlessness that would later produce Mudhoney, Nirvana, and Soundgarden. McKagan was there first.
- **Multiple bands before departure.** Before Ten Minute Warning and before leaving for Los Angeles, McKagan played in a series of Seattle bands including the Fastbacks (briefly, as drummer), the Fartz, the Living, and others. Seattle's early punk scene was small enough that the same twenty musicians cycled through multiple configurations. McKagan was active, hungry, and versatile.
- **The drummer who became a bassist.** McKagan is primarily known as a bassist -- Guns N' Roses' bassist, specifically. But he came to bass from drums. His percussion background gave his bass playing an unusually rhythmic, driving quality: McKagan plays bass like a drummer -- locked in, powerful, and foundational rather than melodic. This approach anchors GN'R's rhythm section in a way that purely melodic bassists cannot.
- **Ten Minute Warning as culmination.** By the time Ten Minute Warning formed in 1982, McKagan was already experienced. Ten Minute Warning was not his first project but arguably his best work before GN'R -- a band with real identity and musical vision beyond the raw energy of earlier punk.

## TEN MINUTE WARNING -- THE BAND

### Formation, Personnel, and Identity

Ten Minute Warning formed in Seattle in 1982 and operated until 1986:

- **Duff McKagan (drums, later bass).** In Ten Minute Warning, McKagan played drums -- his original instrument. His drum work in the band reflects his later bass style: driving, powerful, rhythmically authoritative. The kit was never just timekeeping; it was the engine.
- **Steve Pearson (guitar/vocals).** Pearson is the other primary creative force in Ten Minute Warning. His guitar work anchors the band in the proto-punk/rock tradition -- less pure hardcore than the Fartz, more structured and melodic, with an approach that acknowledges rock history while operating at punk velocity. Pearson's guitar playing is what keeps Ten Minute Warning from being simply fast: there is craft in the riff construction.
- **Paul Solger (guitar).** Additional guitar presence. Ten Minute Warning's two-guitar setup gives the band density and allows for textural variation -- the rhythm guitar locking in with McKagan's drums while the lead work adds definition.
- **The lineup evolution.** Like most bands from this era and milieu, Ten Minute Warning's lineup shifted across their active years. The core identity remained McKagan-Pearson, but the supporting cast changed.
- **The name.** "Ten Minute Warning" as a band name carries apocalyptic connotations appropriate to the early 1980s punk era: the Cold War was at its most intense, nuclear anxiety was pervasive, and punk's nihilism was culturally resonant. A ten-minute warning was the time you'd have before a nuclear strike. The name is historically situated.

## THE DEPARTURE: McKAGAN TO LOS ANGELES

### How Seattle Punk Became Global Rock

The most historically significant event connected to Ten Minute Warning is McKagan's departure for Los Angeles in 1983-1984 to pursue music on a larger scale:

- **Why Los Angeles?** In the early 1980s, Los Angeles was the center of the American rock music industry in a way that Seattle was not and could not yet be. The Sunset Strip scene -- Motley Crue, Ratt, Dokken, and dozens of others -- was commercially dominant. A young musician from Seattle who wanted to break through nationally had limited options: New York or Los Angeles. McKagan chose Los Angeles.
- **The Seattle connection persists.** When McKagan arrived in Los Angeles, he connected with Slash, Axl Rose, Izzy Stradlin, and Steven Adler -- the musicians who would become Guns N' Roses. GN'R formed in 1985 and released Appetite for Destruction in 1987. McKagan joined as bassist, converting from his drumming role in Seattle.
- **Appetite for Destruction.** Guns N' Roses' debut album is one of the best-selling rock albums in history -- approximately 30 million copies sold worldwide. "Welcome to the Jungle," "Sweet Child O' Mine," "Paradise City," "November Rain" (from Use Your Illusion I, 1991) -- these became defining songs of the late 1980s and early 1990s rock era. McKagan's bass is on all of them: driving, powerful, rhythmically exact in exactly the way his Capitol Hill punk drumming trained him to be.
- **What Seattle gave Guns N' Roses.** McKagan brought something specific from Seattle to Guns N' Roses: a punk discipline and directness that cut against the Sunset Strip's tendencies toward excess. Where other LA hard rock bands sometimes disappeared into theatrical posturing, McKagan's grounding in Capitol Hill proto-punk kept GN'R's rhythm section honest. You can trace a line from the Fartz and Ten Minute Warning to the bass line of "Welcome to the Jungle."
- **The return.** McKagan eventually returned to Seattle, got sober (his story is told in the memoir), and continued making music. He has maintained his Seattle identity throughout his career even while achieving global fame as a member of Guns N' Roses. The Capitol Hill kid who played drums for Ten Minute Warning became one of the most recognizable rock bassists in the world.

## THE SEATTLE PROTO-PUNK ECOSYSTEM

### Context: What Existed Before Grunge

Ten Minute Warning operated in a Seattle music scene that is often obscured by the dominant grunge narrative:

- **The Fartz.** The punk band that included McKagan and represented pure hardcore in the Seattle scene. The Fartz were aggressive, fast, and deliberately provocative -- their name was chosen for maximum offense. Ten Minute Warning was a more mature evolution from this starting point.
- **The Fastbacks.** One of the enduring bands of Seattle's pre-grunge era, formed in 1979. Power-pop punk with melodic precision. Kim Warnick and Kurt Bloch built something that lasted decades. McKagan briefly passed through the Fastbacks.
- **U-Men.** Post-punk, art-adjacent, deeply weird. The U-Men occupied a different corner of the Seattle proto-punk ecosystem -- more atmospheric, more influenced by Nick Cave and Birthday Party than by Black Flag or Dead Kennedys.
- **The Living.** Another McKagan project from this era, representing the range of configurations the Seattle proto-punk scene generated.
- **The continuum to grunge.** The early 1980s proto-punk scene in Seattle is the direct ancestor of grunge. The same values -- DIY, independent, Seattle-rooted, loud -- transferred from the Fartz and Ten Minute Warning to Green River (which included future members of Mudhoney and Pearl Jam), Mother Love Bone, and eventually Nirvana and Soundgarden. Ten Minute Warning is part of this genealogy.

(Sources: Seattle punk history 1979-1987; The Fartz; The Fastbacks; U-Men; proto-punk to grunge continuum; Green River; Mother Love Bone)

## RECORDINGS AND LEGACY

### What Survived and What It Means

Ten Minute Warning's recorded legacy is modest in quantity but significant in historical weight:

- **The recorded output.** Ten Minute Warning released limited recorded material during their active years, as was typical for independent Seattle punk bands of the era. Physical distribution was limited. The band's reputation rested primarily on live performance rather than studio documentation.
- **The posthumous documentation.** After McKagan achieved fame with GN'R, interest in his Seattle roots increased, and Ten Minute Warning's recordings received more attention than they might otherwise have. Historical documentation of the Seattle proto-punk scene improved as researchers and fans recognized its importance to what came later.
- **Ten Minute Warning (self-titled).** The band's primary recorded release, a 7-inch and later collected recordings, documented their Capitol Hill proto-punk approach. The recordings are raw, consistent with the era and budget, and serve as primary historical documents of the Seattle scene's earliest coherent punk formation.
- **Legacy assessment.** Ten Minute Warning's legacy is primarily biographical -- they are significant as Duff McKagan's band before GN'R, and secondarily as part of the documented prehistory of Seattle punk. But the music is not merely historical: the E=8 energy is genuine, the songs are real, and Steve Pearson's guitar work stands on its own merits independently of the McKagan connection.
