Biography & The Chicago-to-Seattle Pipeline

Julian Priester was born on June 29, 1935, in Chicago, Illinois — the first S36 artist born outside the Pacific Northwest. He attended DuSable High School on Chicago’s South Side, where Captain Walter Dyett’s music program produced Nat King Cole, Dinah Washington, Gene Ammons, Johnny Griffin, and dozens of other jazz professionals. DuSable was to Chicago jazz what Garfield was to Seattle jazz — the institutional pipeline that converted neighborhood talent into professional musicians.

At eighteen, Priester joined Sun Ra’s Arkestra (1953–56) — the most radical laboratory in American jazz. He then played with Max Roach (late 1950s–60s), Duke Ellington’s orchestra, and became a founding member of Herbie Hancock’s Mwandishi sextet (1970–73), the pioneering electronic jazz group whose three albums — Mwandishi, Crossings, Sextant — are canonical documents of jazz electronics.

Priester moved to Seattle around 1979 and joined the faculty at Cornish College of the Arts, where he became a pillar of the city’s experimental jazz scene alongside Wayne Horvitz (2°) and Bill Frisell (1°). He recorded for ECM Records, connecting him to Frisell’s ECM catalog and to the label’s aesthetic of space, texture, and contemplation.

The Sun Ra / Hancock Bridge

Priester bridges two of the most important experimental traditions in jazz history. Sun Ra’s Arkestra was cosmic Afrofuturism — music as vehicle for ideas larger than entertainment, referencing ancient Egypt, Saturn, intergalactic travel, and the transcendence of terrestrial oppression. Hancock’s Mwandishi was electronic expansion — extending jazz’s timbral palette through Fender Rhodes, wah-wah, Echoplex, and ARP synthesizer without abandoning rhythmic and harmonic sophistication.

The Bridge: Cosmic Philosophy → Electronic Method

Sun Ra’s Arkestra Permission. Music can be anything, go anywhere, is not obligated to satisfy conventional expectations. Cosmic mythology, collective improvisation, total artistic commitment.
Mwandishi Sextet Method. Here is how to extend the territory systematically — electronic processing, new instruments, electroacoustic texture. Not chaos; precisely constructed sonic architecture.
Julian Priester The Bridge. Participated in both at the highest level. Carried the Arkestra’s ambition into Hancock’s technology. One of very few musicians in jazz history to span both traditions.

Both traditions were about expanding the territory of jazz — Sun Ra into space mythology and Afrofuturist consciousness, Hancock into electronic timbre and electroacoustic texture. Priester is the connective tissue between philosophy (“music can be anything”) and technology (“here is how”).

The Trombone: Continuous Pitch in a Discrete World

The trombone is the only standard orchestral brass instrument that produces pitch through a continuous slide mechanism rather than through valves or keys. A trumpet has three valves. A French horn has four. Each valve adds a fixed length of tubing, producing discrete pitch positions. The trombone’s telescoping slide can stop anywhere — producing any frequency within its range, including all microtonal intervals between Western twelve-tone pitches.

This is the same distinction that separates a fretted guitar (discrete) from a fretless bass (continuous), or a piano (discrete) from a human voice (continuous). The trombone is the brass instrument most like the human voice: both produce pitch through continuous, analog mechanisms unconstrained to a fixed scale.

The trombone’s continuous slide gives it direct physical access to the blue note zone — the microtonal region between the major and minor third that defines blues and jazz expression. Every other brass instrument must approximate this through lip-bending (embouchure). The trombonist simply moves the slide.

Three musicians from three experimental traditions converged in Seattle to create a nationally significant experimental jazz scene:

Priester arrived first — nearly a decade before Frisell and Horvitz. He was the advance party. When Frisell and Horvitz arrived in the late 1980s, Priester was already established at Cornish, already part of the local scene, already teaching the next generation. The constellation was built in stages.

Career Arc

1935 — Born in Chicago

Born June 29, 1935, on Chicago’s South Side. Attended DuSable High School under Captain Walter Dyett — the pipeline that produced Nat King Cole, Dinah Washington, Gene Ammons, and a generation of jazz professionals.

1953–1956 — Sun Ra’s Arkestra

Joined at age eighteen. The most radical laboratory in American jazz. Cosmic Afrofuturism, collective improvisation, total artistic commitment. The philosophical foundation for everything that followed.

Late 1950s–1960s — Max Roach & Duke Ellington

Hard bop discipline with Roach (political jazz, We Insist! Freedom Now Suite). Orchestral sophistication with Ellington (composition, arrangement, ensemble precision). From cosmic chaos to concert-hall order — and back again.

1970–1973 — Herbie Hancock’s Mwandishi

The first wave of electronic jazz. Fender Rhodes, wah-wah, Echoplex, ARP synthesizer. Three canonical albums: Mwandishi, Crossings, Sextant. Priester adopted the Swahili name Pepo Mtoto. The trombone became an electroacoustic source — neither purely acoustic nor purely electronic.

~1979 — Move to Seattle

Joined Cornish College of the Arts faculty. Not the Central District, not the Jackson Street corridor (largely gone by then). Seattle at large — the first S36 artist to settle in the city without a CD connection. The Pedagogue-Draw pattern.

1974 & 1980s–present — ECM Records & Cornish

Recorded Love, Love (ECM, 1974) and subsequent ECM projects. Taught at Cornish for decades, influencing generations of Seattle musicians. Part of the Priester-Horvitz-Frisell experimental constellation.

Music Theory Mapping

Five theory nodes — extending the brass branch (from one to three nodes) and adding electronic jazz texture and pedagogical philosophy.

Trombone Slide: Continuous Pitch

Advanced

The only orchestral brass instrument with continuous pitch — the slide can stop at any point, producing any frequency, including all microtonal intervals. No valves, no keys, no frets. The most voice-like brass instrument. Discrete (trumpet/Standifer) vs. continuous (trombone/Priester) = the brass branch’s fundamental spectrum.

Priester’s Mwandishi recordings; J.J. Johnson; New Orleans tailgate trombone (Kid Ory)
Thread: Standifer (trumpet, discrete, 4°) → Priester (trombone, continuous, 7°). The brass branch now spans the discrete-continuous spectrum.

Microtonal Blue Note Continuum

Advanced

The blue note is a zone, not a pitch. The trombone’s slide provides direct physical access to that microtonal zone — no lip-bending approximation required. Seventh deployment of the blue note thread: bass-line (0°), guitar bend (1°), vocal melisma (3°), trumpet vibrato (4°), pianistic crush (5°), altered voicings (6°), trombone slide continuum (7°).

Priester’s extended trombone techniques; J.J. Johnson hard bop blue notes; New Orleans tailgate cries
Seven deployments: The trombone slide is the most literal embodiment because the mechanism itself is continuous. Every other instrument approximates continuous pitch through technique.

Modal Jazz: Dorian / Mixolydian / Lydian

Intermediate

Modal jazz replaces bebop’s rapid chord changes with static harmonic fields. Dorian (minor with raised 6th), Mixolydian (major with flatted 7th — blues foundation), Lydian (major with raised 4th — floating, ethereal). The trombone excels in modal contexts: static fields reward sustained tones, long phrases, and gradual microtonal variation — exactly what the slide produces naturally.

Miles Davis Kind of Blue; Hancock Mwandishi; George Russell Ezz-thetics
Harmonic shift: From ii-V-I chord-change density (Jones 0°, Bown 6°) to static modal fields (Priester 7°). The trombone’s sustained-tone capability is a natural fit.

Electronic Jazz Texture: First Wave

Advanced

Mwandishi (1970–73): Fender Rhodes + wah-wah + Echoplex tape delay + ARP synthesizer. Priester’s trombone was both acoustic source and electronic processing candidate. The trombone’s natural portamento matched the synthesizer’s pitch-bending, the wah-wah’s frequency sweep, and the Echoplex’s time-stretching. First “electroacoustic” jazz in S36.

Mwandishi (1971); Crossings (1972); Sextant (1973)
Three waves: Sun Ra proto-electronic (1950s) → Mwandishi first wave (1970s) → Frisell/Horvitz second wave (1980s+). Priester bridges all three.

Pedagogy: Expansion vs. Preservation

Intermediate

Three educator profiles: Standifer (4°, UW) — preservation-oriented, teaching the canon. Bown (6°, informal) — survival-based, underdocumented. Priester (7°, Cornish) — expansion-oriented, teaching boundary-crossing and experimentalism. Two brass educators, two institutions, two philosophies: “learn this” vs. “go beyond this.”

Cornish experimental curriculum; UW jazz repertoire program; DuSable pipeline
Pedagogical axis: Standifer (preservation, 4°) ↔ Priester (expansion, 7°). A tradition needs both.
