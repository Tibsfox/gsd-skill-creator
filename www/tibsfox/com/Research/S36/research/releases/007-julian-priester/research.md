## Biography & The Chicago-to-Seattle Pipeline

Julian Anthony Priester was born on June 29, 1935, in Chicago, Illinois — the first S36 artist born outside the Pacific Northwest or transplanted via childhood. He is the eighth degree, the seventh jazz artist, and the first whose Seattle connection is not rooted in the Central District. Priester grew up on Chicago's South Side and attended DuSable High School, a public school whose music program under Captain Walter Dyett produced an extraordinary density of jazz talent: Nat King Cole, Dinah Washington, Gene Ammons, Johnny Griffin, Von Freeman, Clifford Jordan, Richard Davis, and dozens of others. DuSable was to Chicago jazz what Garfield High School was to Seattle jazz — the institutional pipeline that converted neighborhood talent into professional musicians. The parallel is structural, not coincidental: segregation concentrated Black musical talent into a small number of public schools, and the quality of individual music teachers determined whether that concentration produced a pipeline or merely a crowd. Captain Dyett produced a pipeline.

Priester's instrument is the trombone. He is the first trombonist in the S36 engine and the second brass player after Floyd Standifer (degree 4, trumpet). The trombone choice matters enormously for the engine's music theory branch, for reasons that will be developed below: the trombone is the only orchestral brass instrument with a continuous pitch slide, making it the most voice-like and the most microtonal of the brass family. Where Standifer's trumpet offered valved precision, Priester's trombone offers analog continuity — the same distinction that separates a fretted guitar from a fretless bass, or a piano from a human voice.

(Source: AllMusic artist biography; ECM Records catalog; DownBeat artist profiles; DuSable High School music program history; Chicago jazz oral history archives; Cornish College of the Arts faculty records)

### Sun Ra's Arkestra (1953-1956)

Priester's professional career began in the most radical laboratory in American jazz. At eighteen years old, he joined Sun Ra's Arkestra in Chicago, playing with the ensemble from approximately 1953 to 1956. Sun Ra — born Herman Poole Blount in Birmingham, Alabama — had arrived in Chicago and assembled an ensemble that would become the longest-running ensemble in jazz history and one of its most philosophically radical. Sun Ra's Arkestra was not merely a band; it was a cosmology. The music was embedded in a comprehensive mythology involving space travel, ancient Egypt, Afrofuturism, and a systematic rejection of the social and musical conventions that constrained Black artistic expression.

For a teenager from the South Side to enter the Arkestra was to receive an education that no conservatory could provide. Sun Ra demanded total commitment — not just to the music but to the ideas. The Arkestra rehearsed obsessively, performed in costume, lived communally (in later periods), and treated music as a vehicle for cosmic liberation rather than entertainment. Priester absorbed this worldview at the most formative period of his development. Whatever he played afterward — hard bop with Max Roach, orchestral jazz with Duke Ellington, electronic jazz with Herbie Hancock — carried the Arkestra's fundamental premise: that jazz could be about anything, could go anywhere, and was not obligated to satisfy conventional expectations.

The Sun Ra connection places Priester in a lineage that no other S36 artist can claim. Jones (degree 0) came from the Lionel Hampton big band tradition — professional, commercial, disciplined. Anderson (degree 3) came from the Hampton band as well and entered the vocalist lineage. Standifer (degree 4) came from the Jackson Street corridor — local, self-made, community-oriented. Priester came from the Arkestra — cosmic, experimental, ideologically total. The S36 engine now has four band-pipeline origins: Hampton (commercial big band), Jackson Street (community corridor), DuSable/Arkestra (radical ensemble), and the Berklee/UC Santa Cruz academic pipeline (Frisell, degree 1).

(Source: John Szwed, *Space Is the Place: The Lives and Times of Sun Ra*, 1997; Sun Ra Arkestra discography and personnel records; AllMusic; DownBeat; Chicago Reader jazz archives)

### Max Roach and the Hard Bop Era (Late 1950s-1960s)

After leaving the Arkestra, Priester moved into the mainstream of jazz's most musically demanding period. He joined drummer Max Roach's ensemble in the late 1950s — a group that was simultaneously one of the most technically rigorous and most politically engaged in jazz. Roach, along with his then-wife Abbey Lincoln, had turned hard bop toward explicit political content with recordings like *We Insist! Freedom Now Suite* (1960). Priester's trombone was part of this project — not merely providing melody and harmony but participating in music that was intended as political statement.

Priester also worked with Duke Ellington, the grand orchestrator of American jazz, whose ensemble had been operating since the 1920s and represented the absolute opposite end of the jazz spectrum from Sun Ra. Where the Arkestra was cosmic and anarchic, Ellington's orchestra was sophisticated and precisely arranged. Priester's ability to function in both contexts — Arkestra and Ellington, chaos and order, cosmos and concert hall — demonstrates a range that few trombonists of any era have matched. This duality would become the defining feature of his artistic identity: the ability to inhabit experimental and traditional forms simultaneously, bringing the intensity of one to the discipline of the other.

The Max Roach period also connects Priester to Patti Bown (degree 6) through the hard bop network. Both were operating in the same New York jazz ecosystem during the late 1950s and early 1960s. Both were connected to arrangers and bandleaders at the top of the profession. But where Bown was erased by the industry's memory system, Priester survived — partly because his subsequent career with Hancock and his ECM recordings provided continuous renewal of visibility. The variable is not talent but trajectory: Bown's career peaked and declined, while Priester's career transformed and continued.

(Source: Max Roach discography; Duke Ellington Orchestra personnel records; DownBeat; AllMusic; Nat Hentoff, liner notes for Max Roach recordings; Ellington archive at the Smithsonian)

### Herbie Hancock's Mwandishi Sextet (1970-1973)

The Mwandishi period is the hinge of Priester's career and one of the most important chapters in the history of jazz electronics. Herbie Hancock formed the Mwandishi sextet around 1969-1970, assembling a group that included Priester (trombone), Bennie Maupin (bass clarinet, saxophones, flute), Eddie Henderson (trumpet), Buster Williams (bass), and Billy Hart (drums). The ensemble adopted Swahili names — Hancock became Mwandishi, Priester became Pepo Mtoto — as part of a broader engagement with African identity and Pan-African consciousness that was central to the Black Arts Movement of the era.

Mwandishi was the first wave of jazz electronics. Hancock played Fender Rhodes electric piano processed through a wah-wah pedal and an Echoplex tape delay. The other instruments were often processed through similar effects. The result was a sound that was unprecedented in jazz — spacious, textured, electronic, and deeply influenced by the cosmic ethos that Priester had absorbed in the Arkestra. The connection is direct: Sun Ra's Arkestra had been using electronic keyboards and unconventional timbres since the 1950s. Priester carried that sonic philosophy into Hancock's ensemble, contributing to a sound that would influence Miles Davis's electric period, the entire ECM aesthetic, and eventually the ambient and electronic music movements of the 1980s and 1990s.

The Mwandishi recordings — *Mwandishi* (1971), *Crossings* (1972), and *Sextant* (1973) — are canonical documents of electronic jazz. Priester's trombone in this context was not playing conventional hard bop lines. It was a textural element — a source of long tones, glissandi, and overtone-rich sounds that blended with the electronic processing to create a composite timbre that no single instrument could produce alone. The trombone's natural capacity for continuous pitch change (the slide) made it the ideal acoustic instrument for this electronic context: it could match the portamento effects of the synthesizer, the pitch-bending of the wah-wah pedal, and the time-stretching of the Echoplex.

The Mwandishi sextet was short-lived — Hancock disbanded it around 1973 for economic reasons (the music was not commercially viable) and pivoted toward the funk-jazz of *Head Hunters*. But the three years of Mwandishi established a sonic vocabulary that would reverberate through jazz for the next five decades. Priester was there for all of it.

(Source: Herbie Hancock with Lisa Dickey, *Possibilities*, 2014; Steven Cerra, jazz profiles; AllMusic; ECM Records; DownBeat; Bob Gluck, *You'll Know When You Get There: Herbie Hancock and the Mwandishi Band*, 2012)

### Seattle and Cornish College of the Arts (1979-present)

Priester moved to Seattle in approximately 1979. He did not arrive as a young musician seeking opportunity (like Jones or Anderson) or as an established New York musician seeking a different scene (like Frisell or Horvitz). He arrived as a mature artist with three decades of experience spanning the full range of jazz expression — from Sun Ra's cosmology to Ellington's orchestration to Hancock's electronics — and he came to teach.

Priester joined the faculty at Cornish College of the Arts, a private arts college in Seattle founded in 1914 and named for Nellie Cornish. Cornish has a distinguished history in progressive arts education: John Cage, Merce Cunningham, and Mark Tobey all taught or studied there. The college's jazz program, while smaller than university programs, emphasized experimental and interdisciplinary approaches to music — a natural fit for an artist who had spent his career crossing boundaries.

At Cornish, Priester became a pillar of Seattle's experimental jazz scene, connecting with Wayne Horvitz (degree 2) and Bill Frisell (degree 1), both of whom arrived in Seattle in the late 1980s. The three formed a loose constellation of musicians whose shared commitment to experimentalism — different in style but aligned in philosophy — helped define Seattle as a city where jazz could mean something other than swing standards or bebop repertoire. Priester brought the Arkestra's cosmic ambition and Mwandishi's electronic vocabulary; Horvitz brought the New York downtown avant-garde; Frisell brought Berklee training filtered through Americana and noise. The three did not play identical music, but they created overlapping zones of experimentation that made Seattle's jazz scene distinct from any other American city.

The Cornish teaching connection places Priester alongside Standifer (degree 4, UW) in the pedagogical lineage — two brass players who became educators at Seattle institutions, transmitting jazz knowledge to subsequent generations. But the pedagogical philosophies differ: Standifer taught within the university system, emphasizing repertoire, theory, and the preservation of the tradition. Priester taught within an arts college, emphasizing experimentation, boundary-crossing, and the expansion of the tradition. Two brass educators, two institutions, two pedagogical orientations — preservation versus expansion.

(Source: Cornish College of the Arts faculty records and program descriptions; Earshot Jazz; Seattle Times; AllMusic; Paul de Barros, *Jackson Street After Hours*, 1993; KNKX Seattle jazz coverage; HistoryLink.org)

## The Trombone as Unique Voice

### The Slide: Continuous Pitch in a Discrete World

The trombone is the only standard orchestral brass instrument that produces pitch through a continuous slide mechanism rather than through valves or keys. A trumpet has three valves. A French horn has four. A tuba has three to six. Each valve, when depressed, adds a fixed length of tubing to the instrument, lowering the pitch by a specific interval. The result is a system of discrete pitch positions — you press a combination of valves, and you get a specific note. The intervals between notes are fixed by the plumbing.

The trombone has no valves (in its standard form). It has a slide — a telescoping tube that the player extends or retracts continuously. There are seven standard slide positions, each corresponding to a harmonic series, but the slide can stop anywhere between those positions. The pitch is not locked to discrete steps. It is continuous. The trombone can produce any frequency within its range, including all the microtonal intervals between the notes of the Western twelve-tone scale.

This is the same distinction that separates a fretted guitar (discrete — the frets lock the string to specific pitches) from a fretless bass or a violin (continuous — the finger can stop the string at any point). It is the distinction between a piano (discrete — each key strikes a fixed string) and a human voice (continuous — the vocal folds can vibrate at any frequency). The trombone is the brass instrument that is most like the human voice: both produce pitch through continuous, analog mechanisms that are not constrained to a fixed scale.

For the S36 engine, this is a significant theoretical addition. The engine's brass branch began with Standifer's trumpet (degree 4) — a valved instrument producing discrete pitches within the bebop/swing vocabulary. Priester's trombone introduces the continuous-pitch dimension: glissando, portamento, microtonal inflection, and the ability to slide between notes rather than jumping from one to another. The brass branch now has two instruments that represent the discrete-continuous spectrum: trumpet (discrete, valved) and trombone (continuous, slide).

### Microtonal Expression and the Blue Note Continuum

The trombone's continuous pitch capability connects directly to the blue note tradition that the S36 engine has been tracking since degree 0. The blue note — the flatted third, fifth, or seventh that defines the emotional character of blues and jazz — is not a single frequency. It is a zone. When a vocalist bends the third, they do not land on one pitch; they hover in a region between the major and minor third, producing a quality that cannot be notated in standard Western staff notation. The blue note is microtonal by nature.

The trombone is the only brass instrument that can produce this microtonal blue note zone without using lip-bending alone. The trumpeter must bend the pitch with embouchure (lip tension and positioning) — a technique that is limited in range and difficult to sustain. The trombonist simply moves the slide. The slide gives the trombone direct, physical access to the microtonal continuum that the blue note inhabits. This is why the trombone has been central to blues, New Orleans jazz, and every tradition that privileges the bend, the smear, the cry — the sounds that exist between the notes of the tempered scale.

Priester's microtonal vocabulary, developed through the Arkestra (which explored non-Western tunings and extended techniques) and Mwandishi (which used electronic processing to further expand the pitch continuum), represents the most sophisticated microtonal deployment in the S36 engine. The blue note thread now has seven deployments:

1. **Bass-line inflection** (Jones, 0) — ii-V-I context
2. **Upper-register ambiguity** (Frisell, 1) — bent strings, volume pedal
3. **Vocal melisma** (Anderson, 3) — voice as microtonal instrument
4. **Trumpet vibrato zone** (Standifer, 4) — embouchure-controlled
5. **Pianistic crush** (Berry, 5) — grace notes approximating the bend
6. **Hard-bop altered voicings** (Bown, 6) — chromatic extensions
7. **Trombone slide continuum** (Priester, 7) — direct physical access to continuous pitch

The trombone slide is the most literal embodiment of the blue note continuum because the mechanism itself is continuous. Every other instrument must approximate continuous pitch through technique (lip bending, string bending, vocal control). The trombone achieves it through architecture.

## Music Theory Mapping

### Modal Jazz: Dorian, Mixolydian, Lydian

Priester's career spans the full arc of modal jazz — from its emergence in the late 1950s through its electronic expansion in the 1970s. Modal jazz replaced the rapid harmonic movement of bebop (where chords change every one or two beats) with a static harmonic field (where a single mode governs an entire section or even an entire piece). Miles Davis's *Kind of Blue* (1959) codified the approach, but its roots extend back to earlier experimentalists including Sun Ra, who had been exploring static harmonic fields and drone-based composition before the term "modal jazz" existed.

The principal modes in jazz context are:

- **Dorian** (the minor mode with a raised sixth): the most common modal jazz scale, used as the basis for Davis's "So What" and hundreds of subsequent compositions. It produces a minor tonality with a distinctive bright quality from the raised sixth degree.
- **Mixolydian** (the major mode with a flatted seventh): produces a dominant-seventh sound that is the harmonic foundation of blues. Every blues-based jazz improvisation implicitly uses the Mixolydian mode.
- **Lydian** (the major mode with a raised fourth): produces a floating, ethereal quality because the raised fourth creates a tritone against the root, generating harmonic tension without resolution. George Russell's *Lydian Chromatic Concept of Tonal Organization* (1953) proposed the Lydian mode as the fundamental scale of tonal gravity — an idea that influenced the entire modal jazz movement.

Priester encountered modal thinking from multiple directions: through Sun Ra's Arkestra (which explored modal and non-Western scales before *Kind of Blue*), through Max Roach's ensemble (which engaged with modal jazz in its political and musical dimensions), and through Hancock's Mwandishi (which extended modal principles into electronic texture). The trombone is particularly effective in modal contexts because the static harmonic field rewards sustained tones, long phrases, and gradual microtonal variation — exactly the sounds the slide trombone produces most naturally. In bebop's rapid chord changes, the trombone's relatively slower articulation is a limitation. In modal jazz's open spaces, it is an advantage.

**Audible examples:** Miles Davis *Kind of Blue* (1959, Dorian and Mixolydian); Priester's ECM recordings (modal and post-modal textures); Hancock's *Mwandishi* (1971, extended modal exploration); George Russell *Ezz-thetics* (Lydian Chromatic Concept in practice)

### Electronic Jazz Texture: The First Wave

Hancock's Mwandishi sextet represents the first systematic integration of electronic processing into a jazz ensemble. The electronic elements included:

- **Fender Rhodes electric piano** — percussive, bell-like tone that replaced the acoustic piano's resonance with a more present, spatial quality
- **Wah-wah pedal** — a voltage-controlled bandpass filter that sweeps the frequency spectrum, creating a vocal-like "wah" effect
- **Echoplex tape delay** — a magnetic tape loop that records and replays the input signal after a variable delay, creating rhythmic echoes, spatial depth, and (when the feedback is high) cascading repetitions that build into drones
- **ARP synthesizer** — (on later Mwandishi recordings) an analog synthesizer adding fully electronic timbres to the ensemble's palette

Priester's trombone in this electronic environment was both an acoustic source and a processing candidate. The trombone signal could be fed through the same effects chain — wah-wah, delay, distortion — creating sounds that were neither purely acoustic nor purely electronic. This is the first instance of "electroacoustic" jazz in the S36 engine: acoustic instrument plus electronic processing equals a new category of sound.

The electronic jazz texture connects forward to Frisell (degree 1), whose guitar processing through delays, loops, and distortion represents the second wave of jazz electronics (1980s-present). It also connects backward to Sun Ra's Arkestra, which used electric keyboards and homemade electronic instruments in the 1950s before anyone called it "electronic music." Priester is the bridge between these two waves: he played with Sun Ra's proto-electronic experiments, participated in Hancock's systematic first wave, and then settled in Seattle alongside Frisell and Horvitz, whose second-wave electronics defined the city's experimental scene.

### Pedagogy at Cornish: Expansion vs. Preservation

Priester's teaching at Cornish College of the Arts introduces a new pedagogical mode to the S36 engine. The engine now has three educator profiles:

1. **Standifer (degree 4, UW):** Institutional, university-based, preservation-oriented. Teaching the canon — bebop vocabulary, standard repertoire, theoretical fluency. The goal is to transmit the tradition accurately so that the next generation can play it.
2. **Bown (degree 6, informal):** Post-career, underdocumented, survival-based. Teaching because the performance opportunities contracted. The pedagogy is real but invisible to institutional records.
3. **Priester (degree 7, Cornish):** Arts-college-based, expansion-oriented. Teaching boundary-crossing, experimentalism, and the idea that jazz is not a fixed repertoire but an evolving practice. The goal is to expand the tradition so that the next generation can change it.

The Standifer-Priester pedagogical axis is the most important: both are brass players, both became Seattle educators, both shaped generations of students. But Standifer's pedagogy says "learn this" and Priester's pedagogy says "go beyond this." Preservation and expansion are not opponents — a tradition needs both — but they represent different answers to the question "what is jazz education for?"

The Cornish connection also extends the S36 engine's institutional map. Eight degrees, eight distinct educational pipelines: Garfield High School (Jones, 0), Berklee (Frisell, 1), UC Santa Cruz (Frisell, 1 — second institution), UW (Standifer, 4), bandstand apprenticeship (Anderson, 3; Standifer, 4), self-taught (Berry, 5), Seattle University (Bown, 6), DuSable High School (Priester, 7), Cornish College of the Arts (Priester, 7). Priester, like Frisell, connects to two institutions — one formative (DuSable), one professional (Cornish).

## The Sun Ra / Hancock Bridge

### Two Experimental Traditions, One Trombonist

Priester's career bridges two of the most important experimental traditions in jazz history, and understanding this bridge is essential to understanding his place in the S36 engine.

**Sun Ra's tradition** was cosmic Afrofuturism — the proposition that Black music is not bound to the blues, not bound to America, not bound to Earth. Sun Ra's Arkestra played music that referenced ancient Egypt, Saturn, intergalactic travel, and a future in which Black people had transcended the conditions of their terrestrial oppression. The music was often atonal, arhythmic, collectively improvised, and designed to disorient as much as to entertain. The costumes, the mythology, the communal living — all of it was in service of a total artistic vision that rejected the entertainment-industry framework within which jazz operated.

**Hancock's tradition** was electronic expansion — the proposition that jazz's timbral palette could be extended through technology without abandoning the music's rhythmic and harmonic sophistication. Mwandishi was not chaos; it was precisely constructed electronic texture. Hancock's Fender Rhodes was not a gimmick; it was a new instrument with its own harmonic and timbral properties. The electronic processing was not noise; it was a systematic exploration of what happens when acoustic and electronic sound interact in real time.

Priester is the only musician in the S36 engine (and one of very few in jazz history) who participated in both traditions at a high level. He did not observe them from a distance; he played in both ensembles. The Arkestra's cosmic philosophy gave him permission to treat music as a vehicle for ideas larger than entertainment. Mwandishi's electronic methodology gave him the tools to execute that vision with technological precision. The bridge between Sun Ra and Hancock is the bridge between philosophy and technology, between "music can be anything" and "here is how."

This bridge is the connective tissue of the entire post-bop experimental tradition. Without the Arkestra's permission to expand, Mwandishi would have been just another jazz ensemble trying new instruments. Without Mwandishi's technological discipline, the Arkestra's cosmic ambition would have remained localized to one ensemble's practice. Priester carried the ambition from one context and the method from the other.

## Career Arc

### The 1950s: DuSable, Arkestra, Max Roach

Priester emerged from the same South Side Chicago pipeline that produced a generation of jazz masters. DuSable High School under Captain Dyett provided the foundational training. The Arkestra provided the philosophical framework. Max Roach provided the hard bop discipline. By the end of the 1950s, Priester had experienced three distinct musical environments: the school, the commune, and the bandstand. Each left a permanent mark on his artistic identity.

(Source: DuSable High School music program history; Sun Ra Arkestra discography; Max Roach discography; AllMusic; DownBeat)

### The 1960s: Duke Ellington and the New York Scene

Priester's work with Duke Ellington placed him in the most prestigious orchestral context in jazz. Ellington's orchestra was not a workshop for experimentation — it was a monument to composition, arrangement, and ensemble precision. Priester's trombone chair in the Ellington orchestra required him to subordinate his experimental instincts to the composer's vision, an experience that deepened his technical command and his understanding of how individual voices function within a larger design.

(Source: Duke Ellington Orchestra personnel records; Smithsonian Ellington archive; DownBeat; AllMusic; Ellington biography sources)

### The 1970s: Mwandishi and the Move to Seattle

The Mwandishi years (1970-1973) were the most sonically radical period of Priester's career. After the ensemble disbanded, Priester faced the question that every experimental musician confronts: what to do when the experiment ends. His answer was to move to Seattle — not to the Central District's jazz corridor (which was by then largely gone) but to the city at large, and to Cornish College, where he could transmit what he had learned.

The move to Seattle marks Priester as a "Draw" in the S36 engine's artist-city taxonomy — an artist cultivated elsewhere who is drawn to Seattle. But Priester's Draw is distinct from Frisell's (degree 1) and Horvitz's (degree 2). Frisell and Horvitz arrived as working musicians who wanted a different scene. Priester arrived as a musician-educator who wanted a different context — a place where experimental jazz could be taught, not just performed.

(Source: Cornish College of the Arts records; Earshot Jazz; AllMusic; Herbie Hancock, *Possibilities*, 2014; Bob Gluck, *You'll Know When You Get There*, 2012)

### The 1980s-Present: ECM, Cornish, Seattle Scene

Priester's Seattle period spans more than four decades. He recorded for ECM Records — the German label founded by Manfred Eicher in 1969, which became the definitive label for contemplative, spacious, atmospheric jazz. ECM's aesthetic — often characterized as "the most beautiful sound next to silence" — aligned naturally with the textural approach Priester had developed in the Mwandishi period. His ECM recordings extended the electroacoustic vocabulary he had helped create.

At Cornish, Priester influenced generations of students who went on to populate Seattle's music scene across multiple genres. His teaching connected the Arkestra's cosmic ambition, the Mwandishi electronic vocabulary, and the ECM aesthetic into a pedagogical practice that emphasized listening, texture, and the expansion of boundaries. The Frisell-Horvitz-Priester constellation made Seattle's experimental jazz scene a nationally recognized force — not through commercial success but through artistic reputation.

The ECM label connection links Priester directly to Frisell (degree 1), who also recorded extensively for ECM. Two S36 artists on the same label is a first for the engine's label genealogy: Mercury (Jones/Anderson), Columbia (Bown), and now ECM (Frisell/Priester). The ECM connection is more than a label — it is an aesthetic commitment to space, texture, and atmosphere that both Frisell and Priester share.

(Source: ECM Records catalog; Cornish College of the Arts; Earshot Jazz; Seattle Times; KNKX; AllMusic; Paul de Barros coverage of Seattle jazz)

## Seattle Context

### Geographic Diversification: Breaking from the Central District

Priester is the first S36 degree that breaks from the Central District concentration. Degrees 0 through 6 were locked into the CD — six of seven artists directly connected to the neighborhood, all seven connected to jazz. Degree 7 rotates on two axes:

1. **Geographic:** Priester is associated with Seattle generally and Cornish College specifically, not the Central District. Cornish is located on Capitol Hill (and has moved between locations), not in the CD. Priester's Seattle identity is city-wide, not neighborhood-specific.
2. **Origin:** Priester was born in Chicago, not Seattle. He is the first S36 artist whose formative musical experience was entirely outside the Pacific Northwest.

The rotation is not a rejection of the Central District thread. The CD thread remains the engine's densest and most analytically productive cluster. But seven consecutive degrees in one neighborhood was unsustainable. Degree 7 demonstrates that the engine can document artists who are connected to Seattle through residence and pedagogy rather than birth and neighborhood.

The artist-city taxonomy gains a new variant of The Draw:

1. **The Export** (Jones, 0) — local cultivation, global departure, global success
2. **The Draw** (Frisell, 1; Horvitz, 2) — global cultivation, local settlement, scene-building
3. **The Exile** (Anderson, 3) — domestic decline, European audience
4. **The Stay** (Anderson, 3) — return to Seattle, decades as resident anchor
5. **The Teacher** (Standifer, 4) — permanent residency, pedagogical transmission
6. **The Persistence** (Berry, 5) — native-born, never left, still playing
7. **The Erasure** (Bown, 6) — exported, succeeded, forgotten
8. **The Pedagogue-Draw** (Priester, 7) — global cultivation, local settlement as educator, expansion-oriented teaching

Priester's pattern is a hybrid of The Draw and The Teacher. He was drawn to Seattle (like Frisell and Horvitz) AND he became a teacher (like Standifer). But his teaching is oriented toward expansion rather than preservation. The eighth pattern is the first compound pattern in the taxonomy — it cannot be reduced to a single mode.

### The Experimental Jazz Constellation

The Priester-Horvitz-Frisell triangle is the defining structure of Seattle's experimental jazz scene. All three arrived from elsewhere. All three brought distinct experimental traditions. All three found in Seattle a context that supported their work without constraining it.

| Artist | Origin | Experimental Tradition | Arrival |
|--------|--------|----------------------|---------|
| Frisell (1) | Denver/Berklee/NYC/ECM | Americana filtered through avant-garde; guitar processing | Late 1980s |
| Horvitz (2) | NYC downtown | Downtown improv; mixed media; composition-as-structure | Late 1980s |
| Priester (7) | Chicago/Sun Ra/Hancock | Cosmic Afrofuturism + electronic jazz texture | ~1979 |

Priester arrived first — nearly a decade before Frisell and Horvitz. He was the advance party. When Frisell and Horvitz arrived in the late 1980s, Priester was already established at Cornish, already part of the local scene, already teaching the next generation. The experimental jazz constellation was not created by three simultaneous arrivals; it was built in stages, with Priester providing the foundation.
