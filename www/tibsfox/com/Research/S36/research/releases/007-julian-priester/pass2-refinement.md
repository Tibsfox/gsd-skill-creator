### The Slide as Continuous Discovery: Trombone Physics and the Van Allen Belt

Pass 1 documented the trombone's continuous-pitch slide as the engine's first analog brass instrument. Pass 2 deepens this into a structural parallel with Explorer 1's radiation detection — both systems that encounter phenomena through continuous sweeping rather than discrete sampling.

Explorer 1 carried a Geiger-Mueller counter designed by James Van Allen's group at the University of Iowa. As the satellite orbited Earth in its highly elliptical orbit (perigee 358 km, apogee 2,550 km), the counter swept through a range of altitudes, continuously sampling the radiation environment at each point along the trajectory. The counter did not jump between discrete measurement points. It swept — the satellite moved through the radiation field the way a hand moves through water, sensing the pressure at every point along the continuous path. What Van Allen's team discovered was that the radiation intensity did not increase smoothly with altitude. It spiked. At certain altitudes, the counter was overwhelmed — particle flux so intense that the counter saturated and appeared to read zero (a phenomenon that initially confused the team, since zero counts at high altitude seemed physically impossible). The saturation was itself the data: the radiation was so intense that it exceeded the instrument's range, and the belt's existence was inferred from the instrument's failure to measure it.

Priester's trombone slide sweeps through pitch space the way Explorer 1 swept through the radiation belt. The slide does not jump between fixed positions. It moves continuously, and at every point along the slide's travel, it encounters a pitch — not a discrete note but a point on the pitch continuum. When Priester performs a glissando in the Mwandishi context, the slide sweeps through the full spectrum between two notes, revealing every microtonal interval between them. The sweep is the technique. The continuous sampling is the discovery mechanism. And like Explorer 1's saturated counter, the moments of greatest intensity in a Priester glissando are often the moments between the named notes — the blue zones where the Western scale has no label but the ear recognizes something essential.

Lobaria pulmonaria — lungwort lichen — is an indicator species for old-growth forest air quality, the same way Usnea longissima (degree 4) is. But Lobaria is a larger, more structurally complex lichen — a foliose (leafy) form rather than Usnea's pendant (hanging) form. The thallus surface is wrinkled and ridged, resembling lung tissue (hence "lungwort"). The surface area is continuous — not segmented into discrete functional units but folded into a continuous absorptive surface that exchanges gases with the atmosphere at every point. The lichen's nitrogen-fixing capacity (through cyanobacterial photobionts) operates across this entire continuous surface. Lobaria fixes nitrogen the way the trombone produces pitch: through a continuous surface, not through discrete ports.

### The Chicago-to-Seattle Pipeline and the Explorer 1 Institutional Lineage

Pass 1 documented Priester's Chicago origin (DuSable High School, Sun Ra's Arkestra) as the first non-PNW origin story in the S36 engine. Pass 2 connects this pipeline to Explorer 1's institutional lineage and develops the geographic diversification that degree 7 represents.

Explorer 1 was not a NASA mission. It was launched on January 31, 1958 — eight months before NASA was founded on October 1, 1958. Explorer 1 was an Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA) / Jet Propulsion Laboratory joint project, born from the same institutional partnership that would later produce Pioneer 3 (degree 4) and Pioneer 4 (degree 5). The satellite was launched on a Juno I — a modified Redstone missile, the same basic architecture as the Juno II that launched the later Pioneers. But Explorer 1 predates the entire NASA institutional framework. It was built by a pre-NASA partnership, launched on a pre-NASA vehicle, tracked by a pre-NASA network, and analyzed by a university scientist (Van Allen at Iowa) who was not a NASA employee.

Priester's career follows a similar pre-institutional trajectory. His formative musical education — DuSable High School, Sun Ra's Arkestra, Max Roach, Duke Ellington, Herbie Hancock's Mwandishi — all predates his Seattle period. By the time he arrived at Cornish in approximately 1979, he had already been a professional musician for over twenty-five years. His artistic identity was fully formed before the Seattle institutional framework (Cornish, the Earshot Jazz Festival, the KNKX radio coverage, the Frisell-Horvitz experimental scene) existed in its current form. Like Explorer 1, Priester is a pre-institutional entity that became foundational to the institution that formed around it.

The geographic pipeline matters. DuSable High School in Chicago produced a generation of jazz musicians through the same mechanism Garfield High School in Seattle did: a dedicated music teacher (Captain Walter Dyett) in a segregated school concentrating Black talent into one institutional pipeline. The Dyett-to-DuSable pipeline (Chicago) and the Cook-to-Garfield pipeline (Seattle) are structural parallels across two cities, two decades, two sets of students, one mechanism: institutional concentration under a charismatic pedagogue. Priester carried the DuSable pipeline to Seattle, connecting the Chicago and Seattle jazz ecologies through a single artist's trajectory.

### The Expansion-vs-Preservation Pedagogical Axis

Pass 1 noted Priester's Cornish College teaching alongside Standifer's UW teaching. Pass 2 develops the pedagogical polarity these two educators represent and connects it to Explorer 1's role as the experiment that discovered the unexpected.

Standifer (degree 4) taught at the University of Washington — a research university with a music department organized around the European classical tradition. His pedagogy was preservation-oriented: transmitting the jazz tradition's theoretical apparatus, its repertoire, its stylistic vocabulary, and its evaluative criteria to the next generation. Standifer taught students how the tradition worked, what it contained, and how to operate within it. This is the pedagogical equivalent of confirming a prediction: Van Allen predicted radiation belts based on Stoermer and Birkeland's earlier theoretical work, and Explorer 1 confirmed the prediction. The data matched the theory. The belt was where theory said it would be.

Priester taught at Cornish College of the Arts — an arts college with an interdisciplinary orientation that emphasized boundary-crossing and experimentation. His pedagogy was expansion-oriented: teaching students to push beyond the tradition's established vocabulary, to incorporate electronics, non-Western influences, and improvisational strategies that the canonical tradition did not include. This is the pedagogical equivalent of discovering the unexpected: Explorer 1's counter saturated at altitudes where Van Allen expected to find radiation, but the saturation — the instrument reading zero when it should have been reading high — was unexpected data that required a new interpretation. The counter's failure to behave as expected was the discovery. Priester's pedagogy teaches students to seek the moments when the expected framework fails and the unexpected emerges.

Two brass educators. Two institutions. Two pedagogical orientations:

| Dimension | Standifer (Degree 4) | Priester (Degree 7) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Institution | UW (research university) | Cornish (arts college) |
| Orientation | Preservation | Expansion |
| Method | Theory, repertoire, tradition | Experiment, texture, boundary-crossing |
| Musical lineage | Jackson Street → UW | Arkestra → Mwandishi → Cornish |
| Instrument | Trumpet (discrete, valved) | Trombone (continuous, slide) |
| NASA parallel | Pioneer 3 (confirming the outer belt) | Explorer 1 (discovering the belt in the first place) |

The preservation/expansion axis is not a value judgment. Both orientations are necessary. Without preservation, the tradition has no coherent body of knowledge to transmit. Without expansion, the tradition ossifies. Standifer and Priester are the two poles of a healthy pedagogical ecosystem — the hemlock and the fireweed, the conservator and the colonizer.
