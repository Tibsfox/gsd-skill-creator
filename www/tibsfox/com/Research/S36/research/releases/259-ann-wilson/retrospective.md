## What Worked

1. **The separation from Nancy is handled correctly.** Ann and Nancy Wilson get individual degrees (259, 260) that are distinct in every dimension: instrument, artistic contribution, career trajectory. Ann is the vocalist; Nancy is the guitarist. The engine treats them as individuals because they are individuals.
2. **The belt technique description is the central analytical contribution.** "Full-body belt = chest voice driven into higher registers with control, not screaming" -- this distinction is important. Wilson's technique is not shouting; it is controlled power. The control is harder to achieve than the power.
3. **"Barracuda" as the defining document is correct.** It combines the aggressive guitar arrangement with Wilson's controlled high-register vocal and demonstrates the technique at its peak. Music schools use it; the song is cited universally when Wilson's contribution is discussed.
4. **The tradition-building argument is documented.** There was no template for Ann Wilson's approach in 1975. She created it. The subsequent female hard rock vocal tradition -- Lzzy Hale, Amy Lee, Taylor Momsen -- runs through Wilson directly.

## Discoveries

1. **The dynamic control demonstrated in "Alone" is the less-documented achievement.** Everyone knows the belt. The ability to hold back, build tension, and then release completely at the end of "Alone" requires a different kind of control from "Barracuda"'s sustained power. The two songs together document the full range of Wilson's technique.
2. **The solo albums as cover documentation is an interesting artistic choice.** Wilson chose to demonstrate the voice through other artists' songs rather than develop a solo songwriter identity. The Hope & Glory approach -- "watch what this voice does to songs you already know" -- is a specific artistic statement.
