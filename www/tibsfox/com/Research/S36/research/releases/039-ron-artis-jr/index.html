The Return: Same Artist, Different Lens

Ron Artis Jr is Ron Artis II. “II” and “Jr” both designate the son of Ron Artis Sr. — the Motown session player who played keyboards on Michael Jackson’s Thriller. At degree 24, this artist was examined through the Soul/Jazz lens: the Motown dynasty, the Hawaiian cultural context, KEXP as institutional amplifier. At degree 39, the same artist is refracted through Blues/Soul: the blues guitar tradition, the raw emotional performance, the self-released independence, and the deepening migration from Hawaii to the Pacific Northwest.

Every dimension has shifted between the two appearances: genre (Soul/Jazz → Blues/Soul), energy (E=4 → E=5), era (2010–present → Hawaii to Seattle), label (KEXP → self-released), pattern (#25 Island Export → #40 Returning Wave), acoustic element (#25 Invocation → #40 Pacific Crossing). The same person occupies different positions in the engine’s multidimensional space.

“We want to give you something to help with your journey and help keep music in your life.” — Ron Artis II, Maui fire instrument donation (ABC Good Morning America, December 2023)

The Blues Lens

At degree 24, the Motown lineage and Hawaiian culture were primary. At degree 39, the blues takes the lead. Ron Artis Jr’s guitar work — self-taught from age 13, shaped by the North Shore music community — enters the blues thread that runs from Robert Cray (35°) through Jimi Hendrix (36°) and Ayron Jones (37°). Critics have compared his playing to Hendrix and Gary Clark Jr. Blues & Soul Magazine noted he “rips, and has a voice to match the big sounds coming from his guitar.”

The Blues/Soul genre compound is new to the engine — blues primary with soul secondary. It bridges the blues zone (35–37) and the soul zone (24, 29–30, 38) through a single artist who embodies both traditions. The Hawaiian genre fluidity documented at degree 24 (kanikapila, multi-ethnic fusion) is the mechanism that makes this dual-genre identity possible: when genre boundaries are cultural defaults, one artist can inhabit multiple genre positions without contradiction.

Hawaii to Seattle: The Pacific Crossing

The era field — “Hawaii to Seattle” — describes a geographic trajectory, not a time period. Ron Artis Jr has been building his Pacific Northwest presence since 2019: Waterfront Blues Festival in Portland (2019, 2022), Neptune Theatre in Seattle (2022, 2023), The Reser in Beaverton (2022), Portland Folk Festival (2023), The Rabbit Box in Seattle (2025), and Central Oregon venues (2026). Seven documented PNW performances across seven years — each return deepening the gravitational bond between island origin and mainland destination.

All four studio albums are self-released: What Music Means to Me (2016), Soul Street (2018), Purpose (2021), and We Are Not Machines (2024, Grammy-considered via John Oates submission). Independent by choice, not by default.
