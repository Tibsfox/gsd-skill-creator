Folk Country — Genre Stage 69 — The Bloodshot Years

Folk Country is the sixty-ninth genre stage. The early Neko Case is rooted in Bloodshot Records — Chicago’s alt-country and insurgent country label. The Virginian (1997) and Furnace Room Lullaby (2000) are folk country: twang, pedal steel, honky-tonk structures filtered through a voice that would later become one of indie music’s most distinctive instruments. But here, in the Bloodshot years, the voice is still emerging.

Genre Stage 69: Folk Country — The Bloodshot Years

Degree 87 reaches Genre Stage 69. Neko Case’s early work introduces Folk Country — distinguished from the Alt Country of degree 66 by its rawer, more traditional folk-country roots. The Bloodshot Records context is key: Bloodshot was (and is) Chicago’s insurgent country label, home to artists who blended punk energy with country structures. Case’s early work fits this mold — The Virginian (1997) has twang, pedal steel, honky-tonk bones. Furnace Room Lullaby (2000) begins the transition toward the more expansive sound of her later Anti- Records work.

The connection to Acid Tongue (degree 78, Folk Country) is direct — both operate in the folk-country space, though Acid Tongue is a band project while Case is a solo artist. The folk-country genre stage advances from its prior appearance.

Bloodshot Records

Bloodshot Records is Case’s early label — Chicago-based, specializing in insurgent country, alt-country, and punk-inflected roots music. Case recorded The Virginian (1997) and Furnace Room Lullaby (2000) for Bloodshot before moving to Anti- Records for her mature work. The label context matters: Bloodshot is scrappier, rawer, more punk-adjacent than Anti-. The early recordings reflect that context.
