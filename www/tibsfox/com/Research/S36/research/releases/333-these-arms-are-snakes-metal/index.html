Capitol Hill Post-Metal

These Arms Are Snakes are one of Capitol Hill's heaviest and most architecturally serious bands — operating from 2003 to 2009 in the post-grunge underground, when Seattle bands were free to pursue complexity without commercial calculation. The band combined post-metal's long-form compositional ambition with a hardcore urgency that prevented the music from becoming purely atmospheric. The result is heavy music with a specific Capitol Hill character: too textured for the metal scene, too aggressive for the indie underground, occupying the space between categories.

Bassist Brian Cook's presence makes degree 333 one of the engine's most deeply cross-referenced entries. Cook played bass in Botch (degree 332, Tacoma, mathcore) and co-founded Russian Circles (Chicago, post-metal instrumental) — creating a documentary line from mathcore's foundations through post-metal's development that runs directly through two consecutive engine degrees.

"Architecture is the right word for it. The songs are built, not discovered. The weight accumulates through structure, not just volume."

Discography

Oxeneers or the Lion Sleeps When Its Antelope Go Home (2004)

Initial Records

The debut. Core approach established: aggressive post-metal, literate lyrics, Brian Cook's precise bass, extreme dynamic range. The Capitol Hill post-metal vocabulary begins here.

Easter (2006)

Suicide Squeeze Records

The definitive statement. Refined dynamic shifts, longer atmospheric passages, vocals integrated into texture. The band's most critically recognized record. Post-metal architecture at full realization.

Tail Swallower and Dove (2008)

Suicide Squeeze Records

The final record. More experimental, extended quiet passages, greater dynamic range. Title references the Ouroboros — the snake eating its own tail. An appropriate conclusion. Dissolution followed in 2009.

E=8 — Architectural Maximum

E=8 is the engine's second-highest energy, calibrated for a band whose heaviness operates through accumulated textural weight rather than pure velocity. These Arms Are Snakes are not the fastest band in the engine — they are one of the architecturally densest. The post-metal tradition's defining mechanism — building from near-silence to crushing distortion over extended song structures — generates its own form of maximum intensity, one that is proportionate to E=8 at the scale of Capitol Hill's most serious underground bands.

The Metal Summit opens at E=8 precisely because architectural complexity is the correct entry to a four-degree arc that escalates through sludge primogeniture, heavy metal evolution, and grunge-metal synthesis. The architecture comes first; the full weight follows.
