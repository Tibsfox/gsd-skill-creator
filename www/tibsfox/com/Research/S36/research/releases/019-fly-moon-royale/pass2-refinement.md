### The Leap: First Human in American Space, First Central District Neo-Soul Collective

Mercury-Redstone 3 — Freedom 7 — launched Alan Bartlett Shepard Jr. on May 5, 1961. The flight lasted 15 minutes and 28 seconds. Shepard reached an altitude of 187 km. He experienced five minutes of weightlessness. He was the first American in space. Not the first human — Yuri Gagarin had orbited Earth 23 days earlier, on April 12, 1961. But Shepard was the first American, and the flight was the culmination of everything the Mercury program had been building toward: MR-1 (four inches, degree 16), MR-2 (Ham suborbital, degree 17), MA-5 (Enos orbital, degree 18), and now Freedom 7 — a human being, strapped into the capsule, riding the Redstone into the sky.

Fly Moon Royale represents its own kind of first leap: the first Central District neo-soul collective, the first time the CD's musical identity explicitly includes soul/R&B alongside its historical jazz foundation. The Central District of 2012 is not the Central District of 1950 — gentrification has transformed the neighborhood. But Parker's decision to root Fly Moon Royale in the CD is a leap of faith: the assertion that the neighborhood still produces music, still sustains artistic community, still matters as a geographic identity even as its demographics shift. Shepard's leap was physical (surface to space). Fly Moon Royale's leap is cultural (jazz tradition to neo-soul future, historic CD to gentrifying CD).

The Pacific giant salamander (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*) is the largest terrestrial salamander in North America — up to 30 cm, capable of climbing, barking (the only salamander with a true vocalization), and surviving in the cold, fast-flowing mountain streams of the PNW. It is a giant hidden in plain sight — most people walk over its habitat without knowing it's there. Shepard was a test pilot hidden in plain sight — a Navy aviator whose name was unknown to the public until NASA selected him. Fly Moon Royale is a collective hidden in plain sight — playing the Central District while the national music press looks elsewhere.

### The Collective as Third Organizational Type

Fly Moon Royale introduces the collective model — a rotating ensemble orbiting a central creative authority (Parker's vocals and vision). The engine now has three organizational types: solo artist (degrees 0-17), fixed band (degree 18, Maktub), and collective (degree 19, Fly Moon Royale). The collective is the most fluid type — the identity is the aesthetic, not the personnel.

Freedom 7 was crewed by one astronaut in a capsule built by one contractor (McDonnell Aircraft) launched on one rocket (Redstone). The organizational model was centralized — one person, one vehicle, one mission. But the infrastructure supporting that single astronaut was a collective: thousands of engineers, technicians, flight controllers, and tracking station operators coordinated by NASA's Space Task Group. The single astronaut was the visible creative authority. The collective was the infrastructure. Fly Moon Royale operates similarly: Parker is the visible creative center, but the music is created by the collective of rotating musicians whose contributions shape each performance.

### Central District: The Thread That Won't Break

The CD returns at degree 19 after a seven-degree absence (12-18). The oscillation pattern is clear: long absences punctuated by single-degree returns (degree 11, now degree 19). Each return is in a different era and genre. The thread demonstrates geographic persistence — the Central District continues to produce music even as gentrification transforms its demographics. The lineage from Berry's stride groove (degree 5) to Fly Moon Royale's neo-soul groove (degree 19) is traceable through the CD geography: different decades, different genres, same neighborhood commitment to groove-first music-making.
