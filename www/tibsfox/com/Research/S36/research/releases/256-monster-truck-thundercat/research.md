## THE BAND AND THE NAME

### Monster Truck + Thundercat: A Compound of Spectacle

The name Monster Truck Thundercat is worth examining:

- **Monster Truck.** Monster trucks -- pickup trucks or similar vehicles with enormous tires, modified suspensions, and reinforced bodies -- emerged as an entertainment phenomenon in the 1970s-1980s. The original monster truck, Bigfoot, was created in 1975 by Bob Chandler in St. Louis. By the 1980s, monster truck competitions -- in which trucks drove over and crushed regular cars -- had become a form of arena entertainment drawing large crowds. Monster Jam, the major monster truck competition series, continues to fill arenas across the United States. The monster truck is a specifically American form of spectacle: enormous, loud, destructive, and entertaining. As a name reference in stoner rock, the monster truck evokes exactly what stoner rock sounds like to outsiders: big, slow, loud, and crushing things.
- **Thundercat.** Thundercats was an American animated series that aired 1985-1987, created by Ted Wolf and produced by Rankin/Bass. The show followed humanoid cat-like aliens (ThunderCats) stranded on a planet called Third Earth. The primary characters -- Lion-O, Tygra, Cheetara, Panthro, Snarf -- became icons of 1980s animated television. The Thundercats have remained in cultural memory as a reference point for 1980s childhood. A different Thundercat -- the solo project and later full-time identity of bassist/vocalist Stephen "Thundercat" Bruner -- represents an entirely different musical tradition (jazz, funk, soul, R&B) and was a key collaborator for Kendrick Lamar and Flying Lotus.
- **The compound name as aesthetic statement.** Stoner rock band names frequently draw from 1970s-1980s pop culture, science fiction, horror, and spectacle. Names like Earthride, Riffmaster General, Greenleaf, Wo Fat, Bongzilla, and Stoned Jesus demonstrate the genre's willingness to be knowingly excessive in its naming conventions. Monster Truck Thundercat operates in this tradition: knowingly excessive, culturally specific to a certain era of American pop culture, and funny in a deadpan way.

(Sources: Monster truck history; Bigfoot 1975 Bob Chandler; Monster Jam; Thundercats 1985-1987; Rankin/Bass; Thundercat Stephen Bruner (distinct); stoner rock naming conventions; 1970s-80s pop culture references in band names)

## SELF-RELEASED STONER ROCK

### The DIY Infrastructure of Heavy Psych

Self-released stoner rock is not marginal -- it is one of the genre's primary distribution modes:

- **Bandcamp as distribution.** Bandcamp (founded 2008) has become the primary distribution platform for self-released stoner, doom, and heavy psych music. The platform allows bands to sell digital downloads and physical merch (including vinyl) directly to listeners, keeping the majority of revenue. For stoner rock specifically, Bandcamp has been transformative: bands that would previously have needed label distribution to reach international listeners can now reach the European heavy psych community directly. A self-released stoner rock band in Seattle can have listeners in Germany, Sweden, and Italy without label mediation.
- **The underground vinyl circuit.** Self-released stoner rock frequently appears on limited-edition vinyl pressings -- 300 to 1000 copies, often on colored vinyl, typically sold at shows, through Bandcamp, and through a small number of underground record stores. This vinyl underground is a genuine distribution network for heavy psych: shops like Aquarius Records (San Francisco, closed 2016), Forced Exposure (Newburyport MA), and various European shops specialize in underground heavy music and carry self-released vinyl from bands that have no label presence.
- **The small venue circuit.** Self-released stoner bands tour the small-venue circuit: bars with stages, DIY spaces, small rock clubs with capacities of 50-300. In Seattle, this means venues like Tractor Tavern, Highline, Barboza, and similar spaces. The touring infrastructure for heavy music exists without label support -- promoters book bands based on genre reputation and existing audience rather than label affiliation.
- **Social proof without institutional support.** In the streaming era, a band can accumulate listeners, critical notice (from heavy music blogs and zines), and show-going audiences without any label involvement. Monster Truck Thundercat operate within this system: the band exists as a real musical entity in the Seattle stoner scene without the institutional infrastructure of a label like Sargent House (Helms Alee, 253) or Heavy Psych Sounds (Valley of the Giants, 255).

(Sources: Bandcamp 2008; self-released stoner rock distribution; European stoner audience via Bandcamp; underground vinyl circuit 300-1000 copies; Aquarius Records; Forced Exposure; small venue circuit Seattle; social proof without institutional support)

## THE SOUND -- STONER ROCK, E=6

### Thick Riffs, Slow Tempo, Fuzz-Forward

Monster Truck Thundercat's sonic approach within the stoner tradition:

- **The core tools.** Fuzz guitar (warm, enormous, slightly out of focus), downtuned bass (adding low-frequency weight), drums at moderate tempo establishing the groove. The combination produces the characteristic stoner rock density: the sound feels thick, warm, and physical.
- **The riff-forward approach.** Stoner rock bands lead with the riff: the riff is the song's primary identity, its marketing hook, its emotional content. A great stoner riff can sustain an eight-minute song with minimal harmonic or structural variation because the riff itself is the content. Monster Truck Thundercat operate in this tradition: build the riff, sustain the riff, extend the riff into psychedelic territory.
- **E=6 in the context of E=6.** Degree 255 also runs at E=6. Two consecutive stoner rock degrees at the same energy level is unusual. The engine holds the energy constant while changing the institutional context: the music is equivalent in energy; the bands exist in different positions relative to the music industry. This is the double stoner degree's specific contribution to the engine's data.
- **Production values.** Self-released stoner rock often has production that foregrounds warmth and density over clarity: recorded in home studios or small tracking studios, mixed to emphasize low-frequency weight, mastered to maximize loudness within vinyl constraints. This is a genre aesthetic as much as a budget constraint -- stoner rock is supposed to sound like it was recorded in 1972. The self-released position makes this aesthetic choice available without institutional argument.
