## LOVE BATTERY -- THE PSYCHEDELIC EVOLUTION

### From Sub Pop to Psychedelic Maturity

The arc from *Between the Eyes* (1990) to *Confusion Au Go-Go* (1995) is a story of deepening psychedelic commitment:

- **Phase 1: Sub Pop Years (1990-1992).** *Between the Eyes* and *Dayglo* are the grunge-adjacent psychedelic records. The fuzz guitar is present but the production aesthetic is in conversation with Sub Pop's grunge catalog. These are the records that defined Love Battery in the cultural moment of the early Seattle explosion.
- **Phase 2: Atlas Records (1993-1994).** *Far Gone* (1993, Sub Pop/Atlas) is the transition record. Love Battery moves from the Sub Pop context to a relationship with Atlas Records, a subsidiary of PolyGram that was working with alternative and indie bands. The sound expands -- more dynamic range, more arrangement variety, longer instrumental passages. The psychedelia is more prominent. The grunge context recedes.
- **Phase 3: Mercury/Atlas (1995).** *Confusion Au Go-Go* is Love Battery at their most adventurous. The title references the Fillmore Auditorium's legendary "Acid Tests" -- the Au Go-Go venue context -- and the psychedelic lineage is explicit. The album is more experimental than its predecessors: longer songs, more unusual structures, richer production. This is the record where Love Battery has fully committed to the psychedelic identity that was always present but shared space with grunge.
- **The E=6 shift.** The move from E=5 to E=6 reflects this deepening. Psychedelic rock at E=5 is swirling and present. At E=6, it acquires additional weight -- the riffs are heavier, the dynamics wider, the songs more demanding. Love Battery at E=6 is not aggressive; it is immersive. The density increases without losing the spaciousness that defines the genre.

## THE SOUND -- E=6 PSYCHEDELIC ROCK

### Heavier, Denser, More Expansive

The E=6 Love Battery is the mature form:

- **Guitar density.** Ron Nine's guitar work thickens over the catalog. The fuzz pedal is more prominent, the reverb trails longer, the chord voicings denser. By *Confusion Au Go-Go*, Nine's guitar creates a wall of textured sound that has moved beyond the grunge-adjacent fuzz of the early Sub Pop material.
- **Song length and structure.** The later Love Battery records feature longer songs with more elaborate structural logic. The verse-chorus template remains, but the instrumental passages between sections grow. The songs develop. This is psychedelic rock's fundamental approach -- the song is a journey, not a statement.
- **Rhythm section weight.** The rhythm section holds the dense guitar work in place. Jason Finn's drumming (present through the Sub Pop period before his attention shifted to PUSA) and Tommy Bonaldo's bass anchor the expansion. The heavier material requires a rhythm section that can sustain momentum without surrendering to chaos.
- **Vocal approach.** Ron Nine's vocals remain understated across the catalog -- the low-key delivery is structural, not lazy. In the psychedelic context, the voice is another instrument contributing to the textural whole rather than dominating it. The later records maintain this approach even as the guitar density increases.
- **The fuzz-reverb matrix.** The combination of fuzz distortion and reverb defines Love Battery's sonic signature. Fuzz makes the guitar enormous; reverb gives it space. The two effects in combination create the "enormous and slightly out of focus" quality that distinguishes Love Battery from grunge (which uses fuzz without reverb for a harder, more defined sound).

## DISCOGRAPHY -- THE E=6 RECORDS

### Far Gone and Confusion Au Go-Go

The two records that define the degree-233 Love Battery:

- ***Far Gone* (1993, Sub Pop/Atlas Records).** The pivot record. Released as Love Battery was transitioning from the Sub Pop context, *Far Gone* expands the sonic palette established on *Dayglo*. Production is cleaner and more spacious than the early Sub Pop work. The songs have more dynamic range -- quieter passages that make the heavy moments land harder. The Sub Pop / Atlas release split indicates the transitional nature: not yet fully committed to a new context but no longer defining themselves primarily through the grunge catalog.
- ***Confusion Au Go-Go* (1995, Atlas/Mercury Records).** The psychedelic statement. The title's Fillmore-era reference is an explicit claim of lineage: Love Battery as inheritors of the 1960s psychedelic tradition filtered through the Seattle 1990s context. The album is more experimental than its predecessors -- unusual song structures, extended instrumental sections, richer and stranger production. Grunge's commercial moment had passed by 1995; Love Battery does not attempt to recapture it. Instead, *Confusion Au Go-Go* is the sound of a band fully committed to its own psychedelic identity.
- ***E.S.P.* (1999, Pop Llama).** Late-period release on a small indie label. The band continues to release music four years after their most adventurous major-label work. This is commitment.

(Sources: Far Gone 1993; Confusion Au Go-Go 1995; E.S.P. 1999; Atlas Records; Mercury Records; Pop Llama)

## THE PSYCHEDELIC ROCK TRADITION

### Love Battery in Context

Understanding Love Battery requires understanding the psychedelic rock tradition they inhabit:

- **The lineage.** Psychedelic rock derives from the 1960s confluence of electric guitar amplification, drug culture, and expanded ideas about consciousness. The core tradition runs through Jimi Hendrix, Blue Cheer, The 13th Floor Elevators, and the San Francisco bands (Jefferson Airplane, Grateful Dead, Quicksilver Messenger Service). The 1980s-90s continuation includes British shoegaze (My Bloody Valentine, Slowdive, Ride), noise rock (Sonic Youth), and Seattle's own psychedelic wing (Love Battery, Wipers).
- **Blue Cheer as reference point.** Blue Cheer is the most accurate ancestor for Love Battery's approach. The San Francisco trio's combination of extreme fuzz guitar, heavy bass, and a kind of psychedelic momentum without conventional song structure is the template. Love Battery adds melody and Sub Pop-era production values but the essential Blue Cheer formula is present.
- **Spacemen 3.** The English duo (Sonic Boom and J. Spaceman) produced drone-psychedelic music in the late 1980s that is the closest British equivalent to Love Battery's American approach. The emphasis on repetition, fuzz, and the meditative quality of extended instrumental passages connects the traditions.
- **The Seattle psychedelic wing.** Love Battery are not isolated in Seattle's psychedelic tradition. The Wipers, the Portland band that strongly influenced Seattle's music scene, had psychedelic elements. Mudhoney (Sub Pop) used fuzz in a more punk-aggressive way but with clear psychedelic influences. Love Battery is the most explicitly psychedelic of the Sub Pop bands.

(Sources: Psychedelic rock tradition; Blue Cheer; Spacemen 3; Wipers; Seattle psychedelic lineage; Sub Pop context)

## RON NINE -- GUITARIST

### The Creative Center

Ron Nine (born Ron Schilling) is Love Battery's creative center and the guitarist whose approach most defines the band:

- **Guitar philosophy.** Nine's approach to the electric guitar is textural rather than technical. He does not pursue virtuosic display -- the solos in Love Battery songs are short and serve the song's texture rather than showcasing technical facility. The primary interest is in what fuzz and reverb can do to a chord or riff: how they can make it enormous, spacious, slightly out of focus.
- **The U-District roots.** Nine's approach to music emerged from the U-District environment -- a neighborhood where you could buy used records cheaply, see shows at small venues, and develop a sound slowly without commercial pressure. The Ave provided the context; Nine provided the work.
- **Influence.** Nine's guitar work influenced Seattle bands that came after Love Battery and moved toward shoegaze and dream pop -- traditions that share the fuzz-reverb matrix but apply it to different ends (more atmospheric, less riff-based). The Love Battery sound is an ancestor of Seattle's post-grunge shoegaze-adjacent scene.
- **The long arc.** Nine continued releasing music with Love Battery into 1999 (*E.S.P.*) -- a decade after the band's formation. The commitment to the psychedelic approach, even as the commercial context changed entirely, is a statement of artistic identity.

(Sources: Ron Nine biography; U-District musical development; fuzz-reverb guitar philosophy; influence on Seattle shoegaze; Love Battery longevity)

## LEGACY -- THE QUIETLY INFLUENTIAL

### What Love Battery Left Behind

Love Battery's legacy in Seattle rock is not measured in platinum albums:

- **Psychedelic credibility.** In a catalog defined by grunge, Love Battery provided Sub Pop with psychedelic credibility. They were the label's proof that it was not a monoculture -- that the eclectic early days of Sub Pop produced a genuine range.
- **The shoegaze bridge.** Love Battery's fuzz-reverb approach is an ancestor of the shoegaze-adjacent Seattle bands that followed. The lineage runs from Love Battery through to bands that took the dense guitar texture and pushed it toward more atmospheric ends.
- **Artistic integrity.** The decision to deepen the psychedelic commitment on *Confusion Au Go-Go* rather than chase the commercial moment is a statement that deserves acknowledgment. By 1995, grunge was commercially overexposed and radio formats were shifting. Love Battery's response was to go further into their own sound, not toward the center. This is artistic integrity.
- **The U-District contribution.** Love Battery's two appearances in the engine (221 and 233) ensure that the U-District's contribution to Seattle music history is documented with appropriate depth. The Ave produced this band; the band represents the Ave across a decade of work.

(Sources: Love Battery legacy; Sub Pop diversity; shoegaze-adjacent Seattle; artistic integrity 1995; U-District contribution to Seattle music)
