## THE THREE VOICES

### Vocal Democracy in Heavy Music

Helms Alee's vocal approach is their defining characteristic:

- **Three singers.** Ben Verellen (guitar/vocals), Dana James (bass/vocals), and Hozoji Matheson-Margullis (drums/vocals). All three sing, all three scream, all three contribute melodic passages. This is rare in heavy music, where vocals are typically dominated by a single frontperson.
- **Male/female contrast.** The alternation between male and female vocals creates a tonal range that monogendered bands cannot achieve. Ben's vocals tend toward gruff, low, sludge-metal. Dana and Hozoji provide higher registers, cleaner tones, and a melodic counterpoint. The gender diversity is not performative -- it's structural. The music requires both tonal registers.
- **Clean/harsh spectrum.** Within a single song, Helms Alee may move from whispered clean vocals to full-throated screaming, from tight harmonies to chaotic overlapping shouts. The vocal dynamics mirror the instrumental dynamics -- quiet to crushing.
- **Vocal democracy.** No single voice dominates. The three voices take turns leading, supporting, and harmonizing. This creates an unpredictability that keeps the listener engaged through dense, heavy passages -- you never know which voice is coming next.

(Sources: Helms Alee vocal approach; Ben Verellen; Dana James; Hozoji Matheson-Margullis; heavy music vocal traditions)

## NOISE ROCK -- SEATTLE'S HEAVY CONTINUUM

### From Melvins to Helms Alee

Helms Alee exists within Seattle's long lineage of heavy, experimental rock:

- **The Melvins.** The ur-band of Seattle heavy music. Buzz Osborne and Dale Crover created sludge before it had a name. The Melvins' influence on Seattle is pervasive -- grunge, noise rock, doom, and sludge all trace lineage back to them.
- **TAD.** Tad Doyle's band brought industrial-strength heaviness to Sub Pop in the late 1980s. TAD was heavier than Nirvana, heavier than Soundgarden. The sheer physical weight of the sound.
- **Boris (Seattle-adjacent).** The Japanese band Boris has strong Seattle ties, frequently collaborating with local musicians and playing extended residencies. Boris's blend of doom, noise, and beauty parallels Helms Alee's approach.
- **Helms Alee's position.** Helms Alee inherits the Melvins' weight, TAD's density, and Boris's genre fluidity. But the three-voice approach and the melodic undertow distinguish them. Helms Alee is heavy but never monotonal. The beauty is always there, buried under sludge, surfacing unexpectedly.
- **Ben Verellen's Verellen Amplifiers.** Guitarist Ben Verellen builds amplifiers by hand (Verellen Amplifiers) -- the instruments of heaviness as craft objects. The amps are known for their warm, thick, punishing tone. The builder of the tool is the user of the tool.

(Sources: Seattle heavy music lineage; Melvins; TAD; Boris; Verellen Amplifiers; noise rock history)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Helms Alee

- **Tempo.** Slow to mid-tempo. 70-120 BPM. Sludge pacing. The music lumbers, swings, occasionally accelerates into punk-adjacent passages.
- **Guitar.** Down-tuned, heavily distorted. Riffs are thick, simple, repetitive. Clean passages emerge unexpectedly -- arpeggiated, reverbed, almost pretty. The contrast between the crushing riffs and the clean openings is Helms Alee's signature instrumental dynamic.
- **Bass.** Massively distorted, low-frequency dominant. Dana James's bass tone is a wall of sub-frequency rumble. The bass often carries the primary riff while guitar provides textural weight above.
- **Drums.** Powerful, dynamic, diverse. Hozoji's drumming ranges from blast-adjacent speed to slow, swinging sludge grooves. The drums are the dynamic engine -- they signal the transitions between heavy and melodic.
- **Vocals.** Three voices alternating: gruff male, clean female, screamed unisex. Harmonies, unison, call-and-response. The vocal trading is unpredictable and keeps the listener off-balance.
- **Volume.** Loud. Helms Alee is a loud band. The physical presence of the low-frequency content requires volume.
- **Production.** Dense but articulate. Unlike purely sludge production (which can be murky), Helms Alee's recordings allow individual instruments to be heard within the density. The melodic passages need clarity.

(Sources: Helms Alee recordings; sludge production; power trio dynamics; Sargent House production)
