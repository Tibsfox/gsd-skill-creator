The Genre-Fluid Powerhouse

Alice Smith is a singer-songwriter with a four-octave vocal range whose music defies single-genre classification. Born November 30, 1977, raised between Washington, D.C. and Augusta, Georgia, educated at Fordham University in New York, now based in Los Angeles — Smith’s geography traces the full American map. Her sound blends neo-soul, rock, blues, jazz, gospel, cabaret, and art-pop into something Rolling Stone recognized in 2006 as “a broader palette” than contemporaries Norah Jones and Alicia Keys.

The engine classifies her as Neo-Soul — the genre she orbits closest to — but acknowledges this is a compression. Alice Smith is neo-soul the way Jimi Hendrix was blues rock: the genre is a starting point, not a boundary.

“Smith could easily be lumped in with expressive chanteuses like Norah Jones and Alicia Keys, but she has a broader palette than either.” — Rolling Stone, 2006

Epic Records is the engine’s first Sony-family major label. The label that released Michael Jackson’s Thriller (produced by Quincy Jones, degree 0°) shelved Alice Smith’s sophomore album for six years. The same corporate family later distributed her Sinners soundtrack work and her 2026 Oscar performance. Full circle — on her terms.

The D.C. Foundation: Gospel, Go-Go, and Genre Freedom

Washington, D.C.’s contribution to American music is go-go — the polyrhythmic, percussion-heavy funk descendant created by Chuck Brown in the 1970s. Smith grew up on “a steady diet of gospel, pop, soul, and a little go-go thrown in for good measure.” The go-go influence is the rhythmic foundation beneath her genre-fluid surface — a D.C.-specific pulse that distinguishes her from New York neo-soul (D’Angelo), Philadelphia neo-soul (The Roots/Jill Scott), or Dallas neo-soul (Erykah Badu).

At Fordham University, Smith joined the Black Rock Coalition — the organization founded by Vernon Reid (Living Colour) to promote Black artists in rock and experimental music. The BRC’s thesis of genre freedom for Black musicians connects directly to Jimi Hendrix (degree 36°) and to Alice Smith’s refusal to fit any single genre box.

The Prince Connection

In 2014, Prince recorded his interpretation of Alice Smith’s “Another Love” on the album Plectrumelectrum with 3rdeyegirl. When Prince covers your song, the genre-fluid lineage is confirmed. Prince’s own practice — funk, rock, pop, soul, jazz, psychedelia — mirrors Smith’s, and his selection of her material is peer recognition from one of the most genre-fluid artists in recorded music history.

The Sinners Moment (2025–2026)

Alice Smith co-wrote and performed “Last Time (I Seen the Sun)” with Miles Caton for Ryan Coogler’s film Sinners (2025), set in 1932 Mississippi Delta. She then performed “I Lied to You” at the 98th Academy Awards (March 15, 2026) alongside Raphael Saadiq, Brittany Howard, Buddy Guy, Christone “Kingfish” Ingram, Misty Copeland, and Bobby Rush.

The Oscar performance is the full-circle moment: an artist shelved by Sony subsidiary Epic Records returned to the Sony ecosystem (Sony Masterworks) two decades later, performing on the biggest stage in entertainment. Not waiting for permission. Arriving on her own terms.
