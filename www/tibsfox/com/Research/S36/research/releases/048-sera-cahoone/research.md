## STRUCTURAL EVENT: The Drummer Who Became a Singer-Songwriter

Degree 48 introduces the engine's **first artist known primarily for a different instrument before their solo career**. Sera Cahoone was a **drummer** before she was a singer-songwriter. She began drumming at age twelve to combat anxiety. She drummed for **Carissa's Wierd** (1998-2003), recorded drums on **Band of Horses'** debut album *Everything All The Time* (2006, Sub Pop), and played session drums for **Patrick Park**, **Panda and Angel**, and **Betsy Olson**. She received a **Gold Record** in 2019 for her contributions to the Band of Horses debut.

The transition from drummer to singer-songwriter is the structural event: Cahoone had to overcome severe stage fright and anxiety about singing in public. She was, by her own account, "a super shy kid" who found it terrifying to have people hear her voice and her lyrics. She forced herself to play open mic nights. She sent a demo to KEXP host **John Richards**, who played it on air -- the first public exposure of Sera Cahoone the songwriter rather than Sera Cahoone the drummer.

This instrument-transition narrative connects to the engine's documentation of what happens when musicians change roles. Cahoone did not abandon drumming -- she added a voice. The drummer's instinct for rhythm, timing, and space persists in her songwriting: spare arrangements, deliberate pacing, room for silence. The drummer became a songwriter who writes like a drummer -- with awareness of what happens between the notes.

Ballard is a neighborhood in northwestern Seattle with deep **Scandinavian heritage**. Originally settled by the Duwamish people, it became a haven for **Norwegian, Swedish, Danish, and Finnish immigrants** drawn by fishing, logging, and milling. Ballard earned the nickname **"Shingle Capital of the World"** by the 1890s. The **National Nordic Museum** (founded 1980, purpose-built facility 2018) preserves this heritage. Each year the neighborhood celebrates **Syttende Mai** (Norwegian Constitution Day, May 17) and the **Ballard SeafoodFest**.

For the engine, Ballard's significance is musical: the **Tractor Tavern** (founded 1992, 5213 Ballard Ave NW, capacity ~400), the **Conor Byrne Pub**, and the **Sunset Tavern** form the **"Ballard trifecta"** -- three venues on or near Ballard Avenue that collectively anchor Seattle's Americana, folk, and roots-rock scene. The Tractor Tavern specifically became a **folkier alternative** to the hard-rock sounds Seattle was known for in the 1990s, leaning into acoustic sounds, alt-country, and Americana during the genre's mid-90s national emergence. **Brandi Carlile** played the Tractor in her early days. **Band of Horses** played there. **Sera Cahoone** considers the Tractor her home venue -- the club where she started her career as a solo artist.

Ballard represents a different geographic archetype from Capitol Hill (scene density / nightlife), West Seattle (domestic retreat), and Greenwood (working neighborhood). Ballard is the **roots-music neighborhood** -- the place where folk, Americana, and country-adjacent indie rock have a dedicated infrastructure of venues, audiences, and community. The Scandinavian heritage is not incidental: the cultural tradition of communal music-making, of songs that serve a community rather than a marketplace, persists in the neighborhood's musical identity.

## Biography: A Dynamite Salesman's Daughter Who Drummed Her Way to Song

### Origins: Littleton, Colorado, and Columbine

Sera Cahoone was born **August 4, 1975**. She grew up in **Littleton, Colorado**, in the foothills southwest of Denver. She is the daughter of **Bill Cahoone**, described as "a Rocky Mountain dynamite salesman," and **Judy Cahoone** (nee Ahrendts), a business executive. Cahoone has said she was "raised by a single mother." She has a sister, **Kal Cahoone**, and a brother, **Nathan Cahoone** -- both musicians.

Cahoone graduated from **Columbine High School** in Littleton. On **April 20, 1999**, two students killed twelve classmates and a teacher at Columbine High School in the deadliest school shooting in American history at the time. By then, Cahoone had already left Colorado -- she moved to Seattle in 1998. But the school was hers. She told Westword: "I remember one day I turned on TV and I saw my principal. It was just a surreal, crazy thing. I felt horrified of course, but also the fact that I was so far away and there's my whole hometown on TV just made it worse."

The Columbine connection is not a musical fact but a biographical one. Cahoone's Colorado identity -- Deer Creek Canyon, the foothills, the dynamite salesman father, the high school -- is the substrate of her songwriting. Her third album is titled *Deer Creek Canyon* (2012), named for the canyon in Littleton where her mother still lives, the place Cahoone would drive to when she was sad.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; Westword -- "Colorado's Sera Cahoone Found Musical Success (and Love) in Seattle"; Boulder Weekly -- "Homeward bound"; Grokipedia)

### Drumming: Anxiety, Blues Jams, and Ziggie's Saloon

Cahoone began playing drums at **age twelve**. She took up drumming specifically to **combat anxiety** -- a condition she has discussed openly across multiple interviews. She discovered she had a natural talent for the instrument. Her first stage performance was at **Ziggie's Saloon** in **North Denver** during a **blues open-mic night**, when she was still a teenager.

Growing up, Cahoone's musical influences spanned her mother's **old folk records**, the **heavy metal** she listened to in junior high, and later **singer-songwriters** like **Tracy Chapman**. The folk records would resurface decades later in her own songwriting. The metal taught her to hit hard. The drumming taught her to listen to space and silence -- the instinct that would define her production aesthetic when she eventually picked up a guitar and started writing songs.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; Westword; Tape Op; Americana Highways; Albumism)

### Seattle: Carissa's Wierd and the Crocodile Cafe (1998-2003)

In **1998**, Cahoone moved to Seattle when the **snowboarding shop** where she worked in Colorado opened a retail store in Washington state. She worked there for two years before quitting. She then worked in a **coffee shop** and focused on playing music.

Cahoone became the **drummer for Carissa's Wierd**, the Seattle indie rock band founded by Mat Brooke and Jenn Champion. The band's sound was slow, sad, string-laden, and deliberate -- "sadcore" in the parlance of the era. The band released three albums: *Ugly But Honest: 1996-1999* (2000), *Songs About Leaving* (2002, Sad Robot Records), and *I Before E* (2003, Sad Robot). Carissa's Wierd played their **final shows at the Crocodile Cafe** in Seattle in **November 2003**.

The Carissa's Wierd experience was formative for Cahoone in two ways. First, it placed her inside a band that valued **restraint, space, and emotional weight** over volume and speed -- values that would carry directly into her solo songwriting. Second, it connected her to a network of musicians who would become the backbone of Seattle's mid-2000s indie scene: **Ben Bridwell** (who formed Band of Horses), **Mat Brooke** (who formed Grand Archives), and **Jenn Champion** (who pursued a solo career). The Crocodile Cafe farewell was the end of one band and the beginning of several careers.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Carissa's Wierd; Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; City Arts Magazine; KEXP; No Depression; Last.fm)

### Band of Horses and Session Work (2003-2006)

After Carissa's Wierd disbanded, Cahoone played drums as a session musician. She recorded **selected drum tracks** on **Band of Horses'** debut album ***Everything All The Time*** (March 21, 2006, Sub Pop Records). The album became Band of Horses' breakthrough, eventually earning **RIAA Gold certification**. Cahoone received a **Gold Record** in 2019 for her contributions. She is credited alongside **Tim Meinig** for drumming on the album.

Cahoone also played drums for **Los Angeles singer-songwriter Patrick Park**, for **Panda and Angel**, and for **Betsy Olson**. This period (2003-2006) represents Cahoone's identity as a **working drummer** in the Seattle scene -- a side musician contributing to other people's records and tours, not yet a solo artist.

The session work is significant for the engine because it documents the **invisible labor** that sustains a music scene. Before Cahoone was a name on a marquee, she was a drummer in other people's bands. The Gold Record from Band of Horses arrived thirteen years after the sessions -- recognition of contributions made when Cahoone was still in the background.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; Wikipedia -- Everything All The Time; Band of Horses official site; Sub Pop Records; RIAA)

### Solo Debut and KEXP Discovery (2005-2006)

Cahoone's transition from drummer to singer-songwriter was **gradual and anxiety-ridden**. She had been writing songs privately but was terrified of performing them in public. She forced herself to play **open mic nights** to overcome her stage fright. She recorded a **self-titled debut album** in 2005, playing guitar and drums herself.

The breakthrough came when she sent a demo to **John Richards**, a host at **KEXP**. Richards played it on air. The unexpected exposure brought Cahoone to the attention of the independent music community. KEXP and **NPR** both praised the self-titled debut. The album was self-released but had **licensed distribution through Sub Pop Records**.

The KEXP discovery story is significant for the engine's documentation of how careers are made in Seattle: not through A&R executives or industry connections, but through a **local radio station** that pays attention to local musicians. KEXP's role in launching Cahoone parallels its documentation of Pedro the Lion, Damien Jurado, Rocky Votolato, and numerous other degree artists. The station functions as the engine's institutional memory and talent pipeline.

(Sources: Westword; KEXP; NPR; Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; Albumism)

### Sub Pop Years: Only as the Day Is Long and Deer Creek Canyon (2007-2012)

In **2007**, Sub Pop Records signed Sera Cahoone. Her Sub Pop debut, ***Only as the Day Is Long***, was released **March 18, 2008** (Sub Pop SP0759). The album established Cahoone's sonic identity as a solo artist: acoustic guitar, pedal steel, understated vocals, songs that move at the pace of thought rather than the pace of commerce. Cahoone performed an **NPR Tiny Desk Concert** on **July 10, 2008** -- five songs in twenty-one minutes, acoustic guitar and harmonica, what one reviewer called "hammock music."

Her second Sub Pop album, ***Deer Creek Canyon***, was released **September 25, 2012** (Sub Pop SP1005). The album was recorded at **Bear Creek Studios** in **Woodinville, Washington** over two weeks with Los Angeles producer **Thom Monahan** (Devendra Banhart, Vetiver). The title track is named for **Deer Creek Canyon in Littleton, Colorado** -- the canyon where Cahoone grew up, where her mother still lives, the place she would drive to when she was sad. Cahoone told interviewers: "I wrote a song called 'Deer Creek Canyon,' and it brought the whole record together. The name felt right because there was so much that I was missing of home in writing this record."

*Deer Creek Canyon* is the album where Cahoone's Colorado heritage and Seattle present converge. The gravitational pull of home -- Littleton, the foothills, the canyon -- persists across the distance. The album maps the tension between the place you're from and the place you live. This tension will become the engine's carry-forward theme for Cahoone: the Draw who never fully leaves her origin.

(Sources: Sub Pop Records; Wikipedia -- Sera Cahoone; NPR -- Tiny Desk Concert; KEXP; Seattle Met; PopMatters; The Arts Desk; Bear Creek Studios)

### Independence: From Where I Started and The Flora String Sessions (2017-2018)

After *Deer Creek Canyon*, Cahoone left Sub Pop (or the contract concluded) and released her fourth album independently. ***From Where I Started*** was released **March 24, 2017** on **Lady Muleskinner Records**. The album was recorded at **Flora Recording and Playback** in **Portland, Oregon** and co-produced by Cahoone and **John Morgan Askew** (Neko Case, Laura Gibson, Alela Diane).

*From Where I Started* features **Jason Kardong** on pedal steel, **Jeff Fielder** on guitar, **Annalisa Tornfelt** on fiddle, **Dave Depper** on bass, and **Rob Burger** on piano, Wurlitzer, pump organ, Farfisa, and mellotron. NPR gave the album a **First Listen** feature. Paste Magazine reviewed it positively. The album is Cahoone's most personal: she told interviewers that it tackled "personal realities in ways its predecessors generally didn't."

***The Flora String Sessions*** (April 20, 2018, Lady Muleskinner Records) reimagined selected songs from across Cahoone's career with new string arrangements by **Alex Guy** (Led to Sea). Guitar, violin, viola, and cello -- stripped back and rebuilt. The sessions document Cahoone's willingness to revisit her own catalog with fresh instrumentation, a practice that connects to Pedro the Lion's autobiographical revisionism (degree 47) and the engine's broader theme of artists reworking their pasts.

(Sources: NPR -- First Listen; Paste Magazine; WBGO; AllMusic; Mother Church Pew; Fretboard Journal; Discogs)

## Production Philosophy: The Drummer's Ear

### Space as Compositional Element

Sera Cahoone's songwriting is shaped by her years as a drummer. A drummer learns to listen to **what isn't playing** -- the space between beats, the silence that gives a groove its character. Cahoone's solo arrangements are **spare by design**: acoustic guitar, pedal steel, occasional fiddle or piano, and a voice that doesn't push. The instrumentation serves the song rather than filling the mix.

This aesthetic connects to the engine's folk-adjacent register. Damien Jurado (degree 45) uses space as whisper. Rocky Votolato (degree 46) uses space as vulnerability. Pedro the Lion (degree 47) uses space as narrative clarity. Sera Cahoone (degree 48) uses space as **rhythm** -- the songwriter who hears silence the way a drummer hears rests. The four-degree folk run shares a commitment to not overplaying, but each artist's relationship to space is different.

### Anxiety as Creative Engine

Cahoone has spoken openly about **anxiety** as the condition that drove her to music and simultaneously made performing terrifying. She took up drums at twelve to combat anxiety. She forced herself through open mics despite stage fright. She describes songwriting as a therapeutic outlet for processing "angst and awkwardness." The anxiety-to-art pipeline is documented across multiple interviews (Tape Op, Albumism, Americana Highways, Bluegrass Situation).

This pattern is distinct in the engine. Prior degree artists have documented ambition, faith, deconstruction, exile, persistence, and community. Cahoone documents **anxiety** as a creative engine -- the condition that both impedes and compels artistic expression. The drummer retreated into rhythm to manage anxiety. The songwriter emerged from that retreat, still anxious, but now with songs.

## The Forty-Ninth Acoustic Element: The Held Rest

### Acoustic Progression: Forty-Nine Elements

The forty-ninth acoustic element is **the held rest** -- the silence within a song that functions as a rhythmic event rather than an absence. When a drummer leaves a beat empty, the space becomes part of the groove. When a singer-songwriter leaves a measure silent between phrases, the silence carries the weight of what was just said and what is about to be said. The held rest is not absence -- it is presence without sound.

Sera Cahoone's songwriting is built on held rests. Her arrangements are spare not because she lacks musicians but because she hears silence as a compositional element. The space between guitar strums, the pause between vocal phrases, the instrumental passages where the voice steps back -- these are deliberate choices made by a songwriter who spent years as a drummer, listening to the spaces between beats. The held rest is the acoustic element where silence becomes rhythm.

The held rest extends the three-degree folk-vocal taxonomy (murmur-confessional-parable tone) into a four-degree folk-acoustic taxonomy: the murmur (degree 45, Jurado -- whispered intimacy), the confessional (degree 46, Votolato -- exposed declaration), the parable tone (degree 47, Bazan -- narrative delivery), and the held rest (degree 48, Cahoone -- rhythmic silence). Four modes of folk-rooted sound, four consecutive degrees, one continuous exploration of what happens when musicians stop filling space and start respecting it.

## The Forty-Ninth Artist-City Pattern: The Slow Bloom

### From Gravitational Center to Slow Bloom

The forty-ninth artist-city pattern is **the slow bloom** -- the artist whose career develops gradually over many years, without a single breakthrough moment, building an audience incrementally through steady work, word of mouth, and the patient accumulation of critical respect. The slow bloom does not explode onto the scene. The slow bloom grows.

Sera Cahoone is the slow bloom in its definitive form. She moved to Seattle in 1998. She drummed for other bands until 2006. She self-released a debut in 2005 that was discovered by KEXP. She signed to Sub Pop in 2007. She released two Sub Pop albums (2008, 2012). She went independent (2017, 2018). She transitioned to producing (2023). No single moment defined her career -- no viral hit, no magazine cover, no festival-slot explosion. The career is the sum of twenty-five years of showing up, writing songs, touring small venues, and building relationships with audiences who return year after year.

The slow bloom is distinct from the quiet draw (degree 9) -- which describes attraction to a city. The slow bloom describes **career pacing**: the opposite of the supernova (degree 37, Jimi Hendrix), which burns bright and fast. The slow bloom burns cool and long. It is the career structure most natural to the folk-adjacent register, where audiences are built one living room, one Tractor Tavern show, one KEXP session at a time.

## The Forty-Eighth Blue Note: The Drummer's Ghost

### Blue Note Deployment 48

The forty-eighth blue note deployment is **the drummer's ghost** -- the blue note that lives in the rhythmic muscle memory of a musician who has changed instruments but cannot leave their first instrument behind. When Cahoone writes a song on guitar, she hears drums. When she arranges a track with pedal steel and fiddle, she feels the pulse. The ghost is not audible as drumming -- there are no drums in many of her solo recordings. The ghost is audible in the **pacing**: the way her songs breathe in measures, the way her vocal phrasing lands on and off beats with a drummer's precision, the way her spare arrangements leave room for a rhythm section that isn't there.

The drummer's ghost follows the suspended resolve (degree 47), the crack (degree 46), and the held breath (degree 45) in the blue note genealogy. Four consecutive blue-note deployments in the folk-adjacent register: the silence where a note should be (the held breath), the voice that breaks under pressure (the crack), the resolution that never arrives (the suspended resolve), and the rhythm that persists in the absence of drums (the drummer's ghost). Four modes of **absence** across four degrees. The engine is mapping how folk music carries what isn't there.

Blue note deployment count: **48 across 49 degrees** (continuous from degree 0).

## The KEXP Connection

KEXP has been Sera Cahoone's most consistent institutional champion. The station's role in Cahoone's career is foundational: **John Richards** played her demo on KEXP, which led to her assembling a band and beginning to play live shows. KEXP premiered the new arrangement of "Deer Creek Canyon" in **April 2018**. KEXP premiered the benefit single "Crows" (a collaboration with **Tomo Nakayama**) in **October 2019**. KEXP presented the album release show for Cahoone with Margo Cilker at the Tractor Tavern in **December 2021**. KEXP featured Cahoone as a "Song of the Day" artist with "Only One" in **March 2017**.

The KEXP connection at degree 48 operates at the same level documented at degrees 45, 46, and 47. Four consecutive folk-adjacent degree artists, all championed by KEXP. The station is not merely documenting Seattle's folk scene -- it is **sustaining** it. KEXP's airplay, premieres, live sessions, and event presentations form the institutional infrastructure that allows artists like Cahoone to build careers without mainstream commercial visibility. KEXP is the folk-adjacent register's oxygen.
