## ANDREA ZOLLO'S VOICE

### Female Power in Post-Punk

Andrea Zollo's vocals define PGMG:

- **Commanding presence.** Zollo's voice is not ethereal or delicate -- it commands. Mid-range, strong, rhythmic, sometimes shouted. The vocals drive the songs forward with the same urgency as the rhythm section.
- **Post-punk lineage.** Zollo's vocal style descends from Siouxsie Sioux, Poly Styrene (X-Ray Spex), and Sleater-Kinney's Corin Tucker and Carrie Brownstein. The post-punk tradition of women who shout rather than sing, who command rather than seduce.
- **Gender dynamics.** PGMG was a mixed-gender band with a female vocalist -- a configuration that was less common in 2001-2007 Seattle indie rock than it should have been. Zollo's presence ensured that PGMG was never coded as "just another guy band." The vocals changed the energy.
- **Vocal urgency.** Zollo's delivery is urgent -- she sounds like she has something important to say and limited time to say it. This urgency matched the band's tight, driving arrangements. No self-indulgent solos. No wasted bars. Everything is heading somewhere.

(Sources: Andrea Zollo vocal style; post-punk female vocal lineage; gender in Seattle indie rock; vocal urgency)

## THE SOUND -- DRIVING POST-PUNK INDIE

### Energy Through Rhythm

PGMG's sound is defined by forward momentum:

- **Rhythm-forward.** The drums and bass drive PGMG more than the guitars. The rhythm section is locked, tight, insistent -- every beat pushes the song forward. This is post-punk's gift to indie rock: rhythm as engine.
- **Angular guitars.** The guitar parts are angular and sparse -- less wall-of-sound, more cutting geometry. Influenced by post-punk (Wire, Gang of Four) more than by grunge or shoegaze.
- **Keyboard textures.** Synthesizers and keyboards add atmospheric layers without slowing the momentum. The electronics serve the drive.
- **Tight arrangements.** PGMG songs are economical. Three to four minutes. No fat. Every section serves the song's forward motion.
- **Nick DeWitt connection.** Guitarist Nick DeWitt came from the Murder City Devils, another high-energy Seattle band (who were at various degrees in the engine). The Murder City Devils connection brings punk energy directly into the PGMG framework.

(Sources: PGMG recordings; post-punk influence; rhythm-forward composition; Murder City Devils connection; angular guitar)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Pretty Girls Make Graves

- **Tempo.** Fast. 130-170 BPM. Post-punk tempos. The music drives forward relentlessly.
- **Guitar.** Angular, sparse, rhythmic. Post-punk geometry. Less distortion than noise rock, more edge than pop.
- **Bass.** Driving, melodic, locked with drums. The bass and drums are the engine.
- **Drums.** Tight, insistent, rhythm-forward. Every beat pushes. No fills for display.
- **Vocals.** Andrea Zollo: mid-range, strong, rhythmic, urgent. Commanding, not ethereal. Sometimes shouted.
- **Keyboards.** Atmospheric layers. Serve the momentum. Electronic textures without slowing down.
- **Production.** Clear, punchy, rhythm-forward. Matador-quality production: professional but not overpolished.
- **Song length.** Short to medium. 2:30-4:00. Economical. No wasted time.
