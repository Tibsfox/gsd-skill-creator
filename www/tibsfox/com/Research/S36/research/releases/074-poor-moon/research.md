## STRUCTURAL EVENT: The Side Project -- Fleet Foxes Connection Returns

Degree 74 introduces **Poor Moon** -- a band whose significance is inseparable from its relationship to **Fleet Foxes** (degree 53). Poor Moon is a **side project**: a band formed by members of a bigger act, creating music outside their primary group's identity. **Christian Wargo** and **Casey Wescott** are both members of Fleet Foxes -- the band that gave the engine its communal harmony at degree 53, one of the most structurally significant entries in the entire series.

The side project is a fundamentally different kind of artistic entity from a solo artist or an independent band. A side project exists in the shadow of its parent act. It is defined, in part, by what it is not -- it is not Fleet Foxes. The musicians are the same, but the aesthetic is different. Where Fleet Foxes built soaring multi-part vocal harmonies that dissolved individual voices into collective sound, Poor Moon creates **dreamy, reverb-heavy indie folk/rock** -- spacier, more psychedelic, more atmospheric. The communal harmony (53) is replaced by the **reverb wash**. The choir becomes the echo.

This is the engine's **second connection to degree 53** (Fleet Foxes). The first was through the genre and aesthetic influence that Fleet Foxes exerts across the Seattle indie folk landscape. Degree 74 makes the connection literal -- same musicians, different band. This is the strongest inter-degree connection in the engine: not influence, not genre overlap, not label shared, but **shared personnel**. Christian Wargo and Casey Wescott are Fleet Foxes. They are also Poor Moon. The same human voices producing different music under different names.

The side project pattern raises questions about artistic identity. When Christian Wargo sings in Poor Moon, is it a Fleet Foxes voice making different music? Or is it a different voice altogether -- the same throat, the same lungs, but a different artistic intention? The side project suggests that an artist is not a fixed entity but a **collection of possibilities**, and that different band configurations unlock different musical registers. Wargo in Fleet Foxes is part of a choir. Wargo in Poor Moon is part of a duo. Same voice, different architecture.

## STRUCTURAL EVENT: One-Album Band -- Second Defunct/Ended Artist

Poor Moon released one self-titled album: *Poor Moon* (2012, Sub Pop). The band is no longer active. This makes Poor Moon the engine's **second defunct/ended artist** after **Pickwick** (degree 62).

The one-album band is a specific category of musical existence. Unlike artists who sustain careers across decades (Neko Case, Phil Elverum, Fleet Foxes themselves), the one-album band burns briefly and completely. The entire artistic statement is contained in a single release. There is no evolution, no second act, no mature period. The one-album band is a **snapshot** -- a moment frozen in time, a particular configuration of musicians and ideas that existed long enough to produce one record and then dispersed.

Poor Moon's brevity is partly explained by its side-project nature. Side projects often have shorter lifespans than primary acts because the musicians' primary commitment is elsewhere. When Fleet Foxes resumed activity, Poor Moon's reason for existing -- the creative space between Fleet Foxes albums -- closed. The side project is often a **temporal artifact**: it exists in the gaps between a bigger act's work, filling the silence between albums with different music.

The comparison with Pickwick (62) is instructive. Pickwick was an independent band that ended on its own terms -- not a side project but a primary act that ran its course. Poor Moon ended because its members returned to their primary act. Different reasons for ending. Same structural result: a finite artistic statement, a closed catalog, a band that exists only in past tense.

Degree 74 classifies Poor Moon as **Indie Folk/Rock** -- a hybrid genre that acknowledges both the folk elements (acoustic instrumentation, melodic sensibility, pastoral atmosphere) and the rock elements (electric guitar, reverb, rhythmic drive, psychedelic influence). Indie Folk/Rock as a genre occupies the space between the acoustic intimacy of indie folk (Horse Feathers, 70; Sam Beam, 68) and the electric energy of indie rock (Death Cab for Cutie, 58). But it is not purely rock -- the melodies are folk-derived, the vocals are soft and unhurried, the atmosphere is pastoral rather than urban. The hybrid genre reflects the hybrid identity of the band: Fleet Foxes members making music that is adjacent to but distinct from Fleet Foxes' pure indie folk.

The genre progression from degree 73: the engine moves from Lake's genre at degree 73 to Indie Folk/Rock at 74. But the /Rock suffix marks a return of electric instrumentation and rock energy that has been absent since the atmospheric lo-fi of Elverum (71) and the art pop of Marela (72).

The hybrid classification also reflects a broader truth about Seattle's music scene: genre boundaries in indie music are porous. The same musicians play in folk bands and rock bands. The same instruments appear in different configurations. Poor Moon exists precisely at the boundary where folk and rock overlap -- and the hybrid classification honors that liminality.

## Biography: Poor Moon and the Reverb Dream

### The Fleet Foxes Connection

**Poor Moon** emerged from the **Fleet Foxes** orbit. The band's core members -- **Christian Wargo** and **Casey Wescott** -- are both members of Fleet Foxes, the Seattle indie folk band that redefined the genre's possibilities with their 2008 self-titled debut and 2011's *Helplessness Blues*. Fleet Foxes at degree 53 gave the engine its **communal harmony** -- the acoustic element of many voices blending into one, individual identity dissolved into collective sound.

Poor Moon is what happens when Fleet Foxes members make music without the communal architecture. Where Fleet Foxes is a choir, Poor Moon is a **duo**. Where Fleet Foxes builds soaring multi-part harmonies, Poor Moon builds **reverb-drenched atmospheres**. Where Fleet Foxes is baroque and precise, Poor Moon is **psychedelic and loose**. The side project strips away the communal harmony and replaces it with space -- reverb, echo, the sound of instruments and voices dissolving into atmospheric haze.

**Christian Wargo** also played with **Crystal Skulls**, another Seattle band, further embedding him in the city's interconnected indie ecosystem. Wargo is a node in Seattle's musical network -- a musician who connects multiple bands, multiple scenes, multiple degrees of the engine. His presence in Poor Moon links degree 74 to degree 53 (Fleet Foxes) through the most direct connection possible: the same person.

**Casey Wescott**, Fleet Foxes' multi-instrumentalist (keyboards, mandolin, vocals), brings the arrangement sensibility of Fleet Foxes into Poor Moon's sparser setting. Where Wescott's contributions to Fleet Foxes are woven into a dense harmonic tapestry, his work in Poor Moon is more exposed -- individual instrumental voices audible in the reverb wash rather than subsumed into the collective.

### The Sound: Dreamy Reverb Folk/Rock

Poor Moon's music is characterized by its **dreamy, reverb-heavy quality**. The reverb is not decoration -- it is the architecture. Every instrument and voice passes through reverb, creating a sense of vast space, of sound dissolving into atmosphere. The effect is psychedelic without being aggressive -- a gentle dissolution of sonic boundaries rather than the hallucinatory intensity of classic psychedelic rock.

The reverb wash (the 75th acoustic element) is distinct from Fleet Foxes' communal harmony. The communal harmony is achieved through multiple voices in precise harmonic alignment -- a compositional technique. The reverb wash is achieved through production technique -- electronic processing that creates the illusion of space. Composition versus production. Harmony versus atmosphere. The choir versus the echo chamber.

The folk/rock hybrid is audible in the instrumentation: acoustic guitars provide the folk foundation, electric guitars provide the rock texture, and the reverb processing transforms both into something spacier than either genre alone. The vocals are soft, melodic, and often layered -- but layered through reverb and studio production rather than through the live vocal stacking of Fleet Foxes. Poor Moon's layers are technological. Fleet Foxes' layers are human.

### Poor Moon (2012, Sub Pop): The Only Album

*Poor Moon* (2012) is the band's sole release -- a self-titled album on Sub Pop Records. The album captures the reverb dream aesthetic in twelve tracks: mid-tempo folk/rock songs wrapped in atmospheric production, melodic vocals drifting through reverberant space, guitars oscillating between acoustic intimacy and electric shimmer.

The album's existence on Sub Pop is significant. Sub Pop did not need another band -- its roster was already deep with Seattle talent. But Poor Moon's Fleet Foxes connection and its distinctive reverb aesthetic earned a place on the label. The album is a document of a particular moment: the gap between Fleet Foxes' *Helplessness Blues* (2011) and their eventual return with *Crack-Up* (2017). Poor Moon filled that gap with different music from the same musicians.

As a one-album catalog, *Poor Moon* has a completeness that multi-album artists lack. There is no "early period" or "mature period." There is only the album -- the entire artistic statement in one forty-minute release. The one-album band's catalog is a closed circle: everything the band had to say, said once, said completely.

## The Seventy-Fifth Acoustic Element: The Reverb Wash

### Acoustic Progression: Seventy-Five Elements

The seventy-fifth acoustic element is **the reverb wash** -- the dreamy, reverb-heavy atmospheric quality where instruments and voices dissolve into spacious, psychedelic haze. In Poor Moon's music, reverb is not an effect applied to the sound but the **environment** the sound inhabits. Every element passes through reverb processing that creates the illusion of vast space -- a sonic room far larger than the physical space of the recording.

The reverb wash is distinct from the layered loop (degree 72, Briana Marela). The layered loop multiplies a single voice through technology -- the voice layered upon itself, building density. The reverb wash dissolves sounds into atmosphere -- the voice and instruments spreading outward, losing their sharp edges, becoming part of a larger sonic environment. The layered loop **accumulates**. The reverb wash **disperses**. Both use technology as instrument. Different relationships to space: the layered loop fills space with density. The reverb wash creates the illusion of infinite space.

The reverb wash is distinct from the communal harmony (degree 53, Fleet Foxes). This distinction is particularly meaningful given that Poor Moon's members are Fleet Foxes members. The communal harmony is built by many voices singing together in precise harmonic alignment -- a human phenomenon, achieved through composition and vocal technique. The reverb wash is built by electronic processing -- reverb pedals, studio effects, the technology that makes a small room sound like a cathedral. The communal harmony is the sound of togetherness. The reverb wash is the sound of space.

The reverb wash is distinct from the hush (degree 68, Sam Beam). The hush is silence and quietness -- the confessional whisper, the held breath before the phrase. The reverb wash is not silent -- it is full, atmospheric, enveloping. But it shares the hush's intimacy: the reverb wash is intimate vastness, the sense that a quiet voice is singing in an enormous room. The hush is intimate through proximity. The reverb wash is intimate through dissolution -- the voice dissolving into the space around it.

The reverb wash fills space through dispersion rather than accumulation or projection. The voice does not get louder (projection) or multiply (accumulation). It spreads -- echoing outward, trailing into reverberant tails, becoming part of the room's acoustic character. The result is dreamy and psychedelic: the music sounds like it is coming from everywhere and nowhere, present but not quite here.

## The Seventy-Fifth Artist-City Pattern: The Side Project

### Band Members from a Bigger Act

The seventy-fifth artist-city pattern is **the side project** -- band members from a bigger act creating music under a different name, in a different aesthetic register, while maintaining their primary group identity. Poor Moon is a Fleet Foxes side project: Christian Wargo and Casey Wescott are Fleet Foxes members who formed Poor Moon to explore music that was spacier, more psychedelic, and more reverb-heavy than Fleet Foxes' baroque folk harmonies.

The side project is distinct from the solo artist. A solo artist (Beam, 68; Elverum, 71; Marela, 72) is a single person creating under their own name or a chosen pseudonym. The side project is a **group** -- multiple musicians collaborating under a band name that is distinct from their primary act. The solo artist is an individual exploring personal vision. The side project is a collaboration exploring shared alternative vision.

The side project is distinct from the supergroup. A supergroup (not yet in the engine) brings together members of different famous bands to form a new entity. The side project draws members from a **single** primary band -- the same people, a different configuration. Poor Moon is not a supergroup because both Wargo and Wescott come from Fleet Foxes. It is a side project because it is a subset of one band exploring a different sound.

The side project is distinct from the departed member (degree 67, Chris Walla). The departed member leaves the primary band permanently -- departure as severance. The side project maintains dual membership -- the musicians are simultaneously in both bands. Wargo and Wescott did not leave Fleet Foxes to form Poor Moon. They formed Poor Moon while remaining Fleet Foxes members. The departed member exits. The side project **straddles**.

The side project pattern reflects the interconnected nature of Seattle's music scene. Musicians in Seattle do not belong to one band in isolation -- they circulate through multiple projects, multiple collaborations, multiple configurations. Christian Wargo alone connects Fleet Foxes, Poor Moon, and Crystal Skulls. The side project is the structural expression of this circulation: musicians flowing between projects, carrying influences and relationships across band boundaries.

## The Seventy-Fourth Blue Note: The Reverb Trail

The seventy-fourth blue note deployment is **the reverb trail** -- the blue note that appears in the decay of reverberant sound, the moment where the direct signal and its reverberant reflections overlap and interfere, creating a shimmer that is neither the original note nor pure echo but something in between.

In Poor Moon's music, reverb is applied generously to vocals and instruments. When a note is sung or played, the direct sound arrives first, followed by a cascade of reflections -- the reverb tail. In the space where direct sound and reverberant reflections coexist, the pitches are almost but not quite identical: the reflections carry micro-variations in pitch, timing, and phase that create a **shimmer** -- a blue note of dissolution, the original pitch blurring at its edges.

The reverb trail is distinct from the echo chamber (degree 72, Briana Marela). The echo chamber is the blue note between a voice and its looped duplicate -- a temporal blue note, the same voice at two different moments. The reverb trail is the blue note between a sound and its **spatial reflections** -- the same sound arriving from multiple directions at slightly different times. The echo chamber is temporal (two moments). The reverb trail is spatial (multiple paths).

The reverb trail is distinct from the thread (degree 70, Horse Feathers). The thread is the blue note between two different instruments approaching the same pitch. The reverb trail is the blue note between one sound and its own reflections -- the same source heard multiple times through different acoustic paths. The thread is between two sources. The reverb trail is between one source and its spatial copies.

The reverb trail as blue note: in Poor Moon's production, the reverb creates a constant shimmer -- a haze of almost-consonant reflections that blur the boundaries between notes, between instruments, between the voice and the room. This shimmer is the reverb trail: the blue note of spatial dissolution, the pitch that was clear when it left the source becoming ambiguous as it bounces through reverberant space.
