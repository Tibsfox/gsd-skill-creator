Green River

E = 7 Proto-Grunge / Punk Sub Pop

The Origin Band — First Sub Pop Release — Deep Six (1986)

Green River is the most important band you've never heard twice. Their split in 1987 created the two most significant bands of the Seattle grunge era: Mark Arm and Steve Turner formed Mudhoney; Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament joined Andrew Wood (Malfunkshun) in Mother Love Bone. That chain leads directly to Pearl Jam. "Come On Down" was Sub Pop's first release. Deep Six (1986) was the scene's founding document. Capitol Hill. 1984–1987.

The Personnel

Green River contained, at the same time, the future founders of two of the most important bands in American rock:

Mark Arm + Steve Turner → Mudhoney

The punk faction. Sub Pop's flagship band. The purest expression of the grunge/punk aesthetic. Bruce Pavitt and Jonathan Poneman at Sub Pop said Mudhoney most completely embodied what they were trying to do. Superfuzz Bigmuff (1988). Sub Pop's identity.

Stone Gossard + Jeff Ament → Mother Love Bone → Pearl Jam

The rock-architecture faction. Joined Andrew Wood. Mother Love Bone. Apple (1990). Wood's death. Temple of the Dog. Eddie Vedder recruited. Pearl Jam. Ten (August 1991). The chain runs from Green River's 1987 split to Pearl Jam's multi-platinum debut.

The Deep Six Compilation (1986)

Deep Six (1986, C/Z Records) is the founding document of Seattle grunge: six bands (Green River, Melvins, Soundgarden, Skin Yard, U-Men, Malfunkshun), recorded by Jack Endino at Reciprocal Recording, released before Sub Pop had fully formalized its commercial operation. Green River and Malfunkshun are both on Deep Six — both are in the engine at consecutive degrees (294 and 295) because they are consecutive steps in the same story.

Hearing Deep Six end to end is to hear, in one listening session, the people who would become Pearl Jam, Mudhoney, Soundgarden, and Temple of the Dog all in early form. Green River is in it: Stone Gossard's guitar, Jeff Ament's bass, Mark Arm's voice, Steve Turner's guitar. The origin, documented.

"Green River's split in 1987 is not a failure. It is the mechanism through which the scene's most important creative energies found their proper forms."

The Creative Fault Line

Green River dissolved because of genuine creative disagreement: Mark Arm and Steve Turner wanted to stay closer to punk — fast, raw, anti-commercial, ideologically committed to underground aesthetics. Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament had broader rock ambitions — bigger songs, wider audiences, larger sonic palettes. Both positions are legitimate. The creative fault line between them produced two bands, each more fully realized in its own direction than either faction could have been within a shared band.
