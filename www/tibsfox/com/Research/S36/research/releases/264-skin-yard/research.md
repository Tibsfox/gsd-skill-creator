## JACK ENDINO -- THE MOST IMPORTANT PRODUCER IN SEATTLE MUSIC HISTORY

### A Detailed Account

Jack Endino is the technical and aesthetic architect of the Seattle sound. No producer in the history of Pacific Northwest rock has had comparable impact. The documentation begins with Endino because the documentation must begin with Endino:

- **Jack Endino: biography.** Born John Endino, he has gone by Jack Endino professionally. He grew up in and around the Seattle area and became active in the Seattle music scene in the mid-1980s as a guitarist (Skin Yard) and producer. His approach to production reflects his background as a musician: he records bands in the way he would want to be recorded, prioritizing the live energy of the performance over studio manipulation.

- **Reciprocal Recordings.** Endino's primary studio base was Reciprocal Recording in Seattle. Reciprocal was a modest facility by major-label standards -- it was not equipped with the expensive gear of major recording studios -- but it was the right studio for the Seattle sound. Endino's production philosophy at Reciprocal was to capture what the band sounded like in a room: real drums, real amplifiers, real performances. The recording was documentary, not transformative. This is the sound that Nirvana, Mudhoney, Soundgarden, and hundreds of other bands brought to him.

- **Nirvana's *Bleach* (1989).** The most historically significant single recording session of Endino's career. Nirvana's debut album, recorded at Reciprocal Recording for approximately $606.17. The budget is legendary: the entire album cost less than $1,000 to record. Endino captured a band at the beginning of their transformation -- Kurt Cobain's songwriting was already evident, the heaviness was clear, the potential was there. *Bleach* is not the album that made Nirvana famous; *Nevermind* did that. But *Bleach* is the record that documented what they were before the transformation. Endino was the witness.

- **Mudhoney's *Superfuzz Bigmuff* (1988).** The EP that defined the Seattle sound before "grunge" was a word used commercially. The two tracks "Touch Me I'm Sick" and "Sweet Young Thing Ain't Sweet No More" are among the most influential recordings in the Seattle underground. Endino's production on this record is the sonic definition of early Seattle grunge: heavy, slightly garagey, driven by the guitar and Mark Arm's voice. The production does not dress the band up; it documents them. *Superfuzz Bigmuff* is the production reference document for the entire early grunge genre.

- **Soundgarden.** Endino worked with Soundgarden early in their career, producing some of their earliest Sub Pop recordings. The Soundgarden he recorded is the underground version -- before A&M Records, before *Badmotorfinger* and *Superunknown*, before the MTV presence. The early Soundgarden recordings are heavy and somewhat rawer than the major-label work; Endino's production captures this.

- **The broader catalog.** Endino's production credits extend far beyond these landmark recordings. He has produced records for TAD, Screaming Trees (co-production on *Sweet Oblivion*, 1992, which connects directly to degree 261), the Fastbacks, 7 Year Bitch, L7, Seaweed, Hammerbox, and hundreds of additional Pacific Northwest bands across four decades of active production work. His catalog is one of the most comprehensive documents of the Pacific Northwest underground that exists.

- **The Endino philosophy.** Endino has articulated his production philosophy consistently across multiple interviews and writings: he is a "minimum interference" producer. He does not impose sounds; he documents them. The goal is to make the recording sound like the band in the room. This approach is not passive -- it requires precise microphone placement, understanding of how the room's acoustics interact with the instruments, and the judgment to know when a performance is worth keeping. But the aesthetic goal is transparency rather than transformation.

- **The "Seattle sound" as Endino artifact.** Much of what is recognizable as the "Seattle sound" -- the specific combination of heavy guitar, prominent drums, and relatively dry (not heavily reverbed) vocals -- is, in part, an Endino production signature. He recorded so many of the bands that defined this sound that his production choices became the genre's sonic reference point. When people describe what "grunge" sounds like, they are often describing what Endino's recordings sound like.

- **Continued activity.** Endino has remained active as a producer through the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s, continuing to record Pacific Northwest bands and international artists who seek his specific approach. The production philosophy has not changed substantially. He is still recording bands in the way he would want to be recorded.

(Sources: Jack Endino biography; Reciprocal Recording; Nirvana Bleach 1989 production; $606.17 recording budget; Mudhoney Superfuzz Bigmuff 1988; "Touch Me I'm Sick"; early Soundgarden productions; TAD production; Sweet Oblivion co-production; Fastbacks, 7 Year Bitch, L7, Seaweed, Hammerbox production credits; minimum interference philosophy; "Seattle sound" as Endino artifact; continued 21st-century activity)

## MATT CAMERON -- BEFORE SOUNDGARDEN AND PEARL JAM

### Skin Yard as the Cameron Origin Document

Matt Cameron's presence in Skin Yard makes the band a document of one of the most significant drummer careers in Seattle rock history:

- **Matt Cameron biography.** Born November 28, 1962, in San Diego, California. Cameron moved to Seattle and became active in the music scene in the early-to-mid 1980s. He joined Skin Yard as drummer in the band's early period, providing the rhythmic foundation for their heavy proto-grunge sound.

- **Cameron's drumming in Skin Yard.** Cameron's playing in Skin Yard is already distinctive. He is a technically proficient drummer with a strong sense of dynamics -- he can play with full intensity when the song requires it and pull back when restraint serves the music better. In the heavy context of Skin Yard's proto-metal, this dynamism stands out: he is not just a timekeeper but a contributor to the compositional arc of each song.

- **Soundgarden (1986-2017, reunion-era).** Cameron joined Soundgarden in 1986, replacing original drummer Scott Sundquist. His tenure with Soundgarden produced the full run of their most important records: *Ultramega OK* (1988), *Louder Than Love* (1989), *Badmotorfinger* (1991), *Superunknown* (1994), *Down on the Upside* (1996). Cameron's drumming on *Superunknown* and *Badmotorfinger* is among the most technically accomplished and musically significant rock drumming of the 1990s. "Black Hole Sun," "Spoonman," "Fell on Black Days," "My Wave" -- all of these are Cameron performances.

- **Pearl Jam (1998-present).** After Soundgarden's original dissolution in 1997 (and before their 2010 reunion), Cameron joined Pearl Jam as drummer, replacing Jack Irons. Cameron has played on every Pearl Jam album from *Binaural* (2000) through the present. He has been with Pearl Jam for more than twice as long as he was with Soundgarden. This represents one of the longest tenures with a major band in Seattle rock history.

- **The Skin Yard origin.** When evaluating Cameron's drumming career, Skin Yard is the origin. The drummer who would execute the complex rhythmic patterns of *Badmotorfinger* and the dynamic nuance of *Superunknown* learned his craft and developed his approach in a band that most people who know Cameron's later work have never heard. Skin Yard is the lab; Soundgarden is the result.

(Sources: Matt Cameron biography; San Diego origin; Skin Yard membership; Soundgarden 1986-1997 2010-2017; Ultramega OK 1988; Louder Than Love 1989; Badmotorfinger 1991; Superunknown 1994; Down on the Upside 1996; Pearl Jam 1998-present; Binaural 2000; Skin Yard as Cameron origin document)

## SKIN YARD -- THE BAND AND SOUND

### Proto-Metal, Proto-Grunge, E=7

Skin Yard's sonic identity and personnel:

- **Jack Endino (guitar).** The band's technical anchor and founder. Endino's guitar work in Skin Yard is heavier than his later role as producer might suggest. He is a genuinely skilled guitarist with a metal-adjacent approach -- thick riffs, heavy distortion, the kind of playing that bridges the metal tradition (Black Sabbath, early heavy rock) with the punk directness of the 1980s underground.
- **Ben McMillan (vocals).** The primary vocalist. McMillan's voice is intense and somewhat operatic by the standards of the underground -- he has more range than most of his contemporaries in the Seattle scene. After Skin Yard, McMillan formed Gruntruck (another proto-grunge band) and later Selffish.
- **Matt Cameron (drums, early period).** See above. Cameron is present in the band's early recordings and establishment of their sound.
- **Daniel House (bass).** House's bass work is thick and deliberate -- he plays with the methodical heaviness that characterizes the metal tradition. The bass in Skin Yard is not flashy; it is foundational.
- **Lineup evolution.** Skin Yard had several lineup changes across their seven-year run (1985-1992), including different drummers after Cameron's departure. The consistent elements were Endino's guitar, House's bass, and the overall heavy approach.

- **The sound.** Skin Yard's music is heavy and dark -- riff-based, drawn from the metal tradition (Black Sabbath is the obvious reference), but faster and more direct than most metal of the period. This is the proto-grunge sound: metal's heaviness with punk's velocity. The songs are not long; they do not have extended solos; they get in and get out. This is metal stripped of its excess and played at punk speed. This is what grunge would sound like when it arrived commercially a few years later.

- **Proto-grunge vs. grunge.** The distinction between proto-grunge and grunge is largely commercial rather than sonic. Skin Yard (1985-1992) sounded like what people would call grunge; they existed before grunge was a commercial category. When Nirvana broke commercially in 1991-1992, the music world retroactively labeled the Seattle sound that had been developing since the mid-1980s as "grunge." Skin Yard was doing this sound when the label did not exist. They are the before-the-name version of the genre they helped create.

(Sources: Skin Yard personnel; Jack Endino guitar; Ben McMillan vocals; Matt Cameron drums; Daniel House bass; Black Sabbath reference; punk velocity; proto-grunge vs. grunge commercial distinction; Gruntruck post-Skin Yard)

## C/Z RECORDS AND THE DEEP SIX COMPILATION

### The Root Document

Skin Yard on C/Z Records is the connection to the grunge origin document:

- **The *Deep Six* compilation (1986, C/Z Records).** Released by C/Z Records in 1986, *Deep Six* is the earliest document of the Seattle underground that would become grunge. The compilation features: Melvins, U-Men, Soundgarden, Green River, Skin Yard, and Malfunkshun. Each of these bands represents a thread of the Seattle underground: the Melvins brought Southern sludge to Washington; the U-Men brought post-punk darkness; Soundgarden brought heavy metal sophistication; Green River (which split into Mudhoney and the Pearl Jam precursors) brought the most direct grunge energy; Skin Yard brought proto-grunge heaviness; Malfunkshun brought Andrew Wood's theatricality (Wood would die of a heroin overdose in 1990; his memory is central to the Mother Love Bone and early Pearl Jam story).
- **Skin Yard's position on Deep Six.** Skin Yard on *Deep Six* is the band at their earliest commercial document -- a year after formation. The record preserves Skin Yard in the same moment as Soundgarden, Green River, and the others. This is the photograph of the entire grunge lineage before it was a lineage.
- **C/Z's role revisited.** C/Z Records (degrees 263 and 264) was operating at the origin of grunge and continued to support the Pacific Northwest underground throughout the late 1980s and 1990s. The label's importance is underappreciated relative to Sub Pop, but the *Deep Six* compilation alone makes C/Z one of the historically essential labels in Seattle music.

(Sources: Deep Six compilation 1986 C/Z Records; compilation roster: Melvins, U-Men, Soundgarden, Green River, Skin Yard, Malfunkshun; Andrew Wood; Green River to Mudhoney and Pearl Jam precursor split; Malfunkshun and Andrew Wood death 1990; C/Z historical importance)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Skin Yard Catalog

- ***Skin Yard* (1987, C/Z Records).** The debut album. Heavy, dark, and direct. Jack Endino's guitar and the proto-metal aesthetic are fully present. Matt Cameron drums here.
- ***Hallowed Ground* (1988, Toxic Shock/C/Z).** Second album. More developed songwriting. The production is Endino-adjacent -- he knows exactly how he wants this to sound.
- ***1000 Smiling Knuckles* (1991, Cruz Records).** Later-period Skin Yard. The sound becomes somewhat more varied; the songwriting matures. The band continues into the commercial grunge moment they helped create.
- ***Inside the Eye* (1993, Cruz Records).** Released after the band's dissolution period. Final documents of the Skin Yard sound.
- ***Deep Six* (1986, C/Z Records).** The compilation context that precedes the debut album. The first commercial document of Skin Yard's music.

(Sources: Skin Yard full discography; C/Z Records; Toxic Shock; Cruz Records; album-by-album analysis; Deep Six compilation context)

## LEGACY -- THE PROTO-GRUNGE FOUNDATION

### What Skin Yard Established

- **The sound before the name.** Skin Yard was doing grunge before grunge was called grunge. This is the foundational claim: proto-grunge means the music existed before the commercial category. When *Nevermind* made grunge a household word in 1991, the ecosystem it was named after had been active since 1985. Skin Yard is 1985.
- **Jack Endino's dual role.** Endino's simultaneous position as musician (Skin Yard guitarist) and producer (the most important producer in Seattle music history) is unusual and historically significant. Most great producers are not also active musicians in the scene they are documenting; most active musicians do not become the definitive documentarians of their era. Endino did both.
- **The Endino-Cameron connection.** Two of the most significant figures in Seattle rock history were in Skin Yard at the same time. This kind of density of future significance in a single early band is rare. The fact that Skin Yard itself never achieved commercial success proportionate to the later achievements of its members is entirely consistent with the pattern the engine documents across Seattle's underground: the formative bands are often invisible to those who discover the later work.
- **The deep roots.** Skin Yard's connection to the *Deep Six* compilation, to C/Z Records, to Jack Endino's production career, to Matt Cameron's drumming trajectory -- these connections form the deep roots of the grunge tree. The commercial branches (Nirvana's platinum, Soundgarden's arena tours, Pearl Jam's continued presence) grow from these roots.
