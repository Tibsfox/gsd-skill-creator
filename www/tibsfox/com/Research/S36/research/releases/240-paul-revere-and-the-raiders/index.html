The Costume, The Organ, The Voice

Paul Revere and the Raiders wore Revolutionary War soldier costumes on stage and television. Tricorn hats, knee breeches, ruffled shirts. In 1965, when most rock bands wore matching suits or street clothes, this was an audacious visual choice. It worked: the Raiders became the house band on Dick Clark’s daily TV show Where the Action Is, appearing on national television nearly every day for two years. The costume made them instantly recognizable across a medium (television) that rewards visual distinction.

The music, underneath the costumes, is genuinely good. Paul Revere Dick’s organ is driving, percussive, garage-forward. Mark Lindsay’s vocals are one of the 1960s’ more distinctive voices: high-register, melodically agile, capable of punk-adjacent aggression and radio-friendly sweetness in the same song.

“Kicks just keep getting harder to find.” — The best anti-drug lyric in American rock. Direct, confrontational, melodically irresistible. #4 Billboard Hot 100, 1966.”

Boise to Portland to Seattle

Paul Revere Dick formed the Raiders in Boise, Idaho in 1960. He moved the band’s base of operations to Portland and the Pacific Northwest, establishing regional roots before the Columbia Records deal made them national figures. The Raiders are identified as a Pacific Northwest act: part of the Tacoma-Portland-Seattle rock circuit of the 1960s that included the Kingsmen (“Louie Louie”), the Wailers, and the early Ventures. The Pacific Northwest had a distinct regional rock sound in the early 1960s, and the Raiders were among its most commercially visible representatives.
