Biography & The Central District’s Forgotten Pianist

Patti Bown was born on June 26, 1931, in Seattle, Washington, and raised in the Central District — the seventh consecutive degree connected to that neighborhood. Bown studied music at Seattle University, adding a seventh educational pipeline to the engine’s taxonomy. She moved to New York City in the mid-1950s and became one of the first Black women to lead a jazz piano trio in the hard bop era.

Her debut album, Patti Bown Plays Big Piano (1958, Columbia Records), placed her on the most prestigious jazz label of the era — home to Miles Davis, Dave Brubeck, and Thelonious Monk. She worked with Quincy Jones (0°), Oliver Nelson, and Duke Pearson. She performed session work for Motown. She was reviewed favorably in DownBeat. And then she was forgotten.

Bown died on March 21, 2008, in Philadelphia, at age 76. No NEA Jazz Master designation. No Grammy nominations. Her Columbia debut is out of print. The city that produced her had largely forgotten her. She is degree 6 because the engine documents what the industry dropped.

Sources: Columbia Records discography; AllMusic; DownBeat reviews 1958–1965; Paul de Barros, Jackson Street After Hours (1993); Seattle Times archives; jazz biography databases

Gender Dimension: Instrumentalist vs. Vocalist

3° Ernestine Anderson Vocalist. The female jazz vocalist was a recognized position — Holiday, Fitzgerald, Vaughan. The barrier was real but permeable. Anderson was heard, recorded, celebrated.
6° Patti Bown Instrumentalist. The female jazz pianist was exceptional, not expected. Bown competed directly with male pianists for the same bandstand. Three barriers: Black, female, pianist. The gender barrier was higher for instrumentalists than vocalists.

Two women in seven degrees. Anderson (vocalist) was recognized despite discrimination. Bown (instrumentalist) was erased despite achievement. The variable is the role, not the talent.

The Erasure Pattern

Degrees 0–5 established six artist-city patterns. Bown introduces the seventh: The Erasure — the dark twin of The Export. Same departure, same destination, opposite outcome. Jones was exported and succeeded globally. Bown was exported, succeeded significantly, and was forgotten. The success was real (Columbia recording, DownBeat reviews, arranger connections, session work). The memory system failed.

Career Arc

1931 — Born in the Central District

Born June 26, 1931, in Seattle. Raised in the Central District. Studied music at Seattle University — the first S36 artist connected to that institution.

Mid-1950s — Departure for New York City

Moved to NYC during the hard bop era. Entered the most competitive piano scene in jazz — Silver, Timmons, Clark, Kelly. Became one of the first Black women to lead a hard bop piano trio.

1958 — Patti Bown Plays Big Piano (Columbia)

Debut album on Columbia Records. Favorably reviewed in DownBeat. “Big Piano” declared scope: the player commands the instrument’s full range. A Columbia debut by a Black woman from Seattle during the hard bop era — two barriers broken simultaneously.

1960s — Peak: Sessions, Arrangers, Motown

Worked with Quincy Jones, Oliver Nelson, Duke Pearson. Session pianist for Motown and pop/R&B recordings. At the center of hard bop’s creative infrastructure. The peak was real. What followed was silence.

1970s–2000s — Educator & Obscurity

Shifted toward music education. Teaching was real and valuable; documentation is sparse. No sustained national profile. No reissues. No retrospectives. The industry moved on.

2008 — Died in Philadelphia, age 76

Died March 21, 2008. No NEA Jazz Master. No Grammy nominations. Columbia debut out of print. The Central District did not mourn publicly. The city that produced her had forgotten her.

Music Theory Mapping

Four theory nodes — all extending the piano branch established at degree 5. The piano branch now holds seven nodes across two degrees (Berry: 3, Bown: 4).

Hard Bop Piano Voicings

Advanced

Rootless voicings — omit the root (leave it to the bassist), voice the 3rd, 7th, and extensions (9th, 13th, altered tones) in close position. Compact, harmonically rich, collaborative. Quartal voicings add open, modern sound connecting to modal jazz. Berry’s stride voicings are rooted and self-sufficient; Bown’s are rootless and assume a rhythm section.

Patti Bown Plays Big Piano (Columbia, 1958); Horace Silver; Bobby Timmons
Thread: Berry (rooted stride, 5°) → Bown (rootless hard bop, 6°). The voicing tells you whether the pianist is alone or in a band.

Call-and-Response (Gospel in Hard Bop)

Intermediate

Right-hand melodic phrase (call) answered by left-hand chordal punctuation (response). Episodic, not continuous — the left hand speaks when the right pauses, creating a conversation between two hands. The gospel dimension connects Bown to the Central District’s church tradition — gospel piano and jazz piano shared the same cultural infrastructure.

Bown’s hard bop trio recordings; Bobby Timmons “Moanin’”; Horace Silver “The Preacher”
Contrast: Berry (continuous stride alternation, 5°) vs. Bown (episodic call-response, 6°). Two left-hand philosophies from one neighborhood.

Hard Bop Rhythmic Drive

Intermediate

Heavier, more insistent than cool jazz. Blues shuffle and gospel backbeat roots. The pianist’s chords are rhythmic events — each voicing lands on a specific beat with a specific attack, contributing to groove as much as harmony. The piano as percussive instrument. Bown’s authority was partly rhythmic — driving a trio from the piano bench.

Patti Bown Plays Big Piano; Art Blakey and the Jazz Messengers; Horace Silver Quintet
New position: Rhythmic spectrum now 5 positions — regular (Berry), polymetric (Horvitz), rubato (Frisell), elastic swing (Anderson), hard bop drive (Bown).

Pianist as Bandleader

Advanced

Leading a trio = choosing repertoire, setting tempo, determining form, signaling transitions, controlling dynamics. The pianist is not one voice among three — she is the leader. For a Black woman in 1958 to exercise that authority over typically male sidemen was to claim a position the industry did not expect her to occupy. Amiga Principle at small-group scale.

Patti Bown Plays Big Piano (trio); Ahmad Jamal trio; Bill Evans Trio
Amiga spectrum: Jones (0°, large ensemble) → Bown (6°, small group) → Berry (5°, solo). Three scales of pianistic authority.
