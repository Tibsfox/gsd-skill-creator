The congregation assembles. Black Stax is a hip-hop soul trio from Seattle’s Central District — MC Jace ECAj, MC Silas Blak, and vocalist/actress Felicia Loud — each with 25+ years on stage. They call their sound vibrational music. Their mission: honor the legacy of Black Arts & Culture by stacking all forms of Black music and art. The Central District returns for the fourth time. Jazz is absent from the genre label for the first time in 26 degrees. The Cultural Guardian.

Duwamish Territorial Acknowledgment

Every artist in the Seattle 360 engine created their music on the ancestral territory of the Duwamish Tribal Organization, the Muckleshoot Indian Tribe, and related Coast Salish peoples. Black Stax are Central District artists whose work is rooted in the same Jackson Street corridor that sustained Quincy Jones, Ernestine Anderson, and the founding cluster. The Central District was shaped by redlining: Black people were confined to specific neighborhoods by discriminatory lending practices. The music that emerged from that confinement became one of America’s great artistic traditions. The Duwamish Tribe remains federally unrecognized. This acknowledgment was established at degree 20.

The Three Pillars

Jace ECAj — MC, Community Organizer

Veteran MC and “Seattle Hip Hop Icon.” Grew up between Seattle and Richmond, California. Director of Community and Artist Development at The Residency (youth hip-hop organization). Creative writing educator for 20+ years. Previously half of Silent Lambs Project (with Silas Blak, formed 1997; K Records releases). Influences: Otis Redding, Earth Wind & Fire, Al Green, Nina Simone.

Silas Blak — MC, Poet-Militant

Formative work in Blind Council, Silent Lambs Project, and Black Stax. Solo releases through K Records (Olympia, WA). Delivery described as “edgy and filled with tension.” Collaborative work in Silent Lambs and Black Stax considered “integral installments in the 206 rap canon.”

Felicia Loud — Vocalist, Actress, Playwright

Born in Louisiana, moved to Seattle at age four. 40+ years on Seattle stages. Youth theater at Langston Hughes Cultural Center. Gregory Award nominee 2018 (Billie Holiday in Lady Day at Emerson’s Bar and Grill). Credits at Seattle Rep, Intiman, ArtsWest, 5th Avenue, Book-It. Wrote and starred in Here There Be Dragons (ArtsWest, 2022). Coined the term “vibrational music.”

The Cultural Guardian — 26th Artist-City Pattern

Black Stax’s relationship to Seattle is defensive: the Central District’s Black population fell from 73% (1970) to ~9% (2020). The group combines performance + education + institutional stewardship into a platform whose purpose is ensuring the tradition survives. Jace runs youth programs at The Residency. Both Jace and Felicia are teaching artists in schools and libraries statewide. They performed at LHPAI’s 50th anniversary. The city is the cause.
