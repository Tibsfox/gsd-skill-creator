## Duwamish Territorial Acknowledgment

This research documents music made on Duwamish land. Every artist in the Seattle 360 engine (degrees 0-25) created their music on the ancestral territory of the **Duwamish Tribal Organization** (duwamishtribe.org), the **Muckleshoot Indian Tribe**, and related Coast Salish peoples. Black Stax are Central District artists -- their work is rooted in the same Jackson Street corridor that sustained Quincy Jones, Ernestine Anderson, Floyd Standifer, Overton Berry, Joe Brazil, and Fly Moon Royale. The Central District was shaped by redlining: Black people didn't settle there by choice but because discriminatory lending practices confined racial minorities to specific neighborhoods. The music that emerged from that confinement became one of America's great artistic traditions. The Duwamish Tribe remains federally unrecognized despite continuous habitation and cultural practice. This acknowledgment was established at degree 20 and is inherited by all subsequent releases.

## Biography & The Central District Returns (Fourth Time)

Black Stax is an **avant-garde hip-hop soul collective** formed in **2009** in **Seattle's Central District**. The group is built around three core members: **MC Jace ECAj**, **MC Silas Blak**, and **vocalist/actress Felicia Loud** -- each bringing over **25 years of professional experience** in arts performance and community work. They are not a band in the conventional sense. They are a **cultural platform** -- a deliberate, named enterprise whose mission is to honor the legacy of Black Arts and Culture by "stacking" all forms of Black music and art. The name itself is the manifesto: **Black** (the culture) + **Stax** (stacking, accumulating, building upon).

The Central District placement at degree 25 is the engine's most structurally significant geographic event since degree 19. The CD oscillation pattern is now fully legible:

| Phase | Degrees | CD Status | Gap Length |
|-------|---------|-----------|------------|
| Founding cluster | 0-6 | Present (7 consecutive) | -- |
| First absence | 7-10 | Absent | 4 degrees |
| First return | 11 | Present (Joe Brazil) | -- |
| Second absence | 12-18 | Absent | 7 degrees |
| Second return | 19 | Present (Fly Moon Royale) | -- |
| Third absence | 20-24 | Absent | 5 degrees |
| **Third return** | **25** | **Present (Black Stax)** | -- |

The **absences are not getting shorter** in a linear way (4, 7, 5), but the returns are becoming more structurally charged. Joe Brazil at degree 11 was a salon keeper -- a private, informal educator. Fly Moon Royale at degree 19 was a neo-soul collective documenting a gentrified CD. Black Stax at degree 25 is something the engine has not seen before: **an explicitly activist cultural platform** that treats the Central District not as a location but as a **cause** -- a place whose Black heritage must be actively defended, taught, and transmitted because gentrification is actively erasing it.

The Central District of 2009-2025 is radically different from the CD of 1950-1970. In 1970, approximately 73% of the CD's residents were Black. By 2020, that number had fallen to approximately **9%**. The Jackson Street jazz corridor that sustained degrees 0-6 is gone -- the nightclubs closed, the Black-owned businesses displaced, the community fragmented by decades of gentrification driven by Seattle's tech economy. But the music did not die. It transformed. Black Stax represents the **third generation** of Central District music in the engine:

### The Three Pillars: Jace ECAj, Silas Blak, Felicia Loud

**Jace ECAj** is a veteran MC and "Seattle Hip Hop Icon" whose roots run deep in the city's underground rap scene. He grew up between **Seattle and Richmond, California**, absorbing both the Bay Area's hyphy/conscious hip-hop traditions and Seattle's rain-soaked, literary-minded rap culture. His parents played **Earth, Wind & Fire, Al Green, and Nina Simone** on Sunday mornings -- a soul-music catechism that shaped his approach to hip-hop as a vehicle for ideas, not just beats. He identifies **Otis Redding** as a defining influence. Jace is not only a performer but a **community organizer and educator**: he serves as **Director of Community and Artist Development at The Residency**, a Seattle organization that builds a community of young hip-hop artists equipped with artistic skills, business acumen, and mentorship. He has worked in creative writing education for **over twenty years** and has organized panel discussions on topics ranging from artist accountability to the cultural impact of gentrification. He is the engine's first **hip-hop pedagogue** -- an artist who teaches the next generation not through institutional faculty positions (like Standifer at UW, degree 4, or Priester at Cornish, degree 7) but through **community organizations and direct mentorship** in the hip-hop tradition.

Before Black Stax, Jace ECAj was half of **Silent Lambs Project** with Silas Blak, a collaboration that began in **1997**. Silent Lambs Project released *Comrade* (EP, 1998), *H.O.R. (House of Respect)* (2000), and *Soul Liquor* (2000) on **K Records** -- the Olympia, Washington independent label founded by Calvin Johnson that helped define the Pacific Northwest indie-music ecosystem. Silent Lambs Project was, by all accounts, ahead of its time: political, dense, literary hip-hop from a city that the national rap world had not yet noticed. The group was described as being "on a black militancy tip" -- confrontation with and destruction of structural racism through bars.

**Silas Blak** is the third pillar, a rapper whose career spans formative Pacific Northwest groups including **Blind Council**, **Silent Lambs Project**, and **Black Stax**. His solo work is released through K Records. His delivery is described as "edgy and filled with tension" -- the yang to Jace's smooth, floating yin. Where Jace is the organizer and educator, Silas is the **poet-militant**, the lyricist whose words cut with deliberate precision. His collaborative work in Silent Lambs Project and Black Stax is considered **"integral installments in the 206 rap canon"** -- 206 being Seattle's area code and shorthand for the city's hip-hop identity.

**Felicia Loud** is the engine's most multidimensional artist since Reggie Watts (degree 17). She is simultaneously a **vocalist, actress, playwright, and teaching artist** with over **40 years on Seattle stages**. Born in **Louisiana**, she moved to Seattle with her single mother when she was four years old. As a child, she imitated **Bessie Smith, Billie Holiday, and Tina Turner**. By her early teens, she was involved with the **summer youth theater program at the Langston Hughes Cultural Center** in the Central District -- the same institution that would later become central to Black Stax's identity and creative practice.

Felicia Loud's theater credits read like a survey of Black American dramatic literature performed through Seattle's institutional theater pipeline:

| Production | Venue | Note |
|-----------|-------|------|
| *Pullman Porter Blues* | Seattle Repertory Theatre | African American railroad labor history |
| *Lady Day at Emerson's Bar and Grill* | Strawberry Theatre / ArtsWest | Billie Holiday portrayal, **Gregory Award nomination 2018** |
| *I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings* | Book-It Repertory Theatre | Maya Angelou adaptation |
| *Rock of Ages* | The 5th Avenue Theatre | Broadway musical |

| *Here There Be Dragons* | ArtsWest (2022) | **Original one-woman show** -- wrote and starred |

The **Gregory Award nomination** for her Billie Holiday portrayal is particularly significant. The Gregory Awards are Seattle's theater community awards -- the local equivalent of the Tonys. Felicia Loud playing Billie Holiday is not impersonation; it is **continuation**. Holiday's blue note -- the raw, emotionally direct vocal inflection that defined jazz singing -- runs through Loud's performance practice and directly into Black Stax's neo-soul vocal texture.

In 2022, Loud wrote and starred in **"Here There Be Dragons"** at ArtsWest -- an original one-woman show combining hip-hop, soul, cabaret, and storytelling. This is the engine's first artist who has **written and performed a solo theatrical work** outside the music context. The show represents Loud's artistic independence: not singing someone else's songs, not acting in someone else's play, but creating a complete theatrical experience from her own material.

### Langston Hughes Performing Arts Institute: The Institutional Anchor

Black Stax's relationship with the **Langston Hughes Performing Arts Institute (LHPAI)** is the most important institutional connection at degree 25. Both Jace and Felicia have described Langston Hughes as **"a critical space for their creative and personal development."** They are both Seattle natives who grew up in Central and South Seattle, and the LHPAI was the cultural incubator that formed them as artists.

The LHPAI is a historic landmark in the Central District. Built in **1915** as the **Chevra Bikur Cholim synagogue**, designed by architect **B. Marcus Priteca** (who also designed the Paramount and Coliseum theaters), the building became a community center and part of the City of Seattle's facilities in **1972**. It was renamed for Langston Hughes -- the Harlem Renaissance poet -- and has served as the Central District's primary Black cultural institution for over fifty years. Black Stax performed at the **LHPAI's 50th Anniversary Celebration** on September 10, 2022.

The LHPAI connection creates a structural parallel with the engine's institutional pipeline:

| Pipeline | Degrees | Institution | Character |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Garfield High School | 0, 3, 4 | Public school | Formal music education |
| Cornish College | 7, 9, 17 | Conservatory | Art-school training |
| UW School of Music | 4, 13, 14 | University | Academic pipeline |
| Berklee College | 0, 1, 16, 23 | Conservatory | National elite |
| Langston Hughes PAI | **25** | **Community center** | **Cultural formation, not formal training** |

The LHPAI pipeline is qualitatively different from all prior institutional pipelines because it is a **community cultural center**, not a school. It does not grant degrees, does not have faculty in the traditional sense, does not produce alumni in the credentialed sense. What it produces is **culturally formed artists** -- people who absorb Black artistic traditions through participation, observation, mentorship, and community rather than through curriculum. This is the closest thing the engine has documented to Joe Brazil's salon model (degree 11) but operating at institutional scale rather than individual scale.

(Source: Seattle.gov; HistoryLink.org; Langston Seattle; Wikipedia; Art Beat Seattle)

## The Discography: Stacking Culture

Black Stax's discography documents the evolution from underground hip-hop collective to community-embedded cultural platform:

| Year | Release | Format | Character |
|------|---------|--------|-----------|
| 2010 | *Talking Buildings* | Album | Debut. Three MCs + vocalist + trumpet (Owuor Arunga). "I Love My Life" video played in Foot Locker stores nationwide. "Spell on You" reached video charts in Nigeria. |
| 2012 | *High Rhymes Smoking Jackets* | EP | Produced by Rob Castro. Neo-soul blues hip-hop hybrid. |
| 2013 | *Black Stax LIVE at the Triple Door* | Live album | Captured at Seattle's Triple Door venue. |
| 2015 | *Movement Music EP* | EP | 6 tracks: Intro, Exit US, Tears, Driftin, Leanin, 99. Political and personal. |
| 2017 | *The Revival* | Album | Full-length statement. |

The debut album *Talking Buildings* (2010) is the defining document. The title itself is the Central District thesis: buildings talk. The architecture of a neighborhood carries its history -- the synagogue-turned-community-center (LHPAI), the demolished jazz clubs on Jackson Street, the new condominiums displacing Black families. Buildings are witnesses. When a building is demolished, its testimony is erased. When a building is repurposed, its testimony transforms. Black Stax's debut listens to what the buildings are saying.

The stand-in trumpet player on *Talking Buildings* was **Owuor Arunga** -- a jazz trumpeter born in **Kisumu, Kenya**, educated at **The New School's Jazz & Contemporary Music Program** in New York, and a **Garfield High School Jazz Band** alumnus. Arunga later became famous as **Macklemore & Ryan Lewis's touring trumpet player**, performing on the multi-Grammy-winning album *The Heist* (2012). The Garfield connection links Black Stax at degree 25 directly to the engine's founding institutional pipeline: Quincy Jones graduated from Garfield in 1950; Owuor Arunga played in Garfield's jazz band decades later and brought that lineage into Black Stax's debut album. The pipeline does not break; it branches.

The phrase **"vibrational music"** -- coined by Felicia Loud to describe Black Stax's sound -- is the most precise genre self-description in the engine. It acknowledges that the music operates at a frequency that cannot be captured by genre labels alone. Neo-soul, hip-hop, blues, spoken word, theater -- these are all components of a vibration that is intended to **move people physically and politically simultaneously**. The term has been used by the group for close to ten years, predating academic discourse on "vibration" as a sonic-political concept.

**The congregation** is the twenty-sixth acoustic element. In both musical and spiritual traditions, a congregation is not a passive audience -- it is an **active assembly** that participates in the creation of the sonic event. The preacher invokes; the congregation responds. The MC drops a bar; the crowd answers. The vocalist sings the hook; the room sings it back. The congregation is the acoustic element where **the boundary between performer and audience dissolves**.

Black Stax's music operates as congregation in multiple registers. Their live shows are not performances TO an audience but performances WITH a community. The teaching-artist practice extends this: when Jace ECAj leads a creative writing workshop in a school, the students are not audience -- they are congregation, participating in the creation of the art. When Felicia Loud performs at the Langston Hughes PAI's 50th anniversary, the community gathered there is not watching a show -- they are witnessing and participating in the continuation of their own cultural history.

The congregation follows the invocation in the second ADSR cycle because the cycle is building toward peak through a natural liturgical sequence: **the groove reset (22) laid the rhythmic foundation, the testimony (23) provided the narrative, the invocation (24) called the energy into the room, and now the congregation (25) answers the call**. The four-element sequence mirrors a church service: the band plays (groove), the preacher testifies, the preacher invokes, and the congregation rises. Black Stax's hip-hop soul fusion IS this liturgical structure secularized -- the Central District as church, the music as sermon, the community as congregation.

In synthesis terms, the congregation is a **resonance amplification through collective participation**. Where the invocation (degree 24) activated dormant sympathetic strings, the congregation is the moment when all those activated strings begin vibrating together, producing a sound larger than any individual source. This is the acoustic signature of community-based art: the output exceeds the input because the audience contributes energy.

## Neo-Soul as Standalone Genre: A First

### Genre Evolution: The Seventh Stage

| Degree | Genre | Stage |
|--------|-------|-------|
| 0-15 | Jazz (various) | Stage 1: Jazz-sovereign |
| 16 | Jazz Fusion | Stage 2: Jazz compound |
| 17 | Jazz/Comedy | Stage 3: Jazz superposition |
| 18-19 | Soul/Jazz | Stage 4: Jazz as modifier |
| 20 | Americana/Soul/Blues | Stage 5: Jazz disappears |
| 21-22 | Jazz/Funk | Stage 6: Funk primary |
| 23-24 | Soul/Jazz | Stage 7: Soul/Jazz returns |
| **25** | **Neo-Soul** | **Stage 8: Soul sovereign -- Jazz fully absent** |

Degree 25 introduces **Neo-Soul as a standalone genre label** -- the first time in twenty-six degrees that a genre appears without Jazz as either primary or modifier. The engine's genre evolution is now complete in its first major transition: from Jazz-sovereign (0-15) through compound genres with Jazz present (16-24) to **Soul-sovereign with Jazz fully absent (25)**. Neo-Soul is not Soul/Jazz. It is its own thing.

The "Neo" prefix is significant. Neo-Soul emerged nationally in the late 1990s through artists like D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Lauryn Hill, and Musiq Soulchild -- a movement that took classic soul structures (Motown, Stax, Philadelphia International) and rebuilt them with hip-hop production, spoken-word consciousness, and Afrocentric spirituality. Black Stax's Neo-Soul is this national movement filtered through **Seattle's Central District**: the classic soul referenced is the Jackson Street tradition (degrees 0-6), the hip-hop production comes from the 206 underground (Silent Lambs Project, K Records), and the Afrocentric consciousness comes from the direct experience of watching your neighborhood's Black heritage be gentrified away.

The genre evolution also confirms a structural hypothesis: **Jazz is a foundation, not a permanent companion**. Through 25 degrees, Jazz's presence has diminished from sovereign (100% of the label) to compound (50%) to modifier (secondary) to absent. The tradition does not disappear -- it is absorbed. Neo-Soul at degree 25 CONTAINS jazz harmony, jazz improvisation instincts, and jazz-trained musicianship, but does not identify as jazz. The foundation is invisible because it has become the ground everyone walks on.

## The Twenty-Sixth Artist-City Pattern: The Cultural Guardian

### Artist-City Taxonomy: Twenty-Six Named Patterns

Black Stax introduces the engine's twenty-sixth named artist-city pattern: **The Cultural Guardian**. This is an artist or collective whose primary relationship to their city is **defense and transmission of cultural heritage** -- not just making music in the city but actively working to ensure the city's cultural traditions survive gentrification, displacement, and erasure.

The Cultural Guardian is distinct from all prior patterns because the **relationship to the city is protective rather than extractive, productive, or residential**:

| Pattern | Relationship to City | Direction |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| Export (Jones, 0) | City as launchpad | Outward |
| The Stay (Standifer, 4) | City as home | Static |
| The Salon Keeper (Brazil, 11) | City as classroom | Inward (private) |
| **The Cultural Guardian (Black Stax, 25)** | **City as cause** | **Defensive (public)** |

Joe Brazil (degree 11, The Salon Keeper) was the closest precedent -- he transmitted jazz knowledge through private mentorship in his Central District home. But Brazil was operating in a CD that was still predominantly Black, where the cultural traditions were still intact and transmission was maintenance rather than rescue. Black Stax is operating in a CD where the Black population has dropped from 73% to 9%, where the jazz clubs are gone, where the community institutions are threatened, and where cultural transmission is an act of **resistance against erasure**.

The Cultural Guardian pattern includes three components that no prior pattern has combined:
1. **Performance** (music as artistic expression)
2. **Education** (teaching-artist practice in schools, libraries, community centers)
3. **Institutional stewardship** (Jace at The Residency, both at LHPAI, connections to Arte Noir and other CD cultural organizations)

The full artist-city taxonomy is now twenty-six patterns: Export, Draw, European Exile, The Stay, The Teacher, The Persistence, The Erasure, Pedagogue-Draw, The Quiet Draw, The Seeker, Late Homestead, The Salon Keeper, The Prodigal, Institutional Anchor, Prodigy-Export, Builder-Stayer, Talent Incubator, The Uncategorizable, The Workshop, The New Root, The Street Laureate, The Ensemble Anchor, The Inheritor, The Rural Draw, The Island Export, **The Cultural Guardian**.

## Music Theory: The Trio Architecture & The Garfield Thread

### Three-Voice Architecture: MC + MC + Vocalist

Black Stax's core architecture -- two MCs and one vocalist -- is unprecedented in the engine. No prior degree has documented a group where **rap and singing coexist as co-equal primary voices**. The closest comparisons:

| Degree | Artist | Architecture | Voice Structure |
|--------|--------|-------------|----------------|
| 17 | Reggie Watts | Solo | Beat-box + singing + comedy (one person, multiple modes) |
| 18 | Maktub | Band | Vocalist (Reggie Watts predecessor) + instruments |
| 19 | Fly Moon Royale | Collective | Vocalist (Parker) + rotating instrumentalists |
| **25** | **Black Stax** | **Trio platform** | **MC (Jace) + MC (Silas) + Vocalist (Felicia) + stand-in instrumentalists** |

The two-MC structure comes directly from hip-hop tradition (Run-DMC, OutKast, Black Star -- Mos Def and Talib Kweli). The addition of a vocalist trained in theater and soul creates a **tripartite voice** that can operate in three registers simultaneously:

1. **Jace ECAj**: Smooth, flowing delivery. Community-conscious bars. The organizer voice.
2. **Silas Blak**: Edgy, tense delivery. Militant poetry. The confrontational voice.
3. **Felicia Loud**: Soul vocals drawing from Holiday, Simone, Turner. The historical voice.

The three voices together produce a sound described as "like Miles Davis, Etta James and Langston Hughes adapted for savvy, hip-hop and neo-soul listeners." This comparison is precise: Davis (instrumental cool), Etta James (raw soul vocals), Hughes (literary consciousness) -- three traditions unified in one group. The literary reference is not accidental: the Langston Hughes PAI is their home institution, and Black Stax's lyrics carry the same social-documentation purpose as Hughes's poetry.

### The Garfield Thread Extends

Owuor Arunga's presence on *Talking Buildings* extends the **Garfield High School thread** into degree 25:

| Degree | Artist | Garfield Connection |
|--------|--------|-------------------|
| 0 | Quincy Jones | Garfield class of 1950 |
| 3 | Ernestine Anderson | Garfield, same era as Jones |
| 4 | Floyd Standifer | Garfield via Central District community |
| **25** | **Black Stax** | **Owuor Arunga (stand-in trumpet), Garfield Jazz Band alumnus** |

Arunga is a **bridge node**: Garfield High School to Black Stax to Macklemore & Ryan Lewis. He connects the engine's founding jazz institution (Garfield, where Jones learned his craft in the 1940s) to the 2010s hip-hop soul scene (Black Stax) and to Seattle's biggest commercial hip-hop export (Macklemore). The pipeline runs: Garfield (1940s jazz education) -> Garfield Jazz Band (2000s continuation) -> Arunga -> Black Stax (*Talking Buildings*, 2010) AND Macklemore (*The Heist*, 2012). One musician, two pipelines, one school, seven decades apart.

### Blue Note Deployment (Twenty-Fifth)

Black Stax's blue note is the **collective-activist** blue note -- vocal and verbal bending deployed not for personal expression but for **communal mobilization**. Felicia Loud's blue note inherits from Billie Holiday (she literally played Holiday on stage) and channels it through hip-hop's rhythmic consciousness. Jace and Silas's verbal blue notes are rhythmic: the way an MC bends a word to make it hit harder, stretches a syllable across the beat, lets silence do the work of emphasis.

Twenty-five deployments across twenty-six degrees. The collective-activist blue note is the first that operates across **three simultaneous voices** -- two MCs and a vocalist deploying different blue-note techniques in the same song, the aggregate producing a blue-note *field* rather than a blue-note *point*.

Felicia Loud is the engine's **fourth female-identified artist** in twenty-six degrees -- after Ernestine Anderson (degree 3), Patti Bown (degree 6), and Whitney Monge (degree 20). However, she appears as a member of a collective rather than as a solo artist or bandleader, which complicates the count:

| 6 | Patti Bown | Woman | Solo pianist/vocalist |
| 20 | Whitney Monge | Woman | Solo vocalist/guitarist |
| **25** | **Felicia Loud (Black Stax)** | **Woman** | **Co-equal member of trio** |

The gender gap narrows slightly: **4:22** if we count Loud as a primary artist (she is a co-equal member, not a backing vocalist or session player). But the gap remains severe. Twenty-two of twenty-six degrees are male or male-led. The engine's soul/neo-soul zone (degrees 18-25) has produced two female artists out of eight degrees.

Felicia Loud's presence is structurally significant beyond the gender count. She is the engine's **most accomplished theater artist** -- over 40 years on Seattle stages, a Gregory Award nomination, a one-woman show, credits at Seattle Rep, Intiman, ArtsWest, 5th Avenue, and Book-It. Her dual practice (music AND theater) is unprecedented at this depth. Reggie Watts (degree 17) crossed comedy and music; Felicia Loud crosses **theater and music** with a depth of theatrical practice that Watts's standup/music hybrid does not match. She is the engine's first artist for whom theatrical performance and musical performance are **co-equal practices, not hierarchy**.
