## The Prodigy-Export Pipeline: The Most Significant Structural Finding

Degree 14's most important contribution is documenting the **prodigy-export pipeline** -- the institutional mechanism that identifies exceptional talent in Seattle, exports it to national training centers, and launches it into global careers. This is the Quincy Jones export pattern (degree 0) modernized for the 2000s.

### The Two Exports

```
BANDSTAND EXPORT (Jones, 0) -- 1950s
  Garfield HS -> Playing with Blackwell/Ray Charles -> Hired by Hampton -> touring -> global career
  |
  | (50+ years of institutional formalization)
  |
INSTITUTIONAL EXPORT (Parks, 14) -- 2000s
  UW pre-college (14) -> MSM conservatory (16) -> Monk Competition (18) -> Blanchard band (19) -> Blue Note (25)
```

The structural insight: the export pipeline has been institutionalized. The informal mechanism (nightclub -> touring band -> career) has been replaced by a formal mechanism (pre-college program -> conservatory -> competition -> apprenticeship -> label). The same fundamental pattern -- Seattle-born talent leaves for a career center and does not return -- but the mechanism is fundamentally different.

The Monk Competition is the institutional trigger that the bandstand gig used to be. The pre-college program is the identification mechanism that the nightclub used to be. The conservatory is the training ground that the touring band used to be. Every stage of the pipeline has been formalized.

## The Prodigy-Export: Fifteenth Artist-City Pattern

Degree 14 adds the fifteenth named pattern: **The Prodigy-Export** -- native-born, identified as exceptional before 16, exported to a major training center before completing secondary education, career based elsewhere.

The Prodigy-Export is structurally distinct from The Export (Jones, degree 0):

- **The Export:** leaves as a young adult after completing local education. Departure is a career decision.
- **The Prodigy-Export:** leaves as a minor, shipped out by the institutional pipeline. Departure is driven by institutional identification.

The pattern also differs from The Erasure (Bown, degree 6):
- **The Erasure:** exported, succeeded, then forgotten. The city does not claim the artist.
- **The Prodigy-Export:** exported, succeeded, and remembered. Parks is claimed by Seattle even though he lives in NYC. The prodigy is a source of civic pride, not erasure.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **The prodigy-export pipeline analysis is the most structurally significant export finding since degree 0.** Documenting the institutional modernization of the export pipeline -- from bandstand to pre-college/conservatory/competition -- provides a clear framework for classifying all subsequent exports.

2. **The piano quartet maps three-dimensional trajectory (stylistic, educational, city-relationship) simultaneously.** Four pianists, four eras, four educational paths, four city-relationship patterns. The richest instrument-family documentation in the engine.

3. **The Seales-Parks complementarity is structurally productive.** Two consecutive degrees, both Seattle-born, both UW-connected, but structurally opposite: one anchors the institution (stays, teaches, directs), the other is launched by it (leaves, never returns). The complementarity demonstrates how the same institutional system produces both anchors and exports.

4. **The post-genre theory node correctly identifies Parks's vocabulary as inherited from the modal revolution and extended with non-jazz textures.** The three-generation harmonic cycle (Tyner innovates quartal -> Seales deploys as vocabulary -> Parks inherits as baseline) is historically accurate and provides a framework for tracking harmonic evolution across generations.

5. **The ECM thread extension to four artists strengthens the ECM-Seattle affinity hypothesis.** Four ECM artists in fifteen degrees suggests structural affinity between the ECM aesthetic and the Seattle jazz sensibility.

### Source Quality

6. **Blue Note Records, ECM Records, and the Monk Institute provide strong institutional sources.** Major label catalogs, competition archives, and conservatory documentation are publicly accessible and verifiable. This is the strongest label-source base in the engine.

7. **Parks's public profile is well-documented.** DownBeat, JazzTimes, AllMusic, NPR Music, and The New York Times provide extensive coverage of Parks's career. Source quality is high for this degree.
