## Biography & The Prodigy-Export

Aaron Parks was born in Seattle, Washington in 1983 -- the youngest artist in the S36 engine to date and the first millennial-generation musician the engine has documented. He is the fifteenth degree, the fifteenth consecutive jazz artist, and the fourth pianist in the series (after Berry at degree 5, Bown at degree 6, and Seales at degree 13). He is the first artist in the engine whose biography follows the classical prodigy-export pipeline in its purest modern form: identified as exceptional in childhood, enrolled in pre-college programs while still in his early teens, shipped to a major East Coast institution at 16, launched into a professional career before turning 20, and never returning to Seattle as a professional base.

Parks attended the University of Washington pre-college music program at age 14 -- the same institution where Marc Seales (degree 13) directs the jazz studies program. By 16, he had left Seattle for the Manhattan School of Music in New York City. In 2001, at age 18, he won the Thelonious Monk International Jazz Piano Competition -- the most prestigious jazz competition in the world, a career-defining credential that has launched musicians from Herbie Hancock to Marcus Roberts. Winning the Monk Competition at 18 placed Parks among the youngest winners in the competition's history and immediately positioned him as a major voice in contemporary jazz piano.

At 19, Parks joined Terence Blanchard's band -- one of the premier working groups in modern jazz. Blanchard, himself a Blue Note recording artist, film composer, and one of the most visible trumpeters of his generation, served as Parks's professional apprenticeship in the post-Blakey lineage of bandleader-as-mentor. This is the Art Blakey model updated for the 21st century: the young pianist learns the profession by working in the leader's band, touring internationally, recording studio albums, and absorbing the discipline of a working professional ensemble.

Parks's debut album *Invisible Cinema* was released on Blue Note Records in 2008. The album was widely acclaimed as one of the most promising jazz piano debuts of the decade. *Invisible Cinema* demonstrated Parks's synthesis of multiple vocabularies: post-bop jazz harmony, indie rock textures (layered guitars, ambient washes, electronic production elements), and a compositional sensibility that drew as much from Radiohead and Bjork as from Herbie Hancock and Keith Jarrett. The Blue Note imprint -- the most storied label in jazz history -- signaled the industry's investment in Parks as a generational voice.

Parks subsequently recorded *Arborescence* for ECM Records in 2013, a solo piano album that connected him to the label's tradition of spacious, reverberant piano recordings (Keith Jarrett's Koln Concert, Paul Bley's solo work). He formed Little Big with guitarist Greg Tuohey, bassist Thomas Morgan, and drummer Marcus Gilmore -- a band whose sound explicitly bridges jazz and indie rock, deploying guitar-driven textures, layered production, and song-form structures alongside improvisation. Little Big recorded for Ropeadope Records, an independent label known for genre-crossing jazz projects.

Parks is currently based in New York City. He has not returned to Seattle as a professional base. He represents the contemporary generation of jazz musicians who grew up with indie rock, electronic music, and hip-hop alongside the jazz tradition -- artists for whom genre boundaries are not walls to be respected but resources to be drawn from.

## Music Theory: Post-Genre Jazz Harmony and the Influence of Non-Jazz Textures

### The Theoretical Framework

Parks's piano vocabulary inherits the entire modal jazz revolution documented at degree 13 (Seales) and extends it in a direction no previous artist in the engine has explored: the systematic integration of non-jazz textures into a jazz harmonic vocabulary. The core theoretical elements:

1. **Quartal voicings as inherited vocabulary.** Parks deploys quartal harmony not as innovation but as received language. Where Tyner invented quartal voicings and Seales (degree 13) deployed them as standard vocabulary, Parks inherited them as baseline -- his starting point, not his destination. Three-generation harmonic cycle: innovation -> vocabulary -> inheritance.

2. **Slash chords and ambiguous tonality.** Parks routinely employs slash chords -- upper-structure chords over non-root bass notes (Bb/C, D/F#, Eb/G) -- producing harmonic ambiguity where two tonal centers coexist. This floating quality resists resolution and is central to the post-genre aesthetic: neither fully jazz functional harmony nor indie rock triadic simplicity. The space between is Parks's signature territory.

3. **Metric modulation and floating time.** Parks shifts the perceived pulse by superimposing new tempi derived from subdivisions. In Little Big, drums may groove in 4/4 while piano implies 6/8, creating rhythmic elasticity experienced as floating time -- elastic rather than complex, stretching without the mathematical rigor of odd-meter playing.

4. **Indie rock textures as compositional resource.** Indie rock guitar textures -- sustained chords with delay, tremolo, shoegaze washes -- inform Parks's piano voicings and production approach. On *Invisible Cinema* and the Little Big recordings, guitar (Tuohey) provides Radiohead/My Bloody Valentine textures while piano serves as color rather than driving harmonic instrument.

5. **Electronic ambient spaces.** Reverb as instrument, delay as structural element, electronic production as extension of acoustic piano. *Arborescence* (ECM, 2013) deploys the ECM aesthetic at its most spacious: piano in a reverberant room, silence as composition. Connects to the ECM lineage (Peacock degree 8, Frisell degree 1).

6. **Tritone substitution as unconscious inheritance (from degree 0).** The tritone substitution thread reaches its endpoint: from innovative device (bebop, degree 12) to standard vocabulary (modal, degree 13) to unconscious inheritance (post-genre, degree 14). Parks does not deploy tritone subs consciously; they appear as naturally as major chords.

**Fourteenth deployment of the chromaticism thread.** Parks's chromaticism is post-genre chromaticism: chromatic movement between distantly related key centers, used not for the functional purpose of voice-leading (bebop) or the modal purpose of color-shifting (Seales, degree 13) but for atmospheric effect. A phrase may begin in one tonal area and end in another without any traditional harmonic connection, and the movement between them is experienced as emotional narrative rather than theoretical logic. This is chromaticism after theory -- deployed by a musician whose theoretical training is extensive but whose aesthetic priority is feel over function.

**Fourteenth deployment of the blue note thread.** Parks's blue note deployment extends the modal integration documented at degree 13 into post-genre territory. In Parks's vocabulary, the blue note (b3, b7) appears not only as a modal defining pitch (Seales, degree 13) but also as a voice-leading element borrowed from indie rock harmony. The minor-over-major ambiguity (is this a blues b3 or an indie-rock minor chord?) is left deliberately unresolved. The blue note floats between jazz and rock semantics, belonging fully to neither -- the post-genre blue note.

## The Prodigy-Export: Quincy Jones Pipeline in Modern Form

### From Garfield to MSM -- The Institutional Upgrade

The S36 engine has now documented two exports: Jones (degree 0) and Parks (degree 14). The two exports bookend the jazz series to date and reveal how the export pipeline has changed across fifty years:

| Position | Artist | Era | Pipeline | Trigger | Destination |
|----------|--------|-----|----------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Jones (0) | 1950s | Bandstand apprenticeship | Playing with Blackwell, Ray Charles | National touring, LA, NYC |
| 2 | **Parks (14)** | **2000s** | **Institutional identification** | **UW pre-college, Monk Competition** | **MSM, NYC** |

The structural insight: the export pipeline has been institutionalized. In Jones's era, talent was identified on the bandstand. In Parks's era, talent is identified through pre-college programs. The pipeline has moved from informal (nightclub -> touring band -> career) to formal (pre-college -> conservatory -> competition -> label). This is the same institutional formalization that the Standifer-to-Seales arc documented for pedagogy (degrees 4 to 13), now applied to the export mechanism.

The Monk Competition is the institutional trigger that the bandstand gig used to be. Winning at 18 did for Parks what sitting in with Lionel Hampton did for Jones: it made the young musician visible and launched a career. But the Monk Competition is an institution with rules, judges, and organizational structure -- the bandstand formalized into a competition.

Parks is the first artist exported through this institutional pipeline. He is also the youngest at departure (16, compared to Jones's ~18). The institutional pipeline exports younger because it identifies talent earlier.

## The Artist-City Taxonomy: Fifteenth Pattern

### The Prodigy-Export

Degree 14 adds the fifteenth named pattern to the artist-city taxonomy: **The Prodigy-Export** -- a native-born artist identified as exceptional in childhood, exported through institutional channels before completing secondary education, launched into a global career through competition or apprenticeship, and based permanently elsewhere.

The Prodigy-Export is structurally distinct from The Export (Jones, degree 0):

- The Export leaves as a young adult after completing local education and apprenticeship. The departure is a career decision made by the musician.
- The Prodigy-Export leaves as a minor, shipped out by the institutional pipeline before completing high school. The departure is driven by the institution's identification of talent and the family's decision to pursue elite training.

The Prodigy-Export requires four conditions:
1. Native-born (or long-resident from formative years)
2. Identified as exceptional before age 16
3. Exported to a major training center (conservatory, academy) before completing secondary education
4. Career based elsewhere -- no return to the origin city as a professional home

Parks meets all four. This is the most institutionally mediated export pattern in the engine. Where Jones left Seattle because the local bandstand led to a touring opportunity, Parks left because the institutional pipeline -- pre-college program, conservatory admission, competition circuit -- was designed to export talent from regional cities to national centers.
