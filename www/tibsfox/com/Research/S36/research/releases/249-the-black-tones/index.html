The Black Tones are a two-piece blues rock band built on twin-sibling chemistry, raw electric guitar, and the force of the classic blues tradition delivered at punk velocity. Sub Pop, 2019 onward. Eva plays guitar and sings. Cedric drums. Between them, they make more sound than the format should permit.

The Rainier Valley Return

The Black Tones mark the return of Rainier Valley to the Seattle 360 engine after 76 degrees of absence. Rainier Valley — running south from the Central District along Rainier Avenue South — is one of Seattle's most culturally diverse neighborhoods, historically a community of working-class families, immigrants, and long-established African American residents. The engine has mapped Capitol Hill, the U-District, Pioneer Square, and Capitol Hill repeatedly. Rainier Valley is a different part of the city with a different character, and The Black Tones bring it back at degree 249.

The neighborhood's proximity to the Central District — where Jimi Hendrix grew up (degrees 245-246) — is not incidental. Rainier Valley and the Central District share a community history, and the blues tradition runs through both. Eva and Cedric Walker's Rainier Valley roots place them in this geographic and cultural lineage.

"Eva Walker's guitar is heavy, electric, and fundamentally physical — not as art-school exercise but as direct statement. The blues is a living form in her hands."

Eva and Cedric Walker -- The Twin Core

The Black Tones are a guitar-drums duo: Eva on guitar and vocals, Cedric on drums. In a two-piece format, both musicians are fully exposed — there is no bass player to anchor low end, no second guitarist for harmonic fill. The drums carry melodic responsibility alongside rhythmic; the guitar carries all the harmonic weight plus the lead. This format demands a musical relationship that goes beyond bandmate — it requires the kind of communication shorthand that Eva and Cedric have from shared lives as twins.

The White Stripes demonstrated at the highest level that guitar-drums duos can function as complete bands. The Black Tones operate in the same tradition but with a deeper blues foundation: where the White Stripes' blues was filtered through Detroit art-punk, The Black Tones' blues runs from Muddy Waters through the Chicago electric tradition with punk velocity applied.

The Blues Thread -- 245 to 249

Three degrees separate Jimi Hendrix (245-246) from The Black Tones (249). The blues thread runs from the Central District (Hendrix, 1942-1970) through Alice in Chains (247, grunge-metal) and Thunderpussy (248, heavy rock) and arrives at Rainier Valley (Black Tones, 2013-present). The line is not direct — different traditions occupy the intervening degrees — but the blues foundation is visible at both ends. Eva Walker's electric guitar carries the same fundamental tradition that Hendrix transformed; she works from a different angle and in a different era, but both are making the blues loud and Seattle.
