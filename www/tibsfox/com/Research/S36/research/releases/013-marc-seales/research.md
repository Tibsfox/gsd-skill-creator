## Biography & The Academic-Performer

Marc Seales was born in Seattle, Washington -- a native-born Seattle musician who represents the institutional formalization of jazz education at the university level. He is the fourteenth degree, the thirteenth jazz artist, and the first PhD-holding artist in the S36 engine. He is the first artist whose primary professional identity integrates academic scholarship with active performance at the highest level, the third pianist in the series (after Berry at degree 5 and Bown at degree 6), and the artist who completes the pedagogical arc that Floyd Standifer began at degree 4: from experiential teaching rooted in bandstand apprenticeship to doctoral-level academic jazz studies.

Seales earned his Doctor of Musical Arts from the University of Washington, where he has served on the faculty of the School of Music for decades, directing the jazz studies program. This is not a peripheral appointment. The University of Washington is the Pacific Northwest's flagship research university, and its jazz program under Seales has become the institutional pipeline for the next generation of Seattle jazz musicians. Where Standifer (degree 4) taught through doing -- playing, demonstrating, transmitting the music through live performance and community engagement -- Seales teaches through a formalized academic structure: curriculum design, ensemble direction, theory courses, graduate advising, and the full apparatus of a university music department.

The distinction matters because it maps a historical transition. Jazz education in America has moved from informal apprenticeship (learning on the bandstand, sitting in, listening to records, the model that produced every musician in the engine through degree 12) to formal academic programs (Berklee, North Texas State, UW, and dozens of other institutions that now grant degrees in jazz studies). Seales stands at the institutional end of this spectrum. He did not learn jazz exclusively on the bandstand -- he studied it at the doctoral level and now transmits it through an institution that grants bachelor's, master's, and doctoral degrees in music. This is a fundamentally different pedagogical model than anything the engine has documented to this point.

As a performer, Seales is a working musician whose piano work spans post-bop, contemporary jazz, and the modal language that defines modern jazz piano. He has recorded for Naim Records, a UK-based audiophile label known for high-quality recordings and an eclectic jazz catalog. His collaborations with Seattle musicians -- including Julian Priester (degree 7), a fellow educator at Cornish College of the Arts -- position him at the center of Seattle's contemporary jazz network. Seales bridges performance and scholarship in a way that neither pole alone can achieve: his playing is informed by theory at the doctoral level, and his teaching is informed by decades of performance.

### The Academic-Performer Integration

The S36 engine has documented five pedagogical models through degree 12: institutional (Standifer, degree 4), bandstand apprenticeship (the model that produced most artists), self-taught (Berry, degree 5), salon (Brazil, degree 11), and elder-as-living-archive (Lanphere, degree 12). Degree 13 introduces a sixth model -- and the most formalized to date: **The University Scholar-Performer** -- an artist who holds a terminal academic degree in music and transmits jazz through the full institutional apparatus of a research university.

This is new territory for the engine. Previous educators in the series taught from experience: Standifer's multi-instrument mastery came from decades of performing; Priester's Cornish faculty position leveraged his global career as a trombonist; Caliman's teaching drew on his working-musician vocabulary. Seales teaches from experience AND from scholarship. The DMA is not a credential bolted onto a performance career -- it represents a deep engagement with the scholarly literature of jazz: its history, its theory, its pedagogy, its place in the broader landscape of American music. When Seales teaches a student about modal jazz, he draws on both his performing knowledge (how Dorian mode feels under the hands at tempo) and his scholarly knowledge (the theoretical framework that explains why Dorian mode produces a particular emotional quality, and the historical context in which Miles Davis deployed it on *Kind of Blue*).

The performer-scholar integration point is not trivial. Many jazz academics are primarily scholars who perform occasionally. Many jazz performers teach without academic credentials. Seales occupies the intersection: a musician who performs at a professional level and publishes, teaches, and mentors at a doctoral level. This integration point is the degree's central contribution to the engine's pedagogical taxonomy.

## Music Theory: Modal Jazz and Post-Bop Harmony

### The Theoretical Framework

Seales's piano vocabulary is built on the modal jazz revolution that Miles Davis initiated with *Kind of Blue* (1959) and that subsequent pianists -- Herbie Hancock, McCoy Tyner, Chick Corea, Keith Jarrett -- developed into the standard language of contemporary jazz piano. The core theoretical elements:

1. **Modal scales as compositional basis.** Where bebop (Lanphere, degree 12) organized improvisation around chord changes (ii-V-I progressions, tritone substitutions, chromatic approach), modal jazz organizes improvisation around scales. A section of music stays on one mode -- Dorian, Mixolydian, Lydian, Aeolian -- for an extended period, allowing the improviser to explore the color and feeling of that mode rather than racing through chord changes. This is a fundamentally different organizational principle: horizontal (scale-based) rather than vertical (chord-based).

2. **Quartal voicings.** McCoy Tyner's innovation, now standard in contemporary jazz piano: building chords from stacked fourths (C-F-Bb-Eb) rather than stacked thirds (C-E-G-B). Quartal voicings produce an open, ambiguous, modern sound that avoids the functional resolution of tertian (third-based) harmony. Seales's voicings deploy quartal structures as a primary harmonic vocabulary, creating the characteristic "open" sound of post-1960s jazz piano.

3. **Extended chord structures.** Beyond the seventh: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths as standard voicing components. Where Berry (degree 5) used triads and seventh chords in stride patterns, and Bown (degree 6) used dense altered voicings in the hard bop tradition, Seales builds voicings that routinely include four or five distinct chord extensions, creating rich harmonic textures that serve both as accompaniment and as solo voice.

4. **Lydian and Lydian Dominant color.** The Lydian mode (major scale with raised fourth degree) and the Lydian Dominant (Lydian with a flatted seventh) produce a bright, floating quality that is a signature of contemporary jazz harmony. George Russell's *Lydian Chromatic Concept of Tonal Organization* (1953) provided the theoretical framework; Davis, Coltrane, and Evans deployed it; Seales and his generation inherited it as standard vocabulary. The raised fourth (the #11 in chord nomenclature) is the single pitch that most distinguishes contemporary jazz harmony from bebop harmony.

5. **Academic jazz pedagogy as integration point.** Seales teaches these concepts not just by demonstrating them at the piano but by articulating the theoretical framework that explains them. The DMA-level understanding allows him to connect modal jazz theory to its historical roots (Russell's Lydian Chromatic Concept, Davis's *Kind of Blue*, Coltrane's modal explorations) and to broader music theory (the relationship between modes and scales in Western art music, the acoustics of interval relationships, the cognitive science of harmonic perception). This scholarly integration is the theory node that is unique to degree 13: the musician who can both play the music and articulate the theoretical system that generates it.

**Thirteenth deployment of the chromaticism thread.** Seales's chromaticism is different from all previous deployments. It is not the structural chromaticism of bebop (Lanphere, degree 12), not the ornamental chromaticism of stride (Berry, degree 5), not the altered-voicing chromaticism of hard bop piano (Bown, degree 6). It is **modal chromaticism** -- the use of chromatic alterations within and between modal frameworks to create color shifts. The raised fourth (Lydian), the flatted seventh over major (Lydian Dominant), the Dorian natural sixth over minor -- these are chromatic departures from the diatonic major/minor system that define the sound of contemporary jazz. They are deployed not as passing tones (bebop) or as blue notes (blues tradition) but as structural modal choices.

## The Standifer-to-Seales Arc: Jazz Pedagogy Formalized

### From the Bandstand to the University

The S36 engine has now documented the complete arc of jazz education in Seattle, from its most informal expression to its most institutional:

| Position | Artist | Model | Knowledge Transmission |
|----------|--------|-------|----------------------|
| 1 | Berry (5) | Self-taught autodidact | Playing IS teaching; no formal instruction given or received |
| 2 | Standifer (4) | Experiential teacher | UW faculty but teaching through demonstration and performance |
| 3 | Brazil (11) | Salon keeper | Home-based informal pedagogy; knowledge in students, not documents |
| 4 | Lanphere (12) | Elder-as-living-archive | Late-career elder carrying living memory of a vanished era |
| 5 | Priester (7), Caliman (9) | Pedagogue-draws | Working musicians teaching at Cornish; performance credential as teaching credential |
| 6 | **Seales (13)** | **University scholar-performer** | **DMA-level academic program; curriculum, degree granting, doctoral advising** |

The Standifer-to-Seales arc is the most structurally significant pedagogical development in the engine. Standifer (degree 4) was a UW faculty member, but his teaching model was rooted in bandstand experience -- he taught by playing, by being present in the community, by embodying the music. Seales is a UW faculty member whose teaching model is rooted in academic scholarship -- he teaches by articulating theory, designing curriculum, directing ensembles within a degree-granting program, and supervising graduate research.

This is not a judgment about which model is better. Both produce musicians. The difference is structural: Standifer's model depended on his personal presence (when he stopped teaching, the model stopped). Seales's model is institutional -- the UW jazz program has a curriculum, degree requirements, faculty positions, and an administrative structure that can persist beyond any individual teacher. The program itself becomes the pedagogy vehicle, not the person.

The engine gains a new axis at degree 13: the **institution-dependency spectrum**. At one pole, the pedagogy is entirely person-dependent (Brazil's salon -- when Brazil dies, the salon dies). At the other pole, the pedagogy is entirely institution-dependent (a university program that survives faculty turnover). Seales is the most institution-dependent pedagogue in the engine. Berry (self-taught, no pedagogy) is outside the spectrum entirely.

(Source: UW School of Music; Cornish College of the Arts; Earshot Jazz; KNKX; de Barros, *Jackson Street After Hours*)

## The Artist-City Taxonomy: Fourteenth Pattern

### The Institutional Anchor

Degree 13 adds the fourteenth named pattern to the artist-city taxonomy: **The Institutional Anchor** -- a native-born artist who is educated locally, holds an advanced degree, and anchors the music through a permanent institutional appointment.

The Institutional Anchor is structurally distinct from all thirteen previous patterns:

- Not The Teacher (Standifer, 4) -- the Teacher transmits through experience and presence. The Institutional Anchor transmits through a formal academic program.
- Not The Persistence (Berry, 5) -- the Persistence endures through decades of performing without institutional support. The Institutional Anchor endures through institutional appointment.
- Not The Pedagogue-Draw (Priester, 7) -- the Pedagogue-Draw arrived from elsewhere. The Institutional Anchor is native-born.
- Not The Working Pedagogue-Draw (Caliman, 9) -- same distinction: arrived from elsewhere.
- Not The Salon Keeper (Brazil, 11) -- the Salon Keeper teaches informally at home. The Institutional Anchor teaches formally at a university.

The Institutional Anchor pattern requires three conditions: native-born (or long-resident from formative years), advanced degree (terminal degree in the field), and institutional appointment (permanent faculty position at a degree-granting institution). Seales meets all three. This is the most institutionally embedded position in the artist-city taxonomy.

## Career Arc

### Born in Seattle, Washington

Born in Seattle -- native-born, not a draw or an export. Fifteenth educational pipeline in the engine -- the first where the artist's entire educational path (through the terminal degree) is completed within the Seattle metropolitan area.

### University of Washington: Student and Faculty

Studied at the University of Washington, earning a DMA (Doctor of Musical Arts). Joined the UW School of Music faculty, eventually becoming director of jazz studies. The UW appointment is the longest-running institutional jazz position in the engine -- decades of continuous faculty service.

### Naim Records and the Audiophile Label

Recorded for Naim Records, a UK-based audiophile label founded in Salisbury, England, by Naim Audio (manufacturers of high-end audio equipment). Naim's jazz catalog emphasizes recording quality -- the label's audio engineering ethos treats the recording as a high-fidelity document of the performance. This is the second international label in the engine (after Fresh Sound at degree 12), and the first UK-based label.

### Seattle Jazz Network: The Collaborator

Performed and recorded with numerous Seattle musicians, including Julian Priester (degree 7). Seales's position at UW and his active performance schedule place him at a network node: the point where the academic jazz pipeline (UW students, faculty colleagues) intersects with the performance community (Seattle clubs, festivals, recording sessions). This node function is unique in the engine -- no previous artist has occupied the intersection of a university program and the local performance scene with equal presence in both.

### The Program Director

As director of jazz studies at UW, Seales designs curriculum, directs student ensembles, supervises graduate research, and shapes the program that produces the next generation of Seattle jazz musicians. The students who pass through the UW jazz program carry Seales's pedagogical influence into their own careers -- as performers, educators, composers, and scholars. This is institutional reproduction: the program generates its own successors.
