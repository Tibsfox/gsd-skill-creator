Biography & The Academic-Performer

Marc Seales was born in Seattle, Washington — a native-born Seattle musician who represents the institutional formalization of jazz education at the university level. He is the first PhD-holding artist in the S36 engine, the third pianist (after Berry at degree 5° and Bown at degree 6°), and the artist who completes the pedagogical arc that Floyd Standifer began at degree 4°.

Seales earned his Doctor of Musical Arts from the University of Washington, where he has served on the faculty of the School of Music for decades, directing the jazz studies program. The UW is the Pacific Northwest’s flagship research university, and its jazz program under Seales has become the institutional pipeline for the next generation of Seattle jazz musicians.

Where Standifer (degree 4°) taught through doing — playing, demonstrating, transmitting through live performance — Seales teaches through a formalized academic structure: curriculum design, ensemble direction, theory courses, graduate advising, and the full apparatus of a university music department.

As a performer, Seales is a working musician whose piano spans post-bop, contemporary jazz, and the modal language that defines modern jazz piano. He has recorded for Naim Records, a UK-based audiophile label. His collaborations with Seattle musicians — including Julian Priester (degree 7°) — position him at the center of Seattle’s contemporary jazz network. Seales bridges performance and scholarship: his playing is informed by theory at the doctoral level, and his teaching is informed by decades of performance.

Modal Jazz & Post-Bop Harmony

Seales’s piano vocabulary is built on the modal jazz revolution that Miles Davis initiated with Kind of Blue (1959) and that Herbie Hancock, McCoy Tyner, Chick Corea, and Keith Jarrett developed into the standard language of contemporary jazz piano:

  • Modal scales as compositional basis: Dorian, Mixolydian, Lydian, Aeolian modes sustained for extended periods. Horizontal (scale-based) rather than vertical (chord-based) improvisation. A fundamentally different organizational principle from bebop’s chord-change navigation.
  • Quartal voicings: McCoy Tyner’s innovation — chords built from stacked fourths (C-F-Bb-Eb) rather than stacked thirds. Open, ambiguous, modern sound avoiding functional resolution. Now standard vocabulary.
  • Extended chord structures: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths as standard voicing components. Where Berry used triads and Bown used dense altered voicings, Seales builds textures with four or five distinct extensions.
  • Lydian and Lydian Dominant color: The raised fourth degree (#11) as the signature pitch of contemporary jazz harmony. George Russell’s Lydian Chromatic Concept (1953) as theoretical foundation; Davis and Coltrane as deployers; Seales’s generation as inheritors.
  • Academic integration: The DMA-level understanding connects performing knowledge (how Dorian feels under the hands) with scholarly knowledge (why Dorian produces its particular emotional quality, and Davis’s historical deployment on Kind of Blue).

Thirteenth chromaticism deployment: Modal chromaticism — chromatic alterations within and between modal frameworks as structural modal choices. The raised fourth (Lydian), the flatted seventh over major (Lydian Dominant), the Dorian natural sixth over minor. Not passing tones (bebop) or blue notes (blues) but defining modal pitches.

Institution-Dependency Spectrum (NEW at Degree 13°)

New analytical axis: the degree to which jazz knowledge transmission depends on an individual person versus an institutional structure.

Person-dependent pole: Brazil’s salon (degree 11°) — when Brazil dies, the salon dies. Knowledge survives only in students who were present.

Institution-dependent pole: Seales’s UW program (degree 13°) — curriculum, degree requirements, faculty positions, administrative structure that can persist beyond any individual teacher. The program generates its own successors through degree granting.

Standifer (4°) is in the middle: UW appointment provided institutional backing, but his teaching model relied on personal presence. Priester and Caliman (7°, 9°) are closer to Standifer’s position: institutional appointments at Cornish, but teaching primarily through performance credential.

Blue Note: Modal Integration

Intermediate

Thirteenth deployment. In modal jazz, the blue note undergoes a conceptual transformation: the b3, b7, and natural-6-over-minor that were blues inflections become defining pitches of the mode. In Dorian, the b3 and b7 ARE the mode — not departures from it. The blue note is absorbed into the scale itself. The most fully integrated deployment: from expressive gesture to structural identity.

Seales (modal blue note); Tyner (quartal voicings with blue-note positions); Hancock (“Maiden Voyage”); Davis (“So What” — Dorian b3 and b7 as mode)
Thirteen deployments: bass-line (0°), guitar (1°), vocal (3°), trumpet (4°), piano crush (5°), altered voicings (6°), trombone slide (7°), bass fundamental (8°), sax voice (9°), alto cool bend (10°), flute bent note (11°), bebop chromatic approach (12°), modal integration (13°).
