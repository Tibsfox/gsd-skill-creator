The Ellensburg Sound

Screaming Trees formed in Ellensburg, Washington in 1984 — not Seattle, not the coast. Ellensburg sits in the Kittitas Valley on the dry eastern side of the Cascades, 1,500 feet above sea level, 100 miles from Puget Sound via Snoqualmie Pass. This is the first time the engine crosses the Cascade Range. The brothers Gary Lee Conner (lead guitar) and Van Conner (bass) grew up here. Mark Lanegan (vocals) is from here. The drier, more isolated, slightly out-of-time quality of the Trees' sound has a geographic logic — they came from across the mountains and they sounded like it.

MARK LANEGAN — THE VOICE BENEATH THE ORDINARY REGISTER

Lanegan's baritone is the most distinctive voice in Pacific Northwest rock — deep, fractured, weighted with something that precedes the recording session. Not a technique. A condition. The voice carries authority that his contemporaries could not replicate. Born January 25, 1964, in Ellensburg. Died February 22, 2022, in Killarney, Ireland. Age 57. His post-Trees solo career — collaborations with Queens of the Stone Age, Greg Dulli (Gutter Twins), solo albums from Whiskey for the Holy Ghost (1994) through Blues Funeral (2012) — extended and deepened what the Trees began. The voice that defines degree 261 was still working when it was finally silenced.

SST Records — The Underground Bridge

Screaming Trees joined SST Records — Black Flag's label, the definitive American underground label of the 1980s — with Even If and Especially When (1987). SST's catalog mapped the American alternative underground before it became commercially visible: Black Flag, Husker Du, the Minutemen, Sonic Youth, Dinosaur Jr., Meat Puppets. To be on SST was to be in a specific conversation: serious, ambitious, commercially indifferent.

The Trees released three SST records: Even If and Especially When (1987), Invisible Lantern (1988), and Buzz Factory (1989). These records established the band's identity before grunge was a commercial category. They were formed on SST's ideas about independence. This pre-commercial formation is audible — they never sound like a band that discovered psychedelic rock because it was fashionable.

"The Trees were too psychedelic for hardcore, too heavy for conventional alternative rock, too anchored in the 1960s to be simply post-punk. This in-between position is precisely where their historical significance lies."

Epic Records — "Nearly Lost You"

Epic Records signed Screaming Trees in 1991. Sweet Oblivion (1992), produced by Don Fleming and Jack Endino, is the Trees at their commercial peak — songs fully formed, Lanegan's voice at its most compelling, production serving the music. "Nearly Lost You," from Cameron Crowe's 1992 Singles soundtrack, gave the Trees their widest audience: a classic piece of heavy psychedelic rock, Lanegan's voice against Gary Lee's ringing guitar figure. The Singles soundtrack placed them alongside Pearl Jam, Alice in Chains, Mudhoney, and Chris Cornell — a document of the Seattle scene at maximum commercial visibility.

Dust (1996, Epic) is generally considered the Trees' artistic peak — Barrett Martin's more complex drumming, Lanegan's deepened voice, post-grunge maturity. The grunge commercial moment was over by 1996. Dust does not chase it.

Energy = 7 — The Geophysics Arc Opens

After the Storm Arc's E=9 maximum (degree 260), the engine steps down to E=7. This is the opening of the GEOPHYSICS ARC in SPS — the underground forces, the tectonic machinery beneath the PNW landscape. E=7 is present and heavy; not the maximum, not restrained. Gary Lee Conner's guitar is full and dense; Lanegan's baritone has weight. The pairing with Mt. Rainier volcanic tremor is apt: both operate below the ordinary register — Lanegan's voice below the usual vocal range, volcanic tremor below the usual acoustic range.
