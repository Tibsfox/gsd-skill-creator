## ELLENSBURG ENTERS THE ENGINE

### The First Non-Coastal City

Degree 261 is a geographic milestone:

- **Ellensburg, Washington.** Ellensburg sits in the Kittitas Valley, on the eastern side of the Cascade Range, approximately 100 miles east of Seattle via Snoqualmie Pass (I-90). It is the county seat of Kittitas County. Population approximately 20,000. Home to Central Washington University. Elevation 1,500 feet. High desert climate -- hot dry summers, cold winters -- a stark contrast to the maritime Pacific Northwest coast.
- **Not Seattle. Not the coast.** Every S36 entry through degree 260 has been anchored to the Seattle metropolitan area or the Puget Sound corridor: Capitol Hill, U-District, Fremont, Belltown, the waterfront. Ellensburg breaks this. The engine crosses the Cascades for the first time.
- **The Conner brothers are from there.** Gary Lee Conner and Van Conner grew up in Ellensburg. This is not a band that moved to Seattle and later claimed its roots -- Screaming Trees were formed in Ellensburg in 1984 and the town is inseparable from the band's origin story. The band's sound -- heavy, slightly out-of-time, rooted in the psychedelic traditions of the 1960s -- makes a certain geographic sense in the isolation of the Kittitas Valley.
- **Central Washington University.** CWU in Ellensburg was a site of regional music activity. The band formed in this college-town context, surrounded by the relative isolation of eastern Washington's high desert rather than the dense urban music infrastructure of Seattle.
- **The Cascade crossing.** When Screaming Trees did engage with Seattle -- signing to SST, then being part of the grunge conversation -- they brought the east-side perspective. Their sound has a drier, more spacious quality than the damp maritime grunge of Mudhoney or Soundgarden. This is not an accident. Ellensburg's air is in the music.

(Sources: Ellensburg, Washington geography; Kittitas Valley; Central Washington University; Cascade Range crossing; Gary Lee Conner and Van Conner biography; engine geographic analysis)

## SCREAMING TREES -- THE BAND

### Personnel and Formation

Screaming Trees formed in Ellensburg in 1984:

- **Mark Lanegan (vocals).** The gravitational center of the band. Lanegan's voice is one of the most distinctive in American rock -- a deep, resonant baritone with a fractured, weathered quality. He sounds older than he is, always, as if the voice has been through events that preceded the recording session by decades. Born January 25, 1964, in Ellensburg. His vocal presence transforms Screaming Trees from a very good psychedelic rock band into something with genuine weight. Lanegan's voice is not a technique; it is a condition.
- **Gary Lee Conner (lead guitar).** The band's primary sonic architect. Gary Lee is the older Conner brother. His guitar playing synthesizes the late 1960s psychedelic rock tradition -- Hendrix, early Cream, the Kinks -- with the heavier post-punk approach of the 1980s underground. He has genuine melodic instinct. His guitar solos in the Trees are among the most underrated in Pacific Northwest rock.
- **Van Conner (bass).** The younger Conner brother. Van's bass playing is thick and driving -- not flashy, not technically ostentatious, but absolutely load-bearing. The low end of Screaming Trees is one of its defining characteristics. Van Conner also had a side project (Solomon Grundy) and was a devoted reader whose deep knowledge of literary and musical history informed the band's cultural context.
- **Mark Pickerel (drums, 1984-1992).** The original drummer through the core SST era. Pickerel's drumming is energetic and slightly propulsive -- he pushes the band forward rather than holding them back, which suits the Trees' forward-leaning psychedelia.
- **Barrett Martin (drums, 1992-2000).** Replaced Pickerel. Martin is a more jazz-influenced drummer, which contributed to the more complex rhythmic approach of the later-period Trees (Sweet Oblivion, Dust). Martin is also a multi-instrumentalist and has remained active in Pacific Northwest music.

(Sources: Screaming Trees personnel history; Mark Lanegan biography; Gary Lee Conner; Van Conner; Mark Pickerel; Barrett Martin; Ellensburg formation 1984)

## SST RECORDS -- THE INDEPENDENT ERA

### Black Flag's Label and the Underground Bridge

Screaming Trees' relationship with SST Records is one of the most historically significant in Pacific Northwest rock:

- **SST Records.** Founded by Greg Ginn of Black Flag in 1978, SST Records became the definitive independent rock label of the American underground in the 1980s. SST's catalog is a map of the American alternative rock landscape before it became commercially viable: Black Flag, Husker Du, the Minutemen, Sonic Youth, Dinosaur Jr., Meat Puppets, Firehose. To be on SST was to be part of a specific conversation -- serious, artistically ambitious, commercially indifferent.
- **Screaming Trees on SST.** The Trees released several records on SST: *Even If and Especially When* (1987), *Invisible Lantern* (1988), and *Buzz Factory* (1989). These records established the band's identity: heavy psychedelic rock with Lanegan's remarkable voice as the defining element, rooted in 1960s influences but executed with the intensity and directness of 1980s underground rock.
- **The SST aesthetic.** SST bands did not sound polished. The label's records had a specific sonic character: loud, present, slightly abrasive. The production was functional rather than luxurious. This suited the Trees well -- their music is not about studio shimmer but about performance energy captured on tape.
- **Bridge between eras.** SST's role in Screaming Trees' history is as a bridge: between the 1960s psychedelic traditions the band absorbed and the early 1990s grunge moment they would be part of. The Trees were formed on SST's ideas about independent rock before grunge existed as a commercial category. This pre-commercial formation is audible in their music -- they never sound like a band that discovered psychedelic rock because it was fashionable.
- **Simultaneously Epic.** By 1991, the grunge moment was commercially visible and Epic Records signed Screaming Trees. This is the SST-to-Epic arc: the full trajectory from underground independence to major-label presence. Few bands made this crossing while retaining artistic credibility as cleanly as the Trees did.

(Sources: SST Records history; Greg Ginn; Black Flag; SST catalog: Husker Du, Minutemen, Sonic Youth, Dinosaur Jr., Meat Puppets; Screaming Trees SST records: Even If and Especially When 1987, Invisible Lantern 1988, Buzz Factory 1989; Epic Records signing; SST aesthetic; underground to major-label crossing)

## EPIC RECORDS AND THE GRUNGE MOMENT

### Sweet Oblivion, Dust, and "Nearly Lost You"

The Epic period is the Screaming Trees that most people encountered:

- **Sweet Oblivion (1992, Epic).** The major-label debut. Produced by Don Fleming and Jack Endino (Jack Endino appears here -- and will appear again at degree 264 as Skin Yard's context). Sweet Oblivion is the Trees at their commercial peak: the songs are fully formed, Lanegan's voice is at its most compelling, the production serves the music rather than overwhelming it. The record received excellent reviews.
- **"Nearly Lost You."** The single from the Singles soundtrack (Cameron Crowe's 1992 Seattle-scene film). "Nearly Lost You" is the Screaming Trees song that reached the widest audience -- a classic piece of heavy psychedelic rock, Lanegan's voice against Gary Lee's ringing guitar figure. The Singles soundtrack also featured Pearl Jam, Alice in Chains, Mudhoney, Chris Cornell, and Paul Westerberg -- a document of the Seattle scene at the exact moment of maximum commercial visibility. The Trees were on it.
- **Dust (1996, Epic).** The final full Trees album. Generally considered their artistic peak -- more mature, more textured, Barrett Martin's drumming pushing the songs into more complex rhythmic territory. Lanegan's voice had deepened further. The grunge commercial moment was over by 1996, but Dust does not chase the moment. It exists outside commercial logic.
- **The grunge context.** Screaming Trees are technically contemporaries of Nirvana, Pearl Jam, and Soundgarden -- they existed in the same Seattle-adjacent music ecosystem. But they were never the commercial equals of those bands. "Nearly Lost You" gave them a moment of mainstream exposure, but they remained a critically acclaimed band with a devoted following rather than a platinum-selling phenomenon.
- **Why this matters.** The Trees' relative commercial underperformance relative to their artistic quality is a consistent pattern in the Seattle scene (see Love Battery at 221, No Anchor at 252, Treepeople at 263). The engine documents the full ecosystem, not just the platinum peaks.

(Sources: Sweet Oblivion 1992 Epic; Don Fleming production; Jack Endino co-production; Nearly Lost You; Singles soundtrack 1992; Cameron Crowe film; Dust 1996 Epic; Barrett Martin rhythmic contribution; grunge commercial context; artistic vs. commercial peak)

## MARK LANEGAN -- THE VOICE

### The Most Distinctive Vocalist in Pacific Northwest Rock

Mark Lanegan's voice deserves separate analysis:

- **The baritone.** Lanegan's voice is a deep baritone -- one of the deepest in rock music. This is unusual for a band that formed in the punk/alternative tradition, where tenors and high voices were more common. The baritone gives his singing an authority and weight that his contemporaries could not replicate.
- **The fracture.** What makes Lanegan's voice distinctive is not just the depth but the fracture: the sense that the voice has been worn, used, possibly damaged. There is a quality to it that suggests the voice is pushing through something -- through distance, through physical condition, through whatever lies between the singer and the words. This fracture is not a deficit; it is the primary emotional carrier in his vocal performances.
- **Post-Trees career.** After Screaming Trees dissolved in 2000, Lanegan had one of the most productive post-band careers in Seattle rock history. He released a series of critically acclaimed solo albums (*Whiskey for the Holy Ghost*, 1994; *Field Songs*, 2001; *Bubblegum*, 2004; *Blues Funeral*, 2012), collaborated extensively with Queens of the Stone Age (recording and touring), collaborated with Greg Dulli as the Gutter Twins, and appeared as a featured vocalist on albums by Moby, Isobel Campbell, and Mark Lanegan Band. He was, arguably, more prolific and more artistically wide-ranging as a solo artist than as a member of Screaming Trees.
- **Loss.** Mark Lanegan died on February 22, 2022, in Killarney, Ireland. He was 57. He had published two memoirs documenting his struggles with addiction and recovery (*Sing Backwards and Weep*, 2020; *Devil in a Coma*, 2021). His death removed one of the genuinely irreplaceable voices in American rock music.

(Sources: Mark Lanegan biography; baritone voice analysis; Whiskey for the Holy Ghost 1994; Field Songs 2001; Bubblegum 2004; Blues Funeral 2012; Queens of the Stone Age collaboration; Gutter Twins with Greg Dulli; death February 22 2022; memoirs Sing Backwards and Weep 2020; Devil in a Coma 2021)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Full Arc

- ***Other Worlds* (1985, Velvetone).** Self-released debut. Rough but present. The Ellensburg origin document.
- ***Clairvoyance* (1986, Velvetone).** Second self-release. The pre-SST proof of concept.
- ***Even If and Especially When* (1987, SST).** SST debut. The Trees' underground arrival. Heavy psychedelic rock, Lanegan's voice emerging as the primary instrument.
- ***Invisible Lantern* (1988, SST).** More confident than the debut. Gary Lee's guitar vocabulary expanding.
- ***Buzz Factory* (1989, SST / Sub Pop).** The final SST record. Arguably the most aggressive Trees album -- the punk energy at its highest.
- ***Uncle Anesthesia* (1991, Epic).** The first Epic record, produced by Terry Date and Chris Cornell. A transitional record.
- ***Sweet Oblivion* (1992, Epic).** The peak. "Nearly Lost You." Don Fleming and Jack Endino. The commercial moment, handled with integrity.
- ***Dust* (1996, Epic).** The artistic peak. Barrett Martin's complex drumming. The post-grunge statement that transcends its moment.

(Sources: Full Screaming Trees discography; Velvetone self-releases; SST Records releases; Epic Records releases; production credits)

## LEGACY

### Screaming Trees' Place in Pacific Northwest Rock

- **The bridge band.** Screaming Trees sat between the SST underground and the grunge mainstream without fully belonging to either. They were too psychedelic for hardcore, too heavy for conventional alternative rock, too anchored in the 1960s to be simply post-punk. This in-between position is precisely where their historical significance lies.
- **Lanegan's voice as the irreplaceable element.** Any assessment of Screaming Trees must center on Lanegan. The band without him would have been competent; with him, they were irreplaceable. The voice carries a weight and specificity that makes the Trees' best records feel like documents of something particular rather than genre exercises.
- **Ellensburg as source.** The Trees' origin in Ellensburg is not incidental. The drier, more isolated, slightly out-of-step quality of their music has a geographic logic. They came from across the mountains and they sounded like it.
- **The post-Trees trajectory.** Lanegan's solo career extended and deepened the Trees' artistic legacy. The voice that defined the band did not stop working when the band dissolved.

(Sources: Screaming Trees legacy analysis; bridge band position between SST underground and grunge mainstream; Lanegan voice as irreplaceable; Ellensburg geographic significance; post-Trees Lanegan career)
