## STRUCTURAL EVENT: Portland Returns -- First Portland-Primary Artist Since Laura Gibson (57)

Degree 61 returns to **Portland** -- the first Portland-primary artist since Laura Gibson at degree 57. Four degrees elapsed. The Capitol Hill three-peat (58-59-60) is broken. Portland re-enters the engine through a fundamentally different artist than its first representative: where Gibson was a literary folk singer from the MFA tradition, Y La Bamba is a bilingual folk-indie project rooted in the Latinx experience and the Willamette Valley's agricultural communities.

Portland's second appearance in the engine reveals something about the city's independent music infrastructure. Portland and Seattle are not interchangeable. They are sibling cities on the I-5 corridor -- related, connected, but culturally distinct. Seattle's indie music infrastructure runs through Sub Pop, Barsuk, KEXP, Capitol Hill. Portland's runs through Tender Loving Empire, Kill Rock Stars, Mississippi Studios, Alberta Street. The labels are different. The venues are different. The neighborhoods are different. The culture is different. But the corridor is shared -- artists move between the two cities, play each other's venues, appear on each other's radio stations, and the I-5 highway that connects them functions as a cultural artery.

The "Portland to Seattle" designation in the CSV captures this corridor identity. Y La Bamba is Portland-based but not Portland-confined. The project tours the Pacific Northwest, plays KEXP sessions in Seattle, shares festival bills with Seattle artists. The I-5 corridor is not a boundary; it is a connector. Degree 61 maps a different kind of relationship to place than the Capitol Hill degrees (58-59-60): not the density of a single neighborhood but the length of a regional corridor.

Portland's reappearance after four degrees also reactivates the territorial acknowledgment for the Portland corridor. Y La Bamba operates on the ancestral territory of the Multnomah, Kathlamet, Clackamas, Chinook, and Tualatin Kalapuya peoples. Mendoza's own family history as migrant farmworkers in the Willamette Valley adds another dimension to the land question -- the indigenous peoples who were displaced, and the migrant laborers who work the land that displacement made available for agriculture.

## STRUCTURAL EVENT: First Latina/Mexican-American Artist -- First Bilingual Artist

Degree 61 introduces the engine's **first Latina and Mexican-American artist** and the engine's **first bilingual artist**. Luz Elena Mendoza sings in both English and Spanish. Her lyrics move between languages within songs, between songs within albums, and across her entire catalog. This is not translation or accommodation -- it is the natural expression of a bilingual mind, a person who thinks and feels in two languages and whose art reflects that cognitive reality.

The significance for the engine is structural. Through sixty degrees, the Seattle 360 engine has documented artists working exclusively in English. The engine has tracked genre diversity (Folk, Indie Rock, Electronic, Hip-Hop, R&B, Punk), energy diversity (E=1 through E=6), gender diversity (17:44 ratio at degree 60), and cultural diversity (Chong the Nomad at degree 44 as the first Filipino-American artist). But linguistic diversity has been zero. Every artist through degree 60 has performed in English. Degree 61 breaks that pattern.

Mendoza's bilingualism is inseparable from her biography. She was raised in a Spanish-speaking household in a migrant farmworker family. English was the language of school and the broader American culture; Spanish was the language of home, family, and community. The bilingual lyrics are not an artistic choice imposed on the music from outside -- they are the direct expression of a life lived between languages, between cultures, between the agricultural Willamette Valley and the indie-rock Portland, between the migrant experience and the artistic community.

The cross-reference to Chong the Nomad (degree 44) is direct. Degree 44 introduced the first Filipino-American artist, expanding the engine's cultural diversity beyond the predominantly white indie rock mainstream. Degree 61 continues that expansion with the first Latina artist. The pattern is slow but real: the engine's representation broadens as it progresses. Seventeen degrees separate the two cultural diversity milestones (44 to 61). The pace matters -- it reflects the actual pace of diversification in the Pacific Northwest's indie music scene, where structural barriers to entry for artists of color have been real and persistent.

Mendoza's background as the daughter of migrant farmworkers is not incidental to her music -- it is the foundation. The farmworker experience is present in the music's themes (home, displacement, family, land, labor), in its sounds (Latin rhythms woven into folk structures, the warmth of Spanish vowels against English consonants), and in its politics (the quiet assertion that Latinx stories belong in indie music spaces that have historically been white). Y La Bamba does not perform diversity; Y La Bamba is diversity -- the natural product of a life lived across cultural boundaries.

## STRUCTURAL EVENT: Folk/Indie Hybrid -- Genre Stage 43

Degree 61 assigns **Folk/Indie** at **Genre Stage 43**. This is neither pure Folk (like Gibbard at degree 60 or Gibson at degree 57) nor pure Indie Rock (like Death Cab at degree 58 or Long Winters at degree 59). It is a hybrid classification -- the engine's recognition that Y La Bamba's sound does not fit neatly into a single genre box.

Y La Bamba's genre hybridity is a product of Mendoza's influences and artistic intent. The folk element comes from the acoustic guitar foundations, the singer-songwriter tradition, the storytelling impulse, the intimacy of the vocal delivery. The indie element comes from the experimental arrangements, the layered production, the non-traditional song structures, the willingness to incorporate noise, distortion, and electronic textures alongside acoustic instruments. And underneath both, there is a Latin element -- rhythmic patterns, melodic contours, and vocal inflections drawn from Mexican folk music, cumbia, ranchera, and other Latin American traditions -- that the Folk/Indie label only partially captures.

Genre Stage 43 follows Folk at Genre Stage 42 (Gibbard, degree 60). The genre has shifted from pure Folk to Folk/Indie -- a lateral move rather than a complete genre change. The folk lineage continues through the hybrid. The folk-adjacent pattern that dominated degrees 45-57 (the thirteen-degree folk-adjacent tridecad) and reappeared at degree 60 is still active at degree 61, but it has absorbed the indie element and -- crucially -- the Latin element. The folk zone is expanding its definition. What began as Americana folk (Damien Jurado, degree 45) has now encompassed literary folk (Laura Gibson, 57), stripped singer-songwriter folk (Ben Gibbard solo, 60), and now bilingual Latinx folk-indie (Y La Bamba, 61). The folk tradition in the engine is not one thing -- it is a family of related approaches to intimate, song-centered music-making.

The hybrid classification also connects to the engine's genre trajectory. After the Indie Rock two-peat (58-59), Folk returned at degree 60, and now Folk/Indie at degree 61 blends the two streams that had been alternating. The slash in "Folk/Indie" is a bridge between genre territories that the engine had been treating as separate.

## STRUCTURAL EVENT: Tender Loving Empire -- A New Label Enters

Degree 61 introduces **Tender Loving Empire** -- a Portland-based independent label and retail store, founded in 2007. First appearance in the engine. After the Barsuk trio (57-58-59) and the self-released degree (60), a new label enters.

Tender Loving Empire is a distinctly Portland institution. Founded by Jared and Brianne Mees, TLE operates both as a record label and as a chain of retail stores in Portland selling locally made goods -- music, art, crafts, food products. The label-and-shop model is a Portland archetype: the independent business that blurs the line between commerce and community, between selling records and building culture.

The label's aesthetic is eclectic but warm -- folk, indie pop, singer-songwriter, experimental. Y La Bamba fits naturally. The label provides the infrastructure that Mendoza's music needs: recording support, distribution, promotion within the Pacific Northwest circuit. TLE is to Portland what Barsuk was to Seattle -- a label that curates a scene, that identifies artists who share an aesthetic sensibility, that provides the institutional support that independent artists need to develop and sustain careers.

The label progression through the engine tells a story of independent music infrastructure: Sub Pop (the landmark), Barsuk (the curator), Kill Rock Stars (the punk feminist institution), self-released (the post-label condition), and now Tender Loving Empire (the community-minded Portland model). Each label represents a different approach to the business of independent music. Tender Loving Empire's approach is the most community-oriented -- the label as local business, the record store as gathering place, the artist roster as neighborhood.

## Duwamish, Coast Salish, and Willamette Valley Territorial Acknowledgment

This research documents music mapped to the Portland-Seattle corridor. Y La Bamba operates primarily in Portland, Oregon, on the ancestral territory of the **Multnomah, Kathlamet, Clackamas, Chinook, and Tualatin Kalapuya** peoples. Luz Elena Mendoza grew up in the Willamette Valley, which is the ancestral territory of the **Kalapuya** peoples -- including the Tualatin Kalapuya, Yamhill Kalapuya, and other bands who were removed to the Grand Ronde Reservation after the Kalapuya Treaty of 1855.

The Willamette Valley dimension adds a specific layer to this acknowledgment. Mendoza's family came to the valley as migrant farmworkers -- laboring on land that was taken from indigenous peoples and converted to agriculture. The farmworker experience and the indigenous displacement are connected through the land itself. The agricultural economy that employed Mendoza's family exists because the Kalapuya were removed from the valley. This is not a moral equivalence -- the migrant farmworker experience and the indigenous experience are distinct -- but they are layered on the same ground.

This acknowledgment was established at degree 20, extended at degree 52, extended for the Portland corridor at degree 57, and is now deepened with the Willamette Valley dimension. Applied to all degrees.

## Biography: Luz Elena Mendoza and the Migrant Farmworker Origin

### From the Willamette Valley to Portland

**Luz Elena Mendoza** is the heart of Y La Bamba -- the songwriter, lead vocalist, and creative director of the project. Mendoza was born to Mexican immigrant parents in the Willamette Valley, Oregon, and raised in a family of migrant farmworkers. The Willamette Valley is one of Oregon's primary agricultural regions -- a flat, fertile stretch of land between the Coast Range and the Cascades where crops are harvested by laborers who follow the growing season from field to field.

Mendoza's childhood was shaped by this migrant reality. The family moved with the work. Home was not a fixed point but a series of temporary locations determined by which crops needed harvesting. Spanish was the language of home; English was the language of school and the broader culture. Music was present in both worlds -- Mexican folk music at home, American pop and rock on the radio, church music in the Catholic parishes where many farmworker families worshipped.

The path from the Willamette Valley to Portland's indie music scene was not linear. It was a leap across class boundaries, cultural boundaries, and geographic boundaries. The Willamette Valley's agricultural communities and Portland's indie music community do not naturally overlap. Mendoza bridged them -- bringing the musical traditions of her family into the indie spaces of Portland, and bringing the experimental energy of Portland's indie scene into contact with Latin rhythms and Spanish lyrics.

This bridge-building is Y La Bamba's defining quality. The project is not Mexican folk music and not Portland indie rock. It is the space between them -- the hybrid that emerges when a person who carries both traditions in their body creates music that honors both without choosing one over the other.

### The Name

"Y La Bamba" translates roughly to "And the Bamba" -- referencing "La Bamba," the traditional Mexican folk song made famous by Ritchie Valens in 1958. The "Y" (and) is the key: it is additive, connective. Not just La Bamba -- the Mexican tradition -- but *and* La Bamba. Something plus the tradition. The name itself is a hybrid marker -- the conjunction that connects two things.

### Y La Bamba as a Project

Y La Bamba is a project rather than a fixed band. Mendoza is the constant; the supporting musicians rotate. The project has included various configurations of guitar, bass, drums, keys, violin, trumpet, and other instruments. This fluidity allows Mendoza to shape the sound to each album's needs rather than being constrained by a fixed lineup. The project model is common in the folk-indie world -- Bon Iver, Sufjan Stevens, Iron & Wine all operate as projects centered on a single songwriter with rotating collaborators.

## The Bilingual Dimension: English, Spanish, and the Code-Switch

### Two Languages, One Voice

Y La Bamba's bilingualism is not a gimmick or a marketing strategy. It is the natural expression of a person who moves between English and Spanish in daily life. Mendoza's lyrics switch between languages within songs -- sometimes mid-verse, sometimes mid-line. The switching is fluid, unpredictable, governed by which language best expresses the idea or emotion at that moment rather than by any formal structure.

This code-switching is linguistically and culturally significant. In sociolinguistics, code-switching refers to the practice of alternating between two or more languages within a single conversation or utterance. It is a natural feature of bilingual speech -- not a failure to master either language but an expression of mastery of both. The bilingual speaker has a larger palette of expression than the monolingual speaker; the code-switch deploys whichever language carries the right shade of meaning.

In Y La Bamba's music, the code-switch does several things simultaneously:

1. **It expands the emotional palette.** Some emotions are better expressed in Spanish -- words like *corazon* (heart), *alma* (soul), *duele* (it hurts) carry cultural and phonetic weight that their English equivalents cannot replicate. Other emotions are better expressed in English. The bilingual songwriter can choose the better tool for each moment.

2. **It marks cultural territory.** When Mendoza switches to Spanish, she signals belonging to a community that the English-dominant indie world often overlooks. The Spanish lyrics assert: *this music comes from somewhere specific, from a culture with its own language, its own traditions, its own way of being in the world.*

3. **It creates intimacy and distance simultaneously.** For bilingual listeners, the code-switch creates intimacy -- the recognition of a shared linguistic experience. For monolingual English listeners, the Spanish lyrics create productive unfamiliarity -- a reminder that the world is larger than English, that there are experiences and emotions that exist outside the monolingual listener's frame.

4. **It mirrors the bicultural experience.** Living between two cultures means living between two languages. The code-switch in the music mirrors the code-switch in daily life -- the constant negotiation of identity that bilingual, bicultural people navigate.

### Bilingualism in the Engine Context

Through sixty degrees, the Seattle 360 engine has been monolingual -- every artist performing in English. This is not surprising; it reflects the linguistic reality of the Pacific Northwest's indie music scene, which operates primarily in English. But the monolingual default has been invisible -- it has not been noted or analyzed because it appeared to be the only option.

Degree 61 makes the monolingual default visible by breaking it. Y La Bamba's bilingualism is the first departure from English-only performance in the engine. The break is gentle -- Mendoza sings mostly in English, with Spanish woven in -- but it is real. The engine now includes a bilingual artist, and the previous sixty degrees of English-only performance are revealed, retroactively, as a pattern rather than a given.

(Sources: NPR Music -- Mendoza on bilingual songwriting; Pitchfork -- Y La Bamba interviews on language; sociolinguistic literature on code-switching; engine internal analysis)

## Discography: Three Albums, One Trajectory

### Court the Storm (2012)

***Court the Storm*** was Y La Bamba's breakout album, released on Tender Loving Empire in 2012 -- the same year as Gibbard's *Former Lives* (degree 60). The album announced Y La Bamba to a wider audience with its blend of folk, rock, and Latin influences. The sound is warmer and more expansive than Mendoza's earlier recordings, reflecting the project's evolution from solo bedroom recordings to a full-band production.

The album's title suggests confrontation with difficulty -- courting the storm rather than sheltering from it. The songs address identity, family, belonging, and the negotiation of cultural space. The production is organic -- acoustic guitars, percussion, layered vocals -- but not austere. There is warmth in the arrangements, a generosity of texture that reflects Mendoza's communal approach to music-making.

Key tracks include songs that move between English and Spanish, establishing the bilingual pattern that would deepen on subsequent albums. The critical reception was positive, with particular attention to Mendoza's voice -- a rich, flexible instrument that can whisper and shout, that carries the warmth of Mexican vocal traditions and the edge of indie rock.

### Ojos Del Sol (2016)

***Ojos Del Sol*** ("Eyes of the Sun") deepened Y La Bamba's exploration of identity and cultural hybridity. The album is more experimental than *Court the Storm* -- more electronic textures, more complex arrangements, more willingness to break conventional song structures. The Spanish-language presence is stronger, reflecting Mendoza's growing confidence in centering her Mexican-American identity rather than accommodating it to indie-rock norms.

The album title is entirely in Spanish -- a statement of intent. Where *Court the Storm* was an English title that contained Spanish lyrics, *Ojos Del Sol* leads with Spanish. The shift is gradual but clear: Y La Bamba is moving toward the center of its bilingual identity rather than approaching it from the English-language periphery.

The songs on *Ojos Del Sol* deal with family, ancestry, the farmworker experience, and the psychic cost of living between cultures. Mendoza has spoken in interviews about the album as an excavation of her roots -- a return to the music she heard as a child in the Willamette Valley, the Mexican folk traditions that were the soundtrack of home, and an integration of those sounds with the indie vocabulary she developed in Portland.

### Mujeres (2019)

***Mujeres*** ("Women") is Y La Bamba's most fully realized album -- the culmination of a trajectory that began with the folk-leaning *Court the Storm* and moved through the experimental *Ojos Del Sol*. The album is a meditation on womanhood, on the women in Mendoza's life -- her mother, her grandmothers, her aunts, the women of the farmworker community who carried families through impossible conditions.

The album is also the most sonically diverse of the three -- folk songs sit alongside synth-driven tracks, acoustic intimacy next to full-band power, whispered confessions next to shouted declarations. The bilingual lyrics are fully integrated, with no sense that one language is the default and the other the exception. English and Spanish coexist as equal partners.

*Mujeres* is the album that makes the case for Y La Bamba as a major voice in Pacific Northwest indie music. It is personal, political, culturally specific, and musically ambitious. It is the sound of an artist who has found her center -- the place where the farmworker daughter and the indie musician are the same person.

(Sources: AllMusic -- Y La Bamba discography; Pitchfork -- *Court the Storm*, *Ojos Del Sol*, *Mujeres* reviews; NPR Music -- Y La Bamba album features; Tender Loving Empire -- release notes)

## The Latin Element: Folk Expanded

### Beyond the Anglo-American Folk Tradition

The folk tradition in the Seattle 360 engine, through degree 60, has been an Anglo-American tradition. The folk artists -- Damien Jurado (45), Laura Gibson (57), Ben Gibbard solo (60), and the various folk-adjacent artists in between -- work within the American folk-singer-songwriter lineage that runs from Woody Guthrie through Bob Dylan through Elliott Smith. It is a tradition of acoustic guitar, English lyrics, verse-chorus-verse structures, and confessional or narrative storytelling.

Y La Bamba expands this tradition. Mendoza's folk draws from two wells: the American singer-songwriter tradition *and* the Mexican folk tradition. The Mexican folk element includes ranchera's emotional directness, cumbia's rhythmic patterns, the corrido's narrative function, and the son jarocho's communal performance practice. These are not ornamental additions to an American folk base; they are structural elements that shape the music's rhythm, melody, and emotional register.

The expansion matters for the engine's folk zone. What began as a single-tradition genre category has become a multi-tradition space:

- **Degrees 45-54**: Americana folk, Pacific Northwest folk -- the Jurado-to-Votolato lineage. Anglo-American tradition.
- **Degree 55-57**: Singer-songwriter folk -- Earl, Lemolo, Gibson. Women's voices. Literary tradition. Still Anglo-American.
- **Degree 60**: Stripped folk -- Gibbard solo. The folk skeleton inside the rock body. Anglo-American.
- **Degree 61**: Bilingual folk-indie -- Y La Bamba. Latin traditions woven into the folk base. **The folk zone's first non-Anglo-American tradition.**

The folk zone is becoming more capacious. It is no longer one thing. It is a family of approaches to intimate, song-centered music-making that now includes traditions from both sides of the border.

### The Willamette Valley as Musical Geography

Mendoza's Willamette Valley origin connects Y La Bamba to a specific geographic and cultural landscape. The Willamette Valley is not Portland. It is the agricultural hinterland that surrounds Portland -- the flat farmland where berries, hops, wine grapes, nursery stock, and Christmas trees are grown by laborers who are, disproportionately, Mexican and Central American immigrants. The valley is the invisible infrastructure that supports Portland's farm-to-table culture -- the fields that produce the ingredients that Portland's restaurants celebrate.

Mendoza grew up in those fields. Her music carries the valley with it -- not as pastoral imagery but as lived experience. The Willamette Valley in Y La Bamba's music is a place of labor, community, family obligation, and cultural negotiation. It is the place where Mendoza's Mexican identity was formed and where her American identity began. The valley is the origin; Portland is the destination. The music exists in the corridor between them.

(Sources: Mendoza interviews on Willamette Valley upbringing; Oregon agricultural labor documentation; engine internal analysis)

## The Sixty-Second Acoustic Element: The Braided Voice

### Acoustic Progression: Sixty-Two Elements

The sixty-second acoustic element is **the braided voice** -- the acoustic quality produced when a singer weaves two languages into a single vocal line, creating a texture that is neither monolingual English nor monolingual Spanish but a third thing: a bilingual braid that carries the warmth, rhythm, and cultural weight of both languages simultaneously. The braided voice is audible in Y La Bamba's recordings when Mendoza switches between English and Spanish within a phrase -- the vowels of Spanish lending warmth to the consonant-heavy English, the precision of English lending structure to the flowing Spanish. Two threads woven into one cord.

The braided voice is distinct from other vocal textures in the engine's acoustic taxonomy:

- **The bare wire** (degree 60, Gibbard solo) is a single strand -- one voice, one language, stripped of arrangement. The braided voice is two strands twisted together -- two languages carrying two cultural traditions in one vocal line.
- **The manuscript page** (degree 57, Laura Gibson) is literary control in one language. The braided voice is literary control in two languages -- the complexity of code-switching as a compositional technique.
- **The ache** (degree 58, Death Cab) is emotional precision in English. The braided voice is emotional precision in two languages -- sometimes the English word is more precise, sometimes the Spanish word is, and the singer knows which.
- **The murmur** (degree 45, Damien Jurado) is whispered dissolution in English. The braided voice is whispered communion in two languages -- the intimacy of speaking the listener's home language.
- **The grain** (degree 46, Pickwick) is texture without words. The braided voice is texture *through* words -- the grain produced by the friction between two language systems in one throat.

The braided voice operates at E=3 -- warmer and more present than the bare wire's E=2, but not as amplified as the ache's E=4. The warmth comes from the Latin vowels, from the rhythmic patterns that Spanish introduces, from the communal tradition of Mexican folk music where singing is a shared activity rather than a solo confession. The braided voice is a communal element in a tradition (indie folk) that has been largely solitary.

## The Sixty-Second Artist-City Pattern: The Corridor Bridge

### The I-5 Migration as Cultural Connection

The sixty-second artist-city pattern is **the corridor bridge** -- the pattern of an artist whose geographic identity is not a single city but a corridor between cities, connecting the cultural infrastructure of one place to another. Y La Bamba bridges Portland and Seattle through the I-5 corridor, but more fundamentally, the project bridges the Willamette Valley's agricultural Latinx community to Portland's indie music community. The corridor is not just geographic; it is cultural, linguistic, and economic.

The corridor bridge operates at multiple scales:

1. **The Willamette Valley to Portland**: The farmworker-to-artist trajectory. Mendoza's journey from the agricultural communities of the valley to the indie music scene of Portland. The bridge between labor and art, between the fields and the stage.

2. **Portland to Seattle**: The I-5 corridor. The regional infrastructure that connects two cities' independent music ecosystems. Y La Bamba plays both cities, appears on both cities' media, benefits from both cities' venue networks.

3. **Mexico to the Pacific Northwest**: The transnational corridor. Mendoza's family's migration from Mexico to Oregon. The cultural bridge between Mexican folk traditions and American indie rock.

4. **Spanish to English**: The linguistic corridor. The code-switch as a bridge between language communities, between ways of thinking and feeling.

The corridor bridge is distinct from other artist-city patterns:

- **The frontman alone** (degree 60, Gibbard solo) operates within a single neighborhood. The corridor bridge operates between cities and cultures.
- **The scene intellectual** (degree 59, Long Winters) maps a single scene. The corridor bridge connects multiple scenes.
- **The Bellingham Ascent** (degree 58, Death Cab) is a one-directional migration. The corridor bridge is bidirectional -- the artist moves back and forth, connects rather than departs.
- **The manuscript page on the trail** (degree 57, Laura Gibson) is Portland-specific. The corridor bridge extends beyond Portland to encompass the region.

## The Sixty-First Blue Note: The Untranslatable Ache

The sixty-first blue note deployment is **the untranslatable ache** -- the blue note that sounds when an emotion exists in one language but not the other, when the singer reaches for the Spanish word because the English word is inadequate, or vice versa. The untranslatable ache is the gap between languages -- the moment when the code-switch reveals that some experiences resist translation, that some feelings have a home in only one language.

Y La Bamba's music contains these moments. A Spanish phrase that has no English equivalent carries an emotional weight that the monolingual listener can feel even without understanding the words. The sound of the Spanish -- its vowels, its rhythm, its cadence -- communicates something that translation would flatten. The blue note plays in the gap between what the words mean and what the sound communicates.

The untranslatable ache is distinct from prior blue notes:

- **The unaccompanied confession** (degree 60, Gibbard solo) is the vulnerability of performing without the band. The untranslatable ache is the vulnerability of performing between languages -- the risk that neither language community will fully claim you.
- **The incomplete catalog** (degree 59, Long Winters) is the silence after the work. The untranslatable ache is the silence between languages -- the moment of reaching for the word that does not exist in the current language.
- **The settling sound** (degree 58, Death Cab) is the acceptance of limitation. The untranslatable ache is the refusal to accept a single language's limitations -- the code-switch as expansion rather than acceptance.
