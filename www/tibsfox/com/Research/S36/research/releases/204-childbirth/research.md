## THE SUPERGROUP -- NETWORK MADE EXPLICIT

### Members From the Network

Childbirth is the explicit expression of what degrees 201-203 implied:

- **Julia Shapiro.** Vocals/guitar. From Chastity Belt (degree 201). Shapiro brings the deadpan delivery to a faster, louder context. In Childbirth, the restraint loosens -- she's louder, more direct, more confrontational.
- **Bree McKenna.** Bass. From Tacocat (degree 203). McKenna bridges Tacocat's pop-punk catchiness into Childbirth's rawer context. The bass is faster, more aggressive.
- **Stacy Peck.** Drums. From Pony Time. Peck adds aggressive, driving drums that push Childbirth faster and louder than either Chastity Belt or Tacocat.
- **The supergroup principle.** Childbirth is greater than the sum of its parts precisely because each member brings a different energy from their primary band. Shapiro's restraint + McKenna's catchiness + Peck's aggression = something none of the source bands could achieve alone.

## CHILDBIRTH -- THE SOUND

### Faster, Louder, More Direct

Childbirth strips away the restraints of its members' primary bands:

- **Speed.** Faster than Chastity Belt. Faster than Tacocat. Childbirth songs are compressed -- 90 seconds to 2 minutes. The punk economy is absolute.
- **Volume.** Louder than Chastity Belt's deliberate looseness. Louder than Tacocat's pop-punk brightness. Childbirth's distortion is heavier, dirtier, more garage-influenced.
- **Subject matter.** The feminist subjects continue but the delivery is more confrontational. Where Chastity Belt shrugs and Tacocat laughs, Childbirth shouts. The supergroup energy permits directness that the individual bands' aesthetics might soften.
- **DIY aesthetic.** Childbirth's recordings are rawer and more lo-fi than their members' primary bands. The production matches the energy: minimal, immediate, un-fussy. This is the basement tape version of the Capitol Hill feminist network.
- **Live energy.** Childbirth is a live band above all. The shows are fast, loud, sweaty, and over quickly. The recorded material captures maybe 70% of the live intensity. The other 30% is in the room.

(Sources: Childbirth discography; live performance; production aesthetic; DIY punk context)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Childbirth

- **Tempo.** Very fast. 160-200 BPM. Punk tempos at the extreme end. Songs that barely pause.
- **Guitar.** Heavy distortion. Power chords. Garage-punk tone -- dirtier than Tacocat's brighter distortion. The guitar is a wall, not a melody.
- **Bass.** Fast, distorted, following the guitar. McKenna's bass is more aggressive here than in Tacocat.
- **Vocals.** Shapiro shouting. Not the deadpan of Chastity Belt. Not the pop delivery of Tacocat. Childbirth's vocal approach is the most direct and confrontational in the Hardly Art streak. Lyrics are shouted over distortion. Clarity is secondary to intensity.
- **Drums.** Peck's drumming is the engine -- fast, aggressive, driving. The drums push the other instruments forward. Fill-free. Groove-free. Pure forward motion.
- **Production.** Lo-fi. Rough. Basement-quality by design. The rawness is the aesthetic. This is not polished pop-punk -- this is DIY punk with feminist politics.
- **Song length.** Very short. 1:00-2:30 minutes. Some songs barely reach 90 seconds. Maximum compression.

(Sources: Childbirth discography; garage punk production; lo-fi aesthetic analysis)
