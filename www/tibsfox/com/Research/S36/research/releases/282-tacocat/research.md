## THE PALINDROME

### What's In a Name

Tacocat is a palindrome -- it reads the same forwards and backwards. This is not accidental:

- **The choice as aesthetic statement.** A band that names itself after a palindrome is announcing something about its sensibility: it is playful, it is attentive to language, it likes a clever reversal. The palindrome name is the first signal that this is a band operating in the register of wit -- where humor and precision coexist with genuine punk energy.
- **The palindrome as structure.** A palindrome has structural symmetry. Tacocat's music shares this quality: hook-driven pop structures that feel complete and balanced, songs that go somewhere and return, energy that rises and resolves. The name is an aesthetic blueprint.
- **The taco component.** Tacos are mundane, beloved, and slightly ridiculous -- exactly the register of a lot of Tacocat's subject matter. The band embraces the everyday without irony: cats, tacos, periods, skateboarders, the Lyft driver. The specificity of the mundane is the band's subject matter.
- **The cultural moment.** Tacocat formed in 2007, before the internet fully colonized palindrome humor. The name is genuinely clever rather than knowing-clever. By the time the internet had exhausted palindrome jokes, Tacocat had already built a body of work that transcended the joke.

(Sources: Tacocat palindrome name; aesthetic implications; structural symmetry; everyday subject matter; formation 2007)

## TACOCAT -- THE BAND

### Emily Nokes, Bree McKenna, Eric Randall, Lelah Maupin

- **Emily Nokes (vocals/guitar).** The front person and voice of Tacocat. Nokes' vocal delivery is warm and direct -- not aggressive, not precious. Her range covers the full arc of the band's emotional material: from the wry observation to the genuine celebration. She is the primary lyricist, and the lyrics are what make Tacocat distinctive. "Bridge to Hawaii," "Dana Katherine Scully," "Crimson Wave" -- songs with a specific point of view, specific observations, specific humor.
- **Bree McKenna (bass).** McKenna is the bass player and co-founder of the Childbirth supergroup (degree 283), where she appears alongside Julia Shapiro. Her bass in Tacocat drives the punk pop momentum. In a band where the guitars and vocals carry the melodic weight, McKenna's bass is the propulsive foundation.
- **Eric Randall (guitar).** The guitar in Tacocat is bright, clean, and hook-forward. Not distorted punk guitar -- more power pop, more Buzzcocks than Black Flag. The guitar provides the melodic scaffold for Nokes' vocals and the songs' hooks.
- **Lelah Maupin (drums).** Maupin's drumming is energetic and precise, keeping the band's brisk tempos consistent without overplaying. In punk pop, the drums are the energy delivery system -- Maupin delivers.
- **The chemistry.** Tacocat has been together in varying forms since 2007. The chemistry of the four-piece is the product of long collaboration: the songs fit the band, the band fits the songs, and the sound is immediately recognizable.

(Sources: Emily Nokes biography and lyrics; Bree McKenna; Eric Randall; Lelah Maupin; band chemistry and longevity)

## HARDLY ART: SUB POP'S SUBSIDIARY

### The Institutional Connection

Hardly Art is one of the most significant institutional connections in the engine's Capitol Hill chapter:

- **Founded 2007.** Hardly Art was founded as a subsidiary of Sub Pop Records in 2007. Sub Pop has been a presence in the engine since the early degrees -- its grunge roster (Mudhoney, Nirvana, Soundgarden, TAD) established the label's identity in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Hardly Art represents Sub Pop's deliberate expansion into a different sonic territory: indie pop, punk pop, and Pacific Northwest acts that didn't fit Sub Pop's grunge-rooted main catalog.
- **The subsidiary model.** Sub Pop's model for Hardly Art is the major-label subsidiary approach applied to independent music: a parent label with resources and distribution, a subsidiary with a distinct curatorial identity and aesthetic. Sub Pop provides infrastructure; Hardly Art provides taste. The bands on Hardly Art are connected to Sub Pop's network but exist in their own sonic space.
- **The Hardly Art roster.** Tacocat, Childbirth (degree 283), Chastity Belt, La Luz, King Tuff, The Intelligence. The Hardly Art aesthetic is consistent: indie pop, Pacific Northwest, often with feminist or politically aware sensibilities, often with a sense of humor. The label is the commercial home of a specific moment in Seattle independent music.
- **Sub Pop enters again.** Sub Pop has appeared in the engine through its main roster bands. Hardly Art as the first subsidiary entry expands Sub Pop's representation in the engine beyond its grunge identity. The label's range -- from Mudhoney (degree circa 100) to Tacocat (282) via Hardly Art -- documents thirty years of Seattle independent music institutional evolution.
- **The Capitol Hill location.** Hardly Art operates from Seattle, and its roster reflects the Capitol Hill indie scene of the 2000s-2010s: the neighborhood that was also home to Tacocat's formation and to the Childbirth supergroup members.

(Sources: Hardly Art founding 2007; Sub Pop subsidiary structure; Hardly Art roster; Sub Pop engine presence history; Capitol Hill indie scene 2000s-2010s; institutional evolution)

## THE SOUND -- PUNK POP, E=7

### Hook-Driven, Warm, Feminist

Tacocat's sound sits at a specific coordinate: punk energy channeled into pop structure, with feminist content and a genuine sense of humor:

- **E=7 calibration.** Above Wimps' E=6 -- Tacocat hits harder, hooks more insistently, brings more melodic brightness. Below the riot grrrl ferocity of Bikini Kill's E=8. Punk pop: the punk is in the energy and the values, the pop is in the hooks and the structures.
- **The hooks.** Tacocat's hooks are immediate and durable. "Bridge to Hawaii" has a chorus that doesn't leave. "Dana Katherine Scully" has a verse that builds to a declaration of admiration. "Crimson Wave" has a rhythm and a lyric that land together with the satisfaction of the right key in the right lock. The songs stay.
- **The feminist content.** Tacocat's feminism is embedded in the subject matter rather than stated as manifesto. "Crimson Wave" is about menstruation -- matter-of-fact, without shame, delivered with a hook. "Men Explain Things to Me" addresses mansplaining directly. The politics are in the subject, not the tone; the tone is warm and often funny, which makes the politics land harder.
- **The humor.** Tacocat is funny. Not satirical (the political edge is real) but genuinely amusing -- the humor comes from precise observation delivered without excess. The band learned this from the Olympia tradition (K Records, Beat Happening) and from the punk pop tradition (Buzzcocks, early Weezer, Lemonheads). The right word in the right place.
- **The production.** Tacocat's recordings are bright and clean -- the guitars ring, the vocals are present in the mix, the drums have energy without muddiness. The production serves the songs' pop sensibilities without sanitizing the punk energy.

(Sources: Tacocat recordings; E=7 calibration; hook analysis; feminist content in lyrics; humor register; production approach; punk pop tradition)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Albums

- ***Shame Spiral* (2012, self-released).** Early recordings. The band finding its form: the punk pop is there, the humor is present, the hooks are developing.
- ***Strangles* (2013, Hardly Art).** The first Hardly Art release. Tacocat fully formed in the label relationship that will define the rest of its catalog. The production is clean, the songs are tight, the feminist content is integrated.
- ***NVM* (2014, Hardly Art).** The breakthrough. "Bridge to Hawaii," "Crimson Wave," "Psychedelic Quinceañera" -- the songs that established Tacocat as one of the defining Seattle punk pop bands of the decade. The album title is millennial internet shorthand (never mind) embedded in a palindrome band name: layers of casual cleverness.
- ***Lost Time* (2016, Hardly Art).** Expanded arrangements, more dynamic range. The band growing within its form. Collaborator Sharon Van Etten appears.
- ***This Mess Is a Place* (2019, Sub Pop).** The band moves from Hardly Art to Sub Pop main label. A larger platform, a more polished production. The songs remain Tacocat: funny, feminist, hook-driven.

(Sources: Tacocat discography; Shame Spiral; Strangles 2013; NVM 2014 Hardly Art; Lost Time 2016; This Mess Is a Place 2019 Sub Pop)

## "CRIMSON WAVE": THE SIGNATURE SONG

### Why This Song Matters

- **The subject.** "Crimson Wave" is about menstruation. This is not unusual as subject matter for Tacocat -- the band regularly addresses topics that mainstream pop music avoids, particularly around women's bodies and experiences. What makes the song work is not the subject's taboo quality but the craft with which Tacocat handles it.
- **The hook.** The chorus of "Crimson Wave" is a genuine pop hook: melodic, rhythmically satisfying, immediately memorable. The song works as a song first. The subject matter is not the gimmick; the song is the vehicle.
- **The humor and the politics together.** Tacocat delivers the song with warmth and a slight wit -- not clinical, not confessional, not angry. The matter-of-fact treatment of menstruation in the form of a bright punk pop song normalizes it in a way that a more earnest or confrontational approach would not. The humor is the politics.
- **The feminist tradition.** "Crimson Wave" belongs to the feminist punk tradition of reclaiming the bodies and experiences that mainstream culture marginalizes. The riot grrrl lineage (Bikini Kill, Sleater-Kinney, Hole) is visible behind the song, but Tacocat makes it its own through the pop-brightness of the delivery.

(Sources: "Crimson Wave" analysis; feminist body politics in punk pop; hook analysis; humor-as-politics tradition; riot grrrl lineage)

## DANA KATHERINE SCULLY

### The X-Files Tribute

- **"Dana Katherine Scully."** One of Tacocat's most beloved songs is an explicit tribute to Special Agent Dana Scully of The X-Files. Scully (played by Gillian Anderson) is one of television's most important feminist science role models: a medical doctor and FBI agent, perpetually rational in the face of her partner's credulous enthusiasm for the paranormal.
- **Why Scully.** Scully is the skeptic, the scientist, the woman in the room who insists on evidence. For a band that practices feminist punk with humor and precision, Scully is a natural subject: she is competent, under-credited, right more often than acknowledged, and deeply good at her job. The tribute is affectionate and sincere.
- **The pop culture feminist.** Tacocat's feminism is not only political or historical -- it is cultural, embedded in the specific texts (television, film, everyday experience) that the band and its audience share. "Dana Katherine Scully" is pop culture feminism: taking seriously a representation that matters, making it a song, making the song a hook.

(Sources: "Dana Katherine Scully" analysis; Dana Scully character; X-Files feminist role model; Gillian Anderson; pop culture feminism in Tacocat's work)

## TACOCAT AND CHILDBIRTH: THE CONNECTED NETWORK

### Bree McKenna and the Capitol Hill Supergroup

- **Bree McKenna in two bands.** Tacocat's bassist Bree McKenna is a founding member of Childbirth (degree 283), the Capitol Hill punk pop supergroup formed in 2013. The overlap between these two bands is one of the defining features of the Capitol Hill indie scene in the 2010s: a small network of musicians who form and participate in multiple projects simultaneously.
- **Julia Shapiro (Chastity Belt / Childbirth).** Childbirth also includes Julia Shapiro from Chastity Belt -- another Hardly Art band. The Hardly Art roster is connected by personnel as well as label: the bands share musicians, share aesthetic values, share the institutional infrastructure of the Sub Pop subsidiary.
- **The Capitol Hill network.** Tacocat (degree 282) → Childbirth (degree 283): consecutive degrees, overlapping personnel, same label, same neighborhood. The engine is documenting a micro-network within the larger Seattle scene.
- **The supergroup dynamic.** When musicians from multiple active bands form a new project together, they bring the influences and approaches of their primary bands. Childbirth's sound -- more aggressive punk pop, darker subject matter -- reflects the combination of Tacocat's McKenna with Chastity Belt's Shapiro and the addition of Stacy Peck.

(Sources: Bree McKenna dual membership; Julia Shapiro Chastity Belt; Childbirth formation; Hardly Art roster connections; Capitol Hill network; supergroup dynamics)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### The Brightest Entry in the Women-Punk Arc

- **The tonal shift.** After degrees 277-280's historical ferocity (Sleater-Kinney, Unwound, Seven Year Bitch, The Gits) and Wimps' sardonic contemporary punch (281), Tacocat brings warmth. The feminist punk spectrum in the engine now includes the manifesto (Bikini Kill), the political complexity (Sleater-Kinney), the rage (Seven Year Bitch, The Gits), and the wit (Wimps, Tacocat).
- **The mainstream crossover.** Tacocat's move from Hardly Art to Sub Pop main label (This Mess Is a Place, 2019) represents a modest mainstream crossover within the independent music world -- a band good enough to be pulled up to the parent label. This is recognition without capitulation.
- **The feminist humor lineage.** Tacocat occupies a specific position in the women-punk feminist lineage: the humor end. Where Bikini Kill was confrontational and Sleater-Kinney was politically complex, Tacocat is funny and warm and just as feminist. The humor is a strategy, not a retreat from politics.
- **The continuing presence.** Tacocat has been active since 2007 -- an eighteen-year-plus run at the time of this entry. The band has not dissolved; it has evolved. This consistency in the independent punk pop world is its own form of success.

(Sources: Tacocat's role in women-punk arc; tonal contribution; mainstream crossover assessment; feminist humor lineage; ongoing activity)
