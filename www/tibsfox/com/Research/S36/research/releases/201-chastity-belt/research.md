## CHASTITY BELT -- DRY WIT AS METHOD

### The Sound of Not Caring (While Caring Deeply)

Chastity Belt's aesthetic is defined by studied nonchalance:

- **Formation.** Formed at Whitman College in Walla Walla, Washington, relocated to Seattle (Capitol Hill). Julia Shapiro (vocals/guitar), Lydia Lund (guitar), Annie Truscott (bass), Gretchen Grimm (drums).
- **The ironic name.** "Chastity Belt" is deliberately provocative -- a feminist reclamation of a medieval instrument of control. The name sets the tone: confrontational but delivered with a shrug. The humor is dry, the feminism is embedded, the delivery is deadpan.
- **Musical style.** Indie rock with post-punk leanings. Loose, slightly shambling rhythms. Clean-to-lightly-distorted guitars. Shapiro's vocals are flat, conversational, deliberately un-performative. The songs sound like they might fall apart but never do. The slacker aesthetic conceals careful craft.
- **Dry wit.** Chastity Belt's lyrics are often funny -- observational humor about relationships, boredom, social media, bodies. But the humor is a vehicle for genuine emotional content. Songs that sound like jokes reveal themselves as deeply felt.
- **Post-punk influence.** The flatness of Shapiro's delivery, the angular guitar lines, the emphasis on rhythm over melody -- these trace back to post-punk (Wire, Raincoats, early Sleater-Kinney). But filtered through 2010s irony and self-awareness.

(Sources: Chastity Belt formation; Whitman College; Julia Shapiro; musical style analysis; post-punk lineage)

## ALL-FEMALE BANDS -- THE NETWORK

### Capitol Hill's Feminist Music Community

Chastity Belt exists within a dense network:

- **The network.** Capitol Hill in the 2010s supported a thriving feminist music community. Chastity Belt, Tacocat (203), Childbirth (204), and others shared members, shows, venues, and audiences. This was not a "scene" in the traditional sense -- no single sound, no manifesto. But a network of mutual support and shared values.
- **Hardly Art as hub.** Hardly Art signed multiple bands from this network, functioning as the label hub for Capitol Hill's feminist punk-indie community. The label's curatorial choices reinforced the network.
- **Historical context.** Seattle's feminist music history runs deep -- riot grrrl (Olympia, early 1990s), Sleater-Kinney, the Fastbacks. Chastity Belt and the 201-204 cluster are the next generation. They inherited the politics but shifted the tone: less anger, more irony. Less manifestos, more deadpan observation.
- **Member overlap.** Chastity Belt members appear in Childbirth (204) and other projects. The network is small enough for personal overlap, large enough for distinct identities.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Chastity Belt

- **Tempo.** Moderate. 100-130 BPM. Unhurried. The tempo feels slightly behind the beat -- deliberate drag.
- **Guitar.** Two guitars: one rhythmic, one melodic. Clean to lightly overdriven. Angular lines influenced by post-punk. Jangly but not bright. The guitars interlock rather than competing.
- **Bass.** Melodic, prominent. Truscott's bass often carries the harmonic foundation while guitars provide texture and punctuation.
- **Vocals.** Julia Shapiro: flat, conversational, deadpan. Not monotone but close. Emotional range communicated through word choice and subtle inflection rather than vocal range. Occasionally harmonized by other members.
- **Drums.** Steady, unhurried. Grimm's drumming provides the foundation without drawing attention. Fills are sparse. The groove is the priority.
- **Production.** Clean but not polished. Room sound present. The production preserves the live-band feel -- four people in a room, playing together. No overdubs for their own sake.

(Sources: Chastity Belt discography; production analysis; post-punk-to-indie guitar lineage)
