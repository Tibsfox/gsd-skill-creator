Scope note: Black Flag were formed in Hermosa Beach, California. This entry documents their influence on Seattle's hardcore and alternative scene — not their origins. The influence mechanism is the SST distribution network, the touring circuit through Seattle venues including the Metropolis, and the direct impact on bands like The Fartz (degree 270) who formed in dialogue with the LA hardcore template.

The SST Pipeline

Black Flag's route to Seattle runs through SST Records (degree 261). Greg Ginn founded SST in 1978 specifically to release Black Flag's music — a response to the mainstream industry's refusal to touch hardcore. SST built an independent distribution network that reached Pacific Northwest record stores. A teenager in Seattle in 1982 could walk into a shop and buy Damaged, My War, and Nervous Breakdown. The pipeline was physical: records leaving Hermosa Beach, reaching Seattle, entering the hands of musicians who would form the next generation of the scene.

SST's catalog extended beyond Black Flag to Minutemen, Husker Du, Dinosaur Jr, and Meat Puppets — the full document of the American underground. But Black Flag was the center. The label existed because of the band; the band's aesthetic defined the label's identity.

"Black Flag demonstrated that a band could own its recording and distribution infrastructure and reach a national audience. This model is the economic foundation of independent rock music."

The Touring Circuit

Black Flag treated touring as a full-time occupation. The West Coast corridor — Los Angeles to San Francisco to Portland to Seattle — was their regular route. They played Seattle venues including the Metropolis, the all-ages hardcore space that was Seattle punk's primary home in the early 1980s. These shows were not casual; Black Flag live was a physical ordeal. When Seattle bands saw Black Flag at the Metropolis, they saw something that had not existed before: the body as a musical instrument, maximum physical presence deployed in service of a political and sonic argument.

The Fartz (degree 270) are the most direct evidence of this transmission. Seattle's first prominent hardcore band formed their aesthetic in dialogue with LA hardcore — Black Flag's template was the input; The Fartz were an early Seattle output of that influence.

Henry Rollins: Body as Instrument

Rollins joined Black Flag in 1981 from Washington DC's hardcore scene. His addition transformed the band's physical and literary character. The Rollins-era records — Damaged (1981), My War (1984), Slip It In (1984), Loose Nut (1985), In My Head (1985) — document a band pushing beyond the form it had created. The long, slow, crushing tracks on My War's side B anticipated sludge metal a decade before the genre crystallized.

Rollins developed a parallel spoken word career that brought the confrontational approach into rooms too small for a full band. His 1994 book Get in the Van — journal documentation of the Black Flag years, van floors, venues, violence, and music — won a Grammy for Best Spoken Word Album and is a primary source for the era.

Discography — The Records That Reached Seattle

Nervous Breakdown EP (1978)

SST • First

Before the genre had a name. Keith Morris vocals. Four songs. The original hardware document. "Nervous Breakdown," "Fix Me," "I've Had It," "Wasted."

Damaged (1981)

SST • The Album

Rollins's first full-length with the band. MCA refused to distribute it. SST distributed independently. Reached Seattle through the indie network, passed hand to hand. "Rise Above." "TV Party." "Depression."

My War (1984)

SST • Evolution

Side A: hardcore. Side B: three long, slow, crushing songs anticipating sludge metal. The proof that Black Flag would not be frozen in the form that made them famous.

Slip It In / Loose Nut / In My Head (1984–85)

SST • Final Phase

Increasingly metal-influenced final records. The band evolving toward something beyond hardcore before disbanding in 1986.

The SST / Alternative Tentacles Mirror

Degrees 286 and 287 document the two poles of West Coast hardcore distribution simultaneously. Alternative Tentacles (degree 270, The Fartz) and SST Records (degree 261) were mirror images: AT in San Francisco under Jello Biafra, SST in Hermosa Beach under Greg Ginn. Both built independent distribution networks. Both reached Seattle. The 286-287 pairing documents both poles: Dead Kennedys (AT, SF) and Black Flag (SST, LA). The engine's most consecutive pairing in terms of energy ceiling — and both document not Seattle bands but the external forces that shaped Seattle's hardest music.
