## What Worked

1. **The SST thread (261 → 287) is one of the engine's cleanest label-to-band narratives.** Degree 261 documented the institution (SST Records); degree 287 documents the band that created the institution. The connection is complete.
2. **The consecutive E=9 pair (286-287) is the engine's most intense block.** Dead Kennedys (SF, Alternative Tentacles) and Black Flag (LA, SST) represent the two poles of West Coast hardcore distribution. Both documented as Seattle influence entries in consecutive degrees. The 286-287 pair is symmetric and complete.
3. **The touring circuit / Metropolis documentation provides the physical mechanism.** Influence does not transfer through abstraction; it transfers through physical presence. Documenting the West Coast touring corridor and the Metropolis venue grounds the entry in specific, traceable events.
4. **My War's side B as proto-sludge anticipation is the engine's best early evolution document.** A hardcore band that moved toward longer, slower, more crushing forms in 1984 was doing something that would not be named for another decade. The documentation of this evolution is analytically important.
5. **The DIY/independent label model is essential history.** Without Black Flag insisting on control and building SST to exercise it, the infrastructure that produced Sub Pop, Alternative Tentacles, Touch and Go, and Dischord would look different. This is not incidental; it is foundational.

## Discoveries

1. **The AT/SST mirror is an underappreciated structural fact.** Alternative Tentacles (Biafra, SF) and SST (Ginn, LA) were almost exactly parallel institutions: both founded to release the founder's band, both building national distribution, both reaching Seattle. Documenting both at degrees 270/286 (AT) and 261/287 (SST) creates a structural completeness in the engine.
2. **Raymond Pettibon's four-bar logo is one of rock's most recognized symbols.** The visual dimension of Black Flag's influence deserves documentation: the logo is in Seattle tattoo culture, in the visual vocabulary of the underground.
3. **Henry Rollins's Get in the Van is a primary source.** For anyone researching this era, the 1994 book (Grammy winner) is the first-person document. The engine should note primary sources when they exist.
