## STRUCTURAL EVENT: Up Records Enters the Label Map

764-HERO introduces **Up Records** to the engine's label map. Up Records was a Bellingham/Seattle independent label founded by Chris Takino in 1994. The label's roster included some of the most important Pacific Northwest indie acts of the late 1990s:

- **Modest Mouse** (early releases before moving to Epic/Sub Pop)
- **764-HERO** (core roster artist)
- **Mirah** (early releases)
- **The Microphones/Mount Eerie** (Phil Elverum, early releases)
- **Built to Spill** (some releases)
- **Lync** (featuring Sam Jayne, later of the Halo Benders)
- **Dub Narcotic Sound System** (Calvin Johnson)

Up Records joins the engine's label map alongside Sub Pop, Kill Rock Stars, K Records, Suicide Squeeze, Barsuk, Sargent House, and others. Up Records represents the **lo-fi, experimental, DIY end** of the Pacific Northwest indie spectrum -- smaller than Sub Pop, more experimental than Barsuk, more electronic-curious than K Records. The label operated from the mid-1990s to the early 2000s and captured a specific moment in PNW indie culture: the post-grunge era when Capitol Hill bands were experimenting with electronics, lo-fi recording, and genre blending.

## 764-HERO -- INDIE/ELECTRONIC -- CAPITOL HILL

### The Band

764-HERO was a Seattle indie band active from 1993 to approximately 2003. The core members were:

- **John Atkins** -- guitar, vocals, electronics. Atkins was the primary songwriter and the architect of 764-HERO's electronic experimentation. His guitar work ranged from jangly indie rock to heavily processed, effect-laden textures. The electronic elements in 764-HERO's music -- loops, samples, synthesizers, drum machines layered under or alongside live instruments -- were largely Atkins's contribution.
- **Polly Johnson** -- bass, vocals. Johnson provided both the rhythmic foundation and a second vocal presence. Her bass work anchored the songs while Atkins's guitar and electronics floated above. Johnson was also active in other Seattle music projects.

The band's lineup shifted over the years, with various drummers including **James Bertram** and others. But the core identity was Atkins and Johnson -- two voices, guitar-and-bass, with electronics woven through.

### The Sound

764-HERO's sound occupies a specific and important space in the history of Pacific Northwest indie music: **lo-fi indie rock that incorporates electronic elements not as a gimmick but as a fundamental compositional tool**. In the mid-to-late 1990s, when most indie rock bands were either following the loud/quiet dynamics of grunge-influenced post-hardcore or the jangly simplicity of K Records-style indie pop, 764-HERO was doing something different: layering electronic textures -- loops, samples, programmed beats, synthesizer washes -- under and around traditional indie rock instrumentation.

The genre classification is **Indie/Electronic** because:
- **Indie** captures the lo-fi recording aesthetic, the DIY ethos, the small-label distribution (Up Records), the Capitol Hill scene embedding, and the fundamental song structures (verse-chorus, guitar-bass-drums).
- **Electronic** captures the synthesizers, loops, samples, drum machines, and processed textures that distinguish 764-HERO from pure indie rock.

The combination was **ahead of its time**. In the mid-1990s, the indie/electronic crossover that would later define bands like Postal Service, Dntel, and LCD Soundsystem was still nascent. 764-HERO was experimenting with this crossover before it had a name. The band's electronic elements were not polished or dance-oriented -- they were **lo-fi, textural, and atmospheric**, matching the DIY aesthetic of the underlying indie rock. The electronics were as rough and handmade as the guitar tones.

### Key Albums

- **Salt Sinks and Sugar Floats** (1997, Up Records) -- the definitive 764-HERO album. Lo-fi indie rock with electronic textures woven through. The album captures the band's essential quality: songs that feel simultaneously handmade and electronic, warm and distant, intimate and abstracted. The production is lo-fi but not accidental -- the roughness is intentional, creating a haze through which the melodies and electronics emerge.

- **Weekends of Sound** (2001, Up Records) -- the band's most ambitious album. More electronic, more expansive, more experimental. The electronic elements are more prominent and the compositions are longer and more complex. If *Salt Sinks* was indie rock with electronic touches, *Weekends of Sound* is closer to electronic music built on indie rock foundations. The album was critically acclaimed but arrived at a moment when the indie music landscape was shifting toward the garage rock revival (The Strokes, The White Stripes) and away from electronic experimentation.

- **Get Here and Stay** (1998, Up Records) -- EP. Further exploration of the indie/electronic intersection.

- **Typeface** (split with Modest Mouse, Up Records) -- evidence of 764-HERO's place in the Up Records ecosystem and their connection to Modest Mouse.

### The Up Records Connection

764-HERO's place in the Up Records ecosystem is central to understanding the band's significance. Up Records was not just a label -- it was a **scene**. The artists on Up Records shared aesthetic values: lo-fi recording, electronic experimentation, genre blending, DIY ethics. 764-HERO existed in direct community with Modest Mouse (before Modest Mouse became a mainstream act), the Microphones, Mirah, and other Up Records artists.

The **Modest Mouse connection** is particularly significant. Modest Mouse and 764-HERO were both core Up Records artists in the mid-to-late 1990s. They shared bills, shared label infrastructure, and shared a commitment to musical experimentation. Isaac Brock (Modest Mouse) and John Atkins (764-HERO) were contemporaries in the same small-label ecosystem. The two bands represent different expressions of the same impulse: Modest Mouse went toward angular, lyrically dense, increasingly ambitious indie rock; 764-HERO went toward electronic-infused, textural, contemplative indie rock. Both paths originated from the same Up Records soil.

## Ahead of Its Time: The Indie/Electronic Crossover

764-HERO's significance is partly **historical**: the band was experimenting with the indie/electronic crossover years before it became a recognized genre. The timeline:

- **1993-2001:** 764-HERO active, incorporating electronic elements into lo-fi indie rock.
- **2001:** Jimmy Tamborello (Dntel) releases *Life Is Full of Possibilities*, featuring "This Is the Dream of Evan and Chan" with Ben Gibbard. This is widely cited as a precursor to the indie/electronic crossover.
- **2003:** The Postal Service releases *Give Up* -- the defining indie/electronic crossover album. Ben Gibbard (Death Cab for Cutie, Barsuk Records) + Jimmy Tamborello (Dntel). The album sells over a million copies and defines the genre.
- **2005:** LCD Soundsystem releases debut. DFA Records. Dance-punk/indie-electronic. The crossover goes mainstream.

764-HERO was doing this in **1997**. *Salt Sinks and Sugar Floats* predates *Give Up* by six years. The band's approach was rougher, more lo-fi, more textural, and less pop-oriented than the Postal Service's -- but the fundamental operation (indie rock + electronic elements as compositional tools, not gimmicks) is the same. 764-HERO was ahead of the curve.

The band's early dissolution (circa 2003) meant they were not around to benefit from the indie/electronic boom they helped anticipate. This is a common pattern in music history: the pioneers are not always the beneficiaries.

(Sources: Pitchfork; Tiny Mix Tapes; The Stranger; engine internal analysis)

## Acoustic Profile: The Lo-Fi Signal

### The Sound

The **lo-fi signal** is the acoustic signature of 764-HERO: a warm, rough, deliberately unpolished sonic texture in which indie rock instrumentation and electronic elements coexist without hierarchy. The "signal" metaphor captures the band's quality: their music sounds like a **transmission** -- slightly degraded, slightly distant, arriving through static and haze, but carrying a clear melodic and emotional signal underneath the noise.

### Key Sonic Characteristics

- **Guitar tone:** Lo-fi, warm, occasionally jangly, frequently processed through effects (delay, reverb, distortion). Atkins's guitar is not the clean precision of post-rock or the aggressive distortion of grunge -- it occupies a middle ground: **warm distortion with reverb haze**.
- **Electronics:** Loops, samples, synthesizer washes, drum machine patterns. The electronic elements are lo-fi -- they match the production aesthetic of the guitar and bass. Not clean electronic music but **fuzzy, textural, handmade electronic sounds**.
- **Bass:** Polly Johnson's bass is warm, round, and anchoring. It provides the rhythmic foundation while guitar and electronics float above.
- **Vocals:** Two voices (Atkins and Johnson). The vocal style is understated -- not shouting, not crooning, but **speaking-to-singing**. The vocals match the lo-fi aesthetic: intimate, slightly distant, emotionally present but not demonstrative.
- **Production:** Deliberately lo-fi. The rough production is not a limitation but an **aesthetic choice** -- the haze and static are part of the music. The lo-fi quality creates intimacy and warmth that polished production would destroy.

### Energy: E=4

E=4 -- moderate energy. The lo-fi signal is quiet, contemplative, textural. After four consecutive E=6 entries, E=4 is a **deliberate descent**. The engine needs the descent: sustained maximum is unsustainable. 764-HERO provides the dynamic contrast that makes the prior E=6 corridor meaningful in retrospect -- without the quiet, the loud has no context.
