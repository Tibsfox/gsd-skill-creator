## CENTRAL DISTRICT: THE GEOGRAPHIC CONTEXT

### Seattle's Central District in the Early 1990s

The Central District (CD) is one of Seattle's oldest and historically most significant neighborhoods:

- **The history.** The Central District has been a center of Seattle's African American community since the early twentieth century, when discriminatory housing covenants restricted where Black residents could live in the city. By the 1980s, the neighborhood had a complex, layered character: a long-established community with deep roots, and an affordable neighborhood attracting artists, musicians, and newcomers drawn by low rents.
- **The music scene.** By the late 1980s and early 1990s, the Central District was home to a cluster of punk and alternative musicians. The Gits, among others, lived and practiced in the CD. The neighborhood's cheap rents, community character, and proximity to Capitol Hill and the rest of Seattle's music infrastructure made it an unlikely but productive home for punk music.
- **The Gits in the CD.** The Gits -- originally from Yellow Springs, Ohio, where they formed as a band before relocating to Seattle -- established themselves in the Central District. The band rehearsed and lived in the neighborhood. The community that formed around them in the CD was the immediate community that would respond most directly to the events of 1993.

(Sources: Central District history; African American community history in Seattle; restrictive housing covenants; 1980s-1990s CD demographics and character; punk musicians in the CD; The Gits in the CD; Yellow Springs Ohio origin)

## THE BAND

### Mia Zapata, Joe Spleen, Matt Dresdner, Steve Moriarty

- **Mia Zapata (vocals).** Zapata's voice is the center of The Gits' music. Her vocal approach is difficult to categorize: it contains the rawness and commitment of punk, the emotional directness of the blues, the power of R&B, and a distinctly personal quality that sounds unlike any other vocalist in the Pacific Northwest punk scene. She sings with her whole body. The emotional range -- from tender to devastating, from quiet to full-band scream -- is wide, and the transitions between these registers are achieved with complete conviction. Zapata had one of the most distinctive voices in American punk music. This is a statement of fact.
- **Joe Spleen (guitar).** Spleen's guitar work is the structural foundation beneath Zapata's voice. The guitar parts are direct and powerful -- punk in the classic sense, with sufficient variety and craft to support the vocal range Zapata brings to each song. Spleen's writing contributions to the band are documented in the catalog.
- **Matt Dresdner (bass).** Dresdner's bass playing anchors the rhythm section with a physicality that is characteristic of the best punk bass work: present, propulsive, not decorative. The bass in The Gits' recordings is a structural element.
- **Steve Moriarty (drums).** Moriarty's drumming is consistent and powerful -- the engine of the band's E=8 energy. His playing in "Frenching the Bully" is the rhythmic foundation that makes the album's dynamics work.
- **The Ohio connection.** The Gits formed in Yellow Springs, Ohio before relocating to Seattle. The band's origins are not Pacific Northwest -- they arrived from elsewhere and became part of the Seattle scene by presence and by music, not by birth. This is worth noting: the Central District punk scene they became part of was not their native context. They chose it.

(Sources: Mia Zapata biography and vocal analysis; Joe Spleen guitar; Matt Dresdner bass; Steve Moriarty drums; Yellow Springs Ohio origin; Seattle Central District adoption)

## MIA ZAPATA: THE VOICE

### What Made Her Irreplaceable

The engine documents Mia Zapata's voice with the specificity it deserves. She was a vocalist of exceptional gifts:

- **The range.** Zapata's vocal range is wide -- from intimate, conversational, almost spoken delivery to full-throated belting to raw punk screaming. What is remarkable is not the range itself but the conviction at every point in it. She never sounds like she is demonstrating a technique; she sounds like she is saying something that needs to be said.
- **The blues dimension.** Zapata's singing has a strong blues component that is unusual in punk rock. The way she bends notes, the way she sustains vowels at the end of phrases, the way she uses silence before attacking the next word -- these are blues techniques applied in a punk context. The combination produces something that sounds neither like conventional blues nor like conventional punk but like something specific: Mia Zapata.
- **The emotional directness.** Punk rock often achieves emotional impact through volume and velocity. Zapata achieved it through directness: the feeling that she is singing exactly what she means, without mediation. The intimacy of her delivery -- even in loud, fast songs -- is what makes her voice memorable.
- **The irreplaceability.** After Zapata's death, The Gits' surviving members tried to continue. They worked with other vocalists, including Joan Jett, who recorded with them on tribute material. The fact that these efforts, however heartfelt, could not replicate the original is not a failure -- it is a testament. Mia Zapata's voice was not a type; it was hers.

(Sources: Mia Zapata vocal range analysis; blues dimension in punk context; emotional directness; note-bending; silent space usage; irreplaceability argument; post-death collaborations with Joan Jett)

## THE DISCOGRAPHY

### "Frenching the Bully" and the Ongoing Catalog

- ***Frenching the Bully* (1992, C/Z Records).** The sole full-length studio album released during the band's active period. Recorded by Steve Albini, whose engineering approach -- minimal processing, close microphone placement, emphasis on the natural sound of the instruments -- is ideal for capturing the raw power of The Gits. The album is thirteen tracks of punk that ranges from fast and aggressive to slower and more deliberate, with Zapata's voice at the center of every dynamic shift. Key tracks: "Second Skin," "Insecure," "Look More Like You," "Seaweed." The album is the band's primary artistic statement.
- **Posthumous releases.** The Gits' catalog includes posthumously released material: live recordings, compilation appearances, and previously unreleased tracks. These releases document the band's range and confirm the quality of what was already known from "Frenching the Bully."
- **The Steve Albini production.** Albini's approach to recording The Gits is an example of his philosophy at its most effective: the band in a room, close-miked, minimal processing, the sound of the performance rather than the sound of production. The result is a recording that sounds as live and direct now as it did in 1992.

(Sources: "Frenching the Bully" (1992, C/Z Records); Steve Albini production; thirteen tracks; key tracks analysis; posthumous releases; live recordings; Albini philosophy applied to punk)

## THE TRAGEDY: A FACTUAL ACCOUNT

### Documented with Respect

Mia Zapata was murdered on July 7, 1993. She was twenty-seven years old. She was found in the Central District neighborhood she lived in.

The murder was unsolved for ten years. In 2003, DNA evidence from a preserved sample was entered into a national database and matched to Jesus Mezquia, a Cuban-born man from Florida with a history of violent criminal conduct. Mezquia was arrested in Miami, Florida in January 2004. He was tried in Seattle and convicted in March 2004 of first-degree murder. He was sentenced to thirty-six years in prison.

The ten-year gap between the murder and the conviction -- a decade during which the case was unsolved -- is part of the community's experience of this event. The uncertainty extended the grief.

This is the factual account. It is documented here because it is part of the historical record. The engine does not elaborate on it beyond what is necessary. The music is what endures; the artistry is what the community honors; the community's response is what matters. The murder is documented as a fact that is inseparable from the history of The Gits, not as a source of sensational content.

(Sources: Murder date July 7 1993; age 27; Central District; DNA match to Jesus Mezquia; arrest January 2004 Miami; conviction March 2004 Seattle; thirty-six year sentence; ten-year investigation)

## THE COMMUNITY RESPONSE

### What the Community Did

The response of the Seattle feminist punk community and the broader music community to Mia Zapata's murder was immediate, sustained, and organized:

- **Home Alive.** Home Alive was a non-profit organization founded in 1993 in the immediate aftermath of Zapata's murder. Its mission: providing self-defense and personal safety resources to artists and community members, particularly women. Home Alive offered free and low-cost self-defense classes, supported community safety education, and was an expression of collective action in response to a specific tragedy. Home Alive operated for many years. Its founding in 1993 is one of the most significant community responses to a music community tragedy in Seattle's history.
- **Seven Year Bitch's tribute.** "¡Viva Zapata!" (1994, degree 279) named in Mia Zapata's honor. The tribute is embedded in the title of Seven Year Bitch's central album. The community honored Zapata through their music.
- **Joan Jett's involvement.** Joan Jett -- the feminist punk rock icon who had produced Bikini Kill's "Rebel Girl" (degree 276) -- collaborated with The Gits' surviving members on tribute recordings. Jett's involvement connects the Pacific Northwest feminist punk community to its lineage: Joan Jett, Bikini Kill, The Gits, Seven Year Bitch -- a network of women in punk music, connected by solidarity.
- **"The Gits" documentary (2005).** A feature documentary about The Gits and Mia Zapata was released in 2005. The film documents the band's history, Zapata's artistry, and the community's response. The documentary is a primary source for understanding The Gits' cultural significance.
- **The investigation support.** The music community contributed to the investigation. Funds for investigation resources were raised in part through benefit concerts and community efforts. The eventual DNA match and conviction came after a decade of sustained effort -- by law enforcement and by a community that refused to let the case go cold.

(Sources: Home Alive organization founding 1993; self-defense mission; free and low-cost classes; Seven Year Bitch "¡Viva Zapata!" tribute (degree 279); Joan Jett collaboration with The Gits survivors; "The Gits" documentary 2005; benefit concerts; investigation support; community refusal to let case go cold)
