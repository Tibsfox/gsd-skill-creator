## E=7 -- THE TRUE HEART OF GRUNGE

### Why E=7

Mudhoney registers at E=7 -- the foundational grunge band at maximum pre-E=8 energy:

- **They were first.** Mudhoney formed in 1988 from the ashes of Green River (often called the first grunge band). Mark Arm and Steve Turner took Green River's raw energy and stripped it further. "Touch Me I'm Sick" (1988) was the grunge anthem before grunge had a name.
- **Mark Arm coined "grunge."** The word itself -- the genre label that would define a decade -- came from a Mark Arm letter to a Seattle zine in the mid-1980s describing the sound as "pure grunge." The word stuck. The man who named the genre is the man who defined it.
- **Sub Pop's cornerstone.** Mudhoney was Sub Pop's first significant signing. Their debut single "Touch Me I'm Sick" / "Halloween" (1988) established the Sub Pop aesthetic: raw, loud, distorted, with a sense of humor. The single sold out. The template was set.
- **They never left.** While Nirvana exploded and imploded, while Pearl Jam grew massive, while Soundgarden achieved arena status -- Mudhoney kept going. Still active in 2026. Still on Sub Pop. Still in Seattle. Still playing the same rooms. The foundational band remains.
- **No commercial compromise.** Mudhoney briefly signed to Reprise/Warner Bros in the early 1990s (three albums), then returned to Sub Pop. The major-label detour produced solid records but the band was never comfortable with the machinery. They came home. The return to Sub Pop was a statement: the music matters more than the market.
- **Why E=7?** Foundational importance (coined the genre, first Sub Pop grunge signing, still active) plus raw sonic intensity plus uncompromising artistic vision. E=7 is the maximum for a genre-founding band. Only DoNormaal's accumulated late-career intensity justified E=8.

## "TOUCH ME I'M SICK" -- THE ANTHEM

### The Song That Started Everything

"Touch Me I'm Sick" (1988) is the foundational grunge document:

- **Three chords, maximum distortion, humor.** The song is simple in structure and overwhelming in delivery. Distorted guitar, pounding drums, Mark Arm's sneering, half-sung vocals. The lyrics are deliberately stupid and deliberately perfect -- "I feel bad, but I've felt worse" energy.
- **Sub Pop Single of the Month.** Released as Sub Pop's Single of the Month Club offering. The 7" sold out immediately. Word spread through college radio and zine networks.
- **The template.** "Touch Me I'm Sick" established the sonic template that Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden, Alice in Chains, and dozens of others would build on: loud/quiet dynamics, distortion as texture, humor and self-deprecation alongside genuine intensity.
- **Still a live staple.** Thirty-eight years later, "Touch Me I'm Sick" still closes Mudhoney sets. The song hasn't aged because it was never polished. Raw materials don't decay.

## GREEN RIVER -- THE PREDECESSOR

### The First Grunge Band

Mudhoney's roots trace directly to Green River (1984-1988):

- **Green River.** Often cited as the first grunge band. Members included Mark Arm, Steve Turner, Stone Gossard, Jeff Ament, and Bruce Fairweather. When Green River split, Gossard and Ament went on to form Mother Love Bone, then Pearl Jam. Arm and Turner formed Mudhoney.
- **The split.** Green River split along philosophical lines: Gossard and Ament wanted a bigger, more commercial sound; Arm and Turner wanted to stay raw. The split produced two of grunge's foundational streams: Mudhoney (raw, underground) and Pearl Jam (arena-ready).
- **Two paths from one source.** Green River is the trunk. Mudhoney and Pearl Jam are the branches. One branch stayed underground; the other became one of the biggest bands in the world. Both are valid. But Mudhoney's path kept the original energy intact.

## CENTRAL DISTRICT -- THE GRUNGE EPICENTER

### The Neighborhood

Mudhoney is rooted in Seattle's Central District:

- **Central District.** Historically one of Seattle's most diverse neighborhoods, the Central District was home to rehearsal spaces, house shows, and the DIY infrastructure that supported early grunge. Cheap rent made it possible for bands to exist.
- **Proximity to Sub Pop.** Sub Pop's offices were in the broader Capitol Hill/Central District area. The geographic proximity between band and label reinforced the tight, scene-based relationship.
- **House show culture.** Before grunge had club venues, it had house shows. Central District basements and living rooms hosted early performances by Mudhoney, Nirvana, and dozens of others.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Mudhoney

The Mudhoney sonic profile is the template for grunge:

- **Guitar tone.** Massively distorted. Mark Arm and Steve Turner use fuzz pedals and overdriven amps to create a wall of harmonic distortion. The tone is not clean or precise -- it is deliberately messy, buzzy, and overwhelming.
- **Vocal delivery.** Mark Arm's vocals are sneering, half-sung, half-shouted. Not technically polished. The delivery conveys attitude, humor, and genuine intensity in equal measure. Influences: Iggy Pop, punk, garage rock.
- **Rhythm section.** Pounding, relentless drums (Dan Peters, Matt Lukin on bass, later Guy Maddison). The rhythm section provides the foundation for the guitar chaos. Simple patterns, maximum impact.
- **BPM range.** 100-180 BPM. Mostly uptempo. Occasional slower, sludgier tracks that emphasize the distortion.
- **Dynamic range.** Loud. The quiet/loud dynamic that Nirvana would perfect was present in Mudhoney but less formalized -- Mudhoney tends toward "always loud" with variation in intensity rather than volume.
- **Humor.** A critical and underappreciated element. Mudhoney's music has humor -- in lyrics, song titles, stage banter, album art. The humor prevents the intensity from becoming self-serious. This distinguishes Mudhoney from the more earnest grunge bands.
