There is a moment every music teacher knows: a student finally hears the ii-V-I in a Bill Evans chord, or feels the tritone substitution drop in a Quincy Jones arrangement, and something irreversible happens. Theory stops being notation on a page and becomes a living thing — audible, tactile, theirs.

Biography & Seattle Origins

Quincy Delight Jones Jr. was born March 14, 1933, in Chicago, Illinois. His early childhood on Chicago's South Side during the Great Depression was marked by instability — his mother was committed to a state institution when he was seven, a rupture he referenced throughout his life as both wound and motivating force.

In 1943, at age ten, Quincy moved with his father and stepmother to Bremerton, Washington, where wartime work at the Puget Sound Naval Shipyard had drawn the family west. They soon relocated to Seattle proper, settling in the Central District — the geographic and cultural heart of Seattle's Black community. This move placed the young Quincy at the intersection of two forces that would define American music: the Jackson Street jazz corridor and Garfield High School's rigorous music program.

Garfield High School

Garfield High School, at 400 23rd Avenue in the Central District, maintained a music program of conservatory caliber under directors Frank Waldron and Parker Cook. The program emphasized reading, theory, and arranging alongside performance — a pedagogical approach that gave Jones the technical vocabulary he would deploy for sixty years. Garfield produced not only Jones but also Ernestine Anderson, Buddy Catlett, and — a decade later — Jimi Hendrix.

Ray Charles: The Pivotal Meeting

In 1947, fourteen-year-old Quincy met sixteen-year-old Ray Charles Robinson on the Jackson Street scene. Charles had arrived from Florida the previous year, already blind, already a formidable pianist. The two became close friends and musical collaborators, practicing arrangements together, learning by doing on the nightly club circuit. Jones would later call this meeting the single most important event of his musical life. Charles taught him that the feeling in music was not separate from the theory but expressed through it.

Berklee & Lionel Hampton

In 1951, Jones received a scholarship to the Schillinger House of Music in Boston (later Berklee College of Music), becoming one of the school's first Black students. He absorbed the Schillinger System of Musical Composition — a mathematical approach treating musical parameters as independent variables combined through permutation. Before completing his studies, he was recruited by Lionel Hampton to join his big band as trumpeter and arranger. He was eighteen.

Career Arc

1950s-1960s — The Arranger's Decade

Established as one of America's most sought-after arrangers. Wrote for Count Basie (modernizing the band's sound with chromatic harmony and extended voicings), Dinah Washington (bridging jazz and pop), and Frank Sinatra (the landmark Sinatra at the Sands with the Basie Orchestra, 1966). In 1964, became the first Black vice president of a major record label at Mercury Records.

1960s-1970s — Film Scoring

Scored 35+ films including In the Heat of the Night (1967), The Italian Job (1969), Roots (1977), and The Wiz (1978). Pioneered through-composed narrative scoring using leitmotif technique — treating the film score as architectural structure, not decoration. The Wiz introduced Jones to the young Michael Jackson.

1979-1987 — The Thriller Era

Produced Michael Jackson's Off the Wall (1979, 20M copies), Thriller (1982, 70M+ copies — best-selling album in history), and Bad (1987, 45M copies). Each album fused jazz harmony with pop production in ways that had no precedent. Produced and co-wrote "We Are the World" (1985) with Jackson and Lionel Richie, raising $63M+ for famine relief.

1990s-2024 — Empire & Legacy

Founded Qwest Records, Vibe Magazine, and Quincy Jones Productions. Produced The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air (1990-1996). Received the Kennedy Center Honor (2001), National Medal of Arts (2010), and Rock and Roll Hall of Fame Ahmet Ertegun Award (2013). Died November 3, 2024, in Bel Air, Los Angeles, at age 91.

Music Theory Mapping

Jones's work maps to six core theory concepts that form the curricular foundation for degree 0. Each concept is directly audible in his recordings — theory made tangible through the music.

ii-V-I Progression

Intermediate

The foundational cadential motion of Western tonal harmony. Jones's arrangements for Basie and Sinatra are saturated with ii-V-I motion decorated with chromatic approach chords and inner-voice leading.

Basie arrangements (1958-64), Sinatra at the Sands (1966)

Tritone Substitution

Advanced

Replacing V7 with bII7, exploiting the shared tritone between the 3rd and 7th of both chords. Jones used this as a signature reharmonization technique in brass voicings that sound inevitable rather than academic.

Sinatra arrangements, walking bass reharmonization

Big Band Orchestration

Advanced

Each instrument section as a module in a larger system. Brass carries foreground; reeds provide inner-voice movement; rhythm section anchors the temporal foundation. The Amiga Principle: 13 musicians producing orchestral complexity.

Hampton band charts (1951-53), Basie modernization

Cross-Genre Production

Advanced

Jazz harmony functioning inside pop/R&B production without losing theoretical depth or commercial accessibility. Architectural translation — theory made invisible while remaining structurally essential.

Off the Wall, Thriller, Bad

Film Score Architecture

Advanced

Through-composed scoring as continuous musical narrative. Leitmotif technique organizes the score as a system of relationships rather than a sequence of moods. Requires understanding the whole before writing any part.

In the Heat of the Night (1967), Roots (1977)

Chromatic Bass Lines

Intermediate

Bass parts moving by half-steps through a progression, creating smooth voice-leading in the lowest register. A jazz reharmonization technique deployed in pop contexts where the theory is invisible but structurally essential.

Billie Jean (1982), Don't Stop 'Til You Get Enough (1979)

Seattle Context

Jackson Street Jazz Corridor

The Jackson Street corridor functioned as a self-contained musical ecosystem created by and for Seattle's Black community. Segregation-era restrictions barred Black performers from downtown venues, concentrating talent, audiences, and venues in the Central District. The resulting hothouse environment produced musicians who developed rapidly through nightly performance.

The Export Pattern

Jones's departure from Seattle to New York and Los Angeles mirrors a pattern repeated across the Central District's musical history: the neighborhood produced extraordinary talent, but the local economy could not sustain careers at the level these artists were capable of. The talent was exported. Jones, Charles, Anderson, and others left Seattle to find the studios, labels, and audiences that could support their ambitions. This pattern — local cultivation, global export — recurs across all 360 degrees of Seattle's musical history.
