## WO' POP -- THE SHOW THAT OPENS THE MAP

### Building the Global Program

Darek Mazzone joined KEXP (then KCMU) in **1996** -- the same year as Cheryl Waters -- and developed **Wo' Pop** as his signature program. The name is a contraction of "World Pop," and the show has become one of KEXP's most distinctive offerings:

- **Musical scope:** Africa (West African highlife, Malian desert blues, Congolese rumba, Ethiopian jazz, South African gqom), South America (Colombian cumbia, Brazilian tropicalia and MPB, Peruvian chicha), Middle East (Moroccan gnawa, Turkish psychedelia, Lebanese electronica), Asia (Indian fusion, Japanese city pop, Indonesian dangdut), Caribbean (reggae, dancehall, soca, zouk), Europe (Balkan brass, Romani music). The scope is genuinely global.
- **Curatorial philosophy:** Mazzone does not treat world music as a homogeneous category. He approaches each regional tradition with specificity -- understanding the history, the context, the evolution of the form. A Malian kora recording is introduced with context about the griot tradition. A cumbia track is placed in the history of tropical dance music. The curation is educated and respectful.
- **Discovery engine:** For many KEXP listeners, Wo' Pop is their primary exposure to non-Western music. The show functions as a discovery engine: introducing artists, labels, and traditions that the listener would never encounter through mainstream media or algorithmic recommendation. This is the most unique contribution Mazzone makes to KEXP's mission.
- **Live sets and in-studio sessions:** Wo' Pop has hosted international artists for KEXP's signature live in-studio sessions. These sessions bring global artists into KEXP's Seattle Center studio, connecting them to the station's worldwide streaming audience. The sessions are among the most-viewed on KEXP's YouTube channel.

### Expanding KEXP Beyond Indie Rock

Wo' Pop serves a specific institutional function:

- **Breaking the genre bubble:** KEXP's identity is rooted in indie rock and alternative music. Wo' Pop expands the station's identity beyond this core -- demonstrating that the curatorial ethos (quality, discovery, context) applies to all music, not just the Western indie canon.
- **Audience expansion:** Wo' Pop brings in listeners who might not otherwise engage with KEXP. World music enthusiasts discover KEXP through Wo' Pop and then explore the station's other programming. The show is a gateway.
- **The KEXP argument:** KEXP's argument for its existence is that human curation matters -- that a trusted curator can introduce you to music you wouldn't find on your own. Wo' Pop is the strongest version of this argument, because the music is the most unfamiliar to the average Western listener. If KEXP can guide you through Moroccan gnawa and make it feel accessible, it can guide you through anything.

## THE GLOBAL EAR -- MUSICAL GEOGRAPHY

### A Map of Sound

Mazzone's Wo' Pop maps the world through music:

- **West Africa:** Highlife (Ghana), Afrobeat (Nigeria/Fela Kuti tradition), Mande music (Mali, Guinea), Sahelian guitar (desert blues -- Tinariwen, Bombino). The African continent is the deepest region in Wo' Pop's rotation.
- **South America:** Cumbia (Colombia, Peru), tropicalia and MPB (Brazil), nueva cancion (Chile, Argentina), chicha (Peru). The rhythmic and melodic richness of Latin American music is a Wo' Pop staple.
- **Middle East and North Africa:** Gnawa (Morocco), rai (Algeria), Turkish psychedelia, Lebanese electronic, Egyptian shaabi. The MENA region provides some of the show's most sonically adventurous material.
- **South and Southeast Asia:** Indian classical and fusion, Bollywood soundtracks, Indonesian dangdut, Thai luk thung. Less represented than Africa and Latin America but growing in Wo' Pop's rotation.
- **Caribbean:** Reggae and dancehall (Jamaica), soca (Trinidad), zouk (French Antilles), calypso. The Caribbean's diasporic influence connects to many other Wo' Pop regions.
- **Eastern Europe and Central Asia:** Balkan brass (Serbia, Romania), Romani music, Turkish and Azerbaijani traditions. The Silk Road musical corridor.

### Cross-Pollination and Fusion

A distinctive feature of Wo' Pop is attention to **cross-pollination** -- music that fuses traditions:

- **Afro-electronic:** African artists combining traditional forms with electronic production (Burna Boy, Black Coffee, Nicola Cruz).
- **Global bass:** The worldwide bass music movement that blends cumbia, dancehall, grime, and electronic production.
- **Diaspora music:** Artists in Western cities creating music that blends their heritage traditions with their adopted environments. London Afrobeats, Paris-based rai, New York cumbia.

Wo' Pop's attention to fusion reflects the reality of contemporary global music: borders are porous, traditions mix, and the most interesting music often happens at the intersections.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Wo' Pop

Mazzone's acoustic contribution is the **soundscape of global music on Seattle radio**:

- **Voice character:** Enthusiastic, informed, rhythmically engaged. Mazzone's voice has a warmth and energy that matches the music -- not the quiet intimacy of Richards or the steady calm of Waters, but a more animated, exploratory quality. The voice of someone genuinely excited about what they're about to play.
- **Pacing:** More dynamic than the Midday Show or Morning Show. Wo' Pop moves between regions and genres with a DJ's sense of flow -- one track leads to another through rhythmic, melodic, or geographic connections. The pacing has more energy, more surprise.
- **Musical selection rhythm:** Wo' Pop's characteristic rhythm is the **geographic journey** -- a set might move from Mali to Colombia to Morocco to Jamaica, connected by rhythm or mood rather than region. The sequencing creates a sense of travel.
- **The KEXP global sound:** Wo' Pop defines a specific dimension of the KEXP sound -- the global dimension. While the Midday Show defines the local/indie sound and the Morning Show defines the personal/eclectic sound, Wo' Pop defines the sound of KEXP as a **world-class station**, not just a Seattle station.

### The DJ as Translator

Mazzone's role goes beyond curation to **translation**:

- **Making the unfamiliar accessible:** The primary challenge of world music programming is bridging the gap between the listener's familiar sonic world and the unfamiliar traditions. Mazzone does this through context (explaining the tradition), connection (drawing parallels to familiar music), and selection (choosing tracks that are musically compelling regardless of the listener's background).
- **Respecting the source:** The translation must not reduce complex traditions to digestible exotica. Mazzone's approach is respectful -- he presents the music on its own terms, with context about its cultural significance, rather than framing it as novelty.
