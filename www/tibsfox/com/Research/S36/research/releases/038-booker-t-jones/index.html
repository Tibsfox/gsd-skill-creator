The Visiting Dignitary

Degree 38 introduces the first artist in the engine explicitly framed as a visitor. The CSV reads “Booker T Jones (Seattle shows)” — the parenthetical is the tell. Jones was born November 12, 1944, in Memphis, Tennessee. He is the leader of Booker T. & the M.G.’s, the house band at Stax Records, and arguably the most famous Hammond B-3 organist in the history of American music. His presence in this engine reflects his repeated performances in Seattle and the Pacific Northwest, and the deep structural connection between the Memphis soul tradition he helped create and the R&B/soul scene that developed in Seattle’s Central District.

“When you walked in the door at Stax, there was absolutely no color.” — Steve Cropper

Stax Records & the M.G.’s

In the summer of 1962, four musicians in the Stax studio — 17-year-old keyboardist Jones, guitarist Steve Cropper, bassist Lewie Steinberg, and drummer Al Jackson Jr. — jammed on a bluesy organ riff during downtime. The result was “Green Onions,” which hit No. 1 on the R&B chart and No. 3 on the Billboard Hot 100, selling over a million copies. Jones was eighteen years old.

The M.G.’s (“Memphis Group”) were two Black musicians (Jones, Jackson) and two white musicians (Cropper, Steinberg — later Donald “Duck” Dunn). In 1962 Memphis, an integrated band was not just unusual — it was dangerous. The records didn’t come with photographs. The music was racially ambiguous. The integration was hidden in plain sight, audible but invisible.

As Stax’s house band, the M.G.’s backed Otis Redding, Sam & Dave, Albert King, Wilson Pickett, Carla Thomas, Eddie Floyd, and dozens of others. They were the engine room of Memphis soul — the musicians who built the sound that other artists inhabited.

The Seattle Connection

Jones has been performing in the Seattle/Pacific Northwest corridor for decades. Documented recent performances include The Triple Door in Seattle (April 2022, October 2025), Kirkland Performance Center (April 2024), and Mount Baker Theatre in Bellingham (October 2025). His typical setlist runs approximately twelve songs, mixing canonical M.G.’s material (“Green Onions,” “Time Is Tight,” “Hip Hug-Her”) with solo work and deep cuts.

Memphis and Seattle developed parallel Black music scenes rooted in the Great Migration, the gospel tradition, and the blues. The Wheedle’s Groove project documented Seattle’s forgotten soul and funk scene of the 1960s–70s — bands who played Central District clubs, recorded on local labels, and were heard on KYAC radio — influenced by the same Stax records Jones was making in Memphis.
