## BAINBRIDGE ISLAND -- FIRST ENGINE ENTRY

### The Island That Produced Andrew Wood

Bainbridge Island enters the Seattle 360 engine for the first time at degree 295. The geographic significance is specific:

- **Bainbridge Island geography.** Bainbridge Island is located in Puget Sound, directly west of Seattle across the Kitsap Peninsula. It is accessible from downtown Seattle by ferry -- a 35-minute crossing from Colman Dock. The island is essentially suburban-to-rural: wooded, residential, with a small downtown (Winslow) and a dramatically different character from Seattle's urban neighborhoods. It is not Capitol Hill. It is not Belltown or the U-District. It is the island across the water.
- **The ferry commute as cultural fact.** Growing up on Bainbridge Island in the 1970s-1980s meant being connected to Seattle's music scene through a ferry ride. This is not incidental: the ferry crossing creates a specific relationship between island-based musicians and the mainland scene. You can access Seattle's clubs, records, and scene, but you are not embedded in it daily. The distance produces a slightly different creative relationship to the city -- more independent, more self-contained, more willing to develop in isolation before connecting to the broader scene.
- **Andrew Wood's upbringing.** Andrew Wood grew up on Bainbridge Island, the son of an aerospace engineer father and an artistic family. His exposure to David Bowie, T. Rex, Elton John, and glam rock came through records and radio in an island setting, removed from the urban punk scene developing in Seattle's Capitol Hill. This geographic isolation helped produce the distinctive combination that Malfunkshun embodied: glam rock's theatricality, punk's aggression, and the Pacific Northwest's specific heaviness.
- **Kevin Wood.** Andrew's brother Kevin Wood played guitar in Malfunkshun. The Wood brothers' collaboration is the musical foundation of the band: Kevin's guitar work against Andrew's vocals and bass, developed on Bainbridge Island before the band moved into Seattle's orbit.

(Sources: Andrew Wood biography; Kevin Wood biography; Bainbridge Island geography; ferry commute cultural analysis; Pacific Northwest island music scene)

## ANDREW WOOD -- THE VOICE AT THE CENTER

### The Most Important Artist You've Never Heard Enough Of

Andrew Wood (January 26, 1966 -- March 19, 1990) is the central figure of the Seattle grunge origin story. His influence on the scene exceeded his commercial exposure during his lifetime:

- **The stage persona.** Wood called himself "L'Andrew, the Love God" on stage. He performed in scarves, tights, ruffled shirts -- full glam regalia. In the denim-and-flannel Seattle scene, this was not mere eccentricity but a principled aesthetic statement: the refusal to dress down, the insistence on theatricality, the claim that rock music could be performed with the full visual amplification of the David Bowie and T. Rex tradition without compromising its power.
- **The voice.** Wood had one of the most naturally gifted voices in the Seattle scene: a tenor range with extraordinary control, capable of the sustained power-rock notes associated with Robert Plant or David Coverdale, but also the vulnerability and emotional openness that distinguished his work from heavy metal's more aggressive vocal tradition. The voice was not learned or manufactured -- it was genuinely organic, the natural instrument of someone who was born to sing this way.
- **The influences.** Wood's primary influences were glam rock: David Bowie (particularly the Ziggy Stardust era), T. Rex (Marc Bolan's acoustic-to-electric transformation), Led Zeppelin (particularly the balladic and epic material), and Aerosmith (the early blues-rock-glam hybrid). These influences are not the punk-and-metal combination that defines most Seattle grunge bands. Wood arrived at the Seattle scene from a completely different direction -- and the collision of his glam sensibility with the scene's heavy guitar tradition produced something genuinely new.
- **Personal warmth.** Wood is remembered by everyone who knew him as a person of unusual generosity, warmth, and charisma. His stage persona -- theatrical, larger-than-life -- was matched by an offstage person who was genuinely kind and enthusiastic about other musicians. Chris Cornell, who would record Temple of the Dog (degree 225) in Wood's honor, described Wood as someone who made everyone around him feel they were witnessing something special. This quality was not separable from the music -- it was the music's source.

(Sources: Andrew Wood biography; Chris Cornell interviews about Wood; Stone Gossard interviews; Jeff Ament interviews; Pearl Jam oral histories; Bruce Pavitt on Andrew Wood)

## MALFUNKSHUN -- THE BAND

### Formation and Identity on Bainbridge Island

- **Formation.** Malfunkshun formed around 1980, when Andrew Wood was approximately 14 years old. The band's formation on Bainbridge Island means it predates the Sub Pop era by several years -- Malfunkshun existed before Sub Pop was a real label, before the grunge aesthetic was named, before the scene had coalesced.
- **The lineup.** The core lineup was the Wood brothers (Andrew on vocals/bass, Kevin on guitar) plus a rotating cast of Bainbridge Island and Seattle-area musicians over the band's eight-year existence. The instability of the lineup outside the Wood brothers reflects Malfunkshun's status as an artistic vehicle primarily for Andrew and Kevin's creative partnership.
- **The sound.** "Glam punk/grunge" is the engine's classification -- and this is accurate. The band combined the theatricality and vocal delivery of glam rock (Bowie, Bolan) with the guitar heaviness and attitude of punk/proto-grunge. The combination was not common in Seattle in 1980-1986: the scene was moving toward a heavier, more earth-toned aesthetic (what would become grunge) while Malfunkshun was insisting on the glam tradition's visual and vocal excess. The synthesis was distinctive, even eccentric, and entirely the product of Andrew Wood's singular vision.
- **Independent operation.** Malfunkshun operated independently -- no major label, no Sub Pop deal, self-booking, self-promoting. Their primary connection to the developing Seattle scene was the *Deep Six* compilation appearance (1986), which placed them in the company of Green River (294), Soundgarden, Melvins, Skin Yard, and U-Men.

(Sources: Malfunkshun history; Kevin Wood biography; glam punk synthesis; Bainbridge Island independent music scene; Deep Six compilation context)

## THE DEEP SIX CONNECTION

### 1986 -- Placed at the Origin

*Deep Six* (1986, C/Z Records) is Malfunkshun's most significant historical document:

- **The compilation's importance.** As established at degree 294 (Green River), *Deep Six* is the founding document of Seattle grunge: six bands, one compilation, recorded by Jack Endino at Reciprocal Recording, released before Sub Pop was a commercial force. Malfunkshun's presence on *Deep Six* alongside Green River places Andrew Wood in the same historical frame as Mark Arm, Steve Turner, Stone Gossard, Jeff Ament, Chris Cornell.
- **Malfunkshun's *Deep Six* tracks.** The compilation includes Malfunkshun tracks that demonstrate the fully developed Wood aesthetic: powerful vocals, glam-influenced approach, heavy guitar, the theatrical delivery that sets them apart from every other band on the record. Hearing *Deep Six* from beginning to end, the Malfunkshun tracks announce themselves as something different -- not punk, not metal, not the emergent grunge template, but something more flamboyant and operatic.
- **The Wood-Gossard-Ament meeting.** Through the *Deep Six* world, Andrew Wood came into contact with Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament -- the Green River duo who shared Wood's ambition for larger rock songs. When Green River dissolved in 1987, Gossard and Ament joined Wood to form Mother Love Bone. This band's formation is the direct consequence of the *Deep Six* scene's cross-pollination. Malfunkshun → Mother Love Bone is the path, and *Deep Six* is where the connection was made.

(Sources: Deep Six compilation; Malfunkshun tracks on Deep Six; Andrew Wood-Gossard-Ament meeting; Mother Love Bone formation; C/Z Records)

## THE GLAM PUNK/GRUNGE SYNTHESIS

### Andrew Wood's Musical Philosophy

Wood's musical approach deserves specific analysis:

- **Glam rock's contribution.** Glam rock (early 1970s, primarily UK) brought theatrical costume, androgyny, melodrama, and the belief that rock performance was a form of show business as well as cultural expression. David Bowie's Ziggy Stardust (1972), T. Rex's *The Slider* (1972), the early Sweet -- these records make extravagance a virtue. Wood absorbed this tradition completely.
- **Proto-grunge's contribution.** The proto-grunge scene in Seattle was developing a different aesthetic: heavy, unfussy, anti-theatrical, flannel-and-boots working-class imagery. The Stooges, Black Sabbath, the Wipers -- music as blunt instrument, not theatrical event.
- **Wood's synthesis.** Wood combined both. The costumes and the stage persona were glam; the guitar sound and the band's heaviness were proto-grunge; the vocal delivery was somewhere between Robert Plant (epic power-rock) and the theatrical commitment of Bowie. This is a genuinely unusual synthesis, and it is Wood's invention. No other band in the Seattle scene was doing this. Green River and Mudhoney would never have dressed in tights; Wood would never have been caught in flannel.
- **The legacy of the synthesis.** When Gossard and Ament joined Wood in Mother Love Bone, they brought their Green River heaviness into contact with Wood's glam sensibility. The result -- the Mother Love Bone sound -- is the direct consequence of Malfunkshun's synthesis scaled up to a full band with major label ambitions. The glam punk/grunge synthesis is Malfunkshun's specific contribution to Seattle rock history.

(Sources: Glam rock history; proto-grunge aesthetic; Wood's synthesis analysis; Mother Love Bone sound genealogy; Robert Plant and David Bowie influence on Wood)

## THE APPROACH OF MOTHER LOVE BONE -- AND WHAT CAME AFTER

### The Story's Next Chapter

Malfunkshun's history is inseparable from its role as the origin point for what followed:

- **Gossard and Ament join Wood.** When Green River dissolved (1987), Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament -- who had been attending Malfunkshun shows and were drawn to Wood's vision -- joined to form Mother Love Bone. This formation is documented at degree 296. Malfunkshun does not continue after Wood's departure: the band's existence was Andrew Wood, and once Wood moved to the next project, Malfunkshun was complete.
- **Kevin Wood's later work.** Kevin Wood continued as a musician after Andrew's death, eventually forming Devilhead and other projects. His connection to the grunge scene through Malfunkshun is documented; his later career is separate from the degree 296 Mother Love Bone story.
- **The *Deep Six* community's ongoing life.** All the musicians on *Deep Six* continued making music after 1986. Soundgarden recorded *Ultramega OK* (1988, SST Records) and then *Louder Than Love* (1989, A&M Records). Green River split into Mudhoney and the core of Mother Love Bone. Skin Yard's Jack Endino continued as the scene's primary producer. The *Deep Six* community did not dissolve -- it transformed, with each band taking its next step. Malfunkshun's next step was Mother Love Bone.

(Sources: Mother Love Bone formation; Kevin Wood career; Deep Six community's evolution; Soundgarden discography; Mudhoney origin)

## LEGACY

### Andrew Wood's Enduring Presence

Malfunkshun's legacy is Andrew Wood's legacy:

- **The voice that influenced everything.** Wood's vocal approach -- the sustained power-rock tenor, the theatrical delivery, the refusal of ironic distance -- can be heard in Eddie Vedder's early Pearl Jam work, in Chris Cornell's most expansive Soundgarden moments, in the general drift of Seattle grunge away from punk's clipped delivery toward more operatic vocal expression. Wood did not invent this -- he absorbed it from Led Zeppelin and glam rock -- but he brought it into the Seattle scene and demonstrated that it belonged there.
- **Temple of the Dog.** Chris Cornell's tribute to Andrew Wood -- the *Temple of the Dog* album (degree 225 in the engine) -- is the most important artifact of Wood's legacy. Cornell wrote songs for Wood because Wood's death required a musical response, not merely a personal one. The album features Gossard, Ament, and the soon-to-be Pearl Jam -- all gathered around the memory of Andrew Wood. Malfunkshun is the first chapter of the story that Temple of the Dog memorializes.
- **What "L'Andrew, the Love God" meant.** Wood's stage persona was not mere costume -- it was a claim about what rock could be. It could be theatrical. It could be camp. It could be beautiful. In the Seattle scene that was choosing flannel and mud-colored album covers, Wood chose gold lamé and called himself the Love God. This was not irony. He meant it.

(Sources: Andrew Wood legacy; Temple of the Dog degree 225; Eddie Vedder vocal influence; Chris Cornell statements about Wood; Pearl Jam oral history; Malfunkshun significance)
