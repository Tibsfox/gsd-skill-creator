Biography & The Builder-Stayer

Thomas Marriott was born in Seattle, Washington in 1979 — the sixteenth artist in the S36 engine, the sixteenth consecutive jazz artist, and the second trumpeter (after Floyd Standifer at degree 4°). He studied jazz at the University of Washington under Marc Seales (degree 13°) and made the choice that defines his structural position in the engine: he stayed.

Where Aaron Parks (degree 14°) used the UW pipeline as a launching pad to Manhattan School of Music at 16 and never returned, Marriott completed his studies in Seattle, built his career in Seattle, and remains in Seattle. The UW pipeline produces both exports and anchors — Marriott is the anchor product, the builder who invested his training back into the local scene.

Marriott became one of Seattle’s most active jazz trumpeters: leading his own groups, recording regularly for Origin Records (Seattle’s premier independent jazz label, founded by John Bishop in 1997), and maintaining a consistent presence at Jazz Alley, Tula’s, the Royal Room, and the Earshot Jazz Festival.

His discography on Origin Records includes Flexicon (2006), Human (2008), Dialogue (2010), and Urban Folklore (2013) — over a decade of consistent output documenting his evolution within the hard bop and post-bop vocabulary.

Marriott has performed with Maynard Ferguson, Benny Golson, and other nationally recognized jazz artists, demonstrating that his chops are competitive at the national level. He chose not to relocate. This is the critical structural distinction: national-level ability, local investment. Where Parks was exported, Marriott could have exported himself and chose not to.

Hard Bop Trumpet & The Origin Records Aesthetic

Marriott’s trumpet vocabulary is rooted in the hard bop tradition as transmitted through the Clifford Brown lineage — the most direct inheritance of the bebop revolution documented at degree 12°, filtered through the hard bop transformation of the late 1950s and refined into a contemporary post-bop vocabulary.

  • Clifford Brown lineage (bebop phrases at brass tempo): Brown demonstrated that bebop’s chromatic vocabulary could be executed on trumpet at comparable speed. Marriott inherits this directly: rapid chromatic runs, arpeggiated chord tones at tempo, and the physical endurance to sustain complex phrases across multiple choruses. Fifteenth chromaticism deployment.
  • Post-bop harmonic language (altered dominants): Altered dominant chords (b9, #9, #11, b13), tritone substitutions as standard vocabulary, upper-structure triads, pentatonic superimposition. The full post-bop harmonic palette deployed as professional standard equipment.
  • The Origin Records sound (documentary realism): Clean recordings, natural acoustic space, minimal post-production. Not the ECM aesthetic (spacious, reverberant) nor the Blue Note/Van Gelder sound (warm compression). A third position: the recording captures the ensemble as it sounds in the room. The label documents the scene rather than defining it.
  • Hard bop rhythmic vocabulary: Swing-to-even-eighths continuum. Predominantly swing-based but flexible enough to straighten when the tune calls for it. The contemporary hard bop player navigates standards (swing), originals (variable), and modern compositions (even eighths).
  • Blue note as brass assertion: Half-valve bend, crushed grace note, scooped approach from below. Brass-specific technique — physical manipulation of the instrument produces microtonal shadings unavailable on piano or guitar. Second brass-specific blue note deployment after Standifer (4°), but at higher energy.
