# Degree 50 -- Mirah: Pass 2 Refinement

## Paired Release v1.49.554 -- NASA 1.51 Alignment

*Cross-referenced with NASA 1.51 (Surveyor 2, September 20 1966 -- midcourse correction failure, vernier engine malfunction, uncontrollable tumble, lunar impact southeast of Copernicus crater. First U.S. lunar probe failure after Surveyor 1's success. Atlas-Centaur launch vehicle performed flawlessly; the spacecraft itself failed).*

---

On September 20, 1966, eight days after Pete Conrad and Dick Gordon splashed down from Gemini 11's record-setting flight, an Atlas-Centaur launched Surveyor 2 from Cape Kennedy's Launch Complex 36A. The launch was perfect. The Atlas stage performed nominal. The Centaur second stage -- still a young and fragile technology, liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen, the highest-energy chemical propellant combination possible -- ignited cleanly and placed the 292-kilogram spacecraft on a translunar trajectory. For sixteen hours, everything worked. Surveyor 2 was on course for its landing site in Sinus Medii, the Bay of the Center, the geometric heart of the lunar nearside. The navigation was good. The spacecraft was healthy. The solar panel was deployed and generating power.

Then, at 16 hours and 11 minutes after launch, the ground controllers at JPL commanded the midcourse correction maneuver. Three vernier engines -- small, precise, designed by the Thiokol Chemical Corporation -- were supposed to fire simultaneously to adjust the trajectory. Vernier engine number 3 failed to ignite. The other two fired normally. The asymmetric thrust immediately sent the spacecraft into an uncontrollable tumble. The spin rate increased. The attitude control system could not compensate. Telemetry became intermittent as the tumbling spacecraft's antenna swept past Earth. JPL sent repeated commands to fire all three verniers, hoping to override whatever had jammed engine number 3. Nothing worked. The tumble was locked in.

Surveyor 2 impacted the Moon on September 23, 1966, southeast of Copernicus crater, at approximately 5.5 degrees North, 12 degrees West. It hit at roughly 6,000 miles per hour. No data was returned from the lunar surface. The mission was a total loss.

Mirah Yom Tov Zeitlyn doesn't tumble. She builds. Slowly, deliberately, with the kind of precision that the Surveyor program's vernier engines were designed to provide -- small corrections, not large burns. Her music is the midcourse correction that actually works: tiny adjustments in arrangement, in lyric placement, in the space between instruments, that keep the trajectory true over albums that span two decades. Where Surveyor 2's single failed engine destroyed the entire mission, Mirah's creative method distributes the thrust across so many small, redundant elements that no single failure can destabilize the whole.

Born in Philadelphia in 1974, raised in various cities as her family moved, Mirah arrived at Evergreen State College in Olympia, Washington, in the early 1990s. Evergreen -- the experimental liberal arts college where you design your own curriculum, where grades are replaced by narrative evaluations, where the institutional philosophy is that education happens through self-directed inquiry. It is the academic equivalent of the K Records ethos: no gatekeepers, no standardized path, no one telling you what the correct sequence is. Phil Elverum was there. Karl Blau was there. The Microphones were forming. K Records was across town. The entire infrastructure of Pacific Northwest DIY music was within walking distance of the campus.

Mirah's first album, *You Think It's Like This But Really It's Like This* (2000, K Records), was recorded in her apartment with Phil Elverum engineering. The title is a thesis statement: the expectation is wrong, the reality is different, and the difference is the art. The production is intimate -- close-miked vocals, acoustic guitar, tape hiss as texture rather than defect. But unlike the lo-fi school that treats low production values as an aesthetic end, Mirah's recordings are precise within their constraints. Every element is placed. The arrangements are spare not because she can't afford more but because she has determined that more would be wrong.

This is the Surveyor resonance, inverted. Surveyor 2 failed because one of three vernier engines didn't fire -- a single point of failure in a system with no redundancy for that specific function. The midcourse correction required all three engines firing simultaneously, and when one failed, the other two made things worse instead of better. Two out of three was not partial success. Two out of three was catastrophe. The asymmetric thrust created a tumble that nothing could stop.

Mirah's production method is the opposite architecture. Her albums are built from many small elements -- voice, guitar, strings, horns, found sounds, electronic textures -- no single one of which is load-bearing. If a string arrangement were removed, the song would change but would not tumble. If the horn were absent, the space it occupied would become meaningful silence, not system failure. The redundancy is in the distribution of creative weight. No single engine. No single point of failure. The midcourse corrections happen continuously, in every arrangement decision, in every mix choice, in every decision about what to leave out. Mirah's albums don't have midcourse correction burns. They have continuous low-thrust guidance -- the creative equivalent of an ion engine rather than a chemical vernier.

The Phil Elverum partnership is the deepest creative collaboration in the Olympia scene documented by this engine. Elverum engineered *You Think It's Like This But Really It's Like This*, co-produced *Advisory Committee* (2003), and collaborated on *Joyride: Remixes* (2003). He also recorded as The Microphones and later Mount Eerie, producing some of the most structurally innovative lo-fi music of the 2000s. The partnership was not a band -- neither artist surrendered their creative autonomy -- but a sustained exchange of engineering sensibility. Elverum's approach to recording -- close, immediate, treating the recording medium itself as an instrument -- informed Mirah's sound without dominating it. Two people working in parallel, sharing tools and methods, maintaining separate trajectories.

Surveyor 2 had no partner. It was alone on its translunar trajectory, receiving commands from JPL 240,000 miles away, with a communication delay that made real-time troubleshooting impossible. When vernier engine 3 failed, the spacecraft could not ask for help. It could not hand the problem to a collaborator. The tumble increased and the telemetry degraded and the mission ended in impact. The isolation was structural: there was one spacecraft, one set of engines, one chance. Mirah's creative practice is structurally the opposite -- embedded in a network of collaborators (Elverum, Blau, the K Records community, Olympia's house-show circuit) that provides exactly the kind of redundancy that Surveyor 2 lacked. When one creative element doesn't work, the network absorbs the failure and routes around it.

The transition from Gemini to Surveyor in the NASA thread marks a fundamental shift in what the engine is documenting. Degrees 43 through 49 followed the Gemini program's crewed flights -- each one a success, each one building capability for Apollo, each one ending with a splashdown and a recovery. The astronauts returned. The data was rich. The failures (Gordon's exhausting EVA, Cernan's fogged visor) were survivable because there were humans in the loop who could adapt. Surveyor 2 had no crew. It was a robot, 292 kilograms of aluminum and solar cells and scientific instruments, following commands that took 1.3 seconds to arrive. When the vernier engine failed, there was no Pete Conrad to talk the spacecraft through it. No Dick Gordon to physically wrestle with the hardware. The machine was alone with its malfunction, and the malfunction won.

This is the first failure in the engine. Fifty degrees of the Seattle 360, fifty-one NASA missions, and every previous mission -- from Explorer 1's discovery of the Van Allen belts through the entire Gemini program -- was a success or at least a partial success. Surveyor 2 is a total loss. Zero data from the lunar surface. Zero science return. The only product of the mission was knowledge of what went wrong, and even that was limited by the degraded telemetry during the tumble.

Mirah's career, by contrast, has no total losses. Albums that received less attention (*Changing Light*, 2014) are not failures -- they are midcourse corrections, adjustments in trajectory that keep the long arc of the career on course. The Olympia-to-Portland move was not a tumble but a planned orbit change. The shift from K Records to Absolutely Kosher to self-release was not a system failure but a series of deliberate navigation decisions. Mirah at degree 50 is the anti-Surveyor: twenty years of continuous, low-thrust guidance, no single point of failure, no catastrophic tumble, the trajectory maintained through redundancy and community and the simple refusal to put all the thrust on three engines when you can distribute it across dozens of small creative decisions.

The vernier engine itself is worth understanding. Thiokol's design for the Surveyor program used a throttleable liquid-propellant engine burning hydrazine and nitrogen tetroxide -- hypergolic propellants that ignite on contact, requiring no ignition system. The vernier engines provided both the midcourse correction capability and the terminal descent thrust for the soft landing. They were the only engines the spacecraft had after the solid-fuel retrorocket separated. Three engines, arranged 120 degrees apart on the spacecraft's underside, providing attitude control through differential throttling: fire one engine harder, the spacecraft tilts away from it. Fire all three equally, the spacecraft descends vertically. The design was elegant and terrifying -- three single-string engines with no backup, each one essential, the failure of any one fatal to the mission.

Surveyor 1, launched May 30, 1966, had used the same engine design and succeeded brilliantly -- the first American soft landing on the Moon, returning over 11,000 photographs. The verniers worked perfectly. Surveyor 2, launched less than four months later with identical hardware, failed on the first midcourse correction. The hardware was the same. The design was the same. The difference was a single valve, a single flow path, a single engine that for reasons never fully determined did not ignite when commanded. The investigation concluded that the most likely cause was a blockage in the fuel line or a failure in the propellant valve of vernier engine 3. The exact mechanism was never confirmed because the evidence was scattered across the lunar surface at 6,000 miles per hour.

This is the lesson that Surveyor 2 teaches, and it is a lesson that resonates with Mirah's creative philosophy: identical systems can produce opposite outcomes. The same design that succeeded on Surveyor 1 failed on Surveyor 2. The same recording approach that produced *You Think It's Like This* produced *Advisory Committee* produced *(a)spทey* produced *Changing Light* -- but each album is different, because the small variations in execution, in context, in the specific humans involved, produce different results from the same method. Mirah's method is resilient not because it eliminates variation but because it accommodates it. Surveyor's design was fragile not because it was bad engineering but because it had no accommodation for the variation that inevitably occurs in mechanical systems.

Evergreen State College connects Mirah to the broader Olympia ecosystem in a way that no other institution in the engine has connected an artist to a city. K Records is a label. KEXP is a radio station. Sub Pop is a business. Evergreen is a school -- a place where people arrive at eighteen and leave transformed, carrying the institutional DNA into everything they do afterward. Mirah arrived at Evergreen and found not just a college but an entire theory of how creative work should happen: self-directed, collaborative, ungraded, evaluated through narrative rather than numbers. The college produced her, and she produced music that embodies its principles. First Evergreen student in the engine. The institution as creative infrastructure.

The midcourse correction that failed on Surveyor 2 was supposed to happen at 16 hours and 11 minutes after launch. The timing was precise. The calculation was correct. The trajectory needed adjustment -- not a large adjustment, but a small one, a few meters per second of delta-v applied in the right direction to shift the impact point from where it was heading (off target) to where it needed to be (Sinus Medii). The correction was small. The failure was total. One engine out of three, a few seconds of asymmetric thrust, and the mission was over. This is the mathematics of fragility: in a system with no redundancy, the distance between success and catastrophe is one valve, one fuel line, one ignition event.

Mirah's music lives in the opposite mathematical space: the mathematics of resilience. Many small elements, no single one essential, the whole greater than the sum not because the parts are extraordinary but because their combination is. The intimate architecture -- her acoustic element, the fifty-first -- is exactly this: a small space with many precisely placed elements creating something that feels larger than its components. The intimate architecture is the anti-vernier: not three large engines that must all fire simultaneously, but dozens of small creative decisions that sustain each other through redundancy.

At degree 50, the engine reaches its own midcourse correction. Fifty degrees out of 360. Just under 14% of the traverse. The first failure in the NASA thread. The Passerellidae streak broken by the Mountain Chickadee's Paridae family. The folk-adjacent run at six consecutive degrees, a new record. And the number itself -- 50 -- carrying a weight that the engine acknowledges without elaborating. Some numbers are just numbers. Some numbers mark where you've been and how far you have left to go. The vernier engines fire or they don't. The traverse continues either way.
