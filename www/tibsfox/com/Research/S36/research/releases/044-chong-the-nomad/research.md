## STRUCTURAL EVENT: First Electronic-Primary Genre -- The Engine Enters the Digital Age

Degree 44 delivers the engine's **first electronic-primary genre stage**. Electronic/R&B at degree 44 is **Genre Stage 25** -- the first time in forty-five degrees that electronic production stands as the primary genre classification. Every prior degree has been rooted in acoustic or analog traditions: jazz, soul, funk, R&B, blues, neo-soul, gospel, rock. At degree 44, the primary instrument is not a voice, not a guitar, not a horn -- it is the **DAW** (digital audio workstation). FruityLoops. The SP-404A sampler. The laptop as instrument.

This is a watershed equal to the arrival of rock-primary at degree 43. The engine has documented the Black American musical tradition from its jazz origins through its soul, funk, R&B, blues, gospel, and rock descendants. Now it documents the generation that inherited all of those traditions and rebuilt them inside a computer. Electronic/R&B does not reject the lineage; it absorbs it. Chong the Nomad's music contains soul vocal textures, R&B harmonic language, hip-hop rhythmic architecture, and the atmospheric depth of ambient electronic music -- all assembled in a DAW by a producer who learned to make beats at age fourteen on FruityLoops.

The R&B qualifier is essential. This is not techno, not house, not EDM in its commercial festival-banger sense. The R&B element acknowledges the vocal intimacy, the emotional content, the love-and-loss thematic territory that runs through Chong the Nomad's catalog. *Love Memo* is literally a diary of heartbreak set to electronic beats. The debut LP *Do We Make of This?* asks its audience to pay attention -- not to dance mindlessly but to listen. The R&B in Electronic/R&B is the emotional substrate; the Electronic is the delivery system.

## STRUCTURAL EVENT: First Self-Released Primary Artist

Chong the Nomad is the engine's **first explicitly self-released artist**. Every prior degree has operated through a record label -- from Mushroom Records (degree 43) to Columbia (degrees 40-42) to Prestige and Impulse (early jazz degrees). Degree 44 marks the point where the engine documents an artist whose primary releases exist outside the label system entirely.

*Love Memo* (2018): self-released digitally on Bandcamp and streaming platforms. *Do We Make of This?* (2024): self-released digitally, priced at $11 or more on Bandcamp. The physical vinyl release of *Love Memo* came through **Crane City Music**, a Seattle independent hip-hop vinyl label run by Gary Campbell, as part of a split LP with Stas THEE Boss -- but Crane City is a pressing operation, not a record label in the A&R/distribution sense. The single "Wrote A Lil Song" with Maiah Manser was released on **Hardly Art** (Sub Pop's subsidiary) -- but as a one-off collaboration, not an artist deal. The Shang-Chi soundtrack credit came through **88rising/Interscope** -- but as a producer-for-hire, not as a signed artist.

Chong the Nomad operates in the modern independent music economy: self-release originals, license to sync (Singapore Airlines, Apple, Universal Audio, Nike, Maybelline, Amazon's *Modern Love*, Freeform's *The Bold Type*, Comedy Central's *Nora from Queens*), produce for other artists (Wafia's Atlantic single "Pick Me," the Shang-Chi soundtrack), DJ for income, and maintain complete creative control. This is the economic model that the internet and the DAW made possible -- the artist as self-contained production unit.

The self-released model is structurally significant because it represents the dissolution of the gatekeeping apparatus that controlled every prior degree in the engine. Quincy Jones needed Mercury Records. Heart needed Mushroom, then Capitol. Chong the Nomad needs Bandcamp, a laptop, and an internet connection.

## Biography: A Line Cook Who Made Beats

### Origins: Indonesian Immigrants, Renton/Kent, Maryland

Alda Agustiano was born circa **1996** (she was described as 22 years old in September 2018 KEXP coverage, 24 in 2020 Sonic Guild coverage, 26 on her website circa 2022, and 29 in a March 2025 NW Asian Weekly article). Her parents are **Indonesian immigrants** who settled in the **Renton/Kent area** of the Seattle metropolitan region -- the suburban corridor south of Seattle proper, along the Green River valley.

The household was musical but not professionally so. Agustiano's parents led a **worship group at their church**, performing traditional Indonesian music. Her father "taught me how to listen" and her mother "taught me how to sing." The family valued music as a **hobby**, not a career path. Traditional Indonesian career expectations -- medicine, law, engineering -- were the presumed destination. Music was something you did on the side.

The family spent time in **Maryland** at some point during Agustiano's youth. While living on the East Coast, she performed annually on **Indonesian Independence Day** in youth choirs and orchestras. She learned **traditional Indonesian folk songs** through these community performances. She has described these experiences as cementing her musicality -- the discipline of performance, the structure of ensemble work, the connection between music and cultural identity.

The musical education began with **piano lessons** as a child, which she disliked. She played **trombone in elementary school band**. She picked up **guitar, ukulele, harmonica, and drums** through self-teaching and experimentation. The multi-instrumental foundation is important: Chong the Nomad is not a laptop-only producer. She is a musician who plays six instruments and chose electronic production as her primary vehicle.

(Sources: NW Asian Weekly, March 2025; KEXP, September 2018; Tom Tom Magazine, September 2018; Roland Articles; Sonic Guild)

### Cornish College of the Arts and the Discovery of FruityLoops

Agustiano attended **Cornish College of the Arts** in Seattle -- the same institution that educated Ann Wilson's contemporaries, trained generations of Pacific Northwest musicians, and operates as one of Seattle's most important art schools. The Cornish connection bridges degree 44 directly to degree 43: Ann Wilson attended Cornish in the late 1960s; Alda Agustiano graduated from Cornish approximately fifty years later. The same institution, two generations, two genres -- rock and electronic.

At Cornish, Agustiano studied **music composition**, initially with the goal of **scoring films**. She spent much of her college career learning to compose for visual media. She also studied **gamelan ensemble** at Cornish -- the traditional Indonesian bronze percussion orchestra that operates outside Western musical theory. She has described gamelan as "one of the most beautiful complex genres ever" and intentionally incorporates gamelan influences into her electronic compositions. Indonesian friends have reported recognizing gamelan textures in her productions.

The transformative discovery came at age **fourteen**, before Cornish, when Agustiano found **FruityLoops** (FL Studio) -- the digital audio workstation that has launched a generation of bedroom producers. She was self-taught. For **seven years** (approximately 2010-2017), she taught herself electronic production, sampling, sound design, and arrangement through experimentation. She tried to imitate other producers. She studied SoundCloud artists. She absorbed the techniques of **Daft Punk** (blending organic and electronic elements), **Pharrell Williams** (production minimalism with maximum impact), and **J Dilla** (sampling as art, swing as identity). She lost herself in other people's sounds before finding her own.

The breakthrough came with **"Pompelo"** (May 2018) -- the track where she stopped imitating and started creating. She has described it as the moment where she applied everything she had learned "in a way I stubbornly wanted to create" rather than copying others. "Pompelo" is equal parts club hit and Merrie Melodies cartoon soundtrack -- playful, surreal, genre-defiant. It marked the birth of the Chong the Nomad sound: whimsical electronic production with emotional depth, impossible to categorize, impossible to ignore.

(Sources: KEXP; Roland Articles; Tom Tom Magazine; NW Asian Weekly; Cornish College of the Arts)

### The Name: Avatar and Gender Neutrality

The stage name **Chong the Nomad** comes from the animated series ***Avatar: The Last Airbender*** -- specifically from a minor character named **Chong**, a nomadic, hippie-like musician who appears in the show. Agustiano chose the name partly for its reference to a character who travels and makes music freely, and partly because it is **gender-neutral**. In the male-dominated landscape of electronic music production, a gender-neutral name meant that listeners would encounter the music before they encountered the gender of the person who made it.

This is a deliberate strategic choice. The electronic music production world has historically assumed male as the default. A gender-neutral name allows the work to precede the identity -- a small but structurally significant act of self-determination.

(Sources: Tom Tom Magazine, September 2018; KEXP)

## Career: From Belltown Yacht Club to the Marvel Red Carpet

### The Beat-Making Competition and First Shows (2017-2018)

In **2017**, Agustiano entered a **beat-making competition** in Seattle. She lost in the second round. But at that competition, she met the person who would become her **manager**. The loss was the beginning.

Her earliest performances were at small Seattle venues. Gary Campbell's documentary ***NEWCOMER: A Seattle Hip-Hop Mixtape*** (2020) captures one of her first shows at the **Belltown Yacht Club** -- Agustiano performing against a brick wall for a small crowd. By the end of the documentary, she is performing at the **Chinatown-International District Block Party** for an audience of five hundred. The growth arc is documented on film: from a handful of people in Belltown to hundreds in the CID, all within the span of the documentary's timeline.

Her **first shows** were in **2018**. The timing aligned with the release of *Love Memo* and "Pompelo." She performed at the **Capitol Hill Block Party** (Seattle's premier neighborhood music festival), **Sasquatch Music Festival** (the now-defunct Gorge Amphitheatre festival that was a Pacific Northwest institution), and the **Upstream Music Festival + Summit**. She opened for **ODESZA** (the Seattle-based electronic duo) and **Death Cab for Cutie** (the Bellingham/Seattle indie rock institution). The ramp from zero to festival stages took approximately one year.

During this period, Agustiano was also working as a **line cook at Kizuki Ramen on Capitol Hill**. She has described the muscle memory of line cooking -- the repetitive physical discipline, the spatial awareness, the ability to execute complex sequences under pressure -- as directly transferable to music production. Memorizing a ramen recipe and memorizing a melodic line are, in her telling, the same cognitive operation.

(Sources: KEXP; NEWCOMER documentary; Respect My Region; EverOut; WASH Magazine)

### *Love Memo* (2018) -- The Debut EP

***Love Memo*** was released on **Valentine's Day 2018** -- a self-released digital EP that became the most acclaimed Seattle debut of the year. The EP earned **"Top Album" recognition** from:

- **The Seattle Times** (2018 Record of the Year)
- **Seattle Weekly**
- **The Stranger**
- **KEXP** (Seattle's globally influential public radio station)
- **107.7 The End** (Locals Only Artist of the Month)

The EP was Agustiano's diary -- a document of heartbreak following the end of a relationship, written during a summer of solitude. The tracks are intimate, vulnerable, and structurally adventurous: electronic beats carrying confessional emotional content. *Love Memo* arrived at the intersection of bedroom pop, lo-fi hip-hop, and R&B -- genres that were converging in the late 2010s into something that didn't have a clean label yet.

In **October 2018**, Crane City Music released *Love Memo* as a **split vinyl LP** with Stas THEE Boss's *S'Women* -- **750 individually numbered copies** on splattered colored vinyl, with a reversible cover designed by Seattle artist Jennifer Campbell. The split was Crane City founder Gary Campbell's idea: two queer women of color producers sharing a record, their EPs thematically linked by love and loss. The vinyl pressing gave *Love Memo* a physical artifact -- a collector's item from Seattle's independent hip-hop infrastructure.

## Production Philosophy: The Laptop as Instrument

### Tools

Chong the Nomad's primary production tools:

- **FL Studio (FruityLoops)** -- the DAW she has used since age fourteen. Self-taught. Seven years of experimentation before the sound clicked.
- **Roland SP-404A sampler** -- a hardware sampler widely used in lo-fi hip-hop and beat-making. Adds physical tactility to digital production.
- **iPhone voice memo function** -- for capturing ideas in the moment. She hums melodies, beatboxes rhythms, records environmental sounds, and later translates these fragments into productions.
- **Roland keyboard** -- for melodic input and live performance.
- **Sawtooth wave synthesizers** -- her preferred lead sound. The sawtooth wave is harmonically rich, containing all harmonics in a series that decreases in amplitude, producing a bright, buzzy timbre that cuts through a mix.
- **808 bass elements** -- the Roland TR-808 drum machine's bass drum, pitched down and sustained, is the foundation of modern hip-hop and electronic bass. Agustiano uses it as a structural element.
- **Chopped vocal samples** -- manipulated for pitch, texture, and unrecognizability. Vocals become instruments rather than carriers of lyrics.
- **Field recordings** -- environmental sounds captured and processed into percussive or textural elements.

### Technique

Agustiano's production technique centers on **experimentation over formula**. She has described her process as: "It's all about experimentation -- knowing what you want in your head and trying to produce that." She does not start with a genre template. She starts with an internal sonic image and works backward to realize it.

The discovery that "if I chop up my claps, it leaves a space in the mix" illustrates her approach: each production technique is discovered through experiment, not textbook. The gap left by a chopped clap becomes a rhythmic feature. The absence becomes the sound.

The vocal treatment on *Do We Make of This?* is particularly notable. Agustiano delivers vocals with what WASH Magazine described as "eccentric contours" -- heavy effects processing, vocals mixed so low they function as subliminal texture rather than primary melodic content. This is a deliberate choice: the voice as one layer in a dense electronic field, not the star sitting atop the arrangement. It inverts the R&B convention where the voice dominates; in Chong the Nomad's music, the voice is embedded.

(Sources: Roland Articles; Tom Tom Magazine; WASH Magazine; KEXP)

## Musical Influences: Sophie, Daft Punk, J Dilla, and Gamelan

### Sophie Xeon (1986-2021)

Agustiano has identified **Sophie** (Sophie Xeon) as her **"North star"** and **"one of my main influences."** Sophie's 2018 album *Oil of Every Pearl's Un-Insides* was a transformative listening experience for Agustiano: "It's just her, and I've always gone back on that." Sophie's approach -- radical sound design, the obliteration of genre boundaries, the creation of sonic textures that sounded like nothing that existed before -- gave Agustiano permission to stop imitating and start inventing. Sophie died in January 2021 at age 34 in an accidental fall in Athens, Greece. The influence persists in every Chong the Nomad production that prioritizes texture over convention.

### Daft Punk and Pharrell Williams

Daft Punk's ability to blend organic and electronic elements -- live-sounding drums inside machine-generated arrangements, human voices processed through vocoders, warmth inside technology -- is a direct antecedent to Chong the Nomad's approach. Pharrell's minimalism -- the ability to make a hit record from three sounds and a swing feel -- informs the stripped-back moments in Agustiano's productions.

### J Dilla (1974-2006)

J Dilla's revolutionary approach to sampling -- the swing, the imperfection, the way a sample can be chopped and reassembled into something emotionally new -- runs through Chong the Nomad's use of vocal and instrumental samples. Dilla's influence is audible in the way Agustiano's beats breathe: they are not quantized to a rigid grid but swing with organic imprecision.

### Gamelan

Agustiano studied **gamelan ensemble** at Cornish College. Gamelan is the traditional ensemble music of Java and Bali -- bronze metallophones, gongs, drums, and sometimes vocals, organized in interlocking rhythmic patterns called **kotekan** (in Balinese gamelan) that create composite melodies from individual parts. Gamelan operates outside Western tonal and rhythmic systems: the tuning is non-tempered, the rhythmic structures are cyclical rather than linear, and the relationship between parts is communal rather than hierarchical.

Agustiano has said she intentionally translates gamelan influences into her electronic productions. Indonesian friends have reported recognizing gamelan textures in her music. This is structurally significant for the engine: it represents the **first non-Western musical tradition** embedded in the production DNA of an engine artist. Jazz, soul, R&B, blues, gospel, and rock are all Western (specifically Black American) traditions. Gamelan introduces an Indonesian sonic vocabulary into the engine's harmonic and rhythmic palette.

### Benny Blanco and Solange Knowles

**Benny Blanco** (pop production maximalism) and **Solange Knowles** (art-R&B, *A Seat at the Table*) are cited influences. Blanco represents the commercial production world Agustiano has touched through sync placements; Solange represents the artistic R&B world that informs Chong the Nomad's emotional register.

(Sources: NW Asian Weekly; Roland Articles; Tom Tom Magazine; KEXP)

## The Forty-Fifth Acoustic Element: The Grain

### Acoustic Progression: Forty-Five Elements

The forty-fifth acoustic element is **the grain** -- the sonic phenomenon produced when a sound source is decomposed into microscopic grains (typically 1-100 milliseconds in duration), individually processed, and reassembled into a texture that shimmers between recognition and abstraction. The grain is the acoustic signature of electronic production at its most sophisticated: the moment where a vocal sample, a field recording, or a synthesizer tone is broken into particles so small that the original source becomes unrecognizable, yet the emotional quality of the source persists as a luminous, shimmering texture.

Chong the Nomad's productions use granular techniques extensively. Her chopped vocal samples -- manipulated for pitch and texture until unrecognizable -- produce the grain. The wordless vocal harmonies that open "Find Me Here" shimmer because they have been processed through granular synthesis or sample-slicing techniques that break the voice into particles. The "stuttering fragments of noise" that WASH Magazine identified in the album's breakdowns are granular events: the beat decomposed into grains, each grain individually processed, the whole reassembled into a texture that shimmers at the boundary between rhythm and ambient noise.

The grain connects to the engine's acoustic progression as the first element describing **digital production physics**. The previous element, the radiance (degree 43, Ann Wilson), described the physics of analog vocal projection -- overtones generated by a mezzo-soprano at rock volume. The grain describes the physics of digital audio manipulation -- particles generated by a DAW operator slicing sound into grains. Both are acoustic phenomena; one is produced by a human body in a room, the other by a human mind operating a computer. The progression from radiance to grain is the progression from analog to digital, from the voice that fills a room to the laptop that fills headphones.

The grain also connects to the gamelan influence. Gamelan's interlocking kotekan patterns produce a composite shimmer: individual metallophone parts that are incomplete alone but create a shimmering whole when combined. Agustiano's electronic granular techniques achieve the same effect through digital means. The gamelan shimmer and the grain are structural analogues across acoustic and digital domains.

## The Forty-Fifth Artist-City Pattern: The Bedroom Architect

### From Lifer to Bedroom Architect

The forty-fifth artist-city pattern is **the bedroom architect** -- an artist who builds an entire sonic world from a bedroom, home studio, or personal production space within the city, without the institutional scaffolding of labels, studios, or industry gatekeepers. The bedroom architect's relationship to the city is intimate and self-contained: the city provides the cultural context (Seattle's electronic and hip-hop scene, KEXP's radio support, Capitol Hill's venues, the Crane City Music vinyl community), but the actual creative work happens in a private space with a laptop and headphones. The city is the soil; the bedroom is the greenhouse.

Chong the Nomad is the bedroom architect of Seattle's electronic music scene. Her first productions were made in her personal space with FruityLoops. Her iPhone voice memos captured ideas in transit. Her SP-404A sampler sat in a home setup. The creative process -- from hum to beatbox to finished production -- occurred inside a private production environment. The city entered the work through cultural osmosis: Capitol Hill's live music scene, KEXP's playlist curation, the Crane City Music community, the 206 Zulu hip-hop organization, the CID Block Party -- all external inputs that were absorbed and transformed in the private studio.

The bedroom architect is distinct from the scene native (degree 41, Shawn Smith), who is embedded in the city's public music spaces. The scene native plays the clubs, sits in on sessions, is physically present in the scene. The bedroom architect may perform at those venues but creates the work in isolation. The bedroom architect is also distinct from the lifer (degree 43, Ann Wilson), whose city connection spans a lifetime of public activity. The bedroom architect's city connection is private, internal, and mediated through technology.

The pattern reflects the structural transformation of how music is made in the twenty-first century. ## The Forty-Fourth Blue Note: The Chopped Bend

### Blue Note Deployment 44

The forty-fourth blue note deployment is **the chopped bend** -- the blue note captured in a vocal sample, sliced into fragments, pitch-shifted, and reassembled inside an electronic production so that the emotional content of the blues bend persists even though the original vocal is unrecognizable. The chopped bend is the blue note in the age of the DAW: the flatted third or seventh not sung live but sampled, chopped, processed, and embedded in a beat as a textural element that carries the ghost of the blues tradition inside digital production.

Chong the Nomad's vocal sample manipulation produces chopped bends throughout her catalog. The vocal fragments in *Love Memo* and *Do We Make of This?* carry the emotional inflection of R&B vocal bending -- the rise and fall of pitch that signals longing, loss, tenderness -- but delivered through digital processing that abstracts the bend into pure texture. The listener feels the blue note without hearing a traditional vocal performance. The emotional content survives the technology.

The chopped bend connects to the engine's blue note genealogy as the first **digitally mediated** blue note deployment. All forty-three prior deployments have been analog: singers bending notes with their voices, guitarists bending strings with their fingers. The chopped bend passes through a digital intermediary. The voice sings the bend; the DAW captures it; the producer slices it; the beat carries it. The blue note's emotional DNA survives four stages of digital processing. This is the proof that the blues tradition is not dependent on analog delivery -- it persists through any medium.

The chopped bend connects specifically to the power bend (degree 43, Ann Wilson). The power bend is the blue note at maximum analog volume -- sustained, projected, room-filling. The chopped bend is the blue note at maximum digital density -- fragmented, processed, headphone-filling. Consecutive deployments, opposite media: one analog, one digital. One fills a room; the other fills a frequency spectrum.

Blue note deployment count: **44 across 45 degrees** (continuous from degree 0).

## Indonesian Heritage and the Gamelan Bridge

### The First Non-Western Musical Tradition in the Engine

The engine has documented forty-five degrees of music rooted in Western (specifically American) musical traditions. Jazz, soul, funk, R&B, blues, gospel, rock, and electronic music all operate within Western tonal systems, rhythmic conventions, and harmonic languages (with African-derived rhythmic traditions as a foundational layer).

At degree 44, **gamelan** enters the engine. Gamelan is fundamentally non-Western: its tuning systems (**slendro** -- a five-note near-equal temperament, and **pelog** -- a seven-note non-equal temperament) do not correspond to the twelve-tone equal temperament that underlies all Western music in the engine. Its rhythmic structures are cyclical (organized around a **gong cycle** called **gongan**) rather than the linear verse-chorus structures of Western popular music. Its ensemble philosophy is communal: no individual player carries the melody; the melody emerges from the interlocking of all parts.

Agustiano's integration of gamelan into electronic production creates a **cultural bridge** between Indonesian traditional music and American electronic music. When her Indonesian friends recognize gamelan textures in her beats, they are hearing the survival of a non-Western musical tradition inside a Western digital production framework. The gamelan survives the DAW. The interlocking metallophone patterns survive as interlocking synthesizer layers. The gong cycle survives as the structural loop. The non-tempered tuning survives as the "alien" quality that WASH Magazine identified in her productions -- the sense that her music "doesn't sound quite like anything else."

(Sources: NW Asian Weekly; Cornish College; ethnomusicology -- gamelan; WASH Magazine)
