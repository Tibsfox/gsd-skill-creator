Biography & “It Is Written”

Maktub formed in Seattle in 1998 on Capitol Hill — the neighborhood that had been Seattle’s bohemian center since the grunge era waned and the arts community redistributed itself across the city’s hills. The name is Arabic for “it is written” — fate, destiny. What the band created over nine years was, in retrospect, something that had to exist: a fusion of neo-soul, funk, jazz improvisation, and rock energy that was simultaneously all of those things and none of them exclusively.

The lineup: Reggie Watts (vocals, keyboards), Kevin Goldman (guitar), Alex Veley (bass, keyboards), and Davis Martin (drums). Watts is the same Reggie Watts documented at degree 17° — this is the first time an artist appears at multiple degrees in the engine. But the Watts who fronted Maktub was a soul singer — raw, powerful, channeling the neo-soul movement that D’Angelo, Erykah Badu, and Maxwell were defining nationally, filtered through a band that could also play straight-ahead rock, jazz improvisation, and deep funk.

The band thrived in Capitol Hill’s post-grunge incubation space — venues like the Crocodile Cafe, the Showbox, Neumo’s, and the Capitol Hill block party circuit. Rock clubs, hip-hop shows, DJ nights, and jazz jams shared the same venues and audiences. Maktub flourished because the neighborhood did not require genre purity.

Released three albums: Khronos (2002, Velour), Say What You Mean (2003, Velour), and Subtle Ways (2006, Livewire). Disbanded in 2007 when Watts moved to New York City to pursue a solo comedy and music career. The band was the incubator; the solo career was the deployment.

Third Genre Shift: Soul/Jazz — Jazz Becomes Method

Third genre shift in three degrees. The engine diversifies rapidly: (1) single genre (degrees 0–15, “Jazz”), (2) compound genre (degree 16, “Jazz Fusion”), (3) superposition (degree 17, “Jazz/Comedy”), (4) Jazz as modifier (degree 18, “Soul/Jazz”). For the first time, Jazz is not the primary genre label but the secondary element. Soul is the what; jazz is the how. The center of gravity shifts from jazz-as-genre to jazz-as-method.

Neo-soul artists like D’Angelo and Erykah Badu demonstrated nationally that soul music could absorb jazz harmony without becoming jazz. Maktub demonstrated the same locally, in Seattle, with the added dimension of rock energy and live improvisation.

Capitol Hill: Second Neighborhood Thread

Second Capitol Hill artist after Wayne Horvitz (degree 2°). Capitol Hill in the late 1990s through mid-2000s was arguably the center of Seattle’s live music scene. Rock clubs, hip-hop shows, DJ nights, and jazz jams shared venues and audiences — the neighborhood’s cultural metabolism was omnivorous, genre-agnostic, live-performance-centered.

Two entries, two decades apart, two completely different genre orientations: Horvitz (composed improvisation, experimental music, artist-as-institution) and Maktub (neo-soul/funk/jazz/rock, band-as-collective). Capitol Hill produces diversity the same way Cornish College does — the environment is permissive enough that artists do not need to conform.

Music Theory: The Groove as Harmonic Vehicle

  • Neo-soul harmony (extended jazz chords in groove context): Major 9ths, minor 11ths, altered dominants, suspended structures — placed over a groove where rhythm provides the structural framework and chords provide color. Harmonic movement through voice-leading within chords rather than chord-to-chord functional progression. The groove IS the composition.
  • Band arrangement (emergent composition): Four musicians listening and responding in real-time. No single composer directing. The groove as shared contract enabling collective exploration. Composed arrangement (top-down) vs. band arrangement (bottom-up) — the democratic alternative.
  • Live improvisation as collective composition: Shows heavily improvised — four-minute studio tracks becoming fifteen-minute live jams. Band improvisation is a conversation: four streams of consciousness negotiating coherence. The groove is the contract.
  • The groove as composition: The rhythm section IS the music. Bass line, drum pattern, and rhythmic feel ARE the composition. Vocals, guitar solos, keyboard pads decorate the groove. Extends Berry (5°) groove-as-composition principle from solo piano to full band.

Career Arc

1998 — Formation, Capitol Hill, Seattle

Four-piece band formed on Capitol Hill: Watts (vocals, keys), Goldman (guitar), Veley (bass, keys), Martin (drums). The name “Maktub” — Arabic for “it is written” — chosen for its fatalistic resonance. Capitol Hill scene: Crocodile, Showbox, Neumo’s.

2002 — Khronos (Velour Recordings)

Debut album establishing the Maktub sound: neo-soul vocals over funk grooves with jazz harmony and rock energy. The title (from Greek chronos, time) reflects the band’s temporal relationship with live improvisation.

2003 — Say What You Mean (Velour Recordings)

Second album pushing further into soul territory. More confident songwriting, tighter arrangements, still built on live-performance energy.

KEXP Sessions

Multiple in-studio performances at KEXP (90.3 FM) capturing the band’s live energy in a controlled setting. Among the most vivid recordings of Maktub’s improvisational approach.

2006 — Subtle Ways (Livewire Records)

Third and final album — the most polished Maktub. Studio craft meeting live energy: delicate ballads alongside explosive funk workouts, intimate vocals alongside full-band improvisation.

2007 — Disbandment

Disbanded when Watts moved to New York City to pursue solo comedy and music. Everything developed in Maktub — vocal power, improvisational instinct, genre-crossing — transferred to the solo context documented at degree 17°. The band was the incubator; the solo career was the deployment.
