## Biography & "It Is Written"

Maktub -- the word is Arabic for **"it is written"**, carrying connotations of fate, destiny, and the inevitability of what must unfold. The band formed in **Seattle in 1998** on **Capitol Hill**, the neighborhood that had been Seattle's bohemian center since the grunge era waned and the arts community redistributed itself across the city's hills. The name was prescient: what Maktub created over the next nine years was, in retrospect, something that had to exist -- a band that fused neo-soul, funk, jazz improvisation, and rock energy into a sound that was simultaneously all of those things and none of them exclusively.

The lineup: **Reggie Watts** (vocals, keyboards), **Kevin Goldman** (guitar), **Alex Veley** (bass, keyboards), and **Davis Martin** (drums). Watts is the same Reggie Watts documented at degree 17 -- this is the **first time an artist appears at multiple degrees** in the S36 engine. But the Watts who fronted Maktub was a different creative entity than the solo loop-artist who would later become the Late Late Show bandleader. In Maktub, Watts was a **soul singer** -- raw, powerful, channeling the neo-soul movement that D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, and Maxwell were defining nationally, but filtered through a band that could also play straight-ahead rock, jazz improvisation, and deep funk. The solo Watts (degree 17) used technology (loop station, beatbox) to become a one-man orchestra. The Maktub Watts used a band -- four humans, four instruments, collective improvisation, the groove as a shared creation rather than a solo construction.

Goldman's guitar provided the rock edge -- overdriven tones, wah-pedal funk, and clean jazz voicings depending on what the moment demanded. Veley anchored the bottom end on bass while also adding keyboard textures, creating a harmonic bed that could shift from neo-soul warmth to jazz tension in a single bar. Martin's drumming drove the energy -- hard-hitting, funk-inflected, capable of the pocket grooves that neo-soul demands and the explosive fills that rock energy requires.

The band was based at the **Capitol Hill music scene** -- venues like the Crocodile Cafe, the Showbox, Neumo's, and the Capitol Hill block party circuit. Capitol Hill in the late 1990s and early 2000s was the post-grunge incubation space: rock clubs, hip-hop shows, DJ nights, and jazz jams shared the same venues and often the same audiences. Maktub thrived in this environment precisely because the audience did not require genre purity. A Capitol Hill crowd in 2002 could absorb neo-soul harmonies, funk grooves, rock power, and jazz improvisation in a single set because that was the neighborhood's cultural metabolism -- omnivorous, genre-agnostic, live-performance-centered.

(Source: Seattle Weekly; The Stranger; KEXP session archives; AllMusic; Velour/Livewire Records catalog; local press archives and reviews)

### The First Band: Creative Unit Shifts from Individual to Group

Every previous degree in the S36 engine has featured a **solo artist** or a **bandleader** -- a single creative authority around whom the music orbits. Quincy Jones (0) was an arranger-producer. Bill Frisell (1) was a guitarist who collaborated but whose name was on the project. Horvitz (2) led ensembles but the vision was his. Anderson (3) was a vocalist. This pattern held through all eighteen prior degrees: one name, one creative center, one person whose artistic identity defined the project.

Maktub is the **first project** -- a **band as collaborative entity**. The creative unit shifts from individual to group. While Watts was the frontman and primary vocalist, Maktub's sound was not "Reggie Watts plus backing band." Goldman's guitar, Veley's bass and keys, and Martin's drums were not accompaniment -- they were co-authors. The improvisation was collective. The arrangements emerged from four people listening and responding in real-time. The band's name was on the records, not Watts's name. This is a structural shift in the engine: the first time the creative subject is a **plural entity**.

The shift matters because it changes what "composition" means. In prior degrees, composition was either a solo act (Frisell's guitar compositions, Parks's piano compositions) or a top-down direction (Jones arranging an orchestra, Horvitz composing for ensembles). In Maktub, composition was **emergent** -- the band played, listened, and the music took shape through collective action. This is a fundamentally different creative model, and the engine must track it: when is the artist a person, and when is the artist a group?

## Third Genre Shift: Soul/Jazz

### The Engine Diversifies Rapidly

Degree 18 marks the **third genre shift** in three degrees. The sequence:

| Degree | Genre Label | Shift Type |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| 0-15 | Jazz (with subgenre variations) | Single genre, stable |
| 16 | Jazz Fusion | First shift: single to compound |
| 17 | Jazz/Comedy | Second shift: compound to superposition |
| **18** | **Soul/Jazz** | **Third shift: new primary genre (Soul) with Jazz as modifier** |

The progression is structurally significant. At degree 16, Jazz gained a modifier (Fusion). At degree 17, Jazz shared space with a non-music discipline (Comedy). At degree 18, **Jazz becomes the modifier** -- "Soul/Jazz" puts Soul first. For the first time in the engine, Jazz is not the primary genre label but the secondary element. The center of gravity has shifted.

This is not the same as genre superposition (degree 17, where both modes operated at full intensity without subordination). Soul/Jazz is a **hierarchy**: the band's primary mode is soul music -- the vocal power, the groove, the emotional directness -- and jazz provides the harmonic vocabulary and improvisational framework. Soul is the what; jazz is the how. Neo-soul artists like D'Angelo and Erykah Badu demonstrated nationally that soul music could absorb jazz harmony without becoming jazz. Maktub demonstrated the same thing locally, in Seattle, with the added dimension of rock energy and live improvisation.

The genre counter now reads: **eighteen consecutive jazz-adjacent degrees** (0-17 had "Jazz" in the primary or compound label), and degree 18 where Jazz appears as a **modifier** rather than the primary tag. The jazz arc does not end here -- it evolves. Jazz is now a technique, a vocabulary, a toolkit that other genres employ. This is the shift from jazz as genre to **jazz as method**.

### First Multi-Degree Artist

Reggie Watts appears at **both degree 17 (solo) and degree 18 (Maktub)**. This is the first time an artist appears at multiple degrees in the S36 engine. The dual appearance is not redundant -- it demonstrates that the **same person can produce radically different music in different contexts**.

| Dimension | Degree 17 (Solo) | Degree 18 (Maktub) |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Creative unit | Solo artist | Band member (one of four) |
| Primary instrument | Voice / beatbox / loop station | Voice (singing) |
| Genre | Jazz/Comedy | Soul/Jazz |
| Technology | Loop station, one microphone | Full band: guitar, bass, keys, drums |
| Composition method | Solo improvisation, real-time looping | Collective improvisation, band arrangement |

| Audience relationship | One-man show, direct engagement | Band performance, collective projection |
| Temporal range | 2000s-present | 1998-2007 (disbandment) |

The Maktub project **preceded** the solo career that degree 17 documents. Maktub was where Watts **developed** the vocal and improvisational skills that would later define his solo work. The band was the workshop; the solo career was the product. The chronological order matters: Maktub (1998-2007) came first, then the solo career that led to Comedy Bang! Bang!, TED talks, and The Late Late Show (2010s onward). When Maktub disbanded in 2007 -- Watts moving to New York to pursue comedy -- the collaborative framework dissolved but the individual capabilities transferred. Everything Watts could do with a loop pedal, he first learned to do with a band.

The dual-degree structure demonstrates that the S36 engine is not a catalog of people but a catalog of **projects**. A project is a specific creative configuration: an artist (or artists), a genre context, an era, a location, and a set of relationships. Watts-as-solo-artist is a different project than Watts-as-Maktub-frontman, even though the same person occupies the center of both. The engine tracks projects, not biographies.

## Music Theory: The Groove as Harmonic Vehicle

### Neo-Soul Harmony: Extended Jazz Chords in a Groove Context

Maktub's harmonic language descends from the **neo-soul movement** that emerged nationally in the mid-1990s. Neo-soul harmony takes the extended chord voicings of jazz -- major 9ths, minor 11ths, altered dominants, suspended structures -- and places them in a **groove context** where the rhythm section provides the structural framework and the chords provide color rather than functional direction.

In traditional jazz harmony, chords follow functional progressions (ii-V-I patterns, circle-of-fifths movement) where each chord creates tension that resolves to the next. In neo-soul harmony, chords **float** -- a minor 9th chord might sustain for eight bars while the bass and drums establish a groove, and the harmonic movement happens through **voice-leading within the chord** (inner voices shifting, extensions changing) rather than through chord-to-chord motion. The groove IS the composition; the harmony provides the atmosphere.

Maktub operated in this harmonic space but with jazz musicians' command of the language. Goldman's guitar voicings drew from jazz chord vocabulary (close-voiced extensions, altered tensions) deployed in a funk/rock rhythmic context. Veley's bass lines combined the deep pocket of R&B bass playing with the harmonic awareness of a jazz bassist -- implying chord changes through bass motion rather than following a written chart. This is the **eighth keyboard/harmony node** in the theory branch, but the first deployed primarily in a soul/groove context rather than a jazz or fusion context.

### Band Arrangement: Multiple Instruments Creating a Unified Voice

The theory of band arrangement in Maktub differs from the ensemble writing documented at prior degrees. Horvitz (degree 2) wrote composed parts for his ensembles -- each instrument had a specific role prescribed by the composer. Jones (degree 0) arranged orchestras -- every note notated, every voicing deliberate. Maktub's arrangement was **emergent**: four musicians listening in real-time, each finding their space, the arrangement coalescing through collective intuition rather than prescription.

This is the difference between **composed arrangement** (top-down, one person's vision distributed to many instruments) and **band arrangement** (bottom-up, many instruments finding a collective voice through real-time negotiation). The band arrangement model is the democratic alternative to the composer model -- no one person controls the outcome. The Eighteenth chromaticism deployment: **groove chromaticism** -- chromatic movement embedded in the bass line and guitar voicings within a groove context, where the chromatic passing tones serve the funk feel rather than melodic development.

### Live Improvisation as Composition

Maktub shows were **heavily improvised** -- different every night, the same songs becoming unrecognizable vehicles for collective exploration. A song that ran four minutes on a studio recording might become a fifteen-minute jam live, the band following wherever the groove led. This connects to the composition-improvisation spectrum tracked since degree 0: Maktub sits at the **collective improvisation** end, the first band (rather than solo artist) to occupy that extreme position.

The distinction from Watts's solo improvisation (degree 17) is structural: solo improvisation is one person's stream of consciousness, constrained only by the performer's own imagination and technique. Band improvisation is a **conversation** -- four streams of consciousness negotiating in real-time, constrained by the need to maintain coherence among four independent voices. The constraint makes the creativity different: solo improvisation can go anywhere; band improvisation must go somewhere everyone can follow. The groove is the contract that makes band improvisation possible -- as long as everyone holds the groove, the improvisation can explore freely above it.

The eighteenth blue note deployment: **soul-vocal blue note** -- Watts's Maktub vocals employed blue notes in the neo-soul tradition, bending between minor and major thirds with the emotional directness of soul singing rather than the instrumental precision of jazz. The soul-vocal blue note descends from the vocal tradition (Anderson degree 3) but operates in a groove context rather than a ballad context, and at energy-3 rather than energy-2. The blue note as emotional weapon: raw, unmediated, deployed for maximum affective impact within the groove.

## The Groove as Composition: The Rhythm Section IS the Music

### What Degree 18 Reveals About Composition

Maktub's core insight is that **the groove is not the background to the composition -- the groove IS the composition**. In Western art music and most jazz, the melody and harmony are "the music" and the rhythm section "accompanies." In soul, funk, and groove-based music, this hierarchy inverts: the bass line, the drum pattern, and the rhythmic feel ARE the music, and everything else -- vocals, guitar solos, keyboard pads -- decorates the groove.

This inversion maps to the engine's ongoing composition-improvisation spectrum. Jones (degree 0) composed for orchestras -- melody and harmony as primary, rhythm as support. Frisell (degree 1) composed atmospheric textures -- harmony as primary, rhythm dissolved into ambient space. Berry (degree 5) played groove-based piano -- rhythm and harmony fused in the left hand. Maktub completes the inversion: four instruments dedicated to **groove-first composition** where the rhythmic feel takes absolute priority.

The Amiga Principle holds but changes shape: instead of one person maximizing output from minimal resources (Watts solo, degree 17), four people create a unified output that exceeds what any of them could produce individually. This is the **collaborative Amiga Principle** -- resource efficiency through collective synergy rather than individual versatility. Four instruments producing one groove is more resource-efficient than four instruments each pursuing separate compositional goals.

## Career Arc

### Formation -- Capitol Hill, Seattle (1998)

Formed in Seattle's Capitol Hill neighborhood in 1998. Four members: Reggie Watts (vocals, keyboards), Kevin Goldman (guitar), Alex Veley (bass, keyboards), Davis Martin (drums). The name "Maktub" (Arabic: "it is written") chosen for its fatalistic resonance. Capitol Hill scene: Crocodile Cafe, Showbox, Neumo's, Capitol Hill block parties.

### *Khronos* (2002)

Debut album released on **Velour Recordings**. *Khronos* established the Maktub sound: neo-soul vocals over funk grooves with jazz harmony and rock energy. The album title (from the Greek *chronos*, time) reflects the band's relationship with temporality -- live performances that stretched songs beyond studio constraints, improvisation as time-exploration.

(Source: AllMusic; Velour Recordings catalog)

### *Say What You Mean* (2003)

Second album, also on **Velour Recordings**. *Say What You Mean* pushed further into soul territory while maintaining the jazz-informed improvisation. More confident songwriting, tighter arrangements, but still built on the live-performance energy that defined the band.

(Source: Seattle Weekly review; AllMusic)

### *Subtle Ways* (2006)

Third and final album, released on **Livewire Records**. *Subtle Ways* represented the most polished Maktub -- studio craft meeting live energy. The album demonstrated the band's range: delicate ballads alongside explosive funk workouts, intimate vocals alongside full-band improvisation.

(Source: The Stranger review; KEXP; Livewire Records catalog)

### KEXP Sessions

Maktub performed multiple **KEXP** sessions that captured the band's live energy in a controlled setting. KEXP (formerly KCMU) is Seattle's legendary independent radio station, and its in-studio performances are critical documents of the local music scene. The Maktub KEXP sessions are among the most vivid recordings of the band's improvisational approach.

(Source: KEXP session archives)

### Disbandment (2007)

Maktub disbanded in **2007** when Watts moved to **New York City** to pursue a solo comedy and music career. The move was pivotal: everything Watts had developed in Maktub -- the vocal power, the improvisational instinct, the genre-crossing -- transferred to the solo context documented at degree 17. The band was the incubator; the solo career was the deployment.

Goldman, Veley, and Martin continued to be active in Seattle's music scene after the disbandment. The band's legacy persists in the Capitol Hill community and in the influence they had on Seattle's live-music culture during the 1998-2007 era.

(Source: Seattle Weekly; The Stranger; AllMusic; various interviews)
