*Degree 152 retrospective complete. PROMETHEUS BROWN at the one hundred fifty-second degree -- FILIPINO-AMERICAN IDENTITY. George Quibuyen, also known as Geologic when he is one half of Blue Scholars, also known as Prometheus Brown when he speaks in his own solo voice, carries a specific history into the microphone: the Filipino-American history of Seattle, which is to say the history of cannery workers in Alaska, agricultural laborers in the Yakima Valley, families who came through military connections and the 1965 Immigration Act, the manong generation who built the community from nothing, the colonial legacy of the Philippines under American rule from 1898 to 1946, and the daily bilingual reality of Tagalog and English on Beacon Hill. His solo work narrows the lens from Blue Scholars' broad political hip-hop to the specific experience of being Filipino in Seattle: the balikbayan boxes, the long-distance family, the pull between two countries that are both home. Prometheus Brown extends the Carlos Bulosan tradition -- Filipino-American activist literature since 1946 -- into hip-hop. Art and activism are inseparable. The community organizing and the music are the same practice. The quartet closes: four degrees, four MCs, four dimensions of the Seattle hip-hop underground. J. Pinder opened the deep underground at 149. Mr. Hill confirmed the self-released tier at 150. Sol brought the new generation and conscious positivity at 151. Prometheus Brown closes with Filipino-American identity and the artist-activist model at 152. Meanwhile, the Rock Pigeon wing clap completes the Columbidae quartet with a percussive exclamation: wings slapped above the body, the loudest sound any Columbidae makes, breaking the E=3 baseline with E=4. Both Prometheus Brown and the Rock Pigeon are urban adapters who create something new from displacement. Both are final entries. Both involve the body in sound. The quartet ends with a clap.*
