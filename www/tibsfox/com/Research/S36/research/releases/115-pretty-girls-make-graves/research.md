## PRETTY GIRLS MAKE GRAVES -- POST-PUNK -- CAPITOL HILL

### The Band

Pretty Girls Make Graves was a Seattle post-punk band formed in **2001** on Capitol Hill and active until **2007**. The band's name comes from a chapter title in Jack Kerouac's *The Dharma Bums* (1958) -- "Pretty Girls Make Graves" -- which itself became the title of a Smiths song (1984, from *The Smiths*). The double literary reference signals the band's lineage: Beat generation restlessness filtered through British post-punk's emotional directness.

The core lineup:
- **Andrea Zollo** -- vocals. Zollo's voice was the band's most distinctive instrument: powerful, urgent, sometimes keening, sometimes commanding. Her vocal style drew on post-punk's tradition of strong female vocalists (Siouxsie Sioux, Poly Styrene, Ari Up) while maintaining a Pacific Northwest directness.
- **Nick Dewitt** -- guitar, keyboards. Dewitt's angular, interlocking guitar lines provided the post-punk architecture -- the rhythmic, cutting guitar work that defines the genre.
- **Jay Clark** -- drums. Clark's drumming was precise and driving -- the post-punk engine that kept the songs moving forward with urgency.
- **Derek Fudesco** -- bass. Fudesco's bass was melodic and propulsive, often carrying the harmonic weight of the songs while the guitars cut and slashed above. Fudesco had previously played in **The Murder City Devils**, another essential Seattle band, connecting Pretty Girls Make Graves to Seattle's broader punk and post-punk lineage.

The band was a product of Capitol Hill's early-2000s scene: Fudesco's connection to The Murder City Devils, the network of musicians playing in each other's bands, the proximity of venues and apartments, the Tuesday night shows that built audiences one show at a time. Pretty Girls Make Graves didn't arrive from outside -- they emerged from the neighborhood.

### The Kerouac Connection

The name "Pretty Girls Make Graves" carries weight beyond literary reference. In Kerouac's *The Dharma Bums*, the phrase captures a tension between desire and mortality, between beauty and its inevitable end. The Smiths' song of the same title (written by Morrissey and Marr) transforms this into post-punk melancholy -- the recognition that attraction is entangled with loss.

For the Seattle band, the name functions as aesthetic declaration: this is music aware of its own intensity and its own brevity. Pretty Girls Make Graves existed for six years, released three albums, and dissolved. The name was prophetic -- the band burned brightly and then stopped. The "graves" in the name are the bands that give everything and cannot sustain that intensity. Pretty Girls Make Graves gave everything.

(Sources: The Stranger; KEXP; Pitchfork; Matador Records; AllMusic)

## DISCOGRAPHY: The Three Albums

### *Good Health* (2002, Lookout! Records)

The debut album, released on **Lookout! Records** (the Berkeley label known for Green Day and Operation Ivy). *Good Health* established Pretty Girls Make Graves' approach: angular post-punk guitar work, driving rhythms, and Andrea Zollo's commanding vocals. The album was raw and urgent -- recorded quickly, with the energy of a band that had something to prove and nowhere to wait. Tracks like "If You Ever" and "Speakers Push the Air" demonstrated the band's ability to build songs from tension and release, with interlocking guitar and bass lines creating a propulsive architecture.

*Good Health* was recorded in the context of a national post-punk revival -- Interpol's *Turn on the Bright Lights*, Yeah Yeah Yeahs' *Fever to Tell*, The Rapture's *House of Jealous Lovers* -- but Pretty Girls Make Graves' Pacific Northwest intensity set them apart. Where New York post-punk revival bands drew on downtown cool, Pretty Girls Make Graves drew on Capitol Hill heat.

### *The New Romance* (2003, Matador Records)

The Matador debut and the band's artistic peak. *The New Romance* expanded the sonic palette while maintaining the post-punk urgency. The production was fuller, the arrangements more ambitious, and Zollo's vocals reached new levels of power and range. Tracks like "This Is Our Emergency" and "All Medicated Geniuses" became indie anthems of the mid-2000s. The album title itself was a statement: this is the new version of romance -- not tender, not sweet, but urgent, angular, and alive.

*The New Romance* placed Pretty Girls Make Graves alongside Matador's national roster. The album was critically acclaimed -- Pitchfork, NME, Spin, and The Stranger all recognized it as one of the strongest post-punk albums of 2003. The Matador connection gave the band national touring and distribution that Lookout! couldn't provide. Capitol Hill's post-punk scene reached its national audience through Matador.

### *Elan Vital* (2006, Matador Records)

The final album. The title -- French for "vital force" or "life energy" (a concept from philosopher Henri Bergson) -- suggested creative vitality, but the album also carried the weight of a band approaching its end. *Elan Vital* was the most expansive Pretty Girls Make Graves album: keyboards featured more prominently, the arrangements were more layered, and the tempo occasionally slowed to allow more atmospheric passages. The post-punk foundation remained, but the band was reaching beyond genre constraints.

*Elan Vital* was released in 2006; the band announced their dissolution in **2007**. The album stands as both culmination and farewell. The "vital force" of the title proved to be finite -- the band had given six years of high-intensity music and could not sustain further. In retrospect, three albums in six years, each one expanding the sonic range, was a perfect arc: debut urgency, peak achievement, expansive farewell.

(Sources: Matador Records; Pitchfork; AllMusic; The Stranger; KEXP)

## Acoustic Element 116: The Burning Elegance

The **burning elegance** is the one hundred sixteenth acoustic element in the Seattle 360 engine. It describes the quality produced when post-punk's angular, rhythmic architecture is performed with an intensity that consumes the performers -- the music is both elegant (structured, precise, architecturally sound) and burning (urgent, passionate, unsustainable).

### The Sound

The burning elegance manifests as:
- **Angular guitar architecture** -- interlocking guitar and bass lines that create rhythmic, geometric patterns. The guitars do not strum -- they cut, slice, and interlock. Each guitar part is precisely placed in the arrangement, creating a lattice of sound.
- **Vocal command** -- Andrea Zollo's voice as the element that transforms architecture into emotion. The guitars provide structure; the voice provides heat. The voice commands attention, demands response, and carries the songs' emotional weight.
- **Rhythmic urgency** -- the drums and bass create a sense of forward motion that is relentless but controlled. Not the motorik patience of Kinski (degree 110) -- this is urgency, the feeling that the song must be delivered NOW, that waiting is not an option.
- **Controlled combustion** -- the band burns, but the structure holds. The tension between emotional intensity and musical precision is the defining quality. Post-punk's gift: you can be passionate and precise simultaneously.

### Contrasts with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **The hypnotic engine** (degree 110, Kinski) -- patient accumulation. The burning elegance is immediate urgency. Kinski hypnotizes; Pretty Girls Make Graves commands.
- **The angular lattice** (degree 52, prior post-punk) -- if the earlier post-punk entry established the angular approach, the burning elegance adds the combustion dimension. Structure + fire.
- **The collapse frequency** (degree 109, No-Anchor) -- structural collapse. The burning elegance maintains structure under pressure. The structure holds; the performers burn. No-Anchor's music collapses; Pretty Girls Make Graves' music endures through intensity.
- **The punk circuit** (degree 108, Veronica Lipgloss) -- explosive and brief. The burning elegance is more architecturally complex. Punk explodes in all directions; post-punk channels energy through structure.

The 116th acoustic element. The engine adds the sound of elegant post-punk architecture burning with unsustainable intensity.
