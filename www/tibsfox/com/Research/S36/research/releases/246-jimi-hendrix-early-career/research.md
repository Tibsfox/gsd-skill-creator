## WHY TWO ENTRIES?

### The Case for Treating Early Career Hendrix as a Distinct Degree

The decision to give Jimi Hendrix's early career its own degree requires justification:

- **The formation and the genius are different things.** Degree 245 documents the fully-formed Jimi Hendrix Experience -- the artist who arrived in London in 1966 already knowing who he was musically. Degree 246 documents how he got there: the specific apprenticeships, the specific musical traditions absorbed, the specific physical skills developed over eight years of obscurity.
- **The Chitlin' Circuit is a subject in its own right.** The circuit was the infrastructure of Black American popular music in the mid-twentieth century -- a network of venues, promoters, transportation routes, and economic relationships that sustained Black musicians during segregation. Hendrix's years on the circuit are a window into this history, not merely a backstory for his later fame.
- **The pre-Experience recordings exist.** Curtis Knight and the Squires recordings, released and repackaged in various forms, document Hendrix's playing before London. These are authentic documents of his development, whatever their complicated commercial history.
- **E=7 vs. E=8 is meaningful.** The early career Hendrix is not at full power -- he is playing in support, constrained by the sideman role, still developing. E=7 captures this: the intensity is high, the potential is evident, but the full expression has not yet arrived.

## THE EARLY SEATTLE YEARS -- 1958-1961

### Learning by Ear in the Central District

The first years of Hendrix's musical life were entirely self-directed:

- **The first guitar (1958).** Around the time his mother Lucille died, Jimi acquired a ukulele and then a one-string acoustic guitar -- accounts vary. The important fact is not the specific instrument but the self-directed learning: no lessons, no formal instruction, no teacher. Learning by ear, by watching, by imitation.
- **The left-hand inversion.** Jimi Hendrix was left-handed. The instruments available to him were right-handed. He restrung them upside-down and played them with his left hand. This forced every chord shape to be rethought from first principles. The standard first-position chord shapes that right-handed guitarists learn first were not available to him. He built his chord vocabulary from scratch.
- **The Ave.** The commercial district of the U-District -- University Way NE -- was where young Seattleites found music. Record stores, jukebox culture, the radio. Hendrix absorbed the current of popular music: Little Richard's screaming gospel-rock, Chuck Berry's two-handed guitar lines, Muddy Waters' electric blues, B.B. King's control of the single note. He was a student of everything on the radio.
- **Early bands.** The Velvetones (1958-1959), then the Rocking Kings -- a series of teenage bands playing R&B covers at dances and small venues. These are not significant in themselves; they are the practice sessions, the repetitions that build physical facility.
- **The TV audition.** There are accounts of Jimi auditioning for a local television program -- not succeeding, but trying. The auditions and rejections of the early years are part of the formation: learning what doesn't work, developing resilience.

(Sources: First guitar 1958-1959; left-hand inversion; Velvetones; Rocking Kings; U-District record stores; jukebox culture; Little Richard; Chuck Berry; Muddy Waters; B.B. King)

## THE ARMY -- 1961-1962

### Fort Campbell and Billy Cox

In 1961, facing minor legal trouble in Seattle (two joyriding arrests), Jimi Hendrix enlisted in the US Army:

- **101st Airborne Division.** Hendrix was assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky -- one of the most demanding postings in the Army, known for its airborne and special forces units. He completed jump school and made at least one parachute jump.
- **Billy Cox.** At Fort Campbell, Hendrix met Billy Cox, a bass player from Pittsburgh. The two bonded over music and began playing together at the base. Cox would become Hendrix's most enduring musical partner -- the bassist for the Band of Gypsys in 1969-1970 and for Hendrix's final touring period in 1970. Their relationship survived the entire arc of Hendrix's career.
- **Discharge.** Hendrix received a general discharge in 1962 -- accounts suggest a letter from a fellow soldier about Hendrix's inappropriate behavior (some accounts suggest the letter described Hendrix falling asleep on duty or other minor infractions). The discharge was not honorable but was not dishonorable -- it was the Army's way of releasing a soldier who clearly had priorities elsewhere.
- **Nashville, post-Army.** After his discharge, Hendrix and Cox played in Nashville briefly. Nashville in 1962-1963 was a music city dominated by country, but it also had an active R&B scene, and Hendrix's time there represents his first post-Army professional musical activity.

(Sources: US Army 101st Airborne; Fort Campbell; jump school; Billy Cox; Army discharge 1962; Nashville 1962-1963; Cox-Hendrix relationship through Band of Gypsys)

## THE CHITLIN' CIRCUIT -- 1963-1966

### The Education

The Chitlin' Circuit was the name given (not always approvingly) to the network of venues that catered to Black audiences across the American South, border states, and Eastern cities during the era of segregation and into the post-Civil Rights period:

- **The circuit's infrastructure.** The Green Book (the travel guide for Black motorists navigating a segregated America) listed hotels, restaurants, and services that served Black travelers. The Chitlin' Circuit was the musical equivalent: a network of venues where Black musicians could work, where Black audiences could attend shows without the indignities of segregated or hostile mainstream venues. Jimi Hendrix navigated this circuit as a working musician from approximately 1963 to 1966.
- **Playing behind Little Richard (1964).** This is the most significant apprenticeship. Little Richard Penniman (born 1932 in Macon, Georgia) was one of the architects of rock and roll -- the screaming, flamboyant, gospel-derived performer who shaped Elvis Presley, the Beatles, James Brown, and virtually everyone who came after. His live show was an overwhelming experience: he commanded complete attention, demanded complete surrender from his audience, and had very specific expectations of his sidemen.
  - **The sideman's role.** Little Richard's sidemen were expected to be invisible -- to provide the musical foundation while Richard delivered the spectacle. Hendrix couldn't maintain this invisibility. He would improvise, add flourishes, play too loud, attract audience attention to himself. Richard reportedly fired and rehired him multiple times.
  - **What Hendrix learned from Little Richard.** The physical performance -- the showmanship, the audience command, the use of the body as theatrical instrument. Hendrix watched Richard obsessively and absorbed every technique of stagecraft. The specific tricks -- playing guitar behind the head, between the legs, with the teeth -- were not invented by Hendrix; they were learned from the Black R&B tradition and then pushed further than anyone had imagined.
- **The Isley Brothers (1964).** Ernie, Rudolph, and Ronald Isley had a string of hits including "Shout" (1959) and "Twist and Shout" (1962). Playing with the Isley Brothers exposed Hendrix to a professional major-label touring operation -- a step up in organizational sophistication from the local circuit bands. The Isley Brothers' guitar-forward sound also gave Hendrix more space than Little Richard's piano-centered arrangements.
- **The King Casuals.** With Billy Cox on bass, briefly in Nashville. The King Casuals were a working R&B band -- not significant historically but another node in Hendrix's expanding map of experience.
- **Curtis Knight and the Squires (1965-1966).** New York. Curtis Knight was a singer and bandleader who worked with various personnel in the mid-1960s. Hendrix played with Knight's Squires from approximately 1965 to 1966. This period is documented by recordings -- made with Knight's producer/manager Ed Chalpin under a contract that would later generate legal disputes after Hendrix became famous.
  - **The PPX contract.** In October 1965, Hendrix signed a contract with Ed Chalpin's PPX Enterprises, agreeing to make recordings for a minimal payment ($1 and a royalty). This contract became the basis for legal action after the Experience became successful. Reprise settled with PPX, resulting in the *Band of Gypsys* album (originally released as a settlement) being delivered to Capitol Records (Chalpin's US distributor). The contract is a reminder that the music industry's exploitation of young Black musicians was systematic and pervasive.
- **Harlem and the Village.** In New York, Hendrix was based in Harlem and began playing the clubs of Greenwich Village -- the folk and blues scene that would eventually lead to Chas Chandler's discovery at the Cafe Wha? The Village in 1965-1966 was a meeting point for folk musicians (Bob Dylan had departed for rock, but his influence lingered), blues revivalists, and the emerging psychedelic scene.

(Sources: Green Book; Chitlin' Circuit history; Little Richard biography; Isley Brothers; King Casuals; Curtis Knight; Ed Chalpin PPX contract; Harlem; Greenwich Village 1965-1966)

## THE MUSICAL DEVELOPMENT -- WHAT THE CIRCUIT BUILT

### The Specific Skills Acquired in Obscurity

The years on the circuit were not merely apprenticeship in the abstract -- they built specific capabilities:

- **Repertoire command.** By 1966, Hendrix knew the entire repertoire of Black American popular music from the late 1940s through the mid-1960s. He could play any R&B standard, any blues in any key, any rock and roll chart. This comprehensive command of the tradition was the foundation on which the Experience era innovations were built. Innovation requires deep knowledge of what is being innovated.
- **Stage physicality.** The showmanship traditions of the circuit -- the acrobatics, the audience manipulation, the physical theatrics -- were absorbed and internalized. When Hendrix played guitar with his teeth at the Monterey Pop Festival in 1967, burning his guitar at the set's end, he was drawing on techniques he had learned watching Black entertainers on the circuit.
- **Harmonic intuition.** Playing across a wide range of songs and styles, accompanying singers with different phrasing and different key preferences, Hendrix developed the harmonic instinct that allowed him to instantly find appropriate accompaniment for any melodic or rhythmic situation. This is not a skill that can be taught directly; it is built through accumulated experience.
- **Restraint and its limits.** The sideman role required restraint -- playing in support without overshadowing the primary artist. Hendrix could not fully maintain this restraint, and the tension between his instinct to express and the professional requirement to support is one of the defining dramas of the early career. He was fired (and rehired) repeatedly for being too expressive. The circuit taught him what expression was worth sacrificing for, and what was not worth containing.

(Sources: Black American popular music repertoire; circuit showmanship traditions; guitar with teeth technique; guitar burning as performance art; harmonic intuition; sideman role tension)

## THE TRANSITION -- NEW YORK TO LONDON

### The Threshold Moment

By mid-1966, Hendrix was playing his own set as "Jimmy James and the Blue Flames" at the Cafe Wha? in Greenwich Village:

- **The Cafe Wha?** The Cafe Wha? on MacDougal Street in Greenwich Village was one of the central venues of the New York folk and rock scene. Bob Dylan had played there. The Mamas & the Papas had played there. In 1966, Jimi Hendrix was playing there with a backing band, playing his own music for the first time as a headliner rather than a sideman.
- **The performance Chas Chandler saw.** Chandler saw Hendrix at the Cafe Wha? in late June or early July 1966. What he saw -- according to Chandler's own accounts -- was something so far beyond anything he had expected that he immediately began planning to bring Hendrix to London. Chandler was himself a professional musician (Animals bassist), not an unsophisticated observer. His recognition of Hendrix was an expert witness's verdict.
- **The E=7 to E=8 threshold.** The early career Hendrix is E=7 -- intense, fully developed, the complete musician in embryo. The London arrival produces E=8. Something changed in the move to London -- not the technique, which was already formed, but the context: the audience, the band, the recording resources, the cultural permission to push further than the American market had allowed. London provided the mirror in which Hendrix could fully see and fully express what he had become.

(Sources: Jimmy James and the Blue Flames; Cafe Wha?; Chas Chandler discovery; Animals; London 1966; E=7 to E=8 threshold analysis)
