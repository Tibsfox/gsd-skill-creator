The Formation -- Before London Knew His Name

From approximately 1958 to 1966, Jimi Hendrix played as a sideman, a backing musician, a supporting act. He played behind Little Richard, the Isley Brothers, Curtis Knight, and dozens of smaller touring acts. He traveled the Chitlin’ Circuit — the network of Black-owned and Black-patronized venues that sustained African American music during and after segregation. He was fired, rehired, and fired again. He signed a bad contract for $1 and a royalty. He was largely invisible to the mainstream music world.

These years built everything. The repertoire command, the stage presence, the physical technique, the harmonic instinct — all of it was constructed in obscurity, through repetition, through watching the masters and absorbing what they had to teach.

"Little Richard reportedly fired Hendrix multiple times for upstaging him. Hendrix couldn’t help it. The capacity for self-expression that would define his career was impossible to contain, even when containment was the professional requirement."

The Chitlin’ Circuit Apprenticeships

Little Richard

1964

The architect of rock and roll spectacle. Hendrix learned stagecraft — audience command, physical theatrics, the body as instrument. Fired multiple times for upstaging. The lesson: what expression is worth containing.

The Isley Brothers

1964

"Shout," "Twist and Shout." A professional major-label touring operation. Exposed Hendrix to a higher tier of organizational sophistication. Guitar-forward arrangements gave him more space than Little Richard's piano-centered sound.

Curtis Knight & the Squires

1965–1966

New York. The PPX contract: Ed Chalpin, $1 and a royalty. These recordings document pre-fame Hendrix. The contract later generated legal disputes that shaped the Band of Gypsys album release. Industry exploitation documented.

Jimmy James & the Blue Flames

1966

Cafe Wha?, Greenwich Village. Hendrix’s own band, his own music, his own name at last. The performance Chas Chandler saw. The last stop before London. E=7 becoming E=8.

The Army — Billy Cox and Fort Campbell

In 1961, Hendrix enlisted in the US Army and was assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky. He completed jump school. At Fort Campbell, he met Billy Cox — a bass player from Pittsburgh who would become the most enduring musical partner of Hendrix's career. Cox played in the Band of Gypsys in 1969-1970 and on the final touring period before Hendrix's death in 1970. Their partnership, forged in the Army, lasted the arc of Hendrix's life.

The Chitlin’ Circuit as History

The Chitlin’ Circuit was the infrastructure of Black American popular music during segregation — the Green Book of the music world, a network of venues where Black musicians could work and Black audiences could attend shows without the indignities of segregated mainstream venues. Hendrix's years on the circuit are a window into this history. The showmanship traditions he absorbed — guitar behind the head, between the legs, with the teeth — were Black entertainment traditions he refined to an extreme that no one had imagined.

The PPX Contract. In October 1965, Ed Chalpin's PPX Enterprises signed Hendrix to a contract for $1 and a small royalty. This contract, standard in its exploitative terms for young Black musicians of the era, generated legal disputes after the Experience became successful. The settlement required delivering an album to Capitol Records — which became the Band of Gypsys live album. The contract is documented here as part of the music industry's systematic history.
