## JOHN RICHARDS -- THE MORNING SHOW (2001-PRESENT)

### Building the Show

John Richards joined KEXP (then still transitioning from KCMU) in **1997** as a DJ, and took over **The Morning Show** in **2001**. The show became his signature -- and over two decades, it became one of the most recognized radio programs in the Pacific Northwest:

- **Time slot:** Weekday mornings, 6-10 AM. The morning drive slot is the highest-profile position at any radio station -- the largest audience, the most consistent listenership, the time when the host's personality becomes inseparable from the station's identity.
- **Format:** Music-forward with personal interjections. Richards plays a wide range of music -- indie, electronic, hip-hop, international, classic -- framed by conversational commentary. The format is not talk radio; it is music radio with a deeply personal host presence.
- **Curatorial philosophy:** Richards describes his approach as playing "what feels right" -- an intuitive, mood-driven selection process. He reads the emotional temperature of the morning (weather, news cycle, cultural moment) and curates music to match. This is the same curatorial instinct that Pavitt brought to label A&R, applied in real time to broadcasting.
- **Evolution:** The show has evolved from a standard morning music program to a personal expression of Richards' worldview. By the 2010s, The Morning Show was as much about Richards' on-air persona -- his enthusiasm, his vulnerability, his community building -- as it was about the music.

### The KEXP Morning Show as Cultural Infrastructure

The Morning Show occupies a specific role in Seattle's cultural ecosystem:

- **Discovery engine:** For many Seattle listeners, The Morning Show is their primary music discovery tool. Richards introduces new artists, plays deep cuts, and connects emerging music to established artists. The show functions as a curated Spotify playlist made by a human with decades of contextual knowledge.
- **Community ritual:** The Morning Show is a daily ritual for tens of thousands of Seattle-area listeners. The consistency of the time slot, the familiarity of Richards' voice, and the predictability of the format (music + personal commentary) create a community of shared experience. Listeners feel they know Richards personally, even though the relationship is one-directional.
- **The "KEXP effect":** Artists featured on The Morning Show often see measurable spikes in streaming numbers, ticket sales, and social media following. The show's audience is highly engaged and responsive. This is the modern version of the Sub Pop effect -- a trusted curator directing attention to music that matters.

## MUSIC HEALS -- MENTAL HEALTH AS BROADCAST MISSION

### The Campaign

**Music Heals** is John Richards' signature initiative and perhaps KEXP's most emotionally resonant program:

- **Origin:** Richards created Music Heals as a response to personal experience with grief and mental health challenges. The campaign explicitly connects music listening with mental health and emotional well-being.
- **Format:** Annual on-air event (typically in October, coinciding with Mental Health Awareness Month) featuring dedications, listener stories, curated playlists focused on emotional resonance, and partnerships with mental health organizations. The event often extends across multiple days.
- **Listener engagement:** Music Heals invites listeners to share their stories of how music has helped them through difficult periods -- grief, depression, anxiety, addiction, loss. The stories are read on air (with permission), creating a public space for vulnerability. The radio becomes a support group.
- **Cultural significance:** Music Heals was ahead of its time in normalizing mental health conversation in public media. Before mental health awareness became a mainstream media priority, Richards was using his morning show to create space for these conversations. The campaign has grown from a single-day event to a year-round ethos that permeates the show.
- **Institutional impact:** Music Heals has become one of KEXP's defining programs, used in fundraising, outreach, and brand identity. It demonstrates that a music radio station can serve a community function beyond entertainment -- that curation can be therapeutic.

### Music Heals as E=3 in Action

The Music Heals campaign embodies E=3 energy perfectly:

- **Vulnerability requires intimacy.** Sharing mental health struggles on air is not a high-energy act -- it is a quiet, brave, personal act. The energy is focused inward and shared carefully.
- **Listening as active engagement.** Music Heals reframes the act of listening to music as a deliberate health practice. Passive background music becomes active therapeutic listening. This transformation requires attention and intention -- E=3 qualities.
- **Community through shared vulnerability.** The campaign builds community not through spectacle but through shared openness. Listeners connect because they recognize their own struggles in others' stories. This is intimate connection at scale -- E=3 broadcast to thousands.

## SOCIAL MEDIA AND THE PUBLIC FACE OF KEXP

### Beyond the Airwaves

John Richards' social media presence has made him KEXP's most visible public figure:

- **Twitter/X:** Richards has been one of the most active KEXP personalities on social media, sharing music recommendations, personal reflections, and community commentary. His social media voice matches his on-air voice: personal, enthusiastic, occasionally vulnerable.
- **Instagram:** Behind-the-scenes studio photos, concert recommendations, Seattle life documentation. The visual social media extends the Morning Show's personal tone into image.
- **KEXP brand ambassador:** At festivals, concerts, and public events, Richards is the person most likely to be recognized as "the KEXP guy." His personal brand and the station's brand have become intertwined to a degree that no other KEXP personality has achieved.
- **The double-edged sword of personality-driven broadcasting:** Richards' high visibility benefits KEXP by putting a human face on the institution. The risk is that the institution becomes identified with a single person. KEXP has managed this tension by developing multiple strong on-air personalities (Waters at 130, Mazzone at 132, others), but Richards remains the most publicly identified face.

### The Morning Show Personality in the Streaming Era

Richards and The Morning Show represent a specific argument about radio's role in the streaming era:

- **The human curator vs. the algorithm:** In a world where Spotify and Apple Music serve algorithmically generated playlists, Richards represents the **human alternative** -- a curator with taste, context, emotional intelligence, and the ability to read a room (or a city's mood). The Morning Show is what an algorithm cannot be: personal, vulnerable, contextual, and surprising.
- **The parasocial relationship as value proposition:** Listeners choose The Morning Show over a playlist because they want to hear from Richards. The parasocial relationship -- the feeling of knowing the host -- is the show's core value proposition. Algorithms don't have bad days, share personal stories, or dedicate songs to grieving listeners. Richards does.
- **Relevance:** In 2024, The Morning Show's listenership remains strong. KEXP's streaming listenership (via the KEXP app and web player) extends the show's reach globally. The Morning Show is heard not just in Seattle but worldwide -- a local voice with global distribution.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of The Morning Show

Richards' acoustic contribution is atmospheric: the **soundscape of a KEXP morning**:

- **Voice character:** Warm, slightly gravelly, conversational. Not a "radio voice" in the traditional deep-toned sense. Richards sounds like a person talking to a friend, not a broadcaster performing for an audience. The voice matches the E=3 energy: intimate, direct, personal.
- **Pacing:** Unhurried. Richards does not rush between songs. The transitions include pauses, personal comments, contextual information about the artist or song. The pacing creates space for the listener to absorb.
- **Music selection rhythm:** The Morning Show has a characteristic arc: quieter, more reflective music in the early morning (6-7 AM), building in energy through the morning drive (7-9 AM), and arriving at more adventurous selections by 9-10 AM. This arc mirrors the listener's waking cycle.
- **The KEXP sound:** Richards' show contributes to the broader "KEXP sound" -- a sonic identity that prioritizes texture, dynamics, and emotional resonance over genre boundaries. A typical Morning Show hour might include indie rock, electronic, hip-hop, folk, and international music. The coherence comes not from genre but from mood and quality.

### The Radio DJ as Sound Curator

Richards' role is analogous to a concert curator or playlist architect:

- **Sequencing:** The order of songs matters. Richards sequences music to create emotional arcs within an hour. A melancholy song followed by an uplifting song followed by an energetic song creates a narrative. This sequencing is an acoustic act -- choosing not just what sounds to present but in what order.
- **Framing:** Richards' spoken introductions and comments frame the music. "This is a band from Portland that..." or "I've been thinking about this song all week..." -- these frames change how the listener hears the music. The DJ's voice becomes part of the acoustic experience.
- **Discovery:** When Richards plays an unknown artist, the listener's acoustic experience of that music is shaped by the trust relationship with the host. The listener gives the unknown song attention because Richards selected it. Trust is an acoustic filter.
