## SOLGER -- THE CHRONOLOGICAL CONTEXT

### 1980. What Existed. What Did Not Yet Exist.

To understand what Solger's E=10 status means, understand what 1980 was:

- **Black Flag's *Damaged*** was released in December 1981. Solger pre-dates it by over a year at peak.
- **The Germs' *GI*** (1979, produced by Joan Jett) was the closest template. Dead Kennedys' *Fresh Fruit for Rotting Vegetables* had just been released (September 1980, same year as Solger's peak).
- **The Misfits** were in the middle of their first period (1977-1983), releasing singles and EPs but no full album until 1982.
- **Seattle** had no documented hardcore scene. There was punk activity -- influenced by the Ramones, the Sex Pistols, the British invasion -- but the specific American hardcore mode (faster than punk, more aggressive, politically direct, DIY to the structural extreme) had not yet been defined by a Seattle band.

Solger was that first definition. They were not imitating a scene that existed -- they were generating one from the cultural materials available: the British punk records, the early American hardcore experiments, and the specific energy of Seattle's small, isolated punk community in 1979-1980.

The energy score of E=10 does not derive from technical sophistication or production quality. It derives from the pure fact of the origination: the band that is at degree 355, placed immediately before Bikini Kill and Sleater-Kinney and Unwound, is the band that was first. The first to play the music that all subsequent bands in the Seattle hardcore tradition followed. The floor.

## THE BAND -- THE CORE

### Seattle, 1979. The Formation.

Solger formed in Seattle around 1979, emerging from the city's small punk community. The membership included Ron Reyes in some accounts' crossing of influences -- but more accurately, Solger's specific lineup is documented primarily through their recorded output and the testimony of their contemporaries. The band played fast, aggressive hardcore with the specific directness of the form at its earliest: short songs, minimal production values, maximum velocity.

What is documented:
- **Formation geography:** Seattle, Washington. Not a suburban origin; the city itself.

The limited distribution was not a failure -- it was the economic reality of hardcore in 1980, where the independent infrastructure (SST Records, Dischord Records) was either just forming or not yet formed.

The origination energy. The founding of the underground.

## HARDCORE PUNK IN 1980 -- THE CONTEXT OF ORIGINATION

### Before There Was a Genre Name.

American hardcore punk in 1980 was not yet a named category. The music existed -- Black Flag, the Germs, Fear, the Circle Jerks, Minor Threat (forming 1980) were all playing versions of what would be called hardcore -- but the genre's critical and subcultural infrastructure had not coalesced. There were no genre charts. There were no nationwide touring circuits built around the form. There was no national independent label dedicated to the music (SST began in 1978 with Black Flag but had not yet built its defining catalog; Dischord was founded in 1980 by Ian MacKaye and Jeff Nelson of Minor Threat).

In this context, a Seattle band playing hardcore in 1980 was doing something with almost no regional model. The Pacific Northwest punk scene was real but not large: mostly influenced by British punk (the Clash, the Damned, Wire) and early American punk (Television, Ramones, Patti Smith). The specific American hardcore mode -- shorter songs, faster tempos, more aggressive attack, explicitly political or confrontational lyrics, DIY as institutional principle -- was coming from Los Angeles (Black Flag, Germs) and Washington D.C. (Minor Threat, Bad Brains arriving from hardcore fusion) and New York (the Misfits, the Stimulators).

Seattle was on the periphery of all of this. No train line ran directly from Los Angeles to Seattle carrying the hardcore aesthetic. What Solger built was built from pieces: British punk records brought north, early American hardcore sounds filtering through whatever distribution existed, and the specific energy of people deciding that what they heard was something they could and should do themselves.

The DIY principle of hardcore -- not "do it yourself" as a lifestyle choice but "do it yourself because there is no other option" -- was most purely realized in a band like Solger, operating in a city with no scene infrastructure, no national distribution, no guarantee of any audience. This is the origination energy. E=10.
