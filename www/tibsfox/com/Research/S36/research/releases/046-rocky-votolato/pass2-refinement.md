Gemini 9A launched June 3, 1966 — Tom Stafford and Gene Cernan, the mission that went wrong at every turn. The original Gemini 9 had been scrubbed when its Agena target vehicle failed during launch (the second Agena loss after Gemini 6's). NASA improvised an Augmented Target Docking Adapter (ATDA) as a replacement. It reached orbit, but the launch shroud failed to separate. When Stafford and Cernan approached for docking, they found the ATDA with its shroud half-open, jaws gaping — Stafford radioed: "It looks like an angry alligator." Docking was impossible. Cernan's EVA was equally punishing: his visor fogged from exertion, his suit ballooned from body heat, and he could barely see or move. He spent two hours and ten minutes outside the spacecraft in a state of near-exhaustion. Every plan failed. Every improvisation cost more than expected. The crew returned having achieved rendezvous but not docking, having attempted EVA but not mastered it. The mission that taught NASA what failure looks like when you are too close to quit.

Rocky Votolato — born in Dallas, raised on a 50-acre horse farm in Frost, Texas (population 647), father in the Scorpions motorcycle gang, parents divorced, relocated to Kenmore, Washington at fourteen. He formed the post-hardcore band Waxwing, played loud rooms, then stripped everything to acoustic guitar and voice. The folk career that followed was defined by the same rhythm as Gemini 9A: plans that don't survive contact with reality. Barsuk Records released his commercial peak (*Makers*, *The Balling of Dirk Bogarde/Suicide Medicine*), but commercial breakthrough never came. A song on The OC. An NPR Song of the Day. Two hundred and fifty shows a year — the touring workhorse driving from club to club across America, arriving at each venue to find the shroud half-open, the docking impossible. The audience was there but never large enough. The reviews were strong but never loud enough. E=3: visible-but-not-mainstream. The angry alligator at every door.

Cernan's EVA on Gemini 9A was the hardest physical labor any astronaut had performed in space to that date — fogged visor, ballooned suit, near-total exhaustion. Votolato's 250-shows-per-year pace was the hardest physical labor in the engine's folk register — West Seattle to the road and back, over and over, the confessional voice deployed in every small room that would have him. Gemini 9A did not fail. It proved that some missions succeed by showing what does not work yet — the docking adapter design was wrong, the EVA suit was inadequate, the thermal management was insufficient. All of that data went into Gemini 10, 11, and 12. Votolato did not fail. His catalog — *Makers*, *True Devotion*, *Hospital Handshakes*, *Wild Roots* — is the data from a career that showed what the independent folk circuit costs a body and a family. The angry alligator is the music industry's shroud: it looks like it should open, but it does not, and you fly around it and come home anyway.
