## COMMUNITY-ORIENTED HIP-HOP

### The Grassroots Model

Draze represents a particular model of hip-hop practice:

- **Community first:** The music is created for and within a community -- the Central District, Seattle's Black community, the local hip-hop scene. The audience is local before it is national. The concerns are neighborhood concerns: gentrification, displacement, community solidarity, cultural preservation.
- **Self-released:** No label. No distribution deal. Music released directly to fans through digital platforms, physical sales at shows, and community networks. This is the purest form of DIY hip-hop: the artist controls every aspect of creation, production, distribution, and marketing.
- **Local venues:** Performance in community spaces, local clubs, neighborhood events, and grassroots gatherings. The live circuit is local. The energy comes from proximity -- artist and audience in the same community, sharing the same experiences.
- **Gentrification context:** The Central District has undergone dramatic demographic change. Historically Seattle's Black neighborhood, the CD has experienced rapid gentrification that has displaced much of the original community. Draze's music engages directly with this displacement: lyrics about neighborhood change, community loss, cultural erasure, and the fight to maintain presence.

### Musical Character

Draze's musical approach is characterized by:

- **Direct lyrics:** Clear, accessible, and message-driven. The lyrics prioritize communication over abstraction. Community issues are addressed directly, not through metaphor or conceptual framing.
- **Boom-bap foundations:** The production draws on classic boom-bap hip-hop foundations -- strong drum patterns, sample-based beats, emphasis on rhythm and flow. The sound is rooted in hip-hop tradition rather than experimental innovation.
- **Vocal presence:** Strong, confident vocal delivery that commands attention. The voice carries the authority of someone speaking from within the community, about the community, to the community.
- **Local references:** Music dense with Seattle and Central District references -- street names, neighborhoods, landmarks, local figures, community events. The specificity grounds the music in place.

The Central District has been a recurring geographic anchor in the hip-hop era:

- **Degree 103/157: Shabazz Palaces.** Ishmael Butler and Tendai Maraire. The CD as site of Afrofuturist experimentation.

Both are valid. Both are necessary. The engine documents both: the CD as site of imaginative transcendence and the CD as site of community presence.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Draze

Draze's acoustic profile reflects the community-oriented, boom-bap-rooted approach:

- **Vocal delivery:** Strong, direct, authoritative. Clear diction prioritizes message delivery. The flow is rhythmically solid without being flashy -- function over form, communication over virtuosity.
- **Production:** Boom-bap foundations. Drum-heavy beats with strong kick-snare patterns. Sample-based production when used. The sound is deliberately rooted in hip-hop tradition -- not experimental, not pop-crossover, but solidly within the genre's core aesthetic.
- **BPM range:** 85-100 BPM. Classic hip-hop tempo range. Steady, head-nodding.
- **Lyrical density:** High. The lyrics carry significant content -- community issues, personal narrative, political commentary. Every bar has something to say.
- **Mix approach:** Voice-forward. The vocal sits on top of the mix, clearly audible and intelligible. The production supports the voice rather than competing with it.
- **Live character:** The live performance is energetic, call-and-response oriented, and community-engaging. Audience participation is central to the live experience.
