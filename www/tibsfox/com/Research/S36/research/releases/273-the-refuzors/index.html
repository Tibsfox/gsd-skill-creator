Seattle’s Longest-Running Hardcore Band

The Refuzors formed in Seattle in 1980 — the precise moment when hardcore punk was crystallizing nationally. Black Flag in Los Angeles, Minor Threat in Washington DC, Dead Kennedys in San Francisco, The Refuzors in Seattle. The hardcore movement was simultaneously everywhere and specifically local. The Refuzors have never stopped.

The name signals the identity: Refuzors, with a Z, is a punk gesture against correctness. To refuse, with attitude, with commitment, for forty-plus years — this is the band’s entire program. Most bands break up. Most scenes dissipate. The Refuzors are still there.

"The Refuzors do not play reunion shows — they have never stopped. This removes them from the nostalgia economy entirely."

The Hardcore Moment: 1980

The transition from punk (1976-1978) to hardcore (1979-1982) was a national movement with regional expressions. Songs got shorter, tempos accelerated to 200-280 BPM, politics became more explicit, production rougher. Seattle had its own version: The Fartz (degree 270), The U-Men (degree 272), and The Refuzors form the city’s founding hardcore generation. The Refuzors entered at the precise transition moment and stayed.

1980 was a Reagan year. Hardcore punk responded to Reagan-era conservatism, nuclear anxiety, economic inequality, and the sense that the American promise was a fraud. The Refuzors emerged in this political moment. Forty years later, the politics are still intact.

The Seattle Hardcore Ecosystem

The Refuzors exist in a specific ecosystem: The Fartz (engine 270), The U-Men (engine 272), and The Refuzors form the city’s founding hardcore generation. The Fartz documented the first wave; the U-Men explored the art-punk branch; The Refuzors sustained the hardcore branch across every decade.
