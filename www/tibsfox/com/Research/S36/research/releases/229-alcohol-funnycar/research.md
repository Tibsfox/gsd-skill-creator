## EMPTY RECORDS -- A NEW LABEL ENTERS

### The Label That Stayed Underground

Empty Records is a Seattle independent label that enters the engine for the first time at degree 229 via Alcohol Funnycar:

- **Empty Records founded.** Empty Records was founded in Seattle in the late 1980s by Mark Arm (of Mudhoney fame) and others closely affiliated with the Seattle underground. The label was a deliberately anti-commercial operation -- a home for records that Sub Pop, C/Z, or Amphetamine Reptile might not have taken on, or that bands preferred to release without the commercial apparatus even those labels carried.
- **The underground ecology.** By 1990, the Seattle scene had a layered label ecosystem: Sub Pop was the most visible (Mudhoney, Nirvana), C/Z was slightly harder (Skin Yard, Coffin Break), Amphetamine Reptile was noise-forward (Gas Huffer, Helmet). Empty sat further underground still -- less interested in distribution scale than in documenting what the scene was actually producing at its most chaotic margins.
- **What Empty sounded like.** The Empty Records catalog skews abrasive, fast, and sloppy in the best sense -- recordings that prioritize urgency and rawness over production polish. Alcohol Funnycar fits this profile completely: a band that valued speed and noise over studio finesse.
- **Label significance in the engine.** The engine has now documented Sub Pop (Love Battery, 221), Frontier (Flop, 231), Roadrunner (Gruntruck, 232), and Amphetamine Reptile (Gas Huffer, 228). Empty Records at 229 completes the picture of Seattle's early-1990s underground label geography.

## ALCOHOL FUNNYCAR -- THE BAND

### Formation and Personnel

Alcohol Funnycar formed in Seattle around 1990 and operated within the underground grunge-punk circuit:

- **The band as collective.** Alcohol Funnycar operated in a loose, rotating-member fashion typical of Seattle underground bands of the era. The lineup was not fixed in the way that commercial bands managed personnel -- members moved in and out based on availability, alignment, and the chaotic social physics of the underground scene.
- **The name.** "Alcohol Funnycar" is a direct reference to the alcohol-fueled funny car drag racing category -- machines that burn methanol or ethanol at enormous power levels, are structurally minimal, and run at the edge of controllable. The name is programmatic: this band burns hot, runs fast, and operates close to structural failure. The drag racing metaphor aligns with the music's urgency and noise.
- **Grunge-punk positioning.** The band occupied the intersection of grunge (the heavy, distorted guitar sound, the Pacific Northwest scene context) and punk (the speed, the anti-commercial stance, the abrasive production). This was not grunge as Sub Pop sold it to the world but grunge as a local underground tradition with deep punk roots.
- **The underground circuit.** Alcohol Funnycar played the same circuit as Gas Huffer (228), Mudhoney, and the other bands that defined Seattle's noise-punk underbelly. Their audience was the audience that came to Satyricon in Portland and the Vogue in Seattle -- people who valued rawness over polish.

(Sources: Alcohol Funnycar biography; drag racing funny car etymology; Seattle underground circuit; grunge-punk intersection; Empty Records context)

## THE GRUNGE-PUNK SOUND -- E=7

### Speed, Noise, and Structural Urgency

Alcohol Funnycar's music is calibrated at E=7 -- two full notches above Love Battery's psychedelic haze (E=5) and one above the Hammerbox/Gas Huffer energy level (E=6-7):

- **E=7 defined at degree 229.** E=7 is the engine's high-energy zone without reaching the absolute wall-of-sound ceiling (E=8-9). At E=7, the music is fast, loud, distorted, and structurally minimal -- songs built for impact rather than development. Alcohol Funnycar operates fully in this zone.
- **The grunge-punk overlap.** Where straight grunge (as Sub Pop marketed it) emphasized heavy riffs and emotional rawness at mid-tempo, grunge-punk bands like Alcohol Funnycar pushed the tempo up into punk territory while maintaining the guitar distortion and noise that characterized the Seattle sound. The result is abrasive and urgent -- songs that function like short detonations rather than extended meditations.
- **Production aesthetic.** Empty Records releases, by design, did not prioritize studio polish. Alcohol Funnycar recordings have a rawness that is aesthetic choice as much as budget constraint -- the noise and distortion are meant to be present in the recording, not cleaned up in the mix. This is the lo-fi tradition that runs from the Velvet Underground through Hüsker Dü through the entire Sub Pop aesthetic.
- **Lyrical territory.** Grunge-punk lyrics at E=7 tend toward sardonic humor, self-deprecating nihilism, and the kind of working-class frustration that defined Seattle's pre-tech scene. Alcohol Funnycar's name alone signals the register: not earnest emotional declaration but dark comedy at speed.
- **Short song structures.** Following the punk tradition, songs tend toward compact structures -- verse, chorus, minimal bridge, out. The emphasis is on momentum and impact per minute, not on the extended instrumental passages of psychedelic rock or the lengthy dynamics of art rock.

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Underground Catalog

- ***In Search of the Funny* (1990, Empty Records).** Early release. Establishes the template: fast, raw, distorted. The production is intentionally rough. The songs are short and hit hard.
- ***Baby Fat* (1993, Empty Records).** Development record. More varied than the debut -- some songs expand beyond the core grunge-punk formula, but the energy level stays high. The Empty Records aesthetic remains.
- ***Splatterhead* (1995, Empty Records).** Later-period record. By 1995, grunge's commercial moment had passed and the underground was reclaiming territory. Splatterhead sounds like a band free from commercial pressure -- focused on making the loudest, most abrasive record possible without worrying about chart placement or radio rotation.
- **Singles and compilation appearances.** Alcohol Funnycar appeared on Seattle underground compilations of the early 1990s -- the kind of sampler records that C/Z, Empty, and other independent labels used to document the scene's breadth. These compilations are often the most direct evidence of where bands stood in the scene's internal geography.

## ALCOHOL FUNNYCAR IN CONTEXT -- PUNK ROOTS OF GRUNGE

### The Structural Base of a Movement

Alcohol Funnycar represents the punk substrate from which grunge grew:

- **Hüsker Dü and the Replacements.** Both key influences on the Seattle underground. Hüsker Dü's combination of hardcore punk speed with melodic sensibility (and double-LP ambition on *Zen Arcade*) demonstrated that punk could be complex. The Replacements' controlled sloppiness showed that chaos could be art. Alcohol Funnycar inherits both traditions.
- **The Wipers.** Portland's Wipers -- Greg Sage's band -- are the most direct Pacific Northwest ancestor of the grunge-punk form. The Wipers' combination of fast tempos, melodic guitar lines over distorted rhythm, and DIY production ethic is the template that bands like Alcohol Funnycar used. The Pacific Northwest underground punk tradition runs through Wipers in a direct line.
- **Mudhoney's shadow.** It is difficult to discuss any Seattle grunge-punk band without placing them in relation to Mudhoney -- the band that most successfully occupied the grunge-punk intersection before Sub Pop packaged it for export. Alcohol Funnycar plays in the same zone as early Mudhoney but stays further from commercial exposure.
- **The funny car metaphor as scene statement.** The name "Alcohol Funnycar" is also a statement about the Seattle underground's relationship to the rock music industry. A funny car is a vehicle built for maximum performance over very short distances -- not efficiency, not durability, not commercial viability. Maximum energy for minimum duration. This is the correct description of underground punk in a scene that was simultaneously producing commercially viable grunge for major labels.

(Sources: Hüsker Dü influence; Replacements influence; The Wipers Portland punk legacy; Mudhoney comparison; funny car drag racing metaphor as music statement)
