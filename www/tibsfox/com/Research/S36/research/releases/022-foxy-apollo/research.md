## Biography & The Young Funk Collective

Foxy Apollo is a jazz/funk collective founded in **2016** by guitarist and vocalist **Sam Ashkenazy** while still in high school in the Seattle area. The project began when Ashkenazy and drummer **Satchel Schwartz** reconnected through their school's jazz band and started making music together, quickly recruiting bassist **Jack Christian**. What started as a teenage jazz-funk trio has grown into a **six-piece ensemble** incorporating keyboards, trumpet, and saxophone -- a trajectory that mirrors the engine's recent organizational escalation from solo to collective to ensemble.

The current lineup includes **Sam Ashkenazy** (guitar, lead vocals), **Zachary Schmidt** (bass), **Dallas Quick** (drums; also of INVICTVS), **Shaun Quick** (guitar; Dallas's brother), **Jackson Cruz** (keyboards, trumpet), and **Andrew Hewitt** (saxophone). Earlier lineups included original drummer Satchel Schwartz, original bassist Jack Christian, vocalist **TyeLeah Nanto** (credited on *Sweet Talk*), saxophonist **Chet Przybysz** (featured on "Someday"), and producer/mixer **Alex Dillard** (who also contributed to early recordings). The band has been described as "sometimes a 4-piece, sometimes a 6-piece musical powerhouse."

The formation story is structurally significant: **Foxy Apollo was born from a high school jazz band.** This is the youngest formation origin in the engine. Every prior artist entered the narrative as a professional, a conservatory product, or an established scene participant. Ashkenazy was a teenager when he founded the project. The implication is generational: Foxy Apollo represents the generation that inherited the Seattle funk/jazz ecosystem built by the artists in degrees 0-21. They did not create the scene, did not crystallize it (that was Polyrhythmics at degree 21) -- they grew up inside it.

The band's geographic identity is distributed across the **Pacific Northwest I-5 corridor**: members hail from Seattle, Bellingham, and Anacortes. The Anacortes Music Project has featured the band, connecting them to that small town's outsized DIY music reputation (Anacortes has been called "a strangely recognizable name in the indie music world" with "a DIY in nature, personal, powerful and creative" scene). Ashkenazy relocated from Seattle to Bellingham, where the band recorded *Saturated, Faded* at Champion Street Studios. This geographic dispersal -- members in multiple PNW cities connected by the I-5 corridor -- is a new pattern in the engine, where every prior artist was rooted in a single Seattle neighborhood.

(Source: Anacortes Music Project Facebook; Cascadia Daily News 2023; Daily UW 2019; Bandcamp; Ratings Game Music 2021; do206.com)

### The Sound: Funk as First Language

Foxy Apollo's sound is a compound that places **funk as the foundation** with jazz, psychedelic rock, R&B, and pop layered on top. The Cascadia Daily News captured it precisely: "There's something in their music for just about everybody, whether you're looking for pop melodies, jazz textures, R&B grooves or psychedelic rock twists." Ashkenazy himself describes the music as embracing multiple genres without hierarchy -- the funk groove is the platform, and everything else is what happens on top of it.

The distinction from Polyrhythmics (degree 21) is critical. Polyrhythmics' Jazz/Funk places funk as **rhythm-sovereign** -- instrumental, no vocals, the groove IS the composition. Foxy Apollo's Jazz/Funk places funk as **platform** -- the groove carries vocals, pop melodies, jazz textures, and psychedelic exploration. Polyrhythmics strips everything down to the groove. Foxy Apollo builds everything up from the groove. Two consecutive Jazz/Funk degrees, two fundamentally different relationships between funk and everything else.

This is the engine's first **consecutive same-genre pair**. In twenty-two prior degrees, no two adjacent entries shared the exact same genre compound. Degrees 21-22 are both Jazz/Funk. The repetition reveals the genre compound's range: Jazz/Funk can be instrumental polyrhythmic Afrobeat (Polyrhythmics) OR vocal psychedelic pop-funk (Foxy Apollo). Same label, different music. The genre compound is a container, not a prescription.

The early material (the 2017 EP) skews heavier -- genre tags include "funk, indie, indie pop, jazz, jazz-funk, rock, garage." A track like "Fucked Beyond Belief" (featuring Brennan Tien on trumpet) channels raw garage-funk energy. The later albums (*Sweet Talk*, *Saturated, Faded*) move toward smoother, more produced territory -- pop-funk with jazz sophistication. The arc is from garage to polish, from rawness to craft, while keeping the funk foundation intact.

(Source: Cascadia Daily News 2023; Bandcamp genre tags; Ratings Game Music 2021)

## Career Arc

### Formation and the Self-Titled EP (2016-2017)

Sam Ashkenazy and Satchel Schwartz started Foxy Apollo in 2016 while still in high school. The original trio -- Ashkenazy on guitar and vocals, Schwartz on drums, Jack Christian on bass -- played their first shows in the Seattle area. The self-titled debut EP **Foxy Apollo** was released on **September 16, 2017**, featuring five tracks: "I Think I'm Mad," "Mental Breakdown," "Free Steppin," "Hey Sexy," and "Fucked Beyond Belief." The EP was tagged as "funk, indie, indie pop, jazz, jazz-funk, rock, garage, Seattle" on Bandcamp -- a genre description that captures the band's early eclecticism. Brennan Tien contributed trumpet on "Fucked Beyond Belief," the first horn appearance in the catalog.

The EP captures a band in its rawest form: three high schoolers playing funk with the energy and irreverence of their age. The song titles alone ("Fucked Beyond Belief," "Mental Breakdown") signal a different relationship to professionalism than any prior degree in the engine. This is not conservatory music. This is garage-funk from teenagers who love jazz.

(Source: Bandcamp; genre tags; credits)

### Tangie EP (2018)

The second EP, **Tangie**, was released on **April 1, 2018**, with four tracks: "Palomino," "Monroe," "Tangerine Dreams," and "Winston." Credits list only Ashkenazy and Schwartz, suggesting the lineup was still in flux. A review on the Kimemeera blog noted: "Listening to Foxy Apollo's music gives you a taste of the many emotions and stories that have come through the group."

(Source: Bandcamp; Kimemeera blog review)

### The U-District House Show Scene and Live Growth (2018-2019)

The Daily UW profiled Foxy Apollo in March 2019, capturing the band in its house-show phase. Ashkenazy, Schmidt, and Quick were interviewed before a show at **Chop Suey** -- a significant venue graduation from house parties. The article describes Foxy Apollo making waves in the **U-District house show scene**. Ashkenazy's quote about the Seattle music scene's mutual support -- "everyone in the music scene is so supportive of each other, and everybody wants everybody to succeed" -- maps to the same ecosystem documented at degrees 18-21 from the perspective of its youngest participants.

A **live recording at The Moth Manor** (November 15, 2019) was released by bassist Zachary Schmidt on his personal Bandcamp, capturing an eighteen-track set that demonstrates the band's live energy. A second live recording, **Live at Louie G's** (January 25, 2019), also exists on Schmidt's page. These live documents are significant: they prove the band was building its identity through performance, not just studio recordings.

By 2019, the lineup had evolved: Schwartz was out, replaced by Dallas Quick on drums. Schmidt had replaced Christian on bass. The band was performing at venues including **The Rendezvous**, **Chop Suey**, and various house shows across Seattle.

(Source: Daily UW March 2019; Zachary Schmidt Bandcamp; The Stranger Tickets; do206.com)

### Sweet Talk (2020)

The debut full-length album **Sweet Talk** was released on **May 9, 2020**. Nine tracks: "BoatPlane" (2:16), "Dolphin" (2:49), "Space Climber" (2:28), "Take a Step Back" (2:34), "Cryptic Life" (2:49), "De Novo" (3:54), "Someday" (3:49), "Free Steppin'" (3:51), and "Thankful" (2:49). The band describes *Sweet Talk* as representing "where the band has been and where the band plans to go," noting they "spent the last two years performing and reworking these songs to get them perfect." The track lengths are notably compact -- the longest is 3:54, the shortest 2:16. This is pop-scale songwriting, not the twenty-minute Afrobeat grooves of Polyrhythmics. The funk foundation is present but compressed into verse-chorus structures.

The release date (May 2020) places the album in the pandemic moment. The band was unable to tour in support of the debut -- a significant interruption for a young band that had been building through live performance.

(Source: Bandcamp; credits; album description)

## Music Theory Mapping: Funk as Platform

### Primary Technique: Vocal Funk -- the Groove as Delivery System

Degree 22's primary music theory contribution is **vocal funk as platform architecture** -- the use of a funk groove foundation to support vocal melody, pop songwriting, and multi-genre exploration. Where Polyrhythmics (degree 21) demonstrated funk-as-sovereign-groove (no vocals, rhythm is the composition), Foxy Apollo demonstrates funk-as-vocal-platform (the groove carries the voice, and the voice carries the meaning).

This is structurally distinct from Maktub's vocal-over-groove (degree 18). In Maktub, Reggie Watts' voice was the creative engine and the groove served it. In Foxy Apollo, Ashkenazy's voice is one element among many -- guitar textures, horn punctuations, keyboard washes, psychedelic effects -- all riding the funk platform. The voice is not sovereign; it is a participant. The funk groove is not servant to the voice; it is the stage on which all elements perform.

The platform architecture is audible in the track structures: compact songs (2-4 minutes), verse-chorus forms, hooks that combine vocal melody with instrumental riffs. This is pop-funk: funk's rhythmic density compressed into radio-friendly formats. The jazz heritage provides improvisational flexibility within the pop frames -- solos, extended bridges, harmonic substitutions that would be unusual in pure pop. The psychedelic element adds texture: effects pedals, reverb, distortion, spatial production.

### The Blue Note Thread: Twenty-Second Deployment -- Vocal-Funk Blue Note

Twenty-second deployment of the blue note thread. In Foxy Apollo's music, the blue note operates primarily through **Ashkenazy's vocal delivery** -- bending between minor and major thirds in a funk/pop context. The vocal blue note has appeared before (Anderson at degree 3, Watts at degree 17, Maktub at degree 18, Monge at degree 20), but Foxy Apollo's deployment is the first in a **pop-funk** context where the blue note serves both jazz authenticity and pop accessibility simultaneously. The bend signals "this is real music, not manufactured" while the pop framing ensures the signal reaches a broad audience.

The guitar also carries blue notes -- Ashkenazy's playing has been compared to Jimi Hendrix (the Daily UW noted the *Tangie* EP "exudes an easy '70s feel, with bluesy guitar licks and vocals in the style of the late, great Jimi Hendrix"). The Hendrix comparison connects the blue note to psychedelic-funk guitar tradition, a lineage that has not appeared in the engine before.

Foxy Apollo's organizational type extends the band-organizational spectrum to **four models**:

The flexible collective is the **natural organizational form of a young band** -- membership is fluid because everyone is still figuring out their lives.
