206 Zulu — Hip-Hop Culture in the PNW

DJ Riz has been active in Seattle’s hip-hop scene since 1993 and is a central figure in 206 Zulu, the Pacific Northwest chapter of the Universal Zulu Nation. Founded in the Bronx by Afrika Bambaataa in 1973, the UZN defines hip-hop through four elements: DJing, MCing, breaking, and graffiti. 206 Zulu brings this mission to Seattle, rooted in the Central District.

DJ Riz is a turntablist — using vinyl records and turntables as a musical instrument. Scratching, beat juggling, mixing, and needle drops are the technical vocabulary. Three decades of continuous practice represent deep mastery of the craft and dedication to hip-hop’s foundational element.

The Four Elements

DJing

The foundation. Turntablism, mixing, scratching, beat juggling. DJ Riz’s primary element.

MCing

Lyrical arts. Freestyle, battle rap, conscious hip-hop. The voice over the DJ’s beats.

Breaking

B-boying/b-girling. Dance as creative expression. The physical element on the cardboard.

Graffiti

Aerosol art. Visual expression. Tags, throws, pieces. The visual element on the wall.

The Central District — Heart of Black Seattle

The Central District is the historical heart of Seattle’s Black community. From the Jackson Street jazz corridor of the 1930s through the hip-hop scene of the 1990s, the CD has been where Black musical culture in Seattle lives. DJ Riz and 206 Zulu are part of this lineage.

The CD’s significance runs deep: churches, clubs, barbershops, community centers, and block parties formed the social infrastructure that sustained the community through decades of segregation and redlining. Seattle’s hip-hop scene emerged from the CD in the late 1980s and early 1990s. Since the 2000s, severe gentrification has transformed the neighborhood — the Black population declining from over 70% (1970s) to under 20% (2020s). DJ Riz represents the persistent presence of hip-hop culture even as the neighborhood changes.
