## STRUCTURAL EVENT: Central District Enters the S36 Axis

Degree 136 introduces the **Central District** as a new neighborhood in the S36 axis. This is a significant geographic expansion:

The Central District (the "CD") is the **historical heart of Seattle's Black community**. From the 1940s through the 1990s, the CD was the center of Black cultural, social, and economic life in Seattle -- home to churches, clubs, barbershops, soul food restaurants, and the social infrastructure that sustained the community through decades of segregation and redlining.

The neighborhood's significance to Seattle music:

- **Jazz heritage:** The CD's Jackson Street jazz corridor (1930s-1960s) was the original center of Black music in Seattle. Clubs along Jackson Street hosted touring jazz musicians and nurtured local talent. This predates the engine's S36 axis (which begins at degree 0) but provides the deep foundation.
- **R&B and soul:** Through the 1960s-1980s, the CD's clubs and community spaces hosted R&B, soul, and funk. The neighborhood was where Black musical culture in Seattle lived.
- **Hip-hop birthplace:** Seattle's hip-hop scene emerged from the CD in the late 1980s and early 1990s. The neighborhood's community centers, parks, and clubs were the incubators. DJ Riz and 206 Zulu are part of this lineage -- the hip-hop culture that grew from the CD's Black community infrastructure.
- **Gentrification pressure:** Since the 2000s, the CD has experienced severe gentrification. The Black population has declined dramatically (from over 70% in the 1970s to under 20% by the 2020s). The cultural institutions that sustained the community have been displaced. The CD's entry into the S36 axis at degree 136 documents a neighborhood in transformation -- the hip-hop culture DJ Riz represents emerged from a CD that was still predominantly Black.

The CD enters alongside Capitol Hill (the electronic scene at degree 135), expanding the S36 axis's geographic map of Seattle's music neighborhoods.

(Sources: Seattle Times; Historylink.org; The Stranger; Real Change)

## 206 ZULU -- THE PACIFIC NORTHWEST ZULU NATION

### The Universal Zulu Nation

The Universal Zulu Nation (UZN) is one of the foundational organizations of hip-hop culture:

- **Founded 1973:** Afrika Bambaataa founded the UZN in the Bronx, New York, as an organization dedicated to the positive development of hip-hop culture. The UZN emerged from the gang culture of the South Bronx, channeling destructive energy into creative expression through the four elements of hip-hop: DJing, MCing, breaking (b-boying/b-girling), and graffiti (aerosol art).
- **The four elements:** The UZN's framework defines hip-hop as a culture with four creative pillars, not just a music genre. DJing is the first element -- the foundation on which the other three are built. DJ Riz's practice sits at the core of this framework.
- **Chapters worldwide:** The UZN has chapters across the United States and internationally. Each chapter maintains the organization's mission of cultural preservation and community building through hip-hop.
- **Knowledge, culture, overstanding:** The UZN's philosophy emphasizes knowledge of self, cultural awareness, and "overstanding" (understanding at a deeper level). This intellectual framework elevates hip-hop from entertainment to a cultural movement.

### 206 Zulu -- The PNW Chapter

**206 Zulu** is the Pacific Northwest chapter of the Universal Zulu Nation, and DJ Riz has been a central figure:

- **Named for the area code:** 206 is Seattle's original area code, and the chapter name roots the Zulu Nation mission in Seattle specifically. The name declares: this is Seattle hip-hop, connected to the global movement.
- **Central District base:** 206 Zulu's activities have been centered in the Central District -- the neighborhood where Seattle's hip-hop culture originated. Community events, DJ battles, cyphers, workshops, and cultural programming.
- **Four-element practice:** 206 Zulu maintains the UZN's four-element framework in the PNW. This means not just DJing but supporting all four pillars: turntablism and mixing (DJing), lyrical arts (MCing), dance (breaking), and visual art (graffiti). The chapter is a complete hip-hop cultural organization.
- **Cultural preservation:** In a Seattle increasingly shaped by tech industry culture, 206 Zulu preserves the city's hip-hop heritage. The organization maintains connections to the foundational culture -- vinyl records, turntables, cyphers, community spaces -- in an era when hip-hop has been heavily commercialized and digitized.
- **Community activism:** 206 Zulu's work extends beyond entertainment to community engagement -- youth workshops, cultural education, anti-violence programming. The organization uses hip-hop culture as a tool for community development, following the UZN's original mission.

## DJ RIZ -- THE CENTRAL DISTRICT TURNTABLIST

### Active Since 1993

DJ Riz has been active in Seattle's hip-hop scene since **1993** -- making him one of the longest-running figures in the city's hip-hop community:

- **Three decades of practice:** From the early 1990s through the present, DJ Riz has maintained a continuous presence in Seattle hip-hop. This longevity spans the genre's evolution from underground to mainstream and back to independent.
- **Turntablist craft:** DJ Riz's primary instrument is the turntable. Turntablism -- the art of using vinyl records and turntables as a musical instrument rather than just a playback device -- requires years of practice. Scratching, beat juggling, mixing, and needle drops are the technical vocabulary. DJ Riz's three-decade practice represents deep mastery of this craft.
- **Live performance:** DJ Riz is primarily a live performer, not a radio DJ. His practice is community-facing -- parties, events, battles, cyphers, club nights. The energy of live performance is different from radio broadcast: the crowd is present, the feedback loop is real-time, the stakes are immediate.
- **Cultural infrastructure:** Beyond individual performance, DJ Riz has contributed to Seattle's hip-hop infrastructure -- organizing events, mentoring younger DJs, maintaining community connections. This infrastructure role parallels the institutional figures of the radio era (126-135) but operates in the community space rather than the broadcast space.

### The CD Hip-Hop Scene

DJ Riz operates within the Central District's hip-hop ecosystem:

- **Community spaces:** Community centers, parks, and local venues in the CD have hosted hip-hop events since the late 1980s. These spaces -- not commercial clubs -- were the incubators of Seattle hip-hop culture.
- **The block party tradition:** CD block parties have been a central site of hip-hop cultural expression -- DJs on the turntables, MCs on the mic, breakers on the cardboard, graffiti on the walls. DJ Riz's practice is rooted in this block party tradition.
- **Interconnected scene:** The CD's hip-hop scene is tightly interconnected -- DJs, MCs, producers, breakers, and writers know each other, collaborate, and build together. DJ Riz is embedded in this network.
- **Displacement and persistence:** As gentrification has transformed the CD, the hip-hop community has been displaced but not destroyed. Events continue, often in new locations. DJ Riz and 206 Zulu represent the persistent presence of hip-hop culture even as the neighborhood changes.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Turntablism

DJ Riz's acoustic contribution is the **sound of turntablism and hip-hop DJ culture**:

- **Sonic character:** Vinyl scratches, crossfader cuts, beat juggling, sample manipulation. The turntablist's sound palette is built from the physical interaction between needle, vinyl, and mixer. The resulting sounds are percussive, rhythmic, and textural -- a unique instrument class.
- **Scratch vocabulary:** Baby scratch, chirp, flare, crab, transformer, tear, orbit. Each scratch technique produces a distinct sound, and the turntablist combines them into phrases and passages. The vocabulary is as complex as any traditional instrument.
- **BPM range:** Hip-hop typically operates at 85-115 BPM -- slower than electronic music (120-180) but with more emphasis on rhythmic complexity within each beat. The DJ's manipulation of breakbeats within this BPM range creates the groove.
- **The breakbeat:** The fundamental unit of hip-hop DJing is the **breakbeat** -- the rhythmic break section of a funk, soul, or jazz record. The DJ isolates, loops, and extends these breaks, creating the rhythmic foundation for the other elements. DJ Riz's practice is rooted in this breakbeat tradition.
- **Live presence:** Turntablism is a visual as well as auditory performance. The DJ's physical manipulation of the record -- the hand on the platter, the fader work, the selection of the next record -- is part of the art. The sound and the gesture are inseparable.

### The Acoustic Bridge: Radio to Community

DJ Riz's acoustic position marks a transition:

- **From radio to community:** The previous DJs in the S36 axis (B-Mello 134, Indica Jones 135) operated primarily through KEXP radio broadcast. DJ Riz operates primarily in live community settings. The acoustic environment shifts from the studio to the block party, from broadcast to presence.
- **From broadcast to physical:** Radio is disembodied sound. Turntablism is embodied performance. The transition from the KEXP sextet (130-135) to DJ Riz (136) mirrors the SPS transition from flock chorus (Anatidae) to solo performance (Great Horned Owl): from collective and distributed to individual and present.
