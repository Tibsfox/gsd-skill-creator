The AM Radio King

Pat O’Day (born Paul Berg, Bremerton, Washington) was Seattle’s most famous Top 40 radio DJ — the dominant on-air personality at KJR 950 AM through the 1960s and 1970s. His booming voice, showmanship, and relentless promotional energy made him Seattle’s original radio king. He didn’t just play records — he promoted concerts, organized teen dances, and built the concert infrastructure that every PNW promoter who followed would inherit.

O’Day co-founded Concerts West, one of the West Coast’s most successful concert promotion companies. He brought the Beatles to the Seattle Center Coliseum on August 21, 1964 (14,300 fans). He promoted shows at the Spanish Castle ballroom — the venue Jimi Hendrix immortalized in “Spanish Castle Magic.” He proved that Seattle was a viable major concert market.

The Radio Arc Reaches Backward

Degrees 126–128 documented the KCMU orbit: Nasty Nes (hip-hop), Poneman (business), Pavitt (curation). All KCMU/FM/independent. Degree 129 reaches back two decades to the AM era that KCMU was born to oppose. KJR was the mainstream. KCMU was the alternative. The alternative could not exist without the mainstream to define itself against. O’Day built the mainstream.

KJR 950 AM was everything KCMU was not: commercial, Top 40, high-powered, mass-audience, playlist-driven. But O’Day was not the villain — he was a genuine champion of rock and roll at a time when the establishment opposed it. He built the concert infrastructure, trained the audience, and proved the market that the alternative movement later inherited.

Concert Promotion — Concerts West

O’Day’s Concerts West booked national acts into Seattle Center venues through the 1960s–70s. The Beatles (1964), the Rolling Stones, Led Zeppelin — acts that might have skipped Seattle without a promoter willing to take the risk and fill the seats. O’Day’s teen dances and all-ages shows built the grassroots infrastructure for live music in the PNW: audiences trained to attend, venues proven viable, production crews experienced, the regional market demonstrated.

The Spanish Castle on Highway 99 between Seattle and Tacoma was one of O’Day’s key venues. Jimi Hendrix played the same circuit in the early 1960s before leaving for London. “Spanish Castle Magic” (1967) immortalized the venue that O’Day helped make viable. The connection is infrastructural: O’Day built the circuit; Hendrix grew up in it.

The AM/FM Divide

The decline of KJR AM mirrors the decline of AM music radio nationally. The FM revolution of the 1970s offered stereo sound, wider frequency response, and format diversity that AM could not match. Album-oriented rock migrated to FM. Young listeners followed. KJR abandoned Top 40 and cycled through formats. O’Day moved to full-time concert promotion.

The AM/FM divide is an acoustic divide: KJR’s compressed, mid-range AM sound vs. KCMU’s full-fidelity FM stereo. It is also a cultural divide: mass audience vs. dedicated listener, playlist vs. freeform, commerce vs. curation. The entire KCMU/KEXP story is built on this divide. O’Day’s KJR is the thesis; KCMU is the antithesis; KEXP is the synthesis.
