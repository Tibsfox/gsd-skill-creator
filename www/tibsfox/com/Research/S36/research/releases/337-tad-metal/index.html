METAL SUMMIT OPENS — HEAVIEST SUB POP BAND. Degree 337 opens the metal summit at 94.2% of the engine's rotation. TAD is the Sub Pop catalog's weight ceiling — heavier than Mudhoney, heavier than Nirvana, maximum mass and velocity combined. Georgetown delivers the summit's first entry.

The Sub Pop Weight Ceiling

TAD is the heaviest band to emerge from the Sub Pop catalog. Not heavy in the punk-velocity sense of Mudhoney, not heavy in the dramatic metal-vocal sense of early Soundgarden — heavy in the physical sense. TAD Doyle's guitar riffs press downward. They are slow, low-tuned, and densely layered. When the band plays at full volume, the sub-bass frequencies move air differently than other Sub Pop bands. This is not an exaggeration: TAD's E=9 is measured in the physical dimension.

Georgetown provides the appropriate origin point. The neighborhood sits in Seattle's industrial south belt alongside the Duwamish River and Boeing Field — working machinery, rail infrastructure, the kind of space where rehearsal volume is not a neighborhood concern. TAD's music needed room to be loud; Georgetown provided it.

"The riff presses down. It does not rush. It does not ornament. It finds the lowest register, applies maximum distortion, and descends."

TAD Doyle — The Band's Center of Gravity

Thaddeus Eugene Doyle — TAD — named the band after himself because the band is, fundamentally, the expression of his musical character. Doyle's guitar playing combines Black Sabbath's riff patience with Sub Pop's noise-rock texture and early heavy metal's tuning approach. His vocals are low-registered and bellowing — not a screamer, not a melodic vocalist, but a massively present physical voice that sits at the center of the band's density.

The lineup's rhythm section — Kurt Danielson (bass) and Steve Wied, then Josh Sinder (drums) — anchored the weight without calling attention to itself. Gary Thorstensen's rhythm guitar doubled Doyle's riff structure to create the dual-guitar wall that defines the recorded sound. The band's architecture was designed for one purpose: maximum sustained weight.

Jack Endino — The Production Partner

Jack Endino at Reciprocal Recording is the production architect of the Sub Pop sound, and his work with TAD is among the most important collaborations in that catalog. Endino understood how to capture guitar density on tape without losing the physical character of the sound. His production approach — drum-forward, guitar-dense, vocal close — matched TAD's aesthetic perfectly.

The Endino-TAD collaboration placed TAD in the same studio ecosystem as Nirvana during the Bleach period. Both bands were Sub Pop, both worked with Endino, both were part of the 1988-1989 Seattle heavy bracket. Nirvana's commercial trajectory after Nevermind separated them permanently. In 1988-1989, TAD and Nirvana were peers.

Discography

God's Balls (1989)

Sub Pop / Endino

The debut. Maximum Sub Pop heaviness from track one. "Ritual Device," "Behemoth," "Cyanide Bath." The guitar tone is the most massive in the Sub Pop catalog to that date.

Salt Lick EP (1990)

Sub Pop

Four tracks. Maximum density in minimum time. "Salt Lick" and "Cooking with Gas" demonstrate TAD's ability to compress the full weight into under three minutes.

8-Way Santa (1991)

Sub Pop / Vig + Endino

The peak TAD statement. Produced by Endino and Butch Vig (who produced Nevermind later that year). "Jinx" and "Stumblin' Man" are the definitive tracks. The dual-guitar density is at maximum.

Inhaler (1993)

Mechanic Records

Transition to major-adjacent label. Larger-budget production, same TAD character. "Leafy Incline" and "Crippled and Crawling" confirm the identity survives the label change.

Infrared Riding Hood (1995)

Futurist Records

Final studio album. Band still in creative development on smaller indie label. Commercial marginalization does not produce creative stagnation.

Eight Arms to Hold You (1995)

Super Electro

Compilation / contractual release. Title originally intended for the second Beatles film (became Help!). TAD's cultural intelligence beneath the heavy exterior.

E=9 — Mass, Velocity, and Downward Pressure

TAD's E=9 is achieved through the combination of two dimensions that most heavy bands maximize separately: mass (how low, how dense, how physically present) and velocity (how hard the band drives). Mudhoney maximizes velocity but not mass — the punk approach is fast and irreverent. Soundgarden maximizes mass but employs dynamics — quiet moments interrupt the weight. TAD sustains both dimensions simultaneously throughout their records, with almost no dynamic relief. This sustained maximum is the E=9 definition for TAD.

The physical experience: TAD at live volume moves air differently. The sub-bass frequency combination of Danielson's bass and two low-tuned guitars creates a physical pressure that most studio recordings only partially capture. The full TAD experience is a full-body acoustic event.
