## What Worked

1. **Two-degree structure is justified.** The Classic/Pop era (234) and Hard Rock era (235) are genuinely distinct musical identities. The one-energy-point spread (E=6 vs. E=7) reflects the real difference. The decision to give Heart two degrees is the engine's most significant genre-within-band distinction.
2. **The Mushroom Records backstory is essential context.** The exploitative promotion that produced "Barracuda" is not trivia -- it is the political context that makes Heart's commercial success more meaningful. They succeeded despite active exploitation.
3. **The gender analysis is appropriately foregrounded.** Heart broke hard rock's gender barrier not by softening the music but by playing it better than the men around them. This deserves prominent placement.

## Discoveries

1. **Ann Wilson's vocal lineage is clearer than expected.** The Joplin-Plant combination: Joplin for emotional rawness, Plant for theatrical power. Both influences are identifiable in the recordings. The combination produces something neither Joplin nor Plant achieves separately.
2. **Nancy Wilson's classical guitar training.** The acoustic guitar passages ("Crazy on You" intro, "Dog and Butterfly") show classical fingerpicking technique that is unusual in hard rock context. The classical-to-hard-rock dynamic range is structural, not decorative.

## Pair Analysis: Heart (Classic/Pop) + American Kestrel

- **Energy:** E=6 + E=5 = E=11. One-point gap. The Heart commercial era is one energy step above the kestrel's resident presence.
