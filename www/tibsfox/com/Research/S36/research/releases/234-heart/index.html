Seattle's Greatest Rock Band

Heart has sold over 35 million records. Ann Wilson's voice is one of the great rock instruments. "Barracuda" is a rock classic. The band was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2013. By any metric — commercial success, artistic achievement, longevity, cultural influence — Heart is Seattle's greatest rock band, and among the most important female-fronted bands in the history of rock music.

Degree 234 documents the classic and pop arc: from "Magic Man" (1975) and "Crazy on You" (1975) through "What About Love" (1985), "These Dreams" (1986), and "Alone" (1987). The Capitol Records years. The arena tours. The MTV era. The two Wilson sisters at the commercial peak of a career that started in Bellevue and moved to Queen Anne, from Vancouver to the world.

"Heart did not ask permission to play hard rock. They played hard rock, and they were women, and the two facts were not in tension. That's the revolution — not the asking, but the doing."

The Wilson Sisters

Ann Wilson (b. 1950, raised Bellevue) is the lead vocalist — four-octave range, Janis Joplin and Robert Plant cited as influences, the defining voice of Heart across six decades. Her live performances are legendary: notes held past the point of physical plausibility, full dynamic range deployed in every set.

Nancy Wilson (b. 1954, raised Bellevue) is the guitarist and co-songwriter — classical guitar training evident in the acoustic fingerpicking, aggressive electric playing on songs like "Barracuda," and decades of songwriting partnership with her sister that produced Heart's most enduring catalog.

Key Songs — The Classic Arc

Magic Man (1975)

Mushroom Records

The breakthrough. Billboard #9. Ann Wilson's vocal from controlled to unleashed. Folk-rock gentleness to hard rock density. The PNW romanticism is in the DNA.

Barracuda (1977)

Portrait Records

Heart's signature song. Written in response to Mushroom Records' exploitative promotional stunt. Nancy Wilson's jabbing riff. Genuine fury producing great rock.

Crazy on You (1975)

Mushroom Records

Nancy's classical acoustic intro, then the band explodes. The Heart dynamic range — fragile to powerful — in one song.

These Dreams (1986)

Capitol Records — #1

Nancy Wilson sings lead. Heart's first number-one hit. The pop/rock ballad that showed the other dimension.

Alone (1987)

Capitol Records — #1

Ann Wilson's ballad showcase. From vulnerable to overwhelming in four minutes. Billy Steinberg / Tom Kelly song transformed by Ann's performance.

What About Love (1985)

Capitol Records

From the self-titled album. Part of the Capitol era commercial peak that established Heart as arena rock headliners.

Breaking Rock's Hardest Barrier

Hard rock in the 1970s was male territory — not by explicit rule but by de facto exclusion. Heart broke this not by softening the music or by asking permission, but by playing hard rock, and by being women, and by not treating these facts as contradictions. Their success forced the rock mainstream to accept women in roles beyond backing vocalist and pin-up.

The Mushroom Records incident — the label's promotional implication that Ann and Nancy Wilson were in a romantic relationship — produced "Barracuda." The response to exploitation was not a complaint but a song. And the song is one of the great rock songs. The anger is authentic; the music matches it.
