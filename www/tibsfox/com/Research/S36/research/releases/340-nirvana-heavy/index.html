The Heavy Nirvana — What This Entry Honors

Degree 340 honors the heavy dimension of Nirvana's catalog specifically — not the pop craftsmanship of "Come as You Are" or the accessibility of "Polly," but the Bleach-era weight, the B-side roar, the In Utero rawness. The metal summit context positions Nirvana correctly: they are not primarily a pop band who happened to play loud; they are a heavy band who happened to write songs that the mainstream could access.

Bleach (1989, Sub Pop): produced by Jack Endino for $606.17. "Blew," "Floyd the Barber," "School" — the heavy template before the polished production. In Utero (1993, DGC): Steve Albini's raw production, Cobain's deliberate attempt to return to the heavy origin after Nevermind's commercial transformation. "Frances Farmer Will Have Her Revenge on Seattle." "Pennyroyal Tea." The loud side of the final album.

The Discography

Bleach (1989)

Sub Pop / Endino / $606.17

The heavy origin. Jack Endino producing. "Blew," "School," "Floyd the Barber." The Melvins influence fully present. Maximum Sub Pop weight before the pop instinct was fully developed. The record that preceded everything.

Nevermind (1991)

DGC / Butch Vig / 30M

The commercial apex. Butch Vig producing. "Smells Like Teen Spirit." 30 million copies worldwide. Ended hair metal's commercial dominance. The proto-grunge template carried to the global mainstream. The heavy dimension is still present beneath the production.

In Utero (1993)

DGC / Steve Albini

The return to raw. Steve Albini producing. Deliberate move away from Nevermind's polish. "Frances Farmer Will Have Her Revenge on Seattle." "Pennyroyal Tea." The heaviest major-label Nirvana record. Cobain's artistic assertion over commercial trajectory.

MTV Unplugged (1994, posthumous)

DGC / acoustic

The quiet side. David Bowie, Lead Belly, Vaselines covers. The breadth of Cobain's listening documented in a single performance. Released November 1994, seven months after his death. Acoustic but not less intense.

Kurt Cobain — With Respect

Kurt Donald Cobain: born February 20, 1967 in Aberdeen, Washington. Died April 5, 1994 in Seattle, Washington. Age 27. He left a wife and a daughter (Frances Bean Cobain, born August 1992). The King County Medical Examiner's Office determined the cause of death to be a self-inflicted gunshot wound. The investigation concluded suicide.

The engine documents this once and completely, with respect for the person and the family he left behind. The manner of his death is a biographical fact; it is not the meaning of his music. The meaning is in the songs — and the songs have been in continuous cultural circulation since 1991. They hold up because the songwriting is genuinely good and the emotional content is genuinely present. The music outlasts the biography.

Frances Bean Cobain (born 1992) is a visual artist. The engine notes her existence and does not proceed further into her life.

The Through-Line: Deep Six to Nirvana

The engine's most carefully constructed sequential arc closes here. Deep Six (1986, C/Z Records): Skin Yard (degree 338) + Green River (degree 339) + Melvins (the Montesano band who taught Cobain to play guitar). Green River contains the Mudhoney wing and the Pearl Jam wing. The Melvins connection runs from the Deep Six co-appearance through Buzz Osborne's direct guitar teaching to Cobain. Nirvana is the specific synthesis of the Sub Pop heavy tradition (Endino, Bleach, TAD as contemporaries) and the proto-grunge template (Green River, the Melvins, the Deep Six underground). The engine documents the complete genealogy across four consecutive degrees.
