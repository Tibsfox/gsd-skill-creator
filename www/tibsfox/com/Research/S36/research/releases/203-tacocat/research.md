## TACOCAT -- THE NAME

### A Palindrome

The name itself is a statement:

- **Tacocat.** T-A-C-O-C-A-T. It reads the same forwards and backwards. The name is playful, absurdist, and memorable. It sets the tone for everything the band does: serious politics delivered through a framework of pop-culture fun and absurdist humor.
- **The palindrome as philosophy.** Symmetry. What you see is what you get, from either direction. No hidden agenda, no pretension. The name is silly because the band is comfortable being silly -- and they use that comfort as a weapon. The silliness disarms. The politics land harder because you were laughing.
- **Branding genius.** "Tacocat" is one of the most memorable band names in Seattle's modern indie scene. It's Google-friendly, merchandise-ready, and instantly communicates the band's aesthetic: fun, irreverent, approachable.

## THE BAND -- FORMATION AND MEMBERS

### Capitol Hill Punk Pop

Tacocat's lineup and approach:

- **Formation.** Formed in 2007 in Seattle. Emily Nokes (vocals), Eric Randall (guitar), Lelah Maupin (drums), Bree McKenna (bass). The core lineup has been remarkably stable.
- **Emily Nokes.** Vocalist and lyricist. Nokes' voice is bright, clear, and conversational -- not the screamed vocals of hardcore punk, but the pop-punk delivery of someone telling you a story that happens to be set over power chords. Her lyrics address feminism, body politics, social media, menstruation, catcalling, and daily life with equal parts anger and humor.
- **Musical style.** Fast, bright, hook-driven punk pop. Two-minute songs with three-chord progressions and choruses that stick. The Ramones filtered through Bikini Kill filtered through 2010s internet culture. Simple structures, maximum catchiness.
- **Subject matter.** Tacocat's lyrics tackle feminist subjects directly: "Crimson Wave" (menstruation), "Men Explain Things to Me" (mansplaining, after Rebecca Solnit), "Hey Girl" (street harassment). The subjects are political. The delivery is pop.
- **The humor.** Tacocat is funny. Not ironic-funny like Chastity Belt (201), but genuinely, openly funny. The humor is the vehicle: laugh first, think second. The political point lands because the song is already stuck in your head.

(Sources: Tacocat formation; Emily Nokes; band members; lyrical analysis; feminist punk pop)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Tacocat

- **Tempo.** Fast. 140-180 BPM. Punk tempos. The songs rush forward -- urgency is built into the rhythm.
- **Guitar.** Distorted but bright. Power chords, simple riffs, occasional melodic leads. The guitar is rhythmic, not atmospheric. No reverb (unlike La Luz). Dry, punchy, fast.
- **Bass.** Fast, driving, following the guitar changes. McKenna's bass provides rhythmic foundation without elaborate melodic lines.
- **Vocals.** Emily Nokes: bright, clear, conversational-to-shouted. Pop delivery with punk edges. Not screamed. Not whispered. The vocals sit on top of the mix, clearly articulated, because the lyrics matter.
- **Drums.** Fast, driving, simple patterns. The drums and bass lock into a punk groove: straight ahead, no fill for its own sake. Energy through speed.
- **Production.** Bright, clean-ish, punchy. More polished than garage punk but not slick. The production serves the hooks and the lyrics -- clarity is the priority.
- **Song length.** Short. 2:00-3:00 minutes. Punk economy: say it, sing it, done.
