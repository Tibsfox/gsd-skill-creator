## JONATHAN PONEMAN -- FROM KCMU TO SUB POP

### The KCMU Years

Jonathan Poneman arrived at Seattle's music scene through **KCMU**, the University of Washington's freeform radio station. KCMU (which became KEXP in 2001) was the crucial broadcast platform for underground Seattle music in the 1980s. The station operated at low power but with enormous cultural influence -- it was the radio station that played the bands nobody else would play.

Poneman's DJ work at KCMU gave him three things:

1. **Access to the underground scene.** KCMU DJs were embedded in the local music ecology. They went to shows, talked to bands, received demo tapes. Poneman was hearing the sound of Seattle before anyone had a name for it.
2. **A meeting with Bruce Pavitt.** Pavitt was already publishing his Sub Pop fanzine and doing a music column when Poneman encountered him through the KCMU orbit. The two recognized complementary skills: Pavitt had the ear, Poneman had the business instinct.
3. **An understanding of how music reaches audiences.** Radio is distribution. A DJ learns what it takes to get music from the artist to the listener. This understanding of the pipeline -- create, distribute, promote -- would become the foundation of Sub Pop's approach.

The KCMU connection also links Poneman to **Nasty Nes Rodriguez** (degree 126), who was a hip-hop DJ at KCMU and later at commercial stations. The KCMU orbit produced radio figures across genres -- Nasty Nes for hip-hop, Poneman for what would become grunge. The station was a **training ground for cultural curators** across the Seattle music spectrum.

## THE FOUNDING OF SUB POP RECORDS

### 1986-1988: From Idea to Label

The founding of Sub Pop Records is one of the most consequential moments in Seattle music history. The timeline:

- **1986:** Poneman and Pavitt formalize their partnership. Pavitt had already been using the "Sub Pop" name for his fanzine and cassette compilations (see degree 128 for Pavitt's origin story). Poneman brought business acumen and startup capital -- reportedly $19,000 of his own money plus money borrowed from his father.
- **1987:** Sub Pop releases its first vinyl singles. The label begins to coalesce around a sound: heavy, distorted, raw guitar rock drawing from punk, metal, and classic rock. The bands are local: **Green River, Soundgarden, Mudhoney, TAD, Nirvana**.
- **1988:** The **Sub Pop Singles Club** launches -- a subscription service that mailed a new 7-inch single to subscribers every month. This was a stroke of marketing genius. The Singles Club created a **collector's market** and a **recurring revenue stream** simultaneously. It also created a sense of community among subscribers -- you were part of something, a member of the club.
- **1988:** Sub Pop releases **Mudhoney's "Touch Me I'm Sick"** and **Soundgarden's "Hunted Down"** -- two of the earliest defining documents of the Seattle sound on vinyl. The label's aesthetic is established: raw production, heavy guitar, underground credibility.

### Poneman's Contribution to the Founding

Poneman's specific contributions were:

- **Capital:** He put up the initial money. Without Poneman's willingness to invest personal funds, there is no Sub Pop.
- **Business structure:** He handled the legal formation, distribution agreements, accounting. The unglamorous work that makes a label function.
- **Marketing vision:** Poneman understood that Sub Pop needed to be more than a collection of records -- it needed to be a **brand**. The Sub Pop aesthetic (logo, design sensibility, attitude) was partly Poneman's creation. He understood that the label itself could be a cultural object, not just a distributor.
- **Promotional instincts:** Poneman was the person who invited British journalist **Everett True** to Seattle to write about the scene for *Melody Maker*. This single act of promotional genius -- flying a UK journalist to Seattle, taking him to shows, letting him see the scene firsthand -- was instrumental in creating the international buzz that preceded the grunge explosion.

## THE NIRVANA DEAL AND THE NEAR-DEATH EXPERIENCE

### 1989-1991: Bleach, Bankruptcy, and the Deal That Saved Everything

Sub Pop's early years were a financial tightrope. The label had cultural cachet but almost no money. The story of Sub Pop's near-bankruptcy and rescue is one of the most dramatic narratives in independent music:

- **1989:** Sub Pop releases **Nirvana's *Bleach*** for a reported $606.17 in recording costs. The album sells modestly but builds Nirvana's reputation.
- **1990-1991:** Sub Pop is hemorrhaging money. The label has signed more bands than it can afford to support. Distribution is unreliable. Checks bounce. Bands are owed royalties that don't exist. **Poneman and Pavitt are personally in debt.** The label that had defined the Seattle sound is months from collapse.
- **1991:** Nirvana signs to **DGC Records** (a Geffen subsidiary) for a reported advance of $287,000. As part of the deal, Sub Pop negotiates **a percentage of future Nirvana royalties and a buyout payment**. The specific terms: Sub Pop received approximately $75,000 upfront and a percentage of royalties on the next two Nirvana albums.
- **September 1991:** Nirvana releases ***Nevermind*** on DGC. The album sells 30 million copies worldwide. Sub Pop's royalty percentage on *Nevermind* generates **millions of dollars** -- enough to pay off all debts, fund operations for years, and transform Sub Pop from a struggling indie into a financially stable institution.

Poneman's role in the Nirvana deal was critical. He negotiated the terms that included the royalty percentage -- a provision that was unusual for a band leaving an independent label for a major. Without that royalty clause, Sub Pop would have received a one-time buyout and nothing more. With it, the label received a financial lifeline that funded its survival and growth for the next decade.

The irony is profound: **the label that championed independence was saved by a major-label deal**. But the lesson Poneman drew from the experience was not that major labels were the answer -- it was that **survival required financial discipline and strategic thinking**. Sub Pop after 1991 was a different animal: still independent, still artistically adventurous, but now with the business infrastructure to sustain itself.

## SUB POP AFTER GRUNGE: THE LONG GAME

### 1992-Present: Building a Label That Outlasts a Genre

The post-grunge years are where Poneman's vision is most visible. Many labels that rode the grunge wave collapsed when the wave receded. Sub Pop survived because Poneman understood that **a label's identity cannot be tied to a single genre**:

- **1990s post-grunge:** Sub Pop signed **The Postal Service, Sebadoh, Eric's Trip, Velocity Girl** -- bands that had nothing to do with grunge. The label's identity shifted from "grunge label" to "independent label with great taste."
- **2000s reinvention:** Sub Pop signed **The Shins, Iron and Wine, Band of Horses, Wolf Parade, Clap Your Hands Say Yeah** -- the indie rock/folk explosion of the mid-2000s. The label was once again at the center of a cultural moment, but this time it was a different sound entirely.
- **2010s expansion:** **Fleet Foxes, Beach House, Shabazz Palaces, Father John Misty, IDLES, Weyes Blood**. Sub Pop's roster in the 2010s spanned baroque folk, dream pop, experimental hip-hop, singer-songwriter, and post-punk. The label had become genre-agnostic -- united only by quality and independence.
- **Warner Bros. partnership (1995):** Sub Pop sold a 49% stake to Warner Music Group in 1995, giving the label access to major-label distribution while retaining artistic independence. This was another Poneman-engineered survival move: the financial security of corporate backing without surrendering curatorial control. The partnership has endured for three decades.

Poneman's long-game strategy is the opposite of the stereotype of the independent label founder as a romantic idealist doomed to financial ruin. He is a **pragmatist** -- willing to take corporate money, willing to negotiate hard, willing to evolve the label's sound -- in service of a principle: **that independent music needs institutions that last**. Sub Pop at 40 years old is that institution.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound That Poneman Built

Jonathan Poneman did not create a sound in the way that a musician creates a sound. He created the **infrastructure** for a sound to exist and be heard. The "acoustic profile" of Poneman's contribution is the acoustic profile of Sub Pop itself:

- **The early Sub Pop sound (1987-1991):** Heavy, distorted, raw. Jack Endino production. Mudhoney, TAD, Nirvana, Soundgarden. Loud guitars, screaming vocals, punk energy filtered through metal heaviness. **E=6 territory** -- the maximum energy of the Seattle sound.
- **The Singles Club sound:** Diverse. Each month brought a different band, a different approach. The Singles Club was the label's curatorial showcase -- not one sound but a range of sounds unified by quality and independence.
- **The post-grunge sound:** Eclectic. From the melodic pop of The Postal Service to the baroque folk of Fleet Foxes to the dream pop of Beach House. Sub Pop's sound after grunge is defined by its refusal to be defined.
- **The label as instrument:** Poneman's contribution is the label itself -- the infrastructure of recording, pressing, distributing, promoting. The label is the instrument through which all these sounds reach the world. E=3 for Poneman reflects this: the energy is not in the performance but in the system that makes performance possible.

### The KCMU Connection

The radio connection is significant acoustically: KCMU was the **broadcast channel** for the sound that became Sub Pop. Before there were records, there was radio. Poneman's time at KCMU gave him an understanding of how sound travels from source to listener -- through airwaves, through speakers, through the act of curation. The DJ selects; the label signs. Both are acts of acoustic curation.
