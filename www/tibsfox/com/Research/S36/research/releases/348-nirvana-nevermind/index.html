Nevermind (1991)

Nevermind was released on September 24, 1991. It entered the Billboard 200 at #144. It reached #1 in January 1992, displacing Michael Jackson’s Dangerous. More than 30 million copies sold worldwide. These facts are the most efficient summary of what the album did: the most commercially dominant artist of the 1980s was displaced by a band from Aberdeen, Washington, on a DGC subsidiary label, playing music that had been considered unmarketable underground content eighteen months earlier.

The quiet verse. The loud chorus. The compression and the release. “Smells Like Teen Spirit” is five minutes and one second that defined a decade’s transition.

The production. Butch Vig produced; Andy Wallace mixed. The achievement: guitar tones heavy enough to be authentic, accessible enough to cross to FM radio. Distorted but melodic. Loud but structured. The production solved the transition record’s defining problem: how to be both things simultaneously.

The arc. Nevermind opens with “Smells Like Teen Spirit” and closes with “Something in the Way” — acoustic guitar, minimal arrangement, Cobain’s voice at its most withdrawn. The album begins with a detonation and ends in near-silence. The arc is not accidental.
