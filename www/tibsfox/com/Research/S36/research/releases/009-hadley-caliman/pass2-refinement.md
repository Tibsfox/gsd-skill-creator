### The Reed as Confirmation: Saxophone Tone Production and the Explorer 3 Tape Recorder

Pass 1 documented Caliman's tenor saxophone as the engine's first reed instrument and analyzed its tone production as the closest to the human voice of any wind instrument. Pass 2 connects this to Explorer 3's mission profile — the confirmation mission, the tape recorder that proved the Van Allen belt discovery was real — and to liverwort's role as the most ancient extant land plant lineage.

Explorer 3 launched on March 26, 1958 — less than two months after Explorer 1 discovered the Van Allen radiation belts. Explorer 3 carried the same Geiger-Mueller counter as Explorer 1, but with a critical addition: an onboard tape recorder. Explorer 1's data had been transmitted in real time — the ground stations received data only when the satellite was within radio range, which was a fraction of each orbit. The tape recorder on Explorer 3 changed this: the satellite recorded radiation measurements continuously throughout each orbit, then transmitted the accumulated data in a burst when the satellite passed over a ground station. The result was complete orbital coverage — radiation measurements from the entire orbit, not just the portions within radio range.

The tape recorder did not discover anything new. It confirmed what Explorer 1 had found: the radiation belt was real, the particle flux profile matched the earlier measurements, and the belt's structure was consistent around the orbit. Explorer 3 was the confirmation mission. Its contribution was not novelty but verification — proving that Explorer 1's discovery was reproducible, that the data was not an artifact of incomplete coverage, and that the belt existed everywhere it was supposed to exist.

Caliman's tenor saxophone performs the same function in the S36 engine. The saxophone is not the first instrument (that was the arrangement, degree 0). It is not the first wind instrument (Anderson's voice, degree 3, is functionally a wind instrument — breath through a vibrating element). It is not the first instrument whose tone production is controlled by embouchure (trumpet, degree 4; trombone, degree 7). What the saxophone is, at degree 9, is the confirmation — the instrument that proves the patterns established by earlier degrees are consistent and reproducible. The hard bop vocabulary that Bown introduced at degree 6 (piano) is confirmed in a different instrumental voice at degree 9 (saxophone). The body-as-sound-source thread that Anderson initiated at degree 3 (voice) is confirmed through a different body-mediated mechanism at degree 9 (reed-embouchure). The pedagogical role that Priester established at degree 7 (Cornish) is confirmed by a second Cornish faculty member at degree 9. Each confirmation strengthens the pattern — the way Explorer 3's tape recorder data strengthened the Van Allen belt discovery.

Marchantia polymorpha — common liverwort — is among the oldest extant lineages of land plants. Liverworts (Marchantiophyta) diverged from the lineage leading to mosses and vascular plants approximately 470 million years ago. They are the most ancient surviving land-plant lineage — the confirmation that the colonization of land by photosynthetic organisms actually happened and the organisms that did it still exist. Marchantia does not innovate. It persists. It confirms, through its continued existence, that a 470-million-year-old strategy — flattened thallus photosynthesis on moist surfaces — still works. The liverwort is the tape recorder: the storage medium that preserves the record of the original event and makes it available for replay.

### The Cornish Pipeline Solidifies: Two Faculty, Two Instruments, One Institution

Pass 1 noted Caliman's Cornish faculty position alongside Priester (degree 7). Pass 2 documents this as the Cornish pipeline's consolidation — the second data point that transforms a single faculty member's presence into an institutional pattern.

One Cornish faculty member (Priester, degree 7) is an anecdote. Two Cornish faculty members (Priester + Caliman, degrees 7 and 9) is a pipeline. The engine now has two institutional pipelines with multiple artists: Garfield (Jones + Anderson, degrees 0 and 3) and Cornish (Priester + Caliman, degrees 7 and 9). The parallel is structural: both institutions concentrated jazz talent in one place, and both transmitted that talent to the broader Seattle scene through decades of teaching.

The Cornish pipeline differs from the Garfield pipeline in pedagogical orientation. Garfield was secondary education — fundamentals, literacy, ensemble participation. Cornish is post-secondary arts education — experimentation, interdisciplinary practice, boundary-crossing. The students who emerged from Garfield (Jones, Anderson) left Seattle for national careers. The students who emerged from Cornish populated Seattle's experimental music scene. The two pipelines produce different kinds of musicians for different kinds of careers.

### The Oklahoma-to-Seattle Migration: Deep South to Pacific Northwest

Caliman's Idabel, Oklahoma origin introduces a new migration path to the engine. Previous non-PNW origins: Chicago (Priester, degree 7), Idaho (Peacock, degree 8). Caliman adds Oklahoma — the Deep South / Southern Plains, a region whose musical culture (blues, gospel, country, early jazz) is fundamentally different from Chicago's urban jazz or Idaho's rural isolation.

The Oklahoma-to-LA-to-Seattle path traces the same Great Migration trajectory that brought millions of Black Americans from the South to West Coast cities during the mid-twentieth century. Caliman's path — rural Oklahoma to urban Los Angeles to Seattle — is a musical instantiation of this demographic movement. The tenor saxophone he carried was shaped by the same forces that shaped the Great Migration: the economic and racial pressures that pushed Black talent out of the South and pulled it toward the opportunities (real and imagined) of the West Coast.

Explorer 3 followed a parallel institutional migration. The satellite was an ABMA/JPL project — the same Army-JPL partnership that built Explorer 1 (degree 7) and Pioneer 3-4 (degrees 4-5). But by March 1958, the institutional landscape was shifting: NASA would be founded six months later, and the ABMA's rocket team would be transferred to the new agency within two years. Explorer 3 was launched from the old institutional context (Army) into a landscape that was transitioning toward the new one (NASA). Caliman was formed in the old musical context (hard bop LA) and migrated into a landscape that was transitioning toward the new one (experimental Seattle). Both entities carry the old tradition into the new context, and the transition enriches both.
