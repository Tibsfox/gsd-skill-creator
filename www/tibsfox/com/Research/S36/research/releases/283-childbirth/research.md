## THE SUPERGROUP STRUCTURE

### Three Bands in One

Childbirth is explicitly a supergroup -- a band formed from members of multiple other active bands:

- **Bree McKenna (bass).** McKenna is the connective tissue between Childbirth and Tacocat (degree 282). She is Tacocat's bassist and Childbirth's founding bassist. Her presence in two consecutive engine degrees makes her the engine's tightest personnel cross-reference since Jason Finn in Love Battery and PUSA.
- **Julia Shapiro (guitar, vocals).** Shapiro is the primary songwriter and guitarist of Chastity Belt, one of the central Hardly Art bands of the 2010s. Chastity Belt's music is indie rock with a sardonic, feminist edge -- songs about being bored, being judged, being overlooked. Shapiro brings this sensibility to Childbirth: the observations are sharper, the politics more explicit, the tone darker than Tacocat.
- **Stacy Peck (drums, vocals).** Peck is Childbirth's third corner and the organizer of the project. Her drumming style is energetic and propulsive; her vocal contributions add another layer to the band's multi-voice approach. Childbirth is unusual in having three vocalists who can all sing lead -- the vocal democracy is part of the band's character.
- **The supergroup dynamic.** McKenna brings Tacocat's melodic instinct and punk pop momentum. Shapiro brings Chastity Belt's sardonic indie rock perspective and songwriting depth. Peck brings the organizational energy that made Childbirth happen. Three different approaches, one band.

## THE NAME: CHILDBIRTH

### The Political Economy of a Band Name

Childbirth as a band name is not accidental:

- **The contested territory.** Childbirth sits at the intersection of multiple cultural and political conflicts: reproductive rights, medicalization of women's bodies, the politics of pain (epidurals, natural birth, C-sections), the language used to describe and regulate women's reproductive experiences. The name claims this territory directly.
- **The humor register.** Childbirth is also funny as a band name in the same way that Tacocat's palindrome is clever: it makes you think twice, it has a knowing quality, it announces the band's willingness to go to uncomfortable places with a smile. The discomfort is the point.
- **The tradition.** Naming a punk band after a medical or bodily process is a tradition: Dead Kennedys, The Misfits, various body-horror bands across punk history. Childbirth places itself in this tradition but redirects it: the bodily experience here is specifically a women's bodily experience, and the claiming of it is a feminist act.
- **The subject matter follows.** Childbirth's songs engage with women's bodies, reproductive experience, and the cultural context of both -- not didactically but with the dark humor that the name promises. The name is the aesthetic contract.

(Sources: Childbirth band name analysis; reproductive rights cultural context; feminist body politics; punk naming tradition; dark humor aesthetic)

## CHILDBIRTH VS. TACOCAT: THE SISTER BANDS

### Same Label, Different Temperature

The comparison between Tacocat (282) and Childbirth (283) is explicit in the engine:

- **E=7 on both entries.** The same energy number. But E=7 in Tacocat sounds different from E=7 in Childbirth. Tacocat is warm, hook-driven, the feminist content delivered with brightness. Childbirth is darker, more confrontational, the feminist content delivered with more explicit irony and discomfort. The energy level is the same; the temperature is different.
- **The subject matter difference.** Tacocat's "Crimson Wave" is matter-of-fact about menstruation. Childbirth goes to harder places: the band's album *Women's Rights* (2015) uses the title's apparent sincerity against itself. The title sounds like a protest chant; the music delivers it as dark punk comedy. The irony is the point.
- **Shared Hardly Art home.** Both bands operate within the same institutional context. The label doesn't prescribe the temperature -- both Tacocat's warmth and Childbirth's darkness fit within the Hardly Art aesthetic range. The label is wide enough for both.
- **The McKenna bridge.** Bree McKenna playing bass in both bands is not just a cross-reference -- it is a literal musical bridge. The same hands on the same instrument, contributing to two different E=7 approaches to the same feminist punk pop tradition.

## THE SOUND -- PUNK POP, DARKER, E=7

### Confrontational Irony and Three Voices

Childbirth's sound is punk pop in the same technical sense as Tacocat, but the approach differs:

- **Three vocalists.** Peck, Shapiro, and McKenna all sing. The vocal texture of Childbirth is therefore denser and more varied than a single-vocalist band. Songs can shift between voices, create harmonies, or deploy different voices for different emotional registers. The vocal democracy is a formal choice that produces a specific sound.
- **The Julia Shapiro influence.** Shapiro's Chastity Belt songwriting background brings depth and structure to Childbirth's songs. The arrangements can be more varied than Tacocat's hook-driven consistency -- Childbirth songs are willing to sit in uncomfortable keys and rhythms when the subject requires it.
- **The guitars.** Childbirth's guitar work is fuzzier and more abrasive than Tacocat's bright pop guitar. The songs have more edge. The punk energy is more prominently expressed in the guitar tone.
- **The humor type.** Childbirth's humor is darker. It doesn't invite you to recognize something amusing; it invites you to recognize something uncomfortable and laugh at that. The irony is more weaponized than Tacocat's warm wit.
- **Song length and structure.** Childbirth songs are compact -- short, direct, to the point. The format is classic punk pop brevity. The brevity makes the dark content land harder: there is no space to recover before the song ends.

(Sources: Childbirth sound analysis; three-vocalist configuration; Julia Shapiro Chastity Belt influence; guitar tone comparison; dark humor type; song length and brevity)

## CHASTITY BELT: THE JULIA SHAPIRO CONNECTION

### The Third Band in the Network

Chastity Belt is Julia Shapiro's primary band and a central figure in the Hardly Art catalog:

- **Chastity Belt.** Formed in Walla Walla, Washington (at Whitman College) in 2010, Chastity Belt moved to Seattle and became one of the defining bands of the Hardly Art 2010s roster. The band's sound -- indie rock with sardonic feminist lyrics, slow-building tension and release -- is the more measured version of what Childbirth does at higher energy.
- **Shapiro's songwriting.** Chastity Belt's songs are notable for their precise observation of boredom, alienation, and women's experience in social and romantic contexts. "Time to Go Home," "Drone," "Seattle Party" -- songs with a deadpan specificity. Shapiro brings this specificity to Childbirth in a more energized form.
- **The Hardly Art triple.** Chastity Belt, Tacocat, and Childbirth form a de facto Hardly Art triple: three bands, overlapping personnel (Shapiro in Chastity Belt and Childbirth; McKenna in Tacocat and Childbirth), same Capitol Hill neighborhood, same label. The engine documents Childbirth (283) and Tacocat (282) directly; Chastity Belt is documented through the cross-references.
- **The Whitman College origin.** Chastity Belt's formation at Whitman College (Walla Walla, WA) and subsequent migration to Seattle is a specific PNW pattern: college town formation, city migration. The Pacific Northwest college-to-Seattle pipeline is a recurring theme in the engine.

(Sources: Chastity Belt biography; Whitman College formation; Seattle migration; Shapiro songwriting; Hardly Art triple network; Walla Walla PNW origin)

## LEGACY AND CURRENT STATUS

### A Productive and Underrated Supergroup

- **The simultaneous careers problem.** Childbirth is a side project for all three members -- each has a primary band that demands attention (Tacocat, Chastity Belt, and Peck's other projects). The band's production pace is therefore constrained by the members' multiple commitments. Two albums in one year (2015) followed by a slower pace is the pattern of a genuinely enthusiastic collaboration that is not the members' full-time focus.
- **The contribution to the feminist punk map.** Childbirth occupies a specific position between Tacocat's warmth and Bikini Kill's ferocity. The dark irony mode -- feminist politics delivered through uncomfortable humor at E=7 -- is its own contribution to the range of feminist punk expression. Without Childbirth, the map of this territory is incomplete.
- **The McKenna axis.** Bree McKenna's participation in both Tacocat (282) and Childbirth (283) makes her the most documented individual in the engine's 282-284 block. The bass player as connective tissue between two expressions of the same Capitol Hill scene moment.

(Sources: Childbirth legacy; simultaneous career constraints; feminist punk position between Tacocat and Bikini Kill; McKenna axis; Capitol Hill scene documentation)
