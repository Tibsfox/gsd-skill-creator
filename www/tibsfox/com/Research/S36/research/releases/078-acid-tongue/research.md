## Biography: Acid Tongue and the Laurel Canyon Sound

### The Band

Acid Tongue is led by **Guy Keltner**, a Seattle-based musician and songwriter. The band is a loose collective -- a core group with rotating members, the kind of project where friends show up to play, the lineup shifts, and the music stays communal. This looseness is structural: it produces the porch-party sound that defines the band.

### The Sound

Acid Tongue's music blends **folk, country, and garage rock** with a loose, communal energy. The Laurel Canyon reference is apt: the warm harmonies, the sun-baked guitar tones, the pedal steel, the sense of friends making music together in a canyon house. But this is Seattle, not Los Angeles, and the Capitol Hill edge sharpens the sound -- there's garage-rock energy in the rhythm section, indie looseness in the arrangements, and a Pacific Northwest dampness in the atmosphere.

Albums:

- **Acid Tongue** (2017, self-released) -- the self-titled debut. Folk, country, and garage rock blend. Loose, communal energy. The sound of a band finding its voice by not trying too hard to find it.
- **Arroyo** (2020, self-released) -- the second album. Named for a dry creek bed. More expansive, more sun-baked. Country-folk with psychedelic edges. The canyon deepens. The looseness becomes a vocabulary.

### The Live Show

Acid Tongue's live shows embody the **porch party** energy -- inclusive, warm, slightly chaotic. The band plays for the room, not for the stage. The audience becomes part of the performance. The energy is communal rather than spectacle.

The jangle is communal because it sounds like a band having a good time together -- not precision performance but collaborative joy.

## Blue Note 78: The Loose Harmony

The **loose harmony** is the seventy-eighth blue note deployment. The blue note that sounds in the intentional imperfection of group singing -- voices not quite aligned, guitars slightly out of phase, the rhythm section pushing and pulling. The beauty is in the gap between perfect alignment and where the musicians actually land. The loose harmony is the sound of trust -- musicians comfortable enough with each other to not need precision.
