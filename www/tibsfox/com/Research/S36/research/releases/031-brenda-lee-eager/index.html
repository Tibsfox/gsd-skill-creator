She was born in Lower Peach Tree, Alabama, and sang in churches before she could read music. She moved to Chicago and became the voice beneath Jerry Butler’s cool — the warmth under the Iceman’s surface. “Ain’t Understanding Mellow” sold a million copies on Mercury Records. She sang backup for Ray Charles, wrote songs for Prince, Aretha Franklin, and Gladys Knight. She carried the soul tradition forward through five decades of duets, session work, songwriting, and choral direction. The relay runner — not the one who starts the race but the one who keeps the baton moving. The mellow undertow.

“Ain’t Understanding Mellow” — The Gold Standard

Written by Homer Talbert and Herscholt Polk, arranged by James Mack, produced by Gerald Sims and Jerry Butler. Released late 1971 on Mercury Records. No. 3 R&B, No. 21 Hot 100. Over one million copies sold. RIAA Gold certification, April 1972. The definitive Chicago soul duet of the early 1970s — Butler’s Iceman cool set against Eager’s warm, gospel-rooted delivery. The tension between the two vocal temperatures IS the song.

The Arc: Lower Peach Tree to Culver City

August 8, 1947 — Mobile, Alabama

Born Brenda Lee Eager. Raised in Lower Peach Tree, Wilcox County, Alabama — a small rural town on the Alabama River. Begins singing in church as a child. By tenth grade, leads her own vocal group.

c. 1964 — Prichard, Alabama

First professional singing engagement at the Kings Club in Prichard, at age seventeen. Develops stage presence on the small-venue circuit of coastal Alabama.

Late 1960s — Chicago, Illinois

Relocates to Chicago, drawn by the city’s soul music infrastructure: Mercury Records, Chess Records, Brunswick/Dakar, Curtom. Joins the Chicago soul scene during its peak period.

1971 — Jerry Butler’s Peaches

Becomes lead singer of Peaches, Jerry Butler’s backup vocal group. Her voice — warm, controlled, gospel-rooted — distinguishes her from other group members and earns her the role of duet partner with “The Iceman.”

1971–1972 — Gold Single

“Ain’t Understanding Mellow” reaches No. 3 R&B, No. 21 Hot 100. Over one million copies sold. RIAA Gold (April 1972). The Butler-Eager partnership also produces “(They Long to Be) Close to You” (No. 6 R&B).

1973 — Duet Album

The Love We Have, The Love We Had — full duet album with Jerry Butler on Mercury Records. Recorded in Memphis and Chicago. Conceptual two-sided structure: “The Love We Have” vs. “The Love We Had.”

1974–1984 — Solo Career

“When I’m With You” (1974, produced by Larry Mizell) — later sampled by Ghostface Killah and Snoop Dogg. “Good Old Fashioned Lovin’” (Playboy Records, 1975, No. 71 R&B). “Watch My Body Talk” (Private-I Records, 1984).

1970s–1990s — Background and Songwriting

Background vocals for Ray Charles (degree 30), Diana Ross, Stevie Wonder, Mavis Staples, Graham Nash. Songs recorded by Aretha Franklin, Gladys Knight, Bobby Womack, Shirley Brown, Peaches & Herb.

1996 — Prince

Co-writes “Somebody’s Somebody” with Prince and Hilliard Wilson for Prince’s Emancipation album. The single achieves double-platinum certification — Eager’s most commercially successful composition.

2006–Present — Los Angeles

Directs The Heaven on Earth (T.H.E.) Choir at the City of Angels Church, Culver City, California. Writes and performs Grace, a one-woman musical theatre show based on her life (premiered 2010). The arc returns to its origin: church singing.

Chicago Soul Duet Form

Butler-Eager as exemplary case; descended from the gospel-secular fusion that Charles (degree 30) pioneered.
