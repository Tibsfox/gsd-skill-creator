## CHERYL WATERS -- THE MIDDAY SHOW

### Twenty-Five+ Years On Air

Cheryl Waters joined KCMU (the predecessor to KEXP) in **1996**, placing her among the station's longest-serving on-air personalities. She was present for the critical transition period when KCMU became KEXP -- the rebranding that accompanied the Paul Allen Foundation's transformative $4.2 million gift in 2001 that freed the station from University of Washington control and enabled its evolution into an independent public radio station with global reach:

- **Time slot:** The Midday Show airs during the midday hours, the second-most prominent slot after the morning drive. The midday position is where the station's musical identity is most fully expressed -- the morning show builds energy, the midday show sustains the station's core musical character, the evening shows explore niche genres.
- **Format:** Music-forward with informed, warm commentary. Waters introduces artists with context -- who they are, where they're from, how they connect to the broader musical landscape. The commentary is educational without being didactic, personal without being confessional.
- **Musical focus:** The Midday Show anchors KEXP's core musical territory: indie rock, alternative, folk, electronic, and the Seattle/PNW scene. Waters is deeply knowledgeable about the local music ecosystem -- the venues, the labels, the bands, the history. The show is the station's **local music compass**.
- **Evolution:** Waters' show has evolved alongside KEXP itself. In the KCMU era, the audience was largely UW students and local music enthusiasts. In the KEXP era, the audience expanded to include global listeners via streaming. The show's character -- warm, knowledgeable, locally rooted -- has remained constant even as the audience grew.

### The Midday Show as Musical Center of Gravity

The Midday Show occupies a specific role in KEXP's daily programming:

- **The core repertoire:** If the Morning Show (Richards, 131) is where listeners discover new music through Richards' personal lens, the Midday Show is where KEXP's core repertoire lives. Waters plays the music that defines the station's identity -- the indie and alternative artists that are KEXP's foundation, alongside new discoveries and deep cuts.
- **Bridge between eras:** Waters connects KEXP's KCMU roots to its present identity. Her knowledge spans the pre-grunge era through grunge, post-grunge, the 2000s indie renaissance, and into the streaming era. She is a living link between the station's college radio past and its global present.
- **Artist relationships:** Over two decades, Waters has developed relationships with artists, labels, and venues throughout the Pacific Northwest. These relationships inform her on-air presentation -- she can provide context that a newer host cannot. The depth of knowledge is the Midday Show's distinguishing quality.

## THE KCMU-TO-KEXP TRANSITION -- A WITNESS AND SURVIVOR

### Living Through the Transformation

Waters is one of the few on-air personalities who lived through the entire KCMU-to-KEXP transformation:

- **KCMU era (pre-2001):** KCMU was a University of Washington-licensed college radio station. Freeform programming. DJ-driven. Low power. Local audience. The station that launched Sub Pop (Pavitt and Poneman at degrees 127-128), introduced hip-hop to Seattle radio (Nasty Nes at degree 126), and maintained the independent alternative to commercial radio. Waters was part of this community.
- **The transition (2001):** The Paul Allen Foundation's $4.2 million gift transformed the station. KCMU became KEXP. The call letters changed. The institutional structure changed (from UW-controlled to independent non-profit). The broadcast power increased. The streaming infrastructure was built. Through this transformation, Waters remained -- adapting her show to the new reality while maintaining the curatorial values of the KCMU era.
- **KEXP era (2001-present):** KEXP became globally recognized as a model for independent, listener-supported radio. The new KEXP building at Seattle Center (opened 2016) provided state-of-the-art facilities including a public-facing live performance space. Waters' show, and the station's programming broadly, maintained the KCMU curatorial ethos within a more professional, better-funded infrastructure.
- **The continuity value:** Waters' presence across the KCMU/KEXP divide provides institutional memory. She remembers what KCMU was, what the freeform tradition meant, and why the curatorial ethos matters. This continuity is valuable: it prevents the station from losing its roots as it grows.

(Sources: KEXP; The Seattle Times; Crosscut)

## ECLECTIC PRESENTATION -- THE WARM HAND

### Curatorial Style

Waters' on-air presentation is characterized by warmth and eclecticism:

- **Musical breadth:** A typical Midday Show hour might include indie rock, folk, electronic, international music, a classic deep cut, and a new PNW artist. The breadth is KEXP's brand -- no genre boundaries, curated by taste rather than format. Waters executes this breadth with a warmth that makes eclecticism feel natural rather than forced.
- **Local knowledge:** Waters knows the Seattle/PNW music scene at a depth that few can match. She knows the venues (the Crocodile, Neumos, the Tractor Tavern), the labels (Sub Pop, Hardly Art, Kill Rock Stars, K Records), and the artists. This knowledge informs her curation -- she can place a new Seattle band in the context of the city's musical history.
- **Introduction style:** When Waters introduces a song, she provides just enough context: the artist's name, where they're from, perhaps a note about the album or the artist's trajectory. Not too much (she's not a music journalist on air) and not too little (she's not an automated playlist). The balance is characteristic.
- **The warm hand vs. the cold algorithm:** Like Richards (131), Waters represents the human curator in the age of algorithmic recommendation. But where Richards makes the argument through personal vulnerability, Waters makes it through **knowledge and warmth**. The Midday Show says: a human who knows music and knows you is better than an algorithm that knows your data.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of the Midday Show

Waters' acoustic contribution is the **soundscape of a KEXP midday**:

- **Voice character:** Warm, clear, measured. A naturally pleasant voice with a quality of genuine engagement. Not a "radio voice" in the commercial sense -- no artificial depth or manufactured excitement. The voice of someone who genuinely loves what she's playing and wants to share it.
- **Pacing:** Steady and unhurried. Waters does not rush between songs or fill silence with chatter. The transitions are smooth -- a brief introduction, the song, a brief back-announce. The pacing creates a flow that listeners can work or relax to without jarring interruptions.
- **Musical selection rhythm:** The Midday Show has a characteristic steady energy -- less arc than the Morning Show (which builds from quiet to energetic) and more sustained mood. The midday position allows for a more even-keeled musical flow.
- **The KEXP sound:** Waters' show is central to the "KEXP sound" -- a sonic identity that prioritizes quality, discovery, and eclecticism. The Midday Show is where a listener tuning in for the first time will hear what KEXP sounds like. Waters defines that sound as much as any individual host.

### Midday as Musical Anchor

- **The steady center:** If the Morning Show is the emotional heart (Richards' vulnerability) and Wo' Pop is the geographic frontier (Mazzone's global reach), the Midday Show is the **musical center** -- the station's core identity expressed in its purest form.
- **Discovery and familiarity:** Waters balances new discoveries with familiar favorites. The midday audience expects both -- something new to learn and something known to enjoy. This balance is the curatorial art.
