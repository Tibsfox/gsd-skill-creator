The Midday Show — KEXP’s Musical Core

Cheryl Waters has hosted The Midday Show on KEXP 90.3 FM for over two decades, making her one of the station’s longest-serving on-air voices. She joined KCMU in 1996 — before the Paul Allen Foundation gift, before the rebranding, before the global streaming audience — and stayed through the entire transformation into KEXP.

The Midday Show is KEXP’s musical center of gravity. Where the Morning Show (Richards, 131) builds energy through personal connection and Wo’ Pop (Mazzone, 132) expands the map into global music, the Midday Show anchors the station’s core repertoire: indie, alternative, folk, electronic, and the Seattle/PNW scene. Waters plays the music that defines what KEXP sounds like.

KCMU to KEXP — Living Through the Transformation

Waters is one of the few on-air personalities who lived through the entire KCMU-to-KEXP transformation — from college radio to globally recognized independent station. Her presence provides institutional memory that prevents the station from losing its roots as it grows.

The 2001 transition from KCMU to KEXP was catalyzed by a $4.2 million gift from the Paul Allen Foundation, freeing the station from University of Washington control. The call letters changed. The infrastructure transformed. The audience went global. Through it all, Waters remained — adapting her show while maintaining the curatorial values of the KCMU freeform era. She remembers what KCMU was and why the curatorial ethos matters.

Eclectic Presentation — The Warm Hand

A typical Midday Show hour: indie rock, folk, electronic, an international track, a classic deep cut, a new PNW artist. The breadth is KEXP’s brand — no genre boundaries, curated by taste. Waters executes this eclecticism with a warmth that makes it feel natural. She knows the venues (the Crocodile, Neumos, the Tractor Tavern), the labels (Sub Pop, Hardly Art, Kill Rock Stars, K Records), and the artists. Two decades of relationships inform every introduction.

The warm hand vs. the cold algorithm: where Richards (131) makes the human-curator argument through personal vulnerability, Waters makes it through knowledge and warmth. A human who knows music and knows you is better than an algorithm that knows your data.
