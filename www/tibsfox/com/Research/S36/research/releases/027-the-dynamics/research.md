## Biography & The Teen-R&B Arc

The Dynamics were **Seattle's premier teen-R&B band of the 1960s** -- a revolving-membership ensemble whose decade-long arc (1959-1968) produced some of the most critically acclaimed rhythm and blues recordings in Pacific Northwest history, launched at least three members into nationally significant careers, and served as a living bridge between Seattle's Black R&B tradition centered in the Central District and the white teen-dance circuit promoted by KJR radio's Pat O'Day.

### Formation and Early Years (1959-1960)

The band formed circa **1959** around founding vocalist **Tom Larson**, with **Terry Afdem** on keyboards, **Jeff Afdem** on saxophone, **Pete Borg** on bass, **Wally Griffin** on guitar, **Tracy Townsend** on drums, and additional vocalists **Jerry Strong** and **Bob Johnston**. Early recordings occurred in the **Afdem family living room** -- a detail that places the band's genesis in the domestic, garage-band tradition that defined Pacific Northwest rock in the late 1950s.

Their debut single, **"Baby" / "Aces Up"** (Penguin Records #1006, July 1959), was described by the Northwest Music Archives as "a joyfully raw single pairing up two 'Kansas City'-type originals." The single attracted enough regional attention that it was picked up for national distribution by **Guaranteed Records** in New York (#201, September 1959) -- the first of several national-distribution events in the band's history.

Tom Larson approached **Tom and Ellen Ogilvy**, the operators of Seafair Records, with the band's second single, **"Onion Salad" / "Lonesome Llama"** (Seafair #100, June 25, 1960). This relationship with the Ogilvy family would prove transformative: their son, **Jimmy Hanna**, would soon join the band as lead vocalist and become its defining voice.

(Source: NW Music Archives; PNW Bands; Peter Blecha, *The Rocket*, January 1984)

### The Jimmy Hanna Era and the Rise (1961-1964)

In **1961**, the lineup underwent a critical reformation. **Jimmy Hanna** joined on vocals, bringing what reviewers described as "rich, smooth -- almost Bobby Bland-like" vocal tone. Crucially, two musicians from outside Seattle also joined: **Larry Coryell** on guitar, arriving from **Yakima's Checkers**, and **Marcus Doubleday** on trumpet. **Ron Woods** replaced Tracy Townsend on drums. This lineup -- Hanna, Coryell, Doubleday, the Afdem brothers, Borg, and Woods -- would record the band's most celebrated material.

The significance of these three incoming members cannot be overstated:

**Larry Coryell** (April 2, 1943 -- February 19, 2017) had moved to Seattle to attend the **University of Washington**. He would later be called the **"godfather of jazz-rock fusion"** -- the guitarist who, alongside John McLaughlin and Al Di Meola, invented an entire genre. His time with The Dynamics (1961-1964) represents the **pre-fusion incubation period**: a jazz-oriented guitarist learning R&B phrasing, horn-section timing, and dance-floor energy in Seattle's teen-R&B circuit. Coryell became best friends with **Rich Dangel** of the Fabulous Wailers, and they shared a houseboat in Seattle before Coryell moved to New York in **September 1965** to attend Mannes School of Music, where he replaced Gabor Szabo in **Chico Hamilton's quintet**.

**Marcus Doubleday** (d. May 1, 2004) would go on to play trumpet in **The Electric Flag** -- the groundbreaking blues-rock band formed by **Mike Bloomfield** (Paul Butterfield Blues Band, Bob Dylan's *Highway 61 Revisited*). Doubleday was recruited to The Electric Flag on Larry Coryell's recommendation. He also appeared on Bloomfield's solo album *It's Not Killing Me* (1969).

**Ron Woods** would relocate to Los Angeles, where he performed with the **Buddy Miles Express**, **Pacific Gas & Electric**, and -- most remarkably -- did session work with **Jimi Hendrix** at Electric Lady Studios in New York, circa 1969. Woods had been Hendrix's schoolmate in Seattle.

The band's recording output in this period was prolific. The **1962 cover of Dave Lewis' "J.A.J."** (Seafair-Bolo #45-730) was so strong it briefly received national distribution. This is structurally significant: **Dave Lewis** was the **king of Seattle R&B**, an African American organist and bandleader based in the **Central District** who had been the house band leader at **Birdland**, the legendary R&B club at 21st and East Madison. Lewis had famously sneaked into Birdland as a teenager too young for the liquor-licensed venue to catch acts like Solomon Burke and James Brown. By covering Lewis' composition, The Dynamics were performing a **genre bridge act** -- a predominantly white teen band channeling the Central District's Black R&B tradition into the suburban teen-dance circuit.

The singles continued: **"Spongy" / "Wild Girl"** (#B-735, November 1962), **"Tennessee Boy" / "Tough Talk"** (#B-740, December 1963). By 1964, the band was ready for its definitive recording.

(Source: NW Music Archives; PNW Bands; Peter Blecha; HistoryLink.org; Indie Musicology)

### The Album: *The Dynamics with Jimmy Hanna* (1964)

In **September 1964**, Seafair-Bolo released **The Dynamics with Jimmy Hanna** (BLP-8001), a **live remote recording** captured at **Parker's** (17001 Seattle-Everett Highway, now Aurora Avenue North). The album was engineered by **Ray Van Patten** and **C.A. (Chet) Noland**.

The personnel on the album represents the band at its peak:

| Member | Instrument | Later Career |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Jimmy Hanna | Vocals | Own 11-piece blues band; music instructor in San Antonio, TX |
| Larry Coryell | Guitar | "Godfather of jazz-rock fusion"; 40+ albums; died 2017 |
| Harry Wilson | Guitar | Replaced Coryell mid-sessions; joined from The Casuals |
| Jeff Afdem | Tenor saxophone | Founding member; d. 2022 |
| Terry Afdem | Keyboards | Founding member; taught and mentored musicians throughout career |
| Marcus Doubleday | Trumpet | Electric Flag; Mike Bloomfield solo sessions; d. 2004 |
| Gary Snyder | Bass | Replaced Pete Borg |
| Ron Woods | Drums | Buddy Miles Express; Pacific Gas & Electric; Jimi Hendrix sessions |

The track listing included covers of **Dave Lewis** ("J.A.J."), **Cannonball Adderley**, **Eddie Holland** ("Leavin' Here"), **Lonnie Mack**, **Earl King**, and originals credited to various members. The album was colloquially known as **"The Dynamics At Parker's"** and is considered the definitive document of 1960s Northwest teen-R&B.

The singles **"Busybody"** and **"Leavin' Here"** were issued from the album sessions and briefly received **national distribution through Atlantic Records** -- the New York label that had released Ray Charles, Aretha Franklin, and the Drifters. Jimmy Hanna's cover of Eddie Holland's "Leavin' Here" (written by the legendary **Holland-Dozier-Holland** songwriting team at Motown) was described as ranking "just behind 'Louie Louie' as a Northwest classic." That a Seattle teen-R&B band's version of a Motown song was picked up by Atlantic Records speaks to the quality of the performance and the national distribution infrastructure that the Seafair-Bolo label had built.

(Source: NW Music Archives; Archive.org; Record Collector Magazine; Amazon)

### Decline, Transformation, and Legacy (1965-1968)

By **1964-1965**, the lineup was shifting. Larry Coryell departed for New York -- "the rest is jazz history," as Peter Blecha wrote in *The Rocket*. **Harry Wilson** (from The Casuals) had already been brought in as guitar replacement. Additional personnel changes brought in **J.C. Reick** on keyboards and various other members.

Jimmy Hanna left circa **1966** to front his own **11-piece blues big-band**, releasing several singles through 1967 before eventually relocating to **San Antonio, Texas**, where he established a recording studio and became a music instructor.

The remaining members signed with **Jerden Records** -- Seattle's most commercially significant independent label of the 1960s, which had secured a national distribution deal with ABC-Paramount. The final single under The Dynamics name was **"I'll Be Standing There" / "All She Said"** (Jerden #800, April 1966).

The band then **revamped as The Springfield Rifle**, pivoting from teen-R&B toward pop-rock. The Springfield Rifle became a successful performing and recording outfit, scoring airplay with **"Will You Love Me Tomorrow"** (1967) and **"That's All I Really Need"** (1968). Their eleven singles (1966-1971) were released on ABC, Jerden, Tower, and Burdette labels. The Springfield Rifle later evolved into **Springfield Flute**.

In **1983**, the archival album **Memory Bank of Early N.W. Sounds** (Seafair-Bolo BSLP 8003) was released, compiling live tracks from 1962-1965 mixed by **Kearney Barton** -- Seattle's legendary recording engineer who had defined the "Northwest sound" at his Audio Recording studios. Barton had recorded The Sonics, The Kingsmen, and scores of other Northwest garage and R&B combos. His involvement in the Dynamics' archival release connected the band to the broader Pacific Northwest recording infrastructure.

A CD reissue, **The Dynamics with Jimmy Hanna 1961-1965** (Seafair-Bolo BSCD 8009-2), followed in **2009**.

On **July 28, 2000**, The Dynamics reunited for the **Grand Opening of the Experience Music Project** (now Museum of Pop Culture / MoPOP) at Seattle Center. The reunion lineup included founding members alongside Larry Coryell, Jimmy Hanna, Harry Wilson, Pete Borg, Ron Woods, and J.C. Reick -- a gathering that acknowledged the band's foundational role in Seattle's musical history.

(Source: NW Music Archives; PNW Bands; HistoryLink.org; Tape Op)

## The Calla Records Question

The CSV assigns "Calla" as the label for The Dynamics at degree 27. **Calla Records** was a New York City-based soul label founded by **Nate McCalla** in 1965, associated with **Morris Levy's Roulette Records** empire. Calla released records by **J.J. Jackson** ("But It's Alright," 1966), **The Sandpebbles**, **Jean Wells**, **The Emotions**, **Little Jerry Williams** (later Swamp Dogg), and other soul and R&B artists. The label was initially distributed by Cameo-Parkway, then absorbed into the Roulette/Warner Bros. orbit.

The Dynamics' documented discography shows releases on Penguin, Guaranteed, Seafair, Seafair-Bolo, and Jerden. A Calla Records release is not confirmed in the major archival sources (NW Music Archives, PNW Bands, Discogs). However, during the 1964-1966 period when the band's singles were receiving national distribution through Atlantic and other major labels, it is plausible that a Calla release existed as part of the broader distribution network connecting Seattle's independent labels to New York's soul infrastructure. The CSV entry may reflect a release or licensing arrangement not captured in the primary Northwest-focused archival sources.

This ambiguity is itself a finding: the documentation gap between Seattle's local archives and New York's national soul-label ecosystem reveals how regional music history can lose track of national distribution events, particularly for bands that existed at the intersection of white teen-dance culture and Black R&B tradition.

(Source: Discogs; Wikipedia; BSN Pubs; Rate Your Music)

**The liquid gurgle** is the twenty-eighth acoustic element. It names the sound of something fermenting, bubbling, not yet resolved into a clear tone -- the acoustic precursor to crystallization. In music, a gurgle is a **cluster of overlapping attacks** where individual notes are not cleanly separated: the horn section bleeding into the vocal line, the guitar overdriving into the saxophone, the rhythm section pushing tempos that the melody cannot quite keep up with. It is the sound of **too many talented people in one room**, all playing at full capacity, before anyone has learned to leave space.

The Dynamics' live recordings at Parker's capture this quality precisely. The album is not polished -- it is a live remote recording of a teen-R&B band at full tilt, with Jimmy Hanna's vocals, Coryell's guitar, Doubleday's trumpet, and Jeff Afdem's saxophone all competing for sonic space. The "gurgle" is the sound of a band that has not yet separated into its component geniuses. Coryell will become the godfather of fusion. Doubleday will play in The Electric Flag. Woods will play with Hendrix. But in 1964, they are all in one room, all gurgling together, and the sound is **the unsorted potential before individual genius emerges**.

The liquid gurgle follows the caroling because the outward movement has now reached a point of **acoustic saturation**: the song has been carried outward (caroling, degree 26) and has arrived at a place where multiple streams of musical energy converge and overflow. The gurgle is what happens when the caroling reaches so many ears that everyone tries to sing at once. It is pre-harmonic -- the state before harmony is established, when the raw material of music is still finding its shape. The Dynamics were literally that: a band where the raw material of multiple future genres (jazz-rock fusion, blues-rock, funk, R&B) was gurgling together before any of those genres had names.

## The Central District Connection: The Genre Bridge

The CSV places The Dynamics in the **Central District**. The primary archival sources describe the band's homebase at 7757 30th Avenue SW (West Seattle/White Center corridor) and their performance circuit as the **suburban teen-dance venues** promoted by KJR's Pat O'Day -- the Spanish Castle, Parker's on Aurora Avenue, school gymnasiums. The band was predominantly white (the Afdem brothers, Coryell, Wilson, Snyder, Borg) with at least one Black member documented across various lineups.

However, the Central District connection is real and structural, even if the band did not live there:

1. **Dave Lewis cover**: The Dynamics' 1962 cover of **"J.A.J."** by Dave Lewis -- the Central District's premier R&B organist, the house band leader at Birdland (21st and East Madison) -- is a direct musical lineage from the CD's Black R&B tradition into the teen-dance circuit. Lewis was the **king of Seattle R&B**, the man who had sneaked into Birdland as a teenager to catch James Brown and Solomon Burke, who performed "Louie Louie" before the Kingsmen made it famous. The Dynamics' cover of his composition is an act of genre translation: Central District R&B rendered for the suburban teen-dance audience.

2. **The club ecosystem**: Seattle's 1960s music scene was organized around two parallel circuits -- the **Central District's Black club scene** (Birdland, the Black and Tan Club, the Golden Crown, the Black Elks Club) and the **suburban teen-dance circuit** (Spanish Castle, Parker's, school dances). These circuits were racially distinct but musically connected. The same songs played in both worlds. The Dynamics existed at the intersection, covering Dave Lewis compositions and performing the same R&B material that Black bands played in the Central District clubs but for a teen audience at the Spanish Castle.

3. **The Hendrix connection**: Ron Woods, the Dynamics' drummer, later did session work with **Jimi Hendrix** -- another Seattle musician shaped by the Central District's music ecosystem. Hendrix's early bands (the Velvetones, the Rocking Kings) gigged at Birdland in 1959-1960, the same years The Dynamics were forming. Woods and Hendrix had been schoolmates. The Dynamics and Hendrix orbit were part of the same gravitational field, even if they played on different stages.

4. **The Quincy Jones lineage**: The engine's degree 0 artist, **Quincy Jones**, emerged from the Central District's **Garfield High School** and the **Jackson Street jazz scene** of the 1940s-1950s. The R&B tradition that The Dynamics channeled through their teen-dance covers was the downstream evolution of the same Central District musical ecosystem that produced Jones. The distance from Quincy Jones (degree 0) to The Dynamics (degree 27) is twenty-seven degrees of rotation but zero degrees of musical separation -- both are products of the Central District's musical gravity, one directly, one through cover versions and shared personnel.

The CSV's "Central District" designation for The Dynamics is therefore not a geographic claim about where the band members lived but a **musical-genealogical claim** about where their sound came from. The Dynamics' R&B was Central District R&B, channeled through a different venue system and a different audience but carrying the same harmonic and rhythmic DNA.

(Source: HistoryLink.org; NW Music Archives; PNW Bands; Cascade PBS; UW Libraries)

## The Pat O'Day Teen-Dance Circuit

### Seattle's Parallel Music Infrastructure

The Dynamics' performing career was built on the **teen-dance circuit** created by **Pat O'Day** (1934-2020), KJR radio's legendary DJ and promoter. O'Day transformed Seattle's live music infrastructure in the late 1950s by renting the **Spanish Castle Ballroom** (on Highway 99 between Seattle and Tacoma) and organizing all-ages dances featuring local bands. At its peak, KJR commanded a **37% audience share** -- an unprecedented dominance for a single radio station.

The teen-dance circuit was a parallel infrastructure to the Central District's nightclub scene. The Central District clubs (Birdland, the Black and Tan, the Golden Crown) were liquor-licensed, adult venues serving a predominantly Black audience. The teen-dance circuit (Spanish Castle, Parker's, school gymnasiums) was all-ages, alcohol-free, and served a predominantly white suburban audience. The two circuits shared **musical repertoire** -- the same R&B songs played in both -- but separated **audiences by race and age**.

The Dynamics were the teen-dance circuit's **premier R&B band**, which means they were the primary vehicle through which Central District R&B reached suburban white teenagers. This is the genre bridge function that defines the Conduit pattern: the band did not create the music, and the audience did not know its origins, but the musical energy traveled from one world to the other through The Dynamics' performances.

The band shared stages with **The Beach Boys, Roy Orbison, Bobby Freeman, Jan & Dean, The Astronauts**, and **Terry Black** -- national acts who played the same teen-dance circuit. They also shared the circuit with other significant Northwest bands: **The Wailers, The Viceroys, The Sonics, The Frantics, The Casuals, The Checkers** (Coryell's pre-Dynamics band from Yakima). This was the ecosystem that produced "Louie Louie," The Sonics' "The Witch," and the raw, reverberant "Northwest sound" that Kearney Barton engineered at Audio Recording studios.

(Source: HistoryLink.org; Seattle Times; PNW Bands; Seattle Met)

The regression also connects to the genre evolution: Soul/R&B at degree 27 is the **historical compound** that preceded the separated categories of "Soul" (degree 26) and "R&B" (earlier). ## The EMP Reunion: Legacy Recognition

On **July 28, 2000**, The Dynamics reunited at the **Grand Opening of the Experience Music Project** (now Museum of Pop Culture) at Seattle Center. This event -- organized by Microsoft co-founder Paul Allen -- was a formal recognition of Seattle's musical heritage. The "Northwest Legends" concert at the Mural Amphitheater featured The Dynamics alongside **The Wailers** and **The Viceroys**.

The reunion lineup brought together members from across the band's history: Larry Coryell (by then a 57-year-old jazz legend with dozens of albums), Jimmy Hanna, Harry Wilson, Pete Borg, Ron Woods, Jeff Afdem, Terry Afdem, J.C. Reick, and Keith Baggerly (trumpet, replacing the late Marcus Doubleday). The event validated the band's place in Seattle's musical canon -- not as a major national act but as a **foundational ensemble** that incubated talent which went on to define multiple genres.

The EMP Grand Opening reunion maps to the engine's broader theme of **institutional recognition arriving decades after the music**: Quincy Jones (degree 0) received his Garfield High School education in the 1940s but his global recognition came over the following sixty years; The Dynamics received their institutional recognition at EMP thirty-six years after their peak album. Seattle's music history is characterized by this temporal gap between creation and recognition.
