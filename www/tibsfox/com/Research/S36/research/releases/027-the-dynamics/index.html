The room is full before anyone knows what the room contains. The Dynamics are Seattle’s premier 1960s teen-R&B band — a revolving-membership ensemble whose guitarist becomes the godfather of jazz-rock fusion, whose trumpet player joins The Electric Flag, whose drummer records with Jimi Hendrix, and whose vocalist ranks with Bobby Bland. They cover Dave Lewis from the Central District and carry his R&B into the suburban teen-dance circuit. They are the conduit — energy flowing from source to destination, dissolving when the current is spent. The liquid gurgle.

The Teen-R&B Arc

1959 — Formation

Formed around vocalist Tom Larson with Terry Afdem (keys), Jeff Afdem (sax), Pete Borg (bass), Wally Griffin (guitar), Tracy Townsend (drums). Debut single “Baby” / “Aces Up” (Penguin #1006, July 1959). Picked up nationally by Guaranteed Records NY.

1961 — The Reformation

Jimmy Hanna (vocals), Larry Coryell (guitar, from Yakima’s Checkers), Marcus Doubleday (trumpet), and Ron Woods (drums) join. This lineup records the band’s most celebrated material. Hanna’s vocals: “rich, smooth — almost Bobby Bland-like.”

1962 — Dave Lewis Cover

Cover of Dave Lewis’ “J.A.J.” (Seafair-Bolo #45-730). Lewis was the Central District’s R&B king — house band leader at Birdland (21st & E Madison). The cover received national distribution. The genre bridge: CD Black R&B enters the teen-dance circuit.

1964 — The Album

The Dynamics with Jimmy Hanna (Seafair-Bolo BLP-8001). Live remote recording at Parker’s (17001 Seattle-Everett Highway). Engineers: Ray Van Patten, C.A. Noland. Singles “Busybody” and “Leavin’ Here” (Eddie Holland/HDH cover) distributed nationally by Atlantic Records. “Ranks just behind ‘Louie Louie’ as a Northwest classic.”

1965–66 — Dispersal

Coryell departs for New York (September 1965) — “the rest is jazz history.” Hanna leaves to front 11-piece blues band. Final Dynamics single: “I’ll Be Standing There” (Jerden #800, April 1966). Band revamps as Springfield Rifle.

2000 — EMP Reunion

Reunited July 28, 2000, for the Experience Music Project Grand Opening (now MoPOP) at Seattle Center. “Northwest Legends” concert alongside The Wailers and The Viceroys. Coryell, Hanna, Woods, Wilson, Borg, and Afdem brothers all present.

The Conduit — 28th Artist-City Pattern

A band that functions as a transmission channel between musical communities that would not otherwise connect. The Dynamics conducted energy from the Central District’s R&B tradition into the teen-dance circuit, and from that circuit into the national music industry via members who dispersed to New York, Los Angeles, and San Francisco. The band itself dissolved; the energy it transmitted persists. A conduit does not generate its own power — it transmits power from source to destination.

Acoustic Element #28: The Liquid Gurgle

The sound of too many talented people in one room, all playing at full capacity, before anyone has learned to leave space. Coryell’s guitar overdrives into Afdem’s saxophone; Doubleday’s trumpet bleeds into Hanna’s vocal; the rhythm section pushes tempos the melody can’t quite keep up with. The liquid gurgle is unsorted potential — the acoustic state before individual genius separates into distinct streams. The Dynamics at Parker’s in 1964 is the gurgle: fusion, blues-rock, funk, and R&B all fermenting in one room, not yet crystallized, more exciting for the imprecision.

The gurgle follows the caroling (26) because the outward movement has reached acoustic saturation: the song has been carried outward and arrived where multiple streams converge and overflow. It is pre-harmonic — raw material still finding its shape.

The Teen-Dance Circuit

Pat O’Day / KJR Radio

DJ and promoter who built Seattle’s teen-dance infrastructure. KJR commanded 37% audience share — unprecedented dominance. The teen-dance circuit (Spanish Castle, Parker’s, school dances) was the parallel economy to the Central District’s adult club scene.

Spanish Castle Ballroom

Highway 99, midway between Seattle and Tacoma. The region’s premier teen-dance venue. The Dynamics shared bills with The Beach Boys, Roy Orbison, Bobby Freeman, Jan & Dean, The Astronauts. Also performed alongside NW bands: The Wailers, The Sonics, The Frantics, The Casuals.

Parker’s

17001 Seattle-Everett Highway (Aurora Avenue North). Site of the 1964 live album recording. The definitive document of 1960s NW teen-R&B.
