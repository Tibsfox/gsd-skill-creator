Born November 27, 1942, in Seattle’s Central District. Third-generation Seattleite. Raised in poverty. Played in the Velvetones with Luther Rabb. Learned the blues from his father’s records — Muddy Waters, B.B. King, Albert King. Expelled from Garfield High School. Left Seattle for the Army, then the chitlin’ circuit — backing Little Richard, the Isley Brothers, Curtis Knight. Discovered in Greenwich Village, 1966. Three studio albums on Reprise. “Red House” as pure blues. “Voodoo Chile” as electrified Delta. “Machine Gun” as blues protest. Marshall stacks at maximum volume. The most physically intense performer in rock history. Died September 18, 1970, London, age 27. The biggest Export from Seattle. The blues foundation beneath the most famous tree in rock music. E=6 — the ceiling rises.

The Blues Lens — Why This Document Exists

The CSV specifies “Jimi Hendrix (blues context)” — a deliberate editorial instruction. The world knows Hendrix as the psychedelic rock innovator. This document tells a different story: Hendrix as a blues musician. The blues was the foundation beneath everything — the first music he loved, the tradition he learned on the chitlin’ circuit, the form he returned to with Band of Gypsys. Every innovation (feedback, distortion, wah-wah) was an extension of the blues, not a departure from it.

“You can call Jimi Hendrix whatever you like, but he was a blues master — it’s really nothing but blues.” — Billy Cox

Career Timeline

1942 — Born

November 27, King County Hospital, Seattle. Mother Lucille Jeter (17). Father Al Hendrix (23), stationed at Fort Rucker, Alabama. Third-generation Seattleite.

1942–1958 — Central District Childhood

Poverty, instability, constant moves across CD addresses. Parents divorce 1950. Mother Lucille dies February 2, 1958, age 33. Absorbs blues from father’s records: Muddy Waters, B.B. King, Howlin’ Wolf, Albert King.

1958 — First Guitar

Acquires first acoustic guitar for $5, mid-1958, age 15. Left-handed, plays right-handed guitars flipped and restrung. No formal instruction.

1958–1959 — The Velvetones

First band. Luther Rabb (degree 32) on saxophone. Pernell Alexander, Walter Jones, Robert Green. Teen dances at Yesler Terrace and CD recreation halls.

1959–1961 — Garfield & The Rocking Kings

Enters Garfield High School fall 1959. Expelled October 1960. Joins the Rocking Kings — Birdland club, Spanish Castle ballroom, Washington Hall. Guitar stolen at Birdland intermission.

1961 — U.S. Army

Enlists 101st Airborne Division, Fort Campbell, Kentucky. Meets Billy Cox — future Band of Gypsys bassist. Discharged 1962.

1962–1966 — Chitlin’ Circuit

Backs Little Richard (fired over stage antics), the Isley Brothers (“Testify,” 1964), Curtis Knight and the Squires (NYC), King Curtis. Blues apprenticeship on the road.

1966 — Greenwich Village & London

Leads Jimmy James and the Blue Flames in Greenwich Village. Discovered by Chas Chandler (Animals). Moves to London. Forms The Jimi Hendrix Experience with Noel Redding and Mitch Mitchell.

1967 — Are You Experienced / Axis

Two albums on Track/Reprise. “Red House” (pure blues, UK only). “Purple Haze,” “The Wind Cries Mary.” Sets guitar on fire at Monterey Pop Festival, June 18.

1968 — Electric Ladyland

Double album. “Voodoo Chile” (15-min blues jam). “Voodoo Child (Slight Return).” “All Along the Watchtower.” The magnum opus.

1969 — Woodstock

August 18. “The Star-Spangled Banner” through a Stratocaster and Marshall stacks. The most famous guitar performance in American history.

1969–1970 — Band of Gypsys

Hendrix, Billy Cox, Buddy Miles. Four concerts at Fillmore East, New Year’s Eve 1969 / New Year’s Day 1970. “Machine Gun” — blues as anti-war protest. Explicit blues-funk direction.

1970-09-18 — Death

September 18, London. Samarkand Hotel, Lansdowne Crescent. Age 27. Asphyxia. Open verdict. Buried at Greenwood Memorial Park, Renton, WA.

Theory Nodes

The Supernova

Advanced

37th artist-city pattern. Intense formation, total departure, compressed career at maximum intensity, premature death. City claims artist posthumously — MoPOP, park, statue, museum exhibits. Maximum impact, minimum duration.

Export (0) = long career elsewhere. Incubator (30) = brief transit. Deep Root (35) = stays regional. Supernova (36) = burns bright, collapses, city keeps the afterglow.
Genealogy: Completes quartet of PNW-to-global modes: Export, Incubator, Deep Root, Supernova.

The Controlled Scream

Advanced

37th acoustic element. Overdriven Stratocaster through Marshall stacks at maximum volume. Feedback = sustained cry. Distortion = rough voice. Wah-wah = vowel sounds. The blues expression of emotion amplified to electromagnetic force.

Stinging clean (35) = precision, restraint. Controlled scream (36) = power, intensity. Same guitar, opposite philosophy. Consecutive Stratocaster pairing.
Genealogy: Guitar blue note (1) → stinging clean (35) → controlled scream (36). Full Stratocaster timbral spectrum mapped.

The Screaming Bend

Intermediate

36th blue note deployment. The bend as full-body physical experience — pitch transition saturated with overtones, sustained by feedback, delivered at bone-resonating volume. Opposite of the articulate sting’s clean transparency.

“Machine Gun,” “Voodoo Chile,” “Red House” bends. Ghost (33) → unison (34) → articulate sting (35) → screaming bend (36).
Genealogy: Both extremes of guitar-bend spectrum mapped: max transparency (Cray) and max saturation (Hendrix).

Transformative Tidal

Advanced

First E=6 mode. Energy that redefines the scale itself. Not merely loud or energetic — transformed what an electric guitar, amplifier, and stage show could physically do. Distribution: 2:13:6:11:4:1.

Personal (32) → scene (33) → collective (34) → breakthrough (35) → transformative (36). Five modes across five consecutive maximum-or-above degrees.
Genealogy: E=6 may be one-of-one. The energy scale expanded to accommodate a singular artist.

Reprise Records — New Label

Founded by Frank Sinatra (1960). Acquired by Warner Bros. (1963). US home of all three Jimi Hendrix Experience studio albums. First appearance in the engine. Reprise omitted “Red House” from the US Are You Experienced — a slow blues didn’t fit the rock marketing strategy. The blues was the part of Hendrix that his American label thought wouldn’t sell.
