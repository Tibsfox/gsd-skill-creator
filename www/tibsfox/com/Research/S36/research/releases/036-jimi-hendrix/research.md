## The Blues Lens: Why This Document Exists

The CSV that drives the Seattle 360 engine specifies "Jimi Hendrix (blues context)" -- not simply "Jimi Hendrix." This is a deliberate editorial instruction. The world knows Hendrix as the psychedelic rock innovator, the man who set his guitar on fire at Monterey, the architect of "Purple Haze" and "The Star-Spangled Banner" at Woodstock. Those stories have been told ten thousand times. This document tells a different story: Hendrix as a blues musician. The blues was the foundation beneath everything -- the first music he loved, the tradition he learned on the chitlin' circuit, the form he returned to in his final recordings with Band of Gypsys. Every innovation Hendrix made -- feedback, distortion, wah-wah, the Stratocaster as electronic sound source -- was an extension of the blues, not a departure from it. This research documents the root system beneath the most famous tree in rock music.

As Billy Cox, Hendrix's longtime bassist and closest musical collaborator, said: "You can call Jimi Hendrix whatever you like, but he was a blues master. It's really nothing but blues."

(Sources: Living Blues #275; Cincinnati Blues Society -- "Blues by Jimi Hendrix"; Guitar World -- "5 times Jimi Hendrix proved he was a master of the blues")

## Biography: A Central District Boy

### Born in Seattle (November 27, 1942)

Johnny Allen Hendrix was born on November 27, 1942, at King County Hospital in Seattle, Washington. His mother, Lucille Jeter Hendrix, was seventeen years old -- still a Seattle schoolgirl. His father, James Allen Ross "Al" Hendrix, was a twenty-three-year-old private in the U.S. Army, stationed at Fort Rucker, Alabama, unable to obtain leave for his son's birth. Al would not meet his firstborn until 1945, when he returned from military service.

Al Hendrix had his own Seattle story. His parents, Zenora and Bertram Philander Ross Hendrix, were traveling vaudeville performers whose troupe disbanded in Seattle in 1911. Al grew up in Vancouver, B.C., and moved to Seattle in 1940. In 1941, he met Lucille Jeter at a dance. They married on March 31, 1942. In 1946, Al renamed his son James Marshall Hendrix, in honor of himself and his late brother Leon Marshall.

Lucille's family -- the Jeters -- were also rooted in Seattle. Her father was Preston Jeter. The Hendrix-Jeter lineage in Seattle stretches back to the early twentieth century. Jimi Hendrix was not a transplant. He was a third-generation Seattleite.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; HistoryLink.org -- "Hendrix, Al (1919-2002)"; Washington Secretary of State -- "Jimi Hendrix's Family History and Path to Seattle"; Find a Grave -- Lucille L. Jeter Hendrix (1925-1958); Find a Grave -- James Allen Ross "Al" Hendrix (1919-2002))

### A Broken Childhood in the Central District

Hendrix's childhood was defined by poverty, instability, and the Central District. Al and Lucille's marriage fractured almost immediately. Al was away at war during Jimi's first three years. When he returned, Lucille had been raising the child alone -- or rather, not alone: a rotating cast of relatives and neighbors in the Central District helped care for the boy during Lucille's absences. Grandmother Clarice Jeter, Aunt Delores Hall, and family friend Dorothy Harding each served as surrogate caregivers.

Al and Lucille reunited but could not sustain a household. Both struggled with alcohol. They fought when intoxicated. The family moved constantly -- cheap hotels, boardinghouses, apartments across Seattle's Central District and surrounding neighborhoods. Al worked odd jobs; Lucille's health deteriorated. They divorced in 1950. Al got custody of the children. Lucille's drinking continued to accelerate. On February 2, 1958, she died of a ruptured spleen caused by cirrhosis of the liver. She was thirty-three years old. Jimi was fifteen.

The Central District held all of this -- the poverty, the instability, the loss, and also the music. The Jackson Street corridor, which had incubated Ray Charles (degree 30) from 1947 to 1950 and which had produced Quincy Jones (degree 0), was still the living artery of Black Seattle's cultural life when young Jimmy Hendrix was growing up in the 1950s. The clubs that Charles had played -- the Black and Tan, the Rocking Chair, the Washington Social and Educational Club -- were still operating. The intergenerational mentorship system that connected older musicians to younger ones was still functioning. The neighborhood that had already exported two of the most important musicians in American history was forming a third.

(Sources: Seattle Times -- "Beyond A Broken Boyhood"; Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; HistoryLink.org -- "Hendrix, Jimi (1942-1970)"; IMDb -- Jimi Hendrix biography)

The neighborhood was a factory for musical genius, and it operated for decades on an area of a few square miles.

Hendrix entered Garfield High School in the fall of 1959. His time there was brief -- he was expelled in October 1960 for non-attendance. His only failing grades were in music. Despite the expulsion, he retained deep affection for the school. On subsequent visits to Seattle, he always asked to be driven past Garfield.

### The First Guitar

In mid-1958, at age fifteen, Hendrix acquired his first acoustic guitar for five dollars. Before that, he had been playing air guitar on a broom -- a detail that appears in nearly every Hendrix biography and that captures the intensity of desire behind the music. He had no formal instruction. He learned by listening to his father's records, by watching musicians at Jackson Street clubs, and by playing along with the radio. He was left-handed, and he learned to play right-handed guitars flipped upside down and restrung -- a practice that would later become one of his most distinctive visual and sonic signatures.

The first records he absorbed were the blues. His father Al had records by B.B. King, Louis Jordan, Muddy Waters, and other blues and R&B artists. The first guitarist Hendrix was aware of was Muddy Waters -- "I heard one of his old records when I was a little boy and it scared me to death," he later told Rolling Stone. He also listened to Howlin' Wolf, Lightnin' Hopkins, Albert King, Slim Harpo, Robert Johnson, Leadbelly, and Elmore James. These were his teachers before he had a guitar, and they remained his touchstones after he became the most famous guitarist on the planet.

(Sources: Rolling Stone -- "Jimi Hendrix On Early Influences, 'Axis' and More"; Living Blues #275; Guitar World -- "5 times Jimi Hendrix proved he was a master of the blues"; Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix)

## The Seattle Bands: Velvetones, Rocking Kings, and the CD Scene

### The Velvetones (c. 1958-1959)

Hendrix's first band was the Velvetones, a group of Central District teenagers who formed in the late 1950s. The lineup included Hendrix on guitar, Luther Rabb (degree 32) on tenor and baritone saxophone, Pernell Alexander on guitar, Walter Jones on drums, and Robert Green on piano. The Velvetones were too young to play the Jackson Street nightclubs, so they performed at teen dances in recreation halls at area housing projects, including Yesler Terrace -- the public housing project on the hillside above Jackson Street that served as the social infrastructure of the CD's youth music scene.

The Hendrix-Rabb connection is a direct cross-reference between degree 36 and degree 32. These were neighborhood kids, growing up on the same blocks, attending the same school, playing in the same band. The Central District did not produce isolated geniuses; it produced networks of musicians who grew up together, pushed each other, and scattered outward to reshape American music. Rabb would go on to found Ballin' Jack (signed to Columbia Records with Hendrix's help), join War, and tour with Santana. The Velvetones were the seed.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Luther Rabb; HistoryLink.org -- "Hendrix, Jimi (1942-1970)"; Jas Obrecht / Line6 Blog -- "How Jimi Learned to Play Guitar Pt. 2"; The Louie Report -- "RIP: Luther Rabb")

### The Rocking Kings (c. 1959-1961)

Hendrix was invited to join a more established outfit -- first called the Rocking Teens, then renamed the Rocking Kings. The band played professionally at venues including the Birdland club in Seattle and the Spanish Castle, a legendary roadhouse on the highway between Seattle and Tacoma (later immortalized in Hendrix's song "Spanish Castle Magic" on the 1967 album *Axis: Bold as Love*). A photograph taken at Washington Hall on February 20, 1960, shows the Rocking Kings in performance: drummer Lester Exkano, sax players Webb Lofton and Walter Harris, pianist Robert Green, and Jimi wearing a white shirt, dark tie, peg-style pants, and powder-blue sports jacket, holding his white Supro Ozark guitar.

The Rocking Kings played PTA dances, teenage sock hops, after-football-game events, the Garfield Funfest, and the club circuit. Hendrix's guitar was stolen during an intermission at the Birdland -- a loss that devastated the teenager. Between 1957 and 1961, he earned a local reputation for consistently showing up at gigs and asking if he could sit in. He was learning the trade the same way every blues musician before him had learned it: by watching, listening, and playing whenever anyone would let him.

(Sources: HistoryLink.org -- "Spanish Castle Ballroom"; Jas Obrecht / Line6 Blog; pnwbands.com -- "The Spanish Castle"; Wikipedia -- "Spanish Castle Magic")

### Leaving Seattle

In 1961, Hendrix enlisted in the United States Army and was assigned to the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky. The decision was partly pragmatic -- he had been caught twice riding in stolen cars, and an understanding with authorities allowed him to choose the military over potential legal consequences. He was discharged in 1962, reportedly after breaking his ankle during a parachute jump, though accounts of the discharge vary. He never returned to Seattle to live. The city that formed him became the city he left behind.

This is the engine's most dramatic instance of the Export pattern (degree 0, Quincy Jones). Jones left Seattle for a national career in music. Charles left Seattle for Los Angeles. Hendrix left Seattle for the Army, then Nashville, then the chitlin' circuit, then New York, then London, then the world. He never came back. He died in London on September 18, 1970, at age twenty-seven. He is buried in Renton, Washington -- in the Puget Sound region, but not in Seattle, not in the Central District. The Export was complete.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; HistoryLink.org -- "Hendrix, Jimi (1942-1970)")

## The Chitlin' Circuit: The Blues Education (1962-1966)

### Nashville and the Apprenticeship

After his Army discharge, Hendrix moved to Clarksville, Tennessee (near Fort Campbell), then to Nashville, and entered the world of the chitlin' circuit -- the network of venues, clubs, theaters, and roadhouses across the American South and urban North that served Black audiences during the era of segregation. This was the blues education that formal music training could not provide. The circuit was a trade school, a hazing ritual, and a conservatory all at once. You played three sets a night, six nights a week, backing established artists whose music you had to learn on the spot, following bandleaders who demanded precision, showmanship, and subordination.

Hendrix served his apprenticeship backing some of the most important figures in rhythm and blues. The timeline of his chitlin' circuit years reads like a curriculum in Black American music:

| Period | Artist/Band | Role | Key Detail |
|--------|------------|------|------------|
| 1962 | Various Nashville bands | Sideman | Club circuit, learning the trade |
| 1963 | Little Richard (The Upsetters) | Touring guitarist | Fired by Richard's brother Robert over tardiness and stage antics. Recorded "I Don't Know What You Got (But It's Got Me)" in Los Angeles, February 1965 |
| 1963-1964 | The Isley Brothers | Touring guitarist | Recorded "Testify" (1964). Given more freedom to stretch out on stage. Left the band in late October 1964, tired of the same set every night |
| 1964 | Gorgeous George Odell | Backing guitarist | Chitlin' circuit touring |
| 1964-1965 | Little Richard (return) | Touring guitarist | Second stint; clashed over wardrobe, showmanship, punctuality |
| 1965-1966 | Curtis Knight and the Squires | Guitarist, co-writer | New York-based. Met Knight in a hotel lobby. Recorded "How Would You Feel" (October 1965) |
| 1966 | King Curtis | Session guitarist | New York R&B scene |
| 1966 | Jimmy James and the Blue Flames | Bandleader | Hendrix leads his own group in Greenwich Village. Discovered by Chas Chandler |

Every one of these gigs was rooted in the blues. Little Richard's band played R&B, but the foundation was blues. The Isley Brothers' music was soul, but the guitar parts were blues vocabulary. Curtis Knight's material was blues-based. The chitlin' circuit was, at its core, a blues institution -- the economic infrastructure that kept the blues alive as a performed art form in the years after it had lost mainstream commercial appeal to rock and roll.

What Hendrix learned on the circuit was not just repertoire but *discipline*. He learned to read a room. He learned to support a vocalist without stepping on the lyrics. He learned to play rhythm guitar with the kind of precision and restraint that most people never associate with his name. He learned the business of professional music -- the van rides, the one-night stands, the cheap hotels, the promoters who might not pay. And he absorbed, through osmosis and repetition, the entire vocabulary of the blues guitar tradition: the bends, the vibrato, the call-and-response patterns, the turnarounds, the twelve-bar structure, the pentatonic scale in all its variations.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; Rolling Stone -- "Jimi Hendrix: 10 Great Pre-Fame Tracks"; Encyclopedia.com -- Jimi Hendrix; Goldmine -- "Before he was a headliner, Jimi Hendrix was a hired hand"; Variety -- "Hendrix rocks the chitlin circuit"; Open Culture -- "See Jimi Hendrix's First TV Appearance, and His Last as a Backing Musician (1965)")

### The Encounter with Albert King

Of all the blues musicians Hendrix encountered on the chitlin' circuit, one made a particularly deep impression: Albert King. King was a left-handed guitarist who played a right-handed Gibson Flying V flipped upside down -- but unlike Hendrix, King did not restring. He played with the strings in reverse order, the bass strings nearest the floor, which gave him a unique physical approach to bending. King's bends were enormous, gravity-assisted pulls that no right-handed guitarist could replicate. His tone was stinging, sharp, and emotionally devastating.

Hendrix called Albert King his idol. He cited King's "Crosscut Saw" as one of his favorite tunes. The connection runs deeper than admiration: both were left-handed guitarists who turned the physical act of playing into something visually and sonically distinct from the right-handed norm. If you listen to Hendrix's "Red House" -- one of the most sophisticated blues recordings in history -- you can hear Albert King in the DNA: the scale of the bends, the deliberate placement of notes, the way silence between phrases carries as much meaning as the notes themselves.

(Sources: PS Audio -- "Guitar Influences, Part Four: Albert King"; Guitar World -- "How Albert King created his own strain of the blues"; Blues Guitar Insider -- "Albert King")

## The Blues in the Recordings

### "Red House" -- Pure Blues

"Red House" is the skeleton key to understanding Hendrix as a blues musician. Recorded during the *Are You Experienced* sessions in 1966-1967, it is a slow blues in the key of B (played as a blues in A with the guitar tuned down a half step), built on the standard twelve-bar blues progression. It is the only track on *Are You Experienced* that would have been completely recognizable to a Delta blues musician transported forward in time. The lyrics are standard blues fare: a man returns home to find his woman has left and his key doesn't fit the lock anymore. The guitar work is pure Albert King, B.B. King, and Muddy Waters -- filtered through Hendrix's hands but unmistakably rooted in the tradition.

"Red House" was included on the UK release of *Are You Experienced* (Track Records, 1967) but omitted from the initial US release (Reprise Records, 1967) -- a telling decision. Reprise positioned Hendrix as a rock act for the American market, and a slow blues didn't fit the "Purple Haze" / "Foxey Lady" image. The blues was the part of Hendrix that his American label thought wouldn't sell. It took the posthumous compilation *Blues* (1994, MCA Records) to place "Red House" in its proper context as the centerpiece of Hendrix's blues identity.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Blues (Jimi Hendrix album); Jas Obrecht / Substack -- "Jimi Hendrix's 'Red House': The Story of a Song"; Cincinnati Blues Society -- "Blues by Jimi Hendrix")

### "Voodoo Chile" -- Electrified Delta Blues

"Voodoo Chile" (the fifteen-minute jam, not "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)") is the track that most explicitly maps Hendrix's blues lineage. Recorded on May 2, 1968, for *Electric Ladyland* (Reprise, 1968), it is based on Muddy Waters' "Rollin' Stone" (1950) -- the same record that had named the Rolling Stones and that had terrified the young Jimi when he heard it on his father's turntable. The song blends two of Hendrix's great loves: Chicago blues and science fiction. The lyrics place the narrator at an intersection of Delta mythology and cosmic consciousness -- a voodoo child born with the ability to chop down mountains with the edge of his hand, who walks through the ocean floor and talks to Neptune.

The musical structure is a slow blues in E, driven by Hendrix's guitar and accompanied by Steve Winwood on organ and Jack Casady (Jefferson Airplane) on bass. It is a fifteen-minute improvisation in the tradition of the Delta blues -- long, hypnotic, repetitive, trance-inducing. The guitar tone is overdriven but controlled, each note placed with the precision of a surgeon and the emotional weight of a preacher. This is the blues at its most expansive -- the twelve-bar structure stretched across a quarter of an hour, with Hendrix using feedback, wah-wah, and volume as expressive tools within the blues vocabulary, not departures from it.

"Voodoo Child (Slight Return)," recorded the next day, May 3, 1968, is the compressed, explosive version -- the same blues DNA packed into a four-minute detonation. It became the last Jimi Hendrix Experience single released during his lifetime, reaching No. 1 in the UK after his death. During the January 1, 1970, Band of Gypsys performance at the Fillmore East, Hendrix introduced it as "the Black Panthers' national anthem" [NEEDS VERIFICATION -- attribution varies by source].

(Sources: Wikipedia -- "Voodoo Chile"; Wikipedia -- "Voodoo Child (Slight Return)"; Rock and Roll Globe -- "Voodoo Chile: Jimi Hendrix at 80"; tedtockscovers.wordpress.com)

### "Machine Gun" -- The Blues as Political Statement

"Machine Gun" is the apex of Hendrix's blues-as-protest work. Performed by Band of Gypsys at the Fillmore East on January 1, 1970, the nearly twelve-minute track uses the blues as a vehicle for Hendrix's anguish about the Vietnam War. The opening dedication -- "I'd like to dedicate this to all the soldiers fighting in Chicago, Milwaukee, and New York... oh yes, and all the soldiers fighting in Vietnam" -- frames the blues as a weapon of social commentary, not merely personal expression.

The guitar work on "Machine Gun" is among the most physically intense in recorded music. Hendrix uses feedback to simulate the sound of gunfire, wah-wah to create the wailing of air-raid sirens, and volume to reproduce the overwhelming sensory assault of combat. But the underlying structure is blues -- the bent notes, the call-and-response between guitar and rhythm section, the emotional arc from tension to release to devastation. "Machine Gun" is what happens when a blues guitarist with military experience turns the blues into a war narrative. It is generally regarded as the *Band of Gypsys* album's highlight and one of Hendrix's greatest achievements.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- "Machine Gun: The Fillmore East First Show"; Wikipedia -- Band of Gypsys; Official Jimi Hendrix Site -- "Live At The Fillmore East"; Ultimate Classic Rock -- "Machine Gun: The Fillmore East First Show review")

### "Hear My Train A Comin'" -- The Delta Standard

"Hear My Train A Comin'" (also known as "Getting My Heart Back Together Again") is Hendrix's most direct engagement with the acoustic Delta blues tradition. Multiple versions exist -- the acoustic performance on the Dick Cavett Show (1969), the electric versions from the Fillmore East (1969-1970), and various studio outtakes. The song's imagery -- waiting at the train station, going away, coming back with money and gold -- is pure Delta blues vocabulary, the language of Robert Johnson, Muddy Waters, and Howlin' Wolf transported into the psychedelic era but structurally unaltered.

The acoustic version is particularly significant for the blues-context lens. Hendrix plays a twelve-string acoustic guitar in open tuning, using a slide-like technique without an actual slide. The performance strips away every electronic innovation -- no Marshall stacks, no wah-wah, no feedback -- and reveals the Delta blues guitarist underneath the rock star. This is the music he learned from his father's records, performed with the same raw emotional directness that Muddy Waters brought to his earliest Chess recordings.

(Sources: Official Jimi Hendrix Site -- encyclopedia; RNfinity -- "Hear my machine a comin"; The Second Disc -- "Power of Soul: Jimi Hendrix's Band of Gypsys Revisited")

### Band of Gypsys: The Blues-Funk Statement

The Band of Gypsys -- Hendrix on guitar, Billy Cox on bass, Buddy Miles on drums -- was Hendrix's most explicitly Black musical project. Formed in late 1969, the trio's four concerts at the Fillmore East (December 31, 1969, and January 1, 1970) produced the *Band of Gypsys* album (Capitol Records, 1970 -- released on Capitol due to a contractual obligation, not on Reprise). The album is the clearest recorded evidence of where Hendrix was heading before his death: toward a blues-funk synthesis that integrated the rhythmic complexity of James Brown with the harmonic language of the Delta blues and the textural experimentation of his Experience years.

Hendrix's scalding version of Elmore James' "Bleeding Heart" from the Fillmore East sessions underscores the new band's emphasis on the blues. Albert King's "Born Under a Bad Sign" appears as an instrumental jam. The set lists from the four Fillmore concerts are heavy with blues material -- a deliberate artistic statement about roots and direction. Band of Gypsys was not a side project; it was a declaration of intent. Hendrix was going deeper into the blues, not further from it.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Band of Gypsys; The Second Disc -- "Power of Soul"; The Reconstructor -- "Jimi Hendrix - Band of Gypsys (1970)")

## Musical Innovation Through the Blues Lens

### The Stratocaster as Blues Instrument

Hendrix acquired his first Fender Stratocaster in 1966, when a girlfriend loaned him enough money to purchase a used model built around 1964. He described the Stratocaster as "the best all-around guitar for the stuff we're doing," praising its "bright treble and deep bass." The Stratocaster became his signature instrument for the rest of his life.

What makes Hendrix's Stratocaster playing distinctively blues -- rather than merely rock -- is the way he used the instrument's design features as blues-expression tools. The left-handed, restrung configuration gave his lowest string (the E string, nearest the ceiling) a brighter sound than intended, and his highest string a darker sound. This reversed tonal profile created a unique palette that conventional right-handed players could not replicate. He rejected the standard barre chord technique in favor of fretting the low sixth-string root notes with his thumb -- a technique borrowed from the blues tradition (Albert King, Otis Rush) that allowed him to sustain bass notes while playing melody on the treble strings. The opening bars of "Little Wing" are the most famous demonstration of this technique.

The Stratocaster's tremolo bar -- designed for subtle vibrato -- became, in Hendrix's hands, a blues-expression device capable of deep pitch dives and returns, emulating the vocal quality of a blues singer's cry. His vibrato was not a decorative effect; it was the blues expression of emotional pain, applied to an electric guitar at a volume and intensity that no acoustic blues instrument could achieve.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; IEEE Spectrum -- "Jimi Hendrix's Analog Wizardry Explained"; Premier Guitar -- "Fender Jimi Hendrix Stratocaster Review"; Rolling Stone)

### Feedback, Distortion, and Wah-Wah as Blues Expression

Hendrix is credited with pioneering the use of guitar amplifier feedback as a controlled musical element, popularizing the wah-wah pedal in mainstream rock, and establishing fuzz distortion as a primary tonal color. These innovations are typically narrated as rock innovations. Through the blues lens, they are something different: they are extensions of the blues guitarist's eternal project of making the guitar sound like a human voice.

The blues guitar tradition, from Robert Johnson to B.B. King to Albert King, has always been about making the instrument sing, cry, moan, scream, and whisper. Johnson used slide techniques. B.B. King used his signature "butterfly" vibrato. Albert King used enormous string bends. Each generation found new ways to extract vocal qualities from steel strings and wood. Hendrix's contribution was to add the electronics of the twentieth century to this project:

- **Feedback:** The guitar sustaining indefinitely, the way a blues singer can hold a note beyond the physical limits of the instrument. Controlled feedback is the electronic equivalent of a field holler -- a note that stretches across space and time.
- **Distortion (fuzz):** The guitar's signal overdriving and breaking apart, adding harmonic overtones that make a single note sound like a chord. In blues terms, this is the "cry" -- the roughness in the voice of a Robert Johnson or a Howlin' Wolf, applied to the guitar signal.
- **Wah-wah:** The guitar's frequency spectrum swept by a foot pedal, producing a vocal "wah" sound. This is the most literal guitar-as-voice technique in Hendrix's arsenal, directly emulating the human vowel sounds that define vocal blues expression.

As Rolling Stone wrote: "Hendrix pioneered the use of the instrument as an electronic sound source. Players before him had experimented with feedback and distortion, but Hendrix turned those effects and others into a controlled, fluid vocabulary every bit as personal as the blues with which he began."

(Sources: Rolling Stone; Wikipedia -- Jimi Hendrix; IEEE Spectrum; Guitar World; customboards.fi -- "A detailed look at Jimi Hendrix' pedals and signal chain")

### Marshall Stacks: Volume as Blues Amplification

Hendrix's relationship with Marshall amplifiers transformed the physical experience of hearing the blues. He plugged his Stratocaster into Marshall Super Lead 100-watt heads connected to stacks of speaker cabinets -- typically three stacks per side, each consisting of two 4x12 cabinets (eight speakers per stack). This produced the loudest sound possible with the technology of the late 1960s. He cranked the amplifiers to maximum volume, sometimes frying vacuum tubes and cracking speakers in the process.

Through the blues lens, volume is not merely spectacle -- it is the amplification of emotion. The Delta blues was quiet because it was played on acoustic instruments in small rooms. The Chicago blues of Muddy Waters was louder because it was electrified, played through amplifiers in bars. Hendrix took the trajectory to its logical conclusion: the blues at the volume of a jet engine, the emotional content of a Robert Johnson recording delivered at a physical intensity that you felt in your chest, your bones, your teeth. The Marshall stack was not a departure from the blues. It was the blues' final amplification -- the technology catching up to the emotional scale of the music.

(Sources: Musiclipse -- "The relationship between Jimi Hendrix and the revolutionary Marshall Amplifiers"; MoPOP -- Marshall Super Lead Amplifier display; jimihendrix.no -- "The Amps of Jimi Hendrix"; Ground Guitar -- "Jimi Hendrix's Woodstock Gear")

## The Export Pattern -- Amplified

### The Biggest Export

Quincy Jones (degree 0) was the engine's first Export -- a Central District musician who left Seattle for a national career. Ray Charles (degree 30) was the Incubator -- a brief Seattle formation period before departure. Robert Cray (degree 35) was the Deep Root -- a PNW lifer who stayed regional. Hendrix is the Export pattern at its absolute maximum: the most famous musician in rock history, born and raised in the Central District, who left Seattle and never returned.

The Export pattern at degree 36 carries a weight that Jones's did not at degree 0 because the engine now has 35 degrees of context. ### Seattle Claims Him Anyway

Despite Hendrix's absence from Seattle during his entire professional career, the city has claimed him comprehensively:

- **Museum of Pop Culture (MoPOP):** Founded by Paul Allen in 2000 as the Experience Music Project, the museum began as a Jimi Hendrix museum. It holds the world's largest collection of Hendrix artifacts -- over 6,000 items including personal instruments, hand-written lyrics, original photographs, and equipment. The museum's central Sky Church is named in homage to Hendrix.
- **Jimi Hendrix Statue:** A bronze statue on Capitol Hill (Broadway and Pine), privately funded by real-estate developer Mike Malone, installed in 1997.
- **Jimi Hendrix Park:** A community green space in the Central District, near where Hendrix grew up. Features a grand stairway entrance at Massachusetts and 25th Avenue South, adorned with Hendrix's signature and the "Shadow Wave Wall." A chronological timeline of his life and career is paved into the pathways.
- **Northwest African American Museum exhibit:** "Bold As Love: Jimi Hendrix at Home" -- an exhibition exploring Hendrix's Seattle roots.
- **Gravesite:** Hendrix is buried at Greenwood Memorial Park in Renton, Washington. The memorial, maintained by his family, draws visitors from around the world.

The irony of the Export pattern: the artist who left Seattle at nineteen and never lived there again is now the city's most visible musical icon, with a museum, a park, a statue, and an entire neighborhood's claim to his legacy. Seattle didn't make Hendrix famous. London did. But Seattle made Hendrix *Hendrix* -- the blues foundation, the Central District formation, the neighborhood that taught him music before he had a guitar.

## Blue Note -- The screaming bend (36th deployment)

The thirty-sixth blue note deployment is **the screaming bend**: the blue note played through overdriven amplification at maximum volume, where the pitch bend is not merely heard but physically felt through speaker vibration, room resonance, and the electromagnetic interaction between guitar pickups and amplifier speakers.
