The Tapping Signal — Two-Hand Technique as Compositional Tool

Dave Knudson’s two-hand tapping is the defining sonic signature: both hands on the fretboard simultaneously, tapping notes to build entire song structures — riffs, melodies, rhythmic patterns. Combined with digital delay and loop pedals, a single guitar sounds like multiple instruments. Where Van Halen used tapping for flashy solos, Knudson used it as a primary compositional method. The cascading, almost piano-like quality is unmistakable. Previously of Botch (Tacoma mathcore) — same hands, same fretboard, radically different music.

Math Rock Enters the Engine — Genre Stage 93

Math rock arrives at degree 113 — the first appearance in the engine. Complex time signatures (7/8, 11/8, alternating meters), angular riff structures, polyrhythmic drumming, technical virtuosity. But Minus the Bear made math rock fun. You can write in 7/8 and still make people dance. You can use two-hand tapping and still write hooks. The math doesn’t have to be forbidding. Technical musicianship disguised as party music.

Suicide Squeeze Records — New Label in the Engine

Founded 1996 in Seattle by David Dickenson. Initially a limited-edition 7” singles label, evolved into a full-service indie. Roster: Minus the Bear, Modest Mouse (early singles), Elliott Smith, Botch, Pretty Girls Make Graves, The Thermals. Joins Sub Pop, Kill Rock Stars, K Records, Up Records, Barsuk, Sargent House in the engine’s label map. Eclectic — no genre specialization, only quality.

Key Albums

Highly Refined Pirates (2002, Suicide Squeeze) — the debut. Establishes the template: Knudson’s tapping, Snider’s smooth vocals, complex structures beneath accessible surfaces. Famously absurd song titles.

Menos el Oso (2005, Suicide Squeeze) — the masterpiece. All elements crystallize. “Pachuca Sunrise” and “The Game Needed Me” demonstrate the ability to write genuinely catchy songs on genuinely complex structures. The album that brought Minus the Bear from math rock niche to indie mainstream.

Planet of Ice (2007, Suicide Squeeze) — the ambitious expansion. Longer songs, more atmospheric, more progressive. “Knights” is a ten-minute epic tracing the full range of the band’s capabilities.

The Band

Jake Snider (vocals, guitar) — smooth, melodic vocal counterpoint to angular instrumentation. Previously of Sharks Keep Moving.
Dave Knudson (guitar) — tapping pioneer. Previously of Botch.
Erin Tate (drums) — polyrhythmic precision, the bridge between math rock complexity and electronic textures.
Cory Murchy (bass) — melodic, active bass lines occupying their own rhythmic space.
Alex Rose (keyboards, synths) — the electronic element that transformed math rock into math rock/electronic.
