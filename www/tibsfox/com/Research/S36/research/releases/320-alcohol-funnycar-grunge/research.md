## EMPTY RECORDS -- THE INDIE UNDERGROUND

### Not Sub Pop, Not DGC, Not A&M

Empty Records is the specific label context that defines Alcohol Funnycar's position in the engine:

- **Empty Records.** Founded in Seattle in 1988 by Fred Canby. A small independent label that operated outside the Sub Pop commercial infrastructure and well outside the major-label system. Empty's catalog in the early 1990s included Alcohol Funnycar, Fastbacks, Flop, and a range of Seattle underground acts whose primary audience was dedicated underground rock listeners rather than the mainstream alternative audience.
- **The Empty aesthetic.** Empty Records releases tend toward faster tempos, rawer production, and more explicit punk influences than the Sub Pop grunge aesthetic. Sub Pop was always slightly glam-influenced -- the packaging was designed, the promotional approach was commercially sophisticated. Empty was more directly DIY: smaller budgets, faster recordings, more explicitly punk in the press materials and approach.
- **Position in the Seattle ecosystem.** Empty Records operated alongside Sub Pop (founded 1988) and C/Z Records as one of the local indie labels that collectively documented the range of Seattle underground rock beyond the grunge mainstream. Empty's contribution is the punk end of the spectrum.
- **Market.** Empty Records releases circulated primarily through independent record stores, college radio, and the underground tape-trading network. They did not achieve Sub Pop's national promotional reach or the major labels' commercial distribution. The audience was specific and committed.

(Sources: Empty Records history; Fred Canby; founding 1988; Fastbacks on Empty; Flop on Empty; Seattle indie label ecosystem; Sub Pop comparison; DIY aesthetic analysis)

## ALCOHOL FUNNYCAR -- THE BAND

### Formation and Identity

Alcohol Funnycar formed in Seattle around 1990:

- **Dave Dederer.** The guitarist and a primary creative force. Dederer would go on to co-found The Presidents of the United States of America (PUSA) with Chris Ballew and Jason Finn in 1993. His time in Alcohol Funnycar precedes the PUSA formation -- this is where he was working before the band that made him commercially famous. The contrast between Alcohol Funnycar (Empty Records, underground punk-grunge) and PUSA (Maverick Records, 1+ million debut) is one of the engine's most dramatic before-and-after career trajectories.
- **The band's identity.** Alcohol Funnycar occupies the punk end of the grunge spectrum -- the zone where grunge's heavy guitar inheritance meets punk's speed and DIY ethos rather than metal's technical precision. The tempos are faster than most grunge acts in the engine; the production is rawer; the songs are shorter. This is grunge's punk wing.
- **The name.** Alcohol Funnycar is a reference to Funny Car drag racing -- a class of heavily modified drag-racing cars that run on alcohol fuel rather than gasoline. The name is aggressive, slightly absurdist, and points toward the punk tradition of deliberately provocative or nonsensical band names (Dead Kennedys, Black Flag, Bad Brains). The name signals that this band is not trying to be taken seriously in the commercial sense.

(Sources: Dave Dederer biography; PUSA formation 1993; Alcohol Funnycar formation; Empty Records context; funny car drag racing reference; punk-naming tradition; Chris Ballew and Jason Finn PUSA co-founders)

## THE DAVE DEDERER TRAJECTORY

### From Empty Records Punk to PUSA Commercial Breakthrough

Dave Dederer's career arc is one of the most interesting in the engine's documentation:

- **Pre-PUSA context.** Dederer was working in Alcohol Funnycar's underground punk-grunge context through the early 1990s. The Empty Records environment -- small budget, committed underground audience, DIY infrastructure -- is where his guitar skills and songwriting were developing.
- **PUSA formation (1993).** Chris Ballew (vocals/bass) and Dederer (guitar) formed The Presidents of the United States of America in 1993. Jason Finn (drums) -- who was simultaneously active with Love Battery (engine degree 221) -- completed the lineup. The creative approach was a deliberate departure from the grunge mode: shorter songs, absurdist lyrics, stripped-down arrangements, comedic sensibility.
- **PUSA debut (1995).** The self-titled debut on Maverick Records sold over 1 million copies. "Lump," "Peaches," and "Kitty" became pop-rock staples. The commercial distance between Alcohol Funnycar (Empty Records underground) and PUSA (Maverick major-label 1M+) is vast.
- **The transition.** What carries from Alcohol Funnycar to PUSA is the punk velocity and the willingness to be absurd. PUSA is not grunge; it is not even post-grunge in the heavy sense. But the punk energy that Dederer developed in Alcohol Funnycar -- fast tempos, economy of arrangement, the idea that a song should be short and land a specific punch -- is audible in PUSA's approach.

(Sources: Dave Dederer Alcohol Funnycar; PUSA formation; Chris Ballew; Jason Finn dual membership; PUSA debut 1995 Maverick; Lump, Peaches, Kitty; 1M+ copies; Empty to Maverick trajectory; punk energy carried to PUSA)

## THE SOUND -- GRUNGE/PUNK, E=7

### Speed and Aggression Without Metal's Technical Precision

Alcohol Funnycar's sonic position at E=7:

- **The punk-grunge fusion.** Grunge as a genre sits between punk and metal. The Seattle mainstream (Soundgarden, Alice in Chains) leaned toward metal's technical elements -- complex tunings, heavy riffs, extended compositions. The Sub Pop mainstream (Mudhoney, Nirvana) leaned toward punk's energy and economy. Alcohol Funnycar is further toward the punk end than any entry thus far in the cooldown sequence.
- **Tempo.** Faster than most grunge. The songs move. Where Soundgarden (E=8) is heavy and measured, Alcohol Funnycar (E=7) is fast and raw. The aggression comes from speed rather than from the slow-building heaviness of the metal-grunge tradition.
- **Production.** Empty Records production context: lower budget, faster recording, rawer final product. The production rawness is not a deficiency -- it is the aesthetic. The roughness is part of the punk identity.
- **Song length.** Shorter songs. The punk economy of the genre means less extended instrumentation, less verse-chorus repetition, more direct arrival at and departure from the hook. Alcohol Funnycar songs tend to be over before the listener expects them to be.
- **E=7 from speed.** The three consecutive E=7 entries (317: grief, 318: accessibility, 319: precision) are now followed by a fourth mode: speed. Alcohol Funnycar's E=7 comes from fast-attack punk energy, not from emotional weight or melodic hooks. The cooldown's E=7 register contains four distinct modes across degrees 317-320.

(Sources: Grunge-punk fusion; tempo analysis; Empty Records production aesthetic; song length analysis; punk economy; speed-as-aggression vs. weight-as-aggression; E=7 fourth mode: velocity)
