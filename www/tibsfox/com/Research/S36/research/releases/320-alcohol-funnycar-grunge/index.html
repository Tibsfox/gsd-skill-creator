Empty Records — The Underground Label

Empty Records was founded in Seattle in 1988 by Fred Canby — a small independent operating outside Sub Pop’s commercial infrastructure and well outside the major-label system. Empty’s catalog included Fastbacks, Flop, and a range of underground acts whose primary audience was the committed underground rock listener rather than the mainstream alternative audience. Empty was more explicitly DIY than Sub Pop: smaller budgets, faster recordings, rawer production, the punk ethos foregrounded rather than the savvy promotional image that made Sub Pop famous.

“The name Alcohol Funnycar — a reference to alcohol-fueled drag racing — signals that this band is not trying to be taken seriously in the commercial sense. The name is aggressive, slightly absurdist, and points toward the punk tradition of deliberately provocative identity.”

Dave Dederer — Before PUSA

Dave Dederer’s time in Alcohol Funnycar is the pre-PUSA context. He and Chris Ballew co-founded The Presidents of the United States of America in 1993 with Jason Finn (who was simultaneously in Love Battery). PUSA’s debut (1995, Maverick) sold over 1 million copies. “Lump,” “Peaches,” “Kitty.”

The distance between Alcohol Funnycar (Empty Records, underground punk-grunge, small committed audience) and PUSA (Maverick Records, 1M+ debut, MTV rotation) is one of the engine’s most dramatic before-and-after career trajectories. What carries: the punk velocity, the economy of arrangement, the willingness to be absurd. PUSA’s songs are short, punchy, and direct — the punk energy from Alcohol Funnycar applied to melodic pop-rock with comedic sensibility.

The Sound — Fast, Raw, Direct

Alcohol Funnycar’s songs are faster than most grunge entries in the engine. Where Soundgarden builds slowly to E=8, Alcohol Funnycar arrives at E=7 immediately and departs before the listener expects it. The punk economy of the form: short songs, minimal repetition, no extended instrumentation. The production rawness is the aesthetic — Empty Records’ budget constraints and DIY ethos produce a roughness that is intentional, not accidental. The roughness is part of the punk identity.

The aggression comes from speed rather than from the slow-building heaviness of the metal-grunge tradition. This is E=7 from momentum rather than E=7 from weight. The difference is audible: metal-grunge (Gruntruck, E=8) presses down; punk-grunge (Alcohol Funnycar, E=7) pushes forward.
