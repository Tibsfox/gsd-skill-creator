The Curatorial Visionary

Bruce Pavitt co-founded Sub Pop Records with Jonathan Poneman, but his path to the label was fundamentally different. Pavitt started as a fanzine editor at The Evergreen State College in Olympia, writing a column called “Sub Pop” (short for “subterranean pop”) for The Rocket. The column reviewed underground American music — cassette releases, small-label 7-inches, regional scenes the national press ignored.

Pavitt’s path: fanzine column (1980) → photocopied fanzine → cassette compilation (Sub Pop #5, 1983) → record label (1986). The critic became the curator. The curator became the person who identified the Seattle sound before anyone had a name for it: heavy, raw, distorted, underground. Green River, Mudhoney, Soundgarden, Nirvana. Pavitt heard the signal in the noise.

The Evergreen State / Olympia Roots

Olympia generated the ideas and aesthetic principles. Seattle provided the infrastructure to amplify them. Pavitt carried the Olympia ethos to Seattle — independence, underground credibility, curation over commerce — and combined it with Poneman’s business acumen to build an institution.

The Evergreen State College produced an outsized share of PNW cultural infrastructure: Calvin Johnson (K Records, degree 43), the riot grrrl movement (Bikini Kill), Slim Moon (Kill Rock Stars, degree 124), and Bruce Pavitt. Three Olympia labels that shaped PNW music: K Records (pure independence), Kill Rock Stars (independence as political statement), Sub Pop (independence with pragmatic survival). An evolutionary sequence from the same city.

Pavitt + Poneman — Complementary Halves

Pavitt brought the ear: years of listening, writing, curating across the American underground. He knew which bands to sign because he could hear what was distinctive. He brought subcultural credibility — fanzine kid, not music executive. He brought national context — the fanzine’s scope meant he understood where Seattle fit in the broader American underground.

Poneman (degree 127) brought the capital, the business structure, the promotional genius. Neither could have built Sub Pop alone. The partnership is the engine’s clearest example of complementary founding: one ear, one wallet, one label.

The Fanzine-to-Label Pipeline

The Sub Pop origin story is a study in organic institutional evolution. A music column becomes a fanzine. A fanzine becomes a cassette compilation. A compilation becomes a label. Each step is a natural extension of curation: from writing about sounds to presenting sounds to building infrastructure for sounds. Pavitt did not set out to create the most important label in Seattle music history. He set out to write about underground music. The label was the logical conclusion of the curatorial impulse.

Pavitt stepped back from day-to-day operations in the mid-1990s after the grunge explosion. His 2014 book Sub Pop USA: The Subterranean Pop Music Anthology, 1980–1988 documents the fanzine years. The label’s curatorial DNA — Pavitt’s imprint — persists in every signing decision Sub Pop makes.
