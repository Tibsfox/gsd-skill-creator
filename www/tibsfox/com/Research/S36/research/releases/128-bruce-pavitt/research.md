## BRUCE PAVITT -- FROM EVERGREEN STATE TO SUB POP

### The Olympia Origin

Bruce Pavitt's story begins in **Olympia, Washington**, at **The Evergreen State College** -- the experimental public college known for its interdisciplinary approach, its emphasis on independent study, and its outsized influence on Pacific Northwest culture. Evergreen produced:

- **The riot grrrl movement:** Kathleen Hanna, Tobi Vail (Bikini Kill), and the Olympia scene that became riot grrrl were rooted in Evergreen and Olympia's DIY culture.
- **Calvin Johnson and K Records:** Johnson, who founded K Records and Beat Happening, was a central figure in the Olympia music community. K Records (degree 43) embodied the "lo-fi as aesthetic choice" philosophy that influenced the entire PNW independent music ecosystem.
- **Kill Rock Stars:** Slim Moon's label (degree 124) was founded in Olympia in 1991, directly influenced by the K Records/Evergreen ethos.
- **Bruce Pavitt and Sub Pop:** Pavitt's fanzine, which became the Sub Pop label, originated in the Evergreen State College newspaper.

Olympia's role in PNW music is disproportionate to the city's size (population ~55,000). The Evergreen State College created a hothouse environment for experimental thinking about music, culture, and independence. Pavitt emerged from this environment with a worldview that valued curation, independence, and the underground as inherently more interesting than the mainstream.

The **Olympia-to-Seattle pipeline** is one of the most important cultural arteries in PNW music. Pavitt moved from Olympia to Seattle, bringing the Olympia ethos with him. This mirrors the broader pattern: Olympia generates ideas and aesthetic principles; Seattle provides the infrastructure (labels, studios, venues, media) to amplify them.

## THE SUB POP FANZINE: FROM COLUMN TO CASSETTE TO LABEL

### 1980-1986: The Origin Story

The name "Sub Pop" predates the label by several years. Pavitt's path from writer to label founder:

- **1980:** Pavitt begins writing a music column called **"Sub Pop"** (short for "subterranean pop") for *The Rocket*, an Olympia/Seattle music newspaper. The column reviews independent and underground American music -- cassette releases, small-label 7-inches, regional scenes that the national music press ignores.
- **1980-1983:** Pavitt also publishes **Sub Pop** as a **fanzine** -- a photocopied, hand-stapled publication that he writes, edits, and distributes himself. The fanzine covers the American underground: Midwest punk, Southern college rock, Pacific Northwest noise, Texas garage. Pavitt's curatorial scope is national, not just local.
- **1983:** Pavitt releases **Sub Pop #5** as a **cassette compilation** -- the first time the Sub Pop name is attached to actual music rather than writing about music. The compilation features underground bands from across the United States. This is the critical transition: from critic to curator, from writing about music to presenting music.
- **1986:** Pavitt and Poneman formalize Sub Pop as a **record label**. The fanzine becomes a label. The critic becomes a A&R executive. The transition from Sub Pop fanzine to Sub Pop Records is one of the most consequential transformations in independent music history.

### Pavitt's Curatorial Vision

What distinguished Pavitt's curation was its **geographic breadth combined with sonic specificity**. The Sub Pop fanzine covered bands from across America, but Pavitt was developing a specific aesthetic preference:

- **Heavy:** Loud, distorted guitars. Not punk's minimalism but a heavier, more textured approach drawing from Black Sabbath and Black Flag simultaneously.
- **Raw:** Unpolished production. The sound of a band in a room, not a band in a studio. Jack Endino's early Sub Pop productions embodied this: quick, cheap, live-sounding.
- **Underground:** Not just unsigned but actively anti-mainstream. Music that existed in basements, community centers, and all-ages venues. Music that was not trying to get on the radio.
- **Regional:** Pavitt recognized that the Pacific Northwest -- specifically Seattle -- was producing a concentration of bands that shared these characteristics. Green River, Soundgarden, the Melvins, the U-Men, Mudhoney. The regional cluster was not coincidental; it reflected shared influences, shared venues, and a shared rejection of the mainstream.

When Pavitt identified the Seattle sound, he did something no one else was doing: he **named it** before it had a name. The Sub Pop fanzine and early label releases created a framework -- a narrative, an aesthetic, an identity -- for music that had previously been just a collection of unconnected bands playing in basements. Pavitt's curation turned a scene into a movement.

## THE PAVITT/PONEMAN COMPLEMENTARITY

### Two Halves of One Vision

The Pavitt/Poneman partnership worked because the two founders brought **complementary and non-overlapping skills**:

**Bruce Pavitt brought:**
- **The ear:** The ability to identify the sound. Pavitt knew which bands to sign because he had spent years listening, writing, and curating. His ear was trained by the fanzine -- thousands of hours of listening to underground music from across America.
- **The aesthetic:** The Sub Pop aesthetic -- raw, heavy, underground, PNW-specific -- was Pavitt's creation. The visual identity (the Sub Pop logo, the design sensibility of early releases) reflected Pavitt's fanzine roots.
- **Subcultural credibility:** Pavitt was embedded in the underground. He had been writing about it for years. When Sub Pop signed a band, the band trusted the label because Pavitt was one of them -- a fanzine kid, not a music industry executive.
- **National context:** The fanzine's national scope meant Pavitt understood where Seattle fit in the broader American underground. He could see that Seattle's heavy, raw sound was distinctive in a national context -- not just another regional scene but something new.

**Jonathan Poneman brought:**
- **Capital and business structure** (documented at degree 127).
- **Promotional genius** (the Everett True invitation, the Singles Club).
- **Financial survival instincts** (the Nirvana royalty negotiation, the Warner deal).

The partnership's **complementarity** is the key: neither founder could have built Sub Pop alone. Pavitt without Poneman would have remained a fanzine editor with great taste but no business infrastructure. Poneman without Pavitt would have been a businessman without a sound to sell. Together, they created the institution that launched the Seattle sound.

### Pavitt's Departure

Bruce Pavitt **stepped back from day-to-day operations** at Sub Pop in the mid-1990s, after the grunge explosion. The reasons are complex:

- **Creative exhaustion:** The grunge explosion transformed Sub Pop from an underground label into a cultural institution. Pavitt had built Sub Pop as an underground project; the mainstream success changed its character.
- **Mission accomplished (in a sense):** Pavitt had set out to identify and amplify the Seattle sound. By 1992-93, the Seattle sound was the most recognized musical movement in the world. The curatorial mission was complete.
- **Continued involvement:** Pavitt remained involved with Sub Pop in an advisory capacity and continued writing about music. He published *Sub Pop USA: The Subterranean Pop Music Anthology, 1980-1988* in 2014, documenting the fanzine years.

Poneman continued to run Sub Pop, guiding the label through its post-grunge reinventions. The fact that Sub Pop survived and thrived after Pavitt's departure is a testament to the institutional infrastructure that Poneman built -- but the label's artistic identity, its curatorial DNA, bears Pavitt's imprint permanently.

## THE OLYMPIA CONNECTION: K RECORDS, KILL ROCK STARS, SUB POP

### Three Labels, One City

Degree 128 connects three Olympia-rooted labels that have appeared in the engine:

1. **K Records (degree 43):** Calvin Johnson. Founded 1982 in Olympia. The philosophical origin point. Lo-fi as aesthetic choice. Beat Happening. "Exploding with the urge to make art." K Records established the principle that Olympia could produce music that mattered -- that a small city with an experimental college could be a cultural center.

2. **Kill Rock Stars (degree 124):** Slim Moon. Founded 1991 in Olympia. The riot grrrl distribution channel. Bikini Kill, Sleater-Kinney, Elliott Smith. Kill Rock Stars took the K Records ethos and applied it to louder, more explicitly political music. Independence as aesthetic and ethical commitment.

3. **Sub Pop Records (degrees 127-128):** Pavitt and Poneman. Originated in Olympia (Pavitt's fanzine), formalized in Seattle. Sub Pop took the Olympia independence ethos and combined it with Seattle's business infrastructure. The result was a label that was both ideologically independent and commercially viable.

The three labels represent an **evolutionary sequence**: K Records (pure independence, anti-commercial), Kill Rock Stars (independence as political statement), Sub Pop (independence with pragmatic survival). Each label pushed the Olympia ethos in a different direction, and together they created the PNW's independent label ecosystem.

Pavitt's Evergreen/Olympia roots place him at the origin point of this sequence. He carried the Olympia ethos to Seattle and built a label that translated underground curation into a sustainable institution. The fanzine kid from Evergreen State became the person who identified the sound that changed American music.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound That Pavitt Heard

Pavitt's acoustic contribution is curatorial: **the ability to hear the signal in the noise**. The "acoustic profile" of Pavitt's contribution is the sonic character he identified and amplified:

- **The Seattle sound (as Pavitt heard it):** Heavy guitars tuned low. Distortion not as punk aggression but as texture and weight. Vocals that ranged from melodic to screaming, often in the same song. Drums hit hard, recorded live, not quantized or polished. The sound of a band playing together in a room, captured with minimal processing.
- **The fanzine ear:** Pavitt's years of listening to underground music from across America gave him a comparative framework. He could hear that Seattle's version of heavy underground music was distinctive: heavier than college rock, more melodic than hardcore punk, more textured than metal. The Seattle sound occupied a space that no other regional scene was filling.
- **Jack Endino's production:** The early Sub Pop sound was defined by producer **Jack Endino**, whose approach aligned with Pavitt's aesthetic: quick sessions, minimal overdubs, the sound of the room as much as the sound of the instruments. Endino's production on Mudhoney's "Touch Me I'm Sick" and Nirvana's *Bleach* established the sonic template that became "grunge."
- **The curatorial act as acoustic act:** When Pavitt selected a band for the Sub Pop roster, he was making an acoustic decision -- choosing which sounds would be amplified, distributed, and heard. Curation is an acoustic act: it determines what the audience hears. Pavitt's ear shaped the Seattle sound as surely as any guitar tone or vocal style.

### The Olympia Sound vs. The Seattle Sound

Pavitt's journey from Olympia to Seattle mirrors a sonic shift:

- **Olympia:** Lo-fi, quiet, deliberately small. K Records' aesthetic. Beat Happening. Intimate, minimal, anti-production.
- **Seattle:** Heavy, loud, raw but powerful. Sub Pop's aesthetic. Mudhoney, Soundgarden, Nirvana. The volume and intensity of the Seattle sound is the opposite of Olympia's lo-fi quietude.

Pavitt carried the **independence ethos** from Olympia but applied it to a **louder, heavier sound** in Seattle. The philosophical framework (independence, underground, anti-mainstream) traveled; the sonic character transformed. This is the Olympia-Seattle corridor in action: ideas flow from Olympia, infrastructure and amplification come from Seattle.
