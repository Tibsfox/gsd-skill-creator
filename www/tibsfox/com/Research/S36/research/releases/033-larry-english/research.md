## The Central District: Context for Disappearance

### The Four-Mile Radius

Most of Seattle's African American population during the 1960s and 1970s lived and made music within a four-mile area called the Central District. This concentration was not voluntary -- it was the product of redlining, racist zoning ordinances, restrictive housing covenants, and the broader architecture of American residential segregation. The Central District was the area where Black Seattleites were permitted to live, and within those boundaries, they built a complete musical ecosystem: churches for gospel training, schools for instrumental education, clubs for professional performance, radio stations for broadcast, and recording studios for documentation.

The ecosystem's key institutions, as documented across the engine's first 33 degrees:

**Schools:** Garfield High School (attended by Quincy Jones, degree 0; Jimi Hendrix; Luther Rabb, degree 32) was the educational node. The school's music program and the proximity of students to the Central District's professional music scene created a pipeline from adolescent practice to adult performance.

**Venues:** The Jackson Street clubs (1930s-1960s) gave way to later venues including the Black and Tan Club, the Golden Crown (a bar above a Chinese restaurant on Jackson), the Trojan Horse, the Birdland Supper Club, and the District Tavern. From 1965 through 1975, funk and soul music played at these venues until 4 AM, seven nights a week.

**Radio:** KYAC (1250 AM) was Seattle's Black-owned and Black-operated radio station from 1965 to 1981. Every bit of news from the Black community came across KYAC's airwaves. Local bands -- Black on White Affair, Cold Bold & Together, Robbie Hill's Family Affair -- topped KYAC's charts, sometimes beating national singles by artists like Marvin Gaye. KYAC was the sonic infrastructure of the Central District: the broadcast medium that connected the clubs to the homes, the performers to the audience, the community to itself.

**Recording:** Kearney Barton's Audio Recording studio was where many of Seattle's soul and funk musicians cut their records. Barton (1931-2012) recorded across genres for five decades, capturing the raw, reverberating analog signature that later archivists called the "Northwest Sound." His client list included Dave Lewis, Black on White Affair, Soul Swingers, and dozens of other local acts. Not every Central District musician could afford studio time, and not every recording session produced a pressed 45 -- the economics of local recording were precarious.

**The economics of local labels:** Seattle's local labels -- Jerden Records (1960-1971), Dolton Records (1959-1966), Etiquette Records, Seafair Records, Panorama Records -- were primarily oriented toward the rock, garage, and teen-dance markets. The R&B and soul musicians of the Central District had fewer label options. Many self-released 45s in tiny pressings -- 200, 500, maybe 1,000 copies -- sold at gigs and through local record shops. These pressings are the physical artifacts that DJ Mr. Supreme later found in dusty bins, some fetching upward of $5,000 on the collector market by the 2000s. But for every 45 that survived, many did not. Vinyl degrades. Basements flood. Estates are dispersed. Thrift stores discard what they cannot sell. The physical medium is fragile, and the smaller the pressing run, the less likely any copy survives.

Larry English, designated with the label "local" in the CSV data, exists in this context. A Central District R&B musician from the 1960s-1970s who recorded locally -- or who performed locally without recording at all -- and whose documentation did not survive the transition from live performance to physical archive to digital discovery.

### The Wheedle's Groove Recovery and Its Limits

The Wheedle's Groove project represents the most significant effort to recover Seattle's lost soul and funk heritage. Beginning in 2001, when DJ Mr. Supreme found a dusty Black on White Affair 45 in a 99-cent bin, the project expanded into two compilation albums on Light in the Attic Records (Volume I: 1965-75, released 2004; Volume II: 1972-1987, released 2014), a documentary film (directed by Jennifer Maas, 2009), and a Kearney Barton tribute compilation.

The compilations include approximately 34 tracks by approximately 20 distinct artists or bands. This is a significant recovery -- but it represents only the musicians whose physical recordings survived in collectible condition. The Wheedle's Groove recovery is bounded by the survival of vinyl: if no 45 was pressed, or if all copies were destroyed, the music could not be recovered. Larry English does not appear on either Wheedle's Groove compilation, which means either his recordings did not survive or he did not record commercially.

The documentary film, available on Netflix and other platforms, includes interviews with surviving musicians and community members. ## The "Local" Era: What the Venues Sounded Like

### 1965-1975: The Central District at Maximum Volume

Even without specific biographical data for Larry English, we can reconstruct the musical environment he performed in with high confidence, because the Central District's 1960s-1970s scene is well-documented at the aggregate level even when individual musicians are not.

**The Sound:** The Central District's soul and funk scene from 1965-1975 was characterized by tight horn sections, heavy bass grooves, gospel-trained vocals, and an energy level calibrated for all-night dancing. The house bands at venues like the Golden Crown and the District Tavern played sets that ran until 4 AM, seven nights a week. The repertoire mixed original compositions with covers of national hits -- Motown, Stax, Philadelphia International -- adapted to the specific energy of a Seattle club audience. The geographic isolation of the Pacific Northwest meant that Seattle musicians developed their own interpretations of national R&B styles, blending influences in ways that national acts did not.

**The Audience:** The Central District's club audience was the African American community itself -- families, couples, workers, students. The clubs were not concert venues in the modern sense; they were social infrastructure. Music was the medium through which the community gathered, and the musicians were the community's own. A Central District R&B musician in the 1960s-1970s was not performing for an abstract "audience" -- he was performing for his neighbors, his church members, his classmates, their parents.

**The Economics:** Local musicians in this era were paid in cash at the door. There was no streaming revenue, no YouTube monetization, no social media following to convert into income. A musician's income came from live performance, and live performance was local. To play outside Seattle required touring infrastructure that local acts rarely had. The gap between local success (packing the Golden Crown every Saturday) and national visibility (a record deal, radio play outside KYAC's signal range) was enormous. Most Central District musicians never crossed it.

**The Decline:** Disco's arrival in the mid-to-late 1970s killed the live soul and funk scene. The Central District's venues closed or changed their programming. The musicians who had packed clubs for a decade found their audience disappearing. As the SeattleMet "Crash Course" article documents: "Disco signaled the genre's death knell, and the Central District scene faded. Its players either left the city or took jobs as teachers or returned, as in [Patrinell] Staten's case, to the church choir."

Larry English's era designation of "1960s-1970s" spans this entire arc: from the scene's emergence to its dissolution. His energy rating of E=5 suggests a musician who was active and impactful throughout this entire period -- not a brief participant but a sustained presence across the scene's full lifespan.

(Sources: SeattleMet "Crash Course: A Century of Seattle Music" 2018; City Arts Magazine "A Legacy of Northwest Soul" 2009; KUOW "Remembering KYAC" 2020; KNKX 2014-07-14; Converge Media "The Rhythm of Resilience")

## Theory Contributions

### The Thirty-Fourth Artist-City Pattern: The Unrecorded

Larry English's relationship to Seattle produces the engine's thirty-fourth artist-city pattern: **The Unrecorded**. This is the pattern where a musician is fully embedded in the city's musical culture -- performing in its venues, known to its community, part of its scene -- but whose documentation does not survive the transition from live performance to permanent archive. The Unrecorded is not about quality or significance; it is about the mechanics of preservation. The Unrecorded musician played. The Unrecorded musician was heard. The Unrecorded musician mattered to the people in the room. But the room did not have a tape recorder, or the tape degraded, or the 45 was never pressed, or the press run was so small that no copy survived. The Unrecorded is the structural foundation beneath even The Foundation (degree 32): you cannot build a foundation without the soil beneath it, and you cannot build a scene without the musicians who perform in it but whose names are not written down.

### The Thirty-Fourth Acoustic Element: The Room Tone

The thirty-fourth acoustic element is **the room tone** -- the ambient sound of a venue before and during a performance, the hum of the crowd, the clink of glasses, the creak of the floor, the resonance of the room itself. In audio engineering, "room tone" is the baseline sound of a space -- what you hear when the performers are silent but the room is alive. The room tone is the acoustic element that cannot be separated from the live performance context. It is what a studio recording removes and what is lost when no recording exists at all.

For Larry English, the room tone IS the music. Without recordings, all that remains is the memory of what the room sounded like when he was playing -- the acoustic environment of the Central District's clubs in the 1960s and 1970s, the specific resonance of the Golden Crown or the Black and Tan Club or whatever venue he performed in, the sound of the community gathered to hear its own music. The room tone follows the groove lock (degree 32) in the acoustic progression: after the bass locks into its immovable pattern, the room responds. The room tone is the audience's presence made audible -- the sound of the people who were there.

### The Blue Note at Degree 33: The Ghost Bend

The thirty-third deployment of the blue note thread is **the ghost bend** -- the blue note that was played but not recorded, that existed in the air of a live performance but was never captured on tape or vinyl. The ghost bend is the blue note's most fundamental form: the micro-tonal inflection that a musician applies in the moment, for the audience in the room, without any intention of preservation. Every blue note in every prior degree -- from Quincy Jones's bass-line blue note at degree 0 through Luther Rabb's funk bass bend at degree 32 -- was captured in a recording. The ghost bend at degree 33 is the blue note that was NOT captured. It existed. It was heard. It moved people. And then it was gone.

The ghost bend completes a philosophical arc in the blue note thread: from the fully documented (degree 0, Jones's recorded bass lines) to the fully undocumented (degree 33, English's unrecorded performances). The blue note has now traversed the entire spectrum of documentation. Thirty-three deployments, thirty-three mechanisms -- and the thirty-third is the mechanism of absence, the note that cannot be heard again because it was never preserved.

## The Significance of Degree 33

### Why This Degree Matters

Degree 33 matters not despite the documentation gap but because of it. The engine's commitment to documenting the Central District's musical culture requires acknowledging that most of that culture was never documented. The famous musicians -- Jones, Charles, Hendrix -- emerged from a musical ecosystem composed overwhelmingly of musicians like Larry English: local performers whose nightly work in the clubs created the conditions from which the famous emerged.

Every scene is a pyramid. The visible peak requires an invisible base. By placing Larry English at degree 33 with an E=5 energy rating and the explicit "Central District" neighborhood designation, the engine makes a structural statement: the base matters as much as the peak. The local musician is not a lesser degree; the local musician is the most representative degree. For every Jones, Charles, or Hendrix who left the Central District and achieved global recognition, there were fifty, a hundred, two hundred Larry Englishes who stayed, who played, who were the scene.

The engine documents what it can and names what it cannot document. This degree is a named space for the unnamed.
