### Godspeed: The Orbit and the Groove

Mercury-Atlas 6 / Friendship 7 — February 20, 1962. John Herschel Glenn Jr. became the first American to orbit Earth. Three orbits. Four hours, 55 minutes. The flight that changed everything — not because it was first (Gagarin, Titov, and Enos had all orbited before Glenn) but because Glenn's three orbits proved that American technology, American astronauts, and American institutions could sustain orbital operations. The orbit is a commitment: once you're circling, you keep circling until the physics or the pilot says otherwise.

Polyrhythmics' groove is an orbit. An eight-piece instrumental funk band locked into a polyrhythmic pocket is a self-sustaining system — drums, bass, percussion, guitar, keys, trumpet, trombone, and saxophone all contributing gravitational pull that keeps the others in trajectory. The groove does not require continuous new energy input. Once locked, it sustains. Glenn's three orbits were sustained by the Atlas rocket's initial boost and the physics of orbital mechanics. Polyrhythmics' groove is sustained by the initial pocket-lock and the physics of rhythmic entrainment — eight musicians' internal clocks synchronized into one collective pulse.

Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) — the King — is the largest Pacific salmon, making the longest migration (ocean to headwaters, thousands of kilometers), the ultimate committed journey. The salmon does not orbit — it migrates, one direction, one purpose, no return. But the commitment is total. Once the salmon enters freshwater, it does not eat. It burns its own body to fuel the upstream journey. Glenn's commitment to orbit was similar: once the Atlas ignited, there was no abort option that returned you to the launch pad. You were going around or you were going into the ocean. The groove's commitment is the same: once the band locks into the pocket, breaking the groove is a catastrophic event, the musical equivalent of an abort.
