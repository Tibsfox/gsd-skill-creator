## Biography & The Groove Collective

Polyrhythmics is a seven-to-eight-piece instrumental ensemble founded in late 2010 in Seattle, Washington, by guitarist **Ben Bloom** and drummer **Grant Schroff**. The band was not conceived as a band at all -- it began as a **recording project**. Bloom and Schroff gathered some of the most talented performers in the Pacific Northwest to record a single for a vinyl release, intending to put out a 45 and move on. What happened next changed everything: the celebratory release show revealed an energy that could not be contained in a studio. Dancing crowds, enthusiastic bodies on the floor, spontaneous groove -- the recording project became a live band, and the live band became one of Seattle's most consistently powerful musical exports.

The current lineup includes **Ben Bloom** (guitar), **Grant Schroff** (drums), **Nathan Spicer** (keyboards), **Jason Gray** (bass), **Lalo Bello** (percussion), **Scott Morning** (trumpet), **Elijah Clark** (trombone), and **Art Brown** (saxophone and flute). Earlier lineups included additional percussionist Karl Olson. The ensemble is **all male** -- the gender gap continues at degree 21 (see Gender Gap section below).

The two co-founders came from different musical communities within the Pacific Northwest. Ben Bloom had been performing and recording 45s with arranger and composer Joel Ricci (aka Lucky Brown) and his "West Sound Union," playing in various soul, funk, rock, and R&B collectives around Seattle. Grant Schroff, a **graduate of Cornish College of the Arts**, had been performing in jazz, funk, hip-hop, and reggae groups throughout the city. The Cornish connection extends the engine's **Cornish pipeline** to four members: Julian Priester (degree 7, faculty), Hadley Caliman (degree 9, faculty), Reggie Watts (degree 17, student), and now Grant Schroff (degree 21, graduate). This is the first time the Cornish pipeline has appeared outside the faculty context -- Schroff is a graduate, not a teacher.

The band's sound originated in Seattle's **underground deep funk scene**, combining what their press materials describe as "impossibly tight grooves with bold brass and hypnotic percussion." The initial palette drew from R&B, progressive jazz, and Afrobeat -- a compound that evolved over fifteen years into what can only be described as **psych-funk**: cinematic, trance-inducing, rhythmically layered instrumental music that fills a room with mood and movement.

(Source: Polyrhythmics official website; KEXP Q&A 2013; KNKX Public Radio 2025; Color Red Music; Royal Artist Group; The Upful LIFE Podcast Episode 093; Pedal of the Day 2017; Music Festivals Podcast)

### The Name: What Polyrhythm Means

The name "Polyrhythmics" is not a brand -- it is a **thesis statement**. Polyrhythm, in music theory, is the simultaneous use of two or more rhythms that are not readily perceived as deriving from one another or as simple manifestations of the same meter. The most foundational polyrhythmic cell in sub-Saharan African music is the **3:2 cross-rhythm** -- three evenly-spaced beats against two -- also known as a **hemiola**. This 3:2 relationship and its permutations form the foundation of most polyrhythmic textures in West African music, which in turn form the rhythmic DNA of funk, jazz, Afrobeat, and the entire diaspora of groove-based music.

The band's name announces its organizing principle: rhythm is not accompaniment, rhythm is the composition itself. Where jazz traditionally foregrounds melody and harmony (the horn line, the chord changes), and where rock foregrounds dynamics and power (the riff, the wall of sound), Polyrhythmics foregrounds **rhythm as primary structure**. The groove is the song. Everything else -- brass melodies, guitar textures, keyboard washes -- exists in service of the rhythmic conversation happening between drums, bass, percussion, and the internal pulse of every instrument.

This makes Polyrhythmics the engine's first **rhythm-primary** ensemble. Every prior degree, even the funk-adjacent ones (Maktub at degree 18, Fly Moon Royale at degree 19), placed rhythm in dialogue with other compositional elements -- lyrics, vocal melody, harmonic sophistication. Polyrhythmics is instrumental: there are no lyrics to share compositional authority, no vocalist to claim the foreground. The groove is sovereign.

- **Nectar Lounge** -- Seattle's largest indoor/outdoor music venue, handmade in 2004, with globally-minded programming focusing on punk, hip-hop, reggae, Americana, Latin, and funk acts. This is the kind of venue where a band like Polyrhythmics would develop its live audience.
- **Hidden Hall** -- a new performance venue opened in 2025, converted from the former Brouwer's Cafe space by Nectar Lounge founder Jed Smithson and his team. A genre-bending live music space described by KNKX as an "ideal hall."

Fremont is not a jazz neighborhood in the Central District sense -- it has no equivalent of the Jackson Street corridor's historical Black musical culture. But it is a **music-making neighborhood** with infrastructure for recording, performing, buying instruments, buying records, and organizing festivals. Where the Central District was a neighborhood that produced musicians, Fremont is a neighborhood that **equips** musicians. The distinction matters.

The Fremont connection to Polyrhythmics is both residential and infrastructural. The band emerged from Seattle's underground deep funk scene, which thrived in exactly the kind of genre-agnostic venues that Fremont provides. The 45 RPM vinyl culture that launched the band (recording a single for a vinyl release) connects to Fremont's record stores and the physical-media ecosystem that deep funk depends on.

**The release** is the twenty-second acoustic element. In the ADSR (attack-decay-sustain-release) envelope model that describes every sound, the release is the final phase -- the moment when the key is lifted, the breath ends, the string is damped, and the sound decays to silence. But in Polyrhythmics' music, the release is something more complex and more powerful than mere decay.

In funk, the release is **the drop** -- the moment when accumulated rhythmic tension resolves into a new groove section, when the horns cut out and the bass drops, when the full ensemble locks into the pocket after a build. It is not silence (that was degree 0). It is not sustain (that was degree 20). The release is the **controlled letting-go** that allows the next groove cycle to begin. In Fela Kuti's music -- the primary influence Polyrhythmics cite -- the release is the moment after twenty minutes of hypnotic circular groove when the pattern shifts, the new section enters, and the trance deepens rather than breaks. The release does not end the music; it **transforms** it.

The placement after sustain (20) completes the ADSR envelope that has been implicitly building:
- **Attack:** the engine's opening -- silence broken by sound (degrees 0-3)
- **Decay:** the initial richness settling into sustained patterns (degrees 4-10)
- **Sustain:** the held tone, the busker's endurance (degrees 11-20, culminating in Monge's raw sustain)
- **Release:** the letting-go that is also a renewal (degree 21)

The ADSR mapping is not forced -- it emerges from the progression. Twenty-two elements, and the sound envelope is complete. What follows degree 21 will be a new envelope cycle, a new beginning. The release is the hinge.

In Polyrhythmics' practice, the release manifests as the **groove reset** -- the moment between sections when the full ensemble releases the current pattern and locks into the next. Ben Bloom describes their method as creating "circular and oval shaped grooves" that put the listener in a trance. The release is what happens at the boundary between circles: the old circle ends, the new one begins, and the transition itself is a moment of controlled freedom where anything could happen before the new groove locks in.

## The Energy Threshold: First E=4 Artist

### The Most Significant Energy Event in the Engine

Degree 21 is the **first energy-4 artist** in the Seattle 360 engine. The energy-3 streak (degrees 15-20, six consecutive) is broken not by a return to lower energy but by an **escalation**. The energy distribution shifts from **2:13:6:0** to **2:13:6:1** (E1:E2:E3:E4).

This is the most significant energy event since the engine started for several reasons:

1. **The energy ceiling rises.** For twenty-one degrees, the engine's maximum energy was 3. Now it is 4. The headroom has expanded. Degrees 22+ will have artists at E=4 and eventually higher -- the CSV shows the energy curve climbing through the funk and rock zones toward E=7-10 in the grunge/punk/metal quadrant.

2. **The transition is not gradual -- it is a threshold.** Six consecutive energy-3 degrees built pressure. The jump to E=4 is not a smooth curve but a **step function**. The engine's energy is quantized, and the quantum just jumped.

3. **The mechanism changes.** Energy-3 artists project through individual effort: trumpet projection (Marriott), electronic drive (Lorber), performance art (Watts), collective groove (Maktub, FMR), solo sustain (Monge). Energy-4 Polyrhythmics projects through **ensemble mass** -- seven-to-eight musicians generating rhythmic density that no soloist or small group can achieve. The energy increase comes from adding bodies, instruments, and rhythmic layers simultaneously.

| Energy | Count | Degrees | Character |
|--------|-------|---------|-----------|
| 1 | 2 | 1, 2 | Atmospheric, textural, ambient |
| 2 | 13 | 0, 3-12, 14 | Standard jazz performance energy |
| 3 | 6 | 13, 15-20 | Assertive, projective, physical commitment |
| **4** | **1** | **21** | **Ensemble mass, rhythmic density, dance-floor energy** |

The energy-4 subtype at degree 21 is **ensemble-kinetic**: the energy comes from the combined rhythmic output of a large instrumental ensemble whose primary function is to move bodies. This is dance music without singing -- the groove itself is the energy source. Where energy-3 artists perform *at* an audience, energy-4 Polyrhythmics performs *with* the audience. The dancing crowd is part of the energy system; the band creates grooves that the audience completes through physical response.

## Career Arc

### Formation and Self-Titled Debut (2010-2011)

Ben Bloom and Grant Schroff founded Polyrhythmics in late 2010. The original intention was a recording project -- gather musicians, cut a 45 RPM vinyl single, and move on. The self-titled debut album **Polyrhythmics** was released in 2011. When the release show generated a dancing, ecstatic crowd response, the project became a permanent band. The transformation from studio project to live ensemble is a key structural fact: the band's identity was forged by audience response, not by artistic intention alone.

(Source: Polyrhythmics official website; Color Red Music; Last.fm biography)

### Labrador Lounge and Early Growth (2012)

The EP **Labrador Lounge** followed in 2012, establishing the band's instrumental identity: no vocalist, brass-forward, groove-centric. The seven-piece configuration solidified.

(Source: Discogs; Bandcamp)

### Libra Stripes (2013)

**Libra Stripes** was the band's second full-length album, released in 2013. EXCLAIM! gave it 8/10, calling it "among the best of the genre, a must-have for fans." City Arts named it Album of the Month in November 2013. This was the album that established Polyrhythmics as a force beyond Seattle -- the first national critical attention.

Ben Bloom told KEXP in a 2013 Q&A: the band plays "dance music without singing," and their goal is "to create a trance for listeners by the use of circular and oval shaped grooves." The Fela Kuti influence is explicit: "Fela would do these single bass lines for, like, 20 minutes and get the audience real tranced out. Then he would start talking, singing his messages and bringing you in to tell you the story. He would put you in a trance to deliver the ideas to you."

(Source: KEXP Q&A 2013; EXCLAIM!; City Arts; Bandcamp)

### Octagon (2015)

The third album, **Octagon**, earned the band tour dates around the country and critical acclaim. The eight-track album (one for each band member -- the title is structural) expanded the palette into more psychedelic territory while maintaining the Afrobeat foundation. KEXP featured the band for a live performance in 2016, playing tracks including "Maruken," "Fairweather Fiends," "Chingador," and "Lord Of The Fries."

(Source: Discogs; KEXP; Jambase)

### Caldera (2017)

**Caldera** is ranked as the best Polyrhythmics album by Best Ever Albums and represents the band at their most cinematic. KNKX described the album as showcasing "the instrumental eight-piece's impossibly tight grooves and virtuosic musicianship as they tear through a singular blend of funk, soul, psychedelic rock, R&B, progressive jazz, and Afrobeat, calling to mind everything from Antibalas and the Dap-Kings to The Meters and Fela Kuti." The comparisons are significant: Antibalas (Brooklyn's Afrobeat orchestra), The Dap-Kings (Sharon Jones' backing band, the heart of the 2000s funk revival), The Meters (New Orleans' foundational funk band), and Fela Kuti (the originator of Afrobeat). This places Polyrhythmics in an international lineage of ensemble funk that transcends any single city.

(Source: KNKX 2017; Best Ever Albums; Discogs; Bandcamp)

### Man from the Future (2020) and Fondue Party EP (2020)

**Man from the Future** was released on **Color Red Music**, the label founded by bassist Eddie Roberts of The New Mastersounds. The album marks Polyrhythmics' "most evocative and cinematic" work. Glide Magazine's review noted the band "explore a variety of funk sounds from psychedelic to jazzy grooves," and the album "transcends any type of formula and creates vibe pockets." The companion EP **Fondue Party** (December 2020) collected tracks from the same sessions, exploring the group's downtempo, chilled-out side with heavy dub influence.

Ben Bloom performed the February 2020 KEXP session in support of the album, opening with "Digital Cowboy" and discussing the band's 10th anniversary.

(Source: Glide Magazine; FunkCity.net; Color Red Music; KEXP 2020; Bandcamp; Jambase)

### Filter System (2023)

The seventh album, **Filter System**, was released on September 29, 2023. The title refers to the band's **democratic songwriting process**: every member writes and arranges, and the "filter system" is the collective process by which individual ideas are filtered through the ensemble into finished compositions. The KEXP November 2023 session showcased the new material.

The album was recorded live in studio, preserving the energy of the band's live performance. The democratic composition process is a key organizational feature: unlike Maktub (degree 18), where Reggie Watts served as a clear creative center, Polyrhythmics distributes compositional authority across all members.

(Source: Jambase; Bandcamp; KEXP 2023; Discogs)

### Life from Below (2025) and the Adryon de Leon Collaboration

**Life from Below**, released September 12, 2025, celebrates the band's **fifteenth anniversary**. FunkCity.net's review praised the album: "Each tune is distinctive in its color and theme while still recognizable as a Polyrhythmics song." The album was recorded live in studio and features the track "Smoke & Mirrors," the band's **first full studio collaboration with a vocalist** -- powerhouse singer **Adryon de Leon**. This is significant: after fifteen years and seven albums of purely instrumental music, the band introduced a vocal element. The track is described as having "early '80s funk bounce and vintage dancefloor energy."

Other standout tracks include "Mayo con Yayo" and "Chophouse." Art Brown is highlighted on flute on the track "Interlube," with the trumpet-trombone combo bookending his lead.

The KNKX August 2025 studio session celebrated the album and the fifteen-year milestone. Ben Bloom described the band's evolving approach: "What do we want to come up with that's cool?"

(Source: FunkCity.net review 2025; KNKX 2025; Jambase; Bandcamp; Discogs)

### KEXP and the Zion-Gorman Mashup (2021)

A remarkable cultural moment: KEXP created an audio mashup of **Amanda Gorman's 2021 Presidential Inauguration poem** "The Hill We Climb" with Polyrhythmics' instrumental track "Zion." The mashup connected Gorman's poetry to Polyrhythmics' groove, demonstrating the band's music as a **vehicle for meaning beyond the purely musical**. The track "Zion" was not written for this purpose -- it was Polyrhythmics' instrumental composition that KEXP's producers recognized as the right sonic bed for Gorman's words. This is a rare case of instrumental music acquiring explicit political resonance through external curation.

(Source: KEXP; Live For Live Music)

## Music Theory Mapping: Polyrhythm and Polymetric Structure

### Primary Technique: Polyrhythmic Layering as Compositional Architecture

Degree 21's primary music theory contribution is **polyrhythmic layering** -- the technique of simultaneously stacking multiple independent rhythmic patterns that interact to create emergent grooves more complex than any single pattern. This is not syncopation (displacement within a single meter), not swing (asymmetric subdivision of beats), not ostinato (a single repeating pattern). It is the **simultaneous operation of multiple rhythmic systems** within a single performance.

The technique operates at several levels in Polyrhythmics' music:

1. **Drums vs. percussion (3:2 and 4:3 cross-rhythms).** Grant Schroff's drum kit operates in one rhythmic framework while Lalo Bello's percussion operates in another. The two rhythmic streams are independent but phase-locked -- they share downbeats at regular intervals but diverge between them. This is the West African 3:2 cross-rhythm applied to a funk context.

2. **Bass vs. drums (the pocket).** Jason Gray's bass lines often operate in a slightly different rhythmic subdivision than the drums, creating the characteristic "pocket" of deep funk -- the groove feels simultaneously tight and loose, locked and free.

3. **Brass section vs. rhythm section (call-and-response as polyrhythm).** The horn section (Morning, Clark, Brown) plays riff patterns that operate in a different rhythmic cycle than the rhythm section (Bloom, Schroff, Gray, Bello, Spicer). This is the Afrobeat model: the horns state a rhythmic theme, the rhythm section states another, and the listener's ear resolves them into a composite groove.

4. **Guitar and keyboards as rhythmic mediators.** Bloom's guitar and Spicer's keyboards occupy a middle ground -- sometimes aligning with the rhythm section, sometimes with the horns, sometimes introducing a third rhythmic layer that bridges or complicates the existing two.

The result is a **polymetric texture** where the groove is not a single rhythmic pattern but a **field** of interlocking patterns. The listener does not hear one beat; the listener hears a rhythmic ecosystem. This is why the band's name is their thesis: the rhythm *is* the composition.

### Trance Through Rhythm: The Fela Method

Ben Bloom's stated method -- creating a trance through "circular and oval shaped grooves" -- maps directly to Fela Kuti's Afrobeat practice. In Kuti's music, a single groove can persist for twenty minutes or more, with the polyrhythmic layers creating a hypnotic cycle that puts the listener into a receptive state. The groove is not background; the groove is the message delivery system.

Polyrhythmics adapts this method for a Western instrumental context: instead of Fela's politically charged Yoruba-English lyrics delivered over the groove, Polyrhythmics delivers melodic content (horn lines, guitar figures, keyboard washes) over the groove. The trance function is preserved: the polyrhythmic foundation puts the listener/dancer into a receptive state, and the melodic content enters through that opening. The release (acoustic element 22) is the moment when the trance cycle resets -- the old groove pattern releases, the new one locks in, and the trance deepens.

### The Democratic Filter System

The band's compositional method -- what they call the "filter system" -- is itself a polyrhythmic process. Every member writes and arranges. Individual ideas enter the ensemble, and the group collectively filters them through rehearsal and performance until only what serves the groove survives. This is composition-as-polyrhythm: multiple creative voices operating simultaneously, with the ensemble resolving them into a unified output. No single member controls the composition; the composition emerges from the interaction of all members.

This organizational principle connects to the music theory: a band that composes polyrhythmically is more likely to produce polyrhythmic music. The filter system is not just a workflow; it is an aesthetic expressed through organizational structure.

## Genre Evolution: Jazz/Funk as Fifth Stage

### The Genre Breaks New Ground

Jazz/Funk at degree 21 introduces a new compound that is **not** a continuation of the jazz-as-modifier pattern. In degrees 18-20, Jazz served as a modifier for other genres (Soul/Jazz, Jazz/R&B). At degree 21, Jazz/Funk is a genuine **compound** where both elements are co-equal: the jazz heritage provides harmonic sophistication and improvisational practice, while the funk provides the rhythmic foundation and the primary energy.

The genre evolution:

| Stage | Degrees | Character |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| 1. Single genre | 0-15 | Jazz as primary identity |
| 2. Compound | 16 | Jazz Fusion (Jazz + electronic) |
| 3. Superposition | 17 | Jazz/Comedy (genre uncertainty) |
| 4. Modifier-inversion | 18-20 | Jazz as secondary (Soul, R&B) |
| **5. Funk-primary compound** | **21** | **Jazz/Funk -- funk as dominant, jazz as equal partner** |

The fifth stage is significant because it represents the first degree where **funk** is named as a genre component. Funk has been present as an influence throughout the engine (Berry's groove at degree 5, Lorber's electronic funk at degree 16, Maktub's neo-soul at degree 18), but degree 21 is the first time funk appears in the genre label itself. The engine has entered the funk zone.

Jazz/Funk is distinct from Jazz Fusion (degree 16) in several ways: Fusion emphasizes electronic textures and harmonic complexity, while Jazz/Funk emphasizes rhythmic density and groove. Fusion asks: what happens when jazz meets electronics? Jazz/Funk asks: what happens when jazz meets the dance floor?

## The Gender Gap Continues

Polyrhythmics is an **all-male ensemble**. The gender distribution remains **3:19** (women:men/male-led) through twenty-two degrees. Whitney Monge's gap-breaking at degree 20 was a single-degree event; the male dominance resumes immediately.

The gender gap at degree 21 is neither unexpected nor excusable. Seattle's instrumental funk scene, like most instrumental music scenes, has been disproportionately male in its visible ensembles. The engine documents what the scene produced; the gap is the scene's. But the engine must continue tracking it honestly.

Note: The band's collaboration with vocalist **Adryon de Leon** on "Smoke & Mirrors" (2025) introduces a female voice into the Polyrhythmics recording catalog for the first time. This is not band membership but it is a significant gender moment in the band's history.
