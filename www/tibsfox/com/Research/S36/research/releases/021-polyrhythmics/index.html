Biography & The Groove Collective

Polyrhythmics is a seven-to-eight-piece instrumental ensemble founded in late 2010 in Seattle by guitarist Ben Bloom and drummer Grant Schroff. The band was not conceived as a band — it began as a recording project. Bloom and Schroff gathered the best available players to record a 45 RPM vinyl single. The celebratory release show generated such an ecstatic crowd response that the recording project became a permanent live band.

Members: Ben Bloom (guitar), Grant Schroff (drums), Nathan Spicer (keys), Jason Gray (bass), Lalo Bello (percussion), Scott Morning (trumpet), Elijah Clark (trombone), Art Brown (sax/flute). All male — gender gap continues (3:19).

Schroff graduated from Cornish College of the Arts, extending the Cornish pipeline to four members: Priester (7° faculty), Caliman (9° faculty), Watts (17° student), Schroff (21° graduate).

Career Timeline

Founded as a recording project by Ben Bloom and Grant Schroff. Self-titled debut album (2011). First release show transforms the studio project into a live band.

EP release establishing the instrumental, brass-forward, groove-centric identity.

Second full-length. EXCLAIM! 8/10: “among the best of the genre.” City Arts Album of the Month. First national critical attention.

Ranked as best album. KNKX: “impossibly tight grooves… calling to mind Antibalas, the Dap-Kings, The Meters, and Fela Kuti.”

KEXP mashup of Polyrhythmics’ “Zion” with Amanda Gorman’s inauguration poem. Instrumental music acquires explicit political resonance through external curation.

Music Theory Mapping

Polyrhythmic Layering

Advanced

Simultaneous stacking of multiple independent rhythmic patterns that interact to create emergent grooves. Drums vs. percussion (3:2 cross-rhythm), bass vs. drums (the pocket), brass vs. rhythm section (Afrobeat call-and-response), guitar/keys as rhythmic mediators. The groove is not a single pattern but a field of interlocking patterns.

Fela Kuti’s 20-minute circular grooves → Polyrhythmics’ “circular and oval shaped grooves” creating trance states. The Meters’ pocket funk. Antibalas’ Afrobeat horn cycles.
Ancestors: West African 3:2 cross-rhythm → Fela Kuti Afrobeat → The Meters New Orleans funk → Antibalas Brooklyn Afrobeat → Seattle deep funk scene → Polyrhythmics

Groove-as-Composition (Ensemble)

Advanced

The groove is sovereign — no vocals, no lyrics to share compositional authority. Rhythm is the primary structure; melody and harmony serve the groove. Third entry in the groove-as-composition thread: Berry (5° solo piano), Maktub (18° band), Polyrhythmics (21° ensemble polyrhythm).

Berry’s piano groove (solo, E=2) → Maktub’s neo-soul groove (band, E=3) → Polyrhythmics’ polyrhythmic groove (ensemble, E=4). Each escalation adds musicians and rhythmic layers.
Ancestors: Overton Berry (5°) piano-as-groove → Maktub (18°) band-groove → Polyrhythmics (21°) ensemble-polyrhythm

Democratic Filter System

Intermediate

Every member writes and arranges. Individual ideas are filtered through collective rehearsal until only what serves the groove survives. Composition-as-polyrhythm: multiple creative voices resolving into unified output. No single member controls the composition.

Contrast with Maktub (18°): Watts as centered creative engine vs. Polyrhythmics’ distributed authority. Contrast with FMR (19°): rotating membership vs. stable ensemble.
Ancestors: Collective jazz improvisation tradition → Afrobeat ensemble composition → Deep funk democratic writing → Polyrhythmics filter system

Blue Note — Funk-Brass

Intermediate

Twenty-first deployment of the blue note thread. Funk-brass blue note: horn section bending between minor and major thirds in ensemble funk context. The blue note as collective expression rather than individual voice. Three horns (trumpet, trombone, sax) share the bending.

Twenty-one mechanisms documented: bass-line (0°), guitar (1°), vocal (3°), through americana-soul (20°), now funk-brass (21°). Twenty-one deployments across twenty-two degrees.
Ancestors: All twenty prior deployments → brass-specific: assertive brass (15° Marriott) → funk-brass ensemble blue note (21° Polyrhythmics)

Acoustic Element #22: The Release

In the ADSR envelope, the release is the final phase — the controlled letting-go. In Polyrhythmics’ music, the release is the groove reset: the moment between sections when the old pattern releases and the new one locks in. The trance deepens rather than breaks.

After sustain (20°, Monge’s raw vocal endurance), the release is the transformation that allows the next cycle to begin. The ADSR envelope that has been implicitly building across twenty-two degrees is now complete: Attack (0–3), Decay (4–10), Sustain (11–20), Release (21).

Artist-City Pattern #22: The Ensemble Anchor

A large, stable ensemble that forms from within the existing music scene, gathers the best available players, develops a distinctive collective identity through years of live performance, and becomes a fixture. The band did not create a new scene; it crystallized the existing deep funk scene into a permanent, touring, recording entity.

Differs from Institutional Anchor (Seales, 13°): Seales anchored through a university position. Polyrhythmics anchors through ensemble mass and fifteen years of continuous performance. No institution appointed them; they became the institution.
