## THE NORTHWEST SOUND INHERITANCE -- 241-244 TO 251

### The Garage Tradition Returns in New Hands

The engine documented the original Northwest Sound at degrees 241-244 (The Sonics, The Wailers, The Frantics, The Kingsmen). Naked Giants arrive at degree 251 as the tradition's current practitioners:

- **What changed in 50+ years.** The original Northwest Sound (1963-1965) emerged from the collision of rock and roll, R&B, surf music, and the raw performance culture of the Pacific Northwest dance hall circuit. Bands played loud because the rooms were loud, played fast because the dances demanded energy, and recorded cheap because nobody offered them money to do otherwise. The aesthetic was partly choice and partly circumstance.
- **Contemporary garage rock.** The garage rock revival of the 2000s-2010s (The White Stripes, The Strokes, Black Keys, and many others) created a contemporary aesthetic context in which the raw production and simple song structures of 1960s garage were chosen deliberately rather than imposed by circumstance. Naked Giants exist in this contemporary context -- they are not recreating the Northwest Sound but practicing a living tradition that the Northwest Sound helped establish.
- **Sub Pop as connective tissue.** The original Northwest Sound bands were on Jerden Records, Etiquette Records, and Wand Records -- small Pacific Northwest labels that served the regional dance circuit. Sub Pop is the successor institution: the PNW label that gives Seattle bands national and international reach. Naked Giants on Sub Pop in 2013 is the structural equivalent of The Sonics on Etiquette Records in 1964 -- the band and the label are both the current version of the same regional rock tradition.
- **Three-piece vs. four/five-piece.** The Sonics were a five-piece; The Kingsmen were a five-piece. Naked Giants are a three-piece -- guitar, bass, drums, with all three singing. The stripped-down format reflects contemporary garage rock's aesthetic preference for minimal personnel and maximum energy per person. Three people making E=7 noise.
- **The psychedelic thread.** Naked Giants incorporate psychedelic influences (reverb, fuzz, tempo variation, extended passages) in ways that the original Northwest Sound bands generally did not. The 1960s psychedelic era is now a reference the way the 1950s blues was a reference to the Sonics -- upstream influence, synthesized into the present.

(Sources: Northwest Sound 1963-1965; Sonics, Wailers, Kingsmen context; garage rock revival 2000s-2010s; contemporary vs. original garage aesthetic; Sub Pop as institutional successor to regional labels; three-piece format; psychedelic incorporation)

## NAKED GIANTS -- THE BAND

### Grant Mullen, Gianni Aiello, Henry LaVallee

The three members are equal contributors to the band's sound:

- **Grant Mullen.** Guitar, vocals. Mullen's guitar work is the band's primary sonic driver -- high-energy, riff-forward, with the kind of rhythmic insistence that comes from garage rock's dance-hall origins. He writes songs with the three-minute discipline of the garage tradition while incorporating the more expansive psychedelic gestures that distinguish Naked Giants from straight revivalism.
- **Gianni Aiello.** Bass, vocals. The bass role in Naked Giants is not the subordinate role it often is in rock bands -- Aiello's bass playing is melodic and active, providing harmonic motion under the guitar rather than just supporting the root notes. In a three-piece, the bass carries more weight than in a larger band.
- **Henry LaVallee.** Drums, vocals. LaVallee's drumming drives the band's energy. Garage rock demands a drummer who can maintain the physical intensity of the performance while allowing the song to breathe. His approach is forceful without being mechanical.
- **Three-part vocals.** All three members sing -- a characteristic of bands where the voice is an equal instrument in the ensemble rather than a featured lead over backing. This creates a different kind of sound than a band with a dedicated frontman: more democratic, more varied, with the possibility of three-part harmony as well as three distinct vocal textures.
- **Formation in Seattle, 2013.** The band formed in Seattle at the same time as The Black Tones (249). 2013 was a productive year for Seattle rock: multiple bands that would go on to Sub Pop deals were beginning to play shows together, developing in the same club ecosystem. Naked Giants and The Black Tones are contemporaries who represent different traditions (garage rock vs. blues rock) in the same city at the same moment.

(Sources: Grant Mullen guitar; Gianni Aiello bass; Henry LaVallee drums; three-part vocals; three-piece format; 2013 formation; Seattle music ecosystem contemporaries)

## THE SOUND -- GARAGE ROCK WITH PSYCHEDELIC AND SURF TEXTURES, E=7

### More Than One Genre at Once

Naked Giants' music is not garage rock in the reductive sense:

- **The garage foundation.** The fundamental approach is garage: short songs, loud guitars, insistent rhythm, raw energy. The garage tradition's essential qualities -- physical directness, three-chord accessibility, performance-first recording approach -- are present in Naked Giants' music.
- **The psychedelic layer.** Fuzz pedals applied not for aggression (as in grunge) or for texture (as in Love Battery at degree 221) but for color -- to make the guitar sound different, to shift the harmonic context, to signal movement between song sections. Reverb as space rather than atmospherics. Tempo variation suggesting the psychedelic tradition's interest in altered time perception.
- **The surf connection.** Surf rock's guitar vocabulary -- the specific picking technique, the spring reverb unit, the melodic patterns that suggest movement and water -- runs through certain Naked Giants songs. This is appropriate: the PNW has a genuine surf connection (proximity to Pacific Ocean surfing culture, the beach towns of the Washington coast, the Ventures' origins at degree 239) and surf rock's influence on garage rock is historically documented.
- **E=7 delivery.** The combined energy is E=7 -- hard rock without the metal intensity, present without the ambience of E=4-5. Naked Giants play loud, they play fast, they move between ideas without dwelling. The garage rock aesthetic at E=7 is about sustaining physical energy through a set without sacrificing song-level clarity.
- **Song length discipline.** Garage rock values short songs -- three minutes, two and a half, occasionally four. The format demands economy. Naked Giants work within this tradition: songs that make their point and stop. The psychedelic passages are brief expansions, not extended explorations.

(Sources: Garage rock as foundation; fuzz application for color vs. aggression; reverb as space; surf rock vocabulary; PNW surf connection; Ventures at degree 239; E=7 delivery; song length discipline)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Sub Pop Catalog

- ***SLUFF* (2016, Sub Pop).** The debut full-length. The album establishes the Naked Giants template: garage rock with psychedelic color, high energy, short songs, three-part vocals. The record announces the band's approach cleanly.
- ***MIJITA* (2019, Sub Pop).** The second full-length. More varied, more confident. The band demonstrates range within their foundational approach -- slower songs, faster songs, more psychedelic passages, harder riffs. The second album is where bands typically either deepen or diffuse their identity; Naked Giants deepened.
- ***Sluff* reissue and ongoing work.** Sub Pop has continued to support the band beyond the initial album cycle. Singles and EPs maintain the band's presence between full-lengths.
- **Live reputation.** Naked Giants have built their reputation substantially on live performance -- the garage rock tradition rewards live experience, where the physical energy of the performance is most directly felt. Their live shows are reportedly high-energy and consistent, which is the marker of a band that has internalized its material.

(Sources: SLUFF 2016; MIJITA 2019; Sub Pop catalog; live performance reputation; single and EP releases)

## CULTURAL CONTEXT -- SEATTLE GARAGE IN THE 2010S

### When Garage Came Back

The context for Naked Giants' formation in 2013:

- **The garage revival was fully underway.** The early 2000s garage revival (White Stripes, Strokes, Hives) had already established the template for contemporary garage rock -- raw production, simple songs, high energy, small band. By 2013, this was an established aesthetic context, not a novelty. Naked Giants formed into a world where garage rock was understood and valued.
- **Seattle's current club ecosystem.** The Tractor Tavern, The Crocodile, Chop Suey, Neumos -- the club circuit where Naked Giants developed their live show is the same circuit where many of the engine's previous entries developed theirs. Seattle's club ecosystem has been remarkably consistent as an incubator for rock bands across multiple decades.
- **Sub Pop's scouting function.** Sub Pop has consistently identified and signed bands from the Seattle club circuit. Their signing of Naked Giants represents the label's ongoing function as the connector between the local scene and national/international audiences.
- **Post-grunge identity.** Bands forming in Seattle in 2013 have a complex relationship to grunge's legacy. The grunge era has been over for 20 years by the time Naked Giants form. They are not reacting to it; they are working in a post-grunge Seattle that has absorbed that history and moved forward. The garage rock tradition is older than grunge and more durable; Naked Giants' choice of that tradition reflects a longer historical arc.

(Sources: Early 2000s garage revival; contemporary garage aesthetic by 2013; Seattle club ecosystem; Sub Pop scouting function; post-grunge Seattle rock identity)
