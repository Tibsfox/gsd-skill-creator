## SABZI -- HIP-HOP/ELECTRONIC PRODUCTION -- THE SAMPLE ARCHITECT

### The Producer

**Saba Mohajerjasbi**, known universally as **Sabzi**, is a Seattle hip-hop producer, beatmaker, and electronic musician. Born to Iranian immigrant parents, raised in the Pacific Northwest, and educated at the **University of Washington**, Sabzi has been one of the foundational figures in Seattle's hip-hop production scene since the early 2000s.

Sabzi's significance in the Seattle 360 engine is unique: he has already appeared at **two prior degrees** through his collaborative work:
- **Degree 99: Blue Scholars** -- Sabzi is the producer half of the duo, with emcee Geo (Prometheus Brown / George Quibuyen). Blue Scholars defined Seattle conscious hip-hop in the 2000s.
- **Degree 101: Common Market** -- Sabzi is the producer half of this duo as well, with emcee RA Scion (Ryan Abeo). Common Market expanded the jazz-rap territory.

At degree 122, the engine documents **Sabzi as a solo production identity** -- the beats, the samples, the electronic landscapes that he creates under his own name. This is the architect examined separately from the buildings he designed for others.

### Iranian-American Identity and Seattle

Sabzi's Iranian-American background is a significant dimension of his artistic identity. He is one of the most prominent Iranian-American musicians in hip-hop, and his production style reflects a **cross-cultural synthesis**:

1. **Middle Eastern melodic sensibility.** While Sabzi's production is rooted in American hip-hop and jazz sampling, there are melodic lines and harmonic choices that echo Persian musical traditions -- minor modes, ornamental melodic figures, a certain melancholy warmth that runs through even his most upbeat productions.

2. **Immigrant narrative.** The themes explored by Blue Scholars and Common Market -- social justice, community, the experience of navigating between cultures -- are informed by Sabzi's perspective as a child of immigrants. The beats carry these themes: the samples chosen, the moods evoked, the spaces created.

3. **University of Washington.** Sabzi and Geo (Blue Scholars) met at UW. The university is the origin point for Seattle's most important conscious hip-hop collective. UW's diverse student body, its proximity to the International District and Capitol Hill, and its activist culture created the conditions for Blue Scholars' formation.

### The Production Style: Jazz-Influenced Sample Architecture

Sabzi's production style is built on several distinctive characteristics:

**1. Jazz sampling as foundation.** Sabzi is a **crate digger** in the truest sense -- his productions are built from jazz records, soul records, funk records, and international music. The jazz influence is primary: piano loops, horn stabs, bass lines, and rhythmic patterns drawn from the vast American jazz catalog. But Sabzi's sampling is not simple loop-and-layer; he **deconstructs and reconstructs** source material, isolating individual melodic phrases, chopping them into new rhythmic patterns, and rebuilding them into compositions that honor the source while creating something new.

**2. Layered beat construction.** A Sabzi production is recognizable by its **density of layers**. Multiple sample sources are stacked, blended, and arranged into richly textured instrumentals. Underneath the primary sample loop, secondary elements provide harmonic depth -- a faint piano ghost, a filtered guitar chord, a distant horn. The layering creates a sense of warmth and fullness that is Sabzi's signature.

**3. Drums as sculpted rhythm.** The drum programming is crisp and deliberate. Sabzi favors **boom-bap rhythmic foundations** (the classic hip-hop drum pattern: punchy kick, snapping snare, shuffling hi-hat) but treats them with careful attention to dynamics and texture. The drums are not merely timekeeping; they are musical elements with their own tonal character. Kick drums are warm and round. Snares have crack and body. Hi-hats shimmer.

**4. Melodic production.** Sabzi's beats are **melodic** -- they carry harmonic progressions and melodic hooks even without vocals. Many of his instrumentals could stand alone as listening music. This melodic quality distinguishes his production from harder, more percussion-driven hip-hop styles. The beats sing.

**5. Electronic expansion.** Under his own name, Sabzi has expanded beyond pure sample-based production into **electronic territory** -- synthesizers, drum machines, and digital processing that move beyond hip-hop's sample tradition toward electronic music's timbral exploration. This expansion makes the solo Sabzi catalog broader than the Blue Scholars or Common Market productions.

### Seattle Hip-Hop Production Scene

Sabzi exists within a broader Seattle hip-hop production ecosystem that deserves mapping:

**The crate-digging tradition.** Seattle's hip-hop producers have a strong tradition of sample-based production rooted in jazz, soul, and funk. The city's record stores (Easy Street Records, Everyday Music, Bop Street Records) provided the physical crates from which beats were dug. Sabzi is part of a lineage that includes Vitamin D, Jake One, Bean One, and others.

**The DIY infrastructure.** Seattle's hip-hop scene has been overwhelmingly independent and self-released. Mass Line Media (Blue Scholars' label) and similar artist-owned operations are the rule, not the exception. The DIY infrastructure means producers like Sabzi control their catalogs, their sound, and their release schedules.

**The UW pipeline.** The University of Washington has been a surprisingly important incubator for Seattle hip-hop. Blue Scholars, Common Market, and related artists emerged from UW's student community. The campus radio station (KEXP's predecessor and KEXP itself) provided early airplay. The university's cultural diversity created the conditions for cross-cultural musical synthesis.

**KEXP as accelerant.** KEXP's support for local hip-hop has been critical. The station's in-studio sessions gave Blue Scholars and Common Market exposure beyond the live circuit. Sabzi's production work reached ears through KEXP that it might not have reached through club shows alone.

Sabzi's solo production catalog includes:

- **Various production credits** across Seattle hip-hop releases -- beats provided for other artists beyond Blue Scholars and Common Market.
- **DJ sets and electronic performances** -- Sabzi's live presence beyond hip-hop, exploring electronic music territory.

The solo catalog is smaller than the collaborative discographies, but it reveals the **production identity stripped bare** -- no vocals to guide the listener, just the beats, samples, and arrangements that constitute Sabzi's artistry.

## Acoustic Profile: The Sample Architecture

Sabzi's production signature can be described as **sample architecture** -- the construction of richly layered, jazz-influenced beat landscapes from carefully selected and deconstructed source recordings.

### The Sound

- **Jazz samples:** piano loops, horn stabs, bass lines drawn from the American jazz catalog. The jazz influence is warm, sophisticated, harmonically rich. Not smooth jazz -- hard bop, modal jazz, the adventurous end of the tradition.
- **Layered construction:** multiple sample sources stacked and blended. Primary loops with secondary harmonic support. The production is dense but clear -- layers create depth, not muddiness.
- **Boom-bap drums:** punchy kick, snapping snare, shuffling hi-hat. Classic hip-hop rhythmic foundation with attention to dynamics and tonal character.
- **Melodic hooks:** the beats carry melody. Harmonic progressions move. Even instrumentals feel complete.
- **Electronic expansion:** synthesizers and digital processing extend beyond pure sampling into electronic territory. The solo work is broader than the collaborative productions.
- **Warmth:** the overall character is warm. Analog-leaning. The production sounds like records, like rooms, like instruments played by people -- even when constructed from samples and electronic elements.

### E=5 -- Active, Dense, Musical

E=5 reflects the **density and musical activity** of Sabzi's production. This is not background music or ambient atmosphere. The beat demands attention: rhythms are active, samples are melodically prominent, layers require listening depth. The energy comes from musical complexity -- multiple elements interacting, rhythmic drive, harmonic motion. E=5 is consistent across all three Sabzi-linked degrees (99, 101, 122), suggesting that the producer's energy level is a constant regardless of which emcee rides the beat.

The E=5 continuity across three degrees is a unique pattern in the engine: one artist, one energy level, three entries. The production energy is the invariant.

## Acoustic Element 123: The Sample Architecture

The **sample architecture** is the one hundred twenty-third acoustic element in the Seattle 360 engine. It describes the production technique of constructing richly layered beat landscapes from jazz and soul samples -- deconstructing source recordings into component parts and rebuilding them into new compositions that honor the source while creating something original.

### The Sound

- **Crate-dug source material** -- vinyl records as raw material. Jazz, soul, funk, international music. The production begins in the record store.
- **Deconstruction** -- isolating melodic phrases, rhythmic patterns, harmonic elements from source recordings. The sample is not a simple loop but a collection of extracted components.
- **Reconstruction** -- reassembling extracted components into new compositions. Multiple sources blended. Harmonic and melodic elements from different records combined into unified productions.
- **Layered density** -- the result is a richly textured instrumental that carries the warmth and musicality of its sources while being architecturally new.

### Contrasts with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **The moody pulse** (degree 121, Smokey Brights) -- synthesizer-forward, electronic atmosphere. The sample architecture is sample-forward, analog warmth. Both are layered and dense; the moody pulse is synthetic, the sample architecture is organic-sourced.
- **The bark and the whisper** (degree 120, Mark Lanegan) -- vocal-centric. The sample architecture is production-centric. Lanegan's artistry is in the voice; Sabzi's is in the beat.
- **Blue Scholars production** (degree 99) -- the same producer, applied to conscious hip-hop with Geo's emceeing. The sample architecture at 122 is the same technique examined independently of vocals.

The 123rd acoustic element. The engine adds the sound of jazz records dismantled and rebuilt into hip-hop landscapes. The sample architecture.
