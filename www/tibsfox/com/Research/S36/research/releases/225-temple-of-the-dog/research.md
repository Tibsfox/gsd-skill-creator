## THE ANDREW WOOD CONNECTION

### The Band That Grief Built

Temple of the Dog cannot be understood apart from Andrew Wood:

- **Andrew Wood (1966-1990).** Wood was the vocalist and central creative force of Mother Love Bone, the band formed in 1987 that combined Glam Rock theatricality with Seattle's emerging heavy sound. His lineup included Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament, who would go on to found Pearl Jam. Wood was a charismatic, visionary vocalist with an operatic range and a stage presence unlike anything else in Seattle. He was also a heroin addict.
- **The death.** Andrew Wood died on March 19, 1990, from a heroin overdose -- or more precisely, from the brain damage caused by the overdose. He had been in a coma for three days after being found unresponsive. He was 24 years old. Mother Love Bone's debut album *Apple* had been completed but not yet released. It shipped posthumously, in July 1990, months after Wood's death.
- **The shock.** Wood's death hit the Seattle music community like a physical blow. He had been the scene's most flamboyant optimist -- the one musician who seemed genuinely convinced that Seattle would become what it eventually did become. His death came just as the scene was gathering its commercial momentum.
- **Chris Cornell's response.** Cornell, Wood's roommate and close friend, wrote a series of songs as a direct response to Wood's death. The songs were too personal for Soundgarden -- they were not Soundgarden material, they were grief material. Cornell proposed recording them with Wood's former bandmates. The project became Temple of the Dog.

(Sources: Andrew Wood biography; Mother Love Bone history; Apple posthumous release; Chris Cornell roommate; Cornell grief response; heroin overdose March 19 1990)

## TEMPLE OF THE DOG -- THE BAND

### The Personnel

Temple of the Dog was a supergroup assembled for a single specific purpose:

- **Chris Cornell (vocals, guitar).** The defining voice of Soundgarden -- one of the most powerful, rangy, and emotionally flexible vocalists in rock. Cornell's four-octave range allowed him to move from gentle acoustic passages to screaming fury within a single song. At the time of recording, Soundgarden had released *Louder Than Love* (1989) and were emerging as one of the most significant bands in Seattle. Cornell brought the project into existence through his personal grief and his musical vision.
- **Stone Gossard (guitars).** Former Mother Love Bone guitarist, future Pearl Jam co-founder. Gossard was one of the primary architects of what would become Pearl Jam's sound -- rhythm guitar playing that is simultaneously heavy and melodic, with chord structures that provide emotional depth rather than mere accompaniment. His presence in Temple of the Dog gave the project an authenticity it could not have had without Wood's actual bandmates.
- **Jeff Ament (bass).** Former Mother Love Bone bassist, future Pearl Jam co-founder. Ament's bass playing is distinguished by its melodic independence -- his bass lines often carry their own melodic content rather than simply doubling the guitar. His contribution to Temple of the Dog is audible throughout the album's low-end richness.
- **Mike McCready (guitars).** Later a Pearl Jam co-founder, McCready was brought in for his lead guitar capabilities. His playing on the album -- particularly on "Hunger Strike" -- is fluid, emotionally responsive, and shows his blues-rock foundations. McCready's guitar work on Temple of the Dog was some of the earliest material he recorded before Pearl Jam's formation.
- **Matt Cameron (drums).** Soundgarden's drummer at the time, and one of the most accomplished drummers in Seattle. Cameron's playing is characterized by dynamic precision -- he can play with enormous power when needed but pulls back in quieter moments with equal skill. His drumming on Temple of the Dog provides the rhythmic foundation that holds the album together across its varied emotional terrain.
- **Eddie Vedder (vocals).** Vedder had just been flown up to Seattle from San Diego after Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament sent him demo tapes. He was being auditioned for the band that would become Pearl Jam. During the Temple of the Dog sessions, Vedder came in and contributed vocals -- most notably the counterpoint vocal on "Hunger Strike" that turned a solo Cornell performance into a duet and one of the album's defining moments. Vedder's inclusion is one of the most remarkable coincidences in the album's production: he was not yet famous, he was not yet a full member of Pearl Jam, he was simply present and available and talented.

(Sources: Chris Cornell biography; Stone Gossard; Jeff Ament; Mike McCready; Matt Cameron; Eddie Vedder San Diego audition; Pearl Jam formation; Soundgarden lineup)

## THE ALBUM -- *TEMPLE OF THE DOG* (1991)

### One Album, One Statement

- **Recording.** Temple of the Dog was recorded at London Bridge Studio in Seattle in 1990, in approximately fifteen days. The sessions were efficient and purposeful -- Cornell came in with the songs already written, the musicians were all experienced, and the emotional context gave the recording a focused urgency. Producer Rick Parashar brought coherence to a project assembled under unusual circumstances.
- **Release.** A&M Records released *Temple of the Dog* on April 16, 1991. Initially it received modest commercial attention -- the album sold well in Seattle but had limited national exposure. The situation changed dramatically in November 1991, when Nirvana's *Nevermind* broke through commercially and drew national attention to Seattle music. In that context, *Temple of the Dog* -- featuring the voices of both Soundgarden and Pearl Jam, two of the new scene's most prominent bands -- was reissued and went platinum.
- **The accidental supergroup.** The album was recorded before Pearl Jam existed as a public entity. By the time the album achieved its broader commercial success, all of the musicians involved had become major figures in mainstream rock. *Temple of the Dog* thus occupies an unusual historical position: it was made before the grunge wave broke, but it was heard primarily after. It documents Seattle's musicians at a transitional moment -- recording a tribute in grief, not knowing that they were about to inherit the world.

(Sources: London Bridge Studio recording; Rick Parashar production; A&M Records April 1991 release; Nevermind November 1991 context; platinum certification; pre-Pearl Jam recording timeline)

## KEY SONGS

### The Catalog of Grief

- **"Hunger Strike."** The album's most famous track and one of the defining songs of the early 1990s. Written by Cornell, it originally featured only his vocals. The counterpoint vocal that Vedder sang in the studio -- with Cornell singing high and Vedder singing low, the two voices weaving around each other in a call-and-response -- was not initially planned but became the song's essential structure. The music video, shot in Seattle, features Cornell and Vedder performing the song in an empty warehouse. The song reached No. 4 on the Billboard Mainstream Rock chart.
- **"Say Hello 2 Heaven."** The album's opening track. A direct address to Andrew Wood, from Cornell, using Wood's own idiosyncratic orthography in the title (Wood used "2" for "to" as a stylistic signature). The song is a cathedral of grief -- long, building, with Cornell's voice moving through registers from contemplative to full-throated. It sets the emotional context for the entire album.
- **"Reach Down."** The album's longest track at nearly eleven minutes. An extended rock piece with a building structure -- quiet passages, loud passages, extended instrumental sections. The song demonstrates that Temple of the Dog was not just a collection of dirges but a full-spectrum musical statement.
- **"Wooden Jesus."** A harder-edged track, with elements of sardonic commentary alongside the grief. The variety of emotional registers on the album -- from raw sorrow to complexity to sardonic observation -- reflects Cornell's range as a songwriter.
- **"Call Me a Dog."** A quieter, more intimate Cornell vocal performance, revealing the more delicate side of his voice. The song shows that the album's emotional range extends to tenderness.
- **"Times of Trouble."** Another fan-favorite track, with a melodic structure that rewards repeated listening. The lyrics are oblique but emotionally specific.

(Sources: Hunger Strike chart history; Say Hello 2 Heaven Andrew Wood dedication; Cornell vocal range; Reach Down runtime; A&M Records catalog; album track listing)

## THE CAPITOL HILL CONTEXT

### Where Grief Lives

- **Capitol Hill's position in the engine.** Capitol Hill has been a recurring location throughout the engine -- home to some of Seattle's most significant artists in the alternative rock era. Mad Season (224) comes from Capitol Hill. Hammerbox (222) from Capitol Hill. Candlebox (223) from Capitol Hill. The neighborhood is the beating heart of early-1990s Seattle alternative rock.
- **The community.** Capitol Hill in 1990-1991 was where Seattle's musicians lived, practiced, and recorded. The proximity of studios (London Bridge, Seattle Recording, Studio X) and the density of musicians in the neighborhood's apartments and houses created the conditions for cross-pollination. Cornell and Wood were roommates in Capitol Hill. The neighborhood is where this story happened.
- **The grief community.** When Wood died in March 1990, the response was not just individual -- it was communal. Capitol Hill's musician community processed the loss together. Temple of the Dog is, in part, the musical expression of that communal grief. The album could only have been made in the Capitol Hill environment -- where the musicians knew each other, lived near each other, and shared the loss.
- **A&M Records' Seattle connection.** A&M had signed Soundgarden in 1989 and released *Louder Than Love*. The label's willingness to support a side project with no commercial guarantees reflected both their confidence in Cornell and their genuine commitment to the Seattle scene. A&M's patience with Temple of the Dog's slow initial sales was rewarded when the album eventually went platinum.

(Sources: Capitol Hill Seattle; London Bridge Studio location; Chris Cornell Andrew Wood roommates; Capitol Hill musician community; A&M Records Soundgarden; communal grief)

## THE SOUNDGARDEN-PEARL JAM AXIS

### Two Bands, One Recording

- **The Soundgarden side.** Cornell and Cameron were the Soundgarden representatives in Temple of the Dog. At the time of recording, Soundgarden was the more established band -- they had released two studio albums (*Ultramega OK* in 1988, *Louder Than Love* in 1989) and were signed to A&M. Cornell's presence gave the project artistic credibility and label support.
- **The Pearl Jam side.** Gossard, Ament, McCready, and Vedder were the Pearl Jam contingent -- though "Pearl Jam" did not yet exist as a name when the album was recorded. The future Pearl Jam members were in the process of auditioning vocalists after the dissolution of Mother Love Bone. Vedder was the leading candidate, and the Temple of the Dog sessions allowed him to record alongside the musicians who would become his bandmates, before anyone knew what those bandmates were to become.
- **The synthesis.** What makes Temple of the Dog sonically remarkable is that it does not sound like either Soundgarden or Pearl Jam. It sounds like Temple of the Dog -- a third thing, shaped by grief and collaboration and Cornell's specific emotional vision. The album has its own identity separate from either of the bands whose members made it.
- **Matt Cameron's long arc.** Cameron remained Soundgarden's drummer through the band's original run (1984-1997), then joined Pearl Jam full-time in 1998 after Soundgarden's breakup. He played with Pearl Jam through the present, while also participating in Soundgarden's reunion (2010-2017). Cameron is thus connected to both bands over the entire subsequent arc -- and Temple of the Dog documented that connection before either band knew what the other would become.

(Sources: Soundgarden Ultramega OK 1988; Louder Than Love 1989; Pearl Jam formation timeline; Matt Cameron Soundgarden to Pearl Jam; Pearl Jam 1998; Soundgarden reunion 2010-2017)

## CHRIS CORNELL -- THE VOICE

### One of Rock's Most Complete Vocalists

Chris Cornell (July 20, 1964 - May 18, 2017) deserves dedicated analysis within the engine:

- **The range.** Cornell's vocal range has been measured at approximately four octaves. He could sing bass-register passages with genuine depth, move through chest voice into powerful midrange territory, and access a screaming upper register without losing tonal quality. This range is audible throughout Temple of the Dog -- "Say Hello 2 Heaven" alone demonstrates lower-register contemplation, midrange power, and upper-register release.
- **The timbre.** Cornell's voice had a distinctive quality even within rock's wide vocal tradition. His upper register had a plaintive, keening quality -- almost like a guitar's sustain -- that made his emotional expressions feel physically urgent. His lower register had weight and resonance. The voice matched the emotional demands of grief songwriting in a way that few voices could.
- **Cornell and Wood.** Cornell has spoken extensively about his friendship with Wood. They were roommates. Cornell was one of the last people to see Wood before the overdose. The grief in Temple of the Dog is not theoretical -- it is the grief of someone who lived in the same house as the person he lost. This proximity gives the album's emotional core a specificity that mere admiration could not have produced.
- **Cornell's subsequent career.** After Temple of the Dog, Cornell returned to Soundgarden and led the band to its greatest commercial success: *Superunknown* (1994) and *Down on the Upside* (1996). He then pursued a solo career, the Audioslave project (with Tom Morello, Tim Commerford, and Brad Wilk of Rage Against the Machine), a second solo phase, and Soundgarden's reunion. He died on May 18, 2017, in Detroit, following a Soundgarden concert. His death was ruled a suicide by hanging. He was 52. Cornell's loss in 2017 added another layer of grief to the already grief-soaked Temple of the Dog legacy.
- **"Hunger Strike" as testament.** Cornell wrote "Hunger Strike" in anger and grief over Wood's death -- a statement about refusing to eat while surrounded by plenty that felt meaningless in the face of loss. The song endures as one of rock's most emotionally direct statements. After Cornell's own death, the song acquired a second layer of mourning -- listeners hear it now as a document of Cornell's voice and Cornell's grief, not only Wood's death.

(Sources: Chris Cornell biography; vocal range four octaves; Cornell-Wood roommates; Superunknown 1994; Down on the Upside 1996; Audioslave; Cornell death May 18 2017 Detroit; Hunger Strike grief)

## DISCOGRAPHY AND LEGACY

### One Album, Permanent Monument

- ***Temple of the Dog* (1991, A&M).** The only studio album. Ten tracks, approximately 46 minutes. Certified platinum (US). Reissued in 2016 in a 25th anniversary edition with bonus tracks including the "Hunger Strike" demo, studio outtakes, and Cornell/Vedder duet variations.
- **The 2016 reunion.** To mark the 25th anniversary of the album, Temple of the Dog reunited for a full tour -- Cornell, Gossard, Ament, McCready, Cameron, and Vedder performing the album live for the first time. The tour was emotionally charged; the musicians were now in their forties and fifties, carrying the full weight of what the intervening 25 years had meant. Cornell performed "Hunger Strike" with Vedder again. It was the last major tour before Cornell's death in May 2017.
- **The 1991 moment captured.** The album's historical value is not just musical. It is a documentary artifact -- a recording that captures a specific moment in Seattle's music history before the commercial wave broke, featuring musicians who did not yet know what they were about to become. Gossard and Ament had no Pearl Jam. McCready had no Pearl Jam. Vedder had no Pearl Jam. Cornell had no *Superunknown*. They were, in 1990, simply musicians grieving a friend. The monument they built in that grief outlasted the moment and the era.

(Sources: Temple of the Dog album 1991; platinum certification; 25th anniversary edition 2016; bonus tracks; reunion tour 2016; Cornell last major tour; historical documentary value)
