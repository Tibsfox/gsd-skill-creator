The Tribute That Became a Monument

Temple of the Dog was not planned as a legacy album. It was planned as a tribute — Chris Cornell writing songs for Andrew Wood, his roommate and closest friend in the Seattle music scene, who died on March 19, 1990, from a heroin overdose. The album was recorded in approximately fifteen days at London Bridge Studio in Seattle, with Cornell joined by Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament (Wood's former Mother Love Bone bandmates, future Pearl Jam co-founders), Mike McCready (future Pearl Jam guitarist), and Matt Cameron (Soundgarden drummer).

What no one knew during those recording sessions was that the musicians assembled were about to inherit the rock world. By the time the album found its largest audience — late 1991, when Nirvana's Nevermind broke commercially and the world turned its attention to Seattle — every person in that studio had become a major figure in mainstream rock. The tribute made in grief became a monument by circumstance.

"The album was recorded before Pearl Jam existed. By the time it was heard, Pearl Jam had changed the world. Temple of the Dog documents the moment between those two things."

Andrew Wood — The Reason

Andrew Wood (1966-1990) was the vocalist of Mother Love Bone, the band that combined Glam Rock theatricality with Seattle's emerging heavy sound. His bandmates included Stone Gossard and Jeff Ament. Wood was the Seattle scene's most flamboyant optimist — the one musician who seemed genuinely convinced the city was about to become what it eventually did. His death came just as the momentum was building. Apple, Mother Love Bone's debut, shipped posthumously in July 1990. Wood never heard it reach an audience.

Chris Cornell was Wood's roommate. The grief in Temple of the Dog is not theoretical. It is the grief of someone who lived in the same house as the person he lost. "Say Hello 2 Heaven" — the album's opening track, using Wood's own stylistic "2" for "to" in the title — is a direct address to a specific person from a specific person. The monument is personal before it is historical.

The Eddie Vedder Coincidence

The album's most remarkable personnel note: Eddie Vedder was flown up from San Diego to audition for the band that would become Pearl Jam. He was not yet famous, not yet a full member of anything. He came into the Temple of the Dog sessions and sang. His counterpoint vocal on "Hunger Strike" — Cornell high, Vedder low, the two voices weaving in call-and-response — was not planned but became the song's essential structure. The duet that resulted is one of the most recognized rock performances of the 1990s. It was recorded before either vocalist knew what they were becoming.

A&M

Harder-edged. Sardonic elements alongside grief. The album's tonal range extends to complex observation, not only mourning.

Chris Cornell — The Voice

Cornell's four-octave vocal range — bass-register depth, chest voice power, upper-register keening — is audible throughout the album. "Say Hello 2 Heaven" alone demonstrates all three registers in a single performance. After Temple of the Dog, Cornell led Soundgarden to its greatest commercial success: Superunknown (1994) and Down on the Upside (1996). He died on May 18, 2017, in Detroit, following a Soundgarden concert. The album that he made in response to losing his friend now serves, in retrospect, as a document of his own voice preserved before his own loss.
