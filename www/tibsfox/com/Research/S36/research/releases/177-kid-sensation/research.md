## THE NASTYMIX ERA BEGINS

### Seattle's First Hip-Hop Label

Nastymix Records was founded by Sir Mix-a-Lot and Ed Locke in 1985. It was the FIRST significant hip-hop label in the Pacific Northwest. Before Sub Pop was Sub Pop, Nastymix was putting Seattle hip-hop on the map. Kid Sensation was one of its flagship artists:

## E=6 -- THE FIRST GENERATION

### Why E=6

Kid Sensation registers at E=6:

- **Regional hit power:** "Back to Boom" was a genuine regional hit in the Pacific Northwest. This was the late 1980s -- hip-hop from Seattle was barely a concept. For an MC from the Central District to get radio play and regional recognition was a significant achievement.
- **Nastymix connection:** Kid Sensation was embedded in the Nastymix ecosystem. He appeared on Sir Mix-a-Lot's "Posse on Broadway" -- the song that defined Seattle hip-hop in the late 1980s. The posse included Kid Sensation. That's not a footnote -- it's a founding credential.
- **Pioneer status:** Kid Sensation was among the first Seattle MCs to release a full-length album. *Rollin' with Number One* (1990) was an early marker of Seattle hip-hop as a viable commercial product. He was there before the infrastructure existed.
- **Career arc:** The trajectory from Nastymix to Ichiban Records traces a characteristic arc of early Seattle hip-hop -- local label to slightly larger regional distribution, always operating outside the major label system that would later absorb Sir Mix-a-Lot.
- **Cultural specificity:** Kid Sensation's work documented Central District Seattle in the late 1980s. The neighborhoods, the culture, the specific geography of Black Seattle -- this was local hip-hop telling local stories before anyone outside the region was listening.

## BACK TO BOOM -- THE REGIONAL HIT

### The Sound of Early Seattle Hip-Hop

"Back to Boom" captured the energy of late-1980s Seattle hip-hop:

- **Production:** Characterized by the Nastymix sound -- bass-heavy, funk-influenced, synthesizer-driven production. The Nastymix production style was distinct from East Coast and West Coast conventions. It borrowed from both but sounded like neither.
- **Lyrical style:** Kid Sensation's delivery was confident, playful, and technically skilled. He could hold a track alongside Sir Mix-a-Lot, which meant his technical ability was substantial. The lyrics documented Central District life, parties, and the emerging Seattle hip-hop identity.
- **Regional impact:** "Back to Boom" received rotation on KUBE 93 and other Pacific Northwest stations. In a market where hip-hop was barely established, this was significant. Radio play meant legitimacy. Legitimacy meant the scene could grow.
- **Album context:** *Rollin' with Number One* extended the regional hit into a full-length statement. The album demonstrated that Seattle hip-hop could sustain album-length projects, not just singles.

(Sources: Kid Sensation discography; KUBE 93 history; Nastymix Records; engine internal analysis)

## POSSE ON BROADWAY -- THE FOUNDING DOCUMENT

### Kid Sensation's Role

Sir Mix-a-Lot's "Posse on Broadway" (1988) is the founding document of Seattle hip-hop. Kid Sensation was part of that posse:

- **The posse:** The song described a drive down Broadway on Capitol Hill -- specific Seattle geography, specific characters, specific energy. Kid Sensation was named in the posse. This is the equivalent of being on a founding charter.
- **Scene building:** "Posse on Broadway" proved that Seattle could produce nationally relevant hip-hop. Kid Sensation's presence in that moment meant he was part of the proof.
- **Continuing legacy:** When Kid Sensation released his own material, he carried the credibility of "Posse on Broadway" with him. The connection to Mix-a-Lot was not just a label relationship -- it was a scene credential.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Kid Sensation

Kid Sensation's sonic profile reflects the Nastymix era:

- **Vocal delivery:** Confident, playful, technically proficient. Clear diction. The delivery style bridges party rap and lyrical skill -- a balance characteristic of late-1980s hip-hop.
- **Production:** Nastymix house style. Bass-heavy. Funk-influenced. Synthesizer-driven. Drum machine patterns. The production is distinctly late-1980s but with a PNW flavor that distinguishes it from LA or New York conventions.
- **BPM range:** 90-110 BPM. Standard late-1980s hip-hop tempo. Groove-oriented.
- **Lyrical content:** Party rap, Central District geography, hip-hop identity, crew loyalty. The content documents a specific time and place in Seattle history.
- **Mix approach:** Vocals forward with heavy bass. The Nastymix signature was low-end emphasis.
