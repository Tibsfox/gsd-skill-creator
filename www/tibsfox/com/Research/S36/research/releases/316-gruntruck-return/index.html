Ben McMillan — June 21, 1963 – January 9, 2008

Vocalist. Capitol Hill. 44 years old. McMillan died January 9, 2008, in Seattle, from complications of insulin-dependent diabetes and substance abuse. He was one of the most powerful voices in Seattle grunge: a baritone-to-tenor with raw physical urgency, less technically controlled than Staley or Cornell but with an immediacy of delivery that is unmistakable. His death adds to the summit’s mortality record: Cobain (1994), Wood (1990), Staley (2002), Cornell (2017), McMillan (2008). The least publicly known of these losses, but equally part of the story the summit documents.

The Skinyard connection: Before Gruntruck, McMillan sang in Skinyard (degree 297), one of the early Capitol Hill grunge bands, from 1985 to 1991. The Skinyard-to-Gruntruck progression traces the development of the Capitol Hill heavy rock sound through the late 1980s into the grunge era proper.

After Gruntruck: McMillan formed Selfishgene with Scott Vanderpool after Gruntruck dissolved in 1994. One album: Monster Truck Explosions (1997). The project continued his creative engagement with heavy rock after the grunge moment’s commercial peak had passed.

The Band

Ben McMillan (vocals) — See above. Tom Niemeyer (guitar, born August 3, 1962) — Former Skinyard guitarist. His down-tuned, rhythmically authoritative guitar approach defines the Gruntruck sound: sits in the Melvins-to-AIC-to-Soundgarden continuum of Capitol Hill heavy rock without directly imitating any of them. Tim Paul (bass) — Supporting the guitar’s heaviness without competing with it. Tim Haney (drums) — Powerful at the tempos Gruntruck operates at.

Production: Jack Endino. Both Gruntruck albums were produced by Jack Endino, the Sub Pop house producer responsible for Nirvana’s Bleach (1989), Mudhoney’s Superfuzz Bigmuff (1988), and dozens of other defining Seattle grunge records. Endino’s production for Gruntruck is consistent with his broader approach: direct, dry, drums prominent, guitar tone preserved. The Capitol Hill heavy rock sound, unadorned.

The Albums

Inside Yours (1992)

Roadrunner Records

Debut. Produced Jack Endino. McMillan’s aggressive baritone over Niemeyer’s down-tuned riffs. College radio and MTV Headbangers Ball airplay. Roadrunner placement: metal-adjacent, heavier than most Sub Pop bands. The Capitol Hill grunge/metal formula established.

Push (1993)

Roadrunner Records

Second and final album. Jack Endino. Wider dynamic range, more internal contrast. “Tribe”: the signature track. McMillan’s vocals at their most complete. Gruntruck’s creative peak. The band dissolved in 1994.

Roadrunner Records

Founded 1980, Netherlands. By the early 1990s: one of the most important heavy metal labels in the world. Roster alongside Gruntruck: Sepultura (Chaos A.D., Roots), Obituary, Type O Negative, Machine Head, Fear Factory. US distribution through Atlantic Records. Gruntruck’s placement on Roadrunner positioned them in the metal section of record stores rather than the alternative section where Sub Pop bands lived commercially. This reflects the sonic reality: Gruntruck is heavier and less punk-influenced than most grunge. The Roadrunner path was appropriate to the sound and different from every other band in the summit.

Capitol Hill — The Summit's Geographic Center

Of the eight grunge summit bands, six center on Capitol Hill. The neighborhood in the late 1980s and early 1990s: affordable rents, walkable venue density, music-permissive culture. The Central Tavern. The Vogue. Moe’s Mo’Roc’N Cafe. The Weathered Wall. These clubs hosted every band in the summit before commercial recognition. Gruntruck as the closing entry is geographically appropriate: the last band in the cluster lived in the neighborhood that hosted most of the cluster.

The Representative Majority

For every Nirvana, there were dozens of Gruntrucks: bands that made good records, toured the clubs, were heard by the people who were there, and dissolved when the commercial moment passed or the personal circumstances became unsustainable. Gruntruck represents this majority — not as a lesser version of the summit’s famous names but as the full and genuine expression of the grunge moment that did not cross over. The E=8 calibration is not charity. Gruntruck at E=8 is accurate. The music was that heavy. Ben McMillan’s voice was that powerful. The records exist and they hold their energy.

The grunge summit closes here. Forty-four degrees from here, the engine completes its 360-degree rotation. Gruntruck, the representative of the majority who did not survive into commercial history, is the correct closing entry.
