### Why E=7

These Arms Are Snakes exceeds E=6 because of the **cumulative intensity** of the band's sonic approach:

- **Angular, dissonant guitar work:** The guitars in These Arms Are Snakes do not follow conventional post-hardcore chord patterns. They are angular -- sharp, dissonant, atonal riffs that create a sense of physical unease. The guitar tone is aggressive, high-gain, and deliberately abrasive.
- **Rhythmic violence:** The drumming is not merely fast or loud -- it is **violent**. Syncopated, relentless, built on patterns that shift unpredictably. The rhythms do not settle into grooves; they attack.

- **Brian Cook's bass:** Cook's bass playing (honed in Botch, one of the most technically extreme bands in Northwest metal/hardcore history) provides a low-frequency foundation that is simultaneously precise and crushing. The bass is not background -- it is a weapon.
- **Dynamic range that never truly rests:** Unlike bands that alternate between quiet and loud (the Pixies formula), These Arms Are Snakes maintain **sustained high intensity** with variations occurring within the upper registers of energy. The quiet moments are tense, not peaceful. The loud moments are overwhelming. The average energy level is higher than any previous entry.

E=7 is the recognition that These Arms Are Snakes operates at a sustained intensity level that E=6 cannot adequately describe. E=6 captures the peak energy of bands like Knife Knights or No-Anchor -- but Those Arms Are Snakes sustains that peak energy as its **baseline** and then exceeds it in its most intense passages.

## THESE ARMS ARE SNAKES -- POST-HARDCORE -- CAPITOL HILL

### Formation and the Botch Connection

These Arms Are Snakes formed in **2002** in **Seattle's Capitol Hill** neighborhood from the wreckage of some of the most intense bands in Northwest heavy music:

- **Brian Cook** -- bassist. Cook's previous band was **Botch**, an Everett/Tacoma mathcore band widely regarded as one of the most technically extreme and influential bands in the genre's history. Botch's *We Are the Romans* (1999) is a canonical document of mathematical, dissonant hardcore. When Botch dissolved in 2002, Cook's next move was These Arms Are Snakes. After TAAS ended, Cook went on to **Russian Circles**, the instrumental post-metal trio from Chicago. The trajectory -- **Botch to These Arms Are Snakes to Russian Circles** -- traces a line through three distinct but related approaches to heavy music: mathcore, post-hardcore, and post-metal. One bass player, three bands, three eras.
- **Steve Snere** -- vocalist and keyboardist. Snere brought a different energy to the post-hardcore template: his vocals were more melodic and chaotic simultaneously, and his keyboard work added an atmospheric, almost gothic dimension to the band's aggression.
- **Chris Common** -- drums. Later replaced by other drummers. Common went on to production work.
- **Ryan Frederiksen** -- guitar. The angular, dissonant guitar architecture that defines the band's sound.

The band was **not a supergroup** in the traditional sense -- it was a **post-dissolution reformation**, musicians from destroyed bands finding new configurations. The Botch connection is critical: Cook brought the technical precision and low-frequency power of Botch into a new framework that was less mathematically complex but more emotionally relentless.

### Capitol Hill in the 2000s

Capitol Hill in the 2000s was the epicenter of Seattle's heavy music underground. Venues like the **Comet Tavern**, **Neumos** (opened 2004), and the broader Capitol Hill house show circuit provided the infrastructure. The neighborhood was not yet the tech-gentrified district it would become in the 2010s -- in the early 2000s, Capitol Hill was still affordable enough for musicians, with a density of bars, clubs, record shops, and rehearsal spaces that created a critical mass.

These Arms Are Snakes were Capitol Hill incarnate: aggressive, angular, confrontational, operating at the intersection of punk energy and art-music ambition. The band played regularly at Capitol Hill venues and was part of the local ecosystem that included bands like **The Blood Brothers**, **Pretty Girls Make Graves**, **Minus the Bear**, and **Murder City Devils** -- the post-hardcore/post-punk/noise axis of early 2000s Seattle.

## Discography: The Angular Arc

### *This Is Meant to Hurt You* (EP, 2003)

The debut EP. Five tracks of concentrated aggression that established the band's template: angular guitars, relentless rhythms, Snere's unhinged vocals, Cook's crushing bass. The title is a statement of intent -- this music is not meant to comfort or entertain. It is meant to **hurt**. The EP announced the band's existence and immediately positioned them in the upper reaches of the energy spectrum.

### *Oxeneers or the Lion Sleeps When Its Antelope Go Home* (2004)

The first full-length album. The title alone signals the band's approach -- oblique, literary, confrontational. *Oxeneers* expanded the sonic palette from the EP: longer songs, more atmospheric passages (Snere's keyboards adding tension and texture), but the core remained angular, aggressive, and relentless. The album was released on **Jade Tree Records** before the band moved to Suicide Squeeze. Key tracks demonstrated the E=7 territory: sustained intensity punctuated by moments of even greater intensity. The dynamics operate within the upper range, not across the full spectrum.

### *Easter* (2006)

The second full-length and the album that solidified the band's reputation as one of the most intense live and recorded acts in Seattle's post-hardcore scene. *Easter* is darker, more atmospheric, and more controlled than *Oxeneers* -- the aggression is channeled rather than explosive. The production tightens the sound: the angular guitars are sharper, the bass more defined, the drums more precise. The keyboards take a more prominent role, adding layers of dissonance and atmosphere. *Easter* is where the E=7 energy is most fully realized -- the album sustains maximum intensity across its runtime while adding enough textural variation to avoid monotony.

### *Tail Swallower and Dove* (2008)

The final album. *Tail Swallower and Dove* pushed the band's sound further into experimental territory: longer compositions, more atmospheric passages, noise elements, and a willingness to extend songs beyond the post-hardcore template's typical 3-4 minute length. The album is simultaneously the band's most ambitious and most demanding work. Cook's bass work is at its most inventive, Frederiksen's guitar is at its most dissonant, and Snere's vocals oscillate between melodic passages and pure aggression.

The band dissolved in **2009**, leaving a discography that spanned six years and four releases -- a concentrated burst of angular post-hardcore that never compromised and never diluted.

(Sources: AllMusic; Pitchfork; Suicide Squeeze catalog)

## THE BOTCH TO RUSSIAN CIRCLES ARC: BRIAN COOK

### One Bass Player, Three Bands, Three Eras

Brian Cook's trajectory through three bands maps the evolution of heavy music in the Pacific Northwest and beyond:

1. **Botch (1993-2002):** Everett, Washington. Mathcore -- technically extreme, rhythmically complex, dissonant. *We Are the Romans* (1999) is a genre-defining album. Botch was pure aggression channeled through mathematical precision. The music was fast, angular, and intellectually demanding. E=6+ territory.

2. **These Arms Are Snakes (2002-2009):** Capitol Hill, Seattle. Post-hardcore -- less mathematically complex than Botch but more emotionally sustained. The aggression shifted from mathematical precision to emotional relentlessness. Cook's bass provided the low-frequency anchor for a sound that was simultaneously more accessible and more intense than Botch. E=7 territory.

3. **Russian Circles (2004-present):** Chicago (Cook joined in 2007). Instrumental post-metal -- no vocals, long-form compositions, the aggression channeled into crescendos and dynamic arcs. Russian Circles represents the maturation of Cook's approach: the intensity is still present but expressed through dynamics and texture rather than constant maximum output. E=5-6 territory.

The arc traces a path from mathematical aggression (Botch) through emotional aggression (TAAS) to atmospheric aggression (Russian Circles). Each band represents a different answer to the same question: **how do you create music that is uncompromisingly heavy?** Botch said: through complexity. These Arms Are Snakes said: through relentlessness. Russian Circles said: through dynamics.

Cook is the constant across all three answers. His bass playing adapts to each context: technically virtuosic in Botch, emotionally crushing in TAAS, atmospherically expansive in Russian Circles. The thread connecting all three is **precision** -- Cook's bass is never sloppy, never vague, always deliberately placed.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Angular Assault

These Arms Are Snakes' acoustic profile is defined by **sustained high-intensity angular post-hardcore**:

- **Guitar tone:** High-gain, angular, dissonant. The guitar does not chug or groove -- it **slashes**. Riffs are built on dissonant intervals, unexpected rhythmic patterns, and sudden shifts in register. The tone is bright and aggressive, cutting through the mix rather than sitting beneath it. The guitar creates tension rather than release.
- **Bass tone:** Brian Cook's bass is a physical presence. Low-frequency, high-gain, precisely articulated. The bass provides both the harmonic foundation and an additional layer of aggression. In TAAS, the bass is not a background instrument -- it is a second attack vector, doubling the intensity of the guitar while adding low-end weight.
- **Drums:** Relentless, syncopated, violent. The drumming shifts between patterns unpredictably, maintaining energy through rhythmic instability. Double-time sections, half-time breakdowns, fills that feel like assaults. The drums never settle into comfortable grooves.
- **Vocals:** Steve Snere's vocals span a range from melodic singing to full-throated howling. The key characteristic is **sustained intensity** -- even the melodic passages are delivered with an urgency that refuses to relax. The vocals are mixed prominently, not buried beneath the instruments. Snere is confrontational.
- **Keyboards:** Snere's keyboards add an atmospheric dimension that distinguishes TAAS from straightforward post-hardcore. Organ-like sustained tones, dissonant textures, gothic atmosphere. The keyboards do not soften the sound -- they add another dimension of unease.

### Dynamic Range

- **Baseline intensity (E=5-6):** The quietest These Arms Are Snakes gets is still louder than most bands' loudest. The "quiet" sections are tense, dissonant, building toward the next explosion. These sections register at E=5-6 -- what would be maximum for other artists is the **resting state** for TAAS.
- **Peak intensity (E=7):** The full-band assaults -- angular guitar, crushing bass, relentless drums, Snere at full intensity -- occupy territory that the engine's previous E=6 maximum cannot capture. This is the new ceiling.
- **Sustained energy:** Unlike the Pixies-formula quiet-loud dynamic, TAAS operates within a narrow band at the top of the energy spectrum. The variation is between "very intense" and "overwhelmingly intense." The average energy across a performance or album is E=6-7.

The overall energy rating of E=7 reflects that These Arms Are Snakes operates at a sustained intensity that exceeds the previous maximum. E=6 was the ceiling for bands that spike to maximum intensity in their peaks. E=7 is for a band whose **baseline** is other bands' maximum.
