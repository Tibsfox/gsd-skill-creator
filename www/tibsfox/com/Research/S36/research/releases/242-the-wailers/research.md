## NOT BOB MARLEY'S WAILERS

### A Critical Disambiguation

The most important fact about The Wailers at degree 242 is the one that requires immediate establishment:

- **Two completely separate bands.** The Wailers of Tacoma, Washington are not Bob Marley's Wailers of Kingston, Jamaica. They share a name and nothing else.
- **The Tacoma Wailers came first.** The Tacoma Wailers formed in 1959. Bob Marley's Wailers formed in Kingston in 1963 -- four years later. The Tacoma Wailers were already recording nationally charting music when the Jamaican group was in its infancy.
- **Different genres, different countries, different histories.** The Tacoma Wailers play American garage rock and proto-punk -- loud, distorted, organ-heavy rock and roll descended from rhythm and blues. Bob Marley's Wailers play reggae, ska, and rocksteady -- Jamaican popular music descended from mento and American rhythm and blues filtered through Kingston's sound system culture.
- **Why it matters.** The Tacoma Wailers have been chronically underappreciated and under-documented partly because their name was absorbed into the global fame of Bob Marley's band. When people encounter "The Wailers" in a historical context, they think Jamaica. This entry is about Tacoma.
- **Original name.** The Tacoma band was officially "The Fabulous Wailers" -- a name that distinguishes them further from the Jamaican group and captures the self-promotional enthusiasm of early 1960s rock and roll. "Fabulous" was a common descriptor for Northwest Sound bands in this era.

(Sources: The Wailers Tacoma history; "The Fabulous Wailers"; Bob Marley's Wailers formation 1963; timeline comparison; disambiguation note)

## FORMATION AND HISTORY

### Tacoma, 1959

The Wailers formed in 1959 in Tacoma -- the same working-class port city that would produce The Sonics the following year:

- **Origin.** The Wailers coalesced around a group of Tacoma high school musicians in the late 1950s. The founding lineup included members who would cycle through the band over its long history.
- **Robin Roberts (vocals).** The first and most important vocalist in the Wailers' history. Robin Roberts -- "Rockin' Robin Roberts" in the promotional materials of the era -- was the charismatic front figure of the band's early years. His departure from the band in the early 1960s marked a transition from the original lineup.
- **Rich Dangel (guitar).** The primary guitarist of the core lineup. Dangel's guitar work is the Wailers' musical backbone -- technically accomplished, rooted in the Chuck Berry tradition but pushing into heavier territory than Berry's clean Cadillac elegance.
- **Mark Marush (guitar).** Rhythm guitar. The Wailers' guitar interplay is one of their distinguishing features -- the band has more harmonic sophistication than most of their garage peers.
- **Buck Ormsby (bass).** Yes, the same Buck Ormsby who would found Etiquette Records. Ormsby's dual role as musician and label executive is one of the Northwest Sound scene's most important connections -- he was simultaneously making the music and building the infrastructure to distribute it.
- **Mike Burk (drums).** The rhythm anchor of the original lineup. Burk's drumming style reflects the era -- more rhythmic precision than the later punk aggression, rooted in the shuffle patterns of 1950s rock and roll.
- **Kent Morrill (keyboards).** The organ player who, along with the guitar work, defines the Wailers' sound. Morrill's organ is less confrontational than Gerry Roslie's at The Sonics -- more melodic, more traditionally musical -- but equally central to the band's identity.

(Sources: Wailers formation 1959; Robin Roberts biography; Rich Dangel; Buck Ormsby dual role; Kent Morrill; original Tacoma lineup)

## "TALL COOL ONE" -- THE NATIONAL CHARTER

### The First Nationally Charting PNW Rock Single

"Tall Cool One" (1959) is the most important single in the Wailers' catalog and one of the most important records in Pacific Northwest music history:

- **The chart position.** "Tall Cool One" reached #36 on the Billboard Hot 100 in 1959. This was the first national chart success for a Pacific Northwest rock band. Before this, PNW rock existed entirely as a regional phenomenon -- known in Tacoma, Seattle, Portland, but invisible to the national market. "Tall Cool One" changed this.
- **The instrumental format.** "Tall Cool One" is primarily instrumental -- a combination of bluesy guitar, sax, and a rhythm section that drives with absolute conviction. The brief vocal passages are almost incidental. This was the era of the rock instrumental -- "Walk Don't Run" (The Ventures, another PNW band), "Green Onions" (Booker T. & the MGs), "Pipeline" (The Chantays) -- and the Wailers contributed one of the genre's most muscular entries.
- **The recording.** Like most Wailers recordings, "Tall Cool One" was made on a limited budget at available studio facilities. The rough edge in the production is not a defect -- it is the sound of Tacoma rock and roll in 1959: unpolished, energetic, and utterly genuine.
- **The Billboard story.** Reaching #36 in 1959 was a genuine commercial achievement. This was a national market dominated by major labels and major-label distribution. An independent label release from Tacoma, Washington reaching the Top 40 required either exceptional commercial appeal, exceptional luck, or both. The Wailers had both.
- **Comparison to "Louie Louie."** "Tall Cool One" preceded The Kingsmen's "Louie Louie" (1963) by four years and reached the same national audience. The Wailers' chart success is often overshadowed by the Kingsmen's bigger achievement -- "Louie Louie" became the most famous garage rock song ever recorded -- but the Wailers got there first.

(Sources: "Tall Cool One" chart history; Billboard Hot 100 1959; rock instrumental genre; Golden Crest Records; comparison with Kingsmen / Louie Louie)

## GOLDEN CREST RECORDS

### New York Distribution for Northwest Music

The Wailers' national chart success was enabled by their label situation:

- **Golden Crest Records.** A New York-based independent label that licensed and distributed music nationally, Golden Crest Records was the vehicle through which "Tall Cool One" and other Wailers recordings reached the national market. This distribution deal was more powerful than anything available to the Sonics through Etiquette Records alone.
- **The label structure.** Golden Crest operated as both a label and a distributor -- it could place records with national distributors and radio promoters in ways that small regional labels could not. Having a New York label connection was the difference between #36 on the Billboard Hot 100 and regional obscurity.
- **The Northwest-New York connection.** The fact that a Tacoma garage band had a New York label deal in 1959 is remarkable. It speaks to the ambition of the Northwest rock scene and to the national hunger for new rock and roll talent in the late 1950s -- the post-Presley period when every label was looking for the next big thing.
- **Later Etiquette connection.** After the Golden Crest period, the Wailers also recorded for Etiquette Records (Buck Ormsby's label), which puts them in the same distribution ecosystem as The Sonics. The Tacoma garage scene was small enough that these connections were inevitable.

(Sources: Golden Crest Records history; national distribution mechanisms; Northwest-New York music business connections; Etiquette Records secondary relationship)

## THE NORTHWEST SOUND -- THE WAILERS' ROLE

### Predecessors and Contemporaries

The Wailers occupy a unique position in the Northwest Sound ecosystem:

- **The first wave.** The Wailers (formed 1959) are the first wave of the Northwest Sound -- the group that established that Pacific Northwest bands could make competitive national-quality rock and roll. The Sonics (formed 1960) are the second wave, taking the same basic materials and pushing them to an extreme the Wailers had not reached.
- **Instrumental excellence.** The Wailers are a more instrumentally sophisticated band than their reputation suggests. Their guitar work is precise, their rhythm section is locked in, and their arrangements show actual musical planning. They are not simply loud and fast (though they can be) -- they have chops.
- **The transition from instrumental to vocal.** The Wailers' early period is primarily instrumental -- "Tall Cool One" is the paradigm case. As the 1960s progressed and the British Invasion changed what commercial rock sounded like, the Wailers shifted toward more vocal-forward material. This transition is audible in their catalog.
- **Live performance.** The Wailers were exceptional live performers. The Tacoma and Seattle club circuit of the early 1960s was competitive, and bands that survived did so through stage presence and the ability to hold an audience. The Wailers developed their skills in this crucible. Their live energy at E=7 reflects performances where the band was competing for attention and respect from audiences who had other options.
- **Influence on The Sonics.** The Wailers' influence on The Sonics is direct and acknowledged. The Sonics came of age in the same city, on the same circuit, watching the Wailers perform. The Sonics took the Wailers' template and pushed it to E=8 extremity -- but the template was the Wailers' contribution.

(Sources: Northwest Sound genesis; Wailers as first wave; instrumental excellence analysis; British Invasion transition; live performance reputation; Sonics as Wailers successors)

## THE CATALOG

### Key Recordings

- **"Tall Cool One" (1959, Golden Crest).** The national charter. #36 Billboard. The instrumental that put PNW rock on the national map.
- **"Mau Mau" (1960).** Another Northwest Sound instrumental with tribal-influenced title and driving rhythm. Less polished than "Tall Cool One" but more aggressive -- pointing toward the Sonics' direction.
- **"Scotch on the Rocks" (1960).** A driving instrumental with the characteristic organ-guitar interplay that defines the Wailers' sound.
- **"It's You Alone" (1962).** A vocal-forward track showing the transition from instrumental rock to song-based material.
- ***Out of Our Tree* (1966, Etiquette Records).** The most important later-period Wailers album. By this point the British Invasion had fundamentally changed what rock music sounded like, and the Wailers were adapting. The title track is one of their most acclaimed: a garage rock anthem with an insistent riff and Roslie-adjacent vocal aggression. (Note: this period of the Wailers sometimes overlaps with Sonics-adjacent personnel and label connections.)
- **"Out of Our Tree" (1966).** The defining track of the later period -- the Wailers at their most aggressive, approaching the Sonics' energy level. This is the track that connects the Wailers to the proto-punk tradition rather than simply to the instrumental rock tradition.

(Sources: Wailers discography; "Tall Cool One" 1959; "Out of Our Tree" 1966; Etiquette Records period; catalog analysis)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### Tacoma's Other Essential Band

- **National viability proven.** The Wailers demonstrated that Pacific Northwest rock bands could reach the national market. Without "Tall Cool One" in 1959, it is less clear that labels and promoters would have given the Sonics the chance they got through Etiquette. The Wailers opened the door.
- **Instrumental rock tradition.** The Wailers' contribution to the instrumental rock tradition is genuine and lasting. "Tall Cool One" belongs in any serious history of the American rock instrumental alongside "Walk Don't Run" (The Ventures), "Pipeline," and "Wipe Out."
- **The Northwest Sound template.** The Wailers defined the Northwest Sound's core formula before the Sonics existed. Organ plus guitar plus driving rhythm plus aggressive vocals (when used) equals Northwest Sound. The Wailers invented the formula; the Sonics pushed it to its limit.
- **Buck Ormsby's dual role.** As both a Wailer and Etiquette's founder, Ormsby's contribution to the scene is uniquely compound. He was simultaneously an artist who developed the Northwest Sound and an executive who built the infrastructure to preserve and distribute it. This dual role is essential to the story.
- **Overshadowed by name confusion.** The Wailers have been chronically underappreciated outside the Pacific Northwest because their name was later occupied by a Jamaican band with global fame. This is historical bad luck. The Tacoma Wailers were there first, and they deserve full credit for what they built.

(Sources: National viability; instrumental rock tradition; Northwest Sound template; Buck Ormsby dual role; name confusion and underappreciation)
