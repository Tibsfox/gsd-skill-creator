## GENRE STAGE 106 -- SURF ROCK ENTERS

### A New Sound in the Engine

La Luz brings the first surf rock to the 360 engine:

- **Genre Stage 106.** Surf rock enters. The engine has covered indie rock, post-punk, grunge, hip-hop, electronic, folk, jazz, metal, noise, twee, power-pop -- but never surf rock. La Luz opens a new genre category at degree 202.
- **Why surf rock in Seattle?** Seattle is not a surf city. The Pacific coast is cold, rugged, and foggy. But La Luz doesn't play beach party surf -- they play dark, reverb-heavy, minor-key surf that matches the PNW coastline. The surf guitar is filtered through Pacific Northwest rain and fog. It's surf rock for people who surf in wetsuits, not bikinis.
- **PNW surf.** The Pacific coast of Washington -- Ocean Shores, Westport, Long Beach -- has a genuine surf culture, albeit colder and smaller than California's. La Luz's sound connects to this reality: Pacific water, not warm water. The reverb echoes like fog.

## SHANA CLEVELAND -- THE GUITAR

### Reverb and Melody

La Luz's identity is built on Shana Cleveland's guitar:

- **Shana Cleveland.** Guitarist, vocalist, songwriter. Cleveland's guitar tone is the defining sound of La Luz: spring reverb, tremolo, clean-to-slightly-overdriven, single-coil pickups. The tone references Dick Dale, Link Wray, and the Ventures, but filtered through indie rock sensibility and PNW melancholy.
- **Reverb as landscape.** Cleveland's use of reverb is not decorative -- it's evocative. The reverb places the guitar in a space: a tiled room, a cave, a foggy coastline. The wet signal is as important as the dry. The guitar sounds like it's playing inside an echo chamber, or on a beach with cliffs behind.
- **Melodic leads.** Surf rock is traditionally instrumental, but La Luz adds vocals. Cleveland's guitar leads are melodic, hooky, and rhythmically precise. The leads carry the songs as much as the vocals do. Double-picking, single-note runs, tremolo-bar dips -- the vocabulary of surf guitar, deployed with indie restraint.
- **The dark end of surf.** La Luz gravitates toward minor keys, slower tempos, and more atmospheric arrangements than classic surf. This is "dark surf" or "witch surf" -- the Pacific at dusk, not the Pacific at noon. The melancholy is quiet but persistent.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of La Luz

- **Tempo.** Moderate to upbeat. 110-140 BPM. The surf beat drives forward. The tempos are consistent -- La Luz doesn't do ballads.
- **Guitar.** Spring reverb. Tremolo. Single-coil tone. Clean to lightly overdriven. Melodic leads with double-picking and single-note runs. Minor keys dominate. The guitar is the defining instrument.
- **Bass.** Walking or driving bass lines. Melodic and rhythmically prominent. The bass-drums combination provides the surf "engine" beneath Cleveland's guitar.
- **Vocals.** Harmonized female vocals. La Luz features multi-part vocal harmonies that add a 1960s girl-group dimension to the surf-rock foundation. The harmonies are precise and arranged, not improvised.
- **Drums.** Surf beat: floor tom heavy, driving, steady. The drums and bass lock into a groove that rarely wavers. The rhythmic consistency is a feature, not a limitation.
- **Keyboards/Organ.** Occasional organ adds a psychedelic dimension. Farfisa-style tones that reference garage rock and 1960s psychedelia.
- **Production.** Reverb-heavy but clear. The production places the listener in a reverberant space -- the "room" is as important as the instruments. Warm, analog-influenced sound.

(Sources: La Luz discography; surf guitar production; vocal harmony analysis; reverb aesthetics)
