Something About Airplanes

1998 · Barsuk Records (BAR-005) · Studio Album #1
Recorded at Dub Narcotic Studio, Olympia, WA · Produced by Christopher Walla
Ben Gibbard · Christopher Walla · Nick Harmer · Nathan Good

Context: Bellingham to Olympia

Death Cab for Cutie began as Ben Gibbard's solo bedroom project in Bellingham, Washington, where he was a student at Western Washington University studying environmental chemistry. The name came from the Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band's song "Death Cab for Cutie," featured in the Beatles' film Magical Mystery Tour (1967). The initial recordings were cassette demos — lo-fi four-track experiments made in Gibbard's apartment, released as the You Can Play These Songs with Chords cassette in 1997 on Elsinor Records.

Those cassette demos caught the ear of Christopher Walla, a fellow WWU student and guitarist. Walla, bassist Nick Harmer, and drummer Nathan Good joined to form the full band. By the time they entered the studio for Something About Airplanes, the project had transformed from a solo experiment into a working band — but the bedroom aesthetic remained foundational. The lo-fi sound was not a limitation; it was a deliberate sonic vocabulary inherited from the cassette culture of the Pacific Northwest.

The album was recorded at Dub Narcotic Studio in Olympia, Washington, owned and operated by Calvin Johnson of Beat Happening and K Records. This is significant. Olympia was the epicenter of Pacific Northwest independent music — not Seattle's grunge explosion, but the quieter, more radical DIY infrastructure that preceded it and outlasted it. K Records, Kill Rock Stars, and the International Pop Underground were the architecture. Recording at Dub Narcotic placed Death Cab squarely in this lineage.

Barsuk Records released the album. Barsuk was brand new in 1998, co-founded by Josh Rosenfeld in Seattle. Something About Airplanes was one of the label's first releases (catalog number BAR-005). Barsuk and Death Cab grew up together — the label's identity became inseparable from the band's. This was the beginning of a partnership that would define both.

Critical reception was modest but warm. The album sold roughly 2,000 copies in its first year, circulating through college radio and independent record stores. It received favorable notice from zines and early online music publications. There was no breakout moment — just steady, word-of-mouth growth in the indie rock underground. The album announced a new voice from the Pacific Northwest, one that favored emotional precision over volume.

Influences

Built to Spill — The Boise-to-Bellingham Guitar Line

Doug Martsch's guitar work on There's Nothing Wrong with Love (1994) and Perfect from Now On (1997) is the single most audible influence on Something About Airplanes. The layered, interlocking guitar lines, the way leads emerge from rhythm parts, the emotional weight carried by melodic guitar figures rather than lyrics alone — this is Built to Spill's vocabulary, and Walla absorbed it completely. Martsch proved that Pacific Northwest guitar music could be intricate without being prog, emotional without being sentimental. The Boise-Bellingham-Seattle corridor carried this sound north.

Modest Mouse — The Other Washington Band

Isaac Brock's The Lonesome Crowded West (1997) had come out just a year earlier, and its influence on the Pacific Northwest indie scene was immediate. The angular, jagged guitar attack, the sense of geographic isolation, the highway-as-metaphor — Modest Mouse provided a template for turning the experience of living in the Pacific Northwest into rock music. But where Modest Mouse was aggressive and confrontational, Gibbard took the same regional DNA and softened it, turning outward anger into inward melancholy. Same landscape, different emotional weather.

The Revolutionary Summer and Olympia DIY

Calvin Johnson's entire ecosystem — Beat Happening, K Records, the International Pop Underground Convention (1991) — gave Death Cab permission to be lo-fi without apology. The Olympia ethos held that technical polish was not a prerequisite for emotional communication. Recording at Dub Narcotic was an act of alignment with this philosophy. The tape hiss, the overdriven vocals, the rough edges on Something About Airplanes are not accidents but choices inherited from this tradition. Beat Happening's approach to melody — simple, direct, carrying enormous emotional weight through repetition and sincerity — runs through Gibbard's vocal approach here.

Pavement — The Slacker Tradition

Stephen Malkmus's vocal delivery — conversational, oblique, literate but never showy — shaped Gibbard's early approach to singing. The Crooked Rain, Crooked Rain (1994) model of indie rock that could be melodic and guitar-driven without being earnest or polished was essential. Gibbard's early lyrics share Malkmus's fondness for unexpected word choices and off-center imagery, though Gibbard always leaned toward emotional directness where Malkmus leaned toward ironic distance.

The Smiths and Morrissey — The Literate Vocalist

The tradition of the literary indie vocalist begins with Morrissey. The self-aware sadness, the elevated vocabulary deployed against guitar-pop melodies, the sense that every lyric is a composed artifact rather than a spontaneous utterance — this is the lineage Gibbard was entering. Johnny Marr's jangly guitar work also echoes in Walla's brighter passages. Death Cab's early work is Pacific Northwest Smiths: the same emotional architecture, relocated from Manchester's terraced houses to Bellingham's overcast skies.

The Promise Ring and Midwest Emo

The Midwest emo movement of the mid-1990s — The Promise Ring, Cap'n Jazz, American Football — provided a melodic and emotional template for the kind of confessional songwriting Gibbard was developing. Davey von Bohlen's vocal approach on Nothing Feels Good (1997) — plaintive, earnest, slightly nasal — shares DNA with Gibbard's early delivery. The twinkly, interlocking guitar parts of Midwest emo merged with the Built to Spill influence to create the layered guitar textures on this album.

Pacific Northwest Geography

Bellingham sits at the very top of Washington state, ninety miles north of Seattle, twenty miles south of the Canadian border. It is a college town surrounded by water, mountains, and forest. The isolation is real — it is not a suburb of anything. The overcast skies, the gray water of Bellingham Bay, the San Juan Islands visible on clear days — this landscape produces introspective music the way it produces rain. The album sounds like Bellingham: muted colors, soft edges, an interior world built to weather long gray seasons.

Literary Influences

Gibbard has cited Raymond Carver and Richard Brautigan as early influences — both Pacific Northwest writers, both practitioners of spare, precise prose. Carver's influence is audible in the early Death Cab approach to lyrics: domestic scenes rendered with emotional precision, the significant detail standing in for the larger feeling. Brautigan's surreal imagery and playful melancholy show up in the more whimsical titles and unexpected metaphors. The band's name itself — borrowed from the Bonzo Dog Band — carries the same kind of absurdist-literary energy that Brautigan deployed.

Production

Christopher Walla produced the album at Dub Narcotic Studio in Olympia. This was Walla's first production credit, and the lo-fi constraints of the studio shaped his approach for years to come. Dub Narcotic was not a high-end facility — it was Calvin Johnson's home studio, built for the K Records aesthetic of accessibility and immediacy. The equipment was functional, not fancy.

The sonic palette is deliberately limited: electric guitar (often overdriven through small amps), bass, drums, and Gibbard's vocals, frequently recorded with audible room tone and tape saturation. There are no orchestral arrangements, no elaborate effects chains. The production is subtractive — Walla's approach throughout his career, but here at its most extreme. What you hear is close to what the band sounded like in the room, with the four-track aesthetic of Gibbard's solo demos still governing the sonic expectations.

The guitar tones favor a mid-range crunch — not the heavy distortion of grunge, but a warmer, more melodic overdrive. Walla often layered two guitar parts, one clean and one driven, creating a texture that reads as fuller than the actual instrumentation. The bass sits relatively high in the mix, melodic and active rather than merely supportive. Nathan Good's drums are mixed with a dry, roomy sound — no click-track precision, no triggered samples, just a drummer in a room.

The vocals are the most lo-fi element. Gibbard's voice is often slightly buried in the mix, emerging from the instruments rather than sitting on top of them. This is a production choice inherited from the Olympia tradition: the voice as one element among equals, not the star of the show. On several tracks, the lyrics are genuinely difficult to parse on first listen — you have to lean in. This created an intimacy that more polished production would have destroyed.

Key Tracks

Bend to Squares

Track 1 · 2:14

The album opener announces the entire Death Cab vocabulary in just over two minutes. Interlocking guitar lines that build through repetition, Gibbard's voice half-buried in the mix, lyrics about geometry and emotional architecture. The title itself is mathematical-emotional: bending to squares, conforming to rigid shapes. The Built to Spill influence is immediate — layered guitars creating a wall of melodic sound. But the brevity is pure punk: say it, mean it, stop. No extended jams, no self-indulgence. Two minutes and fourteen seconds of mission statement.

Champagne from a Paper Cup

Track 3 · 3:08

The title captures the band's early aesthetic perfectly: something elevated served in something disposable. Celebration diminished by its container. This is Gibbard's lyrical mode at its earliest and purest — the metaphor that contains an entire emotional condition. Musically, the song builds from quiet verse to churning chorus, establishing the quiet-loud dynamic that Walla would refine over the next several records. The guitar tone in the chorus is pure Pacific Northwest: overdriven but melodic, angry but beautiful.

Pictures in an Exhibition

Track 5 · 3:53

The title nods to Mussorgsky's Pictures at an Exhibition, reframing classical ambition through indie rock's lo-fi lens. The song is one of the album's more complex arrangements, with Walla's guitar work moving through several distinct sections. Gibbard's lyrics observe a relationship through a series of frozen moments — pictures in the exhibition of memory. The Raymond Carver influence is strongest here: domestic details rendered with the precision of short fiction, each image carrying emotional weight far beyond its surface content.

Line of Best Fit

Track 11 · 6:54

The album closer and its longest track by a significant margin. The title borrows from statistics — the line of best fit is the linear regression that most closely approximates a set of data points. Gibbard was, after all, a science student. The song is the album's most ambitious, building slowly through nearly seven minutes of layered guitars and patient dynamics. It points toward the longer, more expansive compositions that would define Transatlanticism. The ending dissolves into feedback and noise, the lo-fi aesthetic pushed to its breaking point. The math metaphor carries the emotional content: we approximate each other, we draw the best line we can through the scattered data of a relationship, and we know the fit is never exact.

In the Arc

Something About Airplanes is the seed. Everything that follows is already here in embryonic form: the literary lyrics, the layered guitars, the emotional precision, Chris Walla's production instincts, the Pacific Northwest geography embedded in the sound. But it is also genuinely lo-fi in a way the band would never be again. The rough edges are real — not affectation but circumstance. A band recording its first album at a friend's studio in Olympia because that's where you went if you were an indie band in Washington state in 1998.

The album sold modestly and received warm but not rapturous reviews. It did not announce a major new force in indie rock. It announced a promising one. The distance between this record and Transatlanticism, five years later, is the distance between a sketch and a finished painting — but the composition was already there.
