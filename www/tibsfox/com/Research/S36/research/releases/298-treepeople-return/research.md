## DOUG MARTSCH -- THE DEVELOPMENT WITHIN TREEPEOPLE

### From Formation to Built to Spill: The Treepeople Years as Laboratory

Martsch's development across the Treepeople run is the engine's clearest documentation of a songwriter evolving in real time:

- **The beginning (1988).** Treepeople forms in Boise. Martsch is already an unusual guitarist -- his melodic approach, his willingness to follow unexpected intervals, his voice-leading instincts -- these are present from the beginning. But the early recordings are also rougher, less fully realized, still finding the synthesis between post-hardcore energy and melodic sophistication.
- **The middle period (1989-1991).** Treepeople releases *Time Whore* (1989) and *Guilt, Regret and Embarrassment* (1992, though recorded earlier). The band's sound develops. The rhythmic complexity increases -- Schmaljohn's drumming becomes more varied, more demanding. The guitar lines become more harmonically specific. The synthesis is approaching.
- **The final period and *Just Kidding* (1993).** *Just Kidding* is Treepeople at E=8: the most developed recording the band made. Longer songs, more elaborate guitar arrangements, harmonic sophistication that directly anticipates what Built to Spill would pursue with more resources and a larger audience. The album was released as the band was dissolving. It is the most complete statement of what Treepeople could do.
- **Why this matters for Built to Spill.** Built to Spill (formed 1992-1993, initially in Boise) is not a clean break from Treepeople -- it is an extension and deepening. The extended song structures, the melodic guitar arrangements, the rhythmic complexity, the emotional directness -- all of this was present in Treepeople and was carried into Built to Spill. Understanding Built to Spill requires understanding Treepeople first. The return at 298 provides that context.
- **Martsch's guitar vocabulary.** In Treepeople, Martsch develops the guitar vocabulary that will define Built to Spill: complex voice-leading between guitar parts, unexpected harmonic choices that resolve in non-obvious ways, melodic independence between guitar and bass, and a willingness to let songs develop over longer time spans than the post-hardcore tradition typically allowed. This vocabulary is a distinct contribution to Pacific Northwest guitar culture.

(Sources: Doug Martsch development across Treepeople 1988-1993; Time Whore 1989; Guilt Regret and Embarrassment 1992; Just Kidding 1993; rhythmic complexity development; guitar vocabulary development; Built to Spill as extension; Schmaljohn drumming evolution)

## THE FINAL TREEPEOPLE ALBUMS

### *Guilt, Regret and Embarrassment* (1992) and *Just Kidding* (1993)

The two final Treepeople albums are the primary documentation of the band at E=8:

- ***Guilt, Regret and Embarrassment* (1992).** The C/Z Records album that most fully documents the band's post-hardcore sound in mature form. The title reflects a specifically Northwest indie rock sensibility: self-deprecating, aware of its own limitations, honest about emotional complexity. The music is heavy and rhythmically demanding without being aggressive in the metal sense. The guitar lines have the melodic specificity that distinguishes Martsch from any other guitarist working in this tradition.
- ***Just Kidding* (1993).** The final Treepeople album. By the time of its release, Martsch had already begun forming Built to Spill. *Just Kidding* is therefore a document of the transition moment: the last iteration of the Treepeople approach before the new context offered by Built to Spill's formation and Sub Pop's interest. The songs on *Just Kidding* are the most extended and harmonically developed Treepeople recorded.
- **Production context.** Treepeople's recordings were made with C/Z Records' production infrastructure -- not the Reciprocal Recording context of Skin Yard (Endino was the guitarist and producer there), but with similar values: documentation over transformation, live energy preserved, the band's actual sound captured. The production philosophy at C/Z was consistent with the Seattle underground's broader approach to recording.
- **The C/Z Records catalog.** Treepeople's C/Z releases place them alongside Skin Yard (also C/Z) as the two most historically significant bands on the label. C/Z's catalog extends beyond Seattle (the label signed bands nationally) but its Pacific Northwest core -- the *Deep Six* compilation, Skin Yard, Treepeople -- represents the foundational infrastructure of the pre-Sub Pop Seattle heavy underground.

(Sources: Guilt Regret and Embarrassment 1992 C/Z; Just Kidding 1993; album development across the catalog; C/Z production values; C/Z catalog historical significance; Treepeople alongside Skin Yard as C/Z core)

## THE BOISE-SEATTLE CORRIDOR REVISITED

### What the Return Adds

The first entry at degree 263 established the Boise-Seattle corridor conceptually. The return at degree 298 documents the corridor's persistence:

- **The corridor's function.** The I-5 and US-20 routes between Boise and Seattle are not just geographic connections -- they represent a cultural corridor that sustained Pacific Northwest indie rock across decades. Bands moved between cities, played both markets, drew from both talent pools. Treepeople established the pattern for this corridor in the late 1980s; Built to Spill confirmed it in the 1990s.
- **Why Boise produced this kind of band.** The isolation of Boise -- relative to larger music markets -- produced a specific kind of musician: one who learned by listening to recordings rather than by attending shows, who developed a personal musical vocabulary without constant exposure to the trends of a larger scene, who arrived at Seattle with something distinct to offer. Martsch's guitar vocabulary has this quality: it does not sound like it was developed in the Capitol Hill bar scene; it sounds like it was developed in Boise, alone with records.
- **The corridor's endurance.** Built to Spill, which continues to record and tour from Boise, is the corridor's most enduring presence. The Boise-Seattle connection that Treepeople established in 1988-1993 persisted through Built to Spill's entire career. Martsch has occasionally lived in Seattle but has remained based in Boise -- maintaining the corridor position that Treepeople originated.
- **Other corridor travelers.** The Boise-Seattle connection is not unique to Treepeople and Built to Spill. Other musicians have moved through this corridor, though none with as consequential a musical output. The corridor is a structural feature of the Pacific Northwest's geography and music culture.

(Sources: Boise-Seattle corridor function; geographic and cultural connection; Boise isolation producing distinct vocabulary; Built to Spill corridor persistence; Martsch Boise base; corridor as structural feature)

## POST-HARDCORE: THE GENRE CONTEXT

### Treepeople in the Post-Hardcore Tradition

Post-hardcore is a specific tradition that the engine has documented across multiple degrees:

- **The post-hardcore lineage.** Post-hardcore emerged in the mid-1980s as musicians trained in hardcore's speed and aggression began applying those techniques to more melodically and structurally ambitious material. The canonical examples: Husker Du (melodic HC to power pop), Fugazi (DC, post-punk rigor), Jawbreaker (emotional directness over HC energy), Sunny Day Real Estate (Pacific Northwest, emo's emergence). Treepeople occupy this tradition with specific Northwest inflections.
- **Northwest post-hardcore.** The Pacific Northwest's post-hardcore scene had a distinct character from the DC and East Coast versions. Where DC post-hardcore tended toward political directness (Fugazi's lyrical engagement) and East Coast versions toward emotional introspection (Jawbreaker, Sunny Day Real Estate), Northwest post-hardcore -- as represented by Treepeople -- tended toward melodic exploration within rhythmic complexity. The songs are emotionally direct but the statement is made through guitar arrangement rather than confessional lyric.
- **E=8 in the post-hardcore tradition.** The E=8 rating for Treepeople's return reflects the genre's characteristics at full engagement. Post-hardcore at E=8 is not the attack of metal or the chaos of noise rock; it is the sustained effort of maximum melodic and rhythmic ambition. The songs demand everything from the players and from the listener.
- **Contrast with Skin Yard at E=7.** The adjacent C/Z entry -- Skin Yard (297) at E=7 -- provides a calibration point. Skin Yard's proto-grunge is E=7: architectural, pressing, structural. Treepeople's post-hardcore at E=8 is more intense: the rhythmic complexity adds demand that the simpler structures of proto-grunge do not carry. E=8 is the correct escalation.

(Sources: Post-hardcore lineage: Husker Du, Fugazi, Jawbreaker, Sunny Day Real Estate; Northwest post-hardcore distinct character; melodic exploration within rhythmic complexity; E=8 as maximum post-hardcore engagement; Treepeople vs. Skin Yard E comparison; genre tradition analysis)

- **The label's historical position.** C/Z Records occupied the infrastructure gap before Sub Pop had the resources to document the full Seattle heavy underground. The *Deep Six* compilation (1986) was released on C/Z two years before Sub Pop's major commercial breakthrough with Mudhoney's *Superfuzz Bigmuff* (1988). The label's contribution to the Seattle underground -- the *Deep Six* compilation, Skin Yard's proto-grunge catalog, Treepeople's post-hardcore development -- is fully documented across four degrees. The label shift from C/Z to Sub Pop across this cluster mirrors the historical shift: C/Z documented the 1985-1993 underground; Sub Pop carried the commercial moment forward. The engine follows the same arc.
