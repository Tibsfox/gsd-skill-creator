## Biography: The Serial Band-Maker Who Opened a Bar

### Mat Brooke: From Carissa's Wierd to Band of Horses to Grand Archives

**Mat Brooke** is an American musician best known as the lead singer of Grand Archives and a former member of Carissa's Wierd and Band of Horses. His career traces the genealogy of 2000s Seattle independent music through three distinct bands, each representing a different aesthetic and emotional register.

**Carissa's Wierd (1995-2003):** Brooke co-founded Carissa's Wierd with **Jenn Champion** (then Jenn Ghetto) in **Tucson, Arizona, in 1995**. The band relocated to **Seattle** and grew to include **Ben Bridwell** and **Sera Cahoone** (degree 48). Their music was slowcore, chamber-inflected indie rock -- minor-key guitar arpeggios, staccato piano motifs, subtle violin parts, and understated percussion. Their final album, *Songs About Leaving* (2002, Sad Robot Records; reissued 2010, Hardly Art), is considered a cult classic of 2000s indie rock. AllMusic described it as chamber pop with slowcore foundations. The band disbanded in 2003.

**Band of Horses (2004-2006 for Brooke):** After Carissa's Wierd dissolved, Bridwell formed Band of Horses and invited Brooke to join. Brooke played guitar on Band of Horses' debut, *Everything All the Time* (2006, Sub Pop), and wrote two tracks: "I Go to the Barn Because I Like the" and "St. Augustine." But Brooke's involvement was casual: "I'd never really given the commitment to be a formal member. It was just a spur of the moment... and Everything All the Time took off really fast... I still didn't feel quite committed." He departed before the album's commercial success fully materialized. Band of Horses went on to be one of the decade's most successful indie rock acts. Brooke walked away.

**The interval:** After leaving Band of Horses, Brooke took time off from music to **open a bar in Seattle** -- The Redwood, on Capitol Hill. He and his partners bought a Howell Street laundromat, gutted it, found a dilapidated barn in Fife, bought the wood, and built the bar by hand. The Redwood became a Capitol Hill institution. Brooke married his partner **Lisa Jack** (co-owner of The Redwood) in Hawaii in December 2009.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Mat Brooke; Wikipedia -- Carissa's Wierd; Wikipedia -- Band of Horses; KEXP -- Carissa's Wierd 20th Anniversary; Capitol Hill Seattle Blog -- Redwood bar; Washingtonian -- Interview with Mat Brooke; No Depression -- Carissa's Wierd: The Band That Got Away)

### Grand Archives: Formation and the Debut (2006-2008)

In **September 2006**, Brooke formed Grand Archives with a trio of Seattle friends:
- **Curtis Hall** (drums, vocals) -- from the Jeunes
- **Jeff Montano** (bass, vocals) -- from the New Mexicans
- **Ron Lewis** (keyboards, guitar, vocals) -- from Ghost Stories

Guitarist **Thomas Wright** (guitar, vocals) joined soon after, in early 2007. The lineup was a **five-piece** with four-part vocal harmonies at its core.

Grand Archives supported **Modest Mouse** on seven west-coast tour dates, gaining visibility and catching Sub Pop's attention. The band signed to **Sub Pop Records** for a full-length album.

The self-titled debut, **The Grand Archives**, was released on **February 19, 2008**, on Sub Pop. The album was a deliberate departure from Brooke's previous work. Where Carissa's Wierd was dark, minor-key, and mournful, Grand Archives was bright, major-key, and optimistic. Sub Pop described the album as "full of illumination and warmth, responding to turbulent times with hope, high spirits, and a sense of camaraderie." The sonic palette included acoustic guitars, electric guitars, pedal steel, trombone, flugelhorn, cello, accordion, and prominent four-part vocal harmonies. NPR's World Cafe called the sound "soft-rock redux" and compared the vocal harmonies to **The Beach Boys** and **Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young**.

**Critical reception for the debut:**
- **Pitchfork:** 7.5/10 -- "the way Grand Archives come forth with arms outstretched results in a debut that likely exceeds most expectations"
- **Metacritic:** 72/100 (generally favorable, based on 13 critics)
- **Drowned In Sound:** "an impressively alternative take on American indie; blending a number of interesting influences to create a fresh and airy project"
- **AllMusic:** 3.5/5 stars

Tracklist: "Torn Blue Foam Couch," "Miniature Birds," "Swan Matches," "Index Moon," "George Kaminski," "A Setting Sun," "Breezy No Breezy," "Sleepdriving," "Louis Riel," "The Crime Window," "Orange Juice."

"Miniature Birds" became the band's most recognizable track -- its cascading vocal harmonies and fingerpicked guitar are the distilled essence of the Grand Archives sound.

The band toured the US and Europe in support of the debut.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Grand Archives; Sub Pop Records -- The Grand Archives; Pitchfork -- Grand Archives review; Metacritic -- Grand Archives; Drowned In Sound -- Grand Archives review; NPR World Cafe -- Grand Archives: Soft-Rock Redux; AllMusic -- Grand Archives; Discogs -- Grand Archives)

### Keep in Mind Frankenstein (2009) and the Slow Fade

**Ron Lewis** departed Grand Archives in **November 2008** to tour with **The Shins** and rejoin **The Fruit Bats**. The remaining four members -- Brooke, Hall, Montano, Wright -- continued as a quartet.

The second album, **Keep in Mind Frankenstein**, was released on **September 15, 2009**, on Sub Pop. It was recorded at **Paradise Sound Studios in Index, Washington** -- a small town in the Cascade foothills, forty miles east of Seattle. The album's creation was unusual: the band **purposely discarded material they had prepared in advance** and was "forced to create Keep in Mind Frankenstein on the spot" in the studio. Sub Pop quoted Brooke: "It sounded kind of... like guys who don't really play rock music trying to rock out."

The result was a more melancholy, ruminative album than the debut. Where the debut was sunshine and camaraderie, *Keep in Mind Frankenstein* drifted toward the wistful melancholy of Brooke's Carissa's Wierd years. The vocal harmonies remained, but the brightness dimmed.

**Critical reception:**
- **AllMusic:** 3/5 stars -- noted the album "reprises the sound of their 2008 debut, from the emphasis on rich vocal harmonies to the leisurely strum of their instruments"
- **PopMatters:** Mixed-positive, noting the album's spontaneous studio creation
- **Exclaim!:** "wallows in the same wonderful melancholy as Brooke used in his older band Carissa's Wierd," but criticized the album as "a one-note album" that was "a move sideways, with songs drifting by without beginning or end"
- **American Songwriter:** Positive review, noting the band's growth
- **Treble:** Reviewed positively

Tracklist: "Topsy's Revenge," "Witchy Park/Tomorrow Will (Take Care of Itself)," "Silver Among the Gold," "Oslo Novelist," "Lazy Bones," "Siren Echo Valley (Part 1)," "Left for All the Strays," "Dig That Crazy Grave," "Siren Echo Valley (Part 2)," "Willoughby."

The band toured the US, Canada, and Europe in support of the album.

(Sources: Wikipedia -- Grand Archives; Wikipedia -- Keep in Mind Frankenstein; Sub Pop Records -- Keep in Mind Frankenstein; AllMusic -- Keep in Mind Frankenstein; PopMatters -- Keep in Mind Frankenstein; Exclaim! -- Grand Archives review; Discogs -- Grand Archives discography)

### Villains and the Silence (2010-2011)

Grand Archives began work on a third album tentatively titled **Villains**. A demo of four songs from the album was released as a free download on **October 7, 2010**. Three additional free demos followed in **June 2011**. The full album was never completed. The band never officially announced a breakup. They simply stopped.

Mat Brooke turned his energy to **The Redwood** (Capitol Hill bar, which he and Lisa Jack operated until it closed in November 2017 amid development pressure), then to **The Oak** (another Seattle bar/restaurant venture, launched 2012, later sold), and eventually to **The Spruce** in **Port Angeles, Washington** -- a restaurant focusing on locally sourced Pacific Northwest food and beer. The musician became a restaurateur. The serial band-maker became a serial business-maker. The pattern held: build something, run it, move on.

In 2019, Brooke and Jenn Champion reunited for **Carissa's Wierd 20th anniversary shows** -- stripped-down duo performances of the catalog, marking the anniversary of *Ugly But Honest*. The reunion was intimate, acoustic, and temporary. The thread to Carissa's Wierd never fully severed.

## Production Philosophy: Sunshine as Resistance

### From Slowcore to Sunshine Pop

Grand Archives' most significant artistic statement is the **tonal inversion** from Mat Brooke's earlier work. Carissa's Wierd was dark, mournful, slowcore -- chamber-inflected music built on loss and leaving. Band of Horses was anthemic but melancholic. Grand Archives was **deliberately bright**. Sub Pop described the debut as "responding to turbulent times with hope, high spirits, and a sense of camaraderie." Brooke's previous bands "often shied away from light, but he changed direction with this band."

The brightness was a choice, not a default. NPR called it "soft-rock redux" -- a deliberate reaching back to 1970s vocal-harmony traditions (Beach Boys, CSNY, The Association, America) filtered through 2000s Pacific Northwest indie sensibility. The flugelhorns, trombones, pedal steel, cello, and accordion are not ironic deployments but sincere ones. Grand Archives played sunshine pop without apologizing for it.

This is a different kind of folk-adjacent: not the hushed confessional of Jurado (45) or the lo-fi intimacy of Mirah (50), but a communal, harmony-driven warmth. The acoustic guitar is still the foundation, but it supports choral vocals rather than solo testimony. The genre label "Indie Folk" captures the acoustic, melody-forward, narrative songwriting tradition; the production adds warmth and orchestral color.

### Paradise Sound Studios, Index, Washington

The second album was recorded at **Paradise Sound Studios** in **Index, Washington**, a tiny town in the North Cascades with a population under 200. The studio, forty miles east of Seattle at the base of Mount Index, offered isolation that shaped the album's contemplative character. The band discarded prepared material and wrote in the studio -- the space became the creative method. Index is mountain-town PNW at its most remote: Highway 2, the Skykomish River, old-growth forest. The recording location maps the engine's geography deeper into the Cascades than any previous degree.

## The Fifty-Second Acoustic Element: The Communal Harmony

### Acoustic Progression: Fifty-Two Elements

The fifty-second acoustic element is **the communal harmony** -- the acoustic phenomenon that occurs when multiple voices sing together in deliberate, arranged harmony, creating a sound that no single voice could produce alone. The communal harmony is not unison (everyone singing the same note) but structured difference: each voice occupies a different position in the chord, and the combined sound has a richness, warmth, and spatial dimension that transcends the individual voices. The listener hears not four singers but one organism with four throats.

Grand Archives' music is built on four-part vocal harmonies. Every member of the band sang. The harmonies are the band's defining feature -- NPR compared them to the Beach Boys and CSNY. "Miniature Birds" layers cascading vocal lines over fingerpicked guitar. "The Crime Window" mixes vocal harmonies with horn arrangements. The communal harmony is the acoustic element that emerges when folk songwriting meets choral arrangement -- the campfire sing-along formalized into studio architecture.

The communal harmony extends the six-element folk-acoustic taxonomy into a **seven-element folk-acoustic taxonomy**: the murmur (degree 45, Jurado -- whispered intimacy), the confessional (degree 46, Votolato -- exposed declaration), the parable tone (degree 47, Bazan -- narrative delivery), the held rest (degree 48, Cahoone -- rhythmic silence), the lullaby subversion (degree 49, Dawson -- gentle form, subversive content), the intimate architecture (degree 50, Mirah -- small songs with spatial depth), and the communal harmony (degree 51, Grand Archives -- multiple voices as one organism). ## The Fifty-Second Artist-City Pattern: The Serial Founder

### The Pattern of Building and Moving On

The fifty-second artist-city pattern is **the serial founder** -- the artist who repeatedly creates new projects from scratch within a single city, each with its own identity, each with a defined lifespan. The serial founder does not stay in one band and evolve (the Lifer) or leave a city and carry its DNA elsewhere (the incubator graduate). The serial founder stays in the same city but creates a series of distinct entities -- bands, businesses, collaborations -- each built, run, and eventually set down.

Mat Brooke's career is the definitive example. Three bands in fifteen years, all rooted in Seattle (Carissa's Wierd relocated to Seattle from Tucson; Band of Horses was a Seattle band; Grand Archives formed in Seattle). Each band had a different sound, different members (except for the Bridwell overlap between Carissa's Wierd and Band of Horses), and a different emotional register: slowcore grief (Carissa's Wierd), anthemic melancholy (Band of Horses), sunshine folk-pop (Grand Archives). After music, the pattern continued with bars and restaurants: The Redwood, The Oak, The Spruce. Building things is the constant. The specific thing changes.

The serial founder is distinct from the Lifer (who stays in one project), the incubator graduate (who departs the city), the radical transplant (who arrives with a formed identity), and the Draw (who is attracted to a scene). The serial founder's relationship to the city is productive -- the city provides the raw materials (fellow musicians, audiences, venues, labels), and the founder assembles new combinations. Seattle's independent music ecosystem is particularly suited to this pattern: dense enough to provide collaborators, fluid enough to allow recombination.

**Artist-city taxonomy after degree 51: FIFTY-TWO named patterns.**

## The Fifty-First Blue Note: The Afterglow

### Blue Note Deployment 51

The fifty-first blue note deployment is **the afterglow** -- the blue note that arrives after the brightness has peaked, when the warmth is still present but the source is fading. The afterglow is not sadness but the awareness that the bright moment is finite. The listener hears the sunshine in the music and simultaneously senses that the sunshine will not last. The blue note is in the gap between the optimism of the arrangement and the knowledge that the band will not survive.

Grand Archives' music carries the afterglow in its DNA. The debut is sunshine pop, bright and warm. The second album dims toward melancholy. The third album never materializes. The band's arc -- from brightness to fade to silence -- is the afterglow writ large. Within individual songs, the afterglow manifests as vocal harmonies that resolve into minor-key phrases, as major chords that drift into suspended or diminished territories, as the wistful quality that critics hear beneath the brightness. Exclaim! noted that *Keep in Mind Frankenstein* "wallows in the same wonderful melancholy as Brooke used in his older band Carissa's Wierd" -- the melancholy was always there, beneath the sunshine, waiting for the afterglow.

The afterglow is also biographical: Brooke left Band of Horses just as they were taking off. He left music just as Grand Archives was gaining traction. The afterglow is the blue note of the artist who builds beautiful things and then walks away before the light fades on its own. The glow persists after the source has departed.

The afterglow follows the submerged current (degree 50), the Trojan lullaby (degree 49), the drummer's ghost (degree 48), the suspended resolve (degree 47), the crack (degree 46), and the held breath (degree 45) in the blue note genealogy. **Seven consecutive blue-note deployments in the folk-adjacent register**: silence (held breath), rupture (crack), irresolution (suspended resolve), rhythmic memory (drummer's ghost), formal deception (Trojan lullaby), emotional undertow (submerged current), and the glow after the light source departs (afterglow). Seven modes of **absence, misdirection, and hidden motion** across seven degrees.

Blue note deployment count: **51 across 52 degrees** (continuous from degree 0).

Sera Cahoone (Folk, Sub Pop, Ballard, E=2) and Grand Archives / Mat Brooke (Indie Folk, Sub Pop, Seattle, E=3) share the Carissa's Wierd genealogy. Cahoone was the drummer. Brooke was the co-founder, guitarist, and vocalist. They played in the same band (1999-2003), then both ended up on Sub Pop as folk-adjacent artists. Three degrees apart in the engine (48 and 51). Both folk-adjacent. Both quiet. The held rest (48) is the drummer's silence after she puts down the sticks and picks up a guitar. The communal harmony (51) is the singer's brightness after he sets down the grief.

### Degree 50: Mirah -- The Olympia-to-Seattle Return

Two modes of indie folk: solitary depth and communal warmth. K Records intimacy and Sub Pop brightness.

### Degree 49: Kimya Dawson -- Folk Zone Continues

### Degree 45: Damien Jurado -- The Sub Pop Bookend

The murmur and the communal harmony are opposite poles of the folk-acoustic taxonomy -- solitary whisper versus collective voice.
