The Serial Band-Maker Who Opened a Bar

Mat Brooke co-founded Carissa’s Wierd in Tucson, Arizona, in 1995 with Jenn Champion. The band relocated to Seattle, added Ben Bridwell and Sera Cahoone (degree 48), and released three albums of slowcore, chamber-inflected indie rock before disbanding in 2003. Their final album, Songs About Leaving (2002), is a cult classic. After the breakup, Bridwell formed Band of Horses and invited Brooke to join. Brooke played guitar on Everything All the Time (2006, Sub Pop) and wrote two tracks — but left before the band’s success fully materialized.

“I’d never really given the commitment to be a formal member. It was just a spur of the moment… and Everything All the Time took off really fast… I still didn’t feel quite committed.” — Mat Brooke, on leaving Band of Horses

After leaving Band of Horses, Brooke took time off from music to open a bar in Seattle. He and his partners gutted a Howell Street laundromat, salvaged wood from a dilapidated barn in Fife, and built The Redwood on Capitol Hill by hand. Then, in September 2006, he formed Grand Archives with fellow Seattle musicians: Curtis Hall (drums, the Jeunes), Jeff Montano (bass, the New Mexicans), Ron Lewis (keyboards/guitar, Ghost Stories), and Thomas Wright (guitar). A five-piece with four-part vocal harmonies at its core.

Sunshine Pop on Sub Pop

Grand Archives supported Modest Mouse on seven west-coast tour dates, catching Sub Pop’s attention. The self-titled debut, The Grand Archives, was released February 19, 2008, on Sub Pop. Where Carissa’s Wierd was dark and mournful, Grand Archives was deliberately bright. Sub Pop described the album as “full of illumination and warmth, responding to turbulent times with hope, high spirits, and a sense of camaraderie.”

NPR’s World Cafe called the sound “soft-rock redux” and compared the vocal harmonies to The Beach Boys and Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young. The sonic palette: acoustic and electric guitars, pedal steel, trombone, flugelhorn, cello, accordion, and the ever-present four-part harmonies. “Miniature Birds” — cascading vocal lines over fingerpicked guitar — became the band’s signature track.

Pitchfork gave the debut 7.5/10: “the way Grand Archives come forth with arms outstretched results in a debut that likely exceeds most expectations.” Metacritic: 72/100 (generally favorable, 13 critics). AllMusic: 3.5/5. Drowned In Sound: “an impressively alternative take on American indie.”

Keep in Mind Frankenstein & the Fade

Ron Lewis departed in November 2008 to tour with The Shins and rejoin The Fruit Bats. The remaining four-piece recorded their second album at Paradise Sound Studios in Index, Washington — a tiny town in the Cascade foothills, population under 200. The band discarded prepared material and wrote spontaneously in the studio. Brooke: “It sounded kind of… like guys who don’t really play rock music trying to rock out.”

Keep in Mind Frankenstein (September 15, 2009, Sub Pop) was darker and more ruminative than the debut. The sunshine dimmed toward the melancholy of Brooke’s Carissa’s Wierd years. Exclaim!: “wallows in the same wonderful melancholy as Brooke used in his older band.” AllMusic: 3/5.

Demos for a third album, Villains, appeared as free downloads in 2010 and 2011. The full album was never completed. The band never officially disbanded — they simply stopped. Brooke turned to The Redwood (Capitol Hill bar, closed 2017), The Oak (2012), and eventually The Spruce in Port Angeles, WA. The musician became a restaurateur. In 2019, Brooke and Jenn Champion reunited for Carissa’s Wierd 20th anniversary shows — stripped-down, intimate, temporary.

Music Theory Contributions

The Communal Harmony

Advanced

52nd acoustic element. The sound that occurs when multiple voices sing in arranged harmony — creating a richness no single voice could produce. Not unison but structured difference: each voice at a different chord position, the combined sound a single organism with multiple throats. Grand Archives’ four-part harmonies are the defining example. NPR compared them to Beach Boys and CSNY. Extends the folk-acoustic taxonomy to seven elements: murmur (45), confessional (46), parable tone (47), held rest (48), lullaby subversion (49), intimate architecture (50), communal harmony (51).

The Serial Founder

Advanced

52nd artist-city pattern. The artist who repeatedly creates new projects from scratch within a single city, each with its own identity, each with a defined lifespan. Brooke: Carissa’s Wierd (slowcore), Band of Horses (anthemic), Grand Archives (sunshine), then The Redwood, The Oak, The Spruce. Three bands and three businesses in twenty years. Building is the constant. The specific thing changes. Distinct from the Lifer, the Draw, and the incubator graduate.

Blue Note: The Afterglow

Intermediate

51st blue note deployment. The warmth that persists after the light source departs. The awareness that the bright moment is finite. Grand Archives’ arc: bright debut, dimming second album, silence. Within songs: major chords drifting toward suspended territories, optimism shadowed by transience. Brooke left Band of Horses as they ascended. He let Grand Archives fade. The afterglow is the blue note of the artist who builds beautiful things and walks away. Seven consecutive folk-adjacent blue notes (45–51).
