The Name Collision — Primary Research Finding

Searching for “The Tempos Seattle” returns documentation for a big band/swing ensemble founded in 1958 by Boeing engineer Henry Fichtner, co-led by Dr. Vernon J. Harkness. This 16-piece swing band performs dance music at community functions. It does not match the CSV’s R&B/Soul genre, Central District neighborhood, or E=5 energy designation.

The R&B/Soul Tempos are hidden behind their better-documented namesake. Two unrelated bands in the same city adopted the same name. The big band’s 60+ years of continuous performance created a durable archival footprint. The R&B group’s unknown lifespan created none. Band names in the 1960s were not registered or centrally catalogued.

The Scene The Tempos Performed In

Without individual biographical data or confirmed membership, the research documents the 1960s Central District R&B ecosystem that the CSV data places The Tempos within. The 1960s — tighter than Larry English’s 1960s–1970s — spans the pre-Hendrix era through the soul revolution.

Central District R&B/Soul Ecosystem (1960s)

Venues: Birdland (2203 E. Madison, razed 1965), Black and Tan Club, Golden Crown, Trojan Horse, District Tavern, Encore Ballroom (1214 E. Pike, R&B shows booked by Charles Sullivan).

Radio: KYAC 1250 AM (from 1965), Black-owned. Local bands topped KYAC charts over national artists.

Recording: Kearney Barton’s Audio Recording studio. Small labels: Nite Owl, Penguin, Seafair, Jerden. Self-released 45s in runs of 200–1,000 copies.

Documented contemporaries: Dave Lewis Combo, The Dynamics (degree 27), The Gallahads, Kings of Soul, Black on White Affair, Night Owls (degree 28).

Talent pipeline: Gallahads disbanded early 1960s; members dispersed into Dynamics, Viceroys, El Caminos, Statics, Counts, Soul Deacons, Boss Five. The Tempos’ unknown members may have come from this same pool.

Late 1950s

Birdland opens with Dave Lewis Combo as house band. Doo-wop groups (Gallahads, Five Checks) active. The Central District’s R&B scene takes shape.

Early 1960s

Gallahads disperse; members seed new groups across the CD. Teen-R&B circuit peaks (Spanish Castle, Parker’s). The Dynamics form and record prolifically.

Mid 1960s

Birdland razed (1965). KYAC begins broadcasting. Soul revolution reaches Seattle. R&B/Soul groups proliferate. Black on White Affair, Cold Bold & Together emerge. The Tempos’ era designation places them here.

Late 1960s

Scene intensity at maximum. Clubs packed seven nights a week. Hendrix achieves fame elsewhere, reshaping how the world sees Seattle’s music. The Central District scene continues at full capacity.

Why This Degree Matters

Degree 34 introduces a phenomenon more treacherous than the pure documentation gap: the name collision. A researcher who finds the PNW Bands page for “The Tempos” might conclude the search is complete. The data exists. It describes a real band in Seattle. But it is the wrong band. The big band Tempos played swing at Boeing social events. The R&B Tempos played soul in Central District clubs. Same name, same city, same decade — different worlds.

The engine does not accept the decoy. It documents the collision, names the mismatch, and preserves a space for the R&B/Soul group that the archive misidentifies. The Shadow Name is the documentation gap’s more insidious cousin: instead of silence, a wrong answer.
