## The Name Collision: Two Bands, One City, Different Worlds

### What the Search Revealed

The name "The Tempos" in Seattle returns a specific, documented result -- but it is not the R&B/Soul group the CSV describes. **The Tempos** listed on PNW Bands (pnwbands.com) is a **big band/swing ensemble** founded in **1958** by **Henry Fichtner**, a Boeing engineer, along with coworkers and friends. Dr. Vernon J. Harkness, a school principal, joined shortly after as co-leader. The band was not formally named until the fall of 1963, when bassist Wayne Watling suggested "The Tempos" for the nine-piece group. This ensemble grew to sixteen pieces, performed swing and dance music at functions across Washington State, and remains active into the 2010s. Their genre is big band. Their context is Boeing social clubs, community dances, and function halls. Their world is not the Central District's R&B scene.

The CSV assigns The Tempos the genre **R&B/Soul**, the neighborhood **Central District**, the era **1960s**, energy **E=5**, and the label designation **local (or small labels)**. Their leadership was a Boeing engineer and a school principal -- not the profile of a 1960s Central District R&B group.

This creates the engine's first **name collision**: a band name that returns search results, but the results describe a different ensemble from the one the CSV data profiles. ## The Central District R&B Band Ecosystem: Where The Tempos Would Have Performed

### The 1960s Context

The CSV places The Tempos in the 1960s -- a decade earlier than Larry English's 1960s-1970s era and squarely in the pre-Hendrix and peak-teen-dance era of Seattle R&B. The 1960s Central District was the crucible where Seattle's Black musical traditions concentrated:

**The Venues (pre-1965):**

The Birdland Supper Club at 2203 East Madison Street was the epicenter. Dave Lewis's combo held court there from 1957 onward, playing to packed multiracial rooms. The club sat just below the redline that separated the predominantly Black Central District from white neighborhoods to the north. Birdland was where a young Jimi Hendrix snuck in underage to watch shows, where Solomon Burke and James Brown came through on national touring circuits, where the Five Checks and other vocal groups performed at the intersection of doo-wop and R&B. Birdland was razed in 1965, marking a dividing line in the Central District's musical chronology.

Other pre-1965 venues included the Black and Tan Club, which had operated since the 1930s as one of Seattle's few integrated nightclubs, hosting everything from Duke Ellington and Count Basie to local R&B acts. The Golden Crown, perched above a Chinese restaurant on Jackson Street, was a late-night destination where the music ran until 4 AM. The Trojan Horse and the District Tavern rounded out the circuit.

**The Venues (post-1965):**

After Birdland's demolition, the club scene shifted. KYAC radio (1250 AM), which began broadcasting in 1965 as Seattle's Black-owned and Black-operated station, became the sonic infrastructure connecting the remaining venues to the community. From 1965 through the mid-1970s, the Central District's soul and funk scene operated at full intensity -- clubs packed seven nights a week, local bands topping KYAC charts over national artists like Marvin Gaye.

**The Bands:**

## The 1960s: The Decade The Tempos Inhabited

### Pre-Hendrix Seattle

The CSV dates The Tempos to the **1960s** -- a tighter era designation than Larry English's "1960s-1970s" or The Dynamics' "1960-1970." The 1960s without the 1970s places The Tempos in a specific historical window: the era before Jimi Hendrix's return to prominence reshaped how the world saw Seattle's music, and before disco dissolved the Central District's live R&B scene.

The early-to-mid 1960s Central District was a place where multiple musical traditions coexisted and cross-pollinated:

**Doo-wop to R&B transition (early 1960s):** The Gallahads, Seattle's premier doo-wop group, had been active since 1952 and were winding down by the early 1960s. Their members dispersed into other ensembles -- the Dynamics, Viceroys, El Caminos, Statics, Counts, Soul Deacons, and Boss Five. This dispersal seeded the next generation of R&B groups with experienced vocal harmony practitioners. A group called The Tempos forming in the early 1960s would have drawn from this same talent pool of Central District musicians transitioning from doo-wop to harder R&B.

**The teen-dance circuit (1959-1966):** KJR radio's Pat O'Day had built a teen-dance empire spanning Spanish Castle, Parker's Ballroom, and dozens of other venues. The Dynamics (degree 27) operated primarily in this circuit, bridging Central District R&B with suburban teen audiences. An R&B group in the Central District could have worked both the neighborhood club circuit (Birdland, Black and Tan, Golden Crown) and the teen-dance circuit, though the two audiences had different racial compositions and different expectations.

**The soul revolution (mid-1960s):** By 1964-1965, the national soul revolution driven by Motown, Stax, and Atlantic was in full force. Seattle's Central District bands absorbed these influences through radio, touring acts, and the constant flow of records. The "Soul" in The Tempos' R&B/Soul genre designation likely reflects this mid-1960s influence -- the moment when raw R&B incorporated gospel fervor, smoother arrangements, and the vocal group harmonies that defined the era.

**The Encore Ballroom and the evolving circuit:** The Encore Ballroom at 1214 East Pike, while more associated with late-1960s psychedelic and rock acts under Matthew Katz's management, also hosted R&B shows booked by promoter Charles Sullivan, who ran a string of Northwest venues. The R&B performance circuit in 1960s Seattle was broader than just the Central District clubs -- bands could play anywhere from Pioneer Square to the suburban dance halls, though the Central District remained the home base.

## Theory Contributions

### The Thirty-Fifth Artist-City Pattern: The Shadow Name

The Tempos' relationship to Seattle produces the engine's thirty-fifth artist-city pattern: **The Shadow Name**. This is the pattern where a band or artist shares a name with a better-documented entity in the same city, causing searches to return results for the wrong ensemble. The Shadow Name goes beyond the pure documentation gap (degree 33, Larry English) -- where searches return nothing -- into active misdirection, where searches return something, but something false. The researcher finds data, but it is data about a different band. The documented Tempos (big band, Boeing engineer, swing) cast a shadow over the undocumented Tempos (R&B/Soul, Central District, 1960s). The shadow name is the documentation gap's more insidious cousin: instead of absence, there is a decoy.

The Shadow Name pattern is common in music history. "The Tempos" alone appears as at least four distinct entries on Discogs -- a Pittsburgh doo-wop group (who recorded "See You in September," 1959), a different group on separate labels, and others. Band names in the 1960s were frequently recycled because there was no centralized registry. A Central District R&B group calling themselves "The Tempos" in 1963 or 1964 would not have known about a Pittsburgh doo-wop group using the same name, and vice versa. The name collision is a structural feature of the era's music industry, not an accident.

### The Thirty-Fifth Acoustic Element: The Ensemble Blend

The thirty-fifth acoustic element is **the ensemble blend** -- the specific timbral result of multiple instruments and voices combining in a small-group R&B/Soul performance. Unlike solo acoustic elements (the bass groove lock at degree 32, the room tone at degree 33), the ensemble blend is the sound that exists BETWEEN the individual instruments -- the way a horn section's brass integrates with a rhythm guitar's chop, the way a bass line and a kick drum become a single composite sound, the way backing vocals merge into a unified harmonic texture.

The ensemble blend is the acoustic signature of the band format. It is what distinguishes a group performance from a solo performance at the physical level of sound waves. In the Central District clubs of the 1960s, the ensemble blend would have been shaped by the specific room acoustics (small venues, close audience proximity), the available equipment (smaller PA systems requiring tighter balance between instruments), and the musical tradition (R&B/Soul's emphasis on horn-section precision and rhythm-section lock).

The ensemble blend follows the room tone (degree 33) in the acoustic progression: the room tone described the ambient environment of the venue; the ensemble blend describes the composite sound that fills that environment. The thirty-fourth deployment of the blue note thread is **the unison bend** -- the blue note played simultaneously by multiple musicians who bend the pitch together, creating a thicker, more powerful micro-tonal inflection than any individual bend. ## The Significance of Degree 34

### Why This Degree Matters

Degree 34 matters because it introduces the **name collision** as a documentation phenomenon distinct from the pure documentation gap. At degree 33, the engine established that the absence of documentation is itself data. At degree 34, the engine establishes that the presence of wrong documentation is also data -- and in some ways more treacherous, because a researcher who finds the PNW Bands page for "The Tempos" might conclude they have identified the band, when in fact they have identified a different ensemble entirely.

The name collision is a structural feature of music history. In the 1960s, there was no internet, no centralized band-name registry, no way for a Central District R&B group to know that a Boeing engineer's swing band across town had adopted the same name. The collision was inevitable in a city with hundreds of active bands across multiple genres. The collision survived into the archival era because the big band's documentation -- PNW Bands page, Facebook presence, CD with liner notes -- outlasted whatever documentation the R&B group may have produced. The swing band's 60+ years of continuous performance created a durable archival footprint. The R&B group's unknown lifespan created none.

The engine, by placing an R&B/Soul group called "The Tempos" at the Central District coordinate with E=5 energy and a 1960s era, asserts the existence of a band that the online archive misidentifies. This is an act of documentary precision: the CSV data says this band existed, in this genre, in this neighborhood, in this era.
