## The Engine's First Name Collision

### When the Search Finds the Wrong Band

Degree 34 introduces a documentation phenomenon not encountered in the engine's first 33 degrees: the **name collision**. Searching for "The Tempos Seattle" returns a documented result -- a big band/swing ensemble founded in 1958 by Boeing engineer Henry Fichtner, co-led by Dr. Vernon J. Harkness, formally named in fall 1963, performing dance music at functions across Washington State. This is not the R&B/Soul group the CSV describes.

The name collision is structurally distinct from the pure documentation gap at degree 33 (Larry English):

| Degree | Search Result | Documentation State |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| 33 (Larry English) | Zero results | Absence |
| **34 (The Tempos)** | **Results for wrong band** | **Misdirection** |

The misdirection is more dangerous than absence because a researcher who finds the PNW Bands page for "The Tempos" might conclude the search is complete. The data exists; it is simply data about a different ensemble. The engine must distinguish between "no data found" and "wrong data found" -- both are documentation gaps, but the second requires an additional analytical step: verifying that the found data matches the search target.

- **Situation:** Searching for "The Tempos Seattle" returns documentation for a big band/swing ensemble, not the R&B/Soul group described by the CSV.
- **Root Cause:** Two unrelated bands in the same city adopted the same name during the same era. The big band's continuous 60+ year operation and web presence (PNW Bands page, Facebook, CD with liner notes) created a durable archival footprint that the R&B group's unknown lifespan did not. Band names in the 1960s were not registered or centrally catalogued.
- **Recommendation:** Establish THE SHADOW NAME as a documented pattern. When searches return results, verify that the found entity matches the CSV profile (genre, neighborhood, era, energy). Apply this verification to all future degrees.
- **Carry Item:** PATTERN -- The Shadow Name. Name collision at degree 34. Misdirection as documentation state. Apply verification protocol to all searches.

## Group Format Returns: The Unnamed Ensemble

### Band Structure Without Membership Data

The Tempos are the engine's first group since the Night Owls at degree 28 -- a gap of six degrees (29-33, all solo artists). The return to group format introduces a new structural category: the **unnamed ensemble**, where the band name exists but individual membership is unknown.

The engine's group documentation spectrum:

| Degree | Group | Documentation Level | Members Known |
|--------|-------|--------------------| --------------|
| 27 | The Dynamics | Extensive | 12+ members named with instruments, years, later careers |
| 28 | Night Owls | Minimal | Members unknown |
| **34** | **The Tempos** | **Name collision** | **Members unknown** |

The unnamed ensemble raises questions specific to the group format that solo degrees do not encounter: who played what instrument, who sang lead, how many members were there, when did personnel change. These questions have no answers for The Tempos, making the documentation gap structurally deeper than for a solo artist -- more information is lost when a group is undocumented than when an individual is undocumented.

- **Situation:** The Tempos are a group/band with no documented membership.
- **Root Cause:** The R&B/Soul Tempos did not leave recordings, press materials, or archival traces that would identify individual members. The name collision with the big band further obscures research.
- **Recommendation:** Track the unnamed ensemble as a structural category alongside The Unrecorded (individual gap) and The Shadow Name (name collision).
- **Carry Item:** PATTERN -- The unnamed ensemble. Group-level documentation gap. First instance at degree 28 (Night Owls), extended at degree 34 (The Tempos). Track.

## What Worked

### Research Process

1. **The name collision was identified correctly.** The research found the PNW Bands page for "The Tempos" and correctly identified it as a different ensemble from the CSV's R&B/Soul group. This required comparing the found data's attributes (genre: big band; founder: Boeing engineer; context: community dances) against the CSV's attributes (genre: R&B/Soul; neighborhood: Central District; era: 1960s). The mismatch was clear and documented.

2. **The degree 33 precedent applied well.** The documentation gap as data principle established at degree 33 transferred directly to degree 34. The research framework for handling undocumented artists was already in place; degree 34 extended it to handle the more complex case of wrong documentation rather than absent documentation.

3. **The group format analysis adds structural depth.** The engine's first analysis of an undocumented group (as opposed to an undocumented individual) produced new conceptual categories: the unnamed ensemble, collective tidal energy (E=5 mode 3), the ensemble blend (acoustic element), and the unison bend (blue note deployment). The group format is generative for theory even when individual data is absent.

4. **Cross-references to The Dynamics (degree 27) are productive.** The Dynamics provide a documented example of what The Tempos' documentation COULD have looked like -- same city, same era, overlapping genre. The contrast between documented and undocumented groups in the same scene is analytically useful.

### Source Quality

5. **Strong for the scene context; absent for the specific R&B group.** The Central District's 1960s R&B scene is well-documented through HistoryLink, the Wheedle's Groove project, SeattleMet, KUOW/KNKX, and Peter Blecha's work. The name collision entity (big band Tempos) is documented on PNW Bands. The specific R&B/Soul group is not documented anywhere.

   - **Root Cause:** The research treated the two Tempos as entirely separate entities. They may have had indirect connections through Seattle's musical ecosystem.
