## ISSAQUAH ENTERS -- THE EASTSIDE

### Beyond Seattle

Issaquah is a new city and a new geographic concept in the engine:

- **Issaquah.** A small city at the base of the Cascade foothills, east of Lake Washington. Population approximately 40,000. Known for Salmon Days, the Issaquah Alps (Tiger Mountain, Squak Mountain, Cougar Mountain), and a suburban/exurban identity very different from Seattle.
- **The Eastside.** Issaquah is on the Eastside -- the suburban and exurban region east of Lake Washington that includes Bellevue, Kirkland, Redmond, and Issaquah. The engine has been entirely Seattle-centric (Capitol Hill, Ballard, Greenwood, University District). Issaquah breaks that pattern. The Eastside is a different world: strip malls, cul-de-sacs, forests, mountains. Not the urban density of Capitol Hill.
- **Why Issaquah matters for Modest Mouse.** Isaac Brock grew up in Issaquah. The suburban alienation, the boredom, the sense of being in a place that isn't quite anywhere -- these are core themes in Modest Mouse's early work. *The Lonesome Crowded West* is about driving through the suburban West. That West starts in Issaquah.
- **Engine geography update.** First entry outside Seattle city limits (since the engine began). The geographic vocabulary expands from neighborhoods to the broader region. The Eastside enters.

## ISAAC BROCK -- THE RESTLESS VOICE

### Angular, Searching, Unfinished

Isaac Brock is Modest Mouse:

- **Isaac Brock.** Singer, guitarist, primary songwriter. Brock's voice is distinctive: nasal, angular, sometimes cracking, sometimes shouting, always searching. He sounds like someone who can't sit still -- the vocal equivalent of pacing.
- **Guitar style.** Angular, spiky, unpredictable. Brock uses unusual tunings and rhythmic patterns. The guitar parts are not conventionally melodic -- they jab, stutter, circle, and occasionally explode. The style owes something to punk, something to the American West, and something to nobody else.
- **Lyrical approach.** Brock's lyrics are oblique, image-heavy, philosophically restless. They deal with the American landscape, suburban emptiness, mortality, the absurdity of existence. Not confessional (that's Bazan). Not narrative (that's concept albums). More like dispatches from a road trip through the existential desert.
- **The constant.** Like Bazan in Pedro the Lion, Brock is the constant in a changing lineup. Modest Mouse has had many members (most notably drummer Jeremiah Green, who was with the band from 1993 until his death in 2022, and bassist Eric Judy). But the creative center is Brock.

## THE ARC: LO-FI INDIE TO MAINSTREAM

### Three Albums, Three Eras

Modest Mouse's trajectory is one of the most dramatic in PNW indie history:

- ***This Is a Long Drive for Someone with Nothing to Think About* (1996).** The debut. Up Records. Lo-fi, sprawling, restless. Long song titles. The sound of driving through the American West with no destination. Recorded cheaply, sounding like it was made in a garage (which parts of it were). E=5 energy -- angular but not yet full.
- ***The Lonesome Crowded West* (1997).** The masterpiece. Up Records. Everything intensified: the guitar angles are sharper, the rhythms more complex, the lyrics more desperate. "Trailer Trash," "Cowboy Dan," "Teeth Like God's Shoeshine" -- anthems of suburban alienation and Western desolation. This album is widely considered one of the greatest indie rock albums of the 1990s. E=6 energy -- the full Modest Mouse intensity.
- ***The Moon & Antarctica* (2000).** The pivot. Epic Records. Modest Mouse signs to a major label. The production is bigger, cleaner, more layered. The themes become more cosmic -- the moon, Antarctica, the vastness of space as metaphor for the vastness of isolation. The transition from indie to major was smooth enough that fans didn't revolt. E=5-6 energy.
- ***Good News for People Who Love Bad News* (2004).** The breakthrough. Epic Records. "Float On" becomes a massive hit -- one of the biggest indie-to-mainstream crossover songs of the decade. Modest Mouse goes from cult band to arena act. The album is more accessible, poppier, but still angular. E=5 energy -- smoother edges.
- **Post-*Good News*.** Several more albums, including *We Were Dead Before the Ship Even Sank* (2007, featuring former Smiths guitarist Johnny Marr), *Strangers to Ourselves* (2015), *The Golden Casket* (2021). Each with moments of brilliance, none with the concentrated intensity of *The Lonesome Crowded West*.

(Sources: Modest Mouse discography; Up Records; Epic Records; *The Lonesome Crowded West*; *Good News*; career arc)

## UP RECORDS -- THE STARTING POINT

### The Label Connection

Modest Mouse's Up Records connection links them to 764-HERO (degree 196):

- **Up Records roster.** Modest Mouse and 764-HERO were both core Up Records artists. Chris Takino's label was their shared home. The aesthetic connection is real: both bands valued restraint over flash, texture over virtuosity, and emotional intelligence over spectacle. (Modest Mouse would outgrow this aesthetic, but it's where they started.)
- **From Up to Epic.** The transition from Up Records (indie, Seattle, small) to Epic Records (major label, corporate, massive) is one of the defining moves in 2000s indie rock. Modest Mouse proved that an angular, difficult, philosophically complex band could make the jump without entirely losing their identity.
- **Built to Spill connection.** Built to Spill (another Up Records band) toured with Modest Mouse and shared the label. The Up Records community was small but influential.

(Sources: Up Records; Chris Takino; Modest Mouse-764-HERO connection; Up to Epic transition; Built to Spill)

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Modest Mouse

- **Tempo.** Variable. 100-170 BPM. Many songs change tempo mid-track. The rhythm section is not a metronome -- it breathes, rushes, drags.
- **Guitar.** Angular, spiky, unpredictable. Unusual tunings. Staccato picking patterns. Distorted power chords alternate with clean, jangly figures. The guitar does not play conventional melodies -- it creates patterns that are rhythmic and textural as much as melodic.
- **Bass.** Eric Judy's bass is melodic, driving, and essential. Often the most conventionally "musical" instrument in the mix. The bass provides the harmonic foundation while the guitar does angular things on top.
- **Drums.** Jeremiah Green's drumming was the engine. Complex patterns, unusual accents, fills that drove the songs forward with relentless energy. Green's playing was one of the most distinctive elements of the Modest Mouse sound.
- **Vocals.** Brock's voice: nasal, angular, sometimes cracking. He can whisper, speak, sing, and shout, sometimes within a single verse. The vocal style is anti-pretty -- not ugly, but deliberately unpolished. The imperfections are the point.
- **Dynamics.** Extreme. Modest Mouse songs can be nearly silent one moment and overwhelmingly loud the next. The dynamic shifts are often abrupt -- not the gradual builds of SDRE but sudden, jarring transitions.
- **Production.** Lo-fi on early albums (tape hiss, room noise, clipping). Progressively cleaner on later work. The lo-fi quality of the early recordings is part of the aesthetic -- it sounds like it was made by people who couldn't afford better gear, and that scrappiness is part of the emotional truth.

(Sources: Modest Mouse discography; production analysis; Brock vocal style; Green drumming; Judy bass; indie rock sonic characteristics)
