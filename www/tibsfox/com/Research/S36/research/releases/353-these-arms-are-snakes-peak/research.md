## THE PEAK RECORDS -- EASTER (2006) AND THE TAIL WAGGING THE DOG ERA

### Post-Hardcore from Capitol Hill. The Closing Arc Resolves.

These Arms Are Snakes formed in Seattle in 2003, drawing from members of several prior PNW bands. The band's formation represents a specific post-post-grunge moment in the PNW music scene: not grunge, not the 1990s crossover/thrash tradition, but the post-hardcore that emerged in the early 2000s from bands that were processed through the legacy of Botch, Refused, and At the Drive-In.

**Easter (2006, Suicide Squeeze Records).** The peak record of the These Arms Are Snakes catalog. Produced with Toshi Kasai (who also produced the Melvins -- a connection the engine notes). Eleven tracks. The album is the clearest statement of what These Arms Are Snakes were: a band that could operate in the sonic territory of aggressive post-hardcore (loud, rhythmically complex, not conventionally structured) while maintaining a melodic intelligence and an ear for dynamics that differentiated them from the more mechanically aggressive bands in their immediate peer group.

The specific character of *Easter*: it is more varied in its dynamics than Botch's *We Are the Romans* (which uses dynamics as structure between sections) -- *Easter* varies tempo, texture, and intensity within songs as well as between them. The intrasong variation is the album's primary sonic signature. Songs begin in one mode and arrive in a different mode without the transition being predictable. This is post-hardcore's defining compositional challenge: maintain the energy of hardcore punk's urgency while introducing the structural complexity that post-rock and noise rock had developed. *Easter* succeeds.

**The Tail Wagging the Dog era.** The final album *Tail Wagging the Dog* (2008) marked the band's dissolution. It was released after the decision to end the band had been made. The peak period properly ends with *Easter* (2006). The 2003-2007 arc is the engine's calibration window.

**Steve Snere's vocals.** Snere's vocal approach on *Easter* is the post-hardcore vocal -- not the aggressive screaming of metal or the compression-release of grunge, but a voice that alternates between spoken, sung, and shouted registers within the same song, sometimes within the same phrase. The register-switching is not stylistic affectation; it is a compositional tool for expressing the song's emotional complexity without committing to a single emotional register throughout. The ambivalence is in the delivery: the voice that is not sure whether it is singing or shouting is expressing something that the song is about.

**Brian Cook's bass.** Brian Cook appears again -- this time in These Arms Are Snakes. Cook had been in Botch (degree 352). His bass approach in TAAS is different from the compositionally independent voice he developed in Botch: in the TAAS context, the bass is more rhythmically supportive, more closely aligned with the drum part, providing the rhythmic foundation that the guitars and vocals depart from. The comparison documents the bassist's range: Cook's Botch bass and his TAAS bass are different approaches from the same musician at different stages in different band contexts.

**Chris Common's drums.** Common's drumming on *Easter* is the album's rhythmic anchor: powerful enough to hold the sonic intensity of the loud sections, precise enough to provide the foundation for the intrasong dynamics. The drums do not dominate the mix as Botch's drums do -- they are part of the texture, providing momentum rather than percussion events. The approach matches the post-hardcore aesthetic: rhythm as forward motion rather than impact event.

**Ryan Frederiksen's guitar.** The guitar work on *Easter* is angular and dissonant in the post-hardcore tradition, but more tonally varied than the flat-out distortion of TAD or the irregular-time-signature precision of Botch. Frederiksen introduces cleaner passages, texture-based playing, and feedback-oriented sections alongside the more aggressive passages. The tonal range is the post-hardcore guitar's characteristic: not always distorted, not always aggressive, using the full palette.

## THESE ARMS ARE SNAKES -- THE BAND AT PEAK

### Capitol Hill, Seattle. Suicide Squeeze Records. Post-Hardcore. E=9.

The band formed in Capitol Hill, Seattle -- returning the closing arc's geographic origin to the neighborhood where it began. Pearl Jam formed in Capitol Hill (degree 349). These Arms Are Snakes formed in Capitol Hill (degree 353). The arc that opened at Capitol Hill closes at Capitol Hill. The geographic closure is the closing arc's final structural element.

**The Suicide Squeeze Records position.** Suicide Squeeze Records is a Seattle-based independent label founded in 1996. The label's roster in the 2003-2008 period included Death Cab for Cutie (early), Aqueduct, and The Helio Sequence. TAAS occupied the label's heavy end: the most aggressive, most technically demanding band in the Suicide Squeeze catalog of their era. The label's range -- from the indie pop of Death Cab to the post-hardcore of These Arms Are Snakes -- mirrors the broader PNW music ecosystem's range.

**The Botch connection.** Brian Cook's presence in both Botch (degree 352) and TAAS (degree 353) is the engine's most explicit cross-reference in the closing arc: the same musician in consecutive degrees, in different bands, in different sonic registers, at different points in his career. The connection documents a specific lineage in Seattle's technical extreme music scene: the musicians who came through Botch's influence and carried elements of that approach into the post-Botch landscape.

**The Toshi Kasai production.** Kasai's production of *Easter* established the sonic environment for the album's dynamics. The same producer who worked with the Melvins applied a related approach to TAAS: maximizing the dynamic range, preserving the live-room qualities of the performance, and trusting the band's compositional intelligence to carry the album rather than imposing an external sonic framework. The production does not overwhelm the music; it clarifies it.
