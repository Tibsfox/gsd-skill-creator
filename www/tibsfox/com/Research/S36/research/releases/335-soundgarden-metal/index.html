The U-District Foundation

Soundgarden formed in Seattle's University District in 1984, before Sub Pop Records existed and before grunge had a name. Cornell, Thayil, Yamamoto, and Matt Cameron built their sound in the neighborhood's venues and rehearsal spaces: too heavy for the indie underground, too artistic for the metal scene. A&M Records signed them in 1988, making Soundgarden one of the first Seattle alternative bands with major-label support. They were already developing what would become one of the most distinctive sounds in American heavy music: Cornell's extraordinary voice against Thayil's down-tuned, modally-influenced guitar.

"The falsetto is not an effect. It is the release point after the pressure of everything below it. You build to it. The song goes there because it has to."

The Badmotorfinger Moment

Badmotorfinger (October 1, 1991) was released in the same week as Nirvana's Nevermind (September 24) — one of the most densely significant weeks in American music history. The two records represent two approaches to the same moment: Nirvana's pop-accessible soft-loud dynamics vs. Soundgarden's technically demanding heavy metal. Nevermind outsold it massively; Badmotorfinger established Soundgarden's artistic reputation as the most ambitious of the Seattle bands. "Rusty Cage" was later covered by Johnny Cash for Rick Rubin's Unchained (1996) — a cross-genre connection as unexpected as any in the era.

Temple of the Dog — The Tribute

Andrew Wood, the singer of Mother Love Bone, died of a heroin overdose in March 1990. He had been Cornell's roommate. In tribute, Cornell assembled Temple of the Dog, recording the self-titled album with members of what would become Pearl Jam. "Hunger Strike" — Cornell and Eddie Vedder in call-and-response — is one of the defining recordings of the Seattle scene's internal community. The Temple of the Dog record documents the scene's emotional depth: these musicians knew each other, lived together, and the losses were real.
