## THE FREMONT SECOND ENTRY

### Consecutive Fremont Degrees

Degrees 321-322 deliver two consecutive Fremont bands:

- **The neighborhood in two degrees.** Gas Huffer (321, AmRep) and Pond (322, Sub Pop) represent Fremont's two-label contribution to Seattle's 1990s music. Gas Huffer was the AmRep noise-rock alternative; Pond was the Sub Pop grunge band with a slightly more conventional approach. Together they demonstrate that Fremont was producing music from two distinct aesthetic corners simultaneously.
- **Fremont in 1992.** By 1992, Fremont's identity as Seattle's eccentric alternative neighborhood was established. The Fremont Troll (installed 1990) had become a landmark. The neighborhood was home to artists, musicians, light-manufacturing, and the boat canal industrial zone that gave Fremont its blue-collar-adjacent character. Pond formed in this environment: not Capitol Hill's densely networked scene, but Fremont's more neighborhood-rooted community.
- **The Fremont-Sub Pop connection.** Sub Pop was based in Capitol Hill but signed bands from across Seattle's neighborhoods. Pond's Fremont origin with a Sub Pop deal represents the label's geographic reach beyond its home territory.

## POND -- THE BAND

### Formation and Personnel

Pond formed in Seattle in 1992 -- at the apex of the commercial grunge moment:

- **Chris Brady (guitar/vocals).** Primary songwriter and central creative force. Brady's songwriting is the defining element of Pond's sound: heavy grunge riffs with a slightly more melodic approach than the AmRep tradition. His guitar playing prioritizes chord density and mid-tempo heaviness over speed or technical complexity.
- **Charlie Campbell (guitar).** Second guitar. Campbell's role is textural -- adding harmonic depth to Brady's riff-focused playing. The two-guitar arrangement gives Pond a fuller sound than many of their peers.
- **Dave Triebwasser (bass).** Low-end anchor. Triebwasser's bass playing is prominent in the mix -- the Sub Pop production approach for Pond emphasizes low-end weight, which distinguishes Pond from some of the more treble-forward grunge recordings.
- **Phil Ek (drums).** Ek is one of the most important figures in Pacific Northwest recording history -- not just as a musician but as a producer. As a drummer, he was reliable and well-suited to Pond's mid-tempo grunge. As a producer (later), he worked with Built to Spill, Fleet Foxes, Band of Horses, and numerous other Pacific Northwest acts. Ek left Pond to pursue production, and the band replaced him.
- **Various drummers.** Pond went through several drummers during their run, which was not unusual for Seattle bands at the time.

(Sources: Pond biography; Chris Brady; Charlie Campbell; Dave Triebwasser; Phil Ek biography as drummer and producer; Phil Ek production work Built to Spill Fleet Foxes Band of Horses; Pond personnel changes)

## THE SUB POP SECONDARY ROSTER

### The Label at Its Commercial Peak

Sub Pop in 1992-1995 was managing the consequences of Nirvana's major-label departure while simultaneously building a secondary roster:

- **Sub Pop's position after Nirvana.** When Nirvana signed to DGC Records in 1991, Sub Pop lost their most commercially significant act. The label retained Mudhoney (who stayed with Sub Pop through the 1990s by choice), but the loss of Nirvana's commercial momentum required Sub Pop to develop new acts. Pond was part of this secondary roster development.
- **The secondary roster dynamic.** Sub Pop's secondary roster in 1992-1995 included bands that were competent, stylistically consistent with the grunge template, and commercially viable at the indie level without having the breakthrough potential of Nirvana or the artistic distinctiveness of Mudhoney. Pond fits this profile: solid grunge band, Sub Pop-appropriate sound, modest commercial success.
- **The Sub Pop production aesthetic for Pond.** Sub Pop's production approach for their grunge secondary roster was to maintain the signature sound -- heavy guitars, low-end emphasis, mid-tempo rhythm -- without the maximalist production experiments that some major-label grunge records employed. Pond's Sub Pop releases sound like Sub Pop records: the Seattle sound as institutionalized aesthetic.
- **Significance of the secondary roster.** The Sub Pop secondary roster is historically important as documentation of what grunge sounded like as a genre in the hands of competent practitioners without the commercial singularity of the summit bands. Pond documents grunge at E=7 -- not the peak of TAD (E=8) or the singular power of Nirvana (summit), but the solid, working grunge band operating within the established form.

(Sources: Sub Pop post-Nirvana situation; Mudhoney Sub Pop retention; Sub Pop secondary roster 1992-1995; Pond as secondary roster entry; Sub Pop production aesthetic; grunge as institutionalized genre; E=7 working-band analysis)

## THE SOUND -- GRUNGE, E=7

### The Genre at Practice

Pond's music is grunge in its practiced, competent form:

- **E=7 through genre adherence.** Pond reaches E=7 through faithful execution of the grunge template. The guitar tones are heavy and distorted (Sub Pop standard). The rhythm section drives mid-tempo. The vocals are appropriately raw without the distinctive sonic signatures of Cornell's range or Vedder's baritone. E=7 is the energy level of grunge competently executed -- not the energy of a singular band at the genre's peak, but the energy of the form itself.
- **Melody within heaviness.** Pond's distinguishing characteristic within the grunge template is a slightly elevated melodic sense in the songwriting. Where some grunge bands (TAD, Hammerbox at maximum) lead with noise, Pond leads with the riff as a melodic entity. The melodies are not pop-melodic, but they are memorable in a way that more noise-forward grunge bands are not.
- **Production.** Pond's Sub Pop releases are produced at the label's mid-1990s standard -- cleaner than the lo-fi early Sub Pop recordings (the Nirvana Bleach era production), not as expansive as the major-label productions that were competing for attention during the same period. The production serves the music without drawing attention to itself.
- **The mid-tempo groove.** Pond's songs are mid-tempo in a way that distinguishes them from Gas Huffer's punk-paced approach (degree 321). The tempo is grunge-tempo: not slow, not fast, the pace of a heavy riff that needs time to breathe and develop. This tempo is one of grunge's defining formal characteristics -- it comes from Black Sabbath, from Neil Young and Crazy Horse, from the Melvins.
- **Song craft.** Pond's songs have conventional song structure -- verse, chorus, verse, chorus, bridge or guitar break, chorus. This structural conventionality is part of what makes Pond accessible within the grunge context: the songs are songs, not sonic experiments or attitude statements. The form is legible.

(Sources: Pond recordings; E=7 grunge adherence; melodic sensibility within grunge; Sub Pop mid-1990s production standard; mid-tempo grunge as formal characteristic; Black Sabbath Neil Young Melvins tempo lineage; conventional song structure; legible form)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Sub Pop Run

- ***Pond* (1993, Sub Pop).** Debut. Self-titled. Establishes the Pond sound: heavy grunge, mid-tempo, Sub Pop production. The record demonstrates that the grunge template could be executed without the biographical drama of the summit bands.
- ***The Practice of Joy Before Death* (1995, Sub Pop).** Second album. The title alone -- simultaneously earnest and absurdist -- encapsulates Pond's character. The music is denser and more developed than the debut. The production is fuller. Released in the middle of grunge's commercial collapse, the record received positive reviews without commercial traction.
- ***Rock Collection* (1997, Sub Pop).** Third album. Released after the commercial moment for grunge had definitively passed, Rock Collection shows Pond continuing to develop their sound in a commercial environment that had moved on. The record is a document of artistic persistence in an inhospitable climate.

(Sources: Pond discography; self-titled debut 1993 Sub Pop; The Practice of Joy Before Death 1995; Rock Collection 1997; Sub Pop release history; grunge commercial collapse context)

## PHIL EK -- THE HIDDEN SIGNIFICANCE

### A Drummer Who Became a Producer

Phil Ek's significance to the engine extends beyond his tenure in Pond:

- **Ek's production career.** After leaving Pond, Phil Ek became one of the Pacific Northwest's most important record producers. His production credits include: Built to Spill (*There's Nothing Wrong with Love*, *Perfect from Now On*, *Keep It Like a Secret*), Fleet Foxes (*Fleet Foxes*, *Helplessness Blues*), Band of Horses (*Everything All the Time*, *Cease to Begin*), The Shins, Mudhoney, Mark Lanegan, and many others.
- **Ek as Pacific Northwest production continuity.** Ek's production work represents a thread of continuity in Pacific Northwest music: from his drum work with Pond in the grunge era (1992-1995) to his production of the folk-rock, indie-rock, and alternative acts that defined the region's music in the 2000s-2010s. Pond was Ek's training ground; the Pacific Northwest music scene of the 2000s is partly his creation.
- **Fleet Foxes and the engine.** Fleet Foxes -- produced by Ek -- will appear in future engine degrees as one of Seattle's defining post-grunge acts. The Pond-Ek-Fleet Foxes thread is a continuous line through Seattle music from 1992 to 2008. A drummer in a Sub Pop grunge band becomes the producer of one of the most acclaimed folk-rock records of the 2000s. This is how cities produce musical culture: through the people who move between roles.
- **The Ek principle.** Phil Ek's career demonstrates that the musicians in the sub-summit bands are not merely secondary figures; they are often the people who shape the next generation. The secondary roster of Sub Pop's grunge era produced the producers, engineers, and aesthetic mentors who guided Pacific Northwest music through the post-grunge era.

(Sources: Phil Ek producer biography; Built to Spill production credits; Fleet Foxes production credits; Band of Horses production credits; The Shins; Mudhoney production; Mark Lanegan; Ek as PNW production continuity; Fleet Foxes engine future entry; musician-to-producer career arc)

## LEGACY AND SIGNIFICANCE

### The Craft Document

Pond's legacy is documentary:

- **Grunge as a reproducible genre.** Pond demonstrates that grunge was a learnable, reproducible form -- that the aesthetic and sonic characteristics of the genre could be competently executed by bands who were not Nirvana or Pearl Jam. This is important evidence against the mythology that grunge was entirely dependent on biographical singularity. The form was transferable.
- **The Sub Pop secondary roster significance.** The secondary roster of any influential label is historically significant as documentation of the cultural conditions that the label's famous acts emerged from. Pond documents what grunge sounded like as a scene, not just as a handful of exceptional bands.
- **Phil Ek as the lasting contribution.** Pond's lasting contribution to Pacific Northwest music is Phil Ek. The band gave the producer his formative musical experience; the producer then shaped the region's music for the next two decades. Pond is the origin point of one of Seattle's most significant production careers.
- **Fremont's documentation.** Alongside Gas Huffer (321), Pond documents Fremont's specific contribution to Seattle's 1990s music. Two bands, two labels, one neighborhood, two consecutive degrees: the Fremont chapter is complete.
