Post-Botch Capitol Hill

These Arms Are Snakes formed in Seattle in 2003 — the year after Botch dissolved. The connection is personnel, not merely aesthetic: bassist Brian Cook moved directly from Botch into TAAS, bringing the rhythmic approach, the bass register, and the angular sensibility of Pacific Northwest mathcore into a new post-hardcore context. Steve Snere (vocals), Ryan Frederiksen (guitar), and Chris Common (drums) completed the lineup.

The geographic shift matters: Botch was Tacoma. These Arms Are Snakes were Capitol Hill — Seattle's arts and bohemian neighborhood, the center of the city's alternative music scene from the late 1980s through the 2000s. The Tacoma-to-Capitol Hill move maps onto a sonic move: from the industrial, maximum-intensity mathcore of Botch (E=9) to the more expansive, atmospheric post-hardcore of TAAS (E=8).

"E=8 is not a reduction from E=9 — it is an expansion. One step below the ceiling opens space for dynamics, for texture, for directions that maximum intensity forecloses."

Suicide Squeeze Records

These Arms Are Snakes released their catalog on Suicide Squeeze Records, founded in Seattle in 1996 by David Dickenson. Suicide Squeeze is one of the most important Capitol Hill-rooted independent labels in Pacific Northwest music history — home to early Modest Mouse, Hella, Aesop Rock, and others. The label's eclectic range is consistent with Capitol Hill's culture: serious, independent, non-commercial, spanning post-hardcore and experimental rock.

TAAS on Suicide Squeeze placed the band at the center of Capitol Hill's mid-2000s music culture. The label was as much a scene marker as a distribution mechanism — being on Suicide Squeeze meant being embedded in the Capitol Hill independent music community of the era.

The Sound — E=8 Post-Hardcore

TAAS's E=8 post-hardcore maintains the angular unpredictability of Botch-lineage music — the sense that the song might shift direction at any moment, the dense rhythmic interplay, the refusal of conventional song structure. But E=8 is not E=9: the ceiling is lower, which means more room. TAAS incorporates atmospheric passages, varied dynamics, slower sections that build tension through means other than pure technical attack. The Botch DNA is audible in the bass (Cook), in the angular riffs, in the rhythmic complexity; the post-hardcore context adds texture and range.

Snere's vocals are integrated into the sonic texture rather than dominating it — the voice as one instrument among several. This is consistent with Capitol Hill's 2000s aesthetic: the post-hardcore mode where intensity is distributed across the full band rather than concentrated in a single vocal performance.
