## THE BOTCH LINEAGE

### From Mathcore to Post-Hardcore: The Brian Cook Connection

These Arms Are Snakes is inseparable from Botch. Degree 289 brought Botch's return at maximum energy (E=9); degree 290 follows immediately with the band that carries the Botch lineage forward:

- **Brian Cook.** The direct connection. Cook was the bassist in Botch from 1997 until the band's dissolution in 2002. After Botch ended, Cook formed These Arms Are Snakes in Seattle in 2003 alongside Steve Snere (vocals), Ryan Frederiksen (guitar), and Chris Common (drums). The Botch-to-TAAS line is one of the defining genealogical threads in Seattle's post-hardcore history.
- **The Tacoma-Seattle axis.** Botch formed in Tacoma and operated primarily from there (degree 289). These Arms Are Snakes were a Capitol Hill band -- the Seattle proper continuation of the mathcore/post-hardcore trajectory. The geographic shift from Tacoma (industrial, working-class) to Capitol Hill (arts, bohemian, urban) mirrors the sonic shift from Botch's maximum-intensity mathcore to TAAS's more expansive post-hardcore approach.
- **Genre evolution.** Botch's mathcore was built on irregular time signatures and controlled technical violence. These Arms Are Snakes expanded the palette -- maintaining the angular, unpredictable quality of Botch-lineage music while incorporating more textural, atmospheric, and experimental elements. The energy is E=8 (high, engaged, volatile) rather than Botch's E=9 (maximum intensity). The move from 9 to 8 is not a reduction but an expansion: more sonic territory available when not operating at the absolute ceiling.
- **The post-Botch vacuum.** Botch left an enormous absence in Pacific Northwest post-hardcore when they dissolved in 2002. They were one of the defining bands of the genre nationally. These Arms Are Snakes, along with other Botch alumni projects, occupied part of that vacuum. Cook's bass presence was recognizable -- the register, the approach to rhythm section dynamics -- even in the different musical context.

(Sources: Brian Cook Botch bassist 1997-2002; These Arms Are Snakes formation 2003; Capitol Hill Seattle; post-Botch Pacific Northwest post-hardcore; Tacoma-to-Seattle shift; genre evolution mathcore to post-hardcore)

## THESE ARMS ARE SNAKES -- THE BAND

### Formation and Personnel

These Arms Are Snakes formed in Seattle in 2003:

- **Steve Snere (vocals).** TAAS's vocalist brought a different register to the band than Botch's Josh Mans. Snere's vocal approach ranges across the post-hardcore spectrum -- from the intense and confrontational to the more melodic and textural -- giving TAAS a broader emotional range than the controlled aggression of Botch.
- **Ryan Frederiksen (guitar).** The guitar work in TAAS maintains the angular, unexpected quality associated with Pacific Northwest post-hardcore while incorporating more varied textures. The guitar is not purely technical/aggressive in the Botch manner; it is also atmospheric, capable of sustaining mood through dynamics and space.
- **Brian Cook (bass).** Carrying the Botch lineage directly. Cook's bass playing is a recognizable presence -- prominent, rhythmically complex, operating in the space between rhythm section anchor and harmonic voice. In TAAS, the bass is often high in the mix, a continuation of Cook's approach in Botch.
- **Chris Common (drums).** Common's drumming provided TAAS with a rhythmic foundation that could move between the angular, technical patterns associated with Botch-lineage music and the more textured, atmospheric playing required by TAAS's expanded sonic range. Common would go on to significant production work after TAAS.
- **Later lineup changes.** The band saw some member changes across their run. Danny Rossen contributed guitar in later periods. The core identity of Cook and Frederiksen remained consistent.

(Sources: Steve Snere vocals; Ryan Frederiksen guitar; Brian Cook bass; Chris Common drums; Danny Rossen guitar; lineup evolution 2003-2009)

## SUICIDE SQUEEZE RECORDS

### Seattle's Post-Hardcore Home Label

Suicide Squeeze Records is the label context that defines These Arms Are Snakes' position in Seattle music history:

- **Founding.** Suicide Squeeze was founded in Seattle in 1996 by David Dickenson. The label emerged from the same Capitol Hill scene that produced much of Seattle's 1990s and 2000s indie and post-hardcore music. It is one of the most important small labels in Pacific Northwest music history.
- **Catalog.** Suicide Squeeze has released records by a remarkable range of Pacific Northwest and national artists: Modest Mouse (early catalog), Hella, Aesop Rock, This Is Your Captain Speaking, Port O'Brien, and others. The label's range is eclectic but consistently rooted in independent, non-mainstream rock and hip-hop.
- **TAAS on Suicide Squeeze.** These Arms Are Snakes' records on Suicide Squeeze -- *Oxeneers or the Lion Sleeps When Its Antelope Go Home* (2004), *Easter* (2006), and *Tail Swallower and Dove* (2008) -- placed the band in the label's catalog alongside other Pacific Northwest post-hardcore and experimental music.
- **The label as scene marker.** Being on Suicide Squeeze in the mid-2000s placed a band at the center of Capitol Hill's music culture. The label's reputation was built on signing bands that operated at the intersection of technical ability and genuine artistic experimentation -- exactly where TAAS lived.
- **Capitol Hill context.** Suicide Squeeze's Seattle address aligned with Capitol Hill's role as the city's arts and music neighborhood. The label was embedded in the same physical and cultural space as the venues, record shops, and scene infrastructure that sustained Capitol Hill's music community.

(Sources: Suicide Squeeze Records founded 1996 David Dickenson; Suicide Squeeze catalog; Modest Mouse early releases; TAAS on Suicide Squeeze; Capitol Hill Seattle music scene 2000s)

## THE SOUND -- POST-HARDCORE, E=8

### Angular, Textural, Volatile

These Arms Are Snakes' sonic identity is defined by its position between Botch's extreme technical precision and a more expansive post-hardcore approach:

- **E=8 defined.** The engine's high-intensity range -- one step below Botch's maximum (E=9). E=8 is volatile, angular, dense, but not operating at absolute physical limit. TAAS at E=8 has room to breathe, to shift, to use dynamics in ways that pure mathcore does not allow. The lower ceiling opens space.
- **Influence of Botch lineage.** The Botch DNA is audible in TAAS: the angular riffs, the complex rhythmic interplay, the sense that the music might go somewhere unexpected at any moment. This is not imitation but inheritance -- Cook carries the rhythmic approach; the other members contribute different registers.
- **Expanded textural range.** TAAS incorporated elements less present in Botch: atmospheric passages, more varied dynamic range, slower sections that build tension differently from pure technical attack. The post-hardcore genre gave TAAS permission to use the full dynamic spectrum.
- **Density and shift.** Like Botch-lineage music generally, TAAS songs are dense and unpredictable. The density is not always maximally physical; it can be textural density, the density of many elements happening simultaneously at different registers and intensities. The shift -- sudden changes in direction, dynamics, or texture -- is a signature of the lineage.
- **Vocals as texture.** Snere's vocals operate differently from many post-hardcore bands: they are integrated into the sonic texture rather than dominating it. This is consistent with the Capitol Hill aesthetic of the period -- the human voice as one instrument among several rather than the focal point of the song.

(Sources: These Arms Are Snakes recordings; post-hardcore genre characteristics; E=8 calibration; Botch lineage in TAAS sound; dynamic range expansion; vocal approach)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### Three Albums, 2004-2008

- ***Oxeneers or the Lion Sleeps When Its Antelope Go Home* (2004, Suicide Squeeze).** The debut. Establishes the TAAS sonic identity: angular post-hardcore built on the Botch lineage but with more atmospheric and textural range. Received positively in post-hardcore circles. The title's density signals the band's approach: not simple.
- ***Easter* (2006, Suicide Squeeze / Equal Vision).** The second album, recorded with collaboration from outside producers. Easter is considered the band's most complete statement -- the post-hardcore attack is refined, the textural range expanded, the production more detailed. The album drew strong critical response in post-hardcore and experimental rock contexts.
- ***Tail Swallower and Dove* (2008, Suicide Squeeze / Equal Vision).** The final album. More experimental, more varied, willing to go further from the post-hardcore center. The band is expanding even as they approach the end of their run. Reviewed as ambitious and technically demanding. Released one year before the band's dissolution.
- **EPs and splits.** TAAS released several shorter-form works alongside their full-length albums, consistent with the practice of Pacific Northwest post-hardcore bands maintaining catalog presence between LP cycles.

(Sources: Oxeneers or the Lion Sleeps 2004; Easter 2006 Equal Vision; Tail Swallower and Dove 2008; TAAS EP catalog; post-hardcore release practice)

## CAPITOL HILL AND THE 2000s SCENE

### Post-Grunge, Post-Botch: Seattle's Continued Vitality

These Arms Are Snakes operated in Capitol Hill during a specific cultural moment:

- **Post-grunge Capitol Hill.** By 2003, the grunge moment was more than a decade past. Capitol Hill's music scene had evolved through several generations: from the pre-grunge alternative scene through grunge's rise and commercial period, through the dissolution of the major-label grunge era (1996-2000), into the 2000s independent post-hardcore and experimental phase. TAAS is a product of this third generation.
- **Venues.** Capitol Hill's music venues in the mid-2000s included the Crocodile (Belltown adjacent, but serving the Capitol Hill scene), Neumos (reopened 2003 at the site of the legendary Moe), the Chop Suey, and others. These venues sustained the 2000s independent music scene that produced TAAS.
- **Record culture.** Capitol Hill's record shops -- Sonic Boom, Bop Street, and others -- were the distribution and culture points for the post-hardcore scene. The physical record culture of Capitol Hill in the 2000s was essential infrastructure for bands like TAAS.
- **Community.** The Capitol Hill post-hardcore community of the mid-2000s was tight, characterized by musicians who knew each other, shared bills, and operated within an understood common aesthetic. TAAS was embedded in this community from their formation.
- **Seattle's continued output.** Seattle's music scene did not end with grunge. The 2000s Capitol Hill scene was as productive and artistically serious as any period in the city's music history -- simply less visible to national audiences who were not following post-hardcore. TAAS is evidence of this continued vitality.

(Sources: Capitol Hill music scene 2003-2009; post-grunge Capitol Hill evolution; Seattle venues: Neumos, Chop Suey, Crocodile; record shops Capitol Hill; Seattle post-hardcore 2000s community)
