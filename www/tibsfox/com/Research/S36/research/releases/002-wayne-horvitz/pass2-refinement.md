### The Composed-Improvisation Method: Architecture with Moveable Furniture

Pass 1 introduced composed improvisation as Horvitz's defining technique. Pass 2 dissects the methodology at the structural level — what the scores actually look like, how they differ from the two dominant paradigms they sit between, and why the method produces a sound that neither through-composition nor free improvisation can achieve.

The conventional jazz lead sheet — the Real Book model — provides a melody, chord symbols, and an implied form (AABA, twelve-bar blues, rhythm changes). The performer interprets this skeleton by voicing the chords, embellishing the melody, and improvising solos over the changes. The lead sheet specifies *content* (what notes, what harmony) but not *architecture* (what texture, what dynamic shape, what timbral palette). A trio and a big band can play the same lead sheet and produce radically different music because the lead sheet does not prescribe the room — only the furniture.

John Zorn's game pieces (Cobra, Archery, Lacrosse, Hockey) occupy the opposite pole. The game pieces provide *rules* — a system of cue cards, hand signals, and behavioral instructions that govern the interactions between performers. A Cobra performance might instruct a player to imitate the person to their left, or to play as quietly as possible, or to stop suddenly. The rules generate structure in real time, but the rules do not provide content. There is no melody, no harmonic sequence, no thematic material. The architecture is procedural — it emerges from the execution of rules — and the content is entirely improvised. Horvitz played in Zorn's game piece ensembles throughout the 1980s, and the experience trained him in the discipline of responding to structural constraints while maintaining individual musical identity. But he did not adopt the game piece method for his own work. The game pieces produce exhilarating, unpredictable music, but they cannot produce the specific kind of formal coherence that Horvitz was after — the sense that a piece has been *composed*, that it travels an arc from beginning to end with deliberate intent.

Horvitz's composed-improvisation scores are a third thing. They specify both architecture and content at the macro level — thematic material, harmonic progressions, textural instructions (e.g., "piano and cello in unison, quiet, with bassoon entering at measure 12"), dynamic contour, and formal shape — while designating specific structural zones where improvisation is expected. These zones are not "blow over these changes" holes in an otherwise written piece. They are compositionally defined spaces with parameters: a zone might specify that the improviser should use only material derived from the preceding melody, or that the improvisation should begin in the lowest register and expand upward, or that the textural density should thin from full ensemble to a single voice over the course of sixteen bars. The constraints are compositional. The content within them is improvisational.

The Gravitas Quartet recordings — *Way Out East* (2006, Songlines) and *Some Places Are Forever Afternoon* (2015, National Sawdust Tracks) — provide the clearest sonic evidence of this method. In *Some Places Are Forever Afternoon*, a suite of eleven pieces inspired by poet Richard Hugo's depictions of Pacific Northwest landscapes, the compositional frameworks are evocative and programmatic. Each movement evokes a specific place and time through its harmonic color, its instrumental texture, and its pacing. Within these frameworks, Ron Miles's cornet, Peggy Lee's cello, and Sara Schoenbeck's bassoon contribute improvisational passages that feel inevitable — as though the composition had always contained them — because the compositional constraints channel the improvisation toward material that serves the piece's emotional arc. The walls are fixed; the furniture can move; but the furniture always looks like it belongs in the room.

The genealogical position is now clear across three degrees. Jones (degree 0) composed *everything* — full orchestral scores, section parts written to the note, arrangements where the only "improvisation" was a soloist's chorus over changes that Jones controlled through the voicings of the rhythm section and the interjections of the horns. The architecture was total; the content was total; the performer executed. Frisell (degree 1) composes almost nothing — a melody, a mood, a rhythmic feel, a harmonic coloring — and trusts his collaborators to build the texture from the ground up in real time. His recordings with Paul Motian and Joe Lovano, or his *Nashville* album with Viktor Krauss and Jerry Douglas, are improvisational constructions on the barest of foundations. Horvitz mediates: his compositions are more specific than Frisell's sketches but less controlling than Jones's arrangements. The progression from degree 0 to degree 2 on the composition-improvisation spectrum is not linear — it is a movement from maximum compositional control through maximum improvisational freedom to a synthesis that defines the space between them. Three positions. Three solutions. One continuum.

The Royal Room Collective Music Ensemble (RRCME), a 12-to-15-piece group Horvitz directed from the venue's stage, extended the composed-improvisation principle to large ensemble scale. Drawing on his earlier experience with Butch Morris's conduction techniques, Horvitz developed a personal approach to conducted improvisation — using hand gestures and pre-agreed signals to direct musicians through passages that alternated between notated sections and spontaneous group improvisation. The 2014 album *At the Reception* (Songlines) documents this practice, capturing a large ensemble navigating between written and unwritten music with the precision of a chamber group and the energy of a big band. This is composed improvisation at institutional scale — the same principle that governs a Gravitas Quartet score, applied to a room full of musicians whose individual contributions must cohere without eliminating their improvisational identities.

### The Royal Room and Community Infrastructure: The Neighborhood as Institution

In 2009, Horvitz approached Tia Matthies and Steve Freeborn — longtime Seattle venue operators who had previously run the OK Hotel in Pioneer Square and Lottie's Lounge in Columbia City — about partnering on a new music venue. Two years of planning and development followed, and in December 2011, The Royal Room opened at 5000 Rainier Avenue South in the Columbia City neighborhood of Seattle. A 99-seat venue combining a restaurant, bar, and performance space, The Royal Room was designed from the ground up to serve musicians. The stage featured a permanent backline, a Steinway grand piano, recording equipment, and video capability. The room was engineered not as a nightclub where music happened to be performed, but as a *project room* — a workspace where musical ideas could be developed over time through residencies, rehearsals, and multi-night engagements.

The financing tells its own story of community-scale institution building. Horvitz, Matthies, and Freeborn assembled roughly $210,000 from eighteen investors, most of them from the surrounding neighborhood. The venue also received a loan from the Rainier Valley Community Development Fund and a $24,000 grant from 4Culture, King County's cultural funding agency. This was not corporate investment or major-label backing. It was neighborhood capital — eighteen people from southeast Seattle putting money into a room where creative music could happen. The scale is intimate, the ambition is local, and the result is infrastructure that serves a community rather than an industry.

The programming model reflected Horvitz's compositional sensibility applied at the curatorial level. Thursdays through Sundays were reserved for local musicians, with no cover charge — an extraordinary commitment that transformed the venue from a performance hall into a social space, a place where the neighborhood came to spend an evening, not to see a ticketed event. Tuesdays and Wednesdays featured national touring acts with a cover charge. The juxtaposition was deliberate: local and national on the same stage in the same week, with the venue treating both with equal seriousness. Residencies of four to twelve weeks allowed artists to develop material in public — to rehearse, experiment, fail, and refine in front of an audience that was part of the process rather than merely consuming the product.

In 2016, Horvitz and his partners launched the South Hudson Music Project (SHMP), a 501(c)(3) nonprofit created to provide a sustainable funding model for music presentation at The Royal Room. The nonprofit allowed programming to be supported by donations and grants rather than depending entirely on ticket receipts, enabling the venue to book adventurous music that might not sell out a room but that mattered to the community's creative ecosystem. This is the institutional equivalent of the composed-improvisation method: a fixed structural framework (the nonprofit, the venue, the programming calendar) within which creative content can move freely without the constraints of commercial viability dictating every booking.

The degree-chain on institution building now has three data points at three distinct scales. Jones (degree 0) built industry-level infrastructure — Mercury Records' first Black vice president, Qwest Records as a label, the production apparatus that enabled *Thriller* and *We Are the World*. Jones operated at the level where decisions affect entire genres, where a single phone call can assemble forty-six voices in a studio. Frisell (degree 1) built no institutions at all — he is a performer and recording artist who contributes to scenes but does not organize them. Horvitz (degree 2) built community-level infrastructure — a venue, a nonprofit, a network of residencies and ensembles that sustain a neighborhood's creative life. The progression is not from less to more: it is from industry to individual to community. Three scales of cultural production, three models of how an artist's institutional impulse manifests.

The Royal Room's location in Columbia City — not Capitol Hill (Horvitz's home), not Pioneer Square (the traditional jazz district), not Belltown or Ballard (the indie rock axis) — was a deliberate decentralization of Seattle's cultural map. Columbia City in 2011 was historically one of Seattle's most racially and economically diverse neighborhoods, and the venue drew an audience that reflected that diversity. Drummer D'Vonne Lewis, a longtime Royal Room performer and Seward Park resident, noted that the venue attracted "more of a Black audience" than many north-end clubs — a demographic reality that reflects both Columbia City's community and the venue's programming choices. The Royal Room was not a destination venue for cultural tourists. It was a neighborhood room for a neighborhood's musicians and listeners.

After thirteen years and thousands of shows, the founding trio sold The Royal Room in late 2024 to Reese Tanimura, managing director of Northwest Folklife. Horvitz described the transition simply: "We decided it would be best if someone younger with new energy took over. It's been fabulous. It's also been exhausting." The institutional infrastructure persists beyond its founders — the venue, the nonprofit, the programming model, the Steinway on its starlit stage. The institution-builder's greatest achievement is building something that outlives his direct involvement.

### Film Scoring as Compositional Laboratory: Writing to Picture

Horvitz's film and multimedia work is not a sidebar to his concert career — it is a parallel laboratory where the principles of composed improvisation are tested under the most rigorous constraints available: the constraints of picture, timing, and dramatic function.

The scope is broader than most accounts suggest. Horvitz provided sound design for Gus Van Sant's *Psycho* (1998 remake), composed and performed the score for PBS's ninety-minute documentary *Chihuly Over Venice* (1998) — notably the first PBS broadcast in HDTV — produced and performed on Bill Frisell's score for the animated CBS special *Tales from the Far Side* (1994, based on Gary Larson's comics), and served as music producer on its sequel *Tales from the Far Side II* (1997). He composed scores for two films about the creation of Paul Allen's Experience Music Project (now MoPOP) in Seattle. He wrote a score for the feature film *Marrow* (2011, directed by Matt Wilkins), theme music for the animated *Tales from the Far Side II*, and original scores for short films including *Combustible Chef* (2004), *The Atoms of Reality* (2021), *Untangling Chaos* (2021), and *Helen at Dusk* (2022). With Robin Holcomb, he composed scores for five silent films by Japanese director Mikio Naruse, released in 2011 as a Criterion Collection box set.

The silent film rescoring work reveals Horvitz's compositional laboratory at its most disciplined. His 85-minute score to Charlie Chaplin's *The Circus*, premiered in January 2000 in Porto, Portugal, was written for two pianos, two clarinets, and violin — a chamber ensemble whose limited timbral palette forces compositional resourcefulness. The scoring must serve the film's rhythm (Chaplin's physical comedy operates on precise timing), its emotional arc (the tramp's loneliness, the romance, the circus's chaos), and its silence (the film has no dialogue; the music IS the sound). An 85-minute score for five instruments is a marathon of compositional invention — every texture, every dynamic, every silence must be earned.

The score for Ozu's *Woman of Tokyo*, premiered at the Northwest Film Forum in 2005 and later performed at the Winter Garden in New York, extended the silent film practice into a radically different cinematic language. Ozu's films are defined by stillness, geometric composition, and emotional restraint — the polar opposite of Chaplin's kinetic comedy. Scoring Ozu requires a music that can inhabit stillness without filling it, that can accompany a static shot of a hallway without demanding attention. This is the composed-improvisation principle under maximum constraint: the architecture is the film's visual rhythm, the content must serve without dominating, and the composer's ego must subordinate to the director's vision.

In 2015, Horvitz premiered a new score to the 1920 German Expressionist silent film *The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari* at Seattle's Paramount Theatre. Caligari's angular sets, distorted perspectives, and nightmarish narrative demanded a score that could match the visual extremity without descending into pastiche. The Paramount Theatre premiere placed Horvitz's chamber-scale compositional voice in a 2,807-seat Beaux-Arts movie palace — an institutional scale that few of his concert works had achieved.

The theater and dance work adds another dimension. His score for the 1998 production of *Death of a Salesman* at Seattle's ACT Theatre (directed by Gordon Edelstein) required music that could support Arthur Miller's dialogue-driven drama without competing with the actors. In 1992, choreographer Paul Taylor created *OZ* to eleven Horvitz compositions for the White Oak Dance Company — a collaboration with Mikhail Baryshnikov that placed Horvitz's music in the bodies of dancers, where rhythm becomes physical movement and harmonic tension becomes muscular effort. He composed for Bill Irwin's Broadway show *Strictly NY*, for Carey Perloff's productions of Ezra Pound's *Elektra* and the American premiere of Harold Pinter's *Mountain Language*, and for dance works by the Liz Lerman Dance Exchange and the Crispin Spaeth Dance Company. He received a 2016 Doris Duke Performing Artist Award and the 2019 American Prize in Orchestral Composition.

The film scoring practice feeds back into the concert work through a discipline that concert music alone does not impose: the requirement to serve a purpose outside the music itself. A concert piece can be self-referential — its purpose is to be music. A film score must synchronize to picture, support dramatic rhythm, create emotional atmosphere within strict timing constraints, and subordinate its own formal ambitions to the needs of the narrative. This discipline — the discipline of writing music that serves — produces a compositional rigor that Horvitz carries into his non-film work. The Gravitas Quartet pieces sound composed because their composer has spent decades writing music that must function precisely within external constraints. Scoring is not Horvitz's secondary practice. It is the training ground where his compositional precision was forged.

The degree 0 parallel is direct. Jones scored over thirty-five Hollywood features, including *In the Heat of the Night* (1967), *In Cold Blood* (1967), *The Color Purple* (1985), and the television series *Roots* (1977), using full orchestral forces at the blockbuster scale. Horvitz scores independent films, silent film rescorings, and dance productions using chamber ensembles at the indie scale. The methodology is analogous: both treat the score as structural architecture rather than decorative underscore, both compose music that can stand independently while serving the dramatic narrative, and both use the discipline of writing to picture as a compositional laboratory that enriches their non-film work. Jones's industry operated at the level of studio orchestras and Hollywood budgets. Horvitz's operates at the level of two pianos, two clarinets, and a violin in Porto. The scale differs by orders of magnitude. The seriousness does not.

### Pioneer 1 Resonance: The Partial Success and the Persistent Understory

Pioneer 1 launched from Cape Canaveral's Launch Complex 17A at 08:42 GMT on October 11, 1958 — eleven days after NASA officially opened its doors on October 1. It was the first spacecraft launched by the newly formed agency, though the mission was operationally managed by the U.S. Air Force, which had developed the Thor-Able launch vehicle and the probe itself through Space Technology Laboratories (a division of Ramo-Wooldridge Corporation, later TRW). The mission was designed to orbit the Moon, record micrometeoroid impacts, take magnetic field and radiation measurements, and obtain a facsimile image of the lunar surface. It achieved none of these objectives.

During launch, the Thor's guidance system steered the rocket slightly too high and fast, causing the second stage to be lofted 3 degrees above its intended trajectory. The Able second stage shut down ten seconds early due to incorrect information from an accelerometer. The third stage was left pitched up about 15 degrees with a velocity shortfall of approximately 500 feet per second. Pioneer 1 never came close to the Moon. But it did not simply fall back to Earth. The probe reached a peak altitude of 113,854 kilometers — roughly a third of the distance to the Moon — and transmitted data for the entire 43-hour, 17-second duration of its flight before reentering the atmosphere over the South Pacific on October 13 at 03:46 GMT.

What Pioneer 1 returned was more than anyone expected from a failure. The probe verified the existence of the Van Allen radiation belts, mapped the total ionizing flux in near-Earth space, made the first observations of hydromagnetic oscillations of the magnetic field, and measured micrometeoroid impact rates at 0.0052 impacts per second per square meter of sensor. The signal-to-noise ratio was good for approximately 75 percent of the flight. The data established the basic parameters of the near-Earth radiation environment — the operational baseline from which all subsequent NASA missions would calibrate their instruments and plan their trajectories.

The degree 0 mission, Pioneer 0 (Able 1), launched August 17, 1958, and exploded 73.6 seconds after liftoff when a turbopump gearbox failed. It returned nothing — 77 seconds of telemetry from a disintegrating vehicle plummeting into the Atlantic. Pioneer 0 was pure failure. Pioneer 1 was partial success — a mission that failed to reach its target but returned 43 hours of data that proved more valuable than anyone had anticipated. The progression from degree 1 to degree 2 in the NASA track mirrors the progression in the S36 and SPS tracks: from total destruction (Pioneer 0 / 77 seconds) to productive partial failure (Pioneer 1 / 43 hours), from silence to sustained signal.

Horvitz as Pioneer 1. The parallel is structural, not metaphorical. Horvitz has never achieved mainstream recognition. He is not a household name. He does not sell out arena tours or win Grammy Awards. His recordings are released on independent labels — Intuition, Songlines, Tzadik, National Sawdust Tracks — that reach listeners in the thousands, not the millions. By the metrics of the music industry, Horvitz's career has not "reached the Moon." But by the metrics that matter to the creative community he serves, Horvitz's career has returned 43 hours of data from the frontier. The composed-improvisation method, the Royal Room infrastructure, the film scoring discipline, the Gravitas Quartet recordings, the RRCME's conducted improvisations, the dozen-plus ensembles and hundreds of compositions — this is an enormous dataset from a career that the industry barely registers. Pioneer 1 failed to orbit the Moon but mapped the Van Allen belts. Horvitz failed to enter the mainstream but mapped the territory between composition and improvisation, between institution and community, between acoustic and electronic. The data is the point.

The sword fern parallel — *Polystichum munitum* — completes the resonance. The western sword fern is the dominant understory species of PNW lowland conifer forests, an evergreen perennial that forms the structural baseline of the forest floor. Individual plants may live for centuries — researchers at Seward Park have suggested that some specimens could be 1,000 years old or more, established after ancient fire events that exposed the bare mineral soil sword ferns require for sexual reproduction. Once established, the fern persists through disturbance — through canopy changes, through the growth and fall of successive generations of conifers, through the slow geological rhythms of the forest. It is not the tallest thing in the forest (that is the Douglas fir). It is not the most charismatic (that is the western red cedar). It is the thing that is always there — the green constant beneath whatever else is happening overhead. It resproutes after disturbance. It provides habitat structure for birds, small mammals, and invertebrates. It stabilizes soil. It cycles nutrients. It is the understory.

Horvitz is the sword fern of Seattle creative music. He arrived in 1988, a year before Frisell, and began building the understory infrastructure — the venues, the ensembles, the curatorial programs, the teaching at Cornish College of the Arts — that would support the community through whatever happened in the canopy above. The grunge explosion happened above him. The tech boom happened above him. The pandemic happened above him. Through all of it, Horvitz was there on Capitol Hill, composing, performing, booking, curating, building the green constant of Seattle's creative music ecosystem. The spotlight moved. The understory remained.

The three organisms in the first three degrees of the NASA track describe a vertical section of the PNW forest. Degree 0: *Armillaria ostoyae*, the honey fungus — the subterranean mycelial network, the largest organism on Earth, operating invisibly beneath the soil. Degree 1: fireweed (*Chamerion angustifolium*) — the pioneer species, the first visible growth after disturbance, brilliant and temporary. Degree 2: sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*) — the persistent understory, the structure that remains when the pioneers have faded and before the canopy has closed. Three layers: underground, surface, understory. The forest is being assembled from the bottom up.

Pioneer 1's 43-hour dataset is the operational equivalent of the sword fern's persistent understory. It was not the mission's intended product — that would have been lunar orbital data, photographs of the Moon's surface, a full dataset from cislunar space. But the data that Pioneer 1 actually returned became the foundation on which all subsequent near-Earth space science was built. The Van Allen belt measurements alone — confirming that Earth's radiation environment is structured in distinct belts rather than a uniform field — shaped the design of every crewed spacecraft that followed. The mission's failure was its own kind of persistence: the probe kept transmitting for 43 hours, returning data until the moment it burned up over the Pacific. It did not reach the Moon, but it did not stop working.
