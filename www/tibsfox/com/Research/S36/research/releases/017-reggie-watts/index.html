Biography & The Uncategorizable

Reggie Watts was born in Stuttgart, Germany in 1972 to a French mother and an African American father (U.S. Army stationed in Germany) — the first artist in the engine born outside the United States. His family moved to Great Falls, Montana when he was five. He grew up on the high plains east of the Rocky Mountain Front, absorbing music through records, radio, and the eclectic isolation of a small Montana town with no dominant local scene to conform to.

Watts studied jazz piano and acting at Cornish College of the Arts in Seattle — the third Cornish connection in the engine after Julian Priester (degree 7°, faculty) and Hadley Caliman (degree 9°, faculty), and the first Cornish student (both prior connections were faculty). His dual-discipline training is unique in the engine — the first artist to formally study across two disciplines simultaneously.

After Cornish, Watts emerged from Seattle’s performance art, comedy, and music scenes in the 2000s. He performed with Wayne Horvitz (degree 2°) and other Seattle musicians, placing him directly in the network documented at degree 2. Horvitz’s infrastructure (Royal Room, curatorial programming) directly supported artists like Watts — the artist-as-institution pattern enabled the emergence of the uncategorizable artist.

Watts became the bandleader on The Late Late Show with James Corden (CBS, 2015–2023) — eight years on national television. He also performed on Comedy Bang! Bang! (IFC), gave TED talks, and released comedy specials. His work defies categorization: part jazz, part comedy, part performance art, part electronic music, all improvised in real-time using a loop station, his voice, and his body.

Second Genre Shift: Jazz/Comedy — The Absence of Genre

After Lorber’s Jazz Fusion (degree 16°) softened the genre boundary, Watts obliterates it. “Jazz/Comedy” is not a recognized genre in any taxonomy. No record store has a Jazz/Comedy section. No streaming service has the tag. The slash is not a fusion (genres merged into a new whole) but a superposition (two modes coexisting without merging). When Watts performs, he is simultaneously making music and doing comedy — neither subordinate to the other. The refusal to categorize IS the statement.

Three-stage genre evolution in the engine: (1) single genre (degrees 0–15, all “Jazz”), (2) compound genre (degree 16, “Jazz Fusion”), (3) superposition (degree 17, “Jazz/Comedy”). The genre boundary does not soften further — it ceases to apply.

Loop Pedal Lineage: Frisell (1°) to Watts (17°)

Both use technology to multiply a single voice into an ensemble. Frisell (degree 1°): delay pedals creating contemplative atmospheres — sustained guitar tones layered into ambient textures. Watts (degree 17°): loop station creating absurdist collages — beatbox rhythms layered with vocal harmonies, bass lines, and comedy.

Same principle (technology as multiplier), opposite aesthetic (contemplation vs. absurdism). The loop pedal lineage brackets the first quadrant of the circle — Frisell at the beginning, Watts near the end of the jazz arc. Frisell’s delay is analog and continuous (sound decays); Watts’s loop station is digital and persistent (loops hold indefinitely).

Music Theory: The Body as Complete Orchestra

  • Beatboxing as drum synthesis: Biological percussion — kick drums (bilabial plosive with subharmonic chest resonance), snare (alveolar clicks with aspirated fricatives), hi-hats (dental fricatives). The mouth becomes a drum machine with infinite programmability. No external instrument mediates.
  • Loop-based composition (real-time layering as arrangement): Foundation loop (beatbox rhythm), plus vocal bass, plus harmonic fragments, plus melody/speech — built in real-time with zero editing. Each performance unique. Multitrack production performed live.
  • Improvisation as composition: No score, no rehearsal, each performance unique. The purest form of improvisation-as-composition in the engine. Mistakes become features. Imperfections become texture.
  • Genre-crossing as theoretical position: The refusal to categorize IS the statement. Within a single performance: jazz harmony, hip-hop rhythms, electronic textures, spoken-word poetry, physical comedy — not in sequence but in superposition.
