## Biography & The Uncategorizable

Reggie Watts was born in **Stuttgart, Germany in 1972** to a **French mother and an African American father** who was stationed in Germany with the U.S. Army. At age five, his family moved to **Great Falls, Montana** -- a small city on the high plains east of the Rocky Mountain Front, about as far from any major music scene as the American West can offer. He grew up there, absorbing music through records, radio, and the eclectic isolation that comes from being a mixed-race kid in a predominantly white Montana town. The isolation was also an incubator: without a dominant local scene to conform to, Watts developed an omnivorous musical appetite that refused to specialize.

Watts studied at **Cornish College of the Arts** in Seattle -- jazz piano and acting. This makes him the **third Cornish College connection** in the engine after Julian Priester (degree 7, faculty) and Hadley Caliman (degree 9, faculty). But Watts's Cornish connection is structurally different from the prior two: Priester and Caliman were **faculty** who taught at Cornish after established careers. Watts was a **student** who studied there before his career began. The Cornish pipeline now has three members spanning two modes: two faculty (teaching into the institution) and one student (learning from the institution). The pipeline's range expands from experimental jazz (Priester) and hard bop (Caliman) to **performance art** (Watts) -- the widest stylistic spread of any institutional pipeline except Berklee.

After Cornish, Watts emerged from Seattle's performance art, comedy, and music scenes in the **2000s**. He performed with **Wayne Horvitz** (degree 2) and other Seattle musicians, placing him directly in the network documented at degree 2 -- the Horvitz orbit of improvisers, composers, and collaborators who define Seattle's experimental music community. The Horvitz connection is significant: Horvitz's Royal Room and curatorial work created the infrastructure that supported artists like Watts. The artist-as-institution pattern (Horvitz, degree 2) directly enabled the emergence of the uncategorizable artist (Watts, degree 17). Infrastructure precedes innovation.

Watts became known nationally as the **bandleader on The Late Late Show with James Corden** (2015-2023) and through his work on **Comedy Bang! Bang!** with Scott Aukerman. He has performed at major festivals, given **TED talks**, and released albums and comedy specials. His work defies categorization -- part jazz, part comedy, part performance art, part electronic music, all improvised in real-time using a loop station, his voice, and his body.

(Source: Cornish College of the Arts; CBS/The Late Late Show; Comedy Central; TED; AllMusic; NPR interviews; various profiles and interviews)

### The Genre Problem: Jazz/Comedy Is Not a Genre

Degree 17 presents a classification problem the engine has never faced. Every prior artist, even with compound labels, fits within recognizable genre taxonomy. Jeff Lorber (degree 16) is "Jazz Fusion" -- compound but classifiable. Ernestine Anderson (degree 3) is "Jazz (vocal)" -- specific but standard. Watts is **"Jazz/Comedy"** -- and that is not a recognized genre in any taxonomy. No record store has a Jazz/Comedy section. No streaming service has the tag. No music school offers a Jazz/Comedy degree.

The slash in "Jazz/Comedy" is not a fusion (where two genres merge into a new whole) but a **superposition** (where two modes coexist without merging). When Watts performs, he is simultaneously making music and doing comedy. Neither is subordinate to the other. The music is not the setup for the comedy, and the comedy is not the context for the music. Both are happening at full intensity at the same time. This is the **second genre shift** in the engine -- after Lorber's Jazz Fusion (degree 16) -- but it is qualitatively different. Lorber's shift was from one genre to a compound genre. Watts's shift is from genre to **the absence of genre** -- the refusal to be categorized IS the statement.

The engine must accommodate this. Watts is the first artist who **cannot be categorized**, and the engine's response to that is the classification itself: the uncategorizable artist as a category.

## The Loop Pedal as Instrument: Technology Multiplied Through the Body

### Bill Frisell to Reggie Watts: Delay Pedal to Loop Station

The deepest technical connection in degree 17 is the loop pedal lineage that traces back to **Bill Frisell (degree 1)**. Both Frisell and Watts use technology to multiply a single voice into an ensemble. The mechanism is similar: capture sound, layer it, build a composition in real-time. But the outputs are radically different.

**Frisell** uses delay pedals and looping to create **contemplative atmospheres** -- sustained guitar tones layered into ambient textures. The mood is meditative, spacious, introspective. The technology extends the guitar into an orchestra of echoes.

**Watts** uses a loop station to create **absurdist collages** -- beatbox rhythms layered with vocal harmonies, bass lines, melodic fragments, and spoken-word comedy. The mood is chaotic, playful, confrontational. The technology extends the voice into a one-man band of contradictions.

Same principle (technology as multiplier), opposite aesthetic (contemplation vs. absurdism). The loop pedal lineage at degrees 1 and 17 brackets the first quadrant of the circle -- Frisell at the beginning, Watts near the end of the jazz arc. The technology evolves: Frisell's delay pedals are analog and continuous (sound decays over time), Watts's loop station is digital and persistent (loops hold indefinitely until the performer releases them). The shift from delay to looping mirrors the shift from analog to digital that Lorber (degree 16) documented on keyboards.

### The Voice as Synthesizer

Watts's beatboxing is a **biological demonstration of sound synthesis**. Where Lorber (degree 16) used a Minimoog to create bass lines and a DX7 to create timbres, Watts creates equivalent sounds using only his vocal tract. Kick drums, snare drums, hi-hats, bass lines, chord pads, lead melodies -- all produced by manipulating the resonant cavities of the mouth, throat, and nasal passages.

This is the body-as-sound-source thread taken to its logical extreme. The thread has tracked instruments as extensions of the body since degree 0. Watts collapses the distinction: the body IS the instrument, AND the synthesizer, AND the drum machine. No external instrument mediates between the performer and the sound. The **eleventh instrument** in the body-as-sound-source thread, and the most radical -- the voice used not as voice but as **universal sound generator**.

## Music Theory: The Body as Complete Orchestra

### Beatboxing as Drum Synthesis

Beatboxing is **biological percussion synthesis** -- creating drum sounds from vocal tract resonances. The mechanisms:

- **Kick drum:** a bilabial plosive (/b/) with subharmonic chest resonance, producing a low-frequency thump
- **Snare drum:** a combination of alveolar clicks and aspirated fricatives, producing a sharp transient with noise texture
- **Hi-hat:** dental or alveolar fricatives (/ts/, /tss/) with varying durations for open and closed hat sounds
- **Rimshot:** a retroflex click producing a sharp, pitched transient

Each drum sound maps to a specific articulatory gesture. The performer's mouth becomes a drum machine with infinite programmability -- any rhythm that can be conceived can be produced. This is drum synthesis without synthesis hardware: the vocal tract replaces the oscillators, filters, and amplitude envelopes of a drum machine. The seventeenth chromaticism deployment: **vocal-tract chromaticism** -- continuously variable pitch and timbre produced by sliding between articulatory positions, creating chromatic pitch movement without discrete semitone steps.

### Loop-Based Composition: Real-Time Layering as Arrangement

Watts's loop-based composition method is structurally equivalent to **multitrack recording performed live**. The process:

1. **Lay the foundation:** record a beatbox rhythm loop (the drum track)
2. **Add bass:** record a vocal bass line over the rhythm (the bass track)
3. **Layer harmony:** record chord pads or harmonic fragments (the keyboard track)
4. **Add melody:** sing or speak over the accumulated layers (the lead track)
5. **Modify, subtract, replace:** manipulate loops in real-time, removing layers, adding new ones, building and collapsing the arrangement

This process mirrors studio production but with one critical difference: **no editing**. In a studio, tracks can be punched in, comped, and corrected. In Watts's live performance, every loop is a single take. Mistakes become features. Imperfections become texture. The loop-based composition method is the purest form of improvisation-as-composition in the engine -- no score, no rehearsal, each performance unique, and the entire arrangement built in real-time in front of the audience.

### Genre-Crossing as Theoretical Position

The refusal to categorize IS the musical statement. Watts does not cross genres accidentally or through stylistic evolution; he crosses genres **deliberately and simultaneously** as a compositional strategy. Within a single performance, he might traverse jazz harmony, hip-hop rhythms, electronic textures, spoken-word poetry, and physical comedy -- not in sequence but in superposition, layered and interwoven.

This is the Amiga Principle taken to its extreme: **one person, one microphone, one loop pedal, creating entire worlds**. The Amiga Principle has tracked resource efficiency since degree 0 (Quincy Jones leveraging an orchestra through arrangement). At degree 17, the resource count reaches its absolute minimum: one human body, one microphone, one loop station. The architectural leverage reaches its maximum: from these minimal resources, Watts creates multi-layered compositions that span genres, moods, and modes of performance. The ratio of output complexity to input resources is the highest in the engine.

### Sampling Theory Connection

Watts's beatboxing and looping are **biological demonstrations of sampling**. He captures fragments of sound (his own voice), stores them (in the loop station's buffer), and plays them back layered. This is the sampling process -- capture, store, reproduce -- performed without a sampler. The connection to digital audio theory is direct: the loop station samples at a fixed rate, quantizes the captured audio, and reproduces it digitally. The Nyquist theorem applies: the loop station's sample rate determines the fidelity of the captured vocal performance. Connect to BPS (biological-physical systems) and DAA (digital audio analysis) research threads.

The seventeenth blue note deployment: **vocal-glide blue note** -- Watts produces blue notes through continuous vocal pitch glides that approach scale tones from below or above without landing on them. Unlike the synthesizer pitch bend (degree 16, electronic) or the guitar string bend (degree 1, mechanical), the vocal glide is purely physiological -- no instrument mediates. The most direct blue note mechanism in the engine: intent to sound with zero mechanical intermediary.

## Career Arc

### Born Stuttgart, Germany (1972)

Born in Stuttgart, Germany to a French mother and an African American father (U.S. Army). Military-base birth -- the first artist in the engine born outside the United States. The transatlantic origin adds a new geographic thread: European birth, American upbringing.

### Great Falls, Montana (age 5 onward)

Moved to Great Falls, Montana at age five. Small-city upbringing on the high plains east of the Rocky Mountain Front. The isolation incubated an omnivorous musical appetite -- no dominant local scene to conform to. Great Falls to Seattle is the most geographically unusual origin path in the engine since Peacock's Burley, Idaho (degree 8).

### Cornish College of the Arts, Seattle

Studied jazz piano and acting at Cornish College of the Arts. Third Cornish connection (after Priester degree 7, Caliman degree 9). First Cornish student (prior two were faculty). The dual-discipline training (music + acting) is unique in the engine -- the first artist to formally study performance art alongside music.

### Seattle Performance Scene (2000s)

Emerged from Seattle's performance art, comedy, and music scenes. Performed with Wayne Horvitz (degree 2) and other Seattle improvisers. Built reputation through live performances combining music, comedy, and audience interaction. The Seattle scene provided the infrastructure (venues, community, audience) for Watts's uncategorizable art.

### TED Talks and National Profile

TED talks and viral video performances expanded Watts's audience beyond the live performance circuit. His TED performances demonstrated the loop-based composition method to a global audience, making the technique visible outside music circles.

### Comedy Bang! Bang! (IFC)

Regular performer and bandleader on Scott Aukerman's Comedy Bang! Bang! television show on IFC. The show positioned Watts at the intersection of comedy and music, matching his artistic identity.

### The Late Late Show with James Corden (CBS, 2015-2023)

**Bandleader on The Late Late Show with James Corden** for the show's entire CBS run (2015-2023). Eight years as a late-night bandleader placed Watts in front of a national television audience nightly. The position is structurally significant: late-night bandleaders (Paul Shaffer, The Roots, Jon Batiste) are among the most visible musicians in American culture. Watts brought his full performance-art identity to the role -- beatboxing, looping, comedy, and improvisation on network television.

### NYC and LA -- Present

Based in New York and Los Angeles for career purposes, but Seattle remains the formative city. The Seattle-to-national trajectory mirrors the Export pattern (Jones, degree 0) but with a difference: Watts did not leave Seattle for a specific opportunity (as Jones left for Lionel Hampton's band). Watts left because his art required a larger stage.
