## PONY TIME -- INDIE ROCK / GARAGE ROCK -- SEATTLE DIY

### The Duo

Pony Time is a Seattle indie rock/garage rock **duo** -- **Luke Beetham** and **Stacy Peck**. Two people. Guitar, drums, vocals. No bass. No keyboards. No backing tracks. The duo format is not a limitation -- it is a declaration of minimalism. Everything you hear is two people in a room. The sound is raw, immediate, and unmediated.

The duo format connects Pony Time to a lineage of two-piece rock bands that includes The White Stripes, The Black Keys (early), Lightning Bolt, and Death From Above 1979. But where those bands often used effects, loops, or studio trickery to create a bigger sound, Pony Time's approach is more direct: two people, amplified, playing as hard and as fast as they can. The rawness is the point. The immediacy is the product.

### The Garage Rock Foundation

Pony Time's sound is built on **garage rock** fundamentals: loud, fast, distorted, immediate. The guitars are overdriven. The drums are hit hard. The vocals are shouted, sung, and sometimes screamed. The songs are short -- typically 1-3 minutes -- and structured around hooks that arrive immediately and don't waste time on preamble. There is no buildup, no slow introduction, no atmospheric prelude. The song starts, the song delivers, the song ends.

This approach is antithetical to the extended-form, atmospheric, and hypnotic approaches that dominated the Capitol Hill streak (Kinski's motorik jams, Minus the Bear's intricate math-rock, Haunted Horses' industrial noise). Pony Time is the corrective: after six degrees of elaborate construction, degree 116 delivers **raw transmission** -- the simplest possible unit of rock band (two people) playing the simplest possible version of rock music (garage rock).

### The DIY Network

Pony Time's place in Seattle's music ecosystem is the **DIY underground** -- the network of house shows, basement venues, all-ages spaces, and informal gatherings that exists below the level of club shows and bar stages. This is the network where bands play for $50 and a case of beer, where the PA is borrowed, where the audience is 30 people who all know each other, and where the music is made because it has to be made, not because anyone expects to make a living from it.

The DIY network is the foundation of Seattle's music scene. Every band that eventually plays the Showbox or signs to Sub Pop began in the DIY network. Pony Time's significance is that they represent the network itself -- the permanent underground that sustains the scene regardless of which bands rise above it.

Pony Time's discography is entirely self-released -- a series of albums, EPs, and singles distributed through Bandcamp and physical media at shows. The catalog is characterized by **volume and consistency**: the duo has released music regularly, maintaining a steady output that reflects the DIY ethic of constant creation.

### Key Releases

The releases share common characteristics:
- **Short, fast songs** -- garage rock fundamentals. Two-minute explosions of distorted guitar and pounding drums.
- **Lo-fi production** -- recorded in practice spaces, home studios, and borrowed rooms. The production quality is intentionally raw. Clarity is not the goal; energy is.
- **Consistent voice** -- across releases, Pony Time's sound is remarkably consistent. They found their approach early and committed to it. The consistency is a strength: you know what you're getting, and what you're getting is real.

The self-released catalog contrasts with the Matador-released, Pitchfork-reviewed trajectory of Pretty Girls Make Graves (degree 115). Where PGMG moved from a small label (Lookout!) to a prestigious indie (Matador) over three albums, Pony Time has stayed self-released throughout. The trajectories diverge: PGMG sought broader audience; Pony Time seeks nothing but the next show and the next recording.

## Acoustic Element 117: The Raw Transmission

The **raw transmission** is the one hundred seventeenth acoustic element in the Seattle 360 engine. It describes the quality produced when the simplest possible rock unit (two people, guitar/drums) delivers music with no mediation between creation and reception -- no label, no producer, no elaborate production, no atmospheric preamble. The raw transmission is music stripped to its essential components.

### The Sound

The raw transmission manifests as:
- **Duo minimalism** -- two instruments (guitar + drums) with vocals. No bass fills the low end. No keyboards add texture. No samples or loops supplement. The sound is what two people can make in real time.
- **Overdriven immediacy** -- the guitar is distorted, the drums are loud, and the vocals cut through on urgency alone. The signal chain is short: instrument -> amplifier -> room -> listener. Minimal processing.
- **Short-form delivery** -- songs arrive immediately and end quickly. 1-3 minutes. No extended introductions, no gradual builds, no codas. The raw transmission does not waste time.
- **Lo-fi honesty** -- the production quality is intentionally rough. The room is audible. The bleed between instruments is audible. The imperfections are not corrected. The recording is a document of a performance, not a construction in a studio.

### Contrasts with Prior Acoustic Elements

- **The burning elegance** (degree 115, Pretty Girls Make Graves) -- elegant architecture. The raw transmission is anti-architecture. PGMG was precise; Pony Time is blunt. Structure vs. impulse.
- **The hypnotic engine** (degree 110, Kinski) -- extended, patient, layered. The raw transmission is short, urgent, minimal. 12-minute motorik jams vs. 2-minute garage explosions.
- **The collapse frequency** (degree 109, No-Anchor) -- structured collapse. The raw transmission doesn't collapse because there is no structure complex enough to collapse. The simplicity is the resilience.
- **The angular lattice** (degree 52, prior post-punk) -- geometric precision. The raw transmission is blunt force. Precision vs. impact.

The 117th acoustic element. The engine adds the sound of minimal garage rock duo delivering music with no mediation.
