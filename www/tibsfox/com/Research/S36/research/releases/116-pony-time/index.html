The Raw Transmission

Pony Time is a Seattle indie rock/garage rock duo — Luke Beetham and Stacy Peck. Two people. Guitar, drums, vocals. No bass. No keyboards. No backing tracks. Everything you hear is two people in a room. The duo format is a declaration of minimalism: the simplest possible unit of rock band playing the simplest possible version of rock music.

After six degrees of elaborate construction — motorik jams, math-rock intricacy, industrial noise, post-punk architecture — degree 116 delivers the corrective: raw transmission. Two people, amplified, playing for whoever shows up.

The songs are short (1–3 minutes), built on hooks that arrive immediately. The guitar is overdriven. The drums are hit hard. The vocals are shouted, sung, and sometimes screamed. No atmospheric preamble. No extended introduction. The song starts, delivers, and ends. The DIY absolute: no label, no producer, no distribution infrastructure. Self-released through Bandcamp and physical media at shows.

The DIY Underground

Pony Time exists in Seattle’s DIY underground — the network of house shows, basement venues, all-ages spaces, and informal gatherings that exists below the level of club shows and bar stages. This is where bands play for $50 and a case of beer, where the PA is borrowed, where the audience is 30 people who all know each other. The DIY network is the foundation of Seattle’s music scene. Every band that eventually plays the Showbox or signs to Sub Pop began here. Pony Time represents the network itself — the permanent underground that sustains the scene regardless of which bands rise above it.
