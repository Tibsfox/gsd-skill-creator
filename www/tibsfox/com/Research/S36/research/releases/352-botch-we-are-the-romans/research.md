## WE ARE THE ROMANS (1999, HYDRA HEAD RECORDS) -- THE ALBUM

### Eight Tracks. Thirty-Seven Minutes. The Mathcore Maximum.

*We Are the Romans* was released in 1999 on Hydra Head Records. It is the record that defines the mathcore genre's ceiling: the maximum of what the genre is capable of in its fundamental dimensions -- rhythmic complexity, dynamic range, technical precision under maximum intensity, and the integration of extended quiet passages with extreme sonic violence. The album is 37 minutes across eight tracks. Every track is a complete document. There is no filler. There is no transitional material. The album begins with maximum complexity and sustains it for 37 minutes.

**The rhythmic architecture.** Mathcore is distinguished from other extreme music genres by its rhythmic approach: time signatures are not regular, meter changes are frequent and unpredictable, and the rhythmic relationships between guitar, bass, and drums are often non-coincident -- the instruments are in the same time but not necessarily counting in the same subdivisions simultaneously. *We Are the Romans* deploys this approach at the ceiling. The drums are not keeping time; they are constructing rhythmic structures that the guitars and bass respond to and complicate. The sum is music that has no comfortable rhythmic entry point for the listener -- it does not groove in the conventional sense, and this is the point.

**The dynamic range.** The album's dynamic range is exceptional for extreme music: the quiet sections are genuinely quiet, and the transitions from quiet to violent are often sudden and without preparation. The dynamic architecture is not decorative -- it is structural. The quiet sections are not respites from the intensity; they are the accumulation of tension that makes the violent sections carry their full weight. Without the quiet, the loud is just loud. With the quiet, the loud is an event.

**Track analysis.** "To Our Friends in the Great White North" opens the album -- a deceptive opening, with a clean guitar passage that establishes the album's dynamic range before the first violence arrives. "C. Thomas Howell as the Soul Man" is the album's most recognizable track: the stop-start rhythmic structure, the Verellen scream that functions as a rhythmic instrument as much as a vocal, the bass line that operates independently of the guitar riffs rather than supporting them. "Transitions from Persona to Object" is the album's longest track and its most structurally complex: extended quiet sections followed by abrupt violence, the dynamic architecture at its most deliberately disorienting. "We Are the Romans" closes the album -- the title track, beginning quietly and arriving at the album's most sustained violent passage. The arc from opening clean guitar to closing sustained intensity is the album's complete emotional structure.

**The Hydra Head Records position.** Hydra Head Records was the label that documented the American mathcore and extreme metal underground in the late 1990s and early 2000s. The roster included Isis, Cave In, Coalesce, and Converge. *We Are the Romans* was the most critically recognized record in the catalog and the most significant single document the label produced.

## BOTCH -- THE BAND AT THE MAXIMUM

### Tacoma, Washington. Hydra Head Records. Mathcore Ceiling.

Botch formed in Tacoma, Washington, in 1993. They were active through 2002. The band's formation in Tacoma rather than Seattle is significant: Tacoma in the mid-1990s was not part of the grunge narrative, not part of the Sub Pop ecosystem, not receiving the attention that Seattle-based bands received. Tacoma is the industrial port city 35 miles south of Seattle -- historically working-class, historically defined by the Port of Tacoma, the copper smelter, the manufacturing base.

**Brian Cook's bass.** Brian Cook is one of the most important bassists in the history of American extreme music. His approach on *We Are the Romans* is not the standard role of bass in heavy music -- not providing the harmonic foundation that the guitars work over. Cook's bass operates independently: it is a contrapuntal voice that adds harmonic and rhythmic information that the guitars are not providing. The bass is composing in the spaces that the guitars leave, rather than filling those spaces with supportive content. This is the specific innovation in Botch's rhythm section: the bass has compositional independence, and that independence is what gives *We Are the Romans* its harmonic complexity.

**Dave Knudson's guitar.** Knudson's guitar work on *We Are the Romans* is technically demanding and compositionally precise. The mathcore guitar approach requires the ability to play irregular time signatures accurately at high tempos while maintaining the tonal qualities (feedback, dissonance, controlled noise) that make the music sonically extreme. Knudson plays riffs that are not in the standard metal guitar vocabulary: the angular, dissonant passages that make mathcore's guitar sound distinct from thrash, death metal, or hardcore guitar. The angularity is compositional, not accidental.

**Dave Verellen's vocals.** Verellen's vocal approach on the album is not standard extreme metal screaming. The voice is used as a rhythmic instrument: the phrasing and placement of the screams are compositionally determined, fitting into the rhythmic structures of the instruments rather than following standard vocal verse-chorus structures. The scream is a percussion voice added to a percussion-heavy texture, and this integration is part of what gives the album its rhythmic complexity.

**Tim Latona's guitar.** The two-guitar interaction on *We Are the Romans* is not about doubling -- Latona and Knudson are not playing the same riffs. They are playing different riffs that create a composite guitar texture more complex than either riff alone. The non-coincident guitar approach is the mathcore equivalent of the non-coincident bass-guitar relationship: multiple independent voices in simultaneous operation.

**The breakup.** Botch dissolved in 2002 after recording *An Anthology of Dead Ends* (a final EP). The breakup was not due to creative failure or commercial pressure; it was a voluntary end. The band had completed what they wanted to complete. *We Are the Romans* was the peak. The dissolution preserved the integrity of the peak by not extending beyond it.

## TACOMA -- THE ENGINE'S INDUSTRIAL PORT CITY

### 35 Miles South of Seattle. The Periphery Pattern at Maximum.

Tacoma is the largest city in Pierce County, located 35 miles south of Seattle on Puget Sound, at the mouth of the Puyallup River. The city's historical economic base is the Port of Tacoma -- one of the largest container ports in North America -- and the manufacturing infrastructure that grew around it. The Tacoma Smelter (ASARCO copper smelter, Superfund site) is one of the defining industrial monuments of the city: a giant copper smelter stack that contaminated soil across a wide radius before the site's closure.

Botch's formation in Tacoma rather than Seattle placed the band in the periphery of the Seattle music scene. This is the engine's recurring pattern, and it is not coincidental: Aberdeen (Melvins, Nirvana), Oak Harbor (The Accused), Georgetown (TAD), and now Tacoma (Botch). The most technically extreme music in the Pacific Northwest tradition consistently comes from outside the Seattle city center, from industrial and working-class communities where the social conditions produce a specific intensity that more cosmopolitan urban environments do not generate in the same form.

## THE MATHCORE MAXIMUM -- WHY WE ARE THE ROMANS IS E=10

### The Album. The Benchmark. The Ceiling.

*We Are the Romans* earns E=10 as the mathcore genre's ceiling document:

**The genre maximum.** *We Are the Romans* is the record that defined the outer boundary of what mathcore could achieve. The technical demands are as high as the genre has produced. The compositional complexity (Brian Cook's independent bass, the two non-coincident guitars, Verellen's rhythmic vocals, the time-signature architecture) reaches the genre ceiling. No subsequent mathcore record has exceeded *We Are the Romans* as a document of the genre's maximum.

**The dynamic architecture.** Most extreme music operates in a narrow dynamic range -- maximum volume most of the time, with occasional quiet interludes. *We Are the Romans* uses the full dynamic range as a compositional tool, with quiet sections that are structurally functional (they build the tension that the loud sections release) rather than decorative. This is the album's most important compositional innovation: dynamic range as architecture, not as decoration.

**The specific Tacoma/Hydra Head position.** The combination of Tacoma's industrial periphery origin and Hydra Head Records' position as the label documenting the American mathcore underground placed Botch in the national extreme music conversation at precisely the moment when the genre was defining itself. *We Are the Romans* was the record that set the standard.

**The Brian Cook bass innovation.** The compositionally independent bass voice is *We Are the Romans*' most lasting technical contribution to the genre. It established that bass in extreme music could be a compositional voice rather than a supportive harmonic foundation. Every mathcore band that followed had to respond to this precedent.
