## The Two Hundred Fifty-Eighth Degree: The Immortal Riff

### Rock, E=5. "Spirit in the Sky" (1969). Four bars of fuzz guitar written in 15 minutes. Three years at the top, five decades in the cultural atmosphere. v1.49.399.

---

## What Worked

1. **The riff-first approach is correct.** Greenbaum's entry is about the riff first, the artist second. This is honest documentation: the riff is more famous than the name, and that fact is the most interesting thing about the entry.
2. **The fuzz guitar gospel synthesis is the key analytical insight.** Fuzz guitar in 1969 belonged to psychedelia and hard rock. Greenbaum used it for spiritual warmth. This synthesis was unexpected and ahead of its moment -- the Jesus Movement rock of the early 1970s would normalize Christian rock, but Greenbaum's quasi-gospel fuzz predates it.
3. **The one-song catalog economics are documented.** Two UK #1 covers (1986, 2003), continuous sync licensing for five decades. The financial architecture of this entry is unusual: most artists in the engine have catalogs that generate income proportionally across the body of work. Greenbaum has one song and it has sustained him.
4. **The tambourine insight is correct.** The production secret of "Spirit in the Sky" is the contrast between dense low-mid fuzz and bright tambourine shimmer. Neither element is technically complex; together they create a full-spectrum sound from a minimal arrangement.

## Discoveries

1. **Written in 15 minutes, inspired by Porter Wagoner gospel TV.** The origin story is important: Greenbaum was not devout; he was watching television. The song's religious content is observed and imagined, not confessional. This makes "Spirit in the Sky" a fascinating cultural artifact -- a spiritually aspirational song written from the outside by someone who found the form compelling without sharing the theology.
2. **The Doctor and the Medics cover in 1986 introduced the song to a generation that was 17 years younger than the original audience.** This cross-generational reach is what distinguishes the great one-song catalogs from the merely successful ones.
