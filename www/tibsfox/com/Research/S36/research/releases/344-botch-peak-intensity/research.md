## BOTCH -- THE BAND

### Dave Verellen, Brian Cook, Tim Latona, Dave Knudson

Botch's personnel and their contributions:

- **Dave Verellen (vocals).** Verellen's vocal approach is raw-scream at maximum projection: not the melodically inflected screaming of post-hardcore vocalists like Whitney (Blood Brothers) or the controlled delivery of metalcore vocalists, but a full-commitment physical assault on the microphone. Verellen screams with the urgency of someone who cannot wait to be done -- the words arrive faster than comprehension, the delivery is physical rather than melodic, the intensity is at maximum from the opening of every track.
- **Brian Cook (bass).** Cook's bass work in Botch is structurally independent from the guitar -- it is not following the guitar riffs but providing an independent rhythmic and melodic voice. The bass adds a layer of compositional complexity that most metal and post-hardcore bass players do not attempt. Cook went on to significant work with Russian Circles (which he co-founded after Botch's dissolution), and his bass playing in that context demonstrates the musical sophistication that he was developing throughout the Botch years.
- **Tim Latona (drums).** Latona's drumming is the rhythmic engine of Botch's mathcore architecture. The time signatures in Botch's music are irregular, the patterns are non-standard, and the transitions between sections require a drummer capable of executing complex rhythmic figures at high tempo without sacrificing the physical power that the music requires. Latona is among the most technically demanding drummers in the post-hardcore canon.
- **Dave Knudson (guitar).** Knudson's guitar work in Botch is the most technically complex element of the band's sound. The riffs in *We Are the Romans* use irregular rhythmic patterns, dissonant harmonics, and structural complexity that exceeds most guitar work in the engine's documented catalog. The guitar is not playing in a standard tuning or following standard intervallic relationships -- it is operating in a harmonically ambiguous space that creates the characteristic Botch unease.
- **The band's history.** Botch formed in 1993 in Tacoma, started releasing material on local labels and Kill Rock Stars (early work), and then signed with Hydra Head Records. The Hydra Head period produced their most significant work. The band dissolved in 2002, citing creative exhaustion. Reunion tours have occurred in subsequent years, but the primary creative output is from the 1993-2002 period.

## WE ARE THE ROMANS (1999) -- THE E=10 DOCUMENT

### The Career-Defining Record and the Engine's Maximum

*We Are the Romans* is the specific artifact that justifies E=10:

- **The opening.** *We Are the Romans* opens with "C. Thomas Howell as the 'Soul Man,'" which immediately demonstrates the band's full capabilities: the irregular time signature, Verellen's immediate full-commitment scream, Knudson's dissonant guitar riffs, Latona's complex drumming pattern, Cook's independent bass movement. There is no warmup. The record is at E=10 from bar one.
- **"To Our Friends in the Great White North."** Often cited as the album's most technically demanding track. The rhythmic complexity of the guitar and drum interaction in this track is the clearest demonstration of the mathcore architecture at its peak: riffs that last an odd number of beats, drum patterns that comment on the guitar rather than underlining it, transitions that are rhythmically complex and harmonically unexpected.
- **"Transitions from Persona to Object."** The album's longest and most architecturally complex track. It demonstrates Botch's capacity for compositional structure at macro scale -- the track is not a repeated riff but a developed argument that moves through distinct sections, each building on the previous, toward a conclusion that is earned by the journey rather than imposed by convention.
- **"We Are the Romans."** The title track, closing the album. A track that demonstrates the full range of the band's dynamics -- including quiet moments that are not relief but tension, and loud moments that are not simply loud but maximally dense. The title track makes the album's claim: *we are the Romans* is an assertion about historical weight, cultural legacy, and the presence of the band within a tradition they are simultaneously transforming and consuming.
- **The production.** *We Are the Romans* was produced by Chris Common. The production captures the band's live power in the studio without artificial cleanliness -- the record sounds like Botch in a room, not like Botch processed through a commercial production template. The density is maintained; the physicality is preserved.

## THE MATHCORE GENRE -- WHAT E=10 SOUNDS LIKE

### The Technical Architecture of Maximum Intensity

Mathcore as a genre and Botch's position within it:

- **Time signature complexity.** Mathcore's defining characteristic is its use of irregular, constantly shifting time signatures. A mathcore song may move through 7/8, 5/4, 11/16, and 4/4 within a single phrase, with transitions that are rhythmically logical within the composition but immediately disorienting to the unprepared listener. The music is "mathematical" in the sense that the rhythmic and harmonic structures are constructed with deliberate complexity rather than emerging from conventional rock patterns.
- **Botch's specific mathcore style.** Botch's mathcore is neither jazz-derived (as some mathcore adjacent to prog rock is) nor pure-metal derived. It is post-hardcore at maximum complexity: the energy and physicality of hardcore and metal, the rhythmic and harmonic complexity of progressive music, and the refusal of commercial accessibility of the underground DIY tradition. The combination is unique in the Pacific Northwest context.
- **Relation to contemporaries.** Botch's contemporary context includes Converge (Massachusetts), Cave In (Massachusetts), Dillinger Escape Plan (New Jersey), and Refused (Sweden) -- bands that were developing the post-hardcore/mathcore vocabulary simultaneously in the late 1990s. *We Are the Romans* appeared in the same cultural moment as Converge's *When Forever Comes Crashing* (1998) and Dillinger Escape Plan's *Calculating Infinity* (1999) -- the moment when mathcore reached its defining period. Botch is the Pacific Northwest entry in this canon.
- **The influence.** Botch's influence on subsequent Pacific Northwest and national post-hardcore extends through Brian Cook's Russian Circles (post-Botch, instrumental post-rock with technical precision), through Dave Knudson's subsequent work, and through the general influence of *We Are the Romans* on the mathcore and technical metal underground. The record is documented in retrospective critical assessments as one of the most significant heavy records of the 1990s.

(Sources: Mathcore time signature shifting 7/8 5/4 11/16; post-hardcore at maximum complexity; Botch neither jazz-derived nor pure-metal; Converge When Forever Comes Crashing 1998; Dillinger Escape Plan Calculating Infinity 1999; Refused Sweden; We Are the Romans as defining mathcore moment; Brian Cook Russian Circles post-Botch; We Are the Romans retrospective critical reassessment one of most significant 1990s heavy records)
