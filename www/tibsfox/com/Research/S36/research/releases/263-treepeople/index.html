The Boise-Seattle Pipeline

Treepeople formed in Boise, Idaho in 1988. Boise is approximately 400 miles southeast of Seattle via I-84 — a long day's drive, not a short trip. But the Pacific Northwest has always had creative corridors: Portland, Boise, and Seattle form a network that has sustained independent music communities since the early 1980s. Treepeople existed in this corridor — formed in Boise, active in Seattle, part of both communities simultaneously.

DOUG MARTSCH — THE PRE-BUILT TO SPILL CONTEXT

Martsch's melodic guitar sensibility is already fully present in Treepeople. The way a Martsch guitar line follows an unexpected interval, settles on an unlikely note, and resolves in a way that was not predictable — this is not a Built to Spill invention. It is a Treepeople development. Treepeople (1988-1993) is the lab. Built to Spill (1992–present) is the result. Listeners who discovered Martsch through Perfect From Now On or Keep It Like a Secret are hearing the downstream of what Treepeople established.

C/Z Records — The Root Document

C/Z Records was an independent Seattle label operating alongside Sub Pop in the early-to-mid 1990s. The label's most important historical act was releasing the Deep Six compilation in 1986 — one of the first documents of the Seattle underground that would later be called grunge. Deep Six included Melvins, U-Men, Soundgarden (before A&M), Green River (which later split into Mudhoney and Pearl Jam precursors), Skin Yard (see degree 264), and Malfunkshun. This compilation is the root document of the entire grunge lineage. C/Z was there at the beginning.

Treepeople on C/Z and Skin Yard on C/Z (degree 264) creates a label continuity across these two degrees. The label thread runs through the pre-grunge and grunge eras simultaneously.

"Most listeners who discovered Doug Martsch through Built to Spill's major-label work do not know Treepeople exists. Treepeople is where Martsch's approach was developed, tested, and refined."

The Sound — Post-Hardcore at E=7

Post-hardcore emerged when musicians trained in hardcore's speed and aggression began applying those instincts to more melodically complex material. The canonical reference: Husker Du, Bob Mould taking hardcore energy and applying it to pop song structures. Treepeople occupy this tradition — faster and louder than conventional rock, more harmonically sophisticated than hardcore.

The E=7 streak at degrees 261-263 covers three different mechanisms: Screaming Trees achieve it through psychedelic density, Gas Huffer through speed and volume, Treepeople through post-hardcore melodic and rhythmic complexity. The energy level is identical; the approach is entirely different in each case.
