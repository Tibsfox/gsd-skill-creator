## THE BOISE-SEATTLE PIPELINE

### The Northwest Creative Corridor

Treepeople establishes the Boise-Seattle connection in the engine:

- **Boise, Idaho.** Boise is the capital of Idaho, approximately 400 miles southeast of Seattle via I-84. Population approximately 240,000. Not a natural partner for Seattle's music scene in obvious geographic terms. But the Pacific Northwest has always been culturally expansive -- Portland, Boise, and Seattle form a Pacific Northwest corridor that has sustained independent music communities since the early 1980s.
- **Why Boise.** The same isolation and relatively low cost-of-living that characterized Seattle's early music scene also characterized Boise's. Young musicians in Boise in the late 1980s had access to rehearsal space, small venues, and a community of like-minded people -- a similar infrastructure to what Seattle offered, at smaller scale. The proximity to Seattle (a long day's drive) meant that Boise bands could play Seattle; Seattle bands would sometimes come to Boise. The corridor worked.
- **The pipeline.** For Treepeople specifically, the Boise origin is formative: the band formed there, developed their sound there, and then extended their activity to Seattle. This is not a band that relocated to Seattle and left Boise behind; it is a band that existed in the corridor between the two cities.
- **Built to Spill continues the pipeline.** After Treepeople dissolved in 1993, Doug Martsch formed Built to Spill -- initially in Boise, then extending to Seattle. The Boise-to-Seattle creative corridor Treepeople established continued through Built to Spill and has been a persistent feature of Pacific Northwest indie rock.

(Sources: Boise, Idaho geography; Pacific Northwest creative corridor; Boise music scene late 1980s; Treepeople Boise formation; Seattle extension; Built to Spill Boise origin; corridor persistence)

## TREEPEOPLE -- THE BAND

### Personnel and Identity

Treepeople formed in Boise in 1988:

- **Doug Martsch (guitar/vocals).** The central creative figure. Martsch's guitar playing is already distinctive in the Treepeople recordings -- he has an approach to melodic guitar writing and voice-leading that is unusual in the post-hardcore context. His playing is not about aggression or technical display; it is about melodic specificity. The way a Martsch guitar line follows an unexpected interval, settles on an unlikely note, and then resolves in a way that was not predictable -- this is already present in Treepeople and will become the defining characteristic of Built to Spill.
- **Wayne Flower (bass).** Flower's bass work provides the harmonic foundation for Martsch's guitar explorations. Treepeople's bass is not simple -- it has melodic independence, following lines that complement rather than merely double the guitar.
- **Scott Schmaljohn (drums).** Schmaljohn's drumming has the rhythmic complexity that characterizes the post-hardcore tradition -- not the four-on-the-floor simplicity of punk, but something more varied and technically demanding. The rhythmic interest in Treepeople is a preview of the rhythmic complexity Built to Spill would develop further.
- **Additional members.** Treepeople had additional members across their five-year run, including Brad Blackburn. The core songwriting partnership was anchored by Martsch.

(Sources: Treepeople personnel; Doug Martsch biography; Wayne Flower; Scott Schmaljohn; Brad Blackburn; Boise formation 1988; five-year active period)

## DOUG MARTSCH -- THE PRE-BUILT CONTEXT

### Understanding Built to Spill Through Treepeople

Treepeople is the essential context for understanding Doug Martsch's development:

- **What Treepeople established.** Martsch's melodic sensibility, his willingness to let guitar lines be harmonically sophisticated without becoming technically ostentatious, and his approach to song structure that allows for extended development while retaining emotional directness -- all of this is present in Treepeople before Built to Spill existed.
- **The post-hardcore foundation.** Treepeople's genre is post-hardcore/rock -- the tradition that emerged when musicians trained in the speed and aggression of hardcore began applying those instincts to more melodically ambitious material. Husker Du is the canonical example: Bob Mould taking hardcore energy and applying it to pop song structures. Treepeople occupy this space. Martsch heard this tradition and responded.
- **The progression to Built to Spill.** Built to Spill, formed in 1992-1993 in Boise, extended and deepened what Treepeople had begun. The longer songs, the more elaborate guitar arrangements, the increasing willingness to let tracks develop over five, six, seven minutes -- this is Martsch applying the structural ambitions he had developed in Treepeople to a new context with a new band. Treepeople is the lab; Built to Spill is the result.
- **Why Treepeople matters on its own terms.** The engine documents Treepeople not only as a precursor to Built to Spill but as a band with its own distinct identity. The Treepeople records -- *Time Whore* (1989), *Guilt, Regret and Embarassment* (1992), *Just Kidding* (1993) -- are significant documents of the Boise underground scene and of post-hardcore's development in the Pacific Northwest.

(Sources: Doug Martsch biography; Treepeople as Built to Spill precursor; post-hardcore foundation; Husker Du as canonical reference; Built to Spill formation 1992-1993; Treepeople records on own terms)

## C/Z RECORDS

### The Pacific Northwest Independent Label

C/Z Records is the label connection between Treepeople (263) and Skin Yard (264):

- **C/Z Records.** Founded in Seattle. C/Z Records was one of the important Pacific Northwest independent labels of the early-to-mid 1990s, existing alongside Sub Pop and operating in a slightly different aesthetic territory. Where Sub Pop specialized in the heavy grunge sound, C/Z had a broader eclectic range.
- **The C/Z catalog.** C/Z Records released records by Skin Yard (see degree 264), Treepeople, and other Pacific Northwest bands. The label was important as an alternative to Sub Pop's dominant position -- C/Z gave artists options for independent release that were not constrained by the Sub Pop aesthetic.
- **C/Z and the *Deep Six* compilation.** C/Z Records released the *Deep Six* compilation in 1986 -- one of the first documents of the Seattle underground rock scene that would later be called grunge. The compilation included Melvins, U-Men, Soundgarden (before A&M), Green River (which later split into Mudhoney and Pearl Jam precursors), Skin Yard, and Malfunkshun. This compilation is the root document of the entire grunge lineage. C/Z was there at the beginning.
- **Label significance.** The fact that both Treepeople (263) and Skin Yard (264) are on C/Z Records creates a label continuity across these two degrees. C/Z is the structural link.

(Sources: C/Z Records history; Deep Six compilation 1986; C/Z catalog: Skin Yard, Treepeople, others; Deep Six compilation bands: Melvins, U-Men, Soundgarden, Green River, Skin Yard, Malfunkshun; C/Z and Sub Pop as parallel independent labels)

## THE SOUND -- POST-HARDCORE/ROCK, E=7

### Melodic Ambition in a Post-Hardcore Frame

Treepeople's sonic approach:

- **Post-hardcore defined.** Post-hardcore emerged in the mid-1980s as musicians who came up through hardcore began applying hardcore's energy and directness to more melodically complex material. The key moves: songs that are faster and louder than conventional rock but more harmonically sophisticated than hardcore; guitar work that has melodic personality rather than just aggression; song structures that can accommodate development and extension.
- **Treepeople's specific approach.** Martsch's guitar playing is the defining element -- melodically specific, harmonically unexpected, emotionally direct. Treepeople is not a band that plays complicated chords to show that they can; they play those chords because those are the ones the song needs. This distinction between technical display and emotional necessity is the core of Martsch's approach.
- **The Boise quality.** Treepeople's music has a slightly different quality from the Seattle bands at the same period. There is a spaciousness to it -- less urban density, more open. Whether this reflects Boise's geographic context or Martsch's individual aesthetic is difficult to separate, but the quality is real.
- **E=7 as post-hardcore position.** E=7 fits post-hardcore accurately: present, physically engaged, emotionally direct, not at the volume maximum. Gas Huffer (262) achieves E=7 through speed; Treepeople achieve it through melodic and rhythmic complexity. The E=7 streak (261-263) covers three very different mechanisms: psychedelic density, garage speed, post-hardcore complexity.

(Sources: Post-hardcore genre definition; Husker Du, Fugazi as canonical post-hardcore; Martsch guitar approach; Boise spaciousness as sonic quality; E=7 post-hardcore positioning)

## DISCOGRAPHY

### The Complete Treepeople Run

- ***Time Whore* (1989, Silence Records).** The debut. Raw but harmonically interesting. Martsch's guitar voice is already present.
- ***Something Burning* (1989, Nemesis Records).** Second release. The Boise underground scene document.
- ***Guilt, Regret and Embarassment* (1992, C/Z Records).** The C/Z debut. The record that established Treepeople's Pacific Northwest profile. More sophisticated song structures; Martsch's melodic approach fully developed.
- ***Just Kidding* (1993, C/Z Records).** The final Treepeople record. Released as the band was dissolving. Built to Spill had already begun forming. Just Kidding is the transition document.

(Sources: Treepeople full discography; Time Whore 1989; Something Burning 1989; Guilt Regret and Embarassment 1992 C/Z; Just Kidding 1993 C/Z)

## LEGACY

### The Invisible Foundation

- **Treepeople as Built to Spill's invisible foundation.** Most listeners who discovered Doug Martsch through Built to Spill's major-label work (*Perfect From Now On*, 1997; *Keep It Like a Secret*, 1999) do not know Treepeople exists. This is a common pattern -- the formative band is invisible to those who discovered the later work. But Treepeople is where Martsch's approach was developed, tested, and refined.
- **The Boise-Seattle corridor as Pacific Northwest infrastructure.** Treepeople and Built to Spill together document a creative corridor that has been a persistent feature of Pacific Northwest independent rock. Boise is not a satellite of Seattle; it is a node in a network.
- **C/Z and the pre-grunge roots.** Treepeople's placement on C/Z Records connects the band to the *Deep Six* compilation and the pre-grunge lineage. This is significant: the engine documents not only the grunge commercial moment but the underground ecosystem from which it emerged and alongside which it existed.
