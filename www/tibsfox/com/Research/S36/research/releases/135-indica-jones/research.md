## INDICA JONES -- ELECTRONIC MUSIC ON KEXP

- **Curatorial philosophy:** Like all KEXP DJs, Indica Jones approaches electronic music with specificity and context. A Detroit techno track is placed in the lineage of Belleville Three. A UK garage track is connected to the London club scene. The programming is educated, not just beat-matching.
- **Discovery engine for electronic:** For many KEXP listeners, Indica Jones's programming is their primary exposure to electronic music beyond the mainstream EDM that dominates commercial radio and festival circuits. The programming serves as a gateway to the deeper electronic underground -- the independent labels, the DIY producers, the subgenres that don't get Spotify algorithmic promotion.
- **Seattle's electronic scene connection:** Capitol Hill has been the center of Seattle's electronic music scene since the 1990s, with clubs, warehouse parties, and after-hours venues forming a thriving underground. Indica Jones connects KEXP to this local scene while also programming international electronic music. The local-global bridge, specific to electronic music.

## CAPITOL HILL ELECTRONIC CULTURE

### The Neighborhood and the Sound

Capitol Hill is the epicenter of Seattle's electronic music scene, and Indica Jones operates within this ecosystem:

- **Club culture:** Capitol Hill's clubs -- from the now-closed Neighbours to Re-bar, Kremwerk, Timbre Room, and the rotating warehouse venues -- have hosted electronic music since the rave era of the early 1990s. The neighborhood's density, walkability, and late-night culture create the conditions for electronic music to thrive.
- **The rave-to-club pipeline:** Seattle's electronic scene evolved from the Pacific Northwest rave culture of the 1990s (influenced by the broader West Coast rave movement) through the 2000s club era into the current ecosystem of boutique venues, rooftop parties, and DIY spaces. Indica Jones spans this evolution, connecting the historical underground to the contemporary scene.
- **Producer ecosystem:** Capitol Hill houses studios, production spaces, and the creative infrastructure that supports electronic music production. The neighborhood is both a consumption and creation center for electronic music. KEXP's electronic programming amplifies local producers alongside international acts.
- **After-hours culture:** Electronic music is inherently nocturnal. Capitol Hill's late-night culture -- extending past the 2 AM last call through after-hours events and early-morning sets -- is the social infrastructure that electronic music requires. Indica Jones's programming on KEXP brings this nocturnal energy to daytime listeners.

### Seattle's Electronic Music Lineage

Seattle's electronic scene has a deeper history than the grunge narrative suggests:

- **1990s rave culture:** The Pacific Northwest had a vibrant rave scene in the 1990s, running parallel to (and often intersecting with) the grunge era. Warehouse parties, outdoor raves in the Cascades, and the early club nights laid the foundation.
- **2000s maturation:** As the rave scene mainstreamed and fragmented, Seattle's electronic community moved into clubs and bars, developing a more sustainable infrastructure. Labels like Ghostly International, Warp (distributed through local shops), and local imprints supported the scene.
- **2010s-present:** The streaming era and festival circuit (Decibel, Capitol Hill Block Party's electronic stages) brought new audiences. Meanwhile, the underground remained active in Capitol Hill's smaller venues. Indica Jones bridges both worlds -- accessible enough for KEXP's broad audience, deep enough for the underground heads.

## Acoustic Profile

### The Sound of Electronic Music on Radio

Indica Jones's acoustic contribution is the **soundscape of electronic music curated through independent radio**:

- **Sonic character:** Bass-heavy, rhythmically precise, texturally rich. Electronic music's sonic palette is fundamentally different from KEXP's indie rock core -- synthesizers instead of guitars, programmed drums instead of live percussion, bass drops instead of power chords. Indica Jones brings this entire sonic universe to KEXP.
- **Energy contour:** Electronic music's characteristic energy contour -- build, peak, release, transition -- shapes the programming. Sets have a narrative arc built on rising tension and kinetic release. This is a fundamentally different energy structure than the song-by-song curation of talk-heavy shows.
- **BPM range:** From downtempo (70-100 BPM) through house (120-130 BPM) to techno (130-150 BPM) and drum and bass (170-180 BPM). The BPM spectrum itself becomes a curatorial tool -- the pace of the programming mirrors the genre's energy taxonomy.
- **The KEXP electronic sound:** Indica Jones defines KEXP's electronic dimension the way Mazzone defines its global dimension and B-Mello defines its hip-hop dimension. The electronic programming establishes KEXP as a station that takes electronic music seriously as an art form, not just as background beats.

### The DJ as Energy Architect

In electronic music, the DJ's role is fundamentally architectural:

- **Set construction:** Building a set from track to track, managing energy levels, creating peaks and valleys, reading the audience (even a radio audience). This is a different skill from the talk-and-play format of Waters (130) or Richards (131). The DJ is constructing a continuous musical experience.
- **Mixing as art:** Beatmatching, harmonic mixing, EQ transitions, effects. The technical craft of electronic DJing is a performance skill that shapes the listener's experience moment to moment. The mix is the art.
- **Curation as architecture:** Selecting tracks that work together not just thematically but sonically -- in terms of key, BPM, texture, energy. The curatorial decisions are architectural decisions. Every transition is a design choice.
