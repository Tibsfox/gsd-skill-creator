Beacon Hill — The South Seattle Origin

Alice in Chains formed in 1987 in Beacon Hill — south-central Seattle, working-class, diverse, outside the established music neighborhoods. Jerry Cantrell (Tacoma) and Layne Staley (Kirkland) connected in Seattle and found the specific sound within the year: down-tuned guitars, Cantrell's guitar-vocal harmony system, Staley's mid-range power vocal that carries emotional weight without falsetto-based spectacle. The Beacon Hill origin is the correct geographic completion for the Metal Summit: from Capitol Hill through Aberdeen and the U-District to the south of the city. The arc traverses the full Seattle-Aberdeen geography.

"The guitar riff and the vocal melody are the same thing. One is just played on guitar and one is sung. The harmony between them is the song."

Dirt (1992) — The Darkest Statement

Rooster

Vietnam War Combat History

Cantrell wrote this for his father, Jerry Cantrell Sr., who served as a Vietnam War infantryman. "Rooster" was the elder Cantrell's war nickname. A metal song with a specific personal combat narrative. Neither romanticized nor cynical.

Would?

Andrew Wood Tribute

Tribute to Andrew Wood (Mother Love Bone), who died of heroin overdose in March 1990. Cornell wrote "Hunger Strike" for Wood; Cantrell wrote "Would?" Not sentimental -- genuinely grief-stricken. The Seattle scene's two most important tributes to the same person.

Down in a Hole

Staley's Direct Statement

The most emotionally direct track in the AIC catalog. Staley's voice at its most exposed. The guitar-vocal harmony at its most intimate. The weight is earned, not performed.

Nutshell (Jar of Flies, 1994)

Acoustic Maximum

The quietest and most devastating track in the AIC catalog. Acoustic, no distortion. Staley at minimum armor. The arc from Dirt's full-distortion heaviness to Nutshell's acoustic vulnerability: the same weight, different delivery mechanism.

The Guitar-Vocal Harmony System

The most immediately identifiable feature of Alice in Chains' sound is the harmonic relationship between Cantrell's guitar (and his harmony vocals) and Staley's melody. Many metal bands have guitar riffs and lead vocals that are independent; in AIC, the guitar riff is written to function in harmonic relationship with the vocal melody. Cantrell frequently sings a lower harmony against Staley's lead, using intervals that include minor thirds (mildly consonant), perfect fifths (stable), minor seconds (dissonant), and tritones in passing. The dissonant intervals create an unsettled quality even in quiet passages — the harmony system is not reassuring but pressured.
