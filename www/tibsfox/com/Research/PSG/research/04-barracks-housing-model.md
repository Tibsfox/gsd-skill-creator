## Overview

The Pacific Spine workforce pipeline has a housing problem. Students attending Sno-Isle TECH from rural Snohomish and Island counties face commute times of 90+ minutes each way. No barracks-style temporary housing exists near Sno-Isle TECH or Paine Field to enable immersive, residential skill training. This module examines the modular workforce housing industry, documents precedent models, analyzes costs, and maps the application to the Sno-Isle TECH campus.

> The question is not whether workforce housing is feasible. Modular housing has been deployed for over a century in construction, energy, military, and educational contexts. The question is whether the institutions responsible for the Paine Field corridor will recognize that housing is a workforce development tool, not just a real estate problem.

## The Access Problem

### Commute Barriers by District

The geographic spread of Sno-Isle TECH's 14 member school districts creates access barriers that limit program participation. Students from distant districts either endure extreme commutes or simply do not enroll.

| Mukilteo | ~5 miles | 12 min | Minimal |
| Edmonds | ~12 miles | 20 min | Low |
| Marysville | ~12 miles | 20 min | Low |

| Lake Stevens | ~18 miles | 30 min | Moderate |

### The Darrington Case

The Darrington School District provides the clearest illustration of the access problem. The bus from Darrington High School departs at 6:25 AM and returns by 11:15 AM for AM session students. This means a student pursuing Aerospace Manufacturing training -- the program that feeds directly into Boeing employment -- must leave home before dawn and navigate 55 miles of mountain roads each way.

> Darrington to Sno-Isle TECH: 55 miles, mountain roads, 6:25 AM departure. A residential education model eliminates this barrier with a bed, a shower, and a short walk to class.

### Students Not Enrolling

The access barrier does not show up in enrollment data as a failed commute. It shows up as students who never apply. Students in Darrington, Sultan, Granite Falls, Stanwood-Camano, and Oak Harbor who would benefit from Aerospace Manufacturing, Welding, or Maritime Vessel Operations programs simply do not enroll because the daily commute is unreasonable. The true cost of the housing gap is the pipeline capacity that was never created.

## Modular Workforce Housing Industry

### Industry Overview

Modular workforce housing -- also called man camps, crew lodges, or base camps -- has been deployed for over a century. The industry originated with 19th-century railroad construction camps and evolved through military housing, oil field base camps, and construction crew accommodations.

| Feature | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| History | 100+ years of deployment |
| Speed Advantage | 40-50% faster than conventional construction |
| Cost Advantage | 20-40% lower than conventional construction |
| Capacity Range | 20 to 1,000+ residents |
| Deployment Timeline | Weeks at a prepared site |
| Reusability | Modules can be relocated and reconfigured |

### Standard Configurations

Modern modular workforce housing installations include:

- **Sleeping quarters** -- private or semi-private rooms with climate control
- **Kitchen facilities** -- commercial-grade or institutional kitchen for meal preparation
- **Dining halls** -- seated meal service for the full camp population
- **Shower and restroom facilities** -- centralized or distributed per building
- **Laundry facilities** -- commercial washers/dryers for resident use
- **Recreation areas** -- common rooms, fitness equipment, outdoor spaces
- **Medical facilities** -- first aid station or on-site clinic for larger installations
- **Administrative offices** -- camp management, security, maintenance

### Construction Methods

| Method | Description | Timeline |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Flatpack modular | Panels shipped flat, assembled on-site | 2-4 weeks |
| Volumetric modular | Complete rooms shipped as 3D modules | 1-3 weeks |
| Container-based | Shipping containers converted to living units | 1-2 weeks |
| Prefab dormitory | Factory-built dormitory wings delivered whole | 3-6 weeks |
| Hybrid | Permanent foundation with modular superstructure | 4-8 weeks |

## Precedent Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Military Training Center Housing

Military installations worldwide use barracks housing to enable intensive, residential training. The model is directly applicable: students live on or near the training facility, eliminating commute time and enabling immersive skill development.

| Feature | Military Model | Sno-Isle Application |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Residents | Trainees (18-25) | CTE students (16-20) |
| Duration | 8-16 weeks | 3-36 weeks (program dependent) |
| Structure | Shared rooms, common facilities | Same |
| Supervision | Drill instructors, duty officers | Residential advisors, program staff |
| Cost Model | Government-funded | Foundation/district/state funded |
| Meal Service | Dining facility (DFAC) | Culinary Arts program (dual purpose) |

> The military training center model is the most direct precedent. The key adaptation: replace military command structure with educational supervision. The physical infrastructure -- beds, showers, dining, classrooms within walking distance -- is identical.

### Case Study 2: Job Corps Residential Programs

The U.S. Department of Labor's Job Corps program operates 121 residential centers nationwide, providing education and vocational training to young adults ages 16-24. Job Corps residential centers include dormitories, cafeterias, recreation facilities, and on-site health services -- all integrated with vocational training programs.

| Feature | Job Corps | Sno-Isle Application |
|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| Age Range | 16-24 | 16-20 |
| Residential | Full-time, on-campus | Full-time or weekday-only |
| Programs | 10+ vocational trades | 22 CTE programs |
| Duration | 8-24 months | Semester or year-long |
| Funding | Federal (DOL) | State CTE + foundation + district |
| Scale | 121 centers nationwide | Single campus pilot |

### Case Study 3: Construction Crew Base Camps

Large-scale construction projects routinely deploy modular base camps for workers in remote locations. Pipeline construction in Canada and Alaska, dam projects in the Pacific Northwest, and energy facility construction all use temporary workforce housing that is installed in weeks and removed when the project is complete.

| Project Type | Camp Size | Duration | Cost/Bed |
|-------------|-----------|----------|----------|
| Pipeline construction (Canada) | 500-2,000 beds | 6-24 months | $15,000-25,000 |
| Dam/hydro construction | 200-800 beds | 12-36 months | $18,000-30,000 |
| Mining operations (remote) | 100-1,500 beds | Ongoing, rotational | $20,000-35,000 |
| Energy facility construction | 300-1,000 beds | 6-18 months | $12,000-22,000 |

## Residential Education Models

### The Residential Education Concept

Residential education is not boarding school. It is a model where students live near their training facility during intensive skill-building periods, eliminating the commute barrier and enabling immersive learning. The model works for:

- **Multi-week intensives** -- 3-week summer school sessions where students from all 14 districts can participate without daily commuting
- **Semester-long residential** -- Students from Darrington, Sultan, Oak Harbor can live on-campus during the school week and return home on weekends
- **Apprenticeship housing** -- Post-graduation apprentices in BPET or Machinists Institute training can live near Paine Field during their training period

### Operational Models

| Model | Schedule | Target Population | Housing Requirement |
|-------|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Weekday Residential | Mon-Fri on-campus, weekends home | Rural district students (Darrington, Sultan, Oak Harbor) | 30-60 beds |
| Summer Intensive | 3-week on-campus sessions | All districts, incoming 9th-12th grade | 40-80 beds |
| Full Residential | Semester-long, continuous | Out-of-area students, foster youth, special circumstances | 20-40 beds |
| Apprenticeship Housing | Duration of training program | BPET and Machinists Institute trainees (18+) | 50-100 beds |

## UC Berkeley Education Workforce Housing Research

### Center for Cities + Schools

California's Center for Cities + Schools at UC Berkeley, in partnership with cityLAB at UCLA and the Terner Center for Housing Innovation, has pioneered education workforce housing research. Their work documents nine local educational agencies that have navigated planning, financing, and construction of workforce housing on school-district-owned land.

### Key Finding

> By using land already owned by educational institutions, housing can be leased to employees and students at below-market rates, addressing affordability barriers that limit program participation.

This finding is directly applicable to Sno-Isle TECH. The Mukilteo School District (host district) and potentially the Port of Everett own land in the Paine Field corridor. Housing built on institutionally owned land eliminates the land acquisition cost -- often the largest barrier to workforce housing projects.

### Nine Agency Case Studies

The UC Berkeley research documents nine California educational agencies that successfully built workforce housing on school district land. Common patterns include:

- **Ground lease structure** -- district retains land ownership, leases to housing developer
- **Mixed-use design** -- teacher housing combined with student residential or community facilities
- **Below-market rent** -- made possible by removing land cost from development equation
- **Public-private partnership** -- district provides land, private developer builds and manages
- **Tax-exempt financing** -- educational purpose qualifies for favorable bond terms

## Cost Analysis

### Modular vs. Conventional Construction

| Factor | Modular | Conventional | Savings |
|--------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Construction Cost/Bed | $15,000-30,000 | $30,000-60,000 | 40-50% |
| Construction Time | 2-8 weeks (site prep + installation) | 6-18 months | 70-85% |
| Design Flexibility | Standard modules, customizable | Full custom | Lower design cost |
| Relocation | Can be moved to new site | Permanent | Full residual value |

## Deployment Timelines

### From Decision to Occupancy

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Planning and approvals | 2-4 months | Zoning review, permits, site selection |
| Site preparation | 2-4 weeks | Grading, utilities, foundation |
| Module delivery | 1-2 weeks | Transportation from factory to site |
| Installation | 2-4 weeks | Placement, connection, finish work |
| Inspection and commissioning | 1-2 weeks | Code compliance, systems testing |
| **Total** | **4-7 months** | Decision to first residents |

> Four to seven months from decision to occupancy. Compared to 18-36 months for conventional dormitory construction, modular housing provides workforce pipeline capacity within a single school year planning cycle.

## Application to Sno-Isle TECH

### The Proposal

A modular residential education facility near the Sno-Isle TECH campus at Paine Field would:

1. **Eliminate the commute barrier** for students from Darrington, Sultan, Granite Falls, Stanwood-Camano, and Oak Harbor
2. **Enable immersive training** through extended-day and evening programming
3. **Support summer intensives** with 3-week residential sessions for incoming students
4. **Provide apprenticeship housing** for BPET and Machinists Institute trainees
5. **Create a dual-purpose facility** where Culinary Arts students prepare meals for residents (training + food service)

### Dual-Purpose Culinary Integration

The Culinary Arts program at Sno-Isle TECH operates three tracks: Baking & Pastry, Management & Operations, and Production & Service. A residential facility would create a real-world training environment for all three tracks. Students learn food service by feeding real people in a real dining operation -- the most effective training model available.

### Location Options

| Site | Advantages | Challenges |
|------|-----------|------------|
| Adjacent to Sno-Isle TECH campus | Walking distance to classrooms | Limited available land |
| Port of Everett property | Institutional land, possible ground lease | Distance from campus |
| Paine Field corridor | Proximity to Boeing, Machinists Institute | Commercial zoning |
| County-owned property | Public land availability | Possible competing uses |

## Zoning and Permitting Considerations

### Snohomish County Context

Modular workforce housing in Snohomish County must navigate county zoning ordinances, building codes, and potentially city regulations depending on the specific location. Key considerations:

- **Zoning classification** -- Residential, commercial, or institutional zoning may apply depending on site
- **Temporary vs. permanent** -- Modular installations may qualify as temporary structures under some codes
- **Conditional use permits** -- Workforce housing associated with educational institutions may require CUP
- **Environmental review** -- SEPA (State Environmental Policy Act) review for new construction
- **ADA compliance** -- Accessibility requirements for all residential facilities
- **Fire code** -- Sprinkler requirements, egress standards, fire-rated assemblies

> This module documents modular housing capabilities. It does not presume zoning approval. The permitting pathway must be navigated through the specific jurisdiction where the facility is proposed.

## Integration with Pacific Spine

### Workforce Housing as Infrastructure

In the Pacific Spine framework, workforce housing is infrastructure -- as fundamental as fiber optic cable or rail capacity. The corridor cannot train workers faster than it can house them near training facilities. The barracks housing model provides the mechanism to decouple training capacity from local housing markets.

### Replication Pattern

If the Sno-Isle TECH residential pilot succeeds, the modular housing model can be replicated at other training nodes along the corridor: Boeing Renton, Tacoma trades programs, Portland community colleges, and eventually corridor-wide apprenticeship housing managed through FoxEdu.
