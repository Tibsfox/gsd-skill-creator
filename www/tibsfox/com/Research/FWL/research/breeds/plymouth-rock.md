# The Barred Rock --- America's Chicken

There is a moment at any poultry show when you walk past the Plymouth Rock cages and something catches your eye before you understand what you are seeing. It is the barring. Row after row of broad-breasted birds wearing those precise parallel stripes of slate and white, each feather a miniature lesson in genetic regularity, the cumulative effect from ten feet away like a flock of birds dressed in the same well-tailored fabric. The Barred Plymouth Rock is not the flashiest bird in the show hall. It is not the rarest. It is not the most challenging to breed. But it is, by almost any measure, the most *American* chicken ever developed --- the bird that fed a nation, anchored the poultry industry, and still, more than 150 years after its creation, serves as the breed that experienced keepers return to and beginners reach for first.

This document is a deep profile of the Plymouth Rock, with particular attention to the Barred variety that defined the breed, the genetics that produce that iconic plumage, and the qualities that make this bird as relevant in a 2026 Pacific Northwest backyard as it was on an 1880 Massachusetts farm.

---

## Origin: Worcester County and the Boston Show

The Plymouth Rock's origin story is tangled in the way that most nineteenth-century breed histories are tangled --- fragmentary records, competing claims, and a tradition of oral history that was not always scrupulous about attribution. What is clear is that the breed emerged from New England in the 1860s and 1870s, from breeders who were trying to solve a practical problem: they wanted a single bird that laid well, grew to a respectable table weight, tolerated harsh winters, and had the calm temperament needed for farm flocks managed by families, not specialists.

The earliest mention of the name "Plymouth Rock" in connection with a barred fowl appears at the first major American poultry show, held in Boston in 1849. This was the show that George P. Burnham would later immortalize (and satirize) in his book *The Hen Fever*. A barred bird was exhibited under the name Plymouth Rock, generated interest, and then vanished from the record. Whatever that bird was, it did not establish a sustained breeding population. The Plymouth Rock as a continuous breed dates to the 1860s.

The man most consistently credited with developing the modern Barred Plymouth Rock is D.A. Upham of Worcester, Massachusetts, though other breeders --- including John C. Bennett and various unnamed farmers in the Connecticut River Valley --- contributed to the gene pool. Upham's program reportedly involved crosses of Dominique hens (which carried barring and the rose comb) with Black Java cocks (which contributed size, body type, and the single comb), with possible infusions of Cochin blood for additional mass and Malay for frame and hardiness. The resulting birds combined the barred plumage of the Dominique with a larger, more rectangular body type and the single comb that would become a defining characteristic.

The Plymouth Rock was admitted to the first edition of the APA *Standard of Perfection* in 1874 as a founding breed. By the 1880s, it was the most popular chicken in America, and it would hold that position, essentially unchallenged, for more than half a century.

## The Rise of America's Backbone Bird

To understand why the Barred Rock became so dominant, you have to understand what American agriculture needed in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. The typical farm kept a flock of 30 to 100 chickens. These birds were expected to lay eggs for the family and for market, provide meat for the Sunday table, reproduce themselves (broody hens were expected to hatch a clutch or two each spring), and survive with minimal veterinary care in a climate that could range from -20°F in January to 100°F in August. There was no room for a specialist --- no egg-only bird or meat-only bird. The farm needed a dual-purpose breed, and the Barred Rock filled that niche better than anything else available.

The numbers speak clearly. A good Barred Rock hen in a well-managed farm flock would lay 200 to 280 large brown eggs per year, with the higher end achieved by strains selected for production and the lower end typical of exhibition-bred lines. Standard weights in the APA are 9.5 pounds for a cock and 7.5 pounds for a hen, though many farm-bred birds exceeded these weights --- an 8-pound hen was not unusual, and a well-fed cockerel could dress out at 5 to 6 pounds of clean meat at 16 to 20 weeks. The birds were cold-hardy, heat-tolerant (within reason), good foragers, calm around children and livestock, and reliable enough as brooders that a farmer could count on natural reproduction to maintain the flock.

During both World Wars, the U.S. government promoted backyard poultry keeping as a patriotic duty, and the Barred Rock was the recommended breed. Victory Garden posters from the 1940s show barred chickens alongside rows of vegetables. The White Plymouth Rock --- a sport of the Barred, first recognized by the APA in 1888 --- became the maternal line of the American broiler industry, crossed with Cornish to produce the fast-growing meat bird that would eventually industrialize poultry production. But the Barred Rock remained the farmer's bird, the homesteader's bird, the bird that a family bought when they bought their first chickens.

## Barring Genetics: The B Locus

The barred pattern that gives the Barred Plymouth Rock its name is one of the best-studied plumage genetics in all of poultry science. It is governed by the **B locus** (sex-linked barring), located on the Z chromosome, and its behavior illustrates several important principles of avian genetics.

### The Mechanism

The *B* allele causes a cyclical interruption of melanin deposition during feather growth. As each feather grows from its follicle, the melanocytes (pigment-producing cells) alternate between active pigment production and quiescence. During active phases, melanin is deposited normally, producing a dark bar. During quiescent phases, no melanin is deposited, producing a light (white or cream) bar. The result is a series of alternating dark and light bands running perpendicular to the feather shaft, from base to tip.

The width and regularity of the bars are influenced by the rate of feather growth, the duration of the melanocyte on/off cycles, and modifier genes that affect the precision of the switching. In a well-bred exhibition Barred Rock, the bars should be of equal width (dark bars and light bars the same), sharply defined (not blurry or smudged at the edges), and consistent across the entire body --- from hackle to tail, breast to saddle, including the primary and secondary flight feathers. Achieving this level of uniformity is one of the fundamental challenges of Barred Rock breeding.

### Sex Linkage and Dosage Effects

Because the *B* gene is on the Z chromosome, and because chickens have a ZW sex determination system (males are ZZ, females are ZW), the inheritance of barring is sex-linked. This has a visible and practically important consequence: **males and females express barring differently.**

A male Barred Rock carries two copies of *B* (one on each Z chromosome): genotype *B/B*. The double dose of the barring gene produces wider light bars relative to dark bars, giving the male a distinctly lighter, more silvery appearance. The bars are present but the overall impression is of a bird washed in silver-gray.

A female Barred Rock carries only one copy of *B* (on her single Z chromosome; the W chromosome does not carry the B locus): genotype *B/W*. The single dose produces narrower light bars, resulting in a darker, more crisply defined barring pattern. A well-bred female has dark bars and light bars of roughly equal width, producing the classic "pinstripe" look that most people picture when they think of a Barred Rock.

This sex-linked dosage effect means that males and females look noticeably different from one another, even in a breed where both sexes carry the same basic pattern. Experienced breeders use this difference for sex identification at hatch: Barred Rock chicks are black with a white head spot, and the head spot tends to be larger and more diffuse in males (who carry two copies of *B*) and smaller and more sharply defined in females (one copy). This is not 100% reliable, but it is accurate enough that many breeders can sex their Barred Rock chicks at hatch with 80-90% confidence.

### Breeding Implications

The sex-linkage of barring is also the foundation of one of the most practical crossbreeding tools in poultry keeping. Because *B* is dominant and sex-linked, crossing a barred female (*B/W*) with a non-barred male (*b+/b+*) produces:

- **Sons:** *B/b+* --- barred (they receive *B* from their mother on her Z chromosome and *b+* from their father)
- **Daughters:** *b+/W* --- non-barred, solid colored (they receive *b+* from their father on his Z chromosome and the W from their mother)

This is the basis of the **Black Sex-Link** cross (Barred Rock hen x Rhode Island Red rooster), one of the most widely used commercial hybrids. At hatch, barred chicks are male, solid black chicks are female, allowing instant, visual sex identification. The reverse cross (Barred Rock rooster x non-barred hen) produces all barred offspring regardless of sex, which is why the direction of the cross matters.

### The Role of Modifiers

Beyond the *B* locus itself, several modifier genes influence the quality of barring. The **narrow barring** modifier tightens the bar width and increases contrast. The **melanotic** modifier (*Ml*) can darken the base color of the dark bars. Various unnamed polygenic modifiers affect the sharpness of bar edges, the consistency of bar width across different body regions, and the extension of barring into the down and underfluff (which judges examine).

In the show ring, the ideal Barred Rock shows bars that are straight (not wavy), of equal width (not wider on one side of the shaft than the other), and consistent across the body. The bars should extend to the skin --- the underfluff should be barred, not solid gray. Achieving this requires years of selection and a deep understanding of which matings produce the tightest, most uniform barring. Many exhibition breeders maintain separate "cockerel-breeding" and "pullet-breeding" lines: a cockerel-breeding pen uses darker males and lighter females to produce sons with ideal exhibition barring, while a pullet-breeding pen uses lighter males and darker females to produce daughters with the correct contrast. This double-mating system is one of the hallmarks of serious Barred Rock exhibition breeding.

## Dual-Purpose Excellence

The term "dual-purpose" is sometimes used loosely in poultry circles, applied to any breed that lays reasonably well and has a body worth eating. The Plymouth Rock is one of the breeds that genuinely earns the label. It does not just tolerate both roles; it excels at both in a way that few breeds can match.

### Egg Production

A well-managed Barred Rock hen will begin laying at 18 to 22 weeks of age and produce approximately 200 to 280 large brown eggs in her first year. The eggs are a warm, medium brown --- not the deep chocolate of a Marans, but a respectable brown that fills an egg carton with satisfying uniformity. Shell quality is generally good: thick, smooth, with few irregularities. Production declines with age, as it does in all breeds, but Barred Rocks remain productive layers through their second and third years, and many hens continue to lay occasionally into their fifth or sixth year.

Heritage exhibition lines tend toward the lower end of the production range (180-220 eggs/year), while production-bred lines and hatchery stock can approach 280. The trade-off is a familiar one: selection for production tends to reduce body size and type, while selection for exhibition type tends to reduce laying frequency. The best dual-purpose Barred Rocks come from breeders who select for both, accepting that their birds will not win against dedicated production hybrids in a laying contest or against dedicated exhibition birds in a show hall, but will outperform either in the overall balance of traits.

### Meat Quality

Standard weights tell part of the story: 9.5 pounds for a cock, 8 pounds for a cockerel, 7.5 pounds for a hen, 6 pounds for a pullet. These are substantial birds with broad, deep bodies and well-muscled breasts. The meat of a heritage Barred Rock, particularly one raised on pasture with room to forage, has a flavor and texture that supermarket chicken cannot approach. The birds grow more slowly than commercial Cornish Cross broilers (reaching processing weight at 16-20 weeks rather than 6-8 weeks), and that slower growth produces denser muscle fibers, more intramuscular fat, and a deeper, more complex flavor.

The yellow skin of the Plymouth Rock (a breed characteristic maintained by the *BCDO2* allele introgressed from the Grey Junglefowl millennia ago) gives dressed birds the golden appearance that American consumers historically preferred. This was a significant commercial advantage in the era before plastic-wrapped, fluorescent-lit supermarket displays.

## Personality and Temperament

If you ask a hundred chicken keepers to name the friendliest breed, Plymouth Rock will be in the top three answers. These are curious birds. They follow their keepers around the yard. They come when called --- or when they hear the feed scoop. They tolerate being picked up and handled, which makes them excellent for families with children and for 4-H youth exhibition. They are not flighty; they do not panic at the sight of a garden hose or a wheelbarrow. They have enough intelligence to be interesting companions without enough intelligence to be escape artists.

In a mixed flock, Barred Rocks tend to occupy the middle of the pecking order. They are not typically the bullies, nor are they typically the victims. They are assertive enough to hold their own at the feeder and calm enough not to terrorize smaller or more timid breeds. Their foraging instinct is strong --- they are active scratchers and ground-pickers, ranging widely when given the opportunity, turning over leaf litter and mulch with impressive industry.

They are vocal in the normal way of chickens (the egg song, the predator alarm, the conversational clucking of a bird going about its business) but not excessively noisy, which matters in suburban and urban settings where neighbor relations depend partly on the decibel level of one's livestock.

## APA Varieties

The APA recognizes seven varieties of Plymouth Rock, each a distinct color or pattern bred onto the same basic body type. While the Barred variety dominates both numerically and in popular imagination, the other varieties have their own histories, their own breeding challenges, and their own devoted followings.

**Barred (1874):** The original and most common variety. Alternating dark and light bars across all plumage. The benchmark against which all other barred breeds are measured.

**White (1888):** A pure white sport of the Barred, carrying the *c* (recessive white) or *I* (dominant white) allele that suppresses all pigment. The White Plymouth Rock became the maternal foundation of the modern broiler industry when crossed with Cornish. In the show hall, the challenge is producing a bird that is truly white --- no brassiness, no straw tinge, no dark feathers --- with the correct Plymouth Rock type underneath. White birds are unforgiving: every structural flaw is visible without pattern to distract the eye.

**Buff (1894):** A warm, even buff throughout, the color of pale honey. Breeding for uniform buff is notoriously difficult because the color results from a combination of dilution genes (*Db*, *Co*, *Ig*, and polygenic modifiers) acting on a gold base. Sunlight fades buff plumage, and the tips and edges of feathers bleach at different rates than the centers, creating an uneven, washed-out appearance in birds exposed to direct sun. Show breeders of Buff Plymouth Rocks are obsessive about shade management during the months before exhibition.

**Silver Penciled (1907):** A complex and beautiful variety. Males are predominantly white with black striping in the hackle and black tail. Females show fine, concentric dark penciling on a silver-white ground, each breast and body feather carrying multiple parallel lines of dark pigment. The penciling pattern requires the interaction of *Pg*, *Db*, *Ml*, and the Silver (*S*) allele, and producing hens with crisp, well-defined penciling while maintaining correct type is one of the most demanding challenges in Plymouth Rock breeding. This variety is quite rare.

**Partridge (1910):** Males display the classic duckwing pattern (red hackle, black breast, wing bar), while females show fine penciling on a red-brown ground. The Partridge variety is built on the wild-type *e+* allele at the E locus, with pattern genes producing the sex-dimorphic appearance. Like Silver Penciled, this variety often requires double-mating to produce show-quality birds of both sexes.

**Columbian (1910):** A white bird with black hackle lacing, black tail, and black in the wing primaries. The Columbian pattern (genetically the combination of *e^Wh*, *Co*, and restrictor genes that confine black pigment to the points) is shared with the Light Brahma and the Columbian Wyandotte. Clean Columbians --- white body completely free of black ticking or smut --- require careful selection.

**Blue (1920):** A slate-blue plumage produced by the *Bl* (blue dilution) allele in heterozygous form. Blue Plymouth Rocks carry the same breeding challenge as all blue varieties: Blue x Blue produces 25% Black, 50% Blue, 25% Splash, so the breeder must maintain multiple color lines to manage the ratio. The Blue variety is less commonly exhibited than Barred or White.

## Show Standards: What Judges Look For

Judging Plymouth Rocks at an APA-sanctioned show is an exercise in evaluating balance. The ideal bird has correct type (body shape, carriage, proportions), correct color/pattern, and correct condition (feather quality, health, grooming). Points are allocated roughly as follows:

**Type and shape (50+ points):** The Plymouth Rock should be a large, broad, deep bird with a distinctly rectangular profile viewed from the side. The back is long and broad, sloping slightly to the tail. The breast is full and well-rounded. The keel bone should be long and straight, with no crooked or off-center keels. The single comb should be medium-sized, upright, evenly serrated with five points, and firm (not flopping to one side, a disqualification in males). The legs are yellow, clean, and set well apart to support the broad body. Overall impression should be one of substance without coarseness --- a powerful bird that moves with confidence.

**Color and pattern (30+ points):** In the Barred variety, the bars must be sharply defined, of equal width, and consistent across the entire body. Dark and light bars should alternate cleanly. The underfluff should be barred, not solid. In males, the bars will be lighter overall due to the double-dose effect, but they should still be distinct and regular. Common faults include smutty undercolor, uneven barring (bars wider on one side of the shaft), washed-out bars, and "bleeding" where dark and light bars smudge into each other.

**Condition (10-15 points):** The bird should be in full feather, clean, free of external parasites, with bright eyes, clean vent, and smooth, well-fitted plumage. Feather quality matters --- tight, glossy feathers that follow the body contour, not loose, ragged, or frayed plumage.

**Disqualifications** include side sprigs on the comb, feathered legs, incorrect number of toes, wry tail, and any deformity. In the Barred variety, absence of barring or predominantly solid color is a disqualification.

## The Gateway Breed

There is a reason that nearly every "Which chicken breed should I start with?" list puts the Plymouth Rock at or near the top. It is not because the breed is unremarkable --- it is because it is remarkably *balanced.* The Barred Rock asks nothing extraordinary of its keeper. It does not need the humidity management that feather-footed breeds demand. It does not have the flightiness that makes Leghorns challenging for beginners. It does not have the broodiness issues that can frustrate first-time keepers of Cochins and Silkies. It does not have the rarity that makes replacing birds difficult or the fragility that makes losses devastating.

What the Barred Rock offers instead is a gentle introduction to everything that chicken keeping involves. Its eggs teach you the rhythm of production --- the daily egg, the occasional skip, the seasonal slowdown in short winter days, the burst of production in spring. Its behavior teaches you to read chicken body language --- the dust bath sprawl, the alarm call, the contented purr of a bird being hand-fed treats. Its size teaches you about space requirements. Its foraging teaches you about pasture management. Its occasional broodiness (moderate in the breed --- some hens go broody, some never do) introduces you to the decision of whether to break a broody or let her sit.

For the keeper who starts with Barred Rocks and develops a passion for poultry, the breed also serves as an excellent introduction to exhibition. The breed is common enough that competitive classes are large and judging standards are well-established. The barring pattern teaches the breeder to evaluate plumage genetics in a tangible way --- you can see the difference between good barring and mediocre barring, and you can trace those differences to specific mating decisions. And because the breed is popular, there are experienced Barred Rock breeders in virtually every region who are willing to mentor newcomers --- the Plymouth Rock community is one of the most welcoming in the poultry fancy.

## PNW Suitability

The Pacific Northwest is excellent Plymouth Rock country. The breed's attributes align well with the specific challenges and opportunities of keeping chickens in western Washington and Oregon.

**Rain tolerance.** Plymouth Rocks have tight, close-fitting plumage that sheds water effectively. They do not have the fluffy, absorbent feathering of Cochins or Orpingtons that becomes waterlogged in persistent drizzle. They will forage in light rain without hesitation. A covered run is still essential for the sustained rain events that characterize PNW winters (November through March), but Barred Rocks handle wet conditions better than many breeds.

**Cold hardiness.** With their substantial body mass, dense plumage, and medium-sized single comb, Plymouth Rocks handle PNW winters without difficulty. Western Washington winters are mild by continental standards (typically 25-45°F in the coldest months), well within the comfort range of this breed. The single comb can be vulnerable to frostbite in extreme cold snaps, but these are rare enough west of the Cascades that comb frostbite is not a significant concern. East of the Cascades, where winter temperatures regularly drop below 0°F, a dab of petroleum jelly on the comb points on the coldest nights provides adequate protection.

**Hawk awareness.** Plymouth Rocks are alert, ground-aware birds with strong predator instincts. The barred plumage may provide a degree of visual disruption against aerial predators --- the theory, supported by anecdotal observation if not rigorous study, is that the irregular pattern makes it harder for a raptor to lock onto a single bird in a flock of barred birds compared to a flock of solid-colored birds. Whether or not this is visually significant, Barred Rocks are reliably vocal about overhead threats. They give the alarm call, and they head for cover. In a PNW yard shared with Cooper's hawks, red-tailed hawks, and the occasional bald eagle, this alertness matters.

**Foraging quality.** The PNW growing season supports lush, insect-rich pasture from April through October. Plymouth Rocks are enthusiastic foragers who will convert that pasture into eggs with minimal supplemental feed during the growing season. They are excellent at slug control --- and in the PNW, slug control is a year-round concern. They scratch effectively in mulched garden beds and compost piles, accelerating decomposition while finding protein-rich invertebrates that enhance egg quality (deeper yolk color, firmer whites).

**Community.** The poultry community in the Pacific Northwest is large, active, and well-organized. The Pacific Poultry Breeders Association, local 4-H programs, county fairs throughout Washington and Oregon, and informal networking through feed stores and farmers' markets provide a robust support system for both new and experienced keepers. Plymouth Rocks are well-represented at every level of this community.

## The Barred Rock as Living History

When you keep Barred Plymouth Rocks, you are keeping a bird that your great-grandparents might have kept. The breed has been in continuous production in North America for more than 150 years. It has fed families through economic depressions, world wars, and every social transformation of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The White variety built the industrial broiler industry, for better or worse. The Barred variety remained on the small farm and in the backyard, outlasting every trend, every fad breed, every wave of industrial consolidation that reduced America's commercial poultry to a handful of proprietary hybrids.

The Livestock Conservancy lists the Plymouth Rock as **Recovering** --- a status that reflects the breed's resurgence among backyard keepers after decades of decline during the industrial era. This recovery is real and it is important. Every flock of well-bred Barred Rocks maintained by a careful breeder is a repository of genetic diversity that took generations to develop and could be lost in a single generation of neglect.

For the show breeder, the Barred Rock is a lifetime pursuit. The quest for perfect barring --- those ruler-straight, evenly spaced, sharply defined bars from comb to toe, consistent across the body, correct in both sexes --- is a challenge that rewards patience, careful record-keeping, and a willingness to learn from both success and failure. The breed has been bred for exhibition for 150 years, and the best birds today are as fine as any in the breed's history. That is not an accident. It is the result of a continuous chain of dedicated breeders, each generation building on the last, each bird a living argument that beauty and utility are not opposing values.

The Barred Plymouth Rock is America's chicken. It earned the title honestly, and it keeps earning it every morning in thousands of coops across the country, when a broad-breasted, barred hen steps into the nesting box, settles her weight, and gets to work.
