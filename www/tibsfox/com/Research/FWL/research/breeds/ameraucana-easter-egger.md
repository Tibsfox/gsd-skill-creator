# The Blue Egg Question --- Ameraucana, Araucana, and Easter Egger

No topic in backyard poultry generates more confusion, more misinformation, more aggrieved correction in online forums, and more genuinely useful genetics education than the distinction between Ameraucanas, Araucanas, and Easter Eggers. These three names are used interchangeably by feed stores, casually by new chicken keepers, defensively by breeders, and incorrectly by almost everyone at least once. The confusion is understandable because the three categories share a single genetic gift --- the blue eggshell gene --- and because the poultry industry has not always been scrupulous about labeling. But the distinctions are real, they matter for breeding programs, and understanding them opens a window into one of the most remarkable stories in poultry genetics: how a retroviral insertion in a South American chicken created a shell color that captivated the world.

This document profiles all three categories: the Araucana (a true breed with a lethal gene problem), the Ameraucana (an American breed developed to preserve the blue egg without the lethality), and the Easter Egger (not a breed at all, but a category of mixed-heritage birds carrying the blue egg gene in various combinations). It covers the genetics of blue eggshell color, the combinatorics that produce green, olive, and sage eggs, and the practical implications for backyard keepers who want colored eggs in their carton.

---

## The Araucana: Chile, Ear Tufts, and a Lethal Gene

The story begins in Chile, where indigenous Mapuche people kept chickens that laid blue-shelled eggs long before European contact. These birds --- generically called "Araucana" after the Araucanía region of south-central Chile --- were first described to the broader poultry world by Chilean professor Salvador Castelló at the First World Poultry Congress in The Hague in 1921. Castelló presented two distinct types: the *Collonca* (a rumpless, clean-faced bird that laid blue eggs) and the *Quetro* (a tailed, ear-tufted bird that also laid blue eggs). The combination of blue eggs and unusual physical traits --- rumplessness, ear tufts --- generated immediate international interest.

The Araucana as a standardized breed in North America descends from efforts to combine these traits into a single bird. The APA recognized the Araucana in 1976, and the current APA standard requires:

**Rumplessness:** The Araucana lacks a tail. This is not a trimmed or docked tail --- the bird is genetically rumpless, lacking the final vertebrae of the coccyx and the associated musculature. The rump is rounded and smooth, ending abruptly without tail feathers or the oil gland (uropygial gland) that sits at the base of the tail in tailed birds. The absence of the oil gland means rumpless Araucanas cannot preen their feathers with the waxy secretion that provides waterproofing in other breeds, making them somewhat less weather-resistant.

**Ear tufts:** The Araucana carries the *Et* (ear tufts) gene, which produces distinctive tufts of feathers that project outward from a small flap of skin (peduncle) near the ear. These tufts are not the same as the muffs and beard of the Ameraucana --- they are smaller, stiffer projections that emerge from the side of the head rather than from beneath the beak and around the face. In the best exhibition birds, the tufts are symmetrical, well-formed, and project at a jaunty angle.

**Blue eggs:** The defining trait. Araucana hens lay eggs that are blue throughout the shell thickness --- inside and outside, top and bottom, a true structural blue that cannot be rubbed off.

### The Lethal Gene Problem

The ear tufts gene (*Et*) carries a devastating genetic burden. **The *Et* allele is lethal in the homozygous state.** Chicks that inherit *Et* from both parents (*Et/Et*) die in the shell, typically around day 17--19 of incubation. They simply fail to hatch. This means:

- A cross of tufted x tufted (*Et/et+* x *Et/et+*) produces, on average: 25% *Et/Et* (dead in shell), 50% *Et/et+* (tufted), 25% *et+/et+* (clean-faced). Of the live chicks, 2/3 are tufted and 1/3 are clean-faced.
- There is no way to produce a flock of 100% tufted Araucanas. The homozygous lethal allele makes it genetically impossible. Every generation of tufted x tufted matings produces 25% embryonic mortality.

This is not a theoretical concern. It is a real, measurable reduction in hatch rate that every Araucana breeder must accept and manage. A hatch rate of 75% under perfect incubation conditions (where a non-lethal breed might achieve 85--90%) is the genetic ceiling for tufted x tufted matings. Some breeders cross tufted birds with clean-faced birds to improve hatch rates, accepting that only 50% of surviving offspring will be tufted.

The rumpless trait adds further complexity. Rumplessness is controlled by a separate gene (*Rp*), and while *Rp* is not lethal in the same way as *Et*, it is associated with reduced fertility in some matings --- possibly because the absence of the tail and its associated musculature affects the mechanics of mating (the rooster uses the tail for balance during copulation). Combining rumplessness, tufts, and blue egg color in a single bird requires navigating two separate genetic challenges, and it is part of what makes the Araucana a breed for dedicated, experienced breeders rather than beginners.

### APA Recognized Colors

The APA recognizes five Araucana varieties: **Black**, **Black Breasted Red**, **Golden Duckwing**, **Silver Duckwing**, and **White**.

## The Ameraucana: The American Solution

The Ameraucana was developed in the United States in the 1970s as a deliberate response to the Araucana's genetic problems. American breeders wanted to preserve the blue egg --- the trait that made the Araucana fascinating --- while eliminating the lethal tufts gene and the reproductive challenges of rumplessness. The solution was to breed birds that carried the blue egg gene but had normal tails, muffs and beards (instead of ear tufts), and none of the associated lethality.

The Ameraucana was recognized by the APA in 1984. The standard requires:

**Tail:** Present and normal. Ameraucanas have a full tail, carried at a moderate angle.

**Muffs and beard:** Instead of ear tufts, Ameraucanas carry the muff/beard gene (*Mb*), which produces a full, fluffy beard of feathers beneath the beak and prominent muffs (feather tufts on the cheeks). The muffs and beard give the Ameraucana's face a round, puffy appearance that is one of the breed's most appealing visual features. The *Mb* gene is not lethal in any combination --- *Mb/Mb* homozygotes are fully viable, and muffs/beard can be bred to 100% in a flock.

**Blue eggs:** Like the Araucana, Ameraucana hens lay true blue eggs. The shell is blue throughout, inside and out.

**Pea comb:** The Ameraucana carries the pea comb (*P*), a low, compact comb with three longitudinal ridges. The pea comb is practical --- it sits close to the head and is highly resistant to frostbite, making the Ameraucana well-suited to cold climates.

### APA Recognized Colors

The APA recognizes eight Ameraucana varieties: **Black**, **Blue**, **Blue Wheaten**, **Brown Red**, **Buff**, **Silver**, **Wheaten**, and **White**. Each variety has specific plumage standards, and the diversity of recognized colors gives breeders a range of aesthetic options while maintaining the breed's essential characteristics (blue eggs, muffs/beard, pea comb, full tail).

### The Ameraucana as a Breed

The Ameraucana is a true breed: it breeds true for its defining characteristics, it has a published standard, it is recognized by the APA, and organized breed clubs (the Ameraucana Breeders Club, the Ameraucana Alliance) maintain the standard and promote the breed. Ameraucanas are medium-sized birds (standard weights: Cock 6.5 lbs, Hen 5.5 lbs), active, alert, and reasonably good layers of medium-sized blue eggs (approximately 150--200 per year, varying by strain).

Their temperament is variable --- some strains are friendly and handleable, others are more aloof and flighty. This variability reflects the breed's relatively recent development (compared to breeds like the Plymouth Rock, which has had 150+ years of temperament selection) and the diversity of foundation stock used by different breeders. As the breed matures, temperament is becoming more consistent as breeders select for it alongside type and egg color.

## The Easter Egger: What the Feed Store Sells

Here is where the confusion lives. Walk into any feed store in America during chick season (February through May in most regions) and you will find a bin of chicks labeled "Ameraucana" or "Americana" or "Americauna" --- note the creative spelling --- priced at $3--5 each and described as "blue/green egg layers." These chicks are, in the vast majority of cases, **not Ameraucanas.** They are Easter Eggers.

An Easter Egger is not a breed. It is a category --- a catchall term for any chicken of mixed heritage that carries the blue egg gene (*O*) but does not conform to the standard of either the Araucana or the Ameraucana. Easter Eggers are the result of crossing blue-egg birds with other breeds, producing offspring that carry the *O* allele (and may lay blue, green, olive, or even brown eggs depending on the combination of shell color genes) but that vary widely in appearance, body type, comb type, and other characteristics.

Easter Eggers are not inherently inferior birds. Many of them are excellent layers, hardy, friendly, and beautiful in their individuality. They are the "mutts" of the egg-color world, and like mutts, they have the advantage of hybrid vigor and the charm of unpredictability. A flock of Easter Eggers may produce eggs in blue, green, olive, sage, pink, cream, and brown --- the rainbow egg carton that is one of the great pleasures of backyard chicken keeping.

What Easter Eggers are *not* is a standardized breed. They do not breed true. Two Easter Egger parents may produce offspring that lay blue eggs, green eggs, brown eggs, or white eggs. They may or may not have muffs and beards. They may have single combs, pea combs, or rose combs. They may be feather-legged or clean-legged. They may be large-fowl or bantam-sized. The variability is the point: these are mixed-heritage birds, and their genetics are unpredictable.

The problem --- and the reason that breed purists get exercised about the labeling --- is that feed stores and hatcheries frequently sell Easter Eggers under the name "Ameraucana" (or a misspelling thereof). A buyer who thinks they are getting a true Ameraucana may receive a bird that lays green or olive eggs instead of blue, that has a single comb instead of a pea comb, or that has no muffs or beard. For a backyard keeper who simply wants colorful eggs, this is a non-issue --- the bird will lay beautiful eggs regardless of its breed purity. For a breeder who wants to exhibit Ameraucanas at APA-sanctioned shows or to produce birds that breed true for blue eggs, the difference is fundamental.

**How to tell the difference:** A true Ameraucana from a reputable breeder will have a pea comb, muffs and beard, a full tail, slate-blue (not yellow or green) legs in most varieties, and will lay a true blue egg (not green, not olive, not tinted). The breeder will be able to tell you the bird's variety (Black, Blue, Wheaten, etc.) and will likely be a member of the Ameraucana Breeders Club or a similar organization. The chicks will cost $10--25+ each rather than $3--5.

## The Genetics of Blue: SLCO1B3 and the Retrovirus

The blue eggshell color that unites Araucanas, Ameraucanas, and Easter Eggers is produced by a single pigment --- **biliverdin** (also called **oocyanin**) --- and its deposition is controlled by a single genetic event that is one of the most remarkable stories in poultry genetics.

### The O Allele

The gene responsible for blue egg color is designated **O** (for oocyanin) and is located on chicken chromosome 1. The *O* allele is autosomal dominant: a single copy is sufficient to produce blue eggs. The wild-type allele (*o+*) produces no biliverdin deposition, resulting in white or brown eggs depending on other color genes. The dominance of *O* means:

- *O/O* (homozygous blue): Blue eggs. All offspring will carry at least one copy of *O*.
- *O/o+* (heterozygous blue): Blue eggs. Offspring have a 50% chance of inheriting *O*.
- *o+/o+* (homozygous wild-type): No blue. White or brown depending on other genes.

The intensity of blue is reportedly slightly greater in *O/O* homozygotes than in *O/o+* heterozygotes, but the difference is subtle and variable.

### The Retroviral Insertion

The molecular basis of the *O* allele was elucidated by Wang et al. (2013) in a study of Chinese Dongxiang and Lushi chickens, with the findings independently confirmed in South American Araucana chickens. The *O* allele results from an **endogenous retrovirus (EAV-HP) insertion** in the promoter region of the *SLCO1B3* gene (Solute Carrier Organic Anion Transporter Family Member 1B3).

SLCO1B3 encodes a membrane transporter protein that, in its normal context (primarily in the liver), transports bilirubin and other organic anions. The retroviral insertion in the *O* allele causes **ectopic expression** of SLCO1B3 in the shell gland --- an organ where the gene is not normally active. The ectopically expressed transporter protein moves biliverdin from the blood into the shell gland epithelial cells, where it is deposited into the developing eggshell matrix.

This is a genuinely extraordinary piece of biology. A retrovirus --- a relic of an ancient viral infection that integrated into the chicken genome --- landed in a position that hijacked a liver transporter gene and redirected it to the reproductive tract. The result was a new phenotype (blue eggshell) that had no precedent in the species and that was immediately visible and selectable. The same retroviral insertion has been found in geographically separated chicken populations (South American and Chinese), raising the question of whether the insertion occurred once in an ancestral population that was subsequently dispersed, or independently in separate lineages (convergent retroviral insertion, which would be extraordinary).

### Biliverdin vs. Protoporphyrin

Unlike protoporphyrin IX (the brown pigment), biliverdin is deposited **throughout the full thickness of the eggshell**, not just on the surface. A blue egg is blue all the way through. If you crack a blue egg and examine the interior surface of the shell, it is blue. If you sand or scratch the exterior surface of a blue egg, the color beneath is also blue. This is because biliverdin is incorporated into the calcite crystal matrix during shell formation, not painted on afterward.

This structural difference has a practical implication: **blue egg color does not fade over the laying cycle the way brown color does.** Because biliverdin deposition is part of the shell formation process itself (not a late-stage surface coating), the color is relatively consistent from egg to egg. A blue-egg hen's first egg of the cycle and her fortieth will be essentially the same shade of blue. This contrasts sharply with the Marans, where the brown surface coating fades measurably over the cycle.

## Color Combinatorics: Blue + Brown = Green

The full palette of colored eggs emerges from the interaction of the blue gene (*O*) with the brown pigment genes (protoporphyrin IX deposition, polygenic):

**Blue + no brown = Blue.** This is the pure blue egg of the Araucana and Ameraucana. The shell contains biliverdin throughout its thickness and no protoporphyrin on the surface. The color ranges from pale sky blue to a deeper robin's-egg blue, depending on the amount of biliverdin deposited (which varies between individuals and strains).

**Blue + light brown = Green.** When a bird carries both the *O* allele (biliverdin in the shell) and moderate protoporphyrin deposition genes, the result is a green egg. The blue base shows through the brown overlay, and the combination reads as green to the eye. The shade of green depends on the intensity of the brown: lighter brown + blue = mint or sage; moderate brown + blue = olive or moss green.

**Blue + dark brown = Olive.** The deliberate cross of a dark-brown-egg breed (Marans, Barnevelder, Welsummer) with a blue-egg breed (Ameraucana, Araucana) produces what breeders call an **Olive Egger** --- a bird that carries both the *O* allele and the polygenic dark-brown genes, laying eggs that range from khaki to deep olive to a rich, dark greenish-brown. The first-generation (F1) Olive Egger typically produces the darkest olive eggs, because the F1 inherits one copy of *O* from the blue-egg parent and the full complement of brown genes from the dark-egg parent. Subsequent generations (F2, F3) produce more variable egg colors as the brown genes segregate.

**No blue + brown = Brown.** Standard brown eggs, as laid by Plymouth Rocks, Rhode Island Reds, Orpingtons, and other brown-egg breeds. No biliverdin, only protoporphyrin.

**No blue + no brown = White.** Standard white eggs, as laid by Leghorns, Minorcas, and other white-egg breeds. No pigment of either type.

### Why Easter Eggers Produce a Rainbow

Easter Eggers are, by definition, birds of mixed heritage carrying the *O* allele in combination with unknown and variable brown-pigment genetics. An Easter Egger that inherits *O* from one parent and heavy brown-pigment genes from the other will lay olive eggs. One that inherits *O* and light brown genes will lay green eggs. One that inherits *O* and no brown genes will lay blue eggs. And one that does not inherit *O* at all (it received *o+* from the blue-egg parent) will lay brown or white eggs, depending on its other genetics.

This is why a flock of Easter Eggers produces the rainbow carton: each bird has a different combination of blue-gene status and brown-pigment intensity, and the resulting egg colors span the full spectrum from blue through green through olive through brown. The individual hen's egg color is consistent (the same hen will lay essentially the same color throughout her life, subject to the slight brown-fade effect over laying cycles), but the flock-level variation is wide.

## Olive Eggers: The Intentional Cross

The Olive Egger is not a breed. Like the Easter Egger, it is a category --- but unlike the Easter Egger, it is a category with a deliberate purpose. Olive Eggers are intentional crosses of blue-egg breeds (almost always Ameraucanas) with dark-brown-egg breeds (almost always Marans, specifically Black Copper Marans) designed to produce the darkest possible olive eggs.

The breeding logic:

**F1 (first generation):** Ameraucana (O/o+, no brown genes) x Marans (o+/o+, heavy brown genes) = F1 offspring that are O/o+ (carrying one copy of blue) plus heterozygous for the brown-pigment polygenes. These F1 hens lay olive eggs --- the blue base shows through the brown overlay. The quality of the olive depends on the darkness of the Marans parent's egg color: a score-7 Marans parent produces darker olives than a score-4 parent.

**F2 and beyond:** Crossing F1 Olive Eggers with each other produces highly variable offspring. Some will be O/O (two copies of blue), some O/o+, some o+/o+. The brown-pigment polygenes will also segregate. The result is eggs ranging from blue through green through olive through brown. Maintaining deep olive color in subsequent generations requires back-crossing to the dark-egg parent (Marans) while selecting for *O* carriers, a program that takes patience and careful record-keeping.

Some breeders are working toward standardizing the Olive Egger as a recognized breed, but this is a long-term project. The challenge is that olive egg color depends on the interaction of one major gene (*O*) with a polygenic system (brown intensity), making it harder to fix than traits controlled by single genes.

## Why This Matters for Backyard Keepers

For the backyard keeper who wants colored eggs, the practical implications are:

**If you want guaranteed true blue eggs:** Buy Ameraucanas from a reputable breeder (not a feed store), or buy Araucanas if you are prepared for the breed's reproductive challenges. True breeding stock costs more but breeds true.

**If you want a rainbow carton and do not care about breed purity:** Easter Eggers are excellent, affordable, and widely available. They are healthy, often friendly, and produce beautiful eggs in unpredictable colors. Embrace the surprise.

**If you want olive eggs specifically:** Buy F1 Olive Eggers from a breeder who crosses dark-egg Marans with Ameraucanas. The F1 cross is the most reliable way to get deep olive color. Do not expect olive egg color to breed true in the F2 generation without sustained selection.

**If you are buying from a feed store:** Assume that anything labeled "Ameraucana," "Americana," or "Americauna" (or "Araucana," for that matter) is an Easter Egger unless the store can provide specific breed documentation. This is not a criticism of the bird --- Easter Eggers are wonderful chickens --- it is simply accurate labeling. A feed-store "Ameraucana" that lays green eggs is an Easter Egger that lays green eggs, and it will do so beautifully for years.

**For the show hall:** Only true Ameraucanas and true Araucanas can be exhibited at APA-sanctioned shows. Easter Eggers and Olive Eggers have no APA standard and cannot be shown. If your goal includes exhibition, sourcing from a breed club member is essential.

The blue egg gene is one of nature's improbable gifts --- a retrovirus that became a pigment, a mutation that became a breed characteristic, a South American chicken that changed egg cartons worldwide. Whether the blue arrives in a purebred Ameraucana or a feed-store Easter Egger, the egg itself is the same shade of remarkable.
