# The Cream Legbar --- The Auto-Sexing Blue Egg Layer

There is a breed of chicken that exists because one of the greatest geneticists of the twentieth century decided to solve a practical poultry problem using the same principles of inheritance that he taught undergraduates using diagrams of colored squares. The geneticist was Reginald Crundall Punnett --- yes, *that* Punnett, the man whose name graces the Punnett square in every introductory biology textbook on the planet. The problem was this: how do you know which of your newly hatched chicks are hens and which are roosters, without waiting four to six weeks for secondary sex characteristics to develop? And the breed he helped create --- the Cream Legbar --- remains, nearly a century later, the most genetically storied chicken in the world: the only breed that combines auto-sexing (distinguishing males from females at day-old by down color), blue eggs (from Araucana ancestry), and a distinctive crested head, all in a package that is also a vigorous, productive, free-range layer.

The Cream Legbar is a bird that carries the history of twentieth-century genetics in its feathers.

---

## Punnett and Pease: Cambridge, 1930s

### Reginald Punnett (1875-1967)

To understand the Cream Legbar, you must understand the man who made it. Reginald Punnett was not a poultry breeder who dabbled in genetics. He was a geneticist of the first rank who happened to find chickens an excellent experimental organism.

Punnett studied at Cambridge, where he became one of the earliest advocates for Mendelian genetics in Britain. In 1905, working with William Bateson (who coined the word "genetics"), Punnett co-discovered genetic linkage --- the tendency of genes on the same chromosome to be inherited together, violating Mendel's law of independent assortment. This was a landmark finding in the young science of genetics. The Punnett square --- the grid method for predicting offspring genotype ratios --- appeared in his 1905 book *Mendelism* and has been a standard pedagogical tool ever since [FWL-04].

Punnett held the Arthur Balfour Chair of Genetics at Cambridge from 1912 to 1940. During this period, he maintained extensive poultry flocks at the Cambridge University genetics farm, using chickens as model organisms for studying inheritance. Chickens were ideal: short generation time, large clutch sizes, visible sex-linked traits, and an extensive body of existing breed knowledge to draw on. Punnett published extensively on poultry color genetics, feather structure, comb genetics, and the sex-determination system that makes chickens (with their ZW sex chromosomes) the mirror image of mammals.

It was Punnett's work on sex-linked barring that led to the auto-sexing breeds.

### The Auto-Sexing Insight

The principle behind auto-sexing is elegant, and Punnett understood it perfectly because he had spent decades studying exactly this kind of sex-linked inheritance [FWL-04].

In chickens, sex is determined by the Z and W chromosomes: males are ZZ (homogametic) and females are ZW (heterogametic) --- the reverse of the XX/XY system in mammals. Traits carried on the Z chromosome are therefore inherited differently in males and females, just as X-linked traits are in humans.

The sex-linked barring gene (*B*), located on the Z chromosome, produces alternating dark and light bars on each feather. Because *B* shows a dosage effect --- two copies (in ZZ males) produce lighter, more prominent barring than one copy (in ZW females) --- males and females carrying *B* look different even as day-old chicks. Males hatch with a pale, creamy down and a prominent light head spot. Females hatch with darker down and a smaller, less distinct (or absent) head spot [FWL-04].

But here is the critical distinction: sex-linked barring alone creates **sex-linked** crosses (like the Black Sex Link, produced by crossing a barred hen with a non-barred rooster), not **auto-sexing** breeds. In a sex-linked cross, the sexing works only in the first generation. Breed the F1 birds together and the barring segregates unpredictably in the F2.

What Punnett wanted was a breed that was **true-breeding** for sex-linked barring --- a breed where every mating produced chicks that could be sexed at hatch, generation after generation, because all birds in the breed carried the barring gene in the correct configuration. The key was to create a breed where both males and females carried *B* on their Z chromosome(s): males as *B/B* (homozygous barred) and females as *B/W* (hemizygous barred, their only Z chromosome carrying *B*). In such a breed, every cross produces *B/B* or *B/b* males (light chick down, prominent head spot) and *B/W* females (darker chick down, small or absent head spot). The sexing is automatic. It works every time. It breeds true.

### Michael Pease and the Breeding Program

Punnett conceived the auto-sexing principle, but it was his colleague Michael Pease (1906-1993) who carried out much of the practical breeding work. Pease was a research associate in the Cambridge genetics department and later became a distinguished poultry geneticist in his own right. Together, Punnett and Pease developed a series of auto-sexing breeds, each based on a different existing breed crossed with a barred breed (typically the Barred Plymouth Rock) and then backcrossed and selected until the auto-sexing trait bred true within the target breed type.

The Legbar series was their most successful project.

## The Three Legbars

Punnett and Pease created three Legbar varieties, each based on crossing Leghorn varieties with Barred Plymouth Rocks:

### Gold Legbar (1930s)

The first auto-sexing breed, created by crossing Brown Leghorns with Barred Plymouth Rocks. Males are golden-barred; females are darker. The Gold Legbar was standardized in Britain in 1945 but never achieved widespread popularity and is now extremely rare.

### Silver Legbar (1930s)

Created by crossing Silver Leghorns with Barred Plymouth Rocks. The Silver Legbar was standardized in 1951. Like the Gold, it is now very rare.

### Cream Legbar (1930s, standardized 1958)

The Cream Legbar is the masterpiece of the series, and the only Legbar variety that achieved lasting popularity. Its creation involved an additional, revolutionary cross: the introduction of **Araucana** blood.

Punnett obtained Araucana birds --- the blue-egg-laying, ear-tufted Chilean breed --- and crossed them into the Legbar program. The Araucana contributed two traits that transformed the Cream Legbar from a merely useful auto-sexing layer into something unique:

1. **The blue egg gene (*O*).** The Cream Legbar lays blue eggs. Not green, not olive, not blue-tinted-brown --- blue. The *O* allele, a dominant autosomal gene traced to a retroviral insertion in the *SLCO1B3* gene, causes deposition of biliverdin throughout the eggshell, producing structural blue that penetrates the entire shell thickness [FWL-04, FWL-05, breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger]. The Cream Legbar is, as of this writing, the only auto-sexing breed that also lays blue eggs. No other breed combines both traits.

2. **The crest.** Cream Legbars carry a small, upright crest of feathers on the top of the head --- a genetic contribution from the Araucana, though the Cream Legbar's crest is smaller and more upright than the Araucana's ear tufts. The crest gives the bird a distinctive, alert, slightly jaunty appearance. It is a breed requirement in the British Poultry Standards and in the breed clubs that maintain Cream Legbar standards internationally.

The Cream Legbar was standardized by the Poultry Club of Great Britain in 1958. It has not, as of early 2026, been admitted to the American Poultry Association Standard of Perfection, though an APA recognition effort is underway and has been pending for several years. This makes the Cream Legbar one of the most well-known and widely kept breeds that is *not* officially APA-recognized --- a breed whose genetic pedigree is impeccable, whose practical virtues are proven, and whose admission to the American Standard is largely a matter of paperwork and process.

## Auto-Sexing in Practice: What You See at Hatch

The auto-sexing phenotype in Cream Legbar chicks is reliable and, with a little practice, easy to read.

**Female chicks** hatch with dark brown to chipmunk-striped down. The head is dark, with a well-defined dark stripe running from the beak through the eye toward the back of the head. The body down is dark brown with faint lighter striping. The overall impression is of a dark, warmly colored chick.

**Male chicks** hatch with noticeably paler, creamier down. The head shows a prominent pale or white spot (the "head spot") that is larger and more distinct than any spot on female chicks. The body down is lighter, often cream or pale buff with lighter, less distinct striping. The overall impression is of a pale, washed-out chick compared to his sisters.

The accuracy of day-old sexing in Cream Legbars, when performed by someone who knows what to look for, exceeds 95%. Some experienced breeders report 99% accuracy. This is comparable to the accuracy of commercial vent-sexing (which requires a trained technician and causes some chick mortality) and far exceeds the accuracy of feather-sexing in non-auto-sexing breeds.

For the backyard keeper, auto-sexing means:
- You can order or hatch chicks with near-certain knowledge of how many hens you will get
- You can make rehoming decisions for unwanted cockerels on day one rather than waiting 4-8 weeks for crowing
- You can plan your flock composition before the birds are old enough to eat significant feed
- If you hatch your own eggs, you can sell or give away accurately sexed chicks, which are vastly more useful to buyers than unsexed birds

In an era when many cities permit hens but not roosters, and when most backyard keepers want layers rather than crowers, auto-sexing is not a novelty. It is a practical tool that solves a real problem.

## Physical Description

The Cream Legbar is a medium-sized, active, elegantly built bird that shows its Leghorn ancestry in its alertness and carriage and its Plymouth Rock ancestry in its solidity.

**Body:** Medium-sized, well-balanced, with a moderately long back and a full breast. The body is wedge-shaped when viewed from the side, carried at a jaunty forward-leaning angle that gives the bird an alert, ready appearance. This is not a heavy, sedentary breed --- it is an active bird that covers ground when foraging.

**Head:** The head is moderate-sized, with the distinctive small crest projecting upward and slightly backward from the crown. The crest should be upright and compact, not floppy or drooping. The single comb is medium-sized, upright, with five to seven well-defined points. Wattles are medium, pendant, and red. Earlobes are white or cream --- the white earlobe typically (though not infallibly) correlating with white or tinted eggs in other breeds, but overridden in the Cream Legbar by the *O* blue egg gene [FWL-04].

**Plumage:** The base color is a warm cream, overlaid with soft barring in males and more distinct barring in females. Male plumage shows cream and gray barring across the body, with a cream-gold hackle and saddle. Female plumage is darker, with more pronounced gray-brown barring on a cream base, and a distinctive salmon-pink breast. The sexual dimorphism in plumage is characteristic of the breed's auto-sexing genetics and persists into adulthood --- males are noticeably paler and more golden, females are darker and more warmly toned.

**Legs:** Clean (unfeathered), yellow, with four toes.

## Egg Production

Cream Legbars are productive layers: approximately 180-220 eggs per year, with some well-bred strains reaching higher totals. The eggs are medium to large, and they are **blue**. The shade of blue varies from sky blue to a deeper, more turquoise blue depending on the individual hen and her genetic background. Hens with strong *O* allele expression and minimal brown pigment modifier genes produce the clearest, most vivid blue; hens with some brown modifiers may produce eggs with a slightly greenish or sage-tinted cast.

For keepers building a rainbow egg basket [guides/egg-color-rainbow], the Cream Legbar is the premier blue-egg breed: it produces blue eggs reliably, breeds true (unlike Easter Eggers, whose egg color varies [breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger]), and the auto-sexing trait means you can hatch replacements with confidence about the sex ratio of the resulting chicks.

Cream Legbars are not particularly broody. Their Leghorn heritage bred out most of the brooding instinct, and they are, on the whole, production-focused layers rather than dedicated mothers. If you want to hatch Cream Legbar eggs, plan on using an incubator or a broody hen of another breed (a Silkie or Cochin makes an excellent foster mother [breeds/silkie, FWL-02b]).

## Temperament and Foraging

The Cream Legbar's temperament reflects its Leghorn ancestry: active, alert, busy, and somewhat independent. These are not lap chickens. They do not, as a rule, enjoy being picked up and held. They will come to you for treats, and they will follow you across the yard with the single-minded determination of a bird that has learned you are a source of mealworms, but they maintain a boundary of personal space that more docile breeds (Brahmas, Orpingtons) do not insist on [breeds/brahma, breeds/orpington].

This independence is paired with exceptional foraging ability. Cream Legbars are among the most industrious foragers in the poultry world. Turn them loose in a yard and they cover every square foot, scratching through mulch, hunting through grass, picking insects from leaf undersides, and generally conducting themselves like small, feathered bloodhounds on the trail of anything edible. In a free-range or large-run setup, their foraging reduces the feed bill and produces eggs with the deep orange yolks and rich flavor that come from a diverse, insect-supplemented diet [FWL-05].

The flip side of this activity level is that Cream Legbars can be flighty. They startle more easily than heavy breeds, they fly better than heavy breeds (they can clear a four-foot fence without difficulty), and they are more inclined to range far from the coop if given the opportunity. For PNW keepers with unfenced yards adjacent to neighbors, roads, or natural areas, the Cream Legbar's wandering tendency may require management --- either a covered run or wing clipping.

They are also vocal. Not aggressive, not mean, but chatty --- they announce eggs, they announce treats, they announce the arrival of interesting objects in the yard, and they announce their opinions about the weather. This is charm or annoyance depending on your proximity to neighbors and your tolerance for poultry commentary.

## The Cream Legbar in the Pacific Northwest

The Cream Legbar is well-suited to the PNW in several respects:

**Foraging.** The PNW's mild climate and insect-rich environment (even in winter, Pacific Northwest soil harbors earthworms and overwintering invertebrates year-round) suits the Cream Legbar's foraging nature. A flock with access to even a modest yard can supplement their diet substantially.

**Rain tolerance.** The Cream Legbar's tight, close-fitting plumage sheds rain reasonably well --- better than fluffy breeds like Orpingtons or Silkies. They will forage in light rain and dry off quickly. They are not, however, waterproof, and persistent heavy rain will drive them to cover.

**Cold hardiness.** The single comb is the Cream Legbar's vulnerability in cold weather. Unlike the Brahma's pea comb [breeds/brahma] or the Wyandotte's rose comb [breeds/wyandotte], the Cream Legbar's upright single comb can be susceptible to frostbite in the rare PNW cold snaps that drop temperatures into the teens. For most PNW winters (25-45 degrees F), this is not a concern. In unusual cold events, petroleum jelly on the comb provides protection.

**Predator awareness.** The Cream Legbar's alert, active temperament makes it one of the more predator-aware breeds. They are quick to spot aerial threats (hawks, eagles) and quick to seek cover. In a mixed flock, the Cream Legbar may be the first bird to sound the alarm. This is a genuine advantage in the PNW, where Cooper's Hawks and Red-tailed Hawks are common suburban predators [guides/predator-defense-pnw, FWL-09].

## The Punnett Connection: Why This Breed Matters

The Cream Legbar is more than a good chicken. It is a living monument to the power of applied genetics --- proof that the abstract science of inheritance, studied with pea plants by Mendel and diagrammed in squares by Punnett, can be translated into practical tools that solve real agricultural problems.

Every Cream Legbar chick that hatches, showing its sex in the color of its down, is a Punnett square made flesh. The sex-linked barring that distinguishes males from females at hatch is the same ZZ/ZW inheritance pattern that Punnett taught in Cambridge lecture halls. The blue egg is a retroviral insertion --- an ancient natural genetic engineering event --- that Punnett and Pease captured and fixed in a breed. The crest is a contribution from Araucana birds whose blue-egg trait Punnett recognized as genetically dominant and therefore easy to introduce [FWL-04].

No other chicken breed has a pedigree that reads like a genetics textbook. The Cream Legbar is not just descended from interesting birds. It was *designed* by the people who invented the tools we use to understand how inheritance works. It is, in a very real sense, the chicken that genetics built.

## Sourcing and Availability

The Cream Legbar's lack of APA recognition means it is not available from most large commercial hatcheries in the United States. Sourcing quality Cream Legbar stock requires:

- **Specialty breeders.** The Cream Legbar Club (US) and individual breeders who import or maintain lines from British stock are the best sources. Quality varies enormously --- insist on birds from crested, blue-egg-laying lines with demonstrated auto-sexing accuracy.
- **Breed clubs.** The Cream Legbar Club of Great Britain maintains breed standards and breeder directories. The American Cream Legbar Club advocates for APA recognition and connects US breeders.
- **Shows.** Cream Legbars are increasingly shown in exhibition, particularly at shows that accept non-APA breeds. Buying from a show exhibitor is one way to ensure you are getting birds that meet the breed standard.

**Beware of mis-labeled birds.** Because "Legbar" has become a trendy name, some sellers offer birds labeled as Cream Legbars that are actually Easter Eggers [breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger] or mixed-breed blue-egg layers without the auto-sexing trait, the crest, or the correct body type. A true Cream Legbar should auto-sex at hatch, carry a crest, lay blue eggs, and have the distinctive cream-barred plumage. If a bird lacks any of these, it is not a Cream Legbar.
