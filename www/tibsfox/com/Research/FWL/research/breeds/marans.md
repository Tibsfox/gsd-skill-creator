# The Marans --- The Chocolate Egg

Open an egg carton from a serious Marans breeder and you will understand, instantly, why people become obsessed. The eggs are not brown. They are not dark brown. They are *chocolate* --- a deep, reddish-brown that borders on mahogany, that catches the light with an almost lacquered depth, that makes the brown eggs of a Plymouth Rock or Rhode Island Red look anemic by comparison. Hold a Marans egg next to a white Leghorn egg and they look like they belong to different species. Hold one up to the light and it glows, barely, a deep crimson-amber through the thick shell. These eggs are why Marans breeders pay premium prices for breeding stock, why egg color is scored on a numbered scale, and why the breed has gone from near-obscurity in North America to one of the most sought-after backyard and exhibition breeds in less than two decades.

But the Marans is more than an egg. It is a breed with a roughneck port-town origin, a complicated international history involving feathered legs and clean legs and spirited disagreement about which is correct, a James Bond connection that most Bond fans do not know about, and a genetics lesson in how a single shell pigment, applied in varying thickness, can produce the most coveted egg color in the poultry world.

---

## Origin: Marans, France

The Marans takes its name from the town of Marans, a small port on the Sevres Niortaise river in the Charente-Maritime department of western France, roughly 25 kilometers from the Atlantic coast and 30 kilometers from La Rochelle. The town's location is significant: as a port, Marans received ships from across the known world, and those ships carried poultry. The breed's origin story is one of convergence --- local farmyard chickens of the Charente-Maritime region, themselves descendants of centuries of French farm breeding, crossed with fighting fowl and other poultry brought ashore by sailors from Asia, the Middle East, Indonesia, and other maritime trading regions.

The fighting fowl connection is important. Ships of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries commonly carried gamecocks for entertainment during long voyages. When ships made port, roosters were bought, sold, traded, and occasionally escaped to join the local flock. The fighting breeds brought genetic contributions that are visible in the modern Marans: the upright carriage, the athletic body, the alert expression, and the game bird temperament that makes Marans distinctly less docile than breeds like the Orpington or the Plymouth Rock. Marans are not aggressive, but they are *present* --- active, curious, assertive, with a watchful intensity that reflects their gamefowl heritage.

The specific breeds that contributed to the Marans are debated but likely include Croad Langshans (contributing the dark egg color, feathered legs, and body size), Faverolles, Barred Plymouth Rocks (possibly contributing barring genetics for the Cuckoo variety), and various game breeds of Asian origin. The synthesis occurred gradually over the nineteenth century as farmers in the Marans region selected for a bird that laid dark-shelled eggs, carried reasonable body mass, and thrived in the damp, mild maritime climate of western France.

The Marans was first exhibited in France in 1914 at the La Rochelle poultry show and was officially recognized by the French poultry standard in 1931. The breed gained a devoted following among French poultry keepers, particularly in the western and southwestern regions, where the dark eggs commanded premium prices at market --- French consumers have long associated dark-shelled eggs with superior flavor and richness, a preference that has no scientific basis but persists as a cultural tradition that benefits Marans breeders to this day.

## The Chocolate Egg: Protoporphyrin IX

The Marans egg gets its color from a single pigment: **protoporphyrin IX**, a porphyrin molecule that is a byproduct of hemoglobin synthesis. Protoporphyrin IX is the same class of molecule that gives hemoglobin (and blood) its red color, though protoporphyrin itself appears brown when deposited on eggshell.

### Deposition Mechanism

Protoporphyrin IX is secreted by the epithelial cells of the shell gland (uterus) during the final 3 to 5 hours of shell formation, after the calcium carbonate shell has been largely completed. The pigment is deposited on the *surface* of the shell --- it does not penetrate the full shell thickness. This is why a brown egg, if you crack it and look at the interior surface, is white or pale: the pigment is an exterior coating, not an integral component of the shell matrix.

This surface deposition has a practical consequence that is immediately obvious to anyone who has handled a freshly laid Marans egg: when the egg is very fresh (still damp from laying), the pigment can be smudged or rubbed off. Within minutes, the cuticle dries and the color is fixed, but the wet-egg smudge test is a quick confirmation that you are dealing with a genuinely dark egg and not a dyed shell.

### The Color Scale

Marans breeders evaluate egg color using a numerical scale, most commonly the scale developed by the **Marans of America** club (also used with variations by the French Marans Club and other international Marans organizations). The scale runs from 1 to 9:

- **1--3:** Light brown. Unacceptable for serious Marans breeding.
- **4:** Medium brown. The minimum acceptable score for exhibition and breeding programs. Roughly equivalent to a dark Rhode Island Red or Barnevelder egg.
- **5--6:** Dark brown. A solid, respectable Marans egg. This is where most well-bred Marans hens fall.
- **7--8:** Very dark brown. Chocolate. These eggs are the goal of most breeding programs and the standard expected of exhibition-quality birds. The color is deep, rich, and even.
- **9:** Extraordinary dark. Near-black chocolate. Rare and highly prized. Eggs at this level are almost uncommon enough to be museum pieces. Few hens consistently produce 9-level eggs, and those that do are the cornerstones of their breeders' programs.

### The Fade Cycle

A critical aspect of Marans egg color that every breeder must understand: **egg color fades over the course of a laying cycle.** A hen that lays a score-7 egg at the beginning of her laying cycle may be laying score-4 eggs by the end of it. This happens because the hen's protoporphyrin reserves deplete faster than they are replenished. Each egg draws down the pigment supply, and as the supply diminishes, less pigment is deposited on each successive egg.

The color resets after a pause in laying --- a broody period, a molt, or a natural break between laying cycles. After the pause, the first eggs are the darkest, and the fade begins again. This cyclical pattern means that evaluating a Marans hen's egg color from a single egg is unreliable. Serious breeders evaluate color across multiple eggs from different points in the laying cycle, and they judge breeding stock by the average and peak color rather than by any single egg.

### Genetics of Darkness

Egg color in Marans is **polygenic** --- it is not controlled by a single gene with a simple dominant/recessive relationship, but by multiple genes that collectively determine the amount and timing of protoporphyrin deposition. This makes breeding for dark color a matter of sustained selection pressure across many generations rather than a simple genetic switch.

The polygenicity means that crossing two dark-egg birds does not guarantee dark-egg offspring. It increases the probability, but the specific combination of alleles at multiple loci determines each individual hen's color potential. Breeders who achieve consistently dark eggs (score 7+) have typically been selecting for color for many generations, building up the frequency of darkness-promoting alleles across all the relevant loci. Introducing new blood --- even from another Marans line --- can dilute the color if the incoming genetics carry lower-darkness alleles at some of the contributing loci.

## Varieties: The Black Copper and Beyond

The Marans is recognized in multiple varieties, with significant differences in popularity and availability between France, Britain, and America.

**Black Copper (Noir Cuivre):** The most popular variety worldwide and the one most strongly associated with the breed. Males display a stunning combination: iridescent black body and tail with fiery copper-red hackle and saddle feathers. Females are predominantly black with copper lacing on the hackle. The Black Copper variety tends to produce the darkest eggs, which has driven its popularity among egg-color enthusiasts.

**Cuckoo:** A barred pattern (alternating dark and light bars, similar to a Barred Plymouth Rock but typically less sharply defined). Cuckoo Marans were historically the most common variety in France and remain popular. They tend to produce slightly lighter eggs than Black Copper Marans, though good breeders have narrowed this gap.

**Wheaten:** Males show a duckwing-type pattern (red-gold hackle and saddle, black breast), while females are a warm wheaten-buff. The Wheaten variety produces eggs in the medium-dark range.

**Blue Copper:** The blue dilution of Black Copper. Males have a slate-blue body with copper hackle and saddle; females are slate-blue with copper hackle lacing. Breeding challenges follow the standard Blue genetics (25% Black Copper, 50% Blue Copper, 25% Splash Copper from Blue x Blue matings).

**Birchen, White, Columbian, and others** exist in various states of recognition and development across different national standards. The French standard recognizes more varieties than either the British or American standards.

## The James Bond Connection

It is one of the more improbable footnotes in poultry history that James Bond, the fictional British spy created by Ian Fleming, was a Marans enthusiast. In the novel *From Russia with Love* (1957), Fleming wrote that Bond ate only eggs from Marans hens, "from a French farm near Douvres-la-Delivrande" --- and that the dark, rich eggs were his breakfast standard.

This was not a throwaway detail. Fleming himself kept Marans at his estate, Goldeneye, in Jamaica, and was a genuine admirer of the breed's eggs. He apparently believed (in keeping with French tradition) that the dark-shelled eggs were superior in flavor and richness. Whether this is true is debatable --- controlled taste tests have not consistently demonstrated a difference based on shell color alone, though pastured Marans eggs from heritage lines do tend to have the deep-orange yolks and firm whites associated with quality, thanks to the breed's active foraging habits and the small-flock husbandry typical of Marans keepers.

The Bond connection has become a marketing asset. Marans breeders, egg sellers, and poultry supply companies routinely reference Fleming's endorsement. "James Bond's Favorite Egg" appears on more farm stand signs than any literary estate might approve of, but it is a legitimate piece of the breed's cultural history.

## The Feathered Leg Controversy

No discussion of the Marans is complete without addressing the legs. The question of whether Marans should have feathered or clean legs has generated more spirited debate than most questions in the poultry fancy, and the answer depends on which country's standard you follow.

**The French standard** requires feathered legs. In France, where the breed originated, Marans have always had light feathering on the shanks and outer toes. This feathering is modest --- not the heavy boot of a Cochin or Brahma, but a visible scattering of feathers down the outside of the shank and onto the outer toe. The French standard considers clean-legged Marans a fault, and French Marans breeders select for the correct degree of foot feathering as a breed characteristic.

**The British standard** (Poultry Club of Great Britain) has historically accepted clean-legged Marans. British breeders imported Marans from France in the mid-twentieth century and, through either preference or genetic drift, developed lines that lacked foot feathering. The clean-legged British Marans became established as a distinct type, and the British standard was written to reflect these birds.

**The American standard** (APA) recognized the Marans in 2011, accepting Black Copper and Wheaten varieties with feathered shanks --- following the French standard rather than the British. This was a deliberate choice that reflected the breed's origins, but it created a challenge for American breeders who had been working with clean-legged birds imported from Britain or from mixed French/British lines.

The feathered vs. clean debate is partly aesthetic, partly historical, and partly practical. From a practical standpoint, lightly feathered shanks require slightly more management than clean legs (mud accumulation, bumblefoot risk in damp conditions), but the feathering is light enough that it is not a significant husbandry burden. From a historical standpoint, the French origin argues for feathered legs. From a genetic standpoint, the foot-feathering alleles (*Pti-1* and *Pti-2*) are separate from the egg-color genes, so it is entirely possible to breed clean-legged birds with dark eggs or feathered-legged birds with light eggs --- the traits are independent. The ongoing project for American breeders is to achieve both correct leg feathering (per the APA standard) and dark egg color in the same birds, which requires dual selection.

## APA Recognition and American Import History

The Marans arrived in the United States through multiple channels and at various times, but the breed's serious American presence dates to the late 1990s and early 2000s. Prior to that, Marans were essentially unknown in the United States --- the breed was not in the APA Standard, there was no organized breeding community, and the birds that existed in the country were isolated and often of mixed or uncertain quality.

The initial imports that sparked the American Marans movement came primarily from French lines, brought in by dedicated poultry enthusiasts who traveled to France, acquired breeding stock, and navigated the considerable logistical challenges of importing live poultry (quarantine requirements, health certifications, limited shipping options). These early importers --- whose names are well-known in Marans circles but whose efforts were often personal and expensive --- established the foundation flocks from which the American Marans population grew.

The Marans of America club was founded to promote the breed, establish an American color standard for eggs, and work toward APA recognition. That recognition came in 2011, when the APA accepted the Black Copper and Wheaten varieties. The Cuckoo variety was added subsequently. Recognition required demonstrating that the breed could be shown in sufficient numbers, that breeding populations were established and stable, and that the birds met a consistent standard of type.

Since APA recognition, the Marans has exploded in popularity in the United States. The dark egg is the primary driver --- in an era of artisanal food production, farmer's market culture, and social media egg photography, a carton of Marans eggs is a conversation piece and a status symbol. Black Copper Marans breeding stock from dark-egg lines commands premium prices: $50--$200 per dozen hatching eggs is not unusual for eggs from proven score-7+ hens, and breeding pairs or trios from top lines can sell for several hundred dollars.

## Keeping Marans in the PNW

The Marans, having originated in a maritime climate not entirely unlike the Pacific Northwest's, is well-adapted to PNW conditions. The damp, mild winters of western Washington and Oregon are similar in character (if not in detail) to the damp, mild winters of the Charente-Maritime. The breed handles rain, overcast skies, and moderate cold without difficulty.

**Egg color and PNW conditions.** Egg color is influenced by nutrition, stress, and overall health, all of which are affected by management. PNW keepers who want to maintain the darkest possible egg color should ensure that their Marans have access to high-quality layer feed with adequate calcium and phosphorus, supplemented with free-range foraging during the growing season. Stress from predator pressure, flock disruption, or environmental extremes can reduce egg color intensity.

**Temperament in the flock.** Marans are more active and assertive than Orpingtons or Silkies, though less flighty than Leghorns. They are good foragers --- the gamefowl heritage makes them alert, athletic birds that range widely and forage effectively. In a mixed flock, Marans tend to occupy the middle to upper pecking order. They are not typically bullies, but they will not be pushed around. Their alertness to overhead predators is an asset in the PNW's raptor-rich environment.

**The feathered leg in PNW mud.** If keeping feathered-legged Marans (per the APA standard), be mindful of mud accumulation on the shank feathers during the wet season. Provide dry footing in the run (sand, gravel, or rubber mats over mud-prone areas), and inspect feet regularly. The feathering is light enough that serious mud balling is uncommon, but neglected feet in persistently muddy conditions can develop issues.

**Breeding for color.** PNW Marans breeders have access to a growing community of fellow enthusiasts, including active Marans of America members in Washington and Oregon. The Pacific Northwest Poultry Association and regional poultry swaps provide opportunities to source breeding stock and evaluate eggs from different lines. For breeders focused on egg color, establishing relationships with other breeders and comparing eggs against the numbered scale is essential --- color evaluation in isolation, without reference eggs, is unreliable because human color perception adapts to context.

## The Marans and the Egg Carton

There is a practical, almost philosophical pleasure in opening a carton of Marans eggs. In a world where most eggs are white or pale brown, identical, anonymous, and produced by birds that never see sunlight, a Marans egg is a declaration of specificity. It was laid by a specific breed with a specific history, in a specific coop, by a hen whose egg color reflects a genetic heritage that breeders have spent generations refining. The darkness of the shell is not cosmetic --- it is the visible output of a biological process (protoporphyrin synthesis and deposition) that the breeder has selected for with the same care and precision that a rose breeder applies to petal color or a dog breeder applies to coat texture.

For the show breeder, the Marans is a breed that demands attention to two separate but interrelated standards: the bird must meet the physical standard (type, plumage color and pattern, carriage, condition) *and* the egg must meet the color standard. This dual evaluation makes the Marans one of the most intellectually engaging breeds to work with. Every mating decision involves not just the birds' physical appearance but their egg-color genetics --- and because egg color is polygenic, the genetics cannot be reduced to a simple chart. It requires records, observation, and the patience to evaluate results across multiple generations.

The Marans egg, at its best, is among the most beautiful natural objects in agriculture. Deep, dark, warm, glowing with the chemistry of porphyrins laid down in the darkness of the shell gland and revealed in the light of the nest box. It is worth the effort.
