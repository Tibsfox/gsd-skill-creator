# The Egg -- Biology, Color, Production, and Nutrition

> **Research document 05 for the FWL (Fowl) project -- PNW Research Series**
>
> The egg is one of nature's most elegant engineering achievements: a self-contained life support system packaged in a calcium carbonate shell, assembled from raw materials in roughly 25 hours, and capable of sustaining embryonic development for three weeks without any external input beyond heat, humidity, and oxygen exchange. This document traces the egg from its biological origins inside the hen's reproductive tract through the genetics of shell color, the science of incubation, and the nutritional differences between pastured and commercial eggs. It covers the breeds that have been selected over centuries for prolific laying, the breeds prized for the jewel-toned colors of their shells, and the anomalies -- double yolks, fairy eggs, soft shells -- that remind us that biology is a process, not a product.
>
> **Design principle:** Egg science sits at the intersection of reproductive biology, genetics, animal husbandry, and nutrition. This document respects all four disciplines. Data on egg production rates, shell color genetics, and nutritional composition are drawn from university extension services, USDA databases, peer-reviewed poultry science journals, and breed registries with decades of records. Where breed claims are made, they are attributed to specific sources.

## The Reproductive Tract -- Assembly Line of the Egg

A hen's egg does not simply appear. It is manufactured through a precise, sequential process that moves a single ovum through five distinct regions of the oviduct, each contributing a specific structural layer. The entire journey takes approximately 25 to 26 hours, which is why a hen that lays daily will lay slightly later each morning until she skips a day and resets the cycle.

### The Ovary

A hen is born with a single functional ovary (the left; the right ovary regresses during embryonic development) containing thousands of immature ova. At any given time, an active laying hen has a hierarchy of developing follicles visible on the ovary, resembling a cluster of grapes in graduated sizes. The largest follicle -- the one next in line for ovulation -- is a mature yolk, roughly 35-40 millimeters in diameter in a standard large-fowl breed, already loaded with lipids, proteins, vitamins, and minerals. This yolk is the ovum itself: the single cell that, if fertilized, will become a chick.

Ovulation is triggered by a surge of luteinizing hormone (LH), typically occurring 15 to 75 minutes after the previous egg is laid. The follicular wall ruptures along the stigma -- a thin, avascular line on the follicle surface -- releasing the yolk into the body cavity. The stigma's avascularity is critical: rupture along any other line would result in blood spots on the yolk, a cosmetic defect that occurs in roughly 2-4% of eggs from healthy hens.

### The Infundibulum (Funnel)

The infundibulum is a thin, funnel-shaped structure that engulfs the ovulated yolk. The yolk spends only about 15 to 18 minutes here, but this is the only window during which fertilization can occur. Sperm stored in sperm storage tubules (SSTs) at the utero-vaginal junction migrate upward to the infundibulum, where a single spermatozoon penetrates the germinal disc on the yolk surface. Hens can store viable sperm for 10 to 14 days after a single mating, though fertility peaks at 2-5 days post-mating and declines thereafter.

The infundibulum also deposits the first layer of albumen -- a thin, chalaziferous layer that will later twist into the chalazae, the ropy strands that suspend the yolk in the center of the egg.

### The Magnum (Albumen-Secreting Region)

The magnum is the longest section of the oviduct, approximately 33 centimeters in a standard hen, and the egg spends about 3 hours here. The magnum's tubular glands secrete the thick and thin albumen (egg white) in alternating layers. The albumen is approximately 90% water and 10% protein, with ovalbumin comprising 54% of the total protein, followed by ovotransferrin (12%), ovomucoid (11%), and ovomucin (3.5%). Ovomucin gives the thick albumen its gel-like consistency and is responsible for the structural distinction between the thick and thin white visible when a fresh egg is cracked onto a flat surface.

The albumen serves multiple functions: mechanical cushioning for the yolk, antimicrobial defense (ovotransferrin binds iron, starving bacteria; lysozyme lyses bacterial cell walls), and a water and protein reservoir for the developing embryo.

### The Isthmus

The isthmus is a short section (approximately 10 centimeters) where the egg spends about 75 minutes. Here, the inner and outer shell membranes are deposited. These two membranes are composed of keratin-like protein fibers in a meshwork pattern. They are tightly adhered to each other everywhere except at the blunt end of the egg, where they separate after laying to form the air cell. The air cell grows as the egg ages and moisture evaporates through the shell -- a fact exploited in the float test for egg freshness and in candling during incubation.

The isthmus also initiates the formation of mammillary knobs -- the organic nucleation sites on the outer shell membrane where calcification will begin in the next section.

### The Shell Gland (Uterus)

The shell gland is where the egg spends the majority of its formation time -- approximately 20 to 21 hours. This is where calcification occurs: the mammillary knobs serve as nucleation sites for the deposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) in the calcite crystal form. A single eggshell contains approximately 2 grams of calcium, representing about 40% of the total calcium in the hen's body. To meet this extraordinary demand, laying hens mobilize calcium from medullary bone -- a specialized, labile bone tissue found in the marrow cavities of long bones that serves as a calcium reservoir specifically for shell formation. Medullary bone is deposited during the hours when the hen is eating (daytime) and mobilized during the overnight hours when shell calcification occurs and the hen is not consuming feed.

The shell itself is not a simple calcium coating. It has a complex architecture:

- **Mammillary layer:** The innermost layer, anchored to the outer shell membrane. Columnar calcite crystals radiate outward from the mammillary knobs.
- **Palisade layer:** The thickest layer, composed of densely packed columnar calcite crystals. This layer provides the shell's mechanical strength.
- **Vertical crystal layer:** A thin transitional zone.
- **Cuticle (bloom):** The outermost layer, a thin protein coating deposited in the final 1-2 hours before lay. The cuticle seals the shell pores, reducing moisture loss and bacterial penetration. It also carries the pigments that determine shell color.

The shell contains 7,000 to 17,000 pores (averaging about 10,000 in a chicken egg), which allow gas exchange -- oxygen in, carbon dioxide and water vapor out -- during incubation. Pore density is highest at the blunt end of the egg, where the air cell forms.

### The Vagina

The egg passes through the vagina in a matter of minutes. The vagina's primary contributions are the deposition of the final cuticle layer and the rotation of the egg so that it is laid blunt end first (in most cases). The utero-vaginal junction, located at the boundary between the shell gland and vagina, contains the sperm storage tubules where spermatozoa are maintained in a quiescent state between matings.

## Shell Color -- The Genetics of Pigment

The color of an eggshell is determined by genetics, deposited as pigment during the final 3-5 hours of shell formation in the shell gland. Shell color has no effect on the egg's nutritional content, flavor, or hatchability -- but it is one of the most visible and beloved traits in the poultry world. Breeders have selected for shell color for centuries, and the range of colors produced by domestic chickens is remarkable: pure white, cream, tinted, light brown, dark brown, chocolate, blue, green, olive, sage, plum, and speckled.

### The Chemistry of Shell Pigments

Only two pigments are responsible for the full spectrum of eggshell colors:

**Protoporphyrin IX** is a porphyrin pigment -- the same class of molecule that gives hemoglobin its red color, though protoporphyrin itself is brown. It is deposited on the shell surface during the final hours of shell formation and does not penetrate the full thickness of the shell. If you crack a brown egg and examine the interior surface of the shell, it is white or slightly tinted -- the brown is only on the outside. Protoporphyrin is responsible for all brown, tan, terracotta, chocolate, and pinkish-brown shell colors. The intensity of brown coloration depends on the amount of protoporphyrin deposited, which is under polygenic control (multiple genes contribute to the trait).

**Biliverdin (oocyanin)** is a bile pigment, a breakdown product of hemoglobin. Unlike protoporphyrin, biliverdin is deposited throughout the full thickness of the shell -- a blue egg is blue all the way through, inside and out. Biliverdin is responsible for all blue shell coloration. The gene controlling biliverdin deposition is an autosomal dominant allele located on chromosome 1, associated with a retroviral insertion in the SLCO1B3 gene (solute carrier organic anion transporter family member 1B3). This insertion upregulates biliverdin transport into the shell gland. The discovery of this genetic mechanism was reported by Wang et al. (2013) in research on Chinese Dongxiang and Lushi chickens, with the same insertion independently confirmed in South American Araucana chickens -- evidence of convergent evolution or a shared ancestral mutation.

### Color Combinations

The full palette of eggshell colors emerges from combinations and intensities of these two pigments:

- **White:** No pigment. Breeds include White Leghorn, Ancona, Minorca, Hamburg, Polish, Andalusian (some strains).
- **Cream/Tint:** Very light protoporphyrin deposition. Breeds include Dorking, Faverolle (some strains), Light Sussex (some strains).
- **Light brown:** Moderate protoporphyrin. Breeds include Rhode Island Red, New Hampshire, Plymouth Rock.
- **Dark brown:** Heavy protoporphyrin. Breeds include Barnevelder, Penedesenca.
- **Chocolate/Dark chocolate:** Very heavy protoporphyrin. The Marans is the breed most associated with this color, particularly the Black Copper Marans and Cuckoo Marans. Marans eggs are graded on a 1-9 color scale developed by the Marans of America club, with exhibition-quality eggs expected to score 4 or above. The darkest Marans eggs (scores 7-9) are extraordinary -- a deep, reddish-chocolate brown that photographs almost black. The color fades somewhat over the course of a laying cycle as the hen's pigment reserves deplete, then resets after a pause or molt.
- **Blue:** Biliverdin throughout the shell. Breeds include Araucana, Ameraucana, Cream Legbar.
- **Green/Olive:** Blue base (biliverdin) + brown overlay (protoporphyrin). The result is a green, olive, or sage-colored egg. The Olive Egger is a deliberate cross -- typically a blue-egg breed (Ameraucana) crossed with a dark-brown-egg breed (Marans) -- designed to produce deep olive eggs. First-generation (F1) Olive Eggers produce the darkest olives; subsequent generations require careful selection to maintain color intensity.
- **Speckled:** Protoporphyrin deposited unevenly during shell rotation in the shell gland, producing spots, speckles, or blooms over a base color. Welsummers are the classic speckled-egg breed, laying terracotta eggs with dark brown speckles that can sometimes be wiped off with a damp cloth (because the speckles are on the cuticle surface).

### The Easter Egger

The Easter Egger deserves special mention because it is not a recognized breed but a category: any chicken that carries the blue-egg gene but does not conform to any breed standard. Easter Eggers are beloved in backyard flocks because each hen may lay a different color -- blue, green, olive, pink, cream, or tan -- depending on her particular combination of blue-egg genetics and brown-egg genetics. They are the wild cards of the egg basket, and their color unpredictability is a feature, not a bug.

## Top Egg-Laying Breeds

Egg production rates vary enormously among breeds, from heritage breeds that lay 100-150 eggs per year to commercial hybrids that exceed 300. The following breeds represent the most prolific layers in the domestic chicken world, with production figures drawn from university extension data, breed registries, and commercial production records.

### White Leghorn

The White Leghorn is the foundation of the global commercial egg industry. A Mediterranean breed originating in Italy (the port city of Livorno, anglicized to "Leghorn"), the White Leghorn has been selected for egg production for over a century. Modern commercial Leghorn strains (such as the Hy-Line W-36 and Lohmann LSL-Classic) lay 280 to 320 eggs per year, with some flocks averaging over 330 eggs per hen housed in the first laying cycle. Feed conversion efficiency is exceptional: approximately 2.0-2.1 kg of feed per dozen eggs. Eggs are large, white-shelled, and uniform. The White Leghorn is a small, active, flighty bird -- typically 4-5 pounds for hens -- that channels its metabolic energy into egg production rather than body mass.

### ISA Brown

The ISA Brown (Institut de Selection Animale) is a commercial hybrid developed in France in 1978 by crossing Rhode Island Red males with Rhode Island White females (though the exact proprietary cross may involve additional genetics). ISA Browns are the world's most widely used brown-egg layer, with production rates of 300 to 320 eggs per year in commercial settings. They are docile, robust, and tolerant of confinement, making them equally popular in backyard flocks. ISA Browns begin laying at 18-20 weeks and maintain high production through 72-80 weeks before a significant decline.

### Rhode Island Red

Developed in Rhode Island and Massachusetts in the late 19th century from crosses of Malay, Shanghai (Cochin), Java, and Brown Leghorn stock, the Rhode Island Red is one of the great dual-purpose American breeds. Heritage-type Rhode Island Reds lay 250 to 300 brown eggs per year, while production-bred strains approach the output of commercial hybrids. The breed is hardy, assertive, and an excellent forager -- traits that make it a backbone of small-farm and backyard flocks. The deep mahogany plumage of exhibition Rhode Island Reds is one of the most iconic images in American poultry.

### Australorp

The Australorp holds the world record for egg production by a single hen: 364 eggs in 365 days, set in 1922-1923 during an official egg-laying contest in Australia. The breed was developed in Australia from Black Orpington stock imported from England, selected specifically for egg production while retaining the Orpington's gentle temperament and dual-purpose utility. Modern Australorps lay 250 to 300 light-brown eggs per year. They are black-feathered with a beetle-green sheen, medium-sized (6.5-8.5 pounds for hens), cold-hardy, and exceptionally calm.

### Sussex

The Sussex is one of England's oldest breeds, documented since the time of the Roman invasion of Britain. The Light Sussex -- white with a black-laced hackle and tail -- is the most common variety and an excellent layer of 250 to 280 cream to light-brown eggs per year. Sussex hens are curious, friendly, and excellent foragers. The breed has contributed genetics to numerous commercial hybrids.

### Plymouth Rock (Barred Rock)

The Barred Plymouth Rock, developed in Massachusetts in the 1860s, is America's quintessential dual-purpose breed. The barred plumage pattern -- alternating black and white bars on each feather -- is sex-linked, allowing experienced breeders to sex chicks at hatch (males have a larger, more irregular white head spot). Barred Rocks lay 200 to 280 brown eggs per year, are cold-hardy, docile, and long-lived. They were one of the foundation breeds of the American poultry industry before the shift to specialized commercial hybrids in the mid-20th century.

## Colored-Egg Breeds

For many poultry keepers, the color of the egg is the point. The following breeds are prized specifically for the color, intensity, and beauty of their shells.

### Araucana

The Araucana originates from Chile, where Indigenous Mapuche people kept blue-egg-laying chickens for centuries before European contact. The breed carries two distinctive traits: tufted ears (tufts of feathers growing from a fleshy peduncle near the ear) and rumplessness (absence of tail vertebrae and tail feathers). Both traits are lethal when homozygous -- chicks inheriting two copies of the tufting gene die in the shell. This genetic burden makes Araucana breeding challenging; hatchability rates are significantly lower than in most breeds. Araucana eggs are a vivid sky blue.

### Ameraucana

The Ameraucana was developed in the United States in the 1970s from Araucana stock, selected to retain the blue-egg gene while eliminating the lethal tufting allele. Ameraucanas have muffs and beards (not tufts), full tails, and pea combs. They are recognized by the APA in eight color varieties: Black, Blue, Blue Wheaten, Brown Red, Buff, Silver, Wheaten, and White. Eggs are blue -- the shade varies from pale powder blue to a medium robin's-egg blue depending on the strain.

### Cream Legbar

The Cream Legbar was developed in Britain in the 1930s by Reginald Punnett and Michael Pease at Cambridge University, crossing Brown Leghorns, Barred Plymouth Rocks, and Araucanas. The Cream Legbar is an autosexing breed: male and female chicks can be distinguished at hatch by down color (males have a pale head spot; females have darker, more defined stripes). This autosexing trait is genetically fixed and does not rely on feather-sexing accuracy. Cream Legbars lay blue to blue-green eggs, are active foragers, and have a distinctive upright carriage with a small crest of feathers on the head.

### Marans

The Marans (always spelled with the final "s," as it is named for the town of Marans in western France) is celebrated for its dark chocolate-brown eggs. The Black Copper Marans is the most sought-after variety for egg color, with the best hens producing eggs scoring 6-9 on the Marans color scale. French Marans have feathered shanks; English Marans (more common in the UK) have clean legs. The breed was developed in the marshy areas around Marans, where local barnyard chickens were crossed with game birds brought by Indonesian sailors and later with Croad Langshans and Faverolles. The dark egg color was traditionally valued in French markets as a sign of quality.

### Welsummer

Named for the village of Welsum in the Netherlands, the Welsummer lays large, terracotta-brown eggs with dark brown speckles. The speckled pattern is deposited in the cuticle layer and varies between individual hens -- some produce heavily speckled eggs, others lay lightly spotted ones, and a few lay eggs where the speckles can be gently rubbed off. Welsummers are partridge-patterned, active, excellent foragers, and tolerant of cold weather. The iconic rooster on Kellogg's Corn Flakes packaging is often said to be a Welsummer (though it more closely resembles a generic gamecock).

### Olive Egger

The Olive Egger is not a breed but a cross, typically a blue-egg breed mated to a dark-brown-egg breed. The most common cross is Ameraucana x Marans, producing F1 hens that lay olive to dark olive eggs. The shade of olive depends on the intensity of both the blue base and the brown overlay: a Cream Legbar x Black Copper Marans cross tends to produce darker olives than an Easter Egger x Welsummer cross. Because Olive Eggers are hybrids, they do not breed true -- F2 offspring will segregate for egg color, producing some blue, some brown, some olive, and some intermediate shades.

## Egg Anatomy -- Structure and Function

A freshly laid egg is a marvel of biological engineering. Each component serves a specific purpose, whether the egg is destined for the breakfast table or the incubator.

### Shell

The shell comprises approximately 11% of the egg's total weight (about 5-6 grams in a 60-gram egg). It is 94-97% calcium carbonate (calcite), with small amounts of magnesium carbonate, calcium phosphate, and an organic matrix of proteins and glycoproteins. The shell's strength is remarkable: a hen's egg can withstand 25-40 pounds of force applied evenly along its long axis -- a consequence of the arch geometry that distributes compressive force around the shell's curvature.

### Shell Membranes

Two membranes line the interior of the shell: the outer shell membrane (adhered to the shell) and the inner shell membrane (adhered to the albumen). At the blunt end, these membranes separate after laying to form the air cell, which grows from approximately 3 mm in depth at lay to 9-10 mm or more as the egg ages. Air cell depth is the basis of the USDA egg grading system: AA grade eggs have air cells no deeper than 3.2 mm (1/8 inch); A grade allows up to 4.8 mm (3/16 inch); B grade allows larger air cells.

### Albumen (Egg White)

The albumen has four distinct layers, visible when a fresh egg is carefully opened onto a flat surface:

1. **Chalaziferous layer:** The innermost layer, surrounding the yolk and twisted into the chalazae.
2. **Inner thin white:** A layer of watery albumen between the chalaziferous layer and the thick white.
3. **Thick white (albumen gel):** The dense, viscous layer that stands up around the yolk in a fresh egg. High in ovomucin, which gives it structure.
4. **Outer thin white:** The watery layer adjacent to the shell membranes.

As an egg ages, the thick white breaks down (ovomucin fibers degrade) and becomes watery, causing the white to spread thinly when the egg is cracked. This is the basis of the Haugh unit measurement for egg quality: a fresh egg with a tall, firm thick white scores high; an older egg with a flat, runny white scores low.

### Chalazae

The chalazae are twisted, rope-like strands of protein that anchor the yolk in the center of the egg. They are formed from the chalaziferous layer of albumen as the egg rotates during its passage through the oviduct. More prominent chalazae indicate a fresher egg. The chalazae are entirely edible and are composed of the same proteins as the albumen.

### Vitelline Membrane

The vitelline membrane is a thin, transparent membrane surrounding the yolk. In a fresh egg, it is strong and elastic -- when you crack an egg and the yolk holds its shape in a tall dome, the vitelline membrane is intact and healthy. As the egg ages, the vitelline membrane weakens (due to osmotic water transfer from the albumen to the yolk), and the yolk flattens and is more prone to breaking. The vitelline membrane is the primary barrier preventing bacterial penetration of the yolk.

### Yolk

The yolk comprises about 33% of the egg's total weight and contains nearly all of its fat and a significant portion of its protein. The yolk is not homogeneous -- it is composed of alternating layers of light and dark yolk (visible if a hard-boiled yolk is cut carefully), deposited during the day (lighter, when the hen is eating) and at night (darker). The germinal disc (blastoderm if fertilized, blastodisc if not) sits on the yolk surface, appearing as a small white spot approximately 3-4 mm in diameter. In a fertilized egg, the germinal disc shows a characteristic bulls-eye pattern with concentric rings.

Yolk color ranges from pale yellow to deep orange and is determined entirely by the hen's diet -- specifically, the carotenoid pigments (xanthophylls, carotenes) consumed in feed. Hens on pasture with access to green plants, insects, and flowers produce deeply pigmented yolks. Commercial hens fed corn-based diets produce medium-yellow yolks. Hens fed wheat-based diets (common in Europe) produce pale yolks. Some commercial producers add marigold extract or synthetic carotenoids to feed to darken yolk color.

## Nutritional Science -- Pastured vs. Commercial Eggs

The nutritional composition of an egg depends significantly on how the hen was raised and what she ate. A growing body of research has documented meaningful differences between eggs from pastured hens and those from conventional commercial operations.

### Baseline Nutrition

A large egg (50 grams edible portion) provides approximately:

- 72 calories
- 6.3 grams protein (complete protein with all essential amino acids)
- 4.8 grams fat (1.6 g saturated, 1.8 g monounsaturated, 0.7 g polyunsaturated)
- 186 mg cholesterol
- 0.6 mg iron
- 28 mcg selenium (51% DV)
- 0.45 mg riboflavin (B2, 35% DV)
- 0.7 mcg vitamin B12 (29% DV)
- 80 mg choline (15% DV)

Eggs are one of the few food sources of naturally occurring vitamin D, and one of the richest dietary sources of choline, a nutrient critical for brain development and liver function.

### The Pastured Egg Advantage

A landmark 2007 study by Mother Earth News tested eggs from 14 pastured flocks across the United States and compared them to USDA nutrient data for conventional eggs. The pastured eggs contained, on average:

- 1/3 less cholesterol (estimated 227 mg vs. 423 mg in USDA data at the time, though USDA values have since been revised downward)
- 1/4 less saturated fat
- 2/3 more vitamin A
- 2 times more omega-3 fatty acids
- 3 times more vitamin E
- 7 times more beta-carotene

A 2010 study by Karsten et al. at Penn State University confirmed that eggs from hens on pasture had significantly higher levels of vitamins A and E and higher omega-3 fatty acid content compared to eggs from caged hens. The key driver is diet: pastured hens consume insects, worms, green plants, seeds, and small invertebrates -- a diverse, carotenoid-rich, omega-3-rich diet that is reflected directly in egg composition.

### Vitamin D and Sunlight

Vitamin D content in eggs is directly related to the hen's exposure to sunlight. Hens synthesize vitamin D3 in the skin (specifically in the uropygial gland secretion spread on feathers) when exposed to ultraviolet B radiation, just as humans do. A 2014 study in the British Poultry Science journal found that eggs from free-range hens with outdoor access contained 3-4 times more vitamin D3 than eggs from caged hens. In the Pacific Northwest, where winter daylight hours are limited and cloud cover is persistent, this is a meaningful consideration: supplemental vitamin D in the feed becomes important for both hen health and egg nutrition during the dark months from November through February.

### Omega-3 Enrichment

Commercial "omega-3 enriched" eggs are produced by adding flaxseed, fish oil, or marine algae to the hen's feed. These eggs typically contain 100-600 mg of omega-3 fatty acids per egg (compared to 30-40 mg in conventional eggs). Pastured eggs fall in the middle range -- typically 60-150 mg of omega-3s per egg -- with the variation depending on the quality of the pasture and the season.

## Incubation -- From Cell to Chick in 21 Days

The incubation of a chicken egg is a 21-day process of astonishing complexity. A single fertilized cell on the yolk surface divides, differentiates, and organizes itself into a fully formed chick capable of breathing, walking, and feeding within hours of hatch.

### Requirements

**Temperature:** The critical variable. Forced-air incubators (with a fan to circulate air) should maintain 99.5 degrees Fahrenheit (37.5 degrees C) measured at egg level. Still-air incubators (without a fan) require a slightly higher reading of 101-102 degrees F (38.3-38.9 degrees C) at the top of the eggs, because heat stratifies in still air and the thermometer reads the warmest air above the eggs. A deviation of just 1 degree F sustained over several days can significantly reduce hatch rates or cause developmental abnormalities.

**Humidity:** Relative humidity should be maintained at 45-55% during days 1-18 (the incubation period) and raised to 65-75% during days 19-21 (the hatching period, or "lockdown"). Humidity controls the rate of moisture loss through the shell pores. Too little humidity causes excessive moisture loss, resulting in chicks that are too small and may become stuck in the shell ("shrink-wrapped"). Too much humidity causes insufficient moisture loss, resulting in oversized chicks and edematous (waterlogged) tissues.

**Turning:** Eggs must be turned a minimum of 3 times per day (odd numbers are preferred so the egg does not rest on the same side two nights in a row) during days 1-18. Turning prevents the embryo from adhering to the shell membranes and ensures even nutrient distribution from the yolk. Commercial incubators turn eggs automatically once per hour. A broody hen turns her eggs instinctively, tucking eggs from the edges toward the center and rotating them with her beak.

**Ventilation:** The developing embryo requires oxygen and produces carbon dioxide. Incubators must provide adequate air exchange without creating drafts that disrupt temperature and humidity.

### The 21-Day Timeline

**Days 1-3:** The blastodisc (germinal disc) develops into a blastoderm. Blood islands form. The heart begins beating at approximately 30 hours of incubation -- one of the earliest functional organs in any vertebrate embryo. The vitelline circulatory system extends across the yolk surface, absorbing nutrients.

**Days 4-7:** The allantois (a membrane that will handle respiration and waste storage) begins to grow. Limb buds appear. The eye develops pigment and is visible as a dark spot during candling. By day 7, the embryo has a recognizable avian form with beak, wings, and legs.

**Days 8-14:** Feather follicles develop. The beak hardens. The egg tooth -- a small, calcified projection on the tip of the upper beak used to break through the shell at hatch -- forms. The embryo begins to fill the egg, and its outline becomes clearly visible during candling. The allantois fuses with the chorion to form the chorioallantoic membrane (CAM), which lines the inner shell membrane and functions as the embryo's lung, exchanging gases through the shell pores.

**Days 15-18:** Rapid growth. The embryo absorbs the remaining albumen. By day 18, turning should stop ("lockdown") because the embryo is positioning itself for hatch -- orienting head-toward-blunt-end, with the beak near the air cell.

**Day 19:** The embryo pierces the inner shell membrane with its beak and takes its first breath of air from the air cell. This is called "internal pip." The chick's lungs begin to function, and the chorioallantoic membrane starts to dry and detach from the shell.

**Day 20:** The embryo absorbs the remaining yolk sac through the navel. This yolk reserve provides 48-72 hours of nutrition after hatch, which is why day-old chicks can be shipped without food or water. The chick uses its egg tooth to crack the shell from inside -- this is "external pip," visible as a small crack or raised bump on the shell surface.

**Day 21:** The chick "zips" -- rotates inside the shell while chipping a line around the circumference, using the egg tooth and a specialized hatching muscle (the complexus or "pipping muscle") on the back of the neck. Once the zip is complete, the chick pushes the cap off the blunt end of the shell and emerges wet and exhausted. Within hours, the down dries and fluffs, the chick stands and begins to explore, and the pipping muscle atrophies. The egg tooth falls off within 1-3 days.

### Candling

Candling -- holding a bright light against the shell in a dark room -- allows the incubator operator to monitor development without opening the egg. Key checkpoints:

- **Day 7:** A viable embryo appears as a dark spot (the eye) with a web of blood vessels radiating outward (the "spider" stage). Infertile eggs ("clears") show no development and appear uniformly translucent. Early-dead embryos show a blood ring -- a red circle where blood has pooled after the embryo's death.
- **Day 14:** The embryo fills roughly half the egg. The air cell is clearly defined at the blunt end. Movement may be visible.
- **Day 18 (lockdown):** The embryo fills most of the egg, appearing nearly opaque. The air cell should occupy approximately 1/3 of the egg's volume. Eggs with undersized air cells have lost insufficient moisture (humidity was too high).

## Egg Anomalies

Biology is a process, and sometimes the process produces surprising results. Egg anomalies are common, usually harmless, and occasionally alarming to new poultry keepers.

### Double Yolks

A double-yolk egg occurs when two ova are released from the ovary in rapid succession and are encased in the same shell. Double yolks are most common in young pullets whose reproductive systems are still calibrating, and in certain breeds and genetic lines (some strains of ISA Brown and similar hybrids produce double-yolkers at rates of 5-10% during early lay). The eggs are oversized -- often jumbo or super-jumbo -- and can cause laying difficulties in small hens. While theoretically both yolks in a double-yolk egg could be fertilized and develop, twin chicks almost never survive to hatch because the egg does not contain enough space or resources for two embryos.

### Fairy Eggs (Wind Eggs, Fart Eggs)

A fairy egg is a tiny, yolkless egg, typically the size of a marble or grape, containing only albumen and a shell. Fairy eggs occur when a small piece of tissue or albumen enters the oviduct and triggers shell formation without a yolk being released. They are common in pullets just beginning to lay and in older hens winding down production. They are harmless and charming -- many keepers save them as curiosities.

### Soft-Shell and Shell-less Eggs

A soft-shell egg has a thin, pliable shell that feels leathery rather than rigid. A shell-less egg has only the shell membranes with no calcification at all -- it feels like a water balloon. Both conditions occur when the egg passes through the shell gland too quickly for full calcification, or when the hen's calcium metabolism is disrupted. Common causes include calcium deficiency (insufficient oyster shell or layer feed), stress (a startling event can cause premature oviposition), infectious bronchitis virus (which damages the shell gland permanently), and age (older hens produce thinner shells). Occasional soft-shell eggs are normal; frequent ones warrant investigation of diet and health.

### Lash Eggs (Salpingitis)

A "lash egg" is not a true egg at all -- it is a mass of caseous (cheese-like) exudate produced by a hen with salpingitis, an infection and inflammation of the oviduct. Lash eggs may contain layers of coagulated pus, tissue, and sometimes fragments of shell or yolk material. They can be alarming in appearance. Salpingitis is caused by bacterial infection (commonly E. coli, Mycoplasma, or Pasteurella), often ascending from the cloaca. Mild cases may resolve with antibiotic treatment; severe or chronic salpingitis can be fatal. A hen that produces a lash egg should be monitored closely and may benefit from veterinary assessment.

### Blood Spots and Meat Spots

Blood spots (small red spots on the yolk) occur when a blood vessel ruptures during ovulation. Meat spots (brown or tan spots in the albumen) are fragments of tissue shed from the oviduct wall. Both are harmless and do not indicate fertilization. Commercial eggs are candled and any eggs with visible spots are removed from the Grade A supply; backyard eggs are not candled, so spots are encountered more frequently.

### Body-Checked Eggs

A body-checked egg has a visible ridge or band around its circumference, caused by a crack that occurred in the shell gland and was subsequently repaired with additional calcium deposition before lay. The repair creates a raised line or rough texture. Body-checked eggs are structurally sound and perfectly edible.

## Historical Egg Breeds vs. Modern Hybrids

The history of egg production is a story of genetic specialization. Before the mid-20th century, most chickens were dual-purpose -- kept for both eggs and meat -- and most farms maintained small flocks of heritage breeds. The shift to industrial egg production changed everything.

### The Heritage Foundation

In the early 1900s, egg-laying contests held at agricultural experiment stations across the United States and Australia drove the selection of high-producing strains within established breeds. The Storrs, Connecticut egg-laying contest (1911-1958) and the Hawkesbury, Australia contest were among the most influential. It was in one of these contests that the Australorp set its world record. White Leghorns, Rhode Island Reds, Barred Plymouth Rocks, and White Wyandottes were the dominant competition breeds. Average production in these contests climbed from about 150 eggs per year in 1911 to over 300 eggs per year by the 1950s -- a testament to the power of selective breeding within established breeds.

### The Hybrid Revolution

In the 1950s and 1960s, the poultry industry adopted the hybrid breeding model used in corn agriculture: maintaining inbred parent lines and crossing them to produce F1 hybrid offspring with superior performance (heterosis, or hybrid vigor). This model -- controlled by a small number of multinational breeding companies (Hy-Line, Lohmann, Hendrix Genetics/ISA) -- replaced heritage breeds with proprietary hybrids in commercial production. Today, approximately 95% of the world's commercial laying hens are derived from genetics controlled by just two companies: Hendrix Genetics (owner of ISA, Bovans, Babcock, Shaver, and Dekalb brands) and EW Group (owner of Hy-Line and Lohmann).

The consequences for breed diversity were severe. Heritage breeds that had been the backbone of American agriculture -- the Java, the Delaware, the Buckeye, the Dominique -- declined precipitously as small farms gave way to industrial operations. By the 1970s, many heritage breeds were approaching extinction. The countermovement that saved them -- the heritage breed conservation effort led by the American Livestock Breeds Conservancy (now The Livestock Conservancy) and the Society for the Preservation of Poultry Antiquities (SPPA) -- is documented in detail in the Heritage Breeds and Conservation module of this project.

### Living Heritage

Every egg from a heritage breed carries genetic information shaped by centuries of selection in specific climates, on specific feeds, under specific management systems. A Chantecler egg comes from a bird developed by Brother Wilfrid Chatelain in Quebec to withstand Canadian winters. A Buckeye egg comes from a bird created by Nettie Metcalf of Ohio -- the only American breed developed entirely by a woman. A Delaware egg comes from a bird that was, for a brief period in the 1940s, the dominant broiler breed on the Delmarva Peninsula before the Cornish Cross replaced it. These are not just eggs. They are artifacts of agricultural history, genetic repositories, and connections to the people and places that shaped them.
