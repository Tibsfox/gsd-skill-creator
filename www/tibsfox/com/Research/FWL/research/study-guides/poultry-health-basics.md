# Study Guide: Chicken Health 101

*The diseases, conditions, and emergencies every flock keeper must recognize --- what they look like, what to do, and when to call a vet*

---

## Why This Matters

A chicken that is sick this morning may be dead this evening. Birds are prey animals, and evolution has wired them to conceal illness for as long as possible --- a visibly sick bird in the wild is the one the hawk targets. By the time a chicken looks obviously unwell to a casual observer, it has often been sick for days. This means that the earlier you can recognize subtle signs of illness, the better the outcome. And some conditions (egg binding, prolapsed vent, predator injury) are genuine emergencies where the difference between acting now and acting tomorrow is the difference between a live bird and a dead one.

This study guide covers the diseases and conditions that every backyard flock keeper should be able to recognize. For each one, you will learn what it looks like, what causes it, what to do immediately, and when to call a veterinarian. This is not a substitute for professional veterinary care --- it is a triage guide that helps you make the right call at 7 AM on a Saturday when the vet clinic does not open until Monday.

## Part 1: Viral Diseases

### Marek's Disease

**What it is:** A herpesvirus (Gallid alphaherpesvirus 2) that causes tumors, paralysis, and immunosuppression in chickens. Marek's is ubiquitous --- the virus is present in virtually every environment where chickens are or have been kept. It spreads through feather dander and dust, remains infectious in the environment for months, and cannot be eliminated from a contaminated coop.

**What it looks like:**
- Paralysis of one or both legs, often with one leg extended forward and one backward (the "splits" posture). This is the classic presentation in young birds (typically 12-24 weeks of age).
- Gray or irregularly shaped iris (ocular Marek's), sometimes in only one eye.
- Skin tumors (raised, firm lumps under the skin or on feather follicles).
- Weight loss, listlessness, and depression without other obvious symptoms.
- Sudden death in apparently healthy young birds.

**What to do:**
- **There is no treatment.** Once a bird shows clinical signs of Marek's disease, the virus has already caused irreversible damage (nerve infiltration, tumor growth).
- **Prevention is the only strategy:** Vaccinate chicks at the hatchery on the day of hatch. The Marek's vaccine (HVT serotype 3, or the more protective bivalent and trivalent formulations) must be given on Day 1 --- it requires 1-2 weeks to develop immunity, and exposure before immunity develops renders the vaccine ineffective. You cannot vaccinate an adult bird and expect protection.
- **If you hatch your own chicks:** You can purchase Marek's vaccine in ampules (Merial HVT+SB-1, typically sold in 1,000-dose ampules, $20-30). The vaccine must be reconstituted and used within 2 hours. For small-batch hatchers, coordinating with other local hatchers to share an ampule is practical.
- **If a bird in your flock develops Marek's:** Isolate the affected bird. The virus is already in your environment --- all exposed birds have been exposed. Birds that do not develop clinical disease have either been vaccinated or have natural genetic resistance. Cull or euthanize affected birds humanely; they will not recover and may suffer.

**College connection:** Science (virology, immunology); Nature Studies (disease ecology).

### Avian Influenza

**What it is:** Influenza A viruses of various subtypes that infect poultry. Low-pathogenicity avian influenza (LPAI) causes mild respiratory disease. Highly pathogenic avian influenza (HPAI, typically H5N1 subtypes) causes rapid, catastrophic mortality --- 90-100% flock death within days. HPAI outbreaks have occurred in the PNW (Washington and Oregon) in 2022-2024, transmitted primarily by migratory waterfowl.

**What it looks like:**
- Sudden, unexplained deaths (multiple birds dead with no warning signs).
- Severe respiratory distress (gasping, coughing, nasal discharge).
- Swollen head, comb, and wattles with cyanosis (purple discoloration).
- Dramatic drop in egg production.
- Diarrhea (green or white).
- Neurological signs (twisting neck, circling, inability to stand).

**What to do:**
- **If multiple birds die suddenly with severe symptoms: contact your state veterinarian immediately.** In Washington: WSDA (360) 902-1878. In Oregon: ODA (503) 986-4680. HPAI is a reportable disease. Do not move birds, eggs, equipment, or bedding off the property until a veterinarian has assessed the situation.
- **Biosecurity is the primary defense:** Keep backyard flocks away from wild waterfowl. Do not let ducks, geese, or shorebirds share water sources with your chickens. Clean footwear before entering the coop if you have visited other poultry facilities or areas with wild bird concentrations. Cover outdoor runs with netting to prevent wild bird entry.

**College connection:** Science (epidemiology, virology); Nature Studies (migratory bird ecology, disease transmission).

## Part 2: Parasitic Diseases

### Coccidiosis

**What it is:** An intestinal disease caused by protozoan parasites of the genus *Eimeria*. Multiple species of *Eimeria* infect chickens, each targeting a specific region of the intestinal tract. Coccidiosis is the single most common cause of death in young chicks (typically 3-8 weeks of age). Adult birds develop immunity through gradual exposure, but chicks encountering high oocyst loads in contaminated bedding can be overwhelmed before their immune systems respond.

**What it looks like:**
- **Bloody stool.** This is the hallmark sign. Droppings may be dark red, bright red, or contain visible blood streaks. In severe infections, the droppings may be almost entirely blood and mucus.
- Lethargy, huddled posture, ruffled feathers.
- Decreased appetite and water consumption.
- Weight loss or failure to grow in young birds.
- Sudden death in chicks, sometimes without obvious bloody stool (the bird dies before the intestinal hemorrhage becomes visible externally).

**What to do:**
- **Treat immediately with Corid (amprolium).** Corid is available at most farm supply stores (Tractor Supply, Coastal Farm & Ranch, Wilco) without a prescription. Dosage: 2 teaspoons of Corid 9.6% liquid per gallon of drinking water, or 1.5 teaspoons of Corid 20% powder per gallon. Provide as the ONLY water source for 5-7 days. Amprolium works by blocking thiamine uptake in the parasite, starving it without harming the chick at proper doses.
- **Clean the brooder or coop.** Remove and replace all bedding. Coccidiosis oocysts are shed in droppings and sporulate (become infectious) in warm, moist bedding. Dry conditions and fresh bedding reduce reinfection pressure.
- **Prevention:** Medicated chick starter feed contains amprolium at a low, preventive dose. If your chicks were vaccinated for coccidiosis at the hatchery, do NOT use medicated feed --- the amprolium interferes with the vaccine. If they were not vaccinated, medicated starter for the first 8 weeks provides protection during the vulnerable period.

**College connection:** Science (parasitology, pharmacology); Home Economics (livestock management).

### External Parasites: Mites and Lice

**Poultry mites** (Northern fowl mite, *Ornithonyssus sylviarum*; red mite, *Dermanyssus gallinae*) and **poultry lice** (several species of Menacanthus and Menopon) are among the most common parasites of backyard flocks. They cause irritation, feather damage, anemia, reduced egg production, and in severe infestations, death.

**Northern fowl mite --- what it looks like:**
- Mites live on the bird full-time, concentrated around the vent (the area beneath the tail). Part the feathers around the vent and look for tiny (pinhead-sized), dark-colored, fast-moving specks. In heavy infestations, the vent area appears blackened with mites and their excrement, and the skin may be scabbed and irritated.
- Pale comb and wattles (anemia from blood loss).
- Decreased egg production.
- Restlessness, excessive preening.

**Red mite --- what it looks like:**
- Red mites live in the coop, not on the bird. They emerge at night to feed on blood, then retreat to cracks and crevices in the wood. Check under roosting bars, in joints and corners, and along the underside of poop boards at night with a flashlight. You will see tiny red or gray-brown dots moving. Crush one --- if it leaves a blood smear, it is a blood-feeding mite.
- Birds may be reluctant to enter the coop at roosting time.
- Pale combs, lethargy, reduced laying.

**Poultry lice --- what it looks like:**
- Lice live on the bird full-time, usually visible at the base of feathers around the vent, under the wings, and on the breast. They are pale yellow to tan, elongated, and approximately 1-3 mm long. They move quickly through the feathers when disturbed.
- Clusters of white, oval egg masses (nits) cemented to the base of feathers, particularly around the vent.
- Feather damage, irritated skin, excessive preening.

**What to do:**
- **For mites on the bird:** Treat with poultry dust containing permethrin (0.25%), applied directly to the vent area, under wings, and around the neck. Repeat in 7-10 days to kill mites that hatch from eggs laid before the first treatment. Alternatively, Elector PSP (spinosad) can be sprayed on birds and in the coop --- it is more expensive but effective and relatively low-toxicity.
- **For red mites in the coop:** Treat the coop, not just the birds. Remove all bedding. Spray all surfaces, especially cracks and joints, with a permethrin spray or Elector PSP. Let dry before adding fresh bedding and returning birds. Repeat in 7-10 days. Diatomaceous earth (food grade) can be dusted into cracks as a supplemental physical control but is not sufficient alone for heavy infestations.
- **For lice:** Same permethrin dust treatment as for mites. Lice are easier to control than mites because they live full-time on the bird and do not have a reservoir in the coop.
- **Prevention:** Inspect birds monthly. Part the vent feathers, check under wings, look at the skin. Regular dust-bathing access (dry dirt, sand, or wood ash) helps birds control external parasites naturally --- the abrasive particles damage the exoskeletons of mites and lice.

### Internal Parasites: Worms

**What it is:** Several species of nematodes (roundworms) and cestodes (tapeworms) infect chickens. The most common and clinically significant are large roundworms (*Ascaridia galli*), cecal worms (*Heterakis gallinarum*), and capillary worms (*Capillaria* spp.). Chickens acquire worms by eating infected earthworms, insects, or contaminated feed/water.

**What it looks like:**
- Weight loss despite good appetite (the classic sign).
- Decreased egg production.
- Pale comb and wattles.
- Diarrhea (sometimes with visible worms in droppings --- large roundworms are 2-3 inches long and unmistakable).
- Ruffled feathers, lethargy.
- In heavy infestations: intestinal blockage (especially in young birds), emaciation, death.

**What to do:**
- **Treat with fenbendazole (Safeguard/Panacur).** Safeguard Goat Dewormer (10% fenbendazole) is the most commonly available formulation. Dosage: 0.23 ml per pound of body weight, administered orally (directly into the beak with a syringe) for 5 consecutive days. This is an off-label use (fenbendazole is not FDA-approved for poultry), but it is the standard recommendation from poultry veterinarians.
- **Egg withdrawal period:** There is no established withdrawal period for fenbendazole in poultry (because it is not labeled for poultry). Most veterinarians and experienced keepers recommend discarding eggs for 14 days after the last dose.
- **Prevention:** Rotate pasture or run areas if possible. Remove droppings regularly. Avoid chronic soil contamination by not keeping birds on the same small patch of ground indefinitely. Consider fecal float testing (your vet can do this, or you can learn the technique yourself with a microscope) to determine worm burden before treating.

**College connection:** Science (parasitology, pharmacology); Home Economics (livestock health management).

## Part 3: Bacterial Infections

### Respiratory Infections

**What it is:** A constellation of bacterial diseases (Mycoplasma gallisepticum, Mycoplasma synoviae, infectious coryza caused by *Avibacterium paragallinarum*, and others) that cause upper respiratory symptoms. Mycoplasmosis is chronic, incurable, and the single most common bacterial disease complex in backyard flocks. Infected birds become permanent carriers --- they may recover clinically but continue to shed the organism, especially under stress.

**What it looks like:**
- Sneezing, coughing, wheezing.
- Watery or foamy eyes (bubbly discharge in the corner of the eye is characteristic of Mycoplasma).
- Nasal discharge (clear at first, becoming thick and yellowish).
- Swollen sinuses (puffy face, swelling below and around the eye).
- Decreased appetite and egg production.
- In severe cases: open-mouth breathing, rattling sounds from the chest.

**What to do:**
- **Isolate the affected bird immediately.** Respiratory infections spread rapidly through flocks via aerosol, shared water, and direct contact.
- **Supportive care:** Warm, dry, draft-free isolation space. Electrolyte water (Sav-A-Chick or similar poultry electrolyte mix). Ensure the bird is eating and drinking.
- **Antibiotics:** Tylosin (Tylan) and oxytetracycline are the two antibiotics most commonly used for mycoplasmosis in poultry. Tylan 50 injectable can be given orally (1 ml per 5 lbs body weight, mixed into water, for 5 days). These are available at farm supply stores. Note: antibiotics treat the symptoms and reduce bacterial load but do not cure Mycoplasma --- the bird remains a carrier.
- **The carrier problem:** A flock that has been exposed to Mycoplasma is a carrier flock. Selling or giving birds from your flock to other keepers risks spreading the disease. Buying birds from unknown flocks risks introducing it. This is why biosecurity (quarantining new birds for 30 days before introducing to the flock) is critical.

**When to call a vet:** If multiple birds are affected, if symptoms are severe (open-mouth breathing, cyanosis), or if birds are dying. Avian veterinarians can culture the organism, confirm the specific pathogen, and recommend targeted treatment.

### Bumblefoot

**What it is:** A localized bacterial infection (usually *Staphylococcus aureus*) of the foot pad, caused when a small wound (from a splinter, a hard landing from a high roost, rough flooring) becomes infected. The result is an abscess on the bottom of the foot --- a hard, swollen, dark-scabbed lump that makes walking painful.

**What it looks like:**
- A round, dark scab on the bottom of the foot pad. The scab may be black, dark brown, or reddish.
- Swelling of the foot, sometimes extending up the leg.
- Limping, reluctance to walk, resting frequently.
- In advanced cases, the infection can spread to the joint (septic arthritis) or the bloodstream (septicemia), which can be fatal.

**What to do:**
- **Mild cases (small scab, minimal swelling):** Soak the foot in warm Epsom salt water (1/4 cup per gallon) for 15-20 minutes to soften the scab. Apply an antibiotic ointment (non-pain-relief Neosporin --- avoid the "pain relief" formulation, which contains lidocaine/pramoxine that is toxic to birds). Wrap loosely with gauze and vet wrap. Repeat daily. Mild cases may resolve in 1-2 weeks.
- **Moderate to severe cases (large scab, significant swelling, kernel visible under scab):** The abscess contains a solid core (called a "kernel" or "plug") of necrotic tissue and compressed pus. Surgical removal of this kernel is the standard treatment: soak the foot, carefully remove the scab with a sterile scalpel blade, extract the kernel with tweezers, flush the cavity with Betadine solution, pack with antibiotic ointment, and bandage. Many experienced chicken keepers perform this procedure themselves (YouTube has dozens of instructional videos), but if you are not comfortable doing minor surgery on an animal's foot, a veterinarian can do it.
- **Prevention:** Lower the roost height for heavy breeds (Orpingtons, Brahmas). Provide soft, dry bedding or rubber mats on the coop floor. Remove sharp objects (wire ends, splinters, protruding screw heads) from the coop and run. Sand any rough perch surfaces smooth.

**College connection:** Science (microbiology, wound care); Home Economics (animal husbandry, practical veterinary skills).

## Part 4: Emergencies

### Egg Binding

**What it is:** A hen has a fully formed egg stuck in the oviduct and cannot pass it. This is a genuine emergency --- an egg-bound hen can die within 24-48 hours from exhaustion, infection, or prolapse.

**What it looks like:**
- Hen is straining visibly, sometimes with her tail pumping up and down.
- "Penguin walk" --- an upright, wide-legged, waddling gait.
- Sitting on the ground with a puffed-up, miserable posture.
- Frequent, unproductive visits to the nesting box.
- You may be able to feel the egg by gently palpating the abdomen between the legs (it feels like a hard, oval lump just inside the vent).

**What to do (in order):**
1. **Warm bath.** Fill a container with warm water (100-102 degrees F, comfortable to your wrist). Place the hen in the water up to her vent area. The warmth relaxes the muscles of the oviduct and vent. Soak for 20-30 minutes. Many egg-bound hens pass the egg during or shortly after the bath.
2. **Calcium.** Administer calcium, which strengthens uterine contractions. Tums (calcium carbonate) --- crush one tablet and mix with water, administer orally with a syringe. Or calcium gluconate/citrate liquid supplement. Calcium often resolves mild egg binding within a few hours.
3. **Lubrication.** Apply a small amount of water-based lubricant (KY Jelly, NOT petroleum jelly) to the vent area. This helps if the egg is very close to the exit but is catching on dry tissue.
4. **Warm, dark, quiet space.** After the bath, place the hen in a warm, dark, quiet space (a towel-lined carrier or box in a warm room). Darkness reduces stress and encourages the hen to relax and pass the egg.
5. **If no progress in 4-6 hours: call a veterinarian.** A vet can administer injectable calcium gluconate and oxytocin, which usually resolves the binding. In rare cases, manual extraction or surgery is necessary.

**Do NOT attempt to manually push the egg out** unless you have been trained to do so. Breaking the egg inside the hen is catastrophic --- the sharp shell fragments lacerate the oviduct, causing internal bleeding and peritonitis.

### Prolapsed Vent

**What it is:** The tissue of the oviduct or cloaca protrudes through the vent and remains outside the body. This can occur after passing a large egg, in young pullets laying their first eggs, or in hens that have been straining from egg binding. It is a veterinary emergency because exposed tissue dries, swells, becomes infected, and --- critically --- other chickens will peck at the red, protruding tissue (chickens are attracted to the color red and will peck at blood or exposed tissue with lethal persistence).

**What it looks like:**
- Pink, red, or dark red tissue visibly protruding from the vent.
- The hen may be straining, or the tissue may simply be hanging outside the body.
- Other chickens may be pecking at the affected hen's rear end.

**What to do:**
1. **Isolate immediately.** Remove the affected hen from the flock. Other birds will peck the prolapse and can cause fatal damage within minutes.
2. **Clean the tissue.** Gently rinse the exposed tissue with warm, clean water to remove bedding, feces, and debris.
3. **Reduce swelling.** Apply granulated sugar (not powdered --- granulated) to the exposed tissue. The sugar draws fluid out of the swollen tissue through osmosis, reducing the size of the prolapse and making it easier to replace. Leave the sugar on for 5-10 minutes.
4. **Replace the tissue.** With clean, lubricated (water-based lubricant) fingers, gently push the tissue back inside the vent. This requires patience and steady pressure. The tissue is slippery and may try to re-prolapse. Hold gentle pressure for 1-2 minutes after replacing.
5. **Keep the hen isolated in a dark, quiet space** to reduce straining and prevent immediate re-prolapse.
6. **Monitor.** Some prolapses resolve permanently after a single replacement. Others recur. A hen that repeatedly prolapses may need veterinary intervention (a purse-string suture around the vent) or may need to be culled if the condition is chronic.

**When to call a vet:** If the tissue is dark purple, black, or appears necrotic (dead tissue). If the prolapse is large (involving several inches of oviduct). If the hen has been pecked severely by flock mates before you found her.

### Predator Injuries

**What it looks like:** Puncture wounds, lacerations, missing feathers, broken bones, missing limbs. Predator attacks range from a single talon puncture (hawk strike) to catastrophic injury (dog attack). The bird may be in shock: quiet, fluffed up, eyes partially closed, unresponsive.

**What to do:**
1. **Stop any bleeding.** Apply direct pressure with clean gauze or cloth. Styptic powder or cornstarch can be applied to minor bleeding wounds. For arterial bleeding (bright red, pulsing), maintain pressure for 3-5 minutes.
2. **Assess the damage.** If the body cavity is open (visible internal organs), humane euthanasia is the kindest option. If the injuries are limited to soft tissue (skin lacerations, puncture wounds, missing feathers), recovery is possible.
3. **Clean wounds.** Flush with saline (1 teaspoon salt per quart of warm water) or dilute Betadine. Remove any debris.
4. **Treat for shock.** Warm, dark, quiet environment. Offer electrolyte water with a dropper if the bird is responsive.
5. **Ongoing wound care.** Apply antibiotic ointment daily (non-pain-relief formulation). Change bandages if applicable. Keep isolated from the flock until healed --- other chickens will peck at wounds.
6. **Antibiotics if needed.** Deep puncture wounds (especially from mammalian teeth or dirty talons) often become infected. If the wound becomes hot, swollen, or produces pus within 2-3 days, oral amoxicillin (available as fish-mox from aquarium supply stores, 250mg capsule, 1/4 capsule per standard-sized hen twice daily for 7-10 days) is a commonly used treatment.

**The emotional difficulty:** Sometimes the kindest action is euthanasia. A bird that is suffering, that will not recover, or that will live in chronic pain is not being helped by prolonged, heroic treatment. This is a hard decision, and making it well is part of being a responsible animal keeper. Humane euthanasia methods for poultry include cervical dislocation (requires training) or taking the bird to a veterinarian. Do not let a bird suffer because the alternative is emotionally difficult for you.

## Part 5: The Home First-Aid Kit

Every coop should have a basic first-aid kit. A plastic storage bin in the garage works fine.

| Item | Purpose | Cost |
|------|---------|------|
| Vetericyn Plus Poultry Care spray | Wound cleaning, general antiseptic | $15-20 |
| Neosporin (non-pain-relief) | Antibiotic ointment for wounds, bumblefoot | $6 |
| Corid (amprolium) liquid or powder | Coccidiosis treatment | $15-20 |
| Permethrin poultry dust | Mite and lice treatment | $10 |
| Epsom salt | Foot soaks (bumblefoot) | $5 |
| Gauze pads and vet wrap | Wound bandaging | $8 |
| Tums (calcium carbonate) | Egg binding, calcium supplementation | $5 |
| Electrolyte powder (Sav-A-Chick) | Dehydration, heat stress, shock | $5 |
| Syringe (no needle, 3-5 ml) | Oral medication administration | $2 |
| Styptic powder or cornstarch | Stopping minor bleeding | $5 |
| KY Jelly or water-based lubricant | Egg binding, prolapse reduction | $5 |
| Small towel and carrier/box | Catching and transporting birds | free |

**Total cost: approximately $75-100.** Assemble it before you need it. The night you find a hen with a prolapsed vent at 10 PM is not the time to discover that the nearest store with Vetericyn closes at 9.

## Part 6: When to Cull vs. When to Treat

This is the section that no one wants to write and no one wants to read, and it is the section that matters most for the long-term welfare of your birds and your flock.

**Treating is appropriate when:**
- The condition is treatable (coccidiosis, bumblefoot, mild respiratory infection, egg binding, minor predator injuries).
- The bird has a reasonable chance of full recovery.
- The bird's quality of life during treatment is acceptable (not in constant pain, able to eat and drink, responsive).
- The treatment will not create a chronic carrier state that endangers the rest of the flock (note: Mycoplasma-positive birds ARE chronic carriers).

**Culling is appropriate when:**
- The condition is untreatable (advanced Marek's, severe HPAI, catastrophic predator injury with body cavity exposure).
- The bird is suffering and treatment will not relieve the suffering.
- The bird's quality of life is poor and will not improve.
- Keeping the bird alive endangers the rest of the flock (highly contagious, fatal diseases).

**The emotional difficulty of culling** is real, and it does not diminish with experience. You named the bird. You raised it from a chick, possibly from an egg you incubated yourself. It comes to you when you walk into the run. And now you must make a decision that ends its life because continuing its life is cruelty disguised as compassion.

This is the weight of animal keeping. It is the weight that every farmer, rancher, and homesteader carries, and it is part of what separates keeping animals from merely owning them. The bird depends on you for everything --- food, water, shelter, health, and, when the time comes, a merciful end. Honoring that full spectrum of responsibility is what animal stewardship means.
