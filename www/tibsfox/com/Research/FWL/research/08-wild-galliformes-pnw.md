# Wild Galliformes of the Pacific Northwest

> **Document 08 of the FWL (Fowl) research series**
>
> This document bridges the domestic fowl focus of the FWL project to the wild Galliformes that inhabit the Pacific Northwest bioregion. The order Galliformes -- heavy-bodied, ground-dwelling birds with strong legs and short, rounded wings -- is represented in the PNW by both native species adapted over millennia to specific ecological niches and introduced species established through deliberate human action over the past 150 years. Together, these birds occupy habitats from alpine snowfields above treeline to irrigated agricultural lowlands, from old-growth Douglas-fir forests to arid sagebrush steppe. Understanding these wild relatives enriches the FWL project's primary focus on domestic breeds by illuminating the evolutionary context from which all domesticated gallinaceous birds descend.
>
> **Species covered:** 10 species across 3 families (Phasianidae, Odontophoridae, Phasianidae sensu lato)
>
> **Bioregion scope:** Pacific Northwest as defined by the Columbia Basin, Cascadia, and coastal ecoregions -- Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and southern British Columbia.

---

## The Galliformes in Ecological Context

The order Galliformes contains approximately 290 species worldwide. In the PNW, three families are present: Phasianidae (grouse, ptarmigan, pheasants, partridges, turkeys), Odontophoridae (New World quail), and arguably Meleagrididae if turkeys are elevated to family rank. All share fundamental traits: precocial chicks capable of self-feeding within hours, strong legs for walking and scratching, cryptic female plumage, ground nesting, and diets shifting seasonally between invertebrates, seeds, fruits, and green vegetation.

These birds are ecological linchpins -- primary prey for Northern Goshawk, Cooper's Hawk, Great Horned Owl, coyote, bobcat, red fox, and American marten. As seed consumers and dispersers, they shape plant community composition. Their scratching turns soil and redistributes leaf litter. Their droppings fertilize forest floor and grassland soils.

## Native Species

### Sooty Grouse (*Dendragapus fuliginosus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Dendragapus*
**Status:** Year-round resident; altitudinal migrant
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; no state ESA listing in WA or OR

The Sooty Grouse is the quintessential bird of the PNW's western Cascade slopes and coastal mountain ranges. Formerly lumped with the Dusky Grouse as "Blue Grouse" (*Dendragapus obscurus*), the 2006 AOS split recognized the Sooty Grouse as a distinct species based on vocal, morphological, and genetic differences. The split line roughly follows the crest of the Cascade Range -- Sooty Grouse to the west, Dusky Grouse to the east.

**Morphology.** Males are large (1.1-1.4 kg), dark slate-gray to sooty brown, with a yellow-orange comb above the eye and a cervical air sac inflated during courtship to reveal bare yellow to reddish-purple skin. Females are smaller (0.8-1.0 kg), mottled brown with dark barring. The tail is long and dark with a gray terminal band, distinguishing this species from the Dusky Grouse's paler tail.

**Habitat and altitudinal migration.** The Sooty Grouse exhibits a remarkable reverse altitudinal migration that defies intuition. In spring and summer, breeding occurs at middle elevations (300-1,500 m) in open forests, clearings, and meadow edges where hens nest and raise broods. As autumn arrives, both sexes move *uphill* into dense coniferous forests at higher elevations (1,000-2,200 m), where they spend winter feeding almost exclusively on conifer needles -- primarily Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), true firs (*Abies* spp.), and mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*). This uphill winter migration is counterintuitive: most birds descend to lower, warmer elevations in winter. The Sooty Grouse instead exploits the superabundant, reliable, and competition-free food source of conifer needles at elevation, tolerating cold temperatures and deep snow by roosting in dense canopy or burrowing into snowpack.

**Vocalizations and display.** The male's hooting display is one of the most evocative sounds of PNW mountain forests. A series of five to seven deep, resonant hoots (*hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo-hoo*) carries through old-growth forest with ventriloquial quality. Males display from elevated perches with tail fanned and neck feathers raised. The sound frequency (around 100 Hz) is near the lower limit of human hearing and propagates effectively through dense forest. Males maintain territories through the breeding season (April-June) and may display from the same perch for multiple years.

**Ecological role.** Sooty Grouse are important prey for Northern Goshawks (*Accipiter gentilis*), which in the PNW depend heavily on grouse during the breeding season. Great Horned Owls, bobcats, and American martens also take adults. Chicks consume insects (beetles, ants, caterpillars) during their first weeks, shifting to plant material as they grow. Adults function as minor seed dispersers for huckleberry (*Vaccinium* spp.) and other understory shrubs.

**Connection to old-growth.** Dependence on mature coniferous forest for winter habitat links population health to forest management. Clearcut logging removes winter habitat directly; second-growth plantations lack the canopy complexity for thermal cover and needle access. The Sooty Grouse serves as an old-growth indicator alongside the Spotted Owl and Marbled Murrelet, though it has received far less conservation attention.

### Ruffed Grouse (*Bonasa umbellus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Bonasa*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; game species in WA, OR, ID

The Ruffed Grouse is the most widely distributed grouse in North America, found from Alaska to the Appalachians. In the PNW, it occupies deciduous and mixed forests from sea level to mid-montane elevations, particularly where alder (*Alnus* spp.), aspen (*Populus tremuloides*), and birch (*Betula* spp.) form a significant canopy or understory component.

**Morphology.** Medium-sized (450-750 g), with two distinct color morphs -- gray and rufous -- occurring sympatrically throughout the PNW. Gray morphs predominate in drier, more continental habitats east of the Cascades; rufous morphs are more common in the humid coastal forests. Both morphs display the diagnostic "ruff" of elongated dark neck feathers (more prominent in males) and a broad, fan-shaped tail with a dark subterminal band. The tail band pattern -- whether continuous or broken at the central retrices -- has been used by hunters and birders as a rough sex indicator, though overlap exists.

**The drumming display.** Males stand on a "drumming log" -- a fallen tree, typically 25-40 cm in diameter -- and beat their wings rapidly against compressed air, producing a deep, accelerating series of thumps that sounds like a distant engine starting. The sound is produced by air compression as the wings move forward and upward, not by wings striking the body. The acoustic frequency (around 40 Hz) propagates through forest at distances of 400 m or more. Males drum year-round but most intensely from March through May. Choice drumming logs are used for decades.

**Habitat: alder and aspen specialists.** PNW Ruffed Grouse populations correlate tightly with early-successional deciduous growth. Red alder (*Alnus rubra*) on the west side and quaking aspen (*Populus tremuloides*) on the east side provide male catkins as a critical winter food. In late autumn, Ruffed Grouse feed extensively on buds, catkins, and twigs, often perching high in the canopy. Forest management promoting alder and aspen regeneration -- selective logging, controlled burns, beaver-mediated flooding -- directly benefits Ruffed Grouse.

**Predator relationships.** Ruffed Grouse are a primary prey item for Northern Goshawks, comprising 15-30% of goshawk diet by biomass during the breeding season in northern Cascade studies. Cooper's Hawks, Great Horned Owls, red foxes, bobcats, and coyotes are also significant predators. The "crazy flight" behavior -- young birds in autumn flying recklessly, sometimes crashing into windows or vehicles -- is thought to be dispersal rather than disease-related.

### Dusky Grouse (*Dendragapus obscurus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Dendragapus*
**Status:** Year-round resident; altitudinal migrant
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern

The Dusky Grouse, the eastern counterpart to the Sooty Grouse following the 2006 split, inhabits the east slope of the Cascades, the Blue Mountains, Okanogan Highlands, and mountain ranges of central and eastern Oregon, Idaho, and interior British Columbia. It is a bird of open montane coniferous forest -- ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*), Douglas-fir, and grand fir (*Abies grandis*) stands with grassy understories, rocky openings, and mountain meadows.

**Distinguishing from Sooty Grouse.** The Dusky Grouse averages slightly smaller than the Sooty Grouse and is paler overall, with a grayer (less sooty) plumage tone. The male's cervical air sac is purplish-red (versus yellow in Sooty Grouse), and the tail has a wider, paler gray terminal band. Vocalizations differ: the Dusky Grouse's hooting is slightly higher-pitched and the note series shorter (typically five hoots versus five to seven in Sooty Grouse). In the field, range is the most reliable separator -- west of the Cascade crest is Sooty, east is Dusky, with a narrow hybrid zone near the crest.

**Ecology.** Like its western sibling, the Dusky Grouse undertakes reverse altitudinal migration, moving uphill in autumn to winter in dense coniferous forest. East-side populations depend on Douglas-fir, true fir, and lodgepole pine (*Pinus contorta*) needle crops. Summer breeding habitat in east-side forests tends to be drier and more open than Sooty Grouse habitat, with bunchgrass, sagebrush, and bitterbrush understories. Nest success is influenced by spring precipitation patterns -- wet springs that green up understory vegetation earlier provide better concealment for nesting hens.

### White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Lagopus*
**Status:** Year-round resident; alpine obligate
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; State of Washington: Candidate Species

The White-tailed Ptarmigan is the smallest grouse in North America (300-400 g) and the only bird species in the PNW that resides above treeline year-round. It is the avian equivalent of the pika -- a cold-climate specialist whose existence depends on the persistence of alpine and subalpine habitat.

**Seasonal plumage transformation.** The ptarmigan's most remarkable adaptation is its complete seasonal plumage change. In winter, the entire plumage is pure white except for dark eyes and bill, providing nearly perfect camouflage against snow. By June, the bird has molted into a cryptic mottled pattern of brown, gray, buff, and white that matches alpine rock, lichen, and sparse vegetation. The white tail feathers are retained year-round (distinguishing this species from the Rock Ptarmigan and Willow Ptarmigan, which have dark tails). This molt is photoperiod-driven -- lengthening days in spring trigger the shift to summer plumage; shortening days in autumn initiate the return to white.

**Alpine habitat.** In the PNW, White-tailed Ptarmigan are found in the North Cascades (Washington), the Wallowa Mountains (Oregon), and mountain ranges of central Idaho, generally above 1,800 m in summer and ranging down to krummholz and upper subalpine zones in winter. Key habitat features include alpine meadows with willow (*Salix* spp.) thickets for summer brood-rearing, rocky fellfields with lichen for foraging, and snowfields with exposed ground for winter feeding. The birds eat willow buds, alder catkins, and alpine plant shoots in winter; in summer they add insects, seeds, and flowers.

**Climate vulnerability.** The White-tailed Ptarmigan is among the PNW bird species most vulnerable to climate change. As temperatures rise, treeline advances upward, compressing and eventually eliminating alpine habitat. Snowpack reduction exposes ptarmigan to increased predation risk (their white winter plumage becomes a liability against dark rock). Modeling studies suggest that significant alpine habitat loss in the Cascades could occur within decades if warming trends continue. This makes the ptarmigan a sentinel species for climate change impacts on PNW mountain ecosystems -- alongside the pika, the Cascade Red Fox, and alpine plant communities.

### Mountain Quail (*Oreortyx pictus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Odontophoridae, Genus *Oreortyx*
**Status:** Year-round resident; altitudinal migrant
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; State of Washington: Endangered (some populations)

The Mountain Quail is the PNW's only native quail species and the largest New World quail (200-260 g). It is also one of the most secretive and least-studied gallinaceous birds in North America. Its long, straight head plume -- two slender feathers projecting vertically from the crown -- is unique among quail and immediately diagnostic.

**Morphology.** Adults are richly colored: blue-gray breast and nape, chestnut throat bordered by white, chestnut and white flank bars, olive-brown back. Males and females are similar in plumage (unusually so for galliformes), though males average slightly brighter and have longer plumes. The bold flank pattern -- alternating chestnut and white bars -- is visible even in deep cover and is often the first field mark noticed.

**Altitudinal migration on foot.** The Mountain Quail is the only North American bird known to undertake a regular altitudinal migration primarily by walking. In autumn, coveys of 10-20 birds walk downhill from summer breeding habitat (1,000-2,500 m in brushy mountain slopes, burned areas, and early-successional chaparral) to lower-elevation winter habitat (below 1,000 m in dense riparian thickets, brush piles, and canyon bottoms). They walk uphill again in spring. While capable of flight, they prefer to walk, sometimes covering several kilometers on foot along established routes. This behavior makes them vulnerable to habitat fragmentation -- a clearcut or development that breaks the corridor between summer and winter habitat can isolate populations.

**PNW distribution and decline.** Historically, Mountain Quail ranged from southern British Columbia through western Washington and Oregon into California. Populations in Washington have declined dramatically since the mid-20th century and are now largely restricted to the southern Cascades and Blue Mountains. Causes include habitat loss (suppression of fire that maintained early-successional brush), severe winters, and possibly predation by introduced species. Reintroduction efforts, including translocation of birds from Oregon source populations, have had mixed success. The species remains a conservation priority in Washington.

## Introduced but Established Species

### California Quail (*Callipepla californica*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Odontophoridae, Genus *Callipepla*
**Status:** Year-round resident (introduced, established)
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; abundant in suitable habitat

The California Quail is the most familiar galliform in the PNW's urban and suburban landscapes. Although its historical native range extends into southern Oregon, populations throughout most of Oregon and all of Washington result from deliberate introductions beginning in the 1850s. It is Washington's state bird.

**Covey structure.** California Quail are highly social, forming coveys of 10-75 birds outside the breeding season. Covey behavior is complex: communal roosting in tight circles (tails inward, heads outward for predator detection), coordinated flushing with explosive flight when threatened, and sentinel behavior where one or more birds perch on elevated positions to scan for predators while the covey forages. The male's *chi-CA-go* call -- a loud, three-note whistle -- functions as a covey assembly call and is one of the most recognizable bird sounds in PNW suburbia.

**Habitat and diet.** California Quail thrive in edge habitat -- the interface between brushy cover and open ground. Suburban landscapes with hedgerows, brush piles, and weedy vacant lots provide ideal conditions. In rural areas, they occupy agricultural margins, riparian thickets, oak woodlands, and chaparral. Diet is primarily seeds (annual grasses, clover, lupine, chickweed) supplemented by green vegetation and invertebrates, especially for growing chicks.

**Urban adaptation.** California Quail nest under ornamental shrubs, forage in gardens, and use fences as sentinel perches. Their tolerance of human proximity -- far higher than native galliformes -- has made them one of the PNW's most visible birds. Their success in human landscapes should not obscure the decline of native species in wilder habitats.

### Ring-necked Pheasant (*Phasianus colchicus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Phasianus*
**Status:** Year-round resident (introduced, established)
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; widely managed as game species

The Ring-necked Pheasant, native to Asia from the Caucasus to China, was first successfully introduced to the PNW in 1881 when Judge Owen Denny shipped birds from Shanghai to Oregon's Willamette Valley. This introduction -- one of the most successful game bird transplants in history -- established self-sustaining populations that spread throughout agricultural lowlands of Oregon, Washington, and Idaho.

**Habitat: the agricultural connection.** Ring-necked Pheasants are creatures of cultivated landscapes. They thrive in the mosaic of cropland, fallow fields, hedgerows, and wetland edges that characterize PNW valley agriculture. The bird's population density tracks agricultural practices closely: Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) land, which provides undisturbed nesting cover, is the single strongest predictor of pheasant abundance. Conversion of CRP to active cultivation, removal of hedgerows, and autumn plowing of crop stubble all reduce pheasant populations. In the Palouse region of eastern Washington, pheasant densities correlate directly with the proportion of landscape in perennial cover.

**Ecological impact.** The Ring-necked Pheasant occupies a niche that does not precisely overlap with any native PNW galliform, but interactions are not negligible. Abundant pheasant populations attract and sustain higher predator densities, increasing predation on native species. Pheasants can transmit the cecal worm (*Heterakis gallinarum*) that vectors *Histomonas meleagridis* (blackhead disease), devastating to turkeys and harmful to grouse.

### Wild Turkey (*Meleagris gallopavo*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae (or Meleagrididae), Genus *Meleagris*
**Status:** Year-round resident (introduced, established in limited areas)
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern globally; introduced populations in PNW uneven

Wild Turkeys are not native to the Pacific Northwest. The species' historical range extended from the eastern United States through Mexico. Introductions to the PNW began in the mid-20th century, with Merriam's Turkey (*M. g. merriami*) and Rio Grande Turkey (*M. g. intermedia*) subspecies released by state wildlife agencies and hunting organizations. Populations are now established in portions of eastern Washington, eastern and southern Oregon, and Idaho, primarily in open ponderosa pine forest, oak woodland, and agricultural-forest interface.

**Domestic fowl connection.** The Wild Turkey is the only galliform independently domesticated in the Americas. Archaeological evidence places domestication in Mesoamerica by approximately 800 BCE, with a second event in the American Southwest around 200 BCE. All modern domestic turkey breeds descend from the South Mexican subspecies (*M. g. gallopavo*), brought to Europe by Spanish explorers in the early 1500s. The introduction of Wild Turkeys to the PNW brings this story full circle -- wild ancestors now coexist with descendants of their domesticated relatives on PNW farms.

**Ecological concerns.** Wild Turkeys are large, generalist omnivores consuming acorns, pine seeds, berries, insects, and small vertebrates. In Oregon's oak woodlands, competition with native acorn consumers (jays, woodpeckers, deer, rodents) has raised concern. Their ground-scratching can disturb native plant communities. Net ecological impact remains under study.

### Chukar (*Alectoris chukar*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Alectoris*
**Status:** Year-round resident (introduced, established)
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; managed as game species

The Chukar, native to the arid mountains and plateaus of southern Eurasia from Greece to Nepal, was introduced to western North America beginning in the 1930s after decades of failed attempts. It found its niche in the steep, rocky, arid canyon lands of the Columbia Basin, Snake River, and Owyhee River drainages of eastern Oregon, Washington, and Idaho -- habitat that closely resembles its native range.

**Arid-land specialist.** Chukars occupy steep, rocky slopes with bunchgrass, cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*), and scattered shrubs at 200-2,000 m -- terrain no native PNW galliform uses extensively. Population dynamics are driven by cheatgrass seed production: wet winters fuel high reproductive success the following spring. The dependence on cheatgrass, itself invasive, creates an ironic pairing: two introduced species forming a novel mutualism.

**Canyon country icon.** The loud *chuck-chuck-chukar-chukar* call echoing off basalt rimrock defines the east-side arid landscape. Coveys of 5-40 birds run uphill when disturbed, flushing only when pressed, then flying downhill across canyons to reform on the opposite slope.

### Gray Partridge (*Perdix perdix*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Galliformes, Family Phasianidae, Genus *Perdix*
**Status:** Year-round resident (introduced, established)
**Conservation status:** IUCN Least Concern; declining in some areas

The Gray Partridge (also called Hungarian Partridge or "Hun"), native to the agricultural grasslands of Europe and western Asia, was introduced to the PNW beginning around 1900. It established most successfully in the dryland wheat-farming regions of the Palouse, the Columbia Basin, and the Snake River Plain -- landscapes that mirror its native steppe-agricultural habitat.

**Agricultural obligate.** More than any other PNW galliform, the Gray Partridge is tied to grain agriculture. It nests in wheat stubble and fence rows, feeds on waste grain and weed seeds, and thrives where small-grain farming retains fallow ground and uncultivated edges. Populations have declined as farming intensified -- larger fields, fewer fence rows, and herbicide reduction of weed seeds.

## Ecological Roles of PNW Galliformes

### Ground Nesting and Predator Relationships

All PNW galliformes are ground nesters, making their eggs and incubating females vulnerable to a wide array of predators. Nest predation rates for PNW grouse and quail typically range from 40-70%, with the primary nest predators being common ravens, American crows, black-billed magpies, long-tailed weasels, striped skunks, raccoons, coyotes, and domestic cats. This high nest predation rate is a natural part of the system -- galliformes compensate with large clutch sizes (8-16 eggs for most species) and the ability to renest if early clutches fail.

The predator community that depends on adult galliformes is equally diverse. Northern Goshawks in PNW forests may derive 20-40% of their breeding-season diet from grouse. Great Horned Owls take grouse, quail, and pheasants regularly. Coyotes, bobcats, red foxes, and domestic dogs are significant predators. Cooper's Hawks specialize in quail-sized prey. This web of predator-prey relationships means that galliform populations directly influence predator abundance and distribution -- and vice versa.

### Seed Dispersal and Soil Dynamics

Galliformes consume vast quantities of seeds -- a single California Quail may eat 5,000-10,000 seeds per day. While most seeds are digested, some pass through intact, particularly berry seeds (huckleberry, snowberry, rose hips, elderberry). The birds' mobility -- covering hundreds of meters to several kilometers daily while foraging -- means they disperse these seeds across the landscape. In forests, grouse scratching behavior exposes mineral soil and redistributes organic matter, creating microsites favorable for seed germination. The scale of this disturbance is small per individual but significant in aggregate across healthy populations.

### Indicator Species Value

The diversity and abundance of native galliformes in a PNW landscape serves as a reliable indicator of ecosystem health. Sooty Grouse presence indicates mature coniferous forest with intact altitudinal connectivity. Ruffed Grouse abundance reflects the availability of early-successional deciduous habitat. Mountain Quail presence indicates brushy mountain habitat with unbroken migration corridors. White-tailed Ptarmigan are sentinels for alpine ecosystem integrity. When these species decline, something fundamental about their habitat has changed -- usually in ways that affect many other species as well.

## Conservation Challenges

### Habitat Loss and Fragmentation

The dominant conservation threat to PNW galliformes is habitat loss. For forest grouse, this means timber harvest practices that eliminate old-growth winter habitat (Sooty/Dusky Grouse) or reduce early-successional deciduous stands (Ruffed Grouse). For Mountain Quail, fire suppression has allowed forests to close over the brushy openings they need for breeding, while development fragments their walking migration corridors. For White-tailed Ptarmigan, the threat is climate-driven: rising temperatures push treeline higher, shrinking the alpine zone. For agricultural-landscape species (pheasant, partridge), intensification of farming practices removes the cover and edge habitat they depend upon.

### Climate Change

Climate change poses differential threats to PNW galliformes. Alpine species (ptarmigan) face direct habitat loss. Forest grouse face altered phenology -- if spring green-up shifts earlier, the timing mismatch between chick hatch and peak insect availability could reduce chick survival. Drought years in the east-side steppe reduce food availability for chukar and partridge. Increased wildfire frequency and intensity in east-side forests may benefit some species (creating early-successional habitat) while harming others (destroying mature forest structure).

### Hunting Management

Most PNW galliformes are managed as game species. Hunting regulations -- season length, bag limits, method restrictions -- are set by state wildlife agencies based on population monitoring. For native species, the conservation calculus is straightforward: harvest must not exceed recruitment. For introduced species, management goals may differ -- pheasant populations in some areas are supplemented by hatchery-raised birds released for hunting, a practice with questionable conservation value but significant economic impact.

## Connecting Wild to Domestic: The Shared Order

The FWL project's focus on domestic fowl exists within the evolutionary context of the order Galliformes. Every domestic chicken descends from the Red Junglefowl (*Gallus gallus*) of Southeast Asia. Every domestic turkey descends from the Wild Turkey (*Meleagris gallopavo*) of the Americas. The Ring-necked Pheasant, now established as a wild bird in the PNW, is the same species kept in aviaries and occasionally domesticated for meat. The domestic guinea fowl (*Numida meleagris*) comes from the same family as the partridges.

What unites all these birds is their shared galliform heritage: precocial young, ground-dwelling habits, strong legs, crop-based digestion, elaborate male displays, and cryptic female plumage. The domestic breeds profiled in earlier FWL documents are the product of millennia of selective pressure applied to this template. Understanding the wild species -- their adaptations, ecological roles, and conservation challenges -- enriches our appreciation of the domestic birds that share their order.

The farmyard rooster crowing at dawn and the Sooty Grouse hooting in old-growth forest are separated by 50 million years of evolution and 8,000 years of domestication, but they remain recognizably kin.
