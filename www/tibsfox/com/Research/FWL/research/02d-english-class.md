# The English Class: Dual-Purpose Mastery and the Meat Industry's Foundation

The English Class of the American Poultry Association gathers breeds developed in Great Britain and, by honorary inclusion, Australia. These breeds represent some of the finest dual-purpose poultry ever developed — birds that lay well, dress out handsomely for the table, and display the kind of practical beauty that British agricultural tradition has always prized. The English Class also contains the breed that, through crossbreeding, became the foundation of the entire modern meat chicken industry: the Cornish.

British poultry breeding reached its zenith in the Victorian era, when the combination of industrial wealth, agricultural science, and the competitive exhibition culture born from the Hen Fever produced an extraordinary flowering of breed development. The Orpington, the Sussex, the Dorking, and the Cornish all emerged from this tradition, each solving a different equation in the balance between eggs, meat, hardiness, and beauty.

---

## Orpington

### Origin and History

The Orpington was created by William Cook of Orpington, Kent, England in the 1880s. Cook was a practical poultry man with an eye for marketing, and he set out to create the perfect dual-purpose breed for the English market: a heavy, well-muscled bird that laid well, dressed out with white skin (preferred by English consumers), and looked impressive enough to win at shows. He succeeded so well that the Orpington became one of the most popular breeds in the world.

Cook developed the **Black Orpington** first, in 1886, from crosses including Minorcas, Plymouth Rocks, and Langshans. The **Buff Orpington** followed in 1894 and rapidly eclipsed the Black in popularity, becoming the variety most associated with the breed. The Buff Orpington's creation is attributed to both Cook and to other breeders working in parallel. The **White** and **Blue** varieties were developed subsequently.

The Buff Orpington was admitted to the APA Standard of Perfection in 1902.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes four varieties: **Buff** (1902), **Black** (1905), **White** (1905), and **Blue** (1923).

### Physical Description

The Orpington is a large, heavy, broad-bodied bird with an abundance of soft, loose feathering that makes it appear even bigger than it actually is. The body is deep, wide, and carried low, with a short back and a full, rounded breast. The plumage is profuse, soft, and slightly fluffy — not as extreme as the Cochin but noticeably fuller than most breeds. The overall silhouette is a broad, rounded U-shape when viewed from the side.

The **Buff Orpington** --- the "golden retriever of chickens," as it is affectionately known --- has rich, even golden-buff plumage across the entire body. In afternoon sunlight, a Buff Orpington glows --- the plumage catches the light and holds it, warm as honey, each feather blending seamlessly into the next until the bird looks like a single continuous surface of burnished gold. The color should be uniform, without lacing, penciling, or mealiness. Run your hand along an Orpington's back and the feathers give way like a down pillow --- the plumage is astonishingly deep, and your fingers sink an inch or more before you feel the bird's actual body beneath. The single comb is medium-sized, upright, and bright red. Earlobes and wattles are red. Eyes are reddish-bay, with an expression that can only be described as benevolent. Legs are pinkish-white and clean (no feathering), which distinguishes the Orpington from Asiatic breeds of similar size and fluffiness.

The **Black** variety shows dense, beetle-green sheen. The **Blue** variety is an even slate-blue with darker lacing. The **White** is pure white.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 10 lbs, Hen 8 lbs, Cockerel 8.5 lbs, Pullet 7 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

This is where the "golden retriever" comparison earns its keep. Buff Orpingtons are famously gentle, friendly, docile, and tolerant. They seek human company, tolerate handling (including by children), and are among the most approachable chickens available. They are excellent choices for first-time chicken keepers and for families. They are also reliably calm in mixed flocks, though their docility means they can be bullied by more assertive breeds.

Hens lay approximately 180-250 large brown eggs per year. Heritage exhibition strains tend toward the lower end; utility strains are more productive. Orpington hens are reasonably broody and make excellent mothers. The breed handles cold weather well thanks to its dense, fluffy plumage but can struggle in extreme heat.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Orpingtons as **Recovering**. The Buff variety is widely popular, especially among backyard keepers. The Black, White, and Blue varieties are less common, with the Blue being the rarest.

## Cornish

### Origin and History

The Cornish — originally known as the Indian Game — was developed in Cornwall, England in the early nineteenth century by crossing Aseel (Indian game fowl) with Old English Game and possibly Malay. The original goal was to create a superior fighting bird, but the Cornish failed as a pit fighter (too slow) and succeeded spectacularly at something its creators never intended: becoming the foundation of the global broiler industry.

The breed was admitted to the APA as "Cornish" in 1893. For decades, it was regarded as a modestly useful but somewhat impractical breed — too muscular for good laying, too slow-growing for efficient meat production, and too wide-breasted for natural mating in some lines.

### The Cornish Cross Revolution

Everything changed in the 1940s and 1950s when poultry scientists at the University of Connecticut and commercial breeding companies discovered that crossing Cornish males with White Plymouth Rock females produced offspring with extraordinary growth rates and meat conversion efficiency. This hybrid — the **Cornish Cross** — became the modern broiler chicken, the single most consumed meat animal on Earth.

Today, virtually every chicken sold for meat in the developed world is a Cornish Cross or a close derivative. These birds reach market weight (4-6 lbs) in just 6-8 weeks — a growth rate that would have astonished poultry keepers of any previous era. The Cornish Cross accounts for approximately 70 billion chickens slaughtered worldwide per year.

The purebred Cornish, the parent breed, is a very different bird from its industrial offspring.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Dark** (1893), **White** (1898), **White Laced Red** (1909), and **Buff** (1938).

### Physical Description

The purebred Cornish is a wide, muscular, powerfully built bird with a distinctive low, broad stance. The body is compact and thick, with an exceptionally broad breast and widely spaced legs. The breast muscle is more prominent than in any other standard breed — this is the trait that made the Cornish invaluable for meat production. The legs are thick and strong, set wide apart. The plumage is tight and close-fitting, lying flat against the hard, muscular body.

The **Dark Cornish** has a complex double-laced plumage pattern: rich bay or reddish-brown feathers with a distinct inner and outer lacing of greenish-black. Males show a darker pattern with brilliant red shoulders. The **White** variety is pure white with yellow legs. The **White Laced Red** has rich mahogany-red feathers laced with white. The **Buff** is golden-buff with darker lacing.

The comb is a small **pea comb**, tight and close to the head. Earlobes and wattles are red. Eyes are pearl or bright yellowish. The face has a fierce, determined expression reminiscent of the breed's game fowl ancestry.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 10.5 lbs, Hen 8 lbs, Cockerel 8.5 lbs, Pullet 6.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Purebred Cornish are active, somewhat assertive birds with a confident bearing. Males can be aggressive toward other males but are generally manageable. They are surprisingly good foragers given their heavy build. Hens lay approximately 80-160 medium light brown eggs per year — modest production that reflects the breed's meat rather than egg orientation. Hens go broody and are protective mothers, though their wide bodies can sometimes make egg incubation awkward.

It is critical to distinguish the purebred Cornish from the Cornish Cross hybrid. Purebred Cornish grow at a natural rate, live normal lifespans, reproduce naturally, and behave like chickens. Cornish Cross hybrids grow so fast that they can develop skeletal and cardiac problems, rarely survive to breeding age, and are not bred to live as backyard poultry.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists purebred Cornish as **Watch**. Despite being the genetic foundation of the global meat industry, the purebred Cornish is uncommon. Most people who encounter "Cornish" chickens are seeing the industrial hybrid, not the heritage breed.

## Dorking

### Origin and History

The Dorking is one of the oldest chicken breeds in the world, with a history that stretches back to the Roman Empire. The Roman agricultural writer Columella, writing in the first century AD, described a five-toed, broad-breasted fowl in Britain that is widely believed to be the ancestor of the modern Dorking. The breed takes its name from the market town of Dorking in Surrey, England, where it has been associated for centuries.

Whether the Romans brought the Dorking's ancestors to Britain during the occupation (43-410 AD) or whether the breed was already present and merely described by Roman writers is debated. Either way, the Dorking has been a recognized English breed for at least five hundred years. It was admitted to the first APA Standard of Perfection in 1874.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Silver-Gray** (1874), **Colored** (also called Dark; 1874), **Red** (1874), and **White** (1874).

### Physical Description

The Dorking's most famous physical feature is its **five toes** — a genetic trait shared by very few other breeds (Silkies, Houdans, Faverolles, and Sultans). The extra toe is a fully formed fifth digit, set above and behind the normal rear toe, pointing upward and backward. This trait breeds true and is a disqualification if absent.

Dorkings are large, heavy birds with a distinctly rectangular body: very long, deep, and broad, carried low to the ground on short legs. The breast is full and prominent. The back is long and flat. The overall impression is one of solid, low-slung substance. The single comb (or rose comb in some varieties) is medium-large. Earlobes are red. Legs are white or pinkish-white.

The **Silver-Gray** is the most popular variety: males have a white hackle and saddle with a black breast and tail, while females are a beautiful dove-gray with a salmon breast and silver-white hackle laced in black. The **Colored** (Dark) variety shows a complex multi-colored pattern in both sexes.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9 lbs, Hen 7 lbs, Cockerel 8 lbs, Pullet 6 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Dorkings are gentle, calm, and somewhat shy. They are quiet birds that do well in confinement but also forage effectively. Their docility makes them excellent for small, peaceful flocks. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium to large white or creamy eggs per year and go broody readily.

The Dorking has historically been prized above all for its **meat quality**. Dorking meat has been considered among the finest of any chicken breed for centuries, with a texture and flavor that connoisseurs distinguish from other breeds. The broad breast, five-toed carcass, and white skin produce a table bird of exceptional quality.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Dorking carries nearly two thousand years of genetic heritage, and its loss would be an irreplaceable blow to poultry diversity. The Silver-Gray is the most available variety; the Red and White varieties are quite rare.

## Sussex

### Origin and History

The Sussex is an ancient English breed from the county of Sussex in southeastern England, where it has been raised for centuries. Like the Dorking, the Sussex has roots that may extend to the Roman period. The breed was among the most important table fowl in Victorian England, supplying the London market with high-quality poultry. The Speckled Sussex, the oldest variety, was admitted to the APA in 1914.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Speckled** (1914), **Light** (1929), **Red** (1929), and **Buff** (never formally admitted; recognized in UK).

### Physical Description

The Sussex is a large, broad-bodied bird with a rectangular shape, upright carriage, and an alert but gentle expression. The body is deep and well-muscled with a broad, flat back and full breast. The tail is carried at a moderate angle. The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are red. Legs are white or pinkish-white.

The **Speckled Sussex** is one of the most beautiful of all chicken varieties: each feather is rich mahogany-brown tipped with white, with a narrow band of iridescent green-black separating the brown and white. The mottling becomes more extensive with each molt, so older birds become increasingly striking. The **Light Sussex** — the most popular variety in the UK — has a white body with a black hackle (each feather laced with white), black tail, and black wing flights, similar to the Columbian pattern. The **Red** variety is a deep mahogany-red.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9 lbs, Hen 7 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 6 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Sussex are confident, curious, and generally friendly. The Speckled variety is known for being particularly docile and personable. They are excellent foragers and thrive in free-range conditions. Hens lay approximately 200-260 large brown or cream-tinted eggs per year, making them among the most productive of the English breeds. Sussex hens will go broody and are attentive mothers.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Sussex as **Threatened**. Despite its long history and excellent dual-purpose qualities, the Sussex is uncommon in America. The Speckled variety has the largest following; the Red variety is especially rare in the United States.

## Australorp

### Origin and History

The Australorp occupies a unique position in the English Class: it is not English at all, but Australian, developed from Orpington stock. The breed was created in Australia in the early twentieth century by crossing Black Orpingtons (imported from England) with Rhode Island Reds, Minorcas, Langshans, and White Leghorns. Where the original Orpington was bred for show qualities and a balance of meat and eggs, Australian breeders ruthlessly selected for egg production, creating a utility powerhouse.

The Australorp's finest hour came in 1922-1923, when a team of six Australorp hens set a world record of 1,857 eggs in 365 days — an average of 309.5 eggs per hen per year. This achievement made international headlines and established the Australorp as a serious competitor to the Leghorn for commercial egg production.

The breed was admitted to the APA in 1929. Its placement in the English Class reflects its Orpington ancestry rather than its Australian origin.

### Physical Description

The Australorp is a large, solidly built bird with a more streamlined profile than its Orpington ancestor. Where the Orpington is a cloud of feathers shaped like a bird, the Australorp is unmistakably a bird wearing feathers --- the plumage is closer-fitting and more functional, lying smooth against a body that is deep, broad, and well-rounded but clearly muscular beneath. You can feel the difference when you pick up both breeds: the Orpington is a pillow that happens to contain a chicken; the Australorp is a chicken that happens to be wearing silk.

And silk is the right word. The Black Australorp's plumage is one of the great visual experiences in poultry. In shade, the bird appears simply black --- handsome, solid, unremarkable. Step into the sun and the feathers ignite. Every surface explodes with a beetle-green iridescence that shifts and shimmers as the bird moves, the sheen rolling across the breast like light on oil, catching emerald and teal and, at certain angles, a flash of deep purple that vanishes the instant you try to focus on it. Hold a single breast feather to the light and tilt it: the color shifts from midnight black to bottle green to electric teal across a fraction of an inch, the iridescence created by the nanoscale architecture of the feather barbules acting as a diffraction grating. In a flock of Black Australorps on green grass in morning light, the effect is almost disorienting --- the birds seem to glow from within, each one a moving fragment of the iridescent sheen that nature otherwise reserves for hummingbird throats and beetle wings.

The single comb is medium-sized and upright, with five well-defined points. Earlobes are red. Eyes are dark brown to black, giving the face an alert, intelligent expression. Legs are dark slate to black, with white or pinkish foot bottoms --- this pale sole color is a key identification feature that distinguishes the Australorp from similarly dark-legged breeds.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Australorps are calm, friendly, and easy to handle --- but with a purposefulness that distinguishes them from the amiable loafing of the Orpington. An Australorp in the yard is *working*: head down, scratching methodically through leaf litter, turning over mulch with the focused efficiency of someone who has a job to do and intends to do it well. They forage with more intention and range than Orpingtons, covering ground, investigating corners, and generally behaving like the utility breed they were designed to be. But approach one and she will pause, regard you with those dark, attentive eyes, and step toward you with a low, conversational chirrup --- a sound that experienced keepers learn to read as "I see you, you are familiar, do you have mealworms?"

Hens lay approximately 200-280 large brown eggs per year --- significantly more than the Orpington --- and maintain good production through winter. The world record still echoes: 364 eggs in 365 days from a single hen in that legendary 1922-23 trial in Australia, the six-bird team averaging 309.5 eggs apiece. No modern heritage Australorp matches those numbers (the record-holding birds were under optimal management conditions designed to maximize output), but the genetic capacity for extraordinary production is still there, still visible in the steady, day-after-day reliability of a well-bred Australorp hen. They will go broody but less frequently than Orpingtons. They are hardy, adaptable, and one of the most reliable dual-purpose breeds available. In the PNW, their tight, glossy plumage sheds rain better than almost any other heavy breed, and their dark coloration absorbs the weak winter sunlight that the region offers, making them supremely well-adapted to the damp, gray months from October through April.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Australorps as **Recovering**. The breed has seen a strong resurgence among backyard keepers who appreciate its combination of beauty, productivity, and gentle temperament.

## Redcap

### Origin and History

The Redcap is an ancient English breed from the Derbyshire and Yorkshire regions of northern England, where it has been kept for at least two centuries. The breed's defining feature is its enormous **rose comb** — the largest of any breed, sometimes reaching 3-4 inches in width and extending well beyond the back of the head in a long, tapering spike. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1888.

### Physical Description

Redcaps are medium-sized, active birds with the dramatic oversized rose comb that gives the breed its name. The comb is covered with small, regular points or "work" and should be broad, firm, and evenly shaped. Plumage is a rich reddish-brown or mahogany with black spangles, creating a pattern reminiscent of the Speckled Sussex but darker and more uniform. Earlobes are red. Legs are slate-blue.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7.5 lbs, Hen 6 lbs, Cockerel 6 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Redcaps are active, alert, and somewhat wild in temperament. They are excellent foragers and prefer free-range conditions. Their large rose combs provide excellent frostbite resistance despite the breed's relatively light build. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium white or cream eggs per year. They are non-broody.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Redcap is one of the rarest breeds in America and increasingly difficult to find even in England. Its enormous rose comb is a unique genetic feature that would be lost if the breed disappears.

## Old English Game

### Origin and History

The Old English Game is the direct descendant of the fighting cocks that were central to English culture for centuries. Cockfighting was banned in England by the Cruelty to Animals Act of 1835, though enforcement strengthened with the 1849 amendment, and the birds bred for the pit were the ancestors of today's Old English Game. When cockfighting was banned, breeders turned to exhibition, developing the modern show type. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1938.

The Old English Game is recognized in an extraordinary number of color varieties — over 30 in the APA Standard — reflecting the diverse regional fighting strains that contributed to the breed.

### Physical Description

Old English Game are medium-small, tightly feathered, muscular birds with an upright, alert carriage. The body is compact and hard, with tight plumage that fits like a suit. The breast is broad and prominent. The tail is moderately long, carried at a jaunty angle. Exhibition birds are shown **dubbed** — with comb, wattles, and earlobes trimmed close to the head — a practice that traces to cockfighting tradition where excess comb tissue was vulnerable to injury. The natural comb is a single comb.

Color varieties include Black Breasted Red, Brown Red, Silver Duckwing, Golden Duckwing, Birchen, Red Pyle, White, Black, Blue, Spangled, and many others.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 5 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 4 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Old English Game are bold, spirited, and assertive. Males can be aggressive toward other males and should be managed carefully. They are excellent foragers, hardy in all climates, and remarkably self-sufficient. Hens lay approximately 100-160 small to medium cream or tinted eggs per year and are devoted brooders and mothers. The breed is among the most natural and unmodified of all domestic chickens, retaining many traits of its wild jungle fowl ancestors.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Old English Game as **Watch**. While the breed has a dedicated exhibition following, many individual color varieties are quite rare.

## The Cornish Cross: A Breed Apart

No discussion of the English Class is complete without addressing the industrial elephant in the room: the Cornish Cross hybrid. This is not a breed — it is a terminal cross produced by mating specialized Cornish male lines with specialized White Plymouth Rock female lines. The offspring are not bred further; they are raised to market weight and slaughtered.

The Cornish Cross grows from hatch to 5-6 lb market weight in 6-8 weeks. This is roughly twice as fast as any heritage breed. The feed conversion ratio — pounds of feed per pound of weight gain — is approximately 1.7:1, compared to 3:1 or more for heritage breeds. These numbers represent decades of intense genetic selection.

The cost of this efficiency is significant. Cornish Cross birds frequently develop leg problems, heart failure, and ascites (fluid accumulation) due to their extreme growth rate. They cannot reproduce naturally in production conditions. They cannot forage, fly, or live anything resembling a natural chicken life. If not slaughtered at market age, many will die of organ failure within a few months.

The heritage Cornish and heritage Plymouth Rock — the parent breeds — are wholly different animals. They grow at natural rates, live normal lifespans, reproduce naturally, and behave as chickens have behaved for thousands of years. The conservation of these heritage breeds is essential both for their intrinsic value and as genetic reservoirs for a meat industry that may one day need to breed for different traits — disease resistance, heat tolerance, foraging ability, longevity — that the narrow Cornish Cross gene pool cannot provide.

## The Buff Orpington: Why They Call It the Golden Retriever

The Buff Orpington deserves a moment of particular appreciation, because no other breed of chicken has done more to convince skeptical partners, reluctant neighbors, and hesitant beginners that chickens are wonderful animals worth keeping.

The comparison to golden retrievers is apt:

- **Temperament:** Buff Orpingtons are unfailingly gentle. They tolerate being picked up, carried, petted, and fussed over with the patient good humor of a therapy dog. Children can handle them safely.
- **Appearance:** The rich golden-buff plumage is simply beautiful — warm, inviting, and photogenic. A Buff Orpington on green grass is one of the most aesthetically pleasing sights in all of agriculture.
- **Reliability:** They lay well, they grow well, they brood well, they handle cold well. They are not the best at any single metric, but they are good at everything.
- **Personality:** Buff Orpingtons often develop individual personalities and recognize their keepers. They come when called, follow their people around the yard, and generally make themselves part of the family.

For anyone considering their first chickens, the Buff Orpington is rarely a wrong choice. And for experienced keepers who have tried everything from bantams to game fowl, the Buff Orpington often remains a fixture in the flock — a reliable, beautiful, gentle bird that reminds you why you started keeping chickens in the first place.

The English Class represents the golden age of practical poultry breeding. These breeds were developed by people who needed their chickens to work — to produce eggs and meat efficiently, to thrive in the damp English climate, to look handsome enough to win at shows, and to be tractable enough to manage in the small holdings and cottage farms that characterized British agriculture. That these breeds achieved all of these goals while remaining beautiful, personable, and genetically distinct is a testament to the skill and vision of their creators. Every one of them deserves to survive.
