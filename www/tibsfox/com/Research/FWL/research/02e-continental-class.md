# The Continental Class: Europe's Diverse Genius

The Continental Class of the American Poultry Association is the most geographically and genetically diverse class in the Standard of Perfection. Where the English Class draws from one island, the Mediterranean Class from one sea, and the American Class from one country, the Continental Class spans the breadth of Europe — from the polders of the Netherlands to the farmsteads of northern France, from the egg baskets of Germany to the chocolate-egg valleys of Belgium and the Netherlands. It is also the class that contains some of the most visually spectacular breeds in poultry: crested Polish with their fountains of feathers, deep chocolate-egg-laying Marans, auto-sexing Cream Legbars, and the shimmering spangled Hamburgs.

The Continental Class is where poultry breeding becomes art. These are breeds developed by cultures that valued both productivity and aesthetics, and the results are birds that lay beautifully colored eggs — chocolate brown, olive green, sky blue — while wearing some of the most intricate and dazzling plumage patterns in the chicken world.

## Polish

### Origin and History

Despite their name, Polish chickens almost certainly did not originate in Poland. The name likely derives from the Middle Dutch word "pol" meaning "head" — a reference to the breed's most spectacular feature, the enormous feathered crest. Crested fowl have been present in the Netherlands and surrounding regions for centuries, appearing in Dutch paintings as early as the fifteenth century. Some breed historians trace their ancestry to the Paduan fowl of Italy, another ancient crested type.

Polish were among the earliest ornamental breeds imported to America and were admitted to the APA Standard of Perfection in 1874. They have been a fixture of the exhibition circuit ever since, their dramatic crests making them the most visually recognizable breed at any poultry show.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes a remarkable number of Polish varieties, divided into bearded and non-bearded types. Non-bearded: **White Crested Black** (1874), **Golden** (1874), **Silver** (1874), **White** (1874), **Buff Laced** (1883). Bearded: **Golden** (1883), **Silver** (1883), **White** (1883), **Buff Laced** (1883). Additional varieties exist in bantam form.

### Physical Description

The Polish is dominated by its **crest** — an enormous pompom of feathers that erupts from a bony protuberance (vaulted skull) on top of the head. In well-bred specimens, the crest is large, round, and full, cascading evenly in all directions. Bearded varieties add a thick muff and beard of feathers beneath the beak, giving the face a fully framed appearance. The crest can impair vision, which affects the breed's behavior.

Beneath the crest, Polish are medium-sized, active birds with a moderately long body and upright carriage. The **V-shaped comb** is a small, two-pointed horn-like comb hidden beneath the crest feathers. Earlobes are white. Legs are slate-blue or gray.

The **White Crested Black** is the most iconic variety: jet-black body plumage with a brilliant white crest, creating a dramatic tuxedo effect. The **Golden** and **Silver** varieties feature golden or silver-white feathers with neat black lacing, topped with crests in matching colors.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 6 lbs, Hen 4.5 lbs, Cockerel 5 lbs, Pullet 4 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Polish are gentle, quirky, and sometimes anxious — the large crest obstructs their vision, making them startled by sudden approaches. They can seem flighty but are actually quite docile when approached calmly and handled gently. They should not be housed with aggressive breeds, as their impaired vision makes them vulnerable to bullying. Many keepers trim the crest feathers around the eyes to improve visibility.

Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium white eggs per year. They are non-broody. Polish are kept primarily for exhibition and ornamental enjoyment, though they are perfectly functional layers.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Polish as **Watch**. The White Crested Black is the most popular variety; several bearded and laced varieties are quite rare.

## Hamburg

### Origin and History

The Hamburg is one of the most elegant and ancient breeds in the Continental Class. Despite the German-sounding name, the breed was developed primarily in the Netherlands and was refined in both Holland and England. In England, local variants were known as "Moonies" (spangled) and "Pheasants" (penciled). The breed was consolidated under the Hamburg name and admitted to the APA in 1874.

Hamburgs were historically prized as "everlayers" — a term that reflected their ability to lay consistently over long periods without going broody. They were the egg production champions of northern Europe before the Leghorn's rise.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Silver Spangled** (1874), **Golden Spangled** (1874), **Silver Penciled** (1874), **Golden Penciled** (1874), **White** (1874), and **Black** (1874).

### Physical Description

Hamburgs are small to medium-sized, trim, active birds with a sprightly, alert carriage. They are among the most refined and elegant breeds in the Standard. The body is moderately long and somewhat narrow, carried upright. The tail is long, full, and carried at a jaunty upward angle — especially impressive in males, whose sickle feathers can be quite long.

The **rose comb** is broad, flat, and covered with fine, regular points, tapering to a long, slender spike at the rear. This spike should follow the line of the neck, not point upward. Earlobes are white, round, and smooth. Legs are slate-blue.

The **Silver Spangled** is the most popular and arguably the most beautiful variety: white feathers with a distinct, round black spangle at the tip of each feather, creating a precise polka-dot effect across the entire body. The **Golden Spangled** substitutes rich golden-bay for the white ground color. The **Penciled** varieties feature intricate, fine penciling — multiple thin dark lines across each feather — in either silver or golden ground colors.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 5 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 4 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Hamburgs are active, alert, and excellent fliers. They prefer free-range conditions and will roost in trees if given the chance. They are not easily tamed and tend to maintain their independence. Hens lay approximately 200-255 small to medium white eggs per year — impressive production for such a small bird. They are emphatically non-broody.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Silver Spangled remains the most available, but quality exhibition stock in any variety is uncommon.

## Campine

### Origin and History

The Campine originates from the Kempen (Campine) region of northern Belgium, where it has been kept for centuries as a productive farm fowl. The breed was imported to England in the late nineteenth century and to America shortly after. It was admitted to the APA in 1914.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes two varieties: **Silver** (1914) and **Golden** (1914).

### Physical Description

Campines are medium-small, elegant birds with tight plumage and an active carriage. They are **hen-feathered** — a distinctive genetic trait meaning males and females share the same feather pattern, unlike most chicken breeds where males display longer, more pointed hackle, saddle, and tail feathers. This gives Campine males a more rounded, feminine appearance.

The **Silver Campine** has a white head and hackle with a body covered in bold black barring (not lacing — distinct horizontal bars). The **Golden Campine** substitutes rich golden-bay for the white. The barring pattern creates a striking, precise visual effect. Earlobes are white. Legs are slate-blue. The single comb is medium-sized.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 6 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 5 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Campines are active, alert, and excellent foragers. They are not especially tame but are not aggressive. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium white eggs per year and are non-broody.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Campine is rare in America, and maintaining the hen-feathered trait along with correct barring pattern requires dedicated breeding expertise.

## Welsummer

### Origin and History

The Welsummer was developed in the early twentieth century in and around the village of Welsum in the eastern Netherlands, from local farmyard fowl crossed with Partridge Cochins, Partridge Wyandottes, Partridge Leghorns, Rhode Island Reds, and Barnevelders. The breed was standardized in the 1920s and imported to America, gaining APA recognition in 1991.

The Welsummer is famous for one thing above all others: the deep, rich, terra-cotta brown color of its eggs. Welsummer eggs are among the darkest brown eggs available from any breed, often covered with a heavy layer of brown pigment (protoporphyrin) that can be rubbed off when the egg is freshly laid. Many Welsummer eggs also display darker brown speckles and spots.

### Physical Description

Welsummers are medium-sized, upright, alert birds with a classic farmyard appearance. Males display the partridge pattern: rich golden-brown hackle and saddle, reddish-brown shoulders, and a dark beetle-green breast and tail. Females are a warm, reddish-brown with darker stippling and penciling. The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are red or a mix of red and white. Legs are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7 lbs, Hen 6 lbs, Cockerel 6 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Welsummers are friendly, active, and excellent foragers. They are among the more docile of the Continental breeds, tolerating handling well. Hens lay approximately 160-220 large dark brown eggs per year. The egg color can vary between individual hens and tends to lighten as the laying season progresses. Welsummers rarely go broody.

### Conservation Status

Not currently listed by the Livestock Conservancy, as the breed has gained popularity rapidly since its APA recognition. Welsummers are increasingly common in backyard flocks, driven largely by the appeal of their dark eggs.

## Barnevelder

### Origin and History

The Barnevelder was developed in the Barneveld region of the Netherlands in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, from crosses of local Dutch farmyard fowl with imported Asiatic breeds (Brahmas, Cochins, Langshans) and later Buff Orpingtons and possibly Indian Game. The Barneveld region was historically the Netherlands' most important poultry-producing area, and the breed was developed to meet market demand for dark brown eggs.

The Barnevelder was admitted to the APA in 1991.

### Physical Description

Barnevelders are medium-to-large, well-built birds with a moderately upright carriage. The most striking feature of the breed's most common variety is the **double-laced** plumage pattern: each feather shows a warm, reddish-brown ground color with two concentric rings of greenish-black lacing, creating an arrowhead or shield pattern across the body. This double-lacing is one of the most complex and beautiful feather patterns in all of poultry.

Males are predominantly black with a beetle-green sheen, showing the double-lacing only on the breast. The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are red. Legs are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7 lbs, Hen 6 lbs, Cockerel 6 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Barnevelders are calm, friendly, and easy to handle. They are good foragers and adapt well to both confinement and free-range conditions. Hens lay approximately 170-220 large dark brown eggs per year. The egg color is rich chocolate brown, though modern Barnevelders often produce somewhat lighter eggs than historical descriptions suggest — breeders focused on plumage quality for exhibition have sometimes lost intensity in egg color, a reminder that maintaining production traits requires active selection.

### Conservation Status

Not currently on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. Barnevelders are uncommon in America but gaining popularity, particularly among keepers seeking dark-egg-laying breeds.

## Marans

### Origin and History

The Marans (always spelled with the final "s," even in singular) was developed in and around the port town of Marans in western France, near La Rochelle. The breed descends from local French farmyard fowl crossed with fighting game birds brought by English sailors — the port's proximity to England facilitated centuries of poultry exchange. The breed was standardized in France in the early twentieth century.

The Marans is celebrated worldwide for laying the **darkest brown eggs** of any breed. Top Marans hens produce eggs of such deep, rich chocolate brown that they appear almost mahogany. In France, Marans eggs are graded on a numbered darkness scale, with exhibition birds expected to produce eggs at level 4 or higher (on a 1-9 scale). The pigment is applied as a coating in the shell gland, and a freshly laid Marans egg can literally stain your fingers brown.

The Marans gained APA recognition in 2011 — one of the more recent admissions.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Black Copper** (2011), **Wheaten** (2011), and additional varieties are recognized elsewhere. The **Cuckoo** variety is popular but was not among the initial APA admissions.

### Physical Description

Marans are large, well-built dual-purpose birds with a notably muscular, robust appearance. The French standard calls for lightly feathered shanks — a trait that distinguishes French-type Marans from English-type (clean-legged) Marans, which are a separate breeding tradition. The APA standard follows the French type with feathered shanks.

The **Black Copper** is the most popular and visually striking variety: males have brilliant copper-red hackle and saddle feathers contrasting with a lustrous black body, while females are predominantly black with copper coloring on the hackle and breast. The **Wheaten** variety features males similar to the Black Copper male pattern, with females showing a warm, golden-wheat body color. The **Cuckoo** variety (not yet APA recognized but widely kept) displays the classic barred or cuckoo pattern of irregular light and dark bars.

The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are red. Legs are slate or pink, lightly feathered on the outer shanks.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Marans are active, hardy, and good foragers. They are generally calm but maintain more independence than breeds like the Orpington. Hens lay approximately 150-210 large to extra-large dark brown eggs per year. The darkness of the egg color is a major selection criterion among Marans breeders, and the best hens are prized as much for their egg color as for any physical trait. Marans rarely go broody.

### Conservation Status

Not listed by the Livestock Conservancy. The breed is rapidly gaining popularity in America, driven almost entirely by the appeal of its chocolate-brown eggs. Quality Black Copper Marans from proven dark-egg lines command premium prices.

## Cream Legbar

### Origin and History

The Cream Legbar is one of the most scientifically interesting breeds in all of poultry. It was developed at the University of Cambridge in England in the 1930s by Reginald Punnett (of Punnett Square fame in genetics) and Michael Pease. The breed was created by crossing Brown Leghorns, Barred Plymouth Rocks, and Araucanas (the South American blue-egg-laying breed). Punnett's goal was to create an **auto-sexing** breed — one in which male and female chicks can be distinguished at hatch by their down color, without vent sexing or feather sexing.

The result was brilliant: Cream Legbar cockerel chicks hatch with a light, creamy down and a less-defined head stripe, while pullet chicks hatch with a darker, more defined brown stripe. This auto-sexing trait is genetically linked to the barring gene, which operates differently in males (two copies) and females (one copy).

The Cream Legbar also inherited blue-egg-laying genetics from its Araucana ancestry. It is the only standardized auto-sexing breed that lays blue eggs.

The Cream Legbar has not yet been admitted to the APA Standard of Perfection but is recognized by the British Poultry Standards and is widely kept and shown in America under the Cream Legbar Club of North America's guidelines.

### Physical Description

Cream Legbars are medium-sized, active, upright birds with a small **crest** — a modest tuft of feathers on the back of the head, inherited from the Araucana. Males show a cream-colored hackle and saddle barred with gray, a gray-barred body, and a moderately long tail. Females are a warm, silver-gray with distinct cream-and-gray barring. The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are cream or white. Legs are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7.5 lbs, Hen 5.5 lbs (British Standard).

### Temperament and Production

Cream Legbars are active, alert, and excellent foragers. They are livelier than many dual-purpose breeds but less flighty than Leghorns. Hens lay approximately 180-230 medium blue eggs per year. The blue color ranges from pale sky blue to a deeper robin's-egg blue. They rarely go broody.

### Auto-Sexing: A Genetic Innovation

The auto-sexing trait deserves emphasis because it solved a genuine practical problem. In most breeds, determining the sex of day-old chicks requires either vent sexing (a skilled manual technique) or waiting weeks for secondary sex characteristics to appear. Auto-sexing breeds like the Cream Legbar allow instant sexing at hatch based on down color — a practical advantage for breeders and small-flock keepers who want hens for eggs.

Other auto-sexing breeds have been created using the same principle (crossing a barred breed with a non-barred breed to create sex-linked barring), including the Bielefelder (a German auto-sexing breed from Marans and other ancestry), the Rhodebar, and the Legbar's siblings the Gold Legbar and Silver Legbar.

## Lakenvelder

### Origin and History

The Lakenvelder is a striking, ancient breed from the Nordrhein-Westfalen region of Germany and the adjacent Lakerveld area of the Netherlands. The breed's name translates roughly to "shadow on a sheet" — a poetic description of its distinctive color pattern. It was admitted to the APA in 1939.

### Physical Description

The Lakenvelder's plumage pattern is simple and dramatic: a pure white body with solid black hackle (neck) and tail, creating a stark black-white-black tricolor effect. The transition between white and black should be clean and well-defined, without grayish intermixing. Males show the same pattern with more extensive black in the hackle, saddle, and tail. The single comb is medium-sized. Earlobes are white. Legs are slate-blue.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 5 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 4 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Lakenvelders are active, alert, and can be flighty. They are excellent foragers and prefer free-range conditions. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium white or cream-tinted eggs per year. They rarely go broody.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Lakenvelder is rare in America and requires careful breeding to maintain the clean color separation that defines the breed.

## Faverolles

### Origin and History

The Faverolles was developed in the villages of Faverolles and Houdan in northern France in the 1860s through 1880s, from crosses of Houdan, Brahma, Dorking, Malines, and Cochin stock. The breed was intended as a superior winter layer and table bird for the Parisian market. It was admitted to the APA in 1914.

The Faverolles carries an unusual combination of traits: **five toes** (from Dorking and Houdan ancestry), **feathered shanks** (from Brahma/Cochin ancestry), a **beard and muffles** (from Houdan ancestry), and a **single comb**. This unique combination of features makes the Faverolles instantly recognizable.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Salmon** (1914) and **White** (1914). The Salmon variety is by far the more popular.

### Physical Description

Faverolles are large, broad, deep-bodied birds with a distinctive bearded and muffled face — thick feathering covers the cheeks and throat, giving the face a round, owlish appearance. The body is trapezoid-shaped: broad across the back and shoulders, narrowing toward the tail. Five toes are present on each foot. Shanks are feathered, though less heavily than Cochins.

The **Salmon Faverolles** displays dramatic sexual dimorphism. Males have a black beard, breast, belly, and tail, with rich mahogany shoulders and a straw-colored hackle and saddle. Females — the source of the "salmon" name — have a cream breast with a salmon-pink cast, a brown back with darker penciling, and a cream-colored beard and muffles. The female Salmon Faverolles is one of the subtlest and most beautiful birds in the Continental Class.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Faverolles are famously gentle, sweet-tempered, and comically awkward. They are among the most docile and people-friendly breeds, often described as clownish in their behavior. They tolerate confinement well but can be bullied by more assertive breeds due to their extreme docility. Hens lay approximately 180-240 medium to large light brown or cream-tinted eggs per year and maintain good winter production. They will go broody and make devoted mothers.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Salmon variety has gained popularity among backyard keepers, but the White variety is very rare. Quality exhibition stock with correct five toes, feathered shanks, and proper beard and muffles requires dedicated breeding.

## The Chocolate Egg Spectrum

One of the Continental Class's greatest contributions to the poultry world is the **dark brown egg**. Three breeds in particular — Marans, Welsummers, and Barnevelders — are celebrated for eggs that range from deep terra cotta to genuine chocolate brown.

### The Science of Dark Eggs

All chicken eggs start as white or blue/green in the shell gland (uterus). Brown pigment — **protoporphyrin IX** — is deposited as a coating on the surface of the shell during the last 3-5 hours before laying. The depth of brown color depends on the amount and concentration of this pigment.

In most brown-egg breeds, the pigment layer is thin enough that the interior of the shell appears white. In the darkest Marans eggs, the pigment layer is thick enough that it can be scratched or rubbed off, leaving a lighter shell underneath. This surface coating also makes very dark Marans eggs feel slightly different to the touch — smoother or more matte than lighter-pigmented eggs.

### Egg Color Rankings

Among commonly available breeds producing dark brown eggs:

1. **Marans** (Black Copper) — the darkest, ranging from deep chocolate to near-mahogany in the best lines
2. **Welsummer** — deep terra cotta to reddish-brown, often speckled
3. **Barnevelder** — medium chocolate brown, though modern birds often produce lighter eggs than historical standards
4. **Penedesenca** (not APA recognized) — very dark brown, similar to Marans

Egg color fades within individual hens as the laying season progresses — the first eggs of the season are darkest. Young hens (first year of lay) typically produce darker eggs than older hens. Selecting breeding stock based on egg color requires evaluating eggs from early in the laying cycle.

## Blue and Green Eggs: The Araucana Legacy

The Cream Legbar's blue eggs introduce another dimension of egg color genetics. Blue and green egg color is caused by **biliverdin** and **zinc chelate of biliverdin** deposited throughout the shell (not just on the surface), meaning the shell is blue all the way through. This trait originates from the Araucana breed of South America.

When blue-egg genetics combine with brown-egg genetics, the result is **olive or green eggs** — the blue shell color plus the brown surface coating produces a composite that appears green. The specific shade varies from sage to olive to dark olive depending on the intensity of both the blue and brown components.

The Cream Legbar, carrying Araucana blue-egg genetics but not heavy brown-pigment genetics, produces a relatively clean blue. Crossing Cream Legbars with dark-brown-egg breeds like Marans produces "olive eggers" — highly sought after by backyard keepers who prize a colorful egg basket.

The Continental Class is where utility meets artistry. These breeds lay eggs in colors that no supermarket shelf has ever seen — blues, greens, and browns so dark they look hand-painted. They wear plumage patterns of extraordinary complexity, from the mathematical precision of Hamburg spangling to the double-laced beauty of the Barnevelder. And they carry within their genetics the cumulative breeding wisdom of centuries of European poultry culture: Dutch precision, French practicality, Belgian innovation, and German thoroughness. In a world that has largely standardized on white and brown supermarket eggs laid by anonymous hybrids, the Continental breeds are a reminder that egg production can be beautiful, that chickens can be works of art, and that diversity is not a luxury but a treasure.
