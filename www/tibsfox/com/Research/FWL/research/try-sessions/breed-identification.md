# Try Session: Identify That Chicken!

*An interactive breed identification exercise for aspiring poultry enthusiasts*

---

## How This Works

Below are 20 breed identification challenges. Each challenge describes the physical characteristics of a specific chicken breed --- comb type, feather color and pattern, leg color, body shape, overall size, and any distinguishing features. Your job is to read the description carefully and identify the breed before checking the answer.

Some of these are straightforward if you know the breed well. Others are tricky, because several breeds share similar features and the difference comes down to a detail: a leg color, a comb shape, a feather structure. This is exactly the skill that poultry judges develop over years of experience, and it starts with careful observation.

**Rules:**
1. Read the full description before guessing.
2. Write down your answer before scrolling to the answer key.
3. For each one you get wrong, follow the link to the breed profile and study what you missed.
4. Scoring: 18-20 correct = expert; 14-17 = advanced; 10-13 = intermediate; below 10 = keep studying!

**Key terminology refresher:**
- **Single comb:** The classic upright serrated comb (like a rooster on a weather vane)
- **Rose comb:** A flat, wide comb covered with small bumps, ending in a spike
- **Pea comb:** Three low, parallel ridges running front to back
- **Walnut comb:** A lumpy, rounded comb resembling a walnut shell (rose x pea genetics)
- **Hackle feathers:** Long feathers on the neck
- **Saddle feathers:** Long feathers on the back near the tail (roosters)
- **Lacing:** A border of contrasting color around each feather
- **Barring:** Regular stripes of alternating light and dark across each feather
- **Shanks:** The scaled portion of the leg between the hock and the foot

## The Challenges

### Challenge 1

A large, heavy-bodied hen. The plumage is a solid, warm golden buff across the entire body --- hackle, breast, back, wings, and tail are all the same rich buff color with no contrasting markings. The feathers are broad, full, and soft, giving the bird a round, almost pillow-like silhouette. The comb is single, medium-sized, and bright red. Legs and feet are pinkish-white. The bird is famously calm and docile, often described as a "lap chicken." Hens weigh around 8 pounds.

### Challenge 2

A medium-sized hen with striking feather pattern. Each body feather is silvery white with a sharply defined border of black lacing, creating a geometric pattern that looks almost hand-painted. The hackle feathers are also silver with black edging. The comb is rose-type, sitting close to the head. Legs are yellow. The bird has a broad, well-rounded body with a moderately long back. Hens weigh about 6.5 pounds.

---

### Challenge 3

A very large, upright hen with a distinctive pea comb. The plumage is light buff or very pale gold with darker hackle feathering. The most striking feature is heavy feathering on the legs and feet --- the shanks and outer toes are covered with feathers, making the bird appear to be wearing boots. The body is deep, broad, and tall, giving the bird a commanding presence. Hens weigh 9.5 pounds or more. The bird walks with a slow, stately gait.

---

### Challenge 4

A small to medium-sized hen with an extraordinary appearance. The plumage is white (though other colors exist), but the feathers lack normal barbicels, so each feather is fluffy and hair-like rather than smooth, giving the bird the appearance of being covered in fur or silk. The skin, comb, wattles, face, and bones are all dark blue-black. The bird has five toes instead of the usual four. A small walnut comb. Legs are short, dark-skinned, and feathered. The bird weighs only 2-3 pounds.

### Challenge 5

A medium to large hen with a distinctive dark red-brown plumage. The body color is a rich, deep mahogany red, sometimes described as the color of a dark brick. The tail is black, and the hackle may show some black lacing. The body is rectangular and brick-shaped --- longer than it is tall, with a flat back and broad breast. The comb is single. Legs are yellow, sometimes with a reddish tinge. Hens weigh about 6.5 pounds and are prolific layers of large brown eggs.

### Challenge 6

A medium-sized, upright hen with a sleek, athletic build. The plumage is pure white. The body is lean and angular, carried nearly upright, with a long neck and prominent, large single comb (which flops to one side in hens). The earlobes are white --- a distinctive feature. Legs are yellow. The bird is active, flighty, and alert, moving with quick, nervous energy. Hens weigh about 4.5 pounds and are legendary egg producers, laying 280-320 large white eggs per year.

---

### Challenge 7

A medium-sized hen with very dark plumage. The feathers appear pure black at first glance, but in sunlight they show an intense beetle-green sheen across the entire body. The bird has a single comb, bright red face, and dark eyes. Legs are dark slate to black. The body is compact and well-rounded. Hens weigh about 6.5 pounds. The breed holds a world record for egg production (364 eggs in 365 days, set in 1922-23). The eggs are medium to large and brown.

---

### Challenge 8

A medium-sized hen with a broad, well-muscled body. The plumage is a complex pattern of dark brown and black, with each feather showing a dark shaft stripe and stippled brown edges. Hens often show a darker breast with lighter brown on the back. The comb is single. Legs are pinkish-white with a slight slate tinge. The breed is famous for producing very dark brown eggs --- the darkest of any standard breed, sometimes appearing almost chocolate-colored. Hens weigh about 6.5 pounds.

---

### Challenge 9

A large, broad-bodied hen. The plumage is a regular pattern of sharply defined black and white bars across every feather on the body, creating a striking "zebra-stripe" effect visible from a distance. The bars are crisp, even, and parallel. The comb is single and bright red. Legs are yellow. The bird has a long, broad back and deep body. Hens weigh about 7.5 pounds. The breed is one of the oldest in America, developed in Massachusetts in the mid-nineteenth century.

---

### Challenge 10

A medium-sized hen with a distinctive appearance. The plumage can vary widely --- she may be brown, blue, wheaten, or splash-colored, and she often has mottled or mixed coloring because the breed does not breed true for color. The key identifying feature: she lays blue or green-tinted eggs. She has a pea comb, often has muffs and a beard (fluffy cheek feathers), but her leg color varies (green, slate, or yellow). She may or may not have a tail tuft. Hens weigh about 5 pounds. The breed is technically not an APA-recognized breed.

---

### Challenge 11

A medium-sized hen with a pea comb and a distinctive slate-blue or blue-gray leg color. The plumage comes in recognized colors including black, blue, blue wheaten, brown red, silver, wheaten, and white. The bird has muffs and a beard --- prominent fluffy feathers on the cheeks and chin. The tail is present and full. Earlobes are small and red. The breed lays blue eggs (always blue, never green). Hens weigh about 5.5 pounds. Often confused with the previous challenge, but this is a recognized APA breed with specific standards.

---

### Challenge 12

A medium-sized hen with a truly extraordinary crest. A large, spherical pom-pom of feathers erupts from the top of the head, often so voluminous that it partially obscures the bird's vision. Beneath the crest, a small V-shaped comb may be present. The plumage comes in many colors --- white-crested black is the most iconic variety, with a pure black body and a bright white crest. Legs are slate-blue. The bird weighs about 4.5 pounds. Originally from the Netherlands, not Poland.

---

### Challenge 13

A true bantam (no large fowl counterpart) with a rose comb and extraordinarily precise feather pattern. Each feather has a ground color of gold or silver with a sharply defined black border of lacing. The lacing is so exact and uniform that the bird appears to be wearing an intricately patterned garment. The bird is tiny --- roosters weigh only 22 ounces, hens 20 ounces. Legs are slate-blue. The tail is carried at a prominent upward angle. Developed in early nineteenth-century England by Sir John Saunders Sebright.

---

### Challenge 14

A heavy, muscular, broad-breasted bird. The body is extremely wide and deep, carried low to the ground, with thick, powerful legs set wide apart. The plumage in the most common variety is dark --- a rich dark red-brown on the hackle and back with a dark breast. The comb is pea-type. Legs are yellow and thick. The bird is compact and heavy for its size, with a remarkably broad breast. Hens weigh about 8 pounds. The breed forms half of the genetic cross that produces virtually all commercial broiler chickens worldwide.

---

### Challenge 15

A large, graceful hen with a speckled plumage pattern. The most common variety has a base color of rich mahogany-brown with each feather tipped in a white spangle separated from the ground color by a bar of iridescent greenish-black. This three-part pattern --- brown base, black bar, white tip --- gives the bird a spotted appearance that becomes more pronounced with each molt. The comb is single. Legs are pinkish-white with light mottling. Hens weigh about 7 pounds. Developed in Sussex, England.

---

### Challenge 16

A very large, heavily feathered hen. The plumage is profuse, with extremely long, soft feathers that extend down the legs and cover the feet completely, creating the impression that the bird is wearing a feathered ball gown. The body is round, deep, and low to the ground. The most common color varieties are buff, black, white, and partridge. The comb is single. The bird is famously calm and docile, often going broody. Hens weigh about 8.5 pounds. The breed was central to the "Hen Fever" of the 1850s.

---

### Challenge 17

A medium-sized hen with a unique combination of features. The plumage in the most common variety (Salmon) shows a striking difference between sexes: hens are creamy white on the breast with a salmon-pink wash on the hackle and back. The bird has a beard and muffs, feathered legs, and --- most distinctively --- five toes. The comb is single and small. Legs are pinkish-white and feathered. Hens weigh about 6.5 pounds. Originated in the town of Faverolles in north-central France.

---

### Challenge 18

A medium-sized hen with a warm, partridge-like plumage. The hackle feathers are golden-brown with darker striping. The breast is a rich dark red-brown stippled with darker markings. The back shows a characteristic pattern of lighter brown with darker shaft streaks. The overall impression is of a bird in autumn colors. The comb is single. Legs are yellow. The breed lays dark brown eggs with a distinctive dark speckling on the shell surface --- the "bloom" that gives the eggs a terracotta appearance. Hens weigh about 6 pounds.

---

### Challenge 19

A true bantam (no standard-sized counterpart) with a dramatically upswept tail that is carried high and forward, sometimes reaching over the head. The bird is tiny, weighing only about 1 pound for a hen. The body is short, the breast is prominent, and the legs are short, clean (unfeathered), and may be yellow or willow-colored. Comes in many color varieties, including black-tailed white, black-tailed buff, and others. The overall silhouette is U-shaped when viewed from the side. One of the most ancient of all chicken breeds.

---

### Challenge 20

A medium-sized hen with a distinctive auto-sexing trait: day-old chicks can be sexed by color. The plumage in hens is silver-grey with barring, similar to a Barred Rock but lighter. The comb is a single comb. The key distinguishing features are the crest (a small tuft of feathers on the head) and the egg color: the breed lays blue to blue-green eggs, unusual for a barring breed. Legs are yellow. Hens weigh about 5.5 pounds. Developed at the Cream Legbar Breeding Programme at Cambridge University, England, in the early twentieth century.

## Answer Key

**Do not read below this line until you have written down all 20 answers.**

---

### Challenge 1: Buff Orpington

The solid buff color, pillow-like body shape, pinkish-white legs, docile temperament, and single comb are diagnostic. The Orpington was developed by William Cook of Orpington, Kent, in the 1880s. The Buff variety, introduced in 1894, became the most popular and remains the backyard favorite today. What distinguishes the Buff Orpington from other buff-colored breeds (like the Buff Cochin or Buff Brahma) is the combination of clean legs, single comb, and that characteristic round, fluffy body.

*Further reading: [FWL-02d English Class --- Orpington]*

---

### Challenge 2: Silver Laced Wyandotte

The silver-and-black lacing pattern, rose comb, and yellow legs are the signature combination. The Wyandotte was developed in New York state in the 1870s, with the Silver Laced variety as the original. The rose comb was intentionally bred in for cold hardiness --- it resists frostbite better than a single comb. The precision of the lacing is the primary show criterion: each feather should show a clean, even black border against the silver ground.

*Further reading: [FWL-02a American Class --- Wyandotte]; [FWL breeds/wyandotte]*

---

### Challenge 3: Light Brahma

The pea comb, heavily feathered legs and feet, enormous size, and pale buff/gold plumage with darker hackle identify the Light Brahma. Brahmas are the gentle giants of the chicken world --- some roosters exceed 12 pounds. The breed was developed in America from large feather-legged birds imported from China in the 1840s-50s, during the "Hen Fever." The pea comb is a key distinguishing feature from the Cochin (which has a single comb and even more profuse feathering).

*Further reading: [FWL-02b Asiatic Class --- Brahma]; [FWL breeds/brahma]*

---

### Challenge 4: Silkie

There is no mistaking a Silkie. The hair-like feathers (caused by a recessive gene affecting feather barbicels), blue-black skin and bones, five toes, walnut comb, and small size are unique. Silkies are one of the oldest documented chicken breeds, described by Marco Polo in the thirteenth century. The melanotic fibromelanosis that darkens their skin, connective tissue, and bones is caused by a complex rearrangement on chromosome 20 involving the EDN3 gene.

*Further reading: [FWL-02f Ornamental Bantams --- Silkie]; [FWL breeds/silkie]; [FWL-04 Feather Genetics]*

---

### Challenge 5: Rhode Island Red

The deep mahogany-red color, black tail, rectangular brick-shaped body, single comb, and yellow legs are classic Rhode Island Red. Developed in Rhode Island and Massachusetts in the late 1800s as a dual-purpose utility bird, the RIR became the foundation of the commercial brown egg industry. The richness of the red color is a key show criterion --- exhibition birds should be a deep, even mahogany, not the lighter rust-red common in hatchery stock.

*Further reading: [FWL-02a American Class --- Rhode Island Red]*

---

### Challenge 6: White Leghorn

The lean, upright body, large floppy single comb, white earlobes, yellow legs, and pure white plumage identify the Leghorn. White earlobes are strongly correlated with white egg production across breeds. The Leghorn is the foundation of the modern commercial white egg industry --- virtually every white egg in American supermarkets comes from a Leghorn or Leghorn-derived hybrid. The breed originated in Italy (the name derives from the port of Livorno) and was refined for production in America and Denmark.

*Further reading: [FWL-02c Mediterranean Class --- Leghorn]; [FWL breeds/orpington] (for comparison)*

---

### Challenge 7: Black Australorp

The beetle-green sheen on black plumage, dark legs, single comb, and extraordinary egg-laying ability are diagnostic. The Australorp was developed in Australia from Black Orpingtons imported from England, selectively bred for egg production rather than show qualities. The world record of 364 eggs in 365 days (set by a team of six Australorps at a government trial in Geelong, Victoria, in 1922-23) remains one of the most cited figures in poultry history, though individual birds in the trial averaged 309.5 eggs.

*Further reading: [FWL-02d English Class] (Orpington parentage); [FWL breeds/australorp]*

---

### Challenge 8: Marans

The dark brown eggs, stippled plumage pattern, pinkish-white legs (in the clean-legged varieties recognized by the APA), and medium body size point to the Marans. Originally from the French port town of Marans, the breed is prized above all for its egg color. The darkest Marans eggs are rated 7-9 on the Marans egg color scale and appear almost chocolate. The French standard calls for lightly feathered legs, while the APA-recognized variety (Black Copper) has clean legs --- a persistent point of debate.

*Further reading: [FWL-02e Continental Class --- Marans]; [FWL breeds/marans]; [FWL-05 Egg Science]*

---

### Challenge 9: Barred Plymouth Rock

The sharply defined black-and-white barring, single comb, yellow legs, and large body size are classic Barred Rock. Barring is a sex-linked trait --- roosters carry two copies of the barring gene (B/B) and appear lighter than hens (B/w), which is why experienced breeders can sometimes sex Barred Rock chicks by the darkness of their down. The Plymouth Rock was developed in Massachusetts in the 1860s-70s and became the most important American dual-purpose breed.

*Further reading: [FWL-02a American Class --- Plymouth Rock]; [FWL breeds/plymouth-rock]; [FWL-04 Feather Genetics]*

---

### Challenge 10: Easter Egger

The variable appearance, blue or green eggs, pea comb, and the fact that it is not an APA-recognized breed all point to the Easter Egger. EEs are hybrid birds carrying the blue egg gene (from Araucana or Ameraucana ancestry) crossed with other breeds. Because they are mixed heritage, they do not breed true for any particular color, pattern, or body type. The term "Easter Egger" is an informal designation used by poultry enthusiasts; hatcheries sometimes (incorrectly) sell them as "Ameraucanas."

*Further reading: [FWL breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger]; [FWL-05 Egg Science]*

---

### Challenge 11: Ameraucana

The pea comb, muffs and beard, slate-blue legs, blue eggs, and APA recognition with specific color varieties distinguish the Ameraucana from the Easter Egger. The Ameraucana was developed in America from Araucana stock in the 1970s, standardized to breed true for blue egg color while eliminating the lethal tufted gene found in Araucanas. The slate-blue leg color and consistent blue (never green) egg color are key diagnostic features separating it from Easter Eggers.

*Further reading: [FWL breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger]; [FWL-02a American Class]*

---

### Challenge 12: Polish

The enormous crest, V-comb, and slate-blue legs identify the Polish. Despite the name, the breed almost certainly originated in the Netherlands, where it was depicted in Dutch paintings from the fifteenth century onward. The name may derive from the Polish word "pol" (large) or from the resemblance of the crest to the plumed caps of Polish soldiers. The White Crested Black variety is the most recognizable, but many color varieties exist including Golden Laced, Silver Laced, and Buff Laced.

*Further reading: [FWL-02e Continental Class --- Polish]; [FWL-02f Ornamental Bantams]*

---

### Challenge 13: Sebright

The precise lacing on every feather, tiny size, rose comb, slate-blue legs, and upswept tail are unmistakable. The Sebright is one of the most challenging breeds to raise --- roosters are hen-feathered (they lack the pointed hackle, saddle, and sickle feathers normal to roosters) due to a unique aromatase gene expression. This makes them genetically fascinating and visually striking. Sebrights are true bantams with no large fowl counterpart.

*Further reading: [FWL-02f Ornamental Bantams --- Sebright]; [FWL-04 Feather Genetics]*

---

### Challenge 14: Cornish (Indian Game)

The extremely broad breast, pea comb, thick yellow legs, compact heavy body, and low stance identify the Cornish. Originally called the Indian Game in England, where it was developed from Aseel (Indian fighting bird) crosses, the Cornish provides the "broad breast" genetics in modern commercial broiler chickens. A Cornish rooster crossed with a White Plymouth Rock hen produces the Cornish Cross --- the bird that constitutes virtually all commercially produced chicken meat worldwide.

*Further reading: [FWL-02d English Class --- Cornish]*

---

### Challenge 15: Speckled Sussex

The three-layered feather pattern (mahogany base, black bar, white tip), single comb, pinkish-white legs, and large graceful body are diagnostic of the Speckled Sussex. The speckled pattern becomes more dramatic with age, as each successive molt adds slightly more white tipping. A three-year-old Speckled Sussex is more strikingly marked than a pullet. The Sussex breed originated in the historical county of Sussex, England, and comes in many varieties, but the Speckled is the most popular.

*Further reading: [FWL-02d English Class --- Sussex]*

---

### Challenge 16: Cochin

The enormous, profuse feathering that covers the legs and feet completely, round body shape, single comb (not pea), extremely calm temperament, and tendency to go broody identify the Cochin. The key distinction from the Brahma is the comb: Cochins have a single comb, Brahmas have a pea comb. Cochins also have even more voluminous feathering and a rounder, lower body shape. The breed was instrumental in the "Hen Fever" of the 1850s, when exotic Asian chickens drove a speculative frenzy in America and England.

*Further reading: [FWL-02b Asiatic Class --- Cochin]; [FWL breeds/brahma] (for comparison)*

---

### Challenge 17: Faverolles

The combination of five toes, feathered legs, beard and muffs, single comb, and the Salmon color pattern is unique to the Faverolles. No other standard breed has all five of these features together. The Salmon variety shows dramatic sexual dimorphism: roosters are mainly black and straw-colored with a dark breast, while hens show the creamy-salmon pattern described. The fifth toe is inherited as a dominant trait, likely introgressed from Dorking or Houdan ancestry during the breed's development.

*Further reading: [FWL-02e Continental Class --- Faverolles]*

---

### Challenge 18: Welsummer

The partridge-like plumage and dark brown eggs with visible speckling are the key identifiers. The speckled bloom on Welsummer eggs is a surface coating applied just before laying --- it can be wiped off with a wet cloth, revealing a lighter shell underneath. This is a different mechanism from the Marans' pigment, which is applied earlier and penetrates deeper. The Welsummer was developed in the Netherlands near the village of Welsum and is the breed most commonly cited as the inspiration for the traditional speckled brown egg on cereal boxes and egg cartons.

*Further reading: [FWL-02e Continental Class --- Welsummer]; [FWL-05 Egg Science]*

---

### Challenge 19: Japanese Bantam (Chabo)

The dramatically upswept tail carried forward over the body, tiny size, short legs, and U-shaped profile are diagnostic. The Japanese Bantam is one of the oldest bantam breeds, documented in Japanese art for over 400 years. The breed carries a gene for short legs (creeper gene) that is homozygous lethal --- breeding two short-legged birds together results in approximately 25% of embryos dying. This is one of the most well-documented examples of a lethal allele maintained in a domestic animal population by breeders selecting for the heterozygous phenotype.

*Further reading: [FWL-02f Ornamental Bantams --- Japanese Bantam]; [FWL-04 Feather Genetics]*

---

### Challenge 20: Cream Legbar

The auto-sexing ability, blue-green eggs, small crest, barred plumage, and single comb identify the Cream Legbar. Auto-sexing means that male and female chicks are visually distinct at hatch based on down color, unlike sex-linked crosses which only work in the F1 generation. The Cream Legbar was developed by Reginald Punnett (yes, the Punnett square inventor) and Michael Pease at Cambridge University by crossing Brown Leghorns, Barred Plymouth Rocks, and Araucanas. It combines the production genetics of the Leghorn, the barring gene for auto-sexing, and the blue egg gene from the Araucana.

*Further reading: [FWL breeds/cream-legbar]; [FWL-04 Feather Genetics]; [FWL-05 Egg Science]*

## Common Confusion Points

Several breeds in this exercise are frequently confused with each other. Here are the key distinctions to study:

**Brahma vs. Cochin:** Both are large, feather-legged Asiatic breeds. Brahma has a pea comb; Cochin has a single comb. Brahma is taller and more upright; Cochin is rounder and lower.

**Ameraucana vs. Easter Egger:** Ameraucanas are a standardized breed with specific color varieties, consistent slate-blue legs, and always-blue eggs. Easter Eggers are mixed-heritage birds with variable appearance that lay blue or green eggs. If it came from a hatchery labeled "Ameraucana" but has green legs or lays green eggs, it is almost certainly an Easter Egger.

**Marans vs. Welsummer:** Both lay dark brown eggs. Marans eggs are generally darker and smoother; Welsummer eggs have visible speckling. Marans have pinkish-white legs (APA varieties); Welsummers have yellow legs. The plumage patterns differ significantly when compared side by side.

**Barred Rock vs. Cream Legbar:** Both have barred plumage, but Cream Legbars are smaller, have a crest, and lay blue-green eggs. Barred Rocks are larger, have no crest, and lay brown eggs.

**Silkie vs. everything else:** Silkies are genuinely unique. If the feathers look like hair, the skin is blue-black, and there are five toes, it is a Silkie. Nothing else looks like a Silkie.
