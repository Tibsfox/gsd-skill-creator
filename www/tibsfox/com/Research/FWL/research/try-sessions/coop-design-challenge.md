# Try Session: Design Your Dream Coop

*A design exercise in poultry architecture --- requirements analysis, sketching, and creative expression for the PNW climate*

---

## The Design Challenge

Building a chicken coop is a construction project. Designing one is an intellectual exercise --- and this try session is about the design, not the build. (Construction belongs to the trades curriculum and to WDC territory. Here, we think on paper.)

A well-designed coop solves a surprisingly complex set of simultaneous constraints: it must protect birds from predators that can chew through wood, dig under walls, and manipulate latches [RCN-02]. It must ventilate without creating drafts at roost level. It must shed the relentless PNW rain while keeping the interior dry. It must provide enough space per bird to prevent stress, feather-picking, and disease, but not so much unheated volume that the birds cannot maintain warmth in winter. It must allow the keeper to collect eggs, clean bedding, fill feeders, and inspect birds without contorting into positions that guarantee a back injury by Year Two.

All of this is solvable. The fun part is that once the engineering constraints are met, you can make it look like anything you want. A coop can be a miniature craftsman cottage. A mid-century modern box with a flat green roof. A PNW cabin with cedar shingle siding. The birds do not care about aesthetics. You do. And since you will look at this structure from your kitchen window every morning for the next decade, it might as well make you happy.

---

## Part 1: Requirements Analysis

Before sketching a single line, define what your coop must do. This is requirements analysis --- the same process used in software engineering, architecture, and any design discipline. A design without requirements is decoration. A design that meets its requirements is engineering.

### Flock Size

The single most important number in coop design is how many birds it will house. Everything else derives from this.

**Interior space requirement: 4 square feet per bird.** This is the widely accepted minimum for standard-sized breeds (Orpingtons, Plymouth Rocks, Wyandottes, Australorps). Bantam breeds (Silkies, Sebrights, Japanese Bantams) can manage with 2-3 square feet per bird. Large breeds (Brahmas, Jersey Giants) benefit from 5+ square feet per bird.

**Run space requirement: 10 square feet per bird minimum.** More is always better. A truly free-range flock on a large property may not need a dedicated run, but in the PNW suburbs where most backyard flocks live, a secure run is essential for the hours when the birds are outside the coop but you are not watching them.

**Design for the flock you will have in two years, not the flock you have today.** Chicken math is real. The person who starts with four hens has six within a year. Design for 6-8 birds even if you are starting with 4. It is vastly easier to build a slightly larger coop from the start than to retrofit or build a second one.

**For this exercise, we will design for 6 standard-sized hens.** This requires:
- Interior: 6 x 4 = 24 square feet minimum (a 4x6 foot coop is the classic size)
- Run: 6 x 10 = 60 square feet minimum (an 8x8 or 6x10 foot run)

### PNW Climate Requirements

The Pacific Northwest west of the Cascades presents a specific set of climate challenges that drive coop design decisions:

**Rain.** The PNW averages 37-50 inches of rain per year in the lowlands, with some areas (Olympic Peninsula, Coast Range) receiving 80-150+ inches. It rains from October through June. A coop and run must be designed around rain.

- **Covered run is essential.** An uncovered run in the PNW becomes a mud pit by November, and chickens standing in mud develop bumblefoot, respiratory issues, and misery. Cover the run completely with a solid roof (corrugated polycarbonate, metal roofing) or at minimum a heavy-duty tarp. Budget for this. It is not optional.
- **Sloped roof.** A flat roof pools water, leaks, and rots. A single-slope (shed-style) or gable roof with at least a 3:12 pitch sheds rain effectively. Metal or asphalt shingle roofing both work. Standing-seam metal is the most durable choice for the PNW.
- **Overhang.** Extend the roof 6-12 inches past the walls to keep rain off the siding and away from the foundation. This small detail prevents years of water damage.

**Ventilation, not drafts.** This distinction is critical. Ventilation is the movement of air through the coop to remove moisture, ammonia, and CO2. Drafts are direct wind currents hitting birds on the roost. Wet air inside a cold coop causes frostbite (even in the PNW's mild winters, poorly ventilated coops can cause frostbitten combs on breeds with large single combs). The solution: place ventilation openings high --- at the eaves, above roost level --- so air moves through the upper portion of the coop without blowing across roosting birds. Eave vents, ridge vents, or high wall vents with adjustable baffles are ideal.

**Insulation is generally not needed** west of the Cascades. PNW winters rarely drop below 20 degrees F in the lowlands, and well-feathered chickens are comfortable down to 0 degrees F with adequate ventilation and no drafts. Insulation can actually be counterproductive if it reduces ventilation --- a warm, damp coop is more dangerous than a cold, dry one.

### Predator Defense Requirements

This is the non-negotiable engineering constraint. A coop that fails at predator defense fails at everything, no matter how beautiful it is.

**Hardware cloth, not chicken wire.** Chicken wire keeps chickens in. It does not keep predators out. A raccoon can tear through chicken wire. A determined dog can break it. Use 1/2-inch hardware cloth (welded galvanized wire mesh) on all openings, windows, and ventilation gaps. It costs more than chicken wire. It is worth it. Every year, online poultry forums fill with posts from keepers who used chicken wire and lost their entire flock to a raccoon in a single night.

**Two-step latches.** Raccoons can open simple hook-and-eye latches, slide bolts, and even some twist latches [RCN-02]. Use two-step latches that require two distinct, simultaneous motions to open: a carabiner clip through a hasp, a padlock, or a spring-loaded bolt with a separate lift mechanism. The second motion does not need to be complex --- it just needs to be different from the first, because raccoons excel at single-axis manipulation but struggle with bimanual tasks requiring coordinated, dissimilar motions.

**Buried apron or hardware cloth skirt.** Predators dig. Raccoons, coyotes, foxes, and rats will dig under a coop or run wall. Extend hardware cloth 12-18 inches outward from the base of the walls in a horizontal apron (called a "predator skirt") and cover it with soil, gravel, or pavers. A digging predator hits the wire and gives up rather than backing up to dig further out.

**Secure pop door.** The pop door (the small chicken-sized door between the coop and the run) should close and latch securely at night. Automatic pop doors with light sensors ($60-150, Ador, ChickenGuard, Omlet brands) close at dusk and open at dawn, providing consistent security even when you are running late, away for the evening, or just forgot. This is one of the best investments in poultry keeping: predator protection plus lazy mornings.

## Part 2: The Sketch Exercise

You need three drawings. They do not need to be beautiful --- they need to be clear, to scale, and to communicate the spatial relationships of every element.

### Drawing 1: Floor Plan (Top View)

Draw the coop and attached run as seen from directly above. Use a scale of 1 inch = 1 foot (or whatever fits your paper). Include:

- **Exterior walls** with dimensions labeled (4x6 feet for the coop, 6x10 feet for the run)
- **Pop door** location (between coop and run)
- **Human access door** to the coop (large enough to walk through --- 2.5x5 feet minimum, or a dutch door)
- **Nesting boxes** (1 box per 3-4 hens = 2 boxes for 6 hens, each 12x12x12 inches, placed along one wall, ideally accessible from outside the coop via a hinged lid or door)
- **Roosting bars** (position relative to walls, at least 12 inches from any wall)
- **Feeder and waterer** locations
- **Clean-out access** (how does bedding come out? A large door, a removable wall panel, or a poop board under the roost that slides out)

### Drawing 2: Front Elevation

Draw the coop as seen from the front (the side you face most often from your house). Include:

- **Roof profile** (shed-style or gable, with pitch indicated)
- **Human access door** (or dutch door)
- **Windows** (at least one, for light and additional ventilation, covered with hardware cloth)
- **Eave vents** (above roost level)
- **Foundation** (raised off the ground on blocks, posts, or a concrete slab --- never sitting directly on bare earth)
- **External nesting box** access door (if nesting boxes are accessible from outside)
- **Overall height** dimensions

### Drawing 3: Side Elevation

Draw the coop from the side, showing:

- **Roof slope and overhang**
- **Wall height at front and back** (for a shed-style roof, the front wall is taller than the back)
- **Ventilation openings** at the eaves
- **Run attachment** to the coop
- **Pop door** visible in profile

### Dimensioning

Label every dimension. A drawing without dimensions is a picture; a drawing with dimensions is a plan. Key dimensions to include:

| Element | Specification |
|---------|--------------|
| Interior floor area | 24 sq ft (4x6 ft) |
| Interior ceiling height | 5-6 ft at highest point (tall enough to stand in for cleaning) |
| Roosting bar height | 2-4 ft above floor |
| Roosting bar length | 8-12 inches per bird = 48-72 inches total for 6 birds |
| Roosting bar spacing | 12-18 inches between bars if using multiple parallel bars |
| Nesting box size | 12x12x12 inches each |
| Nesting box height | 12-24 inches above floor (lower than roost to discourage roosting in boxes) |
| Pop door size | 10x12 inches minimum (large enough for a Brahma; smaller breeds need less) |
| Ventilation area | 1 sq ft per 10 sq ft of floor = 2.4 sq ft for a 24 sq ft coop |
| Foundation height | 6-12 inches off ground (blocks or posts) |

## Part 3: Interior Design Details

### Roosting Bars

Chickens roost at night --- they prefer to sleep elevated, gripping a bar with their feet. The roost is where they spend 8-14 hours daily (longer in PNW winters with early dark). Design it well.

- **Material:** 2x4 lumber turned flat side up (the 4-inch face is the perch surface). The wide, flat surface allows the bird to settle its body down over its feet, keeping toes warm under the breast feathers in winter. Round dowels and narrow bars force feet to grip continuously, which is tiring and exposes toes to cold.
- **Height:** 2-4 feet above the floor. Higher is fine for athletic breeds (Leghorns, game fowl). Lower is necessary for heavy breeds (Orpingtons, Brahmas) and Silkies (which cannot fly to high perches). If using multiple bar heights, arrange them in a stair-step pattern so birds can hop up in stages.
- **Spacing:** 12-18 inches between parallel bars. Closer spacing causes birds on lower bars to be directly beneath birds on higher bars, receiving droppings from above.
- **Poop board:** A solid tray or board 6-8 inches below the roosting bars catches the majority of the coop's droppings (chickens produce most of their droppings while roosting). A poop board can be scraped clean daily in 30 seconds with a putty knife, dramatically reducing coop cleaning workload and keeping the floor bedding cleaner longer.

### Nesting Boxes

Hens lay eggs in enclosed, dark, comfortable spaces. The nesting box simulates the ground-level cavity that a jungle fowl would choose in the wild.

- **Size:** 12x12x12 inches. Slightly larger for giant breeds. One box per 3-4 hens --- hens share boxes willingly, and providing too many boxes means most go unused while hens line up for the "favorite."
- **Position:** Lower than the roost. If nesting boxes are at or above roost height, hens will sleep in them and fill them with droppings.
- **Bedding:** Pine shavings, straw, or nesting pads. Change when soiled.
- **External access:** Design the nesting boxes so eggs can be collected from outside the coop without entering the coop or disturbing roosting birds. A hinged lid on the outside of the coop that opens to reveal the nesting boxes is the standard approach. This is one of those small design features that you will appreciate every single morning.

### Clean-Out Access

The coop will need to be cleaned regularly. Shavings must come out, fresh shavings must go in, poop boards must be scraped, and occasionally the entire interior needs a thorough cleaning. Design for this from the start.

- **Large door or removable panel:** At least one wall of the coop should have a full-width door or a removable panel that allows you to reach every corner with a rake or shovel.
- **Smooth interior surfaces:** Avoid ledges, crevices, and rough wood inside the coop where mites can hide and droppings can accumulate. Sand any rough spots. Consider painting the interior with a light-colored paint (makes it easier to spot mites, and sealed wood is easier to clean).
- **Floor:** Marine plywood or OSB with a coat of paint or sealant. Some builders install a sheet of linoleum over the floor for the easiest possible cleaning --- droppings do not soak into the wood, and a shovel slides under the bedding smoothly.

## Part 4: The Creative Part

The engineering constraints are solved. The bird welfare requirements are met. The predator defense is specified. Now make it yours.

The coop sits in your yard, visible from your kitchen window, from the patio, from the garden. It is a structure you will interact with twice a day, every day, for years. It should look like it belongs --- not just to the chickens, but to you.

### PNW Aesthetic Ideas

- **Craftsman style:** Cedar lap siding, painted trim in a contrasting color, exposed rafter tails at the eaves. A small window with divided panes. A cupola on the ridge (purely decorative, but the chickens would appreciate the conversation piece if they could see it from inside). This fits naturally in the pre-war neighborhoods of Seattle, Portland, Tacoma.

- **Modern farmhouse:** Board-and-batten siding painted white or gray, black hardware, a simple gable roof with standing-seam metal roofing. Clean lines, no fussiness. A barn door slider for the human access instead of a hinged door.

- **PNW cabin:** Rough-sawn cedar siding left to weather naturally to silver-gray. A shed roof with a deep overhang. A living roof planted with sedum (adds insulation, absorbs rain, looks beautiful). Green trim. River rock foundation surround.

- **The Hobbit coop:** Round pop door. Moss-covered green roof. Rounded windows. Nestled into a hillside or berm. Whimsical and utterly impractical for cleaning, but people have built them and the photos are irresistible.

### Window Boxes and Plantings

Window boxes on the outside of the coop, planted with herbs the chickens cannot reach, add color and make the coop feel like a building rather than a box. Good PNW choices: lavender (drought-tolerant once established, beautiful in summer), nasturtiums (edible flowers --- you can toss them to the chickens as a treat), rosemary (evergreen, fragrant), thyme (low-growing, aromatic).

### Color

Paint the coop. A raw-wood coop grays out within a year in the PNW rain. Paint protects the wood and gives you a chance to express something. Classic colors: barn red, forest green, slate blue, mustard yellow, pure white. Pick something that complements your house or deliberately contrasts with it. The birds will not notice. Your neighbors might compliment it.

For a 4x6 foot coop housing 6 standard hens, here is an approximate materials list:

| Hinges (heavy-duty) | 6-8 | Doors, nesting box lid, clean-out panel |
| Screws (exterior grade, 2-3 inch) | 2-3 lbs | Structural assembly |
| Staples (for hardware cloth, 1/2 inch) | 1 box | Plus screws with fender washers for extra security |

### Interior

| Item | Quantity | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| 2x4 for roosting bars | 6-8 ft | Flat side up |
| Vinyl flooring remnant or linoleum | 24 sq ft | Easy-clean floor surface |
| Exterior paint and primer | 1-2 gal | Exterior-grade latex |

### Approximate Cost: $300-800

The range is wide because material choices, salvage use, and design complexity vary enormously. A basic coop from new materials at commodity lumber prices runs $400-600. Adding premium siding, metal roofing, and decorative elements pushes it higher. Using salvaged lumber, reclaimed windows, and found materials can reduce cost significantly.

## Part 6: Test Your Design

Before committing to construction, check your design against these questions:

1. **Can you reach every corner of the interior for cleaning?** Physically reach, not theoretically reach. Get down on the ground and mime the motions.
2. **Can you collect eggs without entering the coop?** External nesting box access saves time and avoids disturbing the flock.
3. **Where does the water go when it rains?** Trace the path of every raindrop from roof to ground. Does any water pool against the walls? Flow toward the foundation? Enter through ventilation openings?
4. **Where does the air go?** Trace airflow from intake (lower openings or pop door) through the coop and out the high vents. Does air pass through the roost zone? If yes, the birds are in a draft.
5. **Can a raccoon reach through any opening?** A raccoon can reach its arm through any gap wider than 2 inches. Every opening larger than that needs hardware cloth.
6. **Can a predator dig under the walls?** If there is no buried apron or solid foundation extending below grade, the answer is yes.
7. **Is the pop door automated or manually secured every night?** If manual, are you confident you will do it every single night, including when you are tired, it is raining, and the game is on?
8. **Will you enjoy looking at it?** This question matters.
