# Try Session: Chicken Math --- The Economics of Backyard Eggs

*Calculate the real cost of your eggs, discover why they are worth every penny, and understand the phenomenon that turns six hens into twenty*

---

## The Honest Question

Somewhere around week three of keeping backyard chickens, every new flock owner cracks open a gorgeous deep-orange-yolked egg from the coop, fries it in butter, takes a bite, and announces to the kitchen: "This is so much better than store-bought. And we are basically getting free eggs."

They are not getting free eggs.

They are getting eggs that cost somewhere between $4 and $8 per dozen --- sometimes more --- depending on how you account for the startup costs, the monthly feed bill, the infrastructure improvements that seemed optional in January and became urgent in March, and the six additional chickens they did not plan to buy but somehow acquired between April and July. This is chicken math, and it is both a real economic calculation and a running joke in the poultry community, because the math never works out the way you think it will, and it does not matter, because the eggs are not really the point.

This try session walks you through the actual economics of backyard egg production. You will build a spreadsheet, calculate your cost per dozen, and arrive at an honest number. Then we will talk about what that number means --- and why the real return on investment has nothing to do with the price of eggs at the grocery store.

---

## Part 1: Startup Costs --- The First Check You Write

Before a single egg appears, you need infrastructure. The variance here is enormous, because a chicken coop can be anything from a repurposed dog house with hardware cloth stapled to the front ($50 in materials) to a custom-built, insulated, automatically-doored structure with a covered run and integrated storage ($2,000+). Both work. The chickens do not care about aesthetics. They care about being dry, predator-proof, and ventilated.

### The Coop

This is the largest single expense and the one with the widest range.

| Approach | Cost Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Repurposed shed or dog house | $50--150 | Requires modification: ventilation, roost bars, nest boxes, predator-proofing |
| DIY build from plans | $200--600 | Lumber, hardware cloth, roofing, hardware. The [Coop Design Challenge](coop-design-challenge.md) covers PNW-specific design |
| Prefab coop (retail) | $200--500 | Tractor Supply, Amazon, Wayfair. Quality varies wildly. Most prefabs are too small for the number of birds they claim to house |
| Custom-built by a contractor | $1,000--3,000+ | The Cadillac option. Insulated, wired for lighting, automatic door, built to last decades |

**Realistic budget for a first flock of 6 hens:** $200--600 for a DIY coop that meets the minimum requirements (4 sq ft per bird inside, 10 sq ft per bird in the run, predator-proof hardware cloth on all openings, covered run for PNW rain).

For this spreadsheet, enter your actual or planned coop cost. If you have not built yet, use $400 as a reasonable midpoint for a DIY build.

### The Flock

Day-old chicks from a feed store or hatchery cost $3--5 each for common breeds. Sexed pullets (guaranteed female) cost $4--6. Started pullets (8--16 weeks old, past the brooding stage) cost $15--30 each. Adult laying hens cost $20--40. Heritage and rare breeds cost more --- Cream Legbars, Black Copper Marans, and Olive Eggers can run $8--15 as chicks.

For a first flock of 6 production-oriented hens:

| Source | Cost per Bird | Flock of 6 |
|---|---|---|
| Day-old chicks (feed store) | $3--5 | $18--30 |
| Sexed pullets (hatchery) | $4--6 | $24--36 |
| Started pullets (local breeder) | $15--30 | $90--180 |
| Adult laying hens (Craigslist/local) | $20--40 | $120--240 |

**The hidden cost of chicks:** Day-old chicks need a brooder (a box or tub with a heat source) for 6--8 weeks before they can move outside. A heat plate ($35--45) or heat lamp ($15 + bulb), bedding ($10), chick feeder and waterer ($10--15), and chick starter feed ($15 for a 25 lb bag). Brooding adds $50--85 to your startup if you start from chicks. Started pullets skip this entirely, which is why experienced keepers often recommend them for beginners despite the higher per-bird cost.

**Realistic flock cost:** $20--36 for chicks, or $90--180 for started pullets. Plus $50--85 for brooding supplies if you go the chick route.

### Equipment

| Item | Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Feeder (hanging tube type) | $15--25 | Galvanized steel lasts longer than plastic |
| Waterer (3--5 gallon) | $15--30 | Heated waterer ($40--60) is essential for PNW winters with occasional freezes |
| First bag of feed (50 lb layer pellets) | $18--25 | Purina, Nutrena, or local mill |
| Bedding (pine shavings, first bale) | $8--12 | Large compressed bale from feed store |
| Oyster shell (5 lb bag) | $5--8 | Free-choice calcium supplement for shell strength |
| Grit (5 lb bag) | $5--8 | Necessary if birds don't free-range on natural ground |
| First aid supplies | $15--25 | Vetericyn spray, electrolyte powder, Blu-Kote |

**Equipment subtotal:** $80--135

### Startup Summary

| Category | Low Estimate | High Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Coop | $200 | $600 |
| Flock (6 birds) | $20 | $180 |
| Brooding (if chicks) | $50 | $85 |
| Equipment | $80 | $135 |
| **Total Startup** | **$350** | **$1,000** |

Enter your numbers. These are real. Write them down. We will use them.

## Part 2: Monthly Operating Costs --- The Feed Bill That Never Stops

Once the coop is built and the birds are in it, you have entered a subscription service. Chickens eat every day. They produce waste every day. They need clean water every day. The monthly costs are remarkably predictable once you know your flock size and local feed prices.

### Feed

Six standard-sized laying hens eat approximately 1/4 pound of feed per bird per day, or about 1.5 pounds per day for the flock. That is roughly 45 pounds per month --- nearly one 50-pound bag.

| Feed Type | Price per 50 lb Bag | Monthly Cost (6 hens) |
|---|---|---|
| Standard layer pellets (Purina, Nutrena) | $18--22 | $18--22 |
| Organic layer pellets | $28--35 | $28--35 |
| Non-GMO layer pellets | $24--30 | $24--30 |
| Local mill (if available) | $15--20 | $15--20 |

Free-ranging hens eat less purchased feed --- perhaps 15--20% less --- because they supplement with bugs, grass, seeds, and whatever else they find in the yard. But free-ranging also means more predator risk, garden destruction, and porch pooping, so the savings come with trade-offs.

**Treats and supplements:** Mealworms ($8--12/bag, lasts a month or two), scratch grain ($12--15/bag), black soldier fly larvae ($10--15/bag). None of these are necessary. All of them make your chickens ecstatically happy, which makes them hard to resist. Budget $5--10/month for the "I cannot help myself" factor.

### Bedding

Pine shavings in the coop, replaced or refreshed monthly. A compressed bale ($8--12) lasts one to two months depending on coop size and your tolerance for cleanup frequency.

**Monthly bedding cost:** $5--12

### Miscellaneous Recurring

- Oyster shell and grit refills: $2--3/month (amortized)
- Supplements (apple cider vinegar, electrolytes, herbs): $3--5/month
- Occasional veterinary care (amortized): $0--10/month (most chicken health issues are managed at home, but an avian vet visit runs $50--150 when needed)

### Monthly Operating Summary

| Category | Low Estimate | High Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| Feed | $18 | $35 |
| Bedding | $5 | $12 |
| Treats/supplements | $5 | $10 |
| Miscellaneous | $2 | $10 |
| **Monthly Total** | **$30** | **$67** |

For the spreadsheet, we will use $35/month as a reasonable middle estimate for a six-hen flock on standard feed with moderate treat expenditure.

## Part 3: Production --- What the Hens Actually Deliver

Egg production is not a constant. It varies by breed, age, season, health, and day length. Understanding the production curve is essential for honest cost-per-dozen math.

### Breed Expectations

| Breed | Eggs/Year (Average) | Egg Color | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rhode Island Red | 250--300 | Brown | The production workhorse |
| Leghorn (White) | 280--320 | White | Commercial production standard |
| Australorp | 250--280 | Brown | Reliable, gentle, good in PNW |
| Plymouth Rock | 200--250 | Brown | Dual-purpose, friendly |
| Easter Egger | 200--280 | Blue/Green | Variable but usually productive |
| Orpington | 180--220 | Brown | Beautiful, friendly, moderate producer |
| Marans | 150--200 | Dark chocolate brown | Gorgeous eggs, fewer of them |
| Silkie | 100--120 | Cream/tinted | Ornamental; not a production breed |

For cost analysis, assume a mixed flock of good layers averaging **250 eggs per hen per year**. Six hens = 1,500 eggs/year = **125 dozen**.

### The Seasonal Curve

Production peaks in spring and summer when day length exceeds 14 hours. It drops in fall as days shorten, and most hens stop laying or dramatically reduce in winter (November through January in the PNW). First-year pullets often lay through their first winter. Older hens follow the natural photoperiod more strictly.

Supplemental lighting in the coop (a simple timer on a 40-watt bulb, adding light in the early morning to reach 14 hours total) can maintain production through winter. This is common practice and does not harm the hens, though some keepers prefer to let their birds rest naturally. The decision affects your annual egg count by 15--25%.

### The Molt

Every fall, hens molt --- they shed and regrow their feathers over 4--12 weeks. During the molt, egg production stops or drops to near zero. This is normal. It is also deeply alarming if you are not expecting it, because your formerly productive flock suddenly looks ragged and stops producing, and you wonder what you did wrong. You did nothing wrong. They are growing feathers.

### First-Year Reality Check

Chicks hatched in spring will not begin laying until fall --- approximately 18--24 weeks of age. That means you are feeding birds for 4--6 months before seeing a single egg. If you start with started pullets, you may get eggs within weeks. This delay matters for the cost calculation.

Production declines about 10--15% per year after the first full laying year. By year 4--5, your original hens are producing 60--70% of their peak rate, which is why most flock owners add new pullets every 1--2 years (and now you have more chickens --- see Part 5).

### Amortized Over 5 Years

If you spread the startup cost over five years and average the production decline:

**Five-year average cost per dozen: approximately $4.50--6.00**, depending on your specific costs, flock management, and how many "impulse chickens" you acquire along the way.

For comparison, store-bought conventional eggs (2024--2026 prices, subject to volatile fluctuation): $3--5/dozen. Store-bought organic/free-range/pasture-raised: $5--9/dozen. Farmers market pastured eggs: $7--12/dozen.

**The bottom line:** Backyard eggs cost roughly the same as mid-range grocery store eggs, more than the cheapest conventional eggs, and less than premium farmers market eggs. They are not free. They are not cheap. They are, however, genuinely better --- nutritionally, ethically, and experientially.

## Part 5: The Chicken Math Phenomenon

"Chicken math" is the universal experience of backyard poultry keepers: you start with a small, sensible flock, and the flock grows. It grows because:

**The breed catalog is intoxicating.** You started with Buff Orpingtons because they were friendly and available. Then you saw Cream Legbars with their blue eggs. Then Black Copper Marans with their chocolate eggs. Then a Silkie, because have you SEEN a Silkie? Then an Easter Egger, because you want green eggs to complete the rainbow. Each breed offers something the others do not, and the feed store knows this, which is why they stock eight breeds every spring.

**Chick days are irresistible.** Every spring, Tractor Supply, farm stores, and local feed stores stock bins of day-old chicks under heat lamps. You go in for a bag of feed. You leave with six chicks. This is not a failure of willpower. It is a design feature of the retail poultry ecosystem. The chicks are placed between the entrance and the feed aisle for a reason.

**Hatching is addictive.** If you have a rooster (or buy fertile eggs), you can incubate your own chicks. The [Incubation Project](incubation-project.md) try session covers the process. The experience of watching a chick pip through its shell after 21 days of careful temperature and humidity management is one of the most magical things a person can witness. You will not hatch just once. You will hatch "just one more batch" every spring until you are managing a population that has outgrown your original coop.

**Rehoming and rescue.** Someone on your local poultry Facebook group is rehoming a flock because they are moving. A friend's HOA changed the rules and they need to place four hens by Saturday. A neighbor's kid lost interest in their 4-H project. You are the person who has a coop. You are the person who says yes. Now you have fourteen chickens.

**The math:** You start with 6. By year 2, you have 10. By year 3, you have 15--20. By year 5, you are building a second coop and explaining to your spouse that this was always the plan, you just did not mention it at the time because you did not know it yet.

This progression is so universal in the backyard poultry community that "chicken math" has its own hashtag, its own memes, and its own resigned acceptance. It is not a problem to be solved. It is the hobby operating as designed.

## Part 6: The Real ROI --- What the Spreadsheet Cannot Measure

The spreadsheet says your eggs cost $5 a dozen. The grocery store sells eggs for $4. The spreadsheet says you are losing money. The spreadsheet is correct, and it is also completely wrong, because the spreadsheet does not measure any of the things that actually matter.

**The morning ritual.** Every morning, you walk out to the coop with your coffee. You open the door. The hens rush out, clucking and investigating. You check the nest boxes. There are eggs --- warm, clean, sometimes still damp. You collect them in a basket or an old bowl or the pocket of your robe. This takes five minutes. It is the best five minutes of the day. It is grounding, rhythmic, and real in a way that checking your email is not.

**The garden ecosystem.** Chickens turn kitchen scraps into compost. They eat bugs. They scratch and turn garden beds. Their manure, properly composted, is among the best garden fertilizers available. If you garden, chickens close the nutrient loop --- scraps become eggs, manure becomes soil, soil grows food, food becomes scraps. This is not theoretical permaculture. This is what actually happens in a backyard with chickens and a garden.

**The stress reduction.** There is a growing body of research on the therapeutic benefits of animal interaction, and chickens are surprisingly effective therapy animals. They are warm. They are funny. They have individual personalities that are immediately apparent to anyone who spends time with them. Sitting in the yard watching chickens forage is, for many people, a genuinely meditative activity. The term "chicken TV" exists because people will sit and watch their flock the way they would watch a campfire --- not for information, but for the quality of attention it produces.

**The neighborhood connections.** You will give away eggs. You will give away SO many eggs. Peak production from six hens is 4--5 eggs per day, and a household of two people cannot eat that many eggs. You will bring eggs to neighbors, coworkers, friends, family, and the mail carrier. The eggs become a social currency --- a reason to knock on a neighbor's door, a gift that is always welcome, a conversation starter that leads to deeper connection. Many backyard chicken keepers report that their flock has done more for their neighborhood social life than any other single factor.

**The food literacy.** Keeping chickens teaches you where food comes from in a way that no book, documentary, or grocery store label can replicate. You understand what "free-range" means and does not mean. You understand what "pasture-raised" requires in terms of land, labor, and management. You understand why eggs cost what they cost at the farmers market. This literacy extends to all food --- once you have raised one thing yourself, you look at every food product differently.

**The children.** If you have children, backyard chickens provide biology, responsibility, economics, and death --- the full curriculum of life. Children learn that eggs come from animals, that animals require daily care regardless of the weather or your mood, that living things get sick and sometimes die, and that the reward for consistent effort is a warm egg in your hand every morning. No screen-based educational tool teaches these lessons.

## Part 7: Making the Spreadsheet Honest

A truly honest cost analysis would include the intangible benefits as credits:

| Intangible Benefit | Estimated Value |
|---|---|
| Therapy/stress reduction | $50/month (one therapy session) |
| Garden compost value | $10--20/month |
| Social connection / gifted eggs | Hard to price. Priceless? |
| Food education (self and family) | Hard to price |
| Entertainment ("chicken TV") | $15/month (one streaming subscription) |

If you credited even $50/month in intangible benefits against the operating cost, your eggs would be effectively free by Year 2. This is, of course, absurd accounting. But it is no more absurd than pretending the only value of a backyard flock is the market price of the eggs it produces.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Home Economics** --- [Budgeting, Resource Management]: This entire try session is a budgeting exercise --- tracking startup costs, calculating monthly operating expenses, amortizing capital investments, and comparing production costs to market prices. The spreadsheet template is a practical tool that teaches real financial literacy through a project the student cares about. The distinction between financial cost and total value is a sophisticated economic concept that the chicken math problem makes concrete and personal.
- **Science** --- [Nutrition, Biology]: The nutritional comparison between backyard and commercial eggs is grounded in published research --- the carotenoid content, omega-3 fatty acid profiles, and vitamin concentrations are measurable differences driven by diet and husbandry. Understanding WHY pastured eggs are nutritionally different (diverse foraging → varied micronutrient deposition in the yolk) connects animal science to human nutrition in a direct, experiential way.
- **Home Economics** --- [Food Production, Self-Sufficiency]: Raising your own food --- even one category of food --- transforms your relationship with the food system. The practical knowledge of what it takes to produce a single egg (housing, feed, health management, daily care, seasonal variation) provides a baseline understanding that applies to every food purchase decision for the rest of your life.
