# DIY Project: Feather Art --- Pressed Feathers, Wreaths, and Quill Pens

*What to do with naturally molted feathers: three craft projects that connect art, biology, and history*

---

## The Feather Collection

If you keep chickens, you find feathers. They accumulate in the coop, in the run, along the fence line, in the grass near the dust bath. Twice a year --- during the juvenile molt at around 12-18 weeks and the annual adult molt in late summer or early fall --- feathers appear by the handful. A single hen sheds and replaces over 8,000 feathers during a full molt. Multiply by a flock of six and you have a small blizzard of plumage scattered across your property for weeks.

Most people sweep them up and compost them. This is a waste. A naturally molted chicken feather is a miniature masterpiece of biological engineering: a branching structure of keratin with barbs, barbules, and hooklets that interlock into a smooth, aerodynamic surface capable of shedding rain, trapping insulating air, and displaying color patterns that have been refined by centuries of selective breeding. The Silver Laced Wyandotte's black-bordered white feathers [FWL breeds/wyandotte], the Buff Orpington's warm golden plumes [FWL breeds/orpington], the Barred Plymouth Rock's crisp black-and-white stripes [FWL breeds/plymouth-rock] --- these are materials with inherent beauty. They deserve better than the compost bin.

This DIY project covers three feather crafts: a pressed feather botanical arrangement (framed art), a decorative feather wreath, and a functional quill pen. All three use naturally molted feathers --- never pulled from a live bird.

**A note on sourcing:** Only use feathers that have been naturally shed. A molted feather separates cleanly from the follicle; the base (calamus) is smooth, hollow, and translucent white. A plucked feather has tissue or blood at the base and was removed by force. Pulling feathers from a live bird is painful, can cause follicle damage, and is unnecessary when molting provides an abundant supply for free. If you do not keep chickens, feathers from poultry-keeping friends, local farms, or craft supply retailers (which sell sanitized domestic poultry feathers) are all ethical sources.

## Part 1: Feather Identification and Selection

Before starting any project, take the time to identify your feathers. This is where craft meets science, and it is more rewarding than it sounds.

### Feather Types

A chicken produces several distinct feather types, each with a different shape, size, and function:

- **Contour feathers:** The visible outer feathers that define the bird's shape. Broad, flat, symmetrical vanes with a clear rachis (central shaft). These are your primary craft feathers --- they lay flat, display patterns clearly, and have the structural integrity for pressing, gluing, and arranging.
- **Flight feathers (remiges):** The large, stiff wing feathers. In chickens, these are shorter and stiffer than in flying birds, but they are still substantial --- 6-10 inches in heavy breeds. The primaries (outer wing) are asymmetric (one vane narrower than the other). Flight feathers are your best candidates for quill pens.
- **Tail feathers (retrices):** Long, stiff, often curved. Rooster tail feathers --- the dramatic sickle feathers that arch over the tail in breeds like the Phoenix or Old English Game --- are the showpieces of any collection. Hen tail feathers are shorter and straighter.
- **Hackle feathers:** The narrow, pointed feathers from the neck. Rooster hackles are long, slim, and often iridescent. Hen hackles are shorter and rounder. Hackle feathers are prized in fly-tying and are excellent for detail work in pressed arrangements.
- **Saddle feathers:** Found on the rooster's back near the tail. Long, flowing, often with a glossy sheen. Another fly-tying favorite.
- **Down feathers:** The soft, fluffy under-layer closest to the skin. No rigid rachis. Used for insulation. Not suitable for craft projects requiring flat, structured feathers, but beautiful in their own right.

### Matching Feathers to Breeds

If you keep multiple breeds, matching a found feather to its owner is a satisfying identification exercise. The genetics that produce each breed's plumage pattern [FWL-04 Feather Genetics] are expressed in every individual feather. A single contour feather from a Silver Laced Wyandotte shows the same black lacing on a silver ground that defines the breed. A Barred Rock feather shows the alternating black and white bars that result from the sex-linked barring gene. A Marans feather [FWL breeds/marans] shows the characteristic stippled dark-brown pattern.

Use the breed profiles in the FWL research series to identify your feathers by pattern, color, and structure. Pay attention to:

- **Ground color:** White, buff, black, red-brown, blue-grey
- **Pattern type:** Lacing (border around each feather), barring (horizontal stripes), penciling (concentric lines), spangling (tip marking), mottling (irregular spots)
- **Iridescence:** Many black-feathered breeds --- Australorps [FWL breeds/australorp], Black Orpingtons, Marans --- show a beetle-green or purple sheen on contour feathers when held at the right angle to light. This is structural color, produced by thin-film interference in the feather barbules rather than by pigment.

## Part 2: Feather Cleaning and Preservation

Molted feathers collected from the ground need cleaning before craft use. They carry dust, dander, mites, and organic debris.

### Cleaning Protocol

1. **Shake off loose debris.** Hold the feather by the calamus and flick it gently to remove dust and bedding particles.

2. **Wash in mild soap solution.** Fill a basin with warm (not hot --- hot water can denature keratin and make the feather brittle) water and a few drops of mild dish soap or baby shampoo. Submerge the feathers and swish gently for 30 seconds. Do not scrub the vanes --- this can separate the barbule hooklets and make the feather look ragged.

3. **Rinse in clean warm water.** Swish gently to remove soap.

4. **Optional: vinegar rinse.** A brief dip in a solution of one part white vinegar to four parts water helps sanitize and brings out sheen. Rinse again in clean water after.

5. **Reshape while damp.** Smooth the vanes gently between your thumb and forefinger, working from the rachis outward. The barbule hooklets will re-engage as you stroke, restoring the flat, smooth surface. This is the same mechanism by which birds preen --- the hooklets are designed to zip and unzip, and gentle stroking in the direction of the barbs reconnects them.

6. **Dry flat.** Lay feathers on a clean towel, smoothing them into their natural shape. Do not use a hair dryer on high heat. Room temperature air drying preserves the feather's natural oils and flexibility. Drying takes 2-4 hours depending on feather size and humidity.

7. **Storage.** Store clean, dry feathers flat in a box or between sheets of acid-free tissue paper. A shallow cardboard box with layers separated by tissue is ideal. Label by breed if you are maintaining a sorted collection.

### Preservation for Long-Term Display

For feathers destined for framed art or permanent display:

- **Spray with clear acrylic sealant** (matte or satin finish) to protect against dust, UV fading, and moisture. Two light coats, allowing each to dry completely. This adds a subtle protective film without altering the feather's appearance.
- **UV-protective glass** in frames prevents pigment fading over years. Protoporphyrin (the brown pigment in chicken feathers) and carotenoid-derived colors fade faster in direct sunlight. Structural colors (the iridescent greens and purples) are more light-stable because they are produced by physical structure rather than chemical pigment [FWL-04 Feather Genetics].

## Part 3: Project One --- Pressed Feather Botanical Arrangement

This project creates a framed display piece: feathers arranged artistically on a mat board, similar to the pressed-flower botanical prints of the 18th and 19th centuries.

### Materials

- **Frame with mat board:** An 8x10 or 11x14 frame with a white or cream mat board. A shadow box frame (which has depth between the glass and the backing) works best because feathers are not perfectly flat and benefit from a millimeter or two of clearance.
- **Acid-free mat board:** The backing surface where you arrange the feathers. White or cream shows feather colors best. Available at art supply stores.
- **Craft glue or double-sided archival tape:** A small dot of acid-free craft glue (Aleene's Original Tacky Glue is a reliable choice) at the base of each feather secures it without visible adhesive. Alternatively, small strips of acid-free double-sided tape.
- **Tweezers:** For positioning small feathers precisely.
- **Labels (optional):** If you want to create a botanical-style display, add small printed or handwritten labels identifying each feather by breed, feather type, and position on the bird.

### Design Approach

The arrangement is yours to compose, but two classic approaches work well:

**The Breed Portfolio.** Select one representative contour feather from each breed in your flock. Arrange them in a row or grid, each labeled with the breed name. This creates a visual catalog of your flock's genetic diversity --- the buff of the Orpington next to the barred pattern of the Plymouth Rock next to the blue-laced of the Wyandotte. If you keep breeds selected for egg color diversity [FWL guides/egg-color-rainbow], the feather portfolio is the companion piece to the egg rainbow.

**The Anatomy Study.** Select multiple feather types from a single breed: a flight primary, a contour feather, a hackle, a down feather, and a tail feather. Arrange them in order from largest to smallest, or in their approximate anatomical positions. Label each with its type and body position. This creates an educational display that shows the range of feather forms a single bird produces.

### Assembly

1. Lay the mat board flat. Arrange feathers loosely, trying different compositions before committing to glue.
2. When satisfied, apply a small dot of glue to the calamus (base) of each feather and press gently onto the mat board. Hold for 10 seconds.
3. For feathers that want to curl or lift, add a second tiny glue dot at the midpoint of the rachis.
4. Allow to dry for 1-2 hours before framing.
5. Place the mat board in the frame, ensuring the glass (or acrylic) does not crush the feathers. Shadow box frames provide the necessary clearance.

## Part 4: Project Two --- Feather Wreath

A wreath made from naturally molted feathers is a seasonal display piece that uses volume rather than precision. Where the pressed arrangement is about individual feather beauty, the wreath is about collective effect --- the cumulative visual impact of dozens or hundreds of feathers layered in patterns of color and size.

### Materials

- **Grapevine wreath base** (12-16 inch diameter, $5-10): Available at craft stores. The natural wood texture of grapevine provides a rustic background that complements feather colors. Foam wreath forms work but look less natural.
- **Hot glue gun and glue sticks:** The workhorse adhesive for this project. Hot glue bonds feathers to the grapevine quickly and securely.
- **50-200 feathers** (quantity depends on wreath size and desired density): Contour feathers, hackle feathers, and small tail feathers all work. A mix of sizes creates visual interest. If you keep multiple breeds, the color variety is automatic.
- **Ribbon or twine** for hanging.

### Design Approach

**Sort feathers by size first.** Create three piles: large (flight and tail feathers, 4+ inches), medium (contour feathers, 2-4 inches), and small (hackle, saddle, small contour, under 2 inches).

**Work from the outside in.** Start by gluing the largest feathers around the outer edge of the wreath, angling them slightly outward so they extend past the frame. These create the wreath's silhouette. Then fill the middle band with medium feathers, layered and overlapping like shingles on a roof. Finally, tuck small feathers into gaps and add hackle feathers as accent points.

**Color strategy.** Two approaches work well:

- **Gradient:** Arrange feathers in a color progression around the wreath --- light buff at the top, transitioning through brown to black at the bottom, or whatever color sequence your breeds provide.
- **Random mix:** Just glue feathers as they come, mixing colors and patterns freely. The natural variation creates a folk-art aesthetic that is appealing in its own right.

### Assembly

1. Heat the glue gun. Work on a protected surface (newspaper or craft mat).
2. Apply a line of hot glue along the calamus and lower rachis of each feather. Press firmly onto the grapevine, holding for 5 seconds until the glue sets.
3. Layer feathers so that each new feather's base is covered by the vane of the feather above it. This hides the glue joints and creates the shingled effect.
4. Work in one direction around the wreath so all feathers angle the same way.
5. After completing the full circuit, add accent feathers (iridescent black hackles, bright white feathers, unusually patterned feathers) in focal points.
6. Attach a loop of ribbon or twine to the back for hanging.

## Part 5: Project Three --- Quill Pen

Writing with a quill pen connects you to every human who put words on paper before the steel nib was invented in the 1820s. The Declaration of Independence was written with a quill. So were the works of Shakespeare, Newton's Principia, and every letter, ledger, and legal document produced in the Western world for over a thousand years. The quill pen is not a novelty. It is the technology that carried civilization's words for a millennium.

Making one from a chicken feather is straightforward, and the resulting pen is genuinely functional for calligraphy and decorative writing.

### Selecting the Feather

You need a **large flight feather** --- a primary or secondary from the wing. Goose and turkey feathers are the traditional quill materials because of their size and stiffness, but a large chicken flight feather (from a heavy breed like a Brahma [FWL breeds/brahma], Orpington, or Plymouth Rock) works adequately. The feather should be at least 8 inches long with a thick, rigid calamus (the hollow shaft at the base).

**Left-hand or right-hand feather?** Traditionally, right-handed writers use feathers from the left wing (the natural curve arcs away from the writer's face), and left-handed writers use right-wing feathers. This is a comfort preference, not a structural requirement.

### Preparing the Nib

1. **Strip the lower barbs.** Remove the barbs (the soft filaments) from the lower 3-4 inches of the rachis, leaving a clean, bare shaft to grip. Strip by pulling the barbs firmly downward from the rachis.

2. **Harden the calamus (optional but recommended).** The fresh calamus is slightly flexible. To harden it for a more durable nib, fill a small container with clean, dry sand, heat the sand in an oven to 350 degrees F, then bury the stripped calamus in the hot sand for 10-15 seconds. Remove and let cool. The heat drives out residual moisture and toughens the keratin. Alternatively, let the stripped calamus air-dry for several days before cutting.

3. **Cut the nib.** Using a sharp craft knife or X-Acto blade:
   - Make a diagonal cut across the end of the calamus at approximately 45 degrees, creating an angled opening. The cut should be about 3/4 inch long.
   - Scoop out any membrane material from inside the hollow shaft.
   - Turn the feather over and make a small slit (1/8 to 1/4 inch long) up the center of the nib from the tip. This slit is critical --- it acts as a channel that draws ink from the reservoir (the hollow interior of the calamus) to the writing tip by capillary action. Without the slit, the pen will not write.
   - Shape the tip by trimming a tiny amount from each side of the slit to create a slightly pointed or squared-off writing edge. A squared tip produces broad, flat strokes. A pointed tip produces finer lines with more variation between thick and thin strokes.

4. **Test and adjust.** Dip the nib 1/4 inch into ink and write on scrap paper. If the pen does not write, the slit may be too short (extend it slightly) or the nib too blunt (trim the tip). If ink flows too freely and blobs, the slit may be too long or the nib too wide. Quill cutting is iterative --- expect to trim and adjust several times before the pen writes smoothly.

### Ink and Writing

- **India ink or calligraphy ink** works well with quill pens. Avoid ballpoint pen ink (too thin) and acrylic paint (too thick and dries in the nib).
- **An inkwell** (any small, stable container that holds 1/4 inch of ink) keeps the ink accessible and prevents spills. A shot glass works in a pinch.
- **Writing technique:** Hold the pen at approximately 45 degrees to the paper. The nib should rest on the paper with the slit aligned with the direction of writing. Apply light, consistent pressure --- less than you would use with a modern pen. The ink should flow by gravity and capillary action, not by force. Dip the nib every 3-5 words initially; as you develop a feel for ink consumption, you will know when to dip by the way the strokes begin to lighten.

### Calligraphy Basics

A quill pen naturally produces the thick-and-thin line variation that defines calligraphic writing. Downstrokes (pulling the pen toward you) produce thick lines because the nib flexes slightly and the full width of the tip contacts the paper. Cross-strokes and upstrokes produce thin lines. This contrast is the foundation of every historical calligraphic script from Italic to Copperplate.

For beginners, start with basic letterforms: the alphabet in a simple Italic hand. Practice consistent letter height, consistent pen angle, and smooth transitions between thick and thin. Calligraphy books and online tutorials are abundant. The pen you made is the same pen that every calligrapher in history used before 1820. The skills transfer directly.

## Legal Note: The Migratory Bird Treaty Act

A critical distinction exists between domestic poultry feathers and wild bird feathers. The Migratory Bird Treaty Act (MBTA) of 1918 makes it illegal to possess feathers, nests, or eggs of most wild bird species native to North America --- including feathers found on the ground. You cannot legally collect a hawk feather, an eagle feather, a crow feather, a heron feather, or a songbird feather, even if you watched it fall from the sky. The law makes no distinction between a feather plucked from a live bird and one found in your yard. Enforcement varies, but the legal framework is clear and penalties can be significant.

**Domestic poultry feathers are exempt.** Chickens, turkeys, ducks, geese, guinea fowl, peafowl, and other domesticated species are not covered by the MBTA. You can collect, possess, sell, and craft with domestic poultry feathers freely.

**If you find a feather and are not sure what species it came from:** Do not collect it. If it is clearly from one of your chickens (matching a breed you keep, found in or near the coop), it is fine. If it is a wild bird feather of unknown species found in the park, leave it where it is.

The one major exception: feathers of non-native species (European Starling, House Sparrow, Rock Pigeon) are not protected by the MBTA and can be legally collected. However, for craft purposes, domestic poultry feathers are more beautiful, more available, and entirely unambiguous legally.
