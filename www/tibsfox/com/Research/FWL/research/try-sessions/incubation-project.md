# Try Session: Hatch Eggs at Home --- The 21-Day Incubation Project

*The ultimate hands-on chicken experience: guiding life from egg to chick in your own kitchen*

---

## Why Incubate?

There is no shortcut to understanding what a chicken egg actually is --- not an inert food product, not a static capsule, but a self-contained life-support system engineered by 150 million years of evolution to transform a single cell into a living, breathing, peeping organism in exactly 21 days. Reading about embryology teaches you the sequence. Incubating an egg teaches you the weight of it --- the daily responsibility, the anxiety of temperature swings, the discipline of not opening the incubator when every instinct says to check, and the raw, unrepeatable experience of watching a beak punch through a shell from the inside.

This try session walks you through the entire incubation process, from choosing equipment and sourcing fertile eggs through the 21-day timeline to brooding the hatchlings. It is detailed because the details matter: a half-degree temperature error sustained for three days can kill every embryo in the incubator. This is real biology with real stakes, and the precision required is part of what makes it such a powerful learning experience.

---

## Part 1: Equipment

### The Incubator

Two basic types exist, and which one you choose affects your temperature targets, your success rate, and your daily workload.

**Still-air incubator ($50-80).** The entry-level option. The Little Giant 9200 and Hovabator 1602N are the two most common models and have been used by hobbyist hatchers for decades. "Still air" means there is no fan inside --- heat rises from an element in the top and creates a temperature gradient from top to bottom. This gradient means the temperature at egg level must be measured carefully (a thermometer placed on top of the eggs, not mounted on the wall of the incubator). Still-air incubators are adequate for small hatches (12-42 eggs depending on model) but require more monitoring and have lower hatch rates than forced-air models because of less even heat distribution.

**Target temperature (still air): 101.5 degrees F measured at the top of the eggs.** The gradient means the bottom of the eggs may be 1-2 degrees cooler, which is fine --- it mimics the thermal gradient under a sitting hen, where the contact surface is warmest and the bottom of the egg is coolest.

**Forced-air incubator ($100-200+).** A forced-air incubator has an internal fan that circulates air, eliminating the temperature gradient and providing uniform heat to all eggs regardless of position. The Brinsea Mini Advance ($160-200) is widely considered the best small-scale forced-air incubator available. It holds 7 standard eggs, has automatic turning, digital temperature control, and a humidity display. For a first-time hatcher who wants the highest possible success rate, a Brinsea is the recommendation. Other solid forced-air options include the Brinsea Ovation series (24-56 eggs), the Nurture Right 360 ($80-100, 22 eggs, good mid-range), and the GQF Sportsman (large-scale, cabinet-style, $500+).

**Target temperature (forced air): 99.5 degrees F.** The uniform air temperature means the entire egg is at the same temperature, so the set point is lower than still-air to achieve the same embryo temperature.

**The temperature difference matters.** Setting a still-air incubator to 99.5 means the embryo at the top of the egg is at 99.5 but the bottom is at 97-98, which is too cold. Setting a forced-air incubator to 101.5 means the entire embryo is at 101.5, which is too hot. Know your incubator type and use the correct target.

### Egg Turner

An automatic egg turner ($25-40) fits inside most still-air incubators and slowly rotates the eggs several times per day. Turning prevents the embryo from adhering to the shell membrane, which is fatal. Without an automatic turner, you must manually turn the eggs by hand a minimum of three times per day (five is better), at roughly even intervals. Each turn should rotate the egg approximately 180 degrees. Mark each egg with an X on one side and an O on the other with a pencil (not a marker --- solvents can penetrate the shell) so you can confirm that every egg has been turned.

The Brinsea Mini Advance includes automatic turning. For a Little Giant or Hovabator, the matching auto-turner is an add-on.

If you can afford the turner, buy it. The failure mode of forgetting to turn eggs is silent --- you will not know the embryos are stuck until they fail to hatch, and by then it is too late. The turner eliminates that risk entirely.

### Hygrometer

A hygrometer measures relative humidity inside the incubator. Many incubators do not include one, or include one that is inaccurate. Buy a small digital hygrometer ($8-15) and calibrate it using the salt test: seal it in a ziplock bag with a bottle cap containing a paste of table salt and water for 12 hours. It should read 75%. Note the offset and adjust your readings accordingly.

**Why humidity matters:** Too little humidity causes excessive moisture loss through the shell, producing small, weak chicks. Too much humidity drowns the embryo in its own fluids because the air cell at the fat end of the egg does not expand enough. Getting humidity right is the single most common challenge for new hatchers.

### Candling Light

A candling light is simply a bright, focused light source that you press against the eggshell in a dark room to see the developing embryo inside. A high-powered LED flashlight (200+ lumens with a focused beam) works perfectly. Dedicated egg candlers ($10-20) have a cup-shaped end that fits the egg and blocks ambient light, which is convenient but not necessary.

You will candle on Day 7, Day 14, and never again after Day 18.

## Part 2: Sourcing Fertile Eggs

### Local Breeders (Recommended)

Fertile eggs from a local breeder are your best option. They have not been shipped (shipping damages the air cell and reduces hatch rates), they are fresh (fertility declines after 7-10 days from laying), and you can often visit the flock, meet the rooster, and see the conditions the parent birds live in.

Where to find them:
- **Craigslist** --- search "hatching eggs" or "fertile eggs" in your area. The PNW has an active poultry community.
- **Facebook poultry groups** --- search for your state or region plus "poultry" or "chickens." Washington and Oregon both have large, active groups.
- **Feed store bulletin boards** --- Coastal Farm & Ranch, Wilco, and independent feed stores often have physical bulletin boards where local breeders advertise.
- **County poultry clubs** --- Washington Poultry Fanciers, Oregon Poultry Association, and similar clubs connect breeders.

**Expected hatch rate from local eggs: 70-85%.** This means that out of 12 eggs, expect 8-10 to hatch if your incubation technique is good. Some will be infertile (no rooster access, old eggs), some will quit early (genetic or environmental), and some will fail at hatch (malposition, humidity problems).

**Cost:** $2-10 per egg depending on breed. Common breeds (Rhode Island Reds, Barred Rocks, mixed-breed barnyard eggs) are cheap. Rare breeds (Ayam Cemani, quality Marans, show-line Orpingtons) can be $10-30+ per egg.

### Shipped Eggs

If you want a specific breed that is not available locally, shipped eggs are an option. Major hatcheries (Murray McMurray, Cackle Hatchery, Meyer Hatchery) and private breeders sell hatching eggs via priority mail. The eggs arrive in foam-padded boxes, usually within 2-3 days.

**Expected hatch rate from shipped eggs: 40-60%.** The lower rate is due to:
- **Detached air cells.** The air cell at the fat end can detach from the shell membrane during shipping, floating to the side or breaking into bubbles. Detached air cells are visible when candling and significantly reduce hatch probability.
- **Micro-cracks.** Invisible hairline fractures in the shell allow bacteria to enter and moisture to escape.
- **Temperature extremes.** Eggs sitting in a postal truck in summer heat or winter cold can suffer embryo damage before they arrive.

**If using shipped eggs:** Let them rest fat-end-up in an egg carton at room temperature (60-70 degrees F) for 12-24 hours before setting. This allows the air cell to stabilize after the trauma of shipping. Do not wash them. Do not refrigerate them.

## Part 3: The 21-Day Timeline

### Before Day 1: Preparation

- Run the incubator empty for 24-48 hours to stabilize temperature and humidity. Adjust until the thermometer reads your target (99.5 forced air / 101.5 still air) consistently for at least 12 hours.
- Set humidity to 45-55% for the first 18 days. Add water to the incubator's reservoir troughs as needed. Humidity is controlled by the surface area of water exposed inside the incubator --- fill more channels for higher humidity, fewer for lower.
- Gather your eggs. If from a local breeder, collect within the past 7 days. Store at 50-60 degrees F, fat end up, until setting. Turn stored eggs once daily by propping one end of the carton on a book and switching ends each day.

### Day 1: Set the Eggs

Place eggs in the incubator (or on the auto-turner rails) with the **fat end up** or, if laying on their sides, with the fat end slightly elevated. The fat end contains the air cell, and the embryo must orient toward the air cell for proper development and hatching position. An egg set pointy-end-up has a significantly higher rate of malposition at hatch.

Record the date, number of eggs, source, and breed in your incubation log. Close the incubator. Walk away. Do not open it again until candling day.

**The hardest part of Day 1 is the walking away.** The incubator is sealed, the temperature is stable, the humidity is set. There is nothing to do. The eggs do not need you right now. The embryonic disc --- a barely visible cluster of a few hundred cells sitting on top of the yolk --- has begun dividing. By the end of Day 1, the first blood vessels are forming. You cannot see any of this. Trust the process.

### Day 2-6: The Quiet Days

Check temperature and humidity twice daily (morning and evening). Add water if humidity drops below 40%. If you are hand-turning, turn eggs three to five times per day at roughly even intervals. Keep a checkmark log so you do not lose count.

Do not candle yet. On Day 3, the heart begins beating. On Day 4, the eye is visible as a dark spot. On Day 5, the limb buds appear. None of this is visible through the shell yet --- the embryo is too small and the network of blood vessels too sparse. Candling before Day 7 produces ambiguous results that cause more anxiety than information.

**Temperature excursions:** If the temperature spikes (incubator malfunction, power surge, room temperature change), act quickly but do not panic. A brief spike to 103-104 degrees F for under an hour is usually survivable. A sustained spike above 104 for several hours is likely lethal. A brief cool-down (power outage, opened lid too long) to 90-95 degrees F for an hour is usually tolerable --- a broody hen gets off the nest to eat and drink every day, and the eggs cool temporarily. What kills embryos is sustained deviation, not brief fluctuations.

### Day 7: First Candle

This is the day you have been waiting for. Darken the room completely. Take eggs from the incubator one at a time, candle each one, and return it within 30 seconds. The entire candling session should take less than 10-15 minutes for a dozen eggs. Speed matters --- every minute the incubator is open, temperature drops.

**What you are looking for:**

- **Fertile and developing (keeper):** A network of blood vessels radiating from a dark central spot (the embryo). It looks like a red spider web inside the egg. You may see the embryo move. The air cell at the fat end should be visible as a clear, dark space. This is a good egg. Return it to the incubator.

- **Infertile (clear):** The egg looks the same as the day you set it --- uniform yellow/orange yolk shadow with no blood vessels, no dark spot, no development. This egg was never fertilized or the embryo died before blood vessel formation. **Remove it.** An infertile egg occupies space, absorbs heat, and if it goes bad, can explode and contaminate the entire incubator with bacteria.

- **Blood ring (early death):** A ring of blood visible inside the egg with no embryo or vessel network. This indicates that the embryo began developing but died in the first few days, and the blood vessels retracted into a ring pattern. **Remove it.**

- **Questionable:** You cannot quite tell. Maybe there is a faint shadow, maybe a slight vein. Mark this egg with a question mark and recandle on Day 10. If still ambiguous, remove it.

**Expect to remove 10-25% of eggs at the Day 7 candle** from local eggs, and 25-40% from shipped eggs. This is normal and does not indicate a problem with your technique. Infertility and early embryonic death are natural phenomena that occur even under broody hens.

### Day 14: Second Candle

By Day 14, the embryo fills most of the egg. When candled, a developing egg looks mostly dark, with the air cell visible at the fat end and possibly some movement visible as a darker shadow shifting within the dark mass. The extensive blood vessel network is no longer individually visible because the embryo and its membranes have expanded to fill the space.

Remove any eggs that are still clear or show signs of having quit since Day 7 (no growth, foul odor, weeping). A dead egg at this stage may smell --- that sulfurous, unmistakable odor is bacteria breaking down the contents. Remove it immediately. If an egg smells, it is dead and a contamination risk.

### Day 18: Lockdown

This is the critical transition. On Day 18, three things happen:

1. **Stop turning.** Remove the auto-turner (or stop hand-turning) and lay the eggs on their sides on the incubator floor or on a non-slip liner. The chick inside is orienting itself into hatching position: head tucked under the right wing, beak pointed toward the air cell. Turning after Day 18 can disorient the chick and cause malposition.

2. **Raise humidity to 65-70%.** Fill all water channels. Add wet sponges if necessary. The higher humidity prevents the membrane inside the shell from drying out and shrink-wrapping the chick as it tries to hatch. Low humidity at lockdown is the number one cause of chicks that pip but cannot zip.

3. **Do not open the incubator.** From this moment until the last chick has hatched and dried, the incubator stays closed. Every time you open the lid, humidity crashes --- it takes 30-60 minutes to recover, and during that time, pipped chicks can become shrink-wrapped. **Do not open it. Do not peek. Do not "help."** This is the hardest instruction to follow, and it is the most important.

### Day 19: Waiting

Nothing visible may happen. Inside the eggs, the chicks are absorbing the remaining yolk sac into their abdomen (this provides 48-72 hours of nutrition after hatch, which is why newly hatched chicks do not need food immediately). The chick is also transitioning from breathing through the chorioallantoic membrane (the blood-vessel network attached to the inside of the shell) to breathing with its lungs by poking its beak into the air cell. You may hear faint peeping from inside the eggs. This is called "internal pip" --- the chick has pierced the air cell membrane and is breathing air for the first time, but has not yet broken through the shell.

### Day 20-21: Pip, Zip, Hatch

**Pip:** A small crack or bump appears on the shell, usually on the upper third of the egg near the fat end. This is the chick's egg tooth (a small, hard nub on the tip of the upper beak) punching through from the inside. A pip may appear as early as Day 19 or as late as Day 22. Once the pip appears, the chick rests. It may rest for 6-12 hours or even longer. **This is normal.** The chick is absorbing the remaining yolk and blood vessels before proceeding. Do not intervene.

**Zip:** After resting, the chick begins to rotate inside the egg, chipping a line of cracks around the circumference. This is called "zipping." The zip usually takes 1-4 hours but can take longer. The chick rotates by pushing with its feet against the small end of the egg while chipping with the egg tooth at the large end.

**Hatch:** When the zip is complete (or nearly complete), the chick pushes the cap off the egg and tumbles out --- wet, exhausted, and looking like a miniature dinosaur. It will lie still, breathing heavily, for 10-30 minutes. Then it will begin to fluff up as the down dries. Within 2-4 hours, a healthy chick is on its feet, peeping, and stumbling around the incubator looking for something to do.

**Leave hatchlings in the incubator until they are fluffy and active.** A wet chick removed from the warm, humid incubator can become chilled quickly. Dry chicks can stay in the incubator for up to 24 hours without food or water (they are living off the absorbed yolk sac). Wait until you have a batch of dry, active chicks, then move them all to the brooder together.

**The 12-24 hour rule:** The entire process from first pip to fully out can take 12-24 hours, and this is completely normal. First-time hatchers are overwhelmingly tempted to "help" a chick that has been pipped for 12 hours with no visible progress. **Resist this urge.** Premature assisted hatch almost always does more harm than good because the chick may still have active blood vessels in the membrane that will hemorrhage if torn. The chick is resting, absorbing, and preparing. If, after 24+ hours from pip with no progress and no peeping, assisted hatch may be warranted --- but this is a judgment call with significant risk, and many experienced hatchers still choose not to intervene.

## Part 4: Troubleshooting

### Temperature Spikes

**Cause:** Incubator thermostat failure, room temperature change (window left open, heater turned on), incubator placed in direct sunlight.
**Prevention:** Place the incubator in a room with stable temperature (65-75 degrees F), away from windows and vents. Use a separate thermometer to verify the incubator's built-in reading. Check twice daily.
**If it happens:** Open the incubator briefly to let excess heat escape, then close and monitor until temperature stabilizes. Do not add cool water or ice --- this causes humidity spikes and rapid temperature oscillation. A brief spike is usually survivable. Log the event.

### Humidity Crashes

**Cause:** Water reservoirs run dry, incubator opened too long, dry ambient air (forced-air heating in winter).
**Prevention:** Check water levels daily. Add warm (not hot, not cold --- room temperature) water to reservoirs. In dry climates or winter-heated houses, place wet sponges inside the incubator or put a damp towel near it.
**During lockdown:** If humidity drops below 55% during lockdown, add warm water through the ventilation holes using a squeeze bottle or turkey baster without opening the lid. Some hatchers run a length of airline tubing from outside the incubator to the water reservoir, allowing water addition without opening.

### Assisted Hatch: The Controversy

The question of whether to help a struggling chick out of its shell is the single most debated topic in the incubation hobby. The arguments:

**Against intervening:** The hatching process is a natural fitness test. A chick that cannot hatch on its own may have developmental problems that make its long-term viability questionable. Intervening risks tearing blood vessels in the membrane, causing fatal hemorrhage. Chicks that require assistance often have unabsorbed yolk sacs and are weaker than self-hatched chicks.

**For intervening:** Not every failure to hatch indicates genetic unfitness. Humidity problems, temperature spikes, and malposition are environmental/mechanical failures, not genetic ones. A perfectly healthy chick can die in the shell because the membrane dried out due to a humidity crash on Day 19. Helping that chick is correcting your mistake, not overriding natural selection.

**The practical answer:** If a chick has pipped but made no progress in 24+ hours, and the membrane looks dry and papery (white/brown rather than moist and translucent), you can carefully chip away shell around the pip, moistening the membrane with warm water as you go. If you see blood in the membrane, **stop immediately** --- the blood vessels are still active and the chick is not ready. Wrap the egg in a warm, damp paper towel, return it to the incubator, and check again in 6 hours. This graduated approach minimizes risk while giving the chick a chance.

## Part 5: The Emotional Reality

This section exists because no one tells you about it before your first hatch, and you deserve to know.

Not every egg will hatch. In a typical batch of 12 local eggs with good technique, expect 8-10 to hatch. That means 2-4 will not. Some will be clear at Day 7 --- infertile, never started, painless to discard. Some will quit between Day 7 and Day 18 --- the embryo developed partially and then stopped. You will candle them, see the dark mass that is no longer moving, and remove a dead egg that almost became a chick. This is uncomfortable, and it is real biology.

Some chicks will pip and not make it out. You will watch a pip appear, hear peeping, wait the prescribed 24 hours, and then silence. This is the hardest part. You may open the egg later and find a fully formed chick that died during hatch --- malpositioned, membrane dried, yolk sac not absorbed, or sometimes for no reason you can determine. This is not your fault. Hatch failure rates of 10-30% are normal even under expert conditions and under broody hens. Life is not guaranteed.

And some chicks will hatch and not thrive. A chick that is lethargic, splayed-legged, unable to stand, or not eating within 24-48 hours of hatch may not survive despite your best efforts. The decision about how long to intervene, and when to stop, is yours. It is a decision that every animal keeper must eventually face, and there is no formula for it.

What carries you through these moments is the ones that make it. The first chick that punches through the shell and peeps at you with one eye while the other is still stuck shut with membrane fluid --- that chick does not know about your temperature anxiety or your humidity calculations or the three eggs that did not make it. It knows that it is alive, and it is hungry, and you are warm and large and therefore probably relevant to its immediate survival. That first meeting is worth every hour of the 21 days.

## Part 6: After the Hatch --- Brooder Setup

Newly hatched chicks need a brooder --- a warm, enclosed space with heat, food, and water --- for the first 4-6 weeks until they are feathered enough to regulate their own body temperature.

### The Basic Brooder

- **Container:** A large plastic storage tub (30-gallon minimum for 6-8 chicks), a stock tank, or a cardboard box lined with plastic. The walls should be at least 12 inches high (chicks start flying at 1-2 weeks).
- **Bedding:** Paper towels for the first 3 days (prevents chicks from eating bedding), then pine shavings (not cedar --- cedar oils are toxic to chicks). Do not use newspaper --- it is too slick and causes splayed legs.
- **Heat source:** A brooder heat plate ($30-50, e.g., Brinsea EcoGlow) is the modern recommendation. Chicks walk underneath it like they would under a hen, and it eliminates the fire risk of heat lamps. If using a heat lamp, use a red or ceramic bulb and secure the lamp with a clamp AND a chain or wire backup so it cannot fall into the bedding.
- **Temperature:** 95 degrees F at chick level in the first week. Reduce by 5 degrees per week until reaching ambient temperature (approximately 70 degrees F) at 5-6 weeks. With a heat plate, chicks self-regulate --- they go under when cold and come out when warm.
- **Food:** Chick starter feed (medicated or unmedicated, 18-20% protein). Medicated starter contains amprolium, a coccidiostat that helps prevent coccidiosis. If your chicks were not vaccinated for coccidiosis at the hatchery, medicated starter is recommended.
- **Water:** A chick waterer (the inverted jar type, $5-8) filled with clean water. Add marbles or clean pebbles to the trough for the first few days to prevent chicks from falling in and drowning. Chicks drown in remarkably shallow water.

### The First 48 Hours

Dip each chick's beak in the water when you place it in the brooder. This teaches it where the water is. Sprinkle a small amount of starter feed on a paper towel --- the chicks will peck at the crumbs and quickly learn to find the feeder. Monitor temperature: if chicks huddle directly under the heat source and peep loudly, they are cold (lower the heat plate or move the lamp closer). If they scatter to the edges of the brooder and pant, they are hot (raise the heat source). If they are distributed evenly, moving between the warm zone and the cooler edges, the temperature is right.

### Weeks 2-6

Chicks grow astonishingly fast. By Week 2, wing feathers are emerging. By Week 3, they are attempting short flights. By Week 4-5, they are substantially feathered. By Week 6, most breeds can transition to an outdoor coop if nighttime temperatures are above 50 degrees F.

During this period, begin handling the chicks daily. Chicks that are handled become friendly, approachable adults. Chicks that are not handled become flighty adults. Five minutes of gentle handling per day per chick is sufficient.

At 6-8 weeks, you will face the rooster question. If you hatched a straight-run batch (unsexed), approximately 50% are likely male. Most municipalities allow only hens. Rehoming young roosters is possible but not always easy --- post on local poultry groups, ask at feed stores, contact local farms. Have a plan before the crowing starts.

## Part 7: What You Will Learn

This project is a compressed course in multiple disciplines, and that compression is the point. In 21 days plus a few weeks of brooding, you will have:

- Designed and managed a life-support system (temperature, humidity, ventilation)
- Practiced daily data collection and monitoring
- Made high-stakes decisions with incomplete information (Is this egg developing? Should I intervene?)
- Observed embryonic development firsthand through candling
- Confronted the reality that biology does not guarantee outcomes
- Raised a living animal from its first breath

These experiences are not available from a textbook or a video. They are available from an incubator, a dozen eggs, and 21 days of paying attention.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Embryology, Experimental Process]: This try session is a full applied-science project: environmental control, daily measurement, data logging, and direct observation of vertebrate embryonic development. The troubleshooting and decision-making components exercise experimental reasoning under real-world uncertainty --- the kind of science where the variables are alive and the stakes are not abstract.
- **Nature Studies** --- [Life Cycles, Animal Care]: Incubation is the most direct possible engagement with avian life cycles, from fertilization through hatch through juvenile care. The emotional components --- loss, uncertainty, responsibility --- are part of what makes animal care a practice rather than merely a subject.
- **Home Economics** --- [Animal Husbandry, Self-Sufficiency]: Hatching and raising chicks is foundational to small-scale poultry keeping and food self-sufficiency. The skills learned here --- environmental management, animal observation, resource planning --- are the same skills used to maintain a productive backyard flock.
