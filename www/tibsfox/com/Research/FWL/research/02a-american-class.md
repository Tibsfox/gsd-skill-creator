# The American Class: Homegrown Excellence

The American Class of the American Poultry Association represents something profoundly meaningful in the story of poultry: these are the breeds that were born here. Developed on American soil by American breeders who needed birds that could handle harsh winters, lay reliably, put meat on the table, and look magnificent doing it, these breeds embody a tradition of practical beauty that stretches back to the early nineteenth century. Many of them are now rare, their populations dwindling as industrial agriculture favored a handful of commercial hybrids over centuries of careful breeding. To raise American Class birds today is to participate in a living act of conservation.

The APA formally recognized the American Class in its first Standard of Perfection, published in 1874. The class is defined by breeds originating in the United States and Canada. These are overwhelmingly dual-purpose birds: substantial enough for the table, productive enough for the egg basket, and hardy enough to thrive in continental climates with bitter winters and humid summers. They tend toward calm, tractable temperaments, making them ideal for backyard flocks, small farms, and exhibition.

## Plymouth Rock

### Origin and History

The Plymouth Rock is arguably America's most iconic chicken breed. Its development began in the 1860s in New England, though the exact origins are a subject of friendly debate among poultry historians. The Barred variety, the original, was first exhibited at a poultry show in Boston in 1849, though those early birds disappeared from the scene shortly after. The breed as we know it was stabilized in the 1860s and 1870s, likely through crosses involving Dominiques, Black Javas, Cochins, and possibly Malays. D.A. Upham of Worcester, Massachusetts is frequently credited with the breed's refinement.

The Plymouth Rock was admitted to the APA Standard of Perfection in 1874 as a founding breed. It rapidly became the most popular farm chicken in America, a position it held from the late nineteenth century through the Second World War. During that era, the Barred Plymouth Rock was quite simply *the* American chicken.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes seven varieties of Plymouth Rock: **Barred** (1874), **White** (1888), **Buff** (1894), **Silver Penciled** (1907), **Partridge** (1910), **Columbian** (1910), and **Blue** (1920).

### Physical Description

Plymouth Rocks are large, broad-bodied birds with a distinctly rectangular silhouette. They carry themselves with a confident, upright bearing. The body is long, broad, and deep, with a full breast and a moderately long back that slopes slightly toward the tail. The single comb is medium-sized, upright, with five well-defined points. Earlobes, wattles, and comb are bright red. Eyes are reddish bay. Legs are clean (unfeathered) and yellow, with four toes.

The Barred variety displays the breed's most recognizable plumage: alternating bars of dark and light gray running across each feather, creating a striking striped effect that shimmers in sunlight --- hold a single breast feather up to the light and you see the barring resolve into precise, parallel lines of slate and steel, each bar the same width, ruler-straight across the feather vane. From across a yard, a flock of Barred Rocks looks like a convention of pinstripe suits, each bird wearing the same sharp pattern with slight individual variation that a practiced eye can read. Males tend to have lighter, more even barring than females --- a rooster's silver-white bars are noticeably wider, giving him a frosted appearance next to his darker, more crisply patterned hens. This sex difference historically allowed sex-linking in breeding programs and still lets experienced breeders guess the sex of chicks by the intensity and shape of the white head spot at hatch.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9.5 lbs, Hen 7.5 lbs, Cockerel 8 lbs, Pullet 6 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Plymouth Rocks are famously docile and friendly. They tolerate confinement well but also excel as free-range birds. Hens are reliable layers of large brown eggs, producing approximately 200-280 eggs per year depending on the strain. Heritage lines bred for exhibition will produce on the lower end of that range, while production-bred lines approach the higher end. Hens will go broody, though not with the frequency of Asiatic breeds, and they make attentive mothers.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Plymouth Rocks as **Recovering**. The White variety, once the foundation of the American broiler industry (before being supplanted by the Cornish Cross), remains relatively common. The Barred variety has seen a strong resurgence among backyard keepers. However, several varieties, particularly the Silver Penciled and Columbian, remain quite rare.

## Wyandotte

### Origin and History

The Wyandotte was developed in the 1870s in New York and the upper Midwest, named for the Wyandot people indigenous to the region. The Silver Laced variety, the original, was created through crosses involving Sequin or Sebright-type birds, Dark Brahmas, and Silver Spangled Hamburgs. The breed was admitted to the APA Standard in 1883.

The Wyandotte quickly became one of America's premier dual-purpose breeds, prized for its cold hardiness, consistent laying, and the distinctive laced plumage that made it a showstopper on the exhibition circuit.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Silver Laced** (1883), **Golden Laced** (1888), **White** (1888), **Black** (1893), **Buff** (1893), **Columbian** (1905), **Partridge** (1901), and **Silver Penciled** (1902). Additional varieties exist in bantam form.

### Physical Description

The Wyandotte is a round, curvy bird with a distinctly different silhouette from the rectangular Plymouth Rock. Where the Rock is a brick, the Wyandotte is a ball --- broad and deep, with a short back, a full, rounded breast, and a compact rear that gives the bird an almost circular side profile. Pick up a Wyandotte and you feel the density immediately: a solid, warm weight that fills your hands, the body surprisingly muscular beneath feathers that lie smooth and tight against a frame built for cold-weather endurance. The tail is relatively short and carried at a moderate angle, contributing to the rounded silhouette rather than disrupting it.

The most distinctive feature of the Wyandotte is its **rose comb** --- a flat, broad comb that sits close to the head like a textured crimson cap, covered in small rounded points ("work") and tapering to a slender spike at the rear. Run your finger along a rose comb and you feel the pebbly texture, each point a tiny raised dome, the whole structure hugging the skull with no vulnerable lobes or points to catch freezing rain. This comb is supremely practical: where a single comb's tall, thin points are essentially fins radiating body heat (an advantage in summer, a frostbite invitation in winter), the rose comb's low, compact architecture minimizes exposed surface area. In PNW Arctic outflow events --- those bitter east-wind episodes that drop Puget Sound lowland temperatures into the teens --- a Wyandotte's rose comb stays pink and healthy while single-combed birds in the same coop may develop blackened, frost-damaged comb tips.

The **Silver Laced** variety is the breed's signature and one of the most visually arresting patterns in all of poultry. Each feather is a miniature frame: a clean white center bordered by a precise, even band of glossy black that follows the feather's contour like a pen stroke. Hold a single breast feather to the light and you see the architecture --- the white center glowing translucent, the black lacing sharp-edged and lustrous with a faint greenish sheen where the light hits it. From across a yard, the cumulative effect is breathtaking: the hen appears to be wearing chainmail, each link perfectly proportioned, the pattern flowing unbroken from hackle to tail. The lacing should be uniform in width --- not too broad (which produces a muddy, dark bird) nor too narrow (which makes the black disappear) --- and free of "peppering" (black specks in the white) or "bleeding" (white in the black). Achieving this precision across every feather on the body is one of the supreme challenges of exhibition poultry breeding.

**Golden Laced** birds display the same lacing architecture with rich golden-bay centers replacing the white --- imagine the Silver Laced pattern dipped in warm honey. The gold should be even and saturated, not brassy or washed-out. **Blue Laced Red** Wyandottes (not yet APA recognized but wildly popular) substitute slate-blue lacing for black on a deep mahogany-red ground, producing a bird of extraordinary warmth and complexity. The **Columbian** variety wears the classic white-body-with-black-hackle-and-tail pattern, clean and sharp.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Wyandottes are calm but can be assertive, often rising to the top of the pecking order in mixed flocks without the aggressive posturing of a Rhode Island Red --- they simply walk to the feeder first, and the other birds let them. They have a quiet confidence that experienced keepers recognize and appreciate. A Wyandotte hen does not run to you like an Orpington; she strolls over with a deliberate, slightly rolling gait that reflects her wide body and short back, assessing whether you have treats before committing to the approach. Once she decides you are worth her time, she will tolerate handling with dignified patience, sitting in your lap with a low, contented cluck that sounds like a very small engine idling.

They are excellent cold-weather birds and maintain good production through winter --- the combination of rose comb, dense plumage, and substantial body mass makes them among the most winter-hardy breeds in the American Class. Hens lay approximately 180-260 large brown eggs per year, with the shell color a warm, medium brown that varies slightly between strains. They will occasionally go broody --- not as reliably as Cochins or Silkies, but often enough that a keeper who wants natural hatching can usually find a willing Wyandotte. They are dependable, steady birds that reward good care with years of reliable performance. In a PNW flock, a Wyandotte is the bird you can count on: she will lay through the dark months, shrug off the rain, and maintain her composure when the Cooper's Hawk swoops low enough to scatter the Leghorns into hysterics.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Wyandottes as **Recovering**. Silver Laced and Golden Laced varieties are relatively available, but the Blue, Columbian, and Silver Penciled varieties are significantly harder to find in quality breeding stock.

## Rhode Island Red

### Origin and History

The Rhode Island Red is the state bird of Rhode Island and one of the most successful chicken breeds ever developed. Its creation was a collaborative effort by farmers in Little Compton, Rhode Island and nearby Massachusetts, beginning around 1854. The breed was refined over several decades using Red Malay Game birds, Leghorns, and various Asiatic breeds. William Tripp and John Macomber are among the breeders most credited with the foundation work.

The single-comb variety was admitted to the APA in 1904, with the rose-comb variety following in 1906.

### Physical Description

Rhode Island Reds are large, rectangular birds with a distinctive deep, lustrous mahogany-red plumage that should be as dark and even as possible. The undercolor is red or salmon, never slate. The tail is black, and some black may appear in the wings and hackle. The body is long, flat on top, and brick-shaped — breeders describe the ideal silhouette as an oblong block. Eyes are reddish-orange. Legs are yellow, sometimes tinged with reddish horn. Both single-comb and rose-comb varieties are recognized.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Rhode Island Reds are vigorous, alert, and sometimes assertive. Roosters can be protective. The breed is legendary for egg production: heritage strains lay 200-300 large brown eggs per year, with production strains (often lighter in color) approaching the higher end. They are hardy in both heat and cold, efficient foragers, and generally resistant to common poultry diseases. They are the backbone breed behind many modern commercial brown-egg hybrids (ISA Brown, Red Star, etc.).

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Rhode Island Reds as **Recovering**. The production strains are ubiquitous, but the original heritage type — the deep mahogany, heavy-bodied bird that formed the breed — is considerably less common. Rose-comb Rhode Island Reds are genuinely rare.

## Rhode Island White

### Origin and History

The Rhode Island White is a distinct breed, not merely a white variety of the Rhode Island Red. It was developed by J. Alonzo Jocoy of Peacedale, Rhode Island beginning around 1888, using White Wyandottes, Partridge Cochins, and Rose Comb White Leghorns. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1922.

### Physical Description

Rhode Island Whites share the general body type of Rhode Island Reds — long, rectangular, and substantial — but are pure white in plumage with a rose comb. Eyes are reddish-bay. Legs and feet are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Calm and docile, Rhode Island Whites are reliable producers of 200-250 large brown eggs per year. Their temperament is notably more placid than the sometimes-assertive Rhode Island Red, making them excellent choices for mixed flocks and first-time keepers. They are hardy in cold weather --- the rose comb provides meaningful frostbite resistance compared to single-combed breeds, a genuine advantage in PNW winters during Arctic outflow events. Rhode Island Whites handle confinement well but also forage competently on pasture. Their white plumage, while striking, does make them more visible to aerial predators, a consideration for free-range flocks in areas with hawk pressure. The breed dresses out well as a table bird, with the white plumage producing a clean carcass free of the dark pin feathers that can be cosmetically unappealing on darker-feathered breeds.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. Rhode Island Whites are significantly rarer than Rhode Island Reds and deserve active conservation attention. The breed's obscurity is partly a naming problem --- many potential keepers assume it is simply a color variety of the Red rather than a distinct breed with its own history, genetics, and breeding community. Finding quality breeding stock requires dedication and networking within heritage breed communities. The breed's combination of white egg-alternative genetics (brown eggs from a white-feathered bird), gentle temperament, rose comb hardiness, and genuine dual-purpose utility makes it an underappreciated gem in the American Class.

## New Hampshire

### Origin and History

The New Hampshire was developed in the 1910s and 1920s by New Hampshire poultry breeders, most notably Professor "Red" Richardson at the University of New Hampshire, by selectively breeding Rhode Island Reds for faster feathering, faster maturity, larger eggs, and greater meat production. It was admitted to the APA in 1935.

### Physical Description

New Hampshires resemble Rhode Island Reds but are lighter in color — a rich chestnut-red rather than the deep mahogany of the best Reds. They are somewhat broader and deeper in body. The plumage often shows considerable fading in sunlight, a trait that distinguishes the breed. Tail and wing feathers are black with a greenish sheen.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

New Hampshires are robust, vigorous birds with a competitive streak — they tend to be assertive at the feeder. They are fast-growing and were a primary meat breed before the Cornish Cross era. Hens lay approximately 200-280 large tinted to brown eggs per year. They go broody readily and are excellent mothers.

### Conservation Status

Listed as **Watch** by the Livestock Conservancy. New Hampshires were once one of America's most important commercial breeds but have been largely displaced by industrial hybrids. Quality exhibition stock is uncommon.

## Delaware

### Origin and History

The Delaware was developed in 1940 by George Ellis of Delaware, originally from a cross between Barred Plymouth Rock roosters and New Hampshire hens. The offspring that were mostly white with black barring restricted to the hackle, tail, and wings were selected and bred to create this new breed. It was admitted to the APA in 1952.

The Delaware was briefly the dominant broiler breed on the Delmarva Peninsula before being almost entirely replaced by the Cornish Cross in the late 1950s. Its fall from commercial dominance was swift and nearly total.

### Physical Description

Delawares are striking birds: predominantly white with a distinctive Columbian pattern of black barring in the hackle, tail, and wing tips. The barring is a critical show feature — it should be clean and well-defined, not smutty. The body is moderately long and well-muscled, with a broad breast. Comb is single, medium-sized, and bright red. Legs are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Delawares are calm, friendly, and curious. They grow quickly and dress out well as table birds. Hens lay approximately 200-280 large brown eggs per year and are reasonably broody. They handle cold weather admirably and are excellent foragers.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Delaware is a breed that went from commercial ubiquity to near-extinction within a single decade. Conservation breeders have been working to restore quality in the breed, but it remains uncommon. Every flock of Delawares is a hedge against genetic loss.

## Jersey Giant

### Origin and History

The Jersey Giant is the largest purebred chicken breed developed in America. It was created in the 1870s through the 1890s by brothers John and Thomas Black of Burlington County, New Jersey. Their goal was to develop a breed large enough to replace the turkey for table use. The foundation stock included Black Javas, Black Langshans, and Dark Brahmas. The breed was originally called "Black Giants" and was admitted to the APA in 1922.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes three varieties: **Black** (1922), **White** (1947), and **Blue** (2003).

### Physical Description

Jersey Giants are massive, upright birds with deep, broad bodies. Despite their size, they should be well-proportioned and carry themselves with alertness, not lumbering heaviness. The Black variety has black plumage with a beetle-green sheen. Legs are black with yellow foot bottoms — this is a key identification feature. The White variety has pure white plumage with willow-colored legs. The Blue variety has an even blue-gray color with dark lacing.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 13 lbs, Hen 10 lbs, Cockerel 11 lbs, Pullet 8 lbs. Some individuals may grow larger.

### Temperament and Production

Jersey Giants are gentle, docile birds — surprisingly so, given their imposing size. They are slow to mature, often taking 6-9 months to reach full size, which made them uneconomical for commercial production. Hens lay approximately 150-200 extra-large brown eggs per year. They can go broody but their great weight sometimes poses a challenge for egg incubation.

### Conservation Status

Listed as **Watch** by the Livestock Conservancy. The Black variety is the most available, while the White and Blue varieties are considerably rarer. The breed's slow growth rate means it will never return to commercial relevance, but it occupies an irreplaceable niche in heritage poultry.

## Dominique

### Origin and History

The Dominique holds the distinction of being America's oldest chicken breed. Its origins are uncertain but trace to the colonial era, likely descending from fowl brought by early settlers from England and France. The name may derive from birds imported from the French colony of Saint-Domingue (Haiti). The breed was common across the eastern United States by the early 1800s and was admitted to the first APA Standard of Perfection in 1874.

Dominiques were instrumental in the development of the Barred Plymouth Rock, and once the Plymouth Rock gained popularity, Dominiques nearly disappeared. By the 1970s, only four known flocks remained.

### Physical Description

Dominiques are medium-sized birds with a distinctive **rose comb** — this is the primary visual distinction between Dominiques and Barred Plymouth Rocks (which have single combs). The barring pattern is also subtler and less sharply defined than Plymouth Rock barring, appearing somewhat staggered or "cuckoo" rather than the precise parallel bars of the Plymouth Rock. The body is broad and full with a moderately long back. Legs are yellow and clean.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7 lbs, Hen 5 lbs, Cockerel 6 lbs, Pullet 4 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Dominiques are calm, hardy, and resourceful foragers. They are famously cold-hardy, their rose combs and tight, barred plumage insulating them against harsh winters. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium brown eggs per year and go broody willingly. Colonial Americans valued their feathers for stuffing pillows and mattresses.

### Conservation Status

Listed as **Watch** by the Livestock Conservancy. Dominiques have recovered significantly from their 1970s low point thanks to dedicated conservation breeders, but they remain uncommon. As America's oldest breed, they hold irreplaceable genetic heritage.

## Java

### Origin and History

The Java is the second-oldest breed developed in America, tracing to the early 1800s. Despite its name, it is an American creation, though it was likely developed from fowl imported from the island of Java and other Asian sources. The Black Java was admitted to the APA in 1883 and was instrumental in the development of both the Plymouth Rock and the Jersey Giant.

### Physical Description

Javas are large, sturdy birds with long, broad bodies and deep breasts. The Black Java has richly lustrous black plumage with a brilliant beetle-green sheen — it is one of the most beautiful black-feathered birds in all of poultry. The Mottled variety features black plumage with white tips on many feathers. Legs are black (or dark willow in Mottled) with yellow foot bottoms. The single comb is moderate.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9.5 lbs, Hen 7.5 lbs, Cockerel 8 lbs, Pullet 6.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Javas are gentle, slow-maturing birds that excel as dual-purpose farm fowl. They are excellent foragers and remarkably self-sufficient on pasture. Hens lay approximately 150-180 brown eggs per year and will go broody.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Black Java is the rarest variety, and the breed as a whole is among the most endangered of all American chicken breeds. Conservation efforts are active but the gene pool is narrow.

## Buckeye

### Origin and History

The Buckeye is the only American chicken breed created entirely by a woman. Nettie Metcalf of Warren, Ohio developed the breed in the 1890s, crossing Barred Plymouth Rocks, Buff Cochins, and dark-breasted red game fowl. She aimed to produce a hardy, dual-purpose bird suited to Ohio's harsh winters. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1904 and named for the state tree of Ohio, the buckeye (horse chestnut), whose dark mahogany nut resembles the breed's plumage color.

### Physical Description

Buckeyes are compact, muscular birds with unusually broad bodies and a distinctive slanting or sloped posture. They have a unique **pea comb** — the only American Class breed with a pea comb — which provides outstanding frostbite resistance. Plumage is a dark mahogany-red, darker than a Rhode Island Red, with a solid mahogany-to-black undercolor (never the slate undercolor found in many Reds). Tail is black. Legs are yellow.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 8 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Buckeyes are famously active, curious, and intelligent. They are known for hunting mice — a genuine mousing chicken is a peculiar but delightful distinction. They thrive in free-range environments and are among the most cold-hardy of all breeds thanks to their pea comb and tight plumage. Hens lay approximately 150-200 medium to large brown eggs per year.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list, recently upgraded from Threatened. The breed has seen a welcome resurgence among heritage poultry enthusiasts drawn to its unique history, hardiness, and personality.

## Chantecler

### Origin and History

The Chantecler is Canada's contribution to the American Class — the first chicken breed developed in Canada. It was created by Brother Wilfrid Chatelain at the Oka Agricultural Institute in Quebec beginning in 1908, using Dark Cornish, White Leghorn, Rhode Island Red, White Wyandotte, and White Plymouth Rock. His goal was a breed perfectly suited to Canadian winters: productive, meaty, and nearly frostbite-proof. The White variety was admitted to the APA in 1921, with the Partridge variety following in 1935.

### Physical Description

Chanteclers are well-muscled, broad-bodied birds with an almost comically small **cushion comb** — barely visible beneath the feathers — and tiny wattles. This extreme reduction in comb and wattle surface area is the breed's most distinctive adaptation, virtually eliminating frostbite risk even in severe arctic cold. The White variety is pure white; the Partridge variety displays the classic red-and-black partridge pattern. Legs are yellow and clean.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Chanteclers are gentle, calm birds that lay well even through the darkest Canadian winters. Hens produce approximately 180-220 large brown eggs per year. They are true dual-purpose birds with excellent meat qualities.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Chantecler is one of the most endangered breeds in the American Class. The Partridge variety is especially rare. Conservation of this uniquely Canadian breed is an urgent priority.

## Holland

### Origin and History

The Holland was developed at Rutgers University in New Jersey in the 1930s, with the goal of creating an American breed that laid white eggs while still being a useful dual-purpose bird. The foundation stock included White Leghorns, Rhode Island Reds, New Hampshires, and Lamonas. The White Holland was admitted to the APA in 1949, and the Barred Holland followed.

### Physical Description

Hollands are medium-to-large birds with a moderately long body and a well-rounded breast. The White variety is pure white with a single comb and yellow legs. The Barred variety resembles the Barred Plymouth Rock but with a slightly different barring pattern.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8.5 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Hollands are quiet, calm, and easily managed --- a temperament that distinguishes them sharply from the flighty Leghorns that contributed their white-egg genetics. This combination of American Class docility with Mediterranean egg color was the Holland's entire reason for being: Rutgers breeders wanted to prove that an American dual-purpose bird could lay white eggs without inheriting the nervous disposition that made Leghorns unpopular with small-flock keepers. They succeeded. Hens lay approximately 200-240 large white eggs per year, placing them among the more productive American Class breeds. They are cold-hardy, adaptable, and tolerant of both confinement and free-range conditions. The breed's quiet disposition makes it an excellent neighbor in suburban settings where noise complaints are a concern.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Holland is one of the rarest breeds in America. The Barred Holland is practically extinct; the White Holland is only slightly less so. This breed desperately needs conservation breeders.

## Lamona

### Origin and History

The Lamona was developed by Harry S. Lamon at the USDA's Bureau of Animal Industry in Beltsville, Maryland in the 1910s and 1920s, using White Plymouth Rocks, Silver-Gray Dorkings, and White Leghorns. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1933. It was intended as a superior dual-purpose bird for the American market.

### Physical Description

Lamonas are medium-to-large, clean-legged birds with white plumage, red earlobes, and a single comb. They have yellow skin and legs. The body is well-proportioned for a dual-purpose breed.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8 lbs, Hen 6 lbs, Cockerel 6.5 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Lamonas are calm and docile, with a steady temperament that reflects their development at a federal research station where manageability was a practical requirement. Hens lay approximately 180-240 large white eggs per year --- a notable production rate for a dual-purpose bird, and particularly valuable because white eggs from an American Class breed is an unusual combination. Most American breeds lay brown eggs; the Lamona and Holland are the exceptions, carrying Leghorn genetics that express white egg color while maintaining the heavier body type and calmer disposition of the dual-purpose American tradition. Lamonas are reasonably cold-hardy and forage well on pasture, though detailed behavioral information is scarce given the breed's extreme rarity.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Lamona is functionally extinct or nearly so. Some poultry historians believe no pure Lamona stock survives, though occasional claims surface from small flock keepers. If any remain, they represent the most urgent conservation priority in the American Class. The Lamona's story is a cautionary tale: a breed developed with government resources and scientific expertise, admitted to the APA Standard, and then allowed to vanish because no community of breeders formed around it. The breed never captured the public imagination the way the Plymouth Rock or Rhode Island Red did, and without a constituency, it had no safety net when agricultural modernization made it commercially irrelevant.

### PNW Considerations for American Class Breeds

The Pacific Northwest presents specific challenges and advantages for keepers of American Class breeds. The persistent rain from October through April demands covered runs and excellent drainage --- mud management is the defining husbandry challenge of PNW chicken keeping [FWL-06]. Rose-combed breeds like the Wyandotte, Dominique, and Buckeye have a natural advantage in PNW winters, where frostbite risk spikes during occasional Arctic outflow events that drop temperatures into the teens. Hawks are a year-round concern: Cooper's Hawks (*Accipiter cooperii*) are abundant in PNW suburban areas and will take bantam and small standard birds [FWL-08, AVI]. Raccoons (*Procyon lotor*) are the most persistent nocturnal predator, capable of defeating simple latches and reaching through standard chicken wire [RCN-02, RCN-03]. Hardware cloth rated at 1/2 inch, two-step latches, and secure automatic coop doors are essential for any PNW flock.

On the positive side, the PNW's mild winters mean that cold-hardy American breeds like the Plymouth Rock, Wyandotte, Australorp, and Buckeye thrive with minimal supplemental heat. The long growing season supports excellent pasture for foraging breeds like the Dominique and Buckeye. And the region's strong local-food culture has made heritage American breeds increasingly popular at PNW farmers' markets and in backyard flocks from Seattle to Portland [FWL-06, FWL-07].

---

The American Class tells the story of American poultry in microcosm. From the ubiquitous Plymouth Rock and Rhode Island Red, which shaped an entire industry, to the nearly lost Lamona and Holland, which represent what happens when market forces extinguish genetic diversity, these breeds are a living heritage. Each one carries irreplaceable genetic material --- adaptations to climate, disease resistance, foraging ability, temperament --- that no commercial hybrid can replicate. To raise them is to keep a promise to the breeders who spent lifetimes perfecting them, and to the generations who will need the genetic diversity these breeds safeguard.
