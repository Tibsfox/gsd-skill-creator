# Building a Rainbow Egg Basket

Open a carton of grocery-store eggs and you see one color: white, or brown, depending on which shelf you reached for. Open the egg basket of a thoughtfully assembled backyard flock and you see something that looks like a watercolor palette --- chalk white next to speckled terracotta next to sky blue next to deep chocolate next to sage green next to cream next to a blushing pink that defies easy description. Every egg is a different color because every hen is a different breed, and every breed carries a different genetic recipe for shell pigment. The rainbow egg basket is not a gimmick or a marketing trick. It is a visible expression of genetic diversity, a daily reminder that the poultry world is vastly richer than the industrial monoculture suggests, and --- let us be honest --- it is beautiful in a way that makes breakfast feel like an event.

This guide is about building that rainbow. It covers the genetics behind every shell color, the breeds that produce them, and a practical plan for assembling a six-bird flock that fills your basket with every color the domestic chicken can produce.

---

## The Two Pigments: Everything You Need to Know

The entire spectrum of eggshell color --- every white, cream, brown, chocolate, blue, green, olive, and pink-tinted egg that any chicken has ever laid --- is produced by just two pigments. Two. Every color combination you see in a rainbow basket is a variation on the same two-chemical theme.

### Protoporphyrin IX (Brown)

Protoporphyrin IX is a porphyrin molecule --- part of the same molecular family that gives hemoglobin its red color, though protoporphyrin itself appears brown when deposited on eggshell. It is secreted by the shell gland (uterus) during the final 3-5 hours of shell formation and deposited as a surface coating on the outside of the calcium carbonate shell.

The key word is *surface*. Protoporphyrin does not penetrate the shell. Crack a brown egg and look at the inside: it is white (or blue, if the hen also carries the blue-egg gene). The brown is only on the outside, a paint job applied in the last hours before the egg is laid. This is why a freshly laid, still-damp Marans egg can stain your fingers --- the pigment is on the cuticle, the outermost layer, and it has not fully dried. It is also why brown eggs can be scratched or rubbed to reveal the lighter shell beneath.

The intensity of brown coloration is **polygenic** --- controlled by multiple genes, each contributing a portion of the total pigment load. This means breeding for darker brown is a gradual, multigenerational process of selecting the darkest-laying hens and mating them to roosters from dark-laying hen lines. There is no single "dark egg gene" that can be switched on.

### Biliverdin (Blue)

Biliverdin is a bile pigment, a breakdown product of hemoglobin. Unlike protoporphyrin, biliverdin is deposited **throughout the full thickness of the shell**. A blue egg is blue inside and out, top and bottom, surface and core. You cannot scratch off the blue. You cannot wash it away. It is structural.

The blue-egg trait is controlled by a **single autosomal dominant gene** --- the *O* (oocyanin) allele, located on chromosome 1. A single copy of *O* produces blue eggs. Two copies (*O/O*) produce slightly more intense blue, though the difference is modest. The gene was mapped to a retroviral insertion in the *SLCO1B3* gene that causes ectopic expression of biliverdin transport into the shell gland.

### The Color Combinations

From these two pigments, every eggshell color emerges:

| Color | Pigment(s) | Appearance |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **White** | None | Pure calcium carbonate, no pigment |
| **Cream** | Trace protoporphyrin | Very pale warm tint, barely off-white |
| **Light brown** | Moderate protoporphyrin | Classic "brown egg" --- warm, medium tan |
| **Dark brown** | Heavy protoporphyrin | Rich chocolate, terracotta, or mahogany |
| **Speckled** | Uneven protoporphyrin | Dark spots and speckles over a brown base |
| **Blue** | Biliverdin (no protoporphyrin) | Sky blue to robin's-egg blue throughout shell |
| **Green / Olive** | Biliverdin + protoporphyrin | Blue shell with brown overlay = green composite |
| **Pink-tinted** | Light protoporphyrin on white base | Delicate warm blush, almost rose |

## The Rainbow Breeds: One Color at a Time

### White: The Leghorn

The White Leghorn is the world's white egg. Every white egg in every grocery store traces its lineage to this trim, athletic, spectacularly productive Italian breed. Heritage Leghorns lay 250-300 large white eggs per year. The shells are pure calcium carbonate with no pigment --- the eggshell equivalent of a blank canvas.

For a rainbow basket, one heritage Leghorn provides the white anchor. Be aware that heritage Leghorns are active, flighty, and independent --- they are not lap chickens. They are excellent foragers, efficient feed converters, and they will fly over a four-foot fence without apparent effort. If you prefer a calmer bird that also lays white eggs, consider the **Polish** (150-200 medium white eggs, quirky personality, spectacular crested appearance) or the **Ancona** (200-280 large white eggs, alert but less flighty than the Leghorn, with striking mottled plumage).

### Cream: The Faverolles

The Salmon Faverolles lays eggs in a color category that is difficult to pin down: cream, with a faintly warm tint that is neither white nor brown but something in between. The shell color is subtle --- a soft, warm off-white that looks ivory next to a Leghorn's stark white and distinctly lighter than a Plymouth Rock's brown. The Faverolles' contribution to the rainbow basket is this in-between color, filling the gap between white and brown with something gentle and warm.

The Salmon Faverolles is also one of the most charming breeds in existence --- bearded, muffed, five-toed, feather-legged, and possessed of a personality that can only be described as clownish. She lays 180-240 cream eggs per year and will occasionally go broody. She is docile, slightly awkward, and utterly endearing.

### Light Brown: The Orpington

The Buff Orpington lays a warm, pinkish-brown egg that anchors the light-brown section of the rainbow. The color is distinctive: not the medium tan of a Plymouth Rock or the dark chocolate of a Marans, but a warm, rosy brown that some keepers describe as "peachy." It is a color that photographs beautifully and that provides a clear visual step between the cream of the Faverolles and the darker browns that follow.

The Buff Orpington provides more than just egg color, of course. She provides personality --- the calm, friendly, golden presence that makes every flock better. She lays 180-250 large eggs per year, and she makes the whole operation look warm and inviting.

### Dark Brown: The Marans

The Marans --- specifically the Black Copper Marans --- lays the darkest brown egg of any commonly available breed. The best Marans hens produce eggs that score 6-8 on the Marans of America 1-9 color scale: a deep, rich chocolate-brown that borders on mahogany, with a surface that can feel slightly matte or satiny to the touch due to the thick protoporphyrin layer. Freshly laid, the egg may literally stain your fingers brown.

A Black Copper Marans egg next to a Leghorn egg looks like they came from different species. The contrast is dramatic and is the single most impactful visual element in a rainbow basket. Marans hens lay 150-210 large to extra-large eggs per year --- less productive than Leghorns or Australorps, but each egg is a small work of art.

**Important note:** Marans egg color fades over the course of a laying cycle. The first eggs after a molt or break are darkest; subsequent eggs lighten progressively as the hen's protoporphyrin reserves deplete. The color resets after a pause. Evaluating a Marans by a single egg is unreliable --- judge by the average across a cycle.

### Speckled Terracotta: The Welsummer

The Welsummer lays an egg that is unmistakable: a deep terracotta-brown base covered with darker brown speckles, spots, and sometimes irregular blotches. The speckles are protoporphyrin deposited unevenly on the cuticle during the egg's rotation in the shell gland. They can sometimes be gently wiped from a freshly laid egg with a damp cloth, revealing the slightly lighter base color beneath.

A Welsummer egg is the egg that appears on the Kellogg's Corn Flakes box (the rooster on the box is a Welsummer). It looks like a farm egg from a storybook --- warm, earthy, speckled, and thoroughly handsome. In the rainbow basket, the Welsummer provides the terracotta-with-texture slot that no other breed quite fills. Hens lay 160-220 large eggs per year.

### Blue: The Ameraucana

The Ameraucana lays a true blue egg --- blue throughout the shell, inside and out, produced by biliverdin deposited during shell formation. The shade varies from pale sky blue to a deeper robin's-egg blue, depending on the individual hen and her genetic background. The blue is clean, with no brown overlay (which would produce green).

The Ameraucana is a standardized breed with recognized color varieties (Black, Blue, Blue Wheaten, Brown Red, Buff, Silver, Wheaten, White), a pea comb (excellent frostbite resistance), muffs and beard (giving the face a puffy, owl-like appearance), and a full tail. It is a distinct breed from the Easter Egger, which is a mixed-heritage bird that may or may not lay blue eggs [breeds/ameraucana-easter-egger]. If you want a guaranteed blue egg for your rainbow basket, buy from an Ameraucana breeder, not a feed store selling "Ameraucanas" that are actually Easter Eggers.

An excellent alternative for the blue-egg slot is the **Cream Legbar** --- the auto-sexing breed developed by Reginald Punnett at Cambridge. Cream Legbars produce 180-220 blue eggs per year, breed true for both egg color and auto-sexing, and are active, hardy foragers well-suited to the PNW [breeds/cream-legbar].

Ameraucana hens lay 180-250 medium blue eggs per year. They are cold-hardy, active, and good foragers --- well-suited to PNW conditions.

### Green: The Olive Egger

The Olive Egger is not a breed --- it is a cross. Specifically, it is the first-generation (F1) offspring of a blue-egg breed (typically Ameraucana) crossed with a dark-brown-egg breed (typically Marans). The F1 hen carries one copy of the blue-egg gene (*O/o+*) and a moderate complement of brown-pigment genes from the Marans parent. The result: a blue shell base overlaid with brown protoporphyrin, producing a composite color that appears olive green.

The specific shade of olive varies with the intensity of the brown overlay. Light brown on blue produces sage or mint green. Medium brown on blue produces classic olive. Dark brown on blue produces deep, forest-green olive. The darkest Olive Egger eggs are among the most visually striking in the entire rainbow basket --- a color that looks hand-dyed, completely unlike anything the grocery store offers.

Because Olive Eggers are crosses, not a standardized breed, their appearance varies widely. They may be large or medium-sized, black or brown or gray, muffed or clean-faced. Their value is in the egg, not the plumage. Hens typically lay 150-200 medium to large olive eggs per year.

**Breeding note:** If you want to produce your own Olive Eggers, cross an Ameraucana rooster over Marans hens (or vice versa). All F1 daughters will lay olive eggs. Breeding Olive Egger to Olive Egger in the F2 produces a range of egg colors (some blue, some olive, some brown) because the blue-egg gene and the brown-pigment genes segregate independently. Maintaining consistent dark olive color requires backcrossing or careful F2 selection.

### Pink-Tinted: The Light Sussex or Salmon Faverolles

Some hens lay eggs with a delicate pink or rose blush --- a very light protoporphyrin wash on a white or near-white base that produces a color more pink than brown. The Light Sussex and some strains of Salmon Faverolles are known for this color, though it is variable between individual hens and not as reliably predictable as the blue of an Ameraucana or the brown of a Plymouth Rock.

The pink-tinted egg is the subtlest color in the rainbow basket --- a whisper of warmth that is easy to miss next to the dramatic chocolate of a Marans or the vivid blue of an Ameraucana. But in a carton arranged by color, the pink provides a soft transition between the cream and light brown that ties the palette together.

## A Sample Six-Bird Rainbow Flock

For a PNW flock of six hens that produces the full spectrum of egg colors:

| # | Breed | Egg Color | Eggs/Year | Temperament |
|---|-------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **White Leghorn** (or Ancona) | White | 250-300 | Active, independent |
| 2 | **Salmon Faverolles** | Cream / pink-tinted | 180-240 | Gentle, clownish |
| 3 | **Buff Orpington** | Light pinkish-brown | 180-250 | Calm, friendly |
| 4 | **Welsummer** | Speckled terracotta | 160-220 | Friendly, active |
| 5 | **Ameraucana** | Blue | 180-250 | Active, hardy |
| 6 | **Olive Egger** (Ameraucana x Marans) | Olive green | 150-200 | Variable |

**Total expected production:** Approximately 1,100-1,500 eggs per year from six hens, or roughly 3-4 eggs per day averaged across the year. Winter production in the PNW will be lower (2-3 per day), spring and summer production higher (4-5 per day).

**Alternative:** Swap the Olive Egger for a **Black Copper Marans** to replace the green with deep chocolate brown. This sacrifices the green but adds the darkest brown egg in the poultry world. You can always add an Olive Egger as a seventh hen if space permits.

**Another alternative:** Swap the Leghorn for a **Cream Legbar** --- an auto-sexing breed that lays blue eggs, allowing you to have two blue-spectrum birds. Pair the Cream Legbar with the Olive Egger for green, and add the Marans for chocolate, creating a basket that emphasizes the blue-green-brown spectrum.

## Managing the Rainbow: Practical Considerations

### Egg Color Fading

All brown-egg breeds experience color fading over the laying cycle. The first eggs after a molt or break are darkest; subsequent eggs lighten as protoporphyrin reserves deplete. This is normal and unavoidable. The Marans shows the most dramatic fade; the Welsummer less so. Blue eggs do not fade --- biliverdin production is consistent throughout the laying cycle.

### Egg Color and Nutrition

Shell color has **no effect** on nutritional content, flavor, or cooking properties. A Marans egg, a Leghorn egg, and an Ameraucana egg from hens fed the same diet and managed the same way are nutritionally identical. The color is in the shell, not the contents. The rich golden-orange yolk color that pastured eggs are famous for is a function of carotenoid-rich diet (grass, insects, flowers), not shell color.

### Mixed-Flock Compatibility

The breeds in the recommended rainbow flock are generally compatible. The Orpington and Faverolles are the most docile and may need monitoring if housed with more assertive breeds. The Leghorn is the most independent and may roost separately or range farther than the others. The Ameraucana and Welsummer are moderate --- active but not aggressive. The Olive Egger's temperament depends on its parent breeds.

In the PNW, all six breeds handle the climate well, though the Faverolles' foot feathering requires attention to mud management [FWL-06]. Covered runs with deep wood-chip or sand substrate prevent the muddy, matted feet that lead to bumblefoot in feather-legged breeds.

### The Show Bird Community

For keepers who become interested in exhibition, the rainbow flock is a gateway. Each breed has a dedicated show community: the Ameraucana Breeders Club, the Marans of America, the National Wyandotte Breeders Club (for those who add a Wyandotte for dark brown), and breed-specific meets at APA and ABA shows across the country. The egg-color rainbow is one of the most effective hooks for drawing new keepers into the exhibition world, because it demonstrates visually what genetic diversity means --- and once you understand the genetics behind a blue egg or a speckled terracotta egg, the urge to breed for those colors becomes almost irresistible.

## The Rainbow as a Statement

A carton of rainbow eggs is more than a pretty breakfast. It is a visible argument for genetic diversity in a world that has systematically reduced the chicken to two industrial types: the white-egg Leghorn hybrid and the brown-egg Rhode Island Red hybrid. Every blue egg, every olive egg, every speckled terracotta egg, every cream-tinted egg is proof that the domestic chicken's genetic heritage is broader, richer, and more beautiful than the supermarket shelf suggests.

The backyard keeper who assembles a rainbow flock is participating in breed conservation, whether or not that was the original intent. Every Ameraucana that lays a blue egg is maintaining the blue-egg gene that originated in the chickens of the Chilean Mapuche people. Every Marans that lays a chocolate egg is preserving the polygenic dark-pigment complex that French farmers selected for over a century. Every Olive Egger that lays a green egg is demonstrating the combinatorial genetics that make poultry breeding endlessly fascinating.

The rainbow egg basket is where science meets beauty, where genetics meets breakfast, and where the backyard coop connects to eight thousand years of chicken domestication. It is, in its modest way, one of the best things about keeping chickens.
