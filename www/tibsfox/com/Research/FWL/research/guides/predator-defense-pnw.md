# Defending the Flock --- PNW Predator Management

If you keep chickens in the Pacific Northwest, something wants to eat them. This is not pessimism. It is ecology. The PNW is one of the most biologically productive regions in North America, and that productivity sustains a dense, diverse predator community that has evolved over millennia alongside ground-nesting birds. Your backyard flock --- ground-roosting, flightless or nearly so, confined to a small area, and bred for docility rather than predator evasion --- is a buffet. The question is not whether a predator will find your chickens. The question is when, and whether your defenses will hold.

This guide profiles the major predators of backyard poultry in the PNW, ranks them by threat level and frequency, and describes the defense strategies that actually work. The emphasis is on practical, proven measures rather than folk remedies, and on the PNW-specific predator dynamics that make this region particularly challenging for poultry keepers.

---

## The PNW Predator Roster

The Pacific Northwest supports one of the densest and most diverse predator communities in North America. A partial list of animals that will kill, eat, or attempt to access backyard chickens west of the Cascades:

**Mammals:** Raccoons, coyotes, domestic/feral dogs, rats, weasels (long-tailed weasel, short-tailed weasel/ermine), mink, bobcats, black bears, opossums, striped skunks, domestic/feral cats, river otters (near waterways), mountain lions/cougars (rural-wildland interface)

**Raptors:** Cooper's Hawks, Sharp-shinned Hawks, Red-tailed Hawks, Great Horned Owls, Barred Owls, Bald Eagles, Northern Goshawks (forested areas)

**Other:** Corvids (crows, ravens --- egg predators), rats (chick and egg predators), snakes (egg predators, primarily east of Cascades)

What makes the PNW particularly challenging is the **year-round nature** of predator pressure. Unlike northern continental climates where deep winter cold and snow reduce predator activity and populations, the PNW's mild, wet winters keep predators active twelve months of the year. Raccoons do not truly hibernate in the PNW --- they enter periods of reduced activity (torpor) during the coldest weeks but do not undergo the extended dormancy seen in northern populations [RCN-05]. Coyotes are active year-round. Hawks hunt year-round. Rats breed year-round in PNW's mild climate. There is no off-season for predator defense.

## Threat #1: Raccoons

Raccoons are the number-one predator of backyard poultry in the Pacific Northwest, and it is not close. The combination of extreme intelligence, manual dexterity, persistence, nocturnal habits, and extraordinary population density makes them the adversary that defines PNW coop security [RCN-02].

### Why Raccoons Are the Top Threat

**Population density.** PNW suburban raccoon densities range from 30-75 individuals per square kilometer, with urban areas reaching 50-150 per square kilometer [RCN-04]. A typical suburban neighborhood has dozens of raccoons within walking distance of any backyard coop. You are not defending against a single raccoon. You are defending against a population.

**Intelligence.** Raccoons possess approximately 438 million cortical neurons --- a neuronal density approaching that of small primates [RCN-02]. They solve multi-step problems, remember solutions for years, learn by observing other raccoons, and adapt their techniques to novel obstacles. A raccoon that has learned to open a particular latch design will remember that skill indefinitely.

**Dexterity.** The raccoon forepaw contains five long, flexible digits and an extraordinary density of mechanoreceptors [RCN deep-dives/hands-and-intelligence]. Raccoons can open simple latches, slide bolts, lift hooks, turn knobs, and manipulate any closure that requires only a single motion. They can reach through openings as small as two inches to grab a bird, pull it against the wire, and kill it without ever entering the coop.

**Persistence.** A motivated raccoon will work a problem for hours. Where a coyote or dog might probe a fence for a few minutes and leave, a raccoon will sit at a closure point and systematically test every approach until it finds one that works --- or until dawn forces it to retreat. And it will come back the next night.

### Raccoon Attack Patterns

Raccoons kill chickens in several characteristic ways:

- **Reaching through wire.** If the coop or run is made of standard chicken wire (1-inch hex mesh), raccoons reach through the openings, grab the nearest bird by the leg, wing, or neck, and pull it against the wire. The bird is killed and partially consumed through the wire mesh. This is the single most common raccoon attack pattern and the primary reason that **chicken wire is not predator-proof** [FWL-06].

- **Opening latches and doors.** Raccoons open simple hook-and-eye latches, slide bolts that move in a single axis, gravity latches, and unpadlocked hasp closures. Any latch that requires only a single motion to open should be considered raccoon-accessible [RCN deep-dives/raccoon-proofing].

- **Digging under.** Raccoons can dig, though they are not dedicated excavators like dogs. They will dig under a run wall if the gap between the ground and the bottom rail provides a starting point. Aprons of hardware cloth extending outward from the base of the run prevent digging access.

- **Exploiting structural weaknesses.** Rotted wood, loose staples, poorly secured hardware cloth, gaps where the coop meets the ground --- raccoons find and exploit every structural failure.

### Defense Against Raccoons

**Hardware cloth, not chicken wire.** This is the single most important predator defense decision a PNW poultry keeper will make. Replace all chicken wire with 1/2-inch welded wire hardware cloth on all openings --- windows, vents, run walls, and any gap larger than 1/2 inch. Hardware cloth prevents raccoons from reaching through to grab birds. Chicken wire is designed to keep chickens *in*, not predators *out*. It is thin enough for a raccoon to tear, and the mesh is large enough for a raccoon's arm [FWL-06].

**Two-step latches.** Every door, pop hole, and access point on the coop and run must be secured with a latch that requires two distinct motions to open. Examples:
- A hasp secured with a padlock or carabiner
- A slide bolt with a cotter pin through the handle
- A spring-loaded latch that must be lifted *and* pulled simultaneously
- Two separate latches on the same door (the raccoon can open one, but operating two simultaneously defeats most individuals)

The principle is that raccoons can perform any single manipulation but struggle with simultaneous opposing operations [RCN deep-dives/raccoon-proofing]. A carabiner clip, which requires squeezing inward while pulling outward, is one of the most effective and inexpensive raccoon-resistant closures available.

**Lock-up at dusk.** Raccoons are primarily nocturnal. The most reliable defense is a pop door that closes at dusk and opens at dawn, either manually or with an automatic door on a timer or light sensor. A flock that is inside a raccoon-proof coop with the door closed by sundown is vastly safer than a flock with access to an outdoor run at night. This does not protect free-range birds during the day (raccoons occasionally hunt diurnally, especially in urban areas [RCN-04]), but it addresses the primary risk window.

**Hardware cloth aprons.** An apron of hardware cloth extending 12-18 inches outward from the base of the run, buried 2-4 inches below grade or laid on the surface and covered with soil/gravel, prevents digging access. Animals instinctively dig at the base of a barrier, not 18 inches out from it. The apron defeats this instinct without requiring deep burial.

## Threat #2: Raptors (Hawks and Eagles)

Hawks are the second most frequent predator of backyard chickens in the PNW, and they present a challenge that mammalian predators do not: they are **federally protected**. All raptors in the United States are protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act. It is illegal to kill, trap, harass, or injure any hawk, eagle, or owl, regardless of whether it is attacking your chickens. Penalties include fines up to $15,000 and imprisonment. This means that raptor defense must be purely exclusionary --- you must prevent access, because lethal control is not an option.

### The PNW Raptor Threat

**Cooper's Hawk (*Accipiter cooperii*).** The Cooper's Hawk is the most common raptor predator of backyard chickens in the PNW. It is a medium-sized accipiter --- a bird-hunting specialist --- that has adapted remarkably well to suburban environments. Cooper's Hawks hunt by ambush: they fly low and fast through cover, using fences, hedges, and buildings as concealment, then burst into the open to strike prey in a sudden rush. A Cooper's Hawk can kill a chicken up to about the size of a standard-breed hen, though it more commonly takes bantams and young pullets. It is a daily presence in PNW suburban neighborhoods and is the hawk most likely to be sitting on your fence, watching your flock [FWL-09, AVI].

**Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*).** Smaller than the Cooper's Hawk, the Sharp-shinned Hawk primarily takes small birds (songbirds, sparrows) but will kill bantam chicks and very young standard-breed chicks. Less of a threat to adult chickens than the Cooper's Hawk, but relevant for flocks with free-ranging chicks.

**Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*).** The Red-tailed Hawk is the most common large hawk in the PNW. Unlike the accipiters, which are bird-hunting specialists, the Red-tail is a generalist that primarily hunts rodents and rabbits. It can and does take chickens, however --- particularly birds that are isolated in open ground. A Red-tailed Hawk hunting from a soaring position or a perch can strike with tremendous force, and it is large enough to kill and carry a standard-breed hen.

**Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*).** The Great Horned Owl is the most powerful avian predator in the PNW and the only common raptor that regularly takes adult chickens off the roost at night. Great Horned Owls hunt in darkness, silently, striking with talons that exert over 200 PSI of grip force. A Great Horned Owl can kill a twelve-pound turkey; a chicken of any size is easy prey. The owl's nocturnal hunting pattern means that birds on open roosts, in trees, or in coops with uncovered openings are vulnerable after dark.

**Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*).** PNW Bald Eagle populations have recovered spectacularly since the DDT era, and eagles are now common in both rural and suburban areas, particularly near water. Eagles will take chickens opportunistically, especially in open, waterfront areas. They are powerful enough to carry off a standard-breed hen and are protected under both the MBTA and the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act.

### Defense Against Raptors

**Covered runs.** The most reliable raptor defense is a covered run. A run enclosed with hardware cloth on top and sides is functionally raptor-proof. For larger runs where a solid roof is impractical, overhead netting (poultry netting or deer netting) breaks the flight path and deters hawk strikes. The netting does not need to be rigid --- even loosely strung netting creates a visual barrier that hawks are reluctant to fly through.

**Fishing line deterrent.** A surprisingly effective and inexpensive method: run parallel lines of monofilament fishing line (30-50 lb test) across the top of the run at 12-18 inch intervals, approximately 6-7 feet above ground level. The fishing line is nearly invisible to humans from a distance but creates a disorienting obstacle for hawks attempting to dive into the run. Hawks will not fly into areas where their wings may contact unexpected objects. This is not 100% effective --- a determined hawk can navigate through the lines --- but it significantly reduces strike attempts. Refresh the lines periodically, as weathering makes them brittle.

**Cover and shelter in the run.** Provide brush piles, overturned pallets, or roofed shelter areas within the run where birds can dive for cover. Chickens have an instinctive freeze-and-hide response to the shadow of an overhead predator, but they need somewhere to hide. A run that is completely open ground offers no refuge.

**Rooster.** A good rooster is a living hawk alarm. Roosters watch the sky, and most roosters will emit a distinctive aerial predator alarm call when they spot a hawk or eagle. The alarm triggers an immediate scatter-and-freeze response in the hens. The rooster does not fight the hawk (though some roosters will attempt to --- usually unsuccessfully), but his vigilance and alarm calls provide an early warning system that free-ranging flocks lack without a male. This is one of the practical arguments for keeping a rooster, where local ordinances permit.

**Time management.** Cooper's Hawks hunt most actively in early morning and late afternoon. Red-tailed Hawks hunt midday when thermals provide soaring lift. Great Horned Owls hunt in darkness. If you free-range your birds, the safest window is the middle of the day when the most common hawk threats (accipiters) are least active. Confining birds during dawn and dusk reduces exposure during peak Cooper's Hawk hunting hours.

## Threat #3: Coyotes

Coyotes (*Canis latrans*) have expanded dramatically into PNW urban and suburban areas over the past two decades. Seattle's Discovery Park, Portland's Forest Park, and green corridors throughout the metro areas of both cities support resident coyote populations [MAM]. Coyotes are now regularly reported in neighborhoods across the Puget Sound lowlands, the Willamette Valley, and the Portland-Vancouver metropolitan area.

A coyote that gains access to a flock will kill multiple birds in a single attack. Coyotes are efficient predators --- they kill quickly, and they may kill more birds than they can carry, returning later to cache or retrieve additional carcasses.

### Defense Against Coyotes

**Fencing.** A coyote can clear a four-foot fence from a standing start and can scale a six-foot fence if it can get a foothold. A coyote-proof fence is:
- At least six feet tall
- Fitted with a 12-18 inch outward-angled top extension (coyote roller or angled arm with wire)
- Buried or apron-skirted at the base (coyotes dig under fences routinely)
- Free of gaps at gates and corners (coyotes can compress their bodies through surprisingly narrow openings)

**Electric fence.** A low wire (6-8 inches above ground) carrying a pulsed charge is highly effective against coyotes. Coyotes investigate fences nose-first, and a nose contact with electric wire produces a powerful deterrent. Electric fencing can be added to existing fencing as a supplementary barrier. Solar-powered fence chargers make electric fencing practical even in locations without convenient power access.

**Secure housing at night.** Coyotes are primarily crepuscular (dawn and dusk) and nocturnal, though urban coyotes may be active at any hour. A coop that is closed and secured by dusk and not opened until after full dawn eliminates the primary coyote risk window.

**Remove attractants.** Fallen fruit, pet food, unsecured garbage, and compost piles attract coyotes to residential properties. Once a coyote establishes a feeding pattern near a property, the chicken coop becomes an additional target.

## Threat #4: Owls (Night Predators)

Great Horned Owls and Barred Owls are the primary nocturnal raptor threats in the PNW. Both species are common, widespread, and powerful enough to kill adult chickens.

The Great Horned Owl hunts from perches, dropping silently on prey with devastating force. It takes birds off open roosts at night --- a particular risk for breeds that prefer to roost in trees (Sumatras, some game breeds) or for flocks whose coop does not close securely.

The Barred Owl is smaller than the Great Horned Owl but increasingly common in PNW suburban and urban areas. It is an opportunistic predator that takes smaller prey (bantams, young birds) and is known to harass birds through wire.

### Defense Against Owls

**Enclosed housing at night.** This is the definitive solution. A coop with solid walls, a solid roof, and a closed door defeats nocturnal raptors completely. Hardware cloth windows provide ventilation while preventing owl access.

**No open roosts.** Do not allow birds to roost in trees or on open perches outside the coop at night. Some breeds (particularly Sumatras and other semi-wild types) prefer tree roosting, but in the PNW, a bird on an open roost after dark is a bird on an owl's menu.

**Motion-activated lights and sound.** Motion-activated spotlights and sound deterrents can startle owls and interrupt hunting attempts. These are supplementary measures, not primary defenses --- an owl that has learned to hunt a particular location will habituate to deterrents over time.

## Threat #5: Rats

Rats (*Rattus norvegicus*, the Norway rat, and *Rattus rattus*, the black or roof rat) are ubiquitous in PNW urban and suburban areas. They are not primarily chicken killers --- an adult hen can usually defend herself against a rat --- but they kill chicks, steal eggs, consume feed, and spread disease. A rat problem in a chicken coop is a compound problem: the wasted feed attracts more rats, the growing rat population attracts larger predators (owls, hawks, snakes), and the rat feces contaminate bedding and food.

### Defense Against Rats

**Feed management.** Remove or secure feeders at night. Rats are nocturnal and rely heavily on chicken feed as a food source. A treadle feeder (which opens only when a chicken stands on the treadle plate) or a feeder brought inside at dusk eliminates the primary attractant.

**Hardware cloth floor.** In areas with severe rat pressure, a hardware cloth floor (1/2 inch mesh) beneath the bedding prevents rats from tunneling into the coop. Some keepers build coops on raised platforms for the same reason.

**Bait stations.** Snap traps or commercial rodenticide bait stations, placed where chickens cannot access them, reduce rat populations. Bait stations must be tamper-resistant to exclude chickens, children, pets, and non-target wildlife. Be aware that rodenticides pose secondary poisoning risk to raptors and other predators that eat poisoned rats.

**Collect eggs daily.** Eggs left in nesting boxes overnight attract rats. Collect eggs at least once daily, preferably in the afternoon before nightfall.

## Threat #6: Neighborhood Dogs

Domestic dogs are an underrecognized but significant predator of backyard chickens. A single loose dog can kill an entire flock in minutes. Unlike raccoons, which typically kill and eat one bird per visit, dogs in prey-drive mode engage in surplus killing --- they chase, bite, and shake every bird they can catch, often without consuming any. The result is a yard full of dead and injured chickens and a dog owner who insists their pet "would never do that."

### Defense Against Dogs

**Solid fencing.** A four-foot fence deters most dogs, though athletic breeds can clear four feet. A six-foot fence with a latching gate is the standard dog deterrent.

**Secure run.** A run enclosed with hardware cloth or welded wire on all sides defeats dogs completely.

**Know your local leash laws.** In most PNW jurisdictions, a dog that kills livestock (including chickens) is subject to penalties under animal control ordinances. The dog's owner may be liable for the value of the killed birds. Document any dog attacks with photographs and report them to animal control.

## Threat #7: Weasels and Mink

Long-tailed weasels (*Mustela frenata*) and American mink (*Neovison vison*) are rare but devastating poultry predators. Both are capable of entering openings as small as one inch (weasels) or two inches (mink), and both engage in **surplus killing** --- they kill every bird in the coop, often in a single night, driven by a predatory response that exceeds their ability to consume the prey. A weasel or mink attack is the nightmare scenario: you open the coop in the morning to find every bird dead, many with a single bite to the base of the skull, and no predator in sight.

### Defense Against Weasels and Mink

**1/2-inch hardware cloth everywhere.** No gap larger than 1/2 inch should exist anywhere on the coop or run. Weasels can fit through any opening they can get their skull through, and a weasel's skull is about the width of a quarter. Standard hardware cloth (1/2 inch mesh) excludes weasels; 1-inch mesh does not.

**Seal all gaps.** Where the coop meets the ground, where the roof meets the walls, where hardware cloth meets framing --- every junction must be sealed. Weasels are extraordinary contortionists and will find gaps that seem impossibly small.

**Night security.** As with raccoons and owls, a securely closed coop at night is the primary defense. Weasels and mink are primarily nocturnal, and a coop with no openings larger than 1/2 inch and a closed door is effectively weasel-proof.

## The PNW Challenge: Year-Round Pressure

In the upper Midwest, the Great Plains, or the Canadian prairies, winter provides a natural reduction in predator pressure. Raccoons enter prolonged torpor. Snakes are dormant. Insect populations crash, reducing rodent food supply and therefore rodent numbers. The predator landscape simplifies.

In the Pacific Northwest, west of the Cascades, this does not happen. PNW raccoons remain active throughout winter, reducing activity only during the coldest snaps [RCN-05, RCN-06]. Coyotes are active year-round. Hawks and owls do not migrate (most PNW raptor populations are resident year-round [AVI]). Rats breed continuously in the mild PNW climate. The result is a twelve-month predator season that demands twelve months of vigilance.

This year-round pressure means that PNW poultry keepers must build their defenses to a higher standard than keepers in regions with seasonal reprieve. A coop that is "good enough" in a climate where raccoons sleep four months of the year may not be good enough in a climate where raccoons are probing your latches on Christmas Eve.

## The Nightly Routine

The single most effective predator defense is not a piece of hardware. It is a habit.

Every night, at dusk, walk to the coop. Confirm that every bird is inside and on the roost. Close and latch the pop door. Check the latches on every access point. Glance at the hardware cloth for signs of damage or probing. Collect any eggs left in the nesting boxes. Top off water. Walk away knowing that your flock is sealed inside a structure that a raccoon cannot open, a coyote cannot breach, a hawk cannot enter, and an owl cannot reach.

In the morning, at dawn, reverse the procedure. Open the pop door. Count the birds. Check for signs of overnight probing (scratched wood, bent wire, disturbed soil at the base of the run). Refresh feed and water. Let the birds out.

This routine takes five minutes in the evening and three minutes in the morning. It is the most reliable predator defense that exists, and it costs nothing. The keepers who lose birds are, overwhelmingly, the keepers who skip the routine --- who leave the pop door open one night because they are tired, who forget to check the latches because they are in a hurry, who assume one night will not matter.

One night always matters. The raccoons are counting on it.
