# Your First Six Chickens --- A PNW Starter Guide

You have been thinking about it for a while. Maybe you have been looking at coops online. Maybe your neighbor keeps chickens and you have been watching over the fence, noticing the way the hens scratch through the garden bed, the satisfying plunk of an egg hitting the straw in the nesting box, the improbable amount of personality packed into a five-pound bird. Maybe your kids are asking. Maybe you are tired of buying eggs from a store when you have a perfectly good backyard going to waste. Whatever brought you here, the question is the same: how do you start?

This guide is written for the Pacific Northwest --- western Washington, western Oregon, the coastal and Puget Sound lowlands where the climate is mild, wet, overcast for eight months of the year, and home to the densest population of chicken-hungry predators in North America. National chicken-keeping guides will get you partway there, but they do not address the specific realities of keeping birds in a place where it rains 150 days a year, where raccoons are as smart as primates and twice as determined, and where the sun sets at 4:15 PM in December. This guide does.

We are going to start with six chickens, which is the right number for a first flock. It is enough to provide eggs for a family of four, small enough to house in a modest coop, and large enough that the birds can establish a functional social group. Six is also the upper limit permitted by many PNW municipal codes, so it keeps you legal in most jurisdictions. Everything in this guide scales --- if your city allows eight, or if you live in an unincorporated area with no hen limit, the principles are the same; just add space proportionally.

## Choosing Breeds for the PNW

The Pacific Northwest is not hard on chickens the way Montana or Minnesota is hard on chickens. You are not going to lose birds to -30°F wind chill (west of the Cascades, at least). But the PNW has its own challenges --- persistent rain, limited winter daylight, mud, and a predator community that has evolved alongside some of the most productive bird habitat on the continent. Your breed selection should account for all of these.

### What to Look For

**Cold hardiness.** PNW winters are mild (typically 25--45°F in the coldest months west of the Cascades), but the combination of cold and damp is harder on chickens than dry cold alone. Choose breeds with dense plumage, moderate to large body size, and combs that resist frostbite.

**Rain tolerance.** This is the big one. A breed with tight, close-fitting plumage that sheds water is more suitable for the PNW than a breed with fluffy, absorbent feathering. Silkies, for example, are wonderful birds, but their filamentous plumage absorbs water like a sponge and provides no waterproofing. They require fully covered, dry housing at all times. A Plymouth Rock or Australorp, with its smooth, close-fitting feathers, will forage in light rain and dry off quickly.

**Laying persistence in low light.** The PNW is dark in winter. Seattle gets approximately 8.5 hours of daylight at the solstice, and many of those hours are overcast. Some breeds are more sensitive to photoperiod than others --- Mediterranean breeds (Leghorns, Minorcas) tend to shut down production more completely in short days, while English and American breeds maintain more consistent (if reduced) winter laying. You can supplement with artificial light (a timer-controlled bulb providing 14--16 total hours of light), but choosing breeds with inherent persistence reduces the management burden.

**Temperament.** If this is your first flock and you have children, prioritize calm, handleable breeds. You want birds that can be picked up, that do not panic at sudden movements, and that tolerate the somewhat chaotic attention patterns of young children. Flighty breeds (Leghorns, many game breeds, some Mediterranean breeds) are not ideal for a first-timer's backyard.

### Recommended PNW Starter Breeds

**Buff Orpington.** The classic beginner's bird. Friendly, calm, cold-hardy, moderate to good layers (200--280 brown eggs/year), attractive. The fluffy feathering does absorb more rain than some breeds, but the birds are hardy enough to manage.

**Barred Plymouth Rock.** America's dual-purpose standard. Excellent layers (200--280 brown eggs/year), cold-hardy, rain-tolerant (tight plumage), alert to predators, friendly. Arguably the single best breed for a first PNW flock.

**Black Australorp.** An Australian breed that holds the world record for egg production (364 eggs in 365 days, set in 1922--23 by a team of six hens). Modern heritage Australorps lay 250--300 light brown eggs per year. They are calm, cold-hardy, have tight, glossy black plumage that sheds rain beautifully, and forage actively.

**Silver Laced Wyandotte.** A round, curvy, strikingly patterned bird with a rose comb (frostbite-resistant --- particularly valuable east of the Cascades). Lays 200--260 brown eggs per year. Hardy, docile, and one of the most beautiful breeds in the standard.

**Easter Egger** (for color). If you want blue, green, or olive eggs in your carton --- and who does not --- add one or two Easter Eggers. They are hardy, generally friendly, and each bird's egg color is a surprise until her first lay. Not a standardized breed, but a practical and delightful addition to a diverse flock. For a true-breeding blue-egg alternative, consider the **Cream Legbar** --- the auto-sexing blue-egg breed developed by Punnett himself at Cambridge [breeds/cream-legbar].

**Light Brahma** (for winter eggs and sheer presence). If you have space for a larger bird and want eggs through the PNW's dark winter months, the Brahma is unmatched: pea comb (frostbite-proof), feathered feet, 12-pound gentle giant temperament, and a laying cycle that peaks in the cooler months when other breeds slow down [breeds/brahma].

### A Suggested First-Flock Mix

Two Barred Plymouth Rocks, two Buff Orpingtons, one Black Australorp, one Easter Egger. This gives you breed diversity (which is interesting and educational), a mix of egg colors (brown from the Rocks and Orpington, darker brown from the Australorp, blue or green from the Easter Egger), and a flock of birds that are uniformly cold-hardy, rain-tolerant, friendly, and good layers.

## The Coop: Minimum Setup for Six

Your coop is the single most important piece of infrastructure. A good coop keeps birds alive; a bad coop kills them --- through predation, disease, exposure, or the slow attrition of chronic stress. Do not underbuild.

### Space

**Inside the coop:** 4 square feet per bird minimum. For six birds: 24 square feet of interior floor space. A 4x6 or 5x5 footprint works. More is always better --- 6 square feet per bird is more comfortable, especially for large breeds like Orpingtons.

**In the run:** 10 square feet per bird minimum. For six birds: 60 square feet of run space. An 8x8 or 6x10 run is the baseline. Again, more is better. In the PNW, effective outdoor space is *covered* outdoor space --- an uncovered run in winter is a mud pit that birds will avoid. Budget for a roofed or tarped run from the start.

### Ventilation, Not Drafts

This distinction matters more in the PNW than almost anywhere else. Ventilation means airflow above the birds that carries moisture out of the coop. Drafts mean wind blowing directly on roosting birds. You need the first and must prevent the second.

Six hens produce roughly 6 pints of moisture per day through respiration and droppings. In a coop with 24 square feet of floor space, that moisture will saturate the air within hours without ventilation. Saturated air causes respiratory disease, frostbitten combs (because damp air conducts cold far more efficiently than dry air), and ammonia buildup from wet litter.

**Solution:** Install ventilation openings high in the coop --- at or above roost height, ideally in the gable peaks or along the eaves. Cover all openings with 1/2-inch hardware cloth to exclude predators. A minimum of 1 square foot of ventilation per bird (6 square feet total for a flock of six) is appropriate for the PNW's high-humidity climate. The roost should be positioned below and out of the direct ventilation airflow, so that air moves above the birds, not across them.

### Nesting Boxes

One nesting box per 3--4 hens. For six hens: two nesting boxes. In practice, all six hens will probably use the same box, ignoring the other one entirely. This is normal. Do not add more boxes in response --- the hens have chosen, and they are not going to change their minds.

Boxes should be approximately 12x12x12 inches (larger for Orpingtons), mounted lower than the roost bars (or the hens will sleep in the nesting boxes and soil them), and filled with clean bedding (straw or wood shavings). An external access door for egg collection is a quality-of-life feature that you will appreciate every morning.

### Roost Bars

**Width:** A 2x4 board mounted with the 4-inch face up. The flat surface lets birds settle over their feet, covering their toes with breast feathers for warmth. Round dowels work but are less comfortable for heavy breeds.

**Space:** 8--12 inches of roost length per bird. For six birds: 4--6 feet of roost bar total. A single 6-foot bar works, or two shorter bars at the same height.

**Height:** 2--4 feet from the floor, with a ramp or ladder for heavy breeds (Orpingtons are not graceful flyers). All roost bars should be at the same height --- if bars are at different heights, the birds will compete for the highest bar and the losers will be stressed.

## The Chick Phase: Weeks 0--8

Unless you are buying started pullets (young hens aged 16--20 weeks, already feathered and approaching lay), your chickens will arrive as day-old chicks. Chicks are fragile, adorable, and entirely dependent on you for warmth, food, water, and protection. The first eight weeks require a brooder setup and daily attention.

### The Brooder

A brooder is any enclosed, warm, dry space where chicks can be safely raised until they are feathered enough to regulate their own body temperature. Common brooder options include:

- A large cardboard box (temporary; chicks will outgrow it within 2--3 weeks)
- A plastic storage tote (the 50--100 gallon size works for 6 chicks through about 4 weeks)
- A galvanized stock tank
- A dedicated brooder box built from plywood

The brooder needs a heat source, bedding, a chick feeder, and a chick waterer. It should be located indoors or in a heated garage --- not in an unheated outbuilding during PNW spring, when nighttime temperatures can drop to the 30s and 40s°F even in April and May.

### Temperature

Day-old chicks need an ambient temperature of **95°F** at chick level. Reduce the temperature by **5°F per week** until the chicks are fully feathered (usually around 6--8 weeks, depending on breed and ambient temperature).

**Heat sources:** A radiant heat plate (such as the Brinsea EcoGlow or similar) is the modern standard. It sits a few inches off the brooder floor, and chicks huddle beneath it like they would huddle beneath a mother hen. Radiant heat plates are safer than heat lamps (no fire risk, no risk of burns from a bulb at chick level) and allow chicks to self-regulate their temperature by moving closer or farther from the plate.

Traditional heat lamps (250-watt red or white infrared bulbs) work but carry a real fire risk. If using a heat lamp, secure it with multiple attachment points (not just the clamp --- clamps fail), keep it away from flammable bedding, and never leave it unattended in a space with combustible materials. Heat lamp fires are a leading cause of barn and coop fires nationwide.

**Reading chick behavior:** If chicks are huddled directly under the heat source, they are too cold --- lower the heat plate or bring the lamp closer. If chicks are scattered to the far edges of the brooder, panting, they are too hot --- raise the heat source. If chicks are distributed evenly across the brooder, some under the heat and some not, the temperature is correct.

### Feed

**Weeks 0--8:** Chick starter feed, 18--20% protein. Available in medicated (containing amprolium, a coccidiostat that helps prevent coccidiosis, a common and potentially fatal intestinal disease in young chickens) and unmedicated formulations. For a first flock, medicated starter is the safer choice unless you are raising chicks on wire or in a very clean environment where coccidiosis exposure is minimal.

Chick starter is a complete feed --- it provides all the nutrition chicks need. Do not supplement with treats, table scraps, or grit during the first two weeks. After two weeks, small amounts of finely chopped greens or scrambled egg can be offered as treats, along with chick-sized grit (fine granite) to help grind the treats in the gizzard.

**Water:** Fresh, clean water available at all times. Use a chick-sized waterer (the base should be shallow enough that a chick cannot drown in it --- drowning in waterers is a real and depressingly common cause of chick mortality). Add marbles or pebbles to the water tray for the first week to provide footing and prevent drowning.

### Pasty Butt

"Pasty butt" (pasting) occurs when droppings adhere to the down around the vent, forming a crust that can block the vent entirely. An obstructed vent is fatal if not cleared. Check chick vents daily during the first two weeks. If pasting occurs, soften the crust with a warm, damp cloth and gently remove it. Do not pull dry --- you will tear the skin. Pasting is common in shipped chicks (the stress of shipping increases its incidence) and usually resolves after the first week or two.

## The Awkward Teenager Phase: Weeks 8--16

Between 8 and 16 weeks, your fluffy, adorable chicks transform into gangly, partially feathered adolescents that look like they were assembled from spare parts. Heads are too big for bodies. Feathers grow in patches, with bare skin visible between them. Combs and wattles begin to develop, starting as tiny pink nubs and gradually enlarging. The birds establish their pecking order through chest bumps, stare-downs, and brief scuffles that look alarming but rarely cause injury.

This phase requires:

**Transition to grower feed.** At 8 weeks, switch from chick starter (18--20% protein) to grower/developer feed (16--18% protein). The reduced protein slows growth to an appropriate rate and supports feather development. Continue grower feed until the birds begin laying, at which point you switch to layer feed.

**Outdoor time.** If the weather is mild (above 50°F) and the birds are mostly feathered, they can begin spending supervised time outdoors during the day. An enclosed run or a portable chicken tractor provides safe outdoor access. Do not leave partially feathered birds outside overnight until they are fully feathered and acclimated to outdoor temperatures.

**Coop transition.** By 12--16 weeks, most birds are fully feathered and can move to the outdoor coop permanently. If integrating with an existing flock, use the "see but don't touch" method: house the newcomers in a separate enclosure within sight of the existing flock for 1--2 weeks before allowing direct contact. This allows the birds to establish a visual pecking order before physical interaction, reducing aggression during integration.

## The First Egg: Weeks 18--24

Sometime between 18 and 24 weeks --- earlier for production-bred strains, later for heritage exhibition lines --- you will walk into the coop and find the first egg. It will probably be small (pullet eggs start small and gradually increase in size over the first 4--6 weeks of laying). It may be oddly shaped, thin-shelled, or deposited on the coop floor rather than in the nesting box. These irregularities are normal. The hen's reproductive system is calibrating, and the first few weeks of production often include double-yolked eggs, soft-shelled eggs, "fairy eggs" (tiny, yolkless eggs consisting only of albumen and shell), and other novelties.

**Switch to layer feed** when the first egg appears (or at 18 weeks, whichever comes first). Layer feed contains 16% protein and 3.5--4% calcium, the elevated calcium necessary for eggshell formation. Provide supplemental calcium in the form of crushed oyster shell in a separate container (not mixed into the feed), available free-choice. The hens will self-regulate their calcium intake.

**Do not supplement calcium before laying begins.** Excess calcium in growing birds can damage the kidneys. The switch from grower to layer feed should coincide with the onset of laying, not precede it.

## Daily Routine

Chicken keeping takes approximately 15--20 minutes per day in routine operation. The daily tasks:

**Morning (5 minutes):**
- Open the coop door (or confirm that the automatic door has opened).
- Check food and water. Refill as needed.
- Observe the birds as they emerge. A healthy chicken is alert, active, bright-eyed, and moves to the feeder promptly. A sick chicken is lethargic, fluffed up, hunched, or reluctant to leave the roost.

**Afternoon/Evening (10 minutes):**
- Collect eggs. Once daily is sufficient; twice daily in hot weather (heat degrades egg quality) or if you have an egg-eating problem.
- Refresh water if it has been soiled.
- Observe the flock during their active period. Watch for limping, sneezing, abnormal droppings, feather loss, or changes in pecking order that suggest bullying.

**At dusk (2 minutes):**
- Confirm all birds are inside the coop and the door is secured. Count heads. A missing bird at dusk means a bird is either hiding, stuck, or has been taken by a predator. Investigate immediately.

**Weekly (30 minutes):**
- Clean and refill waterers thoroughly.
- Add fresh bedding to the coop as needed (the deep litter method, where you add bedding on top of existing litter and allow the bottom layers to compost in place, works well and requires a full cleanout only 2--4 times per year).
- Inspect the coop for maintenance issues: holes, damaged hardware cloth, standing water, deteriorating wood.

**Seasonally:**
- Full coop cleanout (2--4 times per year).
- Inspect for mites and lice (check under wings and around the vent; look for tiny moving specks or clusters of eggs at the base of feathers).
- Check roost bars, nesting boxes, and structural elements for wear.

## PNW-Specific Concerns

### Covered Run: Essential, Not Optional

A covered run is the single most important PNW-specific investment. An uncovered run in a PNW winter becomes a mud pit within days of the first sustained rain (typically late October). The mud is not just aesthetically unpleasant --- it harbors parasites, bacteria, and the spores of *Histoplasma capsulatum* and *Aspergillus* fungi. Mud-caked feet are a pathway to bumblefoot (a staph infection of the foot pad). And chickens avoid mud, which means that an uncovered run in winter effectively confines the birds to the coop, negating the purpose of having a run at all.

Cover the run with a solid roof (corrugated metal or polycarbonate panels) or heavy-duty tarp. Slope the roof to direct water runoff away from the run. If the run floor is bare earth, consider adding a 4--6 inch layer of coarse sand or pea gravel, which drains quickly and does not compact into mud.

### Mud Boards

Where the run meets the coop, where birds step from covered to uncovered areas, and at the base of run walls, install "mud boards" --- short boards (6--12 inches high) that prevent mud from migrating into clean areas and that provide a dry step-up surface for birds entering the coop. A simple 2x6 across the coop entrance, with a layer of sand or gravel on the run side, makes a significant difference.

### Hawk and Raptor Deterrents

The PNW is raptor country. Cooper's hawks, red-tailed hawks, sharp-shinned hawks, great horned owls, barred owls, and bald eagles all take chickens. The most effective deterrent is a covered run (overhead protection). For free-range periods, consider:

- Overhead lines or fishing line strung across the yard at 5--8 foot height (disrupts raptor flight paths).
- Brush piles, shrubs, or portable shelters that provide cover for birds to hide under.
- Roosters (if legal in your jurisdiction) --- a rooster's primary job in a flock is predator detection and alarm. Hens without a rooster are significantly more vulnerable to surprise aerial attacks.
- A guardian animal (dog, goose, or llama) if your setup supports one.
- Reflective tape, predator decoys (owl decoys, hawk silhouettes), and similar passive deterrents have mixed effectiveness --- they may work briefly before raptors habituate.

### Raccoon-Proof Latches (RCN-02)

Raccoons are the apex predator of PNW chicken coops. They are intelligent (problem-solving comparable to primates in experimental settings), dexterous (they can manipulate latches, turn knobs, and lift hooks), nocturnal, persistent, and utterly without mercy. A raccoon that gains access to a coop will kill every bird it can reach, often killing far more than it can eat.

Standard hook-and-eye latches, sliding bolts, and spring latches are not raccoon-proof. The **RCN-02** latch protocol (referenced from the PNW predator defense documents) specifies:

- **Two-step latches:** Any latch that requires two simultaneous actions to open (e.g., lift and slide, push and turn). A carabiner clip through a hasp is simple and effective.
- **Padlocks** on all exterior access points (coop door, nesting box access door, run gate).
- **Automatic coop doors** with secure closing mechanisms that a raccoon cannot pry open. Quality automatic doors (the Omlet, ChickenGuard, and Run-Chicken brands are common in the PNW) use motor-driven guillotine or sliding doors that lock at the closed position.
- **No gaps larger than 1 inch** anywhere in the coop or run. Raccoons will reach through any gap they can fit a hand through, grab a bird, and pull. Weasels and mink --- also present in the PNW --- can pass through 1-inch openings.
- **Hardware cloth (1/2-inch welded wire mesh)** on all windows, ventilation openings, and run walls. Not chicken wire. Chicken wire keeps chickens in; it does not keep raccoons out.

### Legal Considerations

Most PNW cities allow backyard chickens with restrictions. The specifics vary by municipality:

- **Seattle:** Up to 8 domestic fowl (hens), no permit required. No roosters.
- **Portland:** No limit on hens (as of current code). No roosters within city limits unless permitted.
- **Tacoma:** Up to 6 hens on lots under 10,000 sq ft; more on larger lots. No roosters.
- **Olympia:** Up to 5 hens, no roosters.
- **Bellingham:** Up to 6 hens, no roosters.
- **Eugene:** Up to 6 hens, no roosters within city limits.
- **Unincorporated areas:** County regulations vary widely. Many rural areas have no restrictions on poultry numbers or roosters.

**Before buying chickens:** Check your municipal code. Not the internet consensus, not what your neighbor says, not what a Facebook group tells you --- the actual municipal code, available on your city's website or from your city clerk. Look for:

- Number of hens permitted.
- Rooster restrictions.
- Setback requirements (minimum distance from the coop to property lines and neighboring structures --- typically 10--25 feet).
- Permit or registration requirements (some cities require a small-animal permit).
- HOA restrictions (if applicable --- some homeowners' associations prohibit poultry regardless of municipal law).

The legal landscape for backyard chickens has generally moved toward permissiveness in the PNW over the last two decades, with many cities expanding their limits or relaxing setback requirements. But violations can result in fines and forced rehoming of birds, so it is worth 15 minutes of research before you commit.

## The Payoff

Six weeks after your first egg, you will be standing in the kitchen cracking a brown egg into a skillet and noticing that the yolk is a deep, almost orange gold --- not the pale yellow of a supermarket egg but the rich color that comes from a bird that has been eating grass, insects, and kitchen scraps alongside her balanced layer feed. The white will be thick and firm, standing up in the pan instead of spreading thin. You will eat those eggs, and you will understand, in a way that no amount of reading can prepare you for, why people keep chickens.

Six months in, you will know each bird by name, by personality, by the sound of her particular egg song. You will know which hen always lays in the left nesting box and which one prefers to lay in the corner of the coop behind the waterer. You will have dealt with your first soft-shelled egg, your first broody hen, your first raccoon scare. You will have stood in the rain refilling a waterer and cursed the weather, and then you will have watched your Barred Rock hen forage through the garden bed in the drizzle, utterly unbothered, pulling slugs from beneath the lettuce, and you will have felt something that is hard to name but easy to recognize: the quiet satisfaction of keeping animals well, of participating in a relationship between humans and birds that is 8,000 years old, of doing a small thing that works.

Start with six. Start with good breeds. Build a solid coop. And welcome to the flock.
