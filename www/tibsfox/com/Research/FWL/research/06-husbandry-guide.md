# Keeping Chickens -- A Complete Husbandry Guide

> **Research document 06 for the FWL (Fowl) project -- PNW Research Series**
>
> Chickens are among the most forgiving livestock animals a person can keep. They do not require acreage, specialized equipment, or a veterinary degree. They do require thoughtful housing, consistent nutrition, awareness of flock dynamics, vigilance against predators and disease, and -- in the Pacific Northwest -- a realistic relationship with rain. This document covers the full scope of backyard and small-flock chicken husbandry: coop design, feeding programs, flock management, health and disease, predator defense, seasonal care, legal frameworks, and the community of chicken keepers who make all of it more manageable and more fun.
>
> **Design principle:** This is a practical guide grounded in veterinary science, university extension research, and the accumulated experience of generations of poultry keepers. Where breed-specific advice is given, it is attributed. Where health information is provided, it reflects current veterinary consensus. The Pacific Northwest presents specific challenges -- persistent rain, dense predator populations, limited winter daylight -- and this guide addresses them directly rather than offering generic national advice.

## Housing -- The Coop and Run

A chicken coop is not a decoration. It is a life-support system that must accomplish four things simultaneously: protect birds from predators, protect birds from weather, provide adequate ventilation to manage moisture and ammonia, and offer comfortable spaces for roosting and laying. Every design decision flows from these four requirements.

### Space Requirements

The minimum recommended space allowance for standard-sized chickens is 4 square feet per bird inside the coop and 10 square feet per bird in an attached run. These are minimums, not ideals -- more space reduces stress, pecking, and disease transmission. For bantam breeds, 2-3 square feet inside and 8 square feet outside is generally adequate. For large-fowl breeds like Brahmas, Cochins, and Jersey Giants, 5-6 square feet inside is more appropriate.

In the Pacific Northwest, where rain can confine birds to covered areas for days or weeks at a time, the effective outdoor space is the covered run, not the total yard. An uncovered run in a PNW winter is a mud pit that birds will avoid. Design accordingly: either cover the entire run or provide enough covered space that birds can stay dry and active during prolonged rain events.

### Ventilation

Ventilation is the single most important and most frequently misunderstood element of coop design. Chickens exhale a remarkable amount of moisture -- a single hen produces approximately 1 pint of water per day through respiration and droppings. In an enclosed coop with 6 hens, that is 6 pints of water entering the air every 24 hours. Without adequate ventilation, this moisture condenses on cold surfaces, saturates the bedding, and creates ideal conditions for respiratory disease and frostbite.

The principle is simple: air must flow through the coop to carry moisture out, but drafts must not blow directly on roosting birds. The most effective design places ventilation openings high -- at or above roost height -- along the eaves or in the gable peaks, with the roost positioned below and out of the direct airflow. Hardware cloth (welded wire mesh, typically 1/2-inch) covers all ventilation openings to exclude predators.

A common rule of thumb is 1 square foot of ventilation per bird, though this can be adjusted based on climate. In the PNW, where humidity is already high, err on the side of more ventilation. A coop should never smell of ammonia -- if you can detect ammonia at bird height, ventilation is inadequate.

### Roost Bars

Chickens roost -- they sleep on elevated perches, gripping with their feet. Roost bars should be:

- **Wide and flat-topped:** A 2x4 board mounted with the 4-inch face up (so the flat side is the perch surface) allows birds to settle their bodies over their feet, covering the toes with breast feathers for warmth in cold weather. Round dowels require continuous gripping, which is less comfortable for heavy breeds and provides no frostbite protection for toes.
- **Elevated:** Higher than the nesting boxes (or birds will sleep in the nesting boxes and soil them with droppings). A height of 2-4 feet is typical, with a ramp or ladder for heavy breeds.
- **Spaced adequately:** Allow 8-12 inches of roost space per standard-sized bird. Birds will crowd together in cold weather and spread out in warm weather.
- **Not directly above feeders or waterers:** For obvious sanitary reasons.

### Nesting Boxes

Nesting boxes provide a dark, enclosed, comfortable space for hens to lay eggs. The standard recommendation is one nesting box for every 3-4 hens. In practice, hens will often choose a favorite box and queue for it while adjacent boxes sit empty -- this is normal flock behavior, not a sign of insufficient boxes.

Boxes should be approximately 12x12x12 inches for standard breeds (larger for heavy breeds like Brahmas), mounted lower than the roost bars, and filled with clean bedding -- straw, wood shavings, or nest pads. Some keepers add a curtain across the opening to create a darker, more private space, which can reduce egg-eating behavior.

Nesting boxes should be easy to access from outside the coop for egg collection. Many coop designs include a hinged lid or door on the exterior wall behind the nesting boxes.

### Predator-Proofing

Predator-proofing is not optional. The list of animals that will kill chickens is long, and in the Pacific Northwest, it includes raccoons, coyotes, bobcats, cougars, black bears, mink, weasels, rats, domestic dogs, red-tailed hawks, Cooper's hawks, great horned owls, bald eagles, and barred owls.

Key principles:

- **Hardware cloth, not chicken wire.** Chicken wire keeps chickens in; it does not keep predators out. Raccoons can reach through 2-inch chicken wire, grab a bird, and pull it through piece by piece. Use 1/2-inch welded hardware cloth on all windows, ventilation openings, and run walls.
- **Buried apron or trench.** Diggers (raccoons, coyotes, dogs, rats) will excavate under a run wall. Either bury hardware cloth 12-18 inches deep along the perimeter or bend it outward in an L-shaped apron extending 18-24 inches from the base of the wall. Cover the apron with soil or gravel.
- **Secure latches.** Raccoons are dexterous enough to open simple hook-and-eye latches, sliding bolts, and even some spring-loaded latches. Use carabiner clips, padlocks, or two-step latches that require simultaneous actions a raccoon cannot perform.
- **Covered run or overhead netting.** Hawks and owls attack from above. An uncovered run in raptor territory is an invitation. Heavy-gauge bird netting, welded wire, or a solid roof over the run provides overhead protection.
- **Lock birds in at night.** The most dangerous predators are nocturnal. An automatic coop door that closes at dusk and opens at dawn is one of the best investments a chicken keeper can make. Manual doors work fine if the keeper is consistently home at dusk.
- **No gaps larger than 1 inch.** Weasels and mink can pass through openings as small as 1 inch in diameter. A weasel in a coop is catastrophic -- they kill compulsively and can slaughter an entire flock in a single night.

### PNW-Specific Challenges

**Rain.** The Pacific Northwest receives 37-60 inches of rain annually in the lowlands (more in the foothills and mountains), with the majority falling between October and April. Chickens are not waterfowl -- prolonged exposure to rain soaks through their feathers, chills them, and promotes foot problems. A covered run is not a luxury in the PNW; it is a necessity. Roofing options include corrugated polycarbonate panels (translucent, allowing light), metal roofing, or tarps (temporary and less durable).

**Mud.** Rain plus chickens plus bare ground equals mud. PNW chicken runs become quagmires by mid-November. Effective mud management strategies include:

- Deep wood chips (6-12 inches of arborist chips, refreshed as they decompose)
- Sand (coarse construction sand drains well and is easy to rake clean)
- Gravel base with wood chips on top
- Raised run floor on a gravel pad
- Rotating access to multiple yard areas to prevent any one area from being destroyed

**Hawks.** Cooper's hawks (*Accipiter cooperii*) are abundant in PNW suburban and urban areas and are specialized bird-hunters capable of taking chickens up to bantam and small standard size. Red-tailed hawks (*Buteo jamaicensis*) are common and may take standard-sized birds, particularly young ones. Bald eagles (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*), though primarily fish-eaters, will take chickens opportunistically, especially near waterways. All raptors are federally protected under the Migratory Bird Treaty Act -- it is illegal to harm, harass, or kill them. The only legal defense is physical exclusion (covered runs, netting, overhead wires).

## Feeding -- Nutrition for Every Life Stage

Chickens are omnivores with specific nutritional requirements that change with age and production status. A well-fed hen lays well, resists disease, and lives longer.

### Layer Feed

Layer feed is formulated for hens in active egg production (typically 18 weeks and older). It contains 16-18% protein and 3.5-4.5% calcium -- the elevated calcium supports eggshell formation. Layer feed should not be fed to chicks, growing pullets, or roosters, as the high calcium content can cause kidney damage in birds not producing eggs.

### Grower Feed

Grower feed (14-18% protein, lower calcium) is formulated for pullets from 6-8 weeks through point of lay (18-20 weeks). Starter feed (18-22% protein) is used from hatch through 6-8 weeks. Medicated starter feed contains amprolium, a thiamine analog that helps prevent coccidiosis in young chicks exposed to the protozoan parasite in the soil -- it is not an antibiotic and does not contribute to antibiotic resistance.

### Scratch Grains

Scratch grains -- typically cracked corn, wheat, oats, and/or milo -- are a treat, not a complete feed. They are low in protein, low in calcium, and high in carbohydrates. Feeding excessive scratch dilutes the hen's intake of balanced layer feed and can reduce egg production and shell quality. A small handful per bird in the afternoon is sufficient -- it provides enrichment and a calorie boost for cold nights.

### Grit

Chickens do not have teeth. They grind their food in the gizzard (ventriculus), a thick-walled muscular organ that uses small stones (grit) as grinding media. Birds on pasture or with access to bare ground typically find their own grit. Confined birds need supplemental grit -- insoluble granite grit, available in chick-sized and adult-sized grades. Without grit, birds cannot effectively digest whole grains, fibrous plants, or insects.

### Oyster Shell

Supplemental calcium in the form of crushed oyster shell or limestone should be offered free-choice in a separate container (not mixed into the feed). Hens in heavy lay will consume oyster shell as needed to supplement the calcium in their layer feed. Offering it free-choice allows each bird to self-regulate her calcium intake -- critical because roosters and non-laying hens should not consume excess calcium.

### Kitchen Scraps -- Safe and Toxic Foods

Chickens will eat almost anything, but not everything is safe.

**Safe and beneficial:** Leafy greens (kale, lettuce, chard, spinach), squash, pumpkin, cucumbers, berries, melon, cooked rice, cooked pasta, oats, plain yogurt, mealworms, sunflower seeds, scrambled eggs (a good protein boost during molt).

**Feed in moderation:** Bread (low nutritional value), corn (high carbohydrate), fruit (sugar content).

**Toxic or dangerous:** Raw or dried beans (contain phytohaemagglutinin, a lectin that is toxic to chickens), avocado skin and pit (contain persin), chocolate (theobromine), onions in large quantities (can cause hemolytic anemia), green potato skins and sprouts (solanine), rhubarb leaves (oxalic acid).

### Fermented Feed

Fermented feed is layer feed (or any grain) soaked in water for 3-5 days until it undergoes lactic acid fermentation, similar to sauerkraut or yogurt. The fermentation process increases bioavailability of nutrients, introduces beneficial Lactobacillus bacteria to the gut, reduces feed pH (creating an inhospitable environment for Salmonella and E. coli), and improves feed conversion efficiency. Multiple poultry science studies have documented improvements in egg production, shell quality, and gut health in hens fed fermented versus dry feed. The practice is simple: soak feed in a bucket with enough water to cover it by 1-2 inches, stir daily, and feed after 3-5 days when it has a pleasantly sour smell.

### Fodder

Fodder -- sprouted grains (typically barley, wheat, or oats) grown to 4-6 inches of green growth on a tray -- provides fresh greens year-round, particularly valuable in PNW winters when pasture is dormant. A fodder system can be as simple as seed trays on a shelf with daily watering. A pound of barley seed produces approximately 6 pounds of fodder in 7 days. Fodder is high in moisture and provides vitamins, enzymes, and the behavioral enrichment of foraging through green material.

## Flock Dynamics

Chickens are social animals with complex group behaviors. Understanding flock dynamics is essential for managing a peaceful, productive flock.

### Pecking Order

The "pecking order" is not a metaphor borrowed from chickens -- it is a literal description of chicken social organization. Every flock has a linear or near-linear dominance hierarchy in which each bird knows its rank relative to every other bird. Dominant birds eat first, roost in preferred positions, and have priority access to nesting boxes and dust-bathing sites. Subordinate birds defer. The hierarchy is established through physical confrontations (pecking, chasing, chest-bumping, wing-flapping) and, once established, is maintained through threat displays rather than actual combat.

The pecking order is disrupted by any change in flock composition: adding new birds, removing birds, or the maturation of young birds into adulthood. Each disruption triggers a period of re-establishment that can involve aggressive behavior.

### Introducing New Birds

Adding new birds to an established flock is the most common source of conflict and injury in backyard flocks. The recommended protocol:

1. **Quarantine:** New birds should be isolated from the existing flock for a minimum of 30 days to observe for signs of disease. This is the most important and most frequently skipped step.
2. **Visual introduction:** After quarantine, house the new birds in a separate enclosure within sight of the existing flock -- a wire pen within the run, a crate in the coop, or an adjacent divided yard. This allows birds to see, hear, and assess each other without physical contact. Maintain this arrangement for at least 1-2 weeks.
3. **Supervised free-range:** Release all birds into a large, open area (preferably with multiple hiding spots, feed stations, and water stations) so subordinate birds can escape dominant ones. Late afternoon release gives birds less time to fight before roosting.
4. **Monitor.** Some pecking and chasing is normal and expected. Intervene if blood is drawn, a bird is pinned down and attacked repeatedly, or a bird is prevented from accessing food and water.

### Roosters -- Benefits and Challenges

A rooster is not required for egg production -- hens lay unfertilized eggs without a rooster present. However, a good rooster provides genuine benefits to a flock:

- **Predator alerting:** Roosters are vigilant sentinels. They vocalize specific alarm calls for aerial predators (a short, sharp call that sends hens running for cover) and ground predators (a repeated, lower-pitched call that causes hens to freeze and scan).
- **Flock cohesion:** A rooster tidbits -- finds food, picks it up, drops it, and calls hens over with a distinctive food call. This behavior holds the flock together and ensures hens find food sources.
- **Fertility:** Required if the goal is hatching chicks. One rooster per 8-12 hens is a standard ratio for reliable fertility.

The challenges are equally real:

- **Noise.** Roosters crow. They crow at dawn, at midday, at dusk, in the middle of the night if a light disturbs them, and whenever they feel like announcing their existence. Crowing is the single most common reason roosters are prohibited by municipal ordinance.
- **Aggression.** Some roosters become aggressive toward humans -- attacking with spurs, a sharp, bony growth on the inner leg that can cause genuine injury. Aggression is partly genetic (some breeds are more prone), partly developmental (hand-raised roosters that imprint on humans may see them as rivals rather than threats), and partly management-related (a rooster that has been chased or handled roughly may become defensive).
- **Overbreeding.** A rooster with too few hens will mate excessively, causing feather loss and skin damage on hens' backs and heads. Hen saddles (fabric covers worn on the back) can mitigate the damage, but the root solution is maintaining an appropriate rooster-to-hen ratio.

### Broody Management

Broodiness is the hormonal state in which a hen stops laying, sits tightly on a nest (her own eggs, other hens' eggs, golf balls -- she is not particular), and becomes committed to incubating for 21 days. Broodiness is a valuable trait if chicks are desired and an inconvenience if they are not, because a broody hen stops producing eggs and occupies a nesting box for weeks.

Breeds vary enormously in broodiness tendency. Silkies, Cochins, Orpingtons, and game breeds are notoriously broody. Leghorns, commercial hybrids, and most Mediterranean breeds rarely go broody -- the trait has been selected against in production breeds.

To break a broody hen that is not wanted for hatching: remove her from the nest repeatedly, collect eggs frequently to remove the stimulus, and -- the most reliable method -- place her in a wire-bottomed cage (a "broody breaker") elevated off the ground for 3-5 days. The air circulation under her body cools her breast and disrupts the hormonal feedback loop driving broodiness. It sounds harsh but is effective and does not harm the bird.

## Health and Disease

Chickens are hardy animals, but they are susceptible to specific diseases and parasites. Early recognition and basic biosecurity prevent most problems.

### Marek's Disease

Marek's disease is caused by a herpesvirus (Gallid alphaherpesvirus 2) that is ubiquitous in the environment -- virtually every chicken is exposed. The virus causes T-cell lymphomas that infiltrate nerves, organs, and skin. Symptoms include progressive paralysis (one leg forward, one back -- the classic "Marek's pose"), weight loss, blindness (iris depigmentation, or "gray eye"), and organ tumors. Mortality in unvaccinated flocks can reach 30-60%.

Vaccination is the primary defense. The Marek's vaccine (HVT -- herpesvirus of turkeys, serotype 3) is administered at the hatchery on day of hatch (or in ovo at day 18 of incubation). The vaccine does not prevent infection -- vaccinated birds still become infected and shed the virus -- but it prevents tumor formation and clinical disease. Vaccination must be done early because chicks are most vulnerable during the first weeks of life, before cell-mediated immunity matures. The vaccine takes approximately 7-14 days to confer full protection, during which time chicks should be isolated from older birds and contaminated environments.

### Coccidiosis

Coccidiosis is caused by protozoan parasites of the genus Eimeria, which infect the intestinal lining. There are multiple species of Eimeria that infect chickens, each targeting a specific region of the intestinal tract. Symptoms include bloody or watery diarrhea, lethargy, ruffled feathers, weight loss, and death in severe cases. Coccidiosis is the most common cause of death in young chicks (3-8 weeks old) raised on the ground.

Prevention involves either medicated starter feed (containing amprolium) or vaccination with a live, attenuated Eimeria vaccine given at the hatchery. Amprolium and the coccidiosis vaccine should not be used simultaneously, as the amprolium suppresses the low-level infection that the vaccine depends on to stimulate immunity. Gradual exposure to coccidia in the environment builds natural immunity by 12-16 weeks of age -- this is why adult chickens are rarely affected by coccidiosis even in contaminated environments.

### Respiratory Infections

Respiratory disease in chickens is common, multifactorial, and often frustrating. The major respiratory pathogens include:

- **Mycoplasma gallisepticum (MG):** Causes chronic respiratory disease (CRD). Symptoms include nasal discharge, swollen sinuses (infraorbital sinusitis), sneezing, and reduced egg production. MG is egg-transmitted -- infected hens pass it to their chicks in ovo. Carrier birds are infected for life.
- **Mycoplasma synoviae (MS):** Causes respiratory symptoms and synovitis (joint inflammation). Often a co-infection with MG.
- **Infectious bronchitis (IB):** A coronavirus (unrelated to SARS-CoV-2) that causes respiratory distress, drop in egg production, and -- critically -- permanent damage to the oviduct in young birds, resulting in lifelong shell quality issues, misshapen eggs, or cessation of laying.
- **Infectious laryngotracheitis (ILT):** A herpesvirus causing severe upper respiratory disease with bloody mucus, gasping, and high mortality.

Biosecurity -- limiting exposure to wild birds, new flock additions, and contaminated equipment -- is the first line of defense. Vaccination is available for IB and ILT. There is no practical vaccine for MG in backyard flocks; MG-positive birds should be managed as a closed flock (no birds sold or given away, no birds shown at poultry events).

### Bumblefoot (Pododermatitis)

Bumblefoot is a staphylococcal infection of the foot pad, typically caused by *Staphylococcus aureus* entering through a small cut or abrasion. It presents as a swollen, warm foot pad with a characteristic dark scab (the "bumble") on the bottom surface. Contributing factors include heavy breeds jumping down from high roosts onto hard surfaces, rough or splintered perch material, and wet, unsanitary bedding.

Treatment involves soaking the foot in warm Epsom salt water, carefully debriding the scab and removing the core of infected tissue (a caseous plug), cleaning the wound with antiseptic (dilute chlorhexidine or betadine), packing with antibiotic ointment, and bandaging. Mild cases respond well to this treatment; severe or recurrent cases may require systemic antibiotics prescribed by a veterinarian.

### Egg Binding

Egg binding occurs when a hen is unable to pass an egg that is stuck in the oviduct or cloaca. It is a veterinary emergency -- an egg-bound hen can die within 24-48 hours from exhaustion, infection, or internal organ damage. Signs include a hen straining in the nesting box or on the ground, a penguin-like waddling gait, lethargy, and cessation of eating and drinking. The egg may be palpable in the abdomen.

First-aid measures include a warm water soak (the warmth relaxes the muscles of the oviduct), application of lubricant (vegetable oil or water-based personal lubricant) to the vent, and placement in a warm, dark, quiet environment. Calcium supplementation (oral calcium gluconate or calcium citrate) may help stimulate uterine contractions. If the egg does not pass within a few hours, veterinary intervention is necessary.

### Parasites

**External parasites:**
- *Northern fowl mite (Ornithonyssus sylviarum):* The most common external parasite of chickens in North America. Mites live on the bird (not in the environment) and feed on blood. Heavy infestations cause anemia, reduced egg production, and death in severe cases. Check for mites by examining the vent area -- mites appear as tiny gray-brown dots moving on the skin, with clumps of mite debris at the base of feathers. Treatment includes permethrin-based poultry dust or spray, applied directly to birds, with re-treatment in 7-10 days to kill newly hatched nymphs.
- *Red mite (Dermanyssus gallinae):* Less common in North America than in Europe, but present in PNW flocks. Red mites live in coop crevices during the day and feed on birds at night. Heavy infestations cause anemia and reluctance to roost. Treatment focuses on the coop environment: thorough cleaning, application of diatomaceous earth or permethrin to crevices, and -- in severe cases -- painting coop surfaces with a mite-blocking agent.
- *Poultry lice:* Several species, all living on the bird and feeding on feathers and skin debris (not blood). Lice are visible as pale, elongated insects moving on the skin, particularly around the vent and under the wings. Treatment with permethrin dust is effective.

**Internal parasites:**
- *Roundworms (Ascaridia galli):* The most common intestinal parasite. Heavy burdens cause weight loss, reduced egg production, and intestinal blockage. Fenbendazole (Safe-Guard) is the standard treatment, though it is not FDA-approved for laying hens in the US (an off-label use commonly recommended by poultry veterinarians, with a 14-day egg withdrawal period).
- *Capillary worms, cecal worms, tapeworms:* Also common, treated with fenbendazole (for nematodes) or praziquantel (for tapeworms). Fecal float testing by a veterinarian identifies the specific parasite load and guides treatment.

## Predator Management -- PNW-Specific Threats

The Pacific Northwest hosts a dense and diverse predator community. Understanding each predator's behavior patterns enables targeted defense.

### Raccoons (*Procyon lotor*)

Raccoons are the most persistent and problematic chicken predator in PNW suburban and urban areas. They are nocturnal, intelligent, dexterous, and strong --- equipped with approximately 438 million cortical neurons and forepaws whose tactile sensitivity rivals that of primate hands [RCN-01, RCN-02]. A raccoon can open simple latches, peel back chicken wire, dig under fences, and reach through gaps to grab and kill birds. Raccoons typically kill by reaching through fencing and pulling off a bird's head or limbs. Their long-term memory means a raccoon that defeats a particular latch design will remember the solution for years [RCN-02]. Defense: 1/2-inch hardware cloth on all openings, two-step latches that require simultaneous actions (carabiner clips or padlocks), and secure night housing. Raccoon populations in PNW cities are among the densest in North America, with suburban densities of 30-75 individuals per km² [RCN-04].

### Coyotes (*Canis latrans*)

Coyotes are present in urban, suburban, and rural areas throughout the PNW and are active primarily at dawn, dusk, and night (though urban coyotes may be active during daylight). A coyote can scale a 5-foot fence and will dig under one. They typically carry off entire birds. Defense: 6-foot fencing with a coyote roller or outward-angled extension at the top, buried apron at the base, and secure night housing.

### Hawks and Eagles

Cooper's hawks are the most frequent raptor predator of backyard chickens in PNW urban and suburban areas. They are agile, fast, and specialized in catching birds in cluttered environments -- exactly the conditions of a backyard with trees and fences. Red-tailed hawks take chickens in more open settings. Bald eagles are opportunistic near waterways. All are federally protected. Defense: covered runs, overhead netting, or fishing line strung in a grid pattern over the run area (the visual disruption deters raptors from diving). Providing dense shrubs, lean-to shelters, or covered areas in the free-range yard gives hens escape cover.

### Rats (*Rattus norvegicus*)

Norway rats are abundant in PNW urban and suburban areas. They steal eggs, kill chicks, and chew through wood and plastic feeders. A rat infestation in a coop area is a biosecurity risk -- rats carry leptospirosis, salmonella, and other pathogens. Defense: store feed in metal bins with tight-fitting lids, remove feeders at night, use 1/2-inch hardware cloth on all openings (rats can squeeze through 1-inch gaps), and maintain bait stations if necessary (use tamper-resistant stations to protect non-target animals).

### Neighborhood Dogs

Domestic dogs are a leading cause of chicken mortality in suburban flocks. A dog does not need to be aggressive or hungry -- the pursuit instinct alone can trigger a killing spree. A loose dog can kill an entire flock in minutes. Defense: secure fencing, honest conversations with neighbors about loose dogs, and awareness that even a "friendly" dog is a risk to chickens. Dog attacks are the one predator event most likely to result in total flock loss.

## Seasonal Care

### Winter in the PNW

Pacific Northwest winters are wet, dark, and moderately cold -- typically 35-45 degrees F in the lowlands, with occasional dips to the teens or low 20s during Arctic outflow events. Most chicken breeds handle PNW winter temperatures without supplemental heat if they are dry and out of drafts.

**Frostbite prevention:** Frostbite affects combs, wattles, and toes. It is caused by moisture, not cold -- a dry chicken in still air can tolerate temperatures well below freezing. Frostbite occurs when moisture from respiration or condensation settles on exposed tissue and freezes. Prevention: maintain excellent ventilation (to remove moist air), avoid using heated waterers that create steam, and select breeds with small combs (pea combs, rose combs, walnut combs) for frostbite-prone environments. Applying a thin layer of petroleum jelly to combs and wattles before extreme cold events provides modest additional protection.

**Lighting for egg production:** Chickens require approximately 14-16 hours of daylight to maintain peak egg production. PNW winter daylength drops to approximately 8.5 hours at the solstice (latitude 47-48 degrees N for Seattle-area flocks). Without supplemental lighting, most hens reduce or cease laying during the dark months. A single low-wattage LED bulb (5-9 watts) on a timer, set to turn on in the early morning hours (providing light before dawn), extends the perceived day length and maintains production. The light should supplement the morning, not the evening -- an abrupt lights-out in the evening forces birds off the roost in darkness and can cause pile-ups and injuries.

**Cold-hardy breeds:** Breeds with small combs, dense feathering, and medium to large body mass handle PNW winters well. Excellent choices include Australorp, Orpington, Plymouth Rock, Wyandotte (rose comb), Chantecler (cushion comb, developed for Canadian winters), Sussex, Brahma, and Cochin.

### Summer

PNW summers are generally mild, but heat waves (periods above 90 degrees F) can be dangerous. Chickens dissipate heat through panting and by holding their wings away from their bodies. They cannot sweat. Heat stress begins at approximately 85 degrees F and becomes critical above 95 degrees F, particularly for heavy breeds, feather-legged breeds, and birds in active lay.

**Heat stress mitigation:** Provide deep shade (natural or constructed), unlimited fresh cool water (add ice on extreme days), frozen treats (watermelon, frozen vegetable blocks), and good ventilation. Avoid handling birds during the hottest hours. Watch for signs of severe heat stress: open-mouth breathing, wings held away from body, lethargy, pale comb, and collapse. Severe heat stress is a veterinary emergency -- immerse the bird in cool (not cold) water up to the neck and provide electrolytes.

### Molting

Molt is the annual replacement of feathers, triggered by decreasing day length in late summer and fall. A molting hen looks terrible -- ragged, patchy, sometimes nearly bald -- and she feels terrible, too. Feather growth is protein-intensive (feathers are approximately 85% keratin protein), and hens typically cease egg production during molt, redirecting metabolic resources to feather replacement.

Molt duration varies: "hard molters" drop most feathers simultaneously and regrow them quickly (6-8 weeks), returning to lay sooner. "Soft molters" replace feathers gradually over 3-4 months, maintaining a more normal appearance but taking longer to resume production. Paradoxically, the ugliest molters are often the best layers -- they molt hard because they have been laying hard.

During molt, increase dietary protein to 18-22% (switch to a game bird feed, add mealworms, or offer scrambled eggs) to support feather regrowth. Do not supplement light during molt -- the natural decrease in day length is the trigger for molt, and fighting it with artificial light can extend the process and stress the bird.

## Legal Considerations

### Municipal Chicken Ordinances

Keeping chickens in urban and suburban areas is legal in most PNW jurisdictions but subject to municipal ordinances that vary significantly from city to city.

**Seattle, WA:** Up to 8 small animals (chickens, ducks, rabbits) permitted on any lot without a permit. Roosters are not prohibited by city code, though noise ordinances apply. No minimum lot size requirement.

**Portland, OR:** Up to 3 chickens permitted on standard residential lots without a permit. Additional chickens require a conditional use permit. Roosters subject to noise complaints under general nuisance ordinances.

**Tacoma, WA:** Chickens permitted; no specific limit specified in the current code for hens. Roosters restricted in most residential zones.

**Bellevue, WA:** Up to 6 hens (no roosters) permitted on residential lots. Coop must be at least 10 feet from any property line and 20 feet from any neighboring dwelling.

**Eugene, OR:** Up to 6 chickens permitted. Coop setback requirements apply.

These ordinances change -- always verify current regulations with your local planning or animal control department before establishing a flock. Common restrictions include limits on the number of birds, prohibition of roosters, coop setback requirements from property lines and neighboring dwellings, and requirements that coops be kept clean and free of offensive odors.

### Rooster Restrictions

Roosters are the single most contentious issue in municipal chicken keeping. Their crowing -- which can reach 130 decibels at close range and begins well before dawn -- creates conflict with neighbors. Many PNW jurisdictions either ban roosters outright in residential zones or regulate them under noise ordinances. For keepers who want fertile eggs or the protective benefits of a rooster, rural properties outside city limits typically have fewer restrictions.

## The Backyard Chicken Community

One of the unexpected rewards of keeping chickens is the community. Chicken people are generous, knowledgeable, and passionate. They will diagnose your hen's respiratory infection from a blurry photograph, drive across town to deliver a bag of feather-fixer feed, and spend three hours on a Saturday helping you build a coop.

### Online Forums and Groups

**BackYardChickens.com (BYC):** The largest online poultry forum in the English-speaking world, with over 500,000 registered members. BYC hosts breed-specific forums, health and disease threads, coop design showcases, and regional groups. The "Emergencies / Diseases / Injuries and Cures" forum is an invaluable resource for backyard keepers without ready access to a poultry-experienced veterinarian.

**Facebook groups:** Numerous regional and breed-specific groups. PNW-specific groups include Pacific Northwest Poultry Keepers, Seattle Chicken People, and various state-level groups.

**Reddit:** r/BackYardChickens (600,000+ subscribers) provides a mix of advice, photographs, and community support.

### Local Clubs and Associations

The American Poultry Association (APA) and the American Bantam Association (ABA) are the national breed standard organizations. Regional poultry clubs host meetings, educational programs, and sanctioned shows. In the PNW, clubs such as the Oregon Poultry Association, Washington State Poultry Fanciers, and the Pacific Northwest Poultry Association provide local connections.

### Shows, Swaps, and Sales

Poultry shows are where breeders exhibit birds judged against the written breed standard in the APA's *Standard of Perfection* or the ABA's *Bantam Standard*. Shows are educational, social, and competitive -- and they are the primary venue where show-quality breeding stock changes hands. For someone raising show birds, these events are the crucible in which years of selective breeding are tested, validated, and refined.

Poultry swaps -- informal events where birds, hatching eggs, and equipment are bought, sold, and traded -- are common in rural PNW communities. They range from parking-lot meet-ups to organized events at fairgrounds. Biosecurity at swaps is a legitimate concern: birds from multiple flocks comingle, and disease transmission is a real risk. Experienced keepers quarantine any birds acquired at swaps before integrating them into their home flocks.

### Mentorship

The most valuable resource for a new chicken keeper is an experienced one. A mentor who has kept chickens for 10 or 20 years has already encountered every disease, predator attack, broody hen, egg anomaly, and coop design mistake that a beginner will face. Finding a mentor -- through a local poultry club, an online forum, or a neighbor with a well-managed flock -- accelerates the learning curve enormously and prevents needless bird suffering from novice mistakes.

The backyard chicken community exists because people care about their birds. Not as abstract units of production, but as individual animals with names and personalities, quirks and preferences, beauty and value. The person who builds a show flock over years of careful breeding, who can identify each bird by sight, who knows which hen lays which egg -- that person is carrying forward a tradition of animal stewardship that is older than written history. This guide is in service of that tradition.
