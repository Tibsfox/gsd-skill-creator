# The Asiatic Class: Giants from the East

The Asiatic Class of the American Poultry Association encompasses some of the largest, most imposing, and most historically consequential chicken breeds ever to reach Western shores. These are the birds that arrived in America and Europe in the mid-nineteenth century and fundamentally transformed poultry keeping from a casual farmyard activity into a passionate, organized pursuit. The Brahma, the Cochin, and the Langshan — the three pillars of the Asiatic Class — triggered a cultural phenomenon that poultry historians call "Hen Fever," a speculative mania that rivaled tulip crazes and gold rushes in its intensity. They were joined in the APA Standard by the ancient fighting breeds of Southeast Asia — the Malay, the Aseel, and the Sumatra — birds whose lineages stretch back thousands of years into the deep history of galliform domestication.

Asiatic breeds share certain defining characteristics: large to massive body size, feathered shanks and toes (in the Brahma, Cochin, and Langshan), a general tendency toward docility and broodiness, cold hardiness, and relatively moderate egg production. They mature slowly. They brood devotedly. They are, in a word, magnificent.

## Brahma

### Origin and History

The Brahma is the "King of All Poultry" — a title earned through sheer size, commanding presence, and a regal dignity that few other breeds can match. The breed's origins are a matter of considerable historical debate, but the consensus view traces it to large feather-legged fowl imported from the Chinese port of Shanghai in the 1840s and 1850s, crossed with Chittagong or "Gray" fowl from the Bengal region of India. The name "Brahma" derives from the Brahmaputra River of India, though the breed as we know it was refined primarily in America.

The Brahma's claim to American fame was cemented in December 1852, when George Burnham sent nine Light Brahmas as a gift to Queen Victoria of England. This royal association fueled enormous public interest in the breed. The Light Brahma was admitted to the first APA Standard of Perfection in 1874, along with the Dark variety. The Buff Brahma was admitted in 1924.

For much of the late nineteenth century, Brahmas were the primary meat breed in America. Flocks of Light Brahmas supplied much of New York City's poultry market from the 1850s through the 1930s.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes three varieties: **Light** (1874), **Dark** (1874), and **Buff** (1924).

### Physical Description

Brahmas are massive, imposing birds that stand tall and carry themselves with an unmistakable presence. Up close, the first thing you notice is the sheer physical mass --- a mature Brahma rooster can fill your arms like a feathered toddler, solid and warm, his body dense with muscle beneath feathers that feel like layered silk against your hands. The body is deep, broad, and long, with a wide stance created in part by the profuse feathering on the shanks and toes. The head is comparatively small for the body size, which contributes to the Brahma's gentle expression --- there is something almost owlish about a Brahma's face, those prominent brow ridges shading reddish-bay eyes that regard you with calm intelligence. The **pea comb** is a defining feature --- small, close-fitting, with three low ridges --- providing excellent frostbite resistance. Pick up a Brahma and you feel the architecture of the bird: the keel bone running like a ship's prow beneath dense breast muscle, the surprisingly delicate feet hidden inside those elaborate feathered boots, the slow, strong heartbeat against your palm.

The **Light Brahma** has a white body with a black hackle (each feather white-edged), a black tail, and black in the wing flights. It is one of the most visually striking of all chicken breeds. The **Dark Brahma** shows dramatic sexual dimorphism: males have a silver-white hackle and saddle with a black body, while females are covered in intricate steel-gray penciling on a dark background — a complex and beautiful pattern. The **Buff Brahma** follows the same pattern as the Light but with buff replacing white.

Foot feathering is profuse, extending down the outer toes, creating the appearance of wearing elaborate feathered boots. This feathering must be maintained carefully, especially in muddy or wet conditions.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 12 lbs, Hen 9.5 lbs, Cockerel 10 lbs, Pullet 8 lbs. Historical specimens were reported even heavier, with some exhibition males reaching 14-18 lbs, though such weights are rare in modern stock.

### Temperament and Production

Brahmas are famous for their calm, gentle, almost placid temperament. They tolerate handling well and rarely become flighty. Their enormous size might suggest clumsiness, but healthy Brahmas move with surprising grace. They are outstanding cold-weather birds — the combination of pea comb, dense plumage, and feathered feet makes them nearly impervious to harsh winters. They actually tend to struggle more in extreme heat.

Hens lay approximately 150-200 large brown eggs per year, with production often concentrated in the cooler months (October through May), a pattern that made them economically valuable in an era before artificial lighting and refrigeration. Brahma hens are devoted brooders and excellent mothers, capable of covering a large clutch due to their size.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Brahmas as **Watch**. The Light variety is the most common, with a healthy population among heritage breed enthusiasts. The Dark and Buff varieties are rarer and deserve attention from conservation breeders.

## Cochin

### Origin and History

If the Brahma is the king, the Cochin is the breed that launched a revolution. When the first Cochins arrived in England and America in the late 1840s — imported from the Chinese port of Shanghai (and initially called "Shanghai" fowl) — they triggered a phenomenon known as **"Hen Fever"** or **"The Cochin Craze"** that swept through the English-speaking world from roughly 1845 to 1855.

The fever was extraordinary. Cochin prices soared to staggering heights. A single pair of exhibition Cochins could sell for what a skilled laborer earned in a year. Queen Victoria exhibited Cochins at the first major English poultry show. Newspapers covered poultry shows with the breathless enthusiasm usually reserved for horse races. Poultry clubs formed. Breeding programs proliferated. An entire culture of competitive poultry exhibition was born, essentially overnight, and the Cochin was its catalyst.

The mania eventually subsided, as manias do, but its legacy was permanent. The organized poultry fancy, the system of breed standards, the tradition of exhibition shows — all of these trace directly to the Cochin craze. The APA itself, founded in 1873, was a product of the institutional infrastructure built during the Hen Fever years.

Cochins were admitted to the APA Standard in 1874.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Buff** (1874), **Partridge** (1874), **White** (1874), **Black** (1874), **Blue** (1984), **Brown** (1874), **Silver Laced** (1874), **Golden Laced** (1874), and **Barred** (1982).

### Physical Description

The Cochin is a spectacularly fluffy bird. The plumage is abundant, soft, and loose-fitting, creating an almost spherical silhouette that makes the bird appear even larger than it already is. The body beneath all that fluff is broad and deep, carried low to the ground. The short, densely feathered legs and profuse foot and toe feathering add to the rounded effect. From a distance, a Cochin looks like an enormous, self-propelled feather pillow.

The **Buff** variety — a rich, even golden-buff across the entire body — remains the most popular and is the color most associated with the breed. The Partridge variety features intricate penciling on the female similar to the Dark Brahma hen. The Black variety should show a brilliant greenish sheen.

The single comb is small to medium, with five points. The small head and gentle expression are characteristic.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 11 lbs, Hen 8.5 lbs, Cockerel 9 lbs, Pullet 7 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Cochins are among the most docile breeds in existence. They are calm to the point of being sedentary, which means they can become overweight without management. They tolerate confinement well --- perhaps too well, as they are not ambitious foragers. They are famously friendly and often become genuine pets, seeking human attention and tolerating being carried. A Cochin on your lap is a warm, impossibly soft experience --- the feathers yield to your fingers like the finest down comforter, and the bird settles into your arms with a contented, purring trill that sounds nothing like the sharp cluck of a Leghorn. They make a low, rhythmic cooing when content, almost like a pigeon, and will fall asleep in your hands if you stroke the feathers along their back.

Egg production is moderate: approximately 150-180 large brown eggs per year. But the Cochin's greatest reproductive contribution is **broodiness**. Cochin hens are champion brooders and mothers. They will sit on virtually anything, hatch it reliably, and mother the chicks with fierce devotion. Before artificial incubation became reliable, Cochins (and Cochin crosses) were kept specifically as living incubators, brooding eggs from other breeds, from ducks, and even from game birds.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists Cochins as **Recovering**. The Buff variety is widely available, but several varieties — particularly Silver Laced, Golden Laced, and Brown — are quite rare in quality stock.

## Langshan

### Origin and History

The Langshan completes the trio of great Asiatic breeds. It was imported to England from the Langshan district near the Yangtze River in China by Major F.T. Croad in 1872. The breed arrived in America shortly thereafter. The Black Langshan was admitted to the APA in 1883.

The Langshan's arrival was controversial. Some prominent poultry fanciers insisted Langshans were merely Cochins, leading to heated debates in the poultry press. The "Langshan Controversy" raged through the 1870s and 1880s before the breed's distinct identity was firmly established.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes three varieties: **Black** (1883), **White** (1893), and **Blue** (1987).

### Physical Description

The Langshan is the most upright and elegant of the Asiatic breeds. Where the Cochin is round and the Brahma is massive, the Langshan is tall and refined. The body is deep and somewhat narrow, carried high with a distinctly upright carriage. The tail is long and full, carried high, giving the bird a striking vertical profile. The overall impression is one of height and grace rather than bulk.

The **Black Langshan** is a stunning bird, with solid black plumage showing an intense beetle-green sheen — among the most brilliant green sheens of any breed. The **White** variety is pure white. The **Blue** variety displays an even slate-blue.

Shanks are feathered but less profusely than Brahmas or Cochins — the feathering runs down the outside of the shanks and outer toes only. The shanks themselves are dark slate-blue to black, with pinkish-white foot bottoms. The single comb is medium-sized and well-formed.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9.5 lbs, Hen 7.5 lbs, Cockerel 8 lbs, Pullet 6.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Langshans are active, alert birds — considerably more energetic than Cochins. They are good foragers and tolerate both confinement and free-range conditions. They are gentle and handleable but maintain an air of independence. Hens lay approximately 150-210 large dark brown eggs per year — the Langshan egg is notably dark among the Asiatic breeds, with some strains producing a distinctly plum-toned brown. Hens will go broody and make good mothers.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Black Langshan is the most available, but all three varieties are uncommon. The White and Blue varieties are genuinely rare. The Langshan's elegance and practical virtues deserve wider appreciation.

## Sumatra

### Origin and History

The Sumatra is one of the most ancient and visually dramatic breeds in the Asiatic Class. It originates from the island of Sumatra in Indonesia, where it lived in a semi-wild or feral state. The breed was imported to America and Europe in the mid-nineteenth century, originally valued as a fighting fowl. It was admitted to the APA in 1883.

The Sumatra occupies a unique position in poultry taxonomy — it is among the breeds closest to wild jungle fowl in type, and some ornithologists have suggested it may carry genes from species other than the Red Jungle Fowl (*Gallus gallus*), the primary ancestor of domestic chickens.

### Physical Description

The Sumatra is a long, low, streamlined bird with a pheasant-like carriage. The body is moderately long, the breast full, and the tail extraordinarily long and flowing, carried horizontally or slightly drooping. Males may have tail feathers that sweep the ground. The plumage is tight and close-fitting, with an intense, lustrous beetle-green sheen on the black feathers that surpasses even the Langshan in brilliance — the Sumatra's greenish-purple iridescence is among the most striking in all of poultry.

A distinctive feature is the **multiple spurs**: Sumatra males frequently develop multiple spurs on each leg, a trait rarely seen in other domesticated breeds. The face is dark, often with a distinctive **gypsy face** — dark purple or mulberry coloring on the face and earlobes. The comb is a small pea comb, dark in color. Legs are black.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 5 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 4 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Sumatras are active, alert, and can be flighty. They are excellent fliers — better than most domestic breeds — and will roost in trees given the opportunity. They retain strong semi-wild instincts. Hens lay approximately 100-150 small to medium white or very lightly tinted eggs per year. They go broody and are attentive mothers. Sumatras are not practical production birds; they are kept for their extraordinary beauty and historical significance.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. Sumatras have a dedicated following among exhibition breeders who prize their dramatic appearance, but overall numbers are limited.

## Malay

### Origin and History

The Malay is one of the tallest chicken breeds in the world, capable of reaching 30 inches or more in height, and one of the most ancient. It descends from the great fighting fowl of Southeast Asia, where cockfighting traditions stretch back thousands of years. The breed takes its name from the Malay Peninsula (modern Malaysia), though birds of this type were distributed across a wide region including India, Malaysia, and Indonesia. Malays were imported to England in the early nineteenth century and reached America by the 1840s. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1883.

### Physical Description

The Malay is unmistakable. It is a tall, powerfully built bird with an extreme upright posture, long neck, long legs, and a distinctive "three curves" profile: the neck curves forward, the back slopes downward, and the tail curves down. The body is narrow and hard-muscled, built for fighting rather than egg or meat production. The skull is broad with a cruel, overhanging brow created by prominent eyebrows and a beetling forehead. The expression is fierce. The comb is a small, tight **strawberry comb** or **walnut comb**. The face is smooth with minimal wattles.

Plumage is sparse, tight, and close-fitting, leaving parts of the breastbone and keel exposed in well-bred specimens — this is a feature, not a fault. Legs are long, thick, and heavily scaled, colored yellow. The overall impression is one of angular, muscular power.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9 lbs, Hen 7 lbs, Cockerel 7 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Malays are bold, assertive, and can be aggressive — particularly males toward other males. They require experienced handlers and should generally not be mixed with docile breeds. Despite their fierce reputation, well-socialized Malays can be surprisingly steady with their keepers. Hens lay approximately 70-120 medium brown eggs per year, making them among the lowest producers of any recognized breed. They are kept for exhibition, for their extraordinary appearance, and for their historical significance as the ancestral fighting type from which many modern breeds descend.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Malay is uncommon in America but maintains a dedicated following among game fowl enthusiasts and serious exhibitors.

## Aseel (Asil)

### Origin and History

The Aseel (also spelled Asil, from the Arabic word meaning "pure" or "noble") is perhaps the world's oldest breed of gamefowl, with a lineage traceable to India over 3,500 years ago. These birds were the gladiators of the Indian subcontinent, bred for stamina, courage, and fighting ability across millennia. The breed was imported to England in the early 1800s and reached America in the nineteenth century. It was admitted to the APA Standard in 1981, making it one of the more recent APA admissions despite being one of the oldest breeds on earth.

### Physical Description

The Aseel is a compact, heavily muscled bird with a low, powerful stance. The body is broad and flat across the back, with a prominent, hard breast and extraordinarily wide-set legs. The legs are thick, heavily boned, and set wide apart, giving the bird a distinctive swagger. The head is broad with a pea comb, bright pearl or yellow eyes, and minimal wattles. The expression is alert and determined.

Plumage is sparse and tight, fitting the body like a suit of armor. Feathers are hard and close, not soft or fluffy. This tight plumage gives the Aseel a hard, chiseled appearance quite unlike the softness of most domestic breeds. The APA recognizes several color varieties including Black Breasted Red, Dark, Spangled, White, and Wheaten.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 5.5 lbs, Hen 4 lbs, Cockerel 4.5 lbs, Pullet 3.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Aseels are the most combative of the recognized breeds. Males are often intolerant of other males from a very young age — sometimes from hatch. They must be housed separately or in carefully managed situations. However, Aseels form remarkably strong bonds with their keepers and can be handleable, even affectionate, with familiar people. Hens lay approximately 40-80 small to medium cream or tinted eggs per year — the lowest production of any APA-recognized breed. But hens are phenomenal mothers: fiercely protective, deeply devoted, and willing to fight predators many times their size to defend their chicks.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Aseel's reputation for aggression and low productivity limits its appeal, but it represents thousands of years of selective breeding and carries irreplaceable genetic heritage for hardiness, disease resistance, and maternal instinct.

## The Hen Fever: A Cultural Earthquake

The Cochin craze of the late 1840s and 1850s deserves special attention because it transformed not just poultry keeping but the entire culture of livestock exhibition. Before Hen Fever, chickens were utilitarian farmyard animals. After it, they were objects of art, science, competition, and intense financial speculation.

### The Spark

It began with the arrival of large, feathered-leg fowl from China on ships returning from the Far East. The birds were unlike anything Western poultry keepers had seen: enormous, profusely feathered, docile, and available in extraordinary colors. Queen Victoria received some as diplomatic gifts and exhibited them publicly. The public response was electric.

### The Fever

By 1850, "Hen Fever" had infected both England and America. Poultry shows attracted thousands of spectators. Newspapers devoted columns to breeding and exhibition results. Prices for top specimens reached absurd levels — $100 to $300 for a single bird at a time when a laborer might earn $1 per day. Entrepreneurs imported birds by the shipload. New breeds and varieties appeared with dizzying speed, not all of them honestly represented. Fraud was rampant: birds were dyed, combs were trimmed, and pedigrees were fabricated.

The key figure in the American Hen Fever was **George P. Burnham**, a Boston businessman who both profited from and satirized the craze. His 1855 book, *The History of the Hen Fever: A Humorous Record*, remains the definitive contemporary account. Burnham himself was a shameless promoter — his gift of Brahmas to Queen Victoria was a calculated publicity stunt that succeeded spectacularly.

### The Legacy

When the speculative bubble burst in the mid-1850s, prices crashed and many breeders were ruined. But the infrastructure remained. The poultry clubs, the exhibition circuits, the breed standards, the breeding records, and the public enthusiasm for fine poultry — all endured. The American Poultry Association was founded in 1873, and its first Standard of Perfection in 1874 codified the breeds and varieties that had emerged from the Hen Fever years.

The Asiatic breeds were at the center of this transformation. Cochins, Brahmas, and their crosses formed the foundation stock for dozens of subsequent American and European breeds. The Plymouth Rock, the Wyandotte, the Orpington, the Langshan — all carry Asiatic blood. The history of modern poultry is, in a very real sense, the story of what happened after large Chinese fowl arrived on Western docks and captured the public imagination.

## The Feathered Foot Question

One of the most distinctive features of the Asiatic breeds (excepting the Malay, Aseel, and Sumatra) is feathered shanks and toes. This trait is governed by specific genetic loci and is thought to have evolved as an adaptation to cold mountain environments in central and eastern Asia. The feathering provides insulation and protection from snow and ice, though it comes with management challenges: muddy conditions can cause matting and bacterial infections, and feathered feet require regular attention to keep clean and healthy.

In exhibition, foot feathering is a critical judging criterion. Brahmas should show moderate feathering extending to the outer toes. Cochins should display heavy, profuse feathering giving a rounded, fully covered appearance. Langshans should show lighter feathering confined to the outer shanks and outer toes — they are the most lightly feathered of the three.

For practical flock keepers, feathered feet mean providing dry, clean bedding and avoiding muddy runs. Many keepers of Asiatic breeds maintain deep-litter coops with excellent drainage and raise roosts high enough to keep foot feathers out of bedding contact.

In the Pacific Northwest, feather-footed Asiatic breeds face a particular challenge: the region's persistent rain from October through April transforms uncovered runs into quagmires that mat and soil foot feathering, promoting bacterial infections including bumblefoot (*Staphylococcus aureus*). PNW keepers of Brahmas, Cochins, and Langshans must provide covered runs with deep wood-chip or sand substrates, and should inspect foot feathering regularly for mud-packing and signs of infection [FWL-06]. Despite these challenges, the Asiatic breeds' cold hardiness and winter laying patterns make them excellent choices for the PNW climate --- their pea combs (Brahma) and dense plumage handle the region's damp 35-45 degree F winters far better than the large-combed Mediterranean breeds.

## Cold Hardiness: Built for Winter

The Asiatic breeds are, as a class, among the most cold-hardy chickens available. This hardiness derives from multiple factors:

- **Pea combs** (Brahma, Aseel, Sumatra) or small combs minimize frostbite-vulnerable tissue
- **Dense plumage** with thick down underlayers provides insulation
- **Large body mass** retains heat efficiently
- **Feathered feet** insulate extremities
- **Low metabolic rate** and calm temperament reduce energy expenditure in cold

Brahmas, in particular, are legendary winter layers. Their egg production cycle naturally peaks in the cooler months, making them invaluable for historical egg markets when winter eggs commanded premium prices. A flock of Brahmas and Cochins could keep a New England farm in eggs through January when lighter, more productive breeds had shut down entirely.

## Broodiness: The Living Incubator

Before mechanical incubators became reliable and affordable in the early twentieth century, broodiness was not a fault but a critical economic trait. A hen willing to sit on eggs for 21 days, turn them faithfully, maintain proper temperature and humidity, and then protect and teach the chicks was an invaluable piece of farm infrastructure.

The Asiatic breeds, particularly the Cochin and Brahma, were champion brooders. Cochin hens were kept on farms specifically as brooding machines, hatching eggs from Leghorns, turkeys, ducks, and game birds. Their large bodies could cover enormous clutches — 15 to 20 chicken eggs under a single Cochin hen was not unusual.

This brooding instinct persists today, and it is one of the Asiatic breeds' greatest gifts to the modern heritage poultry keeper. In an era when many production breeds have had broodiness selected out of them entirely, the Cochins and Brahmas still sit, still hatch, and still mother with the same devotion they showed a hundred and fifty years ago.

The Asiatic Class holds more concentrated history per breed than any other class in the APA Standard. From the ancient fighting traditions of India and Southeast Asia to the Victorian parlor mania that created organized poultry culture, these breeds connect us to deep currents of human-animal partnership. They are slow, they are big, they are broody, and they are magnificent. Every one of them is worth keeping.
