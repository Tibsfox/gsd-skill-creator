# The Mediterranean Class: Egg Machines of the Ancient World

The Mediterranean Class of the American Poultry Association gathers together the breeds that originated in the countries ringing the Mediterranean Sea — Italy, Spain, and the islands between them. These are the egg production aristocrats of the poultry world: lean, active, heat-tolerant birds that lay prolifically and eat efficiently. Where the Asiatic breeds are round, heavy, and broody, the Mediterranean breeds are angular, light-framed, and resolutely non-broody. Where the American breeds are dual-purpose generalists, the Mediterranean breeds are specialists — they lay eggs, and they lay them in extraordinary volume.

The Mediterranean Class also holds the breed that conquered the commercial egg industry so thoroughly that its name became virtually synonymous with "egg": the Leghorn. No single breed has had a greater impact on the global food supply. But the class contains other treasures too — ancient Spanish breeds with ghostly white faces, speckled Italian fowl that predate the Roman Empire, and a Sicilian breed with a comb found nowhere else in the chicken world.

These breeds share common traits: white or very lightly tinted eggs, white earlobes (a reliable visual indicator of white egg production), alert and sometimes flighty temperament, excellent heat tolerance, efficient feed conversion, minimal broodiness, and relatively light body weight. They are not cuddly. They are not calm. They are, however, among the most impressive egg-laying machines that centuries of selective breeding have produced.

## Leghorn

### Origin and History

The Leghorn takes its English name from the port city of Livorno (Leghorn in English) on the Tuscan coast of Italy, though the breed has roots across the Italian peninsula. Italian fowl were imported to America beginning in the 1820s, with significant importations in the 1850s and beyond. The early imports were primarily brown and white birds; subsequent breeding in America and England refined the breed into the dozens of varieties recognized today.

The White Leghorn's trajectory from Italian farmyard bird to global industrial powerhouse is one of the most consequential stories in agricultural history. By the early twentieth century, American and Danish breeders had refined the White Leghorn into an egg production machine of staggering efficiency. The breed laid more eggs on less feed than any competitor. When the commercial egg industry industrialized in the 1930s through 1960s, the White Leghorn became its almost universal foundation.

Today, virtually every white egg sold in a modern supermarket traces its lineage to White Leghorn genetics. The global laying hen population — approximately 7.5 billion birds — is overwhelmingly White Leghorn or White Leghorn hybrid. No other chicken breed approaches this level of global economic dominance.

The White Leghorn was admitted to the APA Standard of Perfection in 1874.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes an extensive list of Leghorn varieties. Major ones include: **Single Comb White** (1874), **Rose Comb White** (1874), **Single Comb Brown** (1874), **Rose Comb Brown** (1874), **Single Comb Buff** (1894), **Single Comb Black** (1874), **Single Comb Silver** (1894), **Single Comb Red** (1929), and **Single Comb Columbian** (1929). Additional rose comb varieties mirror several of these.

### Physical Description

The Leghorn is a trim, athletic bird with an alert, upright carriage. The body is long, moderately deep, and carried well forward, giving the bird an active, sprightly look. The breast is full and well-rounded. The back is long and flat, sloping slightly toward the tail, which is carried at a jaunty angle — large, full, and well-spread. Every line of the Leghorn suggests energy and efficiency.

The **Single Comb White** Leghorn — the most iconic variety — has pure white plumage, bright yellow legs and beak, and a large, floppy single comb that in hens often flops dramatically to one side. This flopping comb is not a fault in hens; it is characteristic of the breed. The comb, wattles, and earlobes are vivid red (comb and wattles) and white (earlobes). The white earlobe is a hallmark of the Mediterranean class and correlates with white egg production.

The **Brown** variety features a complex partridge-type pattern in females with rich golden-brown hackle and dark stippled body feathers, while males show the classic red-shouldered pattern with flowing golden hackle and saddle feathers.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 6 lbs, Hen 4.5 lbs, Cockerel 5 lbs, Pullet 4 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Leghorns are active, alert, and can be decidedly flighty. They are not lap chickens. They are excellent foragers, ranging widely and efficiently, and they are among the most feed-efficient of all breeds — converting a minimal amount of feed into a maximal number of eggs. They tolerate heat well but can be stressed by extreme cold, particularly their large single combs (the rose comb varieties handle cold better).

Egg production is the Leghorn's raison d'etre. Heritage strains lay approximately 250-300 large white eggs per year. Industrial strains have been bred to exceed 300 eggs per year, with some modern commercial hybrids approaching 330. The eggs are large to extra-large with white shells.

Leghorns are almost completely non-broody. This trait was deliberately selected because broodiness interrupts laying. A broody Leghorn is a genetic throwback.

### Conservation Status

The Livestock Conservancy lists heritage Leghorns as **Recovering**. This classification may seem paradoxical for a breed with billions of commercial descendants, but the distinction is important: the heritage Leghorn — the robust, colorful, genetically diverse bird that existed before industrial selection narrowed the gene pool to optimize for white eggs in battery cages — is a different animal from the commercial White Leghorn. Heritage strains of Brown, Buff, Black, Silver, and other varieties are genuinely uncommon.

## Minorca

### Origin and History

The Minorca is the largest of the Mediterranean breeds and takes its name from the island of Menorca in the Balearic Islands of Spain. The breed has been present in the Mediterranean region for centuries, likely descending from ancient Castilian fowl. It was imported to England in the early nineteenth century and refined by British breeders before arriving in America. The Black Minorca (single comb) was admitted to the APA in 1888.

### Varieties

The APA recognizes: **Single Comb Black** (1888), **Rose Comb Black** (1898), **Single Comb White** (1888), and **Single Comb Buff** (1907).

### Physical Description

The Minorca is a tall, angular, upstanding bird. The body is long and deep with a flat, sloping back. The most striking features are the **enormous white earlobes** — the largest of any breed, often measuring 3 inches or more in length, smooth-surfaced and almond-shaped — and the **very large single comb**, which in mature males can reach 6 or more points and stand impressively upright. The face is smooth, red, and unfeathered. The Black variety has glossy, greenish-black plumage. Legs are dark slate or black.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 9 lbs, Hen 7.5 lbs, Cockerel 7.5 lbs, Pullet 6.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Minorcas are active and alert, less flighty than Leghorns but still not especially tame. They are heat-tolerant and excellent foragers. Hens lay approximately 200-260 very large white eggs per year — the Minorca egg is among the largest of any standard breed. Like most Mediterranean breeds, Minorcas rarely go broody.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Minorca was once popular but has been overshadowed by the Leghorn. Quality exhibition stock is increasingly difficult to find. The Rose Comb varieties and the Buff variety are especially rare.

## Andalusian (Blue)

### Origin and History

The Blue Andalusian takes its name from the Andalusia region of southern Spain. The breed was developed from the native fowl of the region and imported to England in the mid-nineteenth century. It was admitted to the APA in 1874.

The Blue Andalusian illustrates one of the most fascinating principles in poultry genetics: the **blue dilution factor**. Blue plumage in chickens does not breed true. When two blue birds are mated, the offspring segregate in a classic Mendelian ratio: approximately 50% blue, 25% black (called "splash-less black" or simply black), and 25% splash (white or very pale with irregular dark splashes). Only the blue individuals meet the standard for exhibition. This means breeders of Blue Andalusians must perpetually cull or rehome half their hatch — a reality that has always limited the breed's popularity.

### Physical Description

The Blue Andalusian is a medium-large, active bird with a graceful, upright carriage. The ideal plumage is a clear, medium slate-blue, with each feather distinctly laced in a darker shade of blue-black, creating an intricate laced pattern across the entire body. The lacing should be even and well-defined. The single comb is large and erect in males, often flopping in females. Earlobes are white. Legs are dark slate to blue.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 7 lbs, Hen 5.5 lbs, Cockerel 6 lbs, Pullet 4.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Andalusians are active, alert, and good foragers. They can be flighty and are not a docile breed. Hens lay approximately 150-200 large white eggs per year. They rarely go broody.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The genetic challenge of producing show-quality blue birds from every mating makes this breed inherently difficult to maintain, contributing to its rarity.

## Ancona

### Origin and History

The Ancona takes its name from the Italian port city of Ancona on the Adriatic coast. The breed was imported to England in the mid-nineteenth century and to America somewhat later. The Single Comb variety was admitted to the APA in 1898, with the Rose Comb following in 1914.

### Physical Description

Anconas are medium-sized, active birds with a distinctive mottled plumage pattern: black feathers tipped with white (V-shaped white tips), creating a speckled effect across the entire body. The mottling becomes more extensive with each molt, so older birds appear progressively lighter. This is a desirable feature, not a fault. The body type is similar to the Leghorn but slightly heavier. Both single comb and rose comb varieties are recognized. Earlobes are white. Legs are yellow mottled with black.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 6 lbs, Hen 4.5 lbs, Cockerel 5 lbs, Pullet 4 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Anconas are alert, active, and excellent foragers — among the best in the Mediterranean class. They are good fliers and will range widely if not confined. Hens lay approximately 200-280 large white eggs per year, placing them among the most productive of the heritage Mediterranean breeds. They are non-broody and cold-hardy for their class.

### Conservation Status

**Watch** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. Anconas are uncommon but have a loyal following among heritage poultry enthusiasts who appreciate their beauty, productivity, and vigorous foraging ability.

## Sicilian Buttercup

### Origin and History

The Sicilian Buttercup is one of the most distinctive breeds in all of poultry, owing to its unique **buttercup comb** — a cup-shaped comb consisting of a complete crown of evenly spaced points rising from a central cup. This comb is found in no other APA-recognized breed and is the Sicilian Buttercup's most celebrated feature. The breed originates from Sicily, where it has been present for centuries. It was imported to America in the mid-1800s and admitted to the APA in 1918.

### Physical Description

Sicilian Buttercups are small to medium-sized birds with an alert, active carriage. The plumage is rich golden-buff with parallel rows of black spangles running across each feather, creating a precise, regular pattern of golden and black across the body. Males show a reddish-gold hackle and saddle with a beetle-green black breast and tail. The buttercup comb sits atop the head like a small crown, with a cup or bowl shape formed by two parallel rows of points that join at the front and back. Earlobes are white or creamy. Legs are willow or green.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 6.5 lbs, Hen 5 lbs, Cockerel 5.5 lbs, Pullet 4.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Sicilian Buttercups are active, flighty, and not easily tamed. They are excellent foragers and prefer free-range conditions. Hens lay approximately 140-180 small to medium white eggs per year. They are non-broody.

### Conservation Status

**Threatened** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The Sicilian Buttercup is rare in America, and maintaining the complex buttercup comb genetics requires dedicated breeding. The breed is a genuine treasure of Mediterranean poultry heritage.

## Spanish (White-Faced Black Spanish)

### Origin and History

The White-Faced Black Spanish is one of the oldest breeds in the Mediterranean class and one of the most extraordinary-looking chickens in existence. Its origins trace to the Castilian fowl of Spain, which were among the first chickens brought to the Americas by Spanish colonists. The breed was refined in England and Holland before arriving in America. It was admitted to the APA in 1874.

The breed's most remarkable feature — and its greatest breeding challenge — is the enormous **white face**. The entire face, from comb to throat, is covered in smooth, chalky white skin that extends well beyond the earlobes. In top exhibition specimens, the white face is smooth, free of wrinkles, and extends well back on the head and down the throat. Developing this extreme facial trait requires years of careful selection and management.

### Physical Description

The White-Faced Black Spanish is a medium-sized, upright bird with glossy greenish-black plumage and the dramatic white face described above. The single comb is large and erect. Legs are dark slate. The contrast between the jet-black plumage and the stark white face is startling and beautiful.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8 lbs, Hen 6.5 lbs, Cockerel 6.5 lbs, Pullet 5.5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Spanish are active and can be nervous. The enormous white face is somewhat delicate and vulnerable to frostbite and sun damage, making this a breed that requires more management than typical Mediterranean fowl. Hens lay approximately 150-200 large white eggs per year. They are non-broody.

### Conservation Status

**Critical** on the Livestock Conservancy priority list. The White-Faced Black Spanish is one of the rarest breeds in America. The difficulty of breeding the correct face type, combined with the face's vulnerability to weather damage, has limited the breed's appeal. It is a breed that survives primarily through the devotion of a small number of dedicated exhibition breeders.

## Catalana (Buff Catalana)

### Origin and History

The Catalana, sometimes called the Buff Catalana, originates from the Catalonia region of northeastern Spain, centered around Barcelona. It is one of the few breeds developed in Spain that has been exported widely. The breed was admitted to the APA in 1949.

### Physical Description

Catalans are medium-large birds with a distinctive appearance: the body plumage is a rich buff color, while the tail and some wing feathers are greenish-black, creating a handsome contrast. The single comb is medium to large, and the earlobes show a distinctive mix of white and red — unusual for a Mediterranean breed, which typically has pure white earlobes. Legs are slate-blue to greenish.

**Weight Standards:** Cock 8 lbs, Hen 6 lbs, Cockerel 7 lbs, Pullet 5 lbs.

### Temperament and Production

Catalans are active, good foragers, and tolerate heat exceptionally well — a reflection of their origin in the warm Mediterranean climate of Catalonia. Hens lay approximately 200-255 medium to large white or cream-tinted eggs per year. They are generally non-broody but occasional individuals will sit.

### Conservation Status

The Catalana is rare in America. It does not appear on the Livestock Conservancy priority list, likely because it is not an American breed, but its scarcity in the United States is genuine. Finding breeding stock requires connecting with specialty importers or heritage breed networks.

## The Leghorn's Dominance: How One Breed Fed the World

The story of the White Leghorn's rise to commercial dominance deserves closer examination, because it illuminates how industrial agriculture transforms breeds — and what is lost in the process.

### The Selection Pipeline

Beginning in the early 1900s, American agricultural colleges and commercial breeders began systematic selection programs focused on a single metric: eggs per hen per year. The White Leghorn, already a strong layer, responded to this selection pressure with remarkable gains. By the 1920s, trapnested White Leghorn hens were routinely exceeding 250 eggs per year. By the 1950s, commercial strains approached 300.

This selection was accompanied by a narrowing of the genetic base. Commercial breeding operations focused on a handful of elite lines, and by the mid-twentieth century, the global commercial White Leghorn population descended from a relatively small number of foundation birds. The genetic diversity that had characterized Italian landrace fowl — the variety of colors, body types, foraging behaviors, and disease resistances that developed over centuries of natural and farmer-directed selection — was deliberately discarded in favor of uniformity and production.

### What Was Lost

Heritage Leghorns — the Brown, Buff, Black, Silver, and Rose Comb varieties, along with non-industrial White strains — carry genetic material that the commercial population has lost. Disease resistance genes, heat and cold tolerance adaptations, foraging efficiency, longevity, and the ability to reproduce naturally (commercial Leghorns are typically artificially inseminated) all exist in heritage strains that are now uncommon.

The conservation of heritage Leghorn strains is therefore not merely a sentimental project. It is a genetic insurance policy. When — not if — the narrow genetic base of commercial poultry becomes vulnerable to a novel disease or changing environmental conditions, the heritage strains will carry the genetic raw material needed for adaptation.

## Heat Tolerance: The Mediterranean Advantage

Mediterranean breeds evolved in warm climates and carry adaptations that make them well-suited to heat:

- **Large combs and wattles** serve as radiators, dissipating body heat through blood flow to the highly vascularized comb tissue
- **Lean body mass** generates less metabolic heat than heavy, heavily muscled breeds
- **Tight, close-fitting plumage** allows better airflow to the skin than the loose, fluffy plumage of Asiatic breeds
- **Active foraging behavior** means birds distribute themselves across available shade rather than huddling
- **White or light plumage** (in many varieties) reflects solar radiation

These adaptations make Mediterranean breeds excellent choices for southern climates, tropical regions, and any situation where summer heat is a significant management challenge. Conversely, their large combs make them vulnerable to frostbite in northern winters, and their lean frames and tight feathering provide less insulation than the heavy, fluffy plumage of breeds like the Cochin or Brahma.

The Mediterranean breeds are a study in specialization: what happens when centuries of breeding focus on a single goal — prolific egg production — in a warm, dry climate. The result is a class of lean, efficient, high-strung birds that lay with an intensity unmatched by any other group. They are not for everyone. They require secure fencing (they fly well), patient handling (they are not cuddly), and respect for their independent nature. But for the keeper who wants eggs — mountains of beautiful white eggs — the Mediterranean class delivers with a consistency that borders on the mechanical. Except these machines have feathers, personalities, and a lineage that stretches back to the ancient Mediterranean, where chickens scratched in the same soil that nourished the first civilizations.
