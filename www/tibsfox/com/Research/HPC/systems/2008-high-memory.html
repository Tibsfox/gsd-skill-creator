In Their Own Words

"just because.." — LJ #47232, March 19, 2008

Two words that say everything. This is the same Tyan Thunder board as the $8,761 build, but with top-bin AMD 8360 CPUs at $4,509 each (versus $784 for the 8346) and 128GB of RAM (versus 32GB). Same core count. Same architecture. Five times the price.

The CPU alone accounts for $36,072 of the $43,361 total — 83%. A 39% clock speed improvement costs 475% more. This is the classic top-bin tax: the last 10-20% of performance costs multiples of the base price. The build exists in the archive to document that boundary.

Computing Context: March 2008

The AMD Opteron 8360 was the top-bin Barcelona quad-core at 2.5GHz. The $4,509 price point reflected enterprise demand and limited supply of the highest-frequency parts. AMD's pricing strategy at the time was aggressive at the low end (8346 at $784) but placed a steep premium on top bins, creating exactly the kind of price/performance curve this archive documents.

Where This Fits

The High Memory server is the upper bound of the March 2008 sprint. It proves that the same commodity architecture can scale in price by 5x without changing the core count — the premium is for clock speed and memory density, not parallelism. Paired with the $8,761 build on the same board, these two systems define the elasticity of the white-box approach.
