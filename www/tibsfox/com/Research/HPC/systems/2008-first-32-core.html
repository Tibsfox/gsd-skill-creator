This build is the emotional centerpiece of the 2008 sprint. The 11-year wait (1997 to 2008) from wanting 32 cores to being able to build them from commodity parts captures the arc of the entire archive: the gap between the mathematics you want to compute and the silicon you can afford.

Computing Context: March 2008

The Tyan Thunder n4250QE (S4985) was an 8-socket AMD Opteron board using a mezzanine card (M4985) to bridge two 4-socket complexes. Each AMD 8346 was a Barcelona-class quad-core at 1.8GHz. Eight sockets times four cores equals 32 — a number that in 1997 would have required a multi-rack SGI Origin or IBM SP2. In 2008, it fit in a single chassis from off-the-shelf parts.

The 11-Year Wait

In 1997, 32-processor systems existed but were proprietary: SGI Origin 2000, Sun Enterprise 10000, IBM RS/6000 SP. They cost six to seven figures and required vendor support contracts. The AMD Opteron's HyperTransport architecture — direct-connect, non-blocking, cache-coherent NUMA — was what finally brought this capability to commodity hardware.

At $274/core, the 32-core build actually has a lower cost per core than the 16-core mid-range ($291/core). Scaling up was cheaper per unit of compute than the smaller build. This is the HPC inflection point: commodity multi-socket had arrived.

Where This Fits

This is the pivot point of the archive. Everything before it is reaching toward this moment; everything after builds on the foundation it proved. A 32-core system from off-the-shelf components for under $9,000 validated the white-box approach to HPC and set the methodology for the next 18 years of designs.
