The Metric Evolution

The archive tracks a clear metric evolution across its 20-year span:

2006–2009

$/Core — How much does one CPU core cost? The fractal cluster through the Budget King optimized for this metric.

2009–2018

$/VM — How much does one virtual machine cost? The VM Economics build shifted from raw hardware to economic units of deployment.

2018–2026

$/TFLOP — How much does one teraflop cost? GPU compute makes floating point throughput the atomic unit.

Computing Context: January 2026

The AMD Threadripper PRO 9995WX represents the current pinnacle of consumer/prosumer multi-core: 96 cores in a single socket, surpassing the 32 cores that needed 8 sockets in 2008. The RTX PRO 6000 (Ada Lovelace, TSMC 4N) delivers 91.1 TFLOPS per card. NVIDIA's Blackwell architecture (GB200) dominates the datacenter AI market but at price points starting around $30K per GPU. The white-box workstation remains viable as a personal/small-team compute resource, even as the hyperscale economics of datacenter AI pull the $/TFLOP curve down from above.

Where This Fits

The AI Workstation is the current endpoint of the archive — 20 years after the Fractal Cluster. The compute has changed by six orders of magnitude (6 threads to 637.7 TFLOPS). The workload has changed from fractal rendering to AI inference and training. But the methodology is unchanged: identify the workload, price the parts, calculate the metric, build it from commodity components.
