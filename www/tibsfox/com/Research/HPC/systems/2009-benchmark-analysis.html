Methodology

"only looking at floating point performance as it is what I am most concerned with" — LJ #67441, January 16, 2009
"These numbers really don't mean that much but is meant as a guideline to determine a starting point when building a system" — LJ #67441

The metric is Whetstone per dollar (W/T$) — floating point benchmark throughput divided by CPU retail price. This is a pure $/compute metric with no weighting for power consumption, socket compatibility, or memory bandwidth. It answers one question: for each dollar spent on a CPU, how much floating point performance do you get?

Key Findings

Winner: AMD X4 9600

183.86 W/T$ at $161.98. This CPU was the Phenom 9600 — AMD's first native quad-core desktop part. It went directly into the Budget King build ($291) four months later.

Worst: Intel i7 965

57.27 W/T$ at $1,223.99. Intel's top-of-line Core i7 Extreme delivered excellent absolute performance but terrible value. The same CPU appeared in the $3,630 3-Way SLI build.

AMD vs Intel: The 2009 Landscape

AMD dominated the top of the W/T$ rankings with 9 of the top 10 positions. Intel's advantage was in absolute performance (the i7 965's raw Whetstone score of 70,098 was more than double any AMD part), but at 3.2x the price. The analysis quantifies what enthusiasts already knew: AMD was the value play, Intel was the performance play. For embarrassingly parallel workloads like fractal rendering, value per core matters more than peak per-thread speed.

Where This Fits

This is the only non-system entry in the archive — pure analysis rather than a build spec. It formalizes the intuition behind all the 2008 builds into a quantitative framework. The W/T$ metric directly influenced the Budget King build four months later, and the approach of tracking $/compute persists through the VM Economics (2018) and AI Workstation (2026) entries.
