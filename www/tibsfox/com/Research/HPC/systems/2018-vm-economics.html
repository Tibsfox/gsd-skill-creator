VM Economics Analysis

The distinguishing feature of this build is not the hardware but the economic analysis of virtualization density. The system can be sliced two ways:

VM StrategyVMsPer-VM Allocation$/VM
Core-Based2241 core + 53GB RAM$2,776
Thread-Based4481 thread + 26.5GB RAM$1,388

At $1,388 per VM with 26.5GB RAM each, this approaches cloud pricing economics but as a one-time capital expense rather than recurring operational cost. The RAM density (12TB) is the enabling factor — it's what makes 448 VMs practical rather than theoretical.

Moore's Law Analysis

"Intel 4004 (2,250 transistors, 10µm) to modern (100B/mm²) = 533,333x in 47 years" — LJ #94260, April 2, 2018

The post traced the transistor density arc from the Intel 4004 (1971, 2,250 transistors, 10 micrometer process) to contemporary chips at approximately 100 billion transistors per mm². A 533,333x improvement over 47 years.

The SimCity Analogy

The post compared 3D chip stacking to the leap from SimCity to Cities:Skylines — the transition from 2D planar layout to 3D volumetric design. When you can no longer shrink transistors, you start stacking them. This is the architectural shift that Intel (Foveros), TSMC (SoIC), and AMD (3D V-Cache) would pursue in the years following this post.

Quantum Computing Note

"50 qubits could outperform even the world's most powerful supercomputers" — LJ #94260, April 2, 2018

The post included the first mention of quantum computing in the archive. In April 2018, IBM had 50-qubit systems in testing and Google was building toward quantum supremacy (achieved October 2019 with Sycamore at 53 qubits). The observation about 50 qubits was prescient: this was the exact threshold Google targeted.

Cost Distribution

RAM Dominates

$440,000 — 71% of the total cost is RAM. 96 DIMMs at 128GB each. This is the Storage Monster pattern revisited: when you push one axis to the extreme, it dominates the budget.

CPU + GPU Parity

CPUs: $80,000. GPUs: $80,000. For the first time in the archive, CPU and GPU investment are exactly equal. The Tesla V100 era normalized GPU as a co-equal compute resource.

Computing Context: April 2018

The Intel Xeon Platinum 8180 (Skylake-SP) was Intel's top-bin server chip. The Tesla V100 (Volta, 12nm) was NVIDIA's datacenter AI accelerator, the first with dedicated Tensor Cores for mixed-precision matrix math. The Supermicro 7089P-TR4T was an 8-socket server — one of the last platforms supporting 8-way Intel configurations before the market shifted toward fewer-socket, higher-core-count designs.

Where This Fits

The VM Economics build bridges the GPU compute era (2011) and the AI era (2026). It introduces three new elements to the archive: VM density analysis, Moore's Law historiography, and quantum computing awareness. The shift from $/core to $/VM as the economic unit of analysis reflects the industry's transition from bare-metal to virtualized infrastructure. At 15.5 TFLOPS double-precision, it would have been unremarkable as a supercomputer — but as a single 7U server, it represents extraordinary density.
