In Their Own Words

"I won't actually purchase this... I just play Starcraft" — LJ #76703, May 29, 2009, comment from the author

Published the same day as the $291 Budget King, this build exists in the archive as a design exercise. The three eVGA GTX 285 SSC cards in 3-Way SLI represent the first time GPU investment exceeds CPU investment in a build spec: $1,167 in GPUs versus $985 in CPU.

Key Metrics

GPU Investment

$1,167 — 37% of build cost in GPU. First time GPU exceeds CPU in the archive.

Power Draw

2x 1000W PSU — 2kW total capacity. 3-Way SLI demanded serious power delivery.

Same-Day Contrast

Budget King: $291. 3-Way SLI: $3,630. 12.5x price ratio for different use cases.

Computing Context: May 2009

The GTX 285 was NVIDIA's top single-GPU card, based on the GT200b (55nm) architecture. 3-Way SLI required an NVIDIA nForce 200 bridge chip — the ASUS Rampage II Extreme was one of few boards that supported it. CUDA 2.0 had launched, and GPU compute was transitioning from novelty to serious tool. Tesla dedicated compute cards existed but at much higher prices. The GTX 285 at $389 was the enthusiast's path to GPU compute.

The Starcraft Footnote

The self-deprecating comment — "I just play Starcraft" — is characteristically honest. This build isn't aspirational in the way the $10.5M Render Farm would be two years later. It's a design exercise: what does a maximum-GPU consumer system look like in May 2009? The answer: $3,630, 3 GPUs, and dual kilowatt power supplies. The Starcraft reference dates it precisely — StarCraft II wouldn't ship until July 2010.

Where This Fits

The 3-Way SLI build marks the transition point in the archive from CPU-centric to GPU-aware thinking. It's the first build where GPUs are a primary design element rather than absent or secondary. The architecture — strong single-socket CPU plus multiple GPUs — is the template that would evolve through the VM Economics build (8x Tesla V100) and the AI Workstation (7x RTX PRO 6000).
