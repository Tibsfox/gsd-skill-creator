Notable Components

Violin 3200 (~$200,000)

Violin Memory was a startup (founded 2005, IPO 2013, bankrupt 2017) that built all-flash storage arrays using custom NAND flash modules. The 3200 was their mid-range enterprise product. In 2011, 10TB of all-flash storage at $200K was aggressive pricing — equivalent enterprise SAN solutions cost 3-5x more. The Violin approach foreshadowed the commodity flash revolution by several years.

ViewSonic VP 2290b ($3,998)

A 22.2" IPS panel at 3840x2400 resolution (WQUXGA). In 2011, this was an exotic display — 4K consumer monitors wouldn't arrive until 2013-2014. The 204 PPI density was intended for medical imaging and print production, perfect for verifying the fractal composites at native resolution.

Scalability Note

The post noted the chassis was expandable to 64 cores with future 16-core Opterons. AMD would ship the Opteron 6272 (16-core Bulldozer, Interlagos) in November 2011 — 8 months after this spec.

Computing Context: March 2011

The AMD Opteron 6176 (Magny-Cours) was AMD's first 12-core server CPU. The Quadro Plex was NVIDIA's external GPU compute chassis, predating the Tesla dedicated compute line becoming mainstream. 10GbE was still enterprise-only. This build is transitional — server-class compute meets enterprise storage, with GPU acceleration as an add-on rather than the primary compute engine.

Where This Fits

The Storage Monster is the only build in the archive where storage dominates the budget. At $5,087/core, it has the highest per-core cost of any non-render-farm system. But the $/core metric is misleading here — the system exists to pair high-bandwidth storage with sufficient compute, not to maximize cores. It proves that the white-box approach extends to enterprise-class storage systems, and that cost decomposition reveals what a system is actually designed to do.
