In Their Own Words

"On the other end of the spectrum, good bang for the buck" — LJ #76970, May 29, 2009

Published on the same day as the $3,630 3-Way SLI build, the Budget King is its polar opposite. Where the SLI build spent $985 on a single CPU, the Budget King's entire system costs $291. The Phenom 9600 — the same CPU family that won the January 2009 benchmark analysis at 183.86 W/T$ — is the heart of this build.

From Benchmark to Build

The Budget King is the benchmark analysis made physical. The AMD X4 9600 (Phenom 9600) topped the W/T$ rankings in January 2009. Five months later, it became the centerpiece of the cheapest complete system in the archive.

This is a complete system — not just CPU/board/RAM like most of the 2008 builds, but case, power supply, and 500GB of storage. A render node ready to plug in. At this price, the original Fractal Cluster target of 10 nodes at $250 each becomes achievable: 10 Budget Kings would deliver 40 cores for $2,910, versus the original 6 threads across 4 nodes.

Computing Context: May 2009

The global financial crisis had driven component prices down across the board. The Phenom 9600, which launched at $251 in November 2007, was now $89.99 — a 64% price drop in 18 months. DDR2 memory had cratered. The entire system would have cost 2-3x more a year earlier. The Budget King exists because of both semiconductor economics and macroeconomic timing.

Where This Fits

At $73/core, the Budget King has the lowest cost per core in the entire 20-year archive. It proves the floor: below this point, you cannot assemble a working system from commodity parts. It also proves that the benchmark analysis works — the highest W/T$ CPU becomes the cheapest system.
