Mission Package

When Commodore shipped the Amiga in 1985, they offloaded the OS's heavy lifting onto three specialized chips rather than burdening the single 68000 CPU. Today, that same logic applies to a developer running Claude Code on consumer hardware. You have a GPU sitting in your workstation. It is not just a rendering card anymore — it is a dedicated inference engine waiting to be configured, trained, and wired into your workflow.

The Through-Line

The Amiga did not replace the 68000 with a faster chip. It gave the 68000 partners. Three specialized chips — Agnus, Denise, Paula — each handling what they did best, orchestrated through a shared bus. The Local Intelligence Stack follows the same principle: a local model for the deterministic work, the Anthropic API for judgment-heavy synthesis, and Claude Code as the shell that routes to the right engine. A model fine-tuned on your GSD schemas speaks your dialect. The partner speaks your language because you taught it.

“A local model sitting on a 24GB RTX 4090 is a context window that costs nothing. Route the deterministic work through hardware you own, and reserve the Opus-class reasoning for the work that deserves it.”
