## Module 1: Origins and Formation

### Content Summary

Module 1 traces Geggy Tah from the CoCu (Contemporary Culture) collective in 1987 Los Angeles through the duo's evolution and their signing to David Byrne's Luaka Bop label. Key narrative threads include:

- **The CoCu collective** -- Ten members, five non-musicians, audience as participant. An experimental workshop where the rules hadn't been written yet. The deliberate choice to include non-musicians as creative collaborators rather than passive observers.
- **The duo crystallization** -- How Tommy Jordan (bass, vocals, lyrics) and Greg Kurstin (keyboards, guitar) emerged from the collective as the core creative partnership. The naming story: Jordan's baby sisters couldn't pronounce "Greg" and called him "Geggy."
- **The jazz foundation** -- Kurstin's training under Jaki Byard at The New School in New York. Playing the Village Vanguard with Bobby Hutcherson. The paradox of rigorous jazz training creating the confidence to break every tradition simultaneously.
- **The demo tape journey** -- The recording that traveled from Pomona to Bono (who bought studio time) to David Byrne (who signed them). The moment Byrne heard something "truly like nothing else."
- **The signing paradox** -- An American rock band on a label built for international voices. What this says about Luaka Bop's curatorial philosophy and the limits of genre taxonomy.

### Key Pull Quotes (Phase 1 Sourced)

> "They incorporate so many disparate elements into their sound that one senses a new sensibility afoot, an inclusionary wave... like nothing I've heard before." -- David Byrne

> "We weren't trying to be a band. We were just trying to make noise together." -- Tommy Jordan, on CoCu

## Module 2: Discography

### Content Summary

Module 2 provides track-level analysis of all three Geggy Tah studio albums, with production methodology, critical reception, and personnel documentation for each.

- **Grand Opening (1994)** -- Debut album produced by Susan Rogers and the band. Recorded at home in Pomona on a Stephens 16-track tape machine. Rolling Stone described it as creating "its own aural planet." The home recording philosophy as creative constraint.
- **Sacred Cow (1996)** -- The album containing "Whoever You Are," which reached #16 on Billboard Modern Rock. The story of how an 8-year-old's traffic courtesy song became a chart hit and later a Mercedes-Benz commercial soundtrack. Entertainment Weekly praised its "silliness tempered by keen musicianship."
- **Into the Oh (2001)** -- The expanded palette: theremin (Pamelia Stickney), Moog synths, clavinets, Wurlitzers. Guest appearances from Laurie Anderson and James Gadson. The album that pushed the inclusionary principle to its furthest point.
- **The Unconventional Toolkit** -- Dog claw scratches on hardwood floors, garage door squeals, submerged drums, found sounds integrated as legitimate sonic material. Jordan's articulation that "music is an expression of life -- beautiful, ugly, simple, complicated."

## Module 3: The Creative Network

### Content Summary

Module 3 profiles the individuals who passed through the Geggy Tah creative field and maps the transformative moments that changed their trajectories.

- **Susan Rogers** -- Prince's recording engineer (1983-1988) who co-produced Grand Opening and Sacred Cow. The specific moment of transformation: "Tommy taught me that music is an expression of life." Rogers went on to produce Barenaked Ladies, earn a PhD from McGill, and establish the Music Cognition and Perception Lab at Berklee.
- **David Byrne** -- Talking Heads founder who created Luaka Bop as a curatorial project. His decision to sign Geggy Tah -- an American band on a world music label -- defined the label's philosophy: "strange, but musical."
- **Pamelia Stickney** -- Jazz upright bassist who was handed a theremin during the Into the Oh sessions and became its greatest modern practitioner. Bob Moog called her a "true modern-day maestro." She developed the walking bass technique on theremin, performed on SNL, delivered a TED talk, and relocated to Vienna's experimental music scene.
- **James Gadson** -- Session drummer who had played with Aretha Franklin and Bill Withers before contributing to Into the Oh. His presence represents the bridge between classic soul/funk and Geggy Tah's experimental practice.
- **Laurie Anderson** -- Performance art pioneer who contributed to Into the Oh, describing her piece as a "postcard from a strange cloud."
- **Glen Phillips** -- Toad the Wet Sprocket vocalist who contributed to Sacred Cow.
- **Daren Hahn** -- Session drummer on Sacred Cow who later toured with Sting and Barenaked Ladies.
- **Lavey Millstein** -- LA session percussionist who added percussion, synth, and tabla to Into the Oh.
- **Jaki Byard** -- Mingus's pianist and Kurstin's teacher at The New School (pre-Geggy Tah). The lineage connection: Mingus to Byard to Kurstin to pop music.

## Module 4: Post-Geggy Tah Trajectories

### Content Summary

Module 4 traces where the creative DNA went after Geggy Tah became inactive around 2001.

- **Greg Kurstin's arc** -- The trajectory from indie band keyboardist to the most awarded pop producer of his generation. The decision point: jazz pianist or pop producer. How Geggy Tah was the bridge. "I learned how to produce and record music. Everything happened." Nine Grammy Awards. Adele's "Hello." Foo Fighters' "Run." Sia, Beck, Pink, Halsey. The specific production DNA: multi-instrumental fluency, genre-agnostic arrangement, the willingness to play everything yourself.
- **Tommy Jordan's path** -- Steel drums on Jack Johnson's "Flake." Ben Harper's endorsement. T Bone Burnett's praise. The less commercially visible but artistically consistent post-band trajectory. The question the module poses without answering: what does success look like when measured by creative integrity rather than commercial visibility?
- **Pamelia Stickney's trajectory** -- From a studio introduction to the theremin to Bob Moog's collaborator, SNL performer, TED speaker, and anchor of Vienna's experimental music scene. How a single moment in a recording session redirected an entire instrument's modern history.
- **Susan Rogers' academic turn** -- From Prince's studio to Geggy Tah's home studio to McGill's cognitive psychology program to Berklee's Music Perception and Cognition Lab. The arc from technical mastery to understanding *why* music affects humans the way it does.

## Module 5: Cultural Context

### Content Summary

Module 5 situates Geggy Tah within the broader cultural landscape of 1990s alternative music and the specific curatorial context of Luaka Bop Records.

- **Luaka Bop's founding** -- Named after a brand of Sri Lankan tea. Visual identity by Tibor Kalman (M&Co). The Masonic eye logo. The explicit rejection of "world music" as a marketing category. Byrne's position: the label surfaces music that is "strange, but musical" regardless of geographic origin.
- **The American Exception** -- Why Geggy Tah on Luaka Bop is the most interesting thing about their career from a music industry perspective. An American rock band on a label built to surface international voices. What Byrne's decision says about the limits and possibilities of curatorial identity.
- **The 1990s alternative landscape** -- The paradox of a genre-inclusive band in an era that organized music by rigid genre taxonomy. Discogs labels Geggy Tah "Pop Rock, Downtempo, Avantgarde." Apple Music says "funk, jazz, alternative, and experimental rock." These labels describe output, not practice.
- **Inclusionary aesthetics** -- Byrne's term "inclusionary wave" as a generalizable creative framework. The deliberate practice of absorbing disparate influences without hierarchy, producing work that transcends categorization not through rejection but through radical inclusion.
- **The "world music" paradox** -- How a label dedicated to challenging the "world music" category simultaneously used that category as its market identity. The tension between curatorial philosophy and commercial reality.

## Content Extraction Protocol

### Rules for Phase 2 Content

1. **No new primary research.** All content derives from Phase 1 modules.
2. **Every factual claim must trace to a specific Phase 1 source.** No interpolation.
3. **Quotes must be exact.** No paraphrasing presented as quotation.
4. **Dates must be verifiable.** If approximate, marked as such.
5. **Living persons must be characterized only through published interviews.** No speculation about motivations, relationships, or personal dynamics.
6. **No copyrighted material reproduced.** No lyrics, no audio, no extended quoted text beyond fair use.

### Extraction Workflow

```
Phase 1 Module → Content Index → Panel Assignment → Narrative Draft → Source Verification → Final Panel
```

Each panel in the educational module includes a `sources` array linking to specific Phase 1 bibliography entries. The verification pass (Wave 3) confirms every claim is traceable.
