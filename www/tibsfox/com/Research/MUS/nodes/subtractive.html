In Music

Subtractive synthesis is the foundation of analog synthesizer design, pioneered by Robert Moog in the 1960s and refined through instruments like the Minimoog, ARP 2600, Roland Juno-106, and Sequential Prophet-5. The principle is simple: start with something harmonically rich, then remove what you don't want.

A sawtooth wave contains all harmonics at amplitudes of 1/n (where n is the harmonic number). The fundamental is loudest, the second harmonic is half as loud, the third is a third as loud, and so on to infinity. This makes it the brightest, most harmonically complex basic waveform — the raw material of subtractive synthesis.

A low-pass filter (LPF) removes frequencies above a cutoff point. Turn the cutoff knob down and the sound gets darker, muffled, less "buzzy." Turn it up and the brightness returns. The resonance parameter boosts frequencies right at the cutoff point, creating a nasal, vocal quality — the "wah" of a filter sweep. This is why filter sweeps are so central to electronic music: the single act of moving the cutoff frequency creates enormous timbral variation from a static input.

The ADSR envelope shapes how the sound changes over time: Attack (how quickly it reaches full volume), Decay (how quickly it drops to the sustain level), Sustain (the steady-state level while the key is held), and Release (how long it takes to fade after the key is released). A fast attack + no sustain creates a pluck. Slow attack + long release creates a pad. The envelope is applied to both amplitude and filter cutoff, giving each note a timbral shape over time.

Nearly every synthesizer sound you have ever heard — from Kraftwerk to Depeche Mode to modern EDM — begins with this architecture. Even digital synths and software plugins emulate the saw-filter-envelope signal chain because it is the most efficient path from raw waveform to musical sound.

In the Forest Simulation

Agent perception follows the subtractive synthesis model precisely. The raw sensory input (sawInput) is everything the agent can detect — all signals in its perception radius, unfiltered and overwhelming. This is the saw wave: harmonically rich, containing everything.

The relevance filter (LPF) removes irrelevant signals. A predator's filter passes food-source signals and blocks non-food stimuli. A prey animal's filter passes threat signals. The cutoff frequency is species-specific: a generalist has a wide-open filter (hears everything), a specialist has a tight filter (only responds to specific cues). Resonance maps to sensitivity — a heightened response at the boundary of relevance.

The attention envelope (ADSR) shapes perception over time: Attack = how quickly the agent notices a stimulus. Decay = initial assessment (threat? food? irrelevant?). Sustain = ongoing tracking of confirmed signals. Release = how long the agent remembers after the signal disappears. A prey animal has fast attack (notice threats instantly) and long release (remember where the predator was). A predator has slower attack but higher sustain (patient tracking).

Rosetta Connections

Electronics

An RC low-pass filter is literally the same circuit in audio electronics and signal processing. Resistor + capacitor, cutoff = 1/(2πRC). The math that shapes synthesizer timbre is the same math that smooths noisy sensor data. The filter is universal.

Ecology

Sensory gating in prey species: the auditory cortex filters out background noise (wind, water, distant calls) to focus on predator-frequency sounds. This IS a biological low-pass filter with adaptive cutoff. Resonance = startle response at the threat frequency.

Infrastructure

Rate limiting is a low-pass filter on HTTP requests. The cutoff frequency is requests/second. Resonance is the burst allowance. The ADSR maps to: Attack (connection establishment), Decay (initial throttle), Sustain (steady-state throughput), Release (connection timeout).

The Math

VariableMeaning
sawInputRaw sensory data — all signals within perception radius. Analogous to a sawtooth wave containing all harmonics. Information-rich but noisy.
LPF(cutoff, resonance)Low-pass filter function. Cutoff determines which signals pass (below = relevant, above = noise). Resonance boosts sensitivity at the boundary. Species-specific parameters.
ADSR(t)Time-varying envelope: Attack (0→1, notice speed), Decay (1→S, initial assessment), Sustain (S, steady tracking), Release (S→0, memory fade). Each species has unique ADSR values.
perception(t)Final filtered, enveloped perception signal. This is what the agent actually "sees" — the relevant subset of reality, shaped by attention over time.

The multiplication chain is key: each stage reduces the signal. The saw wave contains everything; the filter removes the irrelevant; the envelope shapes what remains over time. The output is always less than or equal to the input. This is why it's called subtractive — you only ever remove. The art is in what you choose to keep.
