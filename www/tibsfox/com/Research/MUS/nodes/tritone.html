In Music

The tritone is the interval that splits the octave exactly in half — six semitones up from any note. In the key of C, the tritone of C is F#/Gb. Medieval theorists called it diabolus in musica (the devil in music) because of its dissonant, unstable sound. But jazz musicians discovered something remarkable about it: dominant seventh chords a tritone apart share the same tritone interval inside them.

G7 contains the notes G-B-D-F. The tritone interval within it is B to F (the 3rd and 7th). Db7 contains Db-F-Ab-Cb. Its internal tritone is F to Cb (which is the same as F to B, just spelled differently). Because the tension-creating notes are identical, you can swap one dominant chord for the other and the resolution to the tonic still works — often more beautifully, because the bass line now moves by half step instead of by fourth.

In a standard ii-V-I in C (Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7), the tritone substitution replaces G7 with Db7, creating Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7. The bass walks down chromatically: D → Db → C. This chromatic approach is one of the most sophisticated sounds in jazz harmony.

Quincy Jones used tritone substitutions extensively in his arrangements for Count Basie, Frank Sinatra, and on his own recordings. The technique gives arrangers a way to create surprise within a framework of harmonic logic — the substitution is unexpected but never wrong, because the underlying tension is preserved.

In the Forest Simulation

Tritone substitution maps to behavioral inversion: an agent occasionally swaps its seek and flee weights, pursuing what it normally avoids and avoiding what it normally seeks. This creates the simulation's equivalent of musical surprise — unpredictable individual behavior that still operates within the system's harmonic rules.

The inversion probability is very low (0.002 per frame, roughly once every 500 frames or about 8 seconds at 60fps). When triggered, the duration scales with the agent's energy level — a high-energy agent sustains the inverted behavior longer, just as a confident improviser can sustain a bold harmonic substitution for more bars.

This mechanism prevents the simulation from settling into predictable equilibria. A prey animal that briefly ignores a predator, or a predator that temporarily ignores prey, introduces the kind of stochastic noise that real ecosystems exhibit. It is not randomness — it is structured surprise.

Rosetta Connections

Ecology

Mobbing behavior in birds: prey species collectively approach and harass a predator instead of fleeing. The behavioral weights invert (flee → seek) but the underlying survival logic still holds — same tension, different root.

Infrastructure

A/B testing and canary deployments: occasionally route traffic to an unexpected path to test alternatives. The system briefly inverts its normal routing to gather information, then resolves back to the standard path.

Mathematics

The square root of 2 (the tritone frequency ratio 1.414...) is irrational — its decimal expansion never repeats. This is why the tritone divides the octave so perfectly and so disturbingly: it lands exactly between consonance and dissonance, between rational and irrational.

The Math

VariableMeaning
P(inversion)Probability per frame that the agent inverts its behavioral weights. At 0.002, this gives roughly one inversion event every 8.3 seconds at 60fps.
energyThe agent's current energy level (1-5 scale). Determines how long the inversion lasts once triggered.
60 framesBase duration multiplier. An E=1 agent inverts for 60 frames (1 second), an E=5 agent for 300 frames (5 seconds).
durationTotal frames the inversion persists: energy × 60. Higher energy = longer sustained inversion, like a more confident musician holding a substitution over more bars.

The frequency ratio of a tritone is exactly √2 ≈ 1.41421... In equal temperament, each semitone is a ratio of 2^(1/12), and six semitones is 2^(6/12) = 2^(1/2) = √2. This irrational number is the mathematical heart of the substitution: it is the one interval that maps to itself when you invert it (a tritone above a tritone brings you back to the octave).
