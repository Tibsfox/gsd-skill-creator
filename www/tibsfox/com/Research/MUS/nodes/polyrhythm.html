In Music

A polyrhythm is not two rhythms played at the same time. It is one rhythm with two clocks. When a drummer plays three beats in the left hand against two in the right, those are not independent patterns — they are two perspectives on a single composite rhythm that repeats every six beats (the LCM of 2 and 3).

The simplest polyrhythm, 3:2 (called hemiola), is the foundation of almost all groove-based music. It lives in the West African bell pattern (a 7-in-12 Euclidean rhythm that traveled the Middle Passage and became the clave), in the jazz ride cymbal, in the bossa nova, in funk, and in every beat that makes you move your body differently than you count.

More complex polyrhythms — 4:3, 5:4, 7:4 — create longer composite cycles. 4:3 repeats every 12 beats. 5:4 every 20. 7:4 every 28. As the numbers become coprime with larger LCMs, the pattern takes longer to repeat, creating the illusion of non-repetition within a deeply structured form.

Polyrhythmics, the Seattle-based band at S36 degree 21, builds their entire sound on layered polyrhythms. Seven musicians each maintaining independent rhythmic cycles that lock together at mathematically precise intervals. The result is music that feels simultaneously tight and free — because it is. The structure is rigorous; the feel is liquid.

The 50/60 Hz power grid is a planetary polyrhythm. North American electricity cycles at 60 Hz, European at 50 Hz. This difference shaped television frame rates (30fps vs 25fps), audio sampling, and display technology for eight decades. The polyrhythm is not only musical — it is infrastructural.

In the Forest Simulation

Each agent runs on multiple activity cycles simultaneously: solar (24 hours — day/night), tidal (12.4 hours — the lunar semi-diurnal tide), lunar (29.5 days — the synodic month), and seasonal (365 days — the solar year). Because these periods are incommensurate (their ratios are irrational), the composite pattern never exactly repeats.

Each species has its own phase offset for each cycle. A crepuscular animal peaks at dawn and dusk (solar phase shifted 90 degrees from a diurnal animal). A species tuned to spring tides peaks when lunar and tidal cycles align. The product of all sinusoidal cycles creates the agent's instantaneous activity level — an emergent behavioral fingerprint unique to each species-moment combination.

Electronics

Phase-locked loops (PLLs) synchronize oscillators at different frequencies. The beat frequency between two out-of-sync oscillators IS the polyrhythm. Clock dividers in modular synthesis create integer ratio polyrhythms directly.

Infrastructure

Cron jobs at different intervals create polyrhythmic system load: hourly backup + 15-minute health check + 5-minute log rotation. The composite load pattern repeats at the LCM of all intervals.

Ecology

Cicada broods emerge on prime-numbered year cycles (13 and 17 years) specifically to avoid synchronizing with predator cycles. The prime number IS the anti-polyrhythm strategy — maximizing the time between coincidences.

The Math

VariableMeaning
ΠProduct operator — all cycles multiply together rather than add. This means an agent is only active when ALL its cycles are positive, creating narrower activity windows than any single cycle alone.
TiPeriod of cycle i. Solar = 24h, tidal = 12.4h, lunar = 29.5d, seasonal = 365d. Each creates a different timescale of behavioral variation.
φiPhase offset for cycle i. Unique per species. Determines WHEN in each cycle the agent peaks — a dawn-active animal has different solar phase than a dusk-active one.
tCurrent simulation time. The single variable that all cycles share, creating the composite polyrhythmic pattern.

The composite cycle length (when the exact pattern repeats) is the LCM of all periods. For incommensurate periods like 24h and 12.4h, the LCM approaches infinity — the pattern effectively never repeats. This is the mathematical source of ecological complexity: simple sinusoidal components, multiplied together, produce behavior that appears chaotic but is fully deterministic.
