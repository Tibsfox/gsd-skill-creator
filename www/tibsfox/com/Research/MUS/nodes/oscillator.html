In Music

The sound of a single oscillator is thin. A pure sine wave is clinical. A lone sawtooth is buzzy but flat. The magic of synthesis begins when you add a second oscillator. Two saw waves at slightly different frequencies create beating — a rhythmic pulsation at the difference frequency (f1 - f2). Detune two 440 Hz oscillators by 1 Hz and you get a slow, warm undulation at 1 Hz. Detune by 5 Hz and the beating becomes a fast chorus shimmer.

This is the principle behind every lush synthesizer pad, every thick bass sound, every shimmering string section in electronic music. The Roland Juno-106 has a single oscillator but includes a chorus effect that creates a virtual second oscillator through short delay + modulation. The Prophet-5's "fat" sound comes from two oscillators per voice. The Moog's legendary bass is three oscillators detuned against each other.

Add a sub-oscillator one octave below and the sound gains weight and foundation without getting muddy. Add a high oscillator at a fifth above and the sound gains brightness and harmonic interest. Each oscillator contributes a different frequency range, and the composite timbre contains complexity that no single waveform possesses.

The critical technique is modulation — slowly changing the frequency of one oscillator relative to another. "Move it around," as the synthesist says. This creates evolving spectral content: the harmonic relationship between oscillators shifts over time, producing a sound that breathes and lives. A static chord is dead; a modulated oscillator pair is alive. The movement IS the music.

This is additive interference at its most musical: waves combining constructively at some moments (louder, brighter) and destructively at others (quieter, darker). The interference pattern is the timbre. The beat frequency is the rhythm of the timbre. Synthesis is rhythm at the microsecond scale.

In the Forest Simulation

Each agent runs multiple behavioral "oscillators" simultaneously, each at a different timescale. The fast cycle (period: frames/seconds) handles immediate reaction — dodge a predator, snap at prey, avoid a collision. The medium cycle (period: minutes to hours) governs behavioral state — foraging, resting, socializing, migrating. The slow cycle (period: days to months) tracks seasonal energy, reproductive readiness, and territorial range.

These cycles don't operate independently — they interfere. A fast-cycle flee response is amplified when the medium cycle is already in an alert state and the slow cycle is in breeding season (heightened vigilance). The same stimulus produces different behavior depending on the current phase of all three oscillators. This interference between timescales produces emergent behavior that no single cycle contains.

The beat frequency between medium and slow cycles creates behavioral patterns at timescales that neither cycle alone produces. A 6-hour behavioral state cycle beating against a 29.5-day lunar cycle creates subtle behavioral shifts at intervals of days — an emergent rhythm that looks like decision-making but is actually interference.

Rosetta Connections

PRS: Polyrhythm

Multiple oscillators at different frequencies ARE polyrhythm at the timbre scale. The LCM of oscillator periods defines when the composite waveform repeats. Two detuned saw waves are a 2:1.001 polyrhythm. The math is identical.

Ecology

Overlapping territorial signals: two birds singing at slightly different frequencies create an auditory beat pattern that helps each bird distinguish its own song from its neighbor's. The beat frequency between their songs IS a detuned oscillator pair. Bioacoustics is synthesis.

Physics

Fourier's theorem: any complex waveform can be decomposed into a sum of simple sinusoidal oscillators. Oscillator layering is the reverse — building complex behavior from simple periodic components. Both directions are mathematically equivalent.

The Math

VariableMeaning
ΣSummation — all oscillator outputs are added together. The composite behavior is the sum of all individual cycles. (Compare to polyrhythm's Π product — here we add, there we multiply.)
AiAmplitude of oscillator i. The fast cycle might have A=0.3 (reactive but not dominant), medium A=0.5 (primary behavioral driver), slow A=0.2 (subtle seasonal modulation).
wavei()Waveform function for oscillator i. Can be sin (smooth), saw (sharp transitions), square (binary on/off), or triangle (linear ramps). Different waveforms create different behavioral textures.
fiFrequency of oscillator i. Fast cycle: ~1 Hz (per-second reactions). Medium: ~0.001 Hz (hourly states). Slow: ~0.00001 Hz (monthly seasons).
φiPhase offset. Species-specific. Determines WHEN each oscillator peaks relative to the others. Phase relationships between oscillators create the emergent behavioral character of each species.

The key insight is that summation (Σ) creates fundamentally different composite behavior than multiplication (Π, used in polyrhythm). Addition means each oscillator contributes independently — one cycle can push the agent toward action even while others are at rest. Multiplication means all cycles must be positive for the agent to be active. The choice between Σ and Π is an architectural decision about how behavioral cycles interact.
