## 1. Introduction: Sovereignty at the Center of the Energy Transition

The global transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy requires a specific set of materials: lithium, cobalt, nickel, chromite, copper, platinum group elements, and rare earth minerals. These materials are distributed unevenly across the planet's crust. A disproportionate concentration of them lies beneath territories that are simultaneously among the world's most ecologically significant and among the territories of Indigenous peoples whose sovereignty over those lands was suppressed, ignored, or violently contested during the colonial era.

This is not coincidence. The territories that were deemed insufficiently productive for colonial agricultural exploitation — the boreal forests of the Canadian Subarctic, the volcanic highlands of Luzon, the deep-lying mineral belts of the Pacific Rim — were left to Indigenous peoples precisely because colonial economies could not yet extract their value. The technological development that now makes those territories profitable is the same technological development that created climate change and now demands the minerals to address it. The green economy is, in a material sense, being built on the same territories that the fossil fuel economy could not fully exploit.

For Indigenous nations across the Ring of Fire, this creates a set of related but distinct governance challenges. The first is the legal question: what rights do they hold over these resources, under what legal frameworks, and how enforceable are those rights? This is addressed in Module 05. The second is the governance question: given those rights, what models exist for nations to exercise sovereignty over resources on their territories — not merely to veto or permit projects proposed by outsiders, but to be full economic and governance actors in the resource development that affects their futures? The third is the ecological question: what are the cumulative consequences of large-scale mineral extraction for the ecosystems on which Indigenous food systems, cultural practices, and territorial sovereignty depend?

This module addresses all three questions through three primary case areas: the Canadian Ring of Fire in the James Bay Lowlands of Ontario; Pacific geothermal development in New Zealand, the Philippines, and Indonesia; and Pacific fisheries and food sovereignty in the Pacific Northwest. Across these cases, a consistent pattern emerges. The nations with the clearest legal rights, the strongest governance institutions, and the most developed models for equity co-investment are best positioned to shape development outcomes. The nations without those elements face extraction without participation — the pipeline drawn through other people's living rooms.

## 2. The Canadian Ring of Fire: James Bay Lowlands and the Chromite Deposits

**Geography and geology:**
The Canadian Ring of Fire is a mineral belt located in the James Bay Lowlands of northern Ontario, approximately 540 kilometers northeast of Thunder Bay. The name is borrowed from the Pacific tectonic ring; the Ontario geological formation is an ancient greenstone belt rather than a volcanic arc, and the name reflects the circular shape of the mineral deposit concentration rather than volcanic or seismic origin.

The deposit is economically significant by any measure. The Ring of Fire contains one of the largest chromite deposits in the western hemisphere. Chromite is the sole commercial source of chromium, which is essential for stainless steel, chrome alloys, and — increasingly — battery cathode materials for electric vehicle batteries. The deposit also includes cobalt, nickel, copper, platinum, and palladium. Initial estimates placed the value of recoverable minerals at tens of billions of dollars; subsequent assessments have been more conservative but still indicate a resource of national significance.

**The peatland ecosystem:**
The James Bay Lowlands are the second-largest peatland ecosystem in the world. The peatlands function as a significant carbon sink, sequestering carbon accumulated over thousands of years. They also support boreal caribou, a species of high cultural significance to many of the Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities whose territories encompass the Ring of Fire region. The caribou calving grounds overlap with the areas proposed for mine access corridors.

Road construction through peat — even roads not directly adjacent to mine sites — causes drainage of the peat, oxidation of the accumulated organic matter, and release of stored carbon. A 2022 analysis by Boreal Canada estimated that a year-round all-weather road through the James Bay Lowlands peatlands could release between 5 million and 33 million tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent over 100 years, depending on road design, drainage management, and drainage area. The same report noted that the methodology for estimating peatland carbon release under road construction is subject to substantial uncertainty.

The peatland ecosystem also provides the foundation for the freshwater fisheries that Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities depend on for food and cultural practice. The Attawapiskat, Albany, and Moose Rivers drain the Ring of Fire area into James Bay. Contamination, drainage alteration, or thermal destabilization of these watersheds would affect the food security of communities that have limited access to affordable market food and rely heavily on fish, caribou, waterfowl, and berries from the land.

**The mineral companies:**
The earliest significant staking in the Ring of Fire was by Noront Resources, which discovered the Eagle's Nest chromite deposit in 2007-2008. Subsequent staking by Cliffs Natural Resources, KWG Resources, and others established the extent of the chromite belt. Wyloo Consolidated Resources (a subsidiary of Tattarang, the private investment company of Australian billionaire Andrew Forrest) acquired Noront Resources in 2021 for approximately $617 million Canadian. As of 2025, Wyloo/Noront holds the principal mineral development rights in the Ring of Fire, though the project timeline for any development remains uncertain pending infrastructure resolution and regulatory processes.

## 3. Timeline: The Ontario Ring of Fire, 2008-2025

**2008-2012: Discovery and staking:**
The Noront discovery of Eagle's Nest in 2007-2008 triggered extensive staking activity across the James Bay Lowlands. By 2012, the Ring of Fire had been mapped as a mineral district of national significance. The Ontario government under Premier Dalton McGuinty (Liberal) began developing a policy framework for development.

**2014: Liberal framework with Matawa chiefs:**
The Ontario government entered into a framework agreement with nine Matawa First Nations — the group of communities closest to the proposed development area — establishing a process for negotiating how Ring of Fire development would proceed. The framework acknowledged that development required First Nations participation and consent, and established a working relationship between the province and Matawa leadership. Talks proceeded slowly, complicated by disagreements over infrastructure routing, benefit-sharing, environmental standards, and the question of consent versus consultation.

The framework stalled. Matawa communities expressed frustration with the pace and substance of negotiations. The province's priority was establishing a development pathway; the communities' priority was ensuring that development would not replicate the harmful outcomes of previous resource extraction in the north — the mercury contamination of Grassy Narrows, the displacement of communities during dam construction, the broken promises of the hydro era. These priorities were not aligned.

**2018-2019: Change of government, abandonment of framework:**
Premier Doug Ford's Progressive Conservative government took office in 2018. In 2019, the Ford government formally abandoned the Matawa framework agreement and announced a revised approach: a $1 billion provincial commitment to infrastructure development and a pledge to accelerate the regulatory pathway for Ring of Fire mining. The government framed the Ring of Fire as an economic development priority and a resource that Ontario could not afford to leave in the ground.

The abandonment of the framework agreement without the consent of the Matawa First Nations was widely criticized by Indigenous leadership as a unilateral breach of a negotiated process. The practical effect was to restart the relationship-building process from a lower point of trust.

**2020-2023: Dual-track development:**
Two First Nations took divergent approaches to engagement during this period. Marten Falls First Nation and Webequie First Nation, whose traditional territories are most directly affected by proposed mine access routes, engaged in a joint environmental assessment process for road construction with the Ontario government — the Northern Road Link (Webequie Supply Road) and the Marten Falls Community Access Road. These assessments proceeded under Ontario's Environmental Assessment Act. Simultaneously, Neskantaga First Nation and several other Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities refused to participate in provincial processes they considered inadequate and sought federal review of the project's cumulative impacts.

**January 2025: Federal Regional Assessment launched:**
The Impact Assessment Agency of Canada (IAAC), following federal referral, announced the launch of a Regional Assessment of the Ring of Fire in January 2025. The Regional Assessment Terms of Reference, developed with participation from 15 First Nations, established a 30-month timeline for a cumulative assessment of the Ring of Fire as a region — not just individual road projects. This represented a significant jurisdictional development: the federal government asserting a role in the Ring of Fire assessment that goes beyond the provincial processes already underway.

The 15-nation participation in developing the Terms of Reference was a meaningful governance development. It established that the federal process would be shaped by First Nations input from the outset, not simply consulted after terms were set by the agency. However, the Regional Assessment is advisory and non-binding on Ontario's provincial permitting authority. The federal assessment cannot prevent the province from issuing mining permits if provincial requirements are met.

**March 2025: Federal political pressure:**
Pierre Poilievre, leader of the Conservative Party of Canada, pledged during the 2025 federal election campaign to "greenlight" all Ring of Fire permits within six months of forming government. The pledge was framed as an economic efficiency measure and a response to what Poilievre characterized as excessive regulatory delay.

NAN Grand Chief Alvin Fiddler responded directly:

> "Our people will protect these lands, not just in the courts, but on the lands themselves."
>
> — Alvin Fiddler, Grand Chief, Nishnawbe Aski Nation, March 2025

The exchange encapsulated the structural conflict at the center of Ring of Fire governance: the federal government's desire to accelerate mineral extraction for economic and energy transition purposes, and the sovereign authority of the Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities whose territorial rights the accelerated permitting would necessarily bypass.

## 4. Key Actors: Nishnawbe Aski Nation, Neskantaga, and the Marten Falls Model

**Nishnawbe Aski Nation (NAN):**
NAN is the political organization representing 51 First Nations in northern Ontario, covering approximately two-thirds of Ontario's land area. NAN communities include the First Nations whose territories encompass the Ring of Fire deposit. NAN's political role in the Ring of Fire file is to advocate for the collective interests of member communities, to engage with both provincial and federal governments on process and substance, and to communicate the positions of member communities to the public.

NAN's position on the Ring of Fire has not been a categorical rejection of development. It has been a consistent insistence that development require genuine consent from the affected communities, not the procedurally adequate but substantively insufficient consultation the provincial process has typically provided. NAN has repeatedly called for a Nation-to-Nation process at the federal level, arguing that the provincial process cannot substitute for the federal Crown-to-Nation relationship recognized in section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982.

**Neskantaga First Nation:**
Neskantaga First Nation is a remote fly-in community of approximately 300 members located on Attawapiskat Lake, one of the most directly affected communities in the Ring of Fire region. Neskantaga has been among the most vocal opponents of the current development pathway. In October 2020, Chief Chris Moonias led a group of Neskantaga members in a blockade of a Marten Falls First Nation exploration camp, citing lack of consent from Neskantaga. The blockade halted exploration activities for an extended period and drew national and international attention.

Neskantaga's position rests on the legal framework of its unextinguished treaty rights and territorial sovereignty over its traditional territory, which overlaps with proposed infrastructure corridors. Chief Moonias has been consistent: Neskantaga will not permit development that has not obtained its free, prior and informed consent, and the provincial environmental assessment process does not and cannot constitute FPIC under the standards established by UNDRIP and the Canadian UNDRIP Act.

The Land Defence Alliance, formed by Neskantaga and allied communities, represents an organized expression of the territorial sovereignty position — not simply litigation and advocacy through official channels, but direct territorial presence as an assertion of rights. This approach is legally recognized by international human rights frameworks as a legitimate last resort where domestic remedies have been exhausted or are unavailable.

**Marten Falls First Nation and equity co-investment:**
Marten Falls First Nation has taken a substantially different approach to Ring of Fire development than Neskantaga. Marten Falls, whose traditional territory is also in the Ring of Fire area, has engaged in the provincial environmental assessment process for the Marten Falls Community Access Road and has negotiated benefit agreements that include equity participation components with development proponents.

The equity co-investment model pursued by Marten Falls represents one end of the spectrum of Indigenous engagement in resource development. Rather than seeking to block or slow development, Marten Falls has sought to reshape its terms so that the community becomes a co-owner, not merely a benefit-recipient or a consulted stakeholder. An equity stake creates structural accountability: the project developer cannot proceed without the equity partner's ongoing participation, and the equity partner has governance rights proportionate to ownership.

It would be incorrect to suggest that Marten Falls' approach represents a consensus among Ring of Fire nations, or that any equity arrangement has been finalized with specific terms on record. It is accurate to say that the Marten Falls approach demonstrates that the binary of "consent or block" is false: a third path exists in which nations exercise sovereignty by shaping the terms of development rather than simply permitting or refusing it. Whether that path is available or desirable depends on the specific circumstances of each nation, the quality of the agreements offered, the ecological risks involved, and the nation's own governance priorities.

**The divergence and what it means:**
Neskantaga and Marten Falls occupy opposite poles of a position spectrum that also includes many intermediate positions. This divergence is not a sign of Indigenous political incoherence; it is a sign that sovereignty, properly understood, means that each nation makes its own decisions. The Canadian government's challenge — and, under the UNDRIP Act, its obligation — is to engage with each nation on its own terms, not to find a single Indigenous position and negotiate with it.

The federal government's tendency to seek a single representative Indigenous interlocutor for the Ring of Fire — whether NAN collectively or the Matawa group or an individual nation — has repeatedly run into the reality that consent must be obtained from each nation whose rights are affected, not from a collective body that may not have the authority to consent on behalf of its members.

## 5. The Federal-Provincial Gap and the Consent Architecture

**The structural problem:**
As detailed in Module 05, the Canadian UNDRIP Act (2021) binds the federal Crown to an FPIC standard. Ontario has no equivalent legislation. Ontario's relationship with First Nations in the Ring of Fire is governed by the constitutional duty to consult established in *Haida Nation* (2004) and subsequent Supreme Court jurisprudence — a standard that is strong but falls short of requiring consent in all cases.

The significance of this gap for the Ring of Fire is precise. The Ring of Fire requires two distinct categories of approvals:

1. **Federal approvals:** Impact Assessment Agency review, Fisheries Act authorizations, navigable waters approvals, and any federal permits. These are subject to the UNDRIP Act's FPIC framework under the 2023 Action Plan.
2. **Provincial approvals:** Mining leases under the Ontario Mining Act, surface rights under provincial land use legislation, provincial environmental assessment approvals for roads and infrastructure. These are subject to the constitutional duty to consult, not the UNDRIP Act's FPIC standard.

A project can satisfy federal requirements — including the Regional Assessment process and federal FPIC obligations — and still proceed provincially under the weaker duty to consult standard. The federal Regional Assessment is explicitly non-binding on provincial permitting authority. This is the mechanism through which development can advance without consent: the province issues permits under its own authority, having met the constitutional consultation standard, without obtaining the free, prior and informed consent that the UNDRIP Act requires of the federal Crown.

This is not a theoretical vulnerability. It is the live architecture of the Ring of Fire file as of 2025. The Neskantaga position — that no development can proceed without consent — is legally correct as applied to federal Crown conduct under the UNDRIP Act. Whether it is legally correct as applied to provincial Crown conduct under the Mining Act is a more complex question whose answer depends on whether section 35 rights are sufficiently established in specific territories to require something approaching consent.

**The provincial constitutional question:**
The *Tsilhqot'in* decision (2014) established that where Aboriginal title is proven over a specific territory, the title-holding nation has a right to decide how that territory is used, and government override requires compelling justification. This creates, in effect, a consent requirement for title-holders. The practical challenge for Ring of Fire nations is that proving title under the *Tsilhqot'in* framework requires extended and expensive litigation, and most Ring of Fire nations have treaty rights rather than litigated title claims.

Treaty 9, which covers most of the Ring of Fire area, was entered into in 1905-1906 between the Crown and numerous First Nations across northern Ontario. The treaty's terms — as understood by the Crown — included an extinguishment of Aboriginal title in exchange for reserves and certain rights. The treaty's terms — as understood and communicated to First Nations signatories — included a sharing arrangement that preserved First Nations use of the land while allowing settlers and governments to use it as well. The gap between these understandings has never been judicially resolved in a definitive way for the Ring of Fire area.

A legal challenge asserting that Ring of Fire development requires consent on the basis of Treaty 9 rights and section 35 constitutional protections is a substantial litigation position. Its outcome would depend on judicial interpretation of Treaty 9, the extent to which the duty to consult has been satisfied in specific cases, and whether the Supreme Court of Canada's evolving jurisprudence on Indigenous rights continues to move toward the consent end of the spectrum.

## 6. Pacific Geothermal: 40 Percent of World Potential, Indigenous Territory Throughout

**The resource:**
The Ring of Fire is the world's most significant geothermal province. The volcanic arc that circles the Pacific Basin is heated by the same subduction and hotspot processes that produce earthquakes and eruptions; in many locations, that heat is accessible at economically viable depths and temperatures. The US Geological Survey and International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) estimate that the Ring of Fire nations together hold between 40 and 50 percent of the world's technically recoverable geothermal energy potential.

The largest national geothermal producers in the Ring of Fire are the United States (primarily Geysers geothermal complex in California, and Hawaiian geothermal), Indonesia, the Philippines, New Zealand, Japan, and Mexico. Several other Ring of Fire nations — including Papua New Guinea, Chile, Ecuador, and Peru — have substantial undeveloped geothermal potential.

**Why geothermal is a sovereignty issue:**
Unlike some energy resources that can be extracted at a remove from inhabited territory, geothermal development is inherently local. Geothermal reservoirs are located where the volcanic and hydrothermal systems are. In the Pacific Ring of Fire, those systems are concentrated in the volcanic belts that are also the locations of the highest density of Indigenous sacred sites, traditional territories, and culturally significant landscapes.

In Aotearoa New Zealand, the most productive geothermal area is the Waikato volcanic plateau, the heartland of multiple iwi whose connections to the thermal landscape are among the most culturally central in Māori tradition. In Indonesia, geothermal development in Sumatra, Java, and the eastern islands proceeds through territories with substantial Indigenous populations. In the Philippines, geothermal plants in the Visayas and Mindanao operate near territories claimed by Lumad and other Indigenous peoples. In the United States, Hawaii's geothermal resources sit on Pele's territory — a direct intersection between renewable energy development and the most spiritually significant volcanic landscape in Hawaiian tradition.

## 7. Māori Iwi and New Zealand Geothermal

**The Waikato volcanic plateau:**
New Zealand's geothermal fields are concentrated in the Taupo Volcanic Zone (TVZ), which runs approximately 350 kilometers from Tongariro in the south to White Island in the north. The TVZ is one of the most volcanically active regions on Earth and the most geothermally productive in the Southern Hemisphere. The major geothermal power stations — Wairakei (1958), Ohaaki (1989), Rotokawa, Ngatamariki, Te Huka, Te Mihi — are located in or adjacent to territories of Tūwharetoa, Ngāti Whaoa, Ngāti Raukawa, Ngāti Tahu-Ngāti Whaoa, and other iwi.

The cultural significance of the TVZ to Māori iwi is not peripheral. The thermal landscape — geothermal pools, geysers, mudpools, and fumaroles — is central to traditional settlement, food preparation, bathing, healing, and spiritual practice. Specific thermal features are named, have ancestral associations, and are treated as taonga (treasures) protected under Te Tiriti o Waitangi and UNDRIP Article 11. The drilling of geothermal wells, the drawing-down of hydrothermal reservoirs, and the discharge of geothermal brines into waterways all have potential consequences for these features.

**Land tenure and the Resource Management Act:**
Under New Zealand's Resource Management Act 1991 (RMA), geothermal water and heat are classified as a national resource owned by the Crown. Regional councils (in this case the Waikato Regional Council and Bay of Plenty Regional Council) are responsible for allocating resource consents for geothermal extraction. The RMA requires that consent decisions give effect to the provisions of Te Tiriti o Waitangi and consider kaitiakitanga — the Māori concept of stewardship and guardianship — and the relationship of Māori and their culture with their ancestral lands, water, sites, and taonga.

The RMA framework created a space for iwi participation in geothermal resource management, but did not create ownership rights or consent rights equivalent to UNDRIP Article 32. Iwi could participate in consent hearings, could appeal consent decisions, and could negotiate with consent applicants — but could not, under the RMA framework, simply refuse a geothermal development on their traditional territory.

**The evolution toward co-governance and equity:**
Beginning in the mid-2000s, as the Māori Treaty settlement process progressed and iwi with stronger legal and political positions became more assertive, geothermal developers began negotiating consent and equity arrangements directly with iwi. The landmark arrangement was the Mighty River Power (now Mercury NZ) partnership with Ngāti Tūwharetoa and other iwi for the development of the Ngatamariki geothermal field (commissioned 2013). The arrangement included a royalty structure, consultation rights, employment provisions, and — most significantly — provision for Māori equity participation in the geothermal infrastructure.

The Mercury NZ model is not a universal template. Each geothermal development involves different iwi with different territorial claims, different settlement histories, and different priorities. What the Mercury arrangement demonstrated is that geothermal development in Aotearoa is not viable without meaningful iwi engagement, and that the terms of that engagement have consistently moved from pure consultation toward co-governance and equity over time.

The Waitangi Tribunal's 2019 WAI 2523 report found that the Crown had not fully honored its Treaty obligations with respect to geothermal resources in the TVZ and recommended that the Crown work with iwi to develop co-governance arrangements that go beyond the existing RMA framework. As of 2025, the New Zealand government has not enacted the comprehensive statutory co-governance framework the Tribunal recommended, but the practical reality of geothermal development in the TVZ is one of significant iwi influence over project design, management, and benefit distribution.

**Māori energy companies:**
Several iwi have moved beyond consent and equity participation in external projects toward owning and operating geothermal infrastructure themselves. Ngāti Tūwharetoa Geothermal Assets (NTGA) is an iwi-owned company that holds geothermal assets in the TVZ. The Pureora Forest Park is co-managed by Māori and the Crown. These models represent a substantive exercise of tino-rangatiratanga — not just a share of someone else's project, but sovereign ownership and operation of resources on ancestral territory.

## 8. Philippines and Indonesia: Geothermal in Indigenous Uplands

**Philippines:**
The Philippines is the second-largest geothermal energy producer in the world, behind only the United States. Its geothermal capacity of approximately 1,900 megawatts is concentrated in three areas: the Leyte-Samar region in the Visayas; the Bicol region of Luzon; and the southern Mindanao region. The Bicol and Leyte geothermal fields were developed primarily in the 1970s-1990s under the Marcos administration and subsequent governments, with limited Indigenous rights consideration under the governance frameworks of the time.

The Philippines Geothermal Inc. (PGI) and the state-owned National Power Corporation (NAPOCOR) were the primary developers of the first geothermal wave. Community consultation was often limited or absent. In the Mt. Apo geothermal project in Mindanao — one of the most significant cases — the Bagobo, Ata-Manobo, Manobo Kulaman, and other Lumad peoples contested development on what they considered sacred ancestral territory. The controversy produced litigation, direct action, and a sustained national discussion about the intersection of energy development and Indigenous rights that ultimately contributed to the passage of IPRA in 1997.

Post-IPRA geothermal development requires NCIP FPIC certification for projects on ancestral domain. The process has been imperfect in implementation, as discussed in Module 05, but it represents a statutory FPIC requirement that did not exist before 1997. The Energy Regulatory Commission has incorporated NCIP FPIC compliance into its licensing requirements for geothermal projects on or adjacent to ancestral domain.

**Indonesia:**
Indonesia has approximately 29,000 megawatts of identified geothermal potential — the largest in the world by total resource — but has developed only a small fraction of this, approximately 2,300 megawatts as of 2024. The geothermal potential is concentrated in the volcanic arc running from Sumatra through Java, Bali, and the eastern islands. These areas have substantial Indigenous and adat (traditional law) community populations whose land rights are recognized, unevenly, under Indonesian law.

Indonesia's Law 21/2014 on Geothermal Activities established a regulatory framework separating geothermal development from the general mining regulatory framework, allowing geothermal development in protected forest areas (which had previously been prohibited). This created new opportunities for geothermal development but also new pressure on protected forest areas that are often home to Indigenous and adat communities.

Indonesia's approach to Indigenous rights is governed by Constitutional Court Decision No. 35/PUU-X/2012, which established that adat (customary) forests are distinct from state forests and must be recognized as such. However, implementation of this decision has been slow, and many adat communities in geothermal-relevant areas do not have certified territorial recognition. The gap between constitutional recognition and administrative implementation is where geothermal projects proceed in territories whose Indigenous and adat rights are recognized in principle but not yet formalized in practice.

## 9. Pacific Fisheries and Food Sovereignty

**The Pacific salmon:**
Pacific salmon (genus *Oncorhynchus*, comprising Chinook, coho, sockeye, pink, chum, and steelhead) are the ecological and cultural foundation of Indigenous food systems in the Pacific Northwest. The salmon return annually from the Pacific Ocean to the freshwater streams where they were spawned, providing a concentrated pulse of marine-derived nutrients to river ecosystems and to the human communities that have harvested them for at least 10,000 years.

The cultural significance of salmon to Pacific Northwest tribal nations is not an abstraction. Salmon are the basis of ceremonial life, the subject of extensive traditional ecological knowledge, and the primary protein source for communities whose food sovereignty depends on continued access to healthy runs. The decline of salmon populations — attributable to dam construction, habitat destruction, climate change, ocean acidification, and decades of unsustainable commercial fishing — is simultaneously an ecological crisis, a food security crisis, and a cultural crisis for the nations whose treaty rights and traditional territories encompass salmon fisheries.

**Governance framework:**
The governance of Pacific salmon in the United States Pacific Northwest is structured by treaty rights (the Boldt Decision and subsequent case law), the US Endangered Species Act (listing of multiple salmon populations), the Pacific Salmon Treaty between the United States and Canada (governing transboundary salmon runs), and the Pacific Fishery Management Council (a federal advisory body managing West Coast commercial fisheries). Tribal nations are co-managers of salmon resources in Washington and Oregon under the treaty rights framework, with co-management authority that goes beyond consultation to actual decision-making power over harvest levels.

## 10. The 1974 Boldt Decision and the 50-Percent Allocation

**Background:**
The United States v. Washington litigation was initiated by the United States government on behalf of Pacific Northwest tribal nations to enforce treaty fishing rights against the State of Washington's restrictions on tribal fishing. The treaties at issue — signed in the 1850s under territorial Governor Isaac Stevens — reserved to the tribal nations "the right of taking fish, at all usual and accustomed grounds and stations, in common with all citizens of the Territory." Washington state argued that "in common with" meant tribal fishing was subject to the same regulations as non-tribal fishing. Tribal nations argued that the phrase reserved to them a specific share of the resource.

Federal District Judge George Hugo Boldt's 1974 decision — United States v. Washington, 384 F. Supp. 312 (W.D. Wash. 1974) — found for the tribal nations. Judge Boldt interpreted the treaty language as reserving to tribal nations the right to take up to 50 percent of the harvestable salmon in their usual and accustomed fishing areas. The decision established that treaty fishing rights are not equivalent to recreational or commercial fishing licenses that the state can restrict or revoke; they are federally protected treaty rights that the state must accommodate.

**Subsequent litigation:**
The Boldt Decision was appealed repeatedly by the State of Washington and non-tribal fishing interests. The Supreme Court affirmed the 50-percent allocation in Washington v. Washington State Commercial Passenger Fishing Vessel Association (1979). Subsequent proceedings — the Culverts Case (United States v. Washington, sub-proceeding 89-3) — extended the reasoning to find that Washington state had an obligation not to degrade salmon habitat through culvert construction that blocked salmon passage. The 2018 Supreme Court affirmation of the Culverts Case (divided court, affirming the Ninth Circuit) established that treaty fishing rights include the right to a habitat capable of supporting the fish that the treaty promised.

The Culverts Case has enormous implications beyond fisheries. It established that treaty rights are not merely rights to access a resource; they are rights to a living resource, which implies an obligation on the state to maintain the conditions — habitat, water quality, flow regimes — that make the resource viable. This reasoning connects fisheries treaty rights to land use decisions, climate policy, water law, and infrastructure permitting in ways that continue to be litigated.

**Co-management reality:**
The 50-percent allocation established by Boldt is implemented through a co-management system in which the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife and the tribal nations jointly set harvest levels each season. The tribal co-managers — the twenty-plus treaty tribes of Washington and Oregon — have developed technical fisheries management capacity that equals or exceeds state agency capacity in many areas. The Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission (CRITFC), the Northwest Indian Fisheries Commission (NWIFC), and the Columbia River Basin Fish and Wildlife Program all employ tribal fisheries scientists, population modelers, and habitat specialists whose work shapes harvest decisions.

Co-management is not without conflict. Disputes over season timing, gear type, allocation of degraded stocks, and the distribution of conservation burdens between tribal and non-tribal fishers have produced ongoing litigation and political conflict. But the structural reality of co-management is that tribal nations are not merely stakeholders in Pacific Northwest fisheries management. They are legal co-managers with decision-making authority derived from treaty rights that predate and supersede state regulation.

## 11. Lummi Nation: Reef Net Fishing and Ecological Precision

**Lummi Nation:**
The Lummi Nation is a Coast Salish people whose territory encompasses the area around present-day Bellingham, Washington, the San Juan Islands, and portions of the Strait of Georgia. The Lummi signed the Point Elliott Treaty in 1855, reserving fishing rights at their usual and accustomed grounds throughout their traditional territory, including the Fraser River sockeye fishery, the Puget Sound salmon runs, and the reef net fisheries of the San Juan Islands.

Lummi fishing rights were among the most directly at issue in the Boldt Decision. The Lummi Nation was a named plaintiff in United States v. Washington. The decision confirmed Lummi treaty rights to their usual and accustomed fishing grounds and established the 50-percent allocation as applying to Lummi fisheries.

**Reef net fishing:**
Reef net fishing is a traditional Lummi fishing method that is widely recognized as the most ecologically precise large-scale salmon harvesting technique in the Pacific Northwest. Reef nets are set at traditional reef net locations in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and around the San Juan Islands, where geography funnels salmon migrating toward the Fraser River. The gear consists of large nets suspended between two anchored platforms; salmon swimming over a shallow reef location encounter the net naturally and are captured when fishers raise it.

Reef net fishing is species-selective in a way that no industrial fishing method can replicate. Fishers can visually inspect the catch before it is removed from the water, allowing them to release non-target species, weak stocks, or protected salmon populations in live condition. The Lummi reef net fishery has been documented by fisheries biologists as having among the lowest bycatch and post-release mortality rates of any salmon harvesting method in the region.

The traditional locations for reef net fishing are culturally significant as well as ecologically important. Specific reef net locations are associated with specific Lummi families, with fishing rights inherited across generations and managed through family governance structures. The reef net sites are themselves a form of territorial organization: the assignment of specific sites to specific families represents a governance system for managing access to the resource and ensuring that harvesting remains within sustainable limits.

**The Cherry Point conflict:**
Lummi reef net fishing rights intersected with energy infrastructure development in the Cherry Point case. The Gateway Pacific Terminal — a proposed coal export terminal that would have been constructed at Cherry Point, Washington, adjacent to traditional Lummi fishing and harvesting grounds — was denied a Clean Water Act certification by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers in 2016 on grounds that included Lummi treaty rights. The denial represented one of the most significant applications of treaty fishing rights as a constraint on large-scale industrial infrastructure development.

The Lummi Nation's position was that the terminal would damage treaty fishing grounds and that construction could not proceed without Lummi consent. The Army Corps' decision — while framed in procedural terms — effectively accepted this position: the Corps found that the project required consultation with Lummi under Section 106 of the National Historic Preservation Act and that adequate consultation had not occurred. The practical effect was that one of the largest proposed coal export facilities on the West Coast was blocked, in part, by the exercise of tribal treaty rights.

## 12. Stillaguamish: Hatchery Restoration as Governance

**Stillaguamish Tribe:**
The Stillaguamish Tribe of Indians is a federally recognized tribe whose traditional territory encompasses the Stillaguamish River watershed in Snohomish County, Washington. The Tribe is a signatory to the Point Elliott Treaty (1855) and holds treaty fishing rights in the Stillaguamish River and its tributaries. The Stillaguamish River historically supported large runs of Chinook, coho, chum, pink, sockeye, and steelhead — the full complement of Pacific salmon species.

The Stillaguamish River Chinook population is listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act. The listing reflects a decades-long decline attributable to multiple stressors: the Pilchuck River diversion (which altered the river's hydrology in the early twentieth century), downstream habitat fragmentation, agricultural water use, urban development in the lower watershed, and the cumulative effects of hatchery practices that, for much of the twentieth century, were managed to maximize near-term harvest rather than maintain genetic diversity and wild population resilience.

**The Tribe's hatchery program:**
The Stillaguamish Tribe has operated a Chinook salmon hatchery since the 1980s and has substantially expanded and reformed its hatchery practices over subsequent decades. The Tribe's hatchery program is explicitly oriented toward wild population recovery, not harvest supplementation. Key elements include:

- **Integrated hatchery practices:** The Tribe uses broodstock collected from the wild Stillaguamish River population, maintaining genetic diversity and local adaptation. This contrasts with practices common in the twentieth century, in which hatcheries used out-of-basin or domesticated broodstock that diluted the genetic integrity of wild populations.
- **Habitat restoration:** The Tribe's natural resources department conducts large wood placement, riparian planting, and channel restoration in Stillaguamish tributaries to improve wild salmon rearing habitat. Hatchery production is treated as a supplement to wild production, not a substitute for it.
- **Monitoring and adaptive management:** The Tribe conducts independent population monitoring of Stillaguamish Chinook and coho, contributing to the scientific basis for co-management decisions.

The Tribe's hatchery program is an exercise of resource sovereignty: a nation using technical capacity, treaty rights, and territorial governance authority to restore the salmon populations on which its food system, cultural practice, and long-term ecological relationships depend. It is not a dependency on state agency programs. It is an Indigenous institution performing a governance function.

**Coho restoration:**
The Stillaguamish Tribe has also operated a coho enhancement program focused on the Portage Creek tributary, supplementing naturally produced coho with hatchery releases targeted to support recovery of the wild population. The program has been evaluated by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife and the Northwest Indian Fisheries Commission as effective in maintaining coho abundance in the Stillaguamish while wild production rebuilds.

The Tribe's natural resources staff collaborates with Snohomish County, the Washington Departments of Fish and Wildlife and Ecology, and the Army Corps of Engineers on watershed-scale restoration planning. This collaboration is not an equal-partners arrangement in a bureaucratic sense — the Tribe's standing derives from treaty rights, not administrative partnership — but it reflects the practical reality that effective salmon restoration in the Stillaguamish watershed requires coordination across multiple land ownership and regulatory jurisdictions.

## 13. Salmon Sovereignty as a Governance Framework

**What salmon sovereignty means:**
The phrase "salmon sovereignty" captures something that transcends fishing rights litigation. It describes a governance philosophy in which the health of salmon populations is understood as the measure of territorial sovereignty — where a nation's ability to exercise meaningful governance over its traditional territory is demonstrated by whether the salmon return. A territory whose salmon runs have collapsed is, in a functional sense, a territory whose governance has been displaced.

This framing has analytical implications. It means that resource sovereignty is not simply a matter of legal rights to harvest; it is a matter of governance capacity to steward. The tribal nations that have developed the strongest hatchery programs, habitat restoration programs, and monitoring systems are not just fulfilling treaty obligations. They are building governance infrastructure — technical capacity, institutional knowledge, inter-agency relationships — that constitutes the material expression of sovereignty over their territories.

**Traditional ecological knowledge in salmon management:**
Pacific Northwest tribal nations hold millennia of accumulated ecological knowledge about Pacific salmon — their migration timing, habitat preferences, population dynamics, and ecological relationships with other species. This knowledge, built through sustained observation, cultural transmission, and direct relationship with specific river systems, is increasingly recognized by fisheries scientists as complementary to — and in some respects superior to — the shorter timelines of scientific monitoring programs.

Lummi reef net fishers, for example, have multi-generational records of salmon run timing at specific reef net locations. Stillaguamish elders hold knowledge of historical run compositions, spawning locations, and behavioral patterns that predate scientific monitoring by decades or centuries. This knowledge is not simply historical; it is applicable to contemporary management decisions.

UNDRIP Article 31 recognizes the right of Indigenous peoples to "maintain, control, protect and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge and traditional cultural expressions." In the context of salmon management, Article 31 implies that traditional ecological knowledge should be used in resource management decisions with the permission and governance oversight of the nations that hold it — not extracted and aggregated by state agencies without consent or attribution. The principle of data sovereignty applies as fully to traditional ecological knowledge as it does to digital data.

**Climate change and the future of salmon:**
Pacific salmon face compounding stressors from ocean warming, stream temperature increases, snowpack reduction, and changing precipitation patterns attributable to climate change. The Pacific Decadal Oscillation and El Niño/La Niña cycles, which drive multi-year variability in ocean productivity, are being overlaid with a trend toward warmer ocean temperatures that reduces the zooplankton prey base for juvenile salmon in the North Pacific.

For Pacific Northwest tribal nations, the climate threat to salmon is not a future risk. It is a present reality. The 2021 heat dome event in the Pacific Northwest killed an estimated one billion marine animals in Puget Sound and degraded salmon rearing conditions throughout the region. Multiple Chinook and sockeye populations are at historic lows. The Elwha River restoration — the removal of two dams to restore salmon passage to the upper Elwha, completed in 2014 — represents the largest dam removal project in US history and was driven in substantial part by the Lower Elwha Klallam Tribe's treaty rights to the Elwha fishery. The recovery of salmon in the Elwha has been documented as faster than modeled, providing evidence that habitat restoration produces measurable results on timescales relevant to governance decisions.

**From fisheries to governance:**
The governance architecture of Pacific Northwest salmon management — treaty rights, co-management, technical capacity, traditional ecological knowledge — is a model for how resource sovereignty can be exercised in practice. Tribal nations in the Pacific Northwest did not obtain their current co-management role by waiting for governments to voluntarily share power. They litigated treaty rights over decades, built technical capacity to match or exceed state agencies, and established institutional relationships that made tribal participation in management decisions a practical necessity rather than a political gesture.

The Ring of Fire chromite case, the Pacific geothermal case, and the Pacific Northwest fisheries case all point toward the same conclusion: Indigenous sovereignty over resources on traditional territories is not an obstacle to sustainable resource development. It is a condition for it. Development that bypasses sovereignty produces conflict, litigation, direct action, and reputational damage that prolongs uncertainty and delays or cancels projects. Development that engages sovereignty — genuinely, through consent and equity, not procedurally through consultation minimums — produces stable legal foundations, community support, and the ecological governance that sustains resources over multi-generational timescales.

The Ring of Fire was, before colonization, a Ring of Governance. The territorial authority of the Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities over the James Bay Lowlands, of the Māori iwi over the Waikato volcanic plateau, of the Lummi over the reef net grounds of the Strait of Juan de Fuca — these are not historical claims awaiting legal recognition. They are present realities that industrial development either engages with or collides against. Building infrastructure that ignores sovereign nations is not a network. It is a pipeline drawn through other people's living rooms.
