## 1. Introduction: Two Centuries of Legal Suppression and Forty Years of Recovery

The legal history of Indigenous rights in Ring of Fire nations is, in broad outline, a story in three phases: centuries of dispossession under colonial and post-colonial legal doctrine; a post-World War II human rights era that nominally extended protections but rarely reached land and resource sovereignty; and a contemporary era, beginning roughly with ILO Convention 169 in 1989 and crystallizing with the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) in 2007, in which the international community formally acknowledged what Indigenous peoples and their advocates had argued throughout: that sovereignty is not granted by settler governments. It is inherent.

This distinction — inherent versus granted — is not rhetorical. It has structural consequences for how legal frameworks are designed, how courts interpret rights claims, and how infrastructure projects on or near Indigenous territories must proceed. A framework that treats Indigenous rights as grants from the state can revoke them; a framework that treats them as inherent attributes of peoples can only recognize, respect, or violate them. The contemporary international legal architecture, imperfectly and unevenly, has moved toward the recognition model.

The Ring of Fire as a geographic concept encompasses the seismic and volcanic arc ringing the Pacific Basin. As a governance concept, it encompasses the territories of hundreds of distinct Indigenous nations: the First Nations of the Canadian Subarctic and Pacific Coast; the tribal nations of the Pacific Northwest, California, Alaska, and Hawaii; the Māori of Aotearoa; the Ainu of Hokkaido and the Ryūkyūans of Okinawa; the peoples of the Philippine Cordillera, the Indonesian archipelago, and the Pacific Islands; the Mapuche and other nations of Pacific-facing South America. These peoples inhabited, governed, and sustained relationships with these territories for millennia before colonial contact. Their territorial rights did not end with colonization. They persisted, through suppression, adaptation, litigation, and resistance, into the present.

**The legal recovery:**
The contemporary legal architecture governing Indigenous rights in Ring of Fire nations rests on several interlocking frameworks: international declarations (UNDRIP), binding conventions (ILO 169), domestic constitutional provisions, national legislation, and treaty rights. No single layer is sufficient. Each interacts with the others in ways that vary by jurisdiction, political context, and the specific nation asserting rights. Understanding the architecture requires understanding each layer and how they interlock — or fail to.

**Why this matters for infrastructure:**
The Ring of Fire is among the world's most resource-dense regions. The Canadian Ring of Fire (James Bay Lowlands) holds critical minerals essential to energy transition. The Pacific Rim holds 40 percent or more of the world's geothermal potential. Pacific fisheries are the protein base for hundreds of millions of people. Every major infrastructure project proposed in this region — mines, geothermal plants, fisheries management regimes, pipelines, transmission lines, roads — crosses or traverses territories where Indigenous sovereignty either is legally recognized, is contested, or should be but has not yet been acknowledged. Building without engaging that sovereignty is not just ethically wrong. Since 2007, and in many jurisdictions much earlier, it increasingly produces legal consequences: injunctions, permit revocations, court-ordered restitution, and direct action.

## 2. UNDRIP: The Declaration and Its Key Articles

**Adoption and initial opposition:**
The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples was adopted by the UN General Assembly on September 13, 2007, with 144 votes in favor, 11 abstentions, and 4 votes against. The four nations voting against were Canada, the United States, Australia, and New Zealand — precisely the four settler-colonial states with the largest domestic Indigenous rights disputes and the most to lose from an international rights framework with normative force. Their stated objections centered on the collective nature of the rights declared, the potential conflict with domestic constitutional orders, and the scope of land and resource rights implied.

All four have since reversed their opposition. Australia endorsed UNDRIP in 2009, New Zealand in 2010, Canada and the United States in 2010. These endorsements carried varying weight: Canada's 2010 endorsement was described as a "statement of support" without binding force; the United States noted that UNDRIP was a "non-binding aspirational document." The subsequent passage of Canada's UNDRIP Act in 2021 (discussed in Section 6) moved Canadian federal policy toward treating the Declaration as a binding framework — a significant shift from the 2010 position.

UNDRIP is a declaration, not a treaty, and thus creates no binding obligations in international law in the same way a ratified convention does. However, it carries substantial normative weight as evidence of customary international law and as a framework against which domestic legislation is increasingly measured. Courts in multiple jurisdictions have cited UNDRIP articles when interpreting domestic Indigenous rights law, including the Supreme Court of Canada and the courts of New Zealand.

**Article 10 — Prohibition of forced relocation:**
Article 10 provides that "Indigenous peoples shall not be forcibly removed from their lands or territories. No relocation shall take place without the free, prior and informed consent of the Indigenous peoples concerned and after agreement on just and fair compensation and, where possible, with the option of return."

The significance for Ring of Fire infrastructure is direct. Extraction and infrastructure projects in the James Bay Lowlands, the Alaskan North Slope, and the Pacific Rim frequently require access to, alteration of, or occupation of territories that Indigenous peoples have occupied for generations. Where that occupation would effectively displace communities — through flooding, contamination, habitat destruction, or the severing of access routes — Article 10 applies. The Article's protection is not limited to formal, legally ordered removal. Where a project makes continued habitation impractical, it triggers the Article's protections.

**Article 11 — Cultural heritage and intellectual property:**
Article 11 affirms the right of Indigenous peoples "to practise and revitalize their cultural traditions and customs" and to "maintain, protect and develop the past, present and future manifestations of their cultures, such as archaeological and historical sites, artefacts, designs, ceremonies, technologies and visual and performing arts and literature." States are obligated to provide "redress" for cultural heritage "taken without their free, prior and informed consent or in violation of their laws, traditions and customs."

For Ring of Fire governance, Article 11 applies to sacred sites, traditional fishing and hunting territories with cultural significance, and the extraction of traditional ecological knowledge. The Ainu of Hokkaido, for example, hold oral traditions encoding ecological knowledge of Hokkaido's river systems, fisheries, and forest succession patterns. That knowledge has instrumental value for environmental management; Article 11 establishes that its use requires consent, not just acknowledgment.

**Article 19 — Consultation before legislative or administrative measures:**
Article 19 requires that states "consult and cooperate in good faith with the Indigenous peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent before adopting and implementing legislative or administrative measures that may affect them."

Article 19 is the provision most frequently invoked in permit and environmental review proceedings. When a government proposes legislation affecting resource extraction on Indigenous territories — a mining act, a fisheries regulation, an energy policy — Article 19 requires that consultation be conducted through the nation's own governance institutions, in good faith, prior to the measure's adoption, with the purpose of obtaining consent. The consultation cannot be perfunctory; "good faith" has been interpreted by courts to mean genuine engagement with substantive concerns, documented responses to those concerns, and a process designed to reach agreement rather than merely to inform.

**Article 28 — Redress for lands taken without consent:**
Article 28 provides that "Indigenous peoples have the right to redress, by means that can include restitution or, when this is not possible, just, fair and equitable compensation, for the lands, territories and resources which they have traditionally owned or otherwise occupied or used, and which have been confiscated, taken, occupied, used or damaged without their free, prior and informed consent."

The Article addresses the historical dimension of dispossession. Its implications are potentially enormous for Ring of Fire nations: the mineral rights that were assigned to the Ontario or British Columbia Crown without the consent of the First Nations on whose territories those minerals sit, the fisheries whose productive capacity was depleted by non-Indigenous industrial fishing without consent, the geothermal reservoirs developed on Māori whenua without adequate treaty recognition. Article 28 does not merely look forward. It looks backward, and it provides a legal basis for restitution or compensation claims rooted in the full history of unconsented dispossession.

**Article 32 — FPIC for resource projects:**
Article 32 is the most operationally specific FPIC provision in UNDRIP. It provides that states "shall consult and cooperate in good faith with the Indigenous peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent prior to the approval of any project affecting their lands or territories and other resources, particularly in connection with the development, utilization or exploitation of mineral, water or other resources."

Article 32 is specific to resource projects — mines, pipelines, geothermal plants, fisheries operations, water diversions. It names the mechanism (consultation through representative institutions), the standard (free, prior and informed consent), and the timing (prior to approval). A project approved without this process has not complied with Article 32, regardless of the domestic legal regime under which the approval was issued. This is the provision that has most directly challenged the permitting processes of Ring of Fire nations.

## 3. ILO Convention 169: The Binding Instrument

**What Convention 169 is:**
The International Labour Organization's Convention Concerning Indigenous and Tribal Peoples in Independent Countries (ILO C169) was adopted in 1989, replacing the assimilationist ILO Convention 107 of 1957. Convention 169 is a binding treaty in international law. States that ratify it are legally obligated to implement its provisions in domestic law. As of 2024, 24 countries have ratified Convention 169, including several significant Ring of Fire nations: Chile (2008), the Philippines (through its 1997 domestic IPRA legislation, which ILO considers substantively consistent), Bolivia, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, and others. Canada, the United States, Japan, and Indonesia have not ratified.

**Article 6 — Consultation on legislation:**
Article 6 requires governments to "consult the peoples concerned, through appropriate procedures and in particular through their representative institutions, whenever consideration is being given to legislative or administrative measures which may affect them directly." The consultation must be undertaken "in good faith and in a form appropriate to the circumstances, with the objective of achieving agreement or consent to the proposed measures."

The language "achieving agreement or consent" is significant. Article 6 does not merely require governments to inform or hear Indigenous peoples. It requires that the consultation be oriented toward agreement. This is not identical to requiring consent as a veto power in all circumstances — the Convention has been interpreted by ILO supervisory bodies as establishing a duty to seek consent, with the degree of requirement varying with the severity of impact — but it is far stronger than a procedural notice-and-comment regime. Where a project would significantly impair the livelihood or cultural practices of an Indigenous people, the ILO's Committee of Experts has interpreted Article 6 as approaching a consent requirement.

**Article 16 — Relocation:**
Article 16 addresses the relocation of Indigenous peoples from their territories, paralleling UNDRIP Article 10 but with binding force for ratifiers. It provides that Indigenous peoples "shall not be removed from the lands which they occupy" and that "where the relocation of these peoples is considered necessary as an exceptional measure, such relocation shall take place only with their free and informed consent." Where consent cannot be obtained, relocation may proceed only following "appropriate procedures established by national laws and regulations, including public inquiries where appropriate, which provide the opportunity for effective representation of the peoples concerned."

Article 16 further provides for the right of return "as soon as the grounds for relocation cease to exist" and requires that, where return is not possible, compensation that "shall, as far as possible, replicate their original lands" be provided. The land-equivalent compensation standard distinguishes Article 16 from purely monetary compensation regimes: money cannot replace territory. The Convention recognizes this and establishes territory as the measure of restitution.

**ILO 169 and the Ring of Fire:**
For Ring of Fire nations that have ratified Convention 169, every infrastructure project on or near Indigenous territories triggers Article 6 consultation obligations as a matter of binding international law, not merely as domestic policy. Chile's ratification has been tested in the Mapuche land conflicts, with results discussed in Section 11. The Philippines' IPRA is considered the most comprehensive domestic implementation of C169 principles, though implementation gaps are substantial (see Section 10).

Nations that have not ratified C169 — Canada, the United States, Japan — are not bound by its provisions. However, ILO supervisory bodies have expressed concern about the consultation practices of non-ratifiers when complaints from Indigenous organizations are brought before ILO mechanisms, and UNDRIP articles (non-binding but normatively significant) overlap substantially with C169's substantive requirements. The practical gap between C169 ratifiers and non-ratifiers is thus somewhat narrower than the formal legal status suggests, particularly as domestic courts in non-ratifying countries increasingly cite international human rights norms.

## 4. FPIC: From Consultation to Consent

**The doctrine:**
Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) is the conceptual spine of contemporary Indigenous rights law. It appears in UNDRIP Articles 10, 19, 28, and 32 and in ILO Convention 169 Articles 6 and 16. Understanding FPIC requires understanding not just the phrase but each of its components, and understanding the critical distinction between the weaker standard of "consultation" and the stronger standard of "consent."

**Free:**
The consent must be given free of coercion, intimidation, manipulation, or undue pressure. This requirement has direct application in contexts where governments or corporations have economic leverage over communities — where jobs, infrastructure, or services are conditioned on acquiescence to a project. Where a government withholds road access, education funding, or health services contingent on Indigenous support for a project, the "free" element is compromised. Courts in multiple jurisdictions have held that consent obtained under conditions of economic dependency or explicit threats does not satisfy the FPIC standard.

**Prior:**
The consent must be sought before the project is approved, not after. "Prior" does not mean consultation at the earliest stages of project planning while decisions remain open, followed by approval and then engagement with affected communities on mitigation. It means that Indigenous consent is a prerequisite to project approval, not a step in post-approval permitting. The UN Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples has noted that consultations initiated after government approval decisions have been made are structurally incapable of satisfying the "prior" requirement because the decision subject to consent has already been taken.

**Informed:**
Informed consent requires that the affected community have access to full, accurate, and understandable information about the nature, scope, reversibility, and potential impacts of the proposed project — in a language and format accessible to the community, with sufficient time to review and deliberate. Information provided in technical documents that require specialist interpretation, provided days before a required response, in a language not widely spoken by community members, does not satisfy the "informed" element. This requirement has generated substantial litigation over the adequacy of environmental impact assessments, translation and accessibility requirements, and the timeliness of information disclosure.

**Consent (versus consultation):**
The most consequential analytical distinction in FPIC doctrine is between the consultation model and the consent model. Under the consultation model, the government must inform and hear the affected community, consider their concerns, and respond to them, but retains final decision-making authority. Under the consent model, the affected community has effective veto power: a project cannot proceed without the community's agreement.

The distinction is not merely semantic. Projects proceeding under the consultation model can be approved over Indigenous objections, provided the consultation process was procedurally adequate. Projects proceeding under the consent model cannot — regardless of procedural compliance — be approved without the community's agreement.

UNDRIP's language, particularly in Articles 19 and 32, pushes toward the consent model. Article 19 requires states to seek consent; Article 32 requires consent specifically for resource projects. However, UNDRIP itself includes language (Article 46) acknowledging the territorial integrity of states and national unity considerations, and the Declaration does not explicitly characterize consent as an absolute veto in all circumstances. This internal tension has produced a range of interpretive positions, from those treating UNDRIP as establishing a categorical consent requirement for significant projects, to those treating it as establishing a strong consultative obligation with consent as the goal rather than the absolute precondition.

The UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues has consistently interpreted UNDRIP's FPIC provisions as encompassing a genuine right to withhold consent, not merely a right to be consulted. The Special Rapporteur on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples has distinguished contexts in which consent is required (projects that would amount to forced relocation, projects causing irreversible damage to subsistence resources, and projects affecting sacred sites or cultural heritage of fundamental importance) from contexts where the consultation standard may be sufficient.

**Why the distinction has practical stakes:**
The difference between consultation and consent is the difference between a project that faces legal challenge for procedural inadequacy and a project that faces legal challenge for proceeding over a community's clearly expressed refusal. Courts, investors, lenders, and insurers assess these risks differently. Projects proceeding under a consent framework that has been properly implemented carry significantly lower legal and reputational risk than projects approved over documented Indigenous objection under a consultation model that a court later finds inadequate.

**Equity co-investment as a consent signal:**
One emerging indicator that consent has been meaningfully obtained is equity co-investment by the affected Indigenous nation in the project. Where a nation holds an equity stake — ownership, not just employment or royalty — in a project on its territory, the structural relationship between the nation and the project changes. The nation becomes a co-owner with governance rights, financial stake in the project's success, and standing to shape operating decisions. The UN Special Rapporteur has noted equity participation as among the strongest available signals that FPIC has been substantively rather than procedurally satisfied.

This model is emerging, not universal. It is inappropriate to suggest that any specific nation has agreed to any particular equity arrangement in any proposed project. What is accurate is that the equity co-investment model has been adopted in several completed projects — most prominently in certain Canadian First Nations' stakes in pipeline and energy infrastructure — and that it represents a qualitatively different engagement than either pure consultation or community benefit agreements.

**What happens when FPIC is violated:**
The consequences of proceeding without FPIC are not merely normative. They are practical and escalating. In roughly ascending order of severity:

1. **Administrative challenge:** Environmental assessments or permits challenged in domestic courts on grounds of inadequate consultation, resulting in project delays, redesigns, or cancellations.
2. **International complaints:** Indigenous nations filing complaints with ILO supervisory bodies, the UN Special Rapporteur, or the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, generating political pressure and reputational damage.
3. **Direct action:** Blockades, land defense camps, and interference with project access — legally recognized as a last resort by international human rights bodies where domestic remedies have been exhausted.
4. **Investor and lender withdrawal:** Institutional investors applying environmental, social, and governance (ESG) criteria have withdrawn from projects with documented FPIC violations. The Equator Principles, adopted by major development finance institutions, require FPIC compliance for projects in covered categories.
5. **Cancellation by judicial order:** In the most extreme cases, courts have ordered project cancellations or moratoriums — as occurred in Canada in the Trans Mountain Pipeline proceedings and in multiple Philippine cases under IPRA.

## 6. Canada: The UNDRIP Act and the Federal/Provincial Gap

**Constitutional foundation:**
Section 35 of the Constitution Act, 1982 recognizes and affirms "the existing aboriginal and treaty rights of aboriginal peoples of Canada." The word "existing" was interpreted by the Supreme Court of Canada in *R v. Sparrow* (1990) to encompass rights that were not extinguished before 1982 and survived as protected constitutional rights. *Sparrow* established a justification framework: government action that infringes a protected Aboriginal right must be for a compelling and substantial objective and must infringe the right as little as possible.

**The duty to consult:**
The Supreme Court of Canada's trilogy of cases — *Haida Nation v. British Columbia* (2004), *Taku River Tlingit First Nation v. British Columbia* (2004), and *Mikisew Cree First Nation v. Canada* (2005) — established the duty to consult as a constitutional obligation arising from the honour of the Crown. The duty arises when the Crown has knowledge of a potential Aboriginal or treaty right and contemplates conduct that might adversely affect that right. The depth of the duty varies with the strength of the right and the severity of the potential impact: shallow consultation (notice and an opportunity to comment) at the lower end; deep consultation (genuine dialogue, accommodation, and in some cases consent) at the upper end.

The duty to consult applies to both federal and provincial Crown conduct. However, the depth of consultation required varies. Where a project requires provincial permits — mining leases, surface rights, environmental assessments under provincial environmental assessment legislation — the provincial government's consultation obligations may be shallower than the federal government's, depending on the strength of the right at issue.

**The UNDRIP Act (2021):**
The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (S.C. 2021, c. 14) came into force June 21, 2021. It is the most significant federal Indigenous rights legislation in Canada since the Constitution Act, 1982. The Act provides that the federal government must:

1. Take all measures necessary to ensure that federal laws are consistent with UNDRIP;
2. Develop and implement a National Action Plan in consultation with Indigenous peoples; and
3. Report annually to Parliament on progress.

The 2023 National Action Plan established FPIC as a binding standard for federal Crown action. This represents a significant upgrade from the duty to consult: the Action Plan commits the federal government to seeking consent, not merely consulting, before proceeding with measures that affect Indigenous rights.

**The provincial gap:**
The UNDRIP Act is federal legislation. It binds federal Crown conduct. Provincial governments are not bound by the UNDRIP Act and have not passed equivalent legislation in most provinces. Ontario, whose territory includes the Canadian Ring of Fire (see Module 07), operates under the duty to consult standard, not an FPIC standard. This creates a structural gap: a federal environmental assessment conducted under the UNDRIP Act's consent framework may be superseded by a provincial permitting process under the weaker duty to consult standard.

This gap is not merely theoretical. In the context of the Canadian Ring of Fire, it is the precise mechanism through which large-scale mineral extraction projects can be advanced without obtaining consent from the Nishnawbe Aski Nation communities whose territories encompass the chromite deposits. Federal environmental review may require FPIC-level engagement; provincial mining permits may require only duty-to-consult-level engagement. The project can proceed provincially even where federal FPIC has not been obtained, provided the provincial process satisfies the lower constitutional standard.

> "The Province of Ontario has the authority to issue mining permits without requiring the consent of First Nations. The federal government has committed to FPIC. These two things coexist within the same constitutional order, and the gap between them is where projects advance."
>
> — Nishnawbe Aski Nation (NAN), submission to the federal Regional Assessment process, 2025

**Jurisprudence and the consent question:**
The Supreme Court of Canada has not definitively held that consent is required in all cases under section 35. *Tsilhqot'in Nation v. British Columbia* (2014) came closest: in a case involving proven Aboriginal title over a specific territory, the Court held that the title-holding nation has a right to decide how that territory is used, and that government override of that right requires justification. Proof of title creates a strong version of the consent requirement. But the process for establishing title under *Tsilhqot'in* is lengthy, expensive, and uncertain — most nations with unresolved title claims operate under the weaker duty to consult standard while their claims proceed.

The trajectory of Canadian jurisprudence is toward consent. The question is whether domestic law will move there before the projects currently proposed in contested territories proceed.

## 7. United States: Federal Indian Law and the Consultation Floor

**The trust responsibility:**
The federal trust responsibility is a judicially recognized obligation of the United States government to act as a trustee for tribal nations and their resources. It arises from treaties, statutory enactments, and judicial decisions, and has been described by the Supreme Court as a "distinctive obligation" arising from the United States' unique relationship with tribal nations. The trust responsibility does not translate directly into a consultation or consent requirement, but it does impose substantive obligations: the United States must act in the best interests of tribal nations when managing resources held in trust.

The trust responsibility has been unevenly enforced. Courts have held that it creates enforceable duties in some contexts (trust fund mismanagement, treaty fishing rights) and merely aspirational obligations in others. The Cobell litigation — a class action lawsuit that settled in 2009 for $3.4 billion — established that the federal government had systematically mismanaged Individual Indian Money (IIM) trust accounts over more than a century, demonstrating the magnitude of trust responsibility failure possible within the existing legal framework.

**National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA):**
NEPA (42 U.S.C. § 4321 et seq., enacted 1970) requires federal agencies to conduct environmental review of proposed federal actions, including the issuance of permits and approvals for infrastructure projects. Environmental Impact Statements (EIS) under NEPA include a scoping process that includes "consultation with any Indian tribe or Alaska Native Corporation that may be interested in or affected by the proposed action" (40 C.F.R. § 1501.9).

NEPA consultation is procedural, not substantive. An agency that conducts required NEPA consultation and considers tribal concerns has satisfied NEPA even if it proceeds over tribal objections. NEPA does not require that federal agencies obtain tribal consent. It requires that they consult and respond. The adequacy of NEPA consultation has been litigated extensively, and courts have occasionally required supplemental consultation or remanded decisions for inadequate consultation, but no federal court has held that NEPA requires tribal consent as a precondition for project approval.

**Executive Order 13175 (2000) — Tribal Consultation:**
Executive Order 13175, issued by President Clinton in 2000 and reaffirmed by subsequent administrations, requires federal agencies to have a consultation policy with tribal nations and to engage in "regular and meaningful consultation and collaboration with tribal officials in the development of Federal policies that have tribal implications." The Order is executive in origin and does not create legally enforceable rights against the government in the same way a statute does. Its implementation depends substantially on the priorities of the administering agency and the administration in power.

The Biden administration's January 2021 memorandum on "Tribal Consultation and Strengthening Nation-to-Nation Relationships" directed agencies to strengthen consultation practices and consider FPIC principles — the strongest formal acknowledgment of FPIC in federal executive policy to date. The subsequent administration has not maintained this direction uniformly, and no statute codifying FPIC requirements has been enacted.

**No UNDRIP adoption:**
The United States endorsed UNDRIP in 2010 with the explicit caveat that it is a "non-binding aspirational document." No legislation has been enacted to implement UNDRIP in federal law. Proposals to enact a federal UNDRIP implementation statute have been introduced in Congress but have not advanced. The absence of domestic UNDRIP implementation means that US tribal nations cannot invoke UNDRIP as a legal standard in federal courts, though they may cite it as evidence of international customary norms in constitutional and treaty rights litigation.

**Treaty rights:**
The strongest source of Indigenous rights in US law is treaty rights. The United States entered into hundreds of treaties with tribal nations, most in the nineteenth century, in which tribal nations ceded land in exchange for reserved rights — including, critically, off-reservation fishing, hunting, and gathering rights in their traditional territories. The Boldt Decision of 1974 (United States v. Washington) established that treaty fishing rights guarantee Pacific Northwest tribal nations 50 percent of the harvestable salmon and steelhead in their usual and accustomed fishing areas. This is a legally enforceable right, not a policy preference, and it has been upheld repeatedly by federal courts through the present.

The distinction between treaty rights and FPIC is important. Treaty rights are specific rights reserved by specific nations in specific treaties, enforceable in federal court with established legal standards. FPIC is a general framework applicable to all Indigenous peoples regardless of treaty status. In the United States, the practical focus of Indigenous rights advocacy in infrastructure contexts has often been on invoking treaty rights — demonstrating that a specific project would infringe a specific treaty right — rather than on invoking FPIC, because treaty rights have stronger domestic legal footing.

## 8. Aotearoa New Zealand: Treaty Co-Governance and Unresolved Breaches

**Te Tiriti o Waitangi:**
The Treaty of Waitangi (1840) — more properly Te Tiriti o Waitangi, reflecting the primacy of the Māori-language text — is the foundational document of New Zealand's constitutional order. It was signed on February 6, 1840 between the British Crown and approximately 540 Māori rangatira (chiefs). The Treaty has two texts: the English-language version ceded "sovereignty" (kāwanatanga) to the Crown; the Māori-language version, which most signatories signed, used the term kāwanatanga (governance) while reserving tino-rangatiratanga — full chiefly authority — to Māori chiefs over their lands and people.

The divergence between the English and Māori texts is not a drafting error. It reflects the different understandings the parties brought to the agreement and the different claims they made about what was being transferred. The Waitangi Tribunal — a permanent commission of inquiry established in 1975 — has consistently held that the Māori text is the authoritative text for Māori, and that the Crown's subsequent exercise of powers it understood to derive from "sovereignty" as defined in the English text exceeded what the Māori rangatira consented to in signing the Māori text.

**The Waitangi Tribunal:**
The Waitangi Tribunal has the authority to investigate Crown actions that have breached the principles of the Treaty of Waitangi and to make recommendations for redress. Critically, Tribunal recommendations are not binding on the Crown. Parliament retains the power to act contrary to Tribunal recommendations. This has been a persistent limitation of the Treaty settlement process: findings of breach and recommendations for remedy have been accepted and implemented in many cases but overridden in others.

The historical Treaty settlement process has negotiated settlements with most major iwi (tribal groups), acknowledging historic breaches and providing redress in the form of money, returned land (typically a small fraction of what was taken), and cultural redress such as acknowledgment of the significance of specific mountains, rivers, or sites. These settlements do not extinguish future rights or claims. They address specific historical grievances within a framework that leaves the broader constitutional question — the extent of tino-rangatiratanga in the contemporary governance order — unresolved.

**Co-governance:**
The most significant development in Aotearoa's Indigenous rights landscape in recent decades is co-governance — the formal sharing of governance authority between the Crown and Māori in specific domains. The most significant example is the Waikato-Tainui and Ngāti Maniapoto settlement establishing co-governance of the Waikato River (2010), and the legislation establishing the Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017, which granted the Whanganui River legal personhood with joint Māori-Crown governance rights.

These models represent a structural rather than purely monetary response to Treaty obligations. They do not simply compensate for past wrongs; they create new governance arrangements that share ongoing decision-making authority. They are partial, uneven, and contested — some Māori commentators argue co-governance arrangements have been used to foreclose stronger sovereignty claims — but they represent a qualitatively different model than compensation without governance.

**Māori rights and geothermal resources:**
The Waikato volcanic plateau, where New Zealand's largest geothermal resources are located, is heartland for multiple iwi with strong rights under Te Tiriti. The Māori ownership of geothermal resources was contested throughout the twentieth century. The Resource Management Act 1991 (RMA) established that geothermal water and heat are owned by the Crown, but that Māori have culturally significant interests that must be considered in resource consent processes. The Waitangi Tribunal has found Crown geothermal resource management to be in breach of the Treaty in multiple inquiries.

The geothermal industry in New Zealand has increasingly negotiated consent and equity arrangements with iwi as a practical matter, reflecting both legal risk and the realities of operating in territories where Māori governance authority is substantial and well-organized. This is discussed further in Module 07.

## 9. Japan: Recognition Without Rights

**The Ainu:**
The Ainu are the Indigenous people of Hokkaido, the northernmost of Japan's main islands, and historically of Sakhalin and the Kuril Islands. For most of Japan's modern history, the state denied the existence of a distinct Ainu people, pursuing policies of assimilation that suppressed the Ainu language, prohibited traditional ceremonies and subsistence practices, and dispossessed Ainu communities of their lands. The 1899 Hokkaido Former Natives Protection Act codified many of these policies and was not repealed until 1997.

Japan formally recognized the Ainu as an Indigenous people for the first time in a 2008 Diet resolution. The Ainu Policy Promotion Act was enacted in 2019. The Act affirms that the Ainu are "a people indigenous to Japan" with "their own distinct culture," establishes a national policy of promoting Ainu culture, provides funding for cultural preservation activities, and establishes the Upopoy National Ainu Museum (opened 2020 in Shiraoi, Hokkaido) as a center for Ainu cultural heritage.

**What the 2019 Act does not include:**
The Ainu Policy Promotion Act does not include:
- Land rights of any kind. No mechanism exists under Japanese law for Ainu communities to claim ancestral domain, assert land rights, or require consent for development on former Ainu territories.
- Resource rights. The subsistence fishing and hunting practices that were central to Ainu culture and food systems remain restricted by Japanese fisheries and wildlife laws, with limited ceremonial exceptions in some regions.
- FPIC. No consultation or consent mechanism exists for infrastructure or resource projects affecting Ainu territories or traditional practices.
- Self-governance provisions. No Ainu governance institutions are established or recognized.

The 2019 Act represents cultural recognition without rights. It funds museums and cultural events. It does not establish the territorial, resource, or governance rights that UNDRIP Articles 10, 19, 28, and 32 require. Japan has not endorsed UNDRIP. It has not ratified ILO Convention 169. Ainu organizations including the Hokkaido Ainu Association have consistently stated that cultural recognition without land and resource rights does not fulfill the state's obligations to the Ainu people.

**The Ryūkyūans:**
The Ryūkyū Islands (Okinawa) were an independent kingdom — the Ryūkyū Kingdom — until their formal annexation by Japan in 1879, following a period of dual tribute to both Japan and China. The Ryūkyūan people maintain a distinct culture, language, and historical identity. Ryūkyūan languages (including Uchinaaguchi) are classified by UNESCO as endangered, with most speakers elderly.

Japan does not recognize the Ryūkyūans as an Indigenous people under domestic law. The Ainu Policy Promotion Act does not apply to Ryūkyūans. No special legal protections for Ryūkyūan cultural rights, language, land, or self-governance exist. Ryūkyūan civil society organizations have sought recognition as Indigenous peoples through UN mechanisms, including submissions to the UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, without success in obtaining Japanese state recognition.

The significance for Ring of Fire governance is that approximately 70 percent of US military bases in Japan are located in Okinawa, which makes up less than 1 percent of Japan's land area. The sustained presence of military infrastructure on Ryūkyūan territory, in the absence of recognized Indigenous rights, has proceeded without the consent frameworks that UNDRIP would require if Japan had endorsed it. The Okinawan opposition to the construction of the Henoko base — documented in repeated prefecture-wide referenda — illustrates the governance stakes of the recognition gap.

## 10. Philippines: First in Law, Contested in Practice

**IPRA:**
The Indigenous Peoples Rights Act of the Philippines (Republic Act 8371, enacted 1997) is among the most comprehensive national Indigenous rights legislation in the world. Enacted during the Ramos administration following decades of conflict over Indigenous territories in the Cordillera region of Luzon, IPRA recognized ancestral domains as the territories of Indigenous Cultural Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPs), established the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP) as the implementing agency, required FPIC for resource projects on ancestral domain, and created a certification system (Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title, CADT) for legal recognition of ancestral claims.

IPRA's theoretical framework is one of the strongest in the world. It defines ancestral domains as comprising all lands and territories traditionally used and occupied by ICCs/IPs, and it treats FPIC not as a concession from the state but as a right of the people. The FPIC provision (Section 3(g) and implementing regulations) requires that before any government agency or private entity begins resource extraction, infrastructure construction, or research activities on ancestral domain, it must obtain FPIC from the affected ICC/IP through a process conducted by the community's own governance structures, documented in a resolution, and certified by the NCIP.

**Implementation gaps:**
The gap between IPRA's framework and its implementation is substantial and well-documented by academic researchers, civil society organizations, and international human rights bodies.

The NCIP is chronically underfunded. As of recent assessments, hundreds of ancestral domain claims remain unprocessed, leaving many communities in legal uncertainty while their territories are subject to permit applications for mining and other resource extraction. The NCIP certification process has been criticized for lack of independence: as an agency within the executive branch, NCIP decisions on FPIC compliance have, in some cases, been coordinated with project approval processes in ways that compromise the community's ability to refuse.

The FPIC process itself has been subject to manipulation. Documented cases include: community meetings conducted with a minority of community members whose composition was influenced by the proponent; consent resolutions signed by individuals whose authority to represent the community is disputed; benefit packages offered as a condition of consultation (structurally compromising the "free" element); and FPIC processes conducted entirely in Tagalog or English with communities whose primary language is a distinct Cordilleran language. The Commission on Human Rights of the Philippines has documented multiple cases of FPIC violations in the Cordillera.

**The Cordillera context:**
The Cordillera Administrative Region of northern Luzon is the most resource-rich and most contested Indigenous territory in the Philippines. The Igorot peoples of the Cordillera — including the Ifugao, Kankanaey, Bontok, Kalinga, Isneg, and others — maintained self-governing communities that successfully resisted Spanish colonization throughout the colonial period. Their ancestral domains include the Ifugao Rice Terraces (a UNESCO World Heritage Site), substantial mineral deposits including gold and copper, and the watersheds that supply water to the Cagayan Valley.

Large-scale mining in the Cordillera has produced some of the most significant FPIC litigation in the Philippines. The 2010 Tampakan copper-gold mine controversy in Mindanao, which involved one of the largest proposed copper-gold mines in Southeast Asia, stalled in part due to opposition from the B'laan Indigenous people and related FPIC disputes, as well as a Tampakan municipal ordinance prohibiting open-pit mining. The project remained unresolved as of 2024.

## 11. Chile: ILO 169 and the Mapuche Criminalization Contradiction

**Ratification and domestic implementation:**
Chile ratified ILO Convention 169 in 2008, the first South American country to do so. Chile also voted in favor of UNDRIP in 2007. These international commitments created a legal framework obligating Chile to consult with Indigenous peoples — including the Mapuche, Aymara, Atacameño, and other recognized nations — before legislative or administrative measures affecting them, and to seek consent for significant projects on their territories.

Chile has no comprehensive Indigenous rights legislation equivalent to Canada's UNDRIP Act or the Philippines' IPRA. The primary domestic mechanism implementing C169 consultation obligations is a series of executive-issued regulations, most recently updated through Decree 66 of 2013 and subsequent modifications. The 2023 Consultation Regulation (Decreto 14) was introduced to address recognized shortcomings in the 2013 process. The Chilean Supreme Court has annulled environmental approvals on consultation grounds in several significant cases, establishing that inadequate consultation is an actionable legal deficiency — but the court has not established consent as a precondition for projects over Indigenous objection.

**The Mapuche land conflict:**
The Mapuche people occupied approximately 10 million hectares of south-central Chile before Spanish colonization. Spanish military campaigns (the Arauco War, spanning more than three centuries) failed to subjugate the Mapuche, who maintained territorial autonomy through treaty arrangements with the Spanish Crown. Chilean independence did not bring recognition of Mapuche sovereignty. The Chilean state's "pacification" campaigns of the 1860s-1880s reduced Mapuche territorial holdings to small reservations comprising a fraction of ancestral territory.

Contemporary Mapuche land rights advocacy has focused on the recovery of ancestral lands held by large agricultural and forestry corporations — particularly pine and eucalyptus plantations that replaced native forest and destroyed the ecological base of Mapuche subsistence. This advocacy has taken the form of political organizing, legal claims, direct occupation of disputed lands, and, in some cases, property destruction.

**The Antiterrorism Law contradiction:**
The Chilean state has, on multiple occasions, prosecuted Mapuche land rights activists under the Antiterrorism Law (Law 18314, originally enacted under Pinochet). Application of the Antiterrorism Law to Mapuche land defense is a structural contradiction with ILO Convention 169's requirements. An Article 6 consultation framework is structurally compromised when the Indigenous parties to that consultation face criminal prosecution for actions related to their territorial claims. The Inter-American Commission on Human Rights has expressed concern about the application of antiterrorism provisions to Mapuche activists on multiple occasions. The UN Committee for the Elimination of Racial Discrimination has similarly raised concerns.

> "The application of antiterrorism legislation against Mapuche peoples for acts related to their social protest and demands for the recovery of their ancestral lands... constitutes a form of racial discrimination."
>
> — UN Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD), Concluding Observations on Chile, 2013

The "free" element of FPIC is not just a procedural requirement. It requires conditions under which Indigenous peoples can exercise their rights without fear of coercion or reprisal. A consultation regime in which land rights advocates face terrorism charges is structurally incapable of satisfying the "free" element, regardless of the formal procedural adequacy of the consultation process.

**The 2022 constitutional process:**
Chile's 2022 constitutional convention — the first in Chilean history to include guaranteed Indigenous representation — produced a draft constitution that would have recognized Indigenous nations as "autonomous political entities" with territorial rights, included FPIC as a constitutional right, and recognized Mapuche, Aymara, Rapa Nui, and other nations' territorial claims. The draft was rejected in a national referendum in September 2022. A subsequent constitutional drafting process produced a more conservative draft that was also rejected in December 2023. Chile remains operating under its 1980 Pinochet-era constitution, and the constitutional path to Indigenous rights entrenchment has, at least for now, closed.
