## 1. The Seven-Rung Economic Pathway

The Ring of Fire Trade Network is built as a ladder, not a program. Each rung is accessible from the one below. No rung requires the one above it to function. The architecture prevents dependency at any level.

```
  RUNG 7: GLOBAL CO-OP
  |  Pacific Rim trade, individual investors, AI-assisted
  |  Proof of concept for human-AI collaboration going forward
  |
  RUNG 6: REGIONAL CO-OP NETWORK
  |  Ring of Fire Trade Network (cooperative structure)
  |  Shared infrastructure, shared risk, shared revenue
  |
  RUNG 5: SMALL BUSINESS
  |  Individual becomes economic actor
  |  Formation, licensing, tax compliance
  |
  RUNG 4: HIGHER EDUCATION or TRADE
  |  CPA, LTP/LTC, retrofit carpentry, sensor tech
  |  Drone operator, AI system monitor, mesh deployer
  |  Tribal college, community college, SBDC pipeline
  |
  RUNG 3: EDUCATION
  |  Literacy, numeracy, civic knowledge
  |
  RUNG 2: INDIVIDUAL
  |  The person, their needs, their agency
  |
  RUNG 1: UBI (FLOOR)
     Managed by local tribal officials
     Safety net that makes every rung accessible
     Locally administered, not federally algorithmic
     Interim structure until co-op formalizes governance
```

**The UBI principle:**
A person pursuing education cannot learn if they are worried about feeding their family. The UBI floor is locally administered -- tribal officials who know their community determine how the floor functions, who qualifies, and at what level. This is not a federal algorithm applied uniformly. It is a community decision.

**Why cooperative structure prevents capture:**
At every level, ownership is distributed. No single investor, government, or corporation can own the network. The AI is federated, not centralized. The data stays local. The decisions stay human. Each rung is designed so that no external actor can capture the entire pathway by controlling a single node.

## 2. Workforce Pipeline: Tribal College to Co-op Employment

**The institutional backbone:**

- **Northwest Indian College (NWIC):** Located on the Lummi Indian Reservation in Bellingham, Washington. Offers degrees in tribal governance, environmental studies, and business. The natural entry point for the Ring of Fire workforce pipeline in the Pacific Northwest.
- **Salish Kootenai College (SKC):** Tribal college on the Flathead Reservation in Montana. Accredited, with strong STEM programs. Pipeline for Intermountain West tribal communities.
- **Everett Community College:** Community college in Snohomish County, Washington, adjacent to Tulalip tribal territory. Workforce training programs in construction, electronics, and information technology.
- **Small Business Development Centers (SBDCs):** Rung 5 support. SBDCs provide free business planning assistance, licensing guidance, and access to SBA loan programs for small business formation.

**The pipeline stages:**

1. GED/foundational literacy if needed (Rung 3 support)
2. Tribal college enrollment -- 2-year or 4-year program in relevant field
3. Co-op apprenticeship -- paid, on-the-job training in retrofit, sensor installation, drone operations, or AI monitoring
4. Certification -- OSHA construction certifications, FAA Part 107 (commercial drone), vendor certifications for specific AI monitoring tools
5. Co-op employment -- permanent employment with the cooperative network, with ownership stake

**The continuous retraining requirement:**
Technology changes. Sensor systems are upgraded. AI tools evolve. The cooperative is a standing training institution -- not just for initial workforce entry, but for continuous retraining as technology evolves. Members who trained as sensor installers 10 years ago need updated skills as sensor technology changes. The co-op funds and provides this ongoing training.

**Business and governance skills (Rung 4, track B):**

| Skill | Training Path | Co-op Application |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| Cooperative accounting (CPA) | College degree + certification | Co-op financial management |
| Tribal governance | Tribal college | Board membership, treaty navigation |

**The local knowledge preservation:**
No training program can replicate local knowledge. The cooperative's training pipeline explicitly pairs technical training with cultural and ecological knowledge sessions led by tribal knowledge keepers. A drone operator who knows where the salmon spawn, where the cedar groves are, and where the village's sacred sites are located operates differently from one who does not.

## 4. Federated AI Skill-Centers

**The architecture principle:**
Each Ring of Fire community operates a local AI skill-center. The centers are connected by federation -- they share model architectures, training objectives, and evaluation results, but they do not share raw data.

**Why federation, not centralization:**
A centralized AI system collects data from all communities, trains a single model, and deploys it everywhere. This creates:
- A single point of failure (the central system can be hacked, disabled, or politically captured)
- Data sovereignty violations (raw community data leaves the community)
- Dependency (communities cannot operate if the central system is down)
- Lack of local adaptation (a model trained on average Pacific Rim data does not perform well for any specific community)

A federated system allows:
- Local data stays local (each community's data never leaves their control)
- Local models are trained on local data, with better performance for local conditions
- Models share architectures (so each community benefits from the network's collective research)
- The network has no single point of failure (each node operates independently if needed)

**"If you trained the model, you understand the model":**
The cooperative's federated AI governance principle is that community members who are going to use an AI system should understand how it works. Not the underlying mathematics -- but the training objectives, the data used, the evaluation criteria, and the kinds of errors the model makes. Skill-center training includes this education. The goal is not AI literacy as an abstract value -- it is accountability. If you understand the model, you can tell when it is wrong.

## 5. AI Governance: Transparency and Local Control

**Published training objectives:**
Every AI model deployed by the cooperative has published, human-readable training objectives. Not marketing language -- operational specifications. "This model is trained to classify tsunami threat level from seismic and pressure sensor inputs. It is NOT trained to predict earthquake timing. Its error rate at HIGH threat classification is X%."

**Government oversight:**
The cooperative's AI systems are not exempt from government oversight. FEMA, NOAA, and tribal nation emergency management agencies have visibility into the AI systems used for early warning and response. The AI systems do not replace governmental emergency management authority.

**Local data sovereignty:**
- Data generated in tribal territory is owned by the tribal nation
- Data sharing outside the federation requires explicit tribal consent
- No commercial use of community data without community approval
- No retention of imagery data from tribal lands beyond operational necessity

**The colonial trust deficit:**
The cooperative's founding documents explicitly name the history: technology transfer programs involving Indigenous communities have repeatedly created dependency rather than capacity. Promises of partnership have been followed by extraction. The Ring of Fire cooperative's governance is designed with this history in mind. Every governance mechanism is anti-extractive by design: distributed ownership, local data control, transparent AI, community override authority.

## 6. Cooperative Legal Structure

**Washington State cooperative law:**
The Ring of Fire Trade Network is organized under Washington State cooperative law:
- **RCW 23.86:** Washington Cooperative Association Act (general cooperatives)
- **RCW 24.06:** Washington Nonprofit Corporation Act (for non-profit cooperative structures)

**Tribal membership structure:**
Tribal nations are members of the cooperative with ownership stakes. The cooperative structure is designed to accommodate:
- Tribal sovereignty (tribal members maintain their governmental status and treaty rights)
- Multiple states and Canadian provinces (the Ring of Fire spans the Pacific Northwest, Alaska, British Columbia, and beyond)
- Individual members (residents of cooperative communities who are not tribal members can participate at defined membership levels)

**Why cooperative, not corporation:**
A corporation's structure optimizes for return to shareholders. A cooperative's structure optimizes for return to members. In a standard corporation, if the Ring of Fire network becomes highly valuable, an outside investor can acquire a controlling stake and restructure the cooperative's priorities. In a cooperative, ownership is distributed among members; acquisition of control requires buying out the majority of members individually.

**Individual investors participate without capturing:**
The cooperative structure allows individual investors to purchase non-controlling stakes. Investors receive a defined financial return; they do not receive governance rights that would allow them to direct cooperative priorities. The financial return is capped at a defined percentage. Value above the cap returns to members.

## 7. Funding Strategy: Six Programs Plus BRIC Contingency

**The multi-program integration:**

| Program | Funder | Amount Range | Use |
|---------|--------|-------------|-----|
| FEMA BRIC | FEMA (75/25 cost share, 90/10 for impoverished) | $1M-$50M per project | Early warning infrastructure, vertical evacuation |
| NOAA Community Resilience Grants | NOAA | $500K-$5M | Coastal hazard monitoring, sensor deployment |
| BIA Tribal Climate Resilience | BIA | Up to $3M | Tribal community adaptation planning and implementation |
| EDA Economic Development Grants | EDA | $500K-$10M | Workforce development infrastructure, co-op formation |
| SBA 8(a) Business Development | SBA | Set-aside contracts | Tribal small business participation in federal contracting |
| HUD CDBG | HUD | $500K-$5M | Community development in low- and moderate-income areas |

**The BRIC contingency:**
FEMA attempted to terminate the Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC) program in April 2025. A federal court ordered restoration of the program in December 2025. As of March 2026, enforcement is ongoing. The cooperative's funding strategy treats BRIC as available but uncertain -- all projects budgeted to proceed with alternative funding combinations if BRIC is again disrupted.

**Alternative funding pathways:**
If BRIC is unavailable:
- NOAA + BIA + EDA can cover early warning and workforce components
- State hazard mitigation programs (Washington, Oregon, California, Alaska) provide additional capacity
- Tribal Energy Resource Agreement (TERA) funding for energy-related infrastructure (connection to HGE)

## 8. FEMA BRIC Legal Status

**The timeline:**
- **April 2025:** FEMA announced termination of BRIC program, which provided $1.5 billion annually for pre-disaster mitigation
- **December 11, 2025:** Federal court issued order prohibiting FEMA from terminating BRIC
- **March 6, 2026:** Enforcement order set specific deadlines for FEMA compliance with the restoration order
- **Status as of March 2026:** BRIC is legally required to operate; enforcement ongoing

**Source:** Congressional Research Service (IN12609); WA Military Department; FEMA official correspondence.

**Why BRIC matters:**
BRIC is the primary federal program for pre-disaster mitigation infrastructure -- the category that includes:
- Seismic retrofit of critical facilities
- Vertical evacuation structures
- Early warning system expansion
- Community resilience planning

Without BRIC, these investments require alternative funding at significantly higher local cost share. The court order preserving BRIC is a direct win for the Ring of Fire cooperative's funding strategy.

**Tribal priority:**
Under BRIC, tribal governments may apply directly as applicants (not through state applications). This is significant: tribal nations do not need state government intermediation to access federal disaster mitigation funding. The cooperative's tribal member governments can apply independently and receive direct funding.
