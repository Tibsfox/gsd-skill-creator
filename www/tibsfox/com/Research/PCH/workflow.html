Every discipline we currently associate with “senior engineering” — designing before coding, desk-checking, reading stack traces carefully, keeping changes minimal, never touching code you do not understand — was forged in the punch card era because economics made anything else impossible.

A Day in the Life — IBM Poughkeepsie, 1965

Picture a programmer named David. He works at IBM in Poughkeepsie, New York. He is 28. He has been programming for four years, which makes him one of the more experienced people on his floor. He works on a System/360 Model 40, announced April 1964. He writes FORTRAN IV for scientific customers and COBOL for commercial ones. On a good day he will also write some System/360 assembler.

9:00 AM — The Desk

David arrives at 8:45. He sharpens two pencils, lays out a fresh pad of coding sheets, and opens his notebook. The page is dense with handwritten notes: a flowchart sketched in the margins, a list of variable names with their meanings, and a list of test cases with expected outputs.

He does not turn on a computer. There is no computer at his desk. The machine is on the other side of the building, behind a glass wall, operated by men in white short-sleeved shirts and narrow ties. David will not touch it today. He will not touch it most days.

9:30 AM — The Design

Before he writes any code, he sketches the logic on scratch paper. He is going to use Zeller’s congruence. He works three test cases by hand: January 1, 1900; December 31, 1999; and his own birthday. He gets the right answer each time. He writes the test cases into his notebook so he can verify the program output against them when the run comes back.

He sketches a flowchart using the standard IBM Flowcharting Template X20-8020: the plastic stencil with the standard shapes for process, decision, I/O, terminator. Every programmer at IBM had one on their desk.

10:30 AM — The Code

He writes the FORTRAN code on the coding sheet, one statement per line, all uppercase, with columns 1-5 for statement labels, column 6 for continuation, columns 7-72 for the statement body, and columns 73-80 for sequence numbers. He writes in pencil because he will erase.

C     CONVERT JULIAN DAY TO GREGORIAN DATE
      SUBROUTINE JUL2GR (JUL, MONTH, IDAY, IYEAR)
      INTEGER JUL, MONTH, IDAY, IYEAR
      INTEGER A, B, C, D, E, MM
C
      A = JUL + 32044
      B = (4 * A + 3) / 146097
      C = A - (146097 * B) / 4
      D = (4 * C + 3) / 1461
      E = C - (1461 * D) / 4
      MM = (5 * E + 2) / 153
C
      IDAY  = E - (153 * MM + 2) / 5 + 1
      MONTH = MM + 3 - 12 * (MM / 10)
      IYEAR = 100 * B + D - 4800 + MM / 10
      RETURN
      END

11:45 AM — Desk Check

Before anything touches a keypunch, David plays computer. He takes his test cases and traces each statement by hand, writing variable values in a table in his notebook. January 1, 1900 = Julian day 2415021. He walks through JUL2GR with A = 2447065, computes B, C, D, E, MM, and verifies the output is 1, 1, 1900. It is. He checks his birthday. It is.

This is work. This is the most important work of the day. David has spent nearly three hours designing, coding, and desk-checking. He has not yet touched a card. He has not yet used a single minute of machine time. And when his deck does run, there is a good chance it will work on the first try — because the design phase was treated as real engineering, not pre-work.

1:00 PM — The Keypunch Room

After lunch, David takes his coding sheet to the keypunch room. He prefers to punch his own cards rather than leave them with the keypunch pool. The pool operators are fast (8,000-12,000 keystrokes per hour) but they can’t catch logic errors that David would notice while punching.

He sits at an IBM 029 keypunch. The machine is roughly the size of a desk, with a keyboard, a card hopper at the upper right, a punching station, and an output stacker at the lower left. He loads a stack of blank IBM 5081 cards into the hopper. He feeds one card to the punching station. He types C CONVERT JULIAN DAY TO GREGORIAN DATE. Each keystroke produces a mechanical thunk as the die punches a hole. The character is printed along the top of the card so humans can read it. The card advances one column per keystroke.

Typo? There is no backspace. You throw the bad card in the reject bin, take a fresh one, use the duplicate function to copy the good characters from the bad card, and retype from the error point.

2:30 PM — The Deck

David has punched 47 cards. He puts them in order, checks the sequence numbers in columns 73-80, and wraps them with a rubber band. He writes his name and job number on a blank header card and places it on top. He carries the deck to the submission window.

2:45 PM — Submission

At the submission window, an operator takes David’s deck, logs the job in a ledger, and tells him to check back after 5 PM. David’s job will run when the machine has capacity — which is to say, when the higher-priority production jobs have finished. He leaves the deck with the operator and goes back to his desk to work on another program.

5:30 PM — The Printout

David returns to the output room. His printout is on the shelf, a fanfold of green-bar paper with his deck clipped to the top. He carries it to a reading table. The first page is the JCL echo. The second page is the source listing. The third page is compiler output. If there were compile errors, they would be marked with asterisks in the left margin and error codes in the right margin.

Today, the compile is clean. The fourth page is the runtime output. He scans for the three test cases:

 JUL =  2415021    MONTH =   1   DAY =   1   YEAR = 1900
 JUL =  2451544    MONTH =  12   DAY =  31   YEAR = 1999
 JUL =  2438761    MONTH =   4   DAY =  23   YEAR = 1965

They match his hand calculations. David goes home.

The economic tradeoff: machine time was expensive but programmer time was also expensive, AND programmer time was the slower resource. The whole workflow was optimized to minimize machine time at the expense of programmer time — because machines couldn’t wait, but programmers could.

The Keypunch Room — Physical Layer

A dozen IBM 029 keypunches in a row. Almost all operators were women. Keypunch operators earned less than programmers and mainframe operators, and the work was repetitive and physically demanding. The top operators could sustain 8,000-12,000 keystrokes per hour — roughly two cards per minute at sustained pace. The noise was constant: the mechanical thunk of dies punching holes, the whir of cards advancing, the chatter of typebars printing characters along the top.

The 029 was introduced in 1964 as a System/360 peripheral. It punched EBCDIC (Extended Binary Coded Decimal Interchange Code). Its predecessor, the 026, punched BCD (Binary Coded Decimal). These were incompatible. A deck punched on a 026 might fail on a System/360 reader. Programmers learned to check which keypunch they were using.

JCL — Job Control Language — specified the job, the resources needed, the programs to run, the files to attach. It was baroque and famously error-prone. IBM published the green card (System/360 Reference Data card, form X20-1703), a folded plastic reference every mainframe programmer kept in their wallet. It listed JCL syntax, instruction codes, completion codes, ASCII/EBCDIC conversion tables.

The Examine Phase — Reading the Printout

When David picks up his printout, he reads it in a specific order. Every experienced programmer had the same habit:

  1. JCL echo. Did the job actually run? Did the JCL parse?
  2. Condition codes. Each step printed a condition code. 0000 is success. Anything else needs investigation.
  3. Compile output. Look for asterisks in the margin, error codes like IEY0141I, severity levels.
  4. Runtime output. Does the program produce the expected results?
  5. Completion code. 0000 is normal. 0C1 is an operation exception. 0C4 is a protection exception. 0C7 is a data exception (bad decimal arithmetic). 322 is a timeout.
  6. Core dump. If there was a core dump, it’s pages of hex. Skilled programmers could read a core dump and identify the bug by matching the crash address to the compiler listing and reconstructing what went wrong.

MVP Before It Had a Name

Eric Ries wrote The Lean Startup in 2011. But the concept of the Minimum Viable Product was forced on punch card programmers by economics fifty years earlier. You didn’t write a 5000-line program all at once. You wrote a 50-line skeleton, submitted it, saw it compile, added a subroutine, submitted it, saw it run, added real data, submitted it. Incremental development wasn’t a methodology — it was survival.

The rules were:

  • Small decks. A 100-card program was manageable. A 1000-card program was risky. A 10,000-card program was a project.
  • One bug per run. You couldn’t debug ten things at once. You fixed one bug, resubmitted, verified the fix, then fixed the next one.
  • Skeleton first. Your first submission was the smallest thing that could possibly run. Test the skeleton. Then add one feature. Then another.
  • Chesterton’s Fence. Never remove code you don’t understand. The cost of accidentally breaking something was too high.
  • Backup decks. Keep the last working version. If a change breaks things, you can fall back.

Brooks’s Law — Grounded in the Physical Workflow

Fred Brooks’s The Mythical Man-Month (1975) codified the hard-won lessons of this era. His most famous law: “Adding manpower to a late software project makes it later.” This wasn’t abstract management theory. It came directly from OS/360 development, where adding programmers meant more people sharing the same keypunch room, the same submission window, the same overnight turnaround. Coordination overhead grew faster than productivity.

What Was Lost

  • The discipline of desk-checking.
  • The economic pressure to design before coding.
  • The focus that came from expensive iteration.
  • The skill of reading a core dump.
  • The habit of keeping changes minimal.
  • The understanding of the whole program.

What Was Gained

  • Speed of iteration.
  • Accessibility — anyone with a terminal could program.
  • Exploratory development — try things and see.
  • The rise of scripting languages and REPLs.
  • The interactive computing paradigm itself.

Many older programmers felt the new interactivity made younger programmers sloppy. They were not wrong. But the tradeoff was real, and for most purposes the gain in accessibility outweighed the loss in discipline. The exception is now: when you are orchestrating AI agents under token budgets, the old discipline is exactly what you need again.
