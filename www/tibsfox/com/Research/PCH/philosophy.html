Igor Stravinsky, Poetics of Music (1942):
“The more constraints one imposes, the more one frees one’s self. And the arbitrariness of the constraint serves only to obtain precision of execution.”
He was talking about composing for a chamber ensemble. He could have been talking about writing FORTRAN for an IBM 1401. He could have been talking, today, about prompting a language model inside a 200K-token budget. The form changes. The insight does not.

Prologue: The Cost of a Thought

In 1965, a computer science student at the University of Michigan carried a rubber-banded stack of IBM cards down a long linoleum hallway. The deck was perhaps 200 cards thick. It represented roughly three weeks of thinking. She handed it to the window clerk at the Computing Center. The clerk logged it. The deck would ride a cart to the operator, who would feed it into a reader. She would return the next morning, or the next afternoon, or perhaps after lunch on Thursday.

Every run cost money. Eight dollars in 1965. Nineteen dollars in 2025-equivalent terms. Per attempt. She could not afford to be careless. Nobody could. And so — because nobody could — everybody got careful. The carelessness we now consider normal had not yet been invented.

The Creative Paradox

Consider the canvas problem. Give an untrained artist an infinite canvas, infinite paints, infinite subjects, and infinite time. What do they produce? Usually nothing. They freeze. The possibility space is too large. Now give a first-grader a 16×16 pixel grid and eight colors. They will fill it. They will fill twenty of them before lunch. The medium conspires to narrow the choices to a size the mind can hold.

The Commodore 64 had exactly the same effect on a generation of programmers. 64KB of memory. 16 colors. Three sound channels. Eight sprites. These were not limitations to transcend — they were the game itself. Working around them, exploiting them, discovering that you could force the video chip to display more than 16 colors by changing the palette mid-frame — this was the art. The constraint defined the medium.

Haiku works this way. So does the sonnet, the villanelle, the blues. Every durable poetic form is a set of constraints that turn raw feeling into something you can hold. A poem with no rules is a diary entry. A poem with rules is a poem.

The Economics of Thought

The brain runs on cheap calories. The machine runs on expensive electrons. In the punch card era, the exchange rate between them was steeply in favor of the brain, and the programmer who exploited that arbitrage ate well.

Desk-checking a subroutine for forty minutes — tracing the logic by hand, verifying the DO loop terminates, verifying the accumulator is initialized, verifying the format statement matches the I/O list — consumed perhaps one watt of biological compute. The IBM 360 running the same trace was consuming perhaps 60,000 watts and billing out at $300/hour of wall-clock time. Two minutes of operator thought saved an hour of machine time. The ratio was so lopsided that any programmer who submitted sloppy decks was, in a direct economic sense, stealing from the institution.

Fast forward. In 2015, a junior developer on a modern laptop can run a test suite in twelve seconds, and will run it forty times in an afternoon because the cost of each run is zero. Thinking became expensive relative to running. Trying became the cheapest way to learn. And so trying replaced thinking — not because thinking stopped working, but because trying worked well enough and nothing punished the developer for skipping the harder path.

Slow is Smooth, Smooth is Fast

The Navy SEAL marksmanship adage captures something punch card programmers understood in their skeletons. The fastest way to hit a target is not the fastest trigger pull — it is the cleanest trigger pull. Hurry produces flinch, and flinch produces misses, and misses cost more than the time saved by hurrying.

A programmer forced to iterate slowly under a six-hour turnaround learns to make every iteration count. She doesn’t submit a deck to “see what happens” because seeing what happens costs her a day. She submits a deck when she is reasonably sure. And the habit of being reasonably sure — of knowing why you believe your code will work before you run it — makes you faster, not slower, over any time horizon longer than a single session.

A punch card programmer who submitted three decks in a day and converged on a correct program often finished ahead of a modern programmer who ran the test suite forty times and was still chasing a regression at the end of the afternoon. Each deck submission was a careful step. Each test run was a blind stagger.

Desk-Checking as Primary Skill

Desk-checking is reading code line by line, tracing execution mentally, maintaining variable values on paper or in your head, and verifying that the program does what it should. This is a lost skill. Not rare — lost.

Ask a modern programmer to predict the output of a 50-line function containing two loops and a conditional, without running it. Most will balk. They will say “let me just run it and see.” They mean this sincerely; they are not being lazy. They genuinely cannot hold the variable state in their head well enough to trace execution. The muscle has atrophied because it was never built.

The skill can be recovered. The practice looks like this: read a function, predict what it will return for a given input, then run it and check. Keep doing this until your predictions are usually right. Then read longer functions. Then read whole files. Then read code you didn’t write. After a few months of this, something clicks. You start to see the execution when you read the code. The letters on the page become state transitions. The program runs in your mind.

This is what senior programmers mean when they say they can “read code.” They are not reading; they are executing. They are running the program on wetware. And that skill, more than any other, is the ceiling on what problems you can solve — because problems that are too big to fit on wetware are problems you cannot reason about, only grope toward.

Holding the Whole Program in Mind

Fred Brooks, in The Mythical Man-Month (1975), observed that the maximum complexity of a program is limited by the cognitive capacity of the programmers who must hold it in their heads. He was writing about the OS/360 project, the largest software system ever attempted. The problem was not that the programmers were not smart enough. It was that no single mind could hold the whole system, and the coordination overhead between minds grew faster than the productivity gained by adding them.

In 1965, a capable programmer could hold a 1,000-line FORTRAN program in her head. Not memorized character-for-character — comprehended. She knew what every subroutine did, how the data flowed, which variables were live where, which invariants held, which edge cases were handled. She could navigate the program by thought alone, and when a bug was reported, her first move was not to run a debugger — it was to ask her mind where could this have gone wrong? and nine times out of ten the mind would supply the answer.

This is how the Apollo flight software got written. This is how the original Unix kernel got written. This is how most of the foundational software of the twentieth century got written. The programmers held the whole thing in their heads, because the whole thing fit, because the constraints forced the whole thing to be small enough to fit.

The Habits That Plugged the Leaks

In a long debugging session, the enemy is not stupidity. It is cognitive load leakage — the slow seepage of details out of working memory. Punch card programmers could not afford leaks. They developed habits:

  • Comments explaining why, not what. The what is in the code. The why is in the head and will disappear the moment the head moves to the next problem. Good comments said things like /* bubble sort here because the array is almost always already sorted */ — explanations that rescued future-self from re-deriving the design decision.
  • Clear variable names. Variables named x1, x2, x3 forced the reader to hold the mapping in working memory. Variables named head_count, total_weight, current_index carried their meaning with them.
  • Consistent formatting. A program that looked messy took extra cognitive cycles to read, and cognitive cycles were the whole budget.
  • Careful layout. Related code grouped together. The printout was a physical document — the layout determined how fast you could find things.

None of this was mandated by a style guide. There were no style guides in the modern sense. These habits were forged by the economic pressure of expensive reading. When reading your own code tomorrow would cost you a day of turnaround if you got confused, you invested today in making the code un-confusing.

The Ethos of First Do No Harm

There is a Hippocratic Oath for programmers, though nobody has written it down: when a program is working, touch it carefully.

Punch card programmers touched working programs with great reverence. Every modification was planned, reviewed, and rehearsed. You would not change a working subroutine to “try something.” You would analyze the current behavior, design the new behavior, write out the change on paper, walk through the consequences mentally, and only then punch new cards. You would always punch a replacement card rather than modifying the working deck in-place, so that if the change did not work you could reconstitute the original by swapping cards.

The modern programmer, protected by git, refactors aggressively. This is a genuine engineering advance. But aggressive refactoring carries a cost: the programmer loses the habit of reverence for working code. She treats working code as a starting point rather than an achievement. Code written in a reverent culture is different from code written in a permissive one. Reverent code is more stable, more considered, more finished. Permissive code is more lively, more speculative, more in-progress.

A balanced engineer recognizes when each is appropriate: revere the code that has earned reverence, and refactor the code that hasn’t. Knowing which is which is a skill that has to be learned, and the punch card era taught it automatically.

The Fast Feedback Fallacy

Fast feedback is good. Fast feedback that replaces thinking is not. The distinction is subtle, and nobody teaches it explicitly, and so an entire generation of programmers has grown up conflating the two.

Interactive terminals and REPLs removed the punishment for sloppy iteration. Suddenly you could try things — type a line, see what happened, fix it, try again. This was liberating for beginners and experts alike. But it was also, in a subtle way, corrosive to the discipline the old workflow had enforced.

In the hands of a programmer without the discipline of desk-checking, the REPL becomes a crutch: a way to substitute trial-and-error for reasoning, to grope toward a correct expression without ever understanding why the incorrect ones were incorrect. Over time, this produces a programmer who cannot predict behavior without running code — and who is therefore helpless when the code is too expensive or too slow or too dangerous to run.

The Abstraction Tax

Modern programming is built on a tower of abstractions. A typical web app in 2026 passes through: browser DOM, JavaScript runtime, rendering engine, React framework, component library, application code, HTTP/2 stack, TLS layer, network stack, operating system, microcode. Each layer is a convenience. Each layer removes a little bit of understanding from the programmer above.

The punch card programmer had no abstraction tower. She had the machine, the operating system, the compiler, and her program. Four things. When her FORTRAN program produced unexpected output, she could ask: is it my code? A compiler bug? An operating system quirk? A hardware glitch? And she could investigate each of those herself, because each was in her grasp.

The modern programmer confronting an unexpected bug has to triage through dozens of layers, most of which are black boxes. Abstractions pay their own tax. They accelerate the common case, and they render the uncommon case nearly impossible to debug. The punch card era had less abstraction, therefore less convenience, therefore less mystery. When you hit a wall, you could see the wall.

The Skill Atrophy Cycle

Here is the sad arithmetic of abundance. As feedback gets faster, the skill of predicting program behavior atrophies. The brain cuts metabolic investment in muscles it does not use. Over a career, the modern programmer loses the ability to reason about unrun code, because she never has to. She is fast in familiar territory and helpless in unfamiliar territory.

The cycle is self-reinforcing. Each generation of tools makes feedback faster. Each generation of programmers thinks a little less. Each generation of tools has to compensate by offering more features that do the thinking for the programmer. There is no villain here. No conspiracy. Just the gradient of incentives.

The skill atrophy cycle is not irreversible. A programmer who notices it can arrest it by deliberately choosing harder problems, refusing to run the code until she has predicted its behavior, and practicing the old disciplines even though nothing forces her to. Some do. Most don’t. And so the baseline drifts.

The only hope for the baseline is external constraint. Something that forces thinking back into the loop. Something that makes careless running expensive again. Something like — and here we arrive at the core argument — the token budget of an AI agent.

The Return of Constraint

Token budgets are the new punch cards. They are the new forcing function. They are the new economic constraint that separates careful operators from careless ones, and they are already producing, in a small community of engineers who have learned to use them well, a renaissance of the old disciplines.

Consider the mental posture of an engineer running an AI agent well. She does not say “go figure out this problem.” She writes a specification. She reads it back to herself. She trims it. She identifies which files the agent will need. She defines success criteria. She designs the task as a sequence of small verifiable steps. She thinks about context management. She thinks about tool budgets.

Every one of these habits is a direct transplant from the punch card era. The specification is the coding pad. The trimming is the desk-check. The file identification is the deck preparation. The success criteria are the expected output. The step decomposition is the incremental submission. The context management is the memory budget. The tool budget is the compute budget. She is, without being told, running the same workflow as a 1965 programmer. She has reinvented it from first principles, because the economics reinvented her problem.

Closing Meditation

The punch card era was not a primitive phase we outgrew. It was a regime of constraints that produced a kind of craft that abundance destroyed. Now, in the age of AI, constraints are back. Not the same constraints, but analogous ones. The programmer of 2026 working with AI agents has more in common with the programmer of 1965 working with card decks than with the programmer of 2015 working with unlimited cloud compute.

The lesson is not “go back to the old ways.” The lesson is: when abundance seduces you into sloppiness, remember that constraint is what made the original work possible. Honor the constraint. Design before you act. Think before you submit. Read what comes back. Learn. Improve. The loop is eternal, even if the hardware changes.
