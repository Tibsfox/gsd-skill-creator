Before there was a blinking cursor, there was a stack of cards. Before there was a filesystem, there was a tray. Before there was an operating system scheduling jobs, there was a human being with a grease pencil and a clipboard, logging your deck into a queue.

Herman Hollerith and the 1890 Census

In 1880, the United States Census Bureau counted 50,189,209 people by hand. The count took roughly eight years to process. By the time the 1880 results were published, population growth had made parts of the tabulation obsolete. Congress was told that the 1890 census would take so long that the 1900 census would begin before the 1890 numbers were finished. The Constitution requires a decennial census. The mathematics of population growth were threatening to overtake the mathematics of clerical labor.

Herman Hollerith, born February 29, 1860, in Buffalo, New York, was a 20-year-old Columbia School of Mines graduate when he joined the 1880 census as a special agent. He watched rooms full of clerks stroke tally marks for eight years. The scene stuck with him.

The breakthrough came, by Hollerith’s own account, from watching a railroad conductor. Conductors used a system called a “punch photograph” to prevent ticket fraud: holes punched in positions corresponding to the passenger’s height, hair color, eye color. If the ticket was later reused by someone else, the punched photograph would not match. Hollerith realized: if you could encode a person’s attributes as holes in paper, you could encode anything.

Hollerith patented his first electromechanical tabulator in 1884. In 1889, the Census Bureau held a competition among three candidate systems. Hollerith’s machine processed a trial set of data in about 5.5 hours. Its nearest competitor took 44 hours. Hollerith won. The 1890 census was processed in approximately 6 months, not 8 years. Hollerith founded the Tabulating Machine Company in 1896. It became, through mergers, International Business Machines (IBM) in 1924.

The Hollerith Code

Characters were encoded as combinations of zone punches (rows 12, 11, 0) and digit punches (rows 1-9):

Character RangeEncoding
Digits 0-9Single punch in corresponding row
Letters A-IZone 12 + digits 1-9 (A = 12+1, B = 12+2, … I = 12+9)
Letters J-RZone 11 + digits 1-9 (J = 11+1, … R = 11+9)
Letters S-ZZone 0 + digits 2-9 (S = 0+2, … Z = 0+9)
Special charactersMulti-punch combinations. Period = 12+3+8. Dollar sign = 11+3+8.

Why EBCDIC has gaps between I/J and R/S: the alphabet is not a simple sequence in the card. The gaps are exactly where the zone punch changes. EBCDIC is Hollerith with eight-bit clothes on. Why FORTRAN wrote .GE. instead of >=: the > symbol was not reliably portable between 026 and 029 keypunches — they had different special-character encodings.

The drum card (program card): wrapped around a removable drum on the left side of the 026/029, it told the machine how to treat each column — which were alphabetic fields, which were numeric, which should auto-skip, which should auto-duplicate. A well-programmed drum card could reduce keystrokes by 60% on repetitive data entry.

Capacity: A skilled 026 operator could sustain 8,000-10,000 keystrokes per hour, roughly 100-150 cards per hour on dense alphanumeric data. A keypunch pool running three shifts could punch 60,000-90,000 cards per 8-hour shift.

The Verifiers

IBM 056 (1949), 059 (1964). Looked identical to keypunches but compared each key press against the existing punches. If they matched, the card advanced. If they didn’t, the machine locked up and the red light came on. The OK notch was cut in the right edge of the card after successful verification — you could inspect a whole deck’s verification status by looking at the edge, which formed a continuous groove for verified cards.

The radix sort: sorting a deck on a multi-column field required passes from rightmost (least significant) to leftmost column. A 5-digit employee number = 5 passes. A 20-column name field = 40 passes (alphabetic requires two passes per column: zone, then digit). Sorting a 10,000-card deck on a 20-column name field could take two hours of machine time. This is why radix sort is studied in computer science: it was the sort the data processing profession used daily.

The Collators

IBM 085, 087, 088. Two card hoppers (primary + secondary feeds), two reading stations, four or five output pockets, a comparison unit, and a plugboard. The original database JOIN.

A typical match-merge job: given a master file deck (sorted by account number) and a transaction file deck (also sorted by account number), merge the transactions into their proper positions:

  • Master card = transaction card → pocket 1 (matched records)
  • Master card < transaction card → pocket 2 (unmatched master)
  • Master card > transaction card → pocket 3 (unmatched transaction, possibly an error)

The Accounting Machine — The Forerunner of the Computer

IBM 402, 403, 407. Read cards, did arithmetic, printed reports. Programmed by wiring a plugboard — a removable wire-wrap panel that specified what to do with each column of each card. A different kind of programming entirely: physical, tactile, visual. Experienced accounting machine operators could diagnose program problems by looking at the plugboard wiring, tracing the paths with a finger.

The Data Center

The physical layout. Raised floors for cables. The keypunch room (noisy). The operator console. Tape drives. Card readers in a line. The line printer at the end. Fluorescent lighting. Heavy air conditioning required to cool everything.

The noise. Card readers clacking at 1000 cards per minute. Printers hammering at 1100 lines per minute. Tape drives spinning. The hum of the air conditioning. In the sorter room, the 084’s 2000-card-per-minute chatter was deafening. OSHA didn’t require hearing protection until the 1970s.

The smell. Punched paper dust. Machine oil. Ozone from relay contacts. Warm rubber from pinch rollers. Overheated tubes in the earliest machines.

The Operators and the Keypunch Room

Operators were not programmers. They loaded card decks into readers, mounted tapes, handled paper jams, delivered printouts. Shift work — three shifts to keep the machine running 24/7. The computer operator wore a white short-sleeved shirt and narrow tie, and stood in front of the operator console, watching lights and responding to messages.

Women in the data center. Keypunch operators were almost entirely women. So were many of the earliest programmers: the WRENs during WWII working on Colossus, Grace Hopper on UNIVAC, the ENIAC programmers (Kay McNulty, Betty Jennings, Betty Snyder, Marlyn Wescoff, Fran Bilas, Ruth Lichterman). The gender history of early computing is the history of skilled women whose contributions were erased when the field became prestigious. Any honest account of the punch card era has to acknowledge this.

Rubber bands: thick, flat bands were universal. Wrapped around a deck in both directions (long way and short way) for transit. They dried out and snapped. Experienced operators kept a stash in a desk drawer.

Tray 9: the reject pocket on the sorter. Cards that didn’t match any expected punch pattern fell here. Smart operators checked tray 9 after every pass — it was where the anomalies lived.

Blowing Your Stack — A Literal Event

—— BLOWING YOUR STACK ——
The vacuum belt in the card reader fails. The 2540 ejects 600 cards across the room in two seconds.
You spend the next hour sorting them back into order by the sequence numbers in columns 73-80.
The idiom you know? This is where it came from.

Card readers used vacuum belts and pinch rollers to feed cards. When a belt failed or a card caught a rough edge, the output stacker could reject cards violently. Hundreds flying across the room. The deck you spent all morning preparing turned into scattered paper on a linoleum floor.

The sequence numbers in columns 73-80 existed precisely because this happened. If you dropped a deck or the reader blew it, you could run the cards through a sorter and get them back in order. This was not a backup system. This was an essential part of normal operations. Programmers numbered their cards because they knew, with certainty, that cards would eventually scatter. The idiom “blowing your stack” entered the English language from here.

Hollerith, Jacquard, and the Deeper History

Punch cards didn’t begin with Hollerith. Jacquard looms (1804) used punched cards to control weaving patterns. Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine (1830s-1840s) was designed to use punched cards — a direct inheritance from Jacquard. Ada Lovelace wrote about them in her notes on the Analytical Engine. The lineage of programmable machines driven by holes in paper goes back two centuries.

Forests were dedicated to card stock. At 1960s prices, the cost of the cards for a large installation could exceed the cost of the computer itself over several years. The DOJ brought antitrust cases against IBM in 1952 and 1956 specifically over the card tying — IBM required customers who rented its machines to buy IBM cards as well. Cards were IBM’s cash cow.

The Decline and Legacy

The end came quickly. CTSS (1961) introduced time-sharing. TSO (1971) brought terminal editing to System/370. VM/CMS (1972). VMS (1977). The VT100 (1978). The IBM PC (1981). By 1985, punch cards were mostly gone from new installations.

Some holdouts remained: the IRS ran punch card workflows into the 1990s. Some utility billing systems. Academic archives. And infamously, the Florida 2000 presidential election — where Votomatic punch card voting machines produced the “hanging chad” crisis that decided the George W. Bush vs. Al Gore race. The last major public appearance of punch cards was as a cautionary tale.

Today the cards themselves are museum pieces. The Computer History Museum in Mountain View has working keypunches, card readers, and decks on display. IBM’s archives preserve the machines. Blank IBM 5081 cards are still available from specialty vendors for hobbyists, restoration projects, and the occasional art installation.
