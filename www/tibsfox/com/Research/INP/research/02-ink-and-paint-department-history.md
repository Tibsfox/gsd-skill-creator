# The Ink and Paint Department -- Human Labor History

> **Domain:** Social History, Labor History, Animation Studies
> **Module:** 2 -- The Invisible Workforce Behind Every Frame
> **Through-line:** *The credits never named them. The histories rarely mentioned them. But every frame of every hand-painted cel -- over 100,000 per feature film -- passed through their hands.* The ink and paint department was overwhelmingly female, systematically uncredited, and absolutely essential. This is the labor history of the women who translated pencil sketches into the projected color that audiences saw on screen.

## 1. The Gender Division

From the 1920s through the 1960s, the animation industry maintained strict gender segregation. Women were almost entirely restricted to ink and paint departments at all major studios -- Disney, Warner Bros., MGM, Fleischer. The division was explicit, documented, and enforced [1].

### Documentary Evidence

A 1938 letter from the Walt Disney Studio to a female applicant stated directly: "Women do not do any of the creative work in connection with preparing the cartoons for the screen, as that work is performed entirely by young men." This letter, preserved in the Walt Disney Family Museum archive, is the most frequently cited primary source for the studio's gender policy [2].

### Wage Disparities

The compensation gap was stark:

| Role | Typical Weekly Wage (1930s--1940s) | Gender |
|------|-----------------------------------|--------|
| Ink and paint worker (entry) | $18--$20/week | Female |
| Experienced painter | $22--$28/week | Female |
| Head inker | $30--$35/week | Female |
| Inbetween animator | $35--$50/week | Male |
| Key animator | $100--$300/week | Male |

The lowest-paid male position in the animation department typically earned more than the highest-paid female position in ink and paint. This disparity persisted with minor adjustments from the 1930s through the 1960s [3].

### Spatial Segregation

The ink and paint department was physically separated from the animation departments at most studios. At Disney's Hyperion Avenue studio (1926--1939) and later at the Burbank studio (1940--present), ink and paint occupied its own wing. Workers in the department had limited contact with the animators whose drawings they brought to life. As Phyllis Craig recalled, "the girls were pretty much tunnel-visioned" -- the department's isolation made lateral movement within the company extremely difficult [4].

## 2. Department Structure and Hierarchy

### Internal Ranking

The ink and paint department had its own internal hierarchy, with clear distinctions in skill, status, and compensation:

```
INK AND PAINT DEPARTMENT HIERARCHY
================================================================

  DEPARTMENT HEAD
    |-- Supervised all operations
    |-- Liaised with animation supervisors
    |-- Managed palette decisions for each production
    |
  INKERS (higher status)
    |-- Head Inker (supervised ink quality)
    |-- Senior Inkers (complex characters, hero shots)
    |-- Junior Inkers (simpler characters, effects)
    |
  PAINTERS (lower status)
    |-- Head Painter (supervised color consistency)
    |-- Senior Painters (complex multi-color characters)
    |-- Junior Painters (backgrounds, fills, effects)
    |
  CHECKERS
    |-- Verified paint coverage and registration
    |-- Caught errors before camera department
    |
  PAINT LAB (Disney only)
    |-- Mixed proprietary pigments
    |-- Maintained palette continuity across production
```

### Quality Control

The checking stage was critical. A missed spot of unpainted cel, a smudge, or a registration error would be visible on screen. Checkers examined every cel under magnification and backlighting, rejecting any cel that did not meet the department's standards. Rejection rates varied by studio and production but typically ranged from 5% to 15% [5].

## 3. The Art of Inking

### The Inker's Craft

Inking was the most skilled position in the department. The inker's job was to trace the animator's pencil drawing onto the front surface of a transparent cel, using the finest available nibs -- typically the **Gillott 290**, a flexible steel nib capable of producing lines ranging from hairline-thin to bold, depending on pressure [6].

### What Made Inking Difficult

The inker was not simply tracing. She was interpreting the animator's intent -- capturing not just the line but the energy in the stroke. A line that swelled at the beginning and tapered at the end expressed movement differently from a uniform line. The inker had to understand what the animator meant, not just what the pencil did.

### Physical Requirements

- Workers had to move through the department halls softly to avoid causing vibrations that would produce wavy lines
- Ambient temperature and humidity affected ink flow and drying time
- Each inker maintained her own set of nibs, broken in to her hand pressure and angle
- A single cel with a complex character (multiple limbs, flowing hair, facial detail) could take 15--30 minutes of continuous inking

```
INKING WORKFLOW -- SINGLE CEL
================================================================

  1. Animator's pencil drawing placed on lightbox pegs
  2. Blank cel placed over drawing on same pegs
  3. Inker traces outline using fine-nib pen (front of cel)
     |
     |-- PRIMARY LINES: Character outline (black ink)
     |-- SECONDARY LINES: Internal detail, folds, features
     |-- COLORED LINES: Where outline meets skin/clothing
     |     (colored ink reduces harsh contrast at edges)
     |
  4. Cel removed; checked for completeness and accuracy
  5. Passed to painting department for reverse-side color
```

## 4. The Craft of Painting

### Reverse-Side Application

Painting (also called "opaquing") was applied to the reverse side of the cel -- the side away from the camera. This technique allowed the ink lines on the front surface to remain sharp and unobscured by paint. The paint layers built up behind the lines, producing the characteristic flat color fields of cel animation [7].

### Color Knowledge

Each painter specialized in a small number of colors -- typically two to four -- building deep expertise in the behavior of those specific pigments:

- Which colors bled when adjacent to each other
- Which pigments dried at different rates, causing overlap problems
- Which colors appeared translucent when backlit by the rostrum camera lights
- How humidity affected drying time and coverage

### Palette Complexity

A single character might require 8--12 distinct paint colors for normal, shadow, and highlight variants:

| Condition | Colors Needed |
|-----------|--------------|
| Normal lighting | Base skin, base clothing (2--3 colors), hair, eye whites, iris, pupils |
| Shadow variant | Darker version of each base color (matched to cel overlay shadow shape) |
| Highlight variant | Lighter version of each base color (for special lighting scenes) |
| Night/rain variant | Desaturated or blue-shifted version of all colors |

A feature film with 20+ characters, each with multiple lighting conditions, could require palettes of several hundred individual paint colors [8].

## 5. Disney's Paint Laboratory

### Mary Weiser and the Paint Lab

**Mary Weiser** established the Disney in-house paint laboratory in the 1930s, transforming inking and painting quality. The lab mixed pigments from raw materials -- dry pigment powder combined with a binding medium (typically a gum arabic or casein base) -- to produce the proprietary paints used on every Disney production [9].

### Palette Naming

Disney palette names were famously idiosyncratic:

- **"Smog green"** -- a murky olive used for atmospheric effects
- **"Dreiss"** -- a distinctive chartreuse named after a worker who always wore that color; later used for Tinker Bell's costume
- **"Bambi pink"** -- developed specifically for the dawn sequence in *Bambi* (1942)
- Production-specific palettes meant that trained eyes could identify the studio and approximate era of a cel by its paint chemistry alone

### Why Proprietary Paint Mattered

Commercial paints were unsuitable for cel work: they were too thick, too glossy, or too transparent. Cel paint needed to be:

- **Opaque** when applied thinly (to prevent background bleed-through)
- **Matte** (to prevent glare under camera lights)
- **Fast-drying** (to maintain production speed)
- **Chemically compatible** with the cel substrate (to prevent delamination)

The Disney lab's formulations remained proprietary throughout the studio's cel era. When CAPS replaced physical painting in 1990, the lab's formulas were archived but production ceased [10].

## 6. Key Individuals

### Documented Contributors

| Name | Role / Era | Contribution |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Hazel Sewell | Dept. Head, 1920s--30s | Built the first all-female department; championed higher talent standards; sister of Lillian Bounds (Walt Disney's wife) |
| Mary Weiser | Paint lab director, 1930s | Established the Disney in-house paint lab; transformed painting quality across all productions |
| Ruthie Tompson | Ink & Paint, 1930s--1980s | Worked on every Disney feature from *Snow White* to *The Rescuers Down Under*; lived to age 111; Disney Legend 2000 |
| Phyllis Craig | Inker/supervisor, 1950s--1990s | Led the transition to the Xerox era; founding member of Women in Animation; Academy teaching roles |
| Retta Scott | First credited female animator | Broke the ink and paint barrier to animate the hunting dogs in *Bambi* (1942); Disney Legend 2000; born Omak, Washington |
| Grace Bailey | Dept. Head, mid-era | Direct liaison between ink and paint and other departments; one of few supervisors with cross-departmental access |

### The Crediting Problem

The overwhelming majority of ink and paint workers were never credited on the films they worked on. Screen credits listed animators, directors, producers, and background artists -- but not the inkers and painters who translated every pencil line into the finished, colored art that appeared on screen. This systematic exclusion from credits made historical research extremely difficult until Mindy Johnson's 2017 work *Ink & Paint: The Women of Walt Disney's Animation* began to reconstruct the department's personnel history from payroll records and oral histories [11].

## 7. World War II and Its Aftermath

### The Opening

With over 170 Disney staffers enlisting after Pearl Harbor, Walt Disney announced on February 10, 1941, that the studio would begin training women as animators. Women moved into inbetweening, story, and eventually animation roles -- positions that had been explicitly closed to them before the war [12].

### The Closure

When the war ended, most women were returned to their former positions. The WWII disruption is the single most significant moment in the gender history of American animation -- a brief opening followed by systematic closure.

### Retta Scott's Exception

**Retta Scott** -- born in Omak, Washington -- remains the most significant exception to the gender barrier. She was the first woman credited as an animator at Disney, animating the hunting dogs sequence in *Bambi* (1942). Her promotion from ink and paint to the animation department was extraordinary for its time and was not repeated for decades. Scott was named a Disney Legend posthumously in 2000 [13].

### The 1941 Disney Strike

The Disney animators' strike of May--September 1941 further complicated the department's dynamics. The strike pitted unionizing animators against non-union staff. Many ink and paint workers were caught between loyalty to colleagues and fear of job loss. The strike's resolution established the Screen Cartoonists Guild (later IATSE Local 839) as the bargaining unit for animation workers, but ink and paint salaries remained significantly below animator compensation [14].

## 8. The Xerography Displacement

### The Technical Shift

When xerography was introduced in 1961, the inking workforce was largely made redundant. The electrostatic process could copy an animator's pencil drawing directly onto a cel in seconds -- replacing hours of skilled hand-inking [15].

### Human Impact

Disney offered displaced inkers positions on the Xerox crew or return to painting. Some transitioned to WED Enterprises (later Walt Disney Imagineering). The displacement was experienced as devastating by workers who had spent careers mastering an irreplaceable craft.

As **Phyllis Craig** recalled: "the girls were pretty much tunnel-visioned" -- the department's isolation had made lateral movement within the company extremely difficult, leaving Xerox operation as the only path forward for most [16].

### Scale of Displacement

| Pre-Xerography (1950s) | Post-Xerography (1960s) |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| ~40--60 full-time inkers at Disney | ~5--10 Xerox operators |
| ~80--100 painters at Disney | ~60--80 painters (painting survived) |
| Combined department: 150+ workers | Combined department: ~80 workers |

The reduction was not instantaneous -- it occurred over approximately five years as productions transitioned to the new process -- but the trajectory was clear. By the mid-1960s, the Disney ink and paint department was half its former size [17].

## 9. Warner Bros., Fleischer, and Other Studios

### Warner Bros. Animation

Warner Bros.' ink and paint department operated under similar gender divisions as Disney but with a leaner workforce matching the studio's lower budgets and shorter production schedules (theatrical shorts rather than features). Chuck Jones's unit maintained particularly high inking standards -- the clean, bold lines of Bugs Bunny and Road Runner cels were a point of pride. Pre-1964 Warner Bros. cels, when the studio briefly closed its animation division, are among the most valued in the collecting market [18].

### Fleischer Studios

Fleischer Studios (Betty Boop, Popeye, *Gulliver's Travels*) operated out of New York and later Miami, with an ink and paint department that included both men and women -- a notable difference from the West Coast studios. The Fleischer "rotoscope" technique required inkers with particular skill in tracing photographic live-action frames onto cel [19].

### Hanna-Barbera and Television

When animation moved to television in the late 1950s, the ink and paint department adapted to a radically different production model. Hanna-Barbera's "limited animation" style -- fewer drawings per second, more held poses, simpler character designs -- reduced the labor per frame but massively increased the total number of cels needed per year. Television schedules demanded production speeds that feature film departments had never faced [20].

### MGM and Other Studios

MGM's animation unit (home of Tom and Jerry under Hanna and Barbera before their departure) maintained a respected ink and paint department through the 1950s. When MGM closed its animation studio in 1958, the department's workers dispersed to other studios -- some to Hanna-Barbera, some to independent commercial studios [21].

## 10. The Japanese Parallel

### The Genga/Douga System

Japanese anime production developed its own ink and paint tradition with distinct terminology and workflow:

- **Genga** (key animation drawings) were produced by senior animators
- **Douga** (clean-up drawings) were traced from genga by junior artists
- The douga became the template for cel inking, following a similar front-ink / back-paint process as Western studios

### Labor Conditions

Japanese anime studios operated under significantly different labor conditions than their Western counterparts. Pay was lower, hours longer, and outsourcing of ink and paint work to lower-cost studios (in Korea, the Philippines, and China) began in the 1970s and expanded through the 1990s [22].

### Extended Cel Production

Japanese studios continued cel production approximately 10--15 years longer than Western studios. The last major Japanese show using cel animation was *Sazae-san*, which switched to digital production in 2013. This extended cel era means that Japanese anime cels from the 1990s and early 2000s are among the most recently produced physical animation artifacts in existence [23].

## 11. Legacy and Recognition

### Mindy Johnson's *Ink & Paint* (2017)

The most comprehensive account of the Disney ink and paint department is Mindy Johnson's *Ink & Paint: The Women of Walt Disney's Animation* (Disney Editions, 2017). Johnson spent years researching payroll records, conducting oral histories, and accessing the Walt Disney Archives to reconstruct the department's history. The book identified hundreds of previously unnamed workers and documented their specific contributions [24].

### Women in Animation

The organization **Women in Animation** (WIA), co-founded by Phyllis Craig and others, was established to address the persistent gender imbalance in the animation industry. While the ink and paint department no longer exists in its physical form, the organization's work continues to address credit recognition and workplace equity [25].

### Walt Disney Family Museum

The **Walt Disney Family Museum** in San Francisco's Presidio maintains a permanent exhibit (Gallery 3) documenting the ink and paint department, including original tools, paint samples, and excerpts from oral histories. The museum's collection includes the **Herman Schultheis Notebooks** -- detailed technical records of Disney's production processes from the 1930s, providing primary-source documentation of ink and paint methods [26].
