# Preservation Science -- Vinegar Syndrome and Material Decay

> **Domain:** Conservation Science, Material Chemistry, Archival Practice
> **Module:** 5 -- The Chemistry That Threatens Every Surviving Cel
> **Through-line:** *Cellulose acetate was chosen for its clarity and flexibility. Nobody planned for the chemistry that would unravel it.* Vinegar syndrome -- the autocatalytic hydrolysis of acetyl ester bonds in cellulose acetate -- is an active threat to animation cel collections worldwide. The reaction is irreversible once it reaches a threshold concentration, and it is contagious: affected cels release acetic acid vapor that can trigger degradation in adjacent materials. Preservation science is not optional -- it is the difference between collections that survive and collections that silently dissolve.

## 1. The Chemistry of Cellulose Acetate

### What Cels Are Made Of

Cellulose acetate is produced by treating cellulose (from wood pulp or cotton linters) with acetic acid and acetic anhydride. The reaction substitutes acetyl groups (-COCH3) for hydroxyl groups (-OH) on the glucose units of the cellulose polymer chain [1].

### Degree of Substitution

The degree of substitution (DS) -- how many of the three available hydroxyl groups per glucose unit are acetylated -- determines the material's properties:

| Type | DS Range | Properties | Cel Use Era |
|------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Cellulose diacetate (CDA) | ~2.2--2.5 | More hygroscopic; earlier degradation onset | 1940s--1960s |
| Cellulose triacetate (CTA) | ~2.8--2.95 | More moisture-resistant; slower degradation | 1950s--1990 |

CTA is more stable than CDA because the higher degree of acetylation leaves fewer free hydroxyl groups available to absorb moisture. However, both materials are ultimately susceptible to hydrolysis [2].

### Plasticizers

Cel sheets contain plasticizers -- typically diethyl phthalate (DEP) or triphenyl phosphate (TPP) -- to maintain flexibility. Plasticizer loss over time contributes to embrittlement. The rate of plasticizer migration depends on temperature, humidity, and whether the cel is sealed in an enclosure or can breathe [3].

## 2. Vinegar Syndrome: The Autocatalytic Loop

### The Hydrolysis Reaction

Moisture in the environment attacks the acetyl ester bonds of the cellulose acetate polymer. Each broken bond produces:
- One molecule of **acetic acid** (CH3COOH) -- the source of the characteristic vinegar odor
- One restored **hydroxyl group** (-OH) on the polymer backbone

### The Feedback Loop

As hydroxyl groups accumulate, the material becomes more **hygroscopic** -- it absorbs more moisture from the surrounding air. More moisture means more hydrolysis. More hydrolysis produces more acetic acid and more hydroxyl groups. This is the **autocatalytic feedback loop**: above a threshold concentration, acid production accelerates faster than diffusion can remove it [4].

```
VINEGAR SYNDROME -- AUTOCATALYTIC FEEDBACK LOOP
================================================================

  Cellulose Acetate + H2O
       |
       v
  Hydrolysis of Acetyl Ester Bond
       |
       +--> Acetic Acid (CH3COOH)  ---> VINEGAR ODOR
       |
       +--> Free Hydroxyl Group (-OH)
                |
                v
            More Hygroscopic Material
                |
                v
            Absorbs More Moisture
                |
                v
            MORE Hydrolysis (accelerating)
                |
                v
            [AUTOCATALYTIC ZONE: rate > diffusion removal]
                |
                v
            Irreversible Degradation
```

### The Threshold

The autocatalytic phase begins when the acetic acid concentration reaches approximately **1.5 parts per million (ppm)** in the microenvironment around the cel -- corresponding to an A-D strip reading transitioning from blue-green to green. Below this threshold, degradation is slow and manageable with environmental controls. Above it, the reaction self-accelerates even in controlled conditions [5].

### Contagion

Vinegar syndrome is **contagious** in a practical sense: affected cels release acetic acid vapor into the surrounding air. In an enclosed storage container (box, drawer, sealed frame), this vapor accumulates and can trigger degradation in adjacent cels that were previously healthy. This is why affected cels must be isolated from the rest of a collection [6].

> **SAFETY WARNING:** All conservation guidance in this module is consistent with published protocols from the Image Permanence Institute (IPI), Getty Conservation Institute (GCI), and American Institute for Conservation (AIC). This document does not provide DIY conservation advice beyond IPI-endorsed protocols. For actively degrading materials, consult a professional conservator.

## 3. Physical Manifestations

### Progressive Symptoms

Vinegar syndrome manifests through a characteristic progression:

| Stage | Physical Signs | A-D Strip Reading | Reversibility |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Early | Faint vinegar odor; no visible change | Blue-green (1--1.5 ppm) | Manageable with environment |
| Moderate | Noticeable odor; slight buckling/rippling | Green (1.5--2 ppm) | Slowed but not reversed |
| Active | Strong odor; visible warping; surface crystalline deposits | Yellow-green (2--3 ppm) | Requires cold storage |
| Advanced | Severe warping; paint delamination; embrittlement; shrinkage | Yellow (>3 ppm) | Emergency cold/frozen storage |

### Paint Layer Effects

The interaction between degrading acetate and the painted surface creates compound damage:
- **Buckling** stretches and cracks the paint layer
- **Acetic acid vapor** can chemically attack pigment binders
- **Shrinkage** causes paint to flake or delaminate
- **Crystalline deposits** (white powdery material on cel surface) indicate advanced degradation

### Cellulose Nitrate (Early Cels)

Pre-1950s cels made from cellulose nitrate have a separate degradation pathway: nitrate decomposition produces nitrogen dioxide gas, which is both toxic and accelerates further decay. Nitrate materials are also **highly flammable** -- they self-ignite at approximately 300F (150C) and burn with extreme intensity. Institutional collections of nitrate cel material require fire-suppression storage compliant with NFPA 40 [7].

## 4. Detection: A-D Strips

### What Are A-D Strips?

Acid Detection (A-D) strips were developed by the **Image Permanence Institute (IPI)** at the Rochester Institute of Technology. They received a Technical Achievement Award from the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences in 1997. The strips are impregnated with pH-sensitive dye that shifts color from blue to yellow when exposed to acetic acid vapor [8].

### Reading A-D Strips

| Strip Color | Approx. ppm | Diagnosis | Required Action |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| Blue | <1 | Healthy | Standard monitoring; normal storage conditions |
| Blue-green | 1--1.5 | Early degradation | Increase monitoring frequency; improve ventilation |
| Green | 1.5--2 | Active degradation | Move to cool storage; isolate from adjacent cels |
| Yellow-green | 2--3 | Autocatalytic zone | Cold storage required; professional assessment |
| Yellow | >3 | Advanced degradation | Emergency cold/frozen storage; digitize immediately if possible |

### Usage Protocol

1. Place one A-D strip in each storage enclosure (folder, box, frame)
2. Seal the enclosure for a minimum of **24 hours** (the strip needs time to equilibrate with the microenvironment)
3. Read the strip color immediately upon opening -- strip color fades in open air within minutes
4. Record the reading, date, and storage location
5. Repeat at intervals based on the reading: annually for blue, quarterly for blue-green, monthly for green or worse

### Where to Obtain A-D Strips

A-D strips are available from the Image Permanence Institute (IPI) at Rochester Institute of Technology and from archival supply vendors (Gaylord Archival, University Products). They are the only standardized, non-destructive detection tool for acetate degradation in common use [9].

## 5. Storage Environment Standards

### Environmental Controls

The rate of cellulose acetate hydrolysis is governed by two primary environmental factors: **temperature** and **relative humidity (RH)**. Lowering either slows the reaction. The IPI Storage Guide for Acetate Film provides the authoritative guidance [10].

### Tiered Storage

| Storage Tier | Temperature | Relative Humidity | Expected Benefit |
|-------------|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Room storage | 65--70F (18--21C) | 30--50% RH | Baseline; decades of useful life if RH controlled |
| Cool storage | 50--60F (10--15C) | 30--45% RH | Significantly slows hydrolysis; recommended minimum for collections |
| Cold storage | 35--50F (2--10C) | 20--35% RH | Greatly extends life; required for degrading cels |
| Frozen (emergency) | <32F (0C) | -- | Halts degradation; emergency preservation only |

### Key Rules

- **Temperature stability** is more important than absolute temperature. Cycling (repeated warming and cooling) causes condensation that accelerates hydrolysis.
- **RH below 30%** can cause embrittlement of paint layers and cracking of the cel substrate.
- **RH above 50%** provides excess moisture that accelerates hydrolysis.
- The ideal range for mixed-media animation cels is **40--45% RH at 50--60F** -- a compromise between acetate stability and paint layer integrity [11].

### Air Circulation

Cels must be able to "breathe" -- they must not be sealed in airtight enclosures. Acetic acid vapor produced by early-stage degradation needs to diffuse away from the cel surface. Sealing a cel in plastic wrap, a ziplock bag, or a tightly sealed frame **traps acid vapor and accelerates degradation** [12].

## 6. The Mixed-Media Challenge

### Why Animation Cels Are Difficult to Conserve

A painted animation cel is not a single material -- it is a composite of at least three distinct material systems:

1. **Cellulose acetate substrate** (the clear cel sheet)
2. **Ink lines** (front surface; typically India ink or colored paint)
3. **Paint layers** (reverse surface; gouache, watercolor, or proprietary studio paint)

Each material responds differently to temperature and humidity. The conditions that best preserve the acetate (cold, very dry) may cause paint to crack and delaminate. The conditions that keep paint flexible (moderate temperature, moderate humidity) accelerate acetate hydrolysis [13].

### The Getty/ARL Finding

The key finding from the Getty Conservation Institute / Disney Animation Research Library collaboration (initiated 2009): **temperatures and relative humidity levels that improve acetate lifetime may negatively impact inks and paints by causing cracking and delamination.** Finding ideal environments for animation cels requires balancing acetate stability against paint layer stability. The ideal is a cool, stable, low-humidity environment with minimal cycling [14].

## 7. Framing and Display

### Conservation-Grade Framing

If animation cels are to be displayed, framing must follow conservation standards:

- **UV-protective glazing:** UV-filtering glass or acrylic to prevent photodegradation of both paint and substrate
- **Acid-free mat board:** All materials in contact with the cel must be acid-free and lignin-free
- **Spacers:** The cel must not touch the glazing surface (condensation risk)
- **Ventilation:** The frame must not be sealed airtight (acetic acid vapor trap)
- **No metal contact:** Metal hardware must not touch the cel surface (corrosion products can damage acetate and paint)
- **No direct sunlight:** Even with UV glazing, direct sunlight causes heat buildup inside the frame [15].

### Display Time Limits

Prolonged display accelerates degradation. Institutional best practice limits display time for acetate-based materials:
- Rotate displayed pieces every **3--6 months**
- Return to controlled storage between display periods
- Monitor with A-D strips even while on display
- If vinegar odor is detectable from a framed piece, remove immediately

## 8. Getty / Disney ARL Research Program

### Program History

The Getty Conservation Institute and the Walt Disney Animation Research Library initiated a joint research project in 2009 to study the plastic substrates of Disney animation cels. The project grew from the GCI's broader Preservation of Plastics initiative within the Modern and Contemporary Art Research program [16].

### Research Methods

Individual cels from various Disney productions were analyzed using multiple analytical techniques:
- **FTIR spectroscopy** (Fourier Transform Infrared): Identified plastic type and degree of degradation
- **GC-MS** (Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry): Identified plasticizer composition
- **Oddy test:** Assessed off-gassing risk to adjacent materials
- **Accelerated aging studies:** Predicted long-term behavior under controlled conditions

### Key Findings

1. The ARL collection contains cels from multiple acetate formulations, reflecting changes in commercial cel sheet suppliers over decades
2. Degree of degradation varies significantly within a single production -- not all cels from the same film are in the same condition
3. Plasticizer migration is a significant factor in embrittlement, independent of vinegar syndrome
4. Storage environment history (how a cel was stored for the past 50+ years) is the strongest predictor of current condition [17].

### Collection Scale

The ARL collection comprises approximately **65 million pieces** of animation art -- cels, drawings, model sheets, backgrounds, exposure sheets, and reference materials. This is the world's largest animation archive and the primary institutional laboratory for cel conservation research [18].

## 9. Conservation Protocols for Collectors

### Practical Storage Guidelines

Based on IPI and GCI published protocols:

1. **Store each cel in a separate acid-free sleeve** (polyethylene or polypropylene -- NOT PVC)
2. **Never stack cels directly** -- separate with acid-free interleaving tissue
3. **Do not seal in enclosed containers** -- cels need to breathe (acetic acid diffusion)
4. **Store flat, not vertical** -- vertical storage causes paint sag and cel warping over time
5. **Maintain stable temperature and humidity** -- avoid rooms with HVAC cycling
6. **Monitor with A-D strips** -- annual checks minimum; quarterly for any collection over 10 years old
7. **Isolate any cel with detectable vinegar odor** -- bag separately with A-D strip
8. **Acclimatize before moving between environments** -- 24-hour transition period for any move involving >10% RH or >4C temperature change

### What NOT to Do

- **Do not laminate** or seal cels in plastic film
- **Do not use adhesive tape** of any kind on cel surfaces
- **Do not attempt paint touch-ups** with commercial paint
- **Do not use household cleaners** on cel surfaces
- **Do not photocopy cels** on commercial copiers (heat and pressure damage)
- **Do not store near food, chemicals, or printers** (volatile organic compounds accelerate degradation) [19].

## 10. Emergency Response

### If Vinegar Syndrome Is Detected

```
VINEGAR SYNDROME EMERGENCY PROTOCOL
================================================================

  DETECTION (vinegar odor or A-D strip green/yellow)
       |
       v
  ISOLATE immediately from all other materials
       |
       v
  ASSESS with A-D strip (24-hour enclosure test)
       |
       +-- Green (1.5-2 ppm): Move to cool storage (50-60F)
       |   Monitor monthly. Professional consult recommended.
       |
       +-- Yellow-green (2-3 ppm): Move to cold storage (35-50F)
       |   Professional conservator assessment required.
       |   Digitize at high resolution as soon as possible.
       |
       +-- Yellow (>3 ppm): Emergency cold or frozen storage.
           Immediate high-resolution digitization.
           Professional conservator required.
           Material may be past recovery.
```

### Digitization as Insurance

High-resolution scanning (600+ DPI, 48-bit color) of every cel in a collection is the single most valuable preservation step a collector can take. If the original material degrades beyond recovery, the digital record preserves the artwork. Scanning should be done with a flatbed scanner using a transparency adapter, not photographed [20].

## 11. The Digitization Imperative

### Why Digitize?

Physical cels are chemically unstable. Even under ideal storage conditions, cellulose acetate has a finite lifetime. Digitization creates a permanent record that does not degrade.

### Recommended Specifications

| Parameter | Minimum | Preferred |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Resolution | 300 DPI | 600+ DPI |
| Color depth | 24-bit | 48-bit |
| File format | TIFF (uncompressed) | TIFF with embedded ICC profile |
| Color calibration | Gray card reference | IT8 target calibration |
| Metadata | Filename convention | Full catalog record embedded (XMP/IPTC) |

### Scan Workflow

1. Clean scanner glass with lint-free cloth and archival glass cleaner
2. Place cel on scanner bed, paint-side down (ink lines facing up)
3. Scan at preferred resolution and color depth
4. Include a color reference target in the first scan of each session
5. Save as uncompressed TIFF with embedded color profile
6. Record catalog metadata and link scan file to catalog record [21].
