# Origins and Technical Evolution of Cel Animation

> **Domain:** Animation History, Material Culture
> **Module:** 1 -- From Blackboard to Acetate (1906--1990)
> **Through-line:** *Before there was a render farm, there was a room full of women in white smocks, tracing lines fine as spider silk onto transparent sheets of cellulose acetate. The pencil drawing was a promise. The painted cel was the delivery.* Every frame of classical animation passed through a material pipeline that evolved over seventy-six years, from flammable cellulose nitrate to the final hand-painted cel that rolled through the rostrum camera in 1990.

## 1. The Pre-Cel Era

Animation existed before the cel. Understanding what came before reveals why the cel patent was revolutionary and why the entire industry organized itself around a single material innovation.

### Blackboard and Paper Methods (1906--1913)

**J. Stuart Blackton** filmed chalk drawings on a blackboard in 1906 for *Humorous Phases of Funny Faces*, generally considered the first animated film [1]. The technique was primitive but established the frame-by-frame principle: change the image slightly, photograph it, advance the film, repeat.

**Emile Cohl** created *Fantasmagorie* in 1908 using white pencil drawings on black paper -- 700 drawings for less than two minutes of film. Each frame was a complete redrawing. There was no separation between character and background.

**Winsor McCay** debuted *Little Nemo* in April 1911 -- 4,000 pencil drawings on paper, produced over four years. McCay's drawings were exquisite, but the labor was punishing: every element in every frame had to be redrawn completely. A stationary background behind a moving character still required redrawing the background in every single frame [2].

### The Fundamental Problem

Without layer separation, animation was a brute-force proposition. If a character moved against a static background, the background had to be redrawn for every frame -- 24 times per second for theatrical film. McCay's 4,000 drawings for a two-minute film represented an extraordinary personal effort, but the method was economically impossible for longer works. The cel would solve this by introducing the concept of transparent layers.

## 2. The Cel Patent and Industrialization

### Earl Hurd's Patent (1914)

**Earl Hurd** patented the cel process in 1914 (U.S. Patent 1,143,542). The invention separated character drawings from backgrounds: characters were drawn and painted on transparent sheets of celluloid, which were then laid over a separately painted background and photographed. A single background painting could serve for hundreds of frames, with only the character cels changing between shots [3].

### Bray's Industrial Model

**John Randolph Bray** had simultaneously developed related industrial techniques. His 1913 film *Colonel Heeza Liar in Africa* is considered the first commercial cartoon, using preprinted backgrounds on translucent paper. Bray's studio developed a factory model for animation production, establishing the assembly-line workflow that would define the industry for the next seventy years [4].

Hurd and Bray pooled their patents and licensed the cel process to other studios, creating the first animation patent monopoly. By the 1920s, virtually all commercial animation used the cel process.

### Early Materials

Early cels used **cellulose nitrate** (camphor-plasticized), which was highly flammable and dimensionally unstable. Drawings were done with India ink on rice paper (translucent enough for tracing). The production term for the painting stage was "inking," though true ink was rarely used after the earliest years -- colored "ink" lines were actually made with the same paint used for opaquing (filling) the reverse side of the cel [5].

```
CEL ANIMATION -- BASIC LAYER SEPARATION
================================================================

  TRADITIONAL METHOD (pre-1914):
  [Character + Background drawn together] x 24 frames/sec
  = EVERY element redrawn for EVERY frame

  CEL METHOD (post-1914):
  [Transparent cel with character] layered OVER
  [Static background painting]
  = Background painted ONCE; only character cels change
  = 24x reduction in background labor per second of film
```

## 3. Material Substrate Evolution

The physical material of the cel evolved through four distinct generations, each driven by chemical and industrial advances. Understanding this evolution is essential for both historical accuracy and conservation practice.

### Substrate Timeline

| Era | Material | Trade Names | Properties |
|-----|----------|-------------|------------|
| 1914--1950s | Cellulose nitrate | Nitrocellulose film | Highly flammable; dimensionally unstable; archival hazard |
| 1940s--1990 | Cellulose diacetate | "Safety film" | Less flammable; prone to buckling; subject to vinegar syndrome |
| 1950s--1990 | Cellulose triacetate (CTA) | Lumarith, Kodacel | More stable; dominant in Disney production; GCI/ARL research subject |
| 1980s--1990 | Polyester (Mylar) | Estar base | Very stable; used for specialty applications |
| 1990+ | Digital (no physical cel) | CAPS, Harmony | Walt Disney ceased cel production 1990 |

### Chemical Properties

Cellulose acetate is produced by treating wood pulp or cotton cellulose with acetic acid and acetic anhydride. The degree of substitution (DS) -- how many of the three available hydroxyl groups per glucose unit are acetylated -- determines the material's properties [6]:

- **Cellulose diacetate** (DS ~2.4): More hygroscopic; earlier onset of vinegar syndrome; used widely in the 1940s--1960s
- **Cellulose triacetate** (DS ~2.9): More resistant to moisture absorption; slower degradation; the dominant cel material from the 1950s onward

> **SAFETY WARNING:** Cellulose nitrate cels from the earliest era are a genuine fire hazard. Nitrate film self-ignites at approximately 300F (150C) and burns with extreme intensity. Museum and archive holdings of nitrate cel material require specialized fire-suppression storage. The National Film Preservation Foundation classifies all nitrate-based materials as requiring isolated storage [7].

### Cel Dimensions and Standards

Standard American animation cels measured approximately 10" x 12" for theatrical production, with television cels slightly smaller. Japanese anime cels standardized at approximately 9" x 10.5". The cel sheets were pre-punched with registration holes (peg holes) matching the animation pegs on the desk, lightbox, and rostrum camera. Dimensional consistency was critical -- even slight misalignment between frames would produce visible jitter in the projected film [8].

## 4. The Golden Age Pipeline

### Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs (1937)

Disney's *Snow White* was the first feature-length cel-animated film. The production required over 100,000 individual cels, each hand-inked and hand-painted. The scale of the operation established the industrial model that would persist for half a century [9].

### The Assembly Line

The golden age production pipeline was a massively parallel human pipeline:

```
GOLDEN AGE ANIMATION PIPELINE
================================================================

  STORY DEPARTMENT
    Storyboard artists → Scene layouts → Exposure sheets
                                              |
  ANIMATION DEPARTMENT                        v
    Key animators → Key drawings (extremes)
    Breakdown artists → Intermediate poses
    Inbetweeners → Frame-by-frame transitions
                                              |
  CHECKING DEPARTMENT                         v
    Verify line quality, registration, continuity
                                              |
  INK AND PAINT DEPARTMENT                    v
    Inkers → Trace pencil lines to cel (front)
    Painters → Apply color to cel (reverse)
    Checkers → Verify paint coverage, registration
                                              |
  CAMERA DEPARTMENT                           v
    Rostrum camera operator
    Background placement
    Multi-layer registration
    Frame-by-frame photography
                                              |
                                              v
                                         PROCESSED FILM
```

### Disney's Paint Laboratory

Disney established an in-house paint laboratory in the 1930s under the direction of **Mary Weiser**. The lab mixed pigments from raw materials, giving each production its own distinctive palette. Paint names were idiosyncratic -- "smog green," "dreiss" (a chartreuse named after a worker who always wore that color, later used for Tinker Bell). The proprietary formulations meant that Disney cels have a distinctive appearance that differs from cels produced by Warner Bros., MGM, or Fleischer [10].

Painters had to master the behavior of specific pigments: which colors bled when adjacent, which dried at different rates causing overlap problems, which pigments were translucent enough to show through when layered. Each painter typically specialized in two to four colors, building deep expertise in the behavior of those specific pigments on that specific substrate.

## 5. Disney's Multiplane Camera

### The Problem of Depth

Traditional animation photography places all cels and the background on a single plane, creating flat images. Walt Disney wanted depth -- parallax movement where foreground elements move faster than background elements, just as they do when you look out the window of a moving car [11].

### The Machine

The Disney multiplane camera, designed by William Garity and first used in the 1937 *Silly Symphony* short "The Old Mill," was a vertical camera rig standing approximately 12 feet tall. It held up to seven separate glass planes, each at a different distance from the camera lens. By moving the planes at different speeds relative to the camera, the illusion of three-dimensional depth was created.

```
DISNEY MULTIPLANE CAMERA -- CROSS-SECTION VIEW
================================================================

  CAMERA (top, pointing down)
     |
     v
  ┌─────────────────────┐  Plane 1 (foreground foliage)
  │  Glass + painted cel │  -- moves FASTEST relative to camera
  └─────────────────────┘
           |
  ┌─────────────────────┐  Plane 2 (character layer)
  │  Glass + painted cel │  -- primary action layer
  └─────────────────────┘
           |
  ┌─────────────────────┐  Plane 3 (midground elements)
  │  Glass + painted cel │  -- moves SLOWER than characters
  └─────────────────────┘
           |
  ┌─────────────────────┐  Plane 4 (background painting)
  │  Glass + background  │  -- moves SLOWEST; appears farthest
  └─────────────────────┘
           |
         LIGHT SOURCE (bottom illumination)
```

### Technical Challenges

Each glass plane had to be individually lit to avoid reflections and shadows from upper planes. The camera operator had to calculate precise movement increments for each plane to maintain consistent depth perspective. A single multiplane shot in *Pinocchio* (1940) took eight hours to photograph and cost approximately $45,000 -- at a time when an entire *Silly Symphony* short could be produced for under $50,000 [12].

The multiplane camera won an Academy Scientific and Technical Award in 1937. It remained in use at Disney until the CAPS system replaced it in 1990, with the final multiplane shot appearing in *The Little Mermaid* (1989).

### Legacy and Influence

The Fleischer Studios developed their own depth system called the "Setback" or "Tabletop," which used three-dimensional miniature backgrounds that cels were photographed against. While less flexible than Disney's multiplane, it produced a distinctly different aesthetic -- visible in *Gulliver's Travels* (1939) and the *Superman* theatrical shorts [13].

## 6. Rotoscoping: Tracing the Real

### The Fleischer Patent (1917)

**Max Fleischer** patented the rotoscope in 1917 -- a device that projected live-action film frames onto a drawing surface, allowing animators to trace over real human movement. The first use was in the "Out of the Inkwell" series featuring Koko the Clown, with Max's brother Dave Fleischer performing the live-action reference [14].

### Technique and Application

Rotoscoping was never simple tracing. Skilled animators used the projected footage as a guide for timing and proportion while adding the exaggeration and stylization that makes animation distinct from live action. Direct tracing produced movement that looked uncanny -- too smooth, too realistic, falling into what would later be called the "uncanny valley."

Disney used extensive live-action reference (a form of rotoscoping) for the human characters in *Snow White*, *Cinderella*, and *Sleeping Beauty*. The reference footage was shot on the studio lot with actors in costume, then projected for animators to study. The degree of direct tracing versus interpretation varied by animator and by production [15].

### Rotoscoping in the Japanese Tradition

Japanese anime studios adopted rotoscoping selectively, most notably in the work of **Hayao Miyazaki** at Studio Ghibli, where live-action reference was used for realistic movement in films like *Spirited Away* (2001). The Japanese term *rotosuko-pu* entered the production vocabulary alongside the traditional *genga/douga* system [16].

## 7. Xerography: The Line Changes

### The Ub Iwerks Innovation (1957--1961)

Disney engineer **Bill Justice** had patented a pressure-transfer process in 1944. Applied to animation by **Ub Iwerks** at Disney in the late 1950s, the electrostatic copying technique called xerography allowed animators' pencil drawings to be copied directly onto cels, eliminating most hand-inking [17].

### One Hundred and One Dalmatians (1961)

The first fully xerographic Disney feature was *One Hundred and One Dalmatians* (1961). The artistic result was dramatic: instead of the clean, uniform ink lines of hand-inking, xerography preserved the energy and variability of the animator's original pencil strokes. The characteristic sketchy, pencil-line aesthetic defined Disney animation from 1961 through the late 1970s.

### Impact on the Workforce

Xerography directly displaced a large portion of the ink and paint workforce. Inkers -- who had spent careers mastering the art of tracing animators' drawings with the finest Gillott 290 nibs -- found their skills suddenly redundant. Disney offered displaced inkers positions on the Xerox crew or return to painting. The displacement was experienced as devastating by workers who had invested years in an irreplaceable craft [18].

```
INK LINE COMPARISON
================================================================

  HAND-INKED LINE (pre-1961):
  ─ Clean, uniform width
  ─ Consistent opacity
  ─ Lost animator's pencil energy
  ─ Required skilled human inker
  ─ ~8 hours per 100 cels

  XEROGRAPHIC LINE (1961--1985):
  ─ Variable width, pencil texture preserved
  ─ Slightly grainy appearance
  ─ Retained animator's original stroke energy
  ─ Mechanical process, no inker needed
  ─ ~100 cels per hour

  APT LINE (1985--1990):
  ─ Fine-line detail exceeding xerography
  ─ Photographic transfer, no pencil grain
  ─ Cleaner than Xerox, less sterile than hand-ink
  ─ First used in The Black Cauldron (1985)
```

## 8. Animation Photo Transfer

### APT Process (1985)

The Animation Photo Transfer (APT) process was a repro-photographic method first used in *The Black Cauldron* (1985). It transferred artwork via high-contrast litho film to light-sensitive dye on cel. The result was finer line detail than xerography could achieve, without returning to the labor-intensive hand-inking process [19].

### Technical Process

APT worked by photographing the animator's drawing onto high-contrast lithographic film, then using the film negative as a photographic mask to expose dye-sensitized cel material. The result was a precise, clean line that captured detail finer than the Xerox electrostatic process could resolve.

The process was used in *The Great Mouse Detective* (1986) and *Oliver & Company* (1988), bridging the gap between xerography and the fully digital CAPS system.

## 9. CAPS and the End of the Cel

### The Computer Animation Production System (1989--1990)

**CAPS** -- the Computer Animation Production System -- was developed jointly by **Pixar** and **Walt Disney Feature Animation**. It replaced the physical ink-and-paint process with digital scanning, coloring, and compositing [20].

### First Use

CAPS was first used for the rainbow sequence in *The Little Mermaid* (1989) -- a short scene demonstrating the system's ability to produce smooth color gradients impossible with physical paint. The first fully CAPS-produced feature was *The Rescuers Down Under* (1990).

### What CAPS Replaced

CAPS eliminated four physical processes simultaneously:
1. **Inking** -- replaced by digital scanning of pencil drawings
2. **Painting** -- replaced by digital color fill
3. **Multi-layer registration** -- replaced by digital compositing
4. **Rostrum camera photography** -- replaced by digital rendering to film

### The Transition Timeline

| Year | Studio/Event | Significance |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1989 | Disney -- *The Little Mermaid* rainbow | First CAPS scene |
| 1990 | Disney -- *The Rescuers Down Under* | First fully CAPS feature |
| 1991 | Disney -- *Beauty and the Beast* | First CAPS film nominated for Best Picture |
| 1993 | Warner Bros. adopts digital ink and paint | Major Western studio follows Disney |
| Mid-1990s | Most Western studios digital | TV and feature production transitions |
| 2003--2006 | Final Japanese anime holdouts | Major anime studios complete digital transition |
| 2013 | *Sazae-san* switches to digital | Last major show using cel animation |

### Legacy

Physical cel production at Disney ended in 1990. Other Western studios followed over the next five years. Japanese anime studios continued cel production significantly longer -- a critical fact for collectors, as the extended Japanese cel era produced millions of additional physical artifacts [21].

## 10. The Production Artifact Hierarchy

Every stage of the cel animation pipeline produced distinct physical artifacts, each with different material properties, survival rates, and collecting significance.

```
PRODUCTION ARTIFACT HIERARCHY
================================================================

  PENCIL LAYER (paper)
    |-- Rough sketch / layout drawing
    |-- Genga (key animation drawing, Japan) / Key Drawing (West)
    |-- Douga (clean-up drawing, Japan) / Breakdown Drawing (West)
    |-- Inbetween Drawing
    |
  CEL LAYER (acetate / celluloid)
    |-- Production Cel (inked front, painted back)
    |     |-- Key Cel (highest collecting value)
    |     |-- Inbetween Cel
    |     |-- Color Test Cel (rare, pre-production)
    |     |-- Multi-layer Setup (body + mouth + shadow)
    |
  BACKGROUND LAYER (paper / board)
    |-- Master Background (hand-painted gouache/watercolor)
    |-- Pan Background (extended for camera movement)
    |-- Overlay Background
    |
  COMPOSITE PHOTOGRAPH (rostrum camera, frame by frame)
    |
  FILM / FINAL OUTPUT
```

### Key Artifact Types

**Model sheets** defined the standard appearance of each character -- proportions, expressions, color palettes. These were distributed to every artist on the production and are among the most informative surviving documents for understanding character design intent [22].

**Exposure sheets** (also called X-sheets or dope sheets) recorded the precise timing of every frame: which cels on which layers, which background, camera position, duration. The exposure sheet was the production's master score, equivalent to a conductor's marked-up orchestral score.

**Storyboards** preceded all of the above -- sequential panel drawings that mapped out the narrative and composition of each scene before any animation began. Disney's storyboard process, formalized in the early 1930s, became the industry standard [23].

## 11. Animation Paper and Peg Systems

### The Peg System

The peg system is the registration mechanism that keeps all layers -- drawings, cels, backgrounds -- in precise alignment. Without accurate registration, characters would appear to jump or float between frames.

### Standard Peg Configurations

| Standard | Configuration | Primary Use |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Acme peg | Round center + two elongated side pegs | Industry standard (North America) |
| Disney/Oxberry | Three round pegs in a bar | Disney standard; used with Oxberry cameras |
| Japanese standard | Two round pegs + one rectangular | Anime production standard |

The pegs are mounted on the animation desk (for drawing), the lightbox (for tracing and checking), and the rostrum camera compound (for photographing). Paper and cels are pre-punched with matching holes. The entire system depends on sub-millimeter accuracy across all stages of production [24].

### Animation Paper

Professional animation paper was a specialized product: semi-translucent (for lightbox tracing), dimensionally stable, and receptive to both graphite pencil and colored pencil. Standard paper weights ranged from 16lb to 24lb bond. The paper had to be thin enough to allow the animator to see the previous drawing through it (for checking movement) while opaque enough to photograph cleanly.

Pre-punched animation paper was manufactured by companies including Cartoon Colour (now Cartoon Color Company, Inc.) and was one of the few animation supplies that remained in production after the digital transition, serving educational and independent animators [25].
