Project 1 — Orbital Mechanics

Free-Return Trajectory Simulator

Difficulty
★★★
advanced
Time
2–4 hours
setup + coding + visualization
Cost
$0
open source tools

Build a Python simulation that models the free-return trajectory Artemis II will fly. A free-return trajectory is an orbital path that uses lunar gravity to swing the spacecraft back toward Earth without requiring an engine burn — a critical safety feature that guarantees the crew can return home even if propulsion fails after trans-lunar injection.

Materials

  • Python 3.8+ with pip
  • poliastro (open-source astrodynamics library)
  • matplotlib for trajectory visualization
  • NumPy and SciPy for numerical integration

Steps

  1. Install poliastro: pip install poliastro matplotlib
  2. Define initial conditions: spacecraft mass, TLI delta-v (~3.1 km/s), and Moon position at injection time
  3. Set up the restricted three-body problem (Earth, Moon, spacecraft) using poliastro's propagation tools
  4. Propagate the trajectory forward, logging position at each timestep
  5. Plot the resulting path in 2D (Earth-Moon rotating frame) and verify the free-return loop
  6. Experiment: vary TLI delta-v by +/- 50 m/s and observe how the trajectory changes
orbital mechanics numerical integration restricted 3-body problem Python
Connection to Artemis II: The mission's hybrid free-return trajectory is its primary abort safety net. After TLI, if all engines fail, lunar gravity alone redirects the spacecraft back to Earth. Your simulation models the same physics that mission designers at JSC used to certify this trajectory for crew safety.
Project 2 — Photonics

LED Optical Communications Demo

Difficulty
★★★
intermediate
Time
2–3 hours
assembly + coding + testing
Cost
~$30–50
electronics components

Transmit text data across a room using pulses of visible light — the same fundamental principle behind the O2O laser communications system on Orion. The O2O system uses a 1550 nm infrared laser at 260 Mbps; your LED version will be slower but demonstrates the identical concept: encoding information in light instead of radio waves.

Materials

  • Arduino Uno or Nano
  • High-brightness LED (any color)
  • Photodiode or phototransistor
  • Breadboard and jumper wires
  • 220-ohm and 10K-ohm resistors
  • USB cable for Arduino programming

Steps

  1. Wire the LED to an Arduino digital output pin through a 220-ohm current-limiting resistor
  2. Wire the photodiode to an analog input pin with a 10K-ohm pull-down resistor
  3. Write a transmitter sketch that blinks the LED in a pattern encoding ASCII characters (on = 1, off = 0)
  4. Write a receiver sketch that reads the photodiode voltage and decodes the bit pattern back to text
  5. Start at 1 meter distance, then increase separation and measure when the signal degrades
  6. Calculate your achieved data rate (bits per second) and compare to the O2O system's 260 Mbps
signal processing modulation photonics Arduino
Connection to Artemis II: The O2O optical terminal on Orion achieves 130x the data rate of S-band radio by concentrating energy into a tight laser beam. Your LED demo shows why optical beats radio — the photodiode only responds when it catches the light, demonstrating the directional advantage that lets O2O send HD video from lunar distance.
Project 3 — Particle Physics

Cosmic Ray Detector (Cloud Chamber)

Difficulty
★★★
intermediate
Time
1–2 hours
build + observation
Cost
~$20–30
dry ice is the main expense

Build a diffusion cloud chamber and watch cosmic ray tracks with your own eyes. The same high-energy particles that leave visible trails in your chamber — muons, alpha particles, and secondary cosmic rays — are the radiation hazard that the Artemis II crew faces beyond the Van Allen belts. On the ISS, Earth's magnetosphere deflects most of this. On a lunar flyby, the crew is fully exposed.

Materials

  • Clear glass or plastic container (aquarium, glass jar, or petri dish)
  • Black felt or fabric (for the bottom surface)
  • 99% isopropyl alcohol
  • Dry ice (1–2 kg block)
  • Bright flashlight or LED strip (side illumination)
  • Gloves for handling dry ice

Steps

  1. Line the bottom of the container with black felt and saturate it with isopropyl alcohol
  2. Seal the container and place it upside-down on the dry ice block
  3. Wait 5–10 minutes for a temperature gradient to establish (cold bottom, warm top)
  4. Shine a bright light across the bottom of the chamber at a shallow angle
  5. Watch for thin white vapor trails — each trail is a charged particle ionizing alcohol molecules
  6. Identify track types: short fat tracks (alpha particles), long straight tracks (muons), branching tracks (nuclear interactions)
particle physics radiation supersaturation cosmic rays
Connection to Artemis II: The ARCHAR crew health study and K-RadCube CubeSat both measure the deep space radiation environment. Your cloud chamber makes this invisible hazard visible. Every track you see represents a particle that, in deep space without Earth's magnetic shielding, would pass through the crew cabin and potentially damage DNA in the astronauts' cells.
Project 4 — Thermal Protection

Heat Shield Ablation Test

Difficulty
★★★
intermediate
Time
1 hour
setup + tests + analysis
Cost
~$15
household materials

Compare ablative versus reflective thermal protection strategies using kitchen materials. Orion's AVCOAT heat shield works by ablation — the material deliberately chars and vaporizes, carrying heat away from the spacecraft as it burns off. This is fundamentally different from reflective insulation, which tries to bounce heat away. Both approaches have trade-offs that this experiment reveals.

Materials

  • Marshmallows (ablative test material)
  • Aluminum foil (reflective test material)
  • Heat gun or kitchen torch
  • Thermocouple or infrared thermometer
  • Metal baking sheet (safe work surface)
  • Timer and notebook for recording data

Steps

  1. Set up two identical test stations on the baking sheet, each with a thermocouple behind the test material
  2. Station A: stack marshmallows as the thermal barrier (ablative approach)
  3. Station B: wrap multiple layers of aluminum foil as the thermal barrier (reflective approach)
  4. Apply heat from the same distance and angle to both stations for 30 seconds
  5. Record the temperature behind each barrier every 5 seconds
  6. Observe: the marshmallow chars and sacrifices material (ablation), while the foil reflects but eventually conducts heat through
thermodynamics materials science ablation heat transfer
Connection to Artemis II: Orion's 5-meter AVCOAT heat shield will endure temperatures exceeding 2,760°C (5,000°F) during reentry at ~11 km/s. The ablative principle is the same as your marshmallow: the material sacrifices itself, and the vaporized gas creates a boundary layer that blocks further heat transfer. Artemis II uses a steeper direct reentry (unlike Artemis I's skip entry), putting different thermal loads on the shield.
Project 5 — Communications

DSN Signal Delay Calculator

Difficulty
★★★
beginner
Time
30 min
setup + calculations
Cost
$0
spreadsheet or Python

Calculate the speed-of-light communication delay between Earth and spacecraft at various distances, then experience what it feels like to operate under that constraint. At lunar distance, the delay is about 1.3 seconds each way. Manageable, but noticeable. At Mars distance, it becomes 4 to 24 minutes — making real-time conversation impossible.

Materials

  • Spreadsheet (Google Sheets, Excel, LibreOffice) or Python
  • Timer or stopwatch app
  • A partner for the communication delay simulation

Steps

  1. Calculate one-way light time: distance / 299,792.458 km/s. Moon = 384,400 km = 1.28 seconds
  2. Build a table of delays: ISS (0.0013s), Moon (1.28s), Mars closest (182s), Mars farthest (1,342s), Jupiter (33–54 min)
  3. Simulate lunar delay: text a partner, but enforce a 2.6-second round-trip pause before reading each reply
  4. Simulate Mars delay: enforce an 8–48 minute round-trip pause. Try to coordinate a simple task under this constraint
  5. Discuss: how would mission control procedures change when real-time guidance is impossible?
speed of light communication constraints mission planning
Connection to Artemis II: Mission Control at JSC communicates with Orion through the Deep Space Network. At maximum lunar distance, commands take 1.3 seconds to arrive. The crew must handle emergencies semi-autonomously during that gap. This project makes the constraint visceral — and reveals why future Mars crews will need far more onboard autonomy than any previous mission.
Project 6 — Navigation

Build a Star Tracker

Difficulty
★★★
advanced
Time
4–6 hours
hardware + software + calibration
Cost
~$60–80
Raspberry Pi + camera module

Build a device that photographs the night sky, identifies stars in the image, and calculates which direction the camera is pointing. This is the same principle behind Orion's star tracker navigation system, which determines the spacecraft's attitude by matching observed star patterns against a catalog of known star positions.

Materials

  • Raspberry Pi (3B+ or later)
  • Raspberry Pi Camera Module (v2 or HQ)
  • MicroSD card (16GB+) with Raspberry Pi OS
  • astrometry.net (open-source plate solving software)
  • Tripod or stable mount
  • Clear night with low light pollution

Steps

  1. Set up Raspberry Pi with camera module and install astrometry.net: sudo apt install astrometry.net
  2. Download the appropriate index files for your camera's field of view (astrometry.net provides these freely)
  3. Mount the camera on a tripod, point at the night sky, and capture a long-exposure image (2–10 seconds)
  4. Run the plate solver: solve-field your_image.fits
  5. The solver identifies stars in your image, matches them against the catalog, and returns precise RA/Dec coordinates
  6. Compare the computed pointing direction against where you know the camera was aimed — measure the accuracy
astrometry coordinate systems computer vision Raspberry Pi
Connection to Artemis II: Orion's star trackers are a critical navigation backup. If GPS and ground-based tracking were both lost, the spacecraft could determine its orientation from star observations alone — the same method sailors used for centuries, now automated with cameras and pattern-matching algorithms. Your Raspberry Pi version uses the exact same plate-solving mathematics.
Project 7 — Space Weather

Space Weather Monitor

Difficulty
★★★
intermediate
Time
2–3 hours
coding + dashboard design
Cost
$0
NOAA data is public

Build a live dashboard that pulls real-time space weather data from NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center — the same data source NASA mission planners monitor when deciding whether conditions are safe for extravehicular activity or, in extreme cases, whether to shelter crew in a more shielded area of the spacecraft.

Materials

  • Python 3 with requests and matplotlib (or a web stack: HTML/CSS/JavaScript)
  • NOAA SWPC JSON API (free, no API key required)
  • Optional: Plotly or Grafana for a more polished dashboard

Steps

  1. Fetch the planetary Kp index from services.swpc.noaa.gov/products/noaa-planetary-k-index.json
  2. Fetch solar wind speed and density from services.swpc.noaa.gov/products/solar-wind/plasma-7-day.json
  3. Fetch the IMF Bz component (interplanetary magnetic field) from services.swpc.noaa.gov/products/solar-wind/mag-7-day.json
  4. Display current values with color-coded alerts: green (quiet), yellow (unsettled), red (storm)
  5. Add threshold alerts: Kp ≥ 5 means geomagnetic storm; Bz strongly southward (< -10 nT) means increased radiation risk
  6. Run the dashboard continuously and check it during the Artemis II mission window
space weather API consumption data visualization Python
Connection to Artemis II: Space weather directly affects crew safety. A solar particle event during the mission would expose the crew to elevated radiation levels, especially beyond the Van Allen belts where Earth's magnetic field provides no shielding. The Saudi Arabian Space Weather CubeSat-1 will measure these conditions in situ. Your dashboard monitors the same environment from the ground.
Project 8 — Lunar Science

Lunar Observation Session

Difficulty
★★★
beginner
Time
1–2 hours
clear night required
Cost
$0–50
binoculars optional

Observe the same lunar features assigned to the Artemis II crew for visual documentation during their flyby. The crew will photograph and describe approximately 30 targets including impact craters, volcanic mare, mountain ranges, and the Orientale basin — a massive multi-ring impact structure on the Moon's western limb. You can see many of these from your backyard.

Materials

  • Binoculars (7x50 or 10x50 are ideal) or a small telescope (60–80mm aperture)
  • Lunar map or atlas (free printable versions available from lunar observation societies)
  • Sketchbook and pencil (or a smartphone camera held to the eyepiece)
  • Red flashlight to preserve night vision
  • Clear night with the Moon above the horizon (first or last quarter is best for surface detail)

Steps

  1. Plan your session: check the Moon's phase and identify which features will be near the terminator (the day/night boundary), where shadows reveal the most surface detail
  2. Start with naked-eye observation: identify the major mare (dark plains) — Mare Tranquillitatis, Mare Serenitatis, Oceanus Procellarum
  3. With binoculars: locate prominent craters — Tycho (bright ray system), Copernicus (terraced walls), Aristarchus (brightest spot on the Moon)
  4. With a telescope: sketch the Orientale basin region on the western limb (best seen during favorable libration)
  5. For each feature, note the date, time, instrument used, seeing conditions, and make a sketch or photograph
  6. Compare your observations to NASA imagery of the same features — the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter Camera provides high-resolution reference images
lunar geology observation techniques scientific sketching selenography
Connection to Artemis II: The crew's visual observation campaign continues a tradition stretching back to Apollo. Human eyes notice details that cameras sometimes miss — subtle color variations, transient lighting effects, and context that helps geologists interpret surface processes. Your observation session parallels exactly what the crew will do as they sweep past the Moon at closest approach.
