Grand Rapids to Jacksonville

Christina Hammock was born on January 29, 1979, in Grand Rapids, Michigan, and raised in Jacksonville, North Carolina — a military town pressed against the edge of Camp Lejeune and the coastal plain. She grew up near the sea, near the base, near the kind of people who understand that service means showing up when it's hard.

She was the kind of kid who wanted to know how things worked. Not in the abstract — in the specific. How does the circuit close? What does the antenna actually receive? Where does the signal go after it leaves the dish? She followed those questions all the way to North Carolina State University, where she didn't just pick one discipline. She picked two.

The Engineer

At NC State, Koch earned two bachelor's degrees simultaneously — Electrical Engineering and Physics — in 2001. She completed her Master of Science in Electrical Engineering the following year, in 2002. The combination tells you everything about how she thinks: she wanted the theory and the application. The equations and the hardware. The physics of the signal and the engineering of the instrument that captures it.

She went to work at NASA Goddard Space Flight Center as an electrical engineer, developing instruments for space science missions. Not flying the missions yet — building the tools that make the science possible. The instruments that see what human eyes cannot. The detectors, the receivers, the signal chains that turn photons and particles into data.

The Ice

Before Christina Koch went to space, she went to the most space-like place on Earth.

As a research associate with the United States Antarctic Program, Koch was stationed at Palmer Station on the Antarctic Peninsula and at American Samoa. But the assignment that would prove most consequential for her future was the winter-over at Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station — a yearlong deployment that included the long polar night, when the sun does not rise for six months.

A winter-over at the South Pole is one of the closest analogs to deep space isolation that exists on Earth. The station is cut off from the outside world from February to October. No flights in. No flights out. No evacuation. No rescue. The temperature drops below −70°F. The wind carries ice crystals that strip exposed skin. And the darkness — months of it, unbroken — tests something in you that no training facility can simulate.

Koch survived it. She thrived in it. The kind of psychological resilience required to maintain focus, professionalism, and good humor through months of isolation in extreme conditions is exactly the kind of resilience required for a mission to the Moon. The Antarctic Program didn't train her for space. It proved she was already ready.

The darkness, the cold, the months of no sun. If anyone is psychologically prepared for the isolation of deep space — for the silence, the distance, the knowledge that home is a quarter million miles behind you — it's someone who wintered at the Pole.

Astronaut

NASA selected Christina Koch as an astronaut candidate in 2013. She reported to Johnson Space Center and completed two years of training — the full curriculum of systems, EVA, robotics, T-38 flying, survival, Russian language. She was assigned to her first mission in 2019.

What happened next broke records.

ISS Expeditions 59, 60, and 61 (March 2019 – February 2020)

Koch launched to the International Space Station on March 14, 2019, aboard Soyuz MS-12. She was supposed to come home in October. Instead, NASA extended her mission. She stayed until February 6, 2020 — a continuous duration of 328 days in space.

It was the longest single spaceflight by a woman in history.

During those 328 days, Koch performed six spacewalks totaling 42 hours and 15 minutes outside the station. On October 18, 2019, she and NASA astronaut Jessica Meir conducted the first all-female spacewalk in history — 60 years after the first humans walked in space, 55 years after the first woman reached orbit. Koch and Meir would go on to perform the second and third all-female spacewalks as well, turning what had been a milestone into a new normal.

The Arc of History

There is a direct line that runs through the history of women in space — from the Mercury 13, who were tested and then told no, through Valentina Tereshkova (1963), Sally Ride (1983), Mae Jemison (1992), Peggy Whitson, and Jessica Meir. Each one opened a door. Christina Koch walks through the last one.

The computer wore a skirt. Then the first woman flew in space. Then the first American woman. Then the first woman of color. Then the first all-female spacewalk. Then the longest spaceflight by a woman. Then the first woman beyond low Earth orbit.

The line is unbroken. Katherine Johnson calculated the trajectory. Christina Koch flies it.

Selected for Artemis II

On April 3, 2023, NASA announced the crew of Artemis II — the first crewed mission to the Moon since Apollo 17 in 1972. Christina Koch was named Mission Specialist 1.

She is the first woman to travel beyond low Earth orbit. The first woman to fly toward the Moon. The first woman to see the far side of the Moon with her own eyes. After more than half a century of human spaceflight, after 65 years of women fighting for the right to fly, after every record broken and every barrier cleared — she is the one who goes.

Mission Role

As Mission Specialist 1 aboard Orion, Koch is responsible for spacecraft systems management, science objectives, and mission documentation. Her engineering background — the years at Goddard building instruments, the dual degrees in EE and Physics, the 328 days learning every system aboard the ISS — makes her the crew member who understands both the theory behind the spacecraft and the reality of operating it.

She has already proven she can live in space for nearly a year. The Artemis II mission is approximately 10 days. She has done this thirty-two times over.

Connection to Artemis II

On April 1, 2026, Christina Koch launched aboard Orion on a trajectory to the Moon. The path she flies is a descendant of the mathematics Katherine Johnson performed by hand in the 1960s — the same orbital mechanics, the same celestial geometry, the same unforgiving physics that demands the math be right.

When Koch looks out the window of Orion and sees the Moon growing larger — not as a dot in the sky but as a world, close enough to see craters, close enough to see shadows on the surface — she will be the first woman in history to see it from that distance. Every woman who flew before her made this moment possible. Every calculation Katherine Johnson performed led to this trajectory.
