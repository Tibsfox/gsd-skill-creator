The Canadarm Heritage

Four decades of robotic arms that built the space program
Canadarm
The original Shuttle Remote Manipulator System. Flew on 90 Space Shuttle missions, deployed the Hubble Space Telescope, and assembled the International Space Station piece by piece.
1981–2011 · 90 missions · 15.2 meters
Canadarm2
The ISS robotic arm. 17.6 meters of articulated precision, used for every major ISS assembly task — module berthing, cargo capture, crew support, and external maintenance.
2001–present · 17.6 meters · ISS permanent installation
Dextre (SPDM)
The two-armed Special Purpose Dexterous Manipulator. Performs delicate external ISS maintenance that would otherwise require risky and time-consuming spacewalks.
2008–present · dual-arm dexterous robot

Canada's role in Artemis II is the culmination of a partnership that began with the Space Shuttle program in 1981. The original Canadarm transformed how NASA assembled and serviced hardware in orbit, and its successors — Canadarm2 and Dextre — became indispensable to ISS operations. When NASA designed the Artemis architecture, Canada's commitment to build Canadarm3 for the Gateway station made the country an essential partner in the return to the Moon.

That commitment is what put Jeremy Hansen on the crew. His selection as Mission Specialist 2 was not symbolic — it was contractual, tied directly to Canada's C$2.05 billion investment in lunar exploration infrastructure. Hansen brings fighter pilot discipline, CAPCOM experience, and NEEMO underwater analog training to a crew already rich in operational expertise. His 41-minute manual control evaluation on Flight Day 4, conducted alongside Christina Koch, demonstrated that the international dimension of Artemis is substantive, not ceremonial.

The Canadian Space Agency's astronaut bench extends beyond Hansen. Jenni Gibbons, selected by CSA in 2017, served as both backup crew member and active CAPCOM throughout the mission. The agreement that secured Canadarm3 also guarantees future Canadian astronaut assignments to Gateway, ensuring that Canada's presence in deep space exploration will continue well beyond Artemis II.
