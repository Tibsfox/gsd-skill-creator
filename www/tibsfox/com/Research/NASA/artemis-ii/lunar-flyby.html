The farthest humans have traveled from Earth since Apollo 17 (December 1972)

What Happens at Closest Approach

Flight Day 7 · ~MET +5d 14h · April 7, 2026

Orion passes approximately 4,000 miles (~6,400 km) above the lunar far side on a free-return trajectory. No engine burn is required — the Moon's gravity bends the spacecraft's path and sends it back toward Earth. This is the moment the crew will be the farthest any humans have been from Earth since Gene Cernan, Harrison Schmitt, and Ron Evans in December 1972. The far side pass means Loss of Signal (LOS) for approximately 40 minutes as the Moon blocks direct Earth communication.

Free-Return Trajectory

Unlike Apollo missions that entered lunar orbit, Artemis II uses a free-return trajectory — a figure-eight path around the Moon that returns to Earth without requiring additional propulsion. If all systems fail, the spacecraft still comes home. This is the same trajectory used by Apollo 8 (first crewed lunar flyby, December 1968) and Apollo 13 (emergency free return, April 1970). The trajectory was computed by the same group at JSC that computed Apollo trajectories, using modern tools but the same orbital mechanics.

What to Watch For
Science, engineering, and historical significance

Far Side Photography

The crew will photograph the lunar far side through Orion's windows — the first human eyes to see it since Apollo 17's command module pilot Ron Evans in December 1972. Key targets: Tsiolkovsky crater (dark-floored, visible from orbit), South Pole-Aitken Basin (the largest known impact structure in the solar system), and the Von Kármán crater where China's Chang'e 4 lander sits. These photographs will be compared against Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter data.

Deep Space Communication

During the ~40-minute Loss of Signal behind the Moon, the crew will be completely out of contact with Earth. The Deep Space Network (DSN) dishes at Goldstone (California), Madrid (Spain), and Canberra (Australia) will track Orion's signal before and after the far side pass. DSN Now shows real-time tracking.

Radiation Environment

At closest approach, the crew is fully exposed to the deep space radiation environment with no magnetosphere or lunar surface shielding. The Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessor (HERA) aboard Orion continuously monitors radiation levels. Data from the flyby will inform Artemis III surface EVA planning and future Gateway station shielding requirements. GCR (Galactic Cosmic Rays) are the primary source; any solar particle events during the flyby would be a significant test of Orion's storm shelter.

Navigation and Trajectory

The free-return trajectory requires no engine burns at the Moon — but precise navigation is critical. Star trackers, the optical navigation camera, and ground-based tracking confirm Orion's path. The gravity assist effect accelerates Orion for the return trip. Any trajectory correction maneuvers (TCMs) would happen before or after the flyby, not during it. The Optical Communications (O2O) laser link will be tested during the flyby approach.

Thermal Environment

The thermal environment shifts dramatically during the flyby. In the Moon's shadow (if the trajectory passes through it), temperatures drop rapidly. The crew has already demonstrated thermal problem-solving with the Window 1 black t-shirt improvisation and AZ9 panel deployment on FD4. The flyby's thermal profile will validate Orion's heat rejection system design for the Artemis III lunar orbit mission.

53 years, 3 months, 27 days — the gap between Apollo 17's lunar flyby (December 11, 1972) and Artemis II's (April 7, 2026). This is the longest gap in human spaceflight history between visits to the same celestial body. The four Artemis II crew members — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen — are the first humans to see the Moon up close since Ron Evans looked out the window of the Apollo 17 command module America.
Observation Cadence
Data collection plan for the flyby window

Peak Cadence: April 7, 06:00–18:00 EDT

During the 12-hour flyby window, sweeps increase to every 15 minutes (4x normal cadence). Each sweep captures: space weather (Kp, Bz, solar wind), DSN tracking status, live stream state, and any NASA briefing data. This produces approximately 48 sub-version data points covering the entire approach, closest point, and departure.

LOS Window: Special Handling

During the ~40-minute Loss of Signal behind the Moon, DSN will show no active link. This is expected and nominal. The sweep daemon will note "LOS — far side pass" rather than flagging a communication failure. AOS (Acquisition of Signal) will be a key milestone — confirmation that Orion has safely rounded the Moon.

Post-Flyby Analysis

After the flyby window closes, a synthesis sweep will aggregate all 48+ data points into a flyby summary: peak distance, LOS/AOS timing, radiation environment, thermal performance, and communication quality. This summary will be added to the mission timeline and linked from the LIVE page.
