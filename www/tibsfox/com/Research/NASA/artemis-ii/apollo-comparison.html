54 Years

The Longest Gap in Human Deep Space Exploration

On December 14, 1972, Gene Cernan climbed back into the lunar module Challenger and left the surface of the Moon. No human has traveled beyond low Earth orbit since. Fifty-four years later, in April 2026, Artemis II sends four astronauts around the Moon on a free-return trajectory — the first crewed deep space mission of the 21st century.

The physics haven't changed. The Moon is still 240,000 miles away. Light still takes 1.3 seconds to get there. The reentry corridor is still razor-thin. But the technology, the crew, and the ambition behind the mission look nothing like Apollo. This page puts the two eras side by side.

Historic Firsts

Firsts Achieved by Artemis II

  • First woman to fly to the Moon
    Christina Koch — Mission Specialist 1. Fourth woman to fly on a crewed lunar mission profile (none flew during Apollo).
  • First person of color at the Moon
    Victor Glover — Pilot. First Black astronaut on a deep space mission.
  • First non-American beyond low Earth orbit
    Jeremy Hansen — Mission Specialist 2. Canadian Space Agency. All 24 Apollo lunar astronauts were American.
  • Most humans in deep space simultaneously
    4 crew members. Apollo carried 3. No other program has sent humans beyond LEO.
  • Farthest humans from Earth
    252,760 miles — exceeding Apollo 13's record of 248,655 miles set during the emergency free-return in April 1970.
  • First laser communications from crewed deep-space mission
    O2O optical terminal providing 260 Mbps downlink. Apollo used S-band radio at ~51 kbps — a 5,000x improvement.
  • Fastest crewed reentry
    ~25,000 mph (Mach 32). Returning from a trajectory that reaches farther than any previous crewed mission.

Orbital Mechanics

What Didn't Change

The laws of physics don't care about the calendar. Every constraint that shaped Apollo mission planning applies identically to Artemis II. The Moon is the same distance away, gravity works the same way, and the speed of light hasn't increased.

  • Free-return trajectory geometry is identical
    Same Moon, same gravity, same figure-eight path around the Earth-Moon system. The mathematics Euler described in the 18th century still governs the route.
  • Travel time to Moon: ~3 days
    Still limited by the same orbital mechanics. Chemical rockets produce the same delta-v. The transfer orbit hasn't gotten shorter.
  • Radiation environment unchanged
    Same Van Allen belts, same galactic cosmic rays, same solar cycle risks. The crew transits the same radiation hazards Apollo astronauts faced.
  • Signal delay: 1.3 seconds to the Moon
    The speed of light hasn't changed. Round-trip communication delay at lunar distance is still ~2.6 seconds. No technology can fix this.
  • Reentry corridor: ±0.5°
    Too steep and the spacecraft burns up. Too shallow and it skips off the atmosphere into deep space. The margin is the same razor-thin window Apollo faced.
The fundamental lesson: Technology advances. Physics does not. Every improvement in computers, communications, and materials serves one purpose — to operate more precisely within the same physical constraints that Apollo navigated with slide rules and sextants.
Across the Generations

Personal Connections

The thread between Apollo and Artemis isn't only technical. The people who flew those missions are still alive, still watching, and still connected to the crews who follow them.

  • Jim Lovell sent recorded encouragement to the Artemis II crew
    The Apollo 8 and Apollo 13 commander — the first human to fly to the Moon twice — sent a message received on Flight Day 6.
  • Apollo astronaut Charlie Duke sent a special message
    Duke, who walked on the Moon during Apollo 16, sent a message received on Flight Day 5. One of the last living moonwalkers reaching out to the next generation.
  • Crew proposed naming two lunar craters
    "Integrity" for the spacecraft and "Carroll" honoring Commander Wiseman's late wife — carrying personal meaning alongside the mission's technical objectives.
  • Apollo 17 was the last crewed lunar mission
    December 14, 1972. Gene Cernan's final footprints. 54 years, 3 months, and counting until Artemis II closes the gap.
Who Flies

The Diversity Arc

The Apollo program sent 24 astronauts to the Moon between 1968 and 1972. All 24 were white American men. This was not a technical requirement — it was a reflection of who had access to military test pilot programs and NASA's astronaut selection pipeline in the 1960s.

The Artemis commitment: The Artemis program commits to landing the first woman and first person of color on the lunar surface on Artemis III and subsequent missions. Artemis II is the proving flight — demonstrating that the vehicle, the trajectory, and the crew support systems work before anyone sets foot on the Moon again.

Two women were short-listed for the Artemis II crew; one was selected. The crew represents three demographic firsts simultaneously — not as a symbolic gesture, but because the astronaut corps in 2026 reflects a broader pool of talent than it did in 1968. The selection criteria haven't been lowered. The pipeline was opened.
