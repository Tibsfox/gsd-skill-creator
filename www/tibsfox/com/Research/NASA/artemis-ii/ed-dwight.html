The Boy Who Loved Airplanes

Edward Joseph Dwight Jr. was born on September 9, 1933, in Kansas City, Kansas. His father, Ed Dwight Sr., played second base for the Kansas City Monarchs in baseball's Negro Leagues — the same league that produced Jackie Robinson, Satchel Paige, and Buck O'Neil. Both of his parents were children of enslaved people. One generation from slavery to the sky.

Ed loved airplanes from the moment he first saw one. As a boy, he worked at a small airport near his home and asked for airplane rides as payment instead of money. He created his first oil painting at age eight. Art and flight — the two threads that would define his life — were present from the beginning.

His mother, Georgia Baker Dwight, fought for his education. When the local Catholic high school refused to admit her son because of his race, she appealed directly to the Vatican. The Vatican intervened. Ed attended the school. His mother understood that doors don't open themselves — sometimes you have to go to Rome.

The Test Pilot

Dwight enlisted in the U.S. Air Force in 1953, the same year the Korean War ended. He was commissioned as a lieutenant in 1955. While training to fly, he attended night classes at Arizona State University and graduated cum laude in 1957 with a Bachelor of Science in aeronautical engineering. He earned the rank of Captain.

He accumulated over 9,000 flight hours — an extraordinary number that reflects years of sustained, high-performance flying. He became a test pilot, one of the most demanding jobs in aviation. Test pilots fly aircraft at the edges of their performance envelopes, where the engineering models end and the unknown begins. It requires mathematical precision, physical endurance, and the willingness to sit on top of something that might kill you and push it harder.

The President's Astronaut

In 1961, President John F. Kennedy was building the New Frontier. The civil rights movement was reshaping America. Whitney Young, head of the National Urban League, urged Kennedy to integrate the space program. Kennedy agreed. A letter arrived from the Pentagon, authorized by the President himself, asking Captain Ed Dwight if he would like to become the first Black astronaut.

He reported to the Aerospace Research Pilot School (ARPS) at Edwards Air Force Base in California — the same program that had produced NASA astronauts, the same desert where Chuck Yeager had broken the sound barrier. This was the pipeline to space. Ed Dwight was in it.

The media coverage was immediate and intense. Ebony, Jet, and Sepia put him on their covers. Here was a Black test pilot, cum laude in aeronautical engineering, 9,000 flight hours, selected by the President of the United States to go to space. The story wrote itself.

The Air Force had a different story in mind.

The Wall

The commander of the Aerospace Research Pilot School was Colonel Chuck Yeager — the most famous test pilot in history, the man who broke the sound barrier. Yeager did not want Ed Dwight in his program.

You have to isolate him. Don't drink with him. Don't invite him to your parties. — Ed Dwight, describing Yeager's instructions to other students

Dwight completed the training. He advanced to the second round. He met every technical requirement. He was qualified. But in 1963, when NASA selected 14 new astronauts from 720 applicants, Ed Dwight was not among them. Buzz Aldrin — who would walk on the Moon six years later — was selected instead.

On November 22, 1963, President Kennedy was assassinated in Dallas. With Kennedy gone, Dwight lost his political support. He was transferred to Wright-Patterson Air Force Base in Ohio, away from the flight line, away from space. In 1966, he resigned from the Air Force.

He was 32 years old. He had 9,000 flight hours, a degree in aeronautical engineering, and a letter from a dead president. He had never been to space.

The first Black American in space was Guion Bluford, who flew on STS-8 in August 1983 — twenty-two years after Ed Dwight was selected as an astronaut candidate. Twenty-two years of other people going where he was told he would go.

The Sculptor

After leaving the Air Force, Dwight worked at IBM. But the other thread — the one that started with an oil painting at age eight — pulled him back. In the mid-1970s, he enrolled at the University of Denver, learned to operate the university's metal casting foundry, and earned a Master of Fine Arts in sculpture in 1977.

He found a way to put people into space after all. Not in capsules — in bronze.

Ed Dwight became one of America's most prolific sculptors of African American history. Over the next five decades, he created 132 memorial sculptures and over 20,000 gallery pieces — paintings and sculptures that document, honor, and preserve the history that the country he served tried to erase.

Notable Sculptures

  • Frederick Douglass (1978) — Life-size bronze, commissioned by the National Park Service for Douglass's historic home, Washington, D.C.
  • "Jazz: An American Art Form" (1979+) — Series of 70+ bronze sculptures. Louis Armstrong, Miles Davis, Duke Ellington, Ella Fitzgerald, Charlie Parker. The music cast in metal.
  • "The Gateway to Freedom" (2001) — Detroit, Michigan. The Underground Railroad's final stop before Canada. People reaching for freedom, cast in bronze at the riverbank.
  • "Black Frontier in the American West" — Series depicting Black contributions during the frontier era — the cowboys, soldiers, settlers, and families that history books left out.
  • Barack Obama Inauguration (2010) — Life-size bronze of the Obama family and inauguration attendees. Toured as museum exhibit: "The Inauguration of Hope."
  • Martin Luther King Jr. — At least seven statues across the country. When you see King in bronze in a public square, there's a good chance Ed Dwight made it.
  • Harriet Tubman — Multiple sculptures of the abolitionist and Civil War operative.

His studio is in Denver, Colorado. He works every day. He is 92 years old.

The Flight

On May 19, 2024 — sixty-three years after a president asked him to go to space — Ed Dwight flew.

Blue Origin's New Shepard NS-25 carried him above the Kármán line, 100 kilometers above the Earth, into space. The flight lasted approximately 10 minutes. He was 90 years, 8 months, and 10 days old — the oldest person to ever fly in space, surpassing William Shatner's record.

His ticket was sponsored by Space for Humanity, a nonprofit. Commercial tickets on New Shepard cost up to $28 million. He didn't pay. Someone who understood what it meant made sure he got there.

He described the experience as "life-changing."

He had waited 63 years.

Connection to Artemis II

On April 2, 2026, Pilot Victor Glover — a Black naval aviator and NASA astronaut — manually flew the Orion spacecraft during proximity operations and sat at the controls during the trans-lunar injection burn that sent Integrity toward the Moon. He is the second Black astronaut to serve as spacecraft commander/pilot on a lunar mission, after no Black astronaut was ever assigned to Apollo.

Sixty-five years after Ed Dwight was told he would go to space, and then told he wouldn't, a Black pilot is flying to the Moon. The line runs through Ed Dwight. He pushed against a door that wouldn't open, and the people who came after him walked through it.

He didn't get to go on Apollo. He went in bronze instead — 132 sculptures, 20,000 pieces, a lifetime of casting the history that was denied to him into metal that will outlast everyone who denied it. And then, at 90, he finally went himself.

I was not just an astronaut candidate. I was a symbol. And symbols don't get to fail quietly. — Ed Dwight

The Two Threads

Ed Dwight's life is defined by two threads that started in childhood and never broke: flight and art. The Air Force gave him flight and then took it away. Art gave him a way to keep building — not capsules but monuments, not trajectories but legacies. When the system told him he couldn't put himself in space, he put everyone else in bronze.

He is 92 years old. He works every day. He has been to space. He was always going.
