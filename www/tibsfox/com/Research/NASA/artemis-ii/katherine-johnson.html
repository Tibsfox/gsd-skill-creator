The Counting Girl from White Sulphur Springs

Katherine Coleman was born on August 26, 1918, in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia — a small town in the Allegheny Mountains where her father, Joshua Coleman, was a farmer and handyman. From the earliest age, she counted everything. Steps to the road. Steps up to church. The number of dishes and silverware she washed. The stars she could see from the porch.

Her mathematical ability was so extraordinary that she entered high school at ten and graduated at fourteen. In 1930s segregated West Virginia, education for Black children often ended after eighth grade. Her father drove the family 120 miles — each way — to Institute, West Virginia, so she could attend high school. He took a job as a handyman at the school to make it possible.

At West Virginia State College, she found Professor W.W. Schiefflin Claytor — only the third African American to earn a PhD in mathematics. He recognized what she was and created courses specifically for her. He told her: "You'd make a good research mathematician and I'm going to see that you're prepared." She graduated summa cum laude in 1937 with a bachelor's degree in mathematics and French. She was eighteen years old.

The West Area Computers

In 1952, while on vacation from a teaching position that paid $100 a month, Katherine learned that the Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory — part of the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) — was hiring Black women as "computers." Human computers. They performed by hand the complex mathematical calculations that engineers needed but couldn't do fast enough themselves.

The West Area Computing unit was segregated. Separate bathrooms. Separate cafeteria. Separate office. The women who worked there — Dorothy Vaughan, Mary Jackson, Katherine Johnson, and others — did the same work as their white colleagues, to the same standard, with the same rigor, under conditions designed to remind them they were considered less.

Katherine Johnson did not accept the conditions. She asked questions in briefings where no woman, and certainly no Black woman, had asked questions before. When told that women didn't attend editorial meetings, she asked: "Is there a law against it?" There wasn't. She attended.

The Calculations That Mattered

Freedom 7 — Alan Shepard (May 5, 1961)

America's first human spaceflight. Johnson calculated the trajectory for Shepard's suborbital flight — working backward from the desired Atlantic Ocean landing point to determine the precise launch window and flight path. She analyzed the problem as a parabolic trajectory with atmospheric drag, wind effects, and Earth's rotation. The math had to be right. There was no backup computer. There was no second chance. She was the computer.

Shepard splashed down 302 miles downrange, within sight of the recovery ship. The math was right.

Friendship 7 — John Glenn (February 20, 1962)

The first American to orbit the Earth. By 1962, NASA had installed IBM 7090 electronic computers to calculate orbital trajectories. But the machines were new and the code was new, and nobody fully trusted them. Astronaut John Glenn, about to strap himself on top of an Atlas rocket and trust his life to the numbers, made a specific request:

Get the girl. Check the numbers. If she says they're good, then I am ready to go. — John Glenn, referring to Katherine Johnson, February 1962

Katherine hand-verified the IBM's orbital calculations. The trajectory equations for an object orbiting Earth, accounting for gravitational perturbations, atmospheric drag at orbital altitude, and re-entry dynamics. She confirmed the numbers. Glenn flew. Three orbits. Splashdown in the Atlantic. The math was right.

She later reflected: "The early trajectory was a parabola, and it was easy to predict where it would be at any point. Early on, when they said they wanted the capsule to come down at a certain place, they were trying to compute when it should start. I said, 'Let me do it. You tell me when you want it and where you want it to land, and I'll do it backwards and tell you when to take off.'"

Apollo 11 — The Moon (July 20, 1969)

Katherine Johnson calculated the trajectory that carried Neil Armstrong, Buzz Aldrin, and Michael Collins to the Moon and back. The trans-lunar injection parameters. The lunar orbit insertion. The descent trajectory. The ascent from the lunar surface. The trans-Earth injection. The re-entry corridor — an angle so precise that too shallow meant bouncing off the atmosphere into space, and too steep meant burning up.

She co-authored the 1960 technical report "Determination of Azimuth Angle at Burnout for Placing a Satellite Over a Selected Earth Position" — one of the foundational documents for orbital spaceflight. She was the first woman in the Flight Research Division to receive credit as an author on a research report.

Apollo 13 — Saving the Crew (April 1970)

When Apollo 13's service module oxygen tank exploded 200,000 miles from Earth, the crew needed an emergency trajectory to get home. Johnson had already prepared backup navigation charts and procedures for exactly this scenario. Her contingency return-to-Earth calculations — done by hand, verified by hand — provided the crew with a path home using the lunar module as a lifeboat.

Lovell, Swigert, and Haise came home. The math was right.

Space Shuttle Program (1970s–1986)

Johnson contributed to the early development of the Space Shuttle program before her retirement in 1986, after 33 years at NASA. She never missed a day of work. She went to work happy. Every single day.

The Mathematics

What Katherine Johnson did was not simple arithmetic at large scale. It was analytical geometry, differential equations, and celestial mechanics applied to problems that had never been solved before. The specific mathematical contributions include:

Trajectory Analysis
Parabolic & Orbital
Suborbital (Mercury), orbital (Gemini), translunar (Apollo). Each required different mathematical frameworks.
Launch Window Computation
Inverse Problem
"Tell me where you want to land, and I'll work backwards to tell you when to launch." Constrained optimization before the term existed.
Orbital Mechanics
Keplerian + Perturbation
Two-body solutions corrected for atmospheric drag, J2 oblateness, solar/lunar gravitational perturbations, all by hand.
Re-entry Dynamics
Skip Trajectory Analysis
The re-entry corridor is 2° wide. Too shallow = skip off atmosphere. Too steep = burn up. She calculated the path between.
Contingency Navigation
Apollo 13 Return Path
Emergency free-return trajectories using lunar gravity assist. Prepared before they were needed. Used when they were.
Verification Method
Hand vs. Machine
Independently verified IBM 7090 computer output for Friendship 7. The astronaut trusted her over the machine.

  • 1939 First Black woman to enroll in graduate studies at West Virginia University.
  • 1952 Hired by NACA Langley as a computer in the West Area Computing unit.

  • 1960 Co-authors "Determination of Azimuth Angle at Burnout for Placing a Satellite Over a Selected Earth Position" — first woman credited as author in Flight Research Division.

  • 1970 Contingency return calculations used during Apollo 13 emergency. Crew returns safely.
  • 1970s Contributes to Space Shuttle program development.
  • 1986 Retires from NASA after 33 years. Never missed a day.
  • 2015 Awarded Presidential Medal of Freedom by President Obama.
  • 2016 Portrayed by Taraji P. Henson in the film Hidden Figures.
  • 2017 NASA names the Katherine G. Johnson Computational Research Facility at Langley in her honor.
  • 2019 Awarded the Congressional Gold Medal.
  • 2020 Dies February 24 in Newport News, Virginia. Age 101.

Connection to Artemis II

On April 2, 2026, at 4:49 PM PDT, the Orion spacecraft Integrity fired its service module engine for 5 minutes and 49 seconds, adding 1,272 feet per second of velocity, and left Earth orbit on a free-return trajectory to the Moon. The trans-lunar injection calculation — the math that determines the precise moment, duration, and direction of the burn — is a direct descendant of Katherine Johnson's work on Apollo trajectory analysis.

The computers are faster now. The equations are the same. Every trajectory to the Moon passes through her hands first.
