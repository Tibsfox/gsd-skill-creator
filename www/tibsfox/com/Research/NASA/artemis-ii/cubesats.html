Overview

Secondary Payloads from the Orion Stage Adapter

Artemis II carries four CubeSat secondary payloads from international partners, all deployed from the Orion Stage Adapter (OSA) after trans-lunar injection. Each satellite uses the standardized 12U form factor — roughly the size of a large briefcase — and each comes from an Artemis Accords signatory nation. A fifth bay in the adapter ring is occupied by an Avionics Unit for spacecraft systems testing.

After separation from the Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage, the CubeSats enter a highly elliptical Earth orbit that carries them through the Van Allen radiation belts and into cislunar space. This trajectory provides a radiation environment far more intense than low Earth orbit — exactly the conditions that future crewed lunar missions will face, and exactly what these experiments are designed to measure.

Germany · DLR

TACHELES

12U CubeSat — Electrical Component Testing Deutsches Zentrum für Luft- und Raumfahrt

TACHELES tests how the deep space radiation environment affects electrical components intended for future lunar vehicles. Commercial and space-rated electronics behave differently when exposed to galactic cosmic rays and solar energetic particles beyond Earth's magnetosphere. Understanding these degradation modes is essential for designing reliable avionics for sustained lunar surface operations.

The German Aerospace Center (DLR) designed this experiment to characterize single-event effects, total ionizing dose accumulation, and displacement damage in semiconductors and circuit boards under actual deep space conditions — data that ground-based particle accelerator testing can only approximate.

Argentina · CONAE

ATENEA

12U CubeSat — Radiation Shielding & Communications Comisión Nacional de Actividades Espaciales

ATENEA carries a multi-objective payload: radiation shielding assessment, spectrum mapping of the deep space environment, GPS signal reception at lunar distances, and long-distance communication system validation. Each of these measurements addresses a different challenge that future crewed missions beyond low Earth orbit will face.

The radiation shielding experiments evaluate how effectively different materials attenuate the particle flux encountered along cislunar trajectories. GPS data collected at extreme range tests the outer limits of navigation satellite coverage — information directly relevant to planning autonomous navigation for lunar transit vehicles. The communication validation tests signal integrity and data throughput over distances that dwarf anything in LEO operations.

South Korea · KASA

K-RadCube

12U CubeSat — Biological Radiation Dosimetry Korea Aerospace Administration

K-RadCube carries a human-tissue-equivalent dosimeter — an instrument that measures radiation absorption in a material designed to mimic the density and composition of human tissue. As the CubeSat traverses the Van Allen radiation belts on its highly elliptical orbit, the dosimeter records the biological radiation dose that a human body would accumulate along the same trajectory.

The payload also tests Samsung and SK hynix semiconductor components in the space radiation environment. South Korea's semiconductor industry produces a significant share of global memory and logic chips; characterizing how these devices perform under galactic cosmic ray and solar particle bombardment informs both space qualification standards and the design of radiation-tolerant computing hardware for future missions.

Why tissue-equivalent dosimetry matters: Standard radiation detectors measure raw particle flux. A tissue-equivalent dosimeter measures what actually matters for crew safety — the energy deposited in material that responds to radiation the same way human cells do. This gives mission planners a direct measurement of biological risk, not just a physics measurement that requires modeling to translate into health effects.
Saudi Arabia · Saudi Space Agency

Space Weather CubeSat-1 (SWC-1)

CubeSat — Cislunar Space Weather Monitoring Saudi Space Agency

SWC-1 studies the space weather environment along the cislunar corridor — the region between Earth and the Moon that all crewed lunar missions must traverse. The satellite carries instruments to measure solar X-rays, solar energetic particles, and magnetic field strength and orientation throughout its orbit.

Space weather is the single largest variable-risk factor for crewed missions beyond low Earth orbit. A solar energetic particle event during lunar transit could deliver a radiation dose exceeding crew safety limits within hours. SWC-1 provides direct measurements of the particle and electromagnetic environment that future mission planning systems need to model storm shelter timing, trajectory adjustments, and go/no-go decisions for extravehicular activities on the lunar surface.

Cislunar weather forecasting: Earth-orbiting space weather satellites measure conditions near our planet. SWC-1 extends that measurement baseline out to lunar distance, filling a gap in the observation network. Accurate space weather prediction for lunar missions requires knowing what conditions look like along the entire transit path, not just at the departure point.
5th OSA Bay

Avionics Unit

The fifth payload bay in the Orion Stage Adapter is occupied by an Avionics Unit rather than a CubeSat. This unit serves as a technology demonstration for next-generation spacecraft computing and control systems, testing avionics hardware in the deep space radiation and thermal environment without requiring a separate free-flying satellite.

Artemis Accords

International Cooperation

The four CubeSat payloads represent four nations across four continents: Germany (Europe), Argentina (South America), South Korea (Asia), and Saudi Arabia (Middle East). All four are signatories of the Artemis Accords — the multilateral framework establishing principles for peaceful, transparent, and interoperable exploration of the Moon, Mars, and beyond.

  • First Argentine payload beyond Earth orbit
    CONAE's ATENEA marks Argentina's entry into deep space science, extending the nation's satellite heritage beyond LEO for the first time.
  • First Saudi scientific satellite in cislunar space
    SWC-1 represents the Saudi Space Agency's first space weather instrument operating beyond low Earth orbit.
  • Korean semiconductor industry meets space qualification
    K-RadCube directly connects South Korea's globally dominant semiconductor sector with deep space hardware reliability requirements.
  • German deep space electronics heritage continues
    DLR extends its decades of space electronics research from LEO and interplanetary probes into the specific cislunar environment relevant to Artemis.
Why secondary payloads matter: CubeSat ride-shares transform the cost equation for deep space access. Instead of each nation funding a dedicated launch, partner agencies contribute focused experiments that deploy from unused capacity on a vehicle already going where they need data. The Orion Stage Adapter provides this capacity at effectively zero marginal cost to the primary mission, while generating science return for the entire Artemis partnership.
Mission Science

Connection to Artemis II Science Goals

All four CubeSats share a common thread: characterizing the radiation environment that crewed missions must survive. This directly supports Artemis II's broader science objectives, which include understanding how deep space conditions affect crew health, biological systems, and spacecraft hardware.

TACHELES and K-RadCube's semiconductor data feed into the same hardware qualification pipeline that will certify avionics for the Human Landing System and Gateway station. K-RadCube's tissue-equivalent dosimetry complements the AVATAR organ-on-chip and ARCHAR crew health investigations aboard Orion itself. ATENEA's shielding assessments inform the materials science behind radiation protection for future habitation modules. SWC-1's space weather data provides the environmental context needed to interpret all the other radiation measurements — without knowing what the Sun was doing during the measurement period, dosimetry data loses much of its predictive value.

The measurement network: Taken together, the CubeSats and Orion's onboard experiments create a distributed measurement network spanning multiple points along the cislunar trajectory. Orion's instruments record conditions at the crew location. The CubeSats, on their own orbits after deployment, provide spatially separated data points. This multi-point coverage is far more valuable for understanding the radiation environment than any single measurement location could provide alone.
