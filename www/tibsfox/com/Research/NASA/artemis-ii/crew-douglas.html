Chesapeake

Andre Douglas was born on December 11, 1985, in Miami, Florida, and raised in Chesapeake, Virginia — a city in the Hampton Roads metropolitan area, where the Chesapeake Bay meets the Atlantic and the Navy's presence is as constant as the tide. Hampton Roads is shipyard country. Norfolk Naval Station, the largest naval base in the world, is next door. Newport News Shipbuilding, where they build aircraft carriers, is across the water. Langley Air Force Base and NASA Langley Research Center are up the peninsula. A kid growing up in Chesapeake is surrounded by the machinery of national defense and the infrastructure of exploration whether they know it or not.

Douglas graduated from Western Branch High School in 2004, where he was a member of the National Honor Society. From Chesapeake, he went north — not to Annapolis, not to West Point, but to the United States Coast Guard Academy in New London, Connecticut. It is the smallest of the five federal service academies and the most overlooked. The Coast Guard does not have the fighter jets or the aircraft carriers. What it has is every coastline, every port, every waterway, every maritime emergency in the United States. It has search and rescue. It has ice operations. It has drug interdiction and migrant response and environmental protection and marine safety. The Coast Guard is the service that goes out when everyone else is coming in.

Douglas excelled. He served as regimental commander — the highest-ranking cadet position in the Corps of Cadets — and graduated in 2008 with a Bachelor of Science in Mechanical Engineering. He was named to the New England Men's and Women's Athletic Conference Track and Field All-Academic Team. He earned his commission and went to sea.

The Coast Guard

His first assignment was aboard the Coast Guard Cutter Vigilant, homeported at Cape Canaveral, Florida. The irony is almost too perfect: his first operational posting put him at the doorstep of Kennedy Space Center, the place where rockets launch, the place he would return to two decades later as a member of the Artemis program. But in 2008, Douglas wasn't watching rockets. He was standing watch on the bridge of a 210-foot medium endurance cutter, leading crewmembers through alien migrant interdictions in the Caribbean, humanitarian relief operations, and drug enforcement patrols. The work of the Coast Guard is immediate, physical, and consequential. People are in the water. Vessels are sinking. Contraband is moving. You respond.

From Vigilant, Douglas moved to the Coast Guard Marine Safety Center in Washington, D.C., where he served as a naval architect. He conducted hundreds of technical reviews for maritime compliance and provided global support on maritime casualty response. A naval architect in the Coast Guard is the person who determines whether a vessel's structure is sound, whether its stability calculations hold, whether it will survive the conditions it's designed for. When a ship fails — when a hull cracks, when a vessel capsizes, when something breaks that shouldn't have broken — the naval architect is called to figure out why.

He also served as a salvage engineer, damage control assistant, and officer of the deck. These are hands-on roles. Salvage engineering is the discipline of recovering vessels that have gone down or gone wrong. Damage control is the art of keeping a ship afloat when the ocean is trying to take it. Officer of the deck means you are the captain's direct representative on the bridge, responsible for the safe navigation of the vessel and the safety of everyone aboard.

Five Degrees

While serving on active duty and then after transitioning to civilian work, Andre Douglas collected degrees the way some people collect stamps. He earned five of them. This is not normal. Most astronauts have two or three. Douglas has five, from four different institutions, spanning mechanical engineering, naval architecture, electrical engineering, and systems engineering.

His undergraduate degree in Mechanical Engineering came from the Coast Guard Academy in 2008. Then he went to the University of Michigan in Ann Arbor, where he earned two master's degrees simultaneously in 2012: one in Mechanical Engineering and one in Naval Architecture and Marine Engineering. Michigan's naval architecture program is one of the oldest and most respected in the country — a direct pipeline to the shipbuilding industry, the Navy, and the Coast Guard's technical corps.

He then earned a Master of Science in Electrical and Computer Engineering from Johns Hopkins University in 2019, while working full-time at the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory. And finally, a Doctor of Philosophy in Systems Engineering from George Washington University in 2021 — the same year he was selected as a NASA astronaut candidate. Five degrees. Four universities. Thirteen years of continuous education layered on top of full-time professional work.

Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory

In 2015, Douglas transitioned from active duty Coast Guard to the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory — APL, the nation's largest university-affiliated research center, located in Laurel, Maryland, between Baltimore and Washington. APL builds spacecraft. APL built New Horizons, which flew past Pluto. APL built the Parker Solar Probe, which touches the Sun. APL built DART, the Double Asteroid Redirection Test — the first planetary defense mission in human history, the spacecraft that NASA deliberately crashed into an asteroid to see if we could deflect one.

Douglas worked on DART. He supported the fault management team during the spacecraft's development, the engineers who design the systems that keep a spacecraft alive when something goes wrong. Fault management is the discipline of anticipating failure and building in the responses before the failure happens. On a spacecraft 7 million miles from Earth, you cannot send a repair crew. The fault management has to work the first time, autonomously, or the mission is lost.

Beyond planetary defense, Douglas contributed to maritime robotics and ocean systems missions at APL. He was an unmanned surface vessel pilot through numerous real-time Navy operations, and he contributed to development efforts for improving the U.S. ballistic missile defense system. The range of his work — from ship stability calculations to asteroid deflection to ballistic missile defense to autonomous ocean vehicles — reflects a mind that moves between domains without losing depth in any of them.

Christina Koch, Mission Specialist 1 on Artemis II, also worked at Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory early in her career. The APL pipeline to the astronaut corps is real. When you build the spacecraft that explore the solar system, some of the engineers who build them eventually decide they want to ride one.

The Third Coast Guard Astronaut

In the entire history of human spaceflight, the United States Coast Guard has produced exactly three astronauts. The first was Bruce Melnick, Coast Guard Academy class of 1972, selected by NASA in 1987, who flew on the Space Shuttle Discovery in 1990. The second was Daniel Burbank, class of 1985, who flew three missions (STS-106, STS-115, and ISS Expedition 29/30) between 2000 and 2012. The third is Andre Douglas, class of 2008.

Three astronauts in the history of the service. Across nearly seven decades of American human spaceflight, the Coast Guard has sent three people. The Navy has sent hundreds. The Air Force has sent hundreds. The Marine Corps has sent dozens. The Army has sent dozens. The Coast Guard has sent three. Douglas is the rarest kind of astronaut — not just in his path, but in the branch he represents.

On March 19, 2024, Admiral Linda Fagan, the Commandant of the Coast Guard, presided over Douglas's commissioning ceremony at Coast Guard Headquarters in Washington, D.C. He was sworn in as a Commander in the Coast Guard Reserve. The service that trained him, that sent him to sea, that taught him to keep ships afloat and people alive, claimed him back. He is simultaneously a NASA astronaut and a Coast Guard officer. He carries both missions.

Astronaut Group 23

On December 6, 2021, NASA announced its newest class of astronaut candidates — ten people chosen from more than 12,000 applicants. Andre Douglas was one of them. The selection ratio was less than one in a thousand. He reported for duty at Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas, in January 2022.

Astronaut candidate training takes approximately two years. It covers spacecraft systems, spacewalk procedures, robotics, T-38 jet training, Russian language, wilderness survival, and the thousand other skills that prepare a human being to live and work in space. Douglas completed his training on March 5, 2024, and became eligible for spaceflight assignment.

Four months later, on July 3, 2024, NASA named him the backup crew member for Artemis II.

The Path to Artemis

Backup crew is not a consolation prize. It is the hardest seat on any mission. The backup trains exactly as hard as the prime crew. They learn every system, every procedure, every contingency. They prepare their body and their mind for a flight they may never take. And they do it knowing that the only way they fly is if something goes wrong for someone else — if a crewmate is injured, or ill, or otherwise unable to go. The backup hopes to be ready and hopes never to be needed.

Douglas trains alongside Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen. He knows the Orion spacecraft. He knows the mission profile. He knows the emergency procedures. If any of the NASA astronauts on the prime crew cannot fly, Douglas steps in. He is the insurance policy for the first crewed lunar mission in over fifty years.

But backup crew on Artemis II is also a statement about the future. NASA does not assign backup roles to astronauts it doesn't intend to fly. Douglas is being prepared not just for Artemis II but for whatever comes after — Artemis III, IV, V, the missions that will land on the lunar surface, build the Gateway station, and eventually reach Mars. A Coast Guard engineer from Chesapeake, Virginia, with five degrees and a career that spans sea floors and asteroid surfaces, is exactly the kind of person you want building the next chapter of human spaceflight.

His whole hope, Douglas has said, is that space reminds us that there is hope out there for humanity, even with all the things going on here on Earth. He came from the Coast Guard — the service whose motto is Semper Paratus, Always Ready. He has been getting ready his entire life. The sea taught him to keep things afloat. The stars are teaching him to fly.

Personal

Douglas is married to Rachel, formerly Rachel Stapleton of Owensboro, Kentucky. They have two sons. His mother lives in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, and his father lives in Tampa, Florida. He holds a Professional Engineer license in the Commonwealth of Virginia.

He was recognized as the Black Engineer of the Year Award recipient for Most Promising Engineer in Government in 2015. He was a White House Fellowship National Finalist in 2020. He received three Special Achievement Awards from the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory. He is a champion of youth mentoring and development through FIRST robotics competitions and Junior Achievement programs — the kind of work that puts a Coast Guard engineer in front of kids from Chesapeake and Miami and everywhere else, showing them that the path to space does not require a fighter jet.

He enjoys soccer, basketball, kayaking, hiking, running, ping-pong, billiards, chess, and movies. The range is characteristic. This is a person who does not narrow.
