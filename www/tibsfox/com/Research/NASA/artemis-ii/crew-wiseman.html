Baltimore

Reid James Wiseman was born on Veterans Day — November 11, 1975 — in Baltimore, Maryland. He grew up in the suburbs north of the city and graduated from Dulaney High School in Timonium. Baltimore is a Navy town. The shipyards, the harbor, the Academy just down the road in Annapolis. The military is in the water there.

He went to Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute in Troy, New York — RPI, one of the oldest engineering schools in the country — on a Navy ROTC scholarship. He graduated in 1997 with a Bachelor of Science in Computer and Systems Engineering. The Navy had paid for his education. Now it was time to fly.

The Fighter Pilot

Wiseman earned his wings and was assigned to fly the F-14 Tomcat — the twin-engine, variable-sweep-wing interceptor that had been the Navy's premier air superiority fighter since the 1970s. The Tomcat was a two-seat aircraft, built for fleet defense, designed to engage multiple targets at extreme range. It was the jet from Top Gun, but the real thing — carrier launches, night traps, combat air patrol over open ocean.

When the Navy retired the F-14, Wiseman transitioned to the F/A-18 Super Hornet — the multi-role strike fighter that replaced it. He deployed to the Middle East and flew combat missions during Operation Iraqi Freedom. He accumulated thousands of hours in high-performance tactical aircraft, operating from aircraft carriers in contested airspace.

He was assigned to Strike Fighter Squadron 103 — VFA-103, the "Jolly Rogers" — at NAS Oceana, Virginia, flying the F/A-18F. The Jolly Rogers have been a Navy squadron since 1943. Their skull-and-crossbones insignia is one of the most recognized in naval aviation. Wiseman flew under it.

The Test Pilot

Wiseman was selected for the U.S. Naval Test Pilot School at Naval Air Station Patuxent River, Maryland — "Pax River" — Class of 2003. He graduated in 2004. Test pilot school is where the military sends its best pilots to learn to fly aircraft that haven't been proven yet. The curriculum is graduate-level aerodynamics, flight mechanics, and systems engineering combined with daily test flights in aircraft pushed to the edges of their envelopes.

After graduating, he was assigned to Air Test and Evaluation Squadron 23 — VX-23, the "Salty Dogs" — at Patuxent River. This is where the Navy tests its new fighters. Wiseman worked on some of the most significant test programs in naval aviation:

  • F-35C Lightning II — the Navy's variant of the Joint Strike Fighter, testing carrier suitability for a fifth-generation stealth aircraft
  • F/A-18 weapons separation — testing the safe release of ordnance at various speeds, altitudes, and flight configurations
  • Ship Suitability testing — determining whether aircraft can safely operate from carrier decks in all conditions
  • T-45 Goshawk — the Navy's jet trainer, evaluating training aircraft performance

Test piloting is the pipeline to space. It has been since the Mercury program. The same skills — systematic evaluation of complex systems under extreme conditions, precise documentation of performance limits, the ability to remain analytical when the aircraft is doing something it has never done before — are exactly what NASA needs in an astronaut.

The Astronaut

In 2006, Wiseman earned a Master of Science in Systems Engineering from Johns Hopkins University. In 2009, NASA selected him as part of the 20th astronaut class — Group 20, who called themselves "The Chumps." He was one of nine candidates chosen from approximately 3,500 applicants. The selection rate was 0.26 percent.

ISS Expedition 40/41

Wiseman launched to the International Space Station on May 28, 2014, aboard Soyuz TMA-13M from the Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan. His crewmates were Russian cosmonaut Maksim Surayev and European Space Agency astronaut Alexander Gerst of Germany. He served as a flight engineer for Expeditions 40 and 41.

He spent more than 165 days in space. He performed two spacewalks — EVAs — totaling over 13 hours outside the station. He conducted hundreds of scientific experiments. He returned to Earth on November 10, 2014 — one day before his 39th birthday — landing in the Kazakh steppe in the Soyuz descent module.

During his time on the ISS, Wiseman was known for his photography and his social media presence. He shared images of Earth from orbit that captured the planet's beauty with genuine wonder. He understood that part of the job was bringing the experience back to the people who paid for it.

The Chief

After returning from the ISS, Wiseman was appointed Deputy Chief of the Astronaut Office under Patrick Forrester. In late 2020, he became the 17th Chief of the Astronaut Office — the person responsible for astronaut selection, training, crew assignments, and the overall management of the astronaut corps.

The Chief of the Astronaut Office is traditionally a non-flying position. The job is administrative, political, and managerial. It is one of the most important roles in human spaceflight because the Chief decides who flies and who doesn't, who trains for which mission, and how the corps operates. But a Chief doesn't fly. The Chief sends others.

Wiseman served as Chief from late 2020 through November 2022. Then he did something unusual: he stepped down from the position and returned to active astronaut status. Chiefs rarely do this. The role is typically a capstone, not a way station. But Wiseman wanted to fly again. He wanted the Moon.

The Weight He Carries

In 2020, while serving as Chief of the Astronaut Office, Reid Wiseman's wife Carroll died from cancer. Carroll Taylor Wiseman was a nurse. They had two daughters together. She died while he was holding the most demanding administrative position in the astronaut corps, responsible for the careers and safety of every American astronaut.

He continued to serve. He continued to lead. He did not step away.

There is a specific kind of strength required to lead others through danger while carrying private grief. It is not the strength of ignoring the loss. It is the strength of carrying it — every day, into every meeting, every decision, every moment when the lives of others depend on your judgment — and not letting it break the work.

His father attended the April 1, 2026, launch while fighting cancer himself. Reid Wiseman stood on top of the most powerful rocket ever built and left the Earth, knowing his father was watching from the grandstands, knowing what his father was carrying. Three generations of weight. The commander carries them all.

Some people carry grief like cargo. Some carry it like fuel. — on the kind of person who returns to active flight status after everything

Artemis II Commander

On April 3, 2023, NASA announced that Reid Wiseman would command Artemis II — the first crewed mission to the Moon since Apollo 17 in December 1972. He was selected alongside Pilot Victor Glover, Mission Specialist Christina Koch, and Mission Specialist Jeremy Hansen of the Canadian Space Agency.

At 50 years old on mission day, Wiseman is the oldest person to travel beyond low Earth orbit on this mission. He commands the Orion spacecraft Integrity — a name that carries weight not only as a spacecraft designation but as a statement about what the mission represents and who leads it.

The commander's responsibilities are absolute. Crew safety. Mission success. Every critical decision during a 10-day flight around the Moon and back. The commander is the person who calls the abort. The commander is the person who doesn't call the abort. The commander is the last voice before every irreversible maneuver. Trans-lunar injection. Free-return trajectory. Re-entry. The commander says go, or the commander says no.

Connection to Artemis II

The spacecraft is named Integrity. The name was chosen by the crew. It is not an accident that a man who lost his wife, who stepped down from the most powerful non-flying position in the astronaut corps to fly again, who carried his grief into the training and his father's illness into the launch — commands a ship called Integrity.

Reid Wiseman is 50 years old. The same age as the researcher tracking this mission from Mukilteo, Washington, watching the launch on a Tuesday night in the Pacific Northwest rain. There is something about being 50 and watching someone your exact age ride a column of fire toward the Moon. It collapses the distance between observer and observed. The space between the Moon and the Earth becomes personal.

He carries the weight. The loss. The responsibility. The four lives in the capsule. The name on the hull. He carries all of it past the Kármán line and out toward the Moon, farther from Earth than any human has traveled in half a century. The commander doesn't get to set the weight down. The commander carries them all.

You don't choose the weight. You choose to carry it. — on what it means to command Integrity
