01 Vehicle Stack Overview

The Artemis II launch stack stands 98 meters (322 feet) tall and masses 2,610,000 kg at liftoff — the most powerful rocket to carry humans since Saturn V. The stack comprises four major elements: twin solid rocket boosters, the SLS core stage, the Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage, and the Orion spacecraft with its European Service Module.

02 Space Launch System — Core Stage

The SLS core stage is the largest rocket stage ever built for human spaceflight. At 64.6 meters tall and 8.4 meters in diameter, it carries over 2.03 million liters of liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen propellant. Four RS-25D engines at its base produce a combined 7.44 MN of vacuum thrust, burning for approximately 480 seconds.

RS-25D Engines — Artemis II Serial Numbers

The RS-25, originally the Space Shuttle Main Engine (SSME), is the most tested large rocket engine in history. Each engine has flown multiple Shuttle missions before being refurbished for SLS. The RS-25D variant incorporates a new engine controller and updated high-pressure turbopumps.

03 Solid Rocket Boosters

Two five-segment solid rocket boosters (SRBs) provide the majority of thrust at liftoff. Each booster is 54 meters tall and produces 14.6 MN of sea-level thrust — together generating 29.2 MN, roughly 75% of total liftoff thrust. They are the largest human-rated solid rockets ever flown.

Shuttle comparison: The Shuttle SRBs used 4 segments. The SLS 5-segment design adds ~25% more propellant and thrust. Unlike Shuttle SRBs, the SLS boosters are not recovered — they impact the Atlantic and sink.

04 Interim Cryogenic Propulsion Stage

The ICPS is a modified Delta Cryogenic Second Stage (DCSS) derived from the Delta IV rocket. It provides the critical orbit insertion burn after core stage separation, then executes the perigee-raise maneuver to place Orion into a high Earth orbit (HEO) with an apogee of approximately 70,000 km. On Artemis I it also performed the trans-lunar injection (TLI) burn, but on Artemis II the ESM handles TLI from HEO.

05 Orion Crew Module — CM-003 “Integrity”

The Orion crew module is the capsule that will carry four astronauts around the Moon and back. Designated CM-003, it was named “Integrity” by the crew. It is the most advanced human-rated spacecraft ever built, with 355,056 individual parts spanning 77,150 distinct part types.

Apollo comparison: The Apollo Command Module had a habitable volume of 5.9 m³ for three crew. Orion provides 8.9 m³ — 50% more volume — while carrying four astronauts. Its 5.02 m diameter vs. Apollo’s 3.91 m gives substantially more shoulder room.

Crew Displays and Avionics

Orion features a glass cockpit derived from the Boeing 787 Dreamliner display system. Three high-resolution multifunction displays replace the hundreds of analog switches and gauges used on Apollo and Shuttle. The flight software runs on redundant flight computers with radiation-hardened processors. Crew can also monitor and command vehicle systems from handheld tablets.

06 European Service Module — ESM-2

The European Service Module is built by Airbus Defence and Space in Bremen, Germany, under contract to the European Space Agency (ESA). It provides propulsion, power, thermal control, water, and air to the Orion crew module. ESM-2 is the second unit built; ESM-1 flew on Artemis I.

Heritage note: The AJ10-190 engine was the Orbital Maneuvering System (OMS) engine on the Space Shuttle, used for orbit insertion and deorbit burns. It is a pressure-fed, hypergolic engine with a long history of reliable restarts in space. The AJ10 family traces back to the Vanguard rocket of 1957.

Role in Artemis II Trajectory

After the ICPS places Orion into a high Earth orbit, it is the ESM’s AJ10-190 that performs the trans-lunar injection burn from HEO — sending the crew toward the Moon. The ESM then handles all subsequent propulsive maneuvers: mid-course corrections, trajectory shaping, and the return-coast attitude adjustments. With a total delta-v budget of 1,050 m/s, the ESM must execute every burn with precision; there are no backup propulsion options for TLI.

07 Heat Shield — AVCOAT

Orion’s heat shield is the largest ablative heat shield ever built for human spaceflight, measuring 5.02 meters in diameter. It must protect the crew during atmospheric reentry at approximately 40,000 km/h (Mach 32) — the fastest any crewed vehicle has traveled since Apollo 17 in 1972.

Artemis I Heat Shield Investigation

During Artemis I (November 2022), the heat shield performed its primary function — the crew module returned safely — but post-flight inspection revealed unexpected charring patterns. The AVCOAT material experienced greater char loss than models predicted, with evidence of gas-trapped cracking within the ablator cells.

Artemis I finding: Unexpected char loss and gas-trapped cracking in the AVCOAT ablator. The issue was attributed to the skip-entry trajectory profile, which created thermal cycling not fully captured by ground test models.

Resolution for Artemis II

NASA convened an independent review board that concluded in December 2024. The resolution: Artemis II will fly a steeper, direct reentry trajectory instead of the skip-entry profile used on Artemis I. This eliminates the thermal cycling that caused the unexpected char behavior. Critically, the heat shield itself was not replaced — the existing AVCOAT shield on CM-003 was determined to have adequate safety margins for the revised trajectory.

08 Launch Escape System

The Launch Abort System (LAS) is the crew’s lifeline during the most dangerous phase of flight: ascent through the atmosphere. Mounted atop the Orion crew module on a tower structure, the LAS can pull the capsule clear of a failing rocket at any point from the launch pad through SRB separation.

Three Motors, One Mission

The abort motor is the primary escape engine. At 1,800 kN, it produces more thrust than the RS-25 engines — for just 3 seconds. That is enough to accelerate the crew module to a safe distance in under 2 seconds. The attitude control motor (ACM) provides steering during the abort to orient the capsule away from the launch vehicle. The jettison motor fires either after a successful abort (to discard the tower before parachute deployment) or during a nominal ascent to shed the LAS tower mass after it is no longer needed (post-SRB separation).

Apollo comparison: The Apollo Launch Escape System used a single solid motor producing 689 kN. Orion’s abort motor at 1,800 kN is 2.6 times more powerful, reflecting the heavier crew module (10,400 kg vs. Apollo’s 5,806 kg).

09 Recovery Systems

After surviving reentry, the Orion crew module decelerates through a parachute sequence before splashing down in the Pacific Ocean near San Diego, California. The recovery operation is conducted by the U.S. Navy in partnership with NASA’s Exploration Ground Systems.
