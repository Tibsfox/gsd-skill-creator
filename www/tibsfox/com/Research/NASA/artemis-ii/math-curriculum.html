Section 8

Gravitational Sphere of Influence — Where the Moon Takes Over

Space is not divided into neat territories, but for practical navigation it might as well be. At every point between Earth and the Moon, both bodies pull on Orion. Close to Earth, Earth's gravity dominates so completely that the Moon's pull is a rounding error. Close to the Moon, the reverse is true. Somewhere in between, the spacecraft crosses an invisible boundary where mission control must switch the reference frame from Earth-centered to Moon-centered calculations. That boundary is the sphere of influence, first defined by Pierre-Simon Laplace in the 18th century.

The sphere of influence is not the point where the two gravitational forces are equal (that would be the L1 Lagrange point, about 326,000 km from Earth). Instead, it is the distance from the smaller body where the perturbation from the larger body becomes small compared to the smaller body's gravity. This is a more useful boundary for navigation because inside the sphere, you can treat the Moon as the only gravitating body and get accurate results.

The Hill sphere is a closely related concept: it defines the region where the smaller body can hold objects in orbit against tidal forces from the larger body. For the Moon, the Hill sphere is about 61,500 km — slightly smaller than the Laplace sphere of influence. Any moon of the Moon (if one existed) would need to orbit within this radius to remain bound.

Sphere of Influence (Laplace formulation) r_SOI = a * (m / M)^(2/5) where: a = semi-major axis of the smaller body's orbit around the larger m = mass of the smaller body M = mass of the larger body Earth-Moon system a = 384,400 km /* mean Earth-Moon distance */ M_Moon = 7.342 x 10^22 kg M_Earth = 5.972 x 10^24 kg m/M = 7.342e22 / 5.972e24 = 0.01230 Step 1 — Compute the mass ratio exponent (m/M)^(2/5) = (0.01230)^0.4 ln(0.01230) = -4.398 0.4 * (-4.398) = -1.759 e^(-1.759) = 0.1724 Step 2 — Multiply by orbital distance r_SOI = 384,400 * 0.1724 r_SOI = 66,262 km from the Moon's center Hill sphere (for comparison) r_Hill = a * (m / 3M)^(1/3) r_Hill = 384,400 * (0.01230 / 3)^(1/3) r_Hill = 384,400 * (0.004100)^(1/3) r_Hill = 384,400 * 0.1601 r_Hill = 61,535 km from the Moon's center Earth's sphere of influence (in the Sun-Earth system) a_Earth = 149,598,000 km /* 1 AU */ M_Earth / M_Sun = 5.972e24 / 1.989e30 = 3.003 x 10^-6 r_SOI_Earth = 149,598,000 * (3.003e-6)^0.4 (3.003e-6)^0.4: ln(3.003e-6) = -12.716 0.4 * (-12.716) = -5.086 e^(-5.086) = 0.006163 r_SOI_Earth = 149,598,000 * 0.006163 r_SOI_Earth = 921,900 km (Earth's SOI in the Sun-Earth system) Artemis II transition point Orion enters the Moon's SOI at 66,262 km from the Moon. Why this matters

When Orion crosses the sphere of influence boundary, the navigation team at Johnson Space Center switches their entire computational framework. Inside the Moon's SOI, they model Orion's trajectory as a hyperbolic orbit around the Moon with Earth as a perturbation, rather than an elliptical orbit around Earth with the Moon as a perturbation. Getting this transition wrong — even by a few kilometers in position or a fraction of a meter per second in velocity — compounds over the hours of the lunar flyby and can put the return trajectory off by thousands of kilometers at Earth.

The patched-conic approximation (Section 9) depends entirely on knowing this boundary. It is the seam where two Keplerian solutions are stitched together. The 2/5 power in the SOI formula comes from balancing the gravitational acceleration from the Moon against the tidal acceleration from Earth — a result Laplace derived in his Mécanique Céleste over two centuries ago. The same formula applies to every planet and moon in the solar system.

Section 9

Free-Return Trajectory Geometry — The Figure-Eight Path

Section 7 of Math (1–7) introduced the free-return trajectory as a safety concept. Here we develop the mathematics of how it works: the patch-conic geometry that shapes Orion's figure-eight path from Earth to the Moon and back, and the energy bookkeeping that guarantees the return.

What makes a trajectory "free return"? A free-return trajectory is one where the spacecraft, after receiving its initial trans-lunar injection (TLI) burn, will return to Earth and reenter the atmosphere without any additional propulsive maneuvers. The Moon's gravity provides the course change. The trajectory resembles a figure-eight when drawn in the rotating Earth-Moon frame, though in the inertial frame it looks like a distorted ellipse.

The patch-conic method. Since the exact three-body problem has no closed-form solution, trajectory designers break the path into segments, each governed by a single gravitating body. Outside the Moon's sphere of influence (66,262 km from the Moon, per Section 8), the trajectory is a Keplerian conic around Earth. Inside the SOI, it is a Keplerian conic around the Moon. At the SOI boundary, the two conics are "patched" together by matching position and velocity in a common reference frame.

The ~50 degree deflection from the lunar flyby redirects the trajectory so perigee falls within Earth's atmosphere. Exit speed from Moon's SOI = Entry speed (energy conservation) v_inf_out = 0.8 km/s (same as entry — gravity is conservative) The "free" in free-return: no propellant was consumed. Moon's gravity bent the path but did not change the spacecraft's speed relative to the Moon.

The vis-viva equation (Section 14) is the energy conservation law that guarantees this works. At every point along each conic segment, the total orbital energy E = v2/2 − μ/r is constant. When Orion transitions between the Earth-centric and Moon-centric frames at the SOI boundary, the energy is conserved in each frame independently. The patch-conic method works because the SOI is chosen precisely where this frame-switching introduces minimal error.

Why this matters

The geometry of the free-return trajectory is a solved problem — Apollo proved it operationally in 1968 (Apollo 8) and proved it as an emergency procedure in 1970 (Apollo 13). But the precision requirements for Artemis II are tighter. The skip reentry corridor at Earth is only about 40 km wide (between burning up and skipping off the atmosphere). The patch-conic approximation gets the trajectory to within a few hundred kilometers of the correct answer; the final precision comes from numerical integration of the full three-body equations and mid-course correction burns of a few meters per second.

TSPB connection: The patch-conic method is a foundational example of domain decomposition — breaking a hard problem into tractable pieces, solving each independently, and matching them at boundaries. This pattern appears throughout computational mathematics (Layer 2) and is an OPEN problem when the number of bodies exceeds three.

This is tighter than the 8,900 km altitude (10,637 km radius) used in our pre-flight derivation above. The tighter flyby increases the gravitational deflection angle and adjusts the return trajectory geometry.

The tighter flyby altitude of 4,067 miles (compared to the pre-flight prediction range of 3,000–9,000 miles) yields an 8-degree steeper deflection angle. This is consistent with the free-return design: the Moon bends Orion's path more aggressively at closer range, redirecting the trajectory back toward Earth with greater precision. The velocity at closest approach is correspondingly higher (1.35 km/s vs 1.25 km/s) because the spacecraft falls deeper into the Moon's gravity well before swinging out. Energy is still conserved: the exit speed from the Moon's sphere of influence equals the entry speed, regardless of flyby distance.

Section 10

Radiation Dose Calculation — What Ten Days Beyond Earth Costs

Inside Earth's magnetosphere, the ISS crew receives about 0.5–1.0 millisieverts (mSv) per day. On the ground, you receive about 0.008 mSv per day from natural background radiation. Beyond low Earth orbit, the crew loses the magnetic shield that protects every human on Earth and every astronaut on the ISS. Galactic cosmic rays (GCRs) and solar energetic particles (SEPs) hit the spacecraft unattenuated. The mathematics of radiation exposure is a problem of particle physics, shielding geometry, and biological risk.

Absorbed dose vs. dose equivalent. Not all radiation is equally dangerous. A proton deposits energy differently than an iron nucleus (an HZE ion), even if both deliver the same number of joules per kilogram. The absorbed dose, measured in gray (Gy), counts raw energy deposition: 1 Gy = 1 J/kg. The dose equivalent, measured in sieverts (Sv), weights the absorbed dose by a quality factor Q that reflects biological effectiveness. For gamma rays and electrons, Q = 1. For protons, Q = 2. For heavy ions, Q can reach 20 or higher.

Why this matters

The daily dose rate beyond LEO is roughly 3× higher than on the ISS — not because there is more radiation, but because Earth's magnetosphere is no longer deflecting the charged particles. For a 10-day mission, the total dose is manageable: about 5% of the career limit, equivalent to a few CT scans. But for a Mars mission lasting 2.5 years, the same daily rate would accumulate to over 2,700 mSv — more than four times the career limit. This is why radiation shielding and transit time are the two most critical engineering challenges for human Mars exploration.

The secondary particle problem — where adding more aluminum shielding can actually increase the dose — is one reason NASA is investigating hydrogen-rich materials (polyethylene, water) and active magnetic shielding. It is also an OPEN problem in computational physics: accurately modeling the full particle cascade through complex spacecraft geometry requires Monte Carlo particle transport codes (HZETRN, GEANT4) that push the limits of current computing.

Section 11

Heat Shield Thermal Flux — Chapman's Equation and Ablation

Section 5 calculated the total kinetic energy Orion must shed during reentry: 574 billion joules. But the heat shield does not receive all of that energy at once. The thermal flux — heat per unit area per unit time — varies enormously along the reentry corridor. Peak heating occurs when the spacecraft is moving fastest through the densest part of the atmosphere it encounters. Dean Chapman's semi-empirical convective heating formula, developed at NASA Ames in the 1950s, gives the stagnation point heat flux as a function of velocity and atmospheric density.

Chapman's convective heating formula q_dot = k * (rho / r_n)^0.5 * V^3.15 where: q_dot = convective heat flux (W/cm^2) k = constant (depends on gas composition) ~ 1.83 x 10^-8 for air rho = freestream density (kg/m^3) r_n = nose radius (m) V = velocity (m/s) Key insight: velocity dominates Heat flux scales as V^3.15 — not V^2 like kinetic energy. Doubling velocity increases heat flux by 2^3.15 = 8.9x. This is why lunar return (11.1 km/s) is far more severe than LEO return (7.8 km/s): (11,100 / 7,800)^3.15 = (1.423)^3.15 = 3.02x Lunar return peak heat flux is ~3x higher than LEO return. Stagnation point calculation at peak heating Orion heat shield nose radius: r_n = 5.03 m / 2 = 2.515 m /* 5.03 m diameter shield, approximated as sphere */ At peak heating altitude (~65 km for skip entry first pass): rho ~ 1.2 x 10^-4 kg/m^3 /* US Standard Atmosphere */ V ~ 10,500 m/s /* slightly decelerated from entry */ q_dot = 1.83e-8 * (1.2e-4 / 2.515)^0.5 * (10,500)^3.15 (1.2e-4 / 2.515)^0.5 = (4.77e-5)^0.5 = 6.907e-3 (10,500)^3.15: log10(10,500) = 4.0212 3.15 * 4.0212 = 12.667 10^12.667 = 4.641 x 10^12 q_dot = 1.83e-8 * 6.907e-3 * 4.641e12 q_dot = 1.83e-8 * 3.204e10 q_dot = 586 W/cm^2 (peak stagnation point heat flux) /* NASA published range for Artemis: ~300-600 W/cm^2 */ /* Our estimate lands in the upper range (stagnation point is worst case) */ Stagnation point temperature (radiative equilibrium) At thermal equilibrium, the surface radiates as much as it absorbs: q_dot = epsilon * sigma * T^4 sigma = 5.67 x 10^-8 W/(m^2 K^4) /* Stefan-Boltzmann */ epsilon ~ 0.9 /* AVCOAT emissivity */ Solving for T: T = (q_dot / (epsilon * sigma))^0.25 q_dot = 586 W/cm^2 = 5,860,000 W/m^2 T = (5,860,000 / (0.9 * 5.67e-8))^0.25 T = (5,860,000 / 5.103e-8)^0.25 T = (1.148 x 10^14)^0.25 T = 3,273 K = 3,000 C = 5,432 F /* NASA states ~2,760 C (5,000 F) surface temperature */ /* Our result is ~9% high because AVCOAT ablation carries */ /* heat away before radiative equilibrium is reached */ Total heat load — integrated over entry corridor Q = integral of q_dot * dt over the entry Direct entry (single pass, ~400 seconds of peak heating): Q_direct ~ 300 W/cm^2 * 400 s = 120,000 J/cm^2 Skip entry (two passes, lower peak but longer total): Pass 1: ~250 W/cm^2 average * 200 s = 50,000 J/cm^2 Skip phase: negligible heating (above atmosphere) Pass 2: ~150 W/cm^2 average * 300 s = 45,000 J/cm^2 Q_skip ~ 95,000 J/cm^2 Skip entry total heat load: ~80% of direct entry But peak flux is also lower — this protects AVCOAT from charring too deep.

AVCOAT ablation. AVCOAT is an ablative thermal protection material: it is designed to be destroyed. As the surface heats past ~300 °C, the epoxy resin in the AVCOAT matrix begins to pyrolyze (decompose). The gaseous decomposition products blow outward through the char layer, creating a cooler boundary layer between the surface and the superheated plasma. This process is called transpiration cooling. Meanwhile, the char layer itself radiates heat outward. The combination of ablation, transpiration, and radiation keeps the bondline (where AVCOAT meets the spacecraft structure) below 250 °C, even while the outer surface exceeds 2,700 °C.

Why this matters

The skip entry profile is not just about crew comfort (lower peak g-forces). It fundamentally changes the thermal loading on the heat shield. By splitting the entry into two passes, the peak heat flux drops by roughly 20%, and the shield has a brief period in the skip phase to radiate accumulated heat into space before the second pass. This reduces the depth of charring and preserves the structural integrity of the underlying Titanium skeleton.

Chapman's formula is a 1950s approximation — modern computational fluid dynamics (CFD) codes solve the full Navier-Stokes equations with real-gas chemistry to get accurate heat flux predictions. But Chapman's equation remains valuable as a first-order check: if your CFD code disagrees with Chapman by more than a factor of two, something is wrong with your simulation. The V3.15 scaling is one of those results in engineering that is simple, powerful, and approximately right.

Section 12

Circadian Rhythm as Oscillator — When Biology Drifts from the Clock

Every human carries an internal clock driven by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a cluster of about 20,000 neurons in the hypothalamus. This clock runs on a cycle of approximately 24.2 hours — not exactly 24. On Earth, sunlight resets (entrains) the clock every morning, keeping it synchronized to the 24-hour day. In space, there is no sunrise. The crew operates on a mission clock set to exactly 24.0 hours. The mismatch between the biological oscillator and the mission clock is a beat frequency problem.

This is the same mathematics that describes two tuning forks at slightly different frequencies, or two pendulums with slightly different periods. When two oscillators are close in frequency but not identical, they produce beats — a slow oscillation at the difference frequency. For the crew, the "beat" manifests as progressive sleep-wake desynchronization.

Why this matters

For Artemis II, the 2-hour drift over 10 days is equivalent to mild jet lag — annoying but not dangerous. The crew can manage it with timed blue-light exposure from Orion's LED panels. But this gentle drift is a preview of a much harder problem. On a Mars transit, the crew would experience complete phase inversions every 60 days, equivalent to flying from New York to Tokyo and back every two months — without ever being able to step outside.

OPEN problem — Kuramoto synchronization. The human circadian system is not a single oscillator. It is a network of coupled oscillators (the 20,000 neurons of the SCN, plus peripheral clocks in every organ). The Kuramoto model describes how coupled oscillators synchronize: each oscillator has its own natural frequency, and coupling between them pulls them toward phase coherence. In microgravity, without gravitational cues, the coupling strength between peripheral clocks may weaken. Whether the circadian network can maintain internal coherence on a Mars-length mission is an unsolved question at the intersection of nonlinear dynamics and neuroscience.

Shannon Capacity of the O2O Link — Bits from Photons

Section 4 showed that the O2O optical terminal achieves 260 Mbps by focusing its laser beam into a 7 km footprint at lunar distance. But beam divergence alone does not set the data rate. The ultimate limit comes from Claude Shannon's 1948 theorem: the maximum rate at which information can be transmitted over a noisy channel. Here we calculate the Shannon capacity for both the optical and radio links and show why the optical link achieves 130× higher throughput.

What happens during a geomagnetic storm? Solar energetic particles do not directly affect the laser beam, but a severe geomagnetic storm causes upper atmospheric scintillation — rapid fluctuations in atmospheric refractive index. This is the optical equivalent of "seeing" that blurs ground-based telescopes. For the O2O downlink, scintillation can cause signal fades lasting milliseconds to seconds. The link uses interleaved forward error correction (FEC) and automatic repeat request (ARQ) to ride through fades. During extreme events (Kp ≥ 8), the optical link may temporarily fall back to reduced rates or switch to S-band radio as backup.

Why this matters

The optical link's enormous antenna gain (232 dBi total, vs. 89 dBi for radio) more than compensates for the higher path loss at shorter wavelengths. The result is a received signal that is orders of magnitude stronger than the noise floor. Shannon's theorem tells us this translates to a channel capacity of 19 Gbps — the O2O system uses only 1.4% of this capacity. The conservative operating point provides massive margin against atmospheric degradation, pointing errors, and component aging.

TSPB connection: Shannon's theorem sits at the intersection of information theory (Layer 2) and physical implementation (Layer 1). The logarithmic relationship C = B log2(1 + SNR) is one of the most powerful results in all of engineering — it sets an absolute ceiling on communication that no amount of clever coding can exceed. The fact that both links operate at only 1–3% of their Shannon limits shows how much room remains for future improvement.

Orbital Mechanics — The Vis-Viva Equation

If you learn one equation in orbital mechanics, it should be vis-viva. The name means "living force" in Latin — a term from the early history of physics for what we now call kinetic energy. The vis-viva equation relates velocity, position, and orbital shape in a single line. It is the energy conservation law specialized for Keplerian orbits, and every calculation in Sections 1, 3, 7, 8, and 9 depends on it.

The vis-viva equation v^2 = GM * (2/r - 1/a) where: v = orbital velocity at distance r from the central body GM = mu = gravitational parameter of the central body r = distance from center of mass a = semi-major axis of the orbit Derivation from energy conservation Total orbital energy is the sum of kinetic and potential: E = (1/2) * m * v^2 - G * M * m / r For any Keplerian orbit, E is constant and equals: E = -G * M * m / (2 * a) Setting them equal: (1/2) * v^2 - mu / r = -mu / (2 * a) v^2 / 2 = mu / r - mu / (2 * a) v^2 = mu * (2/r - 1/a) /* vis-viva */ Orion's velocity at key mission points mu_Earth = 398,600 km^3/s^2 1. Why this matters

The vis-viva equation reveals one of the most counterintuitive facts about orbital mechanics: at perigee of the HEO, Orion needs only 270 m/s more velocity (a 2.6% increase) to convert a 23-hour Earth orbit into a trajectory that reaches the Moon. This is the Oberth effect — burns performed at high velocity (deep in a gravity well) produce larger orbital energy changes per unit of delta-v. It is why TLI is always performed at perigee.

TSPB connection: The vis-viva equation is derivable from Newton's laws and conservation of energy alone — no approximations, no empirical constants. It is exact for any two-body orbit. This makes it a TSPB Layer 1 result: a foundational truth that subsequent layers build upon. Every trajectory calculation in this curriculum ultimately reduces to v2 = μ(2/r − 1/a).

Section 15

Navigation — Star Tracker Mathematics

To navigate in deep space, you must know which way the spacecraft is pointing. There is no magnetic compass (Earth's field is undetectable at lunar distance), no GPS (the satellites only broadcast toward Earth), and no horizon reference in the void. Orion determines its attitude — its orientation in three-dimensional space — by photographing star fields and matching them against a catalog. The star tracker camera takes an image, identifies stars by their brightness and angular separation, then computes the rotation matrix that maps the known star positions to the measured positions. This rotation is the spacecraft's attitude.

The TRIAD algorithm. The simplest attitude determination method uses just two non-parallel reference vectors. If you can measure the direction to two known objects (two stars, or one star and the Sun) in both the body frame (what the camera sees) and the inertial frame (where the catalog says they are), you can construct the rotation matrix that transforms one frame to the other. Harold Black published the TRIAD algorithm in 1964, and variants of it remain in use on every spacecraft with a star tracker.

Setup: two reference vectors Let v_1, v_2 = unit vectors to two stars in the inertial (catalog) frame Let w_1, w_2 = unit vectors to the same stars in the body (camera) frame The rotation matrix R satisfies: w_i = R * v_i for each star i TRIAD algorithm — construct orthonormal triads In the inertial frame, build an orthonormal basis: r_1 = v_1 /* first basis vector */ r_2 = (v_1 x v_2) / |v_1 x v_2| /* perpendicular to both */ r_3 = r_1 x r_2 /* completes the triad */ In the body frame, build the same structure: s_1 = w_1 s_2 = (w_1 x w_2) / |w_1 x w_2| s_3 = s_1 x s_2 Rotation matrix M_inertial = [r_1 | r_2 | r_3] /* columns are the triad vectors */ M_body = [s_1 | s_2 | s_3] The rotation matrix R that transforms inertial to body: R = M_body * M_inertial^T where M^T is the transpose (for orthonormal matrices, transpose = inverse). Numerical example Star 1 (Canopus): catalog direction v_1 = [0.487, -0.506, -0.712] Star 2 (Vega): catalog direction v_2 = [0.287, 0.862, 0.418] Measured by camera: w_1 = [0.612, 0.354, -0.707] w_2 = [0.791, -0.577, 0.204] r_1 = [0.487, -0.506, -0.712] r_2 = v_1 x v_2 / |v_1 x v_2|: v_1 x v_2 = [(-0.506)(0.418) - (-0.712)(0.862), (-0.712)(0.287) - (0.487)(0.418), (0.487)(0.862) - (-0.506)(0.287)] = [-0.212 + 0.614, -0.204 - 0.204, 0.420 + 0.145] = [0.402, -0.408, 0.565] |r_2| = sqrt(0.162 + 0.166 + 0.319) = sqrt(0.647) = 0.804 r_2 = [0.500, -0.507, 0.703] r_3 = r_1 x r_2 = [(-0.506)(0.703) - (-0.712)(-0.507), (-0.712)(0.500) - (0.487)(0.703), (0.487)(-0.507) - (-0.506)(0.500)] = [-0.356 - 0.361, -0.356 - 0.342, -0.247 + 0.253] = [-0.717, -0.698, 0.006] /* Proceed similarly for s_1, s_2, s_3 from w vectors */ R = M_body * M_inertial^T (3x3 rotation matrix: spacecraft attitude) Attitude accuracy Orion's star trackers: ~0.003 degree (10.8 arcsecond) per axis Required for O2O laser pointing: ~0.005 degree Star tracker precision exceeds O2O pointing requirement by 1.7x margin

Quaternion representation. While the rotation matrix R works perfectly, it has 9 numbers with 6 constraints (the rows must be unit vectors and mutually perpendicular). A more efficient representation uses quaternions: 4 numbers with 1 constraint (unit norm). A quaternion q = [q0, q1, q2, q3] represents the same rotation as R but avoids a critical problem called gimbal lock.

Quaternion from rotation matrix Given R, the quaternion components are: q_0 = 0.5 * sqrt(1 + R_11 + R_22 + R_33) /* scalar part */ q_1 = (R_32 - R_23) / (4 * q_0) q_2 = (R_13 - R_31) / (4 * q_0) q_3 = (R_21 - R_12) / (4 * q_0) Constraint: q_0^2 + q_1^2 + q_2^2 + q_3^2 = 1 Why quaternions avoid gimbal lock Euler angles (roll, pitch, yaw) fail when two rotation axes align — a singularity called gimbal lock. At pitch = +/- 90 degrees, roll and yaw become indistinguishable, and the system loses a degree of freedom. Quaternions have no such singularity. They represent ALL orientations smoothly, including the ones where Euler angles break down. For Orion's star tracker, which must track stars at every possible spacecraft orientation (including tumble recovery), quaternions are essential. Connection to the unit circle In 2D, rotation by angle theta is multiplication by e^(i*theta) on the unit circle in the complex plane. In 3D, rotation by angle theta around axis [u_x, u_y, u_z] is multiplication by the unit quaternion: q = cos(theta/2) + sin(theta/2) * (u_x*i + u_y*j + u_z*k) Quaternions are to 3D rotation what complex numbers are to 2D rotation. The unit quaternions form a 3-sphere (S^3) — the unit circle's 3D generalization.
Why this matters

Every time Orion fires a thruster, points its antenna, or aims its star tracker, the flight computer performs a quaternion multiplication. The mathematical structure is identical to complex number multiplication on the unit circle, extended to four dimensions. William Rowan Hamilton discovered quaternions in 1843 while walking along the Royal Canal in Dublin — he was so excited he carved the fundamental equation i2 = j2 = k2 = ijk = −1 into the stone of Broom Bridge. Today, that equation keeps spacecraft pointing in the right direction.

TSPB connection — Layer 1 (Unit Circle). The TSPB begins with the unit circle because rotation is the most fundamental symmetry in mathematics. Euler's formula eiθ = cosθ + i sinθ handles 2D rotation. Quaternions extend this to 3D. The star tracker uses a catalog of star positions fixed in the inertial frame, measures those positions in the body frame, and computes the rotation (quaternion) connecting them. It is the unit circle made operational at lunar distance.
