Radiation Sources

Three Threats in the Deep-Space Environment

Beyond the protective cocoon of Earth's magnetosphere, Artemis II crew will encounter three distinct radiation populations. Each has different particle types, energies, and risk profiles. Understanding these sources individually is essential because they require different mitigation strategies and monitoring approaches.

Trapped Radiation

Van Allen Belt Transit

Earth's magnetic field traps energetic protons and electrons in two toroidal belts extending from roughly 1,000 km to 60,000 km altitude. The inner belt is dominated by high-energy protons (tens to hundreds of MeV), while the outer belt contains primarily relativistic electrons. Orion must pass through both belts on departure and return.

Orion's trajectory is optimized to minimize time in the densest regions of the belts. The spacecraft traverses the belts quickly — roughly 30 minutes through the highest-flux zones on each pass. Even so, the compressed exposure during belt transit delivers a dose equivalent to approximately one month aboard the International Space Station, which orbits well below the inner belt at ~420 km altitude.

Why it matters: The Van Allen belts represent the most predictable radiation hazard of the mission. The particle populations and flux rates are well-characterized from decades of satellite measurements. Flight planners can model the expected dose with high confidence, unlike the stochastic threats from solar events.
Background Radiation

Galactic Cosmic Rays (GCR)

Galactic cosmic rays originate from supernovae and other high-energy astrophysical processes outside the solar system. They consist of fully ionized atomic nuclei — about 87% protons, 12% helium nuclei (alpha particles), and roughly 1% heavier ions ranging from carbon to iron and beyond. These particles travel at relativistic speeds and carry enormous kinetic energy per nucleon.

The heavy-ion component — known as HZE particles (High Z and Energy) — is particularly concerning for biological tissue. A single iron-56 nucleus traveling at near light speed deposits energy along a narrow, dense track through cells. This creates clustered DNA damage that is more difficult for cellular repair mechanisms to fix than the distributed damage from lighter particles.

GCR flux is modulated by the solar cycle: during solar maximum, the stronger and more turbulent interplanetary magnetic field deflects more galactic particles, reducing the GCR dose by roughly 30–50% compared to solar minimum. For Artemis II, launching approximately 18 months past the Solar Cycle 25 peak, GCR levels are beginning to rise again but remain below solar minimum values.

Apollo phosphenes: Apollo astronauts reported seeing light flashes with their eyes closed — luminous streaks and spots caused by cosmic ray particles traversing the retina or visual cortex. These phosphene events, first systematically documented during Apollo 11 and studied in dedicated experiments on Apollo 14–17, remain one of the most vivid demonstrations of GCR interaction with human tissue.
Acute Hazard

Solar Energetic Particles (SEPs)

Solar energetic particle events are the most dangerous radiation threat to crew safety because they are unpredictable, can arrive with little warning, and deliver large doses in short periods. SEPs are accelerated by solar flares and coronal mass ejections (CMEs), producing bursts of protons, electrons, and heavier ions that propagate outward through the heliosphere.

The intensity of SEP events varies enormously. Most are modest, adding small increments to the background dose. But the largest events — like the August 1972 event that fell between Apollo 16 and Apollo 17 — can deliver potentially lethal doses to unshielded crew in open space. The probability of a major event during any given 10-day mission window is low, but non-zero, and this risk drives much of the radiation protection architecture aboard Orion.

Solar Cycle 25 context: The Sun reached its Solar Cycle 25 maximum approximately 18 months before the Artemis II launch window. While declining from peak activity, the Sun remains in an elevated state. Solar maximum brings more frequent flares and CMEs, but the relationship between cycle phase and extreme events is statistical, not deterministic — large SEP events can occur at any point in the cycle.
Dosimetry

Total Expected Mission Dose

The combined radiation dose for the Artemis II mission — from Van Allen belt transits, continuous GCR exposure, and any solar particle events — is expected to total in the tens of millisieverts. This is more than ten times the annual dose received at ground level (~3 mSv from natural background radiation in the United States) but well within NASA's career exposure limits for astronauts.

The Artemis II crew is expected to receive a dose similar to or slightly lower than Apollo 14's 11.4 mSv over 9 days. The shorter time spent in the lunar vicinity compared to Apollo orbital missions, combined with Orion's modern shielding design, helps offset the higher solar activity environment. A quiet-Sun mission with no SEP events would place the total dose comfortably in the low tens of millisieverts.

Onboard Instrumentation

Radiation Monitoring Systems on Orion “Integrity”

Orion carries a layered radiation monitoring architecture that combines area monitors, particle-tracking detectors, personal dosimeters, and an automated warning system. Together these systems provide real-time dose rate awareness, cumulative dose tracking, particle type identification, and crew alerting — the four capabilities needed to manage radiation risk during a deep space mission.

System Details Function
HERA
 Hybrid Electronic Radiation Assessor
6 sensors distributed throughout the cabin measuring real-time dose rates. Provides area-wide radiation field mapping and feeds directly into the onboard warning system.
Timepix Detectors
 CERN / Medipix2 / ADVACAM
6 hybrid pixel detector units with 256x256 pixel matrices. Each pixel independently detects individual charged particles, measures energy deposits, and distinguishes radiation types by analyzing track patterns (dots, curves, heavy tracks). Heritage from continuous ISS deployment since 2012.
Crew Dosimeters
 Personal Active Dosimetry
Worn by each astronaut to track individual cumulative dose. Provides per-crew-member exposure records for post-mission medical analysis and career dose accounting.
Warning System
 Visual + Audible Alerts
Two-threshold alarm system integrated with HERA data. Caution threshold triggers visual display alerts. Shelter-in-place threshold adds audible alarms, initiating crew protective action protocol.
Timepix heritage: The Medipix2 Collaboration at CERN originally developed these hybrid pixel detectors for high-energy physics experiments. ADVACAM manufactures the spaceflight units. Since 2012, Timepix detectors have operated continuously aboard the ISS, building an extensive dataset of the low-Earth-orbit radiation environment. Artemis II marks their first deployment in deep space beyond the Van Allen belts, where the particle population is fundamentally different.
Crew Safety

Crew Protection Protocol

Radiation protection for Artemis II operates on three layers: continuous monitoring, advance warning from external assets, and onboard crew response procedures. The system is designed so that the crew can transition from normal operations to maximum protection configuration within minutes of a warning.

At the higher shelter-in-place threshold, crew members reconfigure the cabin by repositioning stowed equipment — food, water containers, and other mass — around their crew stations to create additional material shielding. Water and hydrogen-rich materials are particularly effective at attenuating proton radiation because hydrogen nuclei are close in mass to incoming protons, maximizing energy transfer per collision.

On the ground, NASA's Moon to Mars Space Weather Analysis Office at Goddard Space Flight Center maintains continuous watch throughout the mission. Their monitoring network includes the Solar Dynamics Observatory (real-time solar imaging), NOAA weather satellites (particle flux measurements), and — remarkably — the Mars Perseverance rover's Mastcam-Z camera, which observes sunspots on the far side of the Sun roughly two weeks before solar rotation brings those active regions to face Earth and the Orion spacecraft.

Perseverance as a space weather sentinel: Because Mars and Earth are at different points in their orbits, Perseverance can sometimes see portions of the Sun's surface that are not yet visible from Earth. Active regions (sunspots) that might produce flares can be identified as they rotate across the solar disk toward the Earth-Moon system, providing approximately two weeks of advance situational awareness that no Earth-based observatory can match.

Biological Science

AVATAR — Radiation Biology Beyond the Belts

The AVATAR experiment (A Virtual Astronaut Tissue Analog Response) places organ-on-chip devices built by Emulate into the deep space radiation environment for the first time. These microfluidic chips contain living human cells arranged to mimic organ-level tissue function, providing a biological proxy for studying how radiation and microgravity interact at the cellular level.

What makes AVATAR unique in the radiation context is the use of the crew's own bone marrow cells. By studying how each astronaut's tissue responds to the actual deep space radiation environment — not a simulated one — researchers can correlate chip-level cellular changes with the crew member's in-vivo health data from the ARCHAR longitudinal study, creating a personalized radiation response profile.

Why beyond the belts matters: Previous organ-on-chip experiments on the ISS operated within Earth's magnetosphere, which deflects most galactic cosmic rays and solar energetic particles. AVATAR will be exposed to the full, unshielded deep space radiation spectrum — the same environment that future Artemis III surface crews and eventual Mars transit crews will face. The results will directly inform long-duration deep-space mission planning and crew health risk models.
Historical Reference

Apollo Radiation Experience

The Apollo program provided the only previous human experience with deep space radiation. Nine crewed missions traveled beyond low Earth orbit between 1968 and 1972. Their dosimetry records, crew observations, and post-mission medical data remain the primary human reference dataset for deep space radiation exposure — a dataset that Artemis II will update with modern instrumentation for the first time in over fifty years.

Apollo astronauts famously reported seeing light flashes (phosphenes) with their eyes closed — brief streaks, spots, and flashes caused by individual cosmic ray particles traversing the retina or stimulating the visual cortex directly. These observations were first noted informally during Apollo 11 and became the subject of dedicated experiments on later missions, with crew members donning blindfolds during quiet periods to systematically count and categorize the flashes.

The most sobering historical data point is the major solar particle event of August 1972, which occurred during the four-month gap between Apollo 16 (April 1972) and Apollo 17 (December 1972). Had a crew been in transit or on the lunar surface during that event, they could have received a dose of several hundred millisieverts to over a sievert, potentially causing acute radiation sickness. This near-miss underscores why the real-time monitoring and shelter-in-place protocols aboard Orion are not conservative engineering margins but operational necessities.

Fifty-year gap: Between Apollo 17's return on December 19, 1972, and the Artemis II mission, no human has traveled beyond low Earth orbit. The radiation environment data from Apollo was collected with 1960s-era instruments — film badges and thermoluminescent dosimeters. Artemis II's HERA sensors, Timepix pixel detectors, and active personal dosimeters represent a generational leap in measurement capability, providing particle-type discrimination and real-time dose rate data that Apollo-era instruments could not deliver.
