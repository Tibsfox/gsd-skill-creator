Section 1

The Three Types of Navigation

No single system navigates Orion to the Moon. Instead, three independent methods work together, each covering the other's weaknesses. If one fails, the remaining two can still bring the crew home. This is the principle of dissimilar redundancy — the same philosophy that kept Apollo 13 alive.

  • Ground-based (DSN): Three tracking complexes measure range and velocity via radio signals. The primary navigation source — sub-meter accuracy at lunar distance.
  • Onboard (Star Trackers + IMU): Star tracker cameras photograph star fields to determine attitude. Ring laser gyroscopes and accelerometers track rotation and acceleration. Together they provide autonomous navigation when DSN is unavailable.
  • Optical (O2O Laser): The Orion-to-Ground optical communications system uses laser pulses whose round-trip timing provides precise range measurements as a byproduct of data transfer — the first crewed deep space test of optical navigation.
Why Three Systems?

DSN provides the best absolute position, but requires a ground station in view. Star trackers work anywhere but only tell you which way you're pointing, not where you are. The IMU tracks every motion but drifts over time. Laser ranging is extraordinarily precise but needs clear line-of-sight to Earth. Together, these four measurements — range, velocity, attitude, and acceleration — fully determine the spacecraft's state.

Section 2

DSN Tracking — How It Works

The Deep Space Network is humanity's interplanetary telephone system. Three complexes, spaced 120 degrees apart in longitude, ensure that any spacecraft beyond Earth orbit always has at least one antenna in view as the planet rotates. The DSN doesn't just talk to Orion — it simultaneously tracks every active deep space mission: Voyager 1 and 2, Mars rovers, JWST, New Horizons, and dozens more.

Range measurement is conceptually simple: send a coded radio signal to the spacecraft, wait for it to echo back, measure the round-trip time, divide by two, and multiply by the speed of light. Velocity comes from the Doppler shift of the carrier frequency — the same effect that makes a siren change pitch as an ambulance passes. A spacecraft moving away from Earth compresses the return signal to a slightly lower frequency; moving toward Earth stretches it higher.

Section 3

Star Trackers

A star tracker is a camera that looks at the stars and figures out which way the spacecraft is pointing. Orion carries redundant star trackers — multiple units so that if one fails, the others continue working. Each tracker photographs a patch of sky, identifies the star patterns using an onboard catalog of approximately 5,000 stars, and computes the spacecraft's attitude (orientation in three-dimensional space) to within ±10 arcseconds.

Ten arcseconds may not sound impressive until you convert it: that's 1/360th of a degree, or roughly the angular width of a dime viewed from two kilometers away. At lunar distance, a 10-arcsecond pointing error translates to a position uncertainty of about 18.6 kilometers — which is why star trackers are always paired with DSN tracking for precise position fixes.

Worked Example — Pointing Error at Lunar Distance Given: Star tracker accuracy = 10 arcseconds Lunar distance d = 384,400 km Step 1 — Convert arcseconds to radians 10 arcsec * (1° / 3600 arcsec) * (π / 180°) = 10 / (3600 * 57.2958) = 4.848 × 10^-5 radians Step 2 — Position error at lunar distance error = d * θ error = 384,400 * 4.848 × 10^-5 error = 18.6 km /* why DSN is still essential */

Stars in Deep Space

There's no atmosphere in deep space. No clouds, no air glow, no light pollution. Stars don't twinkle — they burn as steady, unwavering points. This makes star tracking far more reliable in deep space than near Earth. The tracker's biggest challenge isn't finding stars — it's filtering out the Sun, Moon, and Earth, which are bright enough to overwhelm the sensor.

Section 4

Inertial Navigation

Orion's Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) is the spacecraft's inner ear. Three ring laser gyroscopes measure how fast the spacecraft is rotating around each axis. Three accelerometers measure how hard the spacecraft is being pushed in each direction. Together, these six measurements fully describe the spacecraft's motion at any instant.

The principle is dead reckoning: if you know where you started, and you measure every acceleration and rotation from that point forward, you can integrate to get velocity, and integrate again to get position. The math is exact. The problem is that sensors aren't perfect — tiny measurement errors accumulate with every integration step. This is called drift.

Worked Example — IMU Drift Over 10 Hours Given: Typical navigation-grade IMU drift rate ≈ 1.5 km/hr /* position error growth */ Time without DSN update: 10 hours /* e.g. during a comm gap */ Step 1 — Accumulated position error error = drift_rate * time error = 1.5 km/hr * 10 hr error = 15 km after 10 hours /* This is why DSN "state vector updates" are critical */ /* Each update resets the IMU's reference, zeroing accumulated drift */ With DSN updates every 2 hours max_error = 1.5 * 2 = 3 km /* error never exceeds this */ Periodic updates keep error bounded at ~3 km

The solution is straightforward: periodically, the DSN sends Orion a "state vector update" — a precise measurement of where the spacecraft actually is and how fast it's actually moving. The onboard computer absorbs this update, resets its reference point, and the drift counter starts from zero. Between updates, the IMU carries the load. It's a partnership: the IMU provides continuous high-rate data, and the DSN provides periodic absolute truth.

Aviation heritage: Orion's IMU is derived from the Honeywell inertial reference units used in the Boeing 787 Dreamliner. The same sensor technology that guides commercial aircraft across oceans now guides astronauts to the Moon. Space-rated versions add radiation hardening and wider measurement ranges, but the core ring laser gyroscope design is identical.
Section 5

Trajectory Corrections (TCMs)

No matter how precisely the Space Launch System performs the Trans-Lunar Injection burn, the resulting trajectory will have tiny errors. A fraction of a degree off in pointing, a fraction of a meter per second off in velocity — and over 384,400 kilometers, these errors grow into misses of hundreds or thousands of kilometers. Trajectory Correction Maneuvers (TCMs) are small thruster firings that bring Orion back onto the planned path.

Maneuver Timing Purpose Typical Δv Thruster
TCM-1 ~TLI + 6 hours Largest correction; removes bulk of TLI dispersions ~5–10 m/s ESM auxiliary thrusters
TCM-2 ~TLI + 24 hours Refines trajectory after first day of tracking ~1–3 m/s ESM auxiliary thrusters
TCM-3 ~Moon approach - 24 hr Fine-tune for lunar flyby geometry ~0.5–2 m/s ESM RCS thrusters
TCM-4 ~Moon approach - 6 hr Final trim before closest approach <0.5 m/s ESM RCS thrusters

All trajectory corrections use the European Service Module's small auxiliary or RCS (Reaction Control System) thrusters — not the main engine. The burns are tiny: the crew would barely feel a few meters per second of acceleration. But in orbital mechanics, a small push at the right time leverages into a large change at the destination.

Worked Example — How a 1 m/s Error at TLI Grows Given: Velocity error at TLI Δv = 1 m/s Coast time to Moon t ≈ 4 days = 345,600 seconds Step 1 — Position error after 4 days (simplified linear) Δx = Δv * t Δx = 1 m/s * 345,600 s Δx = 345.6 km miss at the Moon /* A 1 m/s error grows to a 346 km miss */ /* This is why TCM-1 happens just 6 hours after TLI — */ /* the sooner you correct, the less propellant you need */ Correction cost at different times At TLI + 6 hr: error has grown to 1 * 21,600 = 21.6 km → small correction At TLI + 24 hr: error has grown to 1 * 86,400 = 86.4 km → larger correction At TLI + 4 days: error = 345.6 km → correction costs ~3x more propellant
Planning vs. Execution

The mission plan always includes four TCM opportunities, but in practice some may be skipped if the trajectory is already good enough. Apollo missions frequently skipped TCM-3 or TCM-4 when the earlier corrections were sufficiently accurate. The propellant budget for all four TCMs is relatively small — less than 20 m/s total Δv — because each correction is applied early enough to leverage the long coast phase.

Section 6

The Patched Conic Method

Planning a trajectory from Earth to the Moon requires solving a three-body problem: the spacecraft is pulled by both Earth and the Moon simultaneously. This has no closed-form solution. The patched conic method is an elegant approximation that breaks the problem into pieces that do have closed-form solutions.

The key insight is the concept of the sphere of influence (SOI). Near Earth, the Moon's gravity is negligible compared to Earth's, so the spacecraft follows a Keplerian ellipse around Earth. Near the Moon, Earth's gravity becomes the smaller perturbation, so the spacecraft follows a Keplerian hyperbola around the Moon. At the boundary between these two regimes — the sphere of influence — you "patch" the two conic sections together.

Worked Example — Moon's Sphere of Influence Given: Mass of Moon M_moon = 7.342 × 10^22 kg Mass of Earth M_earth = 5.972 × 10^24 kg Earth-Moon distance a = 384,400 km Formula (Laplace SOI) r_SOI = a * (M_moon / M_earth)^(2/5) Step 1 — Mass ratio M_moon / M_earth = 7.342 × 10^22 / 5.972 × 10^24 = 0.01229 Step 2 — Raise to 2/5 power 0.01229^(2/5) = 0.01229^0.4 ln(0.01229) = -4.399 0.4 * (-4.399) = -1.760 e^(-1.760) = 0.1720 Step 3 — SOI radius r_SOI = 384,400 * 0.1720 r_SOI = 66,117 km /* ~17.2% of Earth-Moon distance */

When Orion crosses this boundary roughly 66,000 km from the Moon, mission controllers switch their mathematical model from an Earth-centered ellipse to a Moon-centered hyperbola. The velocity and position at the SOI boundary must match — that's the "patch" in patched conics. This method was how Apollo trajectories were designed by hand in the 1960s. It's still taught to every aerospace engineering student because it builds physical intuition.

Modern mission planning at JSC uses full numerical integration of the N-body problem: Earth, Moon, Sun, Jupiter, and even solar radiation pressure are all included. Computers integrate the equations of motion in tiny time steps (seconds or less), producing trajectories accurate to meters. But the patched conic method still serves as the conceptual framework — the way engineers think about the trajectory, even when the computer does the heavy lifting.

Conic sections in one sentence: An ellipse is a circle stretched in one direction. A hyperbola is what you get when the object is moving too fast to be captured — it swoops past and escapes. Orion follows an ellipse around Earth (bound orbit), then a hyperbola around the Moon (flyby), then an ellipse back toward Earth (return).
Section 7

Communication Blackout Behind the Moon

When Orion swings behind the Moon on its free-return trajectory, the entire bulk of the Moon stands between the spacecraft and Earth. Radio signals cannot bend around the Moon. Laser light cannot bend around the Moon. For approximately 30 minutes, the crew of Artemis II will have no contact with Mission Control, no contact with their families, no contact with anyone on Earth.

During the blackout, Orion navigates entirely on its own using the onboard IMU and star trackers. The flight computers continue running the trajectory model, comparing predicted position against measured attitude. If something goes wrong, the crew and the onboard computer must handle it without any help from Houston. The spacecraft's automated abort capability is active throughout the blackout — if the computers detect a dangerous deviation, they can autonomously fire thrusters to correct course.

"The far side of the Moon is the most isolated place a human being can be. You are cut off from every living person on Earth. The silence is absolute."
— Apollo astronauts on the experience of lunar far side passage

The Artemis II crew will be the first humans to experience this since Apollo 17 in December 1972 — over 52 years earlier. Unlike Apollo, where the Command Module Pilot was alone during this passage (while the Lunar Module was on the surface), all four Artemis II crew members will be together in the Orion capsule. They will see the far side of the Moon out their windows: a landscape no human eye witnessed before 1968, and only 24 humans have ever seen in person.

Why Not Use a Relay Satellite?

A communications relay satellite in lunar orbit could eliminate the blackout, and NASA plans to deploy exactly this for Artemis III and beyond (the Lunar Gateway and relay orbiters). But for Artemis II, a crewed free-return flyby, the mission design deliberately accepts the blackout. The free-return trajectory is the safest possible path — if everything fails, Orion coasts back to Earth automatically — and adding relay infrastructure would have delayed the mission.
