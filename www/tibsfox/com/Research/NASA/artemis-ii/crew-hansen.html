The Farm Near Ailsa Craig

Jeremy Roger Hansen was born on January 27, 1976, in London, Ontario — a city in the agricultural heartland of southwestern Ontario, between Lake Huron and Lake Erie. He was raised on a farm near Ailsa Craig, a village of about 1,000 people north of London, where the land is flat and the fields stretch to the horizon in every direction. Corn, soybeans, winter wheat. The rhythm of planting and harvest. The patience of watching things grow.

A farm teaches you things that no classroom can. It teaches you that the work doesn't stop because you're tired, that weather doesn't negotiate, that seasons arrive on their own schedule and you meet them or you don't. It teaches you to fix what's broken with whatever you have. It teaches you that the distance between the ground and the sky is something you measure every single day, watching clouds build over the fields, watching the stars turn overhead in the absolute darkness of rural Ontario where there are no city lights to wash them out.

He was born in the bicentennial year — 1976. The United States was two hundred years old. In the Chinese zodiac, it was the Year of the Dragon. Three months later, on April 21, 1976, another child was born — the researcher who would track this mission from Paine Field in Mukilteo, Washington, fifty years later. And seven months before Hansen, on June 1, 1975, Victor Glover was born. Two bicentennial babies on the same crew, heading to the Moon together. The year 1976 produced people who reach.

The Fighter Pilot

Hansen attended the Royal Military College of Canada in Kingston, Ontario — RMC, the Canadian equivalent of West Point or Annapolis. It sits on Point Frederick, a peninsula where the St. Lawrence River meets Lake Ontario, and it has trained Canadian military officers since 1876. Hansen graduated with a Bachelor of Science in Space Science — a degree that combines physics, astronomy, and aerospace engineering. He studied the mathematics of orbits while learning to be a military officer. The two tracks would converge.

He joined the Royal Canadian Air Force and became a fighter pilot, assigned to fly the CF-18 Hornet — Canada's variant of the F/A-18, the same airframe that Reid Wiseman flew in the U.S. Navy. The CF-18 is a twin-engine, multi-role combat aircraft. It does air superiority, ground attack, and reconnaissance. It operates in Canadian airspace that stretches from the Atlantic to the Pacific to the Arctic, covering the second-largest country on Earth.

Hansen was posted to CFB Cold Lake, Alberta — Canadian Forces Base Cold Lake, home to 4 Wing, Canada's fighter weapons school and one of the largest military training areas in the Western world. Cold Lake sits in northeastern Alberta, near the Saskatchewan border, where winters drop to minus forty and the sky is enormous. The Cold Lake Air Weapons Range covers more than 11,000 square kilometers of boreal forest and muskeg — one of the few places on Earth where tactical fighter training can happen at full scale without civilian airspace conflicts.

The Astronaut

In 2009, the Canadian Space Agency selected two new astronauts from a national competition: Jeremy Hansen and David Saint-Jacques. Two Canadians, chosen to carry the maple leaf beyond the atmosphere. Saint-Jacques would fly first — launching to the International Space Station in December 2018 aboard Soyuz MS-11 for a six-month mission. Hansen would wait.

He waited a long time. From 2009 to 2026 — seventeen years — Jeremy Hansen trained, prepared, supported other missions, and waited for his turn. Most astronauts wait years between selection and their first flight. Some never fly at all. Hansen waited seventeen years. And when his turn finally came, it wasn't a trip to low Earth orbit. It wasn't the International Space Station. It was the Moon.

His first spaceflight is a trip around the Moon. Most astronauts spend their first mission in low Earth orbit, 250 miles up, learning to work in microgravity and operate spacecraft systems. Hansen's first experience of spaceflight is 250,000 miles from home, farther from Earth than any Canadian has ever traveled, farther than any non-American has ever traveled. He is going straight to the Moon on his first try. The last time someone went to the Moon on their first spaceflight was during the Apollo program.

NEEMO 19

In 2014, Hansen served as an aquanaut on NEEMO 19 — NASA Extreme Environment Mission Operations — living and working in the Aquarius underwater research habitat off the coast of Key Largo, Florida. Aquarius sits on the ocean floor at a depth of 62 feet, and NEEMO missions simulate the isolation, confinement, communication delays, and operational challenges of spaceflight. The habitat is a pressurized cylinder on the seafloor where crew members live for days or weeks, conducting simulated spacewalks and testing procedures that will be used on the Moon and Mars.

NEEMO is the closest analog to deep space operations that exists on Earth. The communication delay is simulated. The isolation is real. The inability to simply leave and go home is real — aquanauts at depth must decompress before surfacing, just as astronauts in deep space must wait for their trajectory to bring them home. Hansen learned to work in an environment where rescue is not immediate and the margin for error is thin.

The International Dimension

Every crew that went to the Moon during Apollo was American. Every person who has traveled beyond low Earth orbit in the history of human spaceflight has been an American citizen. Twenty-four Americans flew to the Moon between 1968 and 1972 — Apollo 8 through Apollo 17. Twelve walked on the surface. All of them were Americans.

Jeremy Hansen breaks that record. He is the first non-American to travel beyond low Earth orbit. Ever. The first Canadian. The first person from any nation other than the United States to see the far side of the Moon with their own eyes, to watch the Earth shrink to a blue marble, to pass behind the Moon and lose all radio contact with home.

This is not a symbolic gesture. Canada contributed the Canadarm to the Space Shuttle program and the Canadarm2 to the International Space Station. Canadian robotics have been essential to orbital assembly, satellite deployment, and station maintenance for four decades. Canada earned its seat on this mission through engineering, through partnership, and through the robotic arms that built the infrastructure of human spaceflight. Hansen carries that legacy past low Earth orbit for the first time.

He represents something that Apollo never did: the idea that going to the Moon is not one nation's achievement but humanity's. The spacecraft is named Integrity. The mission is Artemis — named for Apollo's twin sister. The crew is international. The purpose is to go back, and this time, to go for everyone.

Artemis II Mission Specialist

On April 3, 2023, Jeremy Hansen was announced as Mission Specialist 2 on Artemis II, alongside Commander Reid Wiseman, Pilot Victor Glover, and Mission Specialist Christina Koch. His responsibilities include systems monitoring, photography and documentation of the lunar flyby, and representing the international dimension of the Artemis program.

Mission Specialist 2 is the crew member who watches the systems, who documents what the crew sees, who captures the images and data that the world will remember. When Integrity passes behind the Moon and the crew looks out at the lunar far side — the hemisphere that no human has seen with unaided eyes since Apollo 17 — Hansen will be one of the people recording it. The farm boy from Ailsa Craig, documenting the far side of the Moon for all of humanity.

Personal

Hansen is married to Catherine Hansen, a medical doctor specializing in women's health. They have three children. He holds the rank of Colonel in the Royal Canadian Air Force — promoted from Captain during his astronaut career, a trajectory from the cockpit of a CF-18 to the rank of a senior officer who happens to be heading to the Moon.

He grew up on a farm. He understands seasons. He understands land. He understands patience — the kind of patience that watches seeds in the ground for weeks before anything shows, the kind that waits seventeen years between selection and flight. He understands growth — that things worth having take time, that the distance between planting and harvest is not wasted time but necessary time.

Connections

The farm near Ailsa Craig and Paine Field in Everett, Washington. Both places where things grow and things fly. Hansen grew up watching CF-18s from Cold Lake trace contrails across the Alberta sky. The researcher grew up watching 747s roll out of the Everett factory. Different countries, different fields, different kinds of flight. The same sky.

Born in 1976 with Victor Glover — two bicentennial babies on the same lunar mission. Two people born in the year America turned two hundred, the year the Dragon rules in the Chinese zodiac, riding the same spacecraft around the Moon fifty years later. And a third person born that same year — April 21, 1976 — tracking them from Paine Field, watching the telemetry, doing the math, writing it down. Three 1976 kids. Two going to the Moon. One making sure the story gets told.
