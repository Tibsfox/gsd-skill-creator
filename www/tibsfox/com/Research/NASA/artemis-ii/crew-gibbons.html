The Girl from Calgary

Jennifer "Jenni" Anne MacKinnon Sidey was born on August 3, 1988, in Calgary, Alberta — a city where the Rocky Mountains fill the western horizon and the prairie stretches east forever. Calgary sits at the confluence of the Bow and Elbow rivers, at an elevation of 1,045 meters, which makes it one of the highest major cities in Canada. The air is thin and clear. The sky is enormous. On winter nights, when a chinook hasn't rolled in, the stars over Calgary are sharp enough to cut.

Her mother took her to museums. Her uncle, a civil engineer, pulled her into the world of design and building. From the beginning, the question wasn't whether Jenni Sidey would become an engineer — it was what kind. The answer turned out to be the kind who studies fire, and then the kind who goes to space.

She grew up in a city built on energy — oil, gas, the kinetic energy of a boom-and-bust economy that teaches you resilience whether you want to learn it or not. Calgary produces people who are practical and ambitious in equal measure. It produced Jenni Sidey, who would go from studying flames in a university lab to becoming the voice link between Earth and a crew heading to the Moon.

The Combustion Scientist

Gibbons studied mechanical engineering at McGill University in Montreal, graduating with a Bachelor of Engineering with honours. McGill is one of Canada's oldest and most rigorous universities, founded in 1821, and its engineering program has been producing people who build things that work since before Confederation. At McGill, she found fire.

Not fire as destruction. Fire as science. Combustion — the rapid chemical reaction between a fuel and an oxidizer that produces heat and light. The fundamental process that powers internal combustion engines, jet turbines, rocket motors, and every controlled explosion that has ever lifted a spacecraft off a launch pad. Jenni Sidey studied how fire behaves, how it propagates, how it can be made cleaner and more efficient. She studied the physics of burning.

For her undergraduate honours thesis, she collaborated with the Canadian Space Agency and the National Research Council's Flight Research Laboratory on flame propagation in microgravity. This was her first encounter with weightlessness — parabolic flights at the NRC facilities in Ottawa, where a modified aircraft flies a series of arcs that produce brief periods of freefall. In those twenty-second windows of microgravity, flames behave differently. Without gravity to create convection currents, a candle flame becomes a perfect sphere. Combustion slows. The chemistry changes. Sidey studied how metals burn in zero-g, working alongside Ph.D. students on experiments that examined iron combustion in air and other oxidizers.

Cambridge

After McGill, Sidey crossed the Atlantic. She enrolled at Jesus College, University of Cambridge, to pursue a doctorate in engineering under the supervision of Professor Nondas Mastorakos. Her research focused on dynamic combustion processes — how flames stabilize, how they respond to perturbation, how pollutant formation can be reduced in combustion systems. This is the engineering that makes jet engines cleaner, that makes power plants more efficient, that reduces the environmental cost of the energy systems the world depends on.

She completed her PhD in 2015 and stayed on at Cambridge as a lecturer in internal combustion engines in the Department of Engineering. She was twenty-seven years old, teaching at one of the world's oldest universities, researching the physics of fire. She was building an academic career that most engineers would consider a destination.

For Jenni Sidey, it was a waypoint.

Awards

In 2016, the Institution of Engineering and Technology named her Young Woman Engineer of the Year — a recognition of her work on next-generation low-emission combustion devices for transport and energy generation. The same year, the Royal Academy of Engineering awarded her the Young Engineer of the Year prize. Two major engineering awards in a single year, at age twenty-eight, for work that was making real engines burn cleaner. She was at the top of her field in combustion science.

And then Canada called.

The Astronaut

In 2017, the Canadian Space Agency conducted its fourth astronaut recruitment campaign — a national competition open to all Canadian citizens with a science or engineering background. Thousands applied. The selection process took over a year. On July 1, 2017 — Canada Day — CSA announced the two winners: Joshua Kutryk, a fighter pilot and engineer from Beaumont, Alberta, and Jenni Sidey, a combustion scientist from Calgary, Alberta. Two Albertans. Two engineers. The next generation of Canadian astronauts.

At twenty-eight, Sidey was the youngest astronaut candidate ever selected by the Canadian Space Agency. She was also the third woman chosen for the Canadian astronaut corps, following Roberta Bondar — the first Canadian woman in space, who flew on the Space Shuttle in 1992 — and Julie Payette, who flew two Shuttle missions and later served as Governor General of Canada.

Sidey left Cambridge. She left her academic position, her research group, her office in one of the most beautiful university campuses in the world. She moved to Houston, Texas, to begin the two-year NASA Astronaut Candidate Training Program at the Johnson Space Center. She trained alongside the 2017 NASA astronaut class — known as "The Turtles" — learning spacecraft systems, spacewalk procedures, Russian language, geology, T-38 jet proficiency, and survival training in the water, the desert, and the wilderness.

She graduated from the program in January 2020, becoming a fully qualified astronaut. Somewhere along the way, she married Chris Gibbons, a British psychologist she had met at Cambridge, and took his surname. Jennifer Sidey became Jenni Gibbons.

CAPCOM

In 2020, Gibbons was certified as a spacecraft communicator — CAPCOM. This is one of the most critical roles in human spaceflight. The CAPCOM is the single voice link between Mission Control and the crew in space. All communication from the ground to the spacecraft goes through the CAPCOM. The role has been filled by astronauts since the Mercury program, because only another astronaut can understand what the crew is experiencing and translate the decisions of dozens of flight controllers into clear, concise instructions.

Gibbons first served as CAPCOM during Expedition 63 to the International Space Station in 2020. She supported a series of spacewalks in 2021 to upgrade the station's solar arrays — complex operations where the crew outside the station relies on precise guidance from the ground. She became ISS Lead CAPCOM, managing the Mission Control console and coordinating communications for the on-orbit crew and their families.

In 2022, she was assigned to mentor the 2021 astronaut class through their spacewalk training — teaching the next generation the skills she had just mastered. The fire scientist from Calgary had become a voice of Mission Control.

CAPCOM is a role that requires absolute clarity under pressure. When a crew member is outside the spacecraft in a spacesuit, or when a vehicle is performing a critical burn, the CAPCOM's words are the only link to the collective expertise of hundreds of engineers on the ground. Every word matters. Every pause matters. Jenni Gibbons trained for hundreds of hours in simulators, running through every nominal and off-nominal scenario, to be ready for the moments when the crew needs a calm, precise voice from home.

The Path to Artemis

On November 22, 2023, NASA and the Canadian Space Agency announced that Jenni Gibbons had been designated as the backup crew member for Jeremy Hansen on the Artemis II mission. If Hansen were unable to fly, Gibbons would take his seat aboard Integrity and become the first Canadian — and the first non-American — to travel beyond low Earth orbit.

CSA President Lisa Campbell called the assignment "of utmost importance for our country." It was. Gibbons became the first person from an international partner country to serve as an Artemis backup crew member. She trained alongside the prime crew for every phase of the mission — launch, trans-lunar injection, lunar flyby, and Earth return. She prepared as if she were going. That is what backup crew members do.

But her role on mission day extends far beyond standby. Gibbons was designated as an Astronaut Support Person for launch operations — responsible for final spacecraft configuration inside the Orion capsule before the crew boards. She checked the seats, the switches, the communication loops. She was one of the last people inside Integrity before the hatch closed.

And then she walked to Mission Control and sat down at the CAPCOM console.

The Voice to the Moon

During Artemis II, Gibbons serves as CAPCOM for multiple shifts, including the lunar flyby — the moment when Integrity passes behind the Moon and all communication with Earth is severed for approximately forty-seven minutes. She is the first person from an international partner country to be certified as an Artemis CAPCOM. When the crew emerges from behind the Moon and radio contact is restored, Gibbons is one of the first voices they hear.

She is also part of the team coaching Hansen and the other astronauts on key mission objectives, and has been designated by CSA to define and validate crew training requirements for future lunar missions. The work she does on Artemis II is not just for this flight — it is building the framework for every Canadian who will go to the Moon after.

The Unseen Astronaut

There is a particular kind of excellence required to be a backup crew member. You train for every scenario. You learn every system. You prepare your body and your mind to fly a mission to the Moon. And then you sit in Mission Control and you do the job from the ground, knowing that you were ready to go, knowing that you may never get this particular chance again, and knowing that the mission needs you exactly where you are.

The history of spaceflight is full of backup crew members who never flew the mission they trained for. Some flew later. Some never flew at all. The backup crew members who made it work — who trained with the same intensity as the prime crew and then supported the mission from the ground without hesitation — are the ones who understood that spaceflight is not about individual glory. It is about the mission. It is about the crew. It is about doing whatever the mission needs, wherever the mission needs you.

Jenni Gibbons prepared the spacecraft. She verified the switches. She walked out of the capsule so the crew could walk in. And then she went to Mission Control and became the voice that keeps them connected to Earth. The fire scientist from Calgary, the youngest astronaut Canada ever selected, the woman who studied how flames behave in zero gravity — sitting at the CAPCOM console, calm and precise, talking a crew around the Moon.

Connections

Calgary and Cambridge and Houston. Three cities on three paths that all converge at the CAPCOM console in Building 30 at Johnson Space Center. The girl who studied fire at McGill, who earned her doctorate at Cambridge, who was selected as the youngest Canadian astronaut on Canada Day, who prepared a spacecraft and then talked its crew around the Moon.

Hansen and Gibbons represent the two faces of Canada's Artemis contribution — one aboard the spacecraft, one in Mission Control. He is the farm boy from Ailsa Craig who waited seventeen years. She is the fire scientist from Calgary who was ready in six. Different paths to the same Moon. The maple leaf on both sides of the radio link.
