Department Deposits

Physics (Primary)

Wing: Radiation Belt Structure and Dual-Peak Detection

Pioneer 3 carried a single Geiger-Mueller tube and ionization chamber to 102,322 km altitude. The outbound and inbound radiation profiles were symmetric, each showing two distinct peaks separated by a minimum — proof of dual-belt structure.

  • Inner belt: trapped protons, peak at ~3,500 km (L-shell ~1.5)
  • Outer belt: trapped electrons, peak at ~16,000 km (L-shell ~3.5–4.5)
  • Slot region: reduced intensity between 8,000–12,000 km
  • Count rate: thousands/s at peak, tens/s outside belts

Mathematics (Primary)

Wing: Superposition and Fourier Analysis

The dual-peak radiation profile IS a superposition problem. Total intensity I(r) is the sum of two populations, each approximately Gaussian in L-shell space. Decomposing the measured profile into components is a nonlinear least squares problem.

  • I(r) = Iinner(r) + Iouter(r)
  • Spatial frequency: ~3 Earth radii between peaks
  • Same mathematics as spectroscopy, audio engineering, medical imaging

Biology (Primary)

Wing: Symbiosis and Dual-Organism Biology

Usnea longissima is not one organism — it is a fungus (mycobiont) and an alga (photobiont) in obligate mutualism. Neither can survive alone. Pioneer 3 discovered dual belts; Schwendener discovered dual organisms. The pattern of resolving duality from apparent unity connects mission to organism.

Chemistry (Secondary)

Wing: Atmospheric Chemistry and Usnic Acid

Usnic acid (C18H16O7): the signature metabolite of Usnea species. Synthesized by the fungal partner via the polyketide pathway. Functions: UV protection, antibacterial defense, antiherbivore deterrent. Usnea longissima absorbs pollutants directly from the air — SO2 sensitivity makes it a biomonitor.

Engineering (Secondary)

Wing: Juno II Launch Vehicle and Clustered Upper Stages

Jupiter first stage (LOX/RP-1, 667 kN) with clusters of solid-fuel Baby Sergeant rockets (11 in second stage, 3 in third, 1 in fourth). Spin stabilization before ignition. The 3.8-second first stage shortfall: similar failure class to Pioneer 1’s Able shortfall, different vehicle, different team.

Art (Secondary)

Wing: Lichen Generative Art and Dual-Structure Visualization

Visual themes of hidden duality. Lichen close-up photography reveals algal cells invisible at naked-eye scale. Generative art from Usnea branching patterns: stochastic fractal L-systems. Color palette: lichen green-gray (#A8B89C), bark brown (#5C4033), inner belt amber (#D4A853), outer belt slate-blue (#5B7C99), slot dark (#1A1A2E).

TRY Sessions

TRY 1: Model the Dual Van Allen Belt Profile as Superposition of Two Gaussians

30 minutes | Beginner | Mathematics / Physics | Python 3.8+, numpy, matplotlib

The moment you realize that Pioneer 3’s radiation profile — a single wiggly line from a single instrument — contains two hidden populations, and that you can separate them with nothing more than curve fitting. Create the synthetic dual-belt profile, plot the dual-peak structure, then recover the two Gaussians by curve fitting using scipy.optimize.curve_fit.

TRY 2: Count Poisson-Distributed Events with a Random Number Generator

20 minutes | Beginner | Mathematics / Physics | Python 3.8+

The statistical heartbeat of every particle detector ever built. Simulate Pioneer 3’s Geiger counter at different altitudes. Generate Poisson-distributed counts and observe: at 500 counts/sec (inner belt), relative error is 4.5%. At 2 counts/sec (beyond belts), relative error is 70.7%. The Poisson floor sets the minimum measurement time needed at each altitude.

TRY 3: Find Usnea Lichen in a Local Forest and Photograph It

2–4 hours (field trip) | Beginner | Biology / Art | Legs, eyes, camera

Find a dual organism in the wild. The field test: gently pull a strand of Usnea apart — the outer cortex breaks, revealing a white elastic central cord. This cord is diagnostic. Photograph the habitat, individual strands, and macro surface texture. Document location, tree species, strand length, and air quality notes. Every photograph is a conservation data point.

DIY Projects

DIY 1: Arduino Dual-Detector Radiation Simulator ($30)

Two LED bar graphs simulating Pioneer 3’s outbound and inbound radiation belt traversals. As simulated altitude increases, the bars light up showing two peaks on the way up, two on the way back. The symmetry between outbound and inbound was Pioneer 3’s proof. Materials: Arduino Nano ($8), 2x LED bar graphs ($4 each), potentiometer ($1), SSD1306 OLED ($8), breadboard + wires ($5). Build time: 3–4 hours.

DIY 2: Grow Lichen on a Surface ($10)

The slowest, most patient project in the entire NASA mission series. Create conditions for lichen colonization on a rock or brick using a lichen/buttermilk slurry. Growth is measured in millimeters per year. If your substrate is near a road or in a polluted area, lichen will not grow — the experiment itself is an air quality test. Duration: months to years.

DIY 3: Cartesian Coordinate Plotter with Stepper Motors ($45)

A small XY plotter built from two stepper motors, a pen holder, and an Arduino — a machine that draws in Cartesian coordinates. Program it to draw Pioneer 3’s trajectory, the dual-belt radiation profile, or Usnea branching patterns. The plotter literalizes Descartes’ contribution: every point on the paper is an (x, y) pair. Build time: 6–8 hours.
