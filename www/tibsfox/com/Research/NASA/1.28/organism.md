# Organism Pairing: Ramalina menziesii (Lace Lichen)

## The Net That Catches Fog

**Scientific Name:** Ramalina menziesii Taylor
**Family:** Ramalinaceae (Ramalina family)
**Common Names:** Lace lichen, fishnet lichen, cartilage lichen
**Paired Mission:** 1.28 -- Ranger 3 (Atlas-Agena B), January 26, 1962
**PNW Status:** Native, coastal -- the pendant lichen of the fog belt, California's state lichen

---

## What Reaches Without Touching

Walk through a coastal forest on the Olympic Peninsula on a February morning when the marine layer has pushed inland overnight and the fog is so dense that every branch drips. Look at the branches themselves -- not the main limbs but the smaller lateral branches that extend into the open air between the trunks. Hanging from these branches, you will see pale yellow-green strands of what looks like torn netting -- delicate, lace-like structures that hang vertically, swaying in any breath of air, wet and glistening with captured fog droplets. These are the thalli of Ramalina menziesii, the lace lichen, and they are doing what they were built to do: intercepting moisture from air that passes through them.

Ramalina menziesii is not a plant. It is a lichen -- a composite organism consisting of a fungal partner (the mycobiont, typically an ascomycete) and a photosynthetic partner (the photobiont, in this case the green alga Trebouxia). The fungus provides the structure, the protection, and the mineral uptake. The alga provides the photosynthesis -- the conversion of captured light and CO2 into sugars that feed the composite organism. Neither partner could survive alone in the habitat they jointly occupy. The fungus without the alga would be a saprophyte, dependent on decaying organic matter. The alga without the fungus would be a microscopic film on wet surfaces, vulnerable to desiccation, UV, and grazing. Together, they become something that neither could be alone: an organism that hangs in the air, captures fog, photosynthesizes in maritime light, and persists for decades on a branch that provides nothing but a physical platform.

The resonance with Ranger 3 begins with the reaching. Ramalina menziesii's pendant morphology is an extension into space -- the lichen hangs from its attachment point and reaches into the air the way an antenna reaches into the electromagnetic field. The lichen is reaching for water. Specifically, it is reaching for fog water -- liquid water suspended as droplets in the air, carried by marine winds from the Pacific Ocean through the coastal forest canopy. The lichen cannot actively draw water from the air; it has no roots, no stomata, no vascular system, no active transport mechanism. It depends entirely on physical contact -- fog droplets must collide with the thallus surface and be absorbed through the cortex. The lichen reaches, and it waits for what the air brings.

Ranger 3 reached for the Moon. The spacecraft's trajectory was designed to intercept the lunar surface -- to bring cameras, a spectrometer, and a seismometer into direct contact with the Moon. The spacecraft could not steer itself to the Moon; its trajectory was set by the launch vehicle during the injection burn. After injection, the spacecraft could apply a small midcourse correction, but the fundamental trajectory was determined by the guidance system during the first few minutes of flight. Ranger 3 reached for the Moon the way Ramalina reaches for fog: extending itself into a medium, aimed at a target, dependent on the initial conditions being right. The fog came too far inland and the lichen prospered. The fog stayed offshore and the lichen desiccated. The guidance system pointed correctly and Ranger 3 would have hit the Moon. The guidance system had an inverted sign and Ranger 3 missed by 36,793 kilometers.

## Morphology: Architecture of Interception

The most distinctive feature of Ramalina menziesii is its pendant, net-like morphology. Unlike crustose lichens that form flat crusts on bark, or foliose lichens that form leafy rosettes, Ramalina menziesii hangs freely from its attachment point in long, branching strands that can exceed one meter in length on old specimens. The thallus is composed of flattened, strap-like branches, typically 2-5 mm wide and 0.3-1 mm thick, that fork repeatedly and interconnect to form a reticulate (net-like) structure. The openings in the net are irregular, ranging from a few millimeters to several centimeters across.

This mesh architecture is the key to the lichen's ecological strategy. A solid plate of the same overall dimension would deflect airflow around it; the air (and the fog droplets it carries) would be diverted rather than intercepted. The mesh allows air to flow through the thallus, bringing fog droplets into contact with internal surfaces that a solid plate could not expose. The principle is identical to that of fog-harvesting nets used in arid coastal regions around the world -- the mesh geometry captures more water per unit of material than any solid surface could.

The thallus surface is covered with soralia -- small, granular structures that contain both fungal hyphae and algal cells. These are the lichen's primary mode of asexual reproduction: soredia (individual granules) are dispersed by wind, rain, and animal contact, and each soredium can establish a new lichen if it lands on a suitable substrate. The soralia also increase the thallus surface area, enhancing both fog capture and gas exchange. The surface texture of Ramalina menziesii is rough at the microscale -- a characteristic that increases droplet adhesion and slows the drainage of captured water, giving the thallus more time to absorb it.

The attachment to the substrate is minimal -- a small holdfast that anchors the thallus to the bark but does not penetrate the bark or derive any nutrition from it. Ramalina menziesii is an epiphyte in the strict sense: it uses the tree only as a physical platform. It takes nothing from the tree -- no water, no minerals, no photosynthate. It contributes to the tree primarily through fog drip: the water that the lichen captures but does not absorb drips from the thallus to the forest floor, increasing the soil moisture beneath fog-heavy trees. In this sense, the lichen is a water harvester that benefits not only itself but the entire understory community below it.

## Ecology: The Fog Belt Organism

Ramalina menziesii's distribution is defined by fog. The species ranges along the Pacific coast from southeastern Alaska to Baja California, with the highest abundances in the fog belt -- the zone, typically 0-30 kilometers from the coast, where marine fog penetrates inland with sufficient frequency and density to sustain fog-dependent organisms. In the Pacific Northwest, this means the coastal forests of the Olympic Peninsula, the Willapa Hills, the Oregon Coast Range, and the fog-prone valleys of the northern California coast.

The fog belt is not a constant. Marine fog forms when warm, moist air moves over the cold California Current and condenses into droplets. The fog is carried inland by prevailing winds, typically overnight and in the early morning, before solar heating burns it off by midday. The frequency, duration, and penetration depth of fog events vary with season, latitude, and local topography. Summer fog is most common in California (the classic San Francisco fog); winter fog is more characteristic of the Pacific Northwest. Ramalina menziesii's distribution tracks the fog frequency: where fog comes regularly, the lichen thrives. Where fog is rare, the lichen is absent.

This makes Ramalina menziesii an indicator species -- a biological marker for the fog belt's extent. Ecologists studying the impacts of climate change on coastal fog patterns use lichen surveys to map how the fog belt is shifting. As California's coastal fog frequency has declined (some studies estimate a 33% reduction since the mid-twentieth century), Ramalina menziesii populations have contracted. The lichen is retreating with the fog. When the fog moves, the lichen moves. When the fog disappears, the lichen disappears.

The lichen's sensitivity to air quality adds another dimension. Ramalina menziesii is highly sensitive to sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides -- the pollutants of fossil fuel combustion. In areas near industrial sources or heavy traffic, the lichen disappears. In areas with clean air, it flourishes. This makes it a dual indicator: a marker for both fog availability and air quality. The lichen tells you two things at once: is the fog coming, and is the air clean? If both answers are yes, lace lichen hangs from every branch. If either answer is no, the branches are bare.

## The Fog-Capture Problem

Ramalina menziesii's water acquisition is a passive interception process that can be modeled quantitatively. The fog capture rate Q depends on:

- **Projected area (A_p):** The area of the thallus perpendicular to the wind direction. For a large Ramalina thallus, A_p may be 200-500 cm². The mesh morphology is important: the holes in the net prevent deflection of airflow, allowing air to pass through and deposit droplets on internal surfaces.

- **Fog liquid water content (LWC):** The mass of liquid water per unit volume of foggy air. For PNW coastal fog, LWC is typically 0.1-0.5 g/m³. Heavy fog events can reach 0.5-1.0 g/m³.

- **Wind speed (v):** Fog must move through the lichen for interception to occur. On calm days, even dense fog provides little water because the air is static. Wind speeds of 1-5 m/s are optimal; higher winds can damage the delicate thallus.

- **Capture efficiency (η):** The fraction of fog droplets in the lichen's projected area that actually contact the thallus surface. This depends on droplet size (larger droplets have more inertia and are more likely to impact rather than follow the airflow around the thallus), mesh density, and thallus surface roughness. Typical η values for Ramalina menziesii are 0.3-0.7.

The capture rate is: Q = η × A_p × v × LWC

For a large thallus (A_p = 0.05 m²) in moderate fog (LWC = 0.25 g/m³) with light wind (v = 2 m/s) and average efficiency (η = 0.5): Q = 0.0125 g/s = 45 g/hr. Over a six-hour fog event, a single large thallus captures approximately 270 grams of water -- more than enough to fully hydrate the lichen and contribute to fog drip that waters the understory below.

On clear days, the capture rate is zero. The lichen desiccates, its metabolic rate drops to near zero, and it enters dormancy. The transition is controlled by thallus water content: above approximately 25% water content (by dry weight), the lichen is metabolically active. Below that threshold, it is dormant. The threshold is sharp -- a binary switch between active and dormant states, the lichen equivalent of escape velocity.

## Resonance Axes

### 1. Reaching Without Touching
Ramalina menziesii hangs from branches and extends into the air -- reaching for fog moisture that it cannot actively pursue. The lichen's entire morphology is an optimized antenna for intercepting a resource that arrives passively or not at all. Ranger 3 extended itself toward the Moon -- cameras, seismometer, spectrometer all aimed at a surface the spacecraft needed to contact. The trajectory was set by the launch vehicle; the spacecraft could not actively pursue its target. Both organisms reach. Whether they touch depends on conditions they do not control. The fog came, or it didn't. The guidance was right, or it wasn't. At 36,793 km and on a clear morning, the answer is the same: reached, did not touch.

### 2. Passive Interception vs. Active Pursuit
Lace lichen is a passive interceptor. It cannot chase fog. It hangs and waits for the air to bring moisture. The Violet-green Swallow, by contrast, is an active pursuer -- it chases insects with real-time guidance corrections, adjusting its trajectory with every wingbeat. Ranger 3 was a passive interceptor: after the injection burn, it could not chase the Moon. Its trajectory was set. The midcourse correction was a partial active capability -- but with 34 m/s against a 375 m/s error, it was like a lichen straining toward fog that was thirty miles offshore. The active/passive distinction determines whether a miss is recoverable. Swallows miss insects and catch the next one. Lichens miss fog events and desiccate until the next one. Ranger 3 missed the Moon and entered heliocentric orbit forever.

### 3. The Indicator Species
Ramalina menziesii is an indicator species -- its presence or absence tells you something about the environment. If you see lace lichen, the air is clean and the fog comes regularly. If you don't see it, one or both conditions has failed. Ranger 3 is an indicator species for the Ranger program: its specific failure mode (guidance error) told JPL something about the testing regime. The fact that the sign inversion passed component testing meant the testing was incomplete. The lichen's absence tells you what is wrong with the air. Ranger 3's miss distance told JPL what was wrong with the test procedures.

### 4. Dual Partnership
Ramalina menziesii is not one organism but two -- a fungus and an alga in obligate mutualism. Neither can occupy the fog-belt niche alone. Ranger 3's mission required a dual partnership of a different kind: the Atlas (built by Convair for the Air Force) and the Agena B (built by Lockheed for the Air Force) were paired with the spacecraft (built by JPL for NASA). The launch vehicle was Air Force hardware operated under NASA mission authority. The dual partnership -- military hardware, civilian mission -- was the institutional equivalent of the lichen's symbiosis. When the partnership worked (Agena B restart), it worked beautifully. When it failed (Atlas guidance), the failure came from the Air Force side of the partnership, in a component (the booster autopilot) that was outside JPL's visibility and testing authority.

### 5. The Fog Belt Is Moving
Climate change is shifting the fog belt. Ramalina menziesii populations are contracting as fog frequency declines. The lichen's habitat is being eroded by a large-scale environmental change that the lichen cannot respond to -- it can adapt its morphology, but it cannot follow the fog if the fog stops coming. The Ranger program's "fog belt" -- the institutional environment that would support repeated lunar missions despite repeated failures -- was also shifting. After three consecutive Ranger failures, NASA Headquarters was losing patience. The political fog that sustained the program was thinning. By Ranger 5 (October 1962), the program was nearly cancelled. The fog of institutional tolerance was moving offshore, and the Ranger program, like the lace lichen, could not follow it. Only Ranger 7's success in July 1964 brought the fog back.

## Taxonomy Note

Ramalina menziesii was described by Thomas Taylor in 1847, based on specimens collected by Archibald Menzies during the Vancouver Expedition (1791-1795) -- the same Menzies who collected the first Douglas-fir specimens (Pseudotsuga menziesii, our v1.5 organism). The species epithet again honors Menzies, and the overlap is not coincidental: Menzies collected both the lichen and the tree during the same expedition to the Pacific Northwest coast. The Douglas-fir and the lace lichen occupy the same forests -- Douglas-fir provides the structural canopy, and Ramalina menziesii hangs from the branches of the canopy trees, intercepting fog that Douglas-fir's needles have not yet captured. The two organisms named for the same collector are vertical neighbors in the same forest, separated by the v1.5-to-v1.28 span of the mission catalog.

Ramalina menziesii became California's state lichen on January 1, 2016 -- the first lichen designated as a state symbol anywhere in the United States. The designation was led by the California Lichen Society and championed by schoolchildren who learned about the species' role in fog-belt ecology. It is a fitting choice: a lichen that depends on California's iconic fog, that indicates the health of the state's coastal forests, and that has no common name more evocative than "lace lichen" -- a description that captures the organism's most distinctive feature with two words.
