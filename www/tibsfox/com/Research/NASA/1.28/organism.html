Organism Pairing: Lace Lichen

Ramalina menziesii — the net that catches fog, the antenna aimed at water that may or may not arrive

Date: January 26, 1962Program: Ranger (NASA/JPL)Organism: Ramalina menziesiiBird: Violet-green Swallow

The Net That Catches Fog

Walk through a coastal forest on the Olympic Peninsula on a February morning when the marine layer has pushed inland overnight and the fog is so dense that every branch drips. Hanging from the branches, you will see pale yellow-green strands of what looks like torn netting — delicate, lace-like structures that hang vertically, swaying in any breath of air, wet and glistening with captured fog droplets. These are the thalli of Ramalina menziesii, the lace lichen, and they are doing what they were built to do: intercepting moisture from air that passes through them.

Ramalina menziesii is not a plant. It is a lichen — a composite organism consisting of a fungal partner and a photosynthetic alga. The fungus provides structure and protection. The alga provides photosynthesis. Neither could survive alone in the habitat they jointly occupy. Together, they become something that neither could be alone: an organism that hangs in the air, captures fog, and persists for decades on a branch that provides nothing but a physical platform.

The resonance with Ranger 3 begins with the reaching. Ramalina menziesii’s pendant morphology is an extension into space — the lichen reaches for water the way an antenna reaches into the electromagnetic field. It cannot actively draw water from the air. It depends entirely on physical contact — fog droplets must collide with the thallus surface and be absorbed. The lichen reaches, and it waits for what the air brings.

Ranger 3 reached for the Moon. The spacecraft’s trajectory was set by the launch vehicle. After injection, the spacecraft could apply a small midcourse correction, but the fundamental trajectory was determined in the first minutes of flight. Ranger 3 reached for the Moon the way Ramalina reaches for fog: extending itself into a medium, aimed at a target, dependent on the initial conditions being right. The guidance system had an inverted sign and Ranger 3 missed by 36,793 kilometers. The fog didn’t come. The lichen dried. The cameras never fired.

Morphology: Architecture of Interception

The most distinctive feature of Ramalina menziesii is its pendant, net-like morphology. Unlike crustose lichens that form flat crusts, Ramalina menziesii hangs freely in strands that can exceed one meter in length. The thallus branches into flat, strap-like segments, 2-5 mm wide, that fork repeatedly and interconnect to form a reticulate mesh. The openings in the net allow air to flow through — bringing fog droplets into contact with internal surfaces. A solid plate would deflect airflow. The mesh intercepts it. The same principle as fog-harvesting nets used in arid coastal regions worldwide.

Ecology: The Fog Belt Organism

Ramalina menziesii’s distribution is defined by fog. The species ranges along the Pacific coast from southeastern Alaska to Baja California, with highest abundances in the fog belt — the zone where marine fog penetrates inland with sufficient frequency to sustain fog-dependent organisms. Where fog comes regularly, the lichen thrives. Where fog is rare, the lichen is absent.

This makes Ramalina menziesii an indicator species. Its presence tells you the air is clean and the fog comes. Its absence tells you what is wrong. As California’s coastal fog frequency has declined — some studies estimate a 33% reduction since the mid-twentieth century — Ramalina menziesii populations have contracted. The lichen is retreating with the fog.

Ranger 3’s miss distance told JPL something analogous about the testing regime: the sign inversion passed component testing because the test was incomplete. The lichen’s absence tells you what is wrong with the air. The miss distance told JPL what was wrong with the test procedures.

Taxonomy Note

Ramalina menziesii was described by Thomas Taylor in 1847, based on specimens collected by Archibald Menzies during the Vancouver Expedition — the same Menzies who collected the first Douglas-fir specimens (Pseudotsuga menziesii, our v1.5 organism). Ramalina menziesii became California’s state lichen on January 1, 2016 — the first lichen designated as a state symbol anywhere in the United States.
