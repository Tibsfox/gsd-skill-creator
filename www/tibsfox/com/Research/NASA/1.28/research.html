Deep Research: The Guidance Error

Ranger 3 — plus instead of minus, 36,793 km of nothing between the cameras and the Moon

Date: January 26, 1962 Program: Ranger (NASA/JPL) Organism: Lace lichen Dedication: Bessie Coleman

Plus Instead of Minus

At 20:30 UTC on January 26, 1962, an Atlas-Agena B launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Canaveral Air Force Station. On top of the Agena B upper stage sat a 329.8-kilogram spacecraft — the heaviest American planetary payload yet launched, carrying the first American television cameras ever aimed at another world, a gamma-ray spectrometer to measure the Moon’s surface composition, and a 42.6-kilogram balsa-wood capsule containing a seismometer designed to survive impact with the lunar surface.

This was Ranger 3, the first Block II Ranger — the first spacecraft in the program designed not merely to test systems in deep space but to actually reach the Moon, crash into it, and return science along the way.

The Agena B restarted successfully — the critical milestone that Rangers 1 and 2 had failed to achieve. The propellant management improvements worked. But during the Atlas booster phase, a sign had been inverted in the booster autopilot’s pitch-rate switching amplifier. The amplifier, designed to provide negative feedback, was instead providing positive feedback. When the vehicle pitched up, the amplifier commanded more pitch-up. A second error in the Atlas guidance computer compounded the deviation.

The combined effect: Ranger 3 left Earth orbit approximately 375 meters per second faster than planned. The spacecraft sailed past the Moon at 36,793 kilometers — close enough to be gravitationally deflected, far enough that every science instrument was useless. The television cameras, designed for the final twenty minutes of descent, saw nothing but empty space. The seismometer capsule was never deployed. Ranger 3 entered heliocentric orbit, carrying America’s first lunar television cameras into a solar orbit where they will photograph the Sun forever instead of the Moon once.

The Midcourse Correction: Not Enough

When the Deep Space Network’s tracking data revealed the trajectory error, JPL computed a midcourse correction. The spacecraft’s hydrazine motor fired, producing 34 m/s of delta-v. The correction was the right idea and entirely inadequate: 34 m/s against a 375 m/s error. Nine percent of what was needed.

The midcourse correction motor was sized for trajectory trimming, not trajectory rescue. The expected injection errors were tens of meters per second. Nobody had anticipated a systematic guidance error seven times the motor’s capacity. The lesson: design the correction budget for the worst case, not the typical case. The worst case is the one that happens.

The Error Diagnosis

Error 1: Booster autopilot sign inversion. The pitch-rate switching amplifier had an inverted polarity. The component test checked magnitude, not sign. A switching amplifier that provides the right amount of correction in the wrong direction passes a magnitude-only test while producing catastrophic results in flight.

Error 2: Guidance computer trajectory computation. An independent error in the guidance computer’s real-time trajectory model caused engine cutoff at a slightly wrong time, adding to the velocity excess.

Two errors. Independent. Same direction. Combined: 375 m/s of excess velocity, 36,793 km of empty space.

Dedication: Bessie Coleman (January 26, 1892 — April 30, 1926)

Bessie Coleman was born on the exact calendar date that Ranger 3 launched, sixty-nine years earlier. She aimed at the sky when every guidance system in American society pointed her toward the ground. American flight schools refused Black women. Coleman’s trajectory correction was radical: she learned French, sailed to France, and earned her pilot’s license at the Caudron Brothers’ school — the first African American woman and first Native American to hold an international pilot’s license.

Coleman’s guidance error was imposed by a nation with an inverted sign on who was allowed to fly. She corrected by changing the reference frame entirely. Ranger 3 could not change its booster. Coleman could and did.
