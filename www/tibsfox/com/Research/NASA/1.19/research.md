# Mission 1.19 -- Mercury-Redstone 3 / Freedom 7

## The Selection

In late 1958 and early 1959, seven military test pilots were selected for Project Mercury -- the United States' first human spaceflight program. They were Scott Carpenter, Gordon Cooper, John Glenn, Gus Grissom, Wally Schirra, Alan Shepard, and Deke Slayton. The Mercury Seven. The press called them astronauts. They called themselves pilots. They had been chosen from a pool of 508 candidates, winnowed through medical examinations that were as inventive as they were invasive, psychological evaluations that bordered on harassment, and engineering interviews that sought a specific and paradoxical combination: a man calm enough to sit in a capsule while someone else flew it, and aggressive enough to take control if nobody else could.

The question of who would fly first was not decided at selection. It was decided later, quietly, by Robert R. Gilruth, director of NASA's Space Task Group at Langley Research Center in Hampton, Virginia. Gilruth was a methodical man -- an aeronautical engineer by training, a wind tunnel researcher by career, a manager by necessity. He had been given the task of putting a human being into space before the Soviets, and the question of who that human being would be was, in his assessment, a question of engineering and temperament, not politics or seniority or the preferences of the press.

By early 1961, the field had narrowed to three: Shepard, Glenn, and Grissom. The other four astronauts knew they were not in the running for the first flight. These three knew they were. What followed was a competition conducted almost entirely in silence. The three men trained together, ate meals together, attended the same briefings, ran the same simulations, underwent the same medical checks. Each knew that he was being evaluated against the other two. Each knew that the decision rested with Gilruth and that Gilruth was not talking.

John Glenn was the most publicly visible of the three -- a decorated Marine pilot, a combat veteran of both World War II and Korea, a man who had set a transcontinental speed record in 1957, a natural in front of cameras, articulate and disciplined and photogenic in the way that the press understood. Glenn was the obvious choice if the choice were being made by the public affairs office. He was also, by several accounts, the choice that the other astronauts least expected Gilruth to make. Glenn was respected but not always liked within the astronaut office. His public persona -- the Marine scout leader, the Sunday school teacher, the man who lectured his colleagues about their personal behavior when the press was watching -- created friction. The other pilots did not question his competence. They questioned whether his instinct for performing for the camera would make him harder to manage in the cockpit.

Gus Grissom was the quietest of the three. A mechanical engineering graduate from Purdue, a veteran of 100 combat missions in Korea flying F-86 Sabres, Grissom was technically gifted, laconic, and uninterested in the public relations dimension of the astronaut role. He flew the simulators with precision and expressed his assessments in short, direct sentences that contained no adjectives. If the choice were being made by the engineering staff, Grissom would have been a strong candidate. He was the pilot most naturally aligned with the machine.

Alan Bartlett Shepard Jr. was neither the most public nor the most quiet. He was the most competitive. Shepard was a Navy test pilot out of the Naval Air Test Center at Patuxent River, Maryland -- Pax River, the Navy's equivalent of Edwards Air Force Base, where the Navy sent its best pilots to test its newest and most dangerous aircraft. Shepard had logged over 8,000 hours of flight time, much of it in experimental aircraft. He was intensely intelligent, intensely ambitious, intensely aware of his own skills, and intensely interested in being first. He was also, by the accounts of people who worked with him, difficult. Where Glenn was warm to strangers and prickly to peers, Shepard was cool to everyone and warm to almost no one. He had a sharp tongue, a competitive edge that other pilots found exhausting, and an ice-water confidence that could read as arrogance. He was, by most internal assessments, the best pilot of the seven. He was also the one most likely to tell you so.

On January 19, 1961, Gilruth called the three finalists into his office and told them the order: Shepard first, Grissom second, Glenn third. Glenn would serve as Shepard's backup. The selection was not announced publicly. The three men were instructed to keep the decision private. The logic of the secrecy was partly security -- if the flight failed, the press would not have spent weeks profiling the man who died -- and partly political, allowing NASA flexibility to change the order if medical or training developments warranted it. The secrecy held for months. The press speculated constantly. NASA said nothing. Shepard knew he was first, Glenn knew he was third, and the space between those facts was a silence that both men carried differently. Glenn carried it visibly, with a professionalism that cost him something the records don't measure. Shepard carried it the way he carried everything -- with an expression that revealed nothing and an interior state that no one was permitted to see.

## The Delays

The first Mercury-Redstone crewed flight was originally targeted for early 1961. The schedule slipped. It slipped again. The Redstone booster and Mercury capsule had been through a development test series that included its own catalog of problems -- MR-1, launched on November 21, 1960, had risen four inches off the pad before the engine cut off and the capsule settled back down, in what became known as the "four-inch flight," one of the most famous non-events in rocket history. The escape tower fired. The capsule stayed on the rocket. The drogue chute deployed from the capsule on the pad. The main chute followed. It was a perfect sequence of pyrotechnic events triggered by a premature shutdown that was caused by a small electrical plug disconnecting from the booster in the wrong order, a few milliseconds before the flight should have been committed. Four inches. The Mercury program's first crewed launch attempt had risen four inches and then demonstrated, with excruciating precision, every abort system working correctly in a scenario that should never have occurred.

MR-1A corrected the problem and flew successfully on December 19, 1960 -- an unmanned suborbital test. MR-2 followed on January 31, 1961, carrying Ham the chimpanzee on the flight that would qualify the system for a human crew. Ham's flight was successful but not smooth -- the Redstone over-performed, giving Ham a higher trajectory and faster reentry than planned, with a peak g-force of 14.7 instead of the expected 11. The capsule also lost cabin pressure during flight. Ham survived, performed his lever tasks, and was recovered from the Atlantic, but the engineering data from MR-2 required one more unmanned test to verify that the anomalies had been addressed. That test -- Mercury-Redstone Booster Development, MR-BD -- launched on March 24, 1961 and flew a nominal profile. The path to a crewed launch was clear.

Then, on April 12, 1961, Yuri Alexeyevich Gagarin launched from Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan aboard Vostok 1 and orbited the Earth. One orbit. 108 minutes. The first human being in space. The first human voice transmitted from orbit. The Soviet Union had won the most consequential race of the Cold War. The United States -- which had spent years and hundreds of millions of dollars building a space program -- had not yet put a man above the atmosphere.

The reaction in Washington was seismic. President Kennedy, who had been in office for less than three months, was furious -- not at NASA specifically, but at the situation. The United States was second. Kennedy had inherited the space program from Eisenhower and had not yet decided what to do with it. Gagarin's flight made the decision for him. The question was no longer whether to invest in human spaceflight but how much and how fast.

NASA pushed the Mercury-Redstone 3 launch forward. The flight was scheduled for May 2, 1961. On May 2, the launch was scrubbed due to weather -- cloud cover over the Cape that would have prevented visual tracking of the rocket during ascent. The scrub pushed the launch to May 4. On May 4, the launch was scrubbed again -- weather, again. Shepard, who had been through the entire suit-up and insertion process twice, who had lain on his back in the capsule for hours waiting for a countdown that never reached zero, was not amused. The launch was rescheduled for May 5.

## Light This Candle

May 5, 1961. Cape Canaveral, Florida. Launch Complex 5. Alan Shepard had been awake since 1:10 AM Eastern Time, roused by Dr. William Douglas, the astronauts' flight surgeon. Shepard ate a breakfast of steak, eggs, and orange juice -- the traditional test pilot breakfast, which would become the traditional astronaut breakfast for years afterward, not because of any nutritional logic but because Shepard ate it first and nobody saw a reason to change the ritual. He underwent a final medical examination, was fitted with biosensors, and suited up in the silver Mercury pressure suit.

The transfer to the pad, the elevator ride to the capsule level, the insertion into Freedom 7 -- capsule number 7, the seventh Mercury capsule produced by McDonnell Aircraft in St. Louis -- took place in the predawn darkness. Shepard was sealed inside the capsule at 5:15 AM. The countdown proceeded. It held. It held again. There were technical issues -- a power supply inverter in the Redstone needed replacement. Weather observations were taken. The countdown clock stopped and started and stopped.

Shepard lay on his back in the capsule, knees up, staring at the instrument panel approximately two feet from his face, waiting. He had been in the capsule for over three hours. The Mercury capsule had no provision for urination. The flight was expected to last fifteen minutes. Nobody had planned for a three-hour-plus wait on the pad followed by a fifteen-minute flight. Nobody had considered that the pilot might need to urinate during the wait. Shepard needed to urinate. He told Mercury Control. Mercury Control told him to hold it. Shepard said he could not hold it. There was a discussion. The biosensors on his body were electrical. His suit was not designed for fluid management. If he urinated in his suit, the liquid might short-circuit the biosensors and corrupt the biomedical data. Mercury Control discussed the problem. The discussion took time. Shepard waited. Finally, the medical team decided to power down the biosensors briefly to allow Shepard to urinate in his suit. He did. The sensors were powered back up. The data resumed. The pad of urine absorbed into the cotton undergarment and began to dry against his skin from body heat. The first American in space launched wet.

Another hold was called. Shepard, who had been in the capsule for over four hours at this point, who had urinated in his pressure suit, who had been through two prior launch scrubs, who had watched Gagarin fly three weeks earlier, who had been told he would be first and then been made to wait while the weather cleared and the engineers debated and the countdown clocks started and stopped -- Shepard keyed his microphone and said the words that would become the most-quoted sentence of the Mercury program: "Why don't you fix your little problem and light this candle?"

The countdown resumed. At 9:34 AM Eastern Standard Time, the Redstone's A-7 engine ignited. The rocket rose from Pad 5 on a column of flame and kerosene exhaust. "Roger, liftoff, and the clock is started," Shepard reported, his voice controlled, the test pilot delivering the data. America's first manned spaceflight had begun.

## Fifteen Minutes and Twenty-Eight Seconds

The Redstone A-7 engine burned for 142 seconds, producing approximately 78,000 pounds of thrust, accelerating the capsule and its occupant to a velocity of 2,294 meters per second -- 8,260 kilometers per hour, approximately Mach 6.8. At engine cutoff, the capsule separated from the spent Redstone booster. Small posigrade rockets pushed Freedom 7 clear of the booster. Shepard was in space.

The capsule followed a ballistic arc -- a parabola, like a thrown ball, governed entirely by gravity and the momentum imparted by the Redstone at burnout. The peak altitude would be 187.5 kilometers -- 116.5 statute miles -- well above the Karman line at 100 kilometers that marks the conventional boundary of space. The flight was suborbital. Freedom 7 would not circle the Earth. It would rise, arc over, and fall back into the Atlantic Ocean approximately 486 kilometers downrange from Cape Canaveral. The entire flight, from liftoff to splashdown, would last fifteen minutes and twenty-eight seconds.

Shepard had approximately five minutes of weightlessness near the top of the arc. He used them. Unlike the Soviet Vostok capsule -- which was entirely automated, with Gagarin essentially a passenger whose manual controls were locked behind a code he was not supposed to need -- the Mercury capsule was designed to be flown. Shepard tested the manual attitude control system. He pitched the capsule nose-up, nose-down, back to center. He yawed left, right, center. He rolled clockwise, counterclockwise, center. Each axis responded correctly. The hydrogen peroxide thrusters fired when he commanded them to fire and stopped when he released the controller. The capsule did what he told it to do. This was the primary test objective: could a human pilot manually control a spacecraft in the space environment? The answer was yes. The controls worked. The pilot worked. The system was flyable.

Shepard looked through the capsule's periscope -- a device mounted in the capsule hull that gave him an external view of the Earth below. The view was extraordinary. He could see the coast of Florida, the Bahamas, Lake Okeechobee. "What a beautiful view," he reported. He was not the first person to see the Earth from space -- Gagarin had seen it twenty-three days earlier -- but he was the first American, and the words he chose were not scripted, not rehearsed, not pulled from a public affairs briefing card. They were the words of a man looking out a window and saying what he saw.

The retrorockets fired -- three small solid-fuel rockets mounted on the heat shield, each burning for approximately ten seconds, decelerating the capsule for reentry. The retropack was jettisoned. Freedom 7 turned heat-shield-forward and dropped into the upper atmosphere. The deceleration began -- the g-forces building as the atmosphere thickened and the capsule slowed from Mach 6 to subsonic speed. Shepard experienced a maximum of 11.6g during reentry -- eleven and a half times his body weight pressing him into the contoured couch. His vision narrowed. His chest compressed. He continued to report instrument readings. At 11.6g, he was still flying the spacecraft.

The drogue parachute deployed at approximately 6,700 meters altitude, stabilizing the capsule's descent. The main parachute deployed at approximately 3,000 meters -- a sixty-three-foot ring-sail canopy that slowed Freedom 7 to a descent rate of approximately 9 meters per second. Shepard watched the main chute deploy through the periscope. It was fully open, fully inflated, undamaged. "Main chute is reefed, and it looks good," he reported, and then, as it fully blossomed: "Main chute is good."

Freedom 7 splashed down in the Atlantic Ocean at 9:49 AM Eastern Standard Time, approximately 486 kilometers downrange from Cape Canaveral and approximately 8 kilometers from the recovery ship USS Lake Champlain. The flight had lasted fifteen minutes and twenty-eight seconds. A Marine helicopter from the Lake Champlain reached the capsule within minutes. The helicopter lowered a horse collar sling. Shepard grabbed it, was hoisted out of the water, and was flown to the carrier deck. He was standing on the deck of the Lake Champlain at 9:57 AM -- eight minutes after splashdown. He was smiling. He was wet. He was the first American in space.

## The Nation Watches

Forty-five million Americans watched the launch live on television. Unlike Gagarin's flight -- which was announced by the Soviet Union only after Gagarin had landed, eliminating any possibility of public failure -- Shepard's flight was broadcast in real time, with no delay, no editing, no safety net. If the rocket exploded, forty-five million people would watch it explode. If Shepard died, forty-five million people would watch him die. NASA and the Kennedy administration made the deliberate decision to launch in the open. The decision was partly philosophical -- the American space program was civilian, transparent, democratic in a way the Soviet program was not -- and partly strategic. If the flight succeeded, the live broadcast would amplify the triumph. If it failed, concealment would have been impossible anyway, and the attempt to conceal would have been worse than the failure itself.

The flight succeeded. The broadcast showed the rocket rising from the pad, the tracking cameras following it upward, the capcom calls relayed over the television audio, and then the recovery -- the helicopter, the carrier, Shepard stepping onto the deck. The entire country had participated. They had waited through the holds, winced at the delays, held their breath at ignition, and exhaled at splashdown. It was the first time that a spaceflight had been experienced collectively, in real time, by a mass audience. The Soviets had announced achievements after the fact. The Americans had invited the world to watch.

The response was enormous. Shepard was flown to Washington, D.C., where President Kennedy presented him with the NASA Distinguished Service Medal in a Rose Garden ceremony on May 8, three days after the flight. Kennedy, who had barely acknowledged the space program before Gagarin forced his hand, was now standing next to an astronaut in the Rose Garden. The political calculation was transparent and the emotional reality was genuine -- Kennedy was moved by the flight, by the broadcast, by the public response. The nation wanted to go to space. The nation had just gone to space. The question was what to do next.

Shepard received a ticker tape parade in New York City and another in his hometown of Derry, New Hampshire (later renamed Derry-Shepard in his honor). He appeared on magazine covers, gave speeches, testified before Congress. The Mercury Seven had been celebrities since their selection in 1959, but Shepard's flight transformed celebrity into something closer to national mythology. He was the man who had ridden the rocket. He was the man who had said "light this candle." He was the man who had gone up and come back down and told everyone what he saw.

Twenty days after Shepard's flight, on May 25, 1961, President Kennedy addressed a joint session of Congress and spoke the words that defined the decade: "I believe that this nation should commit itself to achieving the goal, before this decade is out, of landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth." The Moon speech. The commitment. The starting gun for Apollo. Kennedy made the speech twenty days after a fifteen-minute suborbital hop -- not after an orbital flight, not after a demonstration of any of the capabilities that a lunar mission would require, but after a flight that proved only that an American could ride a rocket to the edge of space and come back alive. Shepard's fifteen minutes gave Kennedy the political foundation to commit the nation to the Moon. The flight was a proof of concept. The concept it proved was not technical. It was political. It proved that the American people would support the venture, that they would watch, that they would care, that the investment of national treasure and national prestige was viable. Shepard's fifteen minutes were worth twenty billion Apollo dollars.

## Freedom 7

Shepard named his capsule Freedom 7. The Mercury astronauts were permitted to name their spacecraft, and each of the six who flew chose a name with a "7" suffix -- Freedom 7, Liberty Bell 7, Friendship 7, Aurora 7, Sigma 7, Faith 7 -- honoring all seven members of the Mercury team. The "7" was Glenn's idea, but Shepard was the first to use it, and the tradition was set.

The name "Freedom" was Shepard's choice, and it was not casual. He considered other names. He rejected them. He chose "Freedom" because the word meant something specific to him in the context of 1961. The Cold War was not a metaphor. It was a strategic reality in which two global powers organized along fundamentally different principles -- individual liberty versus collective authority -- competed for influence, allies, and the future direction of human civilization. Spaceflight was an arena of that competition. Gagarin flew for the Soviet Union. Shepard flew for the United States. The capsule's name was a statement about what the United States represented in the competition: not just technological capability, not just military power, but the idea of individual freedom as a foundational principle of governance and society.

The word also carried a personal meaning that Shepard did not discuss publicly. Freedom, for a test pilot, is the condition of flight -- the moment when the wheels leave the runway and the aircraft is free of the ground, when the pilot is operating in an environment where the constraints are physical rather than bureaucratic, where competence matters more than rank, where the machine either works or it doesn't and the pilot either flies it or he doesn't. Shepard had spent years in that environment. He had tested aircraft that the Navy was not certain would fly. He had accepted risks that were quantifiable and personal. He had earned the right to name his vehicle, and he named it for the condition he valued most.

## The Long Grounding

In 1963, two years after his Mercury flight, Alan Shepard was diagnosed with Meniere's disease -- a condition of the inner ear that causes episodes of vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus, and a sensation of pressure in the affected ear. For a pilot, Meniere's disease is disqualifying. The vestibular system -- the inner ear's balance mechanism -- is the pilot's primary spatial orientation instrument. When the vestibular system malfunctions, the pilot cannot distinguish up from down, level from banked, climbing from descending. The instruments can provide this information, but a pilot with active Meniere's disease cannot trust his own sense of orientation, and the conflict between what his body tells him and what the instruments tell him can be incapacitating. Shepard was grounded. He was removed from flight status and reassigned to administrative duties as Chief of the Astronaut Office.

Shepard spent five years grounded -- 1963 to 1968 -- watching other astronauts fly the missions he had expected to fly. He ran the astronaut office, managed crew assignments, served as a capcom during missions, and performed the administrative work that someone had to do. He did it well. He was an effective administrator -- organized, decisive, and intolerant of incompetence in the same way he had been intolerant of it as a pilot. But he was not flying. The man who had been first in space was watching from the ground.

In 1968, Shepard underwent an experimental surgical procedure to correct his Meniere's disease. The surgery -- an endolymphatic shunt, performed by Dr. William House in Los Angeles -- was designed to drain excess fluid from the inner ear, relieving the pressure that caused the vertigo episodes. The procedure was new, its long-term outcomes uncertain, its success rate unclear. Shepard had the surgery performed under an assumed name -- Victor Poulos -- to keep it out of the press and away from NASA's medical bureaucracy until he knew whether it worked. It worked. The vertigo stopped. The hearing stabilized. In May 1969, Shepard was restored to full flight status. He was forty-six years old.

## Apollo 14

Shepard lobbied for and received command of Apollo 14 -- the third lunar landing mission, launched January 31, 1971. Ten years after his fifteen-minute suborbital flight in Freedom 7, Shepard stood on the surface of the Moon.

Apollo 14 landed in the Fra Mauro highlands, targeting the geological formations that Apollo 13 had been unable to reach when its mission was aborted. Shepard and lunar module pilot Edgar Mitchell spent 33 hours on the lunar surface, conducting two EVAs totaling 9 hours and 17 minutes. They collected 42.8 kilograms of lunar samples and deployed the Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package. They attempted to reach Cone Crater, a 340-meter-wide impact crater whose rim promised access to deep subsurface material, but the terrain was more difficult than anticipated -- rolling, hilly, covered in soft regolith with no clear landmarks -- and they came within approximately 20 meters of the rim without realizing how close they were before mission control told them to turn back.

And then Shepard hit golf balls. He had brought a modified six-iron head that attached to the handle of a lunar sample collection tool, and two golf balls. On live television, broadcast to Earth, standing on the surface of the Moon in a pressure suit that limited his mobility to a one-handed swing, Alan Shepard hit two golf balls. The first shot went into a nearby crater. The second, by Shepard's estimate, traveled "miles and miles and miles." The actual distance was probably 200 to 400 yards. It did not matter. What mattered was that a man who had been grounded for five years by an inner ear disorder, who had been the first American in space and then had been forced to watch from the ground while his colleagues flew to the Moon, had found his way back to flight status, had commanded a lunar mission, and had stood on the Moon and hit golf balls -- not because the science required it, not because the mission plan called for it, but because he could. Because he was Alan Shepard, and Alan Shepard did what Alan Shepard wanted to do, and what he wanted to do on the Moon was play golf. It was the most Shepard act in the history of the space program.

Shepard was the only Mercury astronaut to walk on the Moon. Glenn would return to space in 1998 aboard the Space Shuttle Discovery at the age of 77, but he did not visit the Moon. Grissom died in the Apollo 1 fire on January 27, 1967. Cooper, Schirra, Carpenter, and Slayton never flew lunar missions. Shepard alone made the full journey -- from a fifteen-minute suborbital arc in a one-ton capsule to a nine-hour walk on the lunar surface in a mission that required a three-stage Saturn V and a navigation computer that fit in a spacecraft the size of two minivans stacked on top of each other. The distance between Freedom 7 and Apollo 14 -- technically, philosophically, and biographically -- is the distance between the first step and the destination. Shepard took both.

Alan Bartlett Shepard Jr. died on July 21, 1998, in Monterey, California, at the age of 74. He had been an admiral, an astronaut, a millionaire (real estate and banking investments made him one of the wealthiest astronauts), a golfer, and the first American in space. His ashes were spread into the Pacific Ocean.

## The Kierkegaard Resonance

Soren Aabye Kierkegaard was born on May 5, 1813, in Copenhagen, Denmark -- the same calendar date, 148 years earlier, that Alan Shepard launched from Cape Canaveral. Kierkegaard died on November 11, 1855, at the age of 42, in Copenhagen, after collapsing on the street and spending six weeks in a hospital. He is generally regarded as the first existentialist philosopher, though he would not have used that term and would have resisted the company it later placed him in.

Kierkegaard's central philosophical concern was the nature of individual choice under conditions of uncertainty. He argued that the most important human decisions -- the decisions that define who a person is rather than merely what a person does -- cannot be resolved by rational calculation. They are what he called "leaps" -- acts of commitment that go beyond what evidence and reason can justify, acts in which the individual chooses in the absence of certainty and accepts the consequences of that choice. The "leap of faith" -- Kierkegaard's most famous concept, though the exact phrase does not appear in his works -- is the paradigmatic case: the decision to believe in something that cannot be proven, to commit to a path that cannot be guaranteed, to act when the outcome is unknown and unknowable.

Shepard's flight was a leap in Kierkegaard's sense. The Redstone rocket had a track record that included the four-inch flight. The Mercury capsule had a track record that included a chimpanzee who exceeded the planned g-forces. The Soviet Union had flown a man to orbit three weeks earlier, raising the stakes of failure -- if Shepard died, the United States would have lost both the race and a man. The decision to sit in the capsule, to wait through the holds, to ride the rocket -- this was not a calculated risk in the actuarial sense. It was an act of commitment. Shepard did not know the rocket would work. He believed it would work. He chose to act on that belief. The gap between evidence and action -- the space that Kierkegaard identified as the essential feature of all genuine commitment -- was fifteen minutes and twenty-eight seconds wide, and Shepard crossed it at Mach 6.8.

Kierkegaard also wrote extensively about anxiety -- Angest in Danish, often translated as "dread." Anxiety, in Kierkegaard's analysis, is not fear of a specific thing. Fear has an object: you fear the snake, the fall, the explosion. Anxiety has no object. Anxiety is the awareness of possibility itself -- the vertigo that comes from looking over the edge and realizing that you could jump, that you are free to jump, that nothing prevents you from jumping except your own choice. Kierkegaard called anxiety "the dizziness of freedom." It is the experience of standing at a point of genuine choice, where multiple futures are possible and none is determined, and feeling the weight of that openness.

Shepard, lying on his back in Freedom 7 during the four-hour-plus wait, looking at the instrument panel two feet from his face, urinating in his suit because the engineers had not planned for the possibility that a human being might have a bladder -- Shepard was in a state that Kierkegaard would have recognized instantly. He was at the boundary. Multiple futures were real. The rocket could fly. The rocket could explode. He could become the first American in space. He could become the first American to die in a space launch on live television. The dizziness of freedom is the condition of the astronaut on the pad before ignition: everything is still possible, nothing is yet determined, and the only way forward is to choose.

"Light this candle." That was Shepard's response to the anxiety of the boundary. Not a philosophical treatise. Not a prayer. Not a calculated assessment of risk probabilities. An imperative sentence directed at the engineers: stop deliberating and act. Fix the problem and commit. Kierkegaard argued that deliberation, taken to its extreme, becomes a form of paralysis -- the endless weighing of options that prevents any option from being chosen. The antidote to deliberation paralysis is the leap: the moment when the individual stops calculating and starts acting, when the choice is made not because all the data is in but because the moment for choosing has arrived and to delay further is to choose not to choose, which is itself a choice and usually the worst one. Shepard's "light this candle" is the colloquial form of Kierkegaard's leap. It is the test pilot's version of the existential commitment: enough analysis, enough delay, enough waiting for certainty that will never arrive. Ignite the engine. Accept the outcome. Go.

### Primary Sources

- **Swenson, Loyd S. Jr.; Grimwood, James M.; and Alexander, Charles C. "This New Ocean: A History of Project Mercury." NASA Special Publication SP-4201, NASA History Office, 1966.** The official NASA history of Project Mercury, providing detailed accounts of the MR-3 mission including Shepard's selection, countdown delays, flight timeline, manual control tests, recovery, and the political path from MR-3 to Kennedy's Moon speech.
- **"Results of the First United States Manned Suborbital Space Flight." NASA / Manned Spacecraft Center, June 6, 1961.** The primary MR-3 mission report, containing complete flight data including trajectory parameters, capsule performance, manual attitude control test results, biomedical telemetry, communications logs, and recovery operations. Shepard's physiological data under 11.6g reentry is documented here.
- **Grimwood, James M. "Project Mercury: A Chronology." NASA Special Publication SP-4001, NASA History Office, 1963.** Day-by-day chronology documenting the MR-3 launch scrubs, countdown holds, flight events, and the 20-day interval between Shepard's flight and Kennedy's Moon speech to Congress.

### Secondary Sources

- **Shepard, Alan; and Slayton, Deke. "Moon Shot: The Inside Story of America's Race to the Moon." Turner Publishing, 1994.** Shepard's own account of his Mercury selection, the competition with Glenn and Grissom, the launch day events including the urination incident and the "light this candle" quote, and his later career through Apollo 14 and the lunar golf shots.
- **Wolfe, Tom. "The Right Stuff." Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1979.** The defining literary account of the Mercury program, covering the astronaut selection process, the competitive dynamics among the Mercury Seven, Shepard's personality and ambition, the launch morning, and the cultural significance of the flight.

Crown Publishers, 2004.** The definitive biography of Shepard, drawing on extensive interviews with family, colleagues, and fellow astronauts, covering his childhood, Navy career, Mercury selection, MR-3 flight, Meniere's disease, surgical recovery, and Apollo 14 command.
