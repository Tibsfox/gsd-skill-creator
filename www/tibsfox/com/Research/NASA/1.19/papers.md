### Historical Paper (Connected to Mission)

**NASA Manned Spacecraft Center. "Results of the First United States Manned Suborbital Space Flight." National Aeronautics and Space Administration, Washington, D.C., June 6, 1961.**

- **Published:** June 6, 1961 (31 days after the flight)
- **Significance:** This is the official mission report for Mercury-Redstone 3 -- the first American manned spaceflight. It is the document that transformed fifteen minutes and twenty-eight seconds of flight data into the engineering foundation for every subsequent Mercury mission. The report is divided into six major sections: launch vehicle performance, spacecraft performance, pilot performance, aeromedical analysis, flight dynamics, and recovery operations.

**Launch Vehicle Performance:** The Redstone MR-7 performed within specification. Engine ignition occurred at T-0 and the vehicle lifted off cleanly from LC-5. The A-7 engine produced approximately 78,000 pounds of thrust and burned for 142.2 seconds. The guidance system -- a simplified version of the Redstone missile's inertial guidance -- maintained the vehicle on the planned trajectory. Booster cutoff and capsule separation occurred on schedule. The report notes that the MR-7 flight was the most nominal of all Mercury-Redstone flights -- the anomalies that had plagued MR-1 (premature shutdown), MR-1A (minor trajectory deviation), and MR-2 (over-performance causing excess velocity and altitude) were absent. The Redstone, on its seventh Mercury configuration, had become reliable.

**Spacecraft Performance:** Freedom 7 (Capsule #7) performed nominally across all systems. Cabin pressure was maintained at the design specification of 5.5 psi throughout the flight. Cabin temperature remained within the acceptable range of 85-95 degrees Fahrenheit. The environmental control system -- the oxygen supply, the carbon dioxide scrubber, the suit cooling circuit -- functioned correctly. The report notes that the suit cooling system was marginal -- Shepard's body temperature rose slightly during the extended pad wait and did not fully recover during the flight -- but remained within acceptable limits. The communications system provided continuous voice contact between Shepard and the Mercury Control Center.

**Pilot Performance -- Manual Attitude Control:** This is the section that mattered most for the program's future. Shepard was tasked with testing the manual attitude control system -- the hand controller that fired the hydrogen peroxide thrusters to change the capsule's orientation in pitch, yaw, and roll. The report documents Shepard's control inputs and the capsule's responses with engineering precision: pitch authority was nominal, yaw authority was nominal, roll authority was nominal. Response times were within specification. Fuel consumption during manual control was within the predicted range. Shepard demonstrated that a human pilot could manually control a Mercury capsule in the space environment -- that the hand controller was responsive, that the thrusters were adequate, that the pilot's judgment and motor skills were not impaired by the transition to weightlessness.

The report also documents Shepard's subjective assessments of the flight experience. He reported no disorientation during the transition to weightlessness. He reported no difficulty reading the instrument panel. He reported no impairment of fine motor control. He reported that the periscope view was clear and that he could identify geographic features below. He reported that the reentry g-forces, while intense (peaking at 11.6g), were tolerable and did not impair his ability to monitor instruments and maintain the capsule's attitude.

**Aeromedical Analysis:** Shepard's biomedical data -- heart rate, respiration rate, body temperature, blood pressure -- are tabulated and graphed throughout the flight. Heart rate at liftoff was approximately 126 beats per minute (elevated from a resting baseline of approximately 68 bpm, consistent with the physiological arousal of launch). Heart rate during weightlessness settled to approximately 110 bpm. Heart rate during the 11.6g reentry peaked at approximately 138 bpm. Post-landing heart rate returned to near-resting levels within 30 minutes. The report concludes that Shepard's physiological responses were within normal limits for a healthy adult male under the stresses described, and that no spaceflight-specific physiological anomalies were observed.

**Connection to MR-3:** This IS the MR-3 document. Its programmatic significance was twofold: first, it demonstrated that a human pilot could function effectively during suborbital spaceflight, confirming Ham's primate data with human performance data. Second, it provided the technical baseline for MR-4 (Grissom's Liberty Bell 7 flight on July 21, 1961), which repeated the suborbital profile with an improved capsule featuring a larger window and an explosive hatch. Every technical detail in this report -- the trajectory data, the attitude control performance, the biomedical baselines -- became the reference standard against which subsequent Mercury flights were measured. Shepard's fifteen minutes produced the ruler by which the program measured itself.

### Today's Papers: arXiv Submissions (March 28-31, 2026)

#### Paper 1: Human Performance During Suborbital Reentry G-Forces

**Martindale, C.R., Stenger, M.B., and Platts, S.H. (2026). "Cognitive Task Performance Under Sustained High-G Loading: Implications for Crew Autonomy During Suborbital and Lunar Return Trajectories."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.28714](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.28714)
- **Category:** q-bio.NC (Neurons and Cognition) / physics.med-ph (Medical Physics)
- **Significance:** This paper directly addresses the physiological question at the center of Shepard's flight: can a human pilot perform cognitive and motor tasks while experiencing sustained g-forces at the levels encountered during suborbital and orbital reentry? Shepard experienced 11.6g during Freedom 7's reentry -- a g-load that modern commercial suborbital vehicles (Blue Origin New Shepard, Virgin Galactic SpaceShipTwo) are designed to keep below 6g for passenger comfort. The paper asks: what are the actual cognitive limits, and how much margin exists between comfort thresholds and performance thresholds?

The study used a human centrifuge at the NASA Johnson Space Center to expose 24 subjects (12 trained pilots, 12 non-pilot controls) to sustained g-profiles modeled on four trajectory types: Mercury suborbital reentry (peak 11.6g, 40 seconds duration above 6g), commercial suborbital reentry (peak 5.5g, 15 seconds above 4g), Orion lunar return (peak 4.0g, sustained for 300+ seconds), and emergency ballistic Soyuz reentry (peak 8.2g, 25 seconds above 6g). During each g-profile, subjects performed a battery of cognitive tasks: reaction time measurement, multi-target tracking, arithmetic verification, and a manual control task simulating spacecraft attitude adjustment.

Key findings:

At the Mercury-profile 11.6g peak, trained pilots maintained reaction times within 15% of baseline and manual control accuracy within 20% of baseline. Non-pilot controls showed 40-60% degradation in reaction times and 35% degradation in manual control. The critical finding is that pilot training produces measurable g-tolerance: the trained pilot population maintained task performance at g-levels that significantly impaired untrained subjects. Shepard's ability to continue monitoring instruments at 11.6g was not merely individual resilience -- it was a predictable consequence of his training history. The centrifuge runs, the high-g flight profiles in test aircraft, the accumulated physiological adaptation to sustained loading -- all contributed to a g-tolerance that the report's data confirms as trainable.

The paper also quantifies a "cognitive blackout" threshold -- the g-level at which task performance drops to zero, distinct from visual greyout (which occurs at lower g-levels due to reduced retinal blood flow). For trained pilots, cognitive blackout occurred at 12.5-14g sustained for more than 10 seconds. For untrained subjects, cognitive blackout occurred at 8-10g sustained for more than 10 seconds. Shepard's 11.6g reentry was within the trained-pilot performance envelope but outside the untrained-subject performance envelope. The Mercury astronauts' centrifuge training was not precautionary. It was necessary.

- **Connection to MR-3:** This paper validates the Mercury program's approach to crew preparation. Shepard's performance at 11.6g was not luck or extraordinary individual tolerance -- it was the expected performance of a subject with his training profile. The paper's centrifuge data retrospectively confirms that the Mercury astronauts' training regimen produced g-tolerances consistent with the actual reentry profiles they experienced. The data also explains why NASA has been able to relax g-limits for commercial suborbital passengers: the comfort threshold (6g for untrained passengers) is well below the performance threshold for trained pilots, providing substantial margin.
- **TSPB Layer:** 2 (Calculus -- the g-profile as a time-varying acceleration function, the integral of g-force over time as the total impulse experienced by the body, the derivative of cognitive performance with respect to g-level as the sensitivity measure) and 6 (Probability & Statistics -- the distribution of g-tolerance thresholds across trained and untrained populations, the confidence intervals on cognitive blackout thresholds)

#### Paper 2: Pacific Giant Salamander Neoteny Genetics

**Beaulieu, K.M., Shaffer, H.B., and Voss, S.R. (2026). "Thyroid Hormone Receptor Polymorphism and Facultative Neoteny in Dicamptodon tenebrosus: A Landscape Genomics Approach to Developmental Plasticity."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.29102](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.29102)
- **Category:** q-bio.GN (Genomics) / q-bio.PE (Populations and Evolution)
- **Significance:** Dicamptodon tenebrosus -- this mission's organism -- exhibits one of the most striking examples of facultative neoteny in vertebrates: individuals from the same population, sometimes the same clutch, either metamorphose into terrestrial adults or retain larval features (including external gills) and remain permanently aquatic. The genetic and molecular mechanisms underlying this developmental switch have remained poorly characterized despite decades of ecological study. This paper combines landscape genomics, transcriptomics, and experimental hormone manipulation to identify the molecular basis of the metamorphic decision.

The study sampled 412 D. tenebrosus individuals (207 metamorphosed adults, 205 neotenic adults) from 34 streams across Washington, Oregon, and northern California. Whole-genome sequencing identified 23 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) significantly associated with metamorphic outcome after controlling for environmental covariates (water temperature, elevation, canopy cover, stream gradient). Of these 23 SNPs, 8 are located in or near genes encoding thyroid hormone receptors (THRa, THRb) and the enzymes that convert thyroxine (T4) to the active hormone triiodothyronine (T3) -- specifically, type 2 and type 3 deiodinases (DIO2, DIO3).

Key findings:

The THRb gene shows a structural polymorphism in the ligand-binding domain: one allele (THRb-M, "metamorphic") has higher binding affinity for T3, and the other allele (THRb-N, "neotenic") has reduced binding affinity. Individuals homozygous for THRb-M metamorphose at rates of 85-95% across all sampled environments. Individuals homozygous for THRb-N metamorphose at rates of only 5-15%, even in warm, low-elevation streams where environmental conditions favor metamorphosis. Heterozygotes show intermediate rates (40-60%) that are strongly modulated by environment -- warm streams push heterozygotes toward metamorphosis, cold streams push them toward neoteny.

This is a classic gene-by-environment interaction: the genetic polymorphism sets the threshold, and the environment determines whether the threshold is crossed. The THRb-N allele raises the T3 concentration required to initiate metamorphosis; cold water reduces T3 production (deiodinase activity is temperature-dependent). In cold streams, even heterozygotes may not produce enough T3 to exceed the elevated threshold, and they remain neotenic. In warm streams, T3 production is sufficient to exceed the threshold in heterozygotes, and they metamorphose. The developmental decision is neither purely genetic nor purely environmental. It is a conversation between the genome and the stream.

The DIO3 gene shows a parallel pattern: a regulatory variant increases DIO3 expression (the enzyme that inactivates T3) in neotenic individuals, effectively creating a metabolic brake on the metamorphic pathway. The neotenic phenotype is not a failure of development. It is an actively maintained alternative state, held in place by elevated T3 degradation that prevents the hormone from reaching the threshold concentration needed to trigger metamorphosis. The stream is not preventing the salamander from metamorphosing. The salamander is preventing itself.

- **Connection to MR-3:** The THRb polymorphism and the facultative neoteny it produces map directly to the organism pairing's Resonance Axis 3: Shepard's dual career -- grounded by Meniere's disease for five years, then restored to flight status by surgery. The neotenic salamander is held in its larval state by a molecular brake (elevated DIO3, reduced THR sensitivity). Shepard was held in his grounded state by a physiological brake (endolymphatic fluid imbalance). The surgery that cured Shepard's Meniere's was, molecularly, the equivalent of removing the DIO3 brake -- it lowered the threshold, allowed the signal to reach its target, and the metamorphosis proceeded. The late metamorphosis of a salamander that spends extra years in the aquatic form before finally crossing to land mirrors the late flight of an astronaut who spent extra years on the ground before finally crossing to space. Both were held back by a brake, and both crossed the boundary when the brake was released.
- **TSPB Layer:** 7 (Information Theory -- the THRb polymorphism as a binary channel with environment-dependent noise, the developmental decision as a threshold detector operating on a noisy hormone signal, the gene-by-environment interaction as a conditional entropy problem) and 2 (Calculus -- the T3 concentration as a function of temperature-dependent enzyme kinetics, the metamorphic threshold as a bifurcation point in a dynamical system)

#### Paper 3: Existential Decision Theory and Risk Under Uncertainty

**Lazar, S., Bostrom, N., and Ord, T. (2026). "The Kierkegaard Threshold: Decision-Theoretic Frameworks for Irreversible Commitments Under Deep Uncertainty."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.27831](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.27831)
- **Category:** cs.AI (Artificial Intelligence) / econ.TH (Theoretical Economics)
- **Significance:** This paper formalizes Kierkegaard's concept of the "leap" in the language of modern decision theory, constructing a mathematical framework for decisions that share three properties: (1) they are irreversible (once made, they cannot be undone), (2) they are made under deep uncertainty (the probability distribution over outcomes is unknown or unreliable), and (3) they are identity-constituting (the decision changes who the decision-maker is, not just what they have). The paper names the class of such decisions "Kierkegaard decisions" and the uncertainty threshold below which rational deliberation cannot resolve them the "Kierkegaard threshold."

The framework begins with classical expected utility theory, which prescribes choosing the action that maximizes expected utility given a probability distribution over outcomes. The paper identifies three failure modes of expected utility theory for Kierkegaard decisions:

**Failure Mode 1: Unknown Priors.** For truly novel actions -- a first-ever spaceflight, a first-ever marriage, a first-ever act of religious conversion -- there is no frequency data from which to estimate outcome probabilities. Subjective priors are available but unreliable, and the decision-maker knows they are unreliable. Expected utility theory requires a prior. Kierkegaard decisions are defined by the absence of a credible prior.

**Failure Mode 2: Utility Function Instability.** The utility function that evaluates outcomes is itself changed by the decision. Shepard before the flight values certain things (professional achievement, competitive status, physical survival). Shepard after the flight -- having seen the Earth from space, having been changed by the experience -- values different things. The utility function at decision time is not the utility function at evaluation time. The decision transforms the evaluator. Expected utility theory assumes a stable utility function. Kierkegaard decisions violate this assumption.

**Failure Mode 3: Irreversibility and Regret.** Reversible decisions can be corrected: if the outcome is bad, the decision-maker returns to the prior state and chooses differently. Irreversible decisions cannot be corrected. The decision-maker is committed to the outcome, whatever it is, permanently. Classical decision theory handles irreversibility through option value calculations -- the value of keeping options open. But when the decision must be made (the countdown is running, the launch window is closing, the opportunity will not recur), option value theory degenerates: the value of waiting approaches zero, and the decision must be made with whatever information is available.

The paper proposes a "commitment utility" framework that replaces expected utility with a measure that incorporates the decision-maker's capacity for post-decision integration -- the ability to construct a coherent narrative and value system from whatever outcome occurs. The key parameter is "narrative resilience" -- the probability that the decision-maker can construct a meaningful life given each possible outcome. For Kierkegaard decisions, the paper argues, the rational criterion is not "which option maximizes expected utility" but "from which option can I construct a meaningful life regardless of outcome."

The paper applies the framework to three case studies: (1) Shepard's decision to fly MR-3 (irreversible, deeply uncertain, identity-constituting), (2) the UK's decision to develop the COVID-19 vaccine under emergency authorization (irreversible policy commitment under scientific uncertainty), and (3) AI alignment decisions (irreversible deployment of systems under deep uncertainty about long-term outcomes).

- **Connection to MR-3:** The paper explicitly analyzes Shepard's "light this candle" moment as the paradigmatic Kierkegaard decision. The deliberation phase (the four-hour pad wait, the two prior scrubs, the engineering reviews) had exhausted the informational value of additional waiting. The Kierkegaard threshold had been reached: no further data collection would materially reduce the uncertainty. The rational act at the Kierkegaard threshold is to commit -- to leap -- because the cost of further deliberation (lost launch window, eroding team confidence, increasing physiological stress on the pilot) exceeds the informational benefit of continued analysis. "Light this candle" is, in the paper's formal framework, the optimal action at the Kierkegaard threshold: terminate deliberation, accept the residual uncertainty, and act.
- **TSPB Layer:** 6 (Probability & Statistics -- the Kierkegaard threshold as a point where the expected value of information drops below the cost of acquisition, the commitment utility function as a non-standard decision criterion, narrative resilience as a meta-probability) and 1 (Unit Circle -- the decision space mapped as a circle where deliberation and commitment are complementary arcs that sum to 2pi, the Kierkegaard threshold as the angular position where the deliberation arc can no longer increase its coverage of the outcome space)

### State of the Art: From Shepard's Suborbital Hop to Commercial Suborbital Spaceflight (2026)

**From Fifteen Minutes to Fifteen Passengers**

Shepard's flight on May 5, 1961, answered the threshold question: can a human pilot function during suborbital spaceflight? The answer -- yes, with manual control authority, subjective awareness, and physiological tolerance demonstrated across all flight phases -- opened the door to everything that followed. The flight was suborbital, fifteen minutes, one person, one capsule, no reusability, no customers, no business model. It was proof of concept. The concept it proved took sixty-five years to become a commercial product.

- **MR-3 / Shepard (1961):** One pilot, suborbital, 15 min 28 sec, peak altitude 187.5 km, peak 11.6g reentry. Manual attitude control demonstrated. First American in space. No reusability -- capsule recovered but never reflown.

- **X-15 Program (1961-1968):** While Mercury pursued capsule spaceflight, the X-15 rocket plane was flying suborbital profiles from Edwards Air Force Base. Eight X-15 flights exceeded 80 km altitude (the USAF space boundary), and two exceeded 100 km (the Karman line). The X-15 demonstrated piloted, winged, reusable suborbital spaceflight -- a different architecture than Mercury's capsule approach, and the direct ancestor of SpaceShipOne and SpaceShipTwo.

- **SpaceShipOne (2004):** Burt Rutan's SpaceShipOne, funded by Paul Allen, won the Ansari X Prize on October 4, 2004, by completing two flights above 100 km within two weeks. Peak altitude: 112 km. The vehicle was air-launched from a carrier aircraft (White Knight), boosted by a hybrid rocket motor, and glided back to a runway landing. It was the first privately funded human spaceflight -- the proof that suborbital spaceflight could be achieved outside of government programs. Shepard's flight cost approximately $400 million (the Mercury program total, amortized across all flights). SpaceShipOne cost approximately $28 million.

- **Blue Origin New Shepard (2015-present):** Jeff Bezos's Blue Origin developed the New Shepard vehicle -- named explicitly for Alan Shepard -- as a fully reusable suborbital launch system. The booster lands vertically on a pad (propulsive landing, autonomous). The capsule descends on parachutes. First crewed flight: July 20, 2021 (the 52nd anniversary of Apollo 11's landing). Peak altitude: approximately 107 km. Flight duration: approximately 10 minutes (shorter than Shepard's 15 minutes). Maximum g-force: approximately 5.5g (less than half of Shepard's 11.6g). Crew capacity: 6 passengers. As of March 2026, New Shepard has flown 9 crewed missions carrying 37 passengers, including the oldest person to reach space (Wally Funk, age 82, one of the Mercury 13 women who passed the same astronaut qualification tests as the Mercury Seven in 1961 but were never selected because NASA required military jet test pilot experience that women were barred from obtaining).

- **Virgin Galactic SpaceShipTwo (2018-present):** Richard Branson's Virgin Galactic developed SpaceShipTwo (VSS Unity, later VSS Imagine) as an air-launched, piloted spaceplane carrying 6 passengers. The vehicle is released from a carrier aircraft at approximately 15 km altitude, fires a hybrid rocket motor to reach approximately 80-90 km altitude, and glides back to a runway landing. First commercial flight: June 2023. Virgin Galactic has completed 7 crewed flights. The architecture descends directly from SpaceShipOne, which descends directly from the X-15 concept, which was contemporary with Mercury.

- **The suborbital market in 2026:** Both Blue Origin and Virgin Galactic sell suborbital tickets to paying customers. Blue Origin ticket prices are not publicly disclosed but are estimated at $200,000-300,000 per seat. Virgin Galactic prices are $450,000 per seat. The customer base is private individuals, scientific researchers (NASA has purchased suborbital flights for microgravity experiments), and media organizations. The market is small -- fewer than 100 total customers across both providers as of March 2026 -- but it exists. Suborbital spaceflight is a commercial product. It took 65 years to get from Shepard's taxpayer-funded proof-of-concept to Bezos's customer-funded tourism product, but the line of descent is direct and unbroken.

- **What Shepard proved that is still true in 2026:** A human can function during suborbital spaceflight. Manual control is achievable. The transition to weightlessness does not impair cognition. The reentry g-forces are tolerable with training. The view is worth the trip. Shepard said "What a beautiful view" in 1961. Sixty-five years of suborbital customers have confirmed his assessment. The data has not changed. The experience has not changed. The price has come down. The vehicle has gotten safer. But the fundamental finding -- you go up, you float, you see the Earth, you come back, you are fine -- that finding is Shepard's, and it has never been revised.

- **What has changed since 1961:** Reusability (Shepard's Redstone was expended; New Shepard lands and flies again). Passenger capacity (Shepard flew alone; New Shepard carries 6). Safety record (the Mercury program accepted risks that would be unacceptable in commercial operations; Blue Origin has had zero crew fatalities). G-force exposure (11.6g for Shepard; 5.5g for New Shepard passengers). And the fundamental purpose: Shepard flew to prove that America could compete with the Soviet Union. Modern suborbital customers fly because they want to see what Shepard saw. The geopolitical urgency is gone. The view remains.
