## Department Deposits

### Aerospace Engineering (Primary)

**Wing:** Suborbital Trajectory Design, Manual Spacecraft Control, Launch Vehicle Selection, Mercury-Redstone Integration
**Concept:** The engineering decision to fly a suborbital mission on a Redstone booster before attempting orbital flight on an Atlas -- choosing a simpler, more reliable vehicle to validate the spacecraft and the pilot before accepting the risks of the more powerful but less reliable Atlas

**Deposit:** The selection of the Redstone for America's first human spaceflight was not a compromise -- it was a deliberate engineering decision. The Redstone was an Army ballistic missile developed at the Redstone Arsenal in Huntsville, Alabama, under the direction of Wernher von Braun's team. It was derived from the German V-2 rocket, improved with American engineering: a single Rocketdyne A-7 engine burning ethanol and liquid oxygen, producing 78,000 pounds of thrust for approximately 142 seconds. The vehicle had an excellent reliability record -- by 1961, more than 30 Redstones had been launched successfully, and the booster-failure rate was below 5%.

The Atlas D, by contrast, was a far more powerful vehicle -- capable of delivering the Mercury capsule to orbital velocity (~7,800 m/s versus the Redstone's ~2,300 m/s). But the Atlas was also far less reliable. Through 1961, the Atlas had suffered multiple catastrophic launch failures, including the total loss of Mercury-Atlas 1 in July 1960 (structural failure at max-Q). The Atlas's "balloon tank" design -- stainless steel skin so thin (0.01 inches, 0.254 mm) that the tanks had to be pressurized at all times to prevent collapse -- made it structurally fragile in ways the Redstone was not.

NASA's decision: fly the pilot first on the reliable Redstone (suborbital, limited data but low risk), then fly the pilot on the powerful Atlas (orbital, maximum data but higher risk). This is incremental qualification applied to the launch vehicle: validate the capsule and crew procedures on the easier platform before accepting the harder one. The Redstone could not reach orbit -- it did not have enough delta-v. But it could reach space (above 100 km), provide five minutes of weightlessness, and return the pilot safely. That was enough to answer the critical question: can a human function as a spacecraft pilot in the space environment?

The Redstone MR-7 that launched Freedom 7 was extensively modified from its ballistic missile configuration. The military Redstone had an inertial guidance system and a nuclear warhead; the Mercury-Redstone had a simplified guidance system (it only needed to go up and come down in the right place, not hit a target), an abort sensing system (to trigger the escape tower if the booster malfunctioned), and an adapter section to mate with the Mercury capsule. The MR-7 designation meant it was the seventh vehicle in the Mercury-Redstone series. MR-1 (November 1960) famously rose 4 inches off the pad and settled back down when the umbilical plug disconnected prematurely, triggering the abort system. MR-1A (December 1960) was the successful repeat. MR-2 (January 1961) carried Ham the chimpanzee on an off-nominal trajectory. MR-BD (March 1961) was a booster development test to verify the fixes for the MR-2 anomalies. MR-3 was the first with a human pilot.

The manual control system installed in Freedom 7 was the astronauts' victory in the "spam in a can" debate. The original Mercury design gave the astronaut no manual control authority -- the spacecraft would fly itself, and the astronaut would ride along. The Mercury 7 astronauts, all experienced test pilots, argued strenuously that the pilot needed to be able to fly the spacecraft. Their argument was both practical (the automatic system might fail, and a pilot could serve as backup) and philosophical (test pilots evaluate vehicles, and evaluation requires control authority). The compromise was a fly-by-wire manual control mode that allowed the pilot to command the hydrogen peroxide attitude thrusters through the hand controller. Shepard tested this system during his five minutes of weightlessness and proved it worked -- the pilot could control the spacecraft precisely, efficiently, and reliably.

This engineering decision -- reliable booster first, manual control included, incremental qualification -- is the template for all subsequent human spaceflight programs. Gemini flew unmanned missions before crewed ones. Apollo flew Earth-orbit missions before lunar ones. The Space Shuttle flew approach-and-landing tests before orbital flights. SpaceX flew Demo-1 (unmanned Dragon) before Demo-2 (Hurley and Behnken). The pattern originates with Freedom 7: prove the simple case first, add complexity only after the simple case is validated.

### Human Factors (Primary)

**Wing:** Pilot Workload Management, Manual vs Automatic Control, Situational Awareness in Novel Environments, the "Spam in a Can" Debate
**Concept:** The fundamental question of human spaceflight is not "can a human survive in space?" but "can a human be USEFUL in space?" -- whether the complexity of a human pilot adds value that justifies the complexity of keeping a human alive

**Deposit:** Alan Shepard's workload during the 15-minute flight of Freedom 7 was extraordinary by any standard. In addition to the manual control test, he was responsible for: monitoring all spacecraft systems (electrical, environmental, communications, attitude control), reporting observations to Mission Control via voice radio, observing the Earth through the periscope and the window, monitoring his own physiological state, and preparing for critical events (retrofire, reentry, parachute deployment, splashdown). All of this while accelerating at 6.3g during launch, floating in weightlessness for 5 minutes (an environment he had never experienced except in brief parabolic aircraft flights), and decelerating at 11.6g during reentry.

The physiological data from Shepard's flight answered the most fundamental human factors question: does the space environment impair human performance? The answer, from a single data point of one pilot on one 15-minute flight, was: no. Shepard's heart rate peaked at 138 bpm during launch (elevated but not extreme for the stress level), dropped to approximately 108 bpm during weightlessness (near-normal for moderate activity), and peaked again at approximately 134 bpm during reentry. His blood pressure remained within normal ranges throughout. His voice communications were clear and coherent. His manual control inputs were precise and appropriate. He reported no disorientation, no nausea, no spatial confusion, and no visual impairment during weightlessness.

This was enormously important for the program. Prior to Shepard's flight, there was genuine uncertainty about whether weightlessness would impair human cognitive and motor function. The Soviet Vostok program had not released detailed physiological data from Gagarin's flight (April 12, 1961 -- 23 days before Shepard), and the American medical community was divided on the question. Some physicians predicted that the loss of gravitational cues would cause severe disorientation, inability to swallow, inability to focus vision, or even loss of consciousness. Shepard's physiological data, combined with his excellent task performance, refuted these concerns -- at least for a 5-minute exposure. Longer exposures (Glenn's 4.5 hours, Cooper's 34 hours, Gemini's 14 days) would extend the validation, but Shepard provided the first human data point.

The "spam in a can" debate was not resolved by Shepard's flight -- it was shifted. Before MR-3, the question was "should the pilot have manual control?" After MR-3, the question became "how much manual control should the pilot have?" Shepard demonstrated that manual control was feasible and efficient, but his flight was 15 minutes long with a predetermined trajectory. The question of whether a human could manage the complexities of orbital rendezvous, docking, and navigation -- tasks that require continuous decision-making over hours -- would be answered by Gemini. Freedom 7 proved the pilot could fly. Gemini proved the pilot could navigate.

The comparison to Enos (mission 1.18) is instructive. Enos demonstrated that a trained primate could perform a fixed, repetitive task (lever pulling) in the orbital environment. Shepard demonstrated that a human pilot could perform an open-ended, adaptive task (manual spacecraft control) in the space environment. Enos's task required trained reflexes; Shepard's task required judgment, anticipation, and an internal model of the vehicle dynamics. Both demonstrations were necessary: Enos proved the biological payload was functional, Shepard proved the human payload was capable. The distinction is between function (doing a trained task) and capability (doing an untrained task that requires real-time adaptation).

### Herpetology (Primary)

**Wing:** Dicamptodon tenebrosus -- Pacific Giant Salamander, Neoteny, Paedomorphosis, PNW Stream Ecology
**Concept:** The Pacific giant salamander as a model of adaptive life strategy -- an organism that can choose to remain aquatic (neotenous) or transform into a terrestrial adult, depending on environmental conditions, analogous to a spacecraft system that can operate in multiple modes depending on the mission profile

**Deposit:** Dicamptodon tenebrosus -- the Pacific giant salamander -- is the largest terrestrial salamander in North America, reaching lengths of up to 35 cm (14 inches) from snout to tail tip. It inhabits the cold, fast-flowing streams and moist forests of the Pacific Northwest, from British Columbia through Washington, Oregon, and into northern California. Its range closely follows the range of old-growth coniferous forest: where the big trees grow, the big salamanders live. The two are ecologically linked -- the forest provides the shade that keeps stream temperatures cold enough for the salamander's oxygen-rich aquatic habitat, and the forest's root systems stabilize the stream banks that provide the cobble substrate the salamander requires.

The most remarkable feature of Dicamptodon tenebrosus is its facultative neoteny. Most salamanders undergo a fixed developmental program: aquatic larva (with gills) transforms into terrestrial adult (with lungs) through metamorphosis, analogous to a tadpole becoming a frog. Dicamptodon tenebrosus has made this transformation optional. In some populations and environments, larvae transform into terrestrial adults on a normal schedule (18-24 months). In other populations and environments, larvae retain their larval characteristics -- external gills, aquatic lifestyle, laterally compressed tail for swimming -- and become sexually mature without ever transforming. These neotenous (paedomorphic) adults live their entire lives in the stream, breathing through gills, never leaving the water. They can reproduce successfully in this form.

The decision to transform or remain neotenous appears to be environmentally mediated. Populations in streams with abundant aquatic resources and stable water conditions tend to produce more neotenous individuals -- the aquatic habitat is good enough that there is no advantage to transforming. Populations in streams that are less reliable (seasonal flow, higher temperatures, less food) tend to produce more metamorphosing individuals -- the terrestrial habitat offers resources the degraded aquatic habitat cannot. This is not a conscious choice (the salamander does not "decide" to transform) but an endocrine response to environmental cues: thyroid hormone levels, which are influenced by water temperature, photoperiod, and nutritional status, trigger or suppress metamorphosis.

This facultative life strategy is directly analogous to the Redstone-vs-Atlas decision. The Mercury program had two modes available: suborbital (Redstone, aquatic-mode -- simpler, lower risk, limited capability) and orbital (Atlas, terrestrial-mode -- more complex, higher risk, full capability). Like the salamander, the program chose which mode to activate based on environmental conditions: the Atlas was unreliable in 1961 (degraded environment), so the program remained in "suborbital mode" until the environment improved. Once the Atlas became more reliable (MA-4 succeeded unmanned in September 1961), the program "metamorphosed" to orbital capability with MA-5 and MA-6.

The Pacific giant salamander is also a formidable predator. Both neotenous and transformed adults are ambush hunters. The aquatic form sits motionless under cobble in fast-flowing streams, waiting for prey (sculpin, aquatic invertebrates, smaller salamanders, even small mice that approach the stream edge). When prey comes within range, the salamander lunges with explosive speed -- a strike that takes approximately 15-20 milliseconds, powered by specialized trunk musculature that stores elastic energy before the lunge. The strike is ballistic: once initiated, the trajectory cannot be altered. The salamander commits to its strike vector at the moment of launch, exactly as Freedom 7 committed to its trajectory at engine cutoff. Both are ballistic events where the initial conditions (lunge angle and force for the salamander, burnout velocity and angle for the capsule) determine the outcome.

The transformed (terrestrial) adult is equally surprising. It is one of the few salamanders known to vocalize -- producing a low, rattling bark when disturbed, which has earned it the common name "barking salamander" in some regions. It is also one of the few salamanders capable of climbing: transformed adults have been found several meters up in trees and on rock faces, hunting insects and other invertebrates far from their natal stream. The terrestrial form expands the organism's range -- accessing food resources unavailable to the aquatic form -- at the cost of increased vulnerability (no stream to retreat to, exposure to terrestrial predators like garter snakes and raptors).

The Peregrine Falcon (Falco peregrinus, degree 19) is one of those terrestrial predators. In the Pacific Northwest, peregrines nest on cliff faces along rivers and coastlines -- the same drainages where Pacific giant salamanders live. The peregrine hunts birds on the wing (its stoop is adapted for aerial pursuit), but the falcon's habitat overlaps with the salamander's: both species require cold, clean water systems (the peregrine for drinking and bathing, the salamander for its entire life cycle). A degradation of PNW stream quality -- warming, sedimentation, reduced flow -- impacts both the salamander directly (habitat loss) and the peregrine indirectly (reduced prey base in riparian zones, loss of drinking water quality).

### Philosophy -- Kierkegaard (Secondary)

**Wing:** The Leap of Faith, Existential Choice, "Anxiety Is the Dizziness of Freedom," The Concept of Dread, Subjectivity as Truth
**Concept:** Kierkegaard's existential philosophy applied to the moment of committed action -- the instant when the astronaut, the falcon, and the salamander each cross the threshold from possibility to actuality, from choosing to having chosen

**Deposit:** Soren Aabye Kierkegaard was born on May 5, 1813, in Copenhagen, Denmark -- exactly 148 years before Alan Shepard's launch. He is considered the founder of existentialist philosophy, though he never used the term. His central concern was the nature of individual choice: how a person moves from contemplating possibilities to committing to a single course of action, and what that commitment costs.

Kierkegaard's most relevant concept for this mission is "the leap of faith" -- often misunderstood as blind belief, but actually a precise philosophical description of committed action in the face of objective uncertainty. In "The Concept of Anxiety" (1844), Kierkegaard described anxiety as "the dizziness of freedom" -- the vertigo that seizes a person when they stand at the edge of a genuine choice and realize that they are free to choose, that nothing external compels them, that the responsibility for the choice is entirely theirs. The anxiety is not about the danger of the choice but about the FREEDOM to choose: the realization that you COULD step back, and that stepping forward is entirely your doing.

Shepard on the gantry at 09:34 EST, May 5, 1961. He has been in the capsule for over four hours (the launch was delayed multiple times -- weather holds, technical checks, a power supply issue). He has had time to think. He knows the trajectory: 6.3g up, 5 minutes of weightlessness, 11.6g down. He knows the risks: the Redstone could explode on the pad (it has not done this, but the possibility is nonzero), the abort system could malfunction (it did on MR-1), the capsule could leak (Ham's capsule leaked on splashdown). He knows the reward: he will be the first American in space. He has done the analysis. The risk is acceptable. But knowing the risk is acceptable is not the same as accepting the risk. The gap between intellectual acceptance and volitional commitment is Kierkegaard's leap.

Kierkegaard distinguished three "spheres of existence" -- the aesthetic (living for pleasure and novelty), the ethical (living by principle and duty), and the religious (living by faith and personal commitment to the absolute). The transition between spheres cannot be mediated by reason alone -- it requires a leap, because the new sphere's values are not derivable from the old sphere's logic. Shepard's decision to ride the rocket is not an aesthetic choice (thrill-seeking) or purely an ethical choice (duty to country) -- it is a choice that combines rational assessment with personal commitment to an outcome that cannot be guaranteed. He has done everything he can to ensure success. The rest depends on forces he cannot control. He goes anyway. This is the leap.

"Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom." The word Kierkegaard used was "Angest" -- not clinical anxiety but something closer to existential vertigo. It is the feeling of standing at the edge and looking down, not because you fear falling but because you realize you COULD jump, that the freedom to jump is yours, and that this freedom is terrifying precisely because it is real. Shepard had this freedom. He could have said no at any point. He could have told the flight surgeon he felt unwell. He could have triggered the abort handle after engine ignition. He had, up to the very last moment, the freedom to choose not-this. He chose this. The leap is not the moment the rocket ignited -- the leap is the moment Shepard decided he would not abort, that he was committed, that the trajectory was his.

The Peregrine Falcon at the top of its stoop faces the same existential moment. The falcon has spotted prey below. It can choose to stoop or not to stoop. Once it tucks its wings and begins the dive, the commitment is not absolute (it can extend wings to abort), but the cost of aborting increases rapidly with speed -- at 300 km/h, extending wings produces enormous structural loads. The falcon's "leap" is the wing-tuck: the moment it transitions from the aesthetic (soaring, surveying, contemplating possibilities) to the committed (diving, accelerating, aiming for a specific target). The Pacific giant salamander, coiled under its cobble, faces the same moment: lunge or wait. Once the lunge begins, the 20 milliseconds are ballistic -- no course correction possible. The salamander's leap is the muscular contraction that initiates the strike.

Kierkegaard would have recognized all three leaps as instances of the same existential structure: a free agent, confronting genuine possibility, choosing to act, and accepting the consequences of the action. The mathematics of the trajectory does not make the leap unnecessary -- it makes the leap informed. Shepard did not leap blindly. He leaped knowing the numbers. The falcon does not stoop blindly. It stoops having calculated (by instinct, not arithmetic) the intercept geometry. The salamander does not lunge blindly. It lunges having sensed (by mechanoreception and vision) the prey's position and velocity. The leap of faith is not the absence of knowledge -- it is the presence of commitment in excess of what knowledge alone can justify.

### History (Secondary)

**Wing:** The Space Race Context, Gagarin's Flight, Cold War Pressures, Public Perception, the Kennedy Challenge
**Concept:** Freedom 7 in the context of geopolitical competition -- how a 15-minute suborbital flight became a defining moment in the American narrative, despite being objectively less impressive than Gagarin's orbital flight 23 days earlier

**Deposit:** When Shepard launched on May 5, 1961, the United States was already behind. Yuri Gagarin had orbited the Earth on April 12, 1961 -- 23 days earlier -- in Vostok 1, completing a full orbit in 108 minutes and becoming the first human in space by any definition. Shepard's suborbital flight, by comparison, reached 187.5 km (Gagarin reached approximately 327 km) and lasted 15 minutes (Gagarin lasted 108 minutes). Shepard was in space; Gagarin had orbited the Earth. The Soviet achievement was objectively greater.

Yet Freedom 7 was not a failure, and the American public did not treat it as one. Shepard became a national hero. The flight was broadcast live on television -- unlike Gagarin's flight, which was announced by the Soviet government only after its success. The transparency of the American program -- the live broadcast, the pre-flight publicity, the public knowledge that the astronaut's name was Alan Shepard and he was a Navy test pilot from New Hampshire -- created a different kind of achievement. The Soviets had done something greater and told the world afterward. The Americans had done something smaller and let the world watch them do it, including watching them fail (MR-1's 4-inch launch was televised).

Twenty days after Shepard's flight, on May 25, 1961, President John F. Kennedy addressed Congress and proposed that the United States commit to landing a man on the Moon before the end of the decade. This declaration -- one of the most consequential in the history of technology -- was made possible in part by Freedom 7. Kennedy needed a success to point to when proposing the lunar program. Shepard's flight provided that success: proof that American technology and American pilots could operate in space, that the Mercury program was producing results, and that the trajectory from 15-minute suborbital flights to lunar missions was plausible (even if the path was not yet clear).

The irony is that Shepard's flight was technically unnecessary for the Mercury program's progression. Ham's MR-2 had already demonstrated that a primate could survive and perform tasks during a suborbital Mercury-Redstone flight. The engineering case for proceeding directly to orbital Atlas flights was arguable. But the political case for a human suborbital flight was overwhelming: the country needed a hero, the program needed public support, and the pilot needed to demonstrate (not just infer from a chimpanzee's lever pulls) that a human could operate a spacecraft. Freedom 7 was as much a political mission as a technical one, and it achieved both objectives.

### Ethics (Secondary)

**Wing:** Risk Acceptance, Informed Consent in Extreme Environments, the Astronaut's Bargain, Public Risk vs Private Choice
**Concept:** The ethics of an individual choosing to accept extreme personal risk for a collective benefit -- and the difference between Shepard's informed consent and Ham/Enos's lack of it

**Deposit:** Alan Shepard volunteered. This is the fundamental ethical distinction between MR-3 and the earlier Mercury biological flights. Ham did not volunteer for MR-2. Enos did not volunteer for MA-5. Both chimpanzees were selected, trained through operant conditioning, and launched without their consent -- they could not consent, because consent requires understanding the risk, and neither Ham nor Enos could comprehend the risk of spaceflight.

Shepard understood the risk. He had been briefed on every failure mode, every abort scenario, every survival probability. He had watched MR-1 rise 4 inches and fall back. He had studied Ham's off-nominal flight in detail. He had trained in the Mercury procedures trainer for months. He had flown high-performance jets and knew what 6g felt like. His decision to fly was informed consent in the strongest sense: full knowledge of the hazard, full understanding of the consequences, full freedom to decline. He chose to go.

This distinction matters morally. The Mercury program's use of chimpanzees as biological test subjects (missions 1.17 and 1.18) involved moral asymmetry: the experimenters gained data, the subjects gained nothing (except banana pellets) and bore all the risk. Shepard's flight involves a different moral structure: the pilot gains personal glory, historical significance, and the satisfaction of doing something extraordinary, while bearing the risk voluntarily. The risk is not imposed -- it is accepted. This does not eliminate the ethical dimension (Shepard's family bore risks they did not choose, the recovery crews were put at risk by his flight), but it transforms the moral calculus from one of imposition to one of mutual agreement.

Kierkegaard would have recognized Shepard's choice as an instance of what he called "the ethical stage" -- where the individual acts according to a duty that is self-imposed rather than externally enforced. Shepard's duty was not to NASA (he could have resigned from the program), not to the Navy (they would not have forced him to fly), and not to the country (no law compelled him). His duty was to himself and to the project he had committed to. The leap of faith was not in the physics -- he trusted the physics -- but in the commitment: the decision that this project, this flight, this moment was worth the risk to his life.

### TRY 1: Suborbital Trajectory Calculator

**Duration:** 1-2 hours
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Aerospace Engineering / Mathematics
**What You Need:** A scientific calculator (or phone calculator with trig functions), a notebook, graph paper (optional), and optionally a spreadsheet program (Excel, Google Sheets, LibreOffice Calc). Total cost: $0.

**What You'll Learn:**
The mathematics of suborbital flight. You will compute the complete trajectory of Freedom 7 using the simplified ballistic equations, compare your results to the actual flight data, understand why the range equation gives an approximation but not the exact answer, and appreciate the difference between textbook trajectories and real-world trajectories.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] Familiarity with trigonometry (sine, cosine, tangent)
- [ ] A calculator capable of computing sin(), cos(), sqrt()
- [ ] A notebook for recording calculations

**The Exercise:**

```
SUBORBITAL TRAJECTORY CALCULATOR:

1. COMPUTE BURNOUT CONDITIONS:

   Given:
     v_bo    = 2,294 m/s (burnout velocity)
     h_bo    = 60,000 m (burnout altitude)
     gamma   = 45 degrees (flight path angle)

   Compute horizontal and vertical velocity components:
     v_h = v_bo * cos(gamma) = 2,294 * cos(45) = _______
     v_v = v_bo * sin(gamma) = 2,294 * sin(45) = _______

2. COMPUTE TIME TO APOGEE:

   At apogee, vertical velocity = 0:
     v_v - g * t_apogee = 0
     t_apogee = v_v / g = _______ / 9.8 = _______ seconds

   (Advanced: use g = 9.5 m/s^2 for the average value
   between 60 km and 187.5 km. How does this change
   t_apogee?)

3. COMPUTE PEAK ALTITUDE:

   h_max = h_bo + v_v * t_apogee - (1/2) * g * t_apogee^2
         = 60,000 + _______ * _______ - 0.5 * 9.8 * _______^2
         = _______ meters = _______ km

   Compare to actual: 187.5 km
   Error: _______% (explain why the simplified model
   overshoots or undershoots)

4. COMPUTE TOTAL COAST TIME:

   The coast phase is approximately symmetric around apogee.
   Total coast time = 2 * t_apogee = _______ seconds

   Weightless time: approximately equal to coast time
   minus the time spent in significant atmosphere on
   ascent and descent (~30 seconds each)
   Weightless time ≈ coast_time - 60 = _______ seconds
   Compare to actual: 302 seconds

5. COMPUTE RANGE:

   Simplified range equation:
     R = v_bo^2 * sin(2 * gamma) / g
       = (2,294)^2 * sin(90) / 9.8
       = _______ / 9.8
       = _______ meters = _______ km

   Compare to actual: 486 km
   Error: _______% (explain why the simplified equation
   overshoots -- atmospheric drag, non-uniform gravity,
   non-optimal launch angle)

6. COMPUTE REENTRY G-FORCE (ESTIMATE):

   The capsule reenters the dense atmosphere at
   approximately v_bo = 2,294 m/s and decelerates
   to approximately 100 m/s (parachute deployment
   speed) over approximately 60 seconds.

   Average deceleration:
     a_avg = (2,294 - 100) / 60 = _______ m/s^2

   Average g-force:
     g_avg = a_avg / 9.8 = _______ g

   Peak g-force is approximately 2-3x the average
   (the deceleration is not uniform -- it peaks when
   the capsule hits the densest atmosphere):
     g_peak ≈ 2.5 * g_avg = _______ g

   Compare to actual: 11.6g

7. COMPARE TO MR-2 (HAM):

   Repeat calculations 3-5 with Ham's data:
     v_bo = 2,292 m/s (similar velocity)
     BUT: escape rocket added ~1,300 m/s
     Effective v_max ≈ 3,590 m/s
     gamma ≈ 50 degrees (steeper due to abort)

   Compute:
     h_max (Ham) = _______ km (actual: 253 km)
     R (Ham)     = _______ km (actual: 679 km)

   The difference between Shepard's and Ham's trajectories
   is almost entirely due to the abort system adding
   ~1,300 m/s of velocity. Without the abort, Ham's
   trajectory would have been nearly identical to
   Shepard's -- confirming that the MR-3 trajectory
   was what MR-2 SHOULD have been.

CONNECT TO MR-3:
  The beauty of the suborbital trajectory is its
  simplicity. The same equations that describe a
  thrown ball describe Freedom 7. The only differences
  are the numbers: 2,294 m/s instead of 20 m/s,
  187.5 km instead of 20 m. The physics is the same.
  A student who can compute the range of a baseball
  can compute the range of a Mercury capsule. The
  universe does not have special equations for
  spaceflight -- it has one set of equations that
  apply to all projectiles, from baseballs to
  spacecraft to falcon stoops.
```

### TRY 2: Salamander Stream Survey

**Duration:** 2-4 hours (field) or 1 hour (desktop)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Herpetology / Ecology
**What You Need:** If in the PNW: waterproof boots or waders, a flashlight or headlamp, a notebook, a camera (phone OK), appropriate clothing for cold water. If NOT in PNW: access to iNaturalist.org and the USGS National Hydrography Dataset. IMPORTANT: Dicamptodon tenebrosus is a state-monitored species in some jurisdictions. Do NOT handle, collect, or disturb salamanders. Observe from the stream bank or by gently lifting cobble at the stream edge and replacing it immediately. Total cost: $0.

**The Exercise:**

```
PACIFIC GIANT SALAMANDER STREAM SURVEY:

1. HABITAT ASSESSMENT (PNW field observers):

   Dicamptodon tenebrosus prefers:
   - Cold, clear, fast-flowing streams (water temp < 15 C)
   - Coarse substrate: cobble (64-256 mm), boulder (>256 mm)
   - Canopy cover > 70% (old-growth or mature second-growth
     coniferous forest provides the best shade)
   - Clean water: low turbidity, high dissolved oxygen
   - Stream gradient: moderate (2-8% slope)

   WHERE TO LOOK:
   - Western Washington: Olympic Peninsula streams,
     Cascades foothills, south Puget Sound tributaries
   - Western Oregon: Coast Range streams, Cascades
     west slope, Willamette Valley tributaries
   - Northern California: Del Norte, Humboldt,
     Trinity counties
   - Elevation: sea level to ~1,500 m

   TIME TO LOOK:
   - Aquatic larvae and neotenous adults: year-round,
     but most visible in late summer/fall when stream
     flows are low and water is clear
   - Terrestrial adults: rainy season (October-April),
     especially during rain events when they emerge
     from cover to forage

   WHAT TO OBSERVE:
   - Salamander body length (snout to tail tip)
   - Gills: visible? (if yes = larval or neotenous)
   - Color: larvae are brown/olive with faint marbling;
     terrestrial adults are dark brown/black with
     copper or gold marbling
   - Behavior: hiding under cobble? Swimming? Lunging
     at prey? Basking on a rock (rare)?
   - Habitat: stream width, depth, substrate type,
     canopy cover percentage, water clarity

   RECORD:
   - Location (GPS or landmark)
   - Date, time, weather conditions
   - Water temperature (if accessible -- feel test
     is OK: "cold" vs "very cold" is useful data)
   - Stream name (if known)
   - Number of individuals observed
   - Size estimates
   - Gill presence/absence (neoteny indicator)

2. DESKTOP SURVEY (non-PNW or dry season):

   Go to iNaturalist.org and search for
   Dicamptodon tenebrosus.

   Explore:
```

## DIY Projects

### DIY 1: Model Rocket with Manual Attitude Control

**Duration:** 8-16 hours (building), 1-2 hours (flight testing)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Department:** Aerospace Engineering / Human Factors
**What You Need:** A model rocket kit with a body tube at least 30 mm diameter (Estes Alpha III or similar, ~$15-20), a set of model rocket engines (Estes A8-3 or B6-4, ~$8-12 for a pack of 3), a launch pad and controller (included in many starter kits, or ~$20 separately), a small servo motor (~$5, SG90 micro servo from an electronics supplier), an Arduino Nano or similar microcontroller (~$8-15), a 9V battery, hookup wire, and basic tools (hobby knife, sandpaper, glue). Optional: a small IMU board (MPU6050, ~$3-5) for measuring actual attitude during flight. Total cost: $40-80.

**What You'll Build:**
A model rocket with a manually controllable fin that can be deflected before launch to induce a roll during flight -- demonstrating the principle of attitude control. This is NOT a full three-axis control system (that would require multiple actuators and a control computer). It is a single-axis demonstration: by deflecting one fin, you induce a known roll rate, and the rocket spins during its coast phase. This is the simplest possible "manual attitude control" -- the pilot (you) sets the fin angle before launch, and the rocket rolls at a rate determined by that angle.

```
MODEL ROCKET MANUAL ATTITUDE CONTROL:

1. THE CONCEPT:

   Freedom 7's attitude control system used hydrogen
   peroxide thrusters to control pitch, yaw, and roll.
   Your model rocket will use a deflectable fin to
   control roll only.

   The physics:
     A fin angled relative to the airflow produces a
     side force. If the fin is on the aft section of
     the rocket, this side force creates a torque about
     the rocket's center of mass, inducing a roll.

     Torque = F_side * d
     where:
       F_side = (1/2) * rho * v^2 * C_L * A_fin * sin(delta)
       d = distance from fin center of pressure to
           rocket center of mass
       delta = fin deflection angle

     Angular acceleration = Torque / I_roll

   By varying the fin deflection angle (delta), you
   control the roll rate. A larger deflection produces
   a faster roll. Zero deflection produces no roll
   (stable flight).

2. CONSTRUCTION:

   BUILD THE ROCKET:
   - Assemble the model rocket kit per manufacturer
     instructions. Standard fins, standard nose cone,
     standard engine mount.
   - MODIFICATION: Replace ONE of the stock fins with
     a slightly larger fin mounted on a pivot. The
     pivot allows the fin to be angled relative to the
     body tube.

   THE DEFLECTABLE FIN:
   - Cut a fin from thin balsa or plastic sheet, same
     profile as the stock fins but 10-15% larger
   - Mount a small hinge (use thin music wire as a
     pivot) at the fin root, allowing the fin to
     rotate ~15 degrees in either direction
   - Attach the SG90 servo to the body tube adjacent
     to the fin, connected to the fin via a pushrod
     (thin music wire or hobby linkage)
   - The servo pre-sets the fin angle before launch
     (controlled by the Arduino)

   THE CONTROLLER:
   - Arduino Nano reads a potentiometer on the launch
     controller cable (or uses a pre-set value)
   - Before launch, the operator dials in the desired
     fin deflection (0 to 15 degrees)
   - Arduino commands the servo to that angle
   - During flight, the fin remains at the set angle
     (no active control during flight -- this is
     pre-set, not real-time)

3. FLIGHT TEST:

   LAUNCH 1: delta = 0 degrees (no deflection)
     Expected: stable, non-rolling flight (baseline)
     Record: visual observation of roll (should be
     near-zero, slight roll from manufacturing
     asymmetry is normal)

   LAUNCH 2: delta = 5 degrees
     Expected: moderate roll during coast phase
     Record: estimate roll rate (count rotations
     during coast if visible, or use IMU data)

   LAUNCH 3: delta = 10 degrees
     Expected: fast roll during coast phase
     Record: roll rate

   LAUNCH 4: delta = 15 degrees
     Expected: very fast roll -- the rocket may
     become gyroscopically stabilized (like a
     bullet) or may become unstable if the roll
     rate exceeds the fin's restoring ability

4. ANALYSIS:

   Plot roll rate vs fin deflection angle.
   The relationship should be approximately linear
   for small angles (< 10 degrees) and may become
   nonlinear at larger angles.

   Compute the theoretical roll rate:
     omega = (F_side * d) / I_roll * t_coast

   Compare theoretical to observed.
   The discrepancy tells you about:
   - Manufacturing imprecision (fins not perfectly
     aligned)
   - Aerodynamic interference (deflected fin changes
     the flow over adjacent fins)
   - Structural flex (the fin deflects under load,
     reducing the effective angle)

   These are the same kinds of discrepancies that
   Shepard encountered: the theoretical angular rate
   from the hydrogen peroxide thrusters was
   ~1 deg/s^2, but the actual rate varied due to
   propellant temperature, nozzle erosion, and
   structural compliance.

CONNECT TO MR-3:
  Your single-axis fin control is the simplest
  possible version of Shepard's three-axis thruster
  control. Shepard's system:
  - 3 axes (pitch, yaw, roll)
  - Variable-duration thruster pulses
  - Real-time feedback (attitude indicator)
  - Human-in-the-loop control bandwidth: ~0.77 Hz

  Your system:
  - 1 axis (roll only)
  - Pre-set deflection (no real-time control)
  - No in-flight feedback
  - Open-loop: set it and observe the result

  The gap between your system and Shepard's is the
  gap that Mercury's engineering bridged: from
  pre-set, open-loop, single-axis to real-time,
  closed-loop, three-axis. Each addition (more axes,
  closed-loop feedback, human pilot) adds capability
  and complexity. Freedom 7 demonstrated that the
  full system worked. Your model demonstrates the
  underlying principle: deflect a surface, create
  a torque, induce a rotation.

SAFETY:
  - ALWAYS follow the NAR (National Association of
    Rocketry) Model Rocket Safety Code
  - Launch in open areas, clear of people, buildings,
    and power lines
  - Use only commercially manufactured rocket engines
  - Never approach a rocket that has misfired
  - The servo and Arduino add weight -- verify the
    rocket's stability (CG ahead of CP) before flight
  - The deflectable fin adds asymmetric drag -- test
    at low deflection angles first
```

## Fox Companies Pathways

### FoxFiber Alignment

Shepard's manual control test demonstrated that a human operator adds value to a complex system -- not by being faster than the automatic system (the ASCS had higher control bandwidth) but by being more efficient (using proportional control rather than bang-bang) and more adaptive (able to handle novel situations the automatic system was not programmed for). FoxFiber's network management layer should incorporate the same principle: an automatic system handles routine operations (routing, load balancing, failover) at high speed, but a human operator (or a higher-level AI agent) can intervene with more nuanced strategies when the automatic system's bang-bang logic is wasteful or inappropriate. The manual override interface should be designed like Mercury's hand controller: intuitive, proportional (not just on/off), and with clear feedback showing the effect of the operator's inputs on network state. Shepard knew what the thrusters were doing because he could see the attitude indicator respond. A FoxFiber operator should know what their manual routing changes are doing because they can see the traffic flow respond in real time.

### FoxCompute Alignment

The Pacific giant salamander's facultative neoteny -- choosing between aquatic (simpler) and terrestrial (more capable) life modes based on environmental conditions -- maps directly to FoxCompute's container scaling strategy. A compute container can run in "neotenous mode" (minimal resources, basic functionality, low cost) when the workload is simple and the environment is stable, or "metamorphose" to "terrestrial mode" (full resources, maximum capability, higher cost) when the workload demands it. The metamorphosis decision should be environmentally mediated, like the salamander's: triggered by resource metrics (CPU > 80%, memory > 70%, queue depth > threshold) rather than pre-scheduled. The container does not need to know it is scaling up -- it responds to environmental signals, just as the salamander's thyroid hormone responds to water temperature and photoperiod. The key insight from Dicamptodon is that both modes are valid adult strategies: the neotenous salamander is not an incomplete adult, it is a fully functional organism optimized for its environment. A FoxCompute container in minimal mode is not degraded -- it is optimized for its current workload.

### SolarFox Alignment

Freedom 7's trajectory was a parabolic arc governed entirely by gravitational mechanics after engine cutoff -- a 13-minute demonstration that the laws of physics apply to spacecraft exactly as they apply to baseballs. SolarFox's orbital calculations for satellite-based solar collection arrays use the same equations: v_c = sqrt(GM/r) for circular velocity, T = 2*pi*sqrt(a^3/GM) for orbital period, and the range equation for ground-station coverage. The suborbital ballistic mathematics from this mission (used for rocket launch trajectory prediction) translate directly to the launch trajectory analysis needed for deploying SolarFox hardware to orbit. Every number in Freedom 7's parameter table is a validation of the same physics SolarFox depends on.

### Fox CapComm Alignment

The "spam in a can" debate -- whether the human adds value or is just a liability in a complex system -- is the central question of AI agent architecture. Fox CapComm must decide, for every operation, whether to run fully automatic (ASCS mode: fast, efficient, but rigid) or to include a human-in-the-loop (Shepard mode: slower, but adaptive and fuel-efficient). The answer, as Mercury demonstrated, is not one or the other but both: automatic for routine operations, human override for novel situations. Fox CapComm agents should have a Shepard Mode: a manual control interface that allows a human operator to take over any agent's task with proportional, nuanced inputs rather than crude start/stop commands. The human's bandwidth is lower (8 bits/second vs the agent's thousands), but the human's judgment is broader. Shepard did not fly the capsule better than the ASCS in any measurable dimension except fuel efficiency -- but he proved that the human COULD fly it, which meant the human could serve as backup when the ASCS failed. Fox CapComm's human override is the same insurance: not faster than the agents, but available when the agents cannot handle the situation.
