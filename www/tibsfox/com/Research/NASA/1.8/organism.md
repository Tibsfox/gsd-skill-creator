# Organism Pairing: Polypodium glycyrrhiza (Licorice Fern)

## The Epiphyte

**Scientific Name:** Polypodium glycyrrhiza Maxim.
**Family:** Polypodiaceae (polypody fern family)
**Common Names:** Licorice fern, many-footed fern
**Paired Mission:** 1.8 -- Vanguard 1, March 17, 1958
**PNW Status:** Native, abundant -- epiphytic on bigleaf maple, red alder, and mossy surfaces throughout western Pacific Northwest

---

## What Grows Without Touching the Ground

Walk through any lowland forest on the west side of the Cascades between October and April -- the wet months, the months when the PNW remembers what it is -- and look at the bigleaf maples. Not at the crown, where the canopy is a tangle of branches and moss and filtered light. Not at the base, where the sword fern and oxalis cluster against the roots. Look at the trunk. Look at the limbs where they leave the trunk at steep angles and run outward, shaggy with moss, draped with club moss, hosting an aerial garden that has no contact with the soil and no need for it. There, growing out of the moss mat that coats every horizontal and near-horizontal surface, you will find the fern. A row of fronds, sometimes a dozen, sometimes fifty, rising straight up from a mossy limb like a miniature forest growing on the back of a larger one. The fronds are bright green -- almost electric green in the wet winter light, a green that seems overexposed against the grays and browns and muted greens of the winter forest. Each frond is 15 to 70 centimeters long, once-pinnate, the pinnae arranged in opposite pairs along the rachis like the teeth of a comb, each pinna an elongated triangle with a pointed tip and a smooth or slightly wavy margin. The fronds taper at the base and at the tip, widest in the middle third, and they have a texture that is simultaneously delicate and leathery -- thin enough to be translucent when backlit, tough enough to endure winter storms that rip branches from the trees they grow on.

This is Polypodium glycyrrhiza. Licorice fern. And the thing that defines it, the thing that separates it from the sword ferns and bracken ferns and lady ferns that grow in the soil below, is that it does not grow in soil. It grows on other things. It is an epiphyte -- a word built from the Greek epi (upon) and phyton (plant), meaning a plant that grows upon another plant. The licorice fern grows on the trunks and branches of bigleaf maple, on the trunks of red alder and Oregon white oak, on mossy rock faces, on nurse logs, on the tops of old stumps, on concrete retaining walls where a thin layer of moss has accumulated, on the mossy roofs of abandoned buildings in the wet western valleys. It grows anywhere that a surface is stable enough to hold a mat of moss thick enough to anchor a rhizome. It does not need soil. It does not need the ground. It needs a surface, moisture, and light, and the Pacific Northwest provides all three in abundance on the vertical and horizontal surfaces of its trees.

The rhizome is the key to the fern's life strategy. It is a creeping, branching, horizontal stem that runs along the surface of the substrate -- along the bark of a bigleaf maple limb, through the moss mat, over the rock face -- rooting itself with fine, wiry roots that penetrate the moss and the outer bark but do not penetrate the living tissue of the host tree. The rhizome is slender, typically 3 to 6 millimeters in diameter, covered with golden-brown scales that protect the growing tip and the dormant buds. It branches dichotomously, forking into two growing tips that diverge and explore the substrate in different directions, each fork producing its own fronds and its own roots. Over years and decades, the branching rhizome network spreads across the surface of the host, forming a dense mat that can cover an entire limb, wrap around a trunk, colonize a rock face from one end to the other. The individual fronds are ephemeral -- they live one season, sometimes two, before browning and falling away. The rhizome is the persistent structure, the thread that connects the generations of fronds, the organism's true body.

And the rhizome tastes like licorice. That is the source of the species name: glycyrrhiza, from the Greek glykys (sweet) and rhiza (root). The same root that gives its name to the genus Glycyrrhiza -- true licorice, the Mediterranean legume whose root produces glycyrrhizin, the compound that makes licorice candy sweet. Polypodium glycyrrhiza is not related to true licorice. It is a fern, separated from the legume family by roughly 400 million years of evolutionary divergence. But its rhizome contains a suite of compounds -- including osladin, a steroidal saponin approximately 500 times sweeter than sucrose -- that produce a taste remarkably similar to true licorice root. Convergent chemistry. Two unrelated organisms, separated by the full breadth of the plant kingdom, producing sweet-tasting compounds in their underground or surface-creeping stems. The fern arrived at sweetness by a completely different biosynthetic pathway than the legume, and the result is close enough that the species was named for the resemblance.

## The Reversed Calendar

Most plants in the Pacific Northwest follow the expected phenological calendar: dormant in winter, growing in spring, photosynthetically active through the long days of summer, senescing in fall. The deciduous trees drop their leaves in October, and the understory plants retreat to roots and rhizomes, and the forest is quiet until April.

Polypodium glycyrrhiza does the opposite. Its fronds emerge in October and November, when the fall rains begin. They grow rapidly through the wettest months -- November, December, January, February -- photosynthesizing in the dim, indirect winter light that filters through the leafless canopy of the deciduous trees they grow on. By March and April, the fronds are mature, fully expanded, their sori ripe with spores on the undersurface of each pinna. Through May and into June, as the PNW's dry season begins, the fronds start to senesce -- browning from the tips, curling inward, drying out. By July and August, the driest months, the fronds are brown and crisp and often gone entirely, dropped from the rhizome, shed like deciduous leaves but on the opposite schedule. The rhizome remains, dormant, alive, waiting in the moss mat on the bigleaf maple limb for October to come again.

This reversed phenology is not a quirk. It is an adaptation to the specific niche the fern occupies. An epiphyte on a deciduous tree in the PNW faces a fundamental problem: when the tree has its leaves, the epiphyte is shaded. A fern growing on a bigleaf maple branch during summer is under the densest canopy in the forest -- bigleaf maple leaves are the largest maple leaves in North America, up to 35 centimeters across, and they produce a shade so deep that very little photosynthetically active radiation reaches the trunk and inner branches. But when the maple drops its leaves in October, the canopy opens. Suddenly the trunk and limbs are exposed to full sky -- or what passes for full sky in a PNW winter, which is usually overcast but still orders of magnitude brighter than the understory of a leafed-out maple canopy. The fern grows when its host is bare, photosynthesizing in the open winter light, and goes dormant when its host is leafed out, avoiding the deep shade and the summer drought simultaneously.

The drought avoidance is as important as the light exploitation. The PNW's summer dry season -- June through September in a typical year -- produces conditions that are lethal for an organism that has no roots in the soil and no access to groundwater. An epiphyte depends entirely on atmospheric moisture: rain, fog, dew, the humidity of the ambient air. During the PNW's dry summers, when weeks can pass without significant rainfall and the relative humidity drops below 40 percent on warm afternoons, an actively photosynthesizing fern perched on an exposed tree limb would desiccate in days. The licorice fern avoids this by not being active during the dry season. It drops its fronds, retreats to the rhizome, and waits. The rhizome, protected by its scales and embedded in the moss mat that retains some moisture even during dry periods, survives the summer in a state of metabolic quiescence -- not dead, not growing, just waiting. When the rains return, the rhizome rehydrates, the buds at its growing tips swell, and new fronds unfurl within weeks.

This is a survival strategy built on patience. The fern does not fight the dry season. It does not develop waxy cuticles or deep taproots or succulent water-storage tissues to endure drought the way a plant rooted in soil might. It simply withdraws. It reduces its exposed surface area to zero -- no fronds, no transpiring tissue, nothing for the dry air to desiccate -- and waits for conditions to improve. When they do, it returns. The cycle has repeated for thousands of years on the bigleaf maples of the Pacific Northwest, and the fern's strategy has proven effective: Polypodium glycyrrhiza is one of the most abundant and widespread epiphytes in the PNW, found in every forest type from sea level to approximately 1,200 meters on the west side of the Cascades, present on nearly every bigleaf maple in every lowland forest, unmissable in winter, invisible in summer.

## The Catapult: How Spores Launch

Turn a mature licorice fern frond over and look at the undersurface. On each pinna, arranged in two rows parallel to the midvein, you will find the sori -- clusters of sporangia, the spore-producing structures that are the fern's reproductive organs. In Polypodium glycyrrhiza, the sori are round, 2 to 4 millimeters in diameter, arranged in a single row on each side of the pinna midvein. They are naked -- no indusium, no protective covering, the sporangia exposed directly to the air. Each sorus contains dozens to hundreds of individual sporangia, and each sporangium is a microscopic marvel of mechanical engineering.

A sporangium is a stalked capsule, roughly 0.3 millimeters in diameter, shaped like a biconvex lens, with a single row of specialized cells called the annulus running vertically over the top of the capsule like the handle of a basket. The annulus is the catapult. It is a row of approximately 12 to 16 cells with thick inner and radial walls and thin outer walls, arranged in an arc that runs from one side of the sporangium's stalk attachment, over the top, and partway down the other side, terminating at a group of thin-walled lip cells that form the weak point where the capsule will break open.

When the sporangium is mature and the air is dry, water begins to evaporate from the annulus cells through their thin outer walls. As water leaves each cell, the remaining water is placed under tension -- negative pressure, the cohesive force of water molecules pulling on each other and on the cell walls, resisting the phase change from liquid to vapor. This tension is enormous on a cellular scale: the cohesion-tension in an evaporating annulus cell can exceed 200 atmospheres (20 megapascals), comparable to the pressures inside industrial hydraulic systems. The tension pulls the thick inner walls of the annulus cells inward, causing each cell to flatten. Because the annulus is a curved row of cells that are all flattening simultaneously, the curvature of the annulus changes -- the arc bends backward, away from the spore mass, peeling the capsule open at the lip cells and carrying the spores outward on the annulus like passengers on a slowly bending spring.

Then the cavitation happens. At some critical tension, the water column inside one or more of the annulus cells snaps -- the cohesive force of the water is exceeded by the negative pressure, a vapor bubble nucleates inside the cell, and the tension drops instantaneously to zero. The annulus, which had been held in its bent-back position by the enormous tension of the evaporating water, snaps forward -- back toward its original curvature, propelled by the elastic energy stored in the thickened cell walls. The snap is fast. Measurements of sporangium discharge using high-speed photography and computational models have recorded accelerations on the order of 100,000 g -- one hundred thousand times the acceleration of gravity -- and the spores are flung from the annulus at velocities sufficient to clear the boundary layer of still air around the frond and enter the turbulent airstream beyond.

The mechanism is a catapult powered by the phase transition of water. The energy source is evaporation. The spring is the cell wall. The trigger is cavitation. The entire device is built from dead cells with thickened walls -- no muscles, no nerves, no metabolic energy expenditure at the moment of discharge. The sporangium is a passive machine that converts the energy of evaporation into the kinetic energy of spore launch, and it does this through the geometry of a curved row of cells that exploit the cohesive properties of water. The mechanism has been operating in ferns for approximately 360 million years, since before the first seed plants evolved, since before the first forests, since the Devonian period when the land was colonized by organisms that needed to disperse their propagules through air rather than water.

## Coast Salish Medicine: The Sweet Root

The indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest coast -- the Coast Salish, the Nuu-chah-nulth, the Kwakwaka'wakw, the Haida, the Tlingit, and many others -- knew Polypodium glycyrrhiza intimately and used it extensively. The sweet rhizome was both medicine and food, gathered from the mossy limbs of bigleaf maples and the surfaces of mossy rocks, peeled, and either chewed fresh or dried for later use.

As medicine, the rhizome was used primarily for throat and respiratory complaints. The sweet, slightly mucilaginous interior of the rhizome was chewed for sore throats, coughs, and colds. The sweetness soothed. The mucilage coated. Some groups prepared a decoction by boiling the rhizome and drinking the liquid as a tea for chest congestion. Others dried and powdered the rhizome and mixed it with other materials as a poultice. Ethnobotanical records from the early twentieth century -- the work of Erna Gunther, Nancy Turner, and others who documented indigenous plant use in the PNW before the knowledge was lost -- describe the licorice fern as one of the most commonly used medicinal plants in the Coast Salish pharmacopoeia.

As food, the rhizome was chewed as a sweet treat -- a forest candy, gathered from the limbs of maples and consumed on the spot or carried along on a journey. The sweetness is intense and immediate, followed by a licorice aftertaste that lingers. Children gathered it. Travelers carried it. It was a casual food, not a staple -- the rhizome contains relatively little caloric value, and the sweetness is produced by osladin and related saponins rather than by sugars, so the metabolic contribution was minimal. But the taste was valued, and the gathering was a form of engagement with the forest -- a practice that required knowing which trees hosted the fern, which season the rhizomes were sweetest (winter, when the fern was actively growing), and which surfaces yielded the best rhizomes (mossy limbs of bigleaf maple, where the moss mat was thick enough to produce large, well-developed rhizomes).

The licorice fern was a tree-borne resource, gathered not from the ground but from the canopy -- or at least from the lower reaches of the canopy and the trunk. This is a form of harvesting that requires looking up, reaching up, engaging with the vertical dimension of the forest rather than the horizontal. The fern grows where the moss grows, and the moss grows where the moisture accumulates, and the moisture accumulates on the surfaces that face the rain and the fog -- the tops of limbs, the north sides of trunks, the sheltered angles where branch meets trunk. Gathering licorice fern is an act of reading the three-dimensional structure of the forest, understanding which surfaces in which orientations on which species of tree will host the fern in sufficient abundance to be worth gathering. It is a form of ecological literacy that is learned by doing, passed between generations through practice rather than text, and it encodes knowledge about the forest's hydrology, its canopy architecture, its species interactions, and its seasonal rhythms.

## Resonance Axes

### 1. Epiphyte / Orbit

Polypodium glycyrrhiza perches on trees without touching the ground. Vanguard 1 orbits Earth without touching it. Both persist in elevated positions for extraordinary durations -- the fern's rhizome network can endure for decades to centuries on a single host tree, outlasting individual fronds the way the satellite has outlasted individual tracking stations. The fern's rhizome creeps along the surface of its host, following the contours of bark and moss, maintaining contact without penetration, drawing moisture from the atmosphere rather than the substrate. The satellite follows the contours of the gravitational field, maintaining its orbit without propulsion, drawing energy from sunlight rather than chemical fuel. Both are objects in elevated, persistent, unpowered trajectories around something larger than themselves. The fern orbits the tree. The satellite orbits the planet. The rhizome network maps the surface of the limb the way the satellite's orbit maps the gravitational field of the Earth. Both have been there for a very long time, and both will continue to be there long after the observers who noticed them have moved on.

The fern outlasts its fronds. The satellite outlasts its transmitters. The persistent structure -- rhizome, orbit -- is the real organism, and the ephemeral structures -- fronds, radio signals -- are the expendable outputs that the persistent structure generates when conditions allow.

### 2. Solar-Powered

Both the fern and Vanguard 1 are powered by sunlight. The fern captures photons in its chloroplasts and converts them to chemical energy through photosynthesis, driving the metabolism that builds fronds, produces spores, and extends the rhizome network. The satellite captured photons on its six silicon solar cells and converted them to electrical energy, driving the transmitter that broadcast its signal for six years. The fern's reversed phenology -- active in winter, dormant in summer -- means it photosynthesizes when the light is indirect, diffuse, filtered through overcast skies, coming from angles that change as the leafless canopy shifts in the wind. The satellite's solar cells produced power regardless of the angle of incidence -- the sphere tumbled slowly in orbit, and the six cells were distributed around the surface so that some were always illuminated regardless of orientation. Both organisms harvest sunlight in suboptimal conditions: the fern in the dim winter light of the PNW, the satellite at whatever angle the tumble presents to the Sun. Both succeed not by optimizing for peak solar input but by functioning across a wide range of illumination conditions.

Vanguard 1 was the first solar-powered satellite. Every solar panel on every subsequent spacecraft descends from its demonstration that solar cells work in the space environment. The fern's photosynthesis is the ancestral solar technology -- hundreds of millions of years old, proven across every environment on Earth's surface, the original demonstration that sunlight can be converted to useful energy. The satellite carried the newest version of the oldest technology.

### 3. Small but Enduring

Khrushchev mocked the grapefruit satellite for its size. But the 1.47-kilogram sphere has been in orbit for sixty-eight years and counting, while Sputnik 1 (83.6 kg) lasted three months, Sputnik 2 (over 500 kg with booster) lasted five months, and Explorer 1 (13.97 kg) lasted twelve years. Vanguard 1's small size and high orbit gave it the lowest area-to-mass ratio and the highest perigee of any early satellite, and both factors contribute to its extraordinary orbital lifetime. The smallest satellite of the first generation has lasted the longest.

Polypodium glycyrrhiza is small and easily overlooked. Its fronds are modest -- sword fern fronds reach a meter or more, bracken fronds spread in broad triangular sprays, but licorice fern fronds are typically 15 to 40 centimeters, growing in quiet rows on the backs of mossy limbs where a casual observer might not look. But the rhizome network persists. Individual rhizomes have been estimated to persist for decades. The fern outlasts the moss it grows in. It can outlast the limb it grows on -- when a limb breaks and falls, the fern rhizome on the fallen limb can reestablish on the nurse log that the limb becomes, continuing to grow on the dead wood the way it grew on the living branch. In some cases, the fern outlasts the tree itself, persisting on the stump or the nurse log of a fallen maple for years after the tree has died. The smallest, most overlooked epiphyte in the PNW forest is among the most persistent. Size is not correlated with endurance. The grapefruit outlasted the rockets. The fern outlasts the trees.

### 4. Surface-Clinging Persistence

The licorice fern clings to bark, riding the tree through decades of windstorms, ice events, drought summers, and drenching winters. The rhizome's grip is not strong -- it can be peeled from the substrate with moderate force -- but it does not need to be strong. It needs to be persistent. The rhizome is flexible. It bends with the limb. It follows the contours. When a section is damaged or dislodged, the remaining network continues to grow, branching around the gap, recolonizing the lost territory from the edges. The fern's attachment to its host is not rigid. It is compliant -- absorbing disturbance, flowing around obstacles, persisting through flexibility rather than strength.

Vanguard 1 clings to its orbit the same way. The orbit is not rigid -- it is perturbed by the Moon, the Sun, radiation pressure, atmospheric drag, the non-spherical gravitational field of Earth itself. The orbit flexes. The perigee shifts, the apogee drifts, the orbital plane precesses, the eccentricity oscillates. But the satellite remains in orbit, because the perturbations are small relative to the orbital energy, and the orbit absorbs them the way the rhizome absorbs wind loading -- by flexing, not by resisting. Both the fern and the satellite persist not because they are anchored immovably but because they are compliant enough to absorb the disturbances that their environments produce. Rigidity breaks. Flexibility persists.

### 5. Geodesy / Form

Vanguard 1 proved that Earth is pear-shaped. The discovery came from analyzing the subtle perturbations of the satellite's orbit -- the deviations from the trajectory predicted by a simple oblate-spheroid gravity model. The J3 harmonic, the odd zonal term that describes the north-south asymmetry of the geoid, was detected because a small sphere in a high orbit is exquisitely sensitive to small asymmetries in the gravitational field. The satellite's trajectory is a record written in the language of gravitational geometry -- every bump and bulge in Earth's mass distribution leaves its signature in the orbit.

The fern's sporangium exploits the geometry of curved surfaces to launch spores. The annulus -- the row of thick-walled cells that forms the catapult -- is an arc, a curved structure whose mechanical properties depend entirely on its curvature, its radius, the thickness of its cell walls, and the tension in the water that fills those cells. The catapult works because the annulus is curved. A flat row of cells drying out would simply shrink. A curved row of cells drying out changes its curvature -- bending backward as the cells flatten, storing elastic energy in the bending, then snapping forward when cavitation releases the tension. The geometry of the curve IS the mechanism. The sporangium is a device that converts the physics of curved surfaces into motion.

Both Vanguard 1 and the licorice fern sporangium are systems in which the geometry of curved surfaces is the essential physics. The satellite detects curvature -- the curvature of the geoid, the departures of Earth's gravitational equipotential surface from a perfect sphere. The sporangium exploits curvature -- the curvature of the annulus, the mechanical advantage of a bending arc. One reads the curve. The other rides it. Both depend on the fact that curved surfaces behave differently from flat ones, and both extract information or energy from the difference. The pear-shaped Earth is a curved surface whose subtleties were invisible until a small sphere orbited it for long enough. The annulus is a curved surface whose mechanical potential is invisible until the water evaporates and the spring releases. Both are curves that contain secrets, revealed only by time and the physics of surfaces.

## Taxonomy Note

The species was first described by David Douglas (the same Douglas for whom the Douglas-fir is named) from specimens collected in the Pacific Northwest during his botanical explorations in the 1820s, but the formal taxonomic description is attributed to Carl Johann Maximowicz, the Russian botanist who published the name Polypodium glycyrrhiza in 1859. The genus Polypodium, established by Linnaeus in 1753, means "many-footed" -- a reference to the branching rhizome with its multiple attachment points, which early botanists compared to the multiple feet of a centipede or millipede. The family Polypodiaceae is one of the largest fern families, containing approximately 65 genera and 1,650 species worldwide, predominantly epiphytic in tropical and subtropical forests.

In the Pacific Northwest, Polypodium glycyrrhiza is the dominant member of its genus and one of the most commonly encountered ferns in lowland forests west of the Cascades. It is sometimes confused with Polypodium hesperium (western polypody), a closely related species that tends to grow on rock rather than on tree bark and has a different distribution of secondary metabolites in its rhizome. The two species hybridize where their ranges overlap, producing fertile hybrid populations that have complicated the taxonomy of the group. Recent molecular phylogenetic studies have confirmed that P. glycyrrhiza and P. hesperium are distinct species, not subspecies, though their morphological similarity and ability to hybridize indicate recent divergence.

The common name "licorice fern" is used universally in the PNW and is unambiguous -- no other fern in the region has a sweet-tasting rhizome. The name is both descriptively accurate and practically useful: if you find a fern on a mossy tree limb in the PNW and its rhizome tastes like licorice, it is Polypodium glycyrrhiza. The taste is the diagnostic character that anyone can use, no hand lens required, no dichotomous key necessary. The forest names itself for those who are willing to taste it.
