Gemini 11 — The First Revolution

Maximum commitment on the first attempt

Date: September 12–15, 1966 Program: Gemini (Flight 9 of 10) Duration: 71h 17m 8s · 44 orbits Degree: 49 of 360

Mission Summary

On September 12, 1966, at 13:42 UTC, a Titan II launched Gemini 11 from Launch Complex 19 at Cape Kennedy, carrying Pete Conrad and Dick Gordon into orbit. Eighty-five minutes later — before completing a single revolution of the Earth — Conrad had rendezvoused and docked with the Agena target vehicle. First orbit. First try. The fastest rendezvous of the Gemini program, and the profile Apollo would need when the Lunar Module ascended from the Moon’s surface with no time for a leisurely multi-orbit chase.

Then they went higher than any human had gone before. The Agena’s engine fired for 26 seconds and boosted the docked stack to an apogee of 1,374 km — more than three times higher than the International Space Station, a record that would stand for over two years until Apollo 8 flew to the Moon. From that altitude, Conrad and Gordon could see the curvature of the Earth extending in all directions, from Africa to Australia in a single glance.

Then they did something no one had ever attempted: tethered spaceflight. Gordon had attached a 30-meter Dacron tether between Gemini 11 and the Agena during a difficult EVA. Conrad undocked, backed away until the line was taut, and put the two vehicles into a slow rotation. The accelerometers detected 0.00015 g — the first artificial gravity ever generated in space. Tiny, but real. Finally, Gemini 11 performed the first fully automatic reentry, landing 4.5 km from the target — the most accurate Gemini splashdown. Four firsts in three days, executed by a Navy test pilot from Philadelphia who stood 5’6” and a Seattle native who had set the transcontinental speed record.

Crew

Charles “Pete” Conrad Jr. — Commander
June 2, 1930 – July 8, 1999

Gemini 5 (1965) · Gemini 11 (1966) · Apollo 12 (1969) · Skylab 2 (1973)

Four spaceflights. B.S. Aeronautical Engineering, Princeton. Navy test pilot. Rejected from Mercury for refusing to cooperate with psychological tests. Selected for Group 2 (The New Nine, 1962). Walked on the Moon at the Ocean of Storms — first words: “Whoopee! Man, that may have been a small one for Neil, but that’s a long one for me!” Won a $500 bet with journalist Oriana Fallaci that NASA didn’t script astronaut words. Saved the Skylab space station by leading the repair crew that freed the stuck solar panel and deployed the sunshade. 5’6”, irreverent, competitive, funny. Died in a motorcycle accident in Ojai, California, at 69. The astronaut who proved you didn’t have to be solemn about spaceflight.

Richard F. “Dick” Gordon Jr. — Pilot
October 5, 1929 – November 6, 2017

Gemini 11 (1966) · Apollo 12 (1969)

Born in Seattle, Washington. B.S. Chemistry, University of Washington, 1951. Navy test pilot. Won the Bendix Trophy (1961) — transcontinental speed record, Los Angeles to New York in 2 hours 47 minutes in an F-8U Crusader. Selected for Group 3 (The Fourteen, 1963). Apollo 12 CMP — orbited the Moon alone in Yankee Clipper while Conrad and Bean walked on the surface. Originally assigned to command Apollo 18, which was cancelled. Never walked on the Moon. A Seattle native who flew higher than any human before him — 1,374 km above a planet that included, somewhere below the clouds, the campus where he’d studied chemistry sixteen years earlier.

Mission Timeline

Day 1 — September 12, 1966
T−97 min: Atlas-Agena launches GATV-11 from LC-14. T+0 (13:42 UTC): Titan II launches Gemini 11 from LC-19, 97 minutes later. T+85 min — First-orbit rendezvous: Conrad achieves M=1 rendezvous and docking with GATV-11 on the first revolution. Eighty-five minutes, launch to docking. The fastest rendezvous of the Gemini program. The technique Apollo would need for LM ascent rendezvous — proven in one orbit.
Day 2 — September 13, 1966
Gordon’s EVA: Gordon exits to attach a 30-meter Dacron tether between Gemini 11 and the Agena. The work is brutal — no handholds on the Agena’s smooth surface, visor fogging from exertion, sweat pooling in his eyes. Conrad talks him through it from the cockpit. Gordon attaches the tether but the EVA is terminated early at ~33 minutes. The hardest physical work he had ever done. Standup EVA: 2 hours 8 minutes of astronomical photography, upper body outside the hatch. More successful — no free-floating required. Agena PPS burn: The Bell 8247 engine fires for ~26 seconds. Apogee climbs to 1,374 km — highest altitude any human has reached. The view: Earth’s curvature in all directions, Africa to Australia in a single glance.
Day 3 — September 14, 1966
Tethered flight: Conrad undocks from the Agena and backs away until the 30-meter tether is taut. Thrusters put the dumbbell configuration into slow rotation — one revolution every six minutes. Accelerometers detect 0.00015 g: the first artificial gravity ever generated in space. The tether oscillates, goes slack, re-tensions. Conrad manages the dynamics with thruster inputs. The experiment runs ~3 hours before the tether is released. Every future tethered-spaceflight proposal traces back to this: 30 meters of Dacron rope and two spacecraft spinning slowly above the Earth.
Day 3 — September 15, 1966
First automatic reentry: The onboard computer manages reentry without manual input. Splashdown at 13:59 UTC in the western Atlantic (24°15′N, 70°07′W), 4.5 km from the target — the most accurate Gemini landing. Recovery by USS Guam (LPH-9). Total: 71 hours, 17 minutes, 8 seconds. 44 orbits. Four firsts.

Technical Achievements

First-Orbit Rendezvous

All previous Gemini rendezvous used the M=3 or M=4 profile — third or fourth orbit, hours after launch. The M=1 profile compressed everything into 85 minutes: system checks, phasing burns, braking maneuvers, terminal approach. The margin was smaller. The workload was higher. But Apollo needed it — when the Lunar Module ascended from the Moon, there would be no time for a multi-orbit chase. Conrad proved M=1 worked. The shortest distance between two spacecraft.

Record Altitude: 1,374 km

More than three times the ISS orbit. Nearly twice the Gemini 10 altitude record (763 km). The spacecraft passed through the inner Van Allen belt. The view encompassed roughly 10% of the Earth’s surface in a single glance. The record stood for 26 months — until Borman, Lovell, and Anders flew Apollo 8 to the Moon in December 1968.

Tethered Spaceflight and Artificial Gravity

The 30-meter Dacron tether, the slow rotation, the measured 0.00015 g — the most forward-looking experiment of the Gemini program. The data proved three things: tethers could be deployed and managed in orbit, rotating tethered systems were dynamically stable with active control, and centripetal acceleration was measurable and predictable. From Gemini 11’s Dacron rope to space elevator proposals — fifty-plus years of tethered spaceflight concepts trace back to this experiment.

Automated Reentry

The onboard guidance computer managed reentry without manual input, achieving 4.5 km accuracy — within visual range of the recovery carrier. Apollo’s reentry from lunar distance would be far more demanding, and the success of automated guidance on Gemini 11 validated the approach.

Gordon’s EVA

The third consecutive Gemini EVA marred by exhaustion, after Cernan on Gemini 9A and Collins on Gemini 10. The pattern was unmistakable: working in a pressurized suit in zero gravity was far more physically demanding than anyone predicted. Gordon had to straddle the Agena’s nose, hold himself in position with his legs, and attach the tether connector — all while his suit ballooned, his visor fogged, and sweat pooled in his eyes. Conrad’s voice from the cockpit was steady and calm, guiding him through each step.

The tether was attached. The mission objective was met. But the EVA was terminated early. Three missions, three exhausted astronauts, three abbreviated EVAs. One mission remained: Gemini 12, where Buzz Aldrin would finally solve the problem with underwater neutral-buoyancy training and the simple innovation of putting handholds and foot restraints everywhere. The lesson cost three flights to learn.

Significance for Apollo

  • First-orbit rendezvous works. Apollo required the ascending LM to catch the CSM within one or two orbits. Conrad proved the M=1 profile was operationally viable — launch to docking in 85 minutes.
  • High-altitude docked propulsion is reliable. The Agena PPS burn to 1,374 km demonstrated that docked propulsive maneuvers could achieve large delta-v changes safely, previewing the SPS burns Apollo would use for lunar orbit insertion and transearth injection.
  • Tethered operations are feasible. The first data on tethered dynamics in orbit — slack management, rotation control, measurable centripetal acceleration. A foundation for every tethered-spaceflight concept since.
  • Automated reentry guidance is reliable. The 4.5 km accuracy without manual input validated the approach Apollo would use for the more demanding return from lunar distance.
  • Pete Conrad is ready for the Moon. Two Gemini flights, first-orbit rendezvous, record altitude. He would command Apollo 12 three years later and walk on the Moon at the Ocean of Storms.

The largest Passerellidae in the engine. The Fox Sparrow forages with a signature double-scratch — a two-footed explosive lunge through leaf litter, audible 20 meters away, excavating trenches that other species follow. The Sooty group (unalaschcensis and allies) is the PNW form: dark chocolate-brown, thick-billed, breeding in dense coastal thickets from Alaska through western Washington. Four subspecies groups differ so dramatically they may represent four species — a taxonomic revolution in slow motion.

The song is among the richest in Passerellidae: 5–12 song types per male, clear whistles and complex modulated notes, comparable to the Hermit Thrush for musical quality. Where the White-crowned Sparrow (degree 48) maintains rigid dialects, the Fox Sparrow builds a repertoire — shared elements recombined into individually distinctive sequences. One preserves. The other innovates. Fifth consecutive Passerellidae (new record).
