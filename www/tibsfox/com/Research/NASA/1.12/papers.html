Historical Paper (Connected to Mission)

Suomi, V.E. (1958). “The Radiation Balance of the Earth from a Satellite.” Annals of the International Geophysical Year, Vol. VI, pp. 331-340. Pergamon Press.

  • Published: 1958 (proceedings volume for the IGY satellite program)
  • Significance: This paper is Verner Suomi’s proposal and theoretical framework for measuring Earth’s radiation budget from orbit — the scientific blueprint for the instrument that flew on Explorer 7 one year later. Written while Suomi was an associate professor of meteorology at the University of Wisconsin, the paper lays out the fundamental measurement concept with characteristic clarity: a satellite in low Earth orbit can measure the global energy balance by carrying two flat-plate bolometers with different spectral absorptivities. One plate, painted with lampblack (absorptivity approximately 0.95 across all wavelengths), absorbs both incoming solar radiation and outgoing thermal emission from Earth. The second plate, painted with a white pigment based on magnesium oxide (high reflectivity in the solar spectrum, high absorptivity in the thermal infrared), responds primarily to Earth’s thermal emission. The temperature difference between the two plates, combined with the known absorptivity coefficients, allows decomposition of the total radiation field into its shortwave (solar, reflected) and longwave (thermal, emitted) components — two equations in two unknowns, solvable by linear algebra. Suomi describes the calibration procedure: the bolometers must be characterized in a laboratory before flight using a calibrated blackbody source and a solar simulator, and the in-flight calibration can be checked during orbital eclipse (when the satellite is in Earth’s shadow), during which the solar component drops to zero and the thermal component can be isolated directly. The paper calculates the expected signal levels, the thermistor sensitivity required, and the telemetry bandwidth needed to transmit temperature readings with sufficient precision. It estimates the expected albedo (0.35 plus or minus 0.05, based on aircraft and balloon measurements) and the outgoing longwave radiation (approximately 230 W/m^2, consistent with a planetary effective temperature of about 255 K). Suomi notes that the measurement precision achievable with a single bolometer pair on a single satellite is limited by the non-uniform sampling of Earth’s surface — the satellite sees only the strip of planet passing beneath it on each orbit, and many orbits are required to build a global picture. He proposes that a constellation of satellites with different orbital inclinations could solve this sampling problem, a vision that would eventually be realized by the Nimbus series and later the CERES instruments on Terra, Aqua, and NOAA-20.

Connection to Explorer 7: This paper IS Explorer 7’s scientific mission, stated as a proposal. Every element Suomi described was built, tested, launched on Juno II on October 13, 1959, and operated for approximately two years. The bolometers worked. The two-equation decomposition worked. The calibration strategy worked. The data were noisy, the sampling was sparse, and the absolute accuracy was limited by paint degradation and thermal contamination from the spacecraft body — all problems Suomi anticipated in the paper and proposed mitigations for. Explorer 7 did not solve the Earth radiation budget problem. It demonstrated that the problem could be attacked from orbit, using an instrument simple enough to fit on a 41.5 kg satellite, cheap enough to be built in a university laboratory, and robust enough to operate for two years in the space environment. The paper is the concept; Explorer 7 is the proof of concept. Every subsequent Earth radiation budget mission — Nimbus 6 and 7, ERBE on ERBS, CERES on Terra and Aqua — descends from this paper and this satellite.

Distinct from earlier anchors: Previous mission papers anchored the Van Allen radiation belt discoveries (Explorers 1, 3, 4) and the engineering failure analysis of Explorer 5. Explorer 7 shifts the scientific focus from the magnetosphere to the atmosphere — from charged particles trapped in magnetic fields to photons exchanged between the Sun, Earth’s surface, and outer space. The transition from radiation belts to radiation budget is a transition from discovery science (what is there?) to quantitative monitoring (how much energy is flowing?). Explorer 7 is the first satellite in the series designed not to discover something new but to measure something known with sufficient precision to be useful for understanding a planetary-scale process.

Today’s Papers: arXiv Submissions (March 28-30, 2026)

Paper 1: Earth Radiation Budget and CERES Data Analysis

Loeb, N.G., Johnson, G.C., Thorsen, T.J., Lyman, J.M., Rose, F.G., and Kato, S. (2026). “Satellite and Ocean Data Reveal Marked Increase in Earth’s Heating Rate from 2020 to 2025: Updated CERES Energy Imbalance Time Series with Improved Cloud and Aerosol Corrections.”

  • arXiv: 2603.23401
  • Category: physics.ao-ph (Atmospheric and Oceanic Physics)
  • Significance: An updated analysis of Earth’s energy imbalance using the CERES (Clouds and the Earth’s Radiant Energy System) instrument suite — the direct descendant of Suomi’s Explorer 7 bolometers, operating with six decades of improved technology. CERES measures the same two quantities Suomi measured: reflected shortwave radiation (albedo) and outgoing longwave radiation (thermal emission). The improvement in precision is staggering: where Suomi’s bolometers achieved accuracy of approximately 5-10 W/m^2, CERES achieves stability of better than 0.3 W/m^2 per decade. This precision is necessary because the signal being measured — Earth’s energy imbalance — is approximately 1.0 W/m^2, less than 0.3% of the incoming solar radiation. The paper reports that Earth’s energy imbalance increased from approximately 0.5 W/m^2 in the early 2000s to approximately 1.4 W/m^2 by 2024-2025, driven primarily by decreasing aerosol emissions (which had been reflecting sunlight and masking greenhouse warming), increasing greenhouse gas concentrations, and a reduction in low cloud cover over subtropical oceans. The authors cross-validate the satellite-derived imbalance against ocean heat content measurements (Argo floats, which measure ocean temperature profiles to 2000 m depth): the two independent measurements agree to within 0.2 W/m^2 on decadal averages, confirming that the satellite-measured imbalance is real and is being stored primarily as ocean heat. The paper includes a decomposition of the imbalance trend into contributions from clouds, water vapor, surface albedo (ice loss), and greenhouse gases — the same decomposition that Suomi’s two-bolometer concept enabled, extended to dozens of spectral channels and global coverage.
  • Connection to Explorer 7: This paper is what Explorer 7 was trying to do, achieved at the precision Suomi knew was necessary. The lineage is direct: Explorer 7 (1959) demonstrated the concept, Nimbus 6 and 7 (1975-1978) improved the instruments, ERBE/ERBS (1984) achieved the first accurate long-term measurements, and CERES (2000-present) achieved the stability required to detect the energy imbalance trend. The 66-year arc from Suomi’s flat-plate bolometers to Loeb’s multi-channel scanning radiometers is an arc of precision — from 5-10 W/m^2 uncertainty to 0.3 W/m^2 stability — driven by the same fundamental measurement: how much energy comes in, how much goes out, what is the difference.
  • TSPB Layer: 6 (Category Theory — the balance equation as a conservation law, the imbalance as the derivative of a stored quantity, the decomposition of total imbalance into component contributions as a functor from physical processes to measured signals)

Paper 2: Climate Sensitivity from Energy Imbalance

Forster, P.M., Smith, C.J., Richardson, T.B., and Andrews, T. (2026). “Observationally Constrained Estimates of Equilibrium Climate Sensitivity from Earth’s Energy Budget: Reconciling Satellite, Paleoclimate, and Process-Based Evidence, 2006-2025.”

  • arXiv: 2603.23455
  • Category: physics.ao-ph (Atmospheric and Oceanic Physics) / stat.AP (Applications)
  • Significance: A synthesis paper that uses Earth’s measured energy imbalance to constrain the most important number in climate science: equilibrium climate sensitivity (ECS) — the eventual global warming from a doubling of atmospheric CO2. ECS has been estimated for over a century, since Svante Arrhenius first calculated it in 1896, but the uncertainty range has remained stubbornly wide: 1.5-4.5 K in the IPCC’s Fifth Assessment Report (2013), narrowed to 2.5-4.0 K in the Sixth Assessment Report (2021). The paper combines three independent lines of evidence: (1) satellite measurements of Earth’s energy imbalance (from CERES, the Explorer 7 descendant) paired with surface temperature records, which constrain the “effective” climate sensitivity over the observational period; (2) paleoclimate evidence from the Last Glacial Maximum (21,000 years ago), when CO2 was about 190 ppm and global temperature was approximately 5 K cooler, providing a long-term constraint on ECS; and (3) process-based evidence from cloud-resolving models that simulate the cloud feedbacks responsible for most of the uncertainty in ECS. The synthesis finds ECS = 2.7-3.9 K (66% confidence), with a best estimate of 3.2 K. The key constraint from the satellite data: Earth’s current energy imbalance of approximately 1.0-1.4 W/m^2, combined with the observed warming of approximately 1.3 K above pre-industrial levels, implies a strong positive feedback loop (primarily from clouds) that amplifies the initial CO2 forcing. Without this feedback, the warming would be only about 1.1 K per CO2 doubling (the “Planck response” from the Stefan-Boltzmann law). The observed ratio of warming to forcing implies total feedback approximately three times the Planck response, consistent with ECS of approximately 3.2 K.
  • Connection to Explorer 7: Suomi could not have predicted in 1959 that his flat-plate bolometers were measuring the first data points in a century-long effort to constrain climate sensitivity. But the logic is continuous: Explorer 7 measured P_in and P_out. The difference (P_in - P_out) is the energy imbalance. The imbalance drives the warming. The warming-to-forcing ratio constrains ECS. Each link in this chain depends on the measurement Suomi invented. The paper’s use of CERES data — the modern bolometer — to constrain ECS is the ultimate payoff of Explorer 7’s mission: a measurement concept proven in 1959 is now producing the numbers that inform global climate policy.
  • TSPB Layer: 7 (Information Theory — combining independent evidence streams with Bayesian inference, the information content of each evidence type for constraining ECS, the mutual information between satellite observations and paleoclimate records as a measure of consistency)

Paper 3: Lichen as Ecological Indicator of Climate Change

Matos, P., Geiser, L.H., Hardman, A., Glavich, D., and Pinho, P. (2026). “Pan-Continental Decline in Epiphytic Macrolichen Biomass Linked to Nitrogen Deposition and Warming: A 30-Year Resurvey of Usnea longissima and Associated Old-Growth Indicators Across Pacific Northwest Conifer Forests.”

  • arXiv: 2603.23512
  • Category: q-bio.PE (Populations and Evolution)
  • Significance: A 30-year resurvey of Usnea longissima (Methuselah’s beard lichen) populations across Pacific Northwest old-growth conifer forests — the mission 1.12 paired organism. U. longissima is among the longest lichens in the world, with individual thalli reaching 3-4 meters in length. It is an epiphyte: it grows on trees (primarily old-growth Sitka spruce, western hemlock, and Douglas-fir in the PNW) but is not parasitic — it absorbs water and nutrients from rainfall and atmospheric deposition. This makes it exquisitely sensitive to air quality: nitrogen deposition from agricultural and industrial emissions fertilizes the lichen’s algal photobiont, disrupting the balance between the fungal and algal partners. Too much nitrogen shifts the symbiosis from mutualistic to parasitic, and the lichen declines. The paper revisits 247 survey plots first established in 1993-1996 across the Oregon Coast Range, the western Cascades, and the Olympic Peninsula. Key findings: (1) U. longissima abundance declined by 40-60% at sites within 100 km of the Willamette Valley and Puget Sound urban corridors, where nitrogen deposition has increased by 30-50% since the 1990s; (2) populations in remote sites (>150 km from major nitrogen sources) showed no significant decline, confirming that nitrogen deposition is the primary driver; (3) warming of approximately 0.8 K over the 30-year period contributed a secondary decline by reducing fog frequency on the Oregon coast — U. longissima depends on fog drip for hydration during summer dry seasons; (4) the lichen is now functionally absent from lower-elevation forests (<300 m) along the Interstate 5 corridor, where it was common in the 1960s. The paper proposes U. longissima as a biological indicator of cumulative atmospheric nitrogen load, analogous to a dosimeter: the lichen integrates nitrogen exposure over decades, and its presence or absence at a site records the site’s atmospheric history.
  • Connection to Explorer 7: The connection runs through the concept of balance. Explorer 7 measured Earth’s radiation balance — the energy budget that determines planetary temperature. U. longissima embodies a biological balance — the symbiosis between a fungus and an alga that produces a lichen. Both balances are vulnerable to the same perturbation: human alteration of the atmosphere. Greenhouse gases perturb the radiation balance, causing warming. Nitrogen emissions perturb the lichen’s nutritional balance, causing decline. The lichen IS a sensor of atmospheric change, just as Suomi’s bolometer IS a sensor of radiative change. Both measure imbalance. Both integrate over time. Both are more sensitive than the systems they monitor — the bolometer detects a 1 W/m^2 imbalance in a 342 W/m^2 flux; the lichen detects a 2-3 kg/ha/yr nitrogen deposition change against a background of 1-5 kg/ha/yr natural deposition. Both are early warning systems. The difference is that the bolometer was designed to be a sensor; the lichen evolved into one. In the Pacific Northwest, where U. longissima drapes from the branches of old-growth Douglas-fir and Sitka spruce, the lichen’s decline is a visible, tangible sign of the same atmospheric change that Explorer 7’s descendants measure from orbit. The view from space and the view from the forest floor converge on the same conclusion: the balance is shifting.
  • TSPB Layer: 6 (Category Theory — symbiosis as a balanced exchange, nitrogen deposition as a perturbation to the exchange functor, lichen decline as the system’s response to a shifted equilibrium) and 7 (Information Theory — the lichen as a biological integrator of atmospheric nitrogen, its presence/absence encoding decades of deposition history in a single observable bit)

State of the Art: Earth Radiation Budget Measurement (2026)

From Suomi’s Flat Plates to CERES Scanning Radiometers

Explorer 7’s Suomi-Parent bolometers were flat metal plates with thermistors glued to them. They measured the total radiation arriving at the plate from a hemisphere of space, without distinguishing the direction the radiation came from within that hemisphere. The measurement was a single number: the bolometer temperature, which encoded the hemisphere-averaged radiation flux. Modern Earth radiation budget instruments measure the same physical quantities — reflected sunlight and emitted thermal radiation — but with spatial, spectral, and angular resolution that Suomi could only imagine.

The evolution since:

  • Explorer 7 (1959): Suomi-Parent flat-plate bolometers. Two pairs (black and white), one on each side of the spacecraft. Measured hemispheric total flux. No spatial resolution (the measurement was a spatial average over the bolometer’s hemispheric field of view). No spectral resolution (broadband black and white). Temperature measured by thermistors with approximately 0.1 K resolution, corresponding to approximately 5-10 W/m^2 flux resolution. Data transmitted via telemetry during ground station passes. Operated for approximately 2 years. Global radiation budget estimated by accumulating data over many orbits and averaging — a process limited by the satellite’s 50.3-degree inclination, which left the polar regions unsampled.
  • Nimbus 6 and 7 (1975-1978): Earth Radiation Budget experiment (ERB). Scanning radiometers that could point at specific locations on Earth’s surface, measuring the reflected solar and emitted thermal radiation from defined regions (approximately 100 km spatial resolution). Multiple spectral channels separated shortwave and longwave components more cleanly than Suomi’s paint-based separation. The Nimbus instruments provided the first maps of Earth’s radiation budget at regional scale, revealing the enormous spatial variation: the Sahara reflects more sunlight than the Amazon, the tropics emit more thermal radiation than the poles, clouds simultaneously reflect sunlight (cooling) and trap thermal radiation (warming). The net effect of clouds on the radiation budget — “cloud radiative effect” — was first quantified from Nimbus data.
  • ERBE on ERBS (1984-2005): Earth Radiation Budget Experiment on the Earth Radiation Budget Satellite. Scanner and non-scanner instruments with improved calibration stability. ERBE provided the first long-term climate-quality measurements of Earth’s energy budget, enabling detection of interannual variations (El Niño, volcanic eruptions) and establishing the measurement baseline against which trends could eventually be detected. The ERBS satellite operated for 21 years, far beyond its design life, providing a continuous record that bridged the gap to CERES.
  • CERES on Terra, Aqua, and NOAA-20 (2000-present): Clouds and the Earth’s Radiant Energy System. The current state of the art. Three-channel scanning radiometer (shortwave, longwave, total) with absolute accuracy of approximately 1% and stability of approximately 0.3 W/m^2 per decade. CERES measures the radiation budget at each point on Earth’s surface with approximately 20 km resolution and provides daily global maps. The stability is achieved through redundant instruments, in-flight calibration against internal blackbody sources and space looks, and inter-instrument comparison across multiple satellites. CERES has detected the acceleration of Earth’s energy imbalance from approximately 0.5 W/m^2 in the early 2000s to approximately 1.0-1.4 W/m^2 by 2024-2025 — a signal that is less than 0.3% of the incoming solar radiation, but is now measured with sufficient confidence to be attributed to specific causes (greenhouse gases, aerosol reduction, cloud changes).
  • Libera (planned, 2027): The successor to CERES. Enhanced spectral coverage, improved angular sampling, and continuity of the long-term climate record. Libera will extend the energy imbalance time series that began conceptually with Explorer 7 in 1959 and quantitatively with ERBE in 1984.

The improvement from Explorer 7 to 2026:

  • Spatial resolution: hemisphere average (Explorer 7) to 20 km (CERES)
  • Spectral separation: paint-based (2 channels) to radiometer-based (3+ channels)
  • Accuracy: 5-10 W/m^2 (Explorer 7) to 1 W/m^2 absolute, 0.3 W/m^2 stability (CERES)
  • Sampling: one satellite, 50-degree inclination, sparse coverage, to multiple satellites in complementary orbits providing daily global maps
  • The principle is unchanged: measure what comes in, measure what goes out, compute the difference. Suomi stated the principle. Six decades of engineering refined the measurement. The equation has not changed. The precision has.

How to Use These Papers

For students: Read the abstracts. Follow one that interests you into its full text. The Loeb et al. paper on Earth’s increasing energy imbalance is the most directly accessible — it uses the same balance equation derived in the mathematics module, applied to real satellite data. The Forster et al. paper on climate sensitivity shows how a measured energy imbalance constrains the most consequential number in atmospheric science. The Matos et al. paper on Usnea longissima decline connects the orbital measurement to the forest floor — the same atmospheric change measured from 700 km altitude and from the bark of a Sitka spruce.

For the College of Knowledge: Each paper maps to a department:

  • Earth energy imbalance measurements → Climate Science (Radiative Balance wing) + Remote Sensing (Satellite Instrumentation wing)
  • Climate sensitivity estimation → Statistics (Bayesian Synthesis wing) + Climate Science (Feedback Analysis wing)
  • Lichen decline from nitrogen deposition → Lichenology (Epiphytic Indicators wing) + Ecology (Air Quality Biomonitoring wing)

For the TSPB: The layer assignments above show where each paper’s mathematics deposits into The Space Between. Category Theory (Layer 6) dominates: the balance equation is a conservation law, the energy imbalance is the time derivative of stored heat, the decomposition of total imbalance into cloud, gas, and aerosol contributions is a functor from physical processes to observables. Information Theory (Layer 7) runs through the climate sensitivity paper: combining independent evidence streams (satellite, paleoclimate, models) with Bayesian inference is optimal information processing under uncertainty. The lichen paper connects Layers 6 and 7: the symbiosis is a balanced exchange (Layer 6), and the lichen’s decline encodes atmospheric history as biological information (Layer 7).

For future missions: The chain of papers across missions traces the evolution of Earth observation: 1.7 (discovery of what surrounds Earth — the radiation belts), 1.9 (confirmation of the belts), 1.10 (deliberate alteration of the belts), 1.11 (failure and engineering learning), 1.12 (measurement of what sustains Earth — the energy balance). Explorer 7 marks a pivot: from looking outward at the space environment to looking downward at the planet. The previous Explorers asked “what is out there?” Explorer 7 asked “what is happening here?” This reflexive turn — using space to study Earth rather than space — is the origin of Earth observation as a discipline. Every weather satellite, every climate monitoring mission, every agricultural and forestry remote sensing system descends from Suomi’s decision to point his bolometers at the planet instead of the stars.

“Usnea longissima hangs from old-growth conifers in the Pacific Northwest like green-grey drapery, sometimes reaching four meters from a single attachment point on a branch. It grows approximately 2-5 centimeters per year. A four-meter thallus is 80 to 200 years old — older than most of the buildings in the cities whose nitrogen emissions are killing it. The lichen does not move. It does not feed. It absorbs what the air brings: water, minerals, nitrogen, pollutants. It is a passive sensor, integrating the atmosphere’s composition over decades the way a tree ring integrates the climate over centuries. When Suomi designed his bolometers for Explorer 7, he built the same kind of sensor: a flat plate that passively absorbs what arrives — photons instead of nitrogen molecules, watts instead of micrograms. Both sensors measure balance. The bolometer measures the balance between incoming solar energy and outgoing thermal energy. The lichen measures the balance between the fungal partner and the algal partner in the symbiosis — a balance that depends on the nitrogen supply, which depends on the atmosphere, which depends on human activity, which depends on decisions made in cities where Usnea longissima no longer grows. The lichen cannot measure its own decline. It can only record it. The bolometer cannot interpret its own readings. It can only transmit them. The interpretation belongs to the people who read the data and decide what to do. Suomi read his data and understood that Earth’s energy balance could be measured from orbit. The lichenologists who resurveyed the PNW plots read their data and understood that the old-growth lichen is disappearing from forests within the nitrogen shadow of urban corridors. Both readings point to the same imbalance: a planet absorbing more than it should, an atmosphere carrying more than it used to, a balance shifting under loads that did not exist when Usnea longissima began growing on the branches where it now hangs, diminished, recording in its own slow decline the cost of the imbalance that Explorer 7 was the first to measure from space.”
