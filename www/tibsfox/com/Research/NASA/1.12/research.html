Deep Research: The Thermometer

Explorer 7 — first satellite to measure Earth’s radiation budget

Date: October 13, 1959 Program: Explorer (NASA/Goddard) Organism: Methuselah’s beard lichen Dedication: Rudolf Virchow

The Question Nobody Had Asked From Above

On October 13, 1959, a Juno II launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 5 at Cape Canaveral carrying a satellite designated Explorer 7. The satellite weighed approximately 41.5 kilograms — nearly four times the mass of Explorer 1, the tiny cylinder that had opened the American space age twenty months earlier. Explorer 7 was, in almost every meaningful sense, a different kind of object from the earlier Explorers. Not merely larger, though it was that. Not merely more capable, though it was that too. It was a different kind of question, asked from a different kind of institution, aimed at a different kind of answer.

The earlier Explorers had asked: what is out there? Explorer 1 had discovered the Van Allen radiation belts. Explorer 3 had profiled them. Explorer 4 had measured the radiation from nuclear detonations conducted in the magnetosphere. Explorer 5 had failed to reach orbit. These missions had looked outward and upward, into the radiation environment of near-Earth space, mapping a phenomenon that nobody had suspected existed until a Geiger counter saturated in orbit and James Van Allen realized the silence was the signal. The radiation belts were a discovery about the space environment — about what lay beyond the atmosphere, about the energetic particles trapped by Earth’s magnetic field, about the invisible architecture of the near-Earth cosmos.

Explorer 7 turned around and looked down.

Not at the surface — not at geography, not at weather patterns, not at the visible features of the planet. Explorer 7 looked at the energy. It asked the question that nobody had yet asked from orbit: how much energy does Earth receive from the Sun, and how much energy does Earth radiate back into space? What is the balance? Is it positive — more energy coming in than going out, the planet warming? Is it negative — more energy leaving than arriving, the planet cooling? Or is it in equilibrium — the energy budget balanced, the temperature stable, the system in steady state?

This is the radiation budget. It is the single most fundamental quantity in climate science. Every other climate variable — temperature, humidity, wind speed, ocean current, ice sheet extent, sea level, atmospheric composition — is a consequence of the radiation budget. The radiation budget is the forcing function. Everything else is the response. If you do not know the radiation budget, you do not know the climate. You cannot predict the climate. You cannot model the climate. You cannot understand why the climate changes, or in which direction, or by how much, because you do not know the input that drives the entire system. The radiation budget is to climate science what the heart rate is to cardiology: the first vital sign, the measurement without which no diagnosis is possible.

And on October 13, 1959, Verner Suomi’s instruments rode into orbit aboard Explorer 7 and began measuring it for the first time in the history of the species.

Goddard’s First Satellite

Explorer 7 was the first satellite designed and built at the Goddard Space Flight Center. This fact, easy to pass over in a catalog listing, was a marker of institutional transformation. The earlier Explorers — 1 through 5 — had been built at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, California, under the management of the Army Ballistic Missile Agency and later the Advanced Research Projects Agency. JPL was a military contractor that had been transferred to NASA’s authority on December 3, 1958, two months after the new agency began operations. The Explorer satellites that JPL built were direct descendants of the Army’s crash program to answer Sputnik: small, fast, built from whatever was available, launched on modified ballistic missiles. They were brilliant improvisations. They were not planned science missions in the sense that a civilian research agency would plan them.

Goddard Space Flight Center, established on May 1, 1959, in Greenbelt, Maryland, was NASA’s first new research center — not a facility inherited from the military or transferred from another agency, but built from the ground up as a civilian institution for space science. Goddard’s charter was to develop and manage unmanned scientific spacecraft, and Explorer 7 was its first product. The satellite was designed to answer scientific questions, not to demonstrate launch capability or to respond to a geopolitical crisis. Its instruments were selected through a process that, while still informal by later standards, involved scientific peer review and competitive evaluation. The experiments were proposed by university scientists, evaluated by NASA review panels, and selected on the basis of scientific merit and technical readiness.

This mattered because it changed what the instruments were designed to measure. The Army/JPL Explorers had carried radiation detectors because the radiation environment was unknown and unmapped — the measurements were exploratory in the purest sense, seeking to characterize a phenomenon whose existence had barely been suspected. Explorer 7’s instruments were designed to answer specific, pre-formulated scientific questions. Suomi’s thermal radiation balance experiment was not a survey instrument. It was an answer to a question that had been asked in theoretical climate physics for decades: what is Earth’s radiation budget, measured globally, from above?

The distinction between exploring and measuring, between surveying and answering, between discovering what exists and quantifying what you already suspect — this is the distinction between the first phase of any scientific endeavor and the second. The first phase asks “what is there?” The second phase asks “how much?” Explorer 7 was a second-phase mission for a question that had never had a first phase, because the first phase required being in orbit, and nobody had been in orbit long enough to ask it properly until now.

The Suomi-Parent Experiment

Verner Edward Suomi was a professor of meteorology at the University of Wisconsin-Madison. He was not famous in 1959. He would become famous — he would become, by the end of his career, the father of satellite meteorology, a member of the National Academy of Sciences, a recipient of the National Medal of Science, and the person most responsible for the chain of geostationary weather satellites that now provides continuous monitoring of Earth’s atmosphere. But in 1959, he was a faculty member with an idea and a set of instruments that embodied that idea in aluminum and black paint.

The idea was simple in concept and subtle in execution: measure the total energy entering and leaving the Earth system by placing calibrated thermal detectors in orbit. The detectors would measure radiative flux — the power per unit area arriving at the satellite from all directions below (Earth’s outgoing radiation) and from above (incoming solar radiation). The difference between incoming and outgoing was the radiation budget. If more energy arrived than departed, the planet was storing heat. If more energy departed than arrived, the planet was losing heat. If the two were equal, the planet was in thermal equilibrium.

The instruments Suomi designed with his colleague Robert Parent were a matched pair of hemispheric bolometers — half-spheres of thin metal, approximately 12 centimeters in diameter, mounted on the exterior of the satellite. One hemisphere was painted white. The other was painted black.

The physics was elegant. A black surface absorbs all wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation — both the shortwave radiation from the Sun (visible light and near-infrared) and the longwave radiation emitted by the Earth (thermal infrared). A white surface reflects shortwave radiation and absorbs only longwave radiation. By measuring the temperature of each hemisphere, and knowing the thermal properties of the materials, Suomi could separate the two components of Earth’s outgoing radiation: the reflected sunlight (shortwave, visible in the difference between the black and white sensors) and the emitted thermal radiation (longwave, visible in the white sensor’s signal alone, since the white hemisphere rejected solar input).

The separation of reflected and emitted radiation was the entire point. Earth loses energy to space in two ways. First, it reflects some fraction of the incoming sunlight directly back into space — this is the albedo, the reflectivity of the planet as a whole, determined by clouds, ice sheets, deserts, oceans, and the atmosphere itself. Reflected sunlight carries no information about Earth’s temperature; it is simply bounced away, unchanged, as if the planet were a mirror. Second, the Earth emits thermal radiation — infrared radiation whose intensity depends on the temperature of the emitting surface or atmosphere. Thermal emission is the planet’s cooling mechanism. It is the radiation that carries heat away from Earth and into space. The balance between incoming solar radiation, reflected solar radiation, and emitted thermal radiation determines the planet’s temperature.

Suomi’s pair of bolometers — one black, one white — was a machine for dissecting this three-way balance. The black hemisphere saw everything: incoming solar, reflected solar, and thermal emission. The white hemisphere saw only thermal emission, because it reflected the solar component. The difference between the two readings was the solar contribution. The white reading alone was the thermal contribution. From two numbers and the known solar input, the entire radiation budget could be reconstructed.

The instruments were not sophisticated by later standards. They had no spectral resolution — they measured total flux, not flux as a function of wavelength. They had limited spatial resolution — the hemispheres saw a broad swath of the Earth below, not individual clouds or surface features. Their calibration depended on the paint remaining stable in the harsh radiation and thermal environment of orbit — a non-trivial assumption, since ultraviolet radiation degrades paint surfaces and micrometeorite impacts can alter their reflective properties. But the instruments worked. They measured real energy fluxes. They provided the first data points in a record that, sixty-seven years later, remains the most important continuous measurement in climate science.

The Launch Vehicle and the Orbit

Explorer 7 launched on a Juno II rocket, which was significantly more capable than the Juno I that had launched Explorers 1 through 5. The Juno II used a PGM-19 Jupiter intermediate-range ballistic missile as its first stage, rather than the smaller Redstone missile that formed the Juno I’s first stage. The Jupiter first stage produced approximately 667 kilonewtons of thrust — roughly three times the Redstone’s thrust — and could loft substantially heavier payloads to orbit. The upper stages retained the same spinning-cluster configuration of scaled Sergeant solid rocket motors that the Juno I used, but the greater first-stage performance meant the upper stages did not need to contribute as much velocity, which improved the mass budget for the satellite.

The orbit Explorer 7 achieved was 573 by 1,073 kilometers, with a period of 101.4 minutes and an inclination of 50.27 degrees. This orbit was well-suited to the radiation budget mission. The moderate altitude kept the satellite below the most intense regions of the inner Van Allen radiation belt, reducing radiation damage to the instruments. The inclination of 50.27 degrees meant the satellite’s ground track covered a wide swath of the Earth’s surface, from 50 degrees north latitude to 50 degrees south — encompassing the tropics, the subtropics, and the mid-latitudes, where the majority of the planet’s energy exchange occurs. The orbital period of approximately 101 minutes meant the satellite completed roughly 14 orbits per day, with each orbit shifted slightly westward relative to the previous one due to Earth’s rotation, so that over the course of several days the satellite’s ground track covered essentially the entire surface between 50 degrees north and south.

This coverage pattern was essential for the radiation budget measurement. The radiation budget varies enormously across the Earth’s surface. The tropics receive far more solar radiation than the poles. Cloud-covered regions reflect more sunlight than clear-sky regions. Deserts emit thermal radiation at different rates than oceans. Ice sheets have different albedos than forests. To measure the global radiation budget, you need global coverage — not a snapshot of one location, but an accumulation of measurements from every latitude, every longitude, every time of day, every season. Explorer 7’s orbit provided this accumulation. Over its two-year operational lifetime, the satellite built up a dataset that, while sparse by modern standards, was the first global picture of Earth’s energy balance ever obtained.

The Other Instruments

Explorer 7 was not solely a radiation budget mission. It carried additional experiments that reflected the breadth of Goddard’s scientific interests in the early space program.

A heavy cosmic ray nuclei experiment, designed by Martin Brill of the University of New Hampshire, used nuclear emulsion plates to detect and identify the nuclei of heavy elements in the cosmic ray flux. Cosmic rays are high-energy particles — mostly protons and helium nuclei, but with a small fraction of heavier nuclei up to and including iron and beyond — that originate outside the solar system, accelerated to enormous energies by supernovae, pulsars, and other galactic sources. The heavy nuclei are particularly interesting because their composition carries information about the nuclear processes in the stars that produced them. Brill’s emulsions recorded the tracks of heavy nuclei that penetrated the satellite’s skin, and the emulsions were designed to be recovered and analyzed after the mission. In practice, since Explorer 7 was not a recoverable satellite, the emulsion data was limited to what could be inferred from the particle flux measurements telemetered back to Earth.

A Lyman-alpha detector, contributed by the Naval Research Laboratory, measured solar ultraviolet radiation at the hydrogen Lyman-alpha wavelength of 121.6 nanometers. This wavelength is completely absorbed by Earth’s atmosphere and cannot be measured from the ground. Lyman-alpha radiation is emitted by hydrogen atoms in the solar chromosphere, and its intensity varies with solar activity — more active Sun, more Lyman-alpha. The Lyman-alpha measurement complemented the radiation budget experiment by characterizing the solar input side of the energy balance: not just the total solar flux, but the ultraviolet component that drives photochemistry in the upper atmosphere and that varies more dramatically with solar activity than the total solar output.

Micrometeorite detectors, designed to measure the flux and size distribution of tiny particles impacting the satellite, provided data on the near-Earth dust environment. These detectors used piezoelectric sensors that generated electrical signals when struck by particles, allowing the satellite to count impacts and estimate particle masses. The micrometeorite data was of practical as well as scientific interest: understanding the dust flux was essential for designing future satellites, since micrometeorite impacts could damage optical surfaces, solar cells, and thermal control coatings — including, potentially, the paint on Suomi’s bolometers.

The Father of Cellular Pathology

Rudolf Ludwig Carl Virchow was born on October 13, 1821, in Schivelbein, Pomerania — the same calendar date, one hundred and thirty-eight years before Explorer 7 launched. The dedication is calendrical, but the resonance between Virchow and Explorer 7 runs deeper than the calendar, into the architecture of how systems are understood when you look at them at the right level.

Virchow was a physician, a pathologist, a politician, a public health reformer, an anthropologist, and an archaeologist. He is remembered primarily for one idea, expressed in three Latin words that he did not coin but that he established as the foundation of modern pathology: Omnis cellula e cellula. Every cell from a cell. All cells arise from pre-existing cells. There is no spontaneous generation at the cellular level. Disease does not arise from miasmas or humoral imbalances or moral failings — disease arises from changes in cells, from disruptions in the normal processes of cell division and cell function, from the pathology of the fundamental units of life.

Before Virchow, pathology was primarily an organ-level discipline. Physicians recognized diseased organs — a cirrhotic liver, an emphysematous lung, a cancerous stomach — and classified diseases by their organ-level manifestations. Virchow, using the microscope, pushed the analysis one level deeper. He showed that the organ-level changes were consequences of cell-level changes. The cirrhotic liver was cirrhotic because its hepatocytes were damaged and replaced by fibrous tissue. The cancerous stomach was cancerous because a population of cells had begun dividing without control. The disease was not in the organ. The disease was in the cells. The organ was merely the scale at which the cellular pathology became visible to the unaided eye.

This shift in analytical level — from the organ to the cell, from the visible to the microscopic, from the aggregate to the fundamental unit — is precisely the shift that Explorer 7 performed for climate science. Before Explorer 7, the climate was understood at the weather level: temperature records from surface stations, precipitation measurements from rain gauges, wind observations from weather balloons, cloud observations from ground-based meteorologists. These measurements described the symptoms of the climate system — what the weather was doing at particular places and times. But they did not reveal the underlying forcing that drove the system. They did not measure the radiation budget.

Suomi’s bolometers looked at the climate system the way Virchow’s microscope looked at the diseased organ. They pushed the analysis one level deeper, from the symptoms to the cause. The radiation budget is to climate what the cell is to pathology: the fundamental unit of analysis, the level at which the system’s behavior is determined. Surface temperature is a consequence of the radiation budget, just as organ pathology is a consequence of cellular pathology. You can describe climate by its surface manifestations — warm here, cold there, raining, not raining — but you cannot explain it without knowing the energy balance. You can describe disease by its organ-level manifestations — swollen liver, collapsed lung, spreading tumor — but you cannot explain it without knowing what the cells are doing.

Virchow’s insight required a technology — the microscope — that allowed him to see what was invisible to the unaided eye. Suomi’s insight required a technology — the orbiting satellite — that allowed him to measure what was inaccessible from the surface. Both men needed a platform that elevated them above the resolution limit of existing observation. Virchow needed to see smaller than the organ. Suomi needed to see larger than the weather station. Both achieved their insight by changing the scale of observation, and both discovered that the scale at which the system was understood was not the scale at which the system was determined.

Virchow was also, and perhaps equally importantly, a political reformer and a pioneer of social medicine. In 1848, at the age of twenty-six, he was sent by the Prussian government to investigate a typhus epidemic in Upper Silesia. His report was incendiary. He concluded that the epidemic was not primarily a medical problem but a social one: the people of Upper Silesia were sick because they were poor, malnourished, poorly housed, uneducated, and politically disenfranchised. The solution was not more doctors but more democracy, more education, more economic opportunity, more political power for the people who were dying. “Medicine is a social science,” Virchow wrote, “and politics is nothing else but medicine on a large scale.”

This insistence that the root cause of a health crisis lay not in the immediate pathogen but in the social conditions that made the population vulnerable — this refusal to stop at the proximate cause and the demand to trace the chain of causation back to the systemic failure — is mirrored in the way Explorer 7’s radiation budget data eventually became foundational for understanding anthropogenic climate change. The radiation budget is the proximate mechanism of climate. But the forcing that alters the radiation budget — the increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide from fossil fuel combustion, the changes in land use that alter surface albedo, the industrial emissions that modify atmospheric aerosols — these are social and economic phenomena. The radiation budget changes because human societies burn coal and oil and gas. The climate changes because the radiation budget changes. The chain of causation runs from the social to the physical to the climatic, and Virchow, who insisted that the chain of causation for typhus ran from the political to the social to the biological, would have recognized the structure immediately. The disease is in the cells. The cells are sick because of the conditions. The conditions are set by the society. Fix the society or the cells will keep dying.

Two Years of Continuous Measurement

Explorer 7 operated for approximately two years, transmitting data from October 1959 through the summer of 1961. This was an extraordinary operational lifetime for a satellite of its era. The earlier Explorers had operated for months at most — Explorer 1 transmitted for about four months before its battery-powered transmitter fell silent, though it continued to orbit until 1970. Explorer 7’s extended operation was partly a result of its design (it carried more capable power systems than the earlier, smaller Explorers) and partly a matter of orbital mechanics (its orbit was high enough to avoid significant atmospheric drag, which would have shortened its lifetime through orbital decay).

The two-year dataset was critical. A single orbit’s worth of radiation budget data would be meaningless — the energy balance varies with latitude, longitude, time of day, season, cloud cover, and surface type. A day’s worth of data would be biased by whatever weather happened to be occurring that day. A month’s worth would begin to average out the weather noise but would still be dominated by the seasonal signal. Only a multi-year dataset could begin to separate the signal from the noise, to reveal the underlying energy balance beneath the daily and seasonal variations.

Suomi’s data showed that, over the period of measurement, Earth’s radiation budget was approximately in balance — the incoming solar energy was roughly equal to the outgoing reflected and emitted energy, averaged over the globe and over time. This was not a surprise. The climate had been approximately stable for the preceding several thousand years, which implied an approximately balanced radiation budget. But “not a surprise” and “not important” are different statements. Confirming the approximate balance was essential because it established the baseline. You cannot detect a change without knowing the baseline. You cannot measure a perturbation without knowing the unperturbed state. Explorer 7’s data was the first empirical confirmation that Earth’s radiation budget was approximately balanced, and it was the first measurement against which future perturbations could be compared.

The data also revealed spatial and temporal patterns in the radiation budget that had been predicted but never measured. The tropics had a radiation surplus — more energy coming in than going out, because the Sun shines more directly on the tropics and the outgoing radiation does not fully compensate. The polar regions had a radiation deficit — more energy going out than coming in, because the low Sun angle reduces the solar input and the outgoing thermal radiation from the cold polar surfaces, while low, is still larger than the meager solar input. The surplus in the tropics and the deficit at the poles is the fundamental driver of atmospheric and oceanic circulation: heat flows from where there is too much to where there is too little, and the winds and currents that carry it are the weather and climate that we experience.

The Lineage

Explorer 7’s radiation budget measurement was the first in a lineage that continues to this day. Suomi himself went on to design instruments for the Nimbus satellite series, which carried increasingly sophisticated radiation budget experiments through the 1960s and 1970s. The Earth Radiation Budget Experiment (ERBE) flew aboard multiple satellites in the 1980s and 1990s, providing the first high-quality, long-term global radiation budget dataset. The Clouds and the Earth’s Radiant Energy System (CERES) instruments, first launched in 1997 on the Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission and subsequently on multiple NASA satellites, continue to measure Earth’s radiation budget with a precision and spatial resolution that Suomi’s hemispheric bolometers could not have approached.

The CERES data, accumulated over more than two decades, has confirmed what Explorer 7’s two-year dataset first hinted at: Earth’s radiation budget is not in perfect balance. The planet is absorbing slightly more energy than it emits. The imbalance is small — roughly 0.5 to 1 watt per square meter, out of a total incoming solar flux of approximately 340 watts per square meter — but it is persistent, and it is growing. The cause is the enhanced greenhouse effect: the increase in atmospheric carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases has reduced the amount of thermal radiation that escapes to space, trapping energy in the Earth system, warming the oceans, melting the ice, raising the sea level. The radiation budget imbalance is the smoking gun of anthropogenic climate change, and the instrument that first detected the baseline from which the imbalance deviates was Verner Suomi’s pair of painted hemispheres, riding aboard Explorer 7 in an orbit 573 by 1,073 kilometers above the surface.

The chain from Suomi’s bolometers to modern CERES instruments is unbroken. Every Earth radiation budget satellite traces its intellectual lineage to Explorer 7. The question that Suomi asked — what is the balance between energy in and energy out? — is the same question that CERES answers every day, with better instruments, better calibration, better spatial resolution, and better algorithms, but the same question. The measurement that Explorer 7 made first, crudely, with two painted half-spheres and a telemetry system that could transmit a few hundred bits per second, is the same measurement that modern instruments make continuously, with spectral resolution and angular resolution and calibration accuracy that Suomi could not have imagined. The quality has improved by orders of magnitude. The question has not changed at all.

This is the nature of foundational measurements. They do not become obsolete. They become baselines. Explorer 7’s data is still cited in climate science literature, not because the data itself is still useful for quantitative analysis (it is not — the calibration uncertainties are too large, the spatial coverage too sparse, the temporal resolution too coarse), but because it establishes the historical origin of the measurement. The radiation budget record begins with Explorer 7. Everything that came after was an improvement on Suomi’s original question, asked from Suomi’s original vantage point, with instruments that Suomi’s instruments made possible by demonstrating that the measurement could be made at all.

What the Thermometer Measured

The subtitle of this research is “The Thermometer,” and the analogy is precise. A thermometer does not create temperature. It does not influence temperature. It does not change the thing it measures. It reveals what was already there, makes visible what was invisible, quantifies what was felt but not known. Before the thermometer, temperature was a qualitative experience — warm, cold, hot, cool, tepid, scorching, freezing. After the thermometer, temperature was a number: 37 degrees, 100 degrees, minus 40 degrees. The qualitative experience did not change. The quantitative knowledge transformed everything.

Explorer 7 was a thermometer for the planet. Before Explorer 7, the radiation budget was a theoretical construct — a quantity that climate physicists knew must exist, that they could estimate from surface observations and atmospheric models, that they could bound with thermodynamic arguments, but that they could not measure. After Explorer 7, the radiation budget was a measured quantity. The data was imprecise. The instruments were crude. The coverage was incomplete. But the measurement existed. The thermometer had been read. The planet’s temperature, in the radiative sense, was now known rather than inferred.

The difference between inference and measurement is the difference between theory and science. Inference says: given what we know about the Sun’s output and the Earth’s reflectivity and the atmosphere’s absorption characteristics, the radiation budget should be approximately balanced. Measurement says: we put instruments in orbit and measured the energy coming in and the energy going out, and here is the number. The inference can be wrong. The measurement, within its error bars, is what it is. Explorer 7 converted the radiation budget from an inference to a measurement, and that conversion is irreversible. Once you have measured something, you cannot go back to a state of not-knowing. The thermometer, once read, has been read.

Virchow understood this. His microscope converted pathology from inference to observation. Before the microscope, pathologists inferred cellular processes from organ-level changes. After the microscope, they observed them directly. The inference was often correct — organ pathologists had deduced much about disease processes from macroscopic examination. But the observation was definitive. The conversion from inference to observation, from deduction to measurement, from knowing-because-it-must-be-so to knowing-because-we-looked — this is the step that Explorer 7 took for climate science, and it is the step that Virchow took for pathology. Both men (Suomi and Virchow) provided the instrument that made the invisible visible. Both demonstrated that looking, directly, at the right scale, with the right instrument, reveals what reasoning from indirect evidence can only approximate.
