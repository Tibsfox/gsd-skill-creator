What is GFP?

Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) was first isolated from Aequorea victoria by Osamu Shimomura in 1962 — the same year as Scott Carpenter's Aurora 7 flight.

GFP absorbs blue light (from the chemiluminescent protein aequorin) and re-emits it as bright green fluorescence at 509 nm. The protein contains a chromophore formed autocatalytically from three amino acids (Ser65-Tyr66-Gly67).

In 2008, Shimomura, Martin Chalfie, and Roger Tsien shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the discovery and development of GFP. It revolutionized biology by enabling researchers to tag and visualize proteins, gene expression, and cellular processes in living organisms.

The connection to NASA: Carpenter's "fireflies" were ice crystals, but the real bioluminescent mystery — how Aequorea glows — was being solved that same year in the lab.
