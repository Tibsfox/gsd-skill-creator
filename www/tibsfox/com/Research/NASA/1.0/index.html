NASA Agency Founding

The Invisible Foundation Becomes Visible — paired with Armillaria ostoyae

Date: October 1, 1958 Type: Organization Predecessor: NACA (1915–1958) Employees: 8,000 absorbed

Mission Summary

On October 1, 1958, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration began operations, absorbing the 43-year-old National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) — its 8,000 employees, three research laboratories, and decades of institutional knowledge. The Space Act, signed by President Eisenhower on July 29, 1958, transformed an invisible research agency into the visible face of American spaceflight.

This is Mission 1.0: not a launch, but a founding. The hidden network that had solved the problems of flight for four decades was made visible by crisis — Sputnik's orbit on October 4, 1957 — and reorganized into something the public could see.

NACA: The Root System (1915–1958)

  • Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory (Hampton, VA, 1920) — wind tunnels, airfoil research, transonic breakthroughs
  • Ames Aeronautical Laboratory (Moffett Field, CA, 1940) — high-speed aerodynamics, computational methods
  • Lewis Flight Propulsion Laboratory (Cleveland, OH, 1942) — jet engines, rocket propulsion, materials

NACA's research-authorize methodology — systematic, empirical, pragmatic — became NASA's institutional DNA.

Key Transfers

  • JPL (Caltech/Army) transferred to NASA December 3, 1958
  • Army Ballistic Missile Agency (von Braun team) became Marshall Space Flight Center, 1960
  • First Administrator: T. Keith Glennan; Deputy: Hugh L. Dryden
  • First budget: $89 million (FY1959), ~9,000 employees
