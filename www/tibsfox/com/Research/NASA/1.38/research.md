# Mission 1.38 -- Ranger 9 (Atlas-Agena B)

## Deep Research: The Last Ranger

**Mission:** 1.38 / NASA Mission Series
**Date:** March 21, 1965 (launch) -- March 24, 1965 (impact)
**Type:** Lunar impact probe (television reconnaissance)
**Program:** Ranger (NASA / Jet Propulsion Laboratory)
**Epoch:** 1 (Early Space Age)
**Organism Pairing:** Selasphorus calliope (Calliope Hummingbird)
**Bird (Degree 37):** Calliope Hummingbird
**Dedication:** Research relevant birthday
**Status:** SUCCESS -- 5,814 images of Alphonsus crater; first live television broadcast from another world to the American public; final Ranger mission

## Live from the Moon

At 14:08:20 UTC on March 24, 1965, as Ranger 9 descended toward Alphonsus crater on the Moon, something unprecedented happened: the American television network CBS began broadcasting the spacecraft's camera feed live to millions of viewers. For approximately three minutes, the American public watched in real time as the lunar surface grew larger and more detailed, craters resolving from dots to bowls, rilles sharpening from lines to valleys, the central peak of Alphonsus rising from a smudge to a geological feature. Then the screen went white. Ranger 9 had impacted the Moon at 2.67 kilometers per second, its cameras transmitting until 0.2 seconds before destruction.

It was the first live television broadcast from another world.

The images were not recorded and played back. They were not delayed. They were photons striking a vidicon tube on a spacecraft 380,000 kilometers away, converted to an electrical signal, transmitted at the speed of light to the Goldstone tracking station in the Mojave Desert, relayed to the CBS studios in New York, and broadcast to television sets in living rooms across the United States -- all in approximately 1.3 seconds, the light-travel time from Moon to Earth. The public watched the Moon approach in real time, at the same moment the spacecraft saw it.

Ranger 9 was the ninth and final mission of the Ranger program. It was the third consecutive success, following Ranger 7's 4,308 images of Mare Cognitum and Ranger 8's 7,137 images of Mare Tranquillitatis. It was also, by design, the last. The Ranger program had accomplished its objectives: close-up photography of the lunar surface at multiple sites, confirming that the terrain was suitable for crewed landing. Ranger 9's target -- Alphonsus crater -- was chosen not for Apollo landing site reconnaissance but for pure science: the crater showed signs of possible volcanic activity, and the close-up images could determine whether those signs were real.

The program that had failed six consecutive times and endured Congressional investigation ended with three perfect missions in a row and a live television broadcast that brought the Moon into American homes. It was a fitting finale.

## Alphonsus: The Volcanic Question

Alphonsus is a 118-kilometer-diameter impact crater on the Moon's near side, centered at 13.4 degrees south latitude, 2.8 degrees west longitude. It sits on the eastern edge of Mare Nubium (Sea of Clouds), bordered to the north by the smaller crater Ptolemaeus and to the south by the larger crater Arzachel. Together, the three form a prominent north-south chain visible through modest telescopes.

Alphonsus was of particular scientific interest because of a 1958 observation by the Soviet astronomer Nikolai Kozyrev. On November 3, 1958, Kozyrev, working at the Crimean Astrophysical Observatory, obtained a spectrogram of the central peak of Alphonsus that showed emission features consistent with carbon-bearing gas -- specifically, bands that could be attributed to C2 and C3 molecules. Kozyrev interpreted this as evidence of volcanic outgassing from the crater floor: gases escaping from the lunar interior through vents or cracks near the central peak.

The observation was controversial. The Moon had long been considered geologically dead -- a body too small to retain internal heat, with no atmosphere and no tectonic activity. Kozyrev's claim that gas was escaping from the interior implied residual geological activity, which challenged the prevailing model. Some Western astronomers were skeptical; others found the spectral evidence compelling. The debate could not be resolved from Earth: the spectral features were at the detection limit of ground-based instruments, and the spatial resolution of Earth-based telescopes was far too coarse to reveal the source of any outgassing.

Ranger 9 was targeted at Alphonsus specifically to address this question. The close-up images would reveal the crater floor, the central peak, and the dark-halo craters (small craters surrounded by dark deposits) that some researchers had identified as possible volcanic vents. If the dark halos were truly volcanic in origin -- pyroclastic deposits from gas-driven eruptions -- the images should show features inconsistent with impact cratering: elongated vents, flow-like deposits, collapse structures. If the dark halos were ordinary impact features, the images would show circular craters with radial ejecta patterns indistinguishable from impacts elsewhere on the Moon.

## The Ranger Arc: Six Failures, Three Triumphs

Ranger 9 closed a program arc that began with Ranger 1's launch on August 23, 1961 -- three years and seven months earlier. The program's history is a case study in engineering failure, institutional adaptation, and eventual success.

**Rangers 1 and 2 (August and November 1961):** Both lost to Agena B upper stage restart failures. The spacecraft never reached translunar trajectory. Two missions destroyed before they could be tested.

**Ranger 3 (January 1962):** Reached translunar trajectory but an Atlas guidance error caused an excessive injection velocity. Midcourse correction insufficient. Missed the Moon by 36,793 kilometers. Camera system also failed independently. The first Ranger to leave Earth orbit, and yet a double failure.

**Ranger 4 (April 1962):** Central computer and sequencer failed shortly after launch. The spacecraft became inert -- unable to orient itself, maneuver, or activate cameras. By ballistic chance, it impacted the Moon's far side on April 26, 1962, becoming the first American object to reach the lunar surface. No data returned. An empty achievement.

**Ranger 5 (October 1962):** Solar cell failure cut all power within hours of launch. The spacecraft coasted past the Moon as a dead object, missing by 725 kilometers. Correct trajectory, correct design, one solar panel circuit failure, total mission loss.

**Ranger 6 (January 1964):** The cruelest failure. Perfect trajectory, perfect approach, impact in Mare Tranquillitatis exactly on target. Camera system dead -- an electrical arc during launch had shorted the television electronics. The spacecraft hit the Moon with functioning guidance, functioning telemetry, functioning attitude control, and blind cameras.

After Ranger 6, Congressional hearings were held. JPL overhauled its quality assurance program, established clearer management authority, and subjected the camera system to exhaustive electromagnetic compatibility testing. The arc from the seventh mission forward was a different program.

**Ranger 7 (July 1964):** 4,308 images of Mare Cognitum. The first US close-up photographs of the lunar surface. All systems nominal.

**Ranger 8 (February 1965):** 7,137 images of Mare Tranquillitatis. Surveyed the terrain where Apollo 11 would land. All systems nominal.

**Ranger 9 (March 1965):** 5,814 images of Alphonsus crater. Live television broadcast. All systems nominal. The program closed with its best performance.

The arc from failure to success took 43 months -- from Ranger 1's launch in August 1961 to Ranger 7's photographs in July 1964. The arc from first success to program close took just 8 months -- Ranger 7 in July 1964 to Ranger 9 in March 1965. The program spent most of its life failing and a fraction of its life succeeding. But the successes redeemed the program, justified the investment, and provided the reconnaissance data that NASA needed for the next steps: Surveyor soft landings and Lunar Orbiter mapping, leading to Apollo.

## The Spacecraft

Ranger 9, like Rangers 7 and 8, was a Block III Ranger -- the final design iteration that eliminated the experimental landing capsule of the Block II series and devoted all payload mass to the television camera system. The spacecraft weighed approximately 366 kilograms at launch.

The hexagonal bus structure housed the spacecraft systems:

**Power:** Two solar panels generating approximately 200 watts in sunlight, plus rechargeable silver-zinc batteries for the terminal imaging phase when the spacecraft's orientation might not favor solar illumination.

**Attitude control:** Three-axis stabilization using cold nitrogen gas thrusters, referenced to Sun and Earth sensors. The Sun sensor provided the primary attitude reference; the Earth sensor maintained the high-gain antenna pointing for communication.

**Communications:** S-band transponder operating at 960 MHz, with a high-gain directional antenna (parabolic reflector) for television data transmission and an omnidirectional low-gain antenna for initial acquisition and emergency communication.

**Propulsion:** A single midcourse correction engine -- a monopropellant hydrazine thruster -- for trajectory refinement after injection.

**Television system:** Six vidicon cameras in two independent chains (P-channel and F-channel), identical in design to those on Rangers 7 and 8:

- **F-channel (Full-scan):** Two cameras (FA, FB) with 25mm wide-angle lenses. Full-scan readout producing 1,150-line images at 2.56 seconds per frame. These provided wide-area context views showing the regional geology.

- **P-channel (Partial-scan):** Four cameras (PA, PB, PC, PD). PA and PB with 25mm lenses, PC and PD with 76mm telephoto lenses. Partial-scan readout at 0.2 seconds per frame. The telephoto cameras achieved the highest resolution -- the final frames showing features down to approximately 25 centimeters across.

The 25-centimeter resolution of Ranger 9's best images was the highest achieved by any Ranger mission. The improvement over Ranger 7 (0.5 meters) and Ranger 8 (1.5 meters) was due to the spacecraft's steeper approach angle to Alphonsus, which placed the cameras closer to the surface in the final seconds before impact.

## Launch and Transit

Ranger 9 launched from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Kennedy Air Force Station on March 21, 1965, at 21:37:02 UTC. The Atlas D booster and Agena B upper stage performed nominally -- as they had for Rangers 7 and 8. The Agena B restart in parking orbit, the event that had killed Rangers 1 and 2, proceeded without incident. The spacecraft separated cleanly and acquired its sun-line and Earth-line attitude references.

The transit to the Moon lasted approximately 64.5 hours. During the coast phase, the spacecraft's systems were monitored continuously via telemetry through the Deep Space Network's three tracking stations: Goldstone (California), Canberra (Australia), and Madrid (Spain). The midcourse correction maneuver was executed approximately 16 hours after launch, refining the trajectory from the initial injection path to the desired impact point within Alphonsus crater.

The targeting of Alphonsus was considerably more challenging than the mare targets of Rangers 7 and 8. Mare Cognitum and Mare Tranquillitatis were flat, extensive basaltic plains hundreds of kilometers across -- relatively easy targets for a spacecraft on a ballistic trajectory with limited midcourse correction capability. Alphonsus crater is 118 kilometers in diameter -- still a large target by most standards, but with a prominent central peak and a mountainous rim that created significant topographic relief. The approach trajectory had to deliver the spacecraft to the crater floor, ideally near the central peak and the dark-halo features, while avoiding the rim walls that could have obscured the cameras' view during the final approach.

The navigation team at JPL achieved this with impressive precision. Ranger 9 impacted at 12.83 degrees south latitude, 2.37 degrees west longitude -- within the floor of Alphonsus, approximately 20 kilometers from the central peak.

## The Broadcast

The decision to broadcast Ranger 9's camera feed on live television was made jointly by NASA and the CBS television network. The technical arrangement was straightforward: the video signal received at Goldstone was relayed via landline to the CBS studios in New York, where it was broadcast on the network's regular programming. The delay between the image being captured by the camera and appearing on a television screen in a living room was approximately 1.3 seconds (the speed-of-light travel time from Moon to Earth) plus a few hundred milliseconds of terrestrial relay.

CBS anchor Walter Cronkite narrated the final minutes of Ranger 9's descent. The broadcast began as the cameras first activated, showing the approach to Alphonsus from approximately 2,400 kilometers altitude. At this distance, the crater was recognizable as a roughly circular feature with a visible central peak and a mountain rim. As the spacecraft descended, the images sharpened. Craters within the crater floor became visible. The central peak resolved from a bright dot to a geological structure with visible slopes and shadow.

The final images arrived in rapid succession -- the P-channel cameras transmitting one frame every 0.2 seconds, each frame showing a smaller area at higher resolution. The surface detail increased exponentially in the last minute: boulders appeared, rilles sharpened, individual impact craters the size of houses became visible. Then the signal terminated.

The estimated audience was significant for a weekday afternoon broadcast. The event was covered by all three major networks (CBS, NBC, ABC), but CBS had the most prominent live coverage. The broadcast was repeated and replayed throughout the evening news cycle. Newspapers the next day carried photographs from the transmission -- printed images of the Moon at a resolution the public had never seen.

The live broadcast transformed Ranger 9 from a scientific mission into a public event. Rangers 7 and 8 had produced photographs that were released to the press and published in newspapers and magazines. Ranger 9 produced photographs that people watched being taken. The difference was temporal: not seeing the result, but seeing the process. The audience experienced the spacecraft's descent in real time -- the Moon growing larger, the surface growing more detailed, the resolution increasing until the screen went blank.

## What the Images Showed: Alphonsus Revealed

Ranger 9's 5,814 photographs provided the most detailed close-up views of a lunar highland crater ever obtained at that time. The images revealed Alphonsus as a complex geological environment significantly different from the smooth mare surfaces that Rangers 7 and 8 had photographed.

**The crater floor:** Alphonsus's floor is not flat basalt like the maria. It is partially flooded by mare-type lavas -- a thin layer of basaltic material that seeped into the crater through fractures in the floor -- but the flooding was incomplete. The floor shows a mix of smooth, dark material (mare basalt) and rough, bright material (highland breccia). The texture is heterogeneous, in sharp contrast to the relatively uniform mare surfaces at the Ranger 7 and 8 impact sites.

**The central peak:** Alphonsus has a prominent central peak rising approximately 1,100 meters above the crater floor. Ranger 9's images showed the peak as a complex structure -- not a single summit but a cluster of hills and ridges, consistent with the rebound of the crater floor immediately after the original impact that formed Alphonsus. The peak's slopes showed evidence of mass wasting -- downslope movement of loose material -- suggesting that even the low lunar gravity (1.62 m/s^2) is sufficient to drive slow erosion on steep slopes over geological time.

**The rilles:** The most scientifically significant features visible in the Ranger 9 images were the linear and sinuous rilles that cross Alphonsus's floor. These narrow, trench-like features run for tens of kilometers, typically 1-3 kilometers wide and a few hundred meters deep. Their morphology -- straight segments with occasional sinuous sections -- is consistent with tectonic fracturing (graben) modified by possible volcanic activity. The rilles were visible in telescopic photographs, but Ranger 9 resolved them into geological structures with visible walls, floors, and intersections.

**The dark-halo craters:** Several small craters on the floor of Alphonsus are surrounded by halos of dark material that contrast with the brighter surrounding surface. These dark-halo craters were the primary target of Ranger 9's investigation. The Ranger images showed that the dark halos were composed of fine-grained material with lower albedo (reflectance) than the surrounding terrain. The morphology was ambiguous: the dark material could represent either pyroclastic deposits (volcanic ash erupted from vents within the craters) or excavated dark subsurface material (impact craters that penetrated through a bright surface layer into a darker underlying unit).

The Ranger 9 images alone could not definitively resolve the volcanic-versus-impact debate for the dark-halo craters. The resolution, though excellent (down to 25 centimeters in the final frames), was insufficient to identify the diagnostic features of volcanic vents -- such as collapsed lava tubes, flow levees, or fumarolic deposits -- if they existed at scales below the camera's resolution. The question would ultimately be addressed by later missions: Apollo 16 (1972) investigated similar highland terrain, and the Clementine and Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter missions provided spectral and high-resolution imaging data that confirmed the volcanic origin of at least some dark-halo craters on the Moon.

What Ranger 9 did establish conclusively was that Alphonsus crater's floor was far more geologically complex than the mare surfaces. The highlands were not just "brighter and rougher" -- they were structurally diverse, with multiple geological units (mare fill, highland breccia, central peak material, dark-halo deposits, rille walls) all visible within a single 118-kilometer crater. The geological diversity of the highlands would become increasingly apparent through later missions, culminating in the Apollo 16 landing in the Descartes highlands in 1972.

## Engineering Achievement: Resolution Down to 25 Centimeters

Ranger 9 achieved the highest resolution of any Ranger mission: approximately 25 centimeters per pixel in the final P-channel telephoto frames. This represented a significant improvement over Ranger 7's best resolution of approximately 50 centimeters and Ranger 8's approximately 1.5 meters.

The improvement was primarily due to the spacecraft's approach geometry. Alphonsus crater is in the lunar highlands at moderate latitude, and the approach trajectory brought Ranger 9 in at a steeper angle relative to the surface than the nearly equatorial mare targets of the previous missions. The steeper approach meant that the spacecraft's altitude decreased more rapidly in the final seconds, placing the cameras closer to the surface before impact. Additionally, the P-channel cameras (particularly PC and PD with their 76mm telephoto lenses) had been optimized for close-range imaging based on experience from Rangers 7 and 8.

The 25-centimeter resolution meant that the final Ranger 9 images could resolve features the size of a large boulder or a person. This was sufficient to see individual rocks on the crater floor, to identify the edges of small impact craters, and to characterize the texture of the regolith (the layer of broken rock and dust that covers the lunar surface) at a scale relevant to human exploration. An astronaut standing on the surface of Alphonsus would recognize the terrain from Ranger 9's photographs -- not as an abstract geological map, but as the actual landscape around them.

## The End of Ranger

Ranger 9 impacted the floor of Alphonsus crater at 14:08:20 UTC on March 24, 1965, at approximately 2.67 kilometers per second. The spacecraft was destroyed on contact, creating a small crater estimated at 10-15 meters in diameter. The impact site, at 12.83 degrees south latitude, 2.37 degrees west longitude, is approximately 20 kilometers south-southeast of the central peak.

With Ranger 9, the Ranger program was complete. Nine missions. Six failures. Three successes. A total of 17,259 photographs of the lunar surface (4,308 from Ranger 7, 7,137 from Ranger 8, 5,814 from Ranger 9). A cost of approximately $270 million in 1965 dollars.

The program's legacy is threefold:

**Lunar reconnaissance:** The 17,259 images provided the first close-up views of the lunar surface at three distinct sites: a mare surface (Cognitum), the prime Apollo landing site (Tranquillitatis), and a highland crater (Alphonsus). The images confirmed that the surface was solid enough for a spacecraft to land on, that craters existed at all scales down to centimeters, and that the mare surfaces were navigable terrain. This data directly informed the Surveyor and Apollo programs.

**Television technology:** The Ranger camera systems were among the most advanced imaging instruments of the 1960s. The experience gained in building, testing, and operating vidicon cameras in deep space laid the groundwork for subsequent imaging systems on Mariner, Surveyor, Lunar Orbiter, and ultimately the Apollo television cameras that broadcast the Moon landings to the world.

**Public engagement:** Ranger 9's live broadcast was the first time the public saw another world in real time. This experience -- watching the Moon approach through the eyes of a spacecraft -- established a template that would be repeated with increasingly sophisticated broadcasts through Surveyor, Apollo, and subsequent planetary missions. The act of showing the public what a spacecraft sees, as it sees it, became a defining feature of the American space program.

The Ranger program is often remembered for its failures. The six consecutive losses, the Congressional hearings, the JPL management crisis -- these dominate the narrative. But the program should also be remembered for its ending: three perfect missions in a row, each one targeted at a different site, each one returning thousands of images, the last one broadcast live to the nation. The Ranger that failed six times and then succeeded three times is a more complete story than either half alone.
