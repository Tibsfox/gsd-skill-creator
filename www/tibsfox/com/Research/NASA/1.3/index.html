Pioneer 2 — Forty-Five Minutes

The last Air Force Pioneer, and the salal that feeds what others overlook

Date: November 8, 1958 Program: Pioneer (NASA/AFBMD) Epoch: 1 — Early Space Age Vehicle: Thor-Able I

Mission Summary

Pioneer 2 was the third US attempt to reach the Moon and the last managed by the Air Force Ballistic Missile Division before NASA assumed full control of the Pioneer program. Launched from Cape Canaveral on November 8, 1958, its Thor DM-18 first stage and AJ10-40 second stage both performed correctly — a measurable advance from Pioneer 0 (first stage failure at 77 seconds) and Pioneer 1 (second stage velocity shortfall). But the ABL X-248 third stage solid rocket motor failed to ignite. Without the third stage’s final kick, Pioneer 2 could not reach escape velocity.

Pioneer 2 coasted to a maximum altitude of approximately 1,550 km on its ballistic arc, returned 45 minutes of near-Earth radiation and magnetic field data, and captured the first television camera images ever transmitted from a Pioneer spacecraft. It reentered the atmosphere over northwest Africa and burned up. The mission lasted less than an hour. But the 45 minutes of data from below 1,550 km filled in the low-altitude radiation profile that Pioneer 1’s higher trajectory had passed through too quickly to resolve — a detailed map of the inner Van Allen belt’s lower boundary. And the TV camera, though brief, proved that imaging from space was feasible with the Pioneer bus design.

The Spacecraft

  • Mass: ~39.2 kg (truncated cone, gold-plated)
  • Instruments: Ionization chamber, proportional counter telescope, TV camera (first on a Pioneer), magnetometer, micrometeorite detector, temperature sensors
  • Power: Mercury batteries (nickel-cadmium backup)
  • Transmitter: 108.06 MHz, ~300 milliwatts
  • Stabilization: Spin-stabilized (~5.8 rev/sec)
  • Builder: Space Technology Laboratories (later TRW)
  • Launch vehicle: Thor-Able I (3-stage: Thor DM-18 + AJ10-40 + ABL X-248)
  • New instrument: Onboard TV camera — returned the first Pioneer images during ascent

The Failure

T+0 (07:30 UTC): Thor main engine ignition from LC-17A, Cape Canaveral.

T+0 to T+166: First stage nominal. Thor DM-18 burns for 2 min 46 sec, 667 kN thrust. Perfect performance — the first stage issues that destroyed Pioneer 0 are fully resolved.

T+166 to T+286: Second stage ignition. AJ10-40 engine burns for full duration — correcting the premature cutoff that limited Pioneer 1. Both liquid stages now validated.

T+286: Third stage commanded to ignite. The ABL X-248 solid rocket motor does not fire. No ignition signal reaches the motor. The cause: a failure in the third stage ignition system, likely an electrical fault in the squib firing circuit.

T+286 onward: Without the third stage’s 3,100 m/s delta-v, Pioneer 2 follows a ballistic trajectory. It reaches 1,550 km altitude, arcs over, and begins falling back. Instruments continue operating.

T+45 min: Pioneer 2 reenters the atmosphere over northwest Africa and is destroyed. Total flight time: approximately 45 minutes.

Cause: Third stage solid motor ignition failure. The X-248 motor itself was later found to be sound in design; the ignition sequencer failed to deliver the firing pulse. An electrical failure in the staging circuit prevented the squib from receiving current.

The Third Stage Problem

Three Pioneer missions. Three different failures at three different stages. Pioneer 0: first stage exploded at 77 seconds (turbopump bearing). Pioneer 1: second stage cut off 10 seconds early (accelerometer error). Pioneer 2: third stage never ignited (squib circuit fault). The program was climbing the stack, solving one stage at a time. By Pioneer 2, the first two liquid stages were fully validated — a genuine engineering achievement that would underpin every subsequent Thor-Able launch.

The ABL X-248 solid motor was a new technology in 1958 — a spin-stabilized solid rocket designed by the Allegheny Ballistics Laboratory. Solid motors have no turbopumps, no plumbing, no injectors. Once lit, they burn to completion. The challenge is lighting them. The igniter squib — a small pyrotechnic charge triggered by an electrical signal — must fire reliably in vacuum, at altitude, after surviving the vibration and acceleration of the first two stages. Pioneer 2’s squib circuit failed, probably due to a broken wire or connector loosened during powered flight. The motor itself was sound. The lesson: reliability is a chain, and the weakest link determines the outcome.

The TV Camera

Pioneer 2 carried something its predecessors did not: a television camera. It was a simple device by later standards — low resolution, slow scan — but it represented the first attempt to return visual imagery from a Pioneer spacecraft. During the 45-minute flight, the camera captured images of the Earth from altitudes up to 1,550 km. The images were crude, but they proved the concept. Visual remote sensing from space was possible on the Pioneer platform. This capability would evolve through subsequent missions until cameras became as essential to spacecraft as transmitters.

Dedicated to Robert Bunsen

March 31, 1811 — August 16, 1899

Invented the Bunsen burner — the most ubiquitous tool in chemistry, so fundamental it is invisible. Co-invented spectroscopy with Gustav Kirchhoff, proving that every element emits a unique set of light frequencies when heated. Bunsen and Kirchhoff discovered cesium and rubidium by their spectral lines alone — elements no one had ever seen. Unassuming tools, foundational science. The Bunsen burner is to chemistry what salal is to the forest: everywhere, essential, overlooked.
