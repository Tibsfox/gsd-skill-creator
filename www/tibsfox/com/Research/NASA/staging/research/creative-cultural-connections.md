# NASA Creative, Cultural & Community Connections Research

> "With sparse data sets -- the universe is a perfect example -- when we look up we see mostly
> black, but with science we see much deeper, and with math and creative insight we can ponder
> what lies beyond. The trails exist because animals wore them in -- people just formalized
> what was already there."

This document catalogs the creative, cultural, and community dimensions of NASA missions.
Every mission in the catalog should carry human meaning beyond its technical achievement --
art, music, literature, community participation, indigenous knowledge, or cultural resonance.

## 1. NASA AND THE ARTS

### The NASA Art Program (1962-present)

**Founded:** March 1962 by NASA Administrator James Webb
**Implemented by:** NASA employee and artist James Dean, working with National Art Gallery
Curator of Painting H. Lester Cooke
**Model:** Based on the U.S. Air Force art program
**Purpose:** Commission fine art to communicate the cultural significance of space exploration
**Artists commissioned:** 350+ over 60 years
**Key constraint:** Artists were not constrained in any direction by NASA -- total creative freedom

#### Notable Artists and Their NASA Works

**Norman Rockwell** -- "The Final Impossibility: Man's Tracks on the Moon" (1969)
- Commissioned in autumn 1968 to record the first steps on the Moon
- Visited NASA Manned Spacecraft Center in Houston for research
- A NASA technician accompanied the spacesuit to Rockwell's studio and sat with it daily
- Published as story illustration for "Look" magazine, December 30, 1969
- Rockwell called it "the most complicated and difficult" of his space program pictures
- Now in the collection of the National Air and Space Museum, Smithsonian
- "If Michelangelo were alive, he'd be interested in this shot to the moon" -- Rockwell, 1969
- **Catalog connection:** Pairs with Apollo 11. A master illustrator at age 75 documenting
  the defining moment of the 20th century.

**Robert Rauschenberg** -- Stoned Moon Lithographs (1969-70)
- Invited to Cape Canaveral for Apollo 11 launch, July 1969
- Given unrestricted access to NASA facilities, astronauts, technical documents
- Created 34 lithographs + 1 drawing + 19 drawings/collages for unpublished book
- "Sky Garden" (89 inches tall) was the largest hand-pulled lithograph ever created
- Juxtaposed hand-drawn passages with Florida landscape and space race industrial aesthetic
- Created at Gemini G.E.L. print studio in Los Angeles
- The trip renewed his optimism after years of disillusionment over Vietnam
- **Catalog connection:** The intersection of Pop Art and the Space Age. Rauschenberg
  combined the organic (Florida landscape) with the engineered (rockets, schematics) --
  exactly our organism-mission pairing philosophy.

**Andy Warhol** -- Moonwalk Portfolio (1987)
- Two screenprints based on Neil Armstrong's photograph of Buzz Aldrin on the Moon
- Combined two separate NASA still photographs into one composition
- Version 1: radiant yellows and aquamarine. Version 2: vivid pinks and electric blues
- Intended as part of an unfinished "TV" portfolio (I Love Lucy, MLK Dream speech, Beatles on Ed Sullivan)
- Moonwalk was the only composition printed before Warhol's death
- Left unsigned; estate verified authenticity
- In early 1987 Warhol contacted the NASA Art Program directly to procure the Aldrin photo
- Printed by Rupert Jasen Smith -- among Warhol's final works
- **Catalog connection:** Pop Art's ultimate statement -- the Moon landing as television event,
  as mass media, as shared cultural experience. The most famous photograph in human history
  remixed through the most famous visual language of the 20th century.

**Annie Leibovitz** -- Astronaut Eileen Collins Portrait (1999)
- Photographed Collins, first female shuttle pilot (1995) and first female commander (1999)
- Shot during training at Johnson Space Center, Houston
- Collins shown in the "pumpkin suit" (Launch-Entry Suit worn after the 1986 Challenger tragedy)
- Displayed in "NASA|ART: 50 Years of Space Exploration" traveling exhibition
- **Catalog connection:** The premier portrait photographer of her generation documenting
  the first woman to command a spacecraft. Gender, courage, and the gaze of art.

**Laurie Anderson** -- NASA's First (and Only) Artist-in-Residence (2002-2003)
- First artist-in-residence at NASA, carrying a $20,000 stipend
- Roamed NASA buildings, interacted with scientists and engineers, "eavesdropped" on cosmic conversations
- Debuted "The End of the Moon" -- 90-minute performance piece, second in an "epic poem" trilogy about contemporary American culture
- Congress terminated the residency program after Anderson's tenure due to costs
- Also contributed to the NASA Art Program's broader collection
- **Catalog connection:** The only person to hold this position. A multimedia performance artist
  translating rocket science into poetry. She later created a VR installation about the Moon.

### Suggested Catalog Entries from the Art Program

|---------|------------------|--------------------------|

## 2. NASA AND MUSIC

### Space-Themed Music Inspired by Missions

**David Bowie -- "Space Oddity" (1969)**
- Released July 11, 1969 -- five days before Apollo 11 launch
- Rush-released as single to coincide with the Moon landing
- Partly inspired by 2001: A Space Odyssey (1968) and Bowie's feelings of alienation
- BBC initially ceased playing it during the mission due to dark lyrics (Major Tom is lost in space)
- Resumed airplay after Apollo 11 crew safely returned July 23, 1969
- Reached No. 5 in UK -- Bowie's very first chart hit
- Bowie later claimed the track appeared on some BBC coverage of the moonshot
- **Legacy:** Chris Hadfield's 2013 ISS cover (see below), cultural touchstone for 50+ years
- **Catalog connection:** Apollo 11 cultural soundtrack. The song that captured the Moon
  landing's zeitgeist -- not triumph but loneliness, not certainty but wonder.

**Sun Ra and the Arkestra -- Afrofuturism (1950s-1993)**
- Born Herman Poole Blount in Birmingham, Alabama
- Claimed to be from Saturn, renamed himself Sun Ra
- Founded the Arkestra in Chicago mid-1950s
- Linked ancient African culture (specifically Egypt) with the Space Age
- Pioneered free-form improvisation in ensemble settings
- Took jazz "from the inner city to outer space"
- Created "space music" as a means of building a better future for Black Americans
- Described as "the father of Afrofuturism"
- **Catalog connection:** Any early Space Age mission (Explorer, Mercury). Sun Ra was making
  space music before NASA existed. His work is the cultural parallel to the space program itself.

**Janelle Monae -- Android Afrofuturism (2007-2018)**
- Direct lineage from Sun Ra's vision
- Concept albums: Metropolis: The Chase Suite (2007), The ArchAndroid (2010),
  Electric Lady (2013), Dirty Computer (2018)
- Character Cindi Mayweather, an Android from the future, explores Black humanity,
  creativity, and erotic freedom
- **Catalog connection:** Any modern robotic mission (Mars rovers, New Horizons).
  The android as metaphor for exploration -- what it means to be conscious, alone, far from home.

**Public Enemy -- "Fear of a Black Planet" (1990)**
- Album artwork by B.E. Johnson, a NASA illustrator
- Cover concept: two planets eclipsing -- the "Black" planet and Earth
- Layered up to 200 samples across the album including Parliament-Funkadelic
- The Bomb Squad's production redefined hip-hop sonics
- **Catalog connection:** The NASA illustrator connection makes this literal.
  Space imagery used to explore racial justice on Earth.

**Parliament-Funkadelic -- The Mothership Connection (1975)**
- George Clinton's cosmic mythology: aliens, spaceships, funk as liberation
- The Mothership stage show was the most spectacular concert production of its era
- Direct influence on Public Enemy, OutKast, Janelle Monae, Childish Gambino
- "Funk is its own reward" -- the philosophy maps to exploration for its own sake
- **Catalog connection:** Skylab era. The Mothership Connection album released during
  the gap between Apollo and Shuttle -- Clinton filled the void with cosmic funk.

### Astronauts Making Music in Space

**Chris Hadfield -- "Space Oddity" Cover (May 12, 2013)**
- First music video recorded in space
- Canadian astronaut and commander of Expedition 35
- Sang and played guitar while floating through ISS modules
- Modified lyrics: instead of being lost in space, Major Tom receives orders to land safely
- Bowie's social media team called it "possibly the most poignant version ever created"
- 35+ million YouTube views
- **Catalog connection:** ISS missions. Music as human connection across 400 km of vacuum.

**Cady Coleman -- ISS Flute Duets (2011)**
- Brought four flutes to ISS during Expeditions 26-27
- Played a duet with Ian Anderson (Jethro Tull frontman) -- she aboard ISS, he in Perm, Russia
- **Catalog connection:** The idea that music is essential equipment, not luxury cargo.

**Thomas Pesquet -- Saxophone Surprise (2017)**
- ESA astronaut surprised with an alto saxophone for his 39th birthday on ISS
- Crewmates conspired to have it delivered secretly
- The saxophone remains onboard ISS to this day
- **Catalog connection:** ISS as a living community, not just a laboratory.

**Expedition 55 -- "AstroHawaii" Jam Session (April 2018)**
- Drew Feustel and Scott Tingle on guitars, Oleg Artemyev on pan flute
- Zero-gravity jam session
- **Catalog connection:** International cooperation expressed through music, not protocol.

**First Instrument on ISS:** Acoustic guitar, delivered via STS-105 in August 2001.

### NASA's Sound Library (Creative Commons)

- 60+ samples on SoundCloud: mission control, rockets, Sputnik, Voyager transmissions
- Includes "one small step" (Armstrong), "Godspeed John Glenn" (Mercury), planetary radio waves
- Artemis Audio Library: authentic mission sounds, free for creative use
- All released under Creative Commons license -- credit NASA as source
- Rocket engine sounds, solar system radio waves, shuttle mission audio
- **Catalog connection:** Every mission has sound. These are raw materials for community
  music production -- remix culture meets space exploration.

## 3. NASA AND FILM/PHOTOGRAPHY

### The Photographs That Changed How Humanity Sees Itself

**1. Lunar Orbiter 1 -- First Earth from the Moon (August 23, 1966)**
- First photograph of Earth from the vicinity of the Moon
- Showed Earth as a delicate crescent rising above the lunar horizon
- Not part of original mission objectives -- a spontaneous decision
- Grainy monochrome, but the first time humanity saw itself from outside
- Used Eastman Kodak 68-kg imaging system; film developed, scanned, and transmitted onboard
- **Impact:** Preceded Apollo 8's Earthrise by two years; began the era of looking back

**2. Earthrise -- Apollo 8 (December 24, 1968)**
- Photographed by William Anders during Apollo 8's lunar orbit
- First humans to see Earth rise over another world's horizon
- Anders gasped: "Oh my God, look at that picture over there! There's the Earth comin' up"
- Galen Rowell called it "the most influential environmental photograph ever taken"
- **Impact:** Catalyzed the environmental movement. Within one year: first Earth Day (20 million
  Americans participated). Within two years: EPA created, Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act,
  National Environmental Education Act passed.
- Anders later said: "People realised that we lived on this fragile planet and needed to take care of it"
- **PNW connection:** The environmental movement that Earthrise catalyzed is why old growth
  forests in the Pacific Northwest have legal protection today. This photograph drew a direct
  line from the Moon to the trees.

**3. The Blue Marble -- Apollo 17 (December 7, 1972)**
- Photographed by Harrison Schmitt on the way to the Moon
- 70mm Hasselblad camera with 80mm Zeiss lens
- One of the most reproduced images in history
- Released during the surge of 1970s environmental activism
- Became a symbol of Earth's fragility, vulnerability, and isolation
- **Catalog connection:** The last Apollo mission. The crew of Apollo 17 were the last humans
  to see Earth as a full disk with their own eyes. Nobody has since.

**4. Pale Blue Dot -- Voyager 1 (February 14, 1990)**
- Voyager 1 turns its camera around at 6 billion km from Earth
- Earth appears as a 0.12-pixel dot suspended in a sunbeam
- Carl Sagan advocated for the photograph; NASA agreed
- Taken at 04:48 GMT on February 14, 1990 (Valentine's Day)
- Sagan's essay: "On it everyone you love, everyone you know, everyone you ever heard of,
  every human being who ever was, lived out their lives."
- **See Section 8 for full treatment**

**5. Pillars of Creation -- Hubble (April 1, 1995) and JWST (2022)**
- Eagle Nebula, 6,500 light-years from Earth
- Hubble 1995 original: named one of top ten Hubble photographs
- Featured on "t-shirts to coffee mugs" (National Geographic)
- Hubble 2014 revision: Wide Field Camera 3, sharper/wider visible light view
- JWST 2022 near-infrared version: reveals newly forming stars, protostars, cool gas
  invisible even to Hubble. 1.33x higher resolution.
- **Catalog connection:** Hubble and JWST are already paired with Sequoiadendron giganteum.
  The Pillars themselves are star nurseries -- creation in progress. Trees are star nurseries
  for ecosystems.

**6. Mars Sunsets -- Curiosity (2015, 2023) and Perseverance (2021)**
- Blue-tinged sunsets on Mars (fine dust scatters blue light more efficiently)
- Curiosity's April 15, 2015 sunset: first color Mars sunset widely shared
- Curiosity's February 2, 2023 "Sun rays": first crepuscular rays ever seen on Mars
- Perseverance's November 9, 2021 sunset: Mastcam-Z system
- 96-image mosaic edited with color-enhancing technology unveiled "deceptively" blue sky
- **Catalog connection:** Every Mars mission. The alien familiar -- a sunset that looks
  almost like home, but the colors are wrong. Homesickness as data.

### The Hasselblad Legacy

- NASA partnership began 1962 during Mercury program
- Astronaut Walter Schirra suggested Hasselblad 500C cameras
- Modified: leather removed, auxiliary shutter removed, matte black paint, 70-exposure magazines
- Apollo 11: Silver Hasselblad Data Camera with Zeiss Biogon 60mm f/5.6
- **12 camera bodies left on the Moon** (Apollo 11 through Apollo 17) -- only film returned
- Every iconic space photograph from Mercury through Apollo: Hasselblad
- **Catalog connection:** The cameras are still there. On the Moon. Right now.

### NASA Media Library -- Public Domain

- NASA content (images, audio, video, 3D models) generally not copyrighted in the US
- Free for educational, informational, personal use
- No permission needed for news, schools, textbooks, web pages
- **Exceptions:** NASA logos/insignia are protected; identifiable persons need consent for
  commercial use; some third-party content has separate copyright
- **Community project potential:** Every mission's visual archive is freely available.
  Community art projects, school curricula, remix culture -- all enabled by default.

## 4. NASA AND INDIGENOUS KNOWLEDGE

### NASA's Indigenous Peoples Initiative

NASA's Applied Remote Sensing Training program (ARSET) and Indigenous Peoples Initiative
focus on building relationships between NASA and indigenous communities through place-based
remote sensing training, community engagement, and co-production of knowledge.

**Partner organizations include:**
- Navajo Nation
- Samish Indian Nation (Pacific Northwest -- Coast Salish)
- Sault Ste. Marie Tribe of Chippewa Indians
- Institute of Tribal Environmental Professionals
- Northwest Indian College
- United Tribes Technical College
- Indigenous Mapping Collective

### Earth Observations for Indigenous-Led Land Management (EO4IM)

- NASA-funded project via Conservation International
- Objective: strengthen technical capacities of indigenous organizations in the Americas
- Uses Landsat, MODIS, VIIRS active fire data, GLAD alerts, Planet imagery, drone imagery, GPS
- Applications: forest monitoring, mapping traditional ecological knowledge, cultural heritage,
  fire management, ecosystem threat response

### Traditional Fire Management Validated by Satellites

**Pacific Northwest:**
- Cultural burning: "intentional lighting of smaller, controlled fires to provide a desired
  cultural service, such as promoting the health of vegetation and animals"
- Coast Salish peoples had complex land management practices linked to ecosystem resilience
- "Throughout California, Indigenous nations have used fire for thousands of years as a tool
  to steward the land" -- practices validated by NASA satellite fire data
- The importance of indigenous cultural burning in forested regions of the Pacific West
  documented in peer-reviewed research using satellite fire history data

**Australia:**
- "The world's best fire management system is in northern Australia, and it's led by
  Indigenous land managers"
- Satellite monitoring reveals reduction of late dry season wildfires over 115,000 km²
- Traditional burning practices reconfigured with western tools: helicopters, remote sensing,
  satellite mapping

**Ecuador:**
- NASA meteorological data and satellite imagery used to compare fire severity
- Combined with ancestral knowledge of the Kichwa Saraguro people

### Salish-Specific NASA Connections

- NASA's Indigenous Peoples Initiative worked with the **Samish Indian Nation** (Pacific NW)
- Research highlighted changes in location and availability of sea kelp important for
  traditional salmon cooking
- NASA data limitations acknowledged: "can't identify the location of a species of
  mushroom one tribe was interested in" -- humility about what satellites can and cannot see
- **Salish Kootenai College** received NASA grant for Living Landscapes Climate Science
  Education Project (includes video on indigenous fire use)

### Polynesian Voyaging Society -- Celestial Navigation

- Founded 1973 by artist Herb Kawainui Kane, anthropologist Ben Finney, sailor Charles Tommy Holmes
- **Hokule'a** ("Star of Gladness" = Arcturus, zenith star at Hawaii's latitude)
- 1976 voyage to Tahiti: first traditional navigation between Hawaii and Tahiti in ~800 years
- Master navigator **Nainoa Thompson** developed the Hawaiian star compass (32 houses)
- Navigators memorize risings/settings of 150+ stars, plus ocean color, temperature, salinity,
  floating debris, seabird behavior, cloud patterns, swell direction, wind
- **Connection to orbital mechanics:** Same stars, same spherical geometry, same fundamental
  problem -- where am I, where am I going, how do I get there
- Neil deGrasse Tyson interviewed Nainoa Thompson on StarTalk Radio (August 2018)
- U.S. Naval Academy teaches celestial navigation to midshipmen using similar principles

### Duwamish Territory Acknowledgment (Seattle Context)

- The Duwamish are Lushootseed-speaking Southern Coast Salish people
- Indigenous people of metropolitan Seattle
- 17+ villages around the Seattle area prior to colonization
- Territory includes Seattle, Burien, Tukwila, Renton, Redmond
- Suggested acknowledgment: "I would like to acknowledge that we are on the traditional
  land of the first people of Seattle, the Duwamish People past and present and honor
  with gratitude the land itself and the Duwamish Tribe."
- **PNW research connection:** Every Seattle-connected mission (Boeing/NASA partnerships,
  UW research, Space Grant at WSU) sits on Duwamish land. The research projects at
  www/PNW/ map terrain that was home to the Duwamish for millennia before it was named
  "Pacific Northwest." The trails existed before the maps.

## 5. NASA AND COMMUNITY SCIENCE

### Galaxy Zoo (Launched July 2007)

- Crowdsourced morphological classification of galaxies
- 125 million galaxy classifications
- 60+ peer-reviewed academic papers from 15+ projects
- Part of the Zooniverse platform
- **Participation model:** Anyone with a web browser can classify galaxies.
  No telescope, no degree, no equipment.

### Planet Hunters (Launched December 16, 2010)

- Identifies exoplanets from Kepler Space Telescope light curves
- Exploits the fact that humans are better at recognizing visual patterns than computers
- Volunteers have discovered confirmed exoplanets
- **Participation model:** Look at graphs of star brightness over time. Spot the dips.

### Disk Detective (Launched January 30, 2014)

- Analyzes NASA's WISE telescope data
- Identifies dusty debris disks = stars forming planetary systems
- Expected ~375 previously undiscovered disks
- **Participation model:** Watch short animations of astronomical objects. Mark the ones
  that look like disks.

### GLOBE Observer (NASA citizen science app)

- Download free app, register, go outside, observe
- **Four observation types:**
  1. **Clouds:** Compare sky observations with NASA satellite images
  2. **Mosquito Habitats:** Identify breeding sites, sample larvae, enable disease risk models
  3. **Land Cover:** Document ground conditions
  4. **Trees:** Measure tree height and circumference, track growth over time
- Available in 120+ GLOBE countries
- **PNW connection:** The Trees observation tool directly connects to our PNW forest research.
  Community members measuring the same trees we're documenting. Same forest, different lens.

### Backyard Worlds: Planet 9

- Search for new objects at the edge of our solar system
- Review images from WISE and NEOWISE missions
- Looking for brown dwarfs and low-mass stars
- Volunteers can become co-authors on published papers

### Fresh Eyes on Ice (Award-winning)

- Citizen science project monitoring ice conditions in Alaska
- Won recognition for community-based environmental monitoring
- **PNW connection:** Alaska as PNW's northern extent. Ice monitoring connects to
  climate patterns affecting the entire Pacific coast.

### Catalog Integration Potential

Every mission that produced publicly available data is a potential citizen science gateway:
- Kepler/TESS data -> Planet Hunters -> community exoplanet discovery
- WISE/NEOWISE -> Disk Detective -> community solar system archaeology
- Mars rover images -> pattern recognition projects
- Hubble/JWST images -> Galaxy Zoo classifications
- Earth observation satellites -> GLOBE Observer -> local environmental monitoring

### The Debates: What Was Included and What Was Fought Over

**Chuck Berry's "Johnny B. Goode":**
- Some objected that rock music was "adolescent"
- Sagan replied: "There are a lot of adolescents on the planet"
- The song made the record. Rock and roll left the solar system.

**The Beatles' "Here Comes the Sun":**
- The committee wanted to include it, but EMI (copyright holder) declined
- The Beatles themselves favored the idea but didn't own the copyright
- Timothy Ferris later refuted this story entirely: "We did not strive to include the Beatles'
  'Here Comes the Sun'... It's not the Beatles' strongest work, and the witticism of the title,
  if charming in the short run, seemed unlikely to remain funny for a billion years."
- **The lesson:** Copyright law almost certainly outlasts the civilization that wrote it, but
  the Golden Record will outlast both.

**Other artists considered but not included:**
- Elvis Presley, Bob Marley, Billie Holiday, Jimi Hendrix, Aretha Franklin,
  Miles Davis, the Rolling Stones
- Each exclusion was a philosophical choice about what represents "humanity"

### Blind Willie Johnson -- "Dark Was the Night, Cold Was the Ground"

This track deserves special attention. It is arguably the most emotionally significant
selection on the record.

- Gospel blues, recorded 1927
- Primarily instrumental: bottleneck slide guitar + humming/moaning
- Self-taught musician, born 1897, played on street corners and in mission halls
- Ry Cooder called it "the most soulful, transcendent piece in all American music"
- **Why it was chosen:** Timothy Ferris said Johnson's song "concerns a situation he faced
  many times: nightfall with no place to sleep. Since humans appeared on Earth, the shroud
  of night has yet to fall without touching a man or woman in the same plight."
- Sagan wrote: "For us they express a longing for contact with other beings in the depths
  of space, a musical expression of the principal message of the Voyager itself."
- **The deepest irony:** Johnson died of pneumonia in 1945 after being turned away from a
  hospital that wouldn't treat a blind Black man. His song of homelessness is now the
  farthest human artifact from Earth. He could not get a bed; his music got the stars.

### Navajo Night Chant -- Yeibichai Dance

- Track 20 on the Golden Record
- Performed by Ambrose Roan Horse, Chester Roan, and Tom Roan
- Part of the Nightway -- a spiritual ceremony for healing and restoring balance
- Recorded by ethnomusicologist Willard Rhodes (1939-1952)
- **Significance:** Indigenous North American ceremony selected to represent human spiritual
  heritage to potential extraterrestrial civilizations
- **The tension:** Recorded by a non-indigenous ethnomusicologist; selected by a non-indigenous
  committee. The Navajo people were not consulted about sending their sacred ceremony
  into interstellar space. This deserves acknowledgment.

### Ann Druyan's Brainwaves -- The Hidden Love Letter

- Druyan proposed recording human brain/nervous system electrical impulses as sound
- Session at New York University Medical Center (some sources say Bellevue Hospital)
- One hour of EEG compressed to one minute of analog audio
- Recorded just days after she and Sagan confessed their love and became engaged
- Part of her meditation: "the wonder of love, of being in love"
- Neither Druyan nor Sagan told anyone on the team about their relationship at the time
- **The result:** Somewhere past the heliopause, two spacecraft carry the brainwaves of a
  woman newly in love, compressed into sound, traveling at 17 km/s through interstellar space.
  If any civilization ever decodes it, they will find the neural signature of human love.
  This is the most romantic act in the history of the species.

### Catalog Entry: Voyager as a Cultural Object

The Voyager Golden Record is not just a mission artifact -- it is the single most ambitious
act of art curation in human history. It asks: if you had to explain humanity in 90 minutes
of music, 116 images, and 55 languages, what would you choose?

**Suggested catalog treatment:**
- Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 deserve expanded cultural entries
- The Golden Record itself deserves its own sub-entry in the catalog
- Cross-reference every artist and culture represented on the record
- The Druyan brainwave recording should be noted as a unique data type: biology as art

**Organism pairing note:** Voyager 1+2 are already paired with Posidonia oceanica
(Neptune grass -- oldest living organism, still sending signals from the deep). This pairing
is profound: the Golden Record is a signal from the deep of human culture, carrying DNA-like
information about who we are, traveling through the deep of interstellar space.

## 7. NASA AND EDUCATION PROGRAMS

### National Space Grant College and Fellowship Program

- **Established:** 1988 by Congress; NASA initiated in 1989
- **Coverage:** 52 consortia in all 50 states + DC + Puerto Rico
- **Network:** 850+ affiliates (universities, colleges, industry, museums, science centers,
  state/local agencies)
- **Modeled on:** Sea Grant (oceanic) and Land Grant (agricultural) programs
- **Programs:** Fellowships, scholarships, curriculum enhancement, faculty development,
  pre-college education, public service outreach
- **PNW connection:** Washington State University hosts NASA Space Grant program.
  Direct pipeline from PNW communities to NASA research.

### FIRST Robotics and NASA

- NASA's Robotics Alliance Project provides grants for high school FIRST teams
- Annual sponsorship grants (applications open September, awards November)
- JPL supports FIRST Robotics competitions directly
- **Community connection:** High school students designing and building robots with
  NASA engineering principles. Local teams, national competition, space-grade thinking.

### ISS National Lab Education Programs

**Zero Robotics:**
- Middle and high school students program SPHERES robots on ISS
- 20,000+ students and 4,500+ educators over 10 years
- Students write code that runs in actual microgravity
- **Community connection:** Your code, running in space. Real agency, real results.

**Student Spaceflight Experiments Program (SSEP):**
- 1,600+ organizations supported at local level
- Each community engages minimum 300 students in microgravity experiment design
- Students write proposals, design experiments, and their work flies to ISS
- Supported by school districts, NASA Space Grant institutions, corporate foundations,
  community foundations, local research institutions
- **Community connection:** A 6th grader's experiment, designed in their school,
  launched on a rocket, conducted on the space station. That's not a field trip. That's agency.

## 8. PALE BLUE DOT -- MISSION WITHIN A MISSION

### The Photograph

**Date:** February 14, 1990 (Valentine's Day)
**Time:** 04:48 GMT
**Spacecraft:** Voyager 1
**Distance:** 6.054 billion kilometers (3.762 billion miles, 40.47 AU)
**Earth's appearance:** 0.12 pixels, suspended in a scattered light ray (a sunbeam)
**Advocacy:** Carl Sagan proposed the photograph; NASA agreed despite concerns about
pointing the camera toward the Sun

### Why It Matters

Voyager 1's primary mission was complete. It had visited Jupiter and Saturn. It was
leaving the solar system. Sagan argued that before it left, it should turn around and
take one last picture of home.

The photograph shows Earth as an almost invisible speck -- less than a single pixel --
caught in a band of scattered sunlight. Without knowing which dot to look at, you would
never find it.

### Sagan's Essay (1994, from the book "Pale Blue Dot")

> "Look again at that dot. That's here. That's home. That's us. On it everyone you love,
> everyone you know, everyone you ever heard of, every human being who ever was, lived
> out their lives. The aggregate of our joy and suffering, thousands of confident religions,
> ideologies, and economic doctrines, every hunter and forager, every hero and coward,
> every creator and destroyer of civilization, every king and peasant, every young couple
> in love, every mother and father, hopeful child, inventor and explorer, every teacher
> of morals, every corrupt politician, every 'superstar,' every 'supreme leader,' every
> saint and sinner in the history of our species lived there -- on a mote of dust suspended
> in a sunbeam."

### Catalog Entry

The Pale Blue Dot is not a photograph of Earth. It is a photograph of perspective.
It deserves its own catalog entry, separate from the Voyager mission entries, because
it represents a distinct act of intention: the decision to look back.

**Suggested catalog ID:** Dedicated entry under Voyager 1, flagged as a "cultural mission"
**Date:** February 14, 1990
**Mission duration:** Seconds (the time to take the photograph)
**Impact duration:** Permanent
**PNW connection:** From 6 billion km, the Pacific Northwest is invisible. The ancient
forests, the Salish Sea, the volcanoes -- all contained in less than a pixel. And yet
they are there. The research we do -- documenting ecosystems, mapping terrain, naming
species -- is the opposite of the Pale Blue Dot. We zoom in where Voyager zoomed out.
Both perspectives are necessary.

## 9. APOLLO 8 "EARTHRISE" -- THE PHOTOGRAPH THAT CHANGED EVERYTHING

### The Event

**Date:** December 24, 1968 (Christmas Eve)
**Crew:** Frank Borman (Commander), Jim Lovell (Command Module Pilot),
William Anders (Lunar Module Pilot)
**Context:** First humans to orbit the Moon
**The moment:** As the spacecraft rounded the Moon's limb, Anders saw Earth rising
over the lunar horizon and scrambled for color film

### The Photograph

- Taken by William Anders
- Shows Earth as a vivid blue and white sphere rising above the grey lunar surface
- Anders: "Oh my God, look at that picture over there! There's the Earth comin' up"
- Galen Rowell: "the most influential environmental photograph ever taken"

### The Genesis Reading

- Broadcast reached 1 in 4 people on Earth -- roughly 1 billion across 64 countries
- Most-watched television program of all time (to that date)
- NASA's only instruction: "do something appropriate" (they would have "the largest
  audience that had ever listened to a human voice")
- Christine Laitin (wife of a NASA public affairs official) suggested the Genesis reading
- Anders read verses 1-4, Lovell read verses 5-8, Borman read verses 9-10
- From the King James Bible, Book of Genesis

### Impact

- Within one year: first Earth Day (April 22, 1970) -- 20 million Americans participated
  (10% of the US population)
- Within two years: Environmental Protection Agency created
- Legislation: Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, National Environmental Education Act
- Anders (2018): "People realised that we lived on this fragile planet and that we needed
  to take care of it"

### William Anders (1933-2024)

- Died June 7, 2024, in a plane crash in Washington State's San Juan Islands
- He spent his later years on Orcas Island, Washington -- in the Pacific Northwest
- **PNW connection:** The man who took the most influential environmental photograph
  in history spent his final decades in the San Juan Islands, surrounded by the orcas,
  old growth forest, and Salish Sea that his photograph helped protect. The trail led home.

### Catalog Entry

Apollo 8 is not primarily a technology mission. It is a perspective mission. The crew's
objective was to orbit the Moon, but what they discovered was Earth.

**Suggested treatment:** Full cultural entry with cross-references to:
- Environmental movement
- Earth Day founding
- Clean Air Act / Clean Water Act
- NASA media library (the photograph itself, in public domain)
- William Anders' Washington State connection

## 10. SPACE ART INSTALLATIONS AND CULTURAL MOMENTS

### Trevor Paglen -- Orbital Reflector (2018)

- Reflective mylar sculpture launched as a temporary satellite
- Co-produced by Nevada Museum of Art; $1.3 million project
- Objective: first "purely artistic" object in space
- Launched December 3, 2018, aboard SpaceX Falcon 9
- **Failed:** The 2018-2019 US government shutdown prevented contact;
  the sculpture never inflated properly
- Antecedents: Kazimir Malevich's Planits (1923-24), Soviet Sputnik (1957),
  Yves Klein's Pneumatic Rocket (1958), NASA's Echo 1 and Echo 2
- **Controversy:** Astronomers objected to the "light pollution" of an art satellite
- **Catalog connection:** The intent matters more than the outcome. An artist tried to
  put pure art in orbit -- no science, no communication, no military purpose. Just beauty.
  And it failed. That's its own kind of beauty.

### KEO Time Capsule (Proposed, never launched)

- Space time capsule carrying messages from present Earth to humanity 50,000 years from now
- Name represents three most common sounds across widely spoken languages: /k/, /e/, /o/
- Would re-enter Earth's atmosphere in ~52,000 AD
- Originally planned for 2003 launch; as of August 2024, no launch date confirmed
- Citizens worldwide could submit messages via keo.org
- **Catalog connection:** The Voyager Golden Record for people who want to talk to
  their own species' future rather than to aliens. Introversion vs. extroversion, cosmically.

### Yusaku Maezawa's dearMoon Project (2018-2024, Cancelled)

- Japanese billionaire planned to take 8 civilian artists around the Moon on SpaceX Starship
- Announced September 2018; crew revealed December 2022
- Selected crew included DJ Steve Aoki, YouTuber Tim Dodd ("Everyday Astronaut"), K-pop star TOP
- Vision: the experience of seeing Earth from the Moon would inspire artistic creation
- **Cancelled June 1, 2024** due to Starship development delays
- Crew had no prior knowledge of cancellation possibility
- **Catalog connection:** The mission that didn't happen. The dream of sending artists to
  the Moon -- exactly what the NASA Art Program started in 1962, but as passengers, not
  observers. The intent links directly to the Apollo 8 experience: what happens when
  artists see Earth from afar?

### SpaceX Starman / Tesla Roadster (February 6, 2018)

- Falcon Heavy test flight dummy payload: Elon Musk's cherry-red Tesla Roadster
- Mannequin "Starman" in driver's seat (named after Bowie's "Starman")
- Sound system set to loop Bowie's "Space Oddity" (inaudible in vacuum)
- Dashboard reads "Don't Panic" (Hitchhiker's Guide reference)
- Now in heliocentric orbit crossing Mars's orbit, reaching 1.66 AU from Sun
- **Not NASA**, but part of the cultural landscape of spaceflight
- **Catalog connection:** Corporate space culture. A car in orbit is absurd,
  but 35+ million people watched the launch live. It made people care about
  rockets again. The cultural value is real even if the artifact is a joke.

### NASA Logo: Meatball vs. Worm

- **The Meatball (1959-present):** Original NASA insignia. Blue circle, stars, red chevron,
  white orbital path. Designed by NASA employee James Modarelli.
- **The Worm (1975-1992, 2020-present):** Modernist red logotype, designed by Richard Danne
  and Bruce Blackburn as part of the Federal Graphics Improvement Program (National Endowment
  for the Arts). Clean, minimalist, futuristic.
- **The Rivalry:** The worm was retired May 22, 1992, by Administrator Dan Goldin, who
  felt it lacked the "romance" of the meatball. Reinstated April 3, 2020, for the
  Commercial Crew Program -- a secondary mark alongside the meatball.
- **Design history significance:** One of the great debates in American graphic design.
  The meatball says "adventure." The worm says "precision." NASA needed both.
- **Catalog connection:** Every mission was branded by one or the other. The visual identity
  of space exploration is itself a design artifact worth documenting.
