The Steps

A story told by Hylocomium splendens, stair-step moss, on a nurse log in the Andrews Forest, Oregon Cascades. Paired with Mission 1.30 — Ranger 5.

I do not have a brain. I should say that first, so you understand the terms. I have no neurons, no synapses, no central processing unit. What I have is chemistry. Every cell in my body knows what to do when water arrives and what to do when water leaves. The instructions are in the proteins. The schedule is in the atmosphere. I do not think about growing. I grow when it rains.

— STEP ONE —

The nurse log was already here when I arrived. A Douglas-fir that fell — I cannot tell you when, because I was not here, and I have no way to know what happened before me. The log was soft enough to hold water and firm enough to hold shape. A fragment of me — a piece broken from a colony somewhere uphill, carried by runoff or perhaps by the foot of an elk — landed on the upper surface of the log, in a crevice where bark had peeled away, and found moisture. I do not remember landing. I remember the first rain after landing, because that is when I began.

— STEP SEVEN —

Seven years. Seven steps. Each one a frond branching from the previous year's growth at a slight upward angle, so that the colony rises in a staircase. I am ten centimeters tall now. My base — steps one and two — is brown and decomposing. The nutrients from those oldest tissues are leaching into the nurse log, feeding the fungal network that connects the living trees. I am composting myself from the bottom while growing from the top. This is not a metaphor. This is physiology.

— STEP TWENTY-THREE —

The canopy gap that gave me extra light has closed. A western hemlock — grown from a seed that germinated on my surface, in my moisture, fed by my decomposing base — has reached the understory and is shading me. The hemlock is twenty years old and six meters tall. I am twenty-three years old and twelve centimeters tall. The hemlock will outlive me by centuries. I helped it start.

— STEP THIRTY-ONE —

This year the rain stopped early. June was dry. July was dry. August was — I cannot describe August. The air withdrew its moisture and I withdrew my life. The fronds curled inward. The green faded to brown. The chemistry of survival engaged: sugars crystallized around my proteins, protective molecules wrapped my membranes, antioxidant enzymes positioned themselves at the gates where damage would enter when water returned.

I went dark.

I cannot tell you what happened while I was dormant, because nothing happened. I was not dead. I was not alive. I was preserved, like a letter in an envelope, waiting to be opened. The drought lasted — I do not know how long. Time requires metabolism to measure, and my metabolism was zero.

Then it rained.

The first drops hit my curled fronds and the chemistry reversed. Water entered the cells. Sugars dissolved. Proteins unfolded. Chloroplasts realigned. Within five minutes — five minutes — I was photosynthesizing. The light had been there all along, falling on my desiccated surface, bouncing off without effect. Now I could use it again. The power was back.

— STEP FORTY-SEVEN —

There is a spacecraft in orbit around the Sun. It has been there since October of 1962. Its solar panels generate electricity — 135 watts, enough to power everything aboard. But a short circuit in the wiring means the electricity has nowhere to go. The cameras work. The spectrometer works. The power cannot reach them. The spacecraft has been desiccated for sixty-four years. It cannot recover. There is no rain in space.

I think about this spacecraft sometimes. Not with neurons — I have none. But with chemistry. The chemistry of desiccation is the same whether you are a moss on a nurse log or a spacecraft in heliocentric orbit. The difference is in the recovery. I can come back. The spacecraft cannot. My desiccation is a pause. Its desiccation is permanent.

I grow another step.

— STEP FORTY-SEVEN: 2.4 cm —

Ranger 5 (October 18, 1962) — solar panel failure at T+75 minutes — 725 km lunar miss — heliocentric orbit.
The moss recovers. The spacecraft does not.
Both are waiting for rain.
