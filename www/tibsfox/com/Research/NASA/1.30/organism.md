# Organism Pairing: Hylocomium splendens (Stair-Step Moss)

## The Dormant Green

**Scientific Name:** Hylocomium splendens (Hedw.) Schimp.
**Family:** Hylocomiaceae
**Common Names:** Stair-step moss, splendid feather moss, glittering wood moss
**Paired Mission:** 1.30 -- Ranger 5 (Atlas-Agena B), October 18, 1962
**PNW Status:** Native, dominant -- the most abundant floor moss in Pacific Northwest old-growth conifer forests

---

## What Survives Without Power

Walk through an old-growth Douglas-fir forest on the western slope of the Cascades in November. The canopy is 60 meters above you, dripping. The air is saturated. The forest floor is a continuous carpet of green -- not the bright green of spring grass or the dark green of conifer needles, but a complex, multi-layered green built from overlapping feathery fronds that cascade in miniature staircases across every horizontal surface. This is Hylocomium splendens, the stair-step moss, and it covers the forest floor like a living quilt.

Pick up a handful. It comes away in a loose mat, the fronds separating at their annual growth junctions. Each frond is approximately 2-3 centimeters long, representing one year's growth. The newest growth is at the top -- bright, fresh, photosynthetically active. Below it, last year's growth is slightly darker. Below that, the year before, darker still. Five or six steps down, the fronds are brown, decomposing, returning their nutrients to the soil that the moss does not actually touch. The moss has no roots. The entire structure -- photosynthesis, water absorption, nutrient uptake -- happens through the leaf surfaces. The moss is an atmospheric organism, living on what the air provides.

Now walk through the same forest in August, during a dry spell. The carpet is brown. The fronds have curled inward, their tips contracting as the tissue desiccates. The moss is not dead -- it is dormant. Its water content has dropped below the threshold for active metabolism, and the organism has entered a state of suspended animation. Touch it and it crumbles slightly, the desiccated tissue brittle under your fingers. It looks dead. It is not.

Ranger 5 launched on October 18, 1962, and operated normally for seventy-five minutes. Then a short circuit killed the solar panels. The spacecraft transferred to battery power. Eight hours and forty-four minutes later, the battery died. The spacecraft went dormant -- all systems inert, all instruments functional but unpowered. Unlike the moss, Ranger 5 cannot revive. There is no rain in heliocentric orbit. The spacecraft is permanently desiccated, permanently dark, permanently silent. The structure is intact. The power is gone.

## Morphology and Life History

Hylocomium splendens is a pleurocarpous moss -- a growth form in which the sporophytes arise from the sides of the stems rather than the tips, allowing the tips to continue growing indefinitely. The stems are branched in a pinnate pattern, creating the feathery appearance that earned the species its common name "splendid feather moss" and the Latin epithet splendens (shining, splendid).

The defining morphological feature is the stair-step growth pattern. Each year, a new frond emerges from a point on the previous year's growth, typically at a slight angle. The new frond grows forward and upward while the old growth persists below it, creating a staircase of annual increments. This growth pattern makes Hylocomium splendens one of the easiest mosses to age: count the steps, count the years. A colony with ten visible steps has been growing for approximately ten years. The effective height of the colony is limited by basal decay -- the oldest steps decompose, and the colony reaches a dynamic equilibrium where the growth rate at the top equals the decay rate at the bottom. In PNW old-growth forests, this equilibrium height is typically 10-15 centimeters.

The moss reproduces primarily through fragmentation rather than spores. Pieces broken off by animal traffic, falling branches, or water flow can establish new colonies if they land on suitable substrate -- typically moist, shaded, stable surfaces like decaying wood, humus, or established moss beds. Spore production occurs but is relatively rare in many populations, and spore germination rates are very low. The moss is a clonal organism, spreading laterally through growth and dispersing through breakage.

## Desiccation Tolerance: The Core Resonance

The most remarkable feature of stair-step moss -- and the feature that connects it most directly to Ranger 5 -- is its extreme desiccation tolerance. The moss can survive the loss of virtually all its cellular water, entering a state of cryptobiosis (hidden life) that may persist for months.

When ambient humidity drops, the moss begins to lose water through its leaf surfaces. It has no mechanism to prevent this loss -- no waxy cuticle like most vascular plants, no stomata that can close, no root system to draw replacement water from the soil. The water simply leaves. As tissue water content drops below approximately 300% of dry mass, photosynthesis slows. Below 100%, photosynthesis stops entirely. Below 50%, respiration drops to near-zero. Below 10%, the moss enters full dormancy -- metabolically inert, structurally preserved by sugars and protective proteins that prevent cellular damage.

Upon rehydration, the process reverses with remarkable speed. Photosynthesis resumes within 5-10 minutes of rewetting. Full metabolic activity is restored within an hour. The moss picks up exactly where it left off, as if the desiccation had never occurred.

This is the biological equivalent of a spacecraft entering safe mode and recovering -- but better. The moss requires no computer to manage the transition. The protective mechanisms are chemical and structural, built into the tissue at the molecular level. The spacecraft equivalent would be hardware-level fault tolerance: a power system that could survive complete discharge and resume operation when an external power source became available.

Ranger 5 had no such tolerance. When the battery voltage dropped below the computer's minimum operating threshold, all state was lost. The computer's volatile memory was erased. Even if power could somehow be restored -- if the short circuit spontaneously cleared, if the solar panels reconnected -- the computer would have no program to execute, no attitude reference to restore, no mission timeline to follow. The spacecraft was desiccated permanently.

## Ecological Role

In PNW old-growth forests, Hylocomium splendens performs several critical ecosystem functions that are disproportionate to its small stature:

**Water retention:** The continuous moss carpet acts as a sponge, absorbing rainfall and fog drip and releasing it slowly. A healthy moss carpet can hold 10-20 times its dry weight in water. This buffering capacity moderates soil moisture fluctuations, maintains humidity in the litter layer, and reduces erosion during heavy rain. The moss carpet is the forest floor's power supply -- storing energy (in the form of water) for release during dry periods. When the moss desiccates, this buffering function is lost, and the forest floor's micro-ecosystem experiences the same kind of power failure that killed Ranger 5.

**Nutrient cycling:** The moss intercepts nutrients from throughfall (rain that has passed through the canopy, picking up dissolved nitrogen, phosphorus, and cations from leaf surfaces and canopy lichens). These nutrients are absorbed by the moss and incorporated into tissue, creating a nutrient pool at the soil surface that would otherwise leach into the soil profile. When the moss decomposes (at the base of the staircase), these nutrients are released into the humus layer where they are available to tree roots. The moss acts as a nutrient capacitor -- charging during rainfall, discharging through decomposition.

**Seedbed for conifers:** Western hemlock and Sitka spruce seeds germinate preferentially on moss surfaces rather than bare mineral soil. The moss provides a stable, moist, well-aerated substrate for germination and early root establishment. Old-growth forests regenerate themselves, in part, through the moss carpet.

## The Staircase as Time Series

The stair-step growth pattern of Hylocomium splendens creates a natural time series -- each step representing one year of growth, with the step's size reflecting the growing conditions of that year. Wide steps (3+ cm) indicate favorable years: warm springs, adequate moisture, ample light. Narrow steps (1-2 cm) indicate stress years: drought, cold springs, canopy closure reducing light.

A ten-step colony is a ten-year environmental record. A twenty-step colony (rare, as basal decay usually limits visible steps to 8-12) is a twenty-year record. Ecologists use stair-step moss colonies as miniature dendrochronologies -- tree-ring equivalents at the forest floor level.

Ranger 5's flight data is a time series of a different kind: 8 hours and 44 minutes of telemetry, recording the spacecraft's vital signs as the battery drained. The telemetry shows power dropping, temperature drifting, systems shutting down. Each telemetry frame is a step in the staircase of a dying spacecraft. The moss's staircase records growth. Ranger 5's telemetry records decline.
