The Understory

A short story from Mission 1.2 — Pioneer 1

v1.2 — NASA Mission Series

I have never seen the sky. I want you to understand that first. I have been here for ninety-three years, rooted in the same six inches of duff on the north-facing slope above the Stillaguamish River in Snohomish County, Washington, and in all that time, the canopy has never opened above me wide enough to show me more than scattered coins of light that move across the ground like slow animals. I know the sky exists because the rain comes from there, and the light comes from there, and sometimes in winter a branch falls from the Douglas-fir two hundred feet above me and crashes through the hemlock understory and lands three feet from my rhizome with a sound like a door being shut forever. But I have not seen the sky itself. It is not for me.

I am a sword fern. Polystichum munitum. I have seventy-eight fronds arranged in a rosette around a rhizome that is eleven inches across and has been growing outward at the rate of roughly one millimeter per year since 1933, which was the year a Douglas-fir seedling germinated in a crack in a nurse log forty feet uphill from where I am now, and that seedling's roots, reaching down through the rotting wood and into the mineral soil beneath, displaced enough duff to create a pocket of exposed humus where a single spore of mine — not mine exactly, my parent's — landed and germinated into a prothallus the size of a fingernail, which produced an egg, which was fertilized by a sperm that swam through a film of November rainwater no thicker than a page, and from that union I grew.

The Douglas-fir is now a hundred and eighty feet tall. I am two and a half feet tall. We started in the same year.

• • •

People walk past me. I should tell you this plainly because it is the central fact of my existence. They come up the trail from the river — hikers, families, the occasional botanist — and they look up. They always look up. The cedars, the firs, the hemlocks with their feathered tops swaying two hundred feet above the forest floor. The canopy is what they came for. They photograph it. They crane their necks until their balance shifts and they have to catch themselves. They say words like cathedral and ancient and magnificent, and they are not wrong. The canopy is all of those things.

Then they look down at the trail to check their footing, and their boots pass six inches from my outermost frond, and they do not see me. Or if they see me, they see ferns — a green texture, a background, the stuff between the things that matter. I am not offended by this. I am a background organism. My job is to be the ground that the forest stands on, and ground is not supposed to be noticed. You notice ground when it fails — when the slope gives way, when the soil erodes, when the rain cuts a gully where the roots used to hold. You do not notice ground when it works.

I work.

• • •

On October 11, 1958 — I was twenty-five years old, which for a sword fern is barely adolescent — a rocket lifted off from Cape Canaveral in Florida. I did not know this. I was occupied with the business of October: my older fronds, the ones from the previous spring, had finished producing spores and were beginning the slow bronze decline that would end with them flat on the ground, returning their carbon and minerals to the soil. My newer fronds, unfurled that April, were still deep green and photosynthesizing through every fragment of light that reached me. October in western Washington is the hinge of the year — the rain returns, the light leaves, and everything that is going to survive the winter makes its preparations quietly.

The rocket was called Thor-Able I, and the spacecraft on top of it was called Pioneer 1, and it weighed thirty-four kilograms, which is less than the combined dry weight of my seventy-eight fronds. It was supposed to go to the Moon. It was the first thing that NASA ever launched — the agency had existed for eleven days.

The first stage worked. The Rocketdyne LR-79 engine burned for its full duration. The turbopump bearing that had killed Pioneer 0 two months earlier — the bearing that failed at seventy-three seconds, the bearing that a woman in a clean room had inspected and found acceptable — the new bearing held. The lesson had been learned. The ground that Pioneer 0's failure had prepared was solid, and Pioneer 1 stood on it.

The second stage almost worked. The Aerojet engine burned for a hundred and ten seconds instead of a hundred and twenty, because an accelerometer told the guidance system that the rocket was going fast enough when it was not. Ten seconds short. Two hundred and thirty-four meters per second short. Two point two percent short of the velocity needed to reach the Moon.

Pioneer 1 rose to 113,854 kilometers — one-third of the way to the Moon — and then it stopped rising. The Earth's gravity, patient and absolute, began pulling it back. The spacecraft hung at apogee for a few hours, instruments still running, transmitter still broadcasting, and then it fell. It reentered the atmosphere forty-three hours after launch and burned up over the South Pacific.

It never reached the canopy.

• • •

I want to tell you what I know about not reaching the canopy, because I have been not reaching it for ninety-three years and I have learned a few things.

The first thing is that the light down here is enough. It is not much. On a clear July day, when the sun is directly overhead and the canopy is fully leafed, I receive between one and three percent of the photosynthetically active radiation that hits the top of the forest. One to three percent. If you offered a Douglas-fir seedling one to three percent of full sunlight, it would die. If you offered a fireweed plant one to three percent, it would never flower. Most plants cannot survive on what I survive on. But I am not most plants. I have fronds that are arranged to catch light from any angle — the coins that shift across the forest floor, the ambient glow that filters down through two hundred feet of branches and needles and moss. I waste nothing. Every photon that touches my chloroplasts is used.

Pioneer 1 did not reach the Moon, but its instruments were designed well enough to work wherever they were. As the spacecraft climbed through the inner Van Allen radiation belt, the ionization chamber built by James Van Allen's team at the University of Iowa measured radiation intensities that had never been recorded before. At certain altitudes, the radiation was so intense that the counter saturated — the instrument could not click fast enough to keep up with the particles passing through it. The data looked like silence, but it was the opposite of silence. It was noise so dense it overwhelmed the instrument's ability to count.

Then Pioneer 1 rose through the slot — the region between the inner and outer Van Allen belts, around seven thousand to ten thousand kilometers, where the particle flux drops. The instruments recovered. The counts became meaningful again. And then the outer belt, peaking at sixteen thousand to twenty thousand kilometers, another surge of radiation, and the counter struggled again, and then the spacecraft rose above the outer belt into quieter space, and the instruments measured something close to the background radiation of cislunar space, and the telemetry link — 108.06 megahertz, three hundred milliwatts, a signal weaker than a refrigerator light bulb — carried the data down to the dish at Jodrell Bank and the dish at Goldstone and the receivers at Cape Canaveral.

Forty-three hours. The longest continuous dataset any American spacecraft had returned. A radiation profile from the surface of the Earth to one-third of the way to the Moon. The first measurement of the slot region between the Van Allen belts. The first extended sampling of the outer belt. The micrometeorite count that told spacecraft designers the space between here and the Moon was less abrasive than they feared. The magnetic field measurements that showed where the dipole model held and where it began to fail.

One to three percent of the way to the Moon. And it was enough.

• • •

The second thing I know about not reaching the canopy is that the ground needs tending more than the canopy does. The canopy takes care of itself. A Douglas-fir at a hundred and eighty feet has access to full sunlight, its crown spreads wide, its roots go deep, and its trunk is so thick that nothing short of a chainsaw or a catastrophic windstorm will bring it down. The canopy is powerful and self-sufficient and visible from miles away. People write poems about it. People name national parks after it.

Nobody writes poems about the ground. But the ground is where the water goes when the rain falls. The ground is where the nutrients accumulate when the needles drop. The ground is where the seeds land, where the mycorrhizal networks run, where the salamanders hunt and the decomposers work and the nitrogen fixation happens in the bacterial films on the surfaces of roots. The ground is where the next forest begins. Without the ground, the canopy is just tall wood standing on nothing.

My roots hold the slope. My fronds break the rain. The water that hits my fronds at terminal velocity — kinetic energy that would compact exposed soil into a surface as hard as pavement — is intercepted, slowed, divided into rivulets that run down my rachis and drip gently onto the duff beneath me. I am a shock absorber. I am a hydrological infrastructure component. Every frond in my rosette is a tiny watershed, channeling water from the air to the soil through a path that preserves the soil structure.

Pioneer 1's data held the ground for everything that came after. When NASA began designing the Mercury capsule, they needed to know how much radiation shielding to carry. Too little, and the astronaut would receive a dangerous dose during orbital flight. Too much, and the capsule would be too heavy to launch. The answer came from Pioneer 1's radiation profile. When the Gemini program planned extravehicular activities — astronauts floating outside the spacecraft in nothing but a suit — they needed to know the radiation environment at different altitudes. Pioneer 1 had measured it. When Apollo mission planners chose the trajectory that would carry astronauts through the Van Allen belts en route to the Moon, they chose a fast transit — in and out quickly, minimizing exposure time. They knew exactly how intense the radiation was at each altitude because Pioneer 1 had flown through it with instruments running.

The Apollo astronauts reached the canopy. They stood on the Moon. And the ground they stood on, the engineering ground, the data ground, the we-know-what-the-radiation-looks-like ground, was laid down by a spacecraft that never got past the understory.

• • •

The third thing I know about not reaching the canopy is that persistence is not the same as endurance. Endurance is suffering through something. Persistence is being the thing that is still there when the suffering is over.

In January, when the temperature drops to freezing and the rain turns to sleet and the bigleaf maples are bare and the salal has gone dull and the Oregon grape has pulled its energy down into its roots, I am still green. Not because I enjoy January. I do not enjoy January. My photosynthesis drops to a fraction of its summer rate. My fronds are heavy with water. Some of them are pinned to the ground by fallen branches or the accumulated weight of wet needles from the canopy above. I am cold. I am in the dark. The canopy, which limits my light in summer, at least provides thermal insulation in winter — the forest floor stays a few degrees warmer than the open air. But it is still cold, and it is still dark, and it will be cold and dark for four more months.

I stay green because the alternative is worse. If I dropped my fronds in autumn and grew new ones in spring, I would lose four months of photosynthesis — dim, slow, barely productive photosynthesis, but photosynthesis nonetheless. And I would spend the carbon and energy reserves to build new fronds in spring, reserves that I barely accumulated during the summer, when my one to three percent of sunlight was just barely enough to maintain my existing tissues and store a little for the lean months. The math does not work. Going dormant would cost me more than staying awake. So I stay awake. I persist. Not heroically, not dramatically, not with any particular bravery. I persist because the numbers say persist.

Pioneer 1's transmitter persisted for forty-three hours because the engineers at Space Technology Laboratories built it to work, and it worked. The battery did not care that the spacecraft was on the wrong trajectory. The transmitter did not care that the Moon was getting farther away instead of closer. The ionization chamber did not care that the mission had failed by its primary objective. The instruments measured, the transmitter transmitted, and the ground stations received. For forty-three hours, in the wrong place, on the wrong trajectory, falling back toward a fiery end, the spacecraft did the only thing it could do: it gathered data and sent it home.

The numbers said persist. It persisted.

• • •

When Pioneer 1 reentered the atmosphere over the South Pacific on October 13, 1958, it was traveling at approximately eleven kilometers per second. At that velocity, the friction between the spacecraft's skin and the thickening air generated temperatures in excess of ten thousand degrees. The gold coating on the truncated cone vaporized. The instruments that had faithfully measured radiation for forty-three hours melted. The transmitter that had broadcast its three hundred milliwatts across a hundred thousand kilometers went silent. Everything that had been a spacecraft became a streak of ionized gas in the upper atmosphere, and then nothing.

But the data was already on the ground. Recorded on magnetic tape at Jodrell Bank, at Goldstone, at Cape Canaveral. Forty-three hours of measurements. The radiation profile. The magnetic field survey. The micrometeorite count. The proof that the hydrazine attitude control thrusters worked. All of it safely on the ground before the spacecraft that collected it ceased to exist.

This is what spores are. My fronds produce millions of spores per year. Each spore is a packet of genetic information launched into the air — tiny, light enough to ride the wind, carrying everything needed to start a new fern if it lands on the right substrate. The frond that produced the spore will eventually die. The plant that produced the frond will eventually die. But the spores are already on the ground, already germinating, already becoming the next generation of prothalli that will become the next generation of ferns that will hold the next generation of slopes.

The data is the spore. The spacecraft is the frond. The frond dies. The spore persists.

• • •

There is a varied thrush calling from the hemlock above me. I cannot see it — I have no eyes, I have no sense organs of any kind, I am aware of the bird only because this is a story and stories require certain liberties. But if I could hear it, I would hear a single sustained note, a pure tone held for two seconds in the key of something lonely, and then silence, and then another note at a different pitch, equally pure, equally sustained, equally lonely. The varied thrush is the sound of the understory. It does not sing from the canopy. It sings from the middle layers, the hemlock branches, the vine maple tangles, the shadowed spaces where the light is dim and the air is still and the sound carries without competition.

It is the most beautiful sound in the Pacific Northwest forest, and most people who hear it do not know what it is. They hear it and they stop walking and they look around and they cannot find the source. The sound seems to come from everywhere and nowhere. It seems to come from the forest itself, as if the trees were humming a single note.

I think Pioneer 1's signal must have sounded like that to the technicians at the tracking stations. A single frequency — 108.06 megahertz — steady, pure, carrying data in its modulations but fundamentally just a tone, a sustained note from something they could not see, traveling across a hundred thousand kilometers of empty space to reach a dish pointed at exactly the right patch of sky. The signal getting weaker as the spacecraft climbed higher. The signal pausing at apogee as the spacecraft hung between going and returning. The signal growing stronger as the spacecraft fell back toward Earth. And then the signal stopping, as the spacecraft entered the atmosphere and the plasma sheath of reentry blocked the transmission and then destroyed the transmitter and then destroyed the spacecraft.

A varied thrush sings for a season and then is silent. A spacecraft transmits for forty-three hours and then is gone. A sword fern holds the ground for a century and then its rhizome finally decays and the gap it leaves is filled by the spores it spent decades releasing. Nothing in the understory lasts forever. Everything in the understory lasts long enough.

• • •

I have seventy-eight fronds. Each frond has between sixty and ninety pinnae. Each pinna has a small angular bump at its base — the auricle — that looks like the crossguard on a sword hilt. This is my name: sword fern. I did not choose it. The Coast Salish people who lived here before the settlers had their own name for me, and they used my fronds to line their pit ovens and wrap their fish and cover the floors of their longhouses. They used me because I was everywhere and because I was tough and because I did not impart off-flavors and because I was available in every season. I was infrastructure. I was the green material that made daily life slightly better in small, uncelebrated ways.

Pioneer 1 was infrastructure too. Not the Moon mission it was meant to be, but the radiation survey that made Moon missions possible. Not the destination, but the map of the route. Not the canopy, but the ground.

The ground does not ask to be noticed. The ground asks only to hold.

I hold.

For Vincent van Gogh (March 30, 1853 — July 29, 1890), who painted the ground others walked past — the irises, the wheat, the dark earth beneath the stars.
