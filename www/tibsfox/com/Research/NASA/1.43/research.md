# Mission 1.43 -- Gemini 6A

## Deep Research: The First Rendezvous

## The Mission That Should Not Have Existed

Gemini 6A was not supposed to happen. The mission that achieved the first rendezvous in space was an improvisation -- born from the failure of the original Gemini 6 plan, redesigned in six weeks, and executed with an audacity that makes it one of the most remarkable achievements in spaceflight history.

The original plan was straightforward. Gemini 6 was to launch in late October 1965, rendezvous with an unmanned Agena Target Vehicle (ATV) that would be launched earlier the same day on an Atlas-Agena D, and dock with it -- the first rendezvous and docking in space. The Agena would serve as a passive target: Gemini 6 would use its onboard radar and computer to navigate to the Agena, approach it, and connect the spacecraft's nose to the Agena's docking collar.

On October 25, 1965, the Atlas-Agena carrying the target vehicle launched from Cape Kennedy's Pad 14 at 15:00 UTC. The Atlas performed nominally. The Agena separated from the Atlas and should have ignited its own engine to enter orbit. It did not. Telemetry showed the Agena exploding approximately six minutes after launch. The target vehicle was destroyed. Gemini 6's target was gone.

Wally Schirra and Tom Stafford, suited and sealed in their Gemini spacecraft on Pad 19, were told to stand down. The mission was scrubbed. There was no target to rendezvous with.

---

## The Improvisation

What happened next was unprecedented in the history of spaceflight. Within hours of the Agena failure, engineers at the Manned Spacecraft Center in Houston and at McDonnell Aircraft in St. Louis began discussing an alternative: what if Gemini 6 rendezvoused with Gemini 7 instead?

Gemini 7 was already scheduled to launch on December 3, 1965, for its fourteen-day endurance mission. If Gemini 6 could be launched while Gemini 7 was still in orbit, the two crewed spacecraft could perform a rendezvous without needing the Agena at all. Gemini 7 would serve as the target vehicle -- a far more complex target than the Agena, but one that was guaranteed to be in orbit if it launched successfully.

The idea was elegant but the logistics were daunting. No pad at Cape Kennedy had ever processed two Gemini missions simultaneously. Pad 19 would need to be refurbished after Gemini 7's launch and prepared for Gemini 6A's launch within 8-10 days -- a turnaround that normally took months. The Titan II launch vehicle for Gemini 6A was already erected at the pad (it had been destacked after the October scrub), but it would need to be rebuilt, tested, and certified flight-ready in the compressed timeline.

NASA approved the plan on October 28, three days after the Agena failure. The mission was redesignated Gemini 6A (the "A" indicating the revised mission) to distinguish it from the original Gemini 6 that would have docked with the Agena. Gemini 6A would not dock with Gemini 7 -- the Gemini spacecraft had no docking mechanism compatible with another Gemini. It would rendezvous: approach, station-keep, and fly in formation. The objective was to demonstrate that two spacecraft could find each other in orbit, navigate to within close proximity, and maintain relative position. The docking would wait for a future mission with a future Agena.

## The False Start

Gemini 7 launched on December 4, 1965, and entered a nominal orbit. Borman and Lovell began their fourteen-day mission. The clock was running -- Gemini 6A had to launch before Gemini 7's mission ended.

Pad 19 was refurbished in a record nine days. The Titan II was restacked, tested, and prepared. Gemini 6A's spacecraft was mated to the launch vehicle. On December 12, 1965, Schirra and Stafford climbed into their spacecraft for a launch attempt.

At 14:54 UTC on December 12, the Titan II's engines ignited. For 1.2 seconds, the engines produced thrust. Then the engine shutdown system activated -- an automatic sequence that detected an abnormal condition and shut down the engines. The Titan settled back onto the pad. The hold-down clamps had not released. The booster was sitting on the launch pad with fuel and oxidizer in its tanks, engines recently fired, and two astronauts in the spacecraft on top.

This was a crisis. The mission rules were explicit: if the engines ignited and then shut down, the crew should eject. The Gemini ejection seats would fire the astronauts clear of the launch vehicle, which might be about to explode. The ejection would save the crew but would destroy the spacecraft, ending any possibility of a second launch attempt.

Wally Schirra made a decision that defined his career. He did not eject. He felt no upward motion -- the spacecraft had not lifted off the pad -- and he judged that the shutdown had been clean, the engines were not malfunctioning, and the vehicle was stable. He sat in his seat, hand on the ejection handle, and waited.

The decision was correct. Post-incident analysis revealed two problems. First, an electrical plug at the base of the Titan had disconnected prematurely (at engine ignition rather than at liftoff), triggering the automatic shutdown. Second, an oxidizer dust cap had been left in a fuel line during pad processing -- a maintenance error that would have caused an engine problem if the flight had continued. Schirra's non-ejection saved the mission: the spacecraft was intact, the launch vehicle was repairable, and a second attempt was possible.

The plug disconnection was traced to a technician's error during pad refurbishment -- the compressed timeline had cut into the margin for quality control. The dust cap was a separate error, discovered during the post-shutdown inspection. Two independent failures, both consequences of the accelerated schedule, both caught before they could cause a catastrophe.

## Launch and Chase

Three days later, at 13:37:26 UTC on December 15, 1965, Gemini 6A launched successfully from Pad 19. The Titan II performed nominally. Schirra and Stafford entered a lower orbit than Gemini 7 -- approximately 161 km circular versus Gemini 7's approximately 300 km orbit. This was by design: the lower orbit gave Gemini 6A a faster orbital period, allowing it to "catch up" to Gemini 7 from behind.

The orbital mechanics of rendezvous are counterintuitive. To catch an object ahead of you in orbit, you do not speed up -- speeding up raises your orbit, which actually slows your orbital period. Instead, you slow down (dropping to a lower orbit, which has a faster period), let the orbital mechanics bring you closer, and then raise your orbit at the right moment to match the target. The process is a series of carefully timed thruster burns computed by the onboard guidance computer and verified by ground tracking.

Gemini 6A executed four orbital maneuvers over the course of approximately six hours:

**Maneuver 1 (2h 18m after launch):** A height adjustment burn that refined the orbit from the initial insertion trajectory to the phasing orbit. This burn placed Gemini 6A in a precise orbit that would bring it to the same orbital plane and altitude as Gemini 7 at a predicted time.

**Maneuver 2 (3h 47m):** A co-elliptic maneuver that placed Gemini 6A in an orbit with its apogee matching Gemini 7's orbital altitude. At apogee, the two spacecraft would be at the same altitude but separated by a closing distance.

**Maneuver 3 (5h 4m):** A terminal phase initiation (TPI) burn, executed when Gemini 7 was at a specific elevation angle above Gemini 6A's local horizontal. This burn began the final approach phase, with Gemini 6A climbing toward Gemini 7 along a trajectory designed to intercept the target's orbit.

**Maneuver 4 (5h 50m):** Braking burns to null the closing velocity as Gemini 6A approached Gemini 7. These were manual burns, executed by Schirra using visual reference through the spacecraft window and the onboard radar range and range-rate display.

## Thirty Centimeters

At approximately 20:33 UTC on December 15, 1965 -- five hours and fifty-six minutes after launch -- Gemini 6A closed to within approximately 30 centimeters (one foot) of Gemini 7. The two spacecraft were in the same orbit, traveling at approximately 28,000 km/h, separated by less than an arm's length.

The rendezvous was complete.

For the next five hours and eighteen minutes, Schirra maneuvered Gemini 6A around Gemini 7 in a series of exercises that tested station-keeping, fly-around inspection, and formation flying. The two spacecraft circled each other, approached and retreated, flew in parallel and in tandem. At one point, Schirra flew Gemini 6A in a complete 360-degree loop around Gemini 7 while Borman and Lovell watched from inside their spacecraft and photographed the maneuver.

The photographs from this session became some of the most iconic images in spaceflight history. Gemini 7, seen from Gemini 6A's window, hangs against the black of space with the curve of the Earth below -- a cone-shaped spacecraft trailing its adapter section, the first time one crewed spacecraft had been photographed from another in orbit.

Schirra later described the rendezvous as "the most satisfying thing I ever did in my life." The physics were abstract -- orbital mechanics, relative velocity, radar ranging -- but the result was visceral: two human crews, face to face through spacecraft windows, in the absolute void of space. Borman flashed his spacecraft's lights. Schirra waved through the window. The four astronauts could see each other's faces.

The relative velocity at closest approach was essentially zero -- Schirra had nulled the closing rate through a series of precise braking burns, bringing Gemini 6A to a stop relative to Gemini 7 with centimeter-level accuracy. This was not autopilot. Schirra flew the final approach manually, using the onboard radar for range and range-rate information and the window for visual reference. The precision was a testament to both the astronaut's piloting skill and the accuracy of the onboard guidance computer's solutions.

## The Orbital Mechanics of Rendezvous

The rendezvous problem is one of the most elegantly counterintuitive problems in orbital mechanics. Two spacecraft in different orbits around the same central body must alter their orbits to arrive at the same point in space at the same time with the same velocity. The solution requires an understanding of Kepler's laws that defies terrestrial intuition.

On Earth, if you want to catch something ahead of you, you go faster. In orbit, if you go faster, you enter a higher orbit -- and a higher orbit has a longer period. You actually fall behind. Conversely, if you slow down, you drop to a lower orbit with a shorter period, and you catch up. The rendezvous pilot must fly backward to go forward.

The mathematical framework was developed by Buzz Aldrin (who later flew on Gemini 12 and Apollo 11) in his MIT doctoral thesis, "Line-of-Sight Guidance Techniques for Manned Orbital Rendezvous" (1963). Aldrin's work showed that the terminal phase of a rendezvous could be managed using a simple relationship between the target's elevation angle (as seen from the chaser) and the time to intercept. The TPI burn should be initiated when the target is at a specific elevation angle (approximately 27 degrees above the local horizontal), and the subsequent trajectory will naturally bring the chaser to the target if the initial orbit was correctly phased.

Gemini 6A validated Aldrin's theory. The onboard guidance computer calculated the TPI burn parameters, Schirra executed them, and the spacecraft followed the predicted trajectory to within meters of the target. The rendezvous was not a lucky approach -- it was a mathematical proof executed in hardware, a doctoral thesis tested at 28,000 km/h.

## Wally Schirra: The Only Astronaut to Fly Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo

Walter Marty Schirra Jr. was born on March 12, 1923, in Hackensack, New Jersey. His father was a World War I ace who became a barnstormer; his mother was a wing-walker. Aviation was the family business.

Schirra graduated from the United States Naval Academy in 1945, flew combat missions in Korea (where he shot down one MiG-15 and damaged two others), and became a test pilot at the Naval Air Test Center at Patuxent River. He was selected as one of the original Mercury Seven astronauts in April 1959.

Schirra flew Mercury-Atlas 8 (Sigma 7) on October 3, 1962 -- a six-orbit engineering test flight that was the most technically precise Mercury mission, with the closest-to-target splashdown and the least fuel consumption. Mercury established Schirra as the precision pilot.

Gemini 6A established Schirra as the rendezvous pilot. His manual approach to within 30 centimeters of Gemini 7, his decision not to eject during the pad abort, and his methodical execution of the orbital mechanics were the defining moments of the Gemini program.

Apollo 7, launched on October 11, 1968, was Schirra's final flight -- the first crewed Apollo mission, an eleven-day shakedown of the Command Module after the Apollo 1 fire that killed Gus Grissom, Ed White, and Roger Chaffee. Schirra was grumpy, combative with mission control, and suffering from a severe head cold. He argued with the flight director about removing helmets during reentry (Schirra won) and about conducting a scheduled television broadcast (Schirra lost, grudgingly). The mission was a technical success but a public relations headache.

Schirra retired from NASA and the Navy after Apollo 7, the only astronaut to fly all three of America's first crewed space programs. He died on May 3, 2007, at the age of 84.

## The Jingle Bells Incident

During the rendezvous fly-around on December 15, Stafford reported to mission control: "We have an object, looks like a satellite going from north to south, probably in a polar orbit... Looks like he might be going to re-enter soon... I see a command module and eight smaller modules in front. The pilot of the command module is wearing a red suit."

Then Schirra and Stafford played "Jingle Bells" on a harmonica and sleigh bells they had smuggled aboard the spacecraft. It was four days before Christmas. The music -- thin and tinny through the spacecraft's communication system -- was the first music played in space.

Mission control, after a moment of stunned silence, laughed. The harmonica and bells are now in the collection of the Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum.

## Reentry and Splashdown

Following the rendezvous exercises, Gemini 6A separated from Gemini 7 and prepared for reentry. The two spacecraft would not land together -- Gemini 6A's mission was only 25 hours long, while Gemini 7 had three more days in orbit.

At 15:28:28 UTC on December 16, 1965, Gemini 6A's retrorockets fired. The reentry was nominal. Splashdown occurred at 15:28:50 UTC in the western Atlantic Ocean, approximately 13 kilometers from the planned recovery point -- another precise landing from Schirra, the precision pilot.

The recovery carrier USS Wasp retrieved Schirra and Stafford. The same carrier would retrieve Borman and Lovell two days later, when Gemini 7 completed its fourteen-day mission.

## The Rendezvous Legacy

Gemini 6A proved that rendezvous in orbit was possible, practical, and precise. The implications for Apollo were immediate and specific:

**Lunar Orbit Rendezvous (LOR) was validated.** NASA's chosen mission architecture for reaching the Moon required the Lunar Module to separate from the Command Module in lunar orbit, descend to the surface, ascend back to orbit, and rendezvous with the Command Module for the return to Earth. If rendezvous did not work reliably, LOR was a death trap -- astronauts would be stranded in lunar orbit with no way to get home. Gemini 6A showed that two spacecraft could find each other, approach to within centimeters, and maintain formation. The rendezvous half of LOR was proven.

**The guidance and navigation system worked.** The onboard computer's orbital mechanics solutions were accurate. The radar provided reliable range and range-rate data. The visual approach technique was feasible. The entire rendezvous sequence -- from phasing burns through terminal approach to station-keeping -- executed as predicted by theory and simulation. The hardware and the math were both validated.

**Pilot skill was sufficient.** Schirra flew the final approach manually, demonstrating that a human pilot could manage the delicate task of nulling relative velocity and maintaining station in close proximity to another spacecraft. This was not a given -- many engineers had argued that the fine control required for close-proximity operations was beyond human capability and would require automatic systems. Schirra proved them wrong.

The docking would come on Gemini 8 in March 1966, when Neil Armstrong successfully docked with an Agena target vehicle (before a stuck thruster nearly killed the crew). But the rendezvous -- the harder problem, the one that required both precise computation and precise piloting -- was solved on December 15, 1965, by Wally Schirra in Gemini 6A, closing to within 30 centimeters of a spacecraft that had been in orbit for eleven days.
