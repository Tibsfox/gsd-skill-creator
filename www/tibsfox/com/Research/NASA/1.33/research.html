Deep Research: Perfect Aim, Blind Eyes

Ranger 6 — the most accurate failure in NASA history

The Most Accurate Failure in NASA History

At 15:49:00 UTC on January 30, 1964, an Atlas-Agena B launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Canaveral, carrying the Ranger 6 spacecraft. Sixty-five and a half hours later, the 381-kilogram spacecraft struck the Moon in the Sea of Tranquility at 9.39 km/s, within 32 kilometers of the aim point — the most accurate lunar intercept NASA had ever achieved.

But thirteen minutes before impact, when the command was sent to power on the six television cameras, nothing happened. All six were dead — killed by an electrical arc during Atlas staging at T+107 seconds, when the cameras were briefly energized inside the launch shroud at Paschen minimum pressure.

The full research document is available in research.md.

Key Sections

  • The Camera Failure: Electromagnetic transient during Atlas staging, Paschen minimum arcing, vidicon tube destruction
  • The Trajectory: Navigation triumph — 32 km accuracy from 384,400 km, midcourse correction, terminal approach
  • The Ranger Program: Six consecutive failures (Rangers 1-6), then three successes (Rangers 7-9)
  • The Investigation: 100+ corrective recommendations that enabled Ranger 7's 4,316 photographs
  • The Dedication: Mary Jackson — first African American female engineer at NASA, structural contributions invisible for decades
