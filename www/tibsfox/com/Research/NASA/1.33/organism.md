# Organism Pairing: Thuja plicata (Western Red Cedar)

## The Tree of Life

**Scientific Name:** Thuja plicata Donn ex D.Don
**Family:** Cupressaceae (cypress family)
**Common Names:** Western red cedar, giant arborvitae, canoe cedar, shinglewood
**Paired Mission:** 1.33 -- Ranger 6 (Atlas-Agena B), January 30, 1964
**PNW Status:** Native, dominant -- the Tree of Life of the Pacific Northwest

---

## What Arrives Perfectly and Is Overlooked

Stand in an old-growth forest on the western slope of the Cascades and look for the tree that is not Douglas-fir. The one with the buttressed base -- not round like a Douglas-fir trunk but flared, spreading outward into root buttresses that look like the flying buttresses of a cathedral. The bark is thin, fibrous, grey-brown, and comes apart in long strips if you pull on it -- Coast Salish peoples have been stripping cedar bark for weaving for millennia. The foliage is not needles but flat sprays of scale-like leaves, arranged in overlapping layers that create a lacy, drooping curtain of green. The tree smells like pencil shavings and canoe workshops and ceremony. This is western red cedar. Thuja plicata. The Tree of Life.

Western red cedar is not the tallest tree in the PNW forest -- that distinction belongs to Douglas-fir. It is not the fastest-growing. It is not the most commercially valuable timber species (Douglas-fir again). It does not form the emergent canopy that defines the forest silhouette. Western red cedar grows in the shade, tolerates conditions that would kill a Douglas-fir seedling, and persists for over a thousand years. The oldest confirmed western red cedar specimens exceed 1,500 years. They have been growing since the Roman Empire.

The parallel to Ranger 6 is structural and precise. Ranger 6 arrived at the Moon with flawless trajectory -- the most accurate lunar intercept in the Ranger program. The navigation was perfect. The approach was textbook. But the cameras were dead, and without photographs, the mission was officially a failure. Western red cedar arrives in the forest with flawless ecological timing -- shade-tolerant, rot-resistant, perfectly adapted to the wet, dark understory. But it does not tower. It does not break through the canopy. In Western forestry's metrics, it is less commercially dominant than Douglas-fir.

Both are undervalued by the measurement systems that evaluate them. Ranger 6 was measured by its photographs -- zero. Western red cedar was measured by its board-feet -- less than Douglas-fir. But Ranger 6's trajectory data informed every subsequent lunar mission. And western red cedar's cultural and ecological contributions -- longhouses, canoes, nurse logs, ceremonial life -- are incalculable.

## Morphology and Life History

Western red cedar is a large evergreen conifer, reaching heights of 50 to 65 meters and diameters of 3 to 6 meters in old-growth conditions. The trunk tapers dramatically, broadening at the base into root buttresses that provide structural stability in wet, shallow soils. The crown is conical in young trees and becomes irregular, flat-topped, and multi-leadered in old specimens, often with dead tops where lightning or wind has broken the original leader.

The bark is thin -- 1 to 2 centimeters, compared to Douglas-fir's 15 to 30 centimeters. This thin bark is the tree's vulnerability to fire. Unlike Douglas-fir, which survives ground fires through its thick bark insulation, western red cedar is easily killed by even moderate fire. This fire sensitivity explains cedar's prevalence in wet sites -- riparian corridors, north-facing slopes, valley bottoms -- where fire is rare. In fire-prone upland sites, Douglas-fir dominates because it survives fires that kill cedars.

The wood is perhaps the most remarkable feature. Western red cedar heartwood contains thujaplicins -- tropolone compounds that are toxic to fungi, bacteria, and many insects. This natural preservative makes cedar wood extraordinarily rot-resistant. A fallen cedar log on the forest floor may retain structural integrity for 300 to 500 years -- three to five times longer than a Douglas-fir log. Cedar fence posts outlast fir posts by decades. Cedar shingles resist rot for 50+ years without chemical treatment.

This rot resistance is the material property that made western red cedar the Tree of Life for Coast Salish peoples. The wood is light, straight-grained, easily split with stone or bone tools, and resistant to the wet climate of the PNW. From a single large cedar tree, Coast Salish artisans could produce:

- **Longhouse planks:** Split planks up to 10 meters long and 50 centimeters wide, forming the walls and roofs of permanent winter dwellings
- **Canoes:** Dugout canoes carved from single trunks, ranging from small river canoes to oceangoing war canoes over 15 meters long
- **Totem poles:** Monumental carved poles commemorating lineage, stories, and events, standing 10 to 20+ meters tall
- **Bentwood boxes:** Steam-bent storage boxes carved from single planks, kerfs scored and bent at 90-degree angles, watertight without adhesive
- **Bark products:** Inner bark stripped, processed, and woven into clothing, mats, baskets, rope, and ceremonial regalia

The tree provided the material infrastructure of an entire civilization. Every building, every watercraft, every storage container, every woven garment could be made from western red cedar. The Tree of Life is not a metaphor. It is a material fact.

## Ecological Role: The Patient Builder

Western red cedar's role in the PNW forest is architectural, but it operates on a different timescale than Douglas-fir.

**Shade tolerance:** Cedar is highly shade-tolerant -- it can germinate and grow in as little as 2-5% of full sunlight. This allows it to establish beneath a closed Douglas-fir canopy where Douglas-fir's own seedlings cannot survive. In the absence of fire, cedar gradually fills the understory, waiting for canopy gaps created by windthrow or the death of old Douglas-firs. When a gap opens, the cedar beneath it is already there -- already established, already growing, already positioned. It does not need to race for the gap. It was patient enough to be in position before the gap existed.

**Nurse log ecology:** Western red cedar nurse logs are the most persistent in the PNW forest. A fallen cedar, protected by its thujaplicins from rapid decay, provides a raised seedbed for centuries. Hemlock, spruce, and even Douglas-fir seedlings germinate on cedar nurse logs. The rows of "stilt-rooted" trees walking on raised root arches through old-growth forests are usually standing on the ghost of a cedar nurse log that decomposed long after the seedlings it supported became mature trees.

**Riparian specialist:** Cedar dominates stream corridors, wetland margins, and other sites where water saturation makes fire unlikely. In these wet sites, cedar achieves its maximum size and age. The largest known specimens -- the Quinault Lake Red Cedar in Olympic National Forest (estimated 19.5 meters in circumference) and the Cheewhat Giant on Vancouver Island (estimated 18.3 meters in circumference) -- grow in extremely wet sites.

## Resonance Axes

### 1. Perfect Arrival, No Monument
Western red cedar arrives in the forest with ecological precision -- shade-tolerant, rot-resistant, positioned exactly where it is needed. But it does not tower above the canopy. It does not define the forest silhouette. In Western forestry's metrics, it is secondary to Douglas-fir. Ranger 6 arrived at the Moon with navigational precision -- 32 km from the target, the most accurate Ranger impact. But it returned zero photographs. In NASA's metrics, it was a failure. Both arrived perfectly. Both were measured by the wrong metric.

### 2. The Foundation That Outlasts the Builder
Cedar nurse logs persist for centuries. The seedlings they support grow into trees that outlive the nurse log itself. Ranger 6's failure analysis persisted through Rangers 7, 8, and 9, through Surveyor, through Apollo. The electromagnetic shielding redesign informed every subsequent spacecraft. Both cedar and Ranger 6 built foundations that outlasted their own visible existence.

### 3. Thin Bark, Thick Wood
Cedar bark is thin -- vulnerable to fire, to physical damage, to the transients that its environment delivers. But the wood beneath is nearly indestructible. Ranger 6's electromagnetic shielding was thin -- vulnerable to the staging transient. But the trajectory beneath was nearly perfect. Both organisms have a vulnerability at the surface and strength at the core.

### 4. The Tree of Life
Coast Salish peoples named western red cedar the Tree of Life because every part serves a purpose. Ranger 6, despite its camera failure, served multiple purposes: trajectory validation, navigation technique confirmation, failure analysis that saved the program. The mission that returned zero photographs returned something more important -- the knowledge of how to succeed next time. The tree that does not tower provides the material for everything else.

### 5. Shade Tolerance as Persistence
Cedar survives in conditions that kill other species -- deep shade, saturated soils, minimal resources. It persists not through dominance but through endurance. The Ranger program survived six consecutive failures -- Rangers 1 through 6. Other programs would have been cancelled. Ranger persisted, in the shade of Congressional criticism and public disappointment, until the canopy opened and Rangers 7-9 delivered thousands of photographs. Cedar's shade tolerance and Ranger's institutional persistence are the same adaptation: survive the dark period until the light arrives.

### 6. Cultural Versus Scientific Value
The gap between western red cedar's cultural value to Coast Salish peoples and its ranking in Western forestry is enormous. The most important tree in one value system is secondary in another. Ranger 6 was officially a failure -- zero photographs. But its trajectory data, its failure analysis, and its contribution to Ranger 7's success make it one of the most important missions in the program. The official ranking and the actual value diverge. Cedar and Ranger 6 both deserve re-evaluation by broader criteria.

## Taxonomy Note

*Thuja plicata* was described by David Don in 1824, based on material collected by various expeditions to the Pacific Northwest. The genus name *Thuja* derives from the Greek *thuia* or *thuon*, a name used by Theophrastus for an aromatic wood -- possibly a Mediterranean cypress or juniper. The species epithet *plicata* means "pleated" or "braided," referring to the flat, overlapping arrangement of the scale-like leaves.

The common name "western red cedar" is a misnomer -- Thuja plicata is not a true cedar (genus Cedrus, family Pinaceae). True cedars are Old World conifers: Atlas cedar, Lebanon cedar, deodar cedar. Western red cedar belongs to the cypress family (Cupressaceae), making it more closely related to junipers, redwoods, and bald cypress than to true cedars. The name "red cedar" refers to the heartwood color -- a warm reddish-brown when freshly cut, weathering to the silver-grey that defines PNW architecture.

The misnamed species parallels the misclassified mission. "Cedar" that is not a cedar. "Failure" that was not a failure. The names we give things often reflect our expectations rather than their nature.
