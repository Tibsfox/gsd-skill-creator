# Mission 1.33 -- Ranger 6 (Atlas-Agena B)

## Deep Research: Perfect Aim, Blind Eyes

**Mission:** 1.33 / NASA Mission Series
**Date:** January 30, 1964
**Type:** Lunar impactor (TV imaging)
**Program:** Ranger (NASA / Jet Propulsion Laboratory)
**Epoch:** 2 (Lunar Reconnaissance)
**Organism Pairing:** Thuja plicata (Western Red Cedar)
**Bird (Degree 32):** Bohemian Waxwing
**Dedication:** Mary Jackson (April 9, 1921 -- February 11, 2005)
**Status:** PARTIAL FAILURE -- trajectory perfect, all six television cameras dead on arrival

---

## The Most Accurate Failure in NASA History

At 15:49:00 UTC on January 30, 1964, an Atlas-Agena B launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 12 at Cape Canaveral, carrying the Ranger 6 spacecraft. The mission was the sixth in the Ranger program -- JPL's series of lunar impactor probes designed to photograph the Moon's surface at close range in the final minutes before crashing into it. The Atlas booster performed nominally. The Agena B second stage ignited, burned, and placed the spacecraft in a parking orbit. The Agena reignited at the proper time for translunar injection. The separation from the Agena was clean. Ranger 6 was on its way to the Moon.

The spacecraft weighed approximately 381 kilograms. It carried six RCA television cameras -- two full-scan and four partial-scan -- mounted in a steerable camera platform at the base of the spacecraft. The cameras were the entire scientific payload. Ranger was not a soft lander, not an orbiter, not a flyby probe. It was a bullet aimed at the Moon, designed to take photographs as it fell, transmitting them back to Earth in real time until the moment of impact destroyed the spacecraft. The cameras were Ranger's eyes. Without them, the mission was a 381-kilogram aluminum projectile with no purpose.

Sixty-five and a half hours after launch, at approximately 09:24 UTC on February 2, 1964, Ranger 6 struck the surface of the Moon in the Sea of Tranquility -- Mare Tranquillitatis -- at a velocity of approximately 9.39 kilometers per second. The impact point was within 32 kilometers of the intended target, making Ranger 6 the most accurately guided lunar spacecraft NASA had ever flown. The trajectory was, by every measure, a triumph. The navigation was flawless. The midcourse correction maneuver, executed 16 hours after launch, had placed the spacecraft on a near-perfect terminal approach. The tracking data confirmed that Ranger 6 would hit the Moon within the planned target zone.

But thirteen minutes before impact, when the onboard sequencer sent the command to activate the television cameras, the telemetry showed no response. No video signal. No camera system power draw. Nothing. The command was sent again. Still nothing. Manual commands were sent from the ground. Nothing. All six cameras were dead.

Ranger 6 hit the Moon precisely where it was aimed and returned zero photographs.

## What Killed the Cameras

The investigation that followed was one of the most thorough failure analyses in JPL history. The cameras had worked perfectly in ground testing. The camera system design had been validated through extensive qualification. The telemetry during the three-day transit showed no anomaly in the camera system -- because the cameras were not powered during transit. The first indication that anything was wrong came only when the cameras were supposed to turn on for the terminal imaging sequence, thirteen minutes before impact.

The failure was traced to an event that had occurred 107 seconds after launch -- sixty-five hours before anyone knew the cameras were dead.

During Atlas booster engine cutoff and staging, the explosive separation of the booster engines created a violent electromagnetic transient. Electrical energy surged through the spacecraft wiring harness. The camera system's power bus, which should have been completely isolated during launch, was briefly energized by this transient. For a fraction of a second -- perhaps as little as 50 milliseconds -- the cameras received power.

The cameras were still inside the launch shroud. At 107 seconds after launch, the shroud had not yet been jettisoned. The altitude was approximately 90 kilometers -- high enough that the atmospheric pressure was very low, but not zero. At the pressures inside the partially evacuated shroud, the residual gas was in exactly the wrong regime for high-voltage electronics. This is the Paschen minimum -- the pressure range at which the breakdown voltage of a gas is at its lowest. At atmospheric pressure, the air can withstand high voltages. In hard vacuum, there is no gas to break down. But at pressures between approximately 1 and 10 torr -- the pressure range inside the shroud at 90 km altitude -- electrical arcing occurs at much lower voltages than at either extreme.

The RCA vidicon camera tubes operated with internal voltages exceeding 10,000 volts. When the transient energized the cameras at an altitude where the Paschen minimum was in effect, the high-voltage sections of the camera circuits arced. The arcing destroyed the vidicon tubes -- the electron-gun assemblies, the deflection systems, the target electrodes. All six cameras were killed simultaneously by the same mechanism: transient power during a pressure regime where arcing was inevitable.

The investigation team called it a "design susceptibility" -- the camera system was not designed to survive being powered during the pressure transient of ascent. The electromagnetic shielding between the staging transient and the camera power bus was inadequate. The design assumed the cameras would never receive power until the spacecraft was in hard vacuum, and at hard vacuum the high voltages would be safe. The assumption was correct in vacuum. It was fatally wrong during the 90-kilometer altitude window where the shroud pressure was at the Paschen minimum.

One electromagnetic transient. Less than a second of power. Six dead cameras. Sixty-five hours of silent transit. Zero photographs.

## The Ranger Program: A Troubled History

Ranger 6 was the sixth of nine Ranger missions, and it continued a pattern of failure that had brought the program to the brink of cancellation.

**Ranger 1 (August 23, 1961):** Failed to leave parking orbit. Agena B re-ignition failure. Spacecraft reentered and burned up.

**Ranger 2 (November 18, 1961):** Same failure mode -- Agena B did not reignite. Spacecraft reentered and burned up. Two consecutive Agena failures.

**Ranger 3 (January 26, 1962):** Excessive velocity from Atlas guidance error. Missed the Moon by approximately 36,800 km. Spacecraft entered heliocentric orbit. Camera system was never tested on the Moon because the Moon was never reached.

**Ranger 4 (April 23, 1962):** Computer failure shortly after launch. Spacecraft became uncontrollable. Hit the far side of the Moon -- the first US spacecraft to hit the Moon -- but could not be commanded. No science data returned.

**Ranger 5 (October 18, 1962):** Power failure. Spacecraft went dead 8 hours after launch. Missed the Moon by 725 km. Entered heliocentric orbit. No science data.

**Ranger 6 (January 30, 1964):** Perfect trajectory. Dead cameras. Impacted the Moon with zero photographs.

Six missions. Zero successful science returns. The pattern was devastating: the first two failed to leave Earth orbit, the next three either missed the Moon or hit it uncontrollably, and the sixth arrived perfectly but was blind. Each mission had a different failure mode. The program was not converging -- it was finding new ways to fail.

By early 1964, Congress and NASA management were asking whether JPL could deliver a functioning lunar probe. The Ranger program had consumed approximately $170 million (1964 dollars) with no scientific return. JPL's reputation was on the line. Homer Newell, NASA's Associate Administrator for Space Science and Applications, ordered a comprehensive review of the Ranger program after Ranger 6. The review, led by a board chaired by JPL's own staff but including external reviewers, produced over 100 recommendations for changes to the Ranger 7 spacecraft and testing procedures.

The Ranger 6 failure investigation was the most important outcome of the mission. The electromagnetic shielding redesign, the camera system power isolation improvements, the new testing protocols that simulated the ascent pressure profile -- all of these fed directly into Ranger 7. Six months after Ranger 6 hit the Moon blind, Ranger 7 hit the Moon with open eyes and returned 4,316 photographs -- the first close-up images of the lunar surface from an American spacecraft. Ranger 8 followed with 7,137 images. Ranger 9, the final mission, returned 5,814 images including live television broadcast to the public.

Ranger 6 was the last failure. Everything after it worked. The failure analysis was the bridge.

## The Spacecraft

Ranger 6 represented the Block III configuration -- the third and final redesign of the Ranger spacecraft, optimized for the television imaging mission after the earlier Block I (engineering test) and Block II (scientific lander/capsule) configurations had been abandoned following repeated failures.

**Mass:** 381.3 kg at launch
**Dimensions:** Hexagonal base approximately 1.5 m across, solar panels spanning approximately 5.1 m. Tower structure carrying the high-gain antenna above the bus. Camera platform at the base.
**Power:** Two solar panel wings generating approximately 200 watts, with silver-zinc batteries for peak loads and eclipse periods.
**Attitude control:** Cold-gas nitrogen thrusters and a Sun sensor/Earth sensor combination for three-axis stabilization.
**Communication:** S-band high-gain antenna (960 MHz) for telemetry and ranging; omnidirectional low-gain antenna for backup.
**Propulsion:** Monopropellant hydrazine midcourse correction engine.
**Television camera system:** Six RCA vidicon cameras -- two full-scan (F-channel, 1,152-line resolution) and four partial-scan (P-channel, 300-line resolution) -- providing overlapping fields of view. Designed to return progressively higher-resolution images as the spacecraft approached the Moon, from approximately 40 km altitude down to the final frame before impact. Nominal imaging sequence: 13 minutes of continuous photography at rates between 1 frame/5 seconds (full-scan) and 1 frame/0.2 seconds (partial-scan).
**Launch vehicle:** Atlas-Agena B -- Atlas D booster (Rocketdyne MA-3 engines, 1,600 kN thrust) plus Agena B second stage (Bell 8096 engine, 71 kN thrust, restartable).
**Target:** Mare Tranquillitatis (Sea of Tranquility), selected for its smooth appearance in telescopic observations and its potential as a future landing site.

The Block III design had been simplified relative to earlier Rangers. The scientific experiments were stripped down to the cameras alone. The rough-landing capsule that Block II Rangers carried -- intended to survive impact and transmit from the surface -- was deleted. The spacecraft was single-purpose: fly to the Moon, photograph the surface as it fell, transmit the images, crash. Simplification was the design philosophy. Fewer instruments meant fewer failure modes. Fewer failure modes should have meant higher reliability.

Should have.

## The Electrical Arc: 107 Seconds

The staging transient that killed Ranger 6's cameras deserves close examination because it illustrates a class of failure that haunts every complex electromechanical system: the transient event during configuration change.

At T+107 seconds, the Atlas D booster engines (the two outboard engines and the sustainer center engine continued) reached the staging point. The two booster engines were shut down and separated from the vehicle by explosive bolts. This was a violent event -- pyrotechnic charges fired, mechanical connections broke, the vehicle's thrust profile changed abruptly, and electromagnetic energy was released.

The electromagnetic transient from the pyrotechnic firing coupled into the spacecraft's wiring harness. The coupling path was through the umbilical connections and the structural grounds that tied the spacecraft to the launch vehicle. The camera system's power bus, which was supposed to be completely de-energized during launch, received enough energy from this transient to briefly power the camera electronics.

The cameras were designed to operate in hard vacuum. At orbital altitude and beyond, the vacuum of space prevents electrical arcing -- there is no gas to ionize, no medium to conduct current across gaps. The high voltages inside the vidicon tubes (10+ kV) were safe in vacuum. But at 90 km altitude, inside the launch shroud, the gas pressure was in the Paschen minimum range -- approximately 1-10 torr. At this pressure, the mean free path of gas molecules is comparable to the distances between high-voltage electrodes in the camera tubes. Electrons accelerated by the high voltage collide with gas molecules, ionize them, and create a cascade -- an arc.

The arc was brief, but it was fatal. The electron-gun assemblies in the vidicon tubes were damaged. The deflection electrodes were shorted or burned. The cameras were not merely turned off -- they were destroyed. No amount of commanding could have revived them.

The failure investigation found that the design had not considered the interaction between staging transients and camera vulnerability during the ascent pressure profile. The testing protocol had tested the cameras in vacuum. It had tested the staging sequence with the cameras unpowered. It had never tested the cameras powered during the Paschen minimum pressure regime, because the cameras were supposed to be unpowered during ascent. The failure mode existed in the gap between what was tested and what actually happened.

This is the engineering lesson of Ranger 6: test the system in the conditions it will actually experience, not the conditions you designed it for. The cameras were designed for vacuum. They experienced a transient at 90 km altitude. The design intent and the operational reality diverged for less than a second, and that second was sufficient to destroy the mission.

## The Trajectory: A Navigation Triumph

Strip away the camera failure, and Ranger 6's flight was a masterpiece of celestial navigation.

**Launch (T+0, 15:49:00 UTC, January 30, 1964):** Atlas-Agena B lifted off from LC-12. The Atlas D booster phase performed nominally through staging (T+107s), sustainer burnout, and Atlas-Agena separation.

**Parking orbit (T+12 min):** Agena B first burn placed the spacecraft in a 185-km circular parking orbit. The spacecraft coasted for approximately 20 minutes.

**Translunar injection (T+32 min):** Agena B second burn -- the restartable capability that Rangers 1 and 2 had failed to achieve. The second burn was nominal, injecting Ranger 6 onto a translunar trajectory.

**Agena separation (T+36 min):** Ranger 6 separated from the Agena B and began its three-day transit to the Moon on solar and battery power.

**Midcourse correction (T+16 hr):** The hydrazine monopropellant engine fired for a precisely calculated duration, adjusting the trajectory to target Mare Tranquillitatis. The correction was small -- the initial trajectory was already close. The post-correction trajectory analysis showed an impact point within the target zone.

**Terminal approach (T+65 hr):** Ranger 6 descended toward the Moon at approximately 2.62 km/s relative to the lunar surface. The planned camera activation at T+65.25 hr (13 minutes before impact) failed. The spacecraft continued to descend.

**Impact (09:24:32 UTC, February 2, 1964):** Ranger 6 struck the lunar surface in Mare Tranquillitatis at 9.07°N latitude, 21.48°E longitude, at a velocity of approximately 9.39 km/s. The impact point was within 32 km of the aim point.

The navigation team's achievement was remarkable. Working with the same Deep Space Network stations that had tracked Pioneer 4 five years earlier (now significantly upgraded), the navigators used precise Doppler and range measurements to determine the spacecraft's trajectory and compute the midcourse correction with sufficient accuracy to hit a 32-km circle on the Moon from a launch pad 384,000 km away. This was like throwing a baseball from San Francisco and hitting a specific window in a New York skyscraper.

The trajectory data from Ranger 6 was itself scientifically valuable. The precise tracking of the spacecraft through the Earth-Moon gravitational field provided data on the lunar gravitational potential -- data that would eventually contribute to the mapping of lunar mascons (mass concentrations) that perturb spacecraft orbits near the Moon. The tracking infrastructure and navigation techniques validated on Ranger 6 were used unchanged for Rangers 7, 8, and 9.

## The Dedication: Mary Jackson (April 9, 1921 -- February 11, 2005)

Mary Winston Jackson was born on April 9, 1921, in Hampton, Virginia. She earned a bachelor's degree in mathematics and physical science from Hampton Institute (now Hampton University) in 1942. After teaching and other work, she joined the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics (NACA) in 1951, working at the Langley Memorial Aeronautical Laboratory.

Jackson began as a "computer" -- a human calculator performing complex mathematical analyses for aerospace research -- in the segregated West Area Computing section, the group of African American women mathematicians later known as the "West Computers." She worked under Dorothy Vaughan, alongside Katherine Johnson and others whose calculations supported wind tunnel testing, flight research, and eventually spaceflight trajectory analysis.

In 1953, Jackson was offered a position working with engineer Kazimierz Czarnecki in the Supersonic Pressure Tunnel, a wind tunnel that tested aircraft models at nearly twice the speed of sound. Czarnecki encouraged Jackson to pursue engineering credentials. To do so, she needed to take graduate courses at the University of Virginia -- but the courses were held at all-white Hampton High School. Jackson petitioned the City of Hampton for special permission to attend the segregated classes and was granted it. She completed the courses and in 1958 became the first African American female engineer at NASA (NACA had become NASA on October 1, 1958).

Jackson spent more than two decades as an aerospace engineer at Langley, specializing in boundary layer effects on aerospace vehicles at supersonic speeds. Her work on airflow around aircraft and spacecraft surfaces contributed to the understanding of how vehicles interact with the atmosphere during ascent and reentry -- the same atmospheric regime where Ranger 6's cameras were killed by the Paschen minimum arcing phenomenon.

The connection between Jackson and Ranger 6 runs through the atmosphere itself. Jackson's engineering work analyzed the boundary layer -- the thin region where a vehicle's surface interacts with atmospheric gas. Ranger 6's cameras were destroyed by an interaction between their electronics and atmospheric gas at a specific pressure. Both Jackson and the Ranger investigation dealt with the critical regime where the atmosphere is not negligible but not absent -- the transitional zone where simple assumptions (vacuum, or sea level) do not apply. Jackson's boundary layer research characterized this transition. Ranger 6's camera failure demonstrated its consequences.

In 1979, Jackson transitioned from engineering to management, becoming Langley's Federal Women's Program Manager. In this role, she worked to influence the hiring and promotion of women in science, engineering, and mathematics at NASA Langley. She helped ensure that the next generation of engineers -- of all backgrounds -- would have access to the opportunities that she had to petition and fight for.

Jackson died on February 11, 2005, in Hampton, Virginia. In 2020, NASA renamed its headquarters building in Washington, D.C., the Mary W. Jackson NASA Headquarters Building.

The parallel to Ranger 6 is about invisible structural contributions. Jackson's calculations, her engineering analyses, her advocacy for diversity in STEM -- these were foundational contributions that made NASA's visible successes possible. Like Ranger 6's trajectory, Jackson's work was precise, essential, and underrecognized for decades. The cameras of public attention did not turn on until long after the work was done.
