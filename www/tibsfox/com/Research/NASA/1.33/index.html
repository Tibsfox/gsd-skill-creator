Ranger 6 — Perfect Aim, Blind Eyes

The most accurate lunar impact in NASA history, and six dead cameras

Date: January 30, 1964 Program: Ranger (NASA/JPL) Epoch: 2 — Lunar Reconnaissance Vehicle: Atlas-Agena B

Mission Summary

On January 30, 1964, Ranger 6 launched from Cape Canaveral on an Atlas-Agena B, carrying six RCA television cameras aimed at the Sea of Tranquility. Sixty-five and a half hours later, the 381-kilogram spacecraft struck the Moon at 9.39 km/s, within 32 kilometers of the aim point — the most accurate lunar impact in the Ranger program’s history. The trajectory was flawless. The navigation was a masterpiece.

But thirteen minutes before impact, when JPL sent the command to activate the cameras, the telemetry showed nothing. All six cameras were dead. They had been killed 65 hours earlier, at T+107 seconds, when an electromagnetic transient during Atlas booster staging briefly energized the camera power bus inside the launch shroud. At 90 km altitude, the residual atmosphere was at the Paschen minimum — the pressure regime where electrical arcing occurs at the lowest voltage. The camera vidicon tubes, operating at 10,000+ volts, arced and burned out in less than a second. Ranger 6 flew for almost three days carrying six corpses where its eyes should have been.

The Spacecraft

  • Mass: 381.3 kg (Block III configuration — simplified, cameras-only)
  • Cameras: Six RCA vidicon cameras — two full-scan (1,152-line) and four partial-scan (300-line)
  • Power: Solar panels (200 W) with silver-zinc batteries
  • Communication: S-band high-gain antenna (960 MHz)
  • Propulsion: Monopropellant hydrazine midcourse correction engine
  • Launch vehicle: Atlas-Agena B (Atlas D booster + Agena B restartable upper stage)
  • Target: Mare Tranquillitatis (Sea of Tranquility)
  • Impact accuracy: 32 km from aim point (best in program)
  • Images returned: Zero

The Camera Failure

T+0 (15:49:00 UTC): Atlas-Agena B launches from LC-12, Cape Canaveral. Nominal.

T+107 seconds: Atlas booster staging. Explosive bolts fire. Electromagnetic transient surges through wiring harness. Camera power bus briefly energized. At 90 km altitude, residual atmosphere inside the launch shroud is at Paschen minimum pressure. Camera vidicon tubes (>10 kV) arc. All six cameras destroyed. Nobody knows yet.

T+12 min: Agena B achieves parking orbit. Nominal.

T+32 min: Agena B second burn — translunar injection. Nominal.

T+16 hr: Midcourse correction. Near-perfect.

T+65 hr 15 min: Camera activation command sent. No response. Manual commands sent. No response. All six cameras confirmed dead.

T+65 hr 28 min (09:24:32 UTC, Feb 2): Impact. Mare Tranquillitatis, 9.39°N, 21.48°E. Velocity 9.39 km/s. Within 32 km of target. Zero photographs.

The Ranger Program: From Failure to Triumph

Rangers 1-6 returned zero scientific data from the Moon. Six missions, six different failure modes: Agena failure (twice), trajectory miss, computer failure, power failure, camera failure. The program consumed $170 million with no photographs. Congress questioned whether JPL could deliver. NASA ordered a comprehensive review.

Then Ranger 6’s failure investigation produced 100+ corrective recommendations. Every one was implemented for Ranger 7. Six months later, on July 31, 1964, Ranger 7 struck the Moon and returned 4,316 photographs — the first close-up images of the lunar surface from an American spacecraft. Ranger 8 followed with 7,137 images. Ranger 9 added 5,814 more. The program that returned zero for six missions returned 17,267 images in its final three.

Ranger 6 was the last failure. Everything after it worked. The failure analysis was the bridge.

Like Ranger 6, she did essential work that was undervalued in its time and foundational in retrospect.
