MR-1: Second by Second

November 21, 1960 — Cape Canaveral LC-5 — The 4-Inch Launch

Pre-Launch
T-30m Terminal countdown begins ▶

The terminal countdown for MR-1 begins. The Mercury capsule has been mated to the Redstone booster for weeks. Weather is acceptable. Range is clear. Chris Kraft monitors from the blockhouse 300 feet from the pad.

T - 30:00

Ground power feeds the vehicle through the tail plug umbilical. All systems nominal. The A-7 engine is cold — it will receive propellants only minutes before ignition.

T-5m Propellant loading complete ▶

LOX (liquid oxygen) and ethanol propellant loading complete. The Redstone sits fueled on the pad. Internal batteries are armed. The vehicle is now self-sufficient — except for the ground control signals still flowing through the tail plug.

LOX -183°C • ETOH +20°C
Ignition Sequence
T-0 Ignition command ▶

The ignition sequence fires. The A-7 engine's gas generator ignites, spinning the turbopump. Propellants flow into the combustion chamber. The engine builds thrust over approximately one second.

[IGNITION]

Engineering: The A-7 produces 78,000 lbf (347 kN) at rated thrust. The vehicle weighs 66,000 lbs (294 kN). Thrust-to-weight ratio: 1.18 at rated thrust. The engine must reach approximately 70,000 lbf before the holddown clamps release.

T+1.0s Thrust builds — holddown clamps release ▶

Thrust exceeds vehicle weight. The four holddown clamps at the base of the rocket release. The rocket begins to rise — slowly at first, accelerating as thrust exceeds weight by an increasing margin.

CLAMPS RELEASE • NET FORCE POSITIVE

Engineering: At this moment, the rocket has approximately 15% excess thrust. Acceleration is about 0.15g — very gentle. The tail plug connector is still fully mated.

The Four Inches
T+1.6s Tail plug separates at 4 inches — SNEAK CIRCUIT ▶

The rocket has risen approximately 4 inches (10 cm). The tail plug connector at the base of the rocket pulls free. But the two sets of pins disconnect in the wrong order.

The sneak circuit: The control-inhibit pins separate before the power pins. For a fraction of a second, ground power is still flowing through the connector while the inhibit circuit is open. This creates an unintended electrical path — a "sneak circuit" — that sends a shutdown command to the engine sequencer.

CORRECT: Power(-) → Control(-) → FLIGHT
ACTUAL: Control(-) → Power(+) → SHUTDOWN

The engine obeys the shutdown command. It was designed to obey shutdown commands instantly and without question — that is a safety feature. Thrust drops to zero in less than a second.

T+2s Engine shutdown — rocket settles back on pad ▶

With no thrust, the rocket decelerates and settles back onto the launch pad. It drops the 4 inches it gained and lands on its fins. The rocket is now sitting on the pad with full propellant tanks, a dead engine, and live pyrotechnics.

4 INCHES UP • 4 INCHES DOWN
T+3s Escape tower fires ▶

The Mercury capsule's abort sensing system detects the sudden loss of thrust (acceleration drops below threshold). It does exactly what it was designed to do: fire the escape tower's solid rocket motor to pull the capsule away from a failing booster.

The escape tower ignites and arcs away from the pad trailing flame and smoke. It flies approximately 400 feet before impacting the beach 400 yards downrange.

ESCAPE TOWER → 400 FT ALTITUDE → BEACH

The absurdity: The escape tower is now gone. The capsule is sitting on top of a fully-fueled rocket with no escape system. Every subsequent event will make the situation worse.

The Aftermath
T+6s Drogue parachute deploys ▶

With the escape tower gone, the capsule's baroswitch (barometric pressure switch) senses that altitude is at sea level. Its sequencer, following its programmed logic perfectly, deploys the drogue parachute. The small stabilization chute pops out of the top of the capsule and catches the Cape Canaveral breeze.

DROGUE DEPLOYS AT SEA LEVEL
T+10s Main parachute deploys on pad ▶

The sequencer continues its programmed timeline. The main parachute — a large orange-and-white canopy designed to lower the capsule gently into the Atlantic Ocean — deploys. It billows out in the wind and drapes over the rocket like a tent.

The fully-fueled Mercury-Redstone is now sitting on the pad wearing its own parachute.

PARACHUTES ON PAD
T+24h Batteries die — safe to approach ▶

Nobody can approach the rocket. It has live pyrotechnics (destruct charges, retrorocket igniters, parachute mortars that may not have fully fired). The propellant tanks are full. The batteries are still providing power to systems that might fire at any moment.

The decision: Wait. Do nothing. Let the batteries run down over the next 24 hours until there is no electrical power left to trigger anything. Only then can the safing crew approach.

24 HOURS OF STARING AT A ROCKET IN A PARACHUTE

Engineers used this 24-hour wait to begin analyzing what happened. By the time they could touch the hardware, they already understood the sneak circuit. The fix was simple: make the power disconnect pin physically longer than the control pin, ensuring it always disconnects first regardless of rise height.

MR-1 Summary: Every system on Mercury-Redstone 1 worked exactly as designed. The engine started correctly. The abort system detected a problem and responded correctly. The parachute deployment sequencer followed its logic flawlessly. The only failure was the disconnection order of two sets of pins in a connector — a race condition caused by a rise height of 4 inches instead of the expected several feet.

Sneak Circuit: An unintended electrical path through a circuit that exists only under specific conditions not anticipated by the designer. The term comes from the fact that the path "sneaks" through the circuit topology — it is not visible in the schematic under normal operating conditions. MR-1's sneak circuit existed only during the fraction of a second when the control pins had separated but the power pins had not.

Lesson: Test the complete sequence, not just individual functions. Every system worked. The integration failed.
