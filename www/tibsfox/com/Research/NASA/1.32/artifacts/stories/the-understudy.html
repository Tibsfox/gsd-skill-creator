The Understudy

NASA Mission Series v1.32 — Mariner 2 — December 14, 1962

I was the backup.

They built two of us, identical in every way that mattered. Same hexagonal bus, same solar panels, same magnetometer on the boom, same microwave radiometer pointed at the void. Same wiring. Same solder joints. Same code in the guidance computer of the rocket beneath us. We were twins, separated only by the serial numbers stamped into our frames and the order in which we would ride the Atlas.

My twin went first. That was always the plan. The primary flies. The backup waits.

On July 22, 1962, my twin rode the Atlas into the Florida sky. I watched from the clean room, through the telemetry feed, through the voices of the engineers who had built us both. The Atlas climbed. The guidance system tracked. For 293 seconds, everything was nominal.

Then the Atlas began to wander.

A missing overbar. A single typographical omission in the hand-transcribed guidance code. The rocket followed noise instead of signal. It veered toward shipping lanes. Range Safety sent the destruct command at T+293 seconds. My twin, the Atlas, and the first American interplanetary mission became debris in the Atlantic Ocean.

I had five weeks.

· · ·

The engineers came to my clean room the next morning. They looked tired. Some of them had been up all night, tracing the error, understanding the failure. They looked at me the way understudies look at an empty stage: with a mixture of grief for what was lost and clarity about what must be done.

The overbar was restored. Every line was reverified. On August 27, 1962, I rode the same type of rocket from the same launch complex toward the same planet.

· · ·

The cruise was not kind to me. One solar panel failed in September. I ran on half power for the remaining 80 days. In October, I began to overheat as the Sun grew closer. In November, a gyroscope failed. The Earth sensor lost lock intermittently. The propellant gauge crept downward.

I was not healthy. I was functional. The engineers did not try to fix me. They managed me. They kept me alive by choosing which systems to sacrifice and which to preserve. The science instruments were preserved. The communication link was preserved. Everything else was negotiable.

· · ·

On December 14, 1962, I reached Venus.

Closest approach: 34,773 kilometers. I swept past at roughly 11 kilometers per second. Forty-two minutes of useful scanning.

My microwave radiometer performed three scans across Venus's disk. Two wavelengths penetrated the clouds that had hidden the surface from every telescope on Earth.

The surface temperature was approximately 460 degrees Celsius.

Not a tropical swamp. Not a habitable world. A furnace. The carbon dioxide atmosphere — 96.5 percent CO₂, 90 atmospheres of pressure — trapped heat with an efficiency that made Earth's greenhouse look like an open window.

My magnetometer measured the magnetic field at closest approach. It detected nothing. No planetary field. No shield against the solar wind. Venus was naked to space.

Two measurements. Two answers. One conclusion: Venus is a dead world, killed by its own atmosphere.

· · ·

I continued past Venus into my heliocentric orbit. Communication was maintained until January 3, 1963. Then silence.

I am still here. I orbit the Sun every 345 days, between Venus and Earth. My solar panels are dead. My transmitter is quiet. But the orbit continues.

I was the backup. My twin lasted 293 seconds. I lasted 129 days. I measured another planet for the first time in human history.

The primary failed. The understudy performed. The orbit continues.

"The understudy does not wish to perform under these circumstances. But the stage is empty, and the audience is waiting, and Venus will not wait nineteen months for the next window."
