# Organism Pairing: Thuja plicata (Western Red Cedar)

## The Tree That Lasts

**Scientific Name:** Thuja plicata Donn ex D.Don
**Family:** Cupressaceae (cypress family)
**Common Names:** Western red cedar, giant arborvitae, Pacific red cedar, shinglewood
**Paired Mission:** 1.32 — Mariner 2 (Atlas-Agena B), August 27, 1962
**PNW Status:** Native, dominant shade-tolerant species — the culturally foundational tree of the Pacific Northwest

---

## What Endures

If Douglas-fir (v1.5) is the tree that reaches the sky, western red cedar is the tree that outlasts the sky. It does not grow as tall — its maximum height of 60+ meters is respectable but not record-breaking. It does not grow as fast — in the race for canopy dominance, Douglas-fir wins by decades. It is not as commercially dominant — Douglas-fir built the cities of the Pacific Northwest; western red cedar built the civilizations that preceded them.

What western red cedar does is endure. A mature cedar can live 1,400+ years. A fallen cedar log, killed by windthrow or old age, can persist on the forest floor for another 1,000 years because its heartwood is saturated with thujaplicin — a natural fungicide that makes the wood essentially rot-proof. The living tree resists decay. The dead tree resists decay. The fallen log resists decay. Western red cedar does not go away.

Mariner 2 was built to survive a 109-day journey through interplanetary space and deliver data from Venus. It survived solar panel failures, overheating, gyroscope loss, and the accumulating stress of operating far beyond its design margins. It was never healthy. It was always functional enough. It endured — through malfunction after malfunction, through the deepening silence of increasing distance, through the thermal assault of approaching the Sun — and it delivered the first close-range data from another planet. Then it fell silent. But it endures. It is still orbiting the Sun, 63 years later, in the same heliocentric orbit that carried it to Venus. The wood resists decay. The orbit resists perturbation. Both persist long after the living function has ceased.

## Morphology and Life History

Western red cedar is a large evergreen conifer with a distinctive form: a buttressed base that tapers rapidly upward, fibrous reddish-brown bark that peels in long vertical strips, drooping branches that sweep downward and then curve upward at the tips, and flat, scale-like foliage arranged in spray-like fronds. The silhouette is unmistakable — the drooping leader tip, the conical but irregular crown, the massive fluted base.

The bark is thin compared to Douglas-fir — typically 1-2 cm on mature trees, versus Douglas-fir's 15-30 cm. This thin bark makes western red cedar vulnerable to fire, which is one reason it is a late-successional species: it establishes in the understory of fire-resistant Douglas-fir, growing slowly in the shade, waiting for the canopy to open naturally through windthrow or decay rather than through fire. When fire does sweep through, the cedar burns. But its root system can survive and produce sprouts, and its decay-resistant fallen logs serve as nurse logs for the next generation.

The wood is the defining feature. Western red cedar heartwood is reddish-brown, lightweight (density approximately 370 kg/m³, among the lightest commercial softwoods), dimensionally stable, and extraordinarily resistant to decay. The decay resistance comes from thujaplicin (β-thujaplicin, also called hinokitiol), a tropolone compound that chelates metal ions essential for fungal enzyme function, effectively poisoning any fungus that attempts to colonize the wood. This chemical defense is why cedar fence posts outlast those of any other species, why cedar shingles last 30+ years, and why fallen cedar logs in old-growth forests remain identifiable for centuries after death.

The foliage is scale-like, arranged in flat sprays with overlapping scales that give the fronds a smooth, shingle-like texture when stroked in one direction and a slightly rough texture in the other. Crush the foliage and it releases a sharp, aromatic scent — the distinctive "cedar" smell that is actually the volatile terpenes and tropolones that also contribute to the wood's rot resistance. The tree smells like its own preservative.

## Ecological Role: The Patient Foundation

Western red cedar's ecological strategy is patience. Where Douglas-fir races to the canopy, cedar waits. Where alder fixes nitrogen in disturbed sites, cedar colonizes stable, shaded understories. Where fire-adapted species depend on disturbance, cedar depends on the absence of disturbance — on centuries of uninterrupted growth that allow its slow-growing shade-tolerant seedlings to establish and eventually replace the canopy trees above them.

**Shade tolerance:** Western red cedar is one of the most shade-tolerant conifers in North America. Its seedlings can survive and grow in as little as 5% of full sunlight — the dim conditions beneath a closed Douglas-fir canopy. This tolerance allows cedar to establish beneath the fast-growing pioneer species and slowly build a population in the understory. Over centuries, as the canopy Douglas-firs die one by one from windthrow, disease, or old age, the understory cedars move up. In the absence of fire, a Douglas-fir forest will eventually become a cedar-hemlock forest through this process of gap-phase replacement.

**Nurse log contribution:** When a western red cedar falls, its rot-resistant wood becomes one of the most important substrates for forest regeneration. A cedar nurse log can sustain seedlings for centuries, providing elevated planting sites above the waterlogged forest floor, stored nutrients from slow decomposition, and moisture retention from the spongy wood texture. Walk through an old-growth PNW forest and the rows of trees growing on raised root platforms mark where cedar nurse logs once lay.

**Cultural foundation:** For Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Haida, Tlingit, and dozens of other Pacific Northwest Indigenous nations, western red cedar is the tree of civilization. Longhouses were built from cedar planks split from standing or fallen trees. Ocean-going canoes were carved from single cedar logs, some exceeding 15 meters in length. Bentwood boxes were steamed and bent from single planks. Bark was stripped, shredded, and woven into clothing, mats, baskets, and rope. Ceremonial masks, totem poles, and house posts were carved from cedar. The tree provided material culture spanning every aspect of daily and ceremonial life. No single species was more materially important to the Indigenous peoples of the Northwest Coast. The Salish name for western red cedar translates roughly as "the tree of life."

Mariner 2 provided the material foundation for interplanetary science. Before Mariner 2, planetary flyby data was theoretical — orbital mechanics said it was possible, but no mission had demonstrated it. After Mariner 2, the template existed: spacecraft bus, solar panels, attitude control, instrument boom, high-gain antenna, trans-planetary trajectory, flyby science sequence. Every subsequent planetary mission — Mariner 4 to Mars, Mariner 10 to Mercury, Pioneer 10 to Jupiter, Voyager to the outer planets — used the template that Mariner 2 established. The spacecraft was the cedar of the space program: the material from which everything else was built.

## Resonance Axes

### 1. Endurance Through Malfunction
Western red cedar endures because its chemistry defeats the organisms that would consume it. Thujaplicin poisons fungal enzymes. The heartwood resists decomposition for a millennium. Mariner 2 endured because its engineering team defeated the malfunctions that would have killed it. Solar panel failure — work around it. Overheating — manage the power budget. Gyroscope loss — reconfigure attitude control. Both survive not through perfection but through resistance to the forces that would destroy them.

### 2. The Backup That Became the Foundation
Western red cedar is the understory tree — the backup to Douglas-fir's canopy dominance. But cedar outlasts Douglas-fir. When fire sweeps through, Douglas-fir's thick bark protects it but cedar's root system resprouts. Over millennia, the patient cedar builds the foundation that the forest rests on. Mariner 2 was the backup spacecraft — Mariner 1 was destroyed. But Mariner 2 became the first successful interplanetary mission, the foundation for all planetary exploration that followed.

### 3. Slow Revelation
Western red cedar reveals its value slowly. A young cedar looks unremarkable — thin, shade-tolerant, overshadowed by its neighbors. Cut into the heartwood and the decay resistance becomes apparent. Wait centuries and the longevity emerges. Mariner 2 revealed Venus slowly — 42 minutes of flyby scanning, months of data analysis, decades of implication. The 460°C temperature reading was a number that took years to fully interpret. The missing magnetic field took decades to understand in the context of planetary evolution.

The naming circle closes: cedar tree → cedar waxwing → Brenda Lee Eager (degree 31) → Mariner 2. The waxwing feeds on cedar berries. The tree provides the berries. The mission endures like the tree.

### 5. Cultural Foundation
Western red cedar is the material culture foundation of the Pacific Northwest. Mariner 2 is the mission culture foundation of interplanetary science. Both provided the raw material — physical in one case, institutional in the other — from which civilizations of exploration were built.

## Taxonomy Note

The genus *Thuja* (from the Greek *thuia*, meaning "aromatic tree") was established by Linnaeus in 1753. The species epithet *plicata* (Latin for "pleated" or "braided") refers to the overlapping pattern of the scale-like leaves. The authority "Donn ex D.Don" indicates that James Donn first described the species but David Don formally published the description in 1824. Western red cedar is NOT a true cedar — true cedars belong to the genus *Cedrus* (family Pinaceae), native to the Mediterranean and Himalayas. *Thuja plicata* is in the cypress family (Cupressaceae), more closely related to junipers and true cypresses than to true cedars. The "cedar" name persists because the aromatic, decay-resistant wood reminded early European settlers of Old World cedar (*Cedrus libani*).

This misclassification echoes Mariner 2's own identity: the "backup" spacecraft that was never the primary mission but became the historic one. The name "backup" persists in the history books, but what Mariner 2 actually was — the first successful interplanetary spacecraft — transcends the classification.
