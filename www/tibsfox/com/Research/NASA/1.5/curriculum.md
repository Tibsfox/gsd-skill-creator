## Department Deposits

### Physics (Primary)

**Wing:** Escape Velocity and Orbital Mechanics
**Concept:** The energy threshold between bound and unbound trajectories, hyperbolic orbits, and the permanent transition from geocentric to heliocentric motion

**Deposit:** Pioneer 4 was the first US spacecraft to achieve escape velocity and enter a permanent heliocentric orbit. The physics of this achievement spans the full vocabulary of orbital mechanics:
- Escape velocity: v_escape = sqrt(2GM/r) ≈ 11.0 km/s at 200 km altitude. Pioneer 4 achieved approximately 11.1 km/s -- a margin of roughly 84 m/s (0.8%)
- Specific orbital energy: epsilon = v^2/2 - mu/r. When epsilon crosses zero from negative to positive, the trajectory type changes discontinuously from elliptical (bound) to hyperbolic (unbound). Pioneer 4's epsilon was approximately +9.5 x 10^5 J/kg
- Hyperbolic trajectory: eccentricity e ≈ 1.031 -- barely above the parabolic boundary. The orbit opens and the spacecraft never returns
- Hyperbolic excess velocity: v_infinity ≈ 1.38 km/s -- the speed Pioneer 4 retains at infinite distance from Earth. This velocity added to Earth's orbital velocity (29.78 km/s) determines the heliocentric orbit
- Heliocentric orbit: semi-major axis ≈ 1.15 AU, perihelion ≈ 1.0 AU, aphelion ≈ 1.30 AU. Pioneer 4 orbits between Earth and Mars, completing one revolution every ~1.23 years
- Lunar flyby at ~60,000 km (2.5 times the intended 24,000 km) -- too far for the photoelectric sensor to trigger, but close enough for the Geiger-Mueller counter to sample the cislunar radiation environment
- The convergence of the five Pioneers: P-0 (explosion), P-1 (fell back from 113,854 km), P-2 (fell back from 1,550 km), P-3 (fell back from 102,322 km), P-4 (escaped). Four failures of varying magnitude, one success. Each failure taught the next attempt

This deposit feeds forward into every mission involving escape trajectories, gravity assists, interplanetary transfers, and the permanent placement of objects in heliocentric orbit. The Hohmann transfer, the gravity slingshot, the grand tour -- all begin with the concept Pioneer 4 demonstrated: achieving positive specific orbital energy.

### Engineering (Primary)

**Wing:** Launch Vehicle Reliability and the Juno II
**Concept:** The engineering of reliable multi-stage rockets, and how iterative failure produces successful systems

**Deposit:** Pioneer 4 flew on the Juno II -- a derivative of the Jupiter-C/Juno I that had launched Explorer 1 thirteen months earlier. The engineering story is one of convergence through iteration:
- Juno II configuration: modified Jupiter IRBM first stage (Rocketdyne S-3D engine, LOX/RP-1, 667 kN thrust) + scaled solid upper stages derived from the Explorer 1 design (JPL Sergeant rocket motors in a spinning tub)
- First stage: the Jupiter IRBM booster was more powerful than the Thor used by Pioneers 0-2, providing higher initial delta-v and better margin
- Upper stages: 11 solid rocket motors in three tiers (cluster of 11 → cluster of 3 → single motor), spin-stabilized at approximately 750 rpm
- Pioneer 3 (December 6, 1958) used the same Juno II design but the first stage burned 3.7 seconds short. Pioneer 4 corrected this -- all stages burned to completion
- Mass: Pioneer 4 weighed 6.08 kg -- the lightest of the five Pioneers. Pioneer 1 was 34.2 kg, Pioneer 2 was 39.2 kg. The Juno II's lower payload capacity was compensated by the spacecraft's minimal design
- No TV camera (unlike Pioneer 2): the instrument package was reduced to essentials -- Geiger-Mueller counter, photoelectric sensor, antenna, battery. The mass savings contributed to margin
- Reliability through simplicity: fewer instruments, fewer failure modes, fewer connections to break. Pioneer 4 succeeded partly because it asked less of the spacecraft
- The JPL connection: Pioneer 4 was the first JPL mission to fully succeed. JPL's Juno II was the Army's launcher. The NASA/JPL relationship that would produce Ranger, Surveyor, Mariner, Viking, Voyager, and every Mars rover began here

### Communications (Primary)

**Wing:** Deep Space Tracking
**Concept:** The first sustained tracking of a spacecraft beyond Earth orbit -- the birth of deep space communication

**Deposit:** Pioneer 4's 82-hour contact period established the template for all subsequent deep space communication:
- Transmitter: 180 mW at 960.05 MHz -- less power than a typical LED flashlight
- Antenna (spacecraft): omnidirectional dipole -- no ability to point a beam, radiating in all directions
- Antenna (ground): Goldstone 26-meter dish (Deep Space Station 11), recently completed. The 45 dBi gain of this antenna was the critical enabler. Without Goldstone, contact would have been lost within hours
- Jodrell Bank support: the 76-meter Lovell Telescope at Jodrell Bank, England, provided independent tracking confirmation during the critical escape phase
- Signal path: 180 mW → dipole → 655,300 km of vacuum → Goldstone 26m dish → cryogenic (1959-standard) receiver. Received power at maximum range: approximately 2.6 x 10^-16 watts (0.26 femtowatts)
- Telemetry: approximately 64.8 bits/second. The Geiger-Mueller count rate, battery voltage, and temperature data were multiplexed onto this narrow channel
- Doppler tracking: the frequency shift of the 960 MHz carrier provided velocity measurements to approximately 0.1 m/s precision. This data confirmed escape velocity and allowed orbit determination
- The 82-hour contact duration: at approximately 655,300 km, the signal dropped below the noise threshold. Pioneer 4 continued on its heliocentric orbit, but could no longer be heard. The spacecraft still transmits (the battery has long since died). The orbit continues forever
- Deep Space Network origin: the Goldstone station built for Pioneer tracking became the foundation of NASA's Deep Space Network (DSN), now three complexes (Goldstone, Madrid, Canberra) with 70-meter antennas that can track Voyager at 24 billion km

### Forestry / Ecology (Secondary)

**Wing:** Old-Growth Ecosystems
**Concept:** Pseudotsuga menziesii (Douglas-fir) -- the dominant conifer of the Pacific Northwest, and the ecosystem it creates over centuries of undisturbed growth

**Deposit:** The Douglas-fir is the organism paired with Pioneer 4, and the connection operates on multiple levels:
- Pseudotsuga menziesii is the tallest tree in the PNW outside of coast redwood (Sequoia sempervirens). Old-growth specimens exceed 80 meters in height and 2 meters in diameter, with lifespans exceeding 1,000 years
- Old-growth Douglas-fir forests develop over centuries of undisturbed growth, creating multi-layered canopy structures that support diverse communities of epiphytes, lichens, birds (including the Red-breasted Nuthatch), and fungal networks
- The Douglas-fir's root system connects to mycorrhizal networks -- the "wood wide web" through which trees share nutrients and chemical signals. A single old-growth tree may be connected to hundreds of neighbors through fungal hyphae
- The escape metaphor: a Douglas-fir seedling that survives its first decades can grow into the canopy layer and "escape" the suppression of the understory. Once in full sunlight, growth accelerates. The threshold between suppressed understory tree and dominant canopy tree mirrors the escape velocity threshold -- below it, growth is constrained; above it, the tree achieves its full potential
- Biomass scaling: height scales as a power law of diameter (h ~ D^b, where b ≈ 0.5-0.7 for old growth). This allometric relationship parallels the inverse-square law that governed Pioneer 4's signal strength -- both are power laws that must be characterized at extreme values
- Douglas-fir wood was the structural material of the Pacific Northwest: bridges, buildings, ships, railroad ties. The tree that builds the infrastructure. Pioneer 4 was the mission that built the infrastructure of deep space communication
- The cone: Douglas-fir cones are distinctive -- the three-pointed bracts projecting from between the scales (the "mouse tails") are unique among conifers. Identification is immediate. The tree does not hide

### Mathematics (Secondary)

**Wing:** Hyperbolic Functions and Conic Sections
**Concept:** The hyperbola as the geometry of escape -- the conic section with eccentricity greater than one

**Deposit:** Pioneer 4's trajectory is a hyperbola:
- Conic sections: circle (e = 0), ellipse (0 < e < 1), parabola (e = 1), hyperbola (e > 1). These four curves describe every possible Keplerian orbit. The type is determined solely by specific orbital energy
- Pioneer 4's eccentricity: e ≈ 1.031 -- barely hyperbolic. The trajectory equation r(theta) = a(1-e^2)/(1+e*cos(theta)) gives an open curve with asymptotes at theta_max = arccos(-1/e)
- Hyperbolic functions (sinh, cosh, tanh): the Kepler equation for hyperbolic orbits uses the hyperbolic anomaly H instead of the eccentric anomaly E. The relationship M = e*sinh(H) - H governs the time-position relationship on a hyperbolic trajectory
- The convergence of the five Pioneer eccentricities: 0 (P-0, debris), 0.09 (P-2, 1,550 km), ~0.36 (P-3, 102,322 km), 0.89 (P-1, 113,854 km), 1.03 (P-4, escaped). This sequence from zero toward and past unity traces the entire taxonomy of conic sections
- The semi-major axis is negative for hyperbolic orbits (by convention): a = -mu/(2*epsilon). This mathematical sign change from positive to negative as the orbit transitions from elliptical to hyperbolic is one of the most elegant boundary crossings in classical mechanics

### History (Secondary)

**Wing:** Alexander Graham Bell and the Communication Revolution
**Concept:** The parallel between Bell's amplification of human voice over distance and NASA's amplification of spacecraft signals over interplanetary distance

**Deposit:** Alexander Graham Bell was born on March 3, 1847 -- exactly 112 years before Pioneer 4's launch. The connection is deeper than calendar coincidence:
- Bell's core problem: transmitting acoustic signals (voice) over distance, fighting attenuation. His solution: convert sound to electrical signals, amplify, transmit through wire, convert back. This is the complete communication chain
- Pioneer 4's core problem: transmitting telemetry signals over interplanetary distance, fighting 1/r^2 attenuation. The solution: convert sensor data to radio signals, transmit through vacuum, collect with large antenna, amplify, demodulate. The same chain, different medium
- Bell's patents (1876) established that information can be transmitted over distance using electromagnetic signals. Every deep space communication system descends from this principle
- The telephone network Bell built was the first long-distance communication infrastructure. The Deep Space Network that tracked Pioneer 4 is the interplanetary extension of the same concept
- Bell's interest in the deaf: his mother and wife were deaf. His work on hearing aids and acoustic amplification was driven by the need to make faint signals audible. Goldstone's 26-meter dish is an acoustic amplifier for a deaf Earth trying to hear a whispering spacecraft at 655,300 km
- Bell's breadth: beyond the telephone, Bell worked on the photophone (optical communication, 1880), metal detectors, hydrofoils, and aviation. The photophone -- transmitting voice on a beam of light -- anticipated the deep space optical communication systems now being tested 146 years later
- Overton Berry (the Red-breasted Nuthatch's degree): Berry was a pioneering jazz pianist in Seattle, communicating through sound, interpreting and transmitting musical ideas across audiences. The nuthatch's nasal "yank-yank" call communicates territory and threat through forest acoustics. Bell, Berry, the nuthatch -- three entities using sound to communicate across distance, each adapted to their medium

### TRY 1: Calculate Escape Velocity for Earth, Moon, and Mars

**Duration:** 30 minutes
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Physics / Mathematics
**What You Need:** Python 3.8+, or a scientific calculator

**What You'll Learn:**
How to calculate the velocity required to leave a gravitational body permanently, and why the Moon is easy to leave, Mars is moderate, and Earth is the hardest of the three.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] Python 3.8+ installed (or a scientific calculator)
- [ ] Know what a square root is

**The Exercise:**

**Step 1: The escape velocity formula**

```
v_escape = sqrt(2 * G * M / R)

G = 6.674e-11 m^3/(kg*s^2)
```

One formula. Three bodies. Three very different velocities.

**Step 2: Calculate for all three**

```python
import math

G = 6.674e-11  # gravitational constant

bodies = [
    ("Earth", 5.972e24, 6.371e6),
    ("Moon",  7.342e22, 1.737e6),
    ("Mars",  6.417e23, 3.390e6),
]

print("ESCAPE VELOCITY COMPARISON")
print("=" * 55)
print(f"{'Body':>8} | {'Mass (kg)':>12} | {'Radius (km)':>11} | {'v_esc (km/s)':>12}")
print("-" * 55)

for name, M, R in bodies:
    v_esc = math.sqrt(2 * G * M / R)
    print(f"{name:>8} | {M:>12.3e} | {R/1e3:>11,.0f} | {v_esc/1e3:>12.2f}")

print()
print("To leave Earth:  a bullet isn't fast enough (900 m/s)")
print("To leave Moon:   a bullet IS fast enough (if aimed up)")
print("To leave Mars:   a very fast bullet, roughly half of Earth")
print()
print("Pioneer 4 achieved 11.1 km/s. Earth escape: 11.0 km/s.")
print("Margin: 0.1 km/s. That's it. That's the margin between")
print("orbiting Earth forever and orbiting the Sun forever.")
```

**Step 3: Feel the threshold**

```python
# What happens at 99%, 100%, and 101% of escape velocity?
v_esc_earth = math.sqrt(2 * G * 5.972e24 / 6.571e6)  # at 200 km

for fraction in [0.90, 0.95, 0.99, 1.00, 1.01, 1.05]:
    v = fraction * v_esc_earth
    # Specific energy
    eps = v**2/2 - G * 5.972e24 / 6.571e6
    orbit = "ESCAPE" if eps >= 0 else "BOUND"
    print(f"  {fraction*100:5.1f}% of v_esc = {v/1e3:.2f} km/s → "
          f"energy = {eps/1e6:>+8.2f} MJ/kg → {orbit}")
```

**What Just Happened:**
You proved that escape velocity is a sharp threshold. At 99% you fall back. At 101% you leave forever. Pioneer 4 was at roughly 100.8%. The margin between permanent orbit around Earth and permanent orbit around the Sun is narrower than you expected.

**The NASA Connection:**
Every interplanetary mission must exceed escape velocity. Mars missions need roughly 11.6 km/s (escape + transfer orbit injection). Jupiter missions need even more. The Voyagers launched at approximately 15 km/s. The escape velocity calculation is the first thing a mission planner checks.

### TRY 2: Build a Signal Strength Meter with Arduino

**Duration:** 45 minutes
**Difficulty:** Beginner-Intermediate
**Department:** Communications / Electronics
**What You Need:** Arduino Uno ($15-25), photoresistor ($1), 10K resistor ($0.10), LED ($0.25), breadboard + wires ($5). Total: ~$20.

**What You'll Learn:**
How signal strength decays with distance, following the inverse-square law -- the same law that determined when Pioneer 4's signal became undetectable.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] Arduino IDE installed
- [ ] Arduino Uno, photoresistor, LED, resistor, breadboard on hand

**The Exercise:**

**Step 1: Build the detector**

```
Wiring:
  Photoresistor → A0 (voltage divider with 10K to GND)
  Other leg of photoresistor → 5V
  LED → Pin 9 (PWM) through 220-ohm resistor → GND
```

**Step 2: Upload the signal strength sketch**

```arduino
// pioneer4_signal_strength.ino
// Signal strength meter demonstrating inverse-square law
// Use a flashlight as Pioneer 4's transmitter

const int PHOTO_PIN = A0;
const int LED_PIN = 9;

void setup() {
  Serial.begin(115200);
  pinMode(LED_PIN, OUTPUT);
  Serial.println("PIONEER 4 SIGNAL STRENGTH METER");
  Serial.println("Move a flashlight toward/away from the sensor.");
  Serial.println("Watch the signal strength decay with distance.");
  Serial.println("Distance, Raw, Signal_dB");
}

void loop() {
  int raw = analogRead(PHOTO_PIN);

  // Map raw reading to LED brightness (signal strength indicator)
  int brightness = map(raw, 0, 1023, 0, 255);
  analogWrite(LED_PIN, brightness);

  // Calculate approximate signal in dB (relative)
  float signal_dB = 20.0 * log10((float)raw / 1023.0);

  // Print for serial plotter
  Serial.print(raw);
  Serial.print(",");
  Serial.println(signal_dB, 1);

  delay(100);
}
```

**Step 3: Test the inverse-square law**

Use a small flashlight (your "Pioneer 4 transmitter") in a dark room:
1. Place flashlight 5 cm from sensor → record reading
2. Move to 10 cm (2x distance) → reading should be ~1/4
3. Move to 20 cm (4x distance) → reading should be ~1/16
4. Move to 40 cm (8x distance) → reading should be ~1/64
5. Continue until the LED goes dark (signal lost)

**Step 4: Find your "maximum range"**

The distance at which the LED is completely dark is your Goldstone moment -- that's where Pioneer 4's signal was lost at 655,300 km. Note the distance. Your flashlight has more power than Pioneer 4's transmitter. Your sensor is far less sensitive than Goldstone. But the law is the same.

**What Just Happened:**
You experienced the inverse-square law in your hands. The signal drops by a factor of 4 every time you double the distance. At Pioneer 4's maximum range of 655,300 km, the 180 mW transmitter produced 2.6 x 10^-16 watts at the receiver -- a factor of 430,000 below the signal at 1,000 km. Your flashlight-and-photoresistor demonstrated the same physics at tabletop scale.

**The NASA Connection:**
The Deep Space Network exists because of the inverse-square law. Bigger antennas and more sensitive receivers are the only countermeasures. Bell understood this for sound. Goldstone applied it for radio. Your Arduino proved it for light.

### TRY 3: Identify Douglas-fir by Cone and Bark in Your Neighborhood

**Duration:** 30 minutes to 1 hour (walking time)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Forestry / Ecology
**What You Need:** Your eyes, a phone camera. Cost: $0.

**What You'll Learn:**
How to identify Pseudotsuga menziesii -- the tree that defines Pacific Northwest old-growth forests -- by its unique cones and distinctive bark. If you live in the PNW, you will find Douglas-fir within 15 minutes of walking. If you live elsewhere in the temperate Northern Hemisphere, you may still find planted specimens.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] Willingness to go outside
- [ ] Phone for photos (optional)

**The Exercise:**

**Step 1: Know what you're looking for**

```
Douglas-fir identification features:

CONES:
  - 5-10 cm long (2-4 inches)
  - Hang downward from branches
  - Three-pointed bracts project from between scales
  - The bracts look like mouse hindquarters (tail + two legs)
  - "Mouse-in-the-cone" is the classic field ID
  - No other PNW conifer has these bracts
  - Cones often found on the ground beneath mature trees

BARK (young trees, < 40 years):
  - Smooth, gray, with resin blisters
  - Similar to true fir (Abies) bark at this stage

BARK (mature trees, > 80 years):
  - Deeply furrowed, thick (10-30 cm on old growth)
  - Dark brown to reddish-brown
  - Corky texture -- fire-resistant insulation
  - The bark is the armor. Old-growth Douglas-fir survives
    ground fires because the bark is thick enough to insulate
    the living cambium layer beneath

NEEDLES:
  - Flat, soft, 2-3 cm long
  - Spiral arrangement around the twig (not in flat rows like true fir)
  - Green above, two white stomatal bands below
  - Crush a needle: smells like citrus or balsam

SILHOUETTE:
  - Young: conical, Christmas-tree shape
  - Old growth: massive trunk, broken top, irregular crown
  - The tallest Douglas-fir exceed 80 meters
```

**Step 2: Go find one**

If you're in the Pacific Northwest (western Washington, Oregon, or British Columbia), step outside. Douglas-fir is the most common tree in the region. It grows in parks, along streets, in any area with at least partial sun. Look for cones on the ground first -- the mouse-tail bracts are unmistakable.

If you're outside the PNW but in the temperate Northern Hemisphere, check parks and arboretums. Douglas-fir is widely planted as an ornamental and timber tree across North America and Europe.

**Step 3: Photograph and record**

Take photos of:
1. A cone (close-up showing the three-pointed bracts)
2. Bark (both young smooth and mature furrowed if available)
3. Needles (close-up and whole branch)
4. The whole tree (stand back for the silhouette)

**Step 4: Estimate the age**

```
Rough age estimates from diameter at breast height (DBH):
  10 cm DBH  → ~20-30 years
  30 cm DBH  → ~50-80 years
  60 cm DBH  → ~100-200 years
  100 cm DBH → ~200-400 years
  150+ cm DBH → ~400-1000+ years (old growth)

These are rough estimates. Growth rate depends on site quality,
competition, and disturbance history. But the correlation is
real enough for a field estimate.
```

**What Just Happened:**
You identified the tree that paired with the first US spacecraft to leave Earth. Pseudotsuga menziesii has been growing in the Pacific Northwest for millions of years, building forests that predate human habitation. Pioneer 4 has been orbiting the Sun for 67 years. The tree outlasts the spacecraft by orders of magnitude. The Douglas-fir does not need to escape -- it grows where it is, for centuries, connected to its neighbors through fungal networks. Pioneer 4 escaped and was never heard from again.

**The NASA Connection:**
NASA uses remote sensing (including LiDAR from aircraft and satellites) to measure forest canopy height and biomass -- the same measurement principles that underlie spacecraft tracking. The Douglas-fir forests of the PNW are among the highest-biomass forests on Earth, storing carbon in wood that took centuries to accumulate. Identifying a tree by its field marks is the same skill as identifying a spacecraft by its telemetry signature -- pattern recognition from observable features.

### DIY 2: Model Rocket That Achieves "Escape" from a School Field

**Duration:** 4-6 hours (building) + 1 hour (launch)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Department:** Engineering / Physics
**Cost:** ~$50

**Materials:**
- Estes model rocket starter kit with B6-4 or C6-5 motor ($30-40)
- Launch pad and controller (included in kit)

- Field measuring tape or GPS for landing distance ($5)

**What You Build:**
A standard model rocket launched in a school field, with the explicit objective of reaching the "escape altitude" -- a threshold you define before launch. The exercise translates Pioneer 4's escape velocity into a vertical altitude analog:

```
THE ESCAPE ANALOGY:
  Pioneer 4 needed to exceed 11.0 km/s to escape Earth
  Your rocket needs to exceed [calculated altitude] to "escape" the field

Define the field:
  Field diameter: D meters (measure it)
  "Earth" = the field (you're standing on it)
  "Escape altitude" = D / 2 meters (half the field diameter)

  If the field is 100m across, escape altitude = 50 meters
  A B6-4 motor reaches ~60-80 meters -- borderline escape

  Your mission: calculate whether your rocket can "escape"
  Then launch and measure the actual altitude
  Compare: did you escape or fall back?
```

**The Pioneer 4 connection:**
Before launch, the student calculates predicted altitude using the Tsiolkovsky equation (simplified: specific impulse, mass ratio, drag estimate). This prediction might be wrong. Pioneer's predictions were wrong too -- Pioneer 1 was predicted to reach the Moon but fell back from 113,854 km. The student launches, measures, and learns whether their prediction was accurate. If the rocket exceeds the escape altitude, it "escaped." If not, it "fell back." Same physics, tabletop scale.

**Key learning moment:** The student experiences the uncertainty of rocketry firsthand. Wind, motor variation, fin alignment, parachute drag -- all affect the result. The prediction is a calculation. The launch is a measurement. They may not agree. Pioneer's engineers felt this same tension, five times, before Pioneer 4 proved the calculations correct.

### DIY 3: Plant a Douglas-fir Seedling

**Department:** Forestry / Ecology

- One Pseudotsuga menziesii seedling (bare-root, 1-2 year old, $3-8 from state forestry nurseries or local suppliers)

- Watering can
- Mulch (optional)
- A place to plant it (yard, community garden, school grounds -- with permission)

**What You Build:**
You plant a tree that will outlive you. A Douglas-fir seedling planted today, in suitable climate (USDA zones 4-6, western maritime or mountain), will reach:
- 1 meter in 3-5 years
- 10 meters in 20-30 years
- 30 meters in 60-80 years (taller than most buildings)
- 60+ meters in 200+ years (old growth begins)
- 80+ meters in 400+ years (towering old growth)

```
PLANTING INSTRUCTIONS:

Site selection:
  - Full sun to partial shade (Douglas-fir needs light to grow tall)
  - Well-drained soil (tolerates many soil types)
  - Not too close to structures (this tree WILL get large)
  - USDA zones 4-6 are ideal; zone 7-8 possible in maritime climate
  - In the PNW: plant anywhere that gets rain

Planting:
  1. Dig hole 2x wider than root ball, same depth
  2. Spread roots gently (do not circle or bunch)
  3. Fill with native soil (no amendments needed for Douglas-fir)
  4. Water deeply -- soak the entire root zone
  5. Mulch 5 cm deep around base, not touching trunk
  6. Water weekly for first summer, then rely on rainfall

First year:
  - Protect from deer (wire cage if needed)
  - Water during dry spells
  - Do not fertilize (Douglas-fir thrives in low-nutrient soil)

Monitoring:
  - Measure height annually (photograph with a ruler stake)
  - Record growth rate
  - Compare to allometric predictions: h ~ D^0.6
  - Watch for Douglas-fir cone production (begins at ~20 years)
```

**The Pioneer 4 connection:**
Pioneer 4 is still orbiting the Sun, 67 years after launch. Your Douglas-fir seedling will still be growing 67 years after planting -- and will be far from finished. Pioneer 4 weighs 6.08 kg and travels at 31 km/s. Your seedling will weigh several thousand kilograms at maturity and never move from where you planted it. Both are permanent: Pioneer 4 in its heliocentric orbit, the Douglas-fir in its mycorrhizal network. Both started small. Both persist. The Douglas-fir produces cones, seeds, and shade. Pioneer 4 produces nothing. The tree is the better investment.

**Key learning moment:** This is a decades project. The student plants a seedling and commits to a living experiment that will outlast their education, their career, possibly their lifetime. Every year they check the height. Every year the tree is taller. In 30 years, it shades the yard. In 100 years, it towers over the neighborhood. In 500 years -- if no one cuts it -- it is old growth, and someone will measure it with LiDAR from orbit, and the allometric equations in this year's arXiv papers will describe its height-diameter relationship. The planting is a five-minute exercise. The growth is the experiment. The experiment never ends.
