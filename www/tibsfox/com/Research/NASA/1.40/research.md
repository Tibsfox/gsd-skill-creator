# Mission 1.40 -- Gemini 4 (Titan II GLV)

## Deep Research: Walking in the Void

**Mission:** 1.40 / NASA Mission Series
**Date:** June 3-7, 1965
**Type:** Crewed orbital (66 orbits, 4 days)
**Program:** Gemini (NASA / Manned Spacecraft Center)
**Epoch:** 1 (Early Space Age)

**Dedication:** Ed White (November 14, 1930 -- January 27, 1967)
**Status:** SUCCESS -- first American EVA (extravehicular activity); 23 minutes of spacewalking by Ed White; 97 hours 56 minutes total mission duration; 66 orbits

## Twenty-Three Minutes Outside

At 19:34 UTC on June 3, 1965, approximately three hours and forty-six minutes into the Gemini 4 mission, pilot Ed White opened the right-hand hatch of the spacecraft, stood up on his seat, and floated into the void of space. He was 270 kilometers above the Earth, traveling at 7.8 kilometers per second, protected from the vacuum by a spacesuit and connected to the spacecraft by a 7.6-meter umbilical that provided oxygen and communication.

In his right hand he carried a Hand-Held Maneuvering Unit (HHMU) -- a small gun-shaped device that expelled compressed oxygen to provide thrust. White pointed it and squeezed the trigger, and he moved. He was flying. Not in a spacecraft, not in an airplane, but as a single human body in space, propelled by a jet of oxygen and controlled by pointing the nozzle. He maneuvered away from the spacecraft, then back. He tumbled, rolled, and oriented himself. He floated over the hatch, looked down at the Earth passing below at nearly 8 kilometers per second, and declared it the most natural feeling in the world.

The HHMU exhausted its oxygen supply after approximately three minutes. For the remaining twenty minutes of the EVA, White maneuvered by pulling on the umbilical tether and by pushing off the spacecraft's hull. He was no longer a self-propelled flyer -- he was a man on a rope, dependent on the tether for his connection to the spacecraft and on the friction of his gloves on the hull for directional control.

The view was extraordinary. White could see from the Gulf of Mexico to Florida to the Atlantic Ocean. The spacecraft was a small, shining object beside him, its white surface brilliant in the unfiltered sunlight. Below, the Earth curved away in all directions, the atmosphere a thin blue line along the horizon. Above, the sky was black.

Twenty-three minutes after he opened the hatch, the EVA was scheduled to end. Flight Director Chris Kraft, through capsule communicator (CapCom) Gus Grissom, ordered White back inside. White did not want to come in. The exchange, recorded on the communication loop, became one of the iconic moments of the space program:

Grissom (CapCom): "Gemini 4, get back in."
White: "I'm doing great."
Grissom: "Roger. Come on, let's get back in."
White: "It's the saddest moment of my life."

White reluctantly reentered the spacecraft, pulling himself back through the hatch and settling into his seat. The hatch was closed and latched, the cabin was repressurized. The first American spacewalk was over.

## Context: Leonov's Walk

Ed White was not the first human to walk in space. That distinction belongs to Alexei Leonov of the Soviet Union, who performed a 12-minute EVA from Voskhod 2 on March 18, 1965 -- two months and sixteen days before White's spacewalk.

Leonov's EVA had been harrowing. His spacesuit inflated in the vacuum, becoming rigid and immobile to the point where he could not bend his arms or legs. He was unable to reenter the airlock. In desperation, he partially depressurized his suit, risking the bends (decompression sickness), in order to regain enough flexibility to squeeze back through the hatch. He succeeded, but the experience demonstrated that EVA was far more dangerous and difficult than engineers had predicted.

The Soviets announced Leonov's EVA on March 18, 1965 -- five days before Gemini 3's launch and less than three months before Gemini 4's planned flight. NASA had originally planned for the first American EVA to occur on a later Gemini mission (possibly Gemini 6), but the Soviet achievement accelerated the timeline. The decision to attempt EVA on Gemini 4 was made in early April 1965, giving the mission planning team approximately two months to prepare.

The acceleration was risky. EVA hardware had been in development but was not fully flight-qualified. The HHMU had been tested in zero-gravity aircraft flights (the "Vomit Comet" KC-135) but never in the vacuum of space. The EVA procedures were developed rapidly, based on engineering analysis rather than extensive testing. The compressed timeline was driven by the same competitive pressure that had pushed Mercury and would later push Apollo: the Soviet Union was setting records, and the United States needed to match them.

White's EVA, when it occurred, was smoother than Leonov's. White's suit did not inflate uncontrollably (the Gemini suit operated at a lower pressure than the Voskhod suit). The HHMU worked as designed. The tether and umbilical maintained their connections. The hatch opened and closed without incident. The 23 minutes of EVA produced photographs, engineering data, and confidence that Americans could work outside a spacecraft -- confidence that would be essential for the lunar surface operations of Apollo.

## The Crew

**Command Pilot: James A. McDivitt** -- Major, USAF. Born June 10, 1929, in Chicago, Illinois. Korean War veteran (145 combat missions). Test pilot at Edwards Air Force Base. Selected as a Group 2 astronaut in September 1962. Gemini 4 was his first spaceflight. McDivitt later commanded Apollo 9 (March 1969), the first crewed flight of the Lunar Module in Earth orbit -- a mission directly descended from the orbital maneuvering and EVA work that Gemini 4 pioneered.

**Pilot: Edward H. White II** -- Lieutenant Colonel, USAF. Born November 14, 1930, in San Antonio, Texas. Son of Major General Edward H. White Sr. West Point graduate (Class of 1952). Test pilot at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base. Selected as a Group 2 astronaut in September 1962. White was an athlete -- he had narrowly missed qualifying for the 1952 US Olympic team in the 400-meter hurdles. His physical conditioning was evident during the EVA: he moved with an ease and confidence that contrasted sharply with Leonov's struggle two months earlier.

McDivitt and White were close friends who had trained together since their selection as astronauts. The friendship was evident in their crew dynamics: McDivitt managed the spacecraft systems and maintained communication with Mission Control while White performed the EVA, a division of labor that required absolute trust.

## The Spacecraft

Gemini 4 flew the same basic spacecraft design as Gemini 3, with modifications based on the planned four-day mission duration and the EVA requirement.

**Mass:** Approximately 3,574 kilograms at launch.

**EVA modifications:** The right-hand hatch was modified for EVA operations. A ventilation system was installed to allow White to breathe spacecraft oxygen through the umbilical while outside. An EVA chest pack (the Ventilation Control Module) regulated oxygen flow and provided emergency oxygen. The umbilical was 7.6 meters long, providing oxygen, communication, and (via a tether within the umbilical) a safety connection to the spacecraft.

**HHMU:** The Hand-Held Maneuvering Unit was a small, pistol-shaped device weighing approximately 3.6 kilograms. It contained two small tanks of compressed oxygen with a total capacity of approximately 0.45 kilograms of O2. The unit had a single nozzle that could be pointed in any direction; thrust was produced by squeezing a trigger that opened the oxygen valve. The theoretical delta-v from the HHMU was approximately 1.8 meters per second -- enough for gentle maneuvering near the spacecraft but far too little for any independent orbital travel. The oxygen supply lasted approximately three minutes of intermittent use.

**Fuel cells:** Gemini 4 was the first Gemini mission to fly fuel cells for electrical power, replacing the batteries used on Gemini 3. The fuel cells combined hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity and water -- a technology that would be critical for the longer Gemini missions and later for Apollo.

**Computer:** The Gemini spacecraft carried an early digital computer -- the Gemini Guidance Computer, built by IBM. The computer weighed approximately 26.8 kilograms and had a memory of 4,096 words (each word 39 bits). It was used for orbit calculations, reentry guidance, and rendezvous computations. Gemini 4's computer played a role in the attempted rendezvous with the spent Titan II second stage (described below).

## The Mission

### Launch

Gemini 4 launched from Launch Complex 19 at Cape Kennedy Air Force Station on June 3, 1965, at 15:15:59 UTC (10:16 AM local time). The Titan II GLV performed nominally, inserting the spacecraft into an initial orbit of approximately 163 by 282 kilometers.

### The Stationkeeping Attempt

Before the EVA, McDivitt attempted to perform stationkeeping with the spent Titan II second stage -- flying in formation with the cylindrical rocket body as it tumbled through space. This was an informal objective: the Gemini 4 flight plan included an attempt to maneuver close to the second stage as practice for rendezvous operations that later Gemini missions would perform with Agena target vehicles.

The attempt failed, and the failure taught a critical lesson. McDivitt used the OAMS thrusters to approach the tumbling second stage, which was visible ahead of the spacecraft at distances ranging from a few hundred meters to several kilometers. He fired the thrusters to close the gap, but each time he did so, the orbital mechanics of the situation worked against him: thrusting toward a target in orbit does not close the distance in the intuitive way that driving a car toward an object does.

In orbital mechanics, thrusting in the direction of a target that is slightly ahead and above moves you into a higher orbit, which paradoxically causes you to slow down and fall behind the target. Conversely, thrusting to slow down drops you into a lower orbit, where you speed up and pull ahead. The physics of orbital rendezvous is counterintuitive to a pilot trained in atmospheric flight, where pointing at a target and applying thrust closes the distance. In orbit, the geometry is more complex.

McDivitt expended approximately 42 percent of his OAMS propellant during the stationkeeping attempt before Mission Control called off the exercise. The remaining propellant was needed for the EVA operations, the four-day mission, and the deorbit burn. The lesson was absorbed immediately by the Gemini program: orbital rendezvous required mathematical precision, computer guidance, and radar tracking -- not visual flying. The rendezvous technique that Gemini 6A and 7 would use successfully in December 1965 was developed in direct response to Gemini 4's demonstration that "eyeball" rendezvous does not work.

### The EVA

The EVA was performed during the third revolution, over the United States, while the spacecraft was in contact with ground stations. The sequence was:

**19:34 UTC:** Cabin depressurization began. Both astronauts were in their spacesuits with helmets sealed and gloves locked.

**19:36 UTC:** White opened the right-hand hatch. The residual atmosphere in the cabin vented to space. White stood up and floated out of the spacecraft, connected by the 7.6-meter umbilical.

**19:36-19:39 UTC:** White used the HHMU to maneuver. He translated away from the spacecraft, moved along its length, and returned. The HHMU provided smooth, controllable thrust. The photographs taken during this phase -- showing White floating in space with the Earth below, the gold visor of his helmet reflecting the spacecraft -- became some of the most iconic images of the space program.

**19:39 UTC:** The HHMU exhausted its oxygen supply. White continued to maneuver by pulling on the umbilical and pushing off the spacecraft hull.

**19:39-19:59 UTC:** White floated outside the spacecraft for an additional twenty minutes, photographing the Earth and the spacecraft, testing the mobility of the EVA suit, and evaluating the visual conditions in space (the extreme contrast between sunlit and shadowed surfaces, the absence of atmospheric haze).

**19:59 UTC:** CapCom ordered White to reenter the spacecraft. White expressed reluctance but complied. The hatch closing proved difficult -- thermal expansion in the direct sunlight had slightly warped the mechanism, and White and McDivitt struggled for several minutes to get the hatch fully latched. Eventually, after physical effort from both astronauts, the hatch sealed and the cabin was repressurized.

Total EVA duration: 23 minutes.

### Four Days in Orbit

After the EVA, Gemini 4 continued for an additional three and a half days in orbit. The mission demonstrated that a two-person crew could live and work in the Gemini spacecraft for the duration of a typical Apollo lunar mission.

The crew conducted eleven experiments:

- **Radiation measurement** (dosimeters, emulsion packs)
- **Medical experiments** (inflight exerciser, cardiovascular conditioning)
- **Photography experiments** (terrain photography over the United States, weather photography)
- **Technology experiments** (navigation by star sightings, electrostatic charge measurement)

The four-day duration was the longest American spaceflight at that time (the Soviet Union held the endurance record with Vostok 5's five-day flight in June 1963). The crew reported fatigue, minor discomfort from the cramped cabin, and occasional difficulty sleeping in zero gravity, but no medical problems that would prevent longer missions.

The spacecraft's systems performed well over the four-day period. The fuel cells maintained electrical power. The environmental control system maintained cabin temperature and atmospheric composition. The OAMS retained sufficient propellant for attitude control and deorbit. The mission demonstrated that the Gemini spacecraft was capable of supporting multi-day operations -- a critical requirement for the long-duration missions (Gemini 5 and Gemini 7) that would follow.

### Reentry and Recovery

The deorbit retrofire occurred at 97:00:08 MET. The four solid-fuel retrorockets fired, decelerating the spacecraft. The adapter section was jettisoned, and Gemini 4 reentered the atmosphere.

The reentry was controlled using the rolling technique demonstrated on Gemini 3, with improved aerodynamic modeling to correct the undershoot that Gemini 3 had experienced. The actual splashdown point was approximately 81 kilometers from the planned target -- still an undershoot, though smaller than Gemini 3's 84-kilometer error. Further refinement of the reentry guidance would continue through subsequent Gemini missions.

Gemini 4 splashed down in the Atlantic Ocean at 17:12:11 UTC on June 7, 1965 -- 97 hours, 56 minutes, and 12 seconds after launch. The spacecraft was recovered by the aircraft carrier USS Wasp. McDivitt and White were in good health.

## Engineering Significance: What EVA Taught

Ed White's 23-minute spacewalk appeared effortless. The photographs showed a man floating serenely against the blue and white backdrop of Earth, visor gleaming in the sunlight, tether trailing behind him like the tail of a kite. The reality was more complex, and the engineering lessons were significant.

**Thermal management:** In direct sunlight, surfaces on the spacecraft and suit reached temperatures above 120 degrees Celsius. In shadow, temperatures dropped below minus 100 degrees Celsius. The EVA suit's multilayer insulation handled these extremes, but White reported significant temperature variation depending on his orientation relative to the Sun.

**Mobility:** The pressurized suit was reasonably mobile for the limited activities of this EVA (floating, photographing, maneuvering with the HHMU). But White did not attempt to perform mechanical work -- turning bolts, connecting cables, operating equipment. Later Gemini EVAs (particularly Gemini 9A, 10, 11, and 12) would reveal that performing useful work in a pressurized suit in zero gravity was extraordinarily difficult, requiring handholds, foot restraints, and underwater training to master.

**Hatch operations:** The difficulty in closing the Gemini 4 hatch after EVA highlighted a thermal design problem. The hatch mechanism, designed to operate at room temperature, expanded in the direct sunlight and became stiff. The fix for subsequent missions included thermal coatings and mechanical modifications. The general lesson -- that space hardware must work across a 200-plus-degree temperature range -- was reinforced.

**HHMU limitations:** The compressed-oxygen maneuvering gun worked well but was exhausted in three minutes. Future EVA maneuvering requirements would be met by larger, more capable systems: the Astronaut Maneuvering Unit (AMU) tested on later Gemini missions and the Manned Maneuvering Unit (MMU) flown on Space Shuttle missions in the 1980s.

**Communication:** Voice communication through the umbilical worked, but there were periods of garbled transmission. Later EVA systems would improve communication quality. The photographs taken during EVA -- by a modified Hasselblad 500C camera -- were of exceptional quality and became primary documentation of the event.

## The Race in June 1965

Gemini 4's June 1965 flight occurred during an intense period of the space race. The timeline of the first half of 1965 illustrates the pace:

- **March 18, 1965:** Leonov (USSR) performs first EVA, 12 minutes, from Voskhod 2.
- **March 23, 1965:** Grissom and Young fly Gemini 3, first crewed Gemini, first orbital maneuver.
- **March 24, 1965:** Ranger 9 impacts Alphonsus crater, live TV broadcast.
- **June 3, 1965:** White performs first American EVA, 23 minutes, from Gemini 4.

In four months, the United States had matched and in some cases exceeded every Soviet achievement in crewed spaceflight: two-person crew, orbital maneuvering, and extravehicular activity. The Gemini program was closing the gap. By the end of 1965, with the rendezvous of Gemini 6A and 7 and the 14-day endurance flight of Gemini 7, the United States would hold the lead in every category except total time in space -- a lead it would not relinquish.

## Dedication: Ed White (November 14, 1930 -- January 27, 1967)

Edward Higgins White II was born on November 14, 1930, in San Antonio, Texas. His father, Edward H. White Sr., was an Army Air Corps officer who would retire as a Major General. The elder White had been a barnstormer in the 1920s and took his son flying as a child. Ed White grew up around aircraft and never considered another career.

He graduated from West Point in 1952, earned a master's degree in aeronautical engineering from the University of Michigan in 1959, and became a test pilot at Wright-Patterson Air Force Base. He was selected as a NASA astronaut in September 1962, part of the "New Nine" that included Neil Armstrong, Frank Borman, Jim Lovell, and John Young.

White was an exceptional athlete. He ran the 400-meter hurdles at West Point and trained for the 1952 Olympics, missing the team by a narrow margin. His physical fitness was evident during the Gemini 4 EVA: where Leonov had struggled against an inflated suit, White moved with apparent ease, maneuvering confidently and expressing visible enjoyment.

After Gemini 4, White was assigned to the crew of Apollo 1 alongside Gus Grissom (command pilot) and Roger Chaffee (pilot). On January 27, 1967, during a launch pad test of the Apollo 1 spacecraft at Launch Complex 34 at Cape Kennedy, a fire broke out in the cabin's pure oxygen atmosphere. All three astronauts died. White, seated on the right side of the spacecraft nearest the hatch, was found with his arms raised toward the hatch mechanism -- he had been trying to open it when he was overcome. The inward-opening hatch, which required at least 90 seconds to unbolt under normal conditions, could not be opened against the internal pressure generated by the fire.

White was 36 years old. He had spent 23 minutes floating outside a spacecraft, moving freely in the void of space, reluctant to come back in because it was so extraordinary. Nineteen months later, he died in a fire on the ground, reaching for a hatch that would not open.

The Apollo 1 fire killed three astronauts and nearly killed the Apollo program. The subsequent investigation and redesign delayed the first crewed Apollo flight by twenty-one months. The redesigned Apollo spacecraft -- with a quick-opening outward-swinging hatch, reduced flammable materials, and a mixed-gas (60% oxygen, 40% nitrogen) atmosphere at launch -- was safer than the spacecraft it replaced. The fire was a catastrophe that forced improvements. The improvements made the Moon landings possible.
