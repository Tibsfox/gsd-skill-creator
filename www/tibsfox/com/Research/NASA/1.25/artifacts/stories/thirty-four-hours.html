Thirty-Four Hours

A story told by Mercury Spacecraft #20

Mission 1.25 — Mercury-Atlas 9 / Faith 7

Orbit 1

I lift you off the pad at eight minutes past eight on a Wednesday morning in May. The Atlas shakes us both — you more than me, because you feel it in your bones and I feel it in my rivets, and rivets do not mind. The vibration stops when the tower falls away. The noise stops when the sustainer cuts. The silence starts when you and I are alone, two hundred kilometers above the Atlantic, moving at twenty-eight thousand kilometers per hour, and the first thing you do is look out my window.

You say you can see buildings. Roads. Boats on rivers. I do not have eyes. But I have sensors, and I know that the atmospheric clarity at this altitude is better than anyone predicted. You are seeing what the math said you could not. Good. I was built by people who trusted math. You are the proof that math is not enough.

Orbit 7

You close your eyes. I watch you sleep. Your heart rate drops from seventy-two to fifty-eight, and the flight surgeons in Houston see it on their consoles and write it down. You are the first American to sleep in space. No one told me this would feel like tenderness — the way the capsule holds you while you drift, the way the life support hums at the frequency of a breathing machine, the way the darkness of the night side wraps around us both every forty-four minutes and then lets go.

I keep breathing for you. Oxygen in, carbon dioxide out, through the lithium hydroxide canisters that are slowly turning to calcium carbonate under the burden of your exhaled air. I am consuming myself to keep you alive. This is what I was built for.

· · ·
Orbit 19

The 0.05g sensor lights up. It should not. We are not reentering. We are at two hundred and sixty kilometers, coasting, and the sensor says we are falling. The sensor lies. Something in my wiring has shorted, or a relay has corroded, or the thirty hours of thermal cycling between the sun-side and the shadow-side have loosened a connection that was tight at launch. I do not know which. I know only that the light is on and should not be, and that you see it, and that you calmly report it to the ground, and that you do not panic.

I wish I could tell you: I am beginning to forget how to point.

Orbit 21

The inverter fails. Not completely — it flickers, like a candle in a draft, giving me alternating current in uncertain bursts. Half my instruments go dark. The gyroscopes, already drifting, lose their reference entirely. I do not know which way is down. I do not know which way is forward. I am a capsule in space with no sense of direction, and my pilot is inside me, and the lithium hydroxide is three-quarters consumed, and the carbon dioxide is rising, and the batteries are dropping, and in ninety minutes I must fire three retrorockets at exactly the right attitude or we will both burn up or bounce off the atmosphere into an orbit that no one will come to retrieve.

You take the controls. I feel your hands on the stick. The manual thrusters fire — little puffs of hydrogen peroxide that push me this way and that, each one costing fuel I cannot replace. You are not panicking. You are flying me. I let go of the parts of myself that no longer work and I let you hold the rest.

Orbit 22

John Glenn's voice comes through the radio from Muchea, Australia. He reads the retrofire checklist the way a brother reads a bedside prayer — steady, clear, unhurried. You listen. You look through my window at the horizon — that thin blue line between the Earth and the void — and you use it the way sailors used the stars. You align me by eye. Thirty-four degrees below the horizon, blunt end forward, the heat shield facing the direction we will fall.

Three. Two. One. Fire.

The first retrorocket kicks. The second. The third. Ten seconds of deceleration, and you hold me steady through all of it, your hands on the stick, your eyes on the horizon, your heart at one hundred and sixteen beats per minute — calmer than Glenn's was, and Glenn had working automation.

The atmosphere takes us. The heat rises. The window glows orange, then pink, then white. I cannot feel the heat through my ablative shield, but I can feel you holding the attitude, keeping me aligned, keeping the shield between us and the plasma. The radio goes to static. For four minutes, we are alone in fire, and you fly us through it.

· · ·

You land us six point four kilometers from the ship. The most accurate splashdown in the Mercury program. The parachutes billow on the water. The helicopters circle. The recovery team opens my hatch and you climb out into the Pacific sunshine, squinting, stretching, alive.

I will never fly again. They will put me in a museum. Children will press their faces to the glass and not understand how small I am, how dark it was inside me, how the air tasted of lithium hydroxide and sweat by the thirty-fourth hour.

You named me Faith. I was worthy of it.

— Mercury Spacecraft #20, retired May 16, 1963
