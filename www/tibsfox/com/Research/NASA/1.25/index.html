Mercury-Atlas 9 / Faith 7 — The Longest Day

The final Mercury mission, and the tree that stands alone at the edge

Date: May 15–16, 1963 Program: Mercury (NASA / McDonnell Aircraft) Epoch: 1 — Early Space Age Astronaut: L. Gordon Cooper Jr.

Mission Summary

On May 15, 1963 — L. Frank Baum’s birthday — Gordon Cooper became the last American to orbit alone in a Mercury capsule. Faith 7 circled the Earth 22 times over 34 hours and 19 minutes, the longest American spaceflight to date. Cooper was the first American to sleep in space. When the automatic attitude control, gyroscopes, and main inverter failed progressively during the final orbits, Cooper hand-flew the entire reentry — aligning the capsule using the horizon visible through his window and firing the retrorockets with a wristwatch. He splashed down 6.4 kilometers from the recovery carrier USS Kearsarge: the most accurate landing in the Mercury program.

Mercury was over. Six crewed flights spanning two years: from Shepard’s fifteen-minute suborbital hop to Cooper’s thirty-four-hour endurance marathon. The program that proved Americans could survive in space, function in microgravity, and bring a failing spacecraft home by hand.

The Spacecraft

  • Spacecraft: Mercury #20, call sign Faith 7
  • Mass: ~1,360 kg at launch
  • Dimensions: 1.89 m long × 1.89 m wide (interior volume ~1.7 m³)
  • Power: Silver-zinc primary batteries (~36,000 Wh)
  • Attitude control: Automatic (ASCS) and manual hydrogen peroxide thrusters (27.2 kg H2O2)
  • Life support: 100% O2 at 5.0 psi, LiOH CO2 scrubber, water-cooled heat exchanger
  • Launch vehicle: Atlas 130-D (LC-14, Cape Canaveral)
  • Orbit: 160 × 267 km, 32.5° inclination, 88.5-minute period

The Flight

Orbits 1–6: Nominal operations. Cooper deployed a flashing beacon sphere for visual tracking tests, operated the first live television camera from an American spacecraft, and photographed Earth with unexpected clarity — reporting individual buildings and roads visible from orbit.

Orbits 7–14: Planned sleep period. Cooper achieved approximately 4.5 hours of genuine sleep — the first American to sleep in space. Biomedical telemetry confirmed heart rate dropping from 72 to 58 BPM.

Orbit 19: First warning. The 0.05g sensor (designed to detect reentry onset) produced a false positive. The attitude reference system began drifting beyond usable range.

Orbit 21: Cascading failures. Main inverter intermittent. CO2 partial pressure rising. Automatic control effectively dead.

Orbit 22: Manual reentry. Cooper aligned the capsule by eye using the Earth’s horizon, fired the retrorockets on John Glenn’s countdown from Muchea, Australia, and hand-flew through reentry. Splashdown: 6.4 km from USS Kearsarge. The most accurate Mercury landing.

Resonance Axes

1
Standing at the edge: Pacific Madrone grows at the forest’s edge — on clifftops and rocky bluffs where the conifers end. MA-9 was at the edge of Mercury’s design envelope, the final flight of a program pushed beyond its rated limits. Both inhabit the liminal zone and thrive there.
2
Endurance through self-renewal: Madrone bark peels continuously, revealing photosynthetic green tissue beneath. Cooper’s automated systems peeled away one by one, revealing the manual pilot beneath. Both endure by shedding what fails and functioning with what remains.
3
The singular strategy: Pacific Madrone is the only native broadleaf evergreen tree in the PNW. Cooper’s MA-9 was the only full-day endurance mission in Mercury. Both are singular in their context and both prove that the singular approach works.
4
Deep roots surviving fire: Madrone resprouts from root crowns after fire — 89% resprouting rate. Cooper’s deep training roots survived the “fire” of cascading systems failures and regenerated the mission through manual control.
5
Feeding the migrants: Madrone berries feed migrating Western Tanagers each autumn. Cooper’s 34-hour endurance data fed the Gemini and Apollo programs that would follow Mercury through the edge Cooper defined.
6
Faith at the margin: Madrone persists through faith in its own adaptations: deep roots, thick leaves, resprouting capability. Cooper named his capsule Faith 7 and persisted through faith in his training. Both trust not in conditions being kind, but in themselves being prepared.

What Cooper Proved

Cooper’s flight answered the questions Mercury needed to answer before handing off to Gemini: Can a pilot sleep in space? Yes. Can a spacecraft operate for a full day? Barely — systems fail, but the failures are manageable. Can a pilot fly reentry by hand when the automation dies? Yes, and more accurately than the automation. Every answer fed forward into Gemini and Apollo. The biomedical data informed crew scheduling. The systems failure data informed redundancy design. The manual reentry data informed the pilot-as-backup-system philosophy that carried through every subsequent American spacecraft.

Habitat

The only native broadleaf evergreen tree of the Pacific Northwest. Grows on rocky bluffs, clifftops, and forest edges where no conifer can — the tree at the margin. Its terracotta bark peels in papery sheets to reveal jade-green photosynthetic bark beneath: continuous self-renewal. Survives fire by resprouting from root crowns (89% resprouting rate). Produces bright red-orange berries in autumn that feed migrating Western Tanagers and other frugivorous birds. Named for Archibald Menzies, the same botanist honored in Douglas-fir’s name.

Bird Connection

Piranga ludoviciana (Western Tanager)

Degree 24. A spectacular yellow-and-red bird that migrates 4,000 km from Central America to PNW conifer forests each spring — arriving in May, the month Cooper flew. The tanager that is not a tanager: reclassified from Thraupidae to Cardinalidae by molecular phylogenetics. Its red head color comes from rhodoxanthin, a pigment it cannot synthesize but must obtain from its insect diet — a borrowed beauty. Feeds on Pacific Madrone berries during autumn migration, linking organism and bird directly.

Dedicated to L. Frank Baum

May 15, 1856 — May 6, 1919

Born on the exact date Cooper launched, 107 years apart. Created The Wonderful Wizard of Oz — a story about a journey to a strange and wondrous place and the struggle to return home. Dorothy discovers she has always had the power to go home. Cooper discovered the same when the automation died: he had always had the power to fly the reentry himself. Faith is not the absence of evidence. It is the presence of preparation.
