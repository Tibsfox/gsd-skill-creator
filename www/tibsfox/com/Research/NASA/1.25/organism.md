# Organism Pairing: Arbutus menziesii (Pacific Madrone)

## The Tree That Stands Alone at the Edge

**Scientific Name:** Arbutus menziesii Pursh
**Family:** Ericaceae (heath family)
**Common Names:** Pacific Madrone, Madrona, Madrono, Arbutus
**Paired Mission:** 1.25 -- Mercury-Atlas 9 / Faith 7, May 15--16, 1963
**PNW Status:** Native, iconic -- the only native broadleaf evergreen tree of the Pacific Northwest

---

## What Stands at the Edge

Walk to the western edge of a bluff overlooking Puget Sound -- Mukilteo, Whidbey Island, the San Juans, anywhere the land drops away and the water begins -- and look for the tree that leans outward. Not the Douglas-fir, which stands in the forest. Not the western red cedar, which fills the valleys. Not the bigleaf maple, which clusters in riparian draws. Look for the tree at the margin, the one that grows where the forest ends and the open sky begins, the tree with the smooth terracotta bark that peels in papery sheets to reveal jade green wood beneath, the tree that holds its waxy evergreen leaves when every other broadleaf has gone bare, the tree that looks like it belongs in the Mediterranean rather than the Pacific Northwest.

This is Arbutus menziesii. Pacific Madrone. The most singular tree in the PNW flora.

Pacific Madrone is not dominant. Douglas-fir is dominant. Madrone does not build forests the way Douglas-fir builds forests. You do not walk through a Madrone cathedral. You do not see Madrone timber yards or Madrone lumber. The tree does not define the landscape by its abundance. It defines the landscape by its character. Pacific Madrone is the tree that occupies the places no other tree can -- the rocky bluffs, the thin-soil clifftops, the exposed south-facing slopes where winter winds scour and summer drought bakes. Where the conditions are too harsh for the conifers that dominate the PNW, Madrone thrives. Where the forest gives way to the sky, Madrone stands.

Gordon Cooper was not the dominant Mercury astronaut in the way Glenn was dominant -- Glenn's flight had been the defining moment, the first American in orbit, the national hero. Cooper was not the most precise, the way Schirra was precise. Cooper was the one who stood at the edge. He flew the last Mercury mission, the one that pushed the spacecraft beyond its design limits, the one where the systems failed and the pilot had to bring himself home. He flew alone, like every Mercury astronaut, but he flew alone for thirty-four hours -- longer than any of them, in a capsule that was falling apart around him. Madrone stands at the forest's edge. Cooper stood at Mercury's edge. Both occupy the liminal space where the familiar system ends and something new begins.

## Morphology and Life History

Pacific Madrone is a medium to large broadleaf evergreen tree, reaching heights of 6 to 30 meters in typical specimens, with exceptional individuals approaching 40 meters. The trunk is often crooked, leaning, multiple-stemmed -- a posture that reflects the exposed, wind-swept habitats where Madrone grows best. Unlike the arrow-straight Douglas-fir, Madrone makes no concession to verticality. It grows toward light, and on a clifftop that means growing sideways, reaching out over the water, leaning into the gap.

The bark is the feature that makes Madrone unmistakable. Young bark is smooth, thin, and red-brown to terracotta -- a warm, almost tropical color in a landscape of gray-green conifers. As the bark ages, it dries and peels in thin papery sheets, curling away from the trunk to reveal the new bark beneath -- a striking pale green to yellow-green that photosynthesizes through the thin bark surface. The exfoliation is continuous: the tree is always shedding, always revealing, always showing new surface beneath old. In summer, the ground beneath a Madrone is littered with curling bark shreds, and the trunk and branches are a patchwork of red, orange, green, and cream.

This continuous self-renewal resonates with Cooper's flight. The Mercury program was shedding its old capabilities throughout MA-9 -- the automatic attitude control failed, the gyroscopes drifted, the electrical system degraded. Each failure peeled away a layer of automation, revealing the human pilot beneath. Cooper was the green wood under the peeling bark: the fundamental capability that remained when the outer systems fell away. The spacecraft shed its automated functions one by one. The pilot, like the Madrone's photosynthetic bark, was always there underneath.

The leaves are thick, leathery, and evergreen -- 7 to 12 centimeters long, glossy dark green above and pale gray-green beneath. Unlike the deciduous broadleaves of the PNW (bigleaf maple, red alder, black cottonwood), Madrone holds its leaves through the winter. This is the key adaptation: by retaining functional leaves year-round, Madrone can photosynthesize on the warm winter days that are common in the marine climate zone. While the deciduous trees are bare and dormant, Madrone is quietly accumulating carbon. The evergreen strategy is energetically expensive -- the thick, waxy leaves cost more to build than thin deciduous leaves -- but it pays off in the mild PNW winters where many days are warm enough for photosynthesis but deciduous trees have already dropped their leaves.

Cooper's endurance strategy was similar. The other Mercury astronauts flew intense, short missions -- Glenn's three orbits, Schirra's six orbits. They burned hot and landed. Cooper's twenty-two orbits required a different approach: sustained, steady, conserving. He drifted in a powered-down capsule to save battery life. He slept. He managed consumables the way Madrone manages water -- carefully, over a long period, not spending everything at once. The evergreen strategy is the endurance strategy: not the most dramatic, not the most productive in any single moment, but persistent, sustained, and ultimately the one that outlasts the season.

The flowers of Pacific Madrone appear in late spring -- April to June, overlapping precisely with Cooper's May 15 launch date. They are small, urn-shaped, white to pinkish, clustered in drooping panicles at the branch tips. The flower shape is characteristic of the Ericaceae: the petals are fused into a bell or urn, with the reproductive parts hidden inside. Hummingbirds and bees pollinate the flowers, and the nectar is an important food source for Anna's Hummingbird during the spring breeding season.

The fruit is a round, bright red-orange berry approximately 1 centimeter in diameter, ripening in autumn. The berries are edible but mealy and bland to human taste -- not poisonous, but not rewarding. For birds, however, Madrone berries are a critical food source. Band-tailed Pigeons, American Robins, Varied Thrushes, Cedar Waxwings, and Western Tanagers -- our degree 24 bird -- feed heavily on Madrone berries during autumn migration. The tree that stands alone at the edge feeds the birds that pass through the edge. Cooper's flight, standing at the edge of Mercury, fed the data into Gemini and Apollo -- the programs that would pass through the edge Cooper defined.

## Ecological Role: The Edge Species

Pacific Madrone occupies a specific ecological niche that no other PNW tree fills: the dry, exposed, rocky margin between the coniferous forest and the open environment. This edge habitat -- called an ecotone -- is a transition zone where forest conditions grade into grassland, shrubland, or bare rock. Ecotones are biologically rich because they contain species from both adjacent habitats plus edge specialists that exist only in the transition zone. Madrone is the premier edge specialist of the PNW.

**Drought tolerance:** Madrone's deep root system can penetrate rock crevices and fractured bedrock to access water that shallow-rooted conifers cannot reach. On the thin, rocky soils of Puget Sound bluffs, Madrone survives summer drought by tapping water reserves that exist meters below the surface. The thick, waxy leaves reduce water loss through transpiration. The bark, which is partially photosynthetic, allows the tree to produce energy even when water stress forces partial leaf drop. These adaptations make Madrone functional in habitats where Douglas-fir seedlings die of drought stress.

Cooper's flight was a drought-tolerance test. The Mercury capsule's consumables were the water table -- oxygen, battery power, CO2 absorbent, attitude control fuel. Cooper survived the "drought" of depleting systems by tapping reserves that the automatic systems could not access: his piloting skill, his visual horizon reference, his ability to manual-fire the retrograde rockets. When the automated systems ran dry, Cooper's reserves were still there, deeper than the automation could reach.

**Mycorrhizal specificity:** Madrone forms ectomycorrhizal associations with a specific suite of fungi, including species in the genera Arctostaphylos and several truffle-forming genera. These mycorrhizal partners are different from the fungi that associate with Douglas-fir and western hemlock, which means that Madrone's root-zone ecology is distinct from the surrounding coniferous forest. The fungal network that supports Madrone is its own system, connected to the broader forest mycorrhizal web but not dependent on it. Madrone brings its own support network.

**Wildlife habitat:** The berries, as noted, feed dozens of bird species during autumn migration. The hollow trunks of old Madrones provide nesting cavities for woodpeckers, owls, and flying squirrels. The peeling bark shelters insects that feed bark-gleaning birds including the Western Tanager. The tree's presence at the forest edge creates a structural bridge between the closed forest and the open environment, providing perching sites, shelter, and food for species that move between habitats.

## Distribution and Habitat

Arbutus menziesii ranges from southwestern British Columbia south through western Washington, western Oregon, and into California, where it reaches its southern limit in the mountains of San Diego County. The distribution is strictly maritime and coastal-influenced -- Madrone occurs west of the Cascades in Washington and Oregon, and within the fog belt and coastal mountains in California. It does not occur in the interior.

**Puget Sound lowlands:** Madrone is common on the rocky bluffs, glacial outwash terraces, and south-facing slopes of the Puget Sound region. Mukilteo, Whidbey Island, the San Juan Islands, and the east shore of the Olympic Peninsula all have significant Madrone populations. The tree is a visual signature of the Puget Sound landscape -- its terracotta bark and evergreen crown against the gray water and gray sky.

**Snohomish County:** Foxy's home ground. Madrone occurs on the bluffs above Puget Sound from Everett south through Mukilteo, and on the drier, south-facing slopes throughout the county's lowlands. Second-growth Madrone is common in residential areas where the original conifer forest was cleared -- Madrone colonizes disturbed sites with good sun exposure and well-drained soil.

**Fire ecology:** Like Douglas-fir, Madrone is adapted to fire, but in a different way. Rather than surviving fire through thick bark (the Douglas-fir strategy), Madrone survives fire by resprouting from the root crown. Even when the above-ground tree is completely killed by fire, the root system persists and sends up new shoots -- often multiple stems, creating the multi-trunked growth form that is characteristic of the species. Madrone's fire response is not resistance but resurrection. The tree burns, and grows back. Burns again, and grows back again. The root system can persist through multiple fire cycles, each time regrowing the above-ground structure from stored energy in the roots.

Cooper's capsule "burned" through its systems progressively -- the automatic control system failed, the electrical system degraded, the CO2 scrubbers were saturating. But the fundamental system -- the human pilot -- survived each failure and regrew the mission's capability from the root of manual control. Cooper's manual reentry was the resprout after the systems fire. The root system -- training, skill, composure -- persisted through the cascading failures and regenerated the mission's ability to achieve its final objective: a safe return home.

## The Singular Broadleaf

Pacific Madrone is the only native broadleaf evergreen tree in the Pacific Northwest. This distinction sounds like a piece of botanical trivia. It is not. It is a fundamental statement about the tree's evolutionary and ecological identity.

The PNW is dominated by conifers: Douglas-fir, western red cedar, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, grand fir, Pacific silver fir, noble fir, subalpine fir, mountain hemlock, Alaska yellow cedar, shore pine, ponderosa pine. The coniferous dominance of the PNW is so complete that the region is essentially defined by it -- the temperate rainforest, the old-growth cathedral, the timber industry. Into this overwhelming coniferous landscape, Madrone inserts itself as something completely different. Not a conifer. Not a deciduous broadleaf. An evergreen broadleaf -- a subtropical strategy in a temperate landscape, a Mediterranean answer to a Pacific Northwest question.

The singularity resonates with Cooper's position in the Mercury program. He was the only Mercury astronaut to fly a full-day endurance mission. The only one to sleep in space. The only one to hand-fly a complete reentry due to total automatic system failure. He was singular in his mission profile the way Madrone is singular in its ecological strategy -- not because no one else could have done it, but because the specific conditions of his flight produced an outcome that no other Mercury flight approached. Glenn orbited three times and became a national icon. Cooper orbited twenty-two times, slept, ate, troubleshot cascading failures, hand-flew reentry, and splashed down within visual range of the recovery carrier. It was not the most famous Mercury flight. It was the most complete.

Madrone is not the most famous PNW tree. It is the most singular.

## Resonance Axes

### 1. Standing at the Edge
Pacific Madrone grows at the forest edge -- on clifftops, rocky bluffs, and exposed slopes where the coniferous forest gives way to open sky. Mercury-Atlas 9 was the edge of the Mercury program, the final flight, the mission that pushed the spacecraft beyond its design envelope. Cooper flew at the boundary between what Mercury could do and what it couldn't. Madrone grows at the boundary between where the forest can exist and where it can't. Both inhabit the liminal zone, and both thrive there.

### 2. Endurance Through Self-Renewal
Madrone's bark continuously peels, revealing new photosynthetic tissue beneath. As outer layers shed, the tree renews itself. During MA-9, the spacecraft's automated systems peeled away one by one -- attitude control, gyros, inverters, CO2 scrubbers approaching saturation. Beneath each shed layer, Cooper's manual capabilities remained, functioning, photosynthetic. The tree reveals its green bark. The mission revealed its pilot. Both endure by shedding what fails and functioning with what remains.

### 3. The Singular Strategy
Pacific Madrone is the only native broadleaf evergreen tree in the PNW -- a unique ecological strategy in a landscape dominated by conifers. Cooper's MA-9 was the only full-day endurance mission in the Mercury program -- a unique mission profile in a program that had been defined by short-duration orbital flights. Both are singular in their context. Both prove that the singular strategy works precisely because it is different from everything around it.

### 4. Deep Roots Surviving Fire
Madrone survives fire by resprouting from its root crown -- the above-ground tree can be completely destroyed and the roots will regenerate it. Cooper's automatic systems "burned out" during MA-9, but his deep roots -- years of training, simulator hours, emergency procedures drilled into muscle memory -- survived the systems fire and regenerated the mission's capability through manual control. The root system is the skill base. The resprout is the manual reentry.

### 5. Feeding the Migrants
Madrone berries are a critical food source for migrating birds, including the Western Tanager (our degree 24 bird). The tree at the edge feeds the travelers passing through. Cooper's thirty-four-hour endurance data fed the Gemini and Apollo programs that would pass through the edge Cooper had defined -- the biomedical data, the systems endurance data, the proof that a pilot could sleep in space and hand-fly a reentry. Faith 7's data was the berry crop that fed the migrating programs.

### 6. Faith at the Margin
Madrone grows where conditions are harshest -- thin soil, drought, exposure, wind. It persists through faith in its own adaptations: deep roots, thick leaves, photosynthetic bark, the ability to resprout. Cooper named his capsule Faith 7 and flew it for thirty-four hours into progressive systems failure, persisting through faith in his own adaptations: training, skill, composure, the ability to fly by hand when the automation was gone. Both the tree and the pilot put faith not in the environment being kind but in themselves being prepared.

## The Madrone in Human Culture

Pacific Madrone has been part of the human landscape of the Pacific Northwest for millennia. Coast Salish peoples used the berries as food -- not a primary food source (the berries are mealy and astringent when raw) but a supplementary one, eaten fresh, dried for winter storage, or cooked into porridge. The bark was used medicinally: infusions of the bark and leaves were prepared as treatments for stomach ailments, colds, and skin conditions. The wood, dense and hard, was used for tools and utensils. The tree was not the most important species in the Coast Salish relationship with the forest -- that role belonged to western red cedar, the tree of canoes, longhouses, and clothing -- but Madrone was known, used, and respected.

European settlers regarded Madrone differently. The wood burns hot but splits poorly, making it a frustrating firewood species. The lumber is dense and beautiful -- reddish-brown with a fine grain -- but the tree's tendency to grow crooked, branch irregularly, and produce internal stresses during drying made it unsuitable for commercial timber. Madrone was the tree that settlers could not use the way they used Douglas-fir or cedar, and so they largely ignored it. When land was cleared for agriculture or development, Madrone was removed along with everything else; but when land was left alone, Madrone persisted on the margins where other trees had trouble establishing.

In the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, Pacific Madrone has become an iconic species -- the tree that visual artists, photographers, and landscape architects associate with the Puget Sound aesthetic. The terracotta bark against gray water. The evergreen crown against winter sky. The leaning form on the bluff edge. Madrone has become shorthand for "Pacific Northwest" in a way that complements but does not compete with the coniferous identity. The conifers are the forest. Madrone is the edge of the forest. Both define the place.

The tree faces conservation challenges. Pacific Madrone is susceptible to a suite of fungal diseases, particularly a leaf blight caused by Nattrassia mangiferae and a canker disease that can girdle and kill branches. Climate change is altering the moisture regime that Madrone depends on -- the species requires dry summers (which it tolerates through deep roots) but also cool, moist winters (which provide the water that recharges the bedrock aquifers). As summers grow hotter and drier and winters become less reliably wet, Madrone populations in the southern part of the range are experiencing increased stress and mortality. In the Puget Sound region, Madrone remains relatively common, but foresters and ecologists note declining health in many populations.

The conservation parallel to Mercury is not forced. Mercury was a program that operated at the edge of its capability, pushing spacecraft and astronaut to limits that revealed what worked and what didn't. Madrone is a species that lives at the edge of its ecological capability, surviving conditions that reveal its strengths (drought tolerance, fire resilience, evergreen photosynthesis) and its vulnerabilities (disease susceptibility, sensitivity to climate change). Both the program and the tree teach the same lesson: the edge is where you learn the most, but it is also where you are most vulnerable.

## Taxonomy Note

The species epithet *menziesii* honors Archibald Menzies (1754--1842), the same Scottish surgeon-naturalist who is honored in the name of Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*). Menzies collected the first botanical specimens of Pacific Madrone during the Vancouver Expedition (1791--1795), observing the tree on the shores of Puget Sound. Frederick Pursh formally described the species in 1813, based on Menzies' collections.

The common name Madrone (also spelled Madrona, Madrono) derives from the Spanish *madroño*, referring to the closely related Mediterranean strawberry tree (*Arbutus unedo*). The genus *Arbutus* is placed in the family Ericaceae -- the heath family, which includes rhododendrons, blueberries, huckleberries, manzanita, and salal. Pacific Madrone is, taxonomically, the largest member of the heath family in the Pacific Northwest -- a tree-sized heath in a family of shrubs.

The taxonomic placement in Ericaceae connects Madrone to an unexpected set of relatives. Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*), the organism paired with mission 1.3 (Pioneer 2), is also in Ericaceae. Madrone and salal are family -- the tree at the edge and the understory shrub share an ancestor and a biochemistry (both produce similar secondary metabolites, both form ericoid or arbutoid mycorrhizal associations). The NASA mission series' organism pairings, chosen independently for each mission's character, occasionally reveal unexpected taxonomic connections. Madrone and salal are one such connection: the edge tree and the understory shrub, both Ericaceae, both persistent, both adapted to the specific challenges of their respective niches.

Cooper's position in the Mercury program mirrors this taxonomic connection. He was related to the other Mercury astronauts the way Madrone is related to salal -- same family, same basic structure, adapted to a completely different niche. Shepard, Glenn, Carpenter, Schirra, Grissom, Cooper: all Mercury Seven, all trained on the same spacecraft, all sharing the same fundamental capability. But Cooper's mission occupied a niche that none of the others did. The family is the same. The adaptation is singular.
