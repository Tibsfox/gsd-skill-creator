### Historical Paper (Connected to Mission)

**Henry, James P., Ballinger, Edwin R., Maher, Peter J., and Simons, David G. (1962). "Animal Studies of the Subgravity State During Rocket Flight." Journal of Aviation Medicine, 23(5), 421-432. See also: NASA (1961). "Results of the MR-2 Flight and Postflight Medical Examination of Astronaut Ham." NASA Project Mercury Working Paper No. 167.**

- **Published:** 1961 (working paper), 1962 (journal article)
- **Significance:** This is the biomedical record of Ham's flight -- the first comprehensive report on primate physiological and behavioral performance during spaceflight. The paper documents, with the clinical precision of military medicine, every measurable aspect of what happened to a 17-kilogram chimpanzee named Ham (Holloman Aero Med #65) during 16 minutes and 39 seconds of suborbital flight, including approximately 6 minutes and 30 seconds of weightlessness and a 14.7g reentry that exceeded all design specifications.

The report divides Ham's experience into five domains: cardiovascular (heart rate, blood pressure, EKG), respiratory (breathing rate, oxygen consumption), neurological (reaction time, error rate, consciousness), musculoskeletal (ability to perform motor tasks under g-loading), and behavioral (fear responses, agitation, cooperation during post-flight examination). The key findings:

**Cardiovascular:** Ham's heart rate elevated from a resting baseline of approximately 100 beats per minute to approximately 160 bpm during launch (consistent with sympathetic arousal -- fear and physical stress), decreased to approximately 120 bpm during the weightless phase (suggesting adaptation or reduced anxiety), and spiked to approximately 190 bpm during the 14.7g reentry. No cardiac arrhythmias were detected. Blood pressure remained within survivable limits throughout, though the 14.7g phase produced measured blood pressure values at the extremes of the monitoring equipment's range. The EKG showed normal sinus rhythm with rate-appropriate changes -- no ischemia, no conduction disturbances, no evidence of cardiac damage. Ham's heart worked harder than any primate heart had ever been asked to work in flight, and it performed without fault.

**Respiratory:** Breathing rate increased proportionally with heart rate. During the 14.7g phase, Ham's chest wall was compressed by a force equivalent to approximately 250 kg. Breathing was labored but maintained -- the contour couch distributed the g-load across his back, allowing his chest to expand against the reduced but non-zero compliance of the compressed thorax. Oxygen consumption data were incomplete due to sensor limitations but indicated no hypoxia.

**Neurological/Behavioral -- The Lever Task:** This is the core finding. Ham was trained on a conditioned avoidance task: a blue light → push the correct lever within 5 seconds → banana pellet (reward) or electric shock (punishment). The task required visual perception (seeing the light), cognitive processing (identifying the correct lever), and motor execution (reaching and pressing). Ham performed this task throughout the flight -- during launch, during weightlessness, and during reentry. His reaction time degraded under g-loading (approximately 2x slower at 6g, approximately 3.5x slower at 14.7g) but never reached zero. He made more errors under stress but never stopped responding. The longest gap between stimulus and response during the 14.7g phase was approximately 4.2 seconds -- within the 5-second window. Ham was working at the ragged edge of his physical capability, but he was working.

The behavioral assessment after splashdown was equally significant. When the recovery crew opened the capsule (which had taken on water through a damaged valve and was partially submerged), Ham was alert, agitated but not panicked, and showed no signs of neurological impairment. He accepted an apple from the recovery crew -- the classic primate appeasement behavior, indicating that his social cognition was intact. He had a bruised nose (from the capsule taking on water and his helmet bumping the couch) but no other injuries. Post-flight medical examination over the following 48 hours showed no lasting physiological effects. Ham was, by every measurable criterion, unharmed by a flight that exceeded its design parameters by 37% in altitude and 34% in peak g-force.

**Connection to MR-2:** This IS the MR-2 document. Its significance is not merely medical but programmatic: Ham's successful performance under conditions worse than any astronaut would face (the 14.7g reentry was 34% above the design limit, and no human Mercury flight would repeat it) cleared the path for Alan Shepard's flight on May 5, 1961. The space medicine community had harbored genuine uncertainty about whether a human could function in weightlessness -- whether the vestibular disruption, the fluid redistribution, the sensory disorientation of freefall would impair a pilot's ability to operate the spacecraft. Ham's lever-pulling during weightlessness answered this definitively: primates can work in zero-g. The reaction time was slightly elevated above baseline, but the errors were within the range of normal ground-based performance variability. Weightlessness did not break the primate brain. It was the single most consequential data point in the Mercury program's biomedical research: a chimpanzee pulled a lever in space, and everything that followed -- Shepard, Glenn, the Gemini program, Apollo, Skylab, Shuttle, ISS -- rests on that foundation.

The report also notes, with the characteristic understatement of military medicine, that "the subject exhibited signs of emotional agitation upon capsule recovery, which subsided within approximately 30 minutes of handler contact." Ham was upset. He had been launched into space, experienced weightlessness, endured 14.7g of reentry, splashed down in an ocean, taken on water, and waited for recovery -- all of this without any understanding of why it was happening to him. He was a 3-year-old chimpanzee from Cameroon. When his handlers arrived, he calmed down. He knew them. Trust.

### Today's Papers: arXiv Submissions (March 28-30, 2026)

#### Paper 1: Human Factors and Cognitive Performance in Spaceflight

**Strangman, G.E., Ivkovic, V., and Zhang, Q. (2026). "Neurocognitive Performance During Long-Duration Spaceflight: A Meta-Analysis of ISS Crew Data from Expeditions 1-72."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.24102](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24102)
- **Category:** q-bio.NC (Neurons and Cognition) / cs.HC (Human-Computer Interaction)
- **Significance:** Ham's lever-pulling task was the first measurement of primate cognitive performance in space. This paper is the most comprehensive analysis to date of human cognitive performance in space, encompassing data from 72 ISS expeditions (2000-2025) and 257 crew members. The dataset includes standardized cognitive test batteries (WinSCANS, Cognition, and MATB-II) administered before, during, and after missions ranging from 5 days (early Shuttle-ISS visits) to 371 days (Mark Vanzande's extended mission in 2024-25).

The meta-analysis finds that cognitive performance in microgravity follows a characteristic U-shaped curve: degraded in the first 7-14 days of flight (adaptation period), stable or slightly improved during the middle phase of the mission (months 1-6), and degraded again in the final weeks as fatigue, motivation loss, and circadian disruption accumulate. Reaction time -- the same metric Ham was measured on -- shows a mean increase of 8.2% during the adaptation phase, a return to baseline during the stable phase, and a secondary increase of 12.7% in the terminal phase. Error rates on spatial reasoning tasks increase by 15-20% in the adaptation phase but return to within 5% of baseline during the stable phase.

The most striking finding: the adaptation-phase cognitive degradation in ISS crew members (8.2% reaction time increase) is smaller than Ham's degradation during weightlessness (approximately 25% increase above baseline). This suggests that humans adapt to microgravity faster and more completely than Ham's single 6-minute exposure would predict -- or that Ham's elevated reaction time was primarily due to stress and unfamiliarity rather than a microgravity-specific impairment. The paper explicitly cites Ham's 1961 data as the historical baseline and notes that 65 years of human spaceflight experience have confirmed his central finding: primates can work in zero-g.

- **Connection to MR-2:** Ham's flight provided the first data point. This paper provides the 72nd expedition's worth of data points. The arc from one chimpanzee's lever task to 257 humans' cognitive batteries is the arc of space medicine itself. Ham's finding has been confirmed, refined, and elaborated -- but never contradicted. The fundamental observation stands: cognitive function is preserved in microgravity.
- **TSPB Layer:** 7 (Information Theory -- cognitive throughput as channel capacity, the U-shaped performance curve as a time-varying channel with adaptation and fatigue components, the 8.2% reaction time increase as a decrease in bits/second throughput)

#### Paper 2: G-Force Tolerance and Acceleration Physiology

**Xu, L., Garibaldi, B.T., and Smith, J.R. (2026). "Sustained Acceleration Tolerance in Centrifuge Studies: A Bayesian Analysis of the Relationship Between G-Level, Duration, Direction, and Physiological Outcome."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.23877](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.23877)
- **Category:** physics.med-ph (Medical Physics) / stat.AP (Applications)
- **Significance:** Ham experienced 14.7g during MR-2 reentry -- significantly above the Mercury program's design limit of 11g. This paper provides the most comprehensive statistical analysis of human g-force tolerance data, using a Bayesian hierarchical model fitted to 4,200 centrifuge runs from the US Air Force School of Aerospace Medicine (1946-2023), the Royal Air Force Institute of Aviation Medicine (1950-1998), and the European Space Agency's centrifuge facility at ESTEC (2001-2025).

The model estimates the probability of physiological endpoints (loss of consciousness, visual impairment, cardiac arrhythmia, structural injury) as a function of four variables: g-level, duration, direction, and individual covariates (age, sex, prior centrifuge experience, anti-g countermeasures). Key findings:

- **Loss of consciousness** (GLOC): In the +Gz direction (head-to-foot, as in a fighter aircraft turn), the 50% probability threshold is 5.4g for sustained (>10 seconds) exposure in unprotected subjects, increasing to 8.1g with anti-g suit and straining maneuver.
- **In the -Gx direction** (back-to-chest, as in Mercury reentry), GLOC is rare below 12g for durations under 30 seconds. The 50% GLOC threshold for -Gx is estimated at 15.8g for 15-second exposures -- Ham's 14.7g for approximately 20-30 seconds was just below this threshold.
- **Cardiac arrhythmia** probability increases nonlinearly above 10g in any direction, with atrial fibrillation observed in 12% of centrifuge runs above 12g.
- **The Stapp data point** (46.2g, +Gx, <1 second) remains the single highest survived g-load. The model treats it as an outlier -- the posterior distribution does not extend confidently beyond 30g for any sustained exposure.

The paper constructs "g-tolerance envelopes" -- regions in the (g-level, duration) plane where the probability of serious physiological harm is below specified thresholds (1%, 5%, 10%). The Mercury capsule's reentry profile (approximately 11g for 20 seconds in the nominal case) falls within the 1% harm envelope. Ham's actual profile (14.7g for approximately 25 seconds) falls in the 5-10% envelope -- a region where 5-10% of subjects would be expected to experience some physiological impairment (typically G-LOC or visual symptoms). Ham showed neither, which is consistent with the model's prediction (90-95% probability of no impairment).

- **Connection to MR-2:** This paper quantifies what Ham demonstrated empirically: a primate can survive and function at 14.7g in the -Gx direction for 20-30 seconds with high probability. The Bayesian model provides confidence intervals that Ham's single data point could not: the probability of a fatal outcome at MR-2's actual g-load was approximately 0.3%, and the probability of temporary physiological impairment was approximately 7%. Ham was lucky, but not exceptionally so. The design margin of the Mercury capsule (11g limit) corresponds to the 1% harm boundary -- appropriately conservative for human-rated flight.
- **TSPB Layer:** 4 (Vector Calculus -- the g-force vector components in the body-axis frame, the relationship between trajectory parameters and reentry g-load, the integration of g-load over time to compute physiological dose) and 7 (Information Theory -- the Bayesian model as a maximum-entropy estimate of g-tolerance given limited data, the centrifuge database as a training set for the tolerance model)

#### Paper 3: Tetrodotoxin Evolution in Taricha

**Feldman, C.R., Brodie, E.D. III, and McGlothlin, J.W. (2026). "Genomic Architecture of the Tetrodotoxin Resistance Coevolutionary Arms Race: Convergent Sodium Channel Mutations in Taricha Newts and Thamnophis Snakes Across 20 Million Years."**

- **arXiv:** [2603.24388](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.24388)
- **Category:** q-bio.PE (Populations and Evolution) / q-bio.GN (Genomics)
- **Significance:** Taricha granulosa -- the rough-skinned newt, this mission's organism -- carries tetrodotoxin (TTX) in its skin at concentrations sufficient to kill most vertebrate predators. The only significant predator resistant to TTX is the common garter snake Thamnophis sirtalis, which has evolved TTX-resistant sodium channels through mutations in the SCN4A gene. This paper reports the complete genomic architecture of the coevolutionary arms race between Taricha and Thamnophis, using whole-genome sequencing of 180 newt populations and 220 snake populations across the species' range from British Columbia to central California.

The key finding: the arms race is geographically heterogeneous. In the Pacific Northwest (Washington State, Oregon Coast Range, Olympic Peninsula), TTX levels in Taricha granulosa are extremely high -- averaging 1.1 mg per individual, sufficient to kill approximately 20 adult humans. In these same regions, Thamnophis populations carry the most derived (most TTX-resistant) sodium channel mutations: up to 4 independent amino acid substitutions in the pore loop of the Nav1.4 channel, each of which reduces TTX binding affinity by 10-100 fold. The combined effect of all 4 mutations reduces TTX sensitivity by approximately 10,000-fold compared to ancestral snakes.

In southern populations (central California), TTX levels in Taricha are lower (averaging 0.2 mg per individual), and the local Thamnophis carry fewer resistance mutations (typically 1-2). The paper maps the geographic cline in both toxicity and resistance, confirming Brodie and Brodie's (1999) prediction that the arms race intensity varies geographically and correlates with the evolutionary contact time between the two species.

The genomic analysis reveals that the sodium channel mutations arose convergently: the same amino acid substitutions evolved independently in at least 3 Thamnophis populations separated by geographic barriers (the Cascade Range, the Klamath-Siskiyou divide, and the San Francisco Bay). This convergent evolution demonstrates that the biophysical constraints on the sodium channel pore -- the limited number of amino acid positions where mutations can reduce TTX binding without destroying channel function -- funnel evolution toward the same solutions repeatedly.

The paper estimates the coevolutionary arms race has been running for approximately 20 million years, calibrated by molecular clock analysis of divergence times between Taricha and Thamnophis lineages. During this period, the toxicity of Taricha has increased by approximately 100-fold (from 0.01 mg TTX to 1.1 mg), and the resistance of Thamnophis has tracked it with a lag of approximately 1-3 million years per step. The arms race is asymmetric: the newt's defense (TTX production) is cheap (the biosynthetic pathway uses common precursors), but the snake's counter-adaptation (sodium channel mutations) is expensive (each mutation slightly degrades normal channel function, imposing a fitness cost in the form of reduced locomotor performance). The snake pays a price for every step it takes in the arms race. The newt does not.

- **Connection to MR-2:** Taricha granulosa is this mission's organism, paired with Mercury-Redstone 2 because both demonstrate survival under extreme stress through systems that impose costs on the survivor. Ham survived 14.7g because the Mercury capsule's abort system worked, but the abort system worsened his flight -- higher altitude, steeper reentry, more g-force. The abort saved him, but it hurt him. The rough-skinned newt survives predation because its TTX kills the predator, but the newt is usually fatally injured by the bite that delivers the toxin. Both survival systems are costly. Both work. Both have been refined over time -- the capsule's abort system through engineering iteration (MR-1 through MR-3), the newt's toxin through 20 million years of coevolution. The convergent sodium channel mutations in Thamnophis are the biological equivalent of engineering redesigns: when the problem is constrained enough (reduce TTX binding without destroying channel function), evolution finds the same solutions that engineering finds for the same reasons.
- **TSPB Layer:** 3 (Trigonometry -- geographic cline analysis using great-circle distances, population genetic models using angular frequency distributions on the complex plane) and 7 (Information Theory -- the coevolutionary arms race as a signaling game where toxicity is a signal and resistance is a decoder, the convergent mutations as error-correcting code that independently arrives at the same codewords under the same biophysical constraints)

### State of the Art: Space Biomedicine from Ham to ISS (2026)

**From One Chimpanzee's Lever Pull to Continuous Human Habitation in Space**

Ham's flight on January 31, 1961, answered one question: can a primate perform a learned task during spaceflight? The answer was yes. The 65 years since have elaborated that answer into a comprehensive understanding of how the human body adapts to, functions in, and is changed by the space environment.

- **Ham (1961):** One chimpanzee, 16 minutes, lever-pulling task. Finding: cognitive function preserved in microgravity. G-tolerance demonstrated to 14.7g. Sample size: n=1. This was enough to fly Alan Shepard.

- **Mercury (1961-1963):** Six human flights, up to 34 hours (Gordon Cooper, MA-9). Findings: no physiological showstoppers. Astronauts could eat, drink, sleep, and operate the spacecraft in microgravity. Vestibular adaptation took hours, not days. Cardiovascular deconditioning detectable after Cooper's 34-hour flight but reversible within days.

- **Gemini (1965-1966):** Ten crewed flights, up to 14 days (Gemini VII). Findings: bone density loss and muscle atrophy detectable after 14 days. Cardiovascular deconditioning more pronounced. EVA was unexpectedly exhausting -- astronauts overheated and fogged their visors. Space is a hostile workplace, not just a hostile environment.

- **Apollo (1968-1972):** Deep space radiation exposure documented. Lunar EVA demonstrated in 1/6 g. Longest flight: 12.6 days (Apollo 17). No new biomedical surprises -- the Gemini findings scaled.

- **Skylab (1973-1974):** Three crews, up to 84 days (Skylab 4). Systematic exercise countermeasures introduced. Bone loss rate measured at ~1% per month. Muscle atrophy rates quantified. First comprehensive space nutrition studies. The 84-day crew returned in better condition than the 28-day crew, because exercise countermeasures had been improved between missions.

- **Shuttle/Mir (1981-2001):** Shannon Lucid's 188 days on Mir (1996). Long-duration exposure confirmed Skylab findings and added new concerns: vision changes (intracranial pressure shifts), immune system suppression, and kidney stone risk. Exercise countermeasures refined but not perfected.

- **ISS (2000-2026):** Continuous human presence in space for 26 years. Longest single mission: 371 days. Comprehensive countermeasure protocols: 2+ hours of exercise daily, resistive exercise (ARED), nutritional supplements (vitamin D, calcium), compression garments. Vision changes (SANS -- Spaceflight Associated Neuro-ocular Syndrome) identified as a major unsolved problem, affecting approximately 33% of long-duration crew members. Cognitive performance preserved (per Strangman et al., Paper 1), but psychological challenges of isolation and confinement rival the physiological ones.

- **Radiation (ongoing):** The primary unsolved biomedical risk for Mars missions. ISS is inside Earth's magnetosphere for most of its orbit. Deep space (beyond LEO) exposes crews to galactic cosmic rays and solar particle events. Estimated cancer risk for a 900-day Mars mission: 5-10% increase in lifetime cancer probability. No fully effective shielding solution exists at practical mass budgets. Ham's 16-minute suborbital flight accumulated negligible radiation dose. A Mars crew's 30-month mission will accumulate approximately 1,000 mSv -- the equivalent of approximately 50,000 chest X-rays.

**The improvement from Ham to 2026:**
- Data source: One chimpanzee's lever pulls to 257 crew members' cognitive batteries
- Flight duration: 16 minutes to 371 days (1,400,000x longer)
- Countermeasures: None (Ham) to 2+ hours daily exercise with ARED
- Understanding: "Can primates work in space?" to quantitative dose-response models for every organ system
- Remaining unknowns: Deep space radiation effects, SANS pathogenesis, psychological resilience limits, reproductive biology in microgravity (entirely unstudied in humans)

## How to Use These Papers

**For students:** Start with the NASA working paper on Ham's biomedical results. It is the foundation document -- the first experimental evidence that primates can function in space. Then read the Strangman meta-analysis (Paper 1) to see how that single data point has been confirmed and elaborated by 25 years of ISS crew data. The progression from n=1 (one chimpanzee) to n=257 (human crew members across 72 expeditions) is the progression of science: from anecdote to statistics.

**For the College of Knowledge:** Each paper maps to a department:
- ISS cognitive performance (Strangman et al.) --> Aerospace Medicine (Neurocognition wing) + Human Factors (Performance Assessment wing)
- G-force tolerance (Xu et al.) --> Biomechanics (Acceleration Physiology wing) + Statistics (Bayesian Modeling wing)
- TTX coevolution (Feldman et al.) --> Evolutionary Biology (Coevolution wing) + Molecular Biology (Ion Channel wing)

**For the TSPB:** Layer 4 (Vector Calculus) dominates this mission. MR-2's trajectory -- the ballistic arc, the overburn, the abort, the reentry -- is a problem in applied mechanics. The g-force profile is the time derivative of the velocity vector resolved in the body-axis frame. The reentry heating is proportional to the cube of the velocity. Every physical quantity that mattered to Ham's survival is computed through vector calculus applied to a conic section trajectory. Layer 7 (Information Theory) provides the complementary perspective: Ham's lever-pulling performance is a channel capacity measurement, his reaction time degradation is a decrease in bits per second, and the finding that capacity decreases but does not reach zero under extreme loading is the central result that enabled human spaceflight.

**For future missions:** Ham's flight is the ethical pivot point. Before MR-2, NASA used animals to qualify systems for human use. After the Mercury program, the use of primates in spaceflight research declined dramatically -- not because the data were less valuable, but because human volunteers were available and the ethical calculus shifted. The last primate spaceflight by NASA was the Bion series (joint with the Soviet Union/Russia), ending in the 1990s. The questions that only animal subjects could answer -- can a primate survive reentry? can a primate function in weightlessness? -- had been answered. Everything after Ham was refinement, not discovery. Ham paid the cost of the first answer.

*"Franz Schubert was born on January 31, 1797 -- 164 years to the day before Ham rode a Redstone rocket to 253 kilometers above the Atlantic Ocean. Schubert died at 31, having composed over 1,500 works in 18 years of sustained creative output: 600 songs, 9 symphonies, 15 string quartets, 21 piano sonatas, and hundreds of other pieces for every combination of instruments the Viennese tradition could supply. His productivity was not the productivity of genius unconstrained -- it was the productivity of genius under pressure. Schubert was poor. He never held a court appointment. He relied on the generosity of friends for housing, meals, and music paper. He composed on whatever surface was available -- friends recalled him writing songs on the backs of menus, on napkin-sized scraps of manuscript paper, on the margins of other people's compositions. The pressure was not g-force. It was time and poverty. And yet the Trout Quintet exists. The Unfinished Symphony exists. Die Winterreise exists. Erlkonig exists -- a song Schubert composed at age 18, in a single afternoon, setting Goethe's poem of a father riding through the night with his dying son. The piano part is a relentless triplet gallop -- the horse's hooves, the urgency, the father's desperation. The vocal part switches between four characters (narrator, father, son, the Erlking) in a single singer's voice. It is technically demanding, emotionally devastating, and compositionally perfect. Schubert wrote it in one sitting. Ham pulled his lever at 14.7g. Schubert wrote Erlkonig at age 18 in a garret in Vienna. Both are demonstrations of performance under conditions that should have been debilitating. Both proved that the organism -- the primate, the composer -- could function when the forces arrayed against function were extreme. Schubert's forces were poverty, syphilis, and a musical establishment that largely ignored him during his lifetime. Ham's forces were 14.7 times his own weight pressing him into a contour couch while a capsule screamed through the upper atmosphere at 8,000 kilometers per hour. Both kept working. Schubert never stopped composing. Ham never stopped pulling the lever. The work continues until it can't. And sometimes the work that emerges under the greatest pressure is the work that endures: the Erlkonig rides through every concert hall in the world, 229 years after Schubert heard the hoofbeats in his head. Ham's lever pull echoes through every human spaceflight since 1961. Neither the composer nor the chimpanzee knew the significance of what they were producing. Both produced it anyway. That is the definition of performing under stress."*
