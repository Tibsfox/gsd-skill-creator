## Department Deposits

### Aerospace Medicine (Primary)

**Wing:** Space Physiology, Acceleration Tolerance, Biomedical Qualification
**Concept:** How to determine whether a biological organism can survive and function in an environment that has never been tested on that organism -- the problem of qualifying a system for a domain where no prior data exists

**Deposit:** Before Mercury-Redstone 2, no one knew whether a primate could function during spaceflight. This was not a rhetorical question. The space medicine community in 1960 harbored genuine, scientifically grounded concerns about the effects of weightlessness on cognitive function, motor control, and cardiovascular stability. The concerns were based on what was known about vestibular physiology: the semicircular canals and otolith organs in the inner ear rely on gravity as a reference signal. Remove gravity, and the vestibular system sends no signal -- or worse, sends a contradictory signal. The brain interprets this as either freefall (appropriate) or spatial disorientation (dangerous). Which interpretation would dominate? Nobody knew.

The centrifuge work at Johnsville (the Navy's Aviation Medical Acceleration Laboratory in Warminster, Pennsylvania) had established g-force tolerance limits for humans in various directions and durations. But centrifuge data had a fundamental limitation: the centrifuge produces centripetal acceleration, not linear acceleration. A subject in a centrifuge experiences a rotational component (Coriolis effect, semicircular canal stimulation) that does not occur in a rocket. And the centrifuge cannot produce weightlessness -- only hypergravity. The zero-g question was unanswerable on the ground except through brief parabolic flights in aircraft (KC-135 "vomit comet"), which produced only 20-30 seconds of weightlessness per parabola -- far shorter than the 6+ minutes of a suborbital flight.

Ham was the answer. Not because he was expendable (the Air Force veterinary team that trained him was meticulous about his care and health), but because he was the only ethical path to the data. A human could not be the first subject because the risk was unknown. An animal model that could perform a cognitive task was the minimum necessary data source. Chimpanzees were chosen because they are the closest non-human model for human physiology, their hands can operate levers and switches similar to those in the Mercury capsule, and they can be trained through operant conditioning to perform standardized tasks whose performance can be measured objectively.

The conditioned avoidance task -- blue light, lever, banana pellet or electric shock -- was not arbitrary. It was designed to test three specific capabilities:
1. **Visual perception:** Can the subject see and identify the stimulus (blue light) under g-loading and in weightlessness?
2. **Cognitive processing:** Can the subject recall the trained response (push the correct lever) under stress?
3. **Motor execution:** Can the subject physically perform the action (reach, grasp, push) under the forces of flight?

All three capabilities are required for a pilot to operate a spacecraft. If any one fails, the pilot cannot fly. Ham's performance demonstrated that all three were preserved throughout a flight profile that exceeded design specifications. The data were sufficient: Alan Shepard flew on May 5, 1961.

The Common Murre (Uria aalge, degree 17) faces an analogous qualification problem: murre chicks leave their nesting cliff at approximately 20-25 days of age, leaping from ledges 30-100 meters above the sea. The chick cannot fly -- its wings are too small, its flight feathers undeveloped. It falls, fluttering ineffectively, and splashes into the ocean below. The father accompanies it, calling from the water. The chick must survive the impact, orient itself in the water, and swim to join the father -- all tasks it has never performed before, in an environment (the open ocean) it has never experienced. There is no qualification flight. There is no test. The chick's first ocean swim is its MR-2: an untested organism performing in an untested environment, with survival as the only passing grade. Approximately 85% of murre chicks survive the leap. The 15% that do not are the cost of biological qualification -- the same cost Ham bore, reduced from an individual to a population statistic by 200 million years of murre evolution refining the leap.

### Biomechanics (Primary)

**Wing:** G-Force Loading, Musculoskeletal Response, Contour Couch Design
**Concept:** How to distribute extreme mechanical loads across a biological body so that no single tissue exceeds its structural limit -- the engineering of survivable deceleration

**Deposit:** The contour couch was the single most important piece of hardware in the Mercury capsule from a biomedical perspective. Designed by the crew systems division at Langley, it was a fiberglass shell custom-molded to each occupant's body -- for Ham, a chimpanzee-shaped couch; for the astronauts, individual molds taken with each man lying in the planned flight attitude. The principle was load distribution: spread the deceleration force across the maximum possible body surface area, so that no point on the body bears a concentration of force sufficient to cause injury.

The physics of the contour couch:

At 14.7g, Ham's effective weight was approximately 250 kg (550 lb). This force was distributed across the couch contact surface of approximately 0.25 m^2 (the area of Ham's back, shoulders, buttocks, and thighs in contact with the couch). The resulting average pressure was:

P = F / A = (250 * 9.807) / 0.25 = 9,807 Pa ≈ 1.4 psi

This is a gentle pressure -- roughly equivalent to lying on a moderately firm mattress under 1g. The contour couch converted a potentially lethal concentrated force into a widely distributed, easily survivable pressure. Without the couch (if Ham had been sitting in a flat chair), the contact area would have been perhaps 0.05 m^2, producing a pressure five times higher and concentrated on the spine and pelvis -- sufficient to cause vertebral compression fractures at 14.7g.

The couch also controlled the direction of the g-force relative to the body. By positioning the occupant supine (lying on their back) with knees raised, the reentry deceleration acted in the -Gx direction (back to chest), which is the most tolerable direction for sustained high-g loads. In this position, the blood column between the heart and the brain is short (the heart and brain are at approximately the same level), reducing the hydrostatic pressure gradient that causes blackout (+Gz) or redout (-Gz). The reentry g-force pushes blood toward the front of the body (toward the chest) rather than toward the head or feet, avoiding the most dangerous cardiovascular effects.

The rough-skinned newt (Taricha granulosa) has no contour couch but faces comparable biomechanical challenges during predator encounters. When a garter snake seizes a newt, the snake's jaws apply compressive force across the newt's body -- distributed along the length of the jaw contact rather than concentrated at a single point. The newt's body is essentially cylindrical, which naturally distributes compressive loads circumferentially, similar to how a pressure vessel distributes internal pressure. The newt's ribs are rudimentary and flexible, acting as distributed struts rather than rigid beams, allowing the body to deform under compression without fracturing. This is the biological equivalent of the contour couch: a structure that converts concentrated force into distributed load.

### Animal Behavior (Primary)

**Wing:** Operant Conditioning, Task Performance Under Stress, Primate Cognition
**Concept:** How to train a non-verbal subject to perform a standardized task, measure the performance objectively, and use the results to qualify a system for human use -- the methodology of biomedical spaceflight qualification

**Deposit:** Ham's training at Holloman Air Force Base was a systematic operant conditioning program that lasted approximately 18 months. The training staff (led by neuroscientist Joseph V. Brady and veterinarian Edward Dittmer) used Skinnerian reinforcement schedules to build a reliable lever-pressing response that could be measured quantitatively during flight.

The training progression:

1. **Habituation (months 1-3):** Ham was acclimated to the flight couch, the restraint harness, the helmet, and the enclosed capsule environment. The goal was to eliminate fear responses to the hardware. A frightened chimpanzee would be unable to perform any cognitive task. Habituation was achieved through gradual exposure: first the couch alone, then the couch with loose restraints, then the couch with full restraints, then the couch in the capsule mockup, then the capsule mockup with the hatch closed.

2. **Lever training (months 3-8):** Ham learned to press a lever when a blue light illuminated. Initially, every correct press produced a banana pellet (continuous reinforcement). As proficiency increased, the reinforcement schedule shifted to variable ratio (not every correct press was rewarded), which produces more robust behavior -- the subject continues pressing even when rewards are intermittent, because it has learned that pressing is generally rewarded.

3. **Discrimination training (months 8-14):** A second lever was introduced. The correct lever was indicated by the position of the blue light (left light = left lever, right light = right lever). Wrong-lever presses and missed responses (no press within 5 seconds) produced a mild electric shock to the sole of the foot. This negative reinforcement sharpened the discrimination: Ham learned not just to press a lever, but to press the correct lever.

4. **Stress habituation (months 14-18):** Ham performed the lever task while subjected to increasing levels of physical stress: vibration (simulating launch vibration), acceleration (centrifuge runs at 3g, 6g, 9g), noise (recorded rocket engine noise at flight-equivalent levels), and confinement (increasing durations in the sealed capsule). At each stress level, reaction time and error rate were measured. The training endpoint was defined as: Ham can perform the discrimination task at baseline proficiency under all expected flight conditions.

The ethological context: chimpanzees are tool users. The lever is a tool. Ham's training exploited an innate behavioral capacity -- tool manipulation -- and channeled it through operant conditioning into a standardized, measurable performance metric. The training was not teaching Ham to do something unnatural; it was teaching him to apply a natural capability (hand-tool manipulation) to a specific context (capsule interior, blue light, lever).

The Common Murre (Uria aalge, degree 17) demonstrates analogous behavioral conditioning through a different mechanism: parental instruction. Murre chicks on the cliff ledge spend 20-25 days watching their parents arrive with fish, depart to sea, navigate wind and waves, and land on the narrow ledge. The chick does not practice these behaviors on the ledge -- there is no room, and the consequences of a failed practice flight are lethal. The chick observes, and when it leaps, it performs behaviors it has never executed but has observed thousands of times. This is observational learning, not operant conditioning -- but the result is the same: a naive organism performing a complex motor task in a novel environment, with performance adequate for survival.

### Herpetology (Secondary)

**Wing:** Taricha granulosa -- Rough-Skinned Newt, Tetrodotoxin, Coevolutionary Arms Race
**Concept:** The rough-skinned newt as a model of chemical defense under extreme pressure -- how an organism survives encounters that should be lethal through a toxin that imposes costs on both parties

**Deposit:** Taricha granulosa is a medium-sized salamander (12-18 cm adult length, 10-20 g mass) found from southeastern Alaska to central California, with its core range in the temperate rainforests of the Pacific Northwest -- precisely the cedar, hemlock, and Douglas fir forests of western Washington, Oregon, and northern California. It is the most common newt in the PNW, encountered on trails, in streams, and on roads during its seasonal migrations between upland forest (summer-fall terrestrial phase) and lowland ponds and lakes (winter-spring aquatic breeding phase).

The newt's most remarkable feature is its skin toxin: tetrodotoxin (TTX), a potent sodium channel blocker that is identical to the toxin found in puffer fish, blue-ringed octopuses, and certain tropical frogs. TTX binds to the pore of voltage-gated sodium channels (Nav1.x family), blocking ion flow and preventing action potential generation. The result: paralysis of skeletal muscle (including the diaphragm -- the victim suffocates), cardiac arrest, and death. A single rough-skinned newt from a high-toxicity PNW population carries enough TTX to kill approximately 20 adult humans or 20,000 mice.

The only significant predator that can survive TTX exposure is the common garter snake Thamnophis sirtalis, which has evolved TTX-resistant sodium channels through mutations in the SCN4A gene. The coevolutionary arms race between Taricha and Thamnophis is one of the best-studied examples of predator-prey coevolution in biology (Brodie & Brodie, 1999; Feldman et al., 2012; Paper 3 in this mission's papers file). The race has been running for approximately 20 million years and is geographically structured: in populations where the two species have the longest evolutionary contact (central Oregon, Willamette Valley), newt toxicity and snake resistance are both at their highest. In populations at the range margins (northern California, British Columbia), both are lower.

The connection to MR-2 is through survival under extreme stress. Ham survived 14.7g because his capsule was engineered to distribute the force and his body was tough enough to tolerate what remained. The rough-skinned newt survives predation by being so toxic that the predator dies before digestion is complete. Both survival strategies are costly: the capsule's abort system worsened Ham's trajectory, and the newt's TTX does not prevent it from being bitten (the toxin takes time to act, and the snake can inflict significant injury before succumbing). Both work well enough. Both are refined versions of a strategy that has been iteratively improved: the capsule through engineering cycles (MR-1 connector failure taught sequence reliability, MR-1A verified the fix, MR-BD provided final confidence), the newt through 20 million years of selection on TTX concentration and sodium channel resistance.

The rough-skinned newt's migration is itself a journey of survival under stress. During fall and spring migrations, newts travel up to 3 km overland between forest and breeding pond, crossing roads, exposed meadows, and streams. Mortality during migration is significant: road mortality alone kills millions of newts annually in the PNW (this is visible to anyone who has driven rural roads in western Oregon or Washington during fall rains). The newt crawls slowly, deliberately, with no defense against vehicles except its irrelevance -- it is too small, too slow, and too ground-level for most drivers to see. Its TTX is useless against a tire. Ham's abort system was useless against the forces it was designed to mitigate -- it saved him from a rocket failure but not from the reentry loads the abort created. Both organisms carry defenses calibrated for specific threats, and both are vulnerable to threats outside their evolutionary or engineering envelope.

### Ethics (Secondary)

**Wing:** Animal Testing in Spaceflight, Informed Consent, the Ethics of Using Non-Consenting Subjects for Human Benefit
**Concept:** The moral calculation of using a chimpanzee to qualify a spacecraft for human use -- the tension between scientific necessity and animal welfare

**Deposit:** Ham did not volunteer. He was captured in Cameroon as an infant (1957), sold to the Rare Bird Farm in Miami, purchased by the United States Air Force, and shipped to Holloman Air Force Base in New Mexico, where he was assigned the designation Holloman Aeromedical #65. He was given the name "Ham" only after the flight -- during training and the flight itself, he was referred to by number, to avoid public attachment to an animal that might not survive.

The ethical framework of the time was straightforward: animal testing was an accepted step in qualifying systems for human use. The Mercury program's biomedical qualification sequence followed the same logic as pharmaceutical drug testing: animal trials precede human trials, with animal results used to establish safety margins for human exposure. The chimpanzee was the closest available model for human physiology: similar cardiovascular system, similar musculoskeletal structure, hands capable of manipulating spacecraft controls, cognitive capacity sufficient for standardized performance tasks.

The modern ethical perspective is more complex. Chimpanzees are now recognized as having rich emotional lives, social bonds, and cognitive capabilities that approach (and in some domains match) those of young human children. The US National Institutes of Health retired its last research chimpanzees in 2015 and declared that the agency would no longer support invasive research on chimpanzees. The European Union banned great ape research in 2010. Japan, New Zealand, and Australia have similar restrictions. In 2026, no space agency uses primates for spaceflight qualification.

The ethical question that Ham's flight poses is not "was it wrong?" but "what was the alternative?" In 1961, the alternative was to fly a human with no primate precedent -- to send Alan Shepard into an environment where no one knew whether his cognitive function would be preserved, relying on centrifuge data and parabolic flight data that could not replicate the full flight profile. The risk to Shepard without Ham's precedent was unquantifiable. With Ham's data, the risk was bounded: reaction time degrades but does not cease, cardiovascular function is maintained, consciousness is preserved. Ham's flight reduced Shepard's risk from unknown to estimated. That reduction had a cost: one chimpanzee's autonomy, one chimpanzee's fear, one chimpanzee's 14.7g reentry without consent.

The ethics module for this mission does not resolve this tension. It presents the facts and asks the student to engage with the question: when the only path to essential knowledge runs through the welfare of a non-consenting subject, what is the right thing to do? The Mercury program's answer was: use the minimum number of animal subjects necessary, provide the best possible care, and transition to human volunteers as soon as the data permit. Ham was the last chimpanzee to fly a Mercury capsule. His data were sufficient.

### Music (Secondary)

**Wing:** Franz Schubert -- Lieder, Symphonies, Chamber Music, Performance Under Pressure
**Concept:** Schubert's extraordinary creative productivity under conditions of poverty, illness, and social marginality -- a human parallel to Ham's performance under extreme physical stress

**Deposit:** Franz Peter Schubert was born on January 31, 1797, in Himmelpfortgrund, a suburb of Vienna. He died on November 19, 1828, at age 31, having produced over 1,500 compositions in approximately 18 years of active work. His output is staggering not merely in quantity but in quality: the song cycles Winterreise and Die schone Mullerin are foundational works of the German Lied tradition, the "Unfinished" Symphony (No. 8) and "Great" Symphony (No. 9) are cornerstones of the orchestral repertoire, and the "Trout" Quintet and "Death and the Maiden" Quartet are among the most performed chamber works in existence.

Schubert composed under conditions that would have been debilitating for most creative workers. He was chronically poor, never achieving a salaried position as a court musician or kapellmeister. He relied on the Schubertiad -- informal gatherings of friends in Viennese salons where his music was performed and appreciated -- for both emotional support and financial survival. His friends collected subscriptions to publish his songs, arranged concert opportunities, and provided housing when Schubert could not afford his own. He contracted syphilis in 1822, which produced recurring episodes of illness (rashes, fatigue, joint pain) that interrupted his work and contributed to his early death.

The connection to MR-2 is through performance under stress. Schubert's creative output was not diminished by his circumstances -- it was, if anything, intensified. The works composed during his final years (1827-1828), when syphilis was progressing and his health was failing, include some of his greatest achievements: the Winterreise cycle, the C major Quintet, the last three piano sonatas, and the Symphony No. 9 "Great." These works are marked by emotional depth, formal innovation, and technical mastery that equal or exceed anything he wrote in better health. Schubert's channel capacity -- his creative throughput in bits per second, if such a measure could be applied to composition -- did not decrease as his body deteriorated. It may have increased. The pressure of mortality compressed his output into a concentrated burst of genius.

Ham pulled his lever at 14.7g. Schubert wrote the Erlkonig at 18, the "Unfinished" Symphony at 25, and the Winterreise at 30, dying the following year. Both performed their essential task -- the task they were uniquely qualified for -- under conditions that should have prevented performance. Both produced data that others built upon: Ham's lever pull enabled human spaceflight; Schubert's Lieder enabled the entire tradition of art song that followed, from Schumann through Brahms through Wolf through Strauss through Britten. Both left a legacy disproportionate to the brevity of their contribution. Ham flew for 16 minutes. Schubert composed for 18 years. Both changed what came after them.

## TRY Sessions

### TRY 1: G-Force Simulator -- Spinning Chair Reaction Time Test

**Duration:** 2-4 hours
**Difficulty:** Beginner-Intermediate
**Department:** Aerospace Medicine / Biomechanics
**What You Need:** A swivel chair (office chair or bar stool that rotates freely), a smartphone or laptop with a reaction time test website (humanbenchmark.com or similar), a helper to spin the chair, a notebook for recording data, and optionally a stopwatch. Total cost: $0 (uses existing items).

**What You'll Learn:**
How physical stress degrades cognitive performance -- specifically, how vestibular disruption (dizziness from spinning) affects reaction time, similar to how g-force loading affected Ham's reaction time during MR-2. You will measure your baseline reaction time, then measure it while spinning at various rates, and plot the degradation curve. This is a simplified version of the centrifuge studies that preceded Ham's flight.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] No vestibular disorders (do not perform this if you get severely motion sick)
- [ ] Access to a smooth-rotating swivel chair
- [ ] A reaction time test on phone or laptop (humanbenchmark.com is free)
- [ ] A helper to spin the chair at a controlled rate

**The Exercise:**

```
G-FORCE SIMULATOR: SPINNING CHAIR REACTION TIME TEST

SAFETY:
  - Stop immediately if you feel nauseous or disoriented
  - Do not spin faster than one revolution per 2 seconds
  - Have your helper ready to catch you if you lose balance
  - Perform on a flat, clear floor away from furniture edges
  - Stay seated throughout -- do not stand while dizzy
  - Wait at least 2 minutes between spin trials for recovery

1. BASELINE MEASUREMENT (no spinning):
   Sit in the chair, stationary.
   Open the reaction time test on your phone.
   Complete 20 reaction time trials.
   Record all 20 values.
   Calculate mean and standard deviation.
   This is your BASELINE -- equivalent to Ham's
   ground training performance.

2. SLOW SPIN (1 revolution per 4 seconds):
   Have your helper spin you slowly and steadily.
   While spinning, complete 20 reaction time trials.
   Record all 20 values.
   The vestibular disruption simulates mild g-loading.
   Expected result: 10-30% increase in reaction time.
   This is approximately equivalent to Ham's performance
   during the 6g launch phase.

3. MEDIUM SPIN (1 revolution per 2 seconds):
   Have your helper spin you at moderate speed.
   Complete 20 reaction time trials while spinning.
   Record values.
   Expected result: 30-60% increase in reaction time.
   This is approximately equivalent to the transition
   between launch and weightlessness.

4. POST-SPIN (immediately after stopping):
   Have your helper stop the chair suddenly.
   IMMEDIATELY begin 20 reaction time trials.
   The dizziness and disorientation after stopping
   simulates the vestibular confusion of entering
   weightlessness (the 0g phase). The semicircular
   canals are sending contradictory signals, just
   as they would in microgravity.
   Expected result: 20-50% increase, decreasing
   over the 20 trials as your vestibular system
   recalibrates.

5. RECOVERY (1 minute after stopping):
   Sit still for 1 minute (timed).
   Complete 20 more reaction time trials.
   Expected result: within 10% of baseline.
   This simulates vestibular adaptation -- the
   same adaptation that ISS crew members experience
   in the first 7-14 days.

6. DATA ANALYSIS:
   Plot your reaction times for all 5 conditions.
   Calculate Shannon channel capacity for each:
     C = log2(n+1) / mean_RT
     where n = 1 (binary stimulus: click when green)
     C = log2(2) / mean_RT = 1.0 / mean_RT

   Compare your degradation pattern to Ham's:
     Your baseline: _____ ms (Ham: ~800 ms)
     Your slow spin: _____ ms (Ham at 6g: ~1,750 ms)
     Your medium spin: _____ ms
     Your post-spin: _____ ms (Ham at 0g: ~1,000 ms)
     Your recovery: _____ ms

   The KEY FINDING from Ham's flight: performance
   degrades under stress but DOES NOT REACH ZERO.
   Your spinning chair experiment should confirm
   this: even when dizzy, you can still react to
   stimuli. The channel capacity drops but doesn't
   close. This is what qualified the Mercury capsule
   for human flight.

CONNECT TO MR-2:
  The spinning chair does not produce g-force
  (it produces angular acceleration and Coriolis
  effects). But it disrupts the vestibular system
  in a way that is qualitatively similar to the
  vestibular disruption of spaceflight. The
  dizziness you feel is caused by conflicting
  signals: your semicircular canals say you are
  spinning, but your eyes (if closed) say you
  are stationary. In weightlessness, the otolith
  organs say "no gravity" while the visual system
  may say "up is that way." Same conflict,
  different sensory channels.

  Ham's reaction time increased by ~2.2x at 6g
  and ~3.8x at 14.7g. Your spinning chair will
  likely produce a 1.3-1.6x increase. The
  magnitude is less, but the pattern is the same:
  stress degrades performance without eliminating it.
```

### TRY 2: Newt Survey -- Rough-Skinned Newt Observation

**Duration:** 2-4 hours (field) or 1 hour (video/reference)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Herpetology / Ecology
**What You Need:** If in the PNW: rain gear, rubber boots, a flashlight or headlamp, a field guide to Pacific Northwest reptiles and amphibians (Corkran & Thoms, "Amphibians of Oregon, Washington, and British Columbia," is excellent), and a notebook. If NOT in the PNW: access to iNaturalist (inaturalist.org) for photo observations and distribution data, or YouTube videos of Taricha granulosa in the wild. IMPORTANT: Do not handle rough-skinned newts with bare hands if you have any cuts or open wounds. TTX cannot penetrate intact skin, but it can enter through broken skin or mucous membranes. Wash hands after any contact. Total cost: $0-20 (field guide).

**The Exercise:**

```
ROUGH-SKINNED NEWT SURVEY:

1. FIELD SURVEY (PNW residents, fall/spring):
   The best time to observe Taricha granulosa is during
   their migration between upland forest and breeding
   ponds, which occurs in fall (October-November) and
   spring (March-April) in western Washington and Oregon.

   WHERE TO LOOK:
   - Forest trails near ponds, lakes, or slow streams
   - Under rotting logs and bark in moist forest
   - On roads during or after rain (CAUTION: they
     are extremely vulnerable to road mortality)
   - In and around breeding ponds in spring

   WHAT TO RECORD:
   - Location (GPS coordinates or landmark)
   - Date and time
   - Weather conditions (temperature, rain, cloud cover)
   - Habitat (forest type, substrate, distance to water)
   - Number of newts observed
   - Behavior (crawling, stationary, in water, under cover)
   - Size (estimated length in cm)
   - Color (dorsal: dark brown/black; ventral: orange/yellow)
   - Any defensive posture (unken reflex: back arched,
     tail curled over body, displaying orange belly)

   The unken reflex is the newt's warning display: it
   arches its back and curls its tail to expose the
   bright orange ventral surface, signaling "I am toxic."
   If you gently (GENTLY) touch a newt's back with a
   stick, it may display this reflex. The orange belly
   is aposematic coloration: a visual warning that the
   animal is dangerous to eat.

2. DESKTOP SURVEY (non-PNW or winter):
   Go to iNaturalist.org and search for Taricha granulosa.
   Explore the range map, photo observations, and seasonal
   distribution. Note:
   - The core range (western OR and WA) vs. margins (BC, CA)
   - Seasonal activity patterns (when are most observations?)
   - Habitat associations (what ecosystems appear in photos?)

3. CONNECT TO MR-2:
   Taricha granulosa is this mission's organism because
   both Ham and the newt demonstrate survival under
   extreme stress through costly defense mechanisms.
   Ham's abort system (the escape tower and capsule
   separation) saved him but worsened his trajectory.
   The newt's TTX kills predators but does not prevent
   the bite that triggers the toxin release. Both
   defenses are successful but not free -- survival
   costs the survivor something.

   The geographic structure of TTX concentration
   (highest in the PNW, lower at range margins) mirrors
   an engineering principle: systems under the most
   intense pressure develop the strongest defenses.
   The PNW newts face the most TTX-resistant snakes,
   so they produce the most TTX. Ham faced the highest
   g-forces in the Mercury program (14.7g due to the
   overburn), so his capsule's abort system operated
   at its maximum capacity.

SAFETY:
  - Do not handle newts if you have cuts or open wounds
  - Wash hands thoroughly after any contact
  - Do not disturb breeding ponds during spring
  - Do not collect or remove newts from the wild
    (they are protected in some jurisdictions)
  - If surveying roads during migration: wear
    reflective clothing and watch for traffic
  - TTX is NOT airborne -- you cannot be poisoned
    by being near a newt, only by ingesting the
    toxin or getting it into a wound
```

## DIY Projects

### DIY 1: Reaction Time Tester -- Arduino-Based Performance Measurement

**Duration:** 4-6 hours
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Department:** Aerospace Medicine / Electronics
**What You Need:** An Arduino Uno or compatible microcontroller (~$15-25), 1 bright LED (blue preferred, to match Ham's blue light stimulus), 1 push button, 1 piezo buzzer, 3 resistors (220-ohm for LED, 10K pull-down for button, 220-ohm for buzzer), a breadboard, jumper wires, and a USB cable. Optional: a small LCD display (16x2, ~$5-8) for showing results without a computer. Total cost: $20-40.

**What You'll Build:**
A device that presents a visual stimulus (blue LED illuminating at random intervals), measures your reaction time (time from LED on to button press, in milliseconds), records multiple trials, computes statistics (mean, standard deviation, min, max), and displays a running graph of your performance. This is a simplified version of the lever-task apparatus that Ham used during MR-2.

```

1. WIRING:
   Arduino Uno pin connections:
   - Blue LED -> Pin 8 (through 220-ohm resistor)
   - Push button -> Pin 2 (with 10K pull-down resistor to GND)
   - Piezo buzzer -> Pin 11 (through 220-ohm resistor)
   - (Optional) LCD SDA -> A4, SCL -> A5

   The blue LED is the STIMULUS (Ham's blue light).
   The button is the RESPONSE (Ham's lever).
   The buzzer provides audio feedback (correct/timeout).

2. THE SKETCH (Arduino code):

   /*
    * MR-2 Reaction Time Tester
    * Simulates Ham's lever task:
    *   - Blue light = stimulus
    *   - Button press = response
    *   - Measures time between stimulus and response
    *   - Records statistics across trials
    *
    * Based on Ham's conditioned avoidance task,
    * Mercury-Redstone 2, January 31, 1961.
    */

   const int LED_BLUE = 8;
   const int BTN_RESPONSE = 2;
   const int BUZZER = 11;

   const int NUM_TRIALS = 20;
   const unsigned long TIMEOUT_MS = 5000; // Ham's 5-second window
   const unsigned long MIN_DELAY_MS = 2000;
   const unsigned long MAX_DELAY_MS = 8000;

   unsigned long reactionTimes[20];
   int trialCount = 0;
   int correctCount = 0;
   int timeoutCount = 0;

   void setup() {
     pinMode(LED_BLUE, OUTPUT);
     pinMode(BTN_RESPONSE, INPUT);
     pinMode(BUZZER, OUTPUT);
     Serial.begin(9600);

     Serial.println("=================================");
     Serial.println("MR-2 REACTION TIME TESTER");
     Serial.println("Ham's Lever Task Simulator");
     Serial.println("=================================");
     Serial.println();
     Serial.println("Press the button when the blue");
     Serial.println("light turns on. You have 5 seconds.");
     Serial.println("(Ham had 5 seconds too.)");
     Serial.println();
     Serial.println("Starting in 3 seconds...");
     delay(3000);
   }

   void loop() {
     if (trialCount >= NUM_TRIALS) {
       printResults();
       while(1); // halt
     }

     // Random delay before stimulus (2-8 seconds)
     unsigned long waitTime = random(MIN_DELAY_MS, MAX_DELAY_MS);

     // Check for early presses (false starts)
     unsigned long waitStart = millis();
     while (millis() - waitStart < waitTime) {
       if (digitalRead(BTN_RESPONSE) == HIGH) {
         Serial.println("  FALSE START! Wait for the light.");
         tone(BUZZER, 200, 300); // low buzz = error
         delay(1000);
         waitStart = millis(); // restart wait
         waitTime = random(MIN_DELAY_MS, MAX_DELAY_MS);
       }
     }

     // Present stimulus
     digitalWrite(LED_BLUE, HIGH);
     unsigned long stimulusTime = millis();

     // Wait for response or timeout
     bool responded = false;
     while (millis() - stimulusTime < TIMEOUT_MS) {
       if (digitalRead(BTN_RESPONSE) == HIGH) {
         unsigned long rt = millis() - stimulusTime;
         reactionTimes[trialCount] = rt;
         responded = true;

         // Feedback
         digitalWrite(LED_BLUE, LOW);
         tone(BUZZER, 1000, 100); // high beep = correct
         Serial.print("  Trial ");
         Serial.print(trialCount + 1);
         Serial.print(": ");
         Serial.print(rt);
         Serial.println(" ms -- CORRECT");
         correctCount++;
         break;
       }
     }

     if (!responded) {
       // Timeout -- Ham would have received a shock
       digitalWrite(LED_BLUE, LOW);
       reactionTimes[trialCount] = TIMEOUT_MS;
       tone(BUZZER, 150, 500); // long low = timeout
       Serial.print("  Trial ");
       Serial.print(trialCount + 1);
       Serial.println(": TIMEOUT (>5000 ms) -- MISSED");
       timeoutCount++;
     }

     trialCount++;
     delay(500); // brief pause between trials
   }

   void printResults() {
     Serial.println();
     Serial.println("=================================");
     Serial.println("RESULTS");
     Serial.println("=================================");

     // Calculate statistics
     unsigned long sum = 0;
     unsigned long minRT = 99999;
     unsigned long maxRT = 0;

     for (int i = 0; i < NUM_TRIALS; i++) {
       sum += reactionTimes[i];
       if (reactionTimes[i] < minRT) minRT = reactionTimes[i];
       if (reactionTimes[i] > maxRT) maxRT = reactionTimes[i];
     }

     float meanRT = (float)sum / NUM_TRIALS;

     // Standard deviation
     float sumSq = 0;
     for (int i = 0; i < NUM_TRIALS; i++) {
       float diff = reactionTimes[i] - meanRT;
       sumSq += diff * diff;
     }
     float stdRT = sqrt(sumSq / NUM_TRIALS);

     // Shannon channel capacity
     float C = 1.0 / (meanRT / 1000.0); // bits/sec (1 bit task)

     Serial.print("Trials: ");
     Serial.println(NUM_TRIALS);
     Serial.print("Correct: ");
     Serial.print(correctCount);
     Serial.print(" (");
     Serial.print((float)correctCount/NUM_TRIALS*100, 1);
     Serial.println("%)");
     Serial.print("Timeouts: ");
     Serial.println(timeoutCount);
     Serial.print("Mean RT: ");
     Serial.print(meanRT, 1);
     Serial.println(" ms");
     Serial.print("Std Dev: ");
     Serial.print(stdRT, 1);
     Serial.println(" ms");
     Serial.print("Min RT: ");
     Serial.print(minRT);
     Serial.println(" ms");
     Serial.print("Max RT: ");
     Serial.print(maxRT);
     Serial.println(" ms");
     Serial.print("Channel capacity: ");
     Serial.print(C, 2);
     Serial.println(" bits/sec");

     Serial.println();
     Serial.println("--- COMPARISON TO HAM ---");
     Serial.print("Your mean RT: ");
     Serial.print(meanRT, 0);
     Serial.println(" ms");
     Serial.println("Ham baseline:  800 ms");
     Serial.println("Ham at 0g:     1000 ms");
     Serial.println("Ham at 6g:     1750 ms");
     Serial.println("Ham at 14.7g:  3000 ms");

     if (meanRT < 300) {
       Serial.println("STATUS: Faster than Ham (you're not at 14.7g)");
     } else if (meanRT < 800) {
       Serial.println("STATUS: Similar to Ham's baseline");
     } else if (meanRT < 1750) {
       Serial.println("STATUS: Similar to Ham under launch g-load");
     } else {
       Serial.println("STATUS: Similar to Ham under reentry g-load");
     }
   }
```

## Fox Companies Pathways

### FoxFiber Alignment

Ham's reaction time degradation under g-force loading is a direct model for network latency degradation under load. FoxFiber's mesh network will experience increased latency as nodes approach capacity -- more traffic, more queueing delay, slower response. The key finding from MR-2 is that capacity degrades but does not reach zero: Ham could still respond at 14.7g, just 3.75x slower. FoxFiber's protocol stack should be designed with the same assumption: under load, nodes will respond more slowly, but they will respond. The protocol should accommodate degraded performance rather than treating slow responses as failures. Timeout values should scale with observed network load, just as Ham's 5-second task window was generous enough to accommodate his degraded reaction time at peak g-load. A fixed timeout that works at 1g (low load) will cause false failures at 14.7g (high load). Adaptive timeouts -- measuring recent response times and adjusting the deadline accordingly -- are the network engineering equivalent of the Mercury program's biomedical finding: the organism is slower under stress, but it is still working.

### FoxCompute Alignment

The g-force profile of MR-2 is a load profile: low load (coast/0g) -> peak load (reentry/14.7g) -> normal load (recovery/1g). FoxCompute's container orchestration encounters the same pattern: idle periods, traffic spikes, recovery. The MR-2 lesson for container scaling is that the peak-to-nominal ratio matters more than the absolute peak. MR-2's peak was 14.7g / 1g = 14.7x over nominal. If a FoxCompute service experiences 14.7x traffic spikes, the autoscaler must provision 14.7x capacity -- or the service must degrade gracefully (slower responses, not failures), as Ham degraded gracefully (slower lever pulls, not missed levers). The biological model suggests that graceful degradation is more robust than hard-capacity boundaries: Ham did not have a 14.7g capacity limit that triggered a hard cutoff. He had a continuous degradation curve that approached but never reached zero performance. FoxCompute services should be designed the same way: no hard walls, smooth degradation, recovery when load drops.

### Fox CapComm Alignment

The conditioned avoidance task -- stimulus, response, reinforcement -- is the fundamental unit of agent coordination. Fox CapComm's agents receive stimuli (work items, messages, state changes), generate responses (actions, reports, acknowledgments), and receive reinforcement (success confirmation, error feedback). Ham's training progression -- from continuous reinforcement to variable ratio reinforcement -- is the progression from naive agents (need feedback on every action) to mature agents (continue working reliably even when feedback is intermittent). Fox CapComm's agent training protocol should follow the same schedule: start with dense feedback, thin it out as the agent demonstrates reliability, and use the agent's performance under reduced feedback as the qualification metric. An agent that only works when constantly supervised is an unqualified agent. An agent that continues working reliably when feedback is delayed or missing -- that is Ham's lever at 14.7g. That is a qualified agent.
