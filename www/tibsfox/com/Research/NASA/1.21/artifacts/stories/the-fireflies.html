The Fireflies

A story from Mission 1.21 — Mercury-Atlas 6: Friendship 7

v1.21 — NASA Mission Series

Part OneFirst Light

The countdown reached zero and the Atlas did not move. There was a moment — four seconds, an eternity in the language of rockets — between ignition and release, when the engines had lit but the clamps still held, and the vehicle stood there burning, accumulating thrust, the sound building but the body still fixed to the Earth. John Glenn lay on his back in the capsule at the top of the rocket and felt the vibration come up through the structure like something alive, like the Atlantic Ocean during a storm when the swells were long and the hull of the boat thrummed with the energy of water that wanted to go somewhere but had not yet decided where.

Then the clamps released. The Atlas rose. Not fast at first — the acceleration was only one and a half g at liftoff, the gentlest push, a suggestion rather than a command. The gantry fell away in the periscope. The tower cleared. Scott Carpenter’s voice came through the headset from the blockhouse, the last human sound Glenn would hear from the ground before the roar of the engines swallowed everything else: “Godspeed, John Glenn.”

It was February 20, 1962. Nine forty-seven in the morning, Eastern Standard Time. The weather was clear over Cape Canaveral. The Atlas-D launch vehicle, number 109-D, carried Mercury spacecraft number 13 — redesignated Friendship 7, named by Glenn’s children, who had traced the name in crayon on a piece of paper that Glenn carried in his flight suit. The rocket climbed. The vibration intensified. Glenn reported: “We’re programming in roll okay.” The Atlas rolled to its correct azimuth, pitching over on a trajectory that would carry it northeast across the Atlantic at an angle calculated to insert the capsule into an orbit inclined 32.5 degrees to the equator — an orbit that would carry him over most of the populated world, over deserts and oceans and mountain ranges and cities where people were watching television sets, watching the thin white line of exhaust climb away from Florida into a sky that was, from the ground, unremarkably blue.

At two minutes and fourteen seconds, the booster engines cut off and separated. The sustainer engine burned alone. The acceleration climbed. Glenn felt himself pressed deeper into the contour couch, the load increasing steadily from three g to five to six, the blood pooling in his back, his arms heavy, his vision narrowing slightly at the edges. He had trained for this. The centrifuge at Johnsville, Pennsylvania, had subjected him to loads far beyond what the Atlas would deliver. But the centrifuge was a mechanical experience — a room spinning on an arm, the forces predictable and repeating. This was a ride on a column of fire that was burning its way out of the atmosphere, and the difference between training and the thing itself was the difference between reading about the ocean and drowning in it.

At five minutes and one second, the sustainer engine cut off. The acceleration dropped from six g to zero in an instant — a transition so abrupt that Glenn’s body, still braced for the crush, surged forward against the harness. Dust motes that had been pressed flat against every surface floated upward. A washer that had been wedged behind the instrument panel drifted free and hung in the air in front of his face, turning slowly. He was weightless. He was in orbit.

The capsule separated from the booster with a hollow bang and turned around, heat shield forward, pointing backward along its path. Through the window Glenn saw the spent Atlas tumbling away behind him, sunlight catching the corrugated skin of the fuel tank, the empty engine skirt trailing wisps of residual propellant that crystallized instantly in the vacuum and scattered like — but he did not yet have the word for what they looked like. That would come later, on the dark side.

He looked at the Earth.

He had been told what to expect. He had seen the photographs from the X-15, the high-altitude images that showed the curvature, the thin blue band of atmosphere at the limb, the darkening sky above. Shepard had described it. Grissom had described it. The Soviet cosmonauts — Gagarin and Titov — had described it in the language of state propaganda, which was florid and imprecise but which, Glenn now understood, had not been exaggerating. The Earth from orbit was not what the photographs showed. The photographs could not show it. The photographs captured light and color and geometry, but they could not capture the fact that the thing was alive — that the planet was not a static sphere but a breathing, turning, cloud-streaked organism suspended in a darkness so absolute that the mind rejected it.

Africa was below him. The coast of West Africa, the Sahara stretching inland in shades of tan and orange, the Atlantic to the west flashing silver where the sun caught the wave crests. He could see weather systems — a line of cumulus along the coast, a cyclonic swirl far to the south, the sharp edge where clear sky met cloud deck, a boundary as precise as if someone had drawn it with a ruler. The colors were not the colors of photographs. They were richer, deeper, more saturated, as if the atmosphere acted as a lens that concentrated light rather than diffusing it.

And at the edge of everything, the line. The thin blue line that was the atmosphere — the only atmosphere, the only air, the entire inventory of breathable gas that the planet possessed — visible as a band so narrow it looked like a coat of paint on a basketball. He had known this intellectually. He had studied the physics. The atmosphere extends to roughly one hundred kilometers, and the Earth’s radius is six thousand three hundred and seventy-one kilometers, which means the atmosphere is one-point-six percent of the radius, a rounding error, a skin. But knowing the numbers and seeing the skin were different things. The numbers were abstract. The skin was terrifying. Everything that had ever lived — every lungful of air ever drawn by any creature in the history of the planet — had come from that band. It looked like it could be scraped off with a fingernail.

The Chinook salmon smolt enters the Pacific Ocean in spring, usually between March and June, depending on the river system and the latitude. The fish is between three and seven inches long. It has spent one to two years in freshwater — hatching from an egg buried in gravel, absorbing its yolk sac, emerging as an alevin, growing into a fry, and then, when the biological clock encoded in its DNA determines that the time has come, undergoing smoltification: a transformation as profound as anything in vertebrate biology.

The parr marks fade. The scales turn silver — a camouflage adaptation for open water, where the silver belly is invisible against the bright surface when viewed from below, and the dark back is invisible against the deep water when viewed from above. The gills change. The kidneys change. The osmoregulatory system reverses: in freshwater, the fish must excrete excess water and retain salts; in saltwater, it must excrete excess salts and retain water. The entire physiology of the animal rebuilds itself, organ by organ, cell by cell, in preparation for an environment it has never experienced.

The smolt drifts downriver to the estuary. It lingers in the brackish water for days or weeks, the salinity rising gradually, the body adjusting. And then it crosses the bar. The river ends. The ocean begins. The fish enters a body of water that stretches to the horizon in every direction, without current, without banks, without the familiar architecture of riffle and pool and eddy that has defined its entire life. It has been built for this. Every cell has been rebuilt for this. But the ocean is vast, and the smolt is very small, and the threshold between the known and the unknown is the width of a wave.

Glenn crossed the Atlantic in six minutes. He passed over the Canary Islands and reported the view to the ground station there. He could see the islands clearly — volcanic peaks rising from the ocean, the clouds forming on the windward slopes, the leeward sides dry and brown. He could see the dust plume from the Sahara extending hundreds of miles out over the Atlantic, a tan haze that softened the boundary between land and sea. He took photographs. He made notes on the flight plan card strapped to his knee. He checked the instruments. The orbital parameters were good: perigee one hundred miles, apogee one hundred and sixty-two miles, orbital period eighty-eight minutes and twenty-nine seconds. He would circle the Earth three times in four hours and fifty-five minutes and then come home.

He crossed the terminator — the line between day and night — over the Indian Ocean. The sunset from orbit was nothing like a sunset from the ground. From the ground, the sun descends to the horizon and the colors spread across the sky in a slow arc, the light fading through gold to orange to red to purple to black, the transition taking thirty or forty minutes depending on the latitude. From orbit, the sun dropped below the curved limb of the Earth in seconds, and the colors were compressed into a band so intense it looked artificial — a stripe of electric blue at the top, then white, then yellow, then orange, then a red so deep it was almost black, and then the blackness of space, and the stars, and the stars were not twinkling because there was no atmosphere to distort them, and there were more stars than he had ever seen, more stars than he had known existed, the Milky Way a river of light so dense it cast shadows on the instrument panel.

He was over Australia when the first orbit ended. The city of Perth had turned on all its lights — the whole city, every house, every streetlamp, every car headlight — so that Glenn could see them from orbit. He could. A cluster of light on the dark coast of a dark continent, a human signal sent upward through a hundred miles of atmosphere to a man traveling at seventeen thousand five hundred miles per hour. He reported: “The lights show up very well. Thank everybody for turning them on, will you?”

Perth blinked back. The whole city, winking at him from the far side of the world.

Part TwoThe Fireflies

He saw them as he came around the dark side on the second orbit, just after the orbital dawn broke over the Pacific. They were outside the window — thousands of them, tiny luminescent particles drifting past the capsule in a slow, lazy swirl, each one glowing yellow-green, each one about the size of a pinhead, each one lit from within by a light that had no obvious source. They surrounded the capsule completely. They moved slowly, without urgency, as if they were suspended in water rather than vacuum. They did not behave like debris. They did not tumble or spin or follow the capsule’s wake. They drifted. They floated. They glowed.

Glenn reported to the ground station at Canton Island: “I am in a big mass of some very small particles that are brilliantly lit up like they’re luminescent. I never saw anything like it. They’re coming by the capsule, and they look like little stars. A whole shower of them coming by.”

Canton Island relayed: “Roger, Friendship 7. Can you hear any impact with the capsule?”

“Negative, negative. They’re very slow. They’re not going away from me more than maybe three or four miles per hour.”

He watched them. He had been trained to observe and report, and he observed and reported, but the training had not prepared him for the experience of floating in a tin capsule a hundred miles above the Pacific Ocean at four in the morning, surrounded by ten thousand points of light that should not have existed. They were everywhere. They extended as far as he could see through the window, a field of luminous motes that filled the space around the capsule like snow in a snow globe, except that they were not falling. They were hovering. They pulsed faintly, not flickering like fireflies but glowing steadily, each one a tiny sustained light in the void.

“They’re all over the sky,” he reported. “As far as I can see in either direction.”

Houston was confused. There was no protocol for luminescent particles. The flight plan had anticipated debris from the booster, which would tumble and reflect sunlight in a predictable pattern. It had anticipated thruster residue, which would disperse rapidly. It had not anticipated ten thousand tiny lights drifting peacefully past the window of a spacecraft. The engineers on the ground consulted. They suggested he tap the hull to see if the particles responded. He tapped. Some of the closer ones stirred slightly. Most did not react at all.

He named them the fireflies. It was the only word that fit. Not because they behaved like fireflies — fireflies flash and blink and trace erratic paths through summer air — but because they were alive with light in a place where no light should have been. The word was instinctive. It was the word a boy from New Concord, Ohio, would use — a boy who had grown up catching fireflies in Mason jars on summer evenings along the Muskingum River, screwing the lids on tight, watching the yellow-green pulses through the glass. Those fireflies were Photinus pyralis, the common Eastern firefly, each one carrying an enzyme called luciferase that combined with a substrate called luciferin in the presence of oxygen to produce cold light — a chemical reaction so efficient that nearly one hundred percent of the energy was emitted as light, with almost no heat. The most efficient light source in nature. And here, a hundred miles above the Pacific, Glenn was surrounded by lights that looked exactly like that, except they were not alive, and he did not know what they were.

At the same time, or near enough — time is a flexible thing in a story about orbits — the Automatic Stabilization and Control System began to drift. The ASCS was the autopilot, the system that held the capsule in the correct attitude by firing small hydrogen peroxide thrusters in response to signals from the gyroscopes and horizon sensors. The capsule was supposed to maintain a specific orientation: heat shield forward, pitched slightly down so that the periscope pointed at the Earth. The ASCS had been holding this attitude since orbital insertion. Now, on the second orbit, the yaw channel began to wander. The capsule was turning slowly to the right, a degree at a time, and the ASCS was correcting, but each correction used thruster fuel, and each correction was a little late, and the drift was growing.

Glenn watched the attitude indicator. The yaw needle crept to the right, paused, snapped back as the thruster fired, then crept to the right again. The pattern was clear. The system was hunting — oscillating around the correct attitude, overcorrecting, undershooting, wasting fuel in a feedback loop that was slowly degrading. He had two hundred and sixty pounds of hydrogen peroxide for the thrusters. The mission plan called for using no more than half. If the ASCS continued to hunt, he would burn through his fuel reserve before the third orbit, and without thruster fuel he could not maintain attitude for reentry, and without proper attitude for reentry the heat shield would not be oriented correctly, and without the heat shield oriented correctly the capsule would burn.

He switched to manual control. Fly-by-wire first — the system that translated his hand controller inputs into electronic signals to the thrusters, the same thrusters, but under human direction instead of machine. The yaw stabilized immediately. His hand found the correct input faster than the autopilot, because a human hand responds to rate as well as position — a pilot does not just see where the needle is, he sees where it is going, and he anticipates, and the anticipation is the difference between stability and oscillation.

The fireflies were still there. They drifted past the window while he flew the capsule manually, the luminous particles and the instrument panel occupying separate halves of his attention, beauty and engineering coexisting in the same small space. He was the first human being to fly a spacecraft by hand in orbit. Gagarin’s controls had been locked. Titov had used his only briefly. Glenn was flying, truly flying, controlling the attitude of a vehicle in three axes with no aerodynamic surfaces, no wing, no rudder, only the tiny jets of decomposing hydrogen peroxide pushing against the vacuum. He was a pilot again. The machine had failed and the pilot had taken over, which was the oldest story in aviation, and the fireflies were his witnesses.

The Chinook salmon navigates the North Pacific by means that science has not fully explained. The fish has magnetite crystals in its skull — tiny particles of iron oxide, each one smaller than a red blood cell, arranged in chains that respond to the Earth’s magnetic field. The crystals function as a compass, providing directional information that the fish integrates with other cues: the angle of polarized light, the temperature gradient, the chemical composition of the water, the position of the sun. The salmon builds a map from these inputs, a map that exists nowhere except in the neural architecture of a brain smaller than a walnut.

The fish cannot see where it is going. The Pacific Ocean is opaque beyond a few hundred feet. There are no landmarks. There are no roads. There is only the vast, featureless blue, and within it the invisible architecture of currents and temperature fronts and magnetic gradients that the salmon reads the way a pilot reads instruments — not by looking at the destination, but by monitoring the needles and trusting them to point the way.

The Chinook will spend two to five years in the ocean, ranging as far as two thousand miles from its natal river. It will grow from a seven-inch smolt to a fish of twenty, forty, sometimes sixty pounds. It will eat herring and sand lance and squid and krill. It will dive to depths of over a thousand feet. It will navigate by a system it does not understand, following instructions written in a genetic code that was tested and refined over five million years of trial and error, five million years of fish that navigated correctly and survived and fish that navigated incorrectly and did not. The salmon does not know why it turns north instead of south. It turns because turning is what it does, and it has always worked, and the proof is in the fact that it is alive to turn at all.

The fireflies vanished as the sun came up. Glenn watched them fade — the glow dimming as the sunlight overwhelmed them, the particles still there but no longer visible against the brilliance of the orbital dawn. They had been with him for about eighteen minutes. They would return on each subsequent passage into the dark side, though he saw them most clearly on the second orbit, when the surprise was still fresh and the ASCS had not yet become the dominant concern.

He did not know what they were. NASA did not know. The engineers debated: ice crystals from the life support system, venting through microscopic leaks in the hull? Frozen droplets of hydrogen peroxide from the attitude thrusters? Micrometeorites, somehow trapped in the capsule’s gravitational influence? Bioluminescent organisms carried aloft in the booster exhaust? Each hypothesis had problems. Ice crystals would sublimate. Thruster residue would disperse. Micrometeorites would not glow. Organisms would not survive the vacuum.

Scott Carpenter, who flew the next Mercury-Atlas mission three months later, saw them too. He noticed that when he rapped the hull of his capsule, Aurora 7, the particles multiplied — a burst of new fireflies erupting from the surface each time he struck it. He reported this to the ground, and the mystery collapsed: the fireflies were ice crystals. Frost from the capsule’s exterior, condensation from the cooling system that had frozen on the cold side of the spacecraft during the night pass, shaken loose by vibration or thermal expansion as the capsule entered sunlight. Each crystal was tiny — a fraction of a millimeter — and as it drifted away from the warm hull into the vacuum, it caught the first rays of the orbital dawn and refracted them, glowing yellow-green against the darkness for the seconds or minutes before it sublimated into nothing.

Water. They were water. Frozen water, lit by the sun, drifting in space. The most common molecule in the universe, in its simplest solid form, performing a trick of refraction that made it look like life. Glenn had floated through a cloud of ice, and the ice had glowed, and he had called them fireflies because that was what they looked like, and the explanation — when it came — was more beautiful than the mystery. Not luminescence. Not life. Light. Sunlight, bent through crystals of frozen water, the same physics that makes halos around the moon and sundogs in a winter sky and rainbows after a summer storm. The most ordinary magic in the world, performed in the most extraordinary place a human being had ever been.

Part ThreeThe Shield

Segment 51 was a telemetry signal. One of hundreds that the capsule transmitted continuously to the ground, a stream of data encoding the state of every system in the spacecraft: temperature, pressure, attitude, fuel, oxygen, suit integrity, cabin integrity, and — in the case of Segment 51 — the position of the landing bag deploy switch. The landing bag was a rubberized skirt between the heat shield and the capsule’s pressure vessel. During reentry, after the parachutes deployed and the capsule was descending under canopy, the heat shield would drop down about four feet on straps, and the landing bag would unfold beneath the capsule to absorb the impact of splashdown. The system was simple. The switch was either locked or deployed. On or off.

Segment 51 said deployed.

The signal came in during the second orbit. The Mercury Control Center at Cape Canaveral saw it first. If the landing bag had deployed early, it meant the heat shield was no longer locked against the pressure vessel. It meant the clamp ring that held the shield in place might have released. It meant the heat shield — the blunt, ablative disk that would absorb five thousand degrees Fahrenheit of reentry heating, the only barrier between John Glenn and the plasma sheath that forms when a spacecraft hits the atmosphere at seventeen thousand five hundred miles per hour — might be loose.

If the heat shield was loose, Glenn would die. Not might. Would. The shield would separate during reentry, the plasma would enter the gap, and the pressure vessel would burn through in seconds. There would be no explosion — just a sudden brightening on the tracking radar, a shower of molten metal and ceramic, and silence on the radio.

The flight controllers did not tell Glenn. Not immediately. They discussed it among themselves, in the clipped, technical language of engineers confronting a problem that might be catastrophic or might be a faulty sensor, and they could not determine which. They queried the telemetry again. Segment 51 still read deployed. They cross-checked against other data: the heat shield temperature sensor, the pressure vessel integrity, the capsule’s center of gravity as measured by the attitude thrusters. Nothing else was anomalous. The heat shield temperature was normal. The capsule’s behavior was consistent with a properly seated shield. The only indication of a problem was Segment 51, and Segment 51 could be wrong. Sensors failed. Wires shorted. Telemetry lied.

But if Segment 51 was right, they needed a plan.

The plan they devised was this: during reentry, instead of jettisoning the retropackage — the bundle of three solid-fuel retrorockets strapped across the heat shield by three metal bands — they would leave it in place. The retropack had already fired, on schedule, at the end of the third orbit, slowing the capsule for reentry. The empty casings and their retaining straps served no further purpose and were normally jettisoned to expose the clean heat shield for atmospheric entry. But the metal straps crossed the heat shield in a Y pattern, and if the heat shield was loose, the straps would hold it against the pressure vessel during the initial phase of reentry, buying time — perhaps enough time — for the aerodynamic pressure to build and press the shield into place even after the straps burned through.

It was not a good plan. It was the only plan they had.

• • •

They told Glenn to leave the retropack on. They did not tell him why. The communication came from the ground station in Texas, during the third orbit, in the carefully neutral tone that controllers used when they did not want the pilot to hear the fear in their voices: “We are recommending that you leave the retropackage on through the entire reentry.”

Glenn acknowledged. He did not ask why. But he knew something was wrong. The retropack was always jettisoned. Every simulation, every procedure, every checklist said jettison. Leaving it on was not standard. Leaving it on meant they were worried about something behind the heat shield, and the only thing behind the heat shield that mattered was the heat shield itself. Glenn was a test pilot. He could read the silences in a radio transmission the way a doctor reads the silences in a patient’s answers. The ground was not telling him something, and the thing they were not telling him was about the heat shield, and the heat shield was the only thing between him and five thousand degrees.

He began the reentry checklist. The retros had fired. The capsule was descending, the orbit decaying, the perigee dropping toward the atmosphere. He oriented the capsule: heat shield forward, pitched up fourteen degrees, the attitude that would present the blunt face to the airstream and let the ablative material do its work. He confirmed the retropack was still attached. He checked his harness. He tightened the visor on his helmet.

The capsule entered the atmosphere at an altitude of about fifty-five miles. The first indication was a faint glow outside the window — a pale orange light, barely visible, like the first hint of dawn. The glow brightened. The deceleration began, gently at first, pressing him into the couch with a force that climbed from one g to two to three. The window turned orange. The orange deepened to amber. The amber brightened to white. The capsule was surrounded by a sheath of ionized gas — plasma — that burned at temperatures between three thousand and five thousand degrees Fahrenheit, and the radio went dead because the plasma was electrically conductive and blocked the radio frequency, and Glenn was alone in the capsule with no communication and a window full of fire and a heat shield that might or might not be attached.

The retropack began to burn. He could see it — or rather, he could see pieces of it, chunks of burning material breaking away and streaming past the window in trails of orange and white fire. They tumbled as they burned, irregular shapes trailing sparks and flame, each one a fragment of the retaining straps or the rocket casings or the fiberglass fairing that had covered them. They looked like burning birds. They looked like meteors. They came past the window in ones and twos and then in clusters, a rain of fire streaming backward along the capsule’s trajectory, and Glenn watched them and did not know if they were pieces of the retropack or pieces of the heat shield.

He reported, to no one, because the radio was blacked out: “That’s a real fireball outside.”

The distinction mattered. If the burning fragments were retropack, then the plan was working: the straps were holding the shield in place while they burned away, and the aerodynamic pressure was building, and the shield would stay seated after the straps were gone. If the burning fragments were heat shield, then the ablative material was failing, the shield was breaking apart, and he had seconds to live. He could not tell the difference. Both would burn the same color. Both would break apart in the same tumbling, trailing way. Both would look like fire.

He was calm. His heart rate, recorded by the biomedical sensors in his suit, peaked at the same level it had reached during the launch — elevated but controlled, the heart rate of a man managing fear rather than surrendering to it. He continued to report instrument readings to the dead radio. He maintained attitude control, making small corrections with the hand controller to keep the heat shield pointed into the airstream. He watched the burning fragments stream past and he waited for the heat to come through the wall or not come through the wall, and the waiting was the hardest part, because the waiting was where the uncertainty lived.

The fragments stopped. The glow outside the window shifted from white back to orange, from orange to amber, from amber to a faint pink that was the last remnant of the plasma sheath. The deceleration peaked at seven point seven g and began to fall. The radio crackled. A voice from the ground, broken by static: “How do you read? Over.”

“Loud and clear,” Glenn said. “How me?”

“Roger, reading you loud and clear. How are you doing?”

“Oh, pretty good,” Glenn said.

The heat shield had held. Segment 51 had been wrong. The landing bag deploy switch had a loose signal wire — a telemetry error, not a mechanical failure. The heat shield had been properly seated the entire time. The retropack had burned away during reentry, each piece a false alarm, each burning fragment a piece of a plan that had been devised against a problem that did not exist. Glenn had ridden through fire for four minutes and thirty seconds, watching the evidence of his possible death stream past the window, and the evidence had been meaningless.

The drogue parachute deployed at twenty-eight thousand feet. The main parachute deployed at ten thousand eight hundred feet. The capsule descended under canopy, swinging gently, the heat shield dropping down on its straps, the landing bag inflating beneath. The Atlantic Ocean rose to meet him. Friendship 7 hit the water at approximately two forty-three in the afternoon, Eastern Standard Time — four hours, fifty-five minutes, and twenty-three seconds after launch. The capsule bobbed upright. The waves slapped the hull. Glenn waited for the destroyer USS Noa to come alongside. When the ship was in position, he blew the hatch — the same type of hatch that had blown uncommanded on Grissom’s flight seven months earlier — and he came out through the opening and onto the deck of the ship. His hand was cut where it struck the hatch plunger, and there was a mark on his palm that would last for days.

He was the first American to orbit the Earth. He had circled it three times. He had seen Africa and Australia and the Pacific Ocean and the Atlantic Ocean and the deserts and the cloud systems and the thunderstorms lit from within by lightning and the cities shining on the night side and Perth with all its lights turned on. He had seen the fireflies. He had flown the capsule by hand when the autopilot failed. He had ridden through a fireball that might have been his death and come out the other side with a cut hand and a steady heartbeat and the words “pretty good” on the radio.

The Chinook salmon returns. After two to five years in the Pacific, the fish turns toward shore. The signal that triggers the return is not fully understood — it may be photoperiod, or water temperature, or some internal calendar calibrated to the fish’s age and growth rate. But the return is absolute. The fish does not choose to return. It is compelled. The same genetic program that rebuilt its body for saltwater now demands that it go back.

The fish finds its natal river by smell. The olfactory system of a salmon can detect its home water at concentrations of parts per billion — a few molecules of the specific chemical signature of one particular tributary among the thousands that feed the Pacific coast, a scent imprinted during the days and weeks the smolt spent lingering in the estuary before it crossed the bar into the ocean. The fish follows this scent the way a pilot follows a beacon, homing on a signal so faint it should be undetectable, trusting it absolutely because there is no alternative and because the five million years of refinement that produced this system did not produce it carelessly.

The fish enters the river. The freshwater hits its gills like a wall. The osmoregulatory system reverses again — salt-excreting to water-excreting, the kidneys and gills and gut switching from one mode to the other in a transition that takes days and costs the fish enormously in energy. The body changes. The skin darkens. The males develop a hooked jaw called a kype, the teeth enlarging, the head elongating into a shape that is designed for fighting, not feeding. The fish stops eating. From this point forward, it lives on its own stored fat and muscle, consuming itself to fuel the journey upstream, burning its body to power the last miles to the gravel bed where it was born.

The salmon spawns. The female digs a redd in the gravel with her tail — a depression three to twelve inches deep, carefully positioned in water of the correct depth and current speed, the gravel of the correct size. She deposits her eggs. The male fertilizes them. The female covers the redd with gravel, protecting the eggs from predators and silt. Then the fish dies. Not immediately — the death takes days, sometimes weeks, the body deteriorating, the immune system collapsing, the fungus growing on the damaged skin, the fish drifting in the current of the river where it was born, spent and fading, its purpose fulfilled. The body that left this river as a seven-inch smolt and crossed two thousand miles of ocean and grew to forty pounds has come back and given everything it had and now it is done.

The carcass feeds the river. Nitrogen, phosphorus, calcium, trace elements carried from the ocean into the freshwater ecosystem, the body of the fish becoming the body of the forest that shades the stream that shelters the eggs that will hatch into the next generation. The salmon is a vector. It carries the ocean inland. It carries the nutrients that the trees need to grow tall enough to shade the water cool enough to keep the eggs alive long enough to hatch into the fish that will carry the next load of ocean back to the forest. The cycle is closed. The circle is complete. Everything comes back to where it started.

Ansel Adams was born on February 20, 1902. John Glenn orbited the Earth on February 20, 1962. The coincidence means nothing and everything, which is to say it means exactly what you want it to mean, and what it means to this story is this: Adams spent his life showing Americans their own landscape from perspectives they had never imagined.

He was not the first photographer to point a camera at the American West. Timothy O’Sullivan had photographed the canyons and deserts in the 1860s and 1870s, working with wet-plate collodion in conditions that would have defeated a less stubborn man. Carlton Watkins had photographed Yosemite in 1861, images so striking that they contributed to Lincoln’s decision to grant the valley federal protection. William Henry Jackson had photographed Yellowstone for the Hayden Survey, and his images had helped convince Congress to establish the first national park. Photography had been showing Americans their landscape for a century before Adams loaded a roll of film.

But Adams did something different. He did not merely document. He interpreted. The zone system — his method of controlling exposure and development to produce prints of extraordinary tonal range — was a technical innovation, but the technique served a vision: that the landscape was not scenery to be recorded but a presence to be felt. Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico, taken in 1941, is not a photograph of a moon above a village. It is a photograph of light and darkness and the line between them, the crosses in the cemetery catching the last of the day, the mountains behind them already in shadow, the moon already risen into a sky that is still blue but will not be blue for much longer. The photograph is about time. It is about the moment when one kind of light replaces another, and the landscape exists in both simultaneously, and the viewer is suspended between day and night the way the village is suspended between the mountains and the sky.

Adams showed Americans what their own country looked like when you really looked at it. Not a glance from a car window. Not a snapshot from a scenic overlook. A sustained, disciplined, technically masterful act of attention, the camera held still, the exposure calculated, the development controlled, every variable managed so that the print would convey not just the light that was there but the feeling of being in the presence of that light. His photographs of Yosemite, of the Sierra Nevada, of the deserts and coastlines and forests of the American West, did not show people things they had never seen. They showed people things they had seen every day and never truly looked at.

Glenn did the same thing from orbit. He showed Americans their own planet. Not a map, not a globe, not an abstraction. The actual planet, seen from the outside for the first time by American eyes, the blue and white and tan of it, the curvature, the atmosphere, the fragility. He described it in the language of a pilot — precise, technical, understated — but the description carried the same weight as an Adams photograph: the weight of attention, the weight of really looking at something that has always been there and seeing it as if for the first time.

Adams stood at the foot of Half Dome with a camera. Glenn floated a hundred miles above Africa in a capsule the size of a bathtub. Both of them saw something that everyone knew existed and no one had really seen. Both of them brought back evidence that the world was more beautiful and more fragile than anyone had imagined. Both of them did it on February 20.

• • •

The fireflies were water. The heat shield scare was a faulty wire. The autopilot failure was a stuck yaw thruster. Everything that seemed mysterious or terrifying during the flight turned out to have a simple, mechanical explanation. The wonder reduced to physics. The fear reduced to a loose connection. The flight, stripped of its drama, was ninety minutes of boredom repeated three times, with some manual flying and a bumpy reentry.

But that is not what it was. Strip a thing to its mechanics and you lose the thing. Adams knew this. A photograph of Half Dome is just reflected light captured on silver halide. A sunset is just Rayleigh scattering. A firefly is just luciferin and luciferase. The explanation does not diminish the experience. It deepens it. To know that the fireflies were ice crystals — frozen water, the most common molecule in the solar system, the molecule that makes oceans and rivers and clouds and rain and snow and glaciers and the rings of Saturn — is not to reduce the wonder. It is to connect it. The fireflies were made of the same substance as the ocean Glenn would splash down in, the same substance as the clouds he could see from orbit, the same substance as the snow on the Sierra Nevada that Adams photographed in winter light. Water, in all its forms, following Glenn through space, surrounding his capsule with light, and then sublimating into nothing, returning to the vacuum as vapor, as invisible gas, as the raw material for the next generation of fireflies on the next orbit of the next spacecraft that carried a human being into the dark.

The salmon returns to water. It lives in water, and it dies in water, and its body feeds the forest that shades the water that shelters the eggs. Glenn returned through fire to water. He descended through plasma — a state of matter hotter than the surface of the sun — and splashed down in the Atlantic Ocean, which is the same ocean the Chinook salmon crosses, which is the same water that froze on the outside of his capsule and drifted past his window as fireflies, which is the same water that falls as rain on the Sierra Nevada and fills the rivers that Adams photographed, silver and black in the print, silver and black in the light.

Everything comes back to where it started. The salmon to the river. The water to the ocean. The astronaut to the Earth. The ice crystal to vapor to ice crystal. The fireflies that Glenn saw in 1962 were not the same water that would freeze on later spacecraft, but they were the same kind of water, following the same physics, producing the same light, witnessed by different eyes but identical in every way that mattered. The fireflies were a species. They reproduced. They recurred. Every Mercury astronaut who flew after Glenn saw them. Every Gemini astronaut saw them. Every Apollo astronaut saw them. They were a feature of spaceflight, as reliable as sunrise, as predictable as the tide, and every astronaut who saw them for the first time understood, in the moment before the explanation arrived, why Glenn had called them fireflies.

Because that is what they looked like. And sometimes what a thing looks like is the truest thing about it.

“Godspeed, John Glenn.”
