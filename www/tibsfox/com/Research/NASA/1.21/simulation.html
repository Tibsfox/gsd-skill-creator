A1. Python: Orbital Ground Track

Libraries: numpy, matplotlib, cartopy (optional)  |  Difficulty: Intermediate  |  Duration: 3–4 hours

What it is: A Python simulation that reconstructs John Glenn’s three-orbit ground track as seen from above — three sinusoidal curves sweeping across a Mercator projection of Earth, each shifted westward by approximately 22.5 degrees as Earth rotates beneath the orbiting capsule. The student inputs the orbital elements derived from the actual Friendship 7 trajectory and watches the ground track unfold orbit by orbit, with the worldwide Mercury tracking network marked at each station location. The simulation reveals the geometry of orbital flight: the capsule does not fly “over” the Earth in the way an airplane does. It falls around the Earth in a fixed orbital plane while the planet rotates beneath it, and the ground track is the record of where the capsule’s nadir — the point directly below it — traces across the rotating surface.

Why it matters: Glenn’s flight was the first time an American experienced what Gagarin and Titov had already seen: the Earth turning beneath a fixed orbital path. The ground track is the most intuitive way to understand this geometry. Each orbit traces a sinusoidal curve because the orbital plane is tilted 32.5 degrees relative to the equator, and the intersection of a tilted plane with a rotating sphere produces a sinusoid on a Mercator projection. The westward shift between orbits is purely due to Earth’s rotation — the capsule’s orbital plane does not move (neglecting precession), but the Earth beneath it does. The student who plots this ground track understands, at a visceral level, that orbital mechanics is geometry: the capsule is not steered, it falls, and the planet turns beneath it.

Key learning moments:

  1. Plot 1 reveals the geometry of orbital flight in a single image: three sinusoidal curves, each shifted westward, together forming the characteristic “ground track wallpaper” that every orbital mission produces. The student sees that Glenn did not fly over specific countries by choice — the capsule’s orbital plane was fixed at insertion, and the Earth rotated beneath it. The tracking stations were positioned to catch the capsule as it passed overhead, not because the capsule was aimed at them.
  2. Plot 4 shows the coverage gaps. During a significant fraction of each orbit, Friendship 7 was beyond the radio horizon of every tracking station. Glenn was alone — no voice contact, no telemetry, no way for the ground to know what was happening inside the capsule. The ASCS yaw malfunction that consumed much of the mission’s attention was diagnosed and managed partly during these communication blackouts, when Glenn had to make engineering decisions without ground support.

A2. Python: Reentry Thermal Analysis

Libraries: numpy, scipy (optional), matplotlib  |  Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced  |  Duration: 4–6 hours

What it is: A Python simulation that models the thermal history of Friendship 7’s heat shield during the reentry from orbit — from the moment the capsule first encounters the upper atmosphere at 120 km altitude through peak heating, peak deceleration, and parachute deployment. The simulation solves the one-dimensional heat conduction equation through the ablative heat shield material, tracking the surface temperature, the ablation front position, and the temperature at the back face of the shield (the side facing the astronaut). The student adjusts entry parameters — velocity, entry angle, capsule mass — and watches the thermal protection system fight against 1,650 degrees Celsius of stagnation point heating.

Why it matters: The Mercury heat shield was a beryllium heat sink design for the suborbital missions (MR-3, MR-4) but Friendship 7 used an ablative shield for the higher energy of orbital reentry. Ablation works by sacrificing material: the shield surface heats until the material decomposes, absorbing enormous energy in the phase change and carrying the hot material away in the airstream. The simulation makes this process quantitative. The student sees that the surface temperature hits 1,650 degrees C while the back face temperature — just centimeters away — stays below 65 degrees C. The ablative shield does not resist heat; it absorbs it, transforms it, and ejects it. The shield gets thinner as it protects. Protection is destruction.

The Segment 51 scare — where mission control received a telemetry indication that the heat shield might be loose — adds dramatic weight. If the heat shield had actually been loose, nothing else in this simulation matters. The numbers are only meaningful if the shield stays attached. Glenn reentered not knowing whether the shield would hold. The simulation shows what “hold” means: centimeters of ablative material standing between a human being and temperatures that would vaporize steel.

# ma6_reentry_thermal.py
# Friendship 7 reentry heat shield thermal analysis
#
# Process:
#   1. Define the reentry trajectory (altitude vs. time)
#   2. Compute aerodynamic heating at each altitude using
#      the Sutton-Graves stagnation point correlation
#   3. Solve 1D heat conduction through the ablative shield
#      with surface ablation (moving boundary)
#   4. Track surface temperature, ablation depth, back face
#      temperature, and deceleration g-load
#   5. Plot the complete thermal history of the reentry
#
# Reentry Parameters (Friendship 7):
#   entry_velocity_km_s: 7.8 (orbital velocity minus
#     retro delta-V of 103 m/s, so approximately 7.7 km/s
#     at entry interface; increases slightly due to
#     gravitational acceleration during descent)
#   entry_angle_deg: -1.5 (shallow entry, typical for
#     Mercury ballistic reentry; negative = descending)
#   capsule_mass_kg: 1360 (reentry mass, after retro
#     jettison if it had been jettisoned -- but Friendship 7
#     retained the retropack through reentry, adding ~60 kg
#     so actual reentry mass ~ 1420 kg)
#   shield_diameter_m: 1.89 (Mercury heat shield base diameter)
#   shield_thickness_m: 0.038 (1.5 inches, ablative material)
#   shield_density_kg_m3: 1500 (phenolic-fiberglass ablator)
#   shield_thermal_conductivity_W_mK: 0.2 (low conductivity,
#     the shield is a poor conductor -- this is by design)
#   shield_specific_heat_J_kgK: 1200
#   ablation_temperature_K: 2500 (the temperature at which
#     the shield material begins to decompose and ablate)
#   ablation_energy_J_kg: 10e6 (10 MJ/kg, the energy absorbed
#     per kg of material ablated -- this is the shield's
#     primary protection mechanism: phase change absorbs
#     enormous energy)
#   nose_radius_m: 0.9 (effective nose radius for stagnation
#     point heating calculation -- the heat shield is a
#     spherical segment with this curvature)
#
# Parameters (user-adjustable):
#   entry_velocity_km_s: 5-12 (default 7.8)
#   entry_angle_deg: -0.5 to -5 (default -1.5)
#   capsule_mass_kg: 1000-2000 (default 1360)
#   shield_thickness_m: 0.02-0.06 (default 0.038)
#   retropack_attached: True/False (default True,
#     Friendship 7 kept the retropack -- adds mass and
#     creates asymmetric heating from retropack burnoff)
#
# Atmospheric Model (exponential atmosphere):
#   rho(h) = rho_0 * exp(-h / H)
#   where:
#     rho_0 = 1.225 kg/m^3 (sea level density)
#     H = 7.5 km (scale height for Earth's atmosphere)
#     h = altitude in km
#
#   This is a simplification -- the real atmosphere has
#   multiple scale heights for different altitude regions.
#   For reentry from 120 km, the exponential model is
#   accurate to within ~20% for the heating calculation,
#   which is sufficient for educational purposes.
#
# Heating Model (Sutton-Graves stagnation point):
#   q = C * sqrt(rho / r_n) * V^3
#   where:
#     q = stagnation point heat flux (W/m^2)
#     C = 1.7415e-4 (Sutton-Graves constant for air,
#       SI units: kg^0.5 / m)
#     rho = freestream density (kg/m^3), from atm model
#     r_n = nose radius (m), 0.9 for Mercury
#     V = velocity (m/s)
#
#   The V^3 dependence is critical: heating increases as
#   the CUBE of velocity. At 7.8 km/s, even thin atmosphere
#   produces enormous heating because V^3 = 4.7e11 m^3/s^3.
#
# Deceleration Model:
#   a = (0.5 * rho * V^2 * Cd * A) / m
#   where:
#     Cd = 1.2 (Mercury capsule drag coefficient, blunt body)
#     A = pi * (d/2)^2 = pi * 0.945^2 = 2.806 m^2
#     m = capsule mass
#
#   Peak deceleration for Friendship 7: approximately 7.7 g
#   (Glenn reported "about 8 g's" in debriefing)
#
# 1D Heat Conduction Solver:
#   The heat shield is discretized into N layers (default 50)
#   across the shield thickness. At each timestep:
#     1. Apply heat flux q to the surface (x=0)
#     2. If surface temperature > ablation temperature:
#        a. Compute mass ablated = (q - q_radiated) / ablation_energy
#        b. Remove the ablated layer (moving boundary)
#        c. Surface temperature is clamped at ablation temp
#     3. Conduct heat through remaining layers:
#        dT/dt = k / (rho * cp) * d2T/dx2
#        (explicit finite difference, stable with CFL condition)
#     4. Back face (x = thickness): insulated boundary
#        (dT/dx = 0, no heat flows into the capsule
#        structure -- simplified assumption)
#     5. Radiation from surface: q_rad = epsilon * sigma * T^4
#        where epsilon = 0.8 (ablator emissivity)
#        sigma = 5.67e-8 W/m^2/K^4 (Stefan-Boltzmann)
#
# Retropack Burnoff (Friendship 7 specific):
#   Because the retropack was retained through reentry
#   (ground command due to Segment 51 heat shield concern),
#   the straps holding it burned through during reentry.
#   Glenn reported seeing "flaming chunks" going past
#   the window. The simulation marks the retropack burnoff
#   point -- approximately 45 seconds after reentry
#   interface, when surface temperature first exceeds
#   1000 K. The retropack does NOT significantly affect
#   the heat shield thermal analysis (it sits on top of
#   the shield, not behind it), but it adds a dramatic
#   visual and psychological element: Glenn was watching
#   pieces of his spacecraft burn away past his window
#   while uncertain whether the heat shield was intact.
#
# Visualization:
#   - Plot 1: Surface temperature vs. time (the main drama plot)
#     X-axis: time from entry interface (0-500 seconds)
#     Y-axis: temperature (200-2000 K)
#     Red curve: surface temperature
#     Blue dashed line: ablation onset temperature (2500 K)
#     Green dashed line: structural limit for capsule
#       wall behind shield (400 K / 127 C)
#     Green curve: back face temperature (stays low)
#     Annotations:
#       Peak surface temperature: ~1920 K (1650 C)
#       at T+180 seconds (3 minutes after entry interface)
#       "Retropack burnoff begins": T+45 s, ~1000 K
#       "Peak heating": T+180 s
#       "Plasma blackout begins": T+60 s (ionized air
#         blocks radio communication)
#       "Plasma blackout ends": T+270 s
#       "Back face peak: 338 K (65 C) -- survivable"
#       The gap between the red curve and the green curve
#       is the shield doing its job: 1600 degrees of
#       temperature drop across 3.8 cm of ablative material
#
#   - Plot 2: Ablation depth vs. time
#     X-axis: time from entry interface (0-500 seconds)
#     Y-axis: ablation depth (0-20 mm, with total shield
#       thickness 38 mm shown as horizontal line)
#     Blue curve: cumulative material removed by ablation
#     Shows that the shield loses approximately 5-8 mm
#       of material during reentry (13-21% of total thickness)
#     Annotation:
#       "Material ablated: ~7 mm (18% of shield)"
#       "Shield margin remaining: 31 mm (82%)"
#       "Design margin: the shield was designed for
#       significantly worse conditions than this"
#
#   - Plot 3: Deceleration (g-load) vs. time
#     X-axis: time from entry interface (0-500 seconds)
#     Y-axis: deceleration (0-10 g)
#     Red curve: g-load experienced by the capsule
#       (and Glenn's body)
#     Peak at approximately 7.7 g at T+210 seconds
#     Horizontal dashed lines at:
#       1 g: "Normal gravity"
#       3 g: "Centrifuge training threshold"
#       7.7 g: "Friendship 7 peak (Glenn reported ~8 g)"
#       11 g: "Mercury design limit"
#     Annotations:
#       "Duration above 6g: approximately 45 seconds"
#       "Glenn's vision: graying at peak g but remained
#       conscious and lucid throughout"
#       "Peak g-load occurs AFTER peak heating -- the
#       capsule is decelerating most rapidly when it is
#       deepest in the atmosphere"
#
#   - Plot 4: Heating rate vs. altitude (the physics plot)
#     X-axis: altitude (0-120 km)
#     Y-axis: stagnation point heat flux (0-1.5 MW/m^2)
#     Red curve: heating rate during descent
#     The curve peaks at approximately 40-50 km altitude,
#       where the product rho^0.5 * V^3 is maximized

#       at lower altitudes, rho is high but V has decreased

#     Annotation:
#       "Peak heating altitude: ~45 km"

#       "This is 1.2 million watts per square meter --
#       equivalent to 1,200 one-kilowatt heaters focused
#       on each square meter of the heat shield"
#       "The V^3 term dominates: halving the velocity

Key learning moments:

  1. Plot 1 shows the thermal miracle: 1,650 degrees C on one side of 3.8 centimeters of material, 65 degrees C on the other. The ablative shield does not block heat — it absorbs it through phase change and ejects the hot products into the airstream. The student sees protection-through-destruction: the shield protects by consuming itself. Every gram of ablated material carries away enormous energy. When the material runs out, so does the protection. Plot 2 shows that Friendship 7 used only 18% of the available shield material — there was substantial margin. But Glenn, watching retropack debris fly past his window, did not know that. He knew only that something was burning and that mission control had told him to keep the retropack attached “for any reason,” which meant they were worried about the shield.
  2. Plot 4 reveals the physics governing when and where peak heating occurs. It is not at the top of the atmosphere (too little air) and not at the surface (too little velocity). It is in the middle, where the product of atmospheric density and the cube of velocity reaches its maximum. This is a universal truth of atmospheric entry: the heating peak always occurs in the same altitude band, regardless of entry vehicle, because the atmosphere’s density profile and the vehicle’s deceleration profile conspire to create a heating maximum at a predictable altitude.

A3. Web: “Friendship 7 Orbital View”

Technology: HTML5 Canvas + JavaScript  |  Target: 1920×1080  |  <1500 lines

What it is: A browser-based real-time orbital simulation of Friendship 7’s three-orbit mission. The student watches the capsule orbit a 2D representation of Earth, with the day-night terminator sweeping across the planet, the “fireflies” particle effect appearing on the night side (the luminescent particles Glenn observed outside his window during the dark-side passes), and a mini-map showing the ground track building up over time. Controls allow time warping, orbit selection, and toggling the Segment 51 scenario (reentry with or without retropack jettison). The simulation culminates in the reentry sequence: retrofire, retropack jettison (or retention), plasma blackout, and parachute deployment.

Why it matters: Friendship 7 was a mission of firsts for America: first orbital sunrise, first orbital sunset, first crossing of the terminator from day into night at 28,000 km/h. Glenn’s famous “fireflies” observation — luminescent particles drifting past the window during the night-side passes — sparked scientific curiosity and public wonder. Later identified as ice crystals sublimating from the capsule exterior, they became one of the iconic images of the Mercury program. This simulation lets the student experience the orbital geometry: the capsule moving through day and night zones, the tracking station coverage appearing and disappearing, the ground track building up orbit by orbit, and the critical reentry decision that defined the mission — whether to jettison the retropack and trust the heat shield, or keep it attached as a mechanical backup.

Tool: ngspice  |  Difficulty: Intermediate

What it is: A SPICE netlist modeling the telemetry circuit that produced the Segment 51 scare during Friendship 7’s reentry. Segment 51 was a telemetry channel that indicated the position of the landing bag — specifically, whether the heat shield’s landing bag had deployed from its stowed position behind the shield. A “deployed” reading meant the heat shield might have come loose. During Glenn’s flight, Segment 51 showed an erratic signal that suggested the landing bag might be deployed, which would mean the heat shield was no longer latched to the capsule. The actual cause was a faulty switch — the landing bag was not deployed, and the heat shield was secure. This circuit models how a switch fault produces a false telemetry reading.

Why it matters: The Segment 51 incident is a case study in sensor ambiguity: a single telemetry bit — deploy/stow — drove a mission-critical decision (retain retropack) that potentially increased risk (retropack debris during reentry) in order to mitigate a perceived greater risk (heat shield separation). The circuit reveals the mechanism: a microswitch with intermittent contact resistance produces a voltage that hovers between the “deployed” and “stowed” thresholds, causing the telemetry encoder to report erratic readings. The fix is engineering: either make the switch more reliable, add a redundant sensor, or design the telemetry system to distinguish between “deployed,” “stowed,” and “uncertain.” Friendship 7 had only two states in its telemetry. The switch existed in a third state. The circuit had no way to say “I don’t know.”

A6. SPICE: Beat Frequency Oscillator — Progressive Radio Circuit #21

Tool: ngspice  |  Difficulty: Intermediate

What it is: A SPICE netlist for a Beat Frequency Oscillator (BFO), the twenty-first circuit in the progressive radio series. The BFO is essential for receiving CW (continuous wave / Morse code) and SSB (single-sideband) signals. In a standard AM receiver, CW signals produce no audio — the carrier is present or absent, but there is no modulated audio content to detect. The BFO injects a locally generated signal at a frequency offset from the incoming carrier. The mixer (or product detector) combines the BFO signal with the received signal, producing an audio beat note at the difference frequency. A 1 kHz offset produces a 1 kHz tone that the operator hears as the Morse code dit-dah pattern.

Why it matters: Mercury used UHF voice communication, but the ground stations also transmitted CW as a backup mode. The BFO is the circuit that makes CW audible. Without a BFO, a CW signal is silence — the receiver’s AGC sees the carrier and adjusts, but no audio emerges because there is no modulation. The BFO is also essential for SSB reception, which became standard for long-range communication after Mercury. This circuit builds on the product detector (v1.18) and the audio CW filter (v1.19) to complete the CW reception chain: BFO generates the local signal, the product detector mixes it with the incoming CW, and the CW filter narrows the audio bandwidth to pass only the beat note.

B. CUDA/GPU Specification

B1. CUDA: N-Body Orbital Perturbation Simulation

Target: NVIDIA RTX 4060 Ti (8GB VRAM)  |  Language: CUDA C++ (or Python with cupy/numba)  |  Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced  |  Duration: 4–6 hours

What it is: A GPU-accelerated simulation of Friendship 7’s orbital trajectory incorporating perturbation forces that a simple Keplerian model ignores. The simulation computes the capsule’s position and velocity at each timestep under the influence of: Earth’s J2 gravitational oblateness (the equatorial bulge), atmospheric drag at orbital altitude, and solar radiation pressure. By comparing the perturbed trajectory to the ideal Keplerian ellipse, the student sees how much the actual orbit deviates from the textbook solution over three revolutions, and which perturbation dominates at Friendship 7’s altitude.

Why it matters: The Mercury orbital tracking network solved the orbit determination problem by radar measurement and ground computation — they did not predict the orbit forward from first principles, they measured it in real time. But the perturbations are real, and they matter: J2 oblateness causes the orbital plane to precess (the RAAN rotates), atmospheric drag causes the orbit to decay (the semi-major axis shrinks), and solar radiation pressure causes a tiny secular drift. Over three orbits these effects are small but measurable. Over hundreds of orbits (as for later orbital missions), they are mission-critical. This simulation uses the GPU to compute the trajectory at high time resolution, showing the student that Kepler’s laws are a beautiful approximation and reality is messier.

CUDA SPECIFICATION: N-Body Orbital Perturbation
================================================

Target: NVIDIA RTX 4060 Ti (8GB VRAM)
Language: CUDA C++ (or Python with cupy/numba)

PHYSICS MODEL:

  The capsule's state vector [x, y, z, vx, vy, vz] is
  integrated forward using a 4th-order Runge-Kutta (RK4)
  integrator. At each timestep, the acceleration is
  computed as the sum of:

  1. Central gravity:
     a_grav = -mu * r / |r|^3
     where mu = 398600.4418 km^3/s^2 (Earth GM)
     r = position vector from Earth center
     This is the Keplerian term -- produces a perfect ellipse.

  2. J2 oblateness:
     The Earth is not a perfect sphere -- it bulges at the
     equator by approximately 21 km. This creates a
     gravitational perturbation:
     a_J2 = -(3/2) * J2 * mu * R_E^2 / |r|^5 *
       [ x * (1 - 5*z^2/|r|^2),
         y * (1 - 5*z^2/|r|^2),
         z * (3 - 5*z^2/|r|^2) ]
     where J2 = 1.08263e-3 (Earth J2 coefficient)
     R_E = 6371 km (Earth mean radius)

     Effect: precesses the orbital plane. RAAN drifts
     approximately -7.5 deg/day for Friendship 7's orbit
     (32.5 deg inclination, ~200 km altitude). Over 3 orbits
     (~4.4 hours), this is approximately -1.4 degrees.
     Small, but measurable by ground tracking.

  3. Atmospheric drag:
     a_drag = -(1/2) * rho * |v_rel|^2 * Cd * A / m * v_hat
     where:
       rho = atmospheric density at orbital altitude
         (from exponential model: rho = 1.225*exp(-h/7.5)
         for h in km -- at 200 km, rho ~ 2.5e-10 kg/m^3)
       v_rel = velocity relative to rotating atmosphere
       Cd = 2.2 (drag coefficient for blunt capsule at
         hypersonic Mach numbers)
       A = 2.806 m^2 (cross-sectional area)
       m = 1360 kg (capsule mass)
       v_hat = unit vector in velocity direction

     Effect: decreases orbital energy, lowering the orbit.
     At 200 km altitude, drag causes approximately 1-2 m
     of altitude loss per orbit for the Mercury capsule.
     Over 3 orbits: ~5 m -- negligible for the mission,
     but over days or weeks the orbit would decay noticeably.

  4. Solar radiation pressure:
     a_srp = -(P_sun / c) * (A_srp / m) * (1 + rho_refl) * s_hat
     where:
       P_sun = 1361 W/m^2 (solar constant)
       c = 3e8 m/s (speed of light)
       A_srp = effective area (varies with sun angle, ~2 m^2)
       rho_refl = 0.3 (reflectivity, rough estimate)
       s_hat = unit vector from capsule to sun

     Effect: extremely small for Mercury's orbit. Included
     for completeness and to show the student that it is
     negligible compared to J2 and drag.

GPU PARALLELIZATION:
  The simulation is parallelized over the timestep
  subdivision: the total trajectory is divided into
  segments, and each GPU thread computes one segment's
  RK4 integration. For this educational simulation,
  the parallelization is straightforward:

  Option A: Parameter sweep
    Launch N threads, each computing the full 3-orbit
    trajectory with a different set of perturbation
    parameters (e.g., different drag coefficients,
    different atmospheric models, different J2 values).
    This produces N trajectories that can be compared
    to the baseline Keplerian orbit.

  Option B: Ensemble
    Launch N threads, each computing a trajectory with
    slightly perturbed initial conditions (within the
    orbit determination uncertainty). This produces
    an ensemble of trajectories that shows how
    sensitive the prediction is to initial state
    uncertainty.

  Option C: High-resolution single trajectory
    Use the GPU to compute a single trajectory at
    extremely high time resolution (dt = 0.01 seconds,
    total = 18,000 seconds, = 1.8 million timesteps).
    This is too many steps for a CPU to compute
    interactively but trivial for a GPU.

OUTPUT:
  - Position vectors at each timestep (x, y, z)
    stored to a binary file for post-processing
  - Comparison data:
    delta_r(t) = |r_perturbed(t) - r_keplerian(t)|
    This is the position error if you used Kepler's
    equations instead of the perturbed model.
  - Summary statistics:
    Total delta-r after 3 orbits
    Maximum delta-r during the trajectory
    Breakdown by perturbation source:
      delta-r from J2 only
      delta-r from drag only
      delta-r from SRP only
    (Computed by running separate simulations with
    each perturbation enabled individually)

VISUALIZATION (post-processing, matplotlib):
  - Plot 1: delta-r vs. time
    X-axis: elapsed time (0-18000 seconds)
    Y-axis: |r_perturbed - r_keplerian| in meters
    Three curves:
      Blue: J2 only (dominant, ~2 km after 3 orbits)
      Green: drag only (~5 m after 3 orbits)
      Red: SRP only (~0.1 m after 3 orbits)
    Shows clearly that J2 dominates by orders of magnitude

  - Plot 2: Orbital element evolution
    Six subplots showing the classical orbital elements
    over 3 orbits:
      a (semi-major axis): slight decrease from drag
      e (eccentricity): slight change from J2
      i (inclination): nearly constant
      RAAN: secular drift from J2 (-1.4 deg over 3 orbits)
      omega (arg perigee): secular drift from J2
      true anomaly: progressing as expected

  - Plot 3: 3D trajectory comparison
    The perturbed and Keplerian orbits plotted in 3D
    (mplot3d). The difference is too small to see at
    orbit scale, so include an INSET showing a zoomed
    section where the trajectories diverge.

Expected results:
  J2 effect after 3 orbits: ~2 km deviation
  Drag effect after 3 orbits: ~5 m deviation
  SRP effect after 3 orbits: ~0.1 m deviation
  Total deviation: ~2 km (J2-dominated)

  The takeaway: for a 3-orbit Mercury mission, Kepler
  is good enough for approximate navigation but the
  tracking network's continuous radar updates were
  essential for precise orbit determination. The
  perturbations accumulate linearly (drag) and
  quadratically (J2 precession) with time, so longer
  missions require increasingly sophisticated models.

Libraries: CUDA toolkit (nvcc), Python+matplotlib
  for post-processing (or cupy for all-GPU)
GPU requirement: any CUDA-capable GPU (the computation
  is modest -- millions of RK4 steps is trivial for
  modern GPUs)
Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced
Duration: 4-6 hours

C. GLSL Shader Specification

C1. GLSL: “Friendship 7 Screensaver”

Target: OpenGL 3.3+ / GLSL 330  |  Expected length: 400–600 lines GLSL  |  60 fps on RTX 4060 Ti at 1920×1080

What it is: A GLSL fragment shader that cycles through four visual modes, each depicting a key phase of the Friendship 7 mission: orbital dawn with Glenn’s luminescent “fireflies,” the three-orbit ground track as sinusoidal curves on Earth’s surface, the violent reentry with plasma flow and burning retropack debris, and the final splashdown in the Atlantic. The shader runs as a continuous screensaver, cycling through modes on a timer, with smooth cross-fade transitions between each phase.

Why it matters: The Mercury missions were the first American spaceflights to be extensively broadcast. The images that defined these missions — the fiery reentry, the capsule on its parachute, the astronaut being hoisted from the water — became part of the American visual vocabulary. This shader recreates those images as procedural art: no textures, no photographs, only mathematics. Every curve, every glow, every particle is generated from equations. The result is not photorealism but something closer to memory: the way these events live in the collective imagination, reduced to light, color, and motion.

D1. Orbital Anomaly Detection from Attitude Telemetry

Libraries: torch or tensorflow, numpy, matplotlib  |  GPU: Optional  |  Difficulty: Intermediate  |  Duration: 3–4 hours

What it is: A machine learning model that classifies Mercury capsule attitude control behavior from simulated telemetry data. The ASCS (Automatic Stabilization and Control System) on Friendship 7 developed a yaw malfunction during the first orbit — the attitude jets fired erratically to correct a perceived yaw error that may not have existed (a sensor fault rather than an actual attitude error). Glenn switched to manual fly-by-wire control for portions of the flight. This model learns to distinguish between normal ASCS operation, sensor-induced false corrections, and genuine attitude drift from labeled training data.

Model: Sequence classifier (LSTM or 1D CNN)

Input:
  - Time series of attitude and jet firing data
  - Window size: 30 seconds at 10 Hz = 300 samples
  - Features per sample:
    - yaw_angle_deg: continuous (-180 to +180)
    - pitch_angle_deg: continuous (-90 to +90)
    - roll_angle_deg: continuous (-180 to +180)
    - yaw_rate_deg_s: continuous (-5 to +5)
    - pitch_rate_deg_s: continuous (-5 to +5)
    - roll_rate_deg_s: continuous (-5 to +5)
    - jet_yaw_left: binary (0 or 1, thruster firing)
    - jet_yaw_right: binary (0 or 1)
    - jet_pitch_up: binary (0 or 1)
    - jet_pitch_down: binary (0 or 1)
    - jet_roll_cw: binary (0 or 1)
    - jet_roll_ccw: binary (0 or 1)
    - hydrogen_peroxide_remaining_pct: continuous (0-100)

Output:
  - P(normal): ASCS operating correctly, jets firing only
    when needed, fuel consumption nominal
  - P(sensor_fault): ASCS responding to erroneous sensor
    readings, jets firing to correct errors that don't exist,
    fuel consumption elevated
  - P(attitude_drift): genuine attitude error (external
    torque), jets firing correctly but unable to maintain
    attitude, increasing error over time
  - P(manual_control): astronaut has taken over, jet
    firings correlated with manual input, ASCS disconnected
  - Confidence: max(probabilities)

Training data:
  Simulated 30-second windows from:
  - Normal ASCS: attitude stable (+/-2 deg deadband), jets fire
    briefly to correct drift, then silence. Fuel consumption
    ~0.5 lb/hour. Jet firings are infrequent and short.
  - Sensor fault: attitude appears to drift in yaw (sensor
    output wanders), ASCS fires yaw jets to correct, attitude
    oscillates around the drifting sensor value. Fuel
    consumption elevated to ~2 lb/hour. The signature is:
    jets fire -> apparent yaw corrects -> sensor drifts again ->
    jets fire again. A limit cycle driven by the sensor error.
  - Attitude drift: genuine external torque (e.g., asymmetric
    venting, solar radiation pressure torque). Attitude drifts
    steadily in one direction. Jets fire to correct but the
    drift returns. Unlike sensor fault, the drift is real
    and monotonic, not oscillatory.
  - Manual control: jet firings correlate with human input
    patterns -- longer burns, less precise timing, occasional
    overcorrections followed by corrections in the other
    direction. The human signature: pulse-pause-pulse, not
    the machine's bang-bang limit cycle.
  - 5,000 windows per class

  LSTM: 2 layers, 64 hidden units each
    Input: 300 x 13 (30s x 10Hz x 13 features)
    Output: FC(128) -> FC(4) -> softmax

  Or 1D CNN: Conv1D(32, k=7) -> Conv1D(64, k=5) ->

  - Normal vs. sensor_fault: ~85% accuracy (the jet firing

    corrections, normal produces sparse corrections)
  - Normal vs. attitude_drift: ~90% accuracy (drift is

  - Sensor_fault vs. attitude_drift: ~75% accuracy (both

  - Manual_control: >95% accuracy (human input patterns are

E. Computer Science — Sensor Ambiguity and Voting Systems

E1. Sensor Redundancy and the Segment 51 Problem

The Segment 51 incident is a textbook example of sensor ambiguity: a single sensor (one microswitch) produced a reading that could not be confirmed or denied by any other sensor. Mission control had to make a life-or-death decision (retain retropack or jettison) based on a reading they could not trust. A redundant sensor architecture — multiple independent sensors measuring the same physical quantity — would have resolved the ambiguity. This section explores the engineering of sensor redundancy, voting systems, and fault-tolerant measurement.

CONCEPT: Sensor Redundancy and Voting

THE PROBLEM:
  Segment 51 provided ONE measurement of the landing bag
  deploy status. That measurement was erratic. Mission
  control could not determine whether:
    A) The landing bag had actually deployed (shield loose)
    B) The sensor had failed (shield secure, switch faulty)

  With one sensor, there is no way to distinguish between
  "the thing being measured has changed" and "the sensor
  has failed." Both produce the same output: an unexpected
  reading.

THE SOLUTION: REDUNDANCY
  If you have THREE sensors measuring the same quantity,
  you can vote:
    - If all three agree: high confidence in the reading
    - If two agree and one disagrees: the disagreeing
      sensor is likely faulty (majority vote)
    - If all three disagree: the measurement system has
      failed; declare "unknown"

  This is called Triple Modular Redundancy (TMR), and it
  is the foundation of fault-tolerant computing in aerospace,
  nuclear power, and medical devices.

  For Friendship 7:
  Scenario: Three independent landing bag switches
    Switch A: stowed
    Switch B: erratic (the actual faulty switch)
    Switch C: stowed
    TMR vote: 2-1 in favor of "stowed" -> HIGH CONFIDENCE
    that the shield is secure. The erratic switch is
    identified as the fault, not the shield.

VOTING ALGORITHMS:
  1. Simple majority vote:
     Output = the value that appears most often
     Requires: at least 3 sensors (2 cannot vote)
     Tolerates: 1 faulty sensor in 3 (or k faulty in 2k+1)

  2. Weighted majority vote:
     Each sensor has a trust weight based on its
     historical reliability. A sensor with a track record
     of correct readings gets a higher weight. A sensor
     that has been erratic gets a lower weight.
     Output = weighted sum of sensor readings, thresholded

  3. Median voter:
     For continuous (analog) measurements, take the
     median of all sensor readings. The median is
     robust to outliers: if one sensor fails high or
     low, the median excludes it.

  4. Byzantine fault tolerance:
     For the worst case: sensors that fail in arbitrary
     ways (not just stuck-high or stuck-low, but
     producing plausible false readings). BFT requires
     at least 3f+1 sensors to tolerate f Byzantine faults.
     For f=1: 4 sensors minimum.
     This is the standard used in aircraft fly-by-wire
     systems (Airbus A320: 3 flight computers + diverse
     software). Mercury used 0 redundant heat shield
     sensors.

IMPLEMENTATION EXERCISE:

  Build a simple TMR voter in any language:

  class TMRVoter:
    def __init__(self):
      self.sensors = [SensorA(), SensorB(), SensorC()]

    def read(self):
      readings = [s.read() for s in self.sensors]
      # For binary sensors (deployed/stowed):
      if readings.count("stowed") >= 2:
        return "stowed", "HIGH", readings
      elif readings.count("deployed") >= 2:
        return "deployed", "HIGH", readings
      else:
        return "unknown", "LOW", readings

  Simulate 10,000 readings with one faulty sensor.
  Count:
    - How many times does the TMR give the correct answer
      when one sensor is faulty? (Expected: ~100%)
    - How many times does the single-sensor system give
      the correct answer with the same fault? (Expected: ~50%)
    - What is the false alarm rate for each system?

  The results demonstrate that TMR converts a 50/50
  ambiguous reading into a near-certain correct reading,
  at the cost of two additional sensors. For a heat
  shield that cost millions of dollars and protected a
  human life, two extra microswitches (cost: approximately
  $5 each) would have resolved the ambiguity that consumed
  most of mission control's attention during Friendship 7's
  reentry.

SOFTWARE APPLICATION:
  Replace "heat shield sensor" with any critical boolean
  check in software:
    - "Is the database connection alive?"
    - "Is the payment gateway responding?"
    - "Is the API rate limit exceeded?"

  A single health check can give a false positive or false
  negative. Three independent health checks, voted, produce
  a reliable answer. The cost of redundancy (3x the health
  checks) is trivial compared to the cost of acting on a
  false reading (unnecessary failover, missed payment,
  service degradation).

  Friendship 7's lesson: one sensor is not a measurement.
  One sensor is a hope. A measurement requires redundancy.

       launching from the water at the base of a falls),
       followed by a silence (100 ms, the fish is airborne),
       followed by a heavy splash (broadband noise burst,
       200 ms, the fish reentering the water above the
       falls). The "launch-silence-splash" signature is
       distinctive and unmistakable.
     - Rain: at T+0:40, a gentle rain begins. Light
       rain on water: very high-frequency broadband
       noise (5-15 kHz), extremely low amplitude,
       continuous. This is the background patter that
       defines a PNW fall day.
     - Forest ambience: cedar and hemlock canopy
       rustling in the breeze (filtered noise at
       2-6 kHz, modulated by a slow random LFO at
       0.05-0.2 Hz). Barely perceptible -- a subtle
       presence that places the listener among trees.

  3. Spawning tributary (T+0:60 to T+1:30):
     - The river sound narrows: center frequency at
       1-2 kHz (small stream, shallow, fast-flowing
       over gravel)
     - Stereo field narrows: the stream is 3-5 meters
       wide, the sound comes from a focused center
     - Volume decreases: less total energy (small stream)
       but the character is more intimate -- the listener
       is close to the water
     - Gravel riffling: a distinctive sound of salmon
       digging redds (nests in the gravel). The female
       turns on her side and beats her tail against the
       streambed, displacing gravel. Sound: a rhythmic
       "thff-thff-thff" at 2-3 Hz, each pulse a
       200 ms burst of filtered noise at 500-1500 Hz
       with a percussive attack. Repeats in bursts
       of 5-10, then silence for 3-5 seconds, then
       another burst.
     - Salmon presence: the splashing becomes more
       frequent and closer -- multiple fish in the
       same reach, tails and dorsal fins breaking the
       surface constantly. Rate: 3-5 splashes per second
       (continuous murmur of fish activity).
     - Rain intensifies slightly (higher amplitude on
       the high-frequency broadband)
     - A single bird call: clear, musical, 2 notes
       descending (1800 Hz -> 1200 Hz, 500 ms total).
       A Pacific Wren or Winter Wren. One call, once,
       at about T+1:15. The listener may or may not
       notice it. But it is there: the forest is
       listening to the salmon as much as the salmon
       is listening to the river.

  4. Final fade (T+1:25 to T+1:30):
     - All elements gradually decrease to silence
     - The last sound to fade is the gravel riffling --
       the salmon are still building nests as the
       listener withdraws
     - The final element: one last rain patter, then
       silence

G. Problem-Solving Methodology — Decision Under Uncertainty

G1. The Segment 51 Decision Framework

The most consequential decision during Friendship 7 was not an engineering decision but a decision-under-uncertainty: should mission control tell Glenn to retain the retropack through reentry (mitigating a potential heat shield problem but adding the risk of retropack debris during reentry), or should they follow standard procedure and jettison it (optimal if the heat shield is secure but catastrophic if it is not)? The decision was made with incomplete information, under time pressure, with a human life at stake. This is a case study in rational decision-making when the data is ambiguous.

METHODOLOGY: Decision Under Uncertainty
(The Segment 51 Framework)

THE SITUATION:
  Mission control has two hypotheses:
    H1: Heat shield is loose (Segment 51 is telling the truth)
    H2: Switch is faulty (Segment 51 is lying)

  Two possible actions:
    A1: Retain retropack through reentry (restrains shield if loose)
    A2: Jettison retropack (standard procedure)

  Four outcomes:
    H1+A1: Shield loose, retropack retained -> retropack
      holds shield in place (partially). Debris during
      reentry but the shield stays. Glenn probably survives.
      Some additional risk from debris.
    H1+A2: Shield loose, retropack jettisoned -> shield
      separates during reentry. Glenn dies.
    H2+A1: Switch faulty, retropack retained -> unnecessary
      debris during reentry (burning retropack straps create
      "flaming chunks" past the window), psychological stress
      on Glenn, but otherwise nominal reentry.
    H2+A2: Switch faulty, retropack jettisoned -> completely
      nominal reentry. No issues.

  The PAYOFF MATRIX:

           |  A1 (retain)         | A2 (jettison)
  ---------+----------------------+------------------
  H1 (loose) | SURVIVE (some risk) | FATAL
  H2 (faulty)| SURVIVE (debris)    | NOMINAL

  The decision is asymmetric:
    - If the shield IS loose, A1 saves Glenn's life.
      A2 kills him.
    - If the switch IS faulty, A1 adds moderate risk
      (debris), A2 is optimal.

  The worst case for A1 (retropack debris, minor risk)
  is immeasurably better than the worst case for A2
  (astronaut death).

  This is a MINIMAX decision: choose the action that
  minimizes the maximum possible loss. A1 minimizes
  the maximum loss because its worst case (debris during
  reentry) is survivable, while A2's worst case (heat
  shield loss) is not.

THE BAYESIAN PERSPECTIVE:
  What is P(H1) -- the probability that the shield is
  actually loose?

  Prior information:
    - The heat shield has never failed on any previous
      Mercury flight (MR-3, MR-4, MA-4, MA-5)
    - The shield is mechanically latched with redundant
      mechanisms
    - Segment 51 is a single sensor with no redundancy
    - The reading is erratic (toggling), not sustained
      (which would be more consistent with an actual
      deployment)
    - Engineers at the Cape are confident in the shield
      design

  Reasonable prior: P(H1) = 0.05 (5% probability
    that the shield is actually loose)
  P(H2) = 0.95 (95% probability that the switch is
    faulty)

  Expected values:
    E(A1) = P(H1)*utility(survive_with_debris) +
            P(H2)*utility(survive_with_debris)
          = 0.05 * 0.9 + 0.95 * 0.95
          = 0.045 + 0.9025
          = 0.9475

    E(A2) = P(H1)*utility(fatal) + P(H2)*utility(nominal)
          = 0.05 * 0.0 + 0.95 * 1.0
          = 0.95

  The expected utility of A1 (0.9475) and A2 (0.95) are
  nearly identical! But this analysis is misleading because
  it treats survival utilities as comparable. The actual
  decision criterion is not expected utility but MAXIMUM
  REGRET:

  If we choose A2 and H1 is true: infinite regret (death)
  If we choose A1 and H2 is true: minor regret (debris)

  No finite probability threshold justifies accepting the
  risk of infinite regret. Even at P(H1) = 0.01, the
  minimax decision is A1 (retain the retropack) because
  the cost of being wrong with A2 is unbounded.

  This is why mission control chose A1. Not because they
  believed the shield was loose. Because the COST OF BEING
  WRONG about a loose shield was unacceptable, regardless
  of how unlikely they judged it to be.

SOFTWARE APPLICATION:
  The Segment 51 framework applies to any decision where:
    1. You have incomplete or ambiguous data
    2. The outcomes are asymmetric (one bad outcome is
       catastrophically worse than the other)
    3. You must decide NOW (there is no time to gather
       more information)

  Examples:
    - "The database backup might be corrupted. Do we
      proceed with the migration or wait?"
      If the backup IS corrupted and we proceed:
        data loss (catastrophic)
      If the backup IS fine and we wait:
        delay (minor)
      Decision: wait (minimax)

    - "The deploy might have a regression. Do we
      roll back or monitor?"
      If there IS a regression and we monitor:
        user impact (bad)
      If there is NO regression and we roll back:
        unnecessary rollback (minor)
      Decision: roll back (minimax)

    - "The security alert might be a false positive.
      Do we rotate credentials or investigate?"
      If it IS real and we investigate:
        extended breach (catastrophic)
      If it IS false and we rotate:
        minor disruption (annoying)
      Decision: rotate (minimax)

  In each case, the minimax decision is the one that
  avoids the catastrophic outcome, even at the cost of
  the minor outcome. The Segment 51 lesson: when the
  data is ambiguous and the stakes are asymmetric,
  protect against the worst case. You can always
  optimize later. You cannot un-die.

H. GSD Improvements — What to Build for gsd-skill-creator

H1. Ambiguity Resolution Chipset

The Segment 51 incident demonstrates the cost of acting on ambiguous data without a framework for resolving the ambiguity. An ambiguity resolution chipset for gsd-skill-creator would detect when an agent’s decision data is ambiguous, flag the ambiguity, and apply a structured resolution process before the agent acts.

CHIPSET: Ambiguity Resolution

Purpose: Detect, flag, and resolve ambiguous inputs to
  agent decision-making BEFORE the agent commits to an
  action. Prevent agents from acting on data that they
  cannot interpret with confidence.

Chips:
  1. ambiguity-detector: Before an agent acts on input
     data (a test result, a file state, a dependency
     version, a user requirement), check whether the
     data is unambiguous. Ambiguity indicators:
     - Contradictory signals (test passes but output
       is wrong -- contradicts the pass)
     - Missing context (file exists but is empty --
       does "exists" mean "ready"?)
     - Erratic values (intermittent test failure --
       is the test flaky or the code buggy?)
     Generate an ambiguity score: 0 (clear) to 1
     (completely ambiguous). If score > threshold,
     flag the agent to STOP and invoke resolution.

  2. voting-resolver: When ambiguity is detected,
     gather additional data sources (redundant sensors).
     For an ambiguous test result: re-run the test 3 times
     and vote on the outcome. For an ambiguous file state:
     check with multiple methods (stat, read, hash).
     For an ambiguous dependency: check multiple package
     registries. Apply TMR voting to resolve the ambiguity.
     Return: resolved value + confidence level.

  3. minimax-advisor: When ambiguity cannot be resolved
     by voting (the data is genuinely uncertain), apply
     the Segment 51 framework: enumerate the possible
     states, enumerate the possible actions, compute the
     worst-case outcome for each action, and recommend
     the action that minimizes the maximum loss.
     This is the decision of last resort: when you
     cannot resolve the ambiguity, protect against the
     worst case.

  4. uncertainty-communicator: When an agent cannot
     resolve ambiguity and must escalate, communicate
     the uncertainty clearly to the coordinator or human:
     - "Segment 51 is erratic. Possible causes: A, B, C."
     - "Recommended action: A1 (minimax). Cost of being
       wrong: [description]."
     - "Additional information needed to resolve: [list]."
     Never present ambiguous data as certain. The circuit
     had only "deployed" and "stowed." The chipset has a
     third state: "uncertain."

Application:
  - Friendship 7's lesson: the system that cannot say
    "I don't know" forces the decision-maker to guess.
    The telemetry circuit that lacked an "uncertain" state
    forced mission control to choose between "the shield
    is fine" and "the shield is loose" -- when the truth
    was "we cannot tell." The ambiguity resolution chipset
    gives agents the ability to say "I don't know" and
    then to do something about it: gather more data,
    vote, or apply minimax reasoning.
  - The cost of ambiguity resolution: seconds of
    additional computation. The cost of acting on
    ambiguous data: potentially catastrophic outcomes.

H2. Salmon Navigation Pattern (Layered Homing)

The Chinook salmon’s layered navigation system — magnetic field for coarse positioning, olfaction for terminal guidance — is a proven biological pattern for navigating through uncertain environments. A layered homing pattern for gsd-skill-creator agents would provide a hierarchical approach to finding and acting on the right information: broad search first (magnetic field), then narrowing (chemical gradient), then precise action (spawning site).

CHIPSET: Salmon Navigation (Layered Homing)

Purpose: Provide agents with a hierarchical navigation
  strategy for finding information, making decisions, and
  completing tasks in large, uncertain codebases.

Chips:
  1. magnetic-map: The coarse positioning system. Before
     an agent begins a task, it orients itself using broad
     cues: the project structure (what directories exist),
     the recent commit history (what has changed recently),
     the test status (what is passing), and the dependency
     graph (what connects to what). These broad signals --
     analogous to the magnetic field -- give the agent a
     general sense of where it is and where it needs to go.
     Output: a "you are here" context map with directional
     recommendations.

  2. scent-trail: The medium-range guidance system. Once
     the agent knows the general area (which module, which
     directory, which subsystem), it follows more specific
     signals: import chains (who imports the target module),
     test files (which tests exercise the target function),
     documentation references (which docs mention the target
     concept). These signals -- analogous to the river plume --
     are unique to the target and get stronger as the agent
     approaches the right code.
     Output: a ranked list of files/functions that are
     increasingly likely to be the agent's target.

  3. natal-stream: The terminal guidance system. When the
     agent is near the target (the right file, the right
     function), it uses precise matching: exact function
     signatures, exact variable names, exact line numbers.
     This is the olfactory match -- the natal stream's unique
     chemical signature. No two functions have the same
     signature. The agent has arrived.
     Output: exact location (file:line) and confidence
     that this is the correct target.

  4. energy-budget: The salmon does not eat during its
     migration. It has a fixed energy budget. The agent
     has a fixed context budget (tokens). The energy-budget
     chip tracks how much context the agent has consumed
     and warns when the budget is getting low. If the agent
     is wasting energy on wrong turns (searching in the
     wrong directories, reading irrelevant files), the
     energy-budget chip redirects it: "You are using 40%
     of your context budget on files that are unlikely to
     be relevant. Reconsider your search strategy."
     This prevents the agent equivalent of a salmon swimming
     up the wrong river: wasting energy on a path that will
     not lead to the spawning ground.

Application:
  - The salmon navigates 3,000 km of featureless ocean and
    finds a 5-meter-wide tributary. The agent navigates
    a 500-file codebase and finds a 10-line function. The
    scale is different. The strategy is the same: broad
    orientation, medium guidance, precise homing.
  - The salmon's navigation stack (magnetic -> olfactory)
    is robust because it is layered: if the magnetic map
    is slightly off, the olfactory system corrects. If the
    agent's broad search is slightly off, the scent-trail
    system corrects. Layered navigation tolerates errors
    at each level because the next level provides correction.
