Deep Research: The Mirror

Echo 1 — the first passive communications satellite, a 100-foot balloon visible to millions

Date: August 12, 1960 Program: Echo (NASA/Langley) Organism: Portuguese man o’ war Dedication: Erwin Schrödinger

A Hundred-Foot Mirror in the Sky

On August 12, 1960, a Thor Delta launch vehicle lifted off from Launch Complex 17A at Cape Canaveral, Florida, carrying in its payload fairing a package that weighed approximately 76 kilograms and occupied, in its stowed configuration, a volume roughly the size of an automobile. What emerged from that fairing, once the rocket’s upper stage had placed it in orbit and the clamshell doors opened, was something unlike any satellite that had preceded it. There were no solar panels. There were no antennas. There were no transponders, no tape recorders, no attitude control thrusters, no computers, no batteries, no instruments of any kind. What emerged was a balloon.

It inflated. Residual air trapped in the folds of the material and a small quantity of sublimating chemicals — benzoic acid and anthraquinone, solids that transition directly to gas at low pressure — expanded into the vacuum of space and pressed the folded skin outward. Within minutes, the crumpled package had become a sphere 30.5 meters in diameter — one hundred feet across, the height of a ten-story building, larger than any structure that had ever been placed in orbit. The skin of the sphere was aluminized Mylar, a polyester film coated on one side with a microscopically thin layer of vaporized aluminum. The film was 12.7 micrometers thick — half a thousandth of an inch, thinner than a human hair, thinner than a sheet of tissue paper, thinner than the cellophane wrapping on a pack of cigarettes. It was, by mass, almost nothing. By surface area, it was enormous. It was a mirror. It was placed in orbit to be a mirror. Its designation was Echo 1, and its mission was to reflect.

That was the entire engineering concept. No amplification, no processing, no retransmission, no active electronics. A ground station would aim a radio signal at the balloon. The signal would travel upward through the atmosphere, cross several hundred kilometers of vacuum, strike the aluminized surface of the balloon, and bounce. Some fraction of the reflected energy would travel back downward, cross the vacuum again, pass through the atmosphere again, and arrive at a receiving station that might be thousands of kilometers from the transmitting station. The balloon contributed nothing to the signal except a surface to bounce from. It was, in the most literal engineering sense, a passive reflector — a mirror for radio waves the same way a bathroom mirror is a mirror for light. Point a flashlight at a mirror and the reflected beam illuminates whatever is on the other side. Point a radio transmitter at Echo 1 and the reflected signal illuminates whatever receiver is on the other side of the continent. The physics is identical. The scale is different. The scale is what makes it remarkable.

The Balloon Man

The idea belonged to William J. O’Sullivan Jr., an engineer at NASA’s Langley Research Center in Hampton, Virginia. O’Sullivan had been thinking about inflatable structures in orbit since the mid-1950s, before Sputnik, before NASA existed, when the organization was still the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics and the concept of placing anything in orbit was still theoretical. O’Sullivan’s original interest was not communications but atmospheric density measurement. A large, lightweight sphere in low Earth orbit would experience atmospheric drag proportional to its cross-sectional area and inversely proportional to its mass. By tracking the orbit of such a sphere and measuring how rapidly it decayed — how quickly the tenuous upper atmosphere slowed it and pulled it downward — you could calculate the density of the atmosphere at orbital altitudes, a quantity that was poorly known in the late 1950s and critically important for predicting the lifetimes of satellites and planning re-entry trajectories.

The insight that made Echo possible was O’Sullivan’s recognition that a sphere large enough to be useful for atmospheric studies would also be large enough to reflect radio signals. The same property that made it a good atmospheric probe — a very large cross-section relative to its mass — also made it a good radar target and, by extension, a good radio reflector. If you were going to put a 30-meter balloon in orbit anyway, you might as well bounce signals off it.

The concept had roots in an earlier experiment. In January 1956, the Naval Research Laboratory had bounced a radio signal off the Moon and received the reflection — a technique called Earth-Moon-Earth communication, or moonbounce. The Moon is an excellent passive reflector: it is large (3,474 kilometers in diameter), it is always there, and its surface reflects radio waves with reasonable efficiency. But the Moon is also 384,000 kilometers away, and the signal must travel that distance twice — up and back — which means the path loss is enormous. The round-trip distance is 768,000 kilometers, and the reflected signal is attenuated by the inverse fourth power of the distance (since the signal spreads as the inverse square on the way up AND the inverse square on the way back). Moonbounce worked, but it required enormous transmitter power and exquisitely sensitive receivers, and the Moon’s orbit meant it was only available for communication between two stations when both could see it simultaneously, which limited the geometry to pairs of stations at similar longitudes or latitudes.

A satellite reflector, orbiting at a few hundred to a few thousand kilometers rather than 384,000, would have vastly less path loss. The round-trip distance would be measured in thousands of kilometers rather than hundreds of thousands. The signal strength at the receiver would be millions of times greater than a moonbounce signal, all else being equal. The satellite could be placed in an orbit that passed over any pair of stations on the Earth’s surface. The concept was sound. The question was whether you could build a balloon big enough and reflective enough to make it work, and whether you could fold it small enough to fit inside a rocket fairing and inflate it reliably in the vacuum of space.

O’Sullivan and his team at Langley spent years developing the inflatable sphere technology. They experimented with different materials, different inflation mechanisms, different folding patterns. They built test spheres and inflated them in vacuum chambers. They solved the problem of rigidization — making the inflated sphere hold its shape after the inflation gas had leaked away through the porous Mylar film. The sublimating chemicals were the key: benzoic acid and anthraquinone, packed as pellets inside the folded balloon, would sublimate in vacuum, producing gas that inflated the sphere, and the gas would eventually leak away, but by that time the Mylar had been stretched into its spherical shape and would hold that shape through its own internal stresses, the way a crumpled piece of aluminum foil, once smoothed out, holds its flat shape. The sphere would wrinkle and dimple over time as micrometeorites punctured it and solar radiation pressure distorted it, but the basic spherical form would persist.

A precursor balloon, Sub-Satellite 1, was launched on a suborbital flight in October 1957, just weeks after Sputnik. It did not reach orbit. A second attempt, Echo 1A, was launched on May 13, 1960, and failed when the Thor-Delta’s attitude control system malfunctioned, causing the vehicle to go off course and the range safety officer to destroy it. Echo 1 — technically the third attempt — succeeded on August 12, 1960, reaching an orbit of 1,519 by 1,687 kilometers with a period of 118.3 minutes and an inclination of 47.2 degrees.

The First Phone Call Through Space

The demonstration began immediately. On the same day as launch, August 12, 1960, engineers at JPL’s Goldstone tracking station in the Mojave Desert of California aimed a radio signal at Echo 1 as it passed overhead. The signal was a pre-recorded message from President Eisenhower — a few sentences about the satellite, the future of communications, the peaceful uses of space. The signal traveled upward, struck the aluminized surface of the balloon, and reflected. At Bell Telephone Laboratories’ facility in Holmdel, New Jersey, a horn antenna — a large, precisely shaped metal horn designed to receive microwave signals with minimal noise — captured the reflected signal, amplified it, and played it back. The President’s voice had traveled from California to New Jersey, a transcontinental distance of approximately 4,000 kilometers, by way of a balloon in orbit.

This was the first transcontinental telephone call relayed by satellite. The word “relayed” requires a careful qualifier: the satellite did not relay in the active sense. It did not receive, amplify, and retransmit. It merely reflected. The distinction matters enormously in engineering terms, because a passive reflector introduces far more signal loss than an active transponder. The signal that left Goldstone was powerful — 10 kilowatts transmitted through a 26-meter dish antenna aimed with tracking precision at the balloon. The signal that arrived at Holmdel was vanishingly faint — attenuated by the spreading of the beam, the imperfect reflectivity of the balloon surface, and the re-spreading of the reflected signal on its way back to Earth.

The physics of passive reflection is governed by the radar equation, which describes the power received from a signal reflected off a target. For Echo 1, the relevant form gives the received power as proportional to the transmitted power, the gain of the transmitting antenna, the radar cross-section of the balloon, and the effective area of the receiving antenna, divided by the fourth power of the distance (since the signal travels the distance twice, each leg contributing an inverse-square attenuation). The factor of distance to the fourth power is the fundamental limitation of passive reflection: doubling the satellite’s altitude reduces the received signal by a factor of sixteen. This is why Echo 1 was placed in a relatively low orbit — higher orbits would have provided better coverage geometry but would have made the signal loss catastrophic.

The radar cross-section of Echo 1 was approximately 74,000 square meters — the physical cross-sectional area of a 30.5-meter sphere. This is enormous by radar standards. A fighter aircraft might have a radar cross-section of a few square meters. A stealth bomber is designed to have a cross-section of less than one square meter. Echo 1 had a cross-section larger than a football field. This was the whole point: make the balloon as large as possible to intercept as much of the transmitted signal as possible and reflect as much of it as possible back toward the ground.

The Holmdel horn antenna that received Echo 1’s reflected signals would achieve even greater fame two years later. In 1964, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, using that same antenna for a radio astronomy survey, detected a persistent, uniform, unexplained noise signal coming from every direction in the sky. They could not eliminate it. They cleaned pigeon droppings from the horn. They checked every connection. The noise persisted. It was the cosmic microwave background radiation — the afterglow of the Big Bang, the remnant thermal radiation from the birth of the universe, cooled by 13.8 billion years of cosmic expansion to a temperature of 2.725 Kelvin. Penzias and Wilson received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1978 for this discovery. The antenna that first heard a President’s voice bounced from a balloon went on to hear the echo of creation itself.

The Brightest New Star

Echo 1 was visible. Not visible in the way that most satellites are visible — a faint point of light, barely discernible, moving slowly across the sky if you know where to look and the conditions are right. Echo 1 was a hundred feet across. Its aluminized surface reflected sunlight with the efficiency of a mirror. At its brightest, it reached an apparent magnitude of approximately negative one — brighter than Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky. It was visible to the naked eye from any location on its ground track, easily spotted, impossible to mistake for a star because it moved, gliding silently across the sky from horizon to horizon in ten or fifteen minutes, a bright, steady point of light following its orbital path with the serene indifference of a celestial body that happened to have been built in Virginia.

Millions of people saw it. In the summer and fall of 1960, looking up and seeing Echo 1 became a popular evening activity. Newspapers published its pass times. Radio stations announced when it would be visible. Families went outside after dinner to watch it cross the sky. For most of these observers, Echo 1 was the first satellite they had ever seen with their own eyes. Sputnik had been audible — its radio beeps were broadcast on every frequency — but it was small and faint, and most people had experienced it only through the radio or the news. Explorer 1 was smaller still. The early Vanguards were tiny. Echo 1 was unmistakable. It was bright. It moved. It was obviously not a star, not a planet, not an airplane. It was something new in the sky, something human-made, something that had not been there before and that everyone could see.

The psychological impact of this visibility should not be underestimated. In 1960, three years into the space age, the concept of artificial satellites was still abstract for most people. They knew satellites existed because they had read about them or heard about them on television. They had not seen one. The gap between knowing something intellectually and experiencing it directly is the gap between reading about the ocean and standing on the beach. Echo 1 closed that gap for millions of people. When you stood in your backyard and watched Echo 1 cross the sky, the space age became real in a way that no newspaper article or television broadcast could achieve. That bright point of light was up there, right now, moving through space at 26,000 kilometers per hour, reflecting sunlight from an aluminized Mylar surface that human beings had folded into a rocket in Florida. The abstraction became concrete. The space age became a thing you could see.

NASA understood the public relations value of Echo 1’s visibility, and they leveraged it skillfully. Echo 1 became one of NASA’s most effective public engagement tools — not because of any deliberate communication campaign, but because the satellite was its own advertisement. You did not need a press release to explain Echo 1. You needed to point at the sky.

The Physics of Bouncing Light

The physics of Echo 1 is the physics of reflection, and the physics of reflection is one of the oldest and most fundamental subjects in optics. When an electromagnetic wave strikes a surface, some fraction of the energy is reflected and some fraction is absorbed or transmitted. For a highly conductive surface like aluminized Mylar, the reflected fraction is large — aluminum reflects approximately 92 percent of incident radio energy at the frequencies used for the Echo experiments. The reflected wave leaves the surface at an angle equal to the angle of incidence, following Snell’s law for a flat surface, or scattering in all directions for a curved surface.

Echo 1’s surface was curved — it was a sphere. When a collimated beam of radio energy strikes a sphere, the reflected energy scatters in all directions from the illuminated hemisphere. This is fundamentally different from a flat mirror, which reflects a collimated beam as another collimated beam. A spherical reflector disperses the reflected energy over a wide solid angle, which means that only a tiny fraction of the reflected energy travels in the direction of any particular receiving station. This geometric spreading is the central inefficiency of passive satellite communication: the balloon reflects the signal, but it reflects it everywhere, and the receiving station captures only the minuscule fraction that happens to be traveling in its direction.

The signal path can be understood through the radar equation. The transmitted power leaves the ground station antenna as a focused beam — focused by the dish antenna into a cone with an angular width determined by the antenna diameter and the signal wavelength. This beam travels upward, spreading slightly as it goes, and illuminates a patch of Echo 1’s surface. The illuminated patch reflects the signal in all directions, like a point source of radio emission located at the satellite’s position. The reflected signal then travels back toward Earth, spreading over an ever-larger area, and the receiving antenna captures whatever fraction of this spreading wave falls within its aperture.

The total path loss is severe. For a typical Echo 1 pass, with the balloon at an altitude of roughly 1,600 kilometers and the transmitting and receiving stations separated by 4,000 kilometers, the received signal was roughly 160 to 170 decibels below the transmitted signal. This means that for every trillion trillion watts transmitted, approximately one watt was received. The Goldstone transmitter operated at 10 kilowatts. The received signal at Holmdel was measured in fractions of a picowatt — trillionths of a watt. Extracting a voice signal from a fraction of a picowatt requires receiver technology at the absolute edge of what is physically possible, limited not by engineering but by the thermal noise of the receiving system itself, the irreducible background of random electromagnetic energy produced by the thermal motion of electrons in the antenna and the receiver electronics.

This is where Bell Labs’ contribution became decisive. The horn antenna at Holmdel was designed specifically for low-noise reception. Its horn geometry minimized the antenna’s sensitivity to ground-reflected signals and atmospheric emission — sources of noise that conventional dish antennas cannot fully reject. The receiver behind the antenna used a maser amplifier — a microwave amplification by stimulated emission of radiation device, the microwave precursor to the laser, which amplified the incoming signal while adding less noise than any other amplification technology available in 1960. The combination of the horn antenna’s low sidelobe response and the maser amplifier’s low noise temperature gave the Holmdel receiving system a sensitivity that was unmatched anywhere in the world. It was this sensitivity that made Echo 1’s reflected signals audible. Without it, the experiment would have produced nothing but noise.

The Proof That Proved Its Own Limits

Echo 1 succeeded brilliantly as a demonstration and failed utterly as a practical system. This dual outcome was understood from the beginning — the engineers who designed the experiment knew that passive reflection would never compete with active transponders for commercial communications. The signal loss was too severe, the bandwidth too limited, the dependence on enormous ground station antennas too restrictive. Echo 1 could bounce a voice signal from California to New Jersey, but it required a 10-kilowatt transmitter and a 26-meter dish on one end and the most sensitive receiver on Earth on the other end. A commercial communications system must work with modest antennas and affordable transmitters. Passive reflection cannot scale to those requirements.

The fundamental problem is the inverse fourth power law. With an active transponder satellite — one that receives a signal, amplifies it, and retransmits it — the path loss follows the inverse square law on the uplink (ground to satellite) and the inverse square law on the downlink (satellite to ground), but the satellite’s amplifier breaks the chain. The uplink signal arrives weak, but the transponder amplifies it to full power before retransmitting. The total path loss is the uplink loss plus the downlink loss, not the product. With a passive reflector, there is no amplifier to break the chain. The signal weakens continuously from transmitter to reflector to receiver. The total loss is the product of the uplink loss and the downlink loss — the inverse fourth power, not the inverse second power. This means that increasing the satellite’s altitude, which improves coverage geometry and allows longer communication windows, also increases the path loss by the fourth power of the altitude increase. The system is caught in a geometric trap: the orbits that provide the best coverage provide the worst signal.

Echo 1 demonstrated this trap clearly. The voice signals bounced off the balloon were thin, noisy, barely intelligible. Television transmission was even more difficult — a coast-to-coast TV signal was achieved, but the image quality was marginal. Data rates were low. Communication windows were short — limited to the few minutes when the balloon was simultaneously visible from both the transmitting and receiving stations. The balloon could not be positioned to hover over a particular region, because it was in a relatively low orbit and moved constantly. There was no way to control which part of the surface faced the ground stations, because the balloon had no attitude control.

All of these limitations pointed toward the same conclusion: the future of satellite communications lay in active transponders, not passive reflectors. Telstar 1, launched in July 1962 by AT&T and Bell Labs, was the direct response to Echo 1’s demonstration. Telstar received signals, amplified them, shifted them to a different frequency, and retransmitted them. The improvement was dramatic. Telstar’s active transponder provided a received signal roughly a billion times stronger than Echo 1’s passive reflection, for the same transmitted power. Syncom 2, launched in 1963, added geosynchronous orbit to active transponder technology, creating a satellite that appeared to hover over a fixed point on the equator, providing continuous communication coverage for an entire hemisphere. The modern satellite communications industry — the geostationary satellites that provide television, telephone, internet, and data services to every part of the globe — descends directly from this lineage: Echo 1 proved the concept, Telstar proved the technology, Syncom proved the geometry, and everything after was refinement.

Echo 1’s contribution was to prove that satellite communication worked at all. Before Echo 1, satellite communication was theoretical. After Echo 1, it was demonstrated. The difference between a theory and a demonstration is the difference between a business plan and a revenue stream. Investors, legislators, military planners, and corporate executives respond to demonstrations. Echo 1 demonstrated that a signal could go up, bounce off something in orbit, and come back down carrying intelligible information. The signal was weak. The system was impractical. The concept was proven. That was enough.

The Wrinkled Mirror

Echo 1 did not remain a perfect sphere. From the moment of inflation, the balloon began to degrade. Micrometeorite impacts punctured the thin Mylar skin, creating tiny holes through which the residual inflation gas leaked. Solar radiation pressure — the gentle but persistent push of sunlight striking the reflective surface — applied a force that, over time, deformed the sphere. The Sun-facing hemisphere was pushed inward while the shadowed hemisphere bulged outward. Thermal cycling between sunlight and Earth’s shadow, occurring every 118 minutes as the balloon orbited, caused the Mylar to expand and contract, gradually fatiguing the material and introducing permanent wrinkles.

The wrinkling was observable from the ground. Radar tracking showed that Echo 1’s radar cross-section changed over time, becoming less consistent as the surface became less smooth. Optical observations showed that the balloon’s brightness varied more erratically as the surface developed irregularities that scattered light in unpredictable directions. The smooth, uniform reflector that had launched in August 1960 gradually became a crumpled, dimpled, irregularly shaped object that still reflected but did so with less geometric predictability.

Despite this degradation, Echo 1 persisted in orbit for nearly eight years. The balloon re-entered Earth’s atmosphere on May 24, 1968, burning up as friction with the thickening atmosphere heated the thin Mylar past its decomposition temperature. By that time, Echo 1 had completed more than 20,000 orbits and had been observed, tracked, and used for communications experiments by ground stations around the world. It had served as a calibration target for radar systems. It had provided data on upper atmospheric density through tracking of its orbital decay. It had been used to test new antenna designs and new receiver technologies. Even degraded, even wrinkled, even imperfect, the giant balloon had continued to serve science for years after its primary communications mission was complete.

A second balloon, Echo 2, was launched on January 25, 1964. Echo 2 was a more sophisticated design — a rigidizable structure that used a laminate of aluminum foil and Mylar, which stiffened once inflated and maintained its shape better than Echo 1’s purely flexible Mylar skin. Echo 2 was 41 meters in diameter, even larger than Echo 1, and was used for both communications experiments and atmospheric density measurements until it re-entered the atmosphere on June 7, 1969.

The Cat in the Orbital Box

Erwin Rudolf Josef Alexander Schrödinger was born on August 12, 1887 — the same calendar date, seventy-three years before Echo 1 launched. He died on January 4, 1961, less than five months after the balloon entered orbit. The coincidence of the launch date is the formal connection, the calendrical thread that links the mission to the dedication. But the deeper connection runs through wave mechanics, through the nature of reflection, through the question of what it means for a signal to be both present and absent.

Schrödinger’s wave equation, published in 1926, describes how quantum systems evolve over time. The equation governs the behavior of the wave function — the mathematical object that encodes everything that can be known about a quantum system. The wave function is not the particle. It is not the position, the momentum, the energy, or any other classical property. It is a function whose absolute square gives the probability of finding the system in a particular state when a measurement is made. Before the measurement, the system exists in a superposition of states — not in one state or another, but in a weighted combination of all possible states simultaneously. The act of measurement collapses the superposition into a definite result. This is the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics, and Schrödinger found it deeply unsatisfying.

His famous thought experiment — the cat — was designed to show the absurdity of the Copenhagen interpretation when applied to macroscopic objects. Place a cat in a sealed box with a vial of poison, a radioactive atom, and a Geiger counter connected to a mechanism that breaks the vial if the atom decays. After one half-life, the atom has a fifty percent probability of having decayed. According to the Copenhagen interpretation, until someone opens the box and observes the system, the atom is in a superposition of decayed and not-decayed, and therefore the cat is in a superposition of alive and dead. The cat is both alive and dead simultaneously, until observation collapses the wave function into one state or the other.

Schrödinger proposed this scenario not because he believed cats could be simultaneously alive and dead, but because he believed the proposition was manifestly absurd, and therefore the interpretation that led to it must be flawed. The thought experiment was a reductio ad absurdum, an argument against the Copenhagen interpretation, not an endorsement of it. But history has a sense of irony, and the popular culture absorbed Schrödinger’s cat not as a critique of quantum mechanics but as its mascot — the emblem of quantum weirdness, the symbol of the counter-intuitive nature of the subatomic world.

Echo 1 is Schrödinger’s cat as a communications system. The signal bounced from the balloon exists in a state of profound uncertainty until it is measured. The transmitter at Goldstone sends the signal upward. The signal strikes the balloon and reflects in all directions. At this moment, the signal is everywhere and nowhere — spread over the surface of an expanding sphere centered on the balloon, traveling outward at the speed of light in every direction simultaneously. The signal is present at every point on that sphere with a probability amplitude given by the geometry of the reflection and the shape of the balloon surface. When the receiver at Holmdel captures a fraction of that signal, the measurement collapses the probability distribution into a definite result: the signal arrived, it carried information, the voice of the President was heard. But the vast majority of the reflected signal — the energy that traveled in every direction except toward Holmdel — was never measured, never detected, never collapsed into a definite result. That signal is still traveling outward, even now, sixty-six years later, an expanding sphere of radio energy centered on Echo 1’s former orbit, growing larger every second, growing fainter every second, carrying the President’s voice at the speed of light into interstellar space.

The resonance between Schrödinger and Echo 1 goes deeper than the wave function metaphor. Schrödinger’s fundamental contribution was the recognition that matter behaves as waves. His wave equation replaced the discrete, particulate quantum mechanics of Heisenberg’s matrix formulation with a continuous, wave-based description that was mathematically equivalent but conceptually different. Schrödinger saw the universe as waves. Echo 1 was a machine for manipulating waves — electromagnetic waves, radio waves, the same fundamental phenomenon as the matter waves that Schrödinger described, differing only in frequency and in the fact that electromagnetic waves do not require a medium while matter waves are properties of matter itself. The balloon was a surface. The signal was a wave. The reflection was a wave phenomenon. The entire mission was wave mechanics at macroscopic scale.

Schrödinger also made fundamental contributions to the thermodynamics of living systems. His 1944 book What Is Life? — based on lectures delivered at Trinity College Dublin — asked how the orderly behavior of living organisms could be reconciled with the second law of thermodynamics, which dictates that the entropy of a closed system increases over time. Schrödinger’s answer was that living organisms maintain their internal order by extracting free energy from their environment and exporting entropy — they are open systems, sustained by a continuous flow of energy and matter through them, maintaining their improbable structure only as long as the flow continues. When the flow stops, the organism reaches thermodynamic equilibrium — death.

Echo 1 was an open system in the same thermodynamic sense. The balloon maintained its structure — its inflated spherical shape — only as long as the inflation gas provided internal pressure and the Mylar skin resisted the forces of solar radiation pressure, thermal cycling, and micrometeorite bombardment. When those sustaining processes became insufficient, the structure degraded. The sphere wrinkled, dimpled, lost its shape. The echo grew faint. The mirror clouded. The system moved toward equilibrium — the atmospheric re-entry of May 1968, when the balloon’s remaining structure was destroyed by aerodynamic heating and the material was scattered as atoms into the upper atmosphere, reaching the ultimate thermodynamic equilibrium of dispersal.
