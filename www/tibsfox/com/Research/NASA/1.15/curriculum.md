## Department Deposits

### Meteorology (Primary)

**Wing:** Satellite Meteorology, Synoptic Observation, Cloud Pattern Recognition
**Concept:** The transformation of meteorology from a ground-based, point-measurement science to a space-based, global-observation science -- accomplished in 78 days by a 120-kg drum with two television cameras

**Deposit:** Before TIROS-1, meteorologists mapped the atmosphere from below. Weather stations on the surface measured temperature, pressure, humidity, and wind at a single point. Radiosondes (balloon-borne instruments) measured the same parameters through the vertical column above the station, twice daily, at approximately 800 stations worldwide. Aircraft provided spot measurements along flight routes. Ship reports filled in the oceans, sparsely. From these scattered points, meteorologists interpolated the three-dimensional state of the atmosphere and drew weather maps -- isobars, fronts, jet stream positions -- by hand.

The fundamental limitation was perspective. A surface observer sees weather locally: the sky overhead, the clouds on the horizon, the barometer reading at this station. Even a network of observers sees weather as a collection of points. The pattern -- the cyclone, the front, the jet stream -- must be inferred from the points. TIROS-1 inverted this: instead of inferring the pattern from the points, it photographed the pattern directly.

The first useful images were extraordinary. TIROS-1 captured:

- **Comma clouds:** The signature of mid-latitude cyclones. Seen from above, a mature cyclone has a distinctive comma shape -- a dense cloud shield wrapping counterclockwise (Northern Hemisphere) around the low-pressure center, with a cold front trailing southwestward as the comma's tail. Meteorologists had predicted this shape from theory (the Norwegian cyclone model, developed in the 1920s by Vilhelm and Jacob Bjerknes), but no one had ever seen it. The first TIROS-1 images confirmed the Norwegian model so precisely that meteorologists could identify the cyclone's stage of development (incipient, mature, or occluded) from the cloud pattern alone.

- **Vortex streets:** When wind flows past an island, the island creates a wake -- alternating vortices that spin off downstream, visible as rows of spiral cloud patterns extending hundreds of kilometers. Von Karman vortex streets had been photographed in laboratory fluid experiments, but TIROS-1 showed them at atmospheric scale for the first time, confirming that the atmosphere obeys the same fluid dynamics as a laboratory wind tunnel.

- **The Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ):** A continuous band of deep convective clouds circling the equator, marking where the northeast trade winds of the Northern Hemisphere meet the southeast trade winds of the Southern Hemisphere. The ITCZ had been mapped from ship observations as a region of light winds, heavy rainfall, and frequent thunderstorms (the "doldrums"). TIROS-1 showed it as a visible, photographable structure -- a bright white band of cloud spanning the entire visible equatorial zone. The continuity of the ITCZ was immediately apparent from orbit but had been impossible to confirm from scattered ship reports.

- **Cloud-free zones:** TIROS-1 revealed that large areas of the subtropical oceans are persistently cloud-free -- the subtropical high-pressure zones, where the descending branch of the Hadley cell suppresses convection. These zones had been known from surface climatology, but their sharpness and stability were surprising: the boundary between cloudy and clear was often knife-edge precise, maintained by the thermodynamic structure of the atmosphere.

The Marbled Murrelet (Brachyramphus marmoratus, degree 15) is a small, chunky seabird of the Pacific coast that nests in old-growth forests but feeds at sea. Its discovery parallels TIROS-1's transformation of meteorology: for over a century, ornithologists knew the Murrelet existed (it was described from specimens collected at sea) but could not find its nest. They searched cliff faces, burrows, rocky shorelines -- the places other seabirds nest. The Murrelet nested 45 meters up in old-growth conifers, on a moss-covered branch, where no one thought to look. The nest was not hidden; it was above the searchers' perspective. TIROS-1 did the same for meteorology: the patterns were not hidden; they were above the observers' perspective. A change in altitude -- from surface to orbit, from ground to canopy -- revealed what had been there all along.

### Photography/Imaging (Primary)

**Wing:** Vidicon Camera Technology, Television from Orbit, Analog Image Capture
**Concept:** How a vacuum tube designed for broadcast television became the first instrument to photograph the Earth from space -- and why the choice of TV over film was the critical design decision

**Deposit:** TIROS-1's cameras were RCA vidicon tubes -- the same technology used in television studio cameras since the late 1940s. A vidicon tube works by the photoconductive effect: a thin film of photoconductive material (antimony trisulfide, Sb2S3) is deposited on a transparent faceplate. Light from the scene passes through a lens, through the faceplate, and strikes the photoconductive film. Where light is bright, the film becomes conductive and discharges; where light is dim, the film remains resistive and retains charge. An electron beam scans across the back of the film in a raster pattern (500 lines, left-to-right, top-to-bottom), and the current flowing through the beam at each point is proportional to the charge at that point, which is proportional to the light that fell on that point. The output is an analog video signal -- a voltage that varies with time as the beam sweeps across the stored image.

TIROS-1 carried two vidicon cameras:

- **Wide-angle camera:** 104-degree field of view, f/1.5 lens, 6.9 mm focal length. This camera covered a ground area of approximately 1,800 x 1,800 km from 700 km altitude -- a panoramic view showing large-scale weather patterns. Resolution at nadir: approximately 3.6 km per TV line. This camera was the meteorological workhorse.

- **Narrow-angle camera:** 12-degree field of view, f/1.5 lens, 59 mm focal length. This camera covered approximately 150 x 150 km -- a telephoto view showing cloud detail. Resolution at nadir: approximately 300 meters per TV line (an order of magnitude finer than the wide-angle camera). This camera was used for studying individual storm structures.

The choice of television (electronic imaging with analog readout) over film (photographic emulsion with physical return) was not obvious in 1960. Film had superior resolution: a 70-mm film frame could record details at 50 line pairs per millimeter, corresponding to a ground resolution of approximately 50 meters from 700 km altitude -- far better than the vidicon's 3.6 km. Film also had superior dynamic range and sensitivity. But film cannot be transmitted electronically. It must be physically returned to the ground.

Film return was technically possible: the CORONA reconnaissance satellite (first success in August 1960, four months after TIROS-1) ejected film capsules that were caught in mid-air by specially equipped aircraft. But CORONA was a classified military program, and the film return capsule was expensive, unreliable (early CORONA missions had a 50% capsule recovery rate), and one-time-use. A weather satellite that dropped film capsules would provide data once per orbit until the capsules ran out. A television camera could transmit images indefinitely.

Wexler's insight was that weather forecasting requires timeliness, not resolution. A cloud image delivered 4 hours after capture is vastly more valuable than a high-resolution film returned 3 days later. Television provided real-time or near-real-time data. Film provided archives. For operational meteorology, television won. The 3.6-km resolution was adequate because clouds are large -- a decision that proved correct and that defined the design philosophy of weather satellites for the next six decades.

### Orbital Mechanics (Primary)

**Wing:** Low Earth Orbit Ground Track, Spin-Stabilization, J2 Perturbation
**Concept:** The orbital dynamics of the first satellite designed to observe the Earth rather than explore space -- and how the orbit determined what the satellite could see

**Deposit:** TIROS-1's orbit was determined by the Thor-Able launch vehicle and the launch site (Cape Canaveral, Florida, 28.5 degrees N). The Thor-Able launched due east to exploit the Earth's rotational velocity (approximately 410 m/s at Cape Canaveral). The resulting orbit had an inclination of 48.4 degrees -- the satellite ground track oscillated between 48.4 N and 48.4 S, never passing over the poles.

This was a severe limitation for a weather satellite. The polar regions are critical to global weather: polar vortex dynamics influence mid-latitude weather patterns, and Arctic and Antarctic sea ice extent affects global climate. A satellite that cannot see the poles cannot provide global weather data.

The orbital parameters:
- Perigee: 693 km (above most of the atmosphere, so drag was negligible)
- Apogee: 756 km (nearly circular, eccentricity 0.0044)
- Period: 99.2 minutes (about 14.5 orbits per day)
- Inclination: 48.4 degrees

The spin-stabilization was critical and limiting. TIROS-1 spun at approximately 12 RPM with its spin axis oriented roughly perpendicular to the orbital plane. The cameras were mounted on the satellite's baseplate -- pointing in the direction of the spin axis. As the satellite orbited, the spin axis remained approximately fixed in inertial space (conservation of angular momentum). This meant that the cameras pointed toward Earth only during the portion of the orbit where the spin axis happened to face downward. For roughly half the orbit, the cameras pointed away from Earth -- toward space.

The duty cycle was further reduced by the spin itself: the cameras swept past the Earth during each rotation, but could only capture an image during the fraction of the rotation when they pointed within their field of view of the ground. Combined, these factors meant that TIROS-1 could usefully image the Earth for approximately 25% of its orbit -- wasting 75% of its observing time.

The spin-stabilization was nonetheless the right choice. In 1960, three-axis stabilization (keeping the cameras pointed at Earth at all times) would have required horizon sensors, reaction wheels, and control electronics -- technologies that had not yet been proven in orbit. Spin-stabilization was simple and reliable: set the satellite spinning during deployment, and angular momentum conservation maintains the orientation. The penalty was reduced observing time. The reward was a satellite that worked for 78 days without a single pointing failure.

### Marine Biology (Secondary)

**Wing:** Scyphozoa -- Aurelia aurita Life Cycle, Jellyfish Blooms, Planktonic Ecology
**Concept:** The moon jellyfish as a model of passive atmospheric observation -- a transparent organism that drifts with the currents, responding to the same oceanographic forces that weather satellites observe from above

**Deposit:** Aurelia aurita -- the moon jellyfish -- is the most widespread and well-studied scyphozoan in the world's oceans. Found in every temperate and tropical coastal ecosystem, from Puget Sound to the North Sea, from the Gulf of Mexico to the Sea of Japan, Aurelia is a cosmopolitan drifter with a two-stage life cycle that connects the ocean floor to the surface waters:

- **Polyp stage (benthic):** The polyp is a small (2-5 mm), sessile organism attached to hard substrates -- rocks, pilings, the undersides of docks, artificial reefs. It feeds on microscopic plankton using tentacles, and reproduces asexually by budding. The polyp can persist for years, surviving winter cold that would kill the medusa. It is the population's memory -- the seed bank.

- **Strobilation:** In spring, when water temperature rises above approximately 10-15 degrees C (the threshold varies by population), the polyp undergoes strobilation: it segments into a stack of disc-shaped ephyrae, like a stack of coins, and releases them one by one into the water column. Each ephyra is a miniature medusa, approximately 2 mm in diameter, that grows into the familiar bell-shaped adult over 2-3 months.

- **Medusa stage (planktonic):** The adult medusa is the form people recognize: a translucent bell 10-40 cm in diameter, with four horseshoe-shaped gonads visible through the transparent mesoglea. The medusa feeds on zooplankton (copepods, larval fish, other small gelatinous organisms) by trailing mucus-covered oral arms that trap prey and transport it to the central mouth. Aurelia is a pulsatile swimmer -- it contracts its bell rhythmically, jetting water downward and propelling itself upward and forward. But its swimming speed (1-5 cm/s) is far slower than ocean currents (10-100 cm/s), so the medusa is effectively a drifter, carried by currents and tides to wherever the water goes.

- **Bloom dynamics:** In favorable conditions (warm water, abundant zooplankton, low predation from sea turtles and ocean sunfish), Aurelia populations can explode into blooms of millions of individuals, sometimes dense enough to clog seawater intake screens of coastal power plants and desalination facilities. These blooms are visible from aircraft and, in extreme cases, from satellite ocean-color sensors.

The connection to TIROS-1 is through observation and drift. Aurelia drifts with the currents as TIROS-1 drifted in its orbit -- both are carried by forces they cannot control (ocean currents, orbital mechanics). Both are transparent to the medium they occupy (Aurelia is 95% water, effectively invisible in the ocean; TIROS-1's 120-kg mass was negligible in the context of its orbit, perturbed only by the faintest environmental forces like J2 oblateness). And both are instruments of observation: TIROS-1 observed the atmosphere that drives the ocean currents that carry Aurelia; Aurelia's population dynamics integrate the oceanographic conditions that TIROS-1's descendants now monitor from orbit. The jellyfish is a biological sensor for the same system the satellite measures electronically.

### Information Theory (Secondary)

**Wing:** Shannon Entropy, Image Compression, Channel Capacity
**Concept:** How much information is in a weather photograph, and how efficiently did TIROS-1 transmit it?

**Deposit:** TIROS-1 transmitted analog television images over an FM radio link. The information content of each image can be quantified using Shannon's framework, as detailed in the mathematics file (Deposit 2). The key numbers:

- Raw data per image: approximately 1.5 Mbit (500 x 500 pixels, 6 bits per pixel)
- Shannon entropy per image: approximately 375 kbit (spatial redundancy reduces the actual information content by 4:1)
- Channel capacity of the FM link: approximately 666 kbps
- Effective data rate: approximately 300-500 kbps

TIROS-1 operated at approximately 50-75% of the Shannon limit -- a reasonable efficiency for 1960 FM technology.

The deeper lesson: TIROS-1's images contained approximately 375 kbit of Shannon information each, but the meteorological information was far less. A trained meteorologist looking at a TIROS-1 image would extract perhaps a dozen pieces of actionable information: "cyclone at 45N 60W, stage mature, moving northeast at 30 knots; cold front from 45N 60W to 35N 75W; ITCZ at 8N; subtropical clear zone 20-30N Pacific." This meteorological summary is perhaps 500-1000 bits -- a compression ratio of approximately 375:1 from Shannon entropy to actionable information. The image is a rich source of data; the meteorological interpretation extracts a tiny fraction. Modern machine learning (see Paper 1 in papers.md) automates this interpretation, closing the gap between raw image data and meteorological information.

### Music/Composition (Secondary)

**Wing:** Rachmaninoff -- Symphonic Weather, Musical Dynamics as Atmospheric Dynamics
**Concept:** Sergei Rachmaninoff's orchestral writing as an analog for atmospheric energy and weather dynamics -- the buildup, release, and aftermath of musical and meteorological storms

**Deposit:** Sergei Rachmaninoff was born on April 1, 1873, in Novgorod, Russia -- 87 years to the day before TIROS-1 launched. He died on March 28, 1943, in Beverly Hills, California. His music -- the Piano Concertos, the symphonies, the Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini, the Symphonic Dances -- is characterized by sweeping melodic lines, massive orchestral climaxes, and a harmonic language that builds tension through chromatic movement and resolves it through triumphant or elegiac cadences.

The connection to TIROS-1 is through the dynamics of energy buildup and release -- the fundamental process that drives both weather and Rachmaninoff's music.

A mid-latitude cyclone follows a life cycle:
1. **Genesis:** Warm, moist air from the south meets cold, dry air from the north along a boundary (the polar front). The temperature contrast creates potential energy.
2. **Intensification:** The potential energy converts to kinetic energy as the warm air rises and the cold air sinks. Winds strengthen. Clouds deepen. The comma signature appears.
3. **Climax:** The cyclone reaches maximum intensity. Wind speeds peak. Precipitation is heaviest. The central pressure reaches its minimum. The cloud shield is most extensive.
4. **Occlusion:** The cold front overtakes the warm front. The temperature contrast that powered the cyclone diminishes. The storm weakens. Clouds dissipate. Skies clear.

Rachmaninoff's Piano Concerto No. 2 in C minor follows a parallel arc:
1. **Opening chords:** Slow, quiet, building -- like the initial perturbation on the polar front. Each chord adds a harmonic interval, increasing the tension incrementally.
2. **First theme development:** The melody emerges, carried by the piano against swelling strings. The dynamics rise. The texture thickens. Countermelodies appear, interweaving like converging air masses.
3. **Climax:** The full orchestra erupts in a fortissimo statement of the theme. The piano thunders octaves in the bass while the brass section carries the melody. The sound is massive, enveloping, irresistible.
4. **Resolution:** The energy dissipates. The strings sustain a chord while the piano plays a final, quiet cadence. The storm has passed. The air is clear.

TIROS-1 photographed the visual manifestation of this process: the cloud patterns that trace the energy flow in the atmosphere. Rachmaninoff scored the auditory analog: the orchestral textures that trace the energy flow in music. Both are driven by the same principle -- energy accumulates, reaches a critical threshold, releases catastrophically, and the system relaxes to a new equilibrium. The physicist calls it instability. The meteorologist calls it a cyclone. The composer calls it a concerto.

The Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini (1934) offers a more precise analog to satellite observation. The work consists of 24 variations on a single theme -- Paganini's Caprice No. 24 for solo violin. Each variation transforms the theme: rhythmically, harmonically, melodically, texturally. Variation XVIII (the famous "Eighteenth Variation") inverts the theme -- turns it upside down -- and produces one of the most recognizable melodies in classical music, which had been hidden in the original theme all along. This is what TIROS-1 did to meteorology: it inverted the perspective -- turned weather observation upside down, from below to above -- and revealed patterns that had been hidden in the data all along. The comma cloud was there before TIROS-1. The vortex street was there. The ITCZ was there. They were encoded in the surface observations, as the Eighteenth Variation melody was encoded in Paganini's caprice. Inversion revealed them.

## TRY Sessions

### TRY 1: Time-Lapse Cloud Photography

**Duration:** 4-8 hours (intermittent, over one day)
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Meteorology / Photography
**What You Need:** A camera (phone is fine) with time-lapse capability or a tripod, a fixed location with a clear view of the sky (rooftop, field, hilltop), weather conditions with active clouds (not overcast, not clear). Total cost: $0 (using a phone camera).

**What You'll Learn:**
How cloud patterns evolve over hours, mimicking what TIROS-1 observed from orbit over minutes of each pass. You will photograph the sky from a fixed location at regular intervals and create a time-lapse showing cloud motion, formation, and dissipation.

**Entry Conditions:**
- [ ] Understand that clouds are three-dimensional structures with a visible base (condensation level) and a top
- [ ] Can identify basic cloud types: cumulus (puffy, fair-weather), stratus (flat, layered), cirrus (high, wispy), cumulonimbus (tall, thunderstorm)
- [ ] Have a camera and a location with a view of the sky

**The Exercise:**

```
TIME-LAPSE CLOUD PHOTOGRAPHY:

1. SETUP:
   Choose a day with interesting weather: scattered cumulus
   (fair-weather clouds that grow and dissipate), approaching
   front (watch for cloud type changes), or convective day
   (small clouds in the morning growing into tall towers by
   afternoon).

   Mount your camera (or phone) on a tripod or stable surface
   pointing at the sky. Include some horizon for reference
   (trees, buildings, mountains). The horizon anchors the
   image and makes cloud motion visible.

   Settings:
   - Auto exposure (let the camera adjust to changing light)
   - Fixed white balance (manual, daylight) -- prevents
     color shifts between frames
   - Highest resolution available
   - If using phone time-lapse: set interval to 5-10 seconds
   - If shooting manually: take a photo every 1-5 minutes

2. OBSERVE FOR 4-8 HOURS:
   Start in the morning (9-10 AM) and continue until evening
   (4-6 PM). This captures the full diurnal convective cycle:
   - Morning: surface heating begins, small cumulus form
   - Midday: convection strengthens, clouds grow taller
   - Afternoon: maximum instability, tallest clouds,
     possible thunderstorms
   - Evening: heating stops, clouds dissipate as convection
     shuts down

   Take notes every hour:
   - Cloud types visible
   - Estimated cloud base height (use landmarks for scale)
   - Wind direction (from cloud motion)
   - Temperature and humidity (if you have instruments)
   - Any precipitation

3. REVIEW YOUR TIME-LAPSE:
   Assemble images into a video (most phone cameras do this
   automatically; otherwise use ffmpeg or any video editor).
   Play at 10-30x speed.

   Look for:
   - GROWTH: Small cumulus growing into larger clouds.
     This is convection -- warm air rising, cooling,
     condensing. Each cloud is a thermal plume.
   - MOTION: Clouds move with the wind at their altitude.
     Low clouds may move in a different direction than
     high clouds (wind shear). TIROS-1 observed this
     routinely: cloud layers at different altitudes
     moving in different directions, revealing the
     vertical structure of the wind field.
   - STREETS: If the wind is steady and the atmosphere
     is unstable, cumulus clouds may organize into
     parallel rows (cloud streets) aligned with the
     wind direction. Von Karman would recognize the
     fluid dynamics. TIROS-1 photographed cloud streets
     from orbit -- you're seeing the same physics from
     below.
   - ANVILS: If convection is strong enough, cumulus
     towers may reach the tropopause (10-12 km altitude
     in mid-latitudes) and spread horizontally into
     anvil-shaped cirrus shields. This is the hallmark
     of deep convection -- the atmospheric process that
     drives thunderstorms, hurricanes, and the ITCZ.

4. CONNECT TO TIROS-1:
   Your time-lapse shows weather from below: clouds
   forming, growing, moving, and dissipating.

   TIROS-1 showed weather from above: cloud patterns
   covering hundreds of thousands of square kilometers,
   revealing organization at scales invisible from the
   ground.

   Your observation: "I see cumulus clouds forming in
   the morning and growing through the afternoon."
   TIROS-1's observation: "The cumulus over this entire
   region are organized into streets aligned with the
   prevailing wind, with clear lanes between them,
   covering an area from Portland to Denver."

   Same physics, different perspective, different insight.
   Your time-lapse is the ground truth for what TIROS-1
   saw from orbit.

EXTENSION: Compare your time-lapse to current satellite
imagery for the same day and location. GOES imagery is
available free at https://www.star.nesdis.noaa.gov/GOES/.
Find the image that includes your location at the same
time as your photos. Your ground-level observation and
the satellite's orbital observation are two views of the
same atmospheric event.
```

### TRY 2: Aurelia aurita Observation (Coastal/Aquarium)

**Duration:** 1-2 hours
**Difficulty:** Beginner (OBSERVE ONLY -- do not touch wild jellyfish)
**Department:** Marine Biology
**What You Need:** Access to a coast where moon jellyfish occur (Puget Sound, Chesapeake Bay, Gulf Coast, Mediterranean, North Sea, Sea of Japan -- Aurelia is found almost everywhere in temperate and tropical coastal waters), OR a public aquarium with an Aurelia exhibit (most major aquariums maintain moon jellyfish). Notebook, camera. Total cost: $0 (beach) or aquarium admission.

**What You'll Learn:**
How to identify Aurelia aurita and observe its pulsation rhythm, feeding behavior, and response to light and current. The moon jellyfish is the simplest multicellular predator you will ever see: no brain, no bones, no blood, yet it captures prey, digests food, reproduces, and has thrived for 500 million years.

**The Exercise:**

```
AURELIA OBSERVATION:

1. IDENTIFICATION:
   Aurelia aurita field marks:
   - Bell: flat to slightly domed, translucent, 10-40 cm
     diameter in adults. Margins scalloped with 8 indentations
     (the rhopalia -- sense organs that detect light, gravity,
     and chemical gradients)
   - Gonads: four horseshoe-shaped structures visible through
     the bell. Color varies by sex and maturity: white to
     pink (female with brooded larvae) or purple-blue (male).
     The horseshoes are the most reliable field mark -- no
     other common jellyfish has four symmetrical horseshoes.
   - Oral arms: four long, frilly appendages hanging from
     the underside, surrounding the central mouth. Used for
     feeding: mucus on the arms traps zooplankton and
     transports it to the mouth by ciliary action.
   - Color: mostly transparent, with a slight blue-white or
     yellow-brown tinge. Coloration comes from ingested prey
     and from symbiotic algae (in some populations).

2. OBSERVE PULSATION:
   Count the bell pulsation rate:
   - Beats per minute: typically 10-20 for adults in
     still water. Varies with temperature (faster in
     warmer water) and body size (smaller = faster).
   - Is the rhythm regular or irregular?
   - Does the rate change when the jellyfish encounters
     a current or another jellyfish?

   The pulsation drives both locomotion and feeding.
   Each contraction jets water downward, propelling the
   bell upward and forward. The water expelled from
   beneath the bell also flows across the oral arms,
   carrying food particles to the mouth.

3. OBSERVE FEEDING:
   If the water contains visible zooplankton (tiny
   specks moving in the water -- copepods, larval
   crustaceans), watch for:
   - Prey contacting the oral arms or bell margin
   - Mucus strands on the oral arms (may be visible
     as slight thickening or cloudiness)
   - Particles being transported along the arms toward
     the central mouth
   - Food accumulation in the gastric pouches (visible
     as darkening inside the bell)

4. OBSERVE RESPONSE TO LIGHT:
   If at an aquarium, note:
   - Does Aurelia swim toward or away from light?
   - Does pulsation rate change with lighting?
   Aurelia has photosensitive rhopalia (not eyes) and
   shows negative phototaxis in some conditions (swims
   away from bright light) and positive phototaxis in
   others (swims toward dim light), likely related to
   following zooplankton that migrate toward the surface
   at night (diel vertical migration).

5. CONNECT TO TIROS-1:
   Aurelia is 95% water -- nearly invisible in the ocean.
   TIROS-1 photographed clouds, which are condensed water
   vapor -- water made visible by the atmosphere.

   Both Aurelia and clouds are water in different states
   of visibility:
   - A cloud is water vapor that has condensed on
     nucleation particles, becoming visible
   - Aurelia is liquid water organized into a living
     body, barely distinguishable from its medium

   TIROS-1 could see clouds but not jellyfish. Modern
   ocean-color satellites can sometimes detect jellyfish
   blooms (as changes in water turbidity). The progression:
   see clouds (1960) -> see ocean color (1978, CZCS) ->
   see jellyfish (2010s, MODIS/VIIRS). Each step is a
   refinement in the ability to distinguish water-in-
   different-forms from orbit.

SAFETY: If observing on the beach, do not touch jellyfish.
   Aurelia's sting is mild to humans (most people feel
   nothing or a slight tingle), but individual sensitivity
   varies, and misidentification with more dangerous species
   (Chrysaora, Pelagia, Cyanea) is possible. Observe from
   a distance. Photograph. Leave no trace.
```

### DIY 1: Satellite Ground Station Display -- Receive NOAA APT Images

**Duration:** 4-8 hours (build and first reception)
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Department:** Communications Engineering / Meteorology / Information Theory
**What You Need:** An RTL-SDR USB dongle (~$25-35), a V-dipole or turnstile antenna (built from wire, ~$5 in materials), a computer with SDR software (free: SDR#, GQRX, or WXtoImg), coaxial cable, and a clear view of the sky (no tall buildings blocking the horizon). Total cost: $30-50.

**What You'll Build:**
A ground station that receives real-time weather satellite images directly from NOAA polar-orbiting satellites (NOAA-15, NOAA-18, NOAA-19) as they pass overhead. These satellites transmit Automatic Picture Transmission (APT) images at 137 MHz -- a system that has been operational since TIROS-8 in 1963 and uses nearly identical principles to TIROS-1's image transmission. You will decode the FM signal into a visible image showing cloud patterns, coastlines, and temperature data over your region, delivered directly from orbit to your antenna.

```
SATELLITE GROUND STATION:

1. BUILD THE ANTENNA:
   The simplest APT antenna is a V-dipole:

   Materials:
   - Two pieces of stiff copper or aluminum wire,
     each 53.4 cm long (1/4 wavelength at 137 MHz)
   - A coaxial cable with the right connector for your SDR
   - A mounting point (PVC pipe, wooden dowel, or a camera
     tripod)

   Construction:
   - Strip the coaxial cable: inner conductor to one wire
     element, shield/braid to the other
   - Angle the two wires at 120 degrees apart (60 degrees
     from horizontal on each side) for optimal pattern
   - Mount horizontally, pointed at the sky

   For better results, build a turnstile antenna:
   - Two crossed dipoles, fed 90 degrees out of phase
   - Provides circular polarization matching the satellite's
     signal
   - Plans available at numerous amateur radio websites

2. SET UP THE SDR:
   - Plug the RTL-SDR dongle into a USB port
   - Install software:
     Windows: SDR# (sdrsharp.com) + WXtoImg (retired but
     still available)
     Linux: GQRX + noaa-apt or SatDump
     Mac: CubicSDR + SatDump
   - Tune to the satellite's frequency:
     NOAA-15: 137.620 MHz
     NOAA-18: 137.9125 MHz
     NOAA-19: 137.100 MHz
   - Set FM demodulation, bandwidth ~34 kHz

3. TRACK THE SATELLITE:
   - Use a satellite tracking app or website:
     Heavens-Above (heavens-above.com)
     N2YO (n2yo.com)
     Gpredict (free, Linux/Mac/Windows)
```
