v1.6 — Explorer 6 + Trametes versicolor

The First Photograph of Earth from Orbit

Date: August 7, 1959 Program: Explorer Organism: Turkey tail fungus Bird: Pacific Slope Flycatcher Dedication: Vincent van Gogh Status: Complete

Mission Summary

Explorer 6 was launched on August 7, 1959, into a highly elliptical orbit (237 km x 42,400 km) carrying the first comprehensive suite of space physics instruments. On August 14, 1959, its single photocell — spinning at 2.8 rpm behind a narrow slit — produced the first photograph of Earth from orbit: a crude, 100-line image of a sunlit crescent over the central Pacific, transmitted at one bit per second. Simultaneously, its fluxgate magnetometer recorded the ring current — 5 million amperes flowing westward at 3–5 Earth radii — confirming Chapman and Ferraro’s 1930s prediction. Explorer 6 was both camera and magnetometer, both photographer and physicist, the first spacecraft to see Earth and feel the magnetosphere in the same orbit. The paired organism, Trametes versicolor (turkey tail), decomposes dead wood through the same incremental sampling that Explorer 6 used to decompose Earth’s image into angular brightness data.

Dedication: Vincent van Gogh

This mission is dedicated to Vincent Willem van Gogh, born March 30, 1853, in Groot-Zundert, Netherlands. Van Gogh built images from discrete brushstrokes — each carrying color, direction, and intensity — the same way Explorer 6 built an image from discrete brightness samples swept by a spinning photocell. His late paintings survive extreme downsampling because their composition is low-frequency: large swirling forms, bold contrast, broad color blocks. Explorer 6’s photograph survived extreme downsampling for the same reason: Earth’s disk against space is a fundamentally low-frequency image. Both demonstrate that coarse, discrete samples can represent continuous reality when the observer stands at the right distance. The Starry Night (1889) and the first photograph of Earth from orbit (1959) are the same act: making the invisible visible through patient, incremental observation.
