## Department Deposits

### Engineering (Primary)

**Wing:** Orbital Mechanics and Propulsion Restart
**Concept:** The parking orbit technique, Agena upper stage restart failure, and the engineering challenge of propellant management in zero gravity

**Deposit:** Ranger 1 established and then failed at a technique that became standard for all subsequent lunar and planetary missions:
- Parking orbit: Atlas-Agena A placed Ranger 1 in a 160 km circular orbit before the planned translunar injection burn -- the first American deep space mission to use this technique
- Agena A restart failure: the Bell 8081 engine could not reignite in zero gravity due to propellant settling problems. UDMH and IRFNA, the hypergolic propellants, did not reach the combustion chamber cleanly
- Propellant management in microgravity: without gravity to hold liquids at the bottom of tanks, pressurant gas mixed with propellants, causing incomplete combustion or no ignition
- Ullage motors: small thrusters designed to settle propellants before restart were insufficient on Agena A. The Agena B (Rangers 3+) redesigned this system
- The parking orbit paradox: the technique enables flexible launch windows and system checkout before committing to deep space, but requires engine restart -- a capability the US had not yet demonstrated reliably
- Every Apollo mission used the parking orbit technique that Ranger 1 pioneered. The S-IVB's J-2 engine reignited after orbital checkout for translunar injection
- Seven days of spacecraft operation in the wrong orbit: JPL extracted engineering data on bus performance, thermal cycling, attitude control propellant consumption, and communication link quality from a mission that should have lasted weeks in deep space

### Physics (Primary)

**Wing:** Orbital Decay and Atmospheric Drag
**Concept:** The physics of orbital decay at low altitude -- how residual atmosphere drains energy from a spacecraft and causes reentry

**Deposit:** Ranger 1's seven-day orbital life demonstrated the physics of orbital decay in the 160 km altitude regime:
- At 160 km, the atmosphere is not vacuum. Molecular density is approximately 10^11 molecules/cm^3 -- enough to produce measurable aerodynamic drag on a 900 kg vehicle
- Drag force: F_drag = 0.5 * rho * v^2 * C_d * A, where rho is atmospheric density, v is orbital velocity (~7.8 km/s), C_d is the drag coefficient (~2.2 for a tumbling satellite), and A is the cross-sectional area
- Energy loss per orbit: the drag force removes kinetic energy, lowering the orbit. Lower orbits have higher atmospheric density, creating a positive feedback loop -- decay accelerates as the orbit drops
- Ranger 1 decayed from 160 km to reentry in approximately 7 days. At 200 km, the lifetime would have been weeks. At 300 km, months. At 500 km, years. The exponential relationship between altitude and atmospheric density creates a sharp cliff in orbital lifetime below 200 km
- The spacecraft's orbital period was approximately 91 minutes. In 7 days, it completed roughly 110 orbits -- each one slightly lower, slightly faster (due to the lower orbit), and slightly shorter in period
- The decay was not uniform: atmospheric density varies with solar activity, day/night cycle, latitude, and season. Ranger 1 launched during a period of moderate solar activity (solar cycle 19 was past peak but still active), which heated and expanded the upper atmosphere

### Botany (Primary)

**Wing:** Desert Ecology in Temperate Climates
**Concept:** Opuntia fragilis (brittle prickly pear) -- a desert cactus surviving in the Pacific Northwest through microhabitat exploitation and CAM photosynthesis

**Deposit:** The organism pairing links Ranger 1's survival in the wrong orbit to the cactus's survival in the wrong climate:
- Opuntia fragilis is the northernmost cactus in the Western Hemisphere, found as far north as 52°N in British Columbia
- In western Washington, it grows on glacial outwash prairies, rocky balds, and south-facing outcrops where microclimate contradicts the regional wet climate
- CAM photosynthesis: stomata open only at night, fixing CO2 into malic acid, which is decarboxylated during the day for use in the Calvin cycle. Water loss is reduced by approximately 90% compared to C3 plants
- Vegetative reproduction via pad fragmentation: the name "fragilis" refers to the ease with which pads detach -- this is the primary reproductive strategy, not a weakness
- Survival at -40°C: unlike tropical cacti, O. fragilis tolerates extreme cold through intracellular dehydration -- water moves out of cells before freezing, preventing ice crystal damage to membranes
- The cactus is a pioneer species on bare rock, initiating succession in microhabitats too extreme for any other vascular plant

### Literature (Secondary)

**Wing:** Edgar Lee Masters and the Literature of Entrapment
**Concept:** The parallel between Ranger 1 trapped in its parking orbit and the characters of *Spoon River Anthology* trapped in their small-town lives

**Deposit:** Masters' characters speak from their graves, telling truths they could not tell in life:
- *Spoon River Anthology* (1915): 200+ free-verse monologues from the dead of a fictional Illinois town
- Each character is trapped: by marriage, profession, poverty, respectability, or the gravitational field of community expectation
- The connection to Ranger 1 is structural: the spacecraft was trapped in an orbit it was never meant to occupy, circling Earth with systems designed for deep space, transmitting data from the wrong destination
- Masters himself was trapped in a legal career before *Spoon River* achieved literary escape velocity at age 46 -- and his subsequent work never matched it, a one-burn escape followed by literary orbital decay
- The parking orbit as metaphor: a temporary state that becomes permanent when the injection burn fails

### Mathematics (Secondary)

**Wing:** Exponential Decay and Orbital Lifetime
**Concept:** The exponential relationship between altitude and atmospheric density, and its consequences for orbital decay rate

**Deposit:** Orbital decay is governed by the exponential atmosphere:
- Atmospheric density follows approximately rho(h) = rho_0 * exp(-h/H), where H is the scale height (~8.5 km near the surface, ~50-80 km at orbital altitudes)
- The orbital decay rate is proportional to density: da/dt ∝ -rho(a) * v^2, where a is the semi-major axis
- At 160 km (Ranger 1): lifetime ≈ 7 days. At 200 km: ~2-4 weeks. At 400 km: ~1-2 years. At 600 km: ~25+ years
- The doubling/halving: every 50 km drop in altitude roughly halves the remaining orbital lifetime in this regime
- Ranger 1's decay curve is exponential in character: slow at first (days 1-4), then accelerating (days 5-7), then terminal

### History (Secondary)

**Wing:** The Ranger Program and NASA's Crisis of Confidence
**Concept:** Ranger 1 as the first in a sequence of six consecutive failures that precipitated Congressional investigation and institutional restructuring at JPL

**Deposit:** The institutional context of Ranger 1's failure:
- JPL had succeeded with Explorer 1 (1958) and Pioneer 4 (1959) -- simple, small, spin-stabilized spacecraft
- Ranger represented a leap: 300+ kg, three-axis stabilized, solar-powered, midcourse-correctable -- a real spacecraft
- The Atlas-Agena A was an Air Force vehicle optimized for reconnaissance satellites, not NASA lunar probes
- Institutional friction: NASA depended on Air Force launch vehicles, managed by Air Force program offices, with military priorities taking precedence over civilian science
- Rangers 1-6 all failed (August 1961 -- February 1964), triggering Congressional investigation (Karth Committee, 1964)
- The recovery (Rangers 7-9, 1964-1965) returned 17,000+ photographs of the lunar surface and validated JPL's spacecraft engineering

### TRY 1: Calculate Orbital Lifetime from Altitude

**Duration:** 25 minutes
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Physics / Mathematics

**What You'll Learn:**
How atmospheric drag at low altitude determines how long a spacecraft survives in orbit, and why Ranger 1's 160 km parking orbit was a death sentence.

**The Exercise:**

```python
import math

# Atmospheric density model (simplified exponential)
# Source: US Standard Atmosphere 1976

def atm_density(h_km):
    """Atmospheric density at altitude h (km) in kg/m^3"""
    # Scale height varies with altitude; simplified model
    if h_km < 150:
        H = 22.0  # scale height (km)
        rho0 = 2.0e-9  # reference density at 150 km
        return rho0 * math.exp(-(h_km - 150) / H)
    elif h_km < 300:
        H = 35.0
        rho0 = 2.0e-9
        return rho0 * math.exp(-(h_km - 150) / H)
    else:
        H = 55.0
        rho0 = 2.0e-9 * math.exp(-(300 - 150) / 35.0)
        return rho0 * math.exp(-(h_km - 300) / H)

# Orbital lifetime estimate (Harris-Priester simplified)
def orbital_lifetime_days(h_km, mass_kg, area_m2, Cd=2.2):
    """Rough orbital lifetime estimate"""
    mu = 3.986e14  # Earth gravitational parameter (m^3/s^2)
    R_earth = 6.371e6  # meters
    r = R_earth + h_km * 1000
    v = math.sqrt(mu / r)  # orbital velocity
    rho = atm_density(h_km)
    
    # Energy dissipation rate
    P_drag = 0.5 * rho * v**3 * Cd * area_m2  # watts
    # Orbital energy
    E_orbit = -mu * mass_kg / (2 * r)
    # Rough lifetime: E / P_drag
    lifetime_s = abs(E_orbit) / P_drag if P_drag > 0 else float('inf')
    return lifetime_s / 86400  # convert to days

print("ORBITAL LIFETIME vs ALTITUDE")
print("=" * 60)
print(f"{'Altitude':>10} | {'Density':>12} | {'Lifetime':>15} | {'Note'}")
print("-" * 60)

# Ranger 1 parameters
mass = 900  # kg (spacecraft + Agena)
area = 5.0  # m^2 (approximate cross-section)

for alt in [160, 180, 200, 250, 300, 400, 500, 600, 800]:
    rho = atm_density(alt)
    life = orbital_lifetime_days(alt, mass, area)
    note = ""
    if alt == 160: note = "← RANGER 1 (actual: ~7 days)"
    if alt == 200: note = "← ISS minimum safe"
    if alt == 400: note = "← ISS typical"
    
    if life < 1:
        life_str = f"{life*24:.1f} hours"
    elif life < 365:
        life_str = f"{life:.0f} days"
    else:
        life_str = f"{life/365:.1f} years"
    
    print(f"{alt:>7} km | {rho:>12.2e} | {life_str:>15} | {note}")

print()
print("Below 200 km: days to weeks. The atmosphere wins quickly.")
print("Ranger 1 had no choice. The Agena wouldn't restart.")
print("The orbit was a prison with an expiration date.")
```

**What Just Happened:**
You proved that the difference between 160 km and 400 km altitude is the difference between days and years of orbital life. Ranger 1 at 160 km had approximately 7 days. If the parking orbit had been 50 km higher, the spacecraft might have survived weeks, giving engineers more time to attempt workarounds. But 160 km was selected because it was the altitude the Agena could reach while reserving propellant for the planned restart -- the restart that never happened.

### TRY 3: Simulate Agena Engine Restart with a Syringe Experiment

**Duration:** 15 minutes
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Department:** Engineering / Physics

**What You'll Learn:**
Why propellant settling in zero gravity prevented the Agena A from restarting. A simple syringe experiment demonstrates the problem.

**The Exercise:**

```
MATERIALS:
  - Clear plastic syringe (no needle), 10-20 mL
  - Water
  - Cooking oil
  - Small rubber stopper or tape for the tip

PROCEDURE:
  1. Fill the syringe halfway with water (= propellant)
  2. Add a small air bubble (= pressurant gas)
  3. Seal the tip

  STEP A: "Normal gravity" (hold syringe tip-down)
    - Water settles to the bottom (tip end)
    - Air bubble rises to the top (plunger end)
    - Push the plunger: water comes out cleanly
    - THIS IS THE AGENA DURING POWERED FLIGHT

  STEP B: "Zero gravity" (hold syringe horizontally, shake gently)
    - Air bubble mixes with water
    - Water clings to walls, air distributes randomly
    - Push the plunger: mixture of air and water sputters out
    - THIS IS THE AGENA IN THE PARKING ORBIT

  STEP C: "Ullage burn" (tap the syringe tip-down before pushing)
    - A few taps settle the water back to the tip end
    - Push the plunger: cleaner flow, but not perfect
    - THIS IS WHAT THE AGENA'S ULLAGE MOTORS TRIED TO DO

THE LESSON:
  In zero gravity, you cannot assume liquids stay at the bottom
  of their container. The Agena A's propellant management system
  did not adequately settle the UDMH and IRFNA before restart.
  Result: the engine received a mix of propellant and pressurant
  gas. No combustion. No restart. Mission trapped in parking orbit.
```

## DIY Projects

### DIY 1: Arduino Orbital Decay Simulator

**Duration:** 3-4 hours
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Cost:** ~$25

**Materials:**
- Arduino Nano ($8) + SSD1306 OLED ($8) + 8 LEDs ($2) + pot ($1) + breadboard ($5)

**What You Build:**
An orbital decay simulator. A potentiometer sets the initial altitude (100-600 km). Eight LEDs represent the orbit -- they light up in sequence to show the spacecraft circling Earth. As atmospheric drag lowers the orbit, the LED circle speeds up (shorter orbital period) and the OLED shows altitude dropping, density increasing, and drag force growing. At reentry altitude (~80 km), all LEDs flash and go dark. The potentiometer lets the student see how dramatically initial altitude affects lifetime: 160 km → 7 days, 400 km → years.

### DIY 2: Build a Cactus Terrarium with Opuntia

**Duration:** 1-2 hours
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Cost:** ~$15

**Materials:**
- Small glass container or open terrarium ($5-10)
- Cactus potting mix (mostly sand/perlite) ($3)
- Opuntia humifusa or Opuntia fragilis if available ($3-5 at a nursery)
- Small rocks for drainage layer

**What You Build:**
A microhabitat terrarium that recreates the conditions *Opuntia fragilis* needs: excellent drainage, minimal organic matter, maximum solar exposure. Place the terrarium on a south-facing windowsill. Water once every 2-3 weeks. The cactus will grow slowly -- a few millimeters per month -- demonstrating the extreme patience of desert organisms. If a pad detaches (it will), place it on the soil surface. It will root. You have just reproduced the *Opuntia fragilis* dispersal strategy.

### DIY 3: Model Rocket with Staged Flight Simulation

**Duration:** 4-6 hours
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Cost:** ~$45

**Materials:**
- Estes model rocket kit with two-stage capability ($30-40)
- Launch pad and controller (included in kit)
- Altimeter or phone barometer app

**What You Build:**
A two-stage model rocket that demonstrates the concept of staging and upper-stage ignition. The first stage (booster) lifts the rocket. The second stage (sustainer) ignites at burnout of the first stage. The exercise teaches why staging is necessary for reaching high altitude/velocity and why second-stage ignition is a critical event. Compare to Ranger 1: the Atlas (first stage) worked perfectly. The Agena (second stage) reached orbit but could not reignite for the injection burn. The model rocket's staging event is the analog: if the sustainer doesn't ignite, the rocket falls back -- a miniature Ranger 1.
