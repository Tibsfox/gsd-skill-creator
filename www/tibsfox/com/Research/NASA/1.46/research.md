# Mission 1.46 -- Surveyor 1 (Atlas-Centaur AC-10)

## Deep Research: Soft Landing

**Mission:** 1.46 / NASA Mission Series
**Date:** May 30, 1966 (launch) -- July 14, 1966 (final contact)
**Type:** Lunar soft-lander (photographic survey)
**Program:** Surveyor (NASA / Jet Propulsion Laboratory / Hughes Aircraft Company)
**Epoch:** 1 (Early Space Age)
**Organism Pairing:** Melospiza melodia (Song Sparrow)
**Bird (Degree 45):** Song Sparrow
**Dedication:** Surveyor engineering team at Hughes Aircraft Company
**Status:** SUCCESS -- first US spacecraft to achieve soft landing on the Moon; 11,237 photographs transmitted from Oceanus Procellarum

## The Three-Point Landing

At 06:17:37 UTC on June 2, 1966, Surveyor 1 touched down on the surface of the Moon in Oceanus Procellarum -- the Ocean of Storms -- at coordinates 2.45 degrees south latitude, 43.22 degrees west longitude. Three aluminum honeycomb landing pads, each mounted at the end of a tubular aluminum leg fitted with an articulated shock absorber, contacted the lunar regolith and held. The spacecraft did not sink. It did not tip. It did not bounce. It sat on the Moon's surface, upright and stable, its solar panel drinking sunlight, its planar antenna pointed at Earth, its survey television camera ready to photograph the landscape around it.

The landing was so gentle -- final touchdown velocity approximately 3.4 meters per second, well within the design limit of 4.6 m/s -- that the shock absorbers barely compressed. The three footpads left shallow impressions in the regolith, each approximately 5 centimeters deep. The surface bore the weight. The spacecraft, which weighed approximately 292 kilograms on Earth and 48.5 kilograms in the one-sixth lunar gravity, rested on soil that neither collapsed nor crumbled beneath it.

This was the question that had haunted lunar mission planners since the beginning of the space program: Could you land on the Moon? Not could you reach it -- Rangers had reached it by crashing into it at 2.6 km/s. Could you land on it gently enough to survive, and would the surface support you when you got there? Thomas Gold's deep-dust hypothesis, which posited that the lunar maria were covered in meters-thick unconsolidated powder that would swallow a lander, had been partially addressed by Ranger 7's photographs (which showed a cratered, solid-looking surface) and by Luna 9's landing four months earlier (the Soviet spacecraft had soft-landed on February 3, 1966, transmitting photographs that showed a solid surface). But Surveyor 1 provided the definitive American answer: the surface was firm. The regolith was a thin layer of granular material -- a few centimeters to perhaps a meter -- overlying solid basalt bedrock. You could land on it. A spacecraft could survive. By extension, an astronaut could walk on it.

## The Descent

Surveyor 1 launched from Cape Kennedy Launch Complex 36A at 14:41:01 UTC on May 30, 1966, aboard an Atlas-Centaur launch vehicle (AC-10). The Atlas first stage and Centaur upper stage performed nominally, injecting the spacecraft onto a direct-ascent translunar trajectory -- no parking orbit, no restart. The Centaur second burn placed Surveyor 1 on a path to the Moon with an arrival time of approximately 63 hours.

During the 63-hour transit, two midcourse correction maneuvers refined the trajectory. The spacecraft was spin-stabilized during coast, rotating at approximately 2.3 RPM around its thrust axis for passive thermal control and gyroscopic stability. Star sensors (Canopus tracker) and sun sensors provided attitude reference.

The terminal descent sequence -- the critical phase that had to work perfectly or the spacecraft would crater -- began approximately one hour before landing. The sequence was entirely automatic. No human controlled the descent in real time; the round-trip communication delay of approximately 2.6 seconds (Earth to Moon and back) made real-time piloting impossible. The spacecraft's own sequencer, programmed before launch, executed every step.

**Phase 1 -- Main retro burn (altitude ~75 km, velocity ~2,600 m/s):** At approximately 75 kilometers above the lunar surface and descending at approximately 2.6 km/s, the spacecraft's solid-propellant main retrorocket ignited. This was a large, single-burn motor -- a Thiokol TE-364-2 -- designed to remove the bulk of the approach velocity. The motor burned for approximately 40 seconds, decelerating the spacecraft from 2,600 m/s to approximately 110 m/s. The motor casing, now dead weight, was jettisoned by explosive bolts at burnout.

**Phase 2 -- Vernier engine descent (altitude ~11 km, velocity ~110 m/s to ~3.4 m/s):** Three small, throttleable vernier engines -- liquid-propellant rockets burning monomethyl hydrazine and mixed oxides of nitrogen (MON-10) -- took over after the main retro was jettisoned. These engines were the precision instruments: throttleable from approximately 30 to 104 newtons each, gimbaled for attitude control, and controlled by a radar altimeter and a Doppler velocity sensor (RADVS -- Radar Altimeter and Doppler Velocity Sensor). The RADVS measured altitude and descent rate, feeding data to the autopilot, which commanded the vernier engines to reduce velocity along a preprogrammed descent profile.

The vernier descent lasted approximately 2.5 minutes -- a controlled fall from 11 kilometers to the surface, with the engines continuously throttling to match the commanded descent rate. The radar altimeter's accuracy improved as altitude decreased, providing progressively more precise altitude data.

**Phase 3 -- Touchdown (altitude ~3.4 m, velocity ~1.5 m/s):** At approximately 3.4 meters above the surface, the RADVS detected that altitude had dropped below the cutoff threshold. The vernier engines shut down. The spacecraft fell the remaining distance under lunar gravity alone (1.62 m/s squared), impacting the surface at approximately 3.4 m/s -- a gentle drop, roughly equivalent to jumping off a one-meter-high wall on Earth. The shock absorbers in the three landing legs absorbed the impact.

The entire descent, from main retro ignition to touchdown, lasted approximately 6 minutes. Six minutes to slow from 2,600 m/s to 3.4 m/s, to transform a ballistic projectile into a stationary science platform. The sequence worked on the first try.

## The Deep Dust Question

Before Surveyor 1, the bearing strength of the lunar surface was one of the most consequential unknowns in the Apollo program. If the surface could not support the weight of a Lunar Module (approximately 2,300 kg landing mass in one-sixth gravity, producing a footpad pressure of roughly 7 kPa), then the entire Lunar Orbit Rendezvous architecture was fatally flawed.

The concern was not irrational. Thomas Gold, a Cornell astrophysicist of considerable reputation, had argued since the late 1950s that the lunar maria were covered in a deep layer of fine dust -- meteoritic debris and micrometeorite-pulverized regolith accumulated over billions of years without the winnowing effects of wind or water. Gold's models suggested dust depths of meters to tens of meters in some mare regions. A spacecraft landing on such a surface would sink, potentially disappearing entirely.

Gold's hypothesis was controversial but could not be dismissed without data. Earth-based radar reflections from the lunar surface suggested a relatively rough surface at centimeter to meter scales, consistent with either a rocky surface or a granular surface with embedded rocks. Ranger's photographs had shown craters at all scales, suggesting a solid subsurface, but photographs could not directly measure bearing strength. The Soviet Luna 9 landing in February 1966 had provided a single data point -- one spacecraft, one landing site, one successful surface contact -- but a single data point from a Soviet mission, with limited published engineering data, was not sufficient for Apollo planning.

Surveyor 1 provided the comprehensive answer. The landing itself was the primary bearing-strength measurement: the spacecraft's three footpads, each with a contact area of approximately 0.03 square meters, supported a total lunar weight of approximately 475 newtons (292 kg Earth weight / 6 = 48.7 kg lunar weight * 9.8 m/s squared, then corrected for lunar gravity: 48.7 * 1.62 = 78.9 N per pad approximately). The footpad sinkage of approximately 5 centimeters indicated a surface bearing strength of at least 3-5 kPa under the static load conditions.

Later Surveyor missions (particularly Surveyor 3 and Surveyor 7, which carried soil mechanics surface samplers) would provide more detailed measurements. But Surveyor 1's simple act of sitting on the Moon without sinking was the critical existence proof: the surface was solid enough to land on. Apollo could proceed.

## 11,237 Photographs

The survey television camera on Surveyor 1 was a single 25mm vidicon tube camera mounted on a mast approximately 1.5 meters above the surface. The camera could pan 360 degrees in azimuth and from +40 degrees (above horizontal) to -60 degrees (below horizontal) in elevation, providing a panoramic survey of the surrounding terrain. The camera used a rotating filter wheel (red, green, blue, and clear filters) for color imaging and a variable-focus lens system that could resolve features as small as approximately 1 millimeter at the spacecraft's base and several centimeters at distances of 10-20 meters.

Between June 2 and July 14, 1966, Surveyor 1 transmitted 11,237 photographs of the lunar surface to the Deep Space Network's receiving stations. The images revealed:

**Local terrain:** The landing site was on a relatively flat plain within Oceanus Procellarum, gently sloping toward the northeast. The surface was covered with fine-grained regolith -- particles ranging from submillimeter powder to centimeter-scale pebbles. Small craters (1-10 meter diameter) were scattered across the visible landscape. Larger rocks (5-50 cm) were present but not abundant. The surface was navigable -- no boulder fields, no steep slopes, no crevasses.

**Surface texture:** The close-up images showed a granular surface with a complex microstructure. Individual grains were visible at the camera's resolution limit. The surface had a slightly cohesive quality -- small clumps of regolith held together, and the footpad imprints showed cleanly defined edges rather than the flowing collapse that would characterize a truly unconsolidated powder. The regolith behaved more like damp sand than like dry flour.

**Horizon features:** The distant horizon (approximately 2.5 km away due to the Moon's smaller radius and the flat terrain) showed a gently rolling landscape with subdued crater rims and no visible bedrock outcrops. The absence of atmosphere meant the horizon was sharp and the distant features were as clear as the nearby ones -- no haze, no atmospheric scattering, no blue distance.

**Earth and sky:** Some images captured the Earth hanging above the lunar horizon -- a half-illuminated disk in a black sky. The lunar daytime sky was absolutely black: no scattered light, no atmospheric glow. Stars were not visible in the images because the camera exposure was set for the brightly sunlit surface, but the contrast between the lit ground and the black sky was absolute.

**Shadows and photometry:** The shadow patterns in the images provided indirect information about surface roughness. Long shadows during early and late lunar morning (Surveyor 1 operated through one complete lunar day, which lasts approximately 14 Earth days) revealed small surface features -- millimeter-scale bumps and depressions -- that were invisible in the overhead-sun photographs. The photometric properties of the surface (how it reflected light as a function of illumination and viewing angle) were measured from the multi-filter images, providing data that constrained models of regolith particle size and composition.

## The Spacecraft

Surveyor 1 was designed and built by Hughes Aircraft Company's Space Systems Division in El Segundo, California, under contract to JPL. The spacecraft was a triangular aluminum framework approximately 3 meters tall and 4.3 meters across the deployed landing legs. It weighed 995.2 kilograms at launch (including the main retrorocket) and 292 kilograms on the surface after retro jettison and propellant depletion.

The design was driven by a single requirement: survive the landing and operate on the surface. Every system served this goal:

**Power:** A single flat solar panel (approximately 0.85 square meters of solar cells) provided 85 watts of electrical power during lunar day. A non-rechargeable silver-zinc battery provided power during the 14-day lunar night and during the descent when the solar panel was not optimally oriented. The thermal environment on the lunar surface is brutal: approximately +130 degrees Celsius in direct sunlight, approximately -170 degrees Celsius during the lunar night. Surveyor 1 survived the first lunar night and resumed operations on the second lunar day (July 7-14), though at reduced performance due to thermal degradation of some components.

**Communications:** A planar array antenna (high-gain, directional) transmitted image data to Earth at approximately 4,400 bits per second on S-band (2295 MHz). A smaller omnidirectional antenna provided low-rate command and telemetry. The communication link operated through JPL's Deep Space Network -- the same network that had received Ranger 7's final photographs two years earlier.

**Thermal control:** Passive thermal control using reflective surfaces, thermal blankets, and strategic placement of heat-generating electronics. The spacecraft had no active cooling. Thermal survival through the lunar night depended on sufficient insulation to prevent critical components from dropping below their minimum operating temperatures. Surveyor 1 was designed for a minimum survival temperature of approximately -157 degrees Celsius, achieved by insulating the electronics compartments and pre-charging the thermal mass during the lunar day.

**Landing system:** Three landing legs with crushable aluminum honeycomb shock absorbers, deployed before landing by spring-loaded mechanisms. The footpads were large, flat disks designed to distribute the landing load over a sufficient area to prevent sinking in low-bearing-strength soil. The three-point landing geometry was inherently stable -- the spacecraft could not tip over unless the terrain slope exceeded approximately 12 degrees on a flat surface or unless one leg struck a rock or crater rim that placed the center of gravity outside the support triangle.

## The Surveyor Program

Surveyor 1 was the first of seven Surveyor missions, five of which were successful:

| Mission | Date | Result | Landing Site |
|---------|------|--------|-------------|
| Surveyor 1 | June 2, 1966 | SUCCESS | Oceanus Procellarum |
| Surveyor 2 | September 22, 1966 | FAILURE | Vernier engine failed; crashed |
| Surveyor 3 | April 20, 1967 | SUCCESS | Oceanus Procellarum (Apollo 12 site) |
| Surveyor 4 | July 17, 1967 | FAILURE | Lost contact during descent |
| Surveyor 5 | September 11, 1967 | SUCCESS | Mare Tranquillitatis |
| Surveyor 6 | November 10, 1967 | SUCCESS | Sinus Medii |
| Surveyor 7 | January 10, 1968 | SUCCESS | Near Tycho crater |

The program's cumulative contribution to Apollo was immense. Five successful soft landings at five different locations across the near side provided:
- Surface bearing strength data confirming the surface could support the Lunar Module
- 87,000+ photographs of the surface at resolutions from panoramic to millimeter-scale
- Chemical composition data from alpha-scattering experiments (Surveyors 5, 6, and 7)
- Soil mechanics data from the surface sampler (Surveyors 3 and 7)
- Thermal data confirming survivability of electronics through the lunar day-night cycle

Surveyor 3 achieved particular historical significance when Apollo 12 landed approximately 183 meters from it in November 1969. Astronauts Pete Conrad and Alan Bean walked to the Surveyor, photographed it, and removed several components -- including the television camera -- for return to Earth. The camera was analyzed for micrometeorite damage and potential biological contamination (the question of whether Earth microorganisms on the spacecraft had survived three years in the lunar vacuum), providing the only sample-return data from an unmanned lunar spacecraft.

## Hughes Aircraft Company

The dedication for this mission goes to the Surveyor engineering team at Hughes Aircraft Company -- the people who designed, built, and tested the spacecraft that proved the Moon could be landed on.

Hughes Aircraft Company was founded by Howard Hughes in 1932 as a division of the Hughes Tool Company. By the 1960s, it had evolved from an aviation company into one of the premier aerospace electronics and spacecraft manufacturers in the United States. The Space Systems Division, located in El Segundo, California (adjacent to Los Angeles International Airport), was responsible for the Surveyor program.

The Surveyor team faced an engineering challenge of extraordinary precision. The spacecraft had to:
1. Survive launch loads (up to 10 g acceleration, sustained vibration)
2. Operate in deep space for 63 hours (thermal extremes, vacuum, radiation)
3. Execute an autonomous descent sequence with zero margin for error
4. Land gently on a surface of unknown bearing strength
5. Operate for weeks in the lethal thermal environment of the lunar surface

The team's approach was characterized by redundancy where possible and testing where redundancy was impossible. The vernier engine system was triple-redundant (three engines, any two of which could control the descent). The communication system had redundant transmitters. The camera had a single vidicon tube (a single-point failure) but the tube was the most thoroughly tested vidicon ever built, having survived thousands of hours of qualification testing.

The names of the engineers are largely lost to the published record -- they appear in internal Hughes technical reports, in JPL contract documentation, and in the memories of their colleagues. They were not astronauts. They were not famous. They built a machine that sat on the Moon and proved it could be done. The Apollo astronauts who landed three years later landed on knowledge that the Surveyor team had provided.
