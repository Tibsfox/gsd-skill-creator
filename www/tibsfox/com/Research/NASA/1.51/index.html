Surveyor 2 — The Midcourse Correction

The small adjustment that determines everything

Launch: September 20, 1966 Program: Surveyor (Mission 2 of 7) Impact: September 23, 1966 · SE of Copernicus Degree: 50 of 360

Mission Failure

On September 20, 1966, an Atlas-Centaur launched Surveyor 2 from Cape Kennedy toward the Moon. The launch was flawless. The translunar trajectory was nominal. Sixteen hours and eleven minutes after launch, JPL commanded the midcourse correction: all three vernier engines to fire simultaneously, adjusting the spacecraft’s path toward its landing site at Sinus Medii. Vernier engines 1 and 2 ignited normally. Vernier engine 3 did not. The asymmetric thrust sent the spacecraft into an uncontrollable tumble. Multiple recovery attempts over the following hours failed. Surveyor 2 impacted the Moon on September 23, southeast of Copernicus crater, at approximately 6,000 miles per hour. Total loss. No surface data returned.

This is the first failure in the NASA Mission Series. It is documented here with the same depth and rigor as the successes, because the failure taught the Surveyor program where its fragility lived — and Surveyors 3, 5, 6, and 7 succeeded partly because Surveyor 2 did not.

Mission Timeline

September 20, 1966 — Launch
12:32 UTC: Atlas-Centaur AC-7 lifts off from LC-36A, Cape Kennedy. Atlas booster stage nominal. Centaur stage nominal — first burn achieves parking orbit, second burn achieves translunar injection. Surveyor 2 separates from Centaur at 13:04 UTC. Solar panel deployed. High-gain antenna pointed at Earth. Telemetry nominal at 4,400 bits per second. The launch vehicle that nearly killed the Centaur program through years of development failures performs without error.
September 20–21 — Translunar Coast
Sixteen hours of nominal flight. Deep Space Network stations at Goldstone, Madrid, and Canberra track the spacecraft continuously. Trajectory data confirms a midcourse correction is needed to reach the planned landing site at Sinus Medii. The correction is calculated: a small delta-v, a few meters per second, applied in the correct direction.
September 21 — T+16h11m — Midcourse Correction FAILURE
~04:43 UTC: JPL commands midcourse correction. Spacecraft reorients to correct attitude. Vernier ignition commanded. Engines 1 and 2 ignite. Engine 3 does not. Asymmetric thrust creates immediate torque. Spacecraft begins to tumble. Rotation rate increases. Attitude control system overwhelmed. Telemetry becomes intermittent as the tumbling antenna sweeps past Earth. Multiple recovery commands transmitted over following hours — fire all three engines, shut down and stabilize, timed individual burns. Engine 3 never responds. The tumble is locked in. Battery depletes. Solar panel orientation lost.
September 23 — Lunar Impact
~03:18 UTC: Surveyor 2 impacts the lunar surface southeast of Copernicus crater (~5.5°N, 12°W) at approximately 2,700 m/s. The spacecraft is destroyed. No data is returned from the lunar surface. The impact site is far from the planned landing site of Sinus Medii. Without the midcourse correction, the spacecraft followed an uncorrected ballistic trajectory to whatever point it intersected the Moon.

The Vernier Engine Problem

Three Engines, Zero Redundancy

The Surveyor spacecraft used three Thiokol TD-339 vernier engines, arranged 120 degrees apart, each producing 130 newtons of thrust. Hypergolic propellants (hydrazine and nitrogen tetroxide) — no ignition system needed, but also no second chance if a valve failed to open. The three-engine arrangement was the minimum viable configuration for three-axis attitude control. Two engines cannot provide full control. Four engines would have exceeded the mass budget. Three engines left zero margin for failure.

The Geometry of Asymmetric Thrust

When all three engines fire equally, their horizontal thrust components cancel and the spacecraft accelerates symmetrically. Remove one engine and the cancellation fails. Two engines at 120-degree spacing produce a net horizontal force and — critically — a net torque, because the thrust vectors do not pass through the center of mass. The cold-gas attitude jets could not overcome this torque. The tumble was a mathematical certainty once engine 3 failed to ignite.

Probable Cause

The investigation identified two likely failure mechanisms: propellant valve failure (the fuel or oxidizer valve of engine 3 did not open when commanded) or propellant line blockage (contamination or a frozen slug preventing propellant from reaching the combustion chamber). The exact cause could not be determined — the evidence was destroyed at 6,000 mph on the lunar surface. Telemetry indicated the ignition command was received and tank pressures were nominal, placing the failure downstream of the tanks in the engine 3 feed system.

What the Failure Taught

Surveyor 2’s failure led to concrete design and process changes that improved every subsequent mission:

  • Enhanced valve testing at Hughes Aircraft — additional acceptance testing for all vernier engine propellant valves.
  • Modified propellant line routing to reduce blockage and cold-spot risks.
  • Improved health telemetry for real-time vernier engine monitoring.
  • Tumble-recovery flight software — faster invocation of de-spin sequences if a similar failure occurred.

Surveyor 3, launched April 17, 1967, successfully performed its midcourse correction with all three verniers firing normally and soft-landed in Oceanus Procellarum. Two and a half years later, Pete Conrad — the same Conrad who flew Gemini 11 eight days before Surveyor 2’s failure — walked to Surveyor 3 on the Moon and brought its camera home.

The Atlas-Centaur Stack

The complete launch vehicle — Atlas SLV-3C / Centaur D (AC-7) — stood 34.8 meters tall, weighed 137,000 kg at liftoff, and performed flawlessly. The Atlas booster stage used a balloon-tank design: stainless steel walls just 0.25 mm thick, structurally dependent on internal pressure. The Centaur upper stage was the world’s first production liquid hydrogen/liquid oxygen stage, with a specific impulse of 444 seconds — the highest of any upper stage in 1966. Two Pratt & Whitney RL-10 engines. The most advanced rocket technology of its time delivered Surveyor 2 to a perfect translunar trajectory. Everything the launch vehicle was supposed to do, it did. The failure came from the spacecraft, not the rocket.

September 1966: Triumph and Failure

Eight days separated the highest point of Gemini 11 from the failure of Surveyor 2. September 12: Conrad and Gordon launched, achieved first-orbit rendezvous, flew to 1,374 km, conducted tethered spaceflight, performed the first automatic reentry. Four firsts in three days. September 20: Surveyor 2 launched perfectly and failed silently. The contrast illuminated the central tension of the lunar program: crewed missions had humans who could adapt, troubleshoot, improvise. Robotic missions had only their hardware and the time-delayed commands from JPL. When Gemini 8 tumbled, Armstrong regained control. When Surveyor 2 tumbled, there was no one aboard to try.
