# Mission 1.47 -- Gemini 9A (Titan II-GLV / ATDA)

## Deep Research: The Angry Alligator

**Mission:** 1.47 / NASA Mission Series
**Date:** June 3-6, 1966 (launch to splashdown)
**Type:** Crewed orbital (rendezvous, docking attempt, EVA)
**Program:** Gemini (NASA / McDonnell Aircraft Corporation)
**Epoch:** 1 (Early Space Age)
**Organism Pairing:** Pipilo maculatus (Spotted Towhee)
**Bird (Degree 46):** Spotted Towhee
**Dedication:** Elliot See (July 23, 1927 -- February 28, 1966) and Charles Bassett (December 30, 1931 -- February 28, 1966)
**Status:** PARTIAL SUCCESS -- three rendezvous achieved, docking impossible due to target shroud failure, EVA cut short due to visor fogging; 44 orbits over 72 hours 21 minutes

## The Mission That Lost Its Crew Before It Flew

Gemini 9A is the mission that should have been Gemini 9. The "A" suffix tells the story of a tragedy that preceded the flight and shadowed every moment of it.

On February 28, 1966 -- sixteen days before Armstrong and Scott would fly Gemini 8 -- astronauts Elliot See and Charles Bassett, the prime crew for Gemini 9, flew a T-38 Talon jet trainer from Houston to the McDonnell Aircraft Corporation plant in St. Louis, where their Gemini spacecraft was being prepared for its final checkout. The weather at Lambert Field was poor: low ceiling, reduced visibility, light rain. See, flying the approach, descended below the cloud deck and found himself too fast and too close to the runway. He attempted to go around -- to pull up, add power, and fly the approach again. The T-38 did not have sufficient energy. The aircraft struck the roof of Building 101 at the McDonnell plant -- the very building where the Gemini 9 spacecraft was being assembled -- and crashed into a parking lot beyond. Both See and Bassett were killed instantly.

The backup crew for Gemini 9 -- Thomas P. Stafford and Eugene A. Cernan -- became the prime crew. The mission was redesigned and redesignated Gemini 9A, with Stafford as command pilot and Cernan as pilot. The original Agena Target Vehicle for Gemini 9 had already failed to reach orbit on May 17, 1966 (the Atlas booster malfunctioned during launch), so the target was replaced with an ATDA -- Augmented Target Docking Adapter -- launched separately on June 1. The ATDA reached orbit successfully, but it carried a problem that would define the mission.

## The Angry Alligator

When Stafford and Cernan launched aboard Gemini 9A on June 3, 1966, at 13:39:33 UTC from Cape Kennedy Launch Complex 19, their primary objective was to rendezvous with the ATDA and dock. The rendezvous procedures had been proven on Gemini 8 three months earlier. The docking should have been routine.

Stafford performed a textbook rendezvous, bringing Gemini 9A to within close range of the ATDA approximately six hours after launch. Then he saw it.

The ATDA's payload shroud -- a protective fiberglass fairing that covered the docking port during launch and was supposed to jettison after the ATDA reached orbit -- had failed to separate. The two halves of the clamshell shroud were still attached to the ATDA, gaping open at approximately 25 degrees on each side like the jaws of a giant mouth. The docking port was visible inside the open jaws, but the shroud halves blocked physical access. There was no way to insert Gemini's docking probe into the ATDA's docking collar without shearing through the fiberglass panels.

Stafford's description was instantly iconic: "It looks like an angry alligator."

Ground controllers in Houston considered the options. Could the astronauts manually remove the shroud? Too dangerous -- the fiberglass edges were sharp and unpredictable, and any contact with the Gemini spacecraft's nose section could damage the docking probe, the rendezvous radar, or the spacecraft's thermal protection. Could they use the spacecraft's thrusters to push against the shroud? The force required was unknown, the geometry was wrong, and the risk of damaging both vehicles was unacceptable. The docking was abandoned.

The cause was traced to a manufacturing error in the ATDA's separation system. The explosive bolts that should have blown the shroud free had fired, but a lacing of wires and a disconnect lanyard that should have been separated before launch had not been properly configured. The lanyard held the shroud halves in place even after the explosive bolts fired, preventing full separation. The shroud swung partially open under the explosive force but could not detach. The alligator's jaws opened but would not let go.

## Three Rendezvous

With docking impossible, Stafford and Cernan used the ATDA as a target for three different rendezvous techniques -- the most valuable data the mission could now provide.

**Rendezvous 1 -- M=3 (third orbit):** The standard co-elliptic rendezvous profile, the same technique Armstrong had used on Gemini 8. Gemini 9A performed a series of orbital maneuvers over three orbits to close the range and arrive at the ATDA from below and behind. This was the proved method -- safe, predictable, well-rehearsed.

**Rendezvous 2 -- Equiperiod:** After backing away from the ATDA, Stafford performed a second rendezvous using the equiperiod technique, in which Gemini 9A was placed in an orbit with the same period as the ATDA but at a different altitude. The two spacecraft maintained a constant phase angle, and Stafford used a precisely timed translation burn to move directly to the target. This technique was important for Apollo: if the Lunar Module needed to rendezvous with the Command Module in lunar orbit, the equiperiod approach offered an alternative to the standard co-elliptic method.

**Rendezvous 3 -- Optical (from above):** The most challenging technique. Stafford maneuvered Gemini 9A to a position above and ahead of the ATDA, then performed a rendezvous from above using primarily optical tracking (the onboard sextant and the pilot's window) rather than radar. This "rendezvous from above" was a contingency scenario for Apollo: if the LM ascent stage could not achieve the nominal co-elliptic orbit, the CSM might need to rendezvous from a higher orbit. The technique worked, validating the contingency procedure.

Three rendezvous in a single mission -- each using a different technique, each providing data that Apollo planners needed. The angry alligator prevented docking but could not prevent the rendezvous work. The mission adapted.

## Cernan's EVA

The extravehicular activity on Gemini 9A was planned as the most ambitious EVA yet attempted. Gene Cernan was to open the hatch, exit the spacecraft, translate along the adapter section to the rear of the spacecraft, don the Astronaut Maneuvering Unit (AMU) -- a backpack-mounted jet propulsion system stored in the adapter -- and fly freely in space using the AMU's hydrogen peroxide thrusters. If successful, it would have been the first untethered flight by an astronaut (a capability that would not actually be demonstrated until Bruce McCandless flew the Manned Maneuvering Unit on STS-41-B in February 1984, eighteen years later).

Cernan opened the hatch at the start of the third day of the mission, during the 31st revolution, and climbed out. He immediately encountered problems that no one had anticipated.

**The heat problem:** In the direct sunlight of orbit, Cernan's spacesuit -- the G4C suit, designed for EVA -- absorbed solar radiation at a rate that overwhelmed the suit's rudimentary cooling system. The suit used a small fan to circulate oxygen through the helmet and across the visor, but it had no active cooling for the body. In the vacuum of space, the only way to lose heat is by radiation -- there is no air to carry it away by convection. Cernan's metabolic heat output, combined with solar heating, raised his suit temperature rapidly.

**The exertion problem:** Moving in a pressurized spacesuit in zero gravity is extraordinarily difficult. The suit was pressurized to 3.7 psi above vacuum, making it stiff. Every motion of every joint required working against the suit's resistance. Cernan had to translate hand-over-hand along the spacecraft's exterior, gripping smooth handrails with pressurized gloves that had the dexterity of oven mitts. There were no foot restraints along his path -- nothing to anchor his body while he worked with his hands. Every push against a handrail sent his lower body swinging out. The work of simply moving was enormous.

**The fogging problem:** The combination of heat and exertion produced sweat. Cernan was sweating heavily within minutes of beginning the EVA. The moisture saturated the suit's internal atmosphere. The oxygen circulation system could not remove the humidity fast enough. Water vapor condensed on the coolest available surface: the inside of Cernan's helmet visor. Within twenty minutes, the visor was partially fogged. Within thirty minutes, it was nearly opaque.

Cernan was essentially blind. He was hanging on the outside of a spacecraft traveling at 7.8 kilometers per second around the Earth, working in a stiff, overheated suit, sweating into his own eyes, unable to see through his visor. He had not yet reached the AMU. He could not see the handrails, could not see his tether, could barely see the spacecraft's surface. Every motion was by feel.

Stafford, monitoring from inside the capsule, ordered Cernan back. The EVA was terminated after 2 hours and 7 minutes -- Cernan had spent much of that time struggling to translate along the adapter and had never reached the AMU. The planned free flight was canceled.

Cernan's account of the EVA, published later in his memoir *The Last Man on the Moon*, is harrowing. He described the fogging as progressive blindness, the heat as suffocating, and the physical effort as the most exhausting work he had ever done. His heart rate peaked at over 180 beats per minute. He lost approximately 4.5 kilograms (10 pounds) of body weight during the EVA from fluid loss alone.

The lessons were immediate and systemic:

**Foot restraints:** Future EVA suits and spacecraft would include foot restraints at every work station. The Gemini 9A EVA proved that hand-over-hand translation without foot anchoring was nearly impossible for sustained work. The astronaut's feet must be fixed so the hands can be free.

**Suit cooling:** The passive cooling approach of the Gemini suit was inadequate for any EVA involving physical exertion. Apollo EVA suits would incorporate a liquid cooling garment -- a network of small water tubes worn against the skin, circulated by a pump, removing body heat directly rather than relying on air circulation.

**Visor anti-fog:** The condensation problem demanded a solution. Future suits incorporated improved ventilation across the visor interior, anti-fog coatings, and better humidity management in the life support system.

**Task simplification:** The ambition of the Gemini 9A EVA -- translate the length of the spacecraft, don a jet backpack, and fly freely -- was recognized as far too complex for the state of EVA technology. Future EVAs were simplified: shorter durations, fewer objectives, better-positioned equipment, and more rest periods.

## The Original Crew

Elliot McKay See Jr. was born on July 23, 1927, in Dallas, Texas. He held a bachelor's degree from the University of Texas at Austin and a master's degree from UCLA, both in engineering. He was a naval aviator who transitioned to test pilot work at General Electric, evaluating jet engines. He was selected as a NASA astronaut in the second group (the "New Nine") in September 1962, the same group as Neil Armstrong.

Charles Arthur Bassett II was born on December 30, 1931, in Dayton, Ohio -- the city of the Wright brothers. He earned a bachelor's degree in electrical engineering from Texas Tech and a master's from the University of Southern California. He was an Air Force test pilot and was selected in the third group of astronauts in October 1963.

See was the command pilot and Bassett the pilot for Gemini 9. They had trained together for months. The mission was their first spaceflight.

The crash on February 28, 1966, was attributed to pilot error compounded by poor weather. See descended below the prescribed minimum altitude on the instrument approach, broke out of the clouds too close to the runway, and attempted a go-around at insufficient altitude and airspeed. The T-38, a high-performance military trainer with relatively small wings and high wing loading, needed more altitude and speed than See had to safely go around. The aircraft settled into the roof of Building 101.

The irony was acute. See and Bassett died in the building where their spacecraft was being built. The Gemini 9 capsule, on the second floor of Building 101, was undamaged by the crash. The spacecraft outlived the crew it was built for.

The redesignation from "Gemini 9" to "Gemini 9A" was NASA's way of acknowledging that the mission was not the one originally planned. The "A" stood for the crew change, the target vehicle change (ATDA instead of Agena), and the altered objectives. It was a scarlet letter of loss.

## Stafford and Cernan

Thomas Patten Stafford, born September 17, 1930, in Weatherford, Oklahoma, was already one of NASA's most experienced pilots when he flew Gemini 9A. He had flown Gemini 6A in December 1965, performing the first space rendezvous (with Gemini 7). Gemini 9A was his second flight. He would go on to command Apollo 10 in May 1969 -- the "dress rehearsal" for the Moon landing that flew the Lunar Module to within 15.6 kilometers of the lunar surface -- and would command the Apollo-Soyuz Test Project in July 1975, the first US-Soviet joint space mission.

Eugene Andrew Cernan, born March 14, 1934, in Chicago, Illinois, was on his first spaceflight. The brutal EVA on Gemini 9A was his introduction to space operations. He would fly to the Moon on Apollo 10 (as Lunar Module pilot, with Stafford) and would command Apollo 17 in December 1972 -- the final Apollo lunar mission. When Cernan climbed back into the Lunar Module Challenger on December 14, 1972, he became the last human being to stand on the Moon. As of this writing, he still holds that distinction. The man who could not see through his fogged visor on Gemini 9A walked on the Moon six years later.

Cernan died on January 16, 2017, at the age of eighty-two. Stafford died on March 18, 2024, at the age of ninety-three.

## The ATDA

The Augmented Target Docking Adapter was a substitute for the Gemini Agena Target Vehicle that had failed to reach orbit on May 17, 1966. The ATDA was simpler than the Agena-based target: it consisted of a docking cone (identical to the Agena Target Vehicle's docking collar), a radar transponder to support rendezvous, and a set of acquisition lights for visual tracking. It lacked the Agena's main propulsion engine -- there would be no orbital maneuvers by the target. The ATDA was a passive target: it simply orbited at its injection altitude and let the Gemini crew come to it.

The ATDA launched on June 1, 1966, aboard an Atlas launch vehicle from Cape Kennedy Launch Complex 14. The Atlas performed nominally, and the ATDA reached a 298 km circular orbit. Telemetry indicated that the vehicle was in good health and the radar transponder was functioning. But ground controllers noted an anomaly in the telemetry from the payload shroud separation system: the explosive bolts appeared to have fired, but the shroud did not show the expected separation dynamics. The shroud might still be attached.

The concern was relayed to the crew. They would not know the shroud's status until they saw the ATDA with their own eyes. When Stafford brought Gemini 9A to within visual range and saw the gaping fiberglass panels, the concern was confirmed. Post-mission investigation determined that a lacing wire -- used to secure a disconnect lanyard during ground handling -- had not been removed before launch. The wire held the shroud halves in place even after the explosive bolts fired, preventing full separation. The bolts blew the halves open (producing the alligator-jaw appearance) but could not overcome the wire's tensile strength. A ground-handling error -- a step missed in the pre-launch procedure -- had defeated the separation mechanism.

The failure was a process error, not a design error. The separation system worked. The explosive bolts fired. The mechanical design was sound. The error was human: someone did not remove a wire, and the inspection process did not catch the omission. The lesson was direct and applicable to Apollo: every separation mechanism, every jettison system, every deployable structure must be inspected for ground-handling residue before launch. The angry alligator was a wire that should not have been there.

## What Gemini 9A Proved

Gemini 9A was a mission of failures that produced essential knowledge. The docking failure proved that target vehicle preparation procedures needed to be more rigorous -- the ATDA shroud error was a process failure, not a design failure, and it drove improvements in pre-launch inspection and verification procedures across the entire Gemini and Apollo programs. The EVA failure proved that spacesuit technology was inadequate for complex work and drove the redesign of the entire EVA equipment suite for Apollo. The three rendezvous proved that multiple approach techniques worked, giving Apollo planners confidence in contingency scenarios.

The three rendezvous profiles demonstrated on Gemini 9A became part of the Apollo contingency playbook. If the Lunar Module's ascent trajectory was nominal, the co-elliptic rendezvous (M=3 profile) would be used -- the same proven technique from Gemini 8. If the trajectory was off-nominal, the equiperiod approach provided a fuel-efficient alternative. If the LM ended up in a higher orbit than the CSM (an unlikely but possible scenario involving ascent engine over-performance), the from-above approach provided a workable recovery technique. All three techniques had been demonstrated in flight by Stafford on Gemini 9A. All three were available to Apollo mission planners as tested, validated options.

The EVA lessons were even more consequential. Before Gemini 9A, the assumption was that EVA work in zero gravity would be comparable in difficulty to EVA training underwater or in aircraft parabolic flight. Cernan's experience demolished this assumption. Zero gravity was not merely different from training -- it was qualitatively harder. The suit fought every motion. The lack of foot restraints made every arm motion produce an equal and opposite body rotation. The thermal management failed under real work loads. The visor fogged. The EVA curriculum -- the list of things the space program needed to learn about working outside -- was rewritten after Gemini 9A.

Buzz Aldrin, who flew the EVA on Gemini 12 (the final Gemini mission) in November 1966, benefited directly from every lesson Cernan's failure taught. Aldrin's EVA was planned with foot restraints at every work station, simplified tasks with built-in rest periods, and a suit configuration informed by Cernan's thermal and humidity data. Aldrin's EVA was the first fully successful extended EVA of the Gemini program. It worked because Gemini 9A had shown, with painful clarity, what does not work.

Every failure taught. The angry alligator taught vehicle preparation discipline. The fogged visor taught thermal and humidity management. The exhausted astronaut taught the physics of working in vacuum without foot restraints. Gemini 9A was the mission that discovered problems while there was still time to fix them -- before Apollo, before the Moon, before the stakes included lives at 384,000 kilometers from home.
