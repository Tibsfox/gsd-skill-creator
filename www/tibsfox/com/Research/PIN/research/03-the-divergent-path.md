# The Divergent Path

> **Domain:** Cultural Topology / Post-Industrial Music
> **Module:** 3 -- Current 93, David Tibet, and the Arc from Industrial Noise to Sacred Music
> **Through-line:** *David Tibet founded Current 93 in 1982 from within the Psychic TV orbit, making harsh industrial noise informed by Aleister Crowley's Thelema. Twelve years later, he was making acoustic folk music informed by Christian eschatology. The arc from LAShTAL (1983) to Thunder Perfect Mind (1992) is the most complete documented divergence in the post-industrial network -- and the collaborations that accompanied every phase make it the most richly evidenced.*

## 1. The Divergence Problem

Current 93 presents the post-industrial network's most analytically interesting trajectory because the distance traveled is so great. The band that recorded *LAShTAL* in 1983 -- a harsh, abrasive industrial noise work invoking the Thelemic concept of Malkuth -- bears essentially no sonic resemblance to the band that recorded *Thunder Perfect Mind* in 1992 -- an accessible, often beautiful acoustic folk album with Christian mystical lyrics. The personnel changed continuously, but the one constant -- David Tibet -- underwent a parallel personal transformation that the music documented in real time [1].

The divergence is analytically meaningful because it occurred within the network, not outside it. Tibet's collaborators during every phase included other nodes in the post-industrial graph: John Balance (Coil), Steven Stapleton (NWW), Douglas Pearce (Death in June). The arc was not private or isolated; it was documented, witnessed, and influenced by the network's other participants. This makes Current 93 the best case study for understanding how the post-industrial network's internal diversity was generated: not through separation, but through transformation within an ongoing web of relationships [2].

## 2. Origins: The Psychic TV Orbit

David Tibet (born David Michael Bunting, 1960) entered the post-industrial network through Psychic TV, the group Genesis P-Orridge formed after Throbbing Gristle's dissolution in 1981. Tibet took his surname from P-Orridge -- a reference to Tibetan Buddhism that reflected the occult interests both shared at the time [1].

Tibet was not a formal Psychic TV member but moved in the PTV orbit. His early interests combined:
- Aleister Crowley's Thelema (the name "Current 93" derives from Crowley's concept of the 93 Current)
- Tibetan Buddhism (via P-Orridge's influence)
- Occult sound experimentation (the conviction that sound could function as ritual)
- An emerging interest in apocalyptic and eschatological themes [1]

The PTV connection established Tibet's initial position in the network: adjacent to the TG-descended noise-ritual-occult branch, sharing P-Orridge's magical orientation but developing a distinct aesthetic sensibility that would pull him away from PTV's methods within a few years.

### The TG-to-PTV-to-C93 Cascade

This is the network's longest documented chain of direct personal influence:

```
THE DESCENT OF INFLUENCE
================================================================

  Throbbing Gristle (1976-1981)
       |
       | Genesis P-Orridge leaves TG
       v
  Psychic TV (1981-1999)
       |
       | David Tibet in the PTV orbit
       v
  Current 93 (1982-present)
       |
       | Tibet's own path diverges from PTV
       v
  Apocalyptic Folk / Sacred Music
```

The cascade spans three generations of the network, with each step representing a deliberate departure from the predecessor rather than a continuation. P-Orridge departed TG to pursue more explicitly ritual/magical work in PTV. Tibet departed the PTV orbit to pursue more explicitly theological work in C93. Each departure increased the distance from TG's industrial noise origin while maintaining the network connections that made the departures legible.

## 3. The Equinox Event (1983)

The Equinox Event was a festival of post-industrial acts held in London in 1983. Its significance for the network topology is comparable to the *Prostitution* exhibition's significance for TG's origin: it was a convergence point where multiple future trajectories became visible simultaneously [1].

Key events at the Equinox:

1. **Tibet met Steven Stapleton.** This meeting initiated the longest-duration creative partnership in the network's inner circle. Stapleton would appear on nearly every Current 93 release from 1984 through approximately 2010 -- a 26-year collaborative relationship [2].

2. **Current 93 performed as Dogs Blood Order.** The performance name reflected the early industrial-noise orientation. The Dogs Blood Order lineup included Fritz Catlin of 23 Skidoo, demonstrating C93's connections to the broader London post-punk scene.

3. **John Balance was present.** Balance, who was already connected to the network through Psychic TV, attended the Equinox Event. Tibet later invited Balance to leave Psychic TV and join Current 93 full-time. Balance refused -- a refusal that indirectly led to Coil's formation when Balance instead partnered with Peter Christopherson [1].

The Equinox Event demonstrates a recurring principle in the network's topology: **convergence events generate edges.** The festival brought together individuals who would spend the next two decades collaborating, and several of the network's most significant edges (Tibet-Stapleton, Tibet-Balance, the C93-NWW fusion) trace directly to contacts made at this single event.

## 4. Phase 1: Industrial Noise (1982-1986)

Current 93's early output is recognizably within the post-industrial template established by TG and elaborated by Psychic TV: harsh electronics, tape manipulation, confrontational vocals, and occult/ritual framing.

### Key Early Releases

| Album | Year | Character | Collaborators |
|-------|------|-----------|--------------|
| *LAShTAL* | 1983 | Industrial noise; Thelemic invocation | Fritz Catlin, John Balance |
| *Nature Unveiled* | 1984 | Dark ambient; first extended-form pieces | Co-released with NWW collaboration |
| *Dogs Blood Rising* | 1984 | Harsh electronics; abrasive, ritualistic | John Balance (early Coil period overlap) |
| *In Menstrual Night* | 1985 | Industrial collage; spoken word elements | Multiple collaborators from the PTV orbit |

These releases are sonically coherent with the broader post-industrial scene. They could sit alongside Psychic TV, early Coil, or Whitehouse without requiring explanation. What distinguishes them is Tibet's lyrical content: even in this earliest phase, the words are more literary, more theologically inflected, and more concerned with textual sources (Crowley, Austin Osman Spare, Hebrew mysticism) than the scene's norm [1].

### John Balance's Early Involvement

Balance's presence in the original Current 93 lineup (the Dogs Blood Order period, 1982-1984) is significant for the network's topology. Balance was simultaneously involved with:
- Psychic TV (as a member)
- Current 93 (as a contributor)
- Coil (forming with Christopherson in 1983)

This triple overlap means that for a brief period in 1983-1984, Balance was a node connecting three of the network's four primary post-TG projects (PTV, C93, and Coil). His decision to focus on Coil was thus a network-shaping event: it concentrated his creative energy on a single node rather than distributing it across three [1].

## 5. Phase 2: The Transition (1986-1988)

The transition period is the most analytically significant phase of Current 93's arc because it is where the divergence from the post-industrial template becomes audible. The change was not sudden; it was gradual, with each release moving incrementally further from the noise-ritual origin.

### The Transition Markers

| Release | Year | Key Shift |
|---------|------|-----------|
| *Imperium* | 1987 | First significant use of acoustic guitar textures alongside electronics |
| *Swastikas for Noddy* | 1988 | First full apocalyptic folk album; acoustic guitars dominant |
| *Earth Covers Earth* | 1988 | Transition confirmed; electronic elements minimal |

The transition coincided with two personal developments in Tibet's life:

1. **Living with Douglas Pearce and Freya Aswynn.** Tibet lived for a period with Death in June's Douglas Pearce, during which he was exposed to European pagan traditions through Aswynn (a practitioner of Norse runic magic). This period introduced folk textures and European mythological content that had been absent from the early industrial work [1].

2. **The beginning of Tibet's move away from Thelema.** The occult framework that had informed the earliest C93 releases -- Crowley, Spare, Psychic TV-adjacent ritual practice -- began to give way to a broader theological inquiry that would eventually settle on Christianity.

### Sonic Mechanics of the Transition

The transition is not merely a change of instrumentation. It is a change of method:

- **Early C93 (industrial):** Sound as assault. The music's function is to create a ritual space through overwhelming sonic pressure. Meaning resides in the intensity of the experience, not in individual elements.
- **Transitional C93:** Sound as narrative. Acoustic instruments introduce melodic and harmonic elements that carry semantic weight. Tibet's vocals shift from processed screams to unprocessed singing and spoken word.
- **Late C93 (folk):** Sound as liturgy. The music's function is to create a contemplative space. Individual instruments carry recognizable emotional content. Lyrics are primary.

### The Cashmore Factor

Michael Cashmore joined Current 93 in 1988, precisely when the folk transition was underway. Cashmore's guitar work -- delicate, fingerpicked, classically influenced -- became the sonic signature of C93's folk period. His arrival was not coincidental: Tibet needed a musician who could realize the acoustic textures he was hearing internally, and Cashmore's technique provided exactly that.

Cashmore's presence became so central that C93's folk-period sound is as much defined by his guitar as by Tibet's voice. He remained the most consistent musical partner through the 1990s and 2000s, outlasting even Stapleton's regular involvement. Cashmore's own project, **Nature and Organisation**, explored similar territory (acoustic, atmospheric, melancholic) and functioned as a testing ground for ideas that fed back into C93.

The Cashmore factor illustrates a principle common in the post-industrial network: individual collaborators don't merely support a project -- they redirect it. Cashmore's arrival made C93's folk turn not just possible but inevitable, because his instrument was the vehicle through which the folk idiom entered the project's sonic vocabulary.

### The Spoken Word Dimension

Tibet's vocal transition is as significant as the instrumental transition. In early C93, his voice is processed, distorted, screamed -- another layer of noise in the industrial texture. By *Thunder Perfect Mind*, his voice is often speaking rather than singing -- narrating text in a measured, intense tone that recalls religious recitation.

The spoken-word dimension connects C93 to a literary tradition that runs through the post-industrial network: P-Orridge's use of Burroughs' cut-up technique in TG, Stapleton's use of found text in NWW, and Tibet's own increasingly text-focused approach. The network as a whole treats text not as lyrics but as a distinct sonic element -- words carrying weight independent of their melodic setting.

## 6. Phase 3: Apocalyptic Folk (1988-1994)

The term "apocalyptic folk" was coined by Tibet himself to describe Current 93's post-transition sound. The term is precise: "apocalyptic" in its theological sense (revelation, unveiling) rather than its colloquial sense (destruction), and "folk" in the sense of acoustic instrumentation and melodic accessibility [1].

### The Peak Albums

| Album | Year | Significance | Key Collaborators |
|-------|------|-------------|------------------|
| *Swastikas for Noddy* | 1988 | First full folk album; title deliberately provocative | Douglas Pearce, Rose McDowall |
| *Earth Covers Earth* | 1988 | Consolidation; electronic elements largely absent | Steven Stapleton, Michael Cashmore |
| *Island* | 1991 | Minimal folk; Tibet's vocals at their most vulnerable | Stapleton, Cashmore, Nick Cave |
| *Thunder Perfect Mind* | 1992 | Critical peak; accessible entry point; final album with Douglas Pearce | Pearce, Stapleton, Cashmore, Rose McDowall |

### Thunder Perfect Mind (1992)

*Thunder Perfect Mind* is widely considered Current 93's definitive statement and the apex of the apocalyptic folk form. The album takes its title from a Gnostic text in the Nag Hammadi library -- a series of paradoxical first-person declarations attributed to a divine feminine voice ("I am the first and the last / I am the honored one and the scorned one").

The album's significance for the network is threefold:

1. **Accessible entry point.** For the first time, a Current 93 album could be recommended to a listener with no prior exposure to the post-industrial scene. The melodies are beautiful, the production is clean, the lyrics are compelling without requiring occult knowledge to parse.

2. **Network convergence.** The album features contributions from Pearce (Death in June), Stapleton (NWW), Cashmore (long-term C93 guitarist), and Rose McDowall (Strawberry Switchblade, also a Coil collaborator). It is the network's most densely connected single artifact.

3. **Last Pearce collaboration.** *Thunder Perfect Mind* was the last Current 93 album to feature Douglas Pearce. Tibet would later publicly distance himself from Pearce over Death in June's ideological associations, making this album the final document of one of the network's most significant edges [1].

## 7. Phase 4: Late Mature Period (1994-Present)

After *Thunder Perfect Mind*, Current 93's trajectory continued to evolve:

### Key Later Works

| Album | Year | Character |
|-------|------|-----------|
| *Of Ruine, Or Some Blazing Starre* | 1994 | Post-Pearce; deeper into Christian mysticism |
| *All the Pretty Little Horses* | 1996 | Nursery rhymes reimagined through apocalyptic lens |
| *Sleep Has His House* | 2000 | Drone/ambient return; collaboration with Antony Hegarty |
| *Black Ships Ate the Sky* | 2006 | Ambitious multi-version album; features Marc Almond, Antony, Baby Dee, Bonnie "Prince" Billy |
| *I Am the Last of All the Field That Fell* | 2014 | Mature folk; Tibet now explicitly Christian |
| *The Light Is Leaving Us All* | 2018 | Late-period statement; reflective, elegiac |

The late period is characterized by:
- Increasingly explicit Christian content (Tibet completed a Master's degree in Coptic Language and Grammar in 2009)
- Broader collaborative network (reaching beyond the post-industrial scene to include Antony Hegarty, Marc Almond, and Bonnie "Prince" Billy)
- Intermittent releases with longer gaps between them
- A sense of completion rather than experimentation [3]

### Cornell University Archive

Tibet's personal archives through 2013 are held at Cornell University Library's Division of Rare and Manuscript Collections. The archive includes correspondence, manuscripts, artwork, and unreleased recordings. Its presence in an academic institution reflects the scholarly recognition of C93's cultural significance [4].

## 8. Tibet's Theological Evolution

The theological arc is the spine of Current 93's musical evolution. The two cannot be separated:

```
TIBET'S THEOLOGICAL ARC
================================================================

  1982-1986: THELEMA / OCCULT
  |--> Aleister Crowley's 93 Current
  |--> Austin Osman Spare's sigil magic
  |--> PTV-adjacent ritual practice
  |--> Album: LAShTAL (Malkuth invocation)

  1986-1990: TRANSITION / SYNCRETIC
  |--> Living with Pearce and Freya Aswynn
  |--> Norse runic magic exposure
  |--> Gnostic texts (Nag Hammadi discovery)
  |--> Moving away from Crowley, toward broader mysticism
  |--> Album: Swastikas for Noddy

  1990-2000: CHRISTIAN MYSTICISM EMERGENT
  |--> Thunder Perfect Mind (Gnostic text)
  |--> Apocalyptic eschatology
  |--> Nursery rhymes as sacred text
  |--> Album: All the Pretty Little Horses

  2000-present: EXPLICIT CHRISTIAN FAITH
  |--> Master's degree in Coptic Language (2009)
  |--> Archives donated to Cornell University
  |--> Tibet identifies explicitly as Christian
  |--> Album: I Am the Last of All the Field
```

The trajectory from Crowley to Christ is not a rejection of the earlier work but an evolution through it. Tibet has stated that each phase was genuine at the time and that the progression felt natural rather than contradictory. The Thelemic framework provided the entry point; the Christian framework provided the destination [1].

### The Nursery Rhyme Thread

A distinctive element of Tibet's mature work is the repeated use of nursery rhymes and children's songs as raw material. *All the Pretty Little Horses* (1996) takes its title from a traditional American lullaby. *Black Ships Ate the Sky* features multiple versions of "Idumaea" (a shape-note hymn). The album *Soft Black Stars* (1998) draws on children's counting songs.

The nursery rhyme strategy is theologically deliberate. Tibet treats children's songs as a form of sacred text -- simple structures that carry enormous emotional and cultural weight precisely because they are learned before critical faculties develop. A lullaby operates on the same principle as a liturgical chant: repetition, melody, and the suspension of analytical distance. By placing nursery rhymes in an apocalyptic context, Tibet forces the listener to hear the darkness that was always present in the original material (lullabies about plague, death, loss) and to recognize that the "innocence" of children's music is a projection, not a property [8].

### The Durtro Label

Tibet founded Durtro in 1995 as Current 93's primary release vehicle. The label name derives from "durt" (an archaic English word for "harm" or "hurt"). Durtro releases are characterized by:
- Handmade packaging (limited editions with unique physical elements)
- Small pressing numbers (typically 500-2000 copies)
- Direct distribution to specialist retailers

Durtro's operational model consciously echoes Industrial Records' principles: small-scale, anti-commercial, prioritizing the artifact's physical quality over its commercial reach. The label demonstrates that the anti-commercial architecture established at the network's origin persisted into the network's later generations [8].

## 11. Current 93 and the Neofolk Question

Current 93's relationship to the "neofolk" label is complicated. Tibet coined the term "apocalyptic folk" to describe his own work, distinguishing it from the broader neofolk category that would come to include Death in June, Sol Invictus, and other acts with more politically fraught associations [1].

The distinction matters because:

1. **C93's folk is liturgical.** The acoustic guitars and melodic structure serve a theological purpose -- they create a space for contemplation, invocation, or lament. The folk form is a vessel for sacred content.

2. **DIJ's folk is martial and symbolic.** Death in June's folk textures carry different freight -- European military history, disputed imagery, ideological provocation. The folk form serves a political-aesthetic purpose.

3. **Tibet actively distanced himself from DIJ's ideology.** After *Thunder Perfect Mind* (the last Pearce collaboration), Tibet publicly criticized Death in June's use of fascist imagery and broke the creative partnership. This distancing is itself a network event: it represents the severing of a major edge in the graph.

The neofolk question is explored further in Module 04 (The Folk Turn), which addresses Death in June's trajectory and the ideology problem directly.
