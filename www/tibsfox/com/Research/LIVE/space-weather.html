Why Space Weather Matters for Artemis II

Orion’s crew is beyond the protective shield of Earth’s magnetosphere. In low Earth orbit, the Van Allen belts deflect most solar particle radiation. On the translunar coast, the crew is exposed to galactic cosmic rays and any solar particle events directly.

Radiation Exposure Windows

Van Allen belt transit: SLS punches through the belts in ~30 minutes each way. Projected dose: 5–15 mSv (equivalent to 1–3 chest CT scans). The crew wears passive dosimeters (TLDs) and active electronic dosimeters (TEPCs) that measure dose rate in real time.

Solar particle events (SPEs): A large solar flare can produce a burst of high-energy protons within minutes. During a major event, the crew retreats to Orion’s radiation shelter — the area behind the most shielding (equipment, water, food). The shelter demo is scheduled for Flight Day 8.

Galactic cosmic rays (GCRs): Continuous background radiation from supernovae remnants across the galaxy. Higher-energy than solar particles, harder to shield. The current solar maximum actually reduces GCR flux (the heliosphere inflates, deflecting more GCRs). Good timing for a lunar mission.

Mission Space Weather Timeline

March 30 — T-2d

X1.5 flare from AR4405. CME launched at ~1,845 km/s. G2 Moderate geomagnetic storm watch issued for launch day (April 1). Solar wind 387 km/s, Bz −1 nT. Mission teams begin monitoring for GO/NO-GO space weather assessment.

March 31 20:00 PDT — T-19h

CME effects begin. Kp surges 1.33 → 3.00. Solar wind accelerates to 406 km/s. Bz goes southward (−2.6 nT). G2 watch still active. SWPC updates: storm expected to peak before launch window.

March 31 23:00 PDT — T-16h

CME pulse subsides. Kp drops to 1.33. Solar wind decelerating. The pulse came and went faster than models predicted. Space weather constraints lifting.

April 1 07:00 PDT — T-8h

Launch day morning. Kp 2.0, recovering. G2 watch technically active but conditions improving. Space weather assessed as GO for launch. Aurora possible tonight at 55°N — PNW viewers could see it post-launch.

April 1 15:35 PDT — LAUNCH

SLS lifts off from LC-39B. Space weather GO. Crew transits Van Allen belts within 30 minutes of MECO. Dosimeters nominal.

April 2 — FD2

TLI burn complete. Crew leaves Earth orbit. Now beyond magnetospheric protection. Kp 1.67, solar wind 380 km/s. Quiet conditions. GCR background is the primary radiation concern.

April 3 — FD3

Translunar coast. Kp 1.33, Bz +0.5 nT (northward = good). R1 minor radio blackout active but not affecting S-band/X-band Orion comms. TC#1 cancelled — trajectory already correct. Conditions excellent for crew operations.

Key Concepts

Kp Index

The planetary K-index (Kp) measures geomagnetic disturbance on a 0–9 scale, updated every 3 hours. It’s derived from magnetometer readings at 13 ground stations worldwide. Kp ≥ 5 triggers geomagnetic storm warnings. For Artemis II, any Kp ≥ 7 would prompt the crew to evaluate radiation shelter timing.

Bz Component

The north-south component of the interplanetary magnetic field (IMF). When Bz is southward (negative), it couples with Earth’s magnetosphere and drives geomagnetic activity. When northward (positive), the magnetosphere is more closed and conditions are quieter. Current Bz: +0.5 nT — good news.

Solar Wind

A continuous stream of charged particles from the Sun, typically 300–800 km/s. Background speed ~400 km/s. CME-driven shocks can exceed 2,000 km/s. Current: 370 km/s — nominal. Measured in real time by the DSCOVR satellite at the L1 Lagrange point, giving ~30–60 minutes warning before disturbances reach Earth.
