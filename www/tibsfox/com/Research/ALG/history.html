— Edsger W. Dijkstra, Minority Report on ALGOL 68

The Pre-ALGOL World

In 1957, the programming language landscape was a chaos of incompatible systems. Each computer vendor had its own assemblers, its own Autocode-style high-level language, and its own conventions. Manchester had its Mark 1 Autocode (Brooker, 1954) and later Mercury Autocode (1958). Cambridge had EDSAC Autocode. ETH Zurich had Rutishauser’s Superplan (1951) and various local efforts. IBM shipped FORTRAN (John Backus’s team, first released April 1957), which dominated scientific computing on the 704 and later the 709, 7090, and 7094. The U.S. Department of Defense was assembling the team that would deliver COBOL in 1959.

For numerical analysts and mathematicians who wanted to publish algorithms — in a journal, in a book, in a dissertation — none of these languages was acceptable. Fortran was tied to IBM hardware and full of peculiarities that reflected the 704’s instruction set. Autocode was local to a particular Manchester or Cambridge machine. COBOL was for business data, not for numerics. The European mathematical computing community, in particular, felt acutely the need for a publication language: a notation in which algorithms could be described precisely, portably, and in a form that would carry across national boundaries and across vendor boundaries.

The two relevant organizations were GAMM (Gesellschaft für Angewandte Mathematik und Mechanik, the German Association for Applied Mathematics and Mechanics) in Europe, and the ACM (Association for Computing Machinery) in the United States. GAMM had been working toward a universal algorithmic language since 1955, under the influence of Heinz Rutishauser and Friedrich Ludwig Bauer at the Technische Hochschule Zurich (now ETH Zurich) and Klaus Samelson at the Bavarian Academy of Sciences in Munich. ACM had a parallel committee under Alan Perlis.

In April 1957, representatives of both groups agreed to meet and combine their efforts. The meeting was set for Zurich, May 27 through June 1, 1958.

Zurich, 1958 — ALGOL 58 (Originally IAL)

Eight people attended the Zurich meeting:

  • John Backus (IBM) — already famous as the designer of FORTRAN
  • Friedrich L. Bauer (Munich) — would become the grandfather of German computer science
  • Hermann Bottenbruch (Darmstadt)
  • Charles Katz (Remington Rand Univac)
  • Alan J. Perlis (Purdue, later Carnegie Tech) — would win the first Turing Award in 1966
  • Heinz Rutishauser (ETH Zurich) — had done pioneering work on compilation in the early 1950s
  • Klaus Samelson (Mainz) — Bauer’s collaborator on the famous operator-precedence stack parser
  • Joseph Henry Wegstein (U.S. National Bureau of Standards)

They spent six days arguing about everything: keywords vs identifiers, semicolons vs newlines, Fortran-style arithmetic IF vs the newer if-then-else, how to handle I/O (they punted on this decision, which caused problems for decades). They settled on a language they initially called the International Algebraic Language (IAL), later retroactively renamed ALGOL 58.

Bauer and Samelson brought with them an idea that had been percolating at the Munich academy: the stack-based parser. Their work on expression evaluation using a pair of stacks — one for operators and one for operands — became the basis of operator-precedence parsing, which is still used in hand-written expression parsers today. They applied for a patent on the idea (the first patent ever issued for a software technique, at least in Germany). Their insight that a compiler could be a disciplined state machine with stacks was the theoretical foundation that made ALGOL practical.

ALGOL 58 itself was never a success. A few implementations were attempted:

  • MAD (Michigan Algorithm Decoder) — University of Michigan, by Bruce Arden, Bernard Galler, Robert Graham. Actually a modified ALGOL 58 dialect. Widely used on IBM 7090s at Michigan and MIT.
  • NELIAC — Navy Electronics Laboratory International ALGOL Compiler, 1958–1960. U.S. Navy’s ALGOL 58 dialect.
  • JOVIAL — “Jules’ Own Version of the International Algorithmic Language,” designed by Jules Schwartz at SDC (System Development Corporation) in 1960. Used by the U.S. Air Force for decades in military avionics. Survived in production into the 2000s.

The experiences of these implementations — and the dissatisfactions of the committee members — fed into a second, more ambitious meeting.

Paris, January 1960 — ALGOL 60

The second ALGOL meeting was held in Paris, January 11 through January 16, 1960. Thirteen people attended this time, expanding the committee’s reach on both sides of the Atlantic:

  • John Backus (IBM)
  • Friedrich L. Bauer (Munich)
  • Julien Green (IBM France)
  • Charles Katz (Univac)
  • John McCarthy (MIT) — yes, the inventor of Lisp, who had just finished the Lisp 1 implementation
  • Peter Naur (Regnecentralen, Copenhagen) — Danish, serving as editor and secretary
  • Alan J. Perlis (Carnegie Tech)
  • Klaus Samelson (Mainz)
  • Bernard Vauquois (Institut Blaise Pascal, Paris)
  • Adriaan van Wijngaarden (Mathematisch Centrum, Amsterdam)
  • Joseph Henry Wegstein (NBS)
  • Michael Woodger (National Physical Laboratory, UK)

Naur served as secretary and editor. The committee spent six days locked in the Paris meeting room. Naur would later recall that the tone was intense, argumentative, and exhilarating — there was a sense that they were designing something important.

McCarthy’s Contributions

John McCarthy had just finished the first Lisp implementation at MIT, and he brought Lisp-tradition ideas to the committee. His two most significant contributions to ALGOL 60 were:

  • Conditional expressions — the if B then E1 else E2 form as a first-class expression that returns a value, not just a control-flow statement. This was Lisp’s cond form transplanted into an Algol-family language. It is why every modern language has a ternary operator or an if expression.
  • Recursion as a natural consequence of the language design — McCarthy insisted that ALGOL 60 procedures must be able to call themselves, and that the language must specify this. Fortran I had explicitly forbidden recursion. McCarthy would not have accepted any language that did not support it.

This is the tie-in to the Lisp project: the inventor of Lisp was a member of the committee that designed the language that became the ancestor of every non-Lisp language. ALGOL 60 and Lisp are the two roots of modern programming, and McCarthy sat on both committees. (He also helped found the MIT AI Lab the same year.)

Peter Naur as Editor

Peter Naur was Danish, a mathematician and astronomer by training, working at Regnecentralen in Copenhagen. He had not been at the Zurich meeting, but by 1960 he was running the ALGOL Bulletin, the informal newsletter that had sprung up around the language. The committee chose him as secretary of the Paris meeting, and he took the further step of editing the resulting specification into publishable form.

Naur’s editorial work on the Report — the Report on the Algorithmic Language ALGOL 60, published in Communications of the ACM, Volume 3, Number 5, May 1960 — is one of the great works of technical writing in computing history. He took Backus’s metalinguistic notation (which we now call BNF) and used it consistently throughout the document. He cleaned up the prose. He organized the semantic rules. He produced a document of about twenty pages that was complete, in the sense that a compiler writer could read it and build an ALGOL 60 compiler without guessing.

The Report was revised in January 1963 (the Revised Report on the Algorithmic Language ALGOL 60) to clarify ambiguities and fix minor errors. It remains the reference. Knuth’s 1967 paper “The Remaining Trouble Spots in ALGOL 60” identified a handful of edge cases still unresolved after the revision, but they were minor.

For the full technical content of the Report, see The ALGOL 60 Report.

Implementations

Once the Report was published, compilers started appearing. None of them were commercially dominant, but several were technically important.

The Whetstone Compiler (London, 1961)

An early ALGOL 60 implementation by E. N. Hawkins and D. H. R. Huxtable at the Oxford University Computing Laboratory and the English Electric KDF9. Named after the Whetstone National Physical Laboratory test site.

Dijkstra at the Mathematisch Centrum

Edsger W. Dijkstra and Jaap Zonneveld implemented ALGOL 60 at the Mathematisch Centrum in Amsterdam, starting in 1960. Dijkstra’s work on the implementation — particularly on handling recursion via activation record stacks and on compiling call-by-name into “thunks” — became foundational compiler theory. Dijkstra would later say that ALGOL 60 was the reason he entered computer science, and his implementation of it taught him many of the ideas that would later appear in structured programming.

ALCOR (Europe)

The ALCOR (ALGOL CONverter) consortium was a group of European institutions cooperating on ALGOL 60 implementations for a range of machines. ALCOR compilers were built for the Siemens 2002, the IBM 7090, the Telefunken TR 4, and others. The consortium was a remarkable example of international scientific cooperation during the Cold War — German, Dutch, and Swiss institutions sharing compiler technology.

Randell and Russell’s Book

Brian Randell and L. J. Russell published ALGOL 60 Implementation in 1964. It remains the classic reference on compiling ALGOL 60. The book describes the Whetstone compiler and explains in detail how to handle the language’s hardest features: call-by-name (via thunks), recursion (via the activation record stack), and block structure (via the display, a stack of frames for lexical scope). Randell and Russell’s book, along with Dijkstra’s writings, taught an entire generation how to build compilers for block-structured languages.

The Burroughs B5000

The most notable commercial success of ALGOL 60 was not a compiler but a machine. The Burroughs B5000, announced in 1961 and delivered in 1963, was the first computer whose instruction set was designed specifically to run a high-level language: ALGOL 60. The B5000 was a stack machine. Its architecture mapped directly to ALGOL 60’s block structure, procedure calls, and local variables. There was no separate assembler for the B5000 in the normal sense — ALGOL 60 (or ESPOL, a systems-programming dialect of ALGOL) was the native language. The operating system was written in ESPOL. Applications were written in ALGOL 60. The machine was a commercial success, selling for two decades through its successors (B5500, B5700, B6700, B7700, and the Unisys A-series which is still produced today).

The B5000 is the only example in computing history of a mainstream commercial computer architecture designed around a specific high-level language. Lisp Machines (Symbolics, LMI) were research-grade and niche. The B5000 and its descendants handled Fortune 500 workloads for forty years.

The ACM Algorithms Collection

Starting in the early 1960s, Communications of the ACM began publishing short implementations of useful algorithms in ALGOL 60, each with a number and a certificate of correctness. This was the ACM Collected Algorithms. The early contributions were mostly numerical (root finding, matrix operations, quadrature, sorting) and mostly in ALGOL 60, because ALGOL was the community’s publication language of choice.

The most famous contribution was by a young British mathematician named C. A. R. Hoare, who was working at Elliott Brothers. His Algorithm 64: Quicksort, published in the July 1961 CACM, was accompanied by Algorithm 63: Partition. It is, by a wide margin, the most cited algorithm in computing history. Every implementation of Quicksort in every language since is a translation of Hoare’s ALGOL 60 code.

The ACM Algorithms collection grew into the hundreds, then the thousands. Many of the NAG Library’s numerical routines began life as ALGOL 60 algorithms in CACM. The tradition continues in Fortran and in the ACM Transactions on Mathematical Software, but ALGOL 60 was the original medium.

The ALGOL 68 Committee and the Schism

In 1962, IFIP (the International Federation for Information Processing) established Working Group 2.1 to consider the future of ALGOL. The committee began meeting regularly. It was clear by the mid-1960s that ALGOL 60 needed revision: the language had no I/O, no strings in any serious sense, limited data structures, and some known ambiguities. The question was what direction the revision should take.

Two camps emerged.

The Maximalists: van Wijngaarden and the Extendable Base Language

Adriaan van Wijngaarden, director of the Mathematisch Centrum in Amsterdam, led a faction that wanted to push the language further. They wanted orthogonality: every data type should compose with every other, every operator should be user-definable, every construct should generalize. They wanted coercions (automatic type conversions), unions (tagged variants), first-class references (a pointer-like mechanism that was more principled than C’s would be), parallel programming (the “collateral clauses”), and two-level grammars (a notation more expressive than BNF, capable of describing context-sensitive constraints in the grammar itself).

Key van Wijngaarden allies included Charles Lindsey (Manchester), Cornelis Koster (Amsterdam), and Barry Mailloux (who did much of the spec drafting).

The Minimalists: Dijkstra, Hoare, Wirth, and the Minority Report

The other faction wanted a conservative extension of ALGOL 60: fix the known ambiguities, add proper strings and I/O, maybe add records and pointers, but keep the language small enough that a grad student could implement a compiler in a reasonable time. Leaders of this faction included:

  • Edsger W. Dijkstra — by then at Eindhoven, famous for his Amsterdam ALGOL 60 implementation and for the “Go To Statement Considered Harmful” letter (1968)
  • C. A. R. (Tony) Hoare — the Quicksort author, by then at Belfast
  • Niklaus Wirth — a young Swiss-American researcher who had worked on Euler and would soon propose ALGOL W
  • Brian Randell, Peter Landin, Jan Garwick, Michael Woodger, Gerhard Seegmüller, Fraser Duncan, Władysław Turski

Wirth and Hoare, specifically, drafted an alternative proposal in 1966: ALGOL W. It was smaller, simpler, and closer to ALGOL 60. The WG 2.1 committee rejected it in favor of van Wijngaarden’s grander design. Wirth took ALGOL W back to ETH Zurich and used it as the foundation for Pascal (1970). See Wirth Family.

The Minority Report

In December 1968, at the WG 2.1 meeting in Munich, the ALGOL 68 draft was approved over the objections of the minimalist faction. Eight members of the committee signed a Minority Report protesting the decision. The signers were Dijkstra, Duncan, Garwick, Hoare, Randell, Seegmüller, Turski, and Woodger. The report appeared in the ALGOL Bulletin and in ACM SIGPLAN Notices:

“We regard the current Report on Algorithmic Language ALGOL 68 as the fruit of an effort that should have been distributed differently. As a device for the communication of algorithms and, in general, the exchange of computing knowledge, we believe it to be a failure.”

— Dijkstra, Duncan, Garwick, Hoare, Randell, Seegmüller, Turski, Woodger (1968)

Dijkstra’s individual statement is even harsher: he said the language was too complex, the spec was incomprehensible, the implementation burden was enormous, and the language would not serve its users well. Hoare wrote a separate 1973 paper, “Hints on Programming Language Design,” that is essentially a 20-page critique of ALGOL 68 without ever naming it directly.

History proved the minority right, though it took years. ALGOL 68 was officially published as the Report on the Algorithmic Language ALGOL 68 in 1969 and revised in 1975. A handful of implementations were produced — ALGOL 68-R (Royal Signals Research Establishment UK, 1970), ALGOL 68C (Cambridge), ALGOL 68S (Sue Evans and colleagues) — but none gained traction. The language was too complex for the era’s compiler technology and too different from existing practice.

Meanwhile, Wirth went back to Zurich and built Pascal, which taught a generation of students and became the foundation of Turbo Pascal, Delphi, and eventually C#. Dijkstra pursued structured programming and the correctness movement. Hoare developed axiomatic semantics and the Communicating Sequential Processes (CSP) model that would influence Occam, Go, and Clojure’s core.async. The minimalists, having lost the battle, won the war.

What ALGOL 68 Got Right

In fairness, ALGOL 68 had ideas that were ahead of their time. Many of them quietly influenced later languages:

  • First-class references — a principled treatment of pointers that avoided C’s worst pitfalls. Modern languages are still working through the issues ALGOL 68 raised.
  • Unions (tagged variants) — appeared decades later in ML-family languages and Rust’s enum.
  • User-defined operators — standard in C++, Haskell, Scala, Rust.
  • Collateral clauses — a form of parallel evaluation that anticipated modern parallelism primitives.
  • Coercions — later formalized in subtyping theory.
  • Orthogonality as a design principle — taught in every language-design course.
  • Typed strings — fixed-length and flexible, anticipating later work on string theory.

ALGOL 68 was not wrong; it was just too ambitious for 1968 hardware, 1968 compiler theory, and 1968 programmers. It demonstrated ideas that had to wait a decade or two to be properly implemented.

Selected Timeline

1943 Emil Post publishes production systems

Post’s formal string rewriting systems predate BNF by 16 years. Backus would cite Post as inspiration.

1954 Manchester Mark 1 Autocode (Brooker)

One of the first high-level languages on any machine.

1956 Chomsky “Three Models for the Description of Language”

The Chomsky hierarchy. Context-free grammars are Type 2.

1957 Fortran I released (IBM, Backus team)

The first commercially successful high-level language.

1958 May Zurich meeting — ALGOL 58 (IAL)

Eight participants. Bauer & Samelson bring the operator-precedence stack parser idea.

1959 Backus presents BNF at ICIP Paris

“The Syntax and Semantics of the Proposed International Algebraic Language.” First formal specification notation for a programming language.

1960 January Paris meeting — ALGOL 60 committee

13 participants. McCarthy contributes if-expressions and mandatory recursion. Naur serves as editor.

1960 May ALGOL 60 Report published in CACM

The most lucid language spec ever written. About 20 pages. Uses BNF throughout.

1961 Burroughs B5000 announced

First (and only) commercial machine designed specifically to run ALGOL 60.

1961 July Hoare publishes Quicksort in ALGOL 60

CACM Algorithm 64. The most cited algorithm in computing history.

1962 IFIP Working Group 2.1 formed

The committee that would produce ALGOL 68.

1963 Revised ALGOL 60 Report

Clarifies ambiguities in the 1960 report.

1964 Knuth letter to CACM on “Backus-Naur Form”

Knuth proposes the name Backus-Naur Form instead of Backus Normal Form.

1964 Randell & Russell publish ALGOL 60 Implementation

Classic compiler construction book.

1964 Knuth’s “Man or boy?” test

Designed to separate real ALGOL 60 implementations from fake ones.

1965 Knuth “On the Translation of Languages from Left to Right”

The LR parsing paper. Theoretical foundation of modern parser generators.

1966 Wirth & Hoare propose ALGOL W

Alternative to what would become ALGOL 68. Rejected by WG 2.1.

1967 Simula 67 — Dahl and Nygaard

Adds classes, objects, and inheritance to ALGOL 60. Invents object-oriented programming.

1967 Knuth “The Remaining Trouble Spots in ALGOL 60”

Edge cases in the revised Report.

1968 March Dijkstra “Go To Considered Harmful”

CACM letter. Dijkstra’s critique of unstructured control flow.

1968 December ALGOL 68 approved, Minority Report signed

Dijkstra, Hoare, Wirth, and five others formally protest.

1969 ALGOL 68 Report published

Van Wijngaarden grammars instead of BNF. Highly orthogonal, highly complex.

1970 Pascal published — Wirth, Acta Informatica

The minimalist response. Becomes a teaching language, then a commercial language via Turbo Pascal (1983).

1973 Hoare “Hints on Programming Language Design”

Stanford CS TR 403. A sustained critique of language complexity, aimed at ALGOL 68.

1975 ALGOL 68 Revised Report + yacc + Wirth EBNF

Revised ALGOL 68. Johnson’s yacc at Bell Labs. Wirth’s “What Can We Do About Diversity of Notation” introduces EBNF.

1978 Modula-2 — Wirth

Pascal plus separate compilation and modules.

1983 Ada 83 — DoD MIL-STD-1815

Pascal-family language for safety-critical systems.

1983 Turbo Pascal — Anders Hejlsberg / Borland

$49.95. Fast compiler. Pascal becomes commercial.

1985 Bison — GNU Project

Yacc-compatible parser generator under the GPL.

1986 Oberon — Wirth and Gutknecht

Minimalist Modula successor. Runs on Wirth’s Ceres workstation.

1986 Tomita’s Generalized LR parser

Handles arbitrary context-free grammars, including ambiguous ones. Parse forest.

2001 Dahl and Nygaard win Turing Award

For Simula 67 and inventing object-oriented programming. Both die within 2 years of the award.

2004 Ford “Parsing Expression Grammars”

PEGs as a cleaner alternative to BNF. Packrat parsing.

2018 Tree-sitter by Max Brunsfeld

Incremental parsing library for editors. GLR-like. Used by Atom, Neovim, Helix, VS Code.

2024 January 1 Niklaus Wirth dies

The community loses its most important language designer. Pascal, Modula, Oberon, and the minimalist tradition.
