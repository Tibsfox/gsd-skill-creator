Simula 67 — The Birth of Object-Oriented Programming

The single most consequential descendant of ALGOL 60 is Simula. Simula was created at the Norwegian Computing Center (Norsk Regnesentral) in Oslo, by Ole-Johan Dahl (1931–2002) and Kristen Nygaard (1926–2002). They were working on simulation problems — specifically, problems in operations research involving ships, ports, cargo flow, and discrete-event queueing.

Simula I (1962–1965) ran on a UNIVAC 1107 and was a genuine ALGOL extension: its source language was compiled by a preprocessor that emitted ALGOL 60, which was then compiled by the UNIVAC’s ALGOL compiler. The crucial Simula I concept was the process — an entity that could be suspended and resumed, with its own local state.

Simula 67: The Breakthrough

Dahl and Nygaard realized that the process concept could be generalized. The language was formally specified in Simula 67 Common Base Language (Dahl, Myhrhaug, Nygaard, 1968, Norwegian Computing Center publication S-22). It introduced, for the first time in any programming language, the complete cluster of features we now call object-oriented programming:

  • Classes — templates from which objects are instantiated. Dahl and Nygaard coined this use of the word “class” as a programming concept.
  • Objects — the instances themselves, each with their own state. They coined this use of “object” too.
  • Inheritance — one class can extend another. Simula called this a “subclass” declared with the keyword prefix.
  • Virtual procedures — procedures that can be overridden in subclasses and dispatched dynamically based on the runtime type. Declared with the keyword virtual. The ancestor of every vtable in every C++ program.
  • Attributes with protected and public access — the ancestor of private/protected/public visibility modifiers.
  • Coroutines via detach/resume — the ancestor of every generator, every async/await, every green thread.
  • References — typed pointers to heap-allocated objects, without explicit memory management.
  • Garbage collection — Simula 67 had garbage collection in 1967, three years before Smalltalk and nearly 30 years before Java.
CLASS node;
BEGIN
   REF(node) next;
   INTEGER data;
END;

node CLASS integerList;
BEGIN
   INTEGER length;

   PROCEDURE append(x); INTEGER x;
   BEGIN
      REF(node) n;
      n :- NEW node;
      n.data := x;
      n.next :- next;
      next :- n;
      length := length + 1;
   END;
END;

The :- operator is reference assignment; := is value assignment. NEW allocates on the heap and invokes the class’s initialization. Every modern object-oriented language inherits this shape, even when the syntax has mutated.

The Vocabulary

One of the under-appreciated facts: Dahl and Nygaard did not merely invent object-oriented programming — they invented the vocabulary. Before Simula 67, “class” in computing meant something closer to a mathematical classification, not a program structure. “Object” meant any data item. After Simula 67, these words carried their modern technical meanings.

The phrase “object-oriented” was coined later, by Alan Kay at Xerox PARC around 1972–1974, when Kay was designing Smalltalk. Kay has repeatedly credited Simula as the direct inspiration.

Why Simulation Required Objects

If you are writing a simulation of a harbor — with ships arriving, docks processing cargo, trucks waiting, cranes operating, tides changing, crews going on and off shift — procedural decomposition becomes impossible. Each of those entities has its own state, its own rules, its own schedule, its own interactions. You end up writing a dispatch loop that branches on “what kind of thing is this?” and keeps parallel arrays of state for each kind. Simula made that pattern first-class.

OOP was not invented for UI toolkits or business software. It was invented for scientific simulation — a use case where objects with independent state, behavior, and history are not a design choice but the only reasonable abstraction.

Recognition

Dahl and Nygaard won the ACM A. M. Turing Award in 2001 “For ideas fundamental to the emergence of object-oriented programming, through their design of the programming languages Simula I and Simula 67.” They also won the IEEE John von Neumann Medal in 2002. Both men died in 2002, the year after receiving the Turing Award. Dahl passed away on June 29, 2002; Nygaard on August 10, 2002.

Simula’s Descendants: Two Lines

Simula 67 had two lines of direct influence:

  • Line 1 (dynamic OOP): Smalltalk (1972) → Self, Objective-C, Ruby, Python’s object model, JavaScript’s prototype chain
  • Line 2 (static OOP): C with Classes / C++ (1979–1985) → Java, C#, Scala, Kotlin, Swift

Bjarne Stroustrup, the creator of C++, encountered Simula as a graduate student at Cambridge. He wrote his PhD thesis using Simula 67 for distributed system simulation. He found that Simula’s compile-time static typing combined with inheritance gave him a productivity he could not replicate in any other language of the era. When he moved to Bell Labs and began work on “C with Classes” (1979–1983, renamed C++ in 1983), he was explicitly trying to bring Simula’s features to a language with C’s efficiency. In The Design and Evolution of C++ (1994), Stroustrup writes:

“Simula 67 was the origin. I learned about classes, inheritance, virtual functions, and run-time dispatch from Simula 67, and those concepts form the core of C++. The goal of C++ was to combine the generality and flexibility of Simula’s type system with the efficiency and hardware proximity of C.”

— Bjarne Stroustrup, 1994

There is no C++ without Simula. There is no Java without C++. There is no C# without Java and Delphi. There is no Kotlin without Java. The entire static-OOP branch of modern programming traces directly to Dahl and Nygaard’s 1967 decision to generalize the Simula I process into the Simula 67 class.

C as an Algol Descendant

People sometimes forget that C is in the Algol family. The lineage:

  • CPL (1963) — “Combined Programming Language,” a British effort by Strachey at Cambridge and Barron at London. Ambitious, too complex to implement, never finished.
  • BCPL (1967) — Martin Richards at Cambridge. A simplified, typeless CPL. Used for the first portable systems programming.
  • B (1969) — Ken Thompson at Bell Labs. Further simplification of BCPL, used for early Unix utilities.
  • C (1972) — Dennis Ritchie at Bell Labs. Added types, structures, arrays with pointer decay, preprocessing.

What C Inherited from ALGOL 60

  • Block structure with lexical scope
  • Static type system (simplified, weak)
  • Proper recursion
  • Structured control flow: if, while, for, break, continue, return
  • Typed procedures and functions
  • Stack-based activation records

What C Abandoned from ALGOL 60

  • Call-by-name (good choice)
  • Dynamic array bounds (debatable)
  • Strict typing (bad choice in retrospect, caused decades of vulnerabilities)
  • Proper nested functions (nested functions in C are a GCC extension, not standard)
  • Proper bounds checking (lost to performance)

What C Added

  • Pointers as arithmetic entities (unique to the C family)
  • Explicit memory layout
  • Bit operations as first-class
  • A macro preprocessor (separate language stage)
  • Arrays as syntactic sugar for pointer arithmetic

The K&R book — The C Programming Language by Kernighan and Ritchie, 1978 — is the other most-elegant programming language book after the Algol 60 Report. It is short, clear, and complete, and it taught a generation how to think about systems programming.

Java, C#, Kotlin — The Corporate Algol Descendants

Java (1995, James Gosling at Sun) was explicitly designed to be “simpler than C++.” Java’s heritage:

  • C++ syntax (braces, semicolons, int main style)
  • C blocks and structured control
  • Pascal-like strict typing (Gosling read Wirth)
  • Smalltalk-like garbage collection and single-inheritance object model
  • Simula’s class/method structure

The JVM as a VM target inherits from the Pascal P-machine tradition (see Wirth Family). The idea of compiling to a portable intermediate representation, then interpreting or JIT-compiling that representation, was Wirth’s Pascal P-code from 1975.

C# (2000, Anders Hejlsberg at Microsoft) was designed by the creator of Turbo Pascal and Delphi. Hejlsberg brought Delphi sensibilities to Microsoft. C# inherited from:

  • Java (most syntax, most semantics)
  • Delphi (component model, properties, events)
  • C++ (templates became generics)
  • Pascal (strong typing, properties as first-class members)

Kotlin (2011, JetBrains) is a Java descendant that adds null safety, data classes, and extension functions — all ideas that can be traced back to the Algol-family type-system tradition.

Scala (2003, Martin Odersky) is Java + functional programming, with a type system heavily influenced by ML rather than by Algol. But it still runs on the JVM and interoperates with Java, so its block structure and syntactic foundation are Algol-family.

Go and Rust — The Return to Simplicity

Go (2009, Robert Griesemer, Rob Pike, Ken Thompson at Google) is deliberately simple in a way that echoes Pascal and Oberon. Griesemer was Wirth’s doctoral student at ETH Zürich, and Go’s design sensibilities trace directly to the Wirth tradition:

  • Fast compilation (a core Wirth value)
  • Small language (Go has no classes, no inheritance, no generics until 1.18, no exceptions)
  • Explicit error handling
  • Strong typing but with structural interfaces
  • Built-in concurrency primitives (goroutines from CSP, channels from Hoare’s work)

Rust (2010, Graydon Hoare at Mozilla) is C++ made safe. Its block structure, lexical scope, and type system are ALGOL-family through and through. The ML-family type system is grafted on top. Rust inherits:

  • Block structure from ALGOL 60
  • Lifetime-based memory management from C++’s RAII (which came from Simula)
  • Algebraic data types from ML/Haskell
  • Trait-based polymorphism from Haskell type classes
  • Syntax from C++

Rust is perhaps the purest synthesis of the Algol-family (structure, scope, safety) and ML-family (type system, algebraic data) traditions.

The Algol Family Resemblance

Features that all Algol descendants share:

  • Block structure with { } or begin...end
  • Lexical scoping with static name resolution
  • Structured control flow — if/else, while, for, break, continue
  • Proper recursion as a natural consequence of the stack discipline
  • Typed variables (even if inferred)
  • Compound data types — records/structs, arrays
  • Expression/statement distinction (Algol-family languages tend to have both)

Features that Algol 60 did NOT have but most descendants added:

  • Strong static type systems (Algol 60’s was weak)
  • Explicit pointers/references (Algol 60 had neither properly)
  • Object orientation (Simula added it)
  • Exception handling (CLU and Ada added it)
  • Generics (CLU, Ada, then Java)
  • Modules and separate compilation (Modula)
  • Lambda/closures (functional-influenced languages, 2000s onward)

What the Algol Tradition Means

Algol was a paper language — designed to be read and written by humans, specified for clarity not for machines. The Algol tradition privileges:

  • Readability
  • Structural clarity
  • Formal specification
  • Pedagogical value
  • Portability

The Algol tradition opposes:

  • Machine-specific notation
  • Hand-tuned assembly escape hatches
  • Unreadable shortcuts
  • Undefined behavior

This tradition split from the C tradition in the 1980s. C inherited Algol’s structure but abandoned its ideals of portability and safety. For 30 years the C tradition dominated systems programming. In the 2010s and 2020s, modern languages began leaning back toward the Algol tradition of safety + clarity: Rust, Swift, Go, Kotlin all take Algol ideas seriously. The debate between the two traditions is not over.
