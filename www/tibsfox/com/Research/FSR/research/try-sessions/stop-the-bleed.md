# Try Session: Stop the Bleed --- Hemorrhage Control Basics

## The Second Most Important Skill Any Citizen Can Learn

If hands-only CPR is the most impactful skill a bystander can learn for cardiac emergencies, hemorrhage control is its counterpart for traumatic injuries. Uncontrolled bleeding --- hemorrhage --- is the leading cause of preventable death in trauma. In active shooter events, industrial accidents, car crashes, and everyday incidents involving sharp objects or heavy machinery, the difference between survival and death is often measured in the minutes it takes for someone to apply effective pressure to a wound.

The National Trauma Institute estimates that approximately 30,000 people die annually in the United States from hemorrhagic shock --- blood loss so severe that the circulatory system can no longer maintain organ perfusion. Many of these deaths occur before the person reaches a hospital. A significant number occur while the person is still at the scene, waiting for EMS to arrive. And a meaningful subset of those deaths are preventable. Not by paramedics, who are still en route. Not by surgeons, who are at the hospital. By the person standing next to the bleeding victim, if that person knows what to do.

This try session teaches you three techniques. They are simple. They are effective. They come from military combat casualty care research that has been saving lives on battlefields since 2005 and that the Stop the Bleed campaign has been translating to civilian settings since 2015.

---

## The Origin: From Battlefield to Classroom

The techniques in this try session trace directly to the Committee on Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC), a military medical research body that revolutionized prehospital trauma care for the U.S. armed forces. Before TCCC, the military approach to battlefield trauma was based on civilian ATLS (Advanced Trauma Life Support) protocols --- appropriate for ambulances and emergency rooms, poorly suited to the realities of combat.

TCCC's research, published incrementally from 1996 onward, established that the leading cause of preventable death on the battlefield was hemorrhage from extremity wounds. Soldiers were bleeding to death from gunshot and blast injuries to arms and legs --- injuries that were survivable if bleeding was controlled quickly. The solution was not better surgical care at the hospital. The solution was giving every soldier the tools and training to stop bleeding at the point of injury.

The civilian application of this research accelerated after the Sandy Hook Elementary School shooting on December 14, 2012. In the aftermath, the American College of Surgeons convened the Hartford Consensus, which concluded that the same principles that saved lives on battlefields could save lives in mass casualty events at home. The result was the Stop the Bleed campaign, launched in 2015, with the explicit goal of training the American public in basic hemorrhage control --- the same way the CPR training movement had trained the public in cardiac arrest response.

The parallel is exact. CPR saved lives because citizens learned it and used it before paramedics arrived. Hemorrhage control will save lives for the same reason.

## Recognizing Life-Threatening Bleeding

Not all bleeding is life-threatening. A cut finger bleeds and stops. A scraped knee oozes and clots. Life-threatening bleeding looks different, and recognizing it is the first step.

**Signs of life-threatening hemorrhage:**
- Blood that is spurting or pulsing from the wound (arterial bleeding)
- Blood that is flowing steadily and will not stop with light pressure
- Blood that is pooling on the ground beneath the person
- A person who is confused, pale, clammy, or losing consciousness (signs of hemorrhagic shock)
- Blood soaking through clothing rapidly
- An amputation or partial amputation
- A wound from a high-energy mechanism: gunshot, explosion, machinery, vehicle collision

**The mental model:** If you see blood and think "that's a lot of blood," it probably is. An adult has approximately 5 liters (about 1.3 gallons) of blood. Losing 30-40 percent of blood volume --- 1.5 to 2 liters --- produces life-threatening hemorrhagic shock. That is less than half a gallon. On a floor, it looks like a shockingly small puddle for something that can kill.

## Technique 1: Direct Pressure

Direct pressure is the simplest, most universally applicable hemorrhage control technique. It requires nothing except your hands and something to press with. It works for most wounds in most locations.

### How to Apply Direct Pressure

1. **Expose the wound.** If the wound is covered by clothing, move or cut the clothing away so you can see exactly where the blood is coming from. You cannot effectively treat what you cannot see.

2. **Pack the wound.** If the wound is more than a superficial cut --- if it has depth, if it is a laceration or puncture with a cavity --- you need to pack material INTO the wound. Grab whatever clean cloth is available: gauze if you have it, a t-shirt, a towel, a scarf. Stuff it into the wound cavity. Push it to the bottom. This is not dabbing. This is not laying gauze on top. You are filling the hole with fabric so that the pressure you apply is transmitted to the blood vessels that are bleeding, deep inside the wound.

3. **Press HARD.** Place both hands over the packed wound and lean into it with your body weight. This is not gentle pressure. This is as hard as you can push, sustained. You are trying to compress the torn blood vessels closed so that clotting can begin. Light pressure does not work on significant hemorrhage. Hard pressure hurts the patient. Apply it anyway.

4. **Do not lift to check.** This is the mistake that costs lives. You apply pressure. After a minute, you want to see if it is working. You lift the gauze. The clot that was forming is disrupted. The bleeding restarts. You have just reset the clock. Once you apply pressure, you do not remove it. If blood soaks through the first layer of packing, add more material on top and press harder. Do not remove the blood-soaked material --- it is participating in the clotting process.

5. **Maintain pressure until help arrives.** Direct pressure is a commitment. You may need to hold pressure for 10, 15, 20 minutes or longer. Your hands will cramp. Your arms will fatigue. Keep pressing. If another bystander is available, they can take over, but the transition must be fast --- one person's hands replacing another's without releasing pressure.

### When Direct Pressure Is Not Enough

Direct pressure works for most wounds. It does not work when:
- The wound is so large or so deep that you cannot effectively pack it
- The wound is in a location where you cannot apply sufficient pressure (junction areas: groin, armpit, neck)
- The bleeding is from an extremity and direct pressure alone cannot stop it
- You are the only responder and you need your hands free to manage other injuries or call for help

When direct pressure fails on an extremity wound, you move to a tourniquet.

## Technique 2: Wound Packing

Wound packing is the technique that bridges the gap between simple direct pressure and tourniquet application. It is the technique that saves lives in penetrating trauma --- stabbings, gunshots, deep lacerations, industrial injuries. It is the technique that most civilians have never heard of and that every combat medic knows by heart.

### The Principle

A deep wound bleeds from torn blood vessels that are located inside the wound cavity --- not at the surface. Laying gauze on top of a deep wound is like putting a bandage on the roof of a building with a broken pipe in the basement. The problem is inside. The solution must go inside.

Wound packing places material directly against the bleeding vessels, deep in the wound, and then fills the remaining cavity tightly so that external pressure is transmitted all the way to the source of bleeding. The packed material also provides a surface for clot formation. Hemostatic gauze --- gauze impregnated with clotting agents like kaolin or chitosan --- accelerates this process, but plain gauze or clean cloth works if hemostatic gauze is not available.

### How to Pack a Wound

1. **Expose the wound completely.** Remove or cut away clothing. You need to see the full extent of the wound.

2. **Identify the deepest point of the wound.** This is where you start. Not at the surface. At the bottom.

3. **Take your gauze (or clean cloth) and push it into the wound cavity.** Use your fingers. Push the material to the deepest point of the wound. This will be uncomfortable for you psychologically --- you are pushing your fingers and fabric into a person's body. It will be painful for the patient if they are conscious. You are saving their life. Push to the bottom.

4. **Continue feeding material into the wound, packing tightly.** Layer after layer. Fill the cavity completely. Pack it as tightly as you can. There should be no empty space. The material should be under tension. You are building a plug --- a physical barrier that, combined with external pressure, will compress the bleeding vessels.

5. **Once the wound is packed to the surface level, apply direct pressure on top.** Both hands, body weight, sustained. Hold.

6. **If using hemostatic gauze (QuikClot Combat Gauze, Celox Gauze, or similar):** The technique is identical. The hemostatic agent works by accelerating the clotting cascade at the point of contact. It must be in direct contact with the bleeding source to be effective. This means the hemostatic gauze goes in first, at the deepest point, and plain gauze or cloth fills the remaining cavity.

### Practice Note

You cannot practice wound packing on a pillow. Commercial wound-packing trainers are available for $20-50 and provide realistic wound cavities for practice. Many Stop the Bleed courses include hands-on wound-packing stations. If you take one thing from this section, let it be this: wound packing is the technique that most people do not know about and that makes the biggest difference in penetrating trauma.

## Technique 3: Tourniquet Application

A tourniquet is a device that compresses all blood vessels in an extremity (arm or leg) to stop bleeding distal to (below) the tourniquet. It is the definitive hemorrhage control tool for extremity bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure or wound packing. For decades, tourniquets were considered a last resort in civilian medicine, taught as dangerous devices that would cause limb loss. Military research has thoroughly debunked this. Properly applied tourniquets save limbs and lives. Limb loss from tourniquet application is extraordinarily rare when the tourniquet is applied for less than 2 hours, and the vast majority of civilian tourniquet applications are removed well within that window at the hospital.

### The CAT Tourniquet

The Combat Application Tourniquet (CAT) is the most widely used tourniquet in both military and civilian settings. It is the tourniquet included in most commercial bleeding control kits and the tourniquet used in Stop the Bleed training. A CAT consists of a nylon band with a velcro closure, a windlass rod, and a windlass clip to secure the rod once tightened. It is designed to be applied with one hand (so that a wounded person can apply it to their own limb) and to generate enough circumferential pressure to occlude arterial blood flow.

### How to Apply a CAT Tourniquet

1. **Place the tourniquet HIGH AND TIGHT.** The tourniquet goes as high on the extremity as possible --- as close to the torso as you can get it. On a leg, that means high on the thigh, up in the groin area. On an arm, high on the upper arm, near the armpit. The reason for "high and tight" is that you may not be able to identify the exact source of bleeding, especially if there are multiple wounds or if blood is obscuring the anatomy. Going high ensures you are above the injury.

2. **Pull the band tight and secure the velcro.** The band should be tight enough that it does not slip. Not tight enough to stop the bleeding yet --- that is what the windlass is for.

3. **Turn the windlass rod.** Rotate the windlass rod (the stick that sticks out of the tourniquet mechanism) in a circular motion. Each turn tightens the band. Keep turning until the bleeding stops. This will hurt. On a conscious patient, tourniquet application is extremely painful. Apply it anyway. Pain is survivable. Exsanguination is not.

4. **Secure the windlass.** Once bleeding has stopped, clip the windlass rod into the retention clip so it cannot unwind. Some CAT models have a strap that secures over the windlass for additional retention.

5. **WRITE THE TIME.** This is critical. Write the time of tourniquet application on the tourniquet itself, on the patient's forehead, on their arm, on a piece of tape --- anywhere that the next medical provider will see it. Use "TQ" followed by the time: "TQ 14:32." The time tells the hospital team how long the tourniquet has been in place, which influences their management decisions. Every commercial CAT has a white band specifically designed to be written on.

6. **Do not remove the tourniquet.** Once applied, the tourniquet stays on until it is removed by a medical professional at a hospital. Removing a tourniquet in the field can cause sudden release of toxins from the ischemic limb (reperfusion injury) and the resumption of life-threatening hemorrhage. This is a hospital procedure, not a field procedure.

### The 30-Second Standard

Military training sets a standard of applying a CAT tourniquet in under 30 seconds. For civilians, the standard is less rigid, but the principle is the same: the faster you apply it, the less blood the patient loses. Practice makes speed.

### Practice: Buy a CAT Trainer

A training CAT tourniquet costs approximately $25-30. (Do not practice with a real CAT --- they are single-use devices that may be weakened by repeated application.) Buy a trainer and practice applying it to your own thigh. Time yourself. Practice until you can do it in under 30 seconds. Practice with both hands. Practice in dim light. Practice while your heart rate is elevated. The conditions under which you will need to apply a tourniquet will not be calm, well-lit, or unhurried.

**Where to buy:** CAT tourniquet trainers are available from North American Rescue, TacMed Solutions, Amazon, and most tactical/EMS supply retailers. Ensure you are buying a genuine CAT trainer, not a counterfeit. The genuine article is manufactured by North American Rescue (NAR).

## Where to Take the Class

The Stop the Bleed course is free, takes approximately 60-90 minutes, and includes hands-on practice with direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquet application. It is taught at:

- **Hospitals:** Many trauma centers and community hospitals host monthly or quarterly Stop the Bleed classes. Check your local hospital's community education calendar.
- **Fire departments:** Many fire departments offer Stop the Bleed as part of their community education programs. Call your local station and ask.
- **American Red Cross:** Offers Stop the Bleed as a standalone course and as part of broader first aid training.
- **Schools and workplaces:** Many school districts and employers are integrating Stop the Bleed into safety training. Ask your HR department or school administration.
- **CERT teams:** Community Emergency Response Team training often includes hemorrhage control as part of the medical operations module.

To find a class near you: visit **stopthebleed.org** and use the course finder. Classes are offered in all 50 states.

## Building a Personal Bleeding Control Kit

A basic bleeding control kit costs $30-50 and fits in a glove compartment, a backpack, a desk drawer, or a kitchen cabinet. Having one means you have the right tools immediately available rather than improvising with whatever cloth is at hand.

### Basic Kit Contents

- **1 CAT tourniquet** (genuine, from North American Rescue): ~$30
- **1 package hemostatic gauze** (QuikClot Combat Gauze or Celox Gauze, z-fold): ~$15
- **1 package compressed gauze** (plain, z-fold): ~$5
- **1 emergency trauma dressing** (Israeli Bandage or OLAES): ~$10
- **1 pair trauma shears** (for cutting clothing): ~$5
- **1 pair nitrile gloves**: ~$1

Total: approximately $65 for a kit that covers direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquet application. Pre-assembled kits are available from North American Rescue, TacMed Solutions, and other EMS suppliers for similar prices.

### Where to Keep It

- In your car. Vehicle accidents are the most common scenario requiring hemorrhage control.
- At your workplace. Especially if your workplace involves tools, machinery, or physical labor.
- In your home. Kitchen knife injuries, power tool injuries, and falls through glass are more common than most people realize.
- In your hiking or outdoor recreation pack.

Having the kit matters only if you know how to use it. Practice the techniques in this document. Take a Stop the Bleed class. Then carry the kit.

## The MARCH Mnemonic

Military TCCC organizes its assessment priorities using the MARCH mnemonic, which applies equally to civilian trauma. Understanding MARCH gives you a framework for prioritizing interventions when someone is injured:

- **M --- Massive hemorrhage.** Stop the bleeding first. This is the most immediately life-threatening issue and the most treatable by a bystander. This is what this try session teaches.
- **A --- Airway.** Ensure the airway is open. For an unconscious person, tilt the head back and lift the chin. This is basic first aid.
- **R --- Respiration.** Check for adequate breathing. Look for chest wounds that may be causing a pneumothorax (collapsed lung). Seal open chest wounds with an occlusive dressing (or a credit card and tape, in a pinch).
- **C --- Circulation.** Assess for shock. Signs: pale skin, rapid pulse, confusion, cold extremities. Keep the person warm. Elevate their legs if no spinal injury is suspected.
- **H --- Hypothermia.** Prevent heat loss. Trauma patients lose body heat rapidly. Cover them with blankets, coats, whatever is available.

The order is the priority. M comes first because massive hemorrhage kills fastest.

## The Numbers

- Uncontrolled hemorrhage is the **leading cause of preventable death** in trauma, both military and civilian.
- A person can bleed to death from a severed femoral artery in **2-5 minutes**.
- Average EMS response time in the United States: **7-14 minutes** (longer in rural areas).
- A properly applied tourniquet stops extremity bleeding in **under 60 seconds**.
- The math is stark: if the bleeding will kill in 3 minutes and EMS arrives in 8, the only person who can save that life is the one who is already there.

The Stop the Bleed campaign's goal is the same goal that the CPR training movement pursued: to turn bystanders into the first tier of the emergency response system. Not to turn citizens into paramedics. To teach them one thing, well, so that when the worst happens, they do not stand by and watch.

You are now someone who knows the three techniques. Use them if you need to.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Anatomy/Physiology]: The cardiovascular system's response to hemorrhage --- compensatory tachycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction, the clotting cascade (platelets, fibrin mesh, clot retraction), and how external compression assists the body's hemostatic mechanisms. Understanding arterial vs. venous bleeding and why pressure must reach the vessel level.
- **Physical Education** --- [Physical Endurance]: Sustaining direct pressure on a wound for 10-20 minutes requires upper body strength and endurance. Tourniquet application requires grip strength and the fine motor control to manipulate the windlass under stress. Physical fitness directly translates to hemorrhage control capability.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Performing Under Stress]: The sight of significant bleeding triggers a visceral stress response. Tunnel vision, nausea, trembling hands, and the overwhelming impulse to look away are normal physiological reactions. Training and practice create the neural pathways that allow deliberate action despite the stress response. This is the same mechanism that allows firefighters to enter burning buildings --- not the absence of fear, but the presence of trained behavior that overrides it.
