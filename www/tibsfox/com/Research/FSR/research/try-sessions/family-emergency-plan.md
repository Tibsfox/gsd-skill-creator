# DIY Project: Create a Family Emergency Plan

*The single most important document in your household --- one laminated page that could save your family's life*

---

## Why a Plan Matters More Than Gear

You can build the most comprehensive go bag in the Pacific Northwest [FSR try-sessions/build-a-go-bag]. You can learn CPR [FSR try-sessions/hands-only-cpr], hemorrhage control [FSR try-sessions/stop-the-bleed], and fire extinguisher technique [FSR try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill]. You can stock two weeks of water and food [FSR guides/citizen-preparedness]. And all of it can fail if, when the ground starts shaking at 2 AM, your family does not know where to meet, whom to call, how to communicate, or what to do with the dog.

Gear solves logistics. A plan solves coordination. In a disaster, coordination is what keeps families together, prevents dangerous search-and-rescue decisions driven by panic, and ensures that everyone in the household is working from the same set of assumptions about what happens next.

The family emergency plan is one document. One page. Laminated, posted on the refrigerator, with copies in every go bag and a digital version emailed to your out-of-area contact. It takes an afternoon to create and a yearly drill to maintain. It is the single highest-value emergency preparedness action you can take, because everything else in your preparedness portfolio assumes that the people in your household can find each other and communicate after the event.

## Step 1: Identify Your Hazards

Not every disaster is likely in every location. Your plan should be built around the specific threats your location faces. Start by identifying what could actually happen where you live.

### PNW Hazard Assessment

If you live in western Washington or Oregon, your primary hazards are:

**Earthquake (Cascadia Subduction Zone).** The defining hazard of the Pacific Northwest. A full-margin Cascadia rupture (M9.0) produces 3-5 minutes of shaking, widespread structural damage, bridge failures, utility disruption lasting weeks, and --- for coastal communities --- tsunami arrival within 15-30 minutes. The [Cascadia Earthquake case study](../case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md) covers the scenario in detail. Your plan must account for: structural damage to your home, loss of all utilities, disrupted transportation, and communication system failure lasting days.

**Earthquake (crustal fault).** The Seattle Fault, the Tacoma Fault, the Portland Hills Fault, and numerous other crustal faults can produce M6.0-7.5 earthquakes with little or no warning. These are shallower and more localized than Cascadia events but can cause severe damage in the epicentral area.

**Volcanic hazard (lahars).** If you live in Pierce County within view of Mount Rainier, lahars (volcanic mudflows) are a specific and serious hazard. The Orting-Sumner-Puyallup corridor is built on lahar deposits from previous eruptions. Lahar warning time can be as short as 30-45 minutes. If you live in a lahar zone, your plan must include a specific lahar evacuation route to high ground. USGS and Pierce County Emergency Management publish lahar hazard maps and designated evacuation routes. Know your zone. Know your route. Practice driving it.

**Wildfire.** The wildland-urban interface (WUI) is expanding across the PNW as development pushes into forested areas. If your property borders forested land, brush, or grassland, wildfire is a realistic hazard, particularly in the dry months of July through October. Your plan should include a wildfire evacuation checklist and a pre-identified evacuation route away from the fire's likely path.

**Flooding and landslide.** Winter storms produce river flooding across the PNW lowlands and rain-triggered landslides on steep slopes. If you live in a FEMA-designated flood zone or on or below a steep, unstable slope, these are your seasonal hazards.

**Severe winter storm.** Ice storms, heavy snow, and sustained wind events can knock out power for days, block roads, and isolate neighborhoods. The February 2021 ice storm in Portland demonstrated that even a "non-catastrophic" winter event can disrupt a major city for a week.

### Your Specific Location

Write down the hazards that apply to your address:

```
Our address: ___________________________
Earthquake zone: Yes (everyone in western WA/OR)
Tsunami zone: _____ (check tsunami.wa.gov or oregontsunami.org)
Lahar zone: _____ (check piercecountywa.gov/lahar)
Flood zone: _____ (check FEMA flood maps at msc.fema.gov)
Wildfire interface: _____ (check your county fire district)
Landslide risk: _____ (check WA DNR or DOGAMI maps)
```

## Step 2: Establish Meeting Points

In a disaster, cell phones may not work. Your family may be in different locations (home, work, school). You need pre-agreed meeting points so that everyone converges on the same place without needing to communicate in real time.

### Primary Meeting Point: Your Front Yard

The immediate rally point after an earthquake, fire, or other sudden event at home. Everyone in the house exits and meets at a specific, pre-identified spot in the front yard --- a tree, a mailbox, a specific corner of the driveway. This is the "accountability point" where you confirm that everyone is out of the house and accounted for.

**Choose a spot that is:**
- Away from the house (in case of structural collapse, gas leak, or fire)
- Away from power lines and large trees
- Visible from the street (so arriving responders or neighbors can see you)
- Known to every household member, including children

### Secondary Meeting Point: A Nearby Location

If the house is inaccessible (collapsed, on fire, in a flood zone, in a lahar path), where does the family go? Choose a location within walking distance that everyone knows:

- A neighbor's house (pre-arranged --- talk to them about this)
- A school (if close and not in the same hazard zone)
- A church, community center, or park
- A specific intersection or landmark

**The key criteria:** The secondary meeting point must be reachable on foot from both home and from other locations your family members frequent (work, school, daycare). If the bridges are down and the roads are impassable, can everyone still walk to this point?

### Tertiary Meeting Point: Out-of-Area

If the entire neighborhood is evacuated or inaccessible, where does the family regroup? This could be:

- A relative's house in another city
- A pre-identified shelter or community center outside the affected area
- A hotel or motel in a specific town on a pre-identified evacuation route

## Step 3: Designate an Out-of-Area Contact

This is one of the most important and most overlooked elements of an emergency plan.

**The concept:** In a disaster, local phone lines and cell towers are overwhelmed. Calling across town may be impossible. But calling long-distance often works, because the phone network congestion is local to the disaster area. An out-of-area contact --- someone who lives far away from the Pacific Northwest --- serves as the family's central communication hub.

**How it works:** After the event, every family member calls the out-of-area contact (not each other) and reports their status and location. The out-of-area contact maintains a running log:

```
Mom: Called at 2:47 PM. At secondary meeting point (Lincoln Park). Uninjured.
Dad: Called at 3:12 PM. At work downtown. Roads impassable. Walking home. 
  Estimated arrival 2 hours.
Grandma: Called at 2:55 PM. At home. House intact. No injuries. 
  Has the dog.
```

This centralizes communication through a single person who is NOT in the disaster zone, does NOT have overwhelmed local phone service, and CAN relay information between family members who cannot reach each other directly.

**Choose someone who:**
- Lives outside the PNW (not in the same disaster zone)
- Is reliably available (retirees, work-from-home, semi-retired)
- Has a landline or reliable cell service
- Is organized enough to maintain a log
- Is someone every family member has a relationship with (Aunt in Texas, college friend in Denver, sibling in Chicago)

**Give them the plan.** Send your out-of-area contact a copy of the family emergency plan. They need to know who is in your household, your meeting points, your medical needs, and what to expect.

## Step 4: Build the Communication Tree

Who calls whom, in what order? The communication tree prevents duplication of effort and ensures that critical information flows efficiently.

**Template:**

```
FIRST: Everyone calls Out-of-Area Contact: [Name], [Phone]
  - Report: Your name, location, injuries (Y/N), status
  
SECOND: Family members at same location check in with each other
  
THIRD: If cell service is working, text (don't call) family members directly
  - Texts use less bandwidth and are more likely to get through
  
FOURTH: If cell service is down, use FRS/GMRS radio (Channel __, Tone __)
  - [See radio communications guide: FSR study-guides/radio-communications-basics]
  
FIFTH: If all electronic communication fails, proceed to meeting points on foot
  - Primary: [location]
  - Secondary: [location]
```

## Step 5: Pet Plan

Pets are family members. They need to be in the plan. Leaving the pet plan for the moment of crisis guarantees bad decisions --- releasing animals to fend for themselves, spending dangerous time searching a damaged house for a hiding cat, or arriving at an evacuation shelter that does not accept animals.

### Before the Emergency

- **ID on collar plus microchip.** Every pet should have a collar with a tag listing the owner's name, phone number, and the out-of-area contact's phone number. A microchip is the permanent backup --- collars can be lost, but a microchip scanned at any vet or shelter identifies the animal and returns it to you.
- **Pet go-bag.** A small bag (kept with your go bag) containing: 3-7 days of pet food, collapsible water bowl, a leash and collar (extra, in case the one the pet is wearing breaks), a copy of vaccination records, a recent photo of the pet (for lost-pet identification), any medications, and a carrier for cats or small dogs.
- **Evacuation route with pets.** Know where you will go with your animals. Not all shelters accept pets. Red Cross shelters typically do not. However, FEMA requires that state and local emergency plans address the needs of people with pets (PETS Act, 2006). Check with your local emergency management for pet-friendly shelters, or plan to stay with friends, family, or in your vehicle.

### During the Emergency

- **Secure the pet first.** In the chaos of an earthquake or evacuation, frightened animals bolt. If you can safely reach your pet, leash the dog or crate the cat before doing anything else. A loose, panicked pet in a disaster zone is a pet that disappears.
- **Do not leave pets behind if you evacuate.** If you leave, they leave with you. Animals left in an evacuated home may be trapped for days without food, water, or temperature control.
- **If you must leave the pet temporarily** (because the animal cannot be found immediately): leave food, water, and an open interior door so the animal can access multiple rooms. Leave a sign on the front door: "PET INSIDE --- [species, number, description]." This alerts responders.

## Step 6: Medical Needs

Document every household member's medical requirements on the plan:

```
MEDICAL INFORMATION

[Name 1]:
  Medications: ___________
  Dosage/frequency: ___________
  Pharmacy: ___________ Phone: ___________
  Doctor: ___________ Phone: ___________
  Allergies: ___________
  Blood type: ___________
  Medical devices: ___________

[Name 2]:
  [Same fields]
```

This information goes on the laminated plan AND in every go bag. In a disaster, you may be treated by a medical provider who has no access to your records. The paper copy in your go bag ensures that critical medical information travels with you.

**Medications:** Maintain a 7-day rotating supply in each go bag. Replace monthly with current prescriptions. For medications that require refrigeration (insulin, certain biologics), include a small insulated bag and a cold pack in the go bag, and know the medication's room-temperature stability window.

## Step 7: Insurance and Documents

After the disaster, you will need to prove who you are, what you owned, and what you are covered for.

- **Copies of government ID** (driver's license, passport) --- photocopies in each go bag
- **Insurance documents** --- homeowner's/renter's policy number, carrier name and phone, health insurance card copy, auto insurance card copy
- **Property documentation** --- photos or video of your home's contents (walk through every room with your phone camera, recording the contents; upload to cloud storage). This visual inventory accelerates insurance claims dramatically.
- **Digital backup** --- all of the above on an encrypted USB drive in each go bag, plus emailed to the out-of-area contact

## Step 8: Utility Shutoffs

Know where they are and how to operate them. In an earthquake, gas leaks are the most immediate life-threatening utility hazard.

### Gas Shutoff

- **Location:** The gas meter, typically on an exterior wall of the house. The shutoff valve is on the supply pipe leading into the meter.
- **Tool:** A 12-inch adjustable wrench or a dedicated gas shutoff wrench ($8-12, available at hardware stores). **Strap or wire the wrench to the gas meter** so it is always there when you need it. A wrench in the garage toolbox is useless if you cannot get to the garage.
- **When to shut off:** If you smell gas (rotten egg/sulfur odor), hear hissing, or see damage to gas lines after an earthquake, shut off the gas immediately. Turn the valve 1/4 turn so the tang (the handle/bar on the valve) is perpendicular to the pipe.
- **Do not turn it back on yourself.** Only the gas utility company should restore gas service after a shutoff, because the system needs to be tested for leaks before re-pressurization.

### Electrical Panel

- **Location:** Typically in the garage, basement, or utility room. Know where your panel is and how to access it in the dark (headlamp in the go bag).
- **Main breaker:** The large breaker at the top of the panel that controls all circuits. Flip it to OFF if you see sparking, smell burning, or suspect electrical damage.
- **When to shut off:** Structural damage with exposed or damaged wiring, standing water in contact with electrical outlets, or a persistent burning smell with no visible fire.

### Water Main

- **Location:** The water meter, typically near the street in a covered box or vault. The shutoff valve is on the house side of the meter.
- **Tool:** A water meter key ($10-15) or an adjustable wrench.
- **When to shut off:** If you see water spraying from broken pipes inside or under the house. Shutting off the main prevents flooding of the structure and preserves the water already in your pipes and water heater as an emergency supply.

## Step 9: Practice Drill

A plan that has never been practiced is a document, not a plan. Practice transforms theory into muscle memory.

### The Annual Drill

Once a year --- pick a specific date and put it on the calendar (the Great ShakeOut, held annually on the third Thursday of October, is an ideal date) --- conduct a family emergency drill:

1. **Drop, Cover, and Hold On.** Practice the earthquake response. Drop to hands and knees. Get under a sturdy table or desk. Hold on to the legs. Stay until the "shaking" stops (count to 60).

2. **Exit and meet.** Everyone exits the house and meets at the primary meeting point. Time it. Note any problems: doors that jam, paths that are blocked, a family member who does not know where the meeting point is.

3. **Call the out-of-area contact.** Actually call them. Report your status. Confirm they have a current copy of the plan.

4. **Walk the evacuation route.** If your plan includes an evacuation to the secondary meeting point, walk it. With the pets. With the go bags. Note obstacles, distances, and time.

5. **Check the go bags.** Open them. Inspect the contents. Rotate food and water. Update medications. Replace dead batteries. Update document copies.

6. **Review the utility shutoffs.** Walk each family member to the gas meter, the electrical panel, and the water main. Confirm everyone knows where they are and how to operate them.

### Making It Not Scary for Kids

Children do not need to be frightened into preparedness. They need to be prepared without being frightened. Frame the drill as:

- **A game.** "Today we are going to practice our earthquake game. When I say 'earthquake,' everyone drops and gets under the table. Then we run outside and see who gets to the tree first."
- **Empowerment, not fear.** "You know what most people don't know? Most people don't know where to go after an earthquake. But you do. You know the meeting points and Aunt Sarah's phone number. That makes you better prepared than most adults."
- **Age-appropriate.** Young children (under 6) need to know: drop and cover, go outside with a grown-up, meet at the tree. Older children (6-12) can know meeting points, the out-of-area contact number, and how to use the radio. Teenagers can know everything in the plan, including utility shutoffs and go-bag contents.

Everything above distills into a single laminated page. Here is the template:

```

[Family Name] --- Updated: [Date]
===============================================

  Primary: [specific location in front yard]
  Secondary: [specific nearby location]
  Tertiary: [specific out-of-area location]

  Name: _______________
  Phone: _______________
  Relationship: _______________

COMMUNICATION ORDER
  1. Call out-of-area contact
  2. Text family members (texts > calls in emergencies)
  3. FRS radio Channel ___ Tone ___
  4. Go to meeting points

HOUSEHOLD MEMBERS
  Name: _________ Phone: _________ Medical: _________
  Name: _________ Phone: _________ Medical: _________
  Name: _________ Phone: _________ Medical: _________
  Name: _________ Phone: _________ Medical: _________

PETS
  Name: _________ Species: _________ Microchip#: _________

  Gas: [location, wrench location]

INSURANCE
  Home: [carrier, policy#, phone]
  Health: [carrier, policy#, phone]

  911 (fire/medical/police)

```

Print it. Laminate it. Post it on the refrigerator. Put copies in every go bag. Email a digital copy to the out-of-area contact.

## PNW-Specific: Cascadia Additions

If you live in the Pacific Northwest, add these Cascadia-specific elements to your plan:

- **Two-week supply standard.** 72 hours is not enough for Cascadia. Plan for 14 days of self-sufficiency [FSR guides/citizen-preparedness].
- **Lahar route** (Pierce County residents): Map and practice the lahar evacuation route from your home to high ground. Time it. Walk it. Know alternatives if your primary route is blocked.
- **Tsunami zone** (coastal residents): Know the tsunami evacuation route from every location you frequent --- home, work, the beach, the marina. Tsunami warning time after a Cascadia event may be as short as 15-30 minutes for the nearest coast.
- **Bridge dependency:** If your daily commute or your route home from work crosses a bridge, identify an alternative route that does not require a bridge. Bridges are among the most vulnerable infrastructure elements in an earthquake.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Learning** --- [Planning, Organization, Systems Thinking]: Creating a family emergency plan is an exercise in systems thinking: identifying hazards, mapping dependencies (communication, transportation, utilities), designing redundancy (primary/secondary/tertiary meeting points, multiple communication methods), and testing the system through practice drills. These are the same planning skills used in project management, logistics, and engineering design.
- **Home Economics** --- [Household Management, Family Coordination]: The emergency plan is household management at its most consequential. Inventory management (go bags, medications, documents), communication planning (contact trees, out-of-area contacts), and maintenance scheduling (annual drills, six-month go-bag rotation) are all core Home Economics competencies applied to family safety.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Stress Preparation, Confidence Under Pressure]: A plan reduces panic. When the ground is shaking and the dishes are falling, the person who has practiced knows what to do: drop, cover, hold, exit, meet at the tree, call Aunt Sarah. The drill has written the sequence into muscle memory. The cognitive load of decision-making under acute stress is reduced to executing a rehearsed plan. This is the same principle that underlies fire drills, flight simulator training, and military combat rehearsal --- you do not rise to the occasion, you fall to the level of your training.
