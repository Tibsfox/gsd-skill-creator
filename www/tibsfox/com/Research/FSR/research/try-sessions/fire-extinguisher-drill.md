# Try Session: Use a Fire Extinguisher --- The PASS Method

## The Device You Have But Have Never Used

There is a fire extinguisher somewhere in your house. Maybe under the kitchen sink. Maybe in the garage. Maybe on a wall bracket in the hallway that the previous owner installed and you have never looked at twice. If you are like most Americans, you know it is there. You know it is red. You know it has something to do with fires. And you have never used it.

This is a problem, because the moment you need a fire extinguisher is the worst possible moment to learn how one works. A kitchen grease fire doubles in size every 30 seconds. A candle igniting a curtain can engulf a room in under three minutes. A space heater against a couch produces toxic smoke that fills a room in 90 seconds. In the time it takes you to read the tiny instructions printed on the side of the extinguisher, fumble with the safety pin, aim at the wrong part of the fire, and realize the discharge lasts only 10 seconds, the fire that was the size of a wastebasket is now the size of the room, and you need to be running, not fighting.

The PASS method --- Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep --- takes 30 seconds to learn and turns a fire extinguisher from a decorative cylinder into a lifesaving tool. This try session teaches the method, explains the physics, and gives you a practice drill you can perform today.

Most home fires start small. An extinguisher in the first 30 seconds is the difference between a story you tell at dinner and a catastrophe that changes your life. Learn this. Practice this. Know this.

## The PASS Method

Four letters. Four steps. Thirty seconds to learn. A lifetime of competence.

### P --- Pull the Pin

Every fire extinguisher has a metal or plastic pin that locks the handle in place, preventing accidental discharge. The pin is secured with a tamper seal --- a small plastic ring or wire that breaks when you pull the pin. The pin is located at the top of the handle, and it pulls straight out. You may need to twist it slightly to break the tamper seal.

**The mistake people make:** They try to squeeze the handle before pulling the pin. Nothing happens. They panic. They squeeze harder. Still nothing. Meanwhile, the fire is growing. Pull the pin first. Break the seal. Now the extinguisher is armed.

**Practice this now:** If you have an extinguisher in your home, go look at it. Find the pin. Do not pull it (unless you are ready to practice --- see the practice drill below), but put your hand on it and understand the mechanism. Familiarize yourself with where it is and how it moves. In a fire, muscle memory is faster than reading.

### A --- Aim at the Base of the Fire

This is the single most important instruction and the one that most untrained people get wrong.

Aim at the BASE of the fire. Not at the flames. Not at the top of the fire. Not at the brightest, most dramatic part. At the base --- where the fuel meets the fire, down at the bottom, where the burning material is actually generating the flame. The base is where the chemical reaction is happening. The flames above the base are a visual effect of that reaction. Spraying the flames is like spraying the smoke from a campfire --- it disrupts the symptom, not the cause. The fire reignites immediately from the base that you never addressed.

**The mental model:** Imagine a candle. The wax at the base of the wick is the fuel. The flame is the visible combustion. If you blow on the tip of the flame, it flickers but returns. If you cut the fuel supply at the base --- snuff the wick where it meets the wax --- the flame dies. A fire extinguisher works the same way, at scale.

Stand 6-8 feet from the fire (if safe to do so) and point the nozzle or hose at the base of the flames, not upward into the fire column.

### S --- Squeeze the Handle

Once the pin is pulled and the nozzle is aimed, squeeze the handle. The extinguisher will discharge its agent --- dry chemical powder, CO2, water, or foam, depending on the type --- under pressure. The discharge is immediate and forceful. There will be noise, a cloud of agent, and the fire should begin to diminish.

**Critical fact: You have 8 to 25 seconds of discharge time.** That is all. A standard 5-pound ABC dry chemical extinguisher (the most common home extinguisher) provides approximately 8-12 seconds of continuous discharge. A 10-pound unit provides 15-25 seconds. This is not a garden hose. You do not have unlimited agent. Every second of discharge must count, which is why aim (at the base) and technique (sweeping) matter so much.

If you release the handle, the discharge stops. You can use short, controlled bursts if needed. This gives you some ability to conserve agent and redirect between bursts.

### S --- Sweep Side to Side

While maintaining aim at the base of the fire, sweep the nozzle slowly from side to side, covering the full width of the fire's base. Start at the near edge (closest to you) and sweep to the far edge, then back. This distributes the extinguishing agent across the entire fuel surface, not just a single point.

**The common mistake:** People aim at one spot and hold. They create a circle of suppression in the center of the fire while the edges continue burning. Then the edges re-ignite the center. The sweep ensures full coverage.

As the fire diminishes, move closer (if safe) and continue sweeping. Even when the visible flame is gone, continue applying agent to the base for several seconds --- the fuel surface is still hot enough to reignite. Watch the area for at least 30 seconds after the fire appears out. If it reignites, repeat PASS if you have agent remaining. If you are out of agent, evacuate.

## Know When to Fight and When to Flee

A fire extinguisher is a tool for small fires. The decision to use it or to evacuate is the most important decision you will make in a fire, and it must be made instantly.

**Fight the fire if ALL of the following are true:**
- The fire is small --- no larger than a wastebasket or small trash can
- You have a clear escape route behind you (the fire is not between you and the exit)
- Everyone else in the building is either out or being evacuated by someone else
- You have an appropriate extinguisher (see types below) and it is charged
- The room is not filling with thick, dark smoke

**Evacuate immediately if ANY of the following are true:**
- The fire is larger than a wastebasket or spreading rapidly
- The room is filling with smoke (smoke kills more people than flame)
- You are not sure what is burning (toxic materials, chemicals, unknown contents)
- You do not have an extinguisher, or it is discharged, or you are unsure it is the right type
- You cannot maintain a clear escape route
- Your gut tells you to leave

**The rule is simple:** When in doubt, get out. Close the door behind you (this slows fire spread dramatically). Call 911 from outside. A house can be rebuilt. You cannot.

The fire service trains firefighters to make go/no-go decisions under extreme stress, and even with years of training, full protective gear, and SCBA, they sometimes decide a fire is beyond suppression and order an evacuation. You, in pajamas with a 5-pound ABC extinguisher, have a much lower threshold for that decision. Use the extinguisher if the fire is small and conditions are safe. Otherwise, leave. No property is worth your life.

## Fire Extinguisher Types

Not all extinguishers are the same. Using the wrong type on the wrong fire can be ineffective or dangerous.

### Class A: Ordinary Combustibles
- **Fires involving:** Wood, paper, cloth, trash, plastics --- things that leave ash
- **Agent:** Water, or dry chemical
- **Home examples:** Wastebasket fire, curtain fire, paper fire

### Class B: Flammable Liquids
- **Fires involving:** Gasoline, oil, grease, solvents, paint
- **Agent:** Dry chemical, CO2, or foam
- **Home examples:** Cooking oil fire, gasoline spill in garage, solvent fire in workshop
- **NEVER use water on a Class B fire.** Water spreads flammable liquids and can cause explosive splashing, especially with cooking oil

### Class C: Electrical Equipment
- **Fires involving:** Wiring, outlets, appliances, motors, circuit panels
- **Agent:** Dry chemical or CO2 (non-conductive agents only)
- **Home examples:** Electrical outlet fire, appliance fire, extension cord fire
- **NEVER use water on an energized electrical fire.** Water conducts electricity

### Class K: Kitchen / Cooking Oils
- **Fires involving:** Cooking oils and fats at high temperature (deep fryers, commercial kitchens)
- **Agent:** Wet chemical (potassium acetate based)
- **Home relevance:** Commercial-grade, usually in restaurant kitchens. For home cooking fires, a Class B extinguisher or a pan lid (smothering) is the first response

### ABC Dry Chemical: The All-Purpose Home Extinguisher

**This is the one to buy for your home.** An ABC-rated dry chemical extinguisher handles Class A, B, and C fires --- ordinary combustibles, flammable liquids, and electrical equipment. It covers the vast majority of residential fire scenarios. The agent is monoammonium phosphate, a fine powder that smothers and chemically interrupts the combustion reaction.

**Downsides:** The powder is corrosive to electronics and messy to clean up. For a kitchen fire, the powder will contaminate any exposed food and coat every surface. But you will have a kitchen to clean rather than a kitchen to rebuild.

**Recommended home setup:**
- One ABC extinguisher in the kitchen (mounted on a wall or stored in an accessible cabinet --- NOT next to the stove, but across the kitchen, so you can reach it while retreating from a stovetop fire)
- One ABC extinguisher in the garage
- One ABC extinguisher on each floor of a multi-story home
- One ABC extinguisher near the bedroom area (nighttime fires are the most dangerous because delayed detection allows growth)

## Placement and Accessibility

Where you put the extinguisher matters as much as having one.

**The kitchen mistake:** Most people put the fire extinguisher next to the stove, under the sink directly adjacent, or in the cabinet beside the range. This is wrong. If a grease fire erupts on the stovetop, the extinguisher that is right next to the fire is behind the flames. You have to reach through or past the fire to get it. Instead, mount the extinguisher on the opposite wall of the kitchen, near the entry/exit. This way, you approach the fire with the extinguisher already in hand and a clear escape route behind you.

**General placement principles:**
- Near exits, not near likely fire origins
- At a height accessible to all household members who might use it (3.5 to 5 feet for the top handle)
- In visible or clearly marked locations (not buried behind coats in a closet)
- Within 40 feet of any point in the area it protects (NFPA recommendation for residences)
- Not exposed to extreme heat (do not store in an attic or next to a furnace)

## Maintenance

Fire extinguishers are not maintenance-free. An extinguisher that fails when you need it is worse than no extinguisher, because you waste critical seconds trying to use a dead device.

**Monthly:**
- Check the pressure gauge. The needle should be in the green zone. If it is in the red (under-pressurized), the extinguisher needs to be recharged or replaced.
- Visually inspect for damage: corrosion, dents, cracks, missing pin or tamper seal.
- Confirm the nozzle or hose is clear and unobstructed.

**Annually:**
- For rechargeable (metal) extinguishers: have them professionally inspected annually and hydrostatically tested every 5-12 years (per NFPA 10). Fire extinguisher service companies handle this.
- For disposable (plastic-head) extinguishers: check the gauge and condition. Replace if the gauge is out of the green or if the unit is older than 12 years.

**Replacement schedule:**
- Disposable extinguishers: Replace every 12 years (or sooner if the gauge indicates discharge or loss of pressure).
- Rechargeable extinguishers: Service and recharge as needed; replace if the cylinder fails hydrostatic testing.

## Practice Drill

This is a try session. You should actually try this.

### Option 1: Expired Extinguisher Practice ($0)

If you have an expired or aging fire extinguisher that you are replacing (you should replace it --- see maintenance above), use it for practice.

1. Go outside to an open area (driveway, backyard, parking lot). Away from people, pets, and anything you do not want coated in dry chemical powder.
2. Set a target on the ground to represent the "base of the fire." A cardboard box works.
3. Stand 8 feet away.
4. Perform PASS: Pull the pin. Aim at the base of the box. Squeeze the handle. Sweep side to side.
5. Note how quickly the extinguisher discharges. Count the seconds. Most people are shocked at how short the discharge time is.
6. Note the force of the discharge and the cloud of agent. It is more dramatic than you expect.
7. Clean up the powder (it is not toxic but is mildly corrosive to metal surfaces if left --- sweep or hose off).

### Option 2: Training Extinguisher ($15-25)

Training fire extinguishers are refillable units that discharge water instead of chemical agent. They look and operate identically to a real ABC extinguisher --- same pin, same handle, same nozzle --- but they can be refilled and reused indefinitely.

- **BullEx or similar training extinguishers** ($15-25): Available online. Refill with water from a tap. Practice PASS as many times as you want.
- Some fire departments offer free fire extinguisher training events where they provide training extinguishers and a controlled fire target (a propane-fed fire pit). Check with your local fire department --- these events are excellent and usually free.

### Option 3: Mental Rehearsal ($0)

If you cannot practice physically today, do this now:

1. Walk to your fire extinguisher. Pick it up. Feel the weight (a 5-pound ABC extinguisher weighs about 8 pounds loaded).
2. Find the pin. Put your finger on it. Understand the pull direction and the tamper seal.
3. Find the nozzle or hose. Point it at a spot on the floor 6 feet away.
4. Say out loud: "Pull. Aim at the base. Squeeze. Sweep."
5. Visualize a small fire on your stovetop. Walk through the sequence: grab the extinguisher from across the kitchen, approach the fire with the exit behind you, PASS.

Mental rehearsal is not as good as physical practice, but it is dramatically better than nothing. The neural pathways you establish now --- the motor sequence of pull, aim, squeeze, sweep --- will be available under stress.

## The Chemistry of Why This Works

Fire requires four elements, known as the fire tetrahedron: heat, fuel, oxygen, and a chemical chain reaction. Remove any one of the four, and the fire goes out.

**ABC dry chemical (monoammonium phosphate)** attacks the fire on two fronts:
1. **Smothering:** The fine powder coats the fuel surface, separating it from atmospheric oxygen. Without oxygen, combustion cannot continue.
2. **Chain reaction interruption:** The monoammonium phosphate decomposes in heat, releasing free radical scavengers that disrupt the chemical chain reaction sustaining the flame. Even if oxygen remains, the combustion chemistry is interrupted at the molecular level.

This dual mechanism is why ABC dry chemical is so effective against multiple fire classes: it works on solid fuels (A), liquid fuels (B), and around electrical equipment (C) because the powder is non-conductive and attacks the fire chemistry rather than relying solely on cooling (which is how water works).

**CO2 extinguishers** work by displacing oxygen around the fire. The CO2 is stored as a liquid under pressure and expands to a gas when discharged, flooding the fire area with an inert atmosphere. Effective on Class B and C fires. Less effective on Class A fires because the fuel can reignite when the CO2 dissipates and oxygen returns.

**Water extinguishers** work by cooling the fuel below its ignition temperature. Effective on Class A fires only. Dangerous on Class B (spreads flammable liquids) and Class C (conducts electricity).

Understanding the chemistry is not required to use PASS. But understanding why you aim at the base (that is where the fuel is), why you sweep (to cover the full fuel surface), and why the fire can reignite (the fuel is still hot) makes you a more effective and confident user.

## The Numbers That Matter

- **Home fires in the United States:** approximately 360,000 per year (NFPA)
- **Home fire deaths:** approximately 2,600 per year
- **Most common origin:** Kitchen (cooking fires are the leading cause of home fires and home fire injuries)
- **Most deadly fires:** Start in the bedroom or living room between 11 PM and 7 AM, when occupants are asleep
- **Time to flashover** (when a room fire becomes unsurvivable): **3 to 5 minutes** from a small fire in modern furnishings (synthetic materials burn faster and hotter than older, natural-material furnishings)
- **Fire extinguisher effective range:** 6-20 feet, depending on type and size
- **Fire extinguisher discharge time:** 8-25 seconds for residential units
- **Percentage of fires that are small enough to extinguish:** The majority of home fires are detectable while still small enough to fight with an extinguisher --- IF the occupant has an extinguisher AND knows how to use it

The window between "small fire I can handle" and "room fully involved and I need to evacuate" is measured in minutes. An extinguisher buys you seconds to close that window. The PASS method ensures those seconds count.

## After the Fire

Even if you successfully extinguish a fire, call 911. Every time. Without exception.

**Why:**
- Hidden fire extension: fire may have spread into wall cavities, ceiling spaces, or insulation where you cannot see it. A thermal imaging camera (which firefighters carry) can detect hidden hot spots.
- Structural damage: even a small fire may have weakened structural elements.
- Air quality: combustion products --- including carbon monoxide, hydrogen cyanide, and particulates --- may be present in concentrations you cannot detect by smell.
- Insurance documentation: fire department response and investigation creates the official record your insurance company will require.

Open windows to ventilate. Leave the building if smoke or unusual odors persist. Do not re-enter until the fire department clears the structure. Even a small kitchen fire that you put out in 10 seconds with an extinguisher can produce enough carbon monoxide to be dangerous in a closed room.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Fire Chemistry]: The fire tetrahedron (heat, fuel, oxygen, chain reaction) is applied chemistry. Understanding the combustion process --- pyrolysis of fuel, gas-phase flame propagation, the role of free radicals in sustaining the chain reaction, and how extinguishing agents interrupt these processes --- connects practical fire safety to thermodynamics and reaction kinetics.
- **Physical Education** --- [Emergency Response]: Using a fire extinguisher under stress requires a trained motor sequence (PASS) executed under time pressure, in an environment with reduced visibility and rising temperature. The ability to perform a learned physical sequence despite an elevated heart rate and acute stress is the same skill set that underpins all emergency physical response.
- **Home Economics** --- [Home Safety]: Fire extinguisher selection, placement, and maintenance are home safety management --- the domestic equivalent of occupational fire safety. Understanding where fires start, what types of fires occur in which rooms, and how to position defensive tools relative to egress routes is practical home safety engineering.
