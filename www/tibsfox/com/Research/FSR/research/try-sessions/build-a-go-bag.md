# DIY Project: Build a 72-Hour Go Bag

## The Bag You Grab When the Ground Starts Shaking

There is a moment in every major disaster --- earthquake, wildfire, flood, industrial accident --- when the situation changes from "something is happening" to "we are leaving NOW." That transition happens fast. In the Cascadia subduction zone, it happens in the first 30 seconds of shaking, when the dishes are falling off the shelves and the walls are flexing and you realize this is not a small earthquake, this is the big one, and everything you do in the next two minutes determines how the next two weeks of your life will go.

The people who survive that transition with their essentials intact are not lucky. They are prepared. They have a bag --- packed, checked, staged by the front door --- that contains everything they need for the first 72 hours of self-sufficiency. In disaster planning, this is called a go bag, a bug-out bag, a 72-hour kit, or a grab bag. The name does not matter. What matters is that it exists, that it is packed, that you know where it is, and that you can have it in your hands within arm's reach in the first moments of crisis.

This project builds that bag. Two versions: BASIC ($50-80, covers 72 hours, gets you through the acute phase) and ENHANCED ($150-250, covers up to two weeks, gets you through the period when infrastructure is still down and help has not arrived). The BASIC version is the minimum. The ENHANCED version is what the Pacific Northwest actually needs, because when the Cascadia subduction zone ruptures --- and the geological record says it will, with 41 full-margin events in the last 10,000 years --- the bridges on I-5 will be down, the ports will be damaged, the airports will be inoperable, and federal disaster relief will take days to weeks to establish. The old 72-hour standard was designed for hurricanes in regions with functioning highway access. Cascadia is not that scenario. (For the full seismic context, see the [Cascadia Earthquake case study](../case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md). For the complete preparedness framework, see the [Citizen Preparedness guide](../guides/citizen-preparedness.md).)

Build this bag today. Not tomorrow. Not this weekend. Today.

## The Bag Itself

Start with the container. A 30-40 liter daypack --- the kind you would use for a day hike --- is the right size. It needs to be large enough to carry everything listed below, small enough to carry comfortably, and durable enough to survive being thrown, dragged, and rained on.

**What to look for:**
- 30-40 liter capacity (larger invites over-packing; smaller forces compromises)
- Padded, adjustable shoulder straps and a hip belt (you may carry this for hours)
- Water-resistant or waterproof fabric (this is the PNW --- assume rain)
- External attachment points (molle webbing, daisy chains, or compression straps for securing a sleeping pad or extra gear to the outside)
- Subdued colors (bright colors attract attention; in a disaster, you do not always want attention)

**Budget options ($20-40):** Jansport, Osprey Daylite, or any name-brand day pack from a thrift store. Military surplus MOLLE packs are excellent and available for $25-40. Do not buy the cheapest bag you can find --- a zipper failure in an emergency turns your kit into a pile of loose gear.

**Where to store it:** By the front door. In the hallway closet. On a hook by the garage entry. The location must satisfy one requirement: you can reach it within 30 seconds of deciding to leave, in the dark, while the house is shaking. If the bag is in the basement, or in the bedroom closet behind the winter coats, or in the car, it is not accessible when you need it most.

## Category 1: WATER

Water is the highest priority. Dehydration becomes dangerous in 24-48 hours. Municipal water systems fail in earthquakes --- the mains rupture, treatment plants lose power, the distribution network fractures.

### BASIC ($5-8)
- **3 x 1-liter water bottles** (sealed, commercially bottled): One liter per person per day for three days. Three liters is the minimum for a single person. Adjust for family size. Bottled water in sealed plastic containers has a virtually unlimited shelf life if stored away from heat and light.
- **Water purification tablets** (1 pack, Aquatabs or Potable Aqua, $6-8): Each tablet treats 1 liter. A pack of 50 tablets treats 50 liters. This is your backup when the bottles run out.

### ENHANCED ($25-35)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **Sawyer Mini water filter** ($20-25): Filters up to 100,000 gallons. Removes 99.99999% of bacteria and 99.9999% of protozoa. Weighs 2 ounces. Screws onto any standard water bottle. This single item transforms any fresh water source --- creek, river, lake, rain barrel, even a puddle --- into potable water. It does not remove viruses (uncommon in PNW surface water) or chemicals, but for disaster water sourcing in the Pacific Northwest, a Sawyer Mini is the most cost-effective water security you can buy.
- **Collapsible water container** (2-4 liter, $5-8): Platypus, HydraPak, or any collapsible bladder. Empty, it takes no space in the pack. Full, it carries a day or more of water.

## Category 2: FOOD

You can survive weeks without food, but hunger degrades decision-making, physical capability, and morale within a day. Pack calorie-dense, shelf-stable, no-cook food.

### BASIC ($15-25)
- **3600-calorie emergency food bar** ($8-12): Datrex, SOS, or Mainstay emergency rations. One bar provides 1200 calories per day for three days. They are designed for lifeboat survival --- shelf life of 5 years, withstand extreme temperatures, require no water to prepare, and do not provoke thirst (important when water is limited). Compact, light, and tasteless in a way that is somehow reassuring.
- **Beef jerky** (2-3 pouches, $6-10): Protein, salt, shelf-stable, lightweight. Jack Link's, Tillamook, or whatever brand you prefer.
- **Peanut butter** (1-2 single-serve packets, $2): Fat, protein, calories. The individual squeeze packets from Justin's or similar brands are lighter and more packable than a jar.
- **Instant coffee** (4-6 single-serve packets, $2-3): Optional but significant for morale. If you are a coffee drinker, 72 hours without caffeine while navigating a disaster is a compounding problem. Starbucks VIA or Folgers singles weigh nothing.

### ENHANCED ($25-40)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **Additional emergency food bars** (enough for 7-14 days, $20-30): Two additional 3600-calorie bars, or equivalent in smaller ration packs.
- **Trail mix** (2-3 bags): Calorie-dense, shelf-stable, provides variety.
- **Electrolyte powder** (6-10 packets, $5-8): Liquid IV, LMNT, or Nuun tablets. When you are dehydrated, plain water is not enough --- you need sodium, potassium, and magnesium to retain what you drink.

**Rotation schedule:** Every 6 months, eat the food in the bag and replace it with fresh supplies. This keeps your rations within their shelf life and familiarizes you with what your emergency food actually tastes like. Set a calendar reminder: January 1 and July 1.

## Category 3: SHELTER

If your home is standing and structurally sound, you shelter in place. If it is not --- if the chimney has collapsed into the living room, if the foundation has cracked, if gas lines are ruptured --- you are sleeping outside. In the PNW, sleeping outside without shelter means sleeping in rain, wind, and temperatures that can drop into the low 40s even in summer.

### BASIC ($20-30)
- **Emergency bivy sack** ($10-15): SOL (Survive Outdoors Longer) or similar. A full-body bag made of reflective Mylar-like material, open at the head end. Reflects 90% of body heat back to the user. Not comfortable. Not a substitute for a real sleeping bag. But it is the difference between a cold, miserable night and hypothermia. Weighs 4-8 ounces.
- **Space blanket** ($2-4 for a 2-pack): The classic Mylar emergency blanket. Use as a ground sheet, a rain cover, a signaling device (the reflective surface is visible for miles), or a supplemental heat reflector inside the bivy. Every kit should have at least two.
- **550 paracord** (50 feet, $5-8): Genuine 550-pound test paracord. Uses in a disaster are nearly infinite: lashing tarps for overhead shelter, securing gear, creating clotheslines, improvised splinting, tying down anything that needs tying down. Fifty feet is a generous starting length.
- **Lighter and fire steel** ($5-8): A standard BIC lighter (reliable, inexpensive, stores indefinitely if kept dry) plus a ferro rod / fire steel (produces sparks even when wet, functionally immortal). The lighter is your primary fire starter. The ferro rod is your backup. Fire provides warmth, water purification (boiling), cooking, signaling, and psychological comfort.
- **Waterproof matches** ($2-3): Backup to the backup. UCO stormproof matches burn in wind and rain. A small box takes no space.

### ENHANCED ($40-60)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **Compact tarp** (8x10 feet, $15-25): Ripstop nylon or polyester with grommets. Combined with the paracord, this is a functional overhead shelter that keeps rain off you while you sleep in the bivy. An 8x10 tarp shelters two people.
- **Wool beanie and gloves** ($10-15): Wool insulates when wet. Cotton does not. In the PNW, this distinction saves lives. A wool beanie prevents a significant percentage of heat loss through the head. Wool gloves keep your hands functional in cold rain.
- **Compact sleeping pad** ($10-20): Closed-cell foam pad (like a Therm-a-Rest Z-Lite section) or an inflatable sit pad. Ground insulation prevents conductive heat loss --- lying directly on cold, wet ground will chill you faster than air temperature alone.

## Category 4: MEDICAL

Injuries in disasters are common. Lacerations from broken glass, crush injuries from collapsed structures, burns, fractures, sprains. EMS will be overwhelmed. Hospitals will be triaging. For the first hours or days, you are your own first aid provider.

### BASIC ($25-40)
- **Compact first aid kit** ($8-15): Pre-assembled kits from Adventure Medical Kits, MyMedic, or similar. Should include: adhesive bandages (assorted sizes), sterile gauze pads (4x4), gauze roll, medical tape, antiseptic wipes, triple antibiotic ointment, ibuprofen, acetaminophen, diphenhydramine (Benadryl), tweezers, and nitrile gloves.
- **7-day supply of personal medications**: Whatever you take daily. Prescription medications, insulin, inhalers, epinephrine auto-injectors. Rotate every month to keep within expiration. This is the most commonly forgotten item in go bags and the most personally critical.
- **Tourniquet** ($25-30): A CAT (Combat Application Tourniquet) Gen 7 or SOF-T Wide. This is not optional. If an earthquake drops a bookshelf on someone's leg, or broken glass severs an artery, a tourniquet applied in the first two minutes is the difference between life and death. Buy a genuine military-grade tourniquet --- do not buy knockoffs from Amazon for $5. The [Stop the Bleed try session](stop-the-bleed.md) teaches you how to use it.
- **Israeli bandage / emergency pressure dressing** ($7-10): A single unit. The Israeli bandage is a pressure dressing designed for severe hemorrhage control. It combines a sterile absorbent pad, an elastic wrap, and a pressure bar in one package that can be applied with one hand. Learn the application before you need it.

### ENHANCED ($50-70)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **Hemostatic gauze** ($12-15): QuikClot or Celox gauze. Gauze impregnated with a clotting agent that accelerates hemostasis when packed into a deep wound.
- **SAM splint** ($8-12): A moldable aluminum and foam splint that can be shaped to immobilize a fractured wrist, ankle, finger, or any other extremity. Lightweight, compact, and versatile.
- **Chest seal** ($10-12): HyFin or similar. A vented adhesive seal for penetrating chest wounds. Hopefully you never need it. If you do, nothing else will work.

## Category 5: TOOLS

### BASIC ($25-40)
- **Headlamp** ($15-25): Not a flashlight --- a headlamp. You need both hands free. Black Diamond Spot, Petzl Tikkina, or any reputable brand with at least 200 lumens. Include fresh batteries AND a spare set. Lithium batteries perform better in cold, weigh less, and have a 10-year shelf life compared to 5-7 years for alkaline.
- **Multi-tool** ($15-30): Leatherman or Gerber. Minimum features: knife blade, pliers, screwdriver, can opener, wire cutter. A multi-tool replaces a dozen individual tools in a fraction of the space.
- **Work gloves** ($5-10): Leather or heavy-duty synthetic. After an earthquake, the environment is full of broken glass, splintered wood, exposed nails, and jagged metal. Work gloves protect your hands while clearing debris, helping neighbors, or setting up shelter.
- **N95 masks** (5-10 pack, $5-10): Dust, smoke, particulates from collapsed structures, volcanic ash (yes, the PNW has active volcanoes), airborne debris. N95 masks filter 95% of airborne particles. Pack at least 5.
- **Duct tape** (1 small roll or 10-15 feet wrapped around a pencil, $2): Repairs everything: tarps, packs, shelters, improvised splints, blister treatment (yes, duct tape on a blister works), sealing gaps, marking routes.

### ENHANCED ($40-60)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **Folding saw or wire saw** ($10-15): For cutting branches (shelter building), clearing small debris, or processing firewood.
- **Pry bar** (small, 12-inch, $10-15): The single most useful tool for post-earthquake debris navigation. Opens jammed doors, breaks through drywall, levers heavy objects.
- **Whistle** ($3-5): Fox 40 Classic or similar pealess whistle. Audible for up to a mile. If you are trapped, a whistle carries farther than your voice and requires less energy.

## Category 6: COMMUNICATIONS

When the cell towers go down --- and they will, within hours of a major earthquake --- you need alternatives.

### BASIC ($30-45)
- **Hand-crank emergency radio** ($25-35): Eton, RunningSnail, or similar. Receives NOAA weather radio broadcasts plus AM/FM. The hand crank charges the internal battery. Some models include a small flashlight and USB charging port for your phone. NOAA Weather Radio is the primary emergency broadcast system and will be transmitting instructions, shelter locations, and safety information even when everything else is silent.
- **Charged power bank** ($10-15): 10,000-20,000 mAh. Keeps your phone alive for 2-4 full charges. Your phone is your camera, your flashlight, your map (if you have offline maps downloaded), your contact list, and your connection to information. When the cell network comes back --- and it will, eventually --- you want your phone alive.
- **Paper copy of emergency contacts**: Write it down. Phone numbers for family, friends, your out-of-state emergency contact, your insurance company, your doctor. Include medical information (allergies, medications, blood type). When your phone is dead and your charger is buried, paper still works.

### ENHANCED ($50-75)
- **Everything in BASIC**, plus:
- **FRS/GMRS walkie-talkies** ($30-60 per pair): No license required for FRS (Family Radio Service). GMRS requires a $35 license for the family, valid for 10 years. Range: 1-5 miles in realistic conditions (manufacturer claims of 35 miles are fantasy). The ability to communicate with family members, neighbors, or a search team within a few miles is invaluable when cell service is down.
- **Larger power bank** (20,000+ mAh, $20-30) or a small solar panel charger ($25-40): Extended power independence.

For more on emergency radio communications, see the [Radio Communications study guide](../study-guides/radio-communications-basics.md).

## Category 7: DOCUMENTS

Documents are the most forgettable and one of the most critical categories. After a disaster, proving who you are, what you own, and what you are covered for accelerates everything: insurance claims, medical treatment, family reunification, financial access.

### BOTH VERSIONS ($5 + preparation time)
- **Copies of government ID** (driver's license, passport): Photocopies, not originals. Originals stay in your safe or safe deposit box.
- **Insurance documents**: Homeowner's or renter's insurance policy number and carrier contact information. Health insurance card copy. Auto insurance card copy.
- **Prescription list**: All current medications, dosages, pharmacy, and prescribing physician.
- **Emergency contact list**: Written on paper, not stored only in a phone.
- **Cash in small bills**: $200 minimum, in $5s, $10s, and $20s. ATMs will not work without power. Card readers will not work without internet. Cash is the only universally accepted medium of exchange in a disaster. Small bills matter because nobody will have change.
- **USB drive** (optional): Containing digital copies of all the above, plus: family photos for identification purposes, property records, medical records, insurance policies, and any other critical documents. Encrypt the drive with a password.

Store all paper documents in a waterproof zip-lock bag inside the pack.

## PNW-Specific Additions

The Pacific Northwest has weather and terrain that most generic disaster preparedness lists do not account for. These items are not optional in this region --- they are essential.

- **Rain gear**: A lightweight waterproof jacket and pants. Not water-resistant. Waterproof. In the PNW, rain is the default condition. Being wet in a disaster compounds every other problem: hypothermia, morale collapse, foot injuries, skin breakdown. A $15 Frogg Toggs rain suit weighs 12 ounces and is fully waterproof.
- **Wool layers**: At least one wool base layer top (merino or synthetic equivalent). Wool insulates when wet. Cotton kills in cold, wet conditions --- it absorbs water, holds it against your skin, and accelerates heat loss. This is not hypothetical. People die from hypothermia in the PNW at temperatures that would not be dangerous in dry conditions.
- **Waterproof matches and fire starters**: Redundancy in fire-starting capability. Wet conditions make fire-starting dramatically harder. Pack multiple methods: lighter, ferro rod, waterproof matches, and a small quantity of fire-starting tinder (cotton balls soaked in petroleum jelly, stored in a zip-lock bag --- each one burns for 3-5 minutes, enough to ignite wet kindling).
- **Gaiters or tall waterproof boots**: For walking through flood water, debris fields, or muddy terrain. Post-earthquake and post-flood environments are wet, sharp, and contaminated.

## The Cascadia Context

This section is specific. If you live in the Pacific Northwest, read it carefully.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone runs from Cape Mendocino, California, to northern Vancouver Island. The last full-margin rupture was January 26, 1700 --- a magnitude 9.0 event that generated a tsunami recorded in Japan. The geological record shows 41 such events in 10,000 years, with a recurrence interval averaging 240 years (but ranging from 100 to 500+ years). We are 326 years into the current interval.

When it ruptures, the shaking will last 3-5 minutes. Not 3-5 seconds. Minutes. Everything in your house that can fall will fall. Everything on your shelves will be on the floor. The power will go out. Gas lines may rupture. Water mains will break. Road infrastructure will be damaged or destroyed.

The critical window is the first 30 seconds. The ground starts shaking. You drop, cover, and hold on. When the shaking pauses --- or when you can move safely --- you grab your go bag, check for gas leaks (if you smell gas, shut off the main valve and leave), gather your family, and move outside to open space away from structures that might collapse in aftershocks.

Your go bag needs to be within arm's reach during that transition. Not in the attic. Not in the car. By the front door, ready to grab on the way out. Everything you need for the next 72 hours to two weeks is in that bag, and the 30 seconds between the shaking and the door is when it earns its existence.

## Maintenance Schedule

A go bag is not a build-it-and-forget-it project. It requires maintenance.

**Every 6 months (January 1 and July 1):**
- Rotate food and water. Eat the emergency bars and jerky. Replace with fresh supplies.
- Check batteries. Replace any that are low. If using lithium batteries, check expiration dates (lithium batteries last 10+ years, but verify).
- Update medications. Replace the 7-day supply with current prescriptions.
- Update document copies. If you have moved, changed insurance, changed phone numbers, or changed medications, update the paper copies.
- Check gear condition. Is the bivy sack intact? Is the lighter functional? Is the radio working? Has any gear been borrowed and not returned?
- Update cash. Inflation erodes purchasing power. $200 today may need to be $250 in five years.

**After every use:**
- If you open the bag for any reason --- camping trip, car emergency, power outage --- replace everything you used immediately.

**The discipline of maintenance is the discipline of preparedness.** A go bag with expired food, dead batteries, and outdated medications is a false sense of security. Maintain it like you maintain your car: regular intervals, non-negotiable.

## Weight Check

A fully loaded BASIC kit should weigh 12-18 pounds. An ENHANCED kit will weigh 18-28 pounds. If your kit is heavier than 30 pounds, you have over-packed. Remember: you may need to carry this while walking over debris, through rain, for miles. Every ounce matters. Pack what you need, not what you might want.

**Test your kit:** Put it on. Walk around the block. Adjust the straps. Feel the weight. If it is uncomfortable after 10 minutes, adjust or reduce the load. The best kit is the one you can actually carry.

## The Real Cost of Not Building This Bag

The BASIC kit costs $50-80 (plus $200 in cash that you can spend later). The ENHANCED kit costs $150-250. For context, that is one dinner out for a family of four, or two months of a streaming service, or one tank of gas.

The cost of not having this bag, when the Cascadia subduction zone ruptures or a wildfire forces evacuation or a major storm knocks out power for a week, is measured in suffering: in thirst, in hunger, in cold, in darkness, in the inability to treat an injury, in the panic of not knowing what to do. And in some scenarios, it is measured in lives.

Build the bag. Stage it by the door. Maintain it every six months. And hope you never need it. But know that if you do, it is there.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Learning** --- [Preparedness Planning]: Building a go bag is an exercise in systematic risk assessment and resource prioritization. It requires identifying likely scenarios, ranking needs by urgency (water > shelter > food > medical > tools > comms), selecting equipment against budget and weight constraints, and establishing a maintenance schedule. These are the same analytical skills used in project management, logistics, and systems design.
- **Home Economics** --- [Resource Management]: The go bag is household resource management compressed to survival essentials. Budgeting (BASIC vs. ENHANCED), inventory management (rotation schedule, expiration tracking), and supply chain thinking (what do I need, where do I get it, how do I maintain it) are core home economics skills applied to the most consequential context: keeping your family alive when normal supply chains fail.
- **Physical Education** --- [Survival Skills]: Carrying a loaded pack, building shelter from a tarp and paracord, starting a fire in wet conditions, purifying water from a natural source --- these are physical skills that can be practiced in a weekend camping trip and may be needed in a disaster. The go bag is the equipment. The skills are what make the equipment useful.
