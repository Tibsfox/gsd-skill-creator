# Try Session: Explore CERT --- Community Emergency Response Team

*Twenty hours of free training that makes you the neighbor everyone needs when the big one hits*

---

## What CERT Is

When the Cascadia Subduction Zone ruptures [FSR case-studies/cascadia-earthquake], professional emergency responders --- firefighters, paramedics, police --- will be overwhelmed within minutes. There are approximately 2,000 career firefighters in King County serving 2.3 million people. In Pierce County, the ratio is similar. In a magnitude 9.0 event affecting the entire Pacific Northwest west of the Cascades, every fire station, every ambulance, every police unit will be simultaneously needed in dozens or hundreds of locations. The triage will be brutal: professional responders will go to the worst structural collapses, the largest fires, the most critical medical emergencies. Your neighborhood, with its cracked foundations and broken water mains, will wait.

How long will it wait? The standard emergency management estimate for a Cascadia event is that neighborhoods not experiencing immediate life-threatening emergencies will wait **days** for professional response. Not hours. Days. The infrastructure to move resources --- bridges, roads, fuel supply, communication systems --- will be damaged or destroyed. The mutual aid system that normally brings resources from unaffected areas will be strained because the affected area spans 700 miles of coastline and millions of people.

CERT --- Community Emergency Response Team --- exists to fill that gap. It is a FEMA-designed program that trains ordinary citizens in basic disaster response skills so that, when professional help is delayed, neighborhoods can organize, assess damage, perform light search and rescue, provide basic medical care, and suppress small fires on their own.

CERT is not a substitute for professional responders. It is a bridge. The bridge between the moment the shaking stops and the moment the fire truck arrives. In the Cascadia scenario, that bridge may need to hold for 72 hours or more. CERT training builds the bridge.

## The 7-Session Curriculum

The standard CERT curriculum was developed by the Los Angeles Fire Department in 1985 (after the 1985 Mexico City earthquake demonstrated that trained civilians saved lives when professional responders were overwhelmed) and was adopted by FEMA in 1993 as a national program. The curriculum has been refined over decades and is taught by local fire departments and emergency management agencies across the country.

The course consists of 7 sessions, typically taught one evening per week plus a Saturday field exercise. Total time commitment: approximately 20 hours over 7 weeks.

### Session 1: Disaster Preparedness

The foundation session. Covers the types of disasters that affect your community, the role of CERT within the broader emergency management framework, and personal and family preparedness. Much of this material overlaps with the content in the [FSR guides/citizen-preparedness] guide and the [FSR try-sessions/family-emergency-plan] project, but the CERT course frames it specifically in the context of community-level response.

Key concepts:
- **The nine hazards:** Earthquakes, fire, flood, tsunami, volcanic eruption, severe weather, hazardous materials, terrorism, pandemic. Your instructor will emphasize the hazards most relevant to your jurisdiction.
- **Personal preparedness as a prerequisite for community response.** You cannot help your neighbors if your own family is in crisis. CERT teaches that personal and household preparedness (go bags, emergency plan, water storage, utility shutoffs) comes first.
- **The CERT activation process.** How CERT teams are activated, how they report to the local emergency management structure, and what authority they have and do not have.

### Session 2: Fire Safety and Utility Controls

Practical fire suppression skills for civilians. This session covers:

- **Fire chemistry** and the fire triangle/tetrahedron (the same science covered in [FSR try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill])
- **Fire extinguisher types and the PASS method.** Hands-on practice with training extinguishers on a controlled propane fire is a highlight of this session. If you have only read about PASS, this is where you actually do it.
- **Hazard identification** in damaged structures: gas leaks, electrical hazards, structural instability
- **Utility shutoffs** for gas, electric, and water (the same skills in Step 8 of the [FSR try-sessions/family-emergency-plan])

Many CERT courses include a live fire exercise for this session --- you actually extinguish a controlled fire in a metal burn pan using a real (training) extinguisher. The experience of walking toward a fire, aiming at the base, and watching it die under the chemical stream is qualitatively different from reading about it. Your confidence increases measurably.

### Session 3: Disaster Medical Operations, Part 1

This is the session that most CERT volunteers remember as the turning point --- the moment the training became real.

**START Triage.** Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment is a mass-casualty triage protocol designed for situations where the number of victims exceeds the number of responders. START was developed by the Newport Beach (CA) Fire Department and Hoag Hospital in 1983 and has become the standard triage protocol for civilian mass-casualty incidents in the United States.

START sorts victims into four categories using a 30-second assessment:

| Category | Tag Color | Criteria | Action |
|----------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Immediate** | RED | Life-threatening injuries requiring immediate intervention (airway, breathing, or circulation compromise) | Treat first |
| **Delayed** | YELLOW | Significant injuries that are not immediately life-threatening; the victim can wait for treatment | Treat second |
| **Minor** | GREEN | Walking wounded; injuries that do not require immediate medical attention | Treat last (or self-treat) |
| **Expectant** | BLACK | Injuries incompatible with survival given available resources; or already deceased | Do not treat (in a mass-casualty context) |

The 30-second assessment flow:
1. **Can the person walk?** If yes: GREEN (minor). Direct them to a designated treatment area. Move to the next victim.
2. **Is the person breathing?** If no: open the airway (head-tilt/chin-lift). If they start breathing: RED (immediate). If they still do not breathe: BLACK (expectant). Move to the next victim.
3. **Respiratory rate?** If greater than 30 breaths per minute: RED (immediate). If less than 30: continue assessment.
4. **Circulation (capillary refill or radial pulse)?** Capillary refill >2 seconds or no radial pulse: RED (immediate). Normal: continue.
5. **Mental status?** Cannot follow simple commands: RED (immediate). Can follow commands: YELLOW (delayed).

The entire assessment takes under 30 seconds per victim. In a mass-casualty event with 20 or 50 or 100 victims, this speed is critical. You are not providing treatment during triage --- you are sorting. Treatment comes after everyone has been assessed and resources are directed to the RED victims first.

**The hardest part of START:** Walking past a BLACK-tagged victim to get to the next person. In a CERT scenario, a BLACK tag means "this person's injuries are beyond what we can treat with the resources available." This is not a medical diagnosis of death --- it is a resource allocation decision. It is gut-wrenching. It is also the correct decision when you have three RED victims who will die without intervention and one victim whose injuries are not survivable with your equipment. CERT training does not shy away from this reality.

### Session 4: Disaster Medical Operations, Part 2

Hands-on medical skills for the injuries you are most likely to encounter in a disaster:

- **Bleeding control.** Direct pressure, wound packing, and tourniquet application --- the same skills covered in [FSR try-sessions/stop-the-bleed], practiced in a group setting with moulage (simulated wounds).
- **Splinting.** Improvised splints using SAM splints, boards, rolled newspapers, and other available materials. How to immobilize a fracture to prevent further injury during movement.
- **Head, neck, and spinal injuries.** When to suspect a spinal injury and how to stabilize the victim's head and neck to prevent secondary damage. The critical principle: if you suspect a spinal injury, do not move the victim unless they are in immediate danger (fire, structural collapse).
- **Treating for shock.** Recognizing the signs of hemorrhagic and neurogenic shock, and the simple interventions available to a civilian first aider: elevate legs, maintain body temperature, reassure the victim, provide nothing by mouth.

This session includes practical exercises with simulated victims --- other CERT trainees in moulage (theatrical blood, simulated fractures, fake embedded objects). Practicing these skills on another human being, even a simulated one, is dramatically more effective than practicing on a pillow or reading about it.

### Session 5: Light Search and Rescue

When a building collapses in an earthquake, the occupants may be trapped in voids --- spaces created by fallen debris that are large enough to survive in but too confined to escape from. Professional search and rescue (US&R) teams have specialized training, equipment (listening devices, cameras, hydraulic tools, cutting equipment), and the engineering knowledge to assess structural stability. CERT volunteers do not. CERT search and rescue is therefore "light" --- it operates in the space between "waiting for professional help" and "doing nothing."

Key skills:
- **Structural triage.** Assessing a damaged building's exterior for signs that it is too dangerous to enter: visible collapse, structural lean, hanging debris, gas leaks, active fire. CERT volunteers do NOT enter heavily damaged structures. If the building is unsafe, you mark it and move to the next one.
- **Voice search.** Systematically calling out to trapped occupants from outside the structure and listening for responses. A quiet, coordinated voice search is the most effective tool a CERT volunteer has --- it requires no equipment and can locate trapped survivors through walls and debris.
- **Light debris removal.** Lifting, prying, and removing light materials (drywall, furniture, ceiling tiles) to free trapped victims who are accessible without entering unstable areas. This is where the pry bar in your go bag [FSR try-sessions/build-a-go-bag] becomes relevant.
- **Victim packaging.** Safely moving an injured person from a debris field to a treatment area using improvised stretchers (blankets, doors, boards).

### Session 6: Team Organization

CERT teams do not operate as ad hoc groups. They use a simplified version of the Incident Command System (ICS) [FSR study-guides/incident-command-basics] --- the same organizational framework used by professional responders.

Key concepts:
- **CERT team leader.** The person who receives the initial activation, sizes up the situation, establishes priorities, and directs team members. Every CERT team needs a leader. The training teaches how to assume command, delegate tasks, and maintain situational awareness.
- **Division of labor.** CERT members are assigned to functional teams: fire suppression, search and rescue, medical treatment. Each team has a specific mission and reports to the team leader.
- **Documentation.** Recording search results (which buildings were checked, how many victims found, their triage status), resource status (how many team members are available, what equipment is on hand), and situation reports for handoff to professional responders when they arrive.
- **Safety above all.** The CERT credo: do the greatest good for the greatest number without endangering yourself. A dead or injured CERT volunteer is a drain on resources, not a contribution.

### Session 7: Course Review and Disaster Simulation

The capstone exercise. A full-scale simulated disaster, typically held on a Saturday, where CERT trainees respond to a scenario that integrates all seven sessions: they are activated, they organize into teams under ICS, they assess the scene, they perform START triage on simulated victims (played by volunteers in moulage), they suppress a training fire, they perform light search and rescue in a simulated debris field, and they set up a medical treatment area.

The simulation is evaluated by instructors (usually fire department personnel) who observe, coach, and debrief. It is the closest thing to a real disaster response that most civilians will ever experience, and it is where the training clicks --- the individual skills learned in sessions 1-6 integrate into a coordinated team response.

## Time Commitment and Cost

**Time:** 20 hours total. Typically one evening per week (2.5-3 hours) for 6 weeks, plus a half-day Saturday exercise. Some jurisdictions offer compressed weekend formats or summer intensive formats.

**Cost:** **Free in almost all PNW jurisdictions.** The program is funded by FEMA grants and local emergency management budgets. Participants pay nothing. The only cost is your time.

## Where to Find CERT in the PNW

CERT programs are managed by local fire departments and emergency management offices. Every major PNW jurisdiction offers CERT or an equivalent program:

- **Seattle:** Seattle Emergency Management offers CERT classes multiple times per year. seattle.gov/emergency-management
- **Portland:** Portland Bureau of Emergency Management runs NET --- Neighborhood Emergency Teams --- which is Portland's equivalent of CERT. The training is similar, with Portland-specific hazard emphasis. portland.gov/pbem/net
- **Tacoma:** Tacoma Fire Department offers CERT through their community programs. cityoftacoma.org
- **Bellevue / East King County:** Bellevue Fire Department CERT. bellevuewa.gov
- **Snohomish County:** Snohomish County Department of Emergency Management CERT. snohomishcountywa.gov/2387/CERT
- **Pierce County:** Pierce County Emergency Management CERT. piercecountywa.gov
- **Clark County / Vancouver, WA:** Clark Regional Emergency Services Agency (CRESA) CERT.
- **Salem / Marion County:** Marion County CERT.
- **Eugene / Lane County:** Lane County CERT.

**To find the program nearest you:** Search "[your city or county] CERT" or "[your city or county] community emergency response team." Call your local fire department's non-emergency line and ask about community preparedness training.

## What You Get

### Skills

After 20 hours of training, you will be able to:
- Perform START triage on multiple victims
- Suppress a small fire with an extinguisher
- Apply direct pressure, wound packing, and a tourniquet
- Splint a fracture
- Assess a damaged structure for entry safety
- Conduct a voice search for trapped survivors
- Organize a team response using ICS principles
- Shut off residential gas, electric, and water utilities
- Set up a medical treatment area

### Equipment

Most CERT programs issue a basic go-kit to graduates:
- CERT-branded hard hat (green)
- CERT vest (green, high-visibility)
- Safety goggles
- Work gloves
- N95 masks
- Basic first aid kit
- Flashlight
- Emergency reference card

The kit is modest, but it is enough to respond immediately. It also identifies you to professional responders as a trained CERT volunteer --- the green vest and hard hat are recognized by fire departments across the country.

### Community

This may be the most valuable thing you get. CERT training connects you with 15-30 of your neighbors who also showed up, who also care about preparedness, and who also committed 20 hours of their time to learning how to help. After the course, many CERT teams continue to meet, train, and organize. They conduct neighborhood preparedness assessments. They staff community events. They maintain a phone tree and a response plan.

When the Cascadia earthquake hits, the CERT-trained neighborhoods will self-organize. They will check on elderly neighbors. They will triage injuries. They will suppress small fires. They will search damaged buildings. They will set up treatment areas and maintain order. The untrained neighborhoods will wait for help that may be days away.

The difference between those two outcomes is 20 hours of free training.

### Connection to Your Fire Department

CERT is taught by firefighters and emergency management professionals. Completing the course gives you a direct relationship with the people who respond to emergencies in your community. You will know their names. They will know yours. You will understand how they operate, what their priorities are, and how you can support them when the system is overwhelmed.

This relationship is valuable before a disaster, too. CERT-connected neighborhoods tend to be more fire-safe, more prepared, and more resilient in day-to-day emergencies (house fires, medical events, severe weather) because the residents have been trained to respond rather than only to call and wait.

## The Real Value

The statistics on CERT effectiveness are difficult to isolate (controlled experiments in disaster response are, thankfully, impossible), but the anecdotal evidence from every major disaster since the program's inception is consistent: CERT-trained neighborhoods respond faster, more effectively, and with fewer secondary injuries than untrained neighborhoods.

After the 1994 Northridge earthquake, CERT-trained volunteers in Los Angeles performed 6,000 building inspections and searched 1,500 structures in the first 72 hours --- work that would have taken professional responders weeks to accomplish given the scale of damage. After Hurricane Katrina, CERT teams in the affected areas organized evacuations, maintained shelter operations, and provided basic medical care during the extended period before federal assistance arrived.

In the PNW, the question is not whether Cascadia will test the CERT system. It is whether enough people will be trained when it does. The USGS estimates that a Cascadia event will affect 7-10 million people across a 700-mile zone. The number of trained CERT volunteers in that zone is a tiny fraction of the population.

Every person who completes CERT training is one more node in the resilience network. One more person who knows what to do. One more set of hands that can triage, treat, search, and organize. One more neighbor who does not just wait for help but becomes the help.

The bridge between [FSR try-sessions/hands-only-cpr] (individual medical skill) and [FSR guides/citizen-preparedness] (household-level preparedness) is community-level response. CERT is that bridge.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Learning** --- [Community Education, Structured Training]: CERT is a structured, curriculum-based training program that transforms civilian knowledge into actionable disaster response capability. The learning methodology --- classroom instruction, hands-on practice, and capstone simulation --- is a model for effective adult education in any domain.
- **Physical Education** --- [Practical Skills, Physical Readiness]: Fire suppression, search and rescue, victim extraction, and medical treatment are physical skills that require practice, strength, and endurance. CERT training develops these physical competencies in a realistic, supervised context.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Confidence Under Stress, Decision-Making Under Uncertainty]: The CERT simulation exercise forces participants to make decisions under time pressure with incomplete information --- the same cognitive challenge faced by professional responders, military personnel, and anyone operating in a high-stakes, uncertain environment. The confidence that comes from having practiced these decisions is the single most valuable outcome of the training.
