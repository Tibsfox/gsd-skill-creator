# Try Session: Learn Hands-Only CPR in 10 Minutes

## The Single Most Important Skill You Will Ever Learn

There is one skill that any person, regardless of medical training, physical fitness, or professional background, can learn in ten minutes that will double or triple the survival rate of someone dying in front of them. It is not complicated. It does not require equipment. It does not require certification, a classroom, or a textbook. It requires your hands, your bodyweight, and the willingness to act when every instinct in your body is telling you to freeze.

That skill is hands-only CPR.

Cardiac arrest is not a heart attack. A heart attack is a plumbing problem --- a blocked artery starving part of the heart muscle of blood. The person having a heart attack is usually conscious, talking, complaining of chest pain. Cardiac arrest is an electrical problem. The heart's rhythm degenerates into chaotic quivering --- ventricular fibrillation --- and the heart stops pumping blood. The person in cardiac arrest is unconscious. They are not breathing, or they are gasping in the irregular, agonal pattern that looks like breathing but is not. Without intervention, they will be dead within minutes.

Approximately 350,000 out-of-hospital cardiac arrests occur in the United States each year. About 90 percent of them are fatal. The national survival rate for out-of-hospital cardiac arrest hovers around 10 percent. In King County, Washington, where citizens are trained in CPR at extraordinary rates and the EMS system is designed around a tiered response model, the survival rate for witnessed ventricular fibrillation is 62 percent. The difference between 10 percent and 62 percent is not better hospitals. It is not better paramedics. It is bystanders who start CPR immediately. (For the full story of how King County built the world's best cardiac arrest survival system, see the [King County Medic One case study](../case-studies/king-county-medic-one.md).)

This try session will teach you hands-only CPR. You will practice it today. And if the worst day of someone's life happens near you, you will know what to do.

## The Chain of Survival

Emergency medicine uses a concept called the Chain of Survival to describe the sequence of actions that must occur to give a cardiac arrest victim the best chance of living. Every link matters. A break in any link dramatically reduces survival. The chain has five links, and you --- the bystander --- control the first three.

### Link 1: Early Recognition and Call 911

You see someone collapse. Or you find someone on the ground, unresponsive. The first thing you do is check for responsiveness. Tap their shoulders. Shout at them. "Are you okay? Are you okay?" If they do not respond, if they are not breathing or are only gasping, call 911 immediately. If someone else is nearby, point at them and say: "You, call 911. You, go find an AED." Be specific. Do not say "someone call 911" to a crowd --- diffusion of responsibility means no one will. Point at a specific person. Give them a specific task.

If you are alone, put your phone on speaker, call 911, set the phone on the ground, and start CPR. The 911 dispatcher will stay on the line and coach you through every step. They are trained to do this. Let them help you.

### Link 2: Early CPR --- Push Hard and Fast

This is where you come in. The moment you determine that the person is unresponsive and not breathing normally, you begin chest compressions. Do not wait for the ambulance. Do not wait for someone more qualified. Do not wait. Every minute without CPR reduces the chance of survival by 7 to 10 percent. After 10 minutes without CPR, survival approaches zero. The ambulance is not going to arrive in time to matter unless someone bridges the gap. That someone is you.

### Link 3: Early Defibrillation

An AED --- an automated external defibrillator --- is the device that actually restores a normal heart rhythm. CPR does not restart the heart. CPR keeps blood flowing to the brain and vital organs so that the heart can be restarted by an electrical shock. If an AED is available, use it as soon as possible. AEDs are designed for untrained bystanders. They give voice instructions. They analyze the heart rhythm and will not deliver a shock unless one is indicated. You cannot hurt someone with an AED. You can only help them.

AEDs are increasingly common in public places: airports, malls, gyms, schools, community centers, office buildings. Look for a red or green box mounted on a wall, usually near restrooms or main hallways. The universal AED sign is a heart with a lightning bolt.

### Link 4: Advanced Life Support (Paramedics)

This is the link the professionals provide. IV medications, advanced airway management, cardiac monitoring. You do not need to worry about this. Your job is to keep the blood flowing until they arrive.

### Link 5: Post-Cardiac Arrest Care (Hospital)

Cardiac catheterization, targeted temperature management, ICU care. Also not your problem. Your problem is Links 1 through 3.

## The 2020 AHA Guideline Change: Why Hands-Only Works

For decades, CPR was taught as a combination of chest compressions and rescue breaths --- the mouth-to-mouth resuscitation that most people associate with the term "CPR." The standard was 30 compressions followed by 2 breaths, repeated in cycles. This created a significant barrier. Many bystanders were unwilling to perform mouth-to-mouth on a stranger --- for reasons of hygiene, fear of disease transmission, or simple squeamishness. Studies consistently showed that the requirement for rescue breaths reduced bystander CPR rates. People who knew CPR did not perform it because they did not want to put their mouth on a stranger's face.

The American Heart Association's 2020 guidelines formalized what research had been suggesting for over a decade: for adult cardiac arrest, hands-only CPR --- continuous chest compressions without rescue breaths --- is as effective as conventional CPR when performed by a bystander. The reason is physiology. When a person collapses in sudden cardiac arrest, their blood still contains oxygen. The lungs still contain air. What has stopped is circulation. Chest compressions generate enough blood flow to deliver that remaining oxygen to the brain and heart. For the first several minutes --- exactly the period when bystander intervention is most critical --- the bottleneck is circulation, not oxygenation.

This changes everything. Hands-only CPR is simpler, easier to remember, easier to teach, and eliminates the barrier that prevented millions of people from acting. Two steps: call 911, then push hard and fast on the center of the chest.

**Important exception:** Hands-only CPR is recommended for adult cardiac arrest witnessed by a bystander. For children, for drowning victims, for drug overdose, and for cardiac arrests with a respiratory cause, rescue breaths remain important because the oxygen supply is already depleted. If you are trained in conventional CPR with rescue breaths, use that training. If you are not, hands-only CPR is vastly better than no CPR.

## Hand Position: Where to Push

Getting the hand position right is straightforward, but it matters. Compressions delivered to the wrong spot are less effective and can cause unnecessary injury.

1. **Place the heel of one hand on the center of the chest.** The target is the lower half of the sternum --- the breastbone. An easy landmark: place your hand between the nipples, on the breastbone. Not on the ribs. Not on the stomach. On the hard bone in the center of the chest.

2. **Place your other hand on top of the first hand.** Interlock your fingers. Pull your fingers up so that only the heel of the bottom hand is making contact with the chest. You want to deliver force through the heel of the hand, not through the fingers.

3. **Position your shoulders directly over your hands.** Your arms should be straight --- locked elbows. You are not pushing with your arms. You are using your upper body weight, leveraged through straight arms, to compress the chest. Think of it as leaning into the chest rather than pushing down with your muscles. This is important for sustainability. If you push with your arm muscles, you will fatigue in less than a minute. If you use your body weight, you can sustain compressions for much longer.

4. **Your body position:** Kneel beside the person. Both knees on the ground, perpendicular to the person's chest. Keep your back straight. Rock forward from the hips, letting your weight drive the compression. Rock back to allow full chest recoil. Forward, back, forward, back.

## Compression Depth: At Least 2 Inches

This is where most untrained rescuers fail. They do not push hard enough. The chest must be compressed at least 2 inches (5 centimeters) for an adult. This requires significant force. You will feel ribs flex. You may hear cracks or pops. This is normal. Rib fractures and cartilage separations are common during effective CPR and are not reasons to stop. A person with broken ribs and a beating heart is alive. A person with intact ribs and no pulse is dead.

Two inches is deeper than most people expect. When you practice on a pillow (see below), pay attention to how much force it takes. On an actual human chest, it takes more. Do not be gentle. The American Heart Association's language is deliberate: push HARD.

Allow the chest to fully recoil between compressions. This means lifting your weight completely off the chest so it springs back to its normal position. Incomplete recoil --- leaning on the chest between compressions --- reduces blood flow back to the heart and makes each subsequent compression less effective. Push down hard, come all the way back up. Push down hard, come all the way back up.

## Compression Rate: 100 to 120 Beats Per Minute

The target rate is 100 to 120 compressions per minute. This is faster than most people intuitively push. It is approximately two compressions per second. You need a rhythm, and fortunately, popular culture has handed you one of the most effective medical mnemonics in history.

### The Beat of "Stayin' Alive"

The Bee Gees' 1977 hit "Stayin' Alive" has a tempo of approximately 104 beats per minute --- squarely in the target range for CPR compression rate. If you can hear the song in your head --- "ah, ah, ah, ah, stayin' alive, stayin' alive" --- and compress to that beat, you are compressing at the correct rate.

This is not a joke. The American Heart Association has endorsed this mnemonic in their public education campaigns. Studies have shown that rescuers who use a musical tempo reference maintain more consistent compression rates than those who try to estimate the rate without a reference.

Other songs at approximately 100-120 BPM that work:
- "Crazy in Love" by Beyonce (103 BPM)
- "Hips Don't Lie" by Shakira (100 BPM)
- "Sorry" by Justin Bieber (100 BPM)
- "Dancing Queen" by ABBA (101 BPM)
- "Another One Bites the Dust" by Queen (110 BPM) --- thematically inappropriate but rhythmically perfect

Pick the one that sticks in your head. The song does not matter. The rate matters.

## Do Not Stop Until Help Arrives

This is the rule that has no exceptions. Once you start CPR, you do not stop until one of three things happens:

1. **Professional help arrives and takes over.** Paramedics, firefighters, or other trained responders physically take over compressions from you. Not when you hear the siren. Not when you see the ambulance. When their hands are on the chest and your hands are off the chest.

2. **An AED is available and you need to pause to apply it.** Apply the AED pads, let it analyze, deliver a shock if advised, and immediately resume compressions. Do not wait to see if the shock worked. Resume compressions immediately after the shock. The AED will tell you when to stop for another analysis.

3. **You are physically unable to continue.** This is where bystander fatigue becomes real (see below).

You do not stop to check for a pulse. You do not stop because you think it is not working. You do not stop because the person has not woken up. High-quality CPR does not look dramatic. The person will not gasp and sit up like in a movie. They will remain unconscious. Their color may improve slightly. That is the only sign you are likely to see. Keep pushing.

## Practice: The Firm Pillow Exercise

You are going to practice this right now. Not tomorrow. Not at a class you plan to sign up for. Right now.

### What You Need
- A firm couch cushion or a firm bed pillow. Not a soft one. You want something that provides resistance when you press on it.
- A flat surface. The floor works. A firm carpet is fine.
- A timer. Your phone has one.

### The Exercise

1. Place the pillow on the floor.
2. Kneel beside the pillow, just as you would kneel beside a person.
3. Place the heel of your hand on the center of the pillow. Place your other hand on top, interlace fingers.
4. Position your shoulders directly over your hands. Lock your elbows. Straight arms.
5. Start your timer for **2 minutes**.
6. Compress the pillow at a rate of 100-120 per minute. Hum "Stayin' Alive" in your head. Push hard --- aim for 2 inches of compression.
7. Do not stop for 2 minutes.

### What You Will Discover

Two minutes of continuous chest compressions is exhausting. Your arms will ache. Your lower back will protest. Your shoulders will burn. You will want to slow down. You will want to lean on the pillow between compressions. You will want to take a break.

This is exactly why you are practicing. Bystander fatigue is one of the leading causes of inadequate CPR. Studies show that compression quality --- depth, rate, and recoil --- begins to deteriorate after about 90 seconds of continuous compressions, even in trained rescuers. By 2 minutes, most rescuers are delivering significantly shallower compressions than they were at the start.

The implications are critical:

- **If someone else is available, switch every 2 minutes.** Two-person CPR with regular switches produces dramatically better compression quality than one person pushing until they collapse. When you switch, do it fast --- less than 10 seconds of pause. The incoming compressor places their hands, the outgoing compressor counts "one, two, three" and lifts off, the incoming compressor starts immediately.

- **If you are alone, keep going.** Bad CPR is better than no CPR. Even shallow, slow compressions are circulating some blood. Do not stop because your compressions are not perfect. Stop only when help arrives.

- **Training makes it easier.** The more you practice, the more efficiently your body learns the motion, and the longer you can sustain quality compressions. Practice the 2-minute pillow exercise once a week. It takes less time than brushing your teeth.

## Why This Matters: The Numbers

The statistics on bystander CPR are unambiguous:

- **Without bystander CPR:** Survival from out-of-hospital cardiac arrest is approximately 5-10 percent.
- **With bystander CPR:** Survival doubles or triples, reaching 20-30 percent in many studies, and far higher when combined with early defibrillation.
- **Bystander CPR within 1 minute + AED within 3-5 minutes:** Survival rates of 50-70 percent have been documented.

King County, Washington, is the living proof that this works at population scale. Dr. Leonard Cobb's Medic 2 program trained over 50 percent of King County adults in CPR. The result: bystander CPR rates exceeding 60 percent for witnessed cardiac arrests (compared to approximately 40 percent nationally), and survival rates that are the highest in the world. The [King County Medic One case study](../case-studies/king-county-medic-one.md) documents this system in detail --- how one cardiologist's vision to make every citizen the first link in the chain of survival produced outcomes that most of the world considers impossible.

The lesson is simple. It is not the paramedics who save the most lives in cardiac arrest. It is the bystanders. It is the retired schoolteacher in the food court. It is the teenager at the gym. It is the office worker in the conference room. It is whoever happens to be there when someone's heart stops.

It could be you. And now you know what to do.

## Common Fears and Misconceptions

### "What if I do it wrong?"

Bad CPR is better than no CPR. The person is already clinically dead. You cannot make them more dead. Any chest compressions that generate any blood flow are improving their chances compared to doing nothing.

### "What if I break their ribs?"

You probably will. Rib fractures occur in roughly 30 percent of CPR patients. Cartilage separations are even more common. They heal. Death does not.

### "What if they don't actually need CPR?"

If a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally, they need CPR. You do not need to check for a pulse --- even trained medical professionals have difficulty reliably finding a pulse quickly, and the time spent searching is time not spent compressing. The 2020 AHA guidelines explicitly instruct lay rescuers to skip the pulse check and begin compressions if the person is unresponsive and not breathing or only gasping.

### "Can I get sued?"

Every state in the United States has a Good Samaritan law that provides legal protection to bystanders who provide emergency assistance in good faith. You are protected. The person who does not act because they are afraid of legal liability is the person whose fear costs someone their life.

### "What if it's a stranger?"

You do not need to perform rescue breaths. Hands-only CPR means your hands are on their chest. That is all. You are not performing mouth-to-mouth. You are pushing on their sternum. The barrier that kept generations of potential rescuers from acting has been removed.

## Next Steps: Beyond 10 Minutes

This try session gave you the skill to perform hands-only CPR. It is enough to save a life. If you want to go further:

1. **Take a full CPR/AED class.** The American Heart Association, the American Red Cross, and many local fire departments offer classes ranging from 2 hours to a full day. You will learn rescue breathing, AED operation, choking relief, and infant/child CPR. King County's Medic 2 program offers free citizen CPR classes --- a direct descendant of the program Dr. Cobb started in 1972.

2. **Learn Stop the Bleed.** If CPR is the most important skill for cardiac emergencies, hemorrhage control is the most important skill for traumatic injuries. The [Stop the Bleed try session](stop-the-bleed.md) teaches the basics.

3. **Take FEMA IS-100.** Understanding the Incident Command System helps you understand how the responders who arrive at your 911 call organize and operate. The [ICS study guide](../study-guides/incident-command-basics.md) introduces the concepts.

4. **Get a CPR certification card.** Formal certification, while not required to perform CPR, demonstrates training and may be required for certain volunteer roles. AHA Heartsaver courses provide a 2-year certification card.

5. **Tell someone what you learned today.** Teaching reinforces learning. Show a family member the hand position, the rate, the depth. Practice the pillow exercise together. The more people who know this skill, the smaller the gap between collapse and first compression.

6. **Build a go bag.** The [Build a 72-Hour Go Bag DIY project](build-a-go-bag.md) includes a tourniquet and Israeli bandage --- the physical tools that complement the skills taught in this session and in [Stop the Bleed](stop-the-bleed.md).

7. **Learn to use a fire extinguisher.** The [Fire Extinguisher Drill try session](fire-extinguisher-drill.md) teaches the PASS method --- 30 seconds to learn, and it turns a red cylinder into a lifesaving tool.

8. **Learn emergency radio communications.** The [Radio Communications study guide](../study-guides/radio-communications-basics.md) covers everything from NOAA weather radio to ham radio licensing. When cell towers fail, radio is the only communication that works.

9. **Create a family emergency plan.** The [Family Emergency Plan project](family-emergency-plan.md) builds the one-page document that coordinates your family's response when disaster strikes --- meeting points, communication tree, out-of-area contact, and annual drill.

10. **Explore CERT training.** The [CERT Overview](neighborhood-cert-overview.md) describes the 20-hour free training program that teaches you to organize neighborhood-level disaster response. CPR is the individual skill. CERT is the community-level framework.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Science** --- [Anatomy]: The cardiac conduction system, ventricular fibrillation as an electrical disorder, and the mechanics of chest compression generating artificial circulation through the thoracic pump mechanism. Understanding why compressions work requires understanding the heart's position between the sternum and spine.
- **Physical Education** --- [Physical Endurance]: Two minutes of continuous chest compressions at the correct depth and rate is a genuine physical challenge. Core strength, upper body endurance, and the ability to maintain a rhythmic motor pattern under fatigue are all fitness attributes that directly improve CPR quality.
- **Mind-Body** --- [Stress Response Under Emergency]: The freeze response is the primary barrier to bystander action. Overcoming the bystander effect, managing acute stress, and performing a trained motor skill under extreme psychological pressure --- these are mind-body challenges that practice and mental rehearsal can address. Knowing what to do reduces the cognitive load that causes freezing.
