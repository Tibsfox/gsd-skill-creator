# Answering the Call — A History of Emergency Services

## The Oldest Watch

Before there were sirens, before there were badges, before there were uniforms with reflective striping and apparatus painted in red or white or yellow, there was fire. And where there was fire in a city of wood and stone and thatch, there was the desperate need for someone to do something about it.

The history of emergency services is the history of human settlement itself. The moment people began living close together, the threat of fire, violence, and injury demanded organized response. What we now call "first responders" — firefighters, paramedics, police officers, dispatchers — emerged not from a single moment of invention but from millennia of trial, catastrophe, reform, and hard-won institutional memory.

This is the story of how we got here.

## The Roman Vigiles — Fire Watch and Public Order

The first organized fire service in the Western world belongs to Rome. After a series of devastating urban fires, Emperor Augustus established the Vigiles Urbani in 6 AD — the "Watchmen of the City." Seven cohorts of roughly 560 men each, totaling approximately 3,920 vigiles, patrolled the fourteen districts of Rome.

The vigiles were freedmen — former slaves granted citizenship in exchange for six years of service. They carried buckets, axes, pumps (siphos), and vinegar-soaked blankets (centones) for smothering flames. Their equipment was rudimentary by modern standards, but the organizational concept was revolutionary: a standing force, trained and paid by the state, whose sole purpose was emergency response.

What makes the vigiles particularly relevant to the modern discussion is that they were not purely a fire service. They also served a policing function. Night patrols checked for fire hazards, but they also arrested thieves, broke up disturbances, and maintained public order after dark. The vigiles were, in effect, the first combined fire-police force. The dual mandate — protect property from fire, protect people from crime — established a pattern that would echo through two thousand years of emergency services.

The vigiles persisted for centuries, evolving alongside the empire they served. When Rome fell, the institutional knowledge went with it. Medieval Europe would spend a thousand years relearning what Augustus had codified.

## Medieval Fire Response — Bucket Brigades and Building Codes

In medieval European cities, fire protection was largely a community obligation. When a fire broke out, church bells rang, and every able-bodied citizen was expected to respond with buckets, hooks, and ladders. The "bucket brigade" — a human chain passing water from source to fire — was the primary suppression tactic for centuries.

Cities responded to repeated conflagrations not with better firefighting but with building codes. London mandated stone construction and tile roofs after fires in 1135 and 1212. Paris required party walls between buildings. Nuremberg banned wooden chimneys. These were preventive measures born of desperation — the recognition that once a fire started in a densely packed medieval city, it was nearly impossible to stop.

The night watch tradition continued from Roman times, though in diminished and decentralized form. Watchmen patrolled streets calling out the hour and weather, alert for signs of fire or criminal activity. This was unpaid civic duty in many cities — a rotation of householders taking turns through the night. The quality was uneven, the equipment nonexistent, the training absent. But the concept endured: someone needed to be awake while the city slept.

## The Great Fire of London and the Birth of Insurance Brigades

On September 2, 1666, a fire started in Thomas Farriner's bakery on Pudding Lane. By the time it burned itself out four days later, the Great Fire of London had consumed 13,200 houses, 87 parish churches, and the old St. Paul's Cathedral. An area of 436 acres within the city walls was destroyed. Roughly 70,000 of the city's 80,000 inhabitants were left homeless.

The Great Fire was a turning point. In its aftermath, Nicholas Barbon established one of the first fire insurance companies in 1680. Insurance companies quickly realized that paying claims was expensive, and preventing fires was cheaper than rebuilding after them. They created their own fire brigades — teams of men employed by the insurance company to respond to fires at properties bearing the company's fire mark, a metal plaque affixed to the building's exterior.

This created an extraordinary situation: multiple competing private fire brigades operating in the same city, each responding only to fires at insured properties. If your building burned and you had no fire mark — or the wrong company's mark — the brigade might watch it burn. Stories of brigades arriving, checking the mark, and leaving are common in the historical record, though the degree to which this actually occurred versus the degree to which it has been embellished by later historians is debated.

What is not debated is the fundamental problem: privatized emergency response creates perverse incentives. The insurance brigades were effective for their paying customers and useless for everyone else. The recognition that fire protection was a public good, not a private service, would take another century and a half to fully take hold in London. In 1833, the insurance companies merged their brigades into the London Fire Engine Establishment under James Braidwood. In 1866, after Braidwood's death in the Tooley Street fire and continued inadequacy of the private model, Parliament created the Metropolitan Fire Brigade as a public service.

The insurance brigade era is worth remembering. Every generation seems to rediscover the temptation to privatize emergency services, and every generation eventually learns the same lesson London learned: when a building is on fire, checking the customer's account status is not a viable response model.

## Benjamin Franklin and America's First Volunteer Fire Company

On December 7, 1736, Benjamin Franklin organized the Union Fire Company in Philadelphia — the first volunteer fire company in America. The company had thirty members who agreed to maintain their own equipment (leather buckets, bags for salvage, and baskets) and to respond to any alarm of fire in the city.

Franklin's approach was characteristically practical. He had been writing about fire prevention in the Pennsylvania Gazette for years, drawing on his observations of Boston's fire companies during his youth. The Union Fire Company was not the first fire organization in the colonies — Boston had required households to maintain buckets and hooks since 1631 — but it was the first organized company of volunteers who trained together and responded as a unit.

The volunteer model Franklin established became the dominant form of American fire protection and remains so today. As of the most recent NFPA data, approximately 67% of firefighters in the United States are volunteers. In communities with populations under 25,000, the percentage is far higher. The volunteer fire service is one of the oldest continuously operating civic institutions in American life, and its roots trace directly to Franklin's Philadelphia experiment.

Franklin also helped establish one of America's first fire insurance companies, the Philadelphia Contributionship, in 1752. Unlike the London model, however, the American approach increasingly separated the insurance function from the firefighting function, moving toward publicly funded fire departments.

## The Cincinnati Model — The First Professional Paid Fire Department

The city of Cincinnati, Ohio, established the first fully professional, paid fire department in the United States in 1853. The motivation was not idealism but pragmatism: Cincinnati's volunteer companies had devolved into violent rivalries. Companies fought each other at fire scenes — literally brawling in the streets while buildings burned — over territorial rights and civic pride. The situation had become untenable.

Cincinnati's solution was to abolish the volunteer companies and create a salaried department under centralized command. Firefighters were hired, trained, assigned to stations, and placed under a chain of command with a chief at the top. The city also invested in steam-powered fire engines, replacing the hand-pumped apparatus that required large crews of volunteers to operate.

The Cincinnati model spread rapidly. City after city followed suit, transitioning from volunteer companies to professional departments. The drivers were consistent: growing urban populations, increasing fire loads from industrial activity, the inadequacy of volunteer response times in expanding cities, and — not infrequently — the same kind of intercompany rivalries that had plagued Cincinnati.

The professionalization of firefighting brought standardized training, regular working hours (though "regular" in the fire service would mean extraordinarily long shifts for another century), and the beginnings of a career path. It also brought labor organization — firefighter unions would become some of the most influential in American municipal government.

## Robert Peel and the London Metropolitan Police

On September 29, 1829, the first officers of the London Metropolitan Police stepped onto the streets of London. They wore blue coats (deliberately chosen to distinguish them from the red-coated military), carried wooden truncheons, and operated under principles established by Home Secretary Sir Robert Peel.

Peel's officers were immediately nicknamed "Peelers" and "Bobbies" — the latter term persisting to this day. But the name mattered less than the philosophy. Peel's Nine Principles of Policing, whether actually written by Peel himself or codified later from his approach, established the foundation of modern democratic policing:

The police are the public and the public are the police. The ability of the police to perform their duties depends on public approval of police actions. The police must secure the willing cooperation of the public in voluntary observance of the law to be able to secure and maintain the respect of the public. The degree of cooperation of the public diminishes proportionately to the necessity of the use of physical force.

This was a radical departure from the prevailing model. Before the Metropolitan Police, law enforcement in London was a patchwork of parish constables, private watchmen, and the Bow Street Runners — a small group of thief-takers who were more detective agency than police force. Soldiers were called in for serious disorder. The idea of a permanent, uniformed, civilian police force answerable to civil authority was genuinely new.

Peel's model was not universally welcomed. Critics saw the Metropolitan Police as a standing army in disguise — a tool of state oppression wearing blue instead of red. The tension between police as protectors and police as instruments of state power was present from the first day and has never been fully resolved. It remains the central tension of policing in democratic societies.

The Metropolitan Police model spread across Britain and then across the English-speaking world. American cities adapted it to their own contexts, though the American model developed significant differences — elected sheriffs, decentralized authority, the influence of machine politics on police hiring and operations. The professional reform movement in American policing, beginning in the early twentieth century with figures like August Vollmer in Berkeley, California, sought to bring Peelian professionalism to American departments, with mixed and ongoing results.

## The Neglected Disease — The Birth of Modern EMS

For most of human history, if you were injured or fell ill outside of a hospital, your chances depended on who happened to be nearby and what they knew. Ambulances existed — the first hospital-based ambulance service in the United States was established at Commercial Hospital (now Cincinnati General) in 1865 — but they were transport vehicles, not treatment platforms. The attendants had little or no medical training. The ambulance took you to the hospital. What happened during the ride was largely a matter of luck.

This changed because of a report.

In 1966, the National Academy of Sciences published "Accidental Death and Disability: The Neglected Disease of Modern Society." The white paper documented what was, by any measure, a public health catastrophe. Traffic deaths were skyrocketing — over 50,000 Americans died on the roads in 1966. Trauma was the leading cause of death for Americans under 40. And the prehospital care system was, in the report's assessment, wholly inadequate.

The white paper's findings were damning. Ambulance crews often had no medical training whatsoever. Equipment was minimal or absent. Ambulances were frequently operated by funeral homes — the hearse doubled as the ambulance, since both required a vehicle that could carry a supine patient. There were no standards for training, equipment, or response. In many communities, the "ambulance service" consisted of two men with a station wagon and good intentions.

The report catalyzed federal action. The Highway Safety Act of 1966 and the Emergency Medical Services Systems Act of 1973 created funding and standards for EMS development. The Department of Transportation published the first EMT training curriculum. States began establishing EMS systems with defined levels of care, training requirements, and medical oversight.

The transformation from a transport service to a medical service was neither quick nor uniform. It happened city by city, state by state, over decades. But the trajectory was clear: prehospital care was becoming a medical discipline, not a transport function.

## Johnny and Roy — How a Television Show Created Public Demand for Paramedics

On January 15, 1972, NBC premiered "Emergency!" — a television series created by Jack Webb (of Dragnet fame) and Robert A. Cinader. The show followed Firefighter/Paramedics John Gage (Randolph Mantooth) and Roy DeSoto (Kevin Tighe) of Los Angeles County Fire Department Station 51.

The show was entertainment, but its impact on public policy was extraordinary and well-documented. "Emergency!" depicted paramedics performing advanced medical procedures in the field — starting IVs, administering medications, interpreting EKGs, defibrillating cardiac arrest patients — under radio direction from physicians at Rampart General Hospital. For millions of Americans, this was the first time they had ever seen or heard of paramedics.

Before "Emergency!" aired, paramedic programs existed in only a handful of cities. The concept was still experimental, still controversial. Many physicians opposed the idea of non-physicians performing advanced medical procedures. Many fire departments resisted the expansion of their mission beyond firefighting. Legislative authorization for paramedic programs was absent in most states.

"Emergency!" changed the public conversation. Viewers saw paramedics saving lives and wanted to know why their community didn't have them. Letters flooded state legislatures. Citizens demanded paramedic programs. The show's technical advisor, Los Angeles County Fire Department Captain James O. Page, became a tireless advocate for EMS legislation, using the show's popularity as leverage.

By the time "Emergency!" ended its run in 1977, paramedic programs had been established across the country. The show did not single-handedly create the American paramedic system — the 1966 white paper, federal legislation, and the work of countless physicians, firefighters, and administrators did that — but it created the public demand that made political action possible. It is one of the most consequential television programs in American history, measured by its impact on public policy and public health.

The show also cemented the image of the firefighter/paramedic — the dual-role responder who fights fire and provides advanced medical care. This model, pioneered in Los Angeles, would become the dominant EMS delivery model in American cities. It is the model that defined thirty years of service for the person to whom this research is dedicated.

## September 11, 2001 — The Watershed

On the morning of September 11, 2001, 343 members of the Fire Department of New York died in the collapse of the World Trade Center towers. Twenty-three NYPD officers and thirty-seven Port Authority police officers also died. The toll among EMS providers — eight paramedics and EMTs from FDNY EMS — brought the total first responder death count to 411.

It was the deadliest day for emergency responders in American history. It was also a day that fundamentally reshaped the public's relationship with first responders and the government's approach to emergency preparedness.

Before 9/11, firefighters were respected but not particularly visible in the national consciousness. After 9/11, the image of firefighters climbing the stairs of the burning towers while everyone else was coming down became one of the defining images of American civic life. The phrase "first responder" itself entered common usage largely because of 9/11 — prior to that day, the term existed in emergency management circles but was not part of everyday language.

The policy consequences were massive. The Department of Homeland Security was created in 2002. Federal funding for first responder equipment, training, and interoperability surged. The National Incident Management System (NIMS) and the Incident Command System (ICS) were mandated for all agencies receiving federal preparedness funding.

But 9/11 also revealed catastrophic failures in coordination and communication. The FDNY and NYPD operated on incompatible radio systems. Incident command was fragmented. Evacuation orders did not reach all companies. The 9/11 Commission Report documented these failures in painful detail, and the reforms that followed — improved interoperability, unified command structures, standardized credentialing — were purchased with the lives of the responders who died that morning.

The long-term health consequences for 9/11 responders added another dimension. Thousands of firefighters, police officers, and EMS providers developed cancers, respiratory diseases, and psychological conditions from their exposure to the toxic dust of the collapsed towers. The fight for the Zadroga Act — legislation providing health care and compensation for 9/11 responders — would consume years of advocacy and become a recurring political battle. The lesson was stark: the nation that hails its first responders as heroes on the day of the crisis often struggles to care for them in the decades that follow.

## FIRESCOPE and the Incident Command System

The story of the Incident Command System (ICS) begins not with a single catastrophe but with a season of them. In 1970, wildfires in Southern California burned over half a million acres, destroyed hundreds of structures, and killed sixteen people in thirteen days. The firefighting response involved thousands of personnel from dozens of agencies, and the coordination failures were severe.

In the aftermath, Congress funded a multi-agency project called FIRESCOPE — Firefighting Resources of Southern California Organized for Potential Emergencies. The project brought together the U.S. Forest Service, the California Department of Forestry (now CAL FIRE), and the fire departments of Los Angeles, Los Angeles County, Ventura County, and Santa Barbara County.

The result was the Incident Command System: a standardized, scalable management framework for emergency response. ICS established clear principles that seem obvious in retrospect but were revolutionary in practice:

- **Unity of command:** Every person reports to one supervisor.
- **Span of control:** No supervisor manages more than three to seven subordinates (optimal five).
- **Modular organization:** The structure expands and contracts based on the incident's complexity.
- **Common terminology:** Everyone uses the same words for the same things.
- **Integrated communications:** All responders can communicate with each other.
- **Consolidated action plans:** One plan, written and distributed, governs the operation.

ICS was designed for wildfire but proved applicable to any emergency — hazmat spills, mass casualty incidents, floods, earthquakes, terrorist attacks. After 9/11, the federal government mandated ICS training for all emergency responders through the National Incident Management System (NIMS).

The system is not without critics. ICS can be bureaucratic. In small incidents, the overhead feels excessive. The paperwork requirements can slow decision-making. And the system assumes a level of interagency cooperation that is not always present in practice. But the alternative — the uncoordinated chaos of the 1970 California wildfires or the 9/11 response — is demonstrably worse.

ICS is now the global standard for emergency management. It has been adopted by countries on every continent. It is taught in every fire academy, every paramedic program, every police academy, and every emergency management course in the United States. It is one of the few institutional innovations in emergency services that can claim genuine universal adoption.

## The Arc of Progress

The history of emergency services is not a straight line. It is a series of catastrophes followed by reforms, each disaster exposing gaps that the previous generation had not addressed or had not imagined.

The Great Fire of London led to insurance brigades. The failures of insurance brigades led to public fire departments. The carnage on American highways led to the 1966 white paper. The white paper led to the paramedic system. The 1970 California wildfires led to ICS. The 9/11 attacks led to NIMS and interoperability standards.

The pattern is consistent and sobering: we build the systems we need only after we have paid the price for not having them. The institutions that protect us today were forged in the failures that killed people yesterday.

And the pattern is not finished. The mental health crisis response gap — the subject of a later document in this series — is the current failure point. We are sending firefighters, paramedics, and police officers to mental health emergencies because we have not built the systems to send anyone else. People are dying because of this gap. The history of emergency services suggests that the reform will come, but it also suggests that the reform will come too late for the people who needed it today.

For the person who served thirty years as a firefighter and paramedic, this history is not academic. It is the water they swam in every shift. The equipment they wore was descended from Roman vigiles and London insurance brigades. The protocols they followed were born from the 1966 white paper. The incident command system they operated under was forged in California wildfires. The public's expectations of them were shaped by a television show that premiered before many of them were born.

Every call they answered was a point on this arc. And every call they answered carried the weight of all the calls that came before — all the lessons learned, all the lives lost, all the reforms that arrived just one catastrophe too late.
