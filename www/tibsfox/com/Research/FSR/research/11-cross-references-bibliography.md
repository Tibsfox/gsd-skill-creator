# Cross-References and Bibliography

## Connections Within the Research Series

The FSR (First Responders) research series does not exist in isolation. The topics it covers — emergency response, infrastructure, environmental hazard, public health, and institutional design — intersect with multiple other research projects within the PNW Research Project. This document maps those connections and provides the bibliographic foundation for the entire series.

## Cross-References to Other Research Projects

### CMH — Community Mental Health

The intersection between the FSR and CMH research projects is the mental health crisis response gap documented in FSR Document 08 ("The Gap — Mental Health Crisis Response"). The CMH project examines the community mental health system from the perspective of the patient and the provider; the FSR project examines it from the perspective of the responder who is sent because the system has no one else to send. The two perspectives are complementary and mutually illuminating.

Key connections:
- The deinstitutionalization narrative in CMH provides the historical context for the crisis response gap described in FSR-08
- Community mental health funding models in CMH connect to the underfunding of 988 and mobile crisis teams described in FSR-08
- The CAHOOTS model (FSR-08) is a case study in the intersection of emergency response and community behavioral health
- Peer support models discussed in both FSR-09 and CMH share common principles and evidence bases
- The revolving door phenomenon (FSR-08) is the emergency services manifestation of the treatment access failures documented in CMH

### SYS — Systems Infrastructure

The SYS research project examines the infrastructure systems that underlie modern society — transportation, communication, water, power, and data networks. The first responder system is both a user of these infrastructure systems and a responder to their failures.

Key connections:
- 911 and Next Generation 911 communications infrastructure (FSR-05) are components of the broader communications systems examined in SYS
- Computer-Aided Dispatch systems (FSR-05) integrate with geographic information systems, records management systems, and telecommunications infrastructure studied in SYS
- The FirstNet public safety broadband network (FSR-05) represents a dedicated infrastructure investment for first responders
- Cascadia Subduction Zone preparedness (FSR-10) depends on infrastructure resilience — bridge integrity, water system redundancy, communications survivability — all SYS topics
- The trunked radio and P25 communications systems (FSR-05) are a specialized subset of the communications infrastructure documented in SYS
- Smart building integration with NG911 (automatic fire alarm data, floor plans, elevator status) bridges SYS and FSR

### ECO — Ecology and Environmental Systems

The ECO research project examines Pacific Northwest ecosystems, including forest ecology, watershed dynamics, and the role of fire in landscape evolution. The connection to FSR is direct: wildland fire.

Key connections:
- The fire ecology of Pacific Northwest forests (ECO) provides the scientific context for wildland firefighting operations described in FSR-02
- Indigenous fire management practices documented in ECO are the historical precursor to the fire suppression policies that created modern fuel accumulation problems (FSR-02, FSR-10)
- The 2020 Oregon wildfire season (FSR-10) was an ecological event with emergency response consequences — the intersection of ECO and FSR
- Forest management policy — prescribed fire, mechanical thinning, managed wildfire — is an ECO topic with direct FSR implications: the amount of wildfire that firefighters must suppress is a function of how much fire the management system allows to burn
- Climate change effects on fire season length and intensity (ECO) are driving the increasing demand on wildland fire resources (FSR-02, FSR-10)
- Watershed and water supply systems studied in ECO connect to the water supply infrastructure that fire suppression depends on (FSR-07)

### INP — Infrastructure and Planning

The INP research project examines urban planning, transportation systems, and the physical infrastructure of the built environment. First responder operations are fundamentally shaped by the built environment they serve.

Key connections:
- Building construction types and fire behavior (FSR-02) are a function of building codes and construction practices documented in INP
- The wildland-urban interface (FSR-10) is a land use planning phenomenon — the expansion of development into fire-prone areas is an INP topic with FSR consequences
- Transportation infrastructure — road networks, bridge capacities, highway design — determines response times and mutual aid accessibility (FSR-10)
- The seismic vulnerability of Pacific Northwest infrastructure (FSR-10) connects to INP research on earthquake-resilient design
- Urban density and building height affect fire department apparatus requirements, ladder company placement, and high-rise firefighting operations (FSR-02, FSR-07)
- Water system design — hydrant spacing, main capacity, pressure zones — is an INP topic that directly determines fire suppression capability (FSR-07)

### WDC — Woodcraft

The connection between first responders and woodcraft runs through building construction. Every structure that firefighters enter is a wood structure (in the PNW, almost certainly Douglas fir framing [WDC-09]), and building construction type determines fire behavior, collapse risk, and firefighter safety. The building construction knowledge in WDC-08 (Construction Framing) is the same knowledge that fire officers apply during size-up (FSR-02).

Key connections:
- Building construction types (WDC-08) directly determine fire behavior, ventilation tactics, and collapse indicators (FSR-02)
- Platform framing, post-and-beam, and timber framing (WDC-08) each present different fire behavior profiles
- Engineered lumber (LVL, I-joists, TJIs) loses structural integrity under fire faster than solid dimensional lumber — a critical concern documented in FSR-02 and explained by the wood science in WDC-02
- Western red cedar's natural fire resistance (lower flame spread than many species due to low resin content) makes it a unique exterior cladding material, though its use in shingle roofing has been restricted in some jurisdictions due to wildland-urban interface fire risk (FSR-10)
- The mass timber revolution (WDC-09) creates new fire behavior questions — CLT chars predictably and maintains structural integrity, but fire departments are still developing tactics for mass timber buildings

### HEL — Health and Environmental Linkages

The HEL research project examines the connections between environmental conditions and human health. The occupational health hazards of emergency services — carcinogen exposure, sleep deprivation, cumulative stress — are health-environment linkages of the most direct kind.

Key connections:
- Firefighter cancer risk (FSR-09) is an occupational health topic that connects to HEL research on environmental carcinogens
- PFAS contamination in turnout gear (FSR-09) connects to the broader PFAS contamination research in HEL
- Combustion product toxicology (FSR-07, FSR-09) — the chemicals released by burning modern materials — is a HEL research area with direct FSR implications
- Hazardous materials response (FSR-02) involves the same chemical hazards documented in HEL from an environmental health perspective
- The air quality impacts of wildfire smoke (FSR-10) are a HEL topic experienced by both the public and the firefighters working in it
- Sleep and circadian disruption as a health hazard (FSR-09) connects to HEL research on shift work and health outcomes
- The IARC classification of firefighting as Group 1 carcinogenic (FSR-09) is a reference point for both FSR and HEL

## Key Texts and References

### Fire Service

**"Report from Engine Co. 82"** by Dennis Smith (1972). The definitive first-person account of firefighting in a high-volume urban firehouse. Smith's account of working in the South Bronx during the arson epidemic of the early 1970s remains the most widely read book about the American fire service. It captures the daily reality of fire operations — the camaraderie, the danger, the dark humor, the grief — with an honesty that has not been surpassed.

**"Young Men and Fire"** by Norman Maclean (1992). Maclean's reconstruction of the 1949 Mann Gulch fire, which killed twelve smokejumpers and a forest ranger in Montana, is both a work of narrative nonfiction and a study of decision-making under extreme conditions. The book's analysis of how the Mann Gulch disaster unfolded — the failure to recognize the danger, the collapse of crew cohesion, the one decision (Wag Dodge's escape fire) that saved a life — is foundational to modern wildland fire safety training.

**"Fire Officer's Handbook of Tactics"** by John Norman (multiple editions, current 5th edition). The standard tactical reference for structural firefighting operations. Norman's analysis of fire behavior, building construction, and tactical decision-making is used in fire officer training programs across the country.

**"Collapse of Burning Buildings"** by Vincent Dunn (2nd edition, 2010). The definitive reference on how buildings fail during fires. Dunn's analysis of construction types, structural weaknesses, and collapse indicators is essential knowledge for every firefighting officer.

**"Braving the Flames"** by Peter A. Michaels (1989). First-person accounts from New York City firefighters, documenting the transition from the war years of the 1970s to the professional modernization of the 1980s.

### Emergency Medical Services

**"Paramedic"** by Peter Canning (multiple editions, current: "A Small Place Near God"). Canning's memoir of his career as a paramedic in Hartford, Connecticut is the most honest first-person account of the daily reality of EMS work. The book documents the patients, the protocols, the system failures, and the psychological toll with unflinching clarity. It is required reading in many paramedic programs.

**"Accidental Death and Disability: The Neglected Disease of Modern Society"** — National Academy of Sciences, National Research Council (1966). The white paper that created modern EMS. The document's assessment of the inadequacy of prehospital care in America catalyzed the legislative and institutional changes that built the paramedic system. Every paramedic working today is a product of the chain of events this report set in motion.

**"Emergency!"** — Jack Webb and Robert A. Cinader (NBC television series, 1972-1977). Not a text but a cultural artifact of primary importance. The television series that introduced the concept of the firefighter/paramedic to the American public and generated the political demand for paramedic legislation in states across the country. Discussed in FSR-01.

**"Into the Breach: A Year of Life and Death with EMS"** by J. A. Karam (2002). A year-long immersion in the New York City EMS system, documenting the operational reality of high-volume urban EMS.

### Law Enforcement

**"Between the World and Me"** by Ta-Nehisi Coates (2015). Not a book about policing per se, but essential context for understanding the community experience of law enforcement in America. Coates' analysis of the relationship between the Black body and the institutions of the American state, including policing, provides a framework that the law enforcement document (FSR-04) requires.

**"New Perspectives in Policing"** series — Harvard Kennedy School and the National Institute of Justice. A series of papers addressing contemporary policing challenges — legitimacy, use of force, community trust, technology — by leading scholars and practitioners.

**"Rise of the Warrior Cop: The Militarization of America's Police Forces"** by Radley Balko (2013). An examination of the increasing militarization of American police departments, from the War on Drugs through the post-9/11 equipment transfers from the Department of Defense to local agencies.

### Mental Health and Crisis Response

**"Crazy: A Father's Search Through America's Mental Health Madness"** by Pete Earley (2006). Earley's account of navigating the mental health system with his adult son, who has bipolar disorder, documents the same system failures that first responders encounter from the other side of the revolving door.

**"Insane: America's Criminal Treatment of Mental Illness"** by Alisa Roth (2018). A detailed examination of how the American criminal justice system has become the de facto mental health system — the institutional context for the crisis response gap documented in FSR-08.

**"Crisis Intervention Team Core Elements"** — CIT International (2019). The standard reference for CIT program implementation, describing the Memphis Model and its adaptations.

**"CAHOOTS Media Guide"** — White Bird Clinic, Eugene, Oregon. The primary source for the CAHOOTS program model, outcomes data, and replication guidance discussed in FSR-08 and the [CAHOOTS case study](case-studies/cahoots-model.md).

### Responder Wellness

**"I Love a Fire Fighter: What the Family Needs to Know"** by Ellen Kirschman, PhD (2004). The standard reference on the impact of the fire service career on families. Kirschman's work addresses the relationship dynamics, psychological impacts, and coping strategies specific to fire service families.

**"Emotional Survival for Law Enforcement"** by Kevin M. Gilmartin, PhD (2002). Gilmartin's analysis of the psychological cycle of law enforcement work — the hypervigilance roller coaster — and its impact on officer health and family relationships.

**"On Combat: The Psychology and Physiology of Deadly Conflict in War and in Peace"** by Lt. Col. Dave Grossman (2004). Grossman's analysis of the physiological effects of combat stress applies directly to first responder stress physiology. The book's description of perceptual distortions under extreme stress (tunnel vision, auditory exclusion, time distortion) matches first responder reports of high-stress incidents.

**Ruderman Family Foundation White Paper on First Responder Mental Health** (2018). The study that documented that more firefighters and police officers die by suicide than in the line of duty. The most widely cited source for first responder suicide data.

### Standards and Regulatory References

**NFPA 1001** — Standard for Fire Fighter Professional Qualifications. Defines the job performance requirements for Fire Fighter I and Fire Fighter II certification.

**NFPA 1500** — Standard on Fire Department Occupational Safety, Health, and Wellness Program. The comprehensive standard governing firefighter safety.

**NFPA 1582** — Standard on Comprehensive Occupational Medical Program for Fire Departments. Defines medical fitness requirements for firefighter candidates and incumbents.

**NFPA 1971** — Standard on Protective Ensembles for Structural Fire Fighting and Proximity Fire Fighting. Governs the performance requirements for turnout gear.

**NFPA 1981** — Standard on Open-Circuit Self-Contained Breathing Apparatus for Emergency Services. Governs SCBA performance requirements.

**National EMS Education Standards** — NHTSA, Department of Transportation. Defines the educational content and competency requirements for each EMS certification level (EMR, EMT, AEMT, Paramedic).

**National EMS Scope of Practice Model** — NHTSA. Defines the scope of practice for each EMS certification level.

**National Incident Management System (NIMS)** — FEMA. The national standard for incident management, including the Incident Command System (ICS).

### Federal Agency Resources

**National Fire Academy (NFA)** — Emmitsburg, Maryland. The federal institution for advanced fire service training and education, operated by the United States Fire Administration within FEMA.

**FEMA Independent Study (IS) Courses** — A catalog of free, self-paced courses covering emergency management, incident command, NIMS, and related topics. IS-100 (Introduction to ICS), IS-200 (Basic ICS for Single Resources and Initial Action Incidents), and IS-700 (Introduction to NIMS) are foundational courses required of all emergency responders.

**National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) — Fire Fighter Fatality Investigation and Prevention Program (FFFIPP)**. Investigates line-of-duty deaths and publishes reports with recommendations for prevention. Each NIOSH report is a detailed case study of what went wrong and what could have prevented it.

**NIOSH Firefighter Cancer Study** — The largest epidemiological study of cancer risk among U.S. firefighters, tracking approximately 30,000 firefighters across three cities. Provided key evidence for the elevated cancer risk in firefighters.

**Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) — Emergency Medical Services**. Provides data, research, and guidance on EMS system design, workforce health, and patient outcomes.

### Key Organizations

**International Association of Fire Fighters (IAFF)** — The labor union representing professional firefighters and paramedics in the U.S. and Canada. Operates the Center of Excellence for Behavioral Health Treatment and Recovery.

**International Association of Fire Chiefs (IAFC)** — The professional organization for fire service leaders.

**National Volunteer Fire Council (NVFC)** — The national organization representing volunteer firefighters. Advocates for volunteer recruitment, retention, and wellness.

**National Association of EMTs (NAEMT)** — The professional organization for EMS providers at all levels.

**National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians (NREMT)** — The national certification body for EMS providers.

**National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI)** — Advocacy and support organization for people with mental illness and their families. NAMI chapters were instrumental in developing the CIT model.

**CIT International** — The organization that promotes and supports Crisis Intervention Team programs worldwide.

**Firefighter Behavioral Health Alliance (FBHA)** — Tracks firefighter suicides and advocates for behavioral health awareness in the fire service.

**Code Green Campaign** — Nonprofit focused on mental health awareness and education in fire and EMS.

**First Responder Support Network** — Operates residential treatment and support programs for first responders and their families.

### Pacific Northwest Specific References

**Washington State Council of Fire Fighters** — The state affiliate of the IAFF, representing professional firefighters and paramedics across Washington.

**King County Emergency Medical Services Division** — Administers the Medic One/EMS system, including levy funding, medical direction, and quality assurance.

**Harborview Injury Prevention and Research Center** — University of Washington. Conducts research on injury epidemiology and prevention, including prehospital care outcomes research that informs the King County EMS system.

**Washington State Patrol** — State law enforcement agency providing highway patrol, criminal investigation, and forensic services.

**Oregon Office of State Fire Marshal** — The state agency responsible for fire prevention, training certification, and fire data collection in Oregon.

**Washington Department of Natural Resources (DNR) — Wildfire Division** — Manages wildfire suppression on state and private lands in Washington.

**Northwest Interagency Coordination Center (NWCC)** — Portland, Oregon. Coordinates wildfire resource allocation across the Pacific Northwest.

**Pacific Northwest Seismic Network (PNSN)** — University of Washington. Monitors seismic activity in the Pacific Northwest, including the Cascadia Subduction Zone.

**USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory (CVO)** — Vancouver, Washington. Monitors volcanic activity in the Cascade Range, including the Mount Rainier lahar hazard.

**Oregon Resilience Plan** — Oregon Seismic Safety Policy Advisory Commission (2013). The comprehensive plan for Oregon's resilience to a Cascadia Subduction Zone earthquake.

**Cascadia Rising Exercise Series** — FEMA and state emergency management agencies. The multi-state exercise program that tests the region's response capability for a CSZ event.

### Key Academic Journals

Research in emergency services is published across a wide range of academic and professional journals. The following journals appear most frequently in the references for this series:

**Prehospital Emergency Care** — The official journal of the National Association of EMS Physicians (NAEMSP). Publishes peer-reviewed research on all aspects of prehospital care, from clinical interventions to system design.

**Journal of Emergency Medical Services (JEMS)** — The primary professional publication for the EMS community, combining clinical education, operational analysis, and industry news.

**Fire Engineering** — The oldest continuously published fire service journal in the United States (since 1877). Focuses on fire suppression tactics, training methods, and apparatus.

**Journal of Traumatic Stress** — Publishes research on PTSD, traumatic stress reactions, and treatment outcomes. A primary source for first responder PTSD prevalence data.

**Psychiatric Services** — Published by the American Psychiatric Association. Publishes research on mental health service delivery, including the CIT evaluation studies referenced in FSR-08.

**Resuscitation** — An international journal focused on cardiac arrest research, CPR science, and emergency cardiovascular care. Publishes the evidence base for many of the cardiac protocols described in FSR-03.

**Annals of Emergency Medicine** — The official journal of the American College of Emergency Physicians. Publishes research on emergency medicine across the prehospital and hospital continuum.

### Films and Documentaries

Several documentaries provide visual context for the topics covered in this series:

**"Burn"** (2012) — Documentary following the Detroit Fire Department, one of the busiest and most under-resourced departments in the country. Captures the daily reality of fire operations in an environment of extreme fiscal constraint.

**"Emergency!"** (1972-1977) — The NBC television series discussed in FSR-01. Available in its entirety and remains a valuable historical document of early paramedic operations.

**"After the Fire"** (various) — Several documentaries have examined the long-term health consequences of 9/11 for first responders, including the fight for the Zadroga Act.

**"Ernie & Joe: Crisis Cops"** (2019) — HBO documentary following two San Antonio police officers on the mental health unit, illustrating both the possibilities and limitations of the CIT model discussed in FSR-08.

## A Note on Sources

The FSR research series draws on published research, government reports, professional standards documents, and the accumulated institutional knowledge of the fire service and EMS. Where specific statistics are cited, they reflect the most current data available at the time of writing and are drawn from the organizations listed above.

Some knowledge in the emergency services is not published. It exists in the oral tradition of the firehouse, in the institutional memory of departments, in the stories told after shift by people who were there. This knowledge is not less valid for being unpublished. The person to whom this research is dedicated carries thirty years of it.
