# How to Become a Paramedic --- The Medical Path

## Before You Start

Maybe firefighting is not what drew you. Maybe you watched someone work a cardiac arrest and thought: I want to be the one running the code. Maybe you sat in the back of an ambulance once --- as a patient, as a family member, as a bystander --- and the person who took charge, who assessed, who made decisions under pressure with calm authority, was a paramedic. Maybe you are an EMT already and the limitations of BLS frustrate you. Maybe you are a nurse who wants to practice outside the hospital walls. Maybe you are twenty and figuring out your life, or forty and changing careers, or somewhere in between.

Whatever brought you here, this guide gives you the actual path. Not the brochure version. The real sequence of decisions, training, certification, and employment that takes a person from civilian to paramedic. The medical path through emergency services --- the path for someone drawn to medicine over firefighting, though those paths frequently converge.

The person this research series is dedicated to walked both paths simultaneously for thirty years. Firefighter and paramedic. Pulling hose and starting IVs. That dual role is the gold credential, and this guide will show you how to get there. But it will also show you the medical path on its own, because paramedicine is a career in its own right --- one that is evolving faster than almost any other healthcare profession.

---

## Step 1: EMT-Basic --- The Foundation

Every paramedic career starts here. EMT-Basic (EMT-B, now formally just "EMT" under the National EMS Education Standards) is the entry point into emergency medical services. You cannot skip it. You should not want to skip it. The EMT course teaches you how to assess a patient, how to think systematically about what is wrong, and how to intervene within a defined scope of practice. These skills are the foundation that everything else builds on.

### What EMT Training Covers

The EMT course runs 120 to 150 hours at most programs, though some community college programs expand to 200 hours by adding clinical time and depth. The curriculum is built around the National EMS Education Standards and covers:

- **Patient assessment** --- the systematic approach to evaluating a patient: scene safety, primary survey (airway, breathing, circulation, disability, exposure), history taking (SAMPLE: Signs/Symptoms, Allergies, Medications, Past history, Last oral intake, Events leading to incident), secondary assessment, reassessment. This framework becomes your operating system. You will use it on every patient for the rest of your career.
- **Airway management** --- head-tilt chin-lift, jaw thrust, oropharyngeal airway, nasopharyngeal airway, bag-valve-mask ventilation, suctioning. Basic interventions that keep a patient alive until advanced procedures are available.
- **CPR and AED** --- cardiopulmonary resuscitation and automated external defibrillation. The chain of survival. High-quality compressions. Team dynamics during a resuscitation.
- **Hemorrhage control** --- direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application. The lessons of military medicine translated to civilian practice.
- **Splinting and immobilization** --- managing fractures and suspected spinal injuries.
- **Medical emergencies** --- cardiac presentations (chest pain, shortness of breath), diabetic emergencies (hypoglycemia, hyperglycemia), neurological emergencies (stroke, seizures), allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), respiratory emergencies (asthma, COPD), overdose and poisoning, psychiatric emergencies.
- **Trauma assessment** --- mechanism of injury, rapid trauma survey, managing multi-system trauma patients.
- **Obstetric emergencies** --- imminent delivery in the field, complications (prolapsed cord, breech, postpartum hemorrhage). Rare but catastrophic when they occur.
- **Pediatric emergencies** --- anatomical and physiological differences, pediatric assessment, the Broselow tape.
- **EMS operations** --- scene management, triage (START triage for mass casualty incidents), ambulance operations, documentation.

### The Medications You Can Give

The EMT scope of practice includes a limited but important medication list: oxygen (the most administered drug in EMS), oral glucose (for hypoglycemia in conscious patients), aspirin (for suspected cardiac events), epinephrine via auto-injector (for anaphylaxis), naloxone/Narcan (for opioid overdose), and in many jurisdictions, albuterol via nebulizer (for bronchospasm). Some states allow additional medications at the EMT level. The pharmacological knowledge required is basic compared to paramedic practice, but the decision-making --- when to give what, and when not to --- introduces you to clinical reasoning.

### Where to Get Trained

**Community colleges** are the best value. Programs cost $1,000 to $2,000 for tuition and materials, run one semester (16 weeks at 2-3 evenings per week plus skills labs), and the credits may apply toward a paramedic degree later. In the PNW: Tacoma Community College, Pierce College, Bellevue College, Portland Community College, Clark College, and Spokane Community College all offer EMT programs.

**Fire academies and training centers** often bundle EMT with fire science training. If you already know you want the dual-role firefighter/paramedic path, this can be efficient. The Washington State Patrol Fire Training Academy in North Bend offers programs, and many regional fire training consortiums include EMT in their recruit curriculum.

**Private training centers** charge more ($2,000 to $5,000) but often offer accelerated schedules --- 8 to 10 weeks of full-time immersion. These are useful if you need to get certified quickly but cannot commit to a semester-long evening schedule.

**Cost reality:** $1,000 to $2,000 at a community college. $80 for the NREMT cognitive exam. $20-50 for the practical exam (varies by state). Budget $1,500 to $2,500 total. Financial aid, VA benefits, and workforce development grants apply at community colleges. Some fire departments and ambulance services offer tuition assistance or reimbursement for EMT training as a hiring incentive.

### The NREMT Exam

After completing your EMT course, you take the National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians cognitive exam. It is a computer-adaptive test --- the difficulty adjusts based on your performance. Minimum 70 questions, maximum 120. The pass rate for first-time EMT candidates is approximately 70 percent. If you studied the material and practiced the skills, you will pass. The psychomotor (practical) exam includes skill stations for patient assessment, CPR, and other competencies evaluated by an examiner.

Pass both, and you are a nationally registered EMT. Most states accept NREMT certification for state licensure, though some require additional state-specific steps.

## Step 2: AEMT --- The Optional Bridge (And Why It Exists)

The Advanced Emergency Medical Technician (AEMT) level sits between EMT and Paramedic. It requires 150 to 250 additional hours of training beyond EMT. The AEMT can establish intravenous (IV) access, administer IV fluid therapy, and use a broader range of medications including some intravenous drugs.

### Why the AEMT Level Exists

The gap between EMT and Paramedic is enormous. An EMT's scope is basic assessment and a handful of medications. A Paramedic's scope includes advanced airway management, 12-lead ECG interpretation, cardiac electrical therapy, 30 to 50 medications, invasive procedures, and independent clinical decision-making. The training gap is equally large: 150 hours versus 1,200 to 1,800 hours. For many EMS systems, especially in rural areas, the AEMT fills a crucial middle ground --- a provider who can start an IV, push critical medications, and provide a higher level of care than an EMT but without the full paramedic training pipeline.

### Should You Do AEMT?

For most people on the paramedic path, AEMT is an optional step. It depends on your circumstances:

- **Yes, if** you are working in a rural system where ALS ambulances are far away and AEMT skills will save lives in the interim. AEMT gives you IV access and a broader drug formulary immediately.
- **Yes, if** your paramedic program requires AEMT as a prerequisite (some do).
- **Yes, if** you want to test whether the advanced medical content is something you can handle before committing to a full paramedic program.
- **No, if** you have a direct path to paramedic school available and the time and resources to commit. Most of the AEMT material is covered again in the first months of paramedic training. Doing both is not wasted time, but it is extra time.

### PNW AEMT Programs

AEMT is less common in the urban PNW, where most systems staff with either EMTs or full Paramedics. It is more common in eastern Washington, eastern Oregon, and rural communities. Programs are available through community colleges and regional EMS training cooperatives.

## Step 3: Paramedic --- The Full Scope

This is where the path changes from procedural to intellectual. Paramedic school is not a skills course. It is a medical education compressed into twelve to twenty-four months. You will learn anatomy, physiology, pathophysiology, pharmacology, cardiology, and clinical decision-making at a depth comparable to nursing school. You will be expected to understand not just what to do but why --- why the drug works, why the rhythm is lethal, why the blood pressure is dropping, and what it means for what happens next.

### What Paramedic Training Covers

The National EMS Education Standards define the paramedic curriculum at 1,200 to 1,800 hours. Programs may be structured as a certificate (12 to 18 months), an associate degree (2 years, including prerequisites), or increasingly, a bachelor's degree. The curriculum includes:

**Anatomy and Physiology.** The cardiac conduction system, the respiratory mechanics, the neurological pathways, the endocrine feedback loops, the renal system, the hepatic metabolism. Not at the level of a physician, but deep enough to understand what is going wrong when a patient presents with a set of symptoms. You cannot interpret a 12-lead ECG without understanding cardiac electrophysiology. You cannot manage a diabetic emergency without understanding insulin, glucagon, and hepatic glucose regulation. The A&P is the hardest academic component for most students, and it is non-negotiable.

**Pathophysiology.** What happens when systems fail. Cardiogenic shock. Sepsis. Diabetic ketoacidosis. Pulmonary embolism. Tension pneumothorax. Stroke (ischemic vs. hemorrhagic). Status epilepticus. Acute coronary syndrome. Anaphylaxis. You learn these not as vocabulary words but as clinical entities you will encounter in the field and must recognize, differentiate, and treat.

**Pharmacology.** Thirty to fifty or more medications. For each: mechanism of action, indications, contraindications, dosing (often weight-based, requiring calculation under stress), routes of administration (IV, IO, IM, SL, nebulized, endotracheal), onset of action, duration of action, and side effects. Epinephrine for cardiac arrest. Amiodarone for refractory V-fib. Adenosine for SVT. Atropine for symptomatic bradycardia. Fentanyl for pain management. Midazolam for seizures. Ketamine for sedation. Albuterol and ipratropium for bronchospasm. Nitroglycerin for chest pain and acute pulmonary edema. Naloxone for opioid overdose. Dextrose for hypoglycemia. The pharmacological knowledge required of a paramedic is substantial, and the consequences of getting it wrong are measured in patient harm.

**Cardiology.** The signature discipline. 12-lead ECG acquisition and interpretation. Recognizing STEMI (ST-elevation myocardial infarction) in the field and activating the cath lab. Identifying lethal dysrhythmias: ventricular fibrillation, pulseless ventricular tachycardia, asystole, pulseless electrical activity. Managing symptomatic bradycardia and tachycardia. Electrical therapy: defibrillation, synchronized cardioversion, transcutaneous pacing. ACLS (Advanced Cardiac Life Support) algorithms. Cardiac arrest management --- the full resuscitation, from recognition through ROSC (Return of Spontaneous Circulation) through post-cardiac arrest care. This is the discipline that King County Medic One built its reputation on, and it is the most complex clinical skill set in paramedicine. (See the [King County Medic One case study](../case-studies/king-county-medic-one.md) for the system that sets the global standard.)

**Advanced Airway Management.** Endotracheal intubation --- passing a tube through the vocal cords into the trachea using direct or video laryngoscopy. Supraglottic airways (King LT, i-gel) for when intubation fails or is not indicated. Surgical cricothyrotomy --- the last resort when the patient cannot be intubated and cannot be oxygenated. Waveform capnography for continuous confirmation of tube placement and ventilation adequacy. The stakes are absolute: an esophageal intubation kills the patient. Waveform capnography has made this error far less likely, but the skill itself requires extensive practice.

**Trauma.** The MARCH assessment (Massive hemorrhage, Airway, Respiration, Circulation, Hypothermia). Tourniquet application. Wound packing with hemostatic agents. Needle decompression for tension pneumothorax. Chest seal application. The lessons of Iraq and Afghanistan translated into civilian prehospital care. Trauma is urgent, physical, and algorithmic --- the decisions must be fast because the patient is bleeding.

### Clinical Rotations

Clinical rotations are where the textbook becomes the patient. You rotate through hospital departments under the supervision of physicians and nurses:

- **Emergency Department.** The full spectrum of emergency presentations. You assess patients, propose treatment plans, and perform procedures under physician supervision. This is where your clinical reasoning develops --- the ability to take a chief complaint, build a differential diagnosis, and decide what to do.
- **Operating Room.** Intubation practice under the supervision of an anesthesiologist. The OR is the safest place to learn intubation: the patient is anesthetized, positioned optimally, and a physician is standing next to you ready to take over if needed. You may perform 15 to 25 intubations during OR rotations.
- **Labor and Delivery / OB.** Obstetric emergencies are rare in the field but high-stakes. L&D rotations give you exposure to normal deliveries and complications: breech, prolapsed cord, preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage.
- **Intensive Care Unit.** Ventilator management, invasive monitoring, cardiac monitoring, critical pharmacology. The ICU teaches you what happens to your patient after you deliver them to the hospital.
- **Cardiac Catheterization Lab.** You watch the definitive treatment for the STEMI patient you will identify and transport. Seeing the occluded coronary artery opened and stented gives context to the field 12-lead you obtained that activated this room.

### Field Internship

The capstone. You ride on an ambulance as a third crew member, functioning as the primary care provider under the supervision of a precepting paramedic. The preceptor evaluates your performance on every call. You run the codes. You make the treatment decisions. You manage the scene, the patient, and the crew. The preceptor is there to intervene if you are about to do something dangerous, but otherwise, you are expected to function as a paramedic.

The field internship is where you discover whether you can do this. Not whether you can pass a test, but whether you can integrate assessment, treatment, pharmacology, airway management, communication, and clinical decision-making into a coherent response while a patient is looking at you with fear in their eyes and their family is standing behind you. The internship typically requires 300 to 500 patient contacts and a minimum number of advanced skills (intubations, IV starts, medication administrations, cardiac arrest management).

The preceptor's evaluation --- "ready" or "not ready" --- is the final gate before the national exam.

### The NREMT Paramedic Exam

The National Registry Paramedic cognitive examination is a computer-adaptive test with a maximum of 150 questions. The first-attempt pass rate is approximately 60 to 70 percent. It is the most challenging certification exam in prehospital medicine because the consequences of certifying an incompetent paramedic are measured in patient harm.

The psychomotor examination includes skill stations: patient assessment (trauma and medical), cardiac arrest management (static and dynamic scenarios), oral stations where you verbalize your assessment and treatment plan for complex scenarios, and additional random skill stations (IV start, medication administration, airway management).

Pass both components, and you are a nationally registered Paramedic. You can practice medicine in the field.

## Employment: Where Paramedics Work

### Fire-Based EMS

This is the dominant model in the Pacific Northwest and the model that defined a thirty-year career. The fire department operates the ambulance service. Firefighters are cross-trained as paramedics. The advantage is integration --- fire and EMS arrive together, share stations, share command structure, and share resources. The firefighter/paramedic is the most versatile credential in public safety: you fight fire, you run medical calls, you perform technical rescue, you handle hazmat. The disadvantage is maintaining competency in two demanding disciplines simultaneously.

Fire-based EMS departments in the PNW: Seattle Fire Department, Portland Fire & Rescue, Tacoma Fire Department, Bellevue Fire, Redmond Fire, Everett Fire, and dozens of fire districts throughout Washington and Oregon. Most PNW fire departments require or strongly prefer the dual firefighter/paramedic certification. Getting hired as a firefighter and then attending paramedic school on the department's dime --- often with a 3 to 5 year service commitment after completion --- is the standard path and the one described in the [becoming a firefighter guide](becoming-a-firefighter.md).

### Private Ambulance

Companies like AMR (American Medical Response), Falck, and smaller regional services provide 911 and interfacility ambulance transport. Private ambulance services hire paramedics directly, often with less competitive hiring processes than fire departments. The work is pure EMS --- no firefighting, no rescue, just patient care and transport. Pay is typically lower than fire-based EMS ($40,000 to $55,000 starting in the PNW), but the experience is concentrated medical. You will run more calls and see more medicine than a fire-based paramedic, because medicine is all you do.

For many new paramedics, private ambulance service is the first job. You get field experience, build confidence, and decide whether to stay in EMS or pursue the dual-role firefighter/paramedic path.

### Hospital-Based EMS

Some hospitals operate their own ambulance services, particularly in rural areas. Harborview Medical Center in Seattle is the region's Level 1 trauma center and runs the King County Medic One paramedic training program --- the 10-month residency that produces the highest-trained paramedics in the country. OHSU (Oregon Health & Science University) in Portland has similar integration between hospital medicine and prehospital care.

Hospital-based paramedics often have closer relationships with physicians and access to more advanced continuing education than their fire-based or private-service counterparts.

### Community Paramedicine

The newest and most rapidly evolving employment path. Community paramedics use their assessment skills and clinical judgment outside the traditional 911 response model. They conduct home visits to frequent 911 callers, perform post-discharge follow-ups to reduce hospital readmissions, connect patients with primary care and social services, administer vaccinations, and provide preventive health education.

Community paramedicine addresses a reality that thirty years of running calls makes obvious: many 911 calls are not emergencies. They are the symptoms of a healthcare system with no other access point for people who are uninsured, underinsured, homebound, or lost in the system. Community paramedicine deploys the paramedic's unique combination of clinical skill and community access proactively rather than reactively. It is the future of EMS --- see [FSR-03: EMS and Paramedicine](../03-ems-paramedic.md) for the broader context.

Reimbursement models for community paramedicine are still evolving. Medicare and some state Medicaid programs now cover certain community paramedicine services, but the funding landscape is inconsistent. The programs that exist --- and they are growing --- offer a different kind of career satisfaction: the ability to actually solve problems rather than responding to the same patient in crisis again and again.

## What Paramedics Actually Do: The Daily Reality

The public image of paramedicine is cardiac arrests and multi-vehicle pileups. The reality is that those calls are a small fraction of the work. Understanding what the job actually consists of, day to day, is essential before committing to the training.

### The Bread and Butter

Eighty percent of your calls will be medical, not trauma. The most common calls:

- **Falls.** Elderly patients who have fallen at home. Assessment, possible fracture management, transport. High frequency, often low acuity, but falls in the elderly can be the first sign of a stroke, a cardiac event, or a medication problem. The clinical challenge is figuring out why they fell.
- **Difficulty breathing.** COPD exacerbation, asthma, pneumonia, congestive heart failure, anxiety. You nebulize albuterol, apply CPAP, start an IV, and figure out which of these --- or which combination --- is the actual problem.
- **Chest pain.** Obtain a 12-lead ECG. Is it a STEMI? Unstable angina? GERD? Musculoskeletal? The ECG and your clinical assessment determine whether this patient needs a cardiac cath lab or an antacid.
- **Diabetic emergencies.** Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) is one of the most satisfying calls in EMS --- push dextrose, patient wakes up, thanks you, refuses transport. Hyperglycemia and DKA (diabetic ketoacidosis) are more complex and require transport.
- **Behavioral / psychiatric.** Suicidal ideation, psychotic episodes, severe anxiety, agitation. These calls require communication skills as much as medical skills. De-escalation, scene safety assessment, and knowing when the patient needs a hospital versus a crisis team.
- **Substance-related.** Opioid overdose (naloxone), alcohol intoxication, stimulant toxicity, polysubstance ingestion. Frequent, repetitive, and psychologically wearing.
- **Stroke.** Time-critical assessment using the Cincinnati Prehospital Stroke Scale or LAMS (Los Angeles Motor Scale), pre-notification of the receiving stroke center, rapid transport. Similar to STEMI --- the paramedic's field assessment activates a hospital team before the patient arrives.

### The Calls That Define You

And then there are the calls that you will remember for the rest of your life. The cardiac arrest where you achieved ROSC and the patient walked out of the hospital. The pediatric call where you kept a child alive long enough for the surgeon to fix what was broken. The trauma where your tourniquet stopped the bleeding and the patient kept their leg. These calls are why people become paramedics. They are not daily. They may not even be monthly. But they happen, and when they do, the years of training and practice and continuing education converge in a moment where you are the difference between a person living and a person dying.

The calls you carry home are discussed in [FSR-09: Responder Wellness](../09-responder-wellness.md). Read it. The medical path does not exempt you from the psychological cost of the work.

## Pay and Career Trajectory

### PNW Pay Ranges

Paramedic compensation varies significantly by employer type and experience:

- **Private ambulance (AMR, Falck):** $45,000 to $60,000 starting, up to $70,000 with experience. Benefits vary.
- **Fire-based EMS (firefighter/paramedic):** $60,000 to $90,000 depending on department, rank, and overtime. Seattle Fire Department firefighter/paramedics start around $75,000 and can exceed $100,000 with overtime and longevity. Portland Fire & Rescue is comparable. Smaller departments pay less, but cost of living is also lower.
- **Hospital-based:** $55,000 to $75,000. Benefits tend to be strong (hospital systems).
- **Community paramedicine:** $55,000 to $80,000. Newer programs, compensation is stabilizing.
- **King County Medic One (Harborview-trained):** Among the highest-paid paramedics in the country. The residency training, clinical depth, and system quality justify the premium.

### Career Progression

The paramedic career is not static. Advancement paths include:

- **Field Training Officer (FTO)** --- precepting new paramedics during their field internship
- **EMS Supervisor / Lieutenant** --- operational supervision of ambulance crews
- **EMS Captain / Chief** --- administrative and strategic leadership
- **EMS Educator** --- teaching in paramedic programs (often requires a bachelor's or master's degree)
- **Critical Care Paramedic (CCP / FP-C)** --- advanced certification for interfacility critical care transport, helicopter EMS (HEMS), or specialized units
- **Flight Paramedic** --- helicopter EMS. Competitive, high-acuity, high-risk. Requires FP-C certification and significant field experience.
- **Physician Assistant or Medical School** --- some paramedics use their clinical experience as a foundation for PA or MD/DO programs. Paramedic experience is valued in medical school admissions, particularly for emergency medicine.

## The Dual-Role Decision

At some point on this path, you will face the question: do I want to be a paramedic, or do I want to be a firefighter/paramedic?

The firefighter/paramedic is the gold credential. It is the career that ran for thirty years. It combines the physical, the technical, and the medical into a single role that can handle virtually any emergency. The hiring market recognizes it --- a firefighter/paramedic with experience can find work almost anywhere in the country. The [becoming a firefighter guide](becoming-a-firefighter.md) covers the fire side of the dual path.

But paramedicine alone is a career. It is a career that is growing, evolving, and increasingly valued. Community paramedicine is expanding the role. Critical care transport and flight paramedicine offer high-acuity specialization. Bachelor's and master's degree programs are elevating the profession's educational foundation. The paramedic who chooses medicine as their primary identity --- rather than fire --- has a career path that is broader and more dynamic than it has ever been.

The decision is yours. Both paths answer the call.
