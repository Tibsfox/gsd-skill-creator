# Ready for the Big One --- Citizen Emergency Preparedness (PNW Edition)

## The Conversation Nobody Wants to Have

You live in the Pacific Northwest. You live on top of one of the most dangerous seismic zones on Earth. The Cascadia Subduction Zone --- 700 miles of locked tectonic boundary running from Cape Mendocino, California, to northern Vancouver Island --- will rupture. Not might. Will. The geological record shows 41 full-margin ruptures in the last 10,000 years. The last one was January 26, 1700. The probability of another in the next 50 years is estimated at 7 to 15 percent for a full M9.0 event, and 37 percent for a partial rupture of M8.0 or greater. (The [Cascadia Earthquake case study](../case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md) covers the geology, the history, and the first responder implications in detail.)

This guide is not about the geology. This guide is about what you do before it happens, so that when the ground stops shaking, you and your family and your neighbors have what you need to survive the days and weeks that follow.

The old standard was 72 hours of supplies. Three days. That was the guidance for decades, and it was based on the assumption that federal assistance would arrive within three days of a major disaster. For Cascadia, 72 hours is inadequate. The Oregon Resilience Plan and Washington state emergency management have revised the recommendation to a minimum of two weeks. Fourteen days. Because when the bridges on I-5 collapse, when the ports are damaged by liquefaction, when the airports are inoperable because the runways have buckled, the logistics of moving disaster relief into the region will take days to weeks to establish.

Two weeks of self-sufficiency. That is the standard this guide is built around. It sounds like a lot. It is achievable. And it might save your life.

## Water: The First Priority

Water is the single most critical supply. Dehydration becomes life-threatening within three days. Municipal water systems in the affected area will be compromised for weeks --- the mains will rupture, the treatment plants will lose power, the distribution network will be damaged. You will not have tap water.

### How Much

One gallon per person per day. Minimum. That covers drinking, cooking, and minimal hygiene. If you have pets, add their water needs. If you live in a hot climate or expect to do physical labor (clearing debris, helping neighbors), increase to 1.5 gallons per person per day.

For a family of four, fourteen days at one gallon per person per day: **56 gallons.** That is approximately seven standard 7-gallon water containers, or four 15-gallon barrels, or one 55-gallon water storage barrel. It takes up space. It is heavy. It is non-negotiable.

### Storage Options

- **Commercial water containers** (5 to 7 gallon, food-grade plastic with spigot): Available at camping supply stores and emergency preparedness retailers. Scepter, Reliance, and WaterBrick are reliable brands. Stack them in a cool, dark location (garage, basement, utility room). Replace the water every 6 to 12 months, or add a water preservative (Aquamira, Water Preserver Concentrate) that extends storage life to 5 years.
- **55-gallon water barrel:** The highest-volume option. Food-grade polyethylene, opaque (prevents algae), with a bung wrench for the cap and a siphon or pump for extraction. Available from emergency supply retailers. A full 55-gallon barrel weighs approximately 460 pounds --- place it where you want it before you fill it, because you are not moving it afterward. Treat with water preservative at filling.
- **WaterBOBs or bathtub bladders:** A disposable plastic bladder that sits in your bathtub and holds 100 gallons of tap water. Fill it when you receive an earthquake early warning (ShakeAlert gives seconds to tens of seconds, which may be enough to start filling) or when you know a major storm is approaching. Not a replacement for stored water, but an excellent supplement.
- **Hot water heater:** Your home's water heater holds 30 to 80 gallons of potable water. After an earthquake, shut off the gas or electricity to the water heater, then drain it through the spigot at the bottom. This is emergency water you already have --- know where the spigot is and how to use it.

### Water Purification

Stored water runs out. You need the ability to purify water from natural sources (streams, lakes, rainwater, even suspect tap water).

- **Filtration:** A gravity-fed or pump water filter removes bacteria and protozoa. Sawyer Squeeze, LifeStraw, Katadyn Hiker, or a Berkey gravity filter are all effective. Filters do NOT remove viruses (uncommon in PNW surface water but possible in disaster-contaminated water). For comprehensive protection, filter AND treat.
- **Chemical treatment:** Unscented household bleach (6-8% sodium hypochlorite): 8 drops per gallon of clear water, 16 drops per gallon of cloudy water. Stir, let stand 30 minutes. The water should have a slight chlorine smell. If it does not, repeat the dosage and wait another 30 minutes. This kills bacteria and viruses. Alternatively, water purification tablets (Aquatabs, Potable Aqua) are lightweight, shelf-stable, and effective.
- **Boiling:** A rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 feet elevation) kills all pathogens. Requires fuel and a heat source, which may be limited. Effective but resource-intensive.

**The layered approach:** Store enough water for 14 days. Have filtration and chemical treatment capability to extend beyond that. Know where your nearest natural water sources are (creeks, rivers, lakes, rainwater collection). The backup to the backup is what keeps you alive when the primary fails.

## Food: Fourteen Days of Calories

Shelf-stable, calorie-dense, no-cook capability. Your kitchen may not be functional. Your power will be out. Your refrigerator contents will spoil within 24 to 48 hours.

### What to Store

- **Canned goods:** Vegetables, fruits, beans, soups, stews, tuna, chicken, chili. Calorie-dense, long shelf life (2 to 5 years), require no cooking (edible cold from the can). Include a manual can opener --- two of them, because one will disappear when you need it.
- **Dried foods:** Rice, pasta, oats, dried beans, lentils. Require water and heat to prepare, but are calorie-dense, lightweight, and store for years. Include only if you have a way to cook (camp stove, outdoor grill).
- **Peanut butter and nut butters:** High calorie, high protein, requires no preparation. A jar of peanut butter is one of the most efficient survival foods available.
- **Crackers, granola bars, trail mix, dried fruit:** Quick energy, no preparation, portable. Rotate stock (6 to 12 month shelf life).
- **Freeze-dried meals:** Mountain House, Peak Refuel, and other brands produce shelf-stable meals with 25 to 30 year shelf life. Just add hot water. More expensive per meal but extremely long storage and decent nutrition.
- **Comfort food:** Coffee, tea, hot chocolate, candy, comfort snacks. Morale matters in a disaster. Do not underestimate the psychological value of a cup of coffee when everything else is broken.

### Cooking Without Power

- **Camp stove:** A portable propane or butane camp stove (Coleman, MSR, Jetboil) with 14 days of fuel. Store extra fuel canisters. Use OUTDOORS ONLY --- carbon monoxide from indoor combustion kills.
- **Charcoal or propane grill:** If you have one, it is a functional outdoor kitchen after an earthquake. Stock extra charcoal or propane.
- **Rocket stove or wood-burning camp stove:** Uses small sticks and biomass as fuel. Useful if commercial fuel runs out.
- **No-cook plan:** If you cannot cook, make sure all your stored food is edible without cooking. This is achievable with canned goods, nut butters, crackers, dried fruit, and granola bars.

### Rotation

The most common failure in food preparedness is storing food and forgetting it. Eat from your supply and replace what you use. First in, first out. Check expiration dates twice a year (tie it to daylight saving time changes --- when you change your clocks, check your supplies). The preparedness kit that you build once and never touch is the one that fails when you need it.

## Medical: Thirty Days of Medications and Trauma Readiness

### Prescription Medications

If you take medications for survival --- insulin, blood pressure medication, anti-seizure drugs, thyroid medication, psychiatric medications, blood thinners, immunosuppressants --- a 30-day supply in your emergency kit is essential. Not optional. Essential. Pharmacies will be closed. Supply chains will be disrupted. The medication you depend on daily will not be available.

Talk to your doctor about a 90-day prescription. Many insurance plans allow it for maintenance medications. Fill it, pull 30 days out for your emergency supply, and rotate monthly --- take from the emergency supply and replenish from your regular prescription. This keeps the medication fresh and ensures you always have a 30-day reserve.

### First Aid Kit

Not the bandage-and-aspirin kit from the drugstore. A comprehensive kit that addresses the injuries you will actually encounter after an earthquake:

- **Trauma supplies:** Tourniquets (CAT --- Combat Application Tourniquet --- or SOFTT-W). Hemostatic gauze (QuikClot, Celox). Israeli bandages (emergency compression bandages). Wound packing gauze. Chest seals (for penetrating chest injuries). These are the supplies that stop life-threatening bleeding, and they are the same supplies carried by paramedics and military medics. Take a Stop the Bleed class to learn how to use them --- the supplies are useless without the knowledge.
- **Standard first aid:** Adhesive bandages, gauze pads, medical tape, elastic wrap (ACE bandages), triangular bandages (slings), butterfly closures, antiseptic wipes, antibiotic ointment, burn gel, hydrocortisone cream, tweezers, medical shears, gloves (nitrile), instant cold packs.
- **Over-the-counter medications:** Ibuprofen (pain, inflammation), acetaminophen (pain, fever), diphenhydramine/Benadryl (allergic reactions, sleep aid), antacids, anti-diarrheal (Imodium), electrolyte replacement (Pedialyte packets, Nuun tablets).
- **Improvised splinting materials:** SAM splints (moldable aluminum/foam splints) are lightweight and versatile. Include several sizes.

### Stop the Bleed Training

The single most impactful medical training a civilian can take. Uncontrolled hemorrhage is the leading cause of preventable death in trauma. The Hartford Consensus --- driven by the same military medicine lessons that reformed prehospital EMS --- identifies hemorrhage control as the most critical bystander intervention. A two-hour Stop the Bleed class teaches tourniquet application, wound packing, and pressure application. Classes are offered free or at low cost through hospitals, fire departments, and community organizations throughout the PNW. Take one. Teach your family.

## Communication: When the Grid Goes Down

### NOAA Weather Radio

A battery-powered or hand-crank NOAA weather radio receives continuous broadcasts from the National Weather Service, including emergency alerts for earthquakes, tsunamis, volcanic activity, and severe weather. This is your primary information source when the power is out, the internet is down, and cell towers are overwhelmed. Midland, Sangean, and Eton make reliable weather radios. Keep one in your kit with spare batteries.

### Ham Radio

When everything else fails, amateur radio works. Ham radio operates independently of commercial infrastructure --- no cell towers, no internet backbone, no power grid required. A battery-powered HF transceiver can communicate across the region, across the continent, or around the world.

The Technician class amateur radio license is the entry point. The exam is 35 multiple-choice questions on basic radio theory, regulations, and operating practices. Study materials are available from the ARRL (American Radio Relay League) and online (hamstudy.org). The exam fee is $15 to $35. Most people pass after 10 to 20 hours of study. The license grants access to VHF and UHF frequencies --- the bands used for local and regional communication, which is exactly what you need in a disaster.

**ARES and RACES.** The Amateur Radio Emergency Service (ARES) and Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service (RACES) are organized volunteer networks that provide emergency communications during disasters. They train regularly, participate in exercises with fire departments and emergency management agencies, and are pre-positioned to activate when commercial communications fail. After a Cascadia event, ARES/RACES operators will be the backbone of regional communication until commercial systems are restored. Joining your local ARES group means you are both prepared for your own communication needs and contributing to your community's resilience.

**What to buy:** A handheld VHF/UHF transceiver (Yaesu FT-65R, Baofeng UV-5R, Kenwood TH-D75A) costs $25 to $300 depending on features. Add a magnetic mount antenna for your car and a simple J-pole antenna for your home. Total investment for a basic ham radio setup: $50 to $150.

### Family Communication Plan

- **Designate an out-of-area contact.** After major disasters, long-distance calls often work when local calls do not. Choose a friend or family member in another state as your central communication relay. Everyone in the family knows to check in with this person.
- **Designate a meeting place.** If your family is separated when the earthquake hits (school, work, errands), where do you meet? Pick two: one near home (a neighbor's house, a park) and one outside the neighborhood (a school, a community center) in case you cannot get home.
- **Write it down.** Each family member carries a card with the out-of-area contact's phone number, the meeting places, and any other critical information. Phones may be dead. Memory under stress is unreliable. Paper works when nothing else does.

## Shelter: If Your House Is Damaged

After a major earthquake, your home may be structurally compromised. Cracked foundation, shifted framing, damaged roof, gas leak. You may need to shelter outside your home for days.

- **Tent or tarp shelter:** A quality 4-season tent or a heavy-duty tarp (10x12 minimum) with paracord for rigging. Practice setting it up before you need it.
- **Sleeping bags:** Rated for the season. PNW winter lows can hit the 20s and 30s Fahrenheit. A sleeping bag rated to 20 degrees F for each family member. Add sleeping pads (closed-cell foam or inflatable) for insulation from the ground.
- **Fire-starting:** Waterproof matches, a ferrocerium rod (fire steel), a butane lighter, and fire-starting material (dryer lint, cotton balls with petroleum jelly). Multiple methods, because redundancy saves lives.
- **Warm clothing and rain gear.** This is the Pacific Northwest. It will rain. Hypothermia kills. Layer system: base layer (wool or synthetic, never cotton), insulating layer (fleece or down), waterproof shell. Wool socks. Work boots or sturdy shoes. Gloves. Hat.

## Tools: Functional After the Shaking Stops

- **Gas shutoff wrench.** A crescent wrench or a purpose-built gas shutoff wrench stored next to your gas meter. If you smell gas after an earthquake, shut off the valve. Do not turn it back on --- only the gas company restores gas service. Know where your meter is. Practice the quarter-turn shutoff now.
- **Pry bar.** A 24-inch to 36-inch flat pry bar. For opening jammed doors, lifting debris, and basic rescue operations. Light enough to carry, strong enough to be useful.
- **Work gloves.** Heavy-duty leather or synthetic work gloves. After an earthquake, every surface is a hazard: broken glass, nails, splintered wood, sharp metal. You cannot help anyone if your hands are shredded.
- **Headlamp and flashlights.** LED headlamp (hands-free) with spare batteries. At least one handheld flashlight. Spare batteries for everything. When the power is out, darkness is absolute, and it lasts twelve hours every night.
- **Multi-tool and knife.** A quality multi-tool (Leatherman, Gerber) handles a hundred small tasks. A fixed-blade knife handles the bigger ones.
- **Duct tape, zip ties, plastic sheeting.** Repair tarps, seal broken windows, improvise splints, secure debris. The universal repair kit.
- **Fire extinguisher.** A 5-pound ABC-rated extinguisher. After earthquakes, fires start from ruptured gas lines, overturned stoves, downed power lines. A small fire that could be extinguished in minutes becomes a structure fire when the fire department is responding to a hundred other calls simultaneously.
- **Sturdy shoes by your bed.** When the earthquake hits at 3 AM, the first steps you take will be on broken glass. Keep shoes --- real shoes, not slippers --- beside your bed.

## CERT: Community Emergency Response Team

CERT training is free. It is offered through most fire departments and emergency management agencies in the PNW. It is one of the most valuable things you can do for yourself and your community.

The CERT curriculum covers:

- **Disaster preparedness:** The hazards in your area, how to prepare your household, how to develop a neighborhood plan.
- **Fire suppression:** Using a fire extinguisher on small fires. Not structural firefighting --- basic suppression that prevents a small fire from becoming a big one.
- **Light search and rescue:** Systematic building search, cribbing and leverage for debris removal, victim extrication. The techniques are basic but effective for the residential construction that dominates PNW neighborhoods.
- **Medical triage and first aid:** START triage (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) --- the mass casualty triage system used by fire departments. You learn to categorize patients as immediate, delayed, minor, or dead, and to provide basic first aid with limited supplies.
- **Organization and team management:** How to organize volunteers, communicate with professional responders, and function within the Incident Command System.

A neighborhood with a trained CERT team has residents who can assess structural damage, suppress small fires, perform basic search and rescue, provide first aid, triage casualties, and organize the community's response while waiting for professional help that may be days away. A neighborhood without a CERT team waits.

## Know Your Hazard Zone

### Tsunami Zones

If you live on or visit the outer coast of Washington or Oregon --- from Neah Bay to Brookings --- you are in a tsunami zone. A full Cascadia rupture generates a tsunami that reaches the coast in 15 to 30 minutes.

- **Know your evacuation route.** Tsunami evacuation route signs are posted in coastal communities. Walk the route before you need it. Know which direction leads to high ground and how long it takes on foot.
- **Do not wait for sirens.** If you are on the coast and you feel strong shaking that lasts more than 20 seconds, evacuate to high ground immediately. The earthquake IS the warning. Sirens are a backup that may not function if the power and infrastructure are damaged by the shaking itself.
- **High ground means 50 feet of elevation or higher.** Not the second floor of a building on flat ground. Actual elevation. Know the topography.
- **If you cannot reach high ground,** go as high as you can in the strongest building available. Reinforced concrete is better than wood frame. The third floor is better than the first.

### Lahar Zones (Pierce County)

If you live in the Puyallup, Carbon, or White River valleys --- Orting, Sumner, Puyallup, Auburn, Kent --- you are in a lahar hazard zone. Mount Rainier's volcanic mudflows have filled these valleys before and will again.

- **Know the warning sounds.** Pierce County operates a lahar warning siren system. Know what the sirens sound like (they are distinct from tornado or general emergency sirens). Test alerts are conducted periodically.
- **Know your evacuation route.** Lahar evacuation routes are posted on road signs throughout the valley communities. The route leads uphill, out of the valley floor. Walk it. Drive it. Time it.
- **You have 30 to 45 minutes.** From siren activation to lahar arrival in Orting. Less time the farther downstream you are. This is not a lot of time. Do not pack. Do not wait for confirmation. Move.

### Liquefaction Zones

If you live or work in areas built on fill or alluvial deposits --- SoDo, Harbor Island, Pioneer Square, and the Duwamish corridor in Seattle; the tideflats in Tacoma; areas along the Willamette River in Portland --- the ground beneath you may liquefy during prolonged shaking. Buildings tilt, roads buckle, utilities rupture. Know the risk. Have a plan for getting out of the area on foot if roads are impassable.

## The Preparedness Mindset

The goal is not to be a survivalist. The goal is not to build a bunker. The goal is to be a person who, when the ground stops shaking, can take care of themselves and help their neighbors. The goal is to not be the person who needs rescuing when the fire department is already overwhelmed.

For the person who served thirty years in the Pacific Northwest fire service, community preparedness was not abstract. Every CERT class taught, every public presentation about earthquake readiness, every conversation with a neighbor about supplies and plans was an investment in the capacity of the community to survive the event that every PNW first responder trains for and hopes never comes. The professional response system will arrive, eventually. The question is what happens in the hours and days before it does.

You are the first responder for your family and your neighborhood. The skills you have when the shaking starts are the skills you will have. The supplies you stored are the supplies you have. The plan you made is the plan you execute. Everything you do now --- every gallon of water stored, every tourniquet purchased, every CERT class taken, every family meeting where you discuss the plan --- is an investment in survival.

The Big One is coming. Not maybe. Not probably. Coming. The question is whether you will be ready enough.
