# The PNW Response — Departments and Operations

## Fire From the Beginning

The Pacific Northwest has been shaped by fire since long before European settlement. Indigenous peoples of the region — the Coast Salish, the Yakama, the Chinook, and dozens of other nations — used controlled fire as a land management tool for millennia, maintaining prairies, encouraging food plants, managing forest density, and reducing the fuel loads that produce catastrophic wildfire. The forests and grasslands that European settlers encountered were not untouched wilderness but actively managed landscapes, maintained by people who understood fire as a tool rather than an enemy.

The settlement era brought a different relationship with fire — one defined by density, construction materials, and catastrophic loss. The cities that grew along Puget Sound and the Willamette Valley were built of wood, roofed with wood, heated with wood, and separated by distances measured in feet. When fire came to these cities, it came with devastating totality.

## Seattle Fire Department — Born from the Great Fire

The Seattle Fire Department traces its institutional origins to June 6, 1889 — the day the city burned. The Great Seattle Fire began in a cabinet shop at Front Street (now First Avenue) and Madison Street when a pot of glue boiled over and ignited wood shavings and turpentine. The fire spread through the wooden buildings of the city's commercial district, jumping from block to block. By the time it was over, approximately 25 blocks — 64 acres — of the city center had been destroyed. The loss was estimated at $20 million in 1889 dollars, roughly $600 million today.

Seattle's fire protection before the Great Fire was a volunteer department with inadequate equipment, insufficient water supply, and no coordination worthy of the name. The hydrant system drew from a low-pressure water main that could not supply multiple hose lines simultaneously. When the first companies arrived at the cabinet shop fire, the water pressure was insufficient to suppress even the initial blaze. The fire outran every attempt to contain it.

The Great Fire was Seattle's founding catastrophe — the event that forced the city to rebuild, both physically and institutionally. The rebuilt city mandated brick and stone construction in the commercial district. A professional, paid fire department was established, replacing the volunteer companies. A new water supply system with higher pressure and greater capacity was constructed. The fire department that emerged from the ashes of 1889 was designed to ensure that the catastrophe would not be repeated.

Today, the Seattle Fire Department operates 33 fire stations, employs approximately 1,100 uniformed personnel, and responds to roughly 100,000 calls per year. The department runs a fire-based EMS system — all firefighters are trained to at least the EMT level, and a significant number hold Paramedic certification. The department operates both BLS aid units and ALS medic units, providing tiered EMS response: an aid unit (engine company staffed with EMTs) is dispatched to lower-acuity calls, while a medic unit (staffed with Paramedics) is dispatched to higher-acuity calls or dispatched in addition to the aid unit when ALS care is needed.

Seattle Fire's EMS system is deeply integrated with the Harborview Medical Center trauma and burn system — Harborview is the only Level I trauma center in the region and serves as the receiving facility for the most critical patients across a multi-state area. The relationship between the fire department and Harborview, built over decades, is one of the tightest prehospital-to-hospital care linkages in the country.

The department's marine operations include two fireboats — vessels that provide fire suppression on the waterfront and on the waters of Puget Sound and Lake Union. Seattle's extensive waterfront, floating home communities, and maritime industry create a demand for marine firefighting and rescue capability that most departments do not face.

## King County Medic One — The Gold Standard

King County Medic One is not a department. It is a system — and it is, by every measurable standard, the best emergency cardiac care system in the United States.

The King County EMS system operates on a two-tier model. First-tier response is provided by local fire departments (Seattle Fire, Bellevue Fire, Redmond Fire, and the many fire districts across the county) — firefighter/EMTs who provide BLS care. Second-tier response is provided by Medic One paramedic units operated by several agencies, with the King County Medic One program (based at Harborview Medical Center and the University of Washington) providing the medical direction, training, and quality assurance for the entire system.

The system's cardiac arrest survival rate is the highest in the nation and among the highest in the world. The most recent published data shows that approximately 62 percent of witnessed ventricular fibrillation cardiac arrest patients in the King County system survive to hospital discharge with good neurological function. The national average is approximately 10 to 12 percent. The disparity is not subtle. It is a sixfold difference in survival for the single most time-critical emergency in medicine.

The reasons for King County's cardiac arrest performance are systemic, not individual. The system was designed by Dr. Leonard Cobb, who established the Medic One program in 1970 — one of the earliest paramedic programs in the country. The design principles that Cobb established persist:

Intensive paramedic training. King County Medic One paramedics complete a ten-month training program — significantly longer than the national average — that is conducted through the University of Washington School of Medicine. The academic rigor is comparable to physician assistant training.

Aggressive bystander CPR training. King County has invested decades in teaching CPR to the general public. Bystander CPR rates in King County exceed 60 percent — roughly double the national average. When someone collapses in King County, the probability that someone nearby will immediately start chest compressions is higher than anywhere else in the country.

Rapid defibrillation. The time from collapse to first defibrillation is the single most important determinant of cardiac arrest survival. King County has placed AEDs in public locations, trained first responders to defibrillate immediately, and optimized the dispatch-to-arrival interval to minimize defibrillation delay.

Continuous quality improvement. Every cardiac arrest in the King County system is reviewed. Every resuscitation effort is analyzed. Performance metrics are tracked, feedback is provided, and protocols are updated based on outcome data. The system learns from every case.

The King County model demonstrates that EMS system design determines patient outcomes. The paramedics in King County are excellent, but they are excellent because they operate within a system designed to make excellence possible. The system — the training, the protocols, the quality assurance, the public education, the AED deployment, the dispatch optimization — is what saves lives. Individual heroism helps. Systemic design is what scales.

## Portland Fire and Rescue

Portland Fire and Rescue (PF&R) serves the city of Portland, Oregon, operating 31 fire stations with approximately 700 uniformed personnel. Like Seattle, Portland runs a fire-based EMS system, with firefighter/paramedics providing both fire suppression and emergency medical services. PF&R responds to approximately 85,000 calls annually.

Portland's fire history parallels Seattle's in some respects — the early city was built of wood, and several significant fires drove the development of professional fire protection. The Portland Fire Department was established in 1883, transitioning from volunteer companies to a professional organization.

Portland Fire's contemporary challenges include the city's housing crisis and its impact on fire and EMS operations. The large population of unhoused individuals in Portland generates a significant volume of EMS calls — medical emergencies in encampments, mental health crises, overdoses — and the fire department is the primary response agency for these calls. The strain on resources is considerable, and Portland has been at the forefront of developing alternative response models, including the Portland Street Response program described in the mental health document.

The city's geographic setting creates specific operational challenges. The Willamette River divides the city, and Portland Fire maintains river rescue capability. The city's proximity to the Cascades creates wildland-urban interface (WUI) zones on the eastern edges of the metro area. And Portland's aging infrastructure — unreinforced masonry buildings, particularly in the central city — creates a seismic vulnerability that the department must plan for.

## Tacoma Fire Department

Tacoma Fire Department (TFD) serves the city of Tacoma, the third-largest city in Washington. TFD operates 14 fire stations with approximately 400 uniformed personnel and responds to roughly 45,000 calls per year. Like Seattle and Portland, TFD runs fire-based EMS with ALS capability.

Tacoma's operational environment includes the Port of Tacoma — one of the largest container ports on the West Coast — which creates demand for hazmat response capability, industrial fire protection, and maritime rescue. The Tacoma Narrows, the industrial tideflats, and the proximity to Joint Base Lewis-McChord all shape the department's operational planning.

TFD is part of the Pierce County EMS system, which uses a two-tier model similar to King County. Medic One units, operated by various agencies including TFD, provide ALS care, while engine companies provide BLS first response. The system is modeled on the King County framework, reflecting the regional influence of the Medic One concept.

## Snohomish County — The Fire District Model

Snohomish County, north of King County, illustrates the fire district model that serves the majority of Washington state's population outside of incorporated cities. Fire protection in unincorporated Snohomish County is provided by fire protection districts — independent special-purpose taxing districts, governed by elected commissioners, funded by property tax levies.

South Snohomish County Fire and Rescue (SSCFR, formerly Fire District 7), Snohomish County Fire District 1, Marysville Fire, Arlington Fire, and numerous smaller districts provide fire and EMS coverage across the county. These districts range in size from large, full-service organizations with dozens of stations and hundreds of personnel to small, rural districts with a single station and a handful of volunteers.

The fire district model is distinctly Western — it emerged from the need to provide fire protection in unincorporated areas where no municipal government existed to fund a fire department. The districts are funded by voter-approved levies, which creates a direct democratic relationship between the community and the fire service: the voters decide, at the ballot box, how much fire protection they are willing to pay for.

South Snohomish County provides a specific example. The district covers approximately 60 square miles with a population of roughly 200,000, operating from multiple stations with a combination of career and volunteer personnel. The district responds to a call volume that rivals mid-sized city fire departments, but the funding mechanism and governance structure are fundamentally different from a municipal department.

The EMS system in Snohomish County uses both fire-based and hospital-based models. Medic units are operated by multiple agencies. The medical direction and quality assurance framework is influenced by the King County model but adapted to the county's specific geography and demographics.

## Puget Sound Maritime Rescue

The waters of Puget Sound — the inland sea that defines Western Washington's geography — create a rescue environment that is unique in scale and complexity. The Sound is approximately 100 miles long, up to 930 feet deep, with water temperatures ranging from 45 to 55 degrees Fahrenheit year-round. Immersion in water at these temperatures produces incapacitation within minutes and hypothermic death within an hour.

Maritime rescue in Puget Sound involves multiple agencies: the United States Coast Guard (Sector Puget Sound, based in Seattle, with stations at multiple locations), the Seattle Fire Department's marine operations, county sheriff's offices with marine patrol units, and harbor patrols of various port authorities. The Coast Guard is the primary federal agency for maritime search and rescue, operating cutters, patrol boats, and helicopters from bases throughout the Sound.

The rescue challenges include vessel fires (both commercial and recreational), capsizings, collisions, medical emergencies aboard vessels, and overboard situations. The Sound's heavy commercial traffic — container ships, tankers, ferries, tugs with barges — creates collision risk and the potential for large-scale maritime incidents. The Washington State Ferries system, the largest ferry system in the United States, operates routes throughout the Sound and requires marine emergency planning that accounts for vessels carrying up to 2,500 passengers.

## Wildland Fire in the Cascades

The Cascade Range defines the eastern boundary of the Pacific Northwest's maritime climate zone. On the western slopes, dense coniferous forests — Douglas fir, western red cedar, western hemlock — create enormous fuel loads. On the eastern slopes, drier ponderosa pine forests and grasslands create a different fire regime but equally significant fire risk.

Washington and Oregon have experienced devastating wildfire seasons in recent years. The 2020 Labor Day fires in Oregon burned over one million acres in a single week, destroyed thousands of structures, killed nine people, and forced mass evacuations across the Willamette Valley. The smoke from those fires created hazardous air quality conditions throughout the Pacific Northwest for weeks.

Wildland fire suppression in the Pacific Northwest involves a complex multi-agency coordination structure. The Washington Department of Natural Resources (DNR), the Oregon Department of Forestry (ODF), the United States Forest Service (USFS), the Bureau of Land Management (BLM), and local fire departments all play roles. The Northwest Interagency Coordination Center (NWCC), located in Portland, coordinates wildfire response across the region. Washington's Mobilization Plan enables the statewide deployment of local fire department resources to wildland fires, converting municipal firefighters into wildland responders when the need arises.

The wildland-urban interface (WUI) — the zone where development meets undeveloped wildland — is expanding throughout the Pacific Northwest as population growth pushes housing into forested areas. WUI fires present the most complex firefighting challenge: wildland fire behavior combined with structure protection, evacuation management, and the infrastructure limitations of rural roads and water systems.

## The Cascadia Subduction Zone

The Cascadia Subduction Zone (CSZ) is the 600-mile fault line running from northern California to British Columbia where the Juan de Fuca tectonic plate subducts beneath the North American plate. The last full rupture of the Cascadia Subduction Zone occurred on January 26, 1700, producing a magnitude 9.0 earthquake and a tsunami that devastated the Pacific Northwest coast and was recorded in Japanese historical documents as an orphan tsunami.

The CSZ will rupture again. The geological record shows that full ruptures occur approximately every 200 to 600 years, with the last event 326 years ago. A full CSZ rupture would produce a magnitude 8.7 to 9.2 earthquake lasting three to five minutes — an order of magnitude longer than the 1994 Northridge earthquake and comparable to the 2011 Tohoku earthquake in Japan. The shaking would damage or destroy a significant percentage of the built environment in western Washington and Oregon, particularly unreinforced masonry buildings, older wood-frame structures, bridges, overpasses, and port infrastructure.

The tsunami generated by a CSZ rupture would reach the outer coast within 15 to 30 minutes, with wave heights estimated at 20 to 100 feet depending on location. Coastal communities would have minimal warning. Inland areas — Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca — would experience lower but still significant tsunami effects.

For the fire service, a CSZ event represents the maximum credible emergency. The earthquake damage alone would generate more structural collapse, fire ignition, and mass casualty events than all available resources could address simultaneously. The tsunami would devastate coastal communities. Infrastructure damage — collapsed bridges, broken water mains, disrupted power, impassable roads — would impede response. Communications systems would be degraded or destroyed. Mutual aid from outside the affected region would be delayed by days as damage assessment and logistics caught up with need.

Emergency management agencies throughout the Pacific Northwest plan for the CSZ scenario. The Oregon Resilience Plan and Washington's Cascadia Rising exercise series have identified the gaps and attempted to build capacity. But the honest assessment of every emergency management professional in the region is the same: the first 72 hours after a full CSZ rupture will exceed the capacity of all available resources. Communities will be on their own.

## Mount Rainier — The Lahar Threat

Mount Rainier, at 14,411 feet the highest peak in the Cascade Range, is an active stratovolcano that presents a specific and catastrophic hazard: lahars. A lahar is a volcanic mudflow — a mixture of water, rock, and volcanic debris that flows down river valleys at speeds of 30 to 50 miles per hour, filling valleys to depths of tens of feet and destroying everything in its path.

Mount Rainier has produced lahars repeatedly throughout its geological history. The Osceola Mudflow, approximately 5,600 years ago, traveled over 70 miles from the summit to Puget Sound, filling the valleys of the White and Green rivers to depths of hundreds of feet. The valley floor on which the cities of Enumclaw, Auburn, Kent, and portions of the southern Seattle metropolitan area are built is the Osceola Mudflow deposit.

A recurrence of a major lahar — whether triggered by volcanic activity, glacial outburst, or structural collapse of the summit — would send a wall of debris down the Puyallup, Carbon, White, Nisqually, or Cowlitz river valleys with less than an hour's warning for downstream communities. The lahar detection system (Acoustic Flow Monitors operated by the USGS) provides automated warnings when a lahar is detected, but the warning time for communities in the valleys is measured in tens of minutes. Evacuation planning — pre-identified routes, public education about lahar warning signs, and the "lahar zone" markers posted throughout the valleys — is the primary mitigation strategy.

For fire departments and EMS agencies in the lahar hazard zones — Pierce County, southern King County, Lewis County — lahar planning is an ongoing operational requirement. The scenario involves mass evacuation, mass casualty, infrastructure destruction, and a debris field that may make vehicle access impossible. It is, like the CSZ scenario, a maximum credible event that exceeds available resources.

## Mutual Aid and the Regional Network

The fire service in the Pacific Northwest operates as a regional network connected by mutual aid agreements at the local, county, state, and interstate levels. Washington's mutual aid system is formalized through the Washington State Fire Defense Committee, which organizes the state into regions and defines the mutual aid response protocols that govern how resources move between jurisdictions.

The system is tested regularly — every major wildfire season, every significant structure fire that exceeds local capacity, every mass casualty incident. The 2001 Nisqually earthquake, the 2014 Oso landslide, the 2020 wildfire season, and the COVID-19 pandemic all activated mutual aid at scale. The system works because it is exercised. The departments know each other, they have trained together, they have operated under unified command together, and they trust each other.

The Washington State Patrol provides law enforcement on state highways and supports local agencies through mutual aid, criminal investigation assistance, and forensic laboratory services. WSP's role in major incidents — particularly traffic accidents on the extensive interstate and highway system — makes it a constant partner of local fire and EMS agencies.

For the person who served thirty years in this region, the mutual aid network was not an abstraction. It was the neighboring engine company that arrived on the second alarm. It was the medic unit from the next district that covered the station when the local unit was committed. It was the wildland fire deployment that took them away from home for fourteen days. It was the system that meant no one was alone — that when the call exceeded what one crew could handle, more crews came.
