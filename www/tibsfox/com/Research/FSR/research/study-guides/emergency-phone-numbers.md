# Study Guide: Numbers You Should Know --- Emergency Contacts Reference

*Print this. Laminate it. Put it on the refrigerator.*

---

## Why This Document Exists

In an emergency, your phone may be dead, your internet may be down, and your ability to think clearly may be compromised by adrenaline, fear, or injury. The numbers on this page are the ones you need when everything else has failed. Some of them you already know (911). Some of them you will need once in your life and will be profoundly grateful you have them (Poison Control). Some of them you may never need personally but should know exist so you can share them with someone who does (the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline).

This is not a comprehensive directory of every emergency service in the Pacific Northwest. It is a curated reference designed to fit on a single laminated sheet --- the essential numbers, organized by category, with brief notes on when to call each one. Print it. Post it. Update the personal sections with your own information. Replace it annually or whenever your household circumstances change.

---

## National Emergency Numbers

These numbers work from any phone, anywhere in the United States, 24 hours a day, 365 days a year.

### 911 --- Police, Fire, Medical Emergency

The universal emergency number. Call 911 when there is an immediate threat to life, property, or safety.

**When to call:**
- Someone is unconscious, not breathing, or having a cardiac event
- A fire (structure fire, wildfire, vehicle fire --- any fire)
- A crime in progress (assault, break-in, robbery, active threat)
- A serious injury (heavy bleeding, head trauma, falls from height, drowning)
- A medical emergency (stroke symptoms, severe allergic reaction, poisoning, choking)
- A motor vehicle accident with injuries
- A hazardous materials spill or gas leak

**What to know:** When you call 911, stay on the line. The dispatcher will ask your location first, then the nature of the emergency, then details. If you cannot speak (you are hiding from an intruder, you are choking), the call is still traced --- dispatchers are trained to send help to silent 911 calls. On a cell phone, 911 routing uses GPS/cell tower location, but always state your address verbally if you can --- GPS accuracy varies from 10 to 300 meters.

**Teaching children:** Children old enough to use a phone should know that 911 is for emergencies only, that they should tell the operator their address, and that it is never wrong to call 911 if they are scared and there is no adult to help. Practice the address. Quiz them. Make it a game. A child who can recite their home address and dial 911 has the two most important safety skills a young person can possess.

### 988 --- Suicide and Crisis Lifeline

Launched nationally on July 16, 2022, replacing the previous 10-digit number (1-800-273-8255). Call or text 988 for yourself or someone you are worried about.

**When to call:**
- You or someone you know is thinking about suicide
- You are in emotional distress and need to talk to someone immediately
- Someone near you is in a mental health crisis (severe agitation, psychosis, self-harm)
- You need help de-escalating a situation that feels dangerous but is not yet a law enforcement matter

**What to know:** The 988 system connects to a network of 200+ local and state-funded crisis centers. Calls are answered by trained counselors, not police dispatchers. Calling 988 does not automatically trigger law enforcement response --- it is a mental health resource, not a policing tool. For the deaf and hard of hearing, 988 supports TTY (dial 711 then 988). Veterans can press 1 after dialing 988 for the Veterans Crisis Line. Spanish-language service is available by pressing 2.

**The critical point:** There is no wrong time to call 988. You do not need to be "suicidal enough." If you are struggling, the line is there. If you are worried about a friend, the line is there. If you are not sure whether the situation is serious, call anyway --- the counselors will help you figure it out.

### 211 --- Social Services and Community Resources

A nationwide information and referral service that connects callers to local social services.

**When to call:**
- You need help paying rent, utilities, or food costs
- You need to find a food bank, shelter, or clothing donation center
- You need information about healthcare access, prescription assistance, or mental health services
- You are new to an area and need to find local resources
- You need help after a disaster (disaster relief programs, FEMA applications, temporary housing)

**What to know:** 211 is not an emergency line. It is a navigation tool for the social safety net. Operators are trained to listen to your situation and connect you to the specific programs and agencies that can help. Available 24/7 in most areas. Multilingual services available.

### 311 --- Non-Emergency City Services

Available in most major cities, 311 is the number for municipal services that are not emergencies.

**When to call:**
- Pothole, downed tree, or road hazard
- Noise complaint
- Abandoned vehicle
- Graffiti
- Street light outage
- Non-emergency animal control (stray dog, not aggressive)
- Missed garbage or recycling pickup
- Code violation report

**What to know:** 311 availability varies by jurisdiction. Seattle, Portland, and most larger PNW cities support 311. Smaller cities and unincorporated areas may not --- check your local non-emergency police number as an alternative.

### 1-800-222-1222 --- Poison Control (National)

The American Association of Poison Control Centers. One number connects to your regional poison center, 24 hours a day.

**When to call:**
- A child has ingested a medication, cleaning product, plant, or unknown substance
- An adult has taken too much of a medication (intentional or accidental)
- Someone has been exposed to a chemical (skin, eyes, or inhalation)
- A pet has ingested something toxic (they can advise on animal exposures too)
- You are unsure whether an exposure is dangerous --- call anyway

**What to know:** Poison Control is staffed by toxicologists, pharmacists, and nurses with specialized training. They can tell you whether a specific exposure is dangerous, what to do immediately, and whether emergency room treatment is needed. In many cases, they can manage the situation over the phone, avoiding an unnecessary ER visit. Save this number in your phone under "Poison Control" right now. You will not remember it when your toddler is holding an open bottle of something.

## Crisis and Support Lines

### National Domestic Violence Hotline: 1-800-799-7233

24/7. Bilingual (English/Spanish). Over 200 languages through interpretation services. Chat available at thehotline.org. Text START to 88788.

**When to call:** You or someone you know is experiencing domestic violence (physical, emotional, sexual, financial). You need safety planning, legal information, or referral to local shelters. You are not sure if what you are experiencing is abuse --- call and talk about it. There is no wrong time to call.

**Safety note:** If you are in an abusive household and calling this number could put you at risk, use the chat function from a private browser window or a device your abuser does not monitor. The website has a quick-exit button.

### SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357

Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration. 24/7, 365 days a year. Free, confidential, available in English and Spanish.

**When to call:** You or someone you know is struggling with substance use or mental health. You need information about treatment facilities, support groups, or community-based organizations. You need a referral to a local treatment provider.

### Safe Call Now: 206-459-3020

A crisis line specifically for first responders and their family members. 24/7.

**When to call:** You are a firefighter, paramedic, police officer, dispatcher, or military member experiencing a crisis. You are the family member of a first responder and need support. The counselors on this line understand the unique stressors and culture of first responder life in a way that general crisis lines may not.

**Why it matters:** First responders experience PTSD, depression, substance abuse, and suicidal ideation at rates significantly higher than the general population. The cultural barriers to seeking help --- the stigma, the fear of career consequences, the ethic of self-sufficiency --- are real and documented. Safe Call Now exists to lower those barriers. See [FSR-09: Responder Wellness](../09-responder-wellness.md) for the full context.

### Crisis Connections: 1-866-427-4747

King County, Washington's crisis line. 24/7. Serves King County residents with mental health crisis support, including mobile crisis teams that can come to your location.

**When to call:** You are in King County (Seattle, Bellevue, Renton, Kent, Federal Way, and surrounding areas) and need mental health crisis support. You need a mobile crisis team rather than police. You are calling for a loved one in crisis.

## Wildlife Emergency Numbers

### WDFW (Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife): 360-902-2200

The state agency responsible for all wildlife management in Washington. The WDFW enforcement hotline for poaching, injured wildlife, and nuisance wildlife is available 24/7.

**When to call:**
- You have found an injured wild animal (deer, eagle, owl, seal, etc.)
- You have witnessed poaching or illegal hunting/fishing
- You have encountered a nuisance wildlife situation (bear in yard, cougar near school, aggressive coyote)
- You need information about wildlife regulations or permits

**For injured wildlife specifically:** WDFW will triage and direct you to the nearest licensed wildlife rehabilitator. Do not attempt to transport or treat injured wildlife yourself unless directed to do so.

### PAWS Wildlife Center: 425-412-4040

Progressive Animal Welfare Society, Lynnwood, Washington. One of the largest wildlife rehabilitation facilities in the Pacific Northwest.

**When to call:**
- You have found an injured or orphaned wild animal in the greater Seattle/King County/Snohomish County area
- You have found a baby bird, fawn, or other young animal that may or may not be orphaned (PAWS staff will help you determine whether the animal needs rescue or should be left alone)
- You need advice on coexisting with wildlife (raccoons in attic, birds hitting windows, etc.)

**Important:** Call before bringing an animal in. PAWS triage over the phone determines whether the animal needs help, what kind of help, and whether PAWS is the right facility. Many "orphaned" baby animals are not orphaned --- the parent is nearby and will return. PAWS counselors know the difference.

### Portland Audubon Wildlife Care Center: 503-292-0304

Portland, Oregon's primary wildlife rehabilitation facility. Located in Forest Park.

**When to call:**
- You have found an injured or orphaned wild animal in the Portland metro area or northwestern Oregon
- You need advice on a bird-related issue (window strikes, nest disturbance, injured raptors)
- You have found a marine bird (loon, grebe, pelican) in distress

## Utility Emergency Numbers

### Puget Sound Energy (PSE): 1-888-225-5773

The primary electric and natural gas utility for much of western Washington outside Seattle city limits.

**When to call:**
- Downed power line (also call 911 --- downed lines are life-threatening)
- Power outage (report and check restoration estimates)
- Natural gas smell (leave the area immediately, then call from a safe distance)
- Damaged meter or service line

**Gas leak protocol:** If you smell natural gas (rotten egg odor), do NOT use light switches, phones (landline), or anything that could create a spark. Leave the building. Call PSE and 911 from outside or from a neighbor's location.

### Seattle City Light: 206-684-3000

Seattle's municipal electric utility.

**When to call:**
- Power outage within Seattle city limits
- Downed power line (also call 911)
- Damaged electrical equipment
- Street light outage (also reportable through the Find It Fix It app)

## When to Call Which Number

The most common confusion in an emergency is knowing which resource to contact. This decision tree covers the most frequent scenarios.

**Is someone in immediate physical danger?** (Injury, fire, crime in progress, medical emergency)
- **Yes** → Call **911**

**Is someone in a mental health crisis but not in immediate physical danger?**
- **Yes** → Call **988** (Suicide and Crisis Lifeline) or **Crisis Connections** (866-427-4747 in King County)
- **If the person is a first responder** → Call **Safe Call Now** (206-459-3020)

**Has someone ingested a potentially toxic substance?**
- **If they are unconscious, seizing, or not breathing** → Call **911**
- **If they are awake and responsive** → Call **Poison Control** (1-800-222-1222)

**Do you need social services?** (Food, housing, utilities, healthcare access)
- **Yes** → Call **211**

**Is there a non-emergency city services issue?** (Pothole, noise, graffiti, stray animal)
- **Yes** → Call **311** (or your local non-emergency number)

**Is there a wildlife emergency?** (Injured animal, poaching, dangerous wildlife encounter)
- **Yes** → Call **WDFW** (360-902-2200) or your nearest wildlife rehabilitation center

**Is there a utility emergency?** (Gas leak, downed power line, outage)
- **Gas leak or downed line** → Call **911** AND your utility company
- **Power outage** → Call your utility company

## Maintaining This Document

**Annual review:** Once a year (pick a date --- January 1, your birthday, the anniversary of the last earthquake), review this sheet. Update phone numbers if any have changed. Update household medical information. Check that your ICE contacts are still current. Replace the laminated sheet if it has faded.

**After a household change:** New baby, new housemate, new pet, new school, new job, new address --- update the personal sections. Print a new copy.

**For multiple locations:** If you split time between two homes, keep a copy at each. If you have a workplace where this information would be useful (and you are in a position to post it), make a copy for the break room.

**Digital backup:** Take a photograph of the completed sheet and store it in your phone's photos or a notes app. The laminated sheet is for the wall; the digital copy is for when you are not home.

> **[Create a Family Emergency Plan](../try-sessions/family-emergency-plan.md)** --- This reference sheet is the phone number component of a larger preparedness framework. > **[Build a 72-Hour Go Bag](../try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md)** --- Print this sheet, laminate it, and put a copy inside your go bag. > **[Hands-Only CPR](../try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md)** --- Knowing which number to call is the first step. Knowing what to do while you wait for help to arrive is the second. Hands-only CPR takes 10 minutes to learn and doubles or triples cardiac arrest survival rates.

> **[Stop the Bleed](../try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md)** --- The companion skill to calling 911 for traumatic injuries. While the dispatcher sends help, you can apply direct pressure, pack a wound, or apply a tourniquet --- skills that save lives in the minutes before paramedics arrive.

CERT volunteers are trained in light search and rescue, fire suppression, triage, and team organization.

## College of Knowledge Connections

- **Learning** --- [Information Organization, Reference Design]: This study guide is itself a lesson in information architecture. The numbers are organized by category (national, crisis, wildlife, utility), prioritized by severity (911 first, 311 last), annotated with context (when to call each number, what to expect), and designed for rapid retrieval under stress. The student who creates their own version --- customized with local numbers, household information, and personal contacts --- practices the same information design skills used in technical writing, UX design, and systems documentation.
- **Home Economics** --- [Household Preparedness, Risk Management]: Maintaining a current emergency contact sheet is a foundational household management skill. Like maintaining insurance, stocking a first aid kit, and knowing where your utility shutoffs are, it is a small investment of time that pays off enormously in the specific, rare, high-stakes moments when it is needed. The annual review cycle teaches maintenance discipline --- the practice of periodically auditing and updating critical household systems.
- **Physical Education** --- [Emergency Response, First Aid]: The phone numbers on this sheet are the beginning of the response chain, not the end. Knowing which number to call is necessary but not sufficient. The related try sessions (CPR, Stop the Bleed, CERT) provide the skills that fill the gap between calling for help and help arriving. The study guide and the skill sessions form a complete first-responder capability at the citizen level.
