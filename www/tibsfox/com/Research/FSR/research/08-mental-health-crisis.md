# The Gap — Mental Health Crisis Response

## The Wrong Tools for the Job

The call comes in at 2:47 AM. A woman is standing on an overpass, crying, one hand on the railing. Someone driving past saw her and dialed 911. The dispatcher codes it as a possible suicide attempt, which means law enforcement response. Within minutes, two patrol cars arrive, lights flashing, officers emerging with duty belts that carry a firearm, a taser, handcuffs, pepper spray, and a radio. They have body cameras, ballistic vests, and years of academy training in defensive tactics, pursuit driving, criminal law, and firearms qualification.

What they do not have is a degree in psychology, a background in crisis counseling, any meaningful clinical understanding of suicidal ideation, or the institutional authority to connect this woman to the services that might actually keep her alive past sunrise. They have minutes to assess a situation that clinicians spend hours evaluating. They have tools designed for compliance and control in a moment that requires empathy, patience, and therapeutic skill. They have been sent to do a job that is not their job, with equipment that is wrong for the task, because the system that should be handling this call does not exist in most American communities.

This is the gap. Not a crack in the system — a canyon. When someone is in a mental health crisis, we send people with guns, hoses, and defibrillators. Not therapists. Not social workers. Not peers who have been there. We send the people we have, not the people we need, because we never built the infrastructure to send anyone else.

The consequences of this gap are measured in dead bodies, broken trust, burned-out responders, and a revolving door of suffering that consumes billions of dollars while healing almost no one.

## What the Call Looks Like

To understand the gap, you have to understand the call. Not the sanitized version in policy papers, but the version that plays out on streets and in living rooms and in the back of ambulances thousands of times every night across this country.

A man is off his psychiatric medications. Maybe he lost his insurance. Maybe the side effects were unbearable — the weight gain, the cognitive fog, the sexual dysfunction that his prescriber never warned him about. Maybe he ran out and the pharmacy won't refill without an appointment, and the next available appointment is six weeks away. Whatever the reason, he has been unmedicated for two weeks, and the psychosis is back. He is in his apartment, talking to people who are not there, pacing, agitated, frightened by perceptions that are absolutely real to him even though they correspond to nothing in the physical world.

His neighbor hears the shouting and calls 911. The dispatcher asks if there are weapons. The neighbor does not know. The call is coded accordingly — unknown weapons, emotionally disturbed person, potential for violence. Officers are dispatched. They may have Crisis Intervention Team training. They may not. In many departments, CIT training is voluntary, and in many of those departments, the officers who volunteer for it are already the ones most inclined toward de-escalation. The ones who need it most often do not take it.

The officers arrive. They knock on the door, announce themselves as police. For a man in the grip of psychosis, the arrival of armed strangers at his door is not reassurance — it is confirmation of the threat his disordered mind has been warning him about. His fear escalates. The officers' concern escalates in response. They are trained to read behavioral cues as indicators of threat level. Pacing, refusal to comply with commands, erratic movement, failure to show hands — these are threat indicators in a law enforcement framework. In a clinical framework, they are symptoms. The same behavior means entirely different things depending on who is interpreting it.

What happens next depends on a hundred variables — the officers' training, their temperament, their fatigue level, the department's use-of-force policy, the man's specific behavior in the next thirty seconds. Sometimes it ends with a calm conversation, a voluntary transport to the emergency room, a brief hold, and a discharge at 6 AM with a list of phone numbers the man will never call. Sometimes it ends with a taser. Sometimes it ends with a knee on a back and handcuffs on wrists. Sometimes it ends with a gunshot.

And sometimes — more often than any of us want to admit — it ends with death.

## The Numbers That Should Haunt Us

Approximately 20 percent of fatal police shootings in the United States involve a person experiencing a mental health crisis. One in four people killed by law enforcement has a mental health condition. These are not disputed statistics. They appear in data compiled by the Washington Post, the Treatment Advocacy Center, the National Alliance on Mental Illness, and the Department of Justice. The numbers vary slightly by source and methodology, but the range is consistent and the conclusion is inescapable: a significant proportion of people killed by police in this country are not criminals. They are patients.

Meanwhile, police officers spend an estimated 10 to 20 percent of their time responding to calls involving mental health issues. In some urban departments, the percentage is higher. These are calls that officers are not adequately trained for, that consume enormous amounts of patrol time, that rarely result in meaningful resolution, and that carry disproportionate risk of violence for both the person in crisis and the responding officer. Police executives have been saying for decades that their officers should not be the primary responders to mental health emergencies. Chiefs and sheriffs across the political spectrum agree on this point. The disagreement is not about whether the current system is broken — it is about what to build in its place, and who will fund it.

The broader mental health system provides the backdrop to these encounters. The United States has approximately 37,000 psychiatric beds for a population of 340 million — roughly 11 beds per 100,000 people. In 1955, the ratio was 340 beds per 100,000. The deinstitutionalization movement that began in the 1960s, driven by genuine and justified horror at conditions in state psychiatric hospitals, was supposed to replace those institutions with community-based mental health services. The institutions closed. The community services were never adequately funded. The result is that jails and prisons have become the largest providers of mental health care in the country. The Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail in Chicago, and Rikers Island in New York each hold more people with mental illness than any psychiatric hospital in their respective states.

This is the infrastructure — or the absence of infrastructure — that produces the 2:47 AM call.

## The Memphis Model and the Limits of Better Training

On September 24, 1987, Memphis police officers shot and killed Joseph DeWayne Robinson, a 27-year-old Black man who was experiencing a mental health crisis. Robinson was armed with a knife and had been threatening people in a public housing complex. Officers arrived, the situation escalated, and Robinson was shot multiple times and killed.

The community response was immediate and sustained. Rather than the cycle of outrage and forgetting that typically follows such shootings, Memphis became the site of a genuine institutional innovation. In 1988, the Memphis Police Department, in partnership with the Memphis chapter of the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) and the University of Memphis, developed the Crisis Intervention Team (CIT) model — a 40-hour training program for volunteer officers, designed to give them the skills to de-escalate encounters with people in mental health crisis.

The CIT model, often called the Memphis Model, has since been adopted by over 2,700 communities across the United States and by police departments in multiple countries. The 40-hour training covers mental health diagnoses, de-escalation techniques, community mental health resources, site visits to treatment facilities, and interactions with people who have lived experience of mental illness. Officers who complete CIT training are designated as crisis intervention officers and are dispatched preferentially to mental health calls.

The evidence suggests that CIT works, within its limitations. Studies have found that CIT-trained officers use force less frequently during mental health calls, make more appropriate referrals to treatment rather than arrest, and report higher confidence in their ability to handle these encounters. A 2019 systematic review published in Psychiatric Services found consistent evidence that CIT reduces arrests of people with mental illness and increases diversions to mental health services.

But CIT is, by design, a reform within the existing system. It makes police officers better at handling mental health calls. It does not change the fundamental equation: an armed law enforcement officer is still the primary responder. The person in crisis still sees a uniform, a badge, and a gun. The power dynamic is still one of authority and potential coercion, not care and therapeutic relationship. CIT-trained officers are still police officers, operating under use-of-force policies, carrying the legal authority to detain, and working within a culture that prioritizes officer safety above all other considerations.

CIT is better than nothing. In many communities, it is the best available option. But it is not the answer. It is a bridge — a way of reducing harm while the actual answer is built. The tragedy is that in most communities, CIT has become the destination rather than the bridge. Departments implement CIT and consider the mental health response problem solved. It is not solved. It is mitigated. And mitigation, over thirty years and counting, begins to look like permanent acceptance of a broken system.

## CAHOOTS — The Proof of Concept That Has Run for Thirty-Five Years

In 1989, while Memphis was developing CIT, the city of Eugene, Oregon, was trying something more radical. The White Bird Clinic, a community health organization, launched CAHOOTS — Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets. The model was simple in concept and revolutionary in practice: send civilian crisis workers, not police, to mental health and behavioral health calls.

A CAHOOTS team consists of two people: a medic (an EMT or nurse) and a crisis worker (someone with training and experience in behavioral health crisis intervention). They respond in a van stocked with medical supplies and naloxone, not a patrol car with a cage in the back. They carry no weapons. They have no authority to arrest, detain, or compel. They can only help, and the help they offer is voluntary.

CAHOOTS responds to approximately 17 percent of Eugene's total 911 call volume. In 2019, they handled roughly 24,000 calls. Their annual budget is approximately $2.1 million — a fraction of the Eugene Police Department's budget of over $90 million. Of those 24,000 calls, CAHOOTS requested police backup on only about 150 — less than one percent.

The outcomes are difficult to overstate. In more than three decades of operation, CAHOOTS has never had a serious use-of-force incident. They de-escalate through presence, patience, and clinical skill. They provide warm handoffs to services — connecting people with shelter beds, treatment programs, medication management, food, and the slow, unglamorous work of stabilization that actually keeps people alive. An independent analysis estimated that CAHOOTS saves the city approximately $8.5 million annually in avoided emergency room visits, jail bookings, and police overtime.

CAHOOTS is not a pilot program. It is not an experiment. It has been running continuously for more than thirty-five years. It is the longest-running alternative crisis response program in the United States, and it has produced a body of evidence that no honest evaluation can dismiss.

The question that CAHOOTS forces us to ask is not whether civilian crisis response works. It works. CAHOOTS has proven it works, at scale, for decades, at a fraction of the cost of police response, with vastly better outcomes for the people in crisis and dramatically less risk for everyone involved. The question CAHOOTS forces us to ask is: why has it taken so long for the rest of the country to replicate it?

The answer is not about evidence. It is about politics, funding, institutional inertia, police union opposition, and the deeply embedded assumption that public safety requires armed response. CAHOOTS works in Eugene because Eugene built it, funded it, defended it, and refined it for thirty-five years. Most cities have not been willing to make that investment.

## Co-Responder Models — Better, But Still Armed

Between CIT (training police better) and CAHOOTS (replacing police with civilians), there is a middle ground: the co-responder model. In this approach, a mental health clinician is paired with a police officer. They respond together to mental health calls, with the clinician taking the lead on assessment and de-escalation while the officer provides a safety presence.

Co-responder programs have proliferated across the country. They go by different names — Mobile Crisis Teams, Behavioral Health Response Teams, Crisis Co-Response — but the structure is similar. A licensed clinician, often a social worker or licensed counselor, rides with an officer or responds in parallel, and the two work the call together.

The evidence on co-responder models is generally positive. Calls handled by co-responder teams result in fewer arrests, more connections to treatment, and lower use of force compared to officer-only responses. Clinicians can conduct on-scene assessments that officers cannot, identify appropriate placements that officers may not know exist, and provide the kind of therapeutic engagement that can actually de-escalate a crisis rather than merely contain it.

But co-responder models carry an inherent limitation: the police officer is still there. The person in crisis still sees an armed officer. The power dynamic still favors compliance over collaboration. And the program is still tethered to police scheduling, police culture, and police priorities. When calls stack up and patrol is short-staffed, the co-responder unit may be pulled for other duties. When the clinician's assessment conflicts with the officer's threat assessment, the officer's authority typically prevails.

Co-responder programs are better than police-only response. They are also more expensive than civilian-only models like CAHOOTS, because they require both a clinician's salary and an officer's overtime. And they still frame mental health crisis as a law enforcement event with clinical support, rather than a health event that may occasionally require law enforcement support. The framing matters. It determines who leads, who decides, and whose institutional norms govern the encounter.

## 988 — The Right Idea, Underfunded

On July 16, 2022, the United States activated the 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, replacing the old ten-digit National Suicide Prevention Lifeline number (1-800-273-8255) with a three-digit code intended to be as easy to remember as 911. The goal was straightforward and ambitious: create a mental health equivalent of 911, a number that anyone in crisis could call and receive immediate help from trained crisis counselors.

The activation of 988 was a significant policy achievement. It represented federal recognition that mental health crises are health emergencies that deserve their own response infrastructure, separate from law enforcement. The vision was powerful: call 988, reach a counselor, get help without police, without handcuffs, without an involuntary hold.

The reality has been more complicated. The 988 system is administered by SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) and operated through a network of local and regional crisis centers. Funding was and remains inadequate. Many crisis centers were already struggling before 988 launched, and the dramatic increase in call volume that accompanied the new number strained systems that were not built to handle it.

Wait times vary wildly by region. Some callers reach a counselor quickly. Others wait on hold for minutes that feel like hours when you are in crisis. Some calls roll over to a national backup center staffed by people who may not know the local resources available in the caller's community. The system works well in areas with well-funded crisis centers and poorly in areas without them — which is to say, it works well in some urban and suburban areas and poorly in most rural ones.

More fundamentally, 988 is a phone line. It connects people to counselors who can talk them through a crisis. What it does not do, in most areas, is dispatch a mobile team to come to the person. The connection between calling 988 and receiving an in-person response — a crisis worker at your door, a transport to a stabilization center, a warm handoff to ongoing care — is the critical gap. Some communities have built that connection. Most have not. In those communities, 988 is a conversation, not a response. And conversations, however skilled and compassionate, cannot replace physical presence for someone who is actively suicidal, acutely psychotic, or unable to keep themselves safe.

The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act of 2022 provided some funding for 988 infrastructure, and several states have implemented dedicated 988 surcharges on phone bills to fund their crisis systems. But the investment remains far short of what is needed to build a comprehensive crisis response system in every community. The comparison to 911 is instructive: when you call 911, someone comes. An ambulance, a fire truck, a police car. Physical resources are dispatched to your location. When you call 988, in most of the country, someone talks to you on the phone. The gap between those two experiences is the gap between a crisis number and a crisis system.

## The Revolving Door

Ask any paramedic, any patrol officer, any ER nurse what the mental health system looks like from the ground level, and they will tell you about the revolving door. It is the defining feature of the system's failure, the pattern that repeats so reliably that responders can predict it almost to the day.

A person in crisis calls 911, or someone calls on their behalf. First responders arrive. The person is assessed. If they meet criteria for an involuntary psychiatric hold — typically, that they are a danger to themselves or others, or are gravely disabled — they are transported to the nearest emergency room. Not a psychiatric facility. Not a crisis stabilization center. The emergency room, because that is what exists.

At the emergency room, the person is triaged. Mental health patients are not typically high priority in an ER designed for heart attacks, strokes, and trauma. The person may wait in a hallway, on a gurney, for hours. In many hospitals across the country, the average wait time for a psychiatric patient to be seen by a mental health professional exceeds twelve hours. Wait times of 24, 36, even 48 hours are not anomalies — they are routine. This is called "ER boarding," and it is one of the most degrading experiences in American medicine. A person in acute psychological distress, sitting in a bright, noisy, chaotic emergency room, often restrained or sedated, waiting for a bed that does not exist.

If a psychiatric bed is found — and that is a substantial if, given the shortage described earlier — the person is transferred to an inpatient unit. Lengths of stay have plummeted over the decades, driven by insurance reimbursement pressures. The person may be stabilized on medication, observed for a few days, and discharged with a follow-up appointment that is weeks away, at a clinic they may have no transportation to reach, for a condition that requires continuous, not episodic, care.

They return to the street, or the shelter, or the apartment where the crisis began. The medication runs out, or the side effects drive them to stop taking it, or the follow-up appointment falls through. The psychosis returns, or the depression deepens, or the suicidal ideation intensifies. Someone calls 911. The paramedic who responds recognizes the patient. They responded to this same person three weeks ago. And three weeks before that. And the month before that.

This is not an abstraction. There are patients in every American city who generate fifty, a hundred, two hundred 911 calls per year. First responders know them by name. They know their diagnoses, their medications, their living situations, their family members, their triggers. They know these patients better than any clinician in the system, because they see them more often than any clinician does. And they know, with the certainty that comes from repetition, that nothing they do on any individual call will change the trajectory. The system does not heal these people. It moves them. ER to street to crisis to 911 to ER. A loop that consumes enormous resources, delivers almost no therapeutic value, and grinds down everyone caught in it — the patients, the responders, the ER staff, the families.

The paramedic who has responded fifty times to the same person understands something that policymakers often do not: the system is not failing intermittently. It is functioning exactly as designed. It was designed to respond to acute events, not to treat chronic conditions. It was designed to stabilize and transport, not to heal and sustain. When you apply an acute-event system to a chronic-condition population, you get the revolving door. It is not a bug. It is the architecture.

## What Actually Works

The research is clear. We know what works. The evidence base is not thin or ambiguous — it is robust, replicated, and consistent across settings. What is lacking is not knowledge but political will and funding.

Mobile crisis teams staffed by civilians work. CAHOOTS has demonstrated this for thirty-five years. Newer programs — Denver's STAR (Support Team Assisted Response), Portland's Portland Street Response, Olympia's Crisis Response Unit, Albuquerque's ACS (Albuquerque Community Safety), San Francisco's Street Crisis Response Team — are generating similar results. Civilian teams de-escalate safely, connect people to services, and reduce the burden on police and emergency rooms. The evidence is consistent across every program that has been rigorously evaluated.

Crisis stabilization centers work. These are not emergency rooms. They are purpose-built facilities designed specifically for psychiatric crisis — calm environments with low lighting, comfortable furniture, private rooms, and clinical staff trained in behavioral health. The 23-hour observation model allows for assessment, stabilization, medication initiation or adjustment, and discharge planning without the trauma and expense of an ER visit or an inpatient admission. The evidence shows that crisis stabilization centers reduce ER boarding, reduce inpatient admissions, reduce costs, and produce better patient satisfaction than ER-based crisis care. Arizona's crisis system, built around a network of crisis stabilization centers and a centralized crisis line, is frequently cited as the national model.

Peer support specialists work. These are people with lived experience of mental illness or substance use disorder who are trained to support others in crisis and recovery. Peer support is not a substitute for clinical treatment — it is a complement to it, and a powerful one. Peers bring credibility that clinicians cannot: the credibility of having been there. Research consistently shows that peer support reduces hospitalization, improves engagement with treatment, and increases recovery outcomes. Peers also provide something the formal system rarely does — hope. Living proof that recovery is possible.

Permanent supportive housing works. The Housing First model — providing stable housing without preconditions like sobriety or treatment compliance — has been validated by decades of research. The evidence is overwhelming that Housing First reduces homelessness, reduces ER visits, reduces hospitalizations, reduces jail bookings, and costs less than the revolving door of emergency services. A frequently cited figure: a chronically homeless person costs the public system $30,000 to $50,000 per year in emergency services, incarceration, and hospital care. Permanent supportive housing costs $15,000 to $25,000 per year and produces better outcomes on every measure.

Medication continuity programs work. The gap between psychiatric medication and the person who needs it is one of the most fixable problems in the entire system. Long-acting injectable antipsychotics eliminate the daily compliance problem for people with schizophrenia. Prescription assistance programs bridge insurance gaps. Community health workers can deliver medications directly. The pharmacological tools exist. The delivery infrastructure is what is missing.

Medicaid expansion works. States that expanded Medicaid under the Affordable Care Act saw significant increases in access to behavioral health services and significant decreases in uninsured rates among people with mental illness. The states that refused expansion — disproportionately in the South and rural America — are the same states with the worst mental health outcomes and the most overburdened first responder systems. This is not a coincidence.

Co-located mental health in fire stations works. Some forward-thinking fire departments have embedded behavioral health clinicians directly in stations. These clinicians ride along on mental health calls, provide on-scene assessment, and serve as a resource for the firefighters and paramedics themselves. It bridges the gap between emergency response and clinical care at the point of contact. The model is promising and growing.

## The Pacific Northwest — Leading, But Not There Yet

The Pacific Northwest has been at the forefront of alternative crisis response for decades, in part because the CAHOOTS model originated in the region and in part because the progressive political culture of the I-5 corridor has created space for experimentation.

CAHOOTS in Eugene remains the gold standard. It has survived changes in city government, budget pressures, and the national scrutiny that followed the 2020 protests. It continues to handle roughly 17 percent of Eugene's 911 volume with a budget that is a rounding error in the police department's balance sheet. Springfield, Eugene's neighbor, has launched its own version. The model is expanding within Lane County.

Seattle's LEAD program — Law Enforcement Assisted Diversion — takes a different approach. Rather than replacing police response, LEAD allows officers to divert people engaged in low-level offenses (often driven by mental illness or substance use) away from the criminal justice system and into case management and services. LEAD does not eliminate the police encounter, but it changes the outcome from arrest and prosecution to engagement and support. Evaluations have found that LEAD participants are significantly less likely to be re-arrested and more likely to obtain housing and employment than similar individuals processed through the criminal justice system.

The Downtown Emergency Service Center (DESC) in Seattle operates the largest permanent supportive housing program in Washington State and runs crisis services including the Crisis Solutions Center, a crisis stabilization facility that diverts people from jail and emergency rooms. DESC's model integrates housing, clinical treatment, and crisis response into a single continuum — the kind of system-level integration that produces real results.

Crisis Connections, also based in Seattle, operates the regional crisis line and provides training and resources for crisis intervention. They are part of the 988 network and one of the better-funded crisis centers in the country, though even they face the capacity constraints that plague the national system.

Portland Street Response, launched in 2021, sends unarmed teams of community health workers and paramedics to non-emergency 911 calls involving homelessness, mental health, and substance use. Early evaluations have been positive, with high rates of service connection and very low rates of requiring police backup. The program has expanded from a single pilot area to citywide coverage.

Olympia's Crisis Response Unit, operated through the Olympia Fire Department, pairs firefighters with behavioral health professionals to respond to mental health calls. It is a co-responder model, but one led from the fire service rather than the police side — a distinction that changes the dynamics of the encounter.

These programs demonstrate that alternatives are possible, scalable, and effective. They also demonstrate that alternatives require sustained political commitment, dedicated funding, and the willingness to challenge institutional assumptions about who should respond to what. The Pacific Northwest has been willing to make those investments. Most of the country has not.

## The Policy Ask — Three Words

Treat mental health crises as health crises. That is the policy ask. Three words that carry the weight of everything described in this document.

If a mental health crisis is a health crisis, then the primary response should come from the health system, not the criminal justice system. If a person experiencing psychosis is a patient, not a suspect, then the person who shows up at their door should be a clinician, not an officer. If suicidal ideation is a medical emergency, then the system that responds should be designed for treatment, not containment.

Fund civilian response. Build crisis stabilization centers in every community, the way we built fire stations in every community. Train and employ a workforce of crisis counselors, peer support specialists, and community health workers, the way we trained and employed a workforce of paramedics after the 1966 white paper. Connect 988 to mobile response, so that calling for help means someone comes, not just that someone answers the phone.

This requires money. Substantial money. But the current system already spends enormous sums — it simply spends them on emergency rooms, jail cells, and police overtime rather than on treatment, housing, and prevention. The economic argument for reform is not that it will be cheap. It is that it will be cheaper than what we are doing now, and it will actually work.

## The Human Cost

Behind the statistics and the policy arguments, there are people. On both sides of the gap.

There is the mother who called 911 because her son was suicidal, and officers arrived and shot him. She called for help, and help killed her child. She will never call 911 again, no matter how bad things get. Multiply her by thousands, and you begin to understand why marginalized communities — communities with the highest rates of mental illness and the fewest resources — are the least likely to call for help. The gap does not just fail to save people. It actively teaches them that asking for help is dangerous.

There is the patrol officer who has responded to two hundred mental health calls this year and has had training for exactly zero of them beyond a brief block in the academy fifteen years ago. She knows she is not qualified for this. She knows she is one bad decision away from killing someone who is sick, not criminal. She goes home after every shift carrying the weight of encounters she was never prepared for, and the department offers her an Employee Assistance Program with three free sessions per year as if that is adequate to the task.

There is the paramedic — the one this research is dedicated to — who has responded to the same patient fifty times over five years. He knows the patient's name, his diagnosis, his medication history, his mother's phone number, the shelter he prefers, the intersection where he camps when the shelter is full. He has transported this patient to the ER dozens of times and watched him be discharged every time, knowing that nothing has changed, that the next call is already coming. He does not blame the ER staff. He does not blame the patient. He blames a system that asks him to keep scooping water from a flooding basement without ever fixing the pipe.

There is the person in crisis. Frightened, confused, in pain that the people around them cannot see or measure. They need someone who understands. They need someone who will sit with them without judgment, without a timeline, without the authority to force compliance. They need care. What they get, in most of this country, is an armed stranger with fifteen minutes and a set of options that range from inadequate to catastrophic.

The gap kills in both directions. It kills the people in crisis who encounter a system designed for a different kind of emergency. And it kills the responders — slowly, over decades, through the accumulated moral injury of being asked to do a job they were never built to do, with tools that are wrong for the work, in a system that recycles suffering rather than ending it.

For thirty years, someone carried this weight. Answered the calls. Loaded the stretcher. Made the transport. Watched the revolving door spin. Did the job the system asked, knowing the system was broken, because the person on the stretcher still needed help right now, whatever the system's failures. That is what thirty years of service looks like when the gap is your daily beat.

The reform will come. The history of emergency services tells us that. But it will come too late for the people who needed it yesterday, and it will be built on the exhaustion and moral injury of the responders who held the line while the rest of us debated whether the gap was real.

It is real. It has always been real. And closing it is not a matter of innovation or evidence. It is a matter of deciding that people in mental health crisis deserve the same quality of response that people having heart attacks receive. Nothing more. Nothing less. Just the right people, with the right skills, showing up at the right time.

We know how to do this. We have known for thirty-five years. The only thing missing is the decision to do it.
