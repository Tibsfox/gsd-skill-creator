# FSR Learning Pathway: From Citizen to Community Asset

A structured learning journey through first response -- the skills, knowledge, and mindset that transform an ordinary citizen into someone who can help when it matters most. You don't need to be strong or brave or specially trained to start. You just need to be willing to learn, because the person lying on the ground bleeding doesn't care about your qualifications -- they care that you showed up and you know what to do.

This pathway organizes all FSR research documents, try-sessions, study-guides, case studies, and guides into a coherent sequence from basic safety skills through community emergency preparedness and volunteer service. Each level is self-contained -- completing Level 1 alone could save a life.

**Total estimated time:** 50-80 hours across all five levels (some levels require in-person training)
**Prerequisites:** None. Being an adult who cares about their community is the only prerequisite.
**College of Knowledge connections:** Science, Mind-Body, Physical Education, Learning, Community

**Important note:** This pathway covers knowledge and orientation. Some skills (CPR, Stop the Bleed, ICS certification) require in-person training with certified instructors. The try-sessions prepare you for that training and reinforce it afterward, but they don't replace it.

## Level 1 -- Ready (2-4 hours)

*"I want to be able to help if something bad happens."*

Most emergencies are survived or not in the first minutes, before professional responders arrive. Level 1 gives you the skills that matter most in those minutes: calling for help effectively, stopping life-threatening bleeding, and performing CPR. These three skills cover the vast majority of civilian emergency interventions.

### Try-Sessions -- Life-Saving Skills

1. **[try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md](../try-sessions/hands-only-cpr.md)** -- Learn hands-only CPR: the simplified technique recommended for bystanders who witness sudden cardiac arrest. No mouth-to-mouth, no rescue breaths -- just chest compressions at the right rate and depth. The American Heart Association says hands-only CPR is nearly as effective as conventional CPR for the first several minutes. This try-session teaches the technique and prepares you for in-person certification. (~30 min reading + practice)

**Follow-up:** Take an in-person CPR class. The Red Cross, American Heart Association, and many community colleges offer them. The try-session is preparation, not replacement.

2. **[try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md](../try-sessions/stop-the-bleed.md)** -- Learn to control life-threatening bleeding: direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet application. Based on the national Stop the Bleed campaign, which originated from military combat casualty care research. Hemorrhage is the #1 preventable cause of death in trauma. If you learn one thing from this entire pathway, learn this. (~30 min reading + practice)

**Follow-up:** Take a Stop the Bleed class. They're free, widely available, and take about 2 hours. Check stopthebleed.org for classes near you.

### Reading

3. **[01-history-first-responders.md](../01-history-first-responders.md)** -- The history of emergency response: from Roman fire brigades to the birth of modern EMS in the 1960s. Understanding how organized emergency response developed helps you understand the system you're now learning to support. (~25 min)

### Try-Session

4. **[try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill.md](../try-sessions/fire-extinguisher-drill.md)** -- Learn to use a fire extinguisher: PASS technique (Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep), when to fight a fire and when to evacuate, and the different types of extinguishers. Most people have never discharged a fire extinguisher before their first fire. Don't be most people. (~20 min reading + practice if you have an expired extinguisher)

### What You'll Learn
- How to perform hands-only CPR (enough to sustain a life until paramedics arrive)
- How to control life-threatening bleeding with direct pressure and tourniquets
- How to use a fire extinguisher and when to flee instead
- The historical development of emergency response systems
- How to call 911 effectively (it's more skill than you think)

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Perform hands-only CPR on an adult in cardiac arrest
- Apply direct pressure and a tourniquet to stop life-threatening bleeding
- Operate a fire extinguisher on a small fire
- Make a 911 call that gives dispatchers the information they need
- Stay calm enough to act (the try-sessions build this through practice)

### College Connections
- **Science:** Anatomy basics (circulatory system, airways), fire chemistry
- **Physical Education:** Physical competence under pressure
- **Mind-Body:** Stress management, overcoming freeze response
- **Learning:** Skill acquisition through deliberate practice

## Level 2 -- Prepared (1-2 weeks)

*"I want to be prepared for the big one -- earthquake, storm, extended power outage."*

The Pacific Northwest sits on the Cascadia Subduction Zone. A major earthquake is not a question of if but when. Level 2 moves from individual emergency skills to household and neighborhood preparedness. You'll build a go bag, learn the Incident Command System, and understand how the PNW emergency response system works.

### Try-Session

5. **[try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md](../try-sessions/build-a-go-bag.md)** -- Assemble a go bag (also called a bug-out bag or 72-hour kit): the supplies you grab when you need to leave your home in a hurry. This try-session covers what to include, what to skip, how to maintain it, and the psychology of having a plan. (~2-3 hours to build, 30 min quarterly maintenance)

### Reading -- Emergency Systems

6. **[02-fire-service.md](../02-fire-service.md)** -- How the fire service works: station structure, apparatus, shift schedules, the dual fire/EMS role, and how fire departments are organized. When you call 911 for a medical emergency in most of the US, a fire engine shows up. Understanding why helps you understand the system. (~30 min)

7. **[03-ems-paramedic.md](../03-ems-paramedic.md)** -- Emergency Medical Services: the difference between EMT and paramedic, how EMS systems are structured, the chain of survival, and what happens between the 911 call and the hospital door. (~30 min)

### Study Guides

8. **[study-guides/incident-command-basics.md](../study-guides/incident-command-basics.md)** -- The Incident Command System (ICS): the standardized management structure used for all emergency response in the United States. Understanding ICS is essential for anyone who might participate in organized emergency response, from CERT volunteers to neighborhood preparedness groups. (~25 min)

9. **[study-guides/radio-communications-basics.md](../study-guides/radio-communications-basics.md)** -- Basic radio communications: how emergency radios work, standard protocols, phonetic alphabet, the importance of clear and concise transmissions. If you have a ham radio or FRS radio, this teaches you how to use it effectively in an emergency. (~20 min)

10. **[study-guides/emergency-phone-numbers.md](../study-guides/emergency-phone-numbers.md)** -- Print it, laminate it, put it on the refrigerator. National emergency numbers (911, 988, 211, Poison Control), PNW-specific crisis lines, wildlife emergency contacts, utility companies, and a template for your household's personal emergency information. The companion reference to everything in Level 2. (~15 min to read, 30 min to customize)

### Guide

10. **[guides/citizen-preparedness.md](../guides/citizen-preparedness.md)** -- The comprehensive citizen preparedness guide: household emergency planning, neighborhood organizing, special needs considerations, pet preparedness, and the psychological dimension of being prepared without being paranoid. (~30 min)

### What You'll Learn
- How to build and maintain a 72-hour go bag
- How fire and EMS systems are structured and how they respond
- The Incident Command System and your role in it as a citizen
- Basic radio communication protocols
- Household and neighborhood emergency preparedness planning

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Evacuate your home in 5 minutes with everything you need
- Explain the Incident Command System to your neighbors
- Use a radio for emergency communications
- Create a household emergency plan that accounts for your specific situation
- Help organize neighborhood preparedness (even if it's just talking to your neighbors)

### College Connections
- **Science:** Emergency management systems, communication technology
- **Learning:** ICS as a transferable organizational framework
- **Mind-Body:** Preparedness mindset, managing anxiety productively
- **Home Economics:** Emergency supplies management, household planning

## Level 3 -- Responder (1-3 months)

*"I want to do more than call 911. I want to be part of the response."*

Level 3 is the transition from prepared citizen to active community responder. You'll study the professional response disciplines in depth, learn from real case studies, and begin to develop the judgment that separates someone who helps from someone who makes things worse. This level also prepares you for CERT (Community Emergency Response Team) training.

### Reading -- Professional Disciplines

11. **[04-law-enforcement.md](../04-law-enforcement.md)** -- Law enforcement response: what police do at emergencies, interaction with fire/EMS, de-escalation, and the civilian's role when law enforcement is present. Understanding all three response disciplines helps you understand the whole system. (~25 min)

12. **[05-dispatch-911.md](../05-dispatch-911.md)** -- The 911 dispatch center: how calls are received, prioritized, and dispatched. The dispatcher is the first link in the emergency response chain, and understanding their job makes you a better caller and a better responder. (~25 min)

13. **[06-training-certification.md](../06-training-certification.md)** -- The training and certification landscape: what certifications are available to civilians (CPR, Stop the Bleed, CERT, Wilderness First Aid, EMT), what they require, and how they stack. This is your roadmap to formalized training. (~25 min)

### Case Studies

Read at least two case studies. These are where theory meets reality -- real incidents analyzed for what went right, what went wrong, and what you can learn.

- **[case-studies/king-county-medic-one.md](../case-studies/king-county-medic-one.md)** -- The King County Medic One system: widely considered the best paramedic system in the world. How a Seattle cardiologist's vision created a system that gives King County residents the highest cardiac arrest survival rate anywhere. (~25 min)
- **[case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md](../case-studies/cascadia-earthquake.md)** -- The Cascadia Subduction Zone: the earthquake that's coming, what the response will look like, and why citizen preparedness matters more in the PNW than almost anywhere else. (~30 min)
- **[case-studies/portland-street-response.md](../case-studies/portland-street-response.md)** -- Portland Street Response: an alternative response model that sends unarmed crisis workers instead of police to behavioral health emergencies. A case study in rethinking what "response" means. (~20 min)
- **[case-studies/cahoots-model.md](../case-studies/cahoots-model.md)** -- The CAHOOTS model from Eugene, OR: the original mobile crisis intervention program that inspired Portland Street Response and similar programs nationwide. (~20 min)
- **[case-studies/station-life.md](../case-studies/station-life.md)** -- A day in the life of a fire station: the reality behind the sirens. Shift structure, station duties, training, downtime, and the culture of the firehouse. (~20 min)

### What You'll Learn
- How all three response disciplines (fire, EMS, law enforcement) coordinate
- How the 911 dispatch system works from call to response
- Available civilian training and certification pathways
- Real-world lessons from case studies of both success and failure
- Why the Cascadia earthquake makes PNW preparedness uniquely important

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Explain the full emergency response chain from 911 call to hospital
- Identify your next training step (CERT, Wilderness First Aid, EMT)
- Analyze an emergency response for lessons learned
- Discuss alternative response models (mental health crisis teams)
- Make informed decisions about where to invest your training time

### College Connections
- **Science:** Emergency medicine, seismology (Cascadia), public health
- **Learning:** Case study analysis, systems thinking
- **Mind-Body:** Crisis decision-making, stress inoculation
- **Philosophy:** Ethics of intervention, alternative response models

## Level 4 -- Trained (3-6 months)

*"I want formal training. I want to be certified. I want to be useful in a real emergency."*

Level 4 is about formal training and deep study of the systems that support emergency response. You'll study equipment and apparatus, learn about the mental health dimensions of emergency work, and explore the PNW-specific response landscape. This is also where you pursue formal certifications: CERT, Wilderness First Aid, or EMT-Basic.

### Reading

14. **[07-equipment-apparatus.md](../07-equipment-apparatus.md)** -- The tools of the trade: fire apparatus, EMS equipment, personal protective equipment, communications systems, and the technology that makes modern emergency response possible. (~30 min)

15. **[08-mental-health-crisis.md](../08-mental-health-crisis.md)** -- Mental health crisis response: recognizing mental health emergencies, de-escalation techniques, the intersection of mental health and emergency response, and why this is the fastest-growing challenge in public safety. (~30 min)

16. **[10-pnw-departments.md](../10-pnw-departments.md)** -- PNW fire and EMS departments: the agencies, mutual aid agreements, and organizational structures specific to the Pacific Northwest. This is your local response landscape -- the people and organizations that protect your community. (~25 min)

### Guides -- Career and Volunteer Paths

17. **[guides/becoming-a-firefighter.md](../guides/becoming-a-firefighter.md)** -- The path to becoming a firefighter: physical requirements, academy training, the hiring process, and what the job is actually like (hint: it's 90% medical calls). Read this even if you don't plan to become a firefighter -- it deepens your understanding of the people who respond when you call 911. (~25 min)

18. **[guides/becoming-a-paramedic.md](../guides/becoming-a-paramedic.md)** -- The path to becoming a paramedic: EMT-B prerequisite, paramedic school, clinical rotations, certification, and the reality of the job. The most medically trained providers who come to your house. (~25 min)

### Formal Training (External)

19. **CERT Training** -- Community Emergency Response Team training is offered by local fire departments and emergency management agencies. It's usually free, runs over several weeks, and covers fire safety, light search and rescue, disaster medical operations, and team organization. This is the single most recommended training for prepared citizens. (20-24 hours in-person)

20. **Wilderness First Aid (WFA)** -- A 16-hour course that teaches emergency care in remote settings where help is hours away. Especially relevant for PNW residents who hike, camp, or live in rural areas. Offered by NOLS, SOLO, and other wilderness medicine providers. (16 hours in-person)

### What You'll Learn
- Emergency response equipment and how it's used
- Mental health crisis recognition and de-escalation basics
- PNW-specific response organizations and mutual aid systems
- Career and volunteer pathways in emergency services
- CERT or equivalent formal training content

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Participate effectively in an organized emergency response
- Recognize and appropriately respond to mental health crises
- Identify your local response organizations and know how to volunteer
- Hold formal certifications (CERT, WFA, or equivalent)
- Function as a trained member of a neighborhood response team

### College Connections
- **Science:** Emergency medicine, equipment technology, mental health
- **Physical Education:** Physical fitness for emergency response
- **Mind-Body:** De-escalation, crisis psychology, stress management
- **Community:** CERT teams, volunteer organizations, neighborhood networks

## Level 5 -- Community Asset (Ongoing)

*"I want to serve. I want my community to be safer because I'm in it."*

The highest level of this pathway isn't a certification -- it's a commitment. You're a known, trusted, trained resource in your community. You volunteer. You teach others. You maintain your skills. When something goes wrong, people look to you not because of a title but because they know you're prepared.

### Reading

21. **[09-responder-wellness.md](../09-responder-wellness.md)** -- Responder wellness: burnout, compassion fatigue, PTSD, and the mental health challenges that come with repeated exposure to emergencies. This applies to volunteers as much as professionals. Taking care of yourself is not optional -- it's a prerequisite for taking care of others. (~25 min)

### Cross-References

22. **[11-cross-references-bibliography.md](../11-cross-references-bibliography.md)** -- The full bibliography and connections to other research projects and external resources. (~reference)

### Ongoing Service

23. **Volunteer.** CERT deployment, fire department volunteer programs, Search and Rescue, Red Cross disaster response, Medical Reserve Corps -- the options vary by community but they all need people. The training you've completed makes you useful from day one.

24. **Teach.** Run a Stop the Bleed class for your neighborhood. Teach CPR at your workplace. Help your neighbors build go bags. The skills in this pathway multiply in value when shared.

25. **Maintain.** Skills decay. Certifications expire. Equipment needs checking. Schedule quarterly reviews of your go bag, annual CPR recertification, and regular participation in drills and exercises. The person who trained five years ago and never practiced is not prepared -- they're overconfident.

26. **Advocate.** Push for neighborhood preparedness. Support funding for emergency services. Know your local emergency management plan and help fill its gaps. Community resilience is built person by person, neighborhood by neighborhood.

### What You'll Learn
- How to maintain mental health while engaging with emergency situations
- How to sustain a volunteer commitment without burning out
- How to teach emergency skills effectively to untrained people
- That community resilience is a collective achievement, not an individual one

### What You'll Be Able to DO
- Serve as a trained volunteer in organized emergency response
- Teach life-saving skills to your community
- Maintain personal readiness and equipment year-round
- Contribute to community emergency planning at the neighborhood level
- Recognize and manage your own stress and compassion fatigue

### College Connections
- **Mind-Body:** Wellness, resilience, compassion fatigue management
- **Community:** Volunteer service, teaching, neighborhood organizing
- **Learning:** Teaching methodology, skill maintenance
- **Physical Education:** Ongoing physical readiness

## How to Use This Pathway

**Level 1 is enough to save a life.** If you stop at Level 1 -- CPR, Stop the Bleed, fire extinguisher -- you've already become orders of magnitude more capable in an emergency than most people. Don't let the depth of the later levels intimidate you out of starting.

**Get formal training.** The try-sessions are preparation and reinforcement, not replacement for in-person instruction. Sign up for a CPR class this week. Find a Stop the Bleed class this month. Start CERT training this year.

**Practice under stress.** Emergency skills performed calmly in a classroom need to work when your hands are shaking and someone is screaming. Scenario-based training (available through CERT and other programs) bridges this gap.

**Include your household.** Teach your family CPR. Build the go bag together. Make the emergency plan as a household. Preparedness that only one person knows about is a single point of failure.

**Connect to other projects.** Emergency preparedness intersects with everything:
- **[RCN](../../RCN/research/)** -- Wildlife encounter management, especially during disasters when animals are displaced
- **[FWL](../../FWL/research/)** -- Animal first aid (your chickens during an earthquake)
- **[WDC](../../WDC/research/)** -- Emergency shelter construction, structural assessment
- **[PNT](../../PNT/research/)** -- Art therapy for responder wellness

**Use the cross-project learning paths.** This project appears in:
- [The PNW Naturalist](../../../learning-paths/pnw-naturalist.md) -- citizen preparedness for wilderness settings
- [The Community Responder](../../../learning-paths/community-responder.md) -- the primary cross-project path for emergency skills
