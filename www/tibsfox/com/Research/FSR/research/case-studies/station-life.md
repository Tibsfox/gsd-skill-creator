# Twenty-Four Hours --- A Day in the Life of a Firehouse

## 0700: Shift Change

The engine backs into the bay at 0645. The off-going crew has been up since 0200 --- a structure fire alarm that turned out to be burnt food on a stove, followed by a medical call at 0430 that was a regular: an elderly man with COPD who could not breathe, who calls every few weeks when his oxygen runs low and his anxiety peaks, who knows the crew by name. They transported him to the ER. Again. He will be back home by noon. They will see him again next month.

The on-coming crew arrives by 0645 to 0650. This is not optional. Showing up at 0700 sharp means you are late, because shift change is a working process, not a clock-punch. The face-to-face report between the off-going and on-coming crews happens at the rig, in the kitchen, wherever the crews intersect. The off-going captain briefs the on-coming captain: what happened overnight, what equipment was used and needs restocking, what is broken, what the district looks like. The off-going engineer walks the on-coming engineer through the apparatus: fuel level, water tank level, anything that ran rough, anything that needs attention. The firefighters exchange notes on calls, on the neighborhood, on the guy on Oak Street who has been burning trash in his backyard again.

This is the handoff. The responsibility for the district --- for every person in it, every building, every call that comes in for the next twenty-four hours --- passes from one crew to another. It happens every morning, at every station, in every fire department. It is unremarkable and essential.

By 0700, the off-going crew is heading to the parking lot. Some of them will go home and sleep. Some will go to second jobs --- construction, teaching, personal training, side businesses that the 24/48 schedule makes possible. Some will go to the gym, or to their kids' school, or to the appointment they scheduled for their day off. Their day started twenty-four hours ago. Some of them slept three hours. Some of them did not sleep at all.

---

## 0700 -- 0800: Rig Check

The first duty of every shift is the rig check. Every compartment. Every tool. Every drug.

The engineer --- the person who drives and operates the apparatus, who is responsible for every mechanical and hydraulic system on the rig --- goes through the engine systematically. Engine start and idle check. Pump engagement. Aerial operation (if it is a ladder truck). Generator start. Scene lighting. Siren and air horn. Every compartment door opens; every tool is in its assigned place. Nozzles, adapters, wrenches, couplings, spare SCBA bottles, RIT (Rapid Intervention Team) pack, ventilation fans, saws, hand tools, medical equipment, AED, drug box, oxygen.

The firefighter/paramedics check the medical equipment independently. The drug box is opened, every medication is accounted for, expiration dates are checked. The cardiac monitor is powered on, self-test confirmed, defibrillation pads checked. The airway kit is inspected: laryngoscope blades and handles, endotracheal tubes, supraglottic airways, suction unit, bag-valve-mask, capnography sensor. The oxygen supply is verified --- main tank and portable bottles. The trauma bag is checked: tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, bandages, splints, burn sheets.

This is not busywork. A missing tool, an expired drug, a dead battery in the cardiac monitor --- any of these can mean the difference between saving a life and losing one. The rig check is a ritual of readiness. It takes thirty to forty-five minutes to do properly, and it is done every single shift change, three hundred and sixty-five days a year. The crew that shortcuts the rig check is the crew that finds out at 0300 that the saw will not start, or the defibrillator is not charged, or the intubation kit is missing a blade.

The station itself gets a walk-through. Apparatus bay doors function. Bay floor is clean (oil and water make the floor slick when you are running to the rig at 2 AM). Kitchen and common areas are clean --- or, more accurately, whatever the off-going crew left behind is dealt with. Bunk rooms are checked. The station log is reviewed.

## 0800 -- 1000: Station Duties and Housekeeping

The firehouse is a home. It is lived in twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, by rotating crews who eat, sleep, work, and pass the time within its walls. Keeping it functional is a daily task, and the tasks are divided among the crew.

The apparatus bay is swept and mopped. The bathrooms are cleaned. The kitchen is wiped down. The dayroom (common area) is straightened. The bunk rooms are made up. These are not glamorous tasks, and they are not optional. The captain assigns duties, the crew executes them, and the station stays livable. A dirty firehouse is a dysfunctional firehouse --- it reflects a crew that does not take care of its space, which is a crew that may not take care of its equipment, which is a crew that may not take care of its patients.

Equipment maintenance fills the gaps. A saw blade needs replacing. A length of hose failed the last pressure test and needs to be removed from service. A compartment door hinge is stiff. The engineer handles the mechanical work; the firefighters handle the hand tool maintenance. This is constant, unglamorous, essential work. Every piece of equipment on the rig is a life-safety tool, and every life-safety tool requires maintenance.

Calls interrupt. The tones can drop at any moment --- 0815, 0847, 0903 --- and everything stops. The mop goes in the bucket, the wrench goes on the bench, and the crew is on the rig in sixty to ninety seconds. The call might take twenty minutes (a false alarm, a lift assist, a minor medical) or it might take two hours (a working fire, a multi-vehicle accident, a cardiac arrest). When the crew returns, they pick up where they left off. This is the rhythm of station life: planned activity interrupted by unplanned urgency, repeatedly, for twenty-four hours.

## 1000 -- 1200: Training

Training is the backbone of competency, and it is built into every shift. The captain determines the day's training topic, which may follow a department-mandated monthly training schedule or may be driven by recent incidents, seasonal hazards, or identified weaknesses.

### Training Types

**Fire training.** Live fire evolutions in a burn building (scheduled events at the training center, not daily), fire behavior simulations, hose advancement drills, ladder throws, forcible entry on prop doors, ventilation techniques, RIT (Rapid Intervention Team) deployment, SCBA air management. Some days, the crew pulls the rig out of the bay and runs hose in the station parking lot. Some days, the crew walks the district, identifying buildings, hydrant locations, access points, and pre-plan targets.

**EMS training.** Cardiac arrest scenarios on a CPR manikin. Mega-code drills (full ACLS scenarios: the monitor shows a rhythm, the lead paramedic calls the drugs, the team performs compressions, the scenario evolves). Airway management practice. Trauma assessment stations. Pharmacology review. Protocol updates from the medical director. Pediatric scenarios (rare in the field, which means skill deterioration is a real risk --- training compensates).

**Technical rescue.** Vehicle extrication practice on donated cars: stabilization, glass removal, door pops, dash rolls, roof removal. Rope rescue. Confined space entry. Trench rescue awareness. Water rescue. These are specialized disciplines, and the depth of training depends on the station's assignment --- a station with a rescue company trains deeper in technical rescue than a single-engine station.

**Physical training.** PT is not optional. The captain leads or assigns a workout. The gym at the station --- weight room, pull-up bar, treadmill, sometimes a basketball hoop out back --- is used daily. Cardiovascular endurance, functional strength, flexibility. A firefighter who cannot perform physically is a liability to their crew. The PT expectation does not relax with seniority. A twenty-year veteran who cannot climb stairs in full gear is a twenty-year veteran who cannot do the job.

### The Training Culture

Good fire companies train hard because they have seen what happens when training is inadequate. The structure fire where the hose team cannot advance because they have not practiced nozzle technique. The cardiac arrest where the team fumbles the drug sequence because they have not run a mega-code in months. The vehicle extrication where the cutter fails because no one has serviced it.

Bad fire companies treat training as a checkbox. They sit in the day room, the captain signs the training log, and no one actually drills. The difference between these two cultures is measured in outcomes. It is measured in the lives of the people the company serves and in the lives of the firefighters themselves.

## 1200: Lunch

The crew cooks together. This is sacred.

The kitchen is the heart of the firehouse. The meal is planned, shopped for (often by a designated crew member who swings by the grocery store in the morning), prepared, and eaten together. Everyone contributes to the cost --- a typical firehouse meal fund runs $8 to $15 per person per shift. Someone cooks. Everyone eats. The table is where the crew connects.

Firehouse cooking is a real tradition with real skill. Some firehouses are legendary for their food. Chili, enchiladas, jambalaya, grilled steaks, homemade pasta, elaborate breakfasts. The probie (first-year firefighter) is expected to cook --- and to cook well. Being a terrible cook in a firehouse is a character flaw that the crew will address with directness. Recipes are passed between stations. Competitions happen between shifts. The meal is the social glue of the 24-hour shift.

What happens at the table matters. The conversations are about the calls, about the district, about families, about the news, about nothing important. The jokes are dark --- gallows humor is the default coping mechanism of people who deal with death and suffering as part of their job. The ribbing is constant and affectionate. The rookie gets it worse than everyone else, and the rookie is expected to take it with grace.

But the table is also where the hard conversations happen. Where a veteran admits, in an offhand comment between bites, that a call got to them. Where the captain notices that someone is quieter than usual and asks a question that opens a door. The firehouse kitchen is a social barometer. When the crew eats together and talks, the culture is healthy. When the crew eats alone in their bunks with their phones, something is wrong.

The tones can drop mid-meal. The fork goes down, the plate goes in the oven (if you are lucky), and the crew rolls out. You learn to eat fast. Not because you want to, but because the alternative is coming back two hours later to a cold plate. Or not coming back for the meal at all.

## 1300 -- 1700: The Afternoon

The afternoon is unstructured time filled by calls, maintenance, personal development, and the rhythms of a lived-in building.

### The Calls

The tones drop. The overhead PA crackles with the dispatcher's voice: engine company, address, cross street, type of call. The bay door rolls up. The crew is on the rig. The officer (captain or lieutenant) confirms the address, acknowledges the dispatch, and sizes up the call on the way.

#### Structure Fire Response

The big one. Multiple units dispatched. The column of smoke visible from a mile away. The adrenaline is immediate and physical --- heart rate spikes, hands tighten on equipment, the training kicks in.

Arrival: the officer sizes up the building. Smoke and fire conditions. Construction type. Exposure risk. Number of stories. Reports of occupants. The initial radio report goes out: what you have, what you are doing, what you need. "Engine 7 on scene, two-story wood frame, heavy smoke from the second floor, we are going offensive, pulling a line to the front door."

The crew pulls a line --- 200 feet of inch-and-three-quarter hose charged to 175 psi. The nozzle firefighter and backup firefighter advance into the structure. The heat hits. The smoke eliminates visibility. They work by feel, by training, by the sound of the fire. The nozzle opens and the water hits the seat of the fire. Steam. The thermal layer drops. The room darkens as the fire dies. They push forward, checking for extension --- fire in the walls, fire in the ceiling, fire that has traveled to adjacent rooms through the balloon frame or the duct work.

Simultaneously: the truck company (ladder) is on the roof, opening ventilation holes to release heat and smoke. Search teams are checking bedrooms, closets, bathrooms --- anywhere a person might be trapped. The RIT team is staged outside, ready to enter if a firefighter goes down.

The aftermath: salvage (covering furniture, removing water), overhaul (opening walls and ceilings to ensure the fire has not extended into concealed spaces), and the scene investigation. The whole event --- from tone drop to scene clear --- might be ninety minutes for a room-and-contents fire. It might be four hours for a fully involved structure. It might be twelve hours for a major commercial fire.

The crew returns to the station. The rig is restocked. Hose is replaced. SCBA bottles are refilled. Turnout gear is washed (the carcinogens in fire smoke are now understood to be a primary cancer risk for firefighters --- decontamination of gear after a fire is no longer optional). The report is written. And the crew resets, because there might be another one tonight.

#### Medical Calls

These are the bread and butter. Eighty percent of responses. The call types are described in detail in [FSR-03: EMS and Paramedicine](../03-ems-paramedic.md), but the operational rhythm is this: tones drop, roll out, arrive, assess, treat, transport (or treat and release, if the patient refuses transport). A busy urban engine company runs 8 to 15 medical calls per 24-hour shift. The calls range from the genuinely life-threatening (chest pain, stroke, cardiac arrest, difficulty breathing) to the mundane (lift assists, minor injuries, intoxication).

The mundane calls are not nothing. A lift assist for an elderly person who has fallen is a chance to assess their living conditions, check their medications, notice the trip hazard that caused the fall. A minor injury call is a chance to educate. An intoxication call is a chance to connect someone with resources --- or, at minimum, to ensure they do not die of hypothermia on a park bench on a January night.

#### The Calls That Grind

And then there are the ones that are neither dramatic nor mundane. The frequent flyers --- the patients you respond to week after week, the same address, the same complaint, the same transport to the same ER for the same problem that the system cannot or will not fix. The psychiatric crisis calls where you know the patient needs long-term mental health care and the best you can do is transport them to an ER that will stabilize and release them. The substance calls where the patient you resuscitated with naloxone today will overdose again next week.

These calls accumulate. They are discussed in [FSR-09: Responder Wellness](../09-responder-wellness.md) because their weight is cumulative and insidious. The single dramatic call gets attention. The ten thousandth routine call that slowly erodes your capacity to care --- that gets nothing.

## 1800: Dinner

The second crew meal. Often more elaborate than lunch because the afternoon allows more prep time (between calls). Dinner is the anchor of the evening --- the crew gathers, eats, and the social machinery of the firehouse turns.

In many firehouses, dinner is the best meal you will eat all week. The traditions run deep. Some stations have signature dishes that have been cooked by generations of crews. Some have cookbooks assembled over decades. The captain who can cook is beloved. The probie who shows up on day one with a killer recipe earns immediate social currency.

The meal cost is shared. The designated cook shops, preps, and cooks. The rest of the crew cleans up. The dishwashing duty rotates or falls to the lowest-seniority member. This economy --- small, shared, functional --- is a microcosm of firehouse culture. Everyone contributes. No one free-rides. The person who does not contribute to the meal fund, who does not help with cleanup, who eats but does not participate --- that person is noticed, and the crew adjusts its assessment accordingly.

## 1900 -- 2200: Evening

The evening hours are the most variable part of the shift. In a busy station, calls continue at a steady pace --- the evening hours are prime time for medical calls (people returning home from work discover symptoms they ignored all day, or domestic situations escalate, or the drinking starts). In a slower station, the evening is relatively quiet.

The crew watches television, plays cards, reads, works on personal projects, or studies. Officers handle paperwork --- the administrative burden of a fire company is substantial: daily logs, training records, inspection reports, pre-fire plans, equipment maintenance records, personnel evaluations.

Some firefighters use the evening for continuing education. Paramedic recertification requires 60 hours over two years. Fire service certifications require additional training hours. The motivated firefighter uses quiet evenings at the station to stay current. The unmotivated one watches television. The captain who fosters a learning culture makes a difference that is measured in competency.

Phone calls home. This is the part of the 24-hour shift that the public does not think about. Your kids are going to bed and you are not there. Your partner is handling everything at home --- alone. The holidays, the birthdays, the school events, the bedtime routines --- all happening without you, every third day, for an entire career. The 24/48 schedule gives you more days off than a traditional 40-hour job, but the days you are gone, you are completely gone. The family adjusts or it does not. Both outcomes are common.

---

## 2200: Try to Sleep

Lights out is typically 2200, though the common areas may stay active later. The crew retreats to the bunk room. Boots are beside the bed. Turnout gear is staged --- pants with boots folded into them, coat on a hook, helmet on the shelf. When the tones drop at 0200, you need to go from horizontal to dressed to running in sixty seconds or less. Every firefighter develops a muscle memory for this: swing feet out, step into boots, pull up pants, grab coat and helmet, and move. You learn to sleep in a state of readiness that a civilian would find impossible to maintain.

Sleep quality in a firehouse ranges from adequate to nonexistent. A busy urban station that runs 15 to 25 calls in 24 hours may generate 5 to 10 calls between 2200 and 0700. Each call is a full wake-up: tones, lights, movement, adrenaline, driving, working, returning, decompressing enough to fall asleep again. The cumulative sleep deprivation of a career in a busy station is a documented health risk. Cardiac disease, metabolic disorders, cognitive impairment, and mental health effects are all elevated in firefighters, and disrupted sleep is a significant contributing factor.

A slow station --- a rural station or a suburban station with a lower call volume --- might run 3 to 5 calls in 24 hours and allow 5 to 6 hours of sleep. These stations are coveted by veterans who have done their years at the busy houses and want a shift where they can close their eyes without certainty that they will be on their feet again in ninety minutes.

## 0200: The Tones Drop

The sound that changes everything.

Dispatch tones on the overhead PA. The lights come on. The crew is vertical and moving before they are fully awake. The dispatcher's voice: engine company, address, nature of the call. You are dressed and on the rig in sixty seconds. The bay door rolls up into the dark. The engine pulls out of the station.

At 0200, the calls are different. The medical calls are often more serious --- the person who has been ignoring chest pain all day finally cannot breathe. The cardiac arrests --- the calls that matter most --- disproportionately happen in the early morning hours, when the body's circadian rhythm dips and vulnerable hearts fail. The psychiatric crises escalate at night. The substance calls accumulate.

And sometimes at 0200, the call is nothing. A fire alarm triggered by cooking smoke. A medical alarm with no patient contact. A dumpster fire. You respond with the same urgency, because you do not know what you are getting until you are on scene, and the one time you assume it is nothing and roll slowly, it will be something.

You return to the station. You hang up your gear. You try to sleep. The alarm might go off again in twenty minutes, or it might not go off again until morning. You do not know. You learn to exist in this uncertainty, and you learn to function on whatever sleep the shift allows. This is the deal. This is the 24-hour shift. This is what it means, night after night, for thirty years.

## The Culture

The firehouse is not a workplace. It is a community. You eat together, train together, bleed together, and grieve together. You share a bunk room with people who snore, who talk in their sleep, who smell after a fire. You share a kitchen with people who cannot cook and people who are artists with a cast-iron skillet. You share calls that are boring and calls that break your heart and calls that make you laugh so hard you cannot breathe.

These people become your second family. This is not a metaphor. It is a literal description of the relationship that forms when you spend a third of your life with the same crew in the same building running into the same dangers. You know their families. You attend their weddings and their kids' graduations. You visit them in the hospital. You stand at their funerals.

The culture has its dark side. The hierarchies can be rigid and abusive. The hazing traditions that exist in some departments are genuinely harmful. The "suck it up" mentality that suppresses emotional expression contributes directly to the PTSD, depression, substance abuse, and suicide rates documented in [FSR-09: Responder Wellness](../09-responder-wellness.md). The culture is changing --- peer support programs, mental health awareness, the gradual erosion of the stoic ideal --- but it is changing slowly, against institutional inertia and generational habit.

The culture also has a power that is difficult to overstate. When the worst call of your career happens --- the one that follows you home, that shows up in your dreams, that makes you wonder whether you can do this anymore --- the people who understand, without explanation, are the people you work with. Your partner at home may try. Your friends may try. But the crew was there. They saw it. They smelled it. They carried the same weight. And when you sit at the kitchen table the next morning and someone puts a cup of coffee in front of you and says nothing, and the nothing is enough --- that is the firehouse. For thirty years.
