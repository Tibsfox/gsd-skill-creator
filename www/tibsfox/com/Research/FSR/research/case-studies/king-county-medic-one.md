# King County Medic One --- The Gold Standard of Emergency Medicine

## The Heart That Stopped at Northgate Mall

A man collapses in the food court of Northgate Mall in North Seattle. He is 54 years old, no known cardiac history, eating lunch with his wife. One moment he is reaching for his coffee. The next moment he is on the floor, unresponsive, not breathing, his heart in ventricular fibrillation --- the chaotic electrical storm that, without intervention, becomes death within minutes.

What happens next is a cascade of systems, training, culture, and infrastructure that most of the world does not have and that King County, Washington, has spent more than fifty years building.

A bystander --- a retired schoolteacher who took a CPR class at her community center three years ago --- drops to her knees and starts chest compressions. She learned them in a two-hour course run by the Medic 2 program, one of the largest civilian CPR training initiatives in the world. She is not a medical professional. She is a person who, because of where she lives, was given the training to keep another person's brain alive until real help arrives.

Another bystander finds the AED mounted on the wall near the restrooms. It is there because King County's Public Access Defibrillation program has placed automated external defibrillators in malls, airports, gyms, community centers, and public buildings throughout the region. He opens the case, applies the pads, follows the voice prompts. The AED analyzes the rhythm, identifies ventricular fibrillation, charges, and delivers a shock. The man's heart does not convert. The schoolteacher keeps compressing.

Engine 35, the nearest Seattle Fire Department unit, arrives in four minutes. The firefighters are trained to BLS --- Basic Life Support --- level. They take over CPR with a bag-valve mask and mechanical chest compressions. They confirm the AED's analysis and deliver a second shock. Still V-fib.

Three minutes later, the Medic One unit arrives. Two paramedics, not merely certified but residency-trained at Harborview Medical Center, the region's Level 1 trauma center. They establish an IV line, push epinephrine, intubate, deliver a third shock with their monitor-defibrillator while continuing high-quality CPR. On the fourth shock, the heart converts to a perfusing rhythm. The man has a pulse. He is unconscious but alive. He is transported to Harborview, where he will spend three days in the cardiac ICU, undergo a cardiac catheterization and stent placement, and walk out of the hospital six days later with a functioning brain and a beating heart.

In most of America, this man dies. In King County, Washington, he has a 62 percent chance of walking out of the hospital neurologically intact.

That number is not a typo. It is the result of a system that a physician at the University of Washington started building in 1970, and it is arguably the greatest achievement in prehospital emergency medicine in the world.

## The Founder

Dr. Leonard Cobb was a cardiologist at the University of Washington and Harborview Medical Center who, in the late 1960s, became convinced that most cardiac arrest deaths were preventable. The problem was not that the heart could not be restarted --- defibrillation had been demonstrated in hospitals since the 1950s. The problem was time. Ventricular fibrillation is survivable if defibrillation occurs within minutes. But in 1969, cardiac arrest outside a hospital was a death sentence, because there was no system to deliver defibrillation to where the patient was.

Cobb studied the early paramedic programs in Belfast and Los Angeles, where physician-designed mobile units were demonstrating that advanced cardiac care could be delivered in the field. He then did something that would define the next half-century of emergency medicine in the Pacific Northwest: he designed a system, not just a unit.

In 1970, Cobb launched Medic One as a joint program of the Seattle Fire Department and the University of Washington. The first Medic One unit --- Medic 1 --- was stationed at Harborview Medical Center. It carried a physician and a specially trained firefighter. Within months, the physician was replaced by paramedics, because Cobb recognized that a system dependent on physician field response could never scale. The innovation was not the concept of a paramedic unit. It was the training model, the quality infrastructure, and the integration with the fire department that made the concept work at the system level.

## The Tiered Response Model

The genius of King County's EMS system is not any single component. It is the architecture --- the way the components fit together into a system where each layer reinforces the others.

### Tier 1: Citizen CPR

The foundation of the system is the bystander. Dr. Cobb understood from the earliest data that paramedic response time, no matter how fast, was not fast enough to maintain brain viability in cardiac arrest without intermediate intervention. The brain begins to suffer irreversible damage after approximately four minutes without blood flow. Even the fastest paramedic response cannot reliably beat four minutes. But a bystander can be at the patient's side in seconds.

In 1972, Cobb launched the Medic 2 program --- a massive public education initiative to train ordinary citizens in CPR. The program was revolutionary in its ambition. Previous CPR training had been aimed at healthcare professionals. Cobb wanted to train everyone: office workers, teachers, bus drivers, teenagers, retirees. He believed that CPR was simple enough to be taught to any motivated person in a few hours, and that a population trained in CPR would function as the first tier of the emergency response system --- unpaid, untrained in any other medical skill, but capable of keeping blood moving until the professionals arrived.

The Medic 2 program trained hundreds of thousands of King County residents over the following decades. By the early 2000s, studies estimated that more than 50 percent of King County adults had received CPR training at some point in their lives. The result was measurable: bystander CPR rates in King County consistently exceed 60 percent for witnessed cardiac arrests, compared to a national average that hovers around 40 percent. That 20-percentage-point difference translates directly into survival, because early CPR doubles or triples the chance of surviving cardiac arrest.

### Tier 2: Fire Department BLS First Response

When 911 is called for a cardiac arrest, the nearest fire engine is dispatched simultaneously with the Medic One unit. Seattle and King County fire departments train all firefighters to the EMT-Basic level, with additional training in automated external defibrillation, airway management, and high-performance CPR. The fire engine arrives first --- typically in four to six minutes --- and initiates systematic resuscitation: high-quality chest compressions at the correct rate and depth, ventilation with a bag-valve mask, and early defibrillation with an AED.

The fire department BLS response serves as a bridge between citizen CPR and paramedic ALS. It replaces bystander compressions --- which are often effective but tire quickly --- with professional-quality CPR from trained, equipped, fresh responders. It provides early defibrillation, which is the single most important intervention for ventricular fibrillation. And it establishes scene management so that when the Medic One unit arrives, the paramedics can focus entirely on advanced interventions rather than basic CPR.

### Tier 3: Medic One Paramedic Response

The Medic One paramedic unit is where the system's quality model becomes most visible. King County Medic One paramedics are not merely certified. They complete a residency.

Paramedic certification in most of the United States requires approximately 1,200 hours of training --- classroom instruction, skills labs, clinical rotations, and field internship. It is adequate to produce a competent provider. King County Medic One requires all of that, plus a 10-month clinical residency at Harborview Medical Center. During this residency, paramedic candidates rotate through the emergency department, the cardiac catheterization lab, the operating room, the intensive care unit, and the pediatric unit. They intubate patients under the supervision of anesthesiologists. They establish central venous lines. They manage cardiac arrest, trauma, stroke, and sepsis alongside emergency medicine physicians. They are evaluated continuously, and not everyone passes.

The result is a paramedic corps with clinical skills that far exceed the national standard. A King County Medic One paramedic has performed more intubations, started more IV lines, managed more cardiac arrests, and operated under more physician oversight than paramedics in virtually any other system in the country. This clinical depth shows in outcomes.

### Tier 4: Physician Oversight

Every Medic One operational unit operates under the medical direction of a physician --- typically an emergency medicine physician affiliated with the University of Washington. This is not a distant, bureaucratic relationship. The medical director reviews every cardiac arrest. Every one. The resuscitation data --- the rhythm strip, the compression quality (measured by accelerometer), the drug timing, the ventilation parameters, the shock sequence, the outcome --- is reviewed case by case. Deviations from protocol are identified, discussed, and used to improve both the protocol and the individual paramedic's practice.

This continuous quality improvement model is the engine that keeps the system at the leading edge. It is borrowed from aviation's approach to incident review, and it functions the same way: every event is an opportunity to learn, not to blame. Paramedics are not punished for bad outcomes. They are educated about what the data shows could be done differently. The culture is one of relentless improvement, and the results reflect it.

## The Numbers

### Cardiac Arrest Survival

The headline number: King County Medic One achieves approximately 62 percent survival to hospital discharge for witnessed ventricular fibrillation cardiac arrest. The national average for the same category is approximately 10 percent. King County's survival rate is roughly six times the national average.

To appreciate what this means, consider the denominator. Ventricular fibrillation is the most treatable cardiac arrest rhythm --- it is the one where defibrillation is most effective. But it is also the one where time matters most. V-fib degenerates into asystole (flatline) within minutes, and asystole is rarely survivable regardless of treatment. The difference between 62 percent and 10 percent survival is not primarily a difference in technology. Every EMS system in America has defibrillators. The difference is in time to first shock, quality of CPR in the interval, paramedic clinical skill, and the system architecture that ties all these elements together.

Even for less treatable rhythms --- pulseless electrical activity and asystole --- King County's survival rates exceed national averages, because the fundamentals of high-quality CPR and rapid response improve outcomes across all cardiac arrest etiologies.

### Utstein Comparisons

The Utstein reporting template, developed in 1990 at a conference at the Utstein Abbey in Norway, standardized cardiac arrest outcome reporting to allow comparisons across EMS systems. When systems report using the Utstein template, King County consistently ranks at or near the top of global benchmarks. The systems that approach King County's numbers --- parts of Japan, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia --- share the same architectural features: tiered response, citizen CPR training, early defibrillation, physician-directed paramedic practice, and continuous quality improvement.

### Beyond Cardiac Arrest

While cardiac arrest survival is the metric by which Medic One is most often measured, the system's quality extends to all categories of prehospital care. King County's trauma outcomes, stroke recognition and transport times, STEMI (heart attack) identification and cardiac catheterization lab activation times, and sepsis recognition all exceed national benchmarks. The Harborview residency model produces paramedics who are excellent across the full scope of prehospital medicine, not just cardiac arrest management.

## The Research Engine

Medic One is not only a clinical operation. It is a research enterprise, and the research has shaped emergency medicine worldwide.

### The Seattle Resuscitation Academy

Founded by Dr. Thomas Rea, the current Medic One medical director, the Seattle Resuscitation Academy is a training program for EMS agencies from around the world. The Academy teaches the system-level approach --- high-performance CPR, dispatcher-assisted CPR, continuous quality improvement, community CPR training --- that produces King County's results. Hundreds of EMS agencies from across the United States and from countries including Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Taiwan, and multiple European nations have sent teams to the Academy. Agencies that implement the Academy's recommendations consistently see improvements in their cardiac arrest survival rates.

The Academy's existence reflects a philosophy that is central to Medic One's culture: the goal is not for King County to have the best EMS system. The goal is for every community to have the best EMS system. If King County's methods work --- and they demonstrably do --- then those methods should be taught, shared, and replicated as widely as possible. This is not competitive advantage. It is public health.

### Research Contributions

The Medic One research program, based at the University of Washington, has produced foundational work in resuscitation science:

- **Dr. Peter Kudenchuk's** research on antiarrhythmic medications during cardiac arrest, including the landmark ALPS (Amiodarone, Lidocaine, or Placebo Study) trial, which established the superiority of amiodarone in shock-refractory ventricular fibrillation.

- **Dr. Thomas Rea's** work on dispatcher-assisted CPR, demonstrating that telephone instruction from 911 dispatchers can produce bystander CPR of sufficient quality to improve survival, even when the bystander has no prior training.

- **Dr. Graham Nichol's** Resuscitation Outcomes Consortium (ROC) studies, multi-center trials that used the infrastructure of high-performing EMS systems including King County to test resuscitation interventions at scale.

- **Compression quality research** using accelerometer-equipped defibrillator pads to measure chest compression rate, depth, and fraction in real time during resuscitations, producing data that has refined CPR guidelines worldwide.

- **Hypothermia protocols** for post-cardiac-arrest neuroprotection, studied and refined using Medic One's patient population and outcome data.

The cumulative impact of this research extends far beyond King County. The CPR guidelines published by the American Heart Association and the International Liaison Committee on Resuscitation incorporate findings from Medic One research. When a paramedic in Miami pushes amiodarone during a cardiac arrest, or a dispatcher in Chicago gives CPR instructions over the phone, or a firefighter in Houston pays attention to compression depth --- those practices trace back, in part, to research conducted at the University of Washington using data generated by the Medic One system.

## The Funding Model

King County's EMS system is funded through a dedicated property tax levy. The Medic One/EMS levy is approved by voters every six years and provides stable, predictable funding for paramedic services, BLS support, community CPR training, AED programs, and the quality infrastructure that ties the system together.

The levy model is critical. In most American communities, EMS funding competes with police, fire, roads, parks, and every other municipal priority in the general fund. When budgets tighten, EMS training is cut, equipment upgrades are deferred, and quality improvement programs are eliminated. The levy insulates King County's EMS system from this competition. Voters approve the levy because they understand what it buys --- they have seen the survival data, they know people who have been saved, and they have taken the CPR classes that the levy funds.

The most recent levy renewal, in 2019, passed with approximately 84 percent voter support. Eighty-four percent. In an era of intense political polarization, a tax levy for emergency medical services passes by a margin that most politicians would consider impossible for any ballot measure. This is not random. It is the predictable outcome of a system that delivers visible, measurable, life-saving results to its community and that maintains the public's trust through transparency, quality, and consistent performance.

## Why Other Systems Have Not Replicated It

If King County's model works this well --- and the data leaves no room for doubt --- then why has it not been replicated everywhere? The answer involves funding, politics, institutional structure, and the peculiar American resistance to copying things that work.

### Funding

The levy model requires voter approval, which requires public trust, which requires a track record. Communities that have never had a high-performing EMS system cannot point to results to justify the investment. King County's levy passes at 84 percent because voters have fifty years of evidence. A community starting from scratch would need to build the system first and demonstrate results before voters would fund it --- a chicken-and-egg problem that is difficult to solve.

### Fire Department Integration

King County's tiered model requires deep integration between fire department BLS and paramedic ALS. This integration is not automatic. In many American communities, fire departments and EMS agencies operate independently, with different governance, different funding, different culture, and different institutional priorities. Merging them into a coordinated tiered system requires political leadership, institutional cooperation, and often painful organizational change.

### Physician Oversight

The Medic One model's physician medical director is not a figurehead who signs protocols and attends occasional meetings. The medical director is clinically active, reviews every cardiac arrest, participates in training, and drives continuous quality improvement. This model requires a physician who is willing to invest enormous time in prehospital medicine, an academic institution (the University of Washington) that supports and values that investment, and a relationship between the medical director and the fire department that is collaborative rather than adversarial. This combination is rare.

### Quality Culture

The hardest thing to replicate is culture. Medic One's culture of continuous improvement --- where every cardiac arrest is reviewed, where data drives decisions, where the goal is to be better tomorrow than today --- is not a policy that can be adopted by resolution. It is a set of values, expectations, and norms that have been built over decades. It requires leadership that models the values, a workforce that internalizes them, and an institutional structure that protects them from political interference.

## PNW Pride

King County Medic One is one of the great public health achievements of the Pacific Northwest, and it operates with an humility that belies its stature. Most King County residents know that their EMS system is good. Few realize that it is, by most objective measures, the best in the world. The Harborview paramedic residency, the Medic 2 citizen CPR program, the Seattle Resuscitation Academy, the research enterprise, the voter-approved levy, the continuous quality improvement engine --- these are institutions that the region built, funded, defended, and refined over more than half a century.

The man who collapsed at Northgate Mall is alive because of a system. Not because of a single heroic paramedic, not because of a miracle drug, not because of luck. Because fifty-four years ago, a cardiologist at the University of Washington decided that cardiac arrest was a systems problem, and he built a system to solve it. Because voters approved the levies. Because hundreds of thousands of citizens took CPR classes. Because fire departments embraced their role as the first medical tier. Because paramedics accepted the rigor of a clinical residency. Because medical directors reviewed every case. Because the culture demanded excellence, measured it, and used the measurements to get better.

That system operates in our backyard. It saves lives every day. And it stands as proof that when a community decides to invest in doing something right --- really right, not just adequately, not just within budget, but to the highest standard that science and dedication can achieve --- the results are extraordinary.
