# CAHOOTS --- 35 Years of Proof That Another Way Works

## The Van That Changed Everything

On a winter night in 1989, a white van rolled out of the White Bird Clinic parking lot in Eugene, Oregon, carrying two people and an idea that most of America would not take seriously for another thirty years. The van held no weapons. Its occupants wore no body armor. They carried no handcuffs, no tasers, no authority to arrest anyone. What they carried was a first aid kit, blankets, water, naloxone, a binder of local resource contacts, and the radical premise that when a human being is in crisis, the most effective first response is not force --- it is care.

The program was called CAHOOTS --- Crisis Assistance Helping Out On The Streets. It was born from the culture of Eugene's community health movement, a city where the counterculture had put down institutional roots in the form of clinics, co-ops, and social service organizations that actually functioned. White Bird Clinic had been operating since 1970, providing free medical care, crisis counseling, and social services to people who fell through every other net. CAHOOTS was the clinic's answer to a question that the rest of the country was not yet ready to ask: what if the people we send to help people in crisis were actually trained to help people in crisis?

That van has been rolling every day since. Not as a pilot. Not as a grant-funded experiment with a sunset clause. As a permanent, integrated, dispatched-through-911 component of Eugene's emergency response infrastructure. Thirty-five years of continuous operation. More than half a million calls answered. And a body of evidence so overwhelming that it has become impossible to argue, in good faith, that armed police response is the only viable model for behavioral health emergencies.

## How It Actually Works

The mechanics of CAHOOTS are deceptively simple. The complexity is in the culture, the training, the relationships, and the decades of institutional knowledge that make the simple mechanics function.

### The Dispatch Decision

It starts at 911. When a call comes in to the Eugene-Springfield dispatch center, the call-taker evaluates it against a set of triage protocols. The key question is not "how serious is this?" but rather "what kind of response does this situation require?" If the call involves a mental health crisis, a substance use crisis, a welfare check, a dispute that is not violent, a person down who is not in immediate medical danger, homelessness-related distress, or a range of other behavioral and social service scenarios --- and crucially, if the situation does not involve weapons, active violence, or a crime in progress --- the dispatcher can route the call to CAHOOTS instead of police.

This triage decision is the fulcrum of the entire model. It requires dispatchers who understand the CAHOOTS capability set, who trust the teams to handle the calls they accept, and who have the judgment to distinguish between situations that need a clinician and situations that need an officer. Eugene has spent decades refining these protocols. The dispatchers know the CAHOOTS crews personally. The trust is earned, not assumed, and it is maintained through continuous feedback loops --- CAHOOTS teams debrief with dispatch, and dispatch adjusts protocols based on field experience.

The calls CAHOOTS responds to are not trivial. They include suicidal ideation (without active attempt or weapon), psychotic episodes (without violent behavior), overdoses, intoxication crises, welfare checks on people who have not been heard from, disoriented individuals, people in emotional distress in public spaces, and the thousand variations of human suffering that produce 911 calls every day in every city. These are calls that, in most American communities, would result in the dispatch of armed police officers.

### The Team

Every CAHOOTS shift deploys a two-person team: one medic and one crisis worker.

The **medic** is a certified EMT or, in some cases, a nurse. They handle medical assessment --- vital signs, level of consciousness, signs of overdose or acute medical emergency, injuries that need treatment. They carry the clinical authority to make transport decisions: does this person need an emergency room, or can they be stabilized in the field? The medic also administers naloxone for opioid overdoses, provides basic wound care, and assesses for medical conditions that may be presenting as psychiatric symptoms --- hypoglycemia mimicking intoxication, urinary tract infections causing confusion in elderly patients, thyroid crises producing anxiety and agitation.

The **crisis worker** is the lead on behavioral health calls. They have training and experience in mental health crisis intervention, de-escalation, motivational interviewing, harm reduction, and the local landscape of social services. Many crisis workers have lived experience --- they have been through their own mental health crises, their own substance use struggles, their own periods of homelessness. This is not incidental to their effectiveness. When a person in crisis sees someone who has been where they are, who speaks about it without shame, who is standing upright and employed and present --- that is a different encounter than one conducted by a uniformed officer reading from a training module.

The crisis worker's tools are conversation, patience, assessment, and connection. They talk to the person. They listen. They assess risk --- is this person an immediate danger to themselves or others? They identify needs --- does this person need medical care, shelter, food, medication, a safe place to sober up, a warm bed, someone to call? And they provide warm handoffs --- not a pamphlet, not a phone number, but an actual connection to a specific service, often including transport to that service.

### The Van

The CAHOOTS van is deliberately non-threatening. It is not a patrol car. It does not have a cage in the back, light bars on the roof, or a computer terminal running criminal history queries. It is a van. It carries first aid supplies, naloxone, blankets, water, snacks, hygiene kits, clean socks, resource guides, and a cell phone. The absence of law enforcement trappings is not an oversight --- it is the design. The van communicates, by its appearance, that the people inside it are here to help, not to arrest, detain, or control.

This matters more than most policy discussions acknowledge. A person in a psychotic episode who sees flashing lights and uniformed officers may interpret that as confirmation of persecutory delusions. A person contemplating suicide who sees police may fear involuntary commitment --- a reasonable fear, since that is what often happens --- and flee or escalate. A person using drugs who sees law enforcement will not disclose what they have taken, which means the medic cannot provide appropriate treatment. The van's appearance is not cosmetic. It is clinical. It creates the conditions under which de-escalation, honest communication, and voluntary engagement become possible.

## The Numbers

CAHOOTS operates on data, and the data is unambiguous.

### Call Volume

In a typical year, CAHOOTS handles approximately 24,000 calls --- roughly 17 percent of the total call volume that would otherwise go to the Eugene Police Department. That percentage has grown steadily over the decades as dispatch protocols have expanded and as the community has learned what CAHOOTS can handle. In the early years, the percentage was much smaller. It grew because CAHOOTS demonstrated, call by call, year by year, that it could handle the work safely and effectively.

Seventeen percent of call volume is not a marginal contribution. It represents a significant reduction in the number of calls that police officers must respond to, freeing them to focus on the calls that actually require law enforcement --- violent crimes, property crimes, traffic enforcement, warrant service. Every CAHOOTS call is a call that a police officer does not have to take. Every hour a CAHOOTS team spends with a person in mental health crisis is an hour that a patrol officer can spend on actual police work.

### Budget

CAHOOTS operates on an annual budget of approximately $2.1 million. The Eugene Police Department's annual budget exceeds $90 million. CAHOOTS handles 17 percent of the call volume for approximately 2.3 percent of the cost. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison --- CAHOOTS does not need patrol cars, firearms, body armor, body cameras, or the training infrastructure required for armed response --- but the disparity is instructive. Civilian crisis response is dramatically cheaper than police response, per call, because the personnel costs are lower, the equipment costs are lower, and the liability costs are lower.

### Police Backup Rate

Of the approximately 24,000 calls CAHOOTS handles annually, fewer than 150 --- less than one percent --- require police backup. This is the statistic that should end every argument about whether civilian crisis response is safe. In more than 35 years of operation, across hundreds of thousands of calls, CAHOOTS teams have encountered situations requiring armed intervention less than one percent of the time. The other 99 percent of calls were resolved by two unarmed civilians in a van.

The backup calls are not failures. They are the system working as designed. CAHOOTS was never intended to handle armed situations, active violence, or crime in progress. The triage protocols exist to route those calls to police. When a CAHOOTS team arrives and discovers that a situation has escalated beyond their scope --- a weapon is present, a person is physically violent, a crime is being committed --- they withdraw and call for police. This is not a limitation of the model. It is the model. The 17 percent of calls that CAHOOTS handles are specifically the calls that do not need police. The backup rate confirms that the triage protocols are accurate.

### Use of Force

Zero. In more than three decades of operation, CAHOOTS has not had a single serious use-of-force incident. Not one. This is not because CAHOOTS avoids difficult calls. It is because the entire design of the response --- unarmed personnel, non-threatening vehicle, clinical training, voluntary engagement --- de-escalates situations that would predictably escalate under armed law enforcement response.

When a person in crisis sees a police officer, they see authority, coercion, the possibility of arrest, the possibility of being shot. Their stress response activates. Their behavior becomes more erratic. The officer reads that erratic behavior as a threat. The feedback loop accelerates toward violence. When the same person sees a CAHOOTS worker, they see a civilian, a helper, someone without the power to arrest them. The stress response is lower. The behavioral spiral does not engage. De-escalation happens not because CAHOOTS workers are better at talking people down --- though they often are --- but because the encounter begins at a fundamentally lower level of tension.

### Cost Savings

An independent analysis estimated that CAHOOTS saves the city of Eugene approximately $8.5 million per year in avoided costs. The savings break down across multiple categories:

- **Avoided emergency room visits:** Many CAHOOTS calls would otherwise result in ambulance transport to the ER, where a person in mental health crisis might spend 8-12 hours waiting for a psychiatric evaluation, at a cost of thousands of dollars per visit. CAHOOTS resolves many of these calls in the field or connects people directly to appropriate services, bypassing the ER entirely.

- **Avoided jail bookings:** People in mental health or substance use crisis who encounter police are frequently arrested --- for trespassing, disorderly conduct, resisting arrest, or other charges that are essentially criminalized symptoms. Each booking costs the county hundreds of dollars and adds to jail overcrowding. CAHOOTS diverts these encounters away from the criminal justice system entirely.

- **Avoided police overtime:** Every call that CAHOOTS handles is a call that a police officer does not have to respond to, process, document, and follow up on. At police officer pay rates, 24,000 calls represent a substantial labor cost savings.

- **Avoided lawsuits:** Use-of-force incidents during mental health calls generate lawsuits. Cities pay millions of dollars annually in settlements and judgments arising from police encounters with people in crisis. CAHOOTS has generated zero such liability in 35 years.

## Why It Works

The question is not really why CAHOOTS works. The question is why anyone would expect armed response to work better for behavioral health emergencies. The answer to why CAHOOTS works is straightforward, and it applies universally.

### The Right People for the Job

CAHOOTS sends people trained in crisis intervention to crisis intervention calls. This should not be revolutionary, but in the context of American emergency response, it is. We do not send firefighters to investigate burglaries. We do not send police officers to perform surgery. But we routinely send police officers to manage mental health emergencies, despite the fact that their training, tools, institutional culture, and legal authority are designed for an entirely different purpose.

CAHOOTS crisis workers know what a psychotic episode looks like. They know the difference between a panic attack and a cardiac event. They know how methamphetamine psychosis presents differently from schizophrenia. They know that a person who is shouting and pacing is often more frightened than dangerous. They know that suicidal ideation is a symptom, not a crime. They know these things because their job is to know them, and they have been selected and trained on that basis.

### Voluntary Engagement

CAHOOTS has no coercive authority. They cannot arrest, detain, or compel anyone to do anything. This is routinely cited as a limitation. It is, in fact, the core of their effectiveness. Because the person in crisis knows that the CAHOOTS worker cannot force them to do anything, they are more likely to engage voluntarily. Voluntary engagement produces better outcomes than coerced compliance in virtually every therapeutic context. People who choose to accept help are more likely to follow through with referrals, more likely to return for follow-up services, and more likely to call CAHOOTS again the next time they are in crisis rather than suffering in silence because they fear the consequences of calling 911.

### Institutional Knowledge

CAHOOTS has been operating in the same community for more than three decades. The crisis workers know the streets. They know the shelters and which ones have beds tonight. They know the treatment programs and which ones have openings. They know the frequent callers by name, know their histories, know their triggers, know what worked last time and what did not. This institutional knowledge cannot be replicated by a training manual. It is built over years and decades of continuous presence in the community.

The relationship between CAHOOTS and Eugene's social service ecosystem is symbiotic. CAHOOTS refers people to services, and those services trust CAHOOTS assessments. When a CAHOOTS worker calls a shelter and says "I have someone who needs a bed, they are not intoxicated, they will be appropriate for your program," the shelter trusts that assessment because it has been accurate thousands of times before. These relationships are the invisible infrastructure that makes warm handoffs possible.

## The Replication Wave

For three decades, CAHOOTS was essentially alone. Other cities knew about it. Researchers studied it. Policy papers cited it. But almost no one replicated it. The reasons were political, institutional, and cultural. Police unions opposed civilian response programs as threats to their jurisdiction and budget. City councils feared being blamed if a civilian team was harmed. The assumption that public safety requires armed response was so deeply embedded that even favorable evidence could not dislodge it.

Then came 2020. The murder of George Floyd, the national reckoning with policing, and the urgent public demand for alternatives created a political opening that thirty years of evidence had failed to produce. Cities that had studied CAHOOTS for years suddenly had the political will to act.

### Denver STAR (2020)

Denver's Support Team Assisted Response launched in June 2020 as a six-month pilot. STAR sends a paramedic and a mental health clinician to low-acuity 911 calls --- the same model as CAHOOTS, adapted to Denver's dispatch infrastructure. The pilot was immediately successful. STAR handled hundreds of calls with zero arrests, zero use of force, and zero requests for police backup. The program was made permanent and expanded. By 2023, STAR was operating multiple teams across the city and handling a growing percentage of Denver's behavioral health calls.

### Portland Street Response (2021)

Portland launched its Street Response program in February 2021, initially serving the Lents neighborhood in Southeast Portland. The model pairs a community health worker with a firefighter/EMT, responding to welfare checks, behavioral health calls, and non-emergency medical situations. The program expanded citywide in 2023 after its pilot phase demonstrated results consistent with CAHOOTS --- high call resolution rates, minimal police backup requests, and strong community satisfaction.

Portland Street Response is particularly interesting because it emerged from Portland's Fire Bureau rather than a community health organization. This institutional home gives it access to fire department dispatch infrastructure, which is more tightly integrated with 911 than external organization dispatch would be. It also gives the program a degree of institutional legitimacy within the emergency services world that pure civilian programs sometimes struggle to achieve.

### Olympia Crisis Response Unit (2021)

Olympia, Washington, launched its Crisis Response Unit in 2021, drawing explicitly on the CAHOOTS model. The CRU pairs a behavioral health specialist with an EMT to respond to mental health, substance use, and welfare check calls. As a smaller city with a significant population of people experiencing homelessness, Olympia's CRU handles a call mix that is heavily weighted toward outreach and service connection.

### Other Programs

The wave has continued. San Francisco's Street Crisis Response Team. Albuquerque's Community Safety Department and its Albuquerque Community Safety program. Rochester, New York's Person in Crisis team. Austin's CORE program. Los Angeles, Oakland, Indianapolis, Minneapolis, Tucson, and dozens of other cities have launched or are developing civilian crisis response programs, each adapting the core CAHOOTS principles to local conditions, dispatch systems, and service landscapes.

## What CAHOOTS Cannot Do

An honest assessment of CAHOOTS requires acknowledging its limitations, because the limitations define the model just as much as the capabilities do.

CAHOOTS cannot respond to situations involving weapons. If a person in crisis has a firearm, a knife, or any object being used as a weapon, that call goes to police. CAHOOTS workers are unarmed civilians. They cannot safely manage armed encounters, and they should not be expected to.

CAHOOTS cannot respond to active violence. If a person is physically assaulting someone, the immediate priority is stopping the assault, which requires the authority and capability to use force. CAHOOTS has neither.

CAHOOTS cannot respond to crime in progress. Burglary, robbery, assault, domestic violence with active physical aggression --- these are law enforcement calls. CAHOOTS does not replace law enforcement. It handles the calls that law enforcement was never designed for.

CAHOOTS cannot involuntarily commit anyone. If a person meets the criteria for involuntary psychiatric hold --- imminent danger to self or others --- CAHOOTS can call for police to initiate the hold, but they cannot do it themselves. In practice, CAHOOTS workers often succeed in persuading people to accept voluntary treatment, which produces better outcomes than involuntary commitment, but the option of forced intervention is not available to them.

CAHOOTS cannot solve homelessness, addiction, or chronic mental illness. It can connect people to services in the moment of crisis. It can stabilize. It can provide a warm handoff. But the services have to exist for the handoff to work, and in many communities, the services are inadequate, underfunded, or nonexistent. CAHOOTS is crisis response, not systemic reform. It is an essential piece of a larger infrastructure that most communities have not built.

These limitations are not arguments against CAHOOTS. They are arguments for appropriate triage. The 17 percent of calls that CAHOOTS handles are the 17 percent that genuinely do not need armed response. The other 83 percent still go to police, fire, and EMS. The model does not defund or replace police. It redirects a specific, identifiable category of calls to a more appropriate response.

## The Honest Assessment

Thirty-five years. Hundreds of thousands of calls. Zero use-of-force incidents. Less than one percent police backup rate. $2.1 million annual budget handling work that would cost many times more through police response. $8.5 million in estimated annual cost savings. A growing national replication movement.

The evidence is not ambiguous. For the specific category of calls that CAHOOTS handles --- non-violent behavioral health crises, welfare checks, substance use emergencies, people in distress --- civilian crisis response is safer, cheaper, more effective, and more humane than armed police response. This is not ideology. It is thirty-five years of operational data.

The honest assessment also requires acknowledging that CAHOOTS exists in Eugene because Eugene built it, over decades, with sustained political will and community investment. Replication is not as simple as buying a van and hiring two people. It requires dispatcher training, community trust, institutional relationships with service providers, feedback loops between field teams and dispatch, and a political culture willing to defend the program when critics --- and there are always critics --- demand its elimination after the first difficult incident.

CAHOOTS is proof of concept. It is proof that civilian crisis response works, at scale, sustainably, for decades. It is proof that for the right calls, another way is not just possible but demonstrably superior to the default. What remains is the political and institutional work of making that proof matter in the hundreds of communities that still send armed officers to every 911 call, including the ones where what the caller needs is not a gun and a badge but a blanket, a bottle of water, and someone who knows how to listen.
