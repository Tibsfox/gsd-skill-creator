# Portland Street Response --- CAHOOTS for Portland

## The City That Needed This Yesterday

Portland, Oregon, has always been a city of contradictions. A place that prides itself on progressive values while presiding over one of the worst homelessness crises on the West Coast. A city where you can get a pour-over coffee made with single-origin beans from a roaster who bikes to work, and then step over someone sleeping in a doorway on your way out. A city that talks about compassion at the ballot box and then watches its police bureau cycle through crisis after crisis --- use-of-force scandals, leadership turnover, staffing shortages so severe that response times for priority calls doubled.

Into this mess stepped Portland Street Response. Not as a solution to all of Portland's problems --- no single program can be that --- but as an attempt to answer one specific question that had been festering for decades: when someone calls 911 about a person who is not violent, not armed, but clearly in crisis --- sitting on a curb talking to no one, passed out in a park, wandering in traffic, crying on a bench --- who should actually show up?

For most of Portland's history, the answer was the same as everywhere else: police, or fire, or both. Armed responders dispatched to situations that did not require arms. Medics pulling up to calls that were not medical emergencies in any traditional sense. Patrol officers spending twenty, thirty, forty minutes on scenes that would have been better served by a social worker with a warm blanket and a phone full of shelter numbers. Everyone involved --- the officers, the firefighters, the paramedics, the people in crisis --- knew this was wrong. Knowing it was wrong and building something different are separated by years of political will, funding battles, and institutional inertia.

Portland Street Response was the city's attempt to close that gap. It launched in February 2021, in the Lents neighborhood of Southeast Portland, as a pilot program. It drew explicitly on the CAHOOTS model that had been running ninety minutes south in Eugene for over three decades. And it arrived into one of the most turbulent periods in Portland's modern history --- a city still reeling from the 2020 protests, a police bureau hemorrhaging officers, a homelessness crisis accelerating through the pandemic, and a political environment where every public safety decision was filtered through the lens of a community deeply divided about what policing should look like.

The timing was terrible. The timing was also perfect.

## The Model

Portland Street Response operates under Portland Fire and Rescue, which gives it an institutional home that matters more than most people realize. Housing the program within the fire bureau --- rather than within police, or as a standalone nonprofit, or within a social services agency --- provided three critical advantages: access to the 911 dispatch system, the operational credibility of a uniformed public safety agency, and a funding stream that did not depend on grant cycles or the political whims of the City Council.

### The Team

Each PSR team consists of two people: a community health worker and a firefighter/EMT. This is a deliberate hybrid --- not purely civilian like CAHOOTS, not purely fire-service like a standard medic unit. The community health worker brings behavioral health training, social service navigation skills, and often personal experience with the communities PSR serves. The firefighter/EMT brings medical assessment capability, radio communication with dispatch, and the institutional weight of Portland Fire and Rescue.

The community health workers are the lead on most calls. They are the ones who sit on the curb next to someone and have a conversation. They are the ones who know which shelters have beds tonight, which treatment programs have openings, which day centers serve meals at what hours. They carry resource guides that are updated regularly --- because in Portland's social service landscape, what was true last month may not be true this month. Programs open, programs close, programs run out of funding, programs move locations. The community health worker's value is not just knowing that a resource exists but knowing whether it is actually available right now, for this person, with these specific needs.

The firefighter/EMT provides the medical screen that is essential for safe triage. Many people in crisis on Portland's streets have underlying medical conditions that present as behavioral health issues. Hypoglycemia mimics intoxication. Urinary tract infections cause confusion in elderly patients. Head injuries produce disorientation. A wound that has gone untreated for a week produces delirium from infection. The EMT assesses whether the person needs medical transport --- in which case PSR calls an ambulance --- or whether the crisis is social, behavioral, or situational --- in which case the community health worker takes the lead.

### The Call Types

PSR responds to calls that fall into a specific category: non-emergency situations involving behavioral health, homelessness, substance use, or welfare checks where there is no indication of weapons, active violence, or a crime in progress. The dispatch protocols mirror CAHOOTS: the 911 call-taker evaluates the call against triage criteria and routes it to PSR when appropriate.

The calls look like this: A person sitting on a sidewalk, not responding to passersby. A welfare check on someone who has been sleeping in a park for three days. A person talking to themselves, agitated but not aggressive. Someone who appears intoxicated and unable to get home. A report of someone crying on a bridge. A community member concerned about a neighbor they have not seen in a week. These are the calls that, before PSR, would have generated a police response --- an officer arriving in a marked car with a gun on their hip, often with no training in crisis intervention, and with a set of options (arrest, detain, transport to ER, leave) that rarely addressed the actual need.

## The Lents Pilot

The choice of Lents as the pilot neighborhood was deliberate. Lents, in Southeast Portland's 82nd Avenue corridor, is one of the most diverse and economically challenged neighborhoods in the city. It has a significant unhoused population, high call volumes for behavioral health and welfare check dispatches, and a community that had been vocal about wanting alternatives to police response for non-criminal situations. Lents was also geographically compact enough to be serviceable by a single PSR team during the pilot phase --- important for a program that needed to demonstrate results before scaling.

The pilot launched on February 18, 2021, with a single team operating during daytime hours. The early months were a period of learning --- refining dispatch protocols, building relationships with Lents community members, understanding the specific needs and rhythms of the neighborhood, and establishing trust with the 911 dispatchers who controlled the flow of calls.

Trust with dispatch is worth pausing on. The CAHOOTS model in Eugene works because dispatchers there have had thirty-five years to learn what CAHOOTS teams can handle. Portland's dispatchers were working with a brand-new program, with team members they did not know, operating in a political environment where any failure would be amplified. Early dispatch of PSR calls was conservative --- dispatchers erred on the side of sending police, which meant PSR's initial call volume was lower than it might have been. As PSR teams demonstrated competence, reliability, and good judgment --- call after call, day after day --- dispatchers expanded the range of situations they would route to PSR. This organic trust-building cannot be shortcut. It is the same process that CAHOOTS went through in Eugene in 1989, and it takes time.

The pilot results were strong. In its first year, PSR handled over 4,300 calls. The vast majority were resolved on scene --- the person was connected to services, provided with supplies, or simply talked through a difficult moment. PSR requested police backup on fewer than 1% of calls, mirroring CAHOOTS's backup rate. No PSR team member was injured. No use-of-force incidents occurred. Community satisfaction, measured through follow-up surveys and public feedback sessions, was high.

The data from the pilot was good enough that the City Council voted to expand the program citywide.

## Citywide Expansion

PSR expanded beyond Lents in stages through 2022 and 2023, adding teams to cover Portland's six emergency response districts. The expansion brought new challenges that the Lents pilot had not fully surfaced.

**Geographic scale.** Portland is 145 square miles. Covering the entire city with PSR teams requires enough units to maintain reasonable response times while handling a call volume that is dramatically larger than Lents alone. The expansion added teams incrementally, and there were periods during the rollout when PSR coverage was thin in parts of the city --- a team might be committed to a call in North Portland while a PSR-appropriate call came in on the east side, and the call would default back to police or fire because no PSR team was available.

**Staffing.** Finding community health workers with the right combination of behavioral health training, crisis intervention experience, cultural competence, physical stamina, and willingness to do street-level work in all weather is not easy. The pay, while better than many social service positions, competes with private-sector behavioral health jobs that do not require working outdoors in Portland rain at six in the morning next to a freeway on-ramp. Retention has been a challenge common to every alternative response program in the country --- the work is emotionally demanding, and burnout is real.

**The political environment.** Portland's City Council politics during 2021-2023 were, to use a technical term, a mess. The city was simultaneously debating police funding, implementing a new form of city government, dealing with a recall effort against the district attorney, and navigating a public discourse about homelessness that swung between compassion and fury depending on the news cycle. PSR became a political football at times --- championed by progressives as proof that alternatives to policing work, attacked by critics who argued the city was replacing police with social workers while violent crime rose.

The honest assessment: PSR's expansion happened during a period when Portland was experiencing significant increases in gun violence, property crime, and visible homelessness simultaneously. Critics conflated these trends with PSR's existence, arguing that diverting resources from police contributed to public safety problems. This argument misunderstands what PSR does --- PSR handles calls that police are not trained for and do not want --- but the political narrative was powerful, and it created headwinds for the program's funding and expansion.

## The Numbers

In its first two years of operation, Portland Street Response handled over 10,000 calls. The statistics tell a consistent story:

**Police backup rate: less than 1%.** Of the thousands of calls PSR responded to, the number requiring police involvement was vanishingly small. This is the number that matters most, because it answers the safety question directly: is it safe to send unarmed civilian teams to these calls? The data says yes, overwhelmingly.

**Service connections.** PSR teams connected thousands of people with services --- shelter referrals, behavioral health treatment, medical care, substance use programs, food assistance, clothing, and the dozens of other resources that Portland's social service landscape offers (imperfectly, insufficiently, but offers). A single PSR interaction might result in a person getting into a shelter bed that night, or making an appointment at a clinic the following week, or simply receiving a pair of dry socks and a bottle of water and the knowledge that someone cared enough to stop.

**Call resolution on scene.** The vast majority of PSR calls were resolved without transport to a hospital, without arrest, without involuntary commitment. The person was helped where they were, in the community, by people trained to help them. This is what community-based crisis response looks like: not dramatic interventions, but quiet, unglamorous, person-to-person connection.

**Cost.** PSR operates at a fraction of the per-call cost of police or fire response. A PSR team's fully loaded cost --- salaries, vehicle, equipment, supervision, overhead --- is dramatically lower than a police patrol unit's. The city has not published a comprehensive cost-comparison analysis as rigorous as Eugene's $8.5 million savings estimate for CAHOOTS, but the economics are directionally identical: civilian teams cost less to deploy, generate less downstream cost (no arrests to process, fewer ER transports), and produce less liability exposure.

## What Worked

**The fire bureau home.** Housing PSR within Portland Fire and Rescue gave the program institutional legitimacy, dispatch integration, and a funding stream that alternative models (standalone nonprofit, police-housed) would not have provided. It also gave PSR access to fire bureau infrastructure --- stations for team deployment, radio communications, data systems, and the operational culture of an organization that already understood emergency response.

**The hybrid team model.** The community health worker / firefighter-EMT pairing gives PSR a medical safety net that purely civilian models lack. The EMT can identify medical emergencies that present as behavioral crises. The community health worker can provide the social service navigation and therapeutic engagement that EMTs are not trained for. The combination is more than the sum of its parts.

**Community trust.** PSR built genuine trust in the communities it serves --- particularly in the unhoused community, which has deep and justified distrust of police. People who would not call 911 if the response was going to be a police officer might call if they know PSR is coming. People in crisis who would flee from a patrol car might stay and talk to a PSR team arriving in a van without light bars.

**Dispatch integration.** Being dispatched through the 911 system, rather than operating as a parallel system that people have to know about and call separately, is critical. Most people in crisis or witnessing someone in crisis call 911. If the alternative response is not reachable through 911, it does not reach the people who need it.

## What Did Not Work --- or Did Not Work Yet

**Hours of operation.** During the pilot and early expansion, PSR operated primarily during daytime hours. Mental health crises do not respect business hours. The 2 AM call about a person standing on a bridge --- the call that opens the mental health crisis document in this series --- needs a PSR-type response as much as the 2 PM call about a person sitting on a curb. Expanding to 24/7 coverage requires more teams, more staff, more funding, and is an ongoing challenge.

**Volume versus capacity.** Even with citywide expansion, PSR cannot handle all of the calls that are appropriate for its model. When PSR teams are committed to calls, subsequent PSR-appropriate calls default back to police or fire. The program needs more units to reduce this overflow, but more units require more funding, and funding in Portland's strained budget is a constant fight.

**The service ecosystem behind PSR.** PSR can connect people to services, but it cannot create services that do not exist. Portland's shelter capacity is insufficient. Behavioral health treatment wait times are weeks to months. Substance use treatment beds are scarce. Affordable housing is a crisis unto itself. PSR teams encounter the same limits that paramedics have encountered for decades --- you can do everything right on the call, connect the person to the best available resource, and know that the resource is inadequate. The revolving door does not stop spinning just because the people knocking on it are community health workers instead of police officers.

**Political sustainability.** Portland's political environment is volatile. Programs that depend on City Council support are vulnerable to political shifts, and Portland's 2024 transition to a new form of city government (a city manager system with an expanded council elected by district) created uncertainty about every city program's future. PSR's survival depends on demonstrated results, community support, and institutional champions --- and on those champions remaining in positions of influence.

## The PNW Movement

Portland Street Response is not an isolated experiment. It is part of a regional movement that the Pacific Northwest has been leading for decades.

CAHOOTS in Eugene has been running since 1989 --- thirty-five years of proof that civilian crisis response works. Springfield, Eugene's neighbor, has launched its own version. The model is expanding within Lane County.

Olympia's Crisis Response Unit, launched in 2021, pairs behavioral health specialists with EMTs through the Olympia Fire Department. Olympia's CRU is a co-responder model led from the fire service side rather than the police side --- a distinction that changes the encounter dynamics meaningfully.

Seattle's LEAD program takes a different approach --- diverting people from the criminal justice system into case management at the point of police contact. The Downtown Emergency Service Center (DESC) operates crisis stabilization and permanent supportive housing. Crisis Connections runs the regional crisis line. Seattle's ecosystem is more complex and more institutionally fragmented than Eugene's or Portland's, but the components are there.

The I-5 corridor from Eugene to Seattle is, by any measure, the most advanced laboratory for alternative crisis response in the United States. CAHOOTS proved the concept. Portland Street Response proved it could be adapted to a larger, more complex city. Olympia proved it could work in a smaller city with a different institutional home. Seattle proved the supporting infrastructure --- diversion, stabilization, housing --- could be built at metropolitan scale.

What the PNW has not yet proved is that these programs can survive without the progressive political culture that incubated them. The next test is whether the model can be exported to cities where the political environment is less hospitable, the institutional inertia is stronger, and the funding battles are fiercer. Denver's STAR program and Albuquerque's Community Safety Department suggest the answer is yes --- but the PNW remains the place where this movement was born, where the evidence is deepest, and where the proof of concept is most mature.

## The Honest Assessment

Portland Street Response is not perfect. It is underfunded, understaffed, and operating in a city whose social service infrastructure is insufficient to support the outcomes that PSR's teams are trying to achieve. Its political future is uncertain. Its expansion has been slower than advocates wanted and faster than skeptics thought wise.

But it works.

It works in the way that CAHOOTS works in Eugene: not by solving homelessness, or addiction, or mental illness, or poverty, but by sending the right people to the right calls with the right skills and the right approach. By treating people in crisis as people in crisis rather than as suspects, or nuisances, or problems to be moved along. By doing the quiet, unglamorous, exhausting work of sitting on a curb in the rain and talking to someone who has been invisible to every other system in their life.

Portland Street Response handles calls that police officers do not want, that firefighters are not trained for, and that paramedics cannot resolve with a stretcher and a siren. It handles them safely, effectively, and cheaply. It handles them with compassion that the system it replaces --- through no fault of the individual officers and medics --- was structurally incapable of providing.

For the person who served thirty years as a firefighter and paramedic, responding to mental health calls with a defibrillator and a stethoscope because no one else was available, Portland Street Response is what the next chapter looks like. Not the end of the story. Not the solution to everything. But a real, operating, evidence-based answer to one of the most painful questions the fire service has been asking for decades: who should we send when the person on the other end of the call does not need a fire truck?

Someone who can help.
