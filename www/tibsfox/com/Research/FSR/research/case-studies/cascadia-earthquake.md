# The Big One --- Cascadia Subduction Zone Preparedness

## The Earthquake That Already Happened

At approximately 9 PM Pacific Time on January 26, 1700, the Cascadia Subduction Zone ruptured along its full 600-mile length. The magnitude was approximately 9.0 --- the same scale as the 2011 Tohoku earthquake that devastated Japan. The shaking lasted somewhere between three and five minutes. The ground in what is now western Washington and Oregon moved laterally by as much as 60 feet. The coastal land dropped by as much as 6 feet in some areas, drowning tidal forests that would stand for centuries as ghost forests --- the bleached skeletons of spruce and cedar preserved in tidal mud, their death rings readable by dendrochronologists like a timestamp written in wood.

We know the date because the tsunami crossed the Pacific. Japanese historical records --- meticulous, maintained for centuries in a civilization that understood tsunamis intimately --- document an orphan tsunami that struck the coast of Honshu on January 27, 1700. An orphan tsunami is one with no corresponding local earthquake. The Japanese knew their own seismology well enough to recognize that this wave came from somewhere else. Three centuries later, geologists Brian Atwater and others traced the wave backward across the Pacific using propagation models and matched it to the subduction zone rupture. The ghost forests confirmed it: radiocarbon dating and tree ring analysis placed the coastal forest death at the winter of 1699-1700.

The Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest Coast had their own records. Oral traditions among the Huu-ay-aht, Makah, Quilleute, and other nations describe a great earthquake and flood that devastated coastal villages. These accounts --- transmitted across twelve generations --- describe shaking so violent that people could not stand, followed by a wall of water that swept away everything. The Makah at Neah Bay tell of a night when the ocean receded and then returned with catastrophic force. These are not myths. They are eyewitness accounts of the last Cascadia event, preserved in oral tradition for over three hundred years.

This is the earthquake we are waiting for. Not a hypothetical. Not a model. An event that has happened before, that the geological record shows has happened repeatedly at intervals of 200 to 600 years, and that will happen again. The last one was 326 years ago. The next one is not a question of if. It is a question of when.

## What the Geology Says

The Cascadia Subduction Zone runs from Cape Mendocino in northern California to northern Vancouver Island in British Columbia --- roughly 700 miles of tectonic boundary where the Juan de Fuca plate, a remnant of the once-vast Farallon plate, slides beneath the North American plate at a rate of approximately 40 millimeters per year. That is about the rate your fingernails grow. It does not sound dramatic. But the plates are not sliding smoothly. They are locked together by friction, and the stress accumulates.

The locked zone --- the section of the fault where the plates are stuck and strain is building --- extends from roughly 10 to 30 kilometers depth, running offshore from the coast. GPS stations along the Pacific Northwest coast measure the slow, relentless compression as the locked zone accumulates energy. The coast of Oregon is being pushed eastward by millimeters per year. When the fault ruptures, that accumulated compression releases in seconds. The coast snaps westward. The locked zone slips. Energy equivalent to thousands of nuclear weapons is released into the earth's crust.

The geological record, painstakingly assembled from cores drilled into coastal marshes, offshore sediment layers, and the ubiquitous ghost forests, shows that full-margin Cascadia ruptures have occurred 41 times in the last 10,000 years. The average interval is approximately 240 years, but the distribution is not regular --- the intervals range from as short as 100 years to as long as 600 years. Partial ruptures, involving only a portion of the fault, occur more frequently and produce smaller but still damaging earthquakes in the magnitude 8.0 to 8.5 range.

The current interval --- 326 years since the last full rupture --- is within the historical range but approaching the longer end. This does not mean the earthquake is "overdue" in a strict scientific sense --- geological processes do not run on schedules --- but it means we are within the window. The probability of a full Cascadia rupture in the next 50 years is estimated at 7 to 15 percent by the USGS. The probability of a magnitude 8.0 or greater event from a partial rupture is higher, estimated at 37 percent in the same timeframe.

Those are not small numbers. Seven to 15 percent is roughly the probability that a coin flipped four times will come up heads every time. Thirty-seven percent is roughly the probability of rolling a 1 or 2 on a standard die. These are risks that any other domain --- aviation, nuclear power, financial markets --- would treat as requiring serious mitigation. The Pacific Northwest has been slow to treat them that way.

## Four Minutes of Shaking

When the Cascadia Subduction Zone ruptures, the shaking will last between three and five minutes. For context: the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake in San Francisco lasted 15 seconds. The 1994 Northridge earthquake lasted 10 to 20 seconds. Cascadia will shake for 180 to 300 seconds. At magnitude 9.0, the energy release is roughly 1,000 times greater than Northridge.

In those minutes, the built environment of western Washington and Oregon will be tested against forces it was largely not designed for. The consequences will unfold in categories.

### Unreinforced Masonry

The most dangerous buildings in the Cascadia scenario are unreinforced masonry (URM) structures --- brick buildings with no internal steel reinforcement. URMs fail catastrophically in earthquakes: the mortar joints crack, the bricks separate, and the walls collapse outward, burying sidewalks, streets, and anyone standing nearby. Portland has approximately 1,800 URM buildings, many in the central city, the Pearl District, and historic neighborhoods. Seattle has hundreds. Many of these buildings house businesses, apartments, and cultural institutions. Some have been retrofitted. Many have not. The seismic retrofit mandate that Portland adopted in 2018 (Ordinance 188964) requires URM building owners to evaluate and retrofit their buildings, but the compliance timeline extends decades into the future. The earthquake will not wait for the retrofit schedule.

### Liquefaction

Liquefaction occurs when saturated, loose soil loses its bearing capacity during shaking and behaves like a liquid. The areas most vulnerable to liquefaction are those built on fill --- dredge spoils, industrial fill, reclaimed tidelands. In Seattle, this means SoDo, Harbor Island, the industrial district south of downtown, portions of Pioneer Square, and the Duwamish River corridor. In Portland, this means the fills along the Willamette River, the industrial areas of Northwest Portland, and portions of the central east side. In Tacoma, the tideflats. In Olympia, the areas around Capitol Lake and the Deschutes River estuary.

When these areas liquefy, buildings tilt, roads buckle, utilities rupture, and the ground itself becomes unreliable as a surface to stand on, let alone to drive on or stage emergency operations from. The USGS has mapped liquefaction susceptibility across the Puget Sound region, and the high-susceptibility zones correspond almost exactly to the areas where the region's port facilities, industrial infrastructure, and transportation corridors are concentrated. The economic heart of the Pacific Northwest is built on the ground most likely to fail.

### Bridges and Transportation

The Pacific Northwest's transportation network is held together by bridges, and bridges are particularly vulnerable to seismic forces. The Alaskan Way Viaduct in Seattle was demolished and replaced in part because of seismic vulnerability (the tunnel that replaced it was designed to a higher seismic standard). But hundreds of bridges remain --- the Ship Canal Bridge, the I-90 floating bridge, the West Seattle Bridge, the Aurora Bridge, the I-5 bridges over the Skagit, Snohomish, and Puyallup rivers. In Portland, the Willamette River bridges --- Burnside, Morrison, Hawthorne, Marquam, Fremont, St. Johns --- are lifeline transportation links. The Marquam Bridge carries I-5 through downtown Portland.

A full Cascadia event will damage or destroy an unknown but significant number of these bridges. The Oregon Department of Transportation estimated in the Oregon Resilience Plan that a Cascadia event would render all bridges on I-5 between Salem and the Columbia River unusable. That includes the Interstate Bridge connecting Portland to Vancouver, Washington. North-south transportation through the Willamette Valley would be severed.

For fire departments and EMS, this means mutual aid --- the connective tissue of the American fire service --- may be physically impossible. If the bridges are down, the engine company from the neighboring jurisdiction cannot get to you. If the highways are buckled and the overpasses have collapsed, the ambulance from the next county is not coming. Communities will be on their own for the first 72 hours at minimum, and potentially much longer.

### Tsunami

A full Cascadia rupture generates a tsunami that reaches the outer coast in 15 to 30 minutes. The wave heights depend on local geography --- the shape of the coastline, the depth of the offshore shelf, the configuration of bays and inlets --- but models predict 20 to 100 feet in exposed coastal locations. The outer coast of Washington and Oregon, from Neah Bay to Brookings, is in the direct path.

Coastal communities have tsunami warning systems, evacuation route signage, and public education programs. But 15 to 30 minutes is not much time, especially when the roads may be damaged by the earthquake itself, when power is out, when communication systems are disrupted, and when people are disoriented from minutes of violent shaking. For communities like Seaside, Cannon Beach, Long Beach, Westport, and Ocean Shores, the tsunami is the primary threat. Their evacuation plans are well-developed on paper. Whether those plans survive contact with the reality of a magnitude 9.0 earthquake is the question that no one can answer until it happens.

Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the inland waters are somewhat protected from the direct tsunami wave by the geography of the coast, but they are not immune. Tsunami modeling shows wave heights of 3 to 15 feet at various locations within the Sound, enough to flood low-lying waterfronts and marinas.

## The Lahar Threat

Mount Rainier, at 14,411 feet the highest peak in the Cascade Range, stands 60 miles southeast of Seattle and looms over the communities of Pierce County like a beautiful, dormant threat. Rainier is an active stratovolcano --- it last erupted approximately 1,000 years ago --- and its specific hazard is not lava or ash. It is lahars.

A lahar is a volcanic mudflow --- a slurry of water, rock, mud, and volcanic debris that flows down river valleys at 30 to 50 miles per hour, filling valleys to depths of tens to hundreds of feet. Lahars do not require an eruption. They can be triggered by volcanic activity, by glacial outburst floods, or by structural collapse of the summit. Mount Rainier's summit is composed of heavily altered, weakite-clay-rich rock that is structurally weak. It carries more glacial ice than any other peak in the lower 48 states. The combination of unstable rock, massive glacial ice, and steep river valleys radiating from the summit creates lahar conditions that the USGS classifies as among the most dangerous volcanic hazards in the United States.

The Osceola Mudflow, approximately 5,600 years ago, traveled over 70 miles from Rainier's summit to Puget Sound. It filled the White River and Green River valleys to depths of hundreds of feet. The flat valley floor on which the cities of Enumclaw, Buckley, Auburn, Kent, and portions of the Puyallup and Sumner metropolitan areas are built --- that is the Osceola Mudflow deposit. Those cities are built on the evidence of the last time this happened.

The USGS operates an Acoustic Flow Monitor system on Mount Rainier that detects the ground vibrations produced by a lahar in motion and triggers automated warnings. Pierce County has a lahar warning system with sirens in the valley communities. Evacuation routes are posted on road signs throughout the Puyallup, Carbon, and White river valleys. School districts in the lahar zones conduct evacuation drills. The city of Orting --- population 8,000, located at the confluence of the Puyallup and Carbon rivers, squarely in the path of a Carbon River lahar --- has evacuation plans that give residents approximately 30 to 45 minutes from warning to arrival.

Thirty to 45 minutes. To evacuate an entire community. On roads that may already be damaged by the earthquake that triggered the lahar. With infrastructure --- traffic signals, communications, bridges --- that may not be functioning. With a population that includes children in schools, elderly residents with limited mobility, and people who may not hear the sirens.

For fire departments in Pierce County, the lahar scenario is the most terrifying operational planning exercise they face. The lahar arrives too fast for traditional evacuation, too massively for any structural protection, and too destructively for any search and rescue operation that does not begin after the flow has stopped. Their planning focuses on pre-positioning, evacuation assistance, and the grim reality of mass casualty operations in a debris field that may be miles wide and tens of feet deep.

## First Responder Implications

The Cascadia scenario is the maximum credible event for every fire department, EMS agency, and law enforcement organization in western Washington and Oregon. It exceeds available resources by orders of magnitude. Understanding what first responders will face --- and what they will not be able to do --- is essential context for community preparedness.

### Mass Casualty, Limited Access

The number of casualties in a full Cascadia event is estimated in the thousands to tens of thousands, depending on the time of day (a daytime event produces more casualties in commercial buildings and on highways), the specific characteristics of the rupture, and the degree to which vulnerable structures have been retrofitted. The casualties will be distributed across the entire affected region simultaneously --- this is not a localized event with a defined perimeter. Every community in western Washington and Oregon will have casualties, structural collapses, fires, and infrastructure failures happening at the same time.

Fire departments will face more structural collapses than they have collapse rescue teams. More fires than they have engines. More medical emergencies than they have ambulances. The triage will be brutal: responders will have to decide which collapses to search, which fires to fight, and which patients to treat based on where they can do the most good with the resources available. Some calls will not be answered. Some patients will not be reached. This is not a failure of the responders. It is the arithmetic of a disaster that exceeds every available resource.

### Infrastructure Destruction

Water mains will rupture. Fire departments depend on pressurized water supply for fire suppression --- without hydrants, firefighting capability is drastically reduced. Portable water supply (tanker trucks, draft from open water sources) can partially compensate, but not at the scale needed for multiple simultaneous fires across a metropolitan area.

Roads will be impassable. Broken pavement, collapsed overpasses, liquefied roadbeds, debris from collapsed buildings, downed power lines --- the physical infrastructure that fire apparatus and ambulances need to reach the scene will be damaged or destroyed. Response times that are normally measured in minutes will be measured in hours.

Communications will be degraded. Cell towers require power, and backup batteries last 4 to 8 hours. Trunked radio systems require repeater infrastructure that may be damaged. The 911 system requires functional Public Safety Answering Points that may be in damaged buildings. The interoperability problems that plagued 9/11 will be replicated on a regional scale.

### Self-Reliance: 72 Hours Is Not Enough

FEMA's traditional preparedness guidance tells citizens to maintain a 72-hour emergency supply kit. For a Cascadia event, 72 hours is woefully insufficient. The Oregon Resilience Plan and Washington emergency management guidance have moved toward recommending a minimum two-week supply of food, water, medications, and essential supplies.

The logic is simple: outside help --- federal resources, FEMA, military support, mutual aid from unaffected regions --- cannot reach the affected area until transportation infrastructure is functional enough to move heavy equipment and supplies. If the bridges on I-5 are down, if the ports are damaged by liquefaction, if the airports are inoperable because runways have buckled, the logistics of moving disaster relief into the region will take days to weeks to establish. The military can airlift some supplies by helicopter, but helicopter capacity is finite and the need will be enormous.

Communities that have prepared --- that have water stored, food stockpiled, medications on hand, neighborhood plans for checking on vulnerable members, CERT teams trained in basic search and rescue and first aid, amateur radio operators (ARES/RACES) who can communicate when all other systems fail --- will fare dramatically better than those that have not.

## What Is Being Done

The Pacific Northwest is not ignoring the Cascadia threat. Significant preparation is underway, though it is uneven and insufficient to the scale of the risk.

### FEMA Cascadia Rising

The Cascadia Rising exercise series, conducted by FEMA in partnership with state and local emergency management agencies, tests the region's response capability for a full Cascadia event. The exercises involve thousands of participants across multiple states and simulate the full arc of disaster response from initial impact through weeks of recovery. The exercises have been valuable for identifying gaps --- communications failures, logistics bottlenecks, command coordination challenges --- and for building the relationships between agencies that will be critical during the actual event.

### Seismic Retrofits

Both Oregon and Washington have programs to retrofit vulnerable structures. Oregon's 2018 URM retrofit ordinance requires Portland's unreinforced masonry buildings to be evaluated and retrofitted on a timeline extending to the 2030s and 2040s. Washington has invested in retrofitting critical infrastructure --- hospitals, fire stations, schools, bridges --- but the funding is insufficient to address the full inventory of vulnerable structures. The SR 99 tunnel in Seattle, completed in 2019, was designed to a seismic standard that the Alaskan Way Viaduct it replaced could not meet.

### Early Warning Systems

The Pacific Northwest Seismic Network (PNSN), operated by the University of Washington, monitors seismic activity continuously. ShakeAlert, the USGS earthquake early warning system, provides seconds to tens of seconds of warning before strong shaking arrives at a given location. Seconds of warning may not sound like much, but it is enough to stop trains, open fire station doors (preventing them from jamming in the shaking), pause surgeries, and trigger automated shutoffs for gas lines and industrial processes. ShakeAlert has been operational in the Pacific Northwest since 2021 and is integrated with the Wireless Emergency Alert system that sends alerts to cell phones.

The lahar warning system on Mount Rainier --- the Acoustic Flow Monitors and the community siren systems in Pierce County --- provides the only warning that Puyallup Valley residents will receive. These systems are maintained by the USGS and Pierce County, and they are tested regularly. Whether they will function during a Cascadia event --- when the earthquake itself may have damaged the sensor network, the communication links, and the siren infrastructure --- is unknown.

### CERT Teams

Community Emergency Response Teams (CERT) --- civilian volunteers trained in basic disaster response skills including light search and rescue, fire suppression, first aid, and triage --- are one of the most important preparedness investments a community can make. CERT training is offered through local fire departments and emergency management agencies throughout the Pacific Northwest. A neighborhood with a trained CERT team has residents who can assess structural damage, perform basic search and rescue, provide first aid, organize sheltering, and communicate needs to professional responders when they arrive.

### Amateur Radio

When everything else fails, amateur radio works. ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service) and RACES (Radio Amateur Civil Emergency Service) operators provide communications capability that is independent of commercial infrastructure. A ham radio operator with a battery-powered HF transceiver can communicate across the region --- or across the continent --- when cell towers are down, the internet is out, and trunked radio systems are degraded. Fire departments and emergency management agencies in the Pacific Northwest actively recruit and integrate amateur radio operators into their emergency plans.

## What Citizens Should Do

The individual preparedness checklist for Cascadia is different from the standard earthquake preparedness that most people learn. The scale and duration of the event demand a different level of readiness.

**Water.** One gallon per person per day for a minimum of 14 days. For a family of four, that is 56 gallons --- roughly the volume of three large storage containers. Water is the single most critical supply. Dehydration becomes life-threatening within days. Municipal water systems in the affected area will be compromised for weeks.

**Food.** Two weeks of non-perishable food that your family will actually eat. Rotate it annually. Include a manual can opener.

**Medications.** A 30-day supply of all prescription medications, rotated to maintain freshness. For people who depend on medications for survival --- insulin, blood pressure medication, anti-seizure drugs, psychiatric medications --- this is not optional. Pharmacies will be closed. Supply chains will be disrupted.

**First aid.** A comprehensive first aid kit, not the bandage-and-aspirin kit from the drugstore. Include trauma supplies --- tourniquets, hemostatic gauze, Israeli bandages --- because professional medical care may be hours or days away.

**Communication.** A battery-powered or hand-crank AM/FM radio. A whistle for signaling if trapped. A plan for where family members will meet if they are separated. A contact outside the region who can serve as a communication relay --- after major disasters, long-distance calls often work when local calls do not.

**Cash.** ATMs and card readers require power and network connectivity. Neither will be available. Keep enough cash on hand for two weeks of basic purchases.

**Tools.** A wrench to shut off your gas valve. A fire extinguisher. Sturdy shoes near your bed (you will be walking on broken glass). A pry bar. Work gloves.

**Know your building.** If you live or work in an unreinforced masonry building, know the exit routes and know the risks. If you are in a URM during the shaking, get under a heavy desk or table --- the risk is the walls collapsing inward. If you are outside a URM, get away from the building --- the risk is the walls collapsing outward onto the sidewalk.

**Know your zone.** If you live in a tsunami zone, know your evacuation route and practice walking it. If you live in a lahar zone, know the warning sounds and the evacuation routes. If you live in a liquefaction zone, understand that the ground itself may be unreliable.

**Get trained.** Take a CERT class. Take a CPR class. Take a Stop the Bleed class. Learn to use a fire extinguisher. Learn basic first aid. In the first hours and days after Cascadia, you will not be waiting for professionals. You will be the first responder for your family and your neighbors. The skills you have when the shaking starts are the skills you will have.

## The Weight of Knowing

For first responders in the Pacific Northwest, the Cascadia Subduction Zone is the thing they train for that they hope never comes. Every earthquake drill, every ICS exercise, every CERT class they teach, every public presentation about preparedness is conducted in the knowledge that the event they are preparing for will exceed their capacity. They know the bridges will fall. They know the water mains will rupture. They know the calls will outnumber the units by orders of magnitude. They know people will die waiting for help that cannot get through.

They prepare anyway. Because preparation --- even incomplete, even insufficient preparation --- saves lives. A community that has a plan will function better than one that does not. A population that has supplies will survive longer than one that does not. A neighborhood with a CERT team will rescue people that no fire department engine will reach for days.

The Cascadia Subduction Zone will rupture. The question is not whether we will be ready --- we will not be, not fully, not for an event of this magnitude. The question is whether we will be ready enough. Ready enough to save the lives that preparation can save. Ready enough to reduce the suffering that preparedness can reduce. Ready enough that when the shaking stops and the long recovery begins, the communities of the Pacific Northwest can say: we did what we could, with what we knew, before it came.

For the person who served thirty years in the Pacific Northwest fire service, the Cascadia scenario is not abstract. It is the what-if that shadows every shift. The knowledge that one day, the ground beneath the station will move, the overhead doors will jam or open, the apparatus will roll or stay, and the radio will either work or go silent. And whatever happens in those first minutes will determine everything that follows for days and weeks and months.

The best insurance is not equipment or apparatus. It is community. Neighbors who know each other. Blocks that have plans. Citizens who have taken the training. The professional response system will arrive, eventually. The question is what happens in the hours before it does.
