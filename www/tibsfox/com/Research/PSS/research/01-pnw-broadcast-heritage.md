## 1. The Electromagnetic Commons

The Radio Act of 1927 established a principle that has governed American broadcasting for nearly a century: the electromagnetic spectrum belongs to the public. Licensees do not own their frequencies -- they hold temporary privileges of use, granted under the condition that they serve the public interest, convenience, and necessity [1]. This is not merely a legal abstraction. It is the foundational contract that made possible every broadcast tower, every television station, and every radio signal that has ever reached a PNW household.

The Communications Act of 1934 codified this principle into the creation of the Federal Communications Commission, transferring regulatory authority from the Federal Radio Commission. The FCC was granted power to allocate spectrum, issue licenses, and revoke them when licensees failed to serve the public interest [2]. Every station in the Seattle-Tacoma market exists because the FCC determined, at a specific moment in history, that granting a license to a specific applicant on a specific frequency would serve the public.

```
THE PUBLIC TRUST MODEL
================================================================
  SPECTRUM (public resource)
       |
       v
  FCC LICENSE (temporary privilege)
       |
       v
  BROADCAST STATION (operated under public interest standard)
       |
       v
  LOCAL SERVICE OBLIGATION
       |
       v
  LICENSE RENEWAL (every 8 years, subject to review)
================================================================
```

The tension between this public trust model and the economic reality of commercial broadcasting has defined the PNW broadcast landscape for seven decades. Local families built the original stations. National media conglomerates acquired them. The public interest standard persists in law but has been progressively weakened through deregulation since the Telecommunications Act of 1996 [3].

## 2. Seattle-Tacoma Television: The Founding Era

The Seattle-Tacoma television market -- designated DMA #13 by Nielsen as of 2025, serving approximately 1.98 million households -- has been served continuously by broadcast television since 1948 [4]. The founding stations emerged from the AM radio dynasties that had dominated Pacific Northwest media since the 1920s.

### The Pre-Television Radio Landscape

Seattle's broadcast history begins in the early 1920s with experimental radio stations. KFQX, operated by Roy Olmstead -- a former Seattle police lieutenant turned bootlegger during Prohibition -- transmitted from a private residence. Olmstead's wife Elise read bedtime stories to children on the air while, according to federal prosecutors, coded messages were embedded in the programming to coordinate liquor shipments from British Columbia [5]. The station's license was revoked after Olmstead's conviction in 1926.

KJR (950 AM) emerged as one of the early major commercial stations. Fisher Broadcasting, founded by O.W. Fisher, built KOMO Radio (1000 AM) into a dominant force in Seattle media. The Bullitt family, led by Dorothy Stimson Bullitt -- one of the first women to own and operate a major broadcasting company in the United States -- acquired KING Radio and positioned it for the television era [6].

### The Television Race

The late 1940s saw an intense competition among Seattle broadcasters to secure the first television licenses. The FCC's television allocation plan assigned VHF channels to the Seattle-Tacoma market, and applications flooded in. The critical factor was capital: television transmitting equipment cost orders of magnitude more than radio, and the FCC required demonstrated financial capacity before granting licenses [7].

```
SEATTLE-TACOMA TV TIMELINE (1948-1960)
================================================================
1948  KING-TV Ch.5   Signs on (Nov 25) -- Dorothy Bullitt
1953  KMO-TV Ch.13   Signs on (Aug 2) -- Tacoma
1953  KOMO-TV Ch.4   Signs on (Dec 11) -- Fisher Broadcasting
1954  KCTS Ch.9      Signs on (Dec 7) -- University of Washington
1958  KIRO-TV Ch.7   Signs on (Feb 8) -- Saul Haas
1959  KING-TV        Secures NBC affiliation (lobbying victory)
================================================================
```

KING-TV, Channel 5, signed on November 25, 1948, as the first television station in the Pacific Northwest. Dorothy Bullitt had secured the NBC affiliation, giving KING the most desirable network programming of the era. The station broadcast from a facility at Aurora Avenue North, using equipment that represented a substantial investment by King Broadcasting Company [6].

## 3. The Five Major Stations

### KING-TV (Channel 5) -- NBC

KING-TV holds the distinction of being the Pacific Northwest's oldest continuously operating television station. Under Dorothy Bullitt's leadership, King Broadcasting Company built a media empire that included television stations in Portland (KGW) and Spokane (KREM), plus cable systems across the region [6]. The Bullitt family maintained ownership until the company's sale to Providence Journal Broadcasting in 1992, which was subsequently acquired by A.H. Belo Corporation.

KING-TV's signal originates from a transmitter on West Tiger Mountain, east of Issaquah, at an antenna height above average terrain (HAAT) of approximately 686 meters. The station's digital channel is 48 (virtual channel 5), transmitting at 1,000 kW effective radiated power (ERP) [8].

### KOMO-TV (Channel 4) -- ABC

KOMO-TV signed on December 11, 1953, as the television extension of KOMO 1000 AM radio. Fisher Broadcasting was the original owner. The station briefly held NBC affiliation before KING's successful lobbying campaign in 1959 secured the NBC affiliation for Channel 5. KOMO then became the market's ABC affiliate, a relationship that has persisted to the present day [9].

Fisher Broadcasting was acquired by Fisher Communications, which was in turn purchased by Sinclair Broadcast Group in 2013 for approximately $373 million. This acquisition brought KOMO under the ownership of the largest single operator of local television stations in the United States by total number of stations [10].

### KIRO-TV (Channel 7) -- CBS

KIRO-TV signed on February 8, 1958, under the ownership of Saul Haas, who also operated KIRO Radio (710 AM). Haas had been a prominent Seattle media figure since the 1930s. The station became the market's CBS affiliate and maintained that network relationship through multiple ownership changes [9].

Cox Media Group currently operates KIRO-TV. The station broadcasts from West Tiger Mountain with digital channel 39 (virtual channel 7), at 550 kW ERP.

### KCTS-9 -- PBS

KCTS signed on December 7, 1954, from the University of Washington campus. Dorothy Bullitt of KING donated the initial transmitting equipment -- an act of generosity that reflected the public-interest ethos of the founding broadcast generation [11]. KCTS became the region's PBS affiliate and has operated as a non-profit educational station throughout its history.

KCTS Television (now branded as Cascade PBS) maintains editorial independence and local programming that includes significant coverage of PNW environmental, cultural, and civic topics.

### KCPQ (Channel 13) -- Fox

KMO-TV Channel 13 has one of the most turbulent ownership histories in PNW broadcasting. It began broadcasting August 2, 1953, from Tacoma. Financial difficulties led to its transfer to the Clover Park School District, which operated it as an educational station under the KCPQ call sign. Kelly Broadcasting of Sacramento purchased KCPQ in 1980 and converted it to a commercial independent station [9].

Tribune Broadcasting acquired KCPQ in 1999, and it became the market's Fox affiliate. Fox Television Stations completed ownership through a subsequent trade with Nexstar Media Group. As of 2026, KCPQ is relocating its operations to the Denny Triangle neighborhood in downtown Seattle.

## 4. Ownership Consolidation

The transformation of PNW broadcast ownership from local families to national media conglomerates is one of the defining stories of American telecommunications policy.

### The Deregulation Wave

The Telecommunications Act of 1996 relaxed ownership limits that had previously prevented a single entity from controlling more than a limited number of stations in a market or nationally. The result was a wave of consolidation that fundamentally altered the economics of local broadcasting [3].

| Era | Ownership Pattern | Number of Distinct Local Owners (SEA-TAC Market) |
|-----|------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| 1953-1975 | Founding families | 5-6 independent local owners |
| 1975-1996 | Regional groups | 4-5 regional broadcasting companies |
| 1996-2010 | National consolidation | 3-4 national media groups |
| 2010-2026 | Mega-group dominance | 4 national groups own all major commercial stations |

### Current Ownership Groups

As of 2026, the Seattle-Tacoma market's major commercial stations are owned by four national media groups:

- **Sinclair Broadcast Group:** KOMO-TV (Ch.4, ABC) and KUNS (Ch.51, Univision). Sinclair is the largest television station operator in the US by total station count, with over 185 stations across the country [10].
- **Tegna Inc. / Nexstar:** KING-TV (Ch.5, NBC) and KONG-TV (Ch.6/16, independent). Nexstar's acquisition of Tegna was completed in 2026, creating the largest local television company in the US by revenue.
- **Cox Media Group:** KIRO-TV (Ch.7, CBS). Cox also operates radio stations in the Seattle market.
- **Fox Television Stations:** KCPQ (Ch.13, Fox) and KZJO (Ch.10/22, MyNetworkTV). Fox Television Stations is owned by Fox Corporation, the Murdoch family entity.

> **SAFETY NOTE:** The concentration of broadcast ownership raises questions about editorial diversity and local accountability. This document presents the ownership structure factually without advocating for specific regulatory positions, consistent with SC-ADV safety gate requirements.

The non-commercial stations -- KCTS-9 (PBS, operated by Cascade PBS) and KBTC (Ch.12, PBS, operated by Bates Technical College) -- remain under local institutional control.

## 6. Digital Transition and ATSC 3.0

### The 2009 Digital Transition

The transition from analog to digital television broadcasting, mandated by the Digital Television Transition and Public Safety Act of 2005, was completed on June 12, 2009. All full-power television stations were required to cease analog broadcasting and transmit exclusively in digital format using the ATSC 1.0 standard [12].

For the Seattle-Tacoma market, this transition freed approximately 108 MHz of spectrum in the 700 MHz band (UHF channels 52-69), which was subsequently auctioned by the FCC for mobile broadband use. The freed spectrum became the foundation of T-Mobile's and AT&T's LTE networks in the region.

### ATSC 3.0: NextGen TV

ATSC 3.0 (NextGen TV) represents a fundamental architectural shift in broadcast television. Unlike ATSC 1.0, which uses fixed-rate MPEG-2 encoding over 8-VSB modulation, ATSC 3.0 uses:

- **OFDM modulation** (Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing), the same technique used by LTE, Wi-Fi, and most modern wireless systems
- **HEVC/H.265 video codec**, providing approximately twice the compression efficiency of MPEG-2
- **IP-based transport**, replacing the MPEG transport stream with internet protocol packets
- **Layered Division Multiplexing (LDM)**, allowing simultaneous transmission of a robust mobile signal and a high-bitrate fixed signal within the same 6 MHz channel [13]

ATSC 3.0 enables capabilities that blur the line between broadcasting and broadband:

```
ATSC 3.0 vs ATSC 1.0 COMPARISON
================================================================
Feature              ATSC 1.0           ATSC 3.0
----------------------------------------------------------------
Modulation           8-VSB              OFDM (COFDM)
Video codec          MPEG-2             HEVC/H.265 (+ VVC ready)
Transport            MPEG-TS            IP (ROUTE/DASH)
Max resolution       1080i              4K UHD (2160p)
HDR                  None               HDR10, Dolby Vision, HLG
Audio                AC-3 (Dolby)       AC-4 (Dolby Atmos)
Interactive          None               HTML5 apps, targeted ads
Mobile reception     Poor               Designed for mobile
Emergency alerting   EAS text           Rich media, geo-targeted
Single Frequency     No                 Yes (SFN capable)
Network
================================================================
```

Several Seattle-area stations have begun ATSC 3.0 deployment. The technology's IP-based architecture opens the possibility of using broadcast spectrum as a one-to-many data delivery channel -- effectively transforming television transmitters into wide-area multicast data servers [13].

## 7. PNW Radio Heritage

### AM Broadcasting

Washington state's AM radio heritage stretches back to the earliest days of licensed broadcasting. The AM broadcast band (540-1700 kHz) has been continuously occupied since the 1920s. Key historical stations include:

- **KJR (950 AM):** One of Seattle's original major commercial stations, now operating as a sports talk format
- **KOMO (1000 AM):** Fisher Broadcasting's flagship, paired with KOMO-TV. Currently operating news/talk format under Sinclair
- **KIRO (710 AM):** Paired with KIRO-TV under Saul Haas, now operated by Bonneville International (LDS Church-owned) as a sports talk station
- **KVI (570 AM):** One of Tacoma's heritage stations, originally part of the Mutual Broadcasting System network
- **KISN (910 AM, Vancouver WA):** A legendary top-40 station from 1959-1976. The FCC revoked KISN's license due to violations involving undisclosed connections to political candidates -- one of the most dramatic license revocation cases in PNW broadcast history [14]

### FM Broadcasting

The FM band (88-108 MHz) in Washington state carries approximately 200+ licensed stations ranging from full-power commercial (up to 100 kW ERP) to Low-Power FM (LPFM) stations at 10-100 watts. The non-commercial educational (NCE) portion of the band (88.1-91.9 MHz) is reserved for non-profit broadcasters.

Notable PNW FM stations include:

- **KEXP (90.3 FM):** Formerly KCMU, operated by the University of Washington. KEXP has become internationally recognized for its independent music programming and was instrumental in popularizing Seattle-area artists during the grunge era and beyond [15]
- **KUOW (94.9 FM):** University of Washington's NPR affiliate
- **KNKX (88.5 FM):** Formerly KPLU, a community-licensed NPR/jazz station serving Pierce and South King counties

### Station Count

Washington state holds approximately 400+ FCC-licensed radio stations across AM, FM, LPFM, and translator categories [8]. Oregon adds approximately 350+ stations, and British Columbia's CRTC-licensed stations add another 100+ to the regional broadcast ecosystem.

## 8. Low-Power Television and Translators

### LPTV Stations

Low-Power Television (LPTV) stations operate at significantly reduced power levels compared to full-power stations -- typically 3 kW for VHF and 15 kW for UHF. The Seattle-Tacoma market has approximately 15-20 active LPTV licenses. LPTV stations are secondary to full-power stations, meaning they must accept interference from and cannot cause interference to full-power operations [16].

### Television Translators

Television translators rebroadcast the signal of a parent station on a different channel, extending coverage to areas that cannot receive the parent station's signal directly due to terrain. The mountainous topography of the Pacific Northwest makes translators essential -- communities in the Cascade foothills, Olympic Peninsula, and San Juan Islands often receive their television signals exclusively through translator chains [8].

Washington state has approximately 200+ licensed television translator stations. These translators form chains where one translator receives the signal from another, extending coverage through valleys and around mountain ridges:

```
TRANSLATOR CHAIN EXAMPLE (Cascade East Slope)
================================================================
West Tiger Mtn (full power) --> Snoqualmie Pass translator
    --> Ellensburg translator --> Yakima translator
    --> Richland translator

Signal hops: 4 retransmissions, ~200 miles total coverage
Frequency shifts at each hop to avoid self-interference
================================================================
```

## 9. Oregon and British Columbia Context

### Portland-Salem Television Market

The Portland-Salem market (DMA #22) shares the Pacific Northwest's broadcast heritage. KGW-TV (Channel 8, NBC) was one of Dorothy Bullitt's King Broadcasting properties, extending the Seattle media dynasty into Oregon. KOIN (Channel 6, CBS), KATU (Channel 2, ABC), and KPTV (Channel 12, Fox) round out the major commercial stations [9].

Portland's media landscape has its own distinctive characteristics: a strong tradition of independent broadcasting, community radio (KBOO 90.7 FM has operated as a community-licensed station since 1968), and a more localized ownership structure than Seattle due to market size differences.

### British Columbia

British Columbia's broadcast system operates under the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC), a fundamentally different regulatory framework from the FCC. CRTC requires Canadian content quotas (CanCon) -- a minimum percentage of programming must be of Canadian origin [17].

Key BC stations visible from the PNW border region:

- **CBUT (Channel 2, CBC):** Canadian Broadcasting Corporation's Vancouver flagship
- **CHAN-DT (Channel 8, Global):** Corus Entertainment
- **CIVT-DT (Channel 32, CTV 2):** Bell Media
- **CKNW (980 AM):** News/talk radio, one of Western Canada's most influential stations
- **CFOX (99.3 FM):** Rock format, strong signal reach into Whatcom County

The CRTC/FCC boundary creates interesting spectrum coordination challenges at the border. Television channel assignments in the Bellingham, WA and Vancouver, BC areas are carefully coordinated to avoid co-channel interference, with both regulators maintaining coordination agreements under the ITU framework [17].

### Spokane Market Context

The Spokane market (DMA #73) serves eastern Washington, northern Idaho, and western Montana. Its broadcast infrastructure demonstrates how the Cascade Range creates effectively separate media markets within a single state. Key stations include KHQ-TV (Channel 6, NBC), KXLY-TV (Channel 4, ABC), KREM-TV (Channel 2, CBS, originally a King Broadcasting property), and KAYU-TV (Channel 28, Fox). The Spokane market's isolation from the Puget Sound region -- separated by 280 miles and the Cascade Range -- makes it functionally independent in broadcast coverage terms, requiring its own tower infrastructure, its own translator chains, and its own licensing considerations [9].

This geographic separation is significant for the Signal Stack: HAPS platforms at 20 km altitude could potentially serve both the Puget Sound and Spokane markets simultaneously from a single platform positioned over the Cascade crest, bridging a gap that ground-based broadcast infrastructure has never been able to close efficiently.

The Tri-Cities market (Kennewick-Richland-Pasco) adds another node in eastern Washington, further demonstrating the terrain-fragmented nature of PNW broadcasting.

## 10. The Subchannel Revolution

Digital television's most underappreciated feature is the subchannel. Each full-power station's 6 MHz allocation can carry 19.39 Mbps of data in ATSC 1.0. A single HD program requires 8-12 Mbps, leaving 7-11 Mbps available for additional standard-definition channels or data services [12].

### Subchannel Networks

A new ecosystem of subchannel-only networks has emerged, taking advantage of this unused capacity:

| Network | Genre | Approx. Affiliate Count | PNW Carrier |
|---------|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| MeTV | Classic TV | 500+ | KOMO 4.2 |
| Antenna TV | Classic TV | 300+ | KCPQ 13.2 |
| Heroes & Icons | Drama/Sci-Fi | 250+ | KOMO 4.3 |
| Cozi TV | Classic/Lifestyle | 200+ | KING 5.2 |
| Bounce | Urban entertainment | 200+ | KCPQ 13.3 |
| Court TV | Legal/True crime | 200+ | KING 5.3 |
| Dabl | Home/Lifestyle | 150+ | KIRO 7.2 |

These subchannel networks represent a quiet revolution in broadcast economics: they provide programming to audiences who have cut the cord from cable/satellite services, using spectrum that was previously wasted. For the PNW Signal Stack, subchannels demonstrate that existing broadcast infrastructure has latent capacity waiting to be utilized [12].

### Data Broadcasting

ATSC 3.0's IP-based transport opens the subchannel concept beyond video. A broadcaster could allocate a portion of its bitstream to:

- Software updates for IoT devices (one-to-many multicast)
- Emergency preparedness data packages
- Sensor network data aggregation
- Educational content distribution to rural areas

This transforms a broadcast transmitter from a television-only device into a general-purpose one-to-many data delivery system.

## 11. Broadcast Infrastructure as Public Asset

### Tower Sites as Strategic Infrastructure

The concentration of broadcast transmitters on West Tiger Mountain represents both a strength (centralized maintenance, shared infrastructure costs) and a vulnerability (single point of failure for the entire market's over-the-air television). A severe winter storm, earthquake, or wildfire affecting the West Tiger Mountain site could disable television service for the entire Seattle-Tacoma metropolitan area [8].

The Puget Sound region's broadcast tower infrastructure could be augmented by:

- **Redundant transmitter sites** on geographically separated peaks
- **HAPS and tethered aerostat platforms** providing emergency broadcast relay capability (see M4)
- **Mesh network integration** distributing content from multiple ground points rather than single tower sites

### The Amiga Principle Applied to Broadcasting

The Amiga's architecture succeeded because specialized chips (Agnus, Denise, Paula) each performed their designated function with deterministic timing, while the 68000 CPU handled general logic. Broadcasting works on the same principle: each station occupies its designated frequency, operates at its licensed power level, and delivers its specific content. The FCC's allocation table is the chipset specification. The transmitter towers are the silicon. The content is the data flowing through the DMA channels [18].

The Signal Stack vision extends this analogy: Part 15 devices at 100 mW are the low-power peripheral chips. LPFM stations at 10-100 watts are the I/O controllers. Full-power broadcast stations at hundreds of kilowatts are the main processors. HAPS platforms at stratospheric altitude are the DMA controllers, moving data across coverage areas that ground-based systems cannot reach. Together, they form a chipset -- a communications chipset for the Pacific Northwest.
