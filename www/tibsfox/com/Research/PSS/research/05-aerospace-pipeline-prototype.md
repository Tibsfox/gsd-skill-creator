## 1. The Workforce Thesis

The Pacific Northwest's aerospace workforce represents one of the most technically skilled labor pools on the continent. The training pipeline -- from community college programs through Boeing's Pre-Employment Training (BPET) fast-track to factory floor employment -- produces technicians with hands-on experience in precision assembly, composite materials, avionics installation, quality assurance, and systems integration [1].

These skills map directly to the Signal Stack's hardware needs:

```
SKILL TRANSFER: AEROSPACE → SIGNAL STACK
================================================================
AEROSPACE SKILL              SIGNAL STACK APPLICATION
----------------------------------------------------------------
Avionics installation        RF antenna array deployment
Composite layup              Radome/antenna housing fabrication
Wire harness assembly        Mesh node installation, cabling
Quality assurance/NDT        Signal verification, compliance test
Systems integration          Multi-system deployment coordination
Tool calibration             RF test equipment operation
Environmental testing        Outdoor electronics weatherproofing
Documentation/AS9100         FCC compliance documentation
================================================================
```

The gap between these two domains is measured in weeks of additional training, not years. A technician who can install a 787 avionics rack can learn to install a mesh network node. A quality inspector who validates aircraft wiring can validate FCC-compliant antenna installations. The WATR Center's existing curriculum provides 80% of the foundational knowledge; the remaining 20% is RF-specific theory and FCC regulatory knowledge [1].

## 2. Boeing Everett and Paine Field

### Boeing Everett Factory

Boeing's Everett site is the world's largest building by volume at approximately 472 million cubic feet (13.4 million cubic meters), covering 98.3 acres under a single roof. The facility houses final assembly lines for the 747, 767, 777, and 787 aircraft programs [2].

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Building volume | 472 million cubic feet |
| Floor area | 98.3 acres (4.28 million sq ft) |
| Employment (2026) | ~25,000 (fluctuates with production rates) |
| Aircraft programs | 747, 767, 777, 777X, 787 |
| Location | 3003 W Casino Rd, Everett, WA 98204 |

The Everett factory represents not just a manufacturing facility but a concentration of technical expertise and supply chain infrastructure. Approximately 150 aerospace supplier companies are located within Snohomish County, forming a dense ecosystem of precision manufacturing, tooling, composites, electronics, and testing services [1].

### Paine Field Airport

Paine Field (KPAE, Snohomish County Airport) serves dual roles as Boeing's flight test and delivery facility and as a commercial airport (Alaska Airlines, Delta service began 2019). The airport's infrastructure includes [3]:

- 9,010-foot runway (adequate for all Boeing widebody aircraft)
- Boeing flight test hangars and ramp space
- Commercial passenger terminal (opened 2019)
- Future of Flight aviation center and Boeing factory tour
- WATR Center (Washington Aerospace Training and Research Center)
- General aviation facilities

## 3. WATR Center: Training Pipeline

### Overview

The Washington Aerospace Training and Research (WATR) Center is located at Paine Field and managed by Edmonds College through an operating agreement with the Aerospace Futures Alliance, established in 2010. The WATR Center serves as the primary workforce pipeline for Boeing's Everett operations [4].

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Annual graduates | ~300 students |
| Boeing direct hire rate | >50% of graduates |
| Supplier absorption | Remaining graduates absorbed by ~150 county aerospace suppliers |
| Programs | Aerospace manufacturing, composites, quality assurance |
| Location | 3008 100th St SW, Everett, WA 98204 |
| Managing institution | Edmonds College |

### BPET: Boeing Pre-Employment Training Program

Boeing's BPET program is the key accelerator in the WATR-to-Boeing pipeline. WATR graduates who complete the BPET curriculum are eligible to skip the standard Boeing hiring interview process for designated manufacturing roles, effectively creating a direct pathway from classroom to factory floor [4].

```
WATR CENTER → BOEING PIPELINE
================================================================

  Community College Enrollment
         |
         v
  WATR Center Program (6-12 months)
  - Aerospace manufacturing fundamentals
  - Composite layup and repair
  - Blueprint reading and GD&T
  - Quality assurance and inspection
  - Safety and OSHA compliance
         |
         v
  BPET Completion (additional 4-6 weeks)
  - Boeing-specific processes and tooling
  - Boeing quality management system
  - EH&S (Environment, Health & Safety)
  - Shop floor orientation
         |
         v
  Boeing Direct Hire (interview waived)
  - Mechanic / Installer / Inspector roles
  - Starting wage: $23-35/hour (2026)
  - Union representation (IAM District 751)
         |
         v
  Career Progression
  - Senior mechanic (3-5 years)
  - Lead (5-8 years)
  - Inspector / Quality (specialized track)
  - Technical specialist (engineering support)
================================================================
```

### Curriculum Alignment with Signal Stack

The WATR Center's existing curriculum modules and their Signal Stack relevance:

|-------------|----------|----------------------|
| Blueprint reading / GD&T | 4 weeks | Antenna layout drawings, RF design specs |

## 4. Educational Ecosystem at Paine Field

The WATR Center sits within a broader educational ecosystem that provides multiple pathways into aerospace and technology careers [5]:

### Edmonds College (EdCC)

Edmonds College manages the WATR Center and offers associate degrees and certificates in aerospace and advanced manufacturing. Located at 20000 68th Ave W, Lynnwood, WA 98036, the college serves approximately 20,000 students annually and is part of the Washington State community and technical college system [5].

### Everett Community College (EVCC)

EVCC participates in the University Center of North Puget Sound, offering baccalaureate and graduate degree programs from Washington State University, Central Washington University, and other four-year institutions. Relevant programs include mechanical engineering, electrical engineering, and business administration [5].

### Sno-Isle Technical Skills Center

The Sno-Isle Technical Skills Center provides high school career and technical education in 19 courses across five pathways, including aerospace and advanced manufacturing. This creates an earlier entry point for students interested in technical careers, feeding into the WATR Center and Edmonds College pipeline [5].

### Aerospace Joint Apprenticeship Committee (AJAC)

AJAC operates a statewide registered apprenticeship program combining supervised on-the-job training (OJT) with college-level instruction. AJAC programs operate in Everett and Arlington, providing an alternative pathway that includes paid work experience from day one [6].

### Center of Excellence for Aerospace and Advanced Manufacturing

Located at Paine Field, the Center of Excellence facilitates coordination between industry employers and educational institutions, ensuring curriculum alignment with current workforce needs. The Center conducts regular industry surveys and publishes workforce demand forecasts [6].

```
PAINE FIELD EDUCATIONAL ECOSYSTEM
================================================================

                    |
                    v

                    |                      |
                    v                      v
4-Year Degree → EVCC University Center   BPET → Boeing
                    |
                    v
Apprenticeship → AJAC (Everett/Arlington)
                    |
                    v
Coordination → Center of Excellence for Aerospace

All pathways → Snohomish County aerospace supplier network
```

## 7. Fox Infrastructure Group Co-op Model

### Cooperative Structure

The Fox Infrastructure Group envisions a cooperative network model where subsidiary companies are organized as worker-owned cooperatives, with governance structures appropriate to each subsidiary's domain [7]:

| Subsidiary | Domain | Co-op Type |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| FoxFiber | Fiber/mesh networking | Infrastructure co-op |
| SolarFox | Renewable energy systems | Energy co-op |
| FoxCargo | Logistics and transport | Transport co-op |
| FoxCompute | Edge and cloud compute | Technology co-op |
| Fox CapComm | Capital and communications | Financial co-op |

### Governance Principles

The cooperative model is designed around several core principles:

1. **Worker ownership:** Employees of each subsidiary hold equity stakes and voting rights
2. **Community representation:** Board seats reserved for community members in each operating region
3. **Revenue sharing:** Profits distributed according to worker hours and community reinvestment ratios
4. **Transparent operations:** Financial statements published quarterly; major decisions require supermajority
5. **Inter-cooperative solidarity:** Subsidiaries support each other through preferential procurement and shared infrastructure

### Integration with Signal Stack

The Signal Stack infrastructure benefits from the co-op model:

- **FoxFiber** deploys and maintains the mesh network and fiber backhaul
- **SolarFox** provides off-grid power systems for remote nodes
- **FoxCompute** operates edge compute nodes and data center resources
- **FoxCargo** handles logistics for hardware deployment (leveraging Boeing corridor)
- **Fox CapComm** coordinates communications policy and capital allocation

## 8. Puget Sound Prototype Design Center

### Concept

The Puget Sound Prototype Design Center is a rapid-iteration facility where Signal Stack hardware and systems are designed, built, tested, and refined before deployment. The center operates on a continuous loop [7]:

```
RAPID PROTOTYPING LOOP
================================================================

  DESIGN (Paine Field / WATR Center)
     |
     | Concept → Schematic → Layout → BOM
     |
     v
  BUILD (Paine Field fabrication lab)
     |
     | PCB assembly → Antenna fabrication → Enclosure
     |
     v
  TEST (Boeing Field / Paine Field tower site)
     |
     | Bench test → Field deploy → Performance data
     |
     v
  ITERATE (Design review → Next revision)
     |
     | Data analysis → Design refinement → Next build
     |
     └──────────────── back to DESIGN ──────────────

  Target: 2-week cycle time from design change to field data
================================================================
```

### Temporary Housing / Paine Field Barracks Concept

For intensive training cohorts and prototype development sprints, the Paine Field barracks concept provides temporary, affordable housing near the WATR Center. Modeled on the traditional workforce housing approach used by Boeing during wartime production surges, the barracks concept provides [7]:

- Dormitory-style housing for 20-40 people per cohort
- Shared kitchen, laundry, and common areas
- Walking distance to WATR Center and fabrication lab
- 8-16 week maximum stay (aligned with training certificate duration)
- Cost: target $500/month/person (subsidized by co-op revenue)

### Prototype-to-Deployment Pipeline

Successful prototypes follow a defined deployment pipeline:

| Stage | Location | Duration | Gate |
|-------|----------|----------|------|
| Alpha | Paine Field lab | 2-4 weeks | Bench test pass |
| Beta | Paine Field tower site | 2-4 weeks | Field test pass |
| Gamma | Boeing Field corridor | 4-8 weeks | Integration test |
| Pre-production | Paine Field fab | 2-4 weeks | Manufacturing readiness |
| Initial deployment | Puget Sound metro | 4-8 weeks | Operational validation |
| Scale deployment | Western Seaboard | Ongoing | Revenue and performance |

## 9. Data Sovereignty and Governance

### OCAP Principles

Any co-op governance structure operating on or near Coast Salish territories must address data sovereignty. The OCAP (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession) principles, developed by the First Nations Information Governance Centre, provide the framework [8]:

- **Ownership:** The community or nation collectively owns its data
- **Control:** The community controls data collection, use, and disclosure
- **Access:** The community has the right to manage access to its data
- **Possession:** The data is physically held within the community's jurisdiction

### Application to Signal Stack

Signal Stack deployments that serve tribal nation territories must implement data sovereignty at the infrastructure level:

| Principle | Infrastructure Implementation |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Ownership | Data generated on tribal land owned by tribal authority |
| Control | Tribal network administrators control routing and access policies |
| Access | Data access APIs require tribal authority authentication |
| Possession | Local edge compute nodes physically located on tribal land |

> **SAFETY NOTE:** This module references specific Coast Salish nations (Tulalip, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Quinault, Makah, Hoh, Quileute) by their proper nation-specific names, consistent with SC-IND safety gate requirements. The generic term "Indigenous" is avoided in favor of specific attribution. All governance proposals are advisory and require direct nation-to-nation engagement with each tribal government.

### Specific Nations in the Puget Sound Region

The Puget Sound region includes territories of numerous Coast Salish and other nations whose sovereignty must be respected in any infrastructure deployment:

| Nation | Territory | Relevant Signal Stack Infrastructure |
|--------|-----------|-------------------------------------|
| Tulalip Tribes | Snohomish County coastal | Mesh nodes, LPFM, weather stations |
| Muckleshoot Indian Tribe | Auburn/Green River | Edge compute, mesh backbone |

## 10. Rapid Prototyping Loop

### Moffett Field Integration

The rapid prototyping loop extends beyond Puget Sound via the Boeing Narrowband Research (BNR) transport concept. Prototypes developed at Paine Field can be transported to Moffett Field (Mountain View, CA) for Bay Area testing and iteration [7]:

```
EXTENDED PROTOTYPING LOOP
================================================================

  Paine Field (Design + Build)
       |
       | Boeing freight / I-5 ground transport
       |
  Boeing Field (Initial test)
       |
       | BNR transport concept
       |
  Moffett Field (Bay Area test + partner integration)
       |
       | Data back to Paine Field
       |
  Paine Field (Iterate) ← cycle repeats
       |
       | Successful designs enter pre-production
       |
  Scale deployment (Western Seaboard)
================================================================
```

Moffett Field provides access to:
- NASA Ames Research Center facilities and expertise
- Silicon Valley partner ecosystem (hardware vendors, software companies)
- Bay Area test market (different terrain, population density, climate)
- Google/Alphabet facilities (former Loon team members, cloud integration)

This Puget Sound → Bay Area loop mirrors the historical pattern where PNW aerospace innovation (Boeing) integrates with California technology innovation (Silicon Valley) to produce globally deployable systems.
