The electromagnetic spectrum is one of the last true commons. Unlike land, it cannot be permanently owned — only licensed, temporarily, under the public interest standard that has governed US broadcast law since the Radio Act of 1927. When KOMO 4 first broadcast from Seattle in December 1953, the Fisher Broadcasting family held a trust: the public's airwaves in exchange for local service. The spaces between the broadcast channels are not dead air. They are opportunity. Puget Sound is the workshop where that opportunity gets built.

Research Modules

M1: PNW Broadcast Heritage

Broadcast Track A · Historical survey

Complete history of television and radio broadcasting in the Pacific Northwest from first license to ATSC 3.0. Station-by-station profiles from KING-TV (1948) through digital transition. Ownership consolidation from local families to national media groups. KOMO, KING, KIRO, KCPQ, KCTS and the radio heritage from bootlegger stations to LPFM.

M2: Spectrum & Regulatory Framework

Spectrum Track A · Regulatory codex

Full FCC frequency allocation table applied to the PNW. Part 15, Part 73, Part 95, Part 97, Part 101 rules with power limits and antenna restrictions. LPFM licensing windows and eligibility. HAM radio license pathway from Technician through Extra class. NTIA federal allocations. ITU coordination for HAPS.

M3: DIY RF & Computing Builds

RF Track B · Technical builds

Practical electronics guide: Part 15.219-compliant low-power AM station (100mW, 3m antenna). SDR monitoring rig with RTL-SDR and HackRF. AREDN mesh network nodes on Raspberry Pi and ESP32. Rooftop solar weather station with mesh uplink. Complete bills of materials, power budgets, and FCC compliance verification.

M4: Aerial Network Infrastructure

Aerial Track B · HAPS & aerostats

Tethered aerostats (Altaeros ST-300/ST-Flex) and stratospheric platforms (Raven Thunderhead, SoftBank Sunglider, BAE PHASA-35, Sceye). Project Loon legacy architecture. FAA airspace coordination and NOTAM procedures. ITU Resolution 221 HAPS frequencies. Coverage geometry and link budget analysis for Puget Sound basin.

M5: Aerospace Pipeline & Prototype Design

Aerospace Track C · Workforce & governance

Boeing Everett and Paine Field WATR Center workforce pipeline. BPET fast-track hiring. Retraining pathway from aerospace manufacturing into RF/edge compute. Fox Infrastructure Group co-op governance model. Puget Sound Prototype Design Center with rapid prototyping loops. OCAP-aligned data sovereignty framework.

M6: Pacific Rim Compute & Global Deployment

Compute Track C · Infrastructure integration

Amazon AWS and Microsoft Azure Pacific Rim infrastructure. Grand Coulee hydroelectric advantage. Submarine cable topology and trans-Pacific latency. Western Seaboard deployment corridor: Puget Sound to Bay Area to Pacific Rim. Boeing cargo compute concept. Wasteland federation integration for distributed task routing.
