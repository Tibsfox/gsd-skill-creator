## 1. Arctic-Alpine Flora (ELEV-ALPINE: 9,500-14,410 ft / y=196-319)

Plants of the highest band endure sustained winds, intense UV, desiccating cold, and growing seasons measured in weeks. Vegetation is prostrate, cushion-form, or crustose, hugging volcanic rock and glacial till for warmth.

---

### Alpine Phlox (*Phlox diffusa*)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Polemoniaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Low, mat-forming perennial reaching 2-4 inches tall with dense cushions of narrow, needle-like leaves and profuse pink to lavender flowers. One of the most recognizable alpine plants of the Cascades, forming tight cushions on talus and volcanic pumice.

**Elevation Range:** 6,000-10,500 ft (y=109-222)
**Distribution:** Throughout Cascade Range on volcanic peaks from Mt. Baker to Mt. Shasta; also Olympic Mountains. Most abundant on well-drained pumice and volcanic substrates.

**Ecological Significance:**
Pioneer species on exposed volcanic substrate, stabilizing loose pumice and trapping windblown organic matter to begin soil formation. Cushion form creates microhabitat for invertebrates and smaller plants sheltered from wind. Important early-season nectar source for alpine pollinators.

**Threats:** Climate-driven upslope tree encroachment reducing alpine habitat; recreational trampling at popular hiking destinations; altered snowmelt timing.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in available ethnobotanical sources for PNW nations.

**Key Sources:** Hitchcock & Cronquist (1973); NPS Mt. Rainier plant surveys; Franklin & Dyrness (1988)

### Tolmie's Saxifrage (*Micranthes tolmiei*)

**Taxonomy:** Saxifragales > Saxifragaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Compact rosette-forming perennial, 2-6 inches tall, with fleshy, spatulate leaves and clusters of small white flowers on slender stems. Named for William Fraser Tolmie, Hudson's Bay Company physician who explored Mt. Rainier in 1833.

**Elevation Range:** 6,500-12,000 ft (y=121-257)
**Distribution:** Cascade Range and Olympic Mountains at alpine elevations; moist rock crevices and seepage areas near snowmelt.

**Ecological Significance:**
Indicator of late-persisting snowmelt areas. Root systems stabilize thin alpine soils on steep slopes. Associated with alpine seeps that provide critical water sources for mountain goats and pikas during late summer.

**Threats:** Reduced snowpack duration altering moisture availability; climate-driven habitat compression as tree line advances.

**Key Sources:** Hitchcock & Cronquist (1973); NPS Mt. Rainier alpine vegetation monitoring

### Lyall's Lupine (*Lupinus lyallii*)

**Taxonomy:** Fabales > Fabaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Low-growing alpine lupine, 2-6 inches tall, with silvery-haired palmate leaves and compact racemes of blue-purple flowers. One of several alpine lupine species in the PNW, distinguished by its dense silvery pubescence adapted to intense UV radiation.

**Elevation Range:** 7,000-11,000 ft (y=134-233)
**Distribution:** High Cascades from Mt. Baker to southern Oregon; rocky alpine slopes, moraines, and pumice flats.

**Ecological Significance:**
Nitrogen fixer — bacterial symbiosis in root nodules converts atmospheric N2 to plant-available nitrogen in nutrient-poor alpine soils. This nurse function enables establishment of other alpine species on otherwise sterile volcanic substrate. Critical pioneer on Mt. St. Helens pumice plains and glacial forefields.

**Threats:** Climate change compressing alpine habitat; volcanic disturbance (both creates and destroys habitat).

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Dale et al. (2005) — Mt. St. Helens succession studies

### Whitebark Pine (*Pinus albicaulis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Threatened (2022) | WA: Not listed | IUCN: EN | NatureServe: G3G4

**Description:** High-elevation five-needled pine reaching 30-60 ft in favorable sites but reduced to krummholz (ground-hugging, wind-sculpted) form at tree line. Thick, whitish-gray bark gives the species its name. Seeds are large, wingless, and highly nutritious (30%+ fat content).

**Elevation Range:** 5,500-10,500 ft (y=97-222)
**Distribution:** Throughout Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rockies at subalpine to alpine elevations. Dominates windswept ridges and krummholz zone.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone mutualism with Clark's nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*), which is the sole effective seed disperser — nutcrackers cache 30,000-100,000 seeds per year, and unretrieved caches germinate into new trees. Whitebark pine cannot regenerate without nutcrackers. Seeds also provide critical fat reserves for grizzly bears, red squirrels, and other wildlife. Regulates snowmelt by shading snowpack and trapping wind-transported snow, sustaining downstream water supplies.

**Threats:** White pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*) — an introduced pathogen killing >50% of trees across range; mountain pine beetle outbreaks amplified by warming winters; fire suppression allowing shade-tolerant competitors (subalpine fir) to overtop and replace whitebark pine; climate change compressing suitable habitat upslope.

**Cultural Significance:** Nut pine seeds harvested by Ktunaxa, Secwepemc, and other Interior nations as a high-calorie food source. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Tomback et al. (2001) — *Whitebark Pine Communities*; USFWS ESA listing (2022); Keane et al. — USFS restoration guidelines

**Cross-Module References:**

### Ice Worm Habitat: Snow Algae (*Chlamydomonas nivalis*)

**Taxonomy:** Chlamydomonadales > Chlamydomonadaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Unicellular green alga that produces red pigment (astaxanthin) as UV protection, coloring snowfields pink to red ("watermelon snow"). Cells 10-30 micrometers in diameter; visible only en masse as pink-stained snow surface.

**Elevation Range:** 8,000-14,410 ft (y=159-319)
**Distribution:** Permanent and semi-permanent snowfields on all Cascade volcanic peaks; most visible in late summer as snowpack compacts.

**Ecological Significance:**
Base of the alpine snowfield food web, supporting the glacier ice worm (*Mesenchytraeus solifugus*) — a PNW endemic annelid that lives exclusively in glacial ice. Snow algae photosynthesis on glacial surfaces is a significant contributor to surface albedo reduction, accelerating snowmelt in a positive feedback loop with climate warming.

**Threats:** Glacier retreat eliminating permanent snow habitat; accelerating melt cycles reducing growing season on snowfields.

**Key Sources:** Hoham & Remias (2020) — Snow algae biology; Shain et al. (2000) — ice worm ecology

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-terrestrial: Ice worm (specialist consumer of snow algae)
- See ecological-networks: Watershed Connectivity (glacial melt contribution)

## 2. Subalpine Flora (ELEV-SUBALPINE: 5,000-9,500 ft / y=84-196)

The zone of deep snowpack, explosive summer wildflower displays, and the parkland ecotone. Tree species here define the character of the PNW high country.

---

### Subalpine Fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Narrow, spire-shaped conifer reaching 60-100 ft with smooth, gray bark blistered with resin pockets. The classic "Christmas tree" silhouette of the PNW subalpine. Needles are flat, blue-green, with a distinctive skunk-like fragrance when crushed.

**Elevation Range:** 4,000-7,500 ft (y=59-147)
**Distribution:** Throughout Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Ranges at subalpine elevations. Dominates the closed-canopy subalpine forest below parkland.

**Ecological Significance:**
Defines the subalpine forest type with mountain hemlock. Dense stands create deep shade and maintain cool microclimates that retain snowpack later into summer. Standing dead snags provide nesting cavities for woodpeckers, owls, and cavity-nesting mammals. Heavy seed years support Clark's nutcracker, pine siskin, and red crossbill populations.

**Threats:** Fire suppression increasing stand density and fuel loading; climate change shifting suitable habitat upslope; balsam woolly adelgid (*Adelges piceae*) — an introduced insect causing mortality in some PNW populations.

**Cultural Significance:** Pitch used medicinally by multiple Interior nations for skin poultices and respiratory ailments. Boughs used for bedding and temporary shelter. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Agee (1993) — *Fire Ecology of Pacific Northwest Forests*

### Mountain Hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Graceful conifer reaching 75-100 ft with a characteristically drooping leader and pendulous branches. Needles radiate around the twig (unlike flat-sprayed western hemlock). Bark is dark reddish-brown, deeply furrowed on old trees.

**Elevation Range:** 4,000-7,500 ft (y=59-147)
**Distribution:** Cascades from British Columbia to northern California; Olympics; Coast Ranges where maritime influence creates heavy snowpack. The tree-line species on Mt. Rainier.

**Ecological Significance:**
Co-dominant with subalpine fir in the PNW subalpine zone. Extremely snow-tolerant — adapted to snowpacks exceeding 15 ft that persist into July. In the parkland ecotone, mountain hemlock forms tree clumps (krummholz and tree islands) separated by meadows, creating the high-diversity mosaic habitat of the subalpine. Old mountain hemlocks (500+ years) on exposed ridges serve as seed sources for meadow colonization during warm periods.

**Threats:** Reduced snowpack exposing shallow roots to freeze-thaw damage; warming temperatures favoring competitors at lower elevations; Swiss needle cast in some populations.

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); NPS alpine/subalpine vegetation monitoring programs

### Alaska Yellow Cedar (*Callitropsis nootkatensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Cupressaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Threatened (2023) | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC globally but declining in PNW

**Description:** Slow-growing conifer reaching 80-120 ft with drooping, flattened spray-like branches and shaggy, grayish bark that peels in vertical strips. Wood is extremely rot-resistant with a distinctive sulfurous odor. Can live over 1,500 years.

**Elevation Range:** 2,500-6,500 ft (y=9-121)
**Distribution:** Coastal Alaska through Cascades and Olympics. Most common in maritime-influenced montane and subalpine zones with heavy snowpack.

**Ecological Significance:**
One of the longest-lived trees in the PNW, Alaska yellow cedar is a foundation species of wet montane and subalpine forests. Its decay-resistant wood persists as standing dead snags and downed logs for centuries, providing critical habitat for cavity nesters and nurse log function long after death. Currently experiencing widespread die-off across its range due to reduced snowpack — shallow roots evolved under deep snow insulation now freeze when exposed to winter cold snaps without protective snow cover. This die-off is one of the most visible climate change impacts on PNW forests.

**Threats:** Climate-driven die-off from reduced snowpack (USFS documentation); root disease; extremely slow growth makes recovery from disturbance very prolonged.

**Cultural Significance:** Highly valued by Tlingit, Haida, and Nuu-chah-nulth for carving paddles, masks, and totem poles due to its fine, even grain and rot resistance. Wood used for bows, canoe paddles, and fishing implements. Bark woven into hats, capes, and rope by Tlingit and Haida weavers — considered superior to red cedar bark for some applications. (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Heritage Skills Cross-Reference:** Cedar Path (expanded to include yellow cedar cultural uses)

**Key Sources:** Beier et al. (2008) — yellow cedar decline; USFWS ESA listing (2023); Stewart (1984)

### Avalanche Lily (*Erythronium montanum*)

**Taxonomy:** Liliales > Liliaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Herbaceous perennial with 2 broad, elliptical basal leaves and nodding white flowers with reflexed petals and yellow centers on stems 6-12 inches tall. Among the first plants to bloom after snowmelt, often pushing through the retreating snow margin.

**Elevation Range:** 4,500-7,500 ft (y=72-147)
**Distribution:** Cascades and Olympics in subalpine meadows. Abundant at Paradise (Mt. Rainier), where meadow displays attract hundreds of thousands of visitors annually.

**Ecological Significance:**
Signature species of PNW subalpine meadows. Blooms immediately after snowmelt in dense carpets, providing critical early-season nectar and pollen for bumblebees, butterflies, and hummingbirds emerging from dormancy. Phenology tightly coupled to snowmelt timing — shifting bloom dates are a sensitive indicator of climate change.

**Threats:** Earlier snowmelt decoupling bloom timing from pollinator emergence; recreational trampling at popular subalpine destinations; climate-driven tree encroachment into meadows.

**Key Sources:** NPS Mt. Rainier phenology monitoring; Dunwiddie (1986) — subalpine meadow dynamics

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-terrestrial: Rufous hummingbird (early-season nectar)
- See ecological-networks: Watershed Connectivity (snowmelt phenology)

### Glacier Lily (*Erythronium grandiflorum*)

**Taxonomy:** Liliales > Liliaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Herbaceous perennial similar to avalanche lily but with bright yellow, nodding flowers and mottled basal leaves. Corms (underground storage organs) are starchy and nutritious. Stems 6-12 inches tall.

**Elevation Range:** 3,000-7,000 ft (y=22-134)
**Distribution:** Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rockies in moist subalpine and upper montane meadows.

**Ecological Significance:**
Like avalanche lily, blooms immediately after snowmelt and provides early-season pollinator resources. Corms are an important food source for grizzly bears in the Rockies and were historically harvested by Interior nations. Creates dense post-snowmelt displays that shift seasonally upslope, providing a moving wave of resources from May through August.

**Threats:** Climate-altered snowmelt timing; recreational trampling; deer and elk herbivory in some areas.

**Cultural Significance:** Corms harvested as food by Nlaka'pamux, Secwepemc, Ktunaxa, and other Interior nations. Harvested in spring with digging sticks; specific management practices (selective harvest, timing) ensured sustainable yield. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Turner (2014); Hitchcock & Cronquist (1973)

### Bear Grass (*Xerophyllum tenax*)

**Taxonomy:** Liliales > Melanthiaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Robust perennial with dense tufts of tough, grass-like basal leaves and a spectacular tall (2-5 ft) flowering stalk bearing a dense, creamy-white terminal raceme. Blooms irregularly — individual plants may flower only once every 5-7 years.

**Elevation Range:** 3,000-7,000 ft (y=22-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW montane and subalpine zones; most common on well-drained slopes, forest openings, and burns.

**Ecological Significance:**
Fire-adapted species that resprouts vigorously from rhizomes after burning. Flowers provide nectar for pollinators during the brief subalpine summer. Dense leaf tufts trap snow, create soil moisture reserves, and provide cover for small mammals. Irregular mass flowering years produce dramatic landscape-scale displays visible from miles away.

**Threats:** Fire suppression reducing open habitat; overharvest for commercial floral trade (leaves are commercially harvested for flower arrangements); climate change.

**Cultural Significance:** Leaves are among the most important weaving materials for multiple PNW nations. Used by Nlaka'pamux, Secwepemc, Yakama, Nez Perce, Umatilla, and Coast Salish weavers for watertight baskets, hats, and decorative overlay. Carefully selected and prepared — only the bleached inner leaves are used. Intensive harvest management practices include rotation of gathering sites. (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Heritage Skills Cross-Reference:** Cedar Path (weaving materials)

**Key Sources:** Turner (2014); Hummel & Lake (2015) — bear grass ecology and harvest; USFS PNW Research Station

### Indian Paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Orobanchaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Hemiparasitic perennial herbs, 6-24 inches tall, with spectacular scarlet, orange, or magenta floral bracts that outshine the small, tubular true flowers hidden within. Multiple species occur in the PNW (notably *C. miniata* and *C. hispida*), collectively forming some of the most vivid displays in subalpine meadows. Bracts and upper leaves are the colorful structures; the actual flowers are greenish and inconspicuous.

**Elevation Range:** 4,000-7,500 ft (y=59-146)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW subalpine meadows from British Columbia to Oregon; common on Mount Rainier, in the North Cascades, and Olympic subalpine parklands.

**Ecological Significance:**
A keystone pollinator plant of subalpine meadows — bright bracts attract hummingbirds (rufous and calliope), bumblebees, and butterflies. Hemiparasitic on grass and forb roots via haustorial connections, tapping host vascular tissue for water and nutrients without killing the host. This parasitism subtly alters competitive dynamics in meadow plant communities, suppressing dominant grasses and increasing overall species diversity. Mass blooms in July-August are a signature visual feature of PNW subalpine landscapes.

**Threats:** Climate change reducing snowpack duration and altering meadow phenology; subalpine meadow encroachment by conifers; trampling from concentrated recreational use on popular trails.

**Cultural Significance:** Several PNW nations used paintbrush species for dye (producing red-orange pigments from bracts) and in medicinal preparations. Nlaka'pamux and Secwepemc peoples used root and flower preparations for various ailments. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Mathews (1999) — *Cascade-Olympic Natural History*; USFS PNW Research Station

**Cross-Module References:**

## 3. Montane Flora (ELEV-MONTANE: 3,000-5,000 ft / y=34-84)

The rain-snow transition zone, home to dense silver fir forests and the montane meadows maintained by cold air drainage.

---

### Pacific Silver Fir (*Abies amabilis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Tall conifer reaching 150-200 ft with smooth, silver-gray bark (young trees) becoming plated and rough with age. Needles are flat, dark green above, with two white stomatal bands below arranged in distinct flat sprays. The most shade-tolerant true fir in the PNW.

**Elevation Range:** 2,500-5,500 ft (y=9-97)
**Distribution:** Western Cascades and Olympics at mid-elevation; dominates the Abies amabilis Zone described by Franklin & Dyrness.

**Ecological Significance:**
Defines the montane forest zone on the wet side of the Cascades. Extreme shade tolerance allows regeneration beneath dense canopy, creating forests with >95% canopy closure. Dominant late-successional species that gradually replaces Douglas-fir in the montane zone over centuries. Heavy seed crops support band-tailed pigeon and red crossbill.

**Threats:** Potential upslope migration of western hemlock zone; root diseases; Douglas-fir bark beetle outbreaks during drought.

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Agee (1993)

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fungi-microbiome-survey: Ectomycorrhizal networks (Abies associations)
- See fauna-terrestrial: Northern spotted owl (montane old-growth nesting)

### Noble Fir (*Abies procera*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** The tallest true fir in the world, reaching 200-250 ft. Bark smooth and gray when young, developing reddish-brown plates with age. Needles blue-green, curved upward on the twig. Cones are spectacular — 6-9 inches long, cylindrical, with exserted bracts giving a shingle-like appearance. A PNW endemic.

**Elevation Range:** 3,000-5,500 ft (y=22-97)
**Distribution:** Endemic to the PNW — western Cascades from southern Washington to central Oregon; isolated populations in the Columbia River Gorge and Siskiyous.

**Ecological Significance:**
A PNW endemic that reaches its greatest stature in the montane zone, noble fir is the tallest species of *Abies* on Earth. Mixed noble fir-Pacific silver fir forests on the western Cascades harbor exceptional bryophyte and lichen diversity. The species establishes after fire more readily than Pacific silver fir, occupying an intermediate successional niche.

**Threats:** Limited endemic range makes the species vulnerable to climate change and disease; Swiss needle cast at lower elevations; commercial harvest for Christmas trees reduces genetic diversity in wild populations.

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Packee (1990) — Silvics of North America

### Western White Pine (*Pinus monticola*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC (but drastically reduced by blister rust)

**Description:** Tall five-needled pine reaching 100-175 ft with distinctive silvery-gray bark on young trees becoming checked plates with age. Needles in bundles of 5, slender, 2-4 inches long. Cones elongate, 6-12 inches, drooping on flexible stalks.

**Elevation Range:** 2,000-6,000 ft (y=-3-109)
**Distribution:** Cascades and northern Rockies at mid-elevation; once abundant in mixed conifer stands, now greatly reduced.

**Ecological Significance:**
Historically a major component of montane forests before white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*) killed an estimated 90%+ of trees across much of its range in the early-to-mid 1900s. Surviving trees with natural rust resistance are the focus of intensive USFS breeding and restoration programs. Seeds provide food for squirrels, nutcrackers, and woodpeckers.

**Threats:** White pine blister rust (introduced, ongoing); mountain pine beetle; historical and ongoing logging of large specimens.

**Key Sources:** USFS blister rust resistance breeding program; Tomback et al. (2001)

### Vine Maple (*Acer circinatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Sapindales > Sapindaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Multi-stemmed deciduous shrub or small tree reaching 15-30 ft with distinctive circular (orbicular), 7-9 lobed leaves and paired samaras (winged seeds). Spectacular fall color — brilliant red, orange, and yellow, providing the primary autumn color in PNW forests.

**Elevation Range:** 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from British Columbia to northern California; ubiquitous understory species in Douglas-fir and western hemlock forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
Dominant understory species in PNW conifer forests, vine maple creates dense sub-canopy layers that provide thermal cover for wildlife, food for browsers (deer, elk), and structural complexity. Branches that contact the ground root adventitiously (layering), creating impenetrable thickets. Leaf litter decomposes rapidly, contributing significantly to soil nutrient cycling in conifer-dominated forests where needle litter decomposes slowly.

**Threats:** Generally secure; heavy deer/elk browse can suppress regeneration in some areas.

### Grand Fir (*Abies grandis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large conifer reaching 150-230 ft with a narrow, spire-like crown. Bark smooth and grayish on young trees, becoming thick and furrowed with age. Needles flat, arranged in two distinct horizontal rows on the twig (like a comb), dark green above with two white stomatal bands below. Crushed needles release a distinctive citrus-like fragrance — the most aromatic of all PNW firs.

**Elevation Range:** 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84)
**Distribution:** Both west-side and east-side Cascades, distinguishing it from Pacific silver fir (primarily west-side). Ranges from southern British Columbia through western Montana, Oregon, and Washington. Most common in moist valleys and lower montane slopes.

**Ecological Significance:**
Grand fir occupies an important ecological niche as a mid-elevation species bridging lowland Douglas-fir forests and upper montane Pacific silver fir zones. On the east side of the Cascades, grand fir forests represent some of the most productive ecosystems, supporting high densities of white-tailed deer and elk. Moderate shade tolerance allows persistence in mixed-species stands. Significant timber species — the wood is light, straight-grained, and commercially important for lumber and pulp.

**Threats:** Root diseases (laminated root rot, *Phellinus sulphurascens*); Douglas-fir tussock moth and western spruce budworm defoliation; fire exclusion leading to unnaturally dense stands susceptible to insect outbreaks and stand-replacing fire.

**Cultural Significance:** Lummi and other Coast Salish peoples used grand fir pitch medicinally — applied as a poultice for wounds, burns, and skin infections, and taken internally for colds and respiratory ailments. Boughs used for bedding and spiritual cleansing. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL)

### Western Larch (*Larix occidentalis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Tall, straight conifer reaching 100-180 ft — the only deciduous conifer in the PNW. Needles soft, pale green, in clusters of 15-30 on short spur shoots, turning brilliant gold before dropping in autumn. Bark on mature trees extremely thick (3-6 inches), deeply furrowed, cinnamon-red — among the most fire-resistant bark of any PNW tree. Oldest documented specimens exceed 900 years.

**Elevation Range:** 2,000-5,500 ft (y=9-97)
**Distribution:** East side of the Cascades and Blue Mountains of Oregon and Washington; extends into northern Idaho and western Montana. Absent from the wet west side — a strictly interior species requiring cold winters and relatively dry summers.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only deciduous conifer in the PNW, western larch creates a unique ecological dynamic: its annual needle drop provides a pulse of readily decomposable litter to forest floors otherwise dominated by slow-decaying conifer needles, significantly boosting soil nutrient cycling. Extremely fire-adapted — thick bark, high crown, and self-pruning lower branches allow mature trees to survive moderate surface fires that kill competitors. Post-fire, larch's shade intolerance and fast early growth make it an aggressive colonizer of burned sites. Autumn larch forests in the eastern Cascades are a major scenic attraction.

**Threats:** Fire suppression leading to replacement by shade-tolerant grand fir and Douglas-fir; western larch casebearer (*Coleophora laricella*, an introduced pest); larch dwarf mistletoe; climate change potentially shifting suitable habitat upslope.

**Cultural Significance:** Significant food and material resource for Ktunaxa (Kootenai) and Salish-Kootenai peoples. Cambium (inner bark) was harvested in spring as a food source — peeled, dried, and eaten or mixed with other foods. Sweet sap collected and used as a beverage and sweetener. Wood used for fuel and construction. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Schmidt & Shearer (1990) — *Larix occidentalis* silvics; Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Turner (2014)

### Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Massive conifer reaching 250-330 ft tall and 8-12 ft diameter in old-growth, with deeply furrowed, corky bark up to 12 inches thick (fire-adapted). Needles flat, soft, radiating around the twig. Cones distinctive — 3-4 inches with three-pointed bracts extending beyond the scales ("mouse tails"). The most commercially important timber species in North America.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,500 ft (y=-41-97)
**Distribution:** Dominant tree of PNW lowland and montane forests from British Columbia to northern California. The most widely distributed conifer in western North America.

**Ecological Significance:**
The defining tree of the PNW. Old-growth Douglas-fir forests accumulate over 500 tonnes biomass per hectare — more than any terrestrial ecosystem on Earth, including tropical rainforests. Individual trees live 750-1,200+ years. Massive trunks provide nesting platforms for marbled murrelet and northern spotted owl. Fallen logs persist 200-500+ years, serving as nurse logs for the next forest generation. The tree's thick, corky bark is an adaptation to the low-frequency, high-severity fire regime of the PNW west side — Douglas-fir survives fires that kill all competitors, establishing dominance for centuries.

**Threats:** Historical logging removed >90% of old-growth; ongoing timber harvest of remaining mature stands; climate-driven drought stress and Douglas-fir bark beetle outbreaks; Swiss needle cast fungus (*Nothophaeocryptopus gaeumannii*) increasingly affecting coastal populations.

**Cultural Significance:** Used by many PNW nations for fuel, tools, and pitch. Pitch applied as a sealant, adhesive, and medicine for skin wounds. Not as culturally central as western red cedar but omnipresent in the material landscape. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

### Western Red Cedar (*Thuja plicata*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Cupressaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Massive conifer reaching 200-250 ft tall and 8-20 ft diameter with distinctive fibrous, cinnamon-red bark that shreds in vertical strips. Foliage in flat, overlapping scale-like sprays with a sweet, spicy fragrance. Buttressed, fluted trunk base. Can live over 1,500 years.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,500 ft (y=-41-72)
**Distribution:** Coastal British Columbia through western Cascades and Olympics to northern California. Most abundant in wet, valley-bottom sites and lower montane forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
Co-dominant with Douglas-fir and western hemlock in PNW lowland forests. Extremely rot-resistant heartwood (due to thujaplicin compounds) means that dead cedars persist as snags and logs for centuries, providing long-duration habitat structure. Living cedars develop complex canopy architecture with multiple leaders, dead tops, and epiphyte gardens that provide microsites for dozens of species. Western red cedar is the climatic climax species in many wet lowland forests, gradually replacing Douglas-fir over millennial timescales.

**Threats:** Climate-driven drought stress causing "cedar decline" in drier parts of range; deer and elk browse on seedlings severely impacting regeneration in some areas; cultural harvest pressure for bark and wood.

**Cultural Significance:** THE culturally central tree of all Northwest Coast nations — "the Tree of Life" for Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, Haida, Tlingit, and many others. Used for longhouses, ocean-going canoes (up to 60 ft), bentwood boxes, clothing, blankets, rope, basketry, ceremonial masks, totem poles, and regalia. Cedar bark harvesting follows strict protocols of gratitude and selective harvest, embodying reciprocal relationship with the living tree. Cedar is inseparable from Northwest Coast identity — material culture, spiritual practice, and ecological knowledge are unified in this single species. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

### Sitka Spruce (*Picea sitchensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** The largest spruce species in the world, reaching 200-300+ ft tall and 8-16 ft diameter in old-growth. Sharp, stiff needles radiate around the twig (painfully prickly to handle). Bark thin, scaly, gray-brown. Buttressed trunk base in large specimens.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,000 ft (y=-41--3)
**Distribution:** Strictly coastal — within 50 miles of the Pacific Ocean from Alaska to northern California. Dominates the narrow coastal fog belt. Olympic Peninsula coastal forests contain the largest specimens.

**Ecological Significance:**
Defines the Sitka spruce zone — the narrow coastal fog belt where maritime influence is strongest. The largest trees in the PNW (by volume) are Sitka spruce on the Olympic Peninsula; the Quinault Lake spruce exceeded 300 ft. Spruce forests intercept fog drip, adding 10-30% additional moisture to the ecosystem. Coastal old-growth spruce forests are among the most productive terrestrial ecosystems on Earth, rivaling tropical rainforests in biomass accumulation.

**Threats:** Historical logging removed most large old-growth; storm damage from coastal exposure; spruce aphid outbreaks; climate change reducing fog frequency.

**Cultural Significance:** Used by Makah, Quileute, and other coastal nations for root baskets, pitch, and medicine. Spruce root hats are an iconic art form of Haida, Tlingit, and Nuu-chah-nulth weavers. Wood used historically for aircraft construction due to its exceptional strength-to-weight ratio (the "Spruce Production Division" of WWI). (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); NPS Olympic National Park forest surveys; DellaSala (2011)

### Western Hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*)

**Taxonomy:** Pinales > Pinaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Graceful conifer reaching 150-225 ft with a characteristically drooping leader (top). Needles flat, unequal in length (heterophylla = "different leaves"), arranged in flat sprays. Bark thin and gray-brown, developing deep furrows with age. Small, abundant cones (3/4 inch).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,500 ft (y=-41-72)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from Alaska to northern California; dominant tree of the Tsuga heterophylla Zone (the most extensive vegetation zone in the PNW).

**Ecological Significance:**
The climax dominant of PNW lowland forests — the species that would dominate in the absence of fire. Extremely shade-tolerant, western hemlock regenerates profusely beneath Douglas-fir canopy and eventually replaces it in the multi-century succession sequence. Washington's state tree. Hemlock litter creates acidic, nutrient-poor soils that favor ericaceous understory (salal, huckleberry). Old-growth hemlocks develop massive root systems that, when tipped by windthrow, create pit-and-mound microtopography characteristic of old-growth forests.

**Threats:** Climate-driven drought stress (shallow root system vulnerable to summer water deficit); western hemlock looper moth outbreaks; hemlock woolly adelgid approaching PNW from eastern range.

**Cultural Significance:** Bark used by Coast Salish and other nations to tan hides (high tannin content). Inner bark eaten in spring as emergency food. Boughs used for bedding and temporary shelters. Hemlock pitch used medicinally. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Packee (1990)

### Bigleaf Maple (*Acer macrophyllum*)

**Taxonomy:** Sapindales > Sapindaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** The largest maple in western North America, reaching 80-100 ft with a broad, spreading crown. Leaves are the largest of any maple — 8-12 inches across with 5 deep lobes. Trunk heavily colonized by mosses, ferns (licorice fern), and lichens in the wet PNW climate, creating "moss gardens" in the canopy.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,500 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from British Columbia to California; most common in riparian corridors, forest edges, and disturbed sites.

**Ecological Significance:**
Prolific producer of large leaves that decompose rapidly, contributing significantly to nutrient cycling in otherwise nutrient-poor conifer-dominated forests. The moss-draped trunks of old bigleaf maples support entire epiphytic ecosystems — in Olympic Valley old-growth forests, individual trees may carry 40-80 lbs of epiphytes. Currently experiencing widespread decline from a new disease ("bigleaf maple decline") affecting trees across western Washington.

**Threats:** Bigleaf maple decline (cause under investigation — possibly a combination of drought stress and pathogens); climate change; invasive ivy smothering understory trees.

**Cultural Significance:** Wood used by Coast Salish for bowls, paddles, and spindle whorls (for spinning mountain goat wool and dog hair). Sap can be boiled down to syrup though this was not a major traditional practice. Bark used in some medicinal preparations. (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); WA DNR — bigleaf maple decline research

### Red Alder (*Alnus rubra*)

**Taxonomy:** Fagales > Betulaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Fast-growing deciduous tree reaching 60-100 ft with smooth, pale gray bark mottled with white lichen patches. Leaves doubly serrate, 3-6 inches. Male catkins droop conspicuously in early spring before leaf-out. Short-lived (80-100 years maximum).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,500 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW; dominant early-successional hardwood on disturbed sites, riparian corridors, and landslides.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most important nitrogen-fixing tree in the PNW, red alder hosts *Frankia* bacteria in root nodules that fix 40-300 kg N/ha/year — fundamentally transforming soil chemistry after disturbance. Alder stands enrich depleted soils, enabling subsequent conifer establishment. In riparian zones, alder leaf litter drives stream invertebrate food webs that support juvenile salmon. The species colonizes rapidly after disturbance (logging, fire, landslide), stabilizing exposed soil and beginning the long-term succession toward conifer forest.

**Threats:** Generally secure as a pioneer species; potential decline if disturbance regimes change dramatically.

**Cultural Significance:** Wood used by multiple PNW nations for smoking salmon (alder smoke is preferred for its mild, sweet flavor — this practice continues today and is commercially important). Bark produces a red-orange dye used by Coast Salish, Haida, and Tlingit for baskets, clothing, and fishing nets. Bark also used medicinally as a poultice and for digestive ailments. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Harrington et al. (1994) — Biology of red alder

### Pacific Madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Ericaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Broadleaf evergreen tree reaching 50-100 ft with extraordinary smooth, peeling bark that reveals green/reddish new bark beneath — one of the most visually striking trees in the PNW. Leaves thick, glossy, dark green. Produces clusters of white urn-shaped flowers and bright red-orange berries.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,500 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Puget lowlands, San Juan Islands, Oregon coast, and dry rain-shadow areas; south-facing slopes and rocky sites.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only broadleaf evergreen tree native to the PNW. Madrone berries are a critical late-fall food source for band-tailed pigeons, varied thrushes, robins, and cedar waxwings. The species tolerates poor, rocky, well-drained soils where conifers struggle. Madrone litter decomposes differently from conifer needles, creating distinct soil microbiomes beneath its canopy.

**Threats:** Madrone leaf blight (Didymosporium arbuticola) — a fungal disease causing premature defoliation; fire suppression reducing the open canopy conditions madrone requires; competition from faster-growing Douglas-fir in fire-suppressed landscapes.

**Cultural Significance:** Berries eaten fresh or dried by Coast Salish nations; bark and leaves used medicinally for colds, stomach ailments, and skin conditions. Wood exceptionally hard and used for tool handles. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** McDonald & Tappeiner (1990) — madrone ecology; Franklin & Dyrness (1988)

### Oregon Grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*)

**Taxonomy:** Ranunculales > Berberidaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Evergreen shrub 3-8 ft tall with holly-like compound leaves (5-9 leaflets with spiny margins), clusters of bright yellow flowers in spring, and blue-purple grape-like berries in late summer. Oregon state flower.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,500 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW; dry to mesic forests, rocky slopes, forest edges.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important understory shrub providing year-round cover (evergreen) and late-summer berries for birds (varied thrush, robin, towhee) and mammals (black bear, coyote). Yellow root bark contains berberine — an antimicrobial compound. The species persists under dense canopy but fruits most prolifically in forest openings and edges.

**Threats:** Generally secure; some populations browsed by deer; invasive ivy competition in urban-adjacent forests.

**Cultural Significance:** Berries eaten by many PNW nations, often mixed with other berries. Root bark produces a brilliant yellow dye used by Coast Salish, Nlaka'pamux, and other nations for basketry. Root bark also used extensively as medicine — internal for digestive ailments, external as a wash for skin conditions. Oregon state flower since 1899. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

### Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Ericaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Dense evergreen shrub 2-8 ft tall (can reach 15 ft in coastal forests) with leathery, oval leaves, pinkish urn-shaped flowers, and dark purple-blue berries. Forms dense, often impenetrable thickets in the forest understory.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-22)
**Distribution:** Throughout coastal and lowland PNW; the dominant understory shrub in Sitka spruce and coastal western hemlock forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
The dominant understory shrub across millions of acres of PNW forest. Salal's dense growth suppresses tree seedling establishment, significantly influencing forest regeneration dynamics. The ericoid mycorrhizal fungi associated with salal roots access nutrients from organic matter that conifer mycorrhizae cannot, creating a distinct nutrient cycling pathway in the forest understory. Berries provide food for many bird and mammal species.

**Threats:** Generally secure and abundant; a "problem species" for forest managers because its dense growth inhibits conifer regeneration after harvest.

**Cultural Significance:** One of the most important berry species for Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, and other coastal nations. Berries were dried in large quantities, pressed into cakes, and stored for winter. Salal berry cakes were a major trade item, often mixed with eulachon (oolichan) grease. Leaves used medicinally for digestive ailments and as a poultice. Still harvested commercially today — PNW salal foliage is one of the most commercially harvested non-timber forest products in North America. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

### Sword Fern (*Polystichum munitum*)

**Taxonomy:** Polypodiales > Dryopteridaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large evergreen fern with erect fronds 3-5 ft long arising from a stout, woody rhizome. Individual pinnae (leaflets) have a distinctive "sword hilt" projection at the base. Leathery fronds persist year-round, creating the characteristic green carpet of PNW forest floors.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from Alaska to California; the defining understory fern of PNW conifer forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most abundant fern in PNW forests, sword fern carpets the forest floor across millions of acres. Dense frond cover provides thermal insulation for small mammals, moisture retention for amphibians, and invertebrate habitat. Individual clumps may persist for centuries — some rhizomes are estimated at 500+ years old. Currently experiencing unexplained die-offs in some areas of the Puget lowlands ("sword fern die-off") — cause under investigation.

**Threats:** Sword fern die-off (emerging concern); deer browse in areas of high deer density; invasive plant competition (ivy, laurel).

**Cultural Significance:** Fronds used by many PNW nations to line cooking pits, dry berries and fish, and as temporary floor covering. Rhizomes peeled and roasted as famine food by some Coast Salish nations. Fronds used in ceremonies and as decoration. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

### Huckleberry (*Vaccinium* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Ericaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC (most species)

**Description:** Deciduous or evergreen shrubs, 1-6 ft tall depending on species. Multiple species occur across PNW elevation bands: red huckleberry (*V. parvifolium*) in lowland forests; evergreen huckleberry (*V. ovatum*) on the coast; big huckleberry (*V. membranaceum*) and mountain huckleberry (*V. deliciosum*) at higher elevations. Berries small, round, blue to red depending on species.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW across all forested elevation bands. Different species dominate different elevations, creating a continuous huckleberry resource from sea level to subalpine.

**Ecological Significance:**
Collectively the most ecologically important berry-producing shrubs in the PNW. Berries provide critical pre-winter calories for black bears, grizzly bears, and many bird species. Ericoid mycorrhizal fungi associated with huckleberry roots access nutrients unavailable to other plants. Some species (V. membranaceum) are fire-adapted, resprouting vigorously after burning — historically maintained by Indigenous burning practices.

**Threats:** Fire suppression reducing open canopy conditions that favor fruiting; commercial overharvest in some areas; climate change altering phenology and berry production.

**Cultural Significance:** Among the most culturally important berry species across ALL PNW nations — Coast Salish, Yakama, Warm Springs, Umatilla, Nez Perce, Nlaka'pamux, Secwepemc, and many others. High-elevation berry fields were managed through deliberate burning. Berry gathering camps were (and in many cases still are) important social and cultural events. Huckleberries dried, mashed into cakes, and stored for winter. High-elevation huckleberry fields are named places with deep cultural significance. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MANAGEMENT, CULT-TEK)

### Oregon White Oak (*Quercus garryana*)

**Taxonomy:** Fagales > Fagaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC (but habitat critically endangered)

**Description:** Deciduous broadleaf tree reaching 60-90 ft with a broad, spreading crown and deeply lobed leaves. Bark grayish, deeply furrowed. Acorns 1 inch long in shallow cups. The only native oak in Washington and British Columbia.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,000 ft (y=-41--3)
**Distribution:** Willamette Valley, Puget lowlands, Georgia Basin, rain-shadow areas of the San Juan Islands and eastern Olympics. Range contracted dramatically since 1850.

**Ecological Significance:**
Foundation species of the critically endangered oak woodland/prairie ecosystem. Oak woodlands historically covered hundreds of thousands of acres in the Willamette Valley and Puget lowlands, maintained by deliberate burning by Kalapuya, Coast Salish, and other nations. Less than 5% of historical oak woodland remains — Douglas-fir encroachment since cessation of Indigenous burning has converted most to closed-canopy conifer forest. Oak woodlands support 200+ species of insects, many found nowhere else, plus cavity-nesting birds (western bluebird, white-breasted nuthatch) and the ESA-listed Fender's blue butterfly.

**Threats:** Douglas-fir encroachment from fire suppression (the primary threat); development of remaining lowland sites; competition from invasive plants (Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry); drought stress.

**Cultural Significance:** Acorns processed as food by Kalapuya and other nations (leached to remove tannins, then roasted or made into flour). More importantly, oak woodlands were actively maintained through cultural burning — Kalapuya burned annually to maintain camas prairies and open oak savanna for hunting, food production, and travel. The cessation of burning is the direct cause of oak woodland loss. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

**Key Sources:** Franklin & Dyrness (1988); Thysell & Carey (2001) — oak woodland ecology; Dunwiddie & Bakker (2011) — prairie restoration

### Pacific Dogwood (*Cornus nuttallii*)

**Taxonomy:** Cornales > Cornaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | BC: Species at Risk (Blue List) | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Deciduous tree reaching 30-60 ft with a graceful, layered branching pattern. Famous for large, showy "flowers" that are actually 4-6 white bracts surrounding a cluster of tiny true flowers. Produces bright red berry clusters in autumn. Bark smooth, gray-brown. British Columbia's official provincial flower (the floral emblem since 1956).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,500 ft (y=-41-21)
**Distribution:** From southern British Columbia through western Washington and Oregon to northern California; most common in the understory and edges of Douglas-fir and mixed hardwood forests. Range contracting northward due to disease.

**Ecological Significance:**
A significant understory tree whose abundant red berries provide high-fat autumn food for band-tailed pigeons, cedar waxwings, and other fruit-eating birds critical for pre-migration fattening. Dogwood anthracnose (*Discula destructiva*), a fungal disease first detected in the PNW in the 1980s, has caused widespread mortality — particularly in shaded, moist sites. The disease has fundamentally altered Pacific dogwood populations across its range, with some local populations experiencing >80% mortality. Surviving trees in sunnier, drier microsites may represent disease-resistant genotypes important for species recovery.

**Threats:** Dogwood anthracnose (*Discula destructiva*) — the primary and severe threat since the 1980s; habitat loss from development; drought stress.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in PNW ethnobotany.

### Pacific Rhododendron (*Rhododendron macrophyllum*)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Ericaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Evergreen shrub to small tree, typically 6-20 ft tall (occasionally to 30 ft in old-growth understory), with large, leathery, dark green leaves and showy clusters of pink to rose-purple flowers in late spring. Washington's official state flower since 1892. Thick, twisted trunks and persistent evergreen canopy create dense, shaded understories.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Coastal and lowland western PNW from British Columbia to northern California; most abundant in well-drained, acidic soils under conifer canopy. Particularly common in Douglas-fir and western hemlock forests on the west side of the Cascades.

**Ecological Significance:**
An indicator of old-growth and mature forest conditions in the PNW lowlands. Dense rhododendron understory creates thermal microhabitats — the evergreen canopy moderates temperature extremes at ground level, providing winter cover for small mammals and birds. Ericoid mycorrhizal associations allow rhododendron to thrive in the nutrient-poor, acidic soils typical under conifers where other shrubs struggle. Flowers provide nectar for native bumblebees and other pollinators during the late-spring gap between early and summer blooms.

**Threats:** Generally secure; old-growth loss reduces optimal habitat; can be suppressed by dense salal or invasive species in disturbed sites; deer browse on young plants.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in PNW ethnobotany. The plant's leaves and nectar contain grayanotoxins (mildly toxic), which may have limited historical food or medicinal use.

### Cascara (*Frangula purshiana*)

**Taxonomy:** Rosales > Rhamnaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small deciduous tree or large shrub, 15-35 ft tall, with smooth, silvery-gray bark and prominently veined, elliptical leaves 2-6 inches long. Small greenish-yellow flowers in spring produce dark purple-black berries in late summer. The bark — thin, smooth, and distinctively gray — is the most commercially significant laxative plant material in North America.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,500 ft (y=-41-21)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from British Columbia to northern California; typically found in forest edges, openings, riparian corridors, and second-growth forests. Scattered rather than dominant — rarely forming pure stands.

**Ecological Significance:**
Cascara berries are an important late-summer food source for birds (band-tailed pigeons, cedar waxwings, robins) and small mammals, who serve as seed dispersers. The tree frequently establishes in forest gaps and edges, contributing to early successional plant communities. Cascara bark was so extensively harvested in the 19th and 20th centuries for the pharmaceutical laxative trade that wild populations were significantly depleted in parts of its range — a cautionary example of overharvest of a native medicinal plant.

**Threats:** Historical overharvest for pharmaceutical bark trade (populations have partially recovered since synthetic alternatives reduced demand); habitat conversion; deer browse.

**Cultural Significance:** Widely used by Coast Salish, Quinault, and other PNW nations as a laxative and general tonic medicine — the English name "cascara sagrada" (sacred bark) was given by Spanish colonists who learned of the bark's properties from Indigenous peoples of the Pacific Northwest. Bark typically dried and aged for at least one year before use (fresh bark is too potent). One of the most widely traded medicinal plants in the PNW Indigenous trade network. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Turner (2014); Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Moerman (1998) — *Native American Ethnobotany*

### Red Elderberry (*Sambucus racemosa*)

**Taxonomy:** Dipsacales > Adoxaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Deciduous shrub to small tree, 6-20 ft tall, with pinnately compound leaves (5-7 leaflets), clusters of creamy-white flowers in pyramid-shaped panicles, and conspicuous bright red berry clusters in mid-summer. Stems have thick, spongy pith. Berries contain cyanogenic glycosides when raw — TOXIC if consumed uncooked, but safely eaten after cooking or processing.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW from Alaska to northern California; common in riparian corridors, forest clearings, roadsides, and disturbed sites. A fast-growing pioneer species.

**Ecological Significance:**
An important wildlife food source — berries consumed by 30+ bird species (including band-tailed pigeons, cedar waxwings, Swainson's thrush, varied thrush, and robins), bears, and small mammals. Fast colonization of disturbed sites (clearcuts, landslides, burned areas) makes red elderberry an important early-successional species that provides food and cover before slower-growing trees establish. Flowers are visited by many pollinator species. The soft, pithy stems are excavated by cavity-nesting insects, which in turn attract woodpeckers and other insectivores.

**Threats:** Generally secure; widely distributed and tolerant of disturbance. Invasive species competition on some disturbed sites.

**Cultural Significance:** Coast Salish, Makah, and Quinault peoples used elderberries after cooking — typically steamed, boiled, or dried to neutralize cyanogenic glycosides. Raw berries were recognized as toxic and never consumed fresh. Bark and inner bark used medicinally as an emetic and purgative. Wood (lightweight, with hollow pith) used for making flutes, blowguns, and drinking straws. Elderberry wood fire drills documented among some nations. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Turner (2014); Elias & Dykeman (2009) — *Edible Wild Plants*

### Kinnikinnick (*Arctostaphylos uva-ursi*)

**Taxonomy:** Ericales > Ericaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-DRY-FOREST, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Low-growing, mat-forming evergreen groundcover, rarely exceeding 6 inches in height but spreading 3-6 ft across. Small, leathery, spatula-shaped leaves; tiny pink-white urn-shaped flowers; bright red berries (mealy, persistent through winter). Also known as bearberry — the Latin *uva-ursi* literally translates to "grape of the bear."

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Circumpolar species — throughout the PNW from coastal sand dunes to dry, open forests. Most common on well-drained, sandy or rocky soils in full sun. Found in the San Juan Islands, coastal bluffs, rain-shadow forests, and dry east-side ponderosa pine zones.

**Ecological Significance:**
Excellent erosion control species — dense mats stabilize sandy soils on coastal dunes and dry slopes. Berries persist through winter, providing food for grouse, bears, and small mammals when other food is scarce. Ericoid mycorrhizal associations allow colonization of nutrient-poor, acidic substrates. Drought-adapted leaves reduce water loss, making kinnikinnick an important ground layer species in dry PNW forests where few other groundcovers persist.

**Threats:** Generally secure; widespread and circumpolar. Coastal dune habitat loss from development; invasive grass competition in some areas.

**Cultural Significance:** Among the most widely used plants across ALL PNW and North American nations — the name "kinnikinnick" is from the Algonquin language meaning "smoking mixture." Leaves dried and mixed with tobacco (or used alone) for smoking by Salish, Yakama, Nez Perce, and many other nations across the continent. Berries eaten fresh or dried, though mealy and not highly flavored. Leaves used in medicinal teas for urinary tract ailments. The plant's pan-continental cultural significance — used from the Atlantic to the Pacific by dozens of distinct nations — makes it one of the most broadly shared ethnobotanical species in North America. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** Turner (2014); Moerman (1998); Pojar & MacKinnon (1994)

### Camas (*Camassia quamash*)

**Taxonomy:** Asparagales > Asparagaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Herbaceous perennial from a bulb, 12-20 inches tall, with grass-like basal leaves and a terminal raceme of blue-purple star-shaped flowers. In spring, camas prairies turn vivid blue — Meriwether Lewis described camas prairies as resembling "a lake of fine clear water."

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,000 ft (y=-41--3)
**Distribution:** Puget lowlands, Willamette Valley, Columbia Plateau grasslands, and other prairies maintained by burning. Historically covered hundreds of thousands of acres.

**Ecological Significance:**
A defining plant of PNW prairie ecosystems, camas indicates historically burned landscapes. Dense camas stands support pollinator communities during the brief spring bloom period. Camas prairies are among the most endangered plant communities in the PNW (less than 2% of Willamette Valley prairie remains).

**Threats:** Agricultural conversion; development; fire suppression allowing woody encroachment; invasive grasses and forbs; herbicide drift.

**Cultural Significance:** One of the most important food plants for PNW nations — Kalapuya, Chinook, Nez Perce, Coast Salish, Sahaptin-speaking peoples. Bulbs pit-roasted for 24-72 hours in earth ovens lined with specific plants, converting inulin to fructose (rendering them sweet and digestible). Camas was a dietary staple and a major trade item. Kalapuya management of Willamette Valley camas prairies through annual burning created and maintained the oak savanna landscape that European settlers encountered. Camas prairie locations are named, owned, and managed — these were cultivated landscapes, not wild harvest. **CAUTION:** Death camas (*Zigadenus venenosus*) — a toxic look-alike — grows in the same habitat. Distinguishing the two requires expertise in bloom timing and leaf differences. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MANAGEMENT, CULT-TEK)

### Wapato (*Sagittaria latifolia*)

**Taxonomy:** Alismatales > Alismataceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Aquatic perennial with distinctive arrow-shaped leaves (12-16 inches long) and whorls of white, three-petaled flowers. Produces starchy tubers (1-2 inches diameter) at the ends of underground runners. Grows in shallow water of lakes, ponds, and slow-moving streams.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 1,000 ft (y=-41--38)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW lowlands in shallow freshwater wetlands; historically most abundant in the Willamette Valley, Columbia River floodplain, and Puget lowlands.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important wetland plant providing food (tubers) for waterfowl, muskrats, and beavers. Arrow-shaped leaves provide cover and invertebrate habitat in shallow water margins. Indicates healthy freshwater wetland ecosystems.

**Threats:** Wetland drainage and fill; water level manipulation; competition from invasive plants (reed canary grass, purple loosestrife).

**Cultural Significance:** Major food resource for Chinook, Kalapuya, and Coast Salish nations. Tubers harvested by women wading in shallow water and loosening them with their feet — tubers float to the surface for collection. Sometimes called "Indian potato" in historical accounts. Sauvie Island (Columbia River) was named Wappatoo Island by Lewis and Clark for the abundance of this plant and the extensive harvest by Chinook peoples. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

### Skunk Cabbage (*Lysichiton americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Alismatales > Araceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Herbaceous perennial with massive basal leaves (2-5 ft long — among the largest leaves of any PNW plant) and a bright yellow spathe surrounding a greenish spadix in early spring. Thermogenic — the spadix generates metabolic heat, melting surrounding snow and volatilizing the distinctive skunky odor that attracts early-season pollinators (carrion flies, beetles).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-22)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in swamps, seeps, and wet forest understories.

**Ecological Significance:**
One of the earliest spring bloomers, skunk cabbage is thermogenic — it generates enough heat (up to 35F above ambient) to melt snow around its emerging spathe, providing the first available pollen and nectar to early-emerging insects. The massive leaves create deep shade and moist microclimates in late summer. Indicator species of saturated soils and high water table.

**Threats:** Generally secure in wet habitats; wetland drainage; invasive plant competition.

**Cultural Significance:** Leaves used by many PNW nations as food wrapping for cooking and storage — large, waxy leaves are natural water-resistant packaging. Root used medicinally by some nations for respiratory ailments. Young leaves reportedly eaten after careful preparation by some groups, though the plant contains calcium oxalate crystals (irritant). (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

### Black Cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*)

**Taxonomy:** Malpighiales > Salicaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** The largest cottonwood in North America, reaching 100-150 ft with a broad, open crown. Leaves broadly ovate, 3-6 inches, dark green above and pale below. Bark deeply furrowed on old trees. Buds large, resinous, and intensely aromatic in spring. Name "trichocarpa" (three-haired fruit) refers to the hairy seed capsules that release clouds of cottony seeds in late spring.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-22)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW riparian corridors; the dominant floodplain tree on major rivers (Skagit, Snohomish, Willamette, Columbia).

**Ecological Significance:**
Fast-growing floodplain pioneer that establishes on freshly deposited alluvial bars and gravel. Cottonwood galleries along major rivers create linear habitat corridors connecting mountains to the sea. Large cottonwoods develop cavities used by cavity-nesting birds and bats. When cottonwoods fall into rivers, they create large wood habitat critical for salmon. Cottonwood forests support the highest breeding bird diversity of any PNW forest type.

**Threats:** Dam-regulated flow regimes preventing natural floodplain dynamics needed for regeneration; agricultural and urban development of floodplains; bank armoring preventing natural river meandering.

**Cultural Significance:** Inner bark eaten as spring food by some Interior nations. Bark used for some medicinal preparations (contains salicin, related to aspirin). Roots used for carving by Coast Salish. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

### Salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*)

**Taxonomy:** Rosales > Rosaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Deciduous shrub 6-12 ft tall with arching, sparsely thorned stems and three-lobed leaves. Bright pink flowers in early spring (among the first shrub flowers in the PNW). Berries resemble small raspberries, ranging from yellow-orange to deep salmon-red — hence the name.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-22)
**Distribution:** Throughout coastal and lowland western PNW; dominant riparian shrub in wet forests and stream corridors.

**Ecological Significance:**
The dominant riparian understory shrub in western PNW. Dense salmonberry thickets stabilize stream banks, provide cover for wildlife, and produce berries that feed birds (varied thrush, Swainson's thrush, robin) and bears. Early spring flowers are an important nectar source for rufous hummingbirds returning from migration — the phenological synchrony between salmonberry bloom and hummingbird arrival is one of the PNW's signature ecological relationships.

**Threats:** Generally secure; invasive knotweed can displace salmonberry in some riparian areas.

**Cultural Significance:** Berries and young shoots eaten by many PNW nations. Berry harvest timing is traditionally tied to bird song — when the Swainson's thrush sings, the salmonberries are ripe. Young shoots (peeled) eaten raw as a spring vegetable. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

### Lady Fern (*Athyrium filix-femina*)

**Taxonomy:** Polypodiales > Athyriaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large deciduous fern with graceful, arching fronds reaching 2-5 ft tall from a stout, scaly rhizome. Fronds are bipinnate to tripinnate, finely dissected, giving a lacy, delicate appearance (hence "lady" fern). Fiddleheads (tightly coiled emerging fronds) appear in spring. Sori (spore clusters) are crescent- or horseshoe-shaped, a key identification feature distinguishing lady fern from similar species.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW from coastal forests to montane stream corridors; one of the most widespread ferns in North America. Most abundant in moist, shaded riparian areas and wet forest understory.

**Ecological Significance:**
A reliable indicator of moist, nutrient-rich soils and riparian conditions. Dense lady fern colonies provide ground-level cover for amphibians (Pacific giant salamander, tailed frog) and small mammals. Rapidly decomposing deciduous fronds contribute to soil nutrient cycling in conifer-dominated forests. Spring fiddleheads are among the first green growth in PNW forests, providing food for elk, deer, and bears. The species' wide elevation range — from sea level riparian zones to montane stream banks — makes it a consistent thread linking PNW wet habitats across elevation bands.

**Threats:** Generally secure; widespread and common. Sensitive to stream bank disturbance, forest canopy removal (desiccation), and invasive plant competition in riparian zones.

**Cultural Significance:** Widespread food plant across PNW nations — fiddleheads eaten by Coast Salish, Makah, and Quinault peoples as a spring food source (harvested when tightly coiled, before fronds unfurl). Rhizomes used medicinally by several nations for various ailments. Fronds used as lining for food storage and cooking pits — layered between food and hot rocks in earth ovens. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Turner (2014); Lellinger (1985) — *A Field Manual of the Ferns and Fern-Allies of the United States and Canada*

### Maidenhair Fern (*Adiantum pedatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Polypodiales > Pteridaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Distinctive, elegant fern with a unique fan-shaped frond arrangement — each frond radiates pinnae (leaflets) in a horseshoe or half-circle pattern from the tip of a dark, wiry, polished stipe (stem). Stipes are jet black, smooth, and glossy. Pinnae are delicate, bright green, and tremble in the slightest breeze. Typically 1-2 ft tall. Grows in dense colonies on wet cliff faces, spray zones near waterfalls, and mossy rock outcrops in old-growth forest.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in moist, shaded habitats; most abundant near waterfalls, seeps, wet rock faces, and along streams in old-growth forest. Requires persistent moisture and shade — disappears rapidly when canopy is removed.

**Ecological Significance:**
A strong indicator of undisturbed, moist old-growth forest conditions and persistent water sources. Maidenhair fern colonies mark seeps, springs, and spray zones that provide critical summer moisture in otherwise dry periods. The species' intolerance of canopy disturbance makes it useful as a forest health indicator — presence signals minimal recent disturbance. Dense colonies on wet cliffs create microhabitat for specialized invertebrates and bryophytes. The dark, wiry stipes are among the most distinctive plant structures in the PNW forest understory.

**Threats:** Old-growth forest loss (primary threat — species requires persistent shade and moisture); stream bank disturbance; changes to water table or seep hydrology from logging roads or development.

**Cultural Significance:** Highly significant to multiple PNW nations. Coast Salish weavers used the dark, glossy stems as black weft material in coiled baskets — the jet-black stipes create striking contrast patterns against lighter materials. Quinault and Makah peoples used stems for basket overlay patterns and decorative work. Fronds prepared as a hair wash by women of several nations (hence the common name "maidenhair"). Medicinal uses documented across multiple nations for respiratory ailments and as a general tonic. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Heritage Skills Cross-Reference:** Cedar Path (basket weaving materials)

**Key Sources:** Turner (2014); Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Stewart (1984) — *Cedar: Tree of Life* (basket material references)

Marine plants and macroalgae that form the foundation of PNW ocean ecosystems.

### Bull Kelp (*Nereocystis luetkeana*)

**Taxonomy:** Laminariales > Laminariaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE (but declining dramatically)

**Description:** Annual canopy-forming kelp reaching 60-80+ ft in a single growing season (up to 10 inches/day growth rate). A single stipe (stem) anchored by a holdfast rises from the seafloor to a gas-filled pneumatocyst (float bulb) at the surface, from which long, strap-like blades radiate outward to form a floating canopy.

**Elevation Range:** -80 to -10 ft depth (y=-43--41)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW nearshore waters from Alaska to California; requires rocky substrate for holdfast attachment and water temperatures below 64F.

**Ecological Significance:**
The dominant canopy-forming kelp in the PNW, bull kelp forests create three-dimensional habitat supporting 750+ species — analogous to terrestrial old-growth forests. Kelp canopy provides shelter for juvenile rockfish, lingcod, and invertebrates. Dislodged kelp rafts transport organisms and carbon offshore, linking nearshore and pelagic ecosystems. Kelp forests are experiencing dramatic declines in parts of their range (90%+ loss in northern California) following the sea star wasting syndrome-driven loss of urchin predators and marine heat wave events.

**Threats:** Sea urchin overgrazing where sea otter and sea star predation is absent; marine heat waves; ocean acidification; coastal development increasing sedimentation.

**Cultural Significance:** Used by many coastal PNW nations for fishing line, storage containers, and food. Kelp bulbs used as containers for storing fish oil. Kelp blades eaten as food, wrapped around fish for cooking, and used medicinally. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Springer et al. (2010) — bull kelp ecology; Steneck et al. (2002) — kelp forest review; NOAA

### Eelgrass (*Zostera marina*)

**Taxonomy:** Alismatales > Zosteraceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC (but declining in Puget Sound)

**Description:** True marine flowering plant (angiosperm, not algae) with ribbon-like leaves 1-3 ft long growing from creeping rhizomes in soft sediment. Forms dense underwater meadows in shallow, protected waters. Unlike kelp (which is a brown alga), eelgrass produces flowers, fruits, and seeds.

**Elevation Range:** -30 to 0 ft depth (y=-42--41)
**Distribution:** Throughout protected bays and estuaries of the PNW; Padilla Bay, WA hosts the largest continuous eelgrass bed in the Pacific Northwest (8,000+ acres).

**Ecological Significance:**
Foundation species of nearshore soft-sediment ecosystems. Eelgrass meadows provide: nursery habitat for juvenile salmon, herring, and Dungeness crab; spawning substrate for Pacific herring (herring eggs deposited directly on eelgrass blades); blue carbon sequestration (eelgrass meadows store carbon in sediments at rates comparable to tropical forests per unit area); sediment stabilization reducing shoreline erosion; water quality improvement by filtering particles and absorbing excess nutrients.

**Threats:** Water turbidity from shoreline development; nutrient pollution causing algal overgrowth; propeller scarring from boat traffic; rising water temperatures; direct loss to dredging and filling. Estimated 30% loss in Puget Sound since 1890.

**Cultural Significance:** Herring eggs on eelgrass harvested as food by many coastal nations — a delicacy known as herring roe on kelp/eelgrass. Eelgrass beds recognized as productive fishing and gathering areas in traditional resource management. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Short & Wyllie-Echeverria (1996) — eelgrass ecology; Puget Sound Partnership; Padilla Bay NERR

**Cross-Module References:**

### Rockweed (*Fucus distichus*)

**Taxonomy:** Fucales > Fucaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Olive-brown macroalga with flat, dichotomously branching thalli (frond blades) and gas-filled receptacles at branch tips that provide buoyancy. Attached to rocks by a disk-shaped holdfast. Typically 6-18 inches long. Dominates the mid-to-upper intertidal zone.

**Elevation Range:** -3 to +5 ft relative to MLLW (y=-41)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW rocky intertidal; one of the most abundant intertidal algae on both sheltered and semi-exposed shores.

**Ecological Significance:**
Foundation species of the mid-to-upper intertidal zone, rockweed creates a moist canopy during low tide that shelters hundreds of invertebrate species — amphipods, isopods, snails, crabs, and worms — from desiccation and thermal stress. Without rockweed canopy, many intertidal species cannot survive low-tide exposure. Rockweed provides food (grazing snails), shelter (canopy species), and substrate (epiphytic algae and invertebrates).

**Threats:** Trampling at accessible intertidal sites; oil spill vulnerability; climate-driven thermal stress during low-tide exposure; ocean acidification.

**Key Sources:** Murray et al. (2006) — intertidal algae ecology; NPS Olympic Coast monitoring

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-marine-aquatic: Intertidal invertebrate community
- See ecological-networks: Kelp-Otter-Urchin Cascade (intertidal context)

### Sea Lettuce (*Ulva* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Ulvales > Ulvaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Bright green, sheet-like macroalga with translucent, ruffled blades 6-18 inches across. Grows attached to rocks, shells, and other substrates or free-floating in tidepools and sheltered waters. Multiple species occur in PNW waters.

**Elevation Range:** -15 to +3 ft relative to MLLW (y=-41)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW intertidal and shallow subtidal; most abundant in nutrient-enriched areas.

**Ecological Significance:**
Fast-growing green alga that serves as food for many intertidal grazers (limpets, sea urchins, periwinkles). Excessive sea lettuce growth (green tides) in nutrient-enriched waters is an indicator of eutrophication. In balanced ecosystems, sea lettuce provides a rapid-turnover food source and photosynthetic oxygen production.

**Threats:** Paradoxically, sea lettuce often increases with nutrient pollution; natural populations are generally secure.

**Cultural Significance:** Eaten as food by many coastal PNW nations; dried as snack food or added to soups. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Gabrielson et al. (2012) — *Seaweeds of the Pacific Northwest*

**Cross-Module References:**

### Surfgrass (*Phyllospadix scouleri* and *P. serrulatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Alismatales > Zosteraceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Marine flowering plants (angiosperms) with narrow, bright green, grass-like leaves growing from rhizomes attached to rocks in the low intertidal zone. Unlike eelgrass (which grows in soft sediment), surfgrass grows on rocky substrate in wave-exposed areas.

**Elevation Range:** -5 to 0 ft relative to MLLW (y=-41)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW rocky intertidal; most abundant on semi-exposed and exposed rocky shores.

**Ecological Significance:**
Creates dense, low canopy on rocky substrates that shelters small invertebrates and juvenile fish from wave energy and predation. Surfgrass beds serve as nursery habitat for juvenile rockfish and lingcod. One of very few marine angiosperms worldwide, sharing this distinction with eelgrass and a few tropical species.

**Threats:** Oil spills; physical disturbance; climate-driven thermal stress.

**Key Sources:** Turner (1985) — Phyllospadix ecology; NPS Olympic Coast monitoring

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-marine-aquatic: Juvenile rockfish, lingcod (nursery habitat)

### Pacific Yew (*Taxus brevifolia*)

**Taxonomy:** Cupressales > Taxaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NT (Near Threatened — due to bark harvest impacts)

**Description:** Small to medium understory conifer, 15-50 ft, with thin, reddish-purple scaly bark and flat, dark green needles. Produces bright red, fleshy arils (modified cones) that are eaten by birds. Extremely slow-growing and shade-tolerant. ALL PARTS EXCEPT THE FLESHY ARIL ARE TOXIC TO HUMANS AND LIVESTOCK — contains taxine alkaloids.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in shady, moist forests; scattered as an understory tree, never dominant.

**Ecological Significance:**
Source of taxol (paclitaxel), one of the most important anti-cancer drugs ever discovered (Bristol-Myers Squibb, 1992 FDA approval). Taxol was originally extracted from the bark (killing the tree), leading to conservation concern. Now synthesized or extracted from cultivated *Taxus* species. Pacific yew's shade tolerance and long life (400+ years) make it an indicator of undisturbed old-growth forest.

**Threats:** Historical bark harvest for taxol (reduced but ongoing); slow growth means recovery from disturbance takes centuries; loss of old-growth habitat.

**Cultural Significance:** Wood — extraordinarily hard, dense, and resilient — used by many PNW nations for bows, paddles, wedges, fishhooks, and digging sticks. Considered among the finest bow wood in North America. **SAFETY NOTE: All parts of Pacific yew except the red aril are highly toxic. This species should never be consumed.** (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** USFS silvics manual; Bolsinger & Jaramillo (1990) — taxol and Pacific yew conservation

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-terrestrial: Cedar waxwing, robin (aril consumption and seed dispersal)
- See fungi-microbiome-survey: Yew-associated fungi

### Licorice Fern (*Polypodium glycyrrhiza*)

**Taxonomy:** Polypodiales > Polypodiaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Epiphytic fern with slender, pinnate fronds 8-18 inches long, growing from rhizomes on mossy tree trunks (especially bigleaf maple), logs, and rock faces. Rhizome has a distinctive licorice-sweet taste. Unusual phenology — green and active during the wet PNW winter, dormant in summer drought.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 2,500 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in moist forests; abundant as an epiphyte on bigleaf maple.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most visible PNW epiphyte, licorice fern contributes to the massive epiphyte biomass (up to 80 lbs per tree) on bigleaf maples and old-growth conifers. Winter-green phenology makes it one of the few actively photosynthesizing plants during PNW's dark, wet winter months. Part of the "canopy soil" ecosystem — mats of moss, fern rhizomes, and accumulated organic matter on tree branches that support distinct invertebrate and microbial communities.

**Threats:** Generally secure; dependent on large, old trees for substrate; bigleaf maple decline could reduce habitat.

**Cultural Significance:** Rhizomes chewed for their sweet, licorice flavor by many PNW nations — a trail snack and mild cold/cough remedy. Known widely by coastal and interior groups. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Pojar & MacKinnon (1994); Nadkarni (1984) — canopy epiphyte ecology

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fungi-microbiome-survey: Epiphyte-associated microbiome

### Western Trillium (*Trillium ovatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Liliales > Melanthiaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Herbaceous perennial 8-16 inches tall with a whorl of three broad, ovate leaves and a single three-petaled flower that opens white and gradually turns pink, then deep rose as it ages. One of the most beloved spring wildflowers of the PNW forest floor.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in moist forests; most abundant in rich, undisturbed forest soils.

**Ecological Significance:**
Indicator of rich, undisturbed forest soils with intact fungal communities. Seeds dispersed by ants (myrmecochory) — each seed has a lipid-rich elaiosome that attracts ants who carry seeds to their nests, providing both dispersal and a nutrient-rich germination site. Extremely slow to establish from seed (5-7 years to first flower), making trillium a marker of forest continuity.

**Threats:** Deer browse (trillium is highly palatable); collection for gardens; loss of ant dispersal partners due to invasive species.

**Key Sources:** Jules & Rathcke (1999) — trillium population ecology; Franklin & Dyrness (1988)

**Cross-Module References:**

### Flora Chip Architecture

The Flora chip in the derived chipset processes queries about vascular plants, bryophytes, and marine macroalgae:

- **Memory banks:** 8 elevation bands (ELEV-*) as primary partitions; 17 habitat types (HAB-*) as secondary index
- **Instruction types:** Species identification, distribution query, ecological role lookup, cultural significance query, conservation status check
- **Bus connections:**
  - `mycorrhizal_network` bus → fungi-microbiome chip (all tree species)
  - `salmon_nutrient` bus → fauna-marine chip (riparian species)
  - `cultural_ecological` bus → heritage-bridge chip (culturally significant species)
  - `watershed` bus → all chips (watershed connectivity)
  - `predator_prey` bus → fauna-terrestrial chip (herbivory, seed dispersal)
