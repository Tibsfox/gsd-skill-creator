## Safety Preamble

**MANDATORY IDENTIFICATION DISCLAIMER:** Accurate identification of wild fungi requires extensive training, multiple identifying characteristics, spore prints, and often microscopy or DNA analysis. Many edible species have dangerous or lethal look-alikes. **Never consume any wild fungus based solely on information in this document.** Consult expert mycologists, attend guided forays with local mycological societies, and use multiple field guides before considering any wild mushroom for consumption. When in doubt, throw it out.

**Toxic species noted throughout:** Where edible species have dangerous look-alikes, these are explicitly flagged. The most dangerous PNW fungal species — the death cap (*Amanita phalloides*), an invasive species now established in PNW urban areas — is profiled with full warning.

## 1. Ectomycorrhizal Fungi

Ectomycorrhizal (ECM) fungi form symbiotic associations with the roots of conifers and some hardwoods, creating the "Wood Wide Web" — the underground network through which PNW forests share resources, communicate, and function as superorganisms. ECM fungi colonize root tips, forming a sheath (mantle) around the root and extending hyphae (extramatrical mycelium) into surrounding soil to access water and nutrients far beyond the root's reach. The tree provides photosynthate (sugars); the fungus provides water, phosphorus, nitrogen, and other nutrients. The network retains approximately 30% of the tree's photosynthate — not parasitism, but infrastructure maintenance.

### Pacific Golden Chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*)

**Taxonomy:** Cantharellales > Cantharellaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE | Oregon State Mushroom

**Description:** Golden-yellow to deep orange funnel-shaped mushroom with false gills (blunt, forked ridges rather than true blade-like gills), 2-6 inches across. Fruiting body has a distinctive fruity, apricot-like aroma. Distinguished from the similar *C. cascadensis* and *C. cibarius* complex by molecular analysis. The most commercially harvested wild mushroom in the Pacific Northwest.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,500 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW in Douglas-fir and western hemlock forests; most productive in second-growth stands 40-80 years old with intact moss layers.

**Ecological Significance:**
Ectomycorrhizal partner of Douglas-fir and western hemlock — the fungal sheath on root tips increases the tree's absorptive surface area by 10-100x. Chanterelle mycelium extends meters from the tree root, accessing soil phosphorus and water unavailable to uncolonized roots. Fruiting is strongly correlated with fall rains after summer drought — the first heavy rains of September-October trigger massive fruiting events. The species is among the most commercially important wild-harvested fungi in the world; PNW chanterelle harvest is estimated at 500,000-1,000,000+ lbs/year with an economic value of $5-20 million.

**Threats:** Overharvest concerns (though research suggests fruiting body removal does not damage mycelium); loss of forest habitat; climate change altering precipitation patterns (the fall rain trigger).

**Cultural Significance:** Deep cultural significance for Japanese-American communities in the PNW who harvest chanterelles alongside matsutake. Commercial chanterelle picking is an important livelihood for immigrant communities (Southeast Asian, Latin American) in rural Oregon and Washington. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**SAFETY NOTE:** The Pacific golden chanterelle can be confused with the jack-o'-lantern mushroom (*Omphalotus olearius*), which causes severe gastrointestinal distress. Jack-o'-lanterns have true gills (thin blades) and grow in clusters on wood; chanterelles have false gills (blunt ridges) and grow singly from soil. Jack-o'-lanterns are bioluminescent (faintly) — the "foxfire" that gave Foxfire its name.

**Key Sources:** Pilz et al. (2003) — USFS chanterelle ecology; Dunham et al. (2003) — chanterelle harvest impacts

### Pacific Northwest Matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*)

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Tricholomataceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Large, robust mushroom with a white cap often stained brown, thick white partial veil leaving a ring on the stipe, and a powerful, distinctive spicy-cinnamon aroma (described variously as "gym socks and cinnamon"). Fruiting bodies emerge from soil, often partially buried under duff. Previously considered *T. magnivelare* or a variant of the Japanese *T. matsutake*.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Western PNW in association with Douglas-fir, hemlock, and shore pine; most productive on sandy, well-drained soils. Key harvest areas: Oregon Coast Range, Olympic Peninsula, southern Cascades.

**Ecological Significance:**
Ectomycorrhizal partner of multiple PNW conifers. Matsutake mycelium creates dense, hydrophobic mats ("shiros") in forest soil that exclude competing fungi and create microhabitats with distinct chemistry. Shiros may persist for decades, fruiting annually if conditions are favorable. The species fruits in fall, often at the same sites year after year — experienced pickers guard their matsutake patches.

**Threats:** Loss of mature forest habitat; climate change altering fruiting conditions; competition from other fungi as forests age; overharvest of accessible sites.

**Cultural Significance:** THE culturally significant mushroom linking PNW ecology to Japanese culture. Matsutake (*matsu* = pine, *take* = mushroom) is the most prized mushroom in Japanese cuisine — a single specimen can sell for $100-1,000+ in Japanese markets. Japanese-American communities in the PNW have harvested matsutake for generations, and the mushroom holds deep cultural significance connecting Japanese food traditions with PNW forests. The PNW matsutake harvest is a multi-million dollar export industry. Anna Tsing's *The Mushroom at the End of the World* (2015) documents the global matsutake trade and its cultural dimensions. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**SAFETY NOTE:** Matsutake's distinctive aroma makes misidentification relatively unlikely for experienced collectors. However, beginners should never collect any white Amanita-like mushroom without expert verification — the death cap (*A. phalloides*) is white, grows near similar trees, and is lethal.

**Key Sources:** Hosford et al. (1997) — *Ecology and Management of the Commercially Harvested American Matsutake*; Tsing (2015) — *The Mushroom at the End of the World*; USFS PNW

### King Bolete (*Boletus edulis* complex)

**Taxonomy:** Boletales > Boletaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large, robust bolete with a brown to reddish-brown convex cap 4-12 inches across, a swollen whitish stipe with fine reticulation (net-like pattern), and a sponge-like pore surface (tubes, not gills) that is white when young, turning yellow-green with age. The PNW forms are part of the *B. edulis* species complex and may include several distinct species pending molecular resolution.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; associated with spruce, Douglas-fir, hemlock, and pine. Most abundant in subalpine spruce forests and coastal Sitka spruce stands.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important ectomycorrhizal partner across a broad elevational range, connecting forest trees to soil nutrients. Commercially harvested in the PNW, though less intensively than chanterelles. Large fruiting bodies are consumed by mammals (squirrels, deer, slugs) and insects, making boletes a node in the forest food web.

**Threats:** Climate change altering fruiting phenology; habitat loss; recreational collection pressure.

**Cultural Significance:** Widely collected as food across many cultures in the PNW, including European-American, Eastern European, and Asian-American communities. Indigenous harvest of boletes is documented for some BC First Nations. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**SAFETY NOTE:** While the king bolete is relatively easy to identify, some boletes in the PNW stain blue when cut and can cause gastrointestinal upset. Never eat any bolete with a red pore surface — some red-pored species are toxic.

**Key Sources:** Arora (1986) — *Mushrooms Demystified*; Siegel & Schwarz (2016) — *Mushrooms of Cascadia*

### Rhizopogon (*Rhizopogon vesiculosus* and *R. vinicolor*)

**Taxonomy:** Boletales > Rhizopogonaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Hypogeous (underground-fruiting) truffles — small (1-3 cm), round to irregular, with a smooth to rough exterior and a dark, chambered interior when mature. Not visible without digging — detected by mammals via volatile scent compounds. Technically "false truffles" (Basidiomycota, not Ascomycota).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,500 ft (y=-41-72)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW Douglas-fir forests; among the most common ectomycorrhizal fungi in PNW conifer forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
**THE key mycorrhizal network species in PNW Douglas-fir forests.** Simard's research identified *R. vesiculosus* and *R. vinicolor* as the primary fungi connecting Douglas-fir trees into mycorrhizal networks. These species form extensive mycelial networks that link trees across meters to hundreds of meters, enabling the carbon, nitrogen, water, and chemical signal sharing documented in the "mother tree" research. A single Rhizopogon genotype can connect dozens of trees. The underground fruiting bodies are a critical food source for the northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*), which in turn is the primary prey of the northern spotted owl — creating the truffle-mammal-owl food web.

**Threats:** Clearcutting severs mycorrhizal networks that may have taken decades to centuries to develop; soil disturbance destroys hyphal networks; climate change altering soil moisture regimes.

**Key Sources:** Simard et al. (1997, 2012) — mycorrhizal network research; Beiler et al. (2010) — network architecture; Maser et al. (1978) — truffle-mammal ecology

**Cross-Module References:**
- See flora-survey: Douglas-fir (primary host — "mother tree" connections)
- See fauna-terrestrial: Northern flying squirrel, northern spotted owl (truffle-mammal-owl food web)
- See ecological-networks: Mycorrhizal Network (PRIMARY reference — Rhizopogon is the network backbone)

### Cortinarius (*Cortinarius* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Cortinariaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE (most species)

**Description:** The largest genus of mushrooms in the PNW (and globally) with 200+ species — more than any other genus. Distinguished by the cortina (cobweb-like partial veil) and rusty-brown spore print. Fruiting bodies range from small and brown to large and spectacularly colored (deep violet *C. violaceus*, brilliant blue *C. deliibuti*). Identification to species often requires microscopy and DNA analysis.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests at all forested elevations; maximum diversity in old-growth and mature conifer forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most species-rich ectomycorrhizal genus in PNW forests — Cortinarius diversity is an indicator of forest health and mycorrhizal network complexity. Different Cortinarius species associate with specific tree hosts and soil conditions, making the genus a sensitive indicator of ecological change. Some species produce enzymes capable of accessing nitrogen locked in organic matter (a capability rare among ECM fungi), contributing to nitrogen cycling in nutrient-poor forest soils.

**Threats:** Old-growth loss reduces habitat for species dependent on complex forest structure; some rare species known from only a few collections.

**SAFETY NOTE:** MANY Cortinarius species are highly toxic — particularly the *C. orellanus* and *C. rubellus* groups, which cause delayed-onset kidney failure (orellanine toxin). No Cortinarius species should be eaten without expert identification. The PNW has multiple potentially dangerous Cortinarius species.

**Key Sources:** Trudell & Ammirati (2009) — *Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest*; Siegel & Schwarz (2016)

**Cross-Module References:**

- See ecological-networks: Mycorrhizal Network (network complexity indicator)

### Russula (*Russula* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Russulales > Russulaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE (most species)

**Description:** Colorful, brittle-fleshed mushrooms with caps in virtually every color (red, purple, green, yellow, white, brown). Characterized by crumbly, chalk-like flesh (unlike fibrous Cortinarius) and white to yellow spore prints. Second-most species-rich genus in PNW forests after Cortinarius, with 100+ species.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; nearly every conifer tree in the PNW is colonized by at least one Russula species.

**Ecological Significance:**
Abundant ECM partners of all major PNW conifers, Russula species produce large quantities of fruiting bodies that are consumed by slugs, mammals, and insects, making them important nodes in the forest food web. Russula mycelium is typically shorter-range than Rhizopogon, forming local networks rather than long-distance connections. Some species (notably the shrimp Russula, *R. xerampelina*) are important food fungi.

**Threats:** Generally secure due to high diversity; some rare species threatened by old-growth loss.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Some Russula species cause gastrointestinal upset; the "hot" taste test (a tiny taste of the cap — spit, don't swallow) can indicate toxicity, but this method is not reliable for all species.

**Key Sources:** Trudell & Ammirati (2009); Buyck et al. (2018) — Russula systematics

- See ecological-networks: Mycorrhizal Network

### Suillus (*Suillus* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Boletales > Suillaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Boletes (pore mushrooms) with slimy to glutinous caps, often with partial veils leaving ring zones on the stipe. Typically associated specifically with five-needled pines (whitebark pine, western white pine) or two-needled pines. Some species are host-specific to a single pine species.

**Elevation Range:** 2,000-7,000 ft (y=-3-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW where pine species occur; most diverse in mixed conifer-pine forests and subalpine zones.

**Ecological Significance:**
Obligate ectomycorrhizal partners of pine species. Some Suillus species are among the first ECM fungi to colonize pine seedlings after disturbance, playing a critical role in post-fire forest recovery. Host specificity makes Suillus an indicator of pine presence and health. The mycorrhizal network formed by Suillus in pine forests parallels the Douglas-fir/Rhizopogon network in lower-elevation forests.

**Threats:** Decline of host pine species (whitebark pine from blister rust; western white pine from blister rust); fire suppression reducing pine habitat.

**Key Sources:** Molina et al. (1992) — specificity in mycorrhizal associations; USFS PNW

**Cross-Module References:**
- See flora-survey: Whitebark pine, western white pine (obligate hosts)
- See ecological-networks: Mycorrhizal Network (pine-forest network)

### Amanita — Fly Agaric (*Amanita muscaria*)

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Amanitaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** The iconic red-capped mushroom with white spots — the "fairy tale mushroom" recognized worldwide. Cap 4-10 inches, bright red to orange-red with white wart-like patches (remnants of the universal veil). White gills, ring on stipe, bulbous base with concentric rings (volva).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; associated with birch, spruce, pine, and Douglas-fir.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important ECM partner across a wide range of host trees. The most recognizable mushroom in the world, *A. muscaria* has a complex biogeographic history — recent molecular work suggests the PNW may have been a Pleistocene refugium for the species. Contains ibotenic acid and muscimol (psychoactive compounds).

**Threats:** Generally secure and widespread.

**Cultural Significance:** Used ceremonially and shamanistically by Siberian Indigenous peoples; PNW Indigenous use is not well documented in published sources. Deep significance in European folk culture (Christmas imagery, fairy tales). (CULT-SPIRITUAL — noting this is primarily documented for non-PNW cultures)

**SAFETY NOTE:** TOXIC — contains ibotenic acid and muscimol. Causes delirium, nausea, and in some cases seizures. Not considered deadly for healthy adults (unlike *A. phalloides*), but poisoning is serious and requires medical attention. Do not consume.

**Key Sources:** Geml et al. (2006) — *A. muscaria* molecular phylogeography; Trudell & Ammirati (2009)

### Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) — INVASIVE

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Amanitaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM — invasive)
**Conservation Status:** Not applicable (invasive species)

**Description:** Medium to large mushroom with a greenish-yellow to olive cap 3-6 inches across, white gills, white ring on stipe, and a distinctive sac-like volva at the base (often buried in soil — dig carefully to see it). Odor mild, sometimes described as faintly sweet or honey-like when young.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 1,000 ft (y=-41--38)
**Distribution:** INVASIVE in PNW — established in urban areas of the Pacific coast from British Columbia to California. Introduced from Europe, probably via imported ornamental trees (oaks, lindens, hornbeams). Now spreading via native California live oaks and potentially other hosts.

**Ecological Significance:**
**THE MOST DANGEROUS MUSHROOM IN THE WORLD.** Responsible for 90%+ of mushroom poisoning deaths globally. Contains amatoxins that cause delayed-onset liver and kidney failure — symptoms may not appear for 6-24 hours after consumption, by which time irreversible organ damage has occurred. Mortality rate without treatment is >50%; even with liver transplant, survival is uncertain. Now established in PNW urban areas and expanding its range, posing an increasing public health threat.

**Threats:** This species IS a threat — its expansion into PNW ecosystems is a concern for human safety.

**SAFETY NOTE:** **LETHAL — NO ANTIDOTE.** Half a cap can kill an adult. Frequently mistaken for edible paddy straw mushrooms (*Volvariella volvacea*) by Southeast Asian immigrants and for edible Amanita species by European foragers. Any white to greenish mushroom with a volva and ring should be treated as potentially lethal until expert identification confirms otherwise. If death cap ingestion is suspected, go to an emergency room IMMEDIATELY — do not wait for symptoms.

**Key Sources:** Pringle et al. (2009) — death cap invasion ecology; Puget Sound Poison Center

### Truffle Species (*Tuber*, *Leucangium*, *Kalapuya*)

**Taxonomy:** Pezizales > Tuberaceae (Tuber), Morchellaceae (Leucangium), Tuberaceae (Kalapuya)
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER (partial — also ECM)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Hypogeous (underground-fruiting) ascomycete fungi producing round to irregular fruiting bodies (truffles) beneath the soil surface. PNW species include the Oregon black truffle (*Leucangium carthusianum*), Oregon white truffle (*Tuber oregonense* and *T. gibbosum*), and the recently described *Kalapuya brunnea* (named for the Kalapuya people). Fruiting bodies range from marble-sized to golf-ball-sized with complex internal chambering.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Throughout western PNW Douglas-fir forests; Oregon's Willamette Valley is the commercial center of PNW truffle harvest.

**Ecological Significance:**
Ectomycorrhizal partners of Douglas-fir and other conifers. Truffle spores are dispersed by mammals (flying squirrels, voles, chipmunks, deer) that dig and eat the fruiting bodies — the spores pass through the digestive system intact and are deposited in feces, inoculating new sites. This spore dispersal pathway creates the truffle-mammal-owl food web: truffles feed flying squirrels, flying squirrels feed spotted owls. PNW truffles are increasingly valued culinarily — the Oregon Truffle Festival is a major gastronomic event, and PNW truffles sell for $200-800/lb.

**Threats:** Clearcutting eliminating mature forest habitat; soil disturbance destroying hyphal networks; climate change altering soil moisture.

**Key Sources:** Trappe & Castellano (1991) — PNW truffle taxonomy; Maser et al. (1978) — truffle-mammal ecology

## 2. Saprotrophic / Decomposer Fungi

Saprotrophic fungi decompose dead organic matter — fallen trees, leaf litter, dead animals — recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem. In PNW old-growth forests, decomposition of a single large Douglas-fir log takes 200-500 years, during which the log serves as substrate for over 1,500 species of fungi, invertebrates, mosses, and tree seedlings (nurse log function).

### Honey Mushroom (*Armillaria ostoyae* and *A. solidipes*)

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Physalacriaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Clusters of honey-yellow to brown mushrooms with darker scales on the cap, white spore print, and a ring on the stipe. Grows in dense clusters at the base of trees and on dead wood. Produces black, shoestring-like rhizomorphs beneath bark that can extend meters through soil to colonize new trees.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; one of the most common forest fungi in the region.

**Ecological Significance:**
**Among the largest organisms on Earth.** A single *Armillaria ostoyae* individual in Oregon's Malheur National Forest covers 2,385 acres (965 hectares) and is estimated at 2,400-8,650 years old, making it both the largest known organism by area and one of the oldest. Armillaria occupies a dual ecological role: saprotrophic on dead wood (recycling nutrients) and parasitic on living trees (killing weakened or stressed individuals). As a parasite, Armillaria functions as an agent of natural selection, removing stressed and maladapted trees and creating gaps that increase forest structural diversity.

**Threats:** Not threatened — Armillaria is one of the most successful organisms in PNW forests. Climate-stressed trees are MORE susceptible to Armillaria, so infections may increase with warming.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Edible when thoroughly cooked, but frequently confused with several toxic look-alikes including the deadly *Galerina marginata* (which also grows on wood and has a ring). Honey mushrooms should ONLY be collected by experienced foragers. Some people experience gastrointestinal upset from even properly cooked Armillaria.

**Key Sources:** Ferguson et al. (2003) — world's largest organism (Armillaria in Malheur NF); Shaw & Kile (1991)

**Cross-Module References:**

- See ecological-networks: Mycorrhizal Network (paradoxically, Armillaria can disrupt mycorrhizal networks by killing host trees)

### Agarikon (*Fomitopsis officinalis* / *Laricifomes officinalis*)

**Taxonomy:** Polyporales > Fomitopsidaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Candidate for listing | IUCN: NE | FunDiS: West Coast Rare Fungi Challenge list

**Description:** Large, hoof-shaped to elongated polypore (bracket fungus) growing on the trunks of living and dead old-growth conifers. Individual fruiting bodies can reach 2-3 ft long and persist for decades, adding a new pore layer each year (like tree rings). White to gray exterior; bitter taste (the species name *officinalis* indicates historical medicinal use).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Extremely restricted in the PNW — almost exclusively found on old-growth trees (larch, Douglas-fir, spruce) over 200 years old. Once widespread, now rare due to old-growth loss.

**Ecological Significance:**
**The flagship conservation species for PNW fungi.** Agarikon is an obligate old-growth species — it requires the massive, centuries-old trunks of old-growth conifers that provide the stable substrate needed for its decades-long fruiting cycle. The loss of 90%+ of PNW old-growth has directly reduced Agarikon habitat. Paul Stamets has campaigned for Agarikon conservation, highlighting its potential pharmaceutical value (antiviral compounds active against poxviruses and flu in laboratory tests). Individual fruiting bodies may be 50-75+ years old, making each one irreplaceable on human timescales.

**Threats:** Old-growth logging (primary threat — cannot reproduce without old-growth substrate); extremely slow growth makes recovery from any disturbance nearly impossible on human timescales.

**Cultural Significance:** Used medicinally by PNW Indigenous peoples — documented by Robert Michael Pyle and others as a medicine fungus used by Northwest Coast nations. Ancient Greeks and Romans also used Agarikon medicinally (Dioscorides, 65 AD). (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

### Turkey Tail (*Trametes versicolor*)

**Taxonomy:** Polyporales > Polyporaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Thin, leathery bracket fungus with concentric color zones (typically brown, tan, gray, and white) resembling a wild turkey's tail feathers. Grows in overlapping shelf-like clusters on dead hardwoods and conifers. Pore surface white, finely textured.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; one of the most common wood-decay fungi globally.

**Ecological Significance:**
White-rot fungus that decomposes both cellulose and lignin in wood — one of relatively few organisms capable of breaking down lignin, the tough structural polymer that gives wood its rigidity. Turkey tail thus plays an essential role in carbon cycling and nutrient release from dead wood. Extremely common and widespread, it is one of the first colonizers of dead wood, beginning the multi-century decomposition process. Currently the subject of significant pharmaceutical research — polysaccharide-K (PSK) extracted from turkey tail is approved as an adjunct cancer therapy in Japan.

**Threats:** Not threatened; ubiquitous decomposer.

**Key Sources:** Stamets (2005); Haight et al. (2020) — turkey tail immunological research

### Reishi (*Ganoderma* spp.)

**Description:** Shelf or kidney-shaped polypore with a distinctive lacquered, reddish-brown surface (varnished appearance). Grows at the base of dead or dying hardwoods, especially bigleaf maple and alder. PNW species include *G. oregonense* (the Oregon reishi, growing on conifers) and *G. applanatum* (artist's conk — brown pore surface that can be etched with a stylus).

**Ecological Significance:**
Important wood-decay fungus contributing to nutrient cycling. *Ganoderma applanatum* (artist's conk) produces perennial fruiting bodies that can persist for decades, providing microhabitat for insects. Ganoderma species are among the most commercially important medicinal fungi globally.

### Morels (*Morchella* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Pezizales > Morchellaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Distinctive honeycomb-capped mushrooms with a pitted, ridged cap fused to a hollow stipe. Cap color varies from blonde to gray to black depending on species. Entirely hollow when cut lengthwise (important identifying characteristic). Multiple species in PNW, including post-fire specialists.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW; most abundant in riparian areas, disturbed sites, and recent burn areas.

**Ecological Significance:**
Post-fire fruiting specialists — massive morel fruitings ("burns morels") occur the spring following forest fires, making morels one of the first visible biological responses to wildfire. This phenology connects fire ecology to fungal ecology. Morels may be ECM, saprotrophic, or both (their nutritional mode is debated and may vary by species and life stage). Commercial morel harvest after fires is a significant economic activity in the PNW interior.

**Threats:** Climate-altered fire regimes; overharvest of post-fire populations.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Edible and highly prized, but must be thoroughly cooked (raw morels are toxic). The false morel (*Gyromitra esculenta*) — a wrinkled, brain-like mushroom — contains gyromitrin, which converts to monomethylhydrazine (rocket fuel). False morels can be lethal. Always verify: true morels are hollow when sliced; false morels are not.

**Key Sources:** Pilz et al. (2007) — USFS morel ecology after fire; Siegel & Schwarz (2016)

### Oyster Mushroom (*Pleurotus ostreatus* / *P. pulmonarius*)

**Taxonomy:** Agaricales > Pleurotaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Fan-shaped to oyster shell-shaped mushrooms growing in overlapping shelves on dead and dying hardwoods. Caps 2-8 inches, white to gray to brownish. Gills decurrent (running down the stipe). Spore print white to lilac-gray.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 3,000 ft (y=-41-22)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW on dead alders, maples, and cottonwoods; very common in riparian corridors.

**Ecological Significance:**
Aggressive white-rot decomposer of hardwood. Pleurotus is notable for being one of the few carnivorous fungi — it produces compounds that paralyze nematodes (roundworms) in wood, which the mycelium then colonizes as a nitrogen source. This nematode predation supplements nitrogen acquisition from wood decay. Pleurotus is one of the most commonly cultivated mushrooms worldwide and a key species in mycoremediation (Stamets' experiments using Pleurotus to break down petroleum hydrocarbons and filter agricultural runoff).

**Threats:** Not threatened; easily cultivated.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Generally safe to identify, but the angel wing mushroom (*Pleurocybella porrigens*) — a pure white, thin-fleshed look-alike growing on conifers rather than hardwoods — has caused fatalities in Japan among individuals with kidney disease.

**Key Sources:** Stamets (2005) — mycoremediation research; Barron (2003) — carnivorous fungi

### Cauliflower Mushroom (*Sparassis radicata*)

**Taxonomy:** Polyporales > Sparassidaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Large, spectacular fruiting body resembling a head of cauliflower or brain coral, cream to tan in color. Typically 5-20 lbs but specimens up to 50 lbs have been documented. The western PNW species *S. radicata* is distinct from the eastern North American *S. crispa*. Parasitic on living Douglas-fir root systems, then saprotrophic after tree death.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-59)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW conifer forests; found at the base of living and recently dead Douglas-fir.

**Ecological Significance:**
Causes brown cubical butt rot in living Douglas-fir, weakening the tree's structural base over years to decades. Despite being a pathogen, this decay process plays an important ecological role — hollow trees created by butt rot provide critical denning and nesting habitat for wildlife including black bears, raccoons, and cavity-nesting birds. The cycle of parasitism-to-saprotrophism bridges the living and dead wood ecosystems.

**Threats:** Loss of old-growth Douglas-fir; habitat reduction from intensive forestry practices that remove infected trees.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Edible and choice when young and fresh (firm, white flesh). Older specimens become tough and discolored. No dangerous look-alikes due to its unique cauliflower-like form, but always confirm identification with an experienced forager before consuming any wild fungus.

**Cultural Significance:** Harvested as food by some PNW First Nations, though specific documentation is limited in ethnomycological literature.

**Key Sources:** Trudell & Ammirati (2009); Siegel & Schwarz (2016); USFS forest pathology reports

### Chicken of the Woods (*Laetiporus conifericola*)

**Taxonomy:** Polyporales > Fomitopsidaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Bright orange-yellow shelf brackets growing in dramatic, multi-tiered clusters on standing or fallen conifers. Soft and succulent when young, becoming chalky and brittle with age. The PNW species *L. conifericola* is distinct from the eastern *L. sulphureus* complex — a taxonomic separation recognized by Burdsall & Banik (2001).

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,500 ft (y=-41-71)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW on conifers including Douglas-fir, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce; also on hardwoods occasionally.

**Ecological Significance:**
Causes brown cubical heartwood rot in living and dead conifers. An important habitat creator — by hollowing out heartwood, it produces nesting cavities used by woodpeckers, owls, and other cavity-dependent species. The dramatic orange fruiting bodies are among the most visually striking fungi in PNW forests and serve as indicators of internal wood decay.

**Threats:** Not threatened; common and widespread.

**SAFETY NOTE:** Do NOT consume from conifers without expert identification — some PNW *Laetiporus* species on conifers cause gastrointestinal distress in some people. Always cook thoroughly. Never eat specimens growing on yew (*Taxus*) or eucalyptus, which may concentrate toxic compounds from the host wood.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in PNW ethnomycological literature.

### Lion's Mane (*Hericium abietis*)

**Taxonomy:** Russulales > Hericiaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** White, cascading teeth (spines) hanging from brackets on dead or dying conifers, creating a waterfall-like appearance. The PNW species *H. abietis* is conifer-specific and distinct from the more widely known *H. erinaceus*, which grows on hardwoods in eastern North America and Asia. An old-growth indicator strongly associated with large-diameter dead trees.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-59)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW old-growth conifer forests; most common on large-diameter snags and logs of Douglas-fir and western hemlock.

**Ecological Significance:**
White-rot decomposer of dead conifer wood. Old-growth indicator species — its strong association with large-diameter dead trees means it is rare or absent in young, managed forests. The *Hericium* genus has attracted major medicinal research interest: compounds called hericenones and erinacines have been shown to promote nerve growth factor (NGF) synthesis, with implications for neurodegenerative disease research. While *H. abietis* is less studied than the cultivated *H. erinaceus*, it shares the genus-level biochemistry.

**Threats:** Loss of old-growth habitat; removal of large-diameter snags and dead wood from managed forests.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in PNW ethnomycological literature, though medicinal use of *Hericium* species is well-documented in East Asian traditions.

### Artist's Conk (*Ganoderma applanatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Polyporales > Ganodermataceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Perennial shelf (bracket) fungus with a hard, woody fruiting body. Brown upper surface with concentric growth zones; white pore surface that bruises brown instantly upon touch — hence "artist's conk," as people draw on the white surface. Grows a new pore layer each year, creating visible growth rings in cross-section (a "forest chronicle"). Individual fruiting bodies can persist for 50+ years on the same substrate.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW on both living and dead hardwoods and conifers; one of the most common bracket fungi in PNW forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
White-rot decomposer that is both parasitic on living trees and saprotrophic on dead wood, bridging the living-to-dead wood transition. During sporulation, ejects billions of spores daily — visible as a brown cloud beneath the fruiting body. Related to reishi (*Ganoderma* spp.) but lacks the same concentration of bioactive triterpenoids. The perennial growth habit and extreme longevity of individual fruiting bodies make it a long-term recorder of forest conditions.

**Threats:** Not threatened; extremely common and widespread.

**Cultural Significance:** Some PNW First Nations used the smooth white pore surface as a drawing or recording medium. Dried fruiting bodies used as fire-starting tinder due to the ability of the spore-producing tissue to catch and hold a spark. (CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** Gilbertson & Ryvarden (1987); Siegel & Schwarz (2016)

## 3. Lichens

Lichens are symbiotic organisms composed of a fungal partner (mycobiont) and one or more photosynthetic partners (algae or cyanobacteria). PNW old-growth forests support extraordinary lichen diversity — canopy lichen biomass in old-growth Douglas-fir forests can exceed 1,000 kg/hectare.

---

### Lung Lichen (*Lobaria pulmonaria*)

**Taxonomy:** Peltigerales > Lobariaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-INDICATOR, ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC globally, declining regionally

**Description:** Large, foliose (leaf-like) lichen with a distinctive ridged, lung-like surface texture (hence the name). Upper surface bright green when wet (photobiont is green alga), gray-green when dry. Lobes 2-6 inches across. Contains both a green alga and cyanobacteria (tripartite symbiosis), enabling nitrogen fixation.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 4,000 ft (y=-41-47)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW old-growth forests; most abundant in maritime-influenced forests with high humidity.

**Ecological Significance:**
Sensitive indicator of air quality and old-growth forest continuity. *Lobaria pulmonaria* is one of the most-studied epiphytic lichens in the world — its decline is documented across Europe and eastern North America as a consequence of air pollution (nitrogen deposition, sulfur dioxide). In the PNW, it remains relatively common in old-growth forests but is absent from young forests and polluted urban areas. The tripartite symbiosis (fungus + green alga + cyanobacterium) enables nitrogen fixation — *Lobaria* contributes significant nitrogen input to old-growth canopy ecosystems (estimated 1-5 kg N/ha/yr in wet forests).

**Threats:** Air pollution (nitrogen deposition, particulates); loss of old-growth habitat; climate change reducing fog frequency in coastal forests.

**Key Sources:** McCune & Geiser (2009) — lichen air quality monitoring; USFS PNW

### Old Man's Beard (*Usnea* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Lecanorales > Parmeliaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-INDICATOR, ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC (most species)

**Description:** Pendulous, hair-like (fruticose) lichen hanging from tree branches in long, pale green to gray-green strands. Distinguished from the superficially similar *Alectoria* by the presence of a tough, elastic central cord (visible when a strand is gently pulled apart). Multiple species in the PNW, ranging from short, bushy forms to strands exceeding 3 ft.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests; most abundant in humid, well-ventilated forests. Dramatically drapes branches in fog-belt forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important air quality indicator — *Usnea* is sensitive to sulfur dioxide and serves as a bio-monitor of atmospheric pollution. In PNW forests, Usnea biomass provides nesting material for birds (hummingbirds, kinglets) and winter forage for deer. Contains usnic acid, a well-studied antimicrobial compound. *Usnea longissima* (Methuselah's beard) can reach 10+ ft in PNW coastal forests and is becoming rare.

**Threats:** Air pollution; loss of old-growth forests; climate change; collection for herbal medicine.

**Cultural Significance:** Used medicinally by multiple PNW nations for respiratory infections, wounds, and as a fire-starting tinder. The antimicrobial properties recognized in traditional use are confirmed by modern pharmacology (usnic acid). (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

### Map Lichen (*Rhizocarpon geographicum*)

**Taxonomy:** Rhizocarpales > Rhizocarpaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Distinctive bright yellow-green crustose lichen with black margins (prothallus) forming a "map-like" pattern on exposed rock surfaces. THE alpine lichen — a pioneer organism on bare rock that initiates the long process of soil formation from stone. Extremely slow-growing (0.1-1 mm/year), which makes it invaluable for lichenometry — the science of dating rock surfaces by measuring lichen colony diameter. Some colonies estimated at 8,000+ years old, placing them among the oldest living organisms on Earth.

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-14,000 ft (y=84-308)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW on exposed rock surfaces at high elevations; common on glacial moraines, talus slopes, and volcanic rock above treeline.

**Ecological Significance:**
Pioneer species on bare rock — begins the soil formation process through chemical weathering of rock substrates via oxalic acid secretion. Over centuries, the accumulated organic matter from lichen growth creates thin soil layers that enable moss colonization, then eventually vascular plants (primary succession). Used extensively in lichenometry to date glacial moraines, rockfall events, and building stones — growth curves calibrated for specific mountain ranges. The extreme longevity and slow growth rate make it a living record of landscape history.

**Threats:** Air pollution (acid rain alters rock substrate chemistry); climate change affecting alpine habitats; physical disturbance of rock surfaces.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented for PNW nations. Used historically in European lichenometry for archaeological dating.

**Key Sources:** Brodo et al. (2001) — *Lichens of North America*; Nash (2008) — *Lichen Biology*; lichenometric dating studies (Innes, 1985)

### Pixie Cup Lichen (*Cladonia* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Lecanorales > Cladoniaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-DISTURBED
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER, ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Pioneer lichen on disturbed ground — logged areas, burned sites, roadcuts, and forest floor. Recognized by distinctive cup-shaped fruiting structures (podetia) that earn the "pixie cup" name. The *Cladonia* genus is extremely diverse, including reindeer lichens (*C. rangiferina*), British soldier lichen (*C. cristatella*), and hundreds of other species. Growth forms range from tiny goblets to sprawling mats of branching thalli. Some species contain cyanobacteria that fix atmospheric nitrogen, making them important for soil recovery after disturbance.

**Elevation Range:** Sea level to 7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW; most abundant on disturbed ground, rotting logs, thin soil over rock, and forest floor. Indicator of early succession following logging or fire.

**Ecological Significance:**
Pioneer and nurse species — among the first organisms to colonize disturbed ground, stabilizing soil and adding organic matter and fixed nitrogen. The nitrogen-fixing capability of cyanobacterial *Cladonia* species is important for soil recovery after disturbance events (fire, logging, volcanic deposition). Reindeer lichens (*C. rangiferina* group) form extensive ground cover in subalpine forests and serve as critical winter forage for caribou (historically present in PNW). *Cladonia* mats create microhabitats for invertebrates and seedling establishment.

**Threats:** Generally secure due to pioneer habit; however, intensive site preparation after logging (herbicide application, mechanical scarification) can eliminate lichen cover before it contributes to soil recovery.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented for PNW nations. Broadly significant across northern cultures — reindeer lichens are critical caribou/reindeer forage across the circumpolar region, supporting indigenous subsistence economies.

## 4. Mycorrhizal Networks — The Wood Wide Web

This section synthesizes mycorrhizal network ecology across the species profiled above. The mycorrhizal network is the biological equivalent of the GSD bus architecture — the connective tissue through which the forest operates as a single integrated system.

### Network Architecture (Simard Research)

Suzanne Simard's research at the University of British Columbia (spanning 1997-present) has demonstrated that PNW Douglas-fir forests are connected through mycorrhizal networks with **scale-free topology**:

- **Hub nodes ("Mother Trees"):** The largest, oldest trees connect to dozens of younger trees through shared fungal partners (primarily *Rhizopogon vesiculosus* and *R. vinicolor*). These hub trees have the most mycorrhizal connections and serve as network centers.
- **Peripheral nodes:** Younger, smaller trees with fewer connections; dependent on hub trees for resource supplements during establishment.
- **Resource sharing:** Carbon, nitrogen, water, phosphorus, and chemical defense signals flow through the network. Isotope tracer studies (using C-13 and N-15) have documented:
  - Carbon transfer from paper birch to Douglas-fir (and vice versa) through shared mycorrhizal networks
  - Net carbon flow from shaded trees to sunny trees (competitive facilitation)
  - Increased chemical defense signaling in trees connected to stressed neighbors
- **Network retention:** The mycorrhizal network retains approximately 30% of host photosynthate — not parasitism, but payment for services rendered (nutrient access, water transport, defense signaling, connectivity maintenance).
- **Death redistribution:** When a hub tree dies, it releases its stored carbon into the network for redistribution to connected trees — the tree's "last gift" to the community.
- **Network vulnerability:** Clearcutting severs networks that may have taken decades to centuries to develop. The network cannot regenerate quickly because the fungal partners require established tree roots as hosts.

### Key Network Fungi by Host Tree

| Host Tree | Primary ECM Partners | Network Type |
|-----------|---------------------|--------------|
| Douglas-fir | Rhizopogon, Cortinarius, Russula, Cantharellus | Long-distance, high connectivity |
| Western hemlock | Cortinarius, Russula, Piloderma | Medium-distance |
| Sitka spruce | Cortinarius, Russula, Boletus | Medium-distance |
| Western red cedar | Arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM), not ECM | Local, low connectivity |
| Red alder | Frankia (N-fixing bacteria) + AM | Local + bacterial nitrogen fixation |
| Whitebark pine | Suillus, Rhizopogon | Medium-distance, pine-specific |

**Notable:** Western red cedar uses arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM) rather than ectomycorrhizal networks. AM fungi form networks but typically shorter-range than ECM networks. This means cedar is less connected to the "Wood Wide Web" than Douglas-fir — an important distinction for chipset architecture.

### The Truffle-Mammal-Owl Food Web

A multi-trophic cascade linking mycorrhizal fungi to apex predators:

1. **Truffles** (Rhizopogon, Tuber, Leucangium, Kalapuya) fruit underground in association with Douglas-fir roots
2. **Northern flying squirrels** (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) and red-backed voles (*Myodes californicus*) dig and eat truffles (>80% of flying squirrel diet is truffle)
3. Spores pass through mammal digestive systems intact and are deposited in feces, inoculating new sites
4. **Northern spotted owls** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) prey on flying squirrels (primary prey item in many PNW populations)
5. **Therefore:** Spotted owl conservation depends on truffle productivity, which depends on old-growth Douglas-fir mycorrhizal networks — a multi-trophic cascade linking fungi to raptors through 4 trophic levels

### Nitrogen-Fixing Bacteria (*Frankia* spp.)

**Taxonomy:** Actinomycetales > Frankiaceae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-NURSE
**Conservation Status:** N/A (microorganism)

**Description:** Actinobacteria forming nitrogen-fixing nodules on red alder roots. Each nodule 1-5 mm, visible as clusters of bumps on fine roots when soil is carefully washed away. Cannot be seen without magnification in their soil habitat.

**Ecological Significance:**
The microbial partner enabling red alder's role as the PNW's primary nitrogen fixer (40-300 kg N/ha/yr). Frankia's nitrogen fixation transforms the chemistry of post-disturbance soils, enabling conifer reestablishment. Without Frankia, the PNW's disturbance-recovery cycle would be dramatically slower, and soil nitrogen would remain depleted for far longer after logging or fire.

**Key Sources:** Benson & Silvester (1993); Harrington et al. (1994)

### Forest Soil Microbial Communities

**Elevation Band:** All forested bands
**Habitat:** All forest habitats
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER

**Description:** The soil microbiome of PNW old-growth forests is among the most complex microbial ecosystems on Earth. A single gram of forest soil contains:
- 10^8-10^9 bacteria
- 10^6-10^7 fungal spores and hyphal fragments
- Thousands of bacterial and fungal species
- Complex food webs of protists, nematodes, and microarthropods

**Ecological Significance:**
Soil microbiomes drive nutrient cycling, organic matter decomposition, and plant health in ways that are only beginning to be understood. Old-growth forest soils have distinct microbial communities compared to young forests, with higher fungal-to-bacterial ratios and more diverse mycorrhizal assemblages. Soil disturbance (compaction, mixing, exposure to UV) can reduce microbial diversity by 50%+ and alter community composition for decades.

**Key Sources:** Fierer & Jackson (2006) — soil microbiome ecology; Paul (2015) — *Soil Microbiology*

### Puget Sound Marine Microbiome

**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC, HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-DECOMPOSER, ROLE-PRIMARY-PRODUCER

**Description:** The marine microbiome of Puget Sound includes phytoplankton (diatoms, dinoflagellates driving spring blooms), heterotrophic bacteria decomposing organic matter, and deep-water chemosynthetic communities at cold seeps.

**Ecological Significance:**
Phytoplankton produce 50%+ of Puget Sound's primary productivity through photosynthesis. Spring phytoplankton blooms (March-May) fuel the marine food web from zooplankton to whales. Deep-water bacterial communities mediate nutrient cycling and oxygen consumption in the Sound's glacially carved basins — microbial oxygen consumption in deep basins contributes to the Hood Canal dead zone. Marine bacteria also play critical roles in breaking down salmon carcasses, recycling marine-derived nutrients.

**Key Sources:** Puget Sound Partnership — ecosystem indicators; NOAA NWFSC

## 6. Applied Mycology and Conservation

### Mycoremediation (Stamets Research)

Paul Stamets' Fungi Perfecti (Olympia, WA) has pioneered mycoremediation — using fungi to clean up environmental contaminants:

- **Petroleum hydrocarbon degradation:** Oyster mushroom (*Pleurotus ostreatus*) mycelium breaks down petroleum compounds in contaminated soil. Stamets' experiments demonstrated 95% reduction in total petroleum hydrocarbons over 8 weeks.
- **MycoBooms for Puget Sound:** Hair mats inoculated with oyster mushroom mycelium deployed as floating booms to filter contaminated stormwater before it enters Puget Sound. Prototype testing demonstrates removal of E. coli and petroleum compounds.
- **Heavy metal bioaccumulation:** Some fungi concentrate heavy metals from contaminated soils, potentially enabling phytoremediation strategies.

### FunDiS West Coast Rare Fungi Challenge

The Fungal Diversity Survey (FunDiS) maintains the West Coast Rare Fungi Challenge list — a priority list of rare and poorly documented fungi needing targeted surveys. PNW species on the list include Agarikon (*Fomitopsis officinalis*), various rare Cortinarius, and undescribed species known from few collections.

### Conservation Status of PNW Fungi

Fungi receive almost no formal conservation protection despite their ecological centrality:
- **No ESA-listed fungi** in the PNW (or anywhere in the US)
- **No state-listed fungi** in Washington or Oregon
- Fungi are not systematically surveyed in environmental impact assessments
- The "Fungi Conservation" movement is pushing for fungi to be recognized as their own kingdom deserving independent conservation attention (not lumped with plants or ignored entirely)
- The FunDiS Rare Fungi Challenge is the closest equivalent to a conservation priority list
