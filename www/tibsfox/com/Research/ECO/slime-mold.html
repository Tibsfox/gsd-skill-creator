Slime Molds of the Pacific Northwest

Myxomycetes, Network Optimization, and the Mathematics of Brainless Decision-Making
ECO Overview Flora Fungi Networks Slime Molds Research
It has no brain. No neurons. No nervous system of any kind. It is a single cell — one continuous membrane enclosing millions of nuclei, spreading across the forest floor like a slow yellow river. And yet, given a maze, it will solve it. Given a map of cities, it will design a highway network. Given periodic stimuli, it will learn to anticipate them. The slime mold does not think. But it computes.

1. What Are Slime Molds?

Slime molds are not fungi, not plants, and not animals. For centuries they were misclassified as fungi because of their spore-producing fruiting bodies and their habit of growing on rotting wood. Modern molecular phylogenetics has placed them firmly in the supergroup Amoebozoa — they are, at their core, giant amoebae. They are more closely related to you than to any mushroom.

The name itself is a misnomer inherited from an era when anything slimy growing on dead wood was assumed to be fungal. The German mycologist Heinrich Anton de Bary began to untangle the confusion in the 1850s, recognizing that these organisms behaved nothing like true fungi during their active feeding phase. They engulf bacteria by phagocytosis (like an immune cell), they stream across surfaces using cytoplasmic flow (like an amoeba), and they respond to light, chemical gradients, and even vibration. No fungus does any of this.

Two Main Lineages

Plasmodial slime molds (Myxomycetes) — the classic slime molds. A single cell that can grow to cover several square feet. The plasmodium is a continuous network of protoplasm with no cell walls separating its millions of nuclei. This is the lineage that solves mazes and designs transportation networks. There are roughly 1,000 described species worldwide, with new species still being discovered in temperate rainforests like those of the PNW.

Cellular slime molds (Dictyostelids) — individual amoebae that live independently, hunting bacteria in soil. When food runs out, up to 100,000 individual cells aggregate into a single multicellular slug called a grex, which crawls toward light and warmth before differentiating into a fruiting body. Some cells sacrifice themselves to become the rigid stalk, so that others can become spores and disperse. This is one of the earliest known examples of cooperative behavior and programmed cell death in biology.

Other groups include the Protostelids (tiny, often single-spored fruiting bodies) and Acrasiids (convergent with Dictyostelids but phylogenetically distinct). The classification is still evolving — molecular data continues to reorganize these lineages.

By the Numbers

900+ species of Myxomycetes described worldwide as of 2024.

60+ species documented in Mount Rainier National Park alone, making it one of the most surveyed sites in North America for slime mold biodiversity.

150+ species estimated across the Pacific Northwest bioregion (Washington, Oregon, British Columbia), though many microhabitats remain unsurveyed.

2–3 new species described annually from temperate rainforest regions worldwide. Old-growth PNW forests are among the most promising sites for undiscovered diversity.

Life Cycle

The life cycle of a plasmodial slime mold is one of the most remarkable in biology. It transitions through phases that look like completely different organisms.

SPORE → AMOEBA → SWARM CELL → PLASMODIUM → FRUITING BODY → SPORE
Complete lifecycle of a plasmodial slime mold (Myxomycetes)
  1. Spore — a dormant, resistant structure. Can survive years of drought, freezing, and UV exposure. Dispersed by wind, water, and invertebrates. Spores of some species are as small as 5 micrometers.
  2. Amoeba / Swarm Cell — the spore germinates in moisture, releasing a single haploid amoeba. In liquid water, this amoeba grows flagella and becomes a free-swimming swarm cell. Both forms feed on bacteria.
  3. Plasmodium — two compatible amoebae fuse (syngamy), and the resulting diploid cell begins nuclear division without cell division. The result is a single, enormous, multinucleate cell — the plasmodium. This is the “intelligent” phase. It can grow from microscopic to covering entire logs. Physarum polycephalum plasmodia have been grown to over 900 square centimeters in laboratory conditions. It moves at speeds of 1–4 cm per hour via rhythmic cytoplasmic streaming.
  4. Fruiting Body (Sporangium) — when conditions deteriorate (food exhaustion, light exposure, drying), the plasmodium migrates to an exposed surface and transforms into one or many fruiting bodies. The entire protoplasmic network reorganizes into spore-bearing structures, often on delicate stalks, in a process that takes 8–12 hours. Each fruiting body may contain millions of spores.
Key insight: The plasmodium phase is where all the “intelligence” happens. A single cell, no neurons, no synapses — just a network of flowing protoplasm that computes optimal solutions to spatial problems through physics and chemistry alone.

2. PNW Species Guide

The Pacific Northwest — with its temperate rainforests, abundant dead wood, persistent moisture, and diverse moss communities — is one of the richest slime mold habitats in North America. The following species are among the most commonly encountered in Washington and Oregon forests. This is not an exhaustive list; it is a field guide to recognition.

“Many-Headed Slime”

Physarum polycephalum Habitat: dead logs, leaf litter, bark mulch • Season: spring through fall

The most studied slime mold in history. Bright yellow in its active plasmodial phase, forming a branching, vein-like network across its substrate. The name means “many-headed bladder,” referring to its fruiting bodies. This is the species that solves mazes, designs railway networks, and anticipates periodic events.

In the PNW, it is most commonly found on well-decayed logs in second-growth and old-growth conifer forests, particularly Douglas fir and western red cedar. It feeds primarily on bacteria and fungal spores in the decaying wood. Look for bright yellow fan-shaped networks, especially after rain.

“Dog Vomit Slime Mold”

Fuligo septica Habitat: bark mulch, garden beds, stumps • Season: spring and summer

The species most likely to alarm homeowners. It appears overnight as a bright yellow, foamy, scrambled-egg-like mass on bark mulch, wood chips, or stumps — sometimes covering an area of several square feet. The common name is unfortunate but descriptive.

Fuligo septica is one of the most cosmopolitan slime molds on Earth, found on every continent except Antarctica. In the PNW, it is extremely common in urban and suburban settings wherever bark mulch is used. It is completely harmless — it feeds on bacteria in the decomposing wood and will sporulate and disappear within a day or two. The aethalia (fruiting mass) can reach 20 cm across, making it one of the largest single fruiting structures in the Myxomycetes.

“Wolf’s Milk”

Lycogala epidendrum Habitat: rotting logs (especially conifers) • Season: year-round, peak in spring

Small (5–15 mm) spherical or cushion-shaped fruiting bodies, initially bright pink to salmon-red, darkening to brown with age. When punctured while fresh, they exude a pink paste — the “wolf’s milk” of the common name. This is actually a mass of immature spores.

One of the easiest slime molds to find in PNW forests. Look for clusters of small pink-red balls on the bark of well-decayed fallen logs. Very common in the Hoh Rainforest, Mount Rainier lowland trails, and throughout the western Cascades. Despite its appearance, Lycogala is now considered a true Myxomycete, though its unusual developmental biology initially led to debate about its classification.

“Tapioca Slime Mold”

Brefeldia maxima Habitat: large dead logs, stumps • Season: spring, early summer

One of the largest slime molds by plasmodial mass. The white to cream-colored plasmodium can spread across an entire log face, sometimes covering a square meter or more. When it fruits, it produces a dark, crusty aethalium that resembles a large piece of burned toast.

Named for its tapioca-like texture in the plasmodial phase. Common in PNW old-growth forests in spring, when moisture levels are high and temperatures are mild. The sheer size of this species makes it one of the most impressive slime molds to encounter in the field. A single Brefeldia maxima plasmodium can weigh several hundred grams.

Lindbladia

Lindbladia tubulina Habitat: rotting logs in old-growth forests • Season: spring through fall

Produces a dark brown to jet-black aethalium on the surface of well-rotted logs. The fruiting body has a distinctive tubular internal structure visible under magnification. The plasmodium is dark and inconspicuous compared to the flashier yellow species.

A strong indicator species for old-growth or mature forest conditions in the PNW. More common at lower elevations in the western Cascades and Olympics. Often found alongside Lycogala and various Stemonitis species on the same log.

“Chocolate Tube Slime”

Stemonitis spp. Habitat: rotting wood, bark • Season: spring through fall

Among the most visually striking slime molds. The fruiting bodies consist of dense clusters of slender brown stalks (sporangia), each 5–20 mm tall, rising from a shared base. The spore mass is supported by an internal network of threads (capillitium) that gives the structure a delicate, almost architectural quality.

Multiple Stemonitis species are common throughout PNW forests. S. axifera, S. fusca, and S. splendens are the most frequently encountered. They often appear in dense clusters on the underside of fallen logs — check the side facing the ground. Under a hand lens, the capillitium network is beautiful and geometrically complex.

“Coral Slime”

Ceratiomyxa fruticulosa Habitat: very wet rotting wood • Season: spring through fall

Tiny white branching fingers or antler-like projections, rising 1–5 mm from the surface of very wet, well-decayed wood. Unlike other Myxomycetes, Ceratiomyxa bears its spores on the outside of its fruiting structures rather than inside sporangia — a fact that has led to its placement in a separate order.

Extremely common in the PNW wherever very wet dead wood exists. Found year-round in the Hoh Rainforest and Olympic temperate rainforests. The variety C. fruticulosa var. porioides forms honeycomb-like plates rather than fingers, and is equally common. One of the first slime molds most PNW naturalists learn to recognize.

Hemitrichia

Hemitrichia spp. Habitat: rotting wood, bark • Season: spring through fall

Tiny golden to olive-yellow cups and spheres, typically 0.5–2 mm across, appearing in clusters on dead wood. When mature, the cups open to reveal a mass of spores threaded through a springy, elastic capillitium — the spirally ornamented threads can be teased out to surprising length under a hand lens.

Hemitrichia calyculata and H. serpula are the most common PNW species. H. serpula forms a distinctive net-like plasmodiocarps (fruiting bodies that follow the veins of the original plasmodium) in bright yellow. These tiny species reward close observation — they are easy to walk past but beautiful under magnification.

3. Intelligence Without a Brain

This is the section that matters most. Not because the ecology is unimportant — it is — but because what Physarum polycephalum demonstrates about computation, optimization, and intelligence challenges fundamental assumptions about what it means to “think.”

Physarum polycephalum has no neurons. No brain. No nervous system. No centralized processing of any kind. It is a single cell. And yet it can do all of the following, each demonstrated in peer-reviewed laboratory experiments.

The Experiments

Maze Solving — Nakagaki et al., 2000

At the University of Hokkaido, Toshiyuki Nakagaki placed Physarum polycephalum in a plastic maze with food sources (oat flakes) at the entrance and exit. The plasmodium initially spread throughout the entire maze, exploring all possible paths. Within hours, it withdrew from dead ends and converged on the shortest path between the two food sources. This was not a statistical fluke — it was replicated across multiple maze configurations. The result, published in Nature, was the first formal demonstration of problem-solving in a brainless organism.

Tokyo Rail Network — Tero et al., 2010

Atsushi Tero and colleagues placed oat flakes at locations corresponding to major Tokyo-area cities on a map-shaped agar plate. The Physarum plasmodium grew a transport network connecting all the food sources. The resulting network was strikingly similar to the actual Tokyo rail system — a network that had been designed and optimized by thousands of engineers over decades. The slime mold's network matched the real rail system in efficiency, fault tolerance, and cost. Published in Science.

Highway Network Optimization — Multiple Groups, 2010–2015

Following the Tokyo rail result, research groups applied the same methodology to highway networks in the United Kingdom, Canada, Spain, and other countries. In each case, the slime mold produced networks that closely resembled the actual motorway systems — and in some cases found more efficient solutions than the ones humans had built. The UK motorway experiment showed that Physarum connected 17 major cities with a network that was within 3% of the optimal Steiner tree — a result that would take a supercomputer significant time to verify.

Anticipation of Periodic Events — Saigusa et al., 2008

Tetsu Saigusa and colleagues exposed Physarum to cold, dry conditions at regular intervals. The slime mold slowed its movement during each exposure. After three cycles, they stopped the stimulus — and the organism continued to slow down at the expected times. It had learned the pattern. When they changed the period, it adapted. This is temporal learning without a nervous system — a form of memory encoded in the biochemical oscillation dynamics of the protoplasm.

Spatial Memory via Slime Trail — Reid et al., 2012

At the University of Sydney, Chris Reid showed that Physarum uses its own extracellular slime trail as a form of spatial memory. The organism avoids areas it has already explored by detecting the chemical signature of its own slime. When the slime trail was disrupted (washed away), the organism re-explored areas it had previously visited. This externalized memory is functionally equivalent to the “no-return” constraint in many graph traversal algorithms.

Risk-Reward Trade-offs — Latty & Beekman, 2010

Tanya Latty and Madeleine Beekman at the University of Sydney presented Physarum with two food sources: one high-quality but illuminated (slime molds are photophobic) and one low-quality but in the dark. The organism consistently made rational economic trade-off decisions, and — remarkably — was more consistent in its choices than many human subjects in analogous economic experiments. When presented with a third, “decoy” option (the asymmetric dominance effect), the slime mold was not susceptible to the irrational decoy effect that humans fall for.

Knowledge Transfer via Fusion — Vogel & Bhatt, 2016

When two Physarum plasmodia fuse, they merge into a single organism. Audrey Dussutour’s group at CNRS Toulouse demonstrated that a slime mold habituated to a noxious (but harmless) substance could transfer that habituation to a naive slime mold through fusion. The naive organism, after merging with the experienced one for just one hour and then being separated, retained the learned behavior. This is direct transfer of “memory” between organisms, mediated through cytoplasmic exchange.

The Mechanism: Cytoplasmic Streaming

How does a brainless, single-celled organism perform these computations? The answer lies in cytoplasmic streaming — the rhythmic, oscillatory flow of protoplasm through the network of tubular veins that make up the plasmodium.

The plasmodium is a network of tubes. Inside these tubes, the cytoplasm flows back and forth in a shuttle-like pattern, driven by actin-myosin contractions of the tube walls. The period of this oscillation is roughly 1–2 minutes. The flow rate is not uniform — it varies across the network in response to local stimuli.

How It Computes

Food source detected: the tube near the food source relaxes, increasing its diameter. Flow rate through that tube increases. Nutrients are transported more efficiently to the rest of the organism. This positive feedback strengthens the tube further.

Repellent detected: the tube near the repellent contracts, decreasing its diameter. Flow rate drops. The tube atrophies over time and may be abandoned entirely.

Dead end: a tube that leads nowhere receives no reinforcing signal from a food source. Its flow rate stagnates relative to successful paths. Gradually, the organism withdraws protoplasm from the dead end and reallocates it to productive tubes.

The result: over hours, the network self-prunes. Unproductive branches disappear. High-flow paths thicken and persist. The final network connects all food sources via an optimized topology — minimum material, maximum transport efficiency, and built-in redundancy for fault tolerance.

Murray's Law — Tube Diameter Optimization Q = (π ΔP r⁴) / (8 μ L)

The flow through each tube follows the Hagen–Poiseuille equation: volumetric flow Q is proportional to the fourth power of the tube radius r. This means a small increase in tube diameter produces a large increase in flow. The slime mold exploits this physics: tubes toward food thicken slightly, which dramatically increases flow, which brings more nutrients to expand the tube further. This is positive feedback through fluid dynamics.

Tero Model — Adaptive Network Dynamics (2007) dDij/dt = f(|Qij|) − αDij

Tero’s mathematical model of the Physarum network captures the dynamics in a single equation. Dij is the conductivity (effective diameter) of the tube connecting nodes i and j. Qij is the flow through that tube. The function f is a positive increasing function — more flow means more growth. The term αDij represents decay — tubes that carry no flow shrink. The steady-state solution of this system provably converges to the shortest path (for two food sources) or to a Steiner-tree-like minimum spanning network (for multiple sources).

The Mathematical Connection

The optimization that Physarum performs is equivalent to solving a Steiner tree problem in graphs: given a set of required nodes (food sources), find the minimum-weight tree that connects them, potentially using additional intermediate nodes. This problem is NP-hard — there is no known polynomial-time algorithm for it. The best exact algorithms scale exponentially with the number of required nodes. Approximation algorithms can get within a factor of ln(4) + ε ≈ 1.39 of optimal, but even those are computationally expensive for large instances.

The slime mold “solves” it in hours using physics. Not computation — physics. The fluid dynamics of protoplasmic flow, combined with positive feedback on tube diameter, converges to near-optimal solutions through a continuous analog process. No instruction set. No memory registers. No clock cycles. Just the Hagen–Poiseuille equation running in parallel across every tube in the network simultaneously.

This is analog computing in biology. The slime mold's protoplasmic network is not a metaphor for computation — it is computation. The physical system itself is the computer, and the laws of fluid dynamics are the algorithm. Every tube is a processor. Every junction is a router. The entire network solves the optimization problem in parallel, continuously, with zero energy overhead for instruction fetch or decode. This is what computation looks like before anyone invents the transistor.
Steiner Tree Problem (NP-Hard) Given G = (V, E, w) and required terminals S ⊆ V,
find tree T ⊆ E minimizing ∑e∈T w(e)
such that T connects all nodes in S

4. Ecological Role in PNW Forests

Beyond their computational novelty, slime molds play critical roles in the forest ecosystems of the Pacific Northwest. They are not charismatic megafauna. They will never be the subject of a wildlife documentary. But they are essential components of the decomposition cycle that drives nutrient flow through every temperate forest on Earth.

Decomposition and Nutrient Cycling

Plasmodial slime molds consume bacteria, fungal spores, and organic particles through phagocytosis as they spread across dead wood and leaf litter. This grazing activity accelerates decomposition by creating microchannels in the substrate and by redistributing nutrients. When the plasmodium fruits and dies, its body releases nitrogen, phosphorus, and micronutrients back into the soil. In old-growth Douglas fir forests, the sheer biomass of Myxomycetes on coarse woody debris contributes measurably to the nitrogen cycle.

Bacterial Population Control

Cellular slime molds (Dictyostelids) are major predators of soil bacteria. A single Dictyostelium discoideum amoeba can consume roughly 1,000 bacteria per hour. In PNW forest soils, where bacterial densities can exceed 109 per gram, Dictyostelids exert significant top-down control on bacterial community structure. This is analogous to the role of wolves in controlling ungulate populations — remove the predator and the prey population restructures in ways that cascade through the ecosystem.

Bio-Indicators of Forest Health

Slime mold species diversity correlates strongly with forest maturity and ecosystem health. Old-growth forests support significantly higher Myxomycete diversity than managed second-growth forests or tree plantations. The reasons are straightforward: old-growth forests have more coarse woody debris in more stages of decay, more microhabitat diversity (moss mats, bark crevices, soil pockets), and more stable moisture conditions.

Steven Stephenson at the University of Arkansas has spent decades documenting this pattern across North American forests. His surveys of Mount Rainier, Olympic National Park, and Great Smoky Mountains show that the presence of rare and substrate-specific species (like Lindbladia tubulina and Barbeyella minutissima) is diagnostic of old-growth conditions. If you find these species, you are standing in a forest that has not been logged in at least 200 years.

Connection to Mycorrhizal Networks

Slime molds share their substrate — dead wood, leaf litter, forest soil — with the mycorrhizal fungi that form the “wood wide web.” While slime molds and mycorrhizal fungi are not directly interacting in a symbiotic sense, they are part of the same decomposition and nutrient cycling community. The mycorrhizal network moves carbon and nutrients between living trees. The slime molds help break down the dead wood that feeds the saprophytic fungi that eventually become substrate for the mycorrhizal network. They are adjacent links in the same chain.

In the PNW, this chain is especially important because of the enormous volume of coarse woody debris in old-growth forests. A single hectare of old-growth Douglas fir forest may contain 400–1,000 cubic meters of dead wood. This is the habitat that supports the richest slime mold communities on the continent.

Old growth matters. Slime mold diversity in managed forests is 30–50% lower than in adjacent old-growth stands. The difference is not subtle. When we log old-growth forests, we do not just lose trees — we lose the entire decomposition community that makes the forest floor function. The slime molds are the canary in the coal mine.

5. Connections to the Research Series

This is where the slime mold research connects to the broader themes running through this site. These are not metaphors. These are structural analogies grounded in the same mathematics.

Network Optimization → Mesh Networks

The Physarum transport network is topologically optimal for resilient, decentralized communication: minimum total length, maximum fault tolerance, built-in redundancy through cycles. These are exactly the design criteria for mesh communication networks. Research groups at Hokkaido University and the University of the West of England have explicitly used Physarum-solver algorithms for network routing and topology design. The same physics that drives protoplasmic flow can drive packet routing.

Cooperation → Co-op Models

Dictyostelid slime molds are individual amoebae that aggregate into a cooperative multicellular organism when resources are scarce. Some cells sacrifice themselves (forming the stalk) so that others can survive (forming spores). This is cooperative behavior with a public goods problem, a free-rider problem, and kin selection dynamics — the same game-theoretic structures that govern human cooperatives and co-op networks. A colony of 100,000 individuals forming a functional cooperative structure through chemical signaling, with no central authority. Sound familiar?

Externalized Memory → Stigmergy

The slime trail as spatial memory is a textbook example of stigmergy — indirect coordination through modifications to the environment. Ant colonies use pheromone trails the same way. Termites build cathedrals through stigmergic coordination. In distributed computing, this maps directly to blackboard architectures and shared-state coordination protocols. When AI agents leave context artifacts for other agents to discover, they are implementing the same principle the slime mold invented a billion years ago.

Analog Computing → Beyond Digital

A single-celled organism solving NP-hard optimization problems through physics rather than computation. No instruction set, no memory hierarchy, no clock — just fluid dynamics and chemistry running in continuous parallel. This is what computation looks like before the abstraction of digital logic. It suggests that for certain problem classes (network optimization, shortest path, flow routing), analog physical systems may be fundamentally more efficient than digital ones. The universe has been computing longer than we have.

The Deeper Pattern

What the slime mold reveals is that intelligence is not a property of neurons. It is a property of networks. Any network with feedback, reinforcement, and decay can compute. Neurons are one implementation. Protoplasmic tubes are another. Fiber optic cables are another. Ant pheromone trails are another. The medium does not matter. The topology and the feedback dynamics do.

This is not a philosophical argument. It is a mathematical one. The Tero model that describes Physarum network dynamics is isomorphic to models of neural network plasticity (Hebbian learning: “cells that fire together wire together”), ant colony optimization (ACO), and reinforcement learning in artificial intelligence. The same differential equation — growth proportional to flow, decay proportional to inactivity — appears in all of them. The slime mold did not invent this mathematics. The mathematics was always there, running in the physics of the universe. The slime mold just found it first.

A billion years of R&D. Physarum polycephalum has been optimizing networks since before multicellular life existed. Every transportation engineer, network architect, and AI researcher working on optimization is, in a very real sense, reinventing what a single-celled organism solved in the Proterozoic.

6. Where to Find Them in the PNW

Slime molds are everywhere in the Pacific Northwest. You have walked past hundreds of them without knowing it. Once you learn to look, you will find them on nearly every forest walk.

Best Times

SeasonConditionsWhat to Expect
Fall (Oct–Nov)First heavy rains after summer droughtPeak fruiting season. Maximum species diversity. Plasmodia active on every rotting log. This is the best time.
Spring (Mar–May)Warming temperatures, persistent moistureSecond peak. Brefeldia maxima and Fuligo septica are especially common. Fresh growth on winter-soaked wood.
Summer (Jun–Aug)Dry conditions in most lowland forestsReduced activity in lowlands. Check high-elevation forests (above 3,000 ft) and riparian zones where moisture persists.
Winter (Dec–Feb)Cold, wet, short daysSlowed activity but not absent. Lycogala and Ceratiomyxa can be found year-round. Check mild spells.

Best Habitats

  • Rotting logs — the primary habitat. Logs in intermediate stages of decay (bark falling off, wood softening) are most productive. Check all surfaces, including the underside.
  • Stumps — especially old, mossy stumps in second-growth forests. The cut surface provides excellent substrate.
  • Leaf litter — particularly thick accumulations of bigleaf maple leaves in wet ravines. Plasmodia spread through the litter layer beneath the surface.
  • Bark mulch — in gardens, parks, and trail edges. Fuligo septica and Physarum species are common in urban bark mulch.
  • Moss mats — on rocks, logs, and tree bases. Small species of Hemitrichia, Trichia, and Arcyria fruit within moss cushions.

Best Locations

Old-Growth Forests — Highest Diversity

Olympic National Park — the Hoh Rainforest, Quinault, and Queets valleys are among the wettest and most diverse slime mold habitats in North America. The volume of coarse woody debris and the persistent moisture create ideal conditions. Hoh River Trail, Hall of Mosses, and Spruce Nature Trail are all productive.

Mount Rainier National Park — 60+ species documented. The Carbon River rainforest (lowest-elevation temperate rainforest in the contiguous US) is exceptional. Grove of the Patriarchs and Ohanapecosh are reliable. The NPS has conducted systematic Myxomycete surveys here.

North Cascades — lower-elevation old-growth valleys along the Skagit, Nooksack, and Cascade rivers. Less surveyed than Rainier or Olympic — potential for significant discoveries.

Accessible Locations Near Seattle

Federation Forest State Park — old-growth Douglas fir along the White River. Easy access, abundant dead wood, well-maintained trails.

Seward Park — one of the last old-growth stands in urban Seattle. Fuligo septica and Stemonitis species are regularly reported on iNaturalist from this park.

Tiger Mountain State Forest — second-growth with abundant dead wood. Good for Lycogala, Ceratiomyxa, and Fuligo.

San Juan Islands — Moran State Park on Orcas Island has old-growth stands with documented slime mold populations. The maritime climate extends the active season.

Your own garden — if you use bark mulch, check it after rain. Fuligo septica appears in PNW gardens regularly, especially in spring.

Field Equipment

  • Hand lens (10x or 15x) — essential. Many slime mold fruiting bodies are 1–5 mm. A hand lens reveals structure invisible to the naked eye. This is the single most important tool.
  • Spray bottle — a light mist can revive dried plasmodia and make fresh fruiting bodies more visible. Also useful for moistening substrate to check for slime trails.
  • Camera with macro capability — a smartphone with clip-on macro lens works well. The detail in slime mold fruiting bodies (capillitium, peridium, columella) is photogenic and diagnostic.
  • Small containers — for moist-chamber culture. Collect a piece of bark or dead wood, place it on moist paper towels in a sealed container, and check daily. Slime molds that were present as spores or microscopic plasmodia will fruit within 1–3 weeks. This technique dramatically increases your species count.
  • iNaturalist app — photograph and upload observations. The “Slime Molds of the Pacific Northwest” project on iNaturalist has an active community that can help with identification.
Moist-chamber culture tip: Collect small pieces of bark from standing dead trees (snags). Place them on wet paper towels in a petri dish or plastic container with a loose lid. Keep at room temperature (18–22°C) in indirect light. Check every 2–3 days with a hand lens. This is the standard technique used by professional myxomycetologists and it works remarkably well. A single piece of bark can yield 5–10 species over several weeks.
