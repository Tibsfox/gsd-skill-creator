## 1. The Transition to Production

After departing Prince's operation in 1988, Rogers returned to Los Angeles as an independent engineer. The transition from staff engineer to independent producer happened gradually over the following years, guided by two key mentors and driven by Rogers' growing interest in making creative decisions rather than purely technical ones [1][2].

The shift from engineering to production represents a fundamental change in cognitive role:

```
ENGINEERING vs. PRODUCTION -- COGNITIVE ROLES
=====================================================================

  ENGINEER                              PRODUCER
  ----------------------------------    ----------------------------------
  Question: "How do I capture this?"    Question: "What should this sound like?"
  Authority: Technical execution         Authority: Creative direction
  Relationship: Serves the artist's     Relationship: Shapes the artist's
                sonic vision                          sonic vision
  Decision type: Reversible within      Decision type: Defines the record's
                 technical bounds                      identity
  Risk: Technical failure               Risk: Creative misjudgment
```

Rogers has described this transition as the most psychologically demanding shift of her career. At Paisley Park, the creative authority was entirely Prince's; Rogers' job was to translate his intentions into sound. As a producer, she was now responsible for the creative direction itself [1][3].

## 2. Mentors: Tony Berg and Michael Penn

Rogers credits two specific mentors for guiding her transition from engineering to production:

### Tony Berg

Berg was a Los Angeles-based producer and A&R executive who worked across alternative, rock, and world music. He guided Rogers into the Los Angeles indie production community, connecting her with artists and projects that matched her emerging production sensibility. Berg's approach to production -- emphasizing sonic experimentation and artist development over commercial formula -- was influential in shaping Rogers' own production philosophy [1][4].

### Michael Penn

Penn (singer-songwriter, brother of actors Sean and Chris Penn) provided Rogers with her first experience of making production-level creative decisions. Working on Penn's sessions, Rogers learned how to move beyond technical execution into creative territory: arrangement suggestions, sonic palette choices, and the broader question of what a record should feel like as a unified artistic statement [1][4].

Rogers has described the Berg and Penn mentorships as complementary: Berg showed her the landscape of independent production, and Penn showed her how to operate within it. Together, they provided the bridge between the Prince-era engineering discipline and the creative autonomy that would characterize her independent work [1].

## 3. Geggy Tah: The Complete Chapter

### Band Context

Geggy Tah was formed by Tommy Jordan (vocals, bass, primary songwriter) and Greg Kurstin (keyboards, guitar, multi-instrumentalist). The project existed at the intersection of several musical traditions: avant-garde pop, lo-fi recording aesthetics, jazz-influenced harmony, and absurdist lyrical sensibility [5][6].

**Greg Kurstin's background:** Before Geggy Tah, Kurstin was a jazz piano student who had studied under Jaki Byard -- himself a sideman for Charles Mingus. This lineage places Kurstin's musical formation in the direct line of jazz tradition: Mingus to Byard to Kurstin. The harmonic sophistication that would later make Kurstin one of the most successful pop producers of the 21st century was already present in the Geggy Tah material [5][6][7].

**The name:** "Geggy Tah" derives from the childhood mispronunciations of each member's sister: "Geggy" for Greg, "Tah" for Tommy [5].

**David Byrne's involvement:** Geggy Tah was signed to Luaka Bop, the label founded by David Byrne (Talking Heads) in 1988 as a subsidiary of Warner Bros. Records. Byrne encountered their demo tape and described the music as crossing Ween and Prince with deeper and more articulate lyrics. Byrne's willingness to sign the project reflects Luaka Bop's mission of releasing music that defied commercial genre categories [5][6][8].

### Rogers' Connection to the Project

Rogers was approached for the Geggy Tah project through the Los Angeles independent music network. The project aligned with her emerging production interests: experimental artists working outside commercial formulas, with a need for a producer who could balance lo-fi aesthetics with professional recording technique [1][5].

## 4. Grand Opening (1994)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Title | *Grand Opening* |
| Year | 1994 |
| Label | Luaka Bop / Warner Bros. |
| Tracks | 15 |
| Recording | G-Son Studios; 16-track Stephens tape machine |
| Producer | Susan Rogers (co-produced with the band) |
| Aesthetic | Lo-fi avant-garde; tape-saturated; experimental arrangements |

The debut album was recorded on a 16-track Stephens tape machine at G-Son Studios -- a deliberate choice of analog limitation over digital flexibility. The Stephens machine (manufactured by Stephens Electronics, later John Stephens Design) was a less common alternative to the MCI, Studer, and Otari multitrack machines that dominated major studios. Its sonic character was warmer and more saturated than the MCI machines Rogers had trained on [5][6][9].

### Production Approach

Rogers brought her Prince-era engineering discipline to a radically different creative context. Where Prince's sessions demanded speed and technical precision in service of a singular commercial vision, Geggy Tah sessions prioritized experimentation and happy accidents. The 16-track limitation forced creative decisions about arrangement: with only 16 tracks available, every instrumental part had to earn its place [5][6].

The lo-fi aesthetic was not a limitation but a creative choice. *Grand Opening* sounds deliberately rough, with tape saturation, room ambience, and imperfect performances treated as features rather than flaws. This production philosophy represents the direct opposite of the Paisley Park approach, where Prince demanded technical perfection in service of polished commercial recordings [5].

## 5. Sacred Cow (1996)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Title | *Sacred Cow* |
| Year | 1996 |
| Label | Luaka Bop / Warner Bros. |
| Formation | Expanded to three-piece with drummer Daren Hahn |
| Recording | Home recording environment; multiple locations |
| Producer | Susan Rogers (co-produced with the band) |
| Aesthetic | Punk/funk/prog blend; more structured than debut |

The second album expanded Geggy Tah to a three-piece with the addition of drummer Daren Hahn. The recording approach shifted to home recording environments -- a production decision that placed *Sacred Cow* in the lineage of the emerging home recording movement of the mid-1990s, alongside projects by Beck, Sebadoh, and Guided by Voices [5][6][10].

### Sonic Development

*Sacred Cow* is more rhythmically focused than *Grand Opening*, reflecting the addition of a live drummer and the band's evolution toward a tighter punk/funk/prog hybrid. Rogers' production balances the lo-fi home-recording texture with greater rhythmic precision -- a skill set that directly reflects her Prince-era training in capturing drum sounds under imperfect acoustic conditions [5][6].

The album contains the band's most commercially successful single ("Whoever You Are") alongside material that remains resolutely uncommercial. This range within a single album demonstrates both the band's creative ambition and Rogers' willingness to let the material dictate the production approach rather than imposing a uniform commercial polish [5][6].

## 6. "Whoever You Are" -- Production Detail

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Song | "Whoever You Are" |
| Writers | Tommy Jordan, Greg Kurstin |
| Production | Co-produced by Susan Rogers, Greg Kurstin, and Tommy Jordan |
| Chart Position | #16, Billboard Modern Rock Tracks [6][10] |
| Commercial Sync | Mercedes-Benz commercial, circa 2001 [6] |
| Origin | Melody and refrain written by Jordan as a child, intended to thank polite drivers [5] |

"Whoever You Are" is the most commercially significant recording of Rogers' independent production career. The song's origin story is characteristically Geggy Tah: Tommy Jordan wrote the melody and refrain as a child, intending to thank courteous drivers and calm his father's road frustration. The adult recording preserved the childlike directness of the original sentiment [5][6].

### Production Analysis

The track's production demonstrates the synthesis of Rogers' engineering heritage and her developed production sensibility:

- **Rhythm section:** Clean, present drum and bass sound with minimal processing -- closer to the Paisley Park aesthetic than the lo-fi debut
- **Keyboards:** Kurstin's keyboard parts provide harmonic sophistication without cluttering the arrangement
- **Vocal treatment:** Jordan's lead vocal is mixed forward and dry, allowing the lyric's simplicity to connect directly
- **Mix balance:** The overall mix is notably uncluttered -- each element occupies its own frequency space, a hallmark of Rogers' Prince-era training in building mixes during tracking [5][6]

The co-production credit with Kurstin and Jordan reflects the collaborative working method: Rogers was not imposing an external production vision but facilitating the band's own creative instincts with professional engineering technique [5].

### Rogers' Stated Assessment

Rogers has described Geggy Tah as the band she felt never received the recognition it deserved. On the *Unsung* podcast (Crack Magazine/Sonos Radio, 2021), she cited the project as her clearest example of a record she was proud of that the market underweighted. This assessment is consistent across multiple interviews: Rogers does not frame Geggy Tah as a commercial failure but as a creative success that the market failed to recognize [5][11].

## 7. Greg Kurstin's Subsequent Trajectory

The Geggy Tah production credit placed Rogers alongside Greg Kurstin at an early moment in what would become one of the most prolific and decorated production careers in popular music. After Geggy Tah dissolved, Kurstin became a session musician and then a producer/songwriter of extraordinary commercial success [6][7][12].

Selected Kurstin production credits post-Geggy Tah:

| Artist | Project | Awards/Notes |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| Adele | *25* (2015), *30* (2021) | Multiple Grammys; "Hello" Producer of the Year |
| Foo Fighters | *Concrete and Gold* (2017) | Grammy, Best Rock Album |
| Beck | *Morning Phase* (2014) | Grammy, Album of the Year |
| Paul McCartney | *Egypt Station* (2018) | #1 Billboard 200 |
| Sia | Multiple albums/singles | Global commercial success |
| The Bird and the Bee | Multiple albums | Kurstin's own project |

Kurstin has won multiple Grammy Awards for Producer of the Year, Non-Classical -- among the music industry's highest honors for a producer. The trajectory from Geggy Tah's Luaka Bop releases to Grammy-winning mainstream production demonstrates both Kurstin's exceptional talent and the quality of the musical environment that Geggy Tah represented [7][12].

Rogers' role in this trajectory should not be overstated: Kurstin's success is his own. But the Geggy Tah collaboration is the documented professional intersection where Rogers and Kurstin worked together, and it deserves notation in any complete account of either career [5][6].

## 8. Barenaked Ladies: Stunt (1998)

| Detail | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Title | *Stunt* |
| Year | 1998 |
| Label | Reprise Records |
| Recording | Arlyn Studios, Austin, Texas (approx. 3 weeks, Jan--Feb 1998) |
| Production | Co-produced by Susan Rogers, Barenaked Ladies, and David Leonard |
| Completion | David Leonard, Phase One Studios, Scarborough, Ontario |
| Mixing | East Iris Studios and South Beach Studios |
| Mastering | Precision Mastering, Los Angeles |
| Key Tracks | "One Week," "It's All Been Done" |
| Sales | Over 4 million copies worldwide [1][2][13] |

*Stunt* is the most commercially successful album Rogers produced. The recording sessions at Arlyn Studios in Austin were intensive -- approximately three weeks of tracking in January and February 1998 -- and the album was completed by David Leonard at Phase One in Ontario [1][2][13].

### The Financial Mechanism

Rogers has been explicit and consistent about the financial significance of *Stunt*: the royalties from the album's multi-million-unit sales provided the financial mechanism that enabled her academic career. She did not leave the music industry because of burnout; she left because she had accumulated enough capital to fund approximately eight years of education -- from GED through doctoral dissertation [1][2][3].

This framing is essential to understanding the deliberateness of Rogers' career arc. *Stunt* was not a capstone or a farewell; it was, in Rogers' own telling, the funding mechanism for her reinvention. The album's commercial success was not an end but a means [1][2].

### Arlyn Studios Context

Arlyn Studios (now Arlyn Studios / Austin City Limits) in Austin, Texas, is a purpose-built recording facility originally constructed in 1984. The studio's live room and control room were well-suited to the Barenaked Ladies' ensemble sound, and Rogers has spoken about the quality of the acoustic environment [13].

### "One Week" and "It's All Been Done"

The two signature tracks from *Stunt* illustrate the range of Rogers' production contributions:

**"One Week"** -- a rapid-fire stream-of-consciousness rap-pop track that became a #1 hit on the Billboard Hot 100 -- required precise vocal engineering to capture Ed Robertson's high-speed delivery with clarity and presence. The track's success demonstrated that Rogers could produce commercially dominant pop-rock alongside her experimental work [2][13].

**"It's All Been Done"** -- a more conventionally structured pop-rock song with a singalong chorus -- showcased Rogers' ability to create a polished, radio-friendly mix that served the band's strengths. The song's production is notably clean and uncluttered, with each instrument occupying a defined frequency range -- the same engineering discipline Rogers developed during the Prince years, applied to a very different musical context [2][13].

### The Three-Week Timeline

The approximately three-week tracking timeline at Arlyn Studios is noteworthy. Rogers' experience sustaining 20-hour sessions with Prince had given her the capacity to work intensively under tight timelines. The *Stunt* sessions, while demanding by normal industry standards, were relaxed compared to the Paisley Park pace. This relative comfort under pressure is another artifact of the Prince training: once you have engineered under the most extreme possible conditions, everything else is manageable [1][2].

## 10. The Luaka Bop Context and David Byrne Connection

The Geggy Tah albums were released on Luaka Bop, the label founded by David Byrne in 1988. Understanding the label's mission illuminates why Rogers' production approach was particularly well-suited to the project [5][6][8].

### Luaka Bop's Curatorial Mission

Luaka Bop was not a vanity label or an artist-services imprint. It was a curatorial project: Byrne used it to release music that he found compelling but that did not fit any existing commercial category. The label's catalog includes:

- Brazilian tropicalia (Tom Ze, Os Mutantes reissues)
- West African pop (Zap Mama)
- Cuban dance music (various compilations)
- American avant-pop (Geggy Tah, Tipsy)
- Genre-defying international projects

The common thread was not genre but sensibility: Luaka Bop releases shared an appetite for sonic experimentation, cultural hybridity, and melodic intelligence that refused to answer to marketing categories [8].

### Byrne's Description of Geggy Tah

Byrne's characterization of Geggy Tah -- "crossing Ween and Prince with deeper and more articulate lyrics" -- reveals his assessment of the project's cultural positioning. The Prince reference is significant given Rogers' own history: Byrne was hearing Prince's influence in a project that would be produced by Prince's former engineer [5][6].

### Rogers and Byrne: Professional Intersection

Rogers also engineered David Byrne's 1994 self-titled album, a Luaka Bop release. This professional intersection meant Rogers was already working within the Luaka Bop ecosystem when the Geggy Tah project materialized. Her understanding of the label's aesthetic priorities -- experimentation over commercial polish, artistic integrity over market positioning -- informed her production approach on both Geggy Tah albums [1][4][14].

### The Tricky Sessions

Rogers mixed Tricky's *Angels with Dirty Faces* (1998), a trip-hop/experimental album that represents one of the most sonically radical projects in her discography. Tricky's production aesthetic -- dense, claustrophobic, heavily layered, built from samples and live performance in equal measure -- required a completely different mixing approach from the Geggy Tah albums or *Stunt* [14].

The Tricky sessions demonstrate Rogers' range: within a single year (1998), she co-produced a mainstream pop-rock album that would sell four million copies (*Stunt*) and mixed an experimental trip-hop album that operated in a completely different sonic universe. This versatility -- the ability to serve radically different material without imposing a signature production style -- is the defining characteristic of Rogers' independent career [1][14].

## 11. The Deliberate Exit Strategy

Rogers has been consistent in her account of leaving the music industry. The exit was deliberate, planned, and financially enabled by *Stunt* royalties. She did not burn out; she aged out of cultural alignment [1][2][3].

Her own words describe the logic: at mid-40s, producing records for a college-age alternative rock audience while no longer listening to alternative rock herself, she recognized a structural incompatibility. The disconnect was not about skill but about relevance -- she could still make records, but she could no longer connect to the music as a listener [1][3].

### The Exit as Systems Decision

```
ROGERS' EXIT DECISION -- DECISION MATRIX
=====================================================================

  FACTOR                    STATUS (c. 1999-2000)
  -------------------------+--------------------------------------
  Technical skill           Fully developed; not a limitation
  Creative interest         Shifting toward science; persistent
                            "why does this sound work?" question
  Financial resources       Stunt royalties provide ~8 years
                            of education funding
  Cultural connection       Declining; no longer listening to
                            the music she was producing
  Age vs. audience          Mid-40s producing for college-age
                            demographic
  Available path            GED -> undergraduate -> PhD
                            (estimated 8-10 years)
  -------------------------+--------------------------------------
  DECISION:                 Exit music industry; enter academia
  MECHANISM:                Stunt royalties fund the transition
  TIMING:                   ~2000 (after India Arie/Anderson sessions)
```

The decision is not impulsive; it is the most calculated move of Rogers' career. She identified the question she wanted to answer (why does sound affect listeners differently?), identified the resources she needed to pursue it (a formal scientific education), confirmed she had the financial runway to do it, and left [1][2][3].

## 12. Through-Lines to Later Phases

### To Module 4 (Academic Pivot)

The Geggy Tah experience is directly relevant to Rogers' academic work. Working with a band that answered to no genre, Rogers experienced firsthand the phenomenon her later research would formalize: different listeners respond differently to the same sonic material because they have different listener profiles. Geggy Tah's commercial underperformance despite creative quality is exactly the kind of market outcome that listener profile theory explains [3][5].

### To Module 5 (Berklee Laboratory)

Rogers' diverse production discography -- funk, art-rock, trip-hop, pop-rock, singer-songwriter, world music -- provided her with an empirical dataset of listener responses across genres. When she later developed the seven-dimension listener profile model at Berklee, she was drawing on two decades of observing how different listeners responded to different production approaches [3].

### The Persistent Question

Across the entire production arc, one question kept recurring in Rogers' experience: why does this recording work for this listener but not for that one? The question was present during Prince sessions (why did Prince reject certain mixes instantly while approving others?), during Geggy Tah sessions (why did the market undervalue music that was clearly excellent?), and during *Stunt* sessions (what made "One Week" connect with millions of listeners?). This is the question that drove Rogers to academia [1][3].

## 14. The Production Arc as Experimental Phase

Viewed from the perspective of Rogers' complete career, the production arc (1988-1999) functions as an experimental phase -- the period where the questions that would drive her scientific career were being formulated through practice rather than theory [1][3].

### The Accumulation of Evidence

Each production project during this period provided Rogers with data points about listener response to production choices. She observed:

- Why Geggy Tah's *Sacred Cow* received critical praise but limited commercial traction
- Why *Stunt*'s "One Week" connected with millions of listeners despite its unconventional structure
- Why Tricky's experimental aesthetic appealed to some listeners and repelled others
- Why David Byrne's music attracted a specific audience that overlapped only partially with Prince's audience

These observations constitute an informal empirical dataset -- the producer's equivalent of a research notebook. Rogers was not yet a scientist, but she was accumulating the observations that would inform her scientific questions [1][3].

### The Transition Period's Duration

The production arc spans approximately eleven years (1988-1999) -- longer than any other phase of Rogers' career. This duration reflects the time required for three concurrent processes: developing production skills, accumulating the financial resources for education (culminating in *Stunt*), and formulating the questions that would drive her academic work. The production arc was not aimless wandering; it was a three-body problem converging toward a single solution [1][2][3].
