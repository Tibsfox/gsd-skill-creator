## 1. Appointment and Position

Rogers was hired by Berklee College of Music in 2008 -- before completing her McGill doctorate (completed 2010) -- to teach in the Music Production & Engineering and Liberal Arts departments. She was awarded the Distinguished Faculty Award in 2012, two years into her doctoral completion [1][2][3].

### Berklee Context

Berklee College of Music (Boston, Massachusetts) is the largest independent college of contemporary music in the world. Its student body is internationally diverse, representing over 100 countries, and includes students studying performance, production, songwriting, film scoring, electronic production, and music business. This diversity is directly relevant to Rogers' research: Berklee provides a natural laboratory for studying how musical training interacts with cultural background in shaping auditory processing [1][2][4].

### Position Summary

| Role | Department | Scope |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Professor | Music Production & Engineering | Audio engineering, studio practice |
| Professor | Liberal Arts | Music cognition, psychoacoustics |
| Director | Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory | Research program |
| Course designer | Berklee Online | Psychoacoustics in Music Production (graduate-level online course) |

Rogers designed Berklee's courses in music cognition and psychoacoustics and wrote the Berklee Online Psychoacoustics in Music Production course, part of the Master of Music in Music Production program. This course represents the most direct translation of her research into practitioner education [1][2][3].

## 2. Teaching: Psychoacoustics for Practitioners

Rogers' teaching approach is fundamentally shaped by her own career arc: she teaches science to practitioners, not science to scientists. Her students are musicians, producers, and engineers who need to understand why the auditory system processes sound the way it does -- not as abstract neuroscience but as practical knowledge that informs their craft [1][3][4].

### Curriculum Design Philosophy

Rogers has described her curriculum design as translating the mechanisms of auditory perception into the language of the recording studio. The course content includes:

- **Critical bands and masking:** How simultaneous sounds interact in the cochlea and what this means for EQ and mix decisions
- **Loudness perception:** The Fletcher-Munson equal-loudness contours and their implications for mixing at different monitoring levels
- **Temporal processing:** How the brain integrates sound over time and what this means for groove, tempo perception, and rhythmic feel
- **Timbre perception:** How listeners categorize and discriminate between different sound qualities -- the domain that connects most directly to production decisions
- **Memory for music:** How listeners store and retrieve musical information and what determines which songs become "memorable" [3][4][5]

### The Practitioner-Scientist Bridge

The bridge between practitioner knowledge and scientific knowledge is bidirectional in Rogers' teaching. She draws on her studio experience to illustrate scientific principles, and she uses scientific findings to explain studio phenomena that practitioners experience intuitively but cannot articulate:

```
PRACTITIONER-SCIENTIST TRANSLATION TABLE
=====================================================================

  STUDIO OBSERVATION                    SCIENTIFIC EXPLANATION
```

## 3. The Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory

Rogers directs the Berklee Music Perception and Cognition Laboratory, which studies auditory processing in musicians using auditory brainstem response (ABR) measurement and behavioral testing methods [1][2][6].

### Research Equipment and Methods

| Method | Application | Key Measurement |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| Auditory brainstem response (ABR) | Subcortical auditory processing | Neural response timing and amplitude to musical stimuli |
| Behavioral psychoacoustics | Perceptual discrimination | Threshold detection, roughness evaluation, memory accuracy |
| Questionnaire instruments | Listener profile assessment | Self-reported musical preferences and listening habits |

### Distinctive Methodological Approach

Rogers' laboratory takes a distinctive approach to studying musicians: rather than the standard musician vs. non-musician comparison (which dominates much of the music cognition literature), Rogers studies **intra-musician comparisons** -- differences among musicians with different types of training, different instruments, different cultural backgrounds, and different listening histories [1][6].

This approach is enabled by Berklee's internationally diverse student body. In a single cohort, Rogers can study a classical violinist from Korea, a jazz drummer from New York, a beatmaker from London, and a sitar player from Mumbai. The question is not "do musicians hear differently from non-musicians?" (they do; this is well-established) but "how do different kinds of musical training shape auditory processing differently?" [1][6].

## 4. Research Program: Auditory Memory and Musical Training

Building on her McGill dissertation, Rogers' Berklee research program investigates auditory memory with a focus on how musical training shapes memory encoding and retrieval [6][7].

### Key Research Questions

1. **Genuine auditory memory:** How do listeners store and retrieve musical signals through genuinely auditory channels -- excluding nonauditory coding strategies such as verbal labeling, visual association, or motor imagery? [6]

2. **Training-specific effects:** Do different types of musical training (classical, jazz, electronic production, world music traditions) produce different auditory processing signatures? [6]

3. **Cross-cultural comparisons:** How does enculturation -- lifelong exposure to a particular musical system (Western tonal, Indian raga, Javanese gamelan, Arabic maqam) -- shape the basic auditory processing of musical intervals and timbres? [6]

### Grammy Foundation Research Grant

Rogers received grant funding from the Grammy Foundation (now the Recording Academy's Grammy Museum Grant Program) to support music cognition research at Berklee. The Grammy Foundation grants are competitive awards that support research into the creation, history, and impact of recorded music [1][2][6].

## 5. Tinnitus and Hyperacusis Research

Rogers' laboratory investigates tinnitus (perception of sound without external source) and hyperacusis (abnormal sensitivity to everyday sounds) in musician populations [1][6][8].

### Prevalence Data

Rogers has estimated that roughly 47--49% of audio professionals develop tinnitus. This estimate, drawn from her own research and cited in multiple public presentations, places audio engineers and musicians at substantially higher risk than the general population (estimated at approximately 10--15% for clinically significant tinnitus) [6][8].

### Population-Specific Risk

Rogers' research investigates which musician populations are at greatest risk:

| Population | Risk Factors | Notes |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| Drummers | High SPL exposure at close range; impulse noise from stick impact | Among highest-risk instrumental groups |
| Horn players | Direct acoustic radiation from instrument bell; confined practice spaces | Brass instruments exceed 100 dB SPL at the player's ear |
| Electric guitarists | Amplifier exposure; stage monitor levels; extended rehearsal | Risk increases with monitoring level and years of exposure |
| Vocalists | In-ear monitor levels; tendency to increase monitor volume to hear above stage noise | Risk from sustained in-ear exposure |
| Audio engineers | Long session hours; monitoring at elevated levels; cumulative exposure across career | Rogers' own professional context |

> **SAFETY NOTE:** This module reports Rogers' own published estimates and research findings. No clinical claims, diagnostic guidance, or treatment recommendations are made. Tinnitus and hyperacusis are medical conditions that require professional audiological assessment [6][8].

### Methodological Contribution

Rogers' approach to tinnitus research uses the ABR measurement methodology to investigate subcortical auditory processing changes associated with tinnitus. The auditory brainstem response provides a window into neural processing at the level of the cochlear nerve and brainstem nuclei, below the level of conscious perception. This allows Rogers to investigate whether tinnitus is associated with measurable changes in subcortical signal processing, not just with self-reported symptoms [6][8].

## 6. The Listener Profile Model

Rogers' primary public-facing scientific contribution is the listener profile framework: a model proposing that every listener possesses a unique perceptual profile shaped across a lifetime of listening, structured around seven key dimensions of a musical recording [3][9].

### Theoretical Foundation

The listener profile model synthesizes findings from:

- Rogers' McGill dissertation on consonance/dissonance and auditory memory
- Decades of psychoacoustics research on timbre perception, critical bands, and loudness
- Neuroimaging studies showing that musical preference activates the dopaminergic reward system
- Rogers' own twenty-year empirical experience as a recording engineer and producer observing listener responses [3][9].

### The Core Proposition

The model proposes that listener preference is not arbitrary and not primarily driven by social influence, marketing, or cultural conformity. Instead, each listener's preference pattern reflects a cortically shaped perceptual fingerprint -- a profile that determines which music "feels like home." The auditory cortex is literally shaped by repeated exposure to preferred music, creating a self-reinforcing loop: we prefer what we are wired to hear, and hearing it further reinforces the wiring [3][9].

---

## 7. The Seven Dimensions

Each listener's profile is characterized by their sensitivity to and preference for seven dimensions of a musical recording:

| Dimension | Description | Studio Relevance |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Authenticity** | Emotional genuineness; the sense that the creator's motive is expressive rather than commercial | Production choices that preserve or mask the performer's emotional directness |
| **Realism** | The degree to which the record sounds like live acoustic performance vs. studio construction | Mix aesthetics: natural room sound vs. heavily processed artificial space |
| **Novelty** | Preference for unfamiliar sonic territory vs. the comfort of the familiar | Arrangement complexity; genre boundary crossing; sonic experimentation |
| **Melody** | The role of melodic contour and memorability in the listener's emotional response | Vocal melody treatment; instrumental hook prominence; harmonic support |
| **Lyrics** | The weight the listener gives to linguistic content vs. purely sonic elements | Vocal intelligibility; lyric clarity in the mix; vocal-to-instrument balance |
| **Rhythm** | The degree to which groove, pulse, and rhythmic variation drive the listener's engagement | Drum and bass treatment; rhythmic feel; tempo choices; groove construction |
| **Timbre** | Preference for specific sonic textures; what Rogers calls "the street you live on" (citing Prince) | Instrument selection; synthesis vs. acoustic; distortion; spectral balance |

### Dimensional Interaction

The seven dimensions are not independent axes; they interact. A listener with high timbre sensitivity and high novelty preference will respond differently to the same recording than a listener with high melody sensitivity and high realism preference. The listener profile is a seven-dimensional vector, not seven separate measurements [3][9].

### The "Street You Live On"

Rogers uses the phrase "the street you live on" (attributing the metaphor to Prince) to describe timbre preference. Every listener has a "home" timbre -- a sonic texture that feels natural, comfortable, and right. This is why some listeners prefer the warm distortion of a tube amplifier while others prefer the clean precision of solid-state, why some listeners gravitate toward acoustic instruments while others prefer synthesizers. The preference is not rational; it is cortically encoded [3][9].

## 8. Neuroscience of Listener Preference

The listener profile model is grounded in specific neuroscience findings:

### Dopaminergic Reward

Pleasurable listening activates the mesolimbic dopamine system -- the same neural circuit involved in food, sex, and drug reward. Musical pleasure is not a metaphor for reward; it is the same neural mechanism. Neuroimaging studies (particularly Salimpoor et al., 2011; Zatorre & Salimpoor, 2013) have demonstrated dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens during peak musical pleasure experiences [3][9][10].

### Endorphin-Driven Honing

Rogers argues that endorphins released during pleasurable listening hone the auditory system toward the listener's existing profile. Each rewarding listening experience strengthens the cortical pathways that responded to it, making the listener more sensitive to similar sonic patterns in the future. This creates a positive feedback loop: preference drives listening, listening shapes cortex, cortex shapes preference [3][9].

### Cortical Shaping

Neuroimaging studies show that the auditory cortex is physically shaped by musical experience. Musicians show expanded cortical representations for the frequencies and timbres of their primary instruments (Pantev et al., 1998). Rogers extends this finding to non-musicians: all listeners' auditory cortices are shaped by their cumulative listening history, not just by formal training [3][9][10].

### Sub-Second Timbre Processing

Rogers cites research showing that listeners can dismiss a recording based on timbre processing alone in under one second. This speed indicates that the initial evaluation is subcortical or early-cortical -- faster than conscious deliberation. The listener's brain has already made a preference judgment before the listener is consciously aware of hearing the recording [3][9].

### Practical Implications for Record Production

The listener profile model has direct implications for record production practice that Rogers articulates in her teaching:

1. **There is no objectively perfect mix.** A mix that foregrounds melody will appeal to melody-dominant listeners but may underwhelm rhythm-dominant listeners. Production decisions are always decisions about target audience, whether the producer is conscious of this or not.

2. **First impressions are timbre-driven.** Because timbre evaluation happens in under one second, the opening moment of a record -- the first beat, the first chord, the first vocal syllable -- determines whether the listener's brain engages or rejects. Production must front-load the right timbral cues.

3. **Authenticity is detectable.** Listeners with high authenticity sensitivity can distinguish between genuine emotional expression and performed emotion, often without being able to articulate how they know. This has implications for vocal production: heavy pitch correction and timing quantization can strip the micro-imperfections that signal authenticity.

4. **Novelty and familiarity are in tension.** Every record must balance novel elements (which engage novelty-seeking listeners) with familiar structures (which comfort familiarity-preferring listeners). The balance point varies with the target audience's profile.

5. **Realism preference determines production aesthetic.** Listeners who score high on realism prefer productions that preserve the acoustic reality of performance -- natural reverb, minimal processing, audible room. Listeners who score low on realism are comfortable with (and may prefer) heavily processed, artificial sonic spaces.

These principles do not prescribe specific production techniques. They provide a framework for understanding why production decisions work for some listeners and not others -- a framework grounded in neuroscience rather than subjective taste [3][9].

## 9. This Is What It Sounds Like (2022)

Rogers' primary public-facing publication is *This Is What It Sounds Like: What the Music You Love Says About You* (W.W. Norton & Company, 2022), co-authored with Ogi Ogas [3][9].

### Book Structure

The book translates Rogers' scientific research into language accessible to a general audience. It uses the seven-dimension listener profile model as an organizing framework, illustrating each dimension with examples from popular music and Rogers' own career experience. The book draws on:

- Psychoacoustics research from McGill and Berklee
- Rogers' twenty-year career as a recording engineer and producer
- Neuroimaging studies on musical pleasure and reward
- Case studies of specific recordings and their listener reception [3][9]

### Ogi Ogas

Ogas is a computational neuroscientist and science writer who collaborated with Rogers on translating her research and experience into book form. His background in computational approaches to cognition complemented Rogers' experimental approach [3][9].

### "Above the Neck" and "Below the Neck"

Rogers distinguishes between two orientations in listener response:

- **"Above the neck"** -- intellectual engagement: melody recognition, lyric comprehension, novelty appreciation, harmonic analysis
- **"Below the neck"** -- embodied response: groove, rhythmic entrainment, timbre resonance, physical movement

Both orientations are neurally mediated, but they involve different processing pathways and different temporal dynamics. "Above the neck" responses tend to be slower and more deliberate; "below the neck" responses tend to be faster and more automatic [3][9].

## 11. The Science-to-Studio Translation

Rogers' career trajectory enables a unique translation function: she can speak to recording practitioners in studio language while drawing on scientific findings that those practitioners would not otherwise encounter. This translation is not unidirectional -- it flows both ways:

### Studio to Science

Rogers' twenty years of studio experience provided her with observations and questions that pure laboratory researchers would not generate. The question "why does this mix sound right on Monday and wrong on Tuesday?" -- a common studio experience -- translates to scientific questions about context-dependent auditory processing, attentional state, and circadian effects on perception [3][4].

## 12. Cross-Module Through-Lines: The Three Connections

### Through-Line 1: The Mechanism-First Approach

From Audio Industries (understanding the MCI console at component level) through Paisley Park (engineering sound at signal-path level) through McGill (studying auditory processing at neural level) to Berklee (teaching practitioners how the auditory system works): every phase begins with "how does the mechanism work?" before asking "what should we do with it?" [1][3][6]

### Through-Line 2: The Documentation Discipline

From the Vault (cataloguing Prince's recordings) through the dissertation (empirical research data management) to the laboratory (auditory brainstem response datasets): Rogers' habit of systematic documentation persists across domains. The Vault was her first research database; the laboratory is her current one [1][6].

### Through-Line 3: The Same Question

"What does a sound do to a listener?" -- this question is present in every phase of Rogers' career. At Paisley Park, it manifested as understanding Prince's instantaneous approval or rejection of mixes. During the production arc, it manifested as watching Geggy Tah be critically excellent and commercially invisible. At McGill, it became a formal research question about consonance/dissonance processing. At Berklee, it is the organizing principle of an entire research program and teaching curriculum [1][3][6][9].

## 14. The Convergence

The Berklee laboratory period represents the convergence of every preceding phase. Rogers is now doing all of the following simultaneously:

- **Teaching** studio techniques to the next generation of producers and engineers (drawing on Modules 1-3)
- **Researching** auditory processing and musical cognition (drawing on Module 4)
- **Publishing** scientific findings and a general-audience book (translating between all phases)
- **Presenting** at both scientific conferences (AES) and music industry events (Grammy Foundation)

No other figure in either the music production industry or the psychoacoustics research community occupies this exact position. There are former engineers who became teachers, and there are cognitive scientists who study music. But the specific arc -- self-taught technician to Prince's sole engineer to independent producer to doctoral researcher to laboratory director -- is singular [1][2][3].

### The Laboratory as Studio

There is a final architectural parallel worth noting. Rogers' Berklee laboratory is, in physical terms, a room full of equipment designed to generate and capture sound signals for analysis. It has headphones, signal generators, amplifiers, measurement devices, and calibration tools. It is, in other words, a recording studio -- reconfigured for science rather than art, but using many of the same physical tools and requiring many of the same technical skills.

The woman who maintained MCI consoles in 1978 is now maintaining auditory brainstem response equipment in 2026. The signals are biological rather than electronic, but the discipline is the same: calibrate the system, present the stimulus, measure the response, document the result. The technician's method, applied across a lifetime [1][2][6].
