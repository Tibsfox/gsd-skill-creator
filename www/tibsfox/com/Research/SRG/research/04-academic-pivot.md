## 1. The Decision and Its Logic

Rogers has been consistent across multiple interviews and public presentations in her account of the decision to leave the music industry: she did not burn out, and she was not pushed out. She aged out of cultural connection with her audience [1][2][3].

The logic is structural, not emotional. At mid-40s, she was producing records for a college-age alternative rock audience while no longer listening to alternative rock herself. The disconnect was not about technical capability -- she could still make records at the highest professional level -- but about cultural alignment. A producer who cannot connect to the music as a listener cannot make creative decisions that serve the material [1][3].

The *Stunt* royalties provided the financial mechanism. Rogers has estimated that the royalties bought approximately eight years of education -- from GED through doctoral dissertation. This was not an approximation or a hope; it was a calculated funding plan. Rogers left the industry with a specific destination, a specific timeline, and specific financial resources [1][2][3].

### The Autodidact Pattern Recurs

Rogers' academic career follows the same structural pattern as her entry into audio engineering:

```
AUTODIDACT PATTERN -- RECURRING ACROSS CAREER PHASES
=====================================================================

  PHASE 1 (1978)                        PHASE 4 (2000)
  Entry to Audio                        Entry to Academia
  ----------------------------------    ----------------------------------
  Starting point:  No formal education  Starting point:  No diploma
  Resource:        Army manuals,        Resource:        Stunt royalties,
                   Opamp bookstore                       university system
  Method:          Self-directed study   Method:          Formal coursework
                   from fundamentals                      from fundamentals
  Mentor(s):       None (self-taught)    Mentor(s):       Levitin, McAdams
  Duration:        ~5 years to           Duration:        ~10 years to
                   professional level                     doctoral level
  Outcome:         Hired by Prince       Outcome:         PhD, McGill
  Persistent trait: Mechanism-first      Persistent trait: Mechanism-first
                    understanding                         understanding
```

The parallel is not coincidental. Rogers is an autodidact by temperament -- she learns by starting at the mechanism level and building upward. The formal academic structure of a university education was, for her, simply a more organized version of the Opamp bookstore [1][3][4].

## 2. The GED at 44

Rogers earned her high school equivalency diploma (GED) at age 44. She has discussed this fact openly, without embarrassment, as a practical prerequisite for university admission. The GED was not a symbolic act; it was a bureaucratic requirement. Rogers could not be admitted to the University of Minnesota without a high school credential, regardless of her professional accomplishments [1][2][5].

The GED examination tests competency in five subject areas: language arts (reading), language arts (writing), mathematics, science, and social studies. For a woman who had spent twenty years as a professional recording engineer and record producer, the examination was not intellectually challenging. Its significance is purely biographical: it marks the point at which Rogers formally re-entered the educational system she had left as a teenager [2][5].

## 3. University of Minnesota: Undergraduate Years

Rogers enrolled as a freshman at the University of Minnesota, studying neuroscience and psychology for approximately four years. The choice of university was practical: Rogers had lived in Minneapolis during her Prince years and maintained connections to the area [1][2][5].

### Course of Study

Rogers' undergraduate curriculum focused on:

- **Neuroscience:** Neuroanatomy, neurophysiology, and cognitive neuroscience -- the biological foundations of brain function
- **Psychology:** Experimental psychology, research methods, statistical analysis, and perception -- the behavioral and cognitive frameworks
- **Music cognition prerequisites:** Psychoacoustics, auditory perception, and the emerging field of music psychology [2][5][6]

### The Mature Student Experience

Rogers entered the University of Minnesota as a 44-year-old freshman with no formal academic background but with two decades of professional experience that was directly relevant to her chosen field. She has described the experience of sitting in undergraduate lectures as both humbling and clarifying: the fundamentals she was learning provided the vocabulary she had been missing [1][3].

The question that had driven her to academia -- why does the same sound affect different listeners differently? -- was now being reframed in the language of neuroscience: how does the auditory cortex process musical signals, and how do individual differences in cortical organization produce different perceptual responses? [3][6]

### The Return to Minneapolis

Rogers' choice of the University of Minnesota reconnected her with the city where she had spent her Prince years. This was not sentimental; it was practical. She knew Minneapolis, had existing social and professional connections in the area, and the University of Minnesota's neuroscience and psychology departments offered the foundational courses she needed for graduate school admission [2][5].

### The Neuroscience Foundation

Rogers' undergraduate neuroscience coursework covered material that her audio engineering background made unusually accessible:

| Neuroscience Topic | Audio Engineering Parallel |
|-------------------|--------------------------|
| Action potentials and neural conduction | Electrical signal propagation in analog circuits |
| Sensory transduction (hair cells) | Microphone transduction (diaphragm to voltage) |
| Tonotopic organization of cochlea | Frequency response of analog filters |
| Neural coding of pitch | Frequency analysis in recording |
| Auditory pathway architecture | Signal chain architecture |
| Synaptic plasticity | Adaptive filter weight updates |

This parallelism is not a coincidence. The auditory system IS a signal processing chain -- the cochlea performs a mechanical Fourier transform, the auditory nerve transmits frequency-coded neural signals, and the auditory cortex assembles these signals into perceptual representations. Rogers' engineering training had prepared her to think in terms of signal chains, filters, and transducers -- the same conceptual vocabulary that auditory neuroscience uses, expressed in biological rather than electronic terms [3][6].

### The Mature Student as Asset

Rogers' presence in undergraduate classrooms at the University of Minnesota was not only unusual for its biographical context but potentially valuable to her fellow students and instructors. A professional with twenty years of applied experience in a field closely related to the course material brings a perspective that no textbook can provide. When a neuroscience lecture covers the tonotopic organization of the basilar membrane, the student who has spent years adjusting equalization curves on a recording console has an intuitive understanding of frequency selectivity that enriches the classroom discussion [1][3].

Several of Rogers' later public presentations reflect this bidirectional value: she describes her undergraduate education as transformative for herself, but she also notes that her instructors found her questions illuminating -- the practitioner's questions revealed aspects of the subject matter that pure theoreticians had not considered [3][4].

### Academic Performance

Rogers has not publicly disclosed her GPA or academic honors, but the fact that she was admitted to McGill's graduate program -- one of the most competitive music cognition programs in the world -- indicates strong undergraduate performance. Graduate admission to McGill's psychology department, particularly for specialization in music cognition under Levitin and McAdams, requires demonstrated research aptitude in addition to coursework competence [5][6].

## 4. McGill University: Graduate Admission

After completing her undergraduate work at the University of Minnesota, Rogers was admitted to McGill University's graduate program in psychology, with a specialization in music cognition and psychoacoustics. McGill, located in Montreal, Quebec, is one of the world's leading research universities, and its music cognition program -- anchored by the Centre for Interdisciplinary Research in Music Media and Technology (CIRMMT) -- is among the finest in the world [2][5][6].

### Why McGill

Two factors made McGill the optimal destination for Rogers' graduate work:

1. **Daniel Levitin** was on the McGill psychology faculty. Levitin's research on memory for music and his forthcoming book *This Is Your Brain on Music* (published 2006, written during Rogers' studies) aligned directly with Rogers' questions about listener response. Levitin became Rogers' co-advisor [2][5][6][7].

2. **Stephen McAdams** directed McGill's music perception research program. McAdams is one of the world's leading researchers in timbre perception -- the study of how listeners perceive and categorize the quality of sound independent of pitch and loudness. His expertise in experimental methodology and statistical analysis provided the rigor Rogers' research required. McAdams became Rogers' co-advisor [5][6][8].

## 5. The Dissertation

Rogers completed her doctorate in psychology at McGill in 2010. The doctoral thesis investigates consonance/dissonance (C/D) perception through a series of interrelated studies that bridge psychoacoustics and music cognition [5][6][9].

### Dissertation Structure

The thesis contains three principal investigations:

| Component | Focus | Co-Author(s) | Method |
|-----------|-------|---------------|--------|
| Study 1 | Roughness evaluations of just- and micro-tuned dyads | McAdams | Expert and nonexpert listener comparisons |
| Study 2 | Short-term memory for consonant and dissonant pure-tone dyads | Levitin, McAdams | Novel/familiar memory paradigm |
| Study 3 | Implications for nonlinguistic auditory memory and C/D theory | Levitin, McAdams | Theoretical synthesis |

### Central Question

The dissertation asks: are some musical intervals more mentally robust than others, and if so, what does this tell us about how the auditory system processes musical signals at the most fundamental level? [6][9]

This question is a direct translation of the question Rogers carried from the studio: why does this chord progression feel resolved to some listeners and unresolved to others? The studio version of the question is subjective; the dissertation version is empirical. The mechanism -- auditory processing of interval relationships -- is the same [3][6].

## 6. Consonance/Dissonance and Roughness Perception

The first major component of Rogers' dissertation investigates how listeners evaluate the roughness of musical dyads (two simultaneous tones) across a range of tunings [6][9].

### Background: The C/D Problem

The consonance/dissonance distinction -- why some combinations of tones sound pleasant (consonant) and others sound harsh (dissonant) -- has been debated in Western music theory for centuries. Competing explanations include:

- **Helmholtz's beating theory (1863):** Dissonance arises from rapid beating between closely spaced harmonics, perceived as "roughness"
- **Plomp and Levelt's critical bandwidth model (1965):** Two tones are maximally dissonant when separated by approximately 25% of the critical bandwidth at their center frequency
- **Terhardt's virtual pitch theory (1974):** Consonance is related to the degree to which a combination of tones evokes a clear virtual (fundamental) pitch
- **Cultural learning models:** Consonance and dissonance are partly learned through exposure to a musical culture's harmonic conventions [6][9][10]

### Rogers' Contribution

Rogers' study examined roughness evaluations of just-tuned and micro-tuned dyads (intervals slightly detuned from their just-intonation ratios) from both expert listeners (trained musicians) and nonexpert listeners. The micro-tuning approach allowed her to probe the boundary between consonance and dissonance with finer resolution than previous studies that used only standard equal-tempered intervals [6][9].

Key findings:

- Expert and nonexpert listeners showed broadly similar patterns of roughness evaluation, suggesting that the basic percept of consonance/dissonance is not primarily a function of musical training
- Micro-tuning manipulations revealed sensitivity thresholds: listeners could detect departures from just intonation at surprisingly small intervals, consistent with the critical bandwidth model
- The roughness gradient from consonance to dissonance is not binary but continuous, with individual variation in where listeners place the boundary [6][9]

### Publication

The roughness evaluation study was published in the *Journal of the Audio Engineering Society* (2007) with Stephen McAdams as co-author -- a significant publication venue that bridges the gap between psychoacoustics research and audio engineering practice [6][11].

## 7. Short-Term Memory for Musical Intervals

The second major component of Rogers' dissertation investigates short-term memory for consonant and dissonant pure-tone dyads using a novel/familiar memory paradigm [6][9].

### Experimental Design

The study presented listeners with sequences of pure-tone dyads and tested their ability to distinguish novel dyads from previously heard dyads. The critical variable was consonance: were consonant intervals (octaves, perfect fifths, major thirds) remembered more accurately than dissonant intervals (minor seconds, tritones, major sevenths)? [6][9]

### Key Findings

Rogers found differences in memory strength and fragility between consonant and dissonant intervals:

- **Consonant intervals** showed stronger memory traces -- they were more accurately recognized as familiar when repeated
- **Dissonant intervals** were more fragile in memory -- they were more likely to be misidentified as novel even when previously heard
- These differences provide evidence about the original signal processing of musical dyads: the auditory system appears to encode consonant intervals more robustly than dissonant ones [6][9]

### Implications

The memory findings have two significant implications:

1. **For auditory processing theory:** The differential encoding of consonant vs. dissonant intervals suggests that the auditory system is not a neutral channel -- it treats different frequency relationships differently at the encoding stage, not just at the evaluation stage
2. **For music perception:** The finding that consonant intervals are more robust in memory may partly explain why consonance is preferred across cultures -- not (only) because of cultural convention but because the auditory system physically processes consonant intervals more efficiently [6][9][10]

## 9. The McGill Research Environment

McGill's Centre for Interdisciplinary Research in Music Media and Technology (CIRMMT) provided the institutional context for Rogers' doctoral work. CIRMMT brings together researchers from music, psychology, electrical engineering, and computer science to study the perception, creation, and technology of music [6][8].

### Research Infrastructure

Rogers' dissertation work required:

- **Psychoacoustic testing facilities:** Sound-isolated booths, calibrated headphones, and stimulus generation systems for precise control of frequency, amplitude, and timing
- **Statistical analysis tools:** SPSS and MATLAB for experimental data analysis
- **Access to participant pools:** Both expert (musically trained) and nonexpert (untrained) listeners for comparative studies [6][9]

### The Academic Culture

Rogers has described the McGill experience as intellectually transformative. The rigor of experimental methodology -- hypothesis formation, controlled experimentation, statistical analysis, peer review -- provided a framework for the intuitive knowledge she had accumulated across two decades of recording. The studio engineer who "knew" that certain frequency relationships sounded better than others could now test that knowledge empirically [1][3][6].

## 10. The Centuries-Old Consonance/Dissonance Debate

Rogers' dissertation enters a debate with deep historical roots. The question of why some tone combinations sound pleasant and others harsh has engaged scholars across multiple centuries and disciplines [6][9][10].

### Historical Timeline

| Era | Scholar | Contribution |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| ~500 BCE | Pythagoras | Discovered integer frequency ratios in consonant intervals; monochord experiments |
| 1563 | Gioseffo Zarlino | Codified major/minor system; extended Pythagorean ratios to include thirds and sixths |
| 1638 | Galileo Galilei | Related consonance to coincidence of vibration patterns on the eardrum |
| 1863 | Hermann von Helmholtz | *On the Sensations of Tone*: beating theory, roughness, combination tones |
| 1898 | Carl Stumpf | *Tonpsychologie*: tonal fusion theory -- consonant tones fuse into a single percept |
| 1965 | Plomp & Levelt | Critical bandwidth model: maximum dissonance at ~25% of critical bandwidth |
| 1974 | Ernst Terhardt | Virtual pitch theory: consonance tied to harmonic template matching |
| 1991 | William Sethares | Tuning/timbre relationship: dissonance depends on spectral content, not just interval |
| 2010 | Susan Rogers | Memory strength varies with consonance: differential encoding at processing level |

Rogers' contribution is distinctive because it approaches the C/D question from a memory perspective rather than a purely sensory one. Previous research focused on how intervals sound in the moment of hearing; Rogers asked how intervals persist in memory after the sound stops. This is a question that an engineer -- someone who has spent years watching listeners respond to recordings after the fact, not during playback -- would naturally ask [6][9].

### The Roughness vs. Fusion Debate

The roughness theory (Helmholtz) and the fusion theory (Stumpf) represent two competing explanations that Rogers' work helps adjudicate. Roughness theory predicts that dissonance is caused by rapid beating between closely spaced harmonics. Fusion theory predicts that consonance is perceived when two tones merge into a single auditory event. Rogers' memory findings are more consistent with the fusion account: if consonant intervals are perceived as more unified, they would be expected to form more robust memory traces [6][9][10].

### Cross-Cultural Considerations

Rogers' work at Berklee (Module 5) extends the C/D question into cross-cultural territory. If consonance preference is partly neural, it should appear across cultures with different musical systems. If it is purely learned, it should vary with musical training and cultural exposure. Rogers' intra-musician comparison methodology at Berklee is designed to test exactly this question [3][6].

## 11. Implications for Auditory Processing Theory

Rogers' dissertation contributes to two ongoing debates in auditory science:

### The Consonance/Dissonance Debate

The finding that consonant intervals are encoded more robustly in short-term memory supports models that locate at least part of the consonance preference in low-level auditory processing rather than in cultural learning alone. This does not eliminate the cultural component -- musical enculturation clearly shapes preference -- but it suggests that the preference for consonance has a neural substrate that operates independently of musical training [6][9][10].

### Nonlinguistic Auditory Memory

Rogers' work provides evidence that the auditory memory system treats musical signals differently based on their acoustic properties, not just their semantic content. This finding extends the literature on auditory memory beyond speech and environmental sounds into the domain of musical interval processing [6][9].

### Methodological Innovation

Rogers' use of a novel/familiar memory paradigm represents a methodological contribution to the field. Previous C/D studies had relied primarily on real-time perceptual judgments -- asking listeners to rate how pleasant or unpleasant an interval sounds at the moment of hearing. Rogers' approach asked a different question: after the sound has stopped, what does the auditory system retain? [6][9]

This shift from synchronous evaluation to delayed memory testing revealed information that real-time ratings cannot capture. An interval might be rated as moderately pleasant during listening but prove highly robust in memory, or vice versa. The memory dimension adds a temporal depth to C/D research that purely synchronous methods miss [6][9].

### Statistical Methods

The dissertation employed standard psychophysical statistical methods:

- Analysis of variance (ANOVA) for group comparisons between expert and nonexpert listeners
- Signal detection theory (d-prime) for quantifying memory sensitivity independent of response bias
- Within-subjects and between-subjects designs to control for individual variation
- Careful counterbalancing of stimulus presentation order to eliminate serial position effects

McAdams' supervision ensured that the statistical methodology met the standards of the McGill psychology department -- one of the most methodologically rigorous programs in Canada [6][8][9].

### The Bridge to Production Practice

Rogers has consistently argued that these scientific findings have direct implications for record production. If the auditory system preferentially encodes consonant intervals, then production decisions about harmonic content, EQ, and arrangement are not just aesthetic choices -- they are choices about what the listener's brain will physically retain. This argument forms the basis of her teaching at Berklee [3][6][12].

## 14. The Academic Pivot as Complete Reinvention

The academic pivot is the most structurally dramatic transition in Rogers' career. Unlike the transition from technician to engineer (which happened organically under Prince) or from engineer to producer (which happened gradually through mentorship), the academic pivot was a complete reinvention: new field, new identity, new institutional context, new daily rhythm [1][2][3].

### Identity Transition

Rogers went from "Susan Rogers, record producer" to "Susan Rogers, undergraduate freshman" -- a transition that most people in their mid-40s would find psychologically untenable. The willingness to start over, to sit in undergraduate lectures alongside 18-year-olds, to complete homework assignments and take examinations after two decades of professional autonomy, indicates a specific character trait: Rogers values understanding more than she values status [1][3][5].

### The Ten-Year Investment

The academic pivot consumed approximately ten years of Rogers' life: four years as an undergraduate at the University of Minnesota and six years as a graduate student at McGill (including the time before formal doctoral candidacy). This is an enormous temporal investment, funded entirely by *Stunt* royalties and whatever savings Rogers had accumulated during her production career. The deliberateness of this investment -- choosing to spend a decade of peak professional years on education rather than continued production work -- is the clearest evidence of how deeply the scientific question mattered to her [1][2][3].

### What the Studio Gave the Scientist

Rogers' scientific career benefits from her studio experience in ways that no pure academic training could replicate:

- **Domain expertise:** She knows what recordings sound like at the console, not just in the abstract
- **Ecological validity:** Her research questions come from real-world observation, not theoretical speculation
- **Communication ability:** She can explain scientific findings to practitioners because she is one
- **Perceptual training:** Twenty years of critical listening developed her auditory discrimination to a degree that formal training alone cannot achieve [1][3][4].
