History & Origins

Strawman → Steelman, the Green team, MIL-STD-1815, the mandate, Ada 95/2005/2012/2022 — a language that was procured, not invented
Overview Language Concurrency History Implementations Safety-Critical SPARK Rosetta Learn
Most programming languages emerge from a single person, a small team, or an academic environment. C was Dennis Ritchie at Bell Labs. Pascal was Niklaus Wirth at ETH. Perl was Larry Wall in his garage. Python was Guido van Rossum over Christmas break. These languages evolved organically. Ada is different. Ada was procured. Every serious design decision exists in the public record because the US Department of Defense, which paid for the language’s creation, insisted on transparency and competition. As a consequence, Ada is perhaps the best-documented language in the history of computing.

Part I — Prehistory (1965–1974)

The software crisis

By the mid-1960s, computing had a problem nobody knew how to talk about. Hardware was scaling with something approaching Moore’s Law. IBM had shipped the System/360. DEC had introduced the PDP-8. The Apollo Guidance Computer was flying. But software was falling apart. Projects were routinely over budget by factors of two, three, sometimes ten.

By 1968, US government data showed:

  • Apollo onboard flight software consumed ~1,400 staff-years at Draper Lab and MIT Instrumentation Lab alone.
  • OS/360 at IBM had consumed ~5,000 staff-years at a cost over $500 million in 1960s dollars, with repeated slips and a long tail of known defects.
  • An Air Force survey found software cost in major weapons systems had climbed from ~10% in 1955 to ~50% by 1970.
  • Maintenance — fixing bugs, adapting to changes, porting — ran 60–80% of total lifecycle cost. For every dollar writing code, two to four dollars maintaining it.

Fred Brooks’ The Mythical Man-Month (1975) became the canonical text. The word “crisis” was not hyperbole — extrapolation showed that by the mid-1980s, software costs might dominate DoD budgets entirely if nothing changed.

NATO Software Engineering Conferences (1968, 1969)

In October 1968, NATO’s Science Committee convened a conference in Garmisch-Partenkirchen organized by Friedrich L. Bauer. The attendee list was a who’s who of 1960s computing: Dijkstra, Peter Naur, Alan Perlis, Christopher Strachey, Douglas McIlroy, Brian Randell, Tony Hoare, Ole-Johan Dahl, Kristen Nygaard. Bauer chose the phrase “software engineering” to be provocative — to signal that software development needed to become a disciplined practice comparable to civil or mechanical engineering.

A follow-up in Rome (October 1969) was darker. Randell later recalled that Rome was dominated by a sense that the problems identified at Garmisch had grown worse, not better. The term “software crisis” entered wider usage.

The DoD language zoo

In 1974, the US Army’s Computer Systems Command performed an inventory. DoD embedded systems were using more than 450 distinct programming languages and incompatible dialects: JOVIAL J3/J73, CMS-2, TACPOL, SPL/1, HAL/S, ATLAS, FORTRAN, COBOL, assembly (for every target), PL/I, CORAL 66, LTR, Pascal, ALGOL 60/68. Multiple dialects within each. Maintenance costs were spiraling. Contractors could bid high because only they knew their proprietary dialect. Training was a nightmare. Program managers paid to recompile, revalidate, and re-certify the same functional code multiple times because it could not be shared between services.

In January 1975, Malcolm R. Currie, the Director of Defense Research and Engineering, issued a formal directive that initiated what would become Ada. The directive stated the DoD would not accept the status quo — one of the following would happen: a single existing language would be selected and mandated, an existing language would be substantially modified and mandated, or a new language would be designed from scratch.

Part II — The Requirements Progression (1975–1978)

The HOLWG and David Fisher

The High Order Language Working Group was chaired initially by Lt. Col. William A. Whitaker (USAF), with technical lead David A. Fisher of the Institute for Defense Analyses. The HOLWG’s approach was distinctive: rather than convening a design committee, they produced a requirements document first and then held a competitive procurement. Nobody had ever tried to procure a programming language this way before.

Strawman → Woodenman → Tinman → Ironman → Steelman

  • Strawman (April 1975) — rough starting point, deliberately named to invite attack.
  • Woodenman (August 1975) — systematic reorganization based on Strawman feedback.
  • Tinman (January 1976) — first coherent vision. Strong typing, readable syntax, spec/body separation, explicit exception handling, concurrent processes, generics, real-time support.
  • Ironman (January 1977) — refined. Added named/positional parameters, default values, operator overloading, cross-unit type checking, representation clauses, access types with controlled scope, tasking with rendezvous-based communication.
  • Steelman (June 1978) — the definitive document. 120 numbered requirements. Probably the most detailed, considered specification of what a general-purpose systems programming language ought to provide that has ever been written.

Steelman explicitly required parallel processing (Section 9), strong typing with no implicit conversions, separation of specification from implementation, generics, exception handling, real-time scheduling support, and readability over writability. It was reviewed by essentially every major computing researcher of the era — Hoare, Wirth, Dijkstra all weighed in.

Existing languages evaluated and rejected

Before commissioning a new language, the HOLWG evaluated existing candidates: Algol 68 (too complex), PL/I (too large, too IBM-tied), Pascal (no separate compilation, no concurrency, weak I/O), FORTRAN 77 (no modularity), Simula (too heavyweight), CORAL 66 (too Algol-60-limited), JOVIAL (politically unacceptable), LIS (Ichbiah’s own earlier language). The formal conclusion in 1976: no existing language met Ironman. A new language was justified.

Part III — The Competition (1977–1979)

Seventeen proposals, four finalists

April 1977, the DoD issued an RFP. Seventeen proposals were received. In July 1977, the HOLWG announced four finalists, identified by color codes to prevent evaluation bias:

Each team received ~$500,000 (1977 dollars). In February–April 1978 the designs were evaluated blind by 83 reviewers. Green and Red advanced to Phase II. Phase II ran 1978–1979 with each team receiving ~$1 million to refine to meet Steelman and produce a prototype.

Ichbiah’s design philosophy

  1. Readability over writability. A programmer writes once but reads many times during maintenance. Optimize for the reader.
  2. Explicit over implicit. Nothing happens behind the programmer’s back.
  3. Compile-time checking. Catch as many errors as possible before the program runs.
  4. Concurrency as first-class. Tasks, not a library. Rendezvous based on Hoare’s CSP (CACM, August 1978 — fresh in the design community’s thinking).
  5. Packages as the unit of modularity. Spec declares interface; body contains implementation. Clients depend only on specifications.

The Green team

  • Jean Ichbiah (France) — team leader and principal designer
  • Jean-Claude Heliard (France) — type system
  • Olivier Roubine (France) — tasking model, real-time features
  • Bernd Krieg-Brueckner (Germany) — type system, visibility rules
  • Brian Wichmann (UK, NPL) — numerical model, floating-point semantics
  • John Barnes (UK) — documentation, readability (later author of the canonical Ada textbook)
  • Henry Ledgard (US) — software engineering concerns
  • Paul N. Hilfinger (US) — representation clauses, low-level features

May 1979 — Green wins

In May 1979, the HOLWG announced: Green had won. Jean Ichbiah’s team at CII Honeywell Bull in Louveciennes had designed the winning language. The DoD had its new programming language. It did not yet have a name.

Hoare’s Turing Award warning

In October 1980, C. A. R. (Tony) Hoare delivered his Turing Award lecture at the ACM Annual Conference in Nashville. The lecture, The Emperor’s Old Clothes, warned about Ada without naming it directly for much of the talk. The reference was unmistakable:

“Do not allow this language, in its present state, to be used in applications where reliability is critical… The next rocket to go astray as a result of a programming language error may not be an exploratory space rocket on a harmless trip to Venus: It may be a nuclear warhead exploding over one of our own cities.”

Hoare’s concern was that Ada was too large, too complex, and too ambitious to be implementable without introducing bugs of its own. He was not wrong that Ada was large. What he did not fully anticipate was that the formal standardization, validation, and compiler-checking infrastructure around Ada would mitigate most of those concerns over time. Hoare later qualified his remarks — he acknowledged Ada had features he respected, including the tasking model based on his own CSP work.

Part IV — Naming the Language (1979)

Jack Cooper, an HOLWG advisor, proposed naming the language Ada after Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace (1815–1852). William Whitaker approved. The decision was finalized mid-1979.

Who was Ada Lovelace?

Ada was the only legitimate child of George Gordon, Lord Byron — the Romantic poet — and Anne Isabella Milbanke. She never met her father; Byron left England shortly after her birth and died in Greece in 1824 when she was eight. Ada was raised by her mother, who insisted on an unusually rigorous mathematical education, partly in the hope of countering what she feared were Byron’s “dangerous” poetic tendencies in her daughter’s inheritance.

Ada proved a gifted mathematician. In her teens, she was introduced to Mary Somerville, who tutored her and introduced her to London’s scientific community. Through Somerville she met Charles Babbage in June 1833, aged seventeen. Ada became one of Babbage’s few close intellectual collaborators on the Analytical Engine.

In 1843, Ada translated Luigi Menabrea’s paper on the Analytical Engine into English and added her own Notes, lettered A through G, which were three times the length of the original translation. Note G contained a detailed worked example showing how the Analytical Engine could compute Bernoulli numbers — often called the first computer program. Note A contained the most-quoted passage: “The Analytical Engine has no pretensions whatever to originate anything. It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform.”

Ada died of uterine cancer on 27 November 1852, aged 36.

Why the DoD named a language after her

Symbolic and deliberate. In 1979, computing was overwhelmingly male and the DoD more so. Naming a flagship programming language after a nineteenth-century woman was an unusual gesture of historical recognition. It honored a genuine pioneer. The name was also practical: short, pronounceable in every European language, not a trademark, already associated with the first computer program.

The DoD registered “Ada” as a trademark to enforce compliance with the language standard — a compiler could not call itself an “Ada compiler” without passing official validation. The trademark was released to the public domain in 1987, but validation requirements persisted.

Part V — MIL-STD-1815 and the First Standard

1815 as Ada’s birth year

A revised reference manual was published in November 1980, adopted as a U.S. military standard in December 1980 as MIL-STD-1815. The number was chosen as a deliberate tribute: 1815 was the year of Ada Lovelace’s birth. There is no other reason for the numbering — the DoD’s standards system normally assigns consecutive numbers without historical meaning. An exception was granted specifically to honor the namesake.

The Ada Joint Program Office and the ACVC

The Ada Joint Program Office (AJPO) was established in 1980 under the Office of the Secretary of Defense. It controlled the Ada trademark, funded compiler development and validation, administered validation, published guidance, and ran the Ada Information Clearinghouse.

The Ada Compiler Validation Capability (ACVC) — developed at SofTech (one of the losing Phase I teams) starting in 1980, operational by 1983 — was a test suite that every compiler had to pass before it could be called an “Ada compiler.” Thousands of test cases. If any failed, the compiler could not legally call itself Ada. The consequences were profound:

  1. No fragmentation. Unlike C, C++, or FORTRAN, Ada never had dialects. Every validated compiler behaved the same way on the same program.
  2. Slow compiler startup. Only well-funded vendors could bring Ada compilers to market.
  3. Consistent semantics. Programmers could write portable Ada code that ran unchanged on any platform with a validated compiler.

Ada 83

MIL-STD-1815 was considered provisional. Refinements followed, and the revised standard was published as ANSI/MIL-STD-1815A in January 1983 — Ada 83, the first truly stable standard. ISO 8652:1987 was the ISO version.

Ada 83 features: strong static typing with derived types, subtypes, and constraints; packages with spec/body separation; generics; tasking with rendezvous; exception handling; separate compilation with cross-unit checking; representation clauses; pragmas; attributes. Notably absent: no object-oriented programming, no hierarchical library units, no protected objects.

First validated compiler: NYU Ada/Ed (Robert Dewar and Edmond Schonberg, April 1983). Production compilers followed quickly: Verdix, TeleSoft, DEC, Alsys (Jean Ichbiah’s own company), Intermetrics, Rational.

Part VI — The Mandate Era (1987–1997)

DoD Directive 3405.1 (1987)

On 2 April 1987, the DoD issued Directive 3405.1, the formal Ada mandate: all new DoD mission-critical software must be written in Ada unless a waiver was granted. The mandate applied to weapons systems, C3I, and automatic test equipment. It did not apply to information systems (COBOL ok), scientific research (FORTRAN ok), or one-off tools.

Who resisted and why

  • Unix and C programmers — the largest resistance group. Ada was verbose, bureaucratic, culturally alien.
  • Cost-conscious program managers — Ada compilers were more expensive, training was required, developers charged higher rates.
  • Embedded systems engineers — early Ada compilers sometimes generated larger/slower code than C. By the late 1980s this was becoming less true but the reputation lingered.
  • Academics — often preferred Pascal or C/C++, in some cases still carrying grudges from the Phase I competition or Hoare’s Turing Award speech.
  • Existing contractor teams — did not want to start over.

In practice, roughly 60–70% of new DoD embedded software during the mandate era was Ada; the rest received waivers.

The 1997 repeal

On 29 April 1997, Deputy Secretary of Defense John J. Hamre signed a memorandum canceling Directive 3405.1. The official justification: commercial off-the-shelf tools and middleware had matured. Behind the official justification: the rise of Windows NT, C++, and Java; the end of the Cold War reducing appetite for custom solutions; the culture war over Ada having exhausted both sides.

Did Ada actually lose?

Hamre’s memo was widely interpreted as “Ada is dead.” But Ada was not dead — it was just no longer mandated. Ada remained entrenched in safety-critical, life-critical, and mission-critical niches where its features paid off: aerospace, rail signaling, medical devices, nuclear I&C, air traffic control. The commercial tool vendors consolidated, pivoted, or were acquired, but the technology persisted. GNAT — the free compiler built on GCC by Robert Dewar and Edmond Schonberg at NYU — had entered the market in 1995 and ensured Ada would not die of commercial neglect.

The mandate era is best understood as the period during which Ada achieved critical mass in the application domains that really needed it. When the mandate went away, Ada stopped being the default choice for programs that did not need its features but continued to be the right choice for programs that did.

Part VII — Ada 95

The Ada 9X Project (1988–1994)

By the late 1980s, Ada 83 needed updating. The OOP revolution had arrived. Users wanted hierarchical library units, lighter-weight synchronization, better real-time support, and IEEE 754 alignment.

The Ada 9X Project was funded by the Air Force through a contract with Intermetrics (the former Red team). Technical lead: S. Tucker Taft. Taft’s team included Ben Brosgol (who had been on the Red team in the original competition), Offer Pazy, and other Ada veterans.

The 9X process was structured differently from the original design. Over 750 Revision Requests were received from the community. The team produced successive Mapping Documents for comment, then draft standards, with international participation via ISO Working Group 9.

Ada 95 features

Published as ISO/IEC 8652:1995 in February 1995 — the first internationally standardized object-oriented programming language (beating ISO C++, which did not arrive until 1998).

  • Tagged types — Ada’s mechanism for inheritance. Single inheritance via type Derived is new Parent with record ... end record;. Dispatching happens at class-wide level: Shape'Class. Static dispatch remains the default — dynamic dispatch only when the programmer writes T'Class explicitly.
  • Hierarchical library units — child packages that can extend a parent without modifying it. Ada.Text_IO.Integer_IO.
  • Protected objects — Hoare-style monitors with barriers. The overhaul of concurrency that made Ada practical for passive data sharing. See the concurrency page.
  • The Annex system — optional language additions. Annex C (Systems Programming), D (Real-Time), E (Distributed), F (Information), G (Numerics), H (High Integrity).
  • Unicode support — Wide_Character, Wide_String.
  • Numerics revamp — proper IEEE 754 alignment.

Part VIII — Ada 2005, 2012, 2022

Ada 2005 (ISO/IEC 8652:1995/Amd 1:2007)

  • Java-style interfaces — multiple inheritance of interfaces only
  • Synchronized interfaces — erasing the distinction between task and protected object implementations of a contract
  • Enhanced generics
  • Ada.Real_Time.Timing_Events and Ada.Dispatching.EDF for real-time
  • Ravenscar profile formalized (previously ISO/IEC TR 15942:2000)
  • Overriding keyword — Java-style @Override annotation, checked by the compiler

Ada 2012 (ISO/IEC 8652:2012)

The most significant revision since Ada 95. Headline features:

  • Contract-based programming via Ada 2012 aspects: Pre, Post, Type_Invariant, Dynamic_Predicate, Static_Predicate. These can be dynamically checked or statically proven via SPARK — the bridge to formal verification.
  • Expression functions — short functions as a single expression
  • Quantified expressions — for all X of Arr => X > 0
  • Conditional expressions — if and case as expressions
  • Enhanced container library
  • Multiprocessor dispatching domains

Published December 2012. The integration with SPARK 2014 that followed (using the same Ada 2012 aspects for contracts) was transformative — contracts moved from SPARK comments to first-class Ada.

Ada 2022 (ISO/IEC 8652:2023)

  • Parallel blocks and parallel loops — the Ada 2022 parallel for construct
  • Lightweight syntax for common patterns
  • Improved container library
  • Stable image attributes
  • Target name symbol @ for self-referencing assignments

Published May 2023. The work was led by Tucker Taft (Ada 2012–2022 lead designer), with Randall Brukardt as Ada Reference Manual editor. The standardization cadence is slow (a decade between revisions) and deliberate — which is exactly what the language’s customer base wants.
