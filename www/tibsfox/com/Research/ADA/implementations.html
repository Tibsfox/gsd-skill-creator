Ada tooling is unusually integrated because AdaCore’s business model rewards investment in tools, and because certification customers demand traceability from requirements to bits. A modern Ada developer in 2026 has the kind of toolchain that Rust developers were still building in 2020.

Part I — The GNAT Story

The Ada 9X Project and the birth of GNAT

By 1990, Ada 83 was eight years old, and the Ada 9X revision (which became Ada 95) was in progress. The DoD decided to fund a reference public-domain compiler for the new standard — something that had never been done for Ada 83. The contract went to New York University Courant Institute, led by three professors:

  • Robert Dewar — prolific compiler researcher, co-author of the SETL language, a force of nature in the Ada community and in compiler education.
  • Edmond Schonberg — compiler designer, SETL veteran.
  • Richard Kenner — GCC expert, one of the core GCC developers in the early 1990s who contributed the register allocator and many back-end improvements.

The project was called GNAT — originally GNU NYU Ada Translator, later simply a standalone name. The key technical decision: build GNAT on top of GCC’s RTL back end, reusing the existing code generator, optimizer, and target support. This meant GNAT inherited every processor GCC supported — which by 1994 was most of them.

The first GNAT release targeted Ada 83, then tracked the Ada 9X draft specs as they evolved. The first production Ada 95 GNAT release came in 1995, shortly after the Ada 95 standard was ratified. GNAT was the first validated Ada 95 compiler — it passed ACVC 2.1 ahead of any commercial Ada 95 product. That was a headline result because it turned a research compiler into a legitimate industrial tool overnight.

Ada Core Technologies / AdaCore

In 1994, the NYU team spun out a company to commercialize GNAT: Ada Core Technologies, later renamed AdaCore. The founders were Dewar, Schonberg, Kenner, and Bernard Banner. The model was unusual: GNAT would remain free and open source under GPL-with-runtime-exception, and AdaCore would sell support, qualified versions, and custom engineering.

This “dual model” predates the modern open-source-company playbook by more than a decade and was controversial in the 1990s Ada world, where the expectation was proprietary commercial compilers. The AdaCore model worked because safety-critical customers need support contracts regardless, and a compiler backed by a professional support organization is more valuable than a compiler you own forever with no one to call.

Why GNAT was essential for Ada’s survival

The 1990s were a dangerous decade for Ada. The DoD lifted the mandate in 1997, Java was exploding, and most commercial Ada vendors were struggling or disappearing. Without GNAT, Ada would have survived only as an expensive boutique language usable only by customers who could pay six-figure-per-seat commercial compilers.

GNAT changed the economics:

  • Free at the point of use — students, researchers, hobbyists could learn Ada.
  • Universal target support — GCC’s back end meant Ada ran on Linux, macOS, Windows, ARM, PowerPC, SPARC, MIPS, SH, AVR, and specialized targets like ERC32 (Sparc V7 for ESA spacecraft), LEON (open-source Sparc variant for ESA spacecraft), and embedded RTOS targets like VxWorks, QNX, INTEGRITY, LynxOS.
  • Portable — the same Ada source compiles on every supported target without changes.

Without GNAT, Ada would have become a memory of the 1980s. With GNAT, Ada became a modern, cross-platform, competitive systems language that happens to also satisfy the most stringent safety standards.

The death of Robert Dewar

Robert Dewar died on 30 June 2015, aged 70. His death was a turning point for AdaCore and for the Ada community. Dewar had been the public face of Ada for two decades — the teacher, the evangelist, the compiler writer, the curmudgeon who would argue for the language at any conference that would have him. After his death, leadership passed to a newer generation, with Cyrille Comar and Arnaud Charlet among the long-time technical leaders, and the company continued to grow. The transition was handled cleanly, but Ada’s cultural center of gravity shifted — Dewar was the language’s most effective advocate.

Part II — Other Ada Implementations

Green Hills AdaMULTI

Green Hills Software (Santa Barbara, CA) is a major vendor of embedded compilers and RTOS. Their Ada offering, AdaMULTI, targets VxWorks (Wind River) and INTEGRITY (Green Hills’s own RTOS, used in F-35, 787, and many defense platforms). Green Hills has emphasized absolutely certified tool chains — INTEGRITY-178 is DO-178C DAL A certified, and AdaMULTI has the matching compiler qualification. A smaller player than GNAT, but its customers — defense primes and avionics suppliers — are willing to pay for the certification story.

IBM Rational Ada / Rational Apex (historical)

Rational Software, founded in 1981 by Paul Levy and Mike Devlin, built the Rational Environment — a turn-key Ada development system that bundled compiler, IDE, configuration management, and a specialized Ada workstation (the R1000, a tagged-architecture machine purpose-built for Ada). The Rational Environment was extravagant — you bought a dedicated workstation — but pioneered ideas later popularized by IDEs: incremental compilation, semantic navigation, integrated version control.

In the 1990s Rational retreated from Ada hardware and released Rational Apex, a host-based Ada compiler for Unix workstations, used into the 2000s at Boeing, Lockheed, and other defense primes. IBM acquired Rational in 2003; the Ada products were gradually de-emphasized and discontinued through the 2010s. Grady Booch (later of UML fame) was a Rational VP — Ada’s influence on object-oriented design notation runs through Rational.

DEC Ada / Compaq Ada / HP Ada

Digital Equipment Corporation’s DEC Ada was a first-class compiler for VAX/VMS and Alpha/OpenVMS, notable for deep integration with the VMS runtime and debugger. Became Compaq Ada after Compaq acquired DEC in 1998, then HP Ada after HP acquired Compaq in 2002. Development essentially ceased by the mid-2000s.

Alsys Ada — Ichbiah’s own company

Alsys was Jean Ichbiah’s own company, founded in 1980 specifically to build Ada compilers. Alsys produced compilers for workstations, embedded targets, and mainframes, and was one of the dominant Ada vendors in Europe in the 1980s and early 1990s. Acquired by Thomson Software Products (1991), then Aonix (1996 merger), and the Ada line continued as Aonix ObjectAda. Aonix was acquired in 2010 by Parametric Technology Corporation (PTC) and the product continues as PTC ObjectAda with a small but loyal customer base. Ichbiah himself left Alsys in 1996 and spent his later career on predictive text input (T9), which became ubiquitous on early mobile phones. He died in 2007.

Janus/Ada (RR Software)

RR Software (Madison, WI), run by Randall Brukardt, produces Janus/Ada, a compact compiler targeting DOS, Windows, OS/2, Linux, and some embedded systems. Small footprint — the compiler runs in tight memory and produces compact code, which made it popular for some embedded projects. Supports Ada 83, Ada 95, and a subset of Ada 2005/2012. Randall Brukardt is also the editor of the Ada Reference Manual since Ada 95 and an important institutional figure in Ada standardization. Janus/Ada is still maintained and sold today as a niche product.

SofCheck AdaMagic

SofCheck (acquired by AdaCore in 2009) produced AdaMagic, an Ada front-end technology used inside several other compilers as the parser/semantic-analyzer. Tucker Taft was the technical lead; after the acquisition Taft became a Distinguished Engineer at AdaCore and continues leading Ada standardization.

Historical footnotes

Many Ada compilers existed in the 1980s. A partial list: Verdix VADS (acquired by Rational), TeleSoft TeleGen2 (absorbed by Alsys), Meridian Ada (absorbed by Alsys), ALS Prolog (Aonix), Arity Ada (IBM PC), Irvine Compiler Corp (acquired by LDRA), Tartan Ada, ADT Ada, CTA/Vista Ada. Most are extinct; some still have code in production somewhere.

Part III — GNAT Tooling Ecosystem

GNATstudio (formerly GPS)

GNATstudio, formerly GPS (GNAT Programming Studio), is AdaCore’s flagship IDE. Built on Python bindings and GTK. Provides project management, syntax highlighting, code navigation, debugger integration, build automation, version control integration, and direct hooks into the entire GNAT suite (GNATprove, CodePeer, GNATcoverage, GNATcheck). GNATstudio is written mostly in Ada itself — a characteristic AdaCore flourish. As of the mid-2020s, AdaCore has shifted toward supporting VS Code as the primary editing environment for new users.

GNATprove — the SPARK proof tool

See the SPARK page for the full pipeline (Why3 → SMT solvers). Key command-line flags:

  • --mode=flow — flow analysis only (fast, pre-proof check)
  • --mode=prove — full proof pipeline
  • --mode=all — flow + proof
  • --level=0..4 — effort level for the solver
  • --prover=cvc5,z3,alt-ergo — which solvers to use
  • --counterexamples=on — attempt to produce counterexamples on failed VCs

CodePeer — static analysis

CodePeer is AdaCore’s static analysis tool for Ada, distinct from SPARK. Where SPARK demands contracts and proves from them, CodePeer is a bug-finding tool that runs over ordinary Ada code with no annotations and reports suspicious patterns: possible overflows, possible uninitialized reads, possible null dereferences, dead branches, redundant conditions. Analogous to Coverity, Polyspace, or Clang Static Analyzer for C/C++.

Implemented as an abstract interpretation analyzer. False-positive rate is bounded but non-zero. Qualified for use in DO-178C projects. The typical workflow: run CodePeer on legacy Ada code to get a first pass of safety findings, fix the ones that matter, then transition the highest-criticality modules to SPARK for full proof.

GNATcheck, GNATcoverage, GNATtest

  • GNATcheck — enforces coding standards (Ada Quality and Style Guide, MISRA-like Ada rules, HIS metrics for automotive).
  • GNATcoverage — measures structural code coverage (statement, decision, MC/DC — the three coverage criteria referenced by DO-178C for DAL C, B, and A). Emulator-based trace analyzer: runs the program under an instruction-set simulator and computes coverage against the original Ada source. The simulator-based approach is important for embedded avionics — you can measure MC/DC on the actual object code without instrumenting the production binary.
  • GNATtest — unit test framework generator. Generates test harnesses with stub procedures.

GNATcoll and GPRbuild

GNATcoll is AdaCore’s foundational library: Ada bindings to SQLite, PostgreSQL, JSON, XML, traces, filesystem operations, process management, project handling. The practical “stdlib++” for Ada that fills in what the Ada standard library does not provide (the standard library is deliberately minimal).

GPRbuild is the project build tool, reading .gpr (GNAT Project) files. Handles the dependency graph, incremental compilation, cross-compilation toolchain selection, and library construction. A typical .gpr file:

project Hello is
   for Languages use ("Ada");
   for Source_Dirs use ("src");
   for Object_Dir use "obj";
   for Main use ("main.adb");

   package Compiler is
      for Default_Switches ("Ada") use
        ("-O2", "-gnatwa", "-gnatVa", "-gnato", "-fstack-check");
   end Compiler;
end Hello;

The flags are characteristic Ada paranoia: -gnatwa all warnings, -gnatVa all validity checks, -gnato overflow checks, -fstack-check stack overflow checks.

Part IV — Alire, the Modern Package Manager

Alire (command name alr) is the modern Ada package manager, first released in 2018 and maturing rapidly. It does for Ada what Cargo does for Rust and npm does for JavaScript: dependency resolution, binary and source distribution of libraries, project scaffolding, toolchain management. Alire is the single most important change to the Ada developer experience in a decade.

Representative workflow:

$ alr init --bin my_project
$ cd my_project
$ alr with aws          # add Ada Web Server dependency
$ alr with gnatcoll     # add GNATcoll
$ alr build
$ alr run

Alire ships its own copy of a compatible GNAT toolchain (via alr toolchain), which means a new user can install alr, run alr init, and have a working Ada build without ever manually installing a compiler. This is the first time in Ada’s history that the onboarding experience approached the low friction of modern language package managers.

The Alire community index (alire.ada.dev) has around a thousand crates (Ada packages), including bindings to SDL, OpenGL, Postgres, SQLite, HTTP, WebSocket, JSON, YAML, cryptography, plus native Ada libraries for numerics, testing, containers.

Part V — Language Server and Modern Tooling

ada_language_server

AdaCore implements the Language Server Protocol for Ada in the ada_language_server project (open source). It provides hover for symbols, go-to-definition and find-references, completion, diagnostics from the compiler in real time, refactoring (rename, extract), call hierarchy, type hierarchy.

This was transformative for Ada’s developer experience. Before ada_language_server, serious Ada development meant using GNATstudio or GNATbench (the Eclipse plugin), both of which felt unlike modern editor experiences. After ada_language_server, any editor that speaks LSP — VS Code, Emacs with lsp-mode or eglot, Neovim with built-in LSP, Sublime, Helix, Zed, JetBrains via plugin — can host a first-class Ada editing experience.

VS Code extension

AdaCore ships an official VS Code extension (AdaCore.ada) that bundles ada_language_server, a GNAT debugger integration, syntax highlighting, and Alire workflow support. It is the recommended default for new Ada users on all platforms as of 2024–2025.

Emacs ada-mode

ada-mode for Emacs is long-established. The current generation (Stephe Leake’s) is sophisticated, with its own incremental parser (wisi), semantic indentation, and navigation. Predates ada_language_server and remains popular with Emacs users.

GitHub Actions

- uses: alire-project/setup-alire@v2
- run: alr build
- run: alr exec gnatprove -- -P my.gpr --level=2

Boring, modern, works-like-everyone-else-expects setup. For a long time Ada CI meant homegrown Docker images with hand-installed GNAT. Alire fixed that.

Debuggers

GNAT integrates with GDB via the GNAT-patched GDB (upstream GDB also has Ada mode). GNATstudio and VS Code both wrap GDB for graphical debugging. For embedded, GDB over OpenOCD, ST-Link, or JLink is standard.

Part VI — learn.adacore.com

learn.adacore.com is AdaCore’s free online learning platform. Interactive Ada and SPARK courses, runnable in the browser via a remote GNAT back end, with exercises graded against GNAT and GNATprove. Courses include:

  • Introduction to Ada — full textbook-style course for programmers from other languages
  • Introduction to SPARK — parallel course for SPARK
  • Ada for the Embedded C Developer — the classic “I know C, how do I switch?” track
  • Introduction to Embedded Systems Programming — Ada on STM32 (Cortex-M4) via the Ada Drivers Library

In the author’s opinion, learn.adacore.com is the single best onboarding resource the Ada community has ever had. It is what the Rust community built from scratch; Ada already had it.

Ada Drivers Library

An open-source collection of embedded hardware abstractions and peripheral drivers for Cortex-M and some RISC-V boards. Targets STM32F4/F7, STM32L4, nRF52, Microbit, Arduino Nano, with clean Ada APIs over GPIO, SPI, I2C, UART, DMA, Timers, ADC, USB, and display drivers. The base on which a lot of Ada embedded demos and CubeSat work rests.

Part VII — Performance and Code Generation

Modern GNAT generates code that is competitive with C and C++ on most workloads. It’s the same GCC back end, after all. The differences:

  • Runtime checks are on by default. Range checks, array bounds, null checks, discriminant checks. These cost a few percent in most cases, but they catch real bugs. For the absolute last bit of performance, pragma Suppress disables them for specific checks in specific scopes.
  • Tasking runtime (GNARL) is heavier than a bare C runtime. For embedded targets, the Ravenscar profile gives you a stripped-down runtime that fits in tight ROM.
  • Exception handling has some overhead for setup, but zero-cost (table-based) unwinding on x86-64 means the non-exceptional path pays nothing.
  • Generic instantiation generates separate code for each instantiation (unlike Java erasure). This can increase code size but improves performance.

For numerical code, Ada is often comparable to C or Fortran because it rides the same GCC optimizer. For embedded code, Ada with the right pragmas generates code indistinguishable from hand-written C. For concurrent code, Ada is frequently faster than library-based alternatives because the compiler understands the synchronization and can optimize it.

Part VIII — The Community and the Future

AdaCore’s Rust play

AdaCore itself has a Rust team now. AdaCore’s strategy is to offer one toolchain vendor for both Ada/SPARK and Rust, so customers building new systems with a mix of languages can buy support from one place. AdaCore has had a commercial relationship with Ferrous Systems (Ferrocene) around this since 2021. See the Rosetta page for the Ada-vs-Rust conversation.

Standards work continues

Ada standardization continues under ISO/IEC JTC1/SC22/WG9, the ISO working group for Ada. The ARG (Ada Rapporteur Group) produces ARs (Ada Issues) that accumulate into language revisions. Tucker Taft has been the technical force behind Ada 2012 and Ada 2022; Randall Brukardt has been the ARM editor; Pascal Leroy chaired the ARG for years. The standardization cadence is slow (a decade between revisions) and deliberate — which is exactly what the language’s customer base wants.

Why Ada is having a quiet revival in the 2020s

  1. The security moment. Memory-safety bugs cause ~70% of security vulnerabilities in C/C++ code. Federal mandates now actively recommend Ada alongside Rust, Go, Swift, Java, and C#.
  2. Rust’s success legitimized the category. The conversation about safe systems languages is mainstream again.
  3. Regulatory pressure — EU Cyber Resilience Act (2024), US secure-by-design mandates, CISA pledge, EO 14028. All reward languages with stronger static guarantees.
  4. SPARK’s maturity — Ten years ago, running GNATprove on non-trivial code was a research project. Today, the Bronze and Silver levels are achievable in weeks of part-time effort.
  5. Forty years of fielded code does not go away. The A320 has flown since 1988. Paris Métro Line 14 since 1998. The C130J since 1999. iFACTS since 2011. Canadarm2 is still on the ISS. That code will keep running for decades; maintainers will not rewrite it; every new variant ships more Ada, not less.

The cohort of Ada experts is thinning — many engineers who learned Ada during the DoD mandate era are now in their sixties. The answer — Alire, learn.adacore.com, the VS Code extension, ada_language_server, r/ada — is a deliberate investment in making Ada approachable for the next generation. It appears to be working, slowly; Alire’s crate count, download numbers, and r/ada activity are all trending upward.

Getting started

# Linux / macOS
curl -L https://github.com/alire-project/alire/releases/latest/download/alr-linux.zip -o alr.zip
unzip alr.zip
./alr init --bin hello
cd hello
./alr build
./alr run

That’s it. You have a working Ada compiler, project manager, and build system, all installed from a single binary. In 2026 this is no different from cargo new, but in 2018 it would have seemed miraculous to an Ada developer.
