# Cleanup Technology

## The Ocean Cleanup -- Organization

The Ocean Cleanup was founded in 2013 by Dutch inventor **Boyan Slat**, who was 18 years old at the time. The organization is a **nonprofit** with approximately 145 staff, headquartered in Rotterdam, Netherlands. Their stated goal: **remove 90% of floating ocean plastic by 2040**.

The organization's approach is notable for its engineering-first philosophy. Rather than relying on volunteer beach cleanups or awareness campaigns, The Ocean Cleanup has invested in designing, building, and iterating purpose-built mechanical systems for large-scale ocean plastic extraction. Their development history spans four major system iterations over seven years, each incorporating lessons from the previous version's failures and successes.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup; Wikipedia.

## System Evolution

### System 001 (2018) -- The Failed First Attempt

The first experimental system was deployed in September 2018. Key characteristics:

- **Passive propulsion** -- designed to drift with ocean currents, using natural wind and wave forces to sweep through the garbage patch
- 600-meter floating barrier with a 3-meter-deep skirt to capture plastic below the waterline
- No active retention mechanism -- relied on the speed differential between the system and plastic to create a collection effect
- Cost: approximately $20 million in development

**System 001 failed.** The passive design did not effectively capture plastic because the system and the plastic moved at nearly the same speed -- there was insufficient relative velocity to force plastic into the collection area. Additionally, the barrier experienced structural failures: a fatigue fracture in December 2018 broke off a 60-foot section, requiring the system to be towed to Hawai'i for repairs.

However, the failure provided critical engineering lessons:

- Passive systems cannot generate enough sweep speed in the low-current gyre center
- Structural design must account for the constant fatigue loading of open-ocean conditions
- Retention mechanisms are essential -- plastic must be actively prevented from escaping

Source: The Ocean Cleanup milestones; Wikipedia.

### System 001/B (2019) -- Proof of Concept

A modified version deployed in 2019 validated the core concept and captured the first ocean plastic:

- Added a parachute anchor to create speed differential between the system and surrounding water
- Smaller scale than 001, focused on demonstrating capture rather than volume extraction
- Successfully collected plastic debris, proving the fundamental concept was sound
- Captured plastic was recycled into **The Ocean Cleanup Sunglasses** -- the organization's first consumer product, which **sold out within 18 months**

The sunglasses served dual purposes: generating revenue to fund continued operations and demonstrating that ocean plastic could be recycled into viable consumer products.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup milestones.

### System 002 "Jenny" (2021-2023) -- First Full-Scale Success

Named after Slat's grandmother's rowing boat, System 002 represented the shift to active propulsion and the first full-scale operational system:

- **Active propulsion** -- towed by two ships at approximately 1.5 knots, creating reliable sweep speed independent of ambient currents
- Approximately 800-meter wingspan
- Deployed in the GPGP from August 2021 through 2023
- **Extracted 282,787 kg total** from the GPGP over 8,352 square kilometers of swept area
- **Record single extraction: 31,816 kg** in one haul
- Proved that consistent, large-scale extraction from the open ocean was technically feasible

System 002 was the breakthrough. It demonstrated that a purpose-built vessel towing a floating barrier could reliably collect significant quantities of plastic from the garbage patch. The shift from passive to active propulsion solved the fundamental speed-differential problem that doomed System 001.

Key operational insights from System 002:

- The retention zone (where plastic accumulates before extraction) needed to be emptied regularly to prevent overflow
- GPS tracking and satellite imagery could guide the system toward areas of higher debris density
- Crew safety during extraction operations in open ocean conditions required specialized procedures
- Two vessels were needed for towing -- one on each end of the barrier

Source: The Ocean Cleanup milestones; Wikipedia; multiple reporting sources.

### System 03 (August 2023-Present) -- Current Operational System

System 03 is the current flagship cleanup system and represents a major scale-up from System 002:

**Physical Specifications:**
- Wing length approximately **2.2 kilometers** (3 times the wingspan of System 002)
- Extended screen reaches **4 meters below the surface** to capture subsurface plastic (up from 3 meters on System 002)
- Towed by two vessels in a U-shaped configuration
- Retention zone at the apex of the U, emptied every approximately **4 days**
- Can clean an area equivalent to **a football field every five seconds**

**AI-Guided Targeting:**
- System 03 integrates **computational modeling** to predict plastic hotspot locations within the GPGP
- Historical satellite data, drift modeling, and oceanographic current data are combined to identify areas of highest debris concentration
- This represents a shift from uniform sweeping to **targeted extraction** -- the system is guided to where the plastic is densest, dramatically improving extraction efficiency per unit of fuel and time

**Extraction Performance:**
- Extracted approximately **500,000 kg** from the GPGP by August 2025
- Individual catches sometimes exceed **11,000 kg**
- Extraction rate significantly higher than System 002 due to both larger size and smarter targeting

**2025 Operations:**
- In 2025, extraction operations are **on hiatus** for a "hotspot hunting" initiative
- Purpose: map high-density plastic concentrations throughout the GPGP using survey vessels and remote sensing
- The data collected will make future System 03 deployments significantly more efficient by pre-identifying the richest extraction zones
- This represents a strategic investment: pausing extraction now to extract more efficiently later

Source: The Ocean Cleanup milestones; Wikipedia; multiple reporting sources.

## Scaling Plan -- Ten Systems, One Decade, $7.5 Billion

In September 2024, The Ocean Cleanup declared that the **GPGP could be cleaned within a decade for $7.5 billion**. The scaling plan envisions:

- A fleet of approximately **10 System 03-scale units** operating simultaneously
- Continuous extraction operations across the full extent of the GPGP
- Coordinated deployment guided by the hotspot mapping data being collected in 2025
- Progressive reduction in patch density, with extraction efficiency decreasing as the easiest-to-reach plastic is removed first

### Funding

In 2026, The Ocean Cleanup received **$121 million from The Audacious Project** -- described as their largest single donation -- for their 30 Cities Program (river interception expansion). Major prior funders include:

- Marc Benioff (Salesforce CEO)
- Peter Thiel
- Julius Baer Foundation
- Maersk (shipping company -- provided vessels for System 002/03 operations)
- Recycled product sales revenue

The $7.5 billion total cost estimate is substantial but not unprecedented in the context of environmental remediation. For context:

- Apple's annual revenue exceeds $7.5 billion in less than one day
- Global military spending exceeds $2 trillion annually
- The global plastic packaging market alone is worth approximately $350 billion per year
- US Superfund toxic waste site cleanup has cost over $50 billion since 1980

The cost is not the barrier. The barrier is political will and coordination.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup press releases; Wikipedia.

## River Interceptor Systems

### Program Overview

The Ocean Cleanup operates **Interceptor systems in 9 countries** with **20 deployments as of May 2025**. These systems are designed to capture plastic in rivers before it reaches the ocean -- addressing inflow at the source rather than cleaning up accumulation at the destination.

Interceptors are:

- **Solar-powered** and largely automated
- Positioned at strategic choke points in rivers where debris concentrates
- Capable of operating continuously with minimal human oversight
- Designed to handle both normal flow conditions and flood events

The target is to address the **1,000 most polluting rivers**, which account for approximately **80% of river-sourced ocean plastic inflow**. If even a fraction of these rivers can be intercepted effectively, the annual addition of plastic to ocean gyres drops dramatically.

### Notable Deployments

**Guatemala -- Rio Las Vacas (Interceptor 006):**
- One of the most productive deployments in the program
- Captured **1.5 million+ kg** of debris by June 2024
- During peak flow events, captured **89 truckloads in a single day**
- Demonstrates the enormous volume of plastic flowing through rivers in developing countries with limited waste management infrastructure

**Indonesia:**
- Deployments in Jakarta and on the Cisadane River
- Indonesia is the second-largest contributor to ocean plastic globally
- Interceptors positioned on rivers feeding into the Java Sea

**Malaysia -- Klang River:**
- Deployment near Port Klang, one of the busiest shipping areas in Southeast Asia
- Captures both household waste and industrial plastic debris

### The Interceptor Barricade

Deployed in 2023, the **Interceptor Barricade** is the first high-throughput river system design. Unlike earlier Interceptors that operated as point collection systems, the Barricade spans the full width of a river with a floating barrier that funnels debris to collection points.

During heavy rainfall in April 2024, a barricade deployment captured **1.4 million kg of waste in hours** -- demonstrating the extreme volumes of plastic mobilized during flood events. This is critical because a disproportionate amount of annual river plastic transport occurs during high-flow events (monsoons, tropical storms, spring floods).

### Cumulative Results

- **Total cumulative extraction across all operations:** 30 million kg by June 2025
- **20 million kg milestone** reached December 2024
- River interception accounts for the majority of total extraction volume -- river plastic is far more concentrated and accessible than dispersed open-ocean debris

Source: The Ocean Cleanup milestones and river program data.

## Alternative Cleanup Technologies

### Seabin Project

**Origin:** Australia
**Application:** Coastal and harbor cleanup -- marinas, ports, yacht clubs, calm-water areas
**Mechanism:** Combines a pool-skimmer principle with a submerged collection bin. Water flows over the lip of the bin, carrying floating debris with it. A pump creates suction that holds the debris in the bin while clean water passes through.

**Performance data:**
- Collected **32.9 metric tons** in Sydney in 2022
- Collected **3.34 metric tons** in Los Angeles in 2022
- Estimated cost: **$1.24-1.55/kg** of plastic removed
- Requires approximately **500 units** to keep a 25,000 square meter marina clean

**Limitations:** Seabins only work in protected waters (marinas, harbors) -- they cannot survive open-ocean conditions. Their contribution to the overall plastic problem is limited to coastal cleanup, but they serve a valuable role in preventing land-based plastic from reaching the open ocean.

Source: Seabin Project; PMC cleanup technology review.

### Enzymatic Degradation

In 2016, Japanese researchers discovered **Ideonella sakaiensis**, a bacterium that produces an enzyme (PETase) capable of breaking down **PET plastic** (polyethylene terephthalate, used in water bottles and packaging). Subsequent research has:

- Optimized the enzyme for faster degradation rates
- Engineered variants that operate at higher temperatures
- Demonstrated complete breakdown of PET into its chemical building blocks (terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol), which can theoretically be repolymerized into new PET

Separately, the fungus **Aspergillus tubingensis** has been shown to degrade **polyurethane** -- a plastic type used in foam insulation, coatings, and adhesives.

**Current status:** Both approaches are still at **research/laboratory stage** for ocean-scale application. The challenge is scaling biological degradation to handle the millions of tons of plastic in the ocean. Enzyme stability in saltwater, delivery mechanisms, and potential ecological impacts of releasing engineered microorganisms into the ocean are all unresolved questions.

Source: The Ocean Movement innovation review.

### Magnetic Nanocoils

An experimental nanotechnology approach using **magnetic coils at the nanoscale** to target microplastics:

- Nanocoils are designed to attach to microplastic particles in water
- An applied magnetic field then collects the nanocoil-plastic aggregates
- The approach theoretically works without harming marine life because the magnetic field selectively attracts only the nanocoil-tagged particles
- Still in **laboratory/early testing phase**
- If proven viable at scale, this would be the first technology capable of removing microplastics from open water

Source: The Ocean Movement.

### NASA CYGNSS Satellite Detection

Since 2016, NASA's **Cyclone Global Navigation Satellite System (CYGNSS)** has demonstrated the ability to **detect ocean plastic concentrations from orbit**. The system, originally designed to measure ocean surface winds during hurricanes, produces radar backscatter signals that are altered by the presence of plastic debris at the ocean surface.

Applications for cleanup:

- Large-scale mapping of plastic distribution across entire ocean basins
- Near-real-time detection of high-density plastic aggregations
- Guidance data for directing cleanup vessels to productive extraction zones
- Long-term monitoring of patch density changes to measure cleanup effectiveness

Source: The Ocean Movement; NASA.

### Plastics-to-Fuel and Recycling Technologies

- **Licella Holdings** (Australia) has developed catalytic hydrothermal liquefaction technology to convert plastic waste into oil that can be re-refined or used as fuel
- **PlasticRoad** created a bicycle path in Zwolle, Netherlands, using **70% recycled plastic** -- demonstrating that ocean and waste plastic can be converted into durable infrastructure materials
- **Catalytic pyrolysis** can convert degraded polyethylene and polypropylene into liquid fuels and chemical feedstocks

Source: Frontiers in Marine Science review, 2025.

## Environmental Impact of Cleanup Operations

### Net Environmental Benefit Assessment

A 2025 peer-reviewed study published in **Scientific Reports** by Egger et al. developed the **first comprehensive framework** to assess whether GPGP cleanup delivers a net environmental benefit. This is a critical question: cleanup operations themselves have environmental costs (fuel consumption, CO2 emissions, vessel noise, potential bycatch), and any rigorous assessment must weigh these costs against the benefits of plastic removal.

The study assessed vulnerability of **nine marine ecological guilds** to three stressors on a 1-3 scale:

| Stressor | Average Vulnerability Score |
|----------|---------------------------|
| Macroplastics | **2.3** |
| Microplastics | **1.9** |
| Cleanup activities | **1.8** |

**Key findings:**

- Vulnerability to macroplastics (2.3) exceeds vulnerability to cleanup activities (1.8) for all guilds assessed -- meaning the plastic itself causes more harm than the process of removing it
- An **80% cleanup** of the GPGP could reduce macroplastic concentrations to within **safe levels for marine mammals and sea turtles**
- Marine mammals and sea turtles benefit most from cleanup due to their high vulnerability to entanglement
- Seabirds benefit from reduced ingestion risk but are less directly affected by cleanup operations

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025.

### Carbon Accounting

The study also performed a **carbon trade-off analysis** comparing the greenhouse gas emissions of cleanup operations against the climate impact of leaving plastic in the ocean:

- **Cleanup GHG emissions:** 0.4-2.9 million metric tons CO2 equivalent over the full multi-year cleanup campaign
- **Microplastic impact on ocean carbon export:** 15-30 million metric tons of carbon per year (ongoing, cumulative)
- The cleanup emissions are a **one-time cost** incurred over the decade of active operations
- The microplastic disruption to the biological carbon pump is an **annual, ongoing cost** that accumulates every year cleanup is delayed

Conclusion: the carbon cost of cleanup is **"significantly lower"** than the ongoing carbon cost of leaving the plastic in place. Even accounting for vessel fuel consumption, manufacturing of cleanup systems, and logistics, the net carbon balance strongly favors cleanup.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025; Plymouth Marine Laboratory summary.

### Neuston and Bycatch Concerns

Scientific criticism of ocean cleanup has focused on potential impacts to the neuston community -- organisms living at the ocean surface including:

- Portuguese man-of-war
- Sea snails (Janthina spp.)
- Sail jellyfish (Velella velella)
- Blue sea dragons (Glaucus atlanticus)
- Various larval fish and invertebrates

System 03 addresses these concerns through:

- **Deterrent devices** to guide neuston organisms away from the collection path
- **Cameras** for real-time monitoring of bycatch
- **Escape aids** built into the barrier design allowing small organisms to pass through or under
- **Independent observers** deployed on cleanup vessels to document and report all wildlife interactions

Monitoring data from System 03 operations has confirmed **only minimal environmental effects** on neuston communities. However, scaling to a 10-system fleet raises legitimate questions about cumulative disturbance across a wider area and longer timeframe that have not yet been studied.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup environmental monitoring; Wikipedia.

### Cost-Per-Kilogram Comparison

A comparative analysis of cleanup technologies by cost effectiveness:

| Technology | Cost per kg | Context |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Seabin | $1.24-1.55 | Coastal/harbor only; calm water |
| Manual island cleanup | $8.83 | Seychelles case study; labor-intensive |
| Ocean booms (System 03 type) | $22.5-30.1 | Open ocean; high operational cost |

All costs assume plastic constitutes 80-90% of catches. The cost differential between coastal and open-ocean cleanup is approximately 15-20x, reflecting the enormous logistical challenge of operating in the middle of the Pacific Ocean versus a protected harbor.

Source: PMC cleanup technology review, Falk-Andersson et al.
