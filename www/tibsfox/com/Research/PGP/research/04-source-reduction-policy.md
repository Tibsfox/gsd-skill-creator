# Source Reduction & Policy

## The Governance Challenge

Ocean plastic pollution occupies a uniquely difficult governance position. The Great Pacific Garbage Patch sits in international waters -- no single nation has jurisdiction, no single government has authority, and no existing international framework was designed to address this specific problem. The plastic originates from dozens of countries, crosses multiple national exclusive economic zones, and accumulates in areas governed only by the vague provisions of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).

This governance vacuum means that addressing the problem requires either a new international instrument (which has been attempted and, as of early 2026, has failed) or a patchwork of national and regional measures that may or may not add up to sufficient action. This module documents both approaches.

## UN Global Plastics Treaty Timeline

### The Mandate (2022)

**March 2022 -- UNEA 5.2 (Nairobi):** The United Nations Environment Assembly, at its fifth session, adopted a historic resolution. **175 nations** agreed to a mandate for the first **legally binding international instrument** to end plastic pollution. An Intergovernmental Negotiating Committee (INC) was established to develop the treaty text. The resolution was described by UNEP Executive Director as "the most significant environmental multilateral deal since the Paris Agreement."

The mandate was deliberately broad: the instrument should address the **full lifecycle of plastics** -- from production through use, waste management, and environmental remediation. This breadth became the source of the fundamental disagreement that would stall negotiations for the next four years.

Source: UNEP; World Economic Forum.

### INC-1: Initial Positions (December 2022, Uruguay)

The first session of the Intergovernmental Negotiating Committee convened in Punta del Este, Uruguay. Initial dialogues revealed a fundamental divergence that would define all subsequent negotiations:

- **Upstream approach:** Focus on reducing plastic production volumes and eliminating problematic plastic types at source. Championed by environmentally ambitious nations and small island states whose coastlines bear the brunt of marine debris
- **Downstream approach:** Focus on improving waste management, recycling infrastructure, and cleanup operations without constraining production. Championed by petrochemical-producing nations whose economies depend on plastic feedstock sales

This upstream-versus-downstream divide is not merely technical. It reflects a fundamental conflict between nations whose economic interests are harmed by plastic pollution (fishing nations, tourism-dependent island states, coastal communities) and nations whose economic interests are served by continued plastic production (oil-producing states whose petrochemical industries convert fossil fuel byproducts into plastic).

Source: UNEP INC proceedings; Nature.

### INC-2: The Zero Draft (June 2023, Paris)

The second session in Paris produced a significant milestone: **134 governments** called for global rules across the **full plastics lifecycle**, and a **zero draft mandate** was secured. This meant negotiators would work from a comprehensive text addressing production, consumption, and waste management, rather than limiting the scope to downstream measures only.

Key developments at INC-2:

- Coalition building intensified, with the High-Ambition Coalition (HAC) and the petrochemical bloc solidifying their positions
- Scientific evidence submissions documented the scale of the problem (914 species affected, 100,000 marine mammal deaths annually, microplastics in human blood)
- Civil society organizations and the plastics industry both increased lobbying presence
- The question of financial mechanisms for developing countries began to emerge as a major issue

Source: UNEP; Global Plastic Laws; CNN.

### INC-3: Procedural Delays (November 2023, Nairobi)

The third session, held at UNEP headquarters in Nairobi, was dominated by **procedural delays**. Despite the urgency of the environmental crisis, several delegations used procedural mechanisms to slow progress:

- Additional text was drafted, expanding the negotiating document
- Fundamental disagreements over scope and ambition remained unresolved
- Contact groups were established to address specific issues, but made limited progress
- The session ended without substantive advances on the core upstream-downstream dispute

By INC-3, the pattern was clear: the petrochemical bloc was using procedural tools to run out the clock, hoping to weaken or delay any binding obligations on production volumes.

Source: UNEP; Nature.

### INC-4: Industry Lobbying Escalates (April 2024, Ottawa)

The fourth session in Ottawa, Canada, saw increased acknowledgment of the **health harms** of plastic pollution, including:

- Microplastics detected in human blood, placentas, and breast milk
- Endocrine-disrupting chemicals leaching from common plastic products
- Occupational health risks in plastic production facilities

However, INC-4 also saw the **intensification of industry lobbying**. The petrochemical industry and its trade associations sent the largest non-governmental delegation to any INC session. Key developments:

- Countries agreed to **exclude upstream measures from intersessional work** -- a significant procedural victory for the petrochemical bloc that removed production caps from the active negotiating track between sessions
- The financial mechanism discussion advanced, with developing countries insisting on dedicated funding for waste management infrastructure
- Several countries that had previously supported comprehensive lifecycle regulation softened their positions under industry pressure

Source: UNEP; Global Plastic Laws; World Economic Forum.

### INC-5.1: Collapse in Busan (December 2024, South Korea)

The fifth session, Part 1, was meant to be the final negotiating session. It was not.

**Ten days of negotiations** in Busan, South Korea, ended without agreement:

- The INC Chair issued a **Draft Text** that attempted to bridge the upstream-downstream divide
- The draft included compromise language on production reduction targets and financial mechanisms
- **Insufficient support** -- neither the ambition bloc nor the petrochemical bloc found the text acceptable
- The Chair's text was seen as too weak by environmental advocates (no binding production caps) and too strong by the petrochemical bloc (any mention of production reduction)

Talks collapsed. The session ended with an agreement to reconvene, but no agreed text.

Source: UNEP; CNN; Nature.

### INC-5.2: Geneva Failure (August 2025)

The reconvened fifth session in Geneva was the most intensive negotiating effort in the treaty process:

- **Ten days of negotiations** with **2,600+ participants** from **183 countries**
- **Over 100 countries** called for **binding production caps** on virgin plastic
- **Saudi Arabia, Russia, and the United States** pushed for a focus on recycling and waste management only -- no binding constraints on production
- Multiple contact groups worked in parallel on different sections of the treaty text
- Late-night negotiating sessions extended well past midnight on multiple days
- **Talks ended without agreement**

The former INC Chair **resigned** following the collapse, citing frustration with the inability of the process to overcome opposition from a small number of petrochemical-producing nations.

The failure at INC-5.2 is significant because it exposed the structural weakness of the UN consensus-based negotiating framework when applied to a problem that directly threatens the revenue model of some of the world's wealthiest and most politically connected industries. The petrochemical bloc did not need to win a vote -- it only needed to prevent consensus.

Source: UNEP; Global Plastic Laws; World Economic Forum; CNN; Nature.

### INC-5.3: Administrative Reset (February 2026)

A one-day session convened for **administrative purposes only**:

- A **new Chair** was elected to replace the resigned predecessor
- No substantive negotiations took place
- The path forward for the treaty remains unclear

As of March 2026, there is no legally binding international instrument to address plastic pollution. The treaty process continues in theory, but the fundamental political obstacles remain unchanged.

Source: UNEP; Global Plastic Laws.

## Key Political Positions

### High-Ambition Coalition (HAC)

**Members:** Norway, Rwanda (co-chairs), European Union, many African nations, small island developing states (SIDS), Latin American coastal states, New Zealand, and others.

**Position:**
- Full lifecycle regulation including **binding production caps** on virgin plastic
- Phase-out of problematic and avoidable plastic types
- Elimination of specific chemicals of concern (phthalates, PFAS, flame retardants) from plastic products
- Mandatory Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) schemes globally
- **Dedicated financial mechanism** for low- and middle-income countries to build waste management infrastructure
- Transparency requirements for plastic production volumes and chemical additives

The HAC represents nations that bear disproportionate costs of plastic pollution -- island states whose beaches are buried in foreign plastic, fishing nations whose catches are contaminated, and nations with limited waste management infrastructure that receive waste exports from wealthier countries.

Source: UNEP; Global Plastic Laws.

### Petrochemical Bloc

**Key members:** United States, Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iran, and some Asian nations with large petrochemical industries.

**Position:**
- Focus on **downstream waste management** and recycling infrastructure
- **National action plans** rather than binding international obligations
- No constraints on production volumes -- market forces should determine output
- Voluntary rather than mandatory measures for industry
- Opposition to financial mechanisms that could be seen as transfers to competing economies

The petrochemical bloc's position is rooted in economics: plastic production consumes approximately 12% of global oil and gas output. Production caps would directly reduce demand for petrochemical feedstocks, threatening a major revenue stream for oil-producing nations. The American Chemistry Council and similar industry associations have spent hundreds of millions of dollars lobbying against binding production regulations.

Source: UNEP; Nature; World Economic Forum.

### Production Scale Context

The scale of global plastic production provides essential context for the treaty debate:

- Over **460 million metric tons** of plastic produced annually (2024)
- Less than **10%** recycled globally
- Plastic production accounts for approximately **5% (2.24 Gt CO2e) of global greenhouse gas emissions** -- nearly **twice aviation's carbon footprint**
- Without policy intervention, plastic waste generation is projected to reach **1.7 billion metric tons by 2060** (OECD estimate)
- The plastic production growth rate has outpaced nearly every other material class for the past 50 years

The disconnect between production volumes (460 million+ tons/year) and recycling capacity (<10%) means that even optimistic recycling improvements cannot keep pace with production growth. Source reduction -- producing less plastic in the first place -- is the only pathway that changes the arithmetic of ocean plastic accumulation.

Source: OECD; Nature; World Economic Forum.

## National and Regional Policy Measures

### European Union

**EU Directive 2019/904** (Single-Use Plastics Directive):

- Phase-out of specific single-use plastic products including cutlery, plates, straws, cotton bud sticks, and expanded polystyrene food containers
- **25-30% recycled content targets** for plastic bottles by 2025/2030
- Separate collection targets: 77% of plastic bottles by 2025, 90% by 2029
- Extended Producer Responsibility for packaging, fishing gear, and other categories
- Mandatory labeling for plastic-containing products (wet wipes, sanitary pads, tobacco filters)

The EU directive is the most comprehensive regional plastic regulation in force. It has been adopted or adapted by the EU27 member states and has influenced legislation in non-EU European countries, the UK, and several Asian nations.

Source: European Commission; Frontiers review.

### China

**China's 2025 Phase-Out Plan:**

- Staged phase-out of certain single-use plastics including non-degradable bags (major cities by 2022, all cities by 2025)
- Ban on single-use polystyrene tableware in catering by 2025
- Promotion of biodegradable alternatives
- **State Oceanic Administration Marine Litter and Microplastics Research Center** established in 2017 to coordinate marine debris research and monitoring
- Significant investment in domestic waste management infrastructure

China's position is complex: the country is both a major plastic producer and a major victim of marine plastic pollution. China's ban on importing foreign plastic waste (the "National Sword" policy, 2018) disrupted global waste trading patterns and forced many exporting nations to confront their own waste management deficiencies.

Source: NOAA; Frontiers review.

### United States

**Save Our Seas 2.0 Act (2020):**

- Authorized expansion of the **NOAA Marine Debris Program**
- Established a Marine Debris Foundation for public-private partnerships
- Created a federal interagency marine debris coordinating committee
- Authorized research on innovative cleanup technologies
- Did **not** include production caps or mandatory reductions in plastic use

**Microbead-Free Waters Act (2015):**

- Banned **plastic microbeads** in rinse-off cosmetics (facial scrubs, toothpaste)
- One of the first federal-level microplastic regulations globally
- Limited scope: addressed only one source of microplastics in one product category

The United States was identified as the **largest plastic waste producer globally** (42 million metric tons in 2016) and the **third largest contributor to mismanaged plastic waste** entering the marine environment. Despite this, US federal policy has focused on research and cleanup rather than production reduction.

Source: NOAA; US Congress; Frontiers review.

### Deposit-Return Systems

Germany and Denmark operate the most successful **deposit-return systems** for beverage containers:

- Mandatory deposit on single-use and reusable bottles and cans
- Automated recycling machines in supermarkets and public spaces
- Return rates exceeding **90%** in Germany for deposit containers
- Dramatically reduced litter rates for covered beverage containers

These systems demonstrate that economic incentives (deposit returns) can achieve near-complete collection of specific plastic categories -- but they cover only a fraction of total plastic consumption (beverage containers, not food packaging, industrial plastic, or consumer goods).

Source: NOAA; Frontiers review; search results.

## Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)

Extended Producer Responsibility is the policy principle that producers should bear significant responsibility for the environmental impacts of their products throughout the product lifecycle, including post-consumer waste management. In practice, EPR schemes for plastics typically involve:

- **Mandatory take-back programs** -- producers must accept returned packaging
- **Eco-fees** -- levies on plastic products that fund collection and recycling infrastructure
- **Modulated fees** -- higher fees for harder-to-recycle plastic types, creating economic incentives to design for recyclability
- **Reporting requirements** -- producers must disclose volumes and types of plastic placed on the market

EPR schemes are operational in:

- Most EU member states (mandatory under EU packaging directive)
- Several Canadian provinces
- South Korea
- Japan
- Parts of Australia

The effectiveness of EPR varies dramatically depending on enforcement, fee structure, and the scope of products covered. The best systems (Germany, South Korea) achieve significant recycling rate improvements. The weakest (voluntary programs without penalties) have minimal impact.

## River-Source Targeting

### The 1,000 Rivers Initiative

Research has identified that approximately **1,000 rivers account for 80% of river-sourced ocean plastic inflow**. These rivers are concentrated in:

- Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Philippines, Vietnam, Thailand)
- South Asia (India, Bangladesh)
- East Asia (China)
- Central America (Guatemala, Mexico)
- West Africa (Nigeria, Ghana)

Targeting these 1,000 rivers with interception technology (such as The Ocean Cleanup's Interceptors) could theoretically reduce the annual plastic inflow to the ocean by 80% -- a far more efficient approach than trying to intercept plastic across the hundreds of thousands of rivers and coastlines worldwide.

### Challenges

- **Political sovereignty** -- deploying interception systems requires agreement from national and local governments, which may have competing priorities
- **Infrastructure** -- many target rivers are in countries with limited waste management infrastructure; intercepting plastic is pointless if there is no system to process the collected material
- **Flood events** -- the majority of annual plastic transport occurs during high-flow events (monsoons, storms) when interception systems are most likely to be overwhelmed or damaged
- **Economic costs** -- deployment, operation, and maintenance of 1,000 interception systems requires sustained funding
- **Upstream waste management** -- without improving waste collection in river catchments, interception is a perpetual operation with no end point

Source: The Ocean Cleanup; NOAA Marine Debris Program.

## The Upstream-Downstream Divide

The fundamental policy tension in addressing ocean plastic can be summarized simply:

**Downstream approach (cleanup and recycling):**
- Treats the symptom (plastic in the environment) rather than the cause (plastic production)
- Economically compatible with continued petrochemical industry growth
- Technically feasible with current technology at sufficient scale and funding
- Does not challenge existing economic structures
- Cannot succeed alone because inflow exceeds cleanup capacity by orders of magnitude

**Upstream approach (production reduction):**
- Treats the cause by reducing the total volume of plastic produced
- Directly threatens petrochemical industry revenue
- Requires fundamental changes to manufacturing, packaging, and consumer behavior
- Politically difficult because it opposes powerful industrial interests
- Is the only approach that changes the long-term arithmetic of ocean plastic accumulation

The scientific consensus is clear: **both approaches are necessary simultaneously**. Cleanup addresses legacy pollution (the 100 million kg already in the GPGP). Source reduction prevents new accumulation. Neither alone is sufficient. The policy challenge is that the nations most capable of funding cleanup (wealthy petrochemical-producing states) are the same nations most resistant to production reduction.
