# Environmental Economics

## The Central Economic Question

The economics of cleaning the Great Pacific Garbage Patch reduce to a deceptively simple question: **Is it worth it?**

This question unfolds across multiple dimensions:

- What does cleanup cost, and who pays?
- What are the economic costs of *not* cleaning up -- to fisheries, tourism, marine ecosystems, and human health?
- Do the environmental benefits of cleanup outweigh the environmental costs of cleanup operations themselves?
- Can extracted ocean plastic be recycled into products with market value, offsetting operational costs?
- How does the carbon footprint of cleanup compare to the carbon impact of leaving plastic in the ocean?

Each of these questions has been addressed by published research, and the emerging picture is clear: cleanup is economically justified, but only when paired with source reduction. Without addressing inflow, cleanup is an indefinite operational expense with no endpoint.

## The Ocean Cleanup's $7.5 Billion Estimate

### The Headline Number

In September 2024, The Ocean Cleanup declared that the **GPGP could be cleaned within a decade for $7.5 billion**. This estimate assumes:

- A fleet of approximately **10 System 03-scale units** operating continuously
- AI-guided hotspot targeting to maximize extraction efficiency
- Progressive cleanup from highest-density zones outward
- Simultaneous river interception to reduce inflow during the cleanup decade
- Disposal and recycling infrastructure for extracted material

Source: The Ocean Cleanup press releases; Wikipedia.

### Contextualizing $7.5 Billion

The number $7.5 billion sounds large in isolation. It is not large relative to the entities capable of funding it:

| Comparison | Amount | Timeframe |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Apple quarterly revenue | ~$94 billion | 3 months |
| US annual military spending | ~$886 billion | 1 year |
| Global plastic packaging market | ~$350 billion | 1 year |
| Exxon Mobil annual profit (2023) | ~$36 billion | 1 year |
| Global fossil fuel subsidies | ~$7 trillion | 1 year (IMF estimate) |
| GPGP cleanup total cost | $7.5 billion | 10 years |

The entire GPGP cleanup costs less than one day of Apple's annual revenue. It costs less than 1% of annual global fossil fuel subsidies. The economic barrier to cleanup is not the cost -- it is the allocation of that cost and the political will to prioritize it.

### Cost Breakdown Assumptions

The $7.5 billion estimate encompasses:

- **Capital expenditure** -- construction of 10 System 03-scale vessels with their floating barriers, retention zones, and support infrastructure
- **Operational costs** -- fuel for towing vessels, crew salaries, logistics, port services, supply runs across the open Pacific
- **Technology iteration** -- ongoing improvements to system design based on operational experience
- **Disposal and recycling** -- processing extracted plastic (sorting, cleaning, recycling where viable, responsible disposal otherwise)
- **Monitoring and research** -- environmental impact monitoring, scientific observation, data collection
- **Overhead** -- organizational administration, fundraising, partnerships, communications

The largest single cost component is fuel for the towing vessels. Each System 03 unit requires two vessels operating continuously in the middle of the Pacific Ocean, thousands of kilometers from the nearest port. Fuel supply logistics are a major operational challenge and cost driver.

## Comparative Cost-Per-Kilogram Analysis

### Technology Comparison

Different cleanup technologies operate at vastly different cost points per kilogram of plastic removed:

| Technology | Cost per kg | Application | Key Constraint |
|-----------|------------|-------------|----------------|
| Seabin | $1.24-1.55 | Marinas and harbors | Calm water only; coastal |
| Manual island cleanup | $8.83 | Beach and shoreline | Labor-intensive; Seychelles case study |
| Ocean booms (System 03 type) | $22.5-30.1 | Open ocean GPGP | High operational cost; remote location |

All costs assume plastic constitutes **80-90% of catches** (the remainder being organic debris, algae, and other non-plastic material).

Source: PMC cleanup technology review, Falk-Andersson et al.

### Why Ocean Cleanup Is the Most Expensive

The 15-20x cost differential between coastal cleanup (Seabin at ~$1.40/kg) and open-ocean cleanup (booms at ~$26/kg) reflects the fundamental logistical challenge of operating in the middle of the Pacific:

1. **Distance** -- the GPGP is approximately 1,000-2,000 km from the nearest major port (Honolulu). Every supply run, crew rotation, and maintenance operation requires multi-day ocean transits
2. **Vessel costs** -- deep-sea-capable vessels cost orders of magnitude more to operate than harbor boats. Fuel alone for continuous towing operations runs millions of dollars per year per system
3. **Weather** -- open-ocean operations are weather-dependent. North Pacific storms, especially in winter, can force multi-day operational pauses
4. **Debris density** -- while the GPGP contains enormous total mass, the debris is spread across 1.6 million square kilometers. Average density is relatively low, meaning systems must sweep vast areas to collect significant volumes
5. **Maintenance** -- salt water, UV exposure, and mechanical stress cause continuous wear on floating barriers, requiring regular inspection and repair

Despite these costs, ocean cleanup remains the only way to address the legacy pollution already concentrated in the gyre. Coastal systems and river interception address inflow but cannot remove the 100 million kg already accumulated.

### The Modeling Caveat

Research modeling suggests that even **200 Ocean Cleanup devices** would have only **modest impact** on the GPGP if current production and inflow rates continue unchanged. This finding, from Hohn, Acevedo-Trejos et al. (via PMC), emphasizes a critical economic reality: **cleanup alone cannot solve the problem**. Without simultaneous source reduction, the gyre refills faster than it can be cleaned.

This means the true economic cost of solving ocean plastic pollution is not $7.5 billion for cleanup alone. It includes the economic costs of:

- Transitioning industries away from single-use plastic
- Building waste management infrastructure in developing countries
- Implementing Extended Producer Responsibility globally
- Developing and scaling alternative materials
- Reducing petrochemical production volumes

The total global cost of addressing plastic pollution comprehensively has been estimated at many multiples of the cleanup-only figure.

Source: Hohn, Acevedo-Trejos et al., via PMC.

## Net Environmental Benefit Assessment

### The Framework

The most rigorous assessment of whether GPGP cleanup delivers net environmental benefit was published in 2025 in **Scientific Reports** by Egger et al. This study developed the first comprehensive framework comparing three stressors on marine ecosystems:

1. **Macroplastic presence** -- the ongoing harm from plastic remaining in the ocean
2. **Microplastic presence** -- the ongoing harm from microplastic fragments throughout the water column
3. **Cleanup activities** -- the environmental cost of cleanup operations themselves (vessel noise, fuel emissions, potential bycatch, habitat disturbance)

The study assessed **nine marine ecological guilds** -- groups of species classified by ecological role and habitat -- rating vulnerability to each stressor on a **1-3 scale** (1 = low vulnerability, 3 = high vulnerability).

### Results

| Stressor | Average Vulnerability Score | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------------------|----------------|
| Macroplastics | **2.3** | Highest harm -- entanglement, ingestion, habitat disruption |
| Microplastics | **1.9** | Moderate harm -- chemical contamination, food chain transfer |
| Cleanup activities | **1.8** | Lowest harm -- vessel disturbance, limited bycatch |

The key finding: **vulnerability to macroplastics (2.3) significantly exceeds vulnerability to cleanup activities (1.8)**. This means the environmental cost of leaving plastic in the ocean is demonstrably greater than the environmental cost of removing it for every ecological guild assessed.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025.

### Species-Group Benefits

The net benefit framework identifies which species groups benefit most from cleanup:

**Marine mammals** -- benefit strongly from macroplastic removal. Ghost gear entanglement is the primary mortality vector, and System 03 extracts the large net tangles that cause the most entanglement deaths. An 80% cleanup could reduce macroplastic concentrations to within **safe levels for marine mammals**.

**Sea turtles** -- benefit from removal of plastic bags (mistaken for jellyfish), hard fragments (causing gut impaction), and derelict nets (entanglement). An 80% cleanup would also bring macroplastic concentrations within **safe levels for sea turtles**.

**Seabirds** -- benefit from reduced surface plastic availability (less ingestion, reduced plasticosis risk), but the benefit is somewhat lower than for mammals and turtles because seabird exposure pathways include microplastics that cleanup cannot address.

**Fish and invertebrates** -- moderate benefit from macroplastic removal, primarily through reduced ghost fishing mortality. Microplastic exposure is the dominant harm pathway for fish, and this is largely unaffected by surface cleanup.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025; Plymouth Marine Laboratory summary.

### The 80% Threshold

The study identifies an **80% cleanup threshold** as particularly significant:

- At 80% removal of surface macroplastics, concentrations drop below safe levels for the most vulnerable species groups (marine mammals, sea turtles)
- Below this threshold, significant mortality risk persists
- Above this threshold, diminishing returns apply -- removing the last 20% is disproportionately expensive relative to the additional ecological benefit

This finding supports The Ocean Cleanup's stated goal of removing 90% of floating ocean plastic by 2040, which exceeds the 80% threshold with a safety margin.

Without intervention, macroplastic concentrations are projected to reach **86-140 kg per square kilometer by 2040** -- far exceeding safe levels for all marine megafauna. This projection underscores the urgency: delay increases both the ecological damage and the eventual cleanup cost.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025.

## Carbon Trade-Off Analysis

### Cleanup Emissions

Cleanup operations produce greenhouse gas emissions through:

- **Vessel fuel consumption** -- two vessels towing each System 03 unit continuously, burning marine diesel
- **Manufacturing** -- construction of floating barriers, retention zones, support vessels, and shore infrastructure
- **Logistics** -- supply runs, crew transport, extracted material shipping
- **Processing** -- sorting, cleaning, and recycling or disposing of extracted plastic

Total estimated cleanup GHG emissions: **0.4-2.9 million metric tons CO2 equivalent** over the entire multi-year cleanup campaign. The range reflects uncertainty in fleet size, operational tempo, and campaign duration.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025.

### Microplastic Carbon Impact

Microplastics disrupt the ocean's biological carbon pump -- the natural process by which carbon fixed by surface phytoplankton sinks to the deep ocean, sequestering it from the atmosphere for centuries to millennia. The mechanisms include:

- Reduced phytoplankton productivity from surface shading and chemical contamination
- Altered sinking dynamics of marine aggregates incorporating microplastic particles
- Disruption of zooplankton grazing efficiency (zooplankton ingesting plastic instead of phytoplankton)
- Changes to microbial community composition on the "plastisphere" (biofilms growing on plastic surfaces)

Estimated annual impact: **15-30 million metric tons of carbon per year** in reduced ocean carbon sequestration. This is an **ongoing, annual cost** that accumulates every year cleanup is delayed.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup research; Scientific Reports 2025 impact assessment framework.

### The Trade-Off Arithmetic

The carbon balance strongly favors cleanup:

| Factor | Amount | Timeframe |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Total cleanup GHG emissions | 0.4-2.9 Mt CO2e | One-time (over ~10 years) |
| Annual carbon sequestration loss from microplastics | 15-30 Mt C/year | Ongoing, every year |
| Net carbon savings from cleanup (annual, post-completion) | 15-30 Mt C/year | Permanent |

Even taking the upper bound of cleanup emissions (2.9 Mt) and the lower bound of annual carbon benefit (15 Mt C/year), the cleanup pays for its carbon cost in less than **3 months** of restored ocean carbon sequestration capacity. Over a decade, the net carbon benefit exceeds the cleanup cost by a factor of approximately 50-750x.

The study concludes that cleanup emissions are **"significantly lower"** than the ongoing carbon impact of leaving plastic in the ocean. This finding removes one of the most commonly cited objections to ocean cleanup -- that the fuel burned by cleanup vessels offsets any environmental benefit.

Source: Egger et al., Scientific Reports, 2025; Plymouth Marine Laboratory summary.

## Recycled Ocean Plastic Markets

### Product Partnerships

The Ocean Cleanup has pursued a strategy of converting extracted ocean plastic into consumer products to generate revenue and demonstrate circular economy potential:

**Kia EV3 Trunk Liner:**
- Recycled GPGP plastic incorporated into the trunk liner of Kia's electric vehicle
- Partnership demonstrates that ocean plastic can meet automotive-grade material specifications
- Visibility in a mass-market consumer product raises awareness

**Ocean Cleanup Sunglasses:**
- First consumer product made from GPGP-extracted plastic
- **Sold out within 18 months** of initial release
- Revenue contributed directly to funding continued cleanup operations
- Demonstrated consumer willingness to pay a premium for products with ocean-cleanup provenance

Source: The Ocean Cleanup.

### Market Viability Challenges

Despite these successful partnerships, recycled ocean plastic faces significant market headwinds:

- **Material quality** -- ocean-degraded plastic often scores **lower on functional material tests** compared to virgin plastic. UV degradation, salt exposure, and mechanical wear reduce polymer chain length and material properties
- **Environmental indicators** -- life cycle assessments of recycled ocean plastic can show **higher environmental indicators** than virgin plastic in some categories, because the energy-intensive collection, transport, and reprocessing steps add environmental cost
- **Contamination** -- ocean plastic is contaminated with salt, biofouling, absorbed chemicals, and mixed polymer types. Cleaning and sorting add cost and complexity
- **Scale** -- current extraction volumes (hundreds of thousands of kg per year) are tiny relative to global plastic markets (hundreds of millions of tons per year). Ocean plastic is a niche material, not a commodity feedstock
- **Price competition** -- virgin plastic derived from cheap petrochemical feedstocks is almost always less expensive than recycled ocean plastic. Without regulatory intervention (recycled content mandates, virgin plastic taxes), the market does not favor ocean-sourced material

The economic case for recycled ocean plastic products rests more on brand value and consumer sentiment (willingness to pay a premium for "ocean cleanup" provenance) than on material economics.

Source: The Ocean Cleanup; PMC review.

## Global Economic Scale of Plastic Pollution

### Cumulative Cost Projections

The plastic pollution crisis carries **cumulative costs estimated at $281 trillion** by some projections, encompassing:

- Environmental cleanup and remediation
- Fisheries losses from ghost gear and contamination
- Tourism losses from polluted coastlines
- Healthcare costs from plastic-related chemical exposure
- Loss of ecosystem services (carbon sequestration, fisheries productivity, coastal protection)
- Waste management infrastructure costs

This figure is subject to enormous uncertainty -- valuing ecosystem services and long-term health costs is inherently imprecise. But even conservative estimates place the total economic cost of plastic pollution in the trillions of dollars, dwarfing the $7.5 billion cleanup cost estimate.

Source: World Economic Forum.

### Cost Estimation Frameworks

The **National Center for Ecological Analysis and Synthesis** (NCEAS) is developing a comprehensive framework to help governments estimate the full costs of plastic pollution. The framework accounts for:

- **Environmental persistence** -- plastics last hundreds to thousands of years, so costs accumulate over extremely long timeframes
- **Waste volumes** -- the relationship between annual production (460+ million tons) and waste management capacity (recycling rate <10%)
- **Ecosystem impact valuation** -- monetizing the damage to marine ecosystems, fisheries, and carbon sequestration
- **Health impact accounting** -- quantifying the economic cost of microplastic-related health effects

This framework is significant because it provides governments with a standardized methodology for comparing the costs of action (regulation, cleanup, infrastructure investment) against the costs of inaction (ongoing environmental and economic damage).

Source: NOAA Marine Debris Program.

## Cost of Inaction

### Fisheries Impact

Global marine fisheries generate approximately **$100 billion** annually in revenue. Plastic pollution threatens this revenue through:

- **Ghost fishing** -- derelict gear killing commercially valuable species
- **Contamination** -- microplastic and chemical contamination reducing the marketability and safety of seafood
- **Habitat degradation** -- plastic accumulation in nursery and spawning areas reducing fish recruitment
- **Consumer confidence** -- public awareness of plastic contamination reducing demand for seafood products

The fraction of fisheries revenue at risk from plastic pollution is difficult to quantify precisely, but even a 1% reduction represents $1 billion per year in lost revenue -- recurring annually and growing as plastic concentrations increase.

### Tourism Impact

Coastal tourism is a **multi-trillion dollar** global industry. Polluted beaches and coastal waters directly reduce tourism revenue:

- Beach cleanup costs for coastal municipalities range from millions to hundreds of millions per year
- Visible marine debris reduces tourist visitation rates by measurable percentages
- Coral reef damage from plastic debris and chemical leaching threatens dive tourism
- Coastal communities in Southeast Asia, the Pacific Islands, and the Caribbean are particularly affected

### The Discount Rate Problem

The economics of ocean plastic cleanup are complicated by **discount rate effects**. Because the costs of inaction accumulate slowly over decades and centuries while cleanup costs are incurred upfront, conventional economic analysis (which discounts future costs relative to present costs) systematically undervalues cleanup investment.

At a 5% discount rate, $100 of environmental damage occurring 50 years from now has a present value of approximately $8.70. This means conventional cost-benefit analysis can make inaction appear economically rational even when the undiscounted total cost of inaction vastly exceeds the cost of action. This is a known problem in environmental economics and climate policy, and it applies directly to ocean plastic cleanup.

The net environmental benefit framework (Egger et al., 2025) addresses this by focusing on physical vulnerability metrics rather than discounted economic values, avoiding the distortion introduced by arbitrary discount rate selection.

## The Integration Point

The environmental economics of GPGP cleanup converge on a clear conclusion:

1. **Cleanup is economically justified** when assessed against the full costs of inaction -- fisheries losses, tourism impacts, ecosystem service degradation, carbon sequestration disruption, and healthcare costs
2. **Cleanup is environmentally justified** -- the net environmental benefit assessment shows that harm from plastic exceeds harm from cleanup for all ecological guilds assessed
3. **Cleanup is carbon-positive** -- cleanup emissions are a fraction of the ongoing carbon cost of microplastic disruption to ocean carbon export
4. **Cleanup alone is insufficient** -- without source reduction, the gyre refills faster than it can be cleaned. The $7.5 billion cleanup estimate is meaningful only in the context of simultaneous production reduction
5. **The barrier is political, not economic** -- $7.5 billion over a decade is well within the capacity of the global economy to fund. The obstacle is the allocation of costs and the political influence of the petrochemical industry

The math is clear. Ten systems. One decade. $7.5 billion. The question is whether humanity chooses to do the math.
