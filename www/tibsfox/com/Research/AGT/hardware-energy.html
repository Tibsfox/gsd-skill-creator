1. Compute Architecture

The boundary between computation and physics is dissolving. Memory-centric architectures address the reality that over 50% of CPU time is spent waiting for data. Photonic interconnects promise bandwidth scaling without proportional energy costs. Physical neural networks exploit the noise floor itself as a computational feature. These are not incremental improvements — they are paradigm shifts in what “computing” means.

VLIW: Very Long Instruction Word Architectures

Source: Asianometry (#40 in research queue)

VLIW architectures move scheduling complexity from runtime hardware to compile-time software, packing multiple independent operations into a single wide instruction word. The compiler bears the burden of finding instruction-level parallelism, which eliminates the power-hungry out-of-order execution hardware that dominates modern superscalar CPUs. The tradeoff: VLIW compilers must be extraordinarily sophisticated, and code density suffers when the compiler cannot fill all slots. DSPs and specialized accelerators remain the stronghold — general-purpose VLIW (Itanium) became a cautionary tale about assuming compilers could solve NP-hard scheduling at industrial scale.

Memory-Centric Computing

Source: Onur Mutlu, ETH Zurich (#25 in research queue)

The memory wall is the defining bottleneck of modern computing. Over 50% of CPU execution time is spent stalled, waiting for data to arrive from memory. Mutlu’s research program attacks this from multiple angles: processing-in-memory (PIM) moves computation to where data lives; intelligent memory controllers predict access patterns and prefetch; and new memory technologies (HBM, CXL-attached memory pools) restructure the hierarchy entirely.

The insight applies directly to AI workloads: large language model inference is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. A memory-centric architecture that doubles effective bandwidth has more impact than doubling FLOPS.

Photonic Interconnect Scaling

Source: Research queue #32

Electrical interconnects hit fundamental scaling limits: resistance increases with length, crosstalk worsens with density, and energy per bit grows with bandwidth. Photonic interconnects break all three constraints by using light instead of electrons. Silicon photonics integrates optical waveguides onto standard CMOS dies, enabling chip-to-chip and rack-to-rack communication at terabit speeds with sub-picojoule-per-bit energy.

The Max Planck Institute research on physical neural networks (Hakan Tureci, Princeton) extends this further: optical computing through complex scatterers generates nonlinear features from physical stimuli, with the capacity formula C = Σ[1/(1 + βk2/S)] providing a rigorous measure of computational capacity as a function of measurement shots S. Shot noise — the bane of digital systems — becomes a feature in physical computing, not a bug.

Trapped-Ion Quantum Hardware

Source: Sara Mouradian, Max Planck Institute for the Science of Light

A single trapped ion is “more or less the best qubit you can find” — coherence times exceeding one second, gate fidelities achievable in both macroscopic and surface traps. The scaling challenge is immense: utility-scale quantum computing requires millions of qubits, and trapping frequency must increase from ~1 MHz to ~100 MHz (a 100x improvement) to achieve the gate speeds needed.

Anomalous electric field noise from surfaces scales as D-4 and remains poorly understood despite extensive research — “tons of studies and no one knows.” The modular architecture (groups of ions connected through laser interactions and physical ion transport) provides a hardware model for distributed quantum computation that maps onto federated computing architectures.

2. Energy Systems

Energy is infrastructure’s infrastructure. Every compute cluster, every manufacturing line, every communication network sits on top of the power grid. The research here spans the full energy stack: generation (fusion, solar, geothermal), storage (hydrogen batteries, BESS), distribution (synchronous grids, V2G), and the physics that governs it all.

Commercial Fusion: Helion Energy

Source: Anthony Pancotti, Synapse “Hardware to Save a Planet”

Helion is building the world’s first commercial fusion power plant, with a power purchase agreement to supply Microsoft by 2028. Their Field Reversed Configuration (FRC) approach differs fundamentally from tokamaks: two FRC plasma formations are accelerated, merged, and magnetically compressed until deuterium-helium-3 fusion occurs. The reaction is aneutronic — charged particles push back against magnetic fields, enabling direct energy recovery at 95% circuit efficiency without steam turbines.

The numbers reshape the energy conversation: 1 kg of fusion fuel provides as much energy as 10 million kg of coal. The NRC ruled fusion will be regulated by states (like particle accelerators) rather than by federal nuclear fission regulations, dramatically shortening deployment timelines. Helion’s goal: 1 GW/day of grid capacity addition — the scale required to replace the entire fossil fuel grid within a decade.

Supercritical CO2 Turbines

Source: Asianometry (#44 in research queue)

Supercritical CO2 (sCO2) operates near its critical point where gas and liquid phases merge, achieving fluid densities approaching liquid while maintaining gas-like compressibility. Turbines running sCO2 cycles achieve higher thermal efficiency than steam Rankine cycles at comparable temperatures, in dramatically smaller physical footprints — the turbomachinery can be an order of magnitude more compact. Applications span concentrated solar, nuclear, waste heat recovery, and natural gas combined cycles. The technology is at the demonstration plant stage, with multiple DOE-funded projects operational.

Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) Adoption

Source: Stanford research (#33 in research queue)

Every electric vehicle is a battery on wheels. V2G transforms parked EVs into distributed energy storage, feeding power back to the grid during peak demand. Stanford research identifies the adoption barriers: battery degradation concerns (cycling reduces lifespan), utility interconnection standards, consumer incentive design, and bidirectional charger economics. The potential is enormous — if even 20% of a national EV fleet participated in V2G, the aggregate storage capacity would exceed most grid-scale battery installations combined.

Battery Energy Storage: 10 BESS Parameters

Source: Research queue #27

Grid-scale battery energy storage systems (BESS) are evaluated on 10 critical parameters: energy capacity (MWh), power rating (MW), round-trip efficiency (%), cycle life (count), calendar life (years), response time (ms), depth of discharge (%), self-discharge rate (%/month), energy density (Wh/L), and levelized cost of storage ($/MWh). No single chemistry dominates all 10 — lithium iron phosphate (LFP) leads on cost and cycle life, flow batteries on duration, and solid-state on energy density. The art is matching chemistry to use case: frequency regulation demands millisecond response, while seasonal storage demands minimal self-discharge over months.

Solid-State Hydrogen Batteries

Source: Mia Wrath, Lumen (Synapse “Hardware to Save a Planet”)

Hydrogen stored in solid-state metal hydrides represents a fundamentally different battery paradigm. High-entropy alloys reversibly bond with hydrogen — creating a stable, non-degrading energy storage medium. Unlike diesel fuel (degrades in 12–18 months) or lithium-ion (capacity fade over cycles), metal hydride storage does not degrade over time. The system scales linearly: double the vessels for double the storage.

Lumen (originally “Lunar Manufacturing and Industrial Technologies”) pivoted from space materials to terrestrial energy storage when their 3D-printable high-entropy alloys proved exceptional for hydrogen absorption. Behind-the-meter deployment displaces peaker plants — the most expensive and highest-emission electricity on the grid. Grid resilience is the direct argument: a Texas-style blackout at a cold storage warehouse means $20–50 million in lost inventory.

Perovskite Tandem Solar

Source: Scott Greyiel, Kux (Synapse “Hardware to Save a Planet”)

Perovskite-silicon tandems promise a 25–45% improvement in energy harvest — a step-function change from the 1–4% incremental gains that dominated silicon PV for years. Conventional crystalline silicon approaches its practical ceiling at ~25% (Shockley-Queisser limit for single-junction). Tandems split the solar spectrum: perovskite absorbs high-energy blue photons, silicon handles low-energy red/infrared. Kux scaled from 2″×2″ lab substrates to 2.1 m2 production panels in 5 years. Wright’s Law governs the economics: every doubling of cumulative production yields a predictable cost reduction.

Ground-Source Geothermal

Source: Dan Yates, Dandelion Energy (Synapse “Hardware to Save a Planet”)

Globally, we burn as much fossil fuel heating homes as driving cars. Ground-source heat pumps extract heat from 55°F earth instead of 0°F air — maintaining high COP precisely when air-source pumps fail and fall back to expensive resistance heating. Dandelion (spun out of Google X, 2017) has installed 3,000+ systems and can cut home heating/cooling loads in half. The electrification pathway is self-reinforcing: geothermal electrifies heating today; as the grid decarbonizes, every geothermal home automatically becomes zero-carbon without further intervention.

Synchronous Power Grids

Source: CastlePast, “How Synchronous Grids Balance Continental Power Demands” (~128 min)

A synchronous grid is effectively a single machine stretching across continents. Every generator spins in electromagnetic lockstep at exactly 50 or 60 Hz. The physics is Newton’s second law applied to rotating steel: kinetic energy KE = ½Jω2 stored in massive turbine shafts provides seconds of inertial buffer against sudden load changes. A 1% frequency deviation triggers a 20% change in generator power output through speed governor action — the most sensitive feedback loop in industrial engineering.

The renewable integration crisis: as synchronous generators are displaced by inverter-based wind and solar (which have no rotating mass), the grid loses physical inertia. Ireland has documented RoCoF events exceeding 1 Hz/s during high wind penetration. South Australia’s 2016 blackout and Great Britain’s 2019 frequency event both resulted from insufficient system inertia. Countermeasures: synthetic inertia from wind turbines, grid-forming inverters, battery frequency response (milliseconds vs. seconds for mechanical governors), and synchronous condensers repurposed from retired thermal plants.

3. Electronics Heritage

The entire edifice of modern computing rests on a foundation built in evacuated glass envelopes. Understanding the vacuum tube → transistor lineage is not history for its own sake — it is understanding the physics that still governs every amplifier stage, every power supply, every impedance-matched signal chain in production hardware today.

Vacuum Technology: 2,000 Years from Aristotle to the LHC

Source: CastlePast, “Vacuum Technology” (~120 min)

Aristotle’s “nature abhors a vacuum” stalled investigation for nearly 2,000 years. Von Guericke’s mechanical pump (1649) cracked the door; Sprengel’s mercury dropping pump (1865) blew it open, achieving pressures below 1 micropascal and directly enabling the second scientific revolution: X-rays (1895), radioactivity (1896), the electron (1897). Edison’s light bulb (1879) created the first mass demand for electricity. Fleming’s oscillation valve (1904) became the first thermionic diode. De Forest’s Audion triode (1906) introduced amplification.

Wartime vacuum applications (1940s) built microwave radar (cavity magnetron), proximity fuses (miniature tubes surviving 20,000g), uranium enrichment (calutrons), and the first digital computers (ENIAC: 17,468 vacuum tubes). Today, the LHC maintains 10-10 to 10-12 torr across 27 km of beam pipe, and semiconductor fabs contain thousands of vacuum pumps consuming megawatts — the Sprengel pump lineage runs through every chip on Earth.

Power Grid Physics

Source: CastlePast synchronous grids analysis

The vacuum-to-grid pipeline is causally dependent: Sprengel pump (1865) → light bulb (1879) → mass electricity demand → power stations → frequency standardization → synchronous grid. The 50 Hz vs. 60 Hz split resulted from corporate rivalry (Westinghouse/Tesla drove 60 Hz; AEG in Germany chose 50 Hz as “metric-friendly”), not physics. Japan imports from both traditions and remains split to this day — a persistent infrastructure constraint that complicated disaster response after Fukushima 2011.

The generator-as-magnetic-spring: rotor and stator magnetic fields interact with forces reaching 106 newtons. The load angle (power angle) governs power transfer via sin(δ). If it exceeds ~90°, the magnetic coupling snaps — pole slipping — which can destroy the generator. The synchronous grid is a physical realization of the Kuramoto model of coupled oscillators, connecting directly to mathematical research on oscillator synchronization.

4. Physical AI

NVIDIA Cosmos World Foundation Models

Source: NVIDIA Cosmos (#30 in research queue)

Physical AI requires world models — neural networks that understand physics, geometry, and causality in the physical world. NVIDIA’s Cosmos platform provides pre-trained world foundation models for generating physically plausible video, 3D scenes, and robotic simulation environments. The pipeline: real-world video → tokenization → world model training → synthetic data generation → robotics/AV policy training.

The convergence with compute architecture is direct: Cosmos workloads are among the most memory-bandwidth-intensive in AI, demanding exactly the memory-centric and photonic interconnect innovations described above. Physical AI also closes the loop with energy systems — robotic systems operating in physical environments need reliable power, thermal management, and the kind of fault-tolerant infrastructure that synchronous grids provide.

Physical Neural Networks

Source: Hakan Tureci, Princeton / Max Planck Institute

Physical neural networks differ fundamentally from digital ones: input is physical, transduction is physical, measurement is physical — all governed by physical laws rather than mathematical abstraction alone. The key is noise. Sampling noise (shot noise) makes physical computing systems behave differently from digital counterparts. Reducing input power while maintaining accuracy requires increasing element nonlinearity with a specific power law — a fundamental tradeoff between energy efficiency and computational fidelity.

Eigentasks provide a natural truncation scheme for exponentially many features, ordering special functions by signal-to-noise ratio. This connects reservoir computing to physical substrates: optical scatterers, superconducting microwave oscillators, and potentially ecological systems where stochastic dynamics are features, not noise.
