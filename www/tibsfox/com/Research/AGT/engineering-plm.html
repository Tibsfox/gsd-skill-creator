1. Design Intent as Context

Two tools — Drafter and Trace.Space — are rewiring how engineering intent flows from conception to manufacturing. The distinction matters: AI-native design tools treat intent as structured context from the start, while bolted-on AI retrofits intelligence onto workflows that were never designed for it.

Drafter: Go-to-Manufacturing 2D Drawing Generation

Drafter generates manufacturing-ready 2D drawings from 3D CAD models, automating the tedious last mile of the design-to-production pipeline. The key insight: 2D drawings remain the legal contract between engineering and manufacturing. No matter how sophisticated the 3D model, the shop floor reads prints.

Trace.Space: AI-Native Requirements Engineering

Trace.Space approaches requirements as a context problem. Rather than bolting AI onto existing requirements management tools (DOORS, Jama), it treats requirements as structured data from inception — traceable, versionable, and machine-readable. This maps directly to our GSD planning architecture: requirements are context that flows through the execution pipeline.

2. Configuration Management

BOM (Bill of Materials) management is where engineering complexity meets manufacturing reality. The configuration management problem is deceptively simple at small scale and catastrophically complex at enterprise scale.

The BOM Chaos Problem

A single product can have hundreds of variants. The 150% BOM approach lists every possible component for every possible configuration in a single structure, with variant rules determining which subset applies to each specific configuration. This is the industry-standard solution, but it creates its own complexity:

  • 150% BOM — superset BOM containing all possible components across all variants
  • Variant rules — conditional logic (if option A, include parts X, Y; if option B, include parts X, Z)
  • Effectivity — time-based or serial-number-based activation of configuration changes
  • As-designed vs. as-built vs. as-maintained — three distinct BOMs for the same product at different lifecycle stages

Configuration management reduces to a small set of primitives that parallel version control in software:

3. Structural Engineering

Two deep-dive transcripts on structural dynamics reveal the gap between textbook math and physical-world consequences.

Stiffness Methods & Truss Analysis

Training next-generation truss designers who increasingly depend on FEA software they may not fully understand. The foundational equation ωn = √(K/M) hides the most expensive trap in mechanical engineering: static stiffness can differ from dynamic stiffness by an order of magnitude because polymer chains lock up under rapid oscillation.

Impact Dynamics vs. Textbook Math

The Shock Response Spectrum (SRS) measures the “bruise,” not the “punch” — it calculates maximum structural deflection response, not the frequency content of the input shock pulse. Engineers constantly confuse these with catastrophic results.

The Four Horsemen of Bad Vibration Data

Three Damping Mechanisms

TypeMechanismForce DependencyExample
ViscousFluid forced through orificesProportional to velocityCar shock absorber
CoulombDry friction (asperity welding/shearing)ConstantSteel on concrete
StructuralInternal molecular frictionDisplacement amplitude, frequency-independentSteel beam under cyclic load

Critical modeling error: Engineers frequently model structural damping as viscous. Viscous damping increases energy dissipation with frequency; structural does not. This causes FEA simulations to predict heavy damping at high frequencies that does not exist physically, leading to fatigue failure at frequencies the simulation declared safe.

4. Construction & Fabrication

Hover Bracket System

A modular construction bracket system rated from 150 to 3,000 lb capacity. The engineering interest is in the standardization approach: a single bracket geometry scaled across load ratings, with the connection interface remaining constant while material thickness and fastener grade vary.

This parallels software component design — a stable interface with scaling implementation details. The bracket system demonstrates that even in physical fabrication, the API/implementation boundary produces better outcomes than bespoke design for each load case.

Systems Engineering as Bias-Aware Framework

The systems engineering process is explicitly designed to counteract cognitive biases. Requirements traceability prevents scope creep (anchoring bias). Verification against requirements prevents confirmation bias. Independent review prevents groupthink. The V-model’s left side (decomposition) and right side (integration) force engineers to think at multiple abstraction levels, preventing the tunnel vision that comes from working at a single level for too long.

This maps directly to our GSD workflow: discuss (requirements) → plan (decomposition) → execute (implementation) → verify (integration testing). The phases are not bureaucracy — they are cognitive bias countermeasures.
