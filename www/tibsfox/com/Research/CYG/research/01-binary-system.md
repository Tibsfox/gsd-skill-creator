# Binary System Architecture

## System Overview

Cygnus X-3 is one of the first X-ray binaries identified in astronomical history. It consists of a Wolf-Rayet (WR) donor star and a compact object -- either a stellar-mass black hole or neutron star -- in an extraordinarily tight orbit.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Orbital period | 4.8 hours (among the shortest known X-ray binaries) |
| Distance from Earth | 9.67 +0.53/-0.48 kpc (VLBA trigonometric parallax) |
| Compact object type | Black hole or neutron star (unconfirmed) |
| Companion star | Wolf-Rayet star; dense ionized stellar wind |
| Accretion mode | Super-Eddington / ULX-class |
| Radio behavior | Intense flares (tens of Jy); quenching before major outbursts |
| Location | Cygnus X superbubble direction |

## Wolf-Rayet Companion and Stellar Wind

The WR companion's dense, line-driven stellar wind is central to all hadronic emission models. The wind provides two interaction channels: a gas target for proton-proton collisions, and a UV photon field for photomeson production. The short orbital separation means the inner jet is immersed in this wind at all orbital phases.

## IXPE X-ray Polarimetry (2024)

Recent IXPE observations confirmed the accretion geometry: Cygnus X-3 is a "hidden" ultraluminous X-ray source whose apparent luminosity is lower than intrinsic because the accretion column is oriented away from line-of-sight. This super-Eddington luminosity implies jet kinetic power exceeding 10^39 erg/s -- sufficient by a factor of ~100 to meet cosmic ray acceleration requirements.

## The 4.8-Hour Orbit

The orbital period of 4.8 hours is among the shortest known for any X-ray binary. The separation between the Wolf-Rayet star and the compact object is smaller than the Sun's radius. In this confined space, the interaction between the relativistic jet and the dense stellar wind creates the conditions for PeV particle acceleration.
