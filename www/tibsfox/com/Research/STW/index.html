Snow Goose Migration

One of the greatest wildlife spectacles in the Pacific Northwest.

60,000–100,000 snow geese migrate from Arctic breeding grounds (Wrangel Island, Siberia) to the Stillaguamish and Skagit River flats each winter, arriving in October and departing by April. Fir Island, accessible from Stanwood, is the prime viewing location — a 20-minute drive to fields where thousands of birds feed, roost, and fly in massive wheeling flocks. The annual Snow Goose Festival (February) features guided tours, bird walks, and educational programs. Trumpeter swans, bald eagles, red-tailed hawks, and great blue herons share the flats during migration season.

Parks & Green Spaces

Small-town parks and the vast agricultural landscapes that surround them.

Church Creek Park

communityplayground

Stanwood's primary community park with playground, picnic shelters, sports fields, and walking paths. The park hosts community events and provides green space in the center of town. Church Creek runs through the area, connecting to the broader Stillaguamish watershed.

Heritage Park

historiccommunity

A heritage park preserving Stanwood's Scandinavian settlement history. Interpretive displays and community gathering space honor the Norwegian and Swedish families who established the farming community along the river.

Stanwood-Camano Trail System

trailsbirding

A growing trail network connecting Stanwood to Camano Island and the surrounding agricultural lands. The trails pass through farming landscapes, riparian corridors, and wetland areas. Prime bird-watching territory during migration season, with views of the Cascade foothills.

Notable Features

Gateway to Camano Island

bridgegateway

SR-532 crosses from Stanwood to Camano Island via a bridge over Davis Slough and West Pass. No ferry needed — Stanwood is the only mainland access point to the island. The connection makes Stanwood-Camano a functional community pair, sharing services, schools, and identity.

Stillaguamish Valley Agriculture

dairyfarming

The Stillaguamish River floodplain surrounding Stanwood remains active farmland — dairy farms, hay fields, and vegetable operations. The flat, rich alluvial soil supports agriculture that has continued since the 1870s. The agricultural landscape is also what makes the snow goose habitat possible — the birds feed on waste grain and cover crops in the farm fields.

Fir Island Bird Watching

Oct–Apr60,000+ birds

Fir Island sits between the North and South forks of the Stillaguamish River, accessible from Stanwood. During winter months, the flat agricultural fields host massive flocks of snow geese, swans, and raptors. The Skagit Wildlife Area on Fir Island provides public viewing access, walking trails, and interpretive displays. Bring binoculars.

Getting Here from Mukilteo

Stanwood is 30 miles north of Mukilteo at the Snohomish/Skagit county border. Limited transit; car recommended.

Amtrak Cascades (Intercity Rail)

Stanwood station is a flag stop on the Amtrak Cascades corridor. Two daily trains each direction connecting the Pacific coast from Vancouver BC to Eugene, OR. Stanwood sits between Everett and Mount Vernon on the route.

  • Southbound to Everett: ~20 minutes
  • Southbound to Seattle: ~1.5 hours
  • Northbound to Mount Vernon: ~15 minutes
  • Northbound to Bellingham: ~1 hour

Note: Stanwood is a flag stop — trains may not stop unless passengers are boarding/alighting. Check schedule and reservations in advance.

By Bus (Limited)

Snow Goose Transit: A local minibus service connecting Stanwood and Camano Island. This is primarily a local circulator, not a regional connector.

No direct bus service from Mukilteo to Stanwood. Regional connections would require transfers through Everett Station and Marysville.

By Car

I-5 north from Mukilteo through Everett and Marysville to Exit 212 (Stanwood/Camano Island). West on SR-532 to downtown Stanwood. Approximately 35–40 minutes.
