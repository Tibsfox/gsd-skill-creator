Pacific Northwest Research Series

Rainforest to garden — mapping the living systems of the PNW bioregion
Sixteen research projects, one bioregion. From the Columbia Valley rainforest canopy to the backyard garden bed, from the mycorrhizal network beneath the soil to the structural engineering above it, from the smart thermostat on the wall to the ESP32 inside it, from the orca's biosonar to the fox's magnetic compass, from the cuticle scale to the roughness map, from the LED strip to the oscilloscope trace, from the guardian spirit to the scholarly source, from the server log to the trust-based firewall — each project maps a different layer of the same place. The salmon that feeds the forest, the elevation gradient that stratifies it, the birds that pollinate and disperse it, the mammals that shape its trophic structure, the physics that governs how they all sense their world, the crafts that render their textures in fabric and light, the traditions that teach how to listen, the gardener who tends a small piece of it, the builder who shelters within it, the maker who wires it all together, and the sysadmin who stewards the infrastructure that connects it, the air we breathe and the water we filter — all part of the same story.

The Salmon Thread

Pacific salmon carry marine-derived nitrogen (N-15) from the ocean into riparian forests. Bears, eagles, and floods distribute carcass nutrients. Trees absorb them through mycorrhizal networks. The forest feeds the stream that feeds the salmon.

Bio-Physics Sensing

Every organism in the PNW reads the physical world through evolved sensors. Orcas echolocate Chinook in dark water. Salmon navigate open ocean by Earth's magnetic field. Foxes triangulate prey beneath snow. The physics is the same — the biological implementations are endlessly varied. The oscilloscope and the multimeter are how we stand at that boundary and ask: how fast? what color? when?
