# Module 4: Pollinator Health and Colony Collapse

## Overview

This module documents the state of pollinator populations in North America, with emphasis on the severe 2025 managed honey bee colony losses, the biological mechanisms driving decline, the broader extinction risk landscape for native pollinators, and evidence-based restoration strategies. ## The 2025 Colony Collapse Crisis

### Scale of Loss

Between summer 2024 and spring 2025, the United States experienced one of the most severe managed honey bee die-offs in recorded history. USDA confirmed that approximately 1.7 million bee colonies died during this period, representing a 62% average loss of U.S. commercial beekeeping colonies (Food Tank, August 2025). Survey data from Project Apis M corroborated the 62% loss figure across sampled operations.

Beekeepers first recognized the scale of the losses in January 2025 as hives were being prepared for California almond pollination — the largest single managed pollination event in the world. Colonies that had appeared healthy in autumn 2024 were found dead or critically weakened by early winter, with many exhibiting classic collapse symptoms: absence of adult bees in the hive body despite presence of capped brood and food stores.

### Timeline of the 2025 Crisis

| Date | Event | Source |
|------|-------|--------|
| Summer 2024 | Colony health assessments show normal seasonal variation | USDA-ARS |
| Autumn 2024 | Overwintering preparations; early reports of elevated mite loads | Save the Bee (2025) |
| January 2025 | Mass die-offs discovered as hives prepared for almond pollination | Food Tank (August 2025) |
| February 2025 | California almond growers report acute pollination shortfalls | Food Tank (August 2025) |
| Spring 2025 | Project Apis M survey confirms 62% average loss across commercial operations | Food Tank (August 2025) |
| Mid-2025 | USDA-ARS publishes preliminary findings on amitraz resistance (under peer review) | USDA-ARS (2025) |
| August 2025 | Economic impact assessments quantify $600M+ in direct losses | Food Tank (August 2025) |

### Geographic Distribution

The 2025 losses were not uniform across the United States. Commercial migratory beekeeping operations — those that move colonies seasonally to follow pollination contracts — experienced disproportionate losses. These operations typically winter colonies in southern states (California, Florida, Texas) before moving them north for spring and summer crops. The stress of transportation, combined with exposure to diverse agricultural chemical environments across multiple states, compounds biological pressures on colonies.

### The Migratory Beekeeping System

Understanding the 2025 crisis requires understanding the migratory beekeeping system that underpins U.S. agricultural pollination. Unlike European beekeeping, which tends to be stationary, the majority of U.S. commercial pollination is performed by colonies trucked thousands of miles per year:

| Season | Location | Crop/Activity | Colony Stress Factors |
|--------|----------|--------------|----------------------|
| January-February | California Central Valley | Almond pollination | Transportation stress, crowding with colonies from other operations |
| March-April | Pacific Northwest, Southeast | Cherry, apple, blueberry pollination | Additional transport, exposure to new agricultural chemicals |
| May-June | Upper Midwest, Northern Plains | Clover, alfalfa, canola | Long-distance transport, reduced forage diversity |
| July-August | Northern states | Honey production, summer buildup | Best forage period; colonies must recover for winter |
| September-October | Southern states | Overwintering preparation | Mite treatment window, nutritional supplementation |
| November-December | California, Florida, Texas | Overwintering | Low activity, critical period for colony survival |

This system concentrates colonies from across the country into single locations (particularly California's Central Valley in February), creating conditions for disease transmission between operations. A beekeeper from Montana whose colonies winter in California may bring Varroa genetics — including resistant strains — back to Montana in spring, spreading resistance patterns nationally within a single season.

### The Beekeeping Workforce

The 2025 crisis has human dimensions beyond economic statistics. The United States has approximately 2,600 commercial beekeeping operations (those managing 500+ colonies) that collectively maintain the majority of colonies used for pollination services. These operations are typically family businesses with thin profit margins even in normal years. A 62% loss rate represents not just colony death but potential business failure — the cost of rebuilding colony numbers from a 62% loss exceeds many operations' financial reserves.

The beekeeping workforce also includes an estimated 100,000-150,000 hobbyist and sideliner beekeepers (those managing fewer than 500 colonies). While these smaller operations contribute less to commercial pollination, they maintain genetic diversity, provide local pollination services, and serve as a training pipeline for future commercial beekeepers.

## The Varroa Mechanism

### Biology of Varroa destructor

*Varroa destructor* is an external parasitic mite that feeds on the organs of honey bees (*Apis mellifera*), specifically targeting the fat body — a tissue analogous to the liver in vertebrates that plays critical roles in immune function, nutrient storage, and detoxification. Originally a parasite of the Asian honey bee (*Apis cerana*), *Varroa* jumped host species to European honey bees in the mid-20th century and has since spread globally.

### Mechanism of Colony Damage

The mite inflicts damage through two primary pathways:

**1. Direct Parasitism**

Varroa mites feed on developing pupae within sealed brood cells and on adult bees. Feeding on the fat body compromises the bee's immune system, reduces lifespan, impairs navigation, and degrades the bee's ability to thermoregulate the hive. Bees parasitized during development emerge smaller, with reduced wing area and compromised flight capability.

**2. Viral Transmission**

Varroa acts as a biological vector for multiple bee viruses:

| Virus | Abbreviation | Primary Impact | Transmission Route |
|-------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Deformed Wing Virus | DWV | Wing deformity, inability to fly, shortened lifespan | Mite feeding during pupal stage |
| Acute Bee Paralysis Virus | ABPV | Rapid adult bee death, trembling, paralysis | Mite feeding on adults |
| Israeli Acute Paralysis Virus | IAPV | Colony depopulation, paralysis | Mite feeding and trophallaxis |
| Chronic Bee Paralysis Virus | CBPV | Trembling, flightlessness, dark and hairless bees | Mite facilitation |
| Black Queen Cell Virus | BQCV | Queen larva death | Mite feeding in queen cells |

The synergy between mite feeding and viral load is critical. Individual viruses at low titers may not cause colony-level effects, but Varroa amplifies viral replication by compromising immune function while simultaneously injecting viral particles during feeding.

### Amitraz Resistance

> **SC-COL FLAG:** The following findings are from a USDA-ARS paper that is **under peer review** as of 2025. Results are presented as preliminary pending completion of the peer review process.

USDA-ARS researchers investigating the 2025 colony collapses identified resistance to amitraz — the most widely used miticide in U.S. beekeeping — as a primary factor in the severity of losses (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review). Key preliminary findings:

- All Varroa mites sampled from collapsed colonies showed resistance to amitraz-based miticide treatments (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review)
- Resistance mechanisms appear to involve altered target-site sensitivity in the mite's octopamine receptors (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review)
- Beekeepers who relied exclusively on amitraz-based treatments (e.g., Apivar) experienced higher loss rates than those using integrated pest management with rotational chemistries (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review)

### Chemical Control History

The history of Varroa miticide use illustrates a recurring pattern of resistance development:

| Treatment Era | Active Ingredient | Class | Period of Efficacy | Resistance Documented |
|--------------|-------------------|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| First generation | Fluvalinate (Apistan) | Pyrethroid | Late 1980s-1990s | Mid-1990s |
| Second generation | Coumaphos (CheckMite+) | Organophosphate | Late 1990s-2000s | Early 2000s |
| Third generation | Amitraz (Apivar) | Formamidine | 2000s-2020s | 2025 (under peer review) |
| Organic alternatives | Oxalic acid, formic acid, thymol | Organic acids/botanicals | Ongoing | Limited resistance documented |

The development of resistance to each successive chemical class follows predictable evolutionary dynamics: widespread use creates selection pressure, resistant mite genotypes survive and reproduce, and within a decade the treatment loses efficacy.

### Multifactorial Collapse

While the USDA 2025 paper highlights amitraz resistance as a primary factor, colony collapse is understood to be multifactorial. Contributing stressors documented in the scientific literature include:

- **Varroa destructor parasitism and associated viral load** (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review)
- **Pesticide exposure** — neonicotinoids, fungicides, and miticide residues in hive wax (USDA PDP, 2024)
- **Nutritional stress** — reduced floral diversity in agricultural landscapes (Kline & Joshi, 2024)
- **Habitat loss** — conversion of forage land to monoculture agriculture (Pollinator.org, 2024)
- **Climate disruption** — phenological mismatches between bloom timing and colony development cycles (Jacobson et al., 2025)
- **Migratory stress** — physical transport, novel pathogen exposure, and repeated chemical environments

No single factor fully explains colony collapse. The severity of the 2025 event likely reflects the convergence of amitraz resistance with existing chronic stressors.

## Long-term Pollinator Decline

### Managed Honey Bee Population Trajectory

The 2025 crisis exists within a decades-long decline in U.S. managed honey bee populations:

| Year | Estimated U.S. Managed Colonies | Source |
|------|-------------------------------|--------|
| 1940s | ~5,000,000 | Pollinator.org / USDA APHIS |
| 1990 | ~3,200,000 | USDA APHIS |
| 2008 | ~2,440,000 | USDA APHIS |
| 2023 | ~2,680,000 | Pollinator.org / USDA APHIS |
| Spring 2025 (post-loss) | Estimated below 2,000,000 | Derived from 62% loss rate (Food Tank, August 2025) |

The slight recovery from 2008 to 2023 reflects deliberate colony splitting and queen rearing by commercial beekeepers — an artificial maintenance strategy that masks underlying biological decline. Each surviving colony is split into multiple smaller colonies each spring, a practice that maintains colony counts but reduces per-colony strength and genetic diversity.

### The True Cost of Annual Losses

Annual colony loss rates in the United States have averaged between 30-45% over the past decade, with beekeepers compensating through aggressive splitting and queen replacement. The 62% loss rate in 2024-2025 overwhelmed this compensatory capacity (Food Tank, August 2025). At these loss levels, the biological and financial resources required to rebuild colony numbers exceed many operations' reserves.

## Extinction Risk Assessment

### PNAS 2025 Analysis

In April 2025, Jacobson et al. published a comprehensive extinction risk assessment in the *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* (PNAS, Volume 122, Issue 14). The study assessed approximately 1,600 vertebrate and insect pollinator species in North America.

**Key findings:**

- More than one in five assessed pollinator species face elevated extinction risk (Jacobson et al., 2025)
- Insect pollinators showed higher risk levels than vertebrate pollinators (Jacobson et al., 2025)
- Primary threat factors identified: climate change, agricultural intensification, hydrological modification, and urban development (Jacobson et al., 2025)

### Threat Factor Analysis

| Threat Factor | Mechanism | Species Groups Most Affected | Source |
|--------------|-----------|------------------------------|--------|
| Climate change | Phenological mismatch, range contraction, thermal stress | Alpine and northern-range bumble bees, early-season solitary bees | Jacobson et al. (2025) |
| Agricultural intensification | Pesticide exposure, floral resource elimination, monoculture | Ground-nesting bees, specialist pollinators | Jacobson et al. (2025) |
| Hydrological modification | Wetland drainage, altered water tables, riparian loss | Wetland-dependent butterflies, riparian-nesting bees | Jacobson et al. (2025) |
| Urban development | Habitat fragmentation, impervious surfaces, light pollution | Moths, nocturnal pollinators, ground nesters | Jacobson et al. (2025) |

### Species-Level Declines

Several pollinator groups have experienced declines far exceeding the average:

**Bumble Bees (Bombus spp.)**

Multiple *Bombus* species in North America have experienced abundance declines of 96% or greater (Jacobson et al., 2025). The rusty patched bumble bee (*Bombus affinis*) was listed as federally endangered in 2017, the first bee species in the contiguous United States to receive such protection. The American bumble bee (*Bombus pensylvanicus*) has declined by approximately 89% across its range.

**Monarch Butterflies (Danaus plexippus)**

Eastern monarch butterfly populations have declined by more than 80% from historical baselines (Jacobson et al., 2025). The eastern migratory population overwinters in oyamel fir forests and depends on milkweed (*Asclepias* spp.) as obligate larval host plants. Agricultural herbicide use has eliminated an estimated 850 million milkweed stems from the U.S. corn belt.

**Native Bees (non-Apis)**

North America hosts approximately 4,000 native bee species, the vast majority of which are solitary (not colonial) and receive no management attention. These species are responsible for pollination of native plants and contribute substantially to crop pollination. Population monitoring for most native bee species is minimal, making accurate decline estimates difficult.

### Decline Drivers — Detailed Assessment

The Jacobson et al. (2025) PNAS study identified four primary threat categories affecting pollinator populations. Each operates through distinct mechanisms that interact synergistically:

**Climate Change Impacts on Pollinators**

Climate change affects pollinators through multiple pathways:

- **Phenological mismatch:** When warming temperatures cause plants to bloom earlier but pollinator emergence dates do not shift at the same rate, the temporal overlap between flower availability and pollinator activity narrows. This mismatch reduces foraging efficiency and reproductive success for both plants and pollinators.
- **Range contraction:** Species adapted to cool or cold environments (alpine bumble bees, high-elevation solitary bees) lose habitat as temperatures rise. Unlike mobile bird or mammal species, many bee species have limited dispersal capacity and cannot rapidly colonize new suitable habitat.
- **Extreme weather events:** Droughts reduce floral resource availability. Unseasonal freezes kill early-emerging bees. Flooding destroys ground-nesting bee colonies. The increasing frequency and intensity of extreme weather events adds stochastic mortality to chronic decline pressures.
- **Overwintering disruption:** Many pollinators have finely tuned overwintering physiology. Warm winter periods can trigger premature emergence when no floral resources are available, depleting energy reserves. Freeze-thaw cycles damage overwintering pupae and hibernating adults.

**Agricultural Intensification**

Modern agricultural practices affect pollinators through:

- **Direct pesticide toxicity:** Acute exposure to insecticides (especially neonicotinoids) kills bees outright. Field-realistic exposures documented in USDA PDP (2024) data show that multiple pesticide residues occur simultaneously on crops, creating cocktail effects.
- **Sub-lethal pesticide effects:** Below-lethal doses of neonicotinoids impair honey bee navigation, learning, and immune function. Fungicide exposure reduces bumble bee colony growth. Herbicide use eliminates wildflower forage within and adjacent to crop fields.
- **Landscape simplification:** The conversion of diverse agricultural landscapes (small fields, hedgerows, meadows) to large-scale monoculture eliminates the spatial and temporal diversity of floral resources that support diverse pollinator communities.
- **Soil disturbance:** Intensive tillage destroys ground-nesting bee colonies. Approximately 70% of native bee species in North America nest in the ground.

**Hydrological Modification**

- Wetland drainage eliminates habitat for wetland-dependent pollinators (certain butterfly species, bee species that nest in wetland margins)
- Altered water tables change vegetation composition in riparian zones, affecting floral resource availability
- Irrigation systems in agricultural landscapes alter soil moisture regimes, affecting ground-nesting bee survival

**Urban Development**

- Impervious surfaces eliminate ground-nesting habitat
- Artificial light at night (ALAN) disrupts moth navigation and reproduction
- Habitat fragmentation creates isolated patches that cannot sustain minimum viable pollinator populations
- However, urban areas can also provide habitat when managed appropriately (see Urban Pollinator Conservation section)

## The Pollination Dependency

### Agricultural Reliance

The economic and biological dependence of food production on pollinator services is well-documented:

- 87% of flowering plant species globally require animal pollination (CABI Reviews, 2024)
- 87 of the world's leading food crops depend on animal pollination (CABI Reviews, 2024)
- Crops dependent on pollination include almonds, apples, blueberries, cherries, avocados, cucumbers, squash, watermelons, cranberries, and alfalfa (grown as livestock feed)

### Pollination Deficit

A pollination deficit occurs when available pollinator populations are insufficient to fully pollinate a crop, resulting in reduced fruit set, smaller fruit size, or lower seed count. The 2025 colony losses created acute pollination deficits:

| Crop | Region | Estimated Impact | Source |
|------|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Almonds | California | $428M+ in pollination losses | Food Tank (August 2025) |
| Various crops | National | $600M total lost revenue | Food Tank (August 2025) |

California's almond industry — which requires approximately 2 million managed colonies each February — was the first and most severely affected sector. Almond trees are almost entirely dependent on honey bee pollination; without adequate colony numbers, nut set drops sharply.

### Wild Pollinator Contributions

Managed honey bees are not the only pollinators of agricultural crops. Wild bees, flies, beetles, butterflies, moths, and other insects contribute substantially to crop pollination, often providing more efficient per-visit pollination than honey bees. However, wild pollinator populations are themselves declining (Jacobson et al., 2025), and the agricultural landscape offers diminishing habitat for these species.

### Crop-Specific Pollination Vulnerability

The dependency of specific crops on pollination varies from complete (no fruit set without pollinators) to partial (reduced yield or quality without pollinators) to none (wind-pollinated or self-pollinated). Understanding this spectrum is essential for assessing the agricultural impact of pollinator decline:

| Crop | Pollination Dependency | Primary Pollinators | Vulnerability to 2025 Losses |
|------|----------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Almonds | Complete (>90%) | Managed honey bees | Extreme — $428M+ losses (Food Tank, August 2025) |
| Blueberries | High (>80%) | Bumble bees, honey bees | High — wild bumble bee decline compounds managed bee loss |
| Apples | High (>70%) | Honey bees, mason bees, bumble bees | High — PNW and eastern orchards affected |
| Cherries | High (>70%) | Honey bees, mason bees | High — PNW cherry industry affected |
| Watermelon | High (~70%) | Honey bees, squash bees | Moderate — squash bees provide partial backup |
| Cucumbers/squash | Moderate-high (~65%) | Honey bees, squash bees | Moderate — specialist pollinators supplement |
| Cranberries | Moderate (~50%) | Honey bees, bumble bees | Moderate — bog habitat supports some wild bees |
| Soybeans | Low-moderate (~25%) | Honey bees, wild bees | Low — largely self-pollinating |
| Wheat, corn, rice | None (wind-pollinated) | None | None directly, but see food web effects |

Note that even crops classified as "low dependency" may show significant yield improvements from pollinator activity. Soybean yield increases of 6-15% have been documented in pollinator-accessible fields compared to pollinator-excluded controls.

## Non-Bee Pollinators

### Functional Diversity of Pollinators

Pollination ecology extends far beyond honey bees. Different pollinator groups fill distinct functional niches that collectively maintain plant reproduction across diverse ecosystems:

| Pollinator Group | Functional Niche | Key Adaptations | Plants Served |
|-----------------|------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Bumble bees (Bombus spp.) | Deep-flower access, buzz pollination | Long tongues, sonication behavior, cold tolerance | Tomatoes, peppers, blueberries, clovers, legumes |
| Small beetles (various families) | Tropical tree pollination, primitive flowers | Small body size, thigmotactic behavior | Magnolias, pawpaw, some palms |
| Lepidoptera (butterflies) | Long-distance pollen transfer, open flowers | Long proboscis, diurnal flight | Wildflowers, milkweed, asters, coneflowers |
| Lepidoptera (moths) | Nocturnal pollination | Nocturnal activity, long tongues, hovering | Yuccas (obligate), evening primrose, moonflower, tobacco |
| Hoverflies (Syrphidae) | Open-access flower pollination | Flower mimicry, hover flight | Umbellifers, composite flowers, many crops |
| Hummingbirds | Long-distance pollination, tubular flowers | Hovering flight, high energy metabolism, color vision | Trumpet vine, columbine, fuchsia, penstemons |
| Bats (Chiroptera) | Long-distance nocturnal pollination | Echolocation, large pollen loads, flight range | Agave (tequila), saguaro cactus, durian, banana (wild) |
| Wasps (various) | Fig pollination (obligate), generalist | Small body size, specific host relationships | Figs (obligate), orchids, some crops |

### Moth Pollination — The Night Shift

Nocturnal pollination by moths is increasingly recognized as a critical and underestimated component of pollination networks. Moths visit flowers at night when competition with diurnal pollinators is absent, and many moth species are effective long-distance pollen transporters. The yucca moth (*Tegeticula* spp.) maintains one of the most precisely co-evolved obligate mutualisms in ecology — without the moth, yucca cannot reproduce, and without yucca, the moth cannot reproduce.

Moth populations globally have declined due to artificial light at night (ALAN), habitat loss, and pesticide exposure. Declines in moth pollination may have cascading effects on plant reproduction that are not captured by daytime pollinator surveys.

### Hummingbird and Bat Pollination

Vertebrate pollinators serve ecological niches that insects cannot fill. Hummingbirds operate across a wider temperature range and in higher wind conditions than most insect pollinators, providing pollination services in montane and coastal environments. Nectar-feeding bats in the genera *Leptonycteris* and *Glossophaga* are the primary pollinators of commercially important agave and saguaro cactus, as well as many tropical fruit species.

The lesser long-nosed bat (*Leptonycteris yerbabuenae*), once listed as federally endangered, was delisted in 2018 following successful conservation efforts — one of the few pollinator recovery success stories in North America.

### Fly Pollination — An Overlooked Contribution

Flies (Diptera) are the second most important group of pollinators after bees, yet receive minimal conservation attention. Key pollinating fly groups include:

| Fly Family | Common Name | Pollination Role | Habitat |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|---------|
| Syrphidae | Hoverflies | Generalist flower visitors; significant crop pollinators | Open habitats, gardens, meadows |
| Bombyliidae | Bee flies | Flower visitors mimicking bees; desert and arid specialists | Dry habitats, sandy soils |
| Muscidae | House flies and relatives | Generalist flower visitors in cold and wet climates | Ubiquitous |
| Calliphoridae | Blow flies | Pollinate carrion-scented flowers (Rafflesia, skunk cabbage) | Forest understory, wetlands |
| Culicidae | Mosquitoes | Minor pollinators of orchids and small flowers | Wetlands, forest |

Hoverflies are particularly important in the Pacific Northwest, where cool, wet spring conditions limit bee activity. Hoverflies are active at lower temperatures than most bees and visit a wide range of flowers. Their larvae serve additional ecological functions — some species are predators of aphids (beneficial for agriculture), while others are aquatic filter feeders.

### Beetle Pollination — The Ancient System

Beetle pollination (cantharophily) is among the oldest pollination relationships, predating the evolution of bees and butterflies. Modern beetle-pollinated plants include magnolias, pawpaw, water lilies, and some palms. Beetles are typically attracted to flowers by scent rather than visual cues, and many beetle-pollinated flowers have bowl-shaped architectures that accommodate the beetle's body plan.

While beetle pollination is less economically visible than bee pollination, it sustains plant communities — particularly in tropical forests — that form the foundation of terrestrial food webs (see Module 5 and Module 6).

### Valuation of Pollination Services

The economic value of pollination services in the United States is estimated at $15-20 billion annually, encompassing both managed and wild pollinator contributions. Globally, pollination services are valued at $235-577 billion annually (IPBES, 2016). These estimates capture only direct agricultural value and do not include wild plant reproduction — the foundation of terrestrial food webs that support wildlife (see Module 5).

### The Replacement Cost Fallacy

When pollinator populations decline, the immediate response is to increase managed colony numbers through splitting and importation. This approach has significant limitations:

- Colony splitting weakens existing colonies, reducing honey production and disease resistance
- Queen imports introduce genetic bottlenecks and potential novel pathogens
- Managed honey bees cannot replace the pollination services of native bees with different floral fidelities and foraging behaviors
- The cost of manual pollination (hand-pollination) for crops like apple ranges from $5,000-$10,000 per hectare — economically prohibitive at scale

## Restoration Strategies

### Evidence-Based Interventions

Research from Kline and Joshi (2024) in *Frontiers in Ecology and Evolution* provides a systematic framework for pollinator conservation across natural, agricultural, urban, and industrial landscapes. Their analysis identifies interventions that have demonstrated measurable benefit:

**Wildflower Corridor Planting**

Establishing corridors of native wildflowers between habitat patches creates continuous foraging resources and movement pathways for pollinators. Effective corridor design requires:

- Bloom succession across the full growing season (spring through autumn)
- Diversity of flower morphologies to serve different pollinator groups
- Native species appropriate to the local ecoregion
- Minimum corridor width of 3-6 meters for effective use by bee species

**Hedgerow Restoration**

Long-term hedgerow planting studies provide some of the strongest evidence for restoration efficacy. A PMC landscape restoration study found that hedgerow planting increased wild bee abundance by 8% after one year, with species richness increasing significantly by seven years of hedgerow maturity (PMC, 2023). The delay between planting and species richness gains reflects the time required for hedgerow plants to mature, develop floral resources, and provide nesting substrate.

**Artificial Nesting Sites**

Many native bee species are ground-nesting or cavity-nesting, and their populations are limited by nesting substrate availability. Interventions include:

| Nesting Strategy | Target Species | Materials | Placement |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Bee hotels (cavity nesters) | Mason bees, leafcutter bees | Untreated wood blocks with drilled holes (3-10mm diameter) | South-facing, 1-2m height, protected from rain |
| Bare soil patches (ground nesters) | Mining bees, sweat bees | Compacted bare earth, south-facing slopes | Well-drained areas, minimal vegetation |
| Pithy stem bundles | Small carpenter bees | Dried stems of elderberry, sumac, raspberry | Horizontal or slightly angled, sheltered |
| Sand banks | Sand-nesting bees | Exposed sand or sandy soil | South-facing banks, undisturbed areas |

**Managed Road and Powerline Verge Habitat**

Linear infrastructure corridors — road verges, powerline rights-of-way, and railway embankments — collectively represent millions of hectares of land that can be managed for pollinator habitat with minimal cost beyond changes to mowing regimes (Kline & Joshi, 2024). Reducing mowing frequency from monthly to twice annually and seeding with native wildflower mixes converts ecological dead zones into functional habitat.

### Landscape Context

The spatial context of restoration matters. Research indicates that pollinator habitat patches embedded within intact landscapes support higher biodiversity than isolated patches of equivalent size (PMC, 2023). This finding has direct implications for restoration planning: connecting new habitat to existing natural areas through corridors yields better outcomes than creating isolated patches in heavily modified landscapes.

### Integrated Pest Management for Varroa

Given the documented resistance to amitraz (USDA-ARS, 2025, under peer review), the beekeeping community faces an urgent need to transition from single-chemistry reliance to integrated pest management (IPM) approaches:

**Monitoring**

- Regular mite load assessment using alcohol wash or sugar roll methods
- Treatment threshold of 2-3 mites per 100 bees during brood-rearing season
- Monitoring of treatment efficacy through post-treatment mite counts

**Chemical Rotation**

- Rotation between chemical classes to reduce selection pressure for resistance
- Use of organic acid treatments (oxalic acid, formic acid) as part of rotation
- Avoidance of in-hive strip treatments during honey flow to prevent wax contamination

**Cultural Controls**

- Drone brood removal (Varroa preferentially reproduces in drone cells)
- Brood breaks through queen caging or re-queening
- Selection of Varroa-resistant bee genetics (VSH — Varroa Sensitive Hygiene)

**Biological Controls**

- Research into *Stratiolaelaps scimitus* (predatory mite) as biological control agent is ongoing
- Entomopathogenic fungi (*Metarhizium anisopliae*) under investigation
- These approaches remain experimental and are not yet commercially viable at scale

## Urban Pollinator Conservation

### Cities as Pollinator Habitat

Urban areas, often assumed to be pollinator-hostile, can support significant pollinator diversity when managed appropriately. A 2024 study in *Frontiers in Ecology and Evolution* examining bee faunas across Western European cities found that larger cities host richer bee communities than surrounding agricultural land in many cases (Frontiers, 2024). This counterintuitive finding reflects:

- Greater floral diversity in urban gardens compared to agricultural monocultures
- Reduced pesticide exposure relative to intensively farmed land
- Diversity of microhabitats (walls, roofs, parks, gardens, cemeteries)
- Thermal benefits of urban heat islands extending the foraging season

### Urban Rewilding for Pollinators

Emerging urban management approaches that benefit pollinators include:

| Intervention | Location Type | Cost Relative to Conventional | Biodiversity Impact |
|-------------|--------------|------------------------------|-------------------|
| Reduced mowing of road verges | Urban roads | Lower (fewer mowing passes) | Increased bee and butterfly richness |
| Wildflower roundabouts | Traffic infrastructure | Comparable | Local pollinator habitat |
| Green roof installation | Commercial/residential | Higher initial, comparable lifecycle | Nesting and foraging habitat |
| Pollinator-managed solar parks | Industrial/energy | No additional cost | Bee and butterfly habitat under panels |
| Cemetery wildflower areas | Municipal | Lower maintenance | High-quality permanent habitat |

### Impervious Surface Reduction

Reducing impervious surface cover (asphalt, concrete) in urban areas increases both bee and butterfly species richness by making ground-nesting habitat available and improving water infiltration that supports flowering plant growth (Frontiers, 2024). Permeable paving, bioswales, and green infrastructure simultaneously address stormwater management and pollinator habitat needs.

### Community Science and Urban Monitoring

Urban environments provide unique opportunities for pollinator monitoring through community science programs:

- **Garden surveys:** Homeowners and community gardeners can contribute systematic observation data on pollinator species visiting their gardens
- **Bee identification training:** Programs that train urban residents to identify common bee genera (Bombus, Osmia, Halictus, Andrena) expand monitoring capacity beyond professional entomologists
- **Phenology networks:** Coordinated tracking of first bloom dates and first pollinator emergence dates across urban areas provides data on phenological mismatch
- **Nesting surveys:** Monitoring occupancy rates of installed bee hotels and ground-nesting sites generates data on urban pollinator population trends

These programs generate data that would be prohibitively expensive to collect through professional surveys alone, while building public awareness of pollinator ecology and conservation.

## The Pesticide-Pollinator Nexus

### Neonicotinoid Exposure Pathways

Neonicotinoid insecticides — including imidacloprid, clothianidin, and thiamethoxam — are systemic compounds absorbed by plants and distributed through all tissues, including pollen and nectar. This creates exposure pathways for pollinators that are fundamentally different from contact insecticides:

| Exposure Route | Mechanism | Duration of Exposure |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Contaminated pollen | Bees collect pollen from treated crops; systemic residues present | Duration of bloom period |
| Contaminated nectar | Nectar contains systemic residues; bees ingest during foraging | Duration of bloom period |
| Guttation droplets | Treated plants exude concentrated neonicotinoid in morning dew | Early morning, spring |
| Contaminated soil | Seed treatments leach into soil; taken up by adjacent wildflowers | Persistent — years after application |
| Dust drift during planting | Seed treatment coating abrades during mechanical planting; dust drifts | Hours during planting events |
| Contaminated water | Field runoff carries neonicotinoids to streams and puddles | Days to weeks after application |

The systemic nature of neonicotinoids means that exposure occurs even when applications follow label directions, because treated plant pollen and nectar inherently contain the compound. This differentiates neonicotinoid risk from older contact insecticides where careful application timing (avoiding bloom periods) could reduce pollinator exposure.

### Sub-lethal Effects on Colony Function

Sub-lethal neonicotinoid exposure — doses that do not kill individual bees — can impair colony function through cumulative effects on individual behavior:

**Navigation impairment:** Foraging bees exposed to sub-lethal neonicotinoid doses show reduced ability to navigate back to the hive. Studies document increased foraging trip durations, reduced homing success, and altered flight patterns. Lost foragers represent both individual mortality and colony nutritional loss.

**Learning and memory:** Honey bees learn floral associations (which flowers provide nectar) through olfactory conditioning. Neonicotinoid exposure impairs this learning, reducing foraging efficiency and colony food intake.

**Thermoregulation:** Honey bee colonies maintain internal temperatures of 34-36 degrees C for brood rearing. Sub-lethal pesticide exposure impairs individual thermoregulatory behavior, potentially causing brood chilling and developmental abnormalities.

**Immune suppression:** Pesticide exposure suppresses bee immune responses, increasing susceptibility to viral and fungal pathogens. This interacts synergistically with Varroa-transmitted viruses (see The Varroa Mechanism section).

**Queen quality:** Queens exposed to neonicotinoids during development or mating flights show reduced sperm viability, lower egg-laying rates, and shorter lifespans. Queen failure is a leading cause of colony loss.

### Fungicide Interactions

Fungicides — often perceived as pollinator-safe because they target fungi rather than insects — can potentiate insecticide toxicity through enzyme inhibition. Fungicides that inhibit cytochrome P450 detoxification enzymes in bees reduce the bees' ability to metabolize insecticide compounds, effectively increasing the toxic dose. Agricultural landscapes where fungicides and insecticides are applied simultaneously (common in fruit orchards) create higher effective toxicity than either compound alone.

The USDA Pesticide Data Program (2024) documents the co-occurrence of multiple pesticide residues on produce, reflecting the reality that agricultural systems use multiple chemistries simultaneously. The implications for pollinator health from these chemical cocktails are an active area of research.

### Regulatory Landscape

Regulatory responses to neonicotinoid-pollinator interactions vary globally:

| Jurisdiction | Regulatory Action | Status |
|-------------|------------------|--------|
| European Union | Outdoor use ban on imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam (2018) | Implemented; greenhouse use still permitted |
| Canada | Re-evaluation of neonicotinoid registrations | Phase-out of some outdoor uses proposed |
| United States (EPA) | Interim ecological risk assessments completed; no outdoor use ban | Ongoing re-registration review |
| United States (states) | Various state-level restrictions (Connecticut, Maryland, others) | Patchwork of state regulations |
| Brazil | No restrictions on neonicotinoid use | World's largest neonicotinoid consumer |

## Pollinator Genetics and Breeding

### Genetic Diversity in Managed Honey Bees

U.S. managed honey bee populations have limited genetic diversity relative to their global counterparts, reflecting:

- Bottleneck events during initial importation from Europe (primarily Italian and Carniolan stocks)
- Selection pressure from queen breeding programs that prioritize honey production and temperament
- Geographic isolation from feral populations (feral survival rates have declined as Varroa spread)
- Trade restrictions on bee imports that limit genetic influx

Reduced genetic diversity limits the ability of managed bee populations to adapt to novel stressors — including new Varroa resistance patterns, emerging diseases, and climate-driven changes in floral resource timing.

### Varroa-Resistant Bee Genetics

Several genetic approaches to Varroa resistance are being developed:

| Trait | Mechanism | Development Stage |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| Varroa Sensitive Hygiene (VSH) | Worker bees detect and remove parasitized pupae | Available commercially; limited adoption |
| Grooming behavior | Adult bees physically remove mites from themselves and nestmates | Present in some populations; variable expression |
| Brood interruption tolerance | Colonies that naturally break brood cycles, disrupting Varroa reproduction | Under selection; trade-off with productivity |
| Russian bee genetics | Longer co-evolutionary history with Varroa (from *A. cerana* range) | Available; show improved survival but lower honey production |
| Survivor stock | Feral populations that have survived without treatment for 10+ years | Under study; mechanisms not fully characterized |

The trade-off between Varroa resistance and commercial productivity traits (honey yield, gentleness, spring buildup speed) has limited adoption of resistant genetics in commercial beekeeping. However, the 2025 losses may shift the calculus — a colony that survives with Varroa is more productive than a dead colony, regardless of peak productivity metrics.

## Monitoring and Data Gaps

### Current Monitoring Programs

Systematic pollinator monitoring in North America remains inadequate relative to the scale of the conservation challenge:

| Program | Scope | Species Covered | Limitations |
|---------|-------|----------------|-------------|
| Bee Informed Partnership | National (U.S.) | Managed honey bees | Commercial operations only; no wild bees |
| Project Apis M | National (U.S.) | Managed honey bees | Survey-based; voluntary participation |
| USGS Native Bee Inventory | Selected sites | Native bees | Limited geographic coverage |
| eButterfly / iNaturalist | Continent-wide | Butterflies, some bees | Citizen science; observation bias toward conspicuous species |
| NEON (National Ecological Observatory Network) | 81 U.S. sites | Ground beetles, mosquitoes (not bees) | Pollinators not a primary target |

### Critical Data Gaps

The Jacobson et al. (2025) PNAS assessment highlights that extinction risk for many pollinator species cannot be determined due to insufficient baseline data. Priority gaps include:

- **Population baselines for most native bee species** — without historical population data, declines cannot be quantified
- **Nocturnal pollinator monitoring** — moths and bats are severely under-surveyed
- **Pesticide sub-lethal effects** — chronic exposure effects on navigation, reproduction, and immune function are poorly characterized at population scales
- **Synergistic stressor interactions** — how multiple simultaneous stressors (mites + pesticides + nutrition + climate) interact at the colony and population level

### The Museum Collection Baseline

Natural history museum collections represent the most complete historical baseline for pollinator populations. Pinned bee specimens from the 19th and 20th centuries document species composition, geographic ranges, and seasonal activity periods that predate modern decline. Current efforts to digitize and georeference museum collections are creating datasets that enable formal comparison of historical and contemporary distributions.

Key applications of museum collection data:

- **Range contraction documentation:** Comparing historical collection localities with current survey data reveals range losses for species like *Bombus occidentalis* and *Bombus affinis*
- **Phenological shift detection:** Comparing collection dates across decades reveals shifts in flight period timing correlated with climate warming
- **Species loss identification:** Species represented in historical collections but absent from modern surveys may be extinct or critically reduced
- **Morphological change detection:** Comparison of body size, tongue length, and wing characteristics between historical and modern specimens can reveal selection pressures

### Technology Needs for Pollinator Monitoring

Emerging technologies that could address monitoring gaps include:

| Technology | Application | Current Status |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| Automated camera traps at flowers | Continuous, standardized monitoring of flower visitors | Prototype stage; machine learning for species ID developing |
| Radar-based insect tracking | Monitoring insect movement at landscape scales | Operational for large insects (moths, dragonflies); resolution limits for small bees |
| eDNA from flowers and water | Detecting pollinator species from environmental DNA traces | Early research; proof-of-concept demonstrated |
| Acoustic monitoring | Detecting bee species by wing-beat frequency | Promising for some species; noise interference limits |
| Satellite-based vegetation monitoring | Tracking floral resource availability at landscape scales | Operational; phenology products from Sentinel-2 and MODIS |

## Regional Considerations — Pacific Northwest

### PNW Pollinator Context

The Pacific Northwest supports unique pollinator communities adapted to its maritime climate, coniferous forests, and seasonal precipitation patterns. Key considerations for PNW pollinator conservation:

**Native Bee Diversity**

The PNW hosts several hundred native bee species — Oregon alone has documented over 600 species through the Oregon Bee Atlas (Oregon State University, Oregon Bee Atlas) — including specialists adapted to native plant communities such as:
- *Osmia* species (mason bees) — important pollinators of orchard crops and native Rosaceae
- *Bombus occidentalis* (western bumble bee) — a species of conservation concern with documented range contraction
- *Bombus sibericus* complex — alpine and subalpine specialists at risk from climate-driven range compression

**Habitat Connectivity**

The PNW landscape mosaic of forest, agriculture, and urban development creates both challenges and opportunities for pollinator conservation. Riparian corridors, forest edges, and meadow systems provide natural movement pathways when maintained (see Module 5 for habitat corridor science).

**Seasonal Foraging Calendar — PNW**

The PNW growing season presents a distinct foraging challenge for pollinators: a wet, cool spring with limited early bloom, a productive summer, and an early autumn decline. Managing for continuous bloom across this compressed season is critical:

| Month | Key PNW Floral Resources | Pollinator Activity |
|-------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| February-March | Red-flowering currant, willows, Oregon grape | Early bumble bee queens, mason bees emerging |
| April-May | Native camas, lupine, serviceberry, wild rose | Peak mason bee activity, bumble bee colony growth |
| June-July | Oceanspray, fireweed, native clover, aster | Peak pollinator diversity, honey production |
| August-September | Goldenrod, late asters, native sunflowers | Late-season foraging, winter preparation |
| October-November | Minimal bloom; ivy (invasive but used) | Colony shutdown, overwintering preparation |

The gap between October and February represents a critical period where pollinator survival depends entirely on stored resources (for honey bees) or overwintering physiology (for native bees that hibernate as adults or pupae). Autumn nectar and pollen quality directly determines overwintering success.

**PNW-Specific Conservation Priorities**

- Preservation and restoration of Garry oak (*Quercus garryana*) meadow habitat — one of the most endangered ecosystems in the PNW, and one of the richest pollinator habitats
- Protection of native prairie remnants in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, and Georgia Basin
- Management of forest harvest practices to maintain canopy gaps and edge habitat that support flowering plants and pollinators
- Integration of pollinator habitat into urban planning across the Portland-Seattle-Vancouver corridor

## Global Pollinator Crisis in Context

### International Comparisons

The U.S. 2025 colony collapse exists within a broader global pattern of pollinator decline. Different regions face distinct combinations of pressures:

| Region | Primary Pressures | Colony Loss Rates | Key Species at Risk |
|--------|------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| North America | Varroa + pesticides + habitat loss | 62% (2024-2025) (Food Tank, August 2025) | *Bombus affinis*, *B. occidentalis*, monarch butterfly |
| European Union | Habitat loss + pesticides (pre-ban) + climate | 20-30% annual average | *Bombus subterraneus*, various solitary bees |
| China | Habitat loss + pesticide intensity | Variable; hand-pollination of pears in Sichuan | Native wild bees poorly surveyed |
| Brazil | Deforestation + pesticides + climate | Stingless bee decline documented | *Melipona* spp. (stingless bees), orchid bees |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | Habitat loss + climate + pesticide introduction | Poorly documented | Wild bee diversity at risk; limited monitoring |
| Australia | Varroa arrival (2022) + fire + climate | Early stages of Varroa impact | Native stingless bees, blue-banded bees |

**Australia's Varroa Emergency:** Australia was the last major beekeeping continent free of *Varroa destructor* until the mite was detected in New South Wales in June 2022. Australia's experience will provide a real-time case study of how a previously Varroa-free apiculture system responds to mite introduction — and whether early aggressive management can prevent the establishment patterns seen elsewhere.

### The Pollination Services Value Chain

The economic architecture of pollination services reveals how collapse at the biological level translates into economic impact:

| Value Chain Stage | Actor | Revenue/Cost | Vulnerability to 2025 Crisis |
|------------------|-------|-------------|------------------------------|
| Colony maintenance | Beekeeper | $200-400/colony/year | Direct — colony loss is asset loss |
| Pollination contracts | Beekeeper → grower | $150-200/colony (almonds) | Direct — fewer colonies = fewer contracts |
| Crop production | Grower | Varies by crop | Direct — reduced pollination = reduced yield |
| Processing and distribution | Processors, retailers | Downstream from crop | Indirect — supply reduction increases costs |
| Consumer | End consumer | Food prices | Indirect — price increases for pollinator-dependent crops |
| Ecosystem services | No direct market actor | $15-20B/year U.S. estimated | Not captured in market transactions |

The 2025 crisis exposed the fragility of this value chain. A single biological event (Varroa resistance to amitraz) cascaded through every stage, from beekeeper insolvency to consumer price increases for almonds and other pollinator-dependent crops.

### Historical Colony Collapse Events

The 2025 event is not the first mass die-off of managed honey bee colonies, though its scale is unprecedented in the modern era:

| Year/Period | Event | Scale | Primary Identified Cause |
|-------------|-------|-------|--------------------------|
| 1903-1905 | "Isle of Wight disease" | UK colony losses | Acarine mite (*Acarapis woodi*) |
| 1964-1965 | US colony losses | Significant but regional | Pesticide poisoning (organochlorines) |
| 1995-1996 | French colony losses | Regional; >10,000 colonies | Imidacloprid (neonicotinoid) introduction |
| 2006-2007 | Colony Collapse Disorder (CCD) | ~30% US colonies/year | Multifactorial; Varroa + viruses + pesticides + nutrition |
| 2012-2013 | Elevated winter losses | ~45% US colonies | Varroa + neonicotinoids + nutritional stress |
| 2024-2025 | Amitraz resistance crisis | 62% US commercial colonies (Food Tank, August 2025) | Varroa amitraz resistance + multifactorial stressors |

Each historical event has been followed by partial recovery — beekeepers split surviving colonies, import queens, and rebuild numbers. However, each recovery starts from a lower genetic and economic baseline than the previous one. The 2025 event's severity may exceed the recovery capacity of many operations.

### Looking Forward — Research Priorities

The 2025 crisis has sharply clarified research priorities for pollinator conservation:

**Immediate priorities (1-3 years):**
- Development and validation of new Varroa control chemistries or application methods
- Accelerated breeding of Varroa-resistant honey bee genetics (VSH, grooming behavior, Russian genetics)
- Economic support structures for beekeepers rebuilding after catastrophic losses
- Monitoring of amitraz resistance spread through mite populations nationally

**Medium-term priorities (3-10 years):**
- National native pollinator monitoring program comparable to the Breeding Bird Survey
- Landscape-scale pollinator habitat restoration, particularly wildflower corridors and hedgerow networks
- Integration of pollinator habitat requirements into agricultural policy and land-use planning
- Development of biological control agents for Varroa

**Long-term priorities (10+ years):**
- Understanding and mitigating climate-driven phenological mismatches between pollinators and plants
- Restoration of native pollinator populations to levels that reduce agricultural dependence on managed honey bees
- Development of pollinator-friendly agricultural systems that maintain both food production and ecological function
- International coordination on pesticide regulation and pollinator conservation

## The Human Dimension

### Beekeeper Livelihoods

The 2025 crisis is not only an ecological event — it is a livelihood crisis for thousands of beekeeping families. The U.S. beekeeping industry has consolidated over decades, with a shrinking number of larger operations managing an increasing share of total colonies. The economic structure of modern beekeeping is precarious even in normal years:

| Revenue Source | Percentage of Beekeeper Income | Seasonality |
|---------------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| Pollination contracts | 40-60% for migratory operations | January-July |
| Honey production | 20-40% | July-October |
| Queen and package bee sales | 5-15% | March-June |
| Beeswax and hive products | 2-5% | Year-round |

A 62% colony loss rate eliminates the majority of a beekeeper's productive assets in a single season. Unlike agricultural crop losses — where the farmer still has land that can be replanted next season — colony loss destroys the biological asset itself. Rebuilding from surviving colonies takes 1-2 full seasons under favorable conditions, during which income from both pollination contracts and honey production is severely reduced.

### Food System Resilience

The concentration of U.S. agricultural pollination on a single managed species (the European honey bee) represents a systemic fragility in food production. Alternative pollination strategies that could increase resilience include:

**Diversification of managed pollinators:**
- Blue orchard mason bees (*Osmia lignaria*) for tree fruit pollination (PNW orchards)
- Alkali bees (*Nomia melanderi*) for alfalfa seed production (already used in parts of the western U.S.)
- Bumble bee colonies (*Bombus impatiens*) for greenhouse tomato pollination
- Leafcutter bees (*Megachile rotundata*) for alfalfa and other crops

**Enhancement of wild pollinator populations:**
- Restoration of native plant habitat adjacent to cropland (see Module 5)
- Reduced pesticide use during bloom periods
- Maintenance of nesting habitat within agricultural landscapes

**Crop breeding for reduced pollination dependency:**
- Development of self-fertile crop varieties where possible
- Selection for extended bloom periods to increase pollination opportunity
- Parthenocarpic varieties (fruit set without fertilization) for some crops

No single alternative can replace the scale and flexibility of managed honey bee pollination services. However, a portfolio approach that reduces exclusive dependence on one species would increase food system resilience to events like the 2025 collapse.

### Public Awareness and Engagement

The 2025 colony collapse has elevated public awareness of pollinator issues beyond the scientific and agricultural communities. Effective public engagement channels include:

- **Backyard habitat creation:** Home gardeners who plant native flowers and provide nesting sites create distributed pollinator habitat across urban and suburban landscapes
- **Community gardens:** Shared growing spaces that incorporate pollinator plantings provide both food production and habitat at the neighborhood scale
- **School programs:** Pollinator observation and habitat projects in schools build ecological literacy in the next generation
- **Farmer outreach:** Extension programs that help agricultural producers adopt pollinator-friendly practices (cover crops, reduced mowing, IPM) reach the landscapes where pollinator losses are most acute
- **Policy engagement:** Informed public participation in local land-use planning, pesticide regulation, and habitat conservation decisions

### The Honeybee-Native Bee Relationship

An important nuance in pollinator conservation is the relationship between managed honey bees and native bee populations. These are not interchangeable, and conservation strategies differ:

| Factor | Managed Honey Bees | Native Bees |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| Management | Actively managed by beekeepers | No management; wild populations |
| Nesting | Provided hive boxes | Ground nests, wood cavities, stems (species-specific) |
| Colony size | 20,000-60,000 per colony | Solitary or small colonies (2-500) |
| Foraging range | Up to 5-10 km from hive | 100 meters to 2 km (species-specific) |
| Floral fidelity | Generalist; visit many flower species | Many are specialists tied to specific plant families |
| Pollination efficiency | Moderate per visit; high volume | Often higher per visit; lower volume |
| Conservation approach | Disease management, nutrition supplementation | Habitat protection, pesticide reduction, native plant restoration |
| Climate adaptation | Managed; can be moved to favorable conditions | Must adapt in place; limited dispersal capacity |
| Genetic management | Queen breeding programs | No management; natural selection |

High densities of managed honey bees can compete with native bees for floral resources, particularly in landscapes where flowers are scarce. Placing large apiaries adjacent to native bee habitat can depress native bee populations through resource competition. Pollinator conservation strategies should address both managed and native bees, recognizing that their needs sometimes conflict.
