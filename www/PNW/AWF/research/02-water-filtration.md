## Global Access and Contamination Context

### The Scale of the Access Problem

Safe drinking water access remains one of the most consequential infrastructure gaps in the contemporary world. More than 2 billion people lack access to safely managed drinking water services, as documented in global water access assessments. The global water purification filters market reached $44.22 billion in 2024, with projections to $62.53 billion by 2034 at a compound annual growth rate of 5.2% (Comprehensive Evaluation of Advanced Water Filtration Techniques, MedRxiv preprint, July 2025). Approximately 65% of the global population lacks access to reliable water filtration (MedRxiv preprint, July 2025).

Regional growth patterns are uneven. The Asia-Pacific region is the fastest-growing market for water filtration technologies, projected at an 11.3% CAGR through 2030 (MedRxiv preprint, July 2025). This growth reflects both expanding urban infrastructure investment and recognition of contamination challenges in rapidly industrializing areas.

### Contamination Categories

Drinking water contamination spans four broad categories that require different treatment approaches:

**Biological contamination** — bacteria (Escherichia coli, Salmonella, Campylobacter), viruses (norovirus, hepatitis A, rotavirus), and protozoa (Cryptosporidium, Giardia). Biological contamination is the dominant threat in lower-income settings without adequate sewage infrastructure and is the primary driver of waterborne illness globally.

**Chemical contamination — industrial and agricultural origin** — includes nitrates from fertilizer runoff, heavy metals (arsenic, lead, cadmium, mercury), volatile organic compounds from industrial solvents and petroleum products, and perchlorate from rocket propellant and explosives manufacturing. These contaminants enter water systems through agricultural runoff, industrial discharge, and leaching from contaminated soils.

**Chemical contamination — per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS)** — a class of synthetic compounds used in nonstick coatings, firefighting foams, water-resistant textiles, and food packaging. PFAS contamination of drinking water sources has been documented across the United States and globally. As of April 2024, the EPA finalized the first national drinking water standards for PFAS compounds (EPA, 2024). See dedicated section below.

**Disinfection byproducts (DBPs)** — formed when disinfectants (chlorine, chloramine) react with organic matter naturally present in source water. Trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs) are the primary regulated DBP classes. While disinfection is essential for biological safety, the byproducts it generates are themselves subject to regulatory limits.

**Natural mineral contamination** — arsenic (naturally occurring in groundwater in parts of the American West, Southeast Asia, and elsewhere), fluoride (at concentrations above recommended levels in some natural groundwater sources), radon (in groundwater from granite-bearing aquifers), and hardness minerals (calcium and magnesium, not a health concern at typical levels but affecting water quality and appliance function).

### Drinking Water Quality in the United States

United States municipal water systems operate under EPA Safe Drinking Water Act standards, which set Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) for over 90 regulated contaminants. Municipal systems provide annual Consumer Confidence Reports (CCRs) to customers documenting measured contaminant levels against MCLs. Private well owners — estimated at approximately 43 million Americans (EPA) — are responsible for their own water testing and treatment.

The Environmental Working Group's Tap Water Database documents water quality data from utilities nationwide and provides a Water Filter Guide identifying certifications relevant to specific contaminants (EWG, Tap Water Database). The EWG analysis contextualizes MCL compliance against health guidelines that in some cases are more stringent than regulatory limits — a distinction relevant to consumer decision-making.

## Technology Deep Dives

### Reverse Osmosis

Reverse osmosis applies hydraulic pressure — typically 40–80 psi for residential systems — to force feedwater through a semi-permeable membrane with pore sizes around 0.0001 microns (0.1 nanometers). At this scale, the membrane rejects dissolved ions, molecules, and essentially all contaminants larger than water molecules themselves. The concentrated rejected contaminants are flushed to drain in the concentrate (waste) stream, while purified permeate passes through.

**Contaminant removal profile:** RO is among the most comprehensive single-technology solutions for dissolved chemical contaminants. Documented removal targets include arsenic, hexavalent chromium, nitrates and nitrites, perchlorate, lead and other heavy metals, radionuclides, and PFAS compounds. The EPA's guidance on PFAS filter certification confirms RO as one of three technology categories with documented significant PFAS reduction (EPA, 2024).

**The water waste problem:** Traditional under-sink RO systems produce 3–5 gallons of concentrate waste for every gallon of purified water — a significant efficiency concern in water-scarce regions and a cost concern where water rates are high. Newer tankless high-efficiency RO systems (including designs rated at 800 gallons per day flow) approach 1:1 permeate-to-waste ratios through pressurized recirculation and permeate pump designs. The availability of higher-efficiency systems represents a meaningful advance for household applications where water waste was a barrier to adoption.

**Mineral removal consideration:** RO removes dissolved minerals including calcium and magnesium. In regions where these minerals contribute to dietary intake, or where their absence affects taste preference, remineralization filters (typically calcite or magnesium post-filters) are sometimes added downstream of the RO membrane.

### Granular Activated Carbon (GAC)

Granular activated carbon uses the same adsorption principle as activated carbon in air filtration (see Module 01) applied to dissolved species in water. Loose carbon granules in a bed allow water to flow through. The enormous internal surface area — 500–1,500 m² per gram — provides binding sites for organic compounds.

**Performance factors:** Contact time between water and carbon is the critical variable. Channeling — where water finds preferential pathways through the carbon bed without contacting much of the media — reduces effective contact time and degrades performance. Well-designed GAC vessels maintain uniform flow distribution. Empty Bed Contact Time (EBCT) is the engineering parameter used to size commercial GAC systems for target contaminant removal.

**PFAS reduction:** GAC can achieve significant PFAS concentration reduction, particularly for longer-chain PFAS compounds. Shorter-chain PFAS compounds (which have become more prevalent as regulation of longer-chain compounds drove industry substitution) adsorb less efficiently to GAC. The EPA's PFAS drinking water standards document notes that GAC, ion exchange, and RO can significantly reduce PFAS (EPA, 2024).

**THM and DBP removal:** GAC is the primary technology for removing chlorination byproducts from municipal water. Trihalomethanes (chloroform, bromodichloromethane, dibromochloromethane, bromoform) and haloacetic acids adsorb effectively to activated carbon, with performance dependent on EBCT and carbon age.

### Carbon Block Filters

Carbon block filters compact activated carbon into a solid matrix, eliminating channeling by forcing water through a uniform compressed medium. The smaller effective pore size of carbon block media (typically 0.5–5 microns, versus no fixed pore size in GAC) provides particle filtration in addition to adsorption.

**Lead removal:** Carbon block filters at 0.5-micron certification are effective against particulate lead (lead particles from pipe corrosion). Some carbon block formulations also address dissolved ionic lead through a combination of adsorption and ion exchange mechanisms. NSF/ANSI Standard 53 certification for lead reduction covers both particulate and dissolved lead removal claims.

**PFAS performance:** Carbon block filters generally outperform GAC for PFAS reduction due to longer contact time and more uniform flow. NSF/ANSI Standard 58 and 53 certifications cover specific contaminant reduction claims; certification standards for PFAS-specific performance were undergoing update as of 2024 (EPA, 2024).

### UV Treatment

UV water treatment applies ultraviolet radiation at 254nm wavelength — the germicidal peak — to water flowing through a treatment chamber. The mechanism is identical to air UV-C treatment (Module 01): photons induce DNA damage in microorganisms, preventing replication. At appropriate UV doses (measured in mJ/cm²), UV treatment provides high-level inactivation of bacteria, viruses, and protozoa.

**Turbidity requirement:** UV transmittance in water decreases with turbidity. Suspended particles shield microorganisms from UV exposure, reducing effective dose. Pre-filtration (at minimum sediment filtration; ideally to 5 microns or finer) is required before UV treatment to ensure adequate transmittance. This makes UV most appropriate as a downstream stage in a multi-barrier system.

**No chemical removal:** UV provides no protection against chemical contaminants — not lead, not nitrates, not PFAS, not VOCs. In biological contamination scenarios (private wells, travel, emergency preparedness), UV is highly effective; in mixed biological-chemical contamination scenarios, it must be paired with appropriate chemical removal technology.

**Cryptosporidium:** UV treatment is uniquely effective against Cryptosporidium parvum, a protozoan parasite with documented resistance to chlorine disinfection at typical municipal treatment concentrations. For groundwater and surface water supplies where Cryptosporidium is a concern, UV provides disinfection assurance that chlorination alone cannot.

### Ion Exchange Resins

Ion exchange resins are polymer beads bearing charged functional groups that exchange ions between the resin surface and the water flowing through. Cation exchange resins remove positively charged ions (calcium, magnesium for softening; lead, cadmium); anion exchange resins remove negatively charged ions (nitrates, sulfates; PFAS compounds which carry negative charge at typical water pH).

**Nitrate removal:** Anion exchange resins are the most established technology for nitrate removal in drinking water. Nitrate is a regulated contaminant under the Safe Drinking Water Act (MCL 10 mg/L as nitrogen) with particular relevance to infant health. GAC and RO have limited nitrate removal; ion exchange is the primary technology for systems specifically targeting nitrate.

**PFAS removal:** Anion exchange resins — particularly single-use resins designed for PFAS — have demonstrated significant PFAS reduction for both long-chain and short-chain PFAS compounds in bench and pilot studies. The EPA's guidance confirms ion exchange as one of three technology categories with documented PFAS reduction (EPA, 2024). Single-use resins eliminate the regeneration brine disposal concern but create spent resin waste requiring disposal.

**Regeneration and waste:** Regenerable resins restore capacity using concentrated brine solutions (sodium chloride or sodium bicarbonate). The resulting regenerant waste stream is concentrated in whatever the resin removed — nitrate, heavy metals, or PFAS. Proper disposal of this concentrate is a regulatory consideration for larger systems.

### Electrochemical Oxidation

Electrochemical oxidation is an emerging technology for PFAS destruction — mineralizing PFAS compounds rather than concentrating them (as RO and ion exchange do). The technology applies electrical potential to electrodes in contact with PFAS-contaminated water, generating reactive radicals that cleave the carbon-fluorine bonds that give PFAS compounds their environmental persistence.

**2024 status:** Electrochemical oxidation for PFAS destruction advanced in laboratory and bench-scale demonstrations through 2024, with publications documenting high destruction efficiency under controlled conditions. Key challenges for scaling include energy intensity (the C–F bond is extremely strong and requires significant energy to cleave), electrode fouling, and matrix interference from other water constituents. As of early 2025, electrochemical oxidation for PFAS is not commercially deployed at residential scale but is being evaluated in pilot applications for industrial remediation and PFAS-concentrate destruction downstream of ion exchange or RO systems.

### Solar SODIS

Solar water disinfection (SODIS) fills clear plastic (PET) or glass containers with clear water and exposes them to direct sunlight for 6–48 hours (depending on cloud cover and UV intensity). Pathogen inactivation occurs through both direct UV-A radiation effects and thermal effects as water temperature rises. The WHO documents SODIS as an evidence-based household water treatment option appropriate for low-resource settings.

**Limitations:** SODIS is batch-limited, weather-dependent, and provides no protection against chemical contamination. Turbid water must be pre-settled or pre-filtered before SODIS treatment. The method addresses biological safety only. Despite these limitations, SODIS has meaningful public health value in settings where alternatives are unavailable or unaffordable.

## PFAS: The Emerging Priority

### What PFAS Are

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances are a class of synthetic chemicals comprising thousands of individual compounds characterized by carbon-fluorine bonds — among the strongest bonds in organic chemistry. This stability makes PFAS resistant to environmental degradation (earning the informal label "forever chemicals") and resistant to conventional water treatment processes. PFAS have been manufactured and used in a wide range of industrial and consumer applications since the 1940s, including nonstick cookware coatings, food packaging with grease-resistant linings, water-resistant textiles, and aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used in firefighting.

### EPA April 2024 Drinking Water Standards

The EPA finalized its first national PFAS drinking water standards in April 2024 — a regulatory milestone that established Maximum Contaminant Levels for six PFAS compounds (EPA, 2024):

| Compound | MCL | Basis |
|----------|-----|-------|
| PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid) | 4 parts per trillion (ppt) | Kidney and testicular cancer; thyroid disease; immune effects |
| PFOS (perfluorooctane sulfonate) | 4 parts per trillion | Same health basis as PFOA |
| PFNA (perfluorononanoic acid) | 10 parts per trillion | Combined hazard index approach |
| PFHxS (perfluorohexane sulfonate) | 10 parts per trillion | Combined hazard index approach |
| HFPO-DA (GenX) | 10 parts per trillion | Combined hazard index approach |
| PFBS (perfluorobutane sulfonate) | 10 parts per trillion | Combined hazard index approach |

*Health basis descriptions above reflect EPA regulatory documentation, not independent health claims.*

The total cost of system upgrades to comply with PFAS standards was estimated at $4.7 billion for affected water utilities (EPA, 2024).

### Filter Certification Status

At the time the EPA finalized PFAS drinking water standards in April 2024, certification standards for PFAS-specific filter performance had not yet been fully updated to reflect the new MCLs. The EPA's guidance document on identifying PFAS-reducing filters noted this gap: existing NSF/ANSI certifications for some contaminant claims predate current PFAS understanding (EPA, 2024).

The EPA's practical guidance identifies three technology categories with documented significant PFAS reduction capability:

1. **Granular Activated Carbon (GAC)** — effective particularly for longer-chain PFAS
2. **Ion Exchange Resins** — effective for both long- and short-chain PFAS with appropriate resin selection
3. **Reverse Osmosis (RO)** — broadest PFAS removal across compound classes

The EWG's Tap Water Database and Water Filter Guide provide consumer-accessible information on filter certifications and water utility PFAS measurements (EWG, Tap Water Database).

### PFAS Chain Length and Treatment Implications

The shift in PFAS manufacturing from long-chain compounds (PFOA, PFOS) — driven by voluntary phase-out and regulatory pressure beginning in the early 2000s — toward shorter-chain replacements has complicated treatment. Shorter-chain PFAS adsorb less efficiently to activated carbon. Ion exchange resins designed for PFAS show better performance across the chain length spectrum. RO membranes reject PFAS by size exclusion and are effective regardless of chain length. This treatment dynamic has influenced the design of PFAS-targeting filtration systems.

PFAS contamination spans water, food, and air pathways. EPA PFAS drinking water standards (this module) address the water route. PFAS in food is addressed in Module 03 (Food Safety), covering food packaging migration and PFAS found in agricultural produce irrigated with PFAS-contaminated water. ## Desalination Advances

### Global Desalination Context

Desalination — removing salt and dissolved minerals from seawater or brackish water — is an increasingly important water supply strategy in water-scarce coastal regions. As of 2024, approximately 21,000 desalination plants operate globally, providing drinking water and agricultural water supply in the Middle East, North Africa, parts of Asia, the Mediterranean, and water-stressed coastal regions worldwide.

Conventional seawater desalination uses reverse osmosis membranes (SWRO — seawater reverse osmosis) at higher operating pressures (800–1,200 psi) than freshwater RO applications. Energy consumption is the dominant cost and environmental consideration in desalination operations.

### University of Michigan Boron Removal Advance (Nature Water, January 2025)

A significant technical advance in desalination was reported by researchers at the University of Michigan in *Nature Water* in January 2025: a new electrode-based approach to boron removal from desalinated seawater (Pan et al., 2025).

**The boron problem:** Seawater contains approximately 4–5 mg/L of boron — roughly twice the WHO recommended limit for drinking water (2.4 mg/L) and 5–12 times the tolerance threshold for sensitive agricultural crops (Pan et al., 2025). Conventional SWRO membranes do not reject boron efficiently at seawater desalination operating conditions because boric acid (the dominant boron form at typical seawater pH) has a small, uncharged molecular structure that passes through RO membranes.

**Conventional boron management:** Current desalination facilities address boron through a combination of elevated pH operation (converting boric acid to borate ion, which is better rejected by RO membranes), multiple RO passes, or ion exchange post-treatment. These approaches add operational complexity, cost, and energy consumption.

**The electrode-based approach:** The University of Michigan team developed an electrochemical method that selectively removes boron from desalinated water using electrode materials optimized for boron sorption under applied potential. The approach demonstrated effective boron reduction with lower energy and chemical input compared to conventional methods under laboratory and bench-scale conditions.

**Funding and significance:** The research was funded by the National Alliance for Water Innovation (NAWI) and the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) (Pan et al., 2025). The NAWI is a DOE-sponsored research hub specifically focused on enabling cost-effective treatment of non-traditional water sources, of which brackish groundwater and seawater desalination are primary targets. Advancement of boron removal technology has direct application to expanding agricultural use of desalinated water in coastal regions where freshwater scarcity is a constraint on food production.

**Current stage:** As of the January 2025 publication, the electrode-based approach had been demonstrated at bench scale. Engineering scale-up, long-term electrode stability testing, and integration with existing desalination plant infrastructure represent the next research and development phases.

### Energy Recovery in Modern Desalination

Energy recovery devices (ERDs) — particularly pressure exchangers — recover energy from the high-pressure concentrate stream as it exits the RO membrane array. Modern SWRO plants with advanced ERDs achieve specific energy consumption of 3–4 kWh/m³ of product water, compared to 8–10 kWh/m³ in early SWRO installations. Further reductions remain an active research area.

## Emerging Technologies

### Membrane Distillation

Membrane distillation uses vapor pressure difference across a hydrophobic membrane to drive water vapor transport while rejecting dissolved species. The technology can treat hypersaline waters that exceed the operating limits of pressure-driven RO membranes. Waste heat or solar thermal energy can power membrane distillation, enabling low-carbon operation in appropriate settings. Scaling and membrane wetting are engineering challenges under active investigation.

### Forward Osmosis

Forward osmosis uses osmotic pressure difference (rather than applied hydraulic pressure) to draw water across a semi-permeable membrane from a feed solution into a draw solution of higher osmotic concentration. The draw solution is then treated to recover product water and regenerate the draw solute. Forward osmosis may reduce energy requirements compared to pressure-driven RO for certain feed water compositions. Practical draw solution regeneration at scale remains a challenge.

### Capacitive Deionization (CDI)

Capacitive deionization applies voltage across porous carbon electrodes to adsorb dissolved ions from water. When electrodes are discharged, ions are released in a concentrated brine stream. CDI is potentially energy-efficient for lower-salinity waters (brackish groundwater) and does not require high-pressure membranes. Scale-up and long-term electrode stability are active research questions.

### AI-Optimized Water Treatment

Similar to air purification (Module 01), water treatment is beginning to incorporate machine learning for operational optimization:

- **Predictive coagulant dosing:** Source water quality variability (turbidity, NOM concentration, pH) requires continuous adjustment of coagulant doses in conventional treatment. ML models trained on sensor data can predict optimal doses, reducing chemical use and improving settled water quality.
- **Membrane fouling prediction:** Fouling is the primary operational cost driver in membrane-based treatment. Sensor arrays and ML models can predict fouling onset and optimize cleaning cycles.
- **PFAS monitoring:** Real-time sensors for PFAS detection remain under development. AI integration with conventional TOC, UV254, and turbidity sensors can provide early warning proxies for source water quality changes.

## Technology Selection Guide

### Selection by Primary Concern

| Primary Concern | Recommended Technology | Notes |
|----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Biological contamination (bacteria, viruses) | UV + pre-filtration; or NSF 58-certified RO | UV preferred when no chemical concerns; RO addresses both biological and chemical |
| PFAS reduction | RO, or anion exchange resin, or NSF/ANSI 53/58 certified carbon block | Choose based on co-contaminants and water volume needs |
| Nitrates | Anion exchange resin (most targeted); or RO | GAC does not effectively remove nitrates |
| Lead (household plumbing) | 0.5µm certified carbon block; or RO | Test water first; lead is in plumbing, not typically source water |
| Chlorine taste/odor and DBPs | GAC; carbon block | Effective, low-cost approach for treated municipal water |
| Arsenic (well water) | RO; activated alumina; iron-based media | Test for arsenic type (trivalent vs. pentavalent); affects treatment selection |
| Multiple inorganic contaminants | RO (broadest spectrum) | Paired with post-RO carbon for taste and residual organic removal |
| Biological + chemical mixed | Multi-stage: sediment → carbon block or GAC → UV | Sequence matters; turbid water treated before UV |
| Low-resource emergency | SODIS; boiling | Addresses biological only; chemical contamination unaddressed |

### Water Testing First

Technology selection is most reliable when preceded by water testing identifying actual contaminants. Municipal water consumers have access to annual CCR data. Private well owners are responsible for their own testing. The EPA recommends private well testing at minimum annually, and additionally after flooding events, nearby land use changes, or changes in water taste, odor, or appearance.

NSF International and the Water Quality Association maintain certification databases for water treatment devices, enabling verification of manufacturer performance claims against independent test standards.

## Pacific Northwest Water Quality Context

The PNW's water quality landscape is shaped by its geology, precipitation patterns, and land use — creating regional filtration needs distinct from national averages.

**Municipal Source Water — Exceptionally Clean Baselines**

Portland's Bull Run watershed and Seattle's Cedar River/Tolt River watersheds are among the highest-quality unfiltered municipal water sources in the United States. Both systems draw from protected forested watersheds with restricted public access. Portland received an EPA filtration avoidance waiver for Bull Run, meaning the source water quality is high enough that filtration is not federally required (though UV treatment was added in 2024). Seattle's Cedar River supply serves 1.5 million people from a 90,000-acre protected watershed. These systems deliver water with exceptionally low turbidity and contaminant levels — but household plumbing (lead service lines, copper pipes) can introduce contaminants downstream of the treatment plant.

**PFAS Near Military Installations**

PFAS contamination from aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) used in firefighting training is documented near several PNW military installations. Joint Base Lewis-McChord (JBLM) south of Tacoma, Fairchild Air Force Base near Spokane, and Naval Air Station Whidbey Island have all been identified in the Department of Defense PFAS investigation program. Surrounding communities relying on groundwater wells face PFAS exposure risk. The Washington Department of Ecology maintains a PFAS investigation map tracking contamination sites statewide (WA Ecology, PFAS contamination investigations).

**Arsenic in Eastern Washington and Oregon Groundwater**

Naturally occurring arsenic in volcanic geology affects groundwater in parts of eastern Washington, central Oregon, and the Snake River Plain. The EPA MCL for arsenic is 10 µg/L, but some private wells in these areas exceed that threshold. Reverse osmosis or activated alumina filtration is the standard residential treatment for arsenic in well water. The USGS documents arsenic occurrence patterns in western U.S. groundwater related to volcanic and geothermal geology (USGS, Arsenic in Ground Water of the United States).

**Agricultural Nitrate — Yakima and Willamette Valleys**

Intensive agriculture in the Yakima Valley (WA) and Willamette Valley (OR) contributes nitrate to groundwater through fertilizer application and livestock operations. The Yakima Valley has been the subject of EPA enforcement actions for nitrate contamination of domestic wells, particularly affecting lower-income and agricultural worker communities. Nitrate exceeding the 10 mg/L MCL is a documented concern for private wells in these areas. Anion exchange resins or reverse osmosis are the recommended household treatments for nitrate (see Technology Selection Guide above).

**Seasonal Turbidity and Snowmelt**

PNW water systems fed by mountain snowmelt experience seasonal turbidity spikes during spring melt and heavy rain events. These turbidity events can challenge UV treatment systems (which require clear water for effective pathogen inactivation) and increase sediment load on filters. Multi-barrier systems with sediment pre-filtration are particularly important for PNW surface-water-fed systems during the November-March rainy season.

**High Private Well Prevalence**

Rural communities throughout the PNW — particularly in eastern Washington and Oregon, the San Juan Islands, Whidbey Island, and the Oregon Coast — rely heavily on private wells. Private well owners are responsible for their own water testing and treatment, with no EPA monitoring requirement. The Washington State Department of Health and Oregon Health Authority both maintain well water testing guidance programs, but participation is voluntary. Well water in the PNW may contain arsenic, nitrate, coliform bacteria, or radon depending on local geology.
