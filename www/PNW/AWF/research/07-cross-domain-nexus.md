## Executive Summary

The six modules in this research series document air purification, water filtration, food safety, pollinator health, wildlife habitat, and forest conservation as distinct domains. Examined individually, each reveals pressing challenges: 8.1 million premature deaths annually from air pollution (Health Effects Institute, 2024), more than 2 billion people lacking safely managed drinking water, 62% managed honey bee colony losses in the 2024-2025 season (Food Tank, August 2025), and 8.1 million hectares of forest lost in 2024 alone (Forest Declaration Assessment, 2025). Examined together, a different picture emerges. These are not six separate problems. They are six surfaces of the same system.

The causal pathways linking these domains are not abstract or hypothetical. Neonicotinoid pesticides applied to protect food crops enter soil and water, where they accumulate in pollen and nectar, weakening the pollinators that those same crops depend on for reproduction. Deforestation in the Amazon reduces evapotranspiration that generates roughly 50% of the basin's rainfall, degrading the water supply that sustains downstream agriculture. Agricultural runoff carrying nitrates, PFAS, and pesticide residues contaminates drinking water sources, enters the food supply through irrigated crops, and degrades the aquatic food webs that wildlife depend on. Air pollution damages plant tissues and reduces photosynthetic capacity, weakening the vegetation that anchors terrestrial ecosystems. Each pathway creates a feedback loop: the damage inflicted on one system reduces the capacity of other systems to compensate.

The cross-domain analysis reveals that interventions targeting single domains in isolation are structurally insufficient. Improving water filtration technology does not reduce the agricultural runoff that contaminates the water in the first place. Breeding pollinator-resistant crop varieties does not restore the native plant communities that sustain wild pollinator populations. Planting trees does not reconstitute the mycorrhizal networks, seed dispersal agents, or soil microbiology that make forests function as living filters. The evidence across all six modules converges on a single organizing principle: living systems are the infrastructure. Forests filter water, pollinators produce food, healthy soil processes contaminants, and intact ecosystems regulate the air. When these systems degrade, no engineered replacement operates at comparable scale, efficiency, or cost.

The restoration imperative that emerges from this synthesis is not a policy recommendation — it is a description of physical reality. The systems documented in these six modules are coupled. Their degradation is coupled. Their restoration must be coupled. The evidence base assembled here provides the map; the causal pathways provide the logic; the feedback loops provide the urgency.

## The Living Filter Network

Across the six modules, a unifying concept emerges: Earth's air, water, food, pollination, habitat, and forest systems function as a single interconnected filtration and cycling network. Each system simultaneously depends on and provides services to the others.

### The Network in Operation

| System | What It Filters/Cycles | What It Depends On | Modules |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Forests | Air (carbon sequestration, particulate capture), water (soil filtration, sediment control, stream temperature regulation) | Pollinators for tree reproduction, wildlife for seed dispersal, clean water for root function | 1, 2, 5, 6 |
| Soil biota | Pesticide degradation, nutrient cycling, water retention | Forest litter input, intact mycorrhizal networks, absence of chemical disruption | 3, 5, 6 |
| Pollinators | Crop reproduction, native plant reproduction | Clean air (navigation), uncontaminated food (pollen/nectar free of pesticides), intact habitat (nesting) | 3, 4, 5 |
| Riparian corridors | Water quality (sediment, nutrient, thermal filtration) | Forest canopy for shade, intact soil for filtration, wildlife for nutrient cycling (salmon) | 2, 5, 6 |
| Wildlife food webs | Seed dispersal, pest regulation, nutrient distribution | Native plants for food, clean water, connected habitat corridors | 4, 5, 6 |
| Agricultural systems | Food production | Pollinators for crop set, clean water for irrigation, healthy soil for nutrient supply | 2, 3, 4 |

### The Filter Hierarchy

The network operates in a hierarchy where upstream systems constrain downstream capacity:

1. **Atmospheric layer:** Air quality determines what reaches plant surfaces and enters respiratory systems (Module 1)
2. **Forest canopy layer:** Forests intercept rainfall, regulate transpiration, and generate precipitation (Module 6 feeding Module 2)
3. **Soil layer:** Soil biota process contaminants, cycle nutrients, and regulate water movement (Modules 3, 5, 6)
4. **Water layer:** Surface and ground water quality determines what reaches crops, wildlife, and humans (Module 2)
5. **Biological layer:** Pollinators and wildlife process, transport, and sustain the living systems above (Modules 4, 5)
6. **Food layer:** The end product of all upstream systems — food safety reflects the cumulative health of the entire network (Module 3)

When any layer degrades, the layers below it bear the burden. When forests are removed, soil erodes, water quality declines, and food systems must compensate with engineered filtration. When pollinators decline, food systems must compensate with managed pollination or manual intervention. The living filter network is self-maintaining when intact; when damaged, each broken link increases the load on remaining links.

## Causal Pathway 1: Pesticides to Pollinators to Food Yield

### The Pathway

```
Module 3 (Pesticide application) → Module 4 (Pollinator exposure and decline)
    → Module 3 (Reduced crop yields from pollination deficit)
```

### Evidence Chain

**Step 1 — Pesticide application and residue:** The USDA Pesticide Data Program 2024 documents that more than 75% of non-organic produce samples carry detectable pesticide residues, with 265 distinct compounds detected across 53,692 samples analyzed by EWG (2025). Neonicotinoid insecticides (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam) are systemic — taken up by plants and expressed in pollen and nectar at concentrations documented in USDA monitoring (Module 3).

**Step 2 — Pollinator exposure:** Module 4 documents that neonicotinoids at field-realistic concentrations impair honey bee navigation, learning, and immune function. Sub-lethal exposures reduce bumble bee colony growth. Fungicide co-exposure compounds toxicity. The EU restricted outdoor use of three neonicotinoids in 2018, citing unacceptable risk to bees (Module 3, Module 4). In the U.S., these compounds remain registered for most uses.

**Step 3 — Pollinator decline:** Between summer 2024 and spring 2025, approximately 1.7 million managed honey bee colonies died in the United States, a 62% average loss (Food Tank, August 2025). While the USDA-ARS 2025 paper (under peer review) identified amitraz-resistant Varroa mites as a primary factor, pesticide exposure is documented as a compounding stressor: pesticide residues in hive wax, pollen, and nectar weaken colonies' capacity to resist parasites and disease (Module 4).

**Step 4 — Food yield impact:** 87 of the world's leading food crops depend on animal pollination (CABI Reviews, 2024). The 2025 colony losses produced an estimated $600 million in total lost revenue, with California almond pollination losses exceeding $428 million alone (Food Tank, August 2025). Native pollinators that might provide backup are themselves declining — more than one in five assessed North American pollinator species face elevated extinction risk (Jacobson et al., PNAS, 2025).

### The Circular Trap

This pathway is circular: pesticides are applied to protect food crops, but their impact on pollinators reduces the pollination services those crops depend on, which can reduce yields, which increases pressure to intensify production, which increases pesticide use. The EU IPM mandate (Directive 2009/128/EC) and precision application technologies (Module 3 documents ~80% pesticide reduction from agricultural robotics, PMC 2023) represent attempts to break this cycle.

## Causal Pathway 2: Deforestation to Rainfall to Agricultural Water

### The Pathway

```
Module 6 (Forest loss) → Module 2 (Watershed disruption, water quality decline)
    → Module 3 (Agricultural water impacts on food safety)
```

### Evidence Chain

**Step 1 — Forest loss at scale:** The Forest Declaration Assessment (2025) documents 8.1 million hectares of forest lost in 2024, with an additional 8.8 million hectares degraded. The combined 16.9 million hectares of forest impact in a single year exceeds the area of England (13.0 million hectares). The 2030 target trajectory requires losses below 5 million hectares — the current rate is 63% above that trajectory (Forest Declaration Assessment, 2025).

**Step 2 — Hydrological disruption:** Module 6 documents the forest-water connection: forests generate rainfall through transpiration (the Amazon generates approximately 50% of its own rainfall through evapotranspiration), filter water through soil, regulate flood peaks, recharge groundwater, and maintain stream temperatures. When forests are removed, these functions degrade. Forested watersheds consistently produce higher quality water than deforested or agricultural watersheds (Module 6). Multiple cities — including Portland, OR (Bull Run watershed) and New York City (Catskill/Delaware) — source drinking water from forested watersheds precisely because forest filtration reduces or eliminates the need for engineered water treatment (Module 6).

**Step 3 — Water quality degradation:** Module 2 documents the contaminant burden in water systems: agricultural runoff carrying nitrates, pesticides, and PFAS; industrial discharge of heavy metals and solvents; the first national PFAS drinking water standards finalized by EPA in April 2024 with estimated compliance costs of $4.7 billion for affected utilities (EPA, 2024). The loss of forest watershed filtration increases the contaminant load reaching water treatment systems.

**Step 4 — Food safety impact:** Module 3 documents that agricultural water quality directly affects food safety. The FSMA Produce Safety Rule Subpart E update (April 7, 2025) addresses pre-harvest agricultural water — irrigation water contaminated with pathogens (E. coli, Salmonella), nitrates, or PFAS becomes a pathway for food contamination. PFAS-contaminated irrigation water is documented to result in crop uptake of PFAS compounds (Module 3). The water quality of the irrigation source — shaped upstream by forest cover — determines the contamination risk at the point of food production.

### The Tipping Point Risk

The Amazon tipping point projection illustrates the extreme case of this pathway. If deforestation crosses the 20-25% threshold (currently estimated at approximately 17%, with functional loss higher when degradation is included), the self-reinforcing dieback cycle — less forest, less rain, more fire, less forest — could transition large portions of the Amazon to savanna-like states (Module 6). This would fundamentally alter rainfall patterns across South America, with cascading effects on agricultural water supply throughout the continent.

## Causal Pathway 3: Air Quality to Plant Health to Ecosystem Services

### The Pathway

```
Module 1 (Air pollution) → Module 5/6 (Plant health and forest function degraded)
    → Module 4 (Pollinator habitat and food source loss)
```

### Evidence Chain

**Step 1 — Air pollution at scale:** The Health Effects Institute's *State of Global Air 2024* confirmed 8.1 million premature deaths annually attributable to air pollution (Module 1). The American Lung Association's *State of the Air 2025* found that nearly half the U.S. population — approximately 156 million people — lives in counties that failed at least one air quality measure (Module 1). Wildfire smoke events, increasing in frequency and intensity in the Pacific Northwest, produce PM2.5 concentrations rated "unhealthy" or "very unhealthy" by EPA standards (Module 1).

**Step 2 — Plant and forest impacts:** Ozone (O3) damages plant leaf tissue, reducing photosynthetic capacity and growth. Nitrogen deposition from NOx emissions alters soil chemistry, favoring nitrogen-loving species over the native plant communities that wildlife depends on. Particulate deposition on leaf surfaces reduces light interception. In the Pacific Northwest, wildfire smoke — itself a product of forest loss and climate change — blankets forests with fine particulate matter during the growing season, potentially reducing photosynthesis and seed production. Module 6 documents that PNW old-growth forests are among the most carbon-dense ecosystems on Earth (200-500+ tonnes C/ha above ground), with ecological function dependent on canopy health.

**Step 3 — Ecosystem service degradation:** Air pollution damage to vegetation cascades through ecosystem services. Module 5 documents that native plants support orders of magnitude more insect species than non-native plants — a native oak tree supports approximately 500 caterpillar species while a non-native ornamental may support fewer than 10. When air pollution weakens native vegetation, the insect communities that depend on those plants decline, reducing the prey base for insectivorous birds (Module 5), and reducing the pollinator resources available in natural landscapes (Module 4). Forest canopy damage from pollution or wildfire smoke reduces the shade that maintains stream temperatures for salmon (Module 5, Module 6), connecting air quality directly to aquatic food webs.

### The PNW Wildfire Feedback

In the Pacific Northwest, this pathway has a particularly acute feedback loop: climate change and fire suppression increase wildfire risk (Module 6) → wildfire produces massive PM2.5 emissions that degrade regional air quality (Module 1) → smoke damages the surviving forest canopy and reduces pollinator activity during fire season → weakened forests are more susceptible to future fire. The 2020 Labor Day fires in Oregon burned over 400,000 hectares in a single week (Module 6), producing smoke that blanketed the region for weeks.

## Causal Pathway 4: Water Contamination to Crop Safety to Wildlife Nutrition

### The Pathway

```
Module 2 (Agricultural runoff, PFAS, nitrates) → Module 3 (Food contamination pathways)
    → Module 5 (Wildlife food source contamination)
```

### Evidence Chain

**Step 1 — Water contamination sources:** Module 2 documents multiple contamination pathways entering water systems. Agricultural runoff carries nitrates from fertilizers (regulated at 10 mg/L MCL), pesticide residues (atrazine and others detected in surface water), and sediment. PFAS contamination from industrial discharge, firefighting foam (AFFF), and consumer products has prompted the EPA's first national drinking water standards (April 2024) setting MCLs at 4 parts per trillion for PFOA and PFOS (Module 2). These contaminants enter surface water, groundwater, and ultimately agricultural water supplies.

**Step 2 — Crop contamination:** Module 3 documents that PFAS-contaminated irrigation water results in crop uptake through root systems, with PFAS translocated into edible plant tissues. Concentration and distribution vary by PFAS compound, plant species, and soil chemistry (Module 3). Agricultural land adjacent to AFFF-contaminated military bases and airports has documented elevated PFAS in soil and groundwater (Module 3). Animals consuming PFAS-contaminated water or feed bioaccumulate PFAS in tissues and milk (Module 3). Legacy pesticides — DDT and metabolites detected in 16.2% of leaf lettuce samples, chlordane in 2.3% of canned pumpkin (EWG, 2025, citing USDA PDP data) — demonstrate how persistent contaminants in soil and water continue to enter food decades after the compounds were banned.

**Step 3 — Wildlife food web contamination:** Module 5 documents that wildlife food webs depend on the same water systems and plant communities that human food systems use. Riparian corridors — the vegetated areas along waterways that Module 5 identifies as the most effective natural habitat corridors — are directly exposed to agricultural runoff. Aquatic food webs in contaminated waterways accumulate pollutants through bioconcentration: invertebrates absorb contaminants from water and sediment, fish consume invertebrates and concentrate the contaminants further, and piscivorous wildlife (river otter, bald eagle, osprey, heron) receive the highest doses. DDT's bioaccumulation pathway — the mechanism that caused raptor eggshell thinning and population crashes before the 1972 ban — illustrates how water contamination cascades through the food web to top predators.

### The PFAS Intersection

PFAS contamination uniquely spans all three systems in this pathway. Module 2 covers PFAS in drinking water. Module 3 covers PFAS in food packaging, irrigated crops, and animal products. Module 1 notes PFAS as an airborne particulate from consumer products. Module 5's food web analysis implies that PFAS entering aquatic systems follows the same bioconcentration pathway as legacy persistent organic pollutants. The EPA's integrated PFAS action plan recognizes this multi-pathway exposure, but regulatory limits currently exist only for drinking water (Module 2).

## Additional Pathways

### Pathway 5: Forest Loss to Pollinator Habitat Loss to Plant Reproduction Failure

```
Module 6 (Forest fragmentation) → Module 4/5 (Pollinator habitat and nesting loss)
    → Module 6 (Reduced forest regeneration from pollination failure)
```

Module 6 documents that forest regeneration depends on seed arrival — and seed arrival depends on animal dispersal agents. Module 4 notes that forest edges and canopy gaps provide critical pollinator habitat, and Module 6 confirms that old-growth forests support specialist pollinator communities. When forests are fragmented, edge effects penetrate 50-300 meters into fragments (Module 5), altering microclimate and plant composition. Pollinators that require interior forest conditions lose habitat. Tree species that depend on insect pollination experience reduced reproductive success. The Borneo seedling bottleneck documented by Banin (2025) — persistent 30 years after selective logging — illustrates how disruption of the pollination and dispersal network creates long-term regeneration failure (Module 6).

### Pathway 6: Soil Degradation to Water Quality to Aquatic Food Webs

```
Module 3 (Agricultural soil disturbance, chemical inputs) → Module 2 (Runoff contamination)
    → Module 5 (Aquatic food web disruption)
```

Module 3 documents that intensive tillage, pesticide application, and fertilizer use degrade soil health. Module 5 documents that soil disturbance severs mycorrhizal networks, compacts soil, and reduces water infiltration. Degraded soils have reduced capacity to filter and retain water, increasing runoff volume and contaminant load. This runoff enters streams and rivers, where Module 5 documents the critical connections: riparian shade maintains salmon-bearing stream temperatures, terrestrial insect drop provides approximately 50% of salmonid diet in forested streams, and sediment from erosion smothers fish eggs. The salmon-forest nutrient cycle — marine-derived nutrients transported by spawning salmon into riparian forests — breaks down when either the aquatic or terrestrial half of the cycle is degraded.

### Pathway 7: Humidifier Water Quality to Indoor Air Quality

```
Module 2 (Tap water contaminants) → Module 1 (Aerosolized contaminants in indoor air)
```

Both Module 1 and Module 2 identify this cross-domain link: humidifiers aerosolize tap water, dispersing dissolved minerals, biological contaminants, and chemical residues as fine particles into indoor air. Water filtration quality (Module 2) directly determines indoor air quality (Module 1) in humidified environments. This is a direct technology-to-technology pathway where water treatment choices have immediate air quality consequences.

## Feedback Loops and Reinforcing Cycles

The causal pathways above are not linear chains — they form feedback loops where the output of the system reinforces its own degradation or, in restoration scenarios, its own recovery.

### Degradation Loops

| Loop | Mechanism | Modules | Reinforcement Speed |
|------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| **Deforestation-Drought** | Forest loss reduces rainfall, drought kills more forest, fire increases, more forest lost | 2, 6 | Years to decades |
| **Pesticide-Pollinator** | Pesticide use reduces pollinators, reduced pollination reduces yields, pressure to intensify increases pesticide use | 3, 4 | Annual crop cycles |
| **Contamination-Bioaccumulation** | Water contamination enters food web, bioaccumulates up trophic levels, top predator decline reduces pest control, pest pressure increases chemical use | 2, 3, 5 | Years to decades |
| **Fragmentation-Isolation** | Habitat fragmentation isolates populations, inbreeding reduces fitness, local extinctions reduce seed dispersal and pollination, regeneration fails, fragments degrade further | 4, 5, 6 | Decades to centuries |
| **Fire-Smoke-Forest** | Wildfire produces smoke that damages surviving forest, weakened forest is more fire-susceptible, next fire is more severe | 1, 6 | Annual fire seasons |

### Restoration Loops

| Loop | Mechanism | Modules | Recovery Speed |
|------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Forest-Water-Forest** | Forest restoration improves water quality, better water supports downstream vegetation, expanded vegetation connects habitat | 2, 5, 6 | Decades |
| **Habitat-Pollinator-Plant** | Native plant restoration provides pollinator food and nesting, increased pollinators improve plant reproduction, expanded plant cover provides more habitat | 4, 5 | 1-7 years (PMC, 2023) |
| **Corridor-Gene Flow-Resilience** | Habitat corridors enable gene flow, genetically diverse populations adapt better, adapted populations maintain corridor habitat | 5, 6 | Decades to centuries |
| **Soil-Mycorrhiza-Tree** | Reduced soil disturbance allows mycorrhizal recovery, networks support tree nutrition and disease resistance, healthy trees provide litter that feeds soil biota | 5, 6 | Years to decades |

### The Asymmetry

A critical finding across all six modules: degradation loops operate faster than restoration loops. A forest can be cleared in hours; it takes centuries to reach old-growth condition (Module 6). A pollinator population can crash in a single season (62% loss, 2024-2025); hedgerow restoration takes 7 years to significantly increase pollinator species richness (PMC, 2023; Module 5). PFAS contamination persists indefinitely in the environment; no field-scale remediation technology currently exists. This temporal asymmetry means that prevention is structurally more efficient than remediation — every system in this research series is cheaper and more effective to maintain than to rebuild.

## Contradiction Resolution

### Data Consistency Audit

A cross-module review of numerical claims reveals the following consistency findings:

**Colony loss figures — consistent across modules:**
- Module 4 reports: 1.7 million colonies lost, 62% average loss rate, $600 million total lost revenue, $428 million in almond pollination losses (all sourced to Food Tank, August 2025)
- Module 3 references pollinator decline but does not independently cite colony loss numbers
- Module 5 references Module 4 for pollinator data
- **Resolution:** Numbers are internally consistent. All originate from the same primary source (Food Tank, August 2025, reporting USDA and Project Apis M survey data). No contradiction.

**Deforestation hectares — consistent across modules:**
- Module 6 reports: 8.1 million hectares lost, 8.8 million hectares degraded, 63% above 2030 trajectory (all sourced to Forest Declaration Assessment, 2025)
- Module 5 references Module 6 for forest data
- **Resolution:** Numbers are internally consistent. Single authoritative source used throughout. No contradiction.

**Air pollution mortality — single source, no conflict:**
- Module 1 reports: 8.1 million premature deaths annually (Health Effects Institute, 2024)
- No other module independently cites air pollution mortality
- **Resolution:** No contradiction. Note the coincidental numerical match between air pollution deaths (8.1 million) and forest loss hectares (8.1 million) — these are unrelated figures from different sources measuring different quantities.

**PFAS standards — consistent across modules:**
- Module 2 reports: EPA April 2024 PFAS standards, 4 ppt MCL for PFOA and PFOS, $4.7 billion compliance cost
- Module 3 references the same standards for cross-module PFAS context
- **Resolution:** Consistent. Both modules cite EPA, 2024 as the source.

**U.S. managed colony count trajectory — requires clarification:**
- Module 4 reports: ~5 million colonies (1940s) declining to ~2.68 million (2023), sourced to Pollinator.org/USDA APHIS
- Module 4 also reports 62% loss in 2024-2025, with post-loss estimate below 2 million (derived calculation)
- **Resolution:** The 62% figure represents seasonal loss (summer 2024 to spring 2025), not permanent reduction from the 2.68 million baseline. Beekeepers rebuild colonies through splitting. The "below 2 million" is a seasonal nadir, not a permanent count. This is clearly documented in Module 4 but could be misread by a casual reader. The terminology is precise.

**Pollinator crop dependency — consistent:**
- Module 4 reports: 87 of world's leading food crops depend on pollination, 87% of flowering plant species require animal pollination (both CABI Reviews, 2024)
- **Resolution:** The similar numbers (87 crops, 87% of species) from the same source could be confused but represent different metrics. Module 4 states both clearly. No contradiction.

**Rainforest Alliance deforestation rate comparison — consistent:**
- Module 6 cites Forest Declaration Assessment (2025) for 8.1 million hectares and Rainforest Alliance (2024) for "~18 football fields per minute"
- **Resolution:** These are from different sources expressing the same scale in different units. 8.1 million hectares/year divided by 525,600 minutes/year equals approximately 15.4 hectares/minute. At ~0.53 hectares per American football field, that is approximately 29 fields per minute. The Rainforest Alliance's "~18 fields per minute" figure may use a different year's data, a different field size convention, or exclude degradation. The figures are in the same order of magnitude and not contradictory; they reflect different methodologies and likely different year-baselines.

## The Restoration Imperative

### What the Evidence Says

The six modules, read as a single body of evidence, demonstrate that:

**1. The systems are coupled.** Air, water, food, pollinators, wildlife, and forests do not operate independently. Every causal pathway documented above shows that changes in one system propagate to others. No domain can be restored in isolation.

**2. Living systems outperform engineered replacements at scale.** Forested watersheds produce cleaner water than engineered treatment plants at lower cost — New York City invested $1.5 billion in Catskill/Delaware watershed protection to avoid building a $6-10 billion filtration plant (Module 6). Pollinator services are valued at $15-20 billion annually in the U.S. alone; manual pollination costs $5,000-$10,000 per hectare (Module 4). Forest soil filtration operates at continental scale with zero energy input; engineered water treatment consumes 3-4 kWh per cubic meter for desalination alone (Module 2).

**3. Prevention is faster and cheaper than restoration.** Old-growth forests take centuries to develop but can be destroyed in hours (Module 6). Pollinator populations crash in a single season but require 7+ years of habitat restoration to recover species richness (PMC, 2023; Module 5). PFAS persist indefinitely; destruction technologies remain at bench scale (Module 2). The temporal asymmetry between degradation and restoration is the strongest argument for protecting existing systems.

**4. Connectivity is infrastructure.** Module 5 demonstrates that connected habitat patches support higher biodiversity than isolated patches of equal size (PMC, 2023). Module 6 documents that forest regeneration adjacent to intact forest follows trajectories closer to old-growth, while isolated regeneration stalls (Mongabay, 2025). Corridors, riparian buffers, hedgerows, and continuous forest cover are not amenities — they are the physical infrastructure through which gene flow, seed dispersal, nutrient cycling, and water filtration operate.

**5. Community stewardship works.** The Maya Biosphere Reserve concessions — 12 concessions managed by Q'eqchi' and other Maya communities, 417,269 hectares, near-zero deforestation since 2000 (Rainforest Alliance, 2024) — demonstrate that community management with secure tenure outperforms both government management and the absence of management. This finding is replicated across tropical, temperate, and boreal contexts (Module 6).

### The Cross-Domain Restoration Framework

Restoration that addresses the coupled nature of these systems requires simultaneous action across domains:

| Action | Primary Domain | Cross-Domain Benefits |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| Forest watershed protection | Forest (M6) | Water quality (M2), air quality (M1), habitat (M5), pollinator habitat (M4) |
| Native plant restoration in agricultural landscapes | Habitat (M5) | Pollinator food (M4), soil health (M3), water filtration (M2) |
| IPM and precision agriculture adoption | Food safety (M3) | Pollinator protection (M4), water quality (M2), wildlife food web (M5) |
| Riparian corridor restoration | Habitat (M5) | Water quality (M2), salmon habitat (M5), forest connectivity (M6) |
| PFAS source reduction | Water (M2) | Food safety (M3), air quality (M1), wildlife food web (M5) |
| Community forestry with secure tenure | Forest (M6) | Water supply (M2), biodiversity (M5), carbon (M6), food system resilience (M3) |

The evidence does not prescribe a single intervention. It demonstrates that the most effective interventions are those that restore the coupling between systems — actions that simultaneously improve water quality, pollinator habitat, forest function, and food safety. The living filter network, when maintained, provides these services as emergent properties of intact ecosystems. When degraded, each service must be engineered separately at vastly greater cost and lower efficacy.
