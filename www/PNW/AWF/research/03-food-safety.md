## Regulatory Framework and Monitoring

### The Multi-Layered International Framework

Food safety as it relates to pesticide residues is governed through overlapping national and international regulatory structures. Understanding the distinctions between these frameworks is essential for interpreting monitoring data.

**Maximum Residue Limits (MRLs)** are the legally permitted maximum concentrations of pesticide residues in or on food and feed commodities. MRL-setting involves assessment of pesticide toxicology, commodity consumption patterns, and Good Agricultural Practice (GAP) — the agricultural practices required to achieve adequate pest control with minimum residue. An MRL exceedance indicates that a pesticide was used at higher rates, closer to harvest, or under conditions differing from those assessed in the registration review.

MRL compliance does not by itself establish safety: MRLs are set based on GAP, not solely on toxicological thresholds. In practice, both factors inform regulatory review, but the two concepts are distinct.

### United States: EPA and USDA

**EPA Maximum Residue Limits (U.S. Tolerances):** In the United States, the EPA establishes tolerances (the U.S. equivalent of MRLs) for pesticide residues in food under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FFDCA). Tolerance-setting requires a finding of "reasonable certainty of no harm" at estimated dietary exposure. The EPA is required to consider cumulative exposure from all pesticides sharing a common mechanism of toxicity and aggregate exposure from all routes (food, water, residential).

**USDA Pesticide Data Program (PDP):** The USDA Agricultural Marketing Service operates the Pesticide Data Program, the primary U.S. monitoring system for pesticide residues in food. The PDP collects samples from retail grocery stores, representing food as consumed, and tests them using multi-residue methods covering hundreds of pesticide compounds.

The USDA PDP 2024 annual summary reported that 99% or more of tested foods complied with EPA maximum residue limits (Food Safety Magazine, December 2025, reporting on USDA PDP 2024 data). This compliance rate refers specifically to whether detected residues exceeded U.S. tolerances — not to whether residues were present.

### European Union

The EU operates one of the most stringent pesticide regulatory frameworks globally. EU MRLs are set under Regulation (EC) No 396/2005 and are harmonized across EU member states. Key EU framework elements include:

**The Precautionary Principle:** EU pesticide approval requires demonstration of safety, with the regulatory burden falling on the registrant to demonstrate no unacceptable risk. The EU has withdrawn authorization for several pesticides approved in the United States, including neonicotinoids (for most outdoor uses), chlorpyrifos, and others, based on differing risk assessments.

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM) mandate:** EU Directive 2009/128/EC established a legal requirement for EU farmers to apply IPM principles — prioritizing non-chemical prevention and biological control, using chemical pesticides as a last resort. This directive applies to all professional pesticide users across EU member states.

**European Food Safety Authority (EFSA):** EFSA conducts independent scientific assessments supporting EU MRL decisions and approvals. EFSA's monitoring reports document residue compliance across EU member states annually.

### International: FAO/WHO Joint Meeting on Pesticide Residues (JMPR)

The FAO/WHO Joint Meeting on Pesticide Residues is an international scientific expert group that evaluates pesticide residues in food and recommends Codex Maximum Residue Limits. These Codex MRLs serve as international reference standards for trade and inform national MRL-setting in countries that have not independently evaluated specific pesticides.

The FAO/WHO JMPR (2024) evaluated 37 pesticides and 7 new compounds, issuing MRL recommendations that feed into the Codex Alimentarius Commission's food safety standards (FAO/WHO JMPR, 2024). The JMPR also establishes Acceptable Daily Intakes (ADIs) and Acute Reference Doses (ARfDs) — the toxicological benchmarks against which dietary exposure is assessed.

### Regulatory Framework Comparison

| Regulatory Body | Geographic Scope | MRL Basis | Monitoring Program | Key Distinction |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|

## The Residue Reality

### What Compliance Data Tells Us — and What It Doesn't

The USDA PDP's 99%+ compliance rate (Food Safety Magazine, December 2025) means that in the vast majority of samples, detected residues fall below established U.S. tolerances. This is a meaningful data point: it documents that registered pesticides, used according to label requirements, generally achieve the regulatory goal of residue levels below tolerance.

What compliance data does not tell us:

- **Whether residues are present** — compliance is defined as below tolerance, not as absence of residues
- **Whether multiple residues from different pesticides are additively or synergistically relevant** — U.S. tolerances are typically set compound-by-compound, though cumulative assessment is increasingly required for compound classes with common mechanisms
- **Whether any given tolerance level is appropriately protective for all subpopulations** — tolerances are set for average consumption patterns; infants, children, and high-consumption individuals may have greater dietary exposure

### EWG Shopper's Guide to Pesticides in Produce 2025

The Environmental Working Group's 2025 Shopper's Guide to Pesticides in Produce analyzed 53,692 USDA samples across 47 produce items and reported the following findings (EWG, 2025):

- More than 75% of non-organic produce samples had detectable pesticide residues
- 265 distinct pesticides and pesticide breakdown products were detected across the sample set
- Residues were detected on more than 95% of samples of produce items in the EWG "Dirty Dozen" category
- 95th-percentile samples from some crops carried residues of 10 or more distinct pesticide compounds simultaneously

*Note: EWG is a consumer advocacy organization. Its Shopper's Guide is cited here as a consumer-facing analysis of government USDA PDP data, not as an independent scientific study. EWG's residue concern thresholds and risk characterizations reflect the organization's advocacy perspective. For regulatory compliance context, USDA PDP and EPA tolerance data are the primary government sources.*

The EWG analysis draws on the same USDA PDP data that underlies USDA's compliance reporting. The distinction between the two framings is that USDA PDP reports compliance (residues below tolerance), while EWG reports detection (residues present above detection limits, regardless of tolerance comparison).

### Dirty Dozen and Clean Fifteen (EWG 2025 Categories)

The EWG's Dirty Dozen categorizes the 12 produce items with the highest residue scores in the EWG analysis. The EWG's scoring weights both the frequency of detection and the number of pesticides found per sample. The EWG 2025 Shopper's Guide identified the Dirty Dozen and a "Clean Fifteen" — produce items with lower overall residue scores.

*The Dirty Dozen and Clean Fifteen are EWG analytical categories, not regulatory classifications. The USDA PDP data underlying the analysis shows the same samples as compliant with EPA tolerances.*

### The Gap Between Compliance and Exposure

From a public health research perspective, two distinct questions are relevant:

**Question 1 — Regulatory compliance:** Are pesticide residues in the food supply within legally established limits? USDA PDP 2024 data documents 99%+ compliance (Food Safety Magazine, December 2025).

**Question 2 — Population exposure:** What are the aggregate dietary exposures to pesticide residues, particularly for children, pregnant individuals, and high-produce consumers? This is an active area of epidemiological and toxicological research. Answering it requires dietary exposure modeling combining consumption data with residue monitoring data — a process both EPA and independent researchers conduct.

### FDA Human Foods Program (October 2024)

The FDA launched the Human Foods Program in October 2024, consolidating previously separate FDA centers and offices responsible for food safety and human nutrition under a single unified structure (FDA, 2024). The reorganization includes integration of the Office of Inspections and Investigations function into the Human Foods Program structure. The intent documented in FDA communications is to improve coordination between regulatory, inspection, and scientific functions in food safety oversight.

### FSMA Produce Safety Rule Update (April 7, 2025)

The FDA updated Subpart E of the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) Produce Safety Rule, covering pre-harvest agricultural water — the water used for irrigation and other field applications that can contact produce (FDA, 2024/2025). The update replaced the previous prescriptive numerical standard with a systems-based approach providing more flexibility in how growers assess and manage agricultural water quality risks. The update reflected both practical implementation challenges with the original numeric standard and scientific input on risk-based water quality assessment.

Agricultural water quality is relevant to produce safety because irrigation water can be a pathway for pathogen introduction (Salmonella, E. coli O157:H7) and for agricultural chemical contamination (nitrates, pesticides in reclaimed water).

### EPA DCPA (Dacthal) Cancellation (2024)

The EPA cancelled the registration of DCPA (dimethyl tetrachloroterephthalate, also known as Dacthal) in 2024 after reviewing data indicating thyroid hormone disruption in fetuses of pregnant women exposed to the herbicide (EPA, 2024). DCPA was a pre-emergent herbicide used primarily on vegetable crops. The cancellation was the first unilateral cancellation action by EPA under the Federal Insecticide, Fungicide, and Rodenticide Act (FIFRA) in decades, taken after the registrant declined to voluntarily cancel the product following EPA's emergency suspension action.

### FAO/WHO JMPR 2024

The 2024 FAO/WHO JMPR meeting evaluated pesticide residues in food for 37 pesticides and established assessments for 7 new compounds (FAO/WHO JMPR, 2024). The JMPR evaluations establish ADIs and ARfDs, and recommend Codex MRLs that serve as international reference standards. The 2024 session included evaluations of several fungicides and insecticides with growing use in specialty crop production, and reviewed available data on breakdown products and metabolites that may have independent toxicological relevance.

### SQFI Edition 10 (July 2025)

The Safe Quality Food (SQF) Institute released Edition 10 of its food safety certification program in July 2025. The SQF program provides third-party certification for food safety management systems across the food supply chain, from primary production through food service. Edition 10 updates reflect developments in food safety risk management, supply chain documentation, and FSMA alignment. SQF certification is recognized by the Global Food Safety Initiative (GFSI).

## Persistent Pollutants

### Legacy Contamination: Organochlorine Pesticides

Several organochlorine pesticides banned decades ago continue to be detected in food samples due to their environmental persistence and lipophilicity (tendency to accumulate in fat tissue). USDA PDP data analyzed by EWG (2025) documented:

**DDT and metabolites:** DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane) and its metabolites DDE and DDD were detected in:
- Leaf lettuce: 16.2% of samples (EWG, 2025, citing USDA PDP data)
- Potatoes: 2.3% of samples (EWG, 2025, citing USDA PDP data)
- Salmon: 1.7% of samples (EWG, 2025, citing USDA PDP data)

DDT was banned in the United States in 1972 (EPA). Its detection in 2024 monitoring data reflects the compound's environmental persistence — DDT's primary metabolite DDE has a half-life measured in years to decades in soil and is bioaccumulated through the food chain, particularly in fatty tissues of animals including fish.

**Chlordane:** Chlordane, a cyclodiene insecticide used as a soil insecticide and termiticide, was banned in the United States during the 1970s–1980s. EWG (2025) citing USDA PDP data documented chlordane detection in 2.3% of canned pumpkin samples.

**Significance of legacy detections:** The continuing presence of banned organochlorine compounds in food monitoring data illustrates that regulatory prohibition removes the input pathway but cannot immediately eliminate environmental accumulation from decades of prior use. These compounds are bioaccumulated through the food chain. Fatty foods (fish, dairy, fatty meats) and root vegetables (which draw from soil) are the primary matrices where legacy contamination is most commonly detected.

### Neonicotinoids and Pollinator Intersection

Neonicotinoid insecticides (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam, acetamiprid, and others) are systemic pesticides taken up by plants from soil or seed treatments and expressed in plant tissues including pollen and nectar. Their use in agriculture is documented as a factor in pollinator impacts.

USDA PDP monitoring data include neonicotinoid residues across a range of produce categories. The food safety regulatory question (residues within tolerance) and the ecosystem impact question (exposure pathways for pollinators) are distinct regulatory and scientific matters. Module 04 (Pollinator Health) addresses the pollinator impact dimension; this module addresses the food residue dimension.

**EU neonicotinoid restrictions:** The EU restricted outdoor use of three neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam) in 2018, citing unacceptable risk to bees (European Commission). These restrictions affect EU production but not food imports, which may still contain residues from producing countries where neonicotinoid use continues.

### Herbicide Residues: Glyphosate

Glyphosate (N-(phosphonomethyl)glycine) is the world's most widely used herbicide. It is the active ingredient in Roundup and numerous other formulations, applied in conventional crop production and as a pre-harvest desiccant on some crops (wheat, oats, legumes). Its extensive use means residues are detected broadly in food monitoring programs globally.

**Regulatory status:** The EPA completed its most recent glyphosate registration review and issued an interim decision concluding that glyphosate is "not likely to be carcinogenic to humans" when used according to label directions, consistent with the scientific consensus position of WHO's Core Assessment Group for Glyphosate and EFSA. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified glyphosate as "probably carcinogenic to humans" (Group 2A) in 2015 — a classification based on a different assessment scope than regulatory tolerance-setting (IARC evaluates hazard potential; regulatory agencies evaluate risk at actual exposure levels).

**Residue monitoring:** The USDA PDP does not routinely include glyphosate in its standard monitoring panel due to methodological challenges (glyphosate requires different analytical methods than organophosphates and other conventional pesticides). Oat-based products have received attention in monitoring studies for glyphosate residues due to pre-harvest application practices.

## PFAS in Food

### PFAS Contamination Pathways to Food

PFAS reach the human food supply through multiple documented pathways:

**Food packaging migration:** PFAS have been widely used in food packaging materials — fast food wrappers, microwave popcorn bags, pizza boxes, and other grease-resistant paper and board packaging. PFAS migrate from packaging into food, particularly fatty foods and foods in contact with packaging at elevated temperatures. FDA phased out several PFAS uses in food contact materials in 2020 (fluorinated polymer treatments not marketed for that use). FDA actions are ongoing as industry transitions to alternative materials.

**Irrigation with contaminated water:** Food crops irrigated with PFAS-contaminated water can take up certain PFAS compounds through root systems and translocate them into edible plant tissues. Research has documented PFAS uptake in leafy vegetables, root vegetables, and fruits irrigated with contaminated water. The concentration and distribution of PFAS in plant tissues varies substantially by PFAS compound, plant species, and soil chemistry.

**AFFF-contaminated agricultural land:** Military bases and airports where aqueous film-forming foam (AFFF) was used for fire training and emergency response are documented PFAS contamination sources. Agricultural land adjacent to or downstream from these sites may have elevated PFAS in soil and groundwater, affecting both irrigation water quality and direct soil-plant uptake.

**Animal products from PFAS-exposed livestock:** Animals consuming PFAS-contaminated water or feed bioaccumulate PFAS in tissues and milk. Several documented PFAS contamination incidents in the United States have involved farms where livestock consumed contaminated water, resulting in elevated PFAS in milk and meat.

### Regulatory and Research Status (2024–2025)

The EPA's finalization of PFAS drinking water standards in April 2024 (EPA, 2024) addressed the water route, setting legally enforceable MCLs for six PFAS compounds. Food-route PFAS exposure does not yet have comparable federal regulatory limits for PFAS in food commodities as of early 2025.

FDA's monitoring for PFAS in food has expanded. FDA's Total Diet Study includes PFAS analysis in market basket samples. The FDA Human Foods Program launch (October 2024) positions PFAS in food as part of its integrated oversight scope.

**Cross-module note:** PFAS as a water contaminant is addressed comprehensively in Module 02 (Water Filtration). The drinking water standards documented there establish regulatory context for the irrigation water pathway described in this section.

## Sustainable Farming and Contamination Reduction

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)

Integrated Pest Management is a decision-based approach to pest control that combines multiple strategies — biological control, cultural practices, mechanical interventions, and targeted chemical use — with the objective of maintaining pest populations below economically damaging levels while minimizing risks to human health and the environment.

IPM does not prohibit pesticide use; it positions pesticide use as one element in a broader management system rather than the default first response. The decision to apply pesticides in an IPM framework is based on monitoring pest populations against action thresholds, not on a preventive calendar spray schedule.

**EU IPM mandate:** EU Directive 2009/128/EC established a legal requirement for professional pesticide users in EU member states to apply IPM principles (European Commission, 2009). This represents one of the most extensive policy frameworks mandating IPM adoption globally. Compliance documentation and training requirements are part of the directive's implementation.

**U.S. IPM programs:** In the United States, IPM is promoted through USDA cooperative extension programs and is a condition of some specialty crop marketing orders and retail supply chain requirements. The USDA's National Roadmap for Integrated Pest Management provides programmatic direction. IPM adoption is not mandated at the federal level in the U.S.

### Precision Application and Agricultural Robotics

PMC review of precision agricultural technologies documented that agricultural robots reduce pesticide use by approximately 80% compared to broadcast spraying through targeted application (PMC, 2023). Precision application technologies include:

**Variable rate technology (VRT):** GPS-referenced application equipment that varies pesticide application rate based on field maps of pest pressure, soil type, or historical yield data. Reduces total application volume by applying higher rates only where needed.

**Sensor-guided spot treatment:** Camera and sensor systems that identify individual weed plants or disease lesions and trigger targeted application, leaving pest-free areas untreated. Robotic weeding platforms using this approach for herbicide reduction in row crops are commercially available as of 2025.

**Drone application:** Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs/drones) equipped with spray systems provide precise low-volume application, particularly for specialty crops with complex canopy structures. Regulatory frameworks for agricultural drone operation are established in the U.S. (FAA Part 137 certification for aerial application) and are evolving in other jurisdictions.

**Biological control and biopesticides:** The use of beneficial insects, entomopathogenic fungi, bacteria (Bacillus thuringiensis, Beauveria bassiana), and plant-derived compounds (spinosad, azadirachtin) as alternatives or complements to synthetic chemical pesticides is an expanding segment. Biopesticides are regulated under FIFRA in the United States; USDA organic certification generally permits approved biopesticides while prohibiting synthetic chemical pesticides.

### Organic Production

Organic farming systems prohibit the use of most synthetic pesticides and fertilizers. Permitted inputs are listed on the National Organic Program (NOP) National List for U.S. organic production. Organic certification requires third-party verification of compliance with NOP standards.

Residue monitoring of organic produce shows significantly lower rates of synthetic pesticide detection compared to conventional produce — a predictable finding given the prohibition on synthetic pesticide use in organic production. Residues detected in organic produce derive from environmental sources (drift, soil persistence from prior conventional use), approved organic inputs (copper-based fungicides, sulfur), or mislabeling/fraud.

**Trade-offs in organic production:** Organic systems do not inherently eliminate all pesticide risk (some approved organic pesticides have toxicological profiles warranting consideration) and may have different implications for yield, land use, and greenhouse gas emissions — dimensions beyond the scope of this module's food safety focus.

### Soil Health and Residue Persistence

Pesticide persistence in soil varies from days to decades depending on chemical properties, soil conditions, and microbial community composition. Organochlorine pesticides (DDT, chlordane, lindane) persist for decades to generations. Many modern pesticides are designed for faster degradation, measured in days to weeks under field conditions.

Soil health practices — maintaining organic matter, supporting microbial diversity, minimizing compaction — generally support faster pesticide degradation by maintaining active soil microbial communities that metabolize pesticide compounds. Degradation also reduces soil-to-crop transfer.

**Blockchain and data integration for residue tracing:** While primarily discussed under supply chain traceability (below), blockchain food traceability platforms are beginning to incorporate soil and input records that document pesticide application history — enabling both farm management and third-party audit of residue risk.

## Food Supply Chain Traceability

### The 2024 Traceability Landscape

Blockchain food traceability became a cornerstone of supply chain monitoring in 2024, according to industry analysis (FoodChain ID, February 2025). The technology enables immutable recording of food supply chain events — farm inputs, harvest records, processing steps, distribution chain of custody — in a format that multiple supply chain participants can access and verify without any single party controlling the data.

FoodChain ID's February 2025 report documented that global pesticide residue incidents — detections of residues exceeding regulatory limits in international trade — rose 5.8% in 2024 compared to 2023 (FoodChain ID, February 2025). The report did not identify a single causal factor for the increase; contributing elements discussed include expanded monitoring coverage, climate-driven shifts in pest pressure requiring additional pesticide applications, and supply chain complexity in globally sourced commodity flows.

### FSMA Section 204 (FDA Food Traceability Rule)

The FDA Food Safety Modernization Act Section 204 established enhanced traceability requirements for "high-risk" food categories, implemented through the FDA's Food Traceability Rule. Covered foods include leafy greens, tomatoes, peppers, sprouts, fresh herbs, melons, tropical tree fruits, and specific processed products. The rule requires supply chain participants to maintain Key Data Elements (KDEs) and Critical Tracking Events (CTEs) enabling rapid lot-level traceability within 24 hours during a recall or outbreak investigation.

Full compliance with FSMA Section 204 traceability requirements was phased over implementation timelines. The rule represents a significant infrastructure investment for supply chain participants implementing electronic record-keeping systems.

### Industry Traceability Standards

Multiple industry-level traceability standards operate alongside regulatory requirements:

**GS1 Standards:** GS1 is the global organization maintaining standards for supply chain data — barcodes, Electronic Product Codes (EPCs), and Electronic Data Interchange (EDI). GS1 standards (including GS1-128 barcodes and Electronic Article Surveillance) enable lot-level traceability through standardized identifiers.

**IBM Food Trust / Enterprise Blockchain Platforms:** Enterprise blockchain platforms applied to food traceability provide multi-party shared ledgers for supply chain data. Several major food retailers implemented blockchain-based traceability pilots for produce categories beginning in 2018–2020; by 2024 these have expanded to commercial-scale implementations.

**Rainforest Alliance and Certification Chain-of-Custody:** The Rainforest Alliance's 2024 Annual Report documented expansion of regenerative agriculture certification and forest conservation programs, including chain-of-custody certification for certified commodity flows (Rainforest Alliance, 2024). Certification traceability systems verify that certified practices are maintained through the supply chain, linking on-farm sustainability metrics to retail product claims.

### The Recall System and Traceability Value

The primary practical value of improved food traceability is enabling faster, more targeted recalls when contamination events are identified. Without lot-level traceability, recalls must be broad to ensure coverage of affected product — resulting in significant food waste and economic impact on uninvolved producers. With robust traceability, recall scope can be limited to actually implicated lots.

The 2024 ground beef listeria recall in the United States and multiple produce-associated E. coli outbreaks in recent years illustrate both the food safety stakes and the practical limitations of current traceability infrastructure. Traceability is only as complete as its weakest link; mixed-lot processing and informal farm-level record-keeping remain gaps.

### FAO/WHO JMPR 2024 — Selected Evaluations

The FAO/WHO JMPR 2024 session issued evaluations for 37 pesticides and established new assessments for 7 compounds (FAO/WHO JMPR, 2024). Key outputs from international pesticide review sessions include:

- Establishment or revision of Acceptable Daily Intakes (ADIs) — the amount of a substance that can be ingested daily over a lifetime without appreciable health risk

The JMPR reviews represent international scientific consensus evaluations separate from national regulatory decisions. ## Pacific Northwest Food Safety Context

The PNW is one of the most productive and diverse agricultural regions in the United States, with specific crops, production systems, and food safety considerations that shape regional relevance.

**Washington Tree Fruit — The Nation's Apple Capital**

Washington State produces approximately 65% of all U.S. apples — over 10 billion pounds annually — along with significant pear, cherry, and stone fruit production concentrated in the Yakima, Wenatchee, and Okanogan valleys (USDA NASS, Washington Agricultural Statistics). Tree fruit production requires intensive pest management for codling moth, apple maggot, fire blight, and powdery mildew. The Washington State University Tree Fruit Research and Extension Center develops IPM protocols specific to PNW growing conditions. USDA PDP data routinely includes Washington-grown apples in its monitoring sample set. The state's dominance in apple production means PNW pest management practices directly influence national residue data.

**Oregon Berries and Hazelnuts**

Oregon produces 99% of U.S. hazelnuts (filberts), concentrated in the Willamette Valley (Oregon Hazelnut Commission). Eastern filbert blight (*Anisogramma anomala*) is the primary disease challenge, managed through resistant cultivars and targeted fungicide application. Oregon is also a leading producer of blueberries, blackberries, and raspberries, with Washington ranking first nationally in raspberry production (USDA NASS). Berry crops face spotted wing drosophila (*Drosophila suzukii*), an invasive pest requiring monitoring-based spray timing — a practical IPM application.

**Salmon and Shellfish as Food Safety Matrices**

Pacific salmon and shellfish are central to PNW foodways and commerce. As bioaccumulators, salmon concentrate persistent organic pollutants (DDT/DDE, PCBs, dioxins) from their marine food web, with farmed salmon showing different contaminant profiles than wild-caught (Hites et al., 2004, *Science*). USDA PDP data documents DDT detection in 1.7% of salmon samples (EWG, 2025, citing USDA PDP data — see Persistent Pollutants section above). Shellfish (oysters, clams, mussels) from Puget Sound, Willapa Bay, and Oregon estuaries face biotoxin monitoring for paralytic shellfish poisoning (PSP) and domoic acid — managed by state health departments through harvest closures, not residue limits.

**Regional Organic Production Rates**

Oregon and Washington rank among the top states for organic production and organic acreage. Oregon had the fourth-highest number of organic farms in the 2022 USDA Census of Agriculture, and Washington ranked fifth. The PNW's temperate climate, diverse cropping systems, and consumer demand support organic production at rates above the national average, providing a natural comparison population for conventional vs. organic residue analysis.

| Practice | Documented Effectiveness | Source |
|----------|--------------------------|--------|
| Running water wash (30 seconds) | Removes 10-80% of surface residues depending on compound and produce type | Yang et al., 2017, *Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry* |
| Baking soda solution soak (1% NaHCO3, 12-15 minutes) | More effective than tap water for surface and some systemic residues | He et al., 2023, *Food Chemistry* |
| Peeling | Removes surface and near-surface residues; may reduce nutrient content | EFSA guidance; trade-off with nutrient loss |
| Cooking (boiling, steaming) | Reduces some residues through thermal degradation; effectiveness varies by compound | Variable — some compounds stable at cooking temperatures |
| Buying organic | Reduces synthetic pesticide residue exposure; does not eliminate all pesticide risk (organic-approved pesticides may be present) | USDA PDP comparative data |

*These practices reduce but do not eliminate residue exposure. Regulatory compliance (99%+ per USDA PDP) means residues in compliant produce are within established safety limits.*

## Cross-Module Links

**PFAS in water and food:** The EPA's April 2024 PFAS drinking water standards (documented in Module 02, Water Filtration) address the drinking water route of PFAS exposure. This module's PFAS in Food section covers food-route exposure: irrigation water contamination, food packaging migration, and bioaccumulation in animal products. Both routes contribute to total PFAS exposure. The EPA's integrated PFAS framework addresses both routes as part of a comprehensive PFAS action plan.

**Pesticides and pollinator health (Module 04):** Neonicotinoid and other pesticide residues in food are documented in this module. Module 04 (Pollinator Health) addresses the ecosystem pathway — how pesticide exposure reaches pollinators through soil residues, pollen, nectar, and guttation water. The food safety regulatory question (tolerable human exposure) and the pollinator protection question (tolerable ecosystem exposure) are addressed by different regulatory frameworks with different endpoints and methodologies.

**Agricultural water quality and filtration (Module 02):** The FSMA Produce Safety Rule update (April 2025) addresses pre-harvest agricultural water quality. Irrigation water quality — including both pathogen content and chemical contamination (nitrates, PFAS, pesticide drift) — is a food safety input. Module 02 covers the filtration and treatment technologies applicable to treating contaminated water before or during agricultural use.

**Air quality and agricultural pesticides (Module 01):** Pesticide drift and volatilization from agricultural fields contribute to ambient air quality in agricultural regions. Module 01 addresses indoor air purification. Agricultural workers and residents near sprayed fields face outdoor pesticide air exposure that is separate from and beyond the scope of indoor air purification technologies — but the same compound classes (organophosphates, pyrethroids, fungicides) appear in both air quality and food safety monitoring contexts.
