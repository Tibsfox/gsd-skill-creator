## Section 1: The Cascade Hydrological System (AQUA-01)

The Cascade Range is a water factory. From volcanic summit to valley floor, a continuous hydrological gradient connects alpine ice fields, snowpack reservoirs, spring-fed streams, and gravel-bottomed salmon rivers into a single integrated system. Each component plays a distinct ecological role, and the health of downstream salmonid habitat depends directly on the integrity of upstream water sources.

This section traces water from its highest-elevation origins through the full descent to lowland rivers, documenting the physical hydrology and ecological function at each stage.

### 1.1 Alpine Tarns and Glacial Lakes

Alpine tarns — small mountain lakes formed in glacially carved cirques — represent the highest-elevation standing water in the Cascade Range. Found above 5,000 feet (1,500 m) on both the volcanic peaks and in the North Cascades alpine zone, these lakes are ice-covered 6–9 months per year, ultraoligotrophic (extremely nutrient-poor), and cold year-round (surface temperatures rarely exceed 10degC even in summer). (Source: GOV-04)

**Ecological characteristics:**
- Water clarity often exceeds 20 meters visibility due to minimal nutrient loading
- Primary productivity limited by short ice-free season (90–180 days) and low nutrient input
- Aquatic communities dominated by cold-adapted invertebrates: chironomid midges, copepods, and cladocerans
- Fish populations rare and typically limited to stocked trout species; many tarns are naturally fishless
- Amphibian use significant: Cascades frog (*Rana cascadae*) and long-toed salamander (*Ambystoma macrodactylum*) breed in snowmelt-fed tarns where fish are absent

**Glacial lakes** differ from tarns in receiving continuous glacial meltwater input during summer months. This creates distinctive milky-turquoise coloration from suspended glacial flour (finely ground rock particles). Glacial lakes are colder, more turbid, and even less productive than snowmelt tarns. Examples include the proglacial lakes below Mount Rainier's Carbon, Emmons, and Nisqually glaciers. (Source: GOV-04)

**Climate vulnerability:** Alpine tarns are among the most sensitive aquatic habitats in the Cascades. Earlier snowmelt, reduced ice-cover duration, and warming temperatures alter the thermal regime and hydroperiod these systems depend on. Cascades frogs breeding in snowmelt tarns face timing mismatches when snow melts before breeding cues align with food availability. (Source: GOV-06)

### 1.2 Snowpack as Natural Reservoir

Cascade snowpack functions as the largest natural water storage system in the Pacific Northwest, operating as a slow-release reservoir that captures winter precipitation and delivers it gradually through spring and summer melt. This seasonal storage-and-release cycle is the hydrological engine that sustains downstream aquatic ecosystems during the dry summer months when precipitation is minimal. (Source: PR-05, GOV-06)

**Quantitative snowpack data:**

| Parameter | Western Slopes | Crest / High Elevations | Eastern Slopes |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------|----------------|
| Annual precipitation | 100+ inches (2,540+ mm) | 150–200+ inches (3,800–5,000+ mm) | 9–30 inches (230–760 mm) |
| Annual snowfall | 200–400 inches | 500+ inches (12,700+ mm) at highest sites | 50–150 inches |
| Peak snowpack (April 1) | 60–120 inches SWE | 100–200+ inches SWE | 20–60 inches SWE |
| Melt season | April–July | May–September | March–June |
| Summer baseflow contribution | 60–80% of stream flow | Glacial + snowmelt combined | 40–60% of stream flow |

(Sources: GOV-06, PR-05)

> **SWE = Snow Water Equivalent:** The depth of water that would result if the entire snowpack melted instantaneously. One inch of SWE is approximately 10–15 inches of snow depending on density.

**The storage-release cycle:**

1. **Accumulation (October–March):** Pacific maritime storms deliver moisture as rain at lower elevations and snow above 3,000–4,000 feet. The western Cascades receive the first orographic lift from Pacific-origin weather systems, extracting the bulk of atmospheric moisture. Snow accumulates progressively through winter, reaching maximum depth (peak snowpack) in late March to early April.

2. **Isothermal transition (March–April):** As spring temperatures rise, the snowpack warms to 0degC throughout its depth (isothermal condition). At this point, any additional heat energy goes directly to melting rather than warming. This transition marks the beginning of sustained melt.

3. **Melt and release (April–August):** Snowmelt generates the annual hydrograph peak — the sustained high-water period that fills streams, recharges groundwater, and maintains cold-water baseflow through summer. In undisturbed watersheds, snowmelt sustains 60–80% of summer streamflow in western Cascade rivers. (Source: GOV-06)

4. **Baseflow period (July–October):** After snowpack depletion, streams transition to groundwater-sustained baseflow. Flow volume, temperature, and duration of the baseflow period depend directly on how much water the snowpack stored and how gradually it released. This period is critical for salmonid survival — juvenile rearing, adult migration, and spawning all depend on adequate cold-water baseflow. (Source: GOV-06)

**Why Cascade snowpack is uniquely vulnerable:**

The Cascade Range occupies a narrow temperature band that makes its snowpack disproportionately sensitive to warming compared to interior mountain ranges (Rocky Mountains, Sierra Nevada). The maritime climate of the Pacific Northwest places winter temperatures at the Cascade crest closer to the rain-snow transition threshold (approximately 0–2degC) than continental ranges where winter temperatures are well below freezing. (Source: PR-05)

A 2021 study published in *Nature Climate Change* demonstrated that Cascade snowpack is among the most vulnerable in North America to warming because:

- **Proximity to melting threshold:** Winter temperatures at the elevation of maximum snow accumulation (1,200–1,800 m / 4,000–6,000 ft) are only 1–3degC below the rain-snow transition. A modest temperature increase shifts precipitation from snow to rain across a large elevation band.
- **Maritime moisture dependence:** The Cascades receive heavy precipitation precisely because Pacific storms deliver moisture-laden air. If that moisture arrives as rain rather than snow, the storage function is lost — water runs off immediately rather than accumulating as snowpack.
- **Elevation compression:** Unlike the Rockies where the snowpack zone spans thousands of vertical meters, the Cascade snowpack zone is compressed into a relatively narrow elevation band, making it more vulnerable to a uniform temperature shift. (Source: PR-05)

**Projected impacts (GOV-06):**
- April 1 snowpack in the Cascades has already declined 20–30% from mid-20th century baselines
- North Cascades watersheds are transitioning from snow-dominated to rain-dominated hydrology
- More precipitation falling as rain in autumn/winter produces higher peak flows and earlier runoff
- Summer baseflow period shortened and reduced, with critically lower flows during July–September
- Eastern Cascade slopes projected to experience summer streamflow losses of **40–60% by 2040** and **60–80% by 2080** as snowmelt timing shifts earlier (Source: GOV-06)

### 1.3 Glacial Meltwater Systems

Glacial meltwater provides a supplementary cold-water source that extends beyond the snowpack melt season, delivering the coldest water available to downstream systems during the hottest months (July–September) when snowpack has already melted. (Source: GOV-04)

**Mount Rainier (14,411 ft / 4,392 m):**

Mount Rainier supports the largest glacial system of any peak in the contiguous United States, with 25 named glaciers and numerous smaller ice fields covering approximately 35 square miles (90 km2) of the mountain's upper flanks. These glaciers feed six major river systems: the Nisqually, Puyallup, Carbon, White, Cowlitz, and Mowich rivers. (Source: GOV-04)

Glacial melt characteristics:
- Peak glacial discharge occurs in late July through August, after snowpack melt has largely concluded
- Meltwater temperature at glacier terminus: 0–2degC year-round
- Glacial streams carry heavy sediment loads (glacial flour) that decrease downstream as sediment settles
- Daily meltwater pulses follow diurnal temperature cycles — peak flow in late afternoon, minimum flow at dawn
- Glacial rivers transition from turbid, sediment-laden upper reaches to clearer, cold-water lower reaches suitable for salmonid spawning

**North Cascades Glaciers:**

The North Cascades contain the greatest concentration of active alpine glaciers in the lower 48 states, with over 700 glaciers and permanent snowfields. These glaciers feed headwater streams throughout the Skagit, Nooksack, and Baker river systems. (Source: GOV-04)

North Cascades glaciers have lost an estimated 40–50% of their area since the mid-20th century, with accelerating retreat since the 1980s. This retreat reduces late-summer cold-water inputs to downstream systems at precisely the time when streams are warmest and salmonids are most thermally stressed. (Source: GOV-04, GOV-06)

### 1.4 Spring-Fed Streams: Cold-Water Refugia

Volcanic geology creates an extensive spring-fed stream network that provides critical cold-water refugia — locations where groundwater upwelling maintains stream temperatures below lethal thresholds for salmonids even during summer heat events. (Source: CON-01, GOV-06)

**Mechanism:** Precipitation and snowmelt percolate through porous volcanic rock (basalt, andesite, pumice) and emerge as springs at lower elevations, often months or years after the original precipitation event. Spring-fed streams maintain remarkably stable temperatures (typically 6–10degC year-round) and consistent flow volumes regardless of seasonal precipitation patterns. (Source: CON-01)

**Key Cascade spring-fed systems:**

| Spring System | Location | Temperature Range | Ecological Role | Source |
|--------------|----------|-------------------|----------------|--------|
| Metolius River | Central Oregon Cascades | 7–12degC year-round | Bull trout stronghold; one of Oregon's last wild steelhead streams | GOV-09 |
| McKenzie River springs | Western Oregon Cascades | 5–8degC | Chinook and bull trout cold-water refuge; springs emerge from High Cascade lava flows | GOV-09 |
| Ohanapecosh Springs | Mount Rainier | 6–9degC | Bull trout habitat; pristine old-growth headwater system | GOV-04 |
| Big Spring Creek | South Cascades, WA | 7–10degC | Year-round baseflow; critical summer cold-water source for Lewis River salmon | CON-01 |
| Spring-fed tributaries (multiple) | Throughout volcanic Cascades | 5–12degC | Distributed cold-water refuge network; increasing ecological importance as mainstem temperatures rise | GOV-06 |

**Climate refugia significance:** As mainstem river temperatures rise above salmonid thermal thresholds (typically 18–22degC depending on species and life stage), spring-fed tributaries become increasingly critical as thermal refugia. Bull trout (*Salvelinus confluentus*), the most thermally sensitive Cascade salmonid, already depend almost exclusively on spring-fed headwater reaches. Climate models project that spring-fed refugia will contract as groundwater recharge patterns shift, but their relative importance will increase as mainstem conditions deteriorate. (Source: GOV-06, GOV-09)

### 1.5 Rain Shadow Contrast: Western vs. Eastern Hydrology

The Cascade crest creates one of the most dramatic precipitation gradients in North America. The western slopes receive 100+ inches (2,540+ mm) of annual precipitation, while some eastern foothills in the rain shadow may receive as little as 9 inches (230 mm) annually — a tenfold difference across as little as 30 miles horizontal distance. (Source: GOV-06)

**Western slope hydrology:**
- Precipitation-rich: 60–200+ inches annually, delivered primarily as rain below 3,000 ft, snow above
- Dense forest cover intercepts, stores, and gradually releases moisture through canopy drip, soil infiltration, and transpiration
- Streams run year-round with high baseflow; perennial systems support the full range of salmonid life stages
- Rivers include the major salmon-bearing systems: McKenzie, Clackamas, North Santiam, South Santiam, North Umpqua, North and Middle Forks of the Willamette, Salmon River, Bull Run River (Oregon); Lewis, Cowlitz, Nisqually, Puyallup, White, Green rivers (Washington) (Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Eastern slope hydrology:**
- Rain shadow: 9–30 inches annually; moisture exhausted by orographic precipitation on western slopes
- Dominant vegetation shifts from dense conifer forest to open ponderosa pine, then to sagebrush steppe
- Streams are more intermittent; many headwater reaches dry completely in summer
- Snowpack-dependent: eastern slope streams derive an even higher percentage of summer flow from snowmelt because summer precipitation is negligible
- Projected to lose 40–60% of summer streamflow by 2040 and 60–80% by 2080 — the most severe hydrological losses in the Cascade system (Source: GOV-06)

**Ecological implications of the rain shadow gradient:**

| Parameter | Western Slopes | Eastern Slopes | Ecological Consequence |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Annual precipitation | 100–200+ in | 9–30 in | 5–20x difference in water availability |
| Stream permanence | Perennial | Many intermittent | Eastern streams less reliable for year-round aquatic habitat |
| Salmonid diversity | High (5–6 species) | Lower (2–3 species) | Eastern streams limited to most drought-tolerant species |
| Summer baseflow source | Snowmelt + groundwater | Snowmelt-dominated | Eastern streams more vulnerable to snowpack loss |
| Riparian width | Broad (50–200 m) | Narrow (5–30 m) | Less nutrient transfer capacity on eastern slopes |
| Climate vulnerability | Moderate | Severe | Eastern slope aquatic systems face existential threat from snowpack decline |

(Sources: GOV-06, GOV-09)

### 2.1 Chinook Salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*)

**Common name:** Chinook salmon (king salmon)
**Scientific name:** *Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*
**ESA status:** Threatened (most Columbia River and Puget Sound ESUs)

Chinook salmon are the largest Pacific salmon species, with adults averaging 10–25 kg and exceptional individuals exceeding 50 kg. They are the flagship salmonid of Cascade rivers — the species whose population health most directly reflects watershed condition, and whose decline has driven the most extensive habitat restoration efforts. (Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Cascade-specific populations and runs:**

| Run Type | Timing | Key Cascade Rivers | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------------------|--------|-------|
| Spring Chinook | Mar–Jun | McKenzie, North Santiam, Clackamas, Cowlitz, Lewis, Nisqually | Threatened (most populations) | Most dependent on cold headwater habitat; spawn highest in watersheds; most imperiled run type |
| Summer Chinook | Jun–Aug | Skagit (WA) | Threatened | Limited Cascade distribution; primarily a mid-Columbia species |
| Fall Chinook (Tule) | Aug–Oct | Cowlitz, Lewis, Clackamas, Green, Puyallup | Threatened | Spawn in lower river reaches; more tolerant of warmer water than spring runs |
| Late Fall (Bright) | Oct–Dec | Lewis, Cowlitz | Species of concern | Largest-bodied Chinook; most marine-derived nutrient delivery per individual |

(Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Ecological requirements in Cascade systems:**
- **Temperature:** Spawning requires water below 13degC; eggs incubate optimally at 5–12degC; juveniles rear at 10–17degC. Spring Chinook, which hold in headwater pools through summer before spawning in fall, are the most thermally sensitive run type. (Source: GOV-08)
- **Substrate:** Clean gravel for redd construction; fine sediment intrusion reduces egg-to-fry survival; logging road erosion and bank destabilization are primary sediment sources in Cascade watersheds
- **Flow:** Adequate flow for upstream migration (adults), redd aeration (eggs), and smolt outmigration (juveniles). Reduced summer baseflow from snowpack decline directly threatens spring Chinook holding habitat. (Source: GOV-06)
- **Habitat complexity:** Deep pools for adult holding, riffles for spawning, side channels and off-channel alcoves for juvenile rearing. Large woody debris (LWD) from riparian old-growth forest creates and maintains this complexity.

**Conservation context:** Spring Chinook populations in the Willamette Basin (McKenzie, North Santiam, Middle Fork Willamette) have declined to 5–10% of estimated historical abundance. The McKenzie River retains one of the strongest remaining wild spring Chinook populations in the Oregon Cascades, supported by the river's exceptional spring-fed water quality. (Source: GOV-09)

### 2.2 Steelhead (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*)

**Common name:** Steelhead (anadromous rainbow trout)
**Scientific name:** *Oncorhynchus mykiss*
**ESA status:** Threatened (multiple Distinct Population Segments)

Steelhead are the anadromous form of rainbow trout — genetically identical to resident rainbow trout but expressing a life history that includes ocean migration. This dual life history (anadromous vs. resident) is unique among Pacific salmonids and gives steelhead exceptional ecological flexibility, though both forms depend on cold Cascade headwater streams for spawning and juvenile rearing. (Source: GOV-08)

**Key biological distinction:** Unlike all other Pacific salmon, steelhead are iteroparous — they can survive spawning and return to the ocean to spawn again in subsequent years. While repeat spawning rates are typically low (5–15% of adults), this capacity represents a fundamentally different reproductive strategy from the semelparous (die-after-spawning) Pacific salmon. (Source: GOV-08)

**Cascade-specific populations:**

| Run Type | Timing | Key Cascade Rivers | Status | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------------------|--------|-------|
| Winter steelhead | Nov–Apr | Clackamas, North Santiam, South Santiam, Cowlitz, Lewis, Nisqually, Green, Skagit | Threatened | Most widespread run; enters rivers during high winter flows |
| Summer steelhead | May–Oct | North Umpqua, Deschutes, Klickitat, Skagit | Threatened | Holds in deep pools through summer before spawning; North Umpqua run is premier wild population |

(Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Ecological requirements:**
- **Temperature:** Slightly broader thermal tolerance than Chinook; spawning at 4–11degC; juvenile rearing up to 18degC but optimal below 15degC
- **Habitat:** Steelhead juveniles rear 1–3 years in freshwater before smolting, requiring sustained cold-water habitat for longer than species that migrate to sea as fry (chum, pink). This extended freshwater residency makes steelhead juvenile survival especially sensitive to Cascade stream conditions. (Source: GOV-08)
- **Distribution:** Steelhead penetrate higher into Cascade watersheds than most salmon species, using smaller, steeper tributaries. Their upstream distribution limit often coincides with natural barriers (waterfalls, gradient breaks) rather than thermal limits. This wide distribution across the elevation gradient means steelhead populations sample the full range of Cascade hydrological conditions.

**Anadromous vs. resident dynamics:** In the Cascades, anadromous steelhead and resident rainbow trout interbreed freely where they co-occur. The proportion of offspring expressing each life history is influenced by environmental conditions — colder, more productive streams tend to produce more residents, while warmer, food-limited streams produce more ocean-migrating smolts. Climate-driven stream warming may shift this balance by reducing freshwater growth opportunities, potentially increasing smolt production but decreasing freshwater survival. (Source: GOV-08)

### 2.3 Bull Trout (*Salvelinus confluentus*)

**Common name:** Bull trout
**Scientific name:** *Salvelinus confluentus*
**ESA status:** Threatened (range-wide)

Bull trout are the most thermally sensitive salmonid in the Cascade Range and the species most immediately threatened by climate-driven stream warming. They require the coldest and cleanest water of any fish in the system — spawning in headwater streams with temperatures below 9degC and foraging in waters rarely exceeding 12degC. This cold-water obligate status makes bull trout the sentinel species for Cascade aquatic ecosystem health: where bull trout persist, water quality and thermal conditions remain within historical norms; where bull trout disappear, thermal degradation has exceeded biological thresholds. (Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Cascade-specific distribution:**

| Population Complex | Key Waters | Connectivity Status | Trend | Source |
|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|-------|--------|
| McKenzie River | Upper McKenzie, Separation Creek, Horse Creek | Connected (spring-fed; cold) | Declining | GOV-09 |
| North Fork Willamette | Above reservoir system | Fragmented by dams | Declining | GOV-09 |
| Clackamas River | Upper Clackamas tributaries | Partially connected | Declining | GOV-09 |
| Lewis River | Upper Lewis, tributaries above dams | Fragmented; passage improvements underway | Declining | GOV-08 |
| Skagit River system | Headwater tributaries, Cascade River | Connected | Declining | GOV-08 |
| Nooksack River | South Fork tributaries | Fragmented | Declining | GOV-08 |
| White River (Rainier) | Cold headwater reaches | Naturally fragmented (glacial barriers) | Unknown | GOV-08 |

**Ecological requirements:**
- **Temperature:** The defining constraint. Bull trout require water below 12degC for foraging and below 9degC for spawning. These temperatures occur only in the upper reaches of Cascade watersheds — spring-fed streams, glacial tributaries, and shaded headwater channels.
- **Substrate:** Extremely sensitive to fine sediment. Egg survival drops sharply when fine sediment exceeds 20% of substrate composition. Logging roads, forest practices, and post-fire erosion are primary sediment threats.
- **Connectivity:** Bull trout exhibit complex migratory behavior within freshwater systems. Migratory forms move between headwater spawning areas and larger downstream foraging habitats (rivers, lakes). Dam fragmentation has severed these migration corridors throughout the Cascades.

**Thermal refugia dependence:** Bull trout populations are contracting upstream toward the coldest available headwater reaches. As mainstream temperatures warm, the available habitat volume shrinks — a phenomenon sometimes called "thermal squeeze." In the Oregon Cascades, spring-fed streams (McKenzie, Metolius) represent the most durable cold-water refugia because their temperature is buffered by groundwater rather than dependent on snowpack or air temperature. (Source: GOV-06, GOV-09)

### 2.4 Coho Salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*)

**Common name:** Coho salmon (silver salmon)
**Scientific name:** *Oncorhynchus kisutch*
**ESA status:** Threatened (Lower Columbia ESU), Endangered (Upper Columbia — limited Cascade presence)

Coho salmon are the salmonid most dependent on complex off-channel habitat in Cascade river systems. While Chinook and steelhead spawn primarily in mainstem channels and larger tributaries, coho favor small tributaries, side channels, beaver ponds, and off-channel alcoves — habitats created and maintained by natural disturbance processes (flooding, large wood recruitment, beaver activity). (Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

**Cascade-specific ecology:**
- Juveniles rear 12–18 months in freshwater before ocean migration, requiring year-round habitat availability
- Winter survival of juvenile coho depends on off-channel refugia where fish can escape high winter flows — beaver ponds, log jams, and floodplain alcoves are critical
- Coho presence in a stream system is a strong indicator of habitat complexity; their absence often signals simplified channel conditions from land use impacts
- Key Cascade rivers: Clackamas, Salmon River (Mount Hood), Lewis, Cowlitz, Nisqually, Green, Skagit

**Beaver-coho relationship:** Beaver (*Castor canadensis*) dam construction creates the ponded, low-velocity habitat that juvenile coho require for overwinter survival. Where beavers have been removed from Cascade watersheds, coho productivity declines. Beaver reintroduction in southern Washington Cascades (Cascade Forest Conservancy programs) has been explicitly linked to coho recovery goals. (Source: CON-01)

### 2.5 Additional Cascade Salmonids

**Sockeye salmon (*Oncorhynchus nerka*):**
Limited natural Cascade distribution. Baker Lake (Mount Baker drainage) supports one of the few self-sustaining sockeye runs in the Cascades. Sockeye require lake habitat for juvenile rearing (1–2 years), restricting them to lake-connected river systems. (Source: GOV-08)

**Chum salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*):**
Columbia River chum are federally threatened. Chum spawn in the lower reaches of Cascade rivers (typically below 100 m elevation), using groundwater-upwelling sites in mainstem gravel bars. They are less cold-water dependent than other salmonids but vulnerable to flow alteration in the lower river reaches they occupy. (Source: GOV-08)

**Pink salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*):**
Odd-year pink salmon runs occur in several Puget Sound Cascade rivers (Nisqually, Puyallup, Skagit). Pink salmon have a fixed two-year life cycle and spawn in the lowest river reaches, making them the salmonid least dependent on upper Cascade headwater conditions. Even-year populations are a species of concern. (Source: GOV-08)

**Coastal cutthroat trout (*Oncorhynchus clarkii clarkii*):**
Both anadromous and resident forms throughout western Cascade streams. Less thermally sensitive than bull trout but more sensitive than rainbow trout/steelhead. Anadromous forms are declining in many Cascade streams. Federal species of concern. (Source: GOV-08, GOV-09)

## Section 3: Salmon-to-Forest Nutrient Pathway in Cascade Systems (AQUA-03)

> **Cross-reference:** Module 04 provides the comprehensive PNW-wide treatment of the salmon-forest nutrient cascade (CASCADE-01, Sections 1–2). This section documents Cascade-specific aspects of the pathway, including the particular river systems, riparian forest types, and spatial patterns relevant to the Cascade Range.

### 3.1 Marine-Derived Nitrogen (N-15 Isotope Tracing)

Pacific salmon returning to Cascade rivers carry marine-derived nitrogen with a distinctive isotopic signature. The stable isotope nitrogen-15 (N-15) is enriched in marine environments (delta-N-15 values of +10 to +14 per mil in salmon tissue) compared to terrestrial sources (+0 to +3 per mil), providing a natural tracer that follows marine nutrients from salmon carcass through every step of the terrestrial food web. (Source: PR-02)

In the Cascade Range specifically:
- Western slope rivers with intact salmon runs show marine N-15 signatures in riparian vegetation within 500 m of spawning reaches
- The signature is strongest in riparian conifers (Douglas-fir, western red cedar, western hemlock) nearest to spawning gravels, decreasing with distance from stream
- In watersheds with healthy salmon runs, **40–80% of the nitrogen in riparian vegetation is of marine origin** (Source: PR-02)
- Cascade rivers with depleted salmon populations (many Willamette tributaries above dams) show correspondingly reduced marine nitrogen signatures in riparian soils and vegetation

### 3.2 The Cascade Nutrient Transfer Chain

The pathway from ocean to forest operates through a specific chain of agents, each transferring marine nutrients one step further from the stream:

**Step 1 — Salmon deliver marine nutrients to Cascade rivers:**
Adult Chinook returning to the McKenzie, Clackamas, Lewis, or Cowlitz rivers carry 10–25 kg of marine-origin biomass per individual. A spawning run of 10,000 Chinook delivers 100,000–250,000 kg of marine nutrients to a single river system. After spawning, adults die (except steelhead) and their carcasses decompose in and along the stream. (Source: PR-02)

**Step 2 — Mammalian predators transfer carcasses to forest:**
Black bears (*Ursus americanus*) are the primary terrestrial vector in the Cascades. Individual bears ferry an estimated 500–700 salmon per season from streams into surrounding forest, carrying carcasses up to 500 meters from the stream bank. Bears preferentially consume the protein-rich brain and roe, leaving 40–60% of carcass biomass on the forest floor. (Source: PR-02, CON-05)

Additional Cascade predator-vectors include:
- Bald eagles — carry salmon to perch trees up to 1 km from stream
- River otters — deposit carcasses at latrine sites 10–50 m from stream
- Coyotes, raccoons — scavenge carcasses along stream margins
- Gray wolves (where present in North Cascades) — carry carcasses into forest

**Step 3 — Decomposition releases nutrients to Cascade forest soils:**
Invertebrate and microbial decomposers break down salmon carcass material, releasing marine-derived nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon into the soil nutrient pool. In Cascade old-growth riparian forests, the thick organic soil horizons and extensive fungal networks facilitate rapid nutrient cycling from carcass to plant-available forms. (Source: PR-02)

**Step 4 — Cascade conifers uptake marine nitrogen via mycorrhizal networks:**
Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) — the dominant riparian conifers of the western Cascade montane zone — absorb marine-derived nitrogen through their root systems, aided by ectomycorrhizal fungal networks. (Source: PR-02)

> **Cross-module flag:** Mycorrhizal nutrient distribution connects to Fungi module (ectomycorrhizal networks, EMF diversity); riparian conifer growth connects to Flora module; bear and eagle predation connects to Fauna module.

### 3.3 Quantifying the Cascade Nutrient Subsidy

| Metric | Value | Context | Source |
|--------|-------|---------|--------|
| Marine N in riparian vegetation | 40–80% | Healthy Cascade salmon watersheds | PR-02 |
| Riparian tree growth enhancement | Up to 3x | Near salmon streams vs. salmon-free streams | PR-02 (Reynolds, SFU) |
| Bear carcass transport distance | Up to 500 m | From stream into surrounding forest | PR-02, CON-05 |
| Salmon carcasses per bear per season | 500–700 | Individual bear foraging rate | PR-02, CON-05 |
| Carcass biomass left on forest floor | 40–60% | Bears consume brain/roe, leave muscle/skin/skeleton | PR-02 |
| N-15 detection in tree rings | Centuries of record | Historical salmon abundance reconstructed via dendrochronology | PR-02 |
| Wildlife species dependent on salmon | 137+ | Full dependency web documented in Module 04 | CON-05 |

### 3.4 Cascade-Specific Nutrient Pathway Degradation

The Cascade Range has experienced significant salmon population decline compared to historical levels. The Columbia River basin alone historically supported an estimated 7–16 million adult salmon returning per year; current returns represent 5–10% of that number in many tributary systems. (Source: PR-02, CON-05)

The nutrient consequences are proportional:
- **Reduced marine nutrient delivery:** Fewer salmon = fewer kilograms of marine N, P, and C transported upstream
- **Thinned predator-vector web:** Bear density near streams correlates with salmon abundance; fewer salmon means fewer bears performing carcass-to-forest transport
- **Impoverished riparian soils:** Cascade riparian forests above dams that block salmon passage show no marine nitrogen signature — the nutrient subsidy has been completely eliminated by migration barriers
- **Slowed tree growth:** Dendrochronological N-15 records in Cascade riparian trees show growth declines corresponding to periods of salmon population collapse
- **Cascading trophic effects:** The 137+ species dependency web (Module 04) weakens at every node when the salmon nutrient base contracts

**Dam effects in the Cascades:** Hydroelectric and flood-control dams on the Cowlitz, Lewis, North Santiam, South Santiam, Middle Fork Willamette, and other Cascade rivers have severed salmon access to hundreds of miles of historical spawning habitat. Above these dams, the salmon-forest nutrient pathway is entirely absent — a landscape-scale experiment demonstrating what happens when the marine nutrient subsidy is eliminated. (Source: GOV-09)

## Section 4: Klamath Dam Removal (AQUA-04)

### 4.1 Largest Dam Removal in U.S. History

In 2024, the largest dam removal in United States history was completed on the Klamath River at the southern extent of the Cascades ecoregion. Four dams — J.C. Boyle, Copco No. 1, Copco No. 2, and Iron Gate — were removed from the Klamath River in a single coordinated decommissioning project, restoring approximately 400 miles (640 km) of habitat for salmon and other anadromous fish. (Source: GOV-06)

**The four dams removed:**

| Dam | Year Built | Height (ft) | Reservoir Acreage | Primary Purpose |
|-----|-----------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| J.C. Boyle | 1958 | 68 | 420 | Hydroelectric |
| Copco No. 1 | 1918 | 126 | 1,000 | Hydroelectric |
| Copco No. 2 | 1925 | 33 | 73 | Hydroelectric (diversion) |
| Iron Gate | 1962 | 173 | 944 | Hydroelectric; lowermost barrier to salmon migration |

Iron Gate Dam was the lowermost barrier and served as the de facto terminus of salmon migration in the Klamath system for over 60 years. Below Iron Gate, salmon populations declined due to degraded water quality from reservoir releases — warm, nutrient-loaded water that fueled toxic algal blooms (primarily *Microcystis aeruginosa*) and the parasitic organism *Ceratonova shasta*, which caused lethal infections in juvenile salmon. (Source: GOV-06)

### 4.2 Ecological Significance

**Habitat restoration scale:**
- 400 miles of river habitat reconnected — from Iron Gate Dam site to headwater tributaries in southern Oregon
- Historical salmon spawning habitat accessible for the first time in over a century (Copco No. 1 built in 1918)
- Restored access includes tributaries with cold-water springs critical for salmon survival in the warm Klamath basin
- Sediment trapped behind the four dams (estimated 15–20 million cubic yards) is being redistributed naturally, rebuilding gravel spawning substrates downstream

**Species recovery expected:**
- Chinook salmon — spring and fall runs expected to recolonize upper Klamath tributaries
- Coho salmon — Southern Oregon/Northern California Coast ESU (threatened) gains hundreds of miles of potential rearing habitat
- Steelhead — access restored to cold-water headwater spawning reaches above historical dam sites
- Pacific lamprey (*Entosphenus tridentatus*) — culturally significant species for tribal nations; passage restored
- Suckers — Lost River sucker (*Deltistes luxatus*) and shortnose sucker (*Chasmistes brevirostris*), both endangered, benefit from restored water quality

**Ecological process restoration:**
- Free-flowing river conditions restore natural sediment transport, gravel recruitment, and floodplain connectivity
- Elimination of warm-water reservoirs reduces thermal loading, toxic algae, and fish disease
- Restored nutrient cycling — salmon carcasses will once again deliver marine-derived nutrients to upper Klamath tributaries, reconnecting the salmon-forest nutrient pathway severed for over a century
- Riparian vegetation recovery expected along former reservoir margins as natural flow regimes resume

### 4.3 Tribal Significance

The Klamath dam removal carries profound significance for the Indigenous nations of the Klamath Basin, for whom salmon are a foundational cultural, spiritual, and subsistence resource.

**Yurok Nation:**
The Yurok are the largest Native American tribe in California, with reservation lands along the lower Klamath River. Salmon have been central to Yurok culture, ceremony, and diet since time immemorial. The Yurok Tribe was a leading advocate for dam removal, engaging in decades of legal, political, and scientific advocacy. Yurok elders describe the dam removal as the restoration of a relationship — between people and salmon — that the dams severed. The Yurok Tribal Fisheries Program has documented salmon population declines and water quality degradation attributable to the dams for over 20 years. (Source: GOV-06)

**Karuk Tribe:**
The Karuk Tribe, whose traditional territory encompasses the middle Klamath River, has documented the cultural and ecological impacts of the dams across multiple generations. Karuk traditional ecological knowledge identifies specific salmon runs, spawning timing, and habitat conditions that predated the dams. The Karuk Department of Natural Resources contributed both traditional knowledge and modern fisheries science to the dam removal environmental review process. Karuk cultural practices — including the annual World Renewal Ceremony — depend on healthy salmon returns that the dams disrupted. (Source: GOV-06)

**Broader tribal context in the Cascades:**
Salmon treaty rights and co-management extend throughout the Cascade Range:

| Tribal Nation | Cascade River Systems | Co-Management Role | Source |
|--------------|----------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Yakama Nation | Klickitat, White Salmon, upper Columbia tributaries | Treaty fishing rights; salmon hatchery programs; White Salmon dam removal advocacy | GOV-08 |
| Nisqually Tribe | Nisqually River (Mount Rainier drainage) | Treaty fishing rights; co-manages Nisqually River salmon fishery with WDFW; Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge stewardship | GOV-08 |
| Puyallup Tribe | Puyallup River (Mount Rainier drainage) | Treaty fishing rights; fisheries management; habitat restoration in urbanized lower river | GOV-08 |
| Muckleshoot Tribe | Green/Duwamish River, White River | Treaty fishing rights; co-management of Green River salmon | GOV-08 |
| Tulalip Tribes | Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Skagit | Treaty fishing rights; salmon recovery programs | GOV-08 |
| Confederated Tribes of Warm Springs | Deschutes River, Cascade tributaries | Treaty fishing rights; hatchery and wild fish management | GOV-09 |
| Confederated Tribes of Grand Ronde | Willamette Basin | Treaty rights; salmon habitat restoration | GOV-09 |

### 4.4 Klamath as Restoration Precedent

The Klamath dam removal establishes a precedent that reshapes the policy landscape for aquatic restoration throughout the Cascade Range and beyond:

**Scale precedent:** Previous dam removals in the Pacific Northwest — Elwha River (2011–2014, two dams, Olympic Peninsula) and Condit Dam on the White Salmon River (2011, southern Washington Cascades) — demonstrated that dam removal works at moderate scale. The Klamath project, removing four dams simultaneously and restoring 400 miles of habitat, demonstrates that large-scale dam removal is both technically feasible and ecologically justified.

**Multi-stakeholder precedent:** The Klamath removal required agreement among tribal nations, federal and state agencies, irrigators, conservationists, county governments, and the dam operator (PacifiCorp). The negotiation process — formalized in the Klamath Hydroelectric Settlement Agreement — created a template for resolving complex, multi-party dam removal disputes.

**Monitoring precedent:** The Klamath removal includes the most comprehensive dam removal monitoring program ever implemented:
- Pre-removal baseline data (water quality, fish populations, sediment, vegetation) collected over 10+ years
- Real-time monitoring of sediment release, water quality, and fish recolonization during and after removal
- Long-term monitoring plan extending 15+ years post-removal
- Results will inform dam removal decisions at hundreds of aging dams across the Pacific Northwest

**Cascade context:** The Klamath sits at the southern extent of the Cascades ecoregion, where the range transitions into the Klamath Mountains and Siskiyou ranges. This geographic position makes the Klamath dam removal significant for understanding salmon recovery potential across the full north-south extent of the Cascade Range. (Source: GOV-06)

## Section 5: Climate Threats to Cascade Aquatic Systems (AQUA-05)

### 5.1 Streamflow Projections

The U.S. Geological Survey has produced quantitative projections for Cascade streamflow changes under continued warming scenarios. These projections represent the most comprehensive hydrological modeling available for the region and form the scientific basis for salmonid conservation planning. (Source: GOV-06)

**Eastern Cascade slopes (most severe):**
- Summer streamflow losses of **40–60% by 2040** relative to late-20th-century baseline
- Summer streamflow losses of **60–80% by 2080** as snowmelt timing shifts earlier and total snowpack declines
- Some eastern Cascade streams projected to become intermittent during summer by mid-century — streams that currently run year-round will dry completely in July–September
- Groundwater recharge reductions will further reduce spring-fed baseflow contributions

(Source: GOV-06)

**Western Cascade slopes:**
- Less severe absolute losses due to higher total precipitation, but shift from snow-dominated to rain-dominated hydrology fundamentally alters the timing and character of streamflow
- Peak flows shift from spring snowmelt (May–June) to winter rain events (November–January)
- Higher winter peak flows increase flood frequency and magnitude, scouring salmon redds and destabilizing spawning gravels
- Lower, earlier, and shorter summer baseflow period reduces habitat volume during the critical juvenile rearing season

### 5.2 Rain-Dominated vs. Snow-Dominated Watershed Shift

North Cascades watersheds are transitioning from snow-dominated to rain-dominated hydrology — a fundamental regime shift with cascading ecological consequences. (Source: GOV-06, PR-05)

**Snow-dominated watershed (historical):**
- Winter precipitation stored as snowpack
- Gradual spring/summer melt sustains cold baseflow through dry season
- Moderate, predictable spring peak flows
- Summer stream temperatures buffered by cold snowmelt inputs
- Salmonid life cycles synchronized to snowmelt-driven flow and temperature patterns

**Rain-dominated watershed (emerging):**
- Winter precipitation runs off immediately as rain
- No snowpack reservoir for summer storage
- Higher, flashier winter peak flows; earlier runoff
- Dramatically lower summer flows; warmer stream temperatures
- Autumn/winter flooding increases; summer drought stress intensifies
- Salmonid life cycle timing mismatched with altered flow and temperature patterns

**Elevation of the rain-snow transition:**
The elevation at which winter precipitation falls as snow rather than rain is shifting upward. Each 1degC of warming raises the rain-snow transition by approximately 150 meters (500 feet). In the Cascades, this means:
- At +1degC warming: snow zone retreats above ~1,350 m (4,400 ft) — lower montane zone loses seasonal snowpack
- At +2degC warming: snow zone above ~1,500 m (4,900 ft) — most western Cascade montane forest transitions from snow to rain
- At +3degC warming: snow zone above ~1,650 m (5,400 ft) — only upper montane and subalpine zones retain seasonal snow

(Source: PR-05)

### 5.3 Stream Temperature Increases

Stream temperatures in Cascade rivers are rising toward and in some cases already exceeding thresholds critical for salmonid survival. (Source: GOV-06)

**Salmonid thermal thresholds:**

| Species | Spawning Max (degC) | Rearing Optimal (degC) | Lethal Limit (degC) | Cascade Status |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Bull trout | 9 | 4–10 | 18 | Already near upper limits in many streams |
| Spring Chinook | 13 | 10–17 | 22 | Summer holding habitat shrinking |
| Steelhead | 11 | 10–17 | 23 | Summer rearing habitat warming |
| Coho | 13 | 8–15 | 25 | Less threatened by temperature alone |
| Chum | 14 | 8–14 | 22 | Lower-river thermal exposure |

**Mechanisms of stream warming in the Cascades:**
1. **Reduced snowmelt input:** Less snowpack means less cold meltwater mixing into streams during summer
2. **Glacial retreat:** Retreating glaciers provide diminishing cold-water subsidies during the hottest months
3. **Air temperature increase:** Direct warming of stream surface; effect amplified in streams with reduced riparian shade (logged areas)
4. **Riparian deforestation:** Loss of streamside old-growth canopy increases solar radiation reaching the stream surface; logged riparian zones can increase stream temperature by 3–8degC compared to forested reaches
5. **Reduced baseflow volume:** Lower summer flows = less water volume to absorb solar heat input = faster warming per unit of energy input

### 5.4 Glacial Retreat and Cold-Water Supply

Cascades glaciers are retreating at accelerating rates, reducing the late-summer cold-water subsidy that downstream salmonids depend on during the warmest period of the year. (Source: GOV-04)

**Documented retreat:**
- North Cascades glaciers have lost an estimated 40–50% of area since the mid-20th century
- Mount Rainier's glaciers have thinned by 25–30% in volume since 1970
- Several smaller Cascade glaciers have disappeared entirely since the 1980s
- Glacier Peak and Mount Baker glaciers show accelerating retreat since 2000

**Downstream consequences:**
- Reduced August–September glacial discharge precisely when streams are warmest and flows are lowest
- Loss of daily cold-water pulse (diurnal meltwater cycle) that moderates peak stream temperatures
- Reduced sediment delivery changes downstream channel morphology
- Transition from glacial-meltwater-fed streams to purely snowmelt/rain-fed streams eliminates the thermal buffering that extended the cold-water season beyond snowpack depletion

### 5.5 Compound Threat Interactions

The threats to Cascade aquatic systems do not operate independently — they compound and amplify each other:

| Threat Combination | Mechanism | Severity |
|-------------------|-----------|----------|
| Snowpack loss + stream warming | Less cold meltwater to dilute warming; lower flows warm faster | Critical |
| Rain shift + winter flooding | Rain-on-snow events scour salmon redds; higher peak flows destroy spawning gravel structure | High |
| Glacial retreat + summer drought | Loss of glacial cold-water subsidy during the period of greatest thermal stress | Critical for bull trout |
| Wildfire + sediment loading | Post-fire erosion fills stream gravels with fine sediment; reduces egg survival and invertebrate habitat | High |
| Temperature rise + invasive species | Warming mainstem habitat expands range of warm-water invasives (smallmouth bass, walleye) into historically cold-water salmonid zones | Increasing |
| Reduced flow + water withdrawal | Agricultural and municipal diversions compete with salmon for diminished baseflow | Severe on eastern slopes |

(Sources: GOV-06, PR-05, GOV-08)

## Section 6: Cross-Module Relationships (AQUA-06)

### 6.1 Salmon-Nitrogen to Forest Soil to Mycorrhizal Network

**Connects to:** Fungi module (ectomycorrhizal networks), Flora module (conifer growth), Fauna module (bear, eagle predation)

The salmon-to-forest nutrient pathway documented in Section 3 is the most extensive cross-module relationship in the Cascade Range ecosystem. Marine-derived nitrogen from salmon carcasses enters riparian soils, is absorbed by conifer roots via ectomycorrhizal fungal networks (EMF), and is redistributed through the mycorrhizal "wood-wide web" connecting trees across the forest stand.

**Cascade-specific cross-module chain:**
1. Chinook/coho/steelhead deliver marine N to McKenzie, Clackamas, Lewis, Cowlitz, Nisqually rivers (Aquatic)
2. Black bears, bald eagles transport carcasses into western Cascade montane riparian forest (Fauna)
3. Decomposer invertebrates and soil microbes release marine N into riparian soil (Fungi — decomposer community)
4. Douglas-fir, western hemlock, western red cedar absorb marine N via EMF root symbionts (Flora + Fungi)
5. EMF hyphae redistribute marine N through connected tree networks (Fungi — mycorrhizal network)
6. Enhanced tree growth increases canopy closure, litter production, and coarse woody debris input to streams (Flora → Aquatic feedback loop)

### 6.2 Beaver Hydrology Engineering

**Connects to:** Fauna module (beaver ecology), Flora module (riparian vegetation)

Beaver (*Castor canadensis*) engineering directly modifies Cascade stream hydrology in ways that benefit salmonids:

| Beaver Effect | Hydrological Mechanism | Salmonid Benefit | Source |
|--------------|----------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Dam construction | Creates ponded habitat; raises water table; stores water | Coho juvenile overwinter survival habitat; base flow augmentation | CON-01 |
| Channel widening | Spreads flow across floodplain; reduces stream velocity | Increased rearing habitat area; thermal diversity (warm shallows + cold pools) | CON-01 |
| Sediment trapping | Fine sediment settles in beaver ponds rather than passing downstream | Cleaner spawning gravel in downstream reaches | CON-01 |
| Groundwater recharge | Raised water table from dam impoundment recharges shallow aquifer | Enhanced cold-water baseflow downstream of beaver complexes | CON-01 |
| Riparian vegetation stimulation | Beaver browsing promotes willow/alder regeneration; raised water table supports cottonwood | Enhanced riparian shade, root stabilization, and food web productivity | CON-01 |

Cascade Forest Conservancy programs in southern Washington Cascades have demonstrated measurable improvements in coho salmon habitat where beavers have been reintroduced or allowed to recolonize. (Source: CON-01)

### 6.3 Riparian Vegetation Dependence

**Connects to:** Flora module (riparian forest ecology)

Cascade salmonids depend on riparian vegetation for:
- **Thermal regulation:** Streamside canopy shade reduces solar heating of stream surface by 50–80%; removal of riparian trees can increase stream temperature by 3–8degC
- **Large woody debris (LWD):** Fallen trees create pools, riffles, and side channels that salmonids require for spawning, rearing, and hiding from predators. Old-growth riparian trees produce the largest, most persistent LWD
- **Bank stabilization:** Root networks hold stream banks in place, preventing erosion and fine sediment loading
- **Terrestrial food input:** Insects falling from riparian vegetation are a primary food source for juvenile salmon and steelhead
- **Nutrient cycling:** Leaf litter feeds the aquatic invertebrate community that salmon juveniles consume

In the western Cascade montane zone, old-growth Douglas-fir, western red cedar, and western hemlock provide all of these functions at maximum capacity. Second-growth forests managed on short rotation cycles produce less shade, smaller LWD, and less complex riparian habitat — measurably reducing salmonid productivity. (Source: GOV-09, CON-01)

### 6.4 Climate and Snowpack Decline

**Connects to:** Threats module (climate projections, fire regime changes)

The aquatic climate threats documented in Section 5 are expressions of the broader Cascade climate trajectory:
- Snowpack decline reduces both aquatic habitat and soil moisture for terrestrial vegetation
- Increased wildfire frequency (driven by drought stress in terrestrial systems) produces post-fire sediment pulses that degrade aquatic habitat
- Earlier spring phenology mismatches with salmon migration and spawning timing evolved for snowmelt-driven flow patterns
- Thermal stress in aquatic systems mirrors the thermal stress on subalpine meadow communities, alpine organisms, and cold-adapted terrestrial species documented in other modules

## Section 7: Key Cascade River Profiles

### 7.1 McKenzie River (Oregon)

**Headwaters:** High Cascades volcanic plateau, approximately 5,500 ft elevation
**Length:** 90 miles from source to Willamette River confluence
**Key feature:** Extraordinary spring-fed water clarity and cold temperatures from High Cascade lava flow aquifers
**Salmonids:** Spring Chinook (wild population stronghold), bull trout, steelhead, cutthroat trout
**Significance:** The McKenzie River represents what a healthy Cascade salmon river looks like — spring-fed cold water, intact riparian forest, complex channel structure, and self-sustaining wild fish populations. It serves as a reference system for restoration targets in degraded Cascade rivers.
**Threats:** Upstream reservoir management (Cougar Dam, Blue River Dam) alters temperature and flow regimes in tributary reaches; climate-driven warming of even the spring-fed system is approaching bull trout thermal limits.
(Source: GOV-09)

### 7.2 Skagit River System (Washington)

**Headwaters:** North Cascades, approximately 8,000 ft elevation
**Length:** 150 miles from headwaters to Puget Sound
**Key feature:** Most productive salmon river in the Puget Sound basin; supports all five Pacific salmon species plus steelhead and bull trout
**Salmonids:** Chinook, coho, sockeye, chum, pink, steelhead, bull trout
**Significance:** The Skagit system demonstrates the full potential of a Cascade river supporting the complete Pacific salmonid assemblage. Co-management between Skagit System Cooperative (tribal) and WDFW provides a model for collaborative fisheries management.
**Threats:** Glacial retreat in North Cascades headwaters; ongoing habitat simplification in the lower floodplain; climate-driven hydrological regime shift.
(Source: GOV-08)

### 7.3 Lewis River (Washington)

**Headwaters:** Mount Adams glaciers, approximately 8,300 ft elevation
**Length:** 95 miles from headwaters to Columbia River confluence
**Key feature:** Three hydroelectric dams (Merwin, Yale, Swift) with active fish passage improvement programs
**Salmonids:** Spring Chinook, coho, steelhead, bull trout
**Significance:** The Lewis River represents a major test case for whether fish passage engineering at existing dams can restore salmon access to historical habitat — a strategy with implications for dozens of dammed Cascade rivers.
**Threats:** Continued dam passage challenges; sediment management below dams; habitat degradation from historical logging.
(Source: GOV-08, CON-01)

### 7.4 Nisqually River (Washington)

**Headwaters:** Nisqually Glacier, Mount Rainier, approximately 14,411 ft elevation
**Length:** 78 miles from glacier terminus to Puget Sound
**Key feature:** Glacier-to-estuary system managed cooperatively by Nisqually Tribe and WDFW; Nisqually National Wildlife Refuge protects the estuary
**Salmonids:** Chinook, coho, steelhead, chum, pink
**Significance:** The Nisqually demonstrates the full Cascade hydrological gradient from glacier to tidewater, with tribal co-management providing a governance model. The estuary restoration (largest in Puget Sound history, reclaiming 762 acres of tidal habitat in 2009) complements upstream habitat quality.
**Threats:** Glacial retreat reducing late-summer cold-water input; urbanization in the mid-river corridor; climate-driven stream warming.
(Source: GOV-08)

## Section 8: Aquatic Invertebrate Community

### 8.1 Benthic Macroinvertebrates as Ecosystem Indicators

The health of Cascade cold-water streams is assessed through benthic macroinvertebrate communities — the aquatic insects, crustaceans, and other invertebrates living on and in stream substrates. These organisms form the base of the stream food web that salmonids depend on, and their community composition directly reflects water quality, temperature, substrate condition, and flow regime. (Source: GOV-06)

**EPT Index (Ephemeroptera, Plecoptera, Trichoptera):**

The EPT index — the proportion of the benthic community composed of mayflies (Ephemeroptera), stoneflies (Plecoptera), and caddisflies (Trichoptera) — is the standard metric for cold-water stream health in the Cascades:

| EPT Category | Indicator Value | Cascade Context |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| High EPT diversity (>15 taxa) | Excellent water quality; cold, clean, well-oxygenated | Undisturbed headwater streams; spring-fed systems |
| Moderate EPT (8–15 taxa) | Good water quality; some disturbance | Managed forests with intact riparian buffers |
| Low EPT (<8 taxa) | Degraded conditions; warming, sedimentation, or pollution | Below dams, in logged watersheds, or urbanized reaches |

**Key cold-water indicator taxa in the Cascades:**

| Order | Representative Taxa | Thermal Sensitivity | Ecological Role |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Plecoptera (stoneflies) | *Pteronarcys*, *Sweltsa*, *Zapada* | Most sensitive; many species intolerant above 12degC | Shredders (process leaf litter), predators; primary food for juvenile salmon |
| Ephemeroptera (mayflies) | *Drunella*, *Epeorus*, *Rhithrogena* | Sensitive; optimal below 15degC | Grazers, collector-gatherers; critical salmon prey |
| Trichoptera (caddisflies) | *Glossosoma*, *Rhyacophila*, *Brachycentrus* | Moderate sensitivity; variable by species | Filter-feeders, scrapers, predators; abundant salmon prey |

### 8.2 Salmon Carcass Decomposer Community

Salmon carcasses support a specialized decomposer community in Cascade streams (detailed in Module 04, Section 4 — Invertebrates). In the Cascade-specific context, carcass-dependent invertebrates include aquatic chironomid midges, caddisfly larvae, amphipods, and aquatic snails that colonize and consume carcass material, converting marine-derived nutrients into the aquatic invertebrate biomass that juvenile salmon consume. This creates a nutrient feedback loop: adult salmon deliver marine nutrients → invertebrates process carcass material → juvenile salmon consume enriched invertebrates → juveniles grow, smolt, and eventually return as adults to deliver the next generation of marine nutrients. (Source: PR-02)
