## Introduction

The Cascade Range sustains one of the most floristically complex mountain systems in North America. From the oak savannas of the western foothills to the cushion plants clinging to volcanic scree above 7,000 feet, the range supports an estimated 3,000+ vascular plant species across its Washington and Oregon extent, with North Cascades National Park alone documenting 1,630 vascular plant species — a figure that would likely more than double with the inclusion of non-vascular plants and lichens (GOV-04). This diversity is organized along two dominant gradients: the elevation transect from valley floor to summit, and the west-to-east precipitation gradient created by the Cascade crest rain shadow.

The five elevation zones defined in this survey — Western Foothills, Montane, Subalpine, Alpine, and Eastern Slopes — are not arbitrary divisions but represent distinct ecological regimes, each governed by characteristic temperature ranges, precipitation patterns, growing season lengths, soil development, disturbance regimes, and evolutionary histories. The boundaries between zones are themselves ecological features: ecotones where species from adjacent zones intermingle, creating transition communities of exceptional diversity and conservation significance.

This survey documents plant communities within each zone, profiles at least eight endemic, rare, or threatened species, examines the foothill ecotones now at greatest risk of conversion, and provides a detailed treatment of whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) — the alpine keystone species facing a convergence of threats that makes it one of the most imperiled trees in western North America.

Throughout this document, cross-module relationships are flagged for the Phase 617 ecological network synthesis, connecting the flora survey to fungi (mycorrhizal networks, decomposition), fauna (seed dispersal, herbivory, pollination), aquatic systems (riparian vegetation, hydrological function), and disturbance ecology (fire regimes, volcanic succession).

## Zone 1: Western Foothills (500–2,000 ft / 150–610 m)

### Overview

The Western Foothills zone occupies the transition between the agricultural lowlands of the Willamette Valley and Puget Lowland and the montane forests of the Cascades proper. This zone is defined by a warm, dry-summer Mediterranean climate with 40–60 inches of annual precipitation, a growing season of 200–250 days, and deep alluvial and glacial soils (GOV-03, GOV-07). It is the most threatened zone in the Cascade system — over 90% of its original extent has been lost to agricultural conversion, urban expansion, and the encroachment of Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) following a century of fire suppression (GOV-09, CON-02).

The foothill zone is treated in detail in the Foothill Ecotones section below. This section provides the botanical framework.

### Dominant Plant Communities

#### Oregon White Oak Savanna

The historically dominant community of the Western Foothills is the Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) savanna — an open, park-like landscape of widely spaced oaks over a grassland understory maintained by frequent, low-intensity fire set by the Kalapuya, Molala, and other Willamette Valley nations for millennia (GOV-09, PR-07). This is not a "natural" community in the sense of being unmanaged — it is a cultural landscape shaped by deliberate Indigenous land stewardship.

**Oregon white oak** (*Quercus garryana*) — The signature tree of the Western Foothills, reaching 60–80 feet in open-grown specimens with broad, spreading crowns. Deciduous broadleaf tree with deeply lobed leaves. Fire-adapted: thick bark resists low-intensity surface fire; sprouts vigorously from rootstock after top-kill. In the absence of fire, seedlings and saplings are overtopped and shaded out by Douglas-fir, which grows faster and taller. Oregon white oak savanna has declined by more than 90% from its historical extent in the Willamette Valley (GOV-09). The acorn crop is a critical food resource for western gray squirrel (*Sciurus griseus*), band-tailed pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*), Lewis's woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*), and Steller's jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) — cross-module flag: fauna (GOV-09).

**Characteristic savanna understory species:**
- *Camassia quamash* (common camas) — Perennial forb with blue-purple flowers. Bulbs were a staple food of the Kalapuya and Molala nations, harvested from managed prairies. Camas prairies represent one of the most significant cultural-botanical landscapes in the Pacific Northwest. Cross-module flag: foothill ecotones (GOV-09).
- *Camassia leichtlinii* (great camas) — Larger species, often growing in wetter prairie microsites. Distinguished from common camas by tepals that twist together over the capsule after flowering (GOV-09).
- *Danthonia californica* (California oatgrass) — Perennial bunchgrass, dominant in upland prairie remnants. Fire-adapted: regrows rapidly after burning (GOV-07).
- *Festuca roemeri* (Roemer's fescue) — Fine-leaved perennial bunchgrass, a defining species of Willamette Valley upland prairies. Declining due to competition from non-native grasses (GOV-09).
- *Eriophyllum lanatum* (Oregon sunshine) — Perennial forb with yellow daisy-like flowers. Indicator of dry, open prairie (GOV-09).
- *Dodecatheon hendersonii* (Henderson's shooting star) — Spring ephemeral forb. Insect-pollinated. Indicator of native prairie condition (GOV-09).
- *Delphinium menziesii* (Menzies' larkspur) — Perennial forb of open grasslands and oak savanna margins (GOV-09).
- *Fragaria virginiana* (wild strawberry) — Low-growing perennial forb providing ground cover and food for wildlife (GOV-07).
- *Lomatium nudicaule* (barestem biscuitroot) — Perennial forb; root was a food plant for Kalapuya and Molala nations (GOV-09).
- *Balsamorhiza deltoidea* (deltoid balsamroot) — Large-leaved perennial forb with sunflower-like blooms, conspicuous in spring prairies (GOV-09).

#### Mixed Oak-Conifer Woodland

At the upper margin of the foothill zone and on north-facing slopes, Oregon white oak intermingles with Douglas-fir and, in places, bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*), creating a transitional woodland community. This ecotone is expanding at the expense of open savanna due to fire suppression. The understory shifts from grassland species to a mix of shrubs:

- *Corylus cornuta* (beaked hazelnut) — Deciduous shrub forming dense thickets in the understory of oak-conifer transition zones (GOV-07).
- *Symphoricarpos albus* (common snowberry) — Deciduous shrub with white berries persisting into winter; important wildlife food (GOV-07).
- *Rosa nutkana* (Nootka rose) — Deciduous shrub with single pink flowers and red hips (GOV-07).
- *Toxicodendron diversilobum* (western poison oak) — Deciduous shrub or vine. Dominant in dry, open habitats of the lower foothills and ecotone (GOV-07).
- *Holodiscus discolor* (ocean spray) — Deciduous shrub with cascading white flower clusters (GOV-07).

#### Riparian Corridors

Streams and rivers crossing the foothill zone support distinct riparian communities:

- *Alnus rubra* (red alder) — Deciduous broadleaf tree; nitrogen-fixer via *Frankia* root nodule bacteria, adding 100–300 kg N/ha/year to riparian soils. Cross-module flag: fungi (nutrient cycling), aquatic (stream shading, large woody debris) (GOV-07, PR-06).
- *Fraxinus latifolia* (Oregon ash) — Deciduous broadleaf tree of low-elevation riparian areas and seasonally flooded prairies. Increasingly rare due to habitat conversion (GOV-09).
- *Salix* spp. (willows) — Multiple species along stream banks; important for bank stabilization and riparian habitat structure. Cross-module flag: aquatic (GOV-07).
- *Populus trichocarpa* (black cottonwood) — Fast-growing deciduous tree of floodplains and gravel bars. Supports rich invertebrate communities on leaf litter. Cross-module flag: aquatic, fauna (GOV-07).

### Foothill Bryophytes and Ferns

The relatively dry foothill zone supports fewer bryophyte species than the montane zone, but several ferns characterize the transition:

- *Pteridium aquilinum* (bracken fern) — Deciduous fern, abundant in open and recently burned areas. One of the first species to recolonize after fire (GOV-07).
- *Polystichum munitum* (sword fern) — Evergreen fern, dominant in shaded north-facing slopes and ravines at the upper foothill boundary where moisture increases (GOV-07).
- *Adiantum pedatum* (maidenhair fern) — Delicate deciduous fern of moist, shaded rock faces and seepage areas (GOV-07).

### Ecotone: Foothills to Montane (1,500–2,500 ft)

The transition from Western Foothills to Montane forest is marked by the closing of the canopy. Open oak savanna and mixed woodland give way to continuous Douglas-fir forest with western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*) in the understory. The transition is visible as a shift from:
- Grass-dominated ground layer → fern-dominated ground layer (sword fern, Oregon grape)
- Deciduous broadleaf canopy (oak, maple) → evergreen conifer canopy (Douglas-fir)
- Fire-maintained open structure → shade-maintained closed structure
- Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) and Oregon grape (*Mahonia nervosa*) become continuous understory dominants

This ecotone has been moving downslope over the past century as fire suppression allows Douglas-fir to colonize former oak savanna. The boundary is not a line but a gradient spanning 500–1,000 feet of elevation (GOV-09, PR-07).

**Cross-module flags:**
- Mycorrhizal transition: ectomycorrhizal fungi associated with Douglas-fir replace arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi associated with grassland species at this boundary → Phase 617 fungi
- Fire regime shift: foothill surface fire regime gives way to montane mixed-severity fire regime → Phase 617 disturbance
- Wildlife corridor: elk (*Cervus canadensis*) and black-tailed deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*) move between zones seasonally → Phase 617 fauna

## Zone 2: Montane Forest (2,000–4,500 ft / 610–1,370 m)

### Overview

The Montane zone is the heart of the Cascade forest. Receiving 60–100 inches of annual precipitation with a growing season of 150–200 days, this zone supports the iconic old-growth Douglas-fir/western hemlock forests that define the Pacific Northwest landscape (GOV-03, GOV-07). The Western Cascades Lowlands and Valleys subregion — the largest Cascade subregion at 3,905 square miles in Oregon and 2,397 square miles in Washington — falls primarily within this zone (GOV-07). Less than 10% of this subregion in King County, Washington, remains in late seral (old-growth) condition (GOV-06).

Old-growth stands in this zone support trees exceeding 1,000 years of age and 10 feet (3 m) in trunk diameter, with canopy heights surpassing 250 feet. These forests carry among the highest standing biomass of any terrestrial ecosystem on Earth, rivaled only by coast redwood (*Sequoia sempervirens*) forests to the south (PR-06).

### Dominant Plant Communities

#### Douglas-fir/Western Hemlock Forest

The signature community of the montane zone, occupying the vast majority of the elevation band from 2,000 to 4,500 feet on western Cascade slopes.

**Canopy dominants:**

- *Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Douglas-fir) — Emergent conifer, the dominant seral species. Old-growth specimens reach 250–330 feet in height, 8–12 feet in trunk diameter, and ages exceeding 1,000 years. Deeply furrowed bark is fire-resistant — an adaptation to the mixed-severity fire regime of the montane zone. The single most important host tree for ectomycorrhizal fungi in the Cascade system: a single old-growth Douglas-fir can connect to 47 other trees through Common Mycorrhizal Networks (CMNs) mediated by *Rhizopogon* spp. and other EMF (PR-03). Cross-module flags: fungi (obligate mycorrhizal mutualism), fauna (northern spotted owl habitat), aquatic (large woody debris in streams) (GOV-07, PR-03).

- *Tsuga heterophylla* (western hemlock) — Shade-tolerant climax species of the montane zone. Replaces Douglas-fir in the long absence of fire. Graceful drooping leader, delicate flat needles, small pendulous cones. Regenerates prolifically on nurse logs and stumps — a successional strategy tied to decomposition cycles mediated by saprotrophic fungi. Cross-module flag: fungi (nurse-log decomposition cycle) (GOV-07).

- *Thuja plicata* (western red cedar) — Co-dominant on moist sites, stream terraces, and seepage areas. Lives 1,000+ years. Decay-resistant heartwood creates nurse logs persisting for centuries. Culturally significant to numerous Pacific Northwest nations for canoes, longhouses, baskets, and ceremonial objects. Cross-module flag: aquatic (riparian structure) (GOV-07).

- *Abies amabilis* (Pacific silver fir) — Increasingly abundant toward the upper boundary of the montane zone (3,500–4,500 ft). Shade-tolerant, snow-tolerant. Becomes canopy dominant in the transitional band between montane and subalpine (GOV-07).

**Understory species:**

- *Acer circinatum* (vine maple) — Deciduous shrub/small tree, the dominant mid-canopy species in montane forests. Multi-stemmed, sprawling habit; brilliant red-orange fall color (GOV-07).
- *Gaultheria shallon* (salal) — Evergreen shrub forming dense, continuous cover under closed canopy. Leathery leaves; edible purple-black berries (GOV-07).
- *Mahonia nervosa* (dull Oregon grape) — Low evergreen shrub with holly-like compound leaves and yellow flowers (GOV-07).
- *Vaccinium parvifolium* (red huckleberry) — Deciduous shrub growing almost exclusively on nurse logs and stumps. Visual tracer of decomposition history (GOV-07).
- *Vaccinium membranaceum* (big huckleberry) — Deciduous shrub dominant at upper montane elevations. Fruit historically gathered by Yakama, Molala, and other nations. Cross-module flag: fauna (bear forage) (GOV-09).
- *Oplopanax horridus* (devil's club) — Deciduous shrub with dense spines. Indicator of wet, rich microsites — stream banks, seepage areas, alluvial terraces (GOV-07).
- *Rubus spectabilis* (salmonberry) — Deciduous shrub of riparian areas and disturbed sites. Early-season fruit supports migratory birds. Cross-module flag: fauna (GOV-07).

**Herb and ground layer:**

- *Polystichum munitum* (sword fern) — Dominant ground-layer species, forming continuous carpets under closed canopy. Evergreen; fronds to 5 feet (GOV-07).
- *Oxalis oregana* (redwood sorrel) — Shade-tolerant perennial forb carpeting the forest floor. Leaves close in response to direct sunlight (GOV-07).
- *Blechnum spicant* (deer fern) — Dimorphic evergreen fern with separate sterile and fertile fronds (GOV-07).
- *Athyrium filix-femina* (lady fern) — Large deciduous fern of moist meadows and stream banks (GOV-07).
- *Tiarella trifoliata* (foam flower) — Perennial forb with delicate white flower sprays (GOV-07).
- *Achlys triphylla* (vanilla leaf) — Perennial forb; vanilla-scented when dried. Indicator of mesic forest (GOV-07).
- *Asarum caudatum* (wild ginger) — Ground-hugging perennial forb with heart-shaped leaves and hidden maroon flowers (GOV-07).
- *Linnaea borealis* (twinflower) — Trailing sub-shrub with paired bell-shaped pink flowers. Circumboreal species at southern range limit (GOV-07).
- *Cornus canadensis* (bunchberry) — Ground-layer perennial forb with white bracts; becomes more abundant at upper montane elevations (GOV-07).
- *Goodyera oblongifolia* (western rattlesnake plantain) — Terrestrial orchid with white-veined basal leaves. Mycorrhizal-dependent. Cross-module flag: fungi (GOV-07).

#### Montane Bryophytes and Epiphytes

Old-growth montane forests support extraordinary bryophyte diversity. Thick moss carpets on the forest floor, nurse logs, and tree trunks contribute to moisture retention and nutrient cycling.

- *Hylocomium splendens* (stair-step moss) — The dominant ground moss of Pacific Northwest montane forests. Grows in tiered annual increments, each step representing one year of growth. Indicator of stable, undisturbed forest floor (GOV-07).
- *Rhytidiadelphus loreus* (lanky moss) — Large feathery moss of moist forest floor. Often co-dominant with stair-step moss (GOV-07).
- *Isothecium myosuroides* (cat-tail moss) — Primary canopy epiphyte on trunks and branches; forms thick mats contributing to canopy moisture storage (GOV-07).
- *Lobaria pulmonaria* (lungwort lichen) — Large foliose lichen on old-growth trunks. Fixes atmospheric nitrogen via cyanobacterial symbionts — a significant pathway for nitrogen input to forest floor. Old-growth indicator. Cross-module flag: fungi (nitrogen fixation), nutrient cycling (GOV-07, PR-06).
- *Selaginella* spp. (club mosses) — Carpet nurse logs and wet rock faces. Indicator of persistent moisture and minimal disturbance (GOV-07).
- Liverworts (*Porella*, *Scapania*, *Frullania* spp.) — Diverse epiphytic community on bark and decaying wood. Contribute to canopy biomass exceeding 4 tons per hectare in old-growth stands (GOV-07).

#### Montane Riparian Communities

Streams within the montane zone support distinct riparian assemblages that provide critical ecological linkages between terrestrial and aquatic systems.

- *Alnus rubra* (red alder) — Dominant riparian tree at lower montane elevations. Nitrogen fixation enriches stream-side soils. Leaf litter is a primary food base for aquatic macroinvertebrates. Cross-module flag: aquatic (nutrient input, food web base) (GOV-07).
- *Acer macrophyllum* (bigleaf maple) — Deciduous broadleaf tree of riparian corridors. Massive epiphyte loads — licorice fern (*Polypodium glycyrrhiza*), mosses, and lichens colonize the heavy horizontal branches. Cross-module flag: fauna (cavity-nesting habitat) (GOV-07).
- *Lysichiton americanus* (western skunk cabbage) — Emergent forb of swamps and seepage areas. One of the earliest spring bloomers, generating heat through thermogenesis. Cross-module flag: aquatic (wetland indicator) (GOV-07).
- *Oplopanax horridus* (devil's club) — Dense stands along stream banks provide wildlife cover and indicate high water tables (GOV-07).

### Ecotone: Montane to Subalpine (4,000–5,000 ft)

The upper boundary of the montane zone is a gradual transition marked by:
- Douglas-fir declining in abundance → mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*) and subalpine fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*) increasing
- Western hemlock replaced by Pacific silver fir as the dominant shade-tolerant species
- Snow persistence shifts from seasonal to semi-permanent (October–June)
- Meadow openings begin to appear between tree stands
- Understory shifts from fern-dominated to heather-dominated (*Phyllodoce*, *Cassiope* spp.)
- *Abies procera* (noble fir) — a Cascade Range near-endemic — reaches maximum abundance in this transition band. The largest true fir species in the world, reaching 250+ feet. Cross-module flag: fungi (ectomycorrhizal host) (GOV-07, GOV-03).

## Zone 3: Upper Montane / Subalpine (4,500–6,500 ft / 1,370–1,980 m)

### Overview

The Subalpine zone is the snow kingdom of the Cascades. Annual precipitation of 60–110 inches falls predominantly as snow, with accumulations exceeding 500 inches at higher elevations and persistence from October through June or July (GOV-03, GOV-07). The growing season compresses to 60–120 days. This zone corresponds to the Mountain Hemlock Ecoregion of the EPA Level IV classification and supports the meadow-forest mosaics that are among the most visually spectacular and ecologically important habitats in the range.

Cascade snowpack in this zone is more vulnerable to climate warming than that of interior mountain ranges because the maritime climate places winter temperatures closer to the melting threshold. Small increases in temperature convert snowfall to rain, reducing snowpack depth and duration with cascading effects on growing season length, stream hydrology, and species composition (PR-05, GOV-06).

### Dominant Plant Communities

#### Mountain Hemlock Forest

- *Tsuga mertensiana* (mountain hemlock) — Dominant canopy species of the wettest and coldest Cascade forest zone. Slow-growing; individuals 300–500 years old reach moderate sizes. Distinctive blue-green needles arranged radially around the twig (unlike the flat sprays of western hemlock). Old-growth indicator. Primary source: GOV-07.

- *Abies lasiocarpa* (subalpine fir) — Co-dominant with mountain hemlock. Narrow, spire-shaped crown efficiently sheds heavy snow loads. Shade-tolerant. At its upper elevational limit, assumes krummholz (wind-flagged, stunted) growth forms. Cross-module flag: fauna (red crossbill, *Loxia curvirostra*, feeds on cones) (GOV-07).

- *Abies amabilis* (Pacific silver fir) — Abundant at the lower boundary of the subalpine zone, declining upward. Marks the transition from montane to subalpine (GOV-07).

- *Chamaecyparis nootkatensis* (Alaska yellow-cedar) — A long-lived conifer (specimens exceeding 1,000 years) of wet, high-elevation sites. Currently experiencing dramatic die-off attributed to changing winter snowpack: reduced snowfall leaves shallow roots exposed to lethal freezing temperatures that were historically insulated by deep snow. Federally listed as threatened. This die-off represents one of the clearest examples of climate-driven tree mortality in North America. Cross-module flag: fungi (host for distinctive ectomycorrhizal community), disturbance ecology (GOV-06, PR-05, PR-06).

- *Abies procera* (noble fir) — Reaches its upper limit at the lower boundary of the subalpine zone. Cascade Range near-endemic (GOV-07).

#### Subalpine Meadow-Forest Mosaic

The iconic subalpine landscape is a patchwork of tree islands and open meadows, maintained by the interplay of snowpack duration, wind exposure, and growing season length. Meadows occur where late-lying snow prevents tree establishment; tree islands occupy sites where snow melts earliest.

**Meadow wildflowers:**

- *Erythronium montanum* (avalanche lily) — Spring ephemeral emerging through melting snow at timberline. White flowers with recurved petals. One of the first species to bloom in subalpine meadows, often flowering within days of snowmelt. Cross-module flag: fauna (pollinator resource) (GOV-07).
- *Erythronium grandiflorum* (glacier lily) — Yellow-flowered counterpart to avalanche lily, more common on eastern slopes. Bulbs are important food for pocket gophers and bears. Cross-module flag: fauna (GOV-07).
- *Lupinus* spp. (lupines, multiple species) — Perennial forbs with palmately compound leaves and blue-purple racemes. Nitrogen-fixers via root nodule symbiosis with *Rhizobium*. Dominant in many subalpine meadows. Cross-module flag: fungi (nitrogen cycling) (GOV-07).
- *Castilleja* spp. (paintbrush, multiple species including *C. miniata*, *C. rupicola*) — Hemiparasitic perennial forbs parasitizing the roots of neighboring plants. Brilliant red, orange, or yellow bracts attract hummingbird pollinators. Cross-module flag: fauna (GOV-07).
- *Anemone occidentalis* (western pasqueflower) — Perennial forb with large white flowers followed by distinctive feathery seed heads ("Dr. Seuss plant"). Indicator of late-snow meadows (GOV-07).
- *Bistorta bistortoides* (American bistort) — Perennial forb with dense white bottlebrush flower spikes. Rhizomes historically gathered as food by Yakama and other nations (GOV-07).
- *Valeriana sitchensis* (Sitka valerian) — Perennial forb with clusters of small white flowers. Pungent roots. Indicator of moist subalpine meadows (GOV-07).
- *Veratrum viride* (false hellebore or corn lily) — Large perennial forb with pleated leaves and green flower panicles. Highly toxic. Forms dense stands in wet meadows (GOV-07).
- *Mimulus lewisii* (Lewis's monkey-flower) — Perennial forb of wet meadow margins and stream banks. Pink-red flowers pollinated by bumble bees. Named for Meriwether Lewis. Cross-module flag: fauna (pollinator specialization) (GOV-07).
- *Luetkea pectinata* (partridge foot) — Mat-forming sub-shrub with finely divided leaves and small white flower clusters. Dominant ground cover in some subalpine meadows (GOV-07).

**Heather communities:**

- *Phyllodoce empetriformis* (red mountain heather) — Evergreen dwarf shrub with bell-shaped pink flowers. Dominant in the heather-huckleberry understory of open subalpine forest (GOV-07).
- *Cassiope mertensiana* (white mountain heather) — Evergreen dwarf shrub with scale-like leaves and white bell flowers. Occupies slightly wetter, more sheltered microsites than red heather (GOV-07).
- *Vaccinium deliciosum* (Cascade blueberry) — Low deciduous shrub producing highly prized fruit. Important food source for bears, birds, and historically for Yakama and other nations who maintained burning regimes to enhance berry production (GOV-09). Cross-module flag: fauna, foothill ecotones (fire management).

#### Subalpine Bryophytes

Bryophytes in the subalpine zone play critical roles in moisture retention and soil stabilization on thin, rocky substrates.

- *Racomitrium* spp. (rock mosses) — Dominant on exposed rock surfaces and talus. Tolerant of desiccation and extreme UV. Pioneer species on volcanic substrates (GOV-07).
- *Polytrichum* spp. (haircap mosses) — Robust mosses of meadow margins and snowmelt channels. Among the tallest mosses, with stems to 6 inches (GOV-07).
- *Sphagnum* spp. (peat mosses) — In subalpine bogs and wet meadows. Water-holding capacity critical for maintaining meadow hydrology (GOV-07).
- *Dicranum* spp. — Cushion-forming mosses on humus and decaying wood in subalpine forest (GOV-07).

### Ecotone: Subalpine to Alpine (5,500–7,000 ft)

The treeline ecotone is not a line but a transition band 500–1,000 feet wide, characterized by:
- Progressive thinning of forest into tree islands, then scattered krummholz individuals
- Wind-flagged growth forms (branches only on leeward side)
- Increasing dominance of meadow and heather communities between tree patches
- *Pinus albicaulis* (whitebark pine) at lower alpine boundary — treated in detail in the Whitebark Pine section
- Growing season shortens below 60 days above this transition
- Treeline has advanced approximately 415 feet (126 m) upslope since the 1960s in the North Cascades (GOV-06)
- Alpine specialists are being compressed from below as subalpine species colonize newly favorable terrain

**Cross-module flags:**
- Clark's nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — obligate mutualist with whitebark pine at this boundary → Phase 617 fauna
- Mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*) — expanding into subalpine elevations previously too cold for population persistence → Phase 617 disturbance
- Snowpack hydrology: subalpine meadow-forest mosaic functions as natural water storage system → Phase 617 aquatic

## Zone 4: Alpine / Glacial (6,500+ ft / 1,980+ m)

### Overview

The Alpine zone begins above continuous forest, where the growing season compresses to 30–60 days, freeze-thaw cycles occur year-round, UV radiation is extreme, and soils are thin lithosols over volcanic rock and glacial till (GOV-03). Annual precipitation of 40–80 inches falls almost entirely as snow. The Cascade Range's alpine zone is best developed on the major stratovolcanoes — Mount Rainier (14,411 ft), Mount Adams (12,281 ft), Mount Hood (11,249 ft), Mount Baker (10,781 ft), Glacier Peak (10,541 ft) — and on the high ridges of the North Cascades, which contain the greatest concentration of active alpine glaciers in the lower 48 states (GOV-04).

Plant life in the alpine zone is sparse but highly specialized. Every species present has evolved adaptations to extreme conditions: cushion growth forms that trap heat and reduce wind exposure, deep taproots that anchor in thin rocky soils, waxy or hairy leaf surfaces that reflect UV radiation, and the ability to complete reproductive cycles in weeks rather than months.

### Dominant Plant Communities

#### Alpine Fellfields and Cushion Plant Communities

Fellfields — exposed, rocky areas with thin soil and constant wind — support the most extreme plant communities in the Cascade Range.

- *Phlox diffusa* (spreading phlox) — Mat-forming perennial forb creating tight cushions on rocky substrates. Pink to white flowers. One of the most widespread alpine species in the Cascades (GOV-07).
- *Eriogonum pyrolifolium* (alpine buckwheat) — Cushion-forming perennial with woolly basal leaves and clusters of small flowers that age from white to red. Tolerates extreme UV and thin soil (GOV-07).
- *Penstemon davidsonii* (Davidson's penstemon) — Mat-forming sub-shrub with blue-purple flowers. Grows in crevices on basalt and andesite rock faces. Adapted to extreme wind exposure (GOV-07).
- *Saxifraga* spp. (saxifrages, multiple species) — Cushion and rosette-forming perennials of rock crevices and wet cliff faces. *S. tolmiei* (Tolmie's saxifrage) is common on moist alpine rock (GOV-07).
- *Silene acaulis* (moss campion) — Tight cushion plant with pink flowers. One of the classic alpine cushion species of Northern Hemisphere mountains. Cushions can be decades old and provide microhabitat for other alpine species (GOV-07).
- *Draba* spp. (whitlow-grasses) — Small cushion or rosette-forming biennials and perennials of scree and fellfield. Multiple species in Cascade alpine (GOV-07).
- *Oxyria digyna* (mountain sorrel) — Perennial forb of moist alpine sites and snowbed margins. Circumpolar distribution; kidney-shaped leaves with sour taste (GOV-07).

#### Alpine Sedge-Grass Meadows

Where snowmelt provides more moisture and somewhat deeper soil development, sedge-grass meadows replace fellfield communities.

- *Carex* spp. (sedges, multiple species including *C. nigricans*, *C. spectabilis*) — Dominant in wet alpine meadows and snowbed margins. *C. nigricans* (black alpine sedge) forms dense swards in areas of late-lying snow (GOV-07).
- *Poa* spp. (alpine bluegrasses) — Low-growing perennial grasses of drier alpine meadows (GOV-07).
- *Juncus* spp. (rushes) — In wet alpine meadows and along snowmelt channels (GOV-07).
- *Erigeron peregrinus* (subalpine daisy) — Perennial forb with pink-lavender ray flowers. Common in moist alpine and upper subalpine meadows (GOV-07).
- *Aster alpigenus* (alpine aster) — Perennial forb of dry alpine meadows. Characteristic of the Cascade alpine flora (GOV-07).
- *Potentilla flabellifolia* (fan-leaf cinquefoil) — Perennial forb with yellow flowers and fan-shaped leaflets. Common in moist alpine meadows (GOV-07).
- *Pedicularis* spp. (louseworts) — Hemiparasitic perennial forbs with intricately shaped flowers. Several species in the Cascade alpine, including *P. groenlandica* (elephant-head lousewort) in wet meadows (GOV-07).
- *Gentiana calycosa* (Rainier pleated gentian) — Deep blue-flowered perennial of moist alpine meadows. One of the most spectacular late-season alpine flowers (GOV-07).

#### Alpine Bryophytes and Lichens

- *Racomitrium* spp. — Dominant rock mosses. Pioneer colonizers of bare volcanic surfaces (GOV-07).
- *Andreaea* spp. (granite mosses) — Black, brittle mosses on exposed rock. Among the most stress-tolerant of all land plants (GOV-07).
- *Rhizocarpon geographicum* (map lichen) — Crustose lichen on rock surfaces. Grows extremely slowly (fractions of a millimeter per year); large thalli may be centuries old. Used in lichenometry for dating glacial moraines and rockfalls (GOV-07).
- *Umbilicaria* spp. (rock tripe) — Foliose lichens on exposed rock. Highly tolerant of UV, desiccation, and temperature extremes (GOV-07).
- Crust lichens (diverse genera) — Primary colonizers of bare volcanic rock, contributing to incipient soil formation through mechanical and chemical weathering. Cross-module flag: fungi (lichenized fungi) (GOV-07).

#### Volcanic Succession Communities

The Cascade volcanoes provide natural laboratories for observing primary succession — the colonization of bare substrate from zero biological starting point.

Mount St. Helens (erupted May 18, 1980) has been the subject of intensive study of plant recolonization on blast zone deposits, pyroclastic flows, and lahars. Pioneer species include:

- *Lupinus lepidus* (prairie lupine) — Nitrogen-fixing pioneer on pumice and ash. One of the first vascular plants to colonize the Mount St. Helens blast zone. Its nitrogen fixation facilitates establishment of subsequent species. Cross-module flag: fungi (mycorrhizal facilitation) (GOV-06, PR-06).
- *Epilobium angustifolium* (fireweed) — Perennial forb, classic pioneer of disturbed sites. Wind-dispersed seeds colonize bare ground rapidly (GOV-06).
- *Anaphalis margaritacea* (pearly everlasting) — Wind-dispersed pioneer forb (GOV-06).
- Mosses and liverworts — Primary colonizers of bare volcanic surfaces, establishing thin soil layers that enable vascular plant establishment (GOV-06).

### Ecotone: Alpine to Permanent Snow/Ice

Above the highest alpine plant communities, scattered lichens on rock surfaces represent the absolute upper limit of photosynthetic life in the Cascades. The transition to permanent snow and ice is governed by the glacial equilibrium line altitude (ELA), which has been rising due to warming temperatures. As glaciers retreat, newly exposed rock surfaces become available for primary succession — but the rate of colonization by alpine plants is extremely slow (decades to centuries for initial establishment) (GOV-06).

**Cross-module flags:**
- Glacial retreat → newly exposed substrate → primary succession → decades to establish plant communities → delayed impact on alpine diversity (GOV-06)
- Alpine pollinator communities (bumble bees, butterflies) depend on brief but concentrated flowering periods → Phase 617 fauna
- Pika (*Ochotona princeps*) — alpine herbivore dependent on alpine plant communities for forage and haypile construction → Phase 617 fauna

## Zone 5: Eastern Slopes (Rain Shadow)

### Overview

East of the Cascade crest, precipitation drops dramatically — the rain shadow effect creates a gradient from 40+ inches at upper elevations to less than 10 inches per year at the lowest eastern elevations (GOV-03, GOV-07). Temperature extremes are greater than the west side (10–95°F annual range), reflecting the continental climate influence. The Eastern Slopes support a fundamentally different flora from the western Cascades, dominated by fire-adapted species, drought-tolerant shrubs, and native bunchgrass communities.

The historical fire regime of the Eastern Slopes is one of frequent, low-severity surface fire in ponderosa pine forests, with fire return intervals of 5–25 years (GOV-02, PR-07). A century of fire suppression has created fuel accumulations far exceeding historical baselines, and USGS research identifies low-elevation ponderosa pine forests on the east side as having both the highest current fire risk and the highest projected increase in fire risk due to climate change (GOV-06).

### Dominant Plant Communities

#### Ponderosa Pine Woodland (2,500–4,200 ft)

- *Pinus ponderosa* (ponderosa pine) — The dominant tree of the eastern Cascade slopes, with thick bark (3–6 inches on mature specimens) that resists surface fire. Open-grown specimens develop broad, irregular crowns; mature bark is cinnamon-red and fragrant (smells of vanilla or butterscotch). Fire-adapted: self-prunes lower branches, reducing ladder fuel. Old-growth ponderosa pine forests — with widely spaced trees, bunchgrass understory, and low fuel loads — are the product of millennia of frequent fire. Cross-module flags: fire ecology, fungi (ectomycorrhizal associations) (GOV-02, GOV-07, PR-07).

- *Purshia tridentata* (bitterbrush or antelope-brush) — Deciduous shrub, the dominant understory species in ponderosa pine woodland. Important browse species for mule deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) and elk. Nitrogen-fixer via root nodule symbiosis with *Frankia*. Cross-module flag: fauna (browse) (GOV-07).

- *Pseudoroegneria spicata* (bluebunch wheatgrass) — Perennial bunchgrass, historically the dominant grass of the ponderosa pine understory and the grassland-forest ecotone. Fire-adapted: regrows from basal meristems after burning. Declining due to competition from invasive cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*). Cross-module flag: disturbance (invasive species) (GOV-02, GOV-07).

- *Festuca idahoensis* (Idaho fescue) — Fine-leaved perennial bunchgrass. Co-dominant with bluebunch wheatgrass in higher-quality native grassland remnants. Indicator of intact native plant community (GOV-07).

- *Balsamorhiza sagittata* (arrowleaf balsamroot) — Large perennial forb with sunflower-like yellow blooms. Root was a food plant for Yakama and other nations. Mass springtime blooms create spectacular wildflower displays on eastern slopes (GOV-07, GOV-09).

- *Lupinus sericeus* (silky lupine) — Perennial forb, nitrogen-fixer. Common in ponderosa pine understory and grassland (GOV-07).

- *Eriogonum heracleoides* (parsnip-flowered buckwheat) — Perennial forb of dry, open sites in ponderosa pine woodland (GOV-07).

- *Castilleja* spp. (paintbrush) — Hemiparasitic forbs, several species in dry eastern communities (GOV-07).

#### Mixed Conifer Forest (3,500–5,500 ft)

Above ponderosa pine woodland, the forest transitions to mixed conifer communities:

- *Abies grandis* (grand fir) — Shade-tolerant conifer that has increased dramatically due to fire suppression, filling in the historically open ponderosa pine stands with dense understory. This densification is a primary driver of increased wildfire severity. Cross-module flag: disturbance (fire regime alteration) (GOV-02, PR-07).

- *Pseudotsuga menziesii* (Douglas-fir) — Present on eastern slopes in mixed stands, generally at higher elevations and on north-facing slopes where moisture is more available (GOV-07).

- *Pinus contorta* var. *latifolia* (lodgepole pine) — Dominant at mid-elevations on the eastern slopes, particularly on pumice soils and in post-fire succession. Serotinous cones (sealed by resin, opening in fire heat) are an adaptation to stand-replacing fire. Dense, even-aged stands of lodgepole pine are highly susceptible to mountain pine beetle outbreaks. Cross-module flag: disturbance (fire, beetle) (GOV-02, GOV-07).

- *Larix occidentalis* (western larch) — Deciduous conifer (one of very few worldwide), shedding golden needles in autumn. The most fire-resistant conifer in the Cascade system — thick bark, open crown, and self-pruning habit allow large individuals to survive even moderately severe fires. An important indicator of fire-maintained forests. Cross-module flag: fire ecology (GOV-02, GOV-07).

- *Pinus monticola* (western white pine) — Historically a significant component; devastated by white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*). Reduced by over 90% across much of its range. Cross-module flag: disturbance (introduced pathogen) (GOV-02).

#### Sagebrush Steppe Transition (<2,500 ft)

At the lowest eastern elevations, ponderosa pine woodland transitions to shrub-steppe:

- *Artemisia tridentata* (big sagebrush) — Dominant shrub of the sagebrush steppe. Aromatic evergreen shrub with silvery leaves. Provides habitat structure for sagebrush-obligate wildlife including greater sage-grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*). Cross-module flag: fauna (GOV-07).

- *Bromus tectorum* (cheatgrass) — INVASIVE annual grass that has transformed vast areas of the eastern slope grassland-steppe transition. Matures and dries earlier than native bunchgrasses, creating a continuous fine-fuel layer that burns readily and repeatedly. The cheatgrass-fire cycle converts native bunchgrass communities to cheatgrass monocultures: fire → cheatgrass establishes on bare ground → cheatgrass creates more fine fuel → more fire → cheatgrass dominance. Cross-module flag: disturbance (invasive species, fire regime transformation) (GOV-02, GOV-06).

- *Ventenata dubia* (ventenata) — INVASIVE annual grass, newer invader than cheatgrass, spreading rapidly through eastern Cascade rangeland-forest transitions. Displacing native bunchgrasses and even cheatgrass in some areas. Low forage value for wildlife or livestock (GOV-06).

- *Chrysothamnus* spp. (rabbitbrush) — Native deciduous shrubs of the sagebrush-steppe. Yellow fall flowers provide late-season pollinator resources (GOV-07).

### Eastern Slopes Bryophytes

The dry eastern slopes support far fewer bryophyte species than the west side. Mosses are largely confined to moist microsites:

- *Tortula ruralis* (star moss) — Desiccation-tolerant moss of dry rock surfaces and soil crusts. Can survive complete drying and resume photosynthesis within minutes of rewetting (GOV-07).
- Biological soil crusts — Complex communities of cyanobacteria, mosses, lichens, and fungi on undisturbed soil surfaces in sagebrush-steppe and open ponderosa pine. Stabilize soil, fix nitrogen, and retain moisture. Extremely vulnerable to trampling and disturbance; recovery takes decades. Cross-module flag: fungi (GOV-07).

### Ecotone: Eastern Slopes to Sagebrush Steppe

The grassland-forest ecotone on the eastern slopes — the boundary where ponderosa pine woodland meets sagebrush steppe — is historically maintained by fire. Without fire, the boundary is unstable:
- Ponderosa pine encroaches into grassland on moister sites
- Cheatgrass and ventenata invade from below, transforming fire regimes
- The result is a compressed, degraded ecotone with reduced native species diversity

This transition zone is one of the most fire-vulnerable landscapes in the western United States (GOV-02, GOV-06).

## Foothill Ecotones: Oak Savanna, Camas Prairie, and the Cultural Landscape

### The Oak Savanna Landscape

The Oregon white oak savanna of the Cascade western foothills and Willamette Valley represents one of the most dramatic examples of a cultural landscape in North America — a plant community that owed its extent and structure not to "natural" processes alone but to deliberate, sustained human management over millennia.

Before European settlement, the Willamette Valley supported approximately 400,000 acres of oak savanna and prairie — a vast, park-like landscape of widely spaced oaks over a bunchgrass-and-wildflower understory (GOV-09). This landscape was not a stable equilibrium maintained by climate alone; it was actively created and maintained by the Kalapuya people and neighboring nations through systematic annual burning.

### Indigenous Fire Management

The Kalapuya burned the Willamette Valley prairies and oak savannas annually, typically in late summer and early fall after the seed crop had matured but before fall rains began. The Molala people, occupying the western Cascade foothills from the Clackamas River drainage south, similarly managed their territories with fire (GOV-09, PR-07).

The burning accomplished multiple objectives simultaneously:
1. **Prairie maintenance:** Fire killed Douglas-fir seedlings and saplings, preventing the conversion of open savanna to closed forest. Douglas-fir, while fire-resistant as mature trees, is readily top-killed as seedlings and saplings by even low-intensity surface fire.
2. **Camas production:** Fire recycled nutrients, reduced competing vegetation, and stimulated camas bulb growth. Managed camas prairies produced substantially higher bulb yields than unburned sites.
3. **Acorn production:** Open-grown oaks produce far more acorns than forest-grown oaks. Fire maintained the open structure that maximized acorn mast.
4. **Hunting:** Fire drove game, maintained browse habitat for deer and elk, and kept travel corridors open.
5. **Travel:** Burned landscapes were easier to traverse than the dense undergrowth of unmanaged forest.

### The Douglas-fir Encroachment Crisis

Following European settlement and the suppression of Indigenous burning beginning in the mid-1800s, Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) has aggressively invaded former oak savanna and prairie (GOV-09, PR-07). The mechanism is straightforward:
- Without fire, Douglas-fir seedlings survive in the oak understory
- Douglas-fir grows faster and taller than Oregon white oak
- Within 50–100 years, Douglas-fir overtopping shades out oaks
- Oak savanna converts to closed-canopy Douglas-fir forest
- Prairie-dependent species (camas, native bunchgrasses, savanna-obligate wildlife) are eliminated

This conversion has destroyed over 90% of the Willamette Valley's historical oak savanna. What remains exists primarily on rocky outcrops, steep slopes, and sites too dry or too shallow-soiled for Douglas-fir — refugia where the competitive balance still favors oak (GOV-09).

### Camas Prairie Remnants

Camas prairies were the centerpiece of the Kalapuya food economy. Lewis and Clark, passing through the Willamette Valley in 1806, described camas blooms so extensive that prairies appeared as blue lakes when viewed from a distance. The Kalapuya dug camas bulbs with hardwood digging sticks, sorted bulbs by size (replanting small ones), and pit-roasted large bulbs for 24–72 hours in earth ovens lined with hot stones. The roasted bulbs were a dietary staple, a trade commodity, and a cultural keystone (GOV-09).

Today, camas prairie remnants are scattered and small — typically a few acres or less, surrounded by agriculture, development, or Douglas-fir forest. Key remnant sites are found in:
- **Willamette Valley wet prairies** — seasonally flooded clay-soil prairies in the valley bottom
- **Cascade foothill meadows** — openings in the forest at 500–1,500 feet elevation
- **Rocky balds and outcrops** — shallow-soil sites too harsh for tree establishment

These remnants support not only camas (*Camassia quamash* and *C. leichtlinii*) but associated prairie species including Roemer's fescue, shooting stars (*Dodecatheon hendersonii*), Oregon sunshine (*Eriophyllum lanatum*), deltoid balsamroot (*Balsamorhiza deltoidea*), and chocolate lily (*Fritillaria affinis*) — a community found nowhere else (GOV-09, CON-02).

### Ecotone Conservation Value

The foothill ecotone between oak savanna/prairie and montane forest is at once the most threatened and potentially the most restorable zone in the Cascade system:

- **Threatened:** Over 90% loss; continued pressure from development and encroachment
- **Restorable:** The ecology is well understood; prescribed fire programs (e.g., Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife burns in the West Eugene Wetlands, The Nature Conservancy prescribed fire at Willamette Valley preserves) have demonstrated that prairie and savanna can be restored when fire is reintroduced
- **Conservation priority:** Oregon Conservation Strategy identifies oak savanna and native prairie as "Strategy Habitats" — ecosystems of highest conservation concern requiring active management (GOV-09)

The mycorrhizal transition at this ecotone is also ecologically significant: the arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi associated with grassland species give way to the ectomycorrhizal fungi associated with Douglas-fir and other conifers. This below-ground transition mirrors and reinforces the above-ground shift from open grassland to closed forest. Cross-module flag: fungi (Phase 617).

## Whitebark Pine: Alpine Keystone Under Threat

### Ecological Role

Whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) is a keystone species of the upper subalpine and alpine zones of the Cascade Range. Unlike most pines, its seeds are large, wingless, and contained in indehiscent cones — cones that do not open on their own. The tree depends entirely on Clark's nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) to harvest seeds, transport them (up to 20 miles), and cache them in shallow soil. Unretrieved caches germinate, providing virtually 100% of whitebark pine regeneration. This obligate mutualism — each species dependent on the other for survival — is one of the most tightly co-evolved plant-animal relationships in North American ecology (GOV-06, GOV-04).

Beyond seed dispersal, whitebark pine serves critical ecosystem functions:

1. **Snowpack regulation:** Whitebark pine stands trap and shade snow, slowing snowmelt and extending the period of spring runoff. This hydrological function affects downstream water supply. Cross-module flag: aquatic.
2. **Wildlife food source:** Large, nutritious seeds (high in fat and protein) are consumed by Clark's nutcracker, red squirrel (*Tamiasciurus hudsonicus*), and grizzly bear (*Ursus arctos*) — for grizzly bears, whitebark pine seeds are a critical pre-hibernation food source in years when other foods are scarce. Cross-module flag: fauna.
3. **Soil stabilization:** Roots stabilize thin alpine soils on steep slopes, reducing erosion.
4. **Pioneer function:** Whitebark pine colonizes harsh, exposed sites at treeline where other trees cannot establish, facilitating subsequent succession of other subalpine species.

### The Convergence of Threats

Whitebark pine faces a "perfect storm" of three simultaneous threats, each of which would be serious alone and which together may drive the species toward functional extinction in the Cascade Range:

#### White Pine Blister Rust (*Cronartium ribicola*)

An introduced fungal pathogen from Eurasia, blister rust arrived in western North America around 1910 on imported nursery stock. The fungus requires an alternate host (primarily *Ribes* spp. — currants and gooseberries) to complete its life cycle, but once established in a whitebark pine, it is invariably fatal. The pathogen kills branches progressively (branch cankers girdle and kill individual branches), eventually killing the entire crown.

In the Cascade Range, blister rust infection rates vary geographically but reach 50–90% of trees in some stands. Mortality lags behind infection — trees can survive with partial crown loss for years or decades — but the trajectory is clear: without intervention, blister rust alone would reduce whitebark pine populations by 50–80% within the next century (GOV-06, GOV-04).

A small percentage of whitebark pines (estimated 1–5%) carry genetic resistance to blister rust. Resistance breeding programs, led by USDA Forest Service, are collecting seeds from surviving resistant trees and propagating them for restoration planting. This genetic rescue effort is the primary long-term hope for the species (GOV-06).

#### Mountain Pine Beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*)

The mountain pine beetle is a native bark beetle that has historically been a natural disturbance agent in western pine forests. Beetles bore through bark, inoculate the tree with blue-stain fungi (*Grosmannia clavigera* and allies) that block the tree's water transport, and lay eggs in the phloem. The combination of beetle gallery construction and fungal infection kills the host tree.

Historically, whitebark pine at subalpine elevations was partially protected from beetle outbreaks by cold winter temperatures that killed overwintering beetle larvae. As climate warming raises minimum winter temperatures in the subalpine zone, mountain pine beetle populations can now persist and reproduce at elevations that were previously too cold, bringing them into sustained contact with whitebark pine stands for the first time in the modern record. Cross-module flag: disturbance, fauna (GOV-06, PR-06).

Beetle outbreaks in whitebark pine have been documented across the Cascade Range, with particularly severe mortality in the eastern Cascades and in stands already weakened by blister rust (GOV-06).

#### Climate-Driven Range Compression

Whitebark pine is an alpine and upper subalpine species — it grows at the top of the mountain. As climate warming advances the treeline upslope and allows lower-elevation species to colonize previously marginal habitat, whitebark pine is compressed into an ever-narrower band at the summit. Unlike species that can shift their range poleward or upslope to track favorable conditions, whitebark pine is already at the top — there is nowhere higher to go.

Climate projections for the Cascade Range (GOV-06, PR-05, PR-06) indicate:
- Continued warming of 2–4°C by 2080 under moderate emission scenarios
- Snowpack reduction of 40–80% in the subalpine zone
- Upslope advance of montane and subalpine species into whitebark pine habitat
- Reduction of suitable whitebark pine habitat by 50–90% by end of century

The result: whitebark pine faces simultaneous attack from a pathogen (blister rust from above-ground), an insect (beetle from within the bark), and climate change (habitat compression from below). Each threat amplifies the others — blister rust-weakened trees are more susceptible to beetle attack; beetle-killed trees cannot reproduce; climate change enables both beetle expansion and habitat loss.

### Clark's Nutcracker Mutualism at Risk

The Clark's nutcracker-whitebark pine mutualism creates a feedback loop that can accelerate decline. As whitebark pine cone production drops (due to blister rust killing cone-bearing branches, beetle killing trees, and climate reducing stand density), nutcrackers may abandon stands with insufficient cone crops and shift to alternative food sources at lower elevations. Without nutcrackers, no new whitebark pines are planted. Without new trees, cone production falls further. This positive feedback loop — fewer trees → fewer nutcrackers → fewer seeds cached → fewer trees — is the mechanism by which whitebark pine could pass a functional extinction threshold even while some individual trees survive (GOV-06, GOV-04).

### Conservation Status and Response

Whitebark pine was listed as threatened under the Endangered Species Act in December 2022, recognizing the severity and synergistic nature of its threats. Conservation strategies include:

1. **Resistance breeding:** Collecting seeds from blister rust-resistant trees; establishing seed orchards; planting resistant seedlings in restoration sites (USDA Forest Service)
2. **Prescribed fire:** Burning competing vegetation to create planting sites and restore the open stand structure that whitebark pine requires
3. **Beetle management:** Verbenone (anti-aggregation pheromone) application to protect high-value seed trees from beetle attack
4. **Seed banking:** Ex situ conservation of genetic diversity through seed collections
5. **Monitoring:** Long-term plots tracking infection rates, mortality, regeneration, and nutcracker behavior (GOV-06, GOV-04, GOV-09)

## Bryophyte and Fern Diversity Summary

While this survey focuses primarily on vascular plants, the Cascade Range supports extraordinary non-vascular plant diversity:

### Bryophytes (Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts)

- **Montane zone:** Highest bryophyte diversity, driven by persistent moisture, deep shade, and abundant substrate (bark, nurse logs, rock). Canopy epiphyte biomass exceeds 4 tons per hectare in old-growth stands. Key genera: *Hylocomium*, *Rhytidiadelphus*, *Isothecium*, *Porella*, *Scapania* (GOV-07).
- **Subalpine zone:** Specialized rock mosses (*Racomitrium*, *Polytrichum*) and bog mosses (*Sphagnum*) in wet meadows. Lower diversity but high ecological importance for moisture retention (GOV-07).
- **Alpine zone:** Extreme-specialist mosses (*Andreaea*, *Racomitrium*) and crust lichens on rock. Pioneer colonizers essential for primary succession (GOV-07).
- **Eastern slopes:** Lowest bryophyte diversity due to aridity. Biological soil crusts (cyanobacteria, mosses, lichens) are the most important non-vascular plant community — stabilizing soil, fixing nitrogen, and retaining moisture (GOV-07).

### Ferns and Fern Allies

The Cascade Range supports approximately 50 fern and fern-ally species, with diversity highest in the moist montane zone:

- **Western foothills:** *Pteridium aquilinum* (bracken fern) in open areas; *Polystichum munitum* (sword fern) in shade (GOV-07)
- **Montane:** Maximum diversity — *Polystichum munitum*, *Blechnum spicant*, *Athyrium filix-femina*, *Adiantum pedatum*, *Polypodium glycyrrhiza*, *Dryopteris expansa* (spreading wood fern), multiple *Selaginella* spp. (GOV-07)
- **Subalpine/Alpine:** Very few fern species; *Cryptogramma acrostichoides* (parsley fern) on talus and scree (GOV-07)
- **Eastern slopes:** Ferns restricted to moist microsites (stream banks, north-facing rock crevices); *Cheilanthes gracillima* (lace-lip fern) is adapted to dry rock habitats (GOV-07)

## Climate Change Projections for Cascade Flora

Climate change will restructure Cascade Range plant communities across all elevation zones over the coming century. Key projections from USGS and peer-reviewed sources:

1. **Treeline advance:** Already documented at ~415 feet (126 m) upslope since the 1960s in the North Cascades (GOV-06). Subalpine meadows will be progressively colonized by subalpine forest, reducing open meadow extent.

2. **Snowpack reduction:** Summer streamflow losses of 40–60% by 2040 and 60–80% by 2080 on eastern Cascade slopes (GOV-06). Reduced snowpack will alter growing season length, soil moisture availability, and species competitive dynamics across the subalpine and alpine zones.

3. **Fire regime intensification:** Eastern slope ponderosa pine forests face the highest projected increase in fire risk (GOV-06). Western Cascade forests, historically characterized by infrequent, high-severity fire, may experience more frequent fire as summers warm and dry (PR-06).

4. **Species range shifts:** Drought-tolerant species will expand upslope and northward; moisture-dependent species will contract. Douglas-fir may replace western hemlock at lower montane elevations; ponderosa pine may expand into current Douglas-fir habitat on the eastern slopes (PR-06).

5. **Alaska yellow-cedar decline:** Continued loss in areas where snowpack can no longer insulate roots — range contraction from lower elevational boundary upward (GOV-06, PR-05).

6. **Whitebark pine compression:** Suitable habitat may decline 50–90% by end of century. Combined with blister rust and beetle, this creates existential risk for the species in the Cascade Range (GOV-06).

7. **Invasive grass expansion:** Cheatgrass and ventenata will continue to expand through the eastern slope grassland-forest transition, especially following fire (GOV-06).

These projections indicate that the plant communities documented in this survey are not static — they represent a snapshot of a system in rapid transition. The conservation priorities identified here (foothill prairie/savanna restoration, whitebark pine genetic rescue, fire regime management, invasive species control) are responses to trajectories already underway.
