# Threats & Conservation — Fire, Climate, and Fragmentation

## Overview

The Cascade Range faces five interconnected threat vectors that operate across all elevation zones. Unlike single-species threats, these are systemic — each threat amplifies the others, creating feedback loops that accelerate ecosystem change. This module quantifies each threat using published agency projections and peer-reviewed data, documenting the trajectory of change across the full elevation gradient.

---

## 1. Climate Change

### Temperature Projections

Climate models project annual average temperature increases of 5-9°F (2.8-5°C) in the central Oregon Cascades by late 21st century, with summer showing the greatest warming (GOV-06, PR-06). The North Cascades face similar trajectories, with precipitation shifting from snow-dominated to rain-dominated regimes.

Littell et al. (2010) synthesized climate projections for Washington State forests, finding that the combination of warming temperatures and changing precipitation patterns will alter disturbance regimes (fire, insects, disease) more rapidly than direct physiological effects on trees (PR-06).

### Timberline Advance

The treeline has advanced approximately 415 feet (126 m) upslope since the 1960s in the North Cascades (GOV-06). This represents one of the most measurable signals of climate change in the Pacific Northwest:

- Subalpine fir and mountain hemlock seedlings establishing above historical treeline boundaries
- Alpine meadow area shrinking as trees colonize former tundra
- Rate of advance accelerating: faster in the last 30 years than in the preceding 30
- Alpine-obligate species (hoary marmot, pika, alpine plants) losing habitat from below with no higher ground available

### Snowpack Decline

A 2021 study published in Nature Climate Change found that Cascade snowpack is more vulnerable to warming than interior mountain ranges because the Cascades' maritime climate places snowpack temperatures closer to the melting threshold (PR-05). Key findings:

- April 1 snow water equivalent (SWE) declining across most Cascade stations since 1950
- Western slopes receive 100+ inches annual precipitation; some higher elevations accumulate 500+ inches of snowfall (GOV-06)
- Snowpack functions as natural reservoir — stores winter precipitation, releases gradually through spring and summer melt
- As temperatures rise, more winter precipitation falls as rain rather than snow
- North Cascades watersheds becoming increasingly rain-dominated: more autumn/winter flooding, higher peak flows, critically lower summer flows (PR-05)
- USFS vulnerability assessment projects eastern Cascade slopes may experience summer streamflow losses of 40-60% by 2040 and 60-80% by 2080 as snowmelt timing shifts earlier (GOV-06)

### Glacial Retreat

- Mount Rainier holds the largest glacial system of any peak in the contiguous United States (GOV-04)
- North Cascades contain the greatest concentration of active alpine glaciers in the lower 48 states (GOV-04)
- Glacial retreat reduces cold-water reservoirs that sustain downstream aquatic ecosystems through summer
- Loss of glacial buffering means earlier peak flows, lower late-summer baseflows, and warmer stream temperatures — all harmful to cold-water salmonids

## 2. Wildfire Regime Disruption

### Historical Fire Regimes

The USFS Fire Effects Information System documents distinct fire regimes for east-slope and west-slope Cascade forests (GOV-02):

**East-Slope Ponderosa Pine Forests:**
- Historical regime: frequent, low-severity fire at 5-25 year return intervals
- Maintained open, park-like stands with grass understory
- Ponderosa pine adapted with thick bark, self-pruning lower branches, deep roots
- Historical fire maintained habitat heterogeneity and reduced fuel accumulation

**East-Slope Mixed Conifer:**
- Historical regime: mixed-severity fire at 25-75 year return intervals
- Mosaic of low- and moderate-severity patches with occasional high-severity patches
- Created complex stand structures with diverse age classes

**West-Slope Douglas-fir/Hemlock:**
- Historical regime: infrequent, high-severity stand-replacing fire at 200-500+ year return intervals
- Extensive fires documented by fire-scar evidence: 1701, 1800s fire years
- Long fire-free intervals allowed old-growth development (1,000+ year stands)

Agee (2003) documented the historical range of variability for Eastern Cascades forests, establishing baseline fire return intervals against which current conditions can be measured (PR-07).

### Current Fire Risk

A century of fire suppression has fundamentally altered Cascade fire regimes:

- East-slope fuel accumulations far exceed historical baselines — dense understory, ladder fuels, continuous canopy
- USGS research found that low-elevation ponderosa pine forests on the east side of the Cascade Range — already having the highest fire risk today — also face the highest projected increase in fire risk due to climate change (GOV-06)
- Climate change extends fire seasons and intensifies droughts
- Many west-slope forests are in Fire Regime Condition Class II, with moderate risk of losing ecosystem components (GOV-02)
- Post-1980 Mt. St. Helens eruption zone demonstrates natural disturbance-recovery dynamics distinct from fire

### Fire-Climate Interaction

- Warmer temperatures → earlier snowmelt → longer fire season → drier fuels
- More lightning ignitions during extended dry periods
- Bark beetle outbreaks (mountain pine beetle, Douglas-fir beetle) create additional dead fuel
- Positive feedback: fire → reduced canopy → increased surface temperatures → more fire-prone conditions

## 3. Habitat Fragmentation

### Puget Lowland

The Puget Lowland Ecoregion has undergone the greatest change since European settlement and experienced the steepest biodiversity decline of any Cascade-adjacent region (GOV-06):

- Originally continuous forest and prairie mosaic maintained by Puget Salish nations' burning practices
- Now the most urbanized landscape in Washington State
- Less than 10% of the Western Cascade Montane Highlands in King County remains in late seral (old-growth) condition (GOV-06)

### Eastern Puget Uplands

The Eastern Puget Uplands represent a critical transition zone from lowlands to Cascade foothills (GOV-06):

- 1,307 km² area experiencing expanding development pressure
- Transition zone between lowland urban/agricultural landscape and montane wilderness
- Contains wildlife corridors connecting lowland habitats to mountain refugia
- Once continuous forest now fragmented into patchwork of suburbs, woodlots, and remnant groves

### Northwest Forest Plan

The Northwest Forest Plan, focused on the northern spotted owl but covering over 1,000 species of plants, animals, and fungi, provides the foundation for biodiversity conservation on federal lands (GOV-05):

- Established late-successional reserves and riparian buffers across 24.5 million acres
- Adaptive management component has not been fully implemented
- Reserve network gaps exist along elevation gradients — designed primarily for spotted owl, may not adequately protect species moving upslope with climate change
- Gaps along latitudinal gradients limit species' ability to track changing climate conditions northward

## 4. Invasive Species

### Eastern Slopes — Annual Grass Invasion

Climate change and disturbance are facilitating the spread of invasive exotic plants across the Cascades (GOV-06):

- **Cheatgrass** (*Bromus tectorum*): transforming fire regimes in rangeland-forest transitions on eastern slopes
- **Ventenata** (*Ventenata dubia*): displacing native bunchgrass communities
- Invasive annual grasses increase fire frequency by providing continuous fine fuels that cure rapidly
- Positive feedback: invasion → more frequent fire → more invasion → native bunchgrass displacement
- Native bunchgrass communities (*Pseudoroegneria spicata*, *Festuca idahoensis*) evolved with infrequent fire; cannot compete with annuals under frequent fire regime

### North Cascades — Exotic Plant Spread

In North Cascades National Park, invasive exotic plants have spread through inadvertent redistribution via cars and hikers (GOV-04):

- Trail corridors and road margins serve as invasion pathways into wilderness
- Disturbed sites (trailheads, campgrounds, road cuts) provide establishment points
- Climate warming opens new elevation zones to species that were previously excluded by cold temperatures

### Western Foothills — Ecological Transformation

The western foothills face a compound invasion dynamic:

- Fire suppression allows Douglas-fir encroachment into former oak savanna — not technically "invasive" but ecologically transformative
- Non-native plants including Scotch broom (*Cytisus scoparius*), Himalayan blackberry (*Rubus armeniacus*) dominate disturbed lowland areas
- English ivy (*Hedera helix*) suppresses native forest understory in urban-wildland interface

## 5. Glacial and Snowpack Loss

### Cascade Glacier Systems

The Cascade Range contains two major glacier concentrations:

1. **Mount Rainier:** 25+ named glaciers, largest glacial system in the contiguous U.S. Emmons Glacier is the largest glacier by area in the lower 48 states. Glaciers have lost approximately 25% of their area since 1900 (GOV-04).

2. **North Cascades:** Greatest concentration of active alpine glaciers in the lower 48 states — over 300 glaciers and permanent snowfields. These glaciers provide critical late-summer baseflow to streams and rivers (GOV-04).

### Ecological Consequences

- **Summer streamflow:** Glacial melt contributes 5-25% of late-summer streamflow in glacier-fed rivers. As glaciers shrink, this contribution will initially increase (more melt) then decrease sharply (less ice to melt) (GOV-06)
- **Stream temperature:** Loss of glacial cold-water input raises stream temperatures above thresholds for cold-water salmonids (bull trout require <12°C)
- **Alpine habitat:** Glacial retreat exposes new substrate for colonization — early successional communities establish, but at the cost of ice-dependent species
- **Water supply:** Communities dependent on glacial-fed water systems face long-term supply uncertainty
