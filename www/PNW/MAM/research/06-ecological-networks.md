# Module 6: Ecological Networks — Trophic Cascades, Nutrient Cycling, and Ecosystem Engineering in the Pacific Northwest

> **PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — "Fur, Fin & Fang"**
>
> This module maps the ecological networks that connect PNW mammals to each other, to the plant and fungal kingdoms, to the salmon runs that bring the ocean into the forest, and to the physical landscape itself. Where Modules 1-3 profiled individual species and Module 4 assembled them into zone-specific communities, this module follows the lines of interaction — energy flow, nutrient transport, behavioral modification, and physical habitat engineering — that bind those species into a functioning system. The networks documented here are not abstractions. They are measurable flows of nitrogen atoms from ocean to forest, quantifiable changes in stream temperature when beavers build dams, and observable shifts in elk behavior when wolves howl from the ridgeline above.
>
> **Design principle:** Seven major ecological networks are treated in sequence: (1) trophic cascades, (2) the salmon-bear-forest nutrient pathway, (3) the fungal-mammal-forest mutualism, (4) beaver ecosystem engineering, (5) predator-prey webs and mesopredator dynamics, (6) the marine trophic network, and (7) cross-kingdom soil engineering. Each network section identifies the key mammalian nodes, maps the interaction pathways, quantifies effects where data exist, and identifies how disruption at one node propagates through the system. Cross-references to Modules 1-5 and the AVI companion module are provided throughout.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates, den sites, haul-outs, or calving areas for ESA-listed species. County/watershed level only. Every Indigenous knowledge reference names a specific nation. Population data from USFWS, WDFW, ODFW, IDFG, NOAA, or peer-reviewed research. No policy advocacy. Human-wildlife conflict documented factually. Only Level 1 cultural content.

## Network 1: Trophic Cascades — How Predators Shape Forests

### The Concept

A trophic cascade occurs when a predator suppresses its prey population or modifies prey behavior, and that suppression or modification propagates downward through additional trophic levels — typically from predator to herbivore to vegetation. In the PNW, trophic cascades link the apex predator guild (wolf, cougar, grizzly bear, orca, sea otter) to vegetation communities, salmon habitat, kelp forests, and ultimately to the physical structure of terrestrial and marine landscapes. These are not theoretical constructs. They are measurable, documentable chains of cause and effect that have been studied at specific PNW sites with specific populations of known animals.

### Cascade 1: Cougar → Elk → Riparian Vegetation → Salmon Habitat

**Location:** Olympic National Park, Washington
**Documented by:** Ripple & Beschta (2006, 2012)

The Olympic Peninsula provides a natural experiment in trophic cascade dynamics. Olympic National Park (ONP) has never been subject to wolf recolonization (wolves were extirpated from the Olympics by the 1920s and have not returned), but it maintains a robust cougar population. Roosevelt elk are the dominant herbivore. The interaction chain:

1. **Cougar predation on elk** creates a behavioral effect — the "ecology of fear." Elk avoid areas where cougar ambush risk is highest: dense riparian corridors with limited escape routes, narrow valley bottoms, and areas with heavy cover adjacent to streams. Even when cougars do not directly kill elk in riparian zones, the perceived risk of predation modifies elk spatial behavior — elk spend less time feeding in risky areas.

2. **Reduced elk browsing in riparian zones** allows recovery of willows (*Salix* spp.), black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*), and vine maple (*Acer circinatum*). These are fast-growing, browsing-sensitive species that elk suppress when they feed without predation risk. Where elk avoid riparian zones, canopy closure increases, understory complexity develops, and woody vegetation recolonizes stream banks.

3. **Recovered riparian vegetation improves salmon habitat** through three mechanisms: (a) increased shading reduces stream water temperature — critical for cold-water salmonids whose survival declines sharply above 64 degrees F; (b) root networks stabilize stream banks, reducing erosion and sediment loading that degrades spawning gravels; (c) overhanging vegetation and woody debris provide cover and insect prey (terrestrial insects falling from riparian canopy constitute a significant proportion of juvenile salmonid diet).

**Quantified effects:** In ONP reaches where cougars are present and elk densities are moderate, Ripple & Beschta documented significantly taller and more abundant deciduous riparian trees compared to areas where elk concentrate (such as the Hoh River valley floor, where high elk density suppresses all palatable riparian vegetation). The effect is not uniform — it varies with valley geometry, elk density, and cougar habitat selection — but the directional pattern is consistent: more predation risk correlates with more riparian vegetation correlates with better stream conditions.

**The missing wolf:** ONP's cascade operates through cougar alone. In ecosystems where wolves are present alongside cougars — as is developing in northeast Washington and the east Cascades — the two predators may produce complementary cascade effects. Wolves modify elk behavior in open meadows and ridgelines (where cursorial pursuit is effective), while cougars modify behavior in dense forest and canyon terrain (where ambush is effective). Together, they create a more spatially complete landscape of fear that redistributes ungulate browsing pressure across a wider range of habitats.

### Cascade 2: Wolf → Elk → Aspen/Willow → Songbird/Beaver Habitat

**Location:** Yellowstone National Park (primary), with emerging parallels in WA wolf recovery areas
**Documented by:** Ripple & Beschta (2012); Ripple et al. (2014)

The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction (1995-96) produced the most studied trophic cascade in ecology. While Yellowstone is outside the PNW, its findings are directly applicable to PNW wolf recovery areas where the same species assemblage (wolf, elk, aspen, cottonwood, willow, beaver, songbirds) occurs.

The cascade chain:

1. **Wolf packs suppress elk abundance** and, more importantly, modify elk spatial behavior. Elk avoid wolf-risky areas: open valleys, stream crossings, areas with poor escape terrain.

2. **Reduced elk browsing allows aspen, willow, and cottonwood regeneration** in riparian zones and stream-adjacent valleys. Before wolves, young aspens and willows in Yellowstone's Northern Range were browsed to suppression — many stands had not regenerated in decades. Within 5-10 years of wolf reintroduction, aspen recruitment increased measurably in wolf-influenced areas.

3. **Recovered woody vegetation supports beaver recolonization.** Beavers require willow and aspen for food and dam-building material. In areas where elk browsing had eliminated these species, beavers could not establish. As willows recovered, beavers returned to stream reaches where they had been absent for decades.

4. **Beaver dams create pond complexes** that provide habitat for songbirds (willow flycatcher, yellow warbler — AVI cross-link), fish, amphibians, and a suite of wetland-associated species. The beaver-created wetlands diversify the landscape, increase water storage, and moderate streamflow — effects that cascade through the aquatic food web.

**PNW application:** Washington's recolonizing wolf population (approximately 42 packs, 260+ individuals as of 2024) occupies montane and foothill habitat in northeast WA and the east Cascades — exactly the landscape where the wolf-elk-riparian cascade would be expected to develop. WDFW and university researchers are monitoring whether riparian vegetation recovery and beaver recolonization follow the Yellowstone pattern. The cascade takes time — Yellowstone's effects became measurable 5-10 years after reintroduction, and PNW wolf recovery is still in its early decades.

### Cascade 3: Sea Otter → Sea Urchin → Kelp Forest → Nearshore Ecosystem

**Location:** Washington outer coast (Olympic Peninsula), British Columbia
**Documented by:** Estes & Palmisano (1974); Estes et al. (1998)

The marine counterpart to the terrestrial cascades is the sea otter-urchin-kelp trophic cascade — one of the first and most thoroughly documented trophic cascades in ecology.

The cascade chain:

1. **Sea otters prey on sea urchins** (*Strongylocentrotus* spp.), maintaining urchin populations at levels that prevent overgrazing of kelp.

2. **Kelp forests persist** when urchin grazing is controlled, providing three-dimensional habitat structure comparable to a terrestrial forest — a complex canopy of *Macrocystis* and *Nereocystis* supporting fish, invertebrates, and seabirds.

3. **Without sea otters, urchin populations explode** and overgraze kelp, converting complex kelp forest to "urchin barrens" — featureless rocky seafloor with minimal habitat value. Urchin barrens support a fraction of the fish and invertebrate biomass of intact kelp forest.

**PNW context:** Sea otters were hunted to near-extinction during the maritime fur trade (fewer than 2,000 remained globally by 1911). They were reintroduced to the Washington outer coast (59 individuals from Amchitka Island, Alaska) in 1969-1970. The WA population has grown to approximately 2,000-2,500 and is expanding southward. Where otters have recolonized, kelp forests have recovered and nearshore fish and invertebrate communities have increased in abundance and diversity. This recovery demonstrates the reversibility of trophic cascade collapse — remove the predator and the system degrades; restore the predator and the system recovers.

**The orca complication:** Estes et al. (1998) documented that Bigg's killer whale predation on sea otters in Alaska contributed to otter decline and urchin barren expansion — adding a fourth trophic level to the cascade (orca → otter → urchin → kelp). In the PNW, Bigg's orcas occasionally take sea otters but the interaction is less well-studied than in Alaska. If Bigg's populations continue to grow (they have roughly doubled since the 1990s), increased predation on the recovering otter population could partially suppress the kelp forest recovery — a cascade of a cascade.

### Cascade 4: Wolf/Cougar → Coyote → Small Mammals → Ground-Nesting Birds

**Mesopredator release and its reversal**

This cascade operates through interference competition rather than direct predation on vegetation:

1. **Wolves suppress coyote populations** through direct killing (wolves kill coyotes on sight in territorial encounters) and competitive exclusion (coyotes avoid wolf territories). Where wolves are present, coyote density declines by 30-50%.

2. **Reduced coyote density benefits ground-nesting birds** (including sage-grouse, killdeer, and various shorebirds — AVI cross-link), pronghorn fawns, and small mammals that coyotes prey on. This is mesopredator release reversal — the return of the apex predator re-imposes top-down control on the mesopredator, relaxing predation pressure on the mesopredator's prey.

3. **In the absence of wolves, coyote populations increase** (mesopredator release), driving elevated predation on ground-nesting birds, small mammals, and ungulate neonates. The loss of wolves from the PNW in the early 1900s likely triggered a continent-wide increase in coyote abundance and range — coyotes now occupy the entire North American continent from Alaska to Panama, a range expansion enabled by the removal of their primary interspecific competitor.

**PNW evidence:** The mesopredator release reversal is expected but not yet quantified in PNW wolf recovery areas. In northeast WA, where wolf packs have been established since 2008, coyote density changes have not been formally measured. The prediction is clear from Yellowstone data: as wolf pack density increases, coyote density should decline, and ground-nesting bird success and pronghorn fawn survival should increase in the steppe and foothill zones where coyotes are the dominant predator.

## Network 2: The Salmon-Bear-Forest Nutrient Pathway

### Overview

The salmon-bear-forest nutrient pathway is the PNW's signature ecological cascade — a biogeochemical conveyor belt that moves marine-derived nutrients from the deep ocean into the heart of temperate rainforests. It connects three kingdoms (Animalia, Plantae, Fungi), two ecosystems (marine and terrestrial), and dozens of species across multiple trophic levels. No other nutrient pathway in the temperate world operates at this scale or with this many interacting nodes.

### The Pathway, Step by Step

**Step 1: Marine nutrient accumulation**

Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp. — Chinook, coho, sockeye, chum, pink) spend 1-5 years in the open ocean, accumulating marine-derived nutrients — particularly nitrogen-15 (a heavier isotope of nitrogen enriched in marine food webs relative to terrestrial sources), phosphorus, and carbon-13. By the time a 30-pound Chinook salmon returns to spawn, its body contains approximately 130 grams of nitrogen, 20 grams of phosphorus, and thousands of calories of marine-origin energy. Each fish is a nutrient packet assembled by the ocean and delivered to freshwater.

**Step 2: Spawning transport to freshwater**

Salmon return to their natal streams to spawn and die. In healthy PNW watersheds, spawning runs historically delivered tens of millions of fish — and with them, millions of kilograms of marine-derived nutrients — to freshwater streams from the coast to hundreds of miles inland. The Columbia River system alone historically supported 10-16 million returning salmon annually. Current runs are estimated at 1-3 million, representing an 80-90% reduction in marine nutrient delivery to the basin.

**Step 3: Bear interception and terrestrial transport**

Bears — both grizzly (*Ursus arctos*) and black (*Ursus americanus*) — intercept spawning salmon at streams and carry carcasses into the adjacent forest for consumption. A single grizzly bear may catch and transport 30-40 salmon per day during peak runs, consuming the lipid-rich brain, roe, and skin while discarding the rest. A black bear typically takes fewer fish (5-15 per day) and consumes more completely, but the aggregate transport by hundreds or thousands of bears along a watershed is enormous.

Bears deposit partially consumed carcasses at distances of 10-500 meters from the stream bank. The spatial distribution of carcass deposition is not random — bears carry fish upslope, along trails, and to bedding sites, creating a non-uniform scatter pattern that concentrates nutrients in certain landscape positions. Bear feces and urine also contain substantial marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus, deposited across the bear's home range during the salmon season.

**Quantified transport:** Reimchen (2000) estimated that bears in coastal British Columbia transport 30-60 kg of salmon-derived nitrogen per hectare of riparian forest per year in productive watersheds. Hilderbrand et al. (1999) demonstrated that bear-transported salmon nitrogen constitutes up to 24% of the total nitrogen budget in riparian vegetation within 500 meters of salmon streams — a massive marine subsidy to a terrestrial ecosystem.

**Step 4: Decomposition and soil enrichment**

Salmon carcasses deposited by bears decompose on the forest floor, releasing nitrogen, phosphorus, and other nutrients into the soil. The nitrogen is in marine-isotope form (enriched in nitrogen-15), making it traceable through the food web using stable isotope analysis. Decomposing carcasses are consumed by a succession of organisms: blowflies and beetles within hours, then fly larvae, other invertebrates, and soil microbes over days to weeks. The carcass-associated invertebrate community is itself prey for shrews, mice, and ground-foraging birds.

**Step 5: Uptake by vegetation**

Trees and understory plants absorb salmon-derived nitrogen through their root systems — often facilitated by mycorrhizal fungal networks that extend root access to carcass decomposition sites. Helfield & Naiman (2001) demonstrated that Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) growing within 25 meters of salmon-bearing streams in the Olympic Peninsula contained significantly elevated nitrogen-15 in their foliage and wood, and these trees grew approximately three times faster than equivalent trees far from salmon streams. The marine nitrogen signal was detectable in tree rings, showing that salmon-derived nutrition is incorporated into long-term wood growth.

This result has a profound implication: the PNW's iconic temperate rainforests — among the most productive forests on Earth — are partly fertilized by the ocean, via salmon, via bears. The forest is not purely terrestrial; it is a marine-subsidized ecosystem.

**Step 6: Riparian feedback to salmon habitat**

The trees that grow faster from salmon-derived nutrition provide the shading, bank stability, and woody debris that maintain the cold, clean stream conditions salmon require for spawning and rearing. This completes the cycle: salmon fertilize the forest that shelters the streams that produce the next generation of salmon.

### Avian Nodes in the Network (AVI Cross-Link)

Bears are not the only vectors of salmon-to-forest nutrient transport. Bald eagles, corvids (ravens, crows, jays), and gulls scavenge salmon carcasses and distribute nutrients across the landscape through consumption, carcass transport, and defecation.

- **Bald eagles** concentrate at salmon streams during spawning runs, with congregations of 100+ eagles documented at productive streams on the Skagit River (WA), Chilkat River (AK/BC border), and coastal BC rivers. Eagles carry fish and fish parts to perch trees, depositing nutrients (carcass fragments, fecal material) at the base of large conifers up to several hundred meters from the stream.

- **Ravens and crows** are opportunistic carcass consumers that carry salmon fragments to cache sites across the forest. Their contribution is individually small but collectively significant given their abundance.

- **Gulls** (glaucous-winged, herring, Bonaparte's) concentrate at spawning streams and estuaries, consuming salmon eggs, flesh, and invertebrates associated with decomposing carcasses. Gull colonies on offshore islands receive marine-derived nitrogen from salmon-feeding adults, linking the salmon nutrient pathway to seabird nesting ecology.

### Network Disruption: The Consequences of Salmon Decline

The 80-90% decline in Columbia Basin salmon runs since the mid-1800s represents a proportional reduction in marine nutrient delivery to the terrestrial ecosystem. The consequences are diffuse, cumulative, and difficult to separate from other environmental changes, but the directional effect is clear: forests adjacent to depleted salmon streams receive less marine nitrogen, grow more slowly, and provide poorer stream habitat than forests adjacent to healthy runs. This creates a feedback loop: fewer salmon → less forest fertilization → poorer stream conditions → fewer salmon.

Dams are the primary cause of salmon decline in the interior Columbia Basin, blocking both upstream migration (reducing spawning) and downstream nutrient delivery (trapping carcasses in reservoirs). The removal of the Elwha River dams (2011-2014) on the Olympic Peninsula is the most significant experiment in restoring the salmon-nutrient pathway: with dam removal, salmon runs are rebuilding, bears are returning to former fishing sites, and the nutrient cascade is reactivating after a century of interruption.

### Indigenous Knowledge and the Salmon Network

The salmon-bear-forest nutrient pathway is not a modern scientific discovery. Indigenous peoples of the PNW understood and managed this relationship for millennia.

The Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, and Coast Salish nations recognized that the health of the forest and the health of the salmon were inseparable — not as a metaphor but as an observed ecological relationship embedded in management practices. Salmon streams were tended: woody debris was placed to create spawning habitat, streamside vegetation was managed to maintain shade, and harvest was regulated to ensure adequate spawning escapement. These practices — now rediscovered by Western ecology as "process-based restoration" — maintained the nutrient pathway for thousands of years before Euro-American settlement disrupted it.

The Nez Perce (Nimiipu) maintained similar relationships with salmon in the interior Columbia Basin, managing harvest to sustain runs that fed both their communities and the terrestrial ecosystems along the rivers. The word "nimiipu" means "the people" — but the relationship to salmon is foundational to Nez Perce identity, and the nation's treaty-reserved fishing rights reflect a continuing responsibility for salmon stewardship that predates the state of Idaho by millennia.

## Network 3: The Fungal-Mammal-Forest Mutualism

### The Three-Kingdom Partnership

Beneath the surface of every old-growth Douglas-fir forest, an underground network of mycorrhizal fungi connects tree roots to each other and to the small mammals that disperse fungal spores across the landscape. This three-kingdom mutualism — tree, fungus, mammal — is one of the best-documented examples of obligate multi-species interdependence in temperate ecology, first described by Maser, Trappe & Nussbaum (1978) and elaborated by Carey and colleagues through the 1990s and 2000s.

### The Players

**Trees:** Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), and other PNW conifers form obligate ectomycorrhizal partnerships with fungi. Without mycorrhizal symbionts, these trees cannot efficiently acquire phosphorus, nitrogen, or water from the soil. Seedlings that fail to acquire mycorrhizal colonization within their first growing season typically die. The partnership is not optional — it is a requirement for conifer survival.

**Fungi:** Approximately 200+ species of ectomycorrhizal fungi form partnerships with PNW conifers. Many of these — particularly genera including *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, *Hymenogaster*, *Leucogaster*, and *Elaphomyces* — produce hypogeous (underground) fruiting bodies: truffles. Unlike epigeous mushrooms that release spores into the air, truffles depend on animals to excavate, consume, and disperse their spores through fecal deposition. The truffle has traded wind dispersal for animal dispersal — a strategy that requires a reliable mammalian vector.

**Mammals:** Northern flying squirrels (*Glaucomys sabrinus*), Douglas squirrels (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*), red-backed voles (*Myodes gapperi*), deer mice (*Peromyscus maniculatus*), and Pacific jumping mice (*Zapus trinotatus*) are the primary truffle consumers and spore dispersers in PNW forests. Each species contributes differently to the dispersal network.

### The Mutualism, Step by Step

**Step 1: Fungal fruiting**

Mycorrhizal fungi colonize tree roots, extending the root system's effective surface area by 10-100x through hyphal networks. In return for carbon from the tree (photosynthate), the fungus delivers soil nutrients (phosphorus, nitrogen, micronutrients) and water to the root. When the fungus reproduces, it produces a truffle — a compact, aromatic fruiting body 1-5 cm in diameter, located 2-15 cm below the soil surface. The truffle emits volatile compounds (including dimethyl sulfide and various terpenes) that attract mammalian excavators.

**Step 2: Mammalian excavation and consumption**

Northern flying squirrels are the keystone dispersal agent. Flying squirrel diet consists of 50-90% hypogeous fungi year-round, with the proportion increasing in winter when other food sources decline. Flying squirrels locate truffles using olfaction — their large, sensitive noses detect the volatile signatures of ripe truffles through several centimeters of soil. They excavate truffles with their forepaws, consume them, and move through the forest canopy by gliding between trees — covering distances of 20-50 meters per glide (maximum recorded: 90 meters).

Douglas squirrels consume substantial quantities of truffles seasonally (particularly in spring before cone crops ripen), supplementing the flying squirrel's nocturnal dispersal with diurnal activity. Red-backed voles consume truffles on the forest floor. Deer mice are generalist truffle consumers, less specialized than flying squirrels but operating across a broader range of habitats and forest ages — including young second-growth where flying squirrel density is low.

**Step 3: Spore dispersal through fecal deposition**

Fungal spores pass through the mammalian digestive tract intact and viable. When the animal defecates — often at a site distant from the truffle source — the fecal pellet contains a concentrated inoculum of mycorrhizal spores, bacteria (nitrogen-fixing and other beneficial species acquired in the gut), and organic material that provides a nutrient-rich medium for spore germination. A single flying squirrel fecal pellet is, in effect, a self-contained mycorrhizal inoculation kit.

The spatial pattern of fecal deposition determines the spatial pattern of mycorrhizal colonization. Flying squirrels deposit fecal pellets throughout their home range — including in recently disturbed areas (clearcuts, burned sites, canopy gaps) where tree seedlings are establishing and mycorrhizal inocula are scarce. This makes flying squirrels essential for forest regeneration: they inoculate new seedlings with the mycorrhizal partners those seedlings need to survive.

**Step 4: Mycorrhizal colonization and forest regeneration**

Spores germinate in the soil, colonize nearby tree roots, and establish the mycorrhizal network that will sustain those trees for centuries. In undisturbed old-growth forest, mycorrhizal networks are already dense and seedlings can acquire fungal partners from the existing network. But in disturbed sites — after logging, fire, or blowdown — the mycorrhizal community may be degraded or absent, and mammalian spore dispersal becomes the primary mechanism for re-establishing the tree-fungus partnership.

### Quantification

Carey et al. (1999) documented that northern flying squirrels in old-growth Douglas-fir forests consumed an average of 17 genera of hypogeous fungi, with individual squirrels consuming truffles from 8-12 genera per season. Fecal pellet analysis showed that 90%+ of pellets contained viable mycorrhizal spores. Population density of flying squirrels in old-growth (2-5 per hectare) multiplied by home range size (2-15 hectares) and nightly movement patterns yields an estimated spore dispersal rate of millions of viable inocula per hectare per year across the forest landscape.

### The Spotted Owl Connection (AVI Cross-Link)

The northern flying squirrel's position as the keystone mycorrhizal disperser intersects with its role as the primary prey of the Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*, ESA Threatened). This creates a nested dependency:

**Old-growth forest structure** → supports **flying squirrel populations** → sustains **spotted owl populations** AND → maintains **mycorrhizal dispersal** → enables **forest regeneration**

The barred owl's (*Strix varia*) invasion of the PNW has disrupted this network. Barred owls compete with spotted owls for territory and also prey on flying squirrels — adding a second predator to the flying squirrel population. If combined owl predation suppresses flying squirrel density below the threshold needed for adequate mycorrhizal dispersal, the consequences would propagate from the canopy to the soil: reduced spore dispersal → reduced mycorrhizal colonization → impaired seedling survival → degraded forest regeneration. This scenario has not been documented but represents a plausible cascade pathway connecting an invasive predator to forest ecosystem function through a mammalian intermediary.

### Logging and the Mutualism

Clear-cut logging disrupts every node in the fungal-mammal-forest mutualism simultaneously. Removing the canopy eliminates flying squirrel habitat (no glide paths, no cavity nest trees), removes the photosynthate supply to mycorrhizal fungi (no host trees), and exposes the soil to desiccation that kills moisture-sensitive truffle species. The result is a mycorrhizal "dead zone" where seedlings planted for reforestation may lack the fungal partners they need for establishment.

Recovery is slow. Flying squirrels recolonize stands as canopy closure develops (typically 30-50 years post-harvest), and mycorrhizal diversity rebuilds over decades as different fungal species re-establish. But the full diversity of the old-growth mycorrhizal community — with its 200+ fungal species and complex network architecture — may take centuries to recover. This lag creates a conservation paradox: the forest appears to have regenerated (trees are growing), but the below-ground mutualistic network that sustains long-term forest health may be impoverished for generations.

## Network 4: Beaver Ecosystem Engineering

### The Architect and the Architecture

The American beaver (*Castor canadensis*) is the PNW's preeminent non-human ecosystem engineer. No other mammal (except humans) modifies its physical environment to the extent that beavers do. Beaver dams, lodges, canals, and the pond complexes they create fundamentally alter hydrology, geomorphology, water chemistry, vegetation, and biodiversity at the watershed scale. The ecological effects are not merely local — they cascade through trophic levels and across ecosystem boundaries, making beaver engineering one of the most consequential mammalian activities in the PNW.

### Engineering Effects

**Hydrological transformation**

A beaver dam is a leaky structure — not a concrete barrier but a porous mass of sticks, mud, and debris that slows water rather than stopping it. This slowing transforms stream hydrology:

- **Water table elevation:** A single dam raises the local water table by 0.5-2.0 meters across an area extending 50-200 meters from the pond margin. Groundwater recharge increases, maintaining soil moisture in adjacent riparian zones even during summer drought.

- **Flood attenuation:** Beaver pond complexes store water during high-flow events (snowmelt, rain-on-snow) and release it slowly, reducing downstream flood peaks by 10-40% in small watersheds. This natural flood control service has economic value comparable to engineered retention structures.

- **Baseflow augmentation:** The water stored in beaver ponds and the elevated water table release water slowly during summer low-flow periods, maintaining stream baseflow when unponded reaches may go dry. In the drought-prone east-side PNW, beaver-created water storage is increasingly recognized as natural climate adaptation. Hood & Bayley (2008) found that beaver ponds increased total open-water area by 9x during drought years compared to undammed watersheds.

- **Stream temperature moderation:** Beaver ponds are thermal sinks — they absorb solar radiation during the day and release heat at night, moderating downstream temperature extremes. Paradoxically, while pond surface temperatures may be warmer than stream temperatures, the groundwater recharge effect of beaver dams introduces cold groundwater into downstream reaches, often producing net cooling at the watershed scale. Pollock et al. (2014) documented that beaver dam complexes in the John Day River basin (OR) maintained cooler downstream temperatures during summer heat events.

**Geomorphological transformation**

Beaver dams trap sediment. Over years to decades, the sediment wedge behind a dam builds up, creating a flat, fertile valley floor (a beaver meadow) where a narrow, incised channel previously existed. Naiman et al. (1988) estimated that beaver ponds in North America historically stored approximately 2 billion cubic meters of sediment — a landscape-scale geomorphological force.

When beaver dams are eventually abandoned and breach, the stored sediment is colonized by riparian vegetation, creating lush meadows that persist for decades to centuries. The sequence — stream → pond → meadow → stream (if beavers do not return) — is a cyclical geomorphological process that creates habitat heterogeneity across the landscape. PNW valley bottoms that appear to be "natural" meadows may in fact be former beaver ponds.

**Biodiversity amplification**

Beaver-created wetlands support species assemblages that would not exist in the simplified channel:

| Taxon | Species Benefiting | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Salmonids | Coho salmon, steelhead, cutthroat trout | Rearing habitat (deep pools, side channels, cover) |
| Amphibians | Pacific tree frog, red-legged frog, long-toed salamander | Breeding ponds, larval rearing |
| Waterfowl | Mallard, wood duck, hooded merganser (AVI cross-link) | Nesting habitat, brood-rearing ponds |
| Songbirds | Willow flycatcher, yellow warbler, common yellowthroat (AVI cross-link) | Riparian shrub nesting, insect prey |
| Mammals | Muskrat, mink, river otter, water shrew | Aquatic habitat, prey base |
| Invertebrates | Dragonflies, damselflies, caddisflies, mayflies | Lentic (still-water) habitat |
| Plants | Willow, sedge, cattail, pondweed | Wetland creation in formerly dry valleys |

The biodiversity effect is not subtle. Studies comparing beaver-dammed reaches to undammed control reaches consistently show 2-5x greater species richness for amphibians, 1.5-3x greater species richness for fish, and significantly higher waterfowl density in beaver-influenced habitats.

### Beaver and Salmon: The Partnership

The beaver-salmon relationship is one of the PNW's most important ecological interactions — and one of the most actively managed.

**Juvenile salmon rearing:** Beaver ponds provide critical off-channel rearing habitat for juvenile coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*) and steelhead (*O. mykiss*). Pond complexes offer deep, slow-water areas with overhead cover (from woody debris and overhanging vegetation) where juvenile fish can grow while avoiding the energetic cost of holding position in fast current. In coastal WA and OR streams, coho juveniles that rear in beaver ponds overwinter at higher survival rates and emerge larger in spring than those in unponded reaches — giving them a competitive advantage when they enter the ocean.

**Historical context:** The HBC fur trade's systematic elimination of beavers from the PNW in the early 1800s removed millions of beaver dams and the pond complexes they created. The subsequent loss of off-channel salmon rearing habitat was enormous — potentially eliminating habitat for hundreds of millions of juvenile salmon across the region. Restoring beaver populations is now recognized as a component of salmon recovery plans in multiple WA and OR watersheds.

**Beaver dam analogs (BDAs):** Where natural beaver recolonization is slow or where social tolerance for beaver is limited, restoration practitioners install BDAs — human-built structures of posts and woven woody material that mimic beaver dam function. BDAs slow water, raise water tables, reconnect streams to floodplains, and — critically — attract actual beavers, who recognize BDAs as suitable habitat and often adopt and expand them. Pollock et al. (2014) demonstrated that BDAs in incised streams of the John Day River basin (OR) reconnected streams to floodplains, raised water tables, and converted deeply incised channels to multi-thread anastomosing systems within 5-10 years.

### The Beaver-Muskrat-Mink Sub-Network

Within the beaver-engineered landscape, a secondary food web operates:

- **Muskrats** (*Ondatra zibethicus*) colonize beaver ponds, building their own lodges and feeding platforms within the beaver-created wetland. Muskrats consume aquatic vegetation (cattails, sedges, rushes) that beavers do not eat, partitioning the herbivore niche. Muskrat lodge construction further diversifies habitat structure.

- **American mink** (*Neogale vison*) are semi-aquatic predators that hunt in beaver pond complexes, taking muskrats, fish, crayfish, frogs, and waterfowl. Mink use beaver lodges and bank dens as shelter. The beaver-muskrat-mink chain is a predator-prey-engineer interaction that exists only because beaver engineering created the habitat.

- **River otters** (*Lontra canadensis*) forage in beaver ponds, taking fish (sculpins, suckers, salmonids) and crayfish. Otters may den in abandoned beaver lodges.

This sub-network illustrates how a single engineer species (beaver) creates the physical habitat that supports an entire food web of secondary engineers (muskrat), predators (mink, otter), and prey (fish, frogs, invertebrates).

## Network 5: Predator-Prey Webs and Mesopredator Dynamics

### The Four-Tier Architecture

The PNW's terrestrial predator-prey web is organized into four functional tiers:

**Tier 1 — Apex predators:** Gray wolf, cougar, grizzly bear

These species regulate Tier 2 (mesopredators) through interference competition and direct killing, and regulate herbivore populations through predation and behavioral modification. Their presence or absence determines the structure of the entire food web below them.

**Tier 2 — Mesopredators:** Coyote, bobcat, American mink, marten, fisher, raccoon, spotted and striped skunk

When Tier 1 is intact, mesopredator populations are suppressed and their prey species benefit. When Tier 1 is removed (as has occurred across most of the PNW lowlands), mesopredator populations increase — mesopredator release — with cascading negative effects on ground-nesting birds, small mammals, and other prey.

**Tier 3 — Small-mammal prey base:** Deer mice, voles (6+ species), chipmunks (3+ species), ground squirrels, shrews (10+ species), snowshoe hares, cottontails

This tier contains the greatest biomass and the highest reproductive turnover. Fluctuations in Tier 3 populations (vole cycles, hare cycles) propagate upward through the predator community, driving population cycles in owls, weasels, and other specialist predators.

**Tier 4 — Ungulate prey:** Elk (Roosevelt and Rocky Mountain), black-tailed deer, mule deer, mountain goat, bighorn sheep, pronghorn

Ungulates are regulated by Tier 1 predation and by forage availability. Their browsing intensity, in turn, determines vegetation structure — completing the loop from predators through herbivores to plants.

### Key Predator-Prey Interaction Webs

**The Owl-Squirrel-Vole Web (Montane Forest)**

The montane forest predator-prey web is dominated by the owl-mustelid predator guild and the squirrel-vole prey guild:

- Northern spotted owl → northern flying squirrel (primary prey, 30-60% of diet in old-growth)
- Northern spotted owl → red tree vole (secondary prey in OR, up to 40% of diet in some stands)
- Northern spotted owl → woodrats, deer mice, voles (supplementary prey)
- Barred owl → all of the above, plus a broader prey spectrum including crayfish, frogs, insects
- Northern goshawk → Douglas squirrel, chipmunks, snowshoe hare
- Great horned owl → snowshoe hare, woodrats, skunks, rabbits
- American marten → red squirrels/Douglas squirrels, voles, chipmunks
- Long-tailed weasel → voles, mice, chipmunks
- Short-tailed weasel → voles, pikas, mice

The redundancy in this web — multiple predators sharing prey species — provides resilience. If one predator species declines, others can partially compensate. But the spotted owl-flying squirrel linkage is uniquely vulnerable: no other predator is as dependent on flying squirrels, and no other prey species provides the same mycorrhizal dispersal service. The loss of spotted owls does not merely remove a predator — it removes the ecological indicator that signals whether the flying squirrel-fungus-forest mutualism (Network 3) is intact.

**The Hare Cycle (Subalpine/Boreal)**

The snowshoe hare (*Lepus americanus*) drives one of ecology's most studied predator-prey oscillations. In the boreal forests of Canada, hare populations cycle with a period of 8-11 years, peaking at densities of 1,500+ per square kilometer and crashing to fewer than 10 per square kilometer. Predator populations — particularly Canada lynx — follow the cycle with a 1-2 year lag.

In the PNW, the hare cycle is damped relative to the boreal. PNW populations are at the southern edge of the hare's range, where the cycle amplitude is reduced by milder climate, higher predator diversity (more predator species share the hare prey base, reducing any single predator's dependence), and lower peak hare density. Nevertheless, the cycle operates: lynx, marten, great horned owl, and northern goshawk populations in the north Cascades and Selkirks track hare abundance, and hare crash years correlate with reduced predator reproduction.

**Climate is disrupting the hare cycle** through a novel mechanism: coat-color mismatch. Snowshoe hares molt from brown to white in fall (for crypsis against snow) and back to brown in spring. The timing of the molt is photoperiod-driven (responding to day length), while snowpack timing is climate-driven (responding to temperature). As warming reduces snowpack duration, white hares increasingly appear against brown, snow-free backgrounds — visible to every predator in the forest. Mills et al. (2013) documented that coat-color mismatch increases predation mortality by 7-14%, an effect sufficient to shift the hare cycle toward lower amplitude and longer trough periods.

**The Steppe Prey Web (East-Side)**

The east-side steppe predator-prey web is dominated by diurnal raptor predation (golden eagle, red-tailed hawk, ferruginous hawk — AVI cross-link) and nocturnal mammalian predation (coyote, badger, kit fox, bobcat):

- Golden eagle → jackrabbits, ground squirrels, pronghorn fawns, marmots
- Coyote → jackrabbits, cottontails, ground squirrels, voles, deer fawns
- American badger → ground squirrels, pocket gophers, kangaroo rats
- Kit fox → kangaroo rats, pocket mice, ground squirrels
- Bobcat → rabbits, ground squirrels, deer fawns
- Northern grasshopper mouse → grasshoppers, scorpions, deer mice (intraguild predation)

The badger-coyote cooperative hunting system (Module 4, Zone 7) is notable: badgers excavate ground squirrels from burrow systems while coyotes wait at escape exits, with combined capture success exceeding either species hunting alone. This is one of the few documented examples of interspecific cooperative predation among mammalian carnivores — made more remarkable by the fact that badgers and coyotes are otherwise competitors and occasional mutual predators.

## Network 6: The Marine Trophic Network

### Structure

The PNW marine trophic network is organized by the three killer whale ecotypes, each occupying a distinct trophic pathway:

**Pathway A: Resident Orca → Chinook Salmon → Freshwater Ecosystem**

Southern Resident killer whales (73 individuals, ESA Endangered) are Chinook salmon specialists. Approximately 80% of their identified prey is Chinook salmon aged 3-5 years. This dietary specialization creates a trophic linkage that extends from the open ocean (where whales forage) through the continental shelf (where adult Chinook accumulate before spawning) to every freshwater watershed in the PNW (where Chinook spawn and rear as juveniles).

The conservation implication is extraordinary in its breadth: dam management on the Snake and Columbia Rivers, water allocation in the Yakima Basin, stormwater management in Puget Sound, hatchery reform across the region, and agricultural practices along every salmon-bearing stream in Washington, Oregon, and Idaho all ultimately affect how many Chinook are available to a population of 73 whales in the Salish Sea. No other marine mammal conservation challenge in North America is so deeply entangled with terrestrial land use.

**Pathway B: Bigg's Orca → Pinnipeds/Porpoises → Post-MMPA Recovery**

Bigg's (transient) killer whales eat marine mammals exclusively: harbor seals, harbor porpoises, Steller sea lions, California sea lions, Dall's porpoises, minke whales, and gray whale calves. Their population has roughly doubled since the 1990s (~350+ individuals in the West Coast Transient community), tracking the post-MMPA recovery of pinniped populations.

The Bigg's-pinniped interaction represents the return of natural apex predation to the nearshore marine ecosystem. As Bigg's populations have increased, harbor seal numbers in some areas of the San Juan Islands and southern Gulf Islands have stabilized or slightly declined — suggesting that the predator-prey equilibrium that existed before seal hunting and whale exploitation is re-establishing itself.

The asymmetry between Pathway A and Pathway B tells an ecological story: generalists (Bigg's, feeding on whatever marine mammals are available) track recovering prey bases, while specialists (Southern Residents, feeding only on Chinook) track declining prey bases. The same management regime (MMPA) that protected pinnipeds from hunting also indirectly benefited the predator of those pinnipeds — while the prey base of the fish-eating ecotype deteriorated under entirely different management regimes (dam building, habitat degradation, hatchery competition).

**Pathway C: Offshore Orca → Sharks → Deep-Water Ecosystem**

Offshore killer whales (~300 individuals) are believed to feed primarily on sharks (Pacific sleeper shark, blue shark, possibly salmon sharks), explaining their heavily worn teeth (shark skin dermal denticles are abrasive) and rounded dorsal fin tips. This pathway connects the continental shelf and slope ecosystem to a top predator about which remarkably little is known. The offshore ecotype represents a major gap in PNW marine ecology — basic demographic parameters, dietary composition, and population trajectory are poorly characterized.

### The Pinniped-Salmon Conflict

The intersection of Pathway A and Pathway B creates one of the PNW's most complex ecological management challenges. California sea lions and harbor seals prey on ESA-listed salmon at fish ladders (notably Bonneville Dam on the Columbia River), removing an estimated 2-5% of the spring Chinook run in some years. This predation is legal under the MMPA but conflicts directly with ESA recovery goals for salmon — and, by extension, with recovery goals for Southern Resident killer whales that depend on those same Chinook.

The management response — Congressional authorization for lethal removal of individually identified pinnipeds at Bonneville Dam (MMPA Section 120) — represents one of the few exceptions to blanket marine mammal protection. The conflict illustrates how ecological networks create management paradoxes: protecting pinnipeds (MMPA) benefits Bigg's orcas but may harm salmon and Southern Residents; protecting salmon (ESA) requires managing pinnipeds at specific locations; and protecting Southern Residents (ESA) requires both more salmon and continued pinniped protection (because pinniped declines would harm Bigg's orcas, not help Residents).

### The Sea Otter-Kelp Network

The sea otter keystone effect (documented in Network 1 as a trophic cascade) also functions as a community-level network:

Sea otter → controls sea urchin → preserves kelp forest → supports:
- Fish assemblages (rockfish, lingcod, greenling) that shelter in kelp canopy
- Invertebrate communities (abalone, sea cucumber, crab) associated with kelp holdfast
- Seabirds (pigeon guillemot, marbled murrelet — AVI cross-link) that forage in and around kelp beds
- Marine nutrient cycling (kelp photosynthesis drives primary production; kelp detritus feeds detritivore food webs)

The network extends beyond trophic interactions. Kelp forests physically attenuate wave energy, reducing coastal erosion. Kelp canopy shades the water surface, moderating temperature. Kelp produces oxygen through photosynthesis, supporting aerobic communities in the water column. The sea otter, by maintaining the kelp forest, is effectively maintaining a marine habitat type — analogous to beaver maintaining freshwater wetlands.

## Network 7: Cross-Kingdom Soil Engineering

### The Below-Ground Network

The soil ecosystem is the PNW's most underappreciated ecological network. Beneath every forest, meadow, prairie, and steppe lies a three-dimensional matrix of tunnels, root systems, fungal hyphae, and invertebrate communities — engineered and maintained by mammals that rarely see daylight.

### Engineering Nodes

**Pocket Gophers (genus *Thomomys*, 5 PNW species)**

Pocket gophers are the primary mammalian soil engineers in the PNW. A single gopher displaces 1.5-3 tons of soil per hectare per year. This burrowing:

- **Aerates compacted soils:** Gopher tunnels introduce air channels into clay-heavy soils, improving root penetration and water infiltration. In the glacial outwash prairies of the Puget lowlands, Mazama pocket gopher burrowing is the primary natural soil disturbance maintaining soil porosity.

- **Buries organic matter:** Gophers push surface vegetation and litter into their tunnels, accelerating decomposition and nutrient cycling. Buried organic matter decomposes in the moister, microbially active soil environment faster than it would on the surface.

- **Creates germination sites:** Gopher mounds — piles of excavated soil pushed to the surface — are bare, disturbed microsites where early-successional plants can germinate without competition from established vegetation. Many PNW prairie wildflowers (including rare species dependent on Mazama pocket gopher habitat) require these disturbed microsites for establishment.

- **Maintains mycorrhizal networks:** Gopher tunnels create pathways for mycorrhizal fungal hyphae to extend through the soil matrix, potentially facilitating the "wood wide web" of underground fungal connections between trees.

**Moles (family Talpidae, 4 PNW species)**

Moles complement pocket gophers by operating at different soil depths and in different soil types. Townsend's mole — the largest North American mole — creates deep permanent tunnels (8-18 inches below surface) in the heavy clay and alluvial soils of the Willamette and Puget lowlands. These deep tunnels:

- Improve drainage in heavy soils
- Create habitat for invertebrates (beetles, earthworms, centipedes) that subsequently support the shrew community
- Consume root-feeding insect larvae (wireworms, white grubs), providing agricultural pest control valued at millions of dollars annually

The American shrew-mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*) — a PNW endemic and the smallest mole in North America — operates at the interface between fossorial and surface habitats, tunneling through shallow litter and soil while also foraging on the forest floor. Its semi-fossorial lifestyle makes it a bridge species connecting the below-ground engineering community to the surface food web.

**Shrews (genus *Sorex*, 10+ PNW species)**

Shrews are not engineers in the physical sense (they do not build tunnels or move soil), but they are the dominant predators of the below-ground invertebrate community. Their metabolic intensity — consuming 80-120% of body weight daily — drives predation pressure on beetle larvae, earthworms, spiders, centipedes, snails, and other soil invertebrates that would otherwise reach population levels capable of damaging root systems and altering decomposition rates.

The shrew community's 10+ species partition the soil environment along moisture gradients (from the Pacific water shrew in stream banks to Merriam's shrew in arid steppe) and depth gradients (from the surface-foraging vagrant shrew to the semi-fossorial shrew-mole). This partitioning ensures that invertebrate regulation occurs across the full range of soil habitats in the PNW.

### The Gopher-Plant-Pollinator Network

In prairies and meadows, pocket gopher burrowing creates a network that extends beyond the soil:

1. **Gopher mounds** create bare soil microsites
2. **Prairie wildflowers** (including lupine, camas, and other nitrogen-fixing or nutrient-demanding species) colonize mounds
3. **Wildflower blooms** attract pollinators (native bees, butterflies, hoverflies)
4. **Pollinator populations** support reproduction of the broader plant community

In the Puget lowland prairies where Mazama pocket gophers are ESA-Threatened, this network is at risk of collapse. Without gopher disturbance, prairie vegetation becomes dominated by dense grasses that exclude wildflowers. Without wildflowers, pollinator populations decline. Without pollinators, even surviving wildflower populations fail to reproduce. The gopher is the network's initiator — a below-ground engineer whose activity enables an above-ground cascade from plants to pollinators.

## Network Integration: How the Seven Networks Connect

The seven networks documented above are not independent systems — they overlap, reinforce, and depend on each other. The following integration points illustrate the degree of interconnection:

### Salmon as the Master Connector

Salmon connect Networks 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6:

- **Network 1 (Trophic cascades):** Wolf/cougar predation on elk improves riparian habitat for salmon streams
- **Network 2 (Salmon-bear-forest):** Salmon deliver marine nutrients that fertilize the forest that shades salmon streams
- **Network 4 (Beaver engineering):** Beaver ponds provide critical juvenile salmon rearing habitat
- **Network 5 (Predator-prey webs):** Bears, eagles, and otters are salmon predators; their populations track salmon abundance
- **Network 6 (Marine trophic network):** Southern Resident orcas depend on adult Chinook; salmon decline drives orca decline

Salmon are the thread that sews the PNW's terrestrial and marine ecosystems together. Every network touches salmon, and salmon touch every network. This is why salmon decline reverberates so broadly — it is not the loss of one species but the weakening of the connective tissue that holds the entire system together.

### Old-Growth Forest as Network Hub

Old-growth forest is the physical substrate for Networks 1, 3, 5, and 7:

- **Network 1:** Trophic cascades operate through old-growth and mature forest where predator-prey interactions are most complex
- **Network 3:** The fungal-mammal-forest mutualism depends on old-growth structural complexity (cavity trees, coarse woody debris, intact mycorrhizal networks)
- **Network 5:** The owl-squirrel-vole predator-prey web is most species-rich and functionally complete in old-growth
- **Network 7:** Soil engineering by moles, gophers, and shrews maintains the below-ground ecosystem that old-growth trees depend on

Old-growth logging does not simply remove trees — it simultaneously disrupts trophic cascade dynamics (by simplifying habitat), degrades the mycorrhizal dispersal network (by eliminating flying squirrel habitat), impoverishes the predator-prey web (by reducing structural complexity and prey diversity), and degrades soil engineering (by compacting soils and reducing invertebrate habitat).

### Beaver as Network Multiplier

Beaver engineering (Network 4) amplifies Networks 1, 2, 5, and 6:

- Beaver ponds create the wetland habitat where the wolf-elk-riparian cascade (Network 1) produces its most visible vegetation recovery
- Beaver-created slow water allows salmon carcasses to accumulate and decompose, concentrating nutrient delivery (Network 2)
- Beaver pond complexes support the muskrat-mink-otter food web (Network 5)
- Beaver-maintained stream connectivity sustains the freshwater-to-marine pathway that produces the salmon on which orcas depend (Network 6)

### Climate Change as Network Disruptor

Climate change threatens all seven networks simultaneously through different mechanisms:

| Network | Climate Disruption Pathway |
|---|---|
| 1. Trophic cascades | Snowpack loss degrades lynx competitive advantage; treeline advance compresses alpine predator-prey space |
| 2. Salmon-bear-forest | Warming streams reduce salmon survival; reduced runs deliver less marine nitrogen to forests |
| 3. Fungal-mammal-forest | Drought reduces truffle production; fire eliminates mycorrhizal communities and squirrel habitat |
| 4. Beaver engineering | Reduced summer streamflows limit beaver dam function; increased drought reduces willow/alder food supply |
| 5. Predator-prey webs | Hare coat-color mismatch increases predation; range shifts alter predator-prey spatial overlap |
| 6. Marine trophic network | Ocean warming reduces Chinook productivity; acidification affects marine food web base |
| 7. Soil engineering | Drought reduces soil moisture and invertebrate prey; fire eliminates gopher/mole habitat |

The convergence of these disruption pathways is the central conservation challenge for PNW mammals: the ecological networks that sustain 175+ species and the ecosystem services they provide are being simultaneously weakened from multiple directions. No single-species management action can address this — only landscape-scale conservation that maintains the physical habitat, trophic structure, and biogeochemical flows these networks require.

## Conservation Implications

### Priority Network Nodes

Not all nodes in the PNW's ecological networks are equally important. The following species or functional groups, if removed or severely reduced, would cause the greatest network-wide disruption:

1. **Pacific salmon (all species):** The master connector. Salmon decline weakens Networks 1, 2, 4, 5, and 6 simultaneously. Salmon recovery is the single most important ecological action for the PNW.

2. **American beaver:** The multiplier. Beaver loss eliminates the engineering effects of Network 4 and weakens Networks 1, 2, and 5. Beaver restoration is the most cost-effective watershed-scale conservation action available.

3. **Northern flying squirrel:** The disperser. Flying squirrel loss breaks the fungal-mammal-forest mutualism (Network 3) and removes the key prey linking old-growth structure to spotted owl conservation (Network 5). Flying squirrel conservation requires maintaining old-growth forest.

4. **Gray wolf / cougar:** The cascade initiators. Loss of apex predators triggers mesopredator release (Network 5) and disrupts the trophic cascades (Network 1) that maintain riparian vegetation and salmon habitat. Wolf recovery and cougar population maintenance are essential for network-level ecosystem function.

5. **Sea otter:** The kelp guardian. Sea otter loss triggers urchin barren formation, collapsing the nearshore marine food web (Networks 1, 6). The recovering WA otter population is one of the PNW's most consequential ongoing restorations.

### The Network Perspective

The species-by-species conservation paradigm — protecting individual species through ESA listings, recovery plans, and critical habitat designations — is necessary but insufficient. The PNW's ecological networks demonstrate that species do not exist in isolation: they exist in relationships, and those relationships have their own conservation requirements. Protecting the salmon without protecting the bears that transport salmon nutrients into the forest, the beavers that create salmon rearing habitat, the wolves that improve riparian vegetation through trophic cascades, and the flying squirrels that maintain the mycorrhizal networks on which forest productivity depends — is to protect one node while allowing the network to unravel around it.

The network perspective suggests that the most effective PNW mammal conservation strategy is not a list of individual species actions but a set of landscape-scale commitments:

- Maintain and restore old-growth forest structure (supports Networks 1, 3, 5, 7)
- Recover salmon populations through dam management, habitat restoration, and harvest reform (supports Networks 1, 2, 4, 5, 6)
- Support beaver recolonization and dam building (supports Network 4 and amplifies Networks 1, 2, 5)
- Maintain apex predator populations (wolf, cougar) (supports Networks 1, 5)
- Protect and expand sea otter range (supports Networks 1, 6)
- Reduce climate change impacts through both mitigation and adaptation (protects all networks)

These are not independent actions — they are facets of a single strategy: maintaining the ecological networks that have sustained PNW mammalian biodiversity for millennia.
