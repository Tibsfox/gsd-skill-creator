## Part 1: Rodentia

The Pacific Northwest hosts approximately 70 species of rodents spanning 7 families -- from the ancient Mountain Beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*), the most primitive living rodent with a lineage stretching back 50 million years, to the ecosystem-engineering American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*), whose dams reshape entire watersheds. Rodents occupy every PNW ecoregion band from alpine talus to riparian bottomland, from old-growth canopy to subterranean burrow networks. Their ecological roles -- seed dispersal, mycorrhizal spore transport, soil turnover, and prey base -- make them the invisible infrastructure of PNW forest ecosystems.

**Ecological Interaction** [REL-ENGINEER]
The mountain beaver constructs elaborate subterranean burrow systems -- networks of tunnels, nest chambers, food caches, and specialized "toilet" chambers that may extend 3 meters deep and span 20 meters horizontally. These burrow systems aerate soil, redirect subsurface water flow, create microhabitats for invertebrates and amphibians, and accelerate nutrient cycling through the transport of organic material underground. The burrow entrances, marked by characteristic "haystacks" of cut vegetation left to wilt before consumption, create small canopy gaps that increase understory plant diversity. Abandoned mountain beaver burrows are used by shrews, salamanders, and invertebrates for decades after the beaver departs.

*The mountain beaver is not a beaver at all, and it does not live exclusively in mountains. Its common name is a misnomer inherited from early settlers who observed its habit of felling small vegetation near streams. Its family, Aplodontidae, diverged from all other rodent lineages approximately 50 million years ago during the Eocene, making it the sole surviving member of the most ancient rodent family on Earth. Every other branch of its family tree is known only from fossils. The mountain beaver retains anatomical features lost by all other modern rodents: a primitive jaw musculature, a kidney incapable of producing concentrated urine (requiring it to drink water equivalent to one-third of its body weight daily), and a body plan largely unchanged from Eocene fossils. It is, in the most literal sense, a living fossil -- a Mesozoic-era survival strategy quietly persisting beneath the sword ferns of the Pacific Northwest while the world above changed beyond recognition.*

**Cultural Note**
The Quinault and other coastal Salish peoples of western Washington historically recognized the mountain beaver as a feature of the wet forest landscape. Published ethnographic sources document awareness of the animal's extensive burrow systems in relation to forest floor ecology, though detailed cultural significance specific to mountain beaver is not extensively documented in available published sources (O06, O07).

**Ecological Interaction** [REL-ENGINEER / REL-TROPHIC-CASCADE]
Beaver dams create ponds that raise water tables, store sediment, create wetland habitat, moderate stream temperatures, and provide off-channel rearing habitat critical for juvenile Chinook, coho, and steelhead salmon. A single beaver dam complex can increase aquatic habitat area by an order of magnitude. The impounded water filters nutrients, recharges groundwater, and creates riparian zones that support willows, alders, and sedges -- which in turn provide nesting habitat for songbirds, foraging habitat for elk and deer, and cover for mesocarnivores. The beaver-salmon-forest connection is one of the most significant ecological linkages in the PNW: beaver-created habitat complexity directly enhances salmon juvenile survival, and returning salmon carcasses fertilize the riparian vegetation that beavers depend on.

**Cultural Note**
The Tulalip Tribes (O22) recognize beaver as a culturally significant species whose dam-building activities create and maintain salmon habitat. Coast Salish peoples throughout the Salish Sea region observed the relationship between beaver dams and fish abundance long before Euro-American arrival. The fur trade's near-extirpation of beaver from PNW watersheds in the 1800s simultaneously devastated stream habitat complexity -- a connection now central to modern salmon recovery planning.

**Ecological Interaction** [REL-MUTUALIST]
The northern flying squirrel is the primary dispersal vector for ectomycorrhizal fungi in PNW coniferous forests. It locates truffles -- the underground fruiting bodies of mycorrhizal fungi -- by scent, consumes them, and deposits viable fungal spores in fecal pellets throughout the forest as it glides between trees. These spores colonize conifer rootlets, establishing the mycorrhizal networks that trees depend on for water and nutrient uptake. Without the flying squirrel (and the California red-backed vole), reforestation after disturbance proceeds far more slowly because tree seedlings cannot establish their fungal partners. The flying squirrel is simultaneously the primary prey item for the Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) -- creating a three-way dependency: owl needs squirrel, squirrel needs fungi, fungi need trees, trees need owl's forest protection. This is the mycorrhizal courier loop, and the flying squirrel is its central node.

**Cultural Note**
Not specifically documented in available published sources for PNW nations. The flying squirrel's role as a nocturnal fungal spore disperser -- invisible to most human observers yet critical to forest function -- parallels the middleware pattern in skill-creator: a background service that quietly enables the entire system without being directly invoked.

**Ecological Interaction** [REL-MUTUALIST]
The Douglas squirrel's intensive conifer cone harvesting and caching behavior -- cutting green cones and storing them in moist middens on the forest floor -- serves as inadvertent seed dispersal. Forgotten caches germinate into new trees. The squirrel's loud, cascading alarm call ("brrrrr-chk-chk-chk") serves as an early-warning system for the entire forest community; deer, elk, and other mammals respond to Douglas squirrel alarm calls as indicators of approaching predators, particularly goshawks and martens.

**Cultural Note**
Not specifically documented in available published sources for PNW nations. The Douglas squirrel's territorial alarm calling and vigorous cone caching behavior are among the most conspicuous mammalian activities in PNW conifer forests; John Muir described this species as "the squirrel of squirrels" and considered it the most energetic animal in the Sierra and Cascade forests.

### Family Cricetidae -- Voles, Woodrats, Mice, and Muskrat

The Cricetidae is the largest rodent family in the PNW by species count, with voles (*Microtus*, *Myodes*, *Arborimus*), deer mice (*Peromyscus*), woodrats (*Neotoma*), and muskrats (*Ondatra*) occupying virtually every terrestrial habitat. Their ecological roles are immense: mycorrhizal spore dispersal, prey base for nearly every predator in the system, soil disturbance, and seed processing. The vole-truffle-tree mutualism documented by Maser & Maser (1988, P37) remains one of the foundational discoveries in PNW forest ecology.

The California red-backed vole is the keystone mycorrhizal spore disperser in PNW old-growth forests. Maser & Maser (1988, P37) demonstrated that this species consumes truffles -- the underground fruiting bodies of ectomycorrhizal fungi -- and deposits viable fungal spores in fecal pellets throughout the forest floor. This is one of the defining mutualistic relationships in PNW old-growth ecology.

## Part 2: Lagomorpha

The Pacific Northwest hosts 8 lagomorph species spanning two families -- pikas (Ochotonidae) and rabbits/hares (Leporidae). Despite their superficial resemblance to rodents, lagomorphs are a distinct order characterized by a second pair of upper incisors (peg teeth), hindgut fermentation, and coprophagy. PNW lagomorphs range from the alpine-obligate American pika -- a bellwether of climate change -- to the federally endangered pygmy rabbit of the Columbia Basin sagebrush steppe.

**Ecological Interaction**
American pikas are obligate talus dwellers that do not hibernate. Instead, they cache "haypiles" — collections of dried vegetation weighing up to 6 kg — in rock crevices for winter consumption. ## Part 3: Eulipotyphla (Shrews and Moles)

The order Eulipotyphla (formerly Soricomorpha/Insectivora) includes the shrews and moles — small, hypermetabolic insectivores that operate largely unseen beneath leaf litter, soil, and snow. PNW shrews are among the most species-rich small mammal groups, with 12+ species occupying niches from alpine talus to coastal rainforest floor. Their metabolic rates are extraordinary: shrews must eat nearly their body weight daily or starve within hours. Moles, by contrast, are fossorial engineers whose tunneling aerates soil and redistributes nutrients. The shrew-mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*) is a PNW endemic — the smallest mole in North America.

## Part 4: Chiroptera (Bats)

> **H-9 REQUIREMENT — SAFETY CRITICAL:** Every bat species profile in this section MUST include the White-Nose Syndrome (WNS) status extension. White-nose syndrome, caused by the fungal pathogen *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* (Pd), has killed an estimated 6.7 million bats in North America since 2006. As of 2025, Pd has been confirmed in eastern Washington state but has NOT been detected west of the Cascade Range. The WNS status field is safety-relevant per verification matrix gate SC-WNS (source: G21, G22).

The Pacific Northwest hosts approximately 18 bat species spanning two families — Vespertilionidae (evening bats, 17 species) and Molossidae (free-tailed bats, 1 species). PNW bats occupy every terrestrial ecoregion band from sea level to treeline. They are critical insect population regulators: a single little brown myotis can consume 1,000 mosquito-sized insects per hour. Bat species partition the acoustic space through echolocation frequency diversification, with PNW species spanning 15-115 kHz.

### Townsend's Big-eared Bat (*Corynorhinus townsendii*)

*The cave sentinel — its enormous ears and exquisite sensitivity to disturbance make it the bat most likely to abandon a roost when disturbed.*

**Taxonomy**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Order | Chiroptera |
| Family | Vespertilionidae |
| Genus | Corynorhinus |
| Species | townsendii |
| Subspecies | *C. t. townsendii* (Pacific western population) |
| Taxonomic authority | Cooper 1837 |

**Size & Morphometrics**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Size class | micro (medium-large for a bat) |
| Total length | 9-11 cm |
| Mass | 7-14 g |
| Distinguishing features | Enormous ears (3+ cm, coiled when sleeping); two prominent facial glands (lumps on nose); pale to dark brown fur; very slow, maneuverable flight |

**Habitat & Distribution**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Habitat type | HAB-CAVE, HAB-MOIST-FOREST |
| Locomotion mode | volant |
| Elevation range | Sea level-8,500 ft (0-2,591 m) |
| Ecoregion bands | ELEV-SUBTERRANEAN (primary — caves, mines, lava tubes), ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE |
| PNW distribution | Throughout WA, OR, ID in areas with cave or mine roosting habitat. Requires intact cave systems year-round. |

**Ecological Profile**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Ecological role(s) | ROLE-INSECTIVORE |
| Diet | insectivore |
| Trophic level | secondary consumer |
| Key prey/food items | Moths (primary — 90%+ of diet); beetles; gleaning forager |
| Key predators | Raccoon, ringtail (at cave roosts); owls |

**Conservation Status**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| ESA status | Not listed (Virginia big-eared bat subspecies listed; western subspecies petitioned) |
| WA state status | Candidate |
| OR state status | Sensitive-Critical |
| IUCN Red List | LC |
| NatureServe rank | G4 S2S3 |
| Population trend | declining — highly sensitive to roost disturbance |

**White-Nose Syndrome Status** (H-9 — SAFETY REQUIRED)
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| WNS susceptibility | **confirmed susceptible** — cave-hibernating species at high risk |
| WNS detection in PNW range | Pd not yet confirmed at roost sites in WA/OR/ID |
| Pd exposure | not confirmed in PNW populations |
| Population impact | not yet impacted — but cave-dependent lifestyle creates maximum vulnerability |

**Echolocation & Roosting**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Echolocation frequency | 20-45 kHz FM sweep (low intensity — "whispering bat") |
| Echolocation type | FM sweep (very quiet — gleaning specialist) |
| Roost type | Caves, mines, lava tubes (year-round); occasionally old buildings |
| Winter behavior | hibernate (November-March; extremely sensitive to disturbance during hibernation — a single arousal costs 10-30 days of fat reserves) |
| Colony size | small to large (maternity: 12-200; hibernacula: up to 1,000) |

**Ecological Interaction**
Townsend's big-eared bat is the most roost-faithful and disturbance-sensitive bat in the PNW. Maternity colonies will permanently abandon a roost after a single human visit. This extreme sensitivity makes cave and mine management critical for the species' persistence. The bat's low-intensity echolocation ("whispering" calls) and gleaning foraging strategy — hovering to pluck moths from foliage — represent a fundamentally different approach to insect hunting than the aerial hawking used by most *Myotis* species.

### Pallid Bat (*Antrozous pallidus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Chiroptera | Family Vespertilionidae | *Antrozous pallidus*
**Size:** Micro (large). 10-14 cm, 14-25 g. Pale yellowish-brown; large ears; large eyes; heavy muzzle.
**Habitat:** ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-LOWLAND. Arid rocky habitats; cliffs, canyon walls. Eastern WA, OR, southern ID. 500-6,500 ft.
**Ecology:** Insectivore and **ground-gleaning predator** — unique among PNW bats. Takes scorpions, beetles, crickets, centipedes from the ground. Does not use echolocation for prey capture; instead uses passive listening.
**Conservation:** Not listed ESA. WA: Monitor. OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable. IUCN LC.
**WNS Status** (H-9): Unknown susceptibility; not detected; not impacted. Rock crevice roosting may provide partial protection.
**Echolocation:** 28-49 kHz FM sweep (used for navigation, NOT prey capture). Roost: rock crevices, cliff faces, buildings. Hibernate or use torpor. Colony: small to large (10-200).
**Sources:** G10, G22

### Brazilian Free-tailed Bat (*Tadarida brasiliensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Chiroptera | Family Molossidae | *Tadarida brasiliensis*

**Key finding:** 4 species at CRITICAL or HIGH risk, 5 at MODERATE risk, 8 at LOW risk. Pd has already been detected in eastern Washington, meaning the pathogen is present in the PNW and westward spread is a matter of when, not if.
