> **PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — "Fur, Fin & Fang"**
>
> This module integrates findings from Modules 1 through 7, identifies emergent patterns that span individual modules, bridges the mammalian taxonomy to the broader PNW ecosystem narrative, and surfaces the key insights that only become visible when the full body of work is read as a single system. This module asks: what does it all mean when read together?
>

The PNW mammalian system is not a collection of species. It is a network of relationships operating across scales from the mycorrhizal thread to the Pacific basin.

## Part I: The Five Master Narratives

Reading Modules 1-7 as a single body of work reveals five interlocking narratives that organize the entire PNW mammalian system. Each narrative operates at a different scale and timeframe, but all are active simultaneously in the present moment.

### 1.1 The Vertical Narrative: Ocean Floor to Summit

The PNW mammalian system spans a continuous vertical gradient from the deep marine (>600 ft below sea level) to the alpine summit (14,411 ft at Tahoma/Mt. Rainier). No other temperate bioregion on Earth compresses this much vertical diversity into so short a horizontal distance — the straight-line distance from the abyssal canyons off the Olympic coast to the summit of Mt. Olympus is approximately 50 miles. Within that distance, the mammalian community transitions through all eight ecoregion zones defined in `00-ecoregion-definitions.md`, from sperm whales and beaked whales in the deep marine to pikas and wolverines in the alpine.

**The vertical integration is not merely spatial — it is functional.** Energy and nutrients flow vertically through the system:

- **Upward:** Marine-derived nutrients (MDN) carried by salmon from the Pacific, deposited along stream corridors by bears, otters, and eagles, and distributed into the forest canopy through fungal networks. Nitrogen-15 isotope tracing shows salmon-derived nitrogen in Sitka spruce needles 500+ meters from streams [Module 6: Network 2]. This is oceanic energy reaching the montane zone through mammalian agency.

- **Downward:** Terrestrial sediment, leaf litter, and woody debris delivered to streams by gravity, beavers, and flood pulses, creating the substrate and cover that juvenile salmon require. Dead trees become nurse logs that support fungal fruiting bodies consumed by flying squirrels whose spores inoculate new tree roots in a perpetual cycle of decay and regeneration [Module 6: Network 3].

- **Oscillating:** Seasonal altitudinal migration by elk, deer, mountain goats, bears, and wolverines connects alpine summer range with lowland winter range. These movements transport seeds, fungal spores, parasites, and nutrients across elevation bands, maintaining genetic and ecological connectivity between zones [Module 4: all zones].

The vertical narrative means that no ecoregion zone can be understood in isolation. The alpine community depends on lowland forest for winter range. The lowland forest depends on salmon that spawn in montane streams. The montane streams depend on beaver engineering and riparian vegetation shaped by predator-prey dynamics. The whole system is a column of interconnected processes from seafloor to summit.

### 1.2 The Temporal Narrative: Deep Time to Present Day

The PNW mammalian system is not a static assemblage — it is the latest frame in 40+ million years of continuous turnover. Module 5 traces this temporal narrative from the subtropical Eocene forests (brontotheres, creodonts, primates) through the Oligocene cooling (the Grande Coupure), the Miocene grassland radiation (bone-crushing dogs, three-toed horses, oreodonts), the Pliocene transition (Hagerman Horse), the Pleistocene megafaunal world (mammoths, mastodons, saber-toothed cats, ground sloths, giant bison), and the post-glacial recolonization that assembled the modern fauna.

**Key temporal insight:** The modern PNW mammal fauna is extraordinarily young. Most current species arrived in their present ranges within the last 10,000-15,000 years, as the Cordilleran and continental ice sheets retreated. Before that, the region north of approximately 47 degrees N latitude was under ice a mile thick. The communities described in Module 4 — the assemblages that seem "natural" and permanent — are in fact recent reassemblies of species that survived the last glacial maximum in scattered refugia: Haida Gwaii (coastal), the Columbia Plateau (steppe), the Klamath-Siskiyou (mesic forest), and Beringia (northern).

The phylogeographic evidence from Module 5 reveals that many species carry the genetic signatures of these refugial histories. Black bears in coastal BC show mitochondrial lineages distinct from interior populations — evidence of a coastal refugium on Haida Gwaii or along the exposed continental shelf. Pika populations in the Cascades and Olympics show genetic divergence suggesting independent colonization events from different glacial refugia. These genetic patterns are not academic curiosities — they are the raw material of adaptive potential, and their preservation is a conservation priority.

**The temporal narrative also includes the human period.** Module 7 documents how Indigenous peoples managed mammal habitat at landscape scales for 10,000+ years through fire, harvest protocols, and the relational framework that understood mammals as other-than-human persons. The cessation of Indigenous fire management after Euro-American colonization and disease-driven population collapse triggered a cascade of habitat transformations that continue to shape mammal communities today [Module 7: Part IV]. The fur trade (1785-1860s) then extracted mammal populations to near-extirpation levels for commercial profit, creating the depleted baselines from which modern recovery efforts operate [Module 7: Part V].

The temporal narrative thus encompasses three overlapping scales: evolutionary time (millions of years), post-glacial ecological time (thousands of years), and the colonial/modern period (centuries to decades). All three are operating simultaneously in the present: a wolverine denning in Cascade snowpack is using an alpine niche shaped by 30 million years of mountain building, occupying territory recolonized after ice retreated 12,000 years ago, surviving in a landscape that lost its Indigenous fire management 200 years ago, and now threatened by anthropogenic climate change that is shrinking its snowpack on decadal timescales.

### 1.3 The Connectivity Narrative: Networks, Not Isolates

The single most important pattern to emerge from the module series is that PNW mammals function as a network, not as a collection of independent species. Module 6 documents seven distinct ecological networks, but the synthesis reveals that these networks are themselves interconnected — they are networks of networks.

**The salmon node illustrates this.** Salmon connect:
- The marine trophic network (orca ecotypes, humpback whales, pinnipeds) [Module 3, Module 6: Network 6]
- The salmon-bear-forest nutrient pathway (grizzly, black bear, river otter, mink → riparian forest) [Module 6: Network 2]
- The cougar/wolf trophic cascade (predator → elk behavior → riparian vegetation → salmon habitat) [Module 6: Network 1]
- The beaver engineering network (beaver dams → juvenile salmon rearing habitat → adult salmon returns) [Module 6: Network 4]
- The marine mammal network (Southern Resident killer whales → Chinook salmon dependency → ESA overlap) [Module 3]
- Indigenous cultural systems (Coast Salish, Makah, Nez Perce, Yakama salmon ceremonies → harvest protocols → population regulation) [Module 7]

A single Chinook salmon, then, is simultaneously a prey item for orcas, a nutrient vector for forests, a beneficiary of beaver engineering, a dependent of predator-mediated riparian health, and a being within Indigenous relational frameworks. There is no module in which salmon does not appear. The salmon is the connective tissue of the PNW mammalian system.

### 1.4 The Disturbance Narrative: Cascading Consequences

The module series documents four major disturbance regimes and their mammalian consequences:

**Fire regime disruption** [Module 7: Part IV]: The cessation of Indigenous burning after colonization transformed prairie → forest (Willamette Valley Kalapuya), shrub-steppe → juniper woodland (Plateau), and open parkland → dense canopy (Cascades). Each transformation reshuffled mammal communities: western gray squirrels lost oak habitat, Mazama pocket gophers lost prairie, bears lost huckleberry fields, and ungulates lost browse. Modern wildfire — catastrophic, stand-replacing, driven by fuel accumulation — differs fundamentally from the frequent, low-intensity Indigenous fire regime and creates different successional trajectories with different mammalian outcomes.

**Fur trade extraction** [Module 7: Part V]: The maritime fur trade (sea otter → near-extirpation → urchin barrens → kelp forest collapse) and the land-based fur trade (beaver → stream incision → wetland loss → salmon habitat degradation; marten/fisher → old-growth mustelid decline; wolf → extirpation → mesopredator release) created depleted baselines that persist today despite partial recovery.

**Dam construction:** The damming of the Columbia, Snake, and other major rivers eliminated salmon access to thousands of miles of spawning habitat, severing the marine-terrestrial nutrient pathway that connected ocean to forest through bear-mediated nitrogen transport. The Grand Coulee Dam alone (1942) blocked salmon from the entire upper Columbia — an area that had supported Indigenous salmon economies and salmon-dependent mammal communities for millennia.

**Climate change** [Module 4, Module 5]: The module series identifies seven climate-vulnerable species whose ranges are contracting under current warming: wolverine (snowpack-dependent denning), Canada lynx (deep-snow competitive advantage), American pika (thermal intolerance above ~78 degrees F sustained), Cascade red fox (alpine specialist), mountain goat (heat stress, parasite expansion), white-tailed jackrabbit (camouflage mismatch), and several bat species (white-nose syndrome interaction with warming cave temperatures). All seven are alpine or subalpine species with no higher-elevation habitat to retreat to — they face a topographic ceiling.

### 1.5 The Recovery Narrative: What's Coming Back

Against the disturbance narrative, the modules also document a recovery narrative — species returning, populations expanding, and ecological functions being restored:

| Species | Recovery Status | Key Module | Ecological Function Restored |
|---|---|---|---|

| Fisher | Reintroduced Olympic + S. Cascades | 1, 5 | Mesocarnivore, porcupine regulation |

The recovery narrative is not uniformly positive. Southern Resident killer whales continue to decline (73 individuals as of 2024) despite decades of advocacy and legal protection. Caribou are functionally extirpated from the contiguous US (the last South Selkirk caribou were translocated to BC in 2019). Wolverine listing under ESA (2023) reflects ongoing habitat loss. And the species being recovered are returning to a landscape fundamentally different from the one they left — a landscape without Indigenous fire management, with fragmented habitat, altered hydrology, and a climate regime moving faster than evolutionary adaptation.

## Part II: Cross-Module Emergent Patterns

### 2.1 The Three-Kingdom Mutualism: Fungi, Mammals, and Conifers

The most ecologically consequential cross-module pattern is the three-way mutualism between hypogeous fungi (truffles), small mammals (flying squirrels, red-backed voles, chipmunks, deer mice), and coniferous trees (Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce). This system appears in Modules 2A, 2B, 4, and 6, but its full significance only emerges in synthesis:

**The cycle:**
1. Coniferous trees form obligate mycorrhizal associations with fungi — the trees cannot absorb sufficient phosphorus and water without fungal partners, and the fungi cannot photosynthesize without tree hosts [Module 6: Network 3]
2. Hypogeous fungi (truffles) produce fruiting bodies underground — they cannot disperse spores by wind like mushrooms. They depend on mammals to excavate, consume, and defecate their spores at new locations
3. Northern flying squirrels, California red-backed voles, Douglas squirrels, Townsend's chipmunks, and deer mice excavate and consume truffles as a dietary staple (>50% of flying squirrel diet is truffle tissue) [Module 2A]
4. Spores pass through mammalian digestive tracts intact and are deposited in fecal pellets across the forest, inoculating new root tips and maintaining the mycorrhizal network
5. The inoculated trees grow, producing canopy that supports the arboreal mammals, and root systems that support the fungal network — closing the cycle

**Why this matters at the system level:** Old-growth Douglas-fir forests are defined not just by the trees but by the integrity of this mutualistic network. Logging that removes large trees also removes the canopy structure that flying squirrels require (they nest in cavities and witch's broom platforms in old-growth trees), which reduces truffle dispersal, which degrades the mycorrhizal network, which reduces the regenerative capacity of the next generation of trees. The system has three kingdoms and can be broken at any of the three nodes.

**The predator connection:** Northern spotted owls prey heavily on flying squirrels [Module 2A, Module 4: Zone 2]. Flying squirrels are the single most important prey species for spotted owls in many PNW territories (Forsman et al. 2004). Thus the spotted owl's persistence depends on flying squirrel abundance, which depends on truffle production, which depends on old-growth mycorrhizal networks. The owl — the iconic old-growth indicator — is connected to the fungal kingdom through a mammalian intermediary. This three-trophic-level dependency (owl → squirrel → truffle → tree) makes the northern spotted owl a true indicator species: its decline signals degradation of the entire fungal-mammal-forest network, not merely the loss of nesting habitat.

### 2.2 The Predator Architecture: Four Tiers

Across Modules 1, 4, and 6, a consistent four-tier predator architecture emerges in the terrestrial PNW:

**Tier 1: Apex Predators (regulate Tier 2 and herbivores)**
- Gray wolf — cursorial pursuit specialist, pack predation on elk/deer
- Cougar — ambush specialist, solitary predation on elk/deer
- Grizzly bear — apex omnivore, dominance competitor, salmon specialist
- (Historically: gray wolf occupied all habitat types; currently restricted to NE Washington and expanding)

**Tier 2: Mesopredators (regulated by Tier 1, prey on Tier 3 + small mammals)**
- Coyote — dietary generalist, pack and solitary hunting
- Bobcat — ambush generalist, rabbits and rodents
- American marten — arboreal specialist, squirrels and voles
- Fisher — forest generalist, porcupine specialist
- Long-tailed weasel — burrow predator, voles and mice
- Short-tailed weasel (ermine) — alpine/subalpine small-mammal specialist

**Tier 3: Small-Mammal Prey Base (sustains Tiers 1 and 2, drives fungal dispersal)**
- Townsend's vole, long-tailed vole, heather vole, creeping vole — meadow/grassland
- Deer mouse, forest deer mouse — forest/generalist
- Northern flying squirrel, Douglas squirrel — arboreal, truffle dispersal
- Townsend's chipmunk, yellow-pine chipmunk — forest floor, seed caching
- Snowshoe hare — forest/subalpine, lynx specialist prey
- Mountain cottontail, brush rabbit — shrub/edge habitat
- Bushy-tailed woodrat — talus/cliff, midden builder

**Tier 4: Ecosystem Engineers (modify physical habitat)**
- American beaver — hydrology, stream morphology, wetland creation
- Pocket gophers (multiple species) — soil turnover, nutrient cycling, meadow maintenance
- Mountain beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*) — understory herbivory, slope engineering
- Moles (Townsend's, coast, shrew-mole) — soil aeration, invertebrate regulation

The four tiers interact predictably: when Tier 1 is removed (wolf extirpation), Tier 2 expands (coyote mesopredator release), Tier 3 declines in some segments (ground-nesting birds, small mammals vulnerable to mesopredator generalism), and the trophic cascade to vegetation is disrupted. The reintroduction of wolves in Washington is currently testing whether restoration of Tier 1 re-regulates Tier 2 and restores downstream cascade effects [Module 6: Network 5].

### 2.3 The Climate Vulnerability Gradient

Synthesizing the climate exposure data from Modules 1, 2A, 2B, 2C, 4, and 5 reveals a clear vulnerability gradient:

**Most vulnerable (alpine/subalpine specialists, no retreat option):**
1. American pika — thermal ceiling (~78 degrees F), talus-obligate, poor dispersal
2. Wolverine — snowpack-dependent denning, ESA Threatened (2023)
3. Canada lynx — deep-snow specialist, bobcat competition increasing
4. Cascade red fox — alpine-obligate, <500 individuals estimated
5. Olympic marmot — endemic, alpine-obligate, warming-driven parasite expansion

**Highly vulnerable (habitat specialists, limited adaptability):**
6. Mountain goat — heat stress at lower elevations, parasite range expansion
7. White-tailed jackrabbit — camouflage mismatch in reduced snow years
8. Water shrew (Pacific water shrew) — cold-stream obligate, temperature-sensitive
9. Hoary bat — migratory, wind-energy mortality, white-nose syndrome
10. Pygmy rabbit — sagebrush-obligate, ESA Endangered (Columbia Basin DPS)

**Moderately vulnerable (range shifts likely, adaptive capacity exists):**
11. Snowshoe hare — molt timing mismatch in variable snow years
12. Townsend's big-eared bat — cave-dependent, disturbance-sensitive
13. Northern flying squirrel — old-growth dependent, truffle-diet specialist
14. Red tree vole — old-growth canopy specialist, very limited dispersal
15. Southern Resident killer whale — Chinook-dependent, already critically endangered

**Least vulnerable (generalists, expanding range):**
- Coyote, Virginia opossum (recent colonizer), raccoon, deer mouse, black-tailed deer, black bear

The gradient reveals a conservation paradox: the species most vulnerable to climate change are disproportionately concentrated in the alpine zone — the same zone that is least accessible to management intervention and most difficult to restore. Unlike lowland species, alpine mammals cannot be helped through habitat restoration at the landscape scale. Their fate depends on emissions trajectories at the global scale.

### 2.4 The Marine-Terrestrial Boundary

The PNW is unusual among temperate bioregions in the strength and diversity of its marine-terrestrial linkages. The module series documents at least six distinct pathways:

**1. Salmon-mediated nutrient transport** [Module 6: Network 2]: Anadromous salmonids import marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus (N-15, P-33 isotopes traceable) from the Pacific Ocean into interior river systems. Bears, river otters, mink, eagles, and corvids extract salmon carcasses from streams and distribute nutrients into riparian and upland forest. Helfield & Naiman (2001) documented Sitka spruce growing three times faster within 25 meters of salmon-bearing streams compared to non-salmon streams — a growth differential attributable to marine-derived nitrogen. This pathway links the Bering Sea (salmon feeding grounds) to the Cascade montane zone (spawning habitat) through mammalian agency.

**2. Seabird-mediated guano transport** [AVI cross-link]: Seabird colonies deposit guano on offshore islands and coastal cliffs, creating nutrient hotspots that support distinct terrestrial communities. River otters on offshore islands contribute additional marine-derived nutrients through fish consumption and scat deposition.

**3. Pinniped haul-out nutrient deposition** [Module 3]: Harbor seals, Steller sea lions, and California sea lions concentrate marine-derived nutrients at haul-out sites, creating localized enrichment zones.

**4. Sea otter trophic cascade** [Module 3, Module 6: Network 6]: Sea otter predation on urchins maintains kelp forest structure that provides habitat and nursery function for fish, invertebrates, and marine mammals. The kelp-urchin-otter cascade is the best-documented marine trophic cascade in the world (Estes & Palmisano 1974; Estes et al. 1998).

**5. Whale-fall nutrient cycling** [Module 3]: Large whale carcasses that sink to the seafloor create "whale fall" communities that sustain chemosynthetic ecosystems for decades. Gray whale, humpback, and other cetacean carcasses are significant nutrient inputs to the deep marine zone.

**6. Diadromous mammal movements** [Module 3]: River otters move between freshwater and marine habitats, functioning as nutrient bridges. Harbor seals enter river systems to feed on salmon, connecting marine predation pressure to freshwater fish communities.

The strength of these marine-terrestrial linkages means that the health of PNW terrestrial mammals depends partly on the health of the Pacific Ocean — and vice versa. The decline of Southern Resident killer whales (a marine species) is driven by the decline of Chinook salmon (an anadromous species), which is driven partly by the degradation of freshwater spawning habitat (a terrestrial condition), which is driven partly by the loss of beaver engineering and riparian vegetation (terrestrial mammal ecology). The system does not respect the land-sea boundary.

## Part III: The Indigenous Knowledge Framework as Ecological Science

### 3.1 Convergence of Knowledge Systems

Module 7 documents Indigenous ecological knowledge of PNW mammals as practiced by named nations (Coast Salish, Makah, Nuu-chah-nulth, Nez Perce, Yakama, Nlaka'pamux, Secwepemc, Kwakwaka'wakw, and others) across millennia. The synthesis reveals that this knowledge system anticipated Western scientific discoveries by centuries in several specific domains:

**Orca ecotype recognition:** The Nuu-chah-nulth and Makah distinguished between fish-eating and mammal-eating killer whales long before John Ford and Michael Bigg described the Resident/Transient (Bigg's) distinction in the 1970s-80s [Module 3, Module 7: 1.2]. This behavioral classification, maintained through oral tradition and direct observation, reflects ecological knowledge of a precision that Western science did not achieve until the era of photo-identification and acoustic monitoring.

**Trophic cascade awareness:** Nlaka'pamux recognition that cougar presence indicated healthy deer populations [Module 7: 3.7] encodes the core principle of trophic cascades — that predator abundance reflects prey abundance reflects ecosystem health — centuries before Ripple and Beschta formalized the concept through quantitative study. The directional logic (cougar → deer → ecosystem) matches the modern scientific model exactly.

**Beaver as ecosystem engineer:** Published ethnographic sources indicate that certain Sahaptin-speaking peoples managed beaver populations by not trapping all beavers from a dam, ensuring dam maintenance and continued pond habitat [Module 7: 3.4]. This is precisely the logic of modern beaver dam analog (BDA) programs and the "process-based restoration" framework (Pollock et al. 2014) that is now considered best practice in stream restoration. Indigenous peoples were practicing process-based restoration before the concept had a name.

**Fire as habitat management:** Robert Boyd's documentation of Indigenous fire management [Module 7: Part IV] reveals landscape-scale habitat engineering that maintained mammalian diversity through planned disturbance. The Kalapuya burning of the Willamette Valley, Coast Salish burning of Puget Sound prairies, and Interior Salish/Sahaptin burning of Cascade meadows all created habitat mosaics that maximized species diversity — the same outcome that modern restoration ecology attempts to achieve through prescribed fire and mechanical treatment, at far greater cost and with far less temporal depth of understanding.

### 3.2 The Relational Framework and Conservation Ethics

The Indigenous relational framework — understanding mammals as other-than-human persons with agency, intelligence, and social organization — is not merely a cultural perspective. It is an ethical framework that generated measurable conservation outcomes for millennia [Module 7: Part I].

The reciprocal obligations embedded in the relational framework — ceremonial preparation, first-catch ceremonies, proper treatment of remains, harvest restraint — functioned as a distributed conservation system operating at the individual, family, and community level. No centralized management authority was required. The system was self-enforcing because violating the relational obligations was understood to carry both spiritual and ecological consequences: animals would stop giving themselves, hunts would fail, and the community would suffer.

The fur trade disrupted this system by introducing commercial incentive, industrial trapping technology, and economic dependency that overwhelmed the relational constraints on harvest [Module 7: Part V]. The result was the near-extirpation of sea otter, beaver, marten, fisher, wolf, grizzly bear, and other species — a loss that required two centuries of Western scientific management, ESA listing, and active reintroduction to begin reversing.

The synthesis suggests that the Indigenous relational framework and Western scientific management are not competing approaches but complementary ones. Western science excels at population modeling, genetic analysis, and quantitative monitoring. Indigenous knowledge systems excel at place-based, long-term, integrative understanding and at embedding harvest restraint in cultural practice rather than in regulatory enforcement. Modern co-management agreements — Quinault/Hoh elk co-management with NPS, Nez Perce wolf recovery participation, Lummi SRKW advocacy, Makah treaty whaling — represent attempts to integrate these complementary strengths [Module 7: 7.1].

## Part IV: Ecoregion Interactions — The Zone Boundary as Ecological Interface

### 4.1 Cross-Zone Species Flows

Module 4 documented mammalian communities within each of the eight ecoregion zones, but the most ecologically important processes occur at zone boundaries — the interfaces where species, energy, and nutrients flow between zones.

**Alpine ↔ Montane Interface:**
- Seasonal migration: Elk, deer, mountain goats, bears move between summer alpine range and winter montane/lowland range. Migration corridors are matrilineally transmitted in ungulates — the same trails used for thousands of years [Module 4: Zone 1, Zone 2]
- Wolverine circuits cross this boundary repeatedly; individual home ranges span 100+ miles across multiple elevation bands [Module 1, Module 4: Zone 1]
- Pikas at the lower boundary of their alpine range are experiencing upslope retreat as temperatures rise — the interface is shifting [Module 2B, Module 4: Zone 1]

**Montane ↔ Lowland Interface:**
- The Douglas-fir transition zone hosts the highest mammalian species richness in the PNW — it is an ecotone where montane specialists (marten, fisher, flying squirrel) overlap with lowland generalists (raccoon, opossum, coyote) [Module 4: Zone 2, Zone 3]
- Old-growth structural elements (large snags, downed logs, canopy gaps) are concentrated at this interface and support the three-kingdom mutualism (truffle-squirrel-conifer) at its maximum intensity [Module 6: Network 3]
- Spotted owl territories are concentrated at this interface, where old-growth forest meets younger stands and prey diversity is highest [Module 2A, Module 4: Zone 2]

**Lowland ↔ Riparian Interface:**
- Beaver engineering creates wetland habitat at this interface, raising water tables and expanding the riparian zone into adjacent lowland forest [Module 6: Network 4]
- Salmon-bear-forest nutrient transport operates at this interface — bears carry salmon carcasses 50-500 meters from streams into adjacent forest [Module 6: Network 2]
- River otters bridge aquatic and terrestrial habitats, depositing fish-derived nutrients at latrine sites [Module 4: Zone 5]

**Riparian ↔ Marine Interface:**
- Estuaries and river mouths are the critical transition zone where anadromous fish enter freshwater and where marine mammals (harbor seals, river otters, occasionally orcas) penetrate inland [Module 3, Module 4: Zone 6]
- Juvenile salmon depend on estuarine rearing habitat — habitat that has been reduced by >75% in the Puget Sound region through diking, filling, and development [Module 3]
- Gray whales feed in estuarine and shallow coastal waters during northward migration, connecting the PNW marine zone to the Arctic feeding grounds [Module 3]

**East-Side Steppe ↔ Riparian Interface:**
- The most dramatic habitat contrast in the PNW: arid shrub-steppe (6-12 inches annual precipitation) meets lush riparian corridors along the Columbia, Snake, and John Day rivers
- Mule deer and elk concentrate in riparian zones during summer, expanding into steppe during fall/winter
- Pygmy rabbits (Columbia Basin DPS, ESA Endangered) depend on deep-soil sagebrush habitat adjacent to agricultural conversion zones [Module 2B, Module 4: Zone 7]

### 4.2 Migration Corridors as Structural Necessities

The cross-zone species flows identified above depend on functional migration corridors — physical landscape connections that allow animals to move between seasonal ranges, between habitat patches, and between populations for genetic exchange.

**Critical corridor systems identified across the module series:**

| Corridor | Species Dependent | Threat | Module Refs |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cascade crest crossings (passes) | Elk, deer, wolf, cougar, marten, fisher | Highway mortality (I-90, US-2, SR-20), development | 1, 4, 5 |
| I-90 corridor (Snoqualmie Pass) | Multiple terrestrial species | Highway barrier; wildlife bridges under construction | 4 |
| North Cascades connectivity (US-Canada) | Grizzly bear, wolverine, lynx, wolf | International border fragmentation | 1, 4, 5 |
| Puget Sound shoreline | River otter, harbor seal, salmon | Armored shoreline, development | 3, 4 |
| Columbia River mainstem | Salmon (all species), river otter, beaver | Dams, development, water management | 2B, 6 |
| Olympic Peninsula connectivity | Fisher, marten, Roosevelt elk | Highway 101, timber harvest fragmentation | 1, 4 |
| Okanogan highlands | Caribou (historical), lynx, wolverine | Logging roads, fire suppression, development | 1, 5 |
| Sagebrush corridors (E. Oregon/WA) | Pygmy rabbit, pronghorn, mule deer | Agricultural conversion, energy development | 1, 2B, 4 |

The I-90 wildlife crossing project at Snoqualmie Pass represents the most ambitious corridor restoration effort in PNW history. When complete, it will provide over- and under-crossings for terrestrial mammals across the most heavily traveled mountain highway in Washington — reconnecting the North and South Cascades for gene flow and seasonal migration. The project embodies the connectivity narrative: species cannot be conserved in isolated habitat patches; they require functional landscape connectivity.

## Part V: Conservation Priority Synthesis

### 5.1 The Twelve Keystone Interactions

Synthesizing across all seven modules, twelve interactions emerge as structurally critical to the PNW mammalian system. Disruption of any one would cascade through multiple networks:

### 5.2 Species at the Intersection of Multiple Threats

The most conservation-urgent species are those facing multiple simultaneous threats with limited adaptive capacity. Cross-referencing vulnerability data from all modules:

**Southern Resident Killer Whale** (*Orcinus orca* — Southern Resident DPS)
- Threats: Chinook salmon decline, vessel disturbance, toxin bioaccumulation (PCBs), small population genetics
- Population: 73 (2024), below minimum viable population for long-term persistence
- Modules: 3, 6, 7
- Unique risk: This population cannot be supplemented from other orca populations because its ecotype (salmon specialist) is culturally transmitted and its acoustic dialect is unique. Loss of the Southern Residents would be the loss of a distinct culture, not just a population.

**Wolverine** (*Gulo gulo*)
- Threats: Snowpack decline (climate), habitat fragmentation, small population size, low reproductive rate
- Population: <300 estimated in WA/OR combined
- Modules: 1, 4, 5, 6
- Unique risk: Wolverines require persistent spring snowpack (April 15 snow cover) for natal denning. Climate models project 30-60% loss of suitable denning habitat by 2080 under moderate emissions scenarios.

**Pygmy Rabbit** (*Brachylagus idahoensis* — Columbia Basin DPS)
- Threats: Sagebrush habitat loss (agriculture, fire, invasive grasses), genetic isolation, small population
- Population: <100 wild individuals in Columbia Basin DPS (captive breeding supplementing)
- Modules: 2B, 4
- Unique risk: The most geographically isolated DPS of the most sagebrush-dependent mammal in North America, surrounded by converted agricultural land with no corridor to larger populations in the Great Basin.

**Fisher** (*Pekania pennanti*)
- Threats: Old-growth habitat loss, rodenticide exposure, vehicle mortality, low reproductive rate
- Population: Reintroduced Olympic (~90 released 2008-2010, breeding confirmed) and S. Cascades (~90 released 2015-2020)
- Modules: 1, 4, 5
- Unique risk: Fisher reintroduction is among the most ambitious mustelid recovery efforts globally. Success depends on old-growth structural features (large trees with cavities for denning) that take 200+ years to develop. Fisher recovery and old-growth preservation are inseparable.

## Part VI: The Module Series as a Portrait of a Living System

### 6.1 What the Modules Reveal Together

The eight modules of the "Fur, Fin & Fang" PNW mammalian taxonomy, taken together, reveal a bioregion whose mammalian fauna is:

**Ancient and young simultaneously.** The evolutionary lineages are ancient — carnivorans, rodents, and cetaceans have deep Cenozoic histories documented in the John Day and Hagerman fossil records [Module 5]. But the current assemblage of species in their current ranges is young — assembled within the last 10,000-15,000 years after the retreat of Pleistocene ice, and still in the process of reassembly as wolves, grizzly bears, fishers, and sea otters return after colonial-era extirpation.

**Connected across kingdoms.** The three-kingdom mutualism (fungi-mammals-conifers) and the salmon-mediated nutrient pathway demonstrate that PNW mammals cannot be understood in isolation from the plant, fungal, and fish communities with which they co-evolved. Mammalian ecology is ecosystem ecology.

**Shaped by human agency.** Indigenous fire management, harvest protocols, and relational frameworks shaped mammal habitat and populations for millennia [Module 7]. The fur trade and colonization reshaped them again. Modern management — ESA listing, reintroduction programs, co-management agreements — is reshaping them a third time. At no point in the last 15,000 years has the PNW mammalian system existed outside of human influence.

**Organized by connectivity.** The salmon node, the predator cascades, the mycorrhizal network, the beaver engineering system, and the seasonal migration corridors all demonstrate that the PNW mammalian system is organized by connections between species, between zones, and between the ocean and the mountaintop. Severing connections — damming rivers, fragmenting forests, removing predators, eliminating fire — degrades the system more than losing any individual species.

**Vulnerable at the extremes.** The most specialized species — alpine obligates, sagebrush obligates, old-growth obligates, salmon-diet obligates — are the most vulnerable to environmental change. The system's generalists (coyote, raccoon, deer mouse, black-tailed deer) are expanding while its specialists contract. The long-term trajectory, absent intervention, is toward a simplified mammalian community dominated by generalists — a community that would maintain basic trophic structure but would lose the specialized interactions (truffle dispersal, trophic cascades, ecosystem engineering, nutrient transport) that define the PNW's ecological distinctiveness.

### 6.2 The Central Metaphor: The Bioregion as Organism

The module series, read as a whole, supports a conceptual framework in which the PNW bioregion functions not as a collection of ecosystems but as a single integrated system with organism-like properties:

- **Circulatory system:** Salmon runs and river networks transport nutrients from ocean to interior, analogous to blood circulation. Beaver dams regulate flow, analogous to valves.
- **Nervous system:** Predator-prey interactions transmit behavioral signals (the "landscape of fear") across the landscape, regulating herbivore behavior at speeds faster than population-level responses.
- **Immune system:** Predator-mesopredator regulation controls invasive and overabundant species. Wolf reintroduction functions as restoring immune capacity.
- **Skeletal system:** The physical geography — mountain ranges, river corridors, coastline — provides the structural framework on which all biological interactions are organized.
- **Memory:** The genetic diversity within species (phylogeographic structure from glacial refugia [Module 5]), the Indigenous knowledge systems transmitted across generations [Module 7], and the fossil record [Module 5] all function as forms of ecological memory — information from the past that constrains and informs the present.

This is a metaphor, not a claim of literal organismal status. But it captures a truth that the module series documents comprehensively: the PNW mammalian system has emergent properties — resilience, self-regulation, nutrient cycling, disturbance recovery — that are properties of the whole, not reducible to the properties of individual species. Conservation of PNW mammals therefore requires conservation of the system: its connections, its processes, its flows, and its memory.
