## Jurisdictional Framework

Marine mammal management in the United States is divided between two federal agencies under the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) of 1972:

- **NOAA Fisheries (NMFS):** Jurisdiction over all cetaceans (whales, dolphins, porpoises) and all pinnipeds except walrus. Administers ESA listings for marine mammals under its authority. [G1]
- **US Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS):** Jurisdiction over sea otters, walrus, polar bears, and manatees. The sea otter is the only USFWS-jurisdiction marine mammal in the PNW. [G5]

Both agencies enforce the MMPA's core prohibition: it is illegal to "take" (harass, hunt, capture, or kill) any marine mammal in US waters without authorization. The ESA provides additional protections for species listed as Threatened or Endangered. In Washington State, WDFW coordinates with federal agencies on marine mammal monitoring and stranding response. [G9]

**International dimension:** The Salish Sea spans the US-Canada border. Canada's Species at Risk Act (SARA) and the Fisheries Act govern marine mammal protections in British Columbia. DFO (Fisheries and Oceans Canada) is the Canadian counterpart to NOAA Fisheries. Many marine mammal populations — including Southern Resident killer whales, humpback whales, and gray whales — move freely across the border, requiring binational coordination for effective conservation. [G1, G14]

## Part I: Order Cetacea — Whales, Dolphins, and Porpoises

The PNW continental shelf and inland waters host at least 28 cetacean species, from the 100-foot blue whale to the 5-foot harbor porpoise. Eight species of baleen whales (suborder Mysticeti) have been documented off the Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia coast, along with 20 species of toothed whales (suborder Odontoceti). Some are year-round residents of the Salish Sea; others pass through on annual migrations spanning hemispheres; still others appear only as rare vagrants from warmer or deeper waters.

The recovery of many cetacean populations since the end of commercial whaling (IWC moratorium 1986) is one of the great conservation successes of the twentieth century. Humpback whales, gray whales, and Bigg's killer whales have all increased substantially in PNW waters. But this recovery is unevenly distributed — Southern Resident killer whales, dependent on declining Chinook salmon, continue to decline even as their marine-mammal-eating Bigg's counterparts flourish in the same waters. That asymmetry makes the PNW a natural laboratory for understanding how ecological specialization determines vulnerability. [G1, G4, P6]

### 1.1 Killer Whale / Orca (*Orcinus orca*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Odontoceti > Delphinidae
**Status:** Resident (year-round and seasonal, multiple ecotypes)
**Conservation Status:**
- Southern Resident DPS: Federal ESA Endangered (2005), MMPA Depleted, IUCN Data Deficient (species complex under review), WA State Endangered
- Bigg's (Transient): Not ESA-listed, MMPA strategic stock
- Offshore: Not ESA-listed

The killer whale is the apex predator of the world's oceans and the most ecologically, culturally, and politically significant marine mammal in the Pacific Northwest. Three distinct ecotypes inhabit PNW waters — residents, Bigg's (transients), and offshores — each so different in diet, behavior, social structure, genetics, and morphology that NOAA researchers proposed in 2024 that they represent separate species. The Society for Marine Mammalogy's Committee on Taxonomy is provisionally evaluating the proposed reclassification: resident killer whales as *Orcinus orca ater* and Bigg's killer whales as *Orcinus orca rectipinnus*, pending comprehensive review. [G2, G3, O5]

These three ecotypes have shared the same waters for at least 200,000 years without interbreeding — a period of reproductive isolation longer than the divergence between some recognized whale species. They do not share prey, do not socialize across ecotype boundaries, and produce different vocal dialects that are mutually unintelligible. That three populations of the same apex predator can partition a single marine ecosystem so completely, for so long, is one of the most remarkable examples of sympatric ecological divergence in any mammal. [P6, G3]

#### 1.1.1 Southern Resident Killer Whales (SRKW)

**Population:** 73 individuals (July 2024 census, Center for Whale Research) [O1]
**Historical peak:** 98 individuals (1995) [O1]
**ESA listing:** Endangered (2005), designated as the "Southern Resident" Distinct Population Segment
**MMPA status:** Depleted
**Critical habitat:** Designated 2006 (inland waters of WA), expanded 2021 (outer coast from WA to CA)

**Description:** Large delphinid — males reach 20-26 ft and 8,000-12,000 lbs; females 16-23 ft and 3,000-8,000 lbs. Black dorsal surface, white ventral patch, gray "saddle patch" behind dorsal fin. Male dorsal fin grows to 6 ft tall and upright; female dorsal fin reaches 3 ft and is slightly falcate. Each individual is identifiable by unique saddle patch pigmentation and dorsal fin shape — the basis of the Center for Whale Research photo-ID catalog maintained since 1976. [O1, P6]

**Social Structure — The Matriline:**
Southern Residents live in one of the most stable social systems known in any mammal. The fundamental unit is the matriline: a mother and her descendants of both sexes. Sons never leave their mother's matriline — they remain with her for life, a trait shared with only a few other species (pilot whales, some elephant populations). Daughters may form sub-matrilines but typically remain in close association with their mother's group.

Matrilines associate in pods — stable multi-matriline groups that travel, forage, and rest together. The three SRKW pods are: [O1, P6]

**J Pod (24 members, 2024):**
The most frequently observed pod in the Salish Sea. J Pod uses inland waters (Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands) more consistently than K or L pod, particularly from October through May. J Pod's matriarch through 2016 was J2 "Granny," estimated at 105 years old at death — one of the oldest known orcas. Current eldest members carry decades of ecological knowledge about foraging locations, salmon run timing, and navigation routes that cannot be replaced if lost. J Pod's genealogy has been continuously documented since the mid-1970s, making it one of the longest-studied cetacean populations on Earth.

**K Pod (17 members, 2024):**
K Pod is the smallest pod and spends less time in inland waters than J Pod. K and L pods often travel together along the outer coast from fall through spring, returning to the Salish Sea primarily in summer when Fraser River Chinook runs peak. K Pod's smaller size makes it particularly vulnerable to demographic stochasticity — the loss of even one reproductive female significantly impacts the pod's viability.

**L Pod (32 members, 2024):**
The largest pod, L Pod ranges most widely along the outer coast from central California to Southeast Alaska. L Pod's oceanic movements are less well-documented than its inland water use because vessel-based surveys are constrained by weather and distance. L Pod contained the captive orca Tokitae (Lolita/Sk'aliCh'elh-tenaut), captured from Penn Cove in 1970 and held at Miami Seaquarium until her death in August 2023. The Lummi Nation was instrumental in the decades-long campaign to return Tokitae to Salish Sea waters.

**Diet — Chinook Dependency:**
Southern Residents are fish-eating specialists. Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*) constitutes approximately 80% of identified prey from May through September, with the whales preferentially targeting the largest, oldest fish (aged 3-5 years) for maximum caloric value. In winter, they supplement with chum salmon, steelhead, and other salmonids, but Chinook remains the foundation of their annual energy budget. The whales follow salmon runs — Fraser River Chinook in summer, Columbia River and Puget Sound stocks in other seasons — making their movements a living map of salmon distribution across the PNW coast.

This dietary linkage creates a conservation dependency connecting virtually every freshwater habitat decision in the PNW to the ocean. Dam removal, hatchery reform, water allocation, and urban stormwater management all ultimately affect how many Chinook are available to Southern Residents. No other marine mammal conservation challenge in North America is so deeply entangled with terrestrial land use. [G1, G4, G13]

**Three Primary Threats:**

**1. Prey availability (declining Chinook salmon):**
The single most important factor in SRKW decline. NOAA identifies reduced prey as the leading threat. Chinook salmon abundance in SRKW range has declined by an estimated 60% from historical levels due to hydropower dams, habitat degradation, hatchery competition, harvest pressure, and ocean condition changes. The whales need an estimated 385,000 Chinook annually to sustain their population — current prey availability frequently falls below this threshold. Nutritional stress reduces reproductive success, increases calf mortality, and forces the whales to expend more energy searching for food. Years with poor Chinook returns correlate directly with increased SRKW mortality and decreased birth rates. [G1, G4]

**2. Contaminant accumulation (PCBs, PBDEs, emerging pollutants):**
Southern Residents are among the most contaminated marine mammals in the world. PCBs (banned 1979 but persistent in sediments) and PBDEs (flame retardants) bioaccumulate through the food web and concentrate in blubber. During nutritional stress, blubber metabolism releases these contaminants into the bloodstream, causing immune suppression, endocrine disruption, and reproductive failure. Mothers transfer contaminants to calves through lipid-rich milk — first-born calves receive the highest loads. PCB levels in some individuals exceed reproductive/immune impairment thresholds by 10-100 times. The synergy between nutritional stress (forcing blubber metabolism) and high contaminant loads creates a compound threat that neither factor alone would produce. [G4, G1]

**3. Vessel noise and disturbance:**
The Salish Sea's heavy vessel traffic — commercial shipping, ferries, recreational boats, whale-watching — creates chronic acoustic interference with SRKW echolocation and communication. The whales hunt using echolocation to locate individual salmon in turbid water. Vessels within 400 meters reduce foraging activity by approximately 25%; within 1,600 feet (500 yards), foraging drops by up to 50%. The whales compensate by increasing call amplitude ("the Lombard effect") and shifting frequencies, but these compensations are energetically costly. Vessel disturbance amplifies the prey availability threat: even when Chinook are present, noise may prevent the whales from catching them. [G1, G14]

**Reproductive Crisis:**
Recent research estimates that 69% of detectable SRKW pregnancies are unsuccessful — ending in miscarriage or perinatal calf death. This reflects the combined effects of nutritional stress, contaminant mobilization during pregnancy and lactation, and inbreeding in a small, closed population. The population's low genetic diversity — legacy of live-capture removals in the 1960s-1970s when at least 45 individuals were taken for marine parks — limits adaptive capacity.

The demographic math: 73 individuals, approximately 28-30 reproductive-age females, 69% pregnancy failure rate, and mean calving interval of 5-6 years yields only 2-3 surviving calves per year in good years. In poor Chinook years, zero surviving calves. A population that cannot produce more calves than it loses adults is in demographic decline regardless of other actions. [O1, G1]

**Inbreeding:**
The SRKW bottleneck — from 200+ pre-exploitation to approximately 71 in the early 1970s, partial recovery to 98 in 1995, then decline — has reduced genetic diversity below long-term viability thresholds. Effective population size is estimated at 25-30. Several individuals show inbreeding depression: skeletal deformities, reduced immune function, and lower reproductive success in offspring of closely related parents. Virtually all remaining mating combinations involve some degree of relatedness. [O1, G1]

**2024 Taxonomic Revision Proposal:**
NOAA researchers proposed in 2024 that the three PNW ecotypes represent distinct species or subspecies diverged over 200,000 years ago: residents as *Orcinus orca ater*, Bigg's as *Orcinus orca rectipinnus*. The Society for Marine Mammalogy's Committee on Taxonomy is reviewing. Evidence: fixed genetic differences, morphological divergence, ecological specialization, acoustic divergence, and complete reproductive isolation despite sympatry. If accepted, each species would require separate stock assessment, critical habitat, and recovery plan. [G2, G3, O5]

**Indigenous Relationship — Lummi Nation:**
The Lummi Nation (Lhaq'temish) regards killer whales as relatives — not metaphorically, but as reincarnated ancestors and kin. In Lummi cosmology, orcas are the spirits of deceased chiefs who have taken whale form to continue protecting their people and the waters they share. This relationship imposes obligations: the Lummi are responsible for the well-being of the orcas as they would be for any family member. This understanding is not a "belief" in the Western religious sense — it is a relational framework grounded in thousands of years of coexistence and observation.

The Lummi Nation was the driving force behind the campaign to return Tokitae (also known as Lolita, Lummi name Sk'aliCh'elh-tenaut) from the Miami Seaquarium to Salish Sea waters. Tokitae, a member of L Pod, was captured during the Penn Cove roundup of August 8, 1970, in which at least 80 Southern Residents were herded into the cove and 7 were taken for marine parks. Five whales died during the capture. Tokitae was the last surviving captive Southern Resident when she died on August 18, 2023, after 53 years in captivity. The Lummi Nation's advocacy for Tokitae's return was an expression of kinship obligation, not animal-rights activism — a distinction that matters for understanding Indigenous marine conservation ethics. [O6-C6, O1]

#### 1.1.2 Bigg's (Transient) Killer Whales

**Population:** 350+ individuals in the "West Coast Transient" community (estimated 2023), increasing steadily over 40+ years [G1]
**ESA status:** Not listed
**MMPA status:** Strategic stock (eastern North Pacific transient)

**Description:** Morphologically similar to residents but with subtle differences detectable by experienced observers: Bigg's orcas tend to have more pointed dorsal fins, larger and more uniformly shaped saddle patches, and slightly larger overall body size. Males reach 22-27 ft; females 18-24 ft. Named in honor of Canadian cetologist Michael Bigg (1939-1990), who pioneered photo-ID methodology and first recognized the distinction between resident and transient populations. [P6]

**Diet — Marine Mammal Specialists:**
Bigg's killer whales eat marine mammals exclusively: harbor seals, harbor porpoises, Steller sea lions, California sea lions, Dall's porpoises, minke whales, gray whale calves, and occasionally river otters and sea otters. They never eat fish. Their hunting is characterized by stealth — unlike the highly vocal residents, Bigg's groups travel in near-silence to avoid alerting acoustically sensitive prey. They vocalize only after a kill, apparently as a social signal.

Hunting strategies vary by prey type. Harbor seal hunts are typically brief, violent affairs — a group of 2-5 whales detects a seal at the surface or on a rock, accelerates to full speed, and strikes. Steller sea lion hunts can be prolonged pursuits involving coordinated group tactics. Gray whale calf hunts — documented primarily during the spring northward migration — may last hours, with multiple Bigg's groups working together to separate a calf from its mother and drown it through repeated ramming and submersion. [P6, G1]

**Social Structure:**
Fundamentally different from residents. Bigg's groups are smaller (typically 2-6 individuals) and more fluid in composition. While matrilines exist, adult males frequently disperse and travel alone or in shifting associations. This social flexibility appears to be an adaptation to mammal-hunting ecology: smaller groups can hunt more stealthily, and prey encounters may be too unpredictable to support the large, stable groups that residents maintain on salmon runs. [P6]

**Population Trajectory — A Conservation Success:**
Bigg's killer whales have increased steadily for over four decades, roughly doubling their population since the early 1990s. This growth is driven by the post-MMPA recovery of pinniped populations — harbor seals, California sea lions, and Steller sea lions have all increased dramatically since the 1972 hunting ban, providing an expanding prey base for Bigg's whales. The contrast with Southern Residents is instructive: Bigg's feed on populations that are increasing (pinnipeds protected by MMPA), while Residents feed on populations that are declining (Chinook salmon impacted by dams, habitat loss, and climate change). The same management regime that protected marine mammals from hunting also indirectly benefited the predator of those marine mammals — a cascading conservation success. [G1]

**Ecological Role:**
Bigg's killer whales function as the top-down regulator of pinniped and small cetacean populations in the Salish Sea. As their numbers have increased, some researchers hypothesize they are contributing to declines in harbor seal numbers in certain areas of the San Juan Islands and southern Gulf Islands — a natural predator-prey dynamic that had been absent during the decades when Bigg's populations were depressed. The return of apex predation to the Salish Sea marine food web is a restoration of ecological function, not a problem to be managed. [G1, P6]

**Key Sources:** Ford, Ellis & Balcomb [P6]; NOAA Fisheries stock assessments [G1]; NOAA 2024 ecotype study [G2, G3]

#### 1.1.3 Offshore Killer Whales

**Population:** ~300 individuals in the eastern North Pacific (estimated), poorly studied [G1]
**ESA status:** Not listed
**MMPA status:** Strategic stock

**Description:** The least-studied of the three PNW ecotypes. Offshores are typically smaller than residents or Bigg's, with rounded dorsal fin tips that show distinctive wear patterns — likely from abrasion against shark skin (dermal denticles). Females average 17-20 ft; males 20-23 ft. Tooth wear is extreme, often worn to the gumline, suggesting a diet of abrasive-skinned prey. [P6, G3]

**Diet:** Offshores are believed to feed primarily on sharks, including Pacific sleeper sharks, blue sharks, and possibly salmon sharks. Confirmed prey includes Pacific sleeper shark and broadnose sevengill shark. Their preference for shark prey explains both the tooth wear (shark skin is covered in tooth-like dermal denticles) and the rounded dorsal fin tips. The diet may also include other large fish. Offshores are rarely encountered near shore — most observations come from deep continental shelf and slope waters, 15-50+ miles offshore. [G3, P6]

**Social Structure:** Offshores travel in large groups (25-75+ individuals), suggesting a social system different from both the tight matrilines of residents and the small fluid groups of Bigg's. Their group size may be related to the patchy distribution of shark prey on the continental shelf. [P6]

**Research Gaps:** Offshore killer whales are encountered so infrequently that basic demographic parameters — birth rate, mortality rate, age structure — are poorly known. Their deep-water habitat makes visual surveys difficult, and their acoustic repertoire is less well-characterized than residents' or Bigg's. The 2024 NOAA study included offshore genetic samples in its analysis but the proposed taxonomic revision focused primarily on the resident/Bigg's split. [G3]

### 1.2 Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenopteridae
**Status:** Seasonal (April-November), some year-round presence
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Mexico DPS Threatened, Central America DPS Endangered; WA State Endangered (legacy listing); IUCN: Least Concern (species-wide, following recovery)
**Population in PNW:** 396 individuals catalogued in the Salish Sea by Cascadia Research Collective (2022); outer coast populations substantially larger

**Description:** Large baleen whale — adults 40-52 ft, 25-40 tons. Distinguished by extremely long pectoral flippers (up to one-third body length, the longest of any cetacean), knobby protuberances (tubercles) on head and lower jaw, and a small dorsal fin set on a pronounced hump. Ventral surface of the tail flukes has unique black-and-white pigmentation patterns — used for photo-ID, analogous to fingerprints. Tail fluke catalogs maintained by Cascadia Research Collective, Happywhale, and NOAA contain thousands of identified PNW individuals. [G1, G14]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Virtually absent from PNW inland waters for most of the twentieth century due to commercial whaling (95% population reduction before the 1966 ban). Return to the Salish Sea began in the late 1990s; by 2022, 396 individuals catalogued. NOAA down-listed the Hawaii DPS in 2016; the Mexico DPS (including many PNW animals) remains Threatened, Central America DPS Endangered. Common on the outer coast April-November, with some year-round presence near productive upwelling zones. [G1, G14]

**Feeding Ecology:**
Feed on krill (*Euphausia pacifica*, *Thysanoessa spinifera*) and small schooling fish (herring, anchovy, sand lance). Strategies include lunge feeding, bubble-net feeding (culturally transmitted — calves learn from mothers, populations use distinct configurations), and surface skimming. [G1]

**Threats:**
Vessel strikes (leading mortality cause) and entanglement in fishing gear (crab pot lines, gillnets) — both density-dependent threats increasing as the recovering population expands into the busy Salish Sea shipping corridor. [G1, G14]

### 1.3 Gray Whale (*Eschrichtius robustus*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Eschrichtiidae (sole living member of family)
**Status:** Migratory (annual), with some year-round "resident" individuals
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed (Eastern North Pacific stock delisted 1994); Western North Pacific stock Endangered; WA State Sensitive; IUCN: Least Concern (eastern stock)
**Population:** ~27,000 (Eastern North Pacific stock, 2023 NOAA survey), down from peak of ~27,000 in 2016 following a significant Unusual Mortality Event (UME) 2019-2023

**Description:** Medium-large baleen whale — adults 40-49 ft, 30-40 tons. Mottled gray coloration with extensive scarring from barnacles and whale lice (cyamids). No dorsal fin — instead, a series of 6-12 dorsal bumps ("knuckles") along the rear third of the back. Head profile distinctly arched, with paired blowholes producing a characteristic V-shaped or heart-shaped blow visible at distance. Heavily encrusted with barnacles (*Cryptolepas rhachianecti*, found only on gray whales) and whale lice (*Cyamus scammoni*, *C. ceti*, *C. kessleri*). [G1]

**Migration — The Longest Mammalian Migration:**
Eastern North Pacific gray whales undertake the longest annual migration of any mammal: approximately 10,000-12,000 miles round trip between summer feeding grounds in the Bering and Chukchi Seas (Arctic) and winter breeding/calving lagoons in Baja California, Mexico. The northbound migration passes the PNW coast from February through May, with mothers and calves trailing the main migration by several weeks. The southbound migration occurs from October through January. During migration, gray whales travel close to shore — often within 1-2 miles — making them visible from coastal headlands along the entire Oregon and Washington coast. [G1]

**"Resident" Gray Whales — The Pacific Coast Feeding Group (PCFG):**
A subset of approximately 200-250 gray whales does not complete the full migration. These animals — designated the Pacific Coast Feeding Group — spend the summer and fall feeding along the coast from northern California to southeastern Alaska, rather than continuing to Arctic feeding grounds. Some PCFG individuals are observed year after year in the same bays and estuaries, including Padilla Bay, Whidbey Island nearshore, Depoe Bay (Oregon), and several Vancouver Island sites. PCFG whales have been individually identified through photo-ID and show strong site fidelity. Whether PCFG whales represent a genetically distinct subpopulation or a behaviorally flexible subset of the larger stock remains an active research question. [G1]

**Feeding Ecology:**
Gray whales are the only baleen whale that feeds primarily on benthic (bottom-dwelling) organisms. They roll on their sides in shallow water, suck mouthfuls of sediment from the seafloor, and filter out amphipods, isopods, mysid shrimp, tube worms, and other benthic invertebrates through their baleen plates. This benthic feeding creates distinctive "feeding pits" on the seafloor, visible in side-scan sonar surveys — depressions 2-8 ft wide that are colonized by opportunistic species after the whale moves on. Gray whale benthic feeding bioturbation is an ecosystem engineering process that increases habitat heterogeneity and nutrient cycling on soft-bottom continental shelves. PCFG whales in the PNW also feed on mysid swarms and epibenthic amphipods in shallower coastal waters. [G1]

**Unusual Mortality Event (2019-2023):**
Beginning in 2019, gray whales experienced a significant die-off along the North American coast. NOAA declared an Unusual Mortality Event after hundreds of emaciated gray whales were found dead or stranded. The UME was associated with reduced prey availability in Arctic feeding grounds, potentially linked to changing sea ice conditions and warming ocean temperatures affecting benthic amphipod communities in the Bering and Chukchi Seas. The population declined from approximately 27,000 to an estimated 14,500 before the UME subsided. By 2023, the population showed signs of stabilization, though it remains below pre-UME levels. The UME demonstrated how dependent gray whales are on Arctic benthic productivity — and how vulnerable that productivity is to climate-driven ocean changes. [G1]

**Indigenous Relationship — Makah Nation:**
The Makah have hunted gray whales for at least 3,800 years (Ozette archaeological evidence). Whaling required years of spiritual preparation, specialized seagoing canoes (36 ft, single western red cedar), and coordinated crew effort with detachable harpoon heads and sealskin floats. The Treaty of Neah Bay (1855) is the only treaty explicitly reserving a tribal whale-hunting right. The Makah voluntarily ceased in the 1920s when commercial whaling depleted stocks, and resumed a treaty hunt in 1999. [O8-C8, O7-C7]

**Nuu-chah-nulth Whaling Tradition:**
The Nuu-chah-nulth peoples of western Vancouver Island were open-ocean whalers — among the most technically demanding maritime hunting traditions in human history, requiring navigation in open swells, precision harpoon strikes from a moving canoe, and crew coordination in dangerous seas. The whaler's preparation involved ritual bathing, prayer, fasting, and physical training. Nuu-chah-nulth ecological knowledge of whale behavior reflects millennia of observation passed through oral tradition. [O7-C7]

**Key Sources:** NOAA Fisheries gray whale stock assessments [G1]; NOAA UME investigation reports [G1]; Makah Cultural and Research Center [O8-C8]; Stewart *Indian Fishing* [O7-C7]

### 1.4 Minke Whale (*Balaenoptera acutorostrata*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenopteridae
**Status:** Resident/seasonal, year-round in Salish Sea
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed
**Population:** Unknown for PNW inland waters; North Pacific stock estimated at 25,000+

**Description:** Smallest PNW baleen whale — adults 23-33 ft, 6-10 tons. Dark gray to black dorsal surface, white ventral surface, with distinctive white band on each pectoral flipper (diagnostic in the field). Small, pointed head with single prominent median ridge on rostrum. Tall, sickle-shaped dorsal fin set far back on body. Inconspicuous blow — rarely visible, making minke whales difficult to detect at distance. Fast swimmers, capable of sustained speeds of 12-16 knots. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Minke whales are year-round residents of the Salish Sea and are regularly observed in the San Juan Islands, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and along the outer coast. They are solitary or found in pairs — the most commonly encountered baleen whale in PNW inland waters, though far less conspicuous than humpbacks. Individual minke whales show strong site fidelity to specific foraging areas within the San Juan Islands, returning to the same locations across years. Their small size and inconspicuous blow make them easy to overlook — the PNW population may be substantially larger than casual observation suggests. [G1, G14]

**Feeding Ecology:**
Minke whales in the PNW feed on small schooling fish (Pacific herring, sand lance, juvenile salmonids) and euphausiids. They are lunge feeders, accelerating through prey patches from below and engulfing large volumes of water and prey. In the San Juan Islands, minke whales often feed near current lines where tidal flows concentrate prey — the same hydrographic features that attract harbor porpoises and diving seabirds. [G1]

**Predation:**
Minke whales are prey for Bigg's killer whales. Attacks on minke whales by Bigg's groups have been documented in the San Juan Islands and Strait of Georgia. These hunts can be prolonged — Bigg's groups pursue the minke whale, which is faster than their typical pinniped prey, and must work cooperatively to tire and eventually drown it. [P6]

### 1.5 Fin Whale (*Balaenoptera physalus*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenopteridae
**Status:** Seasonal, primarily outer coast
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Endangered (Northeast Pacific DPS proposed for reclassification, 2024); IUCN: Vulnerable; WA State: Endangered

**Description:** Second-largest animal on Earth — adults 60-80 ft, 40-80 tons. Sleek, streamlined body with a prominent dorsal fin set far back. Unique asymmetric coloration: the right lower jaw is white, the left lower jaw is dark gray — the only bilaterally asymmetric coloration in any cetacean. The reason for this asymmetry may be related to feeding behavior (the whale circles prey clockwise, presenting the white right side, which may startle or concentrate schooling fish). Blow is tall (15-20 ft) and columnar. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Fin whales are primarily outer coast and offshore species in the PNW, uncommon in the Salish Sea. They are observed along the continental shelf edge from spring through fall, particularly in areas of high productivity associated with upwelling. Fin whales were heavily targeted by commercial whaling in the North Pacific — tens of thousands were killed in the twentieth century. Recovery has been slow but detectable, with increasing sightings along the Washington and Oregon coast in recent decades. Acoustic monitoring (passive hydrophone arrays) has documented fin whale vocalizations year-round on the PNW outer coast, suggesting some individuals may not fully migrate. [G1, G14]

**Feeding Ecology:** Fin whales feed on krill and small schooling fish (herring, capelin, sand lance). Their massive size — requiring an estimated 2,000-2,500 lbs of food per day during the feeding season — means they concentrate in areas of exceptionally high prey density. [G1]

---

### 1.6 Blue Whale (*Balaenoptera musculus*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenopteridae
**Status:** Rare, outer coast migrant
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Endangered; IUCN: Endangered; WA State: Endangered

**Description:** Largest animal ever known to have lived on Earth — adults 70-100 ft, 100-200 tons. Blue-gray mottled coloration, relatively small dorsal fin, broad flat U-shaped head. Blow is tall (30+ ft) and columnar. Heart the size of a small car; aorta large enough for a human to crawl through. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Blue whales are rare in PNW waters but not absent. The Eastern North Pacific population (estimated 1,600-2,000 individuals) feeds along the California Current from southern California to British Columbia during summer and fall. Individuals are occasionally documented off the Washington and Oregon coast, particularly during warm-water years when prey distributions shift northward. Acoustic detections of blue whale calls (the lowest-frequency biological sound, audible to hydrophones at hundreds of miles) are regularly recorded on the outer coast. Blue whales were reduced to an estimated 1% of pre-whaling population by commercial hunting before the 1966 ban. Recovery has been slow — their enormous body size means slow reproduction (one calf every 2-3 years) and long generation times (30+ years). [G1]

---

### 1.7 Sei Whale (*Balaenoptera borealis*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenopteridae
**Status:** Rare, outer coast
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Endangered; IUCN: Endangered; WA State: Endangered

**Description:** Large baleen whale — adults 40-60 ft, 20-30 tons. Similar in appearance to fin whale but smaller, with more uniformly dark coloration and a more prominent sickle-shaped dorsal fin. Distinguished from fin whale by lack of asymmetric jaw coloration and by a single prominent median ridge on the rostrum (fin whales also have a single ridge, but the overall head shape differs). [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:** Sei whales are among the least-observed large whales in PNW waters. They are pelagic, preferring deep water far from shore, and their occurrence in the PNW is sporadic. Pre-whaling populations in the North Pacific were reduced by an estimated 80% by commercial hunting. Occasional sightings occur off the continental shelf edge during summer. [G1]

---

### 1.8 North Pacific Right Whale (*Eubalaena japonica*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Mysticeti > Balaenidae
**Status:** Extremely rare vagrant
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Endangered; IUCN: Endangered; WA State: Endangered
**Population:** Eastern North Pacific population estimated at fewer than 30 individuals — among the most endangered large whales on Earth

**Description:** Large, robust baleen whale — adults 45-55 ft, 60-80 tons. Black body, no dorsal fin, distinctive callosities (roughened skin patches colonized by whale lice) on the head in a pattern unique to each individual. Broad, highly arched upper jaw supporting extremely long baleen plates (up to 9 ft). V-shaped blow from widely separated blowholes. Named by whalers as the "right" whale to kill — slow, buoyant (floats when dead), close to shore, and yielding enormous quantities of oil and baleen. [G1]

**PNW Record:**
One North Pacific right whale was verified in the Strait of Juan de Fuca in 2013 — the first confirmed record in PNW inland waters in the post-whaling era, and one of very few records anywhere in the eastern North Pacific south of Alaska in the past century. The sighting underscored both how severely this species was depleted by commercial whaling and how little is known about its current distribution. The Eastern North Pacific population was estimated at 10,000-20,000 before commercial whaling began in the mid-1800s; fewer than 30 are believed to survive. Recovery prospects are considered extremely poor due to the tiny population size, potential Allee effects (difficulty finding mates in a vast ocean), and ongoing threats from vessel strikes and entanglement. [G1]

### 1.9 Harbor Porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Odontoceti > Phocoenidae
**Status:** Resident, year-round
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; MMPA: Not strategic; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Candidate (monitoring status)

**Description:** Smallest cetacean in PNW waters — adults 5-6 ft, 110-170 lbs. Stocky body, small triangular dorsal fin, rounded head without a beak. Dark gray to black dorsal surface, lighter gray flanks, white ventral surface. Inconspicuous at the surface — low, indistinct blow, rolls quietly, rarely breaches. Named for its habit of frequenting harbors and nearshore waters. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
The most common small cetacean in PNW waters. Harbor porpoises are year-round residents of the Salish Sea, Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the outer coast from California to Alaska. They occupy shallow nearshore waters, bays, estuaries, and tidal channels — typically in water less than 200 ft deep. After a period of apparent decline or redistribution in the 1970s-1990s, harbor porpoises have increased in Puget Sound and are now regularly observed in areas where they were formerly rare, including the southern reaches of Puget Sound near Tacoma and Olympia.

WDFW aerial surveys estimate several thousand harbor porpoises in Washington inland waters alone, with additional populations along the outer coast. Their small size, inconspicuous behavior, and preference for nearshore waters make them difficult to census accurately — population estimates carry substantial uncertainty. [G9, G1]

**Feeding Ecology:**
Harbor porpoises feed on small fish — Pacific herring, sand lance, anchovy, smelt, and juvenile salmonids. They are pursuit predators, chasing individual fish rather than lunge-feeding through schools. Their small body size and high metabolic rate (characteristic of small cetaceans in cold water) require frequent feeding — harbor porpoises may consume 5-10% of their body weight daily. [G1]

**Predation:**
Harbor porpoises are a primary prey item for Bigg's killer whales in the Salish Sea. Bigg's groups are frequently observed hunting harbor porpoises in the San Juan Islands, Gulf Islands, and Strait of Georgia. Harbor porpoise kills by Bigg's orcas are observed more frequently than any other prey type in inland waters, though harbor seals likely constitute a larger proportion of total biomass consumed. [P6]

**Conservation Note:**
Harbor porpoises are highly sensitive to acoustic disturbance. Pile-driving noise, sonar, and vessel traffic can displace porpoises from foraging habitat. Bycatch in gillnet fisheries was historically a significant mortality source, reduced by gear modifications and fishery management changes. [G1]

### 1.10 Dall's Porpoise (*Phocoenoides dalli*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Odontoceti > Phocoenidae
**Status:** Resident/seasonal
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed

**Description:** Robust, muscular porpoise — adults 6-7.5 ft, 270-490 lbs. Striking black-and-white coloration: black body with a large white flank patch extending from mid-body to the tail, and white-tipped dorsal fin and tail flukes. The most visually distinctive small cetacean in PNW waters. Extremely fast swimmers — capable of sustained speeds exceeding 30 knots, making them the fastest small cetacean. They produce a distinctive "rooster tail" spray when swimming at speed, created by their stocky body pushing water at the surface. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Dall's porpoises are found throughout PNW waters, from the outer coast to the deeper channels of the Salish Sea. They prefer cooler, deeper water than harbor porpoises and are more common in the Strait of Juan de Fuca, Haro Strait, and the deeper channels of the San Juan Islands than in shallow Puget Sound. They are year-round residents but may move between outer coast and inland waters seasonally. Their bold, fast-swimming behavior — often approaching boats to bow-ride — makes them conspicuous despite their relatively small numbers. [G1, G14]

**Feeding Ecology:** Dall's porpoises feed on small fish and squid, often at depth. They are active nocturnal feeders, diving to 300-500 ft to pursue prey associated with the deep scattering layer — the daily vertical migration of plankton and small organisms that drives much of the ocean's trophic economy. [G1]

### 1.11 Pacific White-Sided Dolphin (*Lagenorhynchus obliquidens*)

**Taxonomy:** Cetacea > Odontoceti > Delphinidae
**Status:** Seasonal to occasional
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed

**Description:** Medium-sized dolphin — adults 5.5-8 ft, 150-300 lbs. Complex tricolor pattern: dark gray dorsal cape, light gray thoracic patch, white ventral surface, with a distinctive pale gray "suspender" stripe running from the forehead along the flank. Prominent falcate dorsal fin, bicolored (dark leading edge, light trailing edge). Highly acrobatic — frequently breaches, somersaults, and rides bow waves. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Pacific white-sided dolphins are primarily an outer coast and offshore species in the PNW, found along the continental shelf and slope. They occur in large groups (50-500+), often in association with other cetacean species (humpback whales, Dall's porpoises) in areas of high prey density. They occasionally enter the Strait of Juan de Fuca and northern Salish Sea, particularly during cooler water periods. In British Columbia's Broughton Archipelago and Johnstone Strait, they are more common, with large groups regularly observed in protected waters. [G1, G14]

**Feeding Ecology:** Feed on squid, herring, anchovy, and lanternfish. Their offshore distribution and deep-diving capability (to 600+ ft) allow them to exploit prey resources unavailable to the smaller, shallower-diving porpoises. [G1]

### 1.12-1.17 Additional Profiled Cetaceans

**Common Bottlenose Dolphin** (*Tursiops truncatus*) — Delphinidae. Rare warm-water vagrant, occasionally off southern Oregon coast; not part of regular PNW fauna. Range extends northward during El Nino events. Adults 8-12 ft, 400-1,100 lbs. Not ESA-listed. [G1]

**Risso's Dolphin** (*Grampus griseus*) — Delphinidae. Rare offshore species found along the continental shelf edge in deep water (3,000-6,000 ft). Distinctive heavily scarred body, becoming whiter with age. Diet almost exclusively squid. Adults 8.5-13 ft. Not ESA-listed. [G1]

**Short-Finned Pilot Whale** (*Globicephala macrorhynchus*) — Delphinidae. Rare on PNW outer coast at northern range limit. Large delphinid (12-18 ft) with prominent bulbous melon. Travels in stable matrilineal groups of 15-50. Squid specialist in deep submarine canyon habitats. Not ESA-listed. [G1]

**Sperm Whale** (*Physeter macrocephalus*) — Physeteridae. ESA Endangered; WA State Endangered. Largest toothed whale — males 49-59 ft, 35-45 tons. Males documented off WA/OR outer coast in waters deeper than 3,000 ft, associated with submarine canyons. Primary prey: deep-water squid including giant squid (*Architeuthis*). Severely depleted by commercial whaling; recovery slow. [G1]

**Baird's Beaked Whale** (*Berardius bairdii*) — Ziphiidae. Largest beaked whale (35-42 ft). Deep submarine canyon habitats along continental slope, documented off WA/OR. Dives to 3,000+ ft for 60+ minutes. Not ESA-listed. [G1, G14]

**Cuvier's Beaked Whale** (*Ziphius cavirostris*) — Ziphiidae. Deep offshore waters beyond continental shelf. Holds the record for the deepest dive by any mammal (9,816 ft) and longest dive (222 minutes). Known primarily from strandings. Not ESA-listed. [G1]

## Part II: Order Carnivora, Suborder Pinnipedia — Seals, Sea Lions, and Fur Seals

Six pinniped species regularly occur in PNW waters, ranging from the ubiquitous harbor seal — the most common marine mammal in Puget Sound — to the rare Guadalupe fur seal. Pinnipeds are united by their semiaquatic lifestyle: they forage at sea but haul out on land or ice for resting, thermoregulation, molting, and breeding. This dual requirement makes them visible components of the coastal landscape in a way that fully aquatic cetaceans are not — and makes their haul-out sites ecologically important interfaces between marine and terrestrial systems.

The MMPA's 1972 prohibition on the hunting and harassment of marine mammals triggered one of the most dramatic pinniped recoveries in conservation history. Harbor seal and California sea lion populations in the PNW increased by 5-10 times within three decades of MMPA passage, reaching or exceeding estimated pre-exploitation levels. This recovery, while a conservation success, has created new ecological dynamics — increased pinniped populations consume substantial quantities of salmon, creating tension between marine mammal protection and salmonid recovery, and the expanded pinniped prey base has supported the growth of Bigg's killer whale populations. [G1, G9]

---

### 2.1 Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina richardii*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Phocidae
**Status:** Resident, year-round, non-migratory
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; MMPA: Not depleted, not strategic; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed
**Population:** Approximately 30,000-40,000 in Washington State; stable or slightly declining from post-MMPA peak

**Description:** Small to medium-sized phocid (true seal) — adults 5-6 ft, 120-370 lbs. Variable coloration: background color ranges from silver-gray to brown to nearly black, with dark spots or rings throughout (individually unique, used for photo-ID in some study populations). Large, dark eyes adapted for low-light underwater vision. No external ear pinnae (diagnostic of phocids, distinguishing them from otariids — sea lions and fur seals). Short flippers; hind flippers cannot rotate forward, so harbor seals move on land by undulating their body ("galumphing"). [G9, G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Most common marine mammal in PNW waters, occupying virtually every accessible shoreline, estuary, and rocky reef in WA, OR, and BC. Year-round residents (non-migratory). Population increased from fewer than 5,000 in the early 1970s (after decades of bounty hunting) to 30,000-40,000 by the 2000s following MMPA passage. Now stabilized or slightly declining, possibly from increased Bigg's killer whale predation — a natural top-down regulatory mechanism. [G9, G1]

**Feeding Ecology:**
Generalist predators: herring, sand lance, anchovy, smelt, salmonids, flatfish, sculpins, rockfish, squid, octopus, shrimp. Individual seals learn to station at fish ladders (notably Ballard Locks, Seattle) and hatchery outfalls, creating management tension between recovering pinniped and declining salmon populations. [G9, G1]

**Haul-Out Behavior:**
Regular haul-outs for thermoregulation, rest, molting (late summer-fall), and pupping (June-September). Sites are ecologically important — concentrating nutrients, attracting predators (eagles, Bigg's orcas), and organizing coastal wildlife distributions. [G9]

- See AVI module: Bald eagle — harbor seals (especially afterbirth and pup carcasses) are eagle scavenging targets at coastal haul-outs
- See AVI module: Glaucous-winged Gull — gull colonies adjacent to seal haul-outs exploit shared tidal zone resources
- Bigg's killer whale primary prey (see Section 1.1.2)

### 2.2 California Sea Lion (*Zalophus californianus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Otariidae
**Status:** Seasonal migrant (non-breeding males, primarily September-May)
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; MMPA: Not depleted; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed
**Population:** US stock estimated at 257,000+ (2023); thousands of males migrate to PNW annually

**Description:** Large otariid (eared seal) — males 7-8 ft, 600-850 lbs; females 5-6.5 ft, 200-240 lbs. Pronounced sexual dimorphism. Males are dark brown to chocolate, with a prominent sagittal crest (bony ridge on skull, giving the forehead a "bumpy" profile). Females are lighter tan to golden brown. External ear pinnae (small, rolled ears) distinguish otariids from phocids. Fore-flippers are long and used for propulsion in water (phocids use hind flippers); hind-flippers rotate forward, allowing sea lions to "walk" on land. Highly vocal — the "barking" marine mammal of popular culture. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Breed off southern California and Mexico (June-July). Males — and increasingly some females and juveniles — migrate north September-May. Thousands haul out on docks, jetties, buoys, and log booms throughout the Salish Sea, lower Columbia River, and outer coast. [G1, G9]

**Feeding Ecology and Salmon Interactions:**
Opportunistic predators consuming herring, anchovy, sardine, mackerel, squid, rockfish, and — critically — adult salmon and steelhead. The sea lion-salmon conflict has been most acute at Bonneville Dam, where pinnipeds intercept ESA-listed fish at fish ladders, removing an estimated 2-5% of the spring Chinook run in some years. This MMPA-vs-ESA conflict led to Congressional authorization (Section 120) for lethal removal of individually identified sea lions at the dam. [G1, G9]

**Predation:** California sea lions are prey for Bigg's killer whales and white sharks. Bigg's orca predation on California sea lions is regularly observed in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and outer coast. [P6]

### 2.3 Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Otariidae
**Status:** Resident/seasonal, breeding colonies on outer coast
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Eastern DPS delisted 2013 (formerly Threatened); Western DPS Endangered (not in PNW); IUCN: Near Threatened; WA State: Not listed (monitoring)
**Population:** Eastern DPS approximately 55,000-60,000 (increasing)

**Description:** Largest otariid — males 9-11 ft, 1,500-2,500 lbs; females 7.5-9 ft, 600-800 lbs. The most sexually dimorphic pinniped in PNW waters. Males are massive, with a thick mane of coarse hair around the neck and shoulders (hence "sea lion"), a broad, flat forehead, and a deep, rumbling roar quite different from the California sea lion's bark. Coloration is tawny to reddish-brown, lightening to pale blond when dry. Distinguished from California sea lion by larger size, lighter coloration, broader snout, and proportionally smaller flippers. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Year-round presence with breeding rookeries on offshore rocks along the WA/OR outer coast, Columbia River mouth jetties, and Olympic coast. Also haul out in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and San Juan Islands. Eastern DPS listed Threatened 1990, delisted 2013 after sustained recovery — paralleling harbor seal recovery under MMPA. [G1, G9]

**Feeding Ecology:** Pollock, Pacific cod, herring, sand lance, flatfish, rockfish, salmon, squid, octopus. Dive to 600+ ft, forage up to 150 miles offshore. [G1]

**Predation:** Bigg's killer whales — hunts are prolonged due to the sea lion's formidable size (males 2,000+ lbs), requiring coordinated group effort. [P6]

### 2.4 Northern Elephant Seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Phocidae
**Status:** Seasonal/increasing, haul-outs expanding into PNW
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; MMPA: Not depleted; IUCN: Least Concern; WA State: Not listed
**Population:** Approximately 240,000 (total US stock, 2023), increasing

**Description:** Largest Northern Hemisphere pinniped — males 14-16 ft, 3,000-5,000 lbs; females 9-12 ft, 900-1,800 lbs. Extreme sexual dimorphism — more pronounced than in any other pinniped. Adult males develop a large, pendulous proboscis (the "elephant" trunk-like nose) used as a resonating chamber for vocalizations during breeding competition. Coloration is dark brown to gray, often scarred from intraspecific combat (males) or shark bites. Deep divers — routinely reaching 1,000-2,600 ft and remaining submerged for 20-60 minutes, with recorded dives exceeding 5,000 ft. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Hunted to as few as 20-100 individuals by 1890; recovery to 240,000+ is one of conservation's greatest comebacks, though genetic consequences persist. Breeds on islands off southern/central California and Mexico. Non-breeding individuals increasingly haul out on PNW outer coast beaches and occasionally in the Strait of Juan de Fuca — for resting and molting, not breeding. Northward expansion likely reflects population growth and possibly warming ocean conditions. [G1, G9]

**Feeding Ecology:** Deep-water foragers — squid, ratfish, small sharks, deep-water fish. Spend 8-10 months at sea, diving to averages of 1,500-2,000 ft (some exceeding 5,000 ft), exploiting mesopelagic prey layers unreachable by other PNW pinnipeds. [G1]

### 2.5 Northern Fur Seal (*Callorhinus ursinus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Otariidae
**Status:** Pelagic, rare near shore
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Not listed; MMPA: Depleted (Pribilof Islands stock); IUCN: Vulnerable; WA State: Not listed
**Population:** Approximately 620,000 (total, declining from historical peaks)

**Description:** Medium-sized otariid — males 6-7 ft, 400-600 lbs; females 4-5 ft, 80-110 lbs. Dense, luxurious underfur (the densest fur of any mammal — approximately 300,000 hairs per square inch) beneath coarse guard hairs. Males are dark brown to black with a grayish mane; females are gray-brown above, reddish below. Proportionally very large hind flippers (used for thermoregulation by waving them in the air while resting at sea). [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Pelagic — spend 8-10 months at sea, breeding on the Pribilof Islands (Alaska), Commander Islands (Russia), and San Miguel Island (California). During oceanic phase (November-June), substantial numbers pass through PNW offshore waters. Rarely seen from shore. The North Pacific Fur Seal Convention of 1911 — one of the first international conservation agreements — ended unregulated pelagic sealing that had reduced the species from 2-3 million to fewer than 300,000. Population recovered but has declined again since the 1990s, particularly at the Pribilofs. [G1]

**Historical Significance:** Northern fur seal pelts drove the Pacific maritime fur trade. Aleut (Unangan) people were forcibly relocated to the Pribilofs by Russia to serve as labor for the seal harvest — colonial exploitation that persisted through Russian and American administration. [G1]

### 2.6 Guadalupe Fur Seal (*Arctocephalus townsendi*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Pinnipedia > Otariidae
**Status:** Rare vagrant
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Threatened; IUCN: Least Concern (recently downlisted from Near Threatened); WA State: Not listed
**Population:** Approximately 41,000+ (2022), increasing from near-extinction

**Description:** Medium-sized fur seal — males 6-7 ft, 300-400 lbs; females 4-5 ft, 90-110 lbs. Dark brown to black, with a distinctive long, pointed snout and large forward-facing eyes. Dense underfur similar to but less valuable commercially than northern fur seal. [G1]

**Distribution in PNW:**
Guadalupe fur seals breed almost exclusively on Guadalupe Island off Baja California, Mexico. They were hunted to presumed extinction by the early twentieth century — the species was believed gone until a small breeding colony was rediscovered on Guadalupe Island in 1954. The population has recovered steadily and expanded to new breeding sites on the Channel Islands (California). Vagrants are increasingly documented north of the breeding range, including occasional strandings and sightings on the Washington and Oregon coasts. Their expanding range likely reflects population growth and possibly changing ocean conditions. [G1]

## Part III: Order Carnivora, Family Mustelidae — The Sea Otter

### 3.1 Sea Otter (*Enhydra lutris kenyoni* — Northern subspecies)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident, reintroduced
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Threatened (Southern sea otter DPS in California; Washington population not separately listed but protected under MMPA); MMPA: Depleted (Southern sea otter DPS); IUCN: Endangered; WA State: Endangered
**Jurisdiction:** US Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) — the sea otter is the only USFWS-jurisdiction marine mammal in the PNW. All other marine mammals fall under NOAA Fisheries.
**Population:** Approximately 2,500-2,800 along the Washington Olympic coast (2023 WDFW/USFWS survey), increasing from 59 reintroduced animals

**Description:** Smallest marine mammal in the PNW — adults 3.5-5 ft (including tail), 45-100 lbs. Males larger than females. Dense, brown to black fur — the densest fur of any mammal (up to 1 million hairs per square inch, approximately three times denser than northern fur seal). Unlike all other marine mammals, sea otters have no blubber layer — they rely entirely on their fur for insulation, maintaining a layer of trapped air against the skin that provides thermal insulation in water as cold as 35 degrees F. This insulation strategy requires constant grooming; if the fur becomes fouled (by oil, for example), insulation fails and the otter dies of hypothermia within hours. [G5, G9]

**Extirpation and Reintroduction:**
Historical range: continuously along the North Pacific rim from Japan to Baja California, estimated 150,000-300,000 animals. The maritime fur trade reduced the species to approximately 1,000-2,000 by the time the 1911 North Pacific Fur Seal Convention provided protection. Completely extirpated from Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia by the early twentieth century.

In 1969-1970, 59 sea otters were translocated from Amchitka Island (Alaska) to the Olympic coast — coordinated by the US Atomic Energy Commission (Amchitka was a nuclear test site), WDFW, and USFWS. From those 59 founders, the current population of approximately 2,500-2,800 has grown — a 40-fold increase, one of the most successful marine mammal reintroductions in North America. The population is currently confined to the outer Olympic coast from Destruction Island to Point Grenville, with gradual range expansion. Sea otters have not recolonized the Strait of Juan de Fuca or Puget Sound. [G5, G9]

**Keystone Engineer — The Kelp Forest Cascade:**
The sea otter is the textbook example of a keystone species — an organism whose ecological impact is disproportionately large relative to its abundance. The mechanism is a three-level trophic cascade:

1. **Sea otters eat sea urchins** (primary prey, plus crabs, clams, mussels, snails). Adults consume 20-25% of body weight daily (15-25 lbs) — the highest mass-specific metabolic rate of any marine mammal.

2. **Controlled urchin populations cannot overgraze kelp.** Without otters, urchins explode and create "urchin barrens" — featureless underwater deserts.

3. **Kelp forests support entire ecosystems.** Bull kelp (*Nereocystis luetkeana*) and giant kelp (*Macrocystis pyrifera*) shelter juvenile fish (rockfish, lingcod, juvenile salmonids), support invertebrate communities, dampen wave energy, sequester carbon, and provide critical habitat for marine birds (murrelets, guillemots, cormorants, sea ducks).

The otter's extirpation cascaded through every trophic level; its return is slowly reversing that cascade. [G5, G9]

**Tool Use:**
Sea otters are one of the few non-primate mammals that use tools. They place hard-shelled prey (mussels, clams, urchins) on their chest while floating on their backs and crack them open with a rock held in the forepaw. Individual otters show "handedness" and carry favored rocks in a loose pouch of skin under the forearm. Tool use in sea otters appears to be a combination of innate propensity and socially learned technique — pups observe their mothers' tool use and begin practicing with soft objects at a young age. [G5]

**Threats:**
- **Oil spills:** The sea otter's dependence on fur insulation (rather than blubber) makes it the marine mammal most vulnerable to oil contamination. The 1989 Exxon Valdez spill in Alaska killed an estimated 2,800 sea otters. The Washington coast, with its heavy tanker and barge traffic, poses ongoing spill risk.
- **Shark predation:** White shark bites are an increasing mortality source as white shark populations recover along the Pacific coast.
- **Disease:** Toxoplasmosis (*Toxoplasma gondii*, transmitted from cat feces through wastewater to the marine environment), domoic acid poisoning (from harmful algal blooms), and other disease agents affect otter health.
- **Limited genetic diversity:** The Washington population descends from only 59 founders, creating a genetic bottleneck that may limit adaptive capacity.
- **Conflict with shellfish fisheries:** Sea otters consume many of the same invertebrate species valued by commercial and recreational shellfish harvesters (Dungeness crab, geoduck, clams, mussels, sea urchins). As otters expand their range, conflicts with shellfish interests are expected to intensify. [G5, G9]

**Indigenous Context:**
Sea otters were sustainably hunted by coastal PNW nations — Makah, Quileute, Quinault, Nuu-chah-nulth, Haida, Tlingit — for thousands of years before contact. The maritime fur trade that extirpated otters was driven by European, Russian, and American commercial demand, not Indigenous harvest. The contrast between sustainable Indigenous harvest and extractive colonial exploitation recurs across all PNW marine mammal species. [O7-C7, O8-C8]

## Part IV: Ecological Networks and Cross-Module Integration

### 4.1 The Salmon-Orca-Eagle Nutrient Pathway

Chinook salmon connect marine, freshwater, and terrestrial systems: (1) salmon accumulate marine-derived nutrients over 2-7 ocean years; (2) SRKW intercept Chinook as the marine apex predator; (3) surviving spawners deposit nutrients in freshwater (130+ scavenger species); (4) bears drag carcasses into riparian forests, eagles carry salmon to nest trees — salmon-derived nitrogen detected in tree rings 1,600 ft from streams; (5) salmon-fertilized forests produce the conifers that shade streams and create juvenile salmon habitat. The cycle is complete.

This means SRKW conservation, salmon recovery, dam policy, forestry, riparian protection, eagle habitat, and bear management are components of a single ecological system. [G1, G4, P6]

### 4.2 Kelp-Otter-Seabird Nexus

Sea otter kelp forest maintenance creates habitat for fish species that in turn feed marine birds:

- **Kelp forests shelter juvenile rockfish, lingcod, greenling, and juvenile salmonids** — all prey for diving seabirds (murrelets, auklets, guillemots, cormorants)
- **Kelp canopy provides surface rest areas** for sea ducks (scoters, goldeneyes) and foraging substrate for shorebirds at the kelp wrack line
- **Kelp forests dampen wave energy**, creating calmer nearshore waters suitable for murrelet and auklet foraging
- **Without otters, urchin barrens replace kelp forests**, and the entire associated bird community loses habitat

The implication: sea otter reintroduction and range expansion is simultaneously a marine mammal conservation action and a seabird conservation action. The AVI module documents the bird species; this module documents the mammal that makes their habitat possible. [G5, G9]

### 4.3 Pinniped Haul-Out and Coastal Bird Overlap

Harbor seal haul-out sites, sea lion resting areas, and coastal bird nesting colonies often occur on the same rocky shoreline features — offshore rocks, reef-tops, and protected beaches. This spatial overlap creates ecological interactions:

- **Black Oystercatcher nesting** occurs on the same rocky intertidal platforms where harbor seals haul out. Seal activity can disturb nesting oystercatchers, but seals also deter some terrestrial predators (raccoons, mink) that would otherwise access nesting rocks.
- **Glaucous-winged Gull colonies** on offshore rocks share space with Steller sea lion haul-outs and California sea lion resting areas. Gulls scavenge pinniped afterbirth and fecal material.
- **Bald eagle foraging** at pinniped haul-outs includes scavenging dead seal pups, afterbirth tissue, and occasionally predating live seal pups.
- **Pelagic and Brandt's Cormorant** nesting colonies on cliff faces above pinniped haul-outs — different vertical zones of the same rock feature.

These shared-space dynamics are a natural feature of the PNW coast. They represent not competition but complementary use of limited rocky shoreline habitat by species with different spatial and temporal requirements. [G9, G1]

### 4.4 Coast Salish Marine Ecological Grammar

Coast Salish peoples of the Salish Sea region — including Lummi, Tulalip, Swinomish, Samish, Snohomish, Squamish, Musqueam, Tsleil-Waututh, and many other nations — developed a sophisticated understanding of marine mammal ecology integrated with their broader ecological knowledge system. This knowledge was not separate from but embedded within a relational framework that understood marine mammals as persons with their own agency, societies, and rights.

Key elements documented in published ethnographic sources:

- **Seasonal timing:** Detailed knowledge of marine mammal movements integrated with salmon run timing, herring spawn, and weather. Marine mammal presence used as indicator of broader ecological conditions.
- **Behavioral observation:** Orca ecotype distinctions (fish-eaters vs. seal-eaters) recognized centuries before Western science described them.
- **Reciprocal obligation:** Relational framework imposing constraints on harvest and behavior that functioned as a conservation ethic.
- **Place-based knowledge:** Accumulated spatial database of marine mammal congregation sites transmitted through oral tradition — paralleling modern habitat suitability modeling but integrating temporal, spiritual, and relational dimensions.

**Sources:** Suttles *Coast Salish Essays* (1987) [O6-C6]; Suttles *Coast Salish Resource Management* (1990); Stewart *Indian Fishing* (1977) [O7-C7]
