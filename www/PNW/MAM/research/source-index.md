## Key Researchers & Research Sites

### Chris Maser -- Oregon State University Press
- Foundational mammalogy reference for the Pacific Northwest (P01)
- Co-authored pioneering work on mycorrhizal dispersal by small mammals (P37)
- Natural history of Oregon Coast mammals (G27)
- The Maser corpus remains the most comprehensive single-author treatment of PNW mammal ecology, natural history, and ecological relationships

### Burke Museum of Natural History and Culture (University of Washington)
- Over 50,000 mammal specimens from Washington and the PNW
- Type specimens and reference collection for taxonomic verification
- Washington mammal checklist maintained as current authority
- Dalquest (1948, P07) and Booth (1947, P08) collections form historical baseline
- Source of paleontological records for Pleistocene megafauna (mammoth, mastodon) from Washington sites

### Center for Whale Research (Friday Harbor, San Juan Island)
- Continuous Southern Resident killer whale census since 1976 (O01)
- Individual photo-identification database for all SRKW individuals
- Population trend data that underpins all ESA recovery planning
- Ken Balcomb founded the census; data continues under current director

### Cascadia Research Collective
- Photo-ID catalogs for humpback whales, gray whales, harbor porpoises in WA/OR waters
- Stranding response and necropsy data
- Population estimates used by NOAA stock assessments
- Primary non-government marine mammal research organization in the PNW

### North Cascades Ecosystem -- Multi-Agency
- Grizzly bear reintroduction site (NPS/USFWS/WDFW collaborative)
- Wolverine monitoring (USGS, Cascades Carnivore Project)
- Fisher reintroduction (NPS, Conservation Northwest)
- The most active large carnivore recovery zone in the PNW

### Columbia Basin -- Multi-Agency
- Pygmy rabbit recovery program (WDFW, Oregon Zoo)
- Shrub-steppe habitat conservation (TNC, ODFW, BLM)
- Washington ground squirrel monitoring (WDFW)
- The most critically endangered mammalian habitat in the PNW

## Source Quality Assessment

### Tier 1: Primary Authority (highest reliability)

Government agency data (G01-G39): Based on systematic field inventories, long-term monitoring, regulatory authority, and peer-reviewed technical reports. Use as primary source for all quantitative claims including population counts, conservation status, distribution records, and regulatory designations.

NOAA stock assessments (G15, G32) and USFWS recovery plans (G05-G07, G16-G18, G33) carry legal regulatory weight under MMPA and ESA respectively. These are the only acceptable sources for official population estimates and conservation status designations.

### Tier 2: Peer-Reviewed Research (high reliability)

Published research (P01-P37): Peer-reviewed journals and university press monographs. Provides specific quantitative data, analytical frameworks, phylogeographic analysis, and ecological relationships not available in agency reports. Use for ecological interpretations, evolutionary analysis, and detailed species biology.

Foundational references that have not been superseded: Maser (1998, P01), Dalquest (1948, P07), and Booth (1947, P08) remain benchmark datasets for PNW mammal distribution and ecology. Newer studies build on rather than replace these baselines.

### Tier 3: Professional Organizations (good reliability)

Professional conservation and research organizations (O01-O33): Data often synthesized from agency and academic sources. Use for conservation program descriptions, regional assessments, long-term monitoring trends, and community science data. The Center for Whale Research (O01) dataset is exceptional -- 47+ years of continuous individual-level census data with no comparable government equivalent.

### Tier 4: Cultural Sources (requires special handling)

Indigenous knowledge sources (O06-O08, O19-O23, O27-O28): Published ethnographies and Nation-authorized cultural resources. All references must name specific nations. Never extrapolate knowledge from one nation to another. Never include culturally restricted information (ceremony details, sacred sites, specific medicine preparations). Cite the community-authorized source, not a secondary interpretation.
