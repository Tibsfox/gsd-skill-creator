# PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — Module 2B: Rodentia (Mice, Beaver, Porcupine) & Lagomorpha

> **"Fur, Fin & Fang" — Pacific Northwest Mammalian Biodiversity**
>
> This module profiles approximately 40 species of small rodents, beavers, porcupines, pikas, rabbits, and hares documented in the Pacific Northwest bioregion (Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia). These animals occupy every ecoregion from alpine talus to sagebrush steppe, from old-growth canopy to the deepest river valleys. They are the base of nearly every terrestrial food web in the region, the primary prey for raptors, mustelids, and mesocarnivores alike, and several rank among the most ecologically consequential mammals on the continent.
>
> Six species receive extended treatment: the American beaver (*Castor canadensis*), the continent's preeminent watershed architect and a cultural keystone species for Coast Salish, Chinook, and Plateau nations; the American pika (*Ochotona princeps*), a climate sentinel whose elevational retreat tracks warming temperatures with scientific precision; the pygmy rabbit (*Brachylagus idahoensis*), North America's smallest rabbit and one of its most endangered; the mountain beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*), a living fossil and the sole surviving member of the world's most primitive rodent family, endemic to the PNW; the deer mouse (*Peromyscus maniculatus*), the most abundant mammal in the region and a key node in hantavirus ecology; and the snowshoe hare (*Lepus americanus*), whose population cycles drive one of ecology's most studied predator-prey oscillations.
>
> **Design principle:** Each species profile follows the standardized template from `00-taxonomy-schema.md`. Rich prose narrative integrates morphometrics, habitat, diet, reproduction, social structure, population, ecological role, ecosystem engineering, evolutionary notes, and sources. Extended profiles explore the deeper ecological and cultural dimensions of keystone and indicator species.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific site locations for ESA-listed species. County/watershed-level location only. Every Indigenous knowledge reference names a specific nation. Population data sourced from USFWS, WDFW, ODFW, IDFG, USGS, or peer-reviewed research. No policy advocacy. Human-wildlife conflict documented factually.

## 1. Cricetidae, Subfamily Neotominae — Deer Mice and Woodrats

The Neotominae represent the New World radiation of cricetid rodents, and in the Pacific Northwest they are everywhere. From the summit scree of Mount Rainier to the driftwood tangles of the Olympic coast, from the sagebrush flats of the Columbia Basin to the moss-draped understory of the Hoh rainforest, deer mice and their relatives occupy virtually every terrestrial habitat the region offers. They are the most species-rich group of small mammals in the PNW, and their collective biomass exceeds that of all other rodent families combined. They are also the primary prey base for an extraordinary diversity of predators — great horned owls, long-eared owls, barn owls, Northern spotted owls, barred owls, red-tailed hawks, American kestrels, weasels, mink, marten, fisher, bobcat, coyote, fox, and rattlesnakes all depend heavily on neotomine rodents.

### Deer Mouse (*Peromyscus maniculatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — ubiquitous throughout PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Common; no special status
- OR: Common; no special status
- ID: Common; no special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 7-10 cm (head-body); tail 5-13 cm, typically shorter than or equal to body length
- Mass: 10-33 g, averaging 18-22 g; substantial geographic variation across subspecies
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; sexes nearly identical in size
- Distinguishing features: Large dark eyes, prominent ears, bicolored tail (dark above, white below) sharply demarcated, white feet (the "white-footed mouse" of older literature), dorsal pelage ranging from pale grayish-brown in grassland forms to dark reddish-brown in forest forms. The species complex encompasses at least two major clades — the forest form (*P. m. rubidus* and allies, west of the Cascades) and the prairie form (*P. m. gambelii* and allies, east-side) — which some authorities now elevate to full species status.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Generalist — occupies virtually every terrestrial habitat in the PNW including old-growth forest, second-growth timber, clearcuts, grasslands, shrub-steppe, riparian corridors, talus, rocky outcrops, agricultural fields, and human structures
- Elevation range: Sea level to 10,000+ ft; one of the few small mammals found at every elevation band
- Ecoregion: All 8 zones — Alpine, Montane, Lowland, Foothill, Riparian, Coastal, Steppe, Fossorial (uses burrows extensively)
- Home range: 0.1-1.6 ha; males range farther than females, especially during breeding season
- Nesting: Nests in virtually any sheltered cavity — hollow logs, stumps, rock crevices, abandoned bird nests, woodrat middens, human buildings, vehicles, storage sheds; constructs spherical nest of shredded bark, grass, moss, feathers, and plant fibers

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — seeds comprise 50-75% of diet year-round (conifer seeds, grass seeds, weed seeds), supplemented by insects and other arthropods (beetles, caterpillars, spiders), fungi (including hypogeous truffles), fruits, and green vegetation
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal, primarily terrestrial but capable of climbing; caches seeds in autumn, sometimes storing several liters of seeds in a single cache location
- Seasonal variation: Insects and green vegetation increase in summer diet; seed-dependent in winter; fungi consumption peaks in autumn

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; both sexes mate with multiple partners
- Breeding season: March-October in lowlands; restricted to June-August at higher elevations
- Gestation: 22-27 days (longer if female is lactating — delayed implantation can extend to 35 days)
- Litter size: 3-8 pups (typically 4-6), born altricial — naked, blind, weighing ~1.5 g
- Weaning: 25-35 days; juveniles disperse rapidly
- Sexual maturity: 5-8 weeks; females can breed at 49 days of age
- Lifespan: Rarely exceeds 1 year in the wild (high predation pressure); up to 8 years in captivity
- Reproductive output: A single female can produce 2-4 litters per year, potentially 20+ offspring annually; this extraordinary fecundity offsets predation mortality exceeding 90% per year

**Social Structure:** Solitary and territorial during breeding season; males' home ranges overlap those of several females. In winter, deer mice become communal — groups of 2-15 individuals share nests for thermoregulation, reducing metabolic costs by up to 30% at sub-freezing temperatures. This communal nesting behavior is critical for winter survival at high elevations and across the east-side steppe.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; population fluctuates annually with food availability but is resilient
- Estimate: Not estimated at regional scale — density ranges from 5-50 individuals per hectare depending on habitat quality and season; likely the most abundant mammal in the PNW by total biomass
- Threats: No population-level threats; locally reduced by intensive agriculture, but rapid recolonization follows any disturbance
- Source: [P1], [P2], [O4]

**Ecological Role:** The deer mouse is arguably the single most important prey species in the PNW terrestrial food web. Its ubiquity, high reproductive rate, and occupation of every habitat type make it the foundational energy transfer node between plant production and the predator community. Barn owls consume an estimated 1,500-3,000 deer mice per breeding pair per year. Northern spotted owls in old-growth forests rely on deer mice as a secondary prey item after flying squirrels. The deer mouse is also a significant seed disperser and mycorrhizal fungus vector — by consuming and caching hypogeous truffles (genera *Rhizopogon*, *Hymenogaster*, *Gautieria*), deer mice inoculate tree roots with fungal symbionts essential for conifer growth, particularly in disturbed soils following logging or fire.

**Hantavirus Ecology:** *Peromyscus maniculatus* is the primary reservoir host for Sin Nombre virus (SNV), the causative agent of hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS) in North America. The virus is maintained in deer mouse populations through horizontal transmission, primarily via aggressive encounters between males. Seroprevalence in PNW deer mouse populations typically ranges from 5-30%, with higher rates in arid east-side habitats than in wet west-side forests. Human infection occurs through inhalation of aerosolized virus from dried rodent urine, feces, or saliva — typically in enclosed spaces (cabins, sheds, barns) where mice have nested. Washington and Oregon report 1-5 HPS cases per year; the case fatality rate is approximately 36%. Risk is highest in rural and peri-urban settings east of the Cascades. The ecological context matters: deer mouse population density, SNV seroprevalence, and human exposure risk all increase in fragmented habitats with high edge-to-interior ratios, suggesting that landscape-level forest management has public health implications.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Peromyscus maniculatus* is one of the most polytypic mammals in North America, with over 60 described subspecies. Molecular phylogenetics has revealed that the species as traditionally defined is paraphyletic — the forest-dwelling forms west of the Cascades (*P. m. rubidus*, *P. m. austerus*) are more closely related to *P. keeni* (Keen's mouse) than to prairie forms east of the Cascades. A comprehensive taxonomic revision is likely to split the deer mouse complex into 2-3 species. The PNW sits at the contact zone between these lineages, making the region a natural laboratory for studying speciation in progress.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Barn owl, great horned owl, spotted owl, barred owl, red-tailed hawk, American kestrel — all major deer mouse predators
- Fungi-forest feedback: Deer mouse truffle dispersal parallels the flying squirrel-fungus-forest loop documented in Module 2A
- Hantavirus ecology intersects with public health and land management

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; [O4] Burke Museum; Mills et al. (1997) — Factors associated with HPS in the Four Corners region; Dragoo et al. (2006) — *Peromyscus* phylogenetics

### Keen's Mouse (*Peromyscus keeni*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — Pacific coastal forests
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status; range restricted to Olympic Peninsula and San Juan Islands
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 8-11 cm (head-body); tail 8-12 cm, typically longer than body (key distinction from deer mouse)
- Mass: 17-40 g, averaging 22-28 g; larger than sympatric deer mice
- Distinguishing features: Longer tail relative to body than *P. maniculatus*, darker dorsal pelage (rich brown to dark brown), larger hind feet (22-25 mm), more prominent ears. In practice, field identification from deer mouse is extremely difficult without genetic confirmation.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Temperate rainforest, dense conifer forest, coastal forest edges; strongly associated with old-growth structure including coarse woody debris, moss mats, and dense understory
- Elevation range: Sea level to 1,500 m; concentrated below 600 m in coastal forests
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest, Coastal, Riparian
- Home range: 0.2-1.0 ha

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds, insects, fungi, berries; more fungus-dependent than deer mouse in some studies
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; semi-arboreal, climbing more readily than deer mouse

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous
- Breeding season: March-September
- Gestation: 23-27 days
- Litter size: 3-6
- Lifespan: 1-2 years in the wild

**Social Structure:** Similar to deer mouse; solitary during breeding, communal nesting in winter.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable where old-growth habitat persists
- Threats: Old-growth logging reduces habitat quality; competitive displacement by deer mice in disturbed habitats

**Ecological Role:** Functionally similar to deer mouse in food web position but more tightly linked to old-growth forest structure. Important truffle disperser in coastal rainforest ecosystems.

**Evolutionary Note:** Keen's mouse was long considered a subspecies of *P. maniculatus*. Molecular work by Zheng et al. (2003) and Lucid & Cook (2004) confirmed it as a distinct species, sister to the forest clade of *P. maniculatus*. The species is named for the Rev. John Henry Keen, a naturalist-clergyman who collected mammals in British Columbia in the 1890s. Its distribution — coastal BC, Southeast Alaska, Haida Gwaii, Olympic Peninsula — reflects glacial refugia along the Pacific coast.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P7] Dalquest 1948; Zheng et al. (2003) — *Peromyscus* phylogeography; Lucid & Cook (2004)

### Canyon Mouse (*Peromyscus crinitus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — east-side arid habitats
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- OR: Present in arid southeast; no special status
- ID: Present in Snake River corridor; no special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 7-8 cm (head-body); tail 7-10 cm, slightly longer than body
- Mass: 15-23 g
- Distinguishing features: Pale buffy-gray dorsal pelage matching desert substrate, long tufted tail (tuft of hairs at tip distinguishes from deer mouse), relatively large ears, soft silky fur

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Rocky canyon walls, lava flows, rimrock, talus slopes in arid environments
- Elevation range: 300-2,100 m
- Ecoregion: Steppe, Foothill (arid)
- Home range: 0.1-0.5 ha; confined to rocky substrates

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds, insects, green vegetation; more insectivorous than deer mouse
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; restricted to rocky habitat, rarely venturing onto open ground

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: April-August
- Gestation: 25-27 days
- Litter size: 2-5 (typically 3-4)
- Lifespan: 1-2 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable within limited range
- Threats: Minimal — habitat is naturally protected by inaccessibility

**Ecological Role:** Rock-specialist prey species for rattlesnakes, long-eared owls, and pallid bats in canyon ecosystems. Limited geographic overlap with deer mouse in the PNW (restricted to Owyhee, Malheur, and Snake River canyon systems in OR and ID).

**Evolutionary Note:** Represents the arid-adapted clade of *Peromyscus*, more closely related to California species than to PNW deer mice. Present in the PNW only at the northern margin of its Great Basin range.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Johnson & Armstrong (1987) — canyon mouse ecology

### Bushy-tailed Woodrat (*Neotoma cinerea*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — widespread in rocky habitats
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status
- OR: No special status
- ID: No special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 17-25 cm (head-body); tail 13-22 cm, densely furred and bushy (diagnostic)
- Mass: 200-585 g; males significantly larger than females
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced — males average 1.4-1.8x female mass, one of the most sexually dimorphic rodents in North America
- Distinguishing features: Large rounded ears, prominent dark eyes, long bushy tail squirrel-like in appearance, dorsal pelage grayish-brown to tawny, white or cream underparts, conspicuous vibrissae. The bushy tail immediately distinguishes this species from all other PNW rodents.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Rocky outcrops, cliff faces, talus slopes, caves, mine shafts, abandoned buildings, old-growth forest snags with cavities
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,200 m; found at higher elevations than any other PNW woodrat
- Ecoregion: Alpine, Montane, Foothill, Steppe (rocky substrates throughout)
- Home range: Males 0.3-3.0 ha; females 0.1-0.5 ha
- Denning: Constructs elaborate stick-and-debris middens (houses) in rock crevices, cliff overhangs, or building attics; middens accumulate over generations and may be used for centuries

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — primarily green vegetation, forbs, leaves, berries, fungi, and bark; conifer needles and twigs consumed in winter
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; caches food extensively in midden; collects and stores a remarkable variety of objects including bones, antlers, shells, coins, keys, and other human artifacts — the "pack rat" behavior that gives the genus its vernacular name
- Seasonal variation: Green vegetation dominates spring-summer diet; cached materials, bark, and dried vegetation sustain winter feeding

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Polygynous; males compete aggressively, and territorial scent-marking with ventral sebaceous glands is a defining behavior
- Breeding season: April-August; typically one litter per year (occasionally two at lower elevations)
- Gestation: 27-32 days
- Litter size: 1-6 (typically 2-4)
- Weaning: 26-30 days
- Sexual maturity: 10-12 months
- Lifespan: 3-5 years (considerably longer-lived than *Peromyscus*)

**Social Structure:** Solitary and strongly territorial. Males mark territories with urine and ventral gland secretions that produce the pungent musky odor characteristic of woodrat dens — the staining and odor can persist in buildings for years. Females are philopatric, often inheriting natal midden sites from their mothers.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable overall; declining in some low-elevation sites where human disturbance has eliminated rock outcrop habitat
- Threats: Building demolition and mine closure eliminate long-established den sites; great horned owl predation significant
- Source: [P1], [P2]

**Ecological Role:** Woodrat middens are ecological archives of extraordinary scientific value. The crystallized urine (amberat) that cements midden materials preserves plant macrofossils, pollen, and invertebrate remains for thousands of years. Radiocarbon-dated midden contents from eastern Oregon and Idaho have provided 40,000+ year records of vegetation change, climate history, and species range shifts — an irreplaceable paleoenvironmental archive. Living middens also provide shelter for numerous commensals including deer mice, various insects, and reptiles.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Neotoma cinerea* is the most widespread woodrat in North America, ranging from the Yukon to Arizona. PNW populations represent the mesic western and northern extent of the range. The genus *Neotoma* originated in North America during the Miocene (~10 Ma) and diversified into ~20 species. The bushy tail is unique within the genus and may represent adaptation to cold climates — the fur-insulated tail reduces heat loss compared to the naked tails of desert-adapted *Neotoma* species.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Smith (1997) — *Neotoma cinerea*, Mammalian Species; Betancourt, Van Devender & Martin (1990) — *Packrat Middens: The Last 40,000 Years of Biotic Change*

### Dusky-footed Woodrat (*Neotoma fuscipes*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — southwestern Oregon only within PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- OR: Present in Klamath-Siskiyou region; no special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 17-23 cm (head-body); tail 15-21 cm, sparsely haired (not bushy — key distinction from *N. cinerea*)
- Mass: 170-340 g
- Distinguishing features: Dusky-colored upper surface of hind feet (hence common name), grayish-brown dorsal pelage, white to grayish underparts, large ears, moderately haired tail

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense chaparral, oak woodland, riparian thickets with heavy understory
- Elevation range: 100-1,800 m
- Ecoregion: Foothill/Oak Woodland, Lowland Forest (Klamath-Siskiyou only)
- Denning: Builds large stick houses (up to 1.5 m tall and 2 m diameter) on the ground, typically against a tree base or in dense brush; houses are multi-chambered with separate food caches, latrines, and nest chambers lined with shredded bark

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Leaves, fungi, fruits, acorns, and bark; California bay laurel leaves consumed despite containing toxic compounds (umbellulone) — an unusual dietary specialization
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; semi-arboreal, foraging in shrub and tree canopy

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-September
- Gestation: 33 days
- Litter size: 1-4 (typically 2-3)
- Lifespan: 2-4 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in Klamath-Siskiyou; range-wide decline in California due to barred owl predation (owls preying on woodrats at houses)
- Threats: Habitat conversion in oak woodland; barred owl expansion

**Ecological Role:** House-building activity creates microhabitat used by numerous commensals — deer mice, Pacific giant salamanders, ring-necked snakes, and various invertebrates shelter in woodrat houses. Houses also serve as seed caches that can germinate, making woodrats inadvertent propagators of oaks and other woody plants.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Carraway & Verts (1991) — *Neotoma fuscipes*, Mammalian Species

### Northern Grasshopper Mouse (*Onychomys leucogaster*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — east-side steppe
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Present in shrub-steppe; no special status
- OR: Present in high desert; no special status
- ID: Present in Snake River plain; no special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 9-13 cm (head-body); tail 3-6 cm, short and thick — strikingly shorter than any *Peromyscus*
- Mass: 25-52 g
- Distinguishing features: Robust build, short thick tail with white tip, grayish-brown dorsal pelage, pure white underparts, relatively short ears; overall stocky and muscular build unlike the slender gracile form of deer mice

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Sagebrush steppe, grassland, desert scrub; strongly associated with open ground and sparse vegetation
- Elevation range: 200-2,400 m
- Ecoregion: Steppe
- Home range: 2-6 ha — exceptionally large for a rodent of this size

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Primarily carnivorous — unique among North American cricetids. Diet comprises 60-90% animal matter including grasshoppers, beetles, scorpions, centipedes, and other small rodents (including deer mice and harvest mice)
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal hunter; stalks prey, pins it with forepaws, and delivers a killing bite to the back of the skull — a predatory technique convergent with mustelids. Kills and eats scorpions by biting off the stinger first. Highly resistant to scorpion venom through amino acid substitutions in voltage-gated sodium channels.
- Territorial howl: Stands on hind legs and emits a high-pitched sustained howl (audible to humans) to advertise territory — one of the only rodents known to vocalize in this manner, earning the nickname "werewolf mouse"

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Monogamous — unusual among cricetids; pair bonds persist across breeding seasons
- Breeding season: April-September
- Gestation: 26-37 days (longer with concurrent lactation)
- Litter size: 2-6 (typically 3-4)
- Lifespan: 2-3 years

**Social Structure:** Monogamous pairs defend territories cooperatively. Both parents provision young. The large home range reflects the energy demands of a carnivorous diet — grasshopper mice require far more space than herbivorous rodents of equivalent body mass.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; declining locally where sagebrush steppe is converted to agriculture
- Threats: Sagebrush removal, agricultural conversion, invasive annual grasses (cheatgrass) alter prey base

**Ecological Role:** Top predator of the nocturnal invertebrate community in steppe ecosystems. Controls grasshopper and scorpion populations. Preys on deer mice and harvest mice, making it a rare example of intraguild predation among rodents. The species is a bioindicator of intact steppe communities — its presence indicates sufficient arthropod prey diversity.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Onychomys* is the only primarily carnivorous genus of cricetid rodents in the New World. The genus diverged from *Peromyscus* approximately 8-10 Ma, and its predatory adaptations — robust skull, enlarged jaw musculature, scorpion venom resistance, monogamous mating system — represent a remarkable evolutionary convergence with small mustelid carnivores. The scorpion venom resistance is one of the most-studied examples of adaptive molecular evolution in mammals.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Ruffer (1968) — *Onychomys leucogaster*, Mammalian Species; Langley (1994) — grasshopper mouse predatory behavior; Rowe et al. (2013) — voltage-gated sodium channel evolution

### Western Harvest Mouse (*Reithrodontomys megalotis*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae > Neotominae
**Status:** Resident — grasslands and open areas
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Present in grasslands; no special status
- OR: Common in grasslands; no special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 5.5-7.5 cm (head-body); tail 5-8 cm
- Mass: 8-15 g — one of the smallest rodents in the PNW
- Distinguishing features: Tiny size, grooved upper incisors (unique among PNW cricetids — the diagnostic character), brownish dorsal pelage with distinct dark median stripe, white or buffy underparts, long tail relative to body

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Grasslands, weedy fields, marsh edges, fence rows, agricultural margins
- Elevation range: Sea level to 1,500 m
- Ecoregion: Lowland, Foothill, Steppe (grassy areas), Riparian margins

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds and insects in roughly equal proportions; green vegetation seasonally
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; constructs globular nests of woven grass above ground in dense vegetation, 15-90 cm above the ground

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: March-November
- Gestation: 23-24 days
- Litter size: 2-7 (typically 3-4)
- Lifespan: Rarely exceeds 1 year

**Ecological Role:** Important prey for barn owls, short-eared owls, and kestrels in grassland habitats. Seed dispersal in grassland communities. The grooved incisors are an ancestral trait shared with South American cricetids, reflecting the genus's origin in the Neotropical-Nearctic interchange.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P1] Maser 1998

## 2. Muridae — Old World Mice and Rats (Introduced)

Three murid species have established feral populations in the PNW — all introduced from Eurasia via maritime commerce. Unlike native cricetids, murids are strongly associated with human-modified environments. Their ecological impact is overwhelmingly negative: competition with native rodents, predation on native invertebrates and ground-nesting birds, crop damage, structural damage to buildings, and disease transmission. They are, however, an inescapable component of the PNW mammal fauna, particularly in urban and agricultural landscapes.

---

### House Mouse (*Mus musculus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Muridae
**Status:** Introduced — commensal with humans, feral populations in agricultural and urban areas
**Conservation Status:**
- Not listed at any level; invasive species

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 7-10 cm (head-body); tail 7-10 cm, nearly naked and scaly
- Mass: 12-30 g
- Distinguishing features: Uniformly gray-brown dorsal pelage (lacks the sharp bicolored pattern of *Peromyscus*), uniformly colored tail (not bicolored), smaller eyes relative to head than deer mouse, distinctly musky odor from urine and sebaceous glands

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Buildings, granaries, warehouses, agricultural fields, urban gardens; feral populations in grasslands and fields near human habitation
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest/Urban, agricultural margins — rarely found far from human structures in the PNW, unlike in drier climates
- Home range: 0.01-0.1 ha in buildings; up to 1 ha in feral populations

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — grains, seeds, insects, virtually any human food; feral populations rely more on seeds and insects
- Foraging strategy: Primarily nocturnal but active at any hour in protected environments; neophilic (attracted to novel objects, in contrast to rats which are neophobic)

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: Year-round in buildings; seasonal in feral populations
- Gestation: 19-21 days — the shortest of any PNW rodent
- Litter size: 3-12 (typically 5-8)
- Sexual maturity: 5-7 weeks
- Lifespan: 1-3 years
- Reproductive output: Under optimal conditions, a single pair can produce 5-10 litters per year; theoretical population growth can reach 500+ descendants per pair per year

**Ecological Role:** Pest species. Competes with native *Peromyscus* in disturbed habitats, though deer mice generally outcompete house mice in natural settings. Important prey for barn owls and other urban raptors. Vector for lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV), salmonella, and leptospirosis.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Mus musculus* originated in the Indo-Pakistani subcontinent and spread westward with the expansion of agriculture ~8,000 years ago. It reached the PNW with Euro-American settlement in the 19th century. The species is the most widely distributed mammal on Earth after humans and the laboratory mouse that underpins modern biomedical research.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Berry (1981) — biology of the house mouse

### Norway Rat (*Rattus norvegicus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Muridae
**Status:** Introduced — commensal, abundant in urban areas
**Conservation Status:**
- Not listed; invasive species

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 18-25 cm (head-body); tail 15-22 cm, shorter than body (key distinction from black rat)
- Mass: 200-500 g; males larger
- Distinguishing features: Robust heavy-bodied build, blunt nose, small ears relative to head, thick tail shorter than head-body length, coarse brown-gray dorsal pelage, pale gray underparts

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Urban environments — sewers, basements, wharves, garbage dumps, commercial buildings, agricultural operations; riparian areas adjacent to human habitation
- Ecoregion: Lowland/Urban, Riparian (near human development)
- Home range: 0.01-0.5 ha; highly dependent on food source proximity

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — grain, meat, fish, garbage, invertebrates, bird eggs, nestling birds; highly opportunistic
- Foraging strategy: Nocturnal; neophobic (avoids novel objects for 2-3 days, making baiting more difficult than for house mice); excellent swimmer — can dive and swim underwater for 30+ seconds

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: Year-round in protected environments
- Gestation: 21-23 days
- Litter size: 6-12 (up to 14)
- Sexual maturity: 8-12 weeks
- Lifespan: 1-3 years

**Ecological Role:** Significant negative ecological impact in the PNW. Preys on ground-nesting seabirds on offshore islands — Norway rats have devastated nesting colonies of auklets, storm-petrels, and other seabirds on islands along the BC and Washington coasts. Island rat eradication programs (e.g., Rat Island in the Aleutians, Langara Island in Haida Gwaii) have demonstrated that seabird populations recover rapidly following rat removal. In urban riparian corridors, Norway rats compete with native muskrats and prey on native invertebrates.

## 3. Dipodidae — Jumping Mice

The jumping mice are among the most distinctive small mammals in the PNW — elongated hind feet, extremely long tails, and a saltatorial (jumping) locomotion that can propel them 1-2 meters per bound. They are also among the most deeply hibernating mammals in North America, spending 6-8 months per year in torpor. Two species occur in the PNW, both associated with moist meadow and riparian habitats.

---

### Pacific Jumping Mouse (*Zapus trinotatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Dipodidae
**Status:** Resident — Pacific Northwest endemic
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (subspecies *Z. t. orarius* in some areas is a species of concern)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status (monitored)
- OR: Strategy species in some ecoregions
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 8-10 cm (head-body); tail 12-16 cm — tail approximately 1.5x body length
- Mass: 18-35 g (pre-hibernation); may reach 50 g when fat-laden before hibernation
- Distinguishing features: Extremely long bicolored tail, elongated hind feet (30-34 mm), olive-brown dorsal pelage with a distinct dark median stripe bordered by brighter yellowish-brown lateral bands, white underparts; overall appearance bright and colorful compared to other small rodents

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Moist meadows, riparian zones, marshes, stream banks with dense herbaceous cover; strongly associated with skunk cabbage marshes and wet meadow communities west of the Cascades
- Elevation range: Sea level to 1,800 m
- Ecoregion: Riparian, Lowland Forest (wet margins), Montane (wet meadows)
- Home range: 0.5-2.0 ha

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds (grasses, forbs), insects (caterpillars, beetles), and subterranean fungal sporocarps (endogone); remarkably high fungus consumption distinguishes this species from eastern *Zapus*
- Foraging strategy: Primarily nocturnal; forages in dense ground cover; must accumulate massive fat reserves (doubling body mass) prior to hibernation

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: May-July (immediately following emergence from hibernation)
- Gestation: 18-21 days — among the shortest of any PNW rodent
- Litter size: 4-8 (typically 5-6)
- Sexual maturity: Following first hibernation (~1 year)
- Lifespan: 2-4 years

**Social Structure:** Solitary; no evidence of pair bonding or communal behavior.

**Hibernation:** Pacific jumping mice are obligate hibernators entering torpor in September-October and not emerging until May-June — a hibernation period of 7-8 months, among the longest of any North American mammal. During torpor, body temperature drops to within 1-2 degrees C of ambient, heart rate falls from 300+ to 5-10 beats per minute, and respiration slows to one breath per several minutes. Animals that fail to accumulate sufficient pre-hibernation fat reserves (minimum ~35% body fat) do not survive winter.

**Ecological Role:** Important fungal spore disperser in wet meadow and riparian ecosystems. The species' consumption of endogone fungi (Endogonales) and subsequent deposition of viable spores in fecal pellets across the landscape facilitates mycorrhizal inoculation of riparian plant communities. Also significant prey for long-tailed weasels, mink, and owls during the active season.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Zapus trinotatus* is a PNW near-endemic — its range extends from southern BC through western WA and OR to northwestern California, entirely west of the Cascade crest. The genus *Zapus* is the sole North American representative of the family Dipodidae (otherwise known for the jerboas of Central Asian deserts). The saltatorial locomotion, elongated hind limbs, and extreme hibernation are ancestral dipodid traits adapted to seasonal environments.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Maser, Trappe & Nussbaum (1978) — fungal-small mammal interrelationships; Krutzsch (1954) — *Zapus* hibernation physiology

## 4. Castoridae — Beaver

### American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*)

**KEYSTONE SPECIES — Extended Treatment**

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Castoridae
**Status:** Resident — recovering throughout PNW after historical over-exploitation
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Furbearer (managed); populations recovered and expanding
- OR: Furbearer; state mammal since 1969 — "The Beaver State"
- ID: Furbearer; common
- BC: Furbearer; abundant

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 60-80 cm (head-body); tail 20-35 cm (flattened, scaly, paddle-shaped)
- Mass: 16-32 kg (adults); exceptional individuals exceed 40 kg — the largest rodent in North America and second-largest in the world (after the South American capybara)
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; females may average slightly larger than males in some populations — an unusual reversal among rodents
- Distinguishing features: The American beaver is unmistakable. Massive flat paddle-shaped tail covered in dark leathery scales, used as a rudder, fat storage organ, prop when standing upright, and alarm signal (tail-slap on water audible at 100+ meters). Dense underfur (800+ hairs per cm2) overlain by long glossy guard hairs — the densest fur of any North American mammal, waterproof and thermally efficient to below -30 degrees C. Pelage typically dark brown, varying from yellowish-brown to nearly black. Hind feet large and fully webbed; forefeet small and dexterous, used for manipulating sticks and food. Orange enamel on the anterior surface of incisors is iron-reinforced, self-sharpening (softer dentine wears faster than enamel, maintaining a chisel edge), and grows continuously at ~12 cm per year. Castor sacs (paired anal glands) produce castoreum, a complex aromatic secretion used in territorial scent-marking that has been commercially valued for centuries in perfumery and folk medicine. Clear nictitating membrane covers eyes underwater. Lips close behind incisors, allowing underwater gnawing. Nose and ear valves seal during dives. Oversized liver and lungs support 15-minute dive capacity.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Streams, rivers, lakes, ponds, marshes, and irrigation canals with adjacent deciduous or mixed forest providing food and building material. Optimal beaver habitat has year-round water, gentle stream gradients (< 6%), and riparian corridors of willow, alder, cottonwood, or aspen within 50-100 m of water
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 m; present from coastal estuaries to subalpine meadow streams
- Ecoregion: Riparian/Wetland/Estuarine (primary); extends into Lowland, Foothill, Montane, and Steppe wherever water courses exist
- Home range: 0.5-2.5 km of stream length; territories defended against neighboring families
- Lodge and bank den: Two denning strategies — (1) lodge: conical pile of sticks, mud, rocks, and vegetation built in pond or lake, 1.5-3 m above waterline, with underwater entrances; walls up to 1 m thick provide insulation and predator protection; interior chamber above water level, ~1 m diameter, lined with wood shavings; (2) bank den: burrow excavated into stream bank with underwater entrance, used on larger rivers where dam-building is not feasible
- Dam construction: Beavers build dams across streams to impound water deep enough to cover lodge entrances and protect food caches from freezing. Dams range from 1 m to over 850 m in length (the longest known beaver dam, in Alberta, is visible from satellite). In the PNW, most dams are 5-50 m long and 1-2 m high. Materials include sticks, branches, logs (up to 20 cm diameter), mud, rocks, vegetation, and whatever else is available. Dams are maintained continuously — beavers respond to the sound of running water and will attempt to plug any leak they can hear, even placing mud on a speaker playing recorded water sounds.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — bark and cambium of deciduous trees (willow, alder, cottonwood, aspen, birch), aquatic plants (water lilies, pondweed, sedges), forbs, grasses, ferns, and in agricultural areas, crops
- Foraging strategy: Primarily crepuscular and nocturnal; fells trees by gnawing a circumferential groove, preferentially selecting stems 2-15 cm diameter within 50 m of water. Larger trees (up to 1 m diameter) are felled occasionally but primarily for dam material rather than food. Beavers drag cut branches to water, where bark is consumed and stripped wood is incorporated into dams or lodges.
- Food cache: In autumn, beavers construct an underwater food cache ("larder") of fresh-cut branches anchored in the mud bottom near the lodge. This submerged cache provides winter food accessible from beneath ice without surface exposure — a critical adaptation in the PNW's cold montane and east-side environments.
- Cecal digestion: Like rabbits, beavers practice cecotrophy (re-ingestion of cecal pellets) to extract maximum nutrition from cellulose-rich bark. A specialized cecum houses cellulolytic bacteria that break down woody fiber.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Monogamous — socially and genetically. Pair bonds persist for life (up to 20+ years); among the strongest pair bonds documented in any rodent
- Breeding season: January-March; mating occurs in water or in the lodge
- Gestation: 105-110 days — exceptionally long for a rodent, reflecting precocial development
- Litter size: 2-6 kits (typically 3-4), born fully furred with eyes open, capable of swimming within hours of birth
- Weaning: 6-8 weeks, but kits remain with parents for 2 years before dispersing
- Sexual maturity: 2-3 years
- Lifespan: 10-20 years in the wild; up to 24 years documented
- Family unit: Beaver colonies are extended family groups typically comprising the adult breeding pair, yearlings from the previous year's litter, and kits of the year — 4-8 individuals per colony. Two-year-olds disperse to establish new territories, sometimes traveling 10-20 km along waterways.

**Social Structure:** The beaver family (colony) is the fundamental social unit — a monogamous pair and their offspring of two age classes. Colony members cooperate in dam maintenance, lodge construction, and food caching. Communication includes tail-slaps (alarm), vocalizations (whines, hisses), and scent-marking with castoreum on mud mounds at territory boundaries. Territorial disputes with neighboring colonies are resolved through scent-marking contests and, rarely, physical combat that can result in serious injury or death.

**Population:**
- Historical: Pre-European beaver populations in the PNW are estimated at several million animals. The Columbia River watershed alone may have supported 500,000-1,000,000 beavers.
- Fur trade era: The Hudson's Bay Company's "fur desert" policy (1820s-1840s), under Chief Factor John McLoughlin and led by Peter Skene Ogden's Snake River Expeditions, systematically trapped beavers to near-extinction across the Oregon Country to create a buffer zone discouraging American settlement. By the 1840s, beaver were functionally extirpated from most of the Columbia Basin.
- Modern: Populations have recovered substantially since trapping regulation began in the early 20th century. Current WA population estimated at 10,000-20,000; OR population estimated at several tens of thousands; BC population robust and widespread. Recovery is ongoing but populations remain far below pre-European levels — perhaps 10-20% of historical numbers.
- Trend: Increasing in most of the PNW as attitudes shift from pest management to ecological restoration
- Threats: Trapping (legal in all PNW jurisdictions as furbearer); road mortality; lethal removal for flood control and agricultural conflicts; habitat loss (stream channelization, riparian clearing, urbanization); climate change reducing summer streamflows in some basins
- Source: [G9], [G11], Müller-Schwarze 2011, Pollock et al. 2014

**Ecological Role — Ecosystem Engineering:**

The American beaver is the preeminent ecosystem engineer among North American mammals. No other species (except humans) modifies its physical environment to the extent that beavers do. Beaver dams and the pond complexes they create fundamentally alter hydrology, geomorphology, water chemistry, vegetation, and biodiversity at the watershed scale. The ecological effects cascade through trophic levels and across ecosystem boundaries.

*Hydrological engineering:*
Beaver dams impound water, raising water tables, reconnecting streams to floodplains, and creating wetland complexes where none previously existed. A single beaver dam can raise the local water table by 0.5-2.0 m across an area extending 50-200 m from the pond margin. In degraded incised streams — common throughout the east-side PNW where overgrazing and channel straightening have lowered water tables — beaver dam analogs (BDAs, human-built structures mimicking beaver dams) and actual beaver reintroduction are now recognized restoration tools. Pollock et al. (2014) demonstrated that beaver dams in incised streams in the John Day River basin (OR) reconnected streams to floodplains, raised water tables, and converted deeply incised channels to multi-thread anastomosing systems within 5-10 years.

*Water storage and drought resilience:*
Beaver ponds store water during high-flow periods and release it slowly during summer baseflow. Hood & Bayley (2008) found that beaver ponds in Alberta increased total open-water area by 9x during drought years compared to undammed watersheds. In the drought-prone east-side PNW — where summer streamflows are declining under climate change — beaver-created water storage is increasingly recognized as a natural climate adaptation. Beaver ponds maintain cool water temperatures through groundwater recharge, benefiting cold-water salmonids.

*Salmon habitat:*
Beaver ponds provide rearing habitat for juvenile coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*), juvenile steelhead (*O. mykiss*), and other salmonids. Pond complexes increase stream habitat complexity — deep pools, side channels, backwater areas, and overhanging cover all benefit juvenile fish survival. In coastal Washington and Oregon streams, coho juveniles that rear in beaver ponds grow faster and survive overwinter at higher rates than those in unponded reaches. The historical loss of beaver from PNW watersheds removed millions of acres of off-channel salmon rearing habitat — restoring beaver populations is now a component of salmon recovery plans in multiple watersheds.

*Riparian vegetation and biodiversity:*
Beaver pond margins develop wet meadow, marsh, and willow thicket communities that support species assemblages absent from free-flowing stream reaches. Amphibians (red-legged frog, Pacific tree frog, rough-skinned newt), waterfowl (wood duck, hooded merganser, mallard), wading birds (great blue heron), and wetland songbirds (common yellowthroat, marsh wren) all benefit from beaver-created habitat. The standing dead trees (snags) killed by beaver pond flooding provide cavity nesting sites for woodpeckers, swallows, and owls. Beaver ponds in the PNW support 2-5x the bird species richness of equivalent-length unponded stream reaches.

*Nutrient cycling and water quality:*
Beaver ponds function as sediment traps and nutrient processors. Fine sediment settles in the low-velocity pond environment, and the accumulated organic material supports dense communities of aquatic invertebrates. Nitrogen cycling in beaver pond sediments reduces downstream nutrient loading. When beaver dams are eventually breached (naturally or through trapping), the exposed pond bottom — rich in organic sediment — becomes a highly productive beaver meadow that supports diverse plant communities for decades to centuries.

*Carbon storage:*
Recent research estimates that beaver pond sediments store 2-8x more carbon per unit area than equivalent free-flowing stream reaches. The global loss of beaver populations over the past 400 years may have released significant quantities of stored carbon, making beaver recovery a potential (modest) component of carbon sequestration.

*Fire resilience:*
In fire-prone east-side PNW landscapes, beaver pond complexes function as fire refugia. The elevated water table and wet riparian vegetation surrounding beaver ponds resist burning even in severe wildfire. Post-fire, beaver complexes serve as seed sources and wildlife refugia from which recolonization proceeds. In the aftermath of recent large fires in eastern Oregon and Washington, riparian areas with active beaver colonies retained green vegetation and flowing water while surrounding uplands burned.

**Cultural Significance:**

The beaver holds deep cultural significance across PNW Indigenous nations. For Coast Salish peoples including the Tulalip, Snoqualmie, and Puyallup, beaver is recognized as a transformer figure whose dam-building altered the landscape and created the wetland environments that supported camas, wapato, and waterfowl — foods central to Coast Salish subsistence and ceremonial life. Chinook and Sahaptin-speaking peoples of the Columbia Plateau recognized the beaver's role in creating the productive river-margin habitats that supported salmon and root gathering. The Syilx (Okanagan) people describe beaver as a relative who works the water, and traditional management practices included periodic breaching of beaver dams to maintain fish passage while preserving the broader pond complex — a form of ecosystem management that predates modern beaver dam analog (BDA) restoration by millennia.

Beaver pelts were the primary commodity of the Pacific Northwest fur trade (1780s-1840s). The Hudson's Bay Company extracted an estimated 3-5 million pelts from the Columbia Department between 1820 and 1850. The ecological consequences were catastrophic: the removal of beavers from virtually every watershed in the region triggered stream incision, water table decline, wetland loss, and the collapse of the beaver-mediated hydrology that had shaped PNW landscapes for millennia. The legacy of beaver extirpation — degraded streams, incised channels, dried meadows — persists across the PNW today and drives the modern beaver restoration movement.

Oregon adopted the beaver as its state mammal in 1969 and features the animal on its state flag. Oregon State University's athletic teams are the Beavers. The cultural resonance of the beaver in Oregon reflects both the ecological centrality of the species and the historical role of the fur trade in shaping the state's Euro-American settlement.

**Evolutionary Note:** The family Castoridae originated in the late Eocene (~37 Ma) in North America. The fossil record includes numerous genera, many much larger than the living species. The giant beaver (*Castoroides ohioensis*) of the Pleistocene reached 2.5 m in length and 100 kg — the largest rodent ever to live in North America — and went extinct approximately 10,000 years ago, likely due to climate change and loss of its preferred wetland habitat. *Castoroides* had enormous incisors (15 cm long) with ridged enamel but, despite its size, may not have built dams — its skull morphology suggests it was more of a grazer than a woodcutter. The sole surviving genus, *Castor*, comprises two species: *C. canadensis* (North America) and *C. fiber* (Eurasia). The two diverged approximately 7.5 Ma and are not known to interbreed despite similar morphology. *Castor canadensis* was introduced to Europe and South America in the 20th century, where it competes with (and potentially hybridizes with) *C. fiber* in some areas — a conservation concern for European beaver recovery programs.

## 5. Aplodontidae — Mountain Beaver

### Mountain Beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*)

**LIVING FOSSIL — Extended Treatment**

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Aplodontidae
**Status:** Resident — PNW endemic
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (subspecies *A. r. nigra* in California is federally endangered, but PNW populations are secure)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status; locally common
- OR: No special status; locally common
- BC: Red-listed (at risk; range restricted to extreme southwestern BC near the Skagit River)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 30-47 cm (head-body); tail vestigial, 2-4 cm — barely visible externally
- Mass: 800-1,800 g (typically 1,000-1,400 g)
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal
- Distinguishing features: Stocky, compact body with dense dark brown fur; very short tail (essentially absent); small rounded ears; small eyes; short powerful limbs with strong claws adapted for digging; overall appearance vaguely reminiscent of a large vole or a very small beaver but in fact resembling no other living rodent closely. Despite the common name, *Aplodontia rufa* is not related to beavers (Castoridae), does not live in water, and does not build dams. The name "mountain beaver" is a historical misnomer applied by early settlers who encountered the animal's burrow systems near streams and associated them with beaver activity. Alternative common names include "boomer" (from its vocalizations) and "sewellel" (from the Chinook Jargon word *shéwallal*, referring to a robe made from mountain beaver pelts).

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense, moist forest understory, typically in areas with deep, moist soils and lush ground cover of sword fern, salal, vine maple, and other moisture-loving vegetation. Strongly associated with seeps, springs, and areas of high soil moisture on steep slopes. Rarely found in dry habitats or far from permanent moisture sources. The species requires constant access to water — it has the most primitive kidneys of any living mammal (see Evolutionary Note) and cannot concentrate urine, necessitating daily water intake exceeding 1/3 of body weight.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 2,200 m; most abundant at 500-1,500 m on the west slope of the Cascades and Olympics
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest (primary), Lowland Forest (wet areas), Riparian, Fossorial (extensive burrow systems)
- Home range: 0.04-0.4 ha — very small, reflecting the high food and water density of optimal habitat
- Burrow system: Mountain beavers are obligate burrowers, constructing extensive tunnel systems 15-90 cm below the soil surface. Tunnels may extend 50-100 m in total length per individual, with multiple entrances (5-30 per system), food storage chambers, nest chambers, and latrine chambers. Tunnels are typically 15-20 cm in diameter with earth pushed out to form characteristic fan-shaped "kickouts" at tunnel entrances — a reliable field sign. Burrow systems are reused across generations and may persist for decades.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — sword fern (the single most important food plant), bracken fern, salal, vine maple leaves and bark, stinging nettle, fireweed, Douglas-fir seedlings and saplings, rhododendron, and a wide variety of forbs and shrubs
- Foraging strategy: Primarily nocturnal and crepuscular; emerges from burrows to clip vegetation near tunnel entrances, dragging cut material underground to feeding or drying chambers. In a distinctive behavior, mountain beavers create "haypiles" — collections of cut vegetation placed at tunnel entrances to wilt before being pulled underground for consumption or storage. This behavior is convergent with pika haypiling (see Section 6.1) and serves the same function: reducing water content and improving storability of green vegetation.
- Forestry conflict: Mountain beavers clip and consume Douglas-fir seedlings in reforestation sites, and their feeding can significantly reduce seedling survival in young plantations. This has historically made them a target for lethal control on managed timber lands. A single mountain beaver can clip 200-500 seedlings per year.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Solitary; brief pair formation during breeding
- Breeding season: January-March (one of the earliest-breeding rodents in the PNW)
- Gestation: 28-30 days — short relative to body size
- Litter size: 2-4 (typically 2-3), born altricial in an underground nest chamber lined with dry vegetation
- Weaning: 6-8 weeks
- Sexual maturity: 2 years
- Lifespan: 5-10 years (documented up to 12 years)

**Social Structure:** Solitary and territorial. Each individual maintains its own burrow system and defends it against conspecifics except during breeding season. Population density in optimal habitat reaches 5-20 individuals per hectare — high for a rodent of this body size, reflecting the species' restricted movement and small home ranges.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in core range (western WA and OR); potentially declining in BC (red-listed)
- Estimate: No regional population estimate; locally abundant in suitable habitat throughout the west slope of the Cascades, Olympic Peninsula, Oregon Coast Range, and Klamath Mountains
- Threats: Timber harvest that removes moist understory habitat; lethal control on managed forest lands; climate change reducing soil moisture on marginal sites; BC population is small and isolated
- Source: [P1], [P2], [G9]

**Ecological Role:** Mountain beaver burrows create soil macropores that influence subsurface water flow, reduce surface erosion on steep slopes, and provide shelter for numerous commensals including salamanders (ensatina, western red-backed salamander), shrews, deer mice, weasels, and various invertebrates. The tunneling activity aerates soil and mixes organic material into the soil profile, functioning as a small-scale analog of pocket gopher soil engineering. Mountain beaver foraging — particularly the clipping of dense shrub and fern understory — creates small openings that promote plant diversity in otherwise closed-canopy forests. The species is prey for bobcat, coyote, great horned owl, and particularly the fisher, which can pursue mountain beavers into their burrow systems.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Aplodontia rufa* is the sole surviving member of the family Aplodontidae, which is universally recognized as the most primitive (basal) living family of rodents. The family originated in the Eocene (~40 Ma) in North America and was once diverse, with over 30 described fossil genera distributed across North America and Eurasia. All aplodontids except *Aplodontia* went extinct by the Miocene (~5 Ma), making the mountain beaver a genuine living fossil — the last survivor of the most ancient rodent lineage on Earth.

The primitive characters of *Aplodontia* are remarkable: it retains the simplest kidney architecture of any living mammal (no renal papilla, short loops of Henle), rendering it incapable of producing concentrated urine. This physiological constraint — shared with no other living terrestrial mammal — ties the species absolutely to moist habitats and has likely prevented it from expanding its range into the drier interior of the continent. Its dental formula, skull morphology, jaw musculature, and brain structure are all more similar to Eocene fossil rodents than to any other living species. The masseter muscle attachment (sciuromorphous pattern) is the ancestral condition from which all other rodent jaw architectures are derived.

The mountain beaver's range — entirely within the PNW, from southern BC to the Sierra Nevada — makes it the only mammalian family endemic to western North America. No other living mammal family has such a restricted global distribution. The species has been aptly described as "the most primitive living rodent, belonging to the most primitive living rodent family, endemic to the most limited range of any rodent family on Earth" (Polly & Sarwar 2014).

**Cultural Note:** The Chinook Jargon term *shéwallal* (source of the alternate common name "sewellel") referred to a robe made from mountain beaver pelts sewn together — a labor-intensive garment that required many pelts due to the animal's small size. Lewis and Clark collected mountain beaver specimens during the 1805-1806 expedition and were among the first Euro-Americans to document the species. Clark described it as "the Sewelel which the natives make great use of the skins."

**Cross-Links:**
- Fossorial zone: Burrow systems parallel pocket gopher engineering but in wetter, steeper terrain
- Fisher-mountain beaver predator-prey: Fisher is one of few predators that can pursue mountain beavers underground
- Forestry interface: Seedling damage makes mountain beaver one of the few native rodents with direct economic impact on timber management

## 6. Erethizontidae — New World Porcupines

### North American Porcupine (*Erethizon dorsatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Erethizontidae
**Status:** Resident — widespread but patchily distributed
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status
- OR: No special status
- ID: No special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 64-86 cm (head-body); tail 15-30 cm, thick and muscular
- Mass: 3.5-18 kg (typically 5-10 kg); considerable seasonal variation — heaviest in autumn
- Sexual dimorphism: Males slightly larger
- Distinguishing features: The porcupine's defining character is its armament — approximately 30,000 modified hairs (quills) covering the dorsum, flanks, and tail. Individual quills are 2-12 cm long, yellow-white with dark tips, loosely attached in the skin, and tipped with microscopic backward-facing barbs that cause quills to work deeper into tissue once embedded. Quills are not thrown or shot — they detach on contact when the porcupine swings its tail or backs into a threat. The face, belly, and inner limbs lack quills. Pelage beneath quills is dark brown to black with a grizzled appearance from long guard hairs. Gait is a slow, flat-footed waddle. Hind feet have four toes; forefeet have four fingers and a vestigial thumb. The porcupine's prehensile pebbly-soled feet and curved claws are excellent arboreal adaptations.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Coniferous and mixed forest; most abundant in montane conifer forests with rock outcrops for denning; uses riparian cottonwood galleries on the east-side steppe
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,500 m
- Ecoregion: Montane, Lowland Forest, Foothill, Riparian, Steppe (cottonwood galleries); avoids dense canopy without rocky den sites
- Home range: 5-40 ha; seasonal shifts between winter conifer stands and summer deciduous/riparian areas
- Denning: Rock crevices, hollow logs, caves, abandoned buildings; does not dig its own den. Latrine behavior — accumulations of fecal pellets (distinctive oval, light brown, fibrous) mark den sites and are a reliable field sign.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — bark and cambium of trees and shrubs (inner bark of hemlock, fir, pine, and spruce in winter; leaves, buds, and catkins of deciduous trees in summer); aquatic plants, forbs, and agricultural crops seasonally
- Foraging strategy: Primarily nocturnal and crepuscular; arboreal feeder — climbs trees and strips bark from branches and upper trunk, often girdling and killing individual trees. Winter feeding concentration on a few preferred trees produces distinctive patches of exposed light-colored sapwood visible from a distance. Porcupines chew antlers, bones, and plywood for mineral (sodium) content — the sodium drive is intense and leads porcupines to gnaw on tool handles, canoe paddles, boots, and other sweat-soaked objects at backcountry camps.
- Sodium ecology: The spring-summer diet of green vegetation provides excess potassium, creating a physiological sodium deficit. This drives the intense sodium-seeking behavior that draws porcupines to road salt, mineral licks, and human equipment.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Polygynous; males compete through vocalizations and urine-spraying; female receptive for only 8-12 hours per year
- Breeding season: October-November
- Gestation: 205-217 days — the longest of any rodent in North America, producing the most precocial rodent young in the region
- Litter size: Single young (twins extremely rare) — the lowest fecundity of any North American rodent
- Young: Born fully quilled (quills soft and pliable at birth, hardening within hours), eyes open, capable of climbing within days
- Weaning: 4-5 months but may remain near mother through first winter
- Sexual maturity: Females 18-24 months; males 24-30 months
- Lifespan: 10-18 years — among the longest-lived rodents in North America

**Social Structure:** Solitary; no pair bonding beyond mating. Winter den sharing occurs but is not cooperative — multiple individuals may use the same rock crevice without social interaction. Home ranges overlap extensively and are not defended.

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining across much of the western range; the cause is not fully understood but fisher reintroduction, changing forest structure, and vehicle mortality are implicated
- Estimate: No regional estimate; density typically 1-10 per sq km in suitable habitat
- Threats: Fisher predation (fishers are specialized porcupine predators — they attack the quill-free face repeatedly until the porcupine weakens); vehicle mortality on roads treated with de-icing salt (sodium attraction draws porcupines to roadsides); habitat loss in mature forest; lead poisoning from ingestion of lead-contaminated objects
- Source: [P1], [P2], [P4]

**Ecological Role:** Porcupine bark-stripping creates wounds that become entry points for heart-rot fungi, eventually producing snag cavities used by cavity-nesting birds and mammals. In this indirect manner, porcupine feeding contributes to the creation of wildlife trees. Porcupine-killed treetops create small canopy gaps that stimulate understory growth. The quill defense makes porcupines a low-predation-risk prey item for most predators — only fishers, mountain lions, and wolverines regularly prey on porcupines. This predator-release, combined with long lifespan and low reproductive rate, gives porcupines an unusual life history strategy among rodents — more similar to ungulates than to typical rodents.

**Evolutionary Note:** Erethizontidae is a New World family (4 genera, ~18 species, mostly Neotropical) that originated in South America and dispersed into North America during the Great American Biotic Interchange (~3 Ma). *Erethizon dorsatum* is the sole North American representative and the only porcupine to have colonized boreal and temperate forests. The quill defense is convergent with Old World porcupines (Hystricidae), which evolved quills independently — the two families are not closely related. New World porcupines retain arboreal adaptations (prehensile tails in tropical species, climbing feet in *Erethizon*) inherited from their South American ancestors, while Old World porcupines are primarily terrestrial.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Roze (2009) — *The North American Porcupine*; Sweitzer & Berger (1997) — fisher-porcupine interactions

## 7. Order Lagomorpha

The lagomorphs — pikas, rabbits, and hares — were once classified with rodents but are now recognized as a distinct order separated by over 80 million years of independent evolution. The key morphological distinction is the presence of a second pair of small peg-like upper incisors behind the large anterior pair (paucidentate condition), a character absent in all rodents. Other lagomorph traits include fully furred soles of the feet, a cecum larger relative to body size than in any rodent, and obligate cecotrophy (consumption of soft cecal pellets to extract nutrients from the first passage of food — functionally analogous to cud-chewing in ruminants).

Eight lagomorph species occur in the PNW, spanning two families: one pika (Ochotonidae) and seven rabbits and hares (Leporidae). Together they occupy habitats from alpine talus to sagebrush steppe, from coastal thickets to montane forest — and one species, the American pika, has become the poster animal for climate change impacts on mountain ecosystems.

### 7.1 Ochotonidae — Pikas

#### American Pika (*Ochotona princeps*)

**CLIMATE SENTINEL — Extended Treatment**

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Ochotonidae
**Status:** Resident — alpine and subalpine talus
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petition denied 2010; USFWS concluded listing not warranted despite climate concerns); considered a candidate by some conservation groups
- IUCN: Least Concern (global); regional populations declining
- WA: No special status; monitored as climate indicator
- OR: Strategy species
- ID: No special status
- BC: Blue-listed (Special Concern)

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 16-22 cm (head-body); no visible tail (< 2 cm, concealed in fur)
- Mass: 120-185 g (typically 150-170 g)
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; sexes indistinguishable in the field by size
- Distinguishing features: Small, compact, round-bodied lagomorph with no visible tail, short rounded ears (25-30 mm, edged with white), dense soft fur (grayish-brown to cinnamon, varying geographically), large dark eyes, and prominent vibrissae. The pika's proportions — short limbs, round body, concealed tail — minimize surface-area-to-volume ratio for heat conservation. The species is so distinctive that field identification is unmistakable: no other PNW mammal combines the round earless-looking profile, rock-dwelling habitat, and piercing nasal call.
- Vocalizations: The sharp, high-pitched nasal *"eeeenk!"* call — typically a single note repeated at intervals — is the most reliable detection method and is audible at 300+ meters across talus slopes. Males and females both call. A short call functions as territorial advertisement; a series of rapidly repeated calls serves as an alarm. The call echoes off rock faces, creating the ventriloquial quality noted by early naturalists.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Talus slopes, boulder fields, rock piles, and broken rock with interstitial spaces for shelter and haypile storage. Talus patches must have sufficient depth (>1 m) and interstitial volume to provide thermal refugia, escape from predators, and protected cache sites. Adjacent vegetation — alpine meadow, subalpine parkland, or montane grassland — must be within foraging distance (typically <30 m from talus edge).
- Elevation range: 1,500-4,000 m in the PNW; as low as 500 m in the northern Cascades and coastal mountains where marine influence moderates temperatures, and up to the highest talus on Mount Rainier, Mount Adams, and the Idaho Batholith
- Ecoregion: Alpine/Subalpine (primary), Montane (upper elevations)
- Home range: 410-700 sq m of talus — among the smallest home ranges of any North American mammal
- Critical microhabitat: Pikas are obligate talus dwellers. The interstices between rocks provide a thermally buffered environment 10-20 degrees C cooler than surface air temperature on hot summer days. Pikas use this thermal refuge to avoid overheating — they have an extremely narrow thermal tolerance and die of hyperthermia within 6 hours of exposure to temperatures above 25.5-28 degrees C (depending on population). This thermal dependency is the mechanism underlying their climate sensitivity.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — grasses, sedges, forbs, and cushion plants from adjacent alpine meadows; also lichens, mosses, and bark. Pikas consume a remarkably diverse diet — over 100 plant species documented.
- Foraging strategy: Diurnal (unusual among small mammals); makes rapid foraging sorties from talus into adjacent meadow, clipping vegetation and carrying it back in a characteristic "moustache" of plant material protruding from both sides of the mouth.
- Haypile (hay pile): The pika's signature behavior. During summer and early fall, pikas harvest and cache large quantities of vegetation ("hay") in protected sites within the talus. A single pika may store 15-28 kg (dry weight) of hay per season — equivalent to a bushel basket of dried plants. Haypiles are placed on protected rock surfaces within the talus where air circulation promotes drying while rain and snow are deflected by overhanging rocks. The cached hay provides food through the winter when pikas remain active beneath snowpack (pikas do not hibernate). Haypile composition is deliberately curated: pikas preferentially harvest plants with natural antimicrobial compounds (e.g., *Ligusticum*, *Eriogonum*) that retard spoilage, and they avoid plants that rot quickly. Old haypile sites, stained with years of urine and fecal pellets, are visible as orange-brown patches on talus rocks — a reliable field indicator of pika occupancy.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Monogamous within a breeding season; pairs occupy adjacent territories along the talus edge
- Breeding season: April-June (immediately after snowmelt)
- Gestation: 30 days
- Litter size: 2-6 (typically 2-3); up to 2 litters per year at lower elevations, typically 1 at high elevation
- Weaning: 30 days; juveniles disperse to find vacant talus territory within 2 months — dispersal is the most dangerous period, requiring movement across open ground between talus patches
- Sexual maturity: 3-4 months (but usually does not breed until the following spring)
- Lifespan: 3-7 years (typically 3-4 years)

**Social Structure:** Pikas are territorial and solitary, each individual defending a section of talus edge against same-sex conspecifics. Males and females hold adjacent territories with minimal overlap. Communication is primarily vocal (territory calls) and olfactory (cheek-gland rubbing on rocks). Population density in optimal habitat reaches 5-15 individuals per hectare of talus, but each animal defends its individual territory aggressively.

**Population & Climate Response:**

The American pika has become one of the most intensively studied mammals in the context of climate change. Multiple long-term monitoring studies across the western United States have documented population declines, range contractions, and local extinctions that correlate with warming temperatures.

*Range contraction:*
Beever et al. (2003, 2011) documented extirpation of pika populations from 7 of 25 historically occupied sites in the Great Basin (NV, OR, CA) between 1994 and 2008, with extinctions concentrated at lower elevations and in warmer microhabitats. The mean lower elevational limit of occupied sites shifted upward by approximately 150 m between surveys. In the Oregon Cascades, pikas persist at most historically occupied sites but monitoring shows declining occupancy at lower-elevation margins.

*Thermal mechanism:*
Pikas are physiologically intolerant of sustained high temperatures. Their basal metabolic rate is high relative to body size, their dense fur (adapted for -30 degree C alpine winters) cannot be shed seasonally, and they lack effective evaporative cooling mechanisms. Laboratory studies show acute lethal hyperthermia at 25.5-28 degrees C after 6 hours of exposure. In the field, pikas avoid overheating by retreating into cool talus interstices during warm hours and restricting foraging to cooler periods (early morning, late afternoon, overcast days). As summer temperatures increase, the foraging window narrows, potentially preventing pikas from harvesting sufficient hay to survive winter.

*Snow cover:*
Paradoxically, pikas also depend on cold-season snow. The snowpack insulates talus from extreme winter cold (-30 to -40 degrees C surface temperatures), maintaining subnivean temperatures near 0 degrees C where pikas remain active. Reduced snowpack exposes pikas to temperature extremes and may also reduce the soil moisture that supports the meadow vegetation they depend on for food.

*Metapopulation dynamics:*
Pikas exist as metapopulations — clusters of small populations on individual talus patches connected by occasional dispersal across inhospitable matrix (meadow, forest, bare rock). As lower-elevation patches become too warm and are abandoned, the metapopulation loses connectivity. Dispersal between patches requires crossing open ground where pikas are vulnerable to predators (weasels, hawks, eagles) and thermal stress. The maximum documented dispersal distance is approximately 2-3 km, and most dispersal occurs over < 500 m — meaning pikas cannot rapidly colonize distant suitable habitat as climate pushes their zone upslope.

*PNW outlook:*
In the PNW specifically, the prognosis is mixed. The maritime climate of the Cascades and Olympics moderates summer extremes compared to the continental Great Basin, and PNW pikas occupy a broader elevational range (down to 500 m in some coastal mountain areas) than their Great Basin counterparts. However, climate projections suggest continued warming and reduced snowpack across the PNW alpine zone. Pikas in the Blue Mountains of eastern Oregon and the southern margins of the Idaho Batholith are most vulnerable. Cascadian and Olympic populations are likely more resilient but face long-term contraction toward summits.

**Ecological Role:** Pikas are a significant food source for weasels (particularly long-tailed weasel and ermine, which can follow pikas into talus interstices), marten, golden eagle, red-tailed hawk, and great horned owl. Pika haypiles that are not fully consumed decompose and fertilize the talus-meadow ecotone, contributing to soil development in harsh alpine environments. Pika foraging selectively removes certain plant species from meadows adjacent to talus, influencing plant community composition. The pika's role as a climate sentinel — a conspicuous, charismatic, easily monitored species with well-understood thermal physiology — gives it outsized importance for communicating climate change impacts to the public.

**Evolutionary Note:** The family Ochotonidae originated in the Oligocene (~30 Ma) in Asia and was once far more diverse than today, with 30+ fossil genera across Eurasia, Africa, and North America. The single living genus *Ochotona* includes approximately 30 species, nearly all in Central and East Asia (the "steppe pikas" and "rock pikas" of Mongolia, China, and the Himalayas). *O. princeps* and the collared pika (*O. collaris*) are the only two species in North America — relict populations separated from their Asian relatives by the flooding of Beringia. The pika's restriction to mountaintop talus in the lower 48 states is a post-glacial phenomenon: during the Pleistocene, pikas occupied lowland habitats across the Great Basin, and their retreat to alpine refugia as the climate warmed mirrors the story of other cold-adapted species.

**Cultural Note:** Multiple PNW Indigenous nations recognized the pika's haypiling behavior. The Ktunaxa (Kootenai) people observed pika hay-gathering as a seasonal indicator of approaching winter. The pika's distinctive call is one of the most recognizable sounds of the PNW alpine environment, earning it the Chinook Jargon nickname "little chief hare" (*Ochotona princeps* — literally "prince of the pikas," named by John Richardson in 1828).

**Cross-Links:**
- Alpine zone indicator: Pika occupancy tracks climate change with high spatial resolution
- Predator-prey: Weasel, marten, golden eagle Module 1 cross-link
- AVI companion: White-tailed ptarmigan and rosy-finch share alpine talus/meadow habitat

(2003, 2011) — pika extirpations and climate; Hafner & Smith (2010) — pika biogeography; Ray, Beever & Rodhouse (2016) — pika monitoring framework

### 7.2 Leporidae — Rabbits and Hares

The Leporidae in the PNW include true hares (genus *Lepus*) and cottontail rabbits (genus *Sylvilagus*), plus the monotypic pygmy rabbit (*Brachylagus*). The ecological distinction between hares and rabbits is fundamental: hares are born fully furred, eyes open, capable of running within hours (precocial), and typically occupy open habitats where flight is the primary anti-predator strategy. Rabbits are born naked, blind, and helpless (altricial) in underground burrows or concealed nests, relying on crypsis and the protection of dense cover. This developmental dichotomy shapes habitat selection, anti-predator behavior, body size, and population dynamics across the family.

---

#### Snowshoe Hare (*Lepus americanus*)

**LYNX CYCLE — Extended Treatment**

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — widespread in forest habitats
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Game species; common
- OR: Game species; common
- ID: Game species; common
- BC: Yellow-listed; abundant

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 36-52 cm (head-body); tail 3-5 cm
- Mass: 1.0-2.3 kg (typically 1.3-1.8 kg); seasonal variation with heaviest weights in autumn
- Sexual dimorphism: Females slightly larger than males (reversed sexual dimorphism, common in lagomorphs)
- Distinguishing features: Large hind feet (12-15 cm long) with dense fur on the soles — the "snowshoes" that give the species its common name, providing flotation on soft snow. The most distinctive feature is the seasonal pelage molt: in winter, the coat is entirely white (except for black ear tips); in summer, it is brown to rusty-brown. The molt is photoperiod-triggered (responding to day length, not temperature or snow) and takes approximately 70-90 days to complete — a timing mechanism that has become a vulnerability under climate change (see below). Ears shorter than other *Lepus* — an adaptation to reduce heat loss in cold environments.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense coniferous and mixed forest with heavy understory cover — dense thickets of young conifers (Douglas-fir, western redcedar, western hemlock), willow, alder, and regenerating clearcuts. Optimal habitat has stem density exceeding 2,000 stems/ha providing escape cover within a few body-lengths. Avoids open habitats where predation risk is extreme.
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 m; most abundant at 300-2,000 m in montane forest
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest (primary), Lowland Forest, Riparian, Alpine (subalpine forest margins)
- Home range: 2-20 ha; compressed in winter when deep snow restricts movement

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Herbivorous — summer diet is diverse green vegetation (grasses, forbs, clover, ferns, leaves of shrubs); winter diet shifts to woody browse (bark and twigs of willow, alder, birch, conifers, maple)
- Foraging strategy: Primarily crepuscular and nocturnal; forages along well-worn trails ("runs") through dense cover. Trail networks are shared among individuals and persist across seasons.
- Browse impact: During population peaks, snowshoe hares can strip bark and browse from virtually every accessible shrub and sapling within a stand, fundamentally altering vegetation structure. Heavy browsing during peak years can suppress willow and aspen regeneration for decades.

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Promiscuous; no pair bonding
- Breeding season: March-August; females produce 2-4 litters per season
- Gestation: 36-37 days
- Litter size: 1-7 (average 3-4 per litter); total annual reproductive output of 8-16 leverets per female
- Young: Born fully furred, eyes open, capable of hopping within 24 hours (precocial); leverets scatter individually and hide in vegetation — the mother visits each to nurse once daily for 25-28 days
- Sexual maturity: 1 year
- Lifespan: 1-5 years; few individuals survive more than 3 years at any point in the population cycle

**Social Structure:** Not truly social, but population density creates extensive home range overlap and shared trail systems. No territorial defense. Anti-predator behavior relies on crypsis (camouflaged pelage), freezing (remaining motionless when a predator is detected), and explosive flight along pre-established runways through dense cover.

**Population & the Lynx Cycle:**

The snowshoe hare is one pole of the most thoroughly documented predator-prey oscillation in ecology. Across the boreal forest from Alaska to Newfoundland, snowshoe hare populations cycle with a period of approximately 9-11 years, with peak densities 10-30x greater than trough densities. This cycle drives corresponding oscillations in the populations of their primary predator, the Canada lynx (*Lynx canadensis*), as well as great horned owl, goshawk, coyote, red fox, and other predators.

*Cycle mechanics:*
The hare cycle is driven by a delayed density-dependent interaction between hare populations, their food supply, and their predators. During the increase phase, hare populations grow rapidly (high reproduction, abundant food, low predation), reaching peak densities of 200-600 hares per sq km. Peak-phase browsing depletes preferred food plants, triggering a decline in body condition and reproduction. Simultaneously, predator populations increase in response to abundant prey. The decline phase is rapid and dramatic: hare populations crash by 80-95% over 1-3 years, driven by the combined effects of predation (now intense because predator populations peaked 1-2 years behind hare populations), food stress, and physiological stress (elevated cortisol, immunosuppression). The trough phase lasts 2-4 years during which predator populations decline from starvation and dispersal, food plants recover, and the cycle resets.

*PNW expression:*
The classic 10-year cycle is most pronounced in boreal forest. In the PNW, the cycle is present but attenuated — population fluctuations occur but with lower amplitude and less regularity than in the boreal. This damping effect may reflect the greater habitat and predator diversity of PNW forests: multiple predator species buffer the oscillation by switching to alternative prey when hare numbers are low, and the diverse vegetation base is less susceptible to total depletion. In the PNW, snowshoe hare populations at peak are typically 5-15x trough densities rather than the 30x booms seen in the boreal.

*Canada lynx in the PNW:*
The Canada lynx — whose population dynamics are tightly coupled to snowshoe hare abundance — occurs in the PNW only in northeastern Washington (Okanogan region), northern Idaho, and southern BC. Lynx are ESA-listed as Threatened in the contiguous US. In the PNW, the lynx population is small (estimated 50-200 animals in WA), and its persistence depends on snowshoe hare populations in montane and subalpine forest. The hare-lynx cycle creates conservation uncertainty: lynx must survive through the trough years when prey is scarce, and habitat fragmentation may prevent lynx from tracking hare populations across the landscape.

**Camouflage Mismatch:**

Climate change is disrupting the snowshoe hare's seasonal coat color molt — a phenomenon with potentially serious fitness consequences. The molt from brown to white (autumn) and white to brown (spring) is triggered by photoperiod and is not adjusted by snow conditions. As snowfall arrives later and snowmelt occurs earlier under warming climate, hares in white coats increasingly find themselves on brown, snow-free ground — a camouflage mismatch that makes them strikingly visible to predators.

Mills et al. (2013) and Zimova et al. (2016) quantified this mismatch and its fitness consequences. At study sites in Montana, the number of days of camouflage mismatch increased significantly over a 20-year period. Mismatched hares (white on brown ground) suffered 7% higher weekly mortality than matched hares. If mismatch continues to increase at current rates, the fitness cost could drive population declines independent of other factors.

The capacity for evolutionary rescue — natural selection favoring individuals whose molt timing tracks changing snow conditions — is an active research question. Some evidence suggests that molt timing has a genetic component and may be capable of rapid evolution, but whether adaptation can keep pace with the rate of climate change remains uncertain.

**Ecological Role:** The snowshoe hare is one of the most important prey species in PNW montane forests, transferring energy from plant production to a predator community that includes Canada lynx, bobcat, coyote, red fox, great horned owl, goshawk, red-tailed hawk, golden eagle, fisher, marten, mink, and weasels. Hare browsing shapes shrub and sapling density in regenerating forests. The cyclic population dynamics propagate through the food web, influencing reproduction and survival of multiple predator species.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Lepus americanus* is one of approximately 30 *Lepus* species worldwide. The seasonal pelage molt occurs in several northern *Lepus* species (Arctic hare, mountain hare of Eurasia) and represents a convergent adaptation to snow environments. The snowshoe hare's large hind feet — with foot-loading values (mass per unit foot area) lower than any other North American mammal — are an extreme adaptation to deep, soft snow that characterizes its montane and boreal habitat.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Krebs et al. (2001) — *Ecosystem Dynamics of the Boreal Forest*; Mills et al. (2013) — camouflage mismatch; Zimova et al. (2016) — coat color mismatch and survival

#### Pygmy Rabbit (*Brachylagus idahoensis*)

**ESA-ENDANGERED — Extended Treatment**

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — restricted to sagebrush steppe
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Endangered (Columbia Basin DPS — WA); Candidate/Species of Concern elsewhere
- IUCN: Least Concern (range-wide); Critically Endangered (Columbia Basin DPS)
- WA: Endangered (Columbia Basin DPS); the Columbia Basin pygmy rabbit is the most endangered mammal in Washington state
- OR: Strategy Species
- ID: Species of Greatest Conservation Need
- BC: Not present

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 23-29 cm (head-body); tail 1.5-2.4 cm
- Mass: 375-500 g (typically 400-460 g) — the smallest rabbit in North America and one of only two rabbits in the world that digs its own burrows
- Sexual dimorphism: Females slightly larger
- Distinguishing features: Tiny size (fits in the palm of a hand), uniformly gray-brown pelage with a pinkish-buff wash, short ears (3.5-5 cm — shortest of any North American rabbit), short hind legs relative to body (an adaptation for burrowing rather than running), tiny white cotton-puff tail. Easily confused with juvenile cottontail in the field, but the uniformly small ears, gray (not brown) dorsal color, and burrowing behavior are diagnostic.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense stands of big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) with 20-30% canopy cover and a perennial grass/forb understory. This is one of the most habitat-specific mammals in North America — pygmy rabbits are virtually never found outside of dense sagebrush. They require tall sagebrush (>60 cm, typically 80-150 cm) for both food and concealment, with deep, friable soils for burrow construction.
- Elevation range: 1,200-2,100 m
- Ecoregion: Steppe (exclusively)
- Home range: 0.2-5.0 ha; varies with sagebrush density and season
- Burrows: Unlike all other North American rabbits (which use surface nests or appropriate existing burrows), pygmy rabbits excavate their own burrow systems with 2-7 entrances. Burrow entrances are typically beneath or adjacent to large sagebrush plants. Burrows extend 0.3-1.0 m below the surface with multiple chambers. The association between pygmy rabbits, deep soils, and tall sagebrush creates a highly specific and shrinking habitat niche.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Sagebrush comprises 50-99% of the diet year-round — one of the most specialized diets of any mammal. In winter, big sagebrush is essentially the sole food source. Pygmy rabbits have physiological adaptations (enlarged liver, specialized hepatic enzymes) for detoxifying the monoterpene compounds (camphor, cineole) that make sagebrush unpalatable or toxic to most herbivores. In summer, grasses and forbs supplement the sagebrush base.
- Foraging strategy: Primarily crepuscular; forages within sagebrush canopy and rarely ventures more than 5 m from dense cover. In winter, forages on sagebrush branches accessible from snow tunnels.

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-July
- Gestation: 27-30 days (estimated — difficult to study in wild populations)
- Litter size: 4-8 (typically 6); 2-3 litters per year
- Young: Born altricial in a fur-lined burrow chamber
- Sexual maturity: ~1 year
- Lifespan: 2-5 years

**Social Structure:** Loosely social; home ranges overlap, and multiple individuals may share burrow complexes. Males' ranges overlap those of several females.

**Population & Conservation:**

*Columbia Basin DPS (Washington):*
The Columbia Basin population of pygmy rabbit is genetically distinct from all other pygmy rabbit populations and is listed as Endangered under the ESA. This population was historically distributed across the sagebrush steppe of Douglas, Grant, Adams, Lincoln, and Benton counties in central Washington. By the time of listing (2003), the population had declined to fewer than 50 individuals at a single site in Douglas County — functionally extinct in the wild.

The decline was driven by conversion of sagebrush steppe to irrigated agriculture (Columbia Basin Irrigation Project), wildfire in remaining sagebrush, fragmentation by roads and development, and potentially genetic inbreeding in the shrinking population. By 2004, the wild Columbia Basin population was believed extinct.

Recovery efforts led by WDFW, in partnership with USFWS, Oregon Zoo, Northwest Trek Wildlife Park, and Washington State University, have focused on captive breeding and reintroduction. Pure Columbia Basin pygmy rabbits were bred in captivity, and beginning in 2007, animals were released at protected sagebrush sites in Douglas County. However, survival of captive-bred animals in the wild was poor — predation by raptors, coyotes, and badgers decimated released cohorts. Beginning in 2011, a semi-wild breeding program using predator-proof enclosures in native sagebrush was implemented to produce wild-born animals better adapted to avoiding predators. Intercrossing with Idaho pygmy rabbits was approved to address genetic bottleneck concerns.

As of recent monitoring, the Columbia Basin pygmy rabbit recovery effort continues with mixed results. Small numbers of rabbits persist at managed sites, but the population remains critically small and entirely dependent on active management. The recovery is one of the most challenging mammal conservation efforts in the Pacific Northwest.

*Range-wide:*
Outside the Columbia Basin, pygmy rabbits are more widespread across the Great Basin sagebrush steppe of Idaho, Oregon, Nevada, Utah, Montana, Wyoming, and California. Range-wide populations are declining due to sagebrush loss from wildfire (particularly cheatgrass-fueled fire cycles), agricultural conversion, energy development, and climate change. In Oregon, pygmy rabbits occur in the high desert of Harney, Lake, Malheur, and Deschutes counties. In Idaho, they are found across the Snake River Plain and adjacent uplands.

**Ecological Role:** Pygmy rabbit burrows provide shelter for numerous commensals including least weasels, various insects, and reptiles. Their selective browsing on sagebrush may influence sagebrush stand structure. As prey, pygmy rabbits are taken by long-eared owls, great horned owls, ferruginous hawks, badgers, coyotes, weasels, and rattlesnakes. The species' extreme sagebrush dependence makes it an indicator of sagebrush ecosystem health — where pygmy rabbits persist, the sagebrush community retains the structural complexity necessary to support the broader sagebrush-obligate wildlife community including greater sage-grouse, sagebrush sparrow, sage thrasher, and pronghorn.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Brachylagus idahoensis* is the sole member of its genus and one of the most phylogenetically distinctive lagomorphs in North America. Molecular data suggest it diverged from the cottontail lineage (*Sylvilagus*) 4-6 Ma, coinciding with the expansion of sagebrush steppe across the Great Basin during late Miocene-Pliocene aridification. The species' obligate sagebrush dependence and burrowing behavior are unique among New World rabbits and may represent adaptations to the extreme continental climate of the Great Basin — where winter temperatures frequently reach -30 degrees C and deep snow covers food plants for months.

**Cross-Links:**
- Sagebrush obligate community: Greater sage-grouse (AVI Module 5), sagebrush sparrow, sage thrasher share habitat and face same threats
- Steppe zone indicator: Pygmy rabbit presence indicates intact tall sagebrush with deep soils
- Conservation parallel: Columbia Basin pygmy rabbit recovery parallels Columbia Basin pygmy white-fish and other DPS-level recovery efforts in the region

**Key Sources:** [G9] WDFW; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; USFWS (2003) — Federal Register, pygmy rabbit ESA listing; Green & Flinders (1980) — *Brachylagus idahoensis*, Mammalian Species; Rachlow & Svancara (2006) — pygmy rabbit habitat; DeMay et al. (2015) — Columbia Basin pygmy rabbit genetics

#### Black-tailed Jackrabbit (*Lepus californicus*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — east-side steppe and grasslands
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status; declining in some areas
- OR: No special status
- ID: No special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 47-63 cm (head-body); tail 5-11 cm, black above (diagnostic)
- Mass: 1.3-3.6 kg (typically 2.0-2.7 kg)
- Sexual dimorphism: Females slightly larger
- Distinguishing features: Large body, extremely long ears (10-13 cm — the longest of any PNW mammal relative to body size), long powerful hind legs, grayish-brown dorsal pelage, white underparts, tail with conspicuous black dorsal stripe extending onto the rump (distinguishing from white-tailed jackrabbit). Ears are lightly furred and richly vascularized — they function as thermoregulatory radiators, dissipating heat through a network of blood vessels visible when backlit.

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Open shrub-steppe, grasslands, agricultural margins, desert scrub
- Elevation range: Sea level (in OR) to 2,000 m
- Ecoregion: Steppe, Foothill (arid grasslands)
- Home range: 10-60 ha; no permanent burrows — rests in shallow depressions ("forms") scraped in the ground beneath shrubs

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses and forbs in spring-summer; shrubs (sagebrush, rabbitbrush, bitterbrush) and dried vegetation in winter; agricultural crops where available
- Foraging strategy: Primarily nocturnal and crepuscular; cecotrophy essential for nutritional extraction from low-quality forage

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: January-August (in PNW)
- Gestation: 41-47 days
- Litter size: 1-8 (typically 2-4); 2-4 litters per year
- Young: Born precocial — fully furred, eyes open, capable of running within hours; leverets are placed in separate forms and nursed individually
- Lifespan: 1-5 years

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining in parts of the PNW; has contracted from historical range in western OR and WA
- Threats: Habitat conversion, vehicle mortality, predation; population fluctuations not cyclic in the classic sense but respond to drought and food availability

**Ecological Role:** Major prey item for golden eagle, coyote, bobcat, and large hawks in steppe ecosystems. Grazing and browsing influence vegetation structure in shrub-steppe. The thermoregulatory ear vasculature is a textbook example of countercurrent heat exchange, studied in comparative physiology courses worldwide.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Best (1996) — *Lepus californicus*, Mammalian Species

#### White-tailed Jackrabbit (*Lepus townsendii*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — east-side grasslands and alpine meadows
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Candidate species; declining
- OR: Strategy species; declining
- ID: No special status

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 47-65 cm (head-body); tail 7-10 cm, white or pale with thin dark dorsal line (diagnostic — contrast black-tailed jackrabbit)
- Mass: 2.5-4.5 kg — largest lagomorph in the PNW
- Distinguishing features: Larger and paler than black-tailed jackrabbit; winter pelage white or very pale (partial molt, not as complete as snowshoe hare); all-white or mostly white tail; longer ears than snowshoe hare but shorter than black-tailed jackrabbit

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Open grasslands, alpine meadows, sagebrush-grassland ecotone; prefers more mesic and higher-elevation habitats than black-tailed jackrabbit. In the PNW, found in high-elevation grasslands and parklands that are increasingly rare.
- Elevation range: 500-3,500 m; occupies higher elevations than black-tailed jackrabbit, with some overlap in foothill steppe
- Ecoregion: Alpine (meadows), Steppe (grasslands), Foothill

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses and forbs; less dependent on shrub browse than black-tailed jackrabbit
- Winter diet: Shifts to shrubs and dried grasses; winter pelage change provides camouflage in snowy grasslands

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-July
- Gestation: 42 days
- Litter size: 1-9 (typically 3-5)
- 1-2 litters per year (lower than black-tailed jackrabbit, reflecting harsher habitat)

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining across the PNW; range has contracted significantly from historical extent
- Threats: Conversion of native grassland to agriculture; predation; competition with expanding black-tailed jackrabbit at lower elevations; climate change affecting alpine meadow habitat
- In WA, considered a state Candidate species due to population declines; less common than black-tailed jackrabbit in most areas

**Ecological Role:** Prey for golden eagle, coyote, and bobcat in alpine and high-steppe habitats. The white-tailed jackrabbit's preference for intact native grassland makes it an indicator of grassland health — its decline parallels the loss of native bunchgrass prairie across the Intermountain West.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Lepus townsendii* was named for John Kirk Townsend, the ornithologist and naturalist who accompanied Nathaniel Wyeth's 1834 expedition to the Oregon Country and collected extensively in the Columbia Basin. The species' winter pelage molt is less complete than the snowshoe hare's, suggesting a more recent evolutionary association with snowy environments.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Rogowitz (1992) — *Lepus townsendii*, Mammalian Species

#### Brush Rabbit (*Sylvilagus bachmani*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — western Oregon, southwestern Washington
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (subspecies *S. b. riparius* — riparian brush rabbit of CA — is ESA-endangered, but PNW populations are secure)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Present only in extreme southwestern corner (Clark, Cowlitz counties); range-limited
- OR: No special status; common in western OR

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 30-37 cm (head-body); tail 2-4 cm
- Mass: 500-900 g (typically 600-800 g) — smaller than cottontails
- Distinguishing features: Small cottontail-type rabbit; dark brown dorsal pelage with grizzled appearance, relatively short ears (5-6 cm), short hind legs (less adapted for speed than jackrabbits), inconspicuous white-gray tail (less cottony than true cottontails)

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense brushy cover — blackberry thickets, salal, Oregon grape, riparian brush, chaparral, edges of oak woodland; strongly cover-dependent and almost never ventures into open ground
- Elevation range: Sea level to 800 m
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest (edges), Foothill/Oak Woodland, Riparian (brushy margins)
- Home range: 0.2-2.0 ha; confined to continuous brush cover

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses, clover, forbs in summer; bark and twigs of shrubs (blackberry, rose, willow) in winter
- Foraging strategy: Crepuscular; moves through brush tunnels created by repeated use, rarely more than a few meters from cover

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-August
- Gestation: 27-30 days
- Litter size: 2-5 (typically 3)
- 3-5 litters per year; nests in shallow depressions concealed in dense brush, lined with fur and dry grass
- Young: Born altricial (naked, blind); hidden in nest

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in OR; range-limited in WA
- Threats: Brush clearing for agriculture and development; predation by domestic cats in suburban settings

**Ecological Role:** Prey for bobcat, coyote, red-tailed hawk, great horned owl, and domestic cats. Brush rabbit populations in suburban edges indicate persistent native brush habitat.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Chapman (1974) — *Sylvilagus bachmani*, Mammalian Species

#### Eastern Cottontail (*Sylvilagus floridanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Introduced — expanding range in PNW
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Introduced species; common in western lowlands
- OR: Introduced species; expanding

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 36-43 cm (head-body); tail 4-7 cm, conspicuously white and cottony (diagnostic)
- Mass: 0.9-1.8 kg (typically 1.0-1.4 kg)
- Distinguishing features: Classic "cottontail" appearance — brown dorsal pelage with rufous nape patch, large conspicuous white puffy tail, relatively long ears (6-7 cm), white underparts, white eye-ring

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Farmland edges, suburban gardens, brushy fence rows, urban parks; thrives in human-modified landscapes
- Ecoregion: Lowland/Urban, Foothill margins
- Introduction history: Released in the Willamette Valley (OR) and Puget Sound lowlands (WA) in the early 20th century for hunting. Has expanded steadily and is now common in agricultural and suburban settings throughout western OR and WA.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses, clover, garden vegetables, tree bark in winter
- Foraging strategy: Crepuscular and nocturnal; uses garden and farmland edges extensively

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: February-September
- Gestation: 28-30 days
- Litter size: 3-8 (typically 4-5)
- 3-4 litters per year
- Young: Born altricial in a shallow nest (form) concealed in grass, lined with fur from the doe's belly

**Population:**
- Trend: Increasing and expanding range in the PNW
- Threats to natives: May compete with native brush rabbit and Nuttall's cottontail; carries parasites (including tularemia, *Francisella tularensis*) that can infect native lagomorphs

**Ecological Role:** Important prey for urban and suburban raptors (red-tailed hawk, great horned owl, Cooper's hawk) and mesopredators (coyote, domestic cat). As an introduced species, its ecological impacts on native lagomorphs are a monitoring concern.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Chapman, Hockman & Ojeda (1980) — *Sylvilagus floridanus*, Mammalian Species

#### Nuttall's Cottontail (*Sylvilagus nuttallii*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Status:** Resident — east-side sagebrush and rimrock
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: No special status
- OR: No special status
- ID: No special status
- BC: Yellow-listed (secure); range limited to southern interior

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 33-39 cm (head-body); tail 4-5 cm
- Mass: 600-900 g (typically 700-850 g)
- Distinguishing features: Smaller and grayer than eastern cottontail; dorsal pelage grayish-brown (less rufous than eastern cottontail), white underparts, relatively shorter ears (5.5-6.5 cm), pale grayish nape (rather than rufous), white cotton-puff tail

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Sagebrush, rimrock, rocky outcrops with scattered brush, juniper woodland; the native cottontail of the PNW east-side steppe
- Elevation range: 300-2,500 m
- Ecoregion: Steppe, Foothill (arid)
- Home range: 1-5 ha; centered on rocky cover or dense sagebrush

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses and forbs in summer; sagebrush, rabbitbrush, and juniper bark in winter
- Foraging strategy: Crepuscular; uses rocky cover and sagebrush as anti-predator refuge

**Reproduction:**
- Breeding season: March-July
- Gestation: 28-30 days
- Litter size: 4-8
- 2-3 litters per year
- Young: Born altricial in a fur-lined nest beneath sagebrush or in rock crevice

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; some range contraction where sagebrush steppe is lost
- Threats: Sagebrush habitat loss (wildfire, cheatgrass invasion, agricultural conversion); potential competition with introduced eastern cottontail in areas of range overlap

**Ecological Role:** Primary prey for long-eared owl, great horned owl, ferruginous hawk, coyote, bobcat, and rattlesnakes in steppe ecosystems. Its reliance on native sagebrush-bunchgrass habitat makes it an indicator species for intact east-side steppe communities, complementing the pygmy rabbit as a sagebrush ecosystem sentinel.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Sylvilagus nuttallii* was named for Thomas Nuttall, the English-American botanist-zoologist who explored the Pacific Northwest in the 1830s and made extensive natural history collections in the Oregon Country. The species is the characteristic cottontail of the Intermountain West — adapted to arid, rocky environments that cottontails of the eastern woodlands would find inhospitable.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Chapman (1975) — *Sylvilagus nuttallii*, Mammalian Species
