## Introduction: The Mammalian Web

An ecosystem is not a list of species. It is a web of relationships — and mammals, by virtue of their size, mobility, metabolic demands, and behavioral complexity, are among the most consequential nodes in that web. A gray wolf does not simply eat elk. It reshapes riparian forests, alters songbird communities, changes the flow of streams, and modifies the growth patterns of willows and cottonwoods decades after its return. A beaver does not simply build a dam. It creates wetlands that filter water, store carbon, raise water tables, buffer floods, create salmon rearing habitat, and provide breeding sites for amphibians, waterfowl, and invertebrates. A northern flying squirrel does not simply eat truffles. It disperses mycorrhizal spores that inoculate tree roots, enabling the fungal networks that connect every tree in the forest into a single communicating system.

These are not poetic exaggerations. They are measurable, documented ecological processes — and they are the subject of this module.

The Pacific Northwest is unusual among temperate ecosystems in the density and strength of its mammalian ecological networks. Three factors drive this:

1. **Topographic compression.** The PNW compresses 11 ecoregion bands — from deep marine (ELEV-DEEP-MARINE, y=-64) to alpine (ELEV-ALPINE, y=319) — into horizontal distances as short as 100 km. This forces species from radically different guilds into proximity, creating interaction opportunities that do not exist in flatter landscapes.

2. **Salmon subsidy.** Anadromous Pacific salmon physically transport marine-derived nutrients (MDN) into terrestrial ecosystems, creating a cross-boundary nutrient pathway that mammals — particularly bears, wolves, otters, and mink — mediate and distribute. No other temperate region has an equivalent biological conveyor belt.

3. **Megafauna recovery.** The ongoing recolonization of gray wolves, the proposed reintroduction of grizzly bears to the North Cascades, the recovery of sea otters, and the expansion of fisher and wolverine populations are restoring trophic interactions that were suppressed for 50-150 years. The PNW is one of the few places on Earth where we can observe apex predator network effects being rebuilt in real time.

This module maps eight network categories:

## 1. Trophic Cascades

A trophic cascade occurs when a predator at the top of a food chain suppresses its prey, indirectly releasing the prey's food source from consumption pressure. The effect ripples downward through trophic levels — apex predator controls herbivore, herbivore release benefits vegetation, vegetation change restructures habitat for dozens of other species. Trophic cascades are among the most powerful organizing forces in ecology, and the PNW hosts three of the best-documented examples on Earth.

### 1.1 Wolf-Elk-Riparian Cascade

**Network pathway:** Gray wolf (*Canis lupus*) → Rocky Mountain elk (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*) → riparian vegetation (willow, cottonwood, aspen) → stream morphology → aquatic invertebrates → salmon → songbirds → raptors

**The Yellowstone model.** The reintroduction of gray wolves to Yellowstone National Park in 1995-96 produced the most thoroughly documented trophic cascade in terrestrial ecology. Before wolves returned, elk populations had grown to 15,000-20,000 animals in the Northern Range, and decades of intensive browsing had eliminated willow (*Salix* spp.), cottonwood (*Populus* spp.), and aspen (*Populus tremuloides*) regeneration along streams. Riparian zones were denuded, streambanks eroded, channel incision deepened, and beaver — which depend on willow — had been functionally extirpated from many drainages.

Within a decade of wolf reintroduction, measurable changes cascaded through the ecosystem:

- **Elk behavioral modification (ecology of fear).** Elk shifted their habitat use away from confined riparian bottoms where wolf predation risk was highest. This "landscape of fear" effect reduced browsing pressure on riparian vegetation even before elk numbers declined significantly (Fortin et al. 2005, Ripple & Beschta 2012).
- **Willow and cottonwood recovery.** Height of willow stands in high-wolf-use areas increased from <1 m to >3 m within 10 years. Cottonwood recruitment resumed for the first time in decades (Ripple & Beschta 2012).
- **Stream channel stabilization.** Recovered riparian vegetation stabilized streambanks, reduced erosion, narrowed and deepened channels, and increased pool formation — improving habitat for cutthroat trout and other salmonids (Beschta & Ripple 2012).
- **Beaver recovery.** Beaver recolonized streams where willow had recovered, building dams that further modified hydrology and created wetland habitat (Smith et al. 2016).
- **Songbird and neotropical migrant increase.** Willow-dependent species such as Wilson's warbler (*Cardellina pusilla*) and yellow warbler (*Setophaga petechia*) increased in recovering riparian zones (Baril et al. 2011).
- **Scavenger subsidy.** Wolf kills provided carrion for ravens, magpies, eagles, coyotes, wolverines, and grizzly bears throughout winter — a food subsidy that had been absent for 70 years.

**Application to PNW recolonization.** Washington State hosts 33 confirmed wolf packs (2024 census), concentrated in the northeastern counties (Okanogan, Ferry, Stevens) with packs now establishing west of the Cascades crest. Oregon hosts 23 confirmed packs, concentrated in the northeast (Wallowa, Union, Umatilla) with range expanding into the central and southern Cascades.

The PNW wolf-elk cascade differs from Yellowstone in several important ways:

| Factor | Yellowstone | PNW (WA/OR) |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| Elk density | Very high (15,000-20,000 in Northern Range) | Moderate (varies by game management unit) |
| Landscape openness | Open grassland-sagebrush valleys | Dense coniferous forest (west side), mixed forest-steppe (east side) |
| Human land use | Minimal (national park) | High (timber, agriculture, roads, towns) |
| Alternative prey | Bison, mule deer, pronghorn | Mule deer, white-tailed deer, moose (limited) |
| Competing predators | Grizzly bear (kleptoparasitism), cougar, coyote | Cougar (primary), black bear, coyote; no grizzly (yet) |
| Riparian condition | Severely degraded | Variable — some streams heavily logged, others intact |
| Wolf-human conflict | Low (park protection) | High (livestock depredation, political opposition) |

PNW-specific cascade effects are beginning to emerge:

- In northeastern Washington, elk have shifted from valley-bottom winter ranges to higher-elevation timber in areas with established wolf packs. This behavioral shift mirrors the Yellowstone "ecology of fear" pattern (Eisenberg et al. 2013).
- Preliminary data from Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) suggests reduced elk browsing pressure on aspen stands in the Wedge Pack territory (Stevens County), though the time series is too short for definitive attribution.
- The absence of grizzly bears west of the Rockies removes a major kleptoparasitic competitor from wolf kills, potentially increasing carrion availability for smaller scavengers (wolverine, marten, fisher).

### 1.2 Cougar-Deer Regulation

**Network pathway:** Cougar (*Puma concolor*) → mule deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) / black-tailed deer (*O. h. columbianus*) / white-tailed deer (*O. virginianus*) → understory browse → forest regeneration

The cougar is the dominant large predator across most of the PNW — it never went extinct here, unlike wolves and grizzlies. Cougar-deer dynamics in the PNW differ structurally from wolf-elk dynamics:

- **Solitary ambush vs. pack pursuit.** Cougars are solitary stalkers that ambush prey from cover. They do not create the same "landscape of fear" effects as wolves because their predation risk is concentrated in dense cover rather than open areas. Deer avoid heavy cover near forest edges — the inverse of the elk-wolf pattern.
- **Prey switching.** PNW cougars switch between mule deer, black-tailed deer, and white-tailed deer depending on local availability. In western Oregon and Washington, Columbian black-tailed deer are the primary prey. In eastern Oregon and Washington, mule deer dominate. Where wolf recovery is occurring, cougars may shift toward smaller prey (beaver, porcupine, snowshoe hare) to reduce competitive overlap.
- **Fawn predation.** Cougar predation on neonatal fawns (first 6 weeks of life) can be a significant source of deer mortality in some PNW game management units. This differs from wolf predation, which targets adult elk disproportionately.
- **Ungulate population regulation.** In areas without wolves, cougars are the primary top-down regulator of deer populations. Where both wolves and cougars are present, the two predators partition prey: wolves preferentially target elk (larger prey, open terrain), while cougars target deer (smaller prey, dense cover). This complementary predation may enhance overall ungulate regulation beyond what either predator achieves alone.

**Cascade effects:**

Where cougar predation maintains deer at moderate densities, measurable effects include:
- Reduced browse damage to western redcedar (*Thuja plicata*) seedlings, which are highly palatable to deer
- Improved regeneration of vine maple (*Acer circinatum*) and salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) in the understory
- Reduced deer-vehicle collision rates in exurban areas adjacent to cougar habitat (confirmed in western WA — Kertson et al. 2011)

Where cougar populations are suppressed (through hunting or habitat fragmentation), deer populations increase and browsing intensifies, producing measurable declines in understory plant diversity — a classic herbivore release pattern.

### 1.3 Sea Otter-Urchin-Kelp Cascade

**Network pathway:** Sea otter (*Enhydra lutris*) → sea urchins (*Strongylocentrotus* spp.) → kelp forests (*Macrocystis pyrifera*, *Nereocystis luetkeana*) → entire nearshore marine community

This is the original trophic cascade — the system that James Estes and John Palmisano described in 1974 in one of the most influential papers in ecology. The mechanism is elegantly simple:

- **With otters:** Sea otters consume sea urchins (their primary prey by biomass). Urchin populations are kept low. Kelp forests flourish. The three-dimensional structure of kelp forests supports >750 species of fish, invertebrates, and algae in the PNW.
- **Without otters:** Sea urchins reproduce unchecked, form dense aggregations ("urchin barrens"), and graze kelp holdfasts to stumps. Kelp forests collapse. The nearshore ecosystem loses its structural foundation.

**PNW status and history:**

Sea otters were hunted to near-extinction in the maritime fur trade (1741-1911). They were extirpated from Washington, Oregon, and most of British Columbia by the early 1900s. Reintroduction efforts brought 59 Alaskan sea otters to the Olympic Peninsula coast (1969-1970), founding the current Washington population of approximately 2,900 animals (2023 survey — USFWS). Oregon has no resident sea otter population; occasional sightings of individual animals (likely dispersers from Washington or California) occur but no breeding colony has established.

The Washington sea otter population is concentrated along the outer coast from Destruction Island to Cape Flattery, with the highest densities around Point Grenville, Makah Bay, and the Strait of Juan de Fuca entrance. Their range has been expanding slowly southward and into the Strait.

**Cascade quantification:**

| Metric | Otter-present zones (WA outer coast) | Otter-absent zones (OR coast, inland waters) |
|--------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Urchin density | 2-5 per m² | 20-100+ per m² |
| Kelp canopy cover | 60-90% | 5-20% |
| Fish species richness | 40-60 species | 15-25 species |
| Invertebrate biomass (non-urchin) | High — crabs, abalones, sea cucumbers | Low — urchin-dominated |
| Carbon sequestration | Kelp forests sequester 4.5-12 tonnes C/ha/yr | Urchin barrens: negligible |

**Indirect cascade effects mediated by kelp:**

1. **Kelp → fish habitat → marine mammal prey.** Dense kelp forests support rockfish (*Sebastes* spp.), lingcod (*Ophiodon elongatus*), and juvenile salmon, which in turn support harbor seals, Steller sea lions, and orca prey bases (indirect link to SRKW through salmon).
2. **Kelp → wave attenuation → shoreline stabilization.** Kelp forests dampen wave energy by 50-70%, reducing coastal erosion and protecting estuarine habitats used by river otters, mink, and raccoons.
3. **Kelp → carbon export → deep sea.** Kelp detritus sinks to the seafloor as "marine snow," contributing to the detrital food web that supports deep-marine scavengers.
4. **Kelp → dissolved oxygen → fish survival.** Kelp photosynthesis oxygenates nearshore waters, reducing hypoxia risk during warm-water events.

**Otter-urchin-kelp as a bistable system:**

The otter-urchin-kelp cascade exhibits bistability — once urchin barrens establish, they are self-reinforcing (urchins concentrate, preventing kelp recruitment, which removes otter food and prevents otter colonization). Transitioning from barren to forested state requires either otter recolonization at sufficient density or an urchin disease event. This has implications for Oregon sea otter reintroduction proposals (USFWS feasibility study ongoing as of 2025).

### 1.4 Mesopredator Release

**Network principle:** When apex predators are removed, mesopredators (mid-sized predators) increase in abundance and alter prey communities in ways that apex predators had prevented.

Mesopredator release is not a single cascade but a general principle that operates across multiple PNW systems:

**Terrestrial mesopredator release:**

| Apex predator removed | Mesopredator released | Effect on prey community |
|----------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Gray wolf (*Canis lupus*) | Coyote (*Canis latrans*) | Increased predation on pronghorn fawns, ground-nesting birds, small mammals; coyote expansion into habitats formerly avoided due to wolf presence |
| Gray wolf | Red fox (*Vulpes vulpes*) | Fox populations increase where both wolves and coyotes are suppressed; increased predation on ground-nesting waterfowl and colonial seabirds |
| Cougar (*Puma concolor*) | Bobcat (*Lynx rufus*) | Increased predation on rabbits, ground squirrels; bobcat density increases 2-3x in cougar-free areas |
| Wolf + cougar (both absent) | Coyote (dominant mesopredator) | Dramatic increase in coyote density; coyotes fill apex predator niche incompletely — suppress smaller mesopredators but cannot control ungulate populations |

**The wolf-coyote-fox intraguild cascade:**

This three-level intraguild predation chain is well-documented in the Northern Rockies and is now observable in PNW wolf recovery areas:

```
Wolf present:     Wolf ──kills──→ Coyote (suppressed) ──releases──→ Red Fox (increases)
                                                                            │
                                                                            ▼
                                                                    Small mammal
                                                                    predation shifts
                                                                    (fox = more vole-focused,
                                                                     coyote = more generalist)

Wolf absent:      [no wolf] ──→ Coyote (abundant) ──kills──→ Red Fox (suppressed)
                                       │
                                       ▼
                               Increased predation on
                               pronghorn fawns, ground squirrels,
                               ground-nesting birds
```

In northeastern Washington where wolf packs have established, WDFW camera trap data shows coyote activity declining at wolf kill sites and red fox activity increasing in formerly coyote-dominated territories — consistent with the intraguild release pattern (preliminary data, Newsome & Ripple 2015 framework).

**Marine mesopredator release:**

The decline of great whales (blue, fin, sei, humpback) due to commercial whaling may have contributed to orca dietary shifts. The "sequential megafaunal collapse" hypothesis (Springer et al. 2003) — controversial but influential — proposes that when great whales were depleted, transient orcas shifted predation to smaller marine mammals (Steller sea lions, harbor seals, sea otters) in sequence, contributing to population declines in each. While this hypothesis remains debated (many alternative explanations exist for each prey population's decline), it illustrates the potential for marine mesopredator release dynamics.

A more directly observable marine mesopredator release in PNW waters: the decline of harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina*) in areas where Bigg's (transient) orcas concentrate may release predation pressure on small schooling fish (herring, sand lance), with cascading effects on seabird prey availability. This interaction is documented but not yet fully quantified.

### 2.2 Ecoregion Food Webs

#### ELEV-ALPINE and ELEV-SUBALPINE (y=196 to y=319)

**Characteristic mammalian food web:**

```

              (*M. caligata*)     (*Oreamnos americanus*)

              Red Fox ───────────── Wolverine
          (*Vulpes vulpes*)    (*Gulo gulo*)

              American Pika        Ptarmigan (AVI)
          (*Ochotona princeps*)
                    ▲
                    │
              Ermine / Short-tailed Weasel
```

**Key interactions:**
- **Wolverine-marmot:** Wolverines dig hibernating marmots from burrows in late winter — a critical protein source when other prey is scarce. Marmot body fat accumulated over the brief alpine summer sustains wolverines through the lean months.
- **Pika-ermine:** Ermine are the primary mammalian predator of American pikas, pursuing them into talus interstices. Pikas are also preyed upon by marten and long-tailed weasels at lower alpine margins.
- **Golden eagle overlap:** Golden eagles (AVI: *Aquila chrysaetos*) prey on marmots, ground squirrels, and snowshoe hares in alpine and subalpine zones — direct competition with mammalian mesopredators for the same prey base (cross-ref: AVI raptors.md — Golden Eagle prey list).
- **Mountain goat:** Adults are effectively predation-free (cliff terrain excludes most predators). Kids are vulnerable to golden eagles and cougars on less precipitous terrain.

#### ELEV-MONTANE and ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-29 to y=196)

**Characteristic mammalian food web:**

```

              Coyote ────────→ Bobcat                   │
              │                 │               Canada Lynx ←→ Snowshoe Hare
              ▼                 ▼               (obligate cycle)
         Voles/Mice        Rabbits/Hares
     (Microtus, Peromyscus)  (Sylvilagus, Lepus)
              ▲                 ▲
              │                 │
     Long-tailed Weasel    Fisher → Porcupine
         Ermine            Marten → Red-backed Vole
                                    Douglas Squirrel
```

**Key interactions:**
- **Wolf-elk:** See Section 1.1 (trophic cascade). Wolves preferentially target elk in PNW montane forests.
- **Cougar-deer:** See Section 1.2. Cougars are the primary deer predator across all PNW montane and lowland forests.
- **Lynx-hare cycle:** The Canada lynx is an obligate specialist on snowshoe hare (*Lepus americanus*). In the PNW, lynx occupy boreal-like subalpine forests in the North Cascades (WA) and Blue Mountains (OR/WA). Hare populations cycle with approximately 10-year periodicity (driven by plant-hare-predator dynamics), and lynx populations track the hare cycle with a 1-2 year lag. PNW lynx populations are at the southern range limit and are ESA-listed as Threatened.
- **Fisher-porcupine:** The fisher (*Pekania pennanti*) is the only regular predator of adult North American porcupines. Fisher attacks porcupines by repeatedly biting the face (the only unquilled surface), requiring 30-60 minutes to kill a single porcupine. Fisher reintroduction to Olympic National Park (2008-2010, 90 animals released) and the southern Cascades of Washington (2015-2020, 81 animals released) has re-established this predator-prey link in forests where porcupines had no effective mammalian predator for a century.
- **Marten-flying squirrel:** American marten prey on northern flying squirrels (*Glaucomys sabrinus*), creating a three-way linkage: marten predation on flying squirrels affects truffle spore dispersal, which affects mycorrhizal inoculation of tree roots (see Section 4.3).
- **Raptor overlap (AVI cross-reference):** Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*) and Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*) are significant predators of snowshoe hares, Douglas squirrels, and mountain cottontails in montane forests — competing directly with mammalian mesopredators. Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*) and Barn Owl (*Tyto alba*) are major predators of voles and mice in lowland and steppe habitats (AVI raptors.md).

#### ELEV-RIPARIAN (y=-41 to y=84)

**Characteristic mammalian food web:**

```
              Wolf / Cougar (transient)
                        │
                        ▼
                   Elk / Deer
                   (browsing riparian vegetation)
                        │
              Black Bear ←── Salmon (seasonal)
                   │              │
                   ▼              ▼
              Berries      Salmon carcass nutrients
              Insects      → riparian vegetation
                           → aquatic invertebrates

              River Otter ←── Fish, crayfish
              American Mink ←── Fish, muskrat, birds

              Beaver ──→ Dam → Wetland → Habitat for all of the above
                   ▲
                   │
              Wolf, cougar, coyote (predation on beaver)
```

**Key interactions:**
- **Beaver-salmon nexus:** Beaver dams create slow-water rearing habitat critical for juvenile coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*). Studies in the Stillaguamish and Skagit River watersheds (WA) document 2-5x higher juvenile coho density in beaver-influenced stream reaches compared to free-flowing reaches (Pollock et al. 2004). This creates a positive feedback: beavers improve salmon habitat → more salmon return → more salmon carcass nutrients fertilize riparian vegetation → more willow and cottonwood for beaver food and dam material.
- **Bear-salmon nutrient vector:** See Section 5.1 for detailed treatment. Bears are the primary terrestrial vector for salmon-derived nutrients.
- **Mink-muskrat-waterfowl:** American mink are significant predators of muskrats and ground-nesting waterfowl in riparian and wetland habitats. Where mink density is high, muskrat populations are suppressed and waterfowl nesting success declines.

## 3. Ecosystem Engineering

Ecosystem engineers are organisms that physically modify, maintain, or create habitat. Among PNW mammals, the American beaver is the most consequential ecosystem engineer — but pocket gophers, moles, elk, deer, and marmots also engineer their environments in measurable ways.

### 3.1 American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*) — Ecosystem Architect

**Engineering role:** Dam construction, wetland creation, water table modification, sediment retention, salmon habitat enhancement, riparian vegetation management, fire resilience.

The American beaver is the most powerful non-human ecosystem engineer in North America. A single beaver family (typically 4-8 animals) maintains 1-5 dams across a stream reach, impounding water to create ponds that range from 0.01 to 5+ hectares. The cumulative effect of beaver activity across a watershed is profound:

**Hydrological engineering:**

| Process | Mechanism | Magnitude | Source |
|---------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| Water storage | Pond impoundment behind dams | 2,000-8,000 m³ per dam complex | Pollock et al. 2003 |
| Water table elevation | Lateral seepage from ponds raises groundwater | 10-50 cm increase within 50 m of pond | Westbrook et al. 2006 |
| Baseflow augmentation | Slow release of stored water during dry season | 30-80% increase in late-summer stream flow | Pollock et al. 2003 |
| Flood attenuation | Pond storage absorbs flood peaks | 10-30% peak flow reduction in small watersheds | Green & Westbrook 2009 |
| Sediment retention | Ponds trap suspended sediment behind dams | 1-5 tonnes/yr per dam; decades of accumulated sediment become "beaver meadows" | Butler & Malanson 2005 |
| Channel complexity | Pond-riffle-pool sequences; multi-thread channels | 2-5x increase in channel sinuosity | Pollock et al. 2014 |

**Salmon habitat engineering:**

Beaver-salmon interactions are among the most important ecological relationships in the PNW. Beaver dams and ponds provide:

1. **Juvenile rearing habitat.** Beaver ponds are low-velocity, food-rich environments ideal for juvenile coho salmon, steelhead, and Chinook. Juvenile coho density in beaver ponds is 2-5x higher than in adjacent free-flowing reaches (Pollock et al. 2004). Beaver ponds provide overhead cover (logs, root wads), reduce predation risk (slower water allows escape maneuvering), and increase invertebrate prey density (benthic communities thrive in depositional environments).

2. **Thermal refugia.** During summer low flows, beaver ponds can be cooler than surrounding stream reaches due to groundwater upwelling stimulated by the elevated water table. In a warming climate, these thermal refugia may become critical for cold-water fish survival.

3. **Winter habitat.** Beaver ponds provide deep-water overwinter habitat where juvenile salmonids survive ice and low-flow conditions that would be lethal in shallow free-flowing reaches.

4. **Spawning gravel sorting.** The hydraulic gradient downstream of beaver dams sorts gravel into spawning-quality substrate, creating incidental spawning habitat.

**Beaver and fire resilience:**

In fire-prone eastern PNW forests and shrub-steppe, beaver complexes function as fire refugia. The elevated water table, wet soils, and lush riparian vegetation around beaver ponds resist fire penetration. Post-fire aerial surveys in the 2020 Oregon fire season documented that beaver-modified stream reaches retained green riparian vegetation while adjacent unmodified reaches burned to the waterline (Fairfax & Whittle 2020). This "fire-proof" quality makes beaver restoration a climate adaptation strategy — a finding that has accelerated "process-based restoration" (PBR) programs using beaver dam analogues (BDAs) across the PNW.

**Current status and restoration:**

- Pre-European beaver population (PNW): estimated 2-5 million animals
- Current PNW beaver population: estimated 200,000-500,000 (heavily reduced by fur trade and habitat loss)
- Active beaver restoration programs: WDFW, ODFW, the Tulalip Tribes, and the Methow Beaver Project relocate "nuisance" beavers from human-conflict sites to restoration watersheds
- Beaver Dam Analogues (BDAs): >5,000 BDAs installed in PNW streams as of 2024, mimicking beaver dam functions in reaches where beavers have not yet recolonized

**Cross-reference:** carnivores.md — beaver is prey for wolf, cougar, coyote, bobcat; marine.md — river otter preys on beaver kits; ECO ecological-networks.md — Pathway 5: Watershed Connectivity.

### 3.2 Pocket Gopher Soil Engineering

**Species:** Mazama pocket gopher (*Thomomys mazama*), northern pocket gopher (*Thomomys talpoides*), Camas pocket gopher (*Thomomys bulbivorus*).

**Engineering role:** Soil turnover, nutrient cycling, water infiltration, plant community heterogeneity, meadow maintenance.

Pocket gophers are the most important fossorial soil engineers in PNW grassland, meadow, and subalpine ecosystems. Their effects are subtle but cumulative:

**Soil engineering quantification:**

| Process | Mechanism | Magnitude |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Soil turnover | Tunnel excavation and mound deposition | 1-5 tonnes/ha/yr of soil moved to surface |
| Soil mixing | Burial of surface organic matter; exposure of mineral soil | Equivalent to deep plowing on a centuries-long cycle |
| Water infiltration | Tunnel networks increase soil porosity | 10-30% increase in infiltration rate in gopher-modified soils |
| Plant diversity | Mound disturbance creates microsites for annual and ruderal plants | 15-25% higher plant species richness in gopher-active vs. gopher-excluded plots |
| Nutrient cycling | Cached food decays underground; fecal pellets enrich tunnel walls | Increased nitrogen and phosphorus availability in tunnel zone |

**Prairie maintenance:** In the south Puget Sound prairies (Thurston, Pierce, Lewis counties, WA), Mazama pocket gophers — federally listed as Threatened (four subspecies) — maintain the open prairie structure that supports rare butterfly species (Taylor's checkerspot), rare plants (golden paintbrush), and a suite of grassland-dependent birds. Without gopher disturbance, prairie soils develop dense thatch that suppresses native forb germination. Gopher mounds create bare-soil microsites where native prairie plants establish — an engineering function analogous to fire in maintaining prairie openness.

**Subalpine meadow engineering:** Northern pocket gophers in subalpine meadows of the Cascades and Olympics turn over soil at rates sufficient to prevent tree encroachment on some meadow margins. Their mounds create heterogeneous microtopography that supports diverse wildflower assemblages.

### 3.3 Mole Tunneling and Soil Aeration

**Species:** Coast mole (*Scapanus orarius*), Townsend's mole (*Scapanus townsendii*), shrew-mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*).

**Engineering role:** Soil aeration, drainage improvement, invertebrate habitat, root zone disturbance.

PNW moles — particularly the large Townsend's mole, which weighs up to 170 g and creates tunnel systems extending over 0.5 ha — are significant but underappreciated soil engineers:

- **Soil aeration:** Mole tunnels create macropore networks in compacted soils, increasing oxygen penetration and root growth. In clay-heavy Willamette Valley soils, mole tunneling measurably improves soil structure.
- **Drainage engineering:** Shallow tunnel networks (5-20 cm depth) function as subsurface drains, reducing waterlogging in poorly drained soils. Deep tunnels (30-60 cm) provide permanent burrow systems that persist for years.
- **Invertebrate prey web:** Moles consume enormous quantities of earthworms, insect larvae, and other soil invertebrates — a single Townsend's mole eats approximately its body weight in invertebrates daily (50-80% earthworms by biomass). This predation regulates soil invertebrate communities and influences decomposition rates.
- **Surface mound creation:** Mole hills (volcano-shaped soil mounds) create bare-soil microsites analogous to gopher mounds, promoting plant diversity in grasslands.

### 3.4 Elk and Deer Browsing → Forest Structure

**Engineering role:** Selective herbivory shapes forest understory composition, tree species dominance, and successional trajectory.

Elk and deer are not traditionally classified as ecosystem engineers, but their browsing has engineering-scale effects on PNW forest structure:

**Elk browsing effects:**

- **Western redcedar suppression.** Western redcedar (*Thuja plicata*) is highly palatable to elk. In areas with high elk density (e.g., Olympic National Park rainforest valleys, elk winter ranges on the east slope of the Cascades), elk browsing suppresses redcedar regeneration, shifting forest composition toward less palatable species (Douglas-fir, western hemlock). Exclosure experiments in Olympic National Park demonstrate that redcedar seedling survival increases 5-10x when protected from elk browsing (Beschta & Ripple 2008).
- **Meadow maintenance.** Elk grazing maintains subalpine and valley-bottom meadows by preventing tree seedling establishment. In the Olympic Mountains, elk exclusion leads to rapid tree encroachment and meadow loss within 20-30 years.
- **Riparian vegetation structure.** As documented in the wolf-elk cascade (Section 1.1), elk browsing on willow and cottonwood shapes riparian forest structure. Where elk density is high and predation risk is low, riparian zones become browse-limited — open, low-stature, and structurally simplified.

**Deer browsing effects:**

- **Understory simplification.** In western WA/OR lowland forests with high black-tailed deer density, browsing can eliminate palatable understory species (vine maple, huckleberry, salal, sword fern) from the regeneration layer, creating "browse lines" where all vegetation below 1.5 m is consumed.
- **Invasive plant facilitation.** Deer preferentially browse native plants over many invasive species (English ivy, Scotch broom, Himalayan blackberry), creating a competitive advantage for invasives in deer-dense landscapes.
- **Garden effect in suburban landscapes.** In the WA/OR urban-wildland interface, deer browsing is a dominant force shaping residential and park vegetation. This is not a natural engineering function but a consequence of high deer density in predator-poor suburban environments — a mesopredator release effect applied to herbivores.

### 3.5 Marmot Burrowing → Alpine Soil

**Species:** Hoary marmot (*Marmota caligata*), yellow-bellied marmot (*Marmota flaviventris*), Olympic marmot (*Marmota olympus*).

**Engineering role:** Alpine and subalpine soil disturbance, nutrient concentration, microhabitat creation.

Marmots are the largest burrowing mammals in PNW alpine and subalpine environments. Their burrow systems, occupied by generations of marmots over decades or centuries, create distinctive features:

- **Burrow-enhanced soils.** Marmot burrow sites accumulate nutrients from food caching, fecal deposition, and decomposition of nesting material. Soil nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations are 2-4x higher in marmot burrow areas than in adjacent undisturbed alpine soils, creating "fertility islands" visible as patches of lush vegetation in otherwise sparse alpine terrain.
- **Snow manipulation.** Marmot burrow entrances create depressions that accumulate windblown snow, modifying snowmelt patterns and providing early-season moisture to the burrow-adjacent vegetation.
- **Microhabitat creation.** Abandoned marmot burrows provide denning sites for ermines, pikas, and ground squirrels, and nesting sites for burrowing birds. The burrow microclimate (stable temperature, high humidity) supports invertebrate communities not found in surrounding alpine soils.

**Olympic Marmot (*Marmota olympus*):** This is a PNW endemic — found only in the Olympic Mountains of Washington State. Olympic marmots are a relict species, genetically isolated since the Pleistocene. Their subalpine meadow engineering is particularly important because Olympic subalpine meadows support an unusually high density of endemic plant species. The Olympic marmot is listed as a Sensitive species by the State of Washington, with population declines attributed to coyote predation (coyotes have expanded into alpine habitat as snowpack declines with climate change — a network effect linking climate → snowpack → coyote range expansion → marmot predation → alpine soil engineering loss).

## 4. Seed Dispersal and Mycorrhizal Networks

Mammals are critical vectors for seed dispersal and fungal spore transport in PNW forests. These mutualistic networks connect plant communities, fungal communities, and mammalian populations into interdependent systems.

### 4.1 Squirrel Seed Caching

**Key species:** Douglas squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*), red squirrel (*Tamiasciurus hudsonicus*), Townsend's chipmunk (*Neotamias townsendii*), yellow-pine chipmunk (*Neotamias amoenus*), golden-mantled ground squirrel (*Callospermophilus lateralis*).

**Mechanism:** Scatter-hoarding and larder-hoarding of conifer seeds, nuts, and acorns. Unretrieved cached seeds germinate, making squirrels unintentional tree planters.

**Douglas Squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*) — The Conifer Farmer:**

The Douglas squirrel is the dominant seed predator and disperser in PNW coniferous forests west of the Cascade crest. Its behavior directly shapes forest regeneration:

- **Cone harvesting.** Douglas squirrels cut green conifer cones from branches and cache them in moist locations (stream margins, rotting logs, burrow sites) called "middens." A single squirrel may cut and cache 10,000-15,000 cones per season. Cached cones that are not retrieved open naturally and release seeds into favorable germination microsites.
- **Species preferences.** Douglas squirrels preferentially harvest Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and shore pine (*Pinus contorta var. contorta*) cones. This preference hierarchy influences which tree species regenerate most successfully in squirrel-dense forests.
- **Midden ecology.** Douglas squirrel middens — accumulations of cone scales and cores built up over generations — can exceed 2 m in diameter and 1 m in depth. These middens create distinct microhabitats with altered soil chemistry (acidic, nutrient-enriched), moisture retention (the cone debris acts as a sponge), and seedling establishment conditions. Some middens have been carbon-dated to >100 years of continuous use.
- **Competition with crossbills.** Red crossbills (AVI: *Loxia curvirostra*) and Douglas squirrels compete directly for conifer seeds. In years of low cone production ("mast failure"), this competition intensifies, potentially limiting squirrel survival and reducing seed dispersal (cross-ref: AVI ecological-networks.md).

**Chipmunk seed dispersal:**

Townsend's chipmunks and yellow-pine chipmunks scatter-hoard seeds of shrubs (hazelnut, chinquapin, manzanita) and herbaceous plants across the forest floor. Unlike Douglas squirrels, which primarily cache conifer cones, chipmunks disperse hardwood and shrub seeds:

- **Dispersal distance:** 5-30 m from parent plant (typical), up to 50 m
- **Retrieval rate:** 50-70% of cached seeds are retrieved; the remainder germinate
- **Uphill dispersal bias:** Chipmunks tend to cache seeds uphill from collection sites, creating an upslope seed dispersal vector that may facilitate species range shifts under climate change

### 4.2 Bear Scat as Seed Dispersal Vector

**Key species:** Black bear (*Ursus americanus*), grizzly bear (*Ursus arctos*).

**Mechanism:** Bears consume enormous quantities of berries during hyperphagia (pre-hibernation feeding frenzy, July-October) and deposit viable seeds in feces across wide-ranging territories.

**Dispersal quantification:**

| Scat volume per deposition | 200-800 mL | 500-2,000 mL |

Bear seed dispersal is particularly important for berry-producing shrub species that form the understory of PNW forests. Many of these species (huckleberry, salal, salmonberry) are fire-followers — they regenerate vigorously after disturbance — and bear-dispersed seeds may be the primary vector for recolonizing burned areas. Black bears move freely between burned and unburned habitat patches, depositing seeds from unburned source populations into post-fire regeneration zones.

The loss of grizzly bears from the PNW Cascades eliminated a long-distance seed dispersal vector with substantially greater range and berry consumption than black bears. If grizzly bear reintroduction to the North Cascades proceeds, it would restore a high-mobility, high-volume seed dispersal pathway across one of the most topographically complex landscapes in the PNW.

### 4.3 Northern Flying Squirrel → Truffle → Mycorrhizal Inoculation

**Network pathway:** Northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) → hypogeous fungi (truffles) → ectomycorrhizal inoculation → Douglas-fir, western hemlock, and other host trees → forest productivity

This is one of the most elegant three-way mutualisms in PNW forest ecology — and one of the least visible.

**The mechanism:**

1. **Truffle fruiting.** Hypogeous (below-ground) fungi — truffles in the genera *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, *Leucogaster*, and others — fruit underground in the duff layer of PNW coniferous forests. These fungi are ectomycorrhizal: they form symbiotic sheaths around tree root tips, exchanging soil nutrients (phosphorus, nitrogen, water) for photosynthetic carbon. The trees need the fungi. The fungi need the trees. But the fungi also need a dispersal vector — and because they fruit underground, wind dispersal (the strategy of above-ground mushrooms) is unavailable.

2. **Flying squirrel mycophagy.** Northern flying squirrels are the primary consumers of truffles in PNW old-growth forests. Truffles constitute 50-90% of their diet year-round (Maser et al. 1978, 2008). Flying squirrels locate truffles by smell (truffles produce volatile aromatic compounds specifically evolved to attract mammalian dispersers), excavate them from the soil, and consume them.

3. **Spore dispersal.** Truffle spores pass through the flying squirrel's digestive tract intact and viable, enclosed in fecal pellets that are deposited across the squirrel's home range (5-15 ha). The fecal pellet matrix contains nitrogen and moisture that enhance spore germination. When deposited near an uninfected tree root tip, the spores germinate and form new mycorrhizal associations.

4. **Forest inoculation.** In recently disturbed forests (clearcuts, burned areas, landslide scars), soil mycorrhizal inoculum may be depleted. Flying squirrels moving between old-growth refugia and regenerating stands transport truffle spores into disturbed areas, inoculating seedling roots with the mycorrhizal fungi they need for nutrient uptake and survival. This makes the flying squirrel a critical vector for forest regeneration.

**Network vulnerability:**

This mutualism is vulnerable at multiple points:

- **Old-growth loss.** Northern flying squirrels reach highest densities in old-growth and mature forests (>200 years), which support the greatest truffle diversity and abundance. Clearcut logging eliminates flying squirrel habitat and truffle fruiting substrate simultaneously.
- **Barred Owl invasion.** Barred owls (*Strix varia*) — an invasive species expanding westward from eastern North America — are significant predators of northern flying squirrels (see Section 7.2). Increased owl predation could reduce flying squirrel populations and suppress truffle spore dispersal.
- **Marten predation.** American martens prey on flying squirrels, creating a three-way interaction: marten population increases (following fisher reintroduction, which reduces marten competitor pressure through intraguild dynamics) could alter flying squirrel density and truffle dispersal.
- **Climate change.** Warming temperatures and reduced snowpack may shift truffle fruiting phenology, creating temporal mismatches with flying squirrel foraging. Some truffle species require specific soil moisture and temperature conditions that may shift upslope with climate change.

**Other mammalian mycophagists in PNW forests:**

| Species | Truffle consumption (% diet) | Dispersal range | Elevation bands |
|---------|------------------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) | 50-90% | 5-15 ha home range | MONTANE, LOWLAND |
| California Red-backed Vole (*Myodes californicus*) | 30-60% | 0.2-0.5 ha home range | MONTANE, LOWLAND |
| Southern Red-backed Vole (*Myodes gapperi*) | 20-40% | 0.2-0.5 ha home range | MONTANE, SUBALPINE |
| Townsend's Chipmunk (*Neotamias townsendii*) | 10-30% (seasonal) | 0.5-2 ha home range | MONTANE, LOWLAND |
| Douglas Squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*) | 5-15% (seasonal) | 0.5-1 ha territory | MONTANE, LOWLAND |

The northern flying squirrel is by far the most important vector due to its high truffle consumption rate, relatively large home range, and nocturnal habit (which reduces seed predation by diurnal birds and provides dispersal in a temporal niche unavailable to other mycophagists).

### 4.4 Pika Haypiles as Seed Banks

**Species:** American pika (*Ochotona princeps*).

**Mechanism:** Pikas harvest vegetation throughout summer and cure it in "haypiles" — cached stores of dried plants placed under overhanging rocks in talus fields. Haypiles contain seeds from dozens of plant species.

**Seed bank function:**

- **Species diversity.** A single pika haypile may contain 30-60 plant species — grasses, forbs, and small shrubs — cut from the meadow-talus ecotone. This is a concentrated seed bank stored in a protected microclimate (the rock overhang shields the haypile from rain and snow).
- **Selective harvesting.** Pikas preferentially harvest plants with high secondary compound content (tannins, phenolics), which act as natural preservatives, extending haypile longevity. This preference incidentally selects for seeds of specific plant taxa, potentially biasing seed bank composition toward chemically defended species.
- **Seed escape.** Seeds that fall from haypiles — through weathering, disturbance, or incomplete consumption — germinate in talus interstices, colonizing microsites that would otherwise be bare rock. Pika haypile sites often support more diverse plant assemblages than adjacent non-pika talus areas.
- **Abandoned haypile decomposition.** When a pika dies and its territory is unoccupied, the haypile decomposes in place, releasing seeds and nutrients into the talus matrix. Over centuries, accumulated haypile material contributes organic matter to alpine soil development.

**Climate vulnerability:**

American pikas are cold-adapted and intolerant of high temperatures (lethal exposure threshold: >28°C for >6 hours). Pika populations have been lost from low-elevation talus sites across the Great Basin, and PNW populations at the lower elevation margin (below ~1,500 m) are considered climate-vulnerable. Loss of pika populations from talus fields would eliminate this seed bank function and reduce alpine plant diversity in the affected areas. Upslope range contraction of pikas is documented in the Oregon Cascades (Beever et al. 2016).

## 5. Marine Nutrient Networks

The Pacific Northwest is defined by cross-boundary nutrient flows between marine and terrestrial ecosystems. Mammals are the primary biological vectors mediating these transfers.

### 5.1 Salmon Carcass Nutrient Cycling (Bear-Mediated)

**Network pathway:** Ocean → salmon → bear → forest

This is the PNW's most important cross-boundary nutrient pathway, and bears are its primary terrestrial vector.

**The nutrient chain:**

1. **Marine accumulation.** Pacific salmon spend 1-7 years in the North Pacific Ocean, accumulating marine-derived nitrogen (MDN, isotopically enriched in N-15) and phosphorus. A single adult Chinook salmon carries approximately 130-260 g of nitrogen and 20-30 g of phosphorus (Naiman et al. 2002).

2. **Upstream transport.** Salmon return to natal streams in the PNW, swimming 10-1,500 km inland from the ocean. This upstream migration physically transports marine nutrients into terrestrial watersheds.

3. **Bear capture and transport.** Black bears (and historically, grizzly bears) capture spawning salmon from streams. Bears are selective feeders — they preferentially consume energy-dense brain and roe, discarding the nitrogen- and phosphorus-rich carcass on the streambank. A single bear may catch 20-30 salmon per day during peak spawning, carrying each carcass 5-50 m from the stream before feeding.

4. **Carcass decomposition.** Discarded salmon carcasses decompose on the forest floor, releasing marine-derived nutrients (MDN) into the soil. Isotope tracing studies (N-15) have documented salmon-derived nitrogen in:

| Recipient | Distance from stream | N-15 enrichment | Source |
|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Riparian soil | 0-10 m | Strong (δ15N +5 to +10‰) | Helfield & Naiman 2001 |
| Streamside trees (Sitka spruce, western redcedar) | 0-50 m | Moderate (δ15N +3 to +7‰) | Helfield & Naiman 2001 |
| Upland trees (Douglas-fir, hemlock) | 50-500 m | Weak but detectable (δ15N +1 to +3‰) | Reimchen et al. 2003 |
| Riparian insects (flies, beetles) | 0-50 m | Strong (δ15N +8 to +12‰) | Hocking & Reimchen 2002 |
| Songbirds (insectivores near salmon streams) | 0-100 m | Moderate (δ15N +3 to +6‰) | Christie & Reimchen 2008 |
| Riparian shrubs (salmonberry, devil's club) | 0-30 m | Strong (δ15N +4 to +8‰) | Bilby et al. 2003 |
| Mycorrhizal fungi (ectomycorrhizae on tree roots) | 0-50 m | Moderate (δ15N +2 to +5‰) | Wilkinson et al. 2005 |

5. **Forest productivity enhancement.** Trees growing along salmon-bearing streams in the PNW grow significantly faster than trees along non-salmon streams. Sitka spruce along salmon streams in coastal British Columbia grow 2-3x faster than trees on non-salmon streams, with the growth difference attributable to marine-derived nitrogen fertilization (Helfield & Naiman 2001). This creates a feedback loop: salmon nutrients grow bigger trees → bigger trees shade streams and provide large woody debris → shaded streams maintain cold water temperatures → cold water supports salmon → salmon bring more nutrients.

**Bear species comparison as nutrient vectors:**

| Parameter | Black Bear | Grizzly Bear (historical) |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Salmon streams utilized | Primarily small-medium streams | Small to large rivers |
| Salmon per day (peak) | 5-15 | 20-40 |
| Carcass transport distance | 5-30 m from stream | 10-100 m from stream |
| Territory size | 25-200 km² | 200-2,000 km² |
| Nutrient dispersal radius | Local (stream-adjacent) | Landscape-scale |
| Latrine sites | Scattered | Concentrated "bear trails" |
| Current PNW status | Abundant (25,000-30,000 WA+OR) | Absent from Cascades; ~50 in Selkirk Mountains (WA/ID border) |

The extirpation of grizzly bears from the PNW Cascades eliminated the high-volume, long-distance component of bear-mediated salmon nutrient dispersal. Black bears partially compensate but transport fewer carcasses shorter distances. If grizzly bear reintroduction to the North Cascades proceeds, it would restore the landscape-scale nutrient vector that has been absent for approximately 100 years.

**Other mammalian salmon nutrient vectors:**

- **River otter (*Lontra canadensis*).** River otters consume salmon and deposit feces (spraint) at communal latrine sites along stream banks. Otter spraint is nitrogen- and phosphorus-rich, creating localized nutrient hotspots. Otters transport nutrients both upstream and downstream of salmon spawning reaches, extending the nutrient pathway beyond the geographic range of spawning.
- **American mink (*Neogale vison*).** Mink scavenge salmon carcasses and transport fragments to riparian den sites, contributing to localized nutrient deposition.
- **Gray wolf (*Canis lupus*).** Wolves in coastal British Columbia and Alaska catch salmon — coastal wolves in the Great Bear Rainforest derive up to 25% of their annual diet from salmon (Darimont et al. 2009). PNW wolves are not yet documented using salmon regularly, but as wolf populations establish in western WA/OR, salmon consumption is expected to develop.
- **Raccoon (*Procyon lotor*).** Raccoons are significant scavengers of salmon carcasses in lowland streams, though their nutrient dispersal range is smaller than bears.

### 5.2 Sea Otter Kelp Forest Productivity

**Network pathway:** Sea otter → urchin control → kelp forest → nearshore marine productivity → cross-boundary nutrient export

Beyond the trophic cascade described in Section 1.3, sea otter presence drives nutrient dynamics in the nearshore marine ecosystem:

- **Kelp productivity.** Healthy kelp forests in otter-present zones produce 500-1,500 g C/m²/yr of primary productivity — rivaling tropical rainforests on a per-area basis. This productivity supports the entire nearshore food web.
- **Detrital export.** Kelp detritus (torn fronds, dissolved organic carbon) is exported to adjacent habitats — sandy beaches, deep water, estuaries — subsidizing food webs in ecosystems that cannot produce their own primary production at comparable rates.
- **Carbon sequestration.** Kelp forest carbon that sinks below the permanent thermocline is effectively sequestered from the atmosphere for centuries to millennia. Otter-mediated kelp recovery represents a marine carbon sink with climate mitigation potential (Wilmers et al. 2012 estimated that sea otter recovery across the North Pacific could sequester 0.18 Pg C through enhanced kelp growth).
- **Otter foraging disturbance.** Sea otters dive to the seafloor (5-40 m depth typical, up to 100 m maximum) to retrieve urchins, crabs, and clams, disturbing benthic sediments and resuspending nutrients into the water column. This bioturbation may enhance local nutrient availability for kelp growth — a positive feedback where the predator enhances its prey's food source's productivity.

### 5.3 Pinniped Haul-Out Nutrient Deposition

**Key species:** Harbor seal (*Phoca vitulina*), Steller sea lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*), California sea lion (*Zalophus californianus*), Northern elephant seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*).

**Mechanism:** Pinnipeds feed at sea and haul out on rocky shorelines, islands, and beaches to rest, thermoregulate, breed, and molt. While hauled out, they deposit feces, urine, and shed hair/skin — all enriched with marine-derived nutrients.

**Nutrient deposition quantification:**

| Parameter | Harbor Seal | Steller Sea Lion | California Sea Lion |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Body mass | 60-100 kg | 250-1,100 kg | 90-390 kg |
| Haul-out duration per day | 6-12 hours | 4-8 hours | 4-10 hours |
| Fecal output per haul-out | 200-500 g | 500-2,000 g | 300-1,000 g |
| N content of feces | 5-8% dry weight | 5-8% dry weight | 5-8% dry weight |
| P content of feces | 1-3% dry weight | 1-3% dry weight | 1-3% dry weight |

**Haul-out site nutrient loading:**

At large haul-out sites (e.g., Sea Lion Caves, OR; Protection Island, WA; Long Beach, WA), pinniped nutrient deposition can be ecologically significant:

- **Nitrogen loading:** A colony of 500 harbor seals depositing 300 g of feces each per day over 300 days per year delivers approximately 2,250-3,600 kg of nitrogen per year to the haul-out site and adjacent nearshore waters.
- **Guano analogy:** Pinniped nutrient deposition functions similarly to seabird guano — it transfers marine-derived nutrients to terrestrial or intertidal substrates, fertilizing nearshore vegetation and intertidal algal communities.
- **Intertidal enrichment.** Nutrients from pinniped haul-outs percolate into adjacent intertidal zones, potentially enhancing algal and invertebrate productivity.

### 5.4 Whale Fall Ecology (Deep Marine)

**Mechanism:** When a large whale dies and its carcass sinks to the seafloor below the continental shelf (>200 m depth), it creates a "whale fall" — a localized, long-duration nutrient pulse that supports a succession of specialized deep-sea communities.

**Whale fall succession:**

| Stage | Duration | Community | Nutrient source |
|-------|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Mobile scavenger stage | Months to 2 years | Hagfish, sleeper sharks, ratfish, crabs, amphipods | Soft tissue (blubber, muscle, organs) |
| Enrichment opportunist stage | 2-5 years | Polychaete worms, cumaceans, small crustaceans | Organic-enriched sediment surrounding carcass |
| Sulfophilic stage | 5-80+ years | Chemosynthetic bacteria, bone-eating worms (*Osedax*), clams, mussels | Lipids in whale bones decompose anaerobically, producing hydrogen sulfide; chemosynthetic bacteria oxidize H₂S for energy |

**PNW context:**

- The PNW continental margin (from the Strait of Juan de Fuca to Cape Blanco, OR) drops rapidly to abyssal depths (>3,000 m in the Cascadia Basin), providing suitable seafloor for whale fall communities.
- Large whale species that die in PNW waters — gray whales (most common whale fall source), humpback whales, fin whales, blue whales, sperm whales — contribute organic carbon and nutrients to a nutrient-poor deep-sea environment where food inputs are otherwise limited to slow-sinking marine snow.
- A single adult gray whale carcass (30,000-35,000 kg) delivers approximately 2,000 kg of carbon to the deep seafloor — equivalent to 2,000 years of background marine snow deposition in the same area.
- Whale bone lipid content (60-70% lipid by dry weight in some bones) fuels the sulfophilic stage for decades, creating an "oasis" of productivity on an otherwise barren abyssal plain.
- *Osedax* (bone-eating worms) — a genus discovered on whale falls off the California coast in 2002 and subsequently found on PNW whale falls — are specialist decomposers of whale bone. They have no mouth or gut; instead, symbiotic bacteria in their root-like tissues digest bone lipids. At least 25 *Osedax* species are now known, with several documented from PNW waters.

**Whale pump (living whales):**

Beyond whale falls, living whales contribute to marine nutrient cycling through the "whale pump" mechanism:

- Whales feed at depth (krill, fish, squid) and defecate at or near the surface. Their liquid, iron-rich fecal plumes release nutrients into the photic zone where phytoplankton can use them.
- Whale feces are particularly rich in iron — a micronutrient that limits phytoplankton growth across much of the North Pacific. A single defecation event by a blue whale can release enough iron to stimulate a measurable phytoplankton bloom.
- The historical depletion of great whales by commercial whaling (1900s-1970s) removed an estimated 80-90% of great whale biomass from the North Pacific. This represents a massive reduction in the whale pump nutrient cycling service. As humpback whale populations recover in PNW waters (~2,900 animals in the WA/OR/CA feeding stock, increasing), the whale pump function is being partially restored.

## 6. Disease and Parasite Networks

Infectious diseases and parasites create their own networks among PNW mammals — transmission pathways that can alter population dynamics, reshape community structure, and interact with other ecological networks in unexpected ways.

### 6.1 White-Nose Syndrome Transmission Network (Bats)

**Pathogen:** *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* (Pd) — a cold-adapted fungus that infects the skin of hibernating bats.

**Transmission network:**

```
Infected bat (Myotis spp.)
        │
        ▼
Cave/mine substrate ←→ Bat-to-bat contact during hibernation
(Pd persists in cave         │
 environment for years)       ▼
        │              Arousal from torpor (increased metabolic cost)
        │                     │
        ▼                     ▼
Other bat species      Dehydration, fat depletion, death
using same hibernaculum       │
        │                     ▼
        ▼              Population decline (90-100% mortality
Cross-species              in some Myotis species)
transmission                  │
                              ▼
                       Insect population release
                       (reduced bat predation on moths,
                        beetles, mosquitoes)
```

**PNW status (as of 2025):**

- *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* was confirmed in Washington State in 2016 (detected on western small-footed myotis, *Myotis ciliolabrum*, in an eastern WA mine). It has since been confirmed at multiple locations across eastern Washington.
- WNS-associated mortality in PNW bat populations has been lower than in eastern North America (where mortality rates of 90-100% have devastated little brown myotis, northern long-eared myotis, and tricolored bat populations). The reasons for this are not fully understood but may include: (1) PNW bats use more dispersed hibernacula (fewer large cave aggregations), (2) milder PNW winters reduce hibernation duration and thus fungal exposure time, (3) genetic differences in PNW bat populations may confer partial resistance.
- Species at highest PNW risk: Little brown myotis (*Myotis lucifugus*), Yuma myotis (*Myotis yumanensis*), long-legged myotis (*Myotis volans*), and other cave-hibernating *Myotis* species.
- Species at lower risk: Big brown bat (*Eptesicus fuscus*) — typically hibernates in buildings and bridges rather than caves; hoary bat (*Lasiurus cinereus*) — migratory, does not hibernate in caves; Townsend's big-eared bat (*Corynorhinus townsendii*) — uses caves but may be less susceptible.

**Ecosystem consequences of bat decline:**

PNW bats consume enormous quantities of nocturnal insects. A single little brown myotis eats 4-8 g of insects per night — approximately half its body weight. A colony of 1,000 bats consumes 4-8 kg of insects nightly, including agricultural pest moths, forest defoliator moths, and mosquitoes. Catastrophic bat declines from WNS in eastern North America have been correlated with increased insect pest damage in agricultural landscapes (Maine et al. 2015). Similar effects in the PNW would impact forestry (increased defoliator outbreaks) and agriculture (increased pest moth damage).

**PNW risk assessment:**

| Factor | Status | Risk level |
|--------|--------|------------|
| CWD currently present in WA/OR? | No confirmed cases as of 2025 | Low (current) |

**Potential cascade effects if CWD reaches PNW:**

1. **Cervid population decline.** CWD causes chronic, progressive weight loss and death in all infected cervids. In areas with established CWD (Wyoming, Colorado), prevalence in mule deer herds has reached 30-50% in some units, with population-level declines evident.
2. **Predator-prey disruption.** Wolves and cougars may preferentially kill CWD-infected cervids (which show abnormal behavior and reduced flight response), potentially slowing CWD spread — the "predator as public health service" hypothesis. However, prions can persist in predator feces and potentially be transmitted by scavenging.
3. **Scavenger network contamination.** Coyotes, ravens, eagles, and other scavengers that consume CWD-infected carcasses could disperse prions across the landscape in feces. While CWD does not infect non-cervid species, prion contamination of soil at scavenger latrine sites could expose naive cervids.
4. **Cultural and economic impacts.** Cervid hunting is culturally and economically important in the PNW (>500,000 hunters in WA and OR combined). CWD detection would trigger mandatory testing, potential hunting closures, and consumer confidence concerns.

### 6.3 Canine Distemper in Mustelids

**Pathogen:** Canine distemper virus (CDV) — a morbillivirus that infects canids, mustelids, procyonids, and other carnivores.

**Transmission network:**

```
Domestic dogs (reservoir)
        │
        ▼
Raccoons, skunks (amplifying hosts — high density, suburban interface)
        │
   ┌────┴────────────┐
   ▼                 ▼
Coyotes          Mustelids (fishers, martens, mink, otters)
(maintain          │
 endemic CDV)      ▼
               High mortality in naive populations
               (50-90% case fatality rate in fishers)
```

**PNW significance:**

Canine distemper is a critical threat to reintroduced fisher populations in Washington State. Fisher reintroduction to Olympic National Park (2008-2010) and the southern Cascades (2015-2020) established small, isolated populations that are highly vulnerable to CDV outbreaks. A single distemper epizootic could cause 50-90% mortality in a reintroduced population of <200 animals, potentially undermining decades of conservation investment.

Monitoring data from GPS-collared fishers in the Olympic Peninsula population has documented CDV exposure (seropositive individuals) and at least two mortality events attributable to canine distemper. The transmission pathway is believed to run from domestic dogs → raccoons/skunks (suburban interface) → forest mustelids (fishers moving through suburban-forest edges).

Wolverine recolonization of the Cascades (currently <50 individuals in WA, ESA listing under consideration) faces similar CDV risk. Wolverines are susceptible to distemper and their extremely low population density means even a few CDV-related deaths could have demographic consequences.

### 6.4 Toxoplasma in Marine Mammals

**Pathogen:** *Toxoplasma gondii* — an obligate intracellular protozoan parasite with domestic and wild cats as definitive hosts.

**Transmission network:**

```
Domestic cat / Bobcat / Cougar (definitive hosts)
        │
        ▼
Oocysts shed in cat feces
        │
        ▼
Stormwater runoff → rivers → estuary → ocean
        │
        ▼
Filter-feeding invertebrates (mussels, oysters) concentrate oocysts
        │
        ▼
Sea otters ingest oocysts while eating invertebrates
Southern sea otters: 16% of mortality attributed to Toxoplasma (CA)
        │
        ▼
Marine mammals (seals, sea lions, cetaceans)
Toxoplasma-associated mortality documented in harbor seals,
Steller sea lions, and dolphins in PNW waters
```

**PNW significance:**

*Toxoplasma gondii* is a land-to-sea disease pathway that exemplifies how urbanization and free-ranging domestic cats create ecological consequences far from the point of origin. Key PNW data:

- **Sea otters.** In California's southern sea otter population (closely related to WA reintroduced population), *Toxoplasma* encephalitis is a leading cause of mortality, responsible for approximately 16% of all deaths in stranded animals (Miller et al. 2004). While the WA sea otter population has not been studied as intensively for *Toxoplasma*, the pathogen is presumed present in WA coastal waters based on domestic cat density in coastal communities and confirmed *Toxoplasma* oocyst contamination in Puget Sound mussels.
- **Harbor seals.** *Toxoplasma* has been documented in stranded harbor seals in Puget Sound. Harbor seals may acquire infection through ingestion of contaminated prey or water.
- **Beluga whales.** In other regions (Arctic Canada), *Toxoplasma* has been detected in beluga whales — a finding attributed to climate change increasing cat oocyst transport to marine environments via altered precipitation and runoff patterns. While beluga whales do not occur in PNW waters, the mechanism is relevant to all PNW marine mammals.

**Pathway quantification:**

A single infected domestic cat can shed 20-100 million *Toxoplasma* oocysts in a two-week period. Oocysts are environmentally resistant (surviving months to years in soil and water), are not killed by standard wastewater treatment, and can be transported via stormwater runoff from land to sea. The estimated 70-100 million free-ranging domestic cats in the United States collectively contaminate terrestrial and aquatic environments with enormous quantities of *Toxoplasma* oocysts — a chronic, diffuse disease input into marine mammal habitat.

## 7. Competition Networks

Competitive interactions among PNW mammals shape community structure, drive species distributions, and interact with trophic cascades and human modifications in complex ways.

### 7.1 Wolf-Coyote Competitive Displacement

**Interaction type:** Intraguild predation and interference competition.

When wolves recolonize an area, coyote populations decline through two mechanisms:

1. **Direct killing.** Wolves kill coyotes — not primarily for food, but as competitive interference. Wolf packs actively pursue and kill coyotes encountered within their territories. In Yellowstone, coyote populations declined 50-90% in areas occupied by wolf packs within 5 years of wolf reintroduction (Berger & Gese 2007).

2. **Spatial displacement.** Surviving coyotes avoid wolf territories, concentrating in areas between wolf pack boundaries ("buffer zones") and in human-modified landscapes (agricultural areas, suburban edges) where wolves are less likely to range.

**PNW observations:**

In northeastern Washington, where wolf packs have been established since ~2009, preliminary data suggests:
- Coyote density in wolf pack core areas has declined relative to pre-wolf conditions
- Coyotes have shifted activity patterns to avoid peak wolf activity times
- Red fox sightings have increased in areas where coyote density has declined — consistent with the three-level intraguild cascade described in Section 1.4

**Ecological consequences:**

Wolf-coyote displacement is not merely a predator reshuffling — it has cascading effects:
- Reduced coyote density → increased survival of pronghorn fawns (documented in Grand Teton, WY; potentially applicable to OR pronghorn populations as wolves expand)
- Reduced coyote density → increased jackrabbit and cottontail populations → increased prey for raptors (Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk — AVI cross-reference)
- Coyote displacement to suburban edges → increased coyote-human conflict in communities near wolf territories

### 7.2 Barred Owl Invasion → Flying Squirrel Predation Shifts

**Network pathway:** Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) invasion → Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) displacement → altered predation pressure on Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) → potential mycorrhizal network disruption

This is one of the most consequential mammalian network effects in the PNW, even though it is driven by a bird.

**The invasion:** Barred owls — native to eastern North America — expanded westward across Canada's boreal forest during the 20th century and entered the PNW in the 1960s-70s. They are now abundant throughout western WA/OR forests, outnumbering Northern Spotted Owls in most of the spotted owl's range.

**Competition with spotted owls:** Barred owls are larger (500-700 g vs. 500-600 g), more aggressive, more generalist in diet and habitat use, and have higher reproductive rates than spotted owls. They displace spotted owls through interference competition (aggression, territorial exclusion) and exploit a broader range of prey and habitats. The Northern Spotted Owl — ESA-listed as Threatened since 1990 — has declined by approximately 3-5% per year across its range since barred owl invasion intensified.

**Consequence for flying squirrels:**

Both spotted owls and barred owls prey on northern flying squirrels, but their predation patterns differ:

| Predation parameter | Northern Spotted Owl | Barred Owl |
|---------------------|---------------------|------------|
| Flying squirrel in diet | 30-60% by biomass (primary prey) | 10-25% by biomass (one of many prey) |
| Hunting habitat | Old-growth forest interior (specialized) | Old-growth, second-growth, forest edge, suburban (generalist) |
| Hunting intensity per unit area | Moderate (limited by territory size, low density) | Higher (higher density, smaller territories) |
| Other mammalian prey | Dusky-footed woodrat, red tree vole, bushy-tailed woodrat | Woodrats, voles, mice, rabbits, squirrels, shrews — broader guild |
| Net effect on flying squirrel population | Coevolved; flying squirrel populations persist under spotted owl predation | Higher total predation pressure due to higher owl density and broader habitat use |

**Cascading network effects:**

If barred owl invasion increases total predation pressure on northern flying squirrels (by replacing one specialist predator with a more abundant generalist), the downstream effects could include:
- Reduced flying squirrel density → reduced truffle spore dispersal → reduced mycorrhizal inoculation of regenerating forest stands (Section 4.3)
- Altered prey community for American marten (which also depends on flying squirrels)
- Feedback to spotted owl: reduced flying squirrel populations reduce prey availability for remaining spotted owls, accelerating their decline

This is an active area of research. The USFWS Barred Owl Management Strategy (2024) includes experimental barred owl removal in key spotted owl habitat areas (including Olympic Peninsula and western Cascades) — an unprecedented management intervention in a predator competition network.

### 7.3 Introduced Species: Feral Pig Ecosystem Disruption

**Species:** Feral pig / wild boar (*Sus scrofa*).

**PNW status:** Feral pigs are currently present in limited numbers in parts of Oregon (southwestern OR — Josephine, Jackson counties) and occasionally detected in Washington. Both states have aggressive eradication programs. The PNW has not experienced the widespread feral pig invasion that has devastated ecosystems in the southeastern US, Texas, and California — but the risk is real and increasing.

**Potential ecosystem disruption if feral pig populations establish:**

| Impact category | Mechanism | Severity (if established) |
|-----------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Soil destruction | Rooting behavior destroys soil structure, mycorrhizal networks, and native plant communities | Catastrophic — single sounder of pigs can disturb 1+ ha per night |
| Native plant consumption | Consume bulbs, roots, tubers, acorns, berries | Severe — compete directly with bears, squirrels, deer for mast |
| Ground-nesting bird destruction | Consume eggs and chicks of ground-nesting species | Severe — affects AVI species (grouse, shorebirds, waterfowl) |
| Predator-prey disruption | Compete with native omnivores (black bear, raccoon); serve as prey for cougars and wolves but reproduce faster than predators can control | Moderate to severe |
| Disease transmission | Carry swine brucellosis, pseudorabies, leptospirosis — transmissible to native wildlife and domestic livestock | Severe — disease reservoir |
| Riparian damage | Rooting in stream banks causes erosion, sedimentation, water quality degradation | Severe — directly impacts salmon habitat |
| Mycorrhizal network damage | Rooting disrupts soil fungal networks and destroys truffle fruiting bodies | Severe — undermines flying squirrel-truffle-tree mutualism |

**Current management:** Oregon treats feral pigs as an invasive species subject to immediate lethal removal. Washington classifies them as a prohibited species — any detected pigs are targeted for eradication. The goal in both states is prevention and early eradication, not population management. This proactive stance has so far prevented the ecological catastrophe that feral pigs have caused in other regions.

### 7.4 Sea Lion-Fisheries-Orca Triangular Competition for Salmon

**Interaction type:** Apparent competition mediated by a shared resource (Pacific salmon).

This three-way competitive network involves marine mammals, commercial/recreational fisheries, and salmon populations — with the ESA-listed Southern Resident killer whale at the center:

```
                    Pacific Salmon
                    (shared resource)
                   /        |        \
                  /         |         \
     Fisheries harvest   SRKW diet   Pinniped predation
     (commercial +       (>80%       (sea lions at dams,
      recreational)      Chinook)     river mouths, spawning
                                      grounds)
                  \         |         /
                   \        |        /
                    Competition for
                    declining salmon
```

**Key dynamics:**

1. **Pinniped predation on salmon at bottleneck sites.** California sea lions (*Zalophus californianus*) and Steller sea lions (*Eumetopias jubatus*) congregate at Columbia River dams (Bonneville Dam in particular), river mouths, and fish ladders where migrating salmon are concentrated and vulnerable. NOAA estimates that pinnipeds consume 5-20% of adult salmon and steelhead returning to some Columbia River tributaries. At Willamette Falls (OR), sea lion predation on ESA-listed winter steelhead has been a major management concern.

2. **Fisheries harvest.** Commercial and recreational salmon fisheries harvest millions of pounds of Chinook, coho, and other Pacific salmon annually. These fisheries are regulated by NOAA, state agencies, tribal co-managers, and the Pacific Fishery Management Council, with harvest rates set to sustain populations — but cumulative harvest reduces the number of salmon available to SRKW and other marine predators.

3. **SRKW prey limitation.** Southern Resident killer whales depend on Chinook salmon for >80% of their diet. Their population decline (from 98 in 1995 to 73 in 2024) is driven primarily by prey limitation. Every Chinook consumed by a sea lion or harvested by a fisher is one less fish available to an ESA-listed orca.

**Management conflicts:**

This triangular competition creates intractable management conflicts:

- **MMPA vs. ESA.** Sea lions are protected under the Marine Mammal Protection Act. SRKW are protected under the ESA. When sea lions eat salmon that SRKW need, two federal laws collide. Congress authorized lethal removal of specific sea lions at Bonneville Dam (Section 120 of the MMPA), but implementation has been contentious and limited.
- **Tribal treaty rights.** Pacific Northwest tribes hold treaty-reserved fishing rights to salmon. These rights are legally binding and culturally foundational. Salmon management must balance tribal harvest, commercial fisheries, pinniped predation, and SRKW prey needs simultaneously.
- **Population trajectories.** California sea lion populations are growing (>300,000 animals, recovered from 1970s lows). SRKW populations are declining. Chinook salmon populations are declining or stable at depressed levels. The trajectory of these three populations is diverging, intensifying the competitive dynamic.

## 8. Network Resilience and Conservation

The long-term persistence of PNW mammalian ecological networks depends on habitat connectivity, population viability, and the ability of network nodes (species) to maintain functional interactions under changing conditions.

### 8.1 Connectivity Corridors for Large Carnivores

Large carnivores — wolves, cougars, bears, wolverines — require landscape-scale connectivity to maintain viable populations, genetic diversity, and ecological function. The PNW's major connectivity challenges and existing/proposed corridors:

**North-south corridors:**

| Corridor | Species served | Status | Key barriers |
|----------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| Cascades Range (WA-OR) | Wolf, cougar, wolverine, fisher, marten | Partially functional | I-90 (Snoqualmie Pass), US-2 (Stevens Pass), I-84 (Columbia Gorge) |
| Coast Range (WA-OR) | Cougar, black bear, fisher (Olympic pop.) | Partially functional | Timber roads, rural development, limited protected lands |
| Blue Mountains (WA-OR-ID) | Wolf, cougar, elk, mule deer | Functional | Agricultural valleys between mountain blocks |
| Selkirk-Cabinet-Yaak (WA-ID-MT) | Grizzly bear, wolverine, lynx, wolf | Critical — only grizzly connectivity to US Rockies | Highway 95 (ID), Highway 200 (MT), Kootenai Valley development |

**East-west corridors:**

| Corridor | Species served | Status | Key barriers |
|----------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| I-90 corridor (Snoqualmie Pass, WA) | All large mammals | Partially restored — wildlife crossing structures under construction (2016-2029) | I-90 highway: 6 lanes, 30,000+ vehicles/day; wildlife overcrossings and undercrossings being built at 15+ locations |
| US-97 corridor (OR) | Mule deer, elk, cougar | Functional but fragmented | Highway mortality, rural development |
| Columbia River crossing | Wolf, cougar | Major barrier — no functional crossing between WA and OR for terrestrial mammals near The Dalles-Portland corridor | River width, bridge infrastructure, urban/agricultural development |

**I-90 Snoqualmie Pass Wildlife Crossing (case study):**

The I-90 wildlife crossing project between Snoqualmie Pass and Keechelus Lake (WA) is the most ambitious highway wildlife connectivity project in the United States. When complete (~2029), it will include:
- 2 large-span wildlife overcrossings (vegetated bridges over I-90)
- 13+ wildlife undercrossings (enlarged culverts and bridge spans)
- 5+ miles of wildlife exclusion fencing directing animals to crossing structures
- Target species: elk, deer, cougar, black bear, wolverine, wolf, marten, fisher

Camera monitoring at completed crossing structures has already documented use by black bears, elk, mule deer, coyotes, bobcats, cougars, and numerous small mammals. The project is expected to reconnect the North Cascades and South Cascades ecosystems for the first time since I-90 was built, restoring genetic flow for isolated populations.

### 8.2 Fragmentation Effects on Small Mammal Metapopulations

While large carnivores require landscape connectivity, small mammals face fragmentation at finer spatial scales. PNW fragmentation effects on small mammal metapopulations include:

**Forest fragmentation:**

| Species | Minimum patch size | Dispersal distance | Fragmentation vulnerability |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Northern Flying Squirrel | 20-40 ha old-growth | 1-3 km | High — requires canopy connectivity for gliding; avoids clearcuts |
| Red Tree Vole (*Arborimus longicaudus*) | 10-20 ha old-growth Douglas-fir | <500 m (rarely disperses through non-forest) | Very high — arboreal obligate, ESA candidate |
| American Marten | 200-1,000 ha mature forest | 5-30 km | Moderate — avoids openings >200 m; requires continuous canopy |
| Fisher | 2,000-5,000 ha mature forest | 10-50 km | Moderate — more tolerant of habitat heterogeneity than marten |

**Prairie fragmentation (south Puget Sound):**

Mazama pocket gophers — the prairie ecosystem engineers described in Section 3.2 — occupy remnant prairies that have been reduced to <3% of their pre-European extent. The remaining prairie patches are:
- Small (most <20 ha)
- Isolated (surrounded by development, forest, or agriculture)
- Functionally disconnected (pocket gophers cannot disperse across non-prairie habitat)

Each isolated prairie patch supports a genetically distinct pocket gopher subpopulation. Four subspecies of Mazama pocket gopher are ESA-listed as Threatened, in part because fragmentation has severed the metapopulation dynamics that historically allowed recolonization of locally extinct patches. Conservation efforts focus on habitat restoration to enlarge and reconnect prairie patches, but land availability in the urbanizing south Puget Sound is extremely limited.

**Riparian fragmentation:**

River otters, mink, and beaver depend on continuous riparian corridors. Culverts, dams, highway crossings, and urban development fragment riparian connectivity:
- Beaver cannot recolonize upstream reaches above impassable culverts
- River otters avoid crossing roads, creating isolated population segments in urbanized watersheds
- Mink populations in fragmented riparian habitats show reduced genetic diversity relative to populations in continuous corridors

### 8.3 Marine Protected Areas and Mammalian Network Integrity

Marine protected areas (MPAs) in PNW waters provide varying levels of protection for marine mammals and their prey:

**Key PNW marine protected areas relevant to mammalian networks:**

| MPA | Designation | Area | Mammal benefits |
|-----|-------------|------|-----------------|
| Olympic Coast National Marine Sanctuary | NOAA NMS (1994) | 8,260 km² | Protects sea otter habitat, gray whale migration corridor, SRKW outer-coast foraging areas; restricts offshore oil/gas development |
| San Juan Islands National Monument | BLM (2013) | 3.6 km² (islands only; no marine extent) | Protects pinniped haul-out sites; indirectly benefits SRKW through vessel management |
| Puget Sound — SRKW Critical Habitat | ESA Critical Habitat (2006, expanded 2021) | ~41,210 km² (inland + outer coast) | Vessel restrictions, water quality protections, prey (salmon) management requirements for SRKW |
| Oregon Islands NWR | USFWS NWR | 371 islands along OR coast | Protects Steller sea lion rookeries, harbor seal haul-outs, seabird colonies |
| Pacific Rim National Park Reserve (BC) | Parks Canada | 511 km² | Protects sea otter recovery habitat (BC population ~8,100 animals, increasing) |

**MPA effectiveness for mammalian networks:**

- **Direct protection.** MPAs reduce direct threats (vessel strikes, harassment, disturbance) to marine mammals within their boundaries. SRKW critical habitat designation triggered vessel speed and distance regulations that reduce acoustic disturbance during foraging.
- **Indirect prey enhancement.** MPAs that restrict fishing (marine reserves) can enhance fish populations, indirectly benefiting marine mammal predators. However, most PNW MPAs do not restrict fishing — they are "multiple use" sanctuaries that regulate specific activities (oil/gas, dumping, some gear types) while allowing fishing.
- **Network connectivity.** Marine mammals are highly mobile and range far beyond MPA boundaries. Orcas travel 100-160 km/day; gray whales migrate 10,000+ km annually; sea otters range along 20-50 km of coastline. No single MPA is large enough to encompass a marine mammal's full range. Network integrity for marine mammals requires connected management across the full spatial extent of their movements — an approach that existing MPAs partially but incompletely address.
- **Climate resilience.** As ocean temperatures warm, prey distributions shift, and marine mammal foraging patterns change. MPAs with fixed boundaries may become less effective if prey move outside protected areas. Adaptive management — adjusting protection boundaries as conditions change — is essential for long-term marine mammalian network integrity.

### 8.4 Network Synthesis: The Integrated PNW Mammalian Web

The eight network categories described in this module are not independent. They intersect, reinforce, and constrain each other in ways that make the PNW mammalian ecosystem a single integrated system:

**Key network intersections:**

| Intersection | Networks involved | Mechanism | Consequence |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|-------------|
| Wolf → elk → riparian → beaver → salmon → bear → forest | Trophic cascade + Engineering + Marine nutrient | Wolf suppresses elk browsing → willow recovers → beaver returns → salmon habitat improves → bears distribute salmon nutrients → forest grows → forest shades stream → salmon thrive | The "green cascade" — wolf recovery initiates a self-reinforcing cycle of ecosystem restoration |
| Flying squirrel → truffle → mycorrhizae → tree → forest structure → owl habitat | Seed dispersal + Predator-prey + Mycorrhizal | Flying squirrels disperse truffle spores → trees get mycorrhizal partners → forest grows old-growth structure → owls nest → owls prey on flying squirrels | Circular mutualism with predator regulation — each node depends on the others |
| Sea otter → urchin → kelp → fish → salmon → orca | Trophic cascade + Marine nutrient + Competition | Otter controls urchins → kelp thrives → fish habitat → juvenile salmon survival → adult salmon return → orca prey | Marine cascade connects to the salmon thread through nursery habitat |
| Climate → snowpack → coyote → marmot → alpine soil | Competition + Engineering + Disease | Reduced snowpack allows coyote range expansion to alpine → increased marmot predation → reduced alpine soil engineering → changed plant community | Climate change cascades through competition to engineering |
| Cat → Toxoplasma → sea otter → urchin → kelp | Disease + Trophic cascade | Domestic cat feces → oocysts in marine environment → sea otter mortality → weakened urchin control → kelp decline | Land-use decision (free-ranging cats) cascades to marine ecosystem |
| Bat disease (WNS) → insect release → forest defoliation → tree stress → mycorrhizal disruption | Disease + Seed dispersal/Mycorrhizal | Bat population crash → moth/beetle increase → increased defoliation → stressed trees → reduced carbon allocation to mycorrhizal partners → weakened fungal networks | Disease in one guild cascades to mutualistic networks in another |

**The salmon thread:**

Salmon are the thread that runs through nearly every PNW mammalian network. They connect:
- Ocean nutrients to terrestrial forests (marine nutrient pathway)
- Bears to riparian vegetation (carcass nutrient dispersal)
- Wolves to salmon culture (coastal wolf ecology)
- Orcas to dam policy (SRKW prey limitation)
- Sea otters to salmon nursery habitat (kelp forest → juvenile fish)
- Beavers to salmon rearing (dam → pond → juvenile coho)
- River otters and mink to stream health (piscivore indicator)
- Eagles and osprey to forest nutrient cycling (AVI cross-reference: salmon carcass scavenging)

The decline of Pacific salmon — driven by habitat loss, dams, overfishing, hatchery competition, and climate change — is not merely a fisheries problem. It is a systemic threat to the entire PNW mammalian network, because salmon are the biological currency that connects marine and terrestrial economies.

**Network resilience principles:**

1. **Redundancy.** Networks are more resilient when multiple species perform similar functions. The loss of grizzly bears from the PNW reduced the redundancy of the salmon nutrient dispersal function — black bears partially compensate, but the system lost its high-volume, long-distance vector.

2. **Connectivity.** Fragmented networks lose function faster than connected networks. I-90 wildlife crossings, riparian corridor protection, and MPA networks all serve the same principle — maintaining the connections that allow network nodes to interact.

3. **Keystone integrity.** Some species are disproportionately important network nodes. Beavers, sea otters, wolves, and salmon are keystone species — their removal causes cascading network failure far exceeding their individual biomass contribution.

4. **Adaptive capacity.** Networks that can rewire — species that can shift prey, alter dispersal routes, or adjust behavior — are more resilient to perturbation. The PNW's ongoing large carnivore recovery is essentially a test of whether damaged networks can rewire when missing nodes are restored.
