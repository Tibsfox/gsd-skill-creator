# PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — Module 5: Evolutionary Biology & Paleontology

> **"Fur, Fin & Fang" — Pacific Northwest Mammalian Biodiversity**
>
> This module traces the evolutionary history of Pacific Northwest mammals from the deep Eocene past through Pleistocene megafaunal extinctions, glacial refugia and post-glacial recolonization, island biogeography, phylogeographic structure, adaptive evolution, and modern conservation genetics. The PNW is not merely a region where mammals live — it is a region that has *made* mammals, through tens of millions of years of tectonic uplift, volcanic activity, climatic oscillation, and glacial sculpting. The modern mammal fauna is the latest frame in a film that has been running since the subtropical forests of the Eocene gave way to the grasslands and conifer forests that define the region today.
>
> The fossil record of the PNW is extraordinary. The John Day Fossil Beds of central Oregon preserve one of the most complete terrestrial Cenozoic sequences anywhere on Earth — 40 million years of mammalian evolution recorded in volcanic ash and floodplain sediments. The Hagerman Fossil Beds of southern Idaho capture the Pliocene transition. The Pleistocene record, scattered across caves, lakebeds, and glacial deposits from the Olympic Peninsula to the Snake River Plain, documents a world of mammoths, mastodons, ground sloths, saber-toothed cats, and giant bison that vanished at the end of the last ice age. Understanding what came before is essential to understanding what remains — and what might return.

## Part I: Deep Time — The Cenozoic Mammal Record of the Pacific Northwest

The Pacific Northwest preserves one of the most complete records of Cenozoic mammal evolution anywhere on Earth. This record is anchored by two extraordinary fossil sites — the John Day Fossil Beds of central Oregon (spanning ~45 to 5 million years ago) and the Hagerman Fossil Beds of southern Idaho (spanning ~3.5 to 3.2 million years ago) — but extends across hundreds of smaller localities from the coal swamps of Puget Sound to the cave deposits of the Blue Mountains. Together, they document the transformation of a subtropical, forested landscape into the mosaic of temperate forests, grasslands, and montane habitats that define the region today.

---

### 1.1 The Eocene (56–34 Million Years Ago): A Subtropical Beginning

The PNW of the Eocene was unrecognizable. The Cascade Range did not exist. The Olympic Mountains were seafloor. The Columbia Plateau was a lowland plain crossed by meandering rivers. The climate was warm and wet — subtropical to tropical, with mean annual temperatures 10–15°C warmer than today and no significant seasonality. Dense broadleaf forests of dawn redwood (*Metasequoia*), ginkgo, sycamore, and early walnut covered the landscape, extending from sea level to elevations that would not exist for another 30 million years.

The mammal fauna of the Eocene PNW was correspondingly exotic. The Clarno Formation (44–40 Ma) of the John Day Basin preserves a community that included:

- **Brontotheres** (Brontotheriidae) — Rhinoceros-sized herbivores with paired nasal horns, represented in the Clarno by *Telmatherium* and related genera. These were the dominant large herbivores of the Eocene forests, browsing on leaves and soft vegetation. They went completely extinct by the early Oligocene, leaving no descendants.

- **Creodonts** (Hyaenodontidae, Oxyaenidae) — The dominant predators before true carnivorans radiated. Creodonts occupied the large-predator niche that would later be filled by bears, cats, and dogs. The Clarno preserves *Patriofelis*, a robust oxyaenid the size of a large jaguar, with massive jaw muscles and crushing premolars adapted for bone.

- **Early primates** (Adapiformes, Omomyiformes) — Small, nocturnal to crepuscular primates inhabited the warm Eocene forests of the PNW. Fossils from the Clarno and contemporaneous deposits in Washington indicate that the region was part of a continuous primate-bearing forest belt that extended across North America and connected via land bridges to Europe and Asia. Primates would disappear from North America entirely during the Oligocene cooling.

- **Early perissodactyls** — Small, multi-toed horses (*Orohippus*, *Epihippus*) and early tapirs browsed the forest floor. These were the size of small dogs, nothing like the modern horse. The Clarno also preserves early rhinoceroses (*Hyrachyus*), already establishing the perissodactyl dominance of the Eocene megaherbivore guild.

- **Early artiodactyls** — Small, generalized even-toed ungulates were present but not yet dominant. Their time would come in the Oligocene and Miocene as the landscape opened up.

- **Multituberculates** — The last survivors of this ancient, rodent-like mammalian order, which had coexisted with dinosaurs for 100 million years, persisted into the early Eocene before finally going extinct — replaced ecologically by true rodents. The PNW Eocene marks the very end of their extraordinary run.

**The Clarno Nut Beds** are one of the most important Eocene fossil localities in North America. Preserved in volcanic mudflows (lahars) from the ancestral Cascade volcanic arc, they capture a snapshot of a tropical forest community complete with fruits, seeds, leaves, wood, insects, fish, reptiles, birds, and mammals. The preservation is exceptional — individual fruits retain their internal anatomy, and some mammal bones are articulated. The flora includes palms, figs, cinnamon, and more than 170 identified plant species, confirming that the Eocene PNW was a wet tropical-to-subtropical environment with no modern analogue in North America (Retallack et al. 2004; Fremd 2010).

---

### 1.2 The Eocene-Oligocene Transition (34 Million Years Ago): The Grande Coupure

The boundary between the Eocene and the Oligocene — approximately 34 million years ago — marks one of the most dramatic climatic and biotic transitions in the Cenozoic. Global temperatures dropped precipitously as the Antarctic ice sheet formed, driven by the opening of the Drake Passage and the thermal isolation of Antarctica. In the PNW, this cooling event triggered a fundamental restructuring of both flora and fauna.

The John Day Formation (39–18 Ma) records this transition in extraordinary detail:

**Climatic shift:** Mean annual temperature dropped by 8–10°C over approximately 400,000 years. Subtropical forests gave way to temperate hardwood forests and, eventually, open woodland savanna. Precipitation decreased. Seasonality intensified — for the first time, the PNW experienced something resembling winter. The warm, wet, aseasonal greenhouse world was ending, replaced by the cooler, drier, seasonal icehouse world that continues today.

**Floral transformation:** Dawn redwoods, palms, and tropical hardwoods disappeared from the PNW fossil record. They were replaced by temperate deciduous trees — oaks, maples, alders, elms — and, critically, by the first significant grasslands. Retallack (2004) documented the earliest bunch grassland paleosols in Oregon dating to the late Oligocene (~25 Ma), with sod grassland paleosols appearing by the early Miocene (~19 Ma). This transition — from closed forest to open woodland to grassland — created entirely new habitats and drove the evolution of entirely new mammal communities.

**Mammalian turnover:** The Grande Coupure (the "great break") was devastating for Eocene mammal lineages. In the PNW John Day record:

- Brontotheres went extinct entirely
- Creodonts were replaced by true carnivorans (early dogs, cats, and mustelids)
- Primates disappeared from the PNW (and from all of North America by the late Oligocene)
- Multi-toed horses began their long evolutionary trajectory toward single-toed grazers
- The first dogs (Canidae) appeared — *Hesperocyon*, a small, fox-like canid, is well-represented in the John Day Formation and is one of the earliest members of the dog family anywhere in the world
- Early nimravids ("false saber-tooths") became the dominant ambush predators, occupying the cat-like ecological niche before true felids arrived in North America
- Oreodonts (Merycoidodontidae) became the dominant herbivores — these pig-to-sheep-sized animals, with no living descendants and no modern analogue, were astonishingly abundant in the Oligocene and Miocene PNW. Their fossils are the most common mammal remains in the John Day Formation

The John Day Fossil Beds preserve this transition with a stratigraphic resolution rivaled by few other sites globally. The "Turtle Cove" assemblage of the lower John Day Formation (early Oligocene, ~33–29 Ma) captures the immediate aftermath of the cooling event: a fauna stripped of its tropical elements but not yet adapted to fully open habitats. The "Kimberly" assemblage (late Oligocene, ~29–25 Ma) shows the emerging grassland-adapted community, with longer-legged ungulates, more cursorial carnivores, and the first burrowing rodents adapted to open terrain (Fremd 2010).

### 1.3 The Miocene (23–5 Million Years Ago): The Rise of Modern Families

The Miocene was the age of mammalian modernization. Global grasslands expanded dramatically, driving the radiation of the major herbivore families that dominate today. The Cascade Range began its major period of uplift, creating the rain shadow that would define the climatic divide between western and eastern PNW. Volcanic activity intensified — the Columbia River Basalt Group (17–6 Ma) buried vast areas of eastern Oregon, Washington, and Idaho under thousands of feet of lava, creating the Columbia Plateau.

**Key Miocene developments in the PNW mammal record:**

**Horses:** The horse family experienced its most dramatic radiation during the Miocene, evolving from small, three-toed forest browsers into large, single-toed grazers. The Mascall Formation (16–12 Ma) and Rattlesnake Formation (7–5 Ma) of central Oregon preserve multiple horse species representing different points along this trajectory. *Merychippus* and its descendants developed high-crowned (hypsodont) teeth for grinding abrasive grasses — a dietary shift that opened the vast new grassland biome to equid exploitation. By the late Miocene, North America hosted more horse species simultaneously than at any other time in equid evolutionary history (Prothero 2006).

**Canids:** The dog family diversified dramatically. The "bone-crushing dogs" (Borophaginae) — heavy-jawed, hyena-like canids — became the dominant large scavenger-predators of the Miocene PNW. *Borophagus* and *Epicyon* (the latter reaching the size of a large bear) are well-represented in the John Day and Rattlesnake formations. Meanwhile, the Caninae subfamily — the lineage leading to modern wolves, coyotes, and foxes — was diversifying in parallel, though they remained smaller and less conspicuous than their borophagine relatives until the Pliocene.

**Cats:** True felids (Felidae) arrived in North America from Asia during the Miocene, crossing land bridges that episodically connected the continents. The earliest North American cats were small, but by the late Miocene, large felids including early saber-toothed forms (Machairodontinae) were established. Nimravids, the "false saber-tooths" that had dominated the ambush-predator niche since the Oligocene, declined and eventually went extinct, replaced by the true cats.

**Bears:** The first bears (Ursidae) appeared in the PNW fossil record during the Miocene, represented by small, dog-like forms. The hemicyonines ("dog-bears") were a transitional group — large, cursorial predators that combined bear-like and dog-like features. They would eventually give way to the more omnivorous ursid lineages that persist today.

**Rodents and lagomorphs:** Small mammals diversified extensively. Beavers evolved into their modern form. Ground squirrels radiated across the expanding grasslands. Pocket gophers developed the fossorial adaptations that make them major soil engineers today. The earliest pikas (Ochotonidae) appeared in the PNW — ancestors of the modern American pika (*Ochotona princeps*) that is now an alpine climate sentinel (see Module 2B).

**The Cascade Rain Shadow:** As the Cascade Range rose, it progressively blocked Pacific moisture from reaching the interior. The contrast between the wet west side and the dry east side intensified through the Miocene and Pliocene, creating two increasingly distinct faunal provinces. West-side forests retained mesic-adapted species — forest-dwelling ungulates, arboreal rodents, moisture-dependent carnivores. East-side grasslands and shrub-steppe selected for cursorial grazers, burrowing rodents, and open-country predators. This biogeographic divide persists today and is reflected in the distribution of virtually every mammal species documented in Modules 1, 2A, 2B, and 2C (see also 00-ecoregion-definitions.md).

### 1.4 The Pliocene (5–2.6 Million Years Ago): The Hagerman Transition

The Pliocene bridge between the Miocene and the Pleistocene ice ages is spectacularly preserved at Hagerman Fossil Beds National Monument in southern Idaho, along the Snake River. The Hagerman fauna (approximately 3.5 Ma) captures a world on the cusp of the Ice Age — warmer than today, but cooling, with grasslands expanding and forests retreating.

**The Hagerman Horse (*Equus simplicidens*):**
The iconic fossil of Hagerman is a horse — not any horse, but the earliest known member of the modern genus *Equus*. More than 200 individuals have been excavated from a single bone bed (the "Hagerman Horse Quarry"), making it one of the largest single-species accumulations in the North American fossil record. *Equus simplicidens* was approximately the size of a modern Arabian horse, with single-toed hooves and high-crowned teeth fully adapted to grazing. It is the direct ancestor of all modern horses, zebras, and donkeys — a lineage that would spread from North America across the world before going extinct on its home continent at the end of the Pleistocene [HF1].

**The broader Hagerman fauna includes:**

- **Saber-toothed cat** (*Megantereon*) — An early machairodontine, smaller than the later *Smilodon* but with the characteristic elongated upper canines
- **Peccaries** (Tayassuidae) — Pig-like ungulates more diverse in the Pliocene than today
- **Mastodon** (*Mammut americanum*) — Already present, browsing on woody vegetation along waterways
- **Giant beaver** (*Castoroides* sp.) — An early relative of the enormous Pleistocene beaver
- **Ground sloth** (Megalonychidae) — Representing the great South American radiation that invaded North America via the Great American Biotic Interchange (GABI) after the Isthmus of Panama closed (~3 Ma)
- **Camelids** — Pliocene camels were diverse in North America; like horses, they would later go extinct on the continent that birthed them

The Hagerman fauna documents the last moment before the Pleistocene glaciations reshaped everything. Many of the lineages present at Hagerman would survive into the Pleistocene only to vanish in the end-Pleistocene extinction event. The Hagerman Horse — Idaho's state fossil — is a poignant symbol of North America's lost equids (NPS Hagerman Fossil Beds interpretive materials).

---

### 1.5 The Great American Biotic Interchange

The closure of the Isthmus of Panama approximately 3 million years ago connected North and South America for the first time in more than 100 million years, triggering one of the greatest biotic exchanges in Earth's history. The effects are visible in the PNW mammal fauna today.

**North-to-south migrants** included horses, tapirs, bears, cats, dogs, deer, rabbits, and squirrels — North American lineages that colonized South America and in many cases diversified spectacularly (e.g., South American deer). None of these south-bound migrants directly affected the PNW, but their departure reshaped community composition across North America.

**South-to-north migrants** that reached the PNW included:

- **Ground sloths** (Xenarthra) — Multiple lineages of ground sloth reached western North America. *Megalonyx jeffersonii* (Jefferson's ground sloth) was present in the PNW during the Pleistocene, with fossils recovered from sites in Oregon, Washington, and Idaho. This cow-sized herbivore browsed on trees and shrubs in temperate forests and woodland edges. Ground sloths were the only xenarthrans to reach the PNW — armadillos and anteaters did not extend this far north.

- **Virginia opossum** (*Didelphis virginiana*) — The only marsupial in the PNW fauna descends from South American didelphid stock that colonized North America during the GABI. The opossum's presence in the modern PNW is actually a relatively recent phenomenon — it was introduced to the Pacific coast in the early twentieth century and has since expanded throughout western Washington and Oregon (see Module 1). The eastern populations, however, represent a natural post-GABI colonization.

The GABI also had indirect effects on the PNW through competitive displacement. The arrival of North American predators (particularly cats and dogs) in South America drove many endemic South American predator lineages to extinction, but the northward flow of herbivores created new prey opportunities for North American carnivores and may have contributed to the diversification of large predator guilds during the Pliocene-Pleistocene transition.

## Part II: Pleistocene Megafauna of the Pacific Northwest (2.6 Million–11,700 Years Ago)

The Pleistocene transformed the PNW into a landscape of extremes. Glacial advances buried the northern third of the region under thousands of feet of ice. Interglacial warm periods created forests and grasslands teeming with the largest terrestrial mammals since the dinosaurs. And then, in a geologic eyeblink at the end of the last ice age, the megafauna vanished. Understanding the Pleistocene megafauna is essential to understanding the modern PNW mammal community, because the modern community is, in a very real sense, the survivors of a mass extinction.

---

### 2.1 The Cordilleran Ice Sheet

During glacial maxima, the Cordilleran Ice Sheet — fed by moisture from the Pacific — covered virtually all of British Columbia, the northern half of Washington, and extended tongues of ice south through the Puget Lowland to the vicinity of present-day Olympia and through the Okanogan Valley. At its maximum extent during the Late Wisconsinan glaciation (~20,000–17,000 years ago), the ice sheet was:

- Up to 2 km (6,500 ft) thick over the present site of Vancouver
- Extended south to approximately 46.5°N in the Puget Lowland
- Merged with the Laurentide Ice Sheet at its eastern margin, creating a continuous ice barrier from the Pacific to the Atlantic
- Blocked all north-south migration corridors through the interior of western North America
- Impounded glacial Lake Missoula (2,500 cubic km of water) behind ice dams, which catastrophically failed at least 40 times, sending megafloods across eastern Washington — the Channeled Scablands — at rates exceeding the combined flow of all modern rivers on Earth

The ecological consequences were profound. The ice sheet eliminated virtually all mammal habitat across British Columbia and northern Washington. Species that had occupied the region during interglacials were forced south, into refugia on unglaciated portions of the Olympic Peninsula, the Willamette Valley, the Puget Lowland south of the ice margin, or — controversially — onto ice-free enclaves within the glaciated zone itself.

---

### 2.2 The Megafaunal Community

The Pleistocene PNW, south of the ice margin, supported a megafaunal community comparable in diversity and biomass to the modern African savanna. The following species are documented from PNW fossil localities:

---

#### Columbian Mammoth (*Mammuthus columbi*)

The largest terrestrial mammal to inhabit the PNW during the Pleistocene. Columbian mammoths stood 3.7–4.0 m (12–13 ft) at the shoulder, weighed 7,000–10,000 kg (15,000–22,000 lbs), and carried tusks up to 4.9 m (16 ft) long. They were grazers and mixed feeders, consuming grasses, sedges, shrubs, and tree bark across the open grasslands and parklands of the PNW lowlands.

**PNW fossil record:** Columbian mammoth remains have been recovered from multiple sites in Washington and Oregon, including the Manis Mastodon site near Sequim (Clallam County, WA), where mammoth bones were found alongside mastodon remains and the famous bone point embedded in a mastodon rib — one of the earliest evidence of human hunting in the PNW (~13,800 years ago; Waters et al. 2011). Additional mammoth fossils have been found in the Willamette Valley, along the Columbia River, and at sites in the Snake River Plain of Idaho.

**Relationship to woolly mammoth:** *M. columbi* was a distinct species from the woolly mammoth (*M. primigenius*), which occupied tundra-steppe habitats further north. The two species' ranges overlapped in some areas, and hybridization has been demonstrated through ancient DNA analysis. In the PNW, Columbian mammoths occupied the temperate lowlands while woolly mammoths were restricted to the northernmost, most recently deglaciated areas. Both species went extinct in North America approximately 11,000–10,000 years ago (Lister & Bahn 2007).

**Ecological role:** As mega-herbivores, mammoths were landscape engineers on a massive scale. Their feeding broke down forest edges, maintained open grasslands, dispersed seeds over long distances in their dung, created water holes through digging, and trampled vegetation in ways that increased habitat heterogeneity. The loss of mammoth ecosystem engineering at the end of the Pleistocene may have contributed to the conversion of open parklands to closed forest across much of the PNW — a transition that reshaped habitat availability for all surviving species.

---

#### American Mastodon (*Mammut americanum*)

Mastodons were forest-dwelling proboscideans, distinct from mammoths in diet, habitat, and evolutionary lineage. While mammoths were grazers of open grasslands, mastodons were browsers of forests and wetland margins, feeding on conifer branches, deciduous leaves, aquatic plants, and shrubs. They were somewhat smaller than Columbian mammoths — standing 2.5–3.0 m (8–10 ft) at the shoulder and weighing 4,000–6,000 kg — with shorter, more curved tusks and a more compact body.

**PNW fossil record:** Mastodons are well-documented from the PNW, with notable finds including:

- **Manis Mastodon (Sequim, WA):** The skeleton of a male mastodon with a bone projectile point embedded in a rib, dating to approximately 13,800 years ago. This site, excavated by Carl Gustafson in 1977 and reanalyzed by Waters et al. (2011), provides some of the earliest evidence of human-megafauna interaction in North America.
- **Tualatin Mastodon (Washington County, OR):** A largely complete skeleton recovered from peat deposits in the Tualatin Valley. The site also yielded wooden stakes and a stone tool, suggesting human presence.
- **Numerous additional finds** from Puget Sound lowlands, Willamette Valley, and the Columbia Basin, typically in wetland or riparian sediments — consistent with mastodon habitat preferences.

**Evolutionary note:** Mastodons (family Mammutidae) and mammoths (family Elephantidae) are only distantly related within the Proboscidea — their lineages diverged approximately 25 million years ago. Mastodons arrived in North America from Asia in the Miocene and had been a persistent component of the PNW fauna for over 15 million years by the time they went extinct. They were among the longest-lived mammalian genera on the continent (Prothero 2006).

---

#### Ground Sloths

At least two genera of ground sloth inhabited the PNW during the Pleistocene:

**Jefferson's ground sloth (*Megalonyx jeffersonii*):** Named by Thomas Jefferson (who initially believed it was a giant lion), this was the most widespread ground sloth in North America and the only species well-documented in the PNW. *Megalonyx* was approximately ox-sized — 2.5–3.0 m (8–10 ft) in length, weighing ~1,000 kg — with massive forelimbs, large curved claws, and a prehensile upper lip adapted for grasping branches. It was a browser, feeding on leaves, twigs, and soft vegetation in forests and woodland edges.

PNW fossils of *Megalonyx* have been recovered from cave deposits in Oregon and from Pleistocene sediments in the Willamette Valley and Snake River Plain. The species ranged from Alaska to Florida, suggesting broad environmental tolerance. It went extinct approximately 11,000 years ago.

**Harlan's ground sloth (*Paramylodon harlani*):** A larger, more heavily built ground sloth than *Megalonyx*, *Paramylodon* (~1,500 kg) had dermal ossicles — small bones embedded in the skin that formed a chain-mail-like armor against predators. It was more of a grazer than *Megalonyx*, using its long tongue to pull grasses and herbs. *Paramylodon* is documented from the southern PNW (Oregon) and was more common in California and the Great Basin. It went extinct at approximately the same time as *Megalonyx*.

---

#### Saber-Toothed Cat (*Smilodon fatalis*)

The most iconic Pleistocene predator. *Smilodon fatalis* was approximately lion-sized (350–400 kg for large males) but with a radically different body plan: shorter legs, more robust forelimbs, a shorter tail, and — most distinctively — upper canine teeth up to 28 cm (11 inches) in length. These sabers were not used for biting in the manner of modern cats; instead, *Smilodon* likely used its powerful forelimbs to wrestle prey to the ground and then delivered a precise, slashing throat bite with the canines — the "canine shear bite" hypothesis.

**PNW presence:** *Smilodon* fossils are uncommon in the PNW compared to their extraordinary abundance at Rancho La Brea (Los Angeles), but the species ranged into Oregon and possibly southern Washington during the Pleistocene. Its primary prey would have been large ungulates — bison, horses, camels, and ground sloths. The PNW was likely the northern fringe of its range, with population density lower than in California and the Great Basin.

**Ecological note:** *Smilodon* was an ambush predator adapted for the open woodland and parkland habitats of the Pleistocene. Its extinction at ~11,000 years ago may have been driven by the loss of its megaherbivore prey base — when bison, horses, and camels disappeared, the specialist predator followed (Barnosky 2004).

---

#### Dire Wolf (*Aenocyon dirus*)

The dire wolf — recently reclassified from *Canis dirus* to *Aenocyon dirus* based on ancient DNA analysis showing it was not closely related to the gray wolf — was the most common large predator of the Pleistocene. Slightly larger than a modern gray wolf (60–70 kg vs. 35–60 kg for *Canis lupus*), the dire wolf had a more robust skull, more powerful jaws, and proportionally shorter legs, suggesting a less cursorial hunting style and greater reliance on scavenging and pack-based ambush hunting.

**PNW record:** Dire wolf fossils have been recovered from Pleistocene deposits in Oregon and Idaho. Like *Smilodon*, the dire wolf is most abundantly represented at Rancho La Brea, but its range extended throughout western North America.

**Taxonomic revision:** The 2021 ancient DNA study by Perri et al. (*Nature*) demonstrated that dire wolves belong to a lineage that diverged from all other canids approximately 5.7 million years ago. They were not closely related to gray wolves, coyotes, or any other living canid — despite superficial morphological similarity. This makes the dire wolf an example of deep convergent evolution: it independently evolved a wolf-like body plan from a completely separate canid lineage. The genus name *Aenocyon* ("terrible dog") reflects this taxonomic isolation.

**Extinction:** Dire wolves went extinct approximately 13,000–12,000 years ago. Unlike gray wolves, which survived the end-Pleistocene extinction, dire wolves had no living relatives and no descendants. Their extinction may reflect an inability to adapt to the rapid environmental changes and prey loss that characterized the terminal Pleistocene — a specialist predator in a world that suddenly had no room for specialists.

---

#### Short-Faced Bear (*Arctodus simus*)

The largest terrestrial carnivoran of the Pleistocene — and the largest terrestrial mammalian carnivore ever to inhabit North America. *Arctodus simus* stood 1.8 m (6 ft) at the shoulder on all fours and over 3.4 m (11 ft) when standing upright. Mass estimates range from 500 to 900 kg (1,100–2,000 lbs) for large males — 2 to 3 times the mass of the largest modern grizzly bears. The "short face" refers to the proportionally shorter snout compared to other bears, which gave it a somewhat cat-like facial profile.

**Diet and ecology:** The role of *Arctodus* has been debated. Early interpretations portrayed it as a hyper-carnivorous pursuit predator — its long legs and proportionally longer limbs than any other bear suggested cursorial ability. More recent stable isotope analysis and biomechanical modeling suggest it was more likely a mega-scavenger — using its enormous size to intimidate other predators (including *Smilodon* and dire wolf packs) away from their kills. Some evidence suggests it was an omnivore with a diet similar to modern grizzly bears, but scaled up.

**PNW record:** Fossils of *Arctodus simus* have been recovered from caves and Pleistocene sediments in Oregon, Idaho, and possibly eastern Washington. The species ranged across North America from Alaska to Mexico. It went extinct approximately 11,000 years ago, possibly in competition with grizzly bears (*Ursus arctos*) that were arriving from Beringia and expanding southward.

**Evolutionary significance:** The extinction of *Arctodus* and the simultaneous expansion of grizzly bears represents one of the clearest examples of ecological replacement in the Pleistocene-Holocene transition. Grizzly bears occupy a broadly similar niche (large omnivore-predator) but with smaller body size, more generalized diet, and greater behavioral flexibility — traits that proved more adaptive in the rapidly changing post-glacial environment.

---

#### Ancient Bison (*Bison antiquus* and *Bison latifrons*)

Two species of bison inhabited the PNW during the Pleistocene:

**Giant bison (*Bison latifrons*):** The largest bovid to inhabit North America. *B. latifrons* had a horn span of up to 2.1 m (7 ft) — more than twice that of the modern bison — and weighed an estimated 1,500–2,000 kg. It was a mixed feeder of open woodlands and grasslands. *B. latifrons* went extinct approximately 20,000 years ago, possibly replaced by the smaller, more grassland-adapted *B. antiquus*.

**Ancient bison (*Bison antiquus*):** The direct ancestor of the modern American bison (*Bison bison*). *B. antiquus* was approximately 25% larger than the modern species, with a broader horn span. It was a grazer of the open grasslands that expanded during the late Pleistocene. *B. antiquus* survived the end-Pleistocene extinction event but subsequently decreased in body size over the early-to-mid Holocene, eventually evolving into (or being replaced by) the modern *B. bison* by approximately 5,000 years ago. This body size decrease is one of the best-documented examples of Holocene dwarfing — potentially driven by habitat change, hunting pressure, or both.

**PNW bison:** Bison were present in eastern Washington and Oregon (steppe and grasslands east of the Cascades) during the Pleistocene but were never abundant west of the Cascade crest, where dense forest was unsuitable habitat. Bison persisted in parts of eastern Oregon and the northern Great Basin into the early historic period but were extirpated from the PNW by the mid-nineteenth century (Grayson 2011).

---

#### Other Pleistocene Megafauna of the PNW

- **Western camel (*Camelops hesternus*):** A large camel (taller than a modern dromedary) that inhabited the grasslands and shrub-steppe of eastern Oregon, Washington, and Idaho. North America was the evolutionary cradle of the camel family — all modern camels descend from North American ancestors that dispersed to Asia and South America. *Camelops* went extinct ~12,000 years ago.

- **American lion (*Panthera atrox*):** Larger than the modern African lion (up to 350 kg), the American lion ranged across western North America including the PNW. It was likely the apex ambush predator in open habitats. Extinct ~12,000 years ago.

- **Stag-moose (*Cervalces scotti*):** A large cervid with complex, palmate antlers, the stag-moose occupied wetland and forest-edge habitats. Rare in the PNW; more common in eastern North America.

- **Shrub ox (*Euceratherium collinum*):** A large bovid adapted to montane and shrubland habitats, distinct from bison. Fossils found in Oregon caves.

- **Horses (*Equus*):** Multiple horse species persisted in the PNW throughout the Pleistocene. All went extinct approximately 10,000–11,000 years ago. Modern horses in North America are descended from Spanish re-introductions in the sixteenth century — but they returned to a continent where their ancestors had lived continuously for 55 million years.

---

### 2.3 The End-Pleistocene Extinction (13,000–10,000 Years Ago)

The end-Pleistocene megafaunal extinction was catastrophic. In North America, approximately 35 genera of large mammals (body mass >44 kg) went extinct between approximately 13,000 and 10,000 years ago — a loss rate of ~72% of all large mammal genera. The PNW lost:

- All proboscideans (mammoth, mastodon)
- All ground sloths
- All saber-toothed cats
- The dire wolf
- The short-faced bear
- All horses
- All camels
- The American lion
- The giant bison
- The shrub ox
- The stag-moose
- The giant beaver (*Castoroides ohioensis* — a 100-kg, 2.5-m beaver unrelated to the ecological role of the modern *Castor canadensis*)

**Survivors** included grizzly bear, black bear, gray wolf, cougar, bobcat, coyote, moose, elk, mule deer, white-tailed deer, caribou, mountain goat, bighorn sheep, pronghorn, American beaver, and all small mammals. The survivors share certain characteristics: most are habitat generalists, dietary generalists, or both; most have relatively high reproductive rates; and none are as large as the largest extinct species. The Pleistocene extinction was strongly size-biased — the largest species were the most likely to go extinct.

**Cause:** The cause of the end-Pleistocene extinction remains one of the most debated topics in paleontology. The two leading hypotheses are:

1. **Human overkill (Martin 1967, updated by others):** The arrival of Clovis and pre-Clovis hunters in North America (~15,000–13,000 years ago) coincided with the extinction pulse. Megafauna that had no evolutionary experience with human predation were catastrophically vulnerable. The Manis Mastodon site in Washington provides direct evidence of human hunting of proboscideans in the PNW. Supporting evidence includes the temporal correlation between human arrival and extinction across multiple continents, the size-bias of extinction (humans preferentially hunted large animals), and modeling studies showing that even low levels of human predation could drive slow-reproducing megafauna to extinction.

2. **Climate change:** The rapid warming at the end of the Pleistocene (approximately 5°C over 1,000 years) fundamentally reorganized plant communities, fragmenting the mammoth steppe and open parklands that had supported megaherbivores. Species adapted to glacial-age habitats could not find equivalent habitat in the warmer, more forested Holocene. Supporting evidence includes the fact that many species survived multiple previous glacial-interglacial transitions, suggesting that the human factor was the novel element in the terminal Pleistocene.

3. **Synergy:** Increasingly, researchers favor a synergistic model in which climate change fragmented habitat and stressed populations, while human predation delivered the coup de grâce to already-declining species. This explains both why megafauna survived previous warm periods (no humans) and why human arrival alone was insufficient to explain the geographic and temporal pattern of extinction (Barnosky 2004; Grayson & Meltzer 2003).

The ecological consequences of megafaunal loss continue to shape the PNW landscape today. The loss of mega-herbivores may have contributed to forest closure, changes in fire regime, loss of open-habitat species, and the simplification of plant communities. Some ecologists argue that the modern PNW is a "ghost landscape" — shaped by ecological interactions with megafauna that no longer exist.

## Part III: Glacial Refugia and Post-Glacial Recolonization

### 3.1 The Ice-Free Refugia

During the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM, ~26,000–19,000 years ago), the Cordilleran Ice Sheet covered British Columbia and northern Washington, while alpine glaciers capped the Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rockies. But not everything was ice. Several areas remained ice-free and served as refugia — places where species survived the glacial maximum and from which they recolonized the deglaciated landscape.

---

#### Haida Gwaii (Queen Charlotte Islands)

**The most controversial PNW refugium.** Haida Gwaii lies off the coast of British Columbia, separated from the mainland by Hecate Strait. During the LGM, sea level was approximately 150 m lower than today, and parts of the continental shelf were exposed — but Haida Gwaii was still largely isolated by water and ice.

**Evidence for a refugium:**

- **Black bear genetic divergence:** Byun, Koop & Reimchen (1997) discovered that black bears on Haida Gwaii carry mitochondrial DNA haplotypes that diverge deeply from all mainland populations — a pattern consistent with isolation in a glacial refugium for tens of thousands of years rather than post-glacial colonization from the mainland. The Haida Gwaii bears form a distinct phylogeographic clade (Clade I) that is sister to all other North American black bear lineages. This degree of divergence implies isolation dating to at least the last interglacial (~120,000 years ago), suggesting that Haida Gwaii has served as a refugium through multiple glacial cycles.

- **Deer mouse divergence:** *Peromyscus keeni* (Keen's deer mouse) on Haida Gwaii shows similar deep genetic divergence from mainland populations — consistent with long isolation in an island refugium.

- **Marten:** Pacific marten on Haida Gwaii also show genetic signatures of long isolation, though the pattern is less well-resolved than for bears.

- **Palynological evidence:** Pollen cores from Haida Gwaii indicate that some lowland areas remained ice-free during the LGM, supporting parkland or tundra vegetation that could have sustained small mammal populations.

**Counterarguments:** Some geologists argue that Haida Gwaii was more extensively glaciated during the LGM than the refugium hypothesis requires, and that the genetic divergence could be explained by founder effects during post-glacial colonization rather than in-situ survival. The debate continues, but the weight of molecular evidence increasingly supports at least partial refugium status (Shafer et al. 2010).

---

#### The Olympic Peninsula

The Olympic Mountains were heavily glaciated during the LGM, with alpine glaciers extending down major valleys. However, the northwestern Olympic lowlands (around the Sequim area) and possibly nunatak habitats (ice-free mountain peaks projecting above the ice sheet) may have supported mammal populations through the glacial maximum.

**Evidence:**

- **Olympic marmot (*Marmota olympus*):** The Olympic marmot is endemic to the Olympic Mountains — it occurs nowhere else on Earth. Its closest relative is the hoary marmot (*M. caligata*) of the Cascades and northern Rockies. The degree of morphological and genetic divergence between the two species suggests isolation on the Olympics for a substantial period, possibly including the LGM. If Olympic marmots survived the glaciation on the peninsula (rather than recolonizing from outside and then speciating), the Olympics must have provided refugial habitat — likely alpine meadows on nunataks or ice-free lowlands at the peninsula's margins (see Module 2A for Olympic marmot ecology; Griffin 2007).

- **Olympic endemic invertebrates:** Multiple invertebrate species are endemic to the Olympic Peninsula, supporting the nunatak refugium hypothesis.

- **The Manis Mastodon site:** Located near Sequim, this site demonstrates that the northeastern Olympic lowlands were ice-free and habitable by 13,800 years ago — very shortly after the Cordilleran Ice Sheet retreated from the area. The rapidity of megafaunal presence suggests that ice-free conditions may have persisted nearby throughout the glacial maximum.

---

#### South of the Ice Margin

The most important refugium for PNW mammals was simply the unglaciated landscape south of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet's maximum extent. This included:

- **The Willamette Valley and southern Oregon Coast Range:** Never glaciated, these areas supported forest and grassland habitats throughout the LGM. Black bear, elk, deer, cougar, and most small mammal species likely persisted here.

- **Eastern Oregon and the Columbia Basin (south of the ice):** The steppe and shrub-steppe of eastern Oregon and southern Idaho remained ice-free, supporting grassland-adapted species including pronghorn, jackrabbits, ground squirrels, and badgers.

- **The Klamath-Siskiyou region:** This biogeographic crossroads, where the Cascades meet the Siskiyou Mountains at the Oregon-California border, is renowned as a refugium for plants (with extraordinary conifer diversity) and likely served the same function for mammals. The region's complex topography — deep valleys, diverse aspects, and a wide elevation range — would have provided microhabitat diversity even during the LGM.

- **The northern Great Basin and Snake River Plain:** Unglaciated and relatively productive during the LGM (with pluvial lakes providing moisture), this region supported the Pleistocene megafauna documented at Hagerman and many smaller sites.

---

### 3.2 Post-Glacial Recolonization

The Cordilleran Ice Sheet began retreating approximately 17,000 years ago. Deglaciation was rapid but uneven — coastal areas were exposed first, followed by lowland valleys, with alpine areas retaining ice for thousands of years longer. The recolonization of the deglaciated PNW by mammals followed multiple routes and timelines.

---

#### The Coastal Migration Route

As the ice retreated, a narrow corridor opened along the Pacific coast. Kelp forests, rocky shores, and river mouths provided habitat and food for marine-adapted species. This coastal route was likely the first pathway for both humans and some terrestrial mammals to move between southern refugia and the newly deglaciated north. Evidence suggests the coast was habitable by ~16,000–15,000 years ago.

**Species using this route:**
- **Sea otter** — Recolonized northward along the coast as kelp forests re-established
- **Coastal black bears** — Genetic evidence suggests coastal populations expanded northward more rapidly than interior populations, creating the distinct coastal clade visible in modern mtDNA phylogeography (Byun et al. 1997)
- **River otter, mink** — Riparian corridors along coastal rivers provided continuous habitat

---

#### The Ice-Free Corridor (Interior Route)

As the Cordilleran and Laurentide ice sheets retreated, a corridor opened between them along the eastern front of the Rocky Mountains (through present-day eastern British Columbia and Alberta). This "ice-free corridor" has long been hypothesized as the route by which humans and other species moved between Beringia and interior North America.

**Timing:** The corridor was likely not fully habitable until approximately 13,000–12,500 years ago — later than the coastal route. Even after the ice retreated, the landscape was initially barren (proglacial lakes, bare till) and required centuries of ecological succession before it could support large mammals.

**Species using the interior route:**
- **Brown/grizzly bear** — Arrived from Beringia, expanding southward through the corridor. Genetic evidence indicates that North American grizzly bears descend from multiple waves of colonization from Asia, with the earliest arrivals ~50,000+ years ago and the most recent post-LGM colonization ~13,000 years ago. The post-glacial expansion of grizzly bears into the PNW coincided with — and may have contributed to — the extinction of the short-faced bear (Davison et al. 2011; see Module 1 for modern grizzly ecology).
- **Moose** — A late-Pleistocene arrival from Beringia. Moose colonized the PNW relatively recently (~10,000 years ago), following the establishment of post-glacial boreal and mixed forests. They are absent from the Pleistocene fossil record of the PNW south of the ice margin.
- **Caribou** — Also a Beringian species. Woodland caribou (*Rangifer tarandus caribou*) colonized the mountains of British Columbia, northern Washington, and northern Idaho during the early Holocene, but have since declined dramatically. The Southern Selkirk herd in northeastern Washington/northern Idaho was extirpated in 2019 (see Module 1).

---

#### The Beringia Connection

Beringia — the land bridge connecting northeastern Asia to northwestern North America — was exposed during glacial periods when sea level was low. It served as both a two-way corridor for mammal dispersal and a refugium in its own right (eastern Beringia, in present-day interior Alaska and Yukon, was largely unglaciated during the LGM).

**Species that entered North America via Beringia:**
- **Brown/grizzly bear** (*Ursus arctos*) — Multiple waves from Asia over the past 100,000+ years
- **Moose** (*Alces alces*) — Late Pleistocene arrival
- **Caribou** (*Rangifer tarandus*) — Pleistocene arrival, circumpolar distribution
- **Wolverine** (*Gulo gulo*) — Circumpolar distribution maintained via Beringia
- **Gray wolf** (*Canis lupus*) — North American wolves descend from multiple Beringian colonization events; the modern PNW wolf population derives from recent post-extirpation recolonization from Canadian/Alaskan populations that maintain connectivity with Eurasian lineages (see Module 1)
- **Muskox** (*Ovibos moschatus*) — Present in the PNW during the Pleistocene but did not persist into the Holocene in this region

**Species that left North America via Beringia:**
- Horses (*Equus*) — Evolved in North America, dispersed to Eurasia and Africa via Beringia, went extinct in North America
- Camels (Camelidae) — Similar pattern: North American origin, Beringian dispersal to Asia, North American extinction

---

### 3.3 Assembly of the Modern Fauna

The modern PNW mammal community assembled gradually over the past 15,000 years, as species expanded from refugia, arrived from Beringia, or tracked shifting habitats across the deglaciated landscape. The process was not a simple reoccupation — it was a complex reassembly of a fundamentally altered community.

**Key patterns:**

1. **Rapid lowland recolonization, slow alpine colonization:** Lowland species (deer, black bear, coyote, small mammals) recolonized deglaciated areas within centuries of ice retreat. Alpine specialists (mountain goat, pika, hoary marmot) took longer, requiring the establishment of alpine meadow and talus habitats above the treeline.

2. **West-side vs. east-side divergence:** As the Cascade Range remained a barrier, populations on either side of the crest diverged genetically. This divergence is documented in black bear, marten, Douglas squirrel, deer mouse, and other species (see Part V below).

3. **Extinction filters:** The end-Pleistocene extinction removed all megaherbivores, all mega-carnivores except the grizzly bear, and all equids and camelids. The surviving community was smaller-bodied, more generalized, and less diverse than the Pleistocene original. The "missing" ecological roles — mega-herbivore, mega-scavenger, mega-ambush-predator — remain unfilled.

4. **Novel arrivals:** Some species in the modern PNW fauna are genuinely new arrivals with no pre-Holocene presence in the region. Moose, for example, are post-glacial colonizers from Beringia. The Virginia opossum was introduced to western Washington and Oregon in the early twentieth century, though its eastern range represents natural post-Pleistocene expansion.

5. **Community disequilibrium:** The modern PNW mammal community may not yet be in equilibrium. Species ranges continue to shift in response to climate change, habitat modification, and the ongoing effects of post-Pleistocene community reorganization. The concept of a "natural" or "baseline" community is complicated by 15,000 years of continuous change — there is no single moment when the PNW mammal fauna was "finished" assembling.

## Part IV: Island Biogeography

The PNW's island archipelagoes — from Haida Gwaii to the San Juan Islands to Vancouver Island — provide natural laboratories for studying how isolation, area, and distance shape mammal communities. Island populations diverge genetically from mainland counterparts, sometimes producing distinct subspecies or species. They also lose species through local extinction, creating depauperate communities that illuminate which ecological traits confer persistence in isolated habitats.

---

### 4.1 Vancouver Island

Vancouver Island is the largest island on the Pacific coast of North America (31,285 km²), separated from the British Columbia mainland by narrow straits (as little as 2 km at the closest point). During the LGM, the island was partly glaciated, but lowland areas — particularly the southeast coast and the Brooks Peninsula — likely served as refugia. Sea level lowstands may have briefly connected the island to the mainland via land bridges, but the connection was intermittent and of unknown duration.

**Endemic and divergent taxa:**

- **Vancouver Island marmot (*Marmota vancouverensis*):** One of the most endangered mammals in the world and the only mammal species endemic to a single Canadian island. The Vancouver Island marmot is restricted to alpine and subalpine meadows on the island's mountains. Its total population dropped to fewer than 30 individuals in the wild in 2003, prompting an intensive captive breeding and reintroduction program. As of 2024, the wild population has recovered to approximately 200–250 individuals, supplemented by captive breeding at the Calgary Zoo, Toronto Zoo, and the Marmot Recovery Foundation facility on Vancouver Island. The species' extreme endangerment results from a combination of small population size, habitat specialization (alpine meadows above 900 m), predation by wolves and golden eagles, and clearcut logging that created temporary meadow habitat but then attracted predators. Genetically, *M. vancouverensis* diverged from mainland hoary marmots (*M. caligata*) approximately 1–2 million years ago, during Pleistocene isolation on the island (Nagorsen 2005; Marmot Recovery Foundation annual reports).

- **Vancouver Island wolf:** Vancouver Island wolves are smaller than mainland gray wolves, with darker coloration and distinct genetic signatures. While not typically classified as a separate subspecies, they show measurable divergence in mtDNA from mainland BC wolves, consistent with partial island isolation.

- **Keen's deer mouse (*Peromyscus keeni*):** Originally considered a subspecies of *P. maniculatus*, Keen's deer mouse was elevated to full species status based on morphological and genetic distinctiveness. It is primarily a Pacific coastal and island taxon, with populations on Vancouver Island, Haida Gwaii, and mainland coastal BC.

- **Vancouver Island ermine (*Mustela richardsonii anguinae*):** A recognized subspecies showing dwarfism relative to mainland ermine — consistent with the "island rule" (small mammals tend to become larger on islands, large mammals tend to become smaller, but small carnivores often become smaller when prey diversity is reduced).

- **Roosevelt elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*):** Vancouver Island supports one of the largest Roosevelt elk populations in North America. While not taxonomically distinct from mainland Roosevelt elk, the island population is genetically differentiated and serves as a core population for conservation.

**Species absent from Vancouver Island:** Several mammals common on the mainland are absent from Vancouver Island, including grizzly bear, moose, mountain goat, wolverine, porcupine, and several species of ground squirrel. These absences likely reflect a combination of over-water dispersal limitation and the species' inability to maintain viable populations on the island's area. The absence of grizzly bears is particularly notable — grizzlies are present on the immediately adjacent mainland but have never colonized Vancouver Island, suggesting that even narrow water barriers are effective barriers to brown bear dispersal.

---

### 4.2 Haida Gwaii (Queen Charlotte Islands)

Haida Gwaii supports one of the most genetically distinctive mammal communities in North America, shaped by extreme isolation and possible refugial persistence through multiple glacial cycles (see Section 3.1).

**Native mammals:** Haida Gwaii has only 10 native terrestrial mammal species — an extremely depauperate fauna for an archipelago of its size (10,180 km²). Native species include black bear, marten, river otter, deer mouse (*Peromyscus keeni*), dusky shrew, ermine, and little brown bat. Notably absent are all ungulates (no deer, elk, or mountain goat are native to Haida Gwaii), all large carnivores except black bear, and most rodents.

**Black bear:** The Haida Gwaii black bear (*Ursus americanus carlottae*) is recognized as a distinct subspecies — the largest black bear subspecies in Canada and possibly in North America. Males average 150+ kg, with some exceeding 270 kg. The subspecies' large body size is attributed to a protein-rich diet of salmon and intertidal invertebrates in the absence of competition from grizzly bears. Genetically, these bears carry ancient mtDNA haplotypes (Clade I, Byun et al. 1997) that diverge deeply from all mainland black bear populations, consistent with isolation in a Haida Gwaii refugium for at least 100,000 years and possibly through multiple glacial cycles.

**Marten:** Haida Gwaii marten show genetic signatures of long isolation and are considered a distinct population. They are smaller than mainland marten, with dietary specialization reflecting the island's limited prey base.

**Introduced species:** Sitka black-tailed deer were introduced to Haida Gwaii in the early twentieth century and have since caused severe ecological damage to the understory vegetation, fundamentally altering forest structure. Raccoons, rats, squirrels, and other introductions have also modified the island ecosystem. The impacts of introduced species on Haida Gwaii illustrate how island mammal communities, shaped by long isolation, are vulnerable to disruption by species from the mainland.

---

### 4.3 San Juan Islands

The San Juan Islands archipelago (approximately 450 islands, of which ~170 are named) lies in the rain shadow of the Olympic Mountains, between Vancouver Island and the Washington mainland. The islands were completely covered by the Cordilleran Ice Sheet during the LGM and only became habitable approximately 14,000–13,000 years ago. Their mammal fauna is therefore entirely post-glacial in origin — all species colonized via over-water dispersal, land bridges exposed during early post-glacial low sea levels, or human introduction.

**Native mammals:** The San Juan Islands support a relatively impoverished terrestrial mammal fauna dominated by small-bodied species with good dispersal ability: deer mouse, Townsend's vole, river otter, mink, and several bat species. Columbian black-tailed deer are present on several islands and likely colonized during early post-glacial periods when some islands may have been connected to the mainland. Harbor seals and Steller sea lions use the islands' rocky shores as haul-outs (see Module 3).

**Absent species:** Most large mammals common on the adjacent mainland (black bear, cougar, coyote, elk, mountain beaver) are absent from the San Juan Islands. The absence of black bears is notable given that bears are strong swimmers — it suggests that the over-water distance combined with the islands' relatively small area makes colonization and persistent occupation unlikely for large-bodied species with extensive home ranges.

**Conservation significance:** The San Juan Islands provide habitat for marine mammals (see Module 3 — orca critical habitat) and serve as a natural experiment in island colonization. The relatively complete documentation of the islands' mammal fauna since European settlement allows researchers to track colonization and extirpation events in near-real-time.

---

### 4.4 The Island Rule and Body Size Evolution

PNW islands demonstrate the "island rule" — the tendency for large mammals to evolve smaller body size on islands (insular dwarfism) and small mammals to evolve larger body size (insular gigantism). Examples:

- **Vancouver Island marmot:** Smaller than its mainland relative, the hoary marmot — consistent with insular dwarfism in a large rodent.
- **Vancouver Island ermine:** Smaller than mainland ermine — consistent with dwarfism in a small carnivore when prey diversity is reduced.
- **Haida Gwaii black bear:** *Larger* than most mainland black bears — an apparent exception to the island rule, but explicable through ecological release (no grizzly bear competition) and abundant marine protein (salmon, intertidal resources). Island gigantism in carnivores can occur when food is abundant and competitors are absent.
- **Deer mouse on islands:** San Juan Island and Haida Gwaii deer mice tend toward larger body size than mainland populations — consistent with the island rule's prediction for small mammals.

The island rule is not a law — it is a statistical tendency shaped by resource availability, predator pressure, and population dynamics. The PNW's multiple island systems, each with different areas, isolation distances, and ecological contexts, provide a rich natural experiment for testing its predictions.

---

## Part V: Phylogeography — Molecular Evidence for Population Structure

Molecular phylogeography — the study of geographic distribution of genetic lineages — has revolutionized our understanding of PNW mammal history. By analyzing mitochondrial DNA, nuclear markers, and increasingly whole genomes, researchers can reconstruct the routes, timing, and barriers that shaped the genetic architecture of modern populations. The consistent theme across species is that Pleistocene glaciations and the Cascade Range are the two primary forces structuring genetic diversity in the PNW.

---

### 5.1 Black Bear (*Ursus americanus*) — A Three-Clade System

The most thoroughly studied phylogeographic system among PNW mammals. Byun et al. (1997) and subsequent studies identified three major mtDNA clades in northwestern North America:

- **Clade I (Coastal Island):** Found on Haida Gwaii, with deeply divergent haplotypes consistent with isolation in an island refugium through one or more glacial cycles. This clade is sister to all other North American black bear lineages — meaning the Haida Gwaii bears carry the most ancient surviving mtDNA lineages in the species.

- **Clade II (Coastal Mainland):** Found along the BC coast, Vancouver Island, and the Olympic Peninsula. These bears descend from coastal refugial populations that expanded northward along the coast as glaciers retreated. Clade II shows moderate divergence from interior populations, consistent with geographic separation by the Cascade-Coast Mountain barrier.

- **Clade III (Continental Interior):** Found east of the Cascades, from the Columbia Basin to the Rocky Mountains. These bears descend from interior refugia (possibly the Klamath-Siskiyou region, the Blue Mountains, or areas south of the PNW). Clade III populations expanded northward through the ice-free corridor and eastward into the Rockies.

**Cascade contact zone:** Western Washington and Oregon represent a zone of secondary contact between Clade II (coastal) and Clade III (interior) bears. Some populations show mixed haplotypes, indicating post-glacial gene flow across the Cascades — but the mountain range remains a significant barrier to dispersal, maintaining distinct genetic structure on either side.

**Conservation genetics implication:** The deep genetic structure in PNW black bears means that different populations carry different evolutionary legacies. The Haida Gwaii population is irreplaceable — its unique haplotypes represent tens of thousands of years of independent evolution that cannot be regenerated by translocating mainland bears to the island (Peacock et al. 2007).

---

### 5.2 American Marten (*Martes americana* / *Martes caurina*) — Cryptic Species

Marten phylogeography in the PNW has revealed one of the most significant recent taxonomic revisions in North American mammalogy. Molecular analysis showed that what was considered a single species — the American marten (*Martes americana*) — actually comprises two species:

- **American marten (*Martes americana* sensu stricto):** A boreal species distributed across Canada and the northern US, including the northern Cascades and northern Rockies. Associated with boreal and subboreal conifer forests.

- **Pacific marten (*Martes caurina*):** A Pacific coastal species distributed from the southern Cascades to the Sierra Nevada and coastal California, with disjunct populations in the Olympics and on Vancouver Island. The Pacific marten was formerly classified as a subspecies (*M. a. caurina*) but was elevated to full species status based on deep mitochondrial and nuclear divergence — the two lineages separated approximately 1–2 million years ago and show limited hybridization even where their ranges overlap in the northern Cascades.

**Phylogeographic structure:** Within each species, populations show additional genetic structure corresponding to glacial refugia. Olympic Peninsula marten are genetically distinct from Cascade marten, consistent with isolation during the LGM. Haida Gwaii marten are divergent from mainland populations.

**Conservation implication:** The Pacific marten is of greater conservation concern than the American marten because of its more restricted range, dependence on old-growth forest, and smaller population sizes. Recognizing it as a distinct species elevates its conservation priority (Aubry et al. 2009; see Module 1 for marten ecology).

---

### 5.3 Mountain Goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) — Alpine Fragmentation

Mountain goats are restricted to steep, rocky alpine terrain — a naturally fragmented habitat that creates isolated populations even in the absence of glaciation. Phylogeographic studies have revealed:

- **Two major lineages:** A coastal lineage (SE Alaska, coastal BC, Olympics) and an interior lineage (Rockies, Cascades). These lineages diverged during Pleistocene glacial isolation — ancestors of the coastal lineage likely survived in coastal refugia (possibly including nunataks or ice-adjacent habitat), while interior lineage ancestors persisted in unglaciated Rockies or areas south of the ice.

- **Olympic Peninsula population:** Mountain goats in the Olympic Mountains are not native. They were introduced by the State of Washington in the 1920s from populations in British Columbia and Alaska. The introduced goats have thrived but cause ecological damage to fragile alpine meadow ecosystems — particularly Olympic's endemic plant communities. NPS has undertaken controversial removal programs to protect native alpine vegetation. This case illustrates how phylogeographic data (showing the Olympic goats are genetically of coastal BC/Alaska origin, not relict Olympic Peninsula origin) can inform management decisions.

- **Cascade populations:** Mountain goats in the Washington Cascades are native and belong to the interior lineage. They show genetic structure corresponding to north-south separation along the Cascade crest, likely reflecting limited dispersal through lower-elevation passes.

---

### 5.4 Mule Deer and Black-Tailed Deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) — A Cascade Cline

Mule deer (interior) and Columbian black-tailed deer (coastal) are subspecies of *O. hemionus* that intergrade across the Cascades. Molecular analysis (Latch et al. 2009) revealed:

- **A broad hybrid zone** across the Cascades and in southern Oregon, where coastal and interior populations meet and interbreed
- **No sharp genetic boundary** between "mule deer" and "black-tailed deer" — the subspecific division is a somewhat arbitrary cut through a continuous cline of morphological and genetic variation
- **Refugial origins:** Interior mule deer likely expanded from Great Basin or Rocky Mountain refugia; coastal black-tailed deer from Pacific coastal refugia. Post-glacial secondary contact across the Cascades created the modern cline

This phylogeographic pattern is important because it demonstrates that the Cascade barrier is permeable to ungulates — a contrast with the sharper genetic boundaries seen in smaller, less mobile species like marten and deer mouse.

---

### 5.5 Deer Mouse (*Peromyscus maniculatus* / *P. keeni*) — Fine-Scale Structure

Deer mice are the most abundant mammal in the PNW (see Module 2B) and show remarkably fine-scale phylogeographic structure:

- **Multiple named subspecies** reflect genuine genetic differentiation shaped by Pleistocene isolation
- **Island populations** (San Juan Islands, Gulf Islands, Haida Gwaii) show measurable divergence from nearest mainland populations — consistent with post-glacial colonization followed by drift in small island populations
- **Keen's deer mouse (*P. keeni*):** Elevated from subspecific status within *P. maniculatus* to a full species based on molecular divergence. *P. keeni* is primarily a Pacific coastal and island form, with its range centered on Haida Gwaii, Vancouver Island, and the BC coast
- **Contact zones:** *P. maniculatus* and *P. keeni* overlap on mainland coastal BC, with some evidence of hybridization

The deer mouse phylogeographic pattern mirrors, in miniature, the patterns seen in larger mammals — glacial refugia, post-glacial recolonization, Cascade barrier effects, and island divergence — but at a finer spatial scale because mice disperse shorter distances and have larger populations subject to more rapid genetic drift in small isolates (Nagorsen 2002, 2005).

---

### 5.6 Douglas Squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*) — Cascade Endemism

The Douglas squirrel is endemic to the Pacific coast conifer forests from BC to California (see Module 2A). Phylogeographic analysis shows:

- **Deep north-south divergence** within the species, with populations north and south of the Columbia River gorge showing significant genetic differentiation
- **The Columbia River as a barrier:** The wide, low-elevation Columbia River gorge interrupts the coniferous forest habitat that Douglas squirrels require. This geographic barrier has limited gene flow and promoted divergence between Washington and Oregon populations
- **West-side restriction:** Douglas squirrels are largely absent from east of the Cascades, replaced by the closely related red squirrel (*T. hudsonicus*). Where the two species' ranges approach each other (at mid-elevation in the Cascades), they may hybridize — but the Cascade crest acts as a strong ecological and genetic barrier

---

### 5.7 Fisher (*Pekania pennanti*) — Reintroduction Genetics

Fisher phylogeography in the PNW illustrates how genetic data informs conservation action:

- **Historical extirpation:** Fishers were extirpated from Washington by the mid-twentieth century due to trapping and old-growth logging
- **Two distinct evolutionary lineages:** Pacific fishers (west of the Rockies) and boreal fishers (east of the Rockies/northern Canada) are genetically distinct, representing long-independent evolutionary lineages. The Pacific lineage is of greater conservation concern because of habitat loss and smaller population sizes
- **Reintroduction source matching:** When WDFW and NPS reintroduced fishers to the Olympic Peninsula (2008–2010) and the southern Cascades (2015–2020), they sourced animals from BC populations — genetically appropriate because BC fishers belong to the Pacific lineage that historically occupied Washington. Using boreal-lineage fishers from eastern Canada would have been inappropriate because it would have introduced a different evolutionary lineage into the PNW (Aubry et al. 2009; see Module 1 for fisher ecology)

## Part VI: Adaptive Evolution

Natural selection has produced remarkable adaptations in PNW mammals, shaped by the region's extreme topographic, climatic, and ecological gradients — from sea level to 14,411 ft (Mt. Rainier), from 200+ inches of rain per year (Hoh rainforest) to 6 inches (Columbia Basin), from dense old-growth to alpine talus to marine pelagic.

---

### 6.1 Mountain Goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) — Alpine Specialist

The mountain goat is one of the most extremely adapted alpine mammals in the world. Its suite of adaptations to steep, snowy, high-elevation terrain includes:

**Locomotion:** Concave, rubbery hoof pads provide exceptional grip on wet and icy rock. The hooves can splay wide for stability on flat surfaces and grip narrow ledges. Mountain goats routinely traverse terrain with slopes exceeding 60° — steeper than most humans can walk on without technical equipment.

**Thermoregulation:** Dense woolly undercoat (used by Coast Salish peoples for weaving — see Module 1) provides insulation at temperatures below -40°C. The thick coat is shed in summer, and mountain goats are more threatened by heat stress than cold stress — they are rarely found below 5,000 ft in summer, retreating to cool north-facing cliffs and snowfields.

**Metabolic adaptation:** Mountain goats maintain activity through PNW alpine winters that last 6–8 months, digging through snow for grasses and browsing on exposed conifers and lichens. Their rumens are highly efficient at extracting nutrition from low-quality forage.

**Anti-predator strategy:** Instead of fleeing predators (as most ungulates do), mountain goats rely on their terrain advantage — retreating to cliff faces and ledges inaccessible to wolves, cougars, and bears. Their sharp, dagger-like horns (present in both sexes) are defensive weapons used against predators and in intraspecific combat. Predation is primarily by golden eagles (targeting kids on exposed ledges) and by cougars willing to follow goats into difficult terrain.

**Evolutionary origin:** Mountain goats are the sole North American representative of the Rupicaprinae (goat-antelopes), a primarily Eurasian group that includes chamois and serow. *Oreamnos* diverged from its Eurasian relatives and crossed Beringia during the Pleistocene, adapting to the alpine environments of western North America. No close relatives exist on the continent — the mountain goat is an evolutionary singleton in the New World.

---

### 6.2 American Pika (*Ochotona princeps*) — Thermal Sensitivity and Climate Vulnerability

The American pika is the most thermally constrained mammal in the PNW and one of the most studied climate sentinel species in North America.

**Thermal physiology:** Pikas have a resting body temperature of approximately 40.1°C — among the highest of any mammal. Their thick fur (adapted for insulation in alpine environments) and high metabolic rate make them extremely vulnerable to heat stress. Experimental studies have shown that pikas die within hours at ambient temperatures above 25–28°C. They cannot dissipate heat effectively — they do not sweat, and their pinna (ear) size is too small for effective radiative cooling (unlike desert lagomorphs such as jackrabbits, which have enormous ears as thermal radiators).

**Talus dependence:** Pikas are obligate talus dwellers, living in the interstitial spaces between rocks on alpine scree slopes. The talus provides thermal buffering — subsurface temperatures remain cool even when surface temperatures are high. Pikas retreat deep into the talus during the hottest part of the day and emerge for foraging only in the cooler morning and evening hours. They do not hibernate; instead, they spend the autumn harvesting vegetation and creating "haypiles" — cured vegetation stores cached within the talus for winter consumption.

**Elevational retreat:** Across the Great Basin and southern Rockies, pika populations at lower elevations have been going extinct at increasing rates over the past century. Beever et al. (2011) documented the loss of pika populations from low-elevation sites in the Great Basin, with the lower elevation limit of viable populations shifting upward at rates consistent with regional warming. In the PNW, pikas remain abundant at high elevations in the Cascades and Olympics, but the species' long-term vulnerability to warming is well-established.

**Evolutionary context:** Pikas (Ochotonidae) diverged from rabbits and hares (Leporidae) approximately 35 million years ago. The family was far more diverse in the past — dozens of species inhabited North America during the Miocene and Pliocene. Today, only two species survive in North America: the American pika (alpine talus) and the collared pika (*O. collaris*, Alaska/Yukon). The American pika's extreme thermal sensitivity is not a "weakness" — it is the legacy of specialization for cold environments over millions of years. The problem is that the climate is now changing faster than the species can adapt (see Module 2B for full pika ecology).

---

### 6.3 Mountain Beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*) — A Living Fossil

The mountain beaver is the most primitive living rodent — sole surviving member of the family Aplodontidae, which was once one of the most diverse rodent families in North America.

**Evolutionary status:** Aplodontidae appear in the fossil record by the late Eocene (~35 Ma). During the Oligocene and Miocene, the family was represented by more than 30 species across North America. Today, *Aplodontia rufa* is the only survivor — a genuine "living fossil" that retains anatomical features (primitive jaw musculature, simple kidney structure, inability to concentrate urine) found in no other living rodent.

**Primitive traits:** The mountain beaver's kidneys cannot produce concentrated urine — a trait shared by Eocene rodents but lost in all other modern rodent lineages. This means the animal must drink or absorb water constantly, restricting it to moist habitats (wet conifer forests of the PNW coast). The mountain beaver is the most water-dependent rodent in North America. Its distribution — from southern BC to northern California, entirely west of the Cascades — is a relict of its ancient ecological requirements in a region that has remained consistently moist for millions of years.

**Fossorial adaptations:** Strong forelimbs, reduced eyes, and an extensive burrow system make the mountain beaver an effective soil engineer. Despite its common name, it is not closely related to beavers (Castoridae), does not climb trees, does not build dams, and does not live near streams. The name is a historical misnomer.

**Endemism:** The mountain beaver is endemic to the Pacific coast of North America — one of the very few mammalian families restricted entirely to the PNW bioregion. Its presence here represents 35+ million years of continuous occupation. No other region on Earth supports a living aplodontid (see Module 2B for full mountain beaver ecology; Polly & Sarwar 2014).

---

### 6.4 Cetacean Deep-Diving Adaptations

The marine mammals of the PNW (Module 3) include some of the most extreme physiological adaptations in any mammal:

**Sperm whale (*Physeter macrocephalus*):** Dives to depths exceeding 2,000 m (6,500 ft), holding breath for up to 90 minutes. Adaptations include: collapsible rib cage (prevents crush injuries from pressure), oxygen-binding myoglobin concentration in muscles 10x higher than terrestrial mammals, bradycardia (heart rate drops from 60+ bpm at the surface to 10–15 bpm during deep dives), and peripheral vasoconstriction that shunts blood to the brain and heart while reducing oxygen demand in non-essential tissues.

**Beaked whales (Ziphiidae):** Cuvier's beaked whale (*Ziphius cavirostris*), documented off the PNW outer coast, holds the mammalian diving record — a confirmed dive to 2,992 m (9,816 ft) lasting 222 minutes. The physiological mechanisms enabling this are incompletely understood but include extreme oxygen stores, tolerance of blood lactate levels that would be lethal in terrestrial mammals, and possible nitrogen management mechanisms to avoid decompression sickness.

**Harbor seal:** Local PNW harbor seals (see Module 3) routinely dive to 100–200 m, with maximum recorded dives to 500+ m. Their adaptations — high blood oxygen capacity, diving reflex, myoglobin-rich muscles — are scaled-down versions of the same suite seen in deep-diving whales.

**Orca echolocation:** Killer whale echolocation (described in detail in Module 3) represents an extraordinary adaptation for hunting in turbid PNW waters. Southern Resident orcas produce broadband clicks at frequencies of 20–100+ kHz, processing returning echoes to detect and discriminate individual salmon at ranges up to 100+ m in near-zero visibility. The neural processing required to extract prey information from complex acoustic environments — distinguishing a Chinook salmon from a sockeye salmon, from a rock, from a boat hull — represents one of the most sophisticated sensory systems in any mammal.

---

### 6.5 Fossorial Adaptations — Moles and Pocket Gophers

The subterranean niche has driven convergent evolution in two unrelated PNW mammal families (see Module 2A and Module 2C for species profiles):

**Talpidae (moles):** Three species in the PNW (Townsend's mole, coast mole, American shrew-mole) show:
- Forelimbs rotated outward with an extra sesamoid bone ("falciform bone") that effectively creates a sixth digit for digging
- Vestigial or covered eyes
- Dense, velvety fur that lies flat in any direction (no grain), allowing forward and backward movement through tunnels
- Highly sensitive Eimer's organs on the snout — touch receptors that function like a tactile "eye" in total darkness

**Geomyidae (pocket gophers):** Five or more species in the PNW show parallel adaptations:
- External fur-lined cheek pouches (the "pockets") for transporting food
- Large, protruding incisors that can be closed behind the lips — allowing the animal to gnaw through roots and soil without swallowing dirt
- Powerful forelimb musculature
- Reduced eyes and ears
- Extremely aggressive territorial behavior — pocket gophers are among the most aggressively solitary mammals known, tolerating conspecifics only during brief mating encounters

These two lineages arrived at remarkably similar solutions to the subterranean environment through wholly independent evolutionary pathways. Talpid moles have been burrowing since at least the Eocene (~45 Ma); pocket gophers since at least the Miocene (~20 Ma). Their convergent evolution in the same PNW forests and grasslands is a textbook example of how environment shapes morphology regardless of ancestry.

## Part VII: Conservation Genetics

The final chapter of PNW mammal evolutionary biology is being written now, in the currency of alleles, effective population sizes, and genetic connectivity. For many PNW species, the greatest threat to long-term viability is not habitat loss or hunting but the insidious erosion of genetic diversity in small, isolated populations.

---

### 7.1 Vancouver Island Marmot (*Marmota vancouverensis*) — The Bottleneck

The Vancouver Island marmot case study illustrates how conservation genetics operates in crisis.

**The bottleneck:** The wild population declined from an estimated 300+ individuals in the 1980s to fewer than 30 by 2003. This crash represented a severe genetic bottleneck — a loss of alleles, heterozygosity, and adaptive potential that cannot be recovered even if population numbers increase.

**Captive breeding genetics:** The captive breeding program (beginning 1997) was designed from the outset with genetic management as a priority. Breeding pairs are selected to maximize retention of genetic diversity across the captive population, using pedigree analysis and molecular data. However, the founding captive population was inevitably a subset of the already-bottlenecked wild population, limiting the genetic base.

**Reintroduction challenges:** Reintroduced marmots face two genetic challenges: (1) inbreeding depression — reduced fitness in offspring of closely related parents, manifesting as lower survival, reduced litter sizes, and increased susceptibility to disease; and (2) genetic drift — in small populations, random fluctuations in allele frequency can cause the loss of potentially adaptive genetic variation.

**Current status:** As of 2024, the combined wild and captive population is ~350 individuals, with ~200–250 in the wild. Genetic monitoring continues, and managers work to minimize relatedness in breeding pairs. The species remains critically endangered — viable long-term persistence requires a wild population of 400–600 individuals with sufficient genetic diversity to withstand environmental perturbation (Marmot Recovery Foundation; Nagorsen 2005).

---

### 7.2 Southern Resident Killer Whales — Inbreeding in the Sea

The Southern Resident killer whale population (73 individuals, 2024; see Module 3 for full profile) is one of the most genetically monitored marine mammal populations on Earth.

**Effective population size:** Estimated at 25–30 — far below the threshold of 50 typically cited as the minimum for avoiding inbreeding depression, and well below the threshold of 500 for maintaining long-term adaptive potential. The effective population size is smaller than the census size because not all individuals breed, variance in reproductive success is high, and the population's social structure (matrilineal pods) constrains mate choice.

**Inbreeding signatures:** Genetic analysis has detected reduced heterozygosity and signatures of inbreeding depression, including skeletal deformities and reduced calf survival in offspring of closely related parents. Virtually all remaining mating combinations involve some degree of relatedness.

**Historical causes:** The live-capture era (1960s–1970s) removed at least 45 individuals for marine parks. These removals — preferentially targeting young, reproductively valuable animals — reduced the effective population size below what it would have been from the census count decline alone. The captures created a genetic bottleneck whose effects compound with each generation.

**Gene flow limitation:** Southern Residents do not interbreed with Northern Resident orcas or with Bigg's orcas, despite sharing the same waters. The three ecotypes are reproductively isolated. This means there is no natural source of genetic rescue for the SRKW population — they cannot receive new genetic material from other orca populations. Their genetic fate is sealed by their social biology (Center for Whale Research; Ford, Ellis & Balcomb; see Module 3).

---

### 7.3 Fisher (*Pekania pennanti*) — Reintroduction and Genetic Rescue

Fisher reintroduction to Washington State provides a positive example of conservation genetics in action.

**Source population genetics:** WDFW and NPS sourced reintroduction animals from British Columbia populations confirmed by molecular analysis to belong to the Pacific fisher lineage — the lineage historically present in Washington. This ensured genetic compatibility with any surviving or future-translocated fishers in the region.

**Genetic diversity goals:** The reintroduction plan specified minimum numbers of founders (90+ individuals across the Olympic and Cascade reintroduction sites) to ensure adequate genetic diversity in the founding population. Animals were sourced from multiple capture locations within BC to maximize representation of the source population's genetic diversity.

**Post-release monitoring:** Genetic sampling (hair snares, scat collection) allows monitoring of reproductive success, population structure, and connectivity between the Olympic and Cascade populations. Long-term viability depends on establishing gene flow corridors between the two populations — particularly through the I-5 corridor, which is currently a major barrier to wildlife dispersal (Aubry et al. 2009; WDFW fisher reintroduction program reports).

---

### 7.4 Wolverine (*Gulo gulo*) — Connectivity and Climate

Wolverines in the PNW occupy a naturally fragmented distribution — alpine and subalpine habitats in the North Cascades, Selkirk Mountains, and northern Rockies. Their conservation genetics challenges include:

**Small population sizes:** The Washington Cascades wolverine population is estimated at fewer than 50 individuals. The North Cascades population is even smaller and may not be viable in isolation. These populations are part of a broader metapopulation that extends into BC and Alberta, but connectivity is increasingly threatened by climate change (upslope habitat contraction) and infrastructure (highways, development in valley bottoms).

**Genetic diversity:** PNW wolverines show relatively low genetic diversity compared to northern Canadian and Alaskan populations — consistent with small population size and limited connectivity. The southern PNW represents the edge of the species' range, where populations are typically more genetically depauperate.

**Snow dependence:** Wolverines are obligate snowfield denners — females require persistent spring snowpack (through mid-May) for denning. Climate projections indicate significant reductions in spring snowpack across the PNW Cascades by mid-century, potentially eliminating denning habitat from lower-elevation sites and fragmenting the remaining suitable habitat into increasingly isolated patches. No denning site locations published per safety boundary. (See Module 1 for wolverine ecology.)

**Connectivity corridors:** Maintaining genetic connectivity between the Cascades, Selkirks, and northern Rocky Mountain wolverine populations is a conservation genetics priority. Key corridors include the BC-Washington border region (Manning/Skagit corridor), the Kettle Range, and the Selkirk-Cabinet-Yaak corridor. Loss of any of these corridors would isolate PNW populations and accelerate genetic erosion.

---

### 7.5 Pygmy Rabbit (*Brachylagus idahoensis*) — The Columbia Basin Population

The Columbia Basin distinct population segment of the pygmy rabbit — the smallest rabbit in North America — provides perhaps the starkest example of conservation genetics failure and attempted rescue in the PNW.

**Decline:** The Columbia Basin pygmy rabbit population, isolated from the main species range by geographic barriers and habitat loss, declined to 16 individuals by 2001. This represented a genetic bottleneck of extraordinary severity — the effective population size was likely in single digits.

**Captive breeding:** Oregon Zoo and Washington State University initiated captive breeding with the surviving 16 founders. However, the extreme bottleneck resulted in severe inbreeding depression: reduced litter sizes, decreased kit survival, increased susceptibility to disease, and low genetic diversity in the captive colony.

**Genetic rescue attempt:** To counter inbreeding depression, managers introduced pygmy rabbits from Idaho populations (the nearest genetically compatible wild populations) into the breeding program. This "genetic rescue" approach — introducing alleles from a larger, more genetically diverse population — is standard practice for severely bottlenecked populations. The hybrid offspring showed improved survival and reproduction compared to pure Columbia Basin individuals — a classic demonstration of heterosis (hybrid vigor) reversing inbreeding depression.

**Current status:** Small numbers of captive-bred pygmy rabbits (both pure Columbia Basin and hybrid) have been released into restored sagebrush habitat in the Columbia Basin. Whether the reintroduced population can establish self-sustaining numbers remains uncertain. The species requires large, contiguous sagebrush steppe — a habitat type that has been reduced by an estimated 50% across the interior PNW due to agricultural conversion, fire, and invasive grasses (see Module 2B for full pygmy rabbit ecology). **No release site locations published per safety boundary.**

---

### 7.6 Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*) — Recolonization Genetics

The return of the gray wolf to Washington and Oregon (see Module 1 for detailed profile) provides insight into the genetics of natural recolonization:

**Source populations:** Wolves recolonizing Washington and Oregon originate from BC and Idaho populations — both of which are part of the broader Rocky Mountain/northern continental wolf population. Genetic analysis confirms that Washington's wolves are predominantly derived from these northern sources rather than from separate colonization events.

**Genetic diversity:** Despite the small current population (~40 packs in Washington as of 2024), PNW wolves benefit from ongoing gene flow with the much larger BC and Idaho populations. As long as dispersal corridors remain open, the recolonizing population should maintain adequate genetic diversity without active management intervention.

**Black coat color:** A notable proportion of PNW wolves carry the K-locus beta-defensin allele responsible for black coat color. This allele was introgressed into the wolf genome from domestic dogs several thousand years ago and has subsequently been maintained (possibly under positive selection) in forested wolf populations across North America. Black wolves are more common in forested habitats (Pacific Northwest, boreal forest) than in open tundra or grassland — suggesting a selective advantage in dense vegetation, possibly related to camouflage or thermoregulation. The persistence of a dog-derived allele in wolf populations for thousands of years illustrates how introgression can provide raw material for adaptive evolution.

---

### 7.7 Bighorn Sheep (*Ovis canadensis*) — Genetic Fragmentation

Bighorn sheep in the PNW (eastern Cascades, Blue Mountains, Selkirks) provide a cautionary example of genetic fragmentation:

**Historical connectivity:** Prior to European settlement, bighorn sheep populations across the interior PNW were connected by gene flow through a network of alpine and canyon habitats. Disease outbreaks introduced through contact with domestic sheep (pneumonia-causing *Mycoplasma ovipneumoniae*) caused catastrophic population declines in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.

**Current fragmentation:** Today, PNW bighorn populations exist as small, isolated herds — many descended from translocation programs that used founders from distant source populations. Genetic analysis reveals:
- Low within-herd genetic diversity in most PNW populations
- Evidence of founder effects from translocation (some herds descended from as few as 10–20 individuals)
- Limited gene flow between herds, even when separated by relatively short distances
- Ongoing disease risk from contact with domestic sheep in shared grazing allotments

**Cryptic refugia:** Loehr et al. (2006) found evidence from mtDNA analysis suggesting that mountain sheep survived the Pleistocene glaciations in multiple small refugia — possibly including nunataks or ice-free valleys within the glaciated zone. This challenges the simpler model of refugial persistence only south of the ice margin and implies that mountain sheep populations in the PNW carry genetic legacies of multiple independent refugial lineages.

## Summary: Evolutionary Themes of the PNW Mammal Fauna

The evolutionary biology of PNW mammals is organized around five recurring themes:

1. **Deep time continuity:** Some PNW mammal lineages — mountain beaver, pocket gopher, moles — have occupied the region continuously for 30+ million years. The PNW is not just a habitat for mammals; it is an evolutionary theater where lineages have originated, diversified, and persisted through tectonic and climatic upheaval.

2. **Glacial sculpture:** The Pleistocene glaciations are the single most important force shaping the genetic architecture of modern PNW mammals. Refugia, recolonization routes, and barriers to dispersal created during and after the ice ages are inscribed in the DNA of every species from black bears to deer mice.

3. **Cascade partition:** The Cascade Range is the primary biogeographic barrier dividing the PNW into distinct coastal and interior faunal provinces. Molecular evidence confirms that this divide is real, ancient, and maintained by limited gene flow in most species.

4. **Island divergence:** PNW islands — Haida Gwaii, Vancouver Island, the San Juan Islands — serve as natural laboratories for evolutionary biology, producing endemic taxa, preserving ancient genetic lineages, and demonstrating the island rule in action.

5. **Conservation urgency:** The genetic diversity of PNW mammals is finite and irreplaceable. Small, isolated populations lose genetic diversity through drift and inbreeding; lost alleles cannot be regenerated. Conservation genetics — source population matching for reintroduction, genetic rescue of bottlenecked populations, connectivity corridor maintenance — is not a luxury but a necessity for the long-term persistence of the PNW's evolutionary heritage.
