## Part I: Order Carnivora

The Pacific Northwest hosts approximately 25 species of terrestrial carnivores spanning 6 families -- from the gray wolf (*Canis lupus*), the apex predator whose return is reshaping entire ecosystems through trophic cascades, to the diminutive least weasel (*Mustela nivalis*), the smallest carnivore on Earth at barely 50 grams. These predators occupy every elevation band from coastal estuaries to alpine talus, and their presence or absence defines the ecological character of PNW landscapes. The ongoing recovery of large carnivores -- wolf, grizzly bear, fisher, wolverine -- represents one of the most significant wildlife management stories in North America.

**Ecological Interaction**
The gray wolf's return to the PNW has initiated measurable trophic cascades: in areas with established wolf packs, elk (*Cervus canadensis*) alter their foraging behavior, reducing browsing pressure on riparian vegetation including willow (*Salix* spp.), black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), and red alder (*Alnus rubra*). This behaviorally mediated trophic cascade -- the "ecology of fear" -- allows riparian recovery that benefits beaver (*Castor canadensis*), songbirds, and salmonid habitat. Wolf predation on coyotes (*Canis latrans*) reduces mesopredator pressure on ground-nesting birds and small mammals (mesopredator release reversal).

**Recovery Programs**
Wolf recovery in the PNW is entirely through natural recolonization from Northern Rocky Mountain populations, not reintroduction. Washington's Wolf Conservation and Management Plan (WDFW, 2011) and Oregon's Wolf Conservation and Management Plan (ODFW, 2019) guide management through phased approaches tied to population thresholds. Livestock depredation remains the primary management conflict, with lethal removal authorized under both state plans when non-lethal deterrents fail. The split ESA jurisdiction (listed in western WA/OR, delisted in eastern WA/OR) creates complex management boundaries.

**Salmon Thread**
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Salmon-connected | Yes |
| Connection | Wolves in coastal and riparian areas consume spawning salmon seasonally, transporting marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen-15, phosphorus) into terrestrial ecosystems via scat and cached carcasses. Wolf-driven elk behavioral shifts allow riparian vegetation recovery, improving salmon habitat by increasing shade (reducing stream temperatures), bank stability, and large wood recruitment. The wolf-elk-riparian-salmon trophic cascade is one of the most documented indirect ecological pathways in temperate ecosystems. |

**Ecological Interaction**
The coyote exemplifies mesopredator release: as gray wolves (*Canis lupus*) were extirpated from the PNW in the early 20th century, coyote populations expanded dramatically in both density and range, occupying ecological space formerly controlled by wolves. Coyotes suppress populations of smaller mesopredators including red fox (*Vulpes vulpes*), striped skunk (*Mephitis mephitis*), and feral cats, and their removal can trigger further cascading mesopredator release. In areas where wolves have returned, coyote densities decline 30-50% through direct killing and behavioral avoidance.

Camera-trap studies on Mt. Rainier show Cascade red fox activity concentrated above 5,500 ft where coyotes rarely venture. As climate warming pushes coyotes to higher elevations, this refugial niche is shrinking. The Cascade red fox is a key predator of alpine-adapted prey species including American pika (*Ochotona princeps*) and white-tailed ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*), both of which are themselves climate-sensitive.

**Ecological Interaction**
The cougar structures ungulate populations and behavior across the PNW through both direct predation and the landscape of fear. Where cougars are present, mule deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*) avoid steep, brushy terrain where ambush risk is highest, altering browse patterns and allowing vegetation recovery in cougar-influenced zones. Cougars and gray wolves (*Canis lupus*) partition the predator guild: wolves dominate open terrain and pursue prey in packs over distance, while cougars are solitary ambush hunters of forest and broken terrain. Where wolves have returned, cougar kill rates on elk decrease as wolves claim a larger share of the prey base, and cougars shift to smaller prey.

**Recovery Programs**
No recovery program needed; PNW cougar populations are robust. Management focuses on sustainable harvest levels, conflict reduction in the wildland-urban interface, and monitoring population trends through harvest data, track surveys, and genetic sampling.

**Ecological Interaction**
The Canada lynx is one of the most specialized predators in the PNW, with its population cycling in near-lockstep with snowshoe hare (*Lepus americanus*) abundance on an approximately 8-11 year cycle in boreal Canada. In the PNW's southern range periphery, the hare cycle is dampened and lynx populations are small and vulnerable to stochastic fluctuations. The lynx's oversized paws (foot loading approximately 30 g/cm2 versus 40-50 g/cm2 for bobcat) give it a competitive advantage over bobcat (*Lynx rufus*) in deep snow, but as climate warming reduces snowpack duration, the competitive boundary shifts in favor of the bobcat, which is a more generalist predator.

**Recovery Programs**
The Canada lynx was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 2000. Critical habitat designations include portions of the North Cascades and Okanogan Highlands in Washington. Recovery depends on maintaining deep-snowpack habitat and connected subalpine forests. Climate change is the primary long-term threat, as reduced snowpack eliminates the lynx's competitive advantage over bobcat and fragments suitable habitat.

**Ecological Interaction**
The grizzly bear is a keystone ecosystem engineer whose foraging activities reshape landscapes. Digging for glacier lily bulbs (*Erythronium grandiflorum*), ground squirrels (*Urocitellus columbianus*), and roots creates soil disturbance that increases plant diversity and nutrient cycling in subalpine meadows -- grizzly excavations turn over an estimated 5-10 tonnes of soil per bear per season. Their dominance over black bears (*Ursus americanus*) at salmon streams creates a competitive hierarchy that distributes marine-derived nutrients differently across the landscape: grizzlies feed at prime stream locations, displacing black bears to less productive reaches and upland areas, broadening the spatial distribution of marine nutrients.

**Recovery Programs**
The North Cascades Grizzly Bear reintroduction is the most significant large carnivore recovery effort in the PNW. Key regulatory milestones:
- **G06 (May 2024):** USFWS Federal Register 2024-09136 -- Final rule establishing a Nonessential Experimental Population (10(j) rule) in the North Cascades Ecosystem (NCE), allowing management flexibility.
- **G07 (Jan 2025):** USFWS Federal Register 2025-00329 -- Revised Section 4(d) rule establishing single DPS for lower-48 grizzly bears.
- **G08 (Mar 2024):** WDFW statement supporting the NPS/USFWS Final EIS for restoring grizzly bears to the North Cascades.
- The plan calls for initial translocation of 3-7 bears per year from source populations in BC or Montana over a 5-10 year period, with a population objective of 200 bears over 60-100 years.
- The Selkirk Mountains recovery zone in northeastern WA is managed as a shared population with Idaho and BC, with approximately 60-80 bears in the transboundary area.

### Family Mustelidae -- The Weasels, Wolverine, and Water Hunters

The Mustelidae are the most species-rich carnivore family in the PNW, occupying every elevation band and nearly every habitat type. **Ecological Interaction**
The fisher is one of the few predators capable of killing North American porcupines (*Erethizon dorsatum*), using a specialized face-attack technique that avoids the quill-protected dorsum. Fisher reintroduction to the Olympics and Cascades is expected to restore this predator-prey regulation, potentially reducing porcupine damage to commercially and ecologically valuable conifers.

**Recovery Programs**
Fisher reintroduction is a multi-agency success story:
- **Olympic Peninsula:** NPS, WDFW, and Conservation Northwest translocated 90 fishers from BC to Olympic NP (2008-2010). Camera-trap and DNA surveys confirm a breeding population of 50-80 individuals by 2024.
- **Southern WA Cascades:** WDFW translocated 81 fishers from BC (2015-2020) to Gifford Pinchot NF and Mt. Rainier NP. Reproduction confirmed; population expanding.
- **Oregon Cascades:** ODFW Fisher Reintroduction Project (G37) translocated fishers from BC to southern Cascades (2015-2024). Population monitoring ongoing.
- Key challenge: habitat connectivity between reintroduction sites to allow gene flow.

Conservation strategies focus on maintaining habitat connectivity across the Cascades crest and I-90 corridor (O14, Conservation Northwest).

## Part II: Ungulates (Orders Artiodactyla and Perissodactyla)

The Pacific Northwest supports approximately 12 species of ungulates spanning 4 families, from the iconic Roosevelt elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) of Olympic rainforests to the invasive feral pig (*Sus scrofa*) threatening native ecosystems. PNW ungulates are the primary large herbivores shaping vegetation communities, and their populations are directly regulated by the carnivores documented in Part I. The wolf-elk-riparian trophic cascade -- where wolf predation and predation risk alter elk foraging behavior, allowing riparian vegetation recovery -- is among the most celebrated ecological narratives in modern conservation biology.

**Ecological Interaction**
Rocky Mountain elk are the keystone prey linking wolf recovery to trophic cascades in the PNW. The cultural harvest of mountain goat wool represents a non-lethal, sustainable relationship with the species spanning millennia.

The greatest threat to PNW bighorn sheep is pneumonia transmitted from domestic sheep and goats (*Mycoplasma ovipneumoniae* and *Mannheimia haemolytica*). A single contact event between a domestic sheep band and a wild bighorn herd can trigger an all-age pneumonia die-off killing 50-90% of the population, with chronic lamb pneumonia suppressing recruitment for years afterward. This disease dynamic forces wildlife managers to maintain spatial separation between domestic and wild sheep -- a management challenge in east-side WA and OR where public-land grazing allotments overlap with bighorn habitat.

The pronghorn's extreme speed (88 km/h, sustained over several kilometers) is an evolutionary relic of Pleistocene arms races with the now-extinct American cheetah (*Miracinonyx trumani*) and other cursorial predators. Pronghorn are obligate sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) consumers in winter, making them entirely dependent on intact sagebrush steppe. The conversion and fragmentation of sagebrush habitat -- the most imperiled ecosystem in the American West -- is the primary long-term threat. Pronghorn cannot jump standard livestock fences; they pass under bottom wires, making fence modification (raised bottom wire to 18 inches) a critical conservation action.

## Cross-Reference: Trophic Cascade Narrative

### The Wolf-Elk-Riparian-Salmon Cascade

The return of gray wolves (*Canis lupus*) to the Pacific Northwest sets in motion one of ecology's most celebrated trophic cascades, connecting apex predators to streamside songbirds through a chain of interactions spanning four trophic levels:

1. **Wolves** (ROLE-APEX) establish territories and prey primarily on Rocky Mountain elk (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*) and white-tailed deer (*Odocoileus virginianus*) in northeastern WA and OR.

2. **Elk** (ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER) respond both numerically (population reduction through predation) and behaviorally (avoiding high-risk areas: riparian corridors, dense forest edges, narrow valleys). The behavioral response -- the "ecology of fear" -- is often more ecologically significant than the numerical response.

3. **Riparian vegetation** -- willow (*Salix* spp.), black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), and red alder (*Alnus rubra*) -- recovers in areas where elk avoidance reduces browsing pressure. Willow stands may increase 3-5x in height within a decade of wolf establishment.

4. **Salmon habitat** improves as riparian recovery provides increased stream shade (reducing water temperatures), bank stability (reducing sedimentation), insect drop (prey for juvenile salmon), and large wood recruitment (creating pool-riffle complexity).

5. **Songbirds and beavers** recolonize recovering riparian zones: beaver (*Castor canadensis*) dam construction in restored willow stands creates additional wetland habitat, further amplifying the cascade.

This cascade was first comprehensively documented in Yellowstone following the 1995 wolf reintroduction. In the PNW, parallel dynamics are emerging in the Blue Mountains (OR), Okanogan (WA), and Wenaha unit (OR/WA border) where wolf packs have been established for 5-10+ years.
