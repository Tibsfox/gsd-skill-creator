# Module 1: Terrestrial Carnivores & Ungulates Compendium

> **PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — "Fur, Fin & Fang"**
>
> This module profiles 38 carnivore and ungulate species native to, recolonizing, or historically present in the Pacific Northwest bioregion (Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia). Species are organized by taxonomic order and family, then by ecological significance within each family. Each profile follows the standardized template from `00-taxonomy-schema.md` and references sources from `00-source-index.md`.
>
> **Design principle:** Every species entry provides common name, scientific name, order/family, conservation status across jurisdictions, morphometrics, habitat, diet, reproduction, social structure, home range, population trend, threats, ecological role, evolutionary note, and sources. Large carnivore recovery species (grizzly bear, gray wolf, cougar) receive extended treatment reflecting their outsized ecological and policy significance.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates, den sites, or calving areas for ESA-listed species. County/watershed-level location only. Population data from USFWS, WDFW, ODFW, IDFG, NOAA, or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Human-wildlife conflict documented factually. Every Indigenous reference names a specific nation.

## 1. Order Carnivora

The PNW hosts 20+ native carnivore species spanning 6 families, from the 600-lb grizzly bear to the 1-oz least weasel. Carnivores structure ecosystems from the top down: apex predators regulate ungulate behavior and abundance, mesopredators control rodent and lagomorph populations, and mustelids serve as indicators of old-growth forest health. Several PNW carnivores are flagship species for landscape-scale conservation, and three — grizzly bear, gray wolf, and wolverine — are subjects of active federal recovery efforts.

### 1.1 Family Ursidae — Bears

---

### Grizzly Bear (*Ursus arctos horribilis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Ursidae
**Status:** Recolonizing (North Cascades) | Extirpated from most historical PNW range
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Threatened (lower-48 DPS)
- IUCN: Least Concern (global); regionally imperiled in contiguous US
- WA: Endangered
- OR: Functionally extirpated (last confirmed sighting 1930s)
- ID: Threatened (Selkirk/Cabinet-Yaak recovery zones)
- BC: Blue-listed (Special Concern), ~15,000 province-wide

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 180-215 cm (males), 150-180 cm (females)
- Mass: 180-360 kg (males), 130-200 kg (females); coastal bears larger due to salmon access
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced; males 1.5-2x female mass
- Distinguishing features: Shoulder hump of muscle mass, concave facial profile, long curved claws (7-10 cm), coat color variable from blond to dark brown — grizzled guard hairs give the common name

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Alpine meadows, subalpine parkland, montane forest, riparian corridors
- Elevation range: 500-8,000 ft; seasonal movement from high-elevation berry fields to low-elevation salmon streams
- Ecoregion: Alpine/Subalpine, Montane Conifer Forest, Riparian
- Home range: Males 200-600 sq mi; females 50-150 sq mi
- Denning: High-elevation north-facing slopes, excavated dens in root balls or hillsides; den entry October-November, emergence April-May. **No den site locations published per safety boundary.**

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — 80-90% plant material by volume in interior populations
- Foraging strategy: Hyperphagia (late summer-fall), consuming 20,000+ kcal/day pre-denning
- Seasonal variation: Spring — roots, emerging grasses, winter-killed ungulates, ground squirrels; summer — berries (huckleberry, serviceberry), insects (army cutworm moths at alpine talus); fall — salmon (coastal), whitebark pine nuts (interior), continued berries
- Prey species: Ungulate calves (elk, deer), ground squirrels, salmon (Oncorhynchus spp.)

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Polygynous, male competition through dominance hierarchies
- Breeding season: May-July; delayed implantation — blastocyst does not implant until November
- Gestation: ~235 days total (including delay); active gestation ~60 days
- Litter size: 1-3 cubs (typically 2), born in den January-February
- Weaning: 2-3 years; cubs remain with mother for 2-3 years
- Sexual maturity: Females 4-7 years; males 5-8 years
- Lifespan: 20-25 years (wild); one of the slowest reproductive rates of any North American mammal
- Interbirth interval: 3-4 years, making population recovery exceptionally slow

**Social Structure:** Solitary except mothers with cubs and aggregations at concentrated food sources (salmon streams, berry patches). Dominance hierarchies at feeding sites. Males may commit infanticide to bring females into estrus.

**Population & Recovery:**

*North Cascades Ecosystem (NCE):*
The NCE Recovery Zone encompasses approximately 9,800 sq mi of predominantly federal land (85%+), spanning North Cascades National Park, Mt. Baker-Snoqualmie and Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forests, and adjacent wilderness areas in Whatcom, Skagit, Chelan, and Okanogan counties, WA. As of 2025, no grizzly bears are confirmed resident in Washington state. The population is functionally zero — the last verified sighting in the North Cascades was a single male in 1996.

In September 2024, USFWS published a Final Environmental Impact Statement and Record of Decision (Federal Register 2024-09136) establishing a Section 10(j) experimental population designation for the NCE. The plan calls for releasing 3-7 grizzlies annually over approximately 5-10 years, sourced from interior BC populations, with a target of 200 bears over 60-100 years. The 10(j) designation provides regulatory flexibility: bears that conflict with livestock or human safety can be relocated or, in extreme cases, lethally removed — a provision designed to increase social tolerance. In March 2025, a revised 4(d) rule (Federal Register 2025-00329) further refined management authority.

*Selkirk/Cabinet-Yaak (ID/MT/BC border):*
Approximately 50-60 bears in the Selkirk ecosystem, 45-55 in Cabinet-Yaak. These small, isolated populations are connected to larger BC populations but remain fragile. Recovery efforts focus on habitat connectivity and reducing human-caused mortality.

*Historical range:*
Grizzlies once occupied the entire PNW from coastal Washington to the Blue Mountains of Oregon. Lewis and Clark documented multiple encounters in 1805-1806 along the Columbia. The species was systematically exterminated from the lower 48 during Euro-American settlement; by the 1920s, grizzlies were gone from most of Washington and Oregon.

**Threats:** Extremely slow reproduction; human-caused mortality (vehicle strikes, illegal killing, management removals); habitat fragmentation and human development in valley bottoms that serve as movement corridors; declining whitebark pine (climate-driven blister rust); reduced salmon runs; climate change compressing alpine habitat; social tolerance — public opinion divided on reintroduction.

**Ecological Role:** Ecosystem engineer and nutrient transporter. Bears carry marine-derived nitrogen from salmon carcasses deep into forests — a single bear may transport 30-40 salmon per day during peak runs, depositing carcasses up to 500 m from streams. Decomposing carcasses fertilize riparian soils, measurably increasing nitrogen content in trees within 500 m of salmon streams. Digging activity aerates alpine soils and disperses mycorrhizal fungi. As apex omnivores, grizzlies influence prey behavior and plant community composition across elevation gradients.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Ursus arctos* colonized North America from Beringia ~50,000-100,000 years ago. PNW populations were historically part of a continuous range from the coast to the Rockies. Genetic work shows interior and coastal ecotypes diverged ~10,000 years ago following glacial retreat, with coastal bears retaining larger body size due to salmon access. The California grizzly (U. a. californicus) went extinct in 1924.

**Cultural Significance:** Grizzly bear holds profound spiritual significance across PNW nations. The Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, and Tlingit include Bear in clan crest systems. In Interior Salish traditions, Grizzly Bear is a powerful spirit figure associated with strength, healing, and the mountains. Multiple nations practiced ceremonial protocols around bear hunting. The Stl'atl'imx (Lillooet) maintain grizzly bear management traditions predating European contact by millennia. Modern tribal nations including the Tulalip Tribes and Colville Confederated Tribes have been active participants in the NCE recovery consultation process.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Bald eagle, osprey share salmon-nutrient pathway; bear carcass deposits feed corvids and raptors
- Predator-prey: Grizzly predation on elk calves affects ungulate recruitment
- Salmon-bear-forest nitrogen cycle is the primary nutrient bridge between marine and terrestrial ecosystems in the PNW

**Key Sources:** [G5] USFWS Grizzly Bear Recovery Program; [G6] Federal Register 2024-09136; [G7] Federal Register 2025-00329; [G8] WDFW/NPS grizzly restoration statement; [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; Servheen et al. (1999) — Bears: Status Survey and Conservation Action Plan; Hilderbrand et al. (1999) — salmon and bear body size

### American Black Bear (*Ursus americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Ursidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Game species; estimated 25,000-30,000
- OR: Game species; estimated 25,000-30,000
- ID: Game species; estimated 20,000+
- BC: Abundant; estimated 120,000-150,000

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 130-190 cm
- Mass: 90-180 kg (males), 60-100 kg (females); highly variable with food availability
- Sexual dimorphism: Moderate; males ~1.5x female mass
- Distinguishing features: Straight facial profile (contrast grizzly's concave), short curved claws, no shoulder hump; coat color variable — black predominates west of Cascades, cinnamon/brown common east-side; rare glacier bears (blue-gray) in coastal BC/SE Alaska

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Mixed conifer and deciduous forest, riparian zones, clearcuts with berry production
- Elevation range: Sea level to 7,000 ft
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest, Lowland Forest, Riparian, Foothill/Oak Woodland
- Home range: Males 10-60 sq mi; females 5-20 sq mi
- Denning: Tree cavities, brush piles, excavated ground dens, under fallen logs; den entry November-December, emergence March-April

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — predominantly plant material (grasses, berries, nuts, cambium); insects (ants, yellow jackets); salmon where available; ungulate fawns/calves opportunistically
- Foraging strategy: Generalist; hyperphagia August-November
- Seasonal variation: Spring — skunk cabbage, grasses, winter-killed carrion; summer — berries (salal, huckleberry, blackberry), insects; fall — salmon, acorns (Oregon), apples in human-interface areas

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Polygynous
- Breeding season: June-July; delayed implantation
- Gestation: ~220 days (active gestation ~60 days)
- Litter size: 1-4 cubs (typically 2), born January-February
- Weaning: 6-8 months; cubs disperse at 16-18 months
- Sexual maturity: Females 3-5 years; males 4-5 years
- Lifespan: 15-20 years (wild)

**Social Structure:** Solitary except mothers with cubs. Less aggressive than grizzlies; will yield at salmon streams to grizzlies and dominant conspecifics. Tolerant of human proximity, leading to conflict in residential areas.

**Population Trend:** Stable to increasing across PNW. Timber harvest has paradoxically benefited black bears by creating berry-producing clearcuts interspersed with forest cover. Populations robust in all four jurisdictions.

**Threats:** Timber-damage kills (bears strip cambium from young Douglas-fir, causing significant economic losses); vehicle strikes; attractant management failures (garbage, bird feeders); poaching for gall bladder trade; climate change effects on berry phenology.

**Ecological Role:** Seed disperser (berries), nutrient transporter (salmon), nest predator (ground-nesting birds and insects). Important secondary disperser of salmon-derived nutrients, though less efficient than grizzlies. Tree-stripping behavior, while economically damaging, creates snag habitat used by cavity-nesting birds and bats.

**Evolutionary Note:** *U. americanus* diverged from the Asian black bear lineage ~4-5 million years ago. Has coexisted with grizzlies for >50,000 years; niche partitioning — black bears use denser forest, grizzlies prefer open meadows and salmon streams. Genetic structure in PNW shows distinction between Olympic Peninsula, Cascade, and east-side populations shaped by Pleistocene refugia.

**Cultural Significance:** Black bear figures prominently in Coast Salish, Sahaptin, and Chinook oral traditions. The Nlaka'pamux (Thompson) and Secwepemc (Shuswap) peoples have extensive traditions regarding bear behavior, denning, and seasonal berry-sharing. Bear grease was a valuable trade commodity across the region.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [G9] WDFW Wildlife Action Plan; Pelton (2003) — black bear ecology

### Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Status:** Recolonizing
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Endangered in western 2/3 of WA and OR; delisted in eastern WA/OR/ID (subject to ongoing litigation; January 2025 federal court ruling reinstated protections in parts of the range — see Federal Register)
- IUCN: Least Concern (global)
- WA: Endangered (statewide)
- OR: Endangered (western OR); state-managed (eastern OR since 2015)
- ID: Game species; managed hunts and trapping seasons since 2011
- BC: Game species; estimated 8,000-11,000 province-wide

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 150-180 cm (plus 35-50 cm tail)
- Mass: 35-60 kg (males), 30-45 kg (females); PNW wolves generally smaller than northern Canadian/Alaskan wolves
- Sexual dimorphism: Moderate; males ~20% heavier
- Distinguishing features: Large head, long legs, large feet for snow travel; coat color highly variable — gray, black, and tawny common in PNW Cascades populations; black coloration more frequent in forested populations (linked to K-locus beta-defensin allele, possibly introgressed from domestic dogs)

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Montane conifer forest, subalpine meadows, riparian corridors
- Elevation range: 1,000-8,000 ft; seasonal movement tracking ungulate prey
- Ecoregion: Montane Conifer Forest, Subalpine, Riparian, East-Side Steppe (ID)
- Home range: 200-500 sq mi per pack territory; home ranges larger where prey density is lower
- Denning: Excavated dens or natural cavities near water; same den sites may be used across years. **No den site locations published per safety boundary.**

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Obligate carnivore; large ungulates comprise 80-95% of diet
- Foraging strategy: Cooperative pack hunting; cursorial pursuit predation
- Seasonal variation: Elk and deer year-round; supplemented with beaver, snowshoe hare, small mammals; scavenging during deep-snow periods
- Prey species: Elk (primary in NE WA and ID), mule deer, white-tailed deer, moose (northern ID/BC), mountain goat (rare)

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Monogamous pair bond; alpha pair typically sole breeders in pack
- Breeding season: February-March
- Gestation: 62-63 days
- Litter size: 4-7 pups, born April-May
- Weaning: 5-8 weeks; pups fed regurgitated meat by pack members
- Sexual maturity: 2-3 years; many disperse to form new packs
- Lifespan: 6-8 years (wild); mortality high from intraspecific conflict, human causes

**Social Structure:** Highly social; pack = family unit of breeding pair plus offspring from 1-3 years. PNW packs average 4-8 members, smaller than Yellowstone packs. Pack size constrained by prey density and human-caused mortality. Dispersing wolves may travel 100-600+ mi to establish new territories.

**Population & Recovery:**

*Washington:*
Wolves began naturally recolonizing WA from BC and ID around 2008. The Lookout Pack (Okanogan County) was the first confirmed breeding pack. By end of 2024, WDFW documented approximately 42 packs and 260+ individuals, concentrated in the northeast (Okanogan, Ferry, Stevens, Pend Oreille counties) and north Cascades (Chelan, Whatcom counties). Expansion into the south Cascades is ongoing but slow. The Wolf Advisory Group — a stakeholder body including ranchers, conservationists, tribes, and hunters — advises WDFW on management policy. Lethal removal has been authorized for packs with documented chronic livestock depredation.

*Oregon:*
ODFW's wolf plan, originally adopted in 2005 and updated in 2019, divides the state into eastern and western management zones. Wolves delisted under state law east of Highways 395/78/95 in 2015. By late 2024, Oregon documented approximately 20+ packs and 175+ individuals, primarily in Wallowa, Union, Baker, and Umatilla counties. A few packs have established in the southern Cascades (Jackson, Klamath counties). Western Oregon wolves remain state-listed as Endangered.

*Idaho:*
Following reintroduction to central Idaho in 1995-96, the population recovered rapidly. By 2024, an estimated 1,500+ wolves in 150+ packs. Idaho has implemented aggressive harvest seasons including year-round hunting on private land and expanded trapping. Population remains well above federal recovery thresholds but has declined from peak estimates.

*British Columbia:*
Estimated 8,000-11,000 wolves province-wide, with coastal wolves (sometimes called sea wolves) forming a genetically and behaviorally distinct ecotype that feeds heavily on salmon, marine mammals, and intertidal resources. BC wolves serve as the primary source population for WA recolonization.

**Threats:** Livestock depredation conflicts and resulting lethal management; illegal killing; highway mortality (I-90, US-97); slow range expansion due to territoriality and low pack density; prey competition with cougars; disease (canine distemper, parvovirus, mange); political polarization around wolf management creating regulatory instability.

**Ecological Role:** Apex predator driving trophic cascades. Wolf predation on elk reduces overbrowsing of riparian vegetation, benefiting salmon habitat through improved stream shading and bank stability — the "ecology of fear" modifies ungulate spatial behavior even when direct predation rates are low. Wolves suppress coyote populations (mesopredator release reversal), benefiting ground-nesting birds, small mammals, and pronghorn fawn survival. Wolf kills provide carrion subsidies to ravens, eagles, magpies, wolverines, bears, and dozens of invertebrate species.

**Evolutionary Note:** PNW wolves descend from Beringian populations that colonized the continent following glacial retreat. Genetic analysis shows WA wolves derive primarily from BC and northern Rocky Mountain populations, with gene flow maintaining connectivity. Coastal BC wolves show partial genetic distinction and behavioral specialization (salmon fishing, swimming between islands) that may represent incipient ecotype divergence, though they remain *C. lupus*.

**Cultural Significance:** Wolf holds deep spiritual significance across PNW nations. Wolf is a clan crest in Kwakwaka'wakw, Tlingit, Haida, and Nuu-chah-nulth traditions. In Nez Perce cosmology, wolf (himiin) is a relative and teacher; the Nez Perce Tribe was instrumental in the 1995 Yellowstone/central Idaho reintroduction. Sahaptin and Interior Salish nations include wolf in creation narratives as a being of intelligence and loyalty.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Wolf kills subsidize bald eagle, golden eagle, raven, magpie (carrion consumers)
- Predator-prey: Wolf predation regulates elk/deer populations, driving forest regeneration and riparian health
- Salmon-bear-forest nutrient cycle: Wolf behavioral effects on elk reduce riparian browse, improving salmon habitat

**Key Sources:** [G6] Federal Register 2024-09136; [G9] WDFW Wolf Conservation and Management Plan; [G11] ODFW Wolf Plan; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Mech & Boitani (2003) — *Wolves: Behavior, Ecology, and Conservation*; Ripple et al. (2014) — trophic cascades review

### Coyote (*Canis latrans*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Unclassified | OR: Unprotected | ID: Unprotected | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 105-130 cm (including 30-40 cm tail)
- Mass: 10-18 kg
- Distinguishing features: Pointed muzzle, erect ears, slender build; grizzled gray-brown coat; bushy, black-tipped tail carried low when running (distinguishing from wolves, which carry tail horizontal)

**Habitat:** Extremely generalist — forests, meadows, agricultural land, sagebrush steppe, urban areas. Sea level to 10,000 ft. Home range: 5-25 sq mi (larger in arid east-side habitats).

**Diet & Foraging:** Omnivorous opportunist. Small mammals (voles, mice, rabbits) comprise 60-70% of diet. Also eats berries, insects, carrion, ungulate fawns, garbage in urban settings. Salmon carcasses in fall where available.

**Reproduction:** Monogamous pair bonds, often multi-year. Breeding January-March. Gestation 63 days. Litter size 4-7, born April-May. Both parents provision pups. Sexual maturity 1-2 years. Lifespan 10-14 years.

**Social Structure:** Typically mated pairs or small family groups. More social in urban environments where food concentration supports larger groups. Territorial; defend boundaries with howling and scent-marking.

**Population Trend:** Stable to increasing across PNW. Expanded range and abundance following wolf extirpation in early 1900s. Populations resilient to persecution — compensatory reproduction (larger litters when population is reduced).

**Threats:** Generally secure. Persecuted through predator control programs but populations recover rapidly. Mange outbreaks. Vehicle strikes. Anticoagulant rodenticide secondary poisoning.

**Ecological Role:** Primary mesopredator. Controls rodent and lagomorph populations. Important scavenger. Where wolves are absent, coyote populations increase (mesopredator release), which can suppress ground-nesting birds and small mammals. Wolf recolonization is expected to reduce coyote density in recovery areas.

**Evolutionary Note:** *C. latrans* originated in North America ~1 million years ago. Range has expanded dramatically since European settlement — historically a Great Plains/western species, now found coast-to-coast. PNW coyotes show gene flow with eastern coyotes (which carry wolf and dog admixture), though western populations remain genetically more "pure."

**Cultural Significance:** Coyote is the preeminent Trickster figure across virtually all PNW First Nations. In Sahaptin, Chinook, Coast Salish, Klamath, Nez Perce, and Okanagan oral traditions, Coyote stories carry moral teachings, explain the origin of salmon, rivers, and death, and encode ecological knowledge about seasonal resource availability.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Bekoff & Gese (2003); [G9] WDFW

### Red Fox (*Vulpes vulpes*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Status:** Resident (native mountain populations); introduced (lowland)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: Unclassified; Cascade red fox (*V. v. cascadensis*) is a WDFW Species of Greatest Conservation Need (SGCN)

**Morphometrics:** Body length 90-110 cm (including 35-45 cm tail). Mass 3.5-7 kg. Distinguishing features: Orange-red coat, black legs and ear tips, white-tipped tail. Melanistic (silver/cross fox) variants occur.

**Habitat:** Native Cascade red fox (*V. v. cascadensis*) — alpine and subalpine meadows above 4,000 ft, one of North America's rarest fox subspecies, estimated <500 individuals. Lowland red foxes in the Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, and agricultural areas are descended from introduced European stock and possibly eastern US populations, and are genetically distinct from native mountain foxes.

**Diet & Foraging:** Voles, mice, pikas, marmots (mountain populations); rabbits, squirrels, berries, insects, garbage (lowland populations). Caches food under snow.

**Reproduction:** Monogamous. Breeding December-February. Gestation 52 days. Litter 3-6 kits. Dens in rock piles, hollow logs, or excavated burrows.

**Ecological Role:** Mesopredator in alpine and lowland systems. Mountain populations are important pika and marmot predators. Lowland introduced foxes may compete with native gray foxes and suppress ground-nesting bird populations.

**Evolutionary Note:** Native PNW mountain red foxes are relicts of a Pleistocene population that retreated to high-elevation refugia as climate warmed. Genetically distinct from lowland introduced foxes — represents a separate colonization from Beringia. Conservation genetics work by Aubry et al. (2009) clarified this dual-origin pattern.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; Aubry et al. (2009) — native vs. nonnative red fox genetics; [G9] WDFW SGCN list

### Gray Fox (*Urocyon cinereoargenteus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Status:** Resident (southern OR, marginally into southern WA)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | OR: Unprotected

**Morphometrics:** Body length 80-110 cm. Mass 3-5 kg. Distinguishing features: Grizzled gray coat with rufous sides and throat, black dorsal stripe on tail (no white tip — distinguishing from red fox). Strong recurved claws.

**Habitat:** Oak woodlands, mixed forests, chaparral. Limited PNW range — primarily Rogue/Umpqua valleys in OR, marginally into SW WA (Clark, Skamania counties). Elevation 500-4,000 ft.

**Diet & Foraging:** Omnivorous — small mammals, insects, berries, acorns, birds. More frugivorous than red fox.

**Reproduction:** Monogamous. Breeding February-March. Gestation 53 days. Litter 2-5. Dens in hollow trees, rock crevices, brush piles.

**Ecological Role:** Unique among canids in its ability to climb trees — uses vertical escape, foraging, and denning. Important seed disperser for manzanita and madrone berries. Indicator species for Oregon oak woodland/mixed forest health.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Urocyon* is the most basal living canid lineage, diverging from all other canids ~9-10 million years ago. Represents an ancient North American radiation predating the Canis/Vulpes split. The PNW populations are at the northern edge of the species' range.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P1] Maser 1998; Fritzell & Haroldson (1982)

### Kit Fox (*Vulpes macrotis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Status:** Resident (east-side steppe, limited range)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable

**Morphometrics:** Body length 70-85 cm. Mass 1.5-3 kg (smallest North American canid). Distinguishing features: Extremely large ears (proportionally), pale sandy-gray coat, slender build adapted for arid environments.

**Habitat:** Sagebrush steppe and shrublands of eastern Oregon (Harney, Malheur, Lake counties). Arid basins with loose soils for denning. Not documented in WA — PNW range limited to OR Basin and Range.

**Diet & Foraging:** Nocturnal hunter of kangaroo rats, pocket mice, ground squirrels, insects, lizards. Water obtained primarily from prey.

**Reproduction:** Monogamous or occasionally polygynous. Breeding December-February. Gestation 49-55 days. Litter 2-6. Extensive den systems with multiple entrances.

**Ecological Role:** Primary nocturnal predator in sagebrush steppe small-mammal guild. Prey base overlap and competition with coyotes — kit fox populations suppressed where coyote density is high.

**Evolutionary Note:** Closely related to swift fox (*V. velox*); some authorities consider them conspecific. PNW populations at the northwestern limit of species range.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; McGrew (1979) — kit fox ecology

### Cougar (*Puma concolor*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Felidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | ID: Game species

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 150-275 cm (including 55-80 cm tail)
- Mass: 55-100 kg (males), 35-60 kg (females); PNW cougars among the largest subspecies
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced; males 1.5-2x female mass
- Distinguishing features: Uniform tawny/tan coat (no spots in adults), long heavy tail with black tip, small rounded head relative to body, powerful hindquarters for leaping (can clear 5 m vertically, 12 m horizontally)

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Dense forest (old-growth and second-growth), rocky terrain, riparian corridors
- Elevation range: Sea level to 10,000 ft; uses all elevation bands
- Ecoregion: All terrestrial zones — generalist in habitat but requires cover for ambush hunting
- Home range: Males 50-150 sq mi; females 20-60 sq mi; male territories overlap multiple female ranges
- Denning: No fixed den; females select sheltered sites (rock overhangs, dense brush, fallen logs) for birthing

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Obligate carnivore; ungulates comprise 60-80% of diet
- Foraging strategy: Solitary ambush/stalk predator; typically kills with bite to base of skull/neck
- Prey species: Black-tailed deer (primary west-side), mule deer (east-side), elk calves, mountain goats, bighorn sheep, beaver, porcupine, snowshoe hare
- Kill rate: 1 ungulate per 7-14 days; caches and revisits kills over 2-5 days

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Polygynous; brief pair bond during estrus
- Breeding season: Year-round, but peak births March-July
- Gestation: 90-96 days
- Litter size: 1-4 kittens (typically 2-3), born spotted
- Weaning: 3-6 months; kittens remain with mother 12-18 months, learning hunting
- Sexual maturity: Females 2-3 years; males 3 years but rarely breed until establishing territory
- Lifespan: 10-13 years (wild)

**Social Structure:** Solitary and territorial. Males aggressively defend territories against other males — intraspecific conflict is a leading cause of mortality. Females are more tolerant of overlapping ranges. Communication through scrapes (ground scratches with urine/feces), scent-marking, and vocalizations (screams, chirps, purrs — cougars cannot roar).

**Population Trend:** Estimated 2,000-2,500 in WA, 6,000+ in OR, 2,000-3,000 in ID. Populations stable to increasing. Cougar density highest in western Cascades with robust deer populations.

**Threats:** Habitat fragmentation and road mortality; human-wildlife conflict at the urban-wildland interface (increasing as development expands); hound-hunting controversy; prey base competition with recolonizing wolves; climate-driven shifts in deer distribution.

**Ecological Role:** Primary apex predator across most of the PNW where wolves are absent. Top-down regulation of deer and elk populations prevents overbrowsing and maintains forest regeneration. In Olympic National Park, cougar presence maintains riparian vegetation critical for salmon by keeping elk herds moving (Ripple & Beschta 2006). Cougar kills provide carrion for scavengers including bears, eagles, ravens, and beetles. Cougars suppress coyote and bobcat populations through interference competition.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Puma concolor* has the largest range of any terrestrial mammal in the Western Hemisphere (Yukon to Patagonia). North American populations passed through a severe genetic bottleneck ~10,000 years ago following Pleistocene megafauna extinction — all North American cougars show remarkably low genetic diversity compared to South American populations. Despite this, behavioral and size variation across the range is substantial. Closely related to the jaguarundi, not to Old World large cats.

**Cultural Significance:** Cougar features in oral traditions of multiple PNW nations. Significant in Sahaptin and Interior Salish cosmology as a powerful, solitary hunter. Among the Nlaka'pamux, cougar is associated with independence and skill. Coast Salish peoples recognized cougar as a top predator whose presence indicated healthy deer populations.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Ripple & Beschta (2006) — trophic cascades in Olympic NP; Logan & Sweanor (2001) — *Desert Puma*; [G9] WDFW

### Canada Lynx (*Lynx canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Felidae
**Status:** Resident (rare, declining)
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Threatened (contiguous US DPS) | IUCN: LC (global) | WA: Threatened | OR: Threatened (historical, extremely rare)

**Morphometrics:** Body length 80-105 cm. Mass 7-14 kg. Distinguishing features: Tufted ears (2-4 cm black tufts), large snowshoe-like feet, short bobbed tail with entirely black tip (distinguishing from bobcat's white-banded tip), long legs, thick gray-brown coat with faint spotting.

**Habitat:** Boreal and subalpine conifer forest with deep snowpack — lynx's large feet give competitive advantage over other predators in soft snow. Elevation 3,000-7,000 ft. In WA, limited to Okanogan, Chelan, and Whatcom counties — north Cascades and Okanogan Highlands. Home range 12-80 sq mi, varying with snowshoe hare density.

**Diet & Foraging:** Specialist predator — snowshoe hare (*Lepus americanus*) comprises 60-97% of diet. Also takes red squirrels, grouse, voles. Population cycles track the 8-11 year snowshoe hare cycle, though the cycle is dampened at the southern range edge (PNW).

**Reproduction:** Solitary except during breeding. Mating March-April. Gestation 63-70 days. Litter 1-5 kittens (mean 2-3, strongly correlated with hare abundance). Kittens disperse at 10 months.

**Population Trend:** Declining in PNW — at southern edge of continental range. Climate change is the primary long-term threat, as warming reduces snowpack depth and duration, eroding lynx's competitive advantage and shifting boreal forest northward. Perhaps 50-200 individuals in WA; status in OR uncertain — sporadic detections.

**Threats:** Climate-driven snowpack loss; forest management reducing mature subalpine habitat; competition with bobcat (which is expanding upslope as snow decreases); highway mortality; trapping (legal in BC, not in WA/OR).

**Ecological Role:** Specialist predator in snowshoe hare cycle. Lynx-hare dynamics are a textbook example of predator-prey oscillation. In the PNW, lynx represents the southernmost extent of the boreal predator guild; its loss would remove the primary specialized predator in subalpine ecosystems.

**Evolutionary Note:** Evolved in North America ~2.5 million years ago, closely related to Eurasian lynx (*L. lynx*). Pleistocene distribution extended much further south. Current PNW populations are relictual boreal fragments, genetically connected to BC populations but increasingly isolated by climate change.

**Key Sources:** [G5] USFWS Lynx Recovery Outline; [G9] WDFW SGCN; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Ruggiero et al. (2000) — *Ecology and Conservation of Lynx in the United States*

### Bobcat (*Lynx rufus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Felidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: Game species | OR: Game species

**Morphometrics:** Body length 70-100 cm. Mass 5-15 kg. Distinguishing features: Spotted/streaked coat, short bobbed tail with black bars and white underside tip (contrasting lynx's all-black tip), smaller ear tufts, smaller feet than lynx.

**Habitat:** Generalist — forests, shrublands, rocky terrain, oak woodlands, urban-edge. Sea level to 7,000 ft. More common at lower elevations than lynx. Home range 5-30 sq mi.

**Diet & Foraging:** Rabbits, rodents, birds, reptiles; occasionally deer fawns. Ambush predator using cover and patience. More dietary generalist than lynx.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Breeding February-March. Gestation 62 days. Litter 2-4 kittens. Dens in rock crevices, hollow logs, brush.

**Population Trend:** Stable to increasing. Expanding into higher elevations as snowpack declines, potentially competing with lynx. Common throughout PNW — estimated thousands in each state.

**Ecological Role:** Mesopredator controlling rabbit and rodent populations across mid-elevation and lowland habitats. Expanding upslope range overlap with lynx is a conservation concern — bobcats may outcompete lynx in reduced-snow conditions.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Anderson & Lovallo (2003)

### 1.4 Family Mustelidae — Weasels, Martens, Otters

---

### Wolverine (*Gulo gulo*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident (rare, alpine specialist)
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Threatened (November 2023 listing for contiguous US DPS) | IUCN: LC (global) | WA: Candidate | OR: Threatened

**Morphometrics:** Body length 80-110 cm (including 17-26 cm tail). Mass 10-18 kg (males), 7-12 kg (females). Distinguishing features: Stocky, powerful build (pound-for-pound among the strongest mammals); dark brown coat with pale lateral stripe ("lope stripe"); massive feet for snow travel; powerful jaws capable of crushing frozen bone.

**Habitat:** Alpine and subalpine zones above 5,000 ft. Requires persistent spring snowpack (>1 m depth through mid-May) for denning — this makes wolverines one of the most climate-vulnerable mammals in the PNW. In WA, documented in North Cascades and limited Cascades south to Mt. Adams. In ID, Selkirk and Sawtooth ranges. Extremely rare in OR — sporadic detections in Wallowas. Home range: Males 100-400+ sq mi; females 50-150 sq mi — among the largest of any carnivore relative to body size.

**Diet & Foraging:** Scavenger-predator. Feeds on winter-killed ungulates, marmots, ground squirrels, pikas, snowshoe hares. Powerful enough to defend carcasses from bears and wolves. Caches food in snow for later retrieval. Can travel 20+ mi in a single day across rugged alpine terrain.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Breeding May-August; delayed implantation with birth in February-March (in snow dens — persistent snowpack is essential). Litter 1-3 kits. Kits remain with mother through first summer. Sexual maturity 2-3 years. Lifespan 7-12 years.

**Social Structure:** Solitary; male territory encompasses multiple female territories. Low population density — perhaps 1 individual per 100 sq mi.

**Population Trend:** Estimated 300-500 in contiguous US, with perhaps 50-100 in WA/OR combined. Declining due to climate change reducing persistent snowpack. The 2023 ESA listing was driven primarily by climate modeling showing >60% loss of wolverine habitat by 2100.

**Threats:** Climate change (snowpack loss threatening reproductive denning); small isolated populations vulnerable to stochastic extinction; low reproductive rate; trapping (historical; now illegal in WA/OR); backcountry recreation disturbing den sites; highway fragmentation.

**Ecological Role:** Alpine scavenger-predator that cleans winter-killed ungulates and maintains nutrient cycling in high-elevation ecosystems. Indicator species for intact alpine wilderness. Wolverine presence signals healthy, connected alpine habitat with adequate snowpack and prey base.

**Evolutionary Note:** Circumboreal distribution — same species in Scandinavia and Siberia. North American populations descended from Beringian ancestors. Remarkable endurance athlete: metabolic rate allows sustained travel across high-altitude snow that would exhaust most other predators. The 100+ mile circuits wolverines make through alpine terrain are among the most impressive movement patterns of any terrestrial mammal.

**Key Sources:** [G9] WDFW SGCN; Federal Register November 2023 wolverine listing; [P4] Moskowitz 2010; Copeland et al. (2010) — wolverine and snow; Inman et al. (2012) — wolverine ecology

### American Marten (*Martes americana*) & Pacific Marten (*Martes caurina*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- *M. americana* (American/northern marten): Federal: Not listed | WA/OR/ID: Furbearer (managed)
- *M. caurina* (Pacific/coastal marten): Federal: Proposed Threatened (coastal DPS in OR/CA); WA: SGCN; OR: Sensitive-Critical (coastal populations)

**Morphometrics:** Body length 50-65 cm (including 15-23 cm tail). Mass 0.5-1.2 kg. Distinguishing features: Sleek, cat-like mustelid; brown coat with orange/cream throat patch; large eyes and rounded ears. Pacific marten slightly smaller with more orange throat.

**Habitat:** Old-growth and mature conifer forest with complex structure — large logs, snags, root wads providing den sites and subnivean (under-snow) access. *M. americana* primarily in Cascades and Rockies montane forest (3,000-7,000 ft). *M. caurina* in coastal old-growth and subalpine forest; coastal populations critically reduced by logging. Home range 1-10 sq mi.

**Diet & Foraging:** Red squirrels, voles, chipmunks, pikas; also berries, birds, insects. Agile climber pursuing squirrels through canopy. Subnivean hunting of voles beneath snowpack.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous with delayed implantation. Breeding July-August; birth March-April. Litter 1-5 (typically 2-3). Dens in tree cavities, rock piles, large downed logs.

**Ecological Role:** Indicator species for old-growth forest health. Marten presence/absence used as proxy for forest structural complexity in management planning. Preys on red squirrels that cache conifer seeds, indirectly influencing forest regeneration patterns. Coastal Pacific marten is the most range-restricted marten taxon in North America.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; Zielinski & Duncan (2004) — marten habitat; USFS PNW Research Station

### Fisher (*Pekania pennanti*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Reintroduced (WA); recovering (OR, BC)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed (western WA/OR population reviewed but not listed) | WA: Endangered | OR: Sensitive-Critical | BC: Blue-listed

**Morphometrics:** Body length 75-120 cm (including 30-40 cm tail). Mass 2-5.5 kg (males), 1.5-2.5 kg (females) — one of the most sexually dimorphic mustelids. Distinguishing features: Dark brown to black coat, broad head, powerful build; name is a misnomer — fishers rarely fish.

**Habitat:** Dense conifer and mixed forest with complex structure (snags, large logs, canopy closure). Elevation 500-6,000 ft. Requires large-diameter trees for denning. Home range 5-30 sq mi (males), 3-15 sq mi (females).

**Diet & Foraging:** The only reliable predator of porcupines — fisher circles and attacks the face repeatedly until the porcupine weakens. Also takes snowshoe hares, squirrels, mountain beaver, birds, berries, carrion. Arboreal and terrestrial hunter.

**Reproduction:** Delayed implantation. Breeding March-April; birth following March (nearly 1-year cycle). Litter 1-4 kits. Dens obligately in large-diameter tree cavities or broken-top snags — this denning requirement makes fishers dependent on old-growth structural elements.

**Population & Recovery:**
Fishers were extirpated from Washington by the mid-1900s due to trapping and old-growth logging. Beginning in 2008, WDFW, NPS, and the U.S. Geological Survey launched a multi-year reintroduction program:
- Olympic Peninsula (2008-2010): 90 fishers released from BC source populations; monitoring shows successful breeding and population establishment
- South Cascades (2015-2020): 69 fishers released; breeding confirmed
- North Cascades (2018-2020): 89 fishers released; population establishing

In Oregon, remnant populations in the Siskiyou Mountains are slowly recovering. Fisher recovery is a conservation success story in progress, demonstrating that large-landscape reintroduction of forest carnivores is feasible.

**Ecological Role:** Porcupine population control — in ecosystems without fishers, porcupine populations can increase and damage trees through bark stripping. Fisher presence maintains this predator-prey balance. Also serves as indicator of mature/old-growth forest with structural complexity.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Pekania* was recently separated from *Martes* based on molecular phylogenetics — fishers are not closely related to martens despite similar appearance. Evolved in North America; no Old World equivalent.

**Key Sources:** [G9] WDFW Fisher Recovery; Lewis et al. (2012) — fisher reintroduction; [P1] Maser 1998

### Short-tailed Weasel / Ermine (*Mustela erminea*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:** Body length 22-32 cm (including 4-9 cm tail). Mass 40-100 g. Distinguishing features: Brown dorsal/white ventral in summer; pure white in winter except black tail tip (retained year-round — distinguishes from long-tailed weasel's proportionally longer tail and least weasel's lack of black tip).

**Habitat:** All forested and meadow habitats from sea level to alpine. Uses rock piles, brush, log jams for cover. Home range 10-100 acres.

**Diet & Foraging:** Obligate carnivore specializing in voles, mice, pikas, chipmunks — enters rodent burrows. Can kill prey larger than itself (rabbits). High metabolic rate requires eating 40-60% of body weight daily.

**Reproduction:** Delayed implantation. Breeding May-July; birth following April-May. Litter 4-9 (large relative to body size). Female may breed while still nursing.

**Ecological Role:** Primary small-mammal predator in alpine and subalpine zones. Population fluctuates with vole cycles. Important prey for raptors (northern goshawk, great horned owl) and larger carnivores.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; King & Powell (2007)

### Long-tailed Weasel (*Neogale frenata*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:** Body length 30-45 cm (including 10-15 cm tail — proportionally longer than ermine). Mass 80-250 g. Summer brown/white; winter white at higher elevations, may retain brown at low elevations.

**Habitat:** More generalist than ermine — forests, meadows, agricultural areas, riparian zones. Sea level to 8,000 ft. Home range 30-200 acres.

**Diet & Foraging:** Voles, mice, chipmunks, ground squirrels, rabbits, birds, eggs. More likely to take larger prey than ermine. Surplus kills and caches food.

**Reproduction:** Delayed implantation. Breeding July-August; birth April-May. Litter 4-8.

**Ecological Role:** Mid-elevation small-mammal predator complementing ermine's alpine specialization. Important in agricultural pest control (rodents in farm fields).

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998

### American Mink (*Neogale vison*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA/OR/ID: Furbearer

**Morphometrics:** Body length 50-70 cm (including 17-23 cm tail). Mass 0.5-1.5 kg. Rich dark brown coat with white chin patch. Semi-aquatic; partially webbed feet.

**Habitat:** Riparian zones, wetlands, lakeshores, coastal areas. Rarely far from water. Sea level to 5,000 ft. Home range: Linear territories along waterways, 1-5 km of stream bank.

**Diet & Foraging:** Semi-aquatic predator — fish, crayfish, frogs, muskrats, waterfowl, eggs, voles. Dives and pursues prey underwater. Dens in bank burrows, muskrat lodges, root tangles.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous with delayed implantation. Breeding January-March; birth April-May. Litter 3-6 kits. Solitary except during breeding.

**Ecological Role:** Apex semi-aquatic mesopredator in riparian ecosystems. Controls muskrat populations (preventing overgrazing of wetland vegetation). Competes with river otter where ranges overlap but is smaller and more terrestrial. Important in fur trade history — fur farms have released domestic mink that hybridize with wild populations.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Lariviere (1999)

### North American River Otter (*Lontra canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA/OR: Furbearer

**Morphometrics:** Body length 90-130 cm (including 30-50 cm tail). Mass 5-14 kg. Distinguishing features: Streamlined body, thick rudder-like tail, webbed feet, dense waterproof underfur (up to 57,000 hairs/sq cm), small ears that close underwater.

**Habitat:** Rivers, lakes, estuaries, coastal marine waters. Requires clean water with adequate fish populations. Dens in bank burrows, beaver lodges, under root wads. Home range: 5-50 km of waterway.

**Diet & Foraging:** Fish (slowbottom species preferred — sculpins, suckers, non-game fish), crayfish, frogs, mussels, occasional waterfowl. Cooperative group hunting documented. Playful behavior (sliding, wrestling) may serve social bonding and hunting practice functions.

**Reproduction:** Delayed implantation. Breeding March-April; birth following February-April (~1 year cycle). Litter 1-5 pups (typically 2-3). Pups remain with family group 6-12 months.

**Social Structure:** More social than most mustelids — family groups of mother with young, and occasional male associations. Play behavior prominent and frequently observed.

**Ecological Role:** Top aquatic predator in freshwater systems. Otter presence indicates good water quality and healthy fish populations — biosentinel species. Sprainting (scat deposition) at latrine sites transfers aquatic nutrients to terrestrial food webs.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Lontra* is endemic to the Americas; diverged from Old World otters (*Lutra*) ~5 million years ago. River otters are the most aquatic mustelid in North America after sea otters. Populations recovered from historical fur trade depletion and are now stable across the PNW.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Melquist & Dronkert (1987); [G9] WDFW

### 1.5 Family Mephitidae — Skunks

### Striped Skunk (*Mephitis mephitis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mephitidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:** Body length 55-80 cm (including 18-30 cm tail). Mass 1.5-5.5 kg. Distinguishing features: Black with two white dorsal stripes; bushy tail; distinctive aposematic warning coloration. Paired anal scent glands can spray musk 3-5 m with remarkable accuracy.

**Habitat:** Generalist — forests, grasslands, agricultural areas, suburban/urban. Sea level to 5,000 ft. Home range 0.5-5 sq mi.

**Diet & Foraging:** Omnivorous — insects (grubs, beetles), small mammals, eggs, berries, garbage. Important consumer of yellow jacket wasp nests (digs them up, immune to stings through thick fur). Nocturnal.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Breeding February-March. Gestation 63 days. Litter 4-7. Communal denning in winter (females and young).

**Ecological Role:** Important insect predator and scavenger. Significant consumer of ground-nesting wasp and bee nests, rodents, and invertebrates. Rabies vector in eastern PNW (less so west of Cascades). Prey for great horned owls (which lack sense of smell), coyotes, and bobcats.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Rosatte & Lariviere (2003)

### Western Spotted Skunk (*Spilogale gracilis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mephitidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA/OR: Game/furbearer — populations appear declining

**Morphometrics:** Body length 35-55 cm (including 10-18 cm tail). Mass 300-700 g — much smaller than striped skunk. Distinguishing features: Black with irregular white spots and stripes (broken pattern); performs a distinctive handstand (vertical threat display) before spraying.

**Habitat:** Rocky areas, brushy forest edges, old buildings. More tied to cover and rocky terrain than striped skunk. Sea level to 6,000 ft.

**Diet & Foraging:** Insects, small mammals, berries, eggs. More carnivorous than striped skunk. Excellent climber — hunts in trees and rock faces.

**Reproduction:** Delayed implantation unique in Mephitidae. Breeding September-October; birth April-May. Litter 2-5.

**Ecological Role:** Small-mammal and insect predator in rocky/brushy habitats. Population status poorly known — may be declining due to habitat loss and rodenticide exposure. The handstand display is an energy-efficient warning that precedes the costly spray.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Verts et al. (2001)

### 1.6 Family Procyonidae — Raccoons & Ringtails

---

### Raccoon (*Procyon lotor*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Procyonidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:** Body length 60-95 cm (including 20-30 cm tail). Mass 4-12 kg. Distinguishing features: Black facial mask, ringed tail, dexterous front paws with tactile sensitivity rivaling primate hands (hypersensitive fingertips, enhanced when wet).

**Habitat:** Extremely generalist — riparian zones, forests, wetlands, urban/suburban. Highest densities near water and in urban areas. Sea level to 5,000 ft. Home range 0.5-3 sq mi (smaller in urban environments).

**Diet & Foraging:** Omnivorous — crayfish, fish, frogs, bird eggs, berries, garbage, pet food. Tactile foraging in water ("washing" behavior is actually sensory exploration with wetted paws). Nocturnal. Highly intelligent problem-solver.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Breeding January-March. Gestation 63 days. Litter 2-5 kits. Dens in tree hollows, chimneys, attics, storm drains.

**Social Structure:** Females form loose neighborhood associations. Males more solitary but form bachelor coalitions. Complex social memory — recognize and remember individual conspecifics.

**Ecological Role:** Omnivorous mesopredator with significant impacts on ground-nesting birds (waterfowl, shorebirds) and turtle nests. Urban populations reach high density, becoming vectors for canine distemper, rabies (less common in PNW than eastern US), and raccoon roundworm (*Baylisascaris procyonis*).

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Gehrt (2003)

### Ringtail (*Bassariscus astutus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Procyonidae
**Status:** Resident (limited PNW range)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | OR: Protected nongame

**Morphometrics:** Body length 60-80 cm (including 30-40 cm tail — tail longer than body). Mass 0.7-1.5 kg. Distinguishing features: Large eyes, oversized ears, fox-like face, long banded tail (14-16 alternating black and white rings). Extremely agile climber — can rotate hind feet 180 degrees for headfirst descent.

**Habitat:** Rocky canyons, talus slopes, cliff faces with adjacent woodland. In PNW, limited to southern Oregon — Rogue River canyon, Klamath Basin, scattered Deschutes and John Day river canyons. Absent from WA. Elevation 500-5,000 ft.

**Diet & Foraging:** Nocturnal predator of insects, small mammals, birds, berries, lizards. Agile enough to catch bats. Dens in rock crevices, hollow trees, abandoned buildings — historically called "miner's cat" for denning in mine shafts.

**Reproduction:** Monogamous pair bond. Breeding February-May. Gestation 51-54 days. Litter 1-4.

**Ecological Role:** Nocturnal small-mammal and insect predator in rocky canyon habitats. Occupies a niche similar to gray fox but more specialized for cliff/rock environments. PNW populations at the far northern extent of species range.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Bassariscus* is one of two living genera in Procyonidae endemic to North America (with *Procyon*). Fossil ringtails date to Miocene — an ancient lineage. PNW populations are genetically connected to California/Great Basin populations through canyon corridors.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Poglayen-Neuwall & Toweill (1988); [P1] Maser 1998

## 2. Order Artiodactyla — Ungulates

The PNW hosts 8+ native ungulate species spanning 3 families, with an additional species (pronghorn) in its own family. Ungulates are the primary large herbivores shaping forest structure, meadow composition, and riparian vegetation. They are also the prey base sustaining apex carnivores — the wolf-elk and cougar-deer relationships are among the most important ecological interactions in the region. Three PNW ungulates carry ESA or equivalent protections, and several are culturally foundational to Indigenous nations.

### 2.1 Family Cervidae — Deer, Elk, Caribou

---

### Roosevelt Elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC (species) | WA: Game species | OR: Game species

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 200-270 cm
- Mass: 320-500 kg (bulls), 260-330 kg (cows) — largest subspecies of elk in North America
- Sexual dimorphism: Extreme; bulls 1.5-2x cow mass, grow antlers up to 120 cm length (shorter but more massive than Rocky Mountain elk antlers)
- Distinguishing features: Dark brown body, lighter tan rump patch, massive neck and shoulder in bulls; antlers shed February-March, regrown by August

**Habitat:**
- Primary: Temperate rainforest, old-growth conifer forest, coastal prairies, riparian areas
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft; primarily lowland to mid-elevation
- Ecoregion: Lowland Forest, Montane Conifer, Riparian, Coastal
- Home range: Herds range over 10-50 sq mi seasonally; limited elevational migration compared to Rocky Mountain elk
- Key areas: Olympic Peninsula (largest unmanaged herd in North America, estimated 5,000+ in Olympic National Park), Willapa Hills, Oregon Coast Range, Quinault Valley, Hoh Rainforest

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasses, sedges, forbs (spring-summer); browse — salal, huckleberry, vine maple, sword fern, conifer seedlings (fall-winter)
- Foraging strategy: Grazer-browser; shifts from grazing in open meadows to browsing in forest understory seasonally
- Daily intake: 6-9 kg dry matter
- Impact: Heavy elk browsing in riparian zones can reduce willow and cottonwood regeneration, affecting salmon habitat — this is the mechanism through which cougar/wolf predation indirectly benefits salmon

**Reproduction:**
- Mating system: Harem polygyny; bulls compete through bugling, posturing, and antler sparring during fall rut
- Breeding season: September-October (rut)
- Gestation: 240-262 days
- Litter size: 1 calf (twins extremely rare), born May-June
- Weaning: 3-5 months
- Sexual maturity: Cows 2-3 years; bulls physiologically mature at 2 but rarely breed until 5-6 (competition)
- Lifespan: 12-18 years (females); 10-14 years (males, higher mortality from rut stress and hunting)

**Social Structure:** Cow-calf herds of 10-50 animals year-round, led by experienced matriarch. Bulls solitary or in bachelor groups outside rut. During rut, dominant bulls assemble and defend harems of 10-30 cows. Post-rut, bulls separate. Complex vocal communication — bugling, barking, mewing.

**Population Trend:** Stable in core areas (Olympic NP, Coast Range). Estimated 7,000-9,000 in WA, 60,000+ in OR (combined Roosevelt and Rocky Mountain). Olympic NP herd is internationally significant — no hunting or predator control, allowing natural dynamics to operate.

**Threats:** Habitat loss to development and timber harvest; agricultural conversion of lowland meadows; disease (hoof rot — *Treponema*-associated digital dermatitis recently documented in SW WA/NW OR, causing severe lameness and mortality); vehicle-elk collisions; poaching; climate change affecting forage quality.

**Ecological Role:** Keystone herbivore structuring forest understory composition through browsing. Elk grazing maintains meadow openings and prairie habitat. Wallows create micro-wetlands used by amphibians. Carcasses (winter mortality) provide critical food for scavengers. The elk-cougar-wolf trophic cascade is the primary top-down force shaping PNW west-side forest structure.

**Evolutionary Note:** *C. c. roosevelti* was named for Theodore Roosevelt. Diverged from Rocky Mountain elk populations during Pleistocene glacial isolation west of the Cascades. Adapted to dense rainforest with shorter, more compact antlers (long tines would tangle in vegetation). Historically ranged from northern California to Vancouver Island; populations contracted but have recovered from early 20th-century lows.

**Cultural Significance:** Roosevelt elk are central to the cultural identity of the Quinault, Hoh, Quileute, and Makah nations of the Olympic Peninsula. Elk hunting, antler and hide use, and seasonal round movements tracking elk herds are documented across these nations. The Quinault Indian Nation manages elk habitat on reservation lands. The Hoh Tribe has co-management agreements with NPS for elk in the Hoh Rainforest.

**Cross-Links:**
- Predator-prey: Primary prey of cougar (Olympic Peninsula) and recolonizing gray wolf
- Riparian impact: Elk browsing of willow/cottonwood affects salmon stream shading and bank stability
- Trophic cascade: Cougar predation on elk in Olympic NP maintains riparian vegetation (Ripple & Beschta 2006)

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; Ripple & Beschta (2006); WDFW elk management; Houston et al. (1990) — *North American Elk: Ecology and Management*

### Rocky Mountain Elk (*Cervus canadensis nelsoni*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | All PNW jurisdictions: Game species

**Morphometrics:** Body length 200-260 cm. Mass 280-450 kg (bulls), 230-300 kg (cows). Somewhat smaller than Roosevelt elk but with longer, more branching antlers (up to 150 cm, 6-7 tines per side typical of mature bulls).

**Habitat:** Montane forest, alpine meadows, sagebrush steppe (winter range). Pronounced elevational migration — summer in subalpine meadows (5,000-8,000 ft), winter in lower-elevation valleys and south-facing slopes. In PNW: eastern WA (Blue Mountains, Selkirks, east Cascades), eastern OR (Blue Mountains, Ochoco Mountains, Hart Mountain), Idaho (throughout mountainous regions), interior BC.

**Diet & Foraging:** Grazer-browser with stronger grazing tendency than Roosevelt elk. Mountain meadow grasses, sedges, and forbs in summer; dried grasses, bitterbrush, and conifer browse in winter. Winter range quality is the primary population-limiting factor.

**Reproduction:** Similar to Roosevelt elk. Rut September-October. Bulls more vocal (bugling) than Roosevelt elk. Gestation 240-260 days. Single calf born May-June.

**Social Structure:** Similar to Roosevelt elk but herd sizes larger on open east-side range — winter aggregations of 100-500+ on feed grounds. Major migration corridors between summer alpine range and winter valleys — these corridors are conservation priorities as development fragments them.

**Population Trend:** Stable to increasing in most PNW range. OR estimated 60,000+ elk statewide (combined subspecies). WA east-side herds managed at population objectives. ID manages one of the largest elk herds in the lower 48. Winter feeding programs (supplemental hay) are controversial — may promote disease transmission (brucellosis, chronic wasting disease).

**Threats:** Habitat fragmentation of migration corridors; potential chronic wasting disease (CWD) introduction — not yet confirmed in WA/OR but present in ID since 2021; vehicle collisions on highways crossing migration routes; fire suppression reducing meadow habitat; competition with livestock for winter range.

**Ecological Role:** Primary large herbivore in east-side ecosystems. Grazing maintains meadow diversity and prevents conifer encroachment. Wallows and trail networks create landscape heterogeneity. Elk are the primary prey sustaining wolf packs in northeast WA and central Idaho.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; WDFW/ODFW/IDFG elk management plans; Wisdom et al. (2005) — elk habitat effectiveness

### Columbian White-tailed Deer (*Odocoileus virginianus leucurus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Resident (restricted range)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Threatened (Columbia River DPS — Wahkiakum/Cowlitz counties WA, Clatsop/Columbia counties OR); Douglas County OR population delisted in 2003 following recovery
- IUCN: Not separately assessed (subspecies)
- WA: Endangered
- OR: Threatened (lower Columbia populations)

**Morphometrics:** Body length 140-175 cm. Mass 45-80 kg (bucks), 35-55 kg (does). Smaller than white-tailed deer subspecies east of the Rockies. Distinguishing features: White underside of tail displayed when alarmed (flagging behavior); antlers with main beam and vertical tines (unbranched, unlike mule deer's dichotomous branching).

**Habitat:** Bottomland riparian forest, wet prairies, and agricultural fields along the lower Columbia River floodplain. Elevation: sea level to 500 ft. Uniquely dependent on lowland riparian habitats that are among the most heavily modified landscapes in the PNW. Core range in Wahkiakum and western Cowlitz counties, WA, and adjacent Oregon. Julia Butler Hansen Refuge for the Columbian White-tailed Deer (USFWS) is the primary protected area.

**Diet & Foraging:** Browse (willow, red-osier dogwood, rose), forbs, agricultural crops (grass hay, clover). Highly dependent on riparian deciduous vegetation.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut November. Gestation 200 days. 1-2 fawns born May-June. Fawns use hiding strategy (unlike mule deer fawns' flight strategy).

**Population Trend:** Columbia River DPS estimated at 900-1,100 individuals (2020s), recovered from fewer than 300 in the 1960s when listed. Douglas County population recovered to 6,000+ and was delisted. Recovery constrained by limited floodplain habitat and competition with more abundant Columbian black-tailed deer.

**Threats:** Floodplain development and agricultural conversion; flooding (both natural and exacerbated by climate change); predation by coyotes and bobcats on fawns; competition with black-tailed deer; vehicle mortality on SR-4 corridor; extremely limited range makes population vulnerable to stochastic events.

**Ecological Role:** Indicator species for lower Columbia River floodplain ecosystem health. Its restricted range encompasses one of the most ecologically important and threatened habitat types in the PNW — lowland riparian forest and wet prairie. Recovery efforts for this deer benefit the entire floodplain community including Columbian white-tailed deer, waterfowl, salmon, and native plants.

**Evolutionary Note:** *O. v. leucurus* is a relict subspecies isolated west of the Cascades when white-tailed deer range contracted during the Holocene. Historically occupied the Willamette, Umpqua, and lower Columbia valleys. Population fragmentation and habitat loss reduced it to two isolated populations by the mid-20th century.

**Key Sources:** [G5] USFWS; USFWS Columbia White-tailed Deer Recovery Plan; [G9] WDFW; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; Smith (1985) — Columbian white-tailed deer ecology

### Mule Deer (*Odocoileus hemionus hemionus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA/OR/ID: Game species

**Morphometrics:** Body length 140-180 cm. Mass 55-100 kg (bucks), 45-70 kg (does). Distinguishing features: Large ears (mule-like), dichotomously branching antlers (forking rather than tines from main beam), white rump patch, black-tipped tail, bounding "stotting" gait (all four feet leaving ground simultaneously).

**Habitat:** East-side sagebrush steppe, pine/juniper woodland, montane forest. Elevational migrant — summer at 4,000-8,000+ ft, winter at lower-elevation shrublands. Core PNW range: eastern WA, eastern OR, throughout ID, interior BC. Home range 2-20 sq mi.

**Diet & Foraging:** Browser — sagebrush, bitterbrush, mountain mahogany, juniper, forbs. Sagebrush is critical winter forage; mule deer cannot persist without it. Less dependent on grasses than elk.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut November-December. Gestation 200 days. 1-2 fawns (twins common in productive habitat). Fawns use stotting escape strategy rather than hiding.

**Social Structure:** Does form matrilineal groups. Bucks in bachelor groups outside rut. Migration corridors are traditional and matrilineally transmitted — does teach migration routes to fawns.

**Population Trend:** Declining in portions of PNW range, particularly eastern OR and parts of ID. Causes complex — likely combination of habitat degradation (juniper encroachment into sagebrush, fire suppression), increasing predation pressure (cougar), disease, highway mortality on migration corridors, and drought reducing forage quality.

**Threats:** Sagebrush habitat loss (conversion, fire, invasive grasses); highway fragmentation of migration corridors; predation (cougar primary predator, coyotes on fawns); epizootic hemorrhagic disease (EHD); potential CWD; energy development (wind farms, solar) fragmenting range.

**Ecological Role:** Primary herbivore in east-side sagebrush/steppe ecosystems. Browse patterns influence shrub community composition. Primary prey for cougar east of Cascades. Migration corridors connect seasonal habitats across 50-100+ miles — these corridors are shared with pronghorn and elk, making corridor protection a multi-species conservation strategy.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; WDFW/ODFW mule deer management; Sawyer et al. (2009) — mule deer migration corridors

### Columbian Black-tailed Deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA/OR: Game species

**Morphometrics:** Body length 130-170 cm. Mass 45-90 kg (bucks), 40-60 kg (does). Smaller and darker than mule deer. Distinguishing features: Black or dark brown upper surface of tail (not white rump like mule deer), antlers branch dichotomously but smaller than mule deer.

**Habitat:** West-side conifer forest (old-growth to clearcut), riparian, suburban edge. Sea level to 5,000 ft on the west slope of the Cascades. Most abundant ungulate in western WA and OR. Home range 0.5-3 sq mi; generally non-migratory or short-distance elevational movements.

**Diet & Foraging:** Browse — salal, huckleberry, red huckleberry, vine maple, Douglas-fir seedlings, sword fern, forbs. Major consumer of forest regeneration, making them both ecologically important and silviculturally significant (seedling damage).

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut November-December. Gestation 200 days. 1-2 fawns born June. Fawn mortality 40-60% in first 6 months (bears, coyotes, eagles, bobcats).

**Social Structure:** Small matrilineal groups of 2-5 animals. Bucks solitary or in small bachelor groups. Less gregarious than mule deer or elk.

**Population Trend:** Stable overall but fluctuates with timber harvest cycles — populations boom in young clearcuts with abundant browse, then decline as forests mature and canopy closes. Estimated 300,000+ in WA, 400,000+ in OR.

**Threats:** Vehicle mortality (extremely common); predation (cougar primary predator; black bears and coyotes take fawns); habitat homogenization from even-aged timber management; urban development eliminating edge habitat; hair-loss syndrome (suspected mite-related, western WA).

**Ecological Role:** The most abundant ungulate in western PNW — primary prey base for cougar, bobcat, coyote, and black bear. Browse pressure shapes forest understory composition. Extremely high fawn mortality makes black-tailed deer a critical prey subsidy for a guild of predators including golden eagle and bald eagle (carrion).

**Evolutionary Note:** Black-tailed deer is the westernmost subspecies of *O. hemionus*, isolated west of the Cascades. Hybridization zone with mule deer along the Cascade crest is well-documented — intermediate forms common in transition habitats. Genetic analysis supports subspecific rather than species-level distinction.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; [G9] WDFW; Wallmo (1981) — *Mule and Black-tailed Deer of North America*

### Woodland Caribou (*Rangifer tarandus caribou*) — South Selkirk DPS

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Status:** Functionally extirpated from contiguous US (2019)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Endangered (South Selkirk DPS)
- IUCN: Vulnerable (species globally)
- WA: Endangered
- ID: Endangered
- BC: Red-listed (Threatened) — southern mountain caribou populations declining

**Morphometrics:** Body length 180-220 cm. Mass 110-210 kg. Sexual dimorphism: Moderate. Distinguishing features: The only cervid in which both sexes grow antlers; large hooves that splay for snowshoe effect and cratering through snow to reach lichens; pelage shifts from dark brown (summer) to grayish-brown (winter) with white neck and mane.

**Habitat:** Old-growth subalpine forest (Engelmann spruce, subalpine fir) in winter; alpine meadows and high-elevation ridges in summer. Caribou require deep-snow subalpine forests where they access arboreal lichens (*Bryoria* spp.) hanging from old-growth trees — this makes them obligate old-growth dependent. Elevation 4,000-7,000 ft. Historical range in PNW: Selkirk Mountains (NE WA, northern ID panhandle, SE BC).

**Diet & Foraging:** Winter: Arboreal lichens (Bryoria, Alectoria) — caribou are the only large mammal in North America that depends on arboreal lichens as a primary winter food. Summer: Forbs, grasses, sedges, mushrooms, ground lichens. Caribou do not compete well with other ungulates and avoid areas of high moose/deer density (these attract predators).

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut October. Gestation 228 days. Single calf born May-June. Calving in isolated high-elevation terrain to avoid predators. Low reproductive rate — 1 calf per year maximum.

**Population & Extirpation:**
The South Selkirk herd was the last remaining caribou population in the contiguous United States. At listing in 1983, approximately 25-30 animals used habitat in the Selkirk Mountains straddling the WA-ID-BC border. Despite decades of recovery effort (predator management, maternal penning in BC, habitat protection), the population declined steadily. Causes: Logging of old-growth subalpine forest fragmented habitat; forest roads increased predator (wolf, cougar) access; moose and deer colonized logged habitat, bringing predators into caribou range (apparent competition); climate change reduced snowpack quality.

In 2019, the last remaining animals from the South Selkirk herd (~3 individuals) were captured and moved to a BC captive breeding facility. The species is now functionally extirpated from the contiguous United States. Whether captive-bred caribou can ever be reintroduced to the Selkirks depends on restoring old-growth forest, managing predator access, and addressing climate-driven habitat changes — a multi-decade proposition at best.

**Threats:** Old-growth logging (primary historical driver); apparent competition (logging creates habitat for moose/deer, which support predator populations that spill over into caribou habitat); climate change reducing snowpack and shifting forest composition; extremely small population size (genetic inbreeding, Allee effects); recovery requires landscape-scale old-growth restoration that conflicts with timber industry.

**Ecological Role:** Indicator species for intact old-growth subalpine forest. Caribou presence requires large, unroaded blocks of mature/old-growth subalpine forest — the same habitat needed by wolverine, grizzly bear, and lynx. Caribou extirpation from the contiguous US is a signal of ecosystem-level failure in old-growth management. Lichen consumption and cratering (digging through snow to reach ground lichens) creates feeding sites used by voles and other small mammals.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Rangifer tarandus* is a Holarctic species — same species as European reindeer. Southern mountain caribou (the ecotype including the Selkirk herd) represent the southernmost naturally occurring caribou population in the world. They are genetically distinct from northern herds and specially adapted to deep-snow mountainous terrain. Their loss from the contiguous US represents the southward contraction of an Ice Age range.

**Cultural Significance:** Caribou are significant to the Kalispel Tribe of Indians and the Ktunaxa Nation, whose traditional territories encompassed the Selkirk caribou range. The Kalispel Tribe participated in recovery planning and has expressed commitment to caribou restoration as a cultural and ecological priority.

**Key Sources:** [G5] USFWS South Selkirk Caribou Recovery Plan; [G9] WDFW; IDFG caribou monitoring; Wittmer et al. (2005) — apparent competition; Seip (1992) — caribou decline and predation

### Mountain Goat (*Oreamnos americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Bovidae (subfamily Caprinae)
**Status:** Resident (native in Cascades; introduced then removed from Olympics)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: Game species (limited harvest) | OR: Sensitive

**Morphometrics:**
- Body length: 140-180 cm
- Mass: 60-100 kg (billies), 50-70 kg (nannies)
- Sexual dimorphism: Moderate; both sexes bear black, dagger-like horns (never shed, unlike antlers), with male horns thicker and more curved
- Distinguishing features: Pure white coat with dense woolly underlayer; beard; muscular shoulders and compact build; specialized hooves with hard outer edge and soft, rubbery inner pad providing extraordinary grip on rock

**Habitat:** Alpine and subalpine cliff faces, rocky outcrops, and steep terrain above 5,000 ft. Uses cliff terrain as predator escape habitat — goats are virtually immune to predation when on cliffs. Descends to lower-elevation forest in severe winter storms. In PNW: Cascades (WA, limited OR), Selkirks (ID/WA border), Coast Mountains (BC).

*Olympics controversy:* Mountain goats were introduced to the Olympic Mountains in the 1920s (not historically native). By the 2010s, approximately 700+ goats were degrading fragile alpine plant communities in Olympic National Park. From 2018-2020, NPS relocated approximately 325 goats to the north Cascades (augmenting native populations) and removed others via helicopter and ground operations. Remaining goats are subject to ongoing management.

**Diet & Foraging:** Alpine grasses, sedges, forbs, mosses, lichens, and shrubs. Mineral licking at natural salt licks — goats will travel long distances and cross dangerous terrain for mineral access. Winter diet: dried grasses, alpine cushion plants, and whatever vegetation protrudes from snow.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous; billies compete through horn displays and lateral sparring during rut (November-December). Gestation 150-180 days. Single kid (rarely twins), born May-June on isolated cliff ledges. Kids follow mothers within days and are climbing steep terrain within weeks. Sexual maturity 2-3 years. Lifespan 12-15 years.

**Social Structure:** Nanny-kid groups; billies solitary or in bachelor groups except during rut. Females are dominant over males outside rut through aggressive horn use. Strict dominance hierarchy within female groups.

**Population Trend:** Estimated 2,000-3,000 in WA Cascades (augmented by Olympic transplants), small populations in OR (Wallowas, limited Cascades), 2,000+ in ID. BC populations robust in Coast Mountains. Generally stable in native range.

**Threats:** Climate change reducing alpine habitat area and snowpack; backcountry recreation disturbance at mineral licks and kid-rearing cliffs; low reproductive rate limits recovery from population declines; disease (pneumonia from domestic sheep/goats — see bighorn sheep entry); small isolated populations on individual peaks vulnerable to local extinction.

**Ecological Role:** Alpine herbivore whose grazing and hoof action on steep terrain contributes to soil disturbance and plant succession in alpine meadows. Mineral lick visitation creates concentrated areas of soil disturbance and nutrient deposition used by other wildlife. Mountain goat wool (shed naturally on rocks and brush) was traditionally collected for weaving.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Oreamnos* is endemic to North America — not a true goat (more closely related to the rupicaprids: chamois and serow) despite its common name. Evolved in mountain habitats of northwestern North America; no Old World equivalent. Pleistocene range extended further south; retreated to current alpine refugia as climate warmed.

**Cultural Significance:** Mountain goat wool was the primary fiber for Coast Salish weaving traditions — one of the few fiber-arts traditions in North America using animal fiber (alongside dog wool). The Squamish, Musqueam, Sto:lo, and Tsleil-Waututh nations developed sophisticated techniques for collecting shed wool from alpine brush and rocks. Mountain goat horn was carved into spoons, ladles, and ceremonial implements. The Stl'atl'imx, Nlaka'pamux, and Secwepemc peoples hunted mountain goats in the Coast and Cascade ranges as part of seasonal rounds.

**Cross-Links:**
- AVI companion: Golden eagle documented predating mountain goat kids at cliff sites
- Cultural: Coast Salish wool source connects to heritage-bridge ethnobotanical textile traditions

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; Festa-Bianchet & Cote (2008) — *Mountain Goats*; Suttles (1987) — Coast Salish weaving; NPS Olympic Mountain Goat Management Plan (2018)

### Bighorn Sheep (*Ovis canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Bovidae (subfamily Caprinae)
**Status:** Resident (restored/augmented populations)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (Rocky Mountain and California bighorn); Sierra Nevada DPS listed as Endangered (not in PNW)
- IUCN: LC
- WA: Game species (limited permits); California bighorn subspecies (*O. c. californiana*) native to east-side
- OR: Game species (limited permits)
- ID: Game species

**Morphometrics:** Body length 150-180 cm. Mass 55-120 kg (rams), 45-70 kg (ewes). Distinguishing features: Massive curling horns in rams (up to 15 kg, a full curl can reach 100+ cm); horns grow continuously and are never shed — age can be determined by growth rings. Ewes have short, slightly curved horns. Brownish-gray coat, white rump patch.

**Habitat:** Steep, rocky terrain with adjacent open grassland — cliffs for escape, grasslands for foraging. In PNW, primarily east-side canyon country: Hells Canyon (Snake River, OR/ID border — one of the most important bighorn areas in the West), John Day River canyon, Yakima River canyon, Blue Mountains, Owyhee canyonlands. Elevation 500-8,000 ft.

**Diet & Foraging:** Grazer — native bunchgrasses (bluebunch wheatgrass, Idaho fescue), forbs, and browse. Requires open sightlines to detect predators — avoids dense brush and forest. Water-dependent; visits water daily in summer.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut November-December. Rams compete through dramatic head-butting clashes — charges at 30+ mph, impact force absorbed by double-layered skull and horn structure. Gestation 175 days. Single lamb born April-May. Lambs follow ewes immediately on steep terrain.

**Social Structure:** Ewe-lamb nursery groups; rams in bachelor herds ranked by horn size. Rams join ewe groups only during rut. Strong site fidelity — herds use traditional seasonal ranges transmitted through social learning.

**Population & Recovery:**
Bighorn sheep were devastated across western North America by disease transmitted from domestic sheep (pneumonia caused by *Mycoplasma ovipneumoniae* and *Mannheimia haemolytica*). Historical PNW populations numbered in the thousands; by mid-20th century, most herds were reduced to dozens or eliminated. Recovery has been achieved through translocation programs:
- Hells Canyon: 600-800+ bighorn, one of the largest herds in the West
- WA: Approximately 1,600-1,800 total (multiple herds)
- OR: Approximately 4,000+ total
- ID: Approximately 3,000+

Disease remains the primary management challenge — a single contact event with domestic sheep or goats can trigger an epizootic that kills 50-90% of a herd. WDFW, ODFW, and IDFG maintain domestic sheep separation policies, and some federal grazing allotments have been closed or modified to create buffers.

**Threats:** Pneumonia from domestic sheep/goat contact (the single greatest threat); habitat loss to juniper encroachment (reducing open sightlines); fire suppression converting grassland to shrubland; competition with livestock for forage; vehicle mortality; cougar predation (can significantly affect small herds); genetic isolation of small herds.

**Ecological Role:** Native grazer maintaining grassland composition in canyon and steppe ecosystems. Bighorn grazing prevents grass stagnation and promotes plant diversity. Horns, hooves, and bedding sites create micro-habitat disturbance. As prey, bighorn support cougar and golden eagle populations in canyon country.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Ovis canadensis* descended from Siberian ancestors that crossed Beringia during the Pleistocene. Diverged from thinhorn sheep (*O. dalli*) ~1.5-2 million years ago. Three subspecies recognized in PNW: Rocky Mountain bighorn (*O. c. canadensis*) in ID and limited WA/OR montane, and California bighorn (*O. c. californiana*) — the primary east-side canyon subspecies. California bighorn was described from specimens collected along the Thompson and Fraser rivers in BC.

**Cultural Significance:** Bighorn sheep were important to the Nez Perce, Umatilla, Yakama, and Cayuse nations whose territories encompassed canyon habitats. Bighorn horns were crafted into bows and spoons; hides used for clothing. Petroglyphs depicting bighorn sheep are found throughout the Columbia Plateau — the sheep was a symbol of sure-footed strength and mountain knowledge.

**Key Sources:** [P1] Maser 1998; [P3] Eder, Ross & Parker 2016; Cassirer et al. (2018) — pneumonia and bighorn recovery; WDFW/ODFW/IDFG bighorn management plans; Valdez & Krausman (1999) — *Mountain Sheep of North America*

### Pronghorn (*Antilocapra americana*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Antilocapridae
**Status:** Resident (east-side steppe, limited PNW range)
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed (but see Oregon subspecies note below) | IUCN: LC | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable (Oregon population); game species in portions of range | WA: Extirpated (historical records from Yakima County grasslands)

**Morphometrics:** Body length 130-150 cm. Mass 40-65 kg (bucks), 35-50 kg (does). Distinguishing features: Tan body with white rump, belly, and facial markings; black face mask and horns in bucks; horns unique — bony core with keratinous sheath that is shed annually (the only animal that sheds horn sheaths); large eyes providing near-360-degree vision.

**Habitat:** Open sagebrush steppe and grasslands of eastern OR and southern ID. Requires vast, unbroken sightlines — will not cross forested areas or tall fences. Critical habitat: Hart Mountain National Antelope Refuge (Lake County, OR), Steens Mountain foothills, Alvord Desert margins, Snake River Plain (ID). Elevation 3,000-7,000 ft.

**Diet & Foraging:** Browser-grazer — sagebrush (critical winter forage, comprising 40-80% of winter diet), forbs, grasses. One of few large herbivores that can digest sagebrush's volatile terpenoids efficiently. Highly selective feeder.

**Reproduction:** Polygynous. Rut September-October. Gestation 250 days — unusually long for body size. Typically twins (unique among ungulates of this size); fawns born with minimal scent, lying motionless for first days (hiding strategy). Fawns can outrun a human within days of birth.

**Social Structure:** Seasonal herds — winter aggregations of 50-200+ on low-elevation range; summer territorial bucks defend breeding territories. Does assess territory quality, not male directly. Bachelor groups of non-territorial males.

**Population Trend:** Oregon populations estimated at 15,000-20,000, concentrated on Hart Mountain and surrounding basins. Historically much more numerous; reduced by market hunting and habitat conversion. Generally stable to slowly increasing under current management. *A. a. oregona* (Oregon pronghorn) sometimes recognized as a subspecies; conservation concern due to isolation from Great Basin populations.

**Threats:** Fencing (pronghorn cannot jump standard barbed-wire fences; must pass under bottom wire — wildlife-friendly fencing with raised bottom wire is critical); sagebrush loss to wildfire and invasive annual grasses (cheatgrass); habitat fragmentation by roads and development; juniper encroachment reducing open sightlines; drought reducing forage quality; highway mortality.

**Ecological Role:** The fastest land mammal in the Western Hemisphere (sustained speeds of 55+ mph, sprint bursts to 60+ mph). This speed is an evolutionary holdover from Pleistocene predators (American cheetah, dire wolf) now extinct — pronghorn are "over-built" for current predators. Primary large herbivore in sagebrush steppe; browsing maintains sagebrush community structure. Fawn predation by coyotes, golden eagles, and bobcats is significant — pronghorn fawn survival is a key indicator of mesopredator balance.

**Evolutionary Note:** *Antilocapra americana* is the sole surviving member of family Antilocapridae, which was once diverse (12+ species) in Miocene/Pliocene North America. It is not closely related to Old World antelopes despite superficial resemblance — a textbook case of convergent evolution. The family is endemic to North America and has no close living relatives. Pronghorn's extreme speed evolved under predation pressure from now-extinct Pleistocene predators; no living PNW predator can catch a healthy adult pronghorn.

**Cultural Significance:** Pronghorn were hunted by Paiute, Bannock, and Shoshone nations across the Great Basin and PNW borderlands. Communal pronghorn drives using V-shaped brush fences (similar to bison jumps) are documented archaeologically in eastern Oregon. The Burns Paiute Tribe maintains cultural connections to pronghorn and the Hart Mountain landscape.

**Key Sources:** [P2] Verts & Carraway 1998; [P1] Maser 1998; O'Gara & Yoakum (2004) — *Pronghorn: Ecology and Management*; ODFW pronghorn management; USFWS Hart Mountain NWR

## 3. Cross-Module Ecological Synthesis

### 3.1 Predator-Prey Networks

The PNW carnivore-ungulate system operates as an interconnected web, not a simple ladder:

**Apex tier:** Grizzly bear, gray wolf, cougar
- Wolf and cougar partition prey by habitat: wolves dominant in open/montane terrain, cougar in dense forest
- Where both are present, wolf packs may displace solitary cougars from kills (kleptoparasitism)
- Grizzly bears are primarily predators of ungulate neonates (elk/deer calves in spring) but also scavenge wolf kills

**Mesopredator tier:** Coyote, bobcat, black bear, wolverine (scavenger-predator)
- Wolf recolonization suppresses coyote populations (mesopredator release reversal)
- Coyote suppression benefits ground-nesting birds, pronghorn fawns, and small mammals
- Black bears are significant predators of elk calves and deer fawns in spring

**Ungulate tier:** Elk (Roosevelt and Rocky Mountain), black-tailed deer, mule deer, mountain goat, bighorn sheep, pronghorn, caribou (extirpated)
- Elk and deer populations regulated by predation, disease, and habitat quality
- Ungulate browsing intensity determines forest regeneration success and riparian vegetation health
- Migration corridors connect seasonal habitats across hundreds of miles — corridor protection is a multi-species priority

### 3.2 Salmon-Bear-Forest Nutrient Pathway

The marine-derived nutrient pathway connecting ocean, salmon, bears, and forest is one of the PNW's defining ecological processes:

1. Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) accumulate marine nutrients (nitrogen-15, phosphorus, carbon-13) during 1-5 years of ocean growth
2. Spawning salmon transport these nutrients to freshwater streams
3. Bears (grizzly and black) catch salmon and carry carcasses into the forest — a single bear may transport 30-40 salmon/day during peak runs
4. Decomposing carcasses release marine-derived nitrogen (identifiable by isotopic signature) into forest soils
5. Trees within 500 m of salmon streams show elevated nitrogen-15, growing up to 3x faster than trees far from streams
6. Eagles, ravens, crows, gulls, and other scavengers further distribute salmon carcasses
7. Riparian vegetation fertilized by salmon nutrients provides shade and bank stability for salmon streams — completing the cycle

This nutrient pathway links Module 1 (bears as nutrient transporters) to the AVI companion (eagles and corvids as carcass distributors) and the broader ecological networks documented in ECO research.

### 3.3 Trophic Cascades and Forest Regeneration

The wolf/cougar-elk-riparian vegetation cascade is the primary top-down force shaping western PNW forest structure:

- Predator presence creates "landscape of fear" that modifies ungulate spatial behavior
- Elk and deer reduce time in exposed riparian zones, allowing willow, cottonwood, and understory recovery
- Recovered riparian vegetation improves salmon stream habitat (shading, bank stability, insect prey production)
- Documented at Olympic National Park (cougar-elk; Ripple & Beschta 2006) and expected to develop in wolf recovery areas
- Without apex predators, ungulate populations increase and overbrowse riparian zones — degrading salmon habitat in a top-down trophic cascade

### 3.4 Climate Vulnerability Rankings

Species most vulnerable to climate change in the PNW carnivore-ungulate guild:

1. **Wolverine** — obligate dependence on persistent spring snowpack for denning; >60% habitat loss projected by 2100
2. **Woodland caribou** — already extirpated from contiguous US; old-growth subalpine forest declining
3. **Canada lynx** — snowpack loss erodes competitive advantage; bobcat expanding upslope
4. **American pika** (Module 2) — alpine specialist with low thermal tolerance
5. **Mountain goat** — alpine habitat area shrinking; upslope retreat limited by summit topography
6. **Cascade red fox** — alpine specialist, extremely small population
7. **Grizzly bear** — whitebark pine decline, reduced salmon, compressed alpine; recovery complicated by climate uncertainty
