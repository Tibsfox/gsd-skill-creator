### 1.1 ELEV-ALPINE: Arctic-Alpine (9,500-14,410 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=196 to y=319 | **Precipitation:** 80-150+ in/yr (predominantly snow)

The alpine mammalian community is the most species-poor terrestrial assemblage in the Pacific Northwest, with only 14 species holding Primary (P) or Secondary (S) presence status in the elevation matrix. This poverty reflects the extreme environmental constraints of the zone: compressed growing season (45-90 days), persistent snow cover (8-10 months), high wind exposure, limited vegetative cover, and the insular "sky island" geography that isolates populations on individual peaks and ridgelines.

**Characteristic species:**
- **American pika** (*Ochotona princeps*) — The sentinel of the alpine. Obligate talus-dweller that does not hibernate, instead caching dried vegetation ("haypiles") in rock interstices to survive winter. Primary presence in ELEV-ALPINE; secondary in ELEV-SUBALPINE. Climate-sensitive: populations at low-elevation range margins are declining as summer temperatures exceed the pika's narrow thermal tolerance (~25.5C lethal threshold). See M2: American pika profile; P25, P26.
- **Hoary marmot** (*Marmota caligata*) — Colonial ground squirrel of alpine meadows and talus. Hibernates 7-8 months (September-May). Alarm whistles serve as community-wide predator warning, benefiting pikas, ground squirrels, and ptarmigan. See M2: hoary marmot profile.
- **Olympic marmot** (*Marmota olympus*) — Endemic to the Olympic Mountains. One of the rarest marmots in North America, restricted to alpine and subalpine meadows of a single mountain range. Classified as Vulnerable (IUCN). See M2: Olympic marmot profile.
- **Cascade red fox** (*Vulpes vulpes cascadensis*) — A high-elevation subspecies distinct from introduced lowland red foxes. Primary habitat in alpine and subalpine meadows of the Cascades. Genetically distinct from the European-origin red foxes that occupy lowland habitats. See M1: Cascade red fox profile.
- **Mountain goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) — The largest mammal to maintain year-round alpine residence. Adapted to extreme terrain with specialized hooves providing traction on near-vertical rock and ice. Excavates mineral licks that concentrate other wildlife. Native status in the Olympics is debated (P13); populations there have been relocated to the North Cascades (G19, G20).

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Cougar (seasonal visitor from subalpine; does not maintain territories in alpine). Golden eagle (AVI: *Aquila chrysaetos*) is the primary aerial apex predator, hunting marmots and pikas.
- *Mesopredators:* Cascade red fox, long-tailed weasel, short-tailed weasel (ermine). These small carnivores are the primary year-round predators in the alpine.
- *Herbivores:* Mountain goat, pika, hoary marmot, yellow-bellied marmot (east side), deer mouse (seasonal), long-tailed vole.
- *Decomposers/detritivores:* Shrews (vagrant shrew, dusky shrew) occupy the micro-carnivore niche, consuming insects and invertebrates in alpine meadow soil.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Pika haypile facilitation (REL-FACILITATION): Cached vegetation stores serve as emergency winter food for woodrats and ground squirrels. Pika alarm calls alert the broader alpine community to predator approach.
- Marmot burrowing (REL-ENGINEER): Marmot colonies turn over alpine soil, creating disturbance patches that promote forb diversity and nitrogen cycling in otherwise nutrient-poor soils.
- Mountain goat mineral lick concentration: Goat-excavated mineral licks attract other ungulates and provide sodium/mineral supplements that are scarce in alpine vegetation.

**Conservation status summary:** No alpine mammals are currently ESA-listed, but the American pika has been petitioned for listing (denied 2010) and remains a Species of Concern. Climate change is the dominant threat: upslope range contraction eliminates habitat with no higher refuge available on most PNW peaks. The Olympic marmot is IUCN Vulnerable and declining. Cascade red fox populations are small and poorly monitored.

### 1.2 ELEV-SUBALPINE: Subalpine (5,000-9,500 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=84 to y=196 | **Precipitation:** 60-120 in/yr

The subalpine zone is a transitional ecotone where the species poverty of the alpine gives way to the richer montane forest communities below. With 40 species at P or S status, the subalpine nearly triples alpine richness. The habitat mosaic -- parkland meadows interspersed with subalpine fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*), mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*), and whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) -- creates edge habitats that support both alpine specialists descending from above and montane generalists ascending from below.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Wolverine** (*Gulo gulo*) — The defining carnivore of the subalpine, requiring persistent spring snow cover for denning. Vast home ranges (200-600 km2 for males) make it one of the most area-demanding mammals in the PNW. Primary habitat in ELEV-ALPINE and ELEV-SUBALPINE. Fewer than 50 individuals estimated in the North Cascades (G39). See M1: wolverine profile; P35.
- **Canada lynx** (*Lynx canadensis*) — Subalpine forest specialist whose population dynamics are tightly coupled to snowshoe hare abundance. Listed as Threatened under the ESA. Primary habitat in ELEV-SUBALPINE and ELEV-MONTANE. Dense, soft-furred paws function as snowshoes, giving it a competitive advantage over bobcat in deep snow. See M1: Canada lynx profile.
- **Snowshoe hare** (*Lepus americanus*) — The keystone prey species of the subalpine, whose population cycles (8-11 year periodicity) drive predator dynamics for lynx, coyote, great horned owl, and northern goshawk. Seasonal pelage change (brown to white) provides camouflage, but climate-driven snow phenology shifts are creating mismatch: hares turn white before snow arrives, increasing predation mortality. See M2: snowshoe hare profile.
- **Red squirrel** (*Tamiasciurus hudsonicus*) — Primary habitat in subalpine conifer forest. Middens (cone caches) are critical food stores. Competes with Douglas squirrel at lower elevations where ranges overlap.
- **Grizzly bear** (*Ursus arctos horribilis*) — Primary habitat in ELEV-SUBALPINE and ELEV-MONTANE. The North Cascades Ecosystem was approved for grizzly reintroduction via USFWS Final EIS (G06, G07, G08), with an initial release of 3-7 bears per year over 5-10 years planned. Currently estimated at fewer than 10 individuals in the U.S. North Cascades (likely zero resident breeding population). See M1: grizzly bear profile.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Wolverine, cougar, gray wolf (recolonizing), grizzly bear (historical/reintroducing).
- *Mesopredators:* Canada lynx, American marten, Cascade red fox, ermine, long-tailed weasel.
- *Herbivores:* Snowshoe hare, mountain goat (lower alpine/upper subalpine), mule deer (summer), elk (summer), hoary marmot, Columbian ground squirrel, golden-mantled ground squirrel, yellow-pine chipmunk, red squirrel, northern pocket gopher, porcupine.
- *Insectivores:* Vagrant shrew, dusky shrew, long-legged myotis, big brown bat (summer foraging).

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Lynx-snowshoe hare cycle (REL-PRED): The classic predator-prey oscillation. In the PNW, the cycle amplitude is dampened compared to boreal Canada due to greater prey diversity and alternative prey availability for lynx.
- Marten-flying squirrel predation (REL-PRED): American marten is the primary predator of northern flying squirrel in subalpine forest, linking the arboreal prey community to the mesocarnivore guild.
- Whitebark pine-Clark's nutcracker mutualism: Though primarily an avian interaction (AVI: Clark's nutcracker), cached whitebark pine seeds are also consumed by red squirrels, chipmunks, and grizzly bears. Whitebark pine decline from blister rust threatens this multi-species food web.
- Wolverine scavenging (REL-SCAVENGE): Wolverines depend heavily on carrion in winter -- ungulate carcasses from avalanche, predation, and winterkill. Their massive jaw muscles and specialized premolars allow them to process frozen carcasses inaccessible to other scavengers.

**Conservation status summary:** The subalpine hosts the highest concentration of conservation-sensitive carnivores in the PNW. Wolverine (ESA candidate), Canada lynx (ESA Threatened), grizzly bear (ESA Threatened), and fisher (ESA Threatened in southern Sierra; reintroduced in WA/OR) all depend on subalpine habitat. Climate change threatens this zone through snowpack reduction, upslope treeline advance, and altered disturbance regimes.

### 1.3 ELEV-MONTANE: Montane Forest (3,000-5,000 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=34 to y=84 | **Precipitation:** 40-80 in/yr

The montane forest is the most species-rich terrestrial ecoregion for mammals in the PNW, with 59 species at P or S status. This is the zone of maximum structural complexity: old-growth Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*), and western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*) create multi-layered canopy architecture with snags, downed logs, and understory gaps that provide habitat at every vertical stratum. The montane is where the great ecological narratives of the PNW converge -- the wolf-elk trophic cascade, the vole-mycorrhizal-conifer mutualism, the spotted owl-flying squirrel-old-growth dependency chain.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Cougar** (*Puma concolor*) — The dominant apex predator of PNW montane forest. Solitary, territorial, and capable of killing prey 5-7 times its body mass. Primary habitat spans ELEV-SUBALPINE through ELEV-LOWLAND, but montane forest with steep terrain is optimal. WA estimate: ~2,100 individuals (G34). See M1: cougar profile.
- **Gray wolf** (*Canis lupus*) — Recolonizing montane and subalpine habitat through natural dispersal from Idaho/Montana. 33 confirmed packs in WA, 23 in OR as of 2024 (G23, G25). The wolf's return is initiating measurable trophic cascades: reduced elk browsing on riparian vegetation, mesopredator suppression of coyote, and cascading benefits to beaver, songbirds, and salmon habitat. See M1: gray wolf profile.
- **Roosevelt elk** (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*) — The largest terrestrial mammal in the PNW west side. Primary habitat in ELEV-MONTANE and ELEV-LOWLAND west of the Cascades. Seasonal elevational migration between montane summer range and lowland/riparian winter range. See M1: Roosevelt elk profile.
- **Northern flying squirrel** (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) — Nocturnal arboreal rodent, primary prey of both northern spotted owl and American marten. Fungivorous diet makes it the most important dispersal vector for ectomycorrhizal fungi in old-growth forests. The flying squirrel-truffle-tree mutualism is foundational to PNW forest nutrient cycling. See M2: northern flying squirrel profile; P37.
- **Fisher** (*Pekania pennanti*) — Medium-sized mustelid reintroduced to WA Cascades (2015-2020) and OR Cascades. Depends on old-growth structural features -- large-diameter snags and live trees with cavities -- for denning. One of the few predators capable of killing porcupines. ESA listing varies by region. See M1: fisher profile; G37, P11.
- **American marten** (*Martes americana*) — Old-growth forest indicator. Requires continuous canopy cover and complex understory structure. Population density correlates with stand age and structural complexity. See M1: marten profile.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Cougar, gray wolf (recolonizing), black bear (omnivore apex).
- *Mesopredators:* Bobcat, coyote, fisher, marten, long-tailed weasel, ermine, spotted skunk.
- *Herbivores:* Roosevelt elk, Rocky Mountain elk (east side), mule deer, black-tailed deer, mountain beaver, porcupine, Douglas squirrel, Townsend's chipmunk, snowshoe hare.
- *Fungivore-dispersers:* Northern flying squirrel, California red-backed vole, southern red-backed vole, deer mouse (partial). These species consume truffles (hypogeous mycorrhizal fungi) and deposit viable spores in fecal pellets across the forest floor, maintaining the mycorrhizal networks on which conifers depend for nutrient uptake.
- *Insectivores:* Trowbridge's shrew, vagrant shrew, American water shrew, little brown bat, big brown bat, silver-haired bat, long-eared myotis.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Wolf-elk-riparian trophic cascade (REL-TROPHIC-CASCADE): Wolf predation and the "ecology of fear" reduce elk browsing in riparian zones, allowing willow, cottonwood, and alder recovery. This cascade benefits beaver (dam-building substrate), salmon (shade, bank stability, large wood), and songbirds (nesting habitat). See M1: gray wolf ecological interaction; G17, P16.
- Vole-mycorrhizal-conifer mutualism (REL-MUTUALIST): California red-backed voles (*Myodes californicus*) consume truffles and disperse spores, maintaining the ectomycorrhizal networks that conifer roots require for nutrient and water uptake. This three-way mutualism (vole-fungus-tree) is the invisible infrastructure of PNW old-growth productivity. See M2: California red-backed vole profile; P37.
- Spotted owl-flying squirrel dependency (REL-PRED): The northern spotted owl (AVI: *Strix occidentalis caurina*) depends on northern flying squirrels for 50-80% of its diet in many PNW study areas. Both species require old-growth structural features. This predator-prey pair is a double indicator of old-growth forest integrity.
- Cougar-deer/elk predation (REL-PRED): Cougar predation is a primary source of deer and elk mortality in montane forest, shaping ungulate behavior, distribution, and population dynamics. Cougar kill sites create nutrient hotspots where carcass decomposition enriches soil and promotes localized plant growth.

**Conservation status summary:** The montane zone has been most heavily impacted by industrial timber harvest. Old-growth dependent species (spotted owl, marten, fisher, red tree vole, flying squirrel) have experienced significant habitat loss. The Northwest Forest Plan (1994) established reserves on federal lands, but private and state forestlands continue to lack equivalent old-growth protections. Wolf recovery is transforming montane community dynamics in real time.

### 1.4 ELEV-LOWLAND: Lowland Forest (500-3,000 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-29 to y=34 | **Precipitation:** 35-100 in/yr

The lowland forest band holds the highest mammalian species richness of any PNW ecoregion: 74 species at P or S status. This richness reflects the zone's moderate climate, high primary productivity, structural complexity, and -- critically -- its position as the overlap zone where montane species descending from higher elevations meet riparian/lowland specialists ascending from below. The lowland is also the most heavily human-modified terrestrial zone in the PNW, with more than 90% of original old-growth forest removed since the mid-19th century.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Black bear** (*Ursus americanus*) — The most abundant large carnivore in the PNW. Omnivorous diet spans berries, insects, salmon, carrion, and garbage. Estimated 25,000-30,000 in WA, 25,000-30,000 in OR. Primary habitat from ELEV-SUBALPINE through ELEV-RIPARIAN; most abundant in ELEV-MONTANE and ELEV-LOWLAND. See M1: black bear profile.
- **Columbian black-tailed deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*) — The most abundant ungulate west of the Cascades. Adapted to early seral and edge habitats created by timber harvest. Population dynamics regulated by cougar predation, vehicle collision, and habitat quality. See M1: black-tailed deer profile.
- **Douglas squirrel** (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*) — The "chickaree" of Pacific coast conifer forests. Territorial, vocal, and a prolific cone cacher. Middens are important food sources for other small mammals. Competes with introduced eastern gray squirrel in urban-adjacent lowlands.
- **Beaver** (*Castor canadensis*) — The premier ecosystem engineer of PNW lowland and riparian habitats. Dam construction creates wetland complexes that increase water storage, reduce flood peaks, elevate water tables, trap sediments, and create habitat for salmon, amphibians, waterfowl, and invertebrates. See Section 4.1 (Keystone Species). See M2: beaver profile.
- **Western gray squirrel** (*Sciurus griseus*) — WA state Threatened. Restricted to oak-conifer ecotone habitats. Competition from introduced eastern gray squirrel and eastern fox squirrel is compressing range. See M2: western gray squirrel profile.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Cougar, black bear (omnivore apex), gray wolf (recolonizing east side lowlands).
- *Mesopredators:* Bobcat, coyote, gray fox, red fox (introduced lowland populations), raccoon, striped skunk, spotted skunk, long-tailed weasel, mink.
- *Herbivores:* Black-tailed deer, mule deer, Roosevelt elk (west side), beaver, mountain beaver, Douglas squirrel, western gray squirrel, Townsend's chipmunk, Townsend's vole, creeping vole, brush rabbit, snowshoe hare, porcupine.
- *Arboreal specialists:* Red tree vole (*Arborimus longicaudus*) — obligate canopy dweller, feeding almost exclusively on Douglas-fir needles. One of the most specialized mammals in the PNW. See M2: red tree vole profile; P10, P30.
- *Insectivores:* Trowbridge's shrew, shrew-mole, Townsend's mole, coast mole, little brown bat, big brown bat, hoary bat, silver-haired bat, Townsend's big-eared bat, Yuma myotis.
- *Introduced species:* Virginia opossum, brown rat, house mouse, eastern cottontail. These species are concentrated in human-modified lowland habitats.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Beaver ecosystem engineering (REL-ENGINEER): Beaver dams create slow-water habitat used by juvenile coho salmon, cutthroat trout, and steelhead. Dam complexes increase summer base flows, moderate water temperature, and trap fine sediment, directly improving salmonid habitat. A single beaver dam complex can increase local wetland area by 50-200%. See Section 4.1.
- Bear-berry seed dispersal (REL-MUTUALIST): Black bears consume enormous quantities of huckleberries, salal berries, and blackberries, depositing viable seeds in scat across large distances. Bear scat germination rates are often higher than ungerminated seeds due to scarification in the digestive tract.
- Raccoon-crayfish-stream interaction (REL-PRED): Raccoons are the primary terrestrial predator of signal crayfish (*Pacifastacus leniusculus*) in lowland streams. Crayfish regulate aquatic invertebrate communities and process leaf litter; raccoon predation keeps crayfish populations from exceeding carrying capacity.

**Conservation status summary:** The lowland zone has the highest density of human-wildlife conflict. Vehicle-wildlife collisions, urban encroachment, domestic animal predation on wildlife, rodenticide poisoning (secondary exposure in bobcats, fishers, owls -- P34), and habitat fragmentation are the dominant threats. Western gray squirrel (WA Threatened), red tree vole (federal Species of Concern), and Columbian white-tailed deer (ESA recovery -- G18) are priority species.

### 1.5 ELEV-RIPARIAN: Riparian/Estuary (0-500 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-41 to y=-29 | **Precipitation:** Variable (follows adjacent upland)

The riparian zone is not defined by elevation alone but by proximity to flowing water. It cuts across multiple elevation bands but has its broadest expression in lowland river valleys and estuaries. With 56 species at P or S status, the riparian zone functions as a linear corridor of high diversity threading through the landscape. Its disproportionate ecological importance stems from the salmon nutrient conveyor: Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) transport marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen-15, phosphorus, carbon-13) from the ocean into riparian forests via their carcasses, fertilizing the trees that shade the streams their offspring need.

**Characteristic species:**
- **North American river otter** (*Lontra canadensis*) — Semi-aquatic apex predator of riparian systems. Diet includes salmon, crayfish, amphibians, and small mammals. Indicator of stream health. See M1: river otter profile.
- **American mink** (*Neogale vison*) — Semi-aquatic mustelid occupying the mesopredator niche in riparian corridors. Preys on voles, muskrats, crayfish, and fish. Smaller and more agile than the river otter, allowing niche partitioning by prey size.
- **American water shrew** (*Sorex palustris*) — The only truly semi-aquatic shrew in the PNW. Fringed hind feet and water-repellent fur allow underwater foraging for aquatic invertebrates. Indicator of stream quality. See M2: water shrew profile.
- **Pacific water shrew** (*Sorex bendirii*) — Larger than the American water shrew, occupying a similar semi-aquatic niche in coastal streams. Federal Species of Concern in some regions.
- **Muskrat** (*Ondatra zibethicus*) — Aquatic rodent building lodges in slow-water wetlands. Herbivorous, feeding on cattails, bulrush, and aquatic vegetation. Lodge construction creates habitat for other wetland species.
- **Columbian white-tailed deer** (*Odocoileus virginianus leucurus*) — ESA-listed subspecies restricted to riparian bottomland along the lower Columbia River and in southwestern OR. Recovery program has increased the Lower Columbia River DPS from ~300 to ~900 individuals (G18). Primary habitat in ELEV-RIPARIAN.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Black bear (salmon specialist during spawning runs), cougar (visiting from adjacent uplands).
- *Semi-aquatic predators:* River otter, mink, raccoon.
- *Herbivores:* Beaver, muskrat, Columbian white-tailed deer, Townsend's vole, white-footed vole.
- *Insectivores:* American water shrew, Pacific water shrew, Trowbridge's shrew, vagrant shrew, Yuma myotis, western red bat, little brown bat.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Salmon-bear-forest nutrient cascade (REL-FACILITATION): Black bears catch salmon during spawning runs and carry carcasses into the riparian forest, where decomposition releases marine-derived nutrients. Studies on PNW salmon streams document that trees within 50 m of salmon-bearing streams contain 22-24% marine-derived nitrogen, directly traceable to salmon carcass decomposition. This is the terrestrial terminus of the salmon thread. See M1: black bear salmon thread.
- Beaver-salmon habitat engineering (REL-ENGINEER): Beaver dam complexes create the slow-water, deep-pool, off-channel habitat that juvenile coho salmon prefer. Streams with active beaver colonies produce significantly more juvenile salmonids per kilometer than unbeavered reaches. See Section 4.1.
- Mink-muskrat-wetland dynamics (REL-PRED): Mink predation is the primary natural control on muskrat populations. Muskrat herbivory on emergent vegetation maintains open water patches in wetlands; without mink predation, muskrat overgrazing can convert marshes to open water.

**Conservation status summary:** Riparian habitats have been disproportionately impacted by agriculture, urbanization, dam construction, and channelization. Columbian white-tailed deer (ESA Threatened/Endangered depending on DPS) is the flagship riparian conservation species. Water shrew populations are poorly monitored but assumed declining with stream degradation. Salmon decline directly reduces nutrient subsidies to riparian mammal communities.

### 1.6 ELEV-INTERTIDAL: Intertidal (-15 to 0 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-41 | **Substrate:** Rocky reef, sand/mud flat, tide pool, salt marsh

The intertidal zone is the narrowest ecoregion band and supports the fewest mammalian species (5 at P or S status). This is the interface between marine and terrestrial systems -- a zone of extreme physical stress (wave energy, tidal exposure, salinity fluctuation) that few mammals can exploit. The species present here are exclusively pinnipeds using the intertidal for hauling out (resting, thermoregulation, pupping) rather than foraging.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Harbor seal** (*Phoca vitulina*) — The most abundant pinniped in PNW nearshore waters. Hauls out on intertidal rocks, sandbars, and mud flats for thermoregulation, rest, and pupping. Diet includes herring, smelt, salmon, and flatfish. Year-round resident. See M3: harbor seal profile.
- **Steller sea lion** (*Eumetopias jubatus*) — The largest otariid. Hauls out on rocky reefs and offshore islets. Eastern DPS was delisted from ESA Threatened in 2013 following population recovery. See M3: Steller sea lion profile.
- **California sea lion** (*Zalophus californianus*) — Males migrate north to PNW waters September-May; females rarely reach PNW latitudes. Concentrated at haul-out sites in the Columbia River, Puget Sound, and Oregon coast.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Bigg's orca patrols nearshore waters, preying on pinnipeds at haul-out sites.
- *Primary consumers:* None among mammals; pinnipeds are marine predators (piscivorous/generalist).
- The intertidal mammal community is entirely composed of upper-trophic-level marine predators using the zone for non-foraging purposes.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Pinniped haul-out colony dynamics: Haul-out site fidelity creates predictable prey concentrations that Bigg's orcas exploit. Orcas have been documented hunting harbor seals at intertidal haul-out sites in the San Juan Islands, creating a landscape of fear that modifies pinniped haul-out behavior and site selection.
- Sea lion-salmon conflict: California sea lions and Steller sea lions congregate at salmon migration bottlenecks (Bonneville Dam, Ballard Locks, Willamette Falls), consuming adult salmon and steelhead. NOAA has authorized limited lethal removal of individually identified sea lions under Section 120 of the MMPA to reduce predation on ESA-listed salmon runs.

**Conservation status summary:** Harbor seal populations have recovered to near historical levels following MMPA protection. Steller sea lion (Eastern DPS) was delisted in 2013. California sea lion populations are robust and increasing. The primary conservation concern in this zone is human disturbance of haul-out sites and the sea lion-salmon management conflict.

### 1.7 ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE: Shallow Marine (-200 to -15 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-46 to y=-41

The shallow marine zone extends from the low-tide line to the edge of the continental shelf (approximately 200 m depth). This zone supports 27 mammalian species at P or S status -- the majority are cetaceans (17 species) and pinnipeds (5 species), with sea otter and both orca ecotypes completing the assemblage. Shallow marine waters are the most productive marine habitat in the PNW, driven by coastal upwelling that brings nutrient-rich deep water to the photic zone, fueling phytoplankton blooms, zooplankton abundance, and the forage fish schools on which marine mammals depend.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Southern Resident killer whale** (*Orcinus orca*) — 73 individuals (July 2024 census; O01). ESA Endangered. Chinook salmon specialist. The most conservation-critical marine mammal in the PNW. See M3: SRKW profile; G01-G04, P17, P18.
- **Bigg's (transient) killer whale** (*Orcinus orca*) — Marine mammal specialist. Population increasing (~500+ individuals; G15). Preys on harbor seals, sea lions, porpoises, and gray whale calves. See M3: Bigg's orca profile.
- **Sea otter** (*Enhydra lutris*) — Reintroduced to WA Olympic coast in 1969-1970 (59 individuals from Alaska). Current population ~2,800 (USFWS; G16). Keystone predator controlling sea urchin populations. See Section 4.3. See M3: sea otter profile.
- **Humpback whale** (*Megaptera novaeangliae*) — Present April-November in PNW waters, feeding on krill and forage fish. Central America DPS is ESA Endangered; Mexico DPS is ESA Threatened. Population recovering. See M3: humpback whale profile.
- **Gray whale** (*Eschrichtius robustus*) — Eastern North Pacific population migrates through PNW waters March-May (northbound) and October-December (southbound). A resident "Pacific Coast Feeding Group" of ~200 whales feeds in PNW shallow waters year-round. See M3: gray whale profile.
- **Harbor porpoise** (*Phocoena phocoena*) — The smallest cetacean in PNW waters. Abundant in inland waters (Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia) and along the outer coast. Population has recolonized southern Puget Sound following decades of absence. See M3: harbor porpoise profile; P20.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Orca (both ecotypes). Bigg's orca is the apex of the marine mammal food web; resident orca is the apex of the salmon food web.
- *Upper-trophic predators:* Humpback whale, gray whale (benthic feeder), Steller sea lion, harbor seal, California sea lion, northern elephant seal.
- *Mesopredators:* Sea otter (benthic invertebrate specialist), harbor porpoise, Dall's porpoise, Pacific white-sided dolphin.
- *Specialists:* Northern fur seal (seasonal pelagic visitor, piscivorous).

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Sea otter-urchin-kelp trophic cascade (REL-TROPHIC-CASCADE): Sea otters suppress sea urchin (*Strongylocentrotus* spp.) populations through predation, preventing urchin overgrazing of kelp forests. In the absence of sea otters, urchin barrens replace kelp, eliminating habitat for hundreds of invertebrate and fish species. The otter's return to the WA Olympic coast has been followed by kelp forest recovery in areas with established otter populations. See Section 4.3.
- SRKW-Chinook dependency (REL-PRED): Southern Residents are obligate Chinook salmon specialists. The 73-individual population is declining primarily due to prey limitation as Chinook stocks collapse. This dependency links the marine apex predator directly to freshwater and terrestrial habitat management (dam removal, riparian restoration, hatchery reform). See M3: SRKW critical threat analysis.
- Bigg's orca-pinniped predation (REL-PRED): Bigg's orcas are the primary predator of harbor seals, Steller sea lions, and harbor porpoises in PNW waters. Their increasing population (~500+ and growing) creates top-down pressure on pinniped and porpoise communities, with potential cascading effects on forage fish and salmon.

**Conservation status summary:** The shallow marine zone contains the most ESA-listed marine mammals: SRKW (Endangered), humpback whale (Endangered/Threatened by DPS), sei whale (Endangered), fin whale (Endangered), blue whale (Endangered), North Pacific right whale (Endangered). Sea otter recovery is ongoing. The dominant threats are prey limitation (salmon decline), vessel noise, contaminant bioaccumulation, entanglement, and ship strike.

### 1.8 ELEV-DEEP-MARINE: Deep Marine (-930 to -200 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-64 to y=-46

The deep marine zone extends from the continental shelf break into the abyssal depths of the Northeast Pacific. With 22 species at P or S status, this zone is dominated by cetaceans (17 species) adapted to deep-water habitats. The deep marine is the least surveyed mammalian habitat in the PNW -- population estimates for many species are uncertain, and ecological interactions are poorly documented. Species here are partitioned primarily by diving depth, prey type, and foraging strategy.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Blue whale** (*Balaenoptera musculus*) — The largest animal to have ever lived. ESA Endangered. Primary habitat in ELEV-DEEP-MARINE where it feeds on krill (*Euphausia pacifica*) aggregations along the continental slope and in submarine canyons. Seasonal presence in PNW waters (summer-fall). See M3: blue whale profile.
- **Sperm whale** (*Physeter macrocephalus*) — Deep-diving specialist, routinely descending to 600-1,200 m to hunt squid and deep-water fish. ESA Endangered. Vagrant to primary in deep marine; rare in shallow marine. See M3: sperm whale profile.
- **Cuvier's beaked whale** (*Ziphius cavirostris*) — The deepest-diving mammal recorded (2,992 m; 137-minute dive). Occurs over the continental slope and in submarine canyons. Poorly known; population estimate uncertain. See M3: Cuvier's beaked whale profile.
- **Northern elephant seal** (*Mirounga angustirostris*) — Males dive to 400-1,500 m during foraging trips lasting months. Hauls out on coastal beaches for breeding and molting. Population has recovered dramatically from near-extinction (~20 individuals in the 1890s) to ~225,000. See M3: northern elephant seal profile.
- **Dall's porpoise** (*Phocoenoides dalli*) — The fastest small cetacean. Year-round resident of PNW waters from nearshore to deep offshore. Abundant; MMPA non-strategic.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Orca (Bigg's and offshore ecotypes). Offshore orcas specialize on sharks, including Pacific sleeper shark.
- *Upper-trophic predators:* Sperm whale (deep-water squid and fish), northern elephant seal (deep-diving generalist).
- *Filter feeders:* Blue whale, fin whale, sei whale, common minke whale (krill and small fish).
- *Mesopelagic specialists:* Beaked whales (Cuvier's, Stejneger's) — extreme deep-diving predators of mesopelagic squid and fish.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Depth-partitioned foraging (REL-COMPETITION avoidance): Deep marine cetaceans partition the water column by diving depth and prey type, reducing interspecific competition. Sperm whales target deep-water squid at 600-1,200 m; beaked whales forage at 500-3,000 m on different squid species; fin and blue whales filter-feed at 50-200 m on krill aggregations.
- Whale fall communities: When large whales die and sink to the deep seafloor, their carcasses support specialized decomposer communities for decades. Whale falls are biodiversity hotspots in the deep sea, supporting unique invertebrate assemblages including bone-eating worms (*Osedax* spp.) found nowhere else.
- Sperm whale nutrient pumping: Sperm whales feed at depth and defecate at the surface, releasing iron-rich fecal plumes that fertilize surface phytoplankton. This "whale pump" enhances primary productivity in otherwise nutrient-limited offshore waters.

**Conservation status summary:** Most deep marine mammals carry ESA Endangered status as legacy listings from the whaling era. Populations of most large whale species are recovering, though slowly. Blue whale, fin whale, sei whale, sperm whale, and North Pacific right whale remain ESA Endangered. Primary threats are ship strike, entanglement in fishing gear, ocean noise (military sonar, seismic survey), and climate-driven prey redistribution.

### 1.9 ELEV-STEPPE: East-Side Steppe/Shrubland (500-4,000 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-29 to y=34 (same as LOWLAND; differentiated by biome)

The east-side shrub-steppe is the ecological inverse of the west-side lowland forest: arid (<15 in/yr precipitation), dominated by sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*), bunchgrass, and lithosol soils. Despite its apparent simplicity, the steppe supports 47 mammalian species at P or S status -- a richness driven by specialized rodent and lagomorph communities adapted to arid conditions, plus a distinct carnivore guild absent from west-side habitats. The steppe is the only PNW ecoregion where burrowing mammals (fossorial guild) dominate community biomass.

**Characteristic species:**
- **American badger** (*Taxidea taxus*) — The apex fossorial predator. Powerful digging claws allow it to excavate ground squirrels, pocket gophers, and rabbits from burrows. Documented cooperative hunting with coyotes: badger digs prey from below while coyote waits above. See M1: badger profile.
- **Pygmy rabbit** (*Brachylagus idahoensis*) — The smallest rabbit in North America and the only one that digs its own burrows. Columbia Basin DPS is ESA Endangered; WA populations were captive-bred for reintroduction (G33, G36). Obligate sagebrush dependent: requires tall, dense sagebrush for food and cover.
- **White-tailed jackrabbit** (*Lepus townsendii*) — The largest lagomorph in the PNW steppe. Seasonal pelage change (brown to white) in northern populations. Declining across much of its range due to sagebrush conversion and agricultural expansion. See M2: white-tailed jackrabbit profile.
- **Washington ground squirrel** (*Urocitellus washingtoni*) — Federal candidate species. Restricted to remnant shrub-steppe in central WA and north-central OR. Colonies are declining due to agricultural conversion and habitat fragmentation.
- **Pallid bat** (*Antrozous pallidus*) — A uniquely terrestrial bat that captures scorpions, centipedes, and large beetles on the ground rather than in flight. Primary habitat in ELEV-STEPPE. Roosts in rock crevices, caves, and abandoned buildings. See M2: pallid bat profile.
- **Ord's kangaroo rat** (*Dipodomys ordii*) — Bipedal, nocturnal, and capable of surviving without drinking water (metabolic water production from seed digestion). Primary habitat in sandy steppe soils.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Cougar (visiting from adjacent montane), coyote (the most abundant large predator in the steppe).
- *Mesopredators:* Badger, bobcat, long-tailed weasel, striped skunk.
- *Herbivores:* Pygmy rabbit, mountain cottontail, white-tailed jackrabbit, black-tailed jackrabbit, mule deer (winter), pronghorn (historical -- H status), various ground squirrels, kangaroo rats, pocket mice.
- *Insectivores:* Pallid bat, canyon bat, California myotis, western small-footed myotis, Merriam's shrew, Preble's shrew.
- *Fossorial guild:* Badger, pocket gopher (Botta's, northern), kangaroo rat, Washington ground squirrel, Belding's ground squirrel. This guild dominates steppe community structure.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Badger-coyote cooperative hunting (REL-MUTUALIST/COMMENSAL): One of the few documented interspecific cooperative hunting relationships among mammals. Badgers dig prey (ground squirrels, rabbits) from burrows while coyotes capture individuals that flee above ground. Both species have higher hunting success when foraging together than alone. The relationship is facultative, not obligate.
- Pygmy rabbit-sagebrush obligacy (REL-FACILITATION): Pygmy rabbits depend entirely on tall, dense big sagebrush for food (>50% of diet year-round) and thermal/predator cover. The rabbit's burrow systems in turn aerate soil and create microsites for sagebrush seedling establishment, creating a weak mutualism.
- Ground squirrel colony as prey base: Ground squirrel colonies are the foundational prey resource of the steppe, supporting badgers, coyotes, red-tailed hawks, ferruginous hawks (AVI), golden eagles, prairie falcons, and rattlesnakes. Colony extirpation cascades through the entire predator community.

**Conservation status summary:** The steppe is the most threatened mammalian ecoregion in the PNW by habitat loss. An estimated 50-60% of original sagebrush-steppe has been converted to agriculture, with remaining habitat fragmented by roads, irrigation infrastructure, and invasive cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*). Pygmy rabbit (ESA Endangered, Columbia Basin DPS), Washington ground squirrel (federal candidate), and white-tailed jackrabbit (state sensitive in OR/WA) are priority conservation targets. Wildfire in cheatgrass-invaded steppe is eliminating sagebrush faster than it can regenerate.

### 1.10 ELEV-SUBTERRANEAN: Subterranean/Fossorial (Subsurface)

**Minecraft Y:** N/A (subsurface dimension)

The subterranean ecoregion band captures habitats below the ground surface: lava tubes, solution caves, mine adits, extensive burrow networks, and hibernacula. With 27 species at P or S status, this band is dominated by bats (12 species using caves and mines for roosting and hibernation) and fossorial rodents (8 species of pocket gophers, ground squirrels, and moles). The subterranean is unique among ecoregion bands in being entirely subsurface -- it overlaps all terrestrial elevation bands but is functionally distinct.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Townsend's big-eared bat** (*Corynorhinus townsendii*) — The flagship cave-roosting bat of the PNW. Extremely sensitive to disturbance: a single human entry into a maternity roost can cause colony abandonment. Requires large, stable cave systems for maternity roosts and hibernacula. WA state candidate; OR sensitive-critical. See M2: Townsend's big-eared bat profile; G10.
- **Little brown bat** (*Myotis lucifugus*) — Historically the most abundant bat in the PNW. Uses caves, mines, and buildings for maternity roosts and hibernacula. White-nose syndrome (WNS) has devastated populations in eastern North America; *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* was first detected in WA in 2016 (King County) but mass mortality events have not yet been documented in the PNW. See M2: little brown bat profile; G21.
- **Northern pocket gopher** (*Thomomys talpoides*) — Fossorial herbivore whose tunneling activity turns over enormous volumes of soil (estimated 5-8 metric tons per hectare per year in active colonies). This soil disturbance promotes plant diversity, facilitates water infiltration, and buries surface organic matter.
- **Townsend's mole** (*Scapanus townsendii*) — The largest mole in North America, restricted to moist lowland soils of western WA and OR. Entirely fossorial; rarely comes to the surface. See M2: Townsend's mole profile.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Mesopredators:* Long-tailed weasel and ermine can enter burrow systems to hunt pocket gophers and voles.
- *Insectivores:* All 12 bat species consume enormous quantities of insects -- a single little brown bat eats an estimated 4-8 g of insects per night, roughly half its body mass. Across the PNW bat population, nightly insect consumption is estimated in the hundreds of tons.
- *Herbivores:* Pocket gophers (strictly herbivorous, consuming roots and tubers underground), mountain beaver (extensive burrow systems).
- *Fossorial insectivores:* Moles consume earthworms, beetle larvae, and soil invertebrates.

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Bat hibernaculum ecology: Cave and mine hibernacula concentrate bat populations during winter torpor. Temperature, humidity, and airflow stability are critical -- disturbance or microclimate change can kill entire hibernating colonies. PNW lava tubes (Oregon Cascades, central WA) and limestone caves (Olympic Peninsula, northern Cascades) are the most important hibernacula.
- WNS threat (REL-PARASITE): White-nose syndrome (*Pseudogymnoascus destructans*) is the most significant disease threat to PNW bats. The fungus attacks bat skin during hibernation torpor, causing arousal, fat depletion, and death. PNW detection occurred in 2016 (WA) and 2023 (OR). Mass mortality has not yet occurred in the PNW, but WNS has killed an estimated 6+ million bats in eastern North America and its westward spread continues. See M2: WNS protocol; G21, G10.
- Pocket gopher soil engineering (REL-ENGINEER): Gopher tunneling aerates soil, buries surface organic matter, mixes soil horizons, and creates microsites for seed germination. In meadow and prairie habitats, gopher disturbance maintains plant species diversity by preventing competitive dominance. Gopher mounds are preferred germination sites for many forb species.

**Conservation status summary:** The primary threats to subterranean mammals are cave/mine disturbance (human recreation, mine closures without bat gates), WNS spread, and habitat loss for fossorial species (agricultural conversion, urbanization). Townsend's big-eared bat is the highest conservation priority, with declining populations across its range due to roost disturbance and habitat loss.

### 1.11 ELEV-COASTAL-DUNE: Coastal Dune/Beach (0-100 ft)

**Minecraft Y:** y=-41 (same as INTERTIDAL; differentiated by substrate)

The coastal dune and beach zone captures the terrestrial coastal margin -- sandy beaches, dune systems, coastal headlands, and the immediate supratidal fringe. With 19 species at P or S status, this zone functions as a transitional interface between marine and terrestrial mammalian communities. Pinnipeds haul out on beaches; terrestrial mesopredators forage along the wrack line; and the zone serves as a corridor connecting riparian outlets to the open coast.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Harbor seal** (*Phoca vitulina*) — Uses sandy beaches and rocky headlands for hauling out, resting, and pupping. Year-round resident. Primary presence in both ELEV-COASTAL-DUNE and ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE.
- **River otter** (*Lontra canadensis*) — Uses coastal habitats extensively, foraging in nearshore marine waters and resting on beaches. Coastal river otter populations consume a diet including marine fish, crabs, and invertebrates, distinct from their freshwater riparian diet.
- **Raccoon** (*Procyon lotor*) — Abundant coastal forager, exploiting intertidal invertebrates (crabs, mussels), bird eggs, and beach wrack. The raccoon is a significant nest predator of shorebirds and seabirds nesting on coastal dunes.
- **Deer mouse** (*Peromyscus maniculatus*) — The most ubiquitous small mammal on PNW beaches and dunes. Subspecific variation along the coast; some coastal island populations are distinct. See M2: deer mouse profile.
- **Coyote** (*Canis latrans*) — Patrols beaches and dune systems, scavenging marine mammal carcasses, preying on shorebirds and rabbits, and consuming beach wrack invertebrates. The primary terrestrial predator of the coastal zone.

**Trophic structure:**
- *Apex predators:* Bigg's orca (nearshore), coyote (terrestrial).
- *Marine predators:* Harbor seal, Steller sea lion, California sea lion, northern elephant seal, northern fur seal (all hauling out).
- *Mesopredators:* River otter, raccoon, mink, striped skunk.
- *Herbivores/omnivores:* Deer mouse, muskrat (estuarine margins), Townsend's vole (dune grassland).

**Key ecological interactions:**
- Marine-terrestrial nutrient flow: Marine mammal carcasses, seabird guano, kelp wrack, and salmon carcasses deposited on beaches transfer marine nutrients into the coastal dune ecosystem. Scavengers (coyote, raccoon, ravens, eagles) redistribute these nutrients inland.
- Pinniped pupping vulnerability: Harbor seal and elephant seal pupping on coastal beaches exposes pups to terrestrial predators (coyotes, raccoons) and human disturbance. Pupping site selection balances marine predator avoidance (orca) against terrestrial disturbance risk.

**Conservation status summary:** Coastal dune habitat is threatened by development, recreational disturbance, invasive European beachgrass (*Ammophila arenaria*), and sea level rise. Pinniped haul-out sites require disturbance buffers. No coastal-dune-specific mammal species are ESA listed, but the zone supports several listed species that use it seasonally (harbor seal, sea otter, Steller sea lion).

## 2. Elevation-Richness Relationship

### 2.1 Species Count Gradient

The PNW mammalian elevation-richness relationship follows a classic mid-elevation peak pattern, with maximum species richness in the lowland forest band (74 species at P or S status) and declining richness toward both alpine (14 species) and deep marine (22 species) extremes.

| Ecoregion Band | Elevation (ft) | Species (P+S) | % of Maximum |
|----------------|----------------|---------------|--------------|
| ELEV-ALPINE | 9,500-14,410 | 14 | 19% |
| ELEV-SUBALPINE | 5,000-9,500 | 40 | 54% |
| ELEV-MONTANE | 3,000-5,000 | 59 | 80% |
| **ELEV-LOWLAND** | **500-3,000** | **74** | **100%** |
| ELEV-RIPARIAN | 0-500 | 56 | 76% |
| ELEV-INTERTIDAL | -15 to 0 | 5 | 7% |
| ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE | -200 to -15 | 27 | 36% |
| ELEV-DEEP-MARINE | -930 to -200 | 22 | 30% |
| ELEV-STEPPE | 500-4,000 | 47 | 64% |
| ELEV-SUBTERRANEAN | Subsurface | 27 | 36% |
| ELEV-COASTAL-DUNE | 0-100 | 19 | 26% |

### 2.2 Mid-Elevation Diversity Peak

The mid-elevation peak in mammalian diversity (maximum at ELEV-LOWLAND, strong richness in ELEV-MONTANE and ELEV-RIPARIAN) is consistent with the biogeographic pattern documented by Rickart (2001, P23) for intermountain mammals and confirmed across multiple PNW taxa. The mid-elevation peak is driven by three convergent factors:

1. **Habitat overlap zone.** The lowland-montane transition is where descending montane specialists overlap with ascending riparian and lowland species, creating a zone of maximum species co-occurrence. Carnivores illustrate this clearly: 13 carnivore species hold P or S status in ELEV-LOWLAND versus only 5 in ELEV-ALPINE.

2. **Structural complexity.** Lowland and montane forests provide the greatest vertical habitat stratification -- from subterranean burrow networks through forest floor, understory, mid-canopy, and canopy to emergent snag tops. This multi-layered architecture supports arboreal specialists (flying squirrel, red tree vole, Douglas squirrel), scansorial species (raccoon, porcupine, marten, fisher), terrestrial species (deer, elk, bear), and fossorial species (mountain beaver, moles) simultaneously.

3. **Primary productivity.** Net primary productivity peaks in the lowland-montane zone, where moderate precipitation, moderate temperatures, and deep soils produce the highest standing biomass of any temperate biome. This productivity supports the prey base (voles, mice, squirrels, rabbits) that in turn supports the most diverse predator community.

### 2.3 Marine Zone: Low Richness, High Biomass

The marine zones present a striking inversion of the terrestrial pattern: species richness is moderate (27 in shallow marine, 22 in deep marine), but total mammalian biomass is enormous. A single blue whale masses 100,000-150,000 kg -- equivalent to approximately 1,500 adult elk or 3 million voles. The marine mammalian community achieves high biomass through large individual body size (gigantothermy) rather than high species count. This is the opposite of the terrestrial pattern, where high richness is driven by many small-bodied species (rodents, shrews, bats).

The intertidal zone (5 species) represents a genuine diversity minimum -- the land-sea transition that is too physically extreme for most terrestrial mammals and too shallow and wave-exposed for most marine mammals. Only pinnipeds bridge this gap, using the intertidal for non-foraging activities (hauling out).

### 2.4 Comparison with AVI Elevation-Richness Pattern

The mammalian elevation-richness gradient parallels the avian pattern documented in AVI ecoregion-high.md and ecoregion-low.md, but with notable differences:

| Pattern | Mammals | Birds |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Richness peak | ELEV-LOWLAND (74 spp.) | ELEV-LOWLAND (highest breeding diversity; AVI ecoregion-low.md) |
| Alpine minimum | 14 species (19% of max) | 15-20 breeding species (AVI ecoregion-high.md 1.2) |
| Marine richness | Moderate (27 shallow, 22 deep) | Lower mammalian richness than avian (seabirds add significantly to marine species counts) |
| Steppe richness | High relative to area (47 spp.) | High -- steppe supports distinctive specialist community (AVI ecoregion-high.md Section 4) |
| Subterranean | 27 species (bat + fossorial guild) | No avian equivalent (birds do not occupy subterranean habitats) |
| Migration effect | Limited -- few PNW mammals are long-distance migrants (bats, cetaceans) | Massive -- migratory species nearly double effective richness in spring/summer |
| Nocturnal niche | Bats (15 spp.), many rodents, mustelids | Owls only (14 spp.); nocturnal niche much smaller for birds |

The most striking difference is the nocturnal dimension. Mammals dominate the night: 15 bat species, most rodents, most mustelids, and many other species are primarily nocturnal. Birds dominate the day. This temporal niche partitioning means the same habitat supports a full mammalian community at night and a full avian community during the day, with owls as the only significant nocturnal avian predators.

## 3. Carnivore-Prey Networks by Ecoregion

### 3.1 ELEV-ALPINE: Sky Island Predator Web

```
                    Golden Eagle (AVI)
                    /              \
               Hoary Marmot    American Pika
              /                      \
    Cascade Red Fox              Ermine / Long-tailed Weasel
         |                            |
    Deer Mouse, Voles           Voles, Pikas (juvenile)
```

**Primary predator-prey pairs:**
- Golden eagle -> hoary marmot, pika, ground squirrel (dominant aerial predator)
- Cascade red fox -> pika, voles, ground squirrel (primary terrestrial predator)
- Ermine -> pika (accesses talus interstices too narrow for fox)
- Long-tailed weasel -> voles, mice, pika
- Cougar -> mountain goat (seasonal; cougar visits alpine to hunt goats in summer)

**Network characteristics:** Simple, linear food web with limited redundancy. Loss of the golden eagle would remove the primary apex predator. The alpine predator web has the lowest connectance (links per species) of any PNW terrestrial ecoregion.

### 3.2 ELEV-SUBALPINE: The Lynx-Hare Engine

```
                Wolverine (scavenger/predator)
                /          |           \
         Marmot      Carrion       Snowshoe Hare
                                   /     |     \
                          Canada Lynx  Coyote  Great Horned Owl (AVI)
                                               Northern Goshawk (AVI)
                                  |
                            Red Squirrel
                                  |
                          American Marten
```

**Primary predator-prey pairs:**
- Canada lynx -> snowshoe hare (specialist; 75-97% of diet)
- Wolverine -> marmots, carrion (primarily scavenger of ungulate carcasses)
- American marten -> red squirrel, flying squirrel, voles
- Cougar -> elk, mule deer (summer range)
- Ermine -> voles, mice (subnivean predator in winter)

**Network characteristics:** The lynx-snowshoe hare cycle is the dominant population oscillation. Hare population peaks every 8-11 years, with lynx populations tracking 1-2 years behind. In the PNW, cycle amplitude is dampened relative to boreal Canada because lynx have more alternative prey and competitors (bobcat, coyote). Wolverine functions as both predator and scavenger, linking the predator web to the ungulate death rate.

### 3.3 ELEV-MONTANE: The Wolf-Elk Cascade

```
            Gray Wolf -----> Elk, Deer
                |                |
                v                v
            Coyote (suppressed)  Riparian vegetation recovery
                |                         |
                v                         v
          Small mammal release     Beaver habitat, Salmon shade
                                          |
                                          v
                                    Ecosystem-wide benefits

     Cougar -----> Elk, Black-tailed Deer
                         |
     Northern Spotted Owl (AVI) ----> Northern Flying Squirrel
                                              |
                                    Mycorrhizal spore dispersal
```

**Primary predator-prey pairs:**
- Gray wolf -> elk, mule deer, white-tailed deer, moose
- Cougar -> elk, black-tailed deer, mule deer
- Bobcat -> snowshoe hare, mountain cottontail, squirrels
- Fisher -> porcupine, snowshoe hare, squirrels
- American marten -> flying squirrel, voles, chipmunks
- Northern spotted owl (AVI) -> northern flying squirrel (50-80% of diet)
- Northern goshawk (AVI) -> Douglas squirrel, snowshoe hare

**Network characteristics:** Highest predator-prey connectance of any terrestrial ecoregion. The wolf-elk trophic cascade is the signature interaction -- wolf predation creates a "landscape of fear" that modifies elk browsing behavior, with cascading benefits to riparian vegetation, beaver, salmon, and songbirds. The cougar-deer pathway operates independently, creating redundant top-down regulation.

### 3.4 ELEV-LOWLAND: Generalist Web

```
     Black Bear (omnivore) -----> Berries, Insects, Salmon, Carrion
                |
     Cougar ---------> Black-tailed Deer
                |
     Bobcat ---------> Brush Rabbit, Squirrels, Voles
                |
     Coyote ---------> Voles, Rabbits, Fawns, Fruit
                |
     Raccoon --------> Crayfish, Bird eggs, Garbage
```

**Primary predator-prey pairs:**
- Cougar -> black-tailed deer (primary prey west side), mule deer (east side)
- Black bear -> salmon (seasonal), berries (primary calories), insects
- Bobcat -> brush rabbit, eastern cottontail, Townsend's vole, squirrels
- Coyote -> voles, rabbits, ground squirrels, deer fawns, fruit
- Gray fox -> small rodents, birds, fruit (southern OR)
- Barn owl (AVI) -> Townsend's vole (agricultural field specialist)

**Network characteristics:** High redundancy with multiple generalist predators sharing prey species. The lowland web is the most resilient to single-species loss because no predator-prey pair is strongly obligate. However, this web is most impacted by human-wildlife conflict: rodenticide enters the food chain via rodents and bioaccumulates in bobcats, fishers, and raptors (P34).

### 3.5 ELEV-RIPARIAN: Salmon-Connected Web

```
     Black Bear -----> Salmon (spawning) -----> Marine nutrients to forest
          |
     River Otter -----> Salmon, Crayfish, Amphibians
          |
```

**Primary predator-prey pairs:**
- Black bear -> spawning salmon (seasonal but energetically critical)
- River otter -> salmon, crayfish, amphibians
- Mink -> Townsend's vole, muskrat, crayfish
- American water shrew -> aquatic invertebrates (stoneflies, mayflies, caddisflies)
- Bald eagle (AVI) -> salmon carcasses (primarily scavenging, not predation)
- Osprey (AVI) -> live salmon and trout (plunge-diving)

**Network characteristics:** The salmon thread defines this web. Salmon carcasses are a shared resource for bears, eagles, ravens, coyotes, and raccoons; marine-derived nutrients from decomposing carcasses fertilize riparian vegetation. The riparian web has the strongest marine-terrestrial nutrient coupling of any ecoregion.

The orca ecotype system is the most dramatic example of culturally mediated resource partitioning in any mammalian system.

## 4. Keystone Species by Ecoregion

### 4.1 American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*) — Riparian Ecosystem Engineer

**Primary ecoregions:** ELEV-LOWLAND (P), ELEV-RIPARIAN (P), ELEV-MONTANE (S), ELEV-STEPPE (S)

The beaver is the most consequential ecosystem engineer in the PNW. A single beaver family (4-8 individuals) can transform a linear stream reach into a complex wetland mosaic within 1-3 years. The ecological effects of beaver dams are extraordinary in scope:

**Hydrological engineering:**
- Dam construction raises the local water table by 10-100 cm, expanding the riparian zone and creating permanent or seasonal wetlands where none existed.
- Dam pools increase water storage, attenuating flood peaks by 10-30% and augmenting late-summer base flows -- the flows most critical for juvenile salmon survival.
- Pond sediment trapping removes fine particles from the water column, improving downstream water clarity and reducing turbidity stress on salmonid gills.

**Salmon habitat creation:**
- Beaver ponds provide the deep, slow-water, off-channel rearing habitat preferred by juvenile coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*). Streams with active beaver colonies produce 2-5 times more juvenile coho per kilometer than unbeavered reaches.
- Dam complexes increase stream habitat complexity, providing a mosaic of pools, riffles, runs, and backwater channels that support diverse salmonid life stages.
- Overwintering habitat: Beaver ponds provide critical winter refuge for juvenile salmonids, protecting them from winter floods and ice.

**Biodiversity amplification:**
- Beaver wetlands support 4-5 times the species richness of equivalent-length unbeavered stream reaches, across all taxa (mammals, birds, amphibians, invertebrates, plants).
- Pond margins create habitat for semi-aquatic mammals (mink, muskrat, water shrew), waterfowl, and amphibians.
- Downed trees from beaver cutting create coarse woody debris that structures stream habitat for decades after abandonment.

**Salmon thread:** Strongly connected. Beaver ecosystem engineering directly improves salmon habitat at every life stage. The beaver-salmon-forest mutualism is one of the defining ecological relationships of the PNW.

**Conservation status:** Not ESA listed. Populations have recovered from near-extirpation in the early 1900s (fur trade) to estimated 100,000+ individuals in WA and OR combined. Management trend is shifting from control (removal of "nuisance" beaver) to conservation and relocation, recognizing the species' role in watershed restoration. "Beaver dam analogs" (BDAs) -- human-built structures mimicking beaver dams -- are now a standard stream restoration tool.

**Sources:** P01, G09, G38 (Elwha restoration), ECO ecological-networks.md

### 4.2 American Pika (*Ochotona princeps*) — Alpine Climate Indicator

**Primary ecoregions:** ELEV-ALPINE (P), ELEV-SUBALPINE (S)

The pika is the defining indicator species of PNW alpine ecosystem health and climate change vulnerability. Its biology makes it uniquely sensitive to warming:

**Climate sensitivity mechanism:**
- Pikas have a high basal metabolic rate and dense fur insulation adapted for cold environments. They cannot tolerate sustained exposure to temperatures above ~25.5C and die within 6 hours at 28C.
- They do not hibernate, instead relying on haypile caches and subnivean foraging to survive winter. The insulating snowpack that protects them from extreme cold is declining in depth and duration.
- Populations at low-elevation range margins (below 5,000 ft in OR, below 5,500 ft in WA) are disappearing. Range contraction is upslope and poleward.
- Because pikas occupy isolated "sky islands" (individual mountain peaks and ridge systems), there is no higher habitat to colonize once a peak's alpine zone warms beyond tolerance -- extinction on that peak is permanent.

**Community facilitation role:**
- Haypile caches serve as emergency winter food for bushy-tailed woodrats, golden-mantled ground squirrels, and mice.
- Alarm calls alert the broader alpine small mammal and bird community to approaching predators (REL-FACILITATION).
- Pika territories create predictable prey concentrations for weasels, foxes, and golden eagles.

**Conservation status:** ESA Species of Concern (listing petition denied 2010; USFWS determined listing was "not warranted" but acknowledged climate vulnerability). Oregon: Sensitive-Vulnerable. IUCN: LC (globally), but PNW populations at low-elevation margins are declining. Long-term monitoring programs at Crater Lake NP, Mt. Rainier NP, and North Cascades NP track pika occupancy as a climate change metric.

**Sources:** P25, P26, G09, G19

### 4.3 Sea Otter (*Enhydra lutris*) — Kelp Forest Trophic Cascade

**Primary ecoregions:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (P), ELEV-INTERTIDAL (S), ELEV-COASTAL-DUNE (S)

The sea otter is the textbook example of a keystone species -- a concept literally defined by observations of sea otter ecology (Paine 1969; Estes & Palmisano 1974). The otter-urchin-kelp trophic cascade is the most thoroughly documented three-level cascade in marine ecology:

**Trophic cascade mechanism:**
- Sea otters eat sea urchins (*Strongylocentrotus* spp.) -- consuming up to 25% of their body mass daily.
- Without otter predation, urchin populations explode and overgraze kelp holdfasts, converting productive kelp forests into barren, species-poor "urchin barrens."
- With otter predation, urchin populations are suppressed and kelp forests flourish, providing habitat structure for hundreds of invertebrate and fish species, nursery habitat for rockfish and lingcod, and wave attenuation that protects coastlines.

**PNW recovery history:**
- Sea otters were extirpated from the entire PNW coast by the maritime fur trade by the early 1900s (P21). The Washington coast was otter-free for approximately 70 years.
- In 1969-1970, 59 sea otters from Amchitka Island, Alaska were translocated to the WA Olympic coast as part of the Atomic Energy Commission's nuclear testing mitigation program.
- The population has grown to approximately 2,800 individuals (2023 USFWS estimate; G16), occupying approximately 60% of historical WA coastal range.
- Kelp forest recovery has followed otter recolonization along the Olympic coast, confirming the trophic cascade in a natural experiment.

**Carbon sequestration co-benefit:** Kelp forests recovered through sea otter presence sequester atmospheric CO2 through photosynthesis and export organic carbon to deep water. Recent estimates suggest that otter-mediated kelp recovery along the North Pacific coast contributes measurably to carbon sequestration -- a co-benefit of apex predator conservation.

**Conservation status:** ESA Threatened (listed 1977). MMPA Depleted. USFWS jurisdiction (the only marine mammal managed by USFWS rather than NOAA). WA state Endangered. Population trend: increasing. Primary threats: oil spill vulnerability (otters lack blubber and depend on fur insulation; oil destroys insulating capacity, causing hypothermia and death), shark predation (increasing as white shark range expands northward), and disease.

**Sources:** G16, P21, P22, O09

### 4.4 Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*) — Trophic Cascade Initiator

**Primary ecoregions:** ELEV-MONTANE (P), ELEV-SUBALPINE (P/S), ELEV-LOWLAND (S), ELEV-RIPARIAN (S), ELEV-STEPPE (S)

The gray wolf is the terrestrial counterpart to the sea otter -- an apex predator whose presence or absence restructures entire communities through trophic cascades. The wolf-elk-riparian cascade is the most extensively documented terrestrial trophic cascade in North America:

**Trophic cascade pathway:**
1. Wolf predation reduces elk density and, critically, modifies elk behavior (the "ecology of fear"). Elk avoid lingering in riparian zones where they are vulnerable to wolf ambush.
2. Reduced riparian browsing allows recovery of willow (*Salix* spp.), black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), and red alder (*Alnus rubra*).
3. Riparian vegetation recovery provides building material for beavers, which construct dams that create salmon rearing habitat.
4. Increased canopy shade from riparian vegetation reduces stream temperature, improving thermal conditions for cold-water fish (salmon, trout).
5. Stabilized stream banks reduce erosion and sedimentation.
6. Large wood recruitment from mature riparian trees creates in-stream habitat complexity.

**Mesopredator suppression:**
Wolf presence suppresses coyote populations through direct killing and territorial exclusion. In areas with established wolf packs, coyote density declines by 33-50%. This mesopredator release reversal benefits ground-nesting birds, snowshoe hares, and small mammals that experience reduced predation pressure from the numerically abundant coyote.

**PNW recovery status:**
- Washington: 33 confirmed packs (2024); growing ~15-20% annually since first breeding pack confirmed in 2008 (G23).
- Oregon: 23 confirmed packs (2024); expanding from northeastern OR into the Cascades (G25).
- Entirely through natural recolonization from Idaho/Montana/BC source populations -- no reintroduction programs.

**Salmon thread:** Strongly connected through the wolf-elk-riparian-salmon indirect pathway. Wolf-driven behavioral changes in elk allow riparian vegetation recovery, improving salmon habitat through shade, bank stability, and large wood recruitment. Wolves also consume spawning salmon directly in coastal areas.

**Sources:** G17, G23, G25, P01, P16, P29

### 4.5 Bats (Order Chiroptera) — Insect Population Regulation and Cave Ecosystem

**Primary ecoregions:** All terrestrial bands plus ELEV-SUBTERRANEAN (roosting/hibernation)

PNW bats are not a single keystone species but a functional guild of 15 species whose collective ecological service -- insect population regulation -- is irreplaceable. Bats are the only nocturnal aerial insectivores in the PNW (swallows and swifts fill the diurnal equivalent) and consume enormous quantities of insects nightly:

**Insect consumption:**
- A single little brown bat (*Myotis lucifugus*) consumes 4-8 g of insects per night -- approximately half its body mass.
- A maternity colony of 500 little brown bats consumes an estimated 2-4 kg of insects per night (>700 kg per season).
- Across all PNW bat populations, nightly insect consumption is estimated in the hundreds of tons during peak summer activity.
- Target prey includes mosquitoes, moths (including crop pest species), beetles, and other night-flying arthropods.

**Agricultural value:**
- Bat insect predation provides measurable pest control services to PNW agriculture. Estimates for the economic value of bat pest control services in the United States range from $3.7 billion to $53 billion annually (Boyles et al. 2011). The PNW share, with its extensive fruit, grape, hop, and timber industries, is substantial.
- Forest moth predation by bats may help suppress defoliator outbreaks (western spruce budworm, Douglas-fir tussock moth) that would otherwise require costly aerial spray programs.

**Cave ecosystem anchoring:**
- Bat guano is the primary energy input for cave invertebrate communities. Without bat colonies, many cave-obligate invertebrates (troglobites) cannot sustain populations.
- Hibernacula and maternity roost sites are non-substitutable resources -- most PNW bat species show extreme site fidelity, returning to the same roost for decades or centuries.

**WNS threat:**
- White-nose syndrome is the existential threat to PNW bat populations. If WNS causes the same mortality rates in the PNW as in eastern North America (>90% for little brown bat, >90% for northern long-eared myotis), the ecological consequences for insect regulation, cave ecosystems, and agricultural pest control would be catastrophic.
- PNW status: *Pseudogymnoascus destructans* detected in WA (2016) and OR (2023). Mass mortality events have not yet occurred, but the fungus continues to spread. Monitoring and cave access management are the primary response strategies (G21, G10).

**Sources:** G10, G12, G21, G22, P05, P12, P27, P31

## 5. Seasonal Community Dynamics

### 5.1 Hibernation Patterns

Hibernation and torpor remove species from ecoregion communities for months at a time, dramatically restructuring winter food webs. The following table summarizes hibernation by ecoregion:

| Species Group | Hibernation Period | Ecoregions Affected | Winter Community Impact |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Bears** (*Ursus* spp.) | Dec-Mar (4 months) | ALP, SUB, MON, LOW, RIP | Loss of largest omnivore predator; no salmon scavenging in winter |
| **Marmots** (*Marmota* spp.) | Oct-Apr/May (7-8 months) | ALP, SUB | Loss of dominant alpine herbivore; marmot burrows used by other species |
| **Ground squirrels** (*Urocitellus*, *Callospermophilus*) | Oct-Feb/Mar (5-6 months) | SUB, MON, STP | Collapse of steppe prey base; predator dietary shifts |
| **Bats** (most species) | Nov-Mar (5 months) | All terrestrial + SUBT | Complete cessation of nocturnal aerial insectivory |
| **Chipmunks** (*Neotamias* spp.) | Nov-Feb (3-4 months, with periodic arousal) | SUB, MON, LOW, STP | Reduced seed dispersal and caching activity |
| **Jumping mice** (*Zapus* spp.) | Oct-Apr (6 months) | MON, LOW, RIP | Loss from meadow seed-disperser guild |

**Winter community restructuring:**
- **Alpine:** Winter alpine communities are reduced to 3-5 mammalian species. Pika (active year-round in talus), ptarmigan, and voles operating beneath the snowpack are the only significant herbivores. Ermine in winter-white pelage is the primary predator.
- **Subalpine:** Wolverine, lynx, and marten become the dominant predators in the absence of hibernating bears. Deep snowpack favors species with low foot-loading (lynx, wolverine, snowshoe hare) and disadvantages bobcat and deer, whose smaller feet cause them to sink.
- **Steppe:** Ground squirrel hibernation collapses the prey base for badger, coyote, and raptors. Badgers enter extended torpor periods (not true hibernation but reduced activity). Coyotes shift to jackrabbits and carrion as primary winter food.

### 5.2 Altitudinal Migration

Seasonal elevational movement is the mammalian equivalent of avian migration, shifting individuals between ecoregion bands on a seasonal cycle:

**Elk (*Cervus canadensis*):**
- Summer: ELEV-SUBALPINE and upper ELEV-MONTANE (alpine meadows and parkland for foraging; escape from insects)
- Winter: ELEV-LOWLAND and ELEV-RIPARIAN (valley bottoms for reduced snow depth and thermal cover)
- Migration distance: 20-100 km one way, depending on topography
- Migration timing: Upslope April-June; downslope October-November
- Community impact: Summer elk grazing shapes subalpine meadow composition; winter elk concentration in lowland valleys creates browsing pressure on riparian vegetation (see wolf-elk trophic cascade, Section 4.4)

**Mule deer / Black-tailed deer (*Odocoileus hemionus*):**
- Summer: ELEV-MONTANE to ELEV-SUBALPINE
- Winter: ELEV-LOWLAND and ELEV-STEPPE (east side mule deer winter on lower-elevation sagebrush ranges)
- Migration distance: 10-80 km
- Traditional migration routes are maintained across generations ("ancestral pathways"); disruption by highway construction, fencing, and development fragments migration corridors

**Black bear (*Ursus americanus*):**
- Not true migration but seasonal elevation shift: spring feeding at low elevations (ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND) on early green-up; summer at ELEV-MONTANE to ELEV-SUBALPINE for berry crops; fall descent to salmon streams (ELEV-RIPARIAN) for spawning season; winter denning at ELEV-MONTANE
- Bears do not defend territories during seasonal movement, allowing overlap with other bears

**Mountain goat (*Oreamnos americanus*):**
- Generally resident at ELEV-ALPINE and upper ELEV-SUBALPINE year-round
- Limited downslope movement during extreme winter storms; return to alpine as conditions improve
- Least migratory of PNW ungulates; specialized hooves and winter-adapted physiology allow alpine residency even in severe conditions

### 5.3 Marine Seasonal Shifts

**Gray whale migration:**
- Northbound: March-May (PNW passage, heading to Arctic feeding grounds)
- Southbound: October-December (heading to Baja California breeding lagoons)
- Pacific Coast Feeding Group (~200 whales) remains in PNW waters year-round, feeding in shallow nearshore habitats
- PNW whale-watching industry is timed to the spring and fall migration pulses

**Humpback whale seasonal presence:**
- Present in PNW waters: April-November (feeding on krill and forage fish)
- Absent: December-March (breeding grounds off Mexico/Central America/Hawaii)
- Returning earlier and departing later as prey availability shifts -- possible climate-related phenology change

**Harbor seal pupping cycle:**
- Pupping season: May-September (peak June-July in WA; slightly later in OR)
- Pupping occurs at established haul-out sites on intertidal and coastal dune substrates
- Mothers nurse pups for 4-6 weeks; pups are weaned abruptly and must learn to forage independently
- Pupping season creates vulnerability to terrestrial predators (coyotes at isolated haul-out sites) and human disturbance

**California sea lion seasonal presence:**
- Males present: September-May (migrate north to PNW after breeding season in Channel Islands)
- Males absent: June-August (return to southern CA breeding rookeries)
- Females rarely reach PNW latitudes
- Winter concentration at Columbia River dams (salmon predation conflict) is a management flashpoint

**Northern fur seal:**
- Present in PNW offshore waters: October-June
- Absent: July-September (breeding on Pribilof Islands, Alaska)
- Winter pelagic habitat; rarely approaches shore in PNW

### 5.4 Subnivean Ecology

The subnivean zone -- the air space between the ground surface and the overlying snowpack -- is a critical winter habitat for small mammals at ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, and ELEV-MONTANE. Stable temperatures near 0C (insulated from ambient air temperatures that may reach -20 to -40C), protection from wind, and concealment from visual predators make the subnivean zone a winter refuge for species too small to migrate or hibernate.

**Active subnivean species:**
- **Voles** (*Microtus* spp., *Myodes* spp.): Continue foraging, breeding (winter breeding documented in some populations), and tunnel-building beneath the snowpack. Vole populations can increase during winter if subnivean conditions are favorable, creating a spring prey pulse for emerging predators.
- **Shrews** (*Sorex* spp.): Active year-round in the subnivean zone. Their extreme metabolic rate (highest of any mammal by mass) requires constant foraging. Shrews must eat every 2-3 hours or risk starvation.
- **Ermine** (*Mustela erminea*): The primary subnivean predator. Its slender body and winter-white pelage are adaptations for hunting voles beneath the snow. Ermine can enter vole tunnels that are too narrow for larger predators.
- **Pikas** (*Ochotona princeps*): Active beneath talus-trapped snow, feeding on cached haypiles. The talus interstices function as a natural subnivean habitat regardless of snowpack depth.
- **Deer mice** (*Peromyscus maniculatus*): Enter torpor during extreme cold but are generally active in the subnivean zone, feeding on cached seeds and insects.

**Subnivean community dynamics:**
- The subnivean zone is effectively invisible to most predators. Owls (especially great gray owl and boreal owl) can detect subnivean voles acoustically through snow and plunge-dive to capture them. Coyotes and foxes can hear vole movement through shallow snow and pounce. These specialized hunting techniques represent some of the most remarkable predatory adaptations in the PNW.
- Snowpack depth and duration are critical. Shallow snowpack (<30 cm) does not provide adequate insulation; deep, stable snowpack (>50 cm) creates optimal subnivean conditions. Climate-driven snowpack reduction at mid-elevations is compressing the subnivean zone and increasing winter mortality for small mammals that depend on it.

### 6.2 Shared Habitat Dependencies

**Old-Growth Forest (HAB-OLD-GROWTH):**
The old-growth forest community represents the tightest avian-mammalian co-dependency in the PNW. Three species -- northern spotted owl, fisher, and American marten -- all require the same structural features: large-diameter snags with cavities (nesting/denning), multi-layered canopy (foraging cover), and downed coarse woody debris (prey habitat). Their shared dependency means that old-growth conservation benefits all three simultaneously, but old-growth loss harms all three simultaneously.

| Species | Taxon | Old-Growth Feature Required | Conservation Status |
|---------|-------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Northern spotted owl | AVI | Canopy cavities for nesting; forest interior for territory | ESA Threatened |
| Barred owl | AVI | Similar to spotted owl (invasive competitor) | Not listed |
| Northern flying squirrel | MAM | Canopy for gliding; snag cavities for nesting | Not listed (but prey base for listed species) |
| Red tree vole | MAM | Old-growth Douglas-fir canopy (obligate arboreal) | Federal Species of Concern |
| Fisher | MAM | Large-diameter cavities in snags/live trees for denning | ESA Threatened (S. Sierra); reintroduced WA/OR |
| American marten | MAM | Continuous canopy cover; complex understory | WA Sensitive |
| Pileated woodpecker | AVI | Large snags for cavity excavation; cavities reused by fisher, marten, flying squirrel | Not listed |

The pileated woodpecker (AVI) creates the large cavities that fisher, marten, and flying squirrel depend on for denning. This makes the pileated woodpecker a keystone facilitator for the old-growth mammal community -- a cross-taxonomy keystone interaction.

### 6.3 Competition for Salmon Resources

Salmon are the shared currency of PNW riparian ecology, and the competition (or more precisely, resource partitioning) between bears and eagles at salmon spawning sites is one of the most visible mammal-bird interactions in the region:

**Black bear vs. Bald eagle salmon access:**
- Bears are dominant at salmon carcasses on the stream bank. Eagles defer to bears, waiting until bears move before scavenging.
- Bears preferentially consume brain and roe (highest caloric density), discarding partially consumed carcasses that eagles then scavenge.
- This is not true competition but sequential resource partitioning: bears harvest first, eagles clean up second, and both transport marine-derived nutrients into the riparian forest (bear via scat, eagle via nest material and prey remains).
- At peak salmon runs, the resource is not limiting and both species feed simultaneously without conflict. At low runs (increasingly common as salmon decline), competitive exclusion by bears may reduce eagle foraging success.

**Other salmon-thread mammals vs. salmon-thread birds:**
- River otter vs. osprey: Both are piscivorous, but otters hunt underwater and osprey plunge-dive from above. Spatial partitioning (otter in pools, osprey in riffles/open water) minimizes interference.
- Mink vs. belted kingfisher (AVI): Similar prey (small fish, crayfish) in the same riparian microhabitat. Mink hunts at night and in turbid water; kingfisher hunts during the day in clear water. Temporal and visual partitioning.
- Harbor seal / sea lion vs. bald eagle: Sea lions consume adult salmon at river mouths and dams; eagles scavenge post-spawning carcasses upstream. Spatial separation along the river continuum.

### 6.4 Bat-Bird Niche Partitioning: Aerial Insectivory Day/Night

The aerial insectivore guild is partitioned into diurnal (avian) and nocturnal (chiropteran) components, creating a 24-hour insect predation cycle with almost no temporal overlap:

| Time Period | Dominant Aerial Insectivore | Species Examples | Ecoregion Peak |
|------------|----------------------------|------------------|----------------|
| Dawn-Dusk | Birds (swallows, swifts, flycatchers) | Vaux's swift, violet-green swallow, tree swallow, olive-sided flycatcher, western wood-pewee | All terrestrial |
| Dusk-Dawn | Bats | Little brown bat, big brown bat, silver-haired bat, hoary bat, California myotis | MON, LOW, RIP |
| Crepuscular overlap (30-60 min at dawn/dusk) | Both | Common nighthawk (AVI) and bats share the twilight insect flush | LOW, RIP, STP |

**Niche partitioning mechanisms:**
- **Temporal:** Near-complete day/night segregation. Bats are physically incapable of sustained diurnal activity (UV-sensitive wing membranes, thermoregulation constraints). Swallows and swifts cannot echolocate and are unable to hunt in darkness.
- **Foraging altitude:** Hoary bats forage at canopy height and above (30-100 m), overlapping with swifts. Little brown bats and Yuma myotis forage at 2-10 m over water surfaces, overlapping with swallows. But temporal separation prevents competition.
- **Prey size:** Bats in the PNW generally take smaller insects (mosquitoes, midges, small moths) than swallows (larger flies, dragonflies). The exception is the pallid bat, which takes large ground-dwelling arthropods (scorpions, beetles) not targeted by any bird.
- **Common nighthawk exception:** The common nighthawk (AVI: *Chordeiles minor*) is the only PNW bird that significantly overlaps with bats in the crepuscular period. Nighthawks are visual hunters (no echolocation) that exploit the dusk insect flight, creating a brief 30-60 minute overlap window with early-emerging bat species.

**Ecological implication:** The bat-bird temporal complementarity means that insect communities experience predation pressure around the clock during the summer activity season (April-October). This continuous predation cycle suppresses nocturnal pest moths (codling moth, spruce budworm adults, Douglas-fir tussock moth adults) that are inaccessible to diurnal avian insectivores. Loss of bat populations to WNS would create a nocturnal insect predation vacuum that no avian species can fill.

## 7. Conservation Priorities by Ecoregion

### 7.1 Most Threatened Ecoregions for Mammals

| Rank | Ecoregion | Primary Threat | Habitat Loss (%) | Priority Species | Urgency |
|------|-----------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------|
| 1 | ELEV-STEPPE | Agricultural conversion, invasive cheatgrass, wildfire | 50-60% | Pygmy rabbit (ESA-E), Washington ground squirrel (candidate) | Critical |
| 2 | ELEV-LOWLAND | Urbanization, fragmentation, rodenticide | >90% old-growth removed | Western gray squirrel (WA-T), red tree vole (SOC) | High |
| 3 | ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE | Prey depletion, contaminants, vessel noise | N/A (prey-based) | SRKW (ESA-E), humpback whale (ESA-E/T) | Critical |
| 4 | ELEV-RIPARIAN | Dams, channelization, agricultural conversion | 50-70% | Columbian white-tailed deer (ESA-T/E), water shrew | High |
| 5 | ELEV-SUBALPINE | Climate-driven snowpack loss, treeline advance | Ongoing (climate) | Wolverine (candidate), Canada lynx (ESA-T), grizzly bear (ESA-T) | High |
| 6 | ELEV-ALPINE | Climate warming, range contraction | Ongoing (climate) | American pika (SOC), Olympic marmot (IUCN-VU) | Moderate-High |
| 7 | ELEV-SUBTERRANEAN | WNS, cave disturbance, mine closures | N/A | Townsend's big-eared bat, little brown bat | High (if WNS spreads) |

**Steppe -- the most threatened:** The shrub-steppe has lost more of its original extent than any other PNW ecoregion and continues to degrade through invasive cheatgrass conversion, agricultural expansion, and altered fire regimes. Cheatgrass promotes frequent, hot fires that kill sagebrush, which cannot resprout; the result is a one-way conversion from sagebrush habitat to annual grassland. The pygmy rabbit, an obligate sagebrush species, is the clearest casualty. The Columbia Basin DPS was ESA-listed as Endangered in 2003, and captive breeding/reintroduction efforts continue (G33, G36).

**Lowland forest -- the most transformed:** More than 90% of original old-growth lowland forest has been harvested. The remaining fragments support disproportionate biodiversity but are under constant pressure from urban expansion, road construction, and edge effects. Rodenticide bioaccumulation (secondary poisoning of bobcats, fishers, and raptors from consuming poisoned rodents) is an emerging threat unique to this human-dominated zone (P34).

**Shallow marine -- the Chinook bottleneck:** The shallow marine zone's conservation crisis is driven not by habitat loss per se but by prey depletion. The SRKW population of 73 individuals cannot recover without substantially increased Chinook salmon abundance. This links marine mammal conservation to freshwater habitat restoration, dam policy, hatchery reform, and fishery management -- a management challenge that crosses jurisdictions, agencies, and political boundaries.

### 7.4 Climate Adaptation Corridors

Maintaining connectivity between ecoregion bands is essential for species that need to shift range in response to climate change. The following corridors are highest priority for mammalian climate adaptation:

**North-south corridors:**
- **Cascade Range spine:** Continuous alpine-subalpine-montane habitat from BC border to southern OR. Critical for wolverine, lynx, marten, and pika dispersal along the mountain range axis.
- **Coast Range corridor:** Lower-elevation forest corridor from Olympic Peninsula through coastal WA/OR. Critical for fisher, marten, and black bear connectivity.
- **East-side steppe-to-mountain connections:** Columbia Basin to Blue Mountains to Wallowa Mountains. Critical for mule deer winter-summer migration and ground squirrel population connectivity.

**Elevation corridors (upslope/downslope):**
- **Major river valleys:** Skagit, Nooksack, Snoqualmie, Columbia Gorge, Willamette, Umpqua, Rogue. These valleys provide low-elevation pathways connecting riparian to montane habitats, used by elk, deer, bear, cougar, and wolf for seasonal altitudinal migration.
- **Olympic Peninsula radial drainages:** Elwha (restored), Hoh, Queets, Quinault. These steep-gradient rivers connect marine, riparian, lowland, montane, subalpine, and alpine zones within a 40 km horizontal distance, creating compressed elevation corridors.
- **I-90 corridor (Snoqualmie Pass):** The most significant highway wildlife crossing infrastructure in the PNW. Bridges and underpasses built as part of the I-90 Snoqualmie Pass East project connect north and south Cascade populations for cougar, elk, deer, marten, and other species. This is a model for transportation-wildlife integration.

**Marine corridors:**
- **Salish Sea-Pacific coast connectivity:** SRKW require unimpeded passage between their inland Salish Sea summer habitat and outer coast winter foraging areas. Vessel traffic, underwater noise, and fishing gear in Haro Strait and the Strait of Juan de Fuca are corridor-level threats.
- **Kelp forest connectivity (sea otter):** Continuous kelp forest along the Olympic coast supports sea otter population expansion. Gaps in kelp coverage (from urchin barrens or storm damage) fragment otter populations.
