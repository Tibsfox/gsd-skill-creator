# PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — Module 7: Cultural Knowledge — Indigenous Mammal Relationships, Fur Trade, and Colonial Transformation

> **"Fur, Fin & Fang" — Pacific Northwest Mammalian Biodiversity**
>
> This module documents the relationships between Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples and the mammals that have sustained, shaped, and been shaped by those peoples for millennia. It is a document about relationships — not about "resources" to be managed, but about beings whose lives are entangled with human lives in ways that Western science is only beginning to understand and that Indigenous knowledge systems have articulated for thousands of years.
>
> The PNW is home to more than 60 federally recognized tribes and First Nations, each with distinct languages, territories, governance structures, and ecological knowledge traditions. No single document can represent the breadth and depth of these traditions. This module draws exclusively from published ethnographic, archaeological, and anthropological literature — primarily the works of Wayne Suttles, Hilary Stewart, Eugene Hunn, Robert Boyd, and institutional sources such as the Makah Cultural and Research Center, the Burke Museum, and the Royal BC Museum. It does not claim to represent any nation's cultural perspective authoritatively, and it acknowledges that the most important knowledge — restricted, sacred, and ceremonially governed knowledge — is not included here because it is not ours to document.
>
> The module closes with the fur trade and colonial transformation — the period when commercial exploitation of PNW mammals by European, Russian, and American interests fundamentally altered both mammal populations and Indigenous economies. This history is inseparable from the biology: the current distribution, population size, and conservation status of every large mammal in the PNW bears the mark of the colonial period.
>
> **OCAP/CARE/UNDRIP Compliance Framework:**
> - **Ownership, Control, Access, Possession (OCAP):** Indigenous communities own their cultural knowledge. This module presents only knowledge already published and available in public scholarship.
> - **Collective benefit, Authority to control, Responsibility, Ethics (CARE):** We use published sources to support understanding and conservation, not to extract or commodify Indigenous knowledge.
> - **UNDRIP (UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples):** Indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage, traditional knowledge, and traditional cultural expressions (Article 31). This module respects that right by limiting scope to Level 1 (published, public) content.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No sacred, restricted, or ceremonially governed knowledge. No specific ESA-listed species den, nest, or roost locations. Every Indigenous reference names a specific nation or cultural group. All content sourced from published ethnographic literature. No policy advocacy. Human-wildlife interactions documented factually within a historical framework.

## Part I: The Relational Framework — How Indigenous Peoples Understood Mammals

### 1.1 Personhood, Reciprocity, and Obligation

Across the diverse cultures of the PNW — from the maritime nations of the outer coast to the Plateau peoples of the interior Columbia Basin — mammals were understood not as "wildlife" or "natural resources" in the Western sense, but as other-than-human persons with their own societies, agency, and spiritual power. This is not metaphor. In the cosmological frameworks documented by Suttles, Stewart, Hunn, Turner, and other ethnographers, bears, whales, wolves, deer, elk, and salmon were beings with whom humans had reciprocal relationships governed by protocols as specific and binding as those governing human social relations.

The core principle was reciprocity: animals gave themselves to humans who demonstrated proper respect. Proper respect included:

- **Ceremonial preparation** before hunting — fasting, ritual bathing, prayer, sexual abstinence, and consultation with spiritual advisors
- **First-catch ceremonies** honoring the first animal of the season — parallel to First Salmon ceremonies and structurally identical in function
- **Proper treatment of remains** — bones returned to water or earth, skulls oriented correctly, organs handled according to protocol — ensuring the animal's spirit could regenerate and return
- **Harvest restraint** — taking only what was needed, not harvesting during vulnerable periods (denning, calving, nursing)
- **Acknowledgment of the animal's sacrifice** — expressed through songs, prayers, dances, and the sharing of meat with the community

When these protocols were violated, the consequences were understood as both spiritual and ecological: animals would stop giving themselves, hunts would fail, and the violator's community would suffer. This framework functioned as a conservation ethic — not because it was designed as one, but because the relational obligations it imposed inherently limited harvest and promoted sustainable use (Suttles 1987, 1990; Turner 2014).

**Critical distinction:** This relational framework is not a "belief system" in the Western religious sense. It is a knowledge system grounded in thousands of years of observation, practice, and adaptation. When Indigenous peoples describe animals as persons, they are making an ontological claim — a statement about the nature of reality — not expressing a subjective belief. Western science's recent recognition that many animals possess complex cognition, social organization, culture, and individual personality converges toward this understanding from a different direction.

### 1.2 Ecological Knowledge as Living Tradition

Indigenous ecological knowledge of PNW mammals is not a static body of information preserved in texts. It is a living tradition transmitted through oral history, ceremony, place-names, seasonal rounds, and daily practice. Published ethnographic sources capture only a fraction of this knowledge — the fraction that was shared with non-Indigenous researchers under specific historical circumstances.

Key characteristics of PNW Indigenous mammalian ecological knowledge, as documented in published sources:

**Behavioral precision:** Indigenous hunters possessed detailed knowledge of mammal behavior, seasonal movements, habitat preferences, diet, social structure, and vulnerability patterns that in many cases anticipated or exceeded Western scientific descriptions by centuries. The Nuu-chah-nulth and Makah distinguished between fish-eating and mammal-eating orcas long before John Ford and Michael Bigg described the resident/transient distinction in the 1970s–1980s (Ford, Ellis & Balcomb; Stewart 1977).

**Temporal depth:** Knowledge systems that had been refined over 10,000+ years of continuous occupation — a time depth that no Western scientific study of PNW mammals can approach. The Sahaptin-speaking peoples of the mid-Columbia documented seasonal movements of elk, deer, and mountain goats across the Cascade Range in a system of place-names that functions as an ecological atlas (Hunn 1990).

**Integrated systems:** Indigenous knowledge did not compartmentalize mammals into separate "species" divorced from their ecological context. Rather, understanding of any single animal was embedded in understanding of the entire system — salmon, forests, rivers, weather, fire, other animals, and human communities were understood as a connected whole. This integrative perspective is what modern ecologists call "systems thinking," but Indigenous peoples practiced it as a way of life rather than as an academic framework.

**Place-based specificity:** Knowledge was tied to specific places — not "bears" in general, but the bears of this particular valley, who use this particular trail, who eat the berries of this particular meadow, who den on this particular slope. This specificity reflects millennia of localized observation and is one reason Indigenous ecological knowledge is irreplaceable — it cannot be reconstructed from general scientific principles (Turner 2014; Hunn 1990).

## Part II: Marine Mammals — Whale and Seal Ceremonies

### 2.1 Makah Whaling — The Most Technically Demanding Hunt in Indigenous North America

The Makah Nation (Kwih-dich-chuh-ahtx) of Neah Bay, Washington, hunted gray whales, humpback whales, and occasionally right whales in the open Pacific for at least 3,800 years. The archaeological evidence from the Ozette Village site — a Makah community buried and preserved by a mudslide approximately 500 years ago — provides the most detailed record of Indigenous whaling technology and culture anywhere in the Americas.

**Technology:**

The whaling canoe was a masterwork of marine engineering: 36 feet long, carved from a single western red cedar log, with a beam of approximately 5 feet. The canoe was designed for open-ocean swells — flared bow to shed waves, deep keel for tracking, and sufficient freeboard for an eight-person crew plus gear. Construction required months of work by specialists, and the canoe itself was a prestige object reflecting the wealth and status of the whaling chief who commissioned it.

The harpoon assembly was equally sophisticated: a yew-wood shaft 12–18 feet long, fitted with a detachable mussel-shell or bone harpoon head connected to a braided line of twisted sinew and bark. Attached to the line were inflated sealskin floats (typically 3–4 per set) that served multiple functions — they prevented the whale from sounding, created drag that exhausted the animal, and marked the whale's position when it was below the surface. After the initial harpoon strike, additional harpoons were planted during subsequent approaches, each adding drag. The whale was eventually dispatched with a killing lance thrust behind the pectoral fin.

The entire assembly — canoe, harpoons, floats, lances, towing gear — represented a technological system as sophisticated as any produced by pre-industrial maritime cultures anywhere in the world (Stewart 1977; Makah Cultural and Research Center).

**Spiritual preparation:**

Whaling was not simply a hunt — it was a spiritual practice that required years of preparation. The whaler (typically a hereditary chief) underwent extended periods of:

- Ritual bathing in cold ocean or freshwater streams, sometimes for months before the whaling season
- Fasting and prayer
- Sexual abstinence
- Private ceremonies at specific ritual sites (details not published, as these remain restricted knowledge)
- Physical conditioning to endure the extreme demands of the hunt

The whaler's wife also played a critical ceremonial role during the hunt — her behavior ashore was understood to directly influence the outcome at sea. The specifics of her protocols are documented in limited published sources but are largely considered restricted knowledge.

**The Treaty of Neah Bay (1855):**

The Makah's treaty with the United States is the only treaty in US history that explicitly reserves the right to hunt whales. Article 4 states: "The right of taking fish and of whaling or sealing at usual and accustomed grounds and stations is further secured to said Indians." The Makah voluntarily ceased whaling in the 1920s when commercial whaling by non-Indigenous operations had depleted whale populations — a decision that reflected the same ethic of restraint embedded in their cultural protocols. When gray whale populations recovered sufficiently, the Makah conducted a treaty hunt in 1999, harvesting a single gray whale — the first Makah whale hunt in approximately 70 years.

The 1999 hunt generated intense controversy, much of it reflecting a failure to understand the distinction between Indigenous treaty rights and commercial whaling. The Makah were exercising a constitutionally protected right that predated the existence of the United States. The hunt was conducted under IWC quota allocation and NOAA supervision. The cultural significance of the hunt for the Makah community — reconnecting with a tradition that defines their identity — cannot be captured in the language of "resource management" (Makah Cultural and Research Center [O8-C8]; Stewart 1977 [O7-C7]).

### 2.2 Nuu-chah-nulth Whaling — Open Ocean Tradition

The Nuu-chah-nulth peoples of western Vancouver Island (formerly referred to in the literature as "Nootka") were among the world's foremost open-ocean whalers. Their whaling tradition is documented in published ethnographic sources and in oral histories shared by Nuu-chah-nulth knowledge keepers through authorized publications.

**Scale and scope:** Nuu-chah-nulth whalers hunted primarily gray whales and humpback whales in the open Pacific. The whaling was conducted from large canoes in seas that are among the most dangerous in the world — the west coast of Vancouver Island is exposed to the full fetch of the Pacific, with swells regularly exceeding 15 feet and currents that can exceed 4 knots. The skill required to navigate these waters, locate whales, make precision harpoon strikes from a moving canoe, and tow a 30-ton animal to shore through heavy seas defies simple description.

**Ecological knowledge:** Nuu-chah-nulth whalers possessed a knowledge of whale behavior — migration timing, feeding patterns, surface behavior, response to weather and current — that reflected millennia of systematic observation. This knowledge was transmitted within whaling families and was considered proprietary — a form of intellectual property held by specific lineages.

**The whaling chief's role:** Whaling was not open to all community members. It was the exclusive prerogative of high-ranking chiefs who had inherited the right and undergone the required spiritual preparation. The whaler's status was defined by his success, and the distribution of whale meat and blubber was a major social and economic event — reinforcing the chief's authority and redistributing wealth across the community.

**Post-hunt protocols:** After a successful hunt, specific ceremonies honored the whale's spirit and ensured its willingness to return. The whale's remains were treated according to detailed protocols. The distribution of meat, blubber, and oil followed strictly prescribed social patterns, with specific portions allocated to the whaler, crew members, and community members according to rank (Stewart 1977; Suttles 1990 — *Northwest Coast*).

### 2.3 Lummi Nation and Killer Whales — Kinship, Not Resource

The Lummi Nation's (Lhaq'temish) relationship with killer whales is documented in published sources and was brought to international attention through the campaign to return Tokitae (Sk'aliCh'elh-tenaut) from the Miami Seaquarium to Salish Sea waters. This relationship is treated in detail in Module 3 (Southern Resident killer whale profile); here we place it in the broader context of Indigenous marine mammal relationships.

**Kinship framework:** The Lummi regard orcas not as wildlife to be managed but as relatives — reincarnated ancestors in whale form who continue to protect the Lummi people and the waters they share. This kinship relationship imposes obligations: the Lummi are responsible for the orcas' well-being as they would be for any family member. The decline of Southern Resident killer whales is therefore not merely an environmental concern for the Lummi — it is a family crisis.

**Tokitae (Sk'aliCh'elh-tenaut):** Captured during the Penn Cove roundup of August 8, 1970, Tokitae was a member of L Pod. The Lummi Nation led a decades-long campaign for her return to Salish Sea waters — an effort rooted in kinship obligation, not animal-rights activism. Tokitae died on August 18, 2023, after 53 years in captivity at the Miami Seaquarium. She was the last surviving captive Southern Resident orca (see Module 3 for full account; Ford, Ellis & Balcomb [P6]).

**Salmon-orca connection:** The Lummi understanding of orcas has always included an understanding of their dependence on salmon. The Lummi's own salmon fishery and their advocacy for salmon habitat restoration are inseparable from their orca kinship obligations — healthy salmon runs mean healthy relatives (Suttles 1987 [O6-C6]).

### 2.4 Seal and Sea Lion Harvest

Pinnipeds — harbor seals, Steller sea lions, California sea lions, and northern fur seals — were hunted by virtually all coastal PNW nations. The harvest was important for food (meat and blubber), technology (skins for clothing, waterproofing, and float bags for whaling), and trade.

**Makah and Nuu-chah-nulth seal hunting:** Sealskin floats were essential components of the whaling harpoon assembly. The procurement and preparation of seal floats was therefore a specialized craft integrated with whaling. Harbor seals were hunted at haul-out areas using clubs and nets. The harvest was seasonally regulated by cultural protocols, and specific haul-out areas were controlled by particular families or chiefs (Stewart 1977).

**Coast Salish seal hunting:** Coast Salish peoples of the Salish Sea region hunted harbor seals and occasionally Steller sea lions. Suttles (1987) documented seal hunting practices among the Musqueam, Squamish, and other nations, noting that harbor seal was an important food source particularly during winter months when salmon were less available. Seal oil was a valued condiment and preservative.

**Fur seal:** Northern fur seals (*Callorhinus ursinus*) were hunted by Makah, Quileute, and Nuu-chah-nulth during the seals' offshore migration along the PNW coast. Fur seal pelts were highly valued trade items, and the hunt required open-ocean canoe voyaging similar to (though less hazardous than) whaling.

**Sea otter:** Sea otter pelts were the most valuable fur in the PNW — and the most consequential. Coastal nations including Makah, Quileute, Quinault, Nuu-chah-nulth, Haida, and Tlingit hunted sea otters sustainably for thousands of years. The otter was hunted with specialized harpoons from canoes, and the pelts were used for clothing and ceremonial regalia. The sustainable Indigenous harvest was fundamentally different in scale and intensity from the commercial maritime fur trade that would nearly exterminate the species in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries (see Part V below; Stewart 1977 [O7-C7]; Makah Cultural and Research Center [O8-C8]).

## Part III: Terrestrial Mammals — Bear Protocols, Deer and Elk Management, and Wool

### 3.1 Bear Protocols — Respect for Power

Bears — both grizzly and black — held a position of exceptional spiritual significance across PNW cultures. The bear was understood as a being of great power, closely related to humans (bears walk upright, eat many of the same foods, nurse their young, and exhibit complex social behavior), and the killing of a bear required more elaborate ceremonial protocol than the killing of any other terrestrial mammal except, in some nations, mountain goat (see below).

**Coast Salish bear protocols:**
Suttles (1987) documented bear-hunting protocols among Coast Salish peoples of the Salish Sea region. Key elements included:

- **Hunter preparation:** Extended periods of ritual cleansing — bathing, fasting, and abstinence — before a bear hunt. The duration and specifics varied among Coast Salish nations.
- **The address to the bear:** The hunter spoke to the bear before and after the kill, acknowledging the bear's power and thanking it for giving itself. This verbal protocol was not optional — it was a requirement as binding as any technological aspect of the hunt.
- **Treatment of remains:** Bear bones were typically handled with specific protocols — not discarded carelessly, not given to dogs, and in some cases returned to specific locations. The specifics varied among nations and are not all published.
- **Bear grease:** Rendered bear fat was one of the most valued trade commodities in the PNW. It was used as a food preservative, cooking medium, medicine, and cosmetic. Bear grease trade networks connected coastal and interior peoples across the Cascades.

**Interior Salish bear traditions:**
The Nlaka'pamux (Thompson), Secwepemc (Shuswap), Stl'atl'imx (Lillooet), and Okanagan peoples had extensive bear knowledge and hunting traditions documented by James Teit (1900, 1906, 1909) and others.

- **Bear as teacher:** In Nlaka'pamux and Secwepemc oral traditions, bears were understood as beings who taught humans about plant medicines, seasonal food sources, and survival skills. The bear's extensive knowledge of edible roots, berries, and tubers was recognized as parallel to — and sometimes superior to — human botanical knowledge.
- **Denning knowledge:** Interior Salish peoples possessed detailed knowledge of bear denning behavior — timing of den entry and emergence, preferred den site characteristics, behavioral signs indicating den readiness. This knowledge was used both for hunting and for avoiding disturbance of denning bears.
- **Grizzly bear and spiritual power:** Grizzly bear was associated with extraordinary spiritual power in Interior Salish traditions. Encounters with grizzly bears could be spiritually transformative, and grizzly bear power was invoked in healing ceremonies. The details of these ceremonial associations are largely restricted knowledge, but the general framework is documented in Teit's published ethnographies.

**Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, and Tlingit bear clan systems:**
Among Northwest Coast nations with moiety and clan systems, Bear was a major crest figure. Bear clan membership conveyed specific rights, obligations, and ceremonial roles. Bear imagery appears prominently in totem poles, masks, ceremonial regalia, and house fronts. The bear's position in the clan system reflects its cultural importance — it is consistently placed among the highest-ranking crest figures (Boas 1921; Suttles 1990 — *Northwest Coast*).

### 3.2 Deer and Elk — The Staple Harvest

While salmon was the foundation of PNW coastal economies, deer and elk were the most important terrestrial mammals for both coastal and interior peoples. Ungulate hunting was conducted on a scale and with a sophistication that constitutes what some scholars describe as "incipient management" — practices that shaped ungulate populations and habitat in ways that parallel modern wildlife management, though embedded in a very different conceptual framework.

**Deer species and cultural context:**
Both Columbian black-tailed deer (west of the Cascades) and mule deer (east of the Cascades) were hunted throughout the PNW. White-tailed deer, present in northeastern Washington, northern Idaho, and the Willamette Valley, were also taken where available. The deer hunt was less ceremonially elaborate than bear hunting but was governed by its own set of protocols — proper preparation, respectful treatment of the animal, and appropriate distribution of meat.

**Hunting technologies documented in published sources:**

- **Individual stalking:** The most common method across all PNW cultures. Hunters used detailed knowledge of deer trails, feeding areas, bedding sites, and seasonal movements to approach within bow range. Disguises — deerskin capes with attached antlers — allowed hunters to approach deer by mimicking their behavior. The skill required for successful stalking was considered a spiritual as well as a physical achievement (Hunn 1990; Stewart 1977).

- **Deer drives:** Multiple hunters working cooperatively to drive deer into narrow valleys, along ridgelines, or into enclosures. Sahaptin-speaking peoples of the mid-Columbia conducted organized deer drives that could involve entire communities. The Yakama, Klickitat, and Warm Springs peoples organized fall deer drives in the eastern Cascades that combined practical harvest with social and ceremonial functions (Hunn 1990).

- **Deer fences and snares:** Linear barriers of brush, logs, or woven fiber constructed along known deer trails, with openings where snares or pit traps were placed. These structures could be elaborate and semi-permanent, maintained across generations. The Nlaka'pamux and Secwepemc used deer fences in the mountainous terrain of the Thompson and Fraser river drainages (Teit 1900, 1906).

- **Fire management:** The most consequential tool. PNW Indigenous peoples used fire to shape deer and elk habitat at landscape scales — burning underbrush to promote the growth of grasses, berries, and browse that attracted ungulates, while maintaining open travel corridors and sightlines for hunting. Robert Boyd (1999) documented extensive evidence for Indigenous fire management across the PNW, demonstrating that the "natural" forests and prairies encountered by Euro-American settlers were in many cases cultural landscapes — artifacts of thousands of years of intentional burning. The Willamette Valley's oak-prairie ecosystem, which supported large deer and elk populations, was maintained by annual Kalapuya burning. When the burning ceased after Euro-American settlement and disease-driven population decline, Douglas-fir forest rapidly invaded former prairies, fundamentally altering the habitat and the species it supported.

**Elk management:**

Roosevelt elk were culturally central to the nations of the Olympic Peninsula — Quinault, Hoh, Quileute, and Makah. The Quinault Indian Nation's relationship with Roosevelt elk includes hunting, hide and antler use, and seasonal movements tracking elk herds. Elk hunting in the Quinault territory followed seasonal protocols coordinated with salmon runs, berry harvests, and other resource cycles. Modern co-management agreements between the Quinault Indian Nation, Hoh Tribe, and the National Park Service reflect continuity of this relationship, though now conducted within a framework of federal land management (see Module 1, Roosevelt elk profile).

Rocky Mountain elk were important to Interior Plateau peoples — Nez Perce, Yakama, Cayuse, Umatilla, and others. The Nez Perce hunted elk in the Clearwater and Salmon River drainages of Idaho and conducted organized hunts during fall migration. Elk hide was the preferred material for tipi covers, clothing, and containers. Antler was worked into tools, weapons, and ceremonial objects (Hunn 1990).

### 3.3 Mountain Goat — Wool and Spiritual Significance

The mountain goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) occupied a unique cultural position in the PNW — valued not primarily for its meat but for its wool, which was the foundation of the most prestigious textile tradition on the Northwest Coast.

**Coast Salish wool weaving:**

Coast Salish peoples — including Salish-speaking nations of the Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, and Fraser River regions — developed a sophisticated weaving tradition using mountain goat wool, sometimes blended with the hair of a now-extinct breed of domesticated dog (the "wool dog" or "Salish wool dog," documented by early explorers and maintained as a distinct breed until the mid-nineteenth century). The textiles produced — robes, blankets, capes — were of exceptional quality and served as prestige goods, trade items, and ceremonial regalia.

Mountain goat wool was obtained through two primary methods:
- **Hunting:** Mountain goats were hunted in alpine terrain — an exceptionally dangerous undertaking requiring specialized knowledge of mountain travel, goat behavior, and weather. The hunt itself was spiritually significant, with protocols comparable in seriousness to bear hunting.
- **Gathering shed wool:** Mountain goats naturally shed their dense undercoat in spring. Coastal peoples traveled to alpine areas to collect shed wool from bushes, rocks, and the ground. This gathering practice was non-lethal and seasonally appropriate.

The wool was processed — washed, dried, and spun — using drop spindles and whorls. Weaving was done on two-bar looms. The resulting textiles featured complex geometric designs that carried cultural meaning — specific patterns were associated with specific families, lineages, or ceremonial contexts. Coast Salish wool weaving is experiencing a contemporary revival, with artists and knowledge keepers working to restore techniques that were suppressed during the colonial period (Stewart 1984; Suttles 1987; Turner 2014).

**Chilkat and Ravenstail weaving:**

Among the Tlingit, Tsimshian, and other northern Northwest Coast peoples, mountain goat wool was the primary fiber for Chilkat blanket weaving — one of the most technically complex textile traditions in the world. Chilkat blankets feature formline designs of clan crests (including Bear, Wolf, Raven, and Eagle) woven in mountain goat wool dyed with natural pigments. The weaving is a ceremonial art form — Chilkat blankets are worn by high-ranking individuals during potlatches and other ceremonies and represent wealth, status, and lineage identity.

### 3.4 Beaver — Watershed Architect and Cultural Keystone

The American beaver (*Castor canadensis*) was one of the most culturally significant mammals for Interior Plateau peoples and for the broader PNW economy — both before and catastrophically after European contact.

**Pre-contact beaver relationships:**

- **Sahaptin and Interior Salish:** Beaver was hunted for its meat, fur, and castoreum (the scent glands used in medicine and as a lure). Beaver dams were recognized as important features of the landscape — they created ponds that supported fish, waterfowl, and plant resources. Some published sources indicate that certain peoples managed beaver populations by not trapping all beavers from a dam, ensuring dam maintenance and continued pond habitat (Hunn 1990).

- **Coast Salish:** Beaver was less central to coastal economies (which were salmon-dominated) but was valued for its teeth (used as chisels and carved implements), fur, and ecological role. Beaver teeth were traded from interior to coastal peoples.

- **Ecological understanding:** Indigenous peoples across the PNW recognized what modern ecologists describe as "ecosystem engineering" — the beaver's ability to transform stream corridors, create wetland habitat, raise water tables, and provide water storage in an increasingly seasonal climate. This understanding was embedded in traditional management practices that maintained beaver populations rather than exterminating them (Turner 2014).

**The fur trade transformation:** Beaver was the currency of the colonial fur trade — the "hairy bank note" that drove European commercial expansion across the PNW (see Part V below). The contrast between pre-contact Indigenous beaver management and the industrial-scale trapping imposed by the fur trade is one of the starkest illustrations of how colonial economics transformed PNW mammal populations.

**Cross-reference:** See Module 2B for American beaver ecology and ecosystem engineering profile.

### 3.5 Wolf — Teacher, Predator, Kin

The gray wolf occupied a position of deep spiritual significance across PNW cultures, though the nature of the relationship varied between coastal and interior peoples.

**Nez Perce (Nimiipuu) — Wolf as Relative:**

In Nez Perce cosmology, wolf (*himiin*) is a relative and teacher — a being of intelligence, loyalty, and social organization whose pack structure mirrors the values Nez Perce society holds most important. The Nez Perce relationship with wolves is documented in both oral traditions and in the Nez Perce Tribe's active role in wolf recovery. The Nez Perce Tribe was instrumental in the 1995 gray wolf reintroduction to central Idaho — providing political support, cultural context, and practical assistance in a reintroduction effort that remains one of the most successful large-carnivore recovery programs in North American history (see Module 1, gray wolf profile).

**Northwest Coast clan systems:**

Wolf is a major crest figure in Kwakwaka'wakw, Tlingit, Haida, and Nuu-chah-nulth clan systems. Wolf clan membership conveyed specific rights, ceremonial roles, and artistic prerogatives — the right to display wolf imagery on totem poles, house fronts, masks, and ceremonial regalia. Wolf dances were performed during potlatches and winter ceremonies. The wolf's position as an apex predator and a highly social, family-oriented animal resonated with the values embedded in clan systems (Suttles 1990; Boas 1921).

**Sahaptin and Interior Salish — Wolf in Creation:**

In Sahaptin (Yakama, Klickitat, Warm Springs) and Interior Salish (Nlaka'pamux, Secwepemc, Okanagan) oral traditions, wolf appears in creation narratives as a being of intelligence and loyalty. Wolf and Coyote are often paired — Wolf as the serious, honorable elder and Coyote as the trickster whose mistakes carry moral teachings. The dynamic between the two figures encodes ecological knowledge about the different roles of apex predators and mesopredators in PNW ecosystems (Hunn 1990; Teit 1900).

---

### 3.6 Coyote — The Trickster

Coyote (*Canis latrans*) is the preeminent Trickster figure across virtually all PNW Indigenous cultures. The Coyote story cycle is one of the most extensive oral literary traditions in Indigenous North America, with hundreds of narratives documented across Sahaptin, Chinook, Coast Salish, Klamath, Nez Perce, Okanagan, and other PNW language groups.

**Ecological knowledge in Coyote stories:**

Coyote stories are not merely entertainment or moral fables — they encode precise ecological knowledge. Published examples include:

- **Origin of salmon:** In many Sahaptin versions, Coyote breaks the fish dam at Celilo Falls, releasing salmon upriver for all peoples. This narrative encodes knowledge of salmon migration barriers, the importance of free-flowing rivers, and the distribution of salmon across watersheds.

- **Seasonal resource availability:** Coyote stories frequently involve the Trickster encountering specific foods (roots, berries, fish, game) in specific locations at specific times of year — encoding the seasonal round of resource harvesting in narrative form.

- **Consequences of greed:** Many Coyote stories depict the Trickster taking more than he needs and suffering consequences. These narratives function as ecological parables about harvest restraint.

- **Species relationships:** Coyote's interactions with other animals — Wolf, Bear, Eagle, Salmon, Deer — encode observations about predator-prey relationships, competitive dynamics, and ecological niche partitioning (Hunn 1990; Suttles 1990).

**Cross-reference:** See Module 1, coyote profile, for the ecological and cultural significance section.

---

### 3.7 Cougar — The Solitary Hunter

The cougar (*Puma concolor*) was recognized across PNW cultures as the supreme solitary hunter — an animal whose skill, patience, and self-sufficiency were admired and in some cases sought as spiritual qualities.

**Sahaptin and Interior Salish:** Cougar was associated with independence, skill, and power. Among the Nlaka'pamux, cougar was recognized as a top predator whose presence indicated healthy deer populations — an ecological insight that aligns precisely with modern understanding of trophic cascades and predator-prey dynamics. Young hunters sometimes sought cougar power through vision quests, hoping to acquire the animal's stealth and precision (Teit 1900; Hunn 1990).

**Coast Salish:** Coast Salish peoples recognized cougar as a powerful and dangerous neighbor, deserving of respect and caution. Cougar encounters were considered spiritually significant events (Suttles 1987).

---

### 3.8 Other Culturally Significant Terrestrial Mammals

**Bighorn sheep:** Important to the Nez Perce, Umatilla, Yakama, and Cayuse nations whose territories encompassed canyon habitats in eastern Oregon and Washington. Bighorn horns were crafted into composite bows (a prestigious weapon) and utensils. Petroglyphs depicting bighorn sheep are found throughout the Columbia Plateau — the sheep was a symbol of sure-footed strength and mountain knowledge. The decline of bighorn sheep through disease introduced by domestic sheep is one of the most culturally consequential mammal losses for Plateau peoples (see Module 1).

**Pronghorn:** Hunted by Paiute, Bannock, and Shoshone nations across the Great Basin and PNW borderlands. Communal pronghorn drives using V-shaped brush fences are documented archaeologically in eastern Oregon. The Burns Paiute Tribe maintains cultural connections to pronghorn and the Hart Mountain landscape (see Module 1).

**Marmot:** Hoary marmots and (on Vancouver Island) Vancouver Island marmots were hunted by alpine and subalpine peoples for their fat and fur. Marmot fur robes were valued for warmth. Interior Salish peoples hunted marmots during late summer when the animals were fattest before hibernation (Teit 1900, 1906; see Module 2A).

**Rabbit and hare:** Snowshoe hares, cottontails, and (in the interior) jackrabbits were important small game for all PNW peoples. Rabbit-skin blankets — strips of rabbit fur woven around cord — were produced across the Plateau region and were valued for their exceptional warmth despite light weight. The Klamath, Modoc, and Paiute peoples produced rabbit-skin blankets in quantity (Hunn 1990; Teit 1900).

**Pika:** The American pika was known to Interior Salish and Sahaptin peoples as a talus dweller whose haypile-curing behavior was observed and incorporated into ecological knowledge. The pika's sensitivity to temperature was understood — peoples who frequented alpine areas noted the pika's dependence on cool talus microclimates (see Module 2B for pika ecology and Module 5 for evolutionary context).

## Part IV: Fire as Mammal Habitat Management

### 4.1 The Burning Landscape

Indigenous fire management is the single most consequential way that PNW peoples shaped mammal habitat at landscape scales. Robert Boyd's *Indians, Fire, and the Land in the Pacific Northwest* (1999) provides the definitive synthesis, documenting extensive evidence that the "natural" PNW landscape encountered by Euro-American settlers was in many cases a cultural landscape — maintained by thousands of years of intentional burning.

**Documented fire management practices and their mammalian consequences:**

**Willamette Valley Kalapuya burning:**
The Kalapuya peoples of the Willamette Valley conducted annual fall burns that maintained the valley's characteristic oak-prairie mosaic. These burns:
- Promoted the growth of camas, tarweed, and other food plants
- Maintained open grasslands and oak savannas that supported large deer and elk populations
- Prevented Douglas-fir encroachment that would have converted prairie to closed forest
- Created a habitat mosaic of prairie, oak woodland, and riparian forest that maximized mammalian diversity
- Supported western gray squirrels (oak-dependent) and brush rabbits (prairie-edge specialists)

When Kalapuya burning ceased after Euro-American settlement and population decline from introduced diseases (Boyd 1999 — *Spirit of Pestilence*), Douglas-fir forest rapidly invaded former prairies. The habitat transformation was dramatic and rapid — within decades, landscapes that had been open prairie for millennia became dense conifer forest. The mammalian community shifted accordingly: forest species (Douglas squirrel, red tree vole) expanded, while prairie and oak-woodland species (western gray squirrel, grassland-associated rodents) declined (Boyd 1999).

**Puget Sound prairie burning:**
Coast Salish peoples maintained prairies in the Puget Lowland (Fort Lewis, Mima Mounds, San Juan Islands) through periodic burning. These prairies supported the Mazama pocket gopher (*Thomomys mazama*) — an ESA-Threatened species now restricted to remnant prairie fragments. The pocket gopher's current conservation crisis is directly connected to the loss of fire-maintained prairies after Indigenous burning ceased (see Module 2A for Mazama pocket gopher ecology).

**Cascade and interior burning:**
Sahaptin-speaking peoples, Nez Perce, and Interior Salish peoples burned forest understory and meadow margins to:
- Maintain huckleberry fields (important food and bear-attracting habitat)
- Keep travel corridors open for hunting
- Promote browse for deer and elk
- Reduce predator cover near camps
- Encourage grassland grazing for horses (post-contact)

The resulting landscape — open parkland forest with grass-and-berry understory — supported different mammal communities than the dense, closed-canopy forests that develop without fire. Modern fire suppression has contributed to the loss of this open-forest structure and the associated mammal communities (Boyd 1999; Hunn 1990).

---

### 4.2 The Fire Cessation Cascade

The cessation of Indigenous burning — driven by population collapse from introduced diseases (smallpox, malaria, measles — reducing some PNW Indigenous populations by 80-90% between 1774 and 1874; Boyd 1999), forced removal to reservations, and active fire suppression by Euro-American land managers — triggered a landscape transformation with cascading mammalian consequences:

1. **Prairie → forest conversion:** Open prairies and savannas that had been maintained by burning were invaded by conifers within decades. Species dependent on open habitat (western gray squirrel, pocket gophers, ground squirrels, badger) lost habitat.

2. **Forest densification:** Without understory burning, forests became denser with heavy fuel loads. Dense forests favored different mammal communities — more arboreal species (Douglas squirrel, flying squirrel, marten) but potentially less browse for deer and elk.

3. **Catastrophic fire regime:** The accumulation of fuel loads from fire suppression has produced the modern era of large, intense wildfires that differ fundamentally from the frequent, low-intensity burns of the Indigenous management regime. These catastrophic fires destroy habitat for decades rather than creating the mosaic of successional stages that supported high mammalian diversity.

4. **Berry field loss:** Huckleberry fields, maintained by periodic burning, have been overgrown by conifers across much of the Cascades and northern Rockies. This has reduced food availability for black bears and grizzly bears, who depend heavily on huckleberries during the late-summer hyperphagia period (see Module 1, black bear and grizzly bear profiles; Module 4, Zone 2).

The relationship between Indigenous fire management and mammal communities is one of the most important but underappreciated aspects of PNW mammalian ecology. The "natural" baselines used in modern wildlife management often reference a landscape that was itself a product of millennia of human management — a landscape that no longer exists and that cannot be recreated without restoring the fire regimes that maintained it.

## Part V: The Fur Trade — Colonial Transformation of PNW Mammal Populations

### 5.1 Overview: Two Fur Trades

The PNW experienced two overlapping but distinct fur trade systems, each targeting different species and operating through different economic and political structures:

**The maritime fur trade (1785–1841):** Conducted from ships, targeting sea otter pelts for the China market. European, Russian, and American trading vessels visited the PNW coast, trading manufactured goods (iron, blankets, firearms, copper) with Indigenous peoples for sea otter pelts. The pelts were shipped to Canton (Guangzhou), where they commanded extraordinary prices — a single prime sea otter pelt could sell for $100 or more in the Canton market, equivalent to several years' wages for a European laborer. The maritime fur trade was the primary driver of sea otter extirpation from the PNW coast (Gibson 1992).

**The land-based fur trade (1793–1860s):** Conducted through fixed trading posts operated by the North West Company (1793–1821) and the Hudson's Bay Company (1821–1860s), targeting beaver, marten, fisher, mink, river otter, fox, wolf, bear, and other terrestrial furbearers. Indigenous trappers harvested the animals and traded pelts at company posts for manufactured goods. The land-based fur trade reshaped both mammal populations and Indigenous economies across the interior PNW (Mackie 1997; Rich 1959).

---

### 5.2 The Maritime Fur Trade and Sea Otter Extirpation

**Pre-trade status:** Sea otters were abundant along the entire PNW coast, from the Aleutian Islands to Baja California. Their population in the northeast Pacific is estimated at 150,000–300,000 before commercial exploitation. Indigenous peoples had hunted sea otters sustainably for thousands of years — the harvest was limited by technology (canoe-based hunting with harpoons), cultural protocols (ceremonial restrictions on harvest), and the relatively small scale of Indigenous populations relative to otter abundance.

**Timeline of exploitation:**

- **1741:** Vitus Bering's expedition returns to Russia with sea otter pelts from Alaska, igniting Russian interest in the North Pacific fur trade
- **1774–1785:** Russian, Spanish, and British expeditions contact PNW coastal nations. Initial trade is small-scale.
- **1785:** The maritime fur trade begins in earnest. British Captain James Hanna trades for 560 sea otter pelts at Nootka Sound (Nuu-chah-nulth territory) and sells them in Canton for $20,000 — word of this profit triggers a rush.
- **1790s–1800s:** Peak period. Dozens of trading vessels operate along the PNW coast each season. Competition among British, American, and Russian traders drives prices up and otter populations down. By 1800, sea otters are becoming scarce in the most heavily traded areas.
- **1810s–1820s:** Decline. Otter populations are depleted to the point where the maritime trade becomes unprofitable. The last significant maritime fur trade voyages to the PNW occur in the 1830s.
- **By 1900:** Sea otters are functionally extinct along the entire PNW coast from British Columbia to California. Scattered remnants survive only in the Aleutian Islands and a few isolated localities.

**Ecological consequences:** The extirpation of sea otters triggered a trophic cascade in nearshore marine ecosystems. Without otter predation, sea urchin populations exploded, overgrazing kelp forests and converting them to "urchin barrens" — barren rocky substrate with minimal algal cover. The loss of kelp forests reduced habitat for fish, invertebrates, and seabirds. The PNW coast existed in this otter-absent, kelp-depleted state for nearly a century before sea otter reintroduction began in the 1960s–1970s (see Module 3, sea otter profile).

**Cultural consequences:** The maritime fur trade transformed Indigenous coastal economies. Nations that had previously traded regionally now had access to iron tools, firearms, copper, and blankets — goods that increased productive capacity but also created dependency on the trade. The trade intensified inter-group competition and in some cases exacerbated conflict. It also brought the first significant exposure to European diseases — smallpox, influenza, and measles — that would devastate PNW Indigenous populations in the decades that followed (Boyd 1999; Gibson 1992).

---

### 5.3 The Hudson's Bay Company and Beaver Depletion

The land-based fur trade, dominated after 1821 by the Hudson's Bay Company (HBC), operated through a network of trading posts across the interior PNW. The HBC's primary target was beaver, whose dense underfur was felted into waterproof hats — the dominant fashion in Europe from the seventeenth through the mid-nineteenth century.

**The "fur desert" policy:**

Between 1824 and the 1840s, HBC Chief Factor John McLoughlin implemented a deliberate policy of intensive trapping in the Snake River country (present-day southern Idaho, eastern Oregon, and northern Nevada) to create a "fur desert" — a zone stripped of beaver and other furbearers — as a strategic barrier to American westward expansion. The logic was that American fur trappers would not cross into HBC territory if there were no furbearers to trap along the way. This policy was explicitly extractive: HBC trappers were instructed to take every beaver they could find, with no regard for population sustainability.

The ecological consequences were severe:
- **Beaver populations collapsed** across the Snake River drainage, the Malheur Basin, the Deschutes, the John Day, and other interior river systems. Beavers were reduced to remnant populations or locally extirpated.
- **Stream habitat degraded:** Without beaver dams, streams incised (cut downward), water tables dropped, wetlands dried, and riparian vegetation declined. The hydrological consequences of beaver removal persist in many PNW stream systems today — modern stream restoration efforts increasingly incorporate beaver dam analogs (BDAs) to reverse the incision and dewatering caused by nineteenth-century trapping (see Module 2B for beaver ecology and ecosystem engineering).
- **Cascading effects on other species:** The loss of beaver-created wetland habitat affected fish (reduced juvenile salmon rearing habitat), waterfowl, amphibians, and riparian mammals (muskrat, mink, river otter).

**Other furbearers traded:**

Beyond beaver, the land-based fur trade targeted:
- **Marten:** Prized for soft, dense fur. Intensive trapping depleted marten populations across much of the PNW by the early twentieth century. Marten depend on old-growth forest structure for denning and resting — the combination of trapping and subsequent logging eliminated marten from most of their historical range in Washington and Oregon. Modern marten populations are recovering but remain habitat-limited (see Module 1).
- **Fisher:** Virtually extirpated from Washington by the mid-twentieth century through trapping and old-growth logging. Reintroduction programs began in 2008 (see Module 1 and Module 5 for fisher conservation genetics).
- **Mink and river otter:** Trapped throughout the interior PNW. Both species have recovered substantially.
- **Wolf:** Wolf pelts were traded, and wolves were also killed as predators of livestock and competitors for game. The fur trade era initiated the systematic persecution of wolves that would culminate in their extirpation from the PNW by the mid-twentieth century (see Module 1, gray wolf profile).
- **Bear (grizzly and black):** Bear hides and bear grease were traded, though bears were less commercially valuable than beaver or marten. Grizzly bears, however, were increasingly killed as "dangerous" animals as Euro-American settlement expanded — a process that would ultimately reduce grizzly populations from across the PNW to a handful of individuals in the most remote mountain areas (see Module 1, grizzly bear profile).

---

### 5.4 Impact on Indigenous Economies and Mammal Relationships

The fur trade transformed Indigenous relationships with mammals in several fundamental ways:

**From subsistence to commodity:** Before the fur trade, Indigenous peoples harvested mammals within a framework of reciprocity, restraint, and spiritual obligation. The fur trade introduced commercial incentive to kill animals beyond subsistence need — to produce pelts for trade rather than for personal use. This shift was not immediately total — many peoples continued to observe ceremonial protocols while participating in the trade — but it fundamentally altered the scale and motivation of harvest.

**Technological change:** The introduction of steel traps (replacing Indigenous deadfalls, snares, and pit traps) and firearms dramatically increased harvest efficiency. Steel traps were indiscriminate and could be deployed in larger numbers over wider areas than traditional hunting methods. The combination of commercial incentive and industrial-scale trapping technology proved devastating for furbearer populations.

**Economic dependency:** As Indigenous peoples incorporated trade goods into their economies — iron tools, firearms, blankets, kettles, metal fishhooks — they became increasingly dependent on the trade and therefore on continued harvest of furbearers. This created a feedback loop: declining furbearer populations required trappers to work harder and range further, while increasing dependency on trade goods made it difficult to reduce trapping effort. The HBC exploited this dependency, using debt systems to bind Indigenous trappers to continued production (Mackie 1997; Rich 1959).

**Disease and depopulation:** The fur trade was a primary vector for the introduction of European diseases to PNW Indigenous populations. Smallpox epidemics struck the PNW coast in the 1770s (before sustained European contact, likely transmitted through Indigenous trade networks from initial coastal encounters) and again in 1801, 1836-1838, and 1853. Malaria devastated the lower Columbia and Willamette Valley populations between 1830 and 1833, reducing some communities by 80-90%. The cumulative effect of epidemic disease was the near-destruction of Indigenous populations throughout the PNW — and with the people went their ecological knowledge, their fire management practices, their ceremonial protocols governing mammal harvest, and their presence on the landscape as a stabilizing ecological force. The cessation of Indigenous fire management alone transformed mammal habitat across millions of acres (Boyd 1999 — *Spirit of Pestilence*).

---

### 5.5 Recovery and Reassertion

The twentieth and twenty-first centuries have seen both the ecological recovery of some exploited mammal populations and the cultural reassertion of Indigenous peoples' relationships with those animals.

**Mammal population recovery:**
- Sea otter: Reintroduced to Washington coast (1969-1970, 59 individuals from Alaska); population now ~2,000+ and expanding (see Module 3)
- Beaver: Recovering across most of the PNW; increasingly valued as a restoration tool (see Module 2B)
- Gray wolf: Recolonizing Washington and Oregon from BC and Idaho; ~40 packs in WA as of 2024 (see Module 1)
- Grizzly bear: NCE reintroduction planned under 10(j) experimental population designation (see Module 1)
- Fisher: Reintroduced to Olympic Peninsula and southern Cascades (see Module 1)
- Marten: Recovering where old-growth habitat remains; Pacific marten recognized as distinct species (see Module 5)

**Indigenous cultural reassertion:**
- **Treaty rights enforcement:** Boldt Decision (1974) affirmed Coast Salish and other PNW tribal treaty fishing rights; subsequent cases extended to hunting and gathering rights. The Makah's treaty whaling right (1855) remains the only explicit whale-hunting treaty right in US history.
- **Co-management:** Quinault Indian Nation and Hoh Tribe co-manage Roosevelt elk habitat with NPS. The Nez Perce Tribe co-manages wolf recovery in Idaho. The Kalispel Tribe participated in caribou recovery planning. The Tulalip Tribes and Colville Confederated Tribes are active participants in North Cascades grizzly bear recovery consultation.
- **Cultural revitalization:** Coast Salish wool weaving revival. Makah whaling cultural education. Nez Perce wolf-relationship teachings integrated into tribal education programs.
- **Knowledge sovereignty:** Increasing Indigenous control over research conducted on tribal lands and with tribal knowledge. OCAP, CARE, and UNDRIP frameworks provide international standards that support Indigenous data sovereignty.

## Part VI: The Lewis and Clark Expedition — A Window into Pre-Settlement Mammal Abundance

The journals of the Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804-1806), edited by Gary Moulton (1983-2001), provide one of the most detailed written accounts of PNW mammal populations before the full impact of Euro-American settlement. While the journals must be read with awareness of their cultural perspective (the explorers observed through a lens of commercial potential and Enlightenment natural history), they document a landscape of mammalian abundance that is difficult to comprehend from a modern perspective.

**Key mammal observations in the PNW:**

**Grizzly bear:** Lewis and Clark encountered grizzly bears repeatedly along the Columbia, Snake, and Missouri river drainages. The frequency of encounters — and the danger they posed — is recorded in multiple entries documenting close calls, attacks, and defensive shootings. Grizzlies were abundant enough along the lower Columbia and in the Blue Mountains to constitute a routine hazard of travel. By the 1920s, grizzly bears had been extirpated from these same areas (see Module 1).

**Elk:** The expedition wintered at Fort Clatsop (1805-1806) near the mouth of the Columbia, and elk hunting was the primary food source during the winter encampment. The journals record that elk were abundant in the coastal forests and along the Columbia — a distribution consistent with pre-settlement Roosevelt elk populations before over-hunting and habitat conversion (see Module 1).

**Deer:** Both black-tailed deer (west of the Cascades) and mule deer (east of the Cascades) were encountered frequently. The journals distinguish between the coastal "black-tail" deer and the interior "mule" deer, providing one of the earliest written descriptions of both subspecies in the PNW.

**Beaver:** Lewis and Clark documented beaver dams and lodges along virtually every waterway they traversed in the PNW. The abundance of beaver sign — dams, lodges, cuttings — reflects pre-exploitation beaver populations that were orders of magnitude larger than modern populations. The landscape they described — with beaver-created ponds, wetlands, and meadows along every stream — was fundamentally different from the incised, dewatered stream corridors that exist today after 150 years of trapping and habitat modification (see Module 2B).

**Sea otter:** Observed along the coast near the Columbia mouth. The expedition traded for sea otter pelts with the Chinook and Clatsop peoples — pelts that were already the most valuable commodity on the coast.

**Wolf and cougar:** Both species were encountered in the interior PNW. Wolf sign (tracks, howling, sightings) was common in the Blue Mountains and along the Snake River.

**Salmon and the mammal connection:** The expedition's journals document the extraordinary abundance of salmon in the Columbia system — runs that supported the mammal community (bears, orcas, eagles) described throughout this module series. The salmon abundance Lewis and Clark witnessed was itself a product of the intact, undammed Columbia River system that no longer exists (Moulton 1983-2001).

## Part VII: Contemporary Indigenous-Mammal Relationships

### 7.1 Treaty Rights and Co-Management

Modern Indigenous-mammal relationships in the PNW operate within a complex legal framework shaped by treaties, federal trust responsibilities, and evolving co-management agreements.

**Key legal foundations:**

- **Treaty of Neah Bay (1855):** Makah Nation's explicitly reserved rights to whale, seal, and fish at usual and accustomed grounds
- **Treaty of Point Elliott (1855):** Coast Salish nations' reserved rights to hunt and fish at usual and accustomed grounds — interpreted through the Boldt Decision (1974) and subsequent cases
- **Treaty with the Nez Perces (1855):** Reserved rights to hunt, fish, and gather on open and unclaimed lands — the basis for Nez Perce participation in wolf and grizzly bear recovery
- **Colville Confederated Tribes:** Treaty and executive-order rights supporting participation in North Cascades grizzly bear recovery planning

**Active co-management examples:**

| Nation | Mammal | Arrangement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Makah Nation | Gray whale | Treaty whaling right; IWC quota; NOAA coordination |
| Quinault Indian Nation | Roosevelt elk | Co-management with NPS on Olympic Peninsula |
| Hoh Tribe | Roosevelt elk | Co-management with NPS in Hoh Rainforest |
| Nez Perce Tribe | Gray wolf | Co-management of Idaho wolf recovery |
| Tulalip Tribes | Grizzly bear | Consultation on NCE restoration |
| Colville Confederated Tribes | Grizzly bear | Consultation on NCE restoration |
| Kalispel Tribe of Indians | Caribou | Participation in Selkirk caribou recovery planning |
| Lummi Nation | Killer whale (orca) | Advocacy for SRKW recovery and salmon restoration |

---

### 7.2 Unresolved Tensions

Contemporary Indigenous-mammal relationships in the PNW exist within several unresolved tensions:

**Sovereignty vs. federal management:** Treaty rights establish Indigenous hunting, fishing, and gathering rights that predate and in some cases supersede federal wildlife management authority. The exercise of these rights — particularly in high-profile cases like Makah whaling — generates controversy that often reflects a failure by non-Indigenous publics to understand the legal and cultural basis of treaty rights.

**Recovery of once-extirpated species:** The recovery of wolves, grizzly bears, and other species that were extirpated during the colonial period raises questions about whose knowledge and values guide management. Indigenous peoples who maintained relationships with these animals through millennia of coexistence bring perspectives that are fundamentally different from — and in some cases in tension with — state and federal management frameworks oriented toward population targets and harvest quotas.

**Knowledge sovereignty:** Indigenous ecological knowledge is increasingly recognized as valuable for conservation, but the terms of its incorporation into management remain contested. The risk of extractive research — in which Indigenous knowledge is taken and used without meaningful Indigenous control — has led to the development of OCAP, CARE, and UNDRIP frameworks that assert Indigenous peoples' rights to control their own cultural and intellectual heritage.
