## Part I: Cetacea

The cetaceans of the Pacific Northwest span the full range of marine habitats from the Salish Sea's sheltered inland waters to the abyssal canyons off the continental shelf. Approximately 20 species occur regularly or occasionally in PNW waters -- 12 toothed whales (Odontoceti) and 7 baleen whales (Mysticeti), plus the exceptionally rare North Pacific right whale. These species collectively represent the apex of marine food webs and serve as integrating indicators of ocean health, prey availability, and anthropogenic pressure across the entire PNW marine ecosystem.

All cetaceans in U.S. waters are protected under the Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972 (MMPA). NOAA Fisheries (National Marine Fisheries Service) holds management authority for all cetacean stocks, publishes annual or periodic Stock Assessment Reports (SARs), and maintains the take reduction and critical habitat programs mandated by the Act. Several PNW cetacean populations carry additional protection under the Endangered Species Act (ESA), including Southern Resident killer whales, humpback whales (specific DPS units), blue whales, fin whales, sei whales, sperm whales, and North Pacific right whales.

The MMPA defines three stock status categories relevant to the profiles below:

- **Strategic:** A stock for which the level of direct human-caused mortality exceeds the Potential Biological Removal (PBR), or which is listed under the ESA, or which is declining and likely to be listed.
- **Depleted:** A stock that has been formally designated as below its Optimum Sustainable Population (OSP) under the MMPA.
- **Non-strategic:** A stock that does not meet strategic criteria; PBR is not exceeded.

Population estimates, PBR values, and Nmin (minimum population estimate used for PBR calculation) cited in the profiles below are drawn from the most recent NOAA West Coast Stock Assessment Reports (G15) unless otherwise noted.

### Killer Whale (*Orcinus orca*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Delphinidae | *Orcinus orca*
**Stock:** Multiple stocks in PNW: Eastern North Pacific Southern Resident, Eastern North Pacific Northern Resident, West Coast Transient (Bigg's), Eastern North Pacific Offshore
**Size:** Males 6.0-8.0 m, 3,600-5,400 kg; Females 5.0-7.0 m, 1,400-3,800 kg
**Features:** Black and white coloration with individually unique saddle patch behind dorsal fin; males have tall erect dorsal fin up to 1.8 m; robust body with rounded pectoral flippers (resident) or pointed pectoral flippers (Bigg's)
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 30-100 m; maximum recorded >300 m (Bigg's ecotype pursuing prey)
**PNW Distribution:** All four ecotypes occur in PNW waters. Southern Residents concentrate in the Salish Sea (San Juan Islands, Strait of Georgia, Strait of Juan de Fuca) from May through October and range along the outer coast of Washington, Oregon, and northern California in winter. Northern Residents occupy British Columbia waters from Campbell River to the Alaska border. Bigg's (transient) orcas range throughout the entire PNW coast from Alaska to California. Offshore orcas occur primarily over the continental shelf and slope.
**Key Sites:** San Juan Islands (J Pod core summer habitat), Haro Strait, Swiftsure Bank, Westport (outer coast foraging), Monterey submarine canyon (winter), Active Pass (BC)
**Diet:** Varies by ecotype -- see ecotype table below | Trophic: Apex predator
**Key Prey:** Residents: Chinook salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*), chum salmon (*O. keta*), coho salmon (*O. kisutch*). Bigg's: harbor seal (*Phoca vitulina*), Steller sea lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*), harbor porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*), Dall's porpoise (*Phocoenoides dalli*), gray whale calves (*Eschrichtius robustus*). Offshore: Pacific sleeper shark (*Somniosus pacificus*), blue shark (*Prionace glauca*), other elasmobranchs
**Predators:** None (apex)
**Reproduction:** Gestation 17 months | Calving interval 3-10 years (mean ~5 years for SRKW) | Maturity: females 12-15 years, males 15-18 years | Lifespan: females 50-80+ years, males 30-50 years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Endangered (2005) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific Southern Resident | 73 (2024) | Decreasing | 73 | 0.14 |
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific Northern Resident | ~300 (2019) | Stable/increasing | 261 | 2.6 |
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | West Coast Transient (Bigg's) | ~500+ (2018) | Increasing | 243 | 2.4 |
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific Offshore | ~300 (2014) | Unknown | 170 | 1.7 |
**Threats:** Prey limitation (Chinook salmon decline) (critical) | Contaminant bioaccumulation (PCBs, PBDEs) (high) | Vessel noise and disturbance (high) | Oil spill risk (moderate) | Inbreeding depression (SRKW) (moderate)
**Interaction:** The killer whale occupies a unique position as the only PNW marine mammal with four sympatric ecotypes exhibiting complete dietary segregation. This ecotypic radiation -- with each form specializing on entirely different prey guilds -- represents one of the most dramatic examples of resource partitioning among marine predators worldwide. Recent taxonomic revision (Morin et al. 2024, G03) proposes elevation of the resident ecotype to *O. orca ater* and Bigg's to *O. orca rectipinnus*, pending final review by the Society for Marine Mammalogy Taxonomy Committee (O05).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes -- Southern and Northern Residents are obligate salmon specialists; Chinook salmon constitutes >80% of SRKW diet by biomass
**Sources:** G01, G02, G03, G04, G13, G14, G15, P06, P17, P18, P36, O01, O02, O05

#### Killer Whale Ecotypes of the Pacific Northwest

| Ecotype | Diet | Social Structure | Range | Pop. Est. |
|---------|------|-----------------|-------|-----------|
| Southern Resident | Chinook salmon (>80% by biomass); chum, coho secondary | Matrilineal pods (J, K, L); lifelong maternal bonds; pods aggregate in superpods | Salish Sea (summer), outer WA/OR/CA coast (winter) | ~73 (2024) |
| Northern Resident | Salmon specialist (Chinook, chum, pink); broader salmon diet than SRKW | Matrilineal pods; 16 clans identified by acoustic dialect; larger pod aggregations | Northern BC coast, Queen Charlotte Strait to SE Alaska | ~300+ (2019) |
| Transient (Bigg's) | Marine mammals: harbor seals, sea lions, porpoises, gray whale calves, minke whales | Small family groups (2-6); less stable than resident pods; matrilineal but with adult male dispersal | Entire PNW coast from SE Alaska to California; unpredictable movements | ~500+ (2018) |
| Offshore | Sharks (sleeper sharks, blue sharks); large pelagic fish | Large groups (20-100+); least studied ecotype; social structure poorly understood | Continental shelf and slope; offshore waters of WA, OR, CA, BC | ~300 (2014) |

**SRKW Pod Structure:** J Pod (~24 individuals), K Pod (~16 individuals), L Pod (~33 individuals) -- as of July 2024 census (O01). Each pod is composed of matrilines -- multi-generational family units led by a post-reproductive matriarch. Matriarchs possess ecological knowledge of salmon foraging locations accumulated over decades, and their survival is correlated with offspring survival. The post-reproductive lifespan of female orcas (up to 30+ years after last reproduction) is one of only five known cases of menopause in non-human mammals, alongside short-finned pilot whales, narwhals, beluga whales, and false killer whales.

**Critical Threat: Chinook Salmon Decline**

The Southern Resident killer whale population declined from an estimated 98 individuals in 1995 to 73 in 2024, a trajectory driven primarily by the collapse of Chinook salmon stocks throughout their range. SRKW depend on Chinook salmon for more than 80% of their diet by biomass (P18), and the whales' body condition, reproductive success, and mortality rates correlate directly with Chinook abundance. Fraser River spring/summer Chinook, Columbia River spring Chinook, and Puget Sound Chinook are the primary stocks targeted, all of which are themselves ESA-listed or declining. When Chinook are scarce, SRKW females experience higher rates of failed pregnancies -- fecal hormone analysis (progesterone and thyroid markers) has documented that approximately 69% of detected SRKW pregnancies fail, with prey limitation as the leading proximate cause (P18). The whales mobilize blubber reserves during food-scarce periods, releasing stored lipophilic contaminants (PCBs, PBDEs) into the bloodstream at concentrations sufficient to cause immunosuppression and reproductive failure. This creates a lethal feedback loop: prey limitation forces fat mobilization, which releases toxins, which suppresses reproduction, which accelerates population decline.

PCB concentrations in SRKW blubber samples are among the highest recorded in any marine mammal globally, with males and post-reproductive females carrying loads exceeding 100 mg/kg lipid weight -- well above the 17 mg/kg threshold associated with adverse health effects in marine mammals (G04, P17). These contaminants entered the Puget Sound food web during the industrial era of the mid-20th century and persist in sediments, continuing to bioaccumulate through the salmon-orca trophic pathway. Bigg's orcas in the same waters carry even higher PCB loads (because marine mammal prey are at a higher trophic level than fish), yet their population is increasing -- highlighting that prey availability, not contaminant load alone, is the binding constraint on SRKW recovery.

Vessel noise compounds the prey limitation problem. SRKW use echolocation to detect, track, and capture individual salmon, and the acoustic environment of the Salish Sea -- one of the busiest shipping lanes on the Pacific coast -- degrades their foraging efficiency. Studies in Haro Strait have documented that SRKW reduce foraging calls and increase call amplitude in response to vessel noise (masking effect), and that vessel presence within 400 m correlates with reduced prey capture rates. Since 2019, Washington State has implemented vessel distance regulations (minimum 300 yards from SRKW, 400 yards in front), commercial whale-watch industry voluntary slowdowns, and a bubble curtain pilot program at key shipping corridors. Canada has implemented seasonal speed restrictions and voluntary vessel exclusion zones in SRKW critical habitat areas. NOAA designated critical habitat for SRKW in 2006 (inland Salish Sea waters) and expanded it in 2021 to include outer coast waters from the Canadian border to Point Sur, California, encompassing approximately 15,910 square miles. Recovery efforts focus on the "three Hs" -- habitat (dam removal, estuary restoration), harvest (salmon fishery management), and hydropower (spill programs at Columbia/Snake River dams) -- with the overarching goal of increasing Chinook abundance to levels that support a self-sustaining SRKW population of 120+ individuals.

### Harbor Porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Phocoenidae | *Phocoena phocoena*
**Stock:** Northern Oregon/Washington Coast, Northern California/Southern Oregon, Washington Inland Waters
**Size:** 1.4-1.9 m length, 45-70 kg mass (females slightly larger than males)
**Features:** Small, rotund body; triangular dorsal fin; dark gray dorsum fading to lighter flanks and white belly; no beak; inconspicuous at surface
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 20-60 m; maximum recorded ~220 m
**PNW Distribution:** The most abundant cetacean in PNW inland waters. Found year-round in Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan archipelago, and along the outer coast from the Columbia River north through British Columbia. Prefer cold, productive waters less than 200 m deep. Inland waters stock has increased substantially since the 1990s, reoccupying areas of southern Puget Sound where they were absent for decades. Outer coast populations inhabit nearshore waters within 20 km of shore.
**Key Sites:** Admiralty Inlet, San Juan Channel, Deception Pass, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Willapa Bay entrance, Grays Harbor entrance, Rosario Strait
**Diet:** Piscivore | Trophic: Tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*), smelt (*Hypomesus pretiosus*)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*); confirmed predation accounts for significant mortality in inland waters
**Reproduction:** Gestation 10-11 months | Calving interval 1-2 years | Maturity 3-4 years | Lifespan 15-20 years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | N. OR/WA Coast | 21,487 (2011) | Unknown | 15,123 | 151 |
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | WA Inland Waters | 11,233 (2015) | Increasing | 10,682 | 107 |
**Threats:** Gillnet bycatch (moderate) | Bigg's killer whale predation (natural) | Habitat degradation from vessel traffic (low-moderate) | Noise from pile driving (localized, high during construction)
**Interaction:** Harbor porpoises are a primary prey species for Bigg's killer whales in Puget Sound, and the increasing Bigg's population may be exerting top-down pressure on harbor porpoise abundance in some areas. Porpoises exhibit strong avoidance behavior when Bigg's orcas enter an area, shifting from active foraging to rapid directed movement away from the predators -- a behavioral response detectable in acoustic monitoring data as a sudden cessation of porpoise echolocation clicks.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- primarily a herring and sand lance specialist
**Sources:** G01, G15, G24, P19, P20, O11

### Dall's Porpoise (*Phocoenoides dalli*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Phocoenidae | *Phocoenoides dalli*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** 1.7-2.2 m length, 130-220 kg mass (males slightly larger)
**Features:** Stocky body with striking black and white coloration -- black body with conspicuous white flank patch extending from mid-body to tail stock; small triangular dorsal fin with white tip; produces a distinctive "rooster tail" spray when swimming at speed
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 50-200 m; maximum recorded ~500 m; deep-diving capability for a porpoise
**PNW Distribution:** Found year-round in PNW waters from the continental shelf edge inward through the Strait of Juan de Fuca and occasionally into Puget Sound. Most abundant over deep channels and the continental shelf break. Prefer cooler water temperatures (below 15 degrees C). More pelagic than harbor porpoise, typically farther offshore. Common in deep-water passages between the San Juan Islands and in the Strait of Georgia.
**Key Sites:** Strait of Juan de Fuca, Haro Strait, Boundary Pass, outer continental shelf (WA/OR), submarine canyons off Cape Flattery
**Diet:** Piscivore/cephalopod predator | Trophic: Tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Pacific hake (*Merluccius productus*), myctophids (lanternfish spp.), market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*), Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), capelin (*Mallotus villosus*)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*)
**Reproduction:** Gestation 11-12 months | Calving interval 1-3 years | Maturity 3-5 years (females), 5-8 years (males) | Lifespan 15-22 years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 25,750 (2014) | Unknown | 17,954 | 180 |
**Threats:** Incidental take in drift gillnet fisheries (moderate) | Prey shifts related to ocean warming (low-moderate) | Bigg's killer whale predation (natural)
**Interaction:** Dall's porpoises are the fastest-swimming cetacean in PNW waters, capable of sustained bursts exceeding 30 knots. They frequently bow-ride on large vessels and gray whales, and hybridize occasionally with harbor porpoises in BC waters -- one of the few confirmed cetacean hybrid combinations in the wild. These hybrids (dubbed "Dall's-harbor porpoise hybrids") have been documented in the San Juan Islands and Strait of Georgia.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- feeds primarily on hake, squid, and mesopelagic fish rather than salmon
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19, O11

### Pacific White-sided Dolphin (*Lagenorhynchus obliquidens*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Delphinidae | *Lagenorhynchus obliquidens*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington (northern and southern stocks may exist but are managed as single stock)
**Size:** 1.7-2.5 m length, 75-200 kg mass (males slightly larger)
**Features:** Strikingly bicolored -- dark gray back, light gray flank stripe, white belly; prominent recurved (hooked) dorsal fin with two-toned coloration (dark leading edge, light trailing edge); short rounded beak
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 20-100 m; maximum ~300 m; primarily epipelagic forager
**PNW Distribution:** Common over the continental shelf and slope from Washington through British Columbia. Occurs in large herds (sometimes hundreds to thousands) offshore, with smaller groups entering inlets and sounds. In BC, Pacific white-sided dolphins have increasingly moved into semi-enclosed waters such as the Broughton Archipelago and Johnstone Strait since the 1980s, possibly in response to ocean regime shifts. Less common in the inland Salish Sea south of the San Juan Islands. Present year-round but abundance peaks in warmer months (June-October) when productive upwelling drives prey aggregation.
**Key Sites:** Continental shelf off WA/OR coast, Monterey submarine canyon (southern extent), Broughton Archipelago (BC), Johnstone Strait, outer coast off Cape Flattery
**Diet:** Piscivore/cephalopod predator | Trophic: Tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Pacific hake (*Merluccius productus*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), Pacific saury (*Cololabis saira*), market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*), capelin (*Mallotus villosus*)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*); cookie-cutter shark (*Isistius brasiliensis*) scarring documented
**Reproduction:** Gestation 12 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 5-10 years | Lifespan 40-46 years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 26,930 (2014) | Unknown | 21,406 | 214 |
**Threats:** Drift gillnet bycatch (moderate) | Ocean warming and prey redistribution (low-moderate) | Ship strikes (low)
**Interaction:** Pacific white-sided dolphins are among the most social cetaceans in PNW waters, frequently forming mixed-species aggregations with Dall's porpoises, northern right whale dolphins, and humpback whales during feeding events. These multi-species associations likely enhance prey detection through collective echolocation and foraging efficiency, and may provide predator dilution benefits against Bigg's killer whales.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- primarily hake and anchovy specialist
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19, O11

### Risso's Dolphin (*Grampus griseus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Delphinidae | *Grampus griseus*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** 2.6-3.8 m length, 300-500 kg mass (males slightly larger)
**Features:** Bulbous, beakless head with distinctive vertical crease on forehead; body becomes progressively scarred with age, turning from dark gray to nearly white in older individuals (scarring from conspecific tooth rakes and squid tentacle marks); tall, falcate dorsal fin; no teeth in upper jaw (2-7 pairs in lower jaw only)
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 100-400 m; maximum recorded >1,000 m; specialist deep-diving dolphin
**PNW Distribution:** Primarily an offshore, warm-temperate species at the northern margin of its range in PNW waters. Most common off southern Oregon and northern California, with occasional sightings off the Washington coast during warmer water years. Prefers continental slope and submarine canyon habitats over waters 400-1,000 m deep. Sightings increase during El Nino and marine heat wave events when warm water pushes northward. Rare in the Salish Sea.
**Key Sites:** Continental slope off southern OR, Heceta Bank, Astoria submarine canyon, outer shelf off Cape Blanco
**Diet:** Cephalopod specialist | Trophic: Tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*), Humboldt squid (*Dosidicus gigas*), Octopus spp., mesopelagic squid (Cranchiidae, Gonatidae)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*); large sharks (rare)
**Reproduction:** Gestation 13-14 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 8-12 years | Lifespan 35-40+ years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 6,336 (2014) | Unknown | 4,817 | 48 |
**Threats:** Drift gillnet bycatch (low-moderate) | Ocean noise from military sonar (moderate in some regions) | Squid fishery competition (low)
**Interaction:** Risso's dolphins are the only PNW cetacean that specializes almost exclusively on cephalopods. Their progressive scarification -- which makes old animals appear nearly white -- functions as a visual age and social status indicator within herds, and may play a role in dominance hierarchy signaling. Each individual's scar pattern is unique, enabling photo-identification of individuals across years.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- cephalopod specialist with no documented connection to salmon ecology
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19

### Northern Right Whale Dolphin (*Lissodelphis borealis*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Delphinidae | *Lissodelphis borealis*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** 2.0-3.1 m length, 60-115 kg mass (males slightly larger)
**Features:** The only dolphin in the North Pacific without a dorsal fin -- smooth, streamlined body; jet-black dorsum with sharply demarcated white ventral patch from chin to tail flukes; slender body with graceful, low-profile swimming style; small, well-defined beak
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE / ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (continental slope/shelf edge)
**Depth:** Typical dives 100-300 m; maximum ~500 m; primarily mesopelagic forager
**PNW Distribution:** An offshore, cold-temperate species found primarily over the continental slope and deep shelf in waters 200-2,000 m deep. Common off the Oregon and Washington coast during fall and winter months, with abundance shifting northward in summer. Frequently travels in large herds of 100-1,000+ individuals. Seldom enters the Salish Sea or inshore waters less than 100 m deep. Associated with productive upwelling zones and the California Current frontal system.
**Key Sites:** Continental slope off Cape Flattery, Heceta Bank (OR), Astoria Canyon, outer shelf off Westport (WA)
**Diet:** Piscivore/cephalopod predator | Trophic: Tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Myctophids (lanternfish spp.), Pacific hake (*Merluccius productus*), market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*), saury (*Cololabis saira*), mesopelagic squid (Gonatidae)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*); large sharks
**Reproduction:** Gestation ~12 months | Calving interval ~2 years | Maturity ~10 years | Lifespan ~40 years (poorly documented)
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 26,556 (2014) | Unknown | 17,535 | 175 |
**Threats:** Drift gillnet bycatch (moderate -- historically significant; reduced with fishery closures) | Ocean regime shifts affecting mesopelagic prey distribution (low-moderate)
**Interaction:** Northern right whale dolphins are the fastest-swimming small cetacean in PNW offshore waters, using their finless body and streamlined profile to reduce hydrodynamic drag during high-speed travel. They frequently associate with Pacific white-sided dolphins and Dall's porpoises in multi-species feeding aggregations on the continental slope, where cooperative prey herding may increase foraging efficiency for all species involved.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- mesopelagic fish and squid specialist
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19

### Sperm Whale (*Physeter macrocephalus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Physeteridae | *Physeter macrocephalus*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** Males 15-18 m, 35,000-57,000 kg; Females 8-12 m, 12,000-24,000 kg (extreme sexual dimorphism -- the most dimorphic of any cetacean)
**Features:** Enormous, block-shaped head comprising one-third of body length containing the spermaceti organ; wrinkled, prune-like skin; narrow, underslung lower jaw with conical teeth (upper jaw teeth vestigial); single S-shaped blowhole offset to left of head; low, hump-like dorsal ridge followed by a series of crenulations ("knuckles"); broad, triangular flukes raised high before deep dives
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives 300-1,200 m for 30-60 minutes; maximum recorded ~2,250 m for 138 minutes; among the deepest-diving mammals on Earth
**PNW Distribution:** Offshore species found primarily over the continental slope and deep ocean basins west of the continental shelf. Males make seasonal migrations from tropical breeding grounds to high-latitude feeding areas, arriving in PNW waters (off WA/OR) in summer and fall. Females and juveniles remain in warmer waters farther south (California and tropical Pacific) year-round and are rarely observed north of ~40 degrees N. Solitary males or bachelor groups occur off the outer Washington and Oregon coast in waters exceeding 1,000 m depth.
**Key Sites:** Astoria Canyon, Juan de Fuca Canyon, continental slope off Cape Flattery, deep-water transit routes west of WA/OR shelf edge
**Diet:** Cephalopod specialist (deep-water squid and octopus) | Trophic: Apex predator
**Key Prey:** Giant Pacific octopus (*Enteroctopus dofleini*), Humboldt squid (*Dosidicus gigas*), Magister armhook squid (*Berryteuthis magister*), deep-sea squid (Cranchiidae, Histioteuthidae, Gonatidae)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) may harass or attack solitary individuals; confirmed predation on calves/juveniles in other ocean basins
**Reproduction:** Gestation 14-16 months | Calving interval 4-6 years | Maturity: females 7-13 years, males 18-21 years (but not socially competitive until ~25 years) | Lifespan 60-80+ years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Endangered (1970) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 2,106 (2014) | Unknown | 1,332 | 2.7 |
**Threats:** Ship strikes (moderate -- slow surface resting behavior increases collision risk) | Entanglement in fishing gear (low) | Ocean noise from seismic surveys and military sonar (moderate -- acoustic disruption of echolocation foraging) | Historical whaling depletion (population has not fully recovered)
**Interaction:** Sperm whales are the largest toothed predator on Earth and the primary apex predator of the deep mesopelagic and bathypelagic zones in PNW waters. Their fecal plumes, deposited at the surface after deep dives, transport iron and nitrogen from the deep ocean to the photic zone, fertilizing phytoplankton growth in a process termed the "whale pump." This biological nutrient cycling links the deep-sea ecosystem to surface primary productivity and atmospheric carbon sequestration.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- deep-water cephalopod specialist with no connection to salmon ecology
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19, O05

### Pygmy Sperm Whale (*Kogia breviceps*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Odontoceti | Family Kogiidae | *Kogia breviceps*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** 2.7-3.7 m length, 315-450 kg mass (slight female-biased dimorphism)
**Features:** Small, robust body with shark-like appearance; underslung lower jaw; blunt, squared head; prominent "false gill" mark (pale bracket-shaped marking behind eye); dorsal fin very small and set far back on body; slow, deliberate movements at surface; often floats motionless
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE / ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (shelf edge/slope)
**Depth:** Typical dives 200-500 m; deepest dives poorly documented but likely exceeds 500 m based on stomach content analysis (deep-water squid)
**PNW Distribution:** Rarely observed at sea due to inconspicuous behavior, small size, and offshore habitat preference. Known primarily from strandings along the Washington and Oregon coasts. Prefers warm-temperate to subtropical waters over the continental slope and deep ocean basins. At the northern margin of its range in PNW waters; more common off California. **Interaction:** Cuvier's beaked whales hold the mammalian records for both deepest dive (2,992 m) and longest dive (222 minutes), reflecting extreme physiological adaptations: enhanced myoglobin concentrations in muscle, collapsible rib cages to withstand pressure at depth, and specialized oxygen storage and management systems. ### Gray Whale (*Eschrichtius robustus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Eschrichtiidae | *Eschrichtius robustus*
**Stock:** Eastern North Pacific (primary PNW stock); Western North Pacific (critically endangered, occasional PNW occurrence)
**Size:** 13-15 m length, 15,000-35,000 kg mass (females slightly larger than males)
**Features:** Mottled gray coloration with extensive barnacle and whale lice (*Cyamus* spp.) encrustations on head, back, and flukes; no dorsal fin -- instead a series of 6-12 dorsal "knuckles" along the lower back; narrow, triangular head with arched rostrum; baleen plates short (5-25 cm) and yellowish-white; heart-shaped blow when viewed from front
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-INTERTIDAL (uniquely feeds in shallow, nearshore waters)
**Depth:** Typical feeding dives 5-50 m (the shallowest-feeding baleen whale); maximum ~170 m; feeds by suction-filtering sediment on the ocean floor
**PNW Distribution:** The most commonly observed large whale in PNW waters. The Eastern North Pacific (ENP) stock migrates annually between summer feeding grounds in the Bering, Chukchi, and Beaufort Seas and winter breeding lagoons in Baja California, Mexico -- a round-trip migration of approximately 16,000-20,000 km, the longest known annual migration of any mammal. The northbound and southbound migrations pass close along the entire PNW outer coast from December through June, with peak southbound passage in December-January and peak northbound passage in March-April. A resident "Pacific Coast Feeding Group" (PCFG) of approximately 200-250 individuals forages in PNW coastal waters from northern California to southeastern Alaska throughout summer and fall, rather than continuing to Arctic feeding grounds.
**Key Sites:** Outer coast of WA/OR (migration corridor), Depoe Bay (OR) -- year-round PCFG feeding, Westport (WA), Grays Harbor, Puget Sound (occasional), Whidbey Island (spring/early summer feeding), Clayoquot Sound (BC)
**Diet:** Benthic invertebrate specialist (unique among baleen whales) | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** Gammarid amphipods (*Ampelisca* spp., *Atylus* spp.), ghost shrimp (*Neotrypaea* spp.), mysid shrimp, polychaete worms, herring eggs and larvae (in eelgrass beds)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) regularly attacks calves and juveniles; attacks on adults less common but documented; gray whale calves are a major spring prey item for Bigg's orcas in the PNW
**Reproduction:** Gestation 12-13 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 5-11 years (females), 6-12 years (males) | Lifespan 55-70 years (possibly longer)
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Delisted (1994) | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific (ENP) | 14,526 (2023) | Decreasing from 2016 peak of ~27,000; UME 2019-present | 14,526 | 809 |
| Endangered (1970) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | W. N. Pacific (WNP) | ~300 (2023) | Uncertain/slowly increasing | ~200 | 0.4 |
**Threats:** Unusual Mortality Event (UME) 2019-present -- elevated strandings linked to Arctic prey failure and emaciation (high) | Bigg's killer whale predation on calves (natural, moderate) | Ship strikes (moderate -- nearshore migration corridor overlaps shipping lanes) | Entanglement in fixed fishing gear (low-moderate) | Noise from coastal construction and pile driving (localized) | Climate-driven Arctic prey shifts (emerging, high)
**Interaction:** Gray whales are the only cetacean that feeds primarily by suction-filtering benthic sediment. When feeding, a gray whale rolls onto its side, scoops a mouthful of sediment (preferring the right side in most individuals, creating an asymmetric wear pattern on baleen), filters out amphipods and other invertebrates, and leaves a pit in the seafloor 1-3 m wide. These feeding pits -- visible from above in shallow water -- increase benthic habitat complexity and may enhance local biodiversity by creating microhabitat heterogeneity. A single gray whale can excavate tons of sediment per day, making them significant agents of benthic bioturbation. The ongoing Unusual Mortality Event (UME), declared by NOAA in 2019, has resulted in hundreds of emaciated gray whale strandings along the Pacific coast and a population decline from ~27,000 in 2016 to ~14,500 in 2023 -- a 46% decline linked to reduced Arctic amphipod prey availability following marine heat waves and sea ice loss.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- benthic invertebrate specialist; indirect connection through shared coastal habitat with salmon-bearing estuaries

### Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenopteridae | *Megaptera novaeangliae*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington -- composed of individuals from multiple ESA Distinct Population Segments (DPS)
**Size:** 12-16 m length, 25,000-40,000 kg mass (females slightly larger)
**Features:** Long, wing-like pectoral flippers (up to one-third of body length -- longest of any cetacean); knobby protuberances ("tubercles") on head and lower jaw; dark dorsum with variable white patterning on ventral surface of flukes (individually unique for photo-ID); bushy, low blow; ventral pleats extending from chin to navel; dark baleen plates
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (feeds over continental shelf; transits deep water)
**Depth:** Typical feeding dives 20-120 m for 5-15 minutes; maximum ~240 m; feeds throughout the water column using bubble-net and lunge-feeding techniques
**PNW Distribution:** Humpback whales in PNW waters constitute one of the most important feeding aggregations on the West Coast. Present from April through November, with peak abundance June through October. Feed on herring, euphausiids (krill), and sand lance over the continental shelf from northern California through British Columbia. In winter, most individuals migrate to breeding grounds off Mexico (mainland Mexico DPS) or Central America (Central America DPS), though a growing proportion of the Mexico DPS may over-winter in PNW waters. Abundance in PNW waters has increased dramatically since the 1990s, with the CA/OR/WA population growing from a few hundred to several thousand individuals.
**Key Sites:** Monterey Bay (CA -- southern extent), Heceta Bank (OR), Cape Flattery (WA), Westport (WA), Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands (increasingly common), Johnstone Strait (BC), Gil Island (BC -- bubble-net feeding aggregation)
**Diet:** Planktivore/piscivore (krill and small schooling fish) | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), euphausiids (krill -- *Euphausia pacifica*, *Thysanoessa spinifera*), Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), juvenile rockfish (*Sebastes* spp.)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) attacks calves; humpback whales actively mob and harass Bigg's orcas (documented inter-specific defense behavior)
**Reproduction:** Gestation 11-12 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 5-10 years | Lifespan 80-100+ years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Mexico DPS: Threatened (2016 revised listing) | Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA (Mexico DPS component) | ~3,200 WC feeding aggregation (2020) | Increasing | 2,784 | 16.7 |
| Central America DPS: Endangered (2016 revised listing) | Strategic, Depleted | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA (Central America DPS component) | ~411 (2020) | Unknown | 388 | 0.8 |

**Interaction:** Humpback whales in PNW waters display a remarkable and poorly understood behavior: active interference in Bigg's killer whale predation events. This "mobbing" behavior is energetically costly and benefits no obvious kin, leading researchers to hypothesize it may represent a generalized anti-predator response triggered by orca vocalizations, possibly a byproduct of humpback mothers' evolved defense of their own calves. The 2016 ESA reclassification of humpback whales is among the most complex regulatory actions in ESA history, splitting a single global listing into 14 Distinct Population Segments (DPS) with varying ESA status from Endangered to Not Listed. In PNW waters, individuals from at least three DPS (Mexico, Central America, and Hawaii) co-occur on the same feeding grounds, creating regulatory complexity for fishery managers.

### Blue Whale (*Balaenoptera musculus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenopteridae | *Balaenoptera musculus*
**Stock:** Eastern North Pacific
**Size:** 22-30 m length, 80,000-150,000 kg mass (females slightly larger) -- the largest animal ever known to have existed on Earth

**PNW Distribution:** The Eastern North Pacific blue whale population feeds along the West Coast from the Gulf of Alaska to the eastern tropical Pacific, with the primary feeding concentration off central and southern California. Blue whales are uncommon but regularly observed in Oregon and Washington waters during summer and fall, particularly during strong upwelling years that produce dense krill aggregations along the continental shelf edge. Sightings in PNW waters have increased since the 2000s, possibly reflecting both population recovery and survey effort. They are rare north of Vancouver Island but have been documented in BC waters. In winter, most individuals migrate to breeding areas off Mexico and Costa Rica.

**Diet:** Obligate krill specialist | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** North Pacific krill (*Euphausia pacifica*), *Thysanoessa spinifera*, *Nyctiphanes simplex* (further south); a single blue whale may consume 3,500-4,000 kg of krill per day during peak feeding
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) attacks documented but rare on adults; calves and juveniles are vulnerable; many blue whales carry orca tooth rake scars
**Reproduction:** Gestation 10-12 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 5-15 years | Lifespan 80-110+ years

N. Pacific)
**Interaction:** Blue whales are the largest animal that has ever lived, exceeding the largest known dinosaurs by a factor of two in mass. Their heart alone weighs approximately 450 kg, and a small child could crawl through their aorta. This extreme body-size-to-prey-size ratio (roughly 100 million to one by mass) is unmatched in the animal kingdom and represents one of the most efficient energy capture strategies in evolutionary history. Blue whale vocalizations, at frequencies as low as 10 Hz and intensities up to 188 dB, are among the loudest sounds produced by any living organism and can propagate across entire ocean basins.

### Fin Whale (*Balaenoptera physalus*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenopteridae | *Balaenoptera physalus*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington (Northeast Pacific stock)
**Size:** 18-25 m length, 40,000-80,000 kg mass (females slightly larger) -- second largest animal on Earth after the blue whale
**Features:** Sleek, streamlined body with prominent dorsal fin set far back; unique asymmetric head pigmentation -- right lower jaw and right front baleen plates are white, left side is uniformly dark gray (the only bilaterally asymmetric coloration known in any cetacean); prominent median ridge on rostrum; V-shaped head; tall, columnar blow (4-6 m); 56-100 ventral pleats; nicknamed "greyhound of the sea" for sustained high swimming speeds
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE / ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (continental shelf edge and deep water)
**Depth:** Typical feeding dives 50-300 m for 5-15 minutes; maximum ~470 m; feeds on krill and schooling fish throughout the water column
**PNW Distribution:** Fin whales occur year-round in PNW offshore waters but are most abundant during summer and fall feeding season (June-November). Found primarily over the continental shelf edge and slope in waters 200-2,000 m deep. More common off the Oregon and Washington coast than blue whales, and observed farther inshore over the continental shelf during strong upwelling events. Occasional sightings in the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Winter distribution is poorly understood -- some individuals may remain in PNW waters year-round, while others move to lower latitudes for breeding.
**Key Sites:** Continental shelf edge off WA/OR coast, Heceta Bank (OR), Astoria Canyon, outer coast off Cape Flattery, Strait of Juan de Fuca (occasional)
**Diet:** Krill and schooling fish | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** North Pacific krill (*Euphausia pacifica*), Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), market squid (*Doryteuthis opalescens*)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) attacks documented on calves and injured adults; many fin whales carry orca tooth rake scars
**Reproduction:** Gestation 11-12 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 6-12 years | Lifespan 80-100+ years (possibly 130+ years based on amino acid racemization of eye lens nucleus)
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Endangered (1970) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 9,029 (2014) | Increasing | 8,127 | 16 |
**Threats:** Ship strikes (high -- fin whales' high-speed surface swimming and habit of resting at the surface make them the most frequently struck large whale species globally) | Entanglement in fishing gear (low-moderate) | Ocean noise (moderate -- disruption of low-frequency communication) | Historical whaling depletion (recovering, but below pre-whaling levels)
**Interaction:** The fin whale's asymmetric head pigmentation -- white on the right, dark on the left -- is hypothesized to function in prey herding: when a fin whale circles a school of fish clockwise (as typically observed), the white right side flashes at the prey, concentrating the school for a more efficient lunge. This is the only known case of asymmetric coloration used as a foraging strategy in any cetacean, and one of the most striking examples of lateralized behavior in mammals. Fin whales were the primary target of 20th-century Antarctic whaling operations, with an estimated 725,000 killed in the Southern Hemisphere alone. The North Pacific population was similarly depleted, and although recovery is underway, the Eastern North Pacific stock remains at a fraction of its pre-whaling abundance.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- krill and schooling fish specialist; herring prey overlap with salmon food web is incidental
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19, O05, O11

### Sei Whale (*Balaenoptera borealis*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenopteridae | *Balaenoptera borealis*
**Stock:** Eastern North Pacific
**Size:** 14-18 m length, 20,000-30,000 kg mass (females slightly larger)
**Features:** Sleek, dark steel-gray body with lighter ventral surface; single prominent median ridge on rostrum (distinguishes from Bryde's whale, which has three ridges); tall, strongly falcate dorsal fin; relatively short ventral pleats (extending only to the navel, unlike fin and blue whales); fine, silky baleen with dense fringes (reflects copepod-filtering specialization); blow 3-4 m, thin and conical
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical dives relatively shallow for a rorqual (15-50 m); does not deep-dive as extensively as fin or blue whales; frequently skims prey at or near the surface
**PNW Distribution:** The least commonly observed large rorqual in PNW waters. Sei whales are highly pelagic, preferring deep, oceanic waters beyond the continental shelf. They occur off the WA/OR coast during summer and fall but are far less predictable in their distribution than humpback, fin, or blue whales. Their movements are driven by copepod and krill abundance, and they may shift latitudinally across years in response to ocean conditions. Sei whales were historically more common in PNW waters before industrial whaling severely depleted the population. Current sightings are sporadic and primarily from dedicated deep-water surveys.
**Key Sites:** Deep offshore waters west of the WA/OR continental shelf; rarely approaches shore; no consistent PNW aggregation sites documented
**Diet:** Copepod specialist (skimming feeder) and opportunistic krill/fish feeder (lunge feeder) | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** Calanoid copepods (*Calanus pacificus*, *Neocalanus* spp.), North Pacific krill (*Euphausia pacifica*), Pacific saury (*Cololabis saira*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) attacks documented but rare
**Reproduction:** Gestation 11-13 months | Calving interval 2-3 years | Maturity 5-15 years | Lifespan 60-80+ years
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Endangered (1970) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific | 519 (2014) | Unknown | 374 | 0.7 |
**Threats:** Ship strikes (low-moderate) | Ocean noise (moderate) | Prey shifts due to ocean warming and copepod distribution changes (moderate-high) | Historical whaling depletion (severely depleted; very slow recovery)
**Interaction:** Sei whales are the fastest rorqual, capable of sustained speeds exceeding 25 knots, earning them the nickname "greyhound of the sea" alongside fin whales. Their feeding ecology is uniquely flexible among rorquals: they can switch between skim-feeding (surface-filtering copepods through fine baleen, similar to right whales) and lunge-feeding (engulfing dense patches of krill or fish, typical of other rorquals). This dual-mode feeding strategy allows sei whales to exploit a broader prey spectrum than other balaenopterids, but their dependence on copepod aggregations makes them vulnerable to the climate-driven changes in zooplankton phenology and distribution documented in the North Pacific since the 2014-2016 marine heat waves.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- copepod and krill specialist; no connection to salmon ecology

### Minke Whale (*Balaenoptera acutorostrata*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenopteridae | *Balaenoptera acutorostrata*
**Stock:** California/Oregon/Washington
**Size:** 7-10 m length, 5,000-10,000 kg mass (females slightly larger) -- the smallest rorqual in PNW waters
**Features:** Small, streamlined body; narrow, pointed, sharply triangular rostrum (most pointed of any rorqual); prominent, tall, falcate dorsal fin appearing simultaneously with the blow during surfacing; diagnostic white band across the pectoral flippers; dark gray-black dorsum with lighter flanks and white belly; 50-70 relatively short ventral pleats; blow small and inconspicuous (1-2 m)
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Depth:** Typical feeding dives 20-100 m; maximum ~200 m; primarily feeds in the upper water column and near the surface
**PNW Distribution:** The most frequently encountered baleen whale in PNW inland waters. Found year-round in Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands, and along the outer WA/OR coast. Minke whales are one of the few baleen whale species that regularly enters the semi-enclosed waters of the Salish Sea, where individuals have been photo-identified and show strong site fidelity to specific feeding areas across years. They prefer productive nearshore waters associated with tidal fronts, upwelling zones, and herring/sand lance concentrations. Typically solitary or in pairs; does not form large aggregations.
**Key Sites:** San Juan Islands (reliable year-round presence), Haro Strait, Admiralty Inlet, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Westport (WA outer coast), Depoe Bay area (OR), Active Pass (BC)
**Diet:** Piscivore/planktivore (small schooling fish and krill) | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), North Pacific krill (*Euphausia pacifica*), Northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), juvenile salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.)
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) confirmed predation; Bigg's orcas regularly hunt minke whales in the San Juan Islands and Strait of Juan de Fuca
**Reproduction:** Gestation 10-11 months | Calving interval 1-2 years | Maturity 5-8 years | Lifespan 40-50 years (maximum ~60 years)
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Not listed | Non-strategic | NOAA Fisheries | CA/OR/WA | 636 (2014) | Unknown | 369 | 3.7 |
**Threats:** Ship strikes (low-moderate -- nearshore habitat increases exposure) | Entanglement in fishing gear (low) | Bigg's killer whale predation (natural, locally significant) | Habitat disturbance from coastal construction (localized)
**Interaction:** Minke whales in the Salish Sea have been the subject of one of the most detailed long-term photo-identification studies of any baleen whale population in the PNW, with individual animals returning to the same feeding areas across multiple years and even decades. Their presence in Puget Sound overlaps extensively with the Bigg's killer whale population, and minke whales are a confirmed prey item for Bigg's orcas. Predation events on minke whales by Bigg's orcas have been documented repeatedly in the San Juan Islands, with attacks sometimes lasting 30-60 minutes involving coordinated group pursuit and drowning of the prey. The increasing Bigg's orca population may be exerting measurable predation pressure on the small Salish Sea minke whale population.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes -- minke whales incidentally consume juvenile salmon during herring and sand lance feeding; a minor salmon-thread connection

### North Pacific Right Whale (*Eubalaena japonica*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Cetartiodactyla | Suborder Mysticeti | Family Balaenidae | *Eubalaena japonica*
**Stock:** Eastern North Pacific (NOAA recognizes eastern and western stocks; eastern stock is the relevant PNW stock)
**Size:** 14-18 m length, 50,000-80,000 kg mass (females slightly larger)
**Features:** Massive, rotund body with no dorsal fin (no ridge, no knuckles -- completely smooth dorsal profile); strongly arched lower jaw with enormous baleen plates (up to 2.8 m long -- among the longest of any whale); diagnostic callosities (raised, roughened patches of cornified skin on the head, lip, and chin, colonized by cyamid whale lice that appear white or yellowish); broad, deeply notched flukes; black coloration, occasionally with white ventral patches; V-shaped blow from widely separated blowholes
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE / ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (historically fed over continental shelf; currently so rare that habitat use in PNW is essentially undocumented)
**Depth:** Typical feeding dives shallow (10-30 m) when skim-feeding copepod patches at or near the surface; maximum depth unknown for this species
**PNW Distribution:** The rarest large whale in the Pacific Ocean and one of the most endangered mammals on Earth. The Eastern North Pacific stock is estimated at fewer than 30-50 individuals (some estimates suggest as few as 30), making any sighting in PNW waters an extraordinary event. Historical whaling records indicate that North Pacific right whales were formerly common along the PNW coast -- they were among the first large whales targeted by Yankee whalers in the 19th century because they were slow, floated when killed (the "right" whale to hunt), and yielded large quantities of oil and baleen. By 1900, the Eastern North Pacific stock was commercially extinct. The species' current range in the eastern North Pacific is centered in the Gulf of Alaska and southeastern Bering Sea, with confirmed sightings extremely rare south of Alaska. There are no confirmed PNW sightings in recent decades, but acoustic detections of right whale calls have been recorded on deep-water hydrophone arrays off the Oregon coast, suggesting occasional transit through PNW waters.
**Key Sites:** No confirmed PNW sighting locations in modern era; historically: outer WA/OR coast, Strait of Juan de Fuca entrance. Modern range: Gulf of Alaska, southeastern Bering Sea
**Diet:** Obligate copepod specialist (skim feeder) | Trophic: Secondary consumer
**Key Prey:** Calanoid copepods (*Calanus marshallae*, *Neocalanus* spp., *Pseudocalanus* spp.) -- feeds by swimming slowly through dense copepod aggregations with mouth open, straining water through long baleen
**Predators:** Killer whale -- Bigg's ecotype (*Orcinus orca*) -- hypothesized but no documented PNW attacks given extreme rarity; large sharks (theoretical)
**Reproduction:** Gestation ~12-13 months | Calving interval 3-5 years (likely longer given population stress) | Maturity estimated 9-15 years | Lifespan potentially 70-100+ years (based on North Atlantic congener *E. glacialis* data)
**MMPA/ESA Status:**
| ESA | MMPA Status | MMPA Agency | Stock | Pop. Est. | Trend | Nmin | PBR |
|-----|-------------|-------------|-------|-----------|-------|------|-----|
| Endangered (1970) | Depleted, Strategic | NOAA Fisheries | E. N. Pacific | ~31 (2017 mark-recapture) | Unknown; no detectable recovery | 26 | 0 (PBR effectively zero -- any human-caused mortality is unsustainable) |
**Threats:** Critically small population size (the overriding threat -- demographic and genetic stochasticity) | Ship strikes (low probability but any single mortality could be catastrophic) | Entanglement in fishing gear (low probability, catastrophic consequence) | Climate-driven shifts in copepod prey distribution (potentially high) | Allee effects (difficulty finding mates at such low density)
**Interaction:** The North Pacific right whale represents the most extreme conservation crisis of any cetacean in the PNW marine ecoregion -- though its current functional presence is essentially zero. With an estimated 31 individuals in the entire Eastern North Pacific, this species exists at the absolute minimum viable population threshold. The PBR of zero means that no human-caused mortality whatsoever is biologically sustainable. The species persists in PNW waters only as a historical memory and an acoustic ghost: right whale calls have been detected on hydrophone arrays off the Oregon coast, suggesting that individual whales may occasionally transit through PNW deep water without being visually observed. If even a single right whale were confirmed in PNW waters, it would trigger immediate NOAA emergency consultation under ESA Section 7 for any federal action in the sighting area. The species' callosities -- unique to each individual like a fingerprint -- would enable immediate identification from the North Pacific right whale photo-ID catalog maintained by NOAA Alaska Fisheries Science Center.
**Salmon Thread:** No -- obligate copepod specialist; no connection to salmon ecology
**Sources:** G01, G15, P19, O05

## Regulatory Framework Summary

### Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA)

The MMPA (16 U.S.C. 1361-1423h), enacted in 1972 and most recently reauthorized in 1994, establishes a moratorium on the taking of all marine mammals in U.S. waters, with limited exceptions for scientific research, subsistence hunting by Alaska Natives, and incidental take in commercial fisheries. NOAA Fisheries (for cetaceans and pinnipeds) and USFWS (for sea otters, walruses, and polar bears) administer the Act. Key MMPA provisions relevant to PNW cetaceans:

- **Potential Biological Removal (PBR):** The maximum number of animals (not including natural mortality) that may be removed from a marine mammal stock while allowing that stock to reach or maintain its optimum sustainable population. PBR = Nmin x 0.5Rmax x Fr, where Nmin is the minimum population estimate, Rmax is the maximum rate of population increase, and Fr is a recovery factor (0.1 for endangered species, 0.5 for strategic stocks, 1.0 for non-strategic stocks).

- **Strategic Stock:** Any stock for which direct human-caused mortality exceeds PBR, or which is listed under ESA, or which is declining and likely to be listed. Strategic stocks require Take Reduction Plans.

- **Depleted Stock:** A stock formally designated as below its Optimum Sustainable Population (OSP). All ESA-listed marine mammal stocks are automatically designated as depleted.

- **Stock Assessment Reports (SARs):** Published annually or periodically by NOAA, SARs provide the official population estimate, PBR, and mortality/serious injury data for each managed stock. SARs are the primary regulatory data source for all population figures cited in this document (G15).

### Endangered Species Act (ESA)

Seven cetacean species/DPS units occurring in PNW waters are listed under the ESA: Southern Resident killer whale (Endangered), humpback whale Central America DPS (Endangered), humpback whale Mexico DPS (Threatened), blue whale (Endangered), fin whale (Endangered), sei whale (Endangered), sperm whale (Endangered), and North Pacific right whale (Endangered). ESA listing triggers Section 7 consultation requirements for all federal actions that may affect listed species, Section 9 take prohibitions, and critical habitat designation.

## Part II: Pinnipedia

The Pacific Northwest coastline and inland waters host six species of pinnipeds -- four year-round residents and two seasonal visitors. Unlike the cetaceans of Part I, pinnipeds live amphibious lives, hauling out on rocky shores, jetties, docks, and sand spits to rest, breed, thermoregulate, and molt. This dual existence places them at the interface of marine and terrestrial ecosystems, making them visible to shore-based observers and frequently contentious neighbors to commercial and sport fisheries. All PNW pinnipeds are protected under the Marine Mammal Protection Act and managed by NOAA Fisheries, with population assessments published in annual Stock Assessment Reports (G15).

Pinnipeds divide into two families in the PNW: the Phocidae (true seals, "earless seals" -- harbor seal and northern elephant seal), which lack external ear pinnae and locomote on land by undulating their bodies; and the Otariidae (eared seals -- sea lions and fur seals), which retain small external ears and can rotate their hind flippers forward for more agile terrestrial movement. This anatomical distinction maps to ecological divergence: phocids tend toward solitary foraging and dispersed haul-outs, while otariids are more gregarious, forming dense breeding rookeries and conspicuous resting aggregations.

The salmon thread runs directly through this group. Harbor seals and both sea lion species forage on returning adult salmon at river mouths, dam tailraces, and estuarine channels -- a behavior that places them in direct competition with human fisheries and creates one of the most politically charged wildlife management conflicts in the PNW. The MMPA framework, which prohibits intentional harassment or killing of marine mammals, intersects with ESA-listed salmon recovery in ways that produce genuine ecological and policy tension. This document records that tension factually.

**Primary sources for Part II:** G01, G15, G24, P06, P19, P32, O05, O10

**Interaction:** Harbor seals are the most commonly encountered marine mammal in the PNW. **Salmon Thread:** Yes -- Harbor seals prey on juvenile and adult salmon at river mouths, estuaries, and near hatchery outfalls. The population's recovery under MMPA protection (from ~8,000 in the 1970s to ~40,000 today across both stocks) has increased predation pressure on ESA-listed salmonid populations, creating genuine management tension between MMPA and ESA mandates.

### Northern Elephant Seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*)

**Taxonomy:** Order Carnivora | Suborder Pinnipedia | Family Phocidae | *Mirounga angustirostris*
**Stock:** California Breeding Stock

*Conservation success story: Northern elephant seals were hunted to near-extinction in the 19th century for their blubber oil. All ~239,000 living northern elephant seals descend from that remnant population -- one of the most dramatic population recovery stories in marine mammal biology. The extreme genetic bottleneck is reflected in remarkably low genetic diversity, a fact that makes the population potentially vulnerable to novel disease or environmental change despite its large current size.*

## Part III: Carnivora -- Mustelidae (Marine)

The mustelids represent one of the most diverse carnivore families, and two species straddle the marine-terrestrial boundary in the PNW: the sea otter and the North American river otter. Of these, only the sea otter is a true marine mammal -- the only marine mammal in the PNW managed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service rather than NOAA Fisheries. This jurisdictional distinction reflects the sea otter's taxonomic position within the terrestrial carnivore order Carnivora rather than the marine-adapted orders Cetacea and Pinnipedia, and it has practical consequences for management authority, stock assessment methodology, and recovery planning.

---

### Sea Otter (*Enhydra lutris kenyoni*)

*The smallest marine mammal in the PNW, the keystone architect of kelp forest ecosystems, and the only marine mammal managed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. Its story on the Olympic coast is one of extirpation, translocation, fragile recovery, and the profound ecological consequences of removing -- and then restoring -- a single species to a nearshore community.*

**Interaction:** [REL-KEYSTONE / REL-TROPHIC-CASCADE]

The sea otter-sea urchin-kelp trophic cascade is one of the most thoroughly documented keystone interactions in ecology and one of the foundational examples of the trophic cascade concept in marine systems.

**The Cascade:**
Where sea otters are present, they suppress sea urchin populations through predation. Sea urchins are voracious grazers of kelp holdfasts -- the root-like structures that anchor kelp to rocky substrate. When urchin populations are controlled by otter predation, kelp forests flourish, creating dense three-dimensional canopy habitat that supports hundreds of associated species. The chain:

*Sea otter present* → sea urchin populations suppressed → kelp forests intact → habitat for rockfish, lingcod, invertebrates, seabirds, gray whales (foraging in kelp beds)

*Sea otter absent* → sea urchin populations explode → kelp forests grazed to "urchin barrens" → loss of canopy habitat → loss of associated fish and invertebrate communities → simplified rocky reef ecosystem

On the Olympic coast, the reintroduction of sea otters in 1969-1970 set this cascade in motion. Areas where sea otters reestablished showed measurable kelp forest recovery within two decades. Research from parallel reintroduction sites in British Columbia and Alaska documents that kelp canopy cover can increase 4-10x in areas where sea otters recolonize, with corresponding increases in kelp-associated fish biomass of 2-5x. The sea otter's keystone role extends beyond kelp: kelp forests buffer coastal erosion, sequester carbon, dampen wave energy, and provide nursery habitat for commercially important species including rockfish and Dungeness crab.

The ecological irony is pointed: commercial shellfish harvesters sometimes oppose sea otter range expansion because otters compete for the same invertebrate prey (crab, clams, urchins). But the kelp forests that otters maintain are the nursery habitat that produces the next generation of those same shellfish species. The short-term loss of harvestable shellfish in otter-occupied areas may be offset by long-term increases in ecosystem productivity -- but the timescales of ecological recovery and commercial harvest are fundamentally different, and the tension is real.

**Olympic Coast Reintroduction History:**

The sea otter was extirpated from Washington by the maritime fur trade. The last confirmed record of a wild sea otter in Washington was in 1910. For nearly six decades, the Washington coast had no sea otters at all.

In 1969-1970, the U.S. Atomic Energy Commission was planning nuclear weapons tests on Amchitka Island in the Aleutian chain of Alaska. To mitigate the anticipated impact on the Amchitka sea otter population, the AEC funded a translocation program. Between 1969 and 1970, **59 sea otters** were captured on Amchitka and released at Point Grenville and La Push on the Olympic coast of Washington.

The founding population faced enormous challenges: unfamiliar territory, no established kelp bed territories, high initial mortality, and a genetic bottleneck that persists to this day. By the early 1970s, the surviving population was estimated at fewer than 30 individuals.

From that precarious beginning, the population grew slowly but steadily:

The occupied range has expanded from the original release sites to cover approximately 100 km of coastline, but remains confined to the outer Olympic Peninsula coast. There is no evidence of southward expansion into the Oregon coast, nor of eastward movement into the Strait of Juan de Fuca.

**Range Expansion vs. Historical Range:**
Pre-fur-trade, sea otters occupied the entire PNW coastline from Alaska to Baja California, including the Oregon coast, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and likely portions of the outer Salish Sea. The current Washington population occupies less than 5% of the species' estimated historical range in the PNW. Natural range expansion is occurring but is slow -- sea otters are not strong long-distance swimmers and tend to expand incrementally along contiguous coastline rather than colonizing distant sites. USFWS has studied but not implemented proposals for additional translocation to the Oregon coast or the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Any such translocation would require extensive environmental review and engagement with affected Tribal nations and commercial fisheries.

**Cultural Note:**
The sea otter holds deep cultural significance for Coast Salish and Makah peoples of the Olympic Peninsula and Strait of Juan de Fuca. The Makah Nation (O20) maintained a traditional sea otter hunting practice documented in archaeological materials from the Ozette village site, which preserved remarkably intact artifacts in a mudslide that sealed portions of the village approximately 500 years ago. Sea otter pelts were items of extraordinary value in pre-contact Coast Salish trade networks, used in high-status garments and as prestige trade items. The Quinault Indian Nation's coast encompasses the core of the current sea otter range, and Quinault tribal members participate in sea otter population surveys alongside USFWS biologists. The species' return to the Olympic coast represents not only an ecological restoration but a cultural one -- the restoration of a relative to the nearshore community that Coast Salish peoples knew for millennia.

**Salmon Thread:** No -- Sea otters are benthic invertebrate specialists and do not prey on salmon. However, their kelp forest maintenance indirectly benefits salmon ecosystems: kelp forests provide nursery habitat for forage fish species that juvenile salmon depend on, and the coastal ecosystem productivity that sea otters support contributes to the nearshore food web that sustains salmon during their marine phase.

**Tool Use:** Sea otters are one of the few non-primate mammals that routinely use tools. They place a flat rock on their chest while floating on their backs and strike hard-shelled prey (sea urchins, clams, mussels, crabs) against it to crack them open. Individual otters maintain preferred "anvil" rocks, sometimes carrying the same stone across multiple foraging bouts. This tool use is learned behavior, transmitted from mother to pup during the extended period of maternal care (~6 months of dependent pup-rearing, with ongoing association up to 12 months).

**Sources:** G01, G16, P19, P21, P22, O05, O08, O20

### North American River Otter (*Lontra canadensis*)

*Semi-marine mustelid that bridges freshwater and saltwater ecosystems. While primarily a freshwater species, the river otter's regular use of marine and estuarine habitats in the PNW earns it a cross-reference here.*

**Taxonomy:** Order Carnivora | Suborder Caniformia | Family Mustelidae | *Lontra canadensis*
**Stock:** Not MMPA-managed (freshwater species; state jurisdiction under WDFW/ODFW)
**Size:** 1.0-1.3 m total length (tail 30-50 cm), 5-14 kg mass (males ~20% larger than females)
**Features:** Sleek torpedo-shaped body; dense short waterproof underfur; long muscular tail (thick at base, tapering); webbed feet; small rounded ears; prominent whiskers (vibrissae) for detecting prey in turbid water
**Marine Zones:** ELEV-RIPARIAN / ELEV-INTERTIDAL / ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (secondary marine use)
**Depth:** Surface to ~15 m (freshwater and nearshore marine foraging; not a deep diver)
**PNW Distribution:** Throughout Washington and Oregon in virtually all watersheds with permanent water -- rivers, lakes, reservoirs, marshes, estuaries, and nearshore marine waters. In the PNW, river otters regularly use saltwater habitats including Puget Sound shorelines, the San Juan Islands, estuaries, and outer coast tide pools. Coastal populations may spend significant time foraging in marine environments, blurring the freshwater/saltwater distinction. The Puget Sound population is notably marine-adapted.
**Key Sites:** Puget Sound shorelines, San Juan Islands, Columbia River estuary, Willapa Bay, Grays Harbor, coastal rivers and estuaries throughout WA/OR
**Diet:** Piscivore/generalist | Trophic: Secondary-tertiary consumer
**Key Prey:** Crayfish (*Pacifastacus leniusculus*), sculpin (Cottidae), suckers (Catostomidae), salmonid smolts (*Oncorhynchus* spp.), Dungeness crab (*Metacarcinus magister* -- in marine habitats), frogs, freshwater mussels
**Predators:** Coyote (*Canis latrans*), bobcat (*Lynx rufus*), bald eagle (AVI: *Haliaeetus leucocephalus* -- takes young), cougar (*Puma concolor* -- rare)
**Reproduction:** Gestation ~10-12 months (includes 8-10 months delayed implantation) | Birth February-April | Litter size 1-5 (typically 2-3) | Maturity 2-3 years | Lifespan 8-13 years in wild
**Conservation Status:**
| ESA | State Status | IUCN | NatureServe | Pop. Trend |
|-----|-------------|------|-------------|-----------|
| Not listed | Not listed (WA/OR -- furbearer, managed) | LC | G5 S5 | Stable to increasing |

> **Note:** The North American river otter is NOT an MMPA-managed species and is not assessed through NOAA or USFWS marine mammal stock assessment processes. It is managed as a furbearer by state wildlife agencies (WDFW, ODFW). It is included in this marine mammal document because PNW coastal populations make extensive use of marine habitats, and its ecological role in estuarine and nearshore environments overlaps with the marine mammal community.

**Threats:** Water quality degradation (contaminants, particularly mercury and PCBs in Puget Sound) [MODERATE]; habitat loss from riparian development [MODERATE]; vehicle strikes at road-stream crossings [LOW]; trapping (legal, regulated harvest in WA/OR) [LOW -- sustainable under current management]
**Interaction:** In marine habitats of the Puget Sound and San Juan Islands, river otters forage on the same nearshore prey as harbor seals and sea otters, creating a three-species mustelid-phocid overlap zone. River otters in Puget Sound regularly consume Dungeness crab, gunnel, sculpin, and other nearshore marine species, sometimes foraging in kelp beds alongside diving birds. Their latrines -- communal defecation sites on shoreline rocks and logs -- concentrate marine-derived nutrients on land, linking marine food webs to terrestrial riparian vegetation.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes -- River otters prey on juvenile salmonids in streams and estuaries, and on salmon carcasses post-spawning. Their scat deposits along stream banks return marine-derived nutrients (from salmon carcasses) to riparian soils, contributing to the salmon-forest nutrient cycle.

## Part IV: Synthesis -- Marine Mammal Ecology of the PNW

### 4.1 Species Richness by Marine Zone

The PNW marine mammal community distributes across three marine elevation bands (defined in ECO shared-attributes and MAM elevation-matrix) with distinct species assemblages:

| Marine Zone | Elevation | Depth Range | Cetaceans | Pinnipeds | Mustelids | Total Species | Characteristic Taxa |
|-------------|-----------|-------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| ELEV-INTERTIDAL | -15 to 0 ft | Surface/shore | 0 | 3 (haul-out) | 2 | 5 | Harbor seal, sea lions (hauling out), sea otter, river otter |
| ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE | -200 to -15 ft | 5-60 m | 10 | 5 | 1 | 16 | Harbor porpoise, minke whale, gray whale, humpback, harbor seal, California sea lion, sea otter |
| ELEV-DEEP-MARINE | -930 to -200 ft | 60-280+ m | 12 | 3 | 0 | 15 | SRKW, Bigg's orca, sperm whale, beaked whales, elephant seal, blue whale, fin whale |
| **ELEV-COASTAL-DUNE** | 0-100 ft | Shore | 0 | 4 (haul-out) | 1 | 5 | Harbor seal, sea lions, elephant seal (rookeries/haul-outs), river otter |

**Patterns:**
- **Shallow marine** is the richest zone for marine mammals, hosting the full breadth of taxa from baleen whales to mustelids. This reflects the convergence of productive coastal upwelling, kelp forest habitat, and estuarine nutrient inputs.
- **Deep marine** hosts the specialized deep-divers: beaked whales, sperm whales, elephant seals, and orca ecotypes. These species exploit mesopelagic and bathypelagic prey inaccessible to shallow-diving taxa.
- **Intertidal and coastal-dune** zones are used almost exclusively by pinnipeds (hauling out) and mustelids. Cetaceans do not use these zones except in stranding events.
- The **total PNW marine mammal community** comprises approximately 30-35 species (depending on how vagrants and rare visitors are counted): ~18-22 cetaceans, ~6 pinnipeds, and 2 mustelids with marine habitat use.

### 4.2 The Salish Sea as Critical Habitat

The Salish Sea -- the interconnected waterway system comprising Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the Strait of Georgia, and associated channels and passages -- functions as the single most important marine mammal habitat complex in the PNW. Its significance derives from:

**Productivity:** The Salish Sea's combination of tidal mixing, river nutrient inputs (Fraser, Skagit, Nooksack, Snohomish, Duwamish, Nisqually, Puyallup), and upwelling creates a nutrient-rich environment that supports high primary productivity and dense forage fish populations.

**Topographic complexity:** The Salish Sea's complex bathymetry -- deep basins (>200 m in some channels), shallow sills, island archipelagos, and narrow passages -- creates diverse habitat niches within a relatively compact geographic area. Tidal rips and current convergence zones concentrate prey and create reliable foraging sites.

**Species using Salish Sea as core habitat:**
- **Southern Resident killer whales:** Designated critical habitat (50 CFR 226.206). Summer core area in San Juan Islands and southern Gulf Islands.
- **Bigg's killer whales:** Year-round presence, foraging on harbor seals and sea lions throughout the inland waters.
- **Harbor porpoise:** Year-round resident in inland waters (P20 -- Byrd et al. 2022 abundance estimate).
- **Dall's porpoise:** Year-round in deeper channels and passages.
- **Harbor seal (inland stock):** ~14,600 individuals in a distinct NOAA management stock.
- **California sea lion:** Seasonally abundant on docks, jetties, and buoys.
- **Steller sea lion:** Present at scattered haul-out sites.
- **Humpback whale:** Increasingly common in spring-fall, particularly in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and San Juan Channel.
- **Gray whale:** "Sounders" -- a subset of ~12 individual gray whales that forage in Puget Sound rather than migrating to Arctic feeding grounds.
- **Minke whale:** Year-round resident, particularly in the San Juan Islands.
- **River otter:** Abundant along all Salish Sea shorelines, functioning as semi-marine.

**Conservation significance:** The Salish Sea's concentration of marine mammal species, including ESA-listed SRKW, creates management challenges that do not exist in the open ocean. Vessel traffic (commercial shipping, ferries, whale-watching boats, recreational vessels), underwater noise, chemical contaminants from urban runoff (Seattle, Tacoma, Vancouver, Victoria), and competition for salmon between marine mammals, fisheries, and orcas all converge in this enclosed waterway.

### 4.3 The Salmon Thread -- Marine Mammal Connections

The salmon thread -- the ecological linkage connecting Pacific salmon to the broader PNW ecosystem -- runs through the marine mammal community at multiple trophic levels:

| Species | Salmon Connection | Thread Type | Severity of Interaction |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Southern Resident killer whale** | Obligate Chinook specialist (>80% of diet) | Direct predation | CRITICAL -- population viability depends on Chinook abundance |
| **Harbor seal** | Preys on juvenile and adult salmon at estuaries and river mouths | Direct predation | MODERATE -- recovered population increases predation pressure on ESA-listed runs |
| **Steller sea lion** | Preys on adult salmon at dam tailraces (Bonneville Dam) | Direct predation | HIGH -- concentrated predation at migration bottlenecks |
| **California sea lion** | Preys on adult salmon at dam tailraces and river mouths | Direct predation | HIGH -- socially transmitted learned foraging behavior at dams |
| **Sea otter** | No direct salmon predation; kelp forest maintenance supports forage fish nursery habitat | Indirect facilitation | LOW -- positive but diffuse |
| **River otter** | Preys on juvenile salmonids; returns marine-derived nutrients to streams via scat | Direct predation + nutrient cycling | LOW -- natural predation within ecosystem capacity |
| **Humpback whale** | Minimal salmon interaction (krill and herring specialist) | None significant | NEGLIGIBLE |
| **Gray whale** | None (benthic amphipod specialist) | None | NONE |
| **Bigg's killer whale** | Preys on pinnipeds that prey on salmon (indirect) | Indirect regulation | Complex -- orca predation on sea lions may reduce pinniped predation on salmon |

**The SRKW-Salmon Thread:** The Southern Resident killer whale's near-complete dependence on Chinook salmon makes it the apex expression of the salmon thread in the marine ecosystem. SRKW population dynamics track Chinook abundance with a lag of 1-2 years. Years with poor Chinook returns correlate with reduced SRKW survival, lower calving rates, and increased calf mortality. The population's decline from 98 individuals in 1995 to 73 in 2024 tracks the decline in Chinook salmon availability across their foraging range (Fraser River, Puget Sound, Columbia River, and coastal rivers). Recovery of SRKW is, in functional terms, recovery of Chinook salmon -- one cannot succeed without the other.

**The Pinniped-Salmon Conflict:** The recovery of harbor seals (~40,000 in WA/OR, up from ~8,000 in the 1970s), California sea lions (~260,000 U.S. stock), and Steller sea lions (~42,000 Eastern DPS) under MMPA protection has increased pinniped predation on salmon runs. This creates a genuine management dilemma: both marine mammals and salmon are protected under federal law (MMPA and ESA respectively), but their interests conflict at specific locations (Bonneville Dam, Ballard Locks, river mouths during spawning runs). The MMPA Section 120 lethal removal program represents the legal system's attempt to resolve this conflict on a case-by-case basis, authorizing removal of individually identified repeat offenders while maintaining population-level protection for pinniped stocks. The ecological question -- whether pinniped predation is additive mortality (killing salmon that would otherwise survive) or compensatory mortality (removing fish that would die from other causes) -- remains actively debated.

### 4.4 MMPA Management Framework

The Marine Mammal Protection Act of 1972 (amended 1994) provides the primary legal framework for managing all PNW marine mammals. Key elements:

**Jurisdiction Split:**
| Agency | Taxa Managed | Key Species in PNW |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| NOAA Fisheries | All cetaceans (whales, dolphins, porpoises) and all pinnipeds (seals, sea lions) | SRKW, humpback, gray whale, harbor seal, Steller sea lion, California sea lion |
| **U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service** | Sea otters, walruses, polar bears, manatees | **Sea otter (*Enhydra lutris kenyoni*)** -- the ONLY USFWS-managed marine mammal in the PNW |

**Stock Assessment Reports (SARs):**
NOAA Fisheries publishes annual Stock Assessment Reports for every marine mammal stock in U.S. waters, as required by the 1994 MMPA amendments. SARs provide:
- **Nmin:** Minimum population estimate (statistically defensible lower bound)
- **PBR (Potential Biological Removal):** Maximum number of animals that can be removed from a stock while allowing it to reach or maintain its optimum sustainable population. Calculated as: PBR = Nmin x (0.5 x Rmax) x Fr, where Rmax is maximum rate of population increase and Fr is a recovery factor (0.1 for endangered/depleted stocks, 0.5 for stocks of unknown status, 1.0 for stocks at optimum sustainable population)
- **Strategic stock designation:** Stocks where human-caused mortality exceeds PBR, or ESA-listed stocks, or stocks that are declining and likely to be listed
- **Depleted designation:** Stocks below their optimum sustainable population level

**PNW Stocks by MMPA Status:**

| Status | Stocks | Key Examples |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Strategic + Depleted | 3 | SRKW, Northern fur seal (Eastern Pacific), Guadalupe fur seal |
| Non-strategic | 5 | Harbor seal (2 stocks), California sea lion, Steller sea lion (Eastern DPS), harbor porpoise |
| USFWS-managed | 1 | Sea otter (Washington) -- assessed under USFWS protocols, not NOAA SARs |

### 4.5 Climate Change Impacts on PNW Marine Mammals

Climate change affects PNW marine mammals through multiple, interacting pathways:

**Prey Shifts:**
- **Ocean warming** is shifting the distribution of key prey species northward. Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance, and forage fish communities are tracking cooler water, potentially moving foraging opportunities away from established marine mammal habitat. For SRKW, the decline of Fraser River and Columbia River Chinook runs -- driven partly by warming freshwater habitat and ocean conditions -- directly threatens population viability.
- **Marine heat waves** (e.g., the 2014-2016 "Blob" -- a persistent mass of anomalously warm water in the North Pacific) caused dramatic shifts in marine food webs, displacing prey species, triggering harmful algal blooms, and causing mass mortality events in California sea lions and Guadalupe fur seals through domoic acid poisoning and prey collapse.
- **Krill and copepod community shifts** affect baleen whale foraging success. Humpback and blue whales depend on dense aggregations of euphausiids (*Euphausia pacifica*, *Thysanoessa spinifera*) and copepods whose abundance and distribution are sensitive to ocean temperature and upwelling timing.

**Ocean Acidification:**
- Reduced pH levels in PNW waters (the California Current system is particularly vulnerable to acidification due to natural upwelling of CO2-rich deep water, amplified by anthropogenic CO2) threaten the calcifying organisms at the base of marine food webs. Pteropods, a key prey item for some forage fish species, show shell dissolution under acidified conditions. The cascade: acidification → pteropod decline → forage fish decline → marine mammal foraging stress.
- For sea otters, acidification threatens the shellfish prey base: sea urchins, clams, and crabs depend on calcium carbonate for shell/test formation. Long-term acidification could reduce the biomass and quality of sea otter prey.

**Temperature Effects:**
- **Pinniped thermoregulation:** Warming ambient temperatures may increase heat stress during haul-out periods, particularly for densely aggregated species like California sea lions and northern elephant seals. The energetic cost of thermoregulation rises as haul-out temperatures increase.
- **Kelp forest decline:** Rising ocean temperatures, combined with marine heat waves, are causing kelp canopy loss along the Pacific coast. In northern California, the combination of sea star wasting disease (which removed the predatory sunflower star *Pycnopodia helianthoides*), purple urchin population explosion, and warming temperatures has converted hundreds of kilometers of kelp forest to urchin barrens. If this pattern extends northward into Washington, it would undermine the sea otter-kelp trophic cascade by removing the kelp substrate that defines the sea otter's habitat.

**Distribution Shifts:**
- Guadalupe fur seal range expansion into PNW waters may be an early climate signal -- warmer waters allowing a subtropical species to exploit northward habitat.
- Gray whale "Sounders" (the small group of gray whales that forages in Puget Sound year-round rather than migrating to Arctic feeding grounds) may represent an adaptive response to changing conditions in the Arctic, or a behavioral tradition that predates modern monitoring.
- Harbor porpoise distribution within the Salish Sea appears to be shifting, with increased sightings in some areas and decreased presence in others, potentially tracking changes in forage fish distribution.

### 4.6 Conservation Success Stories

The PNW marine mammal community includes some of the most dramatic conservation recovery stories in wildlife biology:

**Gray Whale (*Eschrichtius robustus*):**
Hunted to near-extinction by commercial whaling in the 19th and early 20th centuries, the Eastern North Pacific gray whale population recovered from an estimated 1,000-2,000 individuals to approximately 27,000 by 2016 -- close to estimated pre-whaling abundance. Delisted from the ESA in 1994, the gray whale recovery is one of the earliest and most complete large whale recovery stories. The annual gray whale migration past the PNW coast (December-May) is one of the most visible wildlife spectacles in the region.

**Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*):**
Reduced to fewer than 1,400 individuals in the North Pacific by the end of commercial whaling, humpback whale populations have recovered substantially. The species was downlisted from Endangered to Threatened (for the Mexico DPS, the primary PNW stock) in 2016. Humpback sightings in PNW waters -- including inside the Salish Sea -- have increased dramatically since the early 2000s, reflecting population growth and possibly range recovery into historically occupied habitat.

**Sea Otter (*Enhydra lutris kenyoni*):**
From 59 translocated animals in 1969-1970 to approximately 2,787 in 2023, the Washington sea otter population has increased 47-fold from its founding stock. The recovery demonstrates that even extreme population bottlenecks (59 founders, all from a single source population) need not prevent demographic recovery, though the genetic consequences of the bottleneck persist and may limit long-term adaptive capacity.

**Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina*):**
Harbor seal populations in Washington and Oregon declined to approximately 8,000 in the early 1970s due to bounty programs (which offered payments for dead seals as "fish competitors"), incidental take, and habitat disturbance. Following MMPA protection in 1972, the population recovered to approximately 40,000 by the 2000s and has since stabilized near apparent carrying capacity. This recovery, while unambiguously positive from a marine mammal conservation perspective, has intensified the pinniped-salmon conflict (see Section 4.3).

**Northern Elephant Seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*):**
From fewer than 20 individuals in 1892 to approximately 239,000 today -- a 12,000-fold population increase from a bottleneck that was functionally a near-extinction event. While breeding does not occur in the PNW, the species' recovery is evident in the increasing frequency and regularity of non-breeding haul-outs on Oregon and Washington beaches.

**Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus* -- Eastern DPS):**
The Eastern DPS was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 1990 and delisted in 2013 following sustained population growth. The recovery from ~18,000 to ~42,000 individuals demonstrates that MMPA/ESA protections can work for large, long-lived, slow-reproducing marine mammals when the primary threats (shooting, disturbance, reduced prey) are addressed.
