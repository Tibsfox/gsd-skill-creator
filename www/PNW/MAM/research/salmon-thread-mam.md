## Canonical Statement

> The River's Witness is the cross-series ecological thread connecting all PNW Living Systems Taxonomy research through the life cycle of Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.). Each taxonomy module -- mammalian, avian, ecological -- maps its species to the salmon life cycle, tracing direct predation, scavenging, nutrient beneficiary, and habitat-dependent connections. Together these documents form a single integrated picture of the most important biological subsidy in the temperate world: the annual return of marine-derived nutrients from the North Pacific Ocean to the forests, rivers, and soils of the Pacific Northwest, mediated by salmon and the organisms that consume them.

The mammalian thread is the heaviest strand. Bears are the primary large-scale terrestrial vectors of salmon carcasses. Killer whales are the apex marine predators whose population viability depends on Chinook abundance. Pinnipeds create management conflict at dam tailraces. Beavers build the habitat complexity that juvenile salmonids require. Small mammals absorb the nutrient signal through enriched riparian soils. From the deep ocean to the alpine treeline, Pacific salmon connect the mammalian community of the PNW through a web of predation, scavenging, nutrient transfer, and habitat interdependence that has no parallel in any other temperate ecosystem.

## Mammalian Species Connected to Salmon

## Section 1: Terrestrial Direct Predators

These species actively hunt, catch, or consume salmon or salmon products (eggs, carcasses) as a significant component of their diet or ecological role.

---

### American Black Bear (*Ursus americanus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator / Nutrient vector
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult spawning, carcass
**Mechanism:** Black bears are the most abundant large-scale terrestrial consumer of Pacific salmon in the PNW. They wade into spawning streams to catch live fish, targeting brain and roe first and often abandoning partially consumed carcasses on the forest floor 50-150 m from the stream (documented up to 500 m in high-density populations). A single bear may transport 600-700 carcasses per season in productive systems. This selective feeding behavior deposits an estimated 40-70% of carcass nitrogen in the riparian forest, creating the primary terrestrial pathway for marine-derived nutrient transfer. Black bears feed on all five Pacific salmon species, with particular dependence on pink (*O. gorbuscha*), chum (*O. keta*), sockeye (*O. nerka*), and Chinook (*O. tshawytscha*) depending on regional run composition.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-MONTANE (y=-41 to y=84); all PNW salmon-bearing watersheds from the Olympic Peninsula to the eastern Cascades
**Seasonal Timing:** Late summer through fall (July-November), aligned with spawning runs; peak activity during hyperphagia (pre-hibernation fat accumulation, September-November)
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Ursidae, Black Bear profile

---

### Grizzly Bear (*Ursus arctos*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator / Primary nutrient vector
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult spawning, carcass
**Mechanism:** Grizzly bears are the archetypal salmon predator and the most ecologically significant terrestrial vector of marine-derived nutrients in systems where they occur. Grizzlies are more efficient salmon catchers than black bears, with larger body size enabling capture of bigger fish and greater carcass transport distances. In British Columbia's Great Bear Rainforest and Alaska's salmon streams, grizzlies deposit an estimated 70% of consumed salmon nitrogen within 50 m of streams, with documented transport distances exceeding 500 m. Their selective consumption (brain and roe preferred) leaves high-nitrogen carcass tissue for decomposers, maximizing nutrient transfer efficiency. Historical PNW grizzly populations across the Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Range once provided this vectoring service throughout the region; current PNW populations are restricted to the North Cascades Ecosystem (fewer than 10 individuals estimated, status critically endangered) and northern British Columbia.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-SUBALPINE (y=-41 to y=159); historically throughout PNW, now restricted to North Cascades and northern BC
**Seasonal Timing:** Late summer through fall (July-November); hyperphagia period; grizzlies in salmon-rich systems may gain 2-3 lbs per day during peak spawning runs
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Ursidae, Grizzly Bear profile

---

### Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator / Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult spawning, carcass
**Mechanism:** Wolves in coastal British Columbia and southeastern Alaska are documented salmon fishers, wading into streams to catch spawning fish -- a behavior first documented by Darimont et al. (2003) on the BC central coast. Salmon can constitute up to 25% of coastal wolf diet during spawning season, as measured by stable isotope analysis of wolf hair. PNW wolves in Washington and Oregon are primarily inland populations (recolonizing from Idaho/Montana source populations) and have less access to salmon-bearing streams, but wolves in riparian habitats along major river systems (Skagit, Nooksack, Olympic rivers) have the potential to re-establish salmon-foraging behavior as populations expand. The wolf-elk-riparian trophic cascade also connects wolves indirectly to salmon: wolf predation reduces elk browsing in riparian zones, allowing recovery of streamside vegetation (willow, cottonwood, alder) that provides shade, bank stability, and insect drop for salmon-bearing streams.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-SUBALPINE (y=-41 to y=159); coastal watersheds (BC, Olympic Peninsula potential), inland river corridors
**Seasonal Timing:** Fall spawning runs (September-November); trophic cascade effects year-round
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Canidae, Gray Wolf profile

---

### North American River Otter (*Lontra canadensis*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator / Nutrient recycler
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, fry, smolt, adult spawning, carcass
**Mechanism:** River otters are year-round salmon consumers, feeding on juvenile salmonids (fry and smolts), salmon eggs during spawning, adult spawning fish, and post-spawning carcasses. They interact with more salmon life stages than any other mammalian predator. Otters consume sculpin, crayfish, and juvenile salmon in streams, and Dungeness crab and marine fish in coastal habitats. Their communal latrine sites along salmon-bearing streams concentrate marine-derived nutrients from salmon carcasses, creating localized nutrient hotspots in riparian soils -- a secondary nutrient vectoring pathway analogous to but smaller-scale than bear transport. Otter presence indicates high-quality aquatic habitat capable of supporting salmonid populations.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (y=-41 to y=34); all PNW salmon-bearing streams, estuaries, and coastal habitats
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak salmon consumption during spawning runs (fall) and smolt outmigration (spring)
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Mustelidae, River Otter profile; `marine.md` -- River Otter profile

---

### American Mink (*Neogale vison*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator / Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, fry, smolt, carcass
**Mechanism:** Mink are direct predators of juvenile salmonids, particularly in small streams where young coho (*O. kisutch*) and steelhead (*O. mykiss*) rear in pools and side channels. Their semi-aquatic foraging behavior gives them access to stream habitats that larger predators cannot efficiently exploit. Mink also consume salmon eggs exposed in spawning redds and scavenge post-spawning carcasses, carrying small salmon and carcass fragments into riparian corridors. Mink predation on juvenile salmon is part of the natural predator community that shaped salmonid life-history strategies -- the selection pressure for cryptic coloration, nocturnal activity, and predator-avoidance behavior in juvenile salmon.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-41 to y=34); throughout PNW along salmon-bearing streams and wetlands
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; juvenile salmonid predation peaks spring-summer; carcass scavenging peaks fall
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Mustelidae, American Mink profile

---

### American Marten (*Martes americana*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger / Opportunistic predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs
**Mechanism:** Martens in coastal and riparian-adjacent forests opportunistically consume salmon carcasses and eggs scattered by bears and other primary predators. Their primary connection to salmon is indirect: as old-growth forest obligates, marten presence signifies the structurally complex forests that provide large wood recruitment and shade to salmon-bearing streams. Martens are primarily arboreal predators of squirrels, voles, and birds, but in coastal populations (particularly on Vancouver Island and the Olympic Peninsula), salmon carcasses represent a significant seasonal protein source during fall and early winter when berry crops have ended and small mammal populations are declining.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-LOWLAND through ELEV-SUBALPINE (y=-6 to y=159); coastal and riparian-adjacent old-growth forests
**Seasonal Timing:** Fall and early winter (October-December), following bear deposition of carcasses
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Mustelidae, American Marten profile

---

### Raccoon (*Procyon lotor*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger / Opportunistic predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, carcass
**Mechanism:** Raccoons opportunistically consume salmon eggs exposed in spawning redds and scavenge post-spawning carcasses near streams. Their dexterous forepaws allow efficient egg extraction from gravel substrates. While raccoons are common along PNW salmon streams and frequently observed at spawning sites, they are not considered ecologically significant in salmon nutrient transport -- their consumption is largely in-stream or immediately adjacent, and they do not transport carcasses meaningful distances into upland forest. Raccoons function as secondary consumers within the stream-edge zone rather than as nutrient vectors.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-41 to y=34); lowland and coastal salmon streams throughout PNW
**Seasonal Timing:** Fall spawning season (September-December)
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Family Procyonidae, Raccoon profile

## Section 2: Marine Predators

These species prey on salmon in marine or estuarine environments, connecting the ocean phase of the salmon life cycle to the mammalian food web.

---

### Southern Resident Killer Whale (*Orcinus orca* -- Southern Resident DPS)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator (obligate specialist)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult (marine phase), primarily Chinook
**Mechanism:** Southern Resident killer whales (SRKW) are the most salmon-dependent mammal in the PNW marine ecosystem. Chinook salmon constitutes greater than 80% of SRKW diet by biomass. The three SRKW pods (J, K, L) concentrate in the Salish Sea from May through October, foraging on returning Chinook runs from the Fraser River, Puget Sound tributaries, and Columbia River system. In winter, SRKW range along the outer coast of Washington, Oregon, and northern California, tracking Chinook runs to their natal rivers. The SRKW population has declined from approximately 98 individuals in 1995 to 73 in 2024, tracking the decline in Chinook abundance. This is the most urgent mammal-salmon conservation crisis in the PNW -- SRKW population viability depends directly on Chinook recovery.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE through ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (y=-64 to y=-22); Salish Sea, outer WA/OR coast
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; Salish Sea concentration May-October tracking Fraser River Chinook; outer coast November-April
**Cross-ref:** `marine.md` -- Killer Whale profile, SRKW ecotype table

---

### Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Smolt, adult (estuarine and nearshore)
**Mechanism:** Harbor seals prey on juvenile salmon (smolts) in estuaries during spring outmigration and on adult salmon returning through river mouths and nearshore waters. Their recovered population (approximately 60,000-70,000 in Washington and Oregon combined under MMPA protection) has increased predation pressure on ESA-listed salmon runs, creating a conservation conflict between marine mammal protection and salmon recovery. Harbor seals are ambush predators at estuary mouths, river confluences, and tidal rips where salmon concentrate during tidal transitions. Studies in the Salish Sea (Chasco et al. 2017) estimate that pinnipeds (primarily harbor seals) may consume 5-15% of some Chinook populations during the smolt-to-adult transition. In the Columbia River estuary, harbor seal predation on juvenile salmon has been documented using acoustic-tagged fish and seal-mounted cameras.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL through ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (y=-41 to y=-22); all PNW estuaries and nearshore waters
**Seasonal Timing:** Spring smolt outmigration (April-June); fall adult returns (August-November)
**Cross-ref:** `marine.md` -- Harbor Seal profile

---

### Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult (river mouth and dam tailrace)
**Mechanism:** Steller sea lions prey on adult salmon at river mouths and -- critically -- at dam tailraces where migrating fish concentrate in confined areas below fish ladders. Bonneville Dam on the Columbia River has become the focal point of the pinniped-salmon management controversy: Steller sea lions (along with California sea lions) aggregate below the dam in spring, consuming adult spring Chinook, steelhead, and other salmonids as they queue for fish ladder passage. Individual sea lions have been documented consuming 5-20 adult salmonids per day at dam tailraces. Steller sea lions are larger (males up to 1,100 kg) and more aggressive than California sea lions, and their increasing presence at Columbia River dams since the 2000s has intensified the management conflict.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE through ELEV-RIPARIAN (y=-41 to y=-22 primary; riverine incursions upstream to y=0+); Columbia River, Willamette Falls, Bonneville Dam, coastal haul-out sites
**Seasonal Timing:** Spring Chinook and steelhead runs (March-June); fall Chinook runs (August-October)
**Cross-ref:** `marine.md` -- Steller Sea Lion profile

---

### California Sea Lion (*Zalophus californianus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult (river mouth and dam tailrace)
**Mechanism:** California sea lions are the most visible and controversial pinniped salmon predators in the PNW. Males migrate north from California breeding colonies to Oregon and Washington waters from fall through spring, congregating at river mouths and dam tailraces to exploit adult salmon runs. At Bonneville Dam, California sea lions developed socially transmitted foraging behavior targeting spring Chinook at the fish ladder entrance -- learned behavior that spread through the population from a few pioneer individuals in the early 2000s. This concentrated predation at a migration bottleneck on ESA-listed runs triggered the first lethal removal of marine mammals to protect salmon under Section 120 of the MMPA (authorized 2008). Between 2008 and 2020, over 200 California sea lions were lethally removed from the Columbia River system. Willamette Falls has experienced similar concentrated predation on winter steelhead.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE through ELEV-RIPARIAN (y=-41 to y=-22 primary; riverine to y=0+); Columbia River system, Bonneville Dam, Willamette Falls, Astoria
**Seasonal Timing:** Spring Chinook runs (March-June); males present in PNW October-June before returning south to breed
**Cross-ref:** `marine.md` -- California Sea Lion profile

## Section 3: Nutrient Beneficiaries

These species do not prey on salmon directly but benefit from the marine-derived nutrient subsidy that salmon deliver to terrestrial and riparian ecosystems.

---

### Deer Mouse (*Peromyscus maniculatus*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary / Indirect consumer
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (decomposition products)
**Mechanism:** Deer mice in riparian zones near salmon-bearing streams consume invertebrates (blowfly larvae, beetles, mites) that colonize decomposing salmon carcasses, linking the marine-derived nutrient pathway to the terrestrial small-mammal food web. Stable isotope analysis (N-15) of deer mouse tissue in salmon-bearing riparian zones shows elevated marine-derived nitrogen signatures compared to mice from non-salmon streams, confirming trophic incorporation of salmon-origin nutrients. Deer mice also consume and disperse mycorrhizal fungal spores, contributing to the fungal networks that transport salmon-derived nitrogen from soil into tree root systems. Their high reproductive rate and abundance make them an important pathway for distributing salmon nutrients through the lower trophic levels of the forest food web.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-MONTANE (y=-41 to y=84); riparian zones of salmon-bearing streams throughout PNW
**Seasonal Timing:** Fall through spring (October-April); carcass decomposition period and invertebrate colonization of salmon remains
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Deer Mouse profile

---

### Townsend's Vole (*Microtus townsendii*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary / Habitat user
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (nutrient enrichment of riparian soils)
**Mechanism:** Townsend's voles are abundant herbivores in riparian meadows and wetland margins along salmon-bearing streams. Their forage plants (sedges, grasses, herbaceous vegetation) absorb marine-derived nitrogen from decomposing salmon carcasses, meaning voles consuming riparian vegetation are indirect recipients of the salmon nutrient subsidy. Townsend's vole herbivory in riparian meadows also influences vegetation structure along salmon-bearing streams -- their grazing can maintain open sedge meadow habitat used by juvenile salmonids during high-water events when fish move into floodplain habitats. Vole populations in salmon-enriched riparian zones show higher densities and reproductive output compared to non-salmon riparian areas, consistent with nutrient-driven bottom-up effects on primary productivity.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-41 to y=34); lowland riparian meadows along salmon streams, particularly in western WA and OR
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; nutrient signal strongest in vegetation during growing season following fall spawning runs
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Townsend's Vole profile

---

### Pacific Water Shrew (*Sorex bendirii*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary / Indirect consumer
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (aquatic invertebrates enriched by salmon decomposition)
**Mechanism:** Pacific water shrews are semi-aquatic insectivores that forage in and along salmon-bearing streams, consuming aquatic invertebrates (stonefly larvae, caddisfly larvae, chironomid larvae) that feed on decomposing salmon tissue and biofilm enriched with marine-derived nutrients. Stream invertebrate communities in salmon-bearing reaches show N-15 enrichment of +3.0-5.0 per mil relative to non-salmon reference streams (Bilby et al. 1996), and this isotopic signal transfers to the shrews that consume them. Water shrews are among the most direct mammalian recipients of the in-stream salmon nutrient pathway -- they eat the organisms that eat the salmon, within the stream itself.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN (y=-41 to y=0); stream edges and riparian wetlands in western WA, OR, and coastal BC
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; aquatic invertebrate enrichment persists for months after spawning runs
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Shrew profiles

---

### Vagrant Shrew (*Sorex vagrans*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary / Indirect consumer
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (terrestrial invertebrates enriched by salmon decomposition)
**Mechanism:** Vagrant shrews are among the most abundant insectivores in PNW riparian zones, consuming terrestrial invertebrates (earthworms, beetles, spiders, centipedes) that process decomposing salmon tissue on the forest floor. Blowfly larvae (*Calliphoridae*) colonize salmon carcasses within hours of bear deposition, and their pupae and emerging adults are consumed by shrews, transferring marine-derived nutrients into the terrestrial insectivore food web. Shrew tissue from salmon-enriched riparian zones shows measurable N-15 elevation, confirming that even the smallest mammals in the forest incorporate salmon-origin nutrients through invertebrate intermediaries.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-41 to y=34); riparian forests and meadows along salmon-bearing streams
**Seasonal Timing:** Fall through spring (October-May); tracks blowfly colonization and invertebrate decomposition of carcasses
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Shrew profiles

---

### Western Red-backed Vole (*Clethrionomys californicus*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary (indirect, via mycorrhizal network)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (salmon-derived nitrogen in mycorrhizal fungi)
**Mechanism:** The western red-backed vole is the primary mycorrhizal spore disperser in PNW old-growth forests, consuming hypogeous (below-ground) fungi -- truffles and false truffles -- as the dominant component of its diet (up to 90% in some populations). Mycorrhizal fungi in riparian zones absorb marine-derived nitrogen from decomposing salmon carcasses through their hyphal networks and transport it to tree roots. Red-backed voles consuming these fungi therefore incorporate salmon-origin nitrogen at one additional trophic remove. More importantly, by dispersing mycorrhizal spores through their fecal pellets, red-backed voles maintain the fungal networks that are the primary underground transport mechanism for salmon-derived nutrients from soil to trees. The vole-mycorrhiza-tree pathway is a critical link in the salmon-forest nutrient cycle.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-LOWLAND through ELEV-MONTANE (y=-6 to y=84); old-growth and mature forests adjacent to salmon-bearing streams
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; fungi availability peaks fall; nitrogen incorporation continuous through mycorrhizal cycling
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Red-backed Vole profile

---

### Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary (indirect, via mycorrhizal network)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (salmon-derived nitrogen in mycorrhizal fungi)
**Mechanism:** Northern flying squirrels, like red-backed voles, are major consumers and dispersers of mycorrhizal fungi. By eating truffles and dispersing spores, they maintain the ectomycorrhizal networks that transport salmon-derived nitrogen from riparian soils to conifer root systems. Their role is indirect but structurally important: by sustaining mycorrhizal networks, flying squirrels maintain forest canopy integrity that shades salmon-bearing streams and stabilizes stream banks. Old-growth forest loss reduces flying squirrel populations, mycorrhizal networks, forest health, stream shading, and ultimately salmon habitat quality -- a cascade that links this arboreal rodent to the salmon nutrient cycle through multiple intermediate steps.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-LOWLAND through ELEV-MONTANE (y=-6 to y=84); old-growth and mature coniferous forests throughout PNW
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round (nocturnal foraging); truffle availability peaks late summer and fall
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Northern Flying Squirrel profile

## Section 4: Habitat-Dependent Species

These species create or modify habitat that directly affects salmon survival, or depend on habitat features shaped by salmon-bearing ecosystems.

---

### American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*)

**Connection Type:** Habitat engineer (keystone)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, fry, smolt (rearing habitat)
**Mechanism:** Beavers are the most important mammalian habitat engineer for salmon in the PNW. Beaver dam complexes create off-channel rearing habitat for juvenile salmonids, moderate stream temperatures (warming in winter, cooling in summer through groundwater interaction), trap sediment and nutrients, increase hydrologic complexity, and raise water tables in adjacent riparian zones. Beaver dam analogs (BDAs) -- human-built structures mimicking beaver dams -- are now a primary stream restoration tool in PNW salmon recovery programs. The historical removal of an estimated 60-400 million beavers from North America during the fur trade era (1600s-1800s) is considered a significant contributor to salmon habitat degradation. Coast Salish peoples (Tulalip Tribes, O22) recognized the relationship between beaver dams and fish abundance long before Euro-American arrival. Current beaver restoration programs in Washington (Methow Valley, Yakima Basin) and Oregon (Bridge Creek, John Day Basin) document measurable improvements in juvenile salmonid density, survival, and growth in beaver-influenced stream reaches compared to control reaches.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-MONTANE (y=-41 to y=84); all PNW salmon-bearing watersheds
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; dam construction peaks spring-fall; habitat benefits continuous
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- American Beaver profile

---

### Elk (*Cervus canadensis*)

**Connection Type:** Habitat modifier (riparian browser)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, fry (stream habitat quality)
**Mechanism:** Elk browsing in riparian zones directly affects salmon habitat quality through vegetation removal, bank trampling, and trail creation. The wolf-elk-riparian-salmon trophic cascade is one of the best-documented indirect ecological connections in the PNW: wolf predation on elk creates a "landscape of fear" that reduces elk residence time in riparian zones, allowing recovery of willows, cottonwoods, and alders that provide shade (reducing stream temperatures), bank stability (reducing sedimentation), insect drop (prey for juvenile salmon), and large wood recruitment (creating pool-riffle habitat complexity). Elk trails to rivers also create access paths used by bears transporting salmon carcasses into the forest. The wolf-elk-salmon cascade demonstrates that salmon habitat is not just a fisheries issue -- it is shaped by terrestrial predator-prey dynamics across the entire landscape.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-SUBALPINE (y=-41 to y=159); all PNW river valleys with elk populations
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; riparian browsing concentrated in winter when upland forage is limited; wolf behavioral effects continuous
**Cross-ref:** `carnivores.md` -- Ungulates, Elk profile

---

### Muskrat (*Ondatra zibethicus*)

**Connection Type:** Habitat modifier
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, smolt (off-channel habitat)
**Mechanism:** Muskrat channel creation in wetlands and estuary margins provides off-channel habitat used by juvenile salmonids during rearing. In estuary habitats, muskrat feeding activity helps maintain the vegetation mosaic that salmonids use during estuarine rearing -- the transitional phase between freshwater and marine life. Muskrat-created channels and bank burrows provide refuge habitat for juvenile salmon during high-flow events. While not a primary salmon habitat engineer like beaver, muskrats contribute to the habitat complexity that supports salmonid survival in lowland and estuarine environments.
**Ecoregion:** ELEV-RIPARIAN through ELEV-LOWLAND (y=-41 to y=34); lowland wetlands, estuaries, and river margins
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; channel construction continuous; estuarine habitat most critical for salmon during spring outmigration
**Cross-ref:** `small-mammals.md` -- Muskrat profile

## Bear-Salmon Nutrient Pathway

The bear-salmon-forest nutrient cycle is the most studied and most ecologically significant mammal-salmon interaction in the PNW. This section synthesizes the pathway in detail.

### The Mechanism

Bears catching salmon and carrying carcasses into the forest is not simply feeding behavior -- it is the primary mechanism by which marine-derived nutrients cross the aquatic-terrestrial boundary in PNW ecosystems. The pathway operates through a predictable sequence:

1. **Salmon return from the ocean** carrying marine-derived nitrogen (enriched in N-15), phosphorus, carbon, sulfur, and micronutrients accumulated during 1-7 years of oceanic feeding. A single adult Chinook carries approximately 130-260 g of nitrogen and 20-30 g of phosphorus (Naiman et al. 2002).

2. **Bears catch salmon in spawning streams.** Both black bears and grizzlies wade into riffles and pools, catching fish with paws and jaws. In productive systems, a single bear may catch 20-30 fish per day during peak spawning.

3. **Selective feeding concentrates nutrient deposition.** Bears preferentially consume the brain and roe -- the most energy-dense tissues -- and abandon the remainder. This selective consumption means that 40-70% of the carcass nitrogen is deposited on the forest floor rather than consumed and metabolized (Reimchen 2000; Hilderbrand et al. 1999).

4. **Carcass transport distances.** Bears carry carcasses away from the stream to feed in seclusion, depositing them at distances that vary with bear density and forest structure:

| Distance from stream | Carcass density | Primary vector |
|---------------------|----------------|----------------|
| 0-25 m | Very high | Bears, flooding, direct deposition |
| 25-50 m | High | Bear transport (typical feeding distance) |
| 50-150 m | Moderate | Bear transport (dominant individual territories) |
| 150-500 m | Low | Bear transport (high-density populations, large males) |
| >500 m | Rare | Occasional extreme transport events |

### Quantified Marine-Derived Nitrogen in Riparian Soils

Stable isotope analysis provides the definitive evidence for bear-mediated nutrient transfer. Nitrogen-15 (N-15) is enriched in marine systems relative to terrestrial systems, providing a natural tracer. Bear-transported salmon carcasses leave a measurable N-15 signature in:

**Soils:** Riparian soils adjacent to salmon-bearing streams with active bear populations show N-15 enrichment of +1.0-2.0 per mil relative to non-salmon reference sites (Helfield & Naiman 2001). The enrichment is highest in the organic soil horizon (O horizon) where carcass decomposition occurs and decreases with soil depth.

**Riparian vegetation:** Shrubs and herbs in the carcass deposition zone show N-15 enrichment of +1.5-3.0 per mil, confirming plant uptake of salmon-derived nitrogen (Helfield & Naiman 2001).

**Conifer foliage:** Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) foliage within 25 m of salmon-bearing streams shows N-15 enrichment of +2.5-3.5 per mil compared to trees along salmon-free streams on the Olympic Peninsula (Helfield & Naiman 2001).

**Fungal tissue:** Mycorrhizal fungi in the root zone of riparian conifers incorporate salmon-derived nitrogen and transport it through hyphal networks to tree roots, creating a direct pipeline: carcass decomposition products -> fungal absorption -> mycorrhizal transport -> tree root uptake.

### Bear-Mediated Nutrient Transfer: The 70% Rule

Reimchen (2000) and subsequent studies established that approximately 70% of bear-caught salmon carcass nitrogen is deposited within 50 m of the stream. This finding is critical because it establishes the spatial footprint of the bear-mediated nutrient subsidy:

- **~30% of carcass N** is consumed and metabolized by the bear (urine, feces, and respiration distribute this nitrogen more diffusely across the bear's home range)
- **~70% of carcass N** remains in the abandoned carcass tissue on the forest floor within the riparian zone
- Of the deposited 70%, decomposition and mycorrhizal uptake incorporate the majority into the soil-plant system within 1-2 growing seasons

In productive salmon systems, this translates to substantial nutrient loading. A system receiving 10,000 spawning salmon, with bears capturing 15-20% of the run (1,500-2,000 fish) and depositing carcasses averaging 4 kg each at 3% nitrogen content, delivers approximately 126-168 kg of marine-derived nitrogen to the riparian forest per kilometer of stream -- a fertilization rate that exceeds background atmospheric nitrogen deposition by an order of magnitude.

### Effects on Tree Growth Rings

The most striking evidence for bear-salmon-forest nutrient transfer comes from dendrochronology -- the analysis of tree growth rings in riparian conifers:

**Helfield and Naiman (2001)** analyzed Sitka spruce growth rings along salmon-bearing and salmon-free streams on the Olympic Peninsula and found:

- Trees within 25 m of salmon-bearing streams had annual radial growth rates 2-3 times higher than trees along salmon-free reference streams of comparable size, gradient, and elevation
- Growth ring width correlated positively with salmon run size over multi-decadal timescales -- years with large salmon returns produced wider rings in subsequent growing seasons
- The N-15 signature in tree rings confirmed that the growth enhancement was attributable to marine-derived nitrogen rather than other site differences
- The effect was strongest in Sitka spruce and western red cedar, which dominate the immediate riparian zone and have root systems that access the soil horizon where carcass decomposition occurs

**Drake et al. (2006)** extended these findings to white spruce in British Columbia, documenting similar growth-salmon correlations and N-15 enrichment patterns in both inner bark and annual rings.

The tree growth ring data establish that bear-salmon nutrient transfer is not merely detectable -- it is ecologically transformative. The riparian forests of the PNW are measurably larger, denser, and more productive because salmon and bears deliver marine nutrients to their root zones. These larger trees provide more shade (lower stream temperatures), more large wood recruitment (better juvenile salmon habitat), and more bank stability (less sedimentation) -- completing the positive feedback loop between salmon, bears, and forests.

## Orca-Salmon Crisis

The Southern Resident killer whale (SRKW) population represents the most acute mammal-salmon conservation crisis in the PNW. This section details the mechanisms connecting SRKW decline to salmon decline.

### SRKW Dependence on Chinook Salmon

Southern Resident killer whales are obligate salmon specialists. Unlike Bigg's (transient) orcas that eat marine mammals, and offshore orcas that eat sharks, SRKW feed almost exclusively on fish -- and within that fish diet, Chinook salmon dominates:

- **>80% of SRKW diet by biomass is Chinook salmon** (*O. tshawytscha*), based on prey fragment analysis from fecal samples and acoustic prey-tracking studies (Ford et al. 2010; Hanson et al. 2010)
- Secondary prey includes chum salmon (*O. keta*, ~15% of diet) and coho salmon (*O. kisutch*, ~5%)
- SRKW preferentially target the largest Chinook (age 4-5, 20-40 lbs), which carry the highest caloric return per foraging effort
- Prey-sharing behavior within matrilineal pods means that successful catches are distributed among family members, with mothers provisioning dependent calves

### Population Decline Tracking Salmon Decline

The SRKW population trajectory mirrors Chinook salmon abundance with a lag of 1-2 years:

| Year | SRKW Population | Key Chinook Stocks | Notes |
|------|----------------|-------------------|-------|
| 1995 | 98 | Moderate | Pre-decline peak |
| 2001 | 78 | Declining | Listed Endangered 2005 |
| 2005 | 88 | Variable | Brief recovery |
| 2011 | 87 | Low | L25 death, cultural significance |
| 2018 | 75 | Very low | J35 carrying dead calf (17 days) |
| 2020 | 72 | Low | COVID reduced vessel noise |
| 2024 | 73 | Variable | J pod calf born, K pod declining |

The correlation between Chinook abundance and SRKW vital rates is statistically robust:

- **Fecundity** (calving rate) increases in years following high Chinook abundance (Ward et al. 2009)
- **Calf survival** is higher when Chinook are abundant during the first year of life
- **Adult survival** decreases in years of low Chinook availability, particularly for reproductive-age females and post-reproductive females who provision grand-offspring
- **Nutritional stress** is measurable: SRKW photographed from above show declining body condition (peanut-head profiles indicating loss of blubber and muscle mass behind the skull) correlated with low prey availability

### PCB Bioaccumulation Through Salmon to Orca

Persistent organic pollutants -- particularly polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs) -- bioaccumulate through the salmon-orca food chain:

1. **Source:** PCBs enter Puget Sound and the Salish Sea from urban and industrial runoff (Seattle, Tacoma, Vancouver), atmospheric deposition, and legacy contamination from historical industrial use (banned in 1979 but persistent in sediments)
2. **Bioaccumulation in salmon:** Chinook salmon accumulate PCBs during their marine phase, particularly fish that rear in or migrate through contaminated nearshore waters. Larger, older Chinook (the ones SRKW prefer) carry higher PCB loads
3. **Biomagnification in orca:** SRKW accumulate PCBs at the top of the food chain. Adult male SRKW carry PCB concentrations of 100-250+ mg/kg lipid weight -- among the highest of any marine mammal population globally
4. **Reproductive toxicity:** PCBs are endocrine disruptors that impair reproduction, immune function, and calf development. Mothers transfer PCB loads to calves through lipid-rich milk during lactation, meaning first-born calves receive the highest contaminant dose
5. **Nutritional stress amplification:** When SRKW metabolize blubber during periods of low prey availability, stored PCBs are mobilized into the bloodstream at elevated concentrations, creating a toxic synergy between prey limitation and contaminant exposure

SRKW are caught in a contaminant trap: they cannot switch to less-contaminated prey (they are obligate Chinook specialists), and the prey they depend on carries a persistent toxic load.

### Vessel Noise Interference with Echolocation-Based Salmon Hunting

SRKW locate salmon using echolocation -- high-frequency biosonar clicks that reflect off fish targets and return acoustic information about prey location, size, and species. Vessel noise in the Salish Sea directly interferes with this foraging mechanism:

- **Masking:** Vessel noise (particularly from large commercial ships, ferries, and whale-watching boats) overlaps with the frequency range used by SRKW echolocation clicks (15-100 kHz), reducing the effective detection range for salmon targets
- **Behavioral disruption:** SRKW increase call amplitude (Lombard effect), reduce foraging bout duration, and abandon foraging areas in response to vessel proximity (Lusseau et al. 2009)
- **Energetic cost:** Reduced foraging efficiency due to vessel noise increases the energy expenditure per successful prey capture, exacerbating the nutritional stress from low Chinook availability
- **Regulatory response:** NOAA established vessel approach distances (200 yards for Southern Residents) and speed restrictions in core habitat areas; Washington State enacted laws restricting whale-watching vessel proximity; the Canadian government established interim sanctuary zones in the Salish Sea

The combination of prey limitation, contaminant burden, and vessel noise creates a triple threat that makes SRKW recovery dependent on simultaneous progress on all three fronts -- with Chinook recovery as the foundational requirement.

## Pinniped-Salmon Conflict

The interaction between pinnipeds and salmon at Columbia River dams represents the most contentious mammal-salmon management issue in the PNW -- a direct conflict between two federal conservation mandates.

### Sea Lion Predation at Bonneville Dam

Bonneville Dam, the lowest dam on the Columbia River (located at river mile 146), became the focal point of the pinniped-salmon conflict in the early 2000s when California sea lions (*Zalophus californianus*) began aggregating below the dam tailrace to prey on adult spring Chinook, winter steelhead, and other salmonids queuing for fish ladder passage.

**The learned behavior problem:** California sea lions developed a socially transmitted foraging tradition at Bonneville Dam. Pioneer individuals discovered the concentrated prey resource at the tailrace, and the behavior spread through the male population through social learning. By the mid-2000s, dozens of sea lions were present below the dam during peak migration periods, with individual animals consuming 5-20 adult salmonids per day.

**Steller sea lion expansion:** Steller sea lions (*Eumetopias jubatus*) joined California sea lions at Bonneville Dam beginning around 2015, with increasing numbers present each year. Stellers are larger (males up to 1,100 kg vs. 400 kg for California sea lions), more aggressive, and appear to be developing similar learned foraging traditions at the dam.

**Quantified predation impact:** The Army Corps of Engineers and state/tribal fisheries agencies estimated that pinnipeds consumed 1-5% of the adult spring Chinook run and up to 8-10% of the winter steelhead run at Bonneville Dam in peak years. While these percentages appear modest, they are concentrated on ESA-listed populations that are already at critically low abundance -- every individual fish consumed represents a disproportionate impact on population recovery.

### Harbor Seal Predation on Smolts in Estuaries

Harbor seals (*Phoca vitulina*) prey on juvenile salmon (smolts) during the spring outmigration through estuaries, adding a second pinniped predation pressure on the opposite end of the salmon life cycle from dam tailrace predation:

- Harbor seals are ambush predators in estuary channels, tidal rips, and river mouths where smolts concentrate
- Their recovered population (approximately 60,000-70,000 in WA/OR combined) represents a historical high
- Chasco et al. (2017) estimated that predation by marine mammals (primarily harbor seals) in the Salish Sea may account for a substantial proportion of juvenile Chinook mortality during the smolt-to-adult transition
- The cumulative effect of seal predation on smolts (estuary) plus sea lion predation on adults (dam) creates mortality at both ends of the migration, compressing survival into an increasingly narrow window

### Management Controversy: MMPA vs. ESA

The pinniped-salmon conflict pits two foundational federal conservation laws against each other:

**Marine Mammal Protection Act (1972):** Prohibits the take (harassment, hunting, capture, killing) of all marine mammals in U.S. waters. Exceptions require specific authorization. Pinnipeds recovered to robust population levels under MMPA protection.

**Endangered Species Act (1973):** Mandates recovery of listed species, including multiple Columbia River salmon and steelhead populations. Pinniped predation at dams is identified as a significant mortality source for ESA-listed runs.

**Section 120 resolution:** Congress amended the MMPA with Section 120, authorizing lethal removal of "individually identifiable" pinnipeds that are "having a significant negative impact on the decline or recovery" of ESA-listed salmonids at specific locations. Under this authority:

- Oregon, Washington, and Idaho received authorization to lethally remove California sea lions at Bonneville Dam (2008)
- Over 200 California sea lions were lethally removed between 2008 and 2020
- The Endangered Species Act Amendments of 2018 expanded lethal removal authority to include Steller sea lions and to allow removal at additional locations (Willamette Falls, other Columbia tributaries)
- Tribal nations (particularly Columbia River treaty tribes: Yakama, Warm Springs, Umatilla, Nez Perce) have been vocal advocates for pinniped management, citing treaty fishing rights and salmon cultural significance

**Ongoing tensions:** Conservation groups opposing lethal removal argue that pinniped predation is a natural ecological interaction and that salmon decline is primarily driven by habitat loss, dams, and overfishing rather than predation. Salmon recovery advocates counter that ESA-listed populations at critically low abundance cannot absorb additional mortality from artificially elevated predator populations. The debate remains unresolved and politically charged.

## Temporal Phenology: Salmon Runs and Mammalian Life Cycles

The annual cycle of Pacific salmon runs structures the temporal ecology of PNW mammals. Salmon are not a constant resource -- they arrive in seasonal pulses that mammals have evolved to exploit, creating phenological alignments that synchronize mammalian behavior with salmon availability.

### Salmon Run Timing

| Species | Run Timing (PNW) | Peak Spawning | Primary Rivers |
|---------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Chinook** (spring) | March-June | May-June | Columbia, Willamette, Skagit, Nooksack |
| **Chinook** (fall) | August-November | September-October | Columbia, Sacramento, Klamath, Puget Sound tributaries |
| **Sockeye** | June-September | August-September | Fraser, Okanagan, Baker, Quinault |
| **Coho** | September-December | October-November | Coastal streams throughout PNW |
| **Chum** | October-January | November-December | Hood Canal, Skagit, Nooksack, Nisqually |
| **Pink** | August-October | September | Odd years: Skagit, Snohomish, Stillaguamish, Nooksack; Fraser |
| **Steelhead** (winter) | December-April | February-March | Coastal and Puget Sound tributaries |
| **Steelhead** (summer) | June-October | August-September | Skagit, Skykomish, interior Columbia tributaries |

### Bear Hyperphagia Aligned with Fall Spawning Runs

Bear hyperphagia -- the period of intensive feeding before hibernation when bears may consume 15,000-20,000 calories per day -- is precisely aligned with fall salmon spawning:

- **Black bear hyperphagia:** September-November (western WA/OR populations)
- **Fall Chinook spawning:** September-October
- **Coho spawning:** October-November
- **Chum spawning:** November-December (extends the feeding window)

This alignment is not coincidental. Bear populations in salmon-rich systems have evolved the timing and intensity of hyperphagia around the predictable pulse of salmon energy. Bears in the PNW gain 2-3 lbs per day during peak salmon availability, compared to 0.5-1.0 lbs per day on berry diets alone. Salmon provide the protein and fat density that berry-dominated diets lack, enabling bears to enter hibernation with sufficient fat reserves to survive winter and (for pregnant females) produce and nurse cubs in the den.

In years when salmon runs fail or arrive late, bear behavior shifts measurably: bears remain active later into fall, forage more extensively on alternative foods (garbage, livestock, bird feeders), and enter hibernation in poorer body condition -- increasing human-bear conflict and reducing overwinter cub survival.

### Orca Seasonal Movements Tracking Salmon Runs

SRKW movements are governed almost entirely by salmon run timing:

- **May-June:** Pods arrive in the Salish Sea as Fraser River spring Chinook begin entering the Strait of Juan de Fuca; J pod typically arrives first, followed by K and L pods
- **July-September:** Peak Salish Sea residency; pods concentrate around San Juan Islands, Haro Strait, and Boundary Pass, intercepting Fraser River sockeye and summer/fall Chinook runs
- **October-November:** Pods begin dispersing as Salish Sea Chinook availability declines; L pod often departs first
- **December-April:** SRKW range along the outer coast of Washington, Oregon, and northern California, likely tracking winter Chinook runs to the Columbia River, Klamath River, and Central Valley rivers; this winter range is poorly documented due to offshore monitoring challenges

The timing of SRKW arrival in the Salish Sea has shifted in recent decades, potentially reflecting changes in salmon run timing related to ocean temperature and hydrological conditions. Years with early, strong Chinook returns correlate with earlier pod arrival and longer summer residency.

### Pinniped Seasonal Aggregation at Salmon Bottlenecks

Pinniped predation at dams and river mouths follows salmon run timing with learned precision:

- **California sea lions at Bonneville:** Peak presence March-May, tracking spring Chinook and winter steelhead passage
- **Steller sea lions at Bonneville:** Peak presence March-June, with increasing numbers in recent years
- **Harbor seals in estuaries:** Year-round presence but predation on smolts peaks April-June during spring outmigration; adult salmon predation peaks August-November during fall returns
- **California sea lions at Willamette Falls:** December-April, targeting winter steelhead ascending the falls
