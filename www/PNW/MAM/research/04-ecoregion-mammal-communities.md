# Module 4: Ecoregion Mammal Communities — Community Ecology of the Pacific Northwest by Elevation Band

> **PNW Mammalian Taxonomy — "Fur, Fin & Fang"**
>
> This module synthesizes the species profiles from Modules 1-3 into a community-ecology perspective, organized by the eight ecoregion zones defined in `00-ecoregion-definitions.md`. Where the companion modules ask "What does this species do?" this module asks "Who lives together, and what happens because they do?" Every elevation band in the Pacific Northwest hosts a mammalian community assembled by millions of years of evolutionary history, constrained by physical geography and climate, and increasingly reshaped by human activity. The community perspective reveals interactions invisible in species-by-species treatment — competitive exclusion, niche partitioning, trophic cascades, facilitation, and the seasonal movements that link zones into a single integrated system.
>
> **Design principle:** One major section per ecoregion zone. Each section profiles the species assemblage, dominant predator-prey relationships, interspecific competition and niche partitioning, seasonal movements between zones, indicator and conservation species, and the human-wildlife interface. Species assemblage tables summarize community membership. Cross-references to Modules 1, 2A, 2B, 2C, and 3 are provided throughout.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates, den sites, haul-outs, or calving areas for ESA-listed species. County/watershed level only. Every Indigenous knowledge reference names a specific nation. Population data from USFWS, WDFW, ODFW, IDFG, NOAA, or peer-reviewed research. No policy advocacy. Human-wildlife conflict documented factually. Only Level 1 cultural content.

## Zone 1: Alpine / Subalpine (>5,000 ft)

### Community Identity

The alpine and subalpine zone is the PNW's ceiling — a world of exposed rock, talus fields, krummholz, snowfields that persist into August, and wind-blasted meadows where the growing season may last only eight to twelve weeks. The mammalian community here is small in species count but extraordinary in specialization. Every resident has solved the same fundamental problem: how to survive winters that last seven months, bury the landscape under 15-30 feet of snow, and reduce ambient temperatures to -30 degrees Fahrenheit. The solutions are diverse — hibernation (marmots, jumping mice), subnivean activity (voles, pikas), behavioral thermoregulation (mountain goats moving to wind-swept ridges), and sheer metabolic endurance (wolverines crossing 20+ miles of alpine terrain in a single day).

This is also the zone most immediately threatened by climate change. As treeline advances upslope and snowpack duration shrinks, the alpine community is squeezed against a topographic ceiling — there is no higher ground to retreat to. Species that depend on persistent snowpack for denning (wolverine), thermal buffering (pika), or competitive advantage (lynx) face range contraction with no analogue habitat available.

**Seasonal visitors:** Grizzly bear (berry fields, army cutworm moths at talus sites — summer/fall), black bear (huckleberry meadows — late summer), cougar (following mountain goats and mule deer), mule deer (summer range), Rocky Mountain elk (summer meadows).

### Predator-Prey Relationships

The alpine predator community is sparse but specialized. The wolverine sits at the apex — not as a dominant predator of live prey, but as a scavenger-predator that cleans winter-killed ungulates, hunts marmots emerging from hibernation, and takes pikas from talus. Wolverines cache food under snow for later retrieval, and their massive feet and tireless locomotion allow them to exploit territory inaccessible to larger predators during deep-snow months. The Cascade red fox — one of North America's rarest fox subspecies, with an estimated population of fewer than 500 — is the primary mesopredator, hunting pikas, marmots (young), voles, and ground squirrels. Ermines hunt voles and pikas year-round, their white winter pelage providing crypsis against snowfields.

The dominant prey guild consists of the marmot-pika-vole triad. Hoary marmots are colonial, using sentinel behavior and alarm whistles to warn of predators — their piercing whistle is the characteristic sound of the PNW alpine. The American pika is a solitary talus specialist that does not hibernate but instead harvests and cures "haypiles" of dried vegetation in late summer, storing them in rock crevices for winter consumption. Pika populations at lower elevations are declining as temperatures rise above their thermal tolerance (~78 degrees F sustained), making the pika a climate sentinel whose range contraction is measurable in real time. Voles (heather vole, long-tailed vole) maintain subnivean activity throughout winter, feeding on cached seeds and plant material beneath the snowpack.

Golden eagle predation on marmots and mountain goat kids connects the alpine community to the avian food web (AVI cross-link). Great horned owls and northern goshawks take voles and pikas at lower subalpine margins.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

The critical competitive interaction in this zone is the lynx-bobcat dynamic. Canada lynx (*Lynx canadensis*), an ESA-Threatened species, is a snowshoe hare specialist whose large feet provide competitive advantage over other predators in deep, soft snow. As climate change reduces snowpack depth and duration, bobcats (*Lynx rufus*) are expanding upslope into formerly lynx-exclusive habitat. Bobcats are dietary generalists that do not require deep snow — in reduced-snow conditions, they outcompete lynx for the same prey base. This competitive displacement represents a climate-driven reshuffling of the predator guild, with the specialist (lynx) losing ground to the generalist (bobcat).

Among small mammals, niche partitioning is primarily spatial. Pikas occupy talus and rock fields. Marmots occupy meadow-talus interfaces. Voles occupy meadow and heath. Deer mice are generalists occupying all substrates. Chipmunks and ground squirrels forage in open meadow and at forest edge. This spatial partitioning reduces direct competition, but all contribute to the prey base sustaining weasels and foxes.

### Seasonal Movements

The alpine zone functions as a summer destination for multiple lower-elevation species:

- **Grizzly bear:** Moves to alpine from montane/riparian in mid-summer for army cutworm moth aggregations at talus sites (a single moth site may support 20,000+ kcal/day per bear) and late-summer berry fields (huckleberry, bearberry)
- **Mule deer and Rocky Mountain elk:** Summer range in subalpine meadows (5,000-8,000 ft), descending to lower-elevation winter range by October-November; migration corridors are matrilineally transmitted
- **Mountain goat:** Relatively sedentary but descends to forest cover during severe winter storms; returns to cliff terrain as snow consolidates
- **Wolverine:** Circuits of 100+ miles cross multiple elevation bands; uses alpine for denning in persistent snowpack (February-May)

These movements mean the alpine community's species richness peaks dramatically in summer and contracts to only the most cold-hardy specialists in winter (pikas, voles, ermines, and snowshoe hares at subalpine margins).

The alpine zone has the highest concentration of climate-vulnerable mammal species in the PNW. Four of the seven most climate-vulnerable species identified in the Module 1 synthesis (wolverine, lynx, pika, Cascade red fox) are alpine obligates or near-obligates.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

Direct human-wildlife conflict in the alpine zone is limited by remoteness, but three interface issues are significant:

1. **Backcountry recreation disturbance:** Mountain goats habituate to human presence at popular trails and mineral licks, creating management challenges (aggressive encounters at Mt. Ellinor, Olympic NP removal program). Wolverine den sites may be disturbed by backcountry skiing in late winter — the denning period coincides with peak ski-touring season.

2. **Climate change as indirect human impact:** The alpine community's vulnerability to warming is the primary conservation concern. Upslope treeline advance, reduced snowpack, and altered precipitation patterns are already measurable and will accelerate.

3. **Mountain goat management in the Olympics:** The Olympic Mountain goat controversy — introduced goats degrading fragile endemic alpine plant communities, resulting in NPS relocation of 325 goats to the Cascades (2018-2020) — illustrates how a non-native ungulate population can reshape an alpine community lacking co-evolutionary history with that species.

## Zone 2: Montane Conifer Forest (2,500-5,000 ft)

### Community Identity

The montane conifer forest is the ecological heart of the PNW mammalian system. This is the zone of Douglas-fir cathedrals and hemlock understory, where five-hundred-year-old trees host a community of mammals bound together by a three-kingdom mutualism — tree, fungus, and squirrel — that has persisted since before the Cascade Range reached its present height. The montane zone supports the highest mammalian species diversity of any PNW elevation band because it combines structural complexity (old-growth canopy layers, snags, coarse woody debris, understory) with moderate climate, abundant food resources, and connectivity to both alpine above and lowland below.

This is where the PNW's apex predator guild — wolf, cougar, and bear — reaches full expression. It is where the trophic cascade that connects predators to forest regeneration through ungulate behavior plays out. And it is where the old-growth indicator species — marten, fisher, flying squirrel, red tree vole — signal whether the forest's structural complexity is intact or degraded.

### Predator-Prey Relationships

**The Apex Tier: Wolf, Cougar, Bear**

The montane zone is the stage for the PNW's most consequential predator-prey relationships. Cougar and wolf partition prey by habitat and hunting strategy. Cougars are solitary ambush predators of dense forest — they stalk black-tailed deer (west-side) and mule deer (east-side) through old-growth and second-growth, killing with a bite to the base of the skull. A single cougar takes approximately one ungulate every 7-14 days, caching and revisiting kills. Wolves are cooperative cursorial predators of more open terrain — packs pursue elk across montane meadows and through moderate forest, relying on endurance to exhaust prey. Where both predators are present (increasingly, as wolves recolonize), wolves may displace cougars from kills through kleptoparasitism, and wolves suppress coyote populations through interference competition — a mesopredator release reversal that benefits ground-nesting birds and small mammals.

The ecological consequence of this predator guild is the trophic cascade. Wolf and cougar presence creates a "landscape of fear" that modifies ungulate spatial behavior — elk and deer spend less time in exposed riparian zones, reducing browse pressure on willows, cottonwoods, and understory vegetation. Recovered riparian vegetation improves salmon stream habitat (shading, bank stability, insect prey production). This cascade has been documented at Olympic National Park (cougar-elk: Ripple & Beschta 2006) and is expected to develop in wolf recovery areas across the Cascades and northeast Washington.

Black bears are significant predators of ungulate calves and fawns in spring (May-June) but function primarily as omnivores, consuming 80-90% plant material by volume. Their ecological role as nutrient transporters — carrying salmon carcasses from streams into the forest, depositing marine-derived nitrogen — makes them a critical link in the salmon-bear-forest nutrient pathway (see Zone 5: Riparian).

**The Mustelid Predator Guild**

American marten and fisher are the signature predators of the old-growth montane forest. Marten are old-growth indicators — their presence signals that a forest retains the structural complexity (large snags, coarse woody debris, canopy gaps) required for subnivean hunting of voles and squirrels. Marten diet consists primarily of red squirrels, voles, chipmunks, and pikas, supplemented by berries and insects. Fisher, the only reliable predator of porcupines, was extirpated from Washington by the mid-1900s due to trapping and old-growth logging. A multi-year reintroduction program (2008-2020) released 248 fishers to the Olympic Peninsula, South Cascades, and North Cascades; breeding has been confirmed in all three areas.

Weasels (long-tailed and short-tailed) are the most abundant mustelid predators, hunting voles, mice, and chipmunks through forest understory and subnivean tunnels. Their high metabolic rate (eating 40-60% of body weight daily) drives intense predation pressure on small mammals.

**The Flying Squirrel-Fungus-Forest Mutualism**

The northern flying squirrel occupies a unique trophic position in the montane community. It is the primary nocturnal disperser of hypogeous mycorrhizal fungi — the underground truffles produced by genera including *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, and *Hymenogaster* that form obligate symbiotic partnerships with conifer roots. Without squirrel dispersal, these fungi cannot colonize new root systems; without these fungi, Douglas-fir, western hemlock, and Sitka spruce cannot acquire the phosphorus and water they need for growth. The flying squirrel-fungus-forest feedback loop (Maser, Trappe & Nussbaum 1978) is one of the best-documented three-kingdom mutualisms in temperate ecology.

The flying squirrel is simultaneously the primary prey of the Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*; AVI cross-link), creating a chain of dependency: old-growth forest structure supports flying squirrel populations, flying squirrels support spotted owls, and spotted owl presence indicates intact old-growth — a nested indicator system. The barred owl's invasion of the PNW (AVI cross-link) has disrupted this system by adding a generalist predator that competes with spotted owls while also consuming flying squirrels, potentially decoupling the indicator relationship.

Douglas squirrels perform a complementary role as diurnal fungal dispersers and conifer seed cachers. Their middens — decades-old accumulations of cone scales and soil — function as nutrient hotspots and seedling nurseries on the forest floor.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

**Temporal partitioning:** Douglas squirrels are diurnal, northern flying squirrels nocturnal — occupying the same forest canopy and consuming some of the same fungi, but never at the same time. This temporal separation reduces direct competition while maintaining continuous fungal dispersal pressure across the 24-hour cycle.

**Spatial partitioning among small mammals:** Red-backed voles occupy the forest floor among coarse woody debris. Creeping voles are fossorial, tunneling through soil and duff. Townsend's chipmunks forage on the ground surface and in low shrubs. Flying squirrels forage in the canopy. Douglas squirrels defend canopy territories centered on cone-producing trees. Deer mice are generalists occupying all strata. This vertical and horizontal partitioning allows 10+ rodent species to coexist in a single old-growth stand.

**Predator partitioning:** Marten hunt in the canopy and subnivean zone. Weasels hunt in burrows and understory. Bobcats are ground-level ambush predators. Cougars take ungulates. This size-structured predator guild efficiently exploits the full spectrum of mammalian prey from 5-g shrews to 300-kg elk.

**Deer niche partitioning (west-side):** Black-tailed deer and Roosevelt elk share the montane forest but partition by diet — elk are grazer-browsers shifting to meadow grasses, while black-tailed deer are obligate browsers selecting huckleberry, salal, and vine maple. Elk herds (10-50 animals) use open meadows and edges; black-tailed deer (2-5 animals) use dense understory.

### Seasonal Movements

The montane zone is the primary year-round habitat for most of its community members, but seasonal fluxes are significant:

- **Elk and mule deer seasonal migration:** Rocky Mountain elk and mule deer move to montane and lower-elevation winter range from alpine summer range, concentrating prey biomass and predator activity. These migrations may cover 50-100+ miles along traditional corridors that are matrilineally transmitted through ungulate herds.
- **Bear denning:** Black bears enter dens (tree cavities, brush piles, excavated ground) in November-December and emerge March-April, effectively removing the largest omnivore from the community for 4-5 months.
- **Bat migration:** Several bat species (hoary bat, silver-haired bat) migrate south or to lower elevations in fall. Little brown bats shift from summer foraging sites to winter hibernacula — a behavior that concentrates them in caves and mines where white-nose syndrome can devastate colonies.
- **Hibernation pulse:** Marmots, ground squirrels, jumping mice, and chipmunks enter torpor in September-October and emerge April-June, creating a seasonal boom-bust in small-mammal prey availability that affects predator behavior.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Timber harvest and old-growth loss:** The montane zone has been the most heavily logged elevation band in the PNW. Clear-cutting eliminates the structural complexity that supports marten, fisher, flying squirrels, and red tree voles. Second-growth forests (30-80 years) support reduced mammalian diversity compared to old-growth.

2. **Wolf-livestock conflict:** As wolves recolonize montane range in northeast WA and the east Cascades, livestock depredation on grazing allotments creates management tension. Lethal removal of packs with chronic livestock conflict has occurred. The Wolf Advisory Group advises WDFW on balancing wolf recovery with rural economic concerns.

3. **Cougar-human encounters:** Cougar populations are robust (estimated 2,000-2,500 in WA, 6,000+ in OR), and expanding urban-wildland interface pushes human recreation into cougar habitat. Encounters are increasing though fatal attacks remain extremely rare.

4. **White-nose syndrome (WNS) threat to bats:** WNS (*Pseudogymnoascus destructans*) has not yet been confirmed in WA or OR but is advancing westward across North America. The fungal disease kills hibernating bats by disrupting torpor cycles. PNW populations of little brown bats, Yuma myotis, and other cave-hibernating species face catastrophic decline if WNS arrives. The disease has already killed an estimated 6+ million bats in eastern North America.

## Zone 3: Lowland Forest / Urban (<500 ft)

### Community Identity

The lowland zone is where the PNW's mammalian community meets the human one. This is the landscape of Puget Sound, the Willamette Valley, the lower Columbia floodplain, and the coastal strip — the most densely populated and most modified habitat in the region. The original community — Douglas-fir/western red cedar forest, wetlands, prairies — has been transformed into a mosaic of suburban development, agriculture, second-growth forest fragments, parks, and riparian corridors. The mammalian community that persists in this mosaic is a filtered version of the montane assemblage, enriched by introduced species and dominated by human-adapted generalists.

What makes this zone ecologically interesting is not its simplification but its novel assemblages — communities that have no historical analogue, where native black-tailed deer share suburban gardens with introduced Virginia opossums and Eastern gray squirrels, where coyotes den in urban greenbelts, and where raccoons reach population densities 10-100 times their forest baselines. This is the zone where human-wildlife coexistence is not an abstract management goal but a daily reality for millions of residents.

### Predator-Prey Relationships

The lowland predator community is truncated — wolves and cougars are largely absent from the most urbanized areas, leaving coyotes as the de facto apex predator. Urban coyote populations have expanded dramatically since the 1990s, filling the mesopredator role vacated by the absence of larger carnivores and exploiting abundant anthropogenic food sources (garbage, pet food, fruit trees, domestic cats). Urban coyotes in the Puget Sound lowlands maintain territories of 0.5-3 square miles, substantially smaller than their rural counterparts, reflecting food concentration.

Raccoons achieve their highest population densities in suburban and urban environments — 25-50 times forest densities — and function as dominant nest predators on ground-nesting birds (waterfowl, shorebirds) and turtle nests. Their dexterous forepaws and intelligence allow them to exploit virtually every anthropogenic food source. In the ecological literature, the raccoon-dominated urban mesopredator community is cited as a textbook example of mesopredator release — the absence of apex predators (wolves, cougars) removes top-down control, allowing mesopredator populations to explode and suppress vulnerable prey species.

The Virginia opossum, introduced to the PNW in the 1930s-1940s, adds a nocturnal omnivore-scavenger to the lowland community with no native ecological analogue. Opossums consume insects, fruits, carrion, bird eggs, and garbage. Their impact on native species is debated — they compete with raccoons and skunks for food but also consume ticks and venomous insects.

Black-tailed deer are the dominant herbivore, and in the absence of predators, suburban deer populations can reach densities that suppress native plant regeneration and damage gardens and landscaping. Deer-vehicle collisions are the most common form of large-mammal human-wildlife conflict in the PNW lowlands.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

The most consequential competitive interaction in the lowland zone is between native Douglas squirrels and introduced Eastern gray squirrels (*Sciurus carolinensis*). Eastern grays were introduced to urban parks in the early 1900s and have expanded throughout the Puget Sound lowlands, Willamette Valley, and Portland metro area. They outcompete Douglas squirrels in urban and suburban environments through several mechanisms: larger body size (500-700 g vs. 200-250 g), ability to digest acorns (Douglas squirrels are conifer seed specialists), and tolerance of open, fragmented habitats. In intact conifer forest, Douglas squirrels maintain competitive dominance; in urban parks with mixed plantings, Eastern grays dominate. The contact zone moves with urbanization.

The Eastern fox squirrel (*Sciurus niger*), introduced to cities in the Puget Sound region and Willamette Valley, adds a third competitor — larger than the Eastern gray (700-1,000 g) and even more tolerant of open, lawn-dominated environments.

Among small mammals, house mice and Norway rats compete with native deer mice and voles in disturbed habitats near human structures. In natural settings, deer mice outcompete house mice; the competitive balance reverses in buildings and heavily modified landscapes.

### Seasonal Movements

The lowland zone receives relatively little seasonal immigration — most species are year-round residents. Key exceptions:

- **Bat roost shifts:** Building-roosting bats (big brown bat, little brown bat) shift between summer maternity colonies in warm structures and winter hibernacula in cooler, more stable environments
- **Deer elevational movement:** Black-tailed deer at the urban-forest interface make limited elevational movements of a few hundred feet between summer and winter range
- **Migratory bats:** Hoary bats and silver-haired bats pass through the lowland zone during spring and fall migration

The Mazama pocket gopher deserves special note. This ESA-Threatened species is restricted to remnant prairies in Thurston, Pierce, and Mason counties, WA — fragments of the Puget lowland prairie ecosystem, itself one of the most endangered habitat types in the United States (less than 3% of original extent remains). The pocket gopher is an obligate prairie soil engineer; its burrowing aerates soil, buries vegetation, and creates the disturbed microsites that prairie wildflowers require for germination. Without pocket gophers, prairie plant diversity declines. Without prairie, pocket gophers have nowhere to dig. The species' conservation depends entirely on preserving and restoring the grassland fragments where it persists.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Deer-vehicle collisions:** Black-tailed deer are involved in thousands of vehicle collisions annually across western WA and OR. These collisions cause millions of dollars in damage and occasional human injuries.

2. **Coyote-pet conflict:** Urban coyotes prey on domestic cats and small dogs, creating public concern disproportionate to actual risk. Coyote attacks on humans are extremely rare in the PNW.

3. **Mole management:** Townsend's moles and coast moles create extensive tunnel systems and molehills in lawns, gardens, and agricultural fields — the most common source of homeowner wildlife complaints in western WA and OR.

4. **Rat management:** Norway and black rats cause significant structural damage, consume stored food, and vector disease in urban and agricultural settings. On offshore islands, Norway rats devastate ground-nesting seabird colonies — island rat eradication is a high-priority conservation action (AVI cross-link).

5. **Bat-building coexistence:** Little brown bats and big brown bats roost in buildings (attics, under eaves, in wall voids), creating management challenges when guano accumulation or fear of rabies conflicts with bat conservation needs. All PNW bats are insectivorous and provide substantial pest suppression services.

## Zone 4: Foothill / Oak Woodland (500-2,500 ft)

### Community Identity

The foothill and oak woodland zone occupies the transitional elevations between the valley floor and the montane conifer belt. In western Oregon, this is the landscape of Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*), madrone, and Douglas-fir/oak savanna — a fire-maintained ecosystem that supported a distinct mammalian community before Euro-American fire suppression, agricultural conversion, and urban sprawl fragmented it into remnant patches. In eastern Washington and Oregon, the foothills are pine-oak woodland, ponderosa pine savanna, and the upper margins of the sagebrush steppe — a xeric community shaped by aridity rather than maritime moisture.

The foothill zone's mammalian community bridges the lowland generalists and the montane specialists. It hosts the full complement of mesocarnivores — bobcat, gray fox, coyote, ringtail — and the western gray squirrel, a declining oak-woodland specialist that serves as the zone's signature indicator species.

### Predator-Prey Relationships

The foothill zone's predator guild is dominated by mesocarnivores — bobcat, gray fox, coyote, and ringtail — with cougar as the apex predator. Cougar density is often highest in foothill terrain where deer populations are robust and canyon topography provides ambush cover.

The gray fox is the foothill zone's distinctive predator. Unlike other canids, it climbs trees readily (rotating hind feet allow headfirst descent), using vertical escape from coyotes and ground-level predators. In the Rogue and Umpqua valleys of southern Oregon, gray foxes are the dominant mesocarnivore in oak woodland habitat. The ringtail, restricted in the PNW to southern Oregon canyons (Rogue River, Klamath Basin, Deschutes), occupies a similar nocturnal predator niche on rocky canyon walls, hunting insects, small mammals, and birds with extraordinary arboreal agility.

California ground squirrels form large colonial aggregations in foothill grasslands and oak savanna, creating a concentrated prey resource for raptors (red-tailed hawk, golden eagle — AVI cross-link), coyotes, and badgers. Badger-ground squirrel interactions on the east-side are a classic predator-prey system: badgers dig out ground squirrels from below while coyotes wait at escape burrows — a documented cooperative hunting behavior between two species that are otherwise competitors.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

The western gray squirrel (*Sciurus griseus*) is a Conservation Strategy Species in WA and OR, declining due to habitat loss, disease (notoedric mange in WA), and competition with introduced Eastern gray squirrels. Western grays are oak-dependent — they require large oaks for nesting and acorn caching. As oak woodland is converted to development, vineyards, or conifer plantation, western gray squirrel populations fragment. Where Eastern gray squirrels have invaded foothill habitats (suburban edge), they outcompete western grays through faster reproduction and broader diet.

In the Klamath-Siskiyou region, the dusky-footed woodrat builds elaborate stick houses on the ground, creating microhabitat used by deer mice, Pacific giant salamanders, ring-necked snakes, and invertebrates. These woodrat houses function as biodiversity hotspots — a single house may shelter a dozen commensal species. Barred owl expansion into the region has increased woodrat predation, potentially cascading to affect commensal communities.

### Seasonal Movements

- **Mule deer elevational migration:** Mule deer in the east-side foothills migrate between foothill winter range and montane/alpine summer range — corridors of 50-100 miles are traditional and matrilineally transmitted
- **Bear seasonal movement:** Black bears move through foothill oak woodland in fall, feeding heavily on acorns and madrone berries before denning
- **Bat seasonal roost shifts:** Townsend's big-eared bats shift between summer roosts (warm buildings, caves) and winter hibernacula (cool caves, mines) — extreme sensitivity to human disturbance at roost sites makes this species a bellwether for subterranean habitat conservation

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Oak woodland conversion:** The Willamette Valley has lost over 95% of its historical oak savanna to agriculture, development, and fire suppression. The foothill remnants that sustain western gray squirrels, gray foxes, and the distinctive foothill community are increasingly fragmented.

2. **Vineyard expansion:** Rapid vineyard development in the Rogue, Umpqua, and Willamette valleys is converting foothill oak woodland and grassland, displacing deer, ground squirrels, and brush rabbits.

3. **Mine closure impacts:** Abandoned mines provide critical bat roosting habitat (Townsend's big-eared bat, other *Myotis* species). Mine closure for safety purposes without bat surveys can destroy irreplaceable hibernacula.

## Zone 5: Riparian / Wetland / Estuarine

### Community Identity

The riparian zone is the PNW's connective tissue — a linear network of habitat that threads through every other zone, linking alpine headwaters to coastal estuaries, connecting terrestrial and aquatic food webs, and providing movement corridors for species that would otherwise be isolated in habitat fragments. Riparian corridors are disproportionately productive: they support higher mammalian species diversity per unit area than any terrestrial habitat type in the PNW, largely because they provide water, food, cover, and thermal moderation simultaneously.

The community here is defined by water — species that hunt in it (otter, mink), engineer it (beaver), or depend on the vegetation and prey base it produces. The riparian zone is also the primary interface between the salmon-marine nutrient pathway and the terrestrial ecosystem, making it the zone where the PNW's most celebrated ecological cascade plays out.

### Predator-Prey Relationships

**The Beaver as Community Architect**

The American beaver is the keystone species of the riparian zone — arguably the single most ecologically consequential mammal in the PNW after humans. Beaver dams impound water, raising water tables, reconnecting streams to floodplains, and creating wetland complexes that transform simple stream channels into structurally complex mosaics of ponds, side channels, backwaters, and wet meadows. The ecological effects cascade through the community:

- **Salmon habitat:** Beaver ponds provide critical rearing habitat for juvenile coho salmon and steelhead. Pond complexes increase habitat complexity — deep pools, side channels, overhanging cover — that benefit juvenile fish survival. In coastal WA and OR streams, coho juveniles rearing in beaver ponds grow faster and survive overwinter at higher rates than those in unponded reaches.
- **Water storage:** Beaver ponds store water during high-flow periods and release it slowly during summer baseflow, mitigating drought impacts and maintaining cool stream temperatures for cold-water fish. In the drought-prone east-side PNW, beaver-created water storage is increasingly recognized as natural climate adaptation.
- **Biodiversity amplification:** Beaver-created wetlands support amphibians, waterfowl (AVI cross-link), riparian songbirds, and a suite of invertebrates that would not persist in the simplified channel. The muskrat, a secondary wetland engineer, builds lodges and feeding platforms in beaver ponds, further diversifying habitat structure.

**The Salmon-Bear-Forest Nutrient Pathway**

The riparian zone is the stage for the PNW's most celebrated ecological cascade. Pacific salmon returning to spawn transport marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen-15, phosphorus, carbon-13) from ocean to freshwater. Bears — grizzly and black — intercept spawning salmon and carry carcasses into the adjacent forest. A single grizzly bear may transport 30-40 salmon per day during peak runs, depositing partially consumed carcasses up to 500 meters from the stream. Decomposing carcasses release marine-derived nitrogen into forest soils; trees within 500 meters of salmon streams show elevated nitrogen-15 and grow up to three times faster than trees far from streams. Eagles, ravens, crows, and gulls further distribute carcasses (AVI cross-link). This pathway links ocean productivity directly to forest growth through the medium of salmon, bears, and riparian transport.

**Semi-Aquatic Predator Guild**

River otters are the apex aquatic predator in freshwater systems, consuming fish (bottom species preferred — sculpins, suckers), crayfish, frogs, and mussels. Otter presence indicates good water quality and healthy fish populations — they function as biosentinels. Mink occupy a complementary niche as smaller semi-aquatic predators taking crayfish, frogs, muskrats, and small fish. The Pacific water shrew — the most aquatic *Sorex* in North America — hunts invertebrates underwater in headwater streams, using trapped-air buoyancy and stiff-haired hind feet as biological swim fins. This species is a WDFW State Candidate and COSEWIC Endangered in BC, declining where riparian corridor integrity is compromised.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

The semi-aquatic predator guild partitions prey by body size and foraging depth. River otters take fish and crayfish of 5-30 cm. Mink take smaller prey (crayfish, frogs, voles, small fish). Pacific water shrews take aquatic invertebrates of millimeter to centimeter scale. This size-structured partitioning allows three semi-aquatic predators to coexist along the same stream reach.

Beaver and muskrat coexist through diet partitioning: beavers consume woody vegetation (willow, alder bark), while muskrats consume emergent aquatic vegetation (cattails, sedges, rushes). Muskrats often use beaver lodges or construct their own in beaver-created ponds, making them commensals of the beaver-engineered landscape. Mink prey on muskrats, creating a predator-prey-engineer chain within the beaver pond complex.

### Seasonal Movements

- **Bear-salmon seasonal pulse:** Bears converge on salmon streams during spawning runs (July-November, varying by species and watershed), creating temporary concentrations of large omnivores that transform nutrient flow for weeks to months. Grizzly bear seasonal movement from alpine to riparian for salmon is among the most consequential mammalian migrations in the PNW — and currently limited to BC and northern WA recovery areas.
- **Otter seasonal expansion:** River otters expand territorial range in winter when ice cover or high water opens new foraging opportunities. Coastal otters move between freshwater streams and nearshore marine environments.
- **Elk riparian use:** Roosevelt elk intensify riparian browsing in winter when upland forage quality declines, concentrating browse pressure on willows and cottonwoods — the mechanism through which predator-ungulate dynamics affect salmon stream habitat.

|---|---|---|

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Beaver-human conflict:** Beaver dams cause flooding of roads, agricultural fields, and timber stands. Lethal removal has been the historical management response, but attitudes are shifting — "beaver dam analogs" (BDAs) and live relocation programs now complement traditional management as the ecological value of beaver-engineered watersheds is increasingly recognized.

2. **Floodplain development:** The lower Columbia River floodplain, core habitat for the ESA-Threatened Columbian white-tailed deer, is among the most heavily modified landscapes in the PNW. Diking, draining, and agricultural conversion have eliminated >90% of historical floodplain habitat. The Julia Butler Hansen National Wildlife Refuge provides protected floodplain, but the deer's population (~900-1,100) remains constrained by limited habitat.

3. **Salmon-pinniped conflict at fish passages:** River otter and (especially) California sea lion and harbor seal predation on ESA-listed salmon at dams and fish ladders creates tension between marine mammal protection (MMPA) and salmonid recovery (ESA). This conflict is most acute at Bonneville Dam but occurs at fish ladders throughout the Columbia Basin.

## Zone 6: Coastal / Marine / Pelagic

### Community Identity

The coastal and marine zone extends from the spray-soaked rocks of the intertidal to the abyssal canyons of the continental slope — a three-dimensional habitat spanning thousands of vertical feet of ocean. This is the PNW's largest ecoregion by area and the one most fundamentally different from the terrestrial zones: here, mammals are visitors, migrants, and specialists in a medium they cannot breathe. The marine mammal community is assembled by oceanographic forces — upwelling zones, current boundaries, submarine canyons, and the seasonal migrations of prey species — rather than by the topographic and vegetative gradients that organize terrestrial communities.

The PNW marine mammal community is remarkable for two features: its diversity (38 species of cetaceans and pinnipeds documented; see Module 3) and the ecological experiment playing out in real time among the killer whale ecotypes. Three ecotypes of orca — residents, Bigg's (transients), and offshores — have shared these waters for at least 200,000 years without interbreeding, partitioning the marine ecosystem by diet (salmon, marine mammals, sharks) in a pattern of sympatric ecological divergence unmatched among mammals.

The marine zone is organized here into three sub-zones: Intertidal/Nearshore, Shallow Marine (continental shelf), and Deep Marine (continental slope and beyond).

**Community Dynamics:**

The intertidal and nearshore community is dominated by pinnipeds — harbor seals, sea lions, and the sea otter. Harbor seals are the most abundant marine mammal in PNW waters (~30,000-40,000 in WA alone), occupying virtually every accessible shoreline, estuary, and rocky reef. Their haul-out sites are ecologically important interfaces where marine and terrestrial food webs converge — eagles scavenge afterbirth and pup carcasses, Bigg's orcas patrol haul-out perimeters, and concentrated pinniped fecal material fertilizes intertidal communities.

The sea otter is the keystone engineer of the nearshore community. By consuming sea urchins (*Strongylocentrotus* spp.), otters prevent urchin overgrazing of kelp forests. Kelp forests, in turn, provide habitat structure for fish, invertebrates, and seabirds (AVI cross-link). The otter-urchin-kelp trophic cascade is one of ecology's most thoroughly documented examples of top-down ecosystem control. Sea otters were hunted to near-extinction during the maritime fur trade (fewer than 2,000 remained globally by 1911). Reintroduced to the Washington outer coast (Olympic Peninsula) in 1969-1970 from Amchitka Island, Alaska, the WA population has grown to approximately 2,000-2,500 and is expanding southward along the coast. Where otters have recolonized, kelp forests have recovered dramatically.

River otters bridge the freshwater-marine boundary, foraging in coastal tidepools, estuaries, and nearshore waters while maintaining freshwater stream territories. Coastal river otters consume marine fish, crabs, and invertebrates in addition to their freshwater prey base, functionally extending the semi-aquatic predator guild into the marine zone.

**Predator-Prey:**

The dominant predator-prey dynamic in the nearshore is Bigg's killer whale predation on pinnipeds. Bigg's orcas patrol haul-out areas, hunting harbor seals, harbor porpoises, and sea lions with stealth tactics — traveling in near-silence to avoid alerting acoustically sensitive prey. Harbor seal kills are brief, violent pursuits; Steller sea lion hunts can be prolonged coordinated group efforts. This Bigg's-pinniped predation is a natural regulatory mechanism — as Bigg's populations have doubled since the 1990s (recovering from historical exploitation), harbor seal populations in some areas have stabilized or slightly declined, suggesting the return of top-down predation pressure to the nearshore ecosystem.

**Community Dynamics:**

The shallow marine community is organized around the Chinook salmon economy. Southern Resident killer whales (73 individuals, ESA Endangered) are Chinook specialists — approximately 80% of their identified prey is Chinook salmon aged 3-5 years, and they follow salmon runs from the Fraser River to the Columbia and Puget Sound stocks. This dietary specialization creates a conservation dependency connecting virtually every freshwater habitat decision in the PNW to the survival of a marine mammal: dam removal, hatchery reform, water allocation, and urban stormwater management all affect how many Chinook are available to Southern Residents. No other marine mammal conservation challenge in North America is so deeply entangled with terrestrial land use.

The asymmetry between Southern Resident and Bigg's killer whale population trajectories is the shallow marine zone's most instructive ecological story. Bigg's feed on pinniped populations that are increasing (protected by MMPA since 1972), and their numbers have roughly doubled since the 1990s. Southern Residents feed on Chinook populations that are declining (impacted by dams, habitat loss, climate change), and their numbers continue to fall. The same regulatory framework (MMPA) that protected pinnipeds from hunting also indirectly benefited the predator of those pinnipeds — while the prey base of the fish-eating ecotype deteriorated under entirely different management regimes. This divergence illustrates how ecological specialization determines vulnerability: generalists (Bigg's, feeding on whatever marine mammals are available) track recovering prey; specialists (Residents, feeding only on Chinook) track declining prey.

Humpback whales, recovering since the cessation of commercial whaling, have returned to the Salish Sea in increasing numbers (396 individuals catalogued by 2022). Their bubble-net feeding on krill and herring, the Pacific Coast Feeding Group of approximately 200-250 gray whales that do not complete the full Arctic migration, and the year-round presence of minke whales in the San Juan Islands add additional cetacean trophic pathways to the shallow marine community. Humpback whale fecal plumes fertilize surface waters, supporting phytoplankton that feeds the base of the marine food web — a nutrient-cycling role analogous to bears' transport of salmon-derived nitrogen into forests.

The deep marine community is the least observed and least understood mammalian assemblage in the PNW. Species here are known primarily from strandings, acoustic detections, and rare at-sea observations. The sperm whale — the largest toothed whale, with males reaching 59 feet — hunts deep-water squid including giant squid (*Architeuthis*) in submarine canyons off the WA/OR outer coast, diving routinely to 3,000+ feet. Beaked whales (Baird's, Cuvier's, Stejneger's) are canyon specialists that may dive to extraordinary depths (Cuvier's beaked whale holds the record for the deepest mammalian dive: 9,816 feet, sustained for 222 minutes).

Offshore killer whales — the least-studied ecotype — are believed to feed primarily on sharks (Pacific sleeper shark, blue shark), explaining their heavily worn teeth (dermal denticles in shark skin are abrasive) and rounded dorsal fin tips. They travel in large groups (25-75+), suggesting a social system adapted to the patchy distribution of shark prey on the continental shelf.

The baleen whales of the deep marine — fin, blue, sei, right — are all ESA-listed legacies of commercial whaling that reduced their populations by 80-99%. Recovery has been slow; generation times of 30+ years and single-calf reproduction limit population growth rates. The North Pacific right whale (<30 individuals in the eastern stock) is among the most endangered large whales on Earth.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Vessel noise and Southern Residents:** The Salish Sea's heavy vessel traffic (commercial shipping, ferries, recreational boats, whale-watching) creates chronic acoustic interference with SRKW echolocation and communication. Vessels within 400 meters reduce foraging activity by approximately 25%. The "Lombard effect" — whales increasing call amplitude to compensate — is energetically costly.

2. **Vessel strikes:** The leading cause of mortality for recovering humpback whale populations. Fin whales and blue whales are also vulnerable. As whale populations increase in the busy Salish Sea shipping corridor, strike risk rises as a density-dependent threat.

3. **Fishing gear entanglement:** Crab pot lines and gillnets entangle humpback whales, gray whales, and other large cetaceans. Entanglement injuries can be fatal or result in chronic debilitation. Gear modifications (breakaway lines, ropeless gear) are under development.

4. **Pinniped-salmon conflict:** California sea lions and harbor seals intercepting ESA-listed salmon at fish ladders (notably Bonneville Dam) create an MMPA-versus-ESA management conflict. Congressional authorization for lethal removal of individually identified pinnipeds at the dam (Section 120) represents one of the few exceptions to MMPA protections.

5. **Oil spill vulnerability:** The sea otter's dense fur — the insulating layer that replaces the blubber of other marine mammals — is catastrophically vulnerable to oil contamination. A single oil spill on the WA outer coast could devastate the recovering population.

## Zone 7: East-Side Steppe / Shrubland

### Community Identity

East of the Cascade crest, the landscape transforms. Maritime moisture gives way to continental aridity, forest yields to sagebrush and bunchgrass, and the mammalian community shifts accordingly. This is the PNW's dryland — the Columbia Basin, the Blue Mountains' foothill steppe, the Owyhee canyonlands, Hart Mountain, and the great volcanic plateaus of central Oregon. Annual precipitation drops below 15 inches (in some basins, below 8 inches). Temperatures swing from -20 degrees F in winter to 105 degrees F in summer. The mammals here are adapted to extremes: burrowing to escape heat and cold, foraging nocturnally, extracting moisture from prey, and migrating seasonally between elevation bands.

This zone hosts species found nowhere else in the PNW: pronghorn, pygmy rabbit, kit fox, American badger, white-tailed jackrabbit. It is also the zone where the PNW's mammalian community most closely resembles the Great Basin and Great Plains faunas — a reminder that the Cascade crest is a biogeographic barrier as significant for mammals as it is for climate and vegetation.

### Predator-Prey Relationships

The east-side predator community lacks wolves in most of the zone (though wolves are recolonizing northeast WA and the Blue Mountains), making cougar and coyote the dominant predators. Cougars prey primarily on mule deer, using canyon topography for ambush. Coyotes are the ubiquitous mesopredator, controlling jackrabbit, cottontail, and ground squirrel populations. Where wolves return, coyote populations are expected to decline, benefiting ground-nesting birds and pronghorn fawns (coyotes are the primary predator of pronghorn fawns).

The American badger is the steppe's distinctive predator — a powerful fossorial hunter that excavates ground squirrels, pocket gophers, and kangaroo rats from their burrows. Badger-coyote cooperative hunting is documented: badgers dig rodents from below while coyotes wait at escape burrows above, increasing the combined capture success of both species. This is one of the few documented examples of interspecific cooperative predation among carnivores.

The northern grasshopper mouse — the "werewolf mouse" — is a carnivorous rodent that hunts grasshoppers, beetles, scorpions, and other small rodents (including deer mice) in the nocturnal steppe. Its territorial howl, scorpion venom resistance, and predatory hunting style are unique among North American rodents.

Golden eagles (AVI cross-link) are the primary raptor predators in the steppe, taking jackrabbits, ground squirrels, pronghorn fawns, and occasionally bighorn sheep lambs in canyon country.

### Interspecific Competition and Niche Partitioning

The steppe small-mammal community partitions habitat along a moisture-substrate gradient:

- **Sagebrush vole:** Obligate sagebrush associate, burrows beneath sagebrush canopy
- **Kangaroo rat:** Sandy or loose soils in open areas, nocturnal bipedal granivore
- **Pocket mouse:** Sandy soils, smaller and more heat-tolerant than kangaroo rat
- **Northern pocket gopher:** Below-ground, any soil type with sufficient depth
- **Deer mouse:** Generalist, all substrates
- **Canyon mouse:** Rock specialist, confined to rimrock and lava flow

This fine-grained partitioning allows 6-8 rodent species to coexist in a single sagebrush-steppe community. Competitive interactions are modulated by soil type, vegetation structure, and the nocturnal/diurnal divide.

The pygmy rabbit (*Brachylagus idahoensis*) is the most range-restricted and conservation-critical mammal in the zone. The Columbia Basin population — the only ESA-listed population — is critically endangered, with fewer than 100 animals in the early 2000s (though augmented by captive-bred releases). Pygmy rabbits are sagebrush obligates: they eat sagebrush, shelter beneath sagebrush, and cannot persist in converted grassland. Their decline mirrors the decline of the sagebrush steppe itself — an ecosystem losing approximately 1% of its area per year to wildfire, invasive annual grasses (cheatgrass), agricultural conversion, and energy development.

### Seasonal Movements

- **Mule deer migration:** Mule deer in the steppe make some of the longest seasonal migrations of any PNW mammal — 50-100+ miles between winter range in low-elevation sagebrush and summer range in montane/alpine meadows. Migration corridors are traditional, matrilineally transmitted, and increasingly fragmented by highways, fencing, and development.
- **Pronghorn winter aggregation:** Pronghorn concentrate on low-elevation winter range in groups of 50-200+, dispersing to individual territories in spring. Pronghorn cannot jump standard barbed-wire fences and must pass under the bottom wire — wildlife-friendly fencing with raised bottom wire is critical for seasonal movement.
- **Ground squirrel estivation/hibernation:** Townsend's ground squirrels and Belding's ground squirrels estivate (summer dormancy) during the hottest weeks and hibernate through winter — active for only 4-5 months of the year. This creates a seasonal pulse in prey availability for eagles, hawks, badgers, and coyotes.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Sagebrush loss:** The east-side steppe is losing habitat to a cascade of interacting threats: wildfire frequency is increasing as cheatgrass and other invasive annuals create continuous fine fuel where bunchgrass communities once had fire-resistant gaps. Following fire, cheatgrass recolonizes faster than native plants, creating a grass-fire cycle that converts sagebrush steppe to annual grassland. Sagebrush takes 30-80 years to reestablish after fire. Species that depend on mature sagebrush (pygmy rabbit, sagebrush vole, sage-grouse — AVI cross-link) cannot persist in the converted landscape.

2. **Fencing and migration corridors:** Standard barbed-wire fencing on rangeland is a barrier to pronghorn (cannot jump), and a mortality source for mule deer and elk (entanglement, wire cuts). Wildlife-friendly fencing — smooth bottom wire raised 18 inches, top wire no higher than 42 inches — allows pronghorn to pass under while ungulates jump over. Retrofitting thousands of miles of fencing across the east-side steppe is a multi-agency conservation priority.

3. **Wind energy development:** Wind farms on east-side ridgelines create direct bat mortality (blade strikes) and habitat fragmentation for steppe mammals. Hoary bats are the bat species most killed at wind turbines nationally. Siting and curtailment strategies can reduce but not eliminate mortality.

4. **Bighorn sheep-domestic sheep conflict:** Contact between bighorn sheep and domestic sheep or goats transmits pneumonia pathogens (*Mycoplasma ovipneumoniae*, *Mannheimia haemolytica*) that can kill 50-90% of a bighorn herd. Maintaining separation between domestic and wild herds — including retiring or modifying federal grazing allotments near bighorn range — is the single most important management action for bighorn conservation.

## Zone 8: Fossorial / Subterranean (Cross-Cutting)

### Community Identity

Zone 8 is unique in this schema — it is not defined by elevation or geographic position but by habitat dimension. The fossorial community operates below ground surface, in the dark matrix of soil, root network, and tunnel system that underlies every other zone. This is the invisible ecosystem: pocket gophers turning over tons of soil per hectare per year, moles aerating prairie soils, shrews hunting invertebrates through root tangles, and the mycorrhizal networks that connect these below-ground engineers to the forest above. The fossorial zone is cross-cutting because its species occur at every elevation, from sea-level prairies to alpine meadows, unified not by climate or vegetation but by their adaptation to subterranean life.

The ecological significance of fossorial mammals is disproportionate to their visibility. Pocket gopher burrowing aerates soil, buries organic matter, increases water infiltration, and creates the disturbed microsites that many native plants require for germination. Mole tunneling redistributes nutrients, controls root-feeding insect larvae, and creates habitat structure for amphibians and invertebrates. Shrews — the metabolic furnaces of the below-ground food web — consume billions of invertebrates per year across the PNW, regulating soil arthropod populations from within.

### Ecological Interactions

**Pocket Gophers as Soil Engineers**

Pocket gophers (genus *Thomomys*, 5 species in the PNW) are the dominant below-ground engineers. A single gopher moves approximately 1.5-3 tons of soil per hectare per year through its burrowing activity. This tunneling aerates compacted soils, buries surface organic matter (accelerating decomposition and nutrient cycling), increases water infiltration rates, and creates the microsites of disturbed bare soil that many native prairie plants — including rare wildflowers dependent on the Mazama pocket gopher's Puget lowland prairie habitat — require for seed germination.

Gopher mounds — the conspicuous piles of loose soil pushed to the surface — function as colonization sites for early-successional plants. In alpine meadows, northern pocket gopher mounds support plant communities distinct from the surrounding undisturbed meadow, increasing overall habitat heterogeneity. The spatial pattern of gopher activity — patches of disturbed soil interspersed with intact vegetation — creates a mosaic that increases beta diversity (between-patch variation) across the landscape.

The five PNW *Thomomys* species partition habitat by soil type, elevation, and geographic region:

- **Mazama pocket gopher:** Glacial outwash prairies, Puget lowlands (ESA Threatened)
- **Camas pocket gopher:** Willamette Valley agricultural soils (OR endemic, largest species)
- **Northern pocket gopher:** Montane meadows and alpine, east-side steppe
- **Botta's pocket gopher:** Arid soils of southern and eastern Oregon
- **Western pocket gopher:** Mixed distribution, foothill and valley soils

This species-level partitioning means that virtually every soil type in the PNW has a pocket gopher engineer maintaining its structure and function.

**Moles as Complementary Engineers**

Moles (family Talpidae) complement pocket gophers as soil engineers but operate at a different scale and depth. Townsend's mole — the largest mole in North America at 130-170 g — creates deep permanent tunnels (8-18 inches below surface) and shallow surface runs. Deep tunnels aerate heavy clay soils of the Willamette and Puget lowlands; surface runs consume beetle larvae, wireworms, and other root-feeding pests. Mole tunneling activity is estimated to turn over 20-40% of topsoil in agricultural fields over a 10-year period.

The American shrew-mole (*Neurotrichus gibbsii*) is a PNW endemic — the sole living member of its genus, found only from southern BC to northwestern California. At 9-11 g, it is the smallest mole in North America, occupying the niche between true moles and shrews. Unlike other moles, it is semi-fossorial — active both below ground and on the surface, foraging in leaf litter, moss, and shallow soil for invertebrates. Its restriction to moist Pacific coastal forests makes it an indicator of forest-floor moisture and structural complexity.

**Shrew Community: Metabolic Fire in the Soil**

PNW shrews (genus *Sorex*, 10+ species) are the most metabolically intense mammals on Earth relative to body size. A vagrant shrew's heart beats 800-1,200 times per minute. Its metabolic rate per gram of tissue exceeds any other mammal, requiring consumption of 80-120% of body weight in invertebrates every 24 hours. Starvation is never more than a few hours away. This metabolic intensity drives enormous insect and invertebrate consumption: collectively, PNW shrews may consume millions of tons of soil invertebrates annually, regulating beetle, fly, spider, centipede, snail, and earthworm populations across every terrestrial habitat.

Shrew species partition habitat along moisture and elevation gradients:

- **Vagrant shrew:** Generalist, all moist habitats, sea level to alpine — the "deer mouse of shrews"
- **Trowbridge's shrew:** Old-growth forest floor, coarse woody debris dependent
- **Pacific shrew:** Riparian corridors, wet forest understory (OR)
- **Pacific water shrew:** Semi-aquatic, headwater streams
- **Montane shrew:** Montane conifer forest, mid-elevation
- **Masked shrew:** East-side forests, montane meadows
- **Merriam's shrew:** Dry steppe, sagebrush — the only arid-adapted PNW shrew
- **Preble's shrew:** Arid shrubland, rare and poorly known

This fine-grained partitioning by moisture allows 5-8 shrew species to coexist in a single watershed, with minimal direct competition.

### Human-Wildlife Interface

1. **Prairie loss and pocket gophers:** The Mazama pocket gopher's ESA listing reflects the near-total loss of Puget lowland prairie — less than 3% of historical extent remains, fragmented by development, agriculture, and Douglas-fir encroachment following fire suppression. Pocket gopher conservation requires prairie restoration: prescribed fire, invasive species control, and protection of remaining prairie fragments.

2. **Mole-lawn conflict:** Townsend's mole and coast mole burrowing in lawns, golf courses, and agricultural fields is the most common source of homeowner wildlife complaints in western WA and OR. Lethal trapping is legal and widely practiced. No threatened species are involved, but the management tension illustrates the general challenge of coexisting with subterranean engineers whose activity is ecologically beneficial but aesthetically unwelcome.

3. **Soil compaction and engineered landscapes:** Urban development, agricultural tillage, and soil compaction from heavy equipment degrade the below-ground habitat structure that moles, gophers, and shrews require. In compacted soils, burrowing mammals cannot maintain tunnel systems, and invertebrate prey populations decline. Soil health — the physical, chemical, and biological integrity of the soil matrix — is the foundational conservation issue for the fossorial community.

## Cross-Zone Synthesis: Connectivity, Movement, and the Integrated PNW Mammalian System

### Elevation as a Gradient, Not a Boundary

The eight zones treated separately above are, in ecological reality, connected by continuous gradients of temperature, precipitation, vegetation, and soil — and by the mammals that move across them. No zone is an island. Alpine wolverines descend to montane forest for cached food. Montane bears ascend to alpine for berries and moths. Riparian beavers reshape hydrology across lowland and montane zones simultaneously. Marine-derived salmon nutrients, transported by bears and eagles, fertilize forests hundreds of meters from any stream. The PNW mammalian system is a single integrated network organized vertically by elevation and horizontally by the dendritic pattern of watersheds that connect every ridge to the sea.

### Migration Corridors as Conservation Units

The mammals that move between zones — mule deer, elk, pronghorn, wolverine, grizzly bear — depend on corridors that are themselves habitat. A mule deer migration corridor is not empty space between summer and winter range; it is a sequence of stopover habitats where animals rest, forage, and avoid predators during multi-week journeys. Corridor degradation (highway construction, fencing, exurban development, energy infrastructure) does not merely inconvenience migrants — it severs the connection between seasonal habitats, fragmenting populations and undermining the gene flow that maintains genetic health. The PNW's major migration corridors — including routes through the Blue Mountains, the east Cascades, and the Columbia Basin — are increasingly recognized as conservation priorities on par with the habitat endpoints they connect.

### Climate Change as Community Reorganizer

Climate change is not simply pushing species uphill. It is reshuffling competitive relationships (bobcat replacing lynx), disrupting temporal synchrony (snowshoe hare molt timing mismatching snowpack duration), altering prey availability (Chinook decline affecting orcas), and compressing specialist habitat against topographic ceilings (pika, wolverine, mountain goat). The species that will persist are the generalists — deer mice, coyotes, black bears, raccoons — adapted to variable conditions. The species at risk are the specialists — pika, wolverine, lynx, pygmy rabbit, Southern Resident orca — whose ecological requirements are narrowly defined by conditions that are changing faster than evolution can track.

### The Fossorial Foundation

Beneath every zone lies Zone 8 — the soil matrix engineered by pocket gophers, moles, and shrews, colonized by the mycorrhizal fungi that flying squirrels and deer mice disperse, and sustained by the detrital food web that begins with leaf fall and ends with beetle larvae consumed by a shrew whose heart beats a thousand times a minute. The below-ground community is the PNW's most underappreciated ecosystem service provider. Without soil engineers, alpine meadows compact and lose their wildflowers. Without mycorrhizal dispersers, conifers cannot acquire phosphorus. Without shrews, soil invertebrate populations would escape the top-down regulation that maintains community balance. The fossorial foundation is invisible from the trail, but remove it and the forest above begins to fail.

### The Predator-Prey Architecture

The PNW's mammalian food web is organized into four functional tiers that span all zones:

**Tier 1 — Apex predators (wolf, cougar, grizzly, orca):** Regulate ungulate and marine mammal populations; create trophic cascades that propagate through vegetation and fish communities. Present in montane, alpine, riparian, and marine zones. Absent from most lowland and urban zones due to human intolerance.

**Tier 2 — Mesopredators (coyote, bobcat, mink, marten, fisher, badger):** Control small mammal, lagomorph, and ground-nesting bird populations. Their abundance is inversely regulated by Tier 1 — mesopredator release in the absence of apex predators drives elevated nest predation and small-mammal suppression.

**Tier 3 — Small-mammal prey base (voles, mice, chipmunks, squirrels, shrews, hares):** The energetic foundation of the terrestrial food web. Their collective biomass exceeds that of all other mammalian tiers combined. Population fluctuations (vole cycles, hare cycles) propagate upward through predator communities.

**Tier 4 — Ecosystem engineers (beaver, pocket gopher, sea otter, mole):** Modify physical habitat in ways that benefit entire communities. Beaver dams create wetlands. Gopher burrowing structures soil. Sea otter foraging maintains kelp forests. Their engineering effects cross trophic levels and cascade through food webs in ways that predator-prey interactions alone cannot.

This four-tier architecture is the skeleton of the PNW mammalian system. Understand it, and the 175+ species documented across these modules become not a list but a network — connected by the flows of energy, nutrients, information, and evolutionary history that make the Pacific Northwest one of the most ecologically consequential bioregions on the continent.
