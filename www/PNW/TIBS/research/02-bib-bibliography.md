## 2. Priority-Annotated Core Works

Six works of highest significance to this study, annotated in detail.

### 2.1 Animal Speak (1993)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 1993a]
**Full title:** *Animal Speak: The Spiritual & Magical Powers of Creatures Great and Small*
**Publisher:** Llewellyn Publications, 1993
**ISBN:** 0-87542-028-1
**Sales:** Approximately 500,000 copies sold between 1993 and 2009 [Llewellyn-Catalog]
**Priority:** 1 (highest)

*Animal Speak* is the centerpiece of Andrews' career, the defining text of the popular animal totem genre, and the primary subject of this research study. At over 400 pages, it is structured in two major parts: Part I establishes the conceptual framework (totems, power animals, shapeshifting, signs and omens), and Part II provides the totem dictionary -- a species-by-species reference covering 100+ animals organized taxonomically.

The book's success was driven by its unique combination of three qualities that had not previously been united in a single volume: (1) genuine natural history content grounded in Andrews' raptor rehabilitation experience at Brukner Nature Center and Aullwood Audubon Center [Brukner-NC; Aullwood-AC], (2) extensive cross-cultural mythological scholarship drawing on Celtic, Egyptian, Greek, Hindu, Buddhist, and (controversially) various Indigenous traditions, and (3) a warm, accessible, pedagogically sophisticated writing style shaped by Andrews' decade of public school teaching.

The totem dictionary format -- keynote, cycle of power, natural history, mythology, spiritual significance, reflective questions -- became the template for every subsequent animal totem book published in the New Age category. Andrews did not just write a bestseller; he defined a genre's standard format [Llewellyn-Catalog].

**[SENSITIVITY: MODERATE]** The book draws the concept of "power animals" from a lineage that traces through Michael Harner [Harner 1980] back to Coast Salish guardian spirit tradition [Jilek 1982]. Andrews does not explicitly acknowledge this Coast Salish origin in the text. His use of "totem" also carries cultural weight for nations (Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian) for whom totem poles are hereditary clan crests with specific legal and ceremonial significance -- a meaning quite different from Andrews' "personal spiritual ally" usage [see CRT-3].

The book has been continuously in print since 1993 and remains Llewellyn's strongest backlist title in the animal spirituality category. Its Goodreads rating of 4.29/5 with 564 ratings [Goodreads-AS] reflects sustained reader engagement more than three decades after publication.

### 2.2 Animal Wise (1999)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 1999]
**Full title:** *Animal Wise: Understanding the Language of Animal Messengers and Companions*
**Publisher:** Dragonhawk Publishing, 1999
**ISBN:** 1-888767-34-3
**Priority:** 1

*Animal Wise* is the companion volume to *Animal Speak*, published six years later through Andrews' own Dragonhawk Publishing imprint rather than through Llewellyn. The shift to self-publishing reflected Andrews' growing independence and the commercial leverage that *Animal Speak*'s success provided.

Where *Animal Speak* establishes the framework and provides the initial totem dictionary, *Animal Wise* deepens the animal communication dimension. The book focuses on what Andrews calls "the language of animals" -- the idea that animal behavior itself constitutes a communicative system that humans can learn to read through sustained, attentive observation. Additional species are covered beyond the *Animal Speak* dictionary, with particular emphasis on insects, arachnids, and aquatic creatures that received shorter treatment in the first book.

*Animal Wise* also introduces more sophisticated meditation and journeying techniques than *Animal Speak*, reflecting Andrews' six additional years of workshop facilitation and healing practice between the two books. The guided meditations are more detailed, the visualization scripts more elaborate, and the integration exercises more structured.

The book is typically purchased by readers who have already worked through *Animal Speak* and want to go deeper. It does not duplicate the first book's content but extends it, making the two volumes a complementary set rather than competitors.

### 2.3 Animal-Speak Pocket Guide (2015)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 2002a]
**Full title:** *Animal-Speak Pocket Guide*
**Publisher:** Llewellyn Publications, 2015 (posthumous)
**ISBN:** 978-0-7387-3540-2
**Priority:** 2

Published six years after Andrews' death, the *Pocket Guide* is a condensed field reference compiled from Andrews' existing animal totem material. It distills the totem dictionary format into brief entries suitable for quick reference -- the kind of book a reader can carry on a hike and consult when encountering an animal, rather than the 400+ page reference volume that *Animal Speak* represents.

The *Pocket Guide* does not contain new material. It is a posthumous compilation and condensation of Andrews' previously published species entries, reorganized for portability. Its significance lies in its commercial function: it introduced Andrews' work to a new generation of readers who discovered it after his death, and it served as an accessible entry point for readers who found the full *Animal Speak* volume intimidating.

The existence of the *Pocket Guide* as a viable commercial product six years after Andrews' death testifies to the enduring demand for his animal totem framework. Few New Age authors maintain commercial viability this far beyond their deaths.

### 2.4 The Healer's Manual (1993)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 1993b]
**Full title:** *The Healer's Manual: A Beginner's Guide to Energy Healing*
**Publisher:** Llewellyn Publications, 1993
**ISBN:** 0-87542-007-9
**Priority:** 2

*The Healer's Manual* is Andrews' core healing text, published the same year as *Animal Speak* but addressing a different domain of his practice. Where *Animal Speak* focuses on the spiritual significance of animals, *The Healer's Manual* addresses the practitioner's healing toolkit: the modalities Andrews used in his individual therapy sessions.

The book covers five primary healing modalities in detail:

| Modality | Content |
|----------|---------|
| **Color therapy** | Chromotherapy principles; color-chakra correspondences; use of colored light, fabric, and visualization for energetic healing |
| **Sound healing** | Toning, vowel vibration, singing bowls, bells; overlaps with *Sacred Sounds* but focused on clinical application |
| **Fragrance/aromatherapy** | Essential oils; scent-emotion correspondences; practical use of aromatherapy in healing sessions |
| **Herbal healing** | Herbal preparations as energetic medicine; not pharmacological herbalism but the use of herbs as vibrational allies |
| **Gemstone/crystal healing** | Crystal properties; gemstone-chakra correspondences; practical layouts and applications |

The book is structured as a practitioner's manual rather than a reference work: it teaches the reader how to conduct healing sessions using these modalities, with step-by-step protocols, case examples, and safety guidelines. Andrews' certifications in basic hypnosis and acupressure [Andrews-Wiki] inform the hands-on, clinically grounded tone.

*The Healer's Manual* is the most concrete evidence in Andrews' bibliography that he was not merely an author and lecturer but a practicing healer who worked with individual clients using multimodal energy therapies. The book's specificity -- particular gemstone placements, specific toning frequencies, detailed color visualization scripts -- reflects lived clinical experience.

### 2.5 Sacred Sounds (1992)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 1992]
**Full title:** *Sacred Sounds: Transformation through Music and Word*
**Publisher:** Llewellyn Publications, 1992
**ISBN:** 0-87542-018-4
**Priority:** 2

*Sacred Sounds* is Andrews' dedicated sound healing text, published the year before *Animal Speak* and representing one of his deepest areas of expertise. Andrews trained as a pianist from age 12 and was an accomplished multi-instrumentalist (Celtic harp, bamboo flute, shaman rattles, Tibetan bells, Tibetan singing bowls, quartz crystal bowls) [Andrews-Wiki; see BIO-1.6]. This book is the theoretical and practical foundation for his sound-based healing work.

The book's major content areas:

| Topic | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Esoteric toning** | The use of sustained vocal sounds for healing, meditation, and energetic transformation. Andrews presents specific tones and vowel sounds as carrying distinct vibrational qualities |
| **Chakra-vowel correspondences** | Systematic mapping of vowel sounds to the seven primary chakras. Each chakra is associated with a specific vowel, tone, and vibrational quality |
| **Vowel vibration therapy** | Therapeutic application of vowel toning: the practitioner produces specific vowel sounds directed at or in proximity to the client's body to influence energetic balance |
| **Musical intervals** | Specific musical intervals (octave, fifth, fourth, third) as carrying distinct healing properties. Andrews draws on Pythagorean music theory and medieval European musical philosophy |
| **Tibetan influences** | Tibetan singing bowl technique, overtone chanting, bell practice. Andrews studied Tibetan Buddhist sound practices and incorporated them into his method |
| **Instrument-specific healing** | Practical protocols for using each instrument (crystal bowls, singing bowls, rattles, drums, flutes) in healing sessions |

*Sacred Sounds* is the most technically sophisticated book in Andrews' catalog. Its treatment of overtone harmonics, vibrational resonance, and the physics of sound production reflects genuine musical training rather than casual spiritual enthusiasm. The book stands at the intersection of music theory, physics, and esoteric tradition -- a characteristic Andrews combination of empirical grounding and spiritual interpretation.

### 2.6 Simplified Qabala Magic (1987)

**Citation key:** [Andrews 1987]
**Full title:** *Simplified Qabala Magic*
**Publisher:** Llewellyn Publications, 1987 (revised edition 2003)
**ISBN:** 0-7387-0394-0 (2003 revised edition)
**Priority:** 2

Andrews' first published book, *Simplified Qabala Magic* is the Kabbalistic foundation stone of his entire career. Published six years before *Animal Speak*, it reveals that Andrews entered metaphysical publishing not through animal mysticism but through Western esoteric tradition -- specifically, the practical Kabbalah as transmitted through the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and its derivatives.

The book covers:

| Topic | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Tree of Life basics** | The ten Sephiroth, twenty-two paths, and the triadic structure of the Tree as a map of consciousness |
| **Pathworking** | Guided meditation journeys along the paths connecting the Sephiroth; specific visualization scripts for each of the twenty-two paths |
| **Middle Pillar exercise** | The Golden Dawn energy meditation technique: sequential activation of five energy centers along the body's central axis (Kether, Da'ath, Tiphareth, Yesod, Malkuth) |
| **Hebrew letter meditation** | Each of the twenty-two Hebrew letters as a meditative focus with specific symbolic, numerical, and vibrational correspondences |
| **Correspondence tables** | Systematic mapping of colors, planets, elements, angelic names, divine names, and other attributes to each Sephirah and path |
| **Practical application** | Daily rituals, protection techniques, and personal development exercises based on Kabbalistic principles |

The significance of this book to the broader Andrews corpus cannot be overstated. The correspondence-table method that Andrews uses in *Animal Speak*'s totem dictionary -- mapping each animal to a set of qualities, seasons, elements, and spiritual functions -- is structurally identical to the Kabbalistic correspondence method taught in this book. Andrews' intellectual DNA is Kabbalistic: he thinks in systematic correspondences, and every book he wrote applies that template to different domains (animals, sounds, colors, crystals, dreams).

The book's final chapter, *Pathworking and the Tree of Life*, was expanded into the full-length manuscript published posthumously under that title in 2010 [Andrews 2010], creating a bookend: Andrews' career opened and closed with the Kabbalah.

## 3. Series Cluster Analysis

Andrews' 41 titles organize naturally into six thematic clusters. This clustering reveals the architecture of his intellectual project -- not a random collection of New Age topics, but a systematic exploration of how humans can perceive and interact with non-physical dimensions of reality through different sensory and cognitive modalities.

### 3.1 Cluster Table

| Cluster | Titles | Core Theme | Primary Modality |
|---------|--------|------------|------------------|
| **Animal Mysticism** | *Animal Speak*, *Animal Wise*, *Pocket Guide*, *Animal-Wise Tarot*, *Nature Speak*, *Animal Speak Workbook*, *Discover Your Spirit Animal*, *The Art of Shapeshifting*, *Beastly Tales*, *Speak with the Animals* | Human-animal spiritual relationship | Observation, meditation, journeying |
| **Llewellyn "How To" Series** | *How to See & Read the Aura*, *How to Uncover Your Past Lives*, *How to Develop & Use Psychic Touch*, *How to Heal with Color*, *How to Meet & Work with Spirit Guides* | Accessible introductions to psychic and healing skills | Varied: visualization, touch, color, meditation |
| **Sound/Healing** | *Sacred Sounds*, *The Healer's Manual*, *Crystal Balls & Crystal Bowls*, *Music Therapy for Non-Musicians* | Sound, vibration, and energy as healing tools | Sound, color, fragrance, gemstones, herbs |
| **Magical Arts** | *Imagick*, *Magickal Dance*, *Dream Alchemy* | Body, image, and dream as magical instruments | Movement, visualization, dream work |
| **Kabbalah** | *Simplified Qabala Magic*, *More Simplified Magic*, *Pathworking and the Tree of Life*, *Walking between the Worlds* | Western esoteric tradition via Tree of Life | Pathworking, meditation, correspondence study |
| **Faerie/Nature Spirit** | *Enchantment of the Faerie Realm*, *How to See Faeries*, *Treasures of the Unicorn*, *Kingdom of the Elves* | Nature spirit perception and communication | Nature observation, meditation, visualization |
| **Young Person's Series** | *Spirits, Ghosts and Guardians* (Vol. 1), *Star Magic* (Vol. 2), *Psychic Power* (Vol. 3), *Faerie Charms* (Vol. 4), *Ancient Powers* (Vol. 5) | Esoteric education for young readers | Age-appropriate exercises, stories, activities |

### 3.2 Cluster Size Distribution

| Cluster | Title Count | % of Catalog | Commercial Anchor |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Animal Mysticism | 10 | 24% | *Animal Speak* (~500K copies) |
| How To | 5 | 12% | *How to See & Read the Aura* |
| Sound/Healing | 4 | 10% | *Sacred Sounds* |

The Animal Mysticism cluster dominates both by title count (10 titles, 24% of catalog) and by commercial significance (*Animal Speak*'s ~500,000 copies dwarf all other titles combined). However, the Kabbalah cluster is structurally foundational -- it provides the correspondence-thinking method that all other clusters apply [see BIO-1.5].

### 3.3 Publisher Distribution

| Publisher | Title Count | Period | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|--------|--------------|
| Llewellyn Publications | ~22 | 1987--2015 | Primary publisher; commercial backbone; posthumous compilations |
| Dragonhawk Publishing | ~19 | 1999--2008 | Andrews' own imprint; greater creative control; smaller distribution |

Andrews began publishing exclusively with Llewellyn (1987--1998), established his own Dragonhawk Publishing imprint in the late 1990s (enabled by *Animal Speak*'s commercial success), and then published concurrently through both houses. Dragonhawk titles typically had smaller print runs and more niche topics (young person's series, advanced animal work). Llewellyn retained the bestsellers and the posthumous publications.

### 4.3 Phase Summary

Andrews' career exhibits a clear five-phase structure with two peak years (1993 and 2003):

1. **Foundation (1987--1991):** Kabbalah entry; early esoteric works. 4 titles in 5 years.
2. **Peak (1992--1993):** Sound healing + the flagship quartet of 1993. 5 titles in 2 years.
3. **Diversification (1996--1999):** "How To" series + return to animal mysticism. 8 titles in 4 years.
4. **Expansion (2000--2003):** Young Person's series, advanced works, self-publishing growth. 12 titles in 4 years.
5. **Late Career (2005--2008):** Reduced output; specialized topics. 7 titles in 4 years.

**Posthumous (2010, 2015):** 2 titles, both returning to foundational themes (Kabbalah, animals).

## 5. Critical Reception

### 5.1 Quantitative Reception

The primary quantitative reception data available is from Goodreads [Goodreads-AS]:

| Metric | Value | Context |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Rating | 4.29 / 5.0 | Above average for the New Age/Metaphysical category |
| Ratings count | 564 | Moderate engagement for a 1993 title |

The 4.29 rating with 564 ratings indicates strong reader satisfaction but does not reach the engagement levels of mainstream bestsellers. This reflects *Animal Speak*'s position as a category leader within a niche (New Age/metaphysical) rather than a crossover hit.

### 5.2 Three Areas of Critique

Critical reception of *Animal Speak* clusters around three recurring themes identified across published reviews, academic commentary, and reader feedback:

#### Critique 1: Bibliographic Basis -- "Rose-Tinted Idealistic Generalising"

The most substantive academic criticism of *Animal Speak* concerns the quality and specificity of its source attributions. Andrews draws on dozens of mythological and cultural traditions but provides limited bibliographic apparatus -- no footnotes, no endnotes, and a relatively brief bibliography [Andrews 1993a]. Critics characterize this as "rose-tinted idealistic generalising" [Goodreads-AS] -- a tendency to present mythological claims (e.g., "In Egyptian mythology, the hawk was sacred to Ra") without specifying which Egyptian texts, which historical period, or which scholarly source supports the claim.

This critique is substantively valid. *Animal Speak* is a popular reference work, not an academic text, and its mythological claims are often presented as received wisdom rather than documented fact. Readers who attempt to verify specific mythological assertions may find them difficult to trace to primary sources.

However, the critique must be contextualized within the genre. *Animal Speak* was published by Llewellyn, a popular metaphysical publisher, not by an academic press. Its intended audience is spiritual practitioners, not scholars. The absence of scholarly apparatus is a genre convention, not a unique failing of Andrews' work. Whether this convention is defensible is a separate question [see CRT-2].

#### Critique 2: Bird Magic and Indigenous Practices -- "Go Try It Yourself"

A more culturally pointed critique focuses on sections of *Animal Speak* where Andrews describes practices associated with Indigenous traditions -- particularly bird-related ceremonial and divinatory practices -- and effectively encourages readers to adopt them without discussion of cultural ownership, ceremonial protocols, or the distinction between practices that are openly shared and practices that are tribally restricted [Goodreads-AS].

**[SENSITIVITY: MODERATE]** This "go try it yourself" approach is the primary vector for cultural appropriation critique. When Andrews describes a practice that originated within a specific tribal context (e.g., Coast Salish guardian spirit work, various nations' bird-related ceremonial practices) and presents it as a universal technique available to any reader, he effectively separates the practice from its cultural matrix -- the community relationships, elder oversight, ceremonial calendars, and place-based knowledge that give it meaning within its originating tradition [see CRT-3].

Andrews' defenders note that he draws from many traditions (not just Indigenous), that he does not claim his framework is "authentic Native American practice," and that the universalist approach is consistent with his philosophical position that all humans share an innate capacity for animal communication. His critics respond that good intentions do not resolve the structural problem of decontextualization -- that a Midwestern author encouraging readers to do practices derived from Coast Salish tradition without naming that tradition or discussing cultural protocols is appropriation regardless of intent.

#### Critique 3: Beginner vs. Depth Tension

The third recurring critique is internal to Andrews' readership rather than externally imposed: the tension between *Animal Speak*'s accessibility (which makes it an excellent beginner's resource) and its depth limitations (which leave advanced practitioners wanting more). The book's totem dictionary entries, while far more detailed than anything previously published, necessarily compress complex mythological and spiritual traditions into 1-3 page summaries. Readers who use *Animal Speak* as an entry point and then seek deeper engagement with, for example, Celtic animal mythology, Norse shapeshifting traditions, or Coast Salish guardian spirit ceremonialism will find the book's treatment of these traditions introductory rather than comprehensive.

*Animal Wise* (1999) [Andrews 1999] addresses this critique partially by offering deeper treatment of some species and more sophisticated meditation techniques. But the fundamental tension between accessibility and depth is structural to the dictionary format itself: any book that covers 100+ species in 400+ pages cannot treat each one exhaustively.

## 6. Dragonhawk Publishing

In the late 1990s, following the commercial success of *Animal Speak*, Andrews established his own publishing imprint, Dragonhawk Publishing, based at his farm in Jackson, Tennessee. Dragonhawk published approximately 19 of Andrews' titles between 1999 and 2008, including several of his most significant works (*Animal Wise*, the *Young Person's School of Magic & Mystery* series, *Nature Speak*, *The Art of Shapeshifting*).

The Dragonhawk imprint gave Andrews several advantages:

| Advantage | Effect |
|-----------|--------|
| **Creative control** | Full authority over content, design, and packaging decisions |
| **Higher royalty percentage** | Self-published titles yield higher per-copy income than traditionally published titles |
| **Niche viability** | Titles with smaller expected audiences (young person's series, specialized animal topics) could be published profitably at lower volume |
| **Speed to market** | No traditional publisher review and production timeline |

The disadvantage was distribution reach: Dragonhawk titles had smaller print runs and less retail visibility than Llewellyn titles. This is why the posthumous publications (*Pathworking and the Tree of Life*, *Animal-Speak Pocket Guide*) were published through Llewellyn rather than Dragonhawk -- Llewellyn's distribution infrastructure ensured wider availability.

Andrews' establishment of Dragonhawk also reflects the self-directed, entrepreneurially independent character that defined his career. He was never a "house author" subordinate to a publisher's editorial direction. He maintained control of his intellectual property and creative vision throughout his career.
