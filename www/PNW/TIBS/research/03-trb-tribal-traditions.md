# Module TRB: Pacific Northwest Native Shamanic Traditions

Research module for the Animal Speak, Sacred Landscapes, and the Living World study. Documents the shamanic and spiritual specialist traditions of five linguistically and culturally distinct Pacific Northwest Coast nations. Every claim is sourced; every nation is treated as a sovereign cultural entity with its own cosmology, ceremonial forms, and territorial relationship.

## 1. Overview: Shamanic Traditions of the Pacific Northwest Coast

The Pacific Northwest Coast — stretching from the Columbia River mouth through Puget Sound, the British Columbia coast, Haida Gwaii, and the Alaska panhandle — is home to some of the most complex and well-documented shamanic traditions in North America. These traditions share certain structural features: the centrality of guardian spirits, the practice of soul retrieval, elaborate healing ceremonies, and the integration of spiritual life with the annual ecological cycle. They also differ profoundly in cosmology, ceremonial form, language, social organization, and the specific role and training of spiritual specialists [Sturtevant 1990].

The academic term "shaman" itself requires immediate qualification. It derives from the Evenki (Tungusic) language of Siberia and entered Western academic discourse through Mircea Eliade's foundational *Shamanism: Archaic Techniques of Ecstasy* [Eliade 1964]. Pacific Northwest nations have their own terms for their spiritual specialists, and the application of "shaman" as a cross-cultural category — while useful for comparative scholarship — erases important distinctions in role, training, authority, and social function [see CRT-3.1]. Throughout this module, nation-specific terminology is used where it is documented in the ethnographic literature.

### 1.1 Structural Commonalities

Despite linguistic and cultural diversity, Pacific Northwest Coast shamanic traditions share a recognizable set of features [Sturtevant 1990], [Jorgensen thesis]:

| Feature | Description | Distribution |
|---------|-------------|--------------|
| Guardian spirit acquisition | Deliberate process of obtaining alliance with a spirit being, often in animal form | All five nations surveyed |
| Soul retrieval | The shaman travels to the Land of the Dead or spirit realms to recover a lost or stolen soul | Coast Salish, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian |
| Illness as spiritual diagnosis | Sickness understood as having supernatural causes alongside natural ones | All five nations surveyed |
| Object extraction | Removal of spiritually implanted foreign objects from a patient's body | Coast Salish, Lower Chinook, Tlingit |
| Trance induction | Drumming, singing, rattling, dancing, fasting, and/or isolation to enter altered states | All five nations surveyed |
| Winter ceremonial cycle | Major ceremonial season coinciding with the darkest months | Coast Salish (primary), Tsimshian, Haida |
| Matrilineal or clan-based spiritual authority | Spiritual roles transmitted through kinship systems | Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian |
| Potlatch as spiritual-economic event | Ceremonial feasting with spiritual validation of status and relationships | All five nations (varying forms) |

### 1.2 Structural Differences

Commonalities should not obscure the profound differences. The Coast Salish guardian spirit ceremonial is a community-scale winter event open to broad participation. Tlingit shamanism was a hereditary specialization within the matrilineal clan system. Haida spiritual authority was inseparable from the monumental art tradition. Chinookan shamanism was centered on the Columbia River trade network and its mythological figures. Tsimshian ceremonial life operated through four named matrilineal clans with distinct crests and prerogatives. To collapse these into a single "Northwest Coast shamanism" would be as misleading as calling Renaissance Florentine painting and Ming Dynasty ceramics "Old World art" [Sturtevant 1990].

## 3. Coast Salish

### 3.1 Territory and Social Organization

The Coast Salish peoples occupy a broad territory encompassing southwestern British Columbia, western Washington around Puget Sound, and parts of the northwestern Oregon coast. This territory includes the traditional lands of the Squamish, Musqueam, Tsleil-Waututh, Snohomish, Duwamish, Puyallup, Nisqually, Lummi, Nooksack, and many other distinct communities. The Coast Salish are not a single "tribe" but a constellation of autonomous communities united by related Salishan languages (Halkomelem, Lushootseed, Straits Salish, and others), shared cultural practices, and extensive kinship networks [Sturtevant 1990].

Social organization was based on extended family households occupying large cedar longhouses. Status was achieved through a combination of hereditary rank and individual spiritual accomplishment. The relationship between inherited social position and personally acquired spiritual power is central to understanding Coast Salish shamanism: a person of any rank could receive a guardian spirit, but the most powerful spirits — and the shaman's vocation — often appeared to those who demonstrated exceptional capacity for spiritual relationship [Jilek 1982].

### 3.2 Cosmology and Spiritual Worldview

The Coast Salish worldview held that the world was populated by spirit beings who existed alongside and within the physical world. Ancestral closeness to these supernatural beings meant that any person could potentially obtain alliance with spirits through intention, training, and self-purification. The barrier between the human world and the spirit world was understood as permeable, particularly during the winter months when the spirits were believed to be most active and accessible [Jilek 1982].

The theology distinguished between "lay" spirits — guardian spirits available to ordinary people, often inherited within family lines — and shaman spirits, which required a stronger calling and a greater capacity for spiritual mediation. This distinction is important: the guardian spirit tradition was broadly participatory, not restricted to an elite class of specialists. The winter ceremonial brought entire communities into relationship with the spirit world, while the shaman's role was to mediate the most difficult cases — illness, soul loss, spiritual crisis — that lay practitioners could not address [Jilek 1982], [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

### 3.3 The Guardian Spirit Ceremonial

The Coast Salish guardian spirit ceremonial is one of the most extensively documented Indigenous spiritual traditions in the Pacific Northwest, due in large part to the ethnographic and psychiatric research of Wolfgang Jilek, whose *Indian Healing: Shamanic Ceremonialism in the Pacific Northwest Today* [Jilek 1982] provides the foundational clinical account.

The guardian spirit ceremonial combined the Spirit Quest tradition of the Plateau bands (involving solitary vision-seeking through fasting, isolation, and purification) with the rich communal ceremonial life of the Northwest Coast cultures. The result was a tradition in which individual spiritual experience was validated, expressed, and integrated within a community ceremonial framework [Jilek 1982].

**The Winter Ceremonial.** The annual winter spirit dance was the central Coast Salish ceremonial event. Held during the darkest months (roughly November through February), it served simultaneously as:

- A renewal of individual guardian spirit relationships through song, dance, and trance
- A community-scale therapeutic program addressing psychological and social distress
- A public validation of spiritual power and social standing
- A vehicle for the transmission of cultural knowledge across generations

Jilek, writing as a psychiatrist, documented the winter ceremonial's therapeutic function with clinical rigor. He observed that participants who entered the ceremonial in states of depression, addiction, anomie, or social disconnection frequently emerged with renewed sense of purpose, stronger social bonds, and measurable improvements in mental health. He characterized the ceremonial as "a carefully structured group therapy program embedded in a spiritual worldview" — functional both on its own terms and by Western clinical criteria [Jilek 1982].

**Guardian Spirits in Animal Form.** The Coast Salish specifically understood guardian spirits that appeared in animal form as conferring particular powers and characteristics upon their human partners. A person whose guardian spirit was Bear might receive qualities of strength and healing. A person whose guardian spirit was Eagle might receive qualities of far-seeing vision and spiritual authority. The specific animal and its associated qualities were revealed through the Spirit Quest and validated through the winter ceremonial [Jilek 1982].

The Coast Salish term for this relationship — guardian spirit in animal form — is the direct historical source for the term "power animal" now common in neo-shamanic literature. [SENSITIVITY: HIGH] This migration of terminology is documented in detail in [see CRT-4.1].

### 3.4 The Shaman's Role

Within the broader guardian spirit tradition, the Coast Salish shaman (spiritual specialist) occupied a distinct role. The shaman was understood to possess more powerful spirits than ordinary people and to have a greater capacity for entering trance states, traveling to the spirit world, and mediating between human and supernatural realms. Key functions included:

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Illness diagnosis | Determining whether sickness had natural or supernatural causes; identifying the specific spiritual agent responsible |
| Foreign object removal | Extracting spiritually implanted objects (often described as small stones, bone fragments, or animal parts) from a patient's body through sucking, blowing, or manual extraction |
| Soul retrieval | Traveling in trance to the Land of the Dead to recover a soul that had departed or been stolen; returning it to the patient's living body |
| Divination | Entering trance to receive information from spirit allies; interpreting signs, dreams, and omens for the community |
| Ceremonial leadership | Directing the winter spirit dance; managing the ceremonial process; ensuring spiritual safety of participants |

[SENSITIVITY: HIGH — The above describes documented academic observations of shamanic function, not instructional detail on how to perform these practices.]

### 3.5 Contemporary Status

Coast Salish spiritual traditions experienced severe disruption through the colonial period (see Section 8 below) but were never fully extinguished. Suzanne Crawford O'Brien's *Coming Full Circle: Spirituality and Wellness among Native Communities in the Pacific Northwest* [Crawford O'Brien 2013] documents how Coast Salish communities in western Washington have maintained and revived spiritual healing traditions alongside twenty-first-century biomedicine.

Crawford O'Brien's research shows that contemporary Coast Salish communities define health through a triadic framework of self, ecology, and community — a model rooted in Indigenous spiritual understanding of the body as embedded in both social and ecological relationships. This framework is not merely "cultural" in the decorative sense; it structures actual health care decisions, treatment protocols, and community wellness programs in living Coast Salish communities today [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

The Indian Shaker Church, founded in 1881 among Salish-speaking peoples, continues to operate in Coast Salish territory. It represents a syncretic tradition that integrated Christian elements (particularly healing through prayer, trembling, and the laying on of hands) with Coast Salish ceremonial healing roles. The Indian Shaker Church helped preserve ceremonial healing practice during the most intense periods of colonial suppression by providing a nominally Christian framework for what were fundamentally Indigenous healing practices [Crawford O'Brien 2013], [Jilek 1982].

## 4. Lower Chinook

### 4.1 Territory and Social Organization

The Lower Chinook (Chinookan peoples) occupied the territory around the mouth of the Columbia River, encompassing parts of what is now coastal Washington and Oregon. They were historically among the most powerful peoples of the Pacific Northwest, not primarily through military dominance but through their control of trade routes. The Columbia River was the great arterial waterway of the Pacific Northwest, and the Chinook controlled its lower reaches. Their influence was so extensive that the Chinook Jargon — a pidgin trade language based partly on Chinookan vocabulary — became the lingua franca of the entire Pacific Northwest fur trade era [Sturtevant 1990].

Chinookan society was organized around extended family villages situated along the river and coast. Social status was tied to wealth, trade relationships, and spiritual power, with elaborate systems of gift exchange and ceremonial obligation binding communities together [Sturtevant 1990].

### 4.2 Cosmology and Spiritual Worldview

Chinookan cosmology featured a rich mythological tradition dominated by two figures: Coyote and Blue Jay. These were not merely "animal characters" in a storytelling tradition; they were understood as primal beings — transformer-tricksters whose actions shaped the world into its current form. Coyote was the central transformer figure, responsible for establishing the physical landscape, the run of the salmon, and the social order. Blue Jay functioned as a boundary-crossing figure, moving between the world of the living and the world of the dead, serving as messenger and sometimes as deceiver [Judson & Miller 1910].

Katharine Berry Judson's *Myths and Legends of the Pacific Northwest* [Judson & Miller 1910], originally published in 1910 and reissued by the University of Nebraska Press with scholarly apparatus by Jay Miller, documents these mythological traditions in detail. The Chinookan cosmological narratives reveal a worldview in which the boundary between human, animal, and spirit was understood as fluid and narratively productive — animals became humans, humans became animals, and the shaman was the specialist who navigated these transformations [Judson & Miller 1910].

### 4.3 The Shaman's Role

The Chinookan shaman functioned as the primary ritual communicator between the human community and the spirit world. Unlike the Coast Salish tradition, where the guardian spirit ceremonial was broadly participatory, Chinookan shamanism appears to have been more concentrated in specialist practitioners. The shaman used a repertoire of ritual technologies including:

- **Dance rattles** — often constructed from deer hooves, shells, or carved wooden forms, used to invoke spirit presence and maintain trance rhythm
- **Canoe spirit paintings** — the great Chinookan canoes bore painted designs that served not merely as decoration but as spiritual identification and protection
- **Singing and chanting** — spirit songs received through vision and trance, unique to each practitioner
- **Herbal knowledge** — the shaman maintained botanical expertise alongside spiritual practice, though the two were not understood as separate domains [Sturtevant 1990]

### 4.4 Animal Spirits in Chinookan Tradition

The role of animal spirits in Chinookan tradition was inseparable from the mythological framework. Animals were not understood as symbols or metaphors for spiritual qualities; they were understood as the original inhabitants of the world, senior to humans, possessing their own societies, languages, and spiritual power. The shaman's ability to communicate with animal spirits was understood as a form of diplomatic relationship — treating with sovereign beings, not commanding lesser ones [Judson & Miller 1910], [Sturtevant 1990].

| Animal Spirit | Role in Chinookan Tradition |
|---|---|
| Coyote | Primary transformer; established the physical and social world; trickster whose failures teach as much as his successes |
| Blue Jay | Boundary-crosser between living and dead; messenger; trickster with access to the Land of the Dead |
| Salmon | The great food gift; salmon were understood as a "people" who chose to offer themselves to humans; the first salmon ceremony honored this gift |
| Eagle | Associated with high vision and spiritual authority; an upper-world power |
| Thunderbird | A being of immense power associated with storms and the deep sky; more feared than sought as a guardian |

### 4.5 Contemporary Status

The Lower Chinook peoples experienced catastrophic population decline through disease (particularly the malaria epidemics of the 1830s) and colonial dispossession. The Chinook Indian Nation has sought but not yet received federal recognition, a political status that affects access to resources for cultural preservation. Despite these challenges, Chinookan cultural traditions — including the mythological tradition documented by Judson — are actively maintained by descendants. The Chinook Jargon, while no longer a living trade language, persists in place names throughout the Pacific Northwest (Tillicum, Skookum, Potlatch, and many others) [Sturtevant 1990].

## 5. Tlingit

### 5.1 Territory and Social Organization

The Tlingit (Lingit) occupy the coastal panhandle of southeast Alaska and parts of northern coastal British Columbia. Their territory encompasses a dramatic landscape of fjords, islands, temperate rainforest, and glacier-fed waterways. Tlingit society is organized into two moieties — Raven and Eagle/Wolf — with each individual belonging to one moiety through matrilineal descent. Each moiety contains numerous clans (naa), and each clan owns specific crests, stories, songs, dances, and ceremonial prerogatives that constitute a form of intellectual and spiritual property [Sturtevant 1990].

This matrilineal clan system is fundamental to understanding Tlingit shamanism. Spiritual authority, like other forms of authority, was transmitted through kinship. A shaman's power was understood as belonging not only to the individual but to the clan, and the death of a shaman triggered specific succession protocols within the kinship system [Sturtevant 1990], [Lyon 1998].

### 5.2 Cosmology and Spiritual Worldview

Raven (Yeil) is the central figure of Tlingit cosmology — the great transformer who brought light to the world by stealing it from a chief who kept it in a box, who established the tides, the rivers, and the social order. Unlike Coyote in Chinookan tradition, Raven in Tlingit cosmology carries a more ambiguous moral valence: he is simultaneously creator, trickster, and culture hero, motivated by hunger, curiosity, and an insatiable desire to transform the world [Sturtevant 1990].

The Tlingit cosmos was understood as layered — an upper world, a middle (human) world, and an underwater/underworld realm. The shaman was the specialist who could travel between these layers, communicating with beings in all three realms. The spirits of animals, ancestors, and natural forces inhabited these layered worlds and could be called upon — or needed to be propitiated — through ceremonial practice [Lyon 1998].

### 5.3 The Shaman's Role and Training

Tlingit shamanism was perhaps the most dramatically specialized of all Pacific Northwest shamanic traditions. The Tlingit shaman (ixt') occupied a position of extraordinary spiritual authority, but also of social separation. Key features documented in the ethnographic literature include:

**Hair as power vessel.** The Tlingit shaman was forbidden to cut his hair. The shaman's long, often matted hair was understood as a physical vessel of spiritual power — a material anchor for the spirits that the shaman had acquired. The deliberate shaving of a shaman's head was understood as destroying his power, a fact that colonial authorities exploited (see Section 8) [Sturtevant 1990], [Lyon 1998].

**Masks and ritual objects.** Tlingit shaman masks are among the most powerful and recognized art forms of the Pacific Northwest. Each mask represented a specific spirit with whom the shaman had a relationship. The shaman might possess multiple masks for different spirits, each used in specific ceremonial contexts. These masks were not representations of spirits; they were understood as the material presence of spirits — wearing the mask was not "pretending to be" a spirit but entering into the spirit's being [Sturtevant 1990].

**Trance and healing.** The Tlingit shaman entered trance through drumming, singing, dancing, and sometimes fasting. In trance, the shaman could diagnose illness (determining whether it was caused by soul loss, spirit attack, or sorcery), travel to the spirit world for soul retrieval, or identify sorcerers responsible for spiritual harm. The shaman's performance was a public ceremonial event, witnessed by the community, with the shaman's body serving as the medium through which spirit beings communicated [Lyon 1998].

**Burial and succession.** Upon death, a Tlingit shaman was not buried in the ordinary manner but placed in a special grave house away from the village. The shaman's masks, rattles, and ceremonial objects were placed with the body. The next shaman in the matrilineal line inherited the predecessor's spiritual relationships and obligations [Sturtevant 1990].

### 5.4 Animal Spirits in Tlingit Tradition

| Animal Spirit | Role in Tlingit Tradition |
|---|---|
| Raven (Yeil) | Central deity and transformer; moiety crest; bringer of light; trickster-creator |
| Eagle | Opposite moiety crest to Raven; associated with clear vision, strength, and aerial sovereignty |
| Wolf | Alternate name for Eagle moiety in some communities; associated with loyalty, hunting skill, and clan solidarity |
| Bear | Powerful spirit associated with healing, strength, and the forest interior; prominent in shaman masks |
| Killer Whale (Orca) | Ruler of the underwater realm; clan crest; associated with family, power, and the sea |
| Frog | Associated with communication between worlds (water and land); appears on shaman's regalia |

### 5.5 Contemporary Status

Tlingit cultural traditions are actively maintained and revived. The Sealaska Heritage Institute in Juneau, Alaska, works to preserve and transmit Tlingit language, art, and cultural practices. Tlingit shaman masks and ceremonial objects held in museums worldwide are subjects of ongoing repatriation efforts. The Tlingit language, while endangered, has active revitalization programs. The moiety and clan system continues to structure Tlingit social life, and ceremonial practices — including the potlatch, which was banned by Canadian and American authorities from the late nineteenth century through the mid-twentieth century — have been revived and continue in contemporary form [Sturtevant 1990].

## 6. Haida

### 6.1 Territory and Social Organization

The Haida occupy Haida Gwaii (formerly called the Queen Charlotte Islands) in British Columbia and the southern portions of Prince of Wales Island in Alaska. Their island territory — surrounded by some of the most productive marine waters in the Pacific — shaped a culture of extraordinary artistic accomplishment and maritime skill. Haida war canoes, carved from single red cedar logs, were the most feared vessels on the Pacific Northwest Coast [Sturtevant 1990].

Haida society was organized into two moieties — Raven and Eagle — with matrilineal descent determining moiety membership, clan affiliation, and social rank. Like the Tlingit (with whom the Haida share significant structural similarities), each Haida clan owned specific crests, songs, dances, and ceremonial prerogatives. The monumental totem poles for which the Haida are most widely known served as heraldic records — visual genealogies declaring clan identity, history, and spiritual relationships [Sturtevant 1990], [Jorgensen thesis].

### 6.2 Cosmology and Spiritual Worldview

Haida cosmology shares the Raven transformer narrative with the Tlingit but develops it within a distinctly Haida framework. The world was understood as layered: Sky World above, the human world, and the World Below (both undersea and underground). Every being — human, animal, spirit — possessed a soul, and the boundaries between categories of being were understood as permeable. Animals were not "lower" beings but different kinds of persons, capable of removing their animal skins to reveal human forms beneath. This understanding of animal personhood is critical to comprehending Haida spiritual life: animals were potential allies, teachers, and ancestors, not resources to be exploited [Sturtevant 1990].

The concept of supernatural power in Haida thought was associated with specific places, beings, and objects. Certain locations — rocky promontories, forest clearings, underwater channels — were understood as sites of intensified spiritual presence. ### 6.3 The Shaman's Role

The Haida shaman (sgaaga) was a specialist in communication with supernatural beings. Like the Tlingit ixt', the Haida shaman's role was hereditary within the matrilineal system, though the calling also required demonstrated spiritual aptitude. The Haida shaman tradition is documented in detail in Grace Mairi McIntyre Jorgensen's comparative thesis at the University of British Columbia [Jorgensen thesis].

Key features of Haida shamanism included:

**Masked performance.** Haida shaman masks are among the most artistically sophisticated objects in the Pacific Northwest. Each mask represented a specific spirit helper, and the shaman's collection of masks — accumulated over a lifetime of spiritual encounters — constituted both a spiritual arsenal and an artistic legacy. The masks were carved by specialist artists (often not the shaman themselves) and were understood as requiring activation through ceremonial use [Jorgensen thesis], [Sturtevant 1990].

**Healing through spiritual diagnosis.** The Haida shaman diagnosed illness by determining which supernatural agent was responsible and what remedy was required. This might involve soul retrieval (traveling in trance to recover a departed soul), extraction of foreign objects, or identification of sorcery. The distinction between benevolent shamanism (healing) and malevolent sorcery was clearly drawn in Haida thought, though the same powers could theoretically be used for either purpose [Sturtevant 1990].

**Potlatch as spiritual-economic event.** The Haida potlatch was not merely a feast or a display of wealth; it was a ceremonial event in which spiritual authority, social rank, and economic redistribution were integrated into a single performance. The potlatch validated clan crests, confirmed successors, honored the dead, and redistributed material wealth — all within a framework that was simultaneously spiritual, political, and economic [Sturtevant 1990].

### 6.4 Totem Poles as Spiritual Genealogy

The Haida monumental totem pole tradition deserves specific attention because it has been widely misunderstood in popular culture. Totem poles are not objects of worship. They are heraldic records — three-dimensional genealogies that declare the clan identity, mythological history, and spiritual relationships of the household that commissioned them. The figures on a totem pole — Raven, Eagle, Bear, Killer Whale, Frog, Thunderbird — represent clan crests, not personal "totem animals" in the sense that Ted Andrews and other neo-shamanic writers use the term [Sturtevant 1990]. [SENSITIVITY: HIGH — This distinction between clan heraldic crests and personal "totem animals" is critical. The two concepts come from different cultural systems and should not be conflated.]

| Pole Type | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| Frontal pole | Attached to the front of a longhouse; declared the clan identity of the household |
| Memorial pole | Erected to honor a deceased chief or important person; validated the successor's claim |
| Mortuary pole | Contained the remains of a high-ranking person; proclaimed their lineage and achievements |
| Shame pole | Erected to publicly shame a person who had failed to fulfill an obligation; a form of social sanction |

### 6.5 Contemporary Status

Haida art and culture are experiencing a powerful renaissance. The Haida Heritage Foundation and the Haida Gwaii Museum at Kay Llnagaay are centers of cultural preservation and transmission. Master carvers such as the late Bill Reid (1920-1998) brought Haida art to international recognition, and a new generation of Haida artists continues the monumental carving tradition. The Haida language, while critically endangered (fewer than 20 fluent speakers by some estimates), is the subject of intensive documentation and revitalization efforts. The Council of the Haida Nation exercises governance over Haida Gwaii through a framework that integrates traditional governance principles with modern political structures [Sturtevant 1990].

## 7. Tsimshian

### 7.1 Territory and Social Organization

The Tsimshian (Ts'msyen) peoples occupy the northwestern coast of British Columbia around the Prince Rupert area and Annette Island in Alaska (where the Metlakatla community was established in 1887). The name Ts'msyen means "inside the Skeena River," reflecting the centrality of the Skeena River system to Tsimshian territory and identity [Sturtevant 1990].

Tsimshian society is organized through four matrilineal clans (pteex): Raven (Ganhada), Eagle (Laxsgiik), Killer Whale (Gispwudwada), and Wolf (Laxgibuu). Clan membership determines marriage rules (exogamy is required — one must marry outside one's own clan), ceremonial roles, territorial rights, and spiritual prerogatives. This four-clan system distinguishes the Tsimshian from the two-moiety systems of the Tlingit and Haida, creating a more complex web of ceremonial obligations and alliances [Sturtevant 1990].

### 7.2 Cosmology and Spiritual Worldview

Tsimshian cosmology shares the Raven transformer narrative with the Tlingit and Haida but situates it within a distinct cultural grammar. Raven (Txamsm) is the central transformer-trickster who brought light, fire, and fresh water to human beings. The Tsimshian Raven cycle — documented extensively in the Smithsonian's *Handbook of North American Indians* [Sturtevant 1990] — includes elaborate episodes in which Raven transforms between human and bird form, steals treasures from supernatural beings, and establishes the natural and social order through a combination of cunning, greed, and creative intelligence.

The Tsimshian cosmos was understood as containing multiple worlds: the sky world (domain of the sun, moon, and celestial beings), the human world, the underwater world (domain of the sea beings and the Killer Whale people), and the forest world (domain of the land animals and certain dangerous spirits). The shaman navigated between these worlds, serving as diplomat, healer, and interpreter [Sturtevant 1990].

### 7.3 The Shaman's Role

Tsimshian shamanism (the practitioner was called a halaayt) was deeply embedded in the clan system. The halaayt's authority derived from both personal spiritual power and clan-based legitimacy. Key functions included healing, divination, weather control (particularly important for a maritime people dependent on fishing), and the conduct of ceremonial performances associated with the potlatch cycle [Sturtevant 1990].

Tsimshian ceremonial art — masks, rattles, robes, and headdresses — served as the material infrastructure of shamanic practice. The elaborate frontlets (headdresses with carved and inlaid designs) worn by Tsimshian chiefs and spiritual leaders are among the most technically sophisticated objects in Pacific Northwest art, combining abalone shell inlay, ermine skin, and painted cedar in compositions of extraordinary aesthetic complexity [Sturtevant 1990].

### 7.4 The Four Clans and Their Spiritual Associations

| Clan | Tsimshian Name | Primary Crest | Spiritual Associations |
|------|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| Raven | Ganhada | Raven | Transformation, cunning, creativity; the bringer of light |
| Eagle | Laxsgiik | Eagle | Vision, strength, aerial sovereignty; guardianship |
| Killer Whale | Gispwudwada | Killer Whale | Power of the sea; family loyalty; the undersea world |
| Wolf | Laxgibuu | Wolf | Hunting, land sovereignty; forest knowledge; pack loyalty |

### 7.5 Contemporary Cultural Grammar Documentation

The Tsimshian are notable for recent efforts to document and systematize their cultural knowledge in contemporary formats. This work — what might be called "cultural grammar documentation" — involves Tsimshian scholars and community members working with linguists and anthropologists to create comprehensive records of Tsimshian language, oral tradition, ceremonial practice, and social organization. These efforts are driven by Tsimshian communities themselves, not imposed by external researchers, and represent a model of community-controlled cultural preservation [Sturtevant 1990].

### 7.6 Contemporary Status

The Tsimshian maintain active communities both in British Columbia (allied tribal groups in the Prince Rupert area) and in Alaska (the Metlakatla Indian Community on Annette Island, the only federally recognized Indian reservation in Alaska). The Metlakatla community was established in 1887 when a group of Tsimshian, led by Anglican missionary William Duncan, relocated from British Columbia to Alaska. This history — a colonial-era relocation driven by a European missionary — complicates simple narratives of Indigenous cultural continuity, but the Metlakatla community has maintained and adapted Tsimshian cultural traditions for over a century [Sturtevant 1990].

## 8. Colonial Suppression and Revival

### 8.1 The Campaign Against the Shamans (1868-1893)

The colonial suppression of Pacific Northwest shamanic traditions was not a byproduct of general policies of assimilation; it was, in significant part, a targeted campaign against specific spiritual practitioners. From 1868 through the 1890s, United States Naval forces stationed in Alaska and the Pacific Northwest actively sought out tribal shamans, removed them from their communities, and shaved their heads — a deliberate act designed to destroy their spiritual power according to tribal custom [Lyon 1998], [Sturtevant 1990].

This campaign was part of a broader pattern of colonial actions aimed at dismantling the ceremonial infrastructure of Indigenous life on the Pacific Northwest Coast:

| Action | Period | Target | Effect |
|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| U.S. Naval seizure of shamans | 1868-1890s | Individual spiritual specialists (especially Tlingit) | Physical removal from communities; forced hair-cutting destroyed spiritual authority |
| Potlatch ban (Canada) | 1884-1951 | All Northwest Coast nations in Canada | Criminalized the central ceremonial and economic institution; drove ceremonies underground |
| Potlatch ban (U.S.) | Enforced informally through various mechanisms | All Northwest Coast nations in U.S. territory | Suppressed ceremonial activity without explicit legislation |
| Residential/boarding schools | 1870s-1960s+ | Children of all nations | Removed children from families; prohibited language and cultural practice; severed transmission of spiritual knowledge |
| Missionary campaigns | 1800s-present | All nations | Sought to replace Indigenous spiritual systems with Christianity; some missionaries worked with communities (William Duncan, Tsimshian), others worked against them |

### 8.2 The Destruction of Transmission

The most devastating effect of colonial suppression was not the immediate disruption of ceremonial practice but the severing of intergenerational transmission. Shamanic knowledge in all five nations surveyed was transmitted through direct apprenticeship — from practitioner to successor, within kinship systems, over years of training. When shamans were physically removed from their communities, when children were taken to boarding schools where Indigenous languages and practices were forbidden, and when the potlatch — the ceremonial occasion that validated and transmitted spiritual authority — was criminalized, the chain of transmission was broken in ways that could not be easily repaired [Sturtevant 1990], [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

### 8.3 The Indian Shaker Church

The Indian Shaker Church (formally the Indian Shaker Church of Washington and the Northwest) arose in 1881 among Coast Salish peoples in the southern Puget Sound area. Its founder, John Slocum (Squaxin Island), experienced a death-and-revival event that he interpreted as a divine calling. His wife, Mary Thompson Slocum, introduced the characteristic "shaking" — trembling and ecstatic bodily movements — that gave the church its name [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

The Indian Shaker Church integrated elements of Christianity (the cross, the bell, the candle, prayer) with Coast Salish healing practices (laying on of hands, spiritual diagnosis of illness, ceremonial brushing and cleansing). It provided a framework within which Indigenous healing practices could continue under the nominal cover of a Christian denomination, thus preserving ceremonial roles and spiritual knowledge during the most intense period of colonial suppression [Crawford O'Brien 2013], [Jilek 1982].

The Indian Shaker Church is a living tradition with active congregations throughout the Pacific Northwest today. It is neither purely Christian nor purely Indigenous; it is a syncretic tradition that emerged from a specific historical crisis and continues to serve Coast Salish and other Pacific Northwest communities as a spiritual and healing resource [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

### 8.4 Contemporary Revival

The revival of Pacific Northwest shamanic traditions in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries is documented most comprehensively by Suzanne Crawford O'Brien in *Coming Full Circle* [Crawford O'Brien 2013]. Crawford O'Brien's research, conducted with Coast Salish and Chinook communities in western Washington, shows that:

1. **Spiritual healing traditions were never fully extinguished.** Even during the most intense periods of suppression, knowledge was maintained within families and communities — often quietly, without the public ceremonial forms that colonial authorities targeted.

2. **Revival is not simple restoration.** Contemporary practitioners do not merely reproduce pre-colonial practices; they adapt, integrate, and innovate within their traditions. The winter spirit dance, for example, has been revived in forms that incorporate both traditional elements and responses to contemporary social conditions (substance abuse, cultural alienation, intergenerational trauma).

3. **Health is defined holistically.** Crawford O'Brien documents how Coast Salish communities define health through the lens of self, ecology, and community — a triadic framework rooted in Indigenous spiritual views of the body as embedded in relational webs. This framework does not reject biomedicine; it integrates biomedical treatment within a broader understanding of wellness that includes spiritual, ecological, and social dimensions [Crawford O'Brien 2013].

4. **Jilek's clinical validation endures.** Wolfgang Jilek's psychiatric documentation of the therapeutic efficacy of the Coast Salish winter ceremonial [Jilek 1982] remains a significant reference point. His work demonstrated that the ceremonial was therapeutically functional by Western clinical criteria — not merely "culturally significant" in a dismissive sense, but genuinely effective as a treatment modality for depression, addiction, and social alienation.
