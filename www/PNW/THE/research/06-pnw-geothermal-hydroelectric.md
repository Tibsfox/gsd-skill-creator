## 1. Overview — PNW Renewable Energy Context

The Pacific Northwest occupies a singular position in the American energy landscape. Washington and Oregon together generate more hydroelectric power than any other region in the United States, a legacy of massive federal investment in Columbia River dams during the 1930s through 1970s. This hydroelectric foundation gives the PNW one of the lowest-carbon electricity grids in the nation — and simultaneously creates a distinctive vulnerability: dependence on precipitation patterns that climate change is already disrupting.

Two state-level clean energy mandates frame the trajectory:

- **Washington:** 100% clean electricity by 2045, under the Clean Energy Transformation Act (CETA), signed 2019 (EIA-WA).
- **Oregon:** 100% clean electricity by 2040, under House Bill 2021, signed 2021 (EIA-OR).

Both targets assume continued hydroelectric dominance, supplemented by wind, solar, and emerging resources. Geothermal energy — despite the Cascade Range's volcanic heat — has been historically underexploited because cheap hydropower removed the economic incentive to develop it. That calculus is shifting as climate variability introduces uncertainty into hydroelectric generation forecasts and as Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) technology matures.

## 2. Washington Hydroelectric Profile

Washington State is the largest hydroelectric power producer in the United States. This is not a marginal distinction — the state generates roughly one-quarter of all U.S. hydroelectric output, a share that has held relatively stable for decades (EIA-WA).

### 2.1 Generation Overview

In 2024, Washington's total net electricity generation was approximately 102,397 GWh, with hydroelectric power accounting for 63.2% of that total (EIA-WA). The state's hydroelectric capacity is concentrated along the Columbia River and its tributaries, with a smaller but significant contribution from Puget Sound and Olympic Peninsula watersheds.

Key generation statistics:

- **Hydroelectric generation (2024):** approximately 64,715 GWh (EIA-WA)
- **Installed hydroelectric capacity:** approximately 21,100 MW across all facilities
- **Number of hydroelectric facilities:** more than 100, ranging from multi-gigawatt federal dams to small municipal and irrigation-district installations
- **Share of U.S. hydroelectric generation:** approximately 25% (EIA-WA)

### 2.3 Grand Coulee Dam — Technical Profile

Grand Coulee Dam merits detailed treatment because of its outsized role in the PNW grid. At 6,809 MW of installed capacity, it is the largest-capacity power station in the United States and the fifth-largest hydroelectric facility in the world (EIA-WA).

**Physical characteristics:**

- **Structure:** Concrete gravity dam, 550 feet high, 5,223 feet long
- **Reservoir:** Franklin D. Roosevelt Lake, 151 miles long, 82,000 acres surface area
- **Turbine-generators:** 33 units across three powerhouses (Third Powerhouse added 1974)
- **Annual generation:** variable, typically 19,000-23,000 GWh depending on water year
- **Irrigation:** Banks Lake and Columbia Basin Project serve over 670,000 acres of irrigated farmland

Grand Coulee is a storage dam, meaning it can hold water behind the dam and release it according to generation needs. This distinguishes it from run-of-river facilities like Chief Joseph (immediately downstream), which must pass water as it arrives and cannot time-shift generation. The storage capability makes Grand Coulee critical for grid balancing — it can ramp generation up or down within minutes to match demand or compensate for variable wind and solar output.

**Operational constraints:**

Grand Coulee's generation is governed by multiple, sometimes competing, requirements:

1. **Flood control:** The dam must maintain reservoir space to absorb spring snowmelt, limiting winter storage
2. **Irrigation delivery:** Columbia Basin Project water deliveries are contractual obligations
3. **Fish flows:** Biological opinions under the Endangered Species Act require specific flow regimes for salmon passage
4. **Power generation:** Revenue from power sales funds dam operations and BPA obligations
5. **Recreation:** Roosevelt Lake supports a recreation economy with seasonal water-level expectations

The Bureau of Reclamation balances these demands through annual operating plans developed in coordination with BPA, state fish and wildlife agencies, and tribal governments.

### 2.4 Ownership Structures

Washington's hydroelectric facilities fall into three distinct ownership categories, each with different regulatory frameworks, revenue structures, and operational mandates:

**Federal facilities** (operated by Bureau of Reclamation or U.S. Army Corps of Engineers):
- Grand Coulee, Chief Joseph, Bonneville, The Dalles, John Day, Ice Harbor, Lower Monumental, Little Goose, Lower Granite, McNary, Libby, Dworshak, Albeni Falls
- Power marketed by the Bonneville Power Administration (BPA)
- Subject to federal environmental law (ESA, NEPA, CWA)
- Revenue funds dam operations, fish mitigation, and BPA debt service

**Public Utility District (PUD) facilities:**
- Rocky Reach, Wanapum, Priest Rapids (Grant County PUD), Wells (Douglas County PUD)
- Owned by voter-created public utility districts under Washington State law
- Subject to FERC licensing for non-federal dams on navigable waterways
- Revenue serves ratepayers of the respective PUDs

**Municipal and investor-owned utility facilities:**
- Ross, Diablo, Gorge (Seattle City Light — Skagit River)
- Boundary (Seattle City Light — Pend Oreille River)
- Various small facilities operated by Tacoma Power, Puget Sound Energy, and others
- Subject to FERC licensing and state utility commission regulation

This mixed ownership structure means that no single entity controls Washington's hydroelectric system. Coordination across federal, PUD, municipal, and investor-owned operators is essential for grid reliability and is managed through BPA's role as the federal power marketing administration.

### 2.5 Clean Energy Transformation Act (CETA)

Washington's CETA, signed into law in 2019, establishes a binding timeline for decarbonizing the state's electricity sector (EIA-WA):

- **2025:** Coal-fired generation eliminated from Washington utilities' portfolios
- **2030:** Utilities must be carbon-neutral (80% clean, with offsets for remainder)
- **2045:** 100% clean electricity — no fossil fuel generation or offsets

Hydroelectric power is classified as clean under CETA. Because Washington already generates over 63% of its electricity from hydropower, the state starts from a significantly lower-carbon baseline than most U.S. states. The primary CETA challenge is displacing the remaining ~12% natural gas generation and ensuring reliability during low-water years when hydroelectric output drops.

## 3. Oregon Hydroelectric and Renewable Profile

Oregon is the second-largest hydroelectric power producer in the United States, after Washington (EIA-OR). The state's generation mix is more diverse than Washington's, reflecting a larger role for natural gas and wind alongside hydropower.

### 3.1 Generation Mix (2023-2024)

Oregon's 2023 electricity generation mix, the most recent year with complete data, was as follows:

| Source | Share (2023) | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Hydroelectric | 42.1% | Drought-reduced; historically >50% (EIA-OR) |
| Natural gas | 38.1% | Boardman coal plant closed 2020; gas filled the gap (EIA-OR) |
| Wind | 14.7% | Concentrated in Columbia Gorge and eastern Oregon (EIA-OR) |
| Solar | 3.2% | Growing rapidly but still small share (EIA-OR) |
| Geothermal | 0.4% | Neal Hot Springs (22 MW) + OIT (1.75 MW) (EIA-OR) |
| Other | 1.5% | Biomass, small hydro, other |

In 2024, renewable sources (led by hydropower) accounted for approximately 62% of Oregon's total net electricity generation, an increase from the drought-depressed 2023 figures as water conditions improved (EIA-OR).

### 3.2 Drought Impact on Hydroelectric Generation

The 2023 generation data reveals a critical vulnerability. Oregon's hydroelectric share dropped to 42.1% — well below the historical average of over 50% — driven by drought conditions across the Columbia River Basin (EIA-OR, OR-DOE). This was the lowest hydroelectric share in approximately two decades.

The production drop had cascading effects:

1. **Natural gas backfill:** Gas-fired generation increased to fill the hydroelectric deficit, raising the carbon intensity of Oregon's grid
2. **Market purchases:** Oregon utilities purchased additional power from out-of-state sources, including California natural gas plants
3. **Revenue pressure:** Lower hydroelectric generation reduced BPA's revenue, affecting wholesale power rates
4. **Clean energy accounting:** The temporary increase in fossil generation complicated progress toward HB 2021 targets

This single-year event demonstrated in concrete terms the risk that climate variability poses to a hydro-dependent grid — a risk that the PNNL study (Section 5) projects will intensify over coming decades.

### 3.4 Oregon Clean Electricity Target (HB 2021)

Oregon's House Bill 2021, signed in 2021, establishes the following timeline (OR-DOE):

- **2030:** 80% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from electricity (relative to baseline)
- **2035:** 90% reduction
- **2040:** 100% clean electricity

Oregon's 2040 target is five years more aggressive than Washington's 2045 CETA deadline. Given that Oregon currently relies on natural gas for 38% of its generation — nearly three times Washington's gas share — the state faces a steeper decarbonization curve. Replacing that gas capacity requires some combination of expanded wind, solar, battery storage, geothermal, and regional hydroelectric optimization.

## 4. Columbia River System and Federal Hydropower

### 4.1 The Federal Columbia River Power System (FCRPS)

The Columbia River and its tributaries constitute the largest hydroelectric system in North America. The Federal Columbia River Power System (FCRPS) encompasses 31 federal dams operated by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers and the Bureau of Reclamation, with power marketed by the Bonneville Power Administration (BPA).

Key FCRPS statistics:

- **Total installed capacity:** approximately 22,000 MW
- **Average annual generation:** approximately 75,000-85,000 GWh (varies with water year)
- **Service area:** Washington, Oregon, Idaho, western Montana, and parts of Nevada, Utah, Wyoming, and California
- **Customers:** 142 electric utilities, serving approximately 4.3 million customers
- **Transmission:** BPA operates approximately 15,000 circuit miles of high-voltage transmission

The FCRPS is not merely a power system — it is a multi-purpose water management infrastructure that simultaneously serves flood control, navigation, irrigation, fish and wildlife, and recreation. Power generation revenue funds all other mission functions, creating a financial structure where hydroelectric sales cross-subsidize non-power obligations.

### 4.2 Bonneville Power Administration (BPA)

BPA occupies a unique institutional position. It is a federal agency within the U.S. Department of Energy, but it receives no annual appropriations — it is entirely self-funded from power and transmission sales revenue. This structure gives BPA significant operational independence while also creating strong incentives to maximize generation revenue.

**BPA's role in the PNW grid:**

1. **Wholesale power marketing:** BPA sells power from federal dams at cost-based rates to preference customers (public utilities, cooperatives, tribal utilities) and at market rates to investor-owned utilities and out-of-region buyers
2. **Transmission provider:** BPA owns and operates the backbone transmission grid connecting federal dams to load centers across the PNW
3. **Balancing authority:** BPA manages real-time supply-demand balance across its balancing area, integrating variable wind and solar generation with dispatchable hydro
4. **Fish and wildlife funding:** BPA spends approximately $500-700 million annually on fish and wildlife mitigation programs, funded from power sales revenue
5. **Grid reliability coordinator:** BPA coordinates with Western Electricity Coordinating Council (WECC) reliability standards

### 4.3 Seasonal Generation Patterns

Columbia River hydroelectric generation follows a pronounced seasonal cycle driven by the snowmelt hydrology of the Columbia Basin:

| Season | Flow Pattern | Generation Impact | Grid Role |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | Low natural flow; reservoir releases for heating demand | Moderate generation; draw-down storage | Meeting PNW winter heating load |
| Spring (Mar-Jun) | Snowmelt runoff; highest flows | Peak generation; often surplus | Export to California; spill if transmission constrained |
| Summer (Jul-Sep) | Declining flows; high irrigation demand | Reduced generation; storage depletion | Meeting California summer peak via transmission |
| Fall (Oct-Nov) | Lowest flows; reservoir refill begins | Lowest generation | Market purchases supplement hydro |

This seasonal pattern creates both opportunities and challenges. In spring, the PNW often generates more hydroelectric power than it can consume domestically, exporting surplus to California via the Pacific DC Intertie (3,100 MW capacity) and the AC system. In late summer and fall, the PNW may import power from California solar and Southwest thermal generation to compensate for declining hydro output.

The seasonal complementarity between PNW hydro (spring peak) and California solar (summer peak) is a foundation of the Western Interconnection's economic efficiency. However, it also means that both regions are vulnerable when their respective resources underperform simultaneously — as occurred during the 2023 drought, when low Columbia River flows coincided with heat-wave-driven demand spikes.

### 4.4 Run-of-River vs. Storage Facilities

A critical technical distinction in hydroelectric operations is between storage dams and run-of-river dams.

**Storage dams** (Grand Coulee, Libby, Dworshak, Hungry Horse, Detroit) can hold water behind the dam and release it on a schedule that optimizes generation value. They provide:
- Time-shifting: generating during high-demand periods rather than following natural flow
- Flood control: absorbing spring runoff to protect downstream communities
- Grid balancing: ramping generation up or down within minutes to match demand

**Run-of-river dams** (Chief Joseph, Bonneville, McNary, The Dalles, John Day) must pass water largely as it arrives, with minimal storage. They provide:
- Baseload generation: consistent output following natural flow
- Lower environmental impact: reduced reservoir fluctuation
- Less operational flexibility: cannot significantly time-shift generation

The FCRPS relies on the upstream storage dams (primarily Grand Coulee, Libby, and Dworshak) to regulate flows through the entire downstream chain of run-of-river facilities. When Grand Coulee releases stored water, every downstream dam generates more power. This cascading effect means that storage management at a few key facilities controls the output of the entire system.

## 5. Hydroelectric Climate Variability

> **Sensitivity classification: GATE (SC-CLI)**
> Climate projections in this section cite PNNL and associated agency sources. All projections include uncertainty language and scenario specification. No projection is presented as certainty.

### 5.1 PNNL 2024 Hydropower Climate Study

A 2024 study conducted by Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL), drawing on data from more than 1,400 hydropower facilities across the United States, produced the most comprehensive assessment to date of how climate change may affect U.S. hydropower generation (PNNL-HYDRO).

**Key national findings:**

- U.S. hydropower generation is projected to increase by approximately 5% by 2039 relative to recent baselines (PNNL-HYDRO)
- By 2059, the projected increase is approximately 10% (PNNL-HYDRO)
- These national averages mask significant regional variability (PNNL-HYDRO)
- The Southwest is the only U.S. region projected to see average hydropower generation decline (PNNL-HYDRO)

**Important uncertainty qualifications:**

These projections represent ensemble averages across multiple climate model runs. Individual years and regions may deviate substantially from these averages. The PNNL study emphasizes that variability — not just the long-term trend — is the critical planning challenge (PNNL-HYDRO).

### 5.2 PNW Regional Variability

For the Pacific Northwest specifically, the PNNL study and recent observed data paint a more complex picture than the national averages suggest.

**Observed variability:**

- In 2023, Oregon and Washington experienced hydroelectric production approximately 20% below 2021 levels — the lowest output in roughly two decades (EIA-WA, EIA-OR, PNNL-HYDRO)
- This production drop was driven by drought conditions across the Columbia River Basin, including reduced snowpack, earlier snowmelt timing, and below-average precipitation in key headwater regions
- The 2023 event was not unprecedented but was among the most severe in the modern record
- Recovery in 2024 demonstrated the high year-to-year variability: improved precipitation restored much of the deficit

**Projected trends for the PNW (with uncertainty):**

The PNNL study suggests that the PNW may see a modest long-term increase in average annual precipitation, which would support slightly higher average hydropower generation. However, this comes with several critical caveats (PNNL-HYDRO):

1. **Snowpack decline:** Warmer temperatures shift precipitation from snow to rain, reducing the natural reservoir of mountain snowpack. This compresses the runoff season and may increase winter flows while reducing summer flows
2. **Timing shift:** Earlier snowmelt moves the spring freshet forward by 2-4 weeks in many projections, potentially misaligning peak generation with peak demand
3. **Increased drought frequency:** Even with higher average precipitation, drought years may become more frequent and severe due to increased evapotranspiration and altered precipitation patterns
4. **Extreme events:** Both flood and drought extremes are projected to intensify, widening the range of possible generation outcomes in any given year

### 5.3 Implications for PNW Grid Planning

The combination of modest long-term average increases with amplified year-to-year variability creates a planning challenge that static capacity calculations cannot capture. Grid planners must prepare for:

**Low-water years:**
- Hydroelectric generation 15-25% below average (based on historical range and PNNL projections)
- Increased reliance on natural gas backfill (undermining clean energy targets)
- Market purchases from out-of-region sources at premium prices
- Potential for simultaneous stress if drought affects multiple Western regions

**High-water years:**
- Surplus generation during spring runoff exceeding transmission export capacity
- Forced spill (water released without generating power) when turbines are fully loaded and transmission is constrained
- Revenue loss from negative wholesale prices during surplus periods
- Flood management constraints that may conflict with generation optimization

### 5.4 Adaptation Strategies

PNW utilities and grid operators are developing several strategies to buffer against hydroelectric variability:

1. **Pumped storage hydropower** (see Section 9): Storing energy during surplus periods for release during deficits. Multiple PNW projects are in development or permitting
2. **Wind and solar diversification:** Adding non-hydro renewables reduces dependence on any single resource, though wind and solar introduce their own variability
3. **Regional grid coordination:** Closer integration through the Western Resource Adequacy Program and Energy Imbalance Market (see Section 10) enables resource sharing across a larger geographic area
4. **Demand response:** Shifting flexible electricity demand (water heating, EV charging, industrial processes) to align with periods of high hydroelectric output
5. **Battery storage:** Grid-scale batteries can time-shift generation over hours, complementing pumped storage's capacity for longer-duration storage
6. **Geothermal development:** 24/7 dispatchable geothermal generation (see Section 11) could provide the baseload reliability that climate-variable hydro increasingly cannot guarantee

## 6. Hydropower and Salmon

> **Sensitivity classification: ANNOTATE (SC-ADV) + GATE (SC-IND)**
> This section presents ecological and economic evidence regarding dam impacts on salmon without advocating dam removal or retention. All tribal references name specific nations per SC-IND protocol.

### 6.1 The Salmon-Hydropower Conflict

Columbia River Basin dams have affected Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) migration and spawning since the construction of Bonneville Dam in 1938. Salmon are anadromous — born in freshwater, they migrate to the Pacific Ocean as juveniles and return to their natal streams as adults to spawn. Dams create physical barriers at both life stages: adults migrating upstream to spawn must find and ascend fish passage facilities, while juveniles migrating downstream to the ocean must navigate reservoirs and turbine intakes.

Thirteen distinct salmon and steelhead populations in the Columbia River Basin are listed under the Endangered Species Act (ESA), including:

- Snake River spring/summer Chinook (threatened)
- Snake River fall Chinook (threatened)
- Snake River steelhead (threatened)
- Snake River sockeye (endangered)
- Upper Columbia River spring Chinook (endangered)
- Upper Columbia River steelhead (threatened)
- Middle Columbia River steelhead (threatened)
- Lower Columbia River Chinook (threatened)
- Lower Columbia River steelhead (threatened)
- Lower Columbia River coho (threatened)
- Upper Willamette River Chinook (threatened)
- Upper Willamette River steelhead (threatened)
- Columbia River chum (threatened)

### 6.2 Treaty Fishing Rights

The Columbia River salmon fishery has been central to the cultures, economies, and diets of Columbia Basin tribes since time immemorial. Several tribes hold treaty-reserved fishing rights affirmed by federal courts:

- **Yakama Nation** — Treaty of 1855 reserves fishing rights at "usual and accustomed grounds and stations" throughout the Columbia Basin
- **Nez Perce Tribe** — Treaty of 1855 reserves fishing rights; Snake River salmon are of particular cultural and subsistence significance
- **Confederated Tribes of the Umatilla Indian Reservation** — Treaty of 1855 reserves Columbia River fishing rights; member tribes include the Cayuse, Umatilla, and Walla Walla
- **Confederated Tribes of the Warm Springs Reservation** — Treaty of 1855 reserves fishing rights; member tribes include the Wasco, Warm Springs, and Paiute

These four tribes, along with the Confederated Tribes and Bands of the Colville Reservation and the Shoshone-Bannock Tribes, participate in Columbia Basin fish management through the Columbia Basin Fish and Wildlife Authority and direct government-to-government consultation with federal dam operators.

Treaty fishing rights are not grants from the federal government — they are pre-existing rights retained by the tribes when they ceded lands to the United States. Federal courts, including the U.S. Supreme Court in *United States v. Winans* (1905) and the Ninth Circuit in *United States v. Oregon* (various), have consistently upheld these reserved rights.

### 6.3 Fish Passage Infrastructure

Federal dam operators have invested billions of dollars in fish passage infrastructure across the FCRPS. The principal approaches include:

**Adult upstream passage:**
- **Fish ladders:** Series of stepped pools that allow adult salmon to ascend the dam face. Present at Bonneville, The Dalles, John Day, McNary, Ice Harbor, Lower Monumental, Little Goose, Lower Granite, and others. Design capacity and passage efficiency vary by facility and species
- **Counting stations:** Each major fish ladder includes a counting window where biologists tally returning adults by species, providing population monitoring data

**Juvenile downstream passage:**
- **Spillway modifications:** Surface-flow outlets, removable spillway weirs, and turbine intake screens direct juveniles away from turbine intakes and toward less-lethal passage routes
- **Juvenile bypass systems:** Screens and channels that collect juveniles before they enter turbine intakes and route them through bypass pipes to the tailrace
- **Barging and trucking:** At Lower Granite, Little Goose, Lower Monumental, and McNary dams, juvenile salmon and steelhead are collected at bypass facilities and transported by barge or truck downstream past the remaining dams, releasing them below Bonneville Dam. This program is operated by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers during the spring migration season

**Reservoir management:**
- **Spill operations:** Deliberate release of water over the spillway (rather than through turbines) during juvenile migration periods to accelerate downstream passage. Spill reduces generation revenue but improves juvenile survival
- **Flow augmentation:** Release of stored water from upstream reservoirs (Dworshak, Grand Coulee) to increase river velocity through reservoir reaches, reducing juvenile travel time

### 6.4 Ecological and Economic Evidence

The evidence regarding dam impacts on salmon is extensive but does not point to a single policy conclusion. Relevant data includes:

**Ecological indicators:**
- Pre-dam Columbia River salmon runs estimated at 10-16 million adults annually; contemporary returns fluctuate between 1-3 million, with recent poor years below 1 million
- Juvenile dam passage survival at individual dams has improved to 93-98% per dam for most species following infrastructure investments, but cumulative survival across multiple dams remains lower
- Reservoir mortality (predation, elevated water temperature, delayed migration) adds to per-dam passage losses
- Snake River spring/summer Chinook and Snake River sockeye remain at critically low abundance despite decades of mitigation

**Economic indicators:**
- The FCRPS generates approximately $3-4 billion annually in wholesale power value at cost-based BPA rates
- Commercial and recreational salmon fisheries in the Columbia Basin generate significant economic activity, though substantially less than pre-dam levels
- BPA spends $500-700 million annually on fish and wildlife programs, funded from power sales revenue
- Dam removal and river restoration would entail capital costs measured in billions, offset against long-term power replacement and fishery recovery values

### 6.5 Ongoing Legal and Policy Frameworks

Dam operations for salmon protection are governed by a series of Biological Opinions issued by NOAA Fisheries under the ESA, the Columbia Basin Fish Accords (agreements between federal agencies and individual tribes), and the *United States v. Oregon* management agreement. These frameworks are subject to periodic revision through litigation, negotiation, and administrative review.

This module does not advocate for any particular outcome in these proceedings. The technical data regarding hydroelectric generation and salmon passage is presented to inform the broader question of how the PNW grid evolves in a context where hydroelectric dams serve multiple, sometimes conflicting, purposes.

## 7. PNW Geothermal Resources

The Pacific Northwest sits atop the Cascadia Subduction Zone, where the Juan de Fuca Plate subducts beneath the North American Plate, feeding a chain of active stratovolcanoes — the Cascade Range — that stretches from Mount Lassen in California to Mount Garibaldi in British Columbia. This tectonic activity produces abundant subsurface heat. Despite this, the PNW has developed very little geothermal energy capacity, a pattern that industry observers describe as being "very much at the front end of the curve" in geothermal resource characterization (GEO-RISE).

### 7.1 Why So Little Geothermal?

The historical answer is straightforward: cheap hydropower. When Columbia River dams provided electricity at some of the lowest rates in the nation, there was no economic incentive to drill expensive geothermal wells. This dynamic persisted for decades and created an institutional knowledge gap — the PNW has far less geothermal exploration data than states like California and Nevada, where higher electricity prices justified early geothermal investment.

Additional barriers include:

- **Permitting complexity:** Many promising geothermal sites in the Cascades are on federal land (U.S. Forest Service, Bureau of Land Management) subject to NEPA review, wilderness area restrictions, and volcanic hazard considerations
- **Exploration risk:** Geothermal well drilling costs $5-10 million per well, with significant subsurface uncertainty about resource quality at depth
- **Transmission access:** The best geothermal resources in Oregon are in the eastern and central parts of the state, far from the Willamette Valley load centers, requiring new transmission investment
- **Water rights:** Some geothermal systems require water for cooling or reinjection, competing with agricultural and ecological water demands in semi-arid eastern Oregon and Washington

Despite these barriers, several geothermal facilities are operating or under development in the PNW.

### 7.3 Neal Hot Springs

Neal Hot Springs, located near Vale in Malheur County, Oregon, is the PNW's largest producing geothermal power plant. The facility was developed by U.S. Geothermal Inc. (subsequently acquired by Ormat Technologies) and began commercial operation in 2012 (GEO-RISE).

**Technical characteristics:**
- **Capacity:** 22 MW (net)
- **Technology:** Binary cycle (organic Rankine cycle) — geothermal fluid heats a secondary working fluid with a lower boiling point, which drives the turbine. The geothermal fluid is reinjected into the reservoir without exposure to the atmosphere
- **Resource temperature:** approximately 140-150 degrees C at production depth
- **Production wells:** Multiple wells, approximately 800-1,200 meters depth
- **Off-taker:** Idaho Power Company (supply contract)
- **Capacity factor:** approximately 90-95%, demonstrating the baseload reliability advantage of geothermal over other renewables

Neal Hot Springs demonstrates that commercial geothermal power is technically and economically viable in the PNW, even with relatively modest resource temperatures. The facility's consistent output and high capacity factor illustrate geothermal's value as a 24/7 dispatchable complement to variable hydro, wind, and solar.

### 7.4 Oregon Institute of Technology Geothermal

The Oregon Institute of Technology (OIT) in Klamath Falls operates a unique campus geothermal system that provides both electricity and direct heating (GEO-RISE).

**Electrical generation:**
- 1.75 MW binary-cycle power plant, commissioned 2010
- Supplies approximately 25-30% of campus electricity needs
- First university-based geothermal power plant in the United States

**Direct-use heating:**
- OIT has used geothermal direct heating since 1964
- Geothermal fluid from campus wells heats buildings through a district heating loop
- Displaces natural gas heating across the entire campus
- OIT's Geo-Heat Center (now closed) was a nationally recognized center for geothermal direct-use research and technical assistance

### 7.5 Klamath Falls District Heating

Klamath Falls, Oregon, has the longest-running geothermal district heating system in the Pacific Northwest, operational since 1981 (GEO-RISE). The system uses geothermal hot water from shallow wells to heat downtown buildings through a network of underground pipes. Hundreds of additional private wells in the Klamath Falls area provide geothermal heating to individual homes and businesses, making the city one of the most geothermally heated communities in the United States.

The Klamath Falls resource is a relatively shallow, moderate-temperature hydrothermal system associated with Basin and Range extensional faulting. Resource temperatures are typically 70-110 degrees C at accessible depths, suitable for direct heating but generally too low for efficient power generation without binary-cycle technology.

### 7.6 Washington Geothermal Resources Act (2024)

In 2024, Washington State signed the Geothermal Resources Act, a legislative measure designed to remove regulatory barriers to geothermal energy development in the state (GEO-RISE). Key provisions include:

- Clarification of geothermal resource ownership and mineral rights
- Streamlined permitting pathways for geothermal exploration and development
- Coordination between state agencies (Department of Natural Resources, Department of Ecology) on geothermal project review
- Completion of the Washington Geothermal Favorability Model, a statewide assessment of geothermal potential based on geological, geochemical, and geophysical data

Washington has no operating geothermal power plants as of 2025. The Geothermal Resources Act represents a policy shift — an acknowledgment that the state's reliance on hydropower alone may be insufficient under climate variability, and that the Cascade Range's volcanic heat represents a significant untapped resource.

### 7.7 Hot Springs Inventory

The PNW contains dozens of surface hot springs, each representing a visible expression of subsurface geothermal heat. While surface springs alone are insufficient to determine power-generating potential at depth, they serve as exploration indicators and have supported direct-use applications for more than a century.

**Notable Oregon hot springs and geothermal areas:**
- Klamath Falls area: extensive shallow geothermal (described above)
- Summer Lake / Paisley: geothermal wells used for greenhouse heating
- Alvord Hot Springs / Alvord Desert: Basin and Range geothermal with measured temperatures >100 degrees C
- Breitenbush Hot Springs: Cascade Range foothills; surface temperatures 50-90 degrees C
- Belknap Hot Springs: McKenzie River corridor
- Umpqua Hot Springs: North Umpqua drainage

**Notable Washington hot springs and geothermal areas:**
- Wind River area (Skamania County): explored by multiple parties; moderate subsurface temperatures
- Olympic Hot Springs: Olympic National Park; low temperature, limited resource
- Baker Hot Springs: Mount Baker vicinity; volcanic association
- Sol Duc Hot Springs: Olympic Peninsula; commercial resort use

The distribution of hot springs across the PNW confirms the broad extent of the geothermal resource. However, the gap between surface expressions and power-generating reservoirs is large — most hot springs indicate resource temperatures too low for conventional power generation. Enhanced Geothermal Systems (Section 8) aim to bridge this gap by accessing the much hotter rock at greater depths.

## 8. Enhanced Geothermal Systems

### 8.1 The EGS Concept

Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) represent the most significant potential expansion of geothermal energy worldwide. Unlike conventional geothermal, which requires natural reservoirs of hot water or steam at accessible depths, EGS creates artificial reservoirs by injecting fluid into hot, low-permeability rock and fracturing it to establish flow paths. This means EGS can theoretically access geothermal heat anywhere the subsurface temperature is sufficient — not just at the rare locations where natural hydrothermal reservoirs exist.

**How EGS works:**

1. **Drilling:** Wells are drilled to depths where rock temperature exceeds the economic threshold (typically >150 degrees C for power generation, ideally >200 degrees C)
2. **Stimulation:** Fluid is injected at controlled pressures to create or enhance fracture networks in the rock, increasing permeability
3. **Circulation:** Cold water is injected through one well, flows through the fracture network (extracting heat from the rock), and returns to the surface through a production well as hot water or steam
4. **Power generation:** The produced hot fluid drives a power plant (binary cycle or flash, depending on temperature)
5. **Reinjection:** Cooled fluid is reinjected to sustain the circulation loop

### 8.2 Newberry Crater — AltaRock Energy EGS Project

Newberry Crater, located in central Oregon approximately 20 miles south of Bend, hosts what is characterized as the largest geothermal heat reservoir in the western United States (ALTAROCK). AltaRock Energy has been developing an EGS demonstration project at the Newberry site since the early 2010s, working within the Newberry National Volcanic Monument on Bureau of Land Management land.

**Resource characteristics:**
- **Geological setting:** Newberry Volcano is a large shield-type volcano with a summit caldera containing Paulina Lake and East Lake. It is one of the youngest and most recently active volcanic centers in the Cascades
- **Subsurface temperatures:** Measured temperatures exceeding 300 degrees C at depths of 3,000 meters and predicted temperatures above 400 degrees C at greater depths (ALTAROCK)
- **Rock type:** Primarily basaltic and andesitic volcanic rock with low natural permeability — ideal for EGS because the heat is present but natural fluid circulation is insufficient for conventional production
- **Heat flow:** Among the highest measured heat flow values in the continental United States

**Development history:**
- Initial exploration wells drilled in the 1980s and 1990s confirmed high subsurface temperatures
- AltaRock Energy began focused EGS development work in the 2010s
- Multiple stimulation tests have been conducted to demonstrate fracture creation and fluid circulation in the Newberry reservoir
- The project has received DOE funding as part of the Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy (FORGE) program framework, though the primary FORGE site is in Utah

### 8.3 Millimeter-Wave Drilling — Quaise Energy Collaboration

A particularly notable aspect of the Newberry project is the collaboration between AltaRock Energy and Quaise Energy on millimeter-wave (mmWave) drilling technology (ALTAROCK).

**The drilling problem:**

Conventional rotary drilling — the technology that drills oil, gas, and geothermal wells — becomes increasingly difficult and expensive at great depths. Drill bits wear rapidly in hard crystalline rock at high temperatures, drilling fluid systems degrade, and the mechanical forces on the drill string become extreme. These limitations have effectively capped conventional geothermal well depths at approximately 3,000-5,000 meters in most geological settings.

For EGS to access the hottest rock — where temperatures exceed 400 degrees C — wells may need to reach 5,000-10,000 meters or more. At these depths, conventional drilling is either physically impossible or prohibitively expensive.

**Millimeter-wave drilling:**

Quaise Energy is developing a drilling technology that uses high-power millimeter-wave (mmWave) energy — essentially a focused beam of electromagnetic radiation at frequencies around 30-300 GHz — to vaporize rock rather than mechanically grinding it. The concept:

1. A powerful gyrotron (a type of vacuum tube used in fusion research and industrial heating) generates the mmWave beam
2. The beam is directed down the borehole to the rock face
3. The rock absorbs the mmWave energy and heats rapidly, vaporizing and fracturing
4. The vaporized material is removed by circulating gas or fluid
5. The process continues without mechanical contact between a drill bit and the rock

**Potential advantages:**
- No mechanical wear: the "drill bit" is an energy beam that does not degrade
- Potential to drill through any rock type, including the hardest crystalline basement rocks
- Theoretical capability to reach depths of 10,000-20,000 meters, accessing temperatures far above 400 degrees C
- At superhot temperatures (>400 degrees C), water becomes supercritical, and the energy content per unit mass of produced fluid increases dramatically — potentially yielding 10x the energy output of conventional geothermal wells (ALTAROCK)

**Current status:**

MmWave drilling is in the laboratory and early field-demonstration phase. Quaise Energy has demonstrated rock vaporization in controlled settings and is working toward longer-duration borehole penetration tests. The technology has not yet been deployed at full scale in a geothermal well. Timelines for commercial deployment are uncertain, but the PNW is a logical early deployment region given the combination of high subsurface heat, existing geothermal development interest (AltaRock at Newberry), and policy support (Oregon HB 2021 and Washington Geothermal Resources Act).

### 8.4 EGS Challenges and Risks

EGS development faces several technical and non-technical challenges:

**Technical:**
- **Induced seismicity:** Fluid injection at pressure can trigger small earthquakes. While most induced seismic events are below the threshold of human perception, public concern about induced seismicity has delayed or halted EGS projects (notably the Basel, Switzerland project in 2006-2009). Monitoring and management protocols (traffic-light systems) have improved since early projects
- **Reservoir sustainability:** Maintaining fracture permeability and heat extraction rates over decades requires careful management of injection pressure, flow rates, and fluid chemistry. Short-circuiting (fluid flowing through a few dominant fractures rather than the full network) can reduce heat extraction efficiency
- **Well integrity:** High temperatures and corrosive geothermal fluids (when natural formation fluids are present) stress well casing, cement, and downhole equipment. Materials engineering for EGS environments is an active research area
- **Water consumption:** EGS circulation loops lose some water to the formation. In semi-arid regions like central and eastern Oregon, water sourcing for make-up fluid is a permitting and resource-availability concern

**Non-technical:**
- **Exploration risk:** Even with favorable surface indicators, subsurface conditions at depth are uncertain until wells are drilled. A failed exploration well represents millions of dollars in sunk cost
- **Permitting timelines:** Federal land (BLM, USFS) permitting involves NEPA environmental review, which can take years. Wilderness and monument designations add additional constraints
- **Public acceptance:** Induced seismicity concerns, visual impacts of well pads and transmission lines, and competition with recreation and conservation values on public lands can generate community opposition
- **Financing:** The high upfront cost of geothermal wells (before any revenue) and the subsurface uncertainty make geothermal projects less attractive to risk-averse investors than solar or wind, where resource characterization is simpler and construction costs are more predictable

### 8.5 National EGS Context

The DOE's FORGE (Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy) program at the Utah FORGE site near Milford, Utah, is the primary U.S. EGS research platform. In 2024-2025, Fervo Energy's Cape Station project in Nevada became the first commercial-scale EGS project to deliver power to the grid, demonstrating 3.5 MW from a two-well system. These national developments inform the PNW's EGS prospects:

- FORGE research on stimulation techniques, reservoir monitoring, and well completions is directly applicable to the Newberry Crater geology
- Fervo's commercial success demonstrates EGS viability at a scale that improves investor confidence for PNW projects
- DOE funding programs (including IRA geothermal provisions) reduce exploration risk for early projects

## 9. Pumped Storage Hydropower

### 9.1 Technology Overview

Pumped storage hydropower (PSH) is the most mature large-scale energy storage technology in the world. It uses elevation difference between two water reservoirs to store and release energy:

**Storage (charging):** When electricity supply exceeds demand (or prices are low), electric pumps move water from a lower reservoir to an upper reservoir, converting electrical energy to gravitational potential energy.

**Generation (discharging):** When electricity demand exceeds supply (or prices are high), water flows from the upper reservoir through turbines to the lower reservoir, converting gravitational potential energy back to electrical energy.

**Key performance parameters:**

| Parameter | Typical Range | Notes |
|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Round-trip efficiency | 70-85% | Energy recovered as a fraction of energy used to pump |
| Discharge duration | 6-20 hours | Duration of full-power discharge from a full upper reservoir |
| Response time | Seconds to minutes | Time to ramp from standby to full generation |
| Cycle life | Effectively unlimited | No chemical degradation (unlike batteries) |
| Service life | 50-100 years | Civil structures with periodic equipment replacement |
| Capital cost | $1,500-3,000/kW | High upfront cost but very low operating cost |
| Capacity | 100-3,000 MW per project | Large-scale projects dominate |

### 9.2 Why PSH Matters for the PNW

Pumped storage is particularly valuable in the PNW context for three reasons:

1. **Buffering hydroelectric variability:** PSH can store surplus spring runoff energy and release it during low-water periods, partially smoothing the seasonal hydro cycle
2. **Wind and solar integration:** As the PNW adds large amounts of wind (Columbia Gorge, eastern OR/WA) and solar (central and eastern OR), PSH provides the multi-hour storage needed to shift generation to evening peak hours
3. **Grid reliability:** PSH provides fast-ramping generation and black-start capability (restarting the grid after a total blackout), complementing the slow-ramping nuclear plant (Columbia Generating Station) and the flow-dependent hydroelectric system

### 9.3 PNW Pumped Storage Projects

Two major PSH projects are in development in the PNW:

**Goldendale Energy Storage Project (Washington)**

|-----------|--------|
| Location | Klickitat County, WA (Columbia River Gorge area) |

| Capacity | 1,200 MW |
| Storage duration | 12 hours (14,400 MWh) |
| Upper reservoir | Constructed on Columbia Hills plateau |
| Lower reservoir | Constructed at lower elevation near John Day Dam pool |

| Estimated cost | $2-3 billion |

The Goldendale project's 1,200 MW capacity would make it one of the largest pumped storage facilities in the United States. Its location near the Columbia Gorge wind resource area and multiple transmission lines makes it well-positioned for grid integration. The high head (elevation difference) improves both energy density and efficiency.

**Swan Lake North (Oregon)**

| Attribute | Detail |

### 9.4 PSH vs. Battery Storage

Grid-scale lithium-ion battery storage has grown rapidly and now competes with PSH for some applications. The two technologies have different strengths:

| Attribute | Pumped Storage | Li-ion Battery |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Duration | 6-20 hours | 2-4 hours (current economics) |
| Capacity per project | 100-3,000 MW | 10-500 MW (typical) |
| Cycle life | Unlimited | 5,000-10,000 cycles |
| Service life | 50-100 years | 10-15 years |
| Round-trip efficiency | 70-85% | 85-92% |
| Response time | Seconds-minutes | Milliseconds |
| Siting constraints | Requires topography and water | Flexible; modular |
| Environmental footprint | Large (reservoirs, transmission) | Moderate (mineral supply chain) |
| Capital cost trend | Stable (civil works) | Declining rapidly |

For the PNW's specific needs — managing seasonal hydro variability and storing multi-hour wind/solar surpluses — PSH's longer duration and unlimited cycling make it a strong complement to batteries. An optimal PNW grid likely uses both: batteries for fast response and short-duration shifting, PSH for bulk energy storage and seasonal balancing.

### 9.5 Connection to Hydrogen Storage

Module E (Solar Electrolysis and Green Hydrogen) explores hydrogen as a long-duration energy storage medium. In concept, surplus PNW hydroelectric or wind generation could power electrolyzers to produce green hydrogen, which is then stored and later converted back to electricity via fuel cells (Module D) or hydrogen-capable gas turbines. This power-to-hydrogen-to-power pathway offers seasonal-scale storage (weeks to months) that neither PSH nor batteries can economically provide.

The complementarity is:
- **Batteries:** seconds to hours
- **Pumped storage:** hours to days
- **Hydrogen:** days to seasons

Cross-reference: Module D (D-PEM, D-SOFC), Module E (E-AWE, E-LCOH)

## 10. PNW Grid Context and Integration

### 10.1 Western Interconnection

Washington and Oregon are part of the Western Interconnection, one of three major alternating-current electrical grids in North America. The Western Interconnection spans 1.8 million square miles across 14 states, two Canadian provinces, and a portion of Baja California, Mexico. All generators and loads within the interconnection operate in synchrony at 60 Hz.

The Western Electricity Coordinating Council (WECC) is the regional entity responsible for reliability standards within the Western Interconnection. WECC's rules govern transmission planning, resource adequacy, and system operations.

### 10.2 BPA Transmission System

BPA operates approximately 15,000 circuit miles of high-voltage transmission lines, including:

- **500 kV backbone:** The primary transmission arteries connecting Columbia River dams to load centers in Portland, Seattle, and the Puget Sound
- **Pacific DC Intertie:** A 3,100 MW high-voltage direct current (HVDC) line running from The Dalles, Oregon, to Sylmar (Los Angeles), California — the primary conduit for PNW-California power exchanges
- **Pacific AC Intertie:** Two 500 kV AC lines (total capacity approximately 4,800 MW) connecting the PNW to California through the Cascade Range
- **Northern Intertie:** Connections to British Columbia Hydro, enabling energy exchanges with Canada's large storage reservoirs

Total PNW-to-California transmission capacity is approximately 7,900 MW on the combined DC and AC interties. This capacity is heavily utilized during both PNW surplus (spring runoff export) and California surplus (summer solar export) periods.

### 10.3 Transmission Constraints

The PNW grid faces several transmission constraints that affect how hydroelectric, geothermal, wind, and solar resources can be delivered to load centers:

**North-south capacity:**
- The Pacific Interties are frequently congested during peak export periods (PNW spring hydro surplus, California summer evening imports)
- Proposed transmission expansions have faced siting, permitting, and cost-allocation challenges for decades
- Cross-Cascades capacity (connecting eastern Oregon/Washington wind and solar to western load centers) is limited

**East-west capacity:**
- Major wind farms in the Columbia Gorge and eastern Oregon/Washington require transmission west to Portland and Seattle
- Geothermal resources in central and eastern Oregon face the same east-west bottleneck
- BPA and PacifiCorp have proposed transmission reinforcements, but construction timelines extend into the 2030s

**Curtailment consequences:**
- When transmission capacity is insufficient to deliver generation to load, generators must curtail output (generate less than they could)
- BPA has experienced increasing wind curtailment events during high-wind/high-hydro periods when transmission to California is congested
- This represents both economic waste and a barrier to meeting clean energy targets

### 10.4 Energy Imbalance Market (EIM)

The Western Energy Imbalance Market (EIM), operated by the California Independent System Operator (CAISO), provides real-time energy trading across the Western Interconnection. Participating entities — including BPA, PacifiCorp, Puget Sound Energy, Portland General Electric, and others — can buy and sell energy in 5-minute and 15-minute intervals to balance supply and demand more efficiently than each utility could achieve alone.

**EIM benefits for the PNW:**
- **Reduced curtailment:** When BPA has surplus hydro or wind, EIM can find buyers in other regions within minutes, reducing the need to spill water or curtail wind
- **Lower costs:** EIM leverages geographic diversity — when the PNW is cloudy but windy, California may be sunny but calm, and trading benefits both
- **Renewable integration:** Variable resources (wind, solar) create more imbalances, and a larger market can absorb those imbalances with fewer reserves
- **Market data:** EIM pricing signals indicate where the grid is stressed and where new generation or transmission would have the highest value

Since its launch in 2014, the EIM has expanded to include most major PNW utilities and has reported cumulative benefits exceeding $5 billion across all participants.

### 10.5 Extended Day-Ahead Market (EDAM)

Building on EIM, CAISO is developing the Extended Day-Ahead Market (EDAM), which would enable day-ahead energy scheduling across the Western Interconnection. EDAM participation is voluntary, and several PNW entities — including BPA and PacifiCorp — have signaled interest or commitment to join.

EDAM would enable more efficient scheduling of:
- Hydroelectric generation from storage dams (optimizing release timing a day ahead)
- Cross-regional renewable energy transfers (scheduling California solar exports to PNW for evening hours)
- Pumped storage charging and discharging cycles
- Geothermal baseload contract scheduling

### 10.6 Western Resource Adequacy Program (WRAP)

The Western Power Pool's Western Resource Adequacy Program (WRAP) is a binding reliability framework requiring participating utilities to demonstrate they have adequate generation resources to meet peak demand plus a planning reserve margin. WRAP addresses the PNW-specific concern that reliance on a single dominant resource (hydropower) creates resource adequacy risk during drought years.

WRAP participation requires utilities to:
- Maintain sufficient firm capacity (generation or storage that can be dispatched on demand) to cover their peak load plus reserves
- Demonstrate this capacity through forward-looking assessments at 7-month and seasonal horizons
- Share resources with other WRAP participants during emergency conditions

This framework incentivizes PNW utilities to develop dispatchable resources — including geothermal, pumped storage, and batteries — that can fill gaps when hydroelectric generation is below average.

### 10.7 Seasonal Flow Patterns and Inter-Regional Dynamics

The PNW grid's seasonal patterns interact with other Western regions in complex ways:

| Season | PNW Status | California Status | Flow Direction | Key Dynamic |
|--------|-----------|------------------|---------------|------------|
| Spring (Mar-Jun) | Hydro surplus | Moderate demand; growing solar | PNW exports south | Intertie capacity often constraining; BPA may spill |
| Summer (Jul-Sep) | Declining hydro; moderate demand | Peak demand; solar surplus midday, evening deficit | Bidirectional | CA solar exports midday; PNW hydro exports evening |
| Fall (Oct-Nov) | Lowest hydro; mild demand | Declining solar; fire-weather risk | CA exports north (sometimes) | Both regions may be tight; market prices spike |
| Winter (Dec-Feb) | Moderate hydro; heating demand | Moderate demand; low solar | Mixed | PNW draws on storage; limited export capacity |

This seasonal complementarity is the foundation of western grid economics. Geothermal power, as a non-seasonal resource, would reduce the amplitude of these seasonal swings and improve grid stability in all seasons.

## 11. Geothermal as Baseload Complement

### 11.1 The Baseload Gap

As the PNW pursues 100% clean electricity (2040 in Oregon, 2045 in Washington), the grid faces a fundamental challenge: replacing the always-available generation that natural gas currently provides. Neither wind nor solar is dispatchable on demand. Hydroelectric is dispatchable from storage dams but subject to climate-driven variability across seasons and years. Nuclear (Columbia Generating Station, 1,190 MW, operating through 2043 license extension) provides steady baseload but is a single point of failure and faces uncertain long-term economics.

Geothermal energy is the one renewable resource that can provide 24/7 dispatchable generation regardless of weather, season, or hydrological conditions. A geothermal plant generates power continuously because its fuel — subsurface heat — does not vary with atmospheric conditions.

**Capacity factor comparison:**

| Resource | Typical PNW Capacity Factor | Dispatchable? | Climate-Variable? |
|----------|---------------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Geothermal | 90-95% | Yes | No |
| Nuclear | 90-93% | Yes (limited ramp) | No |
| Hydroelectric (storage) | 30-50% | Yes | Yes (precipitation) |
| Wind (onshore) | 25-35% | No | Yes (wind patterns) |
| Solar PV | 15-25% | No | Yes (clouds, season) |
| Natural gas (CCGT) | Varies (dispatched to demand) | Yes | No |

*Sources: EIA-WA, EIA-OR, GEO-RISE. Capacity factors are approximate regional averages.*

Geothermal's 90-95% capacity factor — demonstrated at Neal Hot Springs and confirmed at hundreds of plants worldwide — makes it the renewable resource closest in operational character to natural gas or nuclear. The PNW's clean energy mandates require displacing natural gas; geothermal can fill that role without introducing the weather-dependence of wind and solar or the political complexity of new nuclear.

### 11.2 Cascade Range Volcanic Heat

The Cascade Range contains the largest concentration of potentially exploitable geothermal heat in the PNW. The volcanic chain includes:

| Volcano | State | Elevation (ft) | Last Eruption | Geothermal Relevance |
|---------|-------|----------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Mount Baker | WA | 10,781 | 1880 (fumaroles active) | Active fumaroles indicate high subsurface heat; wilderness proximity |
| Glacier Peak | WA | 10,541 | ~1700 | Remote; limited access and data |
| Mount Rainier | WA | 14,411 | ~1894 | Extensive fumarolic activity; National Park restricts development |
| Mount St. Helens | WA | 8,363 | 2004-2008 | Most recently active; National Volcanic Monument |
| Mount Adams | WA | 12,281 | ~950 CE | Large volume shield volcano; moderate heat flow data |
| Mount Hood | OR | 11,249 | ~1865 | Active fumaroles; Portland's water supply watershed |
| Mount Jefferson | OR | 10,497 | Unknown (Holocene) | Wilderness area; limited data |
| Three Sisters | OR | 10,358 | ~440 CE | Significant heat flow anomaly; nearby Newberry |
| Newberry Volcano | OR | 7,985 | ~690 CE | Highest measured temperatures; AltaRock EGS site |
| Crater Lake (Mazama) | OR | 8,159 | ~5700 BCE (caldera-forming) | National Park; no development possible |

**The paradox of proximity:**

The highest-temperature geothermal resources in the PNW are associated with the most volcanically active peaks, which are also the most protected as national parks, monuments, and wilderness areas. Mount Rainier National Park, Mount St. Helens National Volcanic Monument, Crater Lake National Park, and designated wilderness areas around most other peaks effectively preclude geothermal development at the locations with the best surface indicators.

This is why Newberry Crater is so significant — it is the one major volcanic center in the PNW with both extremely high subsurface temperatures and a regulatory environment that permits energy development (Bureau of Land Management land with a geothermal lease area adjacent to the National Volcanic Monument).

### 11.3 The Great Basin Extension

Eastern Oregon's Basin and Range province — geologically distinct from the Cascade volcanics — hosts a different type of geothermal resource. Here, the earth's crust is being pulled apart by tectonic extension, creating normal faults that allow hot water to circulate from depth to shallow levels. Neal Hot Springs, the PNW's largest geothermal plant, exploits this type of resource.

**Basin and Range geothermal characteristics:**
- Moderate temperatures (typically 120-180 degrees C at 500-2,000 meters depth)
- Associated with mapped faults that provide natural fluid pathways
- Lower per-well output than Cascade volcanic systems but lower exploration risk
- Multiple prospective sites identified in Harney, Malheur, Lake, and Klamath counties
- Proven commercial viability (Neal Hot Springs operational since 2012)

The Basin and Range resources are likely the path of least resistance for near-term PNW geothermal expansion. They do not require EGS technology (conventional hydrothermal production works), they are on private or BLM land with simpler permitting than Cascade wilderness areas, and they have been partially characterized by decades of hot spring mapping and exploration drilling.

### 11.4 Resource Characterization Gap

Industry observers note that the PNW is "very much at the front end of the curve" in geothermal resource characterization (GEO-RISE). This means:

- Much of the PNW's geothermal potential is inferred from surface data (hot springs, heat flow measurements, geological mapping) rather than confirmed by drilling
- The number of deep exploration wells drilled for geothermal purposes in OR and WA is a small fraction of what has been drilled in California, Nevada, and Utah
- Subsurface temperature and permeability data is sparse outside of a few well-characterized sites (Newberry, Neal Hot Springs, Klamath Falls)
- The Washington Geothermal Favorability Model (completed under the 2024 Geothermal Resources Act) represents the first comprehensive statewide assessment for Washington

Closing this characterization gap is the highest-leverage near-term investment for PNW geothermal development. Every dollar spent on exploration reduces the uncertainty that currently deters private investment. The DOE's geothermal play fairway analysis program — which maps subsurface geothermal potential using integrated geological, geochemical, and geophysical data — has funded several PNW assessments, including work by AltaRock in the Cascades and by university researchers in eastern Oregon.

### 11.5 Geothermal Development Scenarios

Based on current resource data, policy frameworks, and technology trajectories, three development scenarios for PNW geothermal emerge:

**Near-term (2025-2030): Incremental expansion**
- 2-4 additional conventional hydrothermal plants in eastern Oregon Basin and Range, each 10-30 MW
- Direct-use expansion in Klamath Falls and Lakeview (thermal only)
- CWU campus geothermal system operational (heating)
- Exploration drilling at 3-5 new sites under state and federal incentive programs
- Cumulative PNW geothermal capacity: ~50-100 MW (from current ~24 MW)

**Medium-term (2030-2040): EGS demonstration and first commercial projects**
- Newberry Crater EGS commercial operation (50-100 MW initial phase)
- 1-2 additional EGS demonstration sites in the Cascades or Basin and Range
- MmWave drilling technology matures from demonstration to commercial availability
- Conventional hydrothermal continues to expand at favorable Basin and Range sites
- Cumulative PNW geothermal capacity: ~200-500 MW

**Long-term (2040-2060): Mature EGS deployment**
- EGS technology proven and costs declining through learning curve
- Multiple Cascade Range EGS projects accessing superhot resources (>400 degrees C)
- Geothermal provides 5-15% of PNW electricity generation
- Combined with hydro, wind, solar, and storage, enables fully clean grid
- Cumulative PNW geothermal capacity: ~1,000-3,000 MW

These scenarios are illustrative, not predictive. The actual trajectory depends on EGS technology maturation, drilling cost reductions, permitting timelines, and the relative economics of geothermal versus other clean energy options. The one near-certainty is that geothermal's share of the PNW grid will grow from its current <0.3% — the combination of state clean energy mandates, hydroelectric variability, and improving geothermal technology makes expansion economically and politically inevitable.

## 12. Cross-Module Integration

### 12.1 Thermal Energy Connections

Module F connects to the broader Thermal & Hydrogen Energy Systems research through several technical pathways:

**Module A (HVAC and Heat Pumps):**
- Geothermal direct-use heating (Klamath Falls, Lakeview, CWU) is the original heat pump — extracting thermal energy from the ground for building heating
- Ground-source heat pumps (Module A) and geothermal power plants (Module F) both exploit subsurface thermal gradients, differing in scale and depth
- Cross-reference: A-COP, A-GRID

**Module B (Waste Heat Recovery):**
- Geothermal power plants, particularly binary-cycle facilities like Neal Hot Springs, are essentially waste heat recovery systems — extracting useful energy from a hot fluid stream
- Binary-cycle ORC technology (Module B: B-ORC) is the same technology used in geothermal binary plants
- Cross-reference: B-ORC, B-TEG

**Module C (Catalytic Conversion):**
- Limited direct connection, though hydrogen production from geothermal heat (high-temperature electrolysis) connects to catalytic hydrogen applications
- Cross-reference: C-TWC (vehicle emissions context for hydrogen fuel cells)

**Module D (Fuel Cell Technology):**
- Geothermal-powered electrolysis could produce green hydrogen for fuel cell applications
- Baseload geothermal electricity is well-suited to continuous electrolyzer operation (avoiding the intermittency challenges of solar/wind-powered electrolysis)
- Cross-reference: D-PEM, D-SOFC

**Module E (Solar Electrolysis and Green Hydrogen):**
- Hydrogen as seasonal storage (Module E) complements pumped storage (Module F: F-PUMP) at different timescales
- Surplus PNW hydroelectric generation could power electrolyzers during spring runoff, producing hydrogen stored for winter use
- Cross-reference: E-AWE, E-LCOH
