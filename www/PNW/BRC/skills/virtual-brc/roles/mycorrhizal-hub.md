## What This Role Does

Beneath the forest floor, mycorrhizal networks connect trees across vast distances. A Douglas fir seedling struggling in shade receives sugars from a distant mother tree through fungal threads it cannot see. The Mycorrhizal Hub operates the Burning Man Hive — the co-learning and knowledge transfer center where participants teach each other skills, share expertise, and build the collective intelligence of the community.

Volunteers facilitate workshops, coordinate teaching schedules, manage classroom spaces, and help match teachers with students. The Hive is one of the most participatory spaces at Burning Man — anyone can teach, anyone can learn, and the role of the volunteer is to make that exchange as frictionless as possible.

In the virtual city, the Mycorrhizal Hub teaches facilitation, knowledge curation, and the art of creating conditions where learning happens naturally. The hub does not generate content — it connects people who have knowledge with people who want it, exactly as mycorrhizal networks connect trees that have resources with trees that need them.

## Skills Taught

1. Workshop facilitation and learning environment design
2. Knowledge curation and curriculum scheduling
3. Matchmaking between teachers and learners
4. Space management for diverse concurrent activities
5. Community engagement and participation encouragement

## Dependencies

- **Requires:** Old Growth Assembly (venue and gathering infrastructure), Hemlock Reference (information routing)
- **Enables:** All camps (cross-pollination of skills), Raven Archive (documentation of teachings)

## Completion Recognition

Hub facilitators earn the Mycelium stamp after coordinating a minimum number of workshop sessions. The Mother Tree endorsement recognizes volunteers who facilitate sustained knowledge transfer across multiple domains or who develop new teaching formats. The hub tracks workshop attendance and participant feedback as quality signals.

## PNW Connection

Mycorrhizal networks are the Pacific Northwest's underground internet — fungal threads connecting 90% of forest plants into a shared resource network. Research in PNW old-growth forests first revealed the "wood wide web," showing that trees actively share nutrients through these connections. The Hive operates on the same principle: invisible infrastructure enabling visible growth.
