## 1. Class Mammalia — Mammals

The PNW hosts 65+ native mammal species across all elevation bands, from alpine-specialist pikas to wide-ranging carnivores. Mammals serve critical ecosystem roles as seed dispersers, nutrient transporters (the salmon-bear pathway), ecosystem engineers (beaver), and apex/mesopredators shaping community structure. Several PNW mammals are flagship species for conservation — their protection drives landscape-scale habitat preservation benefiting hundreds of co-occurring species.

### Cougar (*Puma concolor*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Felidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest native felid in the PNW, 100-200 lbs, tawny coat, long black-tipped tail. Solitary, crepuscular to nocturnal. Powerful ambush predator capable of killing prey 5-7 times its weight.

**Elevation Range:** 0-9,500 ft (y=-41-196)
**Distribution:** Throughout forested PNW from sea level to subalpine; most common in remote, mountainous terrain with adequate ungulate prey. Washington and Oregon together support an estimated 5,000-6,000 individuals.

**Ecological Significance:**
Primary apex predator in PNW terrestrial ecosystems where wolves are absent (most of the region). Exerts top-down control on deer and elk populations, preventing overbrowsing that would degrade forest regeneration and riparian vegetation. Creates "landscape of fear" that keeps ungulates moving, reducing localized overgrazing — an effect demonstrated in Olympic National Park where cougar presence maintains riparian vegetation critical for salmon habitat.

**Threats:** Habitat fragmentation from development and roads; human-wildlife conflict in expanding urban-wildland interface; vehicle strikes; prey base decline in some areas.

**Cultural Significance:** Cougar features in oral traditions of multiple PNW nations. Significant in Sahaptin and Interior Salish cosmology as a powerful, solitary hunter. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** WDFW cougar management plan; Ripple & Beschta (2006) — trophic cascades; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Endangered (western WA/OR) | WA: Endangered | OR: Endangered | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest wild canid in North America, 80-130 lbs in PNW. Gray to black pelage common in Cascade populations. Social, pack-hunting apex predator with complex family structure.

**Elevation Range:** 1,000-8,000 ft (y=-17-159)
**Distribution:** Recolonizing Washington and Oregon from source populations in Idaho and British Columbia. As of 2025, approximately 35+ packs documented in Washington (mostly northeast and north Cascades), with expanding range into southern Cascades. Oregon has 20+ confirmed packs, primarily in northeast.

**Ecological Significance:**
The textbook apex predator driving trophic cascades — wolf reintroduction in Yellowstone demonstrated how apex predators reshape entire landscapes by altering elk behavior, allowing willow and aspen recovery along streams, which improved beaver habitat and songbird populations. PNW wolf recovery is expected to produce similar cascading benefits in degraded riparian zones. Wolf packs also regulate coyote populations (mesopredator control), benefiting ground-nesting birds and small mammals.

**Threats:** Livestock depredation conflicts leading to lethal removal; illegal killing; slow recovery rate due to large territory requirements (200-500 sq mi per pack); highway mortality; social tolerance limits near rural communities.

**Cultural Significance:** Wolf holds deep spiritual significance across PNW nations. Wolf clan/crest in Kwakwaka'wakw, Tlingit, and Nuu-chah-nulth traditions. Nez Perce Nation was instrumental in Yellowstone wolf reintroduction. (CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** WDFW Wolf Conservation and Management Plan; ODFW wolf monitoring reports; Ripple et al. (2014) — trophic cascades review

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-terrestrial: Coyote (mesopredator regulated by wolves), Roosevelt elk, Black-tailed deer (prey)

### Coyote (*Canis latrans*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Unclassified | OR: Unprotected | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-sized canid, 25-40 lbs, grizzled gray-brown coat, pointed ears, bushy tail. Highly adaptable omnivore found from wilderness to urban centers. Distinctive yip-howl vocalizations.

**Elevation Range:** 0-9,000 ft (y=-41-184)
**Distribution:** Ubiquitous across PNW from urban Seattle/Portland to alpine meadows. Most abundant mid-level predator in the region. Expanded range following wolf extirpation in early 1900s.

**Ecological Significance:**
Primary mesopredator in PNW ecosystems. Controls rodent, rabbit, and ground-squirrel populations. Where wolves are absent, coyote populations increase (mesopredator release), which can suppress ground-nesting birds and small mammals. Coyotes also serve as important scavengers, consuming winter-killed ungulates and salmon carcasses.

**Threats:** Generally secure; persecuted through predator control programs despite resilience; urbanization creates human-wildlife conflicts; mange (Sarcoptes scabiei) outbreaks.

**Cultural Significance:** Coyote is the central Trickster figure in oral traditions across virtually all PNW nations — Sahaptin, Chinook, Coast Salish, Klamath, Nez Perce. Coyote stories carry moral teachings, explain natural phenomena, and encode ecological knowledge. (CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); Bekoff & Gese (2003) — coyote ecology; WDFW species accounts

### Bobcat (*Lynx rufus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Felidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-sized cat, 15-35 lbs, spotted coat with short "bobbed" tail, prominent ear tufts. Stealthy ambush predator, primarily nocturnal and crepuscular.

**Elevation Range:** 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout lowland and montane PNW; most common in broken, brushy terrain with adequate prey cover. Avoids deep snow, limiting upper elevation range.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important mesopredator controlling rabbit, rodent, and ground-squirrel populations. Bobcats suppress snowshoe hare populations in lowland forests and regulate mountain beaver in riparian zones. Their stealth hunting strategy makes them effective predators on ground-nesting birds, exerting selective pressure that maintains anti-predator behaviors.

**Threats:** Habitat fragmentation; trapping pressure; rodenticide secondary poisoning; vehicle mortality near urban-wildland interface.

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); WDFW game management data

### Pacific Fisher (*Pekania pennanti*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Proposed Threatened (West Coast DPS) | WA: Endangered | OR: Sensitive-Critical | IUCN: LC (species-wide)

**Description:** Large mustelid, 4-12 lbs, dark brown to black fur, bushy tail. Despite its name, does not fish — named by early trappers. Arboreal agility allows it to hunt porcupines and tree squirrels.

**Elevation Range:** 1,500-5,500 ft (y=-16-97)
**Distribution:** Historically throughout PNW montane forests; reduced to remnant populations by 1950s due to trapping and habitat loss. Reintroduced to Olympic Peninsula (2008-2010, 90 animals) and southern Cascades of Washington (2015-2020, 89 animals). Oregon populations persist in southwestern Cascades and Siskiyou Mountains.

**Ecological Significance:**
Old-growth obligate predator requiring large-diameter hollow trees and snags for denning. One of few predators capable of killing porcupines, making it an important regulator of porcupine damage to conifers. Fishers require large home ranges (10-30 sq mi) of contiguous mature forest, making them an indicator of forest landscape connectivity.

**Threats:** Historical trapping (now closed); old-growth habitat loss from logging; rodenticide poisoning from illegal cannabis cultivation on public lands (documented mortality factor in southern OR/northern CA); vehicle mortality; small population vulnerability.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in available PNW ethnographic sources; fisher pelts were trade items in the historic fur trade.

**Key Sources:** USFWS species status assessment; Lewis et al. — Olympic/Cascades reintroduction monitoring; WDFW recovery plan

**Cross-Module References:**

- See ecological-networks: Old-Growth Food Web (mesopredator, connectivity indicator)

### Pacific Marten (*Martes caurina*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed (Coastal DPS under review) | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small mustelid, 1-3 lbs, golden-brown to dark brown fur with lighter orange-buff throat patch. Agile climber with semi-retractable claws. Solitary and primarily crepuscular.

**Elevation Range:** 3,000-8,000 ft (y=34-159)
**Distribution:** Montane and subalpine forests throughout Cascades, Olympics, and Blue Mountains. Coastal populations (Olympic Peninsula) severely reduced; interior populations more stable.

**Ecological Significance:**
Mesopredator specializing in subnivean (under-snow) hunting of voles and mice during winter, when deep snowpack provides insulating cover. Martens require complex forest structure with abundant downed wood for denning and hunting. As a snow-adapted predator, marten populations are sensitive indicators of snowpack duration and forest structural complexity.

**Threats:** Old-growth habitat loss; reduced snowpack duration (climate change); trapping (regulated); small population isolation in fragmented forests.

**Key Sources:** Moriarty et al. — coastal marten conservation; WDFW species accounts; USFS PNW Research Station

### American Mink (*Neogale vison*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-STREAM, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Furbearer | OR: Furbearer | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Semi-aquatic mustelid, 1.5-3.5 lbs, sleek dark brown fur with white chin patch. Excellent swimmer; hunts both aquatic and terrestrial prey.

**Elevation Range:** 0-4,000 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW along waterways — streams, rivers, lake margins, estuaries, and coastal shorelines. Most common in lowland riparian habitats.

**Ecological Significance:**
Riparian mesopredator linking aquatic and terrestrial food webs. Preys on crayfish, fish, amphibians, muskrats, and ground-nesting waterfowl. Mink presence indicates healthy riparian corridor connectivity; their absence from degraded streams signals habitat quality decline.

**Threats:** Water pollution (bioaccumulation of PCBs and heavy metals); riparian habitat degradation; trapping (regulated).

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); WDFW furbearer management

### Long-tailed Weasel (*Neogale frenata*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small, slender mustelid, 5-12 oz, brown above with white/yellow belly, black-tipped tail. Turns white in winter at higher elevations (ermine phase). Body proportions allow pursuit of rodents into burrows.

**Elevation Range:** 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW from lowland meadows to montane forests. Most common in open or edge habitats with abundant rodent prey.

**Ecological Significance:**
Specialist rodent predator capable of entering vole and mouse burrows. A single weasel can consume 30-40% of its body weight daily, making it one of the most effective small-mammal predators per unit body mass. Important in regulating vole populations that can damage tree plantations and agricultural crops.

**Threats:** Generally secure; secondary rodenticide poisoning; habitat conversion of grasslands and meadows.

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); Sheffield & Thomas (1997)

### Striped Skunk (*Mephitis mephitis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mephitidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-sized carnivore, 6-14 lbs, unmistakable black and white pattern. Potent chemical defense (mercaptan spray). Omnivorous, digging for grubs and insects.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW lowlands, most common near human habitation, agricultural lands, and riparian zones.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important omnivore consuming insects, grubs, rodents, eggs, and fruit. Digging behavior aerates soil and exposes invertebrates. Significant predator on ground-nesting bird and turtle eggs, which can be problematic for declining species like western pond turtle and streaked horned lark.

**Threats:** Vehicle mortality (leading cause of death); rabies (less common in PNW); habitat loss in urban areas.

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); WDFW species accounts

### North American River Otter (*Lontra canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Furbearer | OR: Furbearer | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large semi-aquatic mustelid, 10-30 lbs, sleek brown fur, webbed feet, powerful rudder-like tail. Highly social, often seen in family groups. Playful behavior (sliding) serves social bonding and scent marking.

**Elevation Range:** 0-4,000 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW waterways — rivers, lakes, estuaries, and coastal shorelines including Puget Sound and outer coast. Populations recovered significantly after water quality improvements and trapping regulation.

**Ecological Significance:**
Top aquatic predator in freshwater systems, consuming fish (primarily non-game species like sculpin and suckers), crayfish, and amphibians. River otters transport marine-derived nutrients from estuaries into freshwater systems through defecation at latrine sites. Latrine sites concentrate nutrients that support riparian plant growth. Otter presence indicates healthy aquatic ecosystems with adequate fish populations and water quality.

**Threats:** Water pollution (bioaccumulation of contaminants); habitat degradation; vehicle mortality near waterway crossings.

**Cultural Significance:** Otter features in Coast Salish and Chinook narratives. Otter pelts were important trade items; otter imagery appears in Northwest Coast art. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); WDFW furbearer management; Bowyer et al. (2003)

### Cascade Red Fox (*Vulpes vulpes cascadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Canidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Candidate | IUCN: LC (species-wide; subspecies not assessed)

**Description:** Small, high-elevation fox subspecies, 7-12 lbs, variable pelage from classic red to cross (reddish-brown with dark dorsal cross) to melanistic. Genetically distinct from lowland red foxes, which are non-native introductions.

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-10,000 ft (y=84-222)
**Distribution:** Endemic to the Cascade Range from southern BC to central Oregon, restricted to alpine and subalpine habitats above 5,000 ft. One of the rarest carnivores in the PNW — total population estimated in low hundreds.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only native red fox in the PNW — lowland red foxes throughout the region are descendants of fur farm escapees and deliberate introductions from eastern North America. Cascade red fox is a glacial relict, isolated at high elevation since the end of the Pleistocene. Important alpine mesopredator controlling pika, marmot, and vole populations.

**Threats:** Climate change compressing alpine habitat; genetic swamping from hybridization with non-native lowland red foxes; small population size; disease transmission from domestic dogs and lowland foxes.

**Key Sources:** Aubry et al. (2009) — genetic distinctiveness; Akins et al. — Mt. Rainier population; WDFW candidate species assessment

### Raccoon (*Procyon lotor*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Procyonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-URBAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Furbearer | OR: Furbearer | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-sized omnivore, 10-30 lbs, distinctive black facial mask and ringed tail. Highly dexterous forepaws used for manipulating food. Primarily nocturnal.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW lowlands, most abundant near water and human habitation. Urban populations have increased dramatically.

**Ecological Significance:**
Omnivorous mesopredator with broad dietary breadth — crayfish, amphibians, bird eggs, fruit, garbage. In urban settings, raccoon populations reach high densities (10-25x natural levels), creating significant predation pressure on ground-nesting birds, turtles, and amphibians. This mesopredator release effect (amplified by absence of wolves and cougars in urban areas) is a significant but underappreciated threat to sensitive urban wildlife.

**Threats:** Canine distemper and rabies; vehicle mortality; human-wildlife conflict.

**Key Sources:** Johnson & O'Neil (2001); Gehrt et al. (2010) — urban carnivore ecology

### Roosevelt Elk (*Cervus canadensis roosevelti*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest elk subspecies in North America; bulls 700-1,100 lbs, cows 500-600 lbs. Dark brown neck, lighter tan body. Males carry massive antlers (up to 4 ft) shed annually. The defining large herbivore of PNW coastal forests.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Olympic Peninsula (largest population, ~5,000 in Olympic NP), northern Oregon Coast Range, Cascade foothills, and scattered herds in southwestern Washington. The Olympic population is the largest unmanaged elk herd in the PNW, providing a natural reference for elk-forest interactions.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone herbivore shaping forest structure and meadow maintenance. Elk browsing creates and maintains riparian openings, preventing canopy closure along streams — this maintains habitat complexity critical for salmon. In Olympic's Hoh Valley, elk browsing creates the distinctive "browse line" visible in old-growth forest where all understory vegetation is cropped to ~5 ft. Elk are also major vectors of seed dispersal through pelage transport and fecal deposition.

**Threats:** Habitat fragmentation; disease (hoof rot epidemic in southwestern WA herds — treponeme-associated, causing severe lameness and mortality); competition with livestock; vehicle collisions along Highway 101.

**Cultural Significance:** Central to Quinault, Quileute, Makah, and other Olympic Peninsula nations. Elk provided food, hides, bone tools, and sinew. Quinault Nation has treaty-reserved elk hunting rights on the Olympic Peninsula. Roosevelt elk are named for Theodore Roosevelt, who established Olympic National Monument (1909) partly to protect this subspecies. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** WDFW elk management plan; Jenkins et al. — Olympic elk ecology; Houston et al. (1990)

### Black-tailed Deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Cervidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** West-side subspecies of mule deer, 100-200 lbs. Distinctive dark brown/black tail upperside. Smaller than elk but far more numerous — the most abundant large herbivore in western PNW.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout western WA and OR from coastal forests to Cascade crest. Altitudinal migration: summer in subalpine meadows, winter in lowland forests. Urban-adapted populations common in suburban areas.

**Ecological Significance:**
Primary prey base for cougar and recovering wolf populations — deer population dynamics drive predator-prey cycles across PNW forests. Selective browsing shapes understory composition, favoring browse-resistant species (sword fern, salal) over palatable ones (western red cedar seedlings, huckleberry). In areas without predators, deer overabundance causes "browse deserts" that eliminate forest regeneration — a documented problem in suburban forests and some islands.

**Threats:** Vehicle mortality (estimated 3,000+/year in WA alone); chronic wasting disease (CWD) detected in neighboring states, not yet confirmed in WA/OR; urban-wildland interface conflicts; habitat fragmentation.

**Cultural Significance:** Important food source for all west-side PNW nations. Hide processing was a major craft. Deer feature in oral traditions across Coast Salish, Chinook, and Kalapuya nations. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL)

**Key Sources:** WDFW deer management plan; Johnson & O'Neil (2001); McCorquodale et al. — urban deer ecology

### Mountain Goat (*Oreamnos americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Bovidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species (limited permits) | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Stocky, white-furred ungulate, 150-300 lbs, with black horns (both sexes), beard, and pantaloon-like leg fur. Built for steep, rocky terrain — rubberized hooves provide extraordinary grip on near-vertical cliff faces.

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-10,500 ft (y=84-222)
**Distribution:** Native to Cascades and Olympics (debated — may be introduced to Olympics). Core populations on Mt. Rainier, Mt. Baker, North Cascades, Glacier Peak. Olympic population recently reduced through NPS management to protect endemic alpine plants.

**Ecological Significance:**
Alpine specialist whose grazing and mineral lick use create disturbance patches that maintain habitat heterogeneity in alpine meadows. Mountain goat trails create erosion channels that transport alpine sediment downslope. Their presence at mineral licks concentrates nutrients (urine, feces) that create localized "fertility islands" supporting distinct plant communities. In the Olympics, non-native goats damaged endemic alpine plants, prompting controversial removal — illustrating how herbivore impacts depend on evolutionary context.

**Threats:** Climate change reducing alpine habitat; disturbance from recreational climbing and helicopter traffic; hunting management balances (limited permits maintain genetic diversity); avalanche mortality.

**Cultural Significance:** Mountain goat wool was the primary material for Salish blanket weaving (Coast Salish, Squamish, Musqueam) — among the most technically accomplished textiles in world history. Wool was gathered from natural shedding sites and snag bushes. Chilkat weaving (Tlingit, Tsimshian) also uses mountain goat wool for ceremonial blankets of extraordinary complexity. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** WDFW mountain goat management plan; NPS Olympic goat management EIS; Houston et al. — Cascade goat ecology

### North American Beaver (*Castor canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Castoridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-STREAM, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER, ROLE-KEYSTONE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Furbearer | OR: Furbearer | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest North American rodent, 35-70 lbs. Broad flat tail, webbed hind feet, orange-enameled incisors that grow continuously. Semi-aquatic, primarily nocturnal.

**Elevation Range:** 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW wherever suitable stream and riparian habitat exists, from coastal lowlands to montane valleys. Recovering from near-extirpation during the fur trade era.

**Ecological Significance:**
The preeminent ecosystem engineer of PNW freshwater systems. A single beaver dam complex transforms stream ecosystems: raising water tables 2-3 ft for 100+ meters upstream; creating wetland habitat from dry meadow; trapping sediment that builds floodplain soils; slowing flood peaks by 10-30%; raising base flows during summer drought by 20-40%; moderating stream temperatures through deeper pools and groundwater input; and increasing salmonid rearing habitat by 200-300%. The PNW once supported an estimated 10-20 million beavers; current populations are 5-10% of historical levels. Beaver dam analogs (BDAs) are now a primary stream restoration tool, mimicking beaver effects where natural recolonization is slow.

**Threats:** Historical trapping (near-extirpation by 1900); ongoing conflicts with landowners (flooding of roads, agricultural land); removal permits still issued; culvert barriers preventing recolonization.

**Cultural Significance:** Beaver pelts drove the PNW fur trade that brought European colonization (Hudson's Bay Company, North West Company). Before contact, beaver was food, medicine, and material for many PNW nations. Beaver management practices by Indigenous peoples maintained productive wetland landscapes. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

**Key Sources:** Pollock et al. (2014) — beaver and salmon; Bouwes et al. (2016) — beaver dam analogs; WDFW beaver management

### Douglas Squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Sciuridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND

**Elevation Range:** 500-6,500 ft (y=-29-121)

Key prey species for northern goshawk, Pacific marten, and northern spotted owl.

**Threats:** Generally secure; old-growth loss reduces prime habitat; competition from introduced eastern gray squirrels in urban-forest interface.

### Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Sciuridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Nocturnal gliding squirrel, 4-6 oz, large dark eyes, loose lateral skin folds (patagia) enabling glides of 50-150 ft between trees. Soft gray-brown fur with white belly.

**Elevation Range:** 500-6,000 ft (y=-29-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW conifer forests, most abundant in old-growth and mature stands with high truffle production.

**Ecological Significance:**
The primary prey of the northern spotted owl — flying squirrel abundance directly limits spotted owl productivity. Flying squirrels are also the primary dispersal vector for hypogeous (underground) mycorrhizal fungi (truffles), which constitute 70-90% of their diet. When squirrels consume truffles and defecate throughout the forest, they inoculate new areas with mycorrhizal spores essential for conifer root health. This three-way mutualism (tree-fungus-squirrel) is a keystone interaction in old-growth forest ecosystems.

**Threats:** Old-growth loss (reduces truffle abundance and nesting cavities); barred owl predation (barred owls prey more on flying squirrels than spotted owls do, potentially creating competitive exclusion); habitat fragmentation.

**Key Sources:** Carey et al. (2002) — flying squirrel ecology; Maser et al. (1978) — truffle-mammal-tree mutualism; USFS PNW Research

### Hoary Marmot (*Marmota caligata*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Sciuridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large, stocky marmot, 8-20 lbs, silver-gray "hoary" fur with dark face and feet. Colonial, living in family groups in rock talus. High-pitched whistle alarm call audible across alpine basins — known as "whistle pig."

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-9,500 ft (y=84-196)

### American Pika (*Ochotona princeps*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Ochotonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern (petitioned for ESA listing, denied 2010) | WA: Not listed | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small lagomorph (not a rodent), 5-6 oz, round body, short ears, no visible tail. Gray-brown fur. Distinctive sharp "eek!" alarm call from talus slopes. Does not hibernate — survives winter by caching dried vegetation ("haypiles") under rocks.

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-10,500 ft (y=84-222)
**Distribution:** Talus slopes throughout Cascades and Olympics at alpine and subalpine elevations. Populations occur as isolated "sky islands" on individual peaks, with limited connectivity between them.

**Ecological Significance:**
The defining climate change indicator species in PNW alpine ecosystems. Pikas are physiologically intolerant of temperatures above 78F (25.5C) — even brief heat exposure can be lethal. As temperatures warm, pika populations are retreating upslope, with extirpations documented at lower-elevation sites across the Great Basin and southern Cascades. Each mountain's pika population is effectively isolated (pikas cannot cross low-elevation valleys), making upslope retreat a one-way path toward summit extinction. PNW populations are currently more stable than Great Basin populations due to maritime climate buffering and deeper talus insulation, but continued warming threatens long-term persistence.

**Threats:** Climate change (primary threat — thermal stress and reduced snowpack insulation); habitat isolation on mountain "sky islands"; reduced forage quality with changing plant communities; disease (unconfirmed).

**Cultural Significance:** Not prominently featured in PNW Indigenous traditions, though observed by alpine-traveling peoples. Pika haypile behavior was noted by early ethnographers among Interior Plateau nations. (CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Smith & Weston (1990) — pika thermoregulation; Beever et al. (2016) — pika range contraction; ODFW sensitive species assessment

### Snowshoe Hare (*Lepus americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Lagomorpha > Leporidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-sized hare, 3-4 lbs. Seasonal coat change: brown in summer, white in winter (photoperiod-triggered). Large hind feet act as snowshoes, distributing weight on soft snow.

**Elevation Range:** 1,000-8,000 ft (y=-17-159)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests with dense understory cover. Most common in regenerating forest and brushy habitats.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone prey species — snowshoe hare populations drive predator-prey dynamics for lynx, bobcat, coyote, great horned owl, and goshawk. Classic 10-year population cycles documented in boreal systems are less pronounced in PNW but still detectable. This mismatch is increasing mortality rates.

**Threats:** Climate-driven camouflage mismatch (primary emerging threat); habitat loss in dense understory from fire suppression and plantation forestry; predation amplified by snow timing changes.

### Mountain Beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Aplodontiidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Unclassified | OR: Unprotected | IUCN: LC | NatureServe: G5

**Description:** Primitive rodent (not a true beaver), 2-3 lbs, stocky with vestigial tail, small eyes and ears. The most primitive living rodent — sole surviving member of family Aplodontiidae, unchanged for 40+ million years. Fossorial, burrowing in moist hillsides.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Endemic to Pacific coast from southern BC to northern CA. PNW endemic — found nowhere else on Earth. Most abundant in western WA and OR in areas with deep, moist soils.

**Ecological Significance:**
A living fossil and PNW endemic. Burrowing activity creates extensive tunnel systems that aerate soil, redistribute nutrients, and create drainage channels on steep slopes. Burrow systems provide shelter for amphibians, invertebrates, and small mammals. Mountain beavers have the lowest metabolic rate of any mammal their size and cannot concentrate urine, requiring constant access to water and succulent vegetation — making them obligate residents of wet PNW forests.

**Threats:** Considered a pest in timber plantations (girdling seedlings); poisoning campaigns on managed forests; habitat conversion; generally secure due to PNW's extensive forest cover.

### Red Tree Vole (*Arborimus longicaudus*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Cricetidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Candidate (north Oregon coast DPS) | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: NT

**Description:** Tiny arboreal vole, 0.7-1.3 oz, reddish-brown fur, long prehensile tail. One of the most specialized mammals in the PNW — spends entire life in old-growth conifer canopy, never voluntarily descending to ground.

**Elevation Range:** 200-4,000 ft (y=-34-9)
**Distribution:** Western Oregon and extreme southwestern Washington. Restricted to old-growth and mature Douglas-fir forests with developed canopy structure. One of the rarest mammals in the PNW.

**Ecological Significance:**
Extreme old-growth specialist — feeds almost exclusively on Douglas-fir needles (the least nutritious rodent diet known), building elaborate stick-and-needle nests high in the canopy that can reach basketball size over generations. Nests provide microhabitat for invertebrates, salamanders, and fungi. Red tree vole abundance is one of the best indicators of old-growth forest condition — their absence from structurally simplified forests demonstrates the irreplaceability of old-growth canopy architecture.

**Threats:** Old-growth harvest (primary threat); windthrow of isolated legacy trees; predation by spotted owls and barred owls (spotted owl predation is natural; barred owl predation may be additive).

### North American Porcupine (*Erethizon dorsatum*)

**Taxonomy:** Rodentia > Erethizontidae

### Little Brown Myotis (*Myotis lucifugus*)

**Taxonomy:** Chiroptera > Vespertilionidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Conservation Status:** Federal: Under review for ESA listing | WA: Not listed | IUCN: EN

**Description:** Small insectivorous bat, 0.2-0.5 oz, glossy brown fur, 9-11 inch wingspan. One of the most common bats in the PNW. Echolocation for hunting; forms maternity colonies of hundreds in buildings, bridges, and large-diameter snags.

### American Black Bear (*Ursus americanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Ursidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest terrestrial carnivore in the PNW (in the absence of grizzly), 150-400 lbs. Color varies from black to brown to cinnamon (brown phase common in Cascades). Omnivorous despite classification as carnivore — diet is 85-95% plant material.

**Elevation Range:** 0-9,000 ft (y=-41-184)
**Distribution:** Throughout forested PNW. WA estimated 25,000-30,000; OR estimated 25,000-30,000. Altitudinal migration follows food: lowland berries in summer, salmon streams in fall, subalpine huckleberry fields in late summer.

**Ecological Significance:**
The primary terrestrial vector for marine-derived nutrients in PNW forests. Bears catch spawning salmon and carry carcasses into the forest (average 40-80 meters from stream), where unconsumed portions decompose and release marine nitrogen (identifiable by its N-15 isotope signature). Studies at salmon streams show that trees within 500m of bear-fished streams grow 3x faster than comparable trees without salmon-bear nutrient input. Bear fecal deposits also disperse berry seeds (huckleberry, salmonberry, elderberry) across large home ranges (10-100 sq mi), maintaining genetic connectivity between plant populations. This salmon-bear-tree pathway is one of the most important marine-terrestrial nutrient subsidies on Earth.

**Threats:** Human-wildlife conflict (garbage, bird feeders, orchards); vehicle mortality; illegal killing; habitat fragmentation in lowlands; declining salmon runs reduce critical fall food.

**Cultural Significance:** Bear holds profound significance across all PNW nations. Bear ceremonialism — including first bear ceremonies, bear songs, and bear clan/crest — reflects deep reciprocal relationship. Black bear provided food, fat (critical caloric resource), fur, and medicine. Bear behavior and ecology are encoded in oral traditions that constitute sophisticated ecological knowledge of hibernation, seasonal movements, and habitat use. Specific ceremonial protocols vary by nation and are culturally restricted. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Hilderbrand et al. (1999) — salmon-bear nutrient transport; Reimchen et al. (2003) — N-15 in riparian trees; WDFW bear management plan

## 2. Class Aves — Birds

The PNW hosts 200+ breeding bird species, with additional migrants and winter visitors bringing annual totals above 350. Birds occupy every elevation band and habitat type, serving as pollinators, seed dispersers, insect predators, scavengers, and apex aerial predators. ### Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*)

**Taxonomy:** Strigiformes > Strigidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened | WA State Endangered | OR State Threatened | IUCN: NT

**Description:** Medium-large owl (16-19 in, wingspan 42 in) with dark brown plumage spotted with white on head, breast, and back. Dark eyes. Lacks ear tufts. Nocturnal hunter with asymmetric ears for sound localization.

**Elevation Range:** 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84)
**Distribution:** Old-growth and mature conifer forests from southwestern BC through western WA and OR to northwestern CA. Requires large territories (2,500-12,000 acres per pair depending on habitat quality).

**Ecological Significance:**

Its population decline drove the Northwest Forest Plan (1994), the largest ecosystem-scale conservation plan in US history, protecting 7.4 million acres of late-successional reserves on federal lands. Spotted owls require large-diameter trees with broken tops or natural cavities for nesting and complex forest structure for hunting northern flying squirrels and woodrats. Population declining at 3-5% per year across range.

**Threats:** Barred owl competition and hybridization (primary current threat — barred owl expanded from eastern North America since 1950s, is larger, more aggressive, and ecologically flexible); historical habitat loss from logging; continued old-growth fragmentation.

**Cultural Significance:** Significant in several Coast Salish and Sahaptin oral traditions. Owl's association with old-growth forest aligns with Indigenous relationships to ancient forest stands. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

### Barred Owl (*Strix varia*)

**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed (invasive in PNW) | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large owl (17-20 in, wingspan 44 in), brown and white barred plumage, dark brown eyes, round head without ear tufts. Larger and more aggressive than spotted owl. Distinctive "Who cooks for you? Who cooks for you-all?" call.

**Elevation Range:** 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW — invaded from eastern North America beginning in the 1950s, crossing the Great Plains through boreal forest corridors. Now occupies every habitat type used by spotted owls plus additional habitats (riparian, second-growth, urban-forest edge).

**Ecological Significance:**
The single greatest threat to northern spotted owl survival. Barred owls are larger, more aggressive, reproduce faster, and tolerate a wider range of habitats than spotted owls. They outcompete spotted owls for territories, prey, and nest sites. Where barred owls establish, spotted owl occupancy declines 50-90% within a decade. USFWS has initiated experimental barred owl removal in study areas to test spotted owl recovery response — the most controversial wildlife management action in current PNW conservation. Barred owls also prey on species not historically exposed to owl predation, potentially disrupting prey communities (salamanders, crayfish, small mammals).

**Threats:** Not threatened — barred owls are thriving and expanding. The threat runs in the other direction: barred owl success threatens spotted owl persistence.

**Key Sources:** Wiens et al. (2014) — barred owl competition; USFWS barred owl management strategy; Dugger et al. — spotted owl demography response

### Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*)

**Taxonomy:** Accipitriformes > Accipitridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Delisted (2007, recovered) | WA: Sensitive | OR: Threatened (state) | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Massive raptor (31-37 in, wingspan 6-8 ft), adults with iconic white head and tail, dark brown body. Immatures mottled brown for 4-5 years before achieving adult plumage. PNW's largest raptor by wingspan.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW near large water bodies. Major concentrations along Skagit River (winter — 400+ eagles on salmon carcasses), Klamath Basin, Columbia River, and San Juan Islands. WA and OR together support 2,000+ nesting pairs. One of conservation's greatest recovery stories — from 400 pairs nationally in 1963 to 70,000+ today.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex scavenger and predator linking marine and terrestrial nutrient pathways. Eagles concentrate on salmon runs, consuming carcasses and distributing marine-derived nutrients through defecation at roost trees (documented nutrient enrichment zones around eagle roosts). In winter, Skagit River eagle concentrations represent one of the largest raptor aggregations in the lower 48 states. Nest trees are almost always the largest trees near water — making eagle nesting a de facto old-growth protection mechanism.

**Threats:** Lead poisoning from ammunition fragments in gut piles (leading cause of eagle mortality); habitat loss of large nest trees near shorelines; disturbance during nesting; power line electrocution; DDT legacy (eggshell thinning resolved but contaminant accumulation continues).

**Cultural Significance:** Sacred across virtually all PNW nations. Eagle feathers are protected under the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (BGEPA), with religious use permits managed through the National Eagle Repository. Eagle is clan/crest animal for Tlingit and Haida Eagle moiety. Eagle feathers used in ceremonies by Coast Salish, Sahaptin, and many other nations. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** USFWS delisting and post-delisting monitoring; Skagit River Bald Eagle Natural Area; Stalmaster (1987) — bald eagle biology

### Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)

**Taxonomy:** Accipitriformes > Accipitridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: BGEPA protected | WA: Candidate | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large raptor (26-40 in, wingspan 6-7.5 ft), dark brown with golden nape. More powerful than bald eagle by weight — capable of killing prey up to 10 lbs. Primarily a hunter rather than scavenger.

**Elevation Range:** 2,000-10,000 ft (y=-17-222)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW mountains and open country. Nests on cliff faces in Cascades, Olympics, and eastern OR. Less common than bald eagle in western PNW; more common in eastern OR/WA open habitats.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex aerial predator of alpine and subalpine zones. Primary predator on marmots, ground squirrels, rabbits, and juvenile mountain goats. Golden eagle predation on marmot colonies creates population regulation that prevents overgrazing of alpine meadows. Territorial behavior excludes other large raptors from nesting areas, creating spatial heterogeneity in raptor community structure.

**Threats:** Wind energy development (turbine mortality — golden eagles disproportionately affected); lead poisoning from ammunition fragments; loss of open hunting habitat to tree encroachment; disturbance at nest sites from climbing and aircraft.

**Cultural Significance:** Eagle feathers (both bald and golden) hold deep ceremonial significance across PNW nations. Protected under BGEPA with tribal religious use exemptions. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

### Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*)

Goshawk nest area protection (typically 30-acre buffer) on federal lands effectively protects old-growth microsites. ### Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*)

**Taxonomy:** Accipitriformes > Pandionidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-LAKE, HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large fish-hunting raptor (21-24 in, wingspan 59-71 in), dark brown above, white below with distinctive dark eye stripe. Reversible outer toe and spiny foot pads for gripping fish. Spectacular feet-first diving technique.

**Elevation Range:** 0-4,000 ft (y=-41-9)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW near fishable waters — rivers, lakes, estuaries, coastal bays. Nests on artificial platforms, dead tree tops, power poles, and channel markers. Population recovered strongly from DDT-era declines.

**Ecological Significance:**
Specialized piscivore whose nesting success directly tracks fish population health — making osprey a visible indicator of aquatic ecosystem productivity. Nest platforms are among the largest raptor structures (6+ ft across, accumulated over decades), providing nesting substrate for smaller birds. Partially consumed fish dropped at nests provide food for scavengers including crows, eagles, and mammals.

**Threats:** Contaminant accumulation (mercury, PCBs in fish); nest disturbance; electrocution on power structures; climate-driven shifts in fish availability.

### Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*)

**Taxonomy:** Falconiformes > Falconidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Delisted (1999, recovered) | WA: Sensitive | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-large falcon (15-20 in, wingspan 40-44 in), blue-gray above, barred below, bold dark "moustache" mark. The fastest animal on Earth — stooping (diving) at speeds exceeding 200 mph to strike prey in flight.

**Elevation Range:** 0-8,000 ft (y=-41-159)
**Distribution:** Nesting on cliff faces, bridges, and tall buildings throughout PNW. Major nesting concentrations along Columbia River Gorge, San Juan Islands, and outer coast sea cliffs. Recovered from near-extinction due to DDT.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex aerial predator whose recovery is one of the great conservation success stories. Peregrines exert top-down pressure on pigeon, shorebird, and waterfowl populations. Urban-nesting peregrines control rock pigeon populations in cities. Their extreme sensitivity to pesticide-caused eggshell thinning made them the sentinel species that catalyzed the DDT ban (1972).

**Threats:** Contaminant exposure (legacy DDT, new-generation pesticides); human disturbance at nest cliffs; collision with buildings and vehicles.

**Cultural Significance:** Falconry has ancient roots, though PNW Indigenous falconry traditions are less documented than those of other regions. Peregrine feathers used in ceremony by some PNW nations. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

### Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*)

**Taxonomy:** Charadriiformes > Alcidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH (nesting), HAB-PELAGIC (foraging)
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened | WA State Threatened | OR State Threatened | IUCN: EN

**Description:** Small, chunky seabird (9-10 in), dark above, white below in winter; mottled brown in breeding plumage. Rapid, direct flight between ocean foraging areas and inland nest sites — unique among alcids.

**Elevation Range:** Nests 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84); forages at sea level
**Distribution:** Nests in old-growth conifer forests up to 50 miles inland from the outer coast. Forages in nearshore marine waters from Alaska to central California. WA and OR populations severely reduced.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most unusual nesting ecology of any seabird — murrelets fly from the ocean to nest on large, moss-covered limbs of old-growth trees, sometimes 50 miles inland and 4,000 ft elevation. This absolute dependence on old-growth forest for nesting makes the murrelet a co-indicator with spotted owl for old-growth forest conservation. Murrelets lay a single egg on a moss platform — no nest structure is built, making large-limb old-growth trees irreplaceable. Their decline directly tracks old-growth loss. At sea, murrelets dive for sand lance and other small fish, linking marine and terrestrial ecosystems through nutrient transport.

**Threats:** Old-growth habitat loss (primary); nest predation by corvids (jays, crows) whose populations are inflated by human subsidies (garbage, edge habitat); gill-net bycatch at sea; oil spill vulnerability; marine prey decline.

**Cultural Significance:** Observed by coastal PNW nations; murrelet behavior (rapid inland flights at dawn/dusk) noted in oral traditions but nesting ecology remained scientifically unknown until 1974 when the first nest was discovered in an old-growth tree.

**Key Sources:** USFWS recovery plan; Ralph et al. (1995) — murrelet ecology; Raphael et al. — population monitoring

### Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium thrush (8-10 in), striking orange breast band, blue-gray back and breast band, orange eyebrow and wing bars. The iconic sound of PNW old-growth — a single, ethereal, buzzy whistle on one pitch, followed by silence, then another on a different pitch.

**Elevation Range:** 500-6,000 ft (y=-29-109)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests, most common in mature and old-growth stands with deep shade and moist understory. Winters in lowland forests and suburban gardens.

**Ecological Significance:**
The "voice of the old-growth forest" — varied thrush song defines the acoustic character of PNW ancient forests. Forages by leaf-litter flipping, consuming invertebrates (earthworms, beetles, snails) and berries. Important seed disperser for understory shrubs (huckleberry, salal, elderberry). Nests in dense conifer branches, often in old-growth trees. Their preference for deep-shade, undisturbed forest makes them a cultural and acoustic indicator of forest quality.

**Threats:** Old-growth loss reduces preferred habitat; window strikes during migration; cat predation in suburban wintering areas.

**Key Sources:** George (2000) — varied thrush ecology; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Piciformes > Picidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest woodpecker in the PNW (16-19 in), black with white wing patches, prominent red crest. Powerful chisel-shaped bill creates rectangular excavation holes diagnostic of this species. Loud, rolling "cuk-cuk-cuk" call.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,500 ft (y=-41-97)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests with large-diameter trees and snags. Requires minimum 100-acre territories with multiple large snags for foraging and nesting.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone cavity excavator — pileated woodpecker cavities are used by 30+ species that cannot create their own (wood ducks, saw-whet owls, flying squirrels, martens, bats, swifts). Their large rectangular excavations open heartwood to decay fungi, accelerating snag decomposition and nutrient cycling. Foraging excavations expose carpenter ant colonies (primary food) and beetle larvae, creating feeding opportunities for smaller birds. Pileated woodpeckers are ecosystem engineers whose absence would dramatically reduce cavity-nesting wildlife.

**Threats:** Snag removal in managed forests (fire salvage logging, hazard tree removal); old-growth loss reducing large-diameter trees; minimum territory size makes them vulnerable to forest fragmentation.

**Cultural Significance:** Woodpecker imagery in Northwest Coast art traditions. The pileated woodpecker's crest inspired the cartoon character Woody Woodpecker. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** Bull & Jackson (1995) — pileated woodpecker ecology; Johnson & O'Neil (2001); USFS dead wood management

### Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Corvidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large, crested jay (12-13 in), deep blue body with black head and crest. Bold, intelligent, vocal — produces wide repertoire of calls including mimicry of red-tailed hawk. PNW's most conspicuous corvid in forested habitats.

**Elevation Range:** 0-7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW forests from sea level to subalpine. Common at campgrounds, picnic areas, and suburban yards adjacent to forest.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important seed disperser and scatter-hoarder — caches acorns, pine seeds, and other nuts. Also a significant nest predator on smaller birds, consuming eggs and nestlings of thrushes, warblers, and sparrows. In areas adjacent to old-growth, Steller's jay nest predation on marbled murrelet eggs is a documented threat — jay abundance increases near human food sources (campgrounds, suburbs), creating "predator subsidies" that extend into murrelet nesting habitat. This edge effect is one of the most important but least visible impacts of forest fragmentation.

**Threats:** Not threatened — benefits from human activity and forest fragmentation.

**Key Sources:** Marzluff & Balda (1992) — corvid ecology; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Corvidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium corvid (12 in), pale gray body, black wings with white patches, black central tail feathers. Long, stout bill adapted for prying seeds from pine cones. Sublingual pouch holds 50-150 seeds for transport.

**Elevation Range:** 5,000-10,500 ft (y=84-222)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW subalpine zones where whitebark pine and other large-seeded pines occur. Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains. Descends to lower elevations in winter when food is scarce.

**Ecological Significance:**
The sole effective seed disperser for whitebark pine — one of the most important mutualisms in PNW subalpine ecology. Each nutcracker caches 30,000-100,000 whitebark pine seeds per year in thousands of scattered caches, remembering locations using spatial memory. Unretrieved caches (10-30%) germinate into new trees, establishing whitebark pine in locations favorable for growth. Without nutcrackers, whitebark pine cannot regenerate because its seeds are too large and wingless for wind dispersal. As whitebark pine declines from blister rust, nutcracker populations may decline, further impairing pine recovery — a potential extinction vortex.

**Threats:** Whitebark pine decline (reduced food base); climate change reducing subalpine habitat; if food becomes insufficient, nutcrackers emigrate to lower elevations, abandoning seed caching at high elevation.

**Key Sources:** Tomback (1982) — nutcracker-pine mutualism; Tomback et al. (2001); USFS whitebark pine restoration

### Gray Jay (Canada Jay) (*Perisoreus canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Corvidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium jay (11-12 in), fluffy gray plumage, dark nape, white face. Quiet, tame, and curious — approaches hikers for food. Known as "camp robber" or "whiskey jack" (from Wisakedjak, an Algonquian trickster figure).

**Elevation Range:** 3,000-8,000 ft (y=34-159)
**Distribution:** Montane and subalpine forests throughout PNW. Most common in spruce-fir and mountain hemlock forests.

**Ecological Significance:**
Caches food (insects, berries, fungi, carrion) in bark crevices and lichen clumps using sticky saliva, creating "pantries" of 1,000+ items per bird. This food storage enables year-round residency and early breeding (February-March, while snow still covers the ground). Cache success depends on cold temperatures preserving stored food — warming winters cause cache spoilage, reducing reproductive success. Gray jays are emerging indicators of climate change impacts in subalpine forests.

**Threats:** Climate warming causing food cache spoilage — demonstrated mechanism linking warming to population decline at southern range margins.

**Key Sources:** Strickland & Ouellet (2011) — gray jay biology; Waite & Strickland (2006) — climate impacts on caching

### Common Raven (*Corvus corax*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Corvidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR, ROLE-DECOMPOSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest passerine in the PNW (24-27 in, wingspan 46-56 in), entirely black with thick bill, wedge-shaped tail, and shaggy throat feathers (hackles). Remarkably intelligent — tool use, play, problem-solving, and complex social behavior.

**Elevation Range:** 0-14,410 ft (y=-41-319)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW from sea level to volcanic summits. One of the most widely distributed birds in the Northern Hemisphere. Found in every PNW habitat type.

**Ecological Significance:**
Ultimate generalist — scavenger, predator, seed disperser. Ravens clean carcasses (road-killed deer, salmon, marine mammal remains), accelerating nutrient cycling. Major predator on eggs and chicks of ground-nesting birds and seabirds. Intelligence enables behavioral innovation — ravens have been documented dropping shellfish on rocks to open them, caching food for later retrieval, and working cooperatively.

**Threats:** Not threatened — population increasing. Raven abundance inflation from human food subsidies creates conservation problems (egg predation on threatened species like snowy plover, desert tortoise in other regions).

**Cultural Significance:** Raven is one of the most important figures in Northwest Coast mythology. Raven is the Creator/Transformer figure in Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, and Kwakwaka'wakw traditions — Raven who stole the sun, brought light to the world, and continues to shape reality through trickery. Raven moiety/clan is the complementary half of Eagle moiety in Tlingit and Haida social organization. Raven imagery is ubiquitous in Northwest Coast art — bentwood boxes, totem poles, button blankets, silver jewelry. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** Heinrich (1999) — raven intelligence; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*)

**Taxonomy:** Apodiformes > Trochilidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-SUBALPINE-PARKLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-POLLINATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: NT (declining)

**Description:** Tiny hummingbird (3-4 in, 0.1 oz), males brilliant rufous-orange with iridescent red gorget. Extremely aggressive despite tiny size — defends nectar territories against much larger birds. Longest migration relative to body size of any bird.

**Elevation Range:** 0-8,000 ft (y=-41-159)
**Distribution:** Breeds throughout PNW from coastal lowlands to subalpine meadows. Migrates from Mexico/Central America to Alaska — up to 4,000 miles one way. PNW is core breeding range. Population declining 2-3% per year.

**Ecological Significance:**
Critical pollinator for PNW wildflowers, especially red tubular flowers (red columbine, paintbrush, penstemon) that have co-evolved with hummingbird pollination. Rufous hummingbirds are the primary pollinator for many alpine and subalpine flowers during the brief mountain growing season. Their migration timing is tuned to sequential flower blooming from lowland to alpine — climate change disrupting this phenological synchrony threatens both the birds and the plants they pollinate.

**Threats:** Declining across range (IUCN Near Threatened); climate-driven phenological mismatch with flowering; habitat loss on wintering grounds in Mexico; pesticide exposure in agricultural areas; window and cat mortality at feeders.

**Cultural Significance:** Hummingbird imagery in some Coast Salish art traditions. Observed and appreciated by PNW nations for beauty and energy. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

**Key Sources:** Healy & Calder (2006) — rufous hummingbird biology; Parmesan (2007) — phenological mismatch; BBS trend data

### Anna's Hummingbird (*Calypte anna*)

**Taxonomy:** Apodiformes > Trochilidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Description:** Small hummingbird (4 in), males with iridescent rose-red crown and throat. Larger than rufous hummingbird. The only hummingbird that overwinters in the PNW — a dramatic recent range expansion.

Range expansion represents a climate change and human habitat modification response that has added a new functional role (winter pollinator) to PNW lowland ecosystems.

**Threats:** Vulnerable to winter cold snaps (not fully adapted to PNW winter); feeders that freeze can strand dependent birds; cat predation at feeders.

### White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*)

**Taxonomy:** Galliformes > Phasianidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-ALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, HAB-VOLCANIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Smallest North American grouse (12 in, 12 oz). Cryptic — entirely white in winter, mottled brown and white in summer. Feathered feet act as snowshoes. Reluctant flier, preferring to walk and freeze when threatened.

### American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Cinclidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Chunky, slate-gray songbird (7-8 in) with short tail and legs. North America's only truly aquatic songbird — walks underwater on stream bottoms, using wings to "fly" through current. Nictitating membrane protects eyes; dense waterproof plumage; nasal flaps prevent water entry.

**Elevation Range:** 500-7,000 ft (y=-29-134)
**Distribution:** Fast-flowing mountain streams throughout PNW. Year-round resident — defends linear stream territories. Sings complex songs even in winter, perched on mid-stream boulders.

**Ecological Significance:**
The premier indicator species for stream water quality. Dippers feed on aquatic insect larvae (mayfly, stonefly, caddisfly nymphs) and small fish by walking and diving on stream bottoms. Their presence indicates cold, clean, well-oxygenated water with healthy invertebrate communities. Dippers are absent from polluted, silted, or thermally degraded streams. Dipper territory length (shorter = higher quality) correlates with aquatic invertebrate density.

**Threats:** Stream degradation from sedimentation, warming, and pollution; dam construction eliminating riffle habitat; riparian vegetation removal.

**Cultural Significance:** Observed and noted by PNW nations along streams. Dipper behavior (underwater walking) features in some oral traditions as an example of remarkable animal abilities. (CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Kingery (1996) — dipper biology; Sullivan et al. — dipper as water quality indicator; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*)

**Taxonomy:** Pelecaniformes > Ardeidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest heron in North America (46-52 in, wingspan 6+ ft). Blue-gray plumage, long neck with black stripe, dagger-like bill. Patient wade-and-strike hunting technique. Colonial nester (heronries) in tall trees.

**Elevation Range:** 0-2,000 ft (y=-41--16)

Top wading predator in shallow aquatic habitats. Diet includes fish, amphibians, crabs, snakes, voles, and large invertebrates. Heronries (colonies of 10-200+ nests) concentrate nutrient-rich guano deposits that enrich soils beneath nesting trees. Heron foraging areas indicate productive shallow-water habitat.

**Threats:** Heronry disturbance from development, construction, and bald eagle predation; loss of foraging habitat to shoreline armoring; contaminant accumulation in fish prey.

### Common Loon (*Gavia immer*)

**Taxonomy:** Gaviiformes > Gaviidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Sensitive | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large diving bird (28-36 in), breeding plumage: black head, red eye, striking black-and-white checkered back, white necklace band. Haunting, yodeling call echoes across mountain lakes — one of the most evocative bird sounds in North America.

**Elevation Range:** 1,000-5,000 ft (y=-17-84)
**Distribution:** Breeds on large, clear mountain lakes in WA and OR — rare breeder (fewer than 30 pairs in WA). More common in winter on coastal bays and Puget Sound. Requires large lakes with abundant fish and undisturbed shoreline for nesting.

Indicator of pristine lake quality. Loons require crystal-clear water (visual hunters), abundant fish populations, and undisturbed shoreline for ground nesting. Mercury bioaccumulation in loons (through fish consumption) provides a measurable index of lake mercury contamination. Loon presence on a breeding lake confirms high ecological integrity.

**Threats:** Lake shoreline development and recreational disturbance during nesting; mercury contamination; lead poisoning from fishing sinkers; gill-net mortality in marine wintering areas; boat wake swamping nests.

### Sandhill Crane (*Antigone canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Gruiformes > Gruidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed (Greater subspecies); Lesser subspecies stable | WA: Endangered | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Tall wading bird (3.5-4 ft, wingspan 6-7 ft), gray plumage with red forehead patch. Elaborate dancing courtship display. Trumpeting, bugle-like call audible for miles.

As wetland indicators, crane persistence confirms that marsh hydrology and disturbance regimes remain suitable.

**Cultural Significance:** Crane features in oral traditions of Plateau nations. Crane dancing observed and incorporated into some ceremonial traditions. (CULT-SPIRITUAL)

### Western Snowy Plover (*Charadrius nivosus nivosus*)

**Taxonomy:** Charadriiformes > Charadriidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

### Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*)

**Taxonomy:** Columbiformes > Columbidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC (declining)

**Description:** Large pigeon (14-16 in), gray with iridescent green-bronze nape, pale band on tail, yellow bill with black tip. PNW's native pigeon — occupies the ecological niche filled by passenger pigeon in eastern North America (now extinct).

**Elevation Range:** 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84)
**Distribution:** West-side PNW forests. Nomadic, following fruiting cycles of elderberry, cascara, and huckleberry. Mineral spring/salt lick gatherings concentrate birds — historical gatherings of thousands reported.

Primary seed disperser for multiple native shrubs — elderberry, cascara, wild cherry, huckleberry. Flocking behavior and long-distance movements distribute seeds across watersheds. Historical abundance rivaled that of passenger pigeon in the east; modern populations are significantly reduced. Mineral spring congregations create nutrient concentration hotspots.

**Threats:** Habitat loss; historical overhunting (still recovering); slow reproductive rate (one egg per clutch); Trichomonas gallinae disease.

**Key Sources:** Keppie & Braun (2000) — band-tailed pigeon; BBS population trends

### Red-breasted Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus ruber*)

**Taxonomy:** Piciformes > Picidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium woodpecker (8-9 in), entirely red head and breast (the most red of any PNW woodpecker). Drills characteristic horizontal rows of sap wells in living trees.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout west-side PNW forests, preferring deciduous and mixed forests with birch, alder, and maple.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone sap well provider. Sapsucker wells attract hummingbirds, warblers, butterflies, and other insects that feed on flowing sap. Rufous hummingbird arrival in spring often coincides with sapsucker sap well availability — the wells provide critical early-season nectar before wildflowers bloom. This facilitation cascade (sapsucker creates resource → hummingbird and other species benefit) demonstrates how single-species engineering creates community-wide effects.

**Threats:** Loss of deciduous trees in managed forests (emphasis on conifers); snag removal reducing nesting cavities.

**Key Sources:** Walters et al. (2002) — sapsucker sap wells; Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Northern Pygmy-Owl (*Glaucidium gnoma*)

**Taxonomy:** Strigiformes > Strigidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Tiny owl (6-7 in), brown with white-spotted head and streaked breast. Diurnal — hunts in daylight, unlike most owls. False "eye spots" on back of head deter predators. Fierce predator on birds up to its own size.

**Elevation Range:** 500-6,500 ft (y=-29-121)

**Ecological Significance:**
Small diurnal predator of songbirds, mice, and large insects. When pygmy-owls are detected by other birds, intense mobbing behavior erupts — mixed flocks of chickadees, nuthatches, wrens, and warblers converge to harass the owl. This mobbing response is used by birders to locate pygmy-owls, and the community-wide alarm demonstrates the disproportionate behavioral impact of a small predator.

**Threats:** Generally secure; old-growth loss reduces cavity nesting sites; competition for cavities with starlings in suburban areas.

### Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*)

**Taxonomy:** Strigiformes > Strigidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-URBAN

**Description:** Large owl (18-25 in, wingspan 40-57 in), mottled brown and gray with prominent ear tufts, yellow eyes, white throat bib. Deep, resonant "hoo-hoo-hoo-hooo" call. The most powerful nocturnal predator in PNW forests.

**Elevation Range:** 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109)

Apex nocturnal predator capable of killing prey ranging from mice to snowshoe hares, skunks, and other raptors. Predates on other owl species including spotted owl and barred owl — one of few natural mortality sources for these species. Controls snowshoe hare and rabbit populations. Also significant nest predator on great blue heron colonies.

## 3. Class Amphibia — Amphibians

The PNW west of the Cascades is a global hotspot for amphibian biodiversity, particularly lungless salamanders (Plethodontidae). Plethodontid densities in old-growth forest reach 364-744 per hectare, exceeding the biomass of all birds and mammals combined in some stands. PNW amphibians serve as sensitive indicators of forest floor moisture, stream water quality, and climate change. Several species are PNW endemics found nowhere else on Earth.

### Pacific Giant Salamander (*Dicamptodon tenebrosus*)

**Taxonomy:** Caudata > Dicamptodontidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Largest terrestrial salamander in North America — adults 7-12 in total length. Robust, marbled brown and tan pattern. One of the few salamanders that vocalizes — produces a low-pitched bark or yelp when disturbed.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** West-side PNW from southwestern BC to northern California. Requires cold, clear, fast-flowing streams (larvae) and moist old-growth forest (adults).

**Ecological Significance:**
Top predator in PNW headwater streams — larvae consume aquatic invertebrates, small fish, and other salamander larvae. Terrestrial adults prey on large invertebrates, mice, and smaller salamanders. Some individuals remain permanently aquatic (neoteny), becoming the largest predator in fishless headwater streams. Their biomass in headwater streams exceeds that of all fish in equivalent-order fish-bearing streams, making them the dominant vertebrate in an entire stream class.

**Threats:** Timber harvest increasing stream sedimentation and temperature; road crossings disrupting stream connectivity; climate warming reducing cold-water habitat.

**Cultural Significance:** Not prominently featured in PNW ethnographic records; giant salamanders were observed and sometimes avoided due to their unexpected size and bark-like vocalization.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983) — amphibians of PNW; Bury et al. — old-growth amphibian surveys; USFS PNW Research

**Description:** Smaller than Pacific giant (5-8 in), typically permanently aquatic (paedomorphic — retains larval gills throughout life). Brown with indistinct mottling. Restricted to cold headwater streams.

Permanent resident of fishless headwater streams. ### Cascade Torrent Salamander (*Rhyacotriton cascadae*)

**Taxonomy:** Caudata > Rhyacotritonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: NT | NatureServe: G3

**Description:** Small salamander (3-4 in), brown above with yellow-green belly. Tiny — among the smallest salamanders in North America. Restricted to seeps, headwater streams, and splash zones.

**Elevation Range:** 500-4,500 ft (y=-29-46)
**Distribution:** Cascade Range of WA and OR in cold headwater seeps and streams. Extremely narrow microhabitat requirements — requires water temperatures below 57F (14C) and rock/gravel substrate with interstitial spaces.

**Ecological Significance:**
Among the most sensitive aquatic indicators in PNW forests. Their disappearance from a stream reach is an early signal of habitat degradation. **Threats:** Any disturbance increasing stream temperature or sedimentation (logging, roads); climate warming; extremely limited dispersal ability (cannot cross warm, dry gaps between streams).

### Rough-skinned Newt (*Taricha granulosa*)

**Taxonomy:** Caudata > Salamandridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium newt (5-8 in), dark brown to black above, bright orange belly. Granular (rough) skin texture. Slow, deliberate movements. Produces tetrodotoxin (TTX) — one of the most potent neurotoxins known — in skin glands. The only PNW amphibian dangerous to handle (toxin can enter through cuts or mucous membranes).

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout west-side PNW from southeastern Alaska to central California. Most common amphibian visible on PNW forest trails during fall rains.

**Ecological Significance:**
The textbook example of coevolutionary arms race. Rough-skinned newts produce TTX in their skin; common garter snakes (*Thamnophis sirtalis*) in the PNW have evolved resistance to TTX, with resistance levels closely matching local newt toxicity in a geographic mosaic of coevolution. This newt-snake system (studied by Brodie & Brodie) is one of the most famous examples of predator-prey coevolution in biology. Newts are important predators on aquatic invertebrates and amphibian eggs in ponds and slow streams. Their toxicity means they have almost no other predators, making them one of the most abundant vertebrates in PNW forest floor communities.

**Threats:** Vehicle mortality during fall migration to breeding ponds (mass road-kill events documented); road barriers fragmenting populations; habitat loss of breeding ponds; bullfrog predation on larvae (bullfrogs are non-native).

**Key Sources:** Brodie & Brodie (1999) — TTX coevolution; Nussbaum et al. (1983); Hanifin et al. (2008) — geographic mosaic

### Ensatina (*Ensatina eschscholtzii*)

**Taxonomy:** Caudata > Plethodontidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium plethodontid (3-6 in), smooth skin, constricted tail base. PNW populations (subspecies *oregonensis*) are uniformly brown. Lungless — breathes through moist skin. Curls tail over body when threatened, displaying bright orange underside.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout west-side PNW forests under logs, bark, and leaf litter. One of the most abundant vertebrates in PNW forest floor communities.

**Ecological Significance:**

In the PNW, ensatinas are among the most abundant forest-floor vertebrates, consuming enormous quantities of small invertebrates (mites, springtails, small beetles). ### Long-toed Salamander (*Ambystoma macrodactylum*)

**Taxonomy:** Caudata > Ambystomatidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE, HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium mole salamander (4-7 in), dark brown/black with irregular yellow-green dorsal stripe. Distinctively long fourth toe (hence the name). Largely fossorial — adults spend most of the year underground.

**Elevation Range:** 0-8,000 ft (y=-41-159)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW from lowland to subalpine. Breeds in ponds, lakes, and ephemeral wetlands. Found at higher elevations than any other salamander in the PNW — breeds in alpine ponds near tree line.

**Ecological Significance:**
The widest altitudinal range of any PNW salamander. Breeding migrations to alpine ponds in spring create one of the earliest vertebrate life events at high elevation — larvae developing in ice-margined ponds. Long-toed salamanders are important predators on mosquito larvae and other aquatic invertebrates in fishless ponds and wetlands. Their use of ephemeral (seasonal) wetlands that dry by late summer makes them indicators of temporary wetland habitat quality.

**Threats:** Wetland drainage and fill; fish stocking in historically fishless ponds (introduced trout and sunfish eat larvae); road mortality during breeding migration; climate change altering ephemeral pond hydroperiod.

### Tailed Frog (*Ascaphus truei*)

**Taxonomy:** Anura > Ascaphidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small frog (1-2 in), olive to brown, flattened body adapted to fast-flowing streams. Males possess a unique tail-like copulatory organ (the "tail") — the only frog with internal fertilization. No vocal sac — does not call (uniquely among frogs). Vertical pupils.

**Elevation Range:** 500-5,000 ft (y=-29-84)
**Distribution:** Cold, fast-flowing mountain streams throughout PNW. Requires cobble-bottomed streams in old-growth or mature forest with intact riparian canopy.

**Ecological Significance:**
Among the most ancient frogs alive — the family Ascaphidae diverged from other frogs over 200 million years ago. Tailed frogs are exquisitely adapted to torrential streams: tadpoles have large oral suction discs that grip rocks in fast current, and adults have flattened bodies and rough toe pads for clinging to wet rock surfaces. Their extremely slow life history (tadpoles take 3-4 years to metamorphose — the longest of any frog) makes populations slow to recover from disturbance. Tailed frog presence indicates cold, clean, fast-flowing streams with intact forest — their absence signals degradation.

**Threats:** Any disturbance increasing stream temperature, sedimentation, or flow alteration; logging near streams (even with buffers, increased light raises water temperature); road construction; climate warming.

**Cultural Significance:** Not specifically documented in PNW ethnographic sources.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Dupuis & Steventon (1999) — tailed frog and forestry; Welsh & Lind (2002)

### Oregon Spotted Frog (*Rana pretiosa*)

**Taxonomy:** Anura > Ranidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened | WA State Endangered | OR State Sensitive-Critical | IUCN: VU

**Description:** Medium frog (2-4 in), brown with black spots, reddish-orange coloration on belly and legs intensifying with age. Most aquatic native frog in PNW — rarely found far from water. Upward-turned eyes positioned on top of head.

**Elevation Range:** 0-4,500 ft (y=-41-46)
**Distribution:** Historically from southwestern BC to northern California. Now extirpated from over 90% of historical range. Remaining populations in central WA (Deschutes Basin, Klickitat County), central OR (Deschutes, Klamath basins), and remnant BC sites.

**Ecological Significance:**
One of the most endangered amphibians in the PNW — its 90%+ range contraction reflects catastrophic loss of emergent wetland habitat. Oregon spotted frogs require shallow, warm, marshy wetlands with emergent vegetation — habitat disproportionately destroyed by agricultural drainage and development. They breed earlier than other PNW frogs (February-March), using the warm shallows created by emergent vegetation. Their recovery requires large-scale wetland restoration.

**Threats:** Wetland loss and degradation (primary — 90%+ of suitable habitat destroyed); introduced bullfrog predation; invasive reed canary grass altering wetland structure; water management reducing shallow-water habitat.

### Pacific Tree Frog (*Pseudacris regilla*)

**Taxonomy:** Anura > Hylidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small tree frog (1-2 in), variable green to brown coloration with dark eye stripe. Toe pads for climbing. The iconic "ribbit" frog — its call was recorded in Hollywood and has become the stereotypical frog sound in movies worldwide, though it is native only to western North America.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)

Tadpoles are important algae grazers in small ponds, and adults consume large quantities of flying insects, spiders, and other invertebrates. Pacific tree frog persistence in degraded habitats where other frogs have disappeared makes them "last frog standing" indicators — when even Pacific tree frogs disappear, habitat is severely compromised.

### Western Toad (*Anaxyrus boreas*)

**Taxonomy:** Anura > Bufonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND

**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: NT

**Description:** Large toad (3-5 in), warty skin, variable coloration (gray, green, brown) with light dorsal stripe. Parotoid glands behind eyes produce mild toxin. Walks rather than hops.

## 4. Class Reptilia — Reptiles

PNW reptile diversity is lower than regions to the south and east, reflecting the cool, moist climate that favors amphibians over reptiles west of the Cascades. However, the PNW supports 20+ reptile species including several of conservation concern. Oregon white oak prairie and rocky habitats in the rain shadow east of the Cascades support the highest reptile diversity. West-side reptile diversity is concentrated in the lowland forest-riparian interface and south-facing rocky exposures.

### Western Pond Turtle (*Actinemys marmorata*)

**Taxonomy:** Testudines > Emydidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE, HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern (under review for ESA listing) | WA: Endangered | OR: Sensitive-Critical | IUCN: VU

**Description:** Small freshwater turtle (6-8 in shell length), olive to dark brown carapace, yellowish throat markings. The only native freshwater turtle in the PNW west of the Cascades. Long-lived — individuals can survive 50+ years.

The PNW's only native freshwater turtle — its decline represents loss of a unique functional role in pond and stream ecosystems. ### Western Fence Lizard (*Sceloporus occidentalis*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Phrynosomatidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL (rocky outcrops)
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium lizard (6-9 in including tail), brown/gray with keeled (spiny) scales. Males have bright blue throat and belly patches used in territorial push-up displays. The most commonly seen lizard in the PNW.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Dry, sunny habitats in southern WA and throughout OR. Most common on south-facing rocky outcrops, fences, and log piles. More abundant east of Cascades; west-side populations concentrated in Willamette Valley and Columbia Gorge.

**Ecological Significance:**
Important insect predator in dry habitats. A remarkable biomedical discovery: a protein in western fence lizard blood kills the Lyme disease bacterium (*Borrelia burgdorferi*) when ticks feed on them, effectively "cleansing" infected ticks. This is why Lyme disease is rare in the PNW despite the presence of tick vectors — fence lizards break the disease transmission cycle. This ecosystem service has direct human health implications.

**Threats:** Habitat conversion of rocky outcrops and oak prairie; cat predation; loss of sun-exposed basking sites to vegetation encroachment from fire suppression.

**Key Sources:** Lane & Quistad (1998) — Lyme disease ecology; Nussbaum et al. (1983); Stebbins (2003)

### Northern Alligator Lizard (*Elgaria coerulea*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Anguidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium lizard (8-13 in), elongate body with short legs, long tail. Gray-brown with dark lateral fold. Lateral fold allows body expansion for breathing and egg production. Moves in a sinuous, snake-like manner.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout west-side PNW forests. The most forest-adapted lizard in the region — found under logs, bark, and rocks in moist conifer forest. Tolerates cooler, moister conditions than other PNW lizards.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only lizard commonly found in PNW old-growth forest interior — all other PNW lizard species require open or edge habitats. Alligator lizards prey on spiders, beetles, slugs, and other invertebrates in the forest-floor community. Their cold tolerance (active at temperatures as low as 55F) enables them to occupy the cool PNW forest floor niche unavailable to other reptiles.

**Threats:** Generally secure; decline in coarse woody debris from forest management; road mortality.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Stebbins (2003); Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

### Common Garter Snake (*Thamnophis sirtalis*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium snake (18-52 in), variable coloration with three longitudinal stripes (yellow, green, or blue) on darker ground. PNW populations include spectacularly colored variants — red-spotted morph in Puget Sound region, melanistic (all-black) individuals on some islands.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW. The most common snake in western WA and OR. Found in every lowland habitat with amphibian prey.

**Ecological Significance:**
The central player in PNW's most famous coevolutionary arms race — common garter snakes in the PNW have evolved remarkable resistance to the tetrodotoxin (TTX) produced by rough-skinned newts. Resistance levels vary geographically in a mosaic pattern matching local newt toxicity, with some populations bearing resistance 100x greater than populations outside newt range. This system (studied by the Brodies for decades) is a textbook example of predator-prey coevolution. Garter snakes are also important predators on slugs, earthworms, frogs, and small fish, and significant prey for raptors, herons, and mammals.

**Threats:** Road mortality (snakes bask on warm road surfaces); habitat loss; persecution from humans; decline of amphibian prey.

**Key Sources:** Brodie et al. (2002) — TTX resistance coevolution; Nussbaum et al. (1983); Geffeney et al. (2005) — genetic basis of resistance

**Cross-Module References:**
- See fauna-terrestrial: Rough-skinned newt (coevolutionary arms race partner)
- See ecological-networks: Predator-Prey Dynamics (coevolution exemplar)

### Northwestern Garter Snake (*Thamnophis ordinoides*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-URBAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small garter snake (14-26 in), extremely variable coloration — stripes may be red, orange, yellow, blue, white, or absent. One of the most chromatically variable snakes in North America. Slender build.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** West-side PNW from southwestern BC to northwestern CA. Most common in meadows, clearings, and suburban gardens. Primarily terrestrial.

**Ecological Significance:**
Specialist predator on slugs and earthworms — one of very few PNW vertebrates that routinely consume slugs, including the introduced European slugs that are agricultural pests. This slug-predation role provides a measurable ecosystem service in gardens and agricultural settings. Their extreme color variation within populations is studied as an example of balancing selection and apostatic selection (predators form search images for common color morphs, giving rare morphs a survival advantage).

**Threats:** Generally secure; road mortality; cat predation; habitat loss of open meadows.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Brodie (1992) — color variation and predator avoidance; Stebbins (2003)

### Western Terrestrial Garter Snake (*Thamnophis elegans*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium garter snake (18-42 in), three stripes on darker ground, often with distinct checkered pattern between stripes. More aquatic than northwestern garter snake.

**Elevation Range:** 0-7,000 ft (y=-41-134)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW in habitats near water. Widest altitudinal range of any PNW snake — found from coastal lowlands to subalpine meadows. More common east of Cascades than common garter snake.

**Ecological Significance:**
Semi-aquatic predator on fish, tadpoles, frogs, and leeches. Occupies the highest elevations of any PNW snake — found at alpine lakes where it preys on long-toed salamander larvae and chorus frog tadpoles. This high-elevation range extension represents a thermal challenge for ectotherms and demonstrates behavioral thermoregulation capacity.

**Threats:** Habitat loss; road mortality; stream channelization reducing aquatic foraging habitat.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Gregory (1984) — garter snake ecology; Stebbins (2003)

### Rubber Boa (*Charina bottae*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Boidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE
**Habitat:** HAB-OLD-GROWTH, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small, thick-bodied boa (14-33 in), uniform olive-brown or tan, smooth rubbery-textured skin (hence the name). Blunt tail resembles the head — used as a decoy when confronting predators. The most northerly-ranging boa in the world.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW in forested habitats, though rarely seen due to fossorial and nocturnal habits. Found under logs, rocks, and loose bark.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only boa (family Boidae) native to the PNW and the northernmost boa on Earth. Rubber boas are constrictors that specialize in raiding rodent nests — the female enters a nest, wraps around the pups, and uses her blunt tail to fend off the attacking mother while consuming young. This nest-predation behavior makes rubber boas effective regulators of vole and mouse populations. Their secretive, fossorial lifestyle means they are far more common than sighting records suggest.

**Threats:** Road mortality; habitat loss; persecution by humans (often misidentified as dangerous); decline in down wood from forest management.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Hoyer & Stewart (2000) — rubber boa ecology; Stebbins (2003)

**Cross-Module References:**

### Sharptail Snake (*Contia tenuis*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-OLD-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Tiny snake (8-18 in), reddish-brown above with distinctive sharp spine on tail tip. Among the smallest and most secretive snakes in the PNW. Found under flat rocks, bark, and boards.

**Elevation Range:** 0-2,500 ft (y=-41-21)
**Distribution:** Scattered sites in western WA (south Puget Sound), Willamette Valley, and southwestern OR/northern CA. Strongly associated with Oregon white oak woodland and rocky habitats. Rare and patchily distributed.

**Ecological Significance:**
Specialist predator on slugs — uses recurved teeth to hold slimy slug prey. One of only two PNW snakes (with northwestern garter snake) that regularly consume slugs. Association with oak woodland and rocky microsites makes sharptail snakes indicators of dry, open habitat within the moist PNW forest matrix.

**Threats:** Oak woodland loss (primary); conversion of rocky grassland habitat to development; fire suppression allowing canopy closure over basking/shelter sites.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Cook (1960) — Contia biology; WDFW candidate species

### Ring-necked Snake (*Diadophis punctatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small snake (10-30 in), slate-gray to olive above with distinctive orange or yellow neck ring. Bright orange-red belly used in defensive coiling display — curls tail to expose ventral warning coloration.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Western OR and southwestern WA. Most common in moist woodland with abundant cover objects. Secretive and nocturnal.

**Ecological Significance:**
Predator on salamanders, slugs, and small snakes. Belly-coiling defensive display is a possible Batesian mimicry signal (warning coloration suggests toxicity, though ring-necked snakes are only mildly venomous to prey-sized animals). Important prey for kingsnakes, opossums, and large-bodied snakes.

**Threats:** Generally secure; habitat loss in suburban expansion; road mortality.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Stebbins (2003)

### North American Racer (*Coluber constrictor*)

**Taxonomy:** Squamata > Colubridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large, slender snake (36-60 in), uniform olive-green to dark blue-black above, yellow belly. Fast-moving visual hunter — among the fastest snakes in North America. Diurnal and active.

**Elevation Range:** 0-3,000 ft (y=-41-34)
**Distribution:** Eastern WA and OR primarily; limited west-side occurrence in Willamette Valley and Columbia Gorge. Requires open habitats with good visibility for hunting.

**Ecological Significance:**
Active visual predator on rodents, lizards, frogs, insects, and other snakes. Unlike ambush predators, racers actively pursue prey using speed, making them effective regulators of fast-moving prey species (lizards, grasshoppers). Their dependence on open habitat makes them indicators of grassland and oak prairie condition.

**Threats:** Habitat conversion of open grasslands; road mortality (fast movement across roads increases encounter rate); persecution.

**Key Sources:** Nussbaum et al. (1983); Stebbins (2003); Johnson & O'Neil (2001)

**Cross-Module References:**
- See ecological-networks: Oak Woodland food web (visual predator)

### Key Ecological Themes

1. **Old-growth dependence:** Spotted owl, marbled murrelet, Pacific fisher, red tree vole, flying squirrel, and 10+ amphibian species require structural features (large trees, snags, down wood, moss platforms, canopy complexity) found only in old-growth or late-seral forest. No single restoration action can replicate centuries of structural development.

2. **Marine-terrestrial nutrient transport:** Bears, eagles, river otters, and other species move salmon-derived marine nutrients into terrestrial forests. Trees within 500m of bear-fished streams grow 3x faster. This nutrient subsidy sustains PNW forest productivity far from the coast.

3. **Coevolutionary arms races:** Rough-skinned newt ↔ common garter snake (TTX resistance); whitebark pine ↔ Clark's nutcracker (seed dispersal mutualism); snowshoe hare ↔ predator community (camouflage mismatch). These interspecific dynamics drive evolutionary innovation.

4. **Climate change indicators:** American pika (thermal intolerance), Cascade red fox (alpine habitat compression), snowshoe hare (camouflage mismatch), gray jay (cache spoilage), white-tailed ptarmigan (alpine habitat loss), torrent salamanders (stream warming). PNW alpine and subalpine species are climate sentinels.

5. **Prairie as cultural landscape:** Streaked horned lark, western bluebird, western gray squirrel, Fender's blue butterfly, and the entire oak woodland community depend on habitat historically maintained by Indigenous fire management. Conservation of these species requires restoring cultural burning practices.

6. **Invasive species disruption:** Barred owl displacing spotted owl; non-native red fox threatening Cascade red fox; introduced bullfrogs consuming native frogs and turtles; European starlings outcompeting bluebirds for cavities; eastern gray squirrels displacing western gray squirrels. Every PNW faunal community faces invasive species pressure.
