## 1. Pacific Salmon and Steelhead (Genus *Oncorhynchus*)

Pacific salmon are the ecological and cultural backbone of the PNW. All five species and steelhead are anadromous — born in freshwater, migrating to the ocean, returning to natal streams to spawn and die. This lifecycle creates the largest natural nutrient transport system in the temperate world: salmon carry marine-derived nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon from the Pacific Ocean into thousands of PNW streams, where their carcasses fertilize riparian forests, feed bears, eagles, and 130+ other species, and sustain the productivity of entire watersheds. The decline of salmon runs — from an estimated 10-16 million fish annually in pre-contact times to 1-2 million today — represents the single greatest ecological loss in the PNW.

**ESA note:** Salmon and steelhead are listed by Evolutionarily Significant Units (ESUs) or Distinct Population Segments (DPSs), not species-wide. A single species may have some ESUs listed as Threatened or Endangered while others are not listed. Status below reflects the most imperiled PNW ESUs.

### Chinook Salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-KEYSTONE
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: 9 ESUs Threatened or Endangered in PNW (Puget Sound Chinook Threatened, Upper Columbia Spring Endangered, Snake River Spring/Summer Endangered, Snake River Fall Threatened, Lower Columbia Threatened, Upper Willamette Spring Threatened) | WA: Candidate | IUCN: LC (species-wide)

**Description:** Largest Pacific salmon — adults 10-58 lbs (spring/summer runs) to 30-100+ lbs (fall "king" runs). Greenish-blue back, silver sides, black spots on back and both lobes of tail. Black gum line at base of teeth distinguishes from other salmon. The "King Salmon."

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109); Ocean: 0--930 ft (y=-41--64)
**Distribution:** Every major PNW river system from the Sacramento to the Fraser, historically including 10,000+ miles of stream habitat. Columbia River system supported the largest Chinook runs on Earth — estimated 6-8 million fish annually before dams and habitat loss reduced runs to 1-2% of historical levels. Puget Sound Chinook listed as Threatened since 1999.

**Ecological Significance:**
The keystone species of the PNW. Chinook salmon are the primary prey of Southern Resident killer whales — SRKW survival is directly tied to Chinook abundance, and Chinook decline is the leading cause of SRKW endangered status. Chinook are the largest salmon, carrying the most marine-derived nutrients per fish into freshwater systems. A single spawning Chinook deposits 20-30 lbs of marine-origin biomass on the stream bottom. Bears preferentially select Chinook for their size and fat content. Spring/summer Chinook penetrate deepest into headwater streams, delivering nutrients to the most remote and nutrient-poor watersheds. The Chinook lifecycle — 2-7 years in the ocean before returning — means each generation integrates years of ocean productivity and delivers it to freshwater in a single spawning event.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** Fall runs: September-December in mainstem rivers. Spring/summer runs: May-September in cold headwater streams. Females select gravel beds, excavate redds (nests) with tail, deposit 3,000-14,000 eggs in multiple pockets.
- **Incubation:** 90-150 days depending on water temperature. Eggs require clean, well-oxygenated gravels.
- **Freshwater rearing:** Ocean-type (fall) juveniles migrate to estuary within weeks-months; stream-type (spring) juveniles rear 1 full year in freshwater.
- **Ocean phase:** 2-7 years in the North Pacific, ranging from California to Alaska and across to Japan. Feed on herring, anchovy, squid, and krill. Achieve 95% of adult body mass in the ocean.
- **Return and death:** Navigate back to natal stream using olfactory imprinting. All adults die after spawning (semelparity). Carcasses feed 130+ species and fertilize riparian forests.

**Threats:** Four H's: **Habitat** (dam passage mortality, floodplain disconnection, water withdrawal, sedimentation, riparian degradation); **Hydropower** (8 mainstem Columbia/Snake dams with 5-15% juvenile mortality per dam, cumulative 50-90% mortality); **Harvest** (reduced but still significant in mixed-stock ocean fisheries); **Hatcheries** (genetic dilution, ecological competition with wild fish, masking true wild population status).

**Cultural Significance:** Chinook salmon is the most culturally significant species in the PNW — central to the identity, spirituality, economy, and governance of every PNW Indigenous nation with river or coastal access. First Salmon ceremonies (Lummi, Tulalip, Yakama, Nez Perce, Umatilla, Warm Springs, and many others) honor the salmon's return and express the reciprocal obligation between people and salmon. The Boldt Decision (1974) affirmed tribal treaty rights to 50% of the harvestable salmon — a landmark in US Indian law. Chinook salmon are referenced in virtually every treaty negotiated between PNW nations and the United States. The phrase "salmon people" expresses an identity relationship, not a dietary preference. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); NOAA Fisheries ESU status reviews; NRC (1996) — *Upstream: Salmon and Society in the Pacific Northwest*; NWIFC salmon recovery science

### Coho Salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-KEYSTONE
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Lower Columbia Threatened, Oregon Coast Threatened, Southern OR/Northern CA Coast Threatened | WA: Not state-listed but depressed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium salmon, 8-12 lbs (occasionally to 25 lbs). Silver sides, small black spots on upper back and upper tail lobe only. Males develop dramatic hooked jaw (kype) and deep red body color during spawning. "Silver salmon."

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-4,000 ft (y=-41-9); Ocean: nearshore, rarely deep
**Distribution:** Every coastal and Puget Sound stream system. Coho use smaller streams than Chinook — including tiny headwater tributaries, side channels, and beaver ponds. This makes coho the salmon species most dependent on small-stream and off-channel habitat.

**Ecological Significance:**
The "stream salmon" — coho juveniles rear for one full year in freshwater, making them the most freshwater-dependent Pacific salmon during juvenile life. This year-long rearing period means coho productivity is directly tied to stream habitat quality — pool depth, woody debris complexity, beaver pond availability, and cool water temperatures. Coho juveniles in beaver ponds survive winter at 2-3x the rate of those in unponded streams, making the beaver-coho relationship one of the most important species interactions for salmon recovery. Adult coho spawning extends deep into small tributaries, distributing marine-derived nutrients throughout entire watershed networks.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** October-January in small to medium streams. Females deposit 1,500-4,500 eggs.
- **Freshwater rearing:** Full year in freshwater as fry and parr. Highly territorial. Overwinter in deep pools, beaver ponds, and off-channel habitat.
- **Ocean phase:** 18 months in nearshore ocean. Feed on herring, sand lance, and invertebrates.
- **Return:** At age 3 (1 year freshwater + 18 months ocean). Dramatic upstream migration.

**Threats:** Small-stream habitat degradation (logging, roads, culverts blocking passage); beaver removal (eliminates overwintering habitat); urban stormwater (toxics in tire-wear particles — 6PPD-quinone recently discovered to cause acute coho mortality); marine survival declining.

**Cultural Significance:** Important food fish for all PNW nations with stream access. Coho's use of small, accessible streams made them the most widely available salmon for inland communities. Silver salmon were often dried and smoked for winter stores. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); Pollock et al. (2004) — beaver-coho relationship; Tian et al. (2022) — 6PPD-quinone coho mortality; NOAA ESU reviews

### Sockeye Salmon (*Oncorhynchus nerka*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-LAKE, HAB-STREAM, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Snake River Endangered, Ozette Lake Threatened | WA: Not state-listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium salmon, 5-8 lbs. Brilliant transformation: ocean phase silver-blue with no spots; spawning phase vivid red body with green head — the most dramatic color change of any salmon. "Red salmon" or "blueback."

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-4,500 ft (y=-41-46); Ocean: pelagic
**Distribution:** Lake-rearing populations in major PNW systems: Fraser River (largest runs — millions of fish), Baker Lake, Lake Ozette, Lake Wenatchee, Okanogan system, Columbia/Snake systems. Lake Ozette sockeye are ESA Threatened.

**Ecological Significance:**
The lake specialist among salmon — sockeye juveniles typically rear 1-2 years in freshwater lakes before ocean migration, feeding on zooplankton. This lake-rearing requirement means sockeye populations are tied to specific lakes, making each population uniquely adapted to its natal lake. Kokanee (landlocked sockeye that complete their entire lifecycle in freshwater) demonstrate the evolutionary flexibility of the species. Fraser River sockeye runs historically numbered 20-40 million fish — among the largest animal migrations on Earth.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** August-October in lake tributaries or along lake beaches (beach spawning is unique to sockeye).
- **Lake rearing:** 1-2 years in nursery lake feeding on zooplankton. Juveniles regulate zooplankton communities, creating top-down control of lake food webs.
- **Ocean phase:** 1-3 years in the North Pacific. Highly pelagic — ranges far offshore.
- **Return:** Spectacular mass returns create "walls of red" in rivers. Baker Lake runs time with commercial harvest.

**Threats:** Dam blockage of access to nursery lakes (many historical lake populations extirpated); climate-driven lake warming reducing zooplankton productivity; disease (Ich, BKD) amplified by warming water; Fraser River sockeye experiencing unexplained productivity decline.

**Cultural Significance:** Extremely important food fish — high oil content makes sockeye ideal for smoking and drying. "Red salmon" valued across all PNW nations. Fraser River sockeye supported massive First Nations fisheries for millennia. Lummi and Saanich reef net fisheries selectively targeted sockeye in the San Juan Islands. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); Burgner (1991) — sockeye biology; NOAA ESU reviews; Cohen Commission (2012) — Fraser River sockeye decline

### Pink Salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed in PNW | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Smallest Pacific salmon, 3-5 lbs. Spawning males develop extreme humpback (hence "humpback salmon" or "humpy") and hooked jaw. Ocean phase: blue-black with silver sides, large oval spots on tail.

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-500 ft (y=-41--29); Ocean: nearshore to pelagic
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW coastal streams, most abundant in Puget Sound and BC. Strictly two-year lifecycle creates distinct odd-year and even-year runs that are reproductively isolated from each other.

**Ecological Significance:**
The most abundant Pacific salmon by total biomass — pink salmon runs can be enormous (tens of millions in peak years). Their strict two-year lifecycle and immediate ocean migration after emergence (no freshwater rearing) means pinks are the most marine-influenced salmon species. Large pink runs deliver massive pulses of marine-derived nutrients to streams every other year, creating biennial productivity cycles detectable in riparian tree growth rings. Pink salmon are also critical forage for marine predators including orcas, seals, and marine birds during their ocean phase.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** August-October in lower reaches of coastal streams (don't penetrate far upstream).
- **Emergence:** Fry migrate immediately to saltwater — no freshwater rearing.
- **Ocean phase:** 18 months in the North Pacific. Rapid growth.
- **Return:** At age 2. Rigid two-year cycle.

**Threats:** Stream habitat degradation in lower reaches; estuary loss; hatchery competition; climate-driven marine survival changes. Generally more resilient than other salmon species due to large population sizes and short generation time.

**Cultural Significance:** Important food fish, especially for smoking and canning. Pink salmon's reliability and abundance made it a subsistence staple. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); Heard (1991) — pink salmon biology; WDFW salmon management

### Chum Salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Hood Canal Summer Threatened, Columbia River Threatened | WA: Not state-listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large salmon, 8-15 lbs (occasionally to 35 lbs). Spawning males develop spectacular calico coloration — green and purple vertical bars on flanks with prominent canine-like teeth (hence "dog salmon"). No black spots.

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-500 ft (y=-41--29); Ocean: pelagic
**Distribution:** Coastal streams throughout PNW. Like pink salmon, chum spawn in lower river reaches and juveniles migrate quickly to estuaries. Largest runs in Puget Sound and Hood Canal.

**Ecological Significance:**
The latest spawner among Pacific salmon — chum runs extend from September through January, with some streams receiving fish as late as February. This extended spawning season means chum carcasses provide marine-derived nutrients during the winter months when other nutrient inputs are minimal, sustaining overwintering invertebrates, juvenile coho, and early-breeding birds. Chum are particularly important for eagle winter feeding — Skagit River eagle concentrations peak with chum runs. The Hood Canal summer chum population (ESA Threatened) represents a unique early-returning life history type.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** September-January in lower river reaches and small coastal streams.
- **Emergence:** Fry migrate to estuary within days-weeks. No freshwater rearing.
- **Ocean phase:** 2-5 years in North Pacific. Wide-ranging pelagic feeders.
- **Return:** Adults return to natal streams, often spawning within sight of tidewater.

**Threats:** Estuary habitat loss (chum juveniles are the most estuary-dependent salmon); climate-driven ocean condition changes; Hood Canal summer chum facing low-oxygen dead zone in marine rearing habitat.

**Cultural Significance:** Important for smoking and drying — chum's lower oil content and firm flesh make excellent dried fish. "Dog salmon" name comes from its use as dog food by some communities, though chum was also valued human food. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); Salo (1991) — chum salmon biology; Hood Canal summer chum recovery plan

### Steelhead (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Multiple DPSs Threatened (Puget Sound, Upper Columbia, Snake River, Middle Columbia, Lower Columbia) | WA: Not state-listed | IUCN: LC (species-wide)

**Description:** The anadromous form of rainbow trout, 8-20 lbs. Silver with pink-red lateral band, black spots on back and tail. More streamlined than Pacific salmon. Unique among "salmon" — can survive spawning and return to ocean for repeat spawning (iteroparity).

**Elevation Range:** Freshwater: 0-6,000 ft (y=-41-109); Ocean: nearshore to pelagic
**Distribution:** Every major PNW river system. Two distinct run types: winter steelhead (return December-April, spawn immediately) and summer steelhead (return May-October, hold in freshwater for months before spawning). Summer steelhead penetrate deepest into interior watersheds.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only "salmon" that regularly survives spawning — up to 20% of adults can return to spawn a second or third time. This iteroparity (vs. semelparity in true Pacific salmon) means steelhead populations can recover faster from low numbers and individual genetic lineages persist longer. Steelhead are the same species as resident rainbow trout — the decision to migrate to the ocean or remain in freshwater appears to be environmentally triggered, creating a "portfolio effect" where a single population hedges its bets between resident and anadromous life histories. This flexibility makes steelhead uniquely resilient but also uniquely challenging to manage, as hatchery rainbow trout can interbreed with wild steelhead.

**Lifecycle:**
- **Spawning:** December-June depending on run type. Use gravel riffles in medium to large streams.
- **Freshwater rearing:** 1-3 years as parr in freshwater — the longest freshwater rearing of any anadromous salmonid.
- **Ocean phase:** 1-3 years. Less pelagic than Chinook or sockeye.
- **Return:** Adults may spawn 2-3 times over their lifetime.

**Threats:** Four H's apply (Habitat, Hydropower, Harvest, Hatcheries); catch-and-release mortality from recreational fishing (5-10% delayed mortality); hatchery-wild introgression; dam passage.

**Cultural Significance:** Valued sport and food fish. Steelhead's strength, beauty, and fighting ability make it the most prized recreational fish in the PNW. Tribal harvest for food and ceremony. Summer steelhead runs were particularly important for inland nations (Nez Perce, Yakama) who received fish months before fall salmon. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Quinn (2005); Busby et al. — steelhead status review; NOAA DPS assessments

## 2. Other Freshwater Fish

PNW freshwater systems support 60+ native fish species beyond salmon and steelhead, from ancient lampreys to endemic minnows. Cold-water obligate species (bull trout, torrent sculpin) serve as indicators of stream health, while species like white sturgeon represent living fossils connecting modern PNW to deep geological time.

---

### Bull Trout (*Salvelinus confluentus*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SUBALPINE
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened | WA: Candidate | OR: Sensitive-Critical | IUCN: VU

**Description:** Large char (not technically a trout), 2-20 lbs. Olive-green to dark with distinctive light spots (pink, orange, or cream) on darker background — opposite pattern from trout (dark spots on light). No black spots on dorsal fin. Subtly forked tail.

**Elevation Range:** 1,000-7,000 ft (y=-17-134)
**Distribution:** Cold headwater streams and deep lakes throughout PNW mountains — Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains, and northern Rockies extension. Requires the coldest, cleanest water of any PNW fish — water temperatures must remain below 54F (12C), dissolved oxygen above 7 mg/L.

**Ecological Significance:**
The defining cold-water indicator species. Bull trout requirements are so stringent that their presence confirms pristine conditions: cold temperatures, clean gravels, connected stream networks, and minimal sedimentation. Bull trout exhibit the most diverse life history strategies of any PNW salmonid — resident (stream-dwelling), fluvial (stream-migrating), adfluvial (lake-dwelling), and anadromous (sea-run, rare) forms can all occur within a single connected population. This life history diversity requires connected habitats from headwaters to mainstem rivers or lakes, making bull trout a connectivity indicator. Where passage barriers fragment bull trout populations, isolated groups face inbreeding and local extinction.

**Threats:** Stream warming (primary — bull trout are the "canary in the coal mine" for climate change in freshwater); passage barriers (dams, culverts); competition and hybridization with non-native brook trout (*Salvelinus fontinalis*); sedimentation from roads and logging.

**Cultural Significance:** Caught and consumed by PNW nations, though less prominently featured than salmon in cultural traditions due to headwater distribution away from major population centers. Bull trout presence in a stream was recognized as an indicator of cold, pristine conditions. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** USFWS bull trout recovery plan; Rieman & McIntyre (1993) — demographic patterns; Dunham & Rieman (1999) — habitat fragmentation

### Coastal Cutthroat Trout (*Oncorhynchus clarkii clarkii*)

**Taxonomy:** Salmoniformes > Salmonidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern (some populations) | WA: Not listed | OR: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium trout, 0.5-5 lbs (sea-run fish larger). Greenish-bronze with heavy dark spotting and distinctive red-orange "cutthroat" slash marks under jaw. Multiple life history forms: sea-run, lake-dwelling, and stream-resident.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Every small to medium stream in the west-side PNW. The most ubiquitous native fish in PNW small streams — present in thousands of headwater tributaries too small for salmon.

**Ecological Significance:**
The "everywhere fish" of PNW small streams. Cutthroat trout occupy headwater stream habitats above salmon distribution, serving as the top predator in streams too small for other salmonids. Sea-run cutthroat demonstrate anadromous lifecycle flexibility, migrating to estuaries and nearshore marine waters for feeding before returning to freshwater. Cutthroat are important predators on aquatic invertebrates and serve as prey for kingfishers, herons, otters, and mink.

**Threats:** Competition and hybridization with hatchery rainbow trout and non-native brook trout; stream habitat degradation; passage barriers; climate warming.

**Key Sources:** Trotter (2008) — cutthroat trout biology; Johnson et al. — coastal cutthroat status; WDFW

### Pacific Lamprey (*Entosphenus tridentatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Petromyzontiformes > Petromyzontidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE (freshwater); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Not listed | OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Jawless, eel-like fish, 15-30 in, dark blue-gray. Circular sucker mouth with rasping teeth — parasitic on marine fish during ocean phase. Ancient lineage — lampreys predate dinosaurs by 200 million years.

**Elevation Range:** 0-5,000 ft (y=-41-84)
**Distribution:** Historically throughout PNW river systems from southern California to Alaska. Severely declined — dams block passage more effectively for lamprey than for salmon (lamprey cannot use fish ladders designed for salmon).

**Ecological Significance:**
An ancient, irreplaceable component of PNW stream ecosystems. Lamprey larvae (ammocoetes) live buried in stream sediments for 5-7 years, filter-feeding on algae and detritus — their burrowing bioturbates sediments and improves water infiltration. Spawning adults (like salmon) die and deliver marine-derived nutrients to streams. Adult lamprey are a critical food source for marine mammals, birds, and fish during their ocean residence. Their decline has cascading effects: without lamprey as "buffer prey," predators concentrate more heavily on juvenile salmon.

**Threats:** Dam passage (lamprey cannot climb conventional fish ladders — their sucker mouths cannot grip smooth concrete surfaces designed for salmon); stream habitat degradation; direct mortality in irrigation diversions; population monitoring gaps.

**Cultural Significance:** Extremely important to PNW tribal nations — lamprey are a First Food alongside salmon. Yakama, Umatilla, Warm Springs, and Nez Perce nations harvest lamprey at traditional sites (Willamette Falls, Columbia tributaries). Tribal-led lamprey restoration is a growing priority. Lamprey were historically as abundant as salmon in some systems. Their decline occurred largely unnoticed by non-tribal communities, highlighting the importance of Indigenous ecological knowledge in identifying conservation priorities. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Close et al. (2002) — Pacific lamprey status; USFWS lamprey conservation initiative; Columbia River Inter-Tribal Fish Commission — lamprey restoration

### Eulachon (*Thaleichthys pacificus*)

**Taxonomy:** Osmeriformes > Osmeridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN (spawning); ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (ocean)
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened (Southern DPS) | WA: Candidate | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small, silvery smelt, 6-10 in. Extraordinarily oily — dried eulachon can literally be lit and burned like a candle (hence "candlefish"). Annual spawning runs in major rivers.

**Elevation Range:** 0-100 ft (y=-41--38)
**Distribution:** Major PNW rivers — Columbia, Fraser, Nass, Skeena. Southern DPS (Columbia River) ESA Threatened due to severe population decline (90%+ reduction from historical levels).

**Ecological Significance:**
A critical forage fish linking marine and freshwater food webs. Eulachon runs provided the first large pulse of marine-derived nutrients to rivers in spring, weeks before salmon arrived. Their extreme oil content (15-20% body fat) makes them one of the most energy-dense prey fish in PNW waters — consumed by sturgeon, seals, sea lions, beluga whales, eagles, and gulls. Eulachon decline may be contributing to nutritional stress in marine mammals.

**Threats:** Ocean condition changes reducing survival; bycatch in shrimp trawl fisheries; river habitat degradation; climate-driven shifts in ocean prey availability.

**Cultural Significance:** Among the most important cultural foods of the PNW, particularly for northern nations. Eulachon grease (rendered oil) was the primary trade commodity of the "grease trails" — overland trade routes connecting coastal and interior nations. Nuxalk, Nisga'a, Haisla, and other nations hold eulachon fishing rights and First Fish ceremonies. Grease was traded to interior nations who had no direct coastal access, making it one of the most valuable commodities in pre-contact PNW economy. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** NOAA Fisheries eulachon status review; Willson et al. (2006); Hay & McCarter (2000)

### White Sturgeon (*Acipenser transmontanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Acipenseriformes > Acipenseridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Kootenai River DPS Endangered | WA: Not listed (but impounded populations declining) | IUCN: LC (species-wide)

**Description:** Largest freshwater fish in North America — adults 6-12 ft, up to 1,500 lbs. Ancient appearance: cartilaginous skeleton, bony scutes (plates) along body, barbels for bottom-feeding. Living fossil — sturgeons are essentially unchanged for 200 million years.

**Elevation Range:** 0-1,500 ft (y=-41--16)
**Distribution:** Columbia River system (largest population), Fraser River, and other major PNW rivers. Impounded populations behind dams (e.g., Lake Roosevelt) are isolated and declining due to inability to complete natural lifecycle.

**Ecological Significance:**
A living fossil connecting modern PNW to the Jurassic. White sturgeon can live 100+ years, reaching maturity at 15-25 years — among the longest-lived freshwater fish on Earth. Their benthic feeding (suction-feeding on invertebrates, small fish, and carcasses from the river bottom) stirs sediments and promotes nutrient cycling. Sturgeon are keystone components of large-river food webs, but their extreme longevity means population impacts may not become apparent for decades — a "extinction debt" problem.

**Threats:** Dam impoundment (blocks migration, fragments populations, alters flow and temperature regimes); overharvest (historical — some populations still recovering); altered food web from dam-regulated flows.

**Cultural Significance:** Highly valued food fish for PNW nations with Columbia River access. Sturgeon provided large quantities of food from a single fish. The species' great size and age were noted and respected. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Beamesderfer & Farr (1997) — sturgeon management; USFWS Kootenai sturgeon recovery; Quinn (2005)

### Olympic Mudminnow (*Novumbra hubbsi*)

**Taxonomy:** Esociformes > Umbridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-STREAM
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Sensitive | IUCN: LC | NatureServe: G3

**Description:** Tiny fish (2-3 in), olive-brown with faint vertical bars. The only species in its genus — a monotypic genus endemic to the PNW. Tolerates very low oxygen conditions using air-gulping behavior.

**Elevation Range:** 0-500 ft (y=-41--29)
**Distribution:** Lowland wetlands and slow streams of the Chehalis River basin and Olympic Peninsula of Washington. One of the most geographically restricted freshwater fish in North America.

**Ecological Significance:**
A PNW endemic found nowhere else on Earth — the sole living member of its genus. Olympic mudminnows inhabit low-oxygen wetlands, beaver ponds, and stagnant backwaters that most fish cannot survive. Their ability to gulp air and tolerate near-anoxic conditions allows them to occupy a niche unavailable to other PNW fish. They are indicators of intact lowland wetland habitat with natural hydrology.

**Threats:** Wetland drainage and fill; introduced predatory fish; water quality degradation; very limited range makes any habitat loss significant.

## 3. Marine Mammals

PNW marine waters support 30+ marine mammal species, from year-round residents (harbor seals, orcas) to seasonal migrants (gray whales, humpbacks). Marine mammals serve as apex predators, ecosystem engineers, and nutrient recyclers, linking pelagic food webs to nearshore and even terrestrial ecosystems. The Southern Resident killer whale population is the most endangered marine mammal in the PNW and one of the most closely monitored cetacean populations on Earth.

---

### Southern Resident Killer Whale (*Orcinus orca* — SRKW)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Delphinidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC, HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Endangered | WA: Endangered | IUCN: DD (ecotype not separately assessed)

**Description:** Large, black and white delphinid — males to 26 ft and 12,000 lbs, females to 23 ft and 8,000 lbs. Distinctive tall dorsal fin (males to 6 ft), white eye patch, gray saddle patch. Three pods: J, K, L — each with distinct dialect, matrilineal social structure, and movement patterns.

**Elevation Range:** Surface to -930 ft (y=-41--64)
**Distribution:** Historically concentrated in inland waters (Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, Strait of Juan de Fuca) during summer-fall; increasingly found in outer coastal waters year-round as Chinook runs decline. Current population approximately 74 individuals (as of 2025) — critically low and declining.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex predator of the Salish Sea and one of the most culturally significant animals in the PNW. SRKWs are fish-eating specialists — their diet is approximately 80% Chinook salmon, and their survival is directly linked to Chinook abundance. Each whale requires approximately 200-400 lbs of fish per day. The three SRKW pods represent a unique ecotype distinct from Bigg's (transient) killer whales that eat marine mammals — these are two parallel cultures within a single species, distinguished by diet, dialect, social structure, and genetics despite overlapping ranges. SRKW population decline to 74 individuals places them at immediate risk of extinction from demographic stochasticity, inbreeding, and environmental catastrophe (oil spill in the confined waters of the Salish Sea could be lethal for the entire population).

**Threats:** Chinook salmon decline (primary — food limitation); vessel noise and disturbance (interferes with echolocation-based foraging); contaminant accumulation (PCBs, PBDEs — SRKWs are among the most contaminated marine mammals on Earth, metabolizing blubber-stored toxins during food-limited periods); oil spill vulnerability; small population inbreeding.

**Cultural Significance:** Southern Resident orcas hold profound spiritual and cultural significance for Coast Salish nations — Lummi, Tulalip, Samish, Swinomish, and others. Orca is a prominent crest and clan animal in Northwest Coast art (Coast Salish, Makah, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw). The Lummi Nation has been vocal advocates for SRKW recovery, conducting ceremony and direct advocacy. Individual whales are known by name and genealogy within tribal communities. The Salish Sea orcas are understood as relatives, not resources. (CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** NOAA Fisheries SRKW recovery plan; Ford et al. (2000) — orca ecology; Center for Whale Research — population census; Wasser et al. (2017) — nutritional stress monitoring

### Humpback Whale (*Megaptera novaeangliae*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Balaenopteridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Mexico DPS Threatened (PNW feeding population is mixed DPS) | WA: Endangered | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large baleen whale, 40-50 ft, 25-40 tons. Distinctive long pectoral fins (15 ft — longest of any whale), knobby head, black and white tail flukes unique to each individual (used for photo-ID). Known for complex songs and spectacular breaching.

**Elevation Range:** Surface to -500 ft (y=-41--53)
**Distribution:** Increasingly common in PNW waters, especially Salish Sea and outer coast. Humpbacks feed in PNW waters from April-November before migrating to tropical breeding grounds (Mexico, Hawaii). Population recovering strongly post-whaling — recolonizing Puget Sound/Salish Sea in 2000s after decades of absence.

**Ecological Significance:**
Major consumer of krill, herring, and sand lance in PNW waters. Humpback "bubble-net feeding" — cooperative behavior where whales spiral beneath fish schools, blowing curtains of bubbles to concentrate prey before lunging upward with mouths open — is one of the most remarkable feeding strategies in the animal kingdom. Humpback fecal plumes release iron and nitrogen at the ocean surface, fertilizing phytoplankton growth (the "whale pump" effect). Their recovery in the Salish Sea has created new management challenges: entanglement in fishing gear and vessel strikes are increasing as whale and human activity overlap.

**Threats:** Fishing gear entanglement (increasing with population recovery); vessel strikes (cargo ships, ferries); prey availability (krill and forage fish fluctuations); noise pollution.

**Key Sources:** Calambokidis et al. — PNW humpback research (Cascadia Research); NOAA ESA status; SPLASH project

### Gray Whale (*Eschrichtius robustus*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Eschrichtiidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC, HAB-SANDY-BEACH (nearshore feeding)
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Delisted (1994, recovered) | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium-large baleen whale, 40-50 ft, 30-40 tons. Mottled gray with barnacle-encrusted skin (California gray whale is the most barnacle-covered cetacean). No dorsal fin — series of "knuckles" along dorsal ridge. Bottom-feeder.

**Elevation Range:** Surface to -200 ft (y=-41--46)
**Distribution:** Annual migration along PNW outer coast — longest mammalian migration (10,000 miles one-way from Baja California to Alaska). A "resident" group (Pacific Coast Feeding Group, ~250 whales) feeds in PNW waters year-round rather than continuing to Alaska.

**Ecological Significance:**
The only baleen whale that feeds primarily on the bottom — gray whales roll on their sides and suction-feed on benthic amphipods, creating feeding pits that disturb soft sediment, releasing nutrients and resuspending organic matter into the water column. This "ecosystem engineering" creates habitat heterogeneity on the seafloor and recycles buried nutrients. A single gray whale creates an estimated 0.5 km2 of feeding disturbance per season. The Pacific Coast Feeding Group represents a behavioral variant increasingly studied as climate change may alter migration patterns.

**Threats:** Ship strikes during migration; entanglement; unusual mortality events (2019-2021, cause uncertain — possibly prey limitation from Arctic warming); climate-driven shifts in prey distribution.

**Cultural Significance:** Makah Nation holds treaty-reserved rights to hunt gray whales, maintained since the Treaty of Neah Bay (1855). Makah whaling is documented at Ozette Village archaeological site (3,000+ year record). The 1999 Makah whale hunt was a landmark exercise of treaty rights. Whale hunting is central to Makah cultural identity, requiring years of physical and spiritual preparation. Nuu-chah-nulth (Nootka) are also a historically whaling people. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-SPIRITUAL, CULT-TEK, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

**Key Sources:** Jones & Swartz (2009) — gray whale biology; Makah Tribal Council; Sumich (2014) — gray whale feeding ecology

### Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Phocidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-SANDY-BEACH, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) protected | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium pinniped, 150-300 lbs, spotted gray-brown coat, large dark eyes, V-shaped nostrils. Most common marine mammal in PNW waters. Hauls out on rocks, sandbars, and beaches.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -200 ft (y=-41--46)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW coastal and inland waters — Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, outer coast, Columbia River estuary. Estimated 30,000+ in Washington waters. Population fully recovered under MMPA protection (1972).

**Ecological Significance:**
Most abundant marine mammal in the PNW. Generalist predator on fish (herring, sand lance, sculpin, salmon), squid, and octopus. Harbor seal predation on juvenile salmon is a growing concern — seal populations have increased dramatically under MMPA protection, and their aggregate consumption of salmon smolts at river mouths and in estuaries may be significant. The Ballard Locks (Seattle) seal predation on ESA-listed steelhead became a national controversy in the 1990s, highlighting the tension between marine mammal protection and salmon recovery.

**Threats:** Not threatened — fully recovered. Disturbance at haul-out sites from recreation; contaminant accumulation; entanglement in fishing gear; disease (phocine distemper).

**Key Sources:** Jeffries et al. — WA harbor seal surveys; Schmid & Wright (2015) — seal-salmon predation; NOAA MMPA stock assessments

### Steller Sea Lion (*Eumetopias jubatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Otariidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA: Western DPS Endangered, Eastern DPS Delisted (2013) | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NT

**Description:** Largest otariid (eared seal) — males to 11 ft and 2,500 lbs, females to 9 ft and 800 lbs. Tawny brown, massive head and neck (bulls develop pronounced sagittal crest). Roars like a lion (hence "sea lion").

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -500 ft (y=-41--53)
**Distribution:** Rocky haul-out and breeding colonies along PNW outer coast and Strait of Juan de Fuca. Major colonies at Rogue Reef (OR), Sea Lion Caves (OR), and scattered WA sites. Eastern DPS recovered and delisted 2013.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex pinniped predator consuming a wide range of fish (rockfish, herring, salmon, lamprey, eulachon), squid, and occasionally other pinnipeds. Steller sea lions at Columbia River consume lamprey and salmon at Bonneville Dam and other sites, creating management conflicts. Their massive body size and colonial breeding concentrate nutrients at rookeries, enriching nearshore ecosystems. The western DPS decline in Alaska (90% decline, 1970s-2000s) prompted ESA listing — eastern DPS recovery demonstrates successful marine mammal conservation.

**Threats:** Entanglement in fishing gear and marine debris; shooting (illegal but still occurs near fish runs); prey competition with commercial fisheries; disturbance at breeding rookeries.

**Key Sources:** NOAA Fisheries Steller sea lion recovery plan; Pitcher et al. — population trends; WDFW marine mammal monitoring

### Sea Otter (*Enhydra lutris*)

**Taxonomy:** Carnivora > Mustelidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-KEYSTONE, ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal ESA Threatened | WA: Endangered | IUCN: EN

**Description:** The smallest marine mammal, 45-100 lbs, dense chocolate-brown fur (densest fur of any mammal — 100,000+ hairs per cm2). No blubber — relies entirely on fur insulation and extremely high metabolic rate. Eats floating on back, using belly as dining table. Uses rocks as tools to crack shellfish.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -100 ft (y=-41--43)
**Distribution:** PNW populations were historically extirpated by the fur trade (1740s-1900s). Reintroduced to Washington's Olympic coast (1969-1970, 59 animals from Alaska). Current WA population approximately 2,000, concentrated along Olympic coast. Not present in Oregon or Puget Sound/San Juan Islands (historical range included both).

**Ecological Significance:**
The textbook keystone species in marine ecology. Sea otters eat sea urchins, which eat kelp — remove otters and kelp forests collapse into "urchin barrens" devoid of structure and biodiversity. Where sea otters are present on Washington's outer coast, kelp forests are healthy and support 750+ species. Where otters are absent (Puget Sound, San Juan Islands), sea urchin overgrazing has caused 90%+ kelp canopy loss. The trophic cascade (otter → urchin → kelp → 750 species) is the most studied and powerful example in marine ecology. Sea otter reintroduction to Puget Sound is under active consideration by USFWS as a kelp restoration strategy.

**Threats:** Oil spill vulnerability (any reduction in fur waterproofing is lethal — otters cannot survive with compromised fur); shark predation (increasing as white shark range expands northward); limited genetic diversity in reintroduced populations; disease (Toxoplasma from domestic cat feces entering marine environment via stormwater).

**Cultural Significance:** Sea otter pelts were the most valuable commodity in the maritime fur trade — a single prime pelt was worth 2-3 years' wages for a European sailor in the 1790s. This drove the decimation of both sea otter populations and dramatic changes in PNW Indigenous economies as European and American traders created intense demand. Before the fur trade, sea otters were managed by PNW coastal nations (Nuu-chah-nulth, Haida, Tlingit) as a sustainable resource. Sea otter imagery features prominently in Northwest Coast art. (CULT-MATERIAL, CULT-TEK, CULT-MANAGEMENT)

**Key Sources:** Estes & Palmisano (1974) — keystone species concept; Larson et al. — WA sea otter census; USFWS Puget Sound feasibility assessment (2024)

### Harbor Porpoise (*Phocoena phocoena*)

**Taxonomy:** Artiodactyla > Phocoenidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: MMPA protected | WA: Candidate | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small cetacean, 5 ft, 130 lbs. Dark gray back, lighter sides, no beak. Triangular dorsal fin. Shy and difficult to observe — small, inconspicuous blow. PNW's smallest cetacean.

**Elevation Range:** Surface to -200 ft (y=-41--46)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW coastal and inland waters including Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and San Juan Islands. Recolonized inner Puget Sound beginning around 2010 after decades of absence.

As small, inconspicuous predators, porpoises collectively consume significant quantities of forage fish but attract less attention than charismatic orcas and whales.

## 4. Marine Fish

PNW marine waters support 250+ fish species from coastal tidepools to deep offshore canyons. Marine fish include long-lived rockfish assemblages (some species living 200+ years), commercially vital flatfish, and forage fish species that form the prey base for marine mammals, seabirds, and salmon.

---

### Pacific Herring (*Clupea pallasii*)

**Taxonomy:** Clupeiformes > Clupeidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-PELAGIC, HAB-EELGRASS, HAB-KELP
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-KEYSTONE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Small, silvery schooling fish, 7-10 in. Blue-green back, silver sides. Forms immense schools numbering millions. Spawns on eelgrass and kelp fronds in shallow nearshore waters.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -300 ft (y=-41--48)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW coastal and inland waters. Major spawning populations in Puget Sound (Cherry Point, Squaxin Pass, others), San Juan Islands, and outer coast estuaries.

**Ecological Significance:**
The foundation forage fish of the Salish Sea. Herring are consumed by virtually every marine predator — salmon, orcas, seals, sea lions, humpback whales, bald eagles, diving ducks, herons, and lingcod. Cherry Point (near Bellingham) herring stock has declined 90%+ from historical levels, cascading through the food web. Herring spawning events coat eelgrass with billions of sticky eggs that are consumed by migratory sea ducks, gulls, and surf scoters — a critical energy source for spring migration. Herring are the "keystone prey" — their collapse would ripple through every marine food web in the PNW.

**Threats:** Historical overharvest; habitat degradation (loss of spawning substrate — eelgrass decline); water quality (toxics in Puget Sound); climate-driven changes in plankton prey; predation from recovering marine mammal populations.

**Cultural Significance:** Herring and herring roe (spawn) are important food for many PNW coastal nations. Herring roe on kelp (k'aaw) is a valued food of Haida, Tlingit, and other northern nations. Coast Salish nations managed herring fisheries at specific traditional harvest sites. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Puget Sound Partnership — herring monitoring; Stick et al. — WA herring stock status; Thornton et al. (2010) — Indigenous herring management

### Lingcod (*Ophiodon elongatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Scorpaeniformes > Hexagrammidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Game species | OR: Game species | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Large, predatory greenling, up to 5 ft and 80 lbs. Mottled brown-green, large mouth with prominent canine teeth. Ambush predator lurking in rocky reef and kelp habitat. Flesh sometimes bright blue-green (harmless biliverdin pigment).

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -300 ft (y=-41--48)

Male nest-guarding behavior creates intense site fidelity — males remain with egg masses for 6-10 weeks, making them vulnerable to fishing during this period. Historical overharvest led to widespread population declines; recovery management (seasonal closures, bag limits) has been successful.

**Threats:** Recreational and commercial overfishing (managed); climate-driven shifts in prey availability; nest site disturbance.

### Quillback Rockfish (*Sebastes maliger*)

**Taxonomy:** Scorpaeniformes > Sebastidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC (but heavily depleted in Puget Sound)

**Description:** Medium rockfish (15-24 in), brown-orange with distinctive tall, spiny dorsal fin rays (the "quills"). Yellow-brown mottling. Lives 90+ years — among the longest-lived fish in the PNW.

**Elevation Range:** -30 to -600 ft (y=-42--56)
**Distribution:** Rocky reefs throughout PNW, from Puget Sound to outer coast. Sedentary — individuals occupy small home ranges (100-200 m) for decades.

**Ecological Significance:**
Rockfish represent the "old-growth" of marine ecosystems — their extreme longevity (some PNW rockfish species live 200+ years) means populations take decades to recover from overfishing. Quillback rockfish aggregate at rocky reef structures, creating localized community complexity. Their site fidelity makes them ideal for marine protected area management — small reserves can protect individuals for their entire lives. Puget Sound rockfish populations are severely depleted (estimated 70-95% decline from historical levels), illustrating how slow-growing, long-lived species are uniquely vulnerable to overharvest.

**Threats:** Historical and ongoing overfishing (recreational and commercial); bycatch in trawl fisheries; extremely slow recovery due to late maturity (age 10-15) and longevity; habitat degradation (rocky reef disturbance).

**Key Sources:** Drake et al. (2010) — Puget Sound rockfish; Love et al. (2002) — rockfish biology; WDFW bottomfish management

### Copper Rockfish (*Sebastes caurinus*)

**Taxonomy:** Scorpaeniformes > Sebastidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Description:** Medium rockfish (16-22 in), copper-orange to brown with distinctive white lateral line stripe. Lives 50+ years.

**Elevation Range:** -10 to -300 ft (y=-41--48)
**Distribution:** Rocky reefs and kelp forests throughout PNW. More common in shallower waters than quillback rockfish.

**Ecological Significance:**
Common nearshore rockfish with strong site fidelity. Copper rockfish are aggressive territorial predators on crabs, shrimp, and small fish, maintaining invertebrate population balance on rocky reefs. Their relative abundance compared to quillback makes them a more visible indicator of nearshore reef health for divers and snorkelers.

**Threats:** Recreational overfishing; bycatch; slow recovery from depletion; barotrauma (gas bladder injury when caught from depth and released — often fatal).

**Key Sources:** Love et al. (2002); WDFW bottomfish management; Lamb & Edgell (2010)

### Sixgill Shark (*Hexanchus griseus*)

**Taxonomy:** Hexanchiformes > Hexanchidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-APEX
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NT

**Description:** Large, primitive shark, 10-16 ft (occasionally to 20 ft). Six gill slits (most sharks have five). Single dorsal fin set far back. Ancient lineage — hexanchid sharks are essentially unchanged since the Jurassic period (200+ million years).

**Elevation Range:** -30 to -930 ft (y=-42--64)
**Distribution:** Deep waters of Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and outer coast. Puget Sound supports a unique, semi-resident population that is more accessible to study than any other sixgill population worldwide. Seasonally ascends to shallow water at night.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex predator of the deep basin — the largest predator in Puget Sound's dark, cold basins. Sixgill sharks are deep-water scavengers and predators on fish, marine mammals (seal carcasses), and other sharks. Their seasonal shallow-water movements in Puget Sound (May-September) bring deep-basin energy to nearshore habitats. As a living fossil, the sixgill represents an ancient predator lineage that has persisted through multiple mass extinction events.

**Threats:** Bycatch in deepwater fisheries; contaminant accumulation in deep-water food webs; disturbance from submarine cable laying; limited population data.

### Pacific Halibut (*Hippoglossus stenolepis*)

**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC

## 5. Marine Invertebrates

PNW marine waters support an estimated 3,000+ invertebrate species, from microscopic zooplankton to giant Pacific octopus. Marine invertebrates form the prey base for most marine vertebrates, structure benthic habitats (mussels, barnacles, corals, sponges), and include keystone species whose presence or absence transforms entire communities. Several PNW invertebrates are commercially and culturally vital.

### Dungeness Crab (*Metacarcinus magister*)

**Taxonomy:** Decapoda > Cancridae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-EELGRASS, HAB-SANDY-BEACH, HAB-KELP
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Commercially managed | OR: Commercially managed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Large crab, carapace to 10 in wide. Fan-shaped, reddish-brown. Named for the town of Dungeness on Washington's Olympic Peninsula. PNW's most valuable commercial and recreational shellfish species.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -300 ft (y=-41--48)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW coastal and inland waters from Alaska to central California. Juveniles rear in eelgrass meadows; adults on sandy and muddy bottoms.

**Ecological Significance:**
Keystone predator-prey species — Dungeness crab is both a major predator on clams, mussels, and other invertebrates, and critical prey for octopus, sea otters, lingcod, and marine mammals. The Dungeness crab fishery is the most valuable single-species fishery in the PNW (valued at $200+ million annually across WA, OR, and CA), making crab conservation an economic as well as ecological priority.

### Geoduck Clam (*Panopea generosa*)

**Taxonomy:** Veneroida > Hiatellidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-DEEP-BASIN (soft substrate)
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Commercially managed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** The largest burrowing clam in the world — shell to 8 in, siphon extends 3+ ft. Total weight 2-10 lbs (record 14 lbs). Lives buried 2-3 ft deep in sandy-mud substrate with only siphon tip exposed. Pronounced "gooey-duck" (from Lushootseed *gwídəq*, "dig deep").

**Elevation Range:** -15 to -200 ft (y=-41--46)
**Distribution:** Puget Sound and Strait of Georgia — the global center of geoduck abundance. Subtidal on sandy-mud bottoms, 15-200 ft depth.

**Ecological Significance:**
Among the longest-lived animals on Earth — individual geoducks have been aged at 168 years. Their extreme longevity means populations integrate over a century of environmental conditions, making them living records of marine environmental change. Geoduck filter-feeding removes phytoplankton and bacteria from the water column — dense geoduck beds (10+ per m2 in prime habitat) collectively filter enormous volumes of water, contributing to water clarity. The geoduck fishery is Washington's most valuable shellfish export (primarily to Asian markets), creating a unique intersection of ecology, economy, and cultural food sovereignty.

**Threats:** Overharvest (wild fishery and aquaculture both expanding); habitat disturbance from harvest methods (water-jet dislodging); sea star predation on exposed siphons; sea otter reintroduction could impact exposed geoducks.

**Cultural Significance:** The word "geoduck" comes from Lushootseed (Coast Salish), testifying to long Indigenous harvest tradition. Geoduck clam gardens were traditional harvest areas for Puget Sound nations. Treaty tribes hold co-management rights for geoduck harvest. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE, CULT-TEK)

### Giant Pacific Octopus (*Enteroctopus dofleini*)

**Taxonomy:** Octopoda > Enteroctopodidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** World's largest octopus species — arm span to 20 ft, weight to 100+ lbs (typically 25-50 lbs). Red-brown, capable of dramatic color and texture changes via chromatophores. Eight arms with 2,240 suckers total. Remarkably intelligent — solves mazes, opens jars, recognizes individual humans.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -500 ft (y=-41--53)
**Distribution:** Throughout PNW rocky reef and kelp forest habitats. Puget Sound supports accessible populations studied by divers. Den in rocky crevices and under boulders.

**Ecological Significance:**
Apex invertebrate predator of PNW reef ecosystems. Octopus prey on crab (including Dungeness), shrimp, clams, and fish. Their intelligence, problem-solving ability, and individual personality variation make them the most cognitively complex invertebrate in PNW waters. Female octopus guard egg clutches for 6-10 months without feeding, then die — this semelparous lifecycle and maternal investment is among the most extreme in the animal kingdom. Octopus dens are surrounded by "middens" of discarded prey shells that create microhabitat for small fish and invertebrates.

**Threats:** Bycatch in pot and trawl fisheries; den habitat loss from bottom disturbance; climate-driven warming (octopus are sensitive to temperature); emerging targeted fisheries.

### Ochre Sea Star (*Pisaster ochraceus*)

**Taxonomy:** Forcipulatida > Asteriidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-KEYSTONE
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: LC (but severely affected by SSWS)

**Description:** Large, five-armed sea star, 6-14 in diameter. Variable color — orange, purple, or brown (color determined by diet and genetics). Rough, granular upper surface. Pries open mussels using hydraulic tube feet exerting 25+ lbs of pull force.

**Elevation Range:** 0 ft (y=-41)
**Distribution:** Rocky shores throughout PNW outer coast and San Juan Islands. Intertidal to shallow subtidal.

**Ecological Significance:**
THE keystone species — literally the organism for which Robert Paine coined the term in 1966 on Tatoosh Island (Makah tribal territory). Paine's experiments showed that removing ochre sea stars from intertidal plots caused California mussels to dominate the entire rock surface, eliminating 15+ other species. With sea stars present, mussel beds are held in check, creating space for barnacles, algae, limpets, chitons, and anemones. Sea Star Wasting Syndrome (SSWS, 2013-present) caused catastrophic die-offs (80-90% mortality in some areas), triggering mussel bed expansion and the loss of intertidal biodiversity that Paine predicted 50 years earlier. Partial recovery is underway but remains incomplete.

**Threats:** Sea Star Wasting Syndrome (densovirus-linked, exacerbated by marine heat waves); ocean acidification; marine heat waves; intertidal habitat disturbance.

**Cultural Significance:** Observed and recognized by coastal PNW nations as part of the intertidal landscape. The ochre sea star's role in maintaining mussel bed boundaries was likely understood through centuries of intertidal harvesting experience. (CULT-TEK)

**Key Sources:** Paine (1966) — keystone species concept; Hewson et al. (2014) — SSWS; Menge et al. — long-term intertidal monitoring

### Sunflower Sea Star (*Pycnopodia helianthoides*)

**Taxonomy:** Forcipulatida > Asteriidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-KEYSTONE, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Under ESA review | IUCN: CR (Critically Endangered — assessed 2020)

**Description:** World's largest sea star — up to 24 arms and 3+ ft diameter. Soft-bodied, fast-moving (can travel 3 ft/min — fast for a sea star). Purple, orange, or brown. Voracious predator.

**Elevation Range:** 0 to -300 ft (y=-41--48)
**Distribution:** Historically throughout PNW from Alaska to Baja California. Sea Star Wasting Syndrome caused estimated 90-95% population crash across the range. Some areas (Salish Sea) have experienced near-complete extirpation. Minimal recovery observed as of 2025.

**Ecological Significance:**
The primary predator controlling sea urchin populations in subtidal habitats. Sunflower sea stars are fast enough to chase and capture sea urchins — their presence triggers urchin flight behavior, reducing urchin grazing pressure on kelp. Following SSWS-driven sunflower star extirpation, urchin populations exploded, creating "urchin barrens" that have decimated kelp forests from California to British Columbia. The collapse of sunflower stars is now the leading cause of kelp forest decline in the PNW (alongside the loss of sea otters from most of their historical range). This is a trophic cascade in real-time: remove the predator (sunflower star), prey (urchins) explodes, foundation species (kelp) collapses.

**Threats:** SSWS (primary — near-extinction in many areas); ocean warming exacerbating SSWS vulnerability; extremely slow population recovery (low reproductive rate, broadcast spawning requires minimum density for fertilization success — populations may be below Allee thresholds).

**Key Sources:** Harvell et al. (2019) — SSWS ecology; Hamilton et al. (2021) — urchin barren formation; IUCN Red List CR assessment

- See flora: Bull kelp (kelp forest collapse from star loss → urchin increase)

### California Mussel (*Mytilus californianus*)

**Taxonomy:** Mytilida > Mytilidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-FOUNDATION, ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Large mussel (up to 10 in), dark blue-black shell with prominent radial ribs. Forms dense beds on wave-exposed rocky shores, attached by strong byssal threads. Can withstand wave forces exceeding 25 m/s.

**Elevation Range:** 0 ft (y=-41)
**Distribution:** Outer coast rocky shores from Alaska to Baja California. Dominant space-occupier in mid-intertidal zone on exposed rocky shores.

**Ecological Significance:**
Foundation species of the mid-intertidal zone. Mussel beds create three-dimensional habitat supporting 300+ species of invertebrates living among and between shells — worms, crabs, snails, sea cucumbers, and barnacles. This is the "apartment building" of the intertidal: remove mussels, and 300+ species lose their habitat. Mussel beds are also massive filter feeders, removing phytoplankton and bacteria from the water column during high tide submersion. The mussel bed is held in check by ochre sea star predation — without sea stars, mussels competitively exclude all other intertidal species.

**Threats:** Ocean acidification (weakens byssal thread attachment and shell formation); marine heat waves (thermal stress during low tide exposure); sea star wasting syndrome (release from predation allows mussel expansion but long-term community effects are negative).

**Cultural Significance:** Important food source for coastal PNW nations. Shell middens at archaeological sites along the PNW coast contain California mussel shells accumulated over thousands of years of harvest. Mussel harvest was a year-round activity at traditional intertidal gathering sites. (CULT-FOOD-MEDICINE)

**Key Sources:** Paine (1966, 1974) — mussel-sea star dynamics; Suchanek (1994) — mussel bed ecology; Menge et al. — intertidal monitoring

### Red Sea Urchin (*Mesocentrotus franciscanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Camarodonta > Strongylocentrotidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-KELP
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | WA: Commercially harvested | IUCN: NE

**Description:** Large sea urchin (up to 7 in diameter), long red-purple spines. Grazes on kelp and algae. Can live 100+ years — among the longest-lived marine invertebrates.

**Elevation Range:** -10 to -200 ft (y=-41--46)
**Distribution:** Rocky reefs and kelp forests throughout PNW subtidal zone.

**Ecological Significance:**
Central player in the kelp forest trophic cascade. When urchin populations are controlled by predators (sea otters, sunflower sea stars), kelp forests thrive. When predators are removed, urchin populations explode and overgraze kelp, creating "urchin barrens" — rocky substrates stripped of all macroalgae and the hundreds of species kelp forests support. The current PNW kelp crisis is driven by urchin overgrazing following the double loss of sea otter (historical) and sunflower sea star (SSWS 2013-present) predation. Red urchin uni (roe) is commercially harvested for Japanese markets — providing an economic incentive for urchin removal that aligns with kelp restoration goals.

**Threats:** Not threatened (abundant — overabundant in many areas); commercial harvest may be beneficial for kelp restoration; ocean acidification may affect larvae.

**Key Sources:** Estes & Palmisano (1974) — otter-urchin-kelp cascade; Rogers-Bennett & Catton (2019) — urchin barrens; WDFW urchin fishery

### Pacific Razor Clam (*Siliqua patula*)

### Olympia Oyster (*Ostrea lurida*)

**Taxonomy:** Ostreida > Ostreidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL, HAB-EELGRASS
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Species of Concern | WA: Candidate | IUCN: NE

**Elevation Range:** -10-0 ft (y=-41)

A foundation and ecosystem engineering species. ### Giant Green Anemone (*Anthopleura xanthogrammica*)

**Taxonomy:** Actiniaria > Actiniidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL

### Glass Sponge (*Aphrocallistes vastus*)

**Taxonomy:** Hexactinellida > Aphrocallistidae
**Elevation Band:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Habitat:** HAB-DEEP-BASIN
**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-FOUNDATION
**Conservation Status:** Federal: Not listed | IUCN: NE (but reefs are of significant conservation concern)

**Description:** Siliceous sponge, 2-6 ft tall, white to cream, vase or tube-shaped, with a skeleton of intricate glass spicules (silica). Colonial — individuals fuse to form reef structures. Reef formation takes centuries to millennia.

**Elevation Range:** -60 to -900 ft (y=-42--64)
**Distribution:** Deep waters of the Strait of Georgia and Howe Sound (BC) — glass sponge reefs were thought extinct until living reefs were discovered in 1987. Scattered formations in deeper Puget Sound sites. BC reefs are the only living glass sponge reefs known in the world — all others are known only from the fossil record (Jurassic-age, 200 million years ago).

**Ecological Significance:**
Living fossils — glass sponge reefs were a dominant feature of Jurassic seas, and were thought to have gone extinct 40 million years ago until the Strait of Georgia discoveries. BC glass sponge reefs cover an estimated 700+ km2 and are up to 9,000 years old. These reefs provide three-dimensional habitat for rockfish, prawns, and invertebrates in otherwise featureless deep-water environments. Glass sponges are remarkable filter feeders — they can filter their body volume of water every 5 seconds, processing bacteria and dissolved organic carbon. A single reef filters an estimated 100 billion liters of water per day. These are irreplaceable structures — destruction by bottom trawling eliminates millennia of growth that cannot recover on human timescales.

**Threats:** Bottom trawling (reefs destroyed in minutes that took millennia to form); anchor damage from vessels; sedimentation from coastal development; climate-driven deoxygenation of deep waters.

**Key Sources:** Conway et al. (2001) — glass sponge reef discovery; Leys et al. (2004) — sponge reef ecology; DFO Canada reef protection

### Key Ecological Themes

1. **Salmon as ecosystem backbone:** All 5 Pacific salmon + steelhead connect ocean, freshwater, and terrestrial ecosystems through their anadromous lifecycle. Marine-derived nutrients transported by spawning salmon sustain PNW forest productivity, feed 130+ species, and underpin the cultural identity of every PNW Indigenous nation.

2. **Trophic cascades in real time:** The otter → urchin → kelp cascade is playing out now. Loss of sunflower sea stars to SSWS (2013-present) removed the last urchin predator from most PNW waters, triggering kelp forest collapse. Sea otter reintroduction to Puget Sound is being evaluated as a kelp restoration strategy — bringing the textbook cascade full circle.

3. **Living fossils:** Pacific lamprey (500+ million years), white sturgeon (200+ million years), sixgill shark (200+ million years), glass sponge reefs (Jurassic relicts). PNW marine and freshwater systems harbor species connecting modern ecosystems to deep geological time.

4. **Cultural food sovereignty:** Salmon, lamprey, eulachon, geoduck, Dungeness crab, Olympia oyster, and halibut are all culturally central First Foods for PNW nations. Their conservation is inseparable from treaty rights, cultural identity, and Indigenous food sovereignty. The Boldt Decision (1974) and subsequent legal frameworks affirm tribal co-management rights.

5. **Ocean acidification and warming:** Dungeness crab larvae, mussel attachment, glass sponge growth, and pteropod shell formation are all vulnerable to declining ocean pH. Marine heat waves exacerbate Sea Star Wasting Syndrome, disrupt forage fish availability, and push cold-water species poleward.

6. **Keystone concept born here:** Robert Paine's 1966 experiments at Tatoosh Island (Makah territory) — removing ochre sea stars and documenting mussel bed expansion — created the keystone species concept that transformed ecology. The PNW intertidal remains the world's best-studied keystone system.
