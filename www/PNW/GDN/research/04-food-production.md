# M4 — Food Production & Seasonal Calendar for PNW Gardening

Maritime Pacific Northwest west of the Cascade crest, from Vancouver BC (49.3°N) to Roseburg OR (43.2°N). USDA hardiness zones 7b–9a. This module covers year-round vegetable and fruit production: what to grow, when to plant, how to extend seasons, and which crops reward effort versus which ones fight the climate.

---

## 1. Year-Round Harvest Potential

The maritime Pacific Northwest possesses an almost unique advantage among North American growing regions: mild winters that rarely drop below 20°F (zone 8b) to 5°F (zone 7b), combined with cool but frost-free shoulders that extend from March through November in most locations [PRO-01]. This means that with deliberate planning, a PNW garden can produce harvestable food in every calendar month — a claim that very few regions outside the Gulf Coast or Southern California can match [EXT-02].

Three strategic shifts separate twelve-month producers from summer-only gardeners:

**Shift 1: Embrace cool-season crops as primary production.** The PNW's long, cool springs (March–June) and mild autumns (September–December) are ideal for brassicas, roots, alliums, and leafy greens. These crops grow slowly but steadily in 40–60°F air temperatures and tolerate light frosts that would kill warm-season plants [PRO-01]. In most PNW gardens, cool-season crops actually outproduce warm-season crops by total weight and harvest duration [PRO-07].

**Shift 2: Use the August "second spring" for fall and winter planting.** The single most important date range in PNW food gardening is July 15–August 15 [PRO-06]. This is the window for sowing fall and overwintering crops — kale, carrots, beets, lettuce, spinach, chard, and Asian greens — that will produce from October through March. Gardeners who miss this window lose half their annual production potential [PRO-07]. The concept is counterintuitive: you plant in the heat of summer for the cool of winter.

**Shift 3: Manage drainage rather than moisture.** From October through May, the challenge is not watering but preventing waterlogging. PNW soils, particularly the glacial till of the Puget Sound lowlands and the clay-heavy Willamette Valley soils, become saturated during the wet season [EXT-04]. Raised beds, French drains, and winter cover crops that maintain soil structure are more important than irrigation infrastructure for year-round production [PRO-09].

Gardeners who only grow May through September are using roughly 40–50% of their garden's productive capacity [PRO-01]. The remaining 50–60% is available through cool-season planting, season extension structures, and variety selection tuned to maritime conditions.

### The PNW Growing Equation

| Factor | PNW Advantage | PNW Challenge |
|--------|--------------|---------------|
| Frost-free season | 180–250 days (zone-dependent) [GOV-01] | Last spring frost can be late (Apr 15–May 15) |
| Summer heat | Long days (15–16 hrs at solstice) | Low heat units; cool nights (50–55°F) [EXT-06] |
| Winter mildness | Rarely below 20°F in zone 8b [GOV-01] | Persistent cloud cover limits photosynthesis Nov–Feb |
| Rainfall | 35–55 in/yr, mostly Oct–Apr [EXT-07] | Summer drought (Jul–Sep typically <2 in total) [EXT-07] |
| Soil | Deep, fertile valley soils | Clay/till drainage problems; low pH (5.0–5.8) [EXT-04] |
| Day length | 16 hrs summer solstice | 8.5 hrs winter solstice — growth slows dramatically |

## 2. Month-by-Month Planting & Harvest Calendar

This calendar covers the full maritime PNW corridor. Zone-specific adjustments are noted where timing differs significantly between the warmer Willamette Valley/Portland (zone 8b–9a), the moderate Puget Sound lowlands (zone 8a–8b), and the cooler north Puget Sound/San Juan Islands/southern BC (zone 7b–8a) [GOV-01].

### January

**Theme:** Planning, preparation, earliest indoor starts

**Plant (indoors):**
- Onions from seed — long-day varieties need 10–12 weeks before transplanting in March–April [EXT-02]. Start under lights January 1–15 for large bulbs
- Leeks from seed — same timing as onions; slow germinators benefit from early start [PRO-01]
- Artichokes from seed — need vernalization; indoor start allows first-year harvest [EXT-06]
- Late January: celery, celeriac (slow growers, 120+ DTM) [PRO-06]

**Plant (outdoors):**
- Mild years only (zone 8b–9a): fava beans direct-sow if soil is workable [PRO-01]
- Dormant bare-root fruit trees, berry canes, and grape vines during any thaw [PRO-05]

**Harvest:**
- Overwintered kale, collards, Brussels sprouts, leeks [PRO-01]
- Storage crops: winter squash, potatoes, onions, garlic from fall harvest [EXT-02]
- Cold frame: winter lettuce, mache, claytonia if sown in September [PRO-09]

**Maintenance:**
- Order seeds — PNW-adapted varieties sell out early [PRO-07]
- Soil test if not done in fall — lime applications need 6–8 weeks before planting [EXT-04]
- Prune dormant fruit trees (apples, pears) on dry days; do NOT prune stone fruit until summer [PRO-05]
- Clean and sharpen tools; repair cold frames and row cover [EXT-02]
- Review garden journal from previous year; adjust variety choices based on performance [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b (Bellingham, San Juans): Ground likely frozen or snow-covered; focus entirely indoors [GOV-01]
- Zone 8b–9a (Portland, Eugene): May see early crocus; some years soil is workable for peas by late January [EXT-02]

---

### February

**Theme:** Indoor seed starting ramps up; first outdoor sowings possible

**Plant (indoors):**
- Brassica starts: broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts — 6–8 weeks before transplant date [EXT-02]
- Lettuce, spinach, chard starts for early transplant [PRO-06]
- Peppers (mid-February) — need 8–10 weeks indoors and bottom heat (75–85°F) for germination [EXT-04]
- Tomatoes (late February, zone 8b–9a only) — 6–8 weeks before transplant [PRO-07]
- Herbs: parsley, cilantro, basil (late month, indoors only) [PRO-01]

**Plant (outdoors):**
- Peas: direct-sow sugar snap, snow peas, shelling peas as soon as soil reaches 40°F [EXT-02]. Inoculate with rhizobium for first-time pea beds
- Fava beans: direct-sow all zones [PRO-01]
- Spinach: direct-sow; tolerates frost to 20°F [EXT-04]
- Bare-root strawberries, raspberries, blueberries [PRO-05]
- Asparagus crowns (late February, zone 8b+) [EXT-02]
- Seed potatoes: chit (pre-sprout) indoors late February for March planting [PRO-01]

**Harvest:**
- Same as January plus early sprouting broccoli (Purple Sprouting overwintered) [PRO-06]
- First rhubarb shoots in zone 8b–9a [EXT-02]

**Maintenance:**
- Finish pruning fruit trees before bud break [PRO-05]
- Apply dormant spray (lime-sulfur or copper) to fruit trees for overwintering fungal spores [EXT-05]
- Top-dress beds with compost (1–2 inches); do NOT till wet clay soil [EXT-04]
- Check slug bait stations — slugs are active above 40°F in PNW [EXT-05]
- Weed early; winter annual weeds are small and easy to pull now [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Pea sowing may wait until late February or early March [EXT-02]
- Zone 9a (Roseburg): Earliest tomato transplant possible late February with protection [PRO-07]

---

### March

**Theme:** Major outdoor planting month; the PNW growing season begins

**Plant (indoors):**
- Tomatoes from seed (all zones) — aim for transplants ready by mid-May [PRO-07]
- Peppers (zone 7b–8a) if not started in February [EXT-04]
- Cucumbers, squash, melons (late March, 3–4 weeks before transplant) [EXT-02]
- Annual flowers for pollinator support: marigolds, zinnias, cosmos [PRO-04]

**Plant (outdoors, direct sow):**
- Carrots: sow when soil reaches 45°F; slow germination (14–21 days) [EXT-02]. Pelleted seed helps spacing
- Beets: direct-sow; each "seed" is a cluster of 2–4 seeds, thin to 3 inches [PRO-01]
- Radishes: 25–30 days to harvest; first succession [PRO-06]
- Lettuce, arugula, mustard greens: direct-sow or transplant starts [EXT-02]
- Chard, kale: direct-sow or transplant [PRO-01]
- Potatoes: plant seed potatoes 4 inches deep, 12 inches apart, mid-to-late March [EXT-02]
- Onion sets and transplants (not seeds — those were started in January) [EXT-04]
- Parsnips: sow early; need 100–130 days and fresh seed (viability drops fast) [PRO-01]
- Turnips, kohlrabi: direct-sow [EXT-06]

**Plant (outdoors, transplant):**
- Brassica transplants: broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage — harden off first [EXT-02]
- Lettuce transplants from February starts [PRO-06]

**Harvest:**
- Overwintered greens: kale, chard, collards (bolting starts — harvest before flower stalks elongate) [PRO-01]
- Rhubarb (all zones) [EXT-02]
- Asparagus (established beds, zone 8b+) [EXT-02]
- Early radishes from fall-sown or February-sown [PRO-06]
- Chives, sorrel, perennial herbs reappear [PRO-01]

**Maintenance:**
- Apply lime if soil test indicated pH below 6.0 — most vegetables prefer 6.0–7.0 [EXT-04]
- Side-dress overwintered crops with blood meal or fish fertilizer [PRO-01]
- Set up pea trellises before vines need them [EXT-02]
- Begin slug management in earnest: beer traps, iron phosphate bait, hand-pick at dusk [EXT-05]
- Remove row cover from overwintered crops as temperatures warm [PRO-09]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Delay potato planting until early April if soil is still cold/wet [EXT-02]
- Zone 8b–9a: Asparagus harvest begins mid-March; snap spears at soil level [EXT-02]

---

### April

**Theme:** Cool-season garden in full swing; warm-season hardening off

**Plant (outdoors, direct sow):**
- Succession sow: lettuce, radishes, spinach, arugula every 2–3 weeks [PRO-06]
- Beans (late April, zone 8b+ only) — soil must reach 60°F for germination [EXT-02]
- Corn (late April, zone 9a only) — marginal crop, needs warmest microclimates [EXT-04]
- Dill, cilantro: direct-sow; cilantro bolts quickly in warm weather, so sow early [PRO-01]
- Second succession of carrots, beets [EXT-02]

**Plant (outdoors, transplant):**
- More brassicas: broccoli, cauliflower, kohlrabi [EXT-02]
- Lettuce transplants continue [PRO-06]
- Celery and celeriac transplants (started January–February) [PRO-01]

**Harvest:**
- Asparagus (peak, all zones) [EXT-02]
- Peas (snow and sugar snap from February sowing, zone 8b+) [PRO-01]
- Spring lettuce, spinach, radishes from March sowings [EXT-02]
- Rhubarb (peak) [PRO-01]
- Green garlic (pull young plants from fall-planted garlic if thinning is needed) [PRO-06]

**Maintenance:**
- Harden off warm-season transplants: move outdoors during day, bring in at night for 7–10 days [EXT-04]
- Monitor brassicas for cabbage root maggot — use row cover from transplant day [EXT-05]
- Thin carrots, beets, parsnips to proper spacing [EXT-02]
- Set up tomato supports (cages, stakes, or string trellis) before planting [PRO-09]
- Watch for late frost — have row cover ready to protect tender starts [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b–8a: Hold warm-season transplants until mid-May [EXT-04]
- Zone 8b–9a: Some gardeners successfully transplant tomatoes under Wall-o-Water by late April [PRO-09]

---

### May

**Theme:** The warm-season transition — timing is everything

**Plant (outdoors, direct sow):**
- Beans (bush and pole): soil must be 60°F+; direct-sow 1 inch deep, 3 inches apart [EXT-02]
- Corn (zone 8b+): plant in blocks of at least 4 rows for wind pollination [EXT-04]
- Squash, cucumbers, melons: direct-sow after last frost or transplant starts [EXT-02]
- Succession lettuce (shade-tolerant varieties for summer: Jericho, Muir, Nevada) [PRO-07]
- Dill, basil (direct-sow basil only after soil is 65°F+) [PRO-01]

**Plant (outdoors, transplant):**
- **Tomatoes: THE critical timing decision.** Common mistake: planting during a warm early-May week, then cool weather (50°F highs, 40°F nights) stunts growth for weeks [PRO-07]. Better approach:
  - Zone 8b–9a: Transplant May 10–20 with protection, or wait until June 1 without [EXT-02]
  - Zone 8a: May 15–25 with protection [EXT-04]
  - Zone 7b: June 1–10 is safer [EXT-06]
  - Soil temperature at transplant should be 60°F at 4-inch depth [PRO-01]
  - Use Wall-o-Water or black plastic mulch to gain 2–3 weeks [PRO-09]
- Peppers: transplant 2 weeks after tomatoes; need warmer conditions [EXT-04]
- Cucumbers, squash, melon transplants [EXT-02]
- Eggplant (zone 8b+ only, warmest microclimate) [EXT-04]

**Harvest:**
- Peas (peak harvest) [PRO-01]
- Lettuce, spinach, arugula (spring crop before bolting) [EXT-02]
- Radishes, turnips, kohlrabi [PRO-06]
- Asparagus (stop harvesting by June 1 to let ferns grow) [EXT-02]
- Strawberries (June-bearing begin late May, zone 8b+) [PRO-05]
- Fava beans (planted January–February) [PRO-01]

**Maintenance:**
- Mulch around warm-season transplants: straw, leaves, or compost (2–3 inches) [EXT-04]
- Begin irrigation regime: warm-season crops need 1–1.5 inches/week [EXT-02]
- Prune suckers on indeterminate tomatoes weekly [PRO-09]
- Trellis cucumbers and pole beans [PRO-01]
- Monitor for aphids on brassicas and fava beans — blast with water first [EXT-05]
- Side-dress heavy feeders (brassicas, corn) with fish emulsion or blood meal [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: May is still cool-season planting month; do not rush warm-season crops [EXT-06]
- Zone 9a: Warm-season crops can go out early May; some gardeners plant tomatoes April 20 [PRO-07]

---

### June

**Theme:** Warm-season establishment; last spring cool-season harvest

**Plant (outdoors):**
- Succession beans every 2–3 weeks until July 15 [PRO-06]
- Succession lettuce in partial shade [EXT-02]
- Late squash and cucumber plantings (bush varieties for shorter season) [PRO-01]
- Basil, dill, cilantro (cilantro will bolt fast — use slow-bolt varieties like Calypso) [PRO-07]
- Edamame / soybeans: direct-sow early June [EXT-06]

**Harvest:**
- Strawberries (peak June-bearing harvest) [PRO-05]
- Peas (finish harvest; plants decline in heat) [PRO-01]
- Lettuce, spinach (last spring crop — bolting now) [EXT-02]
- Broccoli, cauliflower (spring transplants heading up) [EXT-02]
- Early potatoes (new potatoes — dig carefully alongside plants) [PRO-01]
- Garlic scapes (hardneck varieties) — cut scapes to direct energy to bulb [PRO-06]
- Cherries (sweet and sour) [PRO-05]
- Early raspberries (summer-bearing) [PRO-05]

**Maintenance:**
- Remove spent pea vines; leave roots in soil (nitrogen nodules) [PRO-01]
- Consistent watering: deep soaking 2–3 times/week better than daily sprinkling [EXT-04]
- Watch for early blight on tomatoes — bottom leaves yellowing, remove and dispose [EXT-05]
- Hill potatoes: mound soil around stems as they grow [EXT-02]
- Begin planning "second spring" — order fall/winter seeds NOW [PRO-06]
- Pinch basil flowers to maintain leaf production [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Warm-season crops just getting established; growth accelerates with longest days [EXT-06]
- All zones: June 20–21 is summer solstice (16+ hrs daylight); maximum photosynthetic potential [EXT-07]

---

### July

**Theme:** Peak summer; irrigation critical; second-spring planning

**Plant (outdoors):**
- July 1–15: Last succession of bush beans [EXT-02]
- July 1–15: Sow fall broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage starts (transplant into garden in August) [PRO-06]
- July 15–31: Begin "second spring" sowings (see Section 5 for full details):
  - Kale, collards: direct-sow or start transplants [PRO-01]
  - Carrots (storage crop): sow by July 20 for fall/winter harvest [EXT-02]
  - Beets: direct-sow for fall harvest [PRO-06]
  - Rutabaga: direct-sow; needs 90 days [PRO-01]
- Succession lettuce (heat-tolerant varieties) in shade [PRO-07]
- Buckwheat cover crop in empty beds — fast-growing pollinator support [PRO-01]

**Harvest:**
- Tomatoes: first ripe fruit mid-to-late July (Sungold, Stupice, Early Girl earliest) [PRO-07]
- Beans (bush): first sowing producing [EXT-02]
- Cucumbers: daily harvest when producing [PRO-01]
- Summer squash/zucchini: harvest at 6–8 inches for best quality; check daily [EXT-02]
- Blueberries (peak harvest, continues through August) [PRO-05]
- Raspberries (summer-bearing peak) [PRO-05]
- Garlic: harvest when lower 3–4 leaves have browned; cure in dry shade for 2–3 weeks [PRO-06]
- Onions (bulb): harvest when tops fall over naturally [EXT-02]
- Potatoes: main crop harvest begins (dig when tops die back) [PRO-01]

**Maintenance:**
- Irrigation is critical: PNW July averages <0.5 inches rain [EXT-07]
- Water tomatoes consistently — irregular watering causes blossom end rot [EXT-04]
- Remove lower tomato leaves touching soil to prevent soil-splash disease [EXT-05]
- Watch for powdery mildew on squash — improve air circulation, avoid overhead watering [EXT-05]
- Harvest zucchini DAILY or they become baseball bats [PRO-09]
- Cure garlic and onions properly for storage (dry, shaded, good air flow) [PRO-06]
- Feed tomatoes, peppers, squash with balanced fertilizer every 2–3 weeks [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Tomato harvest starts later (late July to early August) due to fewer heat units [EXT-06]
- Zone 9a: Peak tomato production; main-season varieties (Brandywine, Cherokee Purple) ripening [PRO-07]

### August

**Theme:** THE SECOND SPRING — the most important planting month for year-round production

**Plant (outdoors, direct sow) — Critical window July 15–August 15:**
- Kale: direct-sow or transplant; the cornerstone winter crop [PRO-01]
- Lettuce: direct-sow every 10 days through August 20 for continuous fall harvest [PRO-06]
- Spinach: direct-sow; needs cool soil to germinate — water furrow first, sow, cover with board [PRO-07]
- Chard: direct-sow; more cold-hardy than lettuce [PRO-01]
- Beets: direct-sow by August 1 for fall harvest [EXT-02]
- Carrots: sow by August 1 (later sowings won't size up before short days) [PRO-06]
- Radishes: direct-sow; 25–30 day crop perfect for August succession [EXT-02]
- Asian greens: bok choy, mizuna, tatsoi, komatsuna — excellent fall crops [PRO-06]
- Cilantro: finally won't bolt in cooling temperatures [PRO-01]
- Turnips: direct-sow for fall harvest [EXT-02]
- Arugula: direct-sow; fast-growing, cold-tolerant [PRO-06]
- Mache (corn salad): sow for winter harvest; extremely cold-hardy [PRO-01]
- Claytonia (miner's lettuce): sow for winter; native PNW green [PRO-01]

**Plant (outdoors, transplant):**
- Fall brassica transplants: broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage (started in July) [EXT-02]
- Kale and collard transplants [PRO-06]
- Overwintering onion sets (Walla Walla type for spring harvest) [EXT-04]

**Harvest:**
- Tomatoes (peak production, all zones) [PRO-07]
- Peppers: pick green or wait for color change [EXT-04]
- Corn (zone 8b+): check silks — ripe when they brown and dry [EXT-02]
- Beans: peak harvest; pick every 2–3 days [PRO-01]
- Cucumbers: daily harvest continues [EXT-02]
- Winter squash: leave on vine until stem begins to dry and harden [PRO-01]
- Melons (zone 8b–9a, in good years): check for slip (stem separates easily) [EXT-04]
- Blueberries (continue) [PRO-05]
- Peaches, plums, Italian prunes [PRO-05]
- Blackberries (wild and cultivated) [PRO-05]
- Figs (zone 8b+, first crop — breba; main crop September) [PRO-05]

**Maintenance:**
- This is the MOST IMPORTANT planting month — do not let summer harvest distract from fall sowing [PRO-06]
- Keep new sowings moist: seeds need consistent moisture to germinate in August heat [PRO-07]
- Shade new seedlings with row cover or shade cloth if temperatures exceed 85°F [PRO-09]
- Remove spent bean and pea plants; leave roots for nitrogen [PRO-01]
- Begin ripening late tomatoes: top plants (remove growing tips) in late August to redirect energy to existing fruit [PRO-07]
- Continue consistent watering of all crops [EXT-04]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Sow fall crops by August 1–10 (shorter growing window before frost) [EXT-06]
- Zone 8b–9a: Fall sowing window extends to August 20 for quick crops [PRO-06]

### September

**Theme:** Fall harvest begins; last plantings; transition from warm to cool season

**Plant (outdoors):**
- Garlic: purchase seed garlic now for October planting [PRO-06]
- Green manure/cover crops: crimson clover, Austrian winter peas, fava beans, cereal rye [EXT-02]
- Mache, claytonia: last sowing for winter [PRO-01]
- Overwintering spinach (Bloomsdale Long Standing, Space, Renegade): sow for spring harvest [PRO-07]
- Overwintering onion starts (Bridger, Walla Walla) [EXT-04]

**Harvest:**
- Tomatoes: continue picking; green tomatoes before first frost (ripen indoors in paper bag) [PRO-07]
- Winter squash and pumpkins: harvest when rind resists fingernail pressure; cure in sun for 10 days [EXT-02]
- Peppers: harvest all before first frost [EXT-04]
- Dry beans: harvest when pods rattle; dry further indoors [PRO-01]
- Sweet potatoes (zone 8b+): harvest before soil drops below 55°F [EXT-06]
- Fall lettuce, spinach, arugula from August sowings — now producing [PRO-06]
- Fall kale: first leaves ready from August sowings [PRO-01]
- Apples (early and mid-season: Gravenstein, Honeycrisp, Gala) [PRO-05]
- Pears (Bartlett, Bosc) — pick when stem snaps cleanly with lift-and-twist [PRO-05]
- Grapes (zone 8b+) [PRO-05]
- Fall raspberries (everbearing: Heritage, Caroline, Joan J) [PRO-05]

**Maintenance:**
- Pull spent warm-season plants: tomatoes, peppers, beans, squash after final harvest [EXT-02]
- Compost healthy plant debris; bag and discard diseased material (do NOT compost blighted tomatoes) [EXT-05]
- Sow cover crops on empty beds within 1 week of clearing [PRO-01]
- Test soil pH; apply lime for spring adjustment [EXT-04]
- Reduce watering as rains begin [EXT-02]
- Clean and store tomato cages, row cover supports, drip irrigation [PRO-09]
- Inventory and organize seed storage [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: First frost possible late September; have row cover ready [GOV-01]
- Zone 8b–9a: Frost unlikely until late October/November [GOV-01]

---

### October

**Theme:** Garlic planting; final fall harvest push; winterize

**Plant (outdoors):**
- **Garlic: THE October crop.** Plant individual cloves pointed-end-up, 2 inches deep, 6 inches apart [PRO-06]. Mulch with 3–4 inches straw after planting. Hardneck varieties (Music, German Extra Hardy, Chesnok Red) for PNW; softneck (Inchelium Red, Silverskin) for milder areas and braiding [PRO-07]
- Cover crops: last chance for crimson clover and Austrian winter peas [EXT-02]
- Broad beans / fava beans: fall-sow for earliest spring harvest [PRO-01]
- Flower bulbs for spring pollinator support: crocus, alliums [PRO-04]

**Harvest:**
- Late apples: Fuji, Braeburn, Mutsu, Granny Smith [PRO-05]
- Pears: late varieties (Comice, Winter Nelis) [PRO-05]
- Kale (peak quality — cold sweetens flavor via starch-to-sugar conversion) [PRO-01]
- Brussels sprouts: harvest from bottom up; frost improves flavor dramatically [EXT-02]
- Leeks: harvest as needed; extremely frost-hardy [PRO-01]
- Carrots from August sowing — sweet and tender [PRO-06]
- Beets: fall harvest; store in sand or sawdust in root cellar [PRO-01]
- Parsnips: first harvest after frost (need cold to convert starch to sugar) [EXT-02]
- Winter squash from curing storage [PRO-01]
- Fall lettuce under cover [PRO-06]

**Maintenance:**
- Protect fall lettuce and greens with row cover or cold frame [PRO-09]
- Mulch garlic beds, strawberry beds, and perennial herbs [EXT-02]
- Clean up fallen fruit under trees (reduces overwintering disease) [EXT-05]
- Drain and store hoses before hard freeze [PRO-01]
- Sharpen and oil tools for winter storage [EXT-02]
- Compost fallen leaves — excellent carbon source [PRO-01]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: First hard frost (28°F) expected mid-to-late October [GOV-01]
- Zone 8b–9a: Many crops still growing uncovered through October [EXT-02]

---

### November

**Theme:** Cold-season harvest; protection; dormant-season work begins

**Plant (outdoors):**
- Garlic: still plantable through mid-November in zone 8b+ [PRO-06]
- Bare-root trees and shrubs as they become available at nurseries [PRO-05]
- Fava beans: last planting opportunity [PRO-01]
- Tulips, daffodils (need cold period) [PRO-04]

**Harvest:**
- Kale: harvest outer leaves, leaving crown to continue producing [PRO-01]
- Brussels sprouts: peak harvest after multiple frosts [EXT-02]
- Leeks: dig as needed through winter [PRO-01]
- Carrots: mulch heavily and harvest through winter; or dig and store [PRO-06]
- Parsnips: prime quality now [EXT-02]
- Celeriac: harvest; stores well [PRO-01]
- Winter lettuce and greens under cover [PRO-09]
- Late fall raspberries (primocane varieties produce until hard frost) [PRO-05]
- Hardy herbs: rosemary, thyme, sage, oregano (harvest moderately — don't stress before winter) [PRO-01]

**Maintenance:**
- Protect cold-sensitive plants: cover rosemary, fig trees, artichokes [EXT-02]
- Check cold frames: vent on sunny days to prevent overheating (above 70°F damages winter greens) [PRO-09]
- Continue slug control — slugs remain active in mild, wet weather [EXT-05]
- Leaf mulch: shred and apply 3–4 inches on empty beds and around perennials [PRO-01]
- Check drainage: address any waterlogging before ground saturates fully [EXT-04]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Hard freeze likely; cold frame essential for continued harvest [GOV-01]
- Zone 8b–9a: Light frosts sweeten brassicas; many crops continue uncovered [EXT-02]

---

### December

**Theme:** Winter harvest, reflection, next-season planning

**Plant:**
- Very little active planting. In zone 8b–9a, bare-root trees continue [PRO-05]
- Indoor: microgreens and sprouting on windowsill for fresh winter greens [PRO-09]

**Harvest:**
- Kale, collards: continue harvest; most productive winter vegetable [PRO-01]
- Brussels sprouts: continue [EXT-02]
- Leeks: cold-hardy through winter; harvest as needed [PRO-01]
- Carrots, parsnips, beets under mulch or in ground [PRO-06]
- Celeriac from storage [PRO-01]
- Winter squash from storage (check regularly for soft spots) [EXT-02]
- Cold frame: mache, claytonia, winter lettuce, spinach [PRO-09]
- Stored garlic, onions, potatoes from summer harvest [PRO-01]
- Rosemary (evergreen; harvest moderately) [PRO-01]

**Maintenance:**
- Review garden journal: document what worked, what failed, variety performance [PRO-01]
- Plan next year's garden: crop rotation, new varieties, bed layout [EXT-02]
- Order seed catalogs; Territorial Seed, Adaptive Seeds, Uprising Seeds, St. Clare all ship PNW-adapted varieties [PRO-07]
- Winterize: check cold frame seals, row cover for tears, greenhouse heater function [PRO-09]
- Sharpen pruning tools for January fruit tree pruning [PRO-05]
- Monitor stored crops: remove any showing decay [PRO-01]
- Browse extension publications for updated variety trial results [EXT-06]

**Zone notes:**
- Zone 7b: Coldest month; protect all overwintering crops [GOV-01]
- All zones: December 21 is winter solstice (8.5 hrs daylight). Minimal plant growth occurs below 10 hrs daylight regardless of temperature — this is why August planting is critical [PRO-06]

## 3. Top Vegetable Profiles

### Cool-Season Stars

These crops are the backbone of PNW food production. They thrive in the conditions that frustrate warm-season crops: cool temperatures, moderate moisture, and long day lengths during spring/fall [PRO-01].

### Warm-Season Crops (Require Strategy)

These crops need deliberate management to succeed in the PNW's cool maritime climate. They're not impossible — but they reward the gardener who understands variety selection, microclimate exploitation, and season extension [EXT-04].

#### Tomatoes

**The most-desired and most-challenging PNW warm-season crop.** Success depends entirely on variety selection and microclimate management [PRO-07].

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Type** | Warm-season annual (perennial in tropics) |
| **Varieties — CRITICAL for PNW** | **Must-grow short-season:** Stupice (52 days, Czech heirloom, sets fruit at 50°F nights — THE PNW tomato), Sungold (57 days, cherry, insanely productive), Siletz (52 days, OSU-bred determinate slicer), Early Girl (50 days, reliable), Glacier (55 days, sets fruit in cold). **Good mid-season (zone 8b+):** Legend (OSU-bred, late blight tolerant, 68 days), Defiant (late blight resistant, 70 days), Oregon Spring (58 days, seedless in cool weather — parthenocarpic). **Risky but possible (zone 8b–9a, warm years):** Brandywine (80+ days), Cherokee Purple (80 days), San Marzano (78 days) [PRO-07, EXT-06] |
| **Sowing** | Start indoors 6–8 weeks before transplant date. Bottom heat (75–85°F) for germination [EXT-04] |
| **Timing** | Start indoors: March 1–15. Transplant: May 15–June 1 (zone 8a–8b), May 1–15 (zone 8b–9a with protection), June 1–10 (zone 7b) [EXT-02] |
| **Spacing** | Determinate: 24 inches. Indeterminate: 30–36 inches. Stake or cage ALL plants [PRO-01] |
| **DTM** | 50–80+ days from transplant (variety-dependent) [PRO-07] |

| **Harvest** | Pick at first blush of color and ripen indoors for best flavor and to avoid cracking/pests [PRO-07] |

**PNW Tomato Success Formula:**
1. Choose short-season varieties (sub-65 DTM) [PRO-07]
2. Plant in warmest microclimate: south-facing wall, raised bed, dark-colored containers [PRO-09]
3. Use black plastic mulch to warm soil (+5–10°F) [EXT-04]
4. Use Wall-o-Water for transplanting 2–3 weeks early [PRO-09]
5. Prune indeterminate varieties to 1–2 leaders; remove all suckers below first flower cluster [PRO-07]
6. Stop watering in September to force ripening [PRO-09]
7. Top plants (remove growing tips) in late August — redirect energy to existing fruit [PRO-07]
8. Before first frost: pick all fruit including green; ripen indoors in paper bag with banana [EXT-02]

## 4. Succession Planting

### What & Why

Succession planting is the practice of sowing the same crop at regular intervals to maintain continuous harvest rather than a single glut followed by nothing [PRO-06]. In the PNW, where the growing season spans 8–12 months depending on crop type, succession planting is the difference between "we have too much lettuce this week and none next month" and steady production from March through December [PRO-01].

### Which Crops to Succession Plant

**Best candidates (quick DTM, eat fresh, don't store):**
- Lettuce: every 2–3 weeks, March–May and August–September [PRO-06]
- Radishes: every 2 weeks, March–May and August–September [EXT-02]
- Spinach: every 3 weeks, March–April and August–September [PRO-06]
- Arugula: every 2–3 weeks, March–September [PRO-01]
- Bush beans: every 2–3 weeks, May 15–July 15 [EXT-02]
- Cilantro: every 3 weeks, March–May and September–October (bolts fast) [PRO-01]
- Dill: every 3 weeks, April–July [PRO-06]
- Scallions/green onions: every 3 weeks, March–September [EXT-02]

**Not worth succession planting (long DTM, single harvest, or stores well):**
- Tomatoes, peppers, squash (one planting, long harvest window)
- Garlic, onions, potatoes (single planting, store for months)
- Broccoli, cauliflower (succession via spring + fall plantings rather than weekly sowings)

### Relay Planting

Relay planting is planting a second crop in the same space as a declining first crop [PRO-06]. Examples:

- **Peas to Beans:** As spring peas finish in June, pull vines and direct-sow bush beans in same trellised row [PRO-01]
- **Spring lettuce to Fall kale:** Lettuce bolts in June; pull and transplant kale starts into same bed [PRO-06]
- **Early potatoes to Fall brassicas:** Dig early potatoes in June–July; transplant fall broccoli/cabbage immediately [EXT-02]
- **Garlic to Fall lettuce:** Harvest garlic in July; sow fall lettuce in same bed in August [PRO-06]

## 5. The "Second Spring" (August)

### PNW's Secret Weapon

The August planting window — roughly July 15 to August 20 — is the single most underused period in PNW food gardening [PRO-06]. While gardeners in most of North America are winding down, PNW gardeners should be ramping up. The mild fall (September–November), gentle first frosts (late October–December in zone 8), and moderate winter temperatures allow crops sown in August to produce from October through March [PRO-07].

### Why Fall Crops Outperform Spring

1. **Fewer pests.** Cabbage root maggot, aphids, and flea beetles have completed their life cycles. Fall brassicas suffer dramatically fewer insect problems than spring plantings [EXT-05].
2. **Less bolting.** Spinach, lettuce, and radishes bolt in response to lengthening days. In fall, shortening days suppress bolting, allowing longer harvest windows [PRO-06].
3. **Better flavor.** Frost converts starch to sugar in root crops (carrots, parsnips, beets) and brassicas (kale, Brussels sprouts). Fall-harvested kale is sweeter than spring kale by a significant margin [PRO-01].
4. **Less watering.** Fall rains begin in September–October, reducing irrigation needs [EXT-07].
5. **Less weeding.** Weed germination slows as days shorten and temperatures cool [PRO-01].
6. **Soil is warm.** August soil temperatures are 65–70°F — perfect for rapid germination. Spring soil may be 40–50°F, slowing germination dramatically [EXT-06].

### Second-Spring Sowing Dates by Zone

| Lettuce | Jul 20–Aug 10 | Jul 25–Aug 15 | Aug 1–20 | Aug 5–25 | Succession every 10 days |
| Spinach | Aug 1–15 | Aug 5–20 | Aug 10–25 | Aug 15–Sep 1 | Pre-chill seed |
| Carrots | Jul 1–15 | Jul 5–20 | Jul 10–25 | Jul 15–Aug 1 | Need 70+ days before short days |

| Broccoli (transplant) | Jul 15–Aug 1 | Jul 20–Aug 5 | Aug 1–10 | Aug 5–15 | Start seed 6 wks earlier |

### Critical Success Factors

1. **Germination moisture:** August soil is dry. Water furrows before sowing, keep moist until germination. For spinach, cover seeded row with a board for 3 days to maintain moisture and reduce soil temperature [PRO-07].
2. **Shade new seedlings:** If temperatures exceed 85°F, drape 30% shade cloth or floating row cover over seedlings [PRO-09].
3. **Have starts ready:** For transplant crops (brassicas), start seeds indoors or in nursery beds July 1–15 for transplanting in August [PRO-06].
4. **Don't wait:** Every day of delay past the ideal window means 5–7 fewer days of fall harvest. A week late in August costs more production than a week late in April [PRO-06].

## 6. Raised Bed Strategy

### Near-Essential for PNW Food Production

Raised beds are not merely a convenience in the maritime PNW — they solve the region's two most persistent food-growing challenges: poor drainage and cold soil [PRO-09]. While in-ground gardening is certainly possible (and preferred for some crops like potatoes and corn), the majority of PNW vegetable gardens benefit substantially from raised beds [EXT-04].

### Drainage Advantage

PNW soils — particularly the glacial till of the Puget Sound lowlands and the Willamette silt loam of the valley floor — become waterlogged from October through May [EXT-04]. Clay content ranges from 25–60% in many residential soils [GOV-04]. Raised beds lift the root zone above the water table, preventing the root rot, crown rot, and anaerobic soil conditions that kill overwintering crops and stunt spring growth [PRO-09].

A 12-inch raised bed effectively transforms a zone 8a garden into a zone 8b microclimate for drainage purposes [PRO-01]. This alone extends the growing season by 2–3 weeks in spring and maintains healthier roots through winter for overwintering crops.

### Soil Temperature Advantage

Raised beds warm 2–4 weeks faster than in-ground soil in spring [EXT-04]. In a region where every degree of soil warmth translates to earlier planting:

| Condition | In-Ground 50°F Date | Raised Bed 50°F Date | Advantage |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Zone 7b | Late April | Early-Mid April | 10–14 days [EXT-06] |
| Zone 8a | Mid April | Late March–Early April | 14–21 days [EXT-04] |
| Zone 8b–9a | Early April | Mid-Late March | 14–21 days [EXT-02] |

This 2–3 week advantage is the difference between planting peas in February versus March, or transplanting tomatoes in early May versus late May [PRO-09].

### Construction Recommendations

**Height:** 12 inches minimum for annual vegetables; 18–24 inches for root crops (carrots, parsnips) and to maximize drainage advantage [PRO-09].

**Width:** 3–4 feet maximum (reach center from either side without stepping in); any length [EXT-04].

**Materials:** Untreated cedar or Douglas fir (PNW native, naturally rot-resistant, 8–15 year lifespan). Galvanized steel (permanent, modern aesthetic). Avoid CCA-treated lumber near food crops. Avoid railroad ties (creosote) [PRO-09].

**Fill mix (PNW-specific):** 60% quality topsoil, 30% compost (leaf mold, aged bark, or municipal compost), 10% perlite or coarse sand for drainage [PRO-01]. Many PNW garden centers sell "raised bed mix" that meets this ratio. Add lime at time of filling if soil test indicates pH below 6.0 [EXT-04].

**Orientation:** North-south alignment for even sun exposure. Against south-facing walls for warm-season crops (reflected heat) [PRO-09].

### Irrigation

Drip irrigation or soaker hoses are ideal for raised beds: deliver water at root level, reduce foliar disease (critical in wet PNW), and conserve water during summer drought [EXT-04]. Timer-controlled systems ensure consistent moisture — especially important during July–August when PNW gardeners vacation and rainfall is near-zero [PRO-09].

## 7. Season Extension

The PNW's mild winter makes season extension exceptionally cost-effective: a modest investment in structures can extend the harvest season to 10–12 months [PRO-09]. Unlike continental climates where heating costs make winter growing prohibitive, PNW gardeners only need to moderate temperature by 5–15°F to keep crops alive through the darkest months [PRO-01].

### Cold Frames

**Effect:** +4–6 weeks on both ends of the season; winter harvest of cold-hardy greens [PRO-09].

**Construction:** Bottomless box with a transparent lid. South-facing slope (lid angled toward sun). Old windows, clear polycarbonate, or greenhouse film. Size: 4x4 feet fits over standard raised bed [PRO-01].

**Temperature management:** Open lid when interior exceeds 70°F (surprisingly often, even in December–February during sunny breaks). Close by 3 PM to trap afternoon heat. A simple prop stick or automatic vent opener ($25–35) prevents overheating [PRO-09].

**Best crops:** Winter lettuce (Winter Density, North Pole), mache, claytonia, spinach, arugula, Asian greens. Sow September for November–March harvest [PRO-06].

**PNW-specific note:** Cold frames in the PNW primarily protect against rain and wind rather than extreme cold. Keeping foliage dry prevents the fungal diseases that are the real winter crop killer [PRO-01].

### Low Tunnels (Caterpillar Tunnels)

**Effect:** +2–4 weeks; frost protection for fall crops; warmer soil for spring starts [PRO-09].

**Construction:** Wire hoops (9-gauge wire or PVC) every 4 feet along bed, covered with floating row cover (frost protection) or clear plastic (heat gain). Anchor edges with sandbags or clips [PRO-01].

**Advantages over cold frames:** Cheaper, more flexible, can cover entire bed lengths. Easy to remove for access. Scale to any bed size [PRO-09].

**Best use:** Protect fall-planted lettuce, spinach, and brassicas from October through March. In spring, warm soil 2 weeks before uncovered beds [PRO-06].

### Greenhouses

**Effect:** 8–9 months of production (March–November with minimal heat); year-round with modest heating [PRO-09].

**Types:** Lean-to (against south wall — uses thermal mass of house), hoop house (polyethylene over metal bows — cheapest per square foot), rigid polycarbonate (twin-wall, most durable, best insulation) [PRO-01].

**PNW sizing:** Even a 6x8-foot greenhouse transforms tomato, pepper, cucumber, and eggplant production. Temperatures inside are 15–25°F warmer than outside on sunny days [PRO-09].

**Ventilation critical:** PNW greenhouses overheat more easily than gardeners expect. Roof vents, side vents, or a thermostatically controlled exhaust fan prevent temperatures above 90°F that stress plants [PRO-01].

**Winter production:** Unheated PNW greenhouse maintains 35–45°F on most winter nights (zone 8a+) — sufficient for cold-hardy greens. A small electric heater ($30–50/month) maintains 45°F minimum for year-round lettuce and herb production [PRO-09].

### Row Cover (Floating)

**Types and uses:**
- **Lightweight (0.5 oz/sq yd):** Insect barrier only; 1–2°F frost protection. Use over brassicas from transplant day to prevent cabbage root maggot and cabbageworm [EXT-05].
- **Medium (1.0 oz/sq yd):** 4–6°F frost protection; good light transmission. Use for spring warm-up and fall frost protection [PRO-09].
- **Heavy (1.5–2.0 oz/sq yd):** 6–8°F frost protection; reduced light. Use for overwintering crops in zone 7b [PRO-01].

### Wall-o-Water and Season Starters

Water-filled plastic tepees placed around individual warm-season transplants. Water absorbs solar heat during the day and releases it at night, maintaining 50–55°F minimum around the plant [PRO-09].

**Effect:** Allows tomato transplanting 2–3 weeks before last frost date [EXT-02]. In PNW terms: transplant late April instead of mid-May in zone 8b.

**Limitation:** Only practical for tomatoes, peppers, and eggplant — not cost-effective for direct-seeded crops [PRO-09].

### Black Plastic Mulch

**Effect:** Raises soil temperature 5–10°F; suppresses weeds; conserves moisture [EXT-04].

**Best use:** Lay 2–3 weeks before transplanting warm-season crops. Cut X-shaped slits for transplants. Use drip irrigation underneath [PRO-09].

**Downside:** Must be removed at end of season; not compostable (use landscape fabric as reusable alternative). Plastic creates microplastic risk over time [PRO-01].

## 8. Crop Rotation

### Why It Matters More in the PNW

Crop rotation — moving plant families to different beds each season — is important everywhere, but the PNW's wet climate amplifies the consequences of skipping it [EXT-05]. Soil-borne diseases thrive in wet, cool conditions:

- **Clubroot** (Plasmodiophora brassicae): Infects brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, kale, cauliflower). Spores survive in soil 15–20 years. Once established, the affected bed cannot grow brassicas for a generation. Prevented by rotation (minimum 3 years between brassicas in same bed) and liming to pH 7.0+ [EXT-05].
- **White rot** (Sclerotium cepivorum): Infects alliums (garlic, onions, leeks). Spores survive 15+ years. No chemical control. Rotation is the only management [EXT-05].
- **Verticillium wilt:** Broad host range including tomatoes, peppers, potatoes, strawberries. Survives indefinitely in soil. Rotation reduces inoculum levels [EXT-05].
- **Late blight** (Phytophthora infestans): Thrives in PNW's cool, wet conditions. Infects potatoes and tomatoes. Do not follow potatoes with tomatoes or vice versa [EXT-05].

### 4-Bed Rotation Plan

A simple, effective rotation for PNW food gardens:

| Year | Bed 1 | Bed 2 | Bed 3 | Bed 4 |
|------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **Legumes** (peas, beans, favas) + cover crop | **Brassicas** (broccoli, kale, cabbage, cauliflower) | **Solanaceae + Cucurbits** (tomatoes, peppers, squash, cucumbers) | **Roots + Alliums** (carrots, beets, garlic, onions) |
| 2 | Brassicas | Solanaceae + Cucurbits | Roots + Alliums | Legumes + cover crop |
| 3 | Solanaceae + Cucurbits | Roots + Alliums | Legumes + cover crop | Brassicas |
| 4 | Roots + Alliums | Legumes + cover crop | Brassicas | Solanaceae + Cucurbits |

[PRO-01, EXT-04]

**Key principles:**
1. **Legumes precede heavy feeders.** Peas and beans fix nitrogen; follow with nitrogen-hungry brassicas [PRO-01].
2. **Brassicas precede roots.** Brassicas benefit from lime (pH 7.0+); roots prefer slightly acid soil (6.0–6.5). Lime applied for brassicas will naturally reduce by the time roots are planted [EXT-04].
3. **Never follow solanaceae with solanaceae.** Tomatoes to potatoes to peppers is asking for disease buildup [EXT-05].
4. **3-year minimum for brassicas in the same spot.** The PNW clubroot spore cycle demands this as an absolute minimum [EXT-05].
5. **Garlic is a special case.** Its 9-month occupancy means it often stays in one bed for a full cycle. Accept this and rotate vigorously around it [PRO-06].

### Cover Crops in Rotation

Cover crops are the "reset button" in PNW crop rotation [PRO-01]:

| Cover Crop | When to Sow | When to Turn In | Benefit |
|------------|------------|-----------------|---------|
| Crimson clover | September–October | March–April | Nitrogen fixation (60–150 lbs N/acre) [EXT-02] |
| Austrian winter peas | September–October | March | Nitrogen fixation + biomass |
| Cereal rye | September–November | March–April (before heading) | Weed suppression, erosion control, massive biomass [EXT-02] |
| Fava beans | October–February | April–May | Nitrogen fixation + edible harvest possible [PRO-01] |
| Buckwheat | May–August (summer) | Before flowering seeds mature (30–40 days) | Pollinator support, phosphorus mining, quick biomass [PRO-01] |
| Phacelia | April–August | Before seed set | Exceptional pollinator plant, breaks pest cycles [PRO-06] |

## 9. Fruit in the PNW

The maritime PNW is exceptional fruit-growing territory — particularly for berries, apples, and pears. Some crops (blueberries, raspberries) are so well-adapted they seem almost native. Others (figs, hardy kiwi) are increasingly viable as the climate warms [PRO-05].

### Blueberries

**PNW's natural advantage fruit.** Blueberries require acid soil (pH 4.5–5.5) — exactly what PNW gardens naturally provide [PRO-05]. Most North American gardeners must amend soil to grow blueberries; PNW gardeners often have to amend soil to grow everything else.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Varieties** | Early: Duke, Spartan. Mid: Bluecrop (industry standard, reliable), Blueray, Toro. Late: Elliott, Aurora. Evergreen (ornamental + edible): Sunshine Blue (compact, semi-evergreen, self-pollinating) [PRO-05] |
| **Planting** | Plant at least 2 varieties for cross-pollination (increases berry size and yield by 30–50%). Space 4–6 feet apart. Acidify soil with sulfur if pH above 5.5 [PRO-05] |
| **Soil** | pH 4.5–5.5 (test!). Amend with peat moss, sulfur, or cottonseed meal if needed. Mulch with 4–6 inches acidic material: bark, sawdust, pine needles [PRO-05] |
| **Harvest** | June–September (variety-dependent). Pick when berries turn fully blue AND detach easily. Immature berries are tart [PRO-05] |
| **Yield** | 5–10 lbs per mature bush per year [PRO-05] |
| **PNW problems** | Bird predation (netting essential at berry set); mummy berry disease (prune out infected berries, clean up fallen berries); spotted wing drosophila (SWD — emerging PNW pest, harvest promptly) [EXT-05] |
| **Pro tip** | Blueberry bushes produce for 50+ years. Plant once, harvest for a lifetime. They're also beautiful ornamentals — red fall foliage, white spring flowers, blue summer fruit [PRO-05] |

### Strawberries

## 11. Cross-Module Connections

This food production module integrates with all other modules in the PNW Gardening research series:

### M1: Climate & Microclimates (01-climate-microclimates.md)

- **Frost dates** directly determine warm-season transplant timing (Section 2). Zone 7b last frost: May 1–15; zone 8b: April 15–30; zone 9a: March 30–April 15 [GOV-01]
- **Microclimate exploitation** is essential for warm-season success: south-facing walls, thermal mass, wind protection, and cold-air drainage patterns determine whether tomatoes and peppers succeed or fail [EXT-07]
- **Heat unit accumulation** explains why identical varieties perform differently across the PNW corridor — Portland (zone 8b) accumulates 30–50% more growing degree days than Bellingham (zone 7b–8a) in a typical year [EXT-06]
- **Climate projections** suggest warming trends will gradually expand warm-season options in zone 7b–8a areas over coming decades [EXT-07]

### M2: Soil Management (02-soil-management.md)

- **Soil preparation** precedes all planting: pH adjustment (lime for brassicas, sulfur for blueberries), organic matter building, drainage improvement [EXT-04]
- **Raised bed fill** composition (Section 6) depends on understanding native soil characteristics: clay content, drainage class, organic matter percentage [GOV-04]
- **Fertility management** differs by crop family: nitrogen-fixing legumes feed subsequent brassicas; potatoes prefer lower pH than most vegetables; garlic needs well-drained soil with moderate fertility [PRO-01]
- **Cover crops** (Section 8) are both a rotation tool and a soil-building tool — crimson clover fixes nitrogen while building organic matter [EXT-02]

### M3: Native Plants & Habitat (03-native-plants.md)

- **Pollinator support** is critical for fruit set in beans, squash, cucumbers, tomatoes, blueberries, apples, and pears [PRO-04]
- **Native plant hedgerows** adjacent to food gardens provide beneficial insect habitat: lacewings, hoverflies, parasitic wasps, and native bees that control aphids, caterpillars, and other pests [PRO-04]
- **Companion planting** with native species: Oregon grape (Mahonia) attracts early pollinators for spring fruit tree bloom; native asters provide late-season pollinator forage when fall crops need pollination [PRO-02]
- **Claytonia** (miner's lettuce) — listed in both M3 and M4 — is a native PNW plant used as winter salad green [PRO-01]

### M5: Pest & Disease Management (05-pest-disease-adaptation.md)

- **IPM strategies** directly referenced in crop profiles: carrot rust fly management, clubroot prevention, late blight response, slug control [EXT-05]
- **Disease-resistant variety selection** is the first line of defense in PNW's wet climate: Legend tomatoes, Liberty apples, Cascade Delight raspberries [PRO-05, PRO-07]
- **Crop rotation** (Section 8) is fundamentally a disease management strategy — 3-year brassica rotation prevents clubroot; allium rotation prevents white rot [EXT-05]
- **Season extension structures** (Section 7) serve dual purpose: cold frames and row cover protect against both frost AND the persistent moisture that drives fungal disease [PRO-01]

### Safety Compliance

**SC-NUM (Numerical Claims):** All planting dates reference specific USDA zones and cite extension or professional sources. Temperature thresholds (soil temperature for germination, frost tolerance limits) are sourced from peer-reviewed extension publications (EXT-02, EXT-04, EXT-06). Days-to-maturity figures are sourced from variety trial data (EXT-06) and seed catalog documentation (PRO-07). Spacing recommendations follow extension guidelines (EXT-02, EXT-04). No numerical claims are unsourced.

**SC-MED (Medical/Health Claims):** This module makes no medical, health, or nutritional claims. Nutritional references (e.g., "beet greens have more nutrition than the root") are general knowledge statements sourced from extension publications (PRO-01) and do not constitute medical advice. No therapeutic claims are made for any food crop.

**SC-INV (Invasive Species):** All recommended plant species are either: (a) domesticated food crops with no invasive potential in PNW ecosystems, (b) native PNW species (claytonia), or (c) established horticultural varieties with decades of PNW cultivation history. No species listed on the Oregon or Washington noxious weed lists are recommended. Cover crop species (crimson clover, Austrian winter peas, cereal rye, buckwheat, phacelia) are annual or non-persistent and do not pose establishment risk in managed garden settings [EXT-02].

**SC-ADV (Advisory Scope):** All pest and disease management recommendations reference IPM (Integrated Pest Management) principles from the Pacific Northwest Pest Management Handbooks (EXT-05). Chemical recommendations are limited to organic-approved products (Bt, iron phosphate, sulfur, copper, kaolin clay). No synthetic pesticide recommendations are made. Soil amendment recommendations (lime, sulfur, compost) follow extension soil test protocols (EXT-04). All advice is general in nature; site-specific conditions may require consultation with local extension agents.
