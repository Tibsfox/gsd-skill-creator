## 2. Citation Format

All modules use a uniform citation system to ensure traceability.

### Inline Citation

Use bracketed source IDs within the text:

> The USDA 2023 zone map places most of Portland in zone 9a [GOV-01], a half-zone increase from the previous 2012 revision.

Multiple sources:

> Maritime PNW soils are typically acidic, ranging from pH 5.0 to 6.5 [GOV-04, EXT-01].

### Source Bibliography (per module)

Each module ends with a bibliography listing every source cited in that module. Format:

```
## Source Bibliography

- **[GOV-01]** USDA Plant Hardiness Zone Map (2023). Oregon State PRISM Climate Group.
  https://planthardiness.ars.usda.gov/
- **[EXT-04]** WSU Extension EM051E: Home Vegetable Gardening in Washington.
  https://pubs.extension.wsu.edu/home-vegetable-gardening-in-washington
```

### Rules

1. Every temperature, pH value, zone number, species count, or date range must cite at least one source.
2. When two sources disagree, present both with their IDs and note the discrepancy.
3. Sources not in the index above must not be used. If a module author identifies a new authoritative source, it must first be added to this foundation document with a new ID.

## 3. Shared Glossary

Terms used across multiple modules. Definitions are region-specific where applicable.

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Hardiness zone (USDA)** | Geographic area defined by average annual minimum winter temperature, in 10°F bands (e.g., zone 8b = 15–20°F). Based on 30-year averages from PRISM climate data [GOV-01]. |
| **Sunset zone** | Climate classification developed by Sunset Western Garden Book that incorporates winter lows, summer highs, humidity, rainfall patterns, and microclimates. Maritime PNW falls in Sunset zones 4–6 [EXT-03]. |
| **AHS Heat Zone** | American Horticultural Society classification based on average number of days per year above 86°F (30°C). Complements USDA hardiness zones by capturing summer heat stress. |
| **Microclimate** | Localized climate conditions within a small area that differ from the surrounding region — caused by slope aspect, proximity to water, urban heat islands, wind exposure, or canopy cover [EXT-01]. |
| **Maritime-Mediterranean climate** | Climate pattern characterized by cool, wet winters (October–April) and warm, dry summers (June–September) with moderate year-round temperatures moderated by Pacific Ocean influence [GOV-03, EXT-07]. |
| **No-till** | Soil management practice that avoids mechanical disturbance of the soil profile, preserving soil structure, mycorrhizal networks, and microbial communities [PRO-01]. |
| **Sheet mulching** | Layered mulching technique using cardboard or newspaper topped with compost and organic matter to suppress weeds and build soil without tilling [PRO-01, PRO-09]. |
| **Cover crop** | Plants grown primarily to protect and enrich bare soil between cash crop cycles — common maritime PNW covers include crimson clover, winter rye, and fava beans [EXT-04, PRO-06]. |
| **IPM (Integrated Pest Management)** | Decision-based pest control strategy that combines cultural, biological, mechanical, and chemical methods, applying the least-toxic effective option first [EXT-05]. |
| **Beneficial insects** | Insects that provide ecosystem services in the garden — pollinators (native bees, syrphid flies), predators (lacewings, lady beetles), and parasitoids (Trichogramma wasps) [PRO-04, EXT-05]. |
| **Mycorrhizal network** | Symbiotic fungal associations with plant roots that extend the root system's effective reach, improving nutrient and water uptake. Dominant types in PNW: ectomycorrhizal (conifers) and arbuscular (most vegetables) [EXT-01]. |
| **Cold-air drainage** | Gravitational flow of dense cold air downslope into valleys and basins, creating frost pockets where temperatures may be 5–15°F colder than adjacent hillsides [GOV-01, EXT-07]. |
| **Heat accumulation** | Cumulative measure of warmth available for plant growth, typically expressed as Growing Degree Days (GDD) above a base temperature (usually 50°F for cool-season crops) [EXT-06]. |
| **Frost date** | Statistical date by which the probability of frost (32°F or below) drops below a given threshold. Maritime PNW last frost: typically March 15–April 15; first frost: October 15–November 15, varying by microclimate [GOV-01, EXT-02]. |
| **Succession planting** | Staggering planting dates of the same crop at 2–3 week intervals to extend the harvest window through the growing season [PRO-01, PRO-06]. |
| **Season extension** | Techniques that lengthen the growing season beyond natural frost dates — includes row covers, cold frames, cloches, hoop houses, and heated greenhouses [EXT-04, PRO-09]. |

## 5. Safety Boundaries

These safety codes apply to every module. Each module must self-audit against all seven codes in its Safety Compliance section. Violations block publication.

| Code | Name | Rule | Verification |
|------|------|------|-------------|
| **SC-SRC** | Source Quality | Every factual claim must cite a source from the foundation index (GOV-XX, EXT-XX, or PRO-XX). No blogs, forums, social media, or unverified websites. | Grep all `[` citations; confirm each ID exists in §1. |
| **SC-NUM** | Numerical Attribution | Every temperature, pH, zone number, species count, date range, measurement, or percentage must be attributed to a specific source with inline citation. | Scan for bare numbers; each must have `[XX-NN]` within the same sentence or table row. |
| **SC-ADV** | No Policy Advocacy | Present evidence-based information without advocating for specific government policies, regulations, or political positions. | Review for imperative statements about policy; flag "should be required," "the government must," etc. |
| **SC-CLI** | Climate Projections | Climate change projections, temperature forecasts, and precipitation trend data may only be sourced from USDA, NOAA, EPA, IPCC, or university climate research groups (EXT-07). | Verify every forward-looking climate claim cites an approved agency. |
| **SC-INV** | Invasive Species | No module may recommend planting, propagating, or encouraging any species listed on the Oregon or Washington state invasive species lists. Native alternatives must be suggested instead. | Cross-reference every recommended species against OR/WA invasive lists. |
| **SC-MED** | Medical/Health Claims | No unattributed health, medicinal, or nutritional claims for edible or medicinal plants. Any health-related statement must cite a peer-reviewed or extension source. | Flag health language ("cures," "treats," "prevents," "boosts immunity") — require citation or remove. |
| **SC-PST** | Pesticide Safety | Every pest management recommendation must state: (1) whether the product/method is OMRI-listed or organic-approved, (2) known non-target impacts (pollinators, aquatic life, soil biology). | Each pesticide/fungicide/herbicide mention must include organic status and non-target note. |

### Violation Handling

- **Pre-publication:** Module author corrects and re-audits.
- **Dispute:** If a safety code conflicts with accurate information (e.g., a legitimate source not in the index), escalate to foundation update — add the source with a new ID, then cite it.
- **Severity:** SC-INV and SC-MED violations are blocking. All others are blocking but may have documented exceptions.

## 6. Study Region

### Geographic Extent

The study region encompasses the **maritime Pacific Northwest west of the Cascade crest**, from Vancouver, British Columbia south through the Umpqua Valley near Roseburg, Oregon.

**Northern boundary:** Greater Vancouver and Fraser Valley, BC
**Eastern boundary:** Cascade Range crest (approximate, follows the rain shadow transition)
**Southern boundary:** Umpqua Valley near Roseburg, OR (southern limit of reliable maritime influence)
**Western boundary:** Pacific coastline

### Key Sub-Regions (north to south)

| Sub-Region | Approximate Extent | USDA Zone | Sunset Zone | Character |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Fraser Valley / Metro Vancouver | Vancouver BC south to Bellingham WA | 8a–8b | 4–5 | Mild winters, moderate summer heat, heavy winter rain |
| Puget Sound Lowlands | Bellingham south through Seattle to Olympia | 8a–8b | 4–5 | Protected marine air, rain shadow pockets (San Juans), reliable moisture |
| SW Washington / Lower Columbia | Olympia south to Portland metro | 8b | 5–6 | Transition zone, slightly warmer summers, Gorge wind influence |
| Portland Metro / N. Willamette Valley | Portland south to Salem | 8b–9a | 5–6 | Warmest urban zones, heat island effect, well-drained valley soils |
| Central Willamette Valley | Salem south to Eugene | 8b | 5–6 | Agricultural heartland, heavier clay soils, reliable summer irrigation needed |
| Southern Willamette / Umpqua Valley | Eugene south to Roseburg | 8b–9a | 5–6 | Warmer summers, reduced maritime influence, transition to interior climate |

### Climate Profile

- **Climate type:** Maritime-Mediterranean [GOV-03]
- **Wet season:** October through April — 70–80% of annual precipitation falls in this window [EXT-07]
- **Dry season:** June through September — prolonged drought stress is normal and expected [GOV-02]
- **Annual rainfall:** 36–60 inches in lowlands; higher on west-facing slopes [GOV-04]
- **Summer highs:** Typically 75–85°F; periodic heat domes can push 100°F+ (increasing frequency per EXT-07)
- **Winter lows:** Typically 25–35°F; rare Arctic outflow events can bring single digits to teens [GOV-01]
- **Growing season:** 180–250 frost-free days depending on sub-region and microclimate [EXT-02]

### USDA Hardiness Zones (2023 Map)

The 2023 USDA revision shifted much of the maritime PNW approximately half a zone warmer compared to the 2012 map [GOV-01].

| Zone | Avg Annual Min (°F) | Where in Study Region |
|------|---------------------|----------------------|
| 7b | 5 to 10 | Higher-elevation edges near Cascade foothills, exposed inland valleys |
| 8a | 10 to 15 | Northern Puget Sound, Fraser Valley, exposed coastal areas |
| 8b | 15 to 20 | Most of the Puget Sound lowlands, Willamette Valley floor, SW Washington |
| 9a | 20 to 25 | Portland urban core, sheltered south-facing sites, southern Willamette/Umpqua |

### Sunset Zones

| Zone | Character |
|------|-----------|
| 4 | Northern Puget Sound and BC — colder winters, shorter growing season, heavier rainfall |
| 5 | Core maritime lowlands — Seattle, Portland, Willamette Valley — mild, moderate, reliable |
| 6 | Southern valleys and thermal belts — warmer summers, longer growing season, drier |

### Elevation Range

**Primary focus:** Sea level to approximately 1,500 feet. Above this elevation, conditions transition toward montane climate with shorter growing seasons, heavier snowfall, and reduced cultivar selection. Foothill gardening (1,000–1,500 ft) is addressed where sub-regional data supports it, but the core audience gardens below 500 feet.
