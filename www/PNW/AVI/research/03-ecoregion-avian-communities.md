# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 3: Ecoregion Avian Communities

> **Module 3 deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document analyzes bird communities within each of the 7 PNW ecoregion zones defined in `00-ecoregion-definitions.md`. For each zone: characteristic species assemblages, indicator species, keystone species, seasonal variation, community composition, elevation gradients, microclimate effects, edge effects, and comparisons between analogous zones. The synthesis section maps cross-zone patterns including species richness gradients, migration corridors, and conservation priority ranking.
>
> **Design principle:** Species do not exist in isolation. They exist in communities shaped by habitat, climate, elevation, seasonality, and interaction with other species. This document maps the communities.

## Zone 1: Alpine and Subalpine

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** >5,000 ft (timberline varies by latitude: 7,700-10,000 ft in Oregon Cascades, 5,500-7,500 ft in Washington Cascades, 4,500-6,000 ft in Olympics)
**Geographic extent:** Cascade Range from Mt. Baker to Mt. McLoughlin, Olympic Mountains, Blue Mountains (Wallowa, Elkhorn, Strawberry ranges), Selkirk Mountains (northeastern WA/northern ID)
**Dominant vegetation:** Alpine meadows, subalpine fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*) / mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*) parkland, krummholz, whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) stands, snowfield margins, talus slopes, alpine scrub
**Climate:** Short growing season (60-90 frost-free days), heavy snowpack (300-600+ inches annually at upper elevations in Cascades), high UV exposure, extreme diurnal temperature variation (40-60F daily swings in summer), persistent wind above treeline
**Key locations:** Mount Rainier (Tahoma) alpine zone, Mount Adams meadows, North Cascades alpine lakes, Olympic alpine (Hurricane Ridge, Royal Basin), Wallowa Mountains (Eagle Cap Wilderness), Steens Mountain summit, Mount Hood timberline, Three Sisters alpine, Glacier Peak wilderness

The alpine and subalpine zone represents the harshest terrestrial environment in the PNW. Above treeline, bird communities face extreme cold, wind exposure, UV radiation, and a compressed breeding season. The subalpine parkland — a mosaic of tree islands, meadows, and snowfields — supports marginally higher diversity than true alpine, but both zones are characterized by very low species richness and high ecological specialization.

Snowpack duration is the single most important variable controlling avian community composition in this zone. Sites retaining snow into late July compress the available breeding season to 8-10 weeks, selecting for species with rapid nesting cycles and cold-tolerant physiology. South-facing slopes and ridge crests melt earlier and support denser bird communities than north-facing cirques and snow-accumulation basins [P17].

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**White-tailed Ptarmigan** (*Lagopus leucura*) — The only ptarmigan native to the contiguous PNW Cascades. Year-round alpine resident above treeline, using cryptic seasonal plumage (white in winter, mottled brown in summer) to avoid predation. Presence reliably indicates alpine tundra and subalpine meadow habitat above 6,000 ft. Feeds on willow buds, alpine forbs, and insects. Population density extremely low (1-3 birds per km2 of suitable habitat). Washington Cascades populations are the southernmost in the species' Pacific coastal range; introduced to Oregon's Wallowa Mountains in the 1960s-70s by ODFW. Status: WDFW candidate species, declining [G9].

#### Keystone Species

**Clark's Nutcracker** (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — The primary seed dispersal mutualist for whitebark pine, a foundation tree species of the subalpine zone. A single nutcracker caches 30,000-100,000 whitebark pine seeds per year, burying them in clusters of 1-15 seeds at 2-3 cm depth across the landscape. Unretrieved caches germinate and establish new whitebark pine stands, making the nutcracker the de facto planter of the subalpine forest [P10]. Whitebark pine is a keystone tree providing high-energy seeds consumed by grouse, squirrels, and bears; its decline from white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*) directly threatens nutcracker populations and the entire subalpine community. Nutcrackers range widely across elevation (subalpine to montane) and across zones (breeding in Zone 1, foraging into Zone 2), making them a critical link between alpine and montane communities.

#### Characteristic Wintering Species

Winter in the alpine zone is profoundly inhospitable. Nearly all breeding species descend to lower elevations or migrate south. The community collapses to a handful of cold-hardy residents:

- **White-tailed Ptarmigan** — remains above treeline year-round, roosting in snow burrows for insulation, metabolic rate suppressed
- **Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch** — descends to subalpine and upper montane; occasionally irrupts to lowland sites during extreme winters. Winter flocks of 50-200+ at ski areas and mountain passes
- **Clark's Nutcracker** — descends to montane, continues retrieving cached seeds from subalpine sites during thaw periods
- **Mountain Chickadee** — descends to montane conifer; regulated hypothermia (torpor) for overnight survival
- **Red Crossbill** — irruptive movements driven by cone crop; may winter at any elevation with adequate food

Winter irruptive species from boreal/interior sources occasionally reaching subalpine margins:
- **Bohemian Waxwing** — irregular winter visitor to mountain ash stands in subalpine parkland
- **Common Redpoll** — rare winter irruptive to subalpine birch/alder in invasion years
- **Evening Grosbeak** — variable, declining; formerly regular in subalpine winter

### Seasonal Variation

The alpine avian calendar is brutally compressed:

**October-May (7-8 months):** Snow-covered. Only ptarmigan and occasional rosy-finch flocks present above treeline. Subalpine zone supports chickadees, nutcrackers, and crossbills in tree islands.

**June:** Snowmelt begins. First arrivals to alpine meadows: American Pipits, Horned Larks, White-crowned Sparrows. Timing varies 2-4 weeks year-to-year depending on snowpack.

**July:** Peak breeding. Pipits and sparrows on nests. Rosy-finches feeding young at cliff sites. Raptors (Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk) hunting alpine meadows. Maximum species diversity for the zone (~20-25 species including transients).

**August:** Fledging period. Post-breeding flocking begins. Southbound migrants (shorebirds, raptors) pass through or over alpine ridges. Rufous Hummingbirds abundant in subalpine meadow flowers.

**September:** Rapid departure. Most breeding species descend or migrate. Raptor migration along Cascade ridges peaks (Chelan Ridge Raptor Migration Project documents 3,000-5,000 raptors annually). First snowfall possible.

**Elevational migration** is the dominant seasonal movement pattern. Dusky Grouse perform a counterintuitive reverse migration: breeding in lower montane/foothill zones in spring-summer, then ascending to subalpine conifer stands in winter to feed on conifer needles — one of the few North American birds that moves upslope in winter [P17].

### Microclimate Effects

**Snowpack duration:** The single strongest predictor of breeding bird density in alpine zones. Early-melting south-facing slopes support 2-3 times the bird density of late-melting north-facing slopes. A warming climate is advancing melt dates by 1-3 weeks over recent decades, potentially expanding breeding habitat temporally but threatening snow-dependent species (ptarmigan, rosy-finch) through habitat mismatch [G4].

**Rain shadow:** Alpine zones on the east side of the Cascades receive 30-60% less precipitation than west-side equivalents. East-side alpine communities (Mt. Adams east slope, Blue Mountains, Steens Mountain) support more open, drier meadow habitats favoring Mountain Bluebird, Prairie Falcon, and Horned Lark over the west-side suite of Fox Sparrow, Hermit Thrush, and Pacific Wren.

**Thermal corridors:** Cascade ridge systems generate thermals and orographic lift exploited by soaring raptors. Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, and Turkey Vulture use alpine ridge thermals during migration. The Chelan Ridge in the north-central Cascades and Bonney Butte in the southern Oregon Cascades are documented hawk-watch sites where thermal corridors concentrate migrating raptors [O16].

**Volcanic influence:** Active volcanism creates unique microhabitats. Volcanic fumaroles on Mt. Rainier and Mt. Baker maintain ice-free zones at extreme elevation, supporting insect communities that attract foraging pipits and rosy-finches above normal habitat range. Lahars and pyroclastic deposits create early-successional alpine habitat colonized by Horned Larks decades before vegetation recovery completes.

**Fire regime:** Subalpine fire is historically infrequent (200-500+ year return intervals) but stand-replacing. Post-fire subalpine landscapes create snag habitat for woodpeckers (Three-toed, Black-backed) and open hunting terrain for raptors. Modern fire suppression has had minimal impact on alpine bird communities compared to lower-elevation zones, but climate-driven increases in fire frequency are now pushing fire into previously fire-free subalpine stands [G8].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Timberline ecotone (Zone 1-2 boundary):** The transition from closed montane forest to alpine parkland is the most species-rich band in the alpine system. Tree islands (krummholz and subalpine fir clusters) within meadow matrix create structural diversity supporting both alpine and montane species. Characteristic timberline species include: Fox Sparrow, White-crowned Sparrow, Hermit Thrush, Townsend's Solitaire, and Dusky Grouse. Species richness peaks at 10-20% tree cover within the meadow-forest mosaic [P17].

**Avalanche chute communities:** Linear openings in subalpine forest created by recurring avalanche disturbance support dense shrub communities (slide alder, willow, huckleberry) with distinct bird assemblages including MacGillivray's Warbler, Fox Sparrow, and Rufous Hummingbird. These chutes function as elevational corridors connecting montane and alpine habitats.

**Snowfield-meadow edge:** The retreating snowfield edge in early summer creates a moving front of insect availability as meltwater releases dormant invertebrates. Rosy-finches, pipits, and Horned Larks track this moving edge, a foraging strategy unique to alpine obligates.

**Rock-meadow interface:** Talus fields and cliff bases adjacent to meadows support rosy-finch nesting (cliff cavities) with meadow foraging. The spatial arrangement of rock and meadow determines rosy-finch territory quality.

### Comparisons Between Analogous Zones

**North Cascades alpine vs. Blue Mountains alpine:**

| Feature | North Cascades | Blue Mountains |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| Timberline elevation | 5,500-6,500 ft | 7,500-8,500 ft |
| Annual precipitation | 80-120 inches | 25-40 inches |
| Snowpack persistence | Through July-August | Melts by June-July |
| Ptarmigan | Native, resident | Absent (too dry/low latitude) |
| Rosy-Finch breeding | Common | Uncommon, local (Wallowa peaks) |
| Mountain Bluebird | Rare (too wet) | Common (open terrain) |
| Prairie Falcon | Absent (no open terrain) | Present (cliff nesting) |
| Dominant sparrow | Fox Sparrow (sooty) | Chipping Sparrow (drier meadows) |
| Key driver | Snowpack duration | Aridity, fire history |

The North Cascades alpine is wetter, colder, and more maritime-influenced, supporting classic Pacific alpine specialists (ptarmigan, rosy-finch). The Blue Mountains alpine is drier, more continental, and supports a mix of alpine and open-country species (Mountain Bluebird, Prairie Falcon) that would be anomalous in the Cascades [P17].

**Olympic alpine vs. Cascade alpine:**

Olympic alpine zones are lower in elevation (timberline 4,500-5,500 ft), wetter, more isolated, and more heavily influenced by maritime fog. The Olympic Mountains support no ptarmigan (isolated mountain range, colonization barrier). Olympic alpine bird communities are depauperate relative to Cascades, consistent with island biogeography predictions — the Olympics function as a habitat island surrounded by lowland forest barriers. Olympic alpine lacks Clark's Nutcracker (no whitebark pine), further reducing the distinctive alpine assemblage. However, Olympic alpine meadows support high densities of American Pipit and Horned Lark, and the range's deep valleys create dramatic elevational compression (sea level to alpine within 20 horizontal miles).

### Conservation Threats

**Climate change (HIGH):** Alpine habitat is being squeezed from below. As temperatures warm, treeline advances upslope at rates of 1-3 meters elevation per decade in the Cascades. For species already at mountaintop, there is no upslope refuge — this is the "escalator to extinction" effect documented globally. White-tailed Ptarmigan, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, and American Pipit face progressive habitat reduction. Climate envelope models project 40-70% loss of suitable ptarmigan habitat in Washington by 2080 under moderate warming scenarios [G9, G4].

**Whitebark pine decline (HIGH):** White pine blister rust has killed 50-90% of whitebark pine in many PNW subalpine stands. Mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*), expanding to higher elevations with warming winters, accelerates mortality. Whitebark pine was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 2022 [G1]. Loss of this keystone tree eliminates the primary food source for Clark's Nutcracker and reduces the high-energy seed base supporting grouse, crossbills, and small mammals that sustain alpine raptors.

**Recreational disturbance (MODERATE):** Alpine meadows are focal points for backcountry recreation. Hiking traffic during the compressed breeding season can displace ground-nesting species (ptarmigan, pipit, Horned Lark). Studies at Mt. Rainier documented reduced pipit nesting success within 50 meters of maintained trails. Off-trail travel and camping on alpine meadows compact soil and damage vegetation recovery [G8].

**Atmospheric deposition (LOW-MODERATE):** Nitrogen deposition from agricultural and industrial sources enriches alpine soils, favoring fast-growing grasses over slow-growing alpine forbs. Community-level shifts in alpine vegetation alter the insect prey base for insectivorous birds (pipits, sparrows). Effects are better documented in European alpine systems but emerging in PNW high-elevation monitoring [G15].

## Zone 2: Montane Conifer Forest

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** 2,500-5,000 ft (variable by latitude and aspect)
**Geographic extent:** Mid-elevation belt on both slopes of the Cascade Range, Olympics (windward and leeward slopes), Blue Mountains, Selkirk Mountains, Siskiyou Mountains (southern OR)
**Dominant vegetation:** Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), Pacific silver fir (*Abies amabilis*), mountain hemlock (*Tsuga mertensiana*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) on west side; grand fir (*Abies grandis*), western larch (*Larix occidentalis*), ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*), lodgepole pine (*Pinus contorta*) on east side
**Climate:** West-side: heavy precipitation (60-120 inches annually), deep winter snowpack, cool summers, persistent cloud/fog. East-side: moderate precipitation (20-40 inches), lighter snowpack, warmer and drier summers, more continental temperature range
**Key locations:** Olympic National Forest mid-elevation, Gifford Pinchot National Forest, Mt. Baker-Snoqualmie National Forest, Willamette National Forest, Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest (east side), Umatilla National Forest (Blue Mountains), Colville National Forest (Selkirks), Rogue River-Siskiyou National Forest

The montane conifer zone is the heartland of PNW forest bird diversity. It spans the greatest geographic area of any zone and encompasses the most dramatic structural variation — from cathedral-like old-growth stands with 200-foot canopy and multi-layered understory to dense second-growth plantations with closed canopy and minimal structural complexity. This structural gradient, more than any other factor, determines bird community composition within the zone.

Old-growth forest (trees >200 years, with large snags, downed logs, canopy gaps, and multi-layered structure) supports a fundamentally different bird community than managed second-growth forest (trees 30-80 years, uniform canopy, minimal understory). This distinction drives the most consequential conservation conflict in PNW ornithology: the Northern Spotted Owl crisis [G6].

The east-side vs. west-side divide within this zone is equally significant. West-side montane forests are wet, dense, and shade-tolerant species-dominated. East-side montane forests are drier, more open, fire-adapted, and support a distinct species assemblage including several species absent from the west side entirely (White-headed Woodpecker, Williamson's Sapsucker, Flammulated Owl).

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**Northern Spotted Owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — ESA-threatened since 1990. The definitive indicator of structurally complex, old-growth and mature conifer forest in the PNW. Requires large territories (2,000-10,000 acres depending on habitat quality) of contiguous late-successional forest with multi-layered canopy for nesting, roosting, and foraging. Spotted Owls are territorial, long-lived (up to 20+ years), and site-faithful. Population declining at 3-5% annually across the range due to combined effects of habitat loss from historical timber harvest and competitive displacement by Barred Owl (*Strix varia*), an invasive congener expanding westward from eastern North America. The USFWS Barred Owl management strategy (initiated 2024) involves lethal removal of Barred Owls in key Spotted Owl territories — a controversial but biologically necessary intervention to prevent extinction [G6, G1].

#### Keystone Species

**Northern Goshawk** (*Accipiter gentilis*) — Apex forest raptor of the montane zone. Hunts grouse, jays, squirrels, and woodpeckers within closed-canopy forest, requiring large stands of mature timber with open understory for aerial pursuit. Goshawk presence indicates intact forest food webs; territories function as umbrella areas protecting habitat for dozens of co-occurring species. Nests in large conifers (typically Douglas-fir or western hemlock >100 years old) with relatively open understory beneath dense canopy — structural features shared with Spotted Owl habitat. Breeding Bird Survey data show stable to slightly declining trends in PNW [G4].

**Pileated Woodpecker** (*Dryocopus pileatus*) — The largest woodpecker in the PNW (16-19 inches) and a keystone cavity excavator. Excavates rectangular nest and roost cavities in large snags and live trees with heart rot, creating cavities subsequently used by 30+ secondary cavity-nesting species including Wood Duck, Bufflehead, Barrow's Goldeneye, Northern Saw-whet Owl, Northern Flying Squirrel, American Marten, and numerous small birds and mammals. A single Pileated Woodpecker territory (250-1,000 acres) may contain 10-20 excavated cavities functioning as community housing for the forest ecosystem. Requires large-diameter snags (>20 inches DBH) and downed logs — structural features abundant in old-growth but scarce in managed second-growth [P18].

#### Characteristic Wintering Species

Winter in west-side montane forests brings some species shifts:

- **Varied Thrush** — partial downslope migration; remains in lower montane, augmented by northern migrants
- **Townsend's Solitaire** — descends from subalpine to montane, defends juniper berry territories
- **Red Crossbill** — irruptive; winter flocks track cone crops across montane elevations
- **Pine Siskin** — abundant but irruptive winter resident; flocks of hundreds in productive cone years
- **Northern Pygmy-Owl** — year-round resident, more detectable in winter (vocalizing)
- **Northern Saw-whet Owl** — year-round resident, augmented by northern migrants
- **Dark-eyed Junco** (*oreganus* group) — common winter resident at lower montane elevations
- **Winter Wren** / Pacific Wren — year-round resident, retreats to lower montane stream corridors

East-side montane forests lose most breeding species in winter. The community simplifies to residents: Mountain Chickadee, Red-breasted Nuthatch, White-breasted Nuthatch, Pygmy Nuthatch, Hairy Woodpecker, Pileated Woodpecker, and irruptive finches. Deep snow and cold temperatures push most passerines to lower elevations or southward.

| Mountain Quail | WDFW Game, declining | Dense understory loss, fragmentation | WDFW [G9] |

### Seasonal Variation

**December-February:** Lowest diversity. Resident cavity nesters (woodpeckers, nuthatches, chickadees), wrens, kinglets, and irruptive finches. Mixed-species foraging flocks (chickadee-led flocks of 8-15 birds including nuthatches, creepers, kinglets) are the dominant social unit. Owl breeding season begins (Great Horned Owl, Northern Spotted Owl territorial calling starts January-February).

**March-April:** Early arrivals. Rufous Hummingbird (one of earliest neotropical migrants, arriving mid-March), Violet-green Swallow, Turkey Vulture. Woodpecker drumming intensifies. Band-tailed Pigeons return. Owls nesting.

**May-June:** Peak breeding season. Neotropical migrant wave: Townsend's Warbler, Hermit Warbler, Wilson's Warbler, Western Tanager, Hammond's Flycatcher, Pacific-slope Flycatcher, Olive-sided Flycatcher, Swainson's Thrush. Maximum species diversity (80-100 species in well-surveyed montane sites). Dawn chorus reaches peak complexity.

**July-August:** Fledging period. Post-breeding dispersal begins. Mixed-species flocks reform. Hummingbird migration through montane meadows. Flycatcher departure. Early-departing migrants (Olive-sided Flycatcher, Wilson's Warbler) leave by late August.

**September-October:** Autumn migration. Most neotropical migrants depart. Varied Thrush, juncos, and sparrows begin elevational descent. Raptor migration along Cascade ridges peaks in September. Band-tailed Pigeon departs. Crossbill and siskin movements begin.

**November:** Transition to winter community. Late migrants (Hermit Thrush, Ruby-crowned Kinglet) move through. Winter finch flocks establish. Community settles into winter baseline of ~25-35 resident species.

### Microclimate Effects

**Rain shadow — west vs. east montane forests:** The Cascade crest creates the most dramatic precipitation gradient in the contiguous United States: west-side montane forests receive 60-120 inches annually; east-side equivalents receive 20-40 inches. This gradient produces two fundamentally different montane bird communities occupying the same elevation band. West-side forests are dense, shade-tolerant, and support moisture-dependent species (Pacific Wren, Varied Thrush, Chestnut-backed Chickadee). East-side forests are open, fire-adapted, and support dry-forest specialists (White-headed Woodpecker, Pygmy Nuthatch, Flammulated Owl). The transition occurs over 5-15 miles across the Cascade crest — one of the sharpest avian community boundaries in North America [P7, P18].

**Fog belt influence:** On the west side, persistent orographic cloud and fog immersion at 2,000-4,000 ft creates a fog-drip zone that supplements precipitation by 20-30%. This zone supports the densest epiphyte loads (mosses, lichens) in the montane forest — the same moss platforms required by Marbled Murrelets for nesting. Fog-belt forests have measurably higher Brown Creeper and Pacific Wren densities than drier montane forests at the same elevation, likely reflecting the richer invertebrate fauna in epiphyte mats [P4, P5].

**Fire regime — east-side montane:** Historical fire regimes in east-side ponderosa pine and mixed-conifer forests were characterized by frequent, low-intensity surface fires (5-25 year return intervals) that maintained open, parklike stand structure. A century of fire suppression has converted these forests to dense, multi-layered stands favoring shade-tolerant species (grand fir, Douglas-fir) at the expense of fire-adapted ponderosa pine. This transformation has restructured the bird community: White-headed Woodpecker and Lewis's Woodpecker (both requiring open pine stands) have declined, while species favoring dense forest (Townsend's Warbler, Hammond's Flycatcher) have expanded into formerly open stands. Post-fire salvage logging removes the snag habitat that supports post-fire specialist communities (Black-backed Woodpecker, Lewis's Woodpecker, cavity nesters) [G8, P18].

**Thermal corridors for raptors:** Mountain valleys and ridge systems channel wind patterns that create thermal lift zones. Northern Goshawk territories cluster along ridgelines and valley edges where updrafts facilitate soaring flight. Cooper's Hawk and Sharp-shinned Hawk use forest corridors along streams and valleys as hunting flyways, concentrating where topography channels prey movement.

**Snowpack duration — montane elevation gradient:** Within the montane zone, snow persistence varies from 2-3 months at 2,500 ft to 5-7 months at 5,000 ft. This gradient compresses breeding seasons at upper montane elevations and creates staggered phenology — the same species (e.g., Pacific-slope Flycatcher) may arrive and initiate nesting 3-4 weeks later at 4,500 ft than at 2,500 ft. Climate warming is advancing snowmelt timing, potentially creating phenological mismatches between nesting birds and their arthropod prey (the "green wave" tracking hypothesis) [G4].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Townsend's x Hermit Warbler hybrid zone:** One of the best-documented avian hybrid zones in North America occurs within the PNW montane zone. Townsend's Warbler (*Setophaga townsendi*) breeds from Alaska south through the Washington Cascades. Hermit Warbler (*S. occidentalis*) breeds from central Oregon south through the Siskiyous and northern California. The two species hybridize in a narrow contact zone in the southern Washington Cascades and northern Oregon Cascades (approximately 44-46°N latitude). Townsend's Warbler is competitively dominant: the hybrid zone is moving south at approximately 0.7 km/year, with Townsend's genes introgressing into Hermit Warbler populations. The contact zone represents an active speciation boundary visible in real time. Hybrids show intermediate plumage and song characteristics. This dynamic illustrates how montane bird communities are not static assemblages but evolving systems [P6, O1].

**Old-growth / second-growth edge:** The boundary between old-growth and managed second-growth forest creates an edge effect zone 50-200 meters wide with distinct species composition. Edge-adapted species (Steller's Jay, American Robin, Dark-eyed Junco) increase at old-growth boundaries, while interior-forest species (Varied Thrush, Pacific Wren, Marbled Murrelet) decline near edges. Fragmented old-growth stands below approximately 100 acres lose area-sensitive interior species disproportionately — a direct application of island biogeography to forest management. The minimum viable patch size for Northern Spotted Owl territories is approximately 2,000-4,000 acres of contiguous late-successional forest, depending on habitat quality [G6, P19].

**Montane-subalpine ecotone (Zone 2-1 boundary):** The transition from closed montane forest to subalpine parkland supports maximum montane bird diversity. Species from both zones overlap: Canada Jay, Mountain Chickadee, and Townsend's Solitaire from above; Varied Thrush, Townsend's Warbler, and Pacific Wren from below. Avalanche chutes and meadow openings within this ecotone support shrub-nesting species (Fox Sparrow, MacGillivray's Warbler) absent from closed canopy above or below.

**Forest-meadow interface:** Natural meadows within the montane zone (maintained by frost pockets, poor drainage, or historical fire) create habitat for open-country species embedded in forested landscape: Olive-sided Flycatcher (singing from meadow-edge snags), Rufous Hummingbird (meadow wildflowers), Mountain Bluebird (east side meadows), and Lincoln's Sparrow (wet meadows). Meadow loss through conifer encroachment (driven by fire suppression and climate warming) eliminates these embedded open-habitat communities.

**Riparian corridors through montane forest (Zone 5 intersection):** Montane streams and their riparian zones create linear habitat diversity within the forest matrix. American Dipper, Harlequin Duck (breeding on mountain streams), and Belted Kingfisher are restricted to these aquatic corridors. Riparian vegetation (alder, willow, cottonwood) within the conifer matrix supports species absent from upland forest: Warbling Vireo, Red-eyed Vireo (east side), Yellow Warbler, and Willow Flycatcher.

**Cascade montane vs. Olympic montane:**

Olympic montane forests are more isolated (peninsula geography), receive higher precipitation (100-200 inches on windward slopes), and support slightly lower bird diversity than equivalent Cascade forests — again consistent with island biogeography. The Olympics lack several Cascade montane species: Williamson's Sapsucker, Mountain Quail, and White-headed Woodpecker are absent. However, Olympic old-growth supports exceptionally high densities of Marbled Murrelet (proximity to marine foraging habitat) and Spotted Owl (extensive unlogged old-growth in Olympic National Park). The Olympic rain shadow (northeastern Olympics receiving <20 inches annually) creates a montane bird community boundary within a single mountain range — Douglas-fir forest on the dry side supporting a different assemblage than Sitka spruce-western hemlock forest on the wet side.

### Conservation Threats

**Old-growth habitat loss (CRITICAL):** Historical timber harvest removed an estimated 80-90% of original old-growth forest in the PNW. Remaining old-growth is concentrated in National Parks, Wilderness Areas, and Late-Successional Reserves designated under the Northwest Forest Plan (1994). Species dependent on old-growth structural features — large snags, cavity trees, moss-laden branches, multi-layered canopy — have declined proportionally. Northern Spotted Owl populations have declined approximately 60-75% since ESA listing in 1990. Marbled Murrelet populations in Washington declined approximately 4.4% per year from 2001-2019 (WDFW monitoring). Vaux's Swift populations are limited by availability of large hollow old-growth snags [G1, G6, G9].

**Barred Owl invasion (CRITICAL):** The Barred Owl (*Strix varia*), native to eastern North America, expanded westward across the boreal forest during the 20th century and reached the PNW by the 1970s. It is now abundant throughout PNW montane forests and competitively excludes Northern Spotted Owl through larger body size, broader diet, higher reproductive rate, and tolerance of younger forest. Barred Owls also prey directly on Spotted Owls and their prey base. The USFWS initiated experimental Barred Owl removal in 2013, with results showing Spotted Owl population stabilization in removal areas. A rangewide Barred Owl management program was authorized in 2024. This is arguably the most consequential invasive species event in PNW forest bird ecology [G1, G6].

**Fire suppression and fuels accumulation (HIGH — east side):** A century of fire suppression on east-side montane forests has transformed open pine parklands into dense mixed-conifer stands. This eliminates habitat for fire-dependent species (White-headed Woodpecker, Lewis's Woodpecker, Flammulated Owl) while paradoxically increasing the risk of catastrophic stand-replacing fire that exceeds historical burn severity. The 2020 Oregon fire season burned over 1 million acres, including montane forests that had not burned in over a century. Post-fire landscapes initially support woodpecker communities but may not regenerate to suitable forest within decades if fire severity was too high [G8].

**Climate change — elevation squeeze (HIGH):** As temperatures warm, the montane conifer belt shifts upslope, compressing between expanding lowland conditions below and fixed alpine terrain above. Species adapted to cool, moist montane conditions (Varied Thrush, Pacific Wren, Hermit Warbler) face progressive habitat reduction. Models project 15-30% reduction in suitable climate envelope for west-side montane forest by 2080 under moderate warming scenarios. East-side montane forests face increased drought stress, bark beetle outbreaks (mountain pine beetle, Douglas-fir beetle), and lengthened fire seasons [G4, G15].

**Fragmentation and edge effects (HIGH — west side):** Checkerboard land ownership patterns in western Oregon and Washington (alternating federal and private sections, a legacy of 19th-century railroad land grants) create a fragmented forest landscape where old-growth patches are isolated within a matrix of managed second-growth. Area-sensitive species (Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Pileated Woodpecker) require large contiguous forest blocks; fragmentation reduces effective habitat area below minimum viable patch sizes. Source-sink population dynamics emerge: small isolated old-growth patches function as population sinks where mortality exceeds reproduction, maintained only by immigration from larger source patches in National Parks and Wilderness [P19, G6].

## Zone 3: Lowland Forest and Urban

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** <500 ft (western lowlands)
**Geographic extent:** Puget Trough (Olympia to Bellingham), Willamette Valley (Eugene to Portland), coastal plains (Grays Harbor to Coos Bay), Fraser River lowlands (southern BC), Portland-Vancouver metro, Seattle-Tacoma metro
**Dominant vegetation:** Remnant Douglas-fir/western red cedar forest, second-growth mixed forest, riparian corridors (cottonwood, bigleaf maple, red alder), urban parks and gardens, agricultural lands (grass seed, nursery, row crops), suburban landscaping with non-native ornamentals
**Climate:** Mild maritime, 36-50 inches precipitation, moderate year-round temperatures (average January 38-42F, July 65-75F), wet winters, dry summers (June-September), frost-free season 200-250 days
**Key locations:** Discovery Park (Seattle), Forest Park (Portland), Ridgefield NWR, Nisqually NWR, William L. Finley NWR, Sauvie Island, Skagit Valley agricultural flats, Ankeny NWR, Baskett Slough NWR

The lowland zone is where the most people live and where birds encounter the most anthropogenically modified landscape. This creates a community divided between native-habitat remnants supporting PNW forest species and the urban-suburban matrix supporting a distinct assemblage of human-adapted generalists and invasive species. The conservation challenge is maintaining connectivity between remnant habitat patches in an increasingly urbanized landscape.

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**Steller's Jay** (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) — Common across the forest-urban interface, bridging intact lowland forest and suburban neighborhoods with mature conifers. Presence indicates at least moderate tree cover with coniferous component. Aggressive, conspicuous, and adaptable — a reliable indicator of the lowland forest-to-urban gradient rather than any single habitat type.

#### Keystone Species

**American Robin** (*Turdus migratorius*) — The dominant frugivore and seed disperser in lowland PNW. Robins consume and disperse seeds of native species (osoberry, cascara, salal, madrone) and non-native species (English holly, Himalayan blackberry) across the urban-wildland matrix. Winter flocks of hundreds to thousands strip fruiting trees, redistributing seeds across the landscape. Robin abundance structures fruit-bearing plant distribution across the lowland zone.

### Seasonal Variation

**Winter (Nov-Feb):** Peak waterfowl abundance on agricultural flats and refuges. Northern raptor visitors (Rough-legged Hawk, Northern Shrike). Sparrow diversity highest. Mixed thrush flocks (robin, varied thrush) in fruiting trees. Anna's Hummingbird overwinters — the only hummingbird resident year-round in the PNW, a dramatic range expansion northward from southern California since the 1960s, facilitated by urban feeders and ornamental plantings [O2].

**Spring (Mar-May):** Neotropical migrants arrive: Rufous Hummingbird (mid-March), swallows (March-April), warblers (April-May), flycatchers (May). Waterfowl depart for northern breeding grounds. Breeding territories established. Dawn chorus peaks late May.

**Summer (Jun-Aug):** Breeding and fledging. Swallow colonies active. Purple Martin colonies at nest boxes (where present). Post-breeding dispersal begins August. Shorebird return migration through lowland wetlands starts July.

**Fall (Sep-Oct):** Sparrow migration peaks. Waterfowl staging begins. Northern visitors arrive. Garden diversity peaks briefly as migrants pass through. Raptor migration visible at lowland hawk-watch sites.

### Microclimate Effects

**Rain shadow — Puget Trough:** The Olympic Mountains create a rain shadow across the Puget Trough lowlands, with the San Juan Islands and Sequim area receiving <20 inches annually — drier than east-side steppe in some measurements. This dry pocket within the maritime lowland supports prairie-oak habitats with species more typical of Zone 4 (Western Bluebird, Say's Phoebe at range margin) than the wet Douglas-fir forests 30 miles to the east [O9].

**Urban heat island:** Metropolitan areas (Seattle, Portland, Vancouver BC) are 2-5F warmer than surrounding rural landscape. This thermal advantage likely facilitates Anna's Hummingbird year-round residency (reduced cold stress), earlier breeding initiation for resident species, and attraction of overwintering species that might otherwise be absent (e.g., increased overwintering by Yellow-rumped Warbler in urban areas) [O2].

**Fog and marine influence:** Coastal lowlands (Grays Harbor, Long Beach Peninsula, Coos Bay) experience persistent summer fog that moderates temperatures and maintains moisture levels absent from inland lowlands. Coastal lowland bird communities retain more forest species (Pacific Wren, Varied Thrush year-round) than interior lowlands at equivalent elevation.

**Agricultural landscape:** Willamette Valley and Skagit Valley agricultural lands create open-country habitat within the forested lowland matrix. Grass seed fields support wintering raptors (Red-tailed Hawk, Rough-legged Hawk, Short-eared Owl, Northern Harrier) and sparrow flocks at densities exceeding any natural PNW grassland. Agricultural flooding in winter creates temporary wetlands used by 200,000+ waterfowl [G3].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Urban-wildland interface:** The expanding urban boundary creates a gradient from built environment to intact forest over 1-5 km. Species composition shifts dramatically across this gradient. Urban core: House Sparrow, Rock Pigeon, European Starling, Crow, Glaucous-winged Gull. Suburban: Black-capped Chickadee, Bushtit, Anna's Hummingbird, Cooper's Hawk, Bewick's Wren. Urban forest remnant: Varied Thrush, Pacific Wren, Pileated Woodpecker. Connectivity between remnant forest patches (greenways, riparian corridors) maintains interior-forest species in urbanized landscapes. Loss of connectivity converts forest remnants to islands, and island biogeography applies — small isolated parks lose area-sensitive species progressively [P19].

**Lowland-foothill transition (Zone 3-4 boundary):** The transition from lowland to foothill at 300-500 ft elevation marks a shift from human-dominated to more natural landscape. Mixed oak-conifer edges at the Willamette Valley margins support both lowland generalists and foothill specialists. This ecotone is critical for Western Bluebird (nesting in oak cavities, foraging in lowland fields), White-breasted Nuthatch (oak bark forager), and Acorn Woodpecker (oak mast storage).

**Lowland-riparian intersection (Zone 3-5):** Every lowland urban area contains riparian corridors that function as wildlife highways through otherwise inhospitable built environment. Riparian species (Song Sparrow, Spotted Towhee, Yellow Warbler, Willow Flycatcher) achieve highest lowland densities along stream corridors. In Portland, Forest Park's 5,200 acres of contiguous lowland forest connected by riparian corridors supports 112 bird species — the richest urban forest bird community in the PNW [O16].

### Conservation Threats

**Habitat loss and urbanization (CRITICAL):** The lowland zone has experienced the most extensive habitat conversion in the PNW. Over 90% of Willamette Valley prairie-oak habitat has been converted to agriculture or urban development since European settlement. Puget Sound lowland prairies have declined by >97%. Species dependent on these habitats (Streaked Horned Lark, Oregon Vesper Sparrow, Western Bluebird) are in severe decline. Urban expansion continues to fragment remaining forest patches [G1, P15, O5].

**Invasive species (HIGH):** Three invasive bird species (European Starling, House Sparrow, Rock Pigeon) compete with native cavity nesters for limited nesting sites. Starlings displace Purple Martins, Violet-green Swallows, and woodpeckers from cavities. Non-native plants (Himalayan blackberry, English ivy, Scotch broom) alter vegetation structure — blackberry provides fruit for robins and waxwings but smothers native understory, while ivy kills host trees, reducing canopy cover and snag recruitment [O9].

**Climate change (MODERATE):** Warming winters reduce freeze events, facilitating northward range expansion of southern species (Anna's Hummingbird, California Scrub-Jay, Eurasian Collared-Dove). Summer drought intensification may stress urban trees, reducing canopy cover. Sea-level rise threatens coastal lowland habitats (estuarine marshes, Skagit Delta agricultural land used by wintering waterfowl) [G15].

**Window strikes and cat predation (MODERATE):** Urban and suburban environments pose direct mortality threats. An estimated 600 million to 1 billion birds are killed annually by window strikes in North America; PNW urban areas contribute proportionally. Domestic and feral cats kill an estimated 1.3-4 billion birds annually in the US (Loss et al. 2013, *Nature Communications*). These mortality sources disproportionately affect lowland and urban-adapted species.

## Zone 4: Foothill / Oak Woodland

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** 500-2,500 ft
**Geographic extent:** Willamette Valley margins and foothills, Umpqua Valley, Rogue Valley, Klamath Mountains (Siskiyou, Klamath ranges), East Cascades lower slopes (Yakima, Klickitat, Deschutes counties), Columbia Gorge south-facing slopes, Puget Sound prairies (remnant)
**Dominant vegetation:** Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) woodland and savanna, mixed oak-conifer (oak with Douglas-fir, ponderosa pine, madrone), Pacific madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), California black oak (southern OR), poison oak understory, bunchgrass prairies between oak groves, manzanita chaparral (Siskiyou)
**Climate:** Warm dry summers (July average 75-85F), mild wet winters, fire-adapted ecosystem with historical fire return interval of 5-25 years, Mediterranean-like precipitation pattern
**Key locations:** Finley NWR (Willamette Valley), Table Rocks (Rogue Valley), Columbia Hills (Klickitat County), Fort Lewis prairies (South Puget Sound), Scatter Creek WMA, Oak Creek Wildlife Area (Yakima), Illinois Valley (Siskiyou), Waldo Hills (Marion County)

The foothill/oak woodland zone is the most threatened terrestrial habitat type in the PNW. Oregon white oak ecosystems have declined by over 90% in the Willamette Valley since European settlement through conversion to agriculture, urban development, and fire suppression allowing conifer encroachment. The remaining fragments support a distinctive bird community with several species found nowhere else in the PNW — species at the northern edge of their range, dependent on the warm, dry, open-canopy conditions that oak woodland provides [O5].

Fire is the keystone process. Without periodic fire (historically set by Indigenous peoples for millennia and by natural ignition), oak woodlands succeed to closed Douglas-fir forest within 50-100 years, eliminating the open savanna structure that oak-associated birds require. Modern fire suppression has initiated this conversion across most remaining oak woodland, making prescribed fire restoration the single most important conservation intervention for this zone's avifauna [O5, P18].

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**Acorn Woodpecker** (*Melanerpes formicivorus*) — Oak obligate present wherever mature Oregon white oak or California black oak stands persist. Colonial, cooperatively breeding, with conspicuous granary trees (snags or utility poles drilled with thousands of acorn-storage holes). Presence reliably indicates oak woodland with sufficient mast production to sustain year-round populations. Northern range limit in PNW roughly follows the northern extent of productive oak stands (southern Puget Sound, Willamette Valley margins, Klamath region, East Cascades lower slopes). Absent from areas where oak has been eliminated [O5].

#### Keystone Species

**Lewis's Woodpecker** (*Melanerpes lewis*) — A uniquely aerial-foraging woodpecker that flycatches from exposed perches in open oak and ponderosa woodland. Requires large-diameter snags in open-canopy habitat for nesting — a structural condition historically maintained by frequent fire. Lewis's Woodpecker populations have declined 70%+ range-wide since the 1960s (BBS data), driven by fire suppression converting open woodland to closed forest and removing snag habitat. Where present, Lewis's Woodpecker excavates cavities later used by secondary cavity nesters (bluebirds, swallows, small owls), functioning as a cavity-creation keystone. ESA listing petitions have been filed but not yet acted upon [G4, O4].

### Seasonal Variation

**Winter (Nov-Feb):** Oak mast crop quality drives winter community. High acorn years attract large flocks of Band-tailed Pigeon, Steller's Jay, California Scrub-Jay, and Acorn Woodpecker activity peaks. Raptors concentrate over oak savanna: Red-tailed Hawk, American Kestrel, White-tailed Kite (southern Willamette Valley, at northern range limit). Sparrow flocks (Golden-crowned, White-crowned, Fox) forage in oak-grassland edges.

**Spring (Mar-May):** Lazuli Bunting and Ash-throated Flycatcher arrive (late April-May). Lewis's Woodpecker returns to breeding territories (if present). Western Bluebird nesting begins in cavities and nestboxes. Prescribed burns conducted by land managers create fresh foraging habitat.

**Summer (Jun-Aug):** Peak breeding. Western Kingbird and Ash-throated Flycatcher active. Acorn Woodpecker colonies raise cooperative broods. Post-breeding flocking begins August.

**Fall (Sep-Oct):** Acorn harvest and caching by jays, woodpeckers, and nutcrackers. Migrant warblers pass through oak canopy. Raptors return to winter territories.

### Microclimate Effects

**Fire regime (dominant driver):** Historical fire at 5-25 year intervals maintained open oak savanna with bunchgrass understory. Without fire, Douglas-fir and grand fir overtop and shade out oaks within 50-80 years, converting open woodland to closed conifer forest and eliminating oak-dependent bird habitat. Prescribed fire is now the primary management tool for maintaining oak bird communities on conservation lands (Finley NWR, Fort Lewis, Scatter Creek). Post-burn oak response (vigorous resprouting, increased acorn production) directly benefits the entire oak bird assemblage within 2-5 years of treatment [O5, P18].

**Rain shadow and thermal pockets:** South-facing slopes in the Columbia Gorge, Rogue Valley, and East Cascades foothills create thermal pockets where oak woodland persists at latitudes/elevations where surrounding landscape is conifer forest. These thermal refugia support disjunct populations of southern species at their northern range limits (Oak Titmouse, Ash-throated Flycatcher, Lesser Goldfinch). Thermal pockets may serve as climate-change refugia if warming makes surrounding areas suitable for oak expansion.

**Aspect and slope:** Oak woodland on south-facing slopes supports different bird communities than north-facing conifer forest at the same elevation and longitude. A single ridge may have Acorn Woodpecker and Western Bluebird on the south face and Varied Thrush and Pacific Wren on the north face — a community boundary measured in hundreds of meters.

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Oak-conifer transition:** The ecotone between oak woodland (Zone 4) and montane conifer forest (Zone 2) is a biodiversity hotspot. Mixed oak-conifer stands support species from both communities plus edge specialists. Hutton's Vireo, Purple Finch, and Nashville Warbler reach highest densities in this transition. The ecotone is dynamic — it moves upslope and north-facing with fire (oak expands), and retreats with fire suppression (conifer encroaches).

**Prairie-oak mosaic:** The interface between remnant native grassland (bunchgrass prairie) and oak savanna supports the most threatened bird community in the PNW. Streaked Horned Lark requires short-stature grassland immediately adjacent to oak savanna. Oregon Vesper Sparrow nests in native bunchgrass within the oak-prairie matrix. Loss of this interface through agricultural conversion, urban development, or woody encroachment eliminates species that depend on neither pure grassland nor pure forest but the specific mosaic of both [P15, O5].

**Oak-riparian intersection:** Where riparian corridors pass through oak foothills (Willamette Valley tributaries, Rogue River, Klickitat River), the intersection of riparian and oak habitats creates exceptional species richness. Lewis's Woodpecker, Acorn Woodpecker, Yellow-breasted Chat, Bullock's Oriole, and Lazuli Bunting co-occur in these rich interfaces.

### Comparisons Between Analogous Zones

**Willamette Valley oak vs. Klamath/Rogue Valley oak:**

| Feature | Willamette Valley | Klamath/Rogue Valley |
|---------|------------------|---------------------|
| Oak species | Oregon white oak | White oak + California black oak |
| Precipitation | 40-50 inches | 20-30 inches |
| Southern species | Few | Oak Titmouse, Ash-throated Flycatcher, Lesser Goldfinch |
| Acorn Woodpecker | Present, local | Common, widespread |
| Lewis's Woodpecker | Rare, declining | Uncommon, more stable |
| Prairie remnant | <3% remaining | Larger remnants in grasslands |
| Fire management | Active on refuges | Active, aided by BLM/USFS |
| Biogeographic affinity | Northern PNW | Klamath-Siskiyou (California transition) |

The Klamath/Rogue Valley represents a biogeographic transition zone where California avifaunal elements (Oak Titmouse, California Towhee at range limit, Lesser Goldfinch) reach their northern extent. Species richness in Klamath oak exceeds Willamette oak due to this overlap of northern and southern faunas [P8].

**West-side foothill oak vs. east-side ponderosa-oak:**

East-side foothills (Klickitat County, Yakima, Deschutes) support Oregon white oak interspersed with ponderosa pine rather than Douglas-fir. The bird community shifts accordingly: Mountain Bluebird replaces Western Bluebird, Pygmy Nuthatch replaces White-breasted Nuthatch as the dominant trunk-forager, and Lewis's Woodpecker occurs at higher densities (more open structure, more snags). White-headed Woodpecker, absent from west-side oak, occurs in ponderosa-oak transitions on the east side.

### Conservation Threats

**Habitat loss (CRITICAL):** Over 90% of Willamette Valley oak woodland has been converted since European settlement. Remaining fragments are small, isolated, and degrading through conifer encroachment. At current loss rates without intervention, several oak-associated species face regional extirpation. The Pacific Birds Habitat Joint Venture estimates that 10,000+ acres of oak restoration are needed to stabilize PNW oak bird populations [O5].

**Fire suppression (CRITICAL):** Without fire, oak woodland converts to closed conifer forest within 50-100 years. Fire suppression has been the dominant driver of oak habitat loss in the 20th century, transforming open savanna into dense Douglas-fir stands that shade out oaks and eliminate cavity-nesting habitat. Prescribed fire programs on conservation lands are reversing this trend locally but insufficient at landscape scale [O5, P18].

**Conifer encroachment (HIGH):** Related to fire suppression. Douglas-fir seedlings established under fire-suppression conditions now overtop oak canopy across thousands of acres. Mechanical removal (conifer thinning) combined with prescribed fire is the recommended treatment, but implementation is limited by cost, liability concerns, and air quality regulations restricting burn windows.

**Fragmentation (HIGH):** Remaining oak patches are small (median <50 acres in Willamette Valley) and isolated, with limited connectivity. Area-sensitive oak species (Lewis's Woodpecker, Acorn Woodpecker colonies) require patches >100 acres with adequate snag density. Landscape-level connectivity between oak patches is essential for metapopulation viability.

## Zone 5: Riparian, Wetland, and Estuarine

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** Variable — follows waterways from montane headwaters (~5,000 ft) to sea level estuaries
**Geographic extent:** Columbia River system (including Snake, Willamette, Deschutes, John Day, Yakima tributaries), Skagit River delta, Fraser River delta, Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Klamath Basin, Malheur Basin, Chehalis River system, Cowlitz River, Nisqually River delta
**Dominant vegetation:** Riparian cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*) and willow (*Salix* spp.) galleries, red alder (*Alnus rubra*), bigleaf maple, marsh vegetation (cattail, bulrush, sedge), tidal mudflats, eelgrass beds, agricultural-wetland interfaces
**Climate:** Moderated by water proximity; riparian corridors buffer temperature extremes. Estuary zones experience tidal influence, salt-fresh water mixing, and persistent wind
**Key locations:** Grays Harbor (WHSRN site of hemispheric importance), Willapa Bay, Skagit River delta, Nisqually NWR, Ridgefield NWR, Klamath Basin NWR complex (Upper Klamath, Lower Klamath, Tule Lake, Clear Lake), Malheur NWR, Steigerwald Lake NWR, Columbia River Estuary, Dungeness NWR, Padilla Bay NERR, Skagit Wildlife Area

The riparian/wetland/estuarine zone is unique among the seven zones because it does not occupy a contiguous geographic area or elevation band. Instead, it threads through all other zones as a linear network of aquatic and semi-aquatic habitats. Riparian corridors function as biological highways, connecting alpine headwaters to marine estuaries and carrying bird communities, nutrients, and ecological processes across the full elevation gradient of the PNW.

This zone supports the highest bird density per unit area of any PNW zone during migration and winter staging periods. Estuaries like Grays Harbor and the Skagit Delta host hundreds of thousands of shorebirds and waterfowl in flocks that represent continental-scale population fractions. The zone's ecological importance is disproportionate to its spatial extent.

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**American Dipper** (*Cinclus mexicanus*) — North America's only truly aquatic passerine. Year-round resident of clear, fast-flowing mountain streams from montane headwaters to lowland foothills. Walks underwater on stream bottoms to forage on aquatic invertebrates. Presence indicates clean, unpolluted, well-oxygenated stream habitat with cobble substrate. Absence from historically occupied streams signals water quality degradation. Found from sea level to 7,000+ ft along suitable streams, making it the most elevation-spanning indicator species in the zone [G9].

#### Keystone Species

**Belted Kingfisher** (*Megaceryle alcyon*) — Riparian predator present along waterways at all elevations. Excavates nest burrows in exposed earthen banks, creating cavities subsequently used by Bank Swallow and other species. Kingfisher territory quality reflects fish abundance and water clarity; its presence along a waterway indicates functional aquatic food webs.

**Bald Eagle** (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) — Apex riparian predator and scavenger, with PNW populations closely tied to salmon-bearing streams. Breeding pairs concentrate along major rivers (Skagit, Nooksack, Columbia system) and marine shorelines. Winter congregations on spawning salmon streams (Skagit River: 300-700+ eagles; Nooksack: 100-300+) represent the largest Bald Eagle aggregations in the contiguous US outside Alaska. Eagles transport salmon-derived nutrients (marine nitrogen and phosphorus) from stream corridors into adjacent upland forest through prey remains and defecation, functioning as biological nutrient vectors — a direct link to the salmon nutrient pathway described in the ECO ecological-networks module [G1, P11].

### Seasonal Variation

**Spring migration (Mar-May):** The most spectacular avian events in the PNW occur in this zone during spring migration. Up to one million Western Sandpipers stage at Grays Harbor in late April, representing 30-50% of the global population fueling on mudflat invertebrates for the final flight to Alaska breeding grounds. Shorebird diversity peaks with 25+ species on estuarine mudflats. Neotropical migrant songbirds pour into riparian corridors: warblers, vireos, flycatchers, orioles. Waterfowl depart for northern breeding grounds; final Snow Goose flocks leave Skagit Delta in April.

**Breeding season (May-Jul):** Marsh-nesting species (rails, bitterns, blackbirds, grebes, terns) at peak activity. Colonial waterbird nesting on islands (Caspian Tern, American White Pelican, Great Blue Heron rookeries). Osprey and Bald Eagle feeding young. Riparian songbird dawn chorus peaks in June.

**Fall migration (Jul-Nov):** Southbound shorebird migration begins in July (adults) and extends through October (juveniles). Waterfowl staging builds through September-November, peaking at Klamath Basin (historically 1 million+ birds), Malheur, and Skagit. Raptor migration funnels through river valleys. Salmon spawning attracts eagle concentrations to major rivers.

**Winter (Nov-Feb):** Waterfowl and shorebird wintering populations establish. Bald Eagle concentrations peak on salmon streams (November-January). Dunlin and Black-bellied Plover winter on coastal mudflats. Trumpeter Swan and Snow Goose flocks on agricultural-wetland interfaces. Marsh wrens and rails remain in permanent marshes. Winter raptor diversity peaks in open wetland-agricultural zones (Northern Harrier, Short-eared Owl, Rough-legged Hawk).

### Microclimate Effects

**Tidal influence on community structure:** Estuarine zones cycle between exposed mudflat (low tide: shorebird foraging) and inundated habitat (high tide: shorebirds concentrate on roost sites, diving birds forage). Tidal timing controls daily activity patterns and roost-site fidelity. Spring tides expose more mudflat area, supporting larger shorebird concentrations; neap tides compress available foraging habitat. Sea-level rise will shift these dynamics, potentially reducing mudflat exposure during critical migration periods [G15].

**Riparian microclimate buffering:** Riparian corridors through east-side steppe (Zone 7) create thermal and moisture refugia supporting bird communities absent from surrounding arid landscape. The John Day, Deschutes, and Snake River riparian corridors support breeding Yellow-breasted Chat, Bullock's Oriole, and Lewis's Woodpecker in cottonwood galleries embedded in sagebrush steppe. These riparian refugia are more biogeographically significant on the east side, where they represent the only tree-associated bird habitat for miles in any direction.

**Klamath Basin water regime:** The Klamath Basin's importance to Pacific Flyway waterfowl is directly tied to water availability. Historical drainage and diversion for agriculture have reduced wetland area by >75% since the early 1900s. In drought years, reduced water allocation to refuges forces waterfowl to concentrate at diminished water bodies, increasing disease transmission (avian botulism, avian cholera) and reducing habitat quality. The 2001, 2014, and 2021 Klamath water crises illustrate how hydrological decisions directly control bird community outcomes [G1].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Riparian-steppe interface (Zone 5-7 boundary):** The sharpest habitat boundary in the PNW: within 50 meters, habitat transitions from cottonwood-willow gallery to sagebrush steppe. Species turnover is nearly complete across this boundary — Song Sparrow, Yellow Warbler, and Warbling Vireo on the riparian side; Sagebrush Sparrow, Brewer's Sparrow, and Loggerhead Shrike on the steppe side. Only Bullock's Oriole and Lazuli Bunting regularly bridge both habitats.

**Estuary-upland transition:** The interface between estuarine marsh and upland (Zone 3 or Zone 4) supports edge species including Northern Harrier, Short-eared Owl (both hunting the boundary), and Savannah Sparrow (nesting in upper marsh/lower grassland). Sea-level rise threatens to compress this interface against developed upland, eliminating transition habitat.

**Freshwater-saltwater mixing zones:** Where rivers enter estuaries, the salinity gradient creates distinct vegetation zones (freshwater marsh → brackish marsh → salt marsh → mudflat) each with characteristic bird communities. Freshwater marsh supports rails and bitterns; brackish marsh supports Marsh Wren and Swamp Sparrow (rare in PNW); salt marsh supports Savannah Sparrow and Short-eared Owl; mudflat supports shorebirds. This compressed gradient may span <1 km in narrow estuaries.

### Conservation Threats

**Water diversion and drought (CRITICAL — interior):** The Klamath Basin, Malheur Basin, and Columbia Basin irrigation systems divert water from wetland habitat, reducing area and quality available for birds. Drought exacerbates this pressure. The 2021 Klamath drought resulted in near-complete drying of Lower Klamath NWR — a site historically supporting millions of waterfowl — triggering avian botulism outbreaks that killed tens of thousands of birds [G1].

**Sea-level rise (HIGH — coastal):** Projected sea-level rise of 1-4 feet by 2100 threatens to inundate current estuarine and tidal flat habitats. Where shoreline development prevents landward migration of marsh and mudflat habitats (coastal squeeze), habitat is permanently lost. Skagit Delta, Nisqually Delta, and Willapa Bay are all vulnerable [G15].

**Riparian habitat loss (HIGH):** Cottonwood-willow riparian habitat has been reduced by 70-90% across much of the Columbia Basin through dam construction, bank stabilization, agricultural conversion, and water diversion. Species dependent on mature riparian corridors (Yellow-billed Cuckoo — now extirpated from the PNW; Yellow-breasted Chat, Lewis's Woodpecker in cottonwood) have declined proportionally [G1].

**Invasive species (MODERATE-HIGH):** Nutria, invasive marsh vegetation (purple loosestrife, reed canarygrass), and invasive predators (bullfrogs, feral cats) degrade wetland habitat quality. Reed canarygrass monocultures replace diverse native marsh vegetation, reducing invertebrate prey diversity for marsh-nesting birds.

## Zone 6: Coastal / Marine / Pelagic

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** Sea level to offshore waters (continental shelf edge at 100-200 km offshore)
**Geographic extent:** Outer coast from Cape Flattery (WA) to the California border, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Salish Sea (Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, San Juan Islands), offshore to continental shelf edge and beyond
**Dominant features:** Rocky sea stacks and offshore islands, sandy beaches, rocky intertidal, kelp forests (*Nereocystis luetkeana*, *Macrocystis pyrifera*), open ocean, coastal headlands, sea cliffs
**Climate:** Heavy maritime influence, persistent summer fog (coastal stratus), moderate temperatures year-round (summer highs 55-65F on outer coast), persistent onshore wind, high salt exposure
**Key locations:** Oregon Islands NWR (1,853 offshore rocks and islands), Protection Island NWR, Flattery Rocks NWR, Three Arch Rocks NWR, Haystack Rock (Cannon Beach), Cape Meares NWR, Yaquina Head Outstanding Natural Area, Cape Flattery, Westport pelagic staging, San Juan Islands, Deception Pass, Destruction Island

The coastal/marine/pelagic zone is the most physically distinct of the seven zones — water, not land, is the primary habitat. Bird communities here divide into three sub-zones with minimal overlap: (1) the nearshore zone (beaches, rocky intertidal, kelp beds, harbors), (2) the offshore breeding colony zone (sea stacks, offshore islands), and (3) the pelagic zone (open ocean beyond the continental shelf). Each sub-zone supports a specialized avifauna with minimal analog in any terrestrial zone.

The PNW outer coast supports some of the largest seabird colonies in the contiguous United States. Oregon's 1,853 offshore rocks and islands (collectively Oregon Islands NWR) host an estimated 1.2 million nesting seabirds of 15 species — the densest seabird aggregation between Alaska and California [G1].

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**Marbled Murrelet** (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — ESA-threatened. The most ecologically unusual bird in the PNW: a seabird that nests in old-growth forest, sometimes 50+ miles inland. Forages in nearshore marine waters on small fish and invertebrates, then flies at dawn/dusk to nest sites on moss-covered old-growth conifer limbs at canopy level (150-250 ft). This dual-habitat requirement — marine foraging + old-growth nesting — makes it an indicator of both intact marine food webs and old-growth forest connectivity to the coast. Population declining at approximately 3-4% per year in Washington, 1-2% in Oregon (WDFW, ODFW monitoring). Loss of either marine prey (anchovy, sand lance, euphausiid) or old-growth nesting habitat drives decline [G1, G9, P4, P5].

#### Keystone Species

**Common Murre** (*Uria aalge*) — The most abundant breeding seabird on the Oregon coast, with an estimated 700,000+ individuals at peak colony counts. Colonial cliff-nester on offshore sea stacks and islands. Murre colonies function as nutrient pumps: marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus from fish prey) are deposited as guano on nesting rocks, enriching intertidal and subtidal communities. Colony-scale mortality events (oil spills, marine heat waves, prey collapse) cascade through the nearshore ecosystem. The 2014-2016 North Pacific marine heat wave ("the blob") caused an estimated 500,000+ murre deaths across the northeast Pacific, with Oregon colonies losing 40-70% of breeding adults — one of the largest seabird die-offs ever recorded [G1].

### Seasonal Variation

**Spring (Mar-May):** Breeding colony establishment. Murres, auklets, and puffins return to colony rocks. Offshore, trans-equatorial migrants arrive (Sooty Shearwater, Pink-footed Shearwater). Gray Whale migration passes nearshore (March-May). Shorebird migration pulses through coastal estuaries. Brown Pelican post-breeding dispersal from California reaches Oregon coast.

**Summer (Jun-Aug):** Peak colony activity. Chick-rearing at colonies requires abundant forage fish (sand lance, anchovy, smelt, euphausiids). Upwelling-driven productivity peaks in July-August, concentrating prey near the surface. Pelagic waters support millions of Sooty Shearwaters. Tufted Puffin chicks fledge and depart colonies (August). Shorebird return migration begins (adults in July, juveniles in August-September).

**Fall (Sep-Nov):** Colony departure. Seabirds disperse to offshore wintering areas. Sea duck migration into Salish Sea begins (scoters, goldeneyes, Harlequin Duck). Loons and grebes arrive on inland marine waters. Jaegers and skuas pass through offshore. Alcid mortality from fall storms occasionally produces beach-cast specimens.

**Winter (Dec-Feb):** Salish Sea wintering community at peak. Sea duck rafts visible from shore. Loon and grebe concentrations in protected bays. Outer coast relatively quiet except gulls, cormorants, and wintering shorebirds (Dunlin, Black Turnstone, Surfbird on rocky shoreline). Offshore winter storms limit pelagic observation but Short-tailed Albatross occasionally documented.

### Microclimate Effects

**Upwelling:** Seasonal coastal upwelling (April-September) driven by northwest winds brings cold, nutrient-rich deep water to the surface along the outer coast. Upwelling fuels phytoplankton blooms that cascade through the food web to support seabird prey (forage fish, euphausiids). Years of strong upwelling correlate with high seabird reproductive success; weak upwelling or delayed upwelling years produce widespread breeding failure and adult mortality. The 2014-2016 "blob" (marine heat wave) suppressed upwelling and collapsed forage fish populations, causing the largest murre die-off in recorded history [G1, G15].

**El Nino / La Nina cycles:** ENSO variability drives 2-7 year cycles in marine bird community composition. El Nino events bring warmer water, reduced productivity, and prey shifts northward — seabird breeding success drops, adult body condition declines, and southern species (Brown Pelican, Heermann's Gull) appear in unusual numbers. La Nina events bring cooler, more productive water — seabird colonies thrive, and northern species (Ancient Murrelet, Thick-billed Murre) may appear farther south than usual.

**Fog belt:** Persistent summer fog along the outer coast creates a distinct microclimate zone extending 1-5 miles inland. Fog-belt forests support Marbled Murrelet nesting habitat at lower elevation than would otherwise be possible (fog moisture sustains epiphyte loads critical for murrelet nest platforms). Fog also limits visibility for visual-foraging seabirds, potentially shifting foraging patterns.

**Salish Sea thermal stratification:** The semi-enclosed Salish Sea develops thermal stratification in summer, with warm surface layers over cold deep water. This stratification affects prey distribution: herring and sand lance concentrate at thermocline boundaries, attracting diving birds (murrelets, auklets, grebes, loons). Climate warming is strengthening stratification and shifting prey distribution, with documented impacts on wintering grebe populations (Western Grebe decline of 80%+ in southern Salish Sea since the 1990s) [G9].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Marine-terrestrial boundary:** The coastline itself is the defining ecotone. Species that cross this boundary are ecologically unusual. Marbled Murrelet and Bald Eagle are the most important marine-terrestrial link species, moving nutrients and ecological influence between ocean and forest. Murrelet transports marine-derived nutrients to old-growth nest sites (egg material, fecal deposition). Eagles transport salmon carcasses from stream mouths into adjacent forest.

**Nearshore-pelagic transition:** The continental shelf edge (100-200 km offshore) marks a community boundary between nearshore species (murres, cormorants, gulls) and true pelagic species (albatrosses, shearwaters, storm-petrels). Upwelling zones at the shelf break concentrate prey and predators, creating productive ecotones visible in seabird distribution maps.

**Salish Sea-outer coast transition:** The Strait of Juan de Fuca connects the protected Salish Sea to the outer coast. Bird communities shift across this transition: Salish Sea species (Barrow's Goldeneye, Pigeon Guillemot, Glaucous-winged Gull) grade into outer coast species (Common Murre, Tufted Puffin, Western Gull). The San Juan Islands and Strait of Juan de Fuca support a hybrid community with elements of both.

### Conservation Threats

**Marine heat waves and climate change (CRITICAL):** The 2014-2016 northeast Pacific marine heat wave killed an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 Common Murres across the region and caused widespread breeding failure at Oregon and Washington colonies. Marine heat waves are projected to increase in frequency and intensity with climate change, threatening the fundamental productivity of the California Current upwelling ecosystem that supports PNW seabird colonies. Prey species (sand lance, anchovy, euphausiid) are particularly sensitive to temperature shifts [G1, G15].

**Oil spill risk (HIGH):** Major shipping lanes (Strait of Juan de Fuca, Columbia River bar, coastal tanker routes) pass through critical seabird habitat. A major oil spill in the Salish Sea or near Oregon colony rocks could kill hundreds of thousands of seabirds. The 1991 *Tenyo Maru* spill off the Washington coast killed an estimated 7,000+ seabirds. Prevention and response capacity are critical [G9].

**Plastic pollution (MODERATE-HIGH):** Pelagic species (albatrosses, shearwaters, storm-petrels) ingest plastic debris mistaken for prey. Black-footed Albatross populations show high plastic load in stomach contents. Microplastic contamination of forage fish transfers plastic-associated toxins through the food web to seabird predators. Nest material on colony rocks increasingly includes plastic debris [G1].

**Forage fish management (HIGH):** Seabird reproductive success is directly linked to forage fish availability (Pacific sand lance, northern anchovy, Pacific herring, eulachon). Management of commercial fisheries for forage fish stocks directly affects seabird food supply. Oregon has prohibited targeted commercial fishing for forage fish species (precautionary management), but indirect effects of fishing on forage fish prey species and habitat remain concerns.

## Zone 7: East-Side Steppe and Shrubland

### Physical Description

**Elevation:** 500-4,000 ft (east of Cascade crest)
**Geographic extent:** Columbia Plateau (central WA and OR), channeled scablands (eastern WA), Malheur Basin (southeastern OR), Great Basin fringe (south-central OR), Owyhee uplands (SW Idaho), Snake River Plain margins, Yakima Training Center, Hanford Reach
**Dominant vegetation:** Big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) steppe, bitterbrush (*Purshia tridentata*), bunchgrass (bluebunch wheatgrass, Idaho fescue), western juniper (*Juniperus occidentalis*) woodland (expanding), rabbitbrush, greasewood (alkaline basins), irrigated agriculture (grain, potato, orchard)
**Climate:** Continental — hot dry summers (July average 85-95F), cold winters (January average 20-30F), low precipitation (6-15 inches annually), extreme diurnal temperature variation, persistent wind, high UV exposure, frequent drought
**Key locations:** Malheur NWR, Hanford Reach National Monument, Yakima Training Center, Steens Mountain (steppe base), Hart Mountain NAR, Crooked River National Grassland, Columbia NWR, Arid Lands Ecology Reserve, Burns area sagebrush, Alvord Basin, Summer Lake Wildlife Area

The east-side steppe is the PNW's great open landscape — a vast expanse of sagebrush, bunchgrass, and sky that extends from the Cascade rain shadow east to the Rockies. It supports a bird community entirely distinct from the forested west side: species that require open terrain, sparse vegetation, and expansive horizons. Many east-side steppe birds are declining faster than any other guild in North America, making this zone arguably the PNW's most urgent avian conservation priority [P14, G4].

The sagebrush ecosystem is the largest ecosystem type in western North America and one of the most threatened. Conversion to agriculture, juniper encroachment (driven by fire suppression and livestock grazing patterns), invasive annual grasses (cheatgrass, *Bromus tectorum*), and altered fire regimes have eliminated or degraded over 50% of historical sagebrush habitat across the Intermountain West. PNW sagebrush (eastern WA, central/southeastern OR) reflects this pattern [P14].

### Bird Community Composition

#### Indicator Species

**Greater Sage-Grouse** (*Centrocercus urophasianus*) — The iconic sagebrush obligate. Males perform elaborate dawn lek displays on traditional display grounds (leks) from late February through May, inflating yellow air sacs and producing distinctive popping sounds audible at distance. Sage-Grouse require sagebrush at every life stage: nesting under sagebrush canopy, feeding on sagebrush leaves (winter diet is 99% sagebrush), and raising broods in sagebrush-meadow interfaces with forb and insect diversity. Presence reliably indicates intact, large-scale sagebrush ecosystem; absence indicates degradation or fragmentation below viability threshold. Washington populations are critically small (<1,000 birds); Oregon populations are larger but declining. A 2015 USFWS decision found ESA listing "not warranted" contingent on state and federal conservation commitments; listing petitions continue [P14, G1, G9].

#### Keystone Species

**Burrowing Owl** (*Athene cunicularia*) — Small, ground-dwelling owl that nests in abandoned mammal burrows (badger, ground squirrel) in open steppe. Burrowing Owl presence indicates intact grassland-steppe ecosystems with active burrowing mammal communities (the owls depend on mammals to excavate nest burrows — they are secondary cavity nesters of the underground world). Populations declining across the PNW: Washington state endangered, Oregon sensitive. Loss of burrowing mammals (ground squirrel control programs, habitat conversion) eliminates nest sites; loss of open steppe to agriculture or development eliminates foraging habitat [G9, G12].

### Seasonal Variation

**Winter (Nov-Feb):** Sparse community. Raptors dominate the active avifauna. Horned Lark and rosy-finch flocks at agricultural sites. Steppe largely quiet — deep snow covers sagebrush at higher elevations. Sage-Grouse shift to windswept ridges where sagebrush protrudes above snow for winter feeding.

**Spring (Mar-May):** Sage-Grouse lek season (March-May) — the most charismatic wildlife spectacle on the east side. Curlews return to grasslands (March). Swainson's Hawks arrive (April). Sagebrush sparrows and thrashers establish territories (April-May). Shorebird migration through Malheur and Summer Lake basins.

**Summer (Jun-Aug):** Peak breeding. Brewer's Sparrow song pervades sagebrush steppe. Sage-Grouse broods in mesic meadow edges. Burrowing Owl chicks at burrow mouths. Ferruginous Hawk and Prairie Falcon fledging. Long-billed Curlew broods in grassland.

**Fall (Sep-Nov):** Raptor migration through east-side valleys. Swainson's Hawk departs early (September — one of the longest migrations of any North American raptor, to Argentina). Waterfowl staging at Malheur, Summer Lake, Columbia NWR. Sagebrush sparrows and thrashers depart south.

### Microclimate Effects

**Rain shadow dominance:** The entire east-side steppe exists because of the Cascade rain shadow. Precipitation drops from 60-120 inches on the west Cascades slope to 6-15 inches on the east side within 50 miles. This moisture gradient determines the fundamental character of the bird community: forest birds to the west, open-country birds to the east. The transition zone (east Cascade lower slopes, 2,000-3,500 ft) compresses these two faunas into a narrow band where ponderosa pine gives way to sagebrush over 5-10 miles horizontally [P8].

**Thermal extremes and nesting phenology:** Summer temperatures exceeding 100F are regular in Columbia Basin lowlands. Ground-nesting species (curlews, grouse, larks) face heat stress during incubation. Sage-Grouse hens select nest sites under sagebrush canopy that provides 10-20F thermal buffering relative to open ground — loss of sagebrush canopy eliminates this thermal refuge. Cold-air drainage into basins (Malheur, Harney, Warner) creates frost pockets that delay green-up and compress breeding seasons [P14].

**Cheatgrass-fire cycle:** The single most destructive microclimate-vegetation interaction on the east side. Cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*), a Eurasian annual, cures to dry fuel by mid-June — weeks before native bunchgrasses. This creates a 4-6 week extended fire season with abundant fine fuels that carry fire through sagebrush stands. Burned sagebrush is replaced by cheatgrass monoculture (sagebrush requires 25-50 years to reestablish from seed); the cheatgrass burns again within 3-5 years, preventing sagebrush recovery. This positive feedback loop converts sagebrush steppe to annual grassland — habitat devoid of value for sagebrush-obligate birds. An estimated 10,000+ acres of sagebrush are lost annually to this cycle in eastern Oregon alone [P14].

### Edge Effects and Ecotones

**Forest-steppe interface (Zone 2/4-7 boundary):** The transition from ponderosa pine forest to sagebrush steppe is one of the sharpest habitat boundaries in the PNW. Over 1-3 km, the bird community transitions from forest (Western Tanager, Mountain Chickadee, Pygmy Nuthatch) to steppe (Brewer's Sparrow, Sage Thrasher, Sagebrush Sparrow). The interface itself supports species from both communities plus specialists of the transition: Green-tailed Towhee, Gray Flycatcher, and Lazuli Bunting reach highest densities in the pine-sage ecotone.

**Juniper encroachment front:** Western juniper has expanded from approximately 1.5 million acres to 6+ million acres in Oregon since the late 1800s, primarily encroaching into sagebrush steppe. The advancing juniper front creates a moving ecotone where juniper-associated species (Juniper Titmouse, Mountain Bluebird in juniper cavities) replace sagebrush-obligate species (Sage-Grouse, Brewer's Sparrow, Sage Thrasher). Juniper management (cutting/burning) to restore sagebrush is a major BLM and NRCS conservation program.

**Agricultural-steppe interface:** Irrigated agriculture (Columbia Basin Project, Treasure Valley) creates abrupt interfaces between cropland and native steppe. These edges support species exploiting both habitats: Swainson's Hawk (nests in riparian cottonwood, hunts over fields and steppe), Long-billed Curlew (nests in grassland, forages on irrigated fields), and Western Meadowlark (singing from fence posts at the boundary).

**Cliff-steppe interface:** Basalt rimrock (channeled scablands, Snake River canyon, Columbia River basalt cliffs) embedded in steppe provides nest sites for cliff-dependent raptors (Prairie Falcon, Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Great Horned Owl) that hunt over open steppe. These cliff-steppe interfaces support the highest raptor density in the PNW — the Snake River Birds of Prey NCA (just east of the PNW study area in Idaho) hosts the densest nesting raptor population in North America.

### Conservation Threats

**Sagebrush loss (CRITICAL):** The cumulative effects of agricultural conversion, cheatgrass invasion, juniper encroachment, and altered fire regimes have eliminated or degraded over 50% of historical sagebrush in the PNW. Sagebrush-obligate species (Sage-Grouse, Brewer's Sparrow, Sagebrush Sparrow, Sage Thrasher) are declining proportionally. Washington's Greater Sage-Grouse population has declined from an estimated 3,000-5,000 in the 1970s to <1,000, with only two active lek complexes remaining. Without landscape-scale sagebrush restoration, extirpation from Washington is projected within decades [P14, G9].

**Invasive annual grasses (CRITICAL):** Cheatgrass and medusahead (*Taeniatherum caput-medusae*) have invaded millions of acres of PNW steppe, altering fire regimes, eliminating native bunchgrass, and degrading habitat structure for ground-nesting birds. The cheatgrass-fire cycle is self-reinforcing and extremely difficult to reverse at landscape scale. No proven restoration technique can restore sagebrush at the pace of cheatgrass invasion [P14].

**Energy development (HIGH):** Wind energy facilities on the Columbia Plateau cause direct raptor and songbird mortality through turbine strikes. Solar development in the Malheur Basin and central Oregon consumes steppe habitat. Oil and gas exploration is minimal in the PNW steppe but infrastructure (roads, transmission lines, fences) fragments habitat and provides perch sites for avian predators (ravens, raptors) that increase predation pressure on ground-nesting species.

**Climate change (HIGH):** Warming temperatures and reduced snowpack project drier conditions across the east-side steppe. Drought stress on sagebrush reduces canopy cover and seed production, degrading habitat quality. Earlier spring green-up may create phenological mismatches for migratory species (Swainson's Hawk, curlews) timed to historical phenology. Increased fire frequency under warmer, drier conditions accelerates sagebrush loss [G4, G15].

## Cross-Zone Analysis

### Species Richness Gradient

Species richness across PNW ecoregion zones follows a predictable pattern driven by structural complexity, habitat area, and productivity:

| Zone | Est. Breeding Species | Est. Total Species (year-round) | Key Driver |
|------|----------------------|-------------------------------|------------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | 15-25 | 25-35 | Climate severity, habitat area |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | 80-100 | 110-130 | Structural complexity, area |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | 60-80 | 100-140 | Human modification, fragmentation |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | 40-60 | 70-90 | Habitat loss limits realized richness |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | 60-80 | 140-180 | Linear habitat, seasonal pulses |
| Zone 6: Coastal/Marine | 40-60 (colony breeders) | 100-140 | Marine productivity, colony habitat |
| Zone 7: Steppe/Shrubland | 30-50 | 50-70 | Aridity, structural simplicity |

**Peak richness** occurs in the montane conifer zone (Zone 2) for breeding species — reflecting the structural complexity of old-growth forest, which provides the greatest number of microhabitat niches (canopy layers, cavity sites, understory, downed wood, epiphytes). The riparian/wetland zone (Zone 5) achieves the highest total species count when migration and winter staging species are included, reflecting its role as the PNW's primary bird movement corridor.

**Lowest richness** occurs at the environmental extremes: alpine (Zone 1) and steppe (Zone 7), where climatic severity and structural simplicity limit the number of viable niches. However, these depauperate zones support the highest proportion of specialist species — birds found nowhere else in the PNW, making them disproportionately important for regional biodiversity conservation.

### Elevation-Diversity Relationship

Bird species richness in the PNW follows a hump-shaped relationship with elevation, peaking at mid-elevations (1,500-3,000 ft) and declining toward both sea level and alpine summits:

```
Species Richness (relative)

    High  |         ****
          |       **    **
          |      *        *
          |    **          **
    Med   |   *              *
          |  *                *
          | *                  *
    Low   |*                    *
          +-----|-----|-----|-----|---
          0   2,000  5,000  8,000  14,000
                  Elevation (ft)
```

**Below 500 ft:** Moderate richness, reduced by urbanization and agricultural conversion from historical levels. Lowland forest remnants in urban parks can support 80+ species where habitat quality is maintained.

**500-3,000 ft:** Peak richness. This band encompasses the montane conifer zone (old-growth and mature forest), the foothill/oak woodland, and the upper riparian corridors — the three most structurally complex habitats in the PNW. The combination of moderate climate, high structural diversity, and ample water produces maximum habitat heterogeneity.

**3,000-5,000 ft:** Gradually declining richness as climate becomes more severe, growing season shortens, and structural complexity decreases (subalpine forest is less structurally diverse than montane). However, this band still supports 60-80 species, including subalpine specialists absent from lower elevations.

**Above 5,000 ft:** Rapid decline. Alpine habitat supports 15-25 breeding species — highly specialized, cold-adapted, and increasingly threatened by climate change.

**East-side variation:** The east-side elevation-diversity curve is shifted lower. Peak richness occurs at 2,000-3,500 ft (ponderosa pine-mixed conifer zone), with rapid decline into sagebrush steppe below 2,000 ft and into subalpine forest above 5,000 ft. The east-side mid-elevation peak is lower than the west-side peak (60-80 species vs. 80-100) due to lower precipitation and structural complexity.

### Migration Corridors Between Zones

Birds move between zones through four primary corridor types:

**1. Elevational corridors (vertical migration):**
- Alpine → Montane → Lowland: Dusky Grouse (unique reverse: upslope in winter), Townsend's Solitaire, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, Mountain Chickadee, Dark-eyed Junco
- Montane → Lowland: Varied Thrush, Hermit Thrush, Golden-crowned Sparrow, Fox Sparrow
- Pattern: Seasonal compression drives species downslope in winter, upslope in summer

**2. Riparian corridors (horizontal connectivity):**
- Zone 5 (riparian) threads through all terrestrial zones, providing migration highways for neotropical migrants
- Columbia River system connects interior steppe (Zone 7) to coastal estuaries (Zone 6) through 300+ miles of continuous riparian habitat
- Riparian corridors through steppe provide the only tree habitat for hundreds of miles, concentrating migrant songbirds into "migrant traps" at desert oases (Malheur HQ, Page Springs, Frenchglen)

**3. Flyway corridors (latitudinal migration):**
- Pacific Flyway: Primary waterfowl and shorebird migration axis, connecting Arctic breeding grounds to PNW wintering sites via coastal and interior routes
- Coastal route: Shorebirds and seabirds moving along the outer coast (Zone 6) and staging at estuaries (Zone 5)
- Interior route: Raptors, waterfowl, and songbirds moving through east-side valleys (Zone 7) and staging at interior wetlands (Malheur, Klamath, Summer Lake)

**4. Ridge corridors (raptor migration):**
- Cascade ridgeline provides thermal lift for southbound raptors (September-October)
- Chelan Ridge (WA) and Bonney Butte (OR) hawk-watch sites document 3,000-5,000+ raptors annually
- Species: Sharp-shinned Hawk, Cooper's Hawk, Red-tailed Hawk, Turkey Vulture, Golden Eagle, Osprey

### Conservation Priority Ranking

Ranking the seven zones by urgency of avian conservation threats, integrating habitat loss rate, species decline severity, irreversibility of threats, and conservation infrastructure:

| Priority | Zone | Urgency | Rationale |
|----------|------|---------|-----------|
| **1** | Zone 7: Steppe/Shrubland | **CRITICAL** | Fastest habitat loss rate (cheatgrass-fire cycle irreversible at landscape scale); highest proportion of declining species (Sage-Grouse near extirpation in WA); least protected habitat (minimal federal conservation land relative to area); self-reinforcing degradation |
| **2** | Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | **CRITICAL** | >90% historical habitat lost; smallest remaining area of any zone; multiple endemic species at risk; requires active fire management (prescribed burn) to maintain — without intervention, remaining habitat converts to conifer within decades |
| **3** | Zone 2: Montane Conifer | **HIGH** | Flagship species (Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet) declining despite ESA protection; Barred Owl invasion altering community structure irreversibly; old-growth habitat cannot be recreated on human timescales (200+ year development) |
| **4** | Zone 6: Coastal/Marine | **HIGH** | Marine heat waves causing unprecedented seabird mortality; climate-driven prey shifts threatening colony viability; oil spill risk; threats largely outside terrestrial management control |

| **6** | Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | **MODERATE** | Climate change is the primary threat but operates on decadal timescale; habitat is largely protected (National Parks, Wilderness); species have nowhere to go if pushed off mountaintops — "escalator to extinction" |
| **7** | Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | **MODERATE** | Highly modified but adaptable community; invasive species established but native species persist; greatest opportunity for community engagement and urban habitat restoration; some species (Anna's Hummingbird, Cooper's Hawk) actively benefiting from urbanization |

### Key Ecological Concepts Across Zones

**Island biogeography applied to old-growth "islands":** Fragmented old-growth stands in the montane zone (Zone 2) function as habitat islands in a "sea" of managed second-growth. Consistent with MacArthur and Wilson's theory, smaller old-growth patches support fewer area-sensitive species (Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Pileated Woodpecker) than larger patches, with species loss following the species-area curve. The minimum patch size for Spotted Owl territories (~2,000-4,000 acres) represents a critical threshold below which the island cannot sustain a viable population [G6, P19].

**Source-sink dynamics:** Small isolated habitat patches may function as population sinks — areas where mortality exceeds reproduction, maintained only by immigration from larger source patches. This pattern applies to Spotted Owl in fragmented old-growth (Zone 2), Sage-Grouse in fragmented sagebrush (Zone 7), and oak-dependent species in fragmented oak woodland (Zone 4). Conservation strategy must prioritize source habitat protection; sink habitat has limited conservation value without connectivity to sources.

**Minimum viable habitat patch size:** Area-sensitive species require minimum habitat areas:
- Northern Spotted Owl: 2,000-4,000 acres of contiguous late-successional forest [G6]
- Greater Sage-Grouse: 10,000+ acres of unfragmented sagebrush within 10 km of active lek [P14]
- Pileated Woodpecker: 250-1,000 acre territories with large snags [P18]
- Marbled Murrelet: Unknown minimum, but occupancy positively correlated with old-growth patch size and negatively correlated with distance to coast [P4, P5]

**Microclimate refugia:** In a warming climate, topographic features create microclimate refugia — locations where local conditions remain suitable for species even as regional climate shifts. North-facing slopes, deep valleys, fog-influenced coastal strips, and cold-air drainage basins may serve as refugia for climate-sensitive species (ptarmigan, rosy-finch, Varied Thrush). Identifying and protecting these refugia is a proactive conservation strategy for species facing range contraction [G15].

**Edge effects and fragmentation:** Habitat edges expose interior species to predation, competition, and microclimate stress. Brown-headed Cowbird parasitism intensifies within 200 meters of forest edges, reducing reproductive success of forest-interior songbirds. Corvid predation on Marbled Murrelet nests increases near forest edges and clearcuts. Fragmentation creates more edge per unit area, disproportionately harming area-sensitive species while benefiting edge-adapted generalists (Steller's Jay, American Robin, Dark-eyed Junco).
