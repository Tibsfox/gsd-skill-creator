# Module 1D: Passeriformes — Larks through Waxwings

> **PNW Avian Taxonomy — "Wings of the Pacific Northwest"**
>
> This module profiles ~35 passerine species from families Alaudidae through Bombycillidae found in the Pacific Northwest bioregion (Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia). Species are organized by taxonomic family following AOS sequence. Each profile follows the standardized template from `00-taxonomy-schema.md` and references sources from `00-source-index.md`.
>
> **Design principle:** These families span a remarkable range of ecological niches — from the alpine tundra larks to the aquatic dipper, from aerial-foraging swallows to bark-gleaning creepers. Several species in this module serve as sensitive indicators of habitat quality: the American Dipper for stream health, the Varied Thrush for old-growth forest integrity, the Purple Martin for snag cavity availability, and the Streaked Horned Lark for native prairie persistence.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Every Indigenous reference names a specific nation.

## 1. Family Alaudidae — Larks

The larks are a predominantly Old World family with a single native representative in North America. In the PNW, Horned Larks occupy open, barren ground from alpine tundra to lowland prairies — habitats that are among the most imperiled in the region. The Streaked Horned Lark (*Eremophila alpestris strigata*), endemic to the Pacific lowland prairies, is federally listed as Threatened and represents one of the most acute conservation challenges in PNW ornithology.

---

### Horned Lark (*Eremophila alpestris*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Alaudidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (varies by subspecies and elevation)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (species level); Threatened (*E. a. strigata* — Streaked Horned Lark DPS)
- IUCN: Least Concern (species level)
- WA: Candidate (*strigata* subspecies)
- OR: Sensitive-Critical (*strigata* subspecies)
- PIF: Common Bird in Steep Decline (species level)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16-20 cm
- Wingspan: 31-35 cm
- Mass: 28-48 g
- Plumage: Sandy-brown upperparts, pale underparts, distinctive black breast band and facial mask, yellow to white face and throat, small black "horns" (feather tufts) erected during display. Females duller with less distinct horns. Juveniles lack facial pattern.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males slightly larger with bolder facial markings and more prominent horns

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Barren or sparsely vegetated open ground — alpine tundra, glacial outwash, dry grasslands, agricultural fields, airfields, coastal dune margins
- Wintering: Similar open habitats at lower elevations; agricultural stubble, plowed fields, coastal flats
- Elevation range: Sea level to 10,000+ ft (subspecies-dependent)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine) for alpine subspecies; Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak) and Zone 7 (Steppe) for lowland subspecies
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 6 (Coastal — dune areas)
- Microhabitat: Requires ground with <50% vegetation cover; avoids tall grass and shrub encroachment

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds and insects; proportions vary seasonally
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager, walking and picking from soil surface and low vegetation
- Seasonal variation: Summer — predominantly insects (beetles, grasshoppers, caterpillars); winter — seeds (grass, forb, grain)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape, often adjacent to a dirt clod, rock, or tuft of grass; lined with fine grass, plant fibers, sometimes animal fur
- Nest location: On bare or sparsely vegetated ground; female selects site
- Clutch size: 2-5 (typically 3-4)
- Incubation: 10-14 days (female only)
- Fledging: 8-10 days (leave nest before capable of sustained flight)
- Broods per year: 1-3; double-brooding common in lowland populations
- Breeding season: April-July (lowland); June-August (alpine)

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show -1.5% annual decline range-wide, steeper declines in PNW lowland populations [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~120 million (PIF 2020); PNW lowland (*strigata*) populations critically low
- Threats: Habitat loss (native prairie conversion), agricultural intensification, grassland fragmentation, invasive grasses reducing bare ground, fire suppression allowing shrub encroachment, predation by feral cats and corvids at lowland sites
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: High, thin, tinkling flight song delivered from circling display flight at 200-800 ft altitude; a long series of sibilant notes *tsee-titi, tsee-titi, tsee-titi* lasting up to several minutes
- Calls: Thin *tsee-ee* or *tsee-titi* flight call; sharp *tseep* alarm note
- Dawn song: Song flights begin before dawn during breeding season, among the earliest singers in open habitats

**Evolutionary Note:** *Eremophila alpestris* is the only New World representative of the predominantly Palearctic family Alaudidae. The species exhibits extraordinary subspecific variation — over 40 subspecies described worldwide, at least 6-8 in western North America. PNW populations include alpine (*arcticola* and similar forms), east-side steppe (*merrilli*), and the critically imperiled Pacific lowland *strigata*. Molecular work suggests the Horned Lark complex may represent multiple species; ongoing genomic analysis may lead to future splits [O3].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Breeding | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland (*strigata*) | Year-round | Rare | Indicator (prairie health) |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding/resident | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 7: Steppe | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G1] USFWS ESA listing; [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW; [O3] AOS; [O4] PIF; [P15] Pearson & Altman 2005; [P16] Pyle 1997

### Streaked Horned Lark (*Eremophila alpestris strigata*) — ESA Threatened

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Alaudidae (subspecies of Horned Lark)
**Status:** Resident / breeding — ESA Threatened
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Threatened (listed October 2013; critical habitat designated 2020)
- IUCN: Not separately assessed (LC at species level)
- WA: Endangered
- OR: Sensitive-Critical
- PIF: Not separately scored; parent species Common Bird in Steep Decline

The Streaked Horned Lark warrants extended treatment as the only federally ESA-listed passerine in this module and one of the most imperiled birds in the PNW.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16-18 cm (at smaller end of Horned Lark range)
- Wingspan: 31-33 cm
- Mass: 28-37 g
- Plumage: Distinguished from other PNW Horned Lark subspecies by more heavily streaked breast (extending below the black breast band), yellow-tinged underparts, brighter yellow face and throat, smaller size. These distinctions are subtle and require careful observation; definitive separation from migrant *merrilli* and *arcticola* requires breeding-season identification at known sites.
- Sexual dimorphism: As in other Horned Larks; males more boldly marked

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Native grasslands with extensive bare ground: coastal sand spits, glacial outwash prairies (south Puget Sound), grass-lined airport fields, agricultural fallow; requires <35% vegetation cover and patches of bare soil
- Wintering: Coastal sand flats, estuarine mudflat margins, plowed agricultural fields; largely resident with local movements
- Elevation range: Sea level to 500 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland — remnant prairies)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — Willamette Valley), Zone 6 (Coastal — sand spits)
- Microhabitat: Highly specific — requires short-stature prairie with bare ground patches. Vegetation height >30 cm and cover >65% renders habitat unsuitable. This microhabitat specificity is the root cause of the subspecies' decline.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Seeds and insects, as in other Horned Larks
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager on bare or sparsely vegetated soil
- Seasonal variation: Insects predominate during breeding season; seeds in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape, typically adjacent to a small object (dirt clod, rock, dried cow pat)
- Nest location: On bare ground in native prairie, airport grassland, or dredge-material islands
- Clutch size: 2-4 (typically 3)
- Incubation: 11-12 days
- Fledging: 8-10 days
- Broods per year: 1-2; renesting after failure common
- Breeding season: March-July (earlier than alpine subspecies)
- Nest success: Highly variable; 20-50% at monitored sites. Major nest predators include American Crow, Common Raven, garter snakes, and feral cats [G9]

**Population:**
- Trend: Severely declining — estimated 90%+ decline from historical abundance
- Estimate: 1,200-1,600 individuals range-wide as of USFWS 2013 listing decision. Washington population concentrated at a small number of sites in the south Puget Sound lowlands (Joint Base Lewis-McChord prairies, Olympia Airport, scattered coastal sites). Oregon population primarily in the Willamette Valley (agricultural fallow, airports) and along the lower Columbia River (dredge-material islands). **No specific site coordinates provided per safety boundary — locations described at county/region level only.**
- Threats: Native prairie loss (>90% of south Puget Sound prairies and >99% of Willamette Valley prairies have been converted since European settlement); invasive grass encroachment (Scotch broom, tall fescue) eliminating bare-ground microhabitat; urban/suburban development; predation; airport management conflicts (mowing schedules vs. nesting timing); vegetation succession on dredge-material islands
- Source: USFWS [G1], Pearson & Altman 2005 [P15], WDFW [G9]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: As in other Horned Larks; thin tinkling flight song. No confirmed vocal differences from other PNW subspecies.
- Calls: Standard Horned Lark repertoire

**Evolutionary Note:** The *strigata* subspecies is distinguished from other western Horned Lark subspecies primarily by plumage (heavier breast streaking, yellow wash) and by geography — it is the only Horned Lark breeding in the Pacific lowlands west of the Cascades from southern BC to the Willamette Valley. Genetic analysis confirms *strigata* as a diagnosable evolutionary unit, supporting its recognition as a listable entity under the ESA [P20]. The subspecies likely differentiated in Pleistocene refugia in the Willamette Valley and Puget lowland prairies. Its obligate association with native prairie habitat makes it a flagship for Pacific lowland grassland conservation — its survival is functionally inseparable from the survival of the prairie ecosystem itself.

**Conservation Context:** The Streaked Horned Lark's decline mirrors the catastrophic loss of PNW native prairies. The south Puget Sound prairies that support the Washington population are maintained by the military training mission at Joint Base Lewis-McChord — an ironic conservation partnership where artillery training and controlled burns maintain the open, disturbed ground conditions the lark requires. Active management including prescribed fire, invasive species removal, and mowing schedule coordination with nesting phenology is critical at all known breeding sites [G1, G9, P15].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland | Year-round | Rare | Indicator (native prairie health) |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak (Willamette) | Breeding | Rare | Indicator |
| Zone 6: Coastal (sand spits) | Year-round | Rare | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G1] USFWS ESA listing and critical habitat designation; [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW; [G12] ODFW Conservation Strategy; [O4] PIF; [P15] Pearson & Altman 2005; [P20] Haig et al. 2004

## 2. Family Hirundinidae — Swallows and Martins

Swallows are aerial insectivores supremely adapted for life on the wing — long, pointed wings, wide gapes, and short legs built for perching rather than walking. The PNW hosts 7 regularly breeding species spanning a diversity of nesting strategies: cavity nesters (Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Purple Martin), mud-nest builders (Barn Swallow, Cliff Swallow), and burrow excavators (Bank Swallow, Northern Rough-winged Swallow). All are Neotropical or temperate migrants, arriving in spring and departing by September-October. Swallow abundance is tightly linked to flying insect availability, making these species sensitive indicators of insect population health and, by extension, pesticide loading and climate-driven phenological shifts.

---

### Tree Swallow (*Tachycineta bicolor*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Common Bird in Steep Decline (declining despite broad range)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-15 cm
- Wingspan: 30-35 cm
- Mass: 17-25 g
- Plumage: Males iridescent blue-green above, clean white below; females duller with brownish-green upperparts, especially in first breeding year. Juveniles grayish-brown above with faint breast band.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males brighter; first-year females distinguishable by brown plumage

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open areas near water — marshes, lake edges, flooded meadows, riparian corridors, agricultural fields with scattered snags or nest boxes
- Wintering: Southern US, Mexico, Central America; concentrates in coastal marshes and agricultural areas
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 4 (Foothill)
- Microhabitat: Requires cavity near open foraging area; readily accepts nest boxes

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — flies, beetles, mayflies, caddisflies, dragonflies
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking over open water and fields; flight agile with frequent turns; forages closer to ground than Violet-green Swallow
- Seasonal variation: Supplements insect diet with berries (bayberry, wax myrtle) in fall and winter — unique among North American swallows

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — natural woodpecker holes, nest boxes, occasionally building crevices
- Nest location: In cavity near open water or fields; lines nest with feathers (preferentially white feathers; feather-lining behavior is well-studied)
- Clutch size: 4-7 (typically 5-6)
- Incubation: 13-16 days (female only)
- Fledging: 18-22 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show significant decline across North America, particularly steep in the Northeast; PNW trends variable but generally negative since the 1990s [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~17 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Flying insect decline (neonicotinoid pesticides, climate-driven phenological mismatch), cavity loss (snag removal, competition with House Sparrows and European Starlings), agricultural intensification reducing foraging habitat
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Liquid, chattering *weet, trit, weet* — rapid twitter of clear notes; less complex than warbler songs
- Calls: Sharp *cheep* contact call; buzzy *bzeer* alarm

**Evolutionary Note:** Genus *Tachycineta* is a New World radiation of 9 species. Tree Swallows are the most northern-breeding member, reaching the boreal forest edge. Their ability to switch to fruit (bayberry) in cold weather is a derived trait that allows earlier spring arrival and later fall departure than strictly insectivorous swallows, expanding their breeding range northward.

| Zone 4: Foothill | Breeding | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 7: Steppe (riparian) | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |

### Violet-green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 28-32 cm
- Mass: 13-18 g
- Plumage: Males brilliant iridescent green above with violet rump and uppertail coverts; white below, with white extending up onto rump sides and above the eye (white facial patches diagnostic — distinguish from Tree Swallow). Females duller greenish-bronze.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males brighter; females with less extensive white facial patches

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open forest, forest edges, cliffs, urban areas with suitable cavities; less tied to water than Tree Swallow
- Wintering: Mexico, Central America
- Elevation range: Sea level to 8,000 ft; higher-elevation forager than Tree Swallow
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 1 (Alpine — foraging)
- Microhabitat: Cliffs, building eaves, tree cavities, nest boxes; forages at higher altitudes than Tree Swallow

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — often at greater height than other PNW swallows
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking, typically higher and more buoyant flight than Tree Swallow; often forages near cliff faces
- Seasonal variation: Insects year-round; does not supplement with berries

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — natural crevices, cliffs, woodpecker holes, building crevices, nest boxes
- Nest location: Often in cliff faces, rock crevices, or building eaves; colonial or semi-colonial at cliff sites
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 13-15 days (female only)
- Fledging: 23-25 days (longer than Tree Swallow)
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to slightly declining in some areas [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~7 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Cavity competition (starlings, House Sparrows), building renovation eliminating nest crevices, flying insect decline
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rapid, twittering *chee-chee-chee-chee*; thinner and higher than Tree Swallow
- Calls: Thin *tsip* contact note

**Evolutionary Note:** *Tachycineta thalassina* is the sister species to the Tree Swallow, having diverged ~3-4 million years ago. The two species show classic niche partitioning in sympatry: Violet-greens forage at greater heights, use cliff/rock cavities more often, and range to higher elevations. Where both species use nest boxes, Violet-greens typically arrive later and occupy the higher-elevation boxes.

| Zone 4: Foothill | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

### Barn Swallow (*Hirundo rustica*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Common Bird in Steep Decline

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15-19 cm (including deeply forked tail)
- Wingspan: 29-32 cm
- Mass: 17-20 g
- Plumage: Deep steel-blue above; rufous forehead and throat; pale to rich buffy-orange underparts; long, deeply forked tail with white spots (tail fork deeper in males). North American birds (*H. r. erythrogaster*) have paler underparts than Eurasian forms.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males with longer tail streamers, deeper fork, richer throat color

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open agricultural land, farmyards, bridges, highway overpasses, culverts — strongly associated with human structures for nesting
- Wintering: South America (as far as central Argentina)
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 7 (Steppe — farmsteads)
- Microhabitat: Requires sheltered vertical surface for mud nest; barns, bridges, and culverts now constitute >99% of nest sites in PNW

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — flies, beetles, wasps, moths
- Foraging strategy: Low, agile aerial hawking, typically within 10 m of ground over fields, water, and livestock; follows mowers and livestock to catch disturbed insects
- Seasonal variation: Insects throughout breeding season; no fruit supplementation

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of mud pellets mixed with grass, lined with feathers
- Nest location: Attached to vertical surface under overhang — barn rafters, bridge undersides, culverts, porch ceilings
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 13-17 days (both sexes, predominantly female)
- Fledging: 18-23 days
- Broods per year: 1-2 (frequently double-brooded in PNW lowlands)
- Breeding season: April-August

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show -1.3% annual decline in North America; PNW declines consistent with continental trend [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~37 million; most widespread swallow globally
- Threats: Agricultural intensification (loss of traditional barns, dairy farm closures), flying insect decline, nest destruction during building renovation, climate-driven phenological mismatch between arrival and insect emergence
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Pleasant, rapid twittering warble interspersed with scratchy rattles; sung from perch or in flight
- Calls: Sharp *kwit* alarm; *vit-vit* contact call in flight

**Evolutionary Note:** *Hirundo rustica* is the most widespread swallow species on Earth, breeding on every continent except Antarctica and Australia. The global radiation from an African ancestral stock likely occurred within the last 1-2 million years. North American Barn Swallows were historically scarce, nesting on cliff faces and in caves; the species explosively expanded following European colonization, which provided barns, bridges, and other artificial nest structures. This is one of the clearest examples of a species benefiting from human landscape modification — though the same agricultural economy now threatens it through modernization.

| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 7: Steppe | Breeding | Common (farmsteads) | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

### Cliff Swallow (*Petrochelidon pyrrhonota*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 13-15 cm
- Wingspan: 28-30 cm
- Mass: 19-34 g
- Plumage: Dark blue back, pale rufous rump (diagnostic in flight), white forehead patch, dark chestnut throat, buffy collar; square tail (contrasts with Barn Swallow's forked tail). Juveniles duller with dark forehead.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open areas near vertical surfaces — highway overpasses, bridges, dam faces, barn exteriors, natural cliff faces; strongly colonial
- Wintering: South America (Paraguay to Argentina)
- Elevation range: Sea level to 6,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 7 (Steppe)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian — bridge colonies)
- Microhabitat: Requires sheltered overhang for gourd-shaped mud nest; colony sites reused for decades

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — beetles, flies, winged ants, mayflies
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking, typically higher than Barn Swallow; often in large flocks; forages ahead of storm fronts
- Seasonal variation: Insects throughout breeding season

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Enclosed gourd-shaped mud nest with narrow entrance tunnel
- Nest location: Clustered on vertical surfaces — colonies from 2 to 3,000+ nests; bridge undersides, dam faces, building eaves
- Clutch size: 3-6 (typically 4)
- Incubation: 12-14 days
- Fledging: 21-24 days
- Broods per year: 1 (occasionally 2)
- Breeding season: May-August
- Colony dynamics: Intraspecific brood parasitism common — females lay eggs in neighbors' nests; egg-tossing behavior (ejecting foreign eggs) documented as counter-strategy

**Population:**
- Trend: Variable — increased historically with bridge and building construction; some recent regional declines linked to House Sparrow nest parasitism [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~80 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: House Sparrow nest usurpation at colony sites, bridge maintenance destroying nest structures, pesticide reduction of flying insects
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Low, squeaky chattering *chur, chur, chur*; colony chorus is a distinctive soundscape
- Calls: Nasal *nyeer* alarm; *churt* contact note

**Evolutionary Note:** Cliff Swallows, like Barn Swallows, expanded dramatically following European colonization — bridges and highway overpasses provided vast new nest substrate. Charles Brown's long-term study colony at sites in Nebraska (spanning 35+ years) has documented rapid microevolution in response to road mortality: road-killed Cliff Swallows have longer wings on average, while the living population has evolved shorter, more maneuverable wings over the same period — an example of natural selection observable within human timescales.

| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 7: Steppe | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

### Bank Swallow (*Riparia riparia*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Candidate (state-level concern due to declining trend)
- PIF: Common Bird in Steep Decline

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 25-29 cm
- Mass: 12-18 g (smallest PNW swallow)
- Plumage: Brown above, white below with distinct brown breast band; no iridescence. Plain, contrasting with the bright plumages of Tree and Violet-green Swallows.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Vertical sandy or earthen banks — riverbanks, gravel pits, road cuts, lakeshores with eroded bluffs; strictly colonial, requiring friable soil for burrow excavation
- Wintering: South America
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — gravel pits), Zone 7 (Steppe — river bluffs)
- Microhabitat: Requires vertical earth banks >1 m tall with fine-grained soil suitable for burrow excavation

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects, especially small flies, mayflies, and caddisflies over water
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking, typically low over water surfaces; erratic, fluttering flight
- Seasonal variation: Insects throughout

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Burrow excavated in earthen bank — horizontal tunnel 45-90 cm deep ending in nest chamber
- Nest location: In colony of 10-2,000+ burrows; both sexes excavate
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 14-16 days (both sexes)
- Fledging: 18-22 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show -3-5% annual decline in some PNW areas; loss of nesting banks is primary driver [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~26 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Bank stabilization (riprap) eliminating natural nesting banks; flood-control structures preventing natural erosion that creates new bank habitat; gravel pit reclamation; human disturbance at colony sites
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], WDFW [G9]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Dry, buzzy twittering — less musical than Tree Swallow
- Calls: Harsh *brrit* or *tsrrt*

**Evolutionary Note:** *Riparia riparia* (Sand Martin in Old World nomenclature) is the most cosmopolitan swallow, breeding across the Holarctic on five continents. Its dependence on erosion-maintained banks links it directly to river geomorphology — any modification of natural river dynamics (damming, channelization, bank armoring) reduces nesting habitat. The species' colonialism concentrates populations at a small number of suitable sites, making it vulnerable to localized habitat loss.

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Locally common | Indicator (bank habitat integrity) |
| Zone 3: Lowland | Breeding | Uncommon | Visitor |
| Zone 7: Steppe | Breeding | Locally common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### Northern Rough-winged Swallow (*Stelgidopteryx serripennis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 27-30 cm
- Mass: 15-18 g
- Plumage: Dusky brown above, dingy whitish below with diffuse brownish wash across throat and breast (no clean breast band — distinguish from Bank Swallow). Plain, nondescript.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Banks, road cuts, bridge crevices, drain pipes, rocky outcrops — uses a broader range of nest sites than Bank Swallow; solitary or in small loose groups rather than large colonies
- Wintering: Southern US, Mexico, Central America
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 4 (Foothill)

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking, often low over water; flight slower and more direct than Tree Swallow
- Seasonal variation: Insects throughout

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Natural or appropriated cavity — uses abandoned Bank Swallow burrows, drain pipes, rock crevices, bridge expansion joints
- Nest location: In bank, structure, or rock face; not strongly colonial
- Clutch size: 5-7 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 15-16 days (female)
- Fledging: 19-21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~16 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Similar to Bank Swallow but less vulnerable due to broader nest-site tolerance
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rough, buzzy *brrit* — less musical than other swallows
- Calls: Low *brrt* contact note

**Evolutionary Note:** The name *serripennis* ("saw-feathered") refers to tiny recurved hooks on the leading edge of the outer primary feathers — unique among North American passerines. The function of these barbs remains debated; hypotheses include sound production during courtship, improved aerodynamics, or phylogenetic vestige with no current function.

### Purple Martin (*Progne subis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Hirundinidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant — declining, conservation concern
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (global)
- WA: Candidate
- OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable
- BC: Blue-listed (Special Concern)
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (western populations)

The Purple Martin warrants extended treatment as the largest North American swallow and a species of significant conservation concern in the PNW, where western populations have declined severely and depend increasingly on artificial nest structures.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 19-20 cm
- Wingspan: 39-41 cm
- Mass: 50-60 g (largest North American swallow — roughly 3x the mass of Tree Swallow)
- Plumage: Males entirely glossy blue-black (only PNW swallow lacking white underparts). Females and subadult males dusky grayish-brown above, whitish below with grayish breast band and mottled throat; no iridescence.
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced — male's all-dark plumage unique; females dull and cryptic. Males do not achieve full adult plumage until second year.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open areas near water with suitable cavities — western populations historically used woodpecker cavities in large snags, especially in marine/riverine settings; now largely dependent on nest boxes and pilings over water
- Wintering: South America (Amazonian Brazil — specific wintering sites poorly known)
- Elevation range: Sea level to 500 ft (PNW populations strongly coastal/lowland)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Microhabitat: Requires large cavities near open foraging areas, preferably over or adjacent to water. Western populations are unique among North American Purple Martins in retaining the ancestral (pre-colonial) nesting ecology of using natural and woodpecker cavities in trees and pilings, rather than the colonial gourd/house nesting of eastern populations.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Flying insects — dragonflies, beetles, wasps, flying ants, winged termites; larger prey items than other PNW swallows, consistent with larger body size
- Foraging strategy: Aerial hawking at medium to high altitudes (higher than Barn or Tree Swallow); capable of sustained soaring flight; often feeds in mixed-species swallow flocks
- Seasonal variation: Insects throughout breeding season

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — natural tree cavities, woodpecker holes in pilings and snags, and (increasingly) purpose-built nest boxes and gourd clusters
- Nest location: Historically in large snags near water; now primarily in nest boxes mounted on pilings over marine or freshwater environments. Colony sizes in PNW range from 2-50 pairs (much smaller than eastern US colonies of hundreds).
- Clutch size: 3-6 (typically 4-5)
- Incubation: 15-18 days (female only)
- Fledging: 26-31 days (long nestling period)
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-August (among latest-arriving PNW migrants — typically mid-May)

**Population:**
- Trend: Severely declining in western North America. PNW populations crashed through the 20th century; WA estimated at fewer than 600 breeding pairs as of early 2000s, recovering slowly to ~800-1,000 pairs by 2020s through intensive nest box programs. Oregon populations similarly small. BC populations critically low. Eastern North American populations stable or increasing with abundant artificial housing.
- Estimate: Continental population ~7 million (PIF 2020), but western populations represent a tiny fraction
- Threats: Loss of large snags and pilings (historic nest sites removed during harbor modernization, waterfront development); European Starling competition for cavities (starlings arrive earlier in spring and usurp martin cavities before martins return from South America); House Sparrow nest parasitism; declining flying insect populations; limited knowledge of wintering ecology hampers full life-cycle conservation
- Source: WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], PIF [O4], BC Blue List [G14]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rich, gurgling *pew-pew, chur-chur*; lower and more musical than smaller swallows; males sing from perch near colony
- Calls: Descending *cheer* alarm; *zweet* contact note; dawn song a sustained gurgling sequence

**Evolutionary Note:** *Progne subis* is a member of a Neotropical radiation of large swallows (genus *Progne* — 9 species). The species shows a striking east-west behavioral divergence in North America: eastern populations are colonial in multi-compartment nest houses provided by humans (a tradition with roots in Indigenous management — southeastern Native nations hung gourds for martins, which may predate European contact by centuries); western populations retain the ancestral behavior of nesting solitarily or semi-colonially in natural cavities. This behavioral divergence has profound conservation implications — eastern populations have a human-subsidized housing supply, while western populations face a housing crisis driven by snag and piling removal.

**Conservation Context:** Purple Martin recovery in the PNW is an active, hands-on conservation effort. WDFW, the Purple Martin Conservation Association, local Audubon chapters, and volunteer networks install and monitor hundreds of nest boxes throughout western Washington and Oregon. Boxes are mounted on pilings in marine environments (Puget Sound marinas, Columbia River), on poles near lakes and wetlands, and on bridge structures. Annual monitoring tracks occupancy, productivity, and starling competition. Nest box programs at Fort Lewis/JBLM, Woodland (WA), and several Willamette Valley sites have been key to stabilizing populations. The management model — purpose-built housing with starling exclusion (restrictor plates on entrance holes sized to admit martins but exclude starlings) — has proven effective where sustained volunteer effort maintains the infrastructure [G9, G12].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland | Breeding | Uncommon | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 6: Coastal/Marine | Breeding | Uncommon | Indicator (snag/piling cavity availability) |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW Candidate species; [G12] ODFW Conservation Strategy; [G14] BC Blue List; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

## 3. Family Paridae — Chickadees and Titmice

Chickadees are among the most familiar and best-studied songbirds in North America. Small, acrobatic, and socially complex, they maintain winter flocks with linear dominance hierarchies, cache food in thousands of sites (requiring spatial memory feats studied extensively in cognitive ecology), and produce one of the most information-rich call systems of any passerine. The PNW hosts 4 chickadee species whose distributions map closely onto the region's major habitat zones, making them excellent indicators of forest type and elevation.

---

### Black-capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Paridae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-15 cm
- Wingspan: 16-21 cm
- Mass: 9-14 g
- Plumage: Black cap and bib, white cheeks, gray back, buffy flanks, whitish underparts. Wing feathers edged white. Short, stout bill.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal — sexes alike

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Deciduous and mixed forest, riparian woodland, urban parks and gardens, forest edges
- Wintering: Same; joins mixed-species flocks with nuthatches, kinglets, creepers, and Downy Woodpeckers
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,000 ft (replaced at higher elevations by Mountain Chickadee)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland Forest/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Microhabitat: Requires trees for foraging and cavities for nesting; thrives in edge habitats and suburban settings

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and spiders (summer); seeds and cached food (winter). Seeds constitute up to 50% of winter diet.
- Foraging strategy: Active gleaner — hangs from branches, probes bark crevices, forages at all canopy levels. Caches thousands of food items in fall; relies on hippocampal spatial memory to retrieve caches — chickadee hippocampus is proportionally larger than non-caching passerines and undergoes annual neurogenesis.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous in summer, shifts to seeds (sunflower, birch, alder catkins) and cached items in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — excavates own cavity in soft, rotten wood; also uses natural cavities, nest boxes
- Nest location: In dead snag or rotting branch, typically 1-5 m up
- Clutch size: 6-8 (among the largest clutches of any PNW passerine)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (female only; male feeds female on nest)
- Fledging: 14-18 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-June

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~41 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Minimal at population level; snag removal reduces nesting sites; window strikes; cat predation
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Clear, whistled *fee-bee* or *fee-bee-ee* (2-3 notes, first note higher); one of the most recognizable bird songs in the PNW
- Calls: The *chick-a-dee-dee-dee* call is one of the most information-rich calls in the avian world — the number of *dee* notes at the end scales with predator threat level. Small, agile raptors (Sharp-shinned Hawk, Northern Pygmy-Owl) elicit more *dee* notes than large, less maneuverable predators. Mixed-species flocks respond to this graded alarm system.
- Dawn song: *Fee-bee* song begins at first light

**Evolutionary Note:** *Poecile atricapillus* forms a narrow hybrid zone with Carolina Chickadee (*P. carolinensis*) in the eastern US; no hybridization issue in the PNW. In the PNW, Black-capped Chickadee is parapatric with Chestnut-backed Chickadee (overlapping broadly in lowland western WA/OR/BC) and with Mountain Chickadee (elevational replacement in montane forest). Cognitive research on chickadee food caching has been foundational for understanding avian spatial memory and hippocampal neurogenesis — individual chickadees can cache and retrieve 80,000+ food items annually.

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

### Mountain Chickadee (*Poecile gambeli*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Paridae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 19-21 cm
- Mass: 10-14 g
- Plumage: Black cap and bib, white cheeks, gray back and flanks; distinguished from Black-capped by white supercilium (eyebrow stripe) bisecting the black cap — diagnostic field mark. Less buffy on flanks.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Montane conifer forest — ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, lodgepole pine, subalpine fir; replaces Black-capped Chickadee at higher elevations
- Wintering: Largely resident; some downslope movement in severe winters; occasional irruption to lowland feeders
- Elevation range: 2,500-8,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Subalpine — tree islands), Zone 4 (Foothill — east-side ponderosa)
- Microhabitat: Coniferous forest; prefers open to moderately dense stands

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, conifer seeds; extensive food caching like Black-capped Chickadee
- Foraging strategy: Bark gleaner and twig prober; forages higher in canopy than Black-capped; joins mixed-species flocks with Red-breasted Nuthatch, Brown Creeper, and Golden-crowned Kinglet
- Seasonal variation: Insects in summer; conifer seeds and cached items in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — uses existing cavities and nest boxes; less likely to excavate own cavity than Black-capped
- Nest location: In conifer snag or dead branch, 1-10 m up
- Clutch size: 5-9 (typically 7-8)
- Incubation: 14 days (female)
- Fledging: 20-21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~12 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Conifer forest loss; high-severity fire at landscape scale; climate change potentially shifting elevational range upward and compressing habitat
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Whistled *fee-bee-bay* or *fee-bee-fee-bee*; more variable and slightly huskier than Black-capped Chickadee
- Calls: Hoarser *chick-a-dee-dee-dee* than Black-capped; nasal quality useful for distinguishing the two species by ear

**Evolutionary Note:** Mountain Chickadee shows population genetic structure correlated with mountain ranges in the west — Cascade, Rocky Mountain, and Great Basin populations are partially differentiated. This montane-island distribution pattern mirrors that of other high-elevation PNW residents (Clark's Nutcracker, White-tailed Ptarmigan) and reflects Pleistocene glacial fragmentation of mountain habitats. Mountain Chickadees that cache more food in fall have larger hippocampi and better spatial memory — a within-species demonstration of the relationship between ecology and brain structure.

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill (east-side) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Paridae
**Status:** Resident — PNW specialist
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 11-13 cm
- Wingspan: 18-20 cm
- Mass: 8-12 g (smallest PNW chickadee)
- Plumage: Sooty-brown cap, white cheeks, rich chestnut back and flanks — the most colorful PNW chickadee. Underparts white with chestnut flanks (flanks less rufous in some BC populations). Small bill.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Moist conifer and mixed forest — western red cedar, Sitka spruce, Douglas-fir, western hemlock; strongly associated with wet west-side forests from sea level to montane
- Wintering: Resident; occasional wandering but generally sedentary
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,000 ft (west side); replaced by Mountain Chickadee on east side and at higher elevations
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — west slope) and Zone 3 (Lowland Forest)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 6 (Coastal forest fringe)
- Microhabitat: Dense, moist conifer forest; tends to forage lower and in denser cover than Black-capped or Mountain Chickadee

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, seeds; forages heavily on arthropods gleaned from conifer foliage and bark
- Foraging strategy: Active foliage gleaner, specializing in dense conifer canopy; joins mixed-species flocks but tends to forage at lower heights
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous emphasis year-round; less reliance on seed caching than Black-capped or Mountain Chickadee

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — excavates in soft rotten wood; uses nest boxes
- Nest location: In conifer or deciduous snag, typically 1-5 m up
- Clutch size: 5-8 (typically 6-7)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (female)
- Fledging: 18-21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-June

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable; range expanding slightly northward in BC [G4, G14]
- Estimate: Continental population ~9 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth loss reduces preferred habitat; range limited by moisture — dry east-side forests exclude this species
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Higher, thinner *tsee-dee-dee*; lacks the clear whistled *fee-bee* of Black-capped Chickadee
- Calls: Rapid, buzzy *tsick-a-dee-dee*; higher-pitched and faster than Black-capped; difficult to distinguish in the field without practice

**Evolutionary Note:** Chestnut-backed Chickadee is the quintessential PNW chickadee — its range is closely circumscribed by the wet conifer forests of the Pacific slope, from southeastern Alaska to central California. This is a narrower range than any other North American chickadee, making it a true regional specialist. Its preference for dense, moist forest links its abundance directly to old-growth and mature forest retention. Genetic work shows limited population structure, suggesting continuous gene flow along the coastal forest corridor [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (west slope) | Year-round | Common | Indicator (moist conifer forest) |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 6: Coastal (forest fringe) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [P18] Hagar 2007

### Boreal Chickadee (*Poecile hudsonicus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Paridae
**Status:** Resident — rare PNW peripheral
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (range-wide decline)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 20-22 cm
- Mass: 9-13 g
- Plumage: Brown cap (not black — distinguishes from all other PNW chickadees), grayish-brown back, rufous flanks, white cheeks with grayish wash. Overall duller and browner than other PNW chickadees.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Boreal and subalpine spruce-fir forest; in PNW restricted to high-elevation spruce and fir stands in northern WA, northern ID, and southern BC
- Wintering: Resident; largely sedentary with minimal elevational movement
- Elevation range: 3,000-6,000 ft in PNW
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — upper elevations)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Subalpine — spruce-fir)
- Microhabitat: Dense spruce-fir forest; specialist of boreal and boreal-transition conifer stands

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, insect larvae (especially spruce budworm), spiders, conifer seeds
- Foraging strategy: Bark and foliage gleaner in dense conifer; methodical, less acrobatic than Black-capped; extensive food caching
- Seasonal variation: Insect larvae and cached seeds in winter; active arthropods in summer

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — excavates in soft rotten wood
- Nest location: In conifer snag, 1-5 m up
- Clutch size: 5-8 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 18 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-June

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show negative trends range-wide, though monitoring is difficult in remote boreal habitat [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~7 million (PIF 2020); PNW populations very small and peripheral
- Threats: Climate change driving boreal zone northward; spruce-fir forest loss; limited PNW range makes regional populations vulnerable to stochastic events
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Slow, wheezy *tsee-day-day*; distinctly slower and more nasal than other PNW chickadees
- Calls: Wheezy, drawn-out *tschick-a-dee-dee*; nasal quality is the best field mark for auditory identification

**Evolutionary Note:** Boreal Chickadee is the most genetically divergent of the North American *Poecile* chickadees, having split from the Black-capped lineage ~4-5 million years ago. Its PNW presence represents the southwestern edge of a vast boreal distribution spanning Canada and Alaska. As climate change shifts boreal habitat northward, PNW populations may become increasingly isolated mountain-top relicts — a pattern already evident in several boreal-associated species (Spruce Grouse, Three-toed Woodpecker).

## 4. Family Aegithalidae — Long-tailed Tits and Bushtits

---

### Bushtit (*Psaltriparus minimus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Aegithalidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 10-11 cm
- Wingspan: 15-17 cm
- Mass: 5-6 g (one of the smallest North American birds, rivaling kinglets)
- Plumage: Plain gray-brown overall; tiny bill; long tail (nearly half of total length). Coastal Pacific populations have brown crown; interior populations have gray crown. Rare melanistic "Black-eared" morph (males with black auriculars) occurs irregularly in the PNW, primarily in interior and southern populations.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal in plumage; eye color differs — adult males have dark brown eyes, adult females have pale cream to yellow eyes (one of the most reliable sex indicators in any PNW passerine)

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Scrubby woodland, oak woodland, chaparral, suburban gardens, riparian thickets
- Wintering: Same; sedentary but nomadic in flocks
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,500 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Riparian thickets)
- Microhabitat: Dense scrub and low woodland; avoids deep forest interior and open ground

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, and their eggs; primarily arthropod gleaner
- Foraging strategy: Acrobatic foliage gleaner in dense shrub and low trees; forages in constantly moving flocks of 10-40 birds, sweeping through vegetation in a loose wave
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round; occasional small seeds

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Elaborate woven pendant — a gourd-shaped hanging pouch 15-25 cm long, constructed of spider silk, lichen, moss, plant fibers, and feathers, with a side entrance near the top. Among the most architecturally complex nests of any North American bird.
- Nest location: Suspended from outer branch of shrub or tree, 1-10 m up
- Clutch size: 5-7 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (both sexes)
- Fledging: 14-15 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: March-June
- Social nesting: Helpers at the nest documented — failed breeders and unmated males assist at other nests, contributing to incubation and feeding

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to increasing in urban/suburban areas [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~3 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Cold winter events can cause significant local die-offs (tiny body size makes them vulnerable to hypothermia); minimal broad-scale threats
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: No true song; constant high, thin *tsit-tsit-tsit* contact calls maintaining flock cohesion
- Calls: Rapid *pit-pit-pit* or *tsri-tsri-tsri*; flock constantly vocalizing while foraging; alarm call a higher, sharper *tseet*

**Evolutionary Note:** Bushtit is the only New World member of the family Aegithalidae (long-tailed tits), which is otherwise an Old World group. The species' pendant nest architecture closely resembles that of Eurasian Long-tailed Tit (*Aegithalos caudatus*), suggesting this nest form is ancestral to the family. The "Black-eared" morph was formerly considered a separate species (*P. melanotis*) but is now recognized as a polymorphism within *P. minimus* based on extensive intergradation. Cooperative breeding behavior (helpers at the nest) in Bushtits has been an important model system for studying the evolution of altruistic behavior in birds [O1].

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Fairly common | Visitor |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

## 5. Family Sittidae — Nuthatches

Nuthatches are compact, strong-footed bark foragers distinguished by their ability to descend tree trunks headfirst — a locomotion strategy unique among North American birds. This headfirst descent allows nuthatches to exploit bark crevices from a different angle than woodpeckers and creepers (which ascend), reducing competition. The PNW hosts 3 species whose distributions map onto forest type and elevation with remarkable precision.

---

### Red-breasted Nuthatch (*Sitta canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Sittidae
**Status:** Resident (with irruptive movements)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 11-12 cm
- Wingspan: 20-22 cm
- Mass: 8-13 g
- Plumage: Blue-gray above, rusty-orange below; black cap, white supercilium, black eyeline through eye; short tail, stout slightly upturned bill. Compact, no-neck profile.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males have darker black cap and richer rusty underparts; females have grayish cap and paler underparts

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Conifer forest — Douglas-fir, spruce, hemlock, pine; most abundant in montane conifer but present from sea level to treeline
- Wintering: Resident where food adequate; irruptive in years of conifer seed crop failure — may appear in large numbers at lowland feeders
- Elevation range: Sea level to 7,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Subalpine), Zone 3 (Lowland — especially winter), Zone 4 (Foothill — east-side pine)
- Microhabitat: Conifer-dominated forest; forages on trunks and large branches

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (bark-dwelling beetles, moth larvae, spiders) and conifer seeds
- Foraging strategy: Bark gleaner — descends trunks headfirst, probing bark crevices; also forages on branches and cones; caches conifer seeds under bark flakes
- Seasonal variation: Insects predominate in summer; conifer seeds and cached items in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — excavates own cavity in soft, rotten wood; uniquely applies conifer resin (pitch) around nest entrance, thought to deter predators and competitors
- Nest location: In dead snag or rotten branch, 1-20 m up
- Clutch size: 5-8 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (female; male feeds)
- Fledging: 18-21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-June

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable with high year-to-year variability driven by irruptive cycles [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~18 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth and mature conifer loss reduces nesting habitat; cavity competition
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Repetitive nasal *ank-ank-ank-ank*; tin-horn quality; tempo and pitch vary geographically
- Calls: High nasal *yank* or *enk*; alarm is rapid series

**Evolutionary Note:** Red-breasted Nuthatch is the most widespread North American nuthatch, ranging from boreal forest to Mexican mountains. Its resin application behavior is shared with no other North American bird — the pitch may function as a chemical deterrent (terpenes) against nest predators, a physical barrier to competitors, or both. In irruption years triggered by boreal cone crop failures, Red-breasted Nuthatches can appear far south of normal range, illustrating the connection between PNW montane and boreal ecological dynamics [O1].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Subalpine | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland | Winter/irruptive | Variable | Visitor |
| Zone 4: Foothill (east-side) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### White-breasted Nuthatch (*Sitta carolinensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Sittidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 13-15 cm
- Wingspan: 22-27 cm
- Mass: 18-30 g (largest PNW nuthatch — roughly twice the mass of Red-breasted)
- Plumage: Blue-gray above, clean white face and underparts; black cap (males) or dark gray cap (females); chestnut undertail coverts; longer bill than Red-breasted. Lacks eye stripe.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males have glossy black cap; females have dull grayish cap

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open deciduous and mixed woodland — Oregon white oak, ponderosa pine, riparian cottonwood; less tied to dense conifer than Red-breasted Nuthatch
- Wintering: Resident; largely sedentary
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,500 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — riparian, parks), Zone 7 (Steppe — ponderosa/cottonwood)
- Microhabitat: Open, mature deciduous or mixed forest; requires large-diameter trees for foraging

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (beetles, ants, caterpillars) and large seeds/nuts (acorns, pine seeds)
- Foraging strategy: Bark gleaner on large trunks and major branches; headfirst descent; more likely than Red-breasted to forage on ground. Caches acorns and seeds.
- Seasonal variation: Insects in summer; seeds and nuts more important in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — uses natural cavities and old woodpecker holes; does not excavate; occasionally uses nest boxes
- Nest location: In large tree, 3-15 m up; sometimes sweeps insects (crushed beetle parts) around cavity entrance — function similar to Red-breasted's resin application
- Clutch size: 5-9 (typically 7-8)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female; male feeds)
- Fledging: 14-18 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: March-May

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to slightly increasing [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~10 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Oak woodland loss (>90% decline in Willamette Valley); fire suppression converting open oak to closed conifer
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], Pacific Birds [O5]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rapid nasal *whi-whi-whi-whi-whi*; lower-pitched and faster than Red-breasted
- Calls: Nasal *yank*; sharp *ip* contact note

**Evolutionary Note:** White-breasted Nuthatch shows striking vocal and genetic differentiation across its range — western populations (Pacific, Interior West, Great Basin) differ vocally and genetically from eastern populations, and the complex may represent 2-3 species pending further study. PNW populations fall into the "Pacific" and "Interior West" vocal groups. The Pacific form is strongly associated with Oregon white oak, making it an indicator of oak woodland ecosystem health in the PNW — its decline would signal continued degradation of this critically reduced habitat [O1, O3].

| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Common | Indicator (oak woodland) |

### Pygmy Nuthatch (*Sitta pygmaea*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Sittidae
**Status:** Resident — east-side specialist
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern (but locally sensitive to ponderosa pine loss)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 10-11 cm
- Wingspan: 19-21 cm
- Mass: 9-11 g
- Plumage: Gray-brown cap reaching to eye level; blue-gray back; buffy-white underparts; small, stubby bill. Compact and short-tailed.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Ponderosa pine forest — strongly associated with open, mature ponderosa stands on the east side of the Cascades
- Wintering: Resident; communal roosting in cavities during cold weather (groups of 10-100+ birds huddle in single cavity for thermoregulation)
- Elevation range: 2,000-5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 7 (Steppe — ponderosa margins)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill — east-side ponderosa), Zone 2 (Montane — lower east slope)
- Microhabitat: Ponderosa pine forest; avoids dense forest and pure Douglas-fir stands

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (bark beetles, weevils) and ponderosa pine seeds
- Foraging strategy: Outer-branch and pine-needle forager — works the terminal portions of pine branches and needle clusters where other nuthatches and woodpeckers forage less. Also descends trunks like other nuthatches.
- Seasonal variation: Insects in summer; pine seeds and cached items in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — uses old woodpecker holes and natural cavities in ponderosa snags
- Nest location: In ponderosa snag, 2-20 m up
- Clutch size: 5-9 (typically 7)
- Incubation: 14-16 days (female)
- Fledging: 20-22 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-June
- Social nesting: Cooperative breeding documented — helpers (typically previous-year offspring) assist at nest

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable where ponderosa habitat is maintained; declining where old-growth ponderosa converted [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~2.4 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth ponderosa removal; fire suppression converting open pine stands to dense fir; logging of large-diameter ponderosa snags
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rapid, high piping *ti-di, ti-di, ti-di*; often given in chorus from foraging flocks
- Calls: High, thin *ti-ti-ti*; flock maintains constant vocal contact

**Evolutionary Note:** Pygmy Nuthatch is the ecological counterpart of the Brown-headed Nuthatch (*S. pusilla*) of southeastern US pine forests — the two species are sister taxa, together forming the "pygmy nuthatch" clade within *Sitta*. Both are pine-forest specialists with cooperative breeding behavior and communal roosting. Communal roosting is an adaptation to cold winter nights at elevation — metabolic savings from huddling can be 30-50%, critical for a 10-gram bird surviving -20C nights in east-side ponderosa forest [O1].

| Zone 4: Foothill (east-side) | Year-round | Common | Indicator (ponderosa pine) |

## 6. Family Certhiidae — Treecreepers

---

### Brown Creeper (*Certhia americana*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Certhiidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 12-14 cm
- Wingspan: 18-20 cm
- Mass: 7-10 g
- Plumage: Cryptic brown-streaked upperparts matching bark; white underparts; long, stiff, pointed tail (used as prop against bark — convergent with woodpeckers); thin, decurved bill. Virtually invisible when pressed against bark.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Mature and old-growth conifer and mixed forest; requires large-diameter trees with textured bark for foraging and loose bark slabs for nesting
- Wintering: Resident; may descend from montane to lowland forest; joins mixed-species flocks
- Elevation range: Sea level to 6,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — mature stands), Zone 4 (Foothill — mature oak/conifer)
- Microhabitat: Large-diameter trees (>30 cm DBH) with deeply furrowed bark; old-growth Douglas-fir, western red cedar, and Sitka spruce are primary foraging substrates

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, insect eggs, and spiders gleaned from bark crevices
- Foraging strategy: Spiraling upward ascent of tree trunks — begins at base, works upward in a spiral, then flies to the base of the next tree and repeats. This ascending spiral complements the nuthatch's descending forage, reducing competition. Joins mixed-species flocks with chickadees, nuthatches, and kinglets.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round; occasionally takes small seeds at feeders in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cup nest wedged behind loose bark slab on a dead or dying tree
- Nest location: Behind loose bark flap, 1-5 m up; requires old-growth or mature trees with peeling bark
- Clutch size: 5-6
- Incubation: 14-17 days (female)
- Fledging: 15-17 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April-June

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable overall; sensitive to old-growth loss [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~8.5 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth and mature forest loss; salvage logging removing large snags and dying trees with loose bark (primary nest substrate); second-growth forest with small-diameter trees provides reduced foraging habitat
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Extremely high-pitched, thin *tsee-tsee-tsee-tsee-tsidee-tsee*; often inaudible to observers with age-related hearing loss. The song has been described as sounding like "trees, trees, trees, beautiful trees."
- Calls: Single high *tsee* contact note

**Evolutionary Note:** Brown Creeper is the sole New World representative of the family Certhiidae, which includes 6 Eurasian treecreeper species. The American species likely diverged from Eurasian ancestors during a trans-Beringian colonization event in the Pliocene. Brown Creeper's bark-matching camouflage and ascending spiral foraging strategy represent a remarkable example of convergent evolution with the Australasian treecreepers (family Climacteridae) — an unrelated group that evolved nearly identical morphology and behavior on a separate continent [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator (mature/old-growth forest) |
| Zone 3: Lowland (mature stands) | Year-round | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Year-round | Uncommon | Visitor |

## 7. Family Troglodytidae — Wrens

Wrens are small, energetic, and extraordinarily vocal birds with cocked tails and densely barred plumage. Despite their small size, many wrens produce songs of remarkable volume and complexity. The PNW hosts 6 regularly breeding species spanning habitats from old-growth forest understory (Pacific Wren) to arid rock canyons (Canyon Wren) to freshwater marshes (Marsh Wren). The 2010 AOS split of Winter Wren into Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*) and Winter Wren (*T. hiemalis*) recognized a PNW-centered cryptic speciation event.

---

### Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Troglodytidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

The Pacific Wren warrants extended treatment as a recently recognized species resulting from the split of the former "Winter Wren" complex, and as a quintessential old-growth indicator of PNW wet forests.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 9-11 cm
- Wingspan: 13-17 cm
- Mass: 8-12 g (among the smallest PNW passerines)
- Plumage: Dark brown overall with dense barring on wings, tail, and flanks; pale supercilium; short, sharply cocked tail; thin bill. Dark overall tone matches the shadowy forest understory it inhabits.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal — sexes alike

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Dense, moist conifer forest with heavy understory — old-growth and mature western hemlock, western red cedar, Sitka spruce; strongly associated with downed logs, root wads, and stream-side tangles
- Wintering: Resident; largely sedentary at breeding sites; may move to lower elevations in severe winters
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — west slope)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — mature/old-growth), Zone 6 (Coastal — forest fringe)
- Microhabitat: Forest floor and understory within 3 m of ground; root wads of fallen trees, streambank undercuts, dense fern and salal understory. Requires complex ground-level structure — logs, stumps, root tangles — for foraging and nesting. Abundance correlates strongly with volume of coarse woody debris.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and spiders gleaned from bark, moss, fern fronds, and leaf litter
- Foraging strategy: Gleaner and prober in dense, dark understory — works through root wads, under fallen logs, along stream banks. Mouse-like movement through tangles; rarely ventures into open or above shrub layer.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round; occasionally takes tiny seeds

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Domed nest with side entrance — constructed of moss, fern, bark fibers, spider silk, and feathers; built within root wad, cavity in fallen log, or bank crevice
- Nest location: In root wad, upturned stump, streambank cavity, or dense vegetation near ground level; 0-3 m up
- Clutch size: 5-8 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 14-17 days (female only)
- Fledging: 15-19 days
- Broods per year: 1-2 (occasionally double-brooded)
- Breeding season: April-July
- Male behavior: Males build multiple "dummy nests" (up to 8) in territory; female selects one to line and use. Males are polygynous where territory quality permits, maintaining 2-3 females within a single territory.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable where old-growth and mature forest is maintained; declining where forest simplified [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~8 million (PIF 2020, split from former Winter Wren estimate)
- Threats: Old-growth and mature forest loss; simplification of forest structure (removal of coarse woody debris, snags, and downed logs); second-growth forest with clean understory provides little habitat
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], USFS [G6]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: An extraordinary cascade of trills, warbles, and rapid note sequences lasting 5-10 seconds; one of the longest and most complex songs of any North American passerine. Song includes more than 300 individual notes compressed into a continuous stream. Volume remarkable for body size — the Pacific Wren's song is among the loudest per gram of any bird, penetrating the dense forest understory where it lives.
- Calls: Sharp, emphatic *chimp-chimp* or *kit-kit*; dry rattling alarm
- Dawn song: Singing begins before first light; territorial males may sing 30+ songs per hour during breeding season peak

**Evolutionary Note:** The 2010 AOS split of the Winter Wren complex into three species — Pacific Wren (*T. pacificus*), Winter Wren (*T. hiemalis*), and Eurasian Wren (*T. troglodytes*) — was based on genetic divergence, vocal differences, and a narrow hybrid zone in northeastern BC where Pacific and Winter Wren ranges meet. Pacific and Winter Wrens are essentially indistinguishable by plumage alone, but their songs differ markedly: Pacific Wren song is higher-pitched, more rapid, and contains more notes per second than Winter Wren song. The two species diverged ~4.3 million years ago, likely separated by the Pleistocene ice sheets that isolated Pacific coastal refugia from interior/eastern forests. This cryptic speciation event was detected entirely through vocalization and genetic analysis — a model case for speciation-by-song in a morphologically conservative group [O1, O3].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (west slope) | Year-round | Common | Indicator (old-growth/mature forest) |
| Zone 3: Lowland (mature forest) | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator |
| Zone 6: Coastal (forest fringe) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G6] USFS old-growth studies; [O1] Cornell; [O3] AOS; [O4] PIF; [P18] Hagar 2007

**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern (western populations stable; eastern populations have collapsed)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 13-14 cm
- Wingspan: 18-20 cm
- Mass: 8-12 g
- Plumage: Gray-brown above, whitish below; bold white supercilium (more prominent than Pacific Wren); long tail with white outer feather tips, frequently flicked and fanned. Barred tail and wings.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Brushy habitats, thickets, suburban gardens, open woodland with dense understory; more tolerant of disturbed and urban habitats than Pacific Wren
- Wintering: Resident; sedentary
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,500 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian thickets)
- Microhabitat: Dense brush, woodpiles, garden hedges, brush piles; avoids deep forest

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and spiders
- Foraging strategy: Active gleaner in brush and low vegetation; investigates crevices, bark, and leaf litter; more visible and open-habitat than Pacific Wren
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cup in cavity or crevice — uses woodpecker holes, nest boxes, building crevices, brush piles, hanging pots
- Nest location: In cavity or enclosed space, 1-5 m up; highly adaptable in nest-site selection
- Clutch size: 5-7 (typically 6)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 14 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: March-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in PNW (western populations); catastrophically declined in eastern US (nearly extirpated east of Mississippi) [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~7 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Minimal in PNW; eastern decline attributed to competition with expanding House Wren populations and habitat shifts; European Starling cavity competition
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Variable, buzzy warble — louder and more varied than House Wren; individual males have repertoires of 9-22 song types
- Calls: Harsh *jit-jit-jit*; buzzy scold

**Evolutionary Note:** Bewick's Wren shows a dramatic east-west population divergence in North America. Western populations (including PNW) are stable and thriving; eastern populations have declined >90% since 1960 and are now virtually absent from the eastern US. The cause appears to be competitive displacement by House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*), which destroys Bewick's Wren eggs and nests — a process well-documented but not reversible under current conditions. The PNW now holds the core of the species' continental range, making regional populations globally significant [O1].

### House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Troglodytidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 11-13 cm
- Wingspan: 15-18 cm
- Mass: 10-12 g
- Plumage: Uniform brown above and below; faint barring on wings and tail; indistinct supercilium; plain, nondescript. No bold markings.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open woodland, shrubby edges, suburban gardens, farmyards; overlaps broadly with Bewick's Wren but tends toward more open, edge habitats
- Wintering: Southern US, Mexico
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft; more widespread on east side of Cascades than west side in PNW
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 7 (Steppe — riparian/residential)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Microhabitat: Edge habitats with cavities available — brush piles, fence rows, garden boxes

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and spiders
- Foraging strategy: Active gleaner in dense brush; investigates cavities, crevices, and bark
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous throughout

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — fills cavity with sticks, then constructs cup within; uses nest boxes readily
- Nest location: In any available cavity — boxes, building crevices, hanging planters, old boots
- Clutch size: 5-8 (typically 6-7)
- Incubation: 12-15 days (female)
- Fledging: 15-17 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: May-August
- Aggressive nest competition: Males puncture eggs and remove nestlings of other cavity nesters (including Bewick's Wren, Tree Swallow, chickadees, and bluebirds) — documented as a significant source of nest failure for these species in areas of sympatry

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to increasing [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~160 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Minimal; window strikes; cat predation
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Exuberant, bubbling cascade of notes; loud and rapid; one of the most conspicuous yard-bird songs
- Calls: Sharp *churr* scold; dry rattling alarm

**Evolutionary Note:** House Wren has the largest latitudinal range of any New World passerine, breeding from southern Canada to Tierra del Fuego. The complex may represent multiple species; South American populations ("Southern House Wren") differ vocally and genetically, and some taxonomists have proposed splitting the complex into 3-4 species. In the PNW, House Wren's aggressive nest-site competition with other cavity nesters has been implicated in the decline of Bewick's Wren in areas of overlap and in reduced productivity of Western Bluebird nest box programs [O1, O3].

| Zone 4: Foothill | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 7: Steppe | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

### Marsh Wren (*Cistothorus palustris*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Troglodytidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (varies by region)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 10-14 cm
- Wingspan: 15-17 cm
- Mass: 9-14 g
- Plumage: Rich brown above with black and white streaking on back; buffy underparts; prominent white supercilium; cocked tail. More warmly colored than other PNW wrens.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Emergent freshwater marsh — cattail, bulrush, and tule stands in wetlands and lake margins
- Wintering: Resident in mild areas (western WA/OR); partially migratory in colder regions
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — urban wetlands), Zone 7 (Steppe — marsh edges)
- Microhabitat: Dense emergent vegetation over water; requires standing cattail or bulrush at least 1 m tall

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, snails gleaned from marsh vegetation and water surface
- Foraging strategy: Active gleaner in dense emergent vegetation; forages close to water surface
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Woven globular nest attached to marsh vegetation — domed with side entrance, constructed of wet cattail and grass strips
- Nest location: Woven around emergent stems, 0.3-1 m above water
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 12-16 days (female)
- Fledging: 12-16 days
- Broods per year: 2 (regularly double-brooded)
- Breeding season: April-July
- Male behavior: Males build multiple dummy nests (up to 20); strongly polygynous; territorial males destroy eggs of neighboring Marsh Wrens and other marsh-nesting species (Red-winged Blackbird, Yellow-headed Blackbird)

**Population:**
- Trend: Variable — stable where wetland habitat maintained; declining where marshes drained or degraded [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~9 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Wetland drainage and conversion; water-level management conflicts; invasive phragmites replacing native cattail in some areas
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Loud, buzzy, mechanical rattling — *tsrrr-trip-trip-trip*; sung from prominent perch atop cattail or while clinging to stem
- Calls: Harsh *check* or *churr*; alarm is rapid, angry-sounding chatter
- Dawn song: Nocturnal singing common during breeding season; males may sing continuously through moonlit nights

**Evolutionary Note:** Marsh Wren shows deep genetic divergence between eastern and western North American populations — genetic distance rivals that between many recognized species. Western populations (including PNW) have larger song repertoires (up to 200 song types per male vs. ~50 in eastern males), more aggressive interspecific egg-destruction behavior, and different vocalizations. A future taxonomic split into eastern and western species is plausible pending further analysis. The western Marsh Wren's extraordinarily large song repertoire is thought to have evolved through sexual selection and territorial competition in dense marsh habitats where acoustic signaling is the primary mode of communication [O1, O3].

**Evolutionary Note:** Canyon Wren is the only member of genus *Catherpes*, suggesting early divergence within the wren radiation. Its long, decurved bill and flattened skull are specialized for probing deep rock crevices — an ecological niche with no close competitor in North America. The species' range closely maps onto exposed rock terrain from southern BC to Mexico, making it a reliable indicator of canyon and cliff habitat. Its extraordinary song, reverberating through rock canyons, is an auditory signature of the east-side PNW landscape [O1].

## 8. Family Cinclidae — Dippers

The dippers are one of the most remarkable passerine families on Earth — five species worldwide, all aquatic, all associated with fast-flowing montane streams. North America has a single representative, the American Dipper, which is the only truly aquatic songbird on the continent. In the PNW, the dipper is an iconic indicator of stream health, and its year-round presence on cold mountain streams — singing even in January — makes it one of the most charismatic and ecologically significant passerines in the region.

---

### American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Cinclidae
**Status:** Resident — year-round on permanent streams
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Not listed (monitored as stream health indicator by WDFW)
- OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

The American Dipper warrants extended treatment as the only aquatic passerine in North America, a primary biological indicator of stream health in the PNW, and a species whose ecology is deeply intertwined with the salmon-nutrient cycling that defines Pacific Northwest riverine ecosystems.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16-19 cm
- Wingspan: 23-26 cm
- Mass: 46-64 g (heavy for body length — dense plumage and heavy musculature contribute to mass)
- Plumage: Uniform slate-gray overall — no streaking, barring, or contrasting marks. Short tail, typically cocked upward when perched. Juveniles slightly paler with faint yellowish bill base. Fresh fall plumage shows faint brownish wash on head. White eyelid (nictitating membrane) conspicuous during the characteristic dipping motion — a rapid, full-body bob performed 40-60 times per minute while perched on streamside rocks.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal — sexes alike in plumage; males average slightly larger (5-10% heavier)

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Fast-flowing, clear, unpolluted mountain streams with rocky substrates — requires riffles, cascades, or waterfalls for foraging; stream width typically 3-15 m; gradient moderate to steep
- Wintering: Resident on breeding streams where open (unfrozen) water persists; moves to lower-elevation streams or spring-fed reaches when high-elevation streams freeze
- Elevation range: Sea level to 8,000+ ft — one of the widest elevational ranges of any PNW passerine, following streams from mountain headwaters to lowland confluences
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland — stream corridors at all elevations)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Alpine — headwater streams), Zone 2 (Montane — primary habitat), Zone 4 (Foothill — lower-elevation streams)
- Microhabitat: Rocky, fast-flowing stream reaches with clear water, moderate depth (15-60 cm), and cobble/boulder substrate. Avoids still water, turbid water, and fine-sediment substrates. Presence strongly correlates with stream health metrics: high dissolved oxygen, low sediment load, intact riparian canopy, and healthy macroinvertebrate communities. Absence from a previously occupied stream reach is a reliable signal of water quality degradation.

**Aquatic Adaptations:**
The American Dipper possesses a suite of anatomical and physiological adaptations unmatched among passerines:

- **Nictitating membrane:** A translucent third eyelid that acts as an underwater goggle, protecting the eye while maintaining vision during submersion. The white flash of this membrane during dipping is the species' most recognizable behavioral trait.
- **Nasal flaps:** Dense feather tufts covering the nostrils, sealed shut during diving to prevent water entry.
- **Preen oil:** The uropygial (preen) gland is approximately 10 times the size found in comparable-sized passerines. The copious oil output waterproofs the dense plumage — a dipper that has not recently preened can become waterlogged within minutes, demonstrating the active maintenance required.
- **Plumage density:** Approximately 6,000 contour feathers — roughly twice the density of a comparably sized terrestrial passerine. This dense feather coat traps an insulating air layer against the skin during submersion.
- **Hemoglobin concentration:** Elevated hemoglobin levels relative to terrestrial passerines of similar size, increasing oxygen-carrying capacity for sustained underwater activity.
- **Low metabolic rate (relative):** Despite living in cold water environments year-round, dippers show metabolic efficiency adaptations that reduce heat loss during submersion — including peripheral vasoconstriction and the insulating plumage layer.
- **Underwater locomotion:** Dippers walk along stream bottoms, gripping cobbles with strong, clawed feet, and "fly" underwater using partially opened wings as hydrofoils. Submersion duration typically 5-15 seconds, occasionally up to 30 seconds. Maximum recorded dive depth approximately 2 m.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Aquatic macroinvertebrates — larval caddisflies (Trichoptera), mayflies (Ephemeroptera), stoneflies (Plecoptera), and midges (Diptera); also small fish (trout fry, sculpin), fish eggs, and aquatic snails
- Foraging strategy: Four distinct methods used depending on water conditions:
  1. *Surface gleaning* — picking invertebrates from exposed rocks and shallow riffles while wading
  2. *Head dipping* — submerging head and neck into shallow water while standing on rocks
  3. *Diving* — plunging from rock or water surface into deeper water, walking along bottom while foraging
  4. *Swimming dives* — swimming on surface and diving from swimming position in deeper pools
- Foraging territory: Defends a linear stream territory averaging 0.5-2.0 km of stream length; territories are strictly linear, following the stream course. Territory length inversely correlates with stream productivity — shorter territories on richer streams.
- Seasonal variation: Aquatic invertebrates year-round; proportions shift with emergence timing — caddisfly and stonefly larvae dominate winter diet; emerging adults taken in summer; salmon eggs and alevins consumed where available (October-March)

**Salmon-Nutrient Cycling Cross-Link:**
In salmon-bearing streams of the PNW, the American Dipper participates directly in the marine-derived nutrient (MDN) cycle that defines Pacific Northwest riverine ecology. Spawning salmon deposit marine-origin nitrogen (N-15) and phosphorus into freshwater systems. This nutrient pulse fuels macroinvertebrate production, which in turn supports higher dipper densities on salmon streams. Stable isotope analysis of dipper feathers from salmon-bearing streams shows elevated N-15 signatures relative to non-salmon streams, confirming direct trophic linkage. Dippers on salmon streams produce larger clutches, fledge more young, and maintain shorter territories than conspecifics on comparable non-salmon streams — a measurable fitness benefit of the salmon subsidy [P11, P12, P13].

This cross-links to the broader salmon-nutrient cascade documented in the PNW ECO research series: salmon → riparian vegetation (via bear and bird carcass transport) → aquatic invertebrates → dipper reproductive success → stream-health indicator function. The dipper is thus a sentinel species for both water quality and salmon run health — its decline on a previously productive stream should trigger investigation of both abiotic (water chemistry, sediment) and biotic (salmon run failure) causes.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Domed, globular nest with side entrance — constructed of moss (exterior), grass, and leaves, with an inner cup of fine grass and bark strips. The moss exterior is kept perpetually moist by stream spray, creating a distinctive green, dripping structure.
- Nest location: On rock ledge, cliff face, bridge abutment, or culvert wall directly over or adjacent to fast-flowing water — spray from the stream keeps the nest moss alive. Height typically 1-4 m above water surface; some nests behind waterfalls, accessible only by flying through the curtain of water.
- Clutch size: 3-6 (typically 4-5; larger clutches on salmon-bearing streams)
- Incubation: 15-17 days (female only; male continues to defend stream territory)
- Fledging: 24-26 days (long nestling period relative to body size — fledglings must be capable of swimming upon leaving the nest)
- Broods per year: 1-2 (double-brooding regular in productive streams at lower elevations)
- Breeding season: March-July (earlier at lower elevations; may begin nest construction while snow still covers streambanks)
- Fledgling behavior: Young can swim and dive within days of fledging — a unique requirement among passerines. Fledglings remain on the parental territory for 2-3 weeks post-fledging before being driven off. Each fledgling establishes its own linear stream territory, often on the same watershed.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable overall; localized declines where stream quality degraded [G4, G9]
- Estimate: Continental population ~130,000 (PIF 2020); PNW populations represent a significant proportion of the US total, given the region's abundance of suitable montane stream habitat
- Threats: Stream degradation (sedimentation, pollution, acidification); dam construction eliminating riffle habitat; riparian deforestation increasing water temperature above tolerance threshold (~18C upper limit for sustained foraging); climate change reducing summer stream flows in rain-shadow watersheds; salmon run declines reducing MDN subsidies; legacy mining contamination (heavy metals) in some east-side watersheds
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: A loud, complex, buzzy warble — a sustained stream of notes delivered from a streamside rock, often competing with the sound of rushing water. Song is high-pitched and penetrating, adapted to cut through the white noise of fast-flowing water. Both sexes sing (unusual among passerines), though male song is louder and more sustained.
- Calls: Sharp, metallic *zeet* or *dzit* — a penetrating contact note delivered in flight, audible over rapids. Alarm call is a rapid series of sharp *zit-zit-zit-zit*.
- Winter singing: Unlike nearly all other PNW passerines, the American Dipper sings year-round, including through the darkest and coldest months. Winter singing functions in year-round territory defense — territories are maintained 12 months, as the stream resource is required continuously. A dipper singing on a snow-banked stream in January is one of the most distinctive auditory experiences of the PNW montane winter.

**Evolutionary Note:** Family Cinclidae comprises five species worldwide — one each in the Americas, Europe, North Africa/Central Asia, and two in montane Asia. All five are obligate stream-dwellers, indicating that the aquatic lifestyle evolved once in the common ancestor and has been maintained across all lineages. Molecular phylogenetics places Cinclidae as sister to the thrushes (Turdidae), suggesting that the ancestral dipper diverged from a thrush-like ancestor and radiated into stream habitats, evolving the remarkable suite of aquatic adaptations described above. The American Dipper's range extends from Alaska to Panama, always following montane stream corridors — its distribution maps the stream network of western North America more faithfully than any other single species. In the PNW, the dipper's year-round residency on streams that freeze, flood, and shift course seasonally makes it a testament to the viability of the aquatic passerine niche — a niche explored by no other bird family in the Western Hemisphere [O1, O3].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine (headwater streams) | Breeding/summer | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane (streams) | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator (stream health) |
| Zone 4: Foothill (lower streams) | Year-round | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian/Wetland | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator (water quality + salmon health) |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW; [G15] NOAA stream temperature; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [P11] Willson & Halupka 1995; [P12] Hilderbrand et al. 1999; [P13] Helfield & Naiman 2001

## 9. Family Regulidae — Kinglets

Kinglets are among the smallest passerines in North America — tiny, hyperactive birds with thin bills, short tails, and brightly colored crown patches that give the family its name (Latin *regulus* = "little king"). The PNW hosts two species: the resident Golden-crowned Kinglet, a year-round inhabitant of conifer forests, and the migratory Ruby-crowned Kinglet, a widespread breeder in montane conifers. Both species have remarkably high metabolic rates and must feed almost continuously during winter daylight hours to survive — a 6-gram Golden-crowned Kinglet can die of starvation overnight if it enters the roosting period with insufficient fat reserves.

---

### Golden-crowned Kinglet (*Regulus satrapa*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Regulidae
**Status:** Resident
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 8-11 cm
- Wingspan: 14-18 cm
- Mass: 5-7 g (among the smallest PNW passerines; rivaled only by hummingbirds and Bushtit)
- Plumage: Olive-green above, pale below; two white wingbars; bold facial pattern with black-bordered crown stripe — orange (male) or yellow (female) crown patch, often concealed unless raised in agitation or display. White supercilium and dark eye stripe.
- Sexual dimorphism: Crown patch color — males orange-centered with yellow margins; females entirely yellow. Otherwise alike.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Dense conifer forest — western hemlock, Sitka spruce, Douglas-fir, Pacific silver fir; strongly associated with mature and old-growth stands with dense canopy
- Wintering: Resident; some downslope movement; joins mixed-species flocks in lowland conifer and mixed forest
- Elevation range: Sea level to 6,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — conifer stands), Zone 6 (Coastal — Sitka spruce forest)
- Microhabitat: Dense conifer canopy; forages primarily on outer branch tips and needle clusters; requires intact canopy structure for insulation and foraging substrate

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Tiny insects, insect eggs, and spiders gleaned from conifer foliage — springtails, bark lice, aphids, and scale insects
- Foraging strategy: Rapid, hovering-gleaning technique — hovers briefly at branch tips to pick prey from needle undersides; joins mixed-species flocks with chickadees, nuthatches, and Brown Creeper in winter
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous year-round; switches to dormant insect eggs and overwintering larvae in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Deep, pensile (hanging) cup suspended from conifer branch — constructed of moss, lichen, spider silk, and bark strips; lined with feathers (up to 10 layers of insulating feathers). One of the most intricate nest constructions of any PNW passerine.
- Nest location: Suspended from horizontal conifer branch, typically 10-20 m up in dense canopy; well-concealed
- Clutch size: 8-11 (one of the largest clutch sizes of any North American passerine for its body mass)
- Incubation: 14-15 days (female)
- Fledging: 14-19 days
- Broods per year: 1-2 (frequently double-brooded)
- Breeding season: April-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~100 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth conifer loss; severe winter weather events (mass mortality during prolonged cold snaps — a 6-gram bird operating at metabolic maximum is extraordinarily vulnerable to multi-day cold events); climate change may increase frequency of late-spring freezing events that destroy early nests
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Extremely high-pitched, thin *tsee-tsee-tsee-tsee* ascending in pitch, followed by a rapid, tumbling terminal phrase; often inaudible to observers with age-related high-frequency hearing loss (fundamental frequency 7-9 kHz)
- Calls: Thin, high *tsee-tsee-tsee* — three-note contact call; the most common sound in PNW mixed-species winter flocks

**Evolutionary Note:** Golden-crowned Kinglet is one of two New World kinglets (family Regulidae, 6 species worldwide). The family is an ancient lineage with no close relatives — molecular work places Regulidae as sister to a clade containing waxwings and silky-flycatchers, though the relationship is distant. The species' clutch size of 8-11 eggs is extraordinary for a 6-gram bird — the combined egg mass can exceed the female's body weight. This extreme reproductive investment is thought to be an adaptation to high winter mortality; the species' survival strategy is to produce large numbers of offspring in a short breeding season to compensate for heavy overwinter losses. Golden-crowned Kinglets survive PNW winter nights through communal roosting — groups of 3-6 birds huddle together in dense conifer foliage, reducing individual heat loss by up to 40% [O1].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland (conifer stands) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 6: Coastal (Sitka spruce) | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### Ruby-crowned Kinglet (*Corthylio calendula*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Regulidae
**Status:** Breeding / wintering (present year-round in PNW but populations shift seasonally)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 9-11 cm
- Wingspan: 16-19 cm
- Mass: 5-10 g
- Plumage: Olive-green above, pale grayish-white below; bold white eye-ring (broken above — gives a wide-eyed appearance); two white wingbars. Males have a brilliant scarlet crown patch, almost always concealed within olive crown feathers — visible only when raised in agitation, display, or aggressive encounters. Females and immatures lack red crown entirely.
- Sexual dimorphism: Male ruby crown patch; otherwise minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Montane conifer forest — spruce-fir, subalpine forest; breeds at higher elevations than Golden-crowned Kinglet in PNW
- Wintering: Lowland forest, woodland edges, riparian thickets, suburban gardens; more habitat-flexible in winter than breeding season
- Elevation range: Breeding 3,000-7,000 ft; wintering sea level to 3,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — breeding)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Subalpine — breeding), Zone 3 (Lowland — wintering), Zone 4 (Foothill — wintering), Zone 5 (Riparian — wintering)
- Microhabitat: Dense conifer for breeding; more eclectic in winter — any wooded or scrubby habitat

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects, spiders, and their eggs; some fruit and seeds in fall/winter
- Foraging strategy: Active gleaner and hover-gleaner in foliage; more likely than Golden-crowned to forage in deciduous trees and shrubs; constant wing-flicking while foraging (diagnostic behavioral field mark)
- Seasonal variation: Insects and spiders in breeding season; supplements with small fruits and seeds in migration and winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Deep, pensile cup similar to Golden-crowned Kinglet — suspended from conifer branch
- Nest location: In dense conifer canopy, typically 10-30 m up; well-hidden
- Clutch size: 7-11 (comparable to Golden-crowned; large for body size)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 16 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~90 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Spruce-fir forest loss at breeding elevations; climate change may compress high-elevation breeding habitat; window strikes during migration (small size and nocturnal migration make them particularly vulnerable)
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Remarkably loud for body size — begins with several high, thin *tsee* notes, followed by a lower *tu-tu-tu* phrase, ending with a loud, emphatic, warbled *cheedle-cheedle-cheedle*. The terminal phrase is powerful enough to be mistaken for a much larger bird. Song volume relative to body mass is among the highest of any PNW passerine.
- Calls: Harsh, dry *je-dit*; distinctive two-part scold note

**Evolutionary Note:** Ruby-crowned Kinglet was recently moved from *Regulus* to the monotypic genus *Corthylio* based on molecular evidence showing it diverged early from the remaining kinglets. This taxonomic revision makes it the sole member of its genus worldwide. Despite being one of the smallest North American songbirds, the Ruby-crowned Kinglet produces one of the loudest songs relative to body mass — a seeming paradox explained by the species' breeding habitat in dense, high-elevation conifer forest where acoustic propagation is challenging. The loud song compensates for the dense, sound-absorbing environment [O1, O3].

| Zone 2: Montane | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

## 10. Family Turdidae — Thrushes and Bluebirds

The thrushes are a cosmopolitan family of medium-sized passerines renowned for their melodious songs and their role as primary frugivore-dispersers in forest ecosystems. In the PNW, the family spans habitats from old-growth rainforest (Varied Thrush) to alpine meadows (Mountain Bluebird) to suburban lawns (American Robin). Several PNW thrushes are among the most sought-after species for birders and naturalists: the Varied Thrush's haunting single-note whistle in old-growth forest, the Hermit Thrush's ethereal fluted song at dusk, and Swainson's Thrush's ascending spiral of notes in riparian corridors are defining sounds of the PNW landscape.

---

### Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (altitudinal migrant)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Not listed (monitored as old-growth indicator)
- PIF: Common Bird in Steep Decline

The Varied Thrush warrants extended treatment as the signature songbird of PNW old-growth conifer forest, an icon of the region's rainforest ecosystem, and a species whose presence is among the most reliable field indicators of mature and old-growth forest structure. Its haunting, single-note whistle reverberating through moss-draped conifers is arguably the most recognizable and emblematic sound of the Pacific Northwest wilderness.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 20-25 cm
- Wingspan: 34-37 cm
- Mass: 65-100 g
- Plumage: Males spectacular — deep burnt-orange breast and supercilium, dark blue-gray upperparts, black breast band, orange wingbars and wing patches. Females similar but with olive-gray replacing the black breast band and blue-gray tones, and somewhat paler orange. Superficially robin-like in shape and size but immediately distinguishable by breast band and wing pattern.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males brighter with bold black breast band; females olive-gray and more subdued

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Dense, moist, mature and old-growth conifer forest — western hemlock, Sitka spruce, western red cedar, Douglas-fir. Strongly associated with forest stands containing deep moss layers, abundant epiphytes, complex understory structure, and large-diameter trees. Abundance correlates positively with stand age, canopy complexity, and volume of coarse woody debris.
- Wintering: Descends to lowland forest, woodland edges, suburban parks and gardens, riparian thickets; becomes conspicuous at lower-elevation feeders during cold snaps. Winter flocks may include 10-50 individuals.
- Elevation range: Sea level (winter) to 5,000 ft (breeding); breeding concentrated at 1,000-4,000 ft in wet west-side forest
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — west slope)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — wintering), Zone 6 (Coastal — mature forest)
- Microhabitat: Breeding habitat requires closed-canopy conifer forest with deep litter layer, moss mats, and minimal understory disturbance. Forages primarily on the forest floor in deep leaf litter and moss, flipping debris with its bill to expose invertebrates — a foraging niche shared with no other PNW thrush at the same intensity.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and other invertebrates in breeding season; fruits and berries in fall/winter
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager — hops through leaf litter and moss, flipping debris with vigorous lateral bill sweeps. More terrestrial and forest-floor-dependent than American Robin. In winter, shifts to frugivory — salal, huckleberry, madrone berries, and crabapple.
- Seasonal variation: Invertebrates (earthworms, beetles, millipedes, spiders) dominate breeding diet; fruits and berries dominate fall-winter diet

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup constructed of twigs, moss, bark strips, leaves, and mud; lined with fine grass and soft plant fibers
- Nest location: On horizontal conifer branch against trunk, 2-8 m up; typically in dense cover, often under an overhanging branch or bark flap. Nest placement shows strong preference for old-growth structural features — platforms created by broken branches, moss mats on large limbs.
- Clutch size: 3-5 (typically 3-4)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 13-15 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: April-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show -1.4% annual decline range-wide; steeper declines in areas of extensive old-growth loss [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~26 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Old-growth and mature forest loss is the primary driver of population decline; simplification of forest structure in managed stands; second-growth conifer plantations lack the moss mats, deep litter, and canopy complexity required for breeding. Window strikes during winter migration to lowland areas are a significant source of mortality. Climate change may reduce snow-free breeding season at higher elevations.
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], USFS [G6]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: A single, long, resonant, buzzy whistle on one pitch — *zhreeeeee* — lasting 1-2 seconds, followed by a pause of 5-15 seconds, then another whistle on a different pitch. Each note is delivered on a single frequency, giving it an eerie, organ-like quality that reverberates through dense forest. Males typically have 5-10 distinct pitch levels, delivered in unpredictable sequence. The effect in old-growth forest — where the sound bounces off massive trunks and is absorbed by moss — is unlike any other North American bird song.
- Calls: Soft, low *tchuk*; thin, high *seee* flight note
- Dawn song: Begins singing before dawn; often the first voice heard in old-growth PNW forest, even before Pacific Wren

**Evolutionary Note:** Varied Thrush is the sole member of genus *Ixoreus*, an ancient lineage within Turdidae that diverged from other North American thrushes early in the family's New World radiation. Its closest relative is debated — some molecular analyses place it near the South American *Ridgwayia* (Aztec Thrush), while others suggest an independent lineage. The species' range is almost entirely within the PNW bioregion, from southeastern Alaska to northern California, making it one of the most range-restricted thrushes in North America and a true PNW endemic in functional terms. Its association with old-growth conifer forest is one of the tightest habitat-species relationships among PNW passerines — abundance tracks old-growth acreage so faithfully that some forest ecologists have proposed it as a quantitative indicator of old-growth condition alongside the Northern Spotted Owl [O1, G6, P18].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (west slope) | Breeding | Fairly common | Indicator (old-growth/mature forest) |
| Zone 3: Lowland | Wintering | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 6: Coastal (mature forest) | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator (old-growth) |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G6] USFS old-growth studies; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [P18] Hagar 2007; [P19] Marzluff & Sallabanks 1998

### American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (partial migrant)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 23-28 cm
- Wingspan: 31-40 cm
- Mass: 72-94 g
- Plumage: Dark gray to blackish head, broken white eye-ring, brick-red breast and belly, gray back and wings, yellow bill. Females slightly paler with lighter head and breast. Juveniles spotted-breasted (classic thrush juvenile plumage).
- Sexual dimorphism: Males darker head and brighter breast; females paler overall

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Extraordinarily broad — lawns, parks, gardens, forest clearings, alpine meadow edges, riparian woodland, suburban neighborhoods; the most habitat-generalist thrush in North America
- Wintering: Partially migratory in PNW; many remain year-round in western lowlands; flocks concentrate around fruiting trees; alpine and high-elevation breeders withdraw to lowlands
- Elevation range: Sea level to 8,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 2 (Montane — forest clearings), Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 7 (Steppe — irrigated areas)
- Microhabitat: Open ground with short grass for foraging; trees or structures for nesting; remarkably flexible

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Earthworms and insects in breeding season; fruit in fall/winter. The dominant frugivore in the PNW lowland avifauna.
- Foraging strategy: Characteristic run-and-pause ground foraging on lawns and open ground — runs a short distance, pauses, cocks head (using visual detection, not auditory, despite popular belief), then pulls earthworm from soil. Shifts to frugivory in fall — forms large flocks in fruiting trees (mountain ash, crabapple, holly, madrone, hawthorn).
- Seasonal variation: Earthworms and invertebrates spring-summer; fruit dominant fall-winter. Single flocks may strip a fruiting tree in hours.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Sturdy open cup of grass, twigs, and mud — mud lining is diagnostic for robin nests
- Nest location: On horizontal branch, building ledge, light fixture, or other structure; 1-15 m up; highly adaptable
- Clutch size: 3-5 (typically 4) — "robin's-egg blue" color is iconic
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 14-16 days
- Broods per year: 2-3 (regularly triple-brooded in PNW)
- Breeding season: March-August

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to increasing [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~370 million (PIF 2020) — one of the most abundant North American land birds
- Threats: Pesticide exposure (lawn chemicals, especially organophosphates affecting earthworm-feeding birds); window strikes; cat predation; West Nile virus (significant mortality documented in some regions)
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Rich, caroling *cheerily, cheer-up, cheerio* — one of the most familiar bird songs in North America; long phrases of whistled notes with alternating rising and falling pitch
- Calls: Sharp *tuk-tuk-tuk*; thin, high *seeee* flight note; loud, rapid *whinny* alarm
- Dawn song: Among the first singers at dawn; often begins 30-60 minutes before sunrise

**Evolutionary Note:** American Robin is a member of the large, cosmopolitan genus *Turdus* (approximately 85 species worldwide), and is the only *Turdus* thrush widespread in North America. It was named "robin" by early European colonists for its red breast, though it is unrelated to the European Robin (*Erithacus rubecula*). The species shows moderate geographic variation — PNW birds (*T. m. caurinus*) average slightly darker and smaller than interior populations. As the dominant frugivore across the PNW lowland landscape, the American Robin is the primary seed disperser for dozens of native and non-native berry-producing plants, making it a keystone mutualist in plant community dynamics [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (clearings) | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Abundant | Keystone (dominant frugivore-disperser) |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 7: Steppe (irrigated/urban) | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

### Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Breeding / wintering (present year-round in PNW but seasonal populations shift)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15-18 cm
- Wingspan: 25-29 cm
- Mass: 23-37 g
- Plumage: Olive-brown above with contrasting reddish-brown (rufous) tail — the rufous tail is the key field mark separating Hermit from other PNW *Catharus* thrushes. Spotted breast, white eye-ring, buffy flanks. Habitually raises tail slowly and then lowers it — a distinctive behavioral field mark.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Montane conifer forest — open to moderately dense stands; more associated with forest edges and openings than Swainson's Thrush; prefers drier forest types than Varied Thrush
- Wintering: Lowland woodland, riparian thickets, suburban gardens; the only regularly wintering *Catharus* thrush in the PNW
- Elevation range: Breeding 2,000-7,000 ft; wintering sea level to 2,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane Conifer)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 1 (Subalpine — forest edges), Zone 3 (Lowland — wintering), Zone 4 (Foothill — wintering)
- Microhabitat: Forest floor and understory; breeds in more open forest than Varied Thrush; winters in dense thickets and woodland edges

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and invertebrates in breeding season; fruit in fall/winter
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager — scratches in leaf litter for invertebrates; less aggressive litter-flipping than Varied Thrush; winter fruit diet includes salal, toyon, and madrone berries
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous in summer; frugivorous in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of moss, twigs, bark strips, and grass; lined with fine materials
- Nest location: On or near ground — in root tangle, under low branch, on forest floor against log or stump; occasionally low in shrub (0-2 m)
- Clutch size: 3-5 (typically 4)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (female)
- Fledging: 12 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~72 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Forest fragmentation at breeding sites; brood parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbird; window strikes during migration
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Ethereal, flute-like phrases — each phrase begins with a single clear introductory note followed by a rapid, quavering cascade of harmonically rich notes. Successive phrases delivered on different pitches, creating a spiraling, meditative quality. Widely regarded as one of the most beautiful bird songs in North America. Typically sings at dusk and dawn, enhancing its otherworldly quality.
- Calls: Soft, low *chuck*; rising, thin *eee* note

**Evolutionary Note:** Hermit Thrush is the only member of the *Catharus* genus that winters in North America (all others winter in Central and South America), and the only one that regularly eats fruit in winter — a behavioral shift that enables its short-distance migration strategy. PNW breeding populations belong to several described subspecies, with west-side birds (*C. g. guttatus* group) differing slightly from east-side birds in plumage tone. The species' late-afternoon singing habit and preference for twilight vocalization have earned it the reputation as the "nightingale of North America" — a comparison that, while hyperbolic, reflects the genuine tonal beauty of its song [O1].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Subalpine (forest edge) | Breeding | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |
| Zone 3: Lowland | Wintering | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Wintering | Fairly common | Visitor |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant — Neotropical migrant
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern (but declining in parts of range)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16-20 cm
- Wingspan: 28-32 cm
- Mass: 23-45 g
- Plumage: Olive-brown above (uniform — no rufous tail contrast, distinguishing from Hermit Thrush); spotted breast; buffy eye-ring and buffy face wash extending above and behind the eye. Overall warmer, more uniform coloration than Hermit Thrush.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Dense, moist forest and riparian thickets — primarily wet west-side conifer forest with dense shrub understory; also riparian corridors east of Cascades. Strongly associated with salmonberry, thimbleberry, and red elderberry thickets.
- Wintering: Central and South America (southern Mexico to Peru)
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 2 (Montane — west slope)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — wet forest), Zone 5 (Riparian — breeding), Zone 6 (Coastal — forest)
- Microhabitat: Dense shrub understory within or adjacent to conifer forest; particularly associated with wet, brushy stream margins and logged areas regenerating with dense shrub growth

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and spiders in breeding season; fruit during migration and on wintering grounds
- Foraging strategy: Forages in understory and on ground — gleans insects from foliage and ground litter; less floor-dependent than Hermit or Varied Thrush. During fall migration, switches heavily to frugivory — critical for fat deposition before long-distance flight.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous in breeding season; frugivorous during fall migration (salmonberry, elderberry, huckleberry)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of twigs, bark strips, moss, and leaves; lined with fine material
- Nest location: In dense shrub or low tree, typically 1-3 m up in salmonberry, vine maple, or conifer branch
- Clutch size: 3-5 (typically 3-4)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 10-13 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — BBS data show -1.0% annual decline range-wide; PNW populations relatively stable but continental trend is negative [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~100 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Wintering habitat loss in Central and South America (primary driver of decline); collision mortality during nocturnal migration (tall buildings, communication towers); brood parasitism by Brown-headed Cowbird at forest edges
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: An ascending spiral of rich, flute-like phrases — each phrase slightly higher in pitch than the last, creating an upward-spiraling effect described as "going up a staircase." One of the most recognizable and evocative songs of the PNW forest; the sound of summer in west-side riparian corridors.
- Calls: Soft *whit*; liquid *quip* note

**Evolutionary Note:** Swainson's Thrush is one of four *Catharus* thrushes breeding in the PNW, a genus confined to the Americas. Recent molecular work reveals a deep split within Swainson's Thrush between Pacific-slope populations (olive-backed, coastal migratory route, breeding in PNW) and interior populations (russet-backed, overland migratory route). These two groups differ in plumage, vocalizations, and migratory orientation, and may represent incipient species. PNW populations belong to the Pacific-slope (*ustulatus*) group. As a long-distance Neotropical migrant that breeds in PNW riparian corridors and winters in Andean cloud forest, the species links PNW ecosystem health directly to tropical forest conservation — a connection that makes its population trend a barometer of habitat conditions across two continents [O1, O3].

| Zone 3: Lowland (wet forest) | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |

| Zone 6: Coastal (forest) | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |

### Western Bluebird (*Sialia mexicana*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Candidate (western WA populations severely declined)
- PIF: Low concern range-wide; Yellow Watch List in some regions

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15-18 cm
- Wingspan: 28-33 cm
- Mass: 24-31 g
- Plumage: Males vivid deep blue above (head, wings, tail) with rusty-orange breast and flanks extending onto upper back; blue throat (distinguishing from Eastern Bluebird's orange throat). Females grayish-blue above with subdued orange breast and grayish throat.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males brightly colored; females duller grayish-blue

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open woodland — oak woodland, ponderosa pine savanna, burned forest with standing snags, agricultural land with nest boxes; requires open ground for foraging and cavities for nesting
- Wintering: Partially migratory — some resident, others move to lower elevations; winter flocks concentrate around fruiting trees (mistletoe, juniper berries)
- Elevation range: 500-5,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 2 (Montane — east-side ponderosa), Zone 7 (Steppe — woodland edges)
- Microhabitat: Open canopy with scattered trees and snags; requires cavities for nesting; benefits from nest box programs

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects in breeding season; fruit and berries in fall/winter
- Foraging strategy: Drop forager — perches on low branch or fence post, scans ground for insects, drops to capture prey; also hawks flying insects from perch. Winter diet shifts to berries (juniper, mistletoe, madrone).
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous spring-summer; frugivorous fall-winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity nester — uses natural tree cavities, old woodpecker holes, and nest boxes
- Nest location: In dead snag, fence post, or nest box; 1-10 m up; strongly benefits from nest box programs in areas where natural cavities are limited
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 13-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 19-22 days
- Broods per year: 1-2 (double-brooding with nest boxes)
- Breeding season: March-July
- Nest competition: Competes with European Starling, House Sparrow, and House Wren for cavity sites; starling competition is a primary factor in decline of populations near agricultural areas

**Population:**
- Trend: Variable — increasing where nest box programs and habitat restoration are active; declining where cavity competition is intense and oak woodland is lost [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~5.6 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Cavity competition (European Starling, House Sparrow); oak woodland loss; snag removal in managed forest; fire suppression converting open pine/oak savanna to dense conifer
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], WDFW [G9]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Soft, warbled *cheer-cheer-chur-lee*; gentle and subdued compared to other thrushes
- Calls: Soft *few* or *chuck*; thin flight note

**Evolutionary Note:** Western Bluebird is one of three *Sialia* bluebirds in North America — all cavity nesters, all ecologically linked to open woodland. The species' PNW distribution is tightly associated with oak woodland and ponderosa pine savanna — two fire-maintained ecosystems that have declined sharply since European settlement. The success of nest box programs for Western Bluebird demonstrates that cavity availability, rather than food or climate, is often the limiting factor — a principle with broad implications for cavity-nester conservation. In western Washington, the species was nearly extirpated by the mid-20th century due to combined cavity competition and habitat loss; ongoing restoration efforts are slowly rebuilding populations [G9, O5].

|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (east-side ponderosa) | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common | Indicator (oak woodland health) |
| Zone 7: Steppe (woodland edges) | Breeding | Uncommon | Visitor |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [G9] WDFW; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O5] Pacific Birds Habitat Joint Venture

### Mountain Bluebird (*Sialia currucoides*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Breeding / resident (partial migrant)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16-20 cm
- Wingspan: 28-36 cm
- Mass: 26-37 g
- Plumage: Males entirely sky-blue — no orange or rust; the bluest bird in the PNW. Paler blue below than above. Females grayish-brown with blue wash on wings, tail, and rump.
- Sexual dimorphism: Extreme — males entirely blue, females grayish-brown with blue highlights

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open, high-elevation habitats — alpine meadow edges, subalpine parkland, montane grassland, sagebrush-grassland interface, burned forest with standing snags
- Wintering: Lower-elevation open country — sagebrush steppe, agricultural fields, juniper woodland; forms loose flocks
- Elevation range: Breeding 4,000-10,000+ ft; wintering 500-4,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — meadow edges)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 2 (Montane — burns and openings), Zone 7 (Steppe — wintering and breeding at lower elevations)
- Microhabitat: Open ground with scattered perches (snags, fence posts, boulders) for drop-foraging; requires cavities for nesting

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects in breeding season; berries (juniper, Russian olive) in winter
- Foraging strategy: Hover-drop forager — hovers over open ground and drops to capture insects; more aerial than Western Bluebird. Also perch-and-drop from fence posts and snags. Hovering ability enables foraging in areas with no perch sites.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous spring-summer; frugivorous fall-winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — uses natural cavities, old woodpecker holes, nest boxes, cliff crevices
- Nest location: In snag, cliff face, or nest box; 1-10 m up; accepts nest boxes readily in high-elevation meadows
- Clutch size: 4-8 (typically 5-6)
- Incubation: 13-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 22-23 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: May-July (later than Western Bluebird due to higher elevation)

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~4.6 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Cavity competition at lower elevations (European Starling); loss of high-elevation snag habitat; fire suppression reducing open meadow habitat
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Soft, warbling *chur-lee, chur-lee*; quieter and more nasal than Western Bluebird
- Calls: Thin *few*; soft chuckling note

**Evolutionary Note:** Mountain Bluebird occupies the highest-elevation breeding habitat of any North American bluebird, nesting in alpine and subalpine zones where Western and Eastern Bluebirds are absent. Its hovering foraging strategy — unusual among thrushes — is an adaptation to open, perch-poor alpine terrain where drop-foraging from a perch would be less effective. The species hybridizes occasionally with Western Bluebird where ranges overlap at intermediate elevations in the east-side PNW, producing offspring with intermediate plumage [O1].

### Townsend's Solitaire (*Myadestes townsendi*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Turdidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (altitudinal migrant)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 20-22 cm
- Wingspan: 33-37 cm
- Mass: 30-35 g
- Plumage: Uniform gray overall with bold white eye-ring, buffy wing patches visible in flight, white outer tail feathers. Slender, upright posture. Superficially flycatcher-like in profile.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open montane and subalpine forest — forest edges, burns, rocky slopes with scattered conifers; nests on the ground, often on steep, rocky banks
- Wintering: Descends to lower elevations; strongly associated with juniper woodland where it defends individual juniper trees as winter food territories
- Elevation range: Breeding 4,000-9,000 ft; wintering 500-4,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 1 (Subalpine — breeding)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 2 (Montane — breeding), Zone 4 (Foothill — wintering), Zone 7 (Steppe — juniper woodland, wintering)
- Microhabitat: Breeding — open, rocky slopes with scattered conifers and bare ground; wintering — juniper stands

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects in breeding season; juniper berries in winter (near-obligate dependency)
- Foraging strategy: Breeding — flycatching from exposed perch (sits upright on snag or rock, sallies out for flying insects); winter — defends individual juniper trees, consuming berries and aggressively excluding other frugivores from "its" tree
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous summer; frugivorous winter (juniper berries comprise >90% of winter diet)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of bark strips, grass, and pine needles; lined with fine grass
- Nest location: On ground — on cutbank, rocky slope, road cut, cliff ledge, or in root cavity; typically in shaded, sheltered location against rock face. Ground nesting among thrushes is unusual.
- Clutch size: 4-5 (typically 4)
- Incubation: 11 days (female)
- Fledging: 14 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~1.6 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Juniper woodland loss or degradation (impacts winter survival); high-elevation habitat loss; ground-nest predation
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: A long, continuous, warbling song — rich, complex, and sustained; often delivered in flight display from high above breeding territory. One of the longer thrush songs, lasting 30+ seconds.
- Calls: Single, bell-like *eek* note — clear and distinctive

**Evolutionary Note:** Townsend's Solitaire is the only North American member of genus *Myadestes*, which includes approximately 12 species of solitaires in the Caribbean, Central America, and Hawaii (including the extinct Kamao and Olomao). The genus is characterized by beautiful, sustained songs and a generally solitary disposition. The species' winter territorial defense of individual juniper trees is one of the most striking examples of individual winter territoriality in North American passerines — a single bird may defend the same tree for an entire winter, spending more energy on territorial defense than on actual foraging. This behavior underscores the critical importance of the juniper berry resource for overwinter survival [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Subalpine | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 2: Montane | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 4: Foothill (juniper) | Wintering | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 7: Steppe (juniper woodland) | Wintering | Fairly common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

## 11. Family Mimidae — Mockingbirds and Thrashers

The mimids are a New World family of medium-sized passerines characterized by complex vocal abilities — many species incorporate mimicry of other birds and environmental sounds into their songs. The PNW sits at the northwestern edge of the family's range, hosting only two regularly occurring species: the Gray Catbird (scarce, eastern edge) and the Sage Thrasher (sagebrush steppe specialist).

---

### Gray Catbird (*Dumetella carolinensis*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Mimidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant — rare in PNW (at western edge of range)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 21-24 cm
- Wingspan: 22-30 cm
- Mass: 23-56 g
- Plumage: Uniform dark gray overall with black cap and rufous undertail coverts; long, dark tail. Slender bill.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Dense thickets, riparian brush, hedgerows; in PNW restricted primarily to eastern WA and OR riparian corridors and scattered lowland sites in western WA
- Wintering: Southeastern US, Caribbean, Central America
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian — east-side corridors)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland — rare, western WA), Zone 7 (Steppe — riparian edges)
- Microhabitat: Dense shrub thickets, especially riparian willow, hawthorn, and rose tangles

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and fruit
- Foraging strategy: Gleaner in dense brush; scratches in leaf litter; frugivorous during migration
- Seasonal variation: Insects in breeding; fruit during migration and winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Bulky open cup of twigs, bark, and grass; lined with fine rootlets
- Nest location: In dense shrub, 1-3 m up
- Clutch size: 3-5 (typically 4)
- Incubation: 12-13 days (female)
- Fledging: 10-11 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: May-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable continent-wide; uncommon to rare in PNW [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~29 million (PIF 2020); PNW population very small
- Threats: Minimal in PNW; riparian habitat loss in east-side corridors
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Long, disjointed series of musical phrases, squeaks, and whistles; includes mimicry of other species. Named for the distinctive cat-like *mew* call.
- Calls: Nasal, mewing *mew* — diagnostic

**Evolutionary Note:** Gray Catbird is the sole member of genus *Dumetella*. Its PNW occurrence represents the far northwestern extent of a primarily eastern/central North American distribution. The species reaches the PNW through riparian corridors east of the Cascades, where suitable thicket habitat persists. It is a recognizer and ejector of Brown-headed Cowbird eggs — one of the few North American passerines that actively rejects brood parasitism [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland (western WA) | Breeding | Rare | Visitor |
| Zone 5: Riparian (east side) | Breeding | Uncommon | Characteristic |
| Zone 7: Steppe (riparian edges) | Breeding | Uncommon | Visitor |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF

### Sage Thrasher (*Oreoscoptes montanus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Mimidae
**Status:** Breeding migrant — sagebrush obligate
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- WA: Candidate
- OR: Sensitive-Vulnerable (Strategy Species)
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (sagebrush-dependent, habitat declining)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 20-23 cm
- Wingspan: 30-33 cm
- Mass: 40-50 g
- Plumage: Gray-brown above with faint wingbars; heavily streaked breast and flanks on whitish underparts; pale yellow eye; short, straight bill (shorter than other thrashers). Relatively short tail for a mimid.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Sagebrush steppe — obligate association with big sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*); requires large, continuous sagebrush stands with mature plants of sufficient height (>60 cm) for nesting
- Wintering: Southwestern US and Mexico — open desert scrub
- Elevation range: 2,000-6,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 7 (Steppe — sagebrush)
- Ecoregion secondary: None — among the most habitat-restricted species in this module
- Microhabitat: Mature, tall sagebrush stands with >30% shrub cover; avoids areas where sagebrush has been removed, burned (without recovery), or replaced by invasive grasses

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects (breeding season); fruit and berries (migration/winter)
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager — runs between sagebrush clumps picking insects from ground and lower shrub branches; also takes flying insects from perch atop sagebrush
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous in breeding; frugivorous during migration (sagebrush berries, greasewood berries)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Bulky open cup of sagebrush twigs, bark, and grass; lined with fine rootlets, hair, and plant down
- Nest location: In sagebrush shrub, typically 0.3-1 m up; rarely on ground or in other shrub species. Nesting success tightly linked to sagebrush shrub size and density.
- Clutch size: 4-7 (typically 4-5)
- Incubation: 13-17 days (both sexes incubate)
- Fledging: 11-14 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: April-July

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining — mirrors sagebrush habitat loss range-wide [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~6.4 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Sagebrush habitat loss and degradation — conversion to agriculture, overgrazing, invasive cheatgrass expansion, wildfire followed by cheatgrass invasion (preventing sagebrush recovery), energy development (wind, solar, fossil fuel extraction). The species' fate is functionally inseparable from the fate of the sagebrush biome.
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4], WDFW [G9]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Long, sustained warbling — a continuous stream of musical phrases lasting 30+ seconds; less mimicry than other mimids but richer and more sustained than most sagebrush-associated species. Typically delivered from the top of a tall sagebrush or in flight display over territory.
- Calls: Low *chuck*; thin whistled note

**Evolutionary Note:** Sage Thrasher is the sole member of genus *Oreoscoptes* and the smallest North American thrasher. Its evolutionary history is tied to the sagebrush biome that developed across the western interior during the late Miocene and Pliocene as the region became more arid. As a sagebrush obligate, the Sage Thrasher is part of a guild of species (with Greater Sage-Grouse, Sagebrush Sparrow, and Brewer's Sparrow) whose conservation depends entirely on sagebrush habitat persistence. The ongoing loss of the sagebrush biome — estimated at 44% of historical extent — directly drives this species' decline [O1, P14].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 7: Steppe (sagebrush) | Breeding | Fairly common | Indicator (sagebrush habitat health) |

## 12. Family Sturnidae — Starlings

The starlings are an Old World family with over 100 species, but only one occurs in the PNW — the European Starling, introduced to North America in 1890-1891 and now one of the continent's most abundant and ecologically disruptive birds.

---

### European Starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Sturnidae
**Status:** Resident — introduced (invasive)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (non-native; not protected under Migratory Bird Treaty Act)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Not assessed (non-native)

The European Starling warrants extended treatment not for conservation concern but for its profound ecological impact on native PNW cavity-nesting birds. Introduced to North America when approximately 60 birds were released in New York's Central Park in 1890-1891 by Eugene Schieffelin (who reportedly sought to establish all birds mentioned in Shakespeare's works), the population has expanded to an estimated 200+ million across the continent. The starling's impact on native cavity nesters is one of the most well-documented cases of invasive species competition in North American ornithology.

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 19-23 cm
- Wingspan: 31-44 cm
- Mass: 60-96 g
- Plumage: Breeding — glossy black with iridescent purple and green sheen; yellow bill. Non-breeding — heavily speckled with white and buff spots (the "starry" appearance that gives the species its name); dark bill. Juveniles uniform gray-brown, lacking both iridescence and spotting.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal in plumage; bill base blue-gray in males, pinkish in females during breeding season

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Urban, suburban, and agricultural areas — buildings, nest boxes, bridge structures, cliff faces, tree cavities; extremely habitat-generalist
- Wintering: Resident; forms enormous communal roosts (thousands to hundreds of thousands of individuals) in urban areas, agricultural buildings, and woodlots
- Elevation range: Sea level to 5,000 ft (rarely higher)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill — agricultural), Zone 7 (Steppe — agricultural)
- Microhabitat: Any human-modified landscape with cavities and open ground for foraging; absent only from dense unbroken forest and wilderness areas far from human habitation

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Omnivorous — insects, earthworms, grain, fruit, garbage, livestock feed
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager on lawns, fields, and open ground; probes soil with bill using a distinctive bill-gaping technique (inserts closed bill, then opens it to spread soil and expose invertebrates). Also takes fruit from trees and garbage from urban areas.
- Seasonal variation: Insects and invertebrates spring-summer; grain, fruit, and garbage year-round; extremely flexible

**Cavity Competition — Ecological Impact:**
The European Starling's impact on native PNW cavity nesters is severe and well-documented:

- **Mechanism:** Starlings are aggressive cavity usurpers — they do not excavate their own cavities but instead evict native cavity nesters by force. A starling will enter an occupied cavity, destroy eggs or kill nestlings, and claim the site. Starlings are larger and more aggressive than most native PNW cavity nesters (chickadees, nuthatches, bluebirds, swallows) and can displace even woodpeckers from their excavated cavities.
- **Affected species:** Western Bluebird, Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Purple Martin, Northern Flicker, Lewis's Woodpecker, American Kestrel, and smaller cavity nesters. Purple Martin populations in the Pacific Northwest have been particularly severely impacted — starling competition for nest cavities is a primary factor in the species' decline west of the Cascades.
- **Nest box programs:** The widespread adoption of nest boxes with entrance hole sizes calibrated to exclude starlings (diameter <3.8 cm for bluebirds; Purple Martin gourds with specific starling-resistant entrance designs) is a direct management response to starling competition. These programs have been partially successful for Western Bluebird and Tree Swallow but have not fully offset starling impacts on larger cavity nesters.
- **Agricultural impact:** Beyond cavity competition, starlings cause significant agricultural damage — feeding on cherries, grapes, and other fruit crops; consuming livestock feed at feedlots; and contaminating stored grain. USDA Wildlife Services manages starling populations at some agricultural sites through lethal control.
- **Murmuration:** Large starling flocks produce spectacular aerial displays ("murmurations") in which thousands of birds move in coordinated, fluid patterns at dusk before settling into roost sites. While visually impressive, these roosts create substantial guano accumulation, health concerns (histoplasmosis from fungal growth in droppings), and noise disturbance in urban areas.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity — fills cavity with grass, leaves, feathers, and miscellaneous debris (paper, plastic, string)
- Nest location: In any cavity — tree hole, building cavity, nest box, bridge crevice, cliff hole
- Clutch size: 4-6 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (both sexes)
- Fledging: 21 days
- Broods per year: 2 (regularly double-brooded; can re-nest rapidly after failure)
- Breeding season: March-July
- Nest-site takeover: Will begin prospecting and fighting for cavities as early as late February, often before native species have begun nesting — this early-season aggression gives starlings a competitive advantage over later-nesting native species

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to slightly declining in some regions (continent-wide); still superabundant [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~200 million (PIF estimate; not formally tracked by PIF due to non-native status)
- Threats (to starling): Minimal — the species thrives in human-modified landscapes. Control programs reduce local populations but have no effect on continental abundance.
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], USFWS [G1]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: A complex, rambling medley of whistles, buzzes, rattles, clicks, and mimicry — starlings routinely incorporate sounds of other bird species (Red-tailed Hawk screams, killdeers, meadowlarks), mechanical sounds (car alarms, phone rings), and other environmental noises into their songs. Individual repertoires can include 15-20 mimicked species.
- Calls: Harsh *tcheer*; various whistles and squealing notes

**Evolutionary Note:** European Starling is one of the most successful avian colonizers in history. From approximately 60 birds released in 1890-1891, the North American population reached an estimated 200 million within 100 years — a doubling time suggesting exponential growth sustained over decades. The species' success is attributed to extreme ecological flexibility: broad diet, aggressive cavity competition, tolerance of human habitation, early breeding phenology, and high reproductive output. In the PNW, the starling's impact illustrates a broader ecological principle: in ecosystems with limited cavity availability (as in managed forests where snag removal has reduced natural cavities), the addition of an aggressive generalist cavity competitor cascades through the entire cavity-nesting community, depressing populations of multiple native species simultaneously. The starling is thus both a competitor and an amplifier of habitat degradation — its impact is worst where native cavity habitat is already reduced [O1, P19].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Abundant | Invasive (cavity competitor) |
| Zone 4: Foothill (agricultural) | Year-round | Common | Invasive |
| Zone 7: Steppe (agricultural) | Year-round | Common | Invasive |

**Sources:** [G1] USFWS; [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [P19] Marzluff & Sallabanks 1998

## 13. Family Motacillidae — Pipits and Wagtails

Pipits are slim, brown, ground-dwelling passerines of open habitats — superficially lark-like but taxonomically unrelated. The family is primarily Old World, but one species breeds regularly in the PNW: the American Pipit, a characteristic bird of alpine tundra and barren high-elevation terrain.

---

### American Pipit (*Anthus rubescens*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Motacillidae
**Status:** Breeding / wintering (altitudinal and latitudinal migrant)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15-17 cm
- Wingspan: 25-28 cm
- Mass: 19-26 g
- Plumage: Breeding — gray-brown above; buffy-white below with variable streaking on breast and flanks; pale supercilium; thin bill; white outer tail feathers (flashed in flight). Non-breeding — more heavily streaked below, browner above. Walks with characteristic tail-bobbing.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Alpine tundra — barren, rocky ground above timberline; snowfield margins, alpine meadows, volcanic pumice fields; one of the few passerines nesting in the true alpine zone of PNW mountains
- Wintering: Lowland agricultural fields, mudflats, estuarine margins, plowed fields, lake shores, coastal beaches; forms loose flocks
- Elevation range: Breeding 5,000-10,000+ ft; wintering sea level to 2,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine — breeding)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Riparian/estuarine — wintering), Zone 6 (Coastal — wintering), Zone 7 (Steppe — agricultural fields, wintering)
- Microhabitat: Breeding — barren, rocky alpine terrain with scattered low vegetation; requires snowmelt pools and damp ground for foraging. Wintering — open, wet ground; plowed fields, mudflats, flooded pastures.

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Insects and other invertebrates; some seeds in winter
- Foraging strategy: Ground forager — walks deliberately across open terrain picking insects from soil surface, rocks, and low vegetation. On alpine breeding grounds, forages along snowfield margins where emerging insects concentrate. In winter, walks through agricultural fields and mudflats.
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous in breeding season; supplements with seeds and small invertebrates from agricultural fields in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of grass, hair, and plant fibers; lined with fine grass and feathers
- Nest location: On ground — in depression sheltered by rock, tuft of grass, or low shrub; above timberline
- Clutch size: 4-7 (typically 5)
- Incubation: 13-15 days (female)
- Fledging: 14-15 days
- Broods per year: 1 (short alpine breeding season)
- Breeding season: June-August (among the latest breeders in the PNW, constrained by alpine snowmelt timing)

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~26 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Climate change compressing alpine breeding habitat (upslope range contraction); alpine recreation disturbance at nest sites; pesticide exposure on agricultural wintering grounds
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: A rapid, repeated *chippy-chippy-chippy-chippy* delivered in display flight high above alpine breeding territory — a simple, rhythmic series of sharp notes that carries across the open terrain
- Calls: Thin, sharp *pip-it* or *tsip-it* — the namesake call, given frequently in flight

**Evolutionary Note:** American Pipit was formerly treated as conspecific with the Eurasian Water Pipit (*Anthus spinoletta*) — the split reflects genetic and vocal differences between New and Old World populations. The species is the most widespread alpine-breeding passerine in western North America, nesting on mountaintops from Alaska to New Mexico. Its alpine-to-lowland altitudinal migration parallels that of Mountain Bluebird and Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, but pipits descend farther, reaching sea-level mudflats and agricultural fields. The diagnostic tail-bobbing behavior, shared with all pipits and wagtails, is thought to function in flushing hidden prey from substrate [O1, O3, P17].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Breeding | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian/estuarine | Wintering | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 6: Coastal (mudflats) | Wintering | Fairly common | Visitor |
| Zone 7: Steppe (agricultural) | Wintering | Common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O3] AOS; [O4] PIF; [P17] Johnson & Dueser 2010

## 14. Family Bombycillidae — Waxwings

Waxwings are among the most beautiful and specialized passerines in the PNW — sleek, crested, fruit-eating birds with unique waxy red tips on their secondary flight feathers (actually flattened extensions of the feather shaft coated in carotenoid pigment). The family contains only three species worldwide; two occur in the PNW. Both are specialist frugivores whose movements are driven almost entirely by fruit availability, making them nomadic and sometimes irruptive.

---

### Cedar Waxwing (*Bombycilla cedrorum*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Bombycillidae
**Status:** Resident / breeding (nomadic)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15-18 cm
- Wingspan: 22-30 cm
- Mass: 30-36 g
- Plumage: Warm brown above, fading to pale yellow belly; sleek crest; black mask with white border; yellow-tipped tail; waxy red tips on secondaries. Immatures similar but with streaked breast and less defined mask.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal — males average slightly wider black chin, more extensive waxy tips

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open woodland, forest edges, riparian corridors, suburban areas with ornamental berry trees; anywhere with abundant fruit-bearing trees and shrubs
- Wintering: Nomadic — moves in flocks following fruit availability; concentrates around fruiting mountain ash, hawthorn, holly, crabapple, and ornamental plantings
- Elevation range: Sea level to 4,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Microhabitat: Wherever fruit is abundant — canopy of fruiting trees; riparian corridors with wild berry thickets; suburban areas with ornamental plantings

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Fruit — one of the most specialized frugivores in North America; berries comprise >80% of annual diet
- Foraging strategy: Perch-and-pluck frugivore — sits in fruiting tree canopy, reaches for berries, swallows them whole. Flocks methodically strip fruit from trees. Also sallies for flying insects over water during breeding season (protein for nestling growth). Known for food-passing behavior — birds in a flock pass berries bill-to-bill down a line of perched individuals.
- Seasonal variation: Fruit year-round; insectivorous component increases in summer (hawking over streams)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup of twigs, grass, bark, and plant fibers; lined with fine grass, rootlets, and hair
- Nest location: On horizontal branch, typically 2-6 m up in deciduous or conifer tree; often near water
- Clutch size: 3-6 (typically 4-5)
- Incubation: 12-14 days (female)
- Fledging: 14-18 days
- Broods per year: 1-2
- Breeding season: Late May-September (one of the latest breeding passerines in the PNW — timing driven by fruit availability for nestlings)

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to increasing [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~64 million (PIF 2020)
- Threats: Window strikes (flocks flushed into glass); alcohol toxicity (fermented berries can intoxicate birds, causing erratic flight and collision mortality); pesticide exposure on fruit crops
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Waxwings do not have a true territorial song — instead, produce a constant, thin, high-pitched *sreeee* trilling, often in chorus from a flock
- Calls: High, thin *sree* or *zeeee* — a constant contact trill; flocks are identifiable by the high-pitched buzzing of many birds calling simultaneously

**Evolutionary Note:** Cedar Waxwing is one of only three species in family Bombycillidae (with Bohemian Waxwing and Japanese Waxwing). The "waxy" red secondary tips are composed of astaxanthin — a carotenoid pigment deposited in a flattened, expanded feather-shaft extension. The number of waxy tips increases with age and may serve as an honest signal of individual quality during mate selection. The species' late breeding phenology (among the latest-nesting passerines in North America) is an adaptation to its frugivorous diet — berries are most abundant in late summer and fall, precisely when waxwing nestlings require the most food. Cedar Waxwings are primary seed dispersers for many PNW native plants (red elderberry, salal, madrone, osoberry) and non-native invasive plants alike — their role in plant community dynamics is substantial and ecologically ambivalent [O1].

**Ecoregion Presence:**
| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common | Characteristic (frugivore-disperser) |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Year-round | Fairly common | Characteristic |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding | Common | Characteristic |

**Sources:** [G4] USGS BBS; [O1] Cornell; [O4] PIF; [O7] Aversa et al.

---

### Bohemian Waxwing (*Bombycilla garrulus*)

**Taxonomy:** Passeriformes > Bombycillidae
**Status:** Wintering visitor (irruptive)
**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 19-23 cm
- Wingspan: 32-37 cm
- Mass: 50-67 g (substantially larger than Cedar Waxwing)
- Plumage: Similar overall shape and structure to Cedar Waxwing but larger and grayer; prominent cinnamon undertail coverts (Cedar has white); white and yellow wing markings more extensive; rufous wash on face. Waxy red secondary tips as in Cedar. Gray belly (Cedar has yellow).
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal — males average more extensive waxy tips and bolder throat patch

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Boreal forest (breeds in Alaska, northern BC, and across boreal Canada — not a regular PNW breeder)
- Wintering: Irruptive visitor to PNW lowlands and towns — appears in large flocks during winters of poor northern fruit crop; concentrates around fruiting trees (mountain ash, hawthorn, crabapple, juniper) in urban and suburban areas
- Elevation range: Sea level to 3,000 ft (in PNW wintering range)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban — wintering)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Foothill — wintering), Zone 7 (Steppe — juniper berry sources)
- Microhabitat: Fruiting trees in urban, suburban, and semi-rural settings during winter irruptions

**Diet & Foraging:**
- Primary diet: Fruit — even more exclusively frugivorous than Cedar Waxwing; mountain ash berries are the primary winter food in PNW
- Foraging strategy: Perch-and-pluck from fruiting trees; flocks of 50-500+ birds descend on a fruiting tree and strip it bare, then move to the next. Food-passing behavior shared with Cedar Waxwing.
- Seasonal variation: Fruit year-round; insects taken during boreal summer breeding season (not applicable to PNW observations)

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Open cup in boreal conifer forest (not in PNW; breeding range is boreal)
- Clutch size: 4-6
- Incubation: 13-14 days
- Fledging: 15-17 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: June-July (boreal)

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable [G4]
- Estimate: Continental population ~3 million (PIF 2020); highly variable annual occurrence in PNW
- Threats: Boreal forest loss at breeding range; window strikes during winter irruptions; fermented berry intoxication
- Source: USGS BBS [G4], PIF [O4]

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Similar to Cedar Waxwing but lower-pitched and rougher — a trilling *sirrr* or *zirrrr*
- Calls: Lower, buzzier *zirr* than Cedar Waxwing; distinguishable with practice

**Evolutionary Note:** Bohemian Waxwing has a Holarctic distribution — it breeds across both the boreal forests of North America and Eurasia, the only waxwing species to span both continents. Its irruptive winter movements into the PNW are driven by failure of the boreal fruit crop (primarily mountain ash) — in years of poor fruit production in the boreal zone, tens of thousands of Bohemian Waxwings may pour south into PNW towns, creating one of the most spectacular birding events of the winter season. These irruptions are unpredictable and highly variable — some winters produce no Bohemian Waxwings in the PNW, while others bring flocks of 500+ to urban mountain ash trees. The species' nomadic, irruptive lifestyle is the most extreme expression of the frugivore's fundamental challenge: fruit is a patchily distributed, temporally variable resource, and specialization on fruit requires the ability to move long distances in search of it [O1, O16].
