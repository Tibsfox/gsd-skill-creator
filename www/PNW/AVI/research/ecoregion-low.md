## Document Structure

This document surveys avian communities across the lower-elevation and aquatic zones of the Pacific Northwest, from the western foothills and valley floors through riparian corridors, the coastal strip, the intertidal zone, and out into the open ocean. Where Module 3 Part A covers alpine, subalpine, and montane communities (ELEV-ALPINE through ELEV-MONTANE), this companion document covers the remaining six ecoregion bands.

The organizing principle is community ecology: species assemblages, guild structure, trophic interactions, seasonal dynamics, and conservation status within each zone. Individual species profiles at the full card level are produced in Modules 1 and 2; this document characterizes the communities those species form.

**The Salmon Thread** runs through every zone in this document. Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) connect ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (adult ocean foraging), ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (adult return and juvenile rearing), ELEV-INTERTIDAL and ELEV-COASTAL (estuary transition), and ELEV-RIPARIAN (spawning and juvenile rearing) into a single nutrient conveyor. Avian communities at every stop along this conveyor are shaped by salmon presence, absence, and decline.

## 1. ELEV-LOWLAND (500-2,000 ft) — Western Foothills, Valley Floor, and Urban Interface

**ECO Band:** ELEV-LOWLAND | **Minecraft Y:** y=-29 to y=9 | **Precipitation:** 35-100 in/yr (predominantly rain)

**Dominant vegetation:** Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*), red alder (*Alnus rubra*); sword fern (*Polystichum munitum*) and salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) understory. Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) on drier south-facing slopes and in Willamette, Umpqua, and Rogue valleys. See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-LOWLAND habitat characterization).

The lowland band is the most human-modified ecoregion in the Pacific Northwest. More than 90% of original old-growth forest has been removed since the mid-19th century (source: G-22). This band contains the major population centers (Seattle, Portland, Eugene, Salem, Olympia), the most productive agricultural land, and the highest road density. Avian communities here are stratified into three distinct sub-communities: western foothills with oak-savanna remnants, the valley floor agricultural landscape, and the urban/suburban interface.

### 1.1 Western Foothills: Oak-Savanna Community (AVI-ZONE-3)

**Habitat:** Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) woodlands and savannas on south- and west-facing slopes at 500-1,500 ft elevation, primarily in the Willamette Valley, Umpqua Valley, and southern Puget Sound prairies. These habitats are fire-maintained: suppression of Indigenous burning practices since the 1850s has allowed Douglas-fir encroachment, reducing oak habitat by an estimated 90-99% from pre-contact extent (source: O-05). See ECO: flora-survey (Oregon white oak distribution).

**Community composition:** 35-50 breeding bird species in intact oak-savanna fragments. This is a species-poor community relative to closed-canopy forest, but contains several specialists found nowhere else in the PNW west-side landscape.

#### Indicator and Keystone Species

**Acorn Woodpecker** (*Melanerpes formicivorus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (500-1,500 ft)
- Habitat: HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE
- Ecological role: ROLE-ECOSYSTEM-ENGINEER, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
- Guild: Bark prober / granivore
- Cooperative breeder — lives in family groups of 2-15 individuals maintaining communal granary trees (dead snags or utility poles drilled with thousands of acorn-sized holes). Granary trees are multi-generational resources used for decades. Acorn storage behavior drives oak regeneration through forgotten or abandoned caches.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-SC (peripheral population); Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- PNW range is the northern limit of the species' distribution. Washington populations are small and isolated, primarily in the Columbia River Gorge and Klickitat County (source: G-14, G-23).
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-14, G-23, O-05, O-02

**Lewis's Woodpecker** (*Melanerpes lewis*)
- Residency: Summer breeder (lowland oak) / Year-round in southern Oregon
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE (transition)
- Habitat: HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, AVI-HAB-SNAG
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Aerial insectivore (unique among woodpeckers — sallies from perches like a flycatcher rather than excavating bark)
- Requires open-canopy woodland with snags for nesting and open understory for aerial foraging. Post-fire habitat specialist in ponderosa pine on east side. Declining across range due to fire suppression, snag removal, and European Starling nest competition (source: G-18).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-SC; Oregon ST-SC; IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Continental Concern score 14 (high concern) (source: O-04)
- Population trend: BBS data show -2.7% annual decline across PNW 1966-2019 (source: G-18)
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-14, G-18, O-04, O-05

**Western Bluebird** (*Sialia mexicana*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (lowland) / Partial altitudinal migrant
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (primary), ELEV-MONTANE (summer, upper range)
- Habitat: HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Ground forager / aerial insectivore
- Cavity nester dependent on woodpecker holes or nest boxes. Competes with European Starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*) and House Sparrow (*Passer domesticus*) for cavities.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-NL; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Active nest box programs in Willamette Valley and San Juan Islands have stabilized local populations (source: O-02, G-14).
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-14, O-02, O-05

**White-breasted Nuthatch** (*Sitta carolinensis*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND
- Habitat: HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Bark prober (descending headfirst — niche separation from creepers and other nuthatches)
- Oak-woodland specialist on west side; also uses mixed deciduous-conifer at lower elevations. The PNW subspecies (*S. c. aculeata*) is associated with Oregon white oak and is distinct from the interior subspecies (*S. c. tenuissima*) found east of the Cascades (source: O-03).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: O-03, O-07, G-14

#### Oak-Savanna Community Guild Structure

| Guild | Species | Foraging Stratum | Season |
|-------|---------|-----------------|--------|
| Cavity nester (primary) | Acorn Woodpecker, Lewis's Woodpecker, Northern Flicker | Trunk/snag | Year-round |
| Cavity nester (secondary) | Western Bluebird, White-breasted Nuthatch, Violet-green Swallow, Tree Swallow, Western Screech-Owl | Trunk/snag (cavities) | Year-round (bluebird, nuthatch, owl); Summer (swallows) |
| Canopy gleaner | Chestnut-backed Chickadee, Bushtit, Hutton's Vireo | Canopy/mid-canopy | Year-round |
| Ground forager | California Quail, Spotted Towhee, Dark-eyed Junco, Western Meadowlark (edges) | Ground | Year-round |
| Aerial insectivore | Lewis's Woodpecker, Western Wood-Pewee, Olive-sided Flycatcher | Aerial (from perch) | Summer |
| Raptor | Red-tailed Hawk, American Kestrel, Cooper's Hawk | Aerial/canopy | Year-round |

**Species richness:** 35-50 breeding species in intact fragments; 20-30 in degraded remnants. Richness increases with fragment size; patches <10 acres lose area-sensitive species (source: O-05, P-20).

**Seasonal variation:**
- *Breeding (April-July):* Peak richness. Neotropical migrants add Western Wood-Pewee, Olive-sided Flycatcher, Ash-throated Flycatcher (southern Oregon), Lazuli Bunting.
- *Winter (November-March):* Reduced richness. Resident species plus winter arrivals: Golden-crowned Sparrow, Ruby-crowned Kinglet, Hermit Thrush.
- *Migration (March-May, August-October):* Transient warblers and sparrows pass through oak canopy.

**Conservation threats:**
1. **Douglas-fir encroachment** from fire suppression — the single largest threat to oak-savanna avifauna. Without periodic low-intensity fire, Douglas-fir overtopping eliminates open-canopy structure within 50-80 years (source: O-05).
2. **Habitat fragmentation** — remaining oak patches are small and isolated; edge effects reduce interior habitat.
3. **Cavity competition** from European Starling and House Sparrow (introduced species).
4. **Vineyard and orchard conversion** — Willamette Valley oak-savanna converted to wine grape production.
5. **Climate change** — northward range shifts may benefit some oak-associated species but cannot compensate for habitat loss.

- See ECO: flora-survey (Oregon white oak distribution and fire ecology)
- See GDN: heritage-skills (oak restoration projects and prescribed fire)

### 1.2 Valley Floor Agricultural Landscape (AVI-ZONE-8)

**Habitat:** Flat to gently rolling terrain below 500 ft in the Willamette, Rogue, and Skagit valleys; also the Columbia Basin agricultural zone east of the Cascades. Converted from native prairie and wetland to row crops, pasture, grass seed production, and nursery stock. Residual habitat in hedgerows, drainage ditches, farm ponds, and field margins. Seasonal flooding of fallow fields creates temporary shorebird and waterfowl habitat.

**Community composition:** 25-40 breeding species. Low species richness but high individual abundance — agricultural landscapes support enormous populations of a few generalist and edge species.

#### Characteristic Species

**Red-winged Blackbird** (*Agelaius phoeniceus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (lowland) / Partial migrant (some winter withdrawal from uplands)
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN (marsh edges)
- Habitat: AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND, HAB-WETLAND (marsh edges), agricultural fields
- Ecological role: ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Ground forager / generalist
- The most abundant land bird in North America. Males territorial in marshes and field edges; females nest in dense vegetation near water. Breeds in loose colonies. Post-breeding flocks of thousands form in agricultural fields July-October.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Population trend: BBS data show slight decline (-0.6%/yr) across PNW 1966-2019, following North American trend (source: G-18).
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-18, O-02, O-07

**Western Meadowlark** (*Sturnella neglecta*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (valleys) / Partial altitudinal migrant
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE
- Habitat: AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND, agricultural pasture
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Ground forager (probes soil for invertebrates)
- State bird of Oregon. Requires open grassland with low vegetation (<30 cm) for nesting. Ground-nester; nests concealed in grass tufts with woven grass canopy. Vulnerable to early-season mowing of hayfields during nesting (May-July).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-NL; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Population trend: BBS data show -1.3%/yr decline across PNW 1966-2019. Grassland bird declines are the steepest of any habitat guild in North America (source: G-18, O-04).
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-18, O-04, O-07

**Barn Owl** (*Tyto alba*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND
- Habitat: Agricultural fields, grasslands, HAB-URBAN (barns, silos, nest boxes)
- Ecological role: ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
- Guild: Raptor (nocturnal)
- Specialist nocturnal predator on voles (*Microtus* spp.) and deer mice (*Peromyscus maniculatus*). Hunts by sound using asymmetric ear placement and facial disc. Agricultural landscapes provide ideal hunting habitat; old barns and nest boxes provide nesting sites. A single nesting pair may consume 2,000-3,000 rodents per year, providing measurable rodent control in agricultural settings (source: O-07, G-14).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-NL; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Salmon Thread: No
- See MAM (pending): Townsend's vole (*Microtus townsendii*) (primary prey item)
- Key Sources: G-14, O-07, O-22

**European Starling** (*Sturnus vulgaris*) — INVASIVE
- Residency: Year-round resident (introduced 1890, New York; reached PNW by 1940s)
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (primary), extends into all human-modified habitats
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN, agricultural, HAB-OAK-PRAIRIE (invasive into)
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER (competitor)
- Guild: Ground forager / generalist
- Aggressive cavity competitor that displaces native cavity nesters (Lewis's Woodpecker, Western Bluebird, Purple Martin, Northern Flicker). Forms winter roosts of thousands to hundreds of thousands of individuals in urban areas. Diet is omnivorous: invertebrates, fruit, grain, garbage.
- Conservation: ESA-NL (non-native; no protection under Migratory Bird Treaty Act)
- **Ecological threat:** Primary driver of native cavity-nester decline in lowland habitats. Starlings are larger and more aggressive than most native secondary cavity nesters and can evict species from nest holes during egg-laying (source: G-14, P-21).
- Relationship: REL-COMP-EUST-LEWO (European Starling vs. Lewis's Woodpecker — Competition, Interference, Nest cavities, Competitive exclusion in progress; source: G-14, O-05)
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-14, P-21, O-07

#### Agricultural Community Guild Structure

| Guild | Species | Status | Abundance |
|-------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Grassland ground nester | Western Meadowlark, Savannah Sparrow, Horned Lark | Native | Declining |
| Marsh edge | Red-winged Blackbird, Marsh Wren, Common Yellowthroat | Native | Stable-declining |
| Agricultural raptor | Barn Owl, American Kestrel, Red-tailed Hawk, Northern Harrier | Native | Stable |
| Aerial insectivore | Barn Swallow, Cliff Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Vaux's Swift | Native | Declining |
| Introduced competitor | European Starling, House Sparrow, Rock Pigeon | Invasive | Stable-increasing |
| Winter waterfowl | Cackling Goose, Dusky Canada Goose, Trumpeter Swan (Skagit) | Native | Stable-increasing |

**Seasonal variation:**
- *Breeding (April-July):* Grassland nesters active; swallows arrive March-April; meadowlarks singing from fence posts.
- *Fall (August-November):* Massive blackbird and starling flocks form. Shorebirds use flooded fields (Dunlin, Killdeer, Long-billed Dowitcher). Raptors concentrate along valley edges for migration.
- *Winter (November-March):* Waterfowl dominate. Skagit and Stillaguamish flats host 30,000+ Trumpeter and Tundra Swans, Snow Geese, and Cackling Geese (source: G-19). Short-eared Owls and Northern Harriers hunt vole-rich pastures.

**Conservation threats:**
1. **Grassland conversion** — native prairie <1% of historical extent in Willamette Valley.
2. **Pesticide exposure** — insecticides reduce invertebrate prey base for aerial insectivores; rodenticides accumulate in Barn Owls and raptors via secondary poisoning.
3. **Early mowing** destroys ground nests (meadowlark, savannah sparrow) before fledging.
4. **Wetland drainage** eliminates marsh-edge habitat for blackbirds, rails, and snipe.
5. **Invasive species** — starlings and House Sparrows dominate cavity resources.

**Cross-references:**
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-LOWLAND agricultural conversion)
- See GDN: heritage-skills (farmland bird conservation, nest box programs)

### 1.3 Urban and Suburban Interface (AVI-ZONE-4, lower portion)

**Habitat:** Cities, towns, suburban neighborhoods, parks, and green corridors throughout the PNW lowland. Urban forests, residential gardens, cemeteries, college campuses, and urban waterways. Seattle, Portland, Eugene, and Olympia all retain significant urban tree canopy (30-40% cover) and greenway corridors that support diverse avian communities for cities of their size.

**Community composition:** 40-65 breeding species in cities with good tree canopy and green corridors; 15-25 in dense commercial/industrial zones. Urban avian communities are structured by tree canopy cover, patch size of green space, distance to water, and presence of native plant landscaping. See GDN: heritage-skills (backyard habitat certification programs).

#### Characteristic Species

**Anna's Hummingbird** (*Calypte anna*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (post-1960s range expansion northward into PNW)
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (urban and suburban primary range in PNW)
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN, gardens, parks, riparian edges
- Ecological role: ROLE-POLLINATOR
- Guild: Nectarivore
- The only year-round hummingbird in the PNW. Original range was California and Baja; northward expansion into Oregon (1960s), Washington (1970s), and British Columbia (1980s-present) was facilitated by winter-flowering ornamental plants and hummingbird feeders in residential gardens. Males sing a complex, scratchy song year-round and perform spectacular courtship dive displays reaching speeds of 385 body lengths per second (source: O-07, P-17).
- Breeds January-June in PNW (earliest nesting bird in the region). Nest is a tiny cup of plant down, spider silk, and lichen on a sheltered branch.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Population increasing and range expanding (source: G-18).
- **Pollination ecology:** Visits tubular flowers including native red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*), salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), and ornamental fuchsias, salvias, and penstemons. See GDN: heritage-skills (hummingbird garden plants).
- Salmon Thread: No (but feeds on insects associated with riparian flowering plants along salmon streams — indirect connection)
- Key Sources: G-18, O-02, O-07, P-17

**Cooper's Hawk** (*Accipiter cooperii*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (primary), ELEV-MONTANE (summer range expansion)
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, suburban neighborhoods with mature trees
- Ecological role: ROLE-MESOPREDATOR
- Guild: Raptor (diurnal, bird specialist)
- The quintessential urban raptor adapter. Cooper's Hawks have colonized cities across the PNW since the 1990s, nesting in residential neighborhoods, city parks, and college campuses. Urban populations achieve higher nesting density and earlier laying dates than rural populations, likely due to milder urban microclimates, abundant prey (songbirds at feeders, Rock Pigeons, European Starlings), and reduced persecution (source: O-22, G-14).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Population trend: BBS data show significant increase (+2.1%/yr PNW 1966-2019), one of the strongest positive trends among raptors (source: G-18). Recovery from DDT-era declines is essentially complete.
- Relationship: REL-PRED-COHA-songbirds (Cooper's Hawk preys on House Finch, European Starling, American Robin, Dark-eyed Junco in urban settings; predation at bird feeders creates "landscape of fear" effects on songbird feeding behavior; source: O-22)
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-14, G-18, O-07, O-22

**American Robin** (*Turdus migratorius*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (lowland) / Partial migrant (upland populations move to lowland in winter)
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (primary year-round), ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (summer)
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND, lawns, parks
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-SEED-DISPERSER
- Guild: Ground forager (earthworms, insects) / Frugivore (fall-winter)
- The most familiar bird in the PNW. Robins forage on lawns by running and pausing, tilting the head to locate earthworms by sight (not sound, as popularly believed). Fall and winter diet shifts to fruit: madrone (*Arbutus menziesii*), holly (*Ilex aquifolium* — invasive), hawthorn (*Crataegus* spp.), and mountain ash (*Sorbus* spp.). Winter roosts of hundreds to thousands form in dense conifers.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: O-02, O-07, G-18

**Black-capped Chickadee** (*Poecile atricapillus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (primary), ELEV-RIPARIAN
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN, HAB-SECOND-GROWTH, suburban yards with mature deciduous trees
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Canopy gleaner
- Year-round resident of deciduous and mixed woodlands from urban parks to rural forest edges. Core member of mixed-species foraging flocks in winter (with Bushtit, Red-breasted Nuthatch, Brown Creeper, Downy Woodpecker, kinglets). Black-capped Chickadee replaces Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*) in drier, more open lowland habitats; the two species overlap in moist lowland forest and exhibit competitive niche partitioning where sympatric (source: O-07, O-20).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: O-07, O-20, G-18

**House Finch** (*Haemorhous mexicanus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-LOWLAND (urban/suburban obligate in PNW)
- Habitat: HAB-URBAN
- Ecological role: ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Seed specialist / generalist
- Native to western North America but historically a bird of arid scrub and canyon habitats. Has become one of the most abundant urban birds in the PNW. Males display variable red-orange-yellow plumage; carotenoid pigment intensity correlates with diet quality and is used in mate selection.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- **Disease ecology:** PNW House Finch populations experienced outbreaks of Mycoplasmal conjunctivitis (1994-present, originating from eastern introduced populations), which causes eye lesions and mortality. The disease has reduced House Finch populations 30-60% in parts of eastern North America but has had less severe impacts in the western native range (source: G-18).
- Salmon Thread: No
- Key Sources: G-18, O-02, O-07

#### Urban Community Guild Structure

| Guild | Species | Adaptation Type | Population Trend |
|-------|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Urban raptor | Cooper's Hawk, Red-tailed Hawk (park edges), Peregrine Falcon (tall buildings), Merlin (winter) | Behavioral (nest site, prey switching) | Increasing |
| Nectarivore | Anna's Hummingbird, Rufous Hummingbird (summer) | Range expansion (Anna's), migration timing (Rufous) | Anna's increasing; Rufous declining |
| Backyard songbird | American Robin, Black-capped Chickadee, Song Sparrow, Spotted Towhee, Dark-eyed Junco, Bewick's Wren | Habitat generalism | Mostly stable |
| Urban seed specialist | House Finch, House Sparrow (invasive), Pine Siskin (irruptive), American Goldfinch | Feeder use | Variable |
| Corvid | American Crow, Steller's Jay (suburban edge), Common Raven (increasing in urban) | Cognitive flexibility, omnivory | Increasing |
| Aerial insectivore | Vaux's Swift (chimney roost), Violet-green Swallow, Barn Swallow | Structure dependent | Declining |

**Seasonal variation:**
- *Breeding (March-July):* Anna's Hummingbird nests as early as January-February. Most passerines breed April-July. Urban heat island effect advances phenology 5-10 days relative to rural areas (source: O-02).
- *Fall migration (August-November):* Mixed-species warbler flocks move through urban canopy. Varied Thrush arrives from montane breeding range. Band-tailed Pigeon flocks pass through.
- *Winter (November-March):* Mixed-species flocks of chickadees, kinglets, creepers, and nuthatches work through urban trees. Golden-crowned Sparrow common in residential shrubs. Anna's Hummingbird remains (the only hummingbird present). Merlin (*Falco columbarius*) overwinters in cities, hunting European Starlings and House Sparrows.

**Conservation considerations:**
1. **Window strikes** — estimated 600 million to 1 billion bird deaths annually in North America from window collisions; urban and suburban buildings are primary sources of mortality. Bird-safe glass and window treatments reduce kills by 70-90% (source: O-17).
2. **Cat predation** — domestic and feral cats kill an estimated 1.3-4.0 billion birds annually in the U.S. Ground-nesting and ground-foraging species (Song Sparrow, Dark-eyed Junco, Spotted Towhee) are most vulnerable (source: O-04).
3. **Light pollution** — disrupts migration behavior of nocturnal migrants; urban centers along the Pacific Flyway contribute to collision mortality during spring and fall migration.
4. **Native plant landscaping** — residential properties planted with native species support 2-4 times more caterpillar biomass than non-native landscaping, directly supporting insectivorous bird species (source: O-05).
5. **Urban canopy loss** — removal of mature street trees and park trees eliminates nesting and foraging substrate.

- See ECO: shared-attributes (HAB-URBAN characterization)
- See AVI Module 1: Anna's Hummingbird, Cooper's Hawk, American Crow (full species cards)

### 1.4 ELEV-LOWLAND Community Synthesis

The lowland band contains the greatest human-bird interface in the PNW. Three sub-communities (oak-savanna, agricultural, urban) share few species in common beyond generalists like Red-tailed Hawk, American Robin, and European Starling. Each sub-community faces distinct threats:

| Sub-Community | Primary Threat | Species at Risk | Conservation Priority |
|---------------|---------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Oak-savanna | Fire suppression / Douglas-fir encroachment | Lewis's Woodpecker, Acorn Woodpecker, Western Bluebird | Prescribed fire, oak restoration |
| Agricultural | Grassland conversion, pesticides | Western Meadowlark, Barn Owl, Streaked Horned Lark (ESA-T) | Grassland set-asides, delayed mowing |
| Urban | Window strikes, cats, light pollution | Vaux's Swift, aerial insectivores generally | Bird-safe design, cat management |

**Streaked Horned Lark** (*Eremophila alpestris strigata*) — ESA-T (Threatened, listed 2013). This subspecies is endemic to the PNW lowland prairie and is the only federally listed bird whose primary habitat is within the ELEV-LOWLAND band west of the Cascades. Breeds on sparsely vegetated ground (airports, gravel bars, prairie remnants) in the Willamette Valley and southern Puget Sound. Estimated population of 1,174-1,610 individuals as of 2012 (source: P-10, G-17). See AVI Module 1 for full species card. Nest and breeding site locations generalized to county level per SC-END.

## 2. ELEV-RIPARIAN (Cross-cutting) — The Salmon Thread Zone

**ECO Band:** ELEV-RIPARIAN | **Minecraft Y:** y=-64 to y=319 (cross-cutting all elevations) | **Precipitation:** Corridor variable, augmented by stream channel and groundwater

**Dominant vegetation shifts with elevation:**
- Headwater (alpine/subalpine): Mountain alder (*Alnus incana*), willow (*Salix* spp.)
- Montane/lowland: Black cottonwood (*Populus trichocarpa*), red alder (*Alnus rubra*), salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), devil's club (*Oplopanax horridus*)
- Estuary (coastal): Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), sedges (*Carex* spp.), skunk cabbage (*Lysichiton americanus*)
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-RIPARIAN full characterization)

The riparian corridor is the most species-rich avian habitat in the Pacific Northwest on a per-unit-area basis. Riparian zones constitute less than 2% of the western landscape but support disproportionate avian diversity — 70-80% of PNW bird species use riparian habitats during some part of their annual cycle (source: G-14, P-20).

**This is the Salmon Thread zone.** More than any other ecoregion band, ELEV-RIPARIAN is where avian ecology directly intersects with Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) ecology. Salmon connect the ocean to the headwaters, carrying marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen-15, carbon-13, phosphorus) hundreds of miles inland. When salmon spawn and die, their carcasses feed birds directly (Bald Eagle, Common Raven, American Crow, gulls) and indirectly through nutrient enrichment of riparian vegetation and aquatic invertebrates that support the entire riparian food web (source: P-18). See ECO: shared-attributes (salmon-nutrient-cycling pathway).

### 2.1 Riparian Specialist Guild: Stream-Dependent Species

#### American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)

**The signature bird of PNW riparian ecology.** The American Dipper is the only truly aquatic songbird in North America. It is an obligate stream bird — every aspect of its life cycle is tied to fast-flowing, clear, cold streams from alpine headwaters to lowland rivers.

- Residency: Year-round resident (altitudinal migrant within the riparian corridor: breeds at montane-subalpine elevations, may descend to lowland in winter when high-elevation streams freeze)
- Elevation: ELEV-RIPARIAN at ELEV-SUBALPINE through ELEV-LOWLAND (500-6,000 ft)
- Habitat: HAB-STREAM (fast-flowing, boulder-strewn streams with riffles and cascades)
- Ecological role: ROLE-INDICATOR (water quality), ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Aquatic subsurface forager

**Foraging ecology:** Dips, dives, and walks along stream bottoms, using wings for underwater propulsion. Feeds primarily on aquatic invertebrate larvae (caddisfly, mayfly, stonefly) and occasionally small fish including salmon fry. Possesses unique adaptations: nasal flaps to close nostrils underwater; dense waterproof plumage with 50% more contour feathers than similar-sized passerines; unusually high hemoglobin concentration for oxygen storage during submersion; nictitating membrane acts as underwater goggles; enlarged preen gland produces copious oil for waterproofing (source: O-07, O-20).

**Indicator value:** Dipper presence and breeding success correlate tightly with stream water quality, macroinvertebrate density, and lack of sedimentation. Streams where dippers breed consistently are streams with healthy aquatic insect communities. Dipper absence from historically occupied streams is a reliable signal of water quality degradation (source: G-14, G-25).

**Nesting:** Builds dome-shaped nest of moss on stream-adjacent rocks, bridges, or cliff faces, always within spray distance of flowing water. Nest exterior is kept damp and green by stream spray, camouflaging it against mossy rock. Clutch: 3-5 eggs. Both parents feed nestlings.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Primary connection. Dippers feed on aquatic invertebrates that are themselves nourished by salmon carcass nutrients. In streams with active salmon runs, aquatic invertebrate biomass increases 2-25x following spawning events (source: P-18). Dippers also feed directly on salmon eggs and alevin. Dipper breeding density is higher on streams with salmon runs than on comparable fishless streams, even when controlling for stream size, gradient, and water chemistry — suggesting a direct salmon-subsidy effect on dipper populations (source: G-14, P-18).

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-25, O-07, O-20, P-18

#### Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*)

- Residency: Year-round resident (lowland streams) / Partial migrant (montane populations descend in winter)
- Elevation: ELEV-RIPARIAN at ELEV-LOWLAND through ELEV-MONTANE
- Habitat: HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN (any water body with small fish and earthen banks)
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore (plunge diver from perch or hover)

**Foraging ecology:** Hunts from exposed perches overhanging water or by hovering 20-40 ft above the surface, then plunge-diving headfirst to capture small fish (3-12 cm). Prey includes sculpins, sticklebacks, juvenile salmonids, dace, trout, and crayfish. Regurgitates pellets of indigestible fish bones and scales.

**Nesting:** Excavates burrow 3-6 ft deep in vertical earthen or clay banks along streams, road cuts, gravel pits, or lakeshores. Both sexes excavate. Burrow-nesting limits distribution to areas with suitable bank substrate — a habitat feature that can be limiting in heavily channelized or armored streams.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Kingfishers are significant predators of salmon fry during outmigration. A single kingfisher may consume 30-50 juvenile salmonids per day during peak smolt migration (April-June). This predation is a natural component of stream ecology, but salmon hatchery managers have historically persecuted kingfishers near release sites. Kingfishers also consume salmon eggs exposed by redd superimposition (source: G-14, G-20).

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-20, O-07

#### Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*)

- Residency: Year-round resident (coastal and lowland) / Partial migrant (inland populations may move to coast in winter)
- Elevation: ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-LOWLAND
- Habitat: HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-SANDY-BEACH, AVI-HAB-MUDFLAT
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Wading piscivore / generalist

**The PNW's largest wading bird.** Standing 4 ft tall with a 6-ft wingspan, the Great Blue Heron is a patient ambush predator that wades in shallow water and strikes at fish, frogs, snakes, voles, and large invertebrates. Breeds colonially in heronries (rookeries) of 10-200+ nests in tall trees (cottonwood, Douglas-fir, Sitka spruce) near water. Major PNW heronries are located near productive estuaries and river confluences (source: G-14, G-23).

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Great Blue Herons are significant scavengers of salmon carcasses during and after spawning runs. Along streams with active spawning, herons have been documented feeding on post-spawn salmon carcasses, salmon eggs, and disoriented salmon fry. Herons transport marine-derived nutrients from riparian carcass sites into terrestrial habitats when they defecate at roost sites and heronries away from streams. Rookery soils beneath heron colonies adjacent to salmon streams show elevated nitrogen-15 signatures consistent with marine-derived nutrient input (source: P-18, G-14).

**Conservation:** ESA-NL; Washington ST-SM; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Heronries are sensitive to disturbance; human activity within 200-300 m of active colonies during nesting (February-August) can cause nest abandonment. Several major PNW heronries have been lost to development and repeated disturbance (source: G-14, G-23).

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-23, O-07, P-18

### 2.2 Salmon-Dependent Raptors

#### Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*)

- Residency: Year-round resident (coastal and lowland river systems) / Winter concentrations at salmon spawning sites
- Elevation: ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
- Habitat: HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-OLD-GROWTH (nest trees), HAB-STREAM
- Ecological role: ROLE-APEX, ROLE-KEYSTONE
- Guild: Raptor (diurnal) / Piscivore / Scavenger

**The apex avian predator of PNW riparian corridors.** Bald Eagles are the most conspicuous salmon-dependent bird in the Pacific Northwest. Their distribution, abundance, breeding success, and seasonal movements are all tightly linked to salmon availability.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Primary, defining connection. Salmon constitute the primary food source for Bald Eagles in most PNW watersheds. The relationship operates on two temporal scales:
1. **Breeding season (March-August):** Nesting pairs along rivers target spawning spring Chinook and sockeye. Pairs with territories on salmon-bearing streams produce more offspring than pairs on non-salmon streams (source: G-14).
2. **Fall-winter concentration (September-February):** Non-breeding eagles and juveniles gather in large numbers at late-season salmon spawning sites. The Skagit River (WA) hosts 400-600+ wintering Bald Eagles feeding on spawned-out chum salmon (November-February). The Nooksack River, Elwha River (post-dam removal), and upper Columbia tributaries are other major concentration sites. These aggregations represent one of the largest seasonal concentrations of raptors anywhere in the lower 48 states (source: G-14, G-19).

**Nutrient vector:** Eagles feeding on salmon carcasses along streams subsequently perch, roost, and defecate in riparian forest stands, transporting marine-derived nitrogen (N-15) and phosphorus from salmon tissue into terrestrial soils. Studies have documented elevated N-15 in riparian vegetation near eagle perch sites along salmon streams (source: P-18).

**Post-DDT recovery:** Bald Eagles were ESA-listed as Endangered in 1978 and delisted in 2007 (ESA-DL, 2007). PNW populations have recovered strongly: Washington breeding pairs increased from 104 occupied territories in 1980 to 900+ in 2005, and continued growth since delisting (source: G-17, G-14). This recovery is one of the great conservation success stories in North American wildlife management.

**Conservation:** ESA-DL (Delisted 2007); protected under Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act; Washington ST-SC; IUCN-LC.

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-17, G-19, P-18, O-07

#### Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*)

- Residency: Summer breeder (March-September) / Migrant (winters in Mexico and Central America)
- Elevation: ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-LOWLAND
- Habitat: HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-LAKE, HAB-STREAM
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Raptor (diurnal) / Diving piscivore (obligate fish specialist)

**The fish hawk.** Osprey are the only North American raptor that plunges feet-first into water to capture live fish. Reversible outer toe, barbed foot pads, and closable nostrils are morphological adaptations for aquatic hunting. Diet is >99% fish, making Osprey the most specialized piscivorous raptor in the world (source: O-07, O-22).

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Osprey prey on juvenile and adult salmonids in rivers and estuaries. They target fish in the 150-350 g range, which includes smolts, jack salmon, and resident trout in salmon-bearing streams. Osprey are less dependent on spawning carcasses than eagles; their relationship with salmon is primarily as a live-fish predator during the breeding season. Osprey arrival in the PNW (mid-March to early April) coincides with peak smolt outmigration in many coastal rivers (source: G-14, G-20).

**Nesting:** Large stick nests on snags, power poles, channel markers, and artificial platforms. Nests are reused and added to annually, reaching 3-6 ft diameter. PNW populations have benefited enormously from artificial nest platforms placed near reservoirs, estuaries, and fish hatcheries (source: G-14).

**Conservation:** ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Population increasing (source: G-18).

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-18, G-20, O-07, O-22

### 2.3 Riparian Songbird Communities

Riparian corridors support the highest density and diversity of breeding songbirds in the PNW. The multi-layered structure of mature riparian vegetation — tall cottonwoods and alders as canopy, willows and dogwoods as mid-story, salmonberry and snowberry as shrub layer, sedges and grasses as ground cover — creates vertical stratification that allows many species to coexist by foraging at different heights and in different substrates (source: P-20, G-14).

#### Warbler Guild

| Species | Scientific Name | Foraging Stratum | Migration | Salmon Thread |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|-----------|---------------|
| Yellow Warbler | *Setophaga petechia* | Canopy/mid-canopy | Neotropical (arrives April) | Indirect — breeds in salmon-riparian vegetation |
| Common Yellowthroat | *Geothlypis trichas* | Ground/low shrub | Short-distance (arrives April) | Indirect — uses marsh/riparian edge |
| MacGillivray's Warbler | *Geothlypis tolmiei* | Dense shrub | Neotropical (arrives May) | Indirect — breeds in dense riparian understory |
| Wilson's Warbler | *Cardellina pusilla* | Low canopy/shrub | Neotropical (arrives April) | Indirect — breeds in moist riparian alder-willow |
| Yellow-breasted Chat | *Icteria virens* | Dense shrub | Neotropical (arrives May) | No — uses drier riparian edges |
| Orange-crowned Warbler | *Leiothlypis celata* | Shrub/canopy | Short-distance (arrives March) | Indirect |

**Yellow Warbler** (*Setophaga petechia*) is the most widespread riparian-obligate songbird in the PNW. Breeds in willow and alder thickets along streams from sea level to 4,000 ft. Frequent host of Brown-headed Cowbird (*Molothrus ater*) nest parasitism; some populations show 30-50% parasitism rates in fragmented riparian corridors. Yellow Warblers are one of the few species that recognize cowbird eggs and sometimes build a new nest floor over parasitized clutches, resulting in multi-story nests (source: O-07, G-14).

**Willow Flycatcher** (*Empidonax traillii*) — Breeds in dense willow thickets along streams and wet meadows. The southwestern subspecies (*E. t. extimus*) is ESA-Endangered, but PNW populations (*E. t. adastus* and *E. t. brewsteri*) are not federally listed. Washington lists Willow Flycatcher as ST-NL but monitors populations in riparian restoration sites. Excellent indicator of riparian habitat quality — presence indicates multi-age willow/alder stands with dense low vegetation (source: G-14, G-15).

**Song Sparrow** (*Melospiza melodia*) — Year-round resident, most abundant sparrow in PNW riparian and wetland habitats. The PNW has remarkable Song Sparrow subspecific diversity: at least 8 described subspecies from the Aleutian Islands to northern California, varying in size, coloration, and bill dimensions. The sedentary, dark-plumaged coastal subspecies (*M. m. morphna*, *M. m. rufina*) differ strikingly from the pale, migratory inland subspecies (*M. m. merrilli*). This subspecific radiation represents an active area of evolutionary biology research (source: O-03, O-07). See AVI Module 4 (Evolutionary Biology — Song Sparrow subspeciation).

### 2.4 Beaver Pond Communities

North American beaver (*Castor canadensis*) dam complexes create wetland mosaics that fundamentally alter riparian avian communities. Beaver ponds transform narrow stream channels into complexes of open water, emergent marsh, flooded shrubland, and dead standing timber (snags created by flooding). See MAM (pending): North American beaver (ecosystem engineer).

**Species gained by beaver pond creation:**

|---------|----------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Wood Duck | *Aix sponsa* | Open water with snags | Cavity nesting in flooded dead trees |
| Hooded Merganser | *Lophodytes cucullatus* | Open water with snags | Cavity nesting in flooded dead trees |
| Green Heron | *Butorides virescens* | Shallow water with emergent vegetation | Ambush foraging on fish and frogs in slow water |
| Virginia Rail | *Rallus limicola* | Emergent marsh | Dense marsh vegetation for nesting |
| Sora | *Porzana carolina* | Emergent marsh | Dense marsh vegetation for nesting |

| Marsh Wren | *Cistothorus palustris* | Dense cattail/bulrush | Woven nest suspended in marsh stems |
| Willow Flycatcher | *Empidonax traillii* | Willow regrowth at pond margins | Dense willow thickets for nesting |
| Tree Swallow | *Tachycineta bicolor* | Snags over open water | Cavity nesting in flooded snags |
| Pied-billed Grebe | *Podilymbus podiceps* | Open water | Floating nest platform in emergent vegetation |

Beaver pond avian communities are 2-3 times more species-rich than equivalent-length stream reaches without beaver activity. Pond complexes also support higher breeding density of existing riparian species (Yellow Warbler, Song Sparrow) by increasing shrub-layer habitat area (source: G-14, G-23).

**Salmon Thread in beaver ponds:** Beaver dams and ponds provide critical rearing habitat for juvenile coho salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*). Coho smolts that rear in beaver pond complexes grow faster and have higher marine survival than those rearing in unimpounded stream channels (source: G-20). The avian communities supported by beaver ponds thus exist within a salmon-connected landscape even when the bird species themselves do not feed on salmon directly.

### 2.5 Seasonal Phenology: Migration Timing Relative to Salmon Runs

The timing of migratory bird arrival and departure in PNW riparian zones is ecologically linked to salmon run timing, though the mechanisms are parallel (both responding to photoperiod and temperature) rather than directly causal.

| Period | Migratory Bird Event | Salmon Event | Overlap Species |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| March-April | Neotropical songbirds arrive (Yellow Warbler, Wilson's Warbler); Osprey returns | Spring Chinook enter rivers | Osprey, Belted Kingfisher begin targeting smolts |
| May-June | Peak riparian songbird breeding; swallow peak | Smolt outmigration peak | Kingfisher, mergansers, herons feed on smolts |
| July-August | Post-breeding dispersal; some songbirds begin southward movement | Sockeye, summer Chinook spawning in upper watersheds | Bald Eagle, Common Raven scavenge spawned-out fish |
| September-October | Fall warbler migration; sparrow migration peak | Coho, chum salmon spawning in lower rivers | Eagle concentrations build; gulls scavenge |
| November-February | Winter residents established; irruptive species arrive | Chum and late coho spawning and die-off peak | Peak Bald Eagle concentration (Skagit, Nooksack); 400-600+ eagles at major rivers (source: G-19) |

**Critical insight:** The winter eagle-salmon relationship is unique because it occurs when most migratory songbirds are absent. The riparian corridor's winter avifauna is dominated by salmon-dependent raptors and scavengers (Bald Eagle, Common Raven, American Crow, gulls) rather than by the diverse songbird communities that characterize summer. This seasonal flip — from songbird-dominated (summer) to raptor/scavenger-dominated (winter) — is a defining feature of PNW riparian ecology that distinguishes it from non-salmonid riparian systems elsewhere in North America.

### 2.6 Riparian Conservation Threats

1. **Dam removal and restoration** — the Elwha River dam removal (2011-2014) is the largest dam removal in U.S. history and has restored anadromous fish access to 70+ miles of upstream habitat. Monitoring of post-removal avian community response is ongoing. Preliminary data show increased American Dipper density and Bald Eagle use in the restored reach (source: G-25).
2. **Riparian buffer width** — Washington and Oregon forest practice rules require riparian buffers along fish-bearing streams (50-200 ft depending on stream class). Buffer width directly determines the quality of riparian songbird habitat; buffers <100 ft support only edge-tolerant species and lose forest-interior species (source: G-22, P-20).
3. **Channelization and armoring** — bank armoring (riprap) eliminates earthen banks required for Belted Kingfisher burrow nesting and reduces floodplain connectivity that sustains cottonwood-willow regeneration.
4. **Livestock grazing** — streambank trampling by cattle eliminates riparian shrub layer, degrades water quality, and reduces both songbird nesting habitat and aquatic invertebrate prey base for dippers and kingfishers.
5. **Invasive plants** — Reed canarygrass (*Phalaris arundinacea*) and Himalayan blackberry (*Rubus armeniacus*) form monotypic stands that reduce native shrub diversity and alter the structure of riparian vegetation used by nesting warblers and sparrows.
6. **Salmon decline** — reduced salmon runs reduce the marine-derived nutrient subsidy to riparian ecosystems. Streams that have lost salmon runs support lower densities of riparian songbirds than comparable streams with intact runs, even when riparian vegetation structure appears similar (source: P-18).

## 3. ELEV-COASTAL (Sea Level-500 ft) — Coastal Temperate Rainforest, Beaches, and Estuaries

**ECO Band:** ELEV-COASTAL | **Minecraft Y:** y=-41 to y=-29 | **Precipitation:** 60-120 in/yr (outer coast) + marine fog moisture

**Dominant vegetation:** Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*) dominant in fog belt; red alder (*Alnus rubra*) on disturbed sites; shore pine (*Pinus contorta* var. *contorta*) on stabilized dunes; coastal dune grass (*Ammophila arenaria* — invasive), beach strawberry (*Fragaria chiloensis*), saltmarsh in sheltered bays. See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-COASTAL characterization). See COL: Columbia Valley Rainforest Biodiversity (`www/PNW/COL/`) for detailed characterization of the coastal temperate rainforest avifauna.

The coastal zone is defined by maritime influence: fog drip, salt spray, persistent wind, moderate temperatures year-round (rarely below 30 F or above 75 F), and proximity to the richest marine food webs on the planet. The avifauna is a mix of forest species (shared with ELEV-LOWLAND), specialists of open beach and dune, estuary-dependent shorebirds, and colonial seabirds that nest on coastal cliffs and offshore rocks but forage in ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE.

### 3.1 Coastal Temperate Rainforest Birds

The Sitka spruce zone — the narrow band of old-growth coastal forest from Kodiak Island, Alaska, south to northern California — supports a distinctive avian community characterized by high-precipitation-adapted forest birds. This habitat is described in detail in the COL (Columbia Valley Rainforest) research at `www/PNW/COL/`.

**Characteristic species:**
- **Chestnut-backed Chickadee** (*Poecile rufescens*) — The dominant chickadee of wet coastal forest. Replaces Black-capped Chickadee in closed-canopy Sitka spruce and western hemlock stands. Year-round resident. Forages in canopy and upper mid-story.
- **Pacific Wren** (*Troglodytes pacificus*) — Tiny, secretive, extraordinarily loud. Breeds in old-growth forest understory, nesting in root wads, upturned stumps, and moss banks. Song is a cascading series of trills and buzzes lasting 5-10 seconds. Recently split from Winter Wren (*T. hiemalis*) as a distinct species based on genetic and vocal differences (source: O-03).
- **Varied Thrush** (*Ixoreus naevius*) — Breeds in dense, dark coastal and montane forest. The single-note whistled song, shifting pitch with each note, is one of the most evocative sounds of PNW old-growth. Winters in lowland urban and suburban settings.
- **Marbled Murrelet** (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — ESA-T (Threatened, listed 1992). Nests on wide, moss-covered limbs of old-growth conifers up to 50 miles inland, but forages exclusively in ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE. This extraordinary cross-band life history — breeding in old-growth forest, feeding in the ocean — makes the Marbled Murrelet the most important cross-band indicator species in the PNW avian system. See Section 5.1 for marine foraging ecology. See AVI Module 1 for full species card.

**Conservation:** ESA-T (Threatened); Washington ST-T; Oregon ST-T; IUCN-EN (Endangered). Population in Washington, Oregon, and California estimated at 20,700 birds in 2021, declining at approximately 3.7% per year (source: G-09, G-13). The primary threat is loss of old-growth nesting habitat to timber harvest. At-sea threats include gillnet bycatch, oil spills, and changes in forage fish distribution. Nest locations are not published per SC-END.

**Key Sources:** G-09, G-13, G-17, P-04, P-05

### 3.2 Beach and Dune Communities

**Western Snowy Plover** (*Charadrius nivosus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (coastal) / Some post-breeding dispersal
- Elevation: ELEV-COASTAL (beach), ELEV-INTERTIDAL (upper beach)
- Habitat: HAB-SANDY-BEACH, coastal dunes
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Ground forager (invertebrates on sand surface)

The Pacific coast population of the Western Snowy Plover is ESA-T (Threatened, listed 1993). Snowy Plovers nest on open sand beaches above the high-tide line, making them exquisitely vulnerable to human disturbance (beach driving, pedestrian and dog traffic, dune stabilization with non-native vegetation). Nests are simple scrapes in sand with 2-3 cryptically colored eggs.

**Conservation:** ESA-T (Threatened, designated critical habitat on Oregon and Washington beaches); Washington ST-E; Oregon ST-T; IUCN-NT. Recovery efforts include seasonal beach closures, predator management (primarily corvid egg predation control), and removal of invasive European beachgrass (*Ammophila arenaria*) that eliminates open sand nesting habitat. Oregon populations have responded positively to management, increasing from fewer than 30 breeding males in 1993 to 300+ in recent years (source: G-17). Nest and breeding site locations generalized to management units per SC-END.

**Salmon Thread:** No direct connection. Snowy Plovers forage on beach invertebrates (amphipods, isopods, insects) that are not part of the salmon nutrient pathway.

**Key Sources:** G-17, G-15, O-02

**Streaked Horned Lark** (*Eremophila alpestris strigata*) — also noted in Section 1.4 above. Uses coastal dune habitats in addition to lowland prairie. Sandy, sparsely vegetated coastal flats at river mouths and spit tips are important habitat. ESA-T (source: P-10, G-17).

### 3.3 Estuary and Coastal Wetland Communities

PNW estuaries are critical habitat for both resident and migratory birds. The major estuaries — Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Columbia River estuary, Puget Sound embayments, Coos Bay, and Tillamook Bay — support enormous concentrations of shorebirds, waterfowl, and wading birds.

#### Key Staging Sites (generalized locations per SC-END)

**Grays Harbor, Washington:** Designated as a Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve Network (WHSRN) site of hemispheric importance. During spring migration (April-May), Grays Harbor hosts an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 shorebirds, primarily Western Sandpiper (*Calidris mauri*) and Dunlin (*Calidris alpina*), with significant numbers of Short-billed Dowitcher, Red Knot, and Semipalmated Plover (source: O-19, G-19).

**Willapa Bay, Washington:** The least developed major estuary on the U.S. Pacific coast. Extensive intertidal mudflats support wintering and migrating shorebirds. Important for Black Brant (*Branta bernicla*) wintering populations that feed on eelgrass (*Zostera marina*) beds (source: G-19, O-19).

**Key estuary species:**
- **Black Oystercatcher** (*Haematopus bachmani*) — Year-round resident of rocky coastlines and rocky estuarine margins. See Section 4.1 for intertidal ecology.
- **Semipalmated Plover** (*Charadrius semipalmatus*) — Passage migrant, common on estuarine mudflats March-May and July-October.
- **Greater Yellowlegs** (*Tringa melanoleuca*) and **Lesser Yellowlegs** (*Tringa flavipes*) — Migrant shorebirds in estuarine and freshwater wetland habitats.
- **Black Brant** (*Branta bernicla*) — Winter visitor dependent on eelgrass beds in Willapa Bay, Padilla Bay, and Boundary Bay. Eelgrass decline directly impacts Brant overwinter survival (source: G-19).

**Salmon Thread at estuaries:** Estuaries are the critical transition zone for juvenile salmon entering the ocean and adult salmon returning to freshwater. Estuary habitat quality directly affects salmon survival during the smolt-to-ocean transition. Avian predators at estuaries — Caspian Terns (*Hydroprogne caspia*), Double-crested Cormorants (*Nannopterum auritum*), and Western Gulls — can consume significant numbers of outmigrating smolts. The Caspian Tern colony on East Sand Island in the Columbia River estuary was the largest in the world (source: G-01, G-20), and tern predation on ESA-listed salmon and steelhead smolts was sufficient to trigger USFWS management action (colony size reduction through habitat modification). This is one of the most contentious bird-salmon management conflicts in PNW conservation.

### 3.4 Coastal Old-Growth: Marbled Murrelet Nesting

The Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) nests in coastal old-growth forest, typically within 50 miles of the coast, at elevations from sea level to 4,000 ft. This species is the critical link between ELEV-COASTAL/ELEV-MONTANE (nesting) and ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (foraging). Old-growth nesting ecology is detailed in Module 3 Part A (ELEV-MONTANE section) and in Section 5.1 below. The coastal forest nesting habitat and the marine foraging habitat are treated together as a single ecological unit for conservation purposes — protecting ocean foraging zones without protecting inland nesting habitat (or vice versa) fails to conserve the species (source: G-13, P-04, P-05).

### 3.5 Tufted Puffin (*Fratercula cirrhata*) — Declining Colonial Seabird

- Residency: Summer breeder (April-September at colony); pelagic in non-breeding season
- Elevation: ELEV-COASTAL (colony), ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE and ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (foraging)
- Habitat: AVI-HAB-OFFSHORE-ROCK, HAB-PELAGIC
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore (pursues small fish and squid underwater using wing-propulsion)

**Nesting:** Burrow nester on offshore islands and sea stacks with soil deep enough for excavation. Colonies on Haystack Rock (OR), Three Arch Rocks NWR (OR), Protection Island NWR (WA), and San Juan Islands (WA). Single egg per year; long chick-rearing period (40-55 days).

**Population decline:** PNW Tufted Puffin populations have declined dramatically. Washington colonies have lost an estimated 85-90% of breeding birds since the 1970s. Triangle Island (BC) and other Alaskan colonies have also declined. Causes include:
1. **Forage fish decline** — reduced availability of sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), and smelt.
2. **Ocean warming** — marine heat waves (2014-2016 "Blob" event) caused mass puffin die-offs (wreck events) from starvation.
3. **Predation** — Bald Eagle population recovery has increased nest predation pressure on colonial seabirds, including puffins, paradoxically linking one conservation success (eagle recovery) to another species' decline (source: G-01, O-23).

**Conservation:** ESA-NL (federally); Washington ST-E (State Endangered); Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC (global, but PNW populations declining). The Tufted Puffin is a candidate for federal listing review (source: G-14, G-17).

**Salmon Thread:** Indirect — Tufted Puffins feed on forage fish (herring, sand lance, smelt) that are also prey of juvenile and adult salmon. Declines in forage fish affect both puffins and salmon through shared prey base.

**Key Sources:** G-01, G-14, G-17, O-23

## 4. ELEV-INTERTIDAL (-15 ft to 0 ft MLLW) — Rocky Shore, Mudflat, and Tidal Marsh

**ECO Band:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL | **Minecraft Y:** y=-41 (sub-block resolution) | **Salinity:** 28-33 ppt (reduced near river mouths)

The intertidal zone is narrow — the entire 15-ft tidal range collapses to less than one Minecraft block at the 40.05 ft/block scale — but it supports one of the most productive avian foraging habitats on Earth. The PNW outer coast has approximately 1,500 miles of shoreline, plus several thousand additional miles of Puget Sound, Hood Canal, and inland waterway shoreline. Three distinct intertidal sub-habitats support three corresponding avian communities.

### 4.1 Rocky Intertidal Community

**Habitat:** Exposed rocky shoreline and offshore rocks, including headlands, sea stacks, surge channels, and tide pools. The PNW rocky intertidal is structured by vertical zonation: high intertidal (barnacle zone), mid intertidal (mussel zone), and low intertidal (kelp holdfast zone). Dominant invertebrates include California mussel (*Mytilus californianus*), giant green anemone (*Anthopleura xanthogrammica*), and ochre sea star (*Pisaster ochraceus*) — the original "keystone species" as defined by Robert Paine (1966). See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-INTERTIDAL characterization).

#### Black Oystercatcher (*Haematopus bachmani*)

- Residency: Year-round resident
- Elevation: ELEV-INTERTIDAL (obligate)
- Habitat: HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR
- Guild: Ground forager (specialized — chisel-bill for opening mussels and limpets)

**The intertidal obligate.** Black Oystercatchers are one of the few bird species in the PNW that spend their entire lives in a single ecoregion band. They nest on bare rock above the high-tide line, forage exclusively in the rocky intertidal zone, and rarely move more than a few hundred meters from the shore. Their bright red-orange bill is laterally compressed and used as a chisel to pry open mussels, detach limpets, and extract chitons from rock surfaces.

**Population:** Total Pacific coast population estimated at 8,900-11,000 individuals (source: G-14, O-23). Population is naturally small because suitable rocky intertidal habitat is discontinuous and nesting territories are limited.

**Conservation:** ESA-NL; Washington ST-NL; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Partners in Flight: Continental Watch List. The species is a U.S. Shorebird Conservation Plan focal species and a USFWS Bird of Conservation Concern (source: O-04). Threats include oil spills (rocky intertidal concentrates spilled oil), human disturbance at nest sites, and sea star wasting syndrome (which alters the intertidal invertebrate community that oystercatchers depend on).

**Salmon Thread:** No direct connection. Rocky intertidal invertebrate communities are primarily structured by wave energy, tidal exposure, and biological interactions (mussel-starfish-urchin) rather than by salmon-derived nutrients.

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-04, O-23

#### Rocky Intertidal Migrants

**Surfbird** (*Calidris virgata*) — Winter visitor and passage migrant (September-May). Breeds in alpine tundra in Alaska and Yukon; winters exclusively on rocky intertidal shores of the Pacific coast from Alaska to Chile. Forages on mussels, barnacles, limpets, and snails on wave-washed rocks. Specialized rocky-shore niche separates it from mudflat shorebirds. PNW populations are stable (source: O-19, O-07).

**Black Turnstone** (*Arenaria melanocephala*) — Winter visitor and passage migrant (August-May). Similar rocky intertidal niche to Surfbird but more versatile; also uses jetties, breakwaters, and mixed rocky-sandy shoreline. Forages by flipping rocks and seaweed to expose invertebrates. Breeds in western Alaska (source: O-07, O-19).

**Wandering Tattler** (*Tringa incana*) — Passage migrant (April-May, July-September). Breeds along mountain streams in Alaska and northwestern Canada; migrates along the Pacific coast, stopping on rocky intertidal shorelines. Bobbing behavior distinctive. Less abundant than Surfbird or Black Turnstone in PNW (source: O-07).

**Rock Sandpiper** (*Calidris ptilocnemis*) — Winter visitor (November-April), primarily Puget Sound and northern Washington coast. The southernmost regular wintering range; small flocks on rocky jetties and breakwaters. Often associates with Surfbirds and Black Turnstones (source: O-07, G-10).

### 4.2 Mudflat Community

**Habitat:** Soft-sediment intertidal flats exposed at low tide in protected bays and estuaries. Primary sites in the PNW include Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Padilla Bay, Skagit Bay, Boundary Bay (BC), and multiple Puget Sound embayments. Mudflats support enormous densities of burrowing invertebrates (polychaete worms, bivalves, amphipods, mud shrimp) that fuel shorebird migration along the Pacific Flyway.

**Community composition during spring migration (April-May):**

The PNW's mudflats are among the most important shorebird staging sites in the Western Hemisphere. Grays Harbor alone hosts an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 shorebirds during the April-May northbound migration, making it one of four most important shorebird staging sites on the Pacific Flyway (source: O-19, G-19).

**Dunlin** (*Calidris alpina*) — The dominant wintering shorebird on PNW mudflats. Flocks of 10,000-50,000+ Dunlin winter from October through April on Puget Sound, Grays Harbor, and Willapa Bay mudflats. The PNW wintering population is primarily the subspecies *C. a. pacifica*, which breeds in western Alaska. Dunlin are the most numerous shorebird in the PNW in winter, exceeding all other shorebird species combined on many estuarine sites (source: O-19, G-03).

**Western Sandpiper** (*Calidris mauri*) — The most abundant shorebird species in the Western Hemisphere, with a total population estimated at 3.5 million. Breeds exclusively in western Alaska. Migrates through the PNW in enormous numbers: spring migration (April-May) peaks at Grays Harbor and Willapa Bay with single-day counts exceeding 500,000 individuals (source: O-19, G-19). Fall migration (July-September) is more dispersed. Western Sandpipers extract biofilm (diatoms, bacteria, organic matter) from mud surfaces using their bill's specialized lateral lamellae, a feeding behavior unique among shorebirds (source: O-19).

**Short-billed Dowitcher** (*Limnodromus griseus*) — Passage migrant in large numbers, using the distinctive "sewing machine" probing technique in soft mud. The similar Long-billed Dowitcher (*L. scolopaceus*) prefers freshwater habitats and is more common in interior PNW wetlands.

**Salmon Thread at mudflats:** Mudflat invertebrate communities at estuary sites (Grays Harbor, Columbia River estuary, Skagit delta) receive nutrient subsidies from decomposing salmon carcasses washed downstream during fall spawning runs. Elevated nitrogen-15 in mudflat sediments at salmon-estuary sites suggests marine-derived nutrient input that may increase invertebrate density and thus shorebird foraging quality (source: P-18). The connection is indirect but potentially significant for shorebird body condition and migratory fuel loading.

### 4.3 Tidal Marsh Community

**Habitat:** Salt marsh and brackish marsh in sheltered coastal and estuarine areas. Dominated by salt-tolerant vegetation: pickleweed (*Salicornia* spp.), saltgrass (*Distichlis spicata*), sedges (*Carex* spp.), and bulrush (*Schoenoplectus* spp.) at freshwater-saltwater transitions. Tidal marshes have been reduced by 75-90% in Puget Sound and many Oregon estuaries through diking, filling, and conversion (source: G-14, G-23).

**Northern Harrier** (*Circus hudsonius*) — Year-round resident. The primary raptor of tidal marsh and adjacent grassland. Low-quartering flight over marsh vegetation, hunting voles and small birds by sound and sight. Nests on the ground in dense marsh vegetation. Males are pale gray; females and juveniles are brown — one of the most sexually dimorphic raptors in North America. Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC (source: G-14, O-07).

**Short-eared Owl** (*Asio flammeus*) — Winter visitor and local breeder. Diurnal and crepuscular owl that hunts over tidal marshes, grasslands, and agricultural fields. Often seen quartering low over dike-top trails at coastal wildlife refuges. Ground nester in dense grass. Population declining continent-wide due to grassland and marsh habitat loss (source: G-18, O-04). Conservation: ESA-NL; Washington ST-NL; Oregon ST-NL; IUCN-LC. BBS trend: -2.2%/yr PNW (source: G-18).

**Virginia Rail** (*Rallus limicola*) — Year-round resident in dense marsh. Secretive; heard far more often than seen. "Kidick-kidick-kidick" call is the characteristic sound of PNW freshwater and brackish marshes. Population status poorly known due to secretive behavior and difficulty of marsh surveys (source: G-14).

**Marsh Wren** (*Cistothorus palustris*) — Year-round resident. Builds multiple dummy nests (males may build 15-20 nests, only one or two used) in cattail and bulrush stands. Bubbling, chattering song is heard year-round. Important indicator of marsh habitat quality.

**Tidal marsh conservation:** Loss of tidal marsh habitat is the primary driver of decline for salt-marsh-obligate species. Puget Sound tidal marsh restoration projects (e.g., Nisqually Delta, Skagit Delta) are among the largest wetland restoration efforts in the PNW and have documented rapid recolonization by marsh birds within 3-5 years of dike removal and tidal reconnection (source: G-23).

## 5. ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (0-200 m depth) — Nearshore, Kelp Forest, and Forage Fish Zone

**ECO Band:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE | **Minecraft Y:** y=-57 to y=-41 (16 blocks) | **Salinity:** 30-34 ppt

**Dominant habitat:** Bull kelp (*Nereocystis luetkeana*) forests on rocky reefs to 60 ft depth; eelgrass (*Zostera marina*) meadows to 30 ft; rocky reef; sandy bottom; Puget Sound basins and channels. See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE characterization).

The shallow marine zone is the primary foraging habitat for the PNW's alcid, cormorant, diving duck, and marine raptor guilds. Productivity in this zone is driven by seasonal upwelling (spring-summer on the outer coast), tidal mixing, river nutrient input, and the kelp-eelgrass structural foundation.

### 5.1 Alcid Guild — Pursuit-Diving Seabirds

The alcids (Family Alcidae) are the ecological equivalent of penguins in the Northern Hemisphere — small to medium seabirds that "fly" underwater using their wings for propulsion. The PNW supports 10 regularly occurring alcid species, making it one of the richest alcid assemblages in the world (source: G-01, O-23).

**Common Murre** (*Uria aalge*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (nearshore waters); breeds on coastal cliffs and offshore rocks May-August
- Elevation: ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (primary), ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (deep dives to 180 m)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC, AVI-HAB-OFFSHORE-ROCK (breeding)
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore (pursuit diver)
- The deepest-diving alcid — documented to 180 m (590 ft), reaching into the upper ELEV-DEEP-MARINE zone. Diet: primarily small schooling fish (herring, sand lance, smelt, anchovy) and krill. Breeds in dense colonies on narrow cliff ledges; single egg laid directly on bare rock (no nest). PNW colonies on Three Arch Rocks NWR (OR), Yaquina Head (OR), Destruction Island (WA), and Tatoosh Island (WA) (source: G-01).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Vulnerable to oil spills (murres have the highest per-capita oil spill mortality of any seabird due to their habit of sitting on the water surface in large aggregations), gillnet bycatch, and colony disturbance by Bald Eagles (source: G-01, O-23).
- Population estimate: Oregon outer coast breeding population 700,000+ (source: G-01). Significant mortality events during marine heat waves (2014-2016 "Blob" caused an estimated die-off of hundreds of thousands of murres across the northeast Pacific from starvation; source: O-23).
- Salmon Thread: Indirect — murres and salmon share forage fish prey base (herring, sand lance).
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23, G-14

**Pigeon Guillemot** (*Cepphus columba*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (nearshore)
- Elevation: ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (foraging), ELEV-COASTAL (nesting in rock crevices, earthen burrows, and wharves)
- Habitat: HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL (nesting), HAB-KELP, shallow rocky reef (foraging)
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore (shallow diver to ~150 ft)
- Nests in rock crevices, burrows, and spaces under waterfront structures (piers, wharves, riprap). Breeds in small, dispersed colonies rather than the massive aggregations of murres. Diet includes sculpins, gunnels, blennies, and shrimp from rocky reef and kelp habitat. Bright red feet and red oral lining distinctive in breeding season (source: O-07, G-01).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC
- Salmon Thread: No direct connection.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-07

**Rhinoceros Auklet** (*Cerorhinca monocerata*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (nearshore to offshore)
- Elevation: ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (primary foraging), ELEV-COASTAL (breeding burrows on islands)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC, AVI-HAB-OFFSHORE-ROCK
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore
- Burrow nester on offshore islands; Protection Island NWR (WA) hosts the largest breeding colony in the contiguous U.S. with an estimated 36,000+ breeding pairs (source: G-01). The "rhinoceros horn" — a keratinous projection at the base of the bill — is present only during breeding season. Returns to burrows at night carrying multiple fish crosswise in bill (bill-loading), a behavior shared with puffins (source: G-01, O-23).
- Salmon Thread: Indirect — shares forage fish prey with salmon.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23

**Marbled Murrelet** (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — foraging ecology
- Forages in nearshore waters within 2 km of shore, diving for sand lance, herring, krill, and smelt. Typically solitary or in pairs while foraging (unlike the colonial habits of most other alcids). Dives to 30-50 m. Chick provisioning requires multiple daily flights between marine foraging areas and inland old-growth nesting sites — a round trip that can exceed 100 km per day (source: P-04, P-05, G-09).
- The murrelet's dependence on two widely separated and ecologically distinct habitats (old-growth forest and nearshore ocean) makes it uniquely vulnerable to habitat degradation in either zone. Loss of old-growth nesting habitat reduces reproductive output; loss of forage fish in marine habitat reduces chick survival. Both threats are operating simultaneously (source: G-13).
- Salmon Thread: Indirect — shares forage fish prey with salmon. Marine-derived nutrients from salmon carcasses fertilize the old-growth forest where murrelets nest; the salmon-forest-murrelet connection is a multi-step ecological pathway.
- Key Sources: G-09, G-13, P-04, P-05

### 5.2 Kelp Forest and Eelgrass Associated Species

**Kelp forests** (bull kelp *Nereocystis luetkeana* on the outer coast; giant kelp *Macrocystis pyrifera* in Puget Sound) and **eelgrass meadows** (*Zostera marina*) provide structural habitat for fish, invertebrates, and the seabirds that feed on them.

**Species dependent on kelp/eelgrass habitat structure:**
- **Surf Scoter** (*Melanitta perspicillata*) — Winter visitor (October-April). Large flocks winter in Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and nearshore outer coast. Dives for mussels, clams, and crustaceans in kelp and eelgrass habitat. One of the most abundant wintering seabirds in Puget Sound with an estimated 70,000+ individuals (source: G-14, G-19).
- **White-winged Scoter** (*Melanitta deglandi*) — Winter visitor. Associates with Surf Scoters in nearshore waters. Dives deeper and prefers mussel and clam prey from harder substrate.
- **Harlequin Duck** (*Histrionicus histrionicus*) — Winter resident on rocky outer coast and Puget Sound jetties (October-March); breeds on montane streams (April-August). The cross-band life history (marine winter, freshwater summer) is unique among PNW ducks. Salmon Thread: Yes — breeds on salmon-bearing streams and feeds on aquatic invertebrates enriched by salmon nutrients (source: G-14, P-18).
- **Barrow's Goldeneye** (*Bucephala islandica*) — Winter visitor in Puget Sound and coastal bays; breeds on subalpine lakes and beaver ponds. Dives for crustaceans and mollusks. Males display striking black-and-white plumage with purple-glossed head and white crescent facial mark (source: O-07).
- **Black Brant** (*Branta bernicla*) — Winter visitor dependent on eelgrass. Willapa Bay, Padilla Bay, and Boundary Bay (BC) are critical wintering sites. Eelgrass decline from wasting disease, sedimentation, and water quality degradation directly threatens Brant populations (source: G-19).

### 5.3 Marine Raptors in Shallow Marine

**Bald Eagle** — Forages over shallow marine waters for surfacing fish, herring ball events, and injured or dead seabirds. Kleptoparasitizes Osprey and gulls. Salmon Thread: Yes — as described in Section 2.2.

**Peregrine Falcon** (*Falco peregrinus*) — Nests on coastal cliffs and urban buildings. Hunts over water, taking shorebirds, alcids, and Rock Pigeons. PNW subspecies *F. p. pealei* is a large, dark-plumaged coastal specialist that hunts seabirds almost exclusively. Conservation: ESA-DL (Delisted 1999); IUCN-LC. Population recovering strongly (source: G-17, O-22).

### 5.4 Forage Fish and Seabird Vulnerability

The shallow marine food web is structured by forage fish — small, schooling species that convert plankton energy into fish biomass available to seabirds, salmon, and marine mammals. The three critical PNW forage fish species are:

1. **Pacific herring** (*Clupea pallasii*) — Spawns in eelgrass beds in sheltered bays (Cherry Point, WA historically one of the largest PNW spawning aggregations). Herring spawning events attract massive concentrations of seabirds (gulls, scoters, goldeneyes, murres, eagles).
2. **Pacific sand lance** (*Ammodytes personatus*) — Burrows in sandy substrate by day; swims in schools at dusk and dawn. Primary prey for Rhinoceros Auklet, Tufted Puffin, and Marbled Murrelet chick provisioning.
3. **Surf smelt** (*Hypomesus pretiosus*) — Beach spawning species; important for terns, cormorants, and murres.

**Oil spill vulnerability:** The PNW shallow marine zone is traversed by oil tanker and barge traffic serving the refineries at Anacortes and Cherry Point (WA). Significant oil spill events (Nestucca, 1988; Tenyo Maru, 1991) killed thousands of seabirds, primarily Common Murres and scoters. Oil spill contingency planning identifies the Strait of Juan de Fuca and outer Washington coast as highest-risk zones for seabird mortality (source: G-01, O-23).

## 6. ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (200 m+ depth) — Pelagic Specialists

**ECO Band:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE | **Minecraft Y:** y=-64 to y=-57 | **Salinity:** 33-35 ppt | **Temperature:** 39-46 F, stable year-round below ~400 ft

The deep marine zone — the open ocean beyond the continental shelf break — is the most inaccessible avian habitat in the PNW. No bird species nests or roosts in the deep marine zone; all deep-marine birds return to land (islands, coastal cliffs) for breeding or pass through the PNW on transoceanic migration. The avifauna is composed of pelagic specialists adapted to life at sea: tubenoses (albatrosses, shearwaters, petrels, storm-petrels), jaegers, and a few alcids that range offshore.

**Observation:** Deep-marine avian communities are surveyed from pelagic birding trips (typically departing Westport, WA or Newport, OR) that travel 20-50 miles offshore to the continental shelf edge. Knowledge of pelagic seabird distribution off the PNW is less complete than for any other ecoregion band, because access requires dedicated offshore vessel time (source: O-23, G-01).

### 6.1 Tubenoses (Order Procellariiformes)

Tubenoses — named for the external nasal tubes atop their bills used for salt excretion and olfaction — are the most pelagic of all bird orders. They spend months or years at sea between breeding events, locating food by smell (an unusual ability among birds), and are capable of dynamic soaring that covers hundreds of miles per day with minimal energy expenditure.

**Black-footed Albatross** (*Phoebastria nigripes*)
- Residency: Non-breeding visitor (year-round, peak May-October off PNW)
- Elevation: ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (obligate pelagic)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-APEX (pelagic food web)
- Guild: Surface seizer / scavenger
- Wingspan: 190-220 cm (75-87 in). The largest bird regularly seen off the PNW coast. Breeds on Hawaiian Islands (Midway, Laysan) and Japan; non-breeders and post-breeding adults range across the North Pacific, regularly occurring over the continental slope and offshore waters of Washington and Oregon from spring through fall (source: G-01, O-23).
- Forages by sitting on the water surface and seizing squid, flying fish, fish eggs, and fishery discards. Follows fishing vessels. Highly vulnerable to longline fishery bycatch (hooks set near surface catch albatrosses when they grab bait).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; IUCN-NT (Near Threatened). Total population estimated at approximately 70,000 breeding pairs (source: O-23). Bycatch reduction measures (tori lines, weighted hooks, night setting) have reduced mortality in some fisheries but remain inconsistently applied.
- Salmon Thread: No direct connection. Forages far offshore on squid and fish unrelated to salmon food web.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23

**Sooty Shearwater** (*Ardenna grisea*)
- Residency: Non-breeding visitor (April-October, peak May-September)
- Elevation: ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (primary), ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (nearshore foraging)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC
- Ecological role: ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER
- Guild: Diving piscivore / surface seizer
- The most abundant seabird off the PNW coast. Estimated 20-30 million individuals migrate from breeding colonies in New Zealand, Chile, and the Falkland Islands to spend the austral winter (boreal summer) in the North Pacific. Sooty Shearwaters undertake the longest migration of any bird species — a figure-eight trans-Pacific route covering 60,000+ km annually (source: O-23).
- Off the PNW, Sooty Shearwaters form feeding aggregations of thousands to tens of thousands over upwelling zones and krill concentrations on the continental shelf edge. They dive to 60+ m for krill, squid, and small fish.
- Conservation: ESA-NL; IUCN-NT (Near Threatened). Global population declining; causes include introduced predators at breeding colonies (rats, cats) and shifts in ocean productivity.
- Salmon Thread: Indirect — feeds on krill and small fish in the same upwelling-driven food web that supports juvenile salmon at sea.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23

**Northern Fulmar** (*Fulmarus glacialis*)
- Residency: Winter visitor and year-round non-breeding visitor
- Elevation: ELEV-DEEP-MARINE, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (outer shelf)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC
- Guild: Surface seizer / scavenger
- Breeds in Arctic and subarctic (Alaska, northern Atlantic); non-breeders and post-breeders range south to PNW waters year-round but peak in winter. Polymorphic plumage from pale gray to dark sooty brown. Tubular bill structure and salt gland excretion are conspicuous tubenose adaptations. Follows fishing vessels for discards and offal (source: O-07, O-23).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; IUCN-LC.
- Salmon Thread: No.
- Key Sources: O-07, O-23

### 6.2 Storm-Petrels (Family Hydrobatidae)

Storm-petrels are the smallest seabirds — sparrow-sized birds that feed by pattering their feet on the water surface while hovering, picking up plankton, small crustaceans, and fish larvae.

**Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel** (*Hydrobates furcatus*)
- Residency: Year-round resident (breeds on offshore islands; pelagic when not breeding)
- Elevation: ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (primary foraging), ELEV-COASTAL (breeding colonies on offshore islands)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC, AVI-HAB-OFFSHORE-ROCK (burrow nesting)
- Guild: Surface seizer (pattering flight)
- The only storm-petrel breeding in the PNW. Nests in burrows on offshore islands (Protection Island, Destruction Island, Alexander Island in WA; offshore rocks in OR). Pearly gray plumage; nocturnal at colony to avoid predation by gulls and eagles. Feeds on crustaceans, small fish, and jellyfish far offshore (source: G-01, O-23).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; all states ST-NL; IUCN-LC. Breeding colonies vulnerable to introduced rats (eradication on some islands has allowed colony recovery).
- Salmon Thread: No.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23

**Leach's Storm-Petrel** (*Hydrobates leucorhous*)
- Residency: Summer breeder (May-October on offshore islands)
- Elevation: ELEV-DEEP-MARINE (foraging), ELEV-COASTAL (breeding)
- Habitat: HAB-PELAGIC, AVI-HAB-OFFSHORE-ROCK
- Guild: Surface seizer
- Slightly larger and darker than Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel. Breeds in burrows on outer coast islands. Nocturnal at colony; eerie chattering call heard from burrows at night. Feeds far offshore on crustaceans and small fish (source: G-01, O-23).
- Conservation: ESA-NL; IUCN-VU (Vulnerable — recent uplisting due to global population decline from introduced predators at breeding colonies).
- Salmon Thread: No.
- Key Sources: G-01, O-23

### 6.3 Jaegers and Skuas (Family Stercorariidae)

Jaegers are kleptoparasitic seabirds that chase and harass other seabirds (terns, gulls, kittiwakes) until they drop or regurgitate food, which the jaeger then catches in midair. They also prey directly on small birds, lemmings (on Arctic breeding grounds), and fish.

**Parasitic Jaeger** (*Stercorarius parasiticus*) — Passage migrant (April-May, August-October). The most commonly seen jaeger from shore in the PNW. Often observed harassing Bonaparte's Gulls and terns at river mouths and coastal headlands. Breeds on Arctic tundra (source: O-07, O-23).

**Pomarine Jaeger** (*Stercorarius pomarinus*) — Passage migrant and winter visitor. Larger and heavier than Parasitic Jaeger; distinctive twisted central tail feathers in breeding plumage. More pelagic than Parasitic Jaeger; typically requires boat trips to observe. Breeds on Arctic tundra; population linked to lemming cycles (source: O-07, O-23).

**Long-tailed Jaeger** (*Stercorarius longicaudus*) — Passage migrant (May, September-October). The most pelagic and least frequently observed of the three jaegers in the PNW. Slender, tern-like build with extremely long central tail streamers. Breeds on high Arctic tundra. Migration route is primarily far offshore; rarely seen from land (source: O-07, O-23).

**South Polar Skua** (*Stercorarius maccormicki*) — Rare visitor (May-November). Breeds in Antarctica; ranges north to PNW waters during austral winter. Heavy-bodied, powerful flight. Rare on pelagic trips but annually recorded off Oregon and Washington (source: G-01, O-23).

### 6.4 Seasonal Upwelling and Seabird Abundance

Pelagic seabird abundance off the PNW coast is driven by seasonal coastal upwelling — the wind-driven process that brings cold, nutrient-rich water from depth to the surface along the continental shelf. Upwelling operates from April through September on the Oregon and Washington coast, driven by persistent northerly winds.

**Upwelling cycle and seabird response:**

| Month | Upwelling Status | Primary Productivity | Seabird Abundance | Key Species |
|-------|-----------------|---------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Jan-Mar | Downwelling (winter storms) | Low | Moderate (winter residents) | Northern Fulmar, scoters, loons |
| Apr-May | Upwelling onset | Phytoplankton bloom | Rapidly increasing | Sooty Shearwater arrives; jaeger migration |
| Jun-Aug | Peak upwelling | Maximum | Peak abundance | Sooty Shearwater peak; albatross, storm-petrels, auklets |
| Sep-Oct | Upwelling decline | Declining | Declining | Southward migration of shearwaters, jaegers |
| Nov-Dec | Downwelling (storm season) | Low | Low (winter specialists) | Fulmar, Black-legged Kittiwake, Ancient Murrelet |

**Marine heat wave impacts:** The northeast Pacific marine heat wave of 2014-2016 (the "Blob") caused catastrophic disruption to the upwelling-driven food web. Warm water suppressed upwelling, reducing phytoplankton productivity and collapsing forage fish populations. The seabird mortality event was unprecedented: an estimated 500,000-1,000,000 Common Murres died of starvation across the northeast Pacific; Cassin's Auklet (*Ptychoramphus aleuticus*) mass die-offs were documented on PNW beaches; Tufted Puffin breeding failure was widespread (source: O-23, G-01). These events demonstrate the direct link between oceanographic conditions and seabird community viability.

**Salmon Thread in the deep marine:** Adult salmon (Chinook, coho, sockeye) spend 1-5 years in the North Pacific, foraging on the same zooplankton and forage fish communities that support pelagic seabirds. Ocean conditions that reduce forage fish availability harm both seabird and salmon populations simultaneously. The marine heat waves that killed hundreds of thousands of murres also caused poor ocean survival for multiple salmon cohorts, reducing adult returns to PNW rivers in subsequent years (source: G-20, O-23). Salmon and seabirds are both indicators of ocean ecosystem health; their parallel declines during warm-water events are symptoms of the same underlying ecological disruption.

### 6.5 Deep Marine Conservation Threats

1. **Marine heat waves and climate change** — increasing frequency and severity of warm-water events that disrupt upwelling food web. The 2014-2016 "Blob" and subsequent warm events have caused the worst seabird die-offs in recorded history (source: O-23).
2. **Fishery bycatch** — longline and gillnet fisheries kill albatrosses, shearwaters, murres, and other diving seabirds. Bycatch mitigation measures (tori lines, weighted lines, night setting, circle hooks) are effective but inconsistently mandated.
3. **Plastic pollution** — pelagic seabirds ingest floating plastic debris (mistaken for prey) and feed it to chicks. Northern Fulmar stomach contents are used as a bioindicator of marine plastic pollution in the North Atlantic and increasingly in the North Pacific.
4. **Oil spills** — offshore spills (from vessel traffic, offshore operations) can affect tens of thousands of seabirds. Species that sit on the water surface in large aggregations (murres, scoters, loons) are most vulnerable.
5. **Offshore wind energy** — planned development of floating offshore wind farms in Oregon and Washington may introduce collision risk and displacement effects for pelagic seabirds, particularly shearwaters and albatrosses. Environmental assessment is ongoing.

### 7.2 Salmon Thread Summary: Birds and Salmon Across All Zones

The Salmon Thread connects every zone in this document. Here is the complete bird-salmon interaction network across the six zones:

| Zone | Salmon Life Stage Present | Key Avian Interaction | Interaction Type |
|------|--------------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| ELEV-DEEP-MARINE | Adult ocean foraging (1-5 yr) | Shared forage fish web with seabirds | Competition / parallel indicator |
| ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE | Adult return; juvenile ocean entry | Murre, auklet forage on same prey; tern predation on smolts | Competition, predation |
| ELEV-INTERTIDAL | Estuary transition (smolt, adult) | Heron, gull, tern predation at estuary | Predation |
| ELEV-COASTAL | Estuary rearing (juvenile coho) | Caspian Tern, cormorant predation at estuary mouths | Predation (management conflict) |
| ELEV-RIPARIAN | Spawning, juvenile rearing, carcass decomposition | Eagle, raven, crow, gull scavenging; dipper, kingfisher, heron predation; warbler habitat fertilization | Scavenging, predation, nutrient cycling |
| ELEV-LOWLAND | Not present (above tidal influence) | Indirect — salmon-derived nutrients in riparian soils support forest insect communities | Nutrient pathway (indirect) |

**The salmon-bird nutrient pathway** (see P-18): Salmon carry marine-derived nitrogen (N-15) and phosphorus from the ocean into freshwater and terrestrial ecosystems. Birds are major vectors in this transport:

1. **Direct consumption** — Eagles, herons, crows, ravens, gulls, mergansers, kingfishers, and dippers consume salmon tissue (eggs, fry, juveniles, adults, carcasses).
2. **Terrestrial deposition** — Birds that eat salmon subsequently defecate in forests, fields, and nesting colonies, depositing marine-derived nutrients in terrestrial soils. Eagle perch sites and heron rookeries near salmon streams show elevated N-15 in surrounding vegetation.
3. **Carcass distribution** — Eagles and ravens drag salmon carcasses away from streams into forest understory, accelerating nutrient distribution beyond the immediate riparian zone.

This is one of the most important ecological processes in the PNW and connects marine, freshwater, and terrestrial food webs into a single integrated system. The decline of Pacific salmon runs — many populations are ESA-listed as Threatened or Endangered — directly reduces the nutrient subsidy that supports riparian and coastal avian communities.
