# PNW Avian Taxonomy -- Module 4: Evolutionary Biology

> **Module 4 deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document synthesizes the evolutionary biology of PNW avian communities: deep phylogenetic history from the K-Pg mass extinction through Neogene diversification, Pleistocene glacial refugia and post-glacial recolonization, adaptive radiation and ecotype formation, hybridization zones, island biogeography, and the evolution of migratory strategies. The PNW is not merely a collection of bird species -- it is a landscape shaped by tectonic, climatic, and ecological forces that have driven speciation, extinction, and adaptation over 66 million years. The avifauna present today reflects the cumulative legacy of these forces.

# PART I: DEEP TIME -- FROM THE K-Pg BOUNDARY TO THE MODERN AVIFAUNA

## 1.1 The K-Pg Mass Extinction and Avian Survival (66 Ma)

The Pacific Northwest avifauna, like all modern bird communities, traces its deepest roots to the Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) mass extinction event 66 million years ago. The asteroid impact at Chicxulub and the ensuing global winter, acid rain, and ecosystem collapse eliminated approximately 75% of all species on Earth, including all non-avian dinosaurs and the toothed birds (Enantiornithes, Hesperornithiformes) that had dominated avian diversity in the Cretaceous. Only members of Neornithes -- the modern bird crown group -- survived the extinction [P1, P2].

The survival of Neornithes across the K-Pg boundary is one of the most consequential events in the history of the PNW avifauna. Every bird species documented in the PNW today -- from the 3-gram Calliope Hummingbird to the 12-kilogram Trumpeter Swan -- descends from the small number of neornithine lineages that passed through the extinction bottleneck. Jarvis et al. (2014) estimated that only 5-10 neornithine lineages survived the K-Pg event, then radiated explosively during the early Paleogene to produce all modern orders [P1].

### What Survived and Why

The traits that enabled neornithine survival across the K-Pg boundary have direct echoes in PNW bird ecology:

**Ground-dwelling and seed-eating ancestors.** Molecular phylogenetics and the fossil record suggest that the earliest diverging modern bird lineages (Palaeognathae -- ostriches, tinamous; Galloanserae -- waterfowl and landfowl) were primarily ground-dwelling and granivorous or omnivorous. The global deforestation that followed the K-Pg impact would have eliminated canopy-dependent species but favored ground-foraging generalists capable of exploiting seeds and invertebrates in disturbed landscapes. The PNW's Galliformes (grouse, quail -- see Module 1b) and Anseriformes (waterfowl -- see Module 1a) are the modern descendants of this surviving lineage [P1, P2].

**Small body size.** K-Pg survivors were predominantly small-bodied, likely under 1 kg. Large body size correlates with higher metabolic demand, larger territory requirements, and lower population density -- all factors that increase extinction risk during prolonged resource scarcity. The recovery of large-bodied avian lineages (eagles, pelicans, cranes) required millions of years of post-extinction ecological evolution [P1].

**Flexible diets.** Dietary specialization was lethal during the K-Pg collapse. Insectivores and frugivores dependent on complex food webs suffered disproportionately. The surviving lineages appear to have been dietary generalists capable of switching between seeds, invertebrates, and plant material. This ancestral flexibility is still visible in the PNW's most successful bird families -- corvids, sparrows, and finches -- whose dietary breadth underlies their ecological dominance [P2].

### The Neoaves Radiation

The explosive diversification of Neoaves -- the clade containing ~95% of living bird species, including all PNW passerines, raptors, woodpeckers, shorebirds, and seabirds -- began within the first 10-15 million years after the K-Pg extinction. Prum et al. (2015) resolved the deep phylogeny of Neoaves, revealing that the major orders diverged rapidly in a series of near-simultaneous splits during the Paleocene and early Eocene (66-50 Ma). This "big bang" of avian diversification produced the ancestors of:

- **Strisores** (nightjars, swifts, hummingbirds) -- represented in the PNW by Common Nighthawk, Vaux's Swift, and eight hummingbird species (see Module 1b)
- **Columbaves** (pigeons, sandgrouse) -- represented by Band-tailed Pigeon and Eurasian Collared-Dove (see Module 1b)
- **Gruiformes** (rails, cranes) -- represented by Sandhill Crane, American Coot, and rails (see Module 1b)
- **Aequorlitornithes** (waterbirds: shorebirds, gulls, alcids, pelicans, herons, penguins) -- the massive radiation underlying PNW shorebird and seabird diversity (see Modules 1b, 2a, 2c)
- **Telluraves** (landbirds: raptors, owls, woodpeckers, falcons, and the passerine mega-radiation) -- the dominant avian assemblage in PNW terrestrial ecosystems [P2]

The rapidity of this diversification explains a persistent challenge in avian systematics: many deep relationships among bird orders remain difficult to resolve because divergence occurred so quickly that few molecular markers accumulated between splitting events. The PNW's owls (Strigiformes) and hawks (Accipitriformes) appear closely related in some analyses but not others, reflecting this ancient radiation "polytomy" [P1, P2].

## 1.2 The Paleogene and Neogene: Building the Modern Landscape (66-2.6 Ma)

The PNW as a landscape hospitable to modern avifauna did not exist at the time of the K-Pg extinction. The Cascade Range had not yet risen. The Columbia Plateau basalts had not yet erupted. The Pacific coast sat further east, and the climate was subtropical to warm temperate. The geological and climatic evolution of the PNW over the past 66 million years created the topographic complexity and habitat diversity that today support 500+ bird species [P28].

### Key Geological Events and Their Avifaunal Consequences

**Eocene thermal maximum (56-34 Ma).** During the early Eocene, the PNW supported subtropical forests with palms, crocodilians, and diverse arboreal bird communities now extinct at these latitudes. The avifauna of this era included early parrots, trogons, and other tropical lineages -- none of which persist in the modern PNW. The Clarno and John Day fossil beds in central Oregon preserve a rich record of this subtropical biota. The cooling that began in the late Eocene (~34 Ma) initiated the transition toward the temperate ecosystems that characterize the modern PNW [P28].

**Cascade Range orogeny (20-5 Ma).** The uplift of the Cascade Range during the Miocene created the single most important biogeographic barrier in the PNW. By intercepting moisture-laden Pacific air masses, the Cascades produced a rainshadow effect that divided the region into a wet, forested west side and a dry, steppe-dominated east side. This barrier partitions avian distributions to the present day:

| Taxon | West-side form | East-side form | Barrier effect |
|-------|---------------|---------------|----------------|
| *Dendragapus* grouse | Sooty Grouse | Dusky Grouse | Full species split (AOS 2006) |
| Northern Flicker | Red-shafted (pure) | Red-shafted × Yellow-shafted hybrid zone | Plumage divergence maintained |
| Song Sparrow | Dark coastal *morphallax* | Pale interior *merrilli* | Subspecies-level divergence |
| Fox Sparrow | Dark Sooty group | Paler Slate-colored group | Group-level divergence |
| Dark-eyed Junco | Oregon group (dark hood) | Oregon × Slate-colored intergrades | Subspecies cline |
| Steller's Jay | Coastal form (darker) | Interior form (paler) | Subspecies variation |

(See Module 1f for detailed subspecies complex treatments)

The Cascades' role as a barrier is not absolute -- numerous species range across the crest, and pass systems (Columbia River Gorge, Snoqualmie Pass, Santiam Pass) allow gene flow. But for taxa with limited dispersal capacity or strong habitat associations, the Cascades function as a semi-permeable filter that promotes genetic divergence over time [P4, P24].

**Columbia River Basalt floods (17-6 Ma).** The massive basalt eruptions that created the Columbia Plateau eliminated pre-existing forests and created a vast, flat substrate that eventually became the sagebrush steppe ecosystem. This new habitat enabled the colonization and radiation of steppe-adapted species, most notably the Greater Sage-Grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*), whose genus diverged from prairie grouse (*Tympanuchus*) approximately 5-7 Ma -- coinciding precisely with the expansion of *Artemisia* steppe across the new basalt plateau (see Module 1b) [P36].

**Late Miocene grassland expansion (10-5 Ma).** The global expansion of C4 grasslands during the late Miocene transformed interior PNW landscapes, creating habitat for grassland-adapted species. Modern PNW grassland birds -- Horned Lark, Western Meadowlark, Savannah Sparrow, Grasshopper Sparrow -- descend from lineages that radiated during this period of grassland expansion [P28].

**Pliocene conifer diversification (5-2.6 Ma).** The diversification and latitudinal sorting of Pacific Northwest conifer species during the Pliocene set the stage for one of the region's most remarkable evolutionary stories: the Red Crossbill call type complex. The mosaic of Douglas-fir, western hemlock, Sitka spruce, ponderosa pine, and lodgepole pine forests that characterize the modern PNW provides the substrate for crossbill ecological speciation -- each call type tracking a different conifer's cone morphology (see Module 1f) [P21].

## 1.3 Molecular Phylogenetics and PNW Avian Relationships

Modern molecular phylogenetics has transformed our understanding of avian relationships, overturning classifications based solely on morphological similarity and revealing unexpected evolutionary affinities. Several PNW-relevant findings deserve emphasis:

### Falcons Are Not Hawks

One of the most surprising results of molecular phylogenetics was the discovery that falcons (Falconidae) are not closely related to hawks and eagles (Accipitridae), despite superficial similarities in body plan and predatory habits. Falcons are instead more closely allied with parrots and passerines, forming the clade Australaves. Hawks belong to the distantly related clade Afroaves. The PNW's Peregrine Falcon, American Kestrel, and Merlin are thus more closely related to Steller's Jay and American Robin than to the Red-tailed Hawk or Bald Eagle -- a convergence of predatory morphology arising from shared ecological roles, not shared ancestry [P2].

### New World Vultures and Storks

New World vultures (Cathartidae), including the Turkey Vulture common in PNW lowlands, were long classified with hawks based on their raptorial appearance. Molecular data initially suggested affinity with storks (Ciconiiformes), though more recent analyses place them as sister to Accipitriformes. The taxonomic placement has shifted multiple times and remains an area of active research. Regardless of systematic position, the convergent similarities between New World vultures and Old World vultures (Accipitridae: Aegypiinae) represent one of the most striking examples of convergent evolution in birds -- independently evolved bare heads, soaring flight, keen olfaction (in Turkey Vulture), and carrion-specialization [P1, P2].

### The Passerine Mega-Radiation

Passerines (Order Passeriformes) constitute approximately 60% of all living bird species and dominate PNW terrestrial bird communities. The order originated in the Southern Hemisphere (probably Australasia) during the late Cretaceous or early Paleocene and reached the Northern Hemisphere via multiple dispersal events during the Eocene and Oligocene. The major PNW passerine families arrived in North America at different times:

| Family | Approximate arrival in North America | PNW representatives |
|--------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|
| Tyrannidae (flycatchers) | Late Oligocene (~25 Ma) | 18+ species |
| Corvidae (corvids) | Middle Miocene (~15 Ma) | 8 species |
| Paridae (chickadees/titmice) | Late Miocene (~10 Ma) | 4 species |
| Parulidae (wood-warblers) | Late Miocene (~10 Ma) | 25+ species |
| Emberizidae/Passerellidae (sparrows) | Pliocene (~5 Ma) | 30+ species |
| Fringillidae (finches) | Pliocene (~4 Ma) | 15+ species |
| Turdidae (thrushes) | Miocene (~12 Ma) | 6 species |

The most recent major avian colonization of North America was the arrival of Fringillidae (true finches), which crossed from Eurasia to North America via Beringia during the Pliocene. The PNW's crossbills, siskins, goldfinches, rosy-finches, and Evening Grosbeak all descend from this relatively recent (~4-5 Ma) Eurasian colonization event [P2, P28].

# PART II: THE PLEISTOCENE ENGINE -- GLACIAL REFUGIA AND POST-GLACIAL RECOLONIZATION

## 2.1 Pleistocene Climate and Landscape (2.6 Ma - 11,700 ya)

The Pleistocene epoch transformed the PNW landscape through repeated cycles of glaciation and deglaciation. The Cordilleran Ice Sheet, centered on the Coast Mountains and Cascades of British Columbia, advanced southward into Washington during at least four major glacial periods, reaching its maximum extent approximately 20,000-18,000 years ago during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM). At the LGM:

- **Ice coverage:** The Cordilleran Ice Sheet covered nearly all of British Columbia, the Puget Sound lowland, the San Juan Islands, and the Olympic Peninsula's northern margins. Alpine glaciers filled Cascade valleys to 1,000-1,500 m elevation in Washington and northern Oregon.
- **Ice-free areas:** Central and southern Oregon, the Snake River Plain of Idaho, and isolated nunataks (ice-free alpine peaks protruding above glaciers) in the North Cascades remained unglaciated.
- **Vegetation zones:** Forests were compressed southward. The PNW lowlands that today support Douglas-fir and western red cedar forests were either under ice or supported tundra-steppe vegetation. Conifer forests persisted in the Klamath-Siskiyou region (southern Oregon/northern California), the Blue Mountains, and portions of the northern Rockies [P4, P6].
- **Sea level:** Approximately 120 m lower than present, exposing continental shelf habitat and connecting islands to the mainland.

### Implications for Avian Distribution

The glacial landscape created a paradox: the PNW as defined today was largely uninhabitable for forest-dwelling birds during glacial maxima, yet the modern PNW avifauna includes hundreds of forest-obligate species. These species must have survived glacial periods somewhere -- in refugia -- and recolonized the PNW as ice retreated. Identifying these refugia and reconstructing recolonization pathways is central to understanding the evolutionary history of PNW birds [P4, P6].

## 2.2 Glacial Refugia of PNW Birds

Five major refugia have been identified for PNW avian populations during the LGM, each contributing distinct genetic lineages to the modern avifauna:

### Refugium 1: The Pacific Coast (Haida Gwaii and Ice-Free Coastal Strips)

Parts of the outer coast of British Columbia, including portions of Haida Gwaii (Queen Charlotte Islands) and the Brooks Peninsula on Vancouver Island, remained ice-free during the LGM. These coastal refugia supported populations of forest birds, seabirds, and shorebirds in a narrow band of maritime-influenced habitat between the Cordilleran Ice Sheet and the Pacific Ocean. Phylogeographic evidence supports this refugium for:

- **Hairy Woodpecker** (*Dryobates villosus*) -- Haida Gwaii populations show genetic distinctiveness consistent with long isolation
- **Steller's Jay** (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) -- coastal BC populations show mitochondrial haplotypes divergent from interior birds
- **Chestnut-backed Chickadee** (*Poecile rufescens*) -- a predominantly coastal species whose current distribution maps closely onto the hypothesized coastal refugium
- **Northern Saw-whet Owl** (*Aegolius acadicus brooksi*) -- the *brooksi* subspecies of Haida Gwaii is phenotypically and genetically distinct, consistent with prolonged isolation in a coastal refugium [P4, O1]

The concept of ice-free coastal refugia remains debated -- the extent of ice-free habitat was small and its capacity to support viable bird populations is uncertain. However, genetic data from multiple species consistently show that coastal BC populations carry unique haplotypes not found in interior populations, supporting at least limited refugial persistence [P4].

### Refugium 2: The Klamath-Siskiyou Region

The mountainous terrain of southern Oregon and northern California -- the Klamath Mountains, Siskiyous, and southern Cascades -- remained unglaciated during the LGM and supported conifer forests throughout the Pleistocene. This region is recognized as a major glacial refugium for temperate forest biota, including:

- **Hermit Warbler** (*Setophaga occidentalis*) -- breeding range centered on the Klamath-Siskiyou, consistent with origin in this refugium. The species' limited northward range expansion compared to Townsend's Warbler may reflect slower post-glacial recolonization from a southern refugium (see Section 4.2)
- **White-headed Woodpecker** (*Dryobates albolarvatus*) -- distribution centered on dry montane forests from the Klamath-Siskiyou through the east Cascades
- **Mountain Quail** (*Oreortyx pictus*) -- another species with Klamath-Siskiyou biogeographic affinity
- **Thick-billed Fox Sparrow** (*Passerella iliaca megarhyncha* group) -- breeds in montane scrub of the Klamath region, distinct from the Sooty and Slate-colored groups to the north and east (see Module 1f)

The Klamath-Siskiyou region's importance as a refugium extends beyond birds. It is the most botanically diverse region in the PNW, hosting relict conifer populations (Brewer spruce, Port-Orford-cedar, foxtail pine) that also survived in this refugium. The co-persistence of plants and their dependent bird species in the same refugium underscores the ecosystem-level nature of glacial survival [P4, P6].

### Refugium 3: The Columbia Plateau and Great Basin

The unglaciated interior steppe of eastern Oregon, eastern Washington (south of the ice sheet margin), and southern Idaho supported cold-adapted steppe and shrubland communities during glacial periods. These habitats served as refugia for:

- **Greater Sage-Grouse** (*Centrocercus urophasianus*) -- the sagebrush steppe of the Columbia Basin and Snake River Plain provided continuous habitat throughout the Pleistocene, and sage-grouse populations in this region show high genetic diversity consistent with long-term occupancy (see Module 1b) [P36]
- **Horned Lark** (*Eremophila alpestris*) -- a widespread species of open habitats whose PNW subspecies (*strigata*, the Streaked Horned Lark) appears to represent a relict population from a Pleistocene steppe refugium. *E. a. strigata* is now restricted to remnant prairies and dune habitats in western WA and OR -- a distribution interpreted as a post-glacial range contraction as forests replaced steppe [G1, O1]
- **Burrowing Owl** (*Athene cunicularia*) -- a steppe-obligate species whose PNW range overlaps closely with the unglaciated Columbia Plateau
- **Sagebrush Sparrow** (*Artemisiospiza nevadensis*) -- obligate sagebrush species whose evolutionary history is tightly linked to the persistence of *Artemisia* steppe in interior refugia

The steppe refugium is distinctive in that it persisted not by being warmer than surrounding areas but by being drier -- the rain shadow east of the Cascades prevented the development of ice sheets in this region even during glacial maxima, and the vegetation (sagebrush, bunchgrass) was adapted to cold, arid conditions [P4].

### Refugium 4: The Northern Rocky Mountains

Portions of northern Idaho, western Montana, and adjacent BC that remained ice-free during the LGM supported montane forests in river valleys and on south-facing slopes. This refugium contributed to the genetic diversity of:

- **Spruce Grouse** (*Falcipennis canadensis*) -- the PNW's most range-restricted grouse, limited to the Selkirk Mountains and Okanogan Highlands, may represent a relict population that survived in a northern Rocky Mountain refugium and failed to expand westward (see Module 1b)
- **Northern Goshawk** (*Accipiter gentilis*) -- phylogeographic analysis by Sonsthagen et al. (2012) revealed that PNW goshawk populations derive from both Pacific coastal and interior Rocky Mountain refugial lineages, with a contact zone in the northern Cascades [P31]
- **Boreal Chickadee** (*Poecile hudsonicus*) -- reaches the PNW only in the northeastern corner (Selkirk Mountains, northern ID), consistent with persistence in and limited expansion from a Rocky Mountain refugium

### Refugium 5: Beringian and Subarctic Refugia

Several PNW bird species derive from populations that survived in Beringia (the unglaciated region spanning eastern Siberia, western Alaska, and the exposed Bering land bridge). After deglaciation, these Beringian lineages expanded southward into the PNW:

- **White-tailed Ptarmigan** (*Lagopus leucura*) -- likely survived on alpine nunataks and in Beringian tundra, recolonizing PNW alpine zones from both northern and southern refugia. PNW populations show mixed genetic signatures consistent with dual-source recolonization (see Module 1b) [P36]
- **Gray Jay** (*Perisoreus canadensis*) -- boreal-zone specialist that expanded southward from Beringian/subarctic refugia during deglaciation. Reaches the PNW in subalpine forests of the Cascades, Olympics, and northern Rockies
- **American Three-toed Woodpecker** (*Picoides dorsalis*) -- boreal-zone species at its southern range limit in PNW montane forests

## 2.3 Post-Glacial Recolonization and Range Dynamics (18,000 ya - present)

The retreat of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet occurred in pulses between approximately 18,000 and 10,000 years ago. As ice retreated, vegetation zones expanded northward and upslope, and bird populations tracked their preferred habitats. The sequence of recolonization shaped the modern PNW avifauna in several ways:

### Recolonization Speed and Species Traits

Not all species recolonized at the same rate. Species with high dispersal capacity (strong fliers, habitat generalists) expanded quickly, while species with low dispersal capacity (weak fliers, habitat specialists) lagged behind. This differential recolonization creates predictable patterns:

**Fast recolonizers:** Habitat generalists with strong flight -- corvids, raptors, waterfowl. These species reached newly available habitat within centuries of deglaciation. Example: the Bald Eagle and Common Raven, both habitat generalists with continental-scale ranges, show minimal genetic structure across the PNW, consistent with rapid post-glacial range expansion and high ongoing gene flow [O1].

**Moderate recolonizers:** Forest-dependent species with moderate dispersal -- woodpeckers, thrushes, most warblers. These species expanded northward as forests established (typically 2,000-5,000 years after ice retreat), but showed enough geographic structure to develop regional genetic differences. Example: the Hermit Warbler's limited northward expansion relative to Townsend's Warbler may reflect slower recolonization from a more southern (Klamath) refugium [P25, P26].

**Slow recolonizers / range-limited:** Habitat specialists with low dispersal -- alpine-obligate species, old-growth specialists. These species recolonized slowly and may still be expanding range or may have reached equilibrium at reduced ranges. Example: the Northern Spotted Owl, dependent on large tracts of old-growth forest, has a distribution shaped by both post-glacial forest establishment and subsequent old-growth loss [G1].

### The Mesic Forest Expansion

The most consequential post-glacial vegetation change for PNW birds was the expansion of mesic conifer forests (Douglas-fir, western hemlock, western red cedar) across the Puget Sound lowland and Willamette Valley beginning approximately 10,000-8,000 years ago. This created a vast new habitat for forest-dependent species and established the ecological foundation of the modern west-side avifauna. Species that today define west-side forest bird communities -- Varied Thrush, Pacific Wren, Chestnut-backed Chickadee, Red-breasted Sapsucker -- expanded into this newly forested landscape from coastal and southern refugia.

The mid-Holocene warm period (~7,000-4,000 years ago) temporarily pushed forest zones upslope and allowed steppe vegetation to expand westward through the Columbia River Gorge and into portions of the Willamette Valley. This warm period may have facilitated the westward expansion of east-side species (Western Meadowlark, Say's Phoebe) into habitats they currently occupy in the rain shadow valleys of the east Cascades [P4].

### The Sooty/Dusky Grouse Split: A Case Study in Glacial Speciation

The Sooty Grouse (*Dendragapus fuliginosus*) and Dusky Grouse (*D. obscurus*), split by the AOS in 2006 from the formerly lumped "Blue Grouse," provide one of the clearest examples of Pleistocene glacial speciation in PNW birds (see Module 1b).

During glacial periods, ancestral Blue Grouse populations were split by the Cordilleran Ice Sheet into western (coastal) and eastern (interior) refugia. The western population, surviving in coastal and Klamath refugia, evolved darker plumage (Sooty), yellow cervical air sacs, and a call frequency of 100-120 Hz. The eastern population, surviving in Rocky Mountain refugia, evolved paler plumage (Dusky), purplish-red cervical air sacs, and a slightly lower call frequency of 80-100 Hz. When ice retreated, the two forms expanded back toward each other and now meet along the Cascade crest, where occasional hybridization occurs but reproductive isolation is largely maintained by display differences [O1, O3].

Genetic divergence between Sooty and Dusky Grouse is estimated at 0.5-1.0 million years, placing the split within the Pleistocene but prior to the Last Glacial Maximum. This suggests that multiple glacial cycles reinforced the initial divergence, with populations repeatedly separated during glacial advances and brought into contact during interglacials [P36].

# PART III: SPECIATION IN ACTION -- ADAPTIVE RADIATION AND ECOTYPE FORMATION

## 3.1 Ecological Speciation: The Red Crossbill Complex

The Red Crossbill (*Loxia curvirostra*) call type complex in the PNW represents the most compelling example of ecological speciation in any North American bird. Ten or more call types -- each associated with a specific conifer species, characterized by distinct bill morphology and flight call, and maintaining reproductive isolation through assortative mating -- coexist across the region (see Module 1f for full treatment).

### The Coevolutionary Framework

Benkman (1993, 2003) proposed that Red Crossbill diversification is driven by coevolution between crossbills and conifers [P21]. The key components of this framework:

1. **Conifer defense:** Conifers evolve cone traits (scale thickness, seed depth, cone structure) that reduce seed extraction by crossbills.
2. **Crossbill counter-adaptation:** Crossbill populations evolve bill morphology (bill depth, palate groove width, jaw muscle mass) optimized for a specific conifer's cone.
3. **Ecological partitioning:** Different crossbill populations specialize on different conifers, reducing interspecific competition.
4. **Vocal divergence:** Flight calls diverge between ecologically distinct populations, serving as assortative mating signals.
5. **Reproductive isolation:** Assortative mating by call type reduces gene flow, allowing morphological and ecological specialization to accumulate.

The PNW -- with its unmatched diversity of conifer species -- provides the ecological stage for this coevolutionary radiation. The key call types in the PNW and their conifer associations:

| Call Type | Bill depth (mm) | Preferred conifer | PNW habitat | Evolutionary status |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| Type 2 | 9.5-10.5 | Ponderosa pine | East Cascades, Blue Mountains (Zone 4, 6) | Stable ecotype |
| Type 3 | 10.0-11.0 | Western hemlock | West Cascades, coast (Zone 2, 3) | Stable ecotype |
| Type 4 | 9.0-10.0 | Douglas-fir | Both Cascade slopes (Zone 2, 3) | Stable ecotype |

### The Cassia Crossbill: Speciation Completed

The Cassia Crossbill (*Loxia sinesciuris*), described as a full species in 2017, represents the endpoint of the ecological speciation process visible in the call type complex. In the South Hills and Albion Mountains of southern Idaho, lodgepole pines lack red squirrels (*Tamiasciurus hudsonicus*) -- the primary competitor for pine seeds across the rest of the crossbill's range. The absence of squirrel competition released crossbills from the need for rapid cone-cracking (squirrels deplete seed crops before crossbills can exploit them), allowing the South Hills population to evolve:

- Larger, deeper bills optimized for the specific lodgepole pine cone morphology of the South Hills
- Sedentary behavior (other crossbill types are nomadic, tracking cone crop variability)
- A unique flight call (Type 9) that differs from all other Red Crossbill call types
- Reproductive isolation from sympatric Type 5 crossbills (which also eat lodgepole pine but are morphologically less specialized)

The estimated population of approximately 5,800 individuals in a range of approximately 70 km2 makes the Cassia Crossbill one of the most range-restricted bird species in North America. It is currently an ESA candidate species and was the first new bird species described from the contiguous United States in over a century [P22, O3, G1].

The Cassia Crossbill demonstrates that ecological speciation can proceed to completion in birds without geographic isolation -- Type 9 crossbills coexist with Type 5 crossbills in the South Hills, yet maintain reproductive isolation through call-type-mediated assortative mating and ecological niche divergence [P22].

## 3.2 Subspecies as Incipient Species: The Song Sparrow Radiation

The Song Sparrow (*Melospiza melodia*) is the most geographically variable bird species in North America, with 24 recognized subspecies spanning a 2.5-fold range in body mass (12-51 g) -- from tiny desert forms to massive coastal-island forms. The PNW, with 6+ subspecies, is the epicenter of Song Sparrow diversification (see Module 1f for detailed subspecies treatments).

### Mechanisms of Divergence

Three evolutionary mechanisms operate simultaneously in PNW Song Sparrow populations:

**Gloger's Rule -- melanin clines.** Song Sparrow plumage darkness increases with environmental humidity. Coastal *morphallax* (dark sooty-brown, annual precipitation >100 inches) grades continuously through lowland *rufina* (rufous-brown) to interior *merrilli* (pale gray-brown, annual precipitation <10 inches). The melanin gradient is adaptive: darker, more melanized feathers resist bacterial degradation better in humid environments, and dark plumage provides crypsis against dark, wet substrates [P24].

**Bergmann's Rule and insular gigantism.** Body size increases with latitude and on islands. Coastal *morphallax* (28-38 g) is substantially larger than interior *merrilli* (20-28 g). Island populations (Aleutian *maxima*, 38-51 g) achieve the most extreme body sizes. The mechanisms likely include thermoregulatory advantage of larger body size in cold, wet coastal climates, and reduced interspecific competition on islands allowing niche expansion and body size increase [P24, P35].

**Bill morphology and diet.** Bill depth and width vary among subspecies in correlation with local diet. Coastal forms with access to intertidal invertebrates have deeper, more robust bills than interior forms that feed primarily on seeds. This morphological divergence mirrors the crossbill-conifer coevolution at a smaller scale -- diet drives bill evolution, which reinforces dietary specialization [P24].

### Ring Species Properties

Patten and Pruett (2009) argued that Song Sparrow geographic variation across western North America exhibits ring-species-like properties: adjacent populations intergrade freely, but terminal forms (e.g., tiny desert *fallax* and massive Aleutian *maxima*) are so different that they would likely fail to interbreed if brought into secondary contact. The PNW sits along the ring where coastal darkening, body size increase, and latitudinal clines converge to produce maximum phenotypic diversity. Whether the Song Sparrow complex represents one highly variable species, multiple incipient species, or a continuum of partially isolated populations depends on the species concept applied -- and this ambiguity is itself informative about the speciation process [P24].

## 3.3 Rapid Diversification: The Dark-eyed Junco Complex

The Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*) complex -- comprising five formerly recognized species lumped by AOS in 1973 (Oregon, Slate-colored, White-winged, Gray-headed, Pink-sided Juncos) -- illustrates Pleistocene speciation in its intermediate stages (see Module 1f).

### Phylogeographic History

Molecular phylogenetics reveals that the junco complex radiated rapidly from a common ancestor in Mexico (the Yellow-eyed Junco, *J. phaeonotus*, is the outgroup). The radiation proceeded through the following sequence:

1. **Ancestral population** in Mexican highlands (~1-2 Ma)
2. **Northward expansion** into temperate North America during an interglacial
3. **Glacial fragmentation** -- populations isolated in separate refugia during glacial advances:
   - Pacific coastal refugium -> Oregon Junco (*oreganus* group)
   - Interior western refugium -> Gray-headed and Pink-sided forms
   - Eastern refugium -> Slate-colored Junco (*hyemalis*)
   - Central Great Plains refugium -> White-winged Junco (*aikeni*)
4. **Post-glacial secondary contact** -- forms expanded out of refugia and met at contact zones where hybridization occurs freely

The Oregon Junco group -- the dominant breeding junco in the PNW -- is the product of the Pacific coastal refugium. As glaciers retreated approximately 15,000-10,000 years ago, Oregon Juncos expanded northward from an Oregon-California refugium. The Cassiar Junco (*cismontanus*) of BC, intermediate between Oregon and Slate-colored forms, marks the secondary contact zone between Pacific and interior lineages [O1, O3].

### Plumage Divergence Without Genetic Divergence

Despite striking plumage differences (the black-hooded Oregon Junco vs. the uniformly gray Slate-colored Junco), molecular divergence among junco forms is remarkably shallow (~0.5-1.0% mtDNA sequence divergence). This indicates that plumage evolution outpaced genetic divergence -- consistent with strong sexual selection on plumage pattern driving rapid phenotypic diversification during brief periods of glacial isolation. The junco complex thus demonstrates that visually dramatic "species" differences can evolve on timescales of tens of thousands of years under strong selection, even while neutral genetic markers show minimal divergence [P30, O1].

## 3.4 Ecotype Formation: Alpine Specialists

The PNW's alpine zone (Zone 1) supports a small but distinctive assemblage of avian ecotypes -- populations adapted to the extreme conditions of high elevation: UV radiation, thin atmosphere, compressed breeding seasons, temperature extremes, and limited food resources.

### White-tailed Ptarmigan: Alpine-Obligate Evolution

The White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) is the most extreme alpine specialist in the PNW avifauna (see Module 1b). Its evolutionary adaptations to alpine life include:

- **Three seasonal plumages** -- the most extensive molt cycle of any PNW bird, driven by intense selection for crypsis across alpine seasons (snow-white in winter, mottled brown in summer)
- **Feathered toes** functioning as snowshoes and thermal insulation
- **Remarkably low basal metabolic rate** enabling survival on nutrient-poor alpine diet
- **Enhanced cecal fermentation** of plant cellulose compared to other grouse
- **Snow-burrowing behavior** reducing metabolic heat loss by 40-50% during severe weather
- **Retention of white tail feathers year-round** (unique in the genus *Lagopus*) -- possibly reflecting relaxed selection on tail coloration or a sexual signaling function

PNW ptarmigan populations likely survived Pleistocene glaciations on nunataks -- ice-free alpine peaks protruding above the Cordilleran Ice Sheet. Genetic data suggest recolonization from both northern (Beringian) and southern (Rocky Mountain) refugia, with PNW populations carrying mixed genetic signatures. White-tailed Ptarmigan is the only ptarmigan endemic to North America and the only member of the genus native to the contiguous United States [P36, O1].

### Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch: Snowfield-Edge Ecology

The Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) occupies a unique ecological niche -- the "aeolian zone" at the margins of permanent and semi-permanent snowfields, where wind-transported arthropods accumulate on snow surfaces. This foraging strategy has no parallel among other PNW birds and has driven morphological adaptations including:

- Relatively long legs for a finch (adapted for walking on uneven alpine terrain)
- Dark plumage (enhanced solar heat absorption at high altitude)
- Large body size for a fringillid (cold-tolerance via Bergmann's Rule)
- Specialized thermoregulatory physiology enabling sustained activity at temperatures below -20C

Two PNW subspecies -- *L. t. tephrocotis* (interior, more extensive gray crown) and *L. t. littoralis* (Hepburn's Rosy-Finch, darker overall, coastal mountains) -- may represent populations from different glacial refugia that have not yet fully merged despite secondary contact (see Module 1f) [P17, O1].

### American Pipit: Holarctic Alpine Lineage

The American Pipit (*Anthus rubescens*) breeds in alpine tundra across the PNW's highest peaks (Zone 1). The species is part of a Holarctic radiation -- its Eurasian counterpart, the Water Pipit (*A. spinoletta*), was formerly considered conspecific. The split reflects Pleistocene vicariance, with the Bering land bridge serving as a corridor during glacial periods and a barrier during interglacials. PNW alpine pipits winter in lowland agricultural fields and mudflats (Zones 5, 7), performing one of the most dramatic seasonal elevational migrations of any PNW passerine -- from 2,500+ m breeding altitude to sea level wintering habitat [O1, O3].

# PART IV: HYBRIDIZATION ZONES -- WHERE SPECIES MEET, MIX, AND MAINTAIN BOUNDARIES

## 4.1 The Northern Flicker Hybrid Zone

The Northern Flicker (*Colaptes auratus*) hybrid zone is one of the most extensively studied avian hybrid zones in North America (see Module 1f for complete treatment). The Red-shafted form (*C. a. cafer*, western) and Yellow-shafted form (*C. a. auratus*, eastern) interbreed through a contact zone running from southern Alaska through British Columbia and south along the eastern Rockies. The PNW sits squarely within this zone.

### Genomic Architecture

Aguillon et al. (2018) used genome-wide markers to characterize the flicker hybrid zone and found:

1. **Minimal overall genomic divergence** -- Red-shafted and Yellow-shafted differ at only ~0.1% of the genome, far less than typical for recognized bird species
2. **Divergence concentrated in few loci** -- the genomic regions controlling feather shaft color (carotenoid pigmentation pathways) show strong differentiation, while the rest of the genome is homogenized by gene flow
3. **Stable hybrid zone width** -- the zone has not moved or narrowed over decades of observation, indicating a dynamic equilibrium between selection against hybrids and dispersal into the zone
4. **Weak assortative mating** -- flickers do not preferentially mate by phenotype, yet the hybrid zone remains narrow, implicating post-mating selection (reduced hybrid fitness) as the primary maintenance mechanism [P3]

### PNW Hybrid Zone Geography

The contact zone enters the PNW through southern BC (Okanogan, Kamloops corridor) and runs south through eastern WA (Okanogan, Chelan, Kittitas counties) into eastern OR (Deschutes, Grant, Union counties). Hybrid frequency reaches 20-50% in eastern Washington, declining rapidly westward -- by the Puget Trough and Willamette Valley, virtually all flickers are phenotypically pure Red-shafted. The hybrid zone width is approximately 200-400 km, broad by avian standards, indicating substantial dispersal and weak selection against hybrids [P3, P37].

The Cascade Range functions as a partial barrier to Yellow-shafted gene flow, reinforcing the east-west plumage gradient. The Columbia River Gorge, which breaches the Cascade barrier, does not appear to serve as a major corridor for hybrid gene flow -- flickers are forest-edge birds and the Gorge's steep, forested slopes may not provide continuous suitable habitat for Yellow-shafted dispersal [P3].

### Evolutionary Significance

The flicker hybrid zone challenges species concepts. Red-shafted and Yellow-shafted flickers interbreed freely, produce fertile offspring, and show almost no genetic divergence -- yet they maintain distinct phenotypes across most of their range. The AOS lumped them in 1973, but the genomic data reveal that despite gene flow, divergent selection on plumage maintains type integrity. The flicker hybrid zone may represent:

- Two incipient species held apart by selection on plumage genes
- A single species with a stable polymorphism maintained by environmental gradients (humid west vs. arid east)
- A case of "speciation in reverse" where formerly isolated populations are merging

All three interpretations have support, and the ambiguity itself illuminates the continuous nature of the speciation process [P3, O3].

## 4.2 Townsend's Warbler x Hermit Warbler: Asymmetric Introgression

The hybrid zone between Townsend's Warbler (*Setophaga townsendi*) and Hermit Warbler (*S. occidentalis*) in the Oregon and Washington Cascades is one of the most consequential avian hybrid zones in the PNW (see Module 1f). Unlike the stable flicker hybrid zone, the warbler hybrid zone is moving.

### The Displacement Pattern

Over the past century, the contact zone has shifted approximately 1-2 km per decade southward, with Townsend's Warbler genotypes progressively replacing Hermit Warbler genotypes. The mechanism is behavioral dominance:

1. **Townsend's males are competitively dominant** over Hermit males in territorial disputes
2. **Hybrid males (Townsend's x Hermit) are also dominant** over pure Hermit males
3. **Asymmetric gene flow** -- Townsend's genes introgress into Hermit populations faster than Hermit genes introgress into Townsend's populations
4. **Net effect** -- the Hermit Warbler breeding range contracts southward as Townsend's and hybrid genotypes replace pure Hermit genotypes at the northern edge of the Hermit range [P25, P26]

### Genomic Underpinnings

Toews et al. (2016) found that genomic divergence between Townsend's and Hermit Warblers is concentrated in very few loci -- primarily genes controlling plumage pattern (melanin-based cheek patch and streak intensity). Across the rest of the genome, the two species are nearly identical. This "plumage genes and little else" pattern suggests that:

- The two species diverged recently (during the Pleistocene, likely in different glacial refugia -- Townsend's in a northern/coastal refugium, Hermit in a southern/Klamath refugium)
- Divergent sexual selection on plumage pattern is the primary force maintaining species boundaries
- The rest of the genome has been homogenized by gene flow since secondary contact
- The hybrid zone is "porous" -- most of the genome passes freely between species, but the plumage loci are under strong enough selection to resist introgression [P25]

### Conservation Implications

If the southward displacement continues at historical rates, the Hermit Warbler could lose much of its PNW breeding range within the coming century. The species would persist in California, but PNW Hermit Warbler populations would be absorbed into the Townsend's Warbler gene pool -- an example of "speciation in reverse" or "genetic swamping." Currently, both species remain common breeders in PNW montane forests (Zone 2) and neither is listed, but the hybrid zone dynamics warrant long-term monitoring [P25, P26, O1].

## 4.3 Glaucous-winged x Western Gull: The Puget Sound Hybrid Swarm

The hybrid zone between Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*) and Western Gull (*L. occidentalis*) along the Pacific coast from southern BC through Oregon is the most visible gull hybridization complex in the PNW. The two species differ primarily in mantle (back) color -- pale gray in Glaucous-winged, dark gray in Western -- and in wingtip pattern (pale gray in Glaucous-winged, black in Western).

### Hybrid Zone Structure

The hybrid zone is centered on the Washington coast, Puget Sound, and the Strait of Juan de Fuca, where intermediate phenotypes ("Olympic Gulls") are the most common large gull. The gradient is roughly latitudinal:

- **Northern BC and Alaska:** Pure Glaucous-winged Gull
- **Southern BC and northern WA:** Predominantly Glaucous-winged with some hybrids
- **Puget Sound and outer WA coast:** Hybrid swarm -- a majority of individuals show intermediate characters (medium gray mantle, partially dark wingtips)
- **Oregon coast:** Mixed Western Gull and hybrid phenotypes
- **Southern Oregon and California:** Predominantly pure Western Gull

The hybrid swarm in Puget Sound is so pervasive that identifying individual gulls to "species" is often impossible -- the population functions as a continuous blend of parental phenotypes. This has led some ornithologists to question whether Glaucous-winged and Western Gull should be considered separate species at all [O1, O3].

### Evolutionary Dynamics

The gull hybrid zone differs from the flicker and warbler zones in several ways:

- **Broader hybrid zone** -- spanning the entire Washington coastline, wider than any other PNW avian hybrid zone
- **Higher hybrid frequency** -- in Puget Sound, intermediate phenotypes may outnumber either parental type
- **Habitat overlap** -- both species are coastal generalists, reducing the opportunity for ecological niche separation
- **Assortative mating appears weak** -- pairing by phenotype is not strongly assortative in the hybrid swarm

The persistence of pure parental forms to the north (Glaucous-winged) and south (Western) despite extensive hybridization in the center suggests either environmental selection maintaining plumage differences across a latitudinal gradient, or limited dispersal keeping parental populations intact at the ends of the range. The gull hybrid zone illustrates a different mode of species interaction from the warbler zone -- stable coexistence and blending rather than competitive displacement [O1].

## 4.4 Other PNW Hybrid Zones

### Black-headed x Rose-breasted Grosbeak

*Pheucticus melanocephalus* (Black-headed, western) and *P. ludovicianus* (Rose-breasted, eastern) hybridize in the Great Plains, with occasional hybrids recorded in the PNW (eastern WA, ID) where both species co-occur as breeding birds. Hybrids show intermediate plumage -- orange breast instead of pure rose-red or pure orange, mixed head patterns. The hybrid zone is geographically distant from the PNW but relevant because both species breed in the study area, and occasional PNW hybrids indicate ongoing gene flow across the continent [O1].

### Lazuli x Indigo Bunting

*Passerina amoena* (Lazuli, western) and *P. cyanea* (Indigo, eastern) hybridize extensively in the Great Plains, and Indigo Buntings have expanded westward into the PNW (eastern WA, ID) where hybridization with Lazuli Buntings is documented. Male hybrids show mixed plumage -- partially blue with variable amounts of white wing bars and cinnamon breast. Unlike the Townsend's x Hermit Warbler zone, this hybrid zone appears to be expanding westward with the Indigo Bunting's range expansion, potentially driven by habitat modification (agricultural clearing creating open habitats favored by Indigo Buntings) [O1].

### Red-breasted x Red-naped Sapsucker

*Sphyrapicus ruber* (Red-breasted, west-side) and *S. nuchalis* (Red-naped, east-side) hybridize along the Cascade crest and in the Blue Mountains of eastern Oregon. Hybrids show intermediate red patterning on the nape and breast. The contact zone closely follows the Cascade rainshadow boundary, supporting the importance of the Cascades as a semi-permeable biogeographic barrier. A third sapsucker species, Yellow-bellied Sapsucker (*S. varius*), meets Red-naped at the eastern edge of the study area in northern Idaho. The *Sphyrapicus* complex thus presents a three-species parapatric system with two nested hybrid zones [O1, O3].

### Bullock's x Baltimore Oriole

*Icterus bullockii* (Bullock's, western) and *I. galbula* (Baltimore, eastern) were lumped as "Northern Oriole" by the AOS in 1973 based on extensive Great Plains hybridization, then re-split in 1995 when genetic analysis showed that hybridization was not eroding species boundaries. In the PNW, Bullock's Oriole is the resident species; Baltimore Oriole occurs as a rare vagrant. Occasional hybrids in the PNW periphery (eastern WA, ID) connect the region to the Great Plains hybrid zone. The re-split of these orioles illustrates how improved genetic tools can overturn hybridization-based taxonomic lumping [O1, O3].

# PART V: ISLAND BIOGEOGRAPHY -- INSULAR EVOLUTION IN THE PNW

## 5.1 Theoretical Framework

The PNW contains several island and island-like systems that have generated endemic and semi-endemic bird populations through the processes described by MacArthur and Wilson's (1967) equilibrium theory of island biogeography, supplemented by population genetic theory:

- **True islands:** San Juan Islands (WA), Haida Gwaii (BC), Vancouver Island (BC), Gulf Islands (BC)
- **Habitat islands:** Alpine "sky islands" (isolated mountain peaks above treeline), old-growth forest fragments, sagebrush steppe patches isolated by agricultural conversion, Willamette Valley prairies
- **Peninsular effects:** Olympic Peninsula (connected by a narrow lowland corridor)

For each system, the predictions are consistent: smaller, more isolated populations experience reduced gene flow, genetic drift, and local adaptation, leading to morphological and genetic divergence from mainland/continuous populations.

## 5.2 Haida Gwaii (Queen Charlotte Islands)

Haida Gwaii, an archipelago of approximately 150 islands 80 km off the northern BC coast, is the PNW's most important island biogeographic system for avian evolution. The archipelago's combination of moderate size (10,180 km2), moderate isolation, glacial refugium status, and unique ecological conditions has produced the highest concentration of endemic bird subspecies in the PNW:

### Endemic and Near-Endemic Subspecies

| Species | Haida Gwaii subspecies | Mainland form | Key differences |
|---------|----------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Northern Saw-whet Owl | *A. a. brooksi* | *A. a. acadicus* | Darker, buffer plumage; unique mtDNA haplotype |
| Hairy Woodpecker | *D. v. picoideus* | *D. v. harrisi* | Smaller, darker |
| Steller's Jay | *C. s. carlottae* | *C. s. stelleri* | Larger, darker, blacker crest |
| Pine Grosbeak | *P. e. carlottae* | *P. e. flammula* | Darker, restricted range |
| Song Sparrow | *M. m. rufina* (variant) | *M. m. rufina* (mainland) | Larger-billed, darker |

The Northern Saw-whet Owl subspecies *brooksi* of Haida Gwaii is perhaps the most genetically distinct island population in the PNW avifauna. It carries mitochondrial haplotypes not found in any mainland population, consistent with survival in a Haida Gwaii glacial refugium and subsequent isolation for at least 10,000-15,000 years. The subspecies is darker and buffer than mainland Saw-whet Owls -- conforming to the insular melanin pattern seen across multiple PNW island taxa [O1, P4].

### Island Biogeographic Patterns

Haida Gwaii bird communities show classic island biogeographic patterns:

- **Reduced species richness** compared to equivalent mainland habitat (~60% of mainland species present)
- **Density compensation** -- species present on the islands occur at higher densities than on the mainland, filling ecological niches left vacant by absent species
- **Niche expansion** -- island populations exploit broader habitat and dietary niches than mainland conspecifics
- **Insular tameness** -- Haida Gwaii populations of several species (e.g., Black Bear, Hairy Woodpecker) show reduced wariness compared to mainland counterparts, consistent with reduced predator diversity on islands [O1]

### Cultural Context

Haida Gwaii's avifauna has been a central element of Haida culture for millennia. The Raven (*Corvus corax*) holds fundamental significance in Haida cosmology and art. Scientific study of Haida Gwaii birds has benefited from collaboration with Haida Nation traditional ecological knowledge keepers, whose observations of bird behavior and seasonal patterns extend continuously over timescales far exceeding Western ornithological records [C2].

## 5.3 Vancouver Island

Vancouver Island (31,285 km2, the largest island on the Pacific coast of North America) is large enough to support near-complete PNW avifaunas but sufficiently isolated (separated from the mainland by narrow but deep straits) to promote subspecific differentiation. Notable Vancouver Island endemic subspecies:

| Species | Vancouver Island subspecies | Key differentiation |
|---------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Blue Grouse (Sooty) | *D. f. fuliginosus* (type locality) | Darkest Sooty Grouse form |
| Band-tailed Pigeon | Local population | Larger-bodied than interior birds |
| Chestnut-backed Chickadee | Local population | Richer chestnut coloration |
| Northern Pygmy-Owl | Local population | Darker plumage |

Vancouver Island's avifauna reflects its complex glacial history. During the LGM, most of the island was glaciated, but the Brooks Peninsula on the northwest coast and possibly portions of the east coast remained ice-free. These small refugia may have harbored resident populations that persisted through glacial maxima, contributing to the genetic distinctiveness of Vancouver Island bird populations [P4].

The narrow straits separating Vancouver Island from the mainland (as little as 2 km at the Seymour Narrows) are sufficient to reduce gene flow for weak-flying species but permit free exchange for strong fliers. This creates a filter effect: species with strong overwater dispersal (corvids, raptors, swallows) show minimal island-mainland divergence, while weaker-flying species (owls, woodpeckers, wrens) show greater differentiation [O1].

## 5.4 San Juan Islands

The San Juan Islands archipelago (approximately 170 named islands in Puget Sound/Strait of Georgia) presents a gradient of island size, isolation, and habitat diversity ideal for testing island biogeographic predictions:

- **Species-area relationship:** Larger islands support more breeding species. San Juan Island (~55 km2) supports ~100 breeding species; smaller islands (<1 km2) support 10-30 species [O2].
- **Isolation effects:** Islands closer to the mainland (e.g., Fidalgo Island, connected by bridge) have near-complete mainland avifaunas. More isolated islands (e.g., Smith Island, Destruction Island) have depauperate communities dominated by seabirds and generalist landbirds.
- **Seabird colonies:** The San Juan Islands support significant breeding colonies of Glaucous-winged Gull, Pigeon Guillemot, Rhinoceros Auklet, and Tufted Puffin (Protection Island NWR). These colony sites are critical for PNW seabird conservation.

The San Juan Islands were fully glaciated during the LGM, meaning their current avifauna is entirely post-glacial in origin (less than ~14,000 years old). The islands were connected to the mainland during periods of lower sea level, allowing colonization by walking/short flights, then became isolated as sea levels rose. This recent isolation has not produced the degree of subspecific differentiation seen on Haida Gwaii or Vancouver Island, but it has created population-level genetic structure detectable with modern genomic tools [P4, O2].

## 5.5 Sky Islands: Alpine Habitat Fragmentation

Above treeline on PNW volcanoes and peaks, alpine tundra habitat exists as isolated "sky islands" separated by forested valleys that are impassable for alpine-obligate species. The major PNW sky islands are:

| Peak/Range | Alpine area (km2) | Key alpine bird species |
|------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Mt. Rainier | ~60 | White-tailed Ptarmigan, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, American Pipit, Horned Lark |
| Mt. Baker | ~25 | White-tailed Ptarmigan, Rosy-Finch, Pipit |
| North Cascades complex | ~150 (fragmented) | All alpine species; highest connectivity |
| Mt. Adams | ~15 | Rosy-Finch, Pipit (ptarmigan status uncertain) |
| Mt. Hood | ~10 | Rosy-Finch, Pipit (no confirmed ptarmigan breeding) |
| Olympic Mountains | ~20 (highly fragmented) | Rosy-Finch (*littoralis*), Pipit (no ptarmigan) |
| Wallowa Mountains | ~15 | Rosy-Finch, Pipit |
| Steens Mountain | ~5 | Rosy-Finch (southern range limit in OR) |

Alpine sky islands demonstrate several island biogeographic principles:

- **Area-dependent occupancy:** White-tailed Ptarmigan, the most area-sensitive alpine obligate, is absent from smaller sky islands (Mt. Hood, Steens Mountain, Olympics) despite apparently suitable habitat, suggesting minimum area requirements or colonization constraints.
- **Subspecies differentiation:** The two Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch subspecies in the PNW (*tephrocotis* on interior peaks, *littoralis* on coastal mountains) correspond to different sky island systems, with the Olympic and Coast Range populations of *littoralis* isolated from the Cascade and Rocky Mountain populations of *tephrocotis* by intervening lowland forest.
- **Climate vulnerability:** As climate warms and treeline advances upslope, alpine sky islands shrink. Species with no higher ground to retreat to face range contraction and potential local extinction (see Module 1b: White-tailed Ptarmigan climate vulnerability) [P17, O1].

# PART VI: THE EVOLUTION OF MIGRATION

## 6.1 Origins of Avian Migration

Migration is one of the most energetically demanding and ecologically consequential behaviors in the avian world. Approximately 40% of PNW breeding bird species are migratory, ranging from extreme long-distance migrants (Rufous Hummingbird: 6,000+ km one way) to short-distance elevational migrants (Dusky Grouse: ~500 m uphill in winter). The evolution of migratory behavior in the PNW avifauna reflects the interplay of phylogenetic history, Pleistocene climate cycles, and ongoing environmental change.

### Two Hypotheses for Migration Origins

**Southern-home hypothesis.** Migration evolved in tropical/subtropical resident species that expanded breeding ranges into seasonal temperate zones, returning to their ancestral range in winter. Under this model, the "home" of migratory PNW species is their tropical wintering ground, and northward breeding migration is a derived behavior driven by the seasonal availability of high-latitude resources (long summer days, insect abundance, reduced competition).

**Northern-home hypothesis.** Migration evolved in temperate resident species forced southward during winter by resource scarcity, with winter migration to warmer areas as the derived behavior. Under this model, the "home" of migratory species is the temperate breeding ground, and southward winter migration evolved as a survival strategy.

Molecular phylogenetic evidence generally supports the southern-home hypothesis for long-distance Neotropical migrants (most PNW warblers, tanagers, and flycatchers have tropical sister taxa), while the northern-home hypothesis better explains short-distance and elevational migrants (sparrows, finches, thrushes with temperate sister taxa) [P27, O1].

### The PNW Migratory Spectrum

PNW birds exhibit a complete spectrum of migratory strategies:

**Category 1: Neotropical long-distance migrants** -- Species breeding in the PNW and wintering in Mexico, Central America, or South America.

| Species | One-way distance (km) | Wintering area | PNW arrival |
|---------|----------------------|----------------|-------------|
| Rufous Hummingbird | 5,000-6,000 | Mexico | March-April |

Neotropical migrants make up approximately 30% of PNW breeding bird species by count but dominate the insectivore guild during the breeding season. Their arrival timing is tightly linked to insect phenology, and climate-driven mismatches between arrival date and peak insect availability may reduce breeding success [P19, O1].

**Category 2: Medium-distance migrants** -- Species breeding in the PNW and wintering within temperate North America (southern US to northern Mexico).

**Category 4: Residents** -- Species present year-round with no seasonal movement.

Approximately 40% of PNW breeding species are year-round residents. Resident status in the PNW requires either: (a) winter food availability (seed-eaters: chickadees, nuthatches; predators: owls, accipiters), (b) physiological cold tolerance, or (c) access to aquatic resources (American Dipper in ice-free streams, Harlequin Duck on winter ocean).

| Species | Trigger for irruption | PNW frequency |
|---------|----------------------|---------------|

Irruptive migrants are particularly characteristic of boreal/subalpine ecosystems whose food resources fluctuate dramatically between years. The PNW receives irruptive pulses from more northern and interior boreal forests when seed and berry crops fail (see Module 1f: Common Redpoll, Evening Grosbeak) [O1].

## 6.2 Partial Migration: A Window into Migratory Evolution

Many PNW species are "partial migrants" -- some individuals in a population migrate while others remain resident year-round. Partial migration is considered an evolutionary intermediate between full residency and full migration, and PNW species provide multiple examples:

**American Robin:** Some PNW populations are fully migratory (northern BC -- departs completely in winter), some are partial (Puget Sound -- some individuals remain, others move south), and some are resident (southern OR -- year-round). Migratory tendency correlates with winter food availability and severity [O1].

**Song Sparrow:** Coastal PNW subspecies (*morphallax*, *rufina*) are largely sedentary, while interior subspecies (*merrilli*) are partially migratory, with some individuals wintering south. The migratory/resident divide corresponds to the climatic divide -- maritime climates with mild winters support residents; continental climates with severe winters select for migration (see Module 1f) [P24].

**Dark-eyed Junco:** Oregon Juncos in the PNW include both resident and migratory individuals. In mild-winter lowland areas (Puget Sound, Willamette Valley), populations include a mix of year-round residents and birds that arrive from higher-elevation or more northerly breeding sites. The proportion of migrants in winter flocks increases during cold winters, suggesting that the resident/migrant decision is phenotypically flexible, influenced by individual condition and environmental cues [O1].

Partial migration is evolutionarily significant because it suggests that the decision to migrate or stay can be labile within species and even within populations. If migratory behavior can shift rapidly in response to environmental change, PNW bird species may have greater capacity to adjust to climate change than a rigid migratory/resident dichotomy would imply [P27].

## 6.3 Climate-Driven Shifts in Migration

Ongoing climate change is altering PNW migratory patterns in detectable ways:

### Earlier Arrival Dates

Long-term banding and eBird data show that many PNW Neotropical migrants are arriving 3-10 days earlier than they did 50 years ago, consistent with warmer spring temperatures advancing insect phenology and vegetation green-up. Species showing documented earlier arrival in the PNW include:

- Violet-green Swallow (3-5 days earlier since 1970) [O2]
- Western Tanager (5-7 days earlier since 1975) [O2]
- Rufous Hummingbird (arrival tracking flowering phenology of salmonberry and red-flowering currant) [O2]

### Range Shifts

Some PNW residents are expanding their range northward as winter conditions moderate:

- **Anna's Hummingbird** -- formerly a California species, has expanded into western WA and BC since the 1960s. Now a year-round resident as far north as southern BC, sustained by winter-blooming plants and hummingbird feeders. This range expansion is one of the most dramatic climate-driven changes in the PNW avifauna [O1, O2].
- **Red-shouldered Hawk** -- expanding northward from California/southern OR into the Willamette Valley and Puget Sound [O2].
- **White-tailed Kite** -- formerly absent from the PNW, now a rare but increasing breeder in western OR [O2].

### Changes in Migratory Strategy

Climate change may shift species from migratory to resident or vice versa:

- PNW American Robin populations appear to be increasingly resident in areas where mild winters reduce snow cover and maintain berry availability [O2].
- European Starling winter roost sizes in western WA have increased, suggesting that more individuals are overwintering rather than migrating south [O2].
- Conversely, alpine-breeding species facing shrinking habitat may be forced to migrate further to find suitable wintering habitat as mid-elevation forests encroach on their breeding zones [P17].

## 6.4 The Rufous Hummingbird: Extreme Migration in Miniature

The Rufous Hummingbird (*Selaschia rufus*) performs the longest migration relative to body size of any bird species -- traveling up to 6,000 km one way between PNW breeding grounds and Mexican wintering areas, at a body mass of 3-4 grams. This migration distance/body mass ratio exceeds even the Arctic Tern when normalized for body size.

### Migratory Route and Strategy

Rufous Hummingbirds follow an elliptical migration route through western North America:

- **Northbound (spring):** Follows the Pacific Coast and lower-elevation inland valleys from Mexico through California, Oregon, and Washington, arriving at PNW breeding sites March-May. Tracks the sequential flowering of nectar plants -- manzanita, currant, salmonberry -- using a "nectar corridor" strategy.
- **Southbound (fall):** Shifts eastward through the Cascade and Rocky Mountain cordillera, following the bloom of high-elevation wildflowers (Indian paintbrush, columbine, penstemon) through alpine meadows at 2,000-3,500 m elevation. This route takes advantage of mid-to-late summer alpine flower peaks.
- **Energy strategy:** Gains 25-40% body mass as fat reserves before each migratory flight leg. Metabolic rate during hovering flight (~200 wing beats per second) is the highest mass-specific metabolic rate of any vertebrate. Enters torpor nightly during migration to conserve energy, reducing body temperature from ~40C to ~18C and metabolic rate by ~95% [O1, P5].

### Evolutionary Origins

Molecular phylogenetics places Rufous Hummingbird within the "bee hummingbird" clade (Mellisugini), which radiated in North America during the Pliocene. The genus *Selasphorus* (Rufous, Allen's, Calliope, Broad-tailed hummingbirds) diversified during the late Pliocene and Pleistocene, likely in response to the development of montane meadow habitats and their associated wildflower communities. The extreme migratory behavior of the Rufous Hummingbird may have evolved from shorter-distance elevational migration as the species expanded its breeding range northward into the PNW during post-glacial warming [P5].

### Population Decline

Rufous Hummingbird shows one of the steepest population declines of any PNW breeding species (BBS: -2.0% per year since 1966, cumulative decline >60%). The causes are multifactorial: loss of breeding habitat, reduced wildflower abundance on migration routes, climate-driven phenological mismatches between hummingbird arrival and flower bloom, and competition with the northward-expanding Anna's Hummingbird for winter and early-spring resources. The species is now classified as Near Threatened by IUCN [G4, O4, O1].

# PART VII: SYNTHESIS -- THE PNW AS AN EVOLUTIONARY CROSSROADS

## 7.1 Why the PNW Is Evolutionarily Exceptional

The Pacific Northwest hosts a disproportionate share of North America's avian evolutionary complexity relative to its geographic area. This is not accidental. Six geographic and climatic features converge in the PNW to create conditions that promote speciation, adaptive radiation, and hybridization:

### 1. Topographic Complexity

The PNW contains more topographic relief per unit area than any comparably sized region in temperate North America. The combination of coastal lowlands, a major north-south mountain range (Cascades), interior basins, and isolated volcanic peaks creates:

- Steep environmental gradients (temperature, precipitation, vegetation) over short distances
- Barriers to gene flow (the Cascades, major river valleys)
- Corridors for gene flow (the Columbia River Gorge, mountain passes)
- Isolated habitat patches (alpine sky islands, interior steppe fragments, oceanic islands)

This topographic mosaic fragments populations, promotes local adaptation, and creates the geographic setting for vicariance, cline formation, and secondary contact -- the fundamental processes of speciation.

### 2. The Cascade Rainshadow

The single most important biogeographic feature in the PNW is the Cascade rainshadow, which creates a precipitation gradient from >3,000 mm/year (west slope) to <250 mm/year (east slope) over a horizontal distance of 50-100 km. This generates two fundamentally different ecosystems -- wet temperate forest and dry continental steppe -- within a single regional landscape. The rainshadow drives east-west subspecies divergence in Song Sparrow, Fox Sparrow, Dark-eyed Junco, Red-breasted/Red-naped Sapsucker, and Sooty/Dusky Grouse, among others.

### 3. Pleistocene Glacial Cycling

Repeated glacial advances and retreats over 2.6 million years created a "speciation pump" -- fragmenting populations into refugia during glacial periods, allowing genetic divergence, then bringing them into secondary contact during interglacials. The PNW's multiple refugia (coastal, Klamath, interior steppe, Rocky Mountain, Beringian) each contributed distinct genetic lineages to the post-glacial avifauna, resulting in the complex phylogeographic patterns visible today.

### 4. Conifer Diversity

The PNW hosts the highest diversity of conifer species in the temperate zone, with ~25 native species spanning elevation and moisture gradients. This conifer diversity provides the ecological substrate for Red Crossbill call type diversification -- arguably the most active speciation process in any North American bird lineage.

### 5. Pacific Flyway Position

The PNW sits at the midpoint of the Pacific Flyway, the major migratory corridor along the Pacific Coast. This position makes the region a bottleneck for migratory species (concentrating populations at key staging areas like the Skagit Delta, Klamath Basin, and Malheur NWR) and a zone of contact between northern and southern migratory populations.

### 6. Oceanic Islands

The PNW's island systems (Haida Gwaii, Vancouver Island, San Juan Islands, and smaller coastal islands) provide natural laboratories for insular evolution, contributing endemic subspecies and genetically distinctive populations to the regional avifauna.

## 7.2 Active Evolutionary Processes in the PNW

Unlike most evolutionary biology, which reconstructs past events from present-day patterns, several PNW avian systems allow direct observation of evolution in progress:

| Process | System | Observable evidence |
|---------|--------|---------------------|
| Ecological speciation | Red Crossbill call types | Bill morphology, vocal divergence, assortative mating, reproductive isolation |
| Asymmetric introgression | Townsend's x Hermit Warbler | Southward hybrid zone movement, behavioral dominance |
| Stable hybrid zone | Northern Flicker Red x Yellow-shafted | Maintained phenotypic divergence despite gene flow |
| Hybrid swarm | Glaucous-winged x Western Gull | Blending of parental phenotypes, breakdown of species boundaries |
| Cultural evolution | White-crowned Sparrow song dialects | Geographic song variation, dialect turnover, cultural transmission |
| Insular differentiation | Haida Gwaii endemics | Melanistic forms, genetic distinctiveness |
| Climate-driven range expansion | Anna's Hummingbird | Northward expansion tracking warming winters |
| Ecotype formation | Song Sparrow melanin clines | Continuous morphological variation along humidity gradient |

These systems make the PNW one of the most important regions in North America for evolutionary biology research. The diversity of evolutionary processes operating simultaneously -- speciation, hybridization, introgression, cultural evolution, adaptation -- provides a natural laboratory unmatched in the temperate zone.

## 7.3 Conservation Implications of Evolutionary Biology

Understanding evolutionary biology is not merely an academic exercise -- it has direct conservation implications for PNW bird management:

### Subspecies and ESA Protection

Under the Endangered Species Act, "distinct population segments" can receive independent protection. Several PNW avian taxa whose conservation status depends on taxonomic resolution include:

| Taxon | Current status | If split | Conservation change |
|-------|---------------|----------|---------------------|
| Northern Spotted Owl (vs. Barred Owl) | ESA Threatened | Already separate species | Barred Owl management as invasive |
| Cassia Crossbill (Type 9) | ESA Candidate | Already split (2017) | Focused conservation on South Hills, ID |
| Streaked Horned Lark (*strigata*) | ESA Threatened (DPS) | Already distinct population segment | Focused prairie habitat conservation |
| Sooty Fox Sparrow group | Not listed (part of widespread Fox Sparrow) | If Fox Sparrow split into 2-4 species | Sooty Fox Sparrow becomes regional near-endemic; population monitoring warranted |
| Red Crossbill Type 10 (Sitka spruce) | Not assessed (part of Red Crossbill) | If types split as species | Small population, coastal habitat dependence |

### Hybrid Zones and Species Boundaries

Hybrid zones create management ambiguity. If two taxa interbreed freely and produce fertile offspring, are they separate species warranting separate conservation attention? The answer affects resource allocation:

- The Townsend's x Hermit Warbler hybrid zone: if Hermit Warbler is genetically swamped in the PNW, does that constitute a conservation loss? Under current taxonomy, both species are secure. But the evolutionary lineage represented by PNW Hermit Warbler populations would be absorbed.
- The Glaucous-winged x Western Gull hybrid swarm: management of the Puget Sound "hybrid gull" is complicated by the impossibility of assigning most individuals to species. Conservation of the breeding gull population requires managing a hybrid swarm rather than a discrete species.

### Evolutionary Potential and Climate Adaptation

Species with high genetic diversity and broad environmental tolerances are better positioned to adapt to climate change than genetically depauperate specialists. PNW species with high adaptive capacity include:

- **Song Sparrow** -- extreme geographic variation demonstrating capacity for local adaptation
- **Dark-eyed Junco** -- broad environmental tolerance across elevation and habitat gradients
- **American Robin** -- dietary flexibility and partial migration enabling rapid response to changing conditions

Species with low adaptive capacity -- small populations, narrow niches, limited genetic diversity -- face the greatest climate risk:

- **White-tailed Ptarmigan** -- alpine-obligate with no upward retreat option
- **Cassia Crossbill** -- sedentary population of ~5,800 in a 70 km2 range
- **Streaked Horned Lark** (*strigata*) -- remnant prairie population with limited suitable habitat

## 7.4 Unresolved Questions

PNW avian evolutionary biology remains an active field of research with several major open questions:

1. **How many species are in the Red Crossbill complex?** The call type system suggests 10+ ecologically distinct forms, but the genetic divergence between types is shallow. As genomic tools improve, the resolution of crossbill taxonomy will clarify whether the PNW hosts one polymorphic species or a cryptic species flock.

2. **Will the Townsend's x Hermit Warbler hybrid zone reach a new equilibrium, or will Hermit Warbler be displaced from the PNW?** Long-term monitoring of the hybrid zone position is needed to distinguish between a moving contact zone (prediction: continued southward displacement) and a zone approaching equilibrium (prediction: deceleration of movement).

3. **What is the taxonomic status of the Fox Sparrow complex?** Molecular data support 2-4 species within the current *Passerella iliaca*. An AOS split would elevate the Sooty Fox Sparrow to a PNW near-endemic species, with implications for regional conservation priority.

4. **How will alpine sky island populations respond to climate change?** White-tailed Ptarmigan, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, and alpine-breeding pipits on PNW sky islands face shrinking habitat as treeline advances. Population monitoring programs in Mt. Rainier National Park, North Cascades, and the Olympic Mountains will be critical baselines.

5. **Are Song Sparrow melanin clines shifting in response to climate change?** If precipitation patterns change (drier west side, wetter east side), the Gloger's Rule predictions suggest that Song Sparrow plumage darkness could shift, providing a real-time test of adaptive phenotypic change in a wild vertebrate population.

6. **What role does cultural evolution play in avian speciation?** The White-crowned Sparrow dialect system and the Red Crossbill call type system both involve culturally transmitted vocalizations that may contribute to reproductive isolation. Whether cultural evolution can drive speciation in birds -- independently of genetic divergence -- is one of the most exciting questions in evolutionary biology.
