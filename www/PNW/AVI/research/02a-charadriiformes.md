# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 2A: Charadriiformes

> **Module 2A deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> Migratory shorebirds, gulls, terns, and alcids of the Pacific Northwest. Order Charadriiformes comprises the most migration-dependent avian assemblage in the PNW, with species undertaking hemispheric journeys from Arctic breeding grounds to South American wintering areas and back, funneled through the Pacific Flyway's estuarine staging areas. This document covers 65+ species: ~8 Charadriidae (plovers), ~25 Scolopacidae (sandpipers, curlews, godwits, phalaropes), ~1 Haematopodidae (oystercatcher), ~2 Recurvirostridae (avocet, stilt), ~18 Laridae (gulls, terns), ~12 Alcidae (alcids), and ~15 vagrant/accidental species.
>
> **Design principle:** Shorebird biology is inseparable from tidal cycles, estuarine productivity, and flyway connectivity. Every species profile includes migration fields (flyway, route, timing, staging areas, breeding/wintering grounds, distance). Extended profiles are provided for species of special PNW significance — ESA-listed taxa, keystone staging species, and declining populations.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations. Only Level 1 (publicly available) cultural content.

## Pacific Flyway Overview

The Pacific Flyway is the westernmost of four administrative flyways recognized by the USFWS for migratory bird management in North America [G2]. For Charadriiformes, the PNW functions as a critical mid-latitude bottleneck where the flyway narrows between the Pacific coast and the Cascade/Coast Range barrier, concentrating millions of shorebirds into a small number of estuarine staging areas.

### Flyway Geography Through the PNW

The Pacific Flyway through the PNW study area follows two primary corridors:

**Coastal corridor:** Arctic Alaska and Siberian breeding grounds → coastal British Columbia → outer Washington coast (Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay) → Oregon coast → California. This is the primary route for the majority of Western Sandpipers, Dunlin, Black-bellied Plovers, Short-billed Dowitchers, and all alcid species. The narrow band between the ocean and the Coast Range/Cascades compresses enormous numbers into estuarine staging areas, creating some of the highest shorebird densities on Earth during spring migration (late April–early May) [O18, G2].

**Interior corridor:** Arctic breeding grounds → interior British Columbia → eastern Washington/Oregon (Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin) → Great Basin → inland California. This route is used by Long-billed Curlew, Wilson's Phalarope, American Avocet, Long-billed Dowitcher, and many inland-breeding shorebirds. The Klamath Basin and Malheur NWR are keystone staging areas on this interior route [G3, G12].

Grays Harbor is the single most important shorebird staging area in the PNW and one of the four most important on the entire Pacific coast of the Americas. During peak spring migration (typically the last week of April and first week of May), the estuary's 35,000 acres of mudflats support 500,000 to 1,000,000 shorebirds simultaneously — primarily Western Sandpipers and Dunlin refueling on biofilm, amphipods, and polychaete worms before continuing north to Arctic breeding grounds. The site was designated as a WHSRN site of Hemispheric Importance in 1988 and is managed cooperatively by USFWS (Grays Harbor NWR), WDFW, and the Quinault Indian Nation [O18, G1, P23].

## Family Charadriidae — Plovers

Plovers are compact, large-eyed, short-billed shorebirds that forage using a distinctive run-stop-peck strategy rather than the continuous probing of sandpipers. Seven species occur regularly in the PNW, with one additional rare visitor.

### Western Snowy Plover — *Charadrius nivosus* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Breeding resident / partial migrant
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: **Threatened** (Pacific coast DPS, listed 1993)
- IUCN: NT (Near Threatened)
- State WA: Endangered
- State OR: Threatened
- PIF score: 17 (High concern)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 15–17 cm
- Wingspan: 34–37 cm
- Mass: 34–58 g (mean ~42 g)
- Plumage: Pale sandy-brown upperparts, white underparts, incomplete dark breast band (distinctive vs. Semipalmated Plover's complete band), dark ear patch, pale legs. Non-breeding plumage paler still. Sexes similar; males slightly darker crown/ear patches in breeding.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; males average slightly darker markings.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open sandy beaches above high tide line, dune systems, sand spits, river gravel bars, occasionally alkaline lake shores. Requires dry, sparsely vegetated sand substrates. In PNW, primarily outer coast beaches (WA, OR).
- Wintering: Similar habitats; some move south to California; others remain on PNW coast year-round.
- Elevation range: Sea level (0–10 ft)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)
- Microhabitat: Nests in shallow scrapes in dry sand, often near driftwood or dried kelp wrack. Extreme camouflage — eggs nearly invisible against sand substrate.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine) — year-round — rare — indicator species (beach ecosystem health)

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small invertebrates — flies, beetles, sand hoppers (amphipods), marine worms, brine flies
- Foraging strategy: Visual run-stop-peck on dry sand and wrack line; rarely probes
- Seasonal variation: Diet broadly consistent year-round; exploits tidally exposed invertebrates

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape in open sand, unlined or minimally lined with shell fragments
- Nest location: Above high tide line on open beach, sand spit, or dune
- Clutch size: 2–3 eggs (typically 3)
- Incubation: 26–31 days, shared by both sexes (male takes larger share)
- Fledging: 27–31 days, precocial (chicks leave nest within hours, self-feeding)
- Broods per year: 1–3 (replacement clutches after nest failure; females may desert first brood to male and re-nest with new mate — sequential polyandry)
- Breeding season: March–September in PNW

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** overall; localized increases where management implemented
- Estimate: ~2,300 breeding adults on Pacific coast (USFWS 2019 5-year review); WA population ~70–100 breeding adults at Leadbetter Point, Midway Beach, and Washaway Beach; OR population ~300–500 breeding adults at 10–15 sites
- Threats: Predation (crows, ravens, foxes, feral cats), human recreation disturbance, vehicle driving on beaches, invasive European beachgrass (*Ammophila arenaria*) encroaching on nesting habitat, sea-level rise reducing available beach, nest flooding from storm surge
- Source: USFWS 5-year review [G1], WDFW species profile [G9], ODFW Conservation Strategy [G12]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: Partial migrant; WA/OR breeders may winter locally or shift south along coast to CA
- Spring arrival: Resident breeders on territory by March; some migrants arrive April
- Fall departure: Most remain through October; some winter on-site
- Staging areas: Not a staging species — direct coastal movements
- Breeding grounds: PNW outer coast beaches (WA, OR)
- Wintering grounds: Central/southern CA coast, some remaining in PNW
- Distance: 0–2,000 km (partial migrant)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Soft, plaintive "tu-wheet" or "kru-wee" given in flight display
- Calls: Low "tew" contact call; sharp "pip-pip" alarm near nest/chicks

**Evolutionary note:** The Snowy Plover was split from the Old World Kentish Plover (*C. alexandrinus*) by AOS in 2011 based on genetic divergence (~2.4% cytochrome-b), plumage differences, and vocal distinctions. The Pacific coast population (DPS) is listed separately under ESA, reflecting its isolation and distinct threats compared to interior western populations. Phylogeographic analysis suggests Pacific coast birds diverged during Pleistocene glacial cycles when coastal habitat was fragmented [P20, O3].

**Conservation management:** USFWS critical habitat designation includes 28 units along the Pacific coast. Active management at WA sites includes predator exclosures around nest sites, seasonal beach closures, removal of European beachgrass, and monitoring by WDFW and volunteer programs. The WA population has shown modest recovery (from ~30 breeding adults in the early 2000s to ~70–100 by 2020) in response to intensive management, but remains extremely vulnerable. Oregon populations are managed by ODFW and USFWS at sites including Coos Bay North Spit, Bandon, Bayocean Spit, and Nehalem Spit [G1, G9, G12, P34].

**Cross-links:** Module 3 Zone 6 beach community; Module 5 coastal conservation threats

**Sources:** [G1], [G9], [G12], [O3], [P20], [P34]

### Semipalmated Plover — *Charadrius semipalmatus*

**Status:** Transient / rare wintering
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 17–19 cm | Wingspan: 37–41 cm | Mass: 22–63 g (mean ~47 g during spring staging)
- Plumage: Brown upperparts, white underparts, single complete dark breast band (diagnostic vs. Snowy Plover's incomplete band), orange-based bill with dark tip, orange legs. Breeding plumage brighter; non-breeding duller with brown breast band.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Arctic and subarctic tundra, gravel lake shores — does not breed in PNW
- Migration/winter: Mudflats, estuarine shores, sandy beaches, river gravel bars
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Estuarine), Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — migration — common — characteristic
- Zone 6 — migration/rare winter — common (migration), uncommon (winter) — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:** Marine worms, crustaceans, insects; run-stop-peck on wet mud and sand

**Reproduction:** Does not breed in PNW. Breeds on Arctic tundra.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: ~150,000 globally (P27)
- Threats: Staging area degradation, Arctic habitat disturbance
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: Arctic Alaska/Canada → coastal BC → WA/OR estuaries → CA/Central America/South America
- Spring arrival: Late April–May
- Fall departure: August–October (adults July–August, juveniles August–October)
- Staging areas: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Boundary Bay, Fraser River Delta
- Breeding grounds: Arctic Alaska, northern Canada
- Wintering grounds: Southern US coast, Central America, northern South America
- Distance: 5,000–12,000 km

**Vocalizations:** Clear, rising "chee-wee" flight call; "tew-ip" alarm

**Evolutionary note:** Semipalmated Plover and Common Ringed Plover (*C. hiaticula*) are sister species that diverged during Pleistocene glacial isolation, with the Semipalmated occupying the New World and the Ringed the Old World. Genetic divergence is shallow (~1.2% cytochrome-b), and occasional hybrids are reported in western Alaska where ranges overlap [O1, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [P27], [G2]

### Killdeer — *Charadrius vociferus*

**Status:** Breeding resident / partial migrant
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 23–27 cm | Wingspan: 46–48 cm | Mass: 72–121 g
- Plumage: Brown upperparts, white underparts, two complete black breast bands (diagnostic — only PNW plover with two bands), rufous-orange rump and uppertail coverts (conspicuous in flight), red orbital ring. Sexes similar.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Open ground — agricultural fields, gravel lots, rooftops, golf courses, river gravel bars, airports. Remarkably tolerant of human-modified landscapes.
- Wintering: Similar open habitats; some shift to lower elevations and milder coastal areas
- Elevation range: Sea level–5,500 ft (occasionally higher)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zones 4, 7

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 3 — year-round — common — characteristic
- Zone 5 — year-round — common — characteristic
- Zone 4 — breeding — fairly common — characteristic
- Zone 7 — breeding — fairly common — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:** Insects (beetles, grasshoppers, flies), earthworms, small crustaceans; run-stop-peck on open ground

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape, often on gravel, lined with pebbles/debris
- Clutch size: 3–5 (usually 4)
- Incubation: 24–28 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 25 days, precocial
- Broods per year: 1–2
- Breeding season: March–July in PNW
- Notable behavior: Famous broken-wing distraction display when predator approaches nest

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to slightly declining
- Estimate: ~1,000,000 globally; widespread in PNW
- Threats: Pesticide exposure on agricultural lands, vehicle traffic at gravel-lot nest sites
- Source: PIF [O4], USGS BBS [G4]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: Partial migrant; high-elevation and interior breeders move to lowland/coastal areas; some coastal birds resident year-round
- Spring arrival: February–March (early arrivals; some never left)
- Fall departure: October–November (partial)
- Staging areas: Not a staging species
- Breeding grounds: Throughout PNW at all elevations
- Wintering grounds: PNW lowlands, south to Mexico
- Distance: 0–3,000 km

**Vocalizations:** Loud, insistent "kill-dee, kill-dee, kill-dee" — one of the most recognizable bird calls in North America. Also a rising "dee-dee-dee" alarm trill.

**Evolutionary note:** Killdeer is the largest Charadrius plover in North America and the only one regularly breeding in interior agricultural landscapes. Its tolerance of human-modified habitats is exceptional among plovers and likely reflects pre-adaptation to riverine gravel bar habitats that functionally resemble parking lots and rooftops [O1].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [G4]

### Black-bellied Plover — *Pluvialis squatarola*

**Status:** Transient / wintering
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 27–30 cm | Wingspan: 56–63 cm | Mass: 170–280 g (heaviest PNW plover)
- Plumage: Breeding — striking black face, breast, and belly with white-speckled upperparts. Non-breeding — grayish overall, pale belly. Black axillaries (armpit feathers) diagnostic in flight in all plumages.

**Habitat:** Coastal mudflats, estuarine shores, sandy beaches. Rarely inland in PNW.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine), Zone 5 (Estuarine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — wintering/migration — common — characteristic
- Zone 5 — migration — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Polychaete worms, bivalves, insects; run-stop-peck on mudflats; territorial on winter feeding grounds

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable globally; PNW wintering numbers stable
- Estimate: ~200,000 in Americas [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: High Arctic breeding → coastal staging/wintering in PNW
- Spring arrival: Present year-round on coast (wintering birds); spring migrants peak April–May
- Fall departure: Wintering birds arrive September–October; some non-breeding immatures oversummer
- Staging areas: Grays Harbor, Boundary Bay, Fraser River Delta, Skagit Bay
- Breeding grounds: High Arctic tundra (Alaska North Slope, Arctic Canada, Siberia)
- Wintering grounds: PNW coast (stays through winter), south to South America
- Distance: 5,000–15,000 km

**Vocalizations:** Plaintive, far-carrying "plee-oo-ee" — a melancholy three-note whistle, one of the most distinctive shorebird calls

**Evolutionary note:** Black-bellied Plover is the only New World member of genus *Pluvialis* that retains the ancestral hind toe (hallux) — the other *Pluvialis* plovers (American and Pacific Golden-Plovers) lack it. This vestigial toe is a phylogenetic marker linking *Pluvialis* to the broader Charadriidae lineage. The species breeds across the Holarctic, making it one of the most widely distributed shorebirds on Earth [O1, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [P27]

### American Golden-Plover — *Pluvialis dominica*

**Status:** Transient (uncommon)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 24–28 cm | Wingspan: 59–67 cm | Mass: 120–200 g
- Plumage: Breeding — black face and underparts with gold-spangled upperparts. Non-breeding — brownish-gray with golden wash. Distinguished from Pacific Golden-Plover by longer primary projection, grayer underwing, longer legs, and proportionally smaller bill.

**Habitat:** Mudflats, short-grass fields, agricultural lands during migration. Not coastal-dependent like Black-bellied Plover.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Estuarine), Zone 7 (Steppe)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — migration — uncommon — visitor
- Zone 7 — migration — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Insects, berries on tundra; earthworms and insects during migration; run-stop-peck in short grass and mudflats

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: ~200,000 [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (minority — most use Central/Atlantic flyways)
- Route: Arctic Alaska/Canada → elliptical migration; spring through interior, fall primarily Atlantic/Gulf. PNW records mostly fall (rare) and spring (very rare)
- Spring arrival: Rare in PNW spring; April–May when present
- Fall departure: August–October
- Staging areas: Rare at PNW staging areas; occasional at Grays Harbor, Malheur
- Breeding grounds: Arctic tundra, Alaska and northern Canada
- Wintering grounds: South American pampas (Argentina, Uruguay, Brazil)
- Distance: 12,000–20,000 km (one of the longest migrations of any shorebird)

**Evolutionary note:** American and Pacific Golden-Plovers were considered conspecific ("Lesser Golden-Plover") until 1993, when AOS split them based on vocal differences, morphology, and genetic divergence. The two species breed sympatrically in western Alaska but maintain reproductive isolation through call differences and habitat partitioning. The American species migrates primarily through the interior/Atlantic, while Pacific birds follow the Pacific coast — a classic case of migratory divide [O1, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [P27]

### Pacific Golden-Plover — *Pluvialis fulva*

**Status:** Transient (uncommon but regular)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 23–26 cm | Wingspan: 56–62 cm | Mass: 110–180 g
- Plumage: Very similar to American Golden-Plover. Breeding — black underparts with broader white stripe along flanks (reaching undertail coverts), gold-spangled upperparts. Shorter primary projection than American; slightly yellower overall. Leg length shorter.

**Habitat:** Coastal mudflats, short-grass fields, golf courses, turf lawns, estuarine margins during migration and winter. More coastal-oriented than American Golden-Plover.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine), Zone 5 (Estuarine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — migration/rare winter — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Insects, crustaceans, marine invertebrates on mudflats and turf; run-stop-peck

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: ~100,000 [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (primary flyway for this species)
- Route: Western Alaska/Siberian breeding → Pacific coast of North America (small numbers), majority to Pacific islands, Australia, SE Asia
- Spring arrival: April–May (uncommon but regular on outer WA/OR coast)
- Fall departure: August–November
- Staging areas: Occasional at Boundary Bay, Grays Harbor, coastal OR
- Breeding grounds: Western Alaska tundra, Siberia
- Wintering grounds: Pacific islands (Hawaii common), Australia, SE Asia; small numbers winter on Pacific coast
- Distance: 4,000–15,000 km (Pacific island route can exceed 4,000 km nonstop over open ocean)

**Evolutionary note:** Pacific Golden-Plover undertakes one of the most remarkable overwater migrations of any shorebird — nonstop trans-Pacific flights of 4,000+ km from Alaska to Hawaii, documented by satellite telemetry. This feat requires pre-migratory fat deposition increasing body mass by 50–70% and sustained flight at altitudes of 1,500–6,000 m for 50+ hours [O1].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [P27]

### Mountain Plover — *Charadrius montanus*

**Status:** Vagrant / accidental
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing) | IUCN: NT

**Occurrence notes:** Very rare in PNW. A handful of records from eastern Oregon (Malheur area, Lake County) and eastern Washington. Most PNW records are fall migrants or wintering birds in agricultural fields and grasslands. Nearest regular breeding range is Wyoming/Montana shortgrass prairie.

**Records:** Fewer than 20 accepted records in WA and OR combined [G10, G12]

**Nearest regular range:** Great Plains shortgrass prairie from Montana to Texas; winter in California Central Valley and southwestern deserts

## Family Scolopacidae — Sandpipers, Curlews, Godwits, Phalaropes

The largest shorebird family in the PNW, Scolopacidae includes sandpipers, curlews, godwits, dowitchers, snipes, yellowlegs, tattlers, turnstones, surfbirds, and phalaropes. Most PNW scolopacids are long-distance migrants using the Pacific Flyway, with the order's center of breeding diversity in the Arctic and subarctic.

### Western Sandpiper — *Calidris mauri* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Transient / wintering
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Moderate concern (declining trend)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 14–17 cm
- Wingspan: 25–29 cm
- Mass: 19–35 g (pre-migratory mass at Grays Harbor averages 28–32 g in spring; departure mass after staging can reach 35 g)
- Plumage: Breeding — rufous crown and scapulars, streaked breast with chevron markings on flanks, fine-tipped slightly decurved black bill (longer and more decurved than Semipalmated Sandpiper — important field mark). Non-breeding — gray above, white below, plain-faced. Sexes similar; females average slightly longer-billed.
- Sexual dimorphism: Female bill averages 2 mm longer (24–27 mm vs. 21–25 mm in males); this translates to different prey-depth partitioning on mudflats.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Coastal tundra of western Alaska (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta, Seward Peninsula, North Slope)
- Migration staging: Estuarine mudflats — Grays Harbor is the single most important spring staging area in the lower 48
- Wintering: Coastal mudflats from WA south through Central America to Peru
- Elevation range: Sea level
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Estuarine), Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)
- Microhabitat: Intertidal mudflats with biofilm (diatom-rich organic layer); forages preferentially on mudflats with high biofilm density rather than bare mud

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — migration/wintering — abundant (migration), common (winter) — characteristic
- Zone 6 — migration/wintering — abundant (migration), common (winter) — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Biofilm (diatoms and associated organic material), amphipods, polychaete worms, bivalve siphons, insects (during breeding)
- Foraging strategy: Rapid probing in soft mud (10–15 probes per second in "sewing machine" style); tongue-tip water transport for biofilm feeding. Bill morphology allows extraction of prey from top 2 cm of substrate.
- Seasonal variation: Biofilm is critical fuel during spring staging at Grays Harbor; switches to arthropod prey on Arctic breeding grounds; winter diet includes more amphipods and polychaetes

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape in tundra, lined with lichen, leaves, grass
- Clutch size: 4 eggs
- Incubation: 20–22 days, both sexes (male takes larger share after female departs)
- Fledging: 17–21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: June–July on western Alaska tundra

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** — estimated 57% decline since 1970s based on staging area counts
- Estimate: 3,500,000 globally (Andres et al. 2012); some estimates suggest current population may be closer to 2,000,000–2,500,000 based on declining trend data from Grays Harbor and other monitoring sites
- Threats: Staging area degradation (mudflat erosion, pollution), sea-level rise affecting estuarine habitats, Arctic breeding habitat change, biofilm reduction from water quality changes, climate-driven phenological mismatch (insect prey emergence vs. chick hatch timing)
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27], USFWS [G1], Wilson 1994 [P24], Fernández et al. 2003 [P22]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (nearly entire population uses Pacific Flyway)
- Route: Western Alaska → coastal BC → Grays Harbor / Willapa Bay → south along coast. Spring migration is compressed (3–4 weeks), fall is protracted (2+ months). Spring route is strictly coastal; fall route may include some interior movement.
- Spring arrival: Mid-April to mid-May; peak at Grays Harbor last week of April to first week of May
- Fall departure: Adults begin arriving southbound July; juveniles August–October
- Staging areas: **Grays Harbor** (primary — 500,000+ in spring), Willapa Bay, Boundary Bay, Fraser River Delta, Copper River Delta AK (major pre-PNW staging)
- Breeding grounds: Western Alaska coastal tundra (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta primary)
- Wintering grounds: Coastal WA (small numbers) south through CA, Mexico, Central America to Peru
- Distance: 5,000–12,000 km one-way (Alaska to Peru route approaches 12,000 km)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Buzzy trill given on Arctic breeding grounds
- Calls: Thin "jeet" or "cheet" flight call; high-pitched "kreep" in flocks

**Evolutionary note:** Western and Semipalmated Sandpipers (*C. pusilla*) are sister species that diverged relatively recently (~1–2 million years ago). The Western breeds exclusively in western Alaska while the Semipalmated breeds across the Arctic to eastern Canada — a classic Beringian vicariance pattern. Bill morphology divergence (Western's longer, more decurved bill) appears adaptive for different foraging niches: Western exploits deeper-probing mudflat foraging while Semipalmated is more of a surface picker. At Grays Harbor, the sexually dimorphic bill lengths of Western Sandpipers create within-species niche partitioning, with longer-billed females accessing deeper prey than shorter-billed males [O1, P24, P32].

**Grays Harbor significance:** During peak spring staging, Western Sandpipers at Grays Harbor achieve densities of 1,000–5,000 birds per hectare on preferred mudflat areas — among the highest documented avian densities in North America. The estuary's biofilm productivity, driven by diatom blooms in the intertidal zone, provides the critical energy subsidy that fuels the final 3,000–5,000 km of northward migration. Birds arriving at Grays Harbor typically carry 2–4 g of fat reserves and must gain 6–10 g of fat during a 3–5 day stopover to complete the journey to Alaska. This dependency on a single staging area makes the species exceptionally vulnerable to estuarine habitat degradation [O18, P22, P23, P24].

### Dunlin — *Calidris alpina* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Wintering / transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 16–22 cm
- Wingspan: 33–40 cm
- Mass: 38–70 g (winter birds average ~55 g; pre-migratory spring birds 60–70 g)
- Plumage: Breeding — rufous-brown upperparts, black belly patch (diagnostic in breeding plumage), streaked breast, slightly decurved bill (longer and more distinctly drooped at tip than most *Calidris*). Non-breeding — plain gray-brown above, white below with grayish breast wash. Relatively long, decurved bill is key field mark year-round.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; females average slightly longer-billed.
- PNW subspecies: *C. a. pacifica* (breeds western Alaska) is the primary PNW form; occasionally *C. a. arcticola* (breeds northern Alaska/Siberia) and *C. a. hudsonia* (breeds central Arctic Canada) recorded.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Arctic and subarctic wet tundra (does not breed in PNW)
- Wintering: Estuarine mudflats, coastal salt marshes, wet agricultural fields, sewage ponds
- Elevation range: Sea level to ~50 ft (tidal zone)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Estuarine), Zone 6 (Coastal)
- Microhabitat: Preferentially uses mudflats with high infaunal density (polychaetes, amphipods); roosts at high-tide roost sites on salt marsh, dikes, gravel bars

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — wintering/migration — abundant — characteristic
- Zone 6 — wintering/migration — common — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Polychaete worms (especially *Nereis*), amphipods, small bivalves, insect larvae, crustaceans
- Foraging strategy: Continuous rapid probing ("stitching") in soft mud; tactile bill-tip organ detects prey by pressure changes in substrate. Feeds throughout tidal cycle but most intensively during falling and low tides.
- Seasonal variation: Winter diet dominated by polychaetes and amphipods; pre-migratory diet shifts to higher-energy prey (bivalves, large amphipods) to fuel fat deposition

**Reproduction:** Does not breed in PNW. Breeds on western Alaska wet tundra (subspecies *pacifica*).
- Clutch: 4 eggs, ground nest in tundra sedge
- Incubation: 20–22 days
- Breeding season: June–July in Arctic

**Population:**
- Trend: Subspecies *pacifica* — **declining** (estimated 30% decline since 1980s based on Christmas Bird Count and staging area data)
- Estimate: ~550,000 *C. a. pacifica* (Andres et al. 2012); 1,500,000+ all subspecies globally
- Threats: Estuarine habitat loss, sea-level rise, disturbance at roosting sites, pollution (heavy metals in estuarine sediments), climate-driven changes in Arctic breeding habitat
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27], Warnock & Gill 1996 [P25]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: Western Alaska → coastal BC/WA/OR for wintering. Spring migration northward March–May; fall migration southward September–November. Subspecies *pacifica* is relatively short-distance compared to other Dunlin subspecies.
- Spring arrival: Present year-round (wintering); spring departure northward April–May
- Fall departure: Fall arrival of wintering birds October–November
- Staging areas: Boundary Bay (major spring staging — 50,000+), Fraser River Delta, Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Padilla Bay/Skagit Bay (major winter sites)
- Breeding grounds: Western Alaska wet tundra (Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta)
- Wintering grounds: Coastal WA, OR, northern CA; some south to Mexico
- Distance: 4,000–8,000 km

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Buzzy trill on Arctic breeding grounds
- Calls: Rough "kreep" or "trree" in flight; rolling "chrr" in winter flocks

**Evolutionary note:** Dunlin is one of the most polytypic shorebird species on Earth, with 9–11 recognized subspecies circling the Arctic in a classic ring species pattern. PNW birds (*C. a. pacifica*) were described by Vieillot in 1818 and are genetically distinct from Eurasian Dunlin populations. The subspecies complex illustrates ongoing speciation across the Holarctic — birds from opposite ends of the ring (European *alpina* and North American *pacifica/hudsonia*) show substantial genetic divergence while adjacent populations intergrade. This circumpolar subspecies radiation likely began during Pleistocene glacial fragmentations of tundra breeding habitat [O1, P25, O3].

**PNW wintering ecology:** Dunlin is the most abundant wintering shorebird in the PNW, with 100,000–200,000+ present from October through April in the Puget Sound/Salish Sea lowlands, Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, and Skagit/Padilla Bay complex. Winter flocks of 5,000–30,000 perform spectacular synchronized flight displays ("murmurations") when flushed by raptors — primarily Peregrine Falcons and Merlins, which are the Dunlin's principal winter predators. The biomechanical precision of these flock maneuvers (individuals separated by ~1 body length, responding to neighbor movements within 14 milliseconds) is one of the most studied examples of collective animal behavior [O1, O2, P25].

### Long-billed Curlew — *Numenius americanus* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Breeding / transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed (USFWS Birds of Conservation Concern) | IUCN: LC | State WA: Monitored | PIF: 14 (Moderate concern)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 50–65 cm (largest PNW shorebird)
- Wingspan: 62–68 cm
- Mass: 490–950 g (females heavier with longer bills)
- Plumage: Warm cinnamon-brown overall, buffy underparts with fine streaking, extremely long decurved bill (12–22 cm — longest bill of any North American shorebird). Cinnamon underwing linings diagnostic in flight.
- Sexual dimorphism: Pronounced — females average 30–40% longer-billed than males (female 14–22 cm, male 12–17 cm), reflecting different foraging niches (females probe deeper for burrowing prey, males take more surface insects).

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Short-grass prairie, sagebrush steppe, agricultural fields (especially irrigated pasture). In PNW, breeds primarily east of Cascades in eastern OR (Malheur, Harney County, Lake County) and eastern WA (Douglas, Grant, Adams counties).
- Wintering: Coastal mudflats, estuaries, agricultural fields. Small numbers winter on WA/OR coast.
- Elevation range: 500–5,000 ft (breeding); sea level (wintering)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 7 (Steppe/Shrubland)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Estuarine — winter/migration)
- Microhabitat: Requires short vegetation (<15 cm) for nesting with nearby taller vegetation/structures for chick concealment

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 7 — breeding — uncommon — characteristic
- Zone 5 — wintering/migration — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars, earthworms on breeding grounds; marine worms, crabs, shrimp on coastal wintering grounds
- Foraging strategy: Deep-probing with extraordinary bill length to extract burrowing invertebrates; also visual surface-picking for grasshoppers in grasslands
- Seasonal variation: Insectivorous on breeding grounds; switches to marine invertebrates in winter

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Shallow ground scrape in short grass or bare ground
- Clutch size: 3–5 (usually 4)
- Incubation: 27–30 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 32–45 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: April–July in eastern OR/WA

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** — estimated 50% decline since 1960s; grassland conversion is primary driver
- Estimate: ~140,000 globally (Andres et al. 2012); OR supports estimated 3,000–5,000 breeding birds (one of the densest populations remaining); WA breeding population estimated 500–1,000
- Threats: Grassland conversion to cropland, loss of native bunchgrass/sagebrush, irrigation development flooding nest sites, predation by ravens (whose populations have increased with human infrastructure), wind energy development in breeding range
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27], ODFW [G12], WDFW [G9]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (and Central)
- Route: Eastern OR/WA breeding → south through interior to Central Valley CA, coastal CA, Mexico. Some birds winter on PNW coast (Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, coastal OR).
- Spring arrival: March–April (early arrivals to eastern OR/WA breeding sites)
- Fall departure: July–September (females depart first, often leaving males with fledging young)
- Staging areas: Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin, Columbia Basin agricultural lands
- Breeding grounds: Eastern OR (Malheur Basin, Lake County), eastern WA (Columbia Basin), interior BC
- Wintering grounds: CA Central Valley, coastal CA/Mexico; small numbers WA/OR coast
- Distance: 1,500–5,000 km

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Loud, ascending "cur-LEE, cur-LEE" — the call that gives the curlew its name
- Calls: Rolling "cur-lee-ew" flight call; rapid "wi-wi-wi-wi" alarm near nest

**Evolutionary note:** Long-billed Curlew is the sole surviving endemic large curlew of the North American grasslands. The Eskimo Curlew (*N. borealis*), which migrated through the Great Plains, was driven to extinction (or near-extinction — last confirmed sighting 1963) by market hunting in the 19th century. Long-billed Curlew survived partly because its breeding range in the western interior was more remote from market hunting pressure, but it too was nearly eliminated before passage of the Migratory Bird Treaty Act. The species' closest relative is the Eurasian Curlew (*N. arquata*), from which it diverged approximately 4–6 million years ago [O1, O3].

**Cultural note:** Long-billed Curlews were noted in published accounts by early fur traders and explorers in the Columbia Basin, where their calls were a characteristic sound of the sagebrush steppe. The species was historically more widespread, breeding across the Great Plains and Pacific grasslands, but contraction of breeding range since the 19th century has concentrated remaining populations in the intermountain west [O1].

**Cross-links:** Module 3 Zone 7 steppe community; Module 5 grassland conservation threats

**Sources:** [G9], [G12], [O1], [O2], [P27]

- Nest type: Ground nest concealed in dense grass/sedge
- Clutch size: 4
- Incubation: 18–20 days, female only
- Fledging: 19–20 days
- Broods per year: 1–2

- Trend: Stable

- Route: Boreal breeding → PNW uncommon → south (most migrate east of Cascades)
- Spring arrival: April–May
- Fall departure: July–September
- Staging areas: Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin, occasionally coastal estuaries
- Breeding grounds: Boreal forests, Alaska and Canada
- Wintering grounds: Southern US to southern South America
- Distance: 5,000–15,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Lesser Yellowlegs is less common in the PNW than Greater Yellowlegs, reflecting the species' primary migration through central/eastern North America. The two yellowlegs species are not sister taxa — molecular data places Lesser Yellowlegs closer to the Willet (*T. semipalmata*), with Greater Yellowlegs being more basally positioned within *Tringa* [O1, O3].

### Spotted Sandpiper — *Actitis macularius*

**Status:** Breeding / transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 18–20 cm | Wingspan: 37–40 cm | Mass: 34–50 g
- Plumage: Breeding — brown upperparts, white underparts with bold dark spots (unique among PNW sandpipers), orange-based bill. Non-breeding — plain brown above, white below, white eyebrow. Distinctive constant body-bobbing (teeter) motion.

**Habitat:** Margins of streams, rivers, lakes, ponds at all elevations. The most widespread breeding shorebird in the PNW.
- Elevation range: Sea level–7,000 ft
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 5 (Riparian)
- Ecoregion secondary: All zones near water

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — breeding — common — characteristic
- Zone 2 — breeding — fairly common (mountain streams) — visitor
- Zone 3 — breeding — common — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:** Insects, spiders, small crustaceans, small fish; picks from substrate and water surface; distinctive low flight over water with stiff, shallow wingbeats

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground scrape near water, concealed in vegetation
- Clutch size: 3–5 (usually 4)
- Incubation: 20–24 days, **male only** (one of the most studied polyandrous shorebirds)
- Fledging: 15–18 days
- Broods per year: 1–4 (female may produce clutches for multiple males)
- Breeding season: May–August
- Notable behavior: Role-reversed polyandry — females defend territories and mate with multiple males, each of whom incubates and raises a clutch independently. Females are larger and more aggressive.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable to slightly declining
- Estimate: ~660,000 [O4]
- Source: PIF [O4]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (and all flyways — continent-wide species)
- Route: PNW breeders move south to CA, Mexico, Central/South America
- Spring arrival: Late April–May
- Fall departure: August–September
- Staging areas: Not a staging species — solitary migrant along waterways
- Breeding grounds: Throughout PNW near freshwater
- Wintering grounds: Southern US to South America
- Distance: 2,000–10,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Spotted Sandpiper's polyandrous mating system is the best-studied example of sex-role reversal in birds. Females produce testosterone levels 3–7 times higher than males during the breeding season, defend territories, and compete for males. This reversal evolved independently in at least two shorebird lineages (Spotted Sandpiper and phalaropes), suggesting convergent selection pressures related to seasonal breeding opportunity and predation risk [O1, P32].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O4], [P32]

- Zone 6 — wintering/migration — fairly common — characteristic (rocky coast)

### Red Knot — *Calidris canutus*

**Status:** Transient (uncommon, declining)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Threatened (*rufa* subspecies; *roselaari* subspecies not listed but declining) | IUCN: NT | PIF: High concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 23–26 cm | Wingspan: 47–53 cm | Mass: 100–200 g (extreme mass variation — pre-migratory birds can weigh nearly double their lean mass)
- Plumage: Breeding — salmon-red face and underparts with gray-spangled upperparts. Non-breeding — plain gray above, white below. Medium-length straight dark bill.

**Habitat:** Coastal mudflats, sandy beaches during migration. PNW subspecies (*C. c. roselaari*) stages on outer coast.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine), Zone 5 (Estuarine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — migration — uncommon — visitor (declining)

**Diet and foraging:** Bivalves (especially thin-shelled clams), snails, horseshoe crab eggs (Atlantic population); in PNW, small bivalves, marine worms, crustaceans on mudflats

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** — *roselaari* subspecies estimated 30–50% decline
- Estimate: *C. c. roselaari* ~22,000 (declining); all subspecies ~1,100,000 globally [P27]
- Threats: Staging area loss, prey depletion (overharvest of bivalves), climate change on Arctic breeding grounds
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27], Fernández et al. 2003 [P22]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: Wrangel Island/NW Alaska → Pacific coast → Mexico/South America
- Spring arrival: April–May (small numbers at Grays Harbor and outer coast)
- Fall departure: August–October
- Staging areas: Grays Harbor (declining numbers), Boundary Bay, outer coast beaches
- Breeding grounds: High Arctic tundra (Wrangel Island, NW Alaska for *roselaari*)
- Wintering grounds: Pacific Mexico, western South America
- Distance: 10,000–15,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Red Knot is the shorebird most famously associated with extreme long-distance migration — the Atlantic subspecies (*rufa*) undertakes one of the longest migrations of any bird (Arctic to Tierra del Fuego, ~30,000 km round-trip). The Pacific subspecies *roselaari* makes a somewhat shorter journey but faces similar physiological challenges. Red Knots undergo one of the most extreme body composition changes of any bird: pre-migratory birds nearly double their mass through fat deposition while simultaneously hypertrophying flight muscles and shrinking digestive organs — a complete physiological remodeling for flight [O1, P26].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [P22], [P26], [P27]

### Sanderling — *Calidris alba*

**Status:** Transient / wintering
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 18–21 cm | Wingspan: 35–40 cm | Mass: 40–100 g
- Plumage: Non-breeding (most commonly seen in PNW) — very pale gray above, bright white below, black shoulder mark, black legs, short straight black bill. Breeding — rufous head and breast with black-centered back feathers. The palest small sandpiper on PNW beaches.

**Habitat:** Sandy ocean beaches (primary), mudflats. The quintessential wave-chasing shorebird.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — sandy beaches)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — wintering/migration — common — characteristic (sandy beaches)

**Diet and foraging:** Sand crabs (mole crabs, *Emerita*), amphipods, small bivalves, polychaete worms; runs along wave edge, picking prey exposed by retreating waves. The classic run-and-pick behavior at the surf line is diagnostic.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable globally
- Estimate: ~300,000 in Americas [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: High Arctic → coastal migration → Pacific coast wintering
- Spring arrival: Present year-round on coast; spring migrants April–May
- Fall departure: Arriving September–October for winter
- Staging areas: Sandy beaches throughout PNW coast
- Breeding grounds: High Arctic tundra (circumpolar)
- Wintering grounds: Pacific coast from WA to Chile; some year-round on PNW beaches
- Distance: 5,000–15,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Sanderling is unique among *Calidris* sandpipers in lacking a hind toe (hallux) — an adaptation for its running-on-sand lifestyle. This toe loss is a derived condition within the genus, not an ancestral trait, and parallels similar toe reductions in other ground-running birds (plovers, ostriches) [O1, O3].

### Least Sandpiper — *Calidris minutilla*

**Status:** Transient / wintering
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 13–15 cm | Wingspan: 27–30 cm | Mass: 15–32 g (smallest sandpiper in the world)
- Plumage: Brown upperparts, streaked breast, white belly, short thin slightly decurved bill, **yellow-green legs** (diagnostic — the only PNW peep with yellow legs; Western and Semipalmated Sandpipers have black legs).

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 5 — migration/wintering — common — characteristic

- Trend: Stable to declining

- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (and all flyways)
- Route: Subarctic/boreal breeding → PNW coastal/interior wetlands → CA, Mexico, Central America
- Spring arrival: April–May
- Fall departure: Adults July–August; juveniles August–October; some winter in PNW
- Staging areas: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Malheur, coastal estuaries

### Red-necked Phalarope — *Phalaropus lobatus*

**Status:** Transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Moderate concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 18–20 cm | Wingspan: 31–34 cm | Mass: 25–50 g
- Plumage: Breeding female (brighter) — dark gray head and back, rufous neck stripe, white chin, needle-fine black bill. Breeding male — duller version. Non-breeding — gray above, white below with dark ear patch. **Females brighter than males** (reversed sexual dimorphism).
- Sexual dimorphism: Reversed — females are larger and more colorful than males

**Habitat:** Pelagic ocean (winter), coastal and freshwater wetlands (migration). Remarkably pelagic for a shorebird.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — pelagic)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Freshwater wetlands — migration only)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — migration — fairly common (offshore) — visitor
- Zone 5 — migration — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Plankton, copepods, larval fish, insects; spins rapidly in tight circles on water surface to create upwelling vortex that draws prey to surface (diagnostic phalarope spinning behavior). Also picks from water surface.

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining (offshore surveys suggest decreases)
- Estimate: ~2,500,000 [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (and all oceans)
- Route: Arctic breeding → offshore Pacific → South American waters
- Spring arrival: May (briefly at coastal and interior wetlands)
- Fall departure: July–September (often far offshore, so less visible than spring)
- Staging areas: Offshore upwelling zones, occasionally coastal estuaries and inland lakes
- Breeding grounds: Arctic and subarctic tundra pools
- Wintering grounds: Pelagic — tropical eastern Pacific (Humboldt Current zone)
- Distance: 8,000–15,000 km (mostly over open ocean)

**Evolutionary note:** Phalaropes exhibit the most extreme sex-role reversal of any shorebird group. Females compete for males, are larger and more colorful, and abandon eggs immediately after laying. Males alone incubate and raise chicks. All three phalarope species share this system, suggesting it evolved once in the phalarope ancestor. The spinning foraging behavior is also shared by all three species and may be homologous [O1, P32].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [P27], [P32]

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 20–22 cm | Wingspan: 34–38 cm | Mass: 40–75 g
- Plumage: Breeding female — deep rufous-red underparts (entire body), white face patch, thick yellow bill with dark tip. Male duller. Non-breeding — pale gray above, white below, dark eye patch; similar to non-breeding Red-necked but heavier-billed.

- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Pelagic/Marine)

- Zone 6 — migration — uncommon — visitor (primarily far offshore)

**Diet and foraging:** Copepods, larval fish, jellyfish; spinning behavior like Red-necked; picks from water surface

### Wilson's Phalarope — *Phalaropus tricolor*

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 22–24 cm | Wingspan: 37–43 cm | Mass: 40–90 g
- Plumage: Breeding female — gray crown, bold black and chestnut neck stripe continuing down back, white underparts. Male — duller. Non-breeding — very plain gray above, white below. Needle-fine black bill, long pale legs. Largest and most terrestrial phalarope.

**Habitat:** Inland marshes, alkaline lakes, prairie potholes, sewage ponds. The only phalarope that breeds in the PNW.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 7 (Steppe — alkaline lakes, marshes)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 7 — breeding — fairly common — characteristic
- Zone 5 — migration — fairly common — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Brine flies, brine shrimp (on alkaline staging lakes), aquatic insects; spinning behavior and surface-picking

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Ground nest in grass near water
- Clutch size: 3–4
- Incubation: 18–27 days, male only
- Fledging: ~21 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–July in eastern OR/WA
- Sex-role reversal like other phalaropes: females compete for males, abandon clutch

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining
- Estimate: ~1,500,000 [P27]
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific and Central
- Route: Interior western breeding → alkaline lake staging → western South America
- Spring arrival: April–May (eastern OR/WA)
- Fall departure: July–September (adults first)
- Staging areas: **Mono Lake CA**, Great Salt Lake, Klamath Basin, Malheur NWR, Lake Abert OR (major staging on alkaline/saline lakes where brine flies and brine shrimp provide fuel)
- Breeding grounds: Interior western North America (eastern OR/WA, Great Basin, northern prairies)
- Wintering grounds: Andean altiplano lakes, Argentine pampas
- Distance: 8,000–14,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Wilson's Phalarope is the only New World-endemic phalarope and the most terrestrial member of the family. Unlike the two seagoing phalaropes, Wilson's breeds in interior freshwater marshes and stages at inland saline/alkaline lakes. Its migration entirely avoids the ocean. The species' dependence on a small number of alkaline lake staging areas (Mono Lake, Great Salt Lake, Lake Abert) makes it vulnerable to water diversion — the Mono Lake case (1994 court decision setting minimum lake levels) was a landmark in shorebird conservation [O1, P32].

## Family Haematopodidae — Oystercatchers

A monotypic family in the PNW represented by a single resident species. Oystercatchers are large, heavy-bodied shorebirds with laterally compressed, chisel-like bills specialized for prying open bivalves and dislodging limpets from rocky intertidal substrate.

### Black Oystercatcher — *Haematopus bachmani* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Resident (year-round)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (USFWS Birds of Conservation Concern)
- IUCN: LC
- State WA: Monitored (WDFW Priority Species)
- State OR: Sensitive
- PIF score: 15 (High concern — small global population, restricted range)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 42–47 cm
- Wingspan: 79–84 cm
- Mass: 500–700 g
- Plumage: Entirely blackish-brown body plumage (no white — distinguishes from American Oystercatcher *H. palliatus* which has white underparts). Bright red-orange bill (long, laterally compressed, chisel-tipped — 7–8 cm), yellow iris with red orbital ring, pink legs. Sexes similar; females average slightly larger with longer bills.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal plumage differences; females average 5–10% longer-billed (enabling slightly different prey-size selection on shared territories).

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Rocky intertidal shoreline — exposed outer coast, rocky islets, jetties, breakwaters. Requires wave-washed rocky platforms with dense mussel beds and limpet populations within 50 m of nest site.
- Wintering: Same rocky intertidal habitat; essentially non-migratory. Some post-breeding dispersal to nearby rocky shores but no true migration.
- Elevation range: Sea level (intertidal zone exclusively — 0–5 ft tidal range)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — rocky intertidal)
- Microhabitat: Nests on rocky shelves, gravel patches above high tide line on rocky islets, occasionally on flat rooftops near coast. Forages exclusively on wave-washed rocky platforms in the mid-to-low intertidal zone, tracking tidal exposure of mussel beds. Territory centered on specific rock outcrop or reef section; pairs defend 100–500 m of rocky shoreline year-round.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — rocky intertidal) — year-round — uncommon (small population) — **indicator species** (rocky intertidal ecosystem health)

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Mussels (*Mytilus californianus*, *M. trossulus*), limpets (*Lottia* spp.), chitons, barnacles, marine worms, sea urchins, occasionally small crabs
- Foraging strategy: Two primary techniques — (1) **stabbing:** rapid insertion of laterally compressed bill between parted mussel shell valves to sever the adductor muscle, then prying shell open; (2) **hammering:** repeated blows to limpets and barnacles to dislodge from rock surface. Individual birds develop specialization in one technique, which is learned from parents during the extended juvenile dependency period (6+ months).
- Tidal dependency: Foraging is strictly tide-dependent — feeds during falling and low tides when mussel beds and limpet zones are exposed. During high tide, rests on rocky roost sites above wave line. Daily activity budget is governed by tidal cycle rather than daylight.
- Seasonal variation: Diet consistent year-round due to year-round intertidal prey availability; slight increase in limpet consumption during winter when storm exposure limits access to lower-intertidal mussel beds.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Shallow scrape on rock, gravel, or shell fragments on rocky shelf above high tide line; minimal to no lining material. Some pairs use the same nest site for decades.
- Nest location: Rocky islets, exposed headlands, jetty tops — typically within 20 m of high tide line and within 50 m of foraging rocks
- Clutch size: 1–3 eggs (usually 2), olive-buff with dark spots, well-camouflaged against rock/gravel
- Incubation: 24–29 days, both sexes share (male often takes night shifts)
- Fledging: 33–40 days; chicks precocial but remain dependent on parents for 6+ months to learn mussel-opening technique
- Broods per year: 1 (will re-nest if first clutch lost, up to 2 replacement attempts)
- Breeding season: May–August in PNW
- Notable behavior: Extended parental care is unusually long for a shorebird — juveniles cannot efficiently open mussels until 3–6 months post-fledging and remain in parental territory through their first winter. Parents actively teach foraging by demonstrating mussel-opening at feeding sites. This extended dependency limits reproductive output and is a primary constraint on population growth rate.

**Population:**
- Trend: **Stable to slightly declining** in parts of range; localized increases where disturbance is managed
- Estimate: ~8,900–11,000 individuals globally (entire species) — one of the smallest global populations of any regularly occurring PNW shorebird. WA population estimated at 800–1,200 birds (Nysewander et al. 2005); OR population estimated at 400–700 birds; BC population ~2,000–3,000 birds.
- Threats: Human disturbance at nest sites (hiking, kayaking, drone flights near nesting rocks), oil spill vulnerability (entire population concentrated in narrow intertidal strip), predation on eggs/chicks by ravens, crows, gulls, river otters, and mink (whose populations have increased with human food subsidies), sea-level rise reducing available nesting substrate, ocean acidification affecting mussel and limpet prey populations (shell formation impaired in lower-pH water), competition for nest sites from increasing gull populations
- Source: USFWS Birds of Conservation Concern [G1], Nysewander et al. 2005 [P30], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (non-migratory)
- Route: Resident; no true migration. Post-breeding dispersal of juveniles and failed breeders to nearby rocky shores (typically <50 km from natal site)
- Spring arrival: Resident year-round
- Fall departure: Resident year-round
- Staging areas: Not applicable — non-migratory
- Breeding grounds: Rocky intertidal coast from Aleutian Islands to Baja California (entire Pacific coast of North America)
- Wintering grounds: Same as breeding range
- Distance: 0 km (resident; juvenile dispersal typically <50 km)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Loud, piping "wheep-wheep-wheep" display call, often given in flight with exaggerated slow wingbeats during territorial display. Mated pairs perform synchronized piping displays on territory boundaries.
- Calls: Sharp "kleep" alarm; soft "hew" contact call between pair members; rapid "pip-pip-pip-pip" in aggressive territorial encounters

**Evolutionary note:** Black Oystercatcher is a Pacific-endemic species, sister to the American Oystercatcher (*H. palliatus*) of the Atlantic and Gulf coasts. The two species hybridize narrowly in Baja California where their ranges overlap, producing intermediate plumage (dark body with some white on belly). The genus *Haematopus* is ancient within Charadriiformes, and oystercatchers represent one of the earliest-diverging lineages in the order — molecular clock estimates place the *Haematopus* stem lineage at ~35–40 million years old. The laterally compressed bill morphology is unique among shorebirds and represents a specialized evolutionary solution to the biomechanical challenge of opening bivalves — a convergence with the unrelated oyster drills (predatory gastropods) that also exploit mussel beds [O1, O3, P36].

**Indicator species value:** Black Oystercatcher is one of the most sensitive indicators of rocky intertidal ecosystem health in the PNW. Because the species requires (1) intact mussel bed communities for food, (2) undisturbed rocky nesting substrate, (3) clean water free of oil contamination, and (4) low human disturbance at nesting sites, changes in oystercatcher breeding success and territory occupancy provide an integrated signal of multiple environmental stressors. Monitoring programs by WDFW and USFWS track oystercatcher nest success at sentinel sites along the WA and OR coast. The species' small global population (~10,000 individuals), limited habitat, and year-round residency mean that localized threats (a single oil spill, chronic disturbance at key nesting rocks) can have population-level impacts. Ocean acidification is an emerging concern: laboratory studies show that reduced seawater pH impairs shell growth in California mussels (*Mytilus californianus*), the oystercatcher's primary prey — if acidification degrades mussel beds, the trophic consequences for oystercatchers could be severe [G1, G9, P30].

**Cross-links:** Module 3 Zone 6 rocky intertidal community; Module 5 coastal conservation threats; ocean acidification impacts

**Sources:** [G1], [G9], [G12], [O1], [O3], [P27], [P30], [P36]

## Family Recurvirostridae — Stilts and Avocets

Recurvirostridae contains the world's longest-legged shorebirds relative to body size. Two species occur in the PNW, both primarily in interior habitats east of the Cascades.

### American Avocet — *Recurvirostra americana*

**Status:** Breeding / transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 40–51 cm | Wingspan: 68–76 cm | Mass: 275–420 g
- Plumage: Breeding — bold black-and-white wing pattern, rusty-orange head and neck, white body, long blue-gray upturned bill (diagnostic — the only PNW shorebird with a distinctly recurved bill). Non-breeding — gray head and neck replacing orange. Long pale blue-gray legs.
- Sexual dimorphism: Females have more sharply upturned bills than males; this sex-linked bill curvature difference is more pronounced than in any other North American shorebird.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Shallow alkaline lakes, mudflats at interior wetlands, salt pans, flooded agricultural fields. In PNW, breeds east of Cascades — Malheur NWR, Summer Lake, Lake Abert, Klamath Basin, and Columbia Basin irrigation wetlands.
- Wintering: Coastal mudflats, estuaries, salt pans in CA and Mexico. Rare in winter in PNW.
- Elevation range: 1,000–5,000 ft (breeding); sea level (wintering)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 7 (Steppe — alkaline wetlands)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland — migration)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 7 — breeding — fairly common — characteristic (alkaline wetlands)
- Zone 5 — migration — uncommon — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Aquatic invertebrates (brine shrimp, aquatic insects, crustaceans), seeds; sweeps upturned bill side-to-side through shallow water (scything motion), filtering prey from water and mud; also pecks and probes. The upturned bill functions as a specialized tool for surface-tension feeding in very shallow water.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Shallow scrape on bare mudflat or low island, sometimes lined with vegetation fragments
- Clutch size: 3–5 (usually 4)
- Incubation: 22–29 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 28–35 days, precocial
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–July in eastern OR/WA

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: ~450,000 [P27]
- Threats: Water diversion reducing alkaline lake levels (Lake Abert and Summer Lake are vulnerable), wetland drainage, agricultural chemical runoff
- Source: Andres et al. 2012 [P27], ODFW [G12]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific and Central
- Route: Interior western breeding → south to CA coast, Mexico. Some birds winter in San Francisco Bay and southern CA estuaries.
- Spring arrival: April–May (eastern OR/WA)
- Fall departure: August–October
- Staging areas: Klamath Basin, Malheur NWR, Lake Abert, Summer Lake, Great Salt Lake
- Breeding grounds: Interior western North America (eastern OR/WA, Great Basin, northern prairies)
- Wintering grounds: CA coast, Mexico, Gulf coast
- Distance: 2,000–5,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** American Avocet's recurved bill is one of the most distinctive feeding adaptations among shorebirds. The degree of curvature is sexually dimorphic, with females having more sharply curved bills — enabling niche partitioning between the sexes on shared foraging grounds. Bill curvature begins developing at approximately 3 weeks of age. The genus *Recurvirostra* contains four species worldwide, all with recurved bills and all using the scything foraging technique, indicating the bill morphology and feeding behavior co-evolved as a functional unit [O1, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [P27], [G12]

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Shallow scrape on mudflat or low island, sometimes built up as a mound in shallow water
- Clutch size: 3–5 (usually 4)
- Incubation: 22–26 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 28–32 days, precocial
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–July

**Population:**
- Trend: **Increasing** — northward range expansion in PNW documented since 1990s

**Migration:**

- Spring arrival: April–May
- Fall departure: August–September
- Staging areas: Klamath Basin, Malheur NWR, Summer Lake
- Breeding grounds: Interior western North America (expanding northward in PNW)
- Wintering grounds: CA, Mexico, Central America

**Evolutionary note:** Black-necked Stilt is part of a cosmopolitan species complex — the *Himantopus* stilts — that has been variously treated as one to five species by different taxonomic authorities. AOS currently treats the New World form as a full species (*H. mexicanus*), but it is closely related to (and sometimes lumped with) the Black-winged Stilt (*H. himantopus*) of Europe, Africa, and Asia. The species' northward range expansion in the PNW correlates with warming climate trends and may represent recolonization of historically occupied range [O1, O3].

## Family Laridae — Gulls and Terns

Laridae is the most visible seabird family in the PNW — abundant, vocal, ecologically versatile, and occupying habitats from pelagic ocean to urban parking lots. The family is divided into two major ecological groups: gulls (larger, omnivorous, generalist foragers) and terns (slender, specialized plunge-divers). Approximately 15 species occur regularly in the PNW, with additional vagrants.

### Glaucous-winged Gull — *Larus glaucescens* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Resident (year-round)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: LC
- State WA: Not listed (common)
- PIF: Low concern
- Trend note: Declining in core Puget Sound/Salish Sea breeding range despite being abundant overall

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 58–68 cm
- Wingspan: 130–150 cm
- Mass: 730–1,690 g (males heavier)
- Plumage: Adult — white head and underparts, medium-gray mantle and upperwings, wingtips gray with white mirrors (same shade as mantle — diagnostic; other large PNW gulls have darker wingtips than mantle). Pink legs, yellow bill with red gonys spot, dark eye. First-year — uniform grayish-brown overall (lighter and more uniform than first-year Western or Herring). Full adult plumage attained in 4th year.
- Sexual dimorphism: Males average 10–15% larger (mass, bill depth, wing chord)
- **Hybrid plumage:** Glaucous-winged × Western Gull hybrids (see hybrid section below) show intermediate wingtip shade — darker than pure Glaucous-winged but lighter than pure Western, ranging from medium to dark gray. This intergradation makes identification of "pure" individuals extremely difficult in the PNW, where the hybrid zone extends from central Washington through Puget Sound.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Coastal islands, rocky islets, jetties, rooftops, bridge structures. Major colonies on Protection Island (WA), Smith Island (WA), Mandarte Island (BC), Chain Islets (BC).
- Year-round: Coastal waters, harbors, landfills, parking lots, river mouths, estuaries. The most commonly encountered large gull in the PNW from Seattle to Victoria.
- Elevation range: Sea level to ~500 ft (rarely higher)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban), Zone 5 (Estuarine)
- Microhabitat: Generalist — exploits every marine and human-subsidized food source available. Urban adaptation extensive: nests on building rooftops, feeds at landfills, dumpsters, parking lots, and restaurant outdoor seating areas. Marine foraging on tidally exposed invertebrates, fish offal, and scavenged prey.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — year-round — abundant — characteristic (dominant large gull)
- Zone 3 — year-round — common — characteristic (urban areas near coast)
- Zone 5 — year-round — common — characteristic

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Extremely omnivorous — fish, marine invertebrates (mussels, clams, sea urchins, crabs, barnacles), garbage, carrion, bird eggs and chicks (including conspecific eggs), berries, french fries, bread, pet food, dead salmon, terrestrial invertebrates
- Foraging strategy: Opportunistic generalist. Drops hard-shelled prey from height onto rocks or pavement to crack open; kleptoparasitizes other birds; scavenges at landfills and fishing boats; surface-dips for fish; wades in intertidal for invertebrates; attends salmon spawning runs for eggs and carcasses
- Seasonal variation: Salmon spawning (fall) provides seasonal protein pulse; winter diet shifts toward more marine invertebrates and scavenging; summer breeding season includes predation on other seabird eggs and chicks at mixed colonies

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Large cup of grass, seaweed, feathers, and debris on ground, rocky ledge, or rooftop
- Nest location: Colonial breeder — nests in dense colonies on offshore islands, rocky islets, and increasingly on rooftops in urban areas
- Clutch size: 2–3 eggs (usually 3)
- Incubation: 26–29 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 35–54 days
- Broods per year: 1 (will replace if lost early)
- Breeding season: May–August in PNW

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** in core Salish Sea breeding range — Protection Island colony declined ~50% between 1980s and 2000s; Mandarte Island colony showing similar trend. Urban-nesting populations may be stable or increasing while natural-colony populations decline.
- Estimate: ~570,000 globally (all forms including hybrids); WA/BC Salish Sea supports ~50,000–80,000 breeding pairs
- Threats: Reduced forage fish availability (herring, sand lance declines in Puget Sound), oil spill vulnerability, bald eagle predation at colonies (eagle recovery has increased nest predation), urbanization altering food web, contaminant accumulation (PCBs, mercury in Puget Sound food web), competition with increasing crow and raven populations
- Source: Nysewander et al. 2005 [P30], Speich & Wahl 1989 [P29], WDFW [G9], Andres et al. 2012 [P27]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (largely non-migratory)
- Route: Largely resident; some dispersal south along coast to CA in winter. Immature birds wander more widely than adults.
- Spring arrival: Resident year-round
- Fall departure: Resident; some southward dispersal October–March
- Staging areas: Not applicable — resident
- Breeding grounds: Pacific coast from Aleutians to Oregon (core: Salish Sea islands)
- Wintering grounds: Same as breeding range; some dispersal to CA
- Distance: 0–2,000 km (mostly resident)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary song: Classic gull "long call" — "kyow-kyow-kyow-kyow-kee-ah" given with head thrown back during territorial and pair-bonding displays
- Calls: Mewing "kyow"; alarm "ga-ga-ga"; begging call of juveniles — insistent nasal whine

**Evolutionary note:** Glaucous-winged Gull is at the center of one of the most complex hybridization events in avian biology. In the PNW, it hybridizes extensively with Western Gull (*L. occidentalis*) where the two species' ranges overlap from central Washington southward through Oregon. The hybrid zone is wide and stable, producing a continuous phenotypic spectrum from pure Glaucous-winged (gray wingtips matching mantle) to pure Western (very dark wingtips contrasting with mantle). Many experienced ornithologists consider "pure" individuals of either species to be rare in the Washington–Oregon overlap zone, and some researchers have proposed treating the hybrid swarm as a separate taxon. The hybrid complex also involves occasional input from Herring Gull (*L. argentatus*) in areas of three-way contact. This multi-species hybridization challenges the biological species concept and provides an active natural laboratory for studying speciation, reproductive isolation, and gene flow in birds [O1, O3, P29].

**Ecological significance:** As the dominant large gull in the Salish Sea, Glaucous-winged Gull plays multiple ecological roles: top predator on intertidal invertebrates, scavenger of marine carrion, predator at other seabird colonies, vector for nutrient transport (guano enriches nesting island soils), and indicator of marine food web health. Declining colony sizes in Puget Sound are considered a warning signal for broader marine ecosystem deterioration, particularly forage fish declines [G9, P30].

**Cross-links:** Module 3 Zone 6 coastal/marine community; Module 3 Zone 3 urban avifauna; Glaucous-winged × Western hybrid complex (below)

**Sources:** [G9], [O1], [O2], [O3], [P27], [P29], [P30]

- Zone 6 — year-round — common (southern OR coast) to uncommon (WA) — characteristic (Oregon coast)

**Diet and foraging:** Fish, marine invertebrates (sea urchins, mussels, crabs), garbage, seabird eggs and chicks; predatory at mixed seabird colonies. Drops hard-shelled prey from height.

### California Gull — *Larus californicus*

**Status:** Breeding (interior) / wintering (coast)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 46–55 cm | Wingspan: 122–137 cm | Mass: 430–1,045 g
- Plumage: Adult — white head and underparts, medium-gray mantle (slightly darker than Herring), black wingtips with white mirrors, yellow-green legs (not pink — key field mark vs. Herring and Western), yellow bill with both red and black spots on gonydeal angle (bicolored bill spot diagnostic). Dark eye (most adults). Four-year maturation.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Interior alkaline and freshwater lakes — colonies at Mono Lake, Great Salt Lake, Malheur NWR, Columbia Basin

### Glaucous-winged × Western Gull Hybrid Complex

**Status:** Resident (year-round through WA/OR hybrid zone)

**Overview:** The Glaucous-winged × Western Gull hybrid swarm is the dominant large gull in the zone from central Washington through northern Oregon, and arguably represents the most extensive avian hybridization event on the Pacific coast. The hybrid zone is approximately 800 km wide, centered on the Washington coast and Puget Sound, where pure Glaucous-winged Gull (from the north) and pure Western Gull (from the south) intergrade through a continuous phenotypic gradient.

**Identification challenge:** Hybrids show intermediate characters between the two parent species in:
- **Wingtip color:** Pure Glaucous-winged has gray wingtips matching mantle; pure Western has dark slate/blackish wingtips contrasting with mantle. Hybrids show every intermediate shade.
- **Mantle shade:** Glaucous-winged is paler gray; Western is darker. Hybrids intermediate.
- **Eye color:** Glaucous-winged typically dark; Western typically pale. Hybrids variable.
- **Orbital ring color:** Variable in hybrids.
- **Body size:** Intermediate.

**Breeding ecology:** Hybrid pairs (and hybrid × parent species backcrosses) breed successfully throughout the hybrid zone with no apparent reduction in fitness — hybrids show normal clutch sizes, hatching success, and fledging rates. This suggests that reproductive isolation between Glaucous-winged and Western Gull is minimal and the two may represent incipient species that have not completed speciation [O1, P29].

**Taxonomic note:** Some researchers have proposed treating the WA/OR hybrid population as a distinct entity ("Olympic Gull" or "Puget Sound Gull"), but no formal taxonomic designation has been accepted by AOS. The hybrid complex presents a practical challenge for eBird reporting and Christmas Bird Count data, where observers must often record birds as "Glaucous-winged/Western Gull (hybrid)" [O1, O2, O3].

**Sources:** [O1], [O2], [O3], [P29]

### Caspian Tern — *Hydroprogne caspia* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Breeding / transient
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: LC
- State WA: Monitored
- State OR: Monitored
- Management concern: Predation on ESA-listed salmon smolts — active management controversy

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 47–54 cm
- Wingspan: 127–140 cm
- Mass: 530–780 g
- Plumage: Breeding — white body, pale gray mantle, black cap (crown and nape), massive red-orange bill (the largest and thickest bill of any tern — heavy enough to be mistaken for a gull bill at distance), slightly forked tail, black legs. Non-breeding — black cap becomes streaked with white on forehead. Largest tern in the world.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; males average slightly larger.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Colonial — nests on bare sand or gravel islands, dredge spoil islands, flat sandy beaches. The Columbia River colony (East Sand Island, near mouth of Columbia) is the largest Caspian Tern colony in the world.
- Non-breeding: Coastal waters, estuaries, large rivers, reservoirs
- Elevation range: Sea level to ~500 ft (along major rivers)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine), Zone 5 (Estuarine/Riverine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — breeding/migration — fairly common — characteristic
- Zone 5 — breeding/migration — fairly common — visitor (major river systems)

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Fish — small to medium-sized schooling fish (anchovies, herring, smelt, shiner perch, juvenile salmon, juvenile steelhead)
- Foraging strategy: Hovers above water, then plunge-dives headfirst from 5–15 m height, capturing fish in bill. Carries single fish crosswise in bill (visible from great distance). Forages up to 50+ km from breeding colony.
- **Salmon predation:** The East Sand Island colony consumes an estimated 5–20 million juvenile salmonids annually (primarily during smolt outmigration through the Columbia River estuary, April–June). Species taken include ESA-listed Upper Columbia Spring Chinook, Snake River Spring/Summer Chinook, Snake River Sockeye, and Columbia River Steelhead. Predation rates on some ESA-listed populations estimated at 3–15% of total smolt run, depending on run size and year. This predation is the subject of intense management debate.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Shallow scrape on bare sand or gravel, unlined
- Nest location: Flat islands with minimal vegetation — East Sand Island (Columbia River mouth) is primary PNW colony site
- Clutch size: 1–3 (usually 2–3)
- Incubation: 25–28 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 28–35 days
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–August
- Colony dynamics: East Sand Island colony grew from ~1,000 pairs in early 1990s to a peak of ~8,800–9,900 pairs in the mid-2000s (the world's largest Caspian Tern colony). USACE/USFWS management actions (habitat reduction, social attraction to alternative sites) reduced the colony to ~3,500–5,000 pairs by 2020.

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable globally; PNW colony managed to reduce salmon predation
- Estimate: ~65,000–75,000 globally (cosmopolitan species — breeds on 6 continents); East Sand Island colony ~3,500–5,000 breeding pairs (post-management reduction)
- Threats: Predation by Bald Eagles at colony sites (increasing with eagle recovery), colony flooding from high water events, management actions to reduce colony size, forage fish declines affecting non-salmon prey availability
- Source: USFWS [G1], USACE Management Plan, WDFW [G9], NOAA [G15]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (and cosmopolitan)
- Route: PNW breeding → south along coast to Mexico, Central America
- Spring arrival: April (to Columbia River colony and other breeding sites)
- Fall departure: August–October
- Staging areas: Columbia River estuary, coastal estuaries
- Breeding grounds: East Sand Island (Columbia River mouth) primary; scattered smaller colonies at interior reservoirs and coastal sites
- Wintering grounds: Southern CA, Mexico, Central America; some to South America
- Distance: 3,000–10,000 km

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary call: Loud, harsh, rasping "kraa-uh" — one of the most distinctive and far-carrying tern calls; audible for 1+ km. Also a rapid "kik-kik-kik" in alarm.

**Evolutionary note:** Caspian Tern is the world's largest tern and the sole member of genus *Hydroprogne* (sometimes placed in *Sterna*). The species breeds on every continent except Antarctica — one of the most cosmopolitan seabirds on Earth. Its massive bill is adapted for catching larger prey fish than other terns can handle, placing it in a unique size niche within Laridae [O1, O3].

**Management controversy:** The East Sand Island Caspian Tern colony represents a genuine collision between two conservation objectives: protecting a native seabird species vs. reducing predation on ESA-listed salmonids. USACE and USFWS developed a management plan (2015 ROD) to reduce the colony by ~60% through habitat removal and social attraction to alternative sites in San Francisco Bay, interior OR/CA, and elsewhere. The plan has been partially implemented, reducing the colony from ~9,900 to ~4,000–5,000 pairs. Critics argue that tern predation is a minor factor compared to hydropower dams, habitat loss, and ocean conditions in driving salmon declines; proponents note that even small predation rates on critically endangered runs (e.g., Snake River Sockeye with <500 returning adults) can have population-level impacts. The management approach — redistributing a native predator to reduce its impact on endangered prey — has few precedents in wildlife management and raises fundamental questions about managing predator-prey relationships in anthropogenically altered ecosystems [G1, G15].

### Arctic Tern — *Sterna paradisaea* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Transient (uncommon — primarily offshore)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: LC
- PIF: Moderate concern
- Notable: Undertakes the longest migration of any animal on Earth

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 28–33 cm
- Wingspan: 66–77 cm
- Mass: 86–127 g (lightest tern regularly occurring in PNW — slighter than Common Tern)
- Plumage: Breeding — white body with pale gray mantle, black cap, blood-red bill (entirely red — no dark tip, unlike Common Tern), red legs, very deeply forked tail with long streamers extending well past folded wingtips. Non-breeding — white forehead, dark bill. Overall impression is paler, more delicate, and shorter-legged than Common Tern, with translucent primaries visible in flight.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; females average slightly smaller.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Arctic and subarctic tundra coastlines, offshore islands. Nearest breeding populations to PNW are in coastal Alaska and Arctic Canada.
- Migration/transit: Pelagic — passes through PNW waters well offshore; rarely seen from shore unless storm-driven. Occasionally detected at outer coast headlands during strong northwest winds.
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Pelagic/Marine — offshore transit)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — migration (offshore) — rare to uncommon — transient

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small fish (sand lance, capelin, herring fry), euphausiids, copepods
- Foraging strategy: Hovers above water on shallow, rapid wingbeats, then plunge-dives for small fish and crustaceans. More buoyant and agile in flight than Common Tern. Feeds primarily in cold, nutrient-rich waters where upwelling concentrates prey near the surface.

**Reproduction:** Does not breed in PNW. Breeds on Arctic coastlines.
- Clutch: 1–2 eggs on ground scrape
- Incubation: 21–23 days
- Breeding season: June–July in Arctic

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** in parts of range (North Atlantic populations declining; Pacific populations less well monitored)
- Estimate: ~2,000,000 globally [O1]
- Threats: Climate change affecting Arctic breeding habitat and prey availability, oil spill vulnerability on migration route, reduced prey at Southern Ocean wintering grounds due to krill declines
- Source: Cornell [O1], IUCN

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Global — transits all major ocean basins
- Route: **Arctic → Antarctic and back — the longest migration of any animal on Earth.** Birds breeding in Arctic Alaska fly south through the Pacific, cross the equator, and winter in Antarctic/sub-Antarctic pack ice edge — then return north the following spring. Some individuals detour through the Atlantic. Total annual round-trip distance: **44,000–90,000+ km** depending on route taken.
- Spring arrival: Passes through PNW offshore waters May–June (rarely detected from shore)
- Fall departure: Passes through July–October (offshore)
- Staging areas: Not a PNW staging species — transits through offshore waters
- Breeding grounds: Arctic coastlines worldwide (Arctic Alaska, Canada, Scandinavia, Siberia — circumpolar)
- Wintering grounds: Antarctic pack ice edge and Southern Ocean (experiences "summer" in both hemispheres — more daylight hours per year than any other animal)
- Distance: **44,000–90,000+ km round-trip annually** (documented by geolocator studies)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary call: High-pitched "kee-yah" and "kree-err" — higher and thinner than Common Tern
- Alarm: Rapid "kip-kip-kip-kip"

**Evolutionary note:** Arctic Tern's migration is the longest of any animal — a single bird may fly the equivalent of three round trips to the Moon during its 25–30 year lifespan (~2.4 million km). Geolocator tracking studies (Egevang et al. 2010) revealed that the route is not a simple north-south line: birds make an S-shaped detour through the Atlantic, following prevailing wind patterns to minimize energy expenditure, then loop through the Southern Ocean. The species experiences more daylight hours per year than any other organism — breeding under the midnight sun in the Arctic, then wintering under the midnight sun in the Antarctic. This "perpetual summer" strategy maximizes foraging time and access to seasonally productive waters at both poles [O1].

**Physiological adaptation:** Arctic Terns have evolved several adaptations for ultra-long-distance flight: very low wing loading (large wing area relative to body mass), efficient aerodynamic profile, and the ability to sleep in brief micro-naps during sustained flight. Their small body mass (under 130 g) means that even modest fat reserves of 20–30 g can fuel hundreds of kilometers of flight. The species' plumage is among the whitest of any tern, potentially reflecting solar radiation and reducing heat stress during equatorial transit [O1].

## Family Alcidae — Alcids

Alcids are the ecological counterparts of penguins in the Northern Hemisphere — stocky, wing-propelled diving seabirds that pursue fish and zooplankton underwater. Unlike penguins, all alcids retain the ability to fly (except the extinct Great Auk). The family reaches its greatest diversity in the North Pacific, and the PNW coast supports approximately 10–12 species. Alcids are among the most marine-dependent PNW birds — most species come to land only to breed, spending the rest of the year entirely at sea.

### Marbled Murrelet — *Brachyramphus marmoratus* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Resident (year-round, nearshore marine + inland old-growth nesting)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: **Threatened** (listed 1992)
- IUCN: EN (Endangered)
- State WA: Threatened
- State OR: Threatened
- State CA: Endangered
- PIF score: 20 (Highest concern)
- COSEWIC (Canada): Threatened

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 23–25 cm
- Wingspan: 40–43 cm
- Mass: 190–270 g
- Plumage: Breeding — unique among alcids: dark brown upperparts with heavy rusty-brown marbling across breast and belly (cryptic — camouflages bird against mossy old-growth branch). Non-breeding — black-and-white: dark above, white below, with white scapular stripe. Small, chunky, short-necked seabird with short wings adapted for underwater propulsion.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; sexes similar in plumage.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: **Old-growth and mature conifer forest** — nests on large moss-covered limbs of old-growth trees (Douglas-fir, Sitka spruce, western hemlock, western red cedar), typically 30–60 m above ground. Requires trees >80 cm DBH with large horizontal limbs covered in moss/lichen pads thick enough to support an egg. This dual marine/terrestrial habitat requirement is unique among alcids and is the species' most critical conservation vulnerability.
- Foraging: Nearshore marine waters — within 2 km of shore in protected bays and along outer coast. Forages by diving from surface, pursuing small fish underwater using wings for propulsion.
- Commuting: Adults fly inland 30–80 km from ocean to nesting trees, typically at dawn and dusk, at speeds of 90–160 km/h through forest canopy. This daily commute between ocean foraging grounds and inland nest is extraordinary and energetically costly.
- Elevation range: Sea level (foraging) to 1,500 m (inland nesting, depending on old-growth availability)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — foraging), Zone 2 (Montane conifer — nesting)
- Ecoregion secondary: Zone 4 (Lowland conifer — nesting, where coastal old-growth persists)
- Microhabitat: Nesting — single egg laid directly on moss pad on large horizontal limb in old-growth canopy (no nest construction). The nest site must be (1) large limb ≥15 cm diameter, (2) covered in moss/lichen pad ≥8 cm thick, (3) in upper canopy with relatively open flyway to and from the ocean, (4) in forest stand with mean tree DBH >80 cm and old-growth structural characteristics. Foraging — nearshore waters <30 m deep over rocky reef and sand substrates.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine) — year-round — uncommon to fairly common — **indicator species** (nearshore marine health)
- Zone 2 (Montane conifer — old-growth) — breeding season (Apr–Sep) — rare — **indicator species** (old-growth forest integrity)
- Zone 4 (Lowland conifer — old-growth) — breeding season — rare — indicator species

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small schooling fish — Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), surf smelt (*Hypomesus pretiosus*), northern anchovy (*Engraulis mordax*), and euphausiid crustaceans (krill)
- Foraging strategy: Wing-propelled pursuit diving from the surface, typically in waters <30 m deep within 2 km of shore. Dives average 15–45 seconds duration, reaching depths of 10–30 m. Captures individual small fish and carries them one at a time in bill to chick at inland nest site — a round trip of 60–160+ km.
- Chick provisioning: Parents make 2–8 food deliveries per day to the nest, each requiring a round-trip commute. Fish are carried crosswise in the bill. This provisioning rate is among the lowest of any alcid due to the extreme commuting distance, and limits reproductive output to a single chick per year.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: No nest — single egg laid directly on moss pad on old-growth tree limb
- Nest location: Large moss-covered limbs in old-growth canopy, 30–60 m above ground, typically 30–80 km inland from ocean
- Clutch size: **1 egg** (no replacement if lost — the smallest clutch of any PNW seabird)
- Incubation: 27–30 days, both sexes alternating in 24-hour shifts (one parent incubates while the other forages at sea)
- Fledging: 27–40 days; the chick fledges by launching itself from the nest limb and flying directly to the ocean — a maiden flight of 30–80+ km through forest canopy to the coast. This flight occurs at night to reduce predation risk. The chick is independent from the moment it reaches the ocean (no post-fledging parental care).
- Broods per year: 1 (no replacement clutch)
- Breeding season: April–September
- Notable behavior: The chick's fledging flight is one of the most remarkable events in PNW avian biology. A 27–40 day old nestling that has never flown launches from a tree branch 30–60 m above the ground and navigates 30–80+ km through dark forest to reach the ocean. Mortality during this flight (from collisions, predation, and disorientation) is significant and may be exacerbated by artificial lighting from coastal development, which can disorient fledglings.

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** — estimated 50–75% decline since 1970s; continuing decline of ~3–5% per year across range
- Estimate: ~22,000–24,000 at sea in WA (WDFW marine surveys, declining); ~18,000–25,000 in OR; ~270,000–400,000 range-wide (Alaska through central CA). BC population ~66,000 (COSEWIC 2012).
- Threats: (1) **Old-growth timber harvest** — the primary historical and continuing threat; ~90% of original old-growth forest in WA/OR has been logged, eliminating nesting habitat. Remaining old-growth is fragmented, increasing edge effects and predation on nests by corvids (Steller's Jays, Common Ravens, American Crows). (2) **Forage fish decline** — reduced availability of sand lance, herring, and smelt in nearshore waters. (3) **Oil spill vulnerability** — concentrated in nearshore waters year-round. (4) **Nest predation by corvids** — increased near forest edges created by logging; Steller's Jay predation on eggs documented as major factor at fragmented sites. (5) **Gillnet bycatch** — entanglement in nearshore fishing nets. (6) **Artificial lighting** — coastal and logging road lighting disorients fledglings during maiden ocean-bound flights. (7) **Climate change** — affecting both marine prey and old-growth forest conditions.
- Source: USFWS ESA listing and 5-year reviews [G1], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], Ralph et al. 1995 [P4], Raphael et al. 2014 [P5], Nelson 1997 [P28], Conservation Northwest [O12]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (non-migratory; resident)
- Route: Non-migratory — remains in nearshore waters year-round. Moves between inland nesting sites and ocean foraging areas during breeding season (daily commute, not migration).
- Spring arrival: Resident; inland commuting flights begin April
- Fall departure: Resident; ceases inland flights by September
- Staging areas: Not applicable
- Breeding grounds: Old-growth forests from Alaska Panhandle through BC, WA, OR, to central CA
- Wintering grounds: Nearshore marine waters in same range
- Distance: 0 km (resident; daily commute 60–160+ km round-trip during breeding)

**Vocalizations:**
- Primary call: High-pitched, rapid "keer-keer-keer-keer" — a distinctive call often heard at dawn and dusk near old-growth stands during breeding season when birds fly overhead en route between ocean and nest. This call is used in audio survey protocols (inland dawn surveys) to detect breeding activity.
- At sea: Generally silent

**Evolutionary note:** Marbled Murrelet's tree-nesting habit is an anomaly within Alcidae — all other alcids nest on the ground, in burrows, or on cliff ledges. Its close relative, the Long-billed Murrelet (*B. perdix*) of northeast Asia, also nests in trees, suggesting the behavior evolved in the ancestor of both species. The evolutionary pressures that drove an ocean-going seabird to nest 80 km inland in old-growth tree canopy are not fully understood, but may relate to reduced mammalian predation in the canopy compared to ground-level nest sites, and the historical abundance of old-growth nesting habitat in the Pacific Northwest's pre-logging landscape. The species' extremely low reproductive rate (1 egg, 1 brood, no replacement, single-chick provisioning through long-distance commuting) makes population recovery exceptionally slow even when threats are reduced [P4, P28, O1, O3, P36].

**Old-growth dependency:** Marbled Murrelet is the most sensitive indicator of old-growth forest condition in the PNW marine-terrestrial interface. The species' nesting requirements (large moss-covered limbs on trees >80 cm DBH) are met almost exclusively by old-growth and late-successional forest stands >200 years old. The ~90% loss of old-growth forest in WA and OR since European settlement directly tracks the species' population decline. Remaining populations are concentrated where large blocks of old-growth persist: Olympic National Park, North Cascades, central Oregon Coast Range, and northern CA redwood/Douglas-fir forests. The species' ESA listing in 1992 was a pivotal moment in PNW forest policy, contributing (alongside the Northern Spotted Owl listing) to the 1994 Northwest Forest Plan that restricted logging on federal old-growth lands [P4, P5, G1, G6, O10, O12].

### Common Murre — *Uria aalge* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Resident (year-round, nearshore and offshore marine)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: LC
- State WA: Candidate (declining colony populations)
- State OR: Sensitive
- PIF: Moderate concern

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 38–43 cm
- Wingspan: 64–73 cm
- Mass: 900–1,100 g (one of the heaviest flying seabirds in the PNW; wing loading is among the highest of any flying bird)
- Plumage: Breeding — chocolate-brown to black head and upperparts, white underparts, sharp demarcation at breast (tuxedo pattern). Non-breeding — white chin, throat, and ear coverts with dark post-ocular stripe. Thin, pointed black bill. Short wings adapted for underwater propulsion (trade-off: high-energy flight in air, efficient propulsion underwater).
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; sexes similar.
- Subspecies: PNW birds are *U. a. californica* (darker and slightly smaller than Atlantic *U. a. aalge*).

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Sea cliffs, rocky offshore islands — nests on bare rock ledges in dense colonies. Major PNW colonies on Oregon coast (Three Arch Rocks, Haystack Rock at Cannon Beach, Yaquina Head), Washington coast (Tatoosh Island, Carroll Island, Alexander Island, Jagged Island), and BC (Triangle Island, Scott Islands).
- Non-breeding: Nearshore and offshore marine waters over continental shelf. Disperses from colonies after breeding but remains in Pacific coastal waters year-round.
- Elevation range: Sea level (cliff ledge nesting 5–100 m above sea surface)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)
- Microhabitat: Nests on bare rock cliff ledges with no nest material — egg is pyriform (pear-shaped) to resist rolling off narrow ledge (spins in circle when disturbed rather than rolling off edge). Colony density extremely high — 20–30 breeding pairs per m² on some ledges.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine) — year-round — common (outer coast) — characteristic (dominant offshore breeding seabird)

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small schooling fish — Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance, surf smelt, northern anchovy, capelin, juvenile rockfish; also euphausiid krill and squid
- Foraging strategy: Wing-propelled pursuit diving. Dives to typical depths of 20–60 m (maximum recorded >180 m — among the deepest dives of any flying bird). Pursues fish schools underwater using wings as flippers, steering with feet. Can remain submerged for 1–3 minutes per dive.
- Foraging range: Typically forages within 30–100 km of breeding colony during nesting season; ranges more widely outside breeding season.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: No nest — single egg laid directly on bare rock ledge
- Nest location: Narrow cliff ledges on sea cliffs and offshore islands, often highly exposed
- Clutch size: **1 egg** (no replacement in most years). Egg is pyriform (pear-shaped) and comes in a remarkable range of colors and patterns — blue, green, white, cream, with variable spots and scrawls. Each female's egg is individually patterned, allowing parents to identify their own egg in the dense colony.
- Incubation: 28–35 days, both sexes alternating in shifts of 12–36 hours
- Fledging: 18–25 days; the partially grown chick launches from the cliff ledge at night, gliding/fluttering to the ocean surface below. The chick (unable to fly, weighing ~25% of adult mass) is escorted to sea by the male parent, who accompanies it for 4–8 weeks on the open ocean, feeding it while it completes growth and learns to forage.
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–August

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** in PNW — OR colonies experienced catastrophic die-off during 2014–2016 marine heatwave ("the Blob"); WA colonies showing long-term decline
- Estimate: ~700,000–1,000,000 in Pacific *californica* population; OR coast supports ~600,000–750,000 birds at peak (Speich & Wahl 1989 baseline); WA coast ~12,000–15,000. Major die-off in 2015–2016: an estimated 500,000+ Common Murres died in the northeast Pacific due to the marine heatwave, with massive beach-cast carcasses from Alaska to California. OR colony attendance dropped 50–80% at some sites and has only partially recovered.
- Threats: Marine heatwaves reducing prey availability (the 2014–2016 "Blob" was the single most devastating event for PNW murre populations in recorded history), oil spill vulnerability (murres are the seabird most frequently killed by oil spills worldwide due to high density on water), gillnet bycatch, Bald Eagle disturbance at colonies (flushing adults from ledges causes egg and chick loss), reduced forage fish, climate change intensifying marine heatwave events
- Source: Speich & Wahl 1989 [P29], Nysewander et al. 2005 [P30], USFWS Seabird Program [G1], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (non-migratory; dispersive)
- Route: Non-migratory — disperses from breeding colonies after nesting season but remains in NE Pacific. Some southward dispersal in winter.
- Spring arrival: Returns to colony ledges February–March (early occupation of ledges is important for site defense)
- Fall departure: Departs colonies August–September; disperses into offshore waters
- Staging areas: Not applicable — pelagic dispersal
- Breeding grounds: Pacific coast sea cliffs from Alaska to central CA
- Wintering grounds: Nearshore and offshore Pacific waters, broadly similar range to breeding
- Distance: 0–2,000 km (dispersive, not truly migratory)

**Vocalizations:**
- Colony calls: Loud, growling "murre-murre-murre" (source of common name); also a harsh "arrr" and various guttural calls. Breeding colonies are extraordinarily loud — audible from several hundred meters.

**Evolutionary note:** Common Murre has the highest wing loading (body mass relative to wing area) of any flying bird in the PNW — its small wings are a compromise between aerial flight and underwater propulsion. The species flies with rapid, buzzing wingbeats at 75–80 km/h and must maintain high speed to remain airborne. Its pyriform egg is one of the most studied examples of adaptive egg shape — the pronounced point on one end causes the egg to spin in a tight arc when disturbed rather than rolling off the narrow cliff ledge. The individual variation in egg color and pattern (comparable to human fingerprints in uniqueness) allows parents to locate their own egg among thousands on a crowded ledge — a necessity given that murre colonies pack 20+ nesting pairs per m² [O1, P29, P35, P36].

**2015–2016 die-off:** The northeast Pacific marine heatwave ("the Blob") of 2014–2016 caused the largest recorded Common Murre die-off in history. Warming sea surface temperatures (2–4°C above average) disrupted the marine food web, reducing availability of cold-water forage fish (sand lance, herring, capelin). An estimated 500,000–1,000,000 murres starved to death across the NE Pacific from Alaska to California. Beach-cast carcasses were found at rates 100–1,000x above baseline along the PNW coast. OR breeding colonies experienced 50–80% attendance declines, and reproductive failure was nearly complete in 2015 and 2016. Recovery has been slow — some colonies had not returned to pre-heatwave levels by 2025. This event demonstrated the extreme vulnerability of colonial, specialized-feeding seabirds to rapid marine climate shifts [P30, P31, G15].

### Tufted Puffin — *Fratercula cirrhata* (EXTENDED)

**Status:** Breeding resident (declining) / year-round marine
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing; under review)
- IUCN: LC (but Pacific populations declining significantly)
- State WA: Candidate (WDFW — severe declines documented)
- State OR: Sensitive
- PIF: High concern
- Trend: **Severe decline in southern part of range** (WA, OR, CA)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 36–41 cm
- Wingspan: 63–68 cm
- Mass: 660–920 g
- Plumage: Breeding — unmistakable: entirely black body, white face patch, massive orange-red laterally compressed bill with yellowish-green base, long golden-yellow head plumes (tufts) curving back behind eye. Orange legs and feet. The most visually striking PNW seabird. Non-breeding — face becomes darker, bill plates partially shed (bill becomes smaller and duller), tufts reduced or lost. First-year birds lack tufts entirely and have smaller, darker bills.
- Sexual dimorphism: Minimal; males average slightly larger.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Offshore islands with soil deep enough for burrow excavation — grassy slopes, turf-covered island tops, steep earthen banks. Major PNW colonies on outer coast islands (WA: Alexander Island, Destruction Island, Carroll Island, Tatoosh Island; OR: Three Arch Rocks, Haystack Rock). Historically bred as far south as the Farallon Islands (CA) and Channel Islands (CA), but extirpated from most southern California colonies.
- Non-breeding: Pelagic — far offshore over continental shelf and beyond. One of the most pelagic PNW seabirds outside breeding season.
- Elevation range: Sea level (burrow nesting 5–100 m above sea surface on island slopes)
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — pelagic and island breeding)
- Microhabitat: Excavates burrow 0.5–2.7 m deep in earthen/turf slopes on offshore islands. Burrow entrance typically concealed by grass or other vegetation. Some pairs nest in rock crevices on cliff faces where soil is unavailable.

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine) — year-round — uncommon (declining) — characteristic (outer coast islands)

**Diet and foraging:**
- Primary diet: Small schooling fish — Pacific sand lance, Pacific herring, capelin, smelt, juvenile rockfish; also squid and euphausiid krill
- Foraging strategy: Wing-propelled pursuit diving, similar to Common Murre. Dives to 30–60 m depth. Can carry multiple fish crosswise in bill simultaneously (bill serrations grip fish — adults deliver 5–20 fish per bill-load to chick, a distinctive sight visible from observation points at colonies).
- Foraging range: Up to 100+ km from colony during breeding; far offshore (pelagic) during non-breeding season.

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Burrow excavated by both parents using bill and feet in grassy/turf slopes; lined with grass, feathers, and debris
- Nest location: Earthen slopes on offshore islands, typically 5–100 m above sea surface
- Clutch size: 1 egg (no replacement)
- Incubation: 42–47 days, both sexes (one of the longest incubation periods of any PNW seabird)
- Fledging: 40–55 days; chick departs burrow at night, walking/fluttering to ocean surface. Unlike Common Murre, the fledgling is independent immediately — no parental escort at sea.
- Broods per year: 1
- Breeding season: May–September (prolonged nesting cycle)
- Colony attendance: Adults return to colony sites in April for burrow maintenance and courtship; egg laying peaks in June; chicks fledge August–September. Total colony attendance period: ~5 months.

**Population:**
- Trend: **Severe decline in PNW** — estimated 80–90% decline in WA breeding population since 1970s. OR populations also declining but less severely.
- Estimate: ~2,960,000 globally (concentrated in Alaska, Russia, Japan); WA breeding population estimated at ~2,500–5,000 individuals (down from ~25,000–50,000+ historically); OR breeding population ~5,000–10,000. The species' global abundance masks precipitous declines at the southern range margin.
- Threats: (1) **Forage fish decline** in nearshore waters — reduced availability of sand lance and herring. (2) **Marine heatwaves** — the 2014–2016 "Blob" caused significant mortality and reproductive failure. (3) **Introduced predators on nesting islands** — rats, foxes, and raccoons on some islands destroy eggs, chicks, and adults in burrows (predator eradication programs underway on some WA islands). (4) **Bald Eagle predation and disturbance** — increasing eagle populations flush adults from burrows, exposing eggs/chicks to crows and gulls. (5) **Oil spill vulnerability** — pelagic habit and wing-propelled diving make puffins extremely vulnerable to surface oil. (6) **Climate change** — shifting prey distributions, increasing marine heatwave frequency, ocean acidification affecting plankton food web base.
- Source: Speich & Wahl 1989 [P29], Nysewander et al. 2005 [P30], WDFW [G9], ODFW [G12], USFWS [G1]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (dispersive, not truly migratory)
- Route: Disperses from colonies after breeding into open Pacific; some southward movement in winter. Essentially pelagic October–March.
- Spring arrival: Returns to colony sites March–April
- Fall departure: Departs colonies September–October
- Staging areas: Not applicable — pelagic dispersal
- Breeding grounds: Offshore islands from Alaska to OR (historically to CA). Core range: Aleutian Islands, Gulf of Alaska.
- Wintering grounds: Open North Pacific, over continental shelf and beyond
- Distance: 0–5,000 km (dispersive)

**Vocalizations:**
- Colony calls: Low, growling "rrrr-rrr" from within burrows; generally silent in flight and at sea
- Display: Bill-gaping and head-waving during courtship; pair members fence with bills at burrow entrance

**Evolutionary note:** Tufted Puffin is the largest member of the puffin subfamily (Fraterculinae) and one of only three puffin species worldwide (Tufted, Horned, and Atlantic). Its massive bill, like all puffin bills, consists of a bony core overlaid with keratinous plates that are partially shed after the breeding season — the bill becomes smaller and duller in winter, one of the few birds to undergo seasonal bill-size change. The golden tufts and massive colored bill are sexual selection ornaments — both sexes display them, suggesting mutual mate choice. Phylogenetically, puffins are nested within Alcidae, most closely related to auklets and murrelets, with the puffin lineage diverging approximately 10–15 million years ago [O1, P36].

**Range contraction:** The decline of Tufted Puffin at the southern margin of its breeding range (WA, OR, historically CA) is one of the most concerning seabird trends in the NE Pacific. The pattern — stable or increasing populations in Alaska alongside severe declines in WA/OR/CA — is consistent with a climate-driven range contraction, as warming ocean conditions reduce cold-water prey availability at the southern range edge. If current trends continue, Tufted Puffin may be functionally extirpated as a breeding species in Washington within decades. This range contraction parallels similar patterns in Cassin's Auklet and Rhinoceros Auklet at southern colonies [P29, P30, G9].

**Cross-links:** Module 3 Zone 6 offshore island community; Module 5 seabird population decline; Module 5 climate-driven range contraction

**Sources:** [G1], [G9], [G12], [O1], [P29], [P30], [P36]

### Rhinoceros Auklet — *Cerorhinca monocerata*

**Status:** Breeding resident / year-round marine
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | State WA: Monitored

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 35–39 cm | Wingspan: 56–58 cm | Mass: 480–620 g
- Plumage: Breeding — dark gray-brown above, paler below, two white facial plumes (one above eye, one from gape), short pale horn ("rhinoceros horn") at base of orange bill. Non-breeding — facial plumes reduced, horn absent, bill duller. Despite the "auklet" name, this species is taxonomically a puffin (Fraterculinae) — the only puffin with a horn.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Offshore islands — excavates burrows in earthen slopes like Tufted Puffin. Major PNW colony on Protection Island (WA) — one of the largest in the world (~36,000 breeding pairs).
- Non-breeding: Nearshore to offshore marine waters
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — year-round — fairly common — characteristic (island breeding colonies)

**Diet and foraging:** Pacific sand lance, Pacific herring, smelt, euphausiids; wing-propelled pursuit diving like other alcids. Carries multiple fish crosswise in bill to chick (like puffins).

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Burrow (0.5–5 m deep) in earthen slopes on offshore islands
- Clutch size: 1 egg
- Incubation: 39–52 days, both sexes
- Fledging: 45–55 days
- Breeding season: April–September

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable in core WA colonies; declining at southern range margin
- Estimate: ~1,400,000 globally; WA population ~75,000–100,000 (Protection Island colony dominant)
- Threats: Forage fish decline, oil spill vulnerability, introduced predators on nesting islands, eagle disturbance
- Source: Speich & Wahl 1989 [P29], Nysewander et al. 2005 [P30], WDFW [G9]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (dispersive)
- Route: Disperses from colonies into marine waters; non-migratory but moves offshore in winter
- Spring arrival: Returns to colonies March–April
- Fall departure: Departs September; offshore October–March
- Staging areas: Not applicable
- Breeding grounds: Offshore islands from Alaska to CA; Protection Island WA is major colony
- Wintering grounds: Nearshore to offshore NE Pacific
- Distance: 0–3,000 km (dispersive)

**Evolutionary note:** Despite its common name, Rhinoceros Auklet is a puffin (subfamily Fraterculinae), not an auklet (Aethiini). Molecular phylogenetics place it as sister to the three *Fratercula* puffins. The keratinous "horn" at the bill base is grown annually for the breeding season and shed afterward — its function appears to be a sexual ornament, like the puffins' bill plates [O1, P36].

**Sources:** [G9], [O1], [P29], [P30], [P36]

### Cassin's Auklet — *Ptychoramphus aleuticus*

**Status:** Breeding resident / year-round marine
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: NT (Near Threatened) | State WA: Candidate

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 20–24 cm | Wingspan: 38–42 cm | Mass: 150–200 g
- Plumage: Uniform dark gray-blue above, slightly paler below with whitish belly, small pale spot above eye (diagnostic but subtle), pale base to lower mandible. Small, chunky, and nondescript — the most plainly plumaged alcid in the PNW. No seasonal plumage change.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Offshore islands — excavates small burrows or nests in rock crevices. Nocturnal at colonies (arrives and departs in darkness to avoid predation by gulls and eagles).
- Non-breeding: Offshore marine waters over continental shelf; more pelagic than many auklets
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — pelagic)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — year-round — uncommon (rarely seen from shore) — characteristic (outer coast)

**Diet and foraging:** Euphausiid krill (primary), copepods, larval fish; wing-propelled pursuit diving in upper water column; specializes on zooplankton rather than fish

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Burrow or rock crevice on offshore islands
- Clutch size: 1 egg
- Incubation: 37–42 days
- Fledging: 41–50 days
- Breeding season: April–August (nocturnal colony attendance)

**Population:**
- Trend: **Declining** — massive die-offs during marine heatwaves (2014–2016, 2019)
- Estimate: ~3,600,000 globally (concentrated in BC and AK); WA population ~10,000–20,000
- Threats: Marine heatwaves, prey (krill) availability shifts, oil spill vulnerability, introduced predators on nesting islands
- Source: Speich & Wahl 1989 [P29], WDFW [G9]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (dispersive)
- Route: Disperses from colonies into offshore waters; essentially non-migratory
- Spring arrival: Returns to colonies March–April (nocturnal)
- Fall departure: Departs August–September
- Staging areas: Not applicable
- Breeding grounds: Offshore islands from Aleutians to Baja California
- Wintering grounds: Offshore Pacific, over continental shelf
- Distance: 0–3,000 km (dispersive)

**Evolutionary note:** Cassin's Auklet is a zooplankton specialist — unusual among PNW alcids, which are predominantly fish-eaters. Its specialization on euphausiid krill makes it particularly sensitive to oceanographic conditions that affect krill abundance (upwelling intensity, sea surface temperature). The species' nocturnal colony attendance is an anti-predation behavior — by arriving and departing only in complete darkness, it avoids the gulls and eagles that patrol colony areas during daylight [O1, P36].

**Sources:** [G9], [O1], [P29], [P36]

### Ancient Murrelet — *Synthliboramphus antiquus*

**Status:** Wintering (rare) / transient
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 24–27 cm | Wingspan: 43–46 cm | Mass: 190–260 g
- Plumage: Breeding — black crown and throat, gray back, white underparts, white plumes on sides of head (giving an "aged" appearance — source of common name), yellow bill. Non-breeding — duller, reduced plumes.

**Habitat:** Offshore marine waters; rarely seen from shore except during storms. Breeds on forested islands in BC and Alaska (not in WA/OR).
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — offshore)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — wintering — rare — visitor

**Diet and foraging:** Euphausiid krill, copepods, small fish; wing-propelled pursuit diving

**Population:**
- Trend: Declining (BC colonies threatened by introduced predators)
- Estimate: ~1,000,000 globally (majority in BC — Haida Gwaii colonies)
- Source: Cornell [O1], Gaston & Jones 1998 [P36]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific
- Route: BC/AK island breeding → southward dispersal into PNW offshore waters in winter
- Spring arrival: Present in PNW waters October–March
- Fall departure: Arrives October
- Staging areas: Not applicable
- Breeding grounds: Forested islands, BC (Haida Gwaii — major colonies) and Alaska
- Wintering grounds: Offshore PNW to northern CA
- Distance: 1,000–3,000 km

**Evolutionary note:** Ancient Murrelet has one of the most remarkable chick-rearing strategies in the bird world: chicks leave the nest burrow when only 2–3 days old (before they can fly) and scramble to the ocean at night, where they join their parents on the water. The family then swims away from the island together, with the chicks being fed at sea for several weeks. This precocial departure from the nest is more extreme than any other alcid and is thought to be an adaptation to reduce predation at the colony [O1, P36].

**Sources:** [O1], [P36]

### Pigeon Guillemot — *Cepphus columba*

**Status:** Breeding resident / year-round
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 30–35 cm | Wingspan: 54–58 cm | Mass: 450–550 g
- Plumage: Breeding — entirely black with large white wing patches (oval, conspicuous in flight and at rest), bright red legs and feet, red gape, dark bill. Non-breeding — white below, mottled gray-brown above, wing patches maintained. Red feet visible at distance.

**Habitat:**
- Breeding: Rocky shorelines, cliff crevices, wharf pilings, jetty rubble — nests in cavities and crevices at low elevation near water (unlike most alcids, which nest on offshore islands, Pigeon Guillemots commonly nest on the mainland)
- Foraging: Nearshore waters <30 m deep, over rocky and sandy bottom
- Ecoregion primary: Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — nearshore)

**Ecoregion presence:**
- Zone 6 — year-round — fairly common — characteristic (nearshore rocky coast and harbors)

**Diet and foraging:** Bottom fish (gunnels, sculpins, blennies), shrimp, marine worms; wing-propelled pursuit diving in shallow water; specializes on benthic prey rather than schooling pelagic fish

**Reproduction:**
- Nest type: Cavity nester — uses rock crevices, cliff holes, spaces under boulders, wharf pilings, even drainage pipes
- Clutch size: 1–2 eggs
- Incubation: 26–32 days
- Fledging: 29–39 days
- Breeding season: May–August

**Population:**
- Trend: Stable
- Estimate: ~550,000 [O1]
- Source: Cornell [O1]

**Migration:**
- Flyway: Pacific (non-migratory)
- Route: Resident — remains near breeding areas year-round
- Spring arrival: Resident
- Fall departure: Resident
- Staging areas: Not applicable
- Breeding grounds: Pacific coast from Alaska to CA (throughout PNW)
- Wintering grounds: Same as breeding range
- Distance: 0 km (resident)

**Evolutionary note:** Pigeon Guillemot is the most commonly seen alcid from shore in the PNW — its nearshore habitat, cavity-nesting on the mainland (not just offshore islands), and conspicuous white wing patches make it accessible to casual observers. Its congener, the Black Guillemot (*C. grylle*), occupies the same niche in the Atlantic. The two are sister species with a classic Beringian vicariance distribution [O1, P36].

**Sources:** [O1], [P36]

### Horned Puffin — *Fratercula corniculata*

**Status:** Vagrant / rare visitor
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 36–41 cm | Wingspan: 56–60 cm | Mass: 480–640 g
- Plumage: Breeding — black upperparts, white underparts, white face, massive orange-red bill with yellow base, small dark fleshy "horn" above eye. Non-breeding — face darker, bill duller and smaller. Resembles Atlantic Puffin more closely than Tufted Puffin.

**Occurrence notes:** Rare to very rare in PNW waters. Breeding range is primarily Alaska (Aleutian Islands, Gulf of Alaska). Occasional individuals detected on PNW pelagic birding trips or found beach-cast after storms. Does not breed south of SE Alaska. Most PNW records are winter or late fall.

**Records:** Irregular occurrence; scattered records from WA and OR pelagic waters, typically during winter storms or marine heatwave displacement events [G9, G10]

**Nearest regular range:** Breeds in Alaska from Aleutians through Gulf of Alaska; winters in offshore North Pacific

**Source:** WDFW [G9], WA Bird Records Committee [G10], Cornell [O1]

## Vagrant and Accidental Shorebirds (Charadriiformes)

The following species have been recorded in the PNW but are not regular in occurrence. Most are long-distance migrants from Arctic or Asian breeding grounds that have overshot or deviated from their normal migration routes. Records are typically single individuals detected by experienced observers during peak migration periods. All records are vetted by state bird records committees (WA: [G10]; OR: [G12]).

### Bar-tailed Godwit — *Limosa lapponica*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare, primarily fall)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Arctic Alaska and Siberia; migrates to Australia, New Zealand, and SE Asia via transoceanic flights of 10,000–12,000 km nonstop (longest documented nonstop flight of any bird — 13,560 km recorded by satellite tag)
**PNW records:** Rare fall migrant; <30 accepted records in WA/OR. Most records from outer coast beaches and estuaries, September–November.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Hudsonian Godwit — *Limosa haemastica*

**Status:** Vagrant (rare, primarily fall)
**Normal range:** Breeds in subarctic Alaska and Canada; migrates through Great Plains and Atlantic coast to southern South America
**PNW records:** Rare transient; <20 accepted records in WA/OR. Most detections in fall at Malheur NWR, Grays Harbor, or coastal estuaries.
**Source:** [G10], [G12], [O1]

---

### Ruff — *Calidris pugnax*

**Status:** Vagrant (rare but nearly annual)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Eurasian Arctic; winters in Africa, India, SE Asia. Small numbers migrate through western Alaska, with rare stragglers reaching Pacific coast.
**PNW records:** Nearly annual in fall; ~50+ accepted WA/OR records. Most at Malheur NWR, Ridgefield NWR, and coastal estuaries, July–October. The most frequently recorded Eurasian vagrant shorebird in the PNW.
**Source:** [G10], [G12], [O1]

---

### Sharp-tailed Sandpiper — *Calidris acuminata*

**Status:** Vagrant (rare but regular in fall)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Siberian Arctic; migrates to Australia. Juveniles regularly overshoot into western North America in fall.
**PNW records:** Regular rare fall visitor; ~40+ accepted records, primarily juveniles in September–October. Found at coastal and interior marshes, often associating with Pectoral Sandpiper flocks.
**Source:** [G10], [G12], [O1]

---

### Buff-breasted Sandpiper — *Calidris subruficollis*

**Status:** Vagrant (rare, fall)
**Normal range:** Breeds in High Arctic North America; migrates through Great Plains to South American pampas
**PNW records:** Rare fall transient; <20 accepted WA/OR records. Dry grassland and short-grass habitats during migration — unusual among shorebirds. Most records September.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Curlew Sandpiper — *Calidris ferruginea*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Siberian Arctic; migrates to Africa, India, Australia. Very rare in the Americas.
**PNW records:** <10 accepted WA/OR records. Most detected in fall among Dunlin or Western Sandpiper flocks at coastal estuaries. Distinguished by longer, more decurved bill and white rump.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Stilt Sandpiper — *Calidris himantopus*

**Status:** Vagrant (rare, primarily fall)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Arctic Canada; migrates through central North America to South America
**PNW records:** Rare but nearly annual; ~30+ accepted WA/OR records. Most at interior wetlands (Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin) and coastal estuaries, July–September. Feeds in deep water with dowitcher-like probing motion.
**Source:** [G10], [G12], [O1]

---

### Red-necked Stint — *Calidris ruficollis*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Siberian Arctic; migrates to Australia, SE Asia
**PNW records:** <10 accepted WA/OR records. Detected among Western Sandpiper flocks at coastal estuaries. Very similar to Semipalmated Sandpiper — identification requires careful study of bare parts, plumage, and vocalizations.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Little Stint — *Calidris minuta*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Eurasian Arctic; winters in Africa, India. Extremely rare in the Americas.
**PNW records:** <5 accepted WA/OR records. Among the rarest vagrant shorebirds in the PNW.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Bristle-thighed Curlew — *Numenius tahitiensis*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare)
**Normal range:** Breeds in western Alaska tundra (very restricted range); migrates to Pacific islands (Hawaii, Polynesia) via transoceanic flight
**PNW records:** <5 accepted WA/OR records. Extremely rare — most PNW records involve spring overshoot birds on outer coast.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]

---

### Upland Sandpiper — *Bartramia longicauda*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare)
**Normal range:** Breeds in Great Plains and northeastern grasslands; migrates to South American pampas
**PNW records:** <15 accepted WA/OR records. Formerly bred in PNW grasslands (eastern WA/OR) but extirpated as breeding species by early 20th century due to grassland conversion. Current records are vagrants from Great Plains breeding populations.
**Source:** [G10], [G12], [O1]

---

### Snowy Plover (interior populations) — *Charadrius nivosus*

**Status:** Vagrant (very rare east of Cascades)
**Normal range:** Interior western populations breed at alkaline lakes (Great Salt Lake, Mono Lake, Great Basin)
**PNW records:** Occasional records from interior OR/WA alkaline lake margins (Lake Abert, Summer Lake, Malheur). Not formally tracked separately from coastal DPS but distinguished by breeding habitat (alkaline lakes vs. ocean beaches). Interior birds are not ESA-listed.
**Source:** [G12], [O1]

---

### Wilson's Plover — *Charadrius wilsonia*

**Status:** Vagrant (accidental)
**Normal range:** Atlantic and Gulf coasts of the Americas; breeds on sandy beaches from Virginia to Brazil
**PNW records:** <3 accepted records in WA/OR. Among the rarest Charadriiformes vagrants in the PNW — far out of normal range.
**Source:** [G10], [O1]
