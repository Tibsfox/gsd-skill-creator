# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 2C: Pelagic Visitors, Offshore Species, and Northern Winter Visitors

> **Module 2C deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document profiles approximately 30 species that reach the PNW from oceanic or high-latitude origins: tube-nosed seabirds (albatrosses, shearwaters, storm-petrels) that spend years on the open Pacific before passing PNW waters, pursuit-pirates (jaegers and skuas) that follow migrating seabirds along the continental shelf, wintering loons that trade Arctic tundra lakes for Salish Sea channels, pelagic phalaropes that stage by the millions on upwelling fronts, and boreal/Arctic landbirds driven south by failing prey cycles into PNW lowlands during winter. Together these species represent the PNW's deepest connections to the global ocean and the circumpolar north.
>
> **Primary ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) for all seabird and marine species. Northern winter visitors occupy Zones 1-4, 7 depending on species.
>
> **Design principle:** These are not PNW birds. They are birds of the Southern Ocean, the North Pacific gyre, the high Arctic tundra, and the boreal taiga that happen to intersect PNW geography during predictable phases of their annual cycles. Understanding them requires understanding the ecosystems they come from.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations. Only Level 1 (publicly available) cultural content.

## I. PROCELLARIIFORMES — Tube-Nosed Seabirds

The tube-nosed seabirds (order Procellariiformes) are the masters of the open ocean. Named for the tubular nasal passages atop their bills — through which they excrete excess salt and detect prey by olfaction across hundreds of kilometers — these birds spend months or years at sea between landfalls. Their relationship to the PNW is defined by the California Current: cold, nutrient-rich water upwelling along the continental shelf edge that fuels one of the most productive marine food webs on the planet. Shearwaters, fulmars, and albatrosses converge on these upwelling fronts to exploit the concentrated prey fields, making PNW offshore waters one of the great seabird staging areas of the Pacific.

All Procellariiformes share a distinctive musky odor — a stomach oil that they can eject at predators or intruders. They are long-lived (albatrosses 40-70 years, shearwaters 20-40 years), slow to reproduce (most lay a single egg per year), and exquisitely adapted to dynamic soaring on oceanic wind gradients. Their vulnerability to longline fisheries, plastic ingestion, and climate-driven prey shifts makes them among the most threatened bird orders globally [O1, P22].

### Sooty Shearwater (*Ardenna grisea*) — EXTENDED PROFILE

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Procellariidae
**Status:** Transient (non-breeding visitor, May-October)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Near Threatened (NT) — steep population decline
- State WA: Not listed (marine, pelagic)
- PIF: Not assessed (Southern Hemisphere breeder)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 40-51 cm
- **Wingspan:** 94-109 cm
- **Mass:** 650-978 g (average ~800 g)
- **Plumage:** Uniformly dark sooty-brown above and below, with distinctive silvery-white flash along the underwing coverts visible in flight. Bill dark, slender, hooked. Dark feet. No seasonal plumage variation in PNW occurrence.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal; sexes similar in the field.

#### Habitat

- **PNW occurrence:** Offshore waters from the continental shelf edge inward, primarily 10-200 km offshore. Occasionally visible from shore during strong onshore winds or concentrated inshore feeding events. Most abundant over upwelling fronts and along the continental shelf break where cold, nutrient-rich water meets warmer surface layers.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — exclusively marine in PNW
- **Ecoregion secondary:** None (does not come ashore in PNW)
- **Microhabitat:** Upwelling fronts, temperature breaks, prey aggregation zones over the continental shelf and slope. Associates with concentrations of euphausiids (*Euphausia pacifica*, *Thysanoessa spinifera*), anchovy schools, and Pacific sardine.

#### Migration

- **Flyway:** Trans-Pacific / Trans-equatorial
- **Route description:** One of the longest migrations in the animal kingdom. Breeds on sub-Antarctic islands (New Zealand, Falklands, southern Chile/Argentina, southeastern Australia) during the Southern Hemisphere summer (November-March). After breeding, undertakes a massive trans-equatorial migration northward through the central Pacific, arriving in the North Pacific (California Current system, Gulf of Alaska, Bering Sea) by May. Follows a figure-eight loop through the North Pacific, exploiting prevailing wind patterns: north along the eastern Pacific in spring, west across the North Pacific in summer, south through the western Pacific in fall. Returns to breeding colonies by October-November.
- **Spring arrival:** Late April to mid-May (vanguard); peak numbers June-August
- **Fall departure:** September-October; stragglers into November
- **Staging areas:** Continental shelf edge from northern California to British Columbia; peak concentrations off Westport, WA and Newport, OR where upwelling is strongest. Monterey Bay (CA) is the single largest staging concentration in the eastern Pacific.
- **Breeding grounds:** New Zealand (Snares Islands, Chatham Islands, Auckland Islands), Falkland Islands, Tierra del Fuego, southeastern Australia (Tasmania, mainland colonies)
- **Wintering grounds (austral summer):** Breeding colonies in Southern Hemisphere
- **Distance:** 64,000+ km round-trip annually — among the longest avian migrations documented. Geolocator studies have tracked individual birds covering 74,000 km in a single annual cycle [O1].

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Euphausiids (krill), small schooling fish (Pacific sardine, northern anchovy, Pacific sand lance), squid
- **Foraging strategy:** Pursuit-diving to depths of 40-68 m (documented maximum 68 m by time-depth recorders). Uses wings for underwater propulsion — essentially "flying" through the water column. Also surface-seizes prey and scavenges fish offal from fishing vessels. Forms dense feeding aggregations of thousands to tens of thousands at upwelling fronts. Foraging flocks are visible as dark, undulating ribbons over the ocean surface, often associated with whale and dolphin feeding activity.
- **Seasonal variation:** In PNW waters (non-breeding season), diet shifts toward euphausiids and anchovy as available; less squid than in Southern Hemisphere breeding waters.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (significant)
- **Estimate:** Global population estimated at 20 million individuals (BirdLife International). However, long-term monitoring at New Zealand breeding colonies has documented a ~40% decline in burrow occupancy since the 1970s. The species was uplisted from Least Concern to Near Threatened by IUCN in 2004 based on projected continued decline [O1].
- **PNW abundance:** Millions of individuals pass through PNW waters annually during the non-breeding season. Peak counts from pelagic surveys off Washington and Oregon regularly record 10,000-50,000+ birds per day during July-August. The species is by far the most numerically abundant bird in PNW offshore waters during summer [P21, P23].
- **Threats:**
  - Climate-driven prey shifts in both hemispheres (marine heat waves reduce euphausiid and forage fish abundance in the California Current; Southern Hemisphere warming affects breeding colony productivity)
  - Longline and trawl fishery bycatch (incidental drowning in fishing gear, particularly in Southern Hemisphere fisheries)
  - Plastic ingestion (stomach contents regularly contain plastic fragments)
  - Introduced predators at breeding colonies (rats, cats, stoats on island colonies)
  - Marine heat wave mortality — the 2014-2016 "blob" event in the NE Pacific coincided with reduced Sooty Shearwater abundance off Oregon and Washington [P25, G15]
- **Source:** BirdLife International, Cornell Lab [O1], Briggs et al. 1992 [P21], Wahl et al. 2005 [P23]

#### Vocalizations

- **At sea:** Generally silent on PNW waters. Occasional harsh, nasal "ka-ka-ka" calls in dense feeding aggregations.
- **Breeding colonies:** Loud, eerie wailing and cooing calls at night in breeding burrows (Southern Hemisphere only).

#### Evolutionary Note

Sooty Shearwaters belong to the recently reclassified genus *Ardenna*, separated from *Puffinus* based on molecular phylogenetics. The genus *Ardenna* includes the large-bodied shearwaters (Sooty, Short-tailed, Pink-footed, Flesh-footed, Great, Buller's) that form a well-supported monophyletic clade sister to the smaller *Puffinus* shearwaters. The trans-equatorial migration pattern — breeding in the Southern Hemisphere, non-breeding in the Northern Hemisphere — evolved independently in multiple seabird lineages and represents an exploitation of the six-month offset in peak marine productivity between hemispheres. Sooty Shearwaters are among the most extreme practitioners, crossing the equator twice annually and experiencing near-continuous summer conditions year-round [O1, O3].

### Pink-footed Shearwater (*Ardenna creatopus*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Procellariidae
**Status:** Transient (non-breeding visitor, May-October)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Vulnerable (VU) — breeding restricted to three island groups off Chile
- State WA: Not listed
- PIF: Not assessed (Southern Hemisphere breeder)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 48 cm
- **Wingspan:** 109 cm
- **Mass:** 700-850 g
- **Plumage:** Dark brownish-gray above, whitish below with variable dusky mottling on flanks and underwing. Pink bill with dark tip — diagnostic in the field. Pink feet (visible when banking in flight). Larger and paler-bellied than Sooty Shearwater.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Continental shelf and slope waters, 10-200 km offshore. Often closer to shore than Sooty Shearwater. Regularly seen from pelagic trips out of Westport, WA and Newport/Depoe Bay, OR.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Flyway:** Trans-equatorial, eastern Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds on Isla Mocha, Juan Fernandez Islands, and Isla Santa Clara off Chile (November-May). Migrates north through the eastern Pacific along the Humboldt/California Current corridor. Arrives PNW waters by May, departs by October-November.
- **Spring arrival:** May
- **Fall departure:** October-November
- **Distance:** ~15,000-20,000 km one-way
- **Staging areas:** Continental shelf off Washington and Oregon; Monterey Bay, CA

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Squid, anchovies, euphausiids, saury
- **Foraging strategy:** Surface-seizing and shallow pursuit-diving (shallower diver than Sooty Shearwater, typically <10 m). Readily follows fishing vessels for offal and discards.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining
- **Estimate:** Global population ~55,000-60,000 breeding pairs, restricted to three Chilean island groups. Vulnerability driven by extremely small breeding range.
- **Threats:** Introduced predators (rats, coatis) at breeding colonies; longline bycatch; climate-driven prey shifts in the Humboldt Current
- **Source:** BirdLife International, IUCN [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Pink-footed Shearwater is part of the *Ardenna* radiation of large Southern Hemisphere shearwaters. Its restricted breeding range on three Chilean island groups makes it one of the most range-restricted breeding seabirds in the Pacific, despite its wide non-breeding oceanic distribution. The species illustrates a pattern common in Procellariiformes: extremely concentrated, vulnerable breeding populations dispersing across vast oceanic non-breeding ranges [O1, O3].

### Buller's Shearwater (*Ardenna bulleri*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Procellariidae
**Status:** Transient (non-breeding visitor, August-November)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Vulnerable (VU) — breeds only at Poor Knights Islands, New Zealand
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 46 cm
- **Wingspan:** 97 cm
- **Mass:** 340-425 g
- **Plumage:** Distinctive and elegant: clean gray above with a bold dark M-pattern across the upperwings (visible in flight), white below. Dark cap, pale forehead. Gray bill with dark tip. The most cleanly patterned of the large shearwaters.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Offshore pelagic waters, typically beyond the continental shelf. Less common than Sooty or Pink-footed Shearwaters but regular in fall.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Flyway:** Trans-equatorial, western-to-eastern Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds exclusively on Poor Knights Islands, New Zealand (October-April). Post-breeding dispersal across the Pacific to North American west coast. Arrives PNW later than other shearwaters (August), peaks September-October.
- **Distance:** ~20,000+ km one-way across the Pacific
- **Staging areas:** Offshore waters of Washington, Oregon, California; pelagic, not associated with nearshore features

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to increasing (colony rat eradication successful)
- **Estimate:** ~2.5 million individuals; breeds at a single island group (Poor Knights Islands, New Zealand) where predator removal has allowed recovery
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Buller's Shearwater breeds at a single location on Earth — the Poor Knights Islands off northeastern New Zealand — making it perhaps the most extreme example of breeding-range restriction among abundant seabirds. The entire global population funnels through one archipelago for reproduction, then disperses across the entire North Pacific. Successful rat eradication on the Poor Knights Islands represents one of the great seabird conservation success stories [O1].

### Short-tailed Shearwater (*Ardenna tenuirostris*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Procellariidae
**Status:** Transient (non-breeding visitor, September-November, uncommon)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 41-43 cm
- **Wingspan:** 95-100 cm
- **Mass:** 500-800 g
- **Plumage:** Very similar to Sooty Shearwater — uniformly dark brown above and below. Differs subtly in shorter bill, more rounded head profile, and typically less extensive silvery underwing panel (often restricted to inner wing). Field separation from Sooty Shearwater is among the most challenging identification problems in PNW pelagic birding.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Offshore pelagic waters, primarily far offshore. Less common than Sooty Shearwater in PNW; more abundant farther north in the Gulf of Alaska and Bering Sea.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Flyway:** Trans-equatorial, western Pacific primary route
- **Route:** Breeds in massive colonies in southeastern Australia (Bass Strait islands, Tasmania) during austral summer. Post-breeding migration follows the western Pacific north to the Bering Sea, then loops south through the eastern Pacific. PNW birds are primarily southbound migrants in fall.
- **Spring arrival:** Rare before September in PNW; main passage October-November
- **Fall departure:** November-December
- **Distance:** 30,000-50,000 km annual circuit
- **Breeding grounds:** Australia (Tasmania, Victoria islands) — some colonies exceed 1 million pairs

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to possibly declining (hard to assess due to colony size)
- **Estimate:** 23 million individuals globally — one of the most abundant seabirds on Earth
- **Threats:** Climate-driven prey shifts in the Southern Ocean; historically harvested as "muttonbirds" by Aboriginal Tasmanians and European settlers (regulated harvest continues in Tasmania)
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Short-tailed Shearwater is the closest relative of Sooty Shearwater; the two species are nearly identical in plumage and were long considered conspecific. Molecular phylogenetics confirms them as sister species that diverged approximately 5-8 million years ago, with Short-tailed Shearwater retaining the ancestral Australian breeding range while Sooty Shearwater colonized New Zealand and the sub-Antarctic. Their non-breeding ranges overlap in the North Pacific but are offset in timing and route, reducing direct competition — an example of temporal niche partitioning in closely related pelagic species [O1, O3].

### Northern Fulmar (*Fulmarus glacialis*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Procellariidae
**Status:** Non-breeding visitor / Offshore resident (year-round presence, peak winter)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 45-50 cm
- **Wingspan:** 102-112 cm
- **Mass:** 450-1,000 g (highly variable seasonally)
- **Plumage:** Polymorphic. Light morph: pale gray above, white below and head, resembling a gull at distance but distinguished by tube-nose bill, stiff-winged flight, and lack of black wingtips. Dark morph: uniformly gray-brown, similar to shearwater silhouette. PNW birds are predominantly light morph with increasing proportion of dark morphs in more northerly populations. Bull-necked, stocky build with thick tube-nose bill (yellow-green with darker tip).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal; males average slightly larger.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Offshore pelagic waters, continental shelf and beyond. Year-round presence but more numerous October-March. Occasionally seen from shore during storms. Regularly attracted to fishing vessels for offal.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Flyway:** North Pacific dispersal (not a classic long-distance migrant)
- **Route:** Breeds on Arctic and subarctic islands (Aleutians, Pribilofs, Bering Sea islands, Arctic Canada, Iceland, Norway). Non-breeding birds and post-breeding dispersers reach PNW waters year-round, with peak numbers in winter. Juveniles may spend 4-6 years at sea before returning to breeding colonies.
- **Staging areas:** Offshore waters along the continental shelf edge; concentrates at upwelling fronts and fishing vessel operations
- **Breeding grounds:** Nearest colonies: Aleutian Islands, coastal Alaska
- **Distance:** Variable dispersal; PNW birds may originate from colonies 3,000-5,000 km to the north

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Fish, squid, crustaceans, jellyfish, carrion, fishing vessel offal and discards
- **Foraging strategy:** Surface-seizing, shallow plunge-diving, scavenging. One of the most omnivorous tube-nosed seabirds. Stomach oil ejection as a defensive weapon — fulmars can accurately project a stream of foul-smelling, sticky oil at predators or competitors from 1-2 meters, permanently matting the feathers of the recipient and potentially causing hypothermia in other seabirds.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to increasing in the North Pacific; massive range expansion in the North Atlantic over the past 200 years
- **Estimate:** 15-20 million individuals globally; North Pacific population (subspecies *F. g. rodgersii*) estimated at 4 million+
- **Threats:** Plastic ingestion (fulmars are a primary bioindicator species for marine plastic pollution in the North Atlantic and increasingly studied in the Pacific); longline bycatch
- **Source:** Hatch & Nettleship 1998 [P26], BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Northern Fulmar is one of only two species in the genus *Fulmarus* (the other being the Southern Fulmar, *F. glacialis* of Antarctic waters — though some authorities split the Southern Fulmar as *F. glacialoides*). Fulmars are phylogenetically ancient within Procellariidae, sitting near the base of the family tree and retaining "primitive" features including the stomach oil defense mechanism shared with petrels but lost in the more derived shearwater lineage. The species' dramatic population expansion in the North Atlantic over the past 200 years — from a single Icelandic colony to occupying coastlines across Britain, Scandinavia, and eastern Canada — is one of the most thoroughly documented range expansions of any seabird, likely driven by increased food availability from commercial fishing discards [P26, O1].

### Black-footed Albatross (*Phoebastria nigripes*) — EXTENDED PROFILE

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Diomedeidae
**Status:** Non-breeding visitor (regular offshore, year-round with peak May-September)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (Candidate species review completed; not warranted)
- IUCN: Near Threatened (NT)
- State WA: Not listed (marine, pelagic)
- PIF: Not assessed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 68-74 cm
- **Wingspan:** 190-220 cm (over 7 feet)
- **Mass:** 2,600-3,600 g (males average larger)
- **Plumage:** Overall dark brown-black, paler on face and at base of bill. White at base of tail (variable, more extensive in older birds). Massive dark bill with tubular nasal passages. Long, narrow wings built for dynamic soaring. Older adults develop more extensive pale facial plumage — "white face" indicative of birds 10+ years old. Dark feet (diagnostic — no other regular North Pacific albatross has dark feet).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males average 10-15% heavier; otherwise similar in the field.

#### Habitat

- **PNW occurrence:** Pelagic waters from the continental shelf edge outward, typically 30-200+ km offshore. The most frequently encountered albatross in PNW waters. Regular on pelagic birding trips out of Westport, WA, where trips to the shelf edge (~45 km) encounter Black-footed Albatross on roughly 80% of summer outings. Occasionally recorded from shore during strong onshore winds.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — exclusively marine
- **Ecoregion secondary:** None
- **Microhabitat:** Forages along upwelling fronts, temperature breaks, and convergence zones where prey concentrates at the surface. Associates with fishing vessels (historically and currently). Rests on the water surface in calm conditions; seldom observed in flight during calm weather (requires wind for dynamic soaring).

#### Migration

- **Flyway:** North Pacific basin-wide dispersal
- **Route description:** Not a classic latitudinal migrant. Breeds on northwestern Hawaiian Islands (Midway Atoll, Laysan Island, French Frigate Shoals, and other atolls) from November through July. Non-breeding birds, failed breeders, and immatures range throughout the North Pacific. Post-breeding adults disperse north and east to productive waters along the California Current, Gulf of Alaska, and Aleutian Arc. Geolocator and satellite tracking studies show individual birds covering the entire North Pacific basin in a single non-breeding season, with some birds making repeated crossings [P24].
- **Peak PNW presence:** May-September, when post-breeding adults and non-breeding immatures concentrate on California Current upwelling productivity
- **Year-round presence:** Non-breeding immatures (1-5 years old) may occur in PNW waters year-round; they do not return to Hawaiian breeding colonies until age 5-7
- **Breeding grounds:** Northwestern Hawaiian Islands — primarily Midway Atoll (largest colony, ~22,000 pairs), Laysan Island (~21,000 pairs), French Frigate Shoals. Also Torishima and Bonin Islands (Japan) in small numbers.
- **Distance:** Basin-wide; individuals may cover 50,000-80,000 km per year of at-sea movement

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Squid (primary prey — surface-layer cephalopods), flying fish eggs (a major food source found floating at the surface), crustaceans, fish
- **Foraging strategy:** Surface-seizing while sitting on the water. Does not dive (unlike shearwaters). Uses dynamic soaring — exploiting wind speed differentials between wave troughs and crests to travel enormous distances with minimal energy expenditure. Foraging range from breeding colony: tracked birds travel 1,000-3,000 km from Midway on single foraging trips during chick-rearing [P24]. Strongly attracted to fishing vessels; historically consumed large quantities of squid offal and bait from longline operations.
- **Seasonal variation:** In PNW waters, exploits summer upwelling productivity; squid and flying fish eggs dominant. Scavenging from fishing vessels contributes substantially to diet.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slowly increasing (recovery from historical harvest)
- **Estimate:** ~67,000 breeding pairs (approximately 155,000 total individuals including non-breeders). Population concentrated on Midway Atoll and Laysan Island within Papahanaumokuakea Marine National Monument [G1].
- **Threats:**
  - Longline fishery bycatch — historically the primary threat; hook-and-line mortality during fishing operations. Mitigation measures (streamer lines, weighted hooks, night setting) have reduced bycatch in some fleets but remain a concern in international waters across the North Pacific [G1]
  - Plastic ingestion — Black-footed Albatross adults feed plastic debris to chicks, which cannot regurgitate it. Studies at Midway Atoll have documented plastic in >95% of chick boluses. Plastic load correlates with reduced chick body condition but causal mortality link is complex [G1, O1]
  - Sea-level rise — low-lying nesting atolls (Midway, Laysan, French Frigate Shoals) are vulnerable to inundation. Storm surge from tropical cyclones and king tides already floods nesting areas; projected sea-level rise threatens the physical existence of primary breeding habitat over the next century [G15]
  - Lead paint contamination — historical military infrastructure on Midway Atoll (former naval air station) has created lead-contaminated soil at nesting sites; chick lead poisoning documented. USFWS remediation efforts ongoing.
  - Marine heat waves — the 2014-2016 NE Pacific "blob" event reduced prey availability in the California Current, potentially affecting non-breeding season body condition [P25]
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], Adams et al. 2012 [P24], BirdLife International [O1]

#### Vocalizations

- **At sea:** Largely silent. Occasional braying or groaning calls when competing for food near fishing vessels.
- **Breeding colonies:** Elaborate courtship displays with clapping, whinnying, bill-clapping, and sky-pointing calls (Hawaiian colonies only).

#### Evolutionary Note

Black-footed Albatross belongs to the North Pacific albatross genus *Phoebastria*, which includes four species: Black-footed, Laysan (*P. immutabilis*), Short-tailed (*P. albatrus*), and Waved (*P. irrorata*, Galapagos). This genus is phylogenetically distinct from the Southern Ocean great albatrosses (*Diomedea*) and the mollymawks (*Thalassarche*), having diverged approximately 10-15 million years ago when albatross lineages colonized the North Pacific from the Southern Hemisphere. The North Pacific albatrosses represent a separate evolutionary experiment in albatross ecology — smaller-bodied and darker-plumaged than their Southern Hemisphere relatives, adapted to the warmer, less wind-driven waters of the subtropical and temperate North Pacific. Black-footed and Laysan Albatrosses breed sympatrically on the same Hawaiian atolls but are partitioned ecologically: Black-footed forages primarily along the productive eastern Pacific boundary (California Current), while Laysan forages in the more oligotrophic central North Pacific — a spatial niche separation maintained over millions of years [O1, O3, P24].

The cultural significance of albatrosses to Pacific maritime peoples is profound. The Makah people of the Olympic Peninsula, whose ancestors hunted marine mammals and pelagic birds from ocean-going canoes, encountered albatrosses on offshore foraging expeditions. Coast Salish maritime traditions recognized the great seabirds as indicators of productive ocean conditions — the appearance of albatrosses signaled convergence zones where marine resources concentrated [C1, C4].

### Laysan Albatross (*Phoebastria immutabilis*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Diomedeidae
**Status:** Vagrant (rare, offshore)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Near Threatened (NT)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 79-81 cm
- **Wingspan:** 195-215 cm
- **Mass:** 2,400-3,600 g
- **Plumage:** Strikingly different from Black-footed: white head, neck, and underparts; dark brown-gray upperwings and back; dark tail. Pink bill with gray tip. White rump. Resembles a smaller version of a Southern Ocean mollymawk.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Rare offshore visitor. Far less frequent than Black-footed Albatross in PNW waters. Most PNW records are from pelagic trips >50 km offshore, primarily in summer and fall. The species' primary non-breeding range is in the central North Pacific rather than the California Current.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Breeding grounds:** Northwestern Hawaiian Islands (Midway Atoll largest colony — ~490,000 pairs, making it the most numerous albatross at Midway); recently established small colonies in Mexico (Guadalupe Island) and Japan.
- **Distance:** Basin-wide dispersal; individuals may range from Hawaii to the Bering Sea and across to Japan

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable; large global population (~1.6 million breeding adults)
- **Threats:** Sea-level rise (same atoll vulnerability as Black-footed); plastic ingestion (severe); bycatch
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Laysan Albatross is the sister species of Black-footed Albatross. Despite breeding on the same atolls (and even adjacent nest sites), the two species maintain reproductive isolation through dramatic plumage divergence and display behavior differences — one of the clearest examples of sympatric species maintenance through sexual selection in seabirds. Occasional hybridization occurs (mixed pairs documented at Midway) but at extremely low frequency, confirming the effectiveness of pre-mating barriers [O1, O3].

### Short-tailed Albatross (*Phoebastria albatrus*) — EXTENDED PROFILE

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Diomedeidae
**Status:** Vagrant / Extremely rare visitor (offshore)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: **Endangered** (listed 2000)
- IUCN: Vulnerable (VU)
- State WA: Endangered
- State OR: Not listed (marine, pelagic — defers to federal ESA)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 84-94 cm (largest North Pacific albatross)
- **Wingspan:** 215-230 cm (over 7.5 feet)
- **Mass:** 4,300-8,500 g (males substantially larger than females)
- **Plumage:** Adult: spectacular — white body and head with golden-yellow wash on crown and nape, dark brown-black wings, white back, pink bill with bluish tip. The only North Pacific albatross with a predominantly white body in adult plumage. Immature: entirely dark chocolate-brown, lightening progressively over 10-15 years through a complex sequence of intermediate plumages. Young immatures can be confused with Black-footed Albatross but are substantially larger, with a diagnostic pink bill at all ages.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males 15-25% heavier; otherwise similar in plumage.

#### Habitat

- **PNW occurrence:** Extremely rare but increasing as the global population recovers. Most PNW records are from offshore waters of the continental shelf edge, 50-200+ km offshore. Documented from pelagic surveys off Washington and Oregon, and increasingly from satellite-tagged birds tracked from breeding colonies to PNW waters. Historical records from the late 1800s indicate the species was formerly more common in PNW offshore waters before near-extinction from feather harvesting.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — exclusively marine in PNW
- **Note:** Safety boundary: No specific location data provided for PNW sightings. Records maintained by WDFW and ODFW pelagic monitoring programs.

#### Migration

- **Flyway:** North Pacific basin-wide
- **Route description:** Breeds on Torishima Island (Izu Islands, Japan — primary colony, ~900 pairs) and Minami-kojima (Senkaku Islands, ~300 pairs) during the boreal winter (October-May). Post-breeding adults and immatures disperse across the North Pacific, with a strong affinity for the productive waters of the California Current, Gulf of Alaska, Aleutian Arc, and Bering Sea. Satellite tracking has documented individual birds visiting PNW continental shelf waters during post-breeding dispersal.
- **Peak PNW occurrence:** Summer-fall (June-October), when post-breeding birds range across the North Pacific
- **Breeding grounds:** Torishima Island, Japan (primary — volcanic island managed as a nature reserve); Minami-kojima, Senkaku Islands (secondary); new colony developing at Midway Atoll from translocated chicks (USFWS/Yamashina Institute program)
- **Distance:** Trans-Pacific; individuals cover 20,000-50,000+ km per year

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Squid, shrimp, fish, flying fish eggs
- **Foraging strategy:** Surface-seizing while sitting on the water; dynamic soaring for transit. Attracted to fishing vessels. Historically consumed large quantities of offal from whaling operations (19th century) and longline fisheries (20th-21st century).

#### Population

- **Trend:** Increasing (strong recovery)
- **Estimate:** Approximately 7,300 individuals as of 2022 (USFWS), recovering from a population nadir of approximately 10 breeding pairs in the 1950s. The species was driven to the brink of extinction by Japanese feather hunters who killed an estimated 5 million birds on their breeding islands between 1885 and 1933. Protection of Torishima breeding colony by Japanese ornithologist Hiroshi Hasegawa beginning in 1954, followed by habitat management (vegetation planting to stabilize nesting substrate on the volcanic island), has enabled a slow but steady recovery at approximately 7-8% annual population growth [G1].
- **Threats:**
  - Volcanic eruption at Torishima — the primary breeding colony sits on an active volcano. An eruption could eliminate the majority of the global breeding population in a single event. The USFWS/Yamashina Institute chick translocation program to Midway Atoll and Mukojima Island (Bonin Islands, Japan) is specifically designed to establish backup colonies on non-volcanic islands.
  - Longline fishery bycatch — the species is incidentally caught on longline hooks in the North Pacific, particularly in Alaskan waters. USFWS and NMFS have implemented Short-tailed Albatross-specific bycatch reduction measures in the Alaska groundfish fishery.
  - Small population size — genetic diversity is extremely low, consistent with a severe population bottleneck. Inbreeding depression is a potential concern as the population recovers.
  - Climate change — prey shifts and sea-level rise affecting Midway Atoll translocation colony
- **Source:** USFWS Short-tailed Albatross Recovery Plan [G1], BirdLife International [O1]

#### Vocalizations

- **At sea:** Silent.
- **Breeding colonies:** Elaborate courtship displays with bill-clapping, whistling calls, and mutual preening (Torishima colony observations).

#### Evolutionary Note

Short-tailed Albatross is the only member of the genus *Phoebastria* to achieve the large body size and predominantly white adult plumage reminiscent of the great albatrosses (*Diomedea*) of the Southern Hemisphere. Molecular phylogenetics confirms it is nested within *Phoebastria* (sister to the Waved Albatross of the Galapagos, *P. irrorata*), meaning its large size and white plumage evolved convergently with *Diomedea* rather than through shared ancestry. This convergence suggests similar selective pressures — perhaps related to sexual selection, thermoregulation at sea, or species recognition — driving parallel plumage evolution in widely separated albatross lineages.

The species' near-extinction and recovery is one of conservation biology's most dramatic narratives. The population bottleneck of ~10 pairs in the 1950s represents a genetic catastrophe — the current global population descended from a tiny handful of survivors. Population genetic studies have confirmed extremely low heterozygosity compared to the abundant Laysan and Black-footed Albatrosses. Despite this genetic impoverishment, the population is growing steadily, suggesting that the species has not yet encountered the fitness consequences (inbreeding depression) that genetic theory predicts for such extreme bottlenecks — or that those consequences may emerge at higher population densities as the most deleterious recessive alleles are exposed [G1, O1, O3].

## II. HYDROBATIDAE — Storm-Petrels

Storm-petrels are the smallest tube-nosed seabirds — sparrow-sized birds that spend their lives on the open ocean, fluttering over wave troughs and pattering their feet on the water surface while picking up tiny prey items. Two species breed in the PNW.

---

### Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel (*Hydrobates furcatus*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Hydrobatidae
**Status:** Breeding resident / Year-round (pelagic)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 20-23 cm
- **Wingspan:** 46 cm
- **Mass:** 45-65 g
- **Plumage:** Pale blue-gray overall — uniquely pale among storm-petrels. Darker flight feathers create subtle M-pattern on upperwings. Dark smudge around eye. Forked tail (diagnostic in genus). The palest storm-petrel in the North Pacific.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Breeds on offshore islands along the outer coast (WA, OR, BC). Nests in burrows on forested or grassy islands free of mammalian predators. At sea, forages over continental shelf and slope waters. Comes ashore only at night to avoid gull and raptor predation.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Breeding colonies:** Carroll Island, Alexander Island, and other offshore rocks (WA); Goat Island, other islands (OR); Triangle Island, Storm Islands (BC). Colony locations generalized per safety boundaries.
- **Migration:** Partial migrant. Some PNW-breeding birds remain in regional waters year-round; others disperse south to California waters in winter. Less migratory than Leach's Storm-Petrel.
- **Distance:** Regional dispersal, typically <3,000 km

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small crustaceans (euphausiids, amphipods, copepods), small fish, squid, jellyfish
- **Foraging strategy:** Patters feet on water surface while hovering (foot-pattering — a behavior unique to storm-petrels). Picks tiny prey from the surface film. Forages singly or in loose aggregations, often at upwelling fronts and temperature breaks.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Burrow (dug in soil on offshore islands)
- **Clutch size:** 1 egg
- **Incubation:** 37-68 days (extremely variable; both parents incubate in shifts)
- **Fledging:** 60-68 days
- **Breeding season:** April-September (PNW)
- **Note:** Extended incubation shifts — one parent may incubate for 5-14 days while the other forages at sea. Chick abandonment by parents before fledging; chick fledges alone at night.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (but poorly monitored due to nocturnal, burrow-nesting habits)
- **Estimate:** 6 million+ individuals globally; PNW population poorly quantified but likely tens of thousands of breeding pairs across WA, OR, BC colonies
- **Threats:** Introduced predators at colony islands (rats, raccoons); light pollution attracting fledglings to land; at-sea bycatch in gill nets
- **Source:** USFWS [G1], WDFW [G9], Cornell Lab [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel was recently reclassified from the family Oceanodromidae into Hydrobatidae following molecular phylogenetic revision of storm-petrel families. The Northern Hemisphere storm-petrels (Hydrobatidae) are not closely related to the Southern Hemisphere storm-petrels (Oceanitidae) — the two groups evolved their small size, foot-pattering foraging behavior, and oceanic lifestyle convergently. Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel's pale blue-gray plumage is unusual among storm-petrels (most are dark) and may function as camouflage against the gray North Pacific sea surface when viewed from above by predators [O1, O3].

---

### Leach's Storm-Petrel (*Hydrobates leucorhous*)

**Order:** Procellariiformes | **Family:** Hydrobatidae
**Status:** Breeding resident (colonies) / Pelagic
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Vulnerable (VU) — global decline due to colony predation and climate effects
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Sensitive

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 19-22 cm
- **Wingspan:** 45-48 cm
- **Mass:** 40-50 g
- **Plumage:** Blackish-brown overall with distinctive white rump band (U-shaped or V-shaped). Pale gray wing bar across upper secondary coverts. Forked tail. Dark bill. Flight is erratic and nighthawk-like — bounding, changing direction unpredictably (contrasts with the more direct flight of Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel).

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Breeds on offshore islands along the outer coast. Pelagic outside the breeding season, ranging widely across the North Pacific. More migratory than Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel — most PNW breeding birds depart south in winter.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Breeding colonies:** Offshore islands of WA, OR, BC (colony locations generalized per safety boundaries). Largest PNW colonies in British Columbia.
- **Migration:** Transoceanic; winter range extends to equatorial Pacific and beyond. PNW breeding birds depart September-November, return April-May.
- **Distance:** 10,000-20,000+ km annual migration

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small fish, crustaceans, squid, oily prey fragments at the water surface
- **Foraging strategy:** Foot-pattering and surface-seizing, similar to Fork-tailed but with more erratic hovering flight. Uses olfaction to locate prey patches (tube-nose enables detection of dimethyl sulfide released by phytoplankton grazed by zooplankton, effectively "smelling" productive water) [P27].

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Burrow or rock crevice on offshore islands
- **Clutch size:** 1 egg
- **Incubation:** 38-46 days
- **Fledging:** 56-70 days
- **Breeding season:** May-October (PNW)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (significant global decline)
- **Estimate:** 12-15 million individuals globally, but steep declines documented at monitored Atlantic colonies and some Pacific colonies. Colony declines driven by introduced predators and climate-related prey shifts.
- **Threats:** Introduced predators (rats, raccoons, cats) at colony islands; light pollution (attracted to artificial light during fledging flights — mass mortality events documented at coastal light sources); marine prey shifts
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1], Ainley 1995 [P27]

#### Evolutionary Note

Leach's Storm-Petrel is a cosmopolitan species complex currently undergoing taxonomic revision. Populations in different ocean basins show substantial genetic divergence, and multiple subspecies have been described (some now split as separate species in certain authorities). The Atlantic population (*H. l. leucorhous*) and Pacific populations may represent incipient species. The PNW population belongs to the Pacific lineage and is genetically distinct from Atlantic birds. The species' use of olfaction for navigation and prey detection is among the most sophisticated in birds — experiments have shown Leach's Storm-Petrels can navigate to their nesting burrow on a dark, foggy island using smell alone [O1, O3, P27].

## III. STERCORARIIDAE — Jaegers and Skuas

The jaegers and skuas are pursuit-pirates and predators of the open ocean. Powerful, aggressive, and aerobatically gifted, they specialize in kleptoparasitism — chasing other seabirds until they disgorge their catch, then seizing the stolen food in mid-air. All four species recorded in the PNW are passage migrants or rare visitors, using PNW offshore waters as a corridor between Arctic breeding grounds and Southern Hemisphere wintering areas.

---

### Pomarine Jaeger (*Stercorarius pomarinus*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Stercorariidae
**Status:** Transient (migration, April-May and August-November)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 46-51 cm (body); 55-67 cm including elongated central tail feathers
- **Wingspan:** 110-127 cm
- **Mass:** 550-900 g (largest jaeger)
- **Plumage:** Polymorphic: light morph (dark cap, pale underparts with dusky breast band, yellow neck hackles in breeding plumage) and dark morph (uniformly dark brown-black). Adults in breeding plumage have distinctive spoon-shaped or twisted elongated central tail feathers — unique among jaegers. Immatures and non-breeding adults confusingly variable with heavy brown barring. White wing flash at base of primaries in all plumages.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Offshore pelagic waters, primarily during spring and fall migration. The largest and most powerful of the three regular jaegers. Less frequently seen from shore than Parasitic Jaeger.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic)
- **Flyway:** Pacific, offshore
- **Route:** Breeds on high Arctic tundra (Alaska North Slope, Yukon, Siberia). Southbound migration takes a primarily offshore route through PNW waters, continuing to wintering areas in the tropical and southern Pacific.
- **Spring passage:** April-May (northbound, primarily far offshore)
- **Fall passage:** August-November (peak September-October)
- **Distance:** 15,000-25,000 km one-way
- **Staging areas:** None documented in PNW; passes through without significant staging

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** On breeding grounds: lemmings and voles (primary prey — breeding success tied to lemming cycles, like Snowy Owl). In migration and winter: kleptoparasitism of gulls, terns, and shearwaters; also takes fish directly and scavenges.
- **Foraging strategy:** Powerful aerial pursuit of other seabirds. Will chase birds up to the size of Herring Gull. Also kills small seabirds directly (storm-petrels, small alcids).

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (fluctuates with lemming cycles)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~100,000-500,000 individuals
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

The jaegers (genus *Stercorarius*) are phylogenetically nested within the order Charadriiformes, closely related to gulls (Laridae). Despite their predatory/piratical lifestyle, they are not raptors — their hooked bill and aggressive behavior evolved independently from hawks and falcons. Pomarine Jaeger is the most marine of the three regular jaegers, spending the non-breeding season entirely at sea, and its breeding ecology is uniquely tied to lemming population cycles — in years of lemming collapse, Pomarine Jaegers may not breed at all [O1, O3].

---

### Parasitic Jaeger (*Stercorarius parasiticus*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Stercorariidae
**Status:** Transient (migration, April-May and July-October)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 41-46 cm (body); 46-67 cm including elongated tail
- **Wingspan:** 107-125 cm
- **Mass:** 330-570 g
- **Plumage:** Polymorphic (light, intermediate, dark morphs). Light morph: dark cap, white neck collar, pale underparts. Breeding adults have elongated, pointed central tail feathers (not twisted/spoon-shaped like Pomarine). White wing flash at primary bases. Most variable in plumage of the three jaegers.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** The most frequently seen jaeger from shore in the PNW. Migrates closer to the coast than Pomarine or Long-tailed, regularly harassing gulls and terns within sight of land. Outer beaches (Ocean Shores, Westport, Long Beach, WA; Cape Meares, Yaquina Head, OR) are reliable observation points during fall migration.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic), occasionally Zone 5 (estuarine — chasing terns at river mouths)
- **Flyway:** Pacific, nearshore to offshore
- **Route:** Breeds on Arctic and subarctic tundra (Alaska, northern Canada, Siberia). Southbound migration through PNW coastal and offshore waters; continues to tropical and southern Pacific and Atlantic. Some birds continue to the Southern Ocean.
- **Spring passage:** April-May (adults, mostly offshore)
- **Fall passage:** July-October (peak August-September). Adults migrate first (July-August), juveniles later (September-October).
- **Distance:** 10,000-20,000+ km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Breeding: lemmings, bird eggs, chicks, insects. Migration/winter: kleptoparasitism of terns, gulls, and small shearwaters; also takes fish directly.
- **Foraging strategy:** Fast, agile aerial pursuit. Typically targets Bonaparte's Gulls, Common Terns, and Arctic Terns in PNW waters. Chases victim in sustained aerial pursuit until the bird disgorges its prey, then swoops to catch the falling food before it hits the water.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to possibly declining (poorly monitored at sea)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~500,000-1,000,000 individuals (the most numerous jaeger globally)
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Parasitic Jaeger is the most generalist of the jaegers, equally adept at kleptoparasitism, direct predation, and carrion scavenging. Its close association with nearshore waters during migration — unlike the more pelagic Pomarine or the more Arctic Long-tailed — suggests a foraging niche partitioned by distance from shore among the three regularly co-occurring jaeger species [O1, O3].

---

### Long-tailed Jaeger (*Stercorarius longicaudus*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Stercorariidae
**Status:** Transient (migration, May and August-October; rare)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 38-41 cm (body); 48-58 cm including extremely elongated central tail streamers
- **Wingspan:** 105-117 cm
- **Mass:** 240-350 g (smallest jaeger — only slightly larger than a medium gull)
- **Plumage:** The most elegantly proportioned jaeger. Light morph predominates (dark morphs rare, <5%). Breeding adult: clean gray-brown above, white breast sharply demarcated from dark belly, very long (15-25 cm) flexible central tail streamers. Dark cap. Lacks the heavy, barrel-chested build of Pomarine. Immatures gray-brown with minimal barring.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** The rarest of the three regular jaegers in PNW waters. Typically far offshore during migration; seldom seen from shore. Pelagic trips in fall occasionally encounter individuals.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — exclusively offshore in PNW
- **Flyway:** Pacific, far offshore
- **Route:** Breeds on high Arctic tundra (Alaska, northern Canada, Scandinavia, Siberia). Migrates primarily offshore through the Pacific; major wintering areas in the Southern Ocean (reaching Antarctic pack-ice edge). Long-tailed Jaeger is the most pelagic of the three jaegers in the non-breeding season.
- **Fall passage:** August-October (peak September)
- **Distance:** 15,000-30,000+ km one-way (among the longest jaeger migrations)

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Breeding: primarily lemmings and voles (most lemming-dependent of all jaegers — typically does not breed in lemming-crash years). Migration/winter: kleptoparasitism of small seabirds, but also takes small fish and invertebrates directly.
- **Foraging strategy:** More delicate and buoyant in flight than other jaegers. Kleptoparasitic, but targets smaller victims (storm-petrels, small terns). Also feeds on floating organic matter and small invertebrates at the ocean surface during the non-breeding season.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (tied to lemming cycles)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~100,000-500,000 individuals
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Long-tailed Jaeger is the most phylogenetically derived (recently evolved) of the three jaegers, and the most extreme specialist — its breeding success is almost entirely dependent on lemming abundance. In lemming-crash years, entire breeding populations may forego nesting. This boom-bust reproductive strategy is an adaptation to the unpredictable Arctic prey base, and it is shared (convergently) with Snowy Owl, another species in this document [O1, O3].

---

### South Polar Skua (*Stercorarius maccormicki*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Stercorariidae
**Status:** Vagrant / Rare transient (September-November)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 50-55 cm
- **Wingspan:** 130-140 cm
- **Mass:** 900-1,600 g
- **Plumage:** Heavy-bodied, gull-like but with dark plumage. Variable: light morph (pale nape and underparts contrasting with dark wings) and dark morph (uniformly dark brown). Large white wing flash at base of primaries (visible from great distance). Heavy, hooked bill. Powerful, direct flight on broad wings.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Rare fall visitor to offshore waters. Breeds in Antarctica and migrates north through the Pacific during the austral winter. Most PNW records are from pelagic trips September-November, far offshore.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — far offshore
- **Flyway:** Trans-equatorial, western/central Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds on Antarctic continent and Antarctic Peninsula. Migrates north across the equator to North Pacific, North Atlantic, and Indian Ocean during austral winter (May-October). Returns south by November. One of the few species to breed on the Antarctic continent itself.
- **Distance:** 20,000-30,000+ km one-way (Antarctica to North Pacific)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable
- **Estimate:** ~10,000-20,000 breeding pairs globally
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

South Polar Skua breeds farther south than almost any other bird — on the Antarctic continent itself, sometimes within sight of the South Pole. Its trans-equatorial migration to the North Pacific means that PNW observers encountering this species are seeing a bird that winters in the land of the midnight sun and summers in the land of the polar night. The species' phylogenetic relationship to the northern jaegers is debated: molecular data place skuas (*S. maccormicki* and Great Skua *S. skua*) as sister to the jaegers, not within them, suggesting the piratical lifestyle evolved once in the common ancestor [O1, O3].

## IV. GAVIIFORMES — Loons

Loons are heavy-bodied diving birds of northern lakes (breeding) and coastal marine waters (wintering). Three species winter regularly in PNW marine waters, where they are characteristic members of the Salish Sea and outer coast winter community.

---

### Pacific Loon (*Gavia pacifica*)

**Order:** Gaviiformes | **Family:** Gaviidae
**Status:** Wintering (October-May)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 58-74 cm
- **Wingspan:** 110-128 cm
- **Mass:** 1,000-2,500 g
- **Plumage:** Breeding (seen in spring before departure): clean gray nape and crown, black throat patch with white striping, dark back with neat white checkering. Non-breeding (typical PNW appearance): dark gray-brown above, white below with sharp chin-strap boundary. Smaller and more slender than Common Loon, with a straighter, more delicate bill.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males slightly larger; plumage similar.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Abundant wintering bird in protected marine waters. The most numerous loon in the Salish Sea during winter. Found in open channels, bays, and nearshore waters of Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, Strait of Juan de Fuca, and the outer coast.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — primarily protected marine waters
- **Flyway:** Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds on tundra lakes and ponds across Arctic and subarctic Alaska and northern Canada. Southbound migration through interior British Columbia and along the coast to PNW wintering waters. Impressive spring migration visible from shore: large flocks of hundreds move north along the outer coast in April-May, with major passage points at Cape Flattery, WA and Tofino, BC.
- **Arrival:** October-November
- **Departure:** April-May
- **Breeding grounds:** Arctic and subarctic lakes (Alaska, northern Canada, rarely Siberia)
- **Distance:** 3,000-5,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small fish (Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance, shiner perch, juvenile salmonids), crustaceans
- **Foraging strategy:** Pursuit-diving powered by feet (not wings). Dives to 10-40 m. Typically forages in open water, often in loose aggregations that move together as a front.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable
- **Estimate:** ~1.5-2 million individuals globally. PNW wintering population: 10,000-20,000+ in the Salish Sea system [G9, O2].
- **Threats:** Oil spill vulnerability (marine wintering habitat intersects shipping lanes); prey base shifts from climate warming; disturbance from vessel traffic in Salish Sea
- **Source:** WDFW [G9], Cornell Lab [O1], eBird [O2]

#### Evolutionary Note

Pacific Loon was split from Arctic Loon (*Gavia arctica*) in 1985 based on differences in plumage, vocalizations, and breeding ecology. The two species breed sympatrically in western Alaska, where hybridization occurs at low frequency — indicating recent divergence (estimated 2-4 million years). Pacific Loon's breeding range is primarily Nearctic (North America), while Arctic Loon is primarily Palearctic (Eurasia), a classic Beringian vicariance pattern [O1, O3].

### Red-throated Loon (*Gavia stellata*)

**Order:** Gaviiformes | **Family:** Gaviidae
**Status:** Wintering (October-May)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 53-69 cm
- **Wingspan:** 91-120 cm
- **Mass:** 1,000-2,500 g (smallest loon)
- **Plumage:** Non-breeding (typical PNW appearance): pale gray-brown above with fine white speckling, white below, white face and front of neck. Slender, slightly upturned bill (unique among loons — all others have straight or down-curved bills). Breeding: gray head and neck with distinctive dark red throat patch (seldom seen in PNW). More delicate and slim-necked than other loons.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Wintering bird in coastal and marine waters. Less abundant than Pacific Loon in the Salish Sea but widespread. Prefers shallower, more nearshore waters than Pacific Loon — harbors, protected coves, estuaries. Also occurs on the outer coast and occasionally on freshwater lakes near the coast in winter.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — nearshore marine, estuarine
- **Flyway:** Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds on small tundra and boreal lakes across Arctic and subarctic Alaska and Canada. Unique among loons in its ability to take off from very small water bodies (can become airborne from ponds too small for other loon species). Migrates along the coast and through interior BC to PNW.
- **Arrival:** October
- **Departure:** April-May
- **Breeding grounds:** Arctic and subarctic (Alaska, northern Canada, northern Eurasia)
- **Distance:** 3,000-6,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small fish (herring, sand lance, sticklebacks), crustaceans, marine invertebrates
- **Foraging strategy:** Pursuit-diving; dives to 2-9 m (shallower diver than Pacific or Common Loon). Often forages alone or in pairs in nearshore waters.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to declining (some monitoring programs show declines in Salish Sea)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~490,000-1,500,000 individuals. PNW wintering population: 5,000-10,000 in the Salish Sea system.
- **Threats:** Oil spills; prey base changes; vessel disturbance; gill net entanglement
- **Source:** WDFW [G9], Cornell Lab [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Red-throated Loon is the most phylogenetically basal (ancestral) member of the genus *Gavia*. It retains several features considered ancestral for loons: smaller body size, the ability to take flight from very small water bodies (other loons require long water runways), and a slightly upturned bill unique among the five extant loon species. Its preference for small breeding ponds — where it nests but commutes to larger lakes or coastal waters to feed — is a distinctive ecological strategy not shared by other loons, which both nest and forage on larger lakes [O1, O3].

## V. OFFSHORE PHALAROPES

Phalaropes are the only shorebirds that have evolved a fully pelagic non-breeding lifestyle. Two species stage in vast numbers on PNW offshore waters.

---

### Red-necked Phalarope (*Phalaropus lobatus*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Scolopacidae
**Status:** Transient / Offshore staging (May and July-October)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 18-19 cm
- **Wingspan:** 31-34 cm
- **Mass:** 20-48 g
- **Plumage:** Non-breeding (common PNW appearance): gray above, white below, with bold dark eye patch on white face ("phalarope face mask"). Breeding female (May migrants): dark gray above with bright rufous-red neck sides and white throat, bold buff back stripes. Breeding male: duller version of female (reversed sexual dimorphism — females are brighter, larger, and dominant).

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Primarily offshore pelagic waters during staging. Enormous concentrations aggregate at upwelling fronts and convergence zones over the continental shelf, where they feed on zooplankton concentrated at the surface. Also occurs on coastal estuaries, lakes, and sewage ponds during migration, particularly in fall when juveniles pass through.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — offshore staging; Zone 5 (Riparian / Estuarine) — coastal migration stopover
- **Flyway:** Pacific, offshore
- **Route:** Breeds on Arctic and subarctic tundra (Alaska, northern Canada, Scandinavia, Siberia). Southbound migration (July-October) passes through PNW offshore waters en route to pelagic wintering areas in the tropical Pacific (Humboldt Current off Peru, equatorial Pacific). Staging aggregations off Washington and Oregon can number in the hundreds of thousands.
- **Spring passage:** May (brief, primarily offshore)
- **Fall passage:** July-October (peak August-September)
- **Staging areas:** Continental shelf waters off Westport, WA; Grays Harbor, WA (coastal); offshore Oregon. Staging concentrations associated with upwelling fronts.
- **Distance:** 10,000-15,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Zooplankton (copepods, amphipods, euphausiid larvae), aquatic insects (on breeding grounds and freshwater stopovers)
- **Foraging strategy:** Spins in tight circles on the water surface, creating a small vortex that draws zooplankton upward within reach. This spinning behavior is diagnostic of phalaropes and represents a unique foraging innovation among shorebirds. At sea, picks zooplankton from the surface film; the birds float very high on the water (dense body feathering traps air).

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (significant declines documented at some staging areas)
- **Estimate:** Global population 3.6-4.5 million individuals; PNW staging numbers variable (tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands depending on upwelling conditions)
- **Threats:** Climate-driven changes to upwelling timing and zooplankton availability at staging areas; Arctic breeding habitat changes; pesticide exposure on agricultural stopovers
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1], WHSRN [O18]

#### Evolutionary Note

Phalaropes are the only shorebird lineage to have evolved reversed sexual dimorphism and sex-role reversal: females are larger, more brightly colored, and compete for males; males incubate eggs and raise chicks alone. This reversal is associated with the evolution of polyandry — females may mate with multiple males, each of whom independently incubates a clutch. The pelagic non-breeding lifestyle (spending months on the open ocean) is unique among scolopacid shorebirds and represents a dramatic ecological shift from the terrestrial, wetland-dependent ancestry of the family [O1, O3].

### Red Phalarope (*Phalaropus fulicarius*)

**Order:** Charadriiformes | **Family:** Scolopacidae
**Status:** Transient / Offshore (April-May and August-November)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 20-22 cm
- **Wingspan:** 34-37 cm
- **Mass:** 35-75 g (larger than Red-necked Phalarope)
- **Plumage:** Non-breeding (common PNW appearance): pale gray above, white below, with reduced dark eye patch. Bill thicker and shorter than Red-necked. Breeding female: rich chestnut-red underparts, white face, dark crown, yellow bill base (spectacular — the "red" in the name). Males duller.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** More strictly pelagic than Red-necked Phalarope. Primarily encountered on offshore waters beyond the continental shelf. Rarely seen from shore except during storms that blow birds inshore (storm-driven birds occasionally appear on coastal ponds and beaches). Pelagic trip species.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — far offshore
- **Flyway:** Pacific, offshore
- **Route:** Breeds on high Arctic tundra (Alaska, northern Canada). Migrates offshore through the Pacific to wintering areas in the Humboldt Current and equatorial Pacific. PNW waters are a transit corridor; staging less concentrated than Red-necked Phalarope.
- **Spring passage:** April-May (far offshore)
- **Fall passage:** August-November (peak September-October)
- **Distance:** 12,000-20,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Zooplankton, small invertebrates, fish larvae (at sea); insects, crustaceans (breeding grounds)
- **Foraging strategy:** Spinning and surface-picking, like Red-necked Phalarope. More committed to pelagic foraging — associates with upwelling fronts, whale-feeding slicks, and oceanic convergence zones.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining
- **Estimate:** Global population 1-2 million individuals
- **Threats:** Climate-driven prey shifts; oil spill vulnerability on offshore wintering waters; Arctic breeding habitat changes
- **Source:** BirdLife International [O1]

#### Evolutionary Note

Red Phalarope is the most pelagic of the three phalarope species (Wilson's Phalarope, not covered here, is a freshwater species that migrates overland). Red and Red-necked Phalaropes are sister species that diverged approximately 5 million years ago, with Red Phalarope specializing on higher-latitude breeding habitat and more pelagic non-breeding habitat. The species' complete dependence on the open ocean outside the breeding season — a 6-8 month period — is unmatched among shorebirds and parallels the lifestyle of tube-nosed seabirds (Procellariiformes) despite the vast phylogenetic distance between the two groups [O1, O3].

## VI. NORTHERN WINTER VISITORS

The PNW's winter avifauna includes a contingent of species that breed in the Arctic tundra, boreal taiga, or high-latitude alpine habitats and move south during the boreal winter — some predictably, others in dramatic irruptions driven by prey crashes thousands of kilometers away. These are not tropical migrants in reverse; they are cold-adapted specialists for whom the PNW's mild, wet winters represent the warm edge of their tolerance. Their appearance in PNW lowlands is governed by forces acting on the breeding grounds: lemming population cycles, berry crop failures, seed mast years, and the density-dependent displacement of juveniles from productive Arctic summers. Understanding these species requires understanding the boom-bust ecology of the circumpolar north.

Northern winter visitors occupy a range of PNW habitats — open agricultural fields, coastal dune systems, suburban berry plantings, airport grasslands — that share one feature: structural similarity to the open, treeless landscapes of their breeding range. A Snowy Owl on a Skagit Valley dike, a Snow Bunting on an Ocean Shores beach, a Northern Shrike on a fence post in the Willamette Valley — each is responding to the same cue: flat, open sightlines that replicate the tundra or taiga edge.

The irruptive species in this section (Snowy Owl, Bohemian Waxwing, Common Redpoll) are characterized by extreme year-to-year variation in PNW abundance. In some winters, hundreds or thousands appear; in others, none. This unpredictability is itself a signal — it reflects the volatile prey dynamics of the Arctic and boreal systems from which these species originate [P28, O1].

### Snowy Owl (*Bubo scandiacus*) — EXTENDED PROFILE

**Order:** Strigiformes | **Family:** Strigidae
**Status:** Irruptive winter visitor (November-March, irregular)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Vulnerable (VU) — uplisted from Least Concern in 2017 due to revised population estimate and projected Arctic habitat loss
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed (extremely rare)
- PIF: Not assessed (Arctic breeder)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 52-71 cm
- **Wingspan:** 126-145 cm (among the largest owls in North America)
- **Mass:** Males 1,600-2,100 g; Females 1,700-2,950 g (significant sexual size dimorphism — females substantially larger)
- **Plumage:** Adult male: predominantly white with sparse dark barring on wings and flanks, becoming whiter with age. Old males can appear almost pure white. Adult female: white ground color with heavy dark brown barring across the entire body, crown, and wings — distinctly more barred than males at all ages. Immature birds (first-winter — the majority of irruptive visitors to PNW): heavily barred like females but with darker, denser markings. Yellow eyes set in a rounded, catlike facial disk. Feathered tarsi and toes (insulation adapted to Arctic temperatures of -40°C). No ear tufts.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Pronounced. Females average 15-20% larger and heavier than males, and are consistently more heavily barred. Pure white birds in the PNW are almost always adult males.

#### Habitat

- **PNW occurrence:** Open, flat landscapes that approximate tundra structure. In Washington: Skagit Valley agricultural flats, Samish Flats, coastal dune systems (Ocean Shores, Damon Point, Leadbetter Point), Dungeness Spit, airport grasslands (SeaTac, Bellingham, Bowerman), and the San Juan Islands. In Oregon: very rare, primarily coastal (Clatsop Plains, South Jetty of the Columbia River). In British Columbia: Boundary Bay, Roberts Bank, Delta agricultural lands, Vancouver International Airport — among the most reliable Snowy Owl sites in North America during irruption years.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — agricultural fields, diked flats
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — coastal dune systems, jetties, beach logs
- **Ecoregion tertiary:** Zone 7 (Human-Modified) — airports, agricultural lands
- **Microhabitat:** Perches on fence posts, hay bales, driftwood logs, jetty rocks, dike tops, airport runway markers. Selects elevated perch sites with 360-degree sightlines, replicating the topographic openness of Arctic tundra. Actively avoids forested areas. Roosts on the ground in open fields or on rooftops of low buildings.

#### Migration

- **Flyway:** Continental, irregular (irruptive)
- **Route description:** Not a regular seasonal migrant. Breeds across the Arctic tundra of North America (Alaska North Slope, northern Canada, Arctic archipelago) and Eurasia. In most years, post-breeding dispersal is short-distance, with birds wintering on the northern Great Plains, southern Canada, and coastal Atlantic provinces. In irruption years, birds push far south of normal winter range, reaching Washington, Oregon (rarely), and occasionally northern California. PNW irruptions are concentrated in coastal Washington and the Fraser River delta of British Columbia.
- **Irruption years (major PNW events):** 2005-06, 2011-12, 2012-13, 2013-14, 2017-18, 2021-22. The 2011-12 irruption was the largest documented in the lower 48 states in at least 50 years, with birds reaching as far south as Oklahoma and Bermuda. Washington hosted 50-100+ individuals during peak irruption winters [P29, O1, O16].
- **Arrival:** Late October to mid-November (earliest arrivals); peak December-January
- **Departure:** March-April; occasional lingering birds into May
- **Breeding grounds:** Arctic tundra, primarily north of the treeline across Alaska, northern Canada, and the Arctic islands
- **Distance:** Variable; irruptive flights may carry birds 3,000-5,000 km south of breeding range

#### The Irruption Mechanism

Snowy Owl irruptions are driven by dynamics on the breeding grounds, not by PNW conditions. The primary driver is the lemming cycle — population fluctuations of collared lemmings (*Dicrostonyx groenlandicus*) and brown lemmings (*Lemmus trimucronatus*) that oscillate on roughly 3-5 year cycles across the Arctic tundra.

**High lemming years:** Abundant prey fuels high Snowy Owl reproductive output — clutch sizes of 7-11 eggs (vs. 3-5 in low prey years), high nestling survival, and successful fledging of large broods. The result is a pulse of juveniles entering the population in late summer.

**Post-breeding displacement:** In the fall following a productive breeding season, territorial pressure from the large cohort of adults and juveniles drives young birds southward. These birds are not starving — studies using satellite telemetry and body condition assessments during the 2013-14 and 2017-18 irruptions documented that the majority of southward-moving Snowy Owls were juveniles in good to excellent body condition, many carrying substantial fat reserves. The irruption represents density-dependent dispersal from a productive population, not a famine-driven exodus [P28, P29].

**The starvation myth:** The popular narrative that irruptive Snowy Owls are "starving birds driven south by food shortage" is largely incorrect. While some late-winter individuals in poor condition do occur (particularly during years when Arctic prey crashes after initial high production), the primary irruption mechanism is juvenile surplus from high-productivity years. Many irruptive birds arriving in the PNW in November-December are fat, vigorous juveniles that have never experienced prey scarcity. Rehabilitation centers and banding programs have consistently documented that early-arriving irruptive birds are heavier than resident Arctic adults [P28, P29, P30].

**Low lemming years:** In contrast, when lemming populations crash, Snowy Owls may not breed at all, or produce tiny clutches with poor nestling survival. Few juveniles enter the population, and southward movement is minimal. These are the "quiet" winters when no Snowy Owls appear in the PNW.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet (PNW):** Small mammals — voles (*Microtus* spp.), rats, mice, pocket gophers. Also takes shorebirds, waterfowl (dabbling ducks, coots, grebes), gulls, and occasionally rabbits. Coastal birds may specialize on waterfowl and shorebirds. Airport birds take voles and rats from grassy margins.
- **Primary diet (breeding):** Lemmings — can consume 3-5 lemmings per day; a breeding pair with a large clutch may consume 2,500+ lemmings during a breeding season [P30].
- **Foraging strategy:** Sit-and-wait predator. Perches on elevated observation post and scans terrain with acute vision (diurnal — Snowy Owls are the most diurnal of North American owls, adapted to the 24-hour daylight of the Arctic summer). Strikes with powerful, silent flight on prey detected visually or acoustically. Capable of catching birds in flight. Caches surplus prey in cold weather — frozen prey items cached on snow or in crevices for later consumption.
- **Seasonal variation:** PNW wintering birds shift diet to locally available prey: voles in agricultural fields, waterfowl and shorebirds at coastal sites. This dietary flexibility enables exploitation of the unfamiliar PNW prey base.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Uncertain; revised downward
- **Estimate:** Global population estimated at 14,000-28,000 mature individuals (BirdLife International 2017 revision). Previous estimates of 200,000+ were based on extrapolation from breeding density in high lemming years, which dramatically overestimated the standing population by conflating peak-year productivity with average standing abundance. The 2017 IUCN uplisting to Vulnerable was driven by this revised population estimate [O1].
- **PNW abundance:** Highly variable. Irruption winters: 50-200+ individuals across Washington and BC. Non-irruption winters: 0-5 birds in Washington, typically none in Oregon. Boundary Bay/Delta, BC, is the most reliable PNW wintering site, with small numbers present in most winters regardless of irruption status.
- **Threats:**
  - Arctic climate change — warming tundra temperatures alter lemming population dynamics, potentially reducing the frequency and amplitude of lemming cycles that drive Snowy Owl reproduction. Greening of tundra habitat (shrub encroachment) changes the open landscape mosaic required for both lemmings and owls [G15]
  - Vehicle collisions — Snowy Owls in the PNW perch on road surfaces and dike tops; vehicle strikes are a primary cause of mortality for wintering birds
  - Airport conflicts — attraction to airport grasslands creates bird-strike risk for aircraft and danger for owls. Several PNW airports (SeaTac, Vancouver International) have trap-and-relocate programs during irruption winters
  - Human disturbance — irruptive Snowy Owls attract intense photographer attention. Repeated approach by photographers causes energy-costly flush responses, displacement from preferred hunting territories, and chronic stress. Organized viewing ethics campaigns (e.g., "Snowy Owl Watch" programs in Washington) attempt to mitigate disturbance [G9]
  - Rodenticide exposure — secondary poisoning from consumption of rodenticide-loaded prey in agricultural landscapes
- **Source:** Parmelee 1992 [P30], Kerlinger & Lein 1988 [P29], Newton 2006 [P28], BirdLife International [O1], WDFW [G9]

#### Vocalizations

- **PNW occurrence:** Generally silent on wintering grounds. Occasional low, barking "hoo-hoo" or hissing/bill-clapping when disturbed at close range.
- **Breeding grounds:** Deep, booming "hoo, hoo-hoo-hoo-HOO" territorial call of males, audible over 10 km on calm tundra. Harsh barking alarm calls near nest.

#### Evolutionary Note

Snowy Owl was formerly classified in the monotypic genus *Nyctea*, reflecting its perceived uniqueness among owls — the diurnal activity pattern, Arctic specialization, ground-nesting habit, and extreme sexual dimorphism in plumage. Molecular phylogenetics has since placed it firmly within *Bubo*, the genus of eagle-owls and horned owls, as sister to the Great Horned Owl (*B. virginianus*). This relationship is counterintuitive — the tundra-dwelling, white, diurnal Snowy Owl is the closest relative of the forest-dwelling, brown, nocturnal Great Horned Owl — but is strongly supported by multiple independent molecular datasets. The implication is that the Snowy Owl's suite of Arctic adaptations (white plumage, feathered feet, diurnal habit, lemming specialization, ground nesting) evolved relatively recently from a forest-dwelling, nocturnal ancestor, representing a dramatic ecological shift within the *Bubo* lineage during Pleistocene glacial expansion [O1, O3].

The cultural significance of Snowy Owls to Arctic peoples is deep and widespread. Inuit peoples across the Arctic recognize the owl as an indicator of lemming abundance and, by extension, of broader tundra ecosystem productivity. The owl's irruptive appearances in southern latitudes have generated fascination and folklore across many cultures — a rare wild predator of the Arctic suddenly appearing in farmlands and on rooftops, visible and approachable in a way that few raptors are [C1].

### Northern Shrike (*Lanius borealis*)

**Order:** Passeriformes | **Family:** Laniidae
**Status:** Winter visitor (October-March)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 23-25 cm
- **Wingspan:** 30-34 cm
- **Mass:** 55-80 g
- **Plumage:** Pearl gray above, white to pale gray below with faint grayish-brown vermiculations (wavy barring) on the breast and flanks. Black mask through the eye (narrower and less clean-edged than Loggerhead Shrike). Black wings with white patch at base of primaries (visible in flight). Black tail with white outer tail feathers. Stout, strongly hooked bill — pale base with dark tip. First-winter birds are browner overall with heavier barring below and a brownish mask.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal; females may average slightly browner with heavier breast barring.

#### Habitat

- **PNW occurrence:** Open and semi-open habitats with scattered perch sites: agricultural fields with fence lines, rural roadsides, clearcut edges, open scrubby wetland margins, and suburban edges with hedgerows. Widely distributed across lowland PNW in winter but never common — typically solitary, with individuals maintaining winter territories of 10-30 hectares.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — agricultural fields, hedgerows
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 7 (Human-Modified) — rural residential edges, clearcuts
- **Microhabitat:** Perches on exposed branch tips, fence posts, wire tops, and snag tops — any elevated perch with clear sightlines over open ground. Maintains "larder" sites: thorny shrubs, barbed wire fences, or sharp branch stubs where captured prey is impaled for storage.

#### Migration

- **Flyway:** Continental, north-south
- **Route:** Breeds in boreal taiga and treeline habitats across Alaska and northern Canada (subarctic forest edge). Migrates south in fall to winter across southern Canada and the northern and central United States. PNW wintering birds originate from Alaska and Yukon breeding populations.
- **Arrival:** October-November
- **Departure:** March-April
- **Breeding grounds:** Boreal forest edge and subarctic scrub, Alaska to Labrador
- **Distance:** 2,000-4,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Small birds (sparrows, juncos, chickadees, kinglets), small mammals (voles, shrews, mice), large insects (when available in early fall/late winter)
- **Foraging strategy:** Perch-and-pounce predator. Watches from an elevated perch, then swoops down on prey in a rapid, direct flight. Kills prey with a bite to the nape, severing the spinal cord — a precision killing technique unusual among passerines. Impales prey on thorns, barbed wire, or sharp branch stubs (the "larder" or "butcher's block" behavior). Impaled prey serves as food cache, territorial display, and mate-attraction signal.
- **Impaling behavior:** The larder is a defining feature of shrike ecology. Prey items — sometimes dozens — are wedged onto thorns of hawthorn (*Crataegus*), blackberry (*Rubus*), or honey locust, or onto barbed wire barbs. Items may be consumed immediately or left for days, partially frozen, to be consumed later. The larder functions as a food cache during cold or wet weather when hunting success is reduced. In late winter, males with larger, better-stocked larders attract females earlier — suggesting the cache also functions as a quality signal to prospective mates [O1].

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to possibly declining (poorly monitored across most of winter range)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~2.5 million individuals (Partners in Flight). PNW wintering population: low thousands across Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and BC.
- **Threats:** Decline of open agricultural habitat with hedgerows (conversion to intensive agriculture removes perch and larder sites); pesticide reduction of large-insect prey base; climate warming reducing boreal breeding habitat
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Evolutionary Note

Northern Shrike was split from Great Grey Shrike (*Lanius excubitor*) of Eurasia in 2017, based on molecular phylogenetics, vocal differences, and plumage distinctions. The two species form a superspecies complex with a Holarctic distribution — Northern Shrike in the Nearctic (North America), Great Grey Shrike in the Palearctic (Eurasia) — a classic Beringian vicariance pattern. The hooked bill and predatory behavior of shrikes are among the most remarkable convergences in passerine evolution: shrikes are true songbirds (suborder Passeri) that have independently evolved raptor-like predatory ecology, including the hooked bill, powerful talons (by passerine standards), and prey-handling behaviors that parallel small falcons. The impaling behavior has no analog among other passerines and appears to compensate for the lack of raptorial feet strong enough to hold prey while tearing it apart [O1, O3].

#### Morphometrics

- **Wingspan:** 32-35 cm
- **Mass:** 50-65 g
- **Plumage:** Soft, silky plumage overall — warm grayish-brown body, paler below. Prominent cinnamon-brown crest (pointed, recurved). Black mask through the eye with thin white border. Black chin and throat. Gray rump. Tail tipped with broad yellow band. Wings complex: white and yellow markings on primary edges, and waxy red tips on secondaries (flattened, shiny extensions of the feather shafts — the "wax" for which waxwings are named). Undertail coverts rich chestnut-rufous (diagnostic — Cedar Waxwing has white undertail coverts).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal; males may have slightly wider yellow tail band and more waxy tips on secondaries.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Irruptive visitor to lowland and mid-elevation areas, driven by boreal berry crop failure. When present, concentrates at fruiting trees and shrubs: mountain ash (*Sorbus*), crabapple (*Malus*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), juniper (*Juniperus*), holly (*Ilex*), ornamental plantings. Most reliable in eastern Washington and BC interior; irregular west of the Cascades. During major irruptions, flocks of 100-500+ birds descend on urban and suburban berry sources, stripping trees bare in hours before moving on.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (Human-Modified) — urban/suburban plantings, orchards
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — valley-floor berry thickets
- **Flyway:** Continental, irregular (irruptive)
- **Route:** Breeds in boreal and subarctic coniferous forests across Alaska, northern Canada, Scandinavia, and Siberia. In normal winters, remains in or near the boreal zone, feeding on persistent berry crops. In irruption years, moves south in large flocks when boreal berry crops fail (mountain ash, rowan, juniper). PNW irruptions are most common east of the Cascades, with occasional major invasions reaching the Puget Trough and Willamette Valley.
- **Arrival:** November-December (irruption years); absent in many winters
- **Departure:** March-April
- **Distance:** Variable; irruptive flights 1,000-4,000 km south of breeding range

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Berries (almost exclusively in winter) — mountain ash, crabapple, hawthorn, juniper, holly, cotoneaster, pyracantha, and other ornamental fruit. Insects (breeding season).
- **Foraging strategy:** Highly social. Flocks descend on fruiting trees en masse, consuming berries at a remarkable rate — individual birds can eat several hundred berries per day, consuming more than their body weight in fruit. Flocks are nomadic within the winter range, depleting one food source before moving to the next. The nomadic, feast-or-famine foraging pattern is an adaptation to patchily distributed, ephemeral fruit resources in the boreal forest.
- **Alcohol note:** Bohemian Waxwings occasionally become intoxicated from consuming fermented berries, particularly mountain ash and crabapple fruits that have undergone freeze-thaw fermentation cycles. Intoxicated birds exhibit erratic flight, inability to perch, and collision with windows and vehicles. Mass intoxication events have been documented in urban areas during late-winter thaws [O1].

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (difficult to assess due to irruptive distribution)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~3.2 million individuals (Eurasia and North America combined). North American population estimated at 1.5-2 million. PNW wintering numbers: zero in most years; up to thousands during major irruptions.
- **Threats:** Window collisions (nomadic, fast-flying flocks in urban areas); vehicle strikes; pesticide application on ornamental fruit trees; loss of native berry-producing shrubs in urban landscapes
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Evolutionary Note

Bohemian Waxwing is one of only three species in the family Bombycillidae (the other two being Cedar Waxwing, *B. cedrorum*, and Japanese Waxwing, *B. japonica*). The family is among the most fruit-specialized in the passerine radiation — no other songbird family is as completely dependent on fruit in the non-breeding season. The waxy red tips on the secondary feathers are structurally unique among birds: they are flattened, pigment-containing extensions of the feather rachis, produced by carotenoid deposition during feather growth. Their function appears to be social signaling — the number and size of waxy tips correlates with age and may function in mate assessment. Cedar Waxwing is the closest relative; the two species overlap in winter in the PNW but are readily separated by size (Bohemian larger), undertail covert color (chestnut vs. white), and belly coloration (gray vs. yellow) [O1, O3].

### Lapland Longspur (*Calcarius lapponicus*)

**Order:** Passeriformes | **Family:** Calcariidae
**Status:** Winter visitor (October-March)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-17 cm
- **Wingspan:** 25-29 cm
- **Mass:** 25-36 g
- **Plumage:** Non-breeding (typical PNW appearance): streaky brown above, white below with streaked flanks and a buffy wash on breast. Rufous-brown greater coverts and tertial edges. Pale central crown stripe bordered by dark lateral stripes. Small yellowish bill. Elongated hind claw (the "longspur" — an adaptation for walking on flat terrain). Breeding male (rarely seen in PNW — departing birds in March may show partial breeding plumage): spectacular black face, throat, and breast; chestnut nape; white supercilium; black-streaked gray back.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Breeding plumage strongly dimorphic; non-breeding plumage similar between sexes (females slightly less strongly marked).

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Open, flat habitats: agricultural stubble fields, coastal beaches above the wrack line, grassy dune flats, airports, recently plowed fields. Often in mixed flocks with Horned Larks and Snow Buntings. Easily overlooked — cryptic, ground-hugging behavior; often not detected until flushed, when flocks burst up with rapid, erratic flight and dry "tik-tik" calls.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — agricultural fields
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — upper beach, dune grass
- **Flyway:** Pacific
- **Route:** Breeds on Arctic tundra across the entire Holarctic (Alaska, northern Canada, Scandinavia, Siberia — one of the most widely distributed Arctic breeders). North American populations migrate south to winter on the Great Plains, Pacific coast lowlands, and Great Basin agricultural lands. PNW birds originate from Alaskan and Yukon breeding populations.
- **Arrival:** October-November
- **Departure:** March-April
- **Breeding grounds:** Arctic tundra, circumpolar
- **Distance:** 3,000-6,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass, weed, and grain seeds in winter); insects (breeding season — primary food for nestlings)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground-gleaner. Walks and runs on flat terrain, picking seeds from the soil surface and stubble. Gregarious — forages in loose flocks that advance across fields in a rolling, leapfrog pattern (birds at the rear fly over the flock to land at the front).

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining (BBS data suggest long-term decline in North American breeding populations)
- **Estimate:** Global population estimated at 130 million individuals (circumpolar — one of the most abundant Arctic songbirds). Despite large global numbers, North American populations show a declining trend.
- **Threats:** Agricultural intensification (loss of stubble fields to clean-tillage practices); climate change on Arctic breeding grounds; pesticide exposure on wintering grounds
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], eBird [O2], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Evolutionary Note

Lapland Longspur belongs to the family Calcariidae, a small New World family of six species (four longspurs and two snow buntings) recently split from the Emberizidae based on molecular phylogenetics. The family is exclusively associated with open, treeless habitats — Arctic tundra, alpine meadows, and Great Plains grasslands — and represents an ancient New World radiation that colonized the Arctic from temperate grassland ancestors. The elongated hind claw ("longspur") is a convergent adaptation for locomotion on flat ground, parallel to the elongated hind claws of larks (Alaudidae) and pipits (Motacillidae) — three unrelated families that independently evolved similar foot morphology in response to similar habitat structure [O1, O3].

### Snow Bunting (*Plectrophenax nivalis*)

**Order:** Passeriformes | **Family:** Calcariidae
**Status:** Winter visitor (November-March)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 15-18 cm
- **Wingspan:** 28-36 cm
- **Mass:** 30-45 g
- **Plumage:** Non-breeding (typical PNW appearance): white underparts and large white wing patches contrasting with warm tawny-brown crown, ear coverts, back, and breast-band. Extensive white in the wings and tail visible in flight — flocks appear to flash white and brown as they wheel and turn. Breeding male (not seen in PNW): stunning pure white head, breast, and back with jet-black wings, tail center, and back (breeds farther north than almost any other passerine).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males have more extensive white in wings and cleaner markings; females browner with less white.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Coastal beaches, especially sandy upper beach and dune systems. Also agricultural stubble fields, airport grasslands, and rocky coastal headlands. Most reliable along the outer coast of Washington (Ocean Shores, Westport, Long Beach Peninsula, Dungeness Spit) and at Boundary Bay, BC. Less common inland. Often in small flocks (5-30 birds), sometimes mixed with Lapland Longspurs and Horned Larks.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Coastal / Marine / Pelagic) — upper beach, coastal dunes
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — agricultural stubble
- **Flyway:** Pacific, coastal
- **Route:** Breeds on high Arctic tundra, rocky outcrops, and coastal cliffs across the Holarctic — the highest-latitude breeding passerine in the world (recorded breeding at 82°N on Ellesmere Island). North American birds winter south to the northern and central United States, with PNW birds primarily coastal. PNW wintering birds originate from Alaskan and high-Arctic Canadian breeding populations.
- **Arrival:** November (occasionally late October)
- **Departure:** March
- **Breeding grounds:** High Arctic tundra, circumpolar (Alaska, Arctic archipelago, Greenland, Svalbard, northern Siberia)
- **Distance:** 3,000-6,000 km one-way

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds (grass seeds, beach grass seeds, invertebrate fragments on beaches); insects (breeding season)
- **Foraging strategy:** Ground-gleaner. Runs along beach wrack lines and through dune grass, picking seeds and small invertebrates from the sand surface. Gregarious; flock movements on the beach are coordinated, with the group advancing as a unit across the sand.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to declining (circumpolar monitoring limited)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~40 million individuals (circumpolar). PNW wintering numbers: hundreds to low thousands across Washington, BC, and northern Oregon coast.
- **Threats:** Climate change on Arctic breeding grounds (warming may reduce habitat quality); beach disturbance and coastal development on wintering grounds; vehicle traffic on beaches used for foraging
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], eBird [O2]

#### Evolutionary Note

Snow Bunting breeds farther north than any other passerine. Males arrive on Arctic breeding grounds as early as late March — weeks before snow melt — and begin establishing territories on rocky slopes and cliff faces while temperatures remain well below freezing. This extreme earliness is driven by intense competition for nest sites (rock crevices and boulder cavities), which are limited on the tundra. The species' high proportion of white plumage reduces radiative heat loss in the Arctic environment and provides camouflage against late-season snow. Snow Bunting is sister to McKay's Bunting (*P. hyperboreus*), an extremely range-restricted species endemic to two Bering Sea islands (St. Matthew and Hall Islands, Alaska) — the two species hybridize on St. Lawrence Island, indicating very recent divergence (likely during the last glacial period) [O1, O3].

### Common Redpoll (*Acanthis flammea*)

**Order:** Passeriformes | **Family:** Fringillidae
**Status:** Irruptive winter visitor (November-March, irregular)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm
- **Wingspan:** 19-22 cm
- **Mass:** 11-20 g
- **Plumage:** Streaked brown above, paler below with dark streaking on flanks. Small red forecrown patch (the "redpoll" — visible at close range). Small dark chin patch. Two white wingbars. Short, conical, pale yellow bill with curved culmen. Males: pink-red wash on breast (variable intensity — brightest in adult males in fresh plumage). Females: lack or show minimal breast pink.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males have rosy-red breast wash; females are browner, streakier, with no red on breast. Both sexes show red forecrown.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Irruptive visitor to birch (*Betula*), alder (*Alnus*), and weedy-field habitats. When present, concentrates at birch and alder trees with catkin seed crops, weedy fields with seed-producing plants, and bird feeders. Most likely in eastern Washington, northeastern Oregon, and interior BC. Rare west of the Cascades, though irruption years may bring birds to Puget Sound lowlands and the Willamette Valley.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — birch groves, weedy fields
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (Mid-Elevation / Mixed Forest) — alder-lined streams, foothill edges
- **Flyway:** Continental, irregular (irruptive)
- **Route:** Breeds in boreal and subarctic forests across Alaska, Canada, Scandinavia, and Siberia. In most years, winters within or near the boreal zone. In irruption years, large numbers move south into the northern and central United States. PNW irruptions occur when boreal birch and alder seed crops fail simultaneously, forcing seed-dependent finches southward.
- **Arrival:** November-December (irruption years); absent in many winters
- **Departure:** March-April
- **Breeding grounds:** Boreal and subarctic scrub forests, circumpolar
- **Distance:** Variable; irruptive flights 1,000-4,000 km south of breeding range

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Seeds — birch, alder, grass, and weed seeds. Feeds on birch and alder catkins (hangs from branch tips like a chickadee). Also visits bird feeders for sunflower and nyjer seed.
- **Foraging strategy:** Active, acrobatic feeder. Hangs from small twigs to extract seeds from catkins. Highly social — forages in restless, twittering flocks of 10-200+. Flocks swirl from tree to tree, chattering constantly. Uses a unique adaptation to cold-climate foraging: a bilobed esophageal diverticulum (an expandable throat pouch) that allows the bird to rapidly ingest seeds, fly to a sheltered roost, and digest them in warmth — reducing exposure time in extreme cold [O1].

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (difficult to assess; irruptive dynamics mask trends)
- **Estimate:** Global population ~90-160 million individuals (circumpolar). PNW numbers: zero in most years; up to thousands during major irruptions.
- **Threats:** Boreal habitat loss (forestry, climate change); limited threats on PNW wintering grounds
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Evolutionary Note

Common Redpoll is at the center of an active taxonomic debate. Three taxa have been described: Common Redpoll (*A. flammea*), Hoary (or Arctic) Redpoll (*A. hornemanni*), and Lesser Redpoll (*A. cabaret*, primarily European). Genomic studies have shown extremely low genetic differentiation between Common and Hoary Redpolls — lower than between many conspecific populations — raising the question of whether they represent distinct species, subspecies, or simply color morphs of a single variable species. The 2024 AOS revision lumped Hoary Redpoll into Common Redpoll, treating them as a single species. However, the debate continues in the broader ornithological community, with some authorities maintaining separate species status based on ecological differences (Hoary Redpoll breeds in higher-latitude, more open tundra habitat) and consistent plumage differences in sympatric populations [O1, O3].

### Hoary Redpoll (*Acanthis hornemanni*)

**Order:** Passeriformes | **Family:** Fringillidae
**Status:** Rare irruptive winter visitor (December-March, very irregular)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC) — as currently recognized
- **Taxonomic note:** Lumped with Common Redpoll by AOS in 2024; species status debated

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 12-14 cm (similar to Common Redpoll)
- **Wingspan:** 21-25 cm
- **Mass:** 12-20 g
- **Plumage:** Paler and frostier than Common Redpoll. White ground color with reduced or absent brown streaking on flanks. Minimal or no streaking on undertail coverts (heavily streaked in Common Redpoll — the single most reliable field mark). Paler back with broader white edges to feathers. Males: pale pink breast wash (less intense than Common Redpoll males). Small, stubbier bill than Common. Overall appearance "frosted" or "bleached" compared to Common Redpoll.

#### Habitat and Migration

- **PNW occurrence:** Rare among Common Redpoll flocks during irruption years. Typically 1-5% of a Common Redpoll flock in the PNW; most winters, none are detected. Most records from eastern Washington and interior BC. Same habitats as Common Redpoll — birch groves, alder stands, weedy fields, feeders.
- **Ecoregion:** Zone 1 (Lowland / Valley Floor) — same as Common Redpoll
- **Route:** Breeds on high Arctic tundra and willow/birch scrub at higher latitudes than Common Redpoll. Irruptive movements parallel Common Redpoll but from a more northerly starting point.
- **Distance:** 3,000-5,000+ km in irruption years

#### Population

- **Trend:** Unknown (extremely difficult to monitor; taxonomy in flux)
- **Estimate:** Global population uncertain — 1-8 million individuals depending on taxonomic treatment
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], AOS [O3]

#### Evolutionary Note

The Hoary Redpoll taxonomic question encapsulates a broader problem in ornithology: where to draw species boundaries in recently diverged, partially differentiated lineages. Genomic studies using whole-genome sequencing have found that Common and Hoary Redpolls show differentiation at a very small number of loci — potentially linked to plumage pigmentation and bill morphology — while the vast majority of the genome is undifferentiated. This pattern is consistent with recent divergence with gene flow, or with a single polymorphic species maintained by disruptive selection on a few key traits. The resolution has implications beyond taxonomy: if plumage and morphology can be maintained as discrete variants within a single interbreeding population, it challenges the assumption that consistent phenotypic differences necessarily indicate reproductive isolation [O1, O3].

### Northern Saw-whet Owl (*Aegolius acadicus*)

**Order:** Strigiformes | **Family:** Strigidae
**Status:** Resident (year-round) with migratory/irruptive winter component
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern (LC)
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 17-22 cm
- **Wingspan:** 42-56 cm
- **Mass:** 54-151 g (females larger; mass varies substantially with prey availability)
- **Plumage:** Rich brown above with white spotting on crown, nape, and scapulars. White below with broad rufous-brown streaking. Round facial disk with prominent white eyebrows radiating from the base of the bill (giving a surprised or wide-eyed expression). Large yellow eyes. Lacks ear tufts. Very small — barely larger than a starling.

#### Habitat and Migration (Winter Movement)

- **PNW occurrence (resident):** Year-round in dense coniferous and mixed forests across the PNW, from sea level to the subalpine zone. Breeds in old woodpecker cavities and nest boxes.
- **PNW occurrence (migratory):** Northern and high-elevation populations are partially migratory, moving south and downslope in fall. Banding stations across the PNW (particularly along the outer coast and in mountain passes) capture significant numbers during fall migration (October-November). The scale of Northern Saw-whet Owl migration was not appreciated until standardized banding programs (Project Owlnet) began documenting large-scale nocturnal movement in the 1990s.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Mid-Elevation / Mixed Forest) — dense conifer/mixed forest
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Montane / Upper Forest) — breeding at higher elevations
- **Route:** Northern and interior populations migrate south and downslope. Coastal banding stations in Washington and Oregon capture hundreds of birds in some fall seasons. The migration is nocturnal and invisible without banding effort — these tiny owls move silently through the night, undetected by most observers.
- **Fall passage peak:** October-November (banding data)
- **Spring return:** March-April (less well documented)
- **Distance:** 500-2,000 km for migratory populations; some individuals are sedentary year-round

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Deer mice (*Peromyscus maniculatus*), voles, shrews — almost exclusively small mammals. Occasionally takes small birds, large insects.
- **Foraging strategy:** Nocturnal sit-and-wait predator. Perches on a low branch (1-4 m) in dense forest and listens for prey movement below. Strikes with a silent pounce. Relies heavily on auditory prey detection — asymmetrical ear placement (one ear higher than the other on the skull) enables precise three-dimensional sound localization in complete darkness.

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable (poorly monitored; secretive nocturnal habits make population assessment challenging)
- **Estimate:** 2-5 million individuals across North America. PNW population poorly quantified; likely widespread but low-density in suitable forest habitat.
- **Threats:** Old-growth and mature forest loss (reduces nest cavity availability); habitat fragmentation; window collisions during nocturnal migration; rodenticide secondary poisoning
- **Source:** Cornell Lab [O1], eBird [O2]

#### Evolutionary Note

Northern Saw-whet Owl belongs to the genus *Aegolius*, which includes four species distributed across the Holarctic and South America. The name "saw-whet" derives from one of its vocalizations — a rasping, metallic note likened to the sound of a saw being sharpened on a whetstone. Its partially migratory behavior was one of the great surprises of modern North American ornithology: until systematic banding programs began capturing migrating saw-whets in the 1990s, the species was thought to be largely sedentary. Banding has since documented large-scale, regular southward movements of northern and mountain populations — a hidden migration that had been invisible because it occurs silently, at night, and because the birds are too small and too secretive to detect by any method other than active capture [O1, O3].

## VII. MIGRATION PHENOLOGY CALENDAR

The Pacific Northwest sits at the intersection of three great migratory systems: the Pacific Flyway (waterfowl, shorebirds, raptors), the neotropical corridor (passerines, swifts, nightjars connecting breeding grounds in the PNW to wintering areas in Mexico, Central America, and South America), and the pelagic routes (seabirds, phalaropes, and jaegers traversing the North Pacific basin). The result is a continuous procession of arrivals and departures that reshapes the PNW's avian community month by month.

This calendar synthesizes timing data from Modules 2A (shorebirds, gulls, alcids), 2B (waterfowl, raptors, neotropical passerines), and 2C (pelagic visitors, northern winter visitors) into a single phenological reference. Dates reflect typical timing for the PNW core region (western Washington and Oregon, the Salish Sea, and adjacent offshore waters); timing shifts 1-3 weeks earlier at the southern edge (Willamette Valley, southern Oregon coast) and 1-3 weeks later at the northern edge (northern BC). Elevation also shifts timing: lowland events precede mountain events by 2-6 weeks.

All dates are approximate and subject to annual variation driven by weather, prey availability, and long-term climate trends. Spring migration has advanced measurably over the past 30 years in several species (Rufous Hummingbird, Tree Swallow, Osprey), consistent with warming spring temperatures [O1, O2, G4, G15].

---

### January-February: Deep Winter

**Community character:** The PNW's winter avifauna is fully established. Resident species are augmented by northern visitors, wintering waterfowl, and marine species. Species diversity is lower than migration peaks but biomass can be enormous — concentrated waterfowl flocks represent some of the largest avian aggregations on the continent.

**Key species and events:**

- **Snowy Owl** — Peak presence in irruption years. Individuals established on winter territories in Skagit Valley, Boundary Bay, and coastal dune systems. Most photographed and publicly visible (Section VI).
- **Snow Goose** — 40,000-80,000+ wintering on Skagit and Stillaguamish Flats, WA (Fir Island, La Conner). Vast white flocks lift off at dawn, settle on agricultural fields to graze, and return to roost on tidal flats at dusk. One of the PNW's great wildlife spectacles (Module 2B).
- **Trumpeter Swan** — 8,000-10,000 in the Skagit Valley, Judson Lake, Puyallup/Sumner, and Ridgefield NWR. The PNW hosts the largest wintering concentration of Trumpeter Swans in the lower 48 states (Module 2B).
- **Bald Eagle** — Winter concentrations peak along salmon spawning rivers (Skagit, Nooksack, Stillaguamish) where chum and coho carcasses provide food. 300-600+ eagles along the Skagit River system in January (Module 1D).
- **Seaduck abundance** — Surf Scoter, White-winged Scoter, Long-tailed Duck, Harlequin Duck, Barrow's Goldeneye peak in the Salish Sea. Aggregations of 10,000+ scoters in Boundary Bay and the San Juan Islands (Module 2B).
- **Pacific Loon** — Peak wintering numbers in the Salish Sea; large flocks visible from shoreline viewpoints (Section IV).
- **Northern Shrike** — Established on winter territories in lowland agricultural areas; impaling prey on hawthorn and barbed wire (Section VI).
- **Lapland Longspur, Snow Bunting** — Present on coastal beaches and agricultural stubble; inconspicuous unless flushed (Section VI).
- **Bohemian Waxwing, Common Redpoll** — Present only in irruption years; Bohemian Waxwings strip mountain ash and crabapple trees in towns; redpolls work birch and alder catkins (Section VI).
- **Northern Saw-whet Owl** — Wintering birds from northern/high-elevation populations settled in lowland forest; silent and cryptic (Section VI).
- **Pelagic alcids** — Marbled Murrelet, Ancient Murrelet, Cassin's Auklet in marine waters. Ancient Murrelet numbers peak in the Salish Sea (Module 2A).
- **Rough-legged Hawk** — Present on open agricultural lands, hovering over fields for voles. A characteristic winter raptor of the Skagit and Samish flats (Module 2B).
- **Christmas Bird Count season** (mid-December through early January) — Hundreds of count circles across the PNW produce baseline abundance data that document long-term trends in winter bird communities [O16].

---

### March: Early Spring Transition

**Community character:** Winter and spring overlap. The first long-distance migrants arrive while winter visitors remain. A period of maximum species diversity as two seasonal communities coexist.

**Key species and events:**

- **Rufous Hummingbird** — First arrivals in late February to early March in western Oregon and Washington lowlands. The earliest neotropical migrant to arrive in the PNW, tracked by flowering of red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*) and salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*). Males arrive first, establishing territories 1-2 weeks before females (Module 2B).
- **Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow** — First swallows appear in late February (south Willamette Valley) to mid-March (Puget Sound). Insectivores — their arrival is constrained by air temperature above the threshold for aerial insect flight (Module 2B).
- **Turkey Vulture** — Returns from California/Mexico wintering areas. First birds in late February in southern Oregon, mid-March in Washington. A conspicuous, easily monitored migrant (Module 2B).
- **Osprey** — Returns to nest platforms and snag-top nests by mid-March. One of the most precisely timed spring arrivals — individual birds return to the same nest within a 3-5 day window year after year (Module 2B).
- **Shorebird spring migration begins** — Early Killdeer and Western Meadowlark territorial behavior (resident/short-distance migrants). First northbound shorebirds (Greater Yellowlegs, Long-billed Dowitcher) appear at coastal estuaries and inland wetlands by late March (Module 2A).
- **Pacific Loon departure** — Northbound migration begins. Large flocks visible moving north along the outer coast, with major passage points at Cape Flattery, WA (Section IV).
- **Snow Goose departure** — Flocks begin departing Skagit Valley for Wrangel Island breeding grounds via staging areas in the Fraser River delta and coastal Alaska. Peak departure late March to early April (Module 2B).
- **Gray whale northbound migration** — Visible from coastal headlands (Cape Meares, Yaquina Head, Cape Disappointment); whale-watching boats active. Not a bird, but a phenological benchmark for the PNW marine spring transition.

---

### April: Spring Migration Peak

**Community character:** Migratory activity peaks. Neotropical passerines flood into the PNW. Shorebird staging areas reach maximum capacity. Breeding territories are established. The acoustic landscape transforms as territorial song fills forests, wetlands, and grasslands.

**Key species and events:**

- **Shorebird staging peaks** — Grays Harbor, WA (Bowerman Basin) hosts 500,000-1,000,000+ Western Sandpipers and Dunlin during peak spring staging (mid-April to early May). The concentration is one of the largest shorebird staging events in the Western Hemisphere. Thousands of shorebirds per hectare on tidal mudflats at low tide; mass departure flights at high tide that darken the sky. Semipalmated Plover, Short-billed Dowitcher, and Red Knot also stage in significant numbers (Module 2A).
- **Wilson's Warbler, Orange-crowned Warbler** — First wave of wood-warblers arrives. Wilson's Warbler: bright yellow with black cap, one of the most numerous spring migrants in PNW lowland riparian corridors (Module 2B).
- **Western Tanager** — Males arrive in mid-to-late April, bringing vivid red-orange and yellow plumage to conifer forests. A neotropical migrant that breeds across PNW mountain forests (Module 2B).
- **Swainson's Thrush** — Arrives in riparian forests by late April. The quintessential neotropical migrant voice — its ascending, spiraling song becomes the sonic signature of PNW evening forests from late April through July (Module 2B).
- **Raptor migration** — Turkey Vultures, Red-tailed Hawks, Sharp-shinned Hawks, Cooper's Hawks moving north through mountain passes and along ridgelines. Some Rough-legged Hawks departing for Arctic breeding grounds (Module 2B).
- **Snow Bunting, Lapland Longspur departure** — Last winter visitors depart coastal beaches and agricultural fields for Arctic breeding grounds (Section VI).
- **Common Loon** — Transitioning to breeding plumage (spectacular black-and-white checked pattern). Most depart for interior breeding lakes by late April (Module 2B reference).

---

### May: Late Spring Arrival

**Community character:** The last neotropical migrants arrive. Breeding activity accelerates — nest-building, egg-laying, and first clutches underway for early arrivals. Pelagic seabird migration begins offshore. The PNW avian community reaches near-maximum species richness.

**Key species and events:**

- **Late neotropical arrivals** — Olive-sided Flycatcher, Western Wood-Pewee, Pacific-slope Flycatcher arrive in the first half of May. Common Nighthawk and Vaux's Swift appear by mid-to-late May — among the last spring migrants (Module 2B).
- **Sooty Shearwater** — First vanguard birds arrive offshore by late April to mid-May. Numbers build rapidly through May as millions of birds stream north through the California Current from Southern Hemisphere breeding colonies (Section I).
- **Black-footed Albatross** — Post-breeding adults and non-breeding immatures increasingly common on pelagic trips off Westport, WA by May (Section I).
- **Red-necked Phalarope** — Spring passage offshore (primarily far offshore, less visible than fall passage). Staging aggregations begin forming at upwelling fronts (Section V).
- **Warbler diversity peaks** — Yellow, Yellow-rumped, Townsend's, Black-throated Gray, MacGillivray's, and Common Yellowthroat all on territory. Forest and riparian habitats at maximum acoustic complexity (Module 2B).
- **Shorebird departure** — Late-staging Western Sandpipers and Dunlin departing Grays Harbor and Willapa Bay for Arctic breeding grounds. Long-billed Curlew and Whimbrel passing through on northbound flights (Module 2A).
- **Band-tailed Pigeon** — Arrives from California/Mexico wintering areas; establishing territory with low, owl-like hooting in PNW conifer forests (Module 2B).

---

### June-July: Breeding Season

**Community character:** Migration nearly ceases. The PNW's breeding bird community is fully established. Territorial defense, incubation, and chick-rearing dominate behavior. Forest and riparian habitats are at peak vocal activity in June; song decreases markedly by late July as breeding concludes. Post-breeding dispersal begins for some species.

**Key species and events:**

- **Minimal migration** — The migratory pause. Essentially no long-distance migrants are in transit through the PNW during June-early July.
- **Seabird colony activity** — Common Murre, Tufted Puffin, Rhinoceros Auklet, Pigeon Guillemot, and Cassin's Auklet colonies at peak activity on offshore islands. Adult birds commute between nesting islands and offshore foraging areas. Pelagic trips encounter dense feeding aggregations near colonies (Module 2A).
- **Marbled Murrelet** — Breeding season. Adults make pre-dawn flights from the ocean to old-growth forest nest sites (typically on moss-covered limbs of ancient conifers 30-60+ m above ground). Among the most cryptic nesting behaviors of any North American bird (Module 2A).
- **Sooty Shearwater abundance builds** — Millions accumulate in PNW offshore waters through June-July, feeding at upwelling fronts (Section I).
- **Post-breeding dispersal begins (July):**
  - **Brown Pelican** — Non-breeding birds (primarily immatures and failed breeders from California/Mexico colonies) begin arriving along the PNW coast by late June to July. Numbers build through the summer. A post-breeding northward disperser, not a classic migrant (Module 2A).
  - **Heermann's Gull** — Arrives from Isla Rasa, Mexico breeding colony. The only North American gull with a northward post-breeding dispersal. Dark gray body, red bill, white head — distinctive and increasingly common on PNW beaches and jetties by late July (Module 2A).
  - **Elegant Tern** — Post-breeding visitors from southern California/Mexico. Rare but annual on the Oregon and Washington coast by late July (Module 2A).
- **Adult shorebird fall migration begins (late July)** — Earliest southbound adults (Western Sandpiper, Least Sandpiper, Short-billed Dowitcher) arrive at PNW estuaries by mid-to-late July. Adults migrate first, before juveniles — adults can depart breeding grounds as soon as chicks fledge or become independent (Module 2A).

---

### August: Southbound Begins

**Community character:** The great southbound movement accelerates. Shorebird migration peaks. Pelagic seabird abundance reaches its annual maximum. Post-breeding dispersal continues. The PNW's role as a migratory corridor is at its most visible.

**Key species and events:**

- **Shorebird fall migration peaks** — PNW estuaries and mudflats host enormous concentrations of southbound shorebirds. Adults in worn breeding plumage and fresh juveniles (2-3 weeks behind adults) overlap. Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Bandon Marsh, and the Columbia River estuary are major stopover sites. 100,000+ shorebirds at Grays Harbor during peak August passage (Module 2A).
- **Sooty Shearwater peak** — Maximum abundance in PNW offshore waters. Pelagic trips out of Westport, WA routinely record 10,000-50,000+ birds per day. Dense feeding aggregations visible from shore during onshore winds. The single most abundant bird in PNW waters during August (Section I).
- **Parasitic Jaeger** — Fall passage along the outer coast. The most visible jaeger from shore, harassing gulls and terns at river mouths and along the beach (Section III).
- **Red-necked Phalarope** — Vast staging aggregations on upwelling fronts offshore. Hundreds of thousands of birds spinning on the ocean surface. Also appearing at coastal estuaries and sewage ponds as juveniles pass through (Section V).
- **Brown Pelican** — Increasing numbers along the coast. Spectacular plunge-diving visible from jetties and coastal viewpoints (Module 2A).
- **Buller's Shearwater** — Arriving from New Zealand breeding colonies. First records typically in late August; becomes regular on pelagic trips September-October (Section I).
- **Rufous Hummingbird departure** — Begins departing PNW breeding territories by late July-August for southbound migration along the Cascade and Rocky Mountain cordillera to Mexico wintering areas. Males depart first, followed by females and juveniles (Module 2B).
- **Early passerine migration** — Barn Swallows, Bank Swallows, and Cliff Swallows begin staging on power lines and at wetlands. Wilson's Warbler and Orange-crowned Warbler southbound movement underway (Module 2B).

---

### September: Fall Migration

**Community character:** Passerine migration peaks. Raptor migration accelerates. The last neotropical migrants depart. Waterfowl from northern breeding areas begin arriving. A period of rapid community turnover — the summer community dissolves while the winter community begins to assemble.

**Key species and events:**

- **Passerine migration peaks** — PNW woodlands, riparian corridors, and coastal migrant traps fill with southbound songbirds. Mixed-species flocks of warblers, vireos, sparrows, and flycatchers move through lowland vegetation. Vagrant traps (coastal headlands, island groves, river-mouth parks) concentrate migrants and produce rare species. Peak birding activity at Neah Bay, Cape Flattery, Reifel Bird Sanctuary, Malheur NWR (Module 2B).
- **Raptor migration** — Sharp-shinned Hawks, Cooper's Hawks, and Red-tailed Hawks in sustained southbound movement through mountain passes and along ridgelines. Swainson's Hawk departs for its 10,000-km migration to Argentina — one of the longest raptor migrations in the Western Hemisphere (Module 2B).
- **Vaux's Swift roost spectacle** — Thousands of Vaux's Swifts stage at traditional communal roost sites (most famously Chapman Elementary School chimney in Portland, OR) during September migration. Swirling tornado-like flocks funnel into chimneys at dusk — a major public wildlife event (Module 2B).
- **Common Nighthawk departure** — Last nighthawks seen in early-to-mid September, heading for South American wintering areas (Module 2B).
- **Pomarine Jaeger, Long-tailed Jaeger** — Peak fall passage offshore (Section III).
- **South Polar Skua** — Rare fall records from offshore pelagic trips (Section III).
- **Buller's Shearwater** — Peak occurrence on pelagic trips (Section I).
- **Sabine's Gull** — Southbound migration through PNW offshore waters en route from Arctic breeding grounds to South American waters. Elegant, tern-like gull with a bold tricolor wing pattern — a highlight of fall pelagic trips (Module 2A).

---

### October: Late Fall Transition

**Community character:** Neotropical migrants have largely departed. Northern winter visitors begin arriving. Waterfowl numbers build rapidly. The autumn transition: summer's breeding community gives way to winter's northern and marine community. Weather shifts to the wet season; storm-driven vagrants appear.

**Key species and events:**

- **Waterfowl arrival accelerates** — Snow Goose flocks arrive on Skagit/Stillaguamish flats (mid-October onward). Trumpeter Swan arrival (late October). Northern Pintail, American Wigeon, Green-winged Teal, and other dabbling ducks flooding into PNW wetlands and agricultural lands. Cackling Goose flocks arrive in the Willamette Valley (Module 2B).
- **Pacific Loon, Red-throated Loon arrival** — Wintering loons establish in the Salish Sea and along the outer coast (Section IV).
- **Most neotropical passerines departed** — By late October, the last lingering warblers, flycatchers, and swallows have gone. Hermit Thrush lingers latest among thrushes (into November) (Module 2B).
- **Northern Shrike arrival** — First birds appear on lowland agricultural territories (Section VI).
- **Rough-legged Hawk arrival** — First birds appear over open agricultural fields from Arctic breeding grounds (Module 2B).
- **Northern Saw-whet Owl migration** — Peak banding captures at PNW coastal and mountain-pass banding stations (October-November) (Section VI).
- **Sooty Shearwater departure** — Numbers declining offshore as birds begin the trans-equatorial return to Southern Hemisphere breeding colonies (Section I).
- **Scoter arrival** — Surf Scoter, White-winged Scoter, and Black Scoter flocks building in the Salish Sea and along the outer coast (Module 2B).
- **Storm-driven vagrants** — October storms from the Pacific can deposit unusual species on the PNW coast: tropical storm petrels, boobies, frigatebirds, and Asian vagrants carried by trans-Pacific weather systems. The rarest PNW bird records are disproportionately concentrated in October.

---

### November-December: Transition to Winter

**Community character:** The winter avian community is established. Marine and estuarine species reach peak numbers. The last northern winter visitors settle into territories. The PNW landscape enters its period of lowest species diversity but highest localized biomass — concentrated waterfowl, seabird, and raptor populations occupying marine, estuarine, and agricultural habitats.

**Key species and events:**

- **Snowy Owl irruption assessment** — In irruption years, first birds appear in late October to November. By December, the scale of the irruption becomes clear. Major irruption years produce 50-200+ birds across Washington and BC; non-irruption years produce zero (Section VI).
- **Bohemian Waxwing, Common Redpoll irruption assessment** — If present, flocks established at berry sources and birch groves by November-December (Section VI). Their appearance is unpredictable — the PNW may go 3-5 years between irruption events.
- **Snow Bunting, Lapland Longspur** — Established on coastal beaches and agricultural stubble. Numbers stable through the winter months (Section VI).
- **Seaduck concentrations build** — Peak scoter, goldeneye, and Long-tailed Duck numbers in the Salish Sea by late November-December. Harlequin Duck established at rocky headlands and jetties (Module 2B).
- **Winter raptor community** — Bald Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk, Rough-legged Hawk, Northern Harrier, Short-eared Owl, Northern Shrike — the full complement of winter raptors is present on lowland agricultural landscapes (Module 2B, Section VI).
- **Pelagic alcid winter** — Ancient Murrelet numbers peak in the Salish Sea (November-February). Cassin's Auklet and Rhinoceros Auklet present offshore. Marbled Murrelet in nearshore waters (Module 2A).
- **Christmas Bird Count** — The National Audubon Society's CBC (mid-December through early January) engages thousands of PNW observers in standardized winter bird surveys. CBC data provide the longest continuous dataset on PNW winter bird abundance (dating to 1900) and have documented long-term trends including Snow Goose population increase, Trumpeter Swan recovery, scoter declines, and the expansion of wintering hummingbird populations [O16].
- **Yellow-billed Loon** — Rare winter records in the San Juan Islands, Bellingham Bay, and Strait of Juan de Fuca. The rarest of the three regular PNW wintering loons (Section IV).
- **Northern Fulmar** — Peak numbers offshore. Winter pelagic trips encounter fulmars regularly at the continental shelf edge (Section I).
- **The cycle resets** — By late December, the PNW's avian community has completed its annual transformation. The breeding birds of summer are 5,000-15,000 km to the south, in tropical forests and South American grasslands. The Arctic and boreal birds of winter are 3,000-6,000 km from their breeding grounds, occupying a PNW landscape that will sustain them until the lengthening days of March signal the return north. The flyway rests, briefly, before the cycle begins again.
