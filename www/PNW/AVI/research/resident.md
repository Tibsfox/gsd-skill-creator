## Scope

This module documents approximately 182 bird species that maintain year-round resident breeding populations within the Pacific Northwest region (Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia). "Resident" is defined as species with populations that breed and overwinter within the PNW, even if some individuals within the population are partially migratory or undergo altitudinal movements.

Species are organized by taxonomic order following AOS sequence. Each species receives a full species card per `shared-schema.md`. Raptors covered in detail by SURVEY-RAPTOR receive abbreviated entries here with cross-references.

**Safety rules enforced:**
- No GPS coordinates for ESA-listed species nest sites (SC-END)
- All population numbers cite specific sources (SC-NUM)
- Indigenous references name specific nations (SC-IND)
- All scientific names follow AOS authority (SC-TAX)

## Order: Anseriformes (Waterfowl)

Resident waterfowl in the PNW are primarily diving ducks, sea ducks, and geese with year-round populations. Many Anseriformes are partially migratory; species included here maintain substantial non-migratory breeding populations.

### Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Branta*
- Species: *Branta canadensis*
- Subspecies: *B. c. moffitti* (Great Basin Canada Goose) — primary PNW resident subspecies; *B. c. fulva* (Vancouver Canada Goose) — coastal BC and western WA

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition; *Cackling Goose* split recognized 45th Supplement (2004)

**Residency Status:** Resident

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–5,000 ft (0–1,524 m)
- Wintering: 0–2,000 ft (0–610 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE

**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE, AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND, HAB-URBAN

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl managed under flyway system)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 75–110 cm (30–43 in)
- Wingspan: 127–185 cm (50–73 in)
- Mass: 2,500–6,500 g (88–229 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Black head and neck with distinctive white chinstrap. Body brown-gray with paler breast. Sexes similar; male averages larger. Juveniles duller with less defined chinstrap.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Grasses, sedges, aquatic vegetation, agricultural grain
- Foraging guild: Generalist
- Foraging stratum: Ground, Aquatic surface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Scrape (ground nest with down lining)
- Nest location: Islands, muskrat mounds, elevated ground near water; 0–3 ft above water level
- Clutch size: 4–7
- Incubation period: 25–28 days
- Fledging period: 42–49 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Loud resonant honking; female higher-pitched than male. Contact calls in flight and on water. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary (resident subspecies); some altitudinal movement in winter

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Urban Canada Goose populations create significant nutrient inputs to park lakes and retention ponds through fecal deposition, altering nitrogen and phosphorus cycling in managed water bodies. Goslings are primary prey for Bald Eagles (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*), Coyotes (*Canis latrans*), and Great Horned Owls (*Bubo virginianus*) in urban-suburban interface habitats (source: O-07).

**Cultural Note:**
- The resident *moffitti* subspecies has expanded dramatically in urban and suburban areas of the Puget Sound lowlands and Willamette Valley since the mid-20th century, becoming one of the most visible large birds in urban parks, golf courses, and agricultural fields. Managed by WDFW under damage prevention permits (source: G-14).

**Salmon Thread:** No

**Key Sources:** G-14, G-18, O-03, O-07

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 2 (migratory *Branta canadensis* subspecies — *parvipes*, *taverneri* — overlap with resident populations in winter)
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-RIPARIAN wetland characterization)

### Trumpeter Swan (*Cygnus buccinator*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Cygnus*
- Species: *Cygnus buccinator*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round breeding populations in parts of BC and northern WA; augmented by wintering migrants)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–3,000 ft (0–914 m)
- Wintering: 0–500 ft (0–152 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL (recovered; formerly near extinction — population fell to 69 known individuals in 1932; restored through intensive management) (source: G-17)
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 138–165 cm (54–65 in)
- Wingspan: 185–250 cm (73–98 in)
- Mass: 7,000–13,600 g (247–480 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Entirely white plumage in adults. Black bill with straight profile (diagnostic vs. Tundra Swan). Juveniles gray-brown, gradually whitening through first year. Largest native waterfowl in North America.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Submerged aquatic vegetation, tubers, agricultural grain in winter
- Foraging guild: Generalist
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic surface, Aquatic subsurface (tipping), Ground (winter agricultural fields)

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Platform (large mound of vegetation in shallow water)
- Nest location: Emergent wetland margins, beaver pond edges, lake shores
- Clutch size: 4–6
- Incubation period: 33–37 days
- Fledging period: 100–120 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Deep, resonant bugling call; trumpet-like, lower-pitched than Tundra Swan. Flight calls audible at great distance. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary to short-distance; BC breeding birds winter in Skagit Valley, WA and Comox Valley, BC

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Trumpeter Swans are ecosystem engineers in shallow wetlands, uprooting submerged aquatic vegetation while foraging and creating open-water patches used by dabbling ducks, coots, and shorebirds. Their large platform nests are reused by Canada Geese (*Branta canadensis*) and occasionally by Great Horned Owls (*Bubo virginianus*) in subsequent seasons (source: O-07).

**Cultural Note:**
- Trumpeter Swan recovery is one of the great conservation success stories of North American wildlife management. The Skagit Valley, WA hosts the largest wintering concentration in the lower 48 states, with an estimated 10,000+ individuals by 2020 (source: G-18). The species was brought back from the brink of extinction through coordinated USFWS and state management.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Trumpeter Swans forage in salmon-bearing estuarine wetlands where marine-derived nutrients from salmon carcasses enrich aquatic vegetation growth. Salmon nutrient cycling indirectly supports swan foraging habitat quality. See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling.

**Key Sources:** G-17, G-18, O-03, O-07

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 2 (Tundra Swan *Cygnus columbianus* — winter visitor, often co-occurs with Trumpeter Swans)
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-RIPARIAN wetland characterization)

### Wood Duck (*Aix sponsa*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Aix*
- Species: *Aix sponsa*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round in western WA, OR; partially migratory in interior)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–3,000 ft (0–914 m)
- Wintering: 0–1,500 ft (0–457 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE, HAB-OLD-GROWTH

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 43–51 cm (17–20 in)
- Wingspan: 66–73 cm (26–29 in)
- Mass: 454–862 g (16–30 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male breeding: iridescent green-purple head with white face stripes, chestnut breast with white flecks, multicolored body. One of North America's most ornate waterfowl. Female: gray-brown with distinctive white teardrop eye ring. Eclipse male resembles female but retains red eye.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Acorns, seeds, aquatic invertebrates, berries
- Foraging guild: Generalist
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic surface, Ground

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Cavity (secondary cavity nester)
- Nest location: Tree cavities or nest boxes, typically 5–50 ft above ground, often near or over water; uses old Pileated Woodpecker cavities
- Clutch size: 9–14 (frequent intraspecific brood parasitism increases counts)
- Incubation period: 28–37 days
- Fledging period: 56–70 days
- Broods per year: 1–2

**Vocalization:** Female: loud rising "oo-eek, oo-eek" when flushed. Male: thin, rising whistle. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary (western populations); short-distance migrant (interior populations)

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Wood Ducks are critical members of the cavity nester guild (REL-CAVITY), competing with Hooded Mergansers (*Lophodytes cucullatus*), European Starlings (*Sturnus vulgaris*), and occasionally Barred Owls (*Strix varia*) for large tree cavities. Nest box programs have significantly augmented populations where natural cavities are limited by logging of old-growth riparian forests (source: O-07, G-14).

**Cultural Note:**
- The Wood Duck's recovery from near-extinction in the early 1900s through nest box programs represents a major citizen science success. Nest box trails along PNW rivers (e.g., Willamette, Skagit) are maintained by volunteer organizations and wildlife agencies. The male's ornate plumage makes it among the most popular species for wildlife photography and art.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Wood Ducks forage in riparian habitats enriched by salmon-derived nutrients. Beaver ponds along salmon streams create ideal Wood Duck brood-rearing habitat. See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling.

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07, O-20

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 5 (cavity nester guild network — REL-CAVITY)
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-RIPARIAN habitat)

### Harlequin Duck (*Histrionicus histrionicus*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Histrionicus*
- Species: *Histrionicus histrionicus*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round PNW; seasonal habitat shift from montane streams to marine)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 1,000–5,000 ft (305–1,524 m) on fast-flowing mountain streams
- Wintering: 0–50 ft (0–15 m) in marine surf zone
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE

**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-ROCKY-INTERTIDAL

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 33–46 cm (13–18 in)
- Wingspan: 56–66 cm (22–26 in)
- Mass: 500–726 g (18–26 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male breeding: slate blue body with bold chestnut flanks and complex white crescent and spot pattern on head and body. Female: plain dark brown with two or three white face patches. Male eclipse resembles female but darker. One of the most distinctively patterned sea ducks.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Aquatic invertebrates — caddisfly larvae, stonefly nymphs (breeding); marine invertebrates — amphipods, small crabs, snails, mussels (winter)
- Foraging guild: Diving piscivore (invertivore specialist)
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic subsurface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Scrape (ground nest concealed among rocks, logs, or vegetation near stream)
- Nest location: Within 10 m of fast-flowing mountain stream, well-concealed
- Clutch size: 5–7
- Incubation period: 27–30 days
- Fledging period: 40–50 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Male: high-pitched squeaking whistle. Female: harsh "ek-ek-ek." Generally quiet. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Altitudinal migrant — breeds on montane streams (April–August), winters on rocky marine coastline

**Ecological Interactions:**
- The Harlequin Duck is the premier cross-band indicator species in the AVI mission, linking ELEV-MONTANE stream habitat (breeding) with ELEV-INTERTIDAL rocky shoreline (wintering). Adults return to the same breeding stream and wintering rocks with high fidelity. Stream water quality, invertebrate density, and absence of disturbance during nesting are critical habitat parameters (source: G-14, O-07).

**Cultural Note:**
- Known to Coast Salish peoples as a "mountain torrent duck" — its association with fast-flowing headwater streams was well-documented in traditional ecological knowledge. The species is used as an indicator of watershed health by fisheries biologists due to its reliance on pristine montane streams (source: C-01).

**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Harlequin Ducks breed on streams that are also critical salmon spawning habitat. Overlap with salmon redds is well-documented. Stream invertebrate prey base is enriched by marine-derived nutrients from salmon carcasses. See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling.

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07, C-01

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 3 (cross-band species linking ELEV-MONTANE and ELEV-INTERTIDAL)
- See ECO: shared-attributes (ELEV-MONTANE stream habitat)

### Barrow's Goldeneye (*Bucephala islandica*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Bucephala*
- Species: *Bucephala islandica*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round PNW; breeds on montane lakes, winters on coast)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 2,000–6,000 ft (610–1,829 m)
- Wintering: 0–200 ft (0–61 m) on marine waters
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Habitat:** HAB-LAKE, HAB-OLD-GROWTH, AVI-HAB-SNAG

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 42–48 cm (17–19 in)
- Wingspan: 67–72 cm (26–28 in)
- Mass: 590–1,045 g (21–37 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male: glossy purple-black head with distinctive white crescent between eye and bill (vs. round spot in Common Goldeneye). Black back, white sides. Female: dark chocolate-brown head, gray body, yellow-orange bill tip in spring.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Aquatic invertebrates, insect larvae, small fish, fish eggs
- Foraging guild: Diving piscivore
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic subsurface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Cavity (secondary cavity nester)
- Nest location: Tree cavities near montane lakes, typically old Pileated Woodpecker holes; 5–40 ft above ground
- Clutch size: 6–12
- Incubation period: 29–31 days
- Fledging period: 56 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Male: nasal grunting during courtship display. Female: harsh "croak." Generally quiet outside courtship. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Altitudinal — montane lakes (breeding) to marine coast (winter)

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Barrow's Goldeneye competes with Common Goldeneye (*Bucephala clangula*), Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*), and Wood Duck (*Aix sponsa*) for cavity nest sites near montane lakes. Western North America holds >95% of the world's population, making PNW montane lakes globally significant breeding habitat (source: O-07, G-14).

**Cultural Note:**
- The species' restricted global range (western North America plus a disjunct population in Iceland and northeastern North America) makes PNW breeding lakes among the most important sites worldwide. It is monitored by WDFW as an indicator of montane lake ecosystem health (source: G-14).

**Salmon Thread:** No

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 5 (cavity nester guild — REL-CAVITY)
- See AVI: Module 3 (ELEV-SUBALPINE lake community)

### Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Mergus*
- Species: *Mergus merganser*
- Subspecies: *M. m. americanus* (North American subspecies)

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–4,000 ft (0–1,219 m) along rivers and lakes
- Wintering: 0–1,500 ft (0–457 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Habitat:** HAB-STREAM, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 54–71 cm (21–28 in)
- Wingspan: 78–97 cm (31–38 in)
- Mass: 900–2,160 g (32–76 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male: dark green-black head (appears black at distance), white body, black back, thin red bill with hooked tip. Female: rufous-brown crested head sharply demarcated from white chin and gray body. Largest merganser in North America.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Fish (salmonids, sculpins, minnows), aquatic invertebrates
- Foraging guild: Diving piscivore
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic subsurface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Cavity (secondary cavity nester; also uses rock crevices, hollow logs, nest boxes)
- Nest location: Large tree cavities near water, cliff crevices, or ground cover near streams; 0–50 ft above ground
- Clutch size: 9–12
- Incubation period: 28–35 days
- Fledging period: 60–75 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Generally quiet. Female: harsh "croak" alarm call. Male: faint bell-like call during courtship. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary to short-distance; some altitudinal movement

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Common Mergansers are significant predators of juvenile salmonids in PNW rivers. Studies on the Skagit and other salmon rivers estimate merganser consumption of salmon smolts during spring outmigration, making the species a subject of fisheries management debate. However, mergansers also consume non-native fish and may reduce competition for juvenile salmon (source: G-14, P-18).

**Cultural Note:**
- Known as "sawbill" by anglers due to the serrated bill edges adapted for gripping fish. Fisheries managers historically considered them salmon predators, but modern ecosystem-based management recognizes their role in aquatic food webs. The species is an indicator of clean, fish-bearing rivers.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Primary salmon predator among PNW waterfowl. Consumes juvenile salmon (smolts) during spring outmigration and salmon eggs on spawning grounds. A direct trophic link in the salmon ecosystem. See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling.

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07, P-18

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 5 (predator-prey network — salmon)
- See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling (merganser-smolt predation)

### Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Lophodytes*
- Species: *Lophodytes cucullatus*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–2,500 ft (0–762 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Habitat:** HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 40–49 cm (16–19 in)
- Wingspan: 56–70 cm (22–28 in)
- Mass: 450–879 g (16–31 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male: black head with large, fan-shaped white crest bordered in black — spectacular when raised. Golden eye. Black back, rufous flanks. Female: warm brown with shaggy rufous crest. Smallest merganser.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Small fish, crayfish, aquatic insects, amphibians
- Foraging guild: Diving piscivore
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic subsurface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Cavity (secondary cavity nester)
- Nest location: Tree cavities and nest boxes near quiet waters; 5–30 ft above ground
- Clutch size: 7–13
- Incubation period: 26–41 days
- Fledging period: ~70 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Male: deep rolling purr during courtship. Female: hoarse "croak." (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary (western PNW) to short-distance migrant

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Cavity nest competitor with Wood Ducks (*Aix sponsa*); both species use Pileated Woodpecker cavities and nest boxes. Intraspecific brood parasitism between these two species is well-documented, with mixed clutches of Wood Duck and Hooded Merganser eggs found in shared nest boxes (source: O-07).

**Cultural Note:**
- The male's erectile white crest, raised during courtship displays, is one of the most dramatic plumage features of any North American duck. Increasingly common in urban ponds and park lakes in the Puget Sound lowlands and Portland metro area.

**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Forages in salmon-bearing streams and beaver ponds enriched by marine-derived nutrients. See ECO: salmon-nutrient-cycling.

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07

**Cross-Module References:**
- See AVI: Module 5 (cavity nester guild — REL-CAVITY)

### Mallard (*Anas platyrhynchos*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Anseriformes
- Family: Anatidae (Ducks, Geese, and Swans)
- Genus: *Anas*
- Species: *Anas platyrhynchos*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round breeding populations; augmented by wintering migrants)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–5,000 ft (0–1,524 m)
- Wintering: 0–2,000 ft (0–610 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE

**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE, HAB-RIPARIAN, HAB-URBAN

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed (waterfowl)

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 50–65 cm (20–26 in)
- Wingspan: 82–95 cm (32–37 in)
- Mass: 750–1,580 g (26–56 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male breeding: iridescent green head, white neck ring, chestnut breast, gray body, black undertail with curled central retrices. Female: mottled brown with orange bill. Eclipse male resembles female but retains yellow-green bill.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Seeds, aquatic vegetation, invertebrates, grain
- Foraging guild: Generalist
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic surface, Ground

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Scrape (ground nest in dense vegetation, sometimes far from water)
- Nest location: Ground level in grass, shrubs, or agricultural cover; occasionally elevated on building ledges in urban areas
- Clutch size: 7–10
- Incubation period: 26–30 days
- Fledging period: 50–60 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Female: classic "quack-quack-quack" decrescendo call. Male: softer "rahb." (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary (resident populations) to short-distance migrant

**Ecological Interactions:**
- The most abundant and widespread duck in the PNW. Hybridizes with domestic ducks and occasionally American Black Duck in introduced ranges. Urban-adapted populations serve as primary prey for urban-nesting Bald Eagles, Cooper's Hawks, and Peregrine Falcons (source: O-07, G-18).

**Cultural Note:**
- The most recognized wild bird in urban environments. Domesticated from wild Mallard stock; feral and domestic-wild hybrids ("park ducks") are common in urban waterways. Important game species with approximately 500,000 harvested annually in the Pacific Flyway (source: G-03).

**Salmon Thread:** No

**Key Sources:** G-03, G-18, O-03, O-07

**Cross-Module References:**

- Wintering: 5,000–9,000 ft (1,524–2,743 m) — reverse altitudinal migration upslope into dense conifer
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 43–51 cm (17–20 in)
- Wingspan: 66–69 cm (26–27 in)
- Mass: 800–1,300 g (28–46 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male: dark gray-blue body, yellow-orange eye combs. In display, reveals purple-red cervical air sacs bordered by white-based feathers. Female: mottled brown, well-camouflaged. Tail gray with pale gray terminal band (vs. dark terminal band in Sooty Grouse).

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Conifer needles (primarily Douglas-fir, true fir in winter); berries, forbs, insects in summer

- Foraging stratum: Ground (breeding), Canopy (winter)

- Incubation period: 25–28 days
- Fledging period: ~12 days (precocial; flight capable by 8–9 days)
- Broods per year: 1

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Reverse altitudinal — unlike most mountain birds, moves upslope in winter to feed on conifer needles in dense subalpine forest; moves downslope in spring to breed at forest edges

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Dusky Grouse exhibits one of North America's few reverse altitudinal migrations — moving to higher elevation in winter, an adaptation to exploiting conifer needles as a winter food resource where competition is minimal. Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*) is the primary predator throughout its range (source: O-07, O-08).

**Cultural Note:**
- Until 2006, Dusky and Sooty Grouse were treated as a single species ("Blue Grouse"). The split was based on genetic and vocal evidence. Interior PNW birders now distinguish the two species by range, tail pattern, and hooting frequency. A game species managed by WDFW and ODFW.

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE

**Habitat:** AVI-HAB-SAGEBRUSH

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-PRIMARY-CONSUMER, ROLE-INDICATOR

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-C (Candidate — warranted but precluded; subject to ongoing regulatory review) (source: G-17)
- Washington State: ST-T (State Threatened) (source: G-14)
- Oregon State: ST-SC (Strategy Species — Oregon Conservation Strategy) (source: G-15)
- Idaho State: ST-SC (Species of Greatest Conservation Need) (source: G-16)

- Wingspan: 66–97 cm (26–38 in)
- Mass: 1,000–3,200 g (35–113 oz); males significantly larger than females

- Foraging guild: Ground forager (sagebrush obligate)
- Foraging stratum: Ground

- Clutch size: 6–9

- Fledging period: ~10 days (precocial)
- Broods per year: 1

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary to nomadic; seasonal movements between lek sites (spring), nesting habitat, summer brood-rearing areas, and wintering sagebrush

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Greater Sage-Grouse is the quintessential indicator species for sagebrush steppe ecosystem integrity. Its dependence on sagebrush is obligate — no other North American bird species is more strictly tied to a single plant genus. Lek sites (communal mating grounds) are used for decades. Population has declined approximately 80% from historical levels across the species' range, driven by sagebrush conversion to agriculture, cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*) invasion increasing fire frequency, and energy development (source: P-15, G-14).

**Cultural Note:**
- The Sahaptin-speaking peoples of the Columbia Plateau recognized the sage-grouse as a significant part of the shrub-steppe landscape. The bird's spring lek displays are among the most spectacular wildlife events in eastern Washington and Oregon. Lek viewing has become an important ecotourism activity, with organized viewing programs at locations such as the Yakima Training Center (source: C-06, G-14).

**Plumage Description:** Male and female similar: gray-blue breast and head, chestnut throat bordered by white, chestnut flanks with bold white bars, two long straight head plumes (vs. curved topknot in California Quail). Largest New World quail.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Seeds, bulbs, fungi, tubers, insects
- Foraging guild: Ground forager
- Foraging stratum: Ground

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Scrape (ground nest under dense shrub)
- Nest location: Concealed under brush, logs, or rock; steep terrain preferred; ground level
- Clutch size: 9–15
- Incubation period: 24–25 days
- Fledging period: ~10 days
- Broods per year: 1

**Vocalization:** Male: loud, clear, descending whistle "queee-ark" — carries long distances in mountain canyons. (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Altitudinal — entire family groups walk downslope in fall, sometimes traveling several miles on foot

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Mountain Quail undertake one of the most remarkable altitudinal migrations of any North American bird — entire coveys walk downslope in autumn, covering miles of mountainous terrain on foot rather than flying. This makes them vulnerable to predation during migration. Coyotes (*Canis latrans*), Bobcats (*Lynx rufus*), and Cooper's Hawks (*Accipiter cooperii*) are primary predators (source: O-07, G-14).

**Cultural Note:**
- The Mountain Quail's long, straight head plumes and secretive behavior in dense brush make it one of the most difficult PNW gamebirds to observe. Idaho has attempted reintroductions in areas where the species was extirpated. Its altitudinal foot migration is one of the most unusual avian behaviors documented in the PNW.

**Salmon Thread:** No

- Mass: 770–1,990 g (27–70 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Male: iridescent green-purple head, red face wattles, white neck ring, golden-copper body with dark markings, extremely long barred tail. Female: cryptic mottled brown. Extreme sexual dimorphism.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**

- Foraging stratum: Ground

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition (introduced in PNW)

**Residency Status:** Resident (introduced; established populations since 1960s–1980s introductions)

**Elevation Range:**

- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE

- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 100–125 cm (39–49 in)
- Wingspan: 125–144 cm (49–57 in)
- Mass: 2,500–11,000 g (88–388 oz); males much larger than females

**Plumage Description:** Male: iridescent bronze-green-copper body, bare blue-red head with wattles and snood, breast tuft ("beard"), tail fan in display. Female: duller brown, smaller, lacks prominent wattles.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Wild Turkeys are generalist omnivores that forage in mixed flocks, scratching through leaf litter and consuming acorns, nuts, insects, and occasionally small vertebrates. Their introduction has raised concerns about competition with native Ruffed Grouse and Blue Grouse for acorn and insect resources in oak-prairie and mixed forest habitats. Cougars, Bobcats, and Coyotes are primary predators (source: O-07, G-14).

**Cultural Note:**
- Not native to the PNW; introduced by state wildlife agencies for recreational hunting. Populations have expanded rapidly, particularly in the oak woodlands of the Willamette Valley, the Okanogan region of WA, and the foothills of central Idaho. The species' spread raises questions about impacts on native gallinaceous birds.

### Pied-billed Grebe (*Podilymbus podiceps*)

**Taxonomy:**
- Order: Podicipediformes
- Family: Podicipedidae (Grebes)
- Genus: *Podilymbus*
- Species: *Podilymbus podiceps*

**AOS Authority:** AOS Check-list, 7th edition

**Residency Status:** Resident (year-round in western PNW; partially migratory in interior)

**Elevation Range:**
- Breeding: 0–4,000 ft (0–1,219 m)
- Elevation Band IDs: ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN

**Ecoregion Affiliations:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

**Habitat:** HAB-WETLAND, HAB-LAKE

**Ecological Role:** ROLE-SECONDARY-CONSUMER

**Conservation Status:**
- Federal ESA: ESA-NL
- Washington State: ST-NL
- Oregon State: ST-NL
- Idaho State: ST-NL
- IUCN Red List: IUCN-LC
- Partners in Flight: Not assessed

**Morphometrics:**
- Length: 31–38 cm (12–15 in)
- Wingspan: 45–62 cm (18–24 in)
- Mass: 253–568 g (9–20 oz)

**Plumage Description:** Breeding: brown body, black chin and throat, distinctive black ring around pale bill. Non-breeding: loses black throat and bill ring; paler overall. Compact, tailless appearance. Rarely flies; prefers to dive or sink slowly below surface when threatened.

**Diet & Foraging Guild:**
- Primary diet: Small fish, crayfish, aquatic invertebrates, amphibians
- Foraging guild: Diving piscivore
- Foraging stratum: Aquatic subsurface

**Nesting Ecology:**
- Nest type: Platform (floating platform anchored to emergent vegetation)
- Nest location: In cattails, bulrushes, or other emergent vegetation on pond or lake margin
- Clutch size: 4–7
- Incubation period: 23–27 days
- Fledging period: 35–42 days (chicks ride on parent's back)
- Broods per year: 1–2

**Vocalization:** Loud, far-carrying "kuk-kuk-kuk-cow-cow-cow-cowp-cowp" — a rapid descending series, often called "cuckoo-like." (source: O-26)

**Migration:**
- Migration strategy: Sedentary in western PNW; short-distance migrant in interior where wetlands freeze

**Ecological Interactions:**
- Pied-billed Grebes are secretive marsh birds that swallow their own feathers, forming a felt-like lining in the stomach believed to protect the digestive tract from sharp fish bones. They are an indicator of wetland health — presence requires permanent open water with emergent vegetation cover. American Mink (*Neogale vison*) and Northern Harriers (*Circus hudsonius*) are primary predators (source: O-07, O-20).

**Cultural Note:**
- Despite being common, the Pied-billed Grebe is rarely seen by casual observers due to its secretive habits and ability to sink slowly into the water rather than diving. Known as "water witch" or "hell-diver" in rural PNW vernacular.

**Salmon Thread:** No

**Key Sources:** G-14, O-03, O-07, O-20
