# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 5: Ecological Networks and Trophic Relationships

> **Module 5 deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document synthesizes the ecological networks in which PNW birds function as nodes — food webs, nutrient pathways, mutualism networks, trophic cascades, keystone interactions, and scavenging systems. Birds are not isolated species; they are participants in interaction webs that connect marine, freshwater, and terrestrial ecosystems across all seven PNW ecoregion zones. Every ecological relationship described here draws from species data documented in Modules 1-3 (species profiles, marine/migratory specialists, ecoregion communities) and cross-references mammalian interactions documented in the MAM companion series.
>
> **Design principle:** An ecological network is a map of who eats whom, who helps whom, and what happens when nodes are removed. This document maps the avian nodes and edges of PNW ecosystems — the food webs that structure communities, the mutualisms that regenerate forests, the cascades that connect predators to plants, and the nutrient pathways that link ocean to mountaintop. Every network described here has been disrupted by human activity; understanding the network is the prerequisite for understanding the disruption.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation evidence presented without legislative positions. Only Level 1 (publicly available) cultural content. All Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations. ## I. Avian Food Webs of the PNW

### 1.1 Trophic Position Framework

PNW birds occupy every trophic level from primary consumer to apex predator. The following framework organizes approximately 500 regularly occurring species into functional feeding guilds:

**Trophic Level 1 — Primary Consumers (Herbivores)**
- **Granivores:** Sparrows, finches, juncos, towhees — consume seeds directly from plants or ground. Dominant guild in grassland, shrub-steppe, and agricultural zones (Zone 6, 7).
- **Folivores:** Grouse and ptarmigan — consume buds, leaves, and needles. Dusky Grouse and Sooty Grouse eat conifer needles in winter (>95% of diet); Greater Sage-Grouse is an obligate sagebrush folivore (>99% of winter diet) [Module 1B]. White-tailed Ptarmigan consumes alpine willow buds year-round [Module 1B].
- **Frugivores:** Waxwings (Cedar and Bohemian), thrushes (Varied Thrush, Hermit Thrush, Swainson's Thrush), American Robin — consume berries and soft fruits. Band-tailed Pigeon is the largest PNW frugivore, consuming elderberry, cascara, and madrone fruits in quantity [Module 1B].
- **Nectarivores:** Rufous Hummingbird and Anna's Hummingbird — primary nectar consumers and pollinators [Module 1B]. See Section III.1 for pollination networks.
- **Herbivorous waterfowl:** Trumpeter Swan, Tundra Swan, American Wigeon, and other dabbling ducks consume submerged aquatic vegetation, algae, and agricultural waste grain [Module 1A].

**Trophic Level 2 — Secondary Consumers (Insectivores and Omnivores)**
- **Aerial insectivores:** Swifts (Vaux's Swift, Black Swift), swallows (7 PNW species), nightjars (Common Nighthawk, Common Poorwill), flycatchers (11 Tyrannidae species) — capture insects on the wing [Modules 1B, 1C, 1D].
- **Bark/wood insectivores:** Woodpeckers (12 PNW species) — extract wood-boring beetle larvae and ants from tree bark and wood [Module 1C]. Nuthatches, Brown Creeper, and Black-and-white Warbler glean insects from bark surfaces [Modules 1D, 1E].
- **Foliage gleaners:** Warblers (20+ PNW species), vireos (4 species), kinglets (2 species), chickadees (4 species), bushtits — pick insects from leaf surfaces and twigs [Modules 1D, 1E].
- **Ground insectivores:** American Robin, Varied Thrush, Northern Flicker — probe and scratch for soil invertebrates. American Dipper — aquatic insectivore, foraging in fast-flowing streams for stonefly, mayfly, and caddisfly larvae [Module 1D].
- **Omnivores:** Corvids (10 PNW species), most waterfowl, many sparrows — consume mixed plant and animal material across trophic levels [Module 1C].

**Trophic Level 3 — Tertiary Consumers (Predators)**
- **Avian predators of birds (accipiters):** Cooper's Hawk, Sharp-shinned Hawk — specialized bird-hunting hawks. Northern Goshawk takes medium-sized birds (jays, grouse, woodpeckers) [Module 1B].
- **Mammalian-prey raptors (buteos):** Red-tailed Hawk, Ferruginous Hawk — primarily consume rodents and lagomorphs [Module 1B]. Northern Harrier uses sound-localization (facial disc) to hunt voles in grassland [Module 1B].
- **Fish-eating raptors:** Osprey (>99% fish diet), Bald Eagle (50-90% fish) — apex aquatic-system predators [Module 1B].
- **Generalist nocturnal predators:** Great Horned Owl — takes prey from insects to full-grown Snowshoe Hare; predates other owls and hawks [Module 1B]. Barred Owl — broad diet including mammals, crayfish, frogs, and birds [Module 1B].
- **Specialist nocturnal predators:** Northern Spotted Owl — Northern Flying Squirrel and red tree vole specialist [Module 1B]. Barn Owl — >90% voles and mice; the most acoustically precise predator in the PNW [Module 1B]. Great Gray Owl — vole specialist at forest-meadow ecotones [Module 1B].

**Trophic Level 4 — Apex Predators**
- **Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*):** Apex of aquatic food webs — fish, waterfowl, small mammals, carrion. Kleptoparasitizes Osprey. Occupies the terminal position in the DDT bioaccumulation chain: water → plankton → fish → eagle [Module 1B].
- **Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*):** Apex of open-terrain food webs — medium-sized mammals (jackrabbits, marmots, ground squirrels, young ungulates), game birds. Stoops from height at speeds to 240 km/h [Module 1B].
- **Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*):** Apex aerial predator — takes birds exclusively, in flight, at speeds exceeding 320 km/h in diving stoop. DDT-recovery flagship [Module 1C].
- **Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*):** Apex nocturnal predator — the only PNW predator that regularly kills skunks, porcupines, and other raptors. Talons exert approximately 500 psi grip force [Module 1B].

### 1.2 Food Web Structure by Ecoregion

The trophic architecture varies dramatically across PNW ecoregions. Each zone has a distinct food web structure shaped by the dominant vegetation, prey base, and predator guild:

#### Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine) Food Web

```
Whitebark pine seeds → Clark's Nutcracker (caching mutualist)
Alpine willows/forbs → White-tailed Ptarmigan → Golden Eagle
Insects (snowfield edges) → American Pipit, Horned Lark, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch
Ground squirrels → Golden Eagle, Prairie Falcon
Ptarmigan/Rosy-Finch → Golden Eagle (apex)
```

**Structure:** Short food chains (2-3 links), low species diversity, high specialization. The food web collapses to 3-5 resident species in winter. Golden Eagle is the sole apex predator above treeline. Clark's Nutcracker links Zone 1 to Zone 2 through seed-caching movements [Module 3].

**Vulnerability:** Extreme — removal of any node (whitebark pine, ptarmigan, nutcracker) fundamentally restructures the web. Climate-driven treeline advance is compressing the alpine zone from below [G7, G9].

#### Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest) Food Web

```
Conifer seeds/insects → Chickadees, Nuthatches, Kinglets, Creepers
Wood-boring beetles → Woodpeckers (12 species, cavity creation)
Woodpecker cavities → Secondary cavity nesters (38+ species)
Northern Flying Squirrel → Northern Spotted Owl (specialist)
Voles/Mice → Northern Saw-whet Owl, Great Gray Owl, Northern Pygmy-Owl
Grouse/Jays/Squirrels → Northern Goshawk (apex closed-canopy predator)
Varied Thrush/Robins → Cooper's Hawk, Sharp-shinned Hawk
Snowshoe Hare → Great Horned Owl, Northern Goshawk, Canada Lynx (MAM-01)
All small/medium birds → Great Horned Owl (apex nocturnal, cross-trophic)
```

**Structure:** Complex, multi-layered web with 200+ species during breeding season. The woodpecker cavity network (Section IV) is the structural keystone. Northern Spotted Owl, Northern Goshawk, and Great Horned Owl partition the predator niche by habitat specialization, prey size, and activity period [Module 1B, Module 3]. Barred Owl invasion is inserting a novel generalist predator into this web, disrupting the historical partitioning [G6].

**Vulnerability:** Moderate-to-high — old-growth loss simplifies the web by removing large snags (woodpecker habitat), multi-layered canopy (flying squirrel habitat), and complex understory (foraging substrate for thrushes and warblers). Barred Owl competitive displacement of Northern Spotted Owl represents real-time competitive exclusion [G6].

#### Zone 5 (Riparian/Wetland) Food Web

```
Salmon carcasses → Bald Eagle, Common Raven, American Crow, gulls
Aquatic insects → American Dipper, warblers, swallows
Fish (live) → Osprey (plunge-diver), Bald Eagle (surface-snatcher)
Belted Kingfisher (plunge-diver into shallow water)
Great Blue Heron (wading strike predator)
Frogs/Crayfish → Barred Owl, Great Blue Heron
Voles/Mice → Barn Owl, Short-eared Owl, Red-tailed Hawk
Riparian songbirds → Cooper's Hawk, Merlin
Waterfowl → Bald Eagle, Peregrine Falcon
All trophic levels → Bald Eagle (terminal consumer/scavenger)
```

**Structure:** High connectivity — riparian zones function as ecological corridors linking aquatic and terrestrial food webs. The salmon-nutrient pathway (Section II) passes through this zone, connecting marine, freshwater, and forest ecosystems. Riparian zones support the highest bird species richness per unit area of any PNW habitat [P18, Module 3].

#### Zone 6 (Shrub-Steppe) Food Web

```
Sagebrush → Greater Sage-Grouse (obligate folivore)
Seeds/Grain → Horned Lark, Vesper Sparrow, Brewer's Sparrow
Insects (grasshoppers) → Swainson's Hawk, Loggerhead Shrike
Ground squirrels → Ferruginous Hawk, Golden Eagle, Burrowing Owl
Jackrabbits → Golden Eagle, Ferruginous Hawk
Voles/Mice → Northern Harrier, Barn Owl, Short-eared Owl, Burrowing Owl
Sage-Grouse → Golden Eagle, Great Horned Owl, Common Raven (nest predator)
```

**Structure:** Horizontally extensive but vertically simplified — fewer trophic levels than forested zones. Ground-nesting dominates. Common Raven population increase (subsidized by human food sources) has elevated nest predation on sage-grouse beyond historical levels [G1, G4]. This is a human-modified food web: raven subsidies → increased predation → sage-grouse decline [Module 1C].

#### Zone 7 (Coastal/Marine) Food Web

```
Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Forage fish (herring, sand lance, eulachon)
Forage fish → Murrelets, Auklets, Puffins, Cormorants, Terns
Forage fish → Gulls, Bald Eagle, Osprey
Kelp forest fish → Pigeon Guillemot, Marbled Murrelet, Pelagic Cormorant
Sea urchins → Sea otter (MAM-03) → Kelp forest maintenance → Seabird habitat
Intertidal invertebrates → Black Oystercatcher, Surfbird, shorebirds
Marine mammals (seal pups, afterbirth) → Bald Eagle (scavenger)
Salmon (offshore) → SRKW (MAM-03) [marine link]
All levels → Bald Eagle, Glaucous-winged Gull (terminal consumers/scavengers)
```

**Structure:** Pelagic food webs are driven by upwelling productivity and forage fish abundance. The forage fish node (Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance, northern anchovy) is the critical bottleneck — virtually all marine bird species depend on forage fish directly or indirectly [Module 2C]. Forage fish population collapse cascades to seabird breeding failure within a single season.

### 1.3 Cross-Zone Food Web Connections

No PNW food web exists in isolation. Species that move between zones create network connections:

| Connection | Species/Pathway | Zones Linked | Mechanism |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| Altitudinal migration | Dusky/Sooty Grouse | 2 → 1 (winter) | Reverse elevation migration for conifer needles |
| Salmon nutrient transport | Bald Eagle, Common Raven | 7 → 5 → 2 | Carry salmon carcasses from streams into forest |
| Seed caching | Clark's Nutcracker | 1 ↔ 2 | Cache whitebark pine seeds across elevations |
| Irruptive movements | Red Crossbill, Evening Grosbeak | 1 ↔ 2 ↔ 3 | Track cone crops across elevation bands |
| Raptor migration | Red-tailed Hawk, Swainson's Hawk | All zones | Use Cascade ridge thermals connecting alpine to lowland |
| Seabird-forest nesting | Marbled Murrelet | 7 → 2 | Forage in marine, nest in old-growth forest |
| Urban-wild interface | Cooper's Hawk, Peregrine Falcon | 3 ↔ 4,5 | Hunt suburban bird feeders, nest on urban structures |

The Marbled Murrelet represents the most extreme cross-zone connection: a seabird that forages in coastal waters (Zone 7) but nests on moss-covered limbs of old-growth conifers up to 80 km inland and 1,500 m elevation (Zone 2). Loss of either habitat — marine forage fish or inland old-growth — breaks this connection [G9, P4, P5].

## II. The Salmon-Nutrient Pathway: Avian Nodes

### 2.1 The Marine-Derived Nutrient Cycle

The salmon-nutrient pathway is the PNW's defining cross-ecosystem process. Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) accumulate marine-derived nutrients — nitrogen-15, phosphorus, carbon-13 — during 1-5 years of ocean growth, then transport those nutrients to freshwater streams during spawning. The avian community is deeply embedded in every stage of this cycle.

**The complete pathway, with avian nodes identified:**

```
OCEAN
  Pacific salmon accumulate marine nutrients (N-15, P, C-13)
    ↓
FRESHWATER (spawning streams)
  Spawning salmon → American Dipper (forages on salmon eggs and alevin)
  Spawning salmon → Belted Kingfisher (captures juvenile salmonids)
  Post-spawning carcasses → Bald Eagle (primary avian scavenger)
  Post-spawning carcasses → Common Raven (secondary scavenger)
  Post-spawning carcasses → American Crow (riparian scavenger)
  Post-spawning carcasses → Glaucous-winged Gull, Herring Gull (stream aggregations)
  Post-spawning carcasses → Great Blue Heron (stream-edge forager)
    ↓
CARCASS TRANSPORT (away from stream channel)
  Grizzly bear drags carcasses 50-500 m into forest (MAM-01)
  Black bear drags carcasses 20-200 m into forest (MAM-01)
  Bald Eagle carries salmon to perch trees and nest platforms
  Common Raven carries salmon fragments to cache sites
  American Crow carries fragments to feeding areas
    ↓
DECOMPOSITION AND NUTRIENT RELEASE
  Marine-derived nitrogen (identifiable by N-15 isotopic signature)
  enters forest soil → trees within 500 m of salmon streams grow
  up to 3x faster than trees far from streams (Helfield & Naiman 2001)
    ↓
FOREST FEEDBACK
  Salmon-fertilized conifers shade streams (thermal regulation)
  Salmon-fertilized forests produce large woody debris → stream complexity
  Large conifers → old-growth forest → nesting habitat for
  Northern Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Northern Goshawk
    ↓
SALMON RETURN
  Healthy streams produce more salmon → cycle repeats
```

### 2.2 Avian Species at Salmon Streams

The following species aggregate at PNW salmon-spawning streams in predictable seasonal patterns, forming a distinct "salmon assemblage" within the riparian food web:

**Primary salmon consumers (direct feeding on carcasses, eggs, or juveniles):**

| Species | Role at Salmon Streams | Peak Season | Abundance |
|---------|----------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Bald Eagle | Carcass scavenger, kleptoparasite | October-January | 400-700+ at Skagit River alone |
| Common Raven | Carcass scavenger, fragment distributor | October-January | Common |
| American Crow | Carcass scavenger (riparian edge) | October-January | Common to abundant |
| Glaucous-winged Gull | Carcass gleaner, egg consumer | September-December | Common (coastal streams) |
| Great Blue Heron | Carcass scavenger, alevin predator | Year-round (peak spawning) | Uncommon to common |
| American Dipper | Salmon egg and alevin consumer | September-March | Uncommon (stream-specific) |
| Belted Kingfisher | Juvenile salmonid predator | Year-round | Uncommon |
| Common Merganser | Juvenile salmonid predator | Year-round | Common |
| Steller's Jay | Carcass gleaner (opportunistic) | October-January | Common |
| Black-billed Magpie | Carcass gleaner (east-side streams) | October-January | Common (east of Cascades) |

**Nutrient-pathway significance:** Willson & Halupka (1995) identified over 130 vertebrate species that directly consume Pacific salmon carcasses or eggs in the PNW. Among birds, Bald Eagle is the most significant carcass distributor after bears — eagles carry salmon from streams to perch trees (often large conifers 100-500 m from the stream), where unconsumed portions decompose and release marine-derived nitrogen into the surrounding forest soil. This creates a "halo" of elevated nitrogen around eagle perch trees, measurable through isotopic analysis [P11, P12, P13].

### 2.3 The Bald Eagle-Salmon Concentration

The winter aggregation of Bald Eagles at PNW salmon-spawning rivers represents one of the most spectacular wildlife concentrations in temperate North America and one of the most ecologically significant avian nutrient-transfer events documented anywhere.

**Key concentration sites:**

- **Skagit River, WA:** 400-700+ eagles annually, feeding on chum salmon (*O. keta*) carcasses. The largest wintering eagle concentration in the lower 48 states. Eagles occupy perch trees within 200 m of the river, creating a network of nutrient deposition nodes [G9, Module 1B].
- **Nooksack River, WA:** 200-400+ eagles. Chum and coho salmon.
- **Chilkat River, BC/AK:** 3,000-4,000 eagles in peak years — the largest documented eagle concentration in the world. Late-run chum salmon in open water.
- **Columbia River tributaries:** Smaller concentrations (50-200 eagles) at multiple salmon-bearing streams.

**Trophic dynamics at eagle concentrations:**

```
Spawned-out chum salmon (carcasses in river)
  → Bald Eagle (primary avian consumer — perch feeding and scavenging)
    → Kleptoparasitism: Eagle steals from other eagles (dominance hierarchy)
    → Carcass fragments dropped under perch trees
      → Common Raven and American Crow (secondary scavengers)
      → Steller's Jay (tertiary gleaner)
      → Soil decomposition → marine nitrogen in forest floor
  → Great Blue Heron (wading in shallows near carcass aggregations)
  → Glaucous-winged Gull (gleaning at carcass margins)
  → American Dipper (feeding on salmon eggs in spawning gravel)
```

Eagles at spawning streams exhibit a distinct dominance hierarchy: adult eagles (white head and tail) dominate perch sites and carcass access; subadult eagles (dark plumage, 1st-4th year) are displaced to secondary perches and marginal feeding sites. This age-stratified access creates a spatial gradient of nutrient deposition — adult perch trees receive more salmon-derived nitrogen than subadult perches [O1, G9].

### 2.4 The Salmon-Forest Feedback Loop

The avian contribution to the salmon-forest feedback loop is quantifiable through nitrogen isotope analysis:

**Measured effects (from published studies):**
- Trees within 500 m of salmon-bearing streams show elevated nitrogen-15 ratios (Helfield & Naiman 2001) [P13]
- Marine-derived nitrogen constitutes 22-24% of the foliar nitrogen in riparian Sitka spruce near salmon streams in British Columbia
- Salmon-fertilized trees grow at rates up to 3x higher than comparable trees along non-salmon-bearing streams [P13]
- Bear carcass-drag distance: 50-500 m (primary forest fertilization zone) [P12]
- Eagle carcass-carry distance: 100-500 m (secondary fertilization, concentrated at perch trees)
- Raven and crow fragment dispersal: 50-200 m (tertiary, dispersed pattern)

The combined effect of bear, eagle, and corvid carcass distribution creates a fertilization zone extending 500+ meters on either side of salmon streams — a total nutrient deposition corridor of 1+ km width. In watersheds with dense eagle populations (Skagit, Nooksack), the avian contribution to this corridor may equal or exceed the bear contribution, particularly in areas where grizzly bears are absent (most of western Washington and Oregon) and black bear density is lower than in coastal British Columbia [P11, P12, P13].

**The feedback completes:** Salmon-fertilized forests produce larger conifers. Larger conifers provide better nesting habitat for Bald Eagles (prefer trees >75 cm DBH, strongly prefer >100 cm DBH — Module 1B), Northern Spotted Owl (require old-growth >200 years), and Marbled Murrelet (nest on moss-covered limbs of large conifers). Larger trees fall into streams as large woody debris, creating pool-riffle complexity that improves salmon spawning and rearing habitat. Salmon return. The cycle completes.

## III. Mutualism Networks

### 3.1 Pollination: Hummingbirds and PNW Wildflowers

The PNW supports two primary hummingbird species — Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) and Anna's Hummingbird (*Calypte anna*) — plus two secondary species (Calliope Hummingbird, Black-chinned Hummingbird) with more limited PNW ranges. These hummingbirds are the primary avian pollinators in the region, forming mutualism networks with native tubular-flowered plants.

**The Rufous Hummingbird pollination network:**

Rufous Hummingbird is the most ecologically significant avian pollinator in the PNW. Its migration timing (arrival in PNW breeding grounds March-April, departure July-August) is synchronized with the sequential blooming of native nectar plants. The pollination network follows a phenological sequence:

| Month | Primary Nectar Plant | Habitat | Pollination Role |
|-------|---------------------|---------|-----------------|
| April | Red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*) | Forest edge, riparian | Primary pollinator — bill length matches corolla depth |
| May | Salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*) | Lowland forest understory | Important pollinator |
| May-June | Red columbine (*Aquilegia formosa*) | Forest openings, meadow edges | Primary pollinator — spurred corolla excludes most insects |
| June-July | Indian paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.) | Subalpine meadows, dry meadows | Primary pollinator — tubular red flowers |
| July-August | Fireweed (*Chamerion angustifolium*) | Burn scars, clearings, subalpine | Significant pollinator |
| July-August | Penstemon (*Penstemon* spp.) | Dry meadows, rocky slopes | Primary pollinator of red/tubular species |

**Network structure:** Rufous Hummingbird is a "central node" pollinator — it connects multiple plant species that bloom in sequence, maintaining pollen transfer across the growing season. The bird's innate preference for red and orange tubular flowers (color preference is innate, not learned — Module 1B) creates a functional guild of "hummingbird flowers" that share corolla morphology and bloom timing adapted to hummingbird service.

**Territorial dynamics and pollination efficiency:** Male Rufous Hummingbirds defend nectar patches of 200+ flowers, aggressively excluding all competitors including birds 10x their mass (Steller's Jay, Western Tanager). This territorial behavior concentrates pollen transfer within a defended patch, increasing pollination efficiency for the defended plant species. During periods of nectar scarcity, males abandon territorial defense and switch to "traplining" — visiting scattered flowers along a regular circuit — which increases cross-pollination distance but reduces per-flower visit frequency [Module 1B].

**The Anna's Hummingbird expansion:** Anna's Hummingbird, historically a California species, has expanded northward into the PNW over the past 60 years, reaching Puget Sound by the 1970s and now resident year-round from southern BC to Oregon. This range expansion has created a novel pollination dynamic — Anna's is the only PNW hummingbird present in winter, and its survival depends on winter-blooming ornamental plants (winter jasmine, flowering currant cultivars, Mahonia) and hummingbird feeders. The species' year-round residency creates winter pollination services unavailable before its arrival, but its dependence on anthropogenic nectar sources represents a novel and potentially fragile ecological dependency [O1, O2, Module 1B].

**Calliope Hummingbird (*Selasphorus calliope*):** The smallest bird in the PNW (2.5-3.3 g) occupies subalpine and montane meadows on the east side of the Cascades, pollinating alpine paintbrush and penstemon at elevations where Rufous Hummingbird is less common. Calliope and Rufous partition the pollination network by elevation and habitat: Rufous dominates low-to-mid-elevation forest edges, Calliope occupies higher-elevation open meadows [Module 1B].

**Climate vulnerability of pollination networks:** Rufous Hummingbird populations have declined 60-65% rangewide since 1970 (BBS trend -2.3% per year; Partners in Flight assessment — Module 1B). The primary hypothesized cause is phenological mismatch — climate warming advances flower bloom dates faster than hummingbird migration timing adjusts, creating temporal gaps between nectar availability and bird presence. A 2-week mismatch between red-flowering currant bloom (which has advanced by approximately 1 week per decade) and Rufous Hummingbird arrival can reduce nectar availability during the energy-critical post-migration period, with cascading effects on breeding success [O4, G4].

### 3.2 Seed Dispersal: Corvids and Forest Regeneration

#### 3.2.1 Clark's Nutcracker and Whitebark Pine

The Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) and whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) mutualism is the most ecologically consequential bird-plant interaction in PNW subalpine ecosystems. This mutualism, maintained for 5-10 million years across western North America, is now threatened by a convergence of disease, insects, and climate change.

**Mutualism mechanics:**

```
Whitebark pine produces large, wingless seeds (15 mm, energy-rich)
  → Cannot wind-disperse (too heavy, no wing)
  → ENTIRELY dependent on Clark's Nutcracker for dispersal
    ↓
Clark's Nutcracker harvests seeds from cones
  → Sublingual pouch carries up to 150 seeds per trip
  → Caches seeds in clusters of 1-15 at 2-3 cm depth
  → Cache sites: ridge crests, south-facing slopes, wind-scoured areas
    (selected for early snowmelt, enabling winter/spring cache retrieval)
  → Caching radius: 1-10 km from harvest tree
  → Annual cache total: 30,000-100,000 seeds per individual bird
    ↓
50-80% of cached seeds retrieved for food
20-50% of cached seeds germinate in situ
  → Unretrieved caches establish next generation of whitebark pine
  → Multi-seed caches produce characteristic clumped growth form
    (whitebark pine groves = germinated nutcracker caches)
```

**Mutualism collapse dynamics:**

The whitebark pine decline is driving a positive-feedback collapse loop:

```
White pine blister rust (Cronartium ribicola, introduced pathogen)
  → Kills 50-90% of whitebark pine in many PNW subalpine stands
    ↓
Mountain pine beetle (Dendroctonus ponderosae, expanding with warming)
  → Kills stressed, rust-weakened trees at accelerating rates
    ↓
Reduced cone crop
  → Nutcrackers abandon areas where mortality exceeds ~70%
    ↓
Fewer nutcrackers → Fewer cached seeds
  → Reduced whitebark pine regeneration
    ↓
Positive feedback: less pine → fewer birds → less regeneration → less pine
  → Subalpine pine forest converts to spruce-fir or alpine meadow
    → Loss of high-energy seed base for grouse, crossbills, bears (MAM-01)
    → Loss of snow-trapping function (whitebark pine groves create
       snowdrifts that persist into summer, feeding subalpine streams)
```

Whitebark pine was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 2022, making it the first tree species listed primarily because of the cascading effects on its mutualist disperser and the broader ecosystem [G1, P10, Module 1C].

#### 3.2.2 Steller's Jay and Oak/Pine Regeneration

Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) is the primary avian seed-disperser for Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) and several pine species in the PNW lowlands and foothills.

**Caching behavior:** Steller's Jays cache acorns, pine seeds, and other mast in bark crevices, soil, leaf litter, and other concealed locations throughout their home range. Unlike Clark's Nutcracker, whose caching is concentrated in specific microhabitats, Steller's Jay caches are dispersed widely across the forest landscape. Unretrieved caches germinate, contributing to oak and pine regeneration — particularly important in the Willamette Valley, Rogue Valley, and Puget lowland prairies where Oregon white oak savanna has declined by >90% from its historical extent [O5, Module 1C].

**Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*) caching:** Canada Jay caches food items (insects, berries, fungi, carrion, seeds) on tree branches using sticky saliva as adhesive. This non-soil-based caching strategy differs from the ground caches of nutcrackers and Steller's Jays and serves primarily as individual food storage rather than seed dispersal. However, cached berries may be colonized by fungi, and berry caches on branches may contribute to epiphytic dispersal in subalpine forests [Module 1C].

### 3.3 Mycorrhizal Spore Dispersal by Ground-Foraging Birds

Ectomycorrhizal fungi form obligate symbioses with PNW conifer roots, facilitating nutrient and water uptake in exchange for photosynthetic carbon. Fungi reproduce through spores contained in fruiting bodies (mushrooms, truffles). Many PNW mycorrhizal species produce hypogeous (below-ground) fruiting bodies (truffles) that cannot disperse spores by wind — they depend on animal consumption and fecal dispersal.

**Avian contributors to mycorrhizal spore dispersal:**

- **Sooty Grouse and Dusky Grouse:** Ground-foraging in montane forest; consume fungi and berries, dispersing spores through fecal deposits across the forest landscape [Module 1B].
- **Ruffed Grouse:** Ground-foraging in deciduous and mixed forest understory; frequent consumer of forest-floor fungi in autumn [Module 1B].
- **Band-tailed Pigeon:** Consumes mineral soil at traditional mineral springs (sodium-supplementation behavior), incidentally consuming mycorrhizal spore inoculum in soil. As a long-distance flier (daily commutes of 10-30 km), Band-tailed Pigeon may transport spore-bearing soil across landscape distances unmatched by any other PNW ground-foraging bird [Module 1B].
- **American Robin, Varied Thrush, Hermit Thrush:** Ground-foraging insectivores that incidentally consume and disperse fungal spores through soil disturbance and fecal deposits [Module 1D].

**Cross-module connection:** The Northern Flying Squirrel — the primary prey of Northern Spotted Owl — is the most important mammalian disperser of hypogeous mycorrhizal fungi in PNW forests. Flying squirrels consume truffles as 30-80% of their diet and deposit viable spores in feces across their home range. The Spotted Owl → Flying Squirrel → Truffle → Conifer Root → Forest Health chain represents a four-link dependency where the apex predator's presence maintains the integrity of the fungal network that sustains forest growth. Removal of old-growth forest breaks this chain at every link simultaneously [G6, MAM-02a, Module 1B].

## IV. Keystone Species and Community-Structuring Interactions

### 4.1 Woodpeckers as Ecosystem Engineers

Woodpeckers (Family Picidae, 12 PNW species) are the primary cavity creators in PNW forests. Their excavation activity produces the nesting and roosting infrastructure used by dozens of secondary cavity-nesting species — a classic ecosystem engineering role where one species creates physical habitat structure that other species require.

**The cavity cascade:**

```
Woodpecker excavates cavity in snag or dead wood
  → Woodpecker uses cavity for 1-2 breeding seasons
    → Abandoned cavity becomes available to secondary cavity nesters
      → Cavity persists for 5-30+ years depending on tree species and decay rate
        → Multiple species use the same cavity sequentially or in different seasons
```

**Cavity creator hierarchy (by cavity size and species served):**

| Woodpecker Species | Cavity Entrance (cm) | Tree DBH Required | Secondary Users |
|-------------------|---------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Pileated Woodpecker** | 8-10 (oval) | >38 cm | Northern Saw-whet Owl, Barrow's Goldeneye, Bufflehead, Wood Duck, American Kestrel, Vaux's Swift, Northern Flying Squirrel, Douglas Squirrel, bats (*Myotis* spp.) |
| **Northern Flicker** | 5-7 (round) | >20 cm | American Kestrel, Western Screech-Owl, Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Mountain Bluebird, European Starling, Northern Flying Squirrel |
| **Hairy Woodpecker** | 4-5 (round) | >15 cm | Mountain Chickadee, Chestnut-backed Chickadee, Red-breasted Nuthatch, House Wren |
| **Downy Woodpecker** | 3-4 (round) | >10 cm | Black-capped Chickadee, Chestnut-backed Chickadee, Red-breasted Nuthatch |
| **Red-breasted Sapsucker** | 3-4 (round) | >12 cm | Chickadees, nuthatches, small swallows |
| **White-headed Woodpecker** | 4-5 (round) | >25 cm (ponderosa) | Mountain Bluebird, Pygmy Nuthatch, White-breasted Nuthatch |
| **Black-backed Woodpecker** | 4-5 (round) | >15 cm (post-fire) | American Three-toed Woodpecker, Mountain Chickadee |

**Pileated Woodpecker as keystone:** The Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*) creates the largest cavities of any PNW woodpecker and is the only species that produces cavities large enough for Barrow's Goldeneye, Bufflehead, Wood Duck, and Vaux's Swift. At least 38 species of secondary cavity nesters have been documented using Pileated Woodpecker cavities in PNW forests. The loss of Pileated Woodpeckers from a forest stand initiates a cascade of secondary cavity nester declines within 5-15 years as existing cavities degrade and no new ones are created [G6, P18, Module 1C].

**Snag dependency:** Woodpecker cavity creation requires snags (standing dead trees) and trees with heartwood decay. PNW old-growth forests support 15-30+ snags per hectare of suitable excavation size; managed second-growth forests typically support 2-5 snags per hectare after harvest rotation. This 5-10x reduction in snag density directly limits woodpecker populations and cascades to the entire cavity-nesting community. Forest management guidelines for federal lands in the PNW require retention of minimum snag densities (typically 3-6 snags per hectare >25 cm DBH), but these thresholds are below the levels documented in old-growth stands and may be insufficient to maintain the full cavity-nesting community [G6, G8, P18].

### 4.2 Sapsucker Wells and Commensal Networks

Red-breasted Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus ruber*), Red-naped Sapsucker (*S. nuchalis*), and Williamson's Sapsucker (*S. thyroideus*) create and maintain sap wells — organized rows of small holes drilled through bark into the phloem of living trees. These sap wells attract insects to the sap flow, creating a concentrated food resource exploited by other species.

**Sapsucker well commensals in PNW forests:**

```
Sapsucker drills wells in living tree bark
  → Sap flows from wells (sugar-rich phloem exudate)
    → Sapsucker feeds on sap and insects attracted to flow
    → Rufous Hummingbird feeds on sap (supplemental nectar source)
    → Anna's Hummingbird feeds on sap (critical winter energy)
    → Ruby-crowned Kinglet, Orange-crowned Warbler (sap)
    → Butterflies, wasps, flies attracted to sap
      → Warblers and flycatchers prey on sap-attracted insects
```

**Hummingbird-sapsucker dependency:** In early spring, before nectar-producing wildflowers bloom, sapsucker sap wells may be the primary energy source for newly arrived Rufous Hummingbirds. The geographic correlation between sapsucker breeding range and early-season hummingbird distribution in the PNW supports a commensal dependency — hummingbirds track sapsucker activity as an early-season food bridge between winter dormancy and wildflower bloom [O1, Module 1B, Module 1C].

### 4.3 Cormorant and Seabird Colony Nutrient Deposition

Colonial nesting seabirds — cormorants (Double-crested, Pelagic, Brandt's), gulls (Glaucous-winged, Western, California), murres (Common Murre), and auklets — concentrate marine-derived nutrients at nesting colonies through guano deposition. This creates localized nutrient hotspots on offshore islands and coastal cliffs.

**Nutrient pathway:**

```
Marine food web (fish, invertebrates)
  → Colonial seabirds consume marine prey
    → Guano deposited at nesting colonies
      → Nitrogen and phosphorus enrichment of colony substrate
        → Enhanced vegetation growth on colony islands
        → Nutrient runoff into nearshore waters
          → Increased nearshore primary productivity
            → Enhanced intertidal invertebrate communities
              → Foraging habitat for shorebirds and sea ducks
```

**Quantitative significance:** A colony of 10,000 Double-crested Cormorants deposits approximately 20-30 metric tons of guano per breeding season, containing nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations 10-100x higher than surrounding soils. This guano-mediated nutrient transfer from pelagic marine food webs to terrestrial and nearshore ecosystems is a measurable ecological process at PNW colony sites including East Sand Island (Columbia River), Protection Island (WA), Mandarte Island (BC), and numerous offshore rocks along the Olympic and Oregon coasts [G1, G9].

### 4.4 American Dipper: Stream Health Indicator

The American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*) is the only truly aquatic passerine in the PNW — it forages by walking along stream bottoms, plunging into fast-flowing water, and swimming underwater using its wings. Dipper presence is a reliable indicator of stream water quality:

**Indicator relationships:**
- **Dippers require clean, well-oxygenated water** with abundant benthic macroinvertebrate communities (stoneflies, caddisflies, mayflies — the same organisms used in formal bioassessment protocols).
- **Dipper density correlates with macroinvertebrate density,** which correlates with water quality, stream temperature, dissolved oxygen, and absence of fine-sediment loading.
- **Dipper absence from historically occupied streams** signals degraded water quality, increased sedimentation, or elevated water temperature — all of which simultaneously degrade salmonid habitat.
- **At salmon streams,** dippers feed directly on salmon eggs and alevin, linking them to the salmon-nutrient pathway. Dipper populations along salmon-bearing streams are typically 2-3x denser than on comparable non-salmon streams, reflecting the energy subsidy from salmon-derived resources [O1, P11, Module 1D].

## V. Trophic Cascades Involving Birds

### 5.1 Wolf/Cougar → Elk → Riparian Vegetation → Songbird Habitat

The most consequential trophic cascade in PNW terrestrial ecosystems flows from apex mammalian predators through ungulate herbivores to riparian vegetation and ultimately to avian communities:

**The cascade mechanism:**

```
APEX PREDATOR PRESENT (wolf or cougar)
  → Elk and deer populations regulated by predation
  → Elk modify behavior: reduce time in exposed riparian zones ("ecology of fear")
    → Riparian vegetation recovers: willow, cottonwood, alder, dogwood
      → Increased canopy cover → stream shading → cooler water temperatures
      → Increased insect production on deciduous foliage
      → Dense shrub layer → nesting habitat for riparian songbirds
        → Yellow Warbler, Song Sparrow, Willow Flycatcher, Common Yellowthroat
        → Warbling Vireo, Red-eyed Vireo, Cedar Waxwing
        → American Robin (berry-producing shrub association)
      → Large cottonwood growth → cavity nesting (woodpeckers, owls)
      → Stream bank stabilization → salmon habitat quality
        → Salmon → eagles, dippers, herons, kingfishers
          ↓
COMPLETE CASCADE: wolf presence → songbird diversity through
  4 intermediate trophic links
```

**Documented in the PNW:**

- **Olympic National Park:** Ripple & Beschta (2006) documented that cougar population decline in the 1920s-1990s led to elk population increase, riparian vegetation overbrowsing, and catastrophic loss of cottonwood and bigleaf maple regeneration along the Hoh River. Riparian bird communities declined as shrub and tree nesting habitat was consumed by unnaturally large elk herds. This cascade operates in reverse from the wolf/elk cascade — cougar decline (rather than absence) drove the effect [MAM-01, Ripple & Beschta 2006].

- **Yellowstone (analog for PNW wolf recovery areas):** Following wolf reintroduction in 1995-96, willow and cottonwood recovery along Yellowstone's northern range riparian corridors increased songbird diversity by 25-40% within 15 years. Willow-nesting species (Yellow Warbler, Willow Flycatcher, Song Sparrow) showed the strongest responses. PNW wolf recovery areas in northeastern Washington and the north Cascades are expected to develop comparable cascading effects as wolf packs become established and elk behavior adjusts, though the timeline for measurable avian community change may be 15-30 years [MAM-01, Ripple et al. 2014].

- **Prediction for PNW:** As wolf populations expand (42 packs, 260+ individuals in WA by 2024 — MAM-01), riparian vegetation recovery in wolf-occupied watersheds should produce measurable increases in riparian songbird diversity relative to wolf-absent control areas. This natural experiment is currently underway; monitoring programs that pair wolf distribution mapping with point-count bird surveys along riparian corridors would provide the first PNW-specific data on wolf-to-songbird cascading effects.

**The reverse cascade (predator removal):**

```
APEX PREDATOR ABSENT
  → Elk/deer populations increase (released from predation)
  → Elk overbrowse riparian vegetation (no behavioral avoidance)
    → Riparian shrub layer eliminated
      → Songbird nesting habitat lost → species richness declines
      → Stream banks erode → water temperature increases
        → Salmon habitat degrades → eagle/dipper/kingfisher food base declines
    → Cottonwood regeneration fails → cavity trees lost in 50-100 years
      → Woodpecker populations decline → cavity-nesting cascade (Section IV.1)
```

### 5.2 Sea Otter → Kelp Forest → Marine Birds

The sea otter (*Enhydra lutris*) drives a three-level trophic cascade in nearshore marine ecosystems that directly structures the habitat available to marine bird communities:

**The cascade:**

```
Sea otter (keystone predator)
  → Eats sea urchins (20-25% body weight daily — MAM-03)
    → Controlled urchin populations cannot overgraze kelp
      → Kelp forest (bull kelp, giant kelp) persists
        → Kelp forest fish communities (juvenile rockfish, lingcod, greenling)
          → Pigeon Guillemot, Marbled Murrelet (diving for kelp-forest fish)
          → Pelagic Cormorant, Brandt's Cormorant (pursuing school fish in kelp)
          → Rhinoceros Auklet (foraging in kelp-adjacent waters)
        → Kelp canopy surface areas
          → Surf Scoter, White-winged Scoter (resting, foraging)
          → Harlequin Duck (nearshore kelp-associated foraging)
        → Kelp wrack line (beach-cast kelp debris)
          → Shorebirds (Black Turnstone, Surfbird) foraging invertebrates in wrack
        → Wave energy dampening
          → Calmer nearshore waters suitable for murrelet and auklet foraging
```

**Without sea otters:**

```
Sea otter absent (extirpated)
  → Sea urchin populations explode (no predator control)
    → "Urchin barrens" replace kelp forests
      → Featureless underwater desert — no fish shelter, no invertebrate habitat
        → Kelp-associated seabird species lose foraging habitat
        → Cormorant and guillemot foraging success declines
        → Murrelet foraging range extends (increased energy cost)
        → Sea duck resting habitat reduced
```

**PNW status:** Sea otters were extirpated from Washington, Oregon, and British Columbia by the maritime fur trade by the early 1900s. Reintroduction of 59 otters to the Olympic coast in 1969-70 has produced a population of approximately 2,500-2,800 by 2023, confined to the outer Olympic coast (Destruction Island to Point Grenville). As this population expands, the kelp forest cascade is slowly reestablishing — and kelp-associated seabird communities are expected to benefit. The sea otter has not recolonized Puget Sound, the San Juan Islands, or the Oregon coast, meaning that marine bird communities in these areas continue to experience the otter-absent food web state [MAM-03, G5, G9].

### 5.3 Spotted Owl / Barred Owl Competitive Displacement

The invasion of the Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) into the PNW from eastern North America represents a real-time competitive exclusion event that is restructuring the nocturnal predator guild of old-growth forests:

**Pre-invasion food web (historical, pre-1970s):**

```
Northern Spotted Owl — specialist predator in old-growth
  Primary prey: Northern Flying Squirrel (30-60% of diet by biomass)
  Secondary prey: Red tree vole (10-30%), woodrats, insects
  Niche: old-growth forest interior, strictly nocturnal, sit-and-wait predation

Great Horned Owl — generalist apex predator
  Niche: forest edges, open areas, all habitat types
  Predates: Spotted Owl fledglings and adults (significant mortality source)

Western Screech-Owl — small cavity-nesting owl
  Niche: lowland woodland, riparian, suburban

Northern Pygmy-Owl — diurnal small-bird predator
  Niche: forest edge, canopy gaps
```

**Post-invasion food web (current):**

```
Barred Owl (INVASIVE) — generalist predator, expanding rapidly
  Primary prey: broader than Spotted Owl — voles, mice, shrews,
    crayfish, frogs, salamanders, fish, birds, flying squirrels, woodrats
  Niche: old-growth AND second-growth forest, riparian, suburban
    → Overlaps completely with Spotted Owl niche
    → Extends into habitats Spotted Owl cannot use
  Competitive advantages:
    → 15-25% larger and heavier than Spotted Owl
    → Higher reproductive rate (0.8-1.5 vs. 0.4-0.8 fledglings/pair/year)
    → Broader habitat tolerance (60-80 year second-growth sufficient)
    → More generalized diet (crayfish, frogs expand resource base)
    → Earlier age at first breeding (2-3 vs. 3-5 years)

  → Displaces Northern Spotted Owl from territories
  → Outcompetes Spotted Owl for shared prey
  → Occasionally hybridizes ("Sparred Owls" — fertile offspring)
  → Possibly predates Western Screech-Owl and Northern Pygmy-Owl
  → Population growing at 10-15% per year in newly colonized PNW habitat

Northern Spotted Owl — declining 3-8% per year
  → WA population: <500 individuals (down ~70% since monitoring began)
  → OR population: ~1,500-2,500 (down ~50-60%)
  → USFWS experimental Barred Owl removal program (2015-present)
    → Preliminary results: Spotted Owl territory occupancy stabilizes
       or increases in removal areas
```

**Cascading effects of the competitive displacement:**

The replacement of a specialist predator (Spotted Owl) by a generalist predator (Barred Owl) has community-level consequences beyond the owls themselves:

1. **Prey community restructuring:** Barred Owl's broader diet means increased predation pressure on species not historically targeted by Spotted Owl — crayfish, amphibians, small birds, and a wider range of small mammals. The net effect is a diffuse increase in predation pressure across more prey species rather than concentrated pressure on flying squirrels and tree voles [G6, O1].

2. **Small owl displacement:** Western Screech-Owl populations have declined in areas of dense Barred Owl colonization. Barred Owls may be predating or competitively displacing screech-owls through direct interference (larger owl evicting smaller owl from territory). Northern Pygmy-Owl, a diurnal microraptor, may also face increased predation risk from Barred Owl [G6, O1, Module 1B].

3. **Hybridization risk:** Barred Owl × Spotted Owl hybrids ("Sparred Owls") produce fertile offspring, confirming that the two species diverged recently (~4-5 Ma) and are not fully reproductively isolated. If hybridization rates increase, the Northern Spotted Owl genome could be functionally absorbed into the more abundant Barred Owl population — genetic extinction without complete population extinction [G6, Module 1B].

### 5.4 Raven Subsidies and Nest Predation Cascades

Common Raven (*Corvus corax*) populations have increased dramatically across the PNW over the past 40 years (BBS trend +2.5% per year, 1966-2019), driven by human food subsidies — landfills, agricultural waste, roadkill, and other anthropogenic food sources. This human-subsidized population increase has cascading effects on ground-nesting species:

**The subsidy-predation cascade:**

```
Human activity provides food subsidies
  → Landfills, livestock operations, roadkill, agricultural waste
    → Raven populations increase beyond natural carrying capacity
      → Subsidized ravens disperse into previously unoccupied habitat
        → Increased nest predation on:
          → Greater Sage-Grouse (egg predation at ground nests — primary concern)
          → Marbled Murrelet (egg predation at old-growth nest platforms)
          → Snowy Plover (ground nests on beaches)
          → Desert tortoise (juvenile predation — analog from Great Basin)
```

**Sage-grouse-raven dynamics:** In the sagebrush steppe (Zone 6), raven nest predation is now a significant additional mortality factor for Greater Sage-Grouse, which nests on the ground under sagebrush canopy. Historically, raven density in remote sagebrush habitats was low. Anthropogenic infrastructure (power lines providing perch sites, livestock operations providing food) has increased raven presence in sagebrush habitat by 3-10x over the past 50 years. The combination of habitat fragmentation (increasing nest-edge exposure) and elevated raven density (human-subsidized) creates a synergistic predation pressure that exceeds the historical predation regime under which sage-grouse evolved [G1, G4, P14, Module 1B, Module 1C].

**Management response:** USFWS and state agencies have implemented raven management programs in sage-grouse priority areas, including lethal removal of ravens at livestock facilities and experimental egg oiling to reduce raven reproduction. These programs are controversial — the Common Raven is a native species, and its population increase is a consequence of human activity rather than an invasion. The ethical complexity of killing one native species to protect another, when the proximate cause of the problem is human food subsidies, reflects the broader challenge of managing novel ecological interactions in human-modified landscapes [G1, Module 1C].

## VI. Predator-Prey Dynamics

### 6.1 Raptor-Small Mammal Cycles

Several PNW raptor species show population dynamics linked to cyclical or irruptive prey populations:

#### Owl-Vole Cycles

**Barn Owl (*Tyto alba*) — Vole (*Microtus*) dynamics:**

Barn Owl reproductive output is directly tied to vole abundance. In years of high vole density, Barn Owls:
- Produce larger clutches (up to 18 eggs documented, vs. typical 4-7)
- Occasionally double-brood (2 successful clutches per year)
- Achieve higher fledging success
- Show increased population the following year

In vole crash years, Barn Owl reproduction declines sharply, and adult winter mortality increases. This numerical response tracks the approximately 3-4-year vole cycle documented in Willamette Valley and Puget lowland agricultural landscapes. A single Barn Owl family consumes an estimated 3,000-4,000 rodents per year — ecological rodent control valued by agricultural operations [Module 1B, O1].

**Short-eared Owl (*Asio flammeus*) — Vole dynamics:**

Short-eared Owl shares the Barn Owl's vole dependency but hunts crepuscularly and diurnally over open grassland rather than nocturnally over agricultural fields. Short-eared Owl density tracks vole abundance even more tightly than Barn Owl, with nomadic irruptive movements — owls concentrate in areas of high vole density and largely disappear from areas where voles are scarce. Short-eared Owl and Northern Harrier occupy the same grassland habitat and share the vole prey base, creating intraguild competition. The two species partition prey access temporally: harrier hunts diurnally, short-eared owl hunts crepuscularly and nocturnally [Module 1B, Module 3].

**Great Gray Owl (*Strix nebulosa*) — Vole dynamics at forest-meadow ecotones:**

Great Gray Owl forages exclusively at the interface between dense conifer forest (roosting and nesting) and open meadow (hunting). Its enormous facial disc — the largest of any PNW owl, up to 50 cm diameter — functions as a parabolic sound reflector, enabling precise localization of vole movements beneath 30-50 cm of snow. Great Gray Owl can plunge through crusted snow to capture voles detected entirely by sound. This snow-plunging behavior represents the most extreme auditory-hunting adaptation documented in PNW raptors [Module 1B, O1].

#### Lynx-Snowshoe Hare-Raptor Interactions

The Canada Lynx (*Lynx canadensis*) — Snowshoe Hare (*Lepus americanus*) population cycle (8-11 year period in boreal systems, dampened in PNW) indirectly affects the raptor community through prey availability:

```
Snowshoe Hare population peaks
  → Northern Goshawk benefits (hare = significant prey item, especially winter)
  → Great Horned Owl benefits (hare = regular prey, especially in forest-edge habitat)
  → Canada Lynx populations track hare cycle (MAM-01)
    ↓
Snowshoe Hare population crashes
  → Goshawk may switch to alternative prey (grouse, squirrels)
  → Great Horned Owl switches to alternative prey (skunks, rabbits, other owls)
  → Lynx population crashes with 1-2 year lag
  → Competitive release for smaller prey species → temporary increase in
     voles, mice → benefits Saw-whet Owl, Long-eared Owl, Pygmy-Owl
```

The hare cycle is dampened at the southern edge of the boreal zone (PNW), and PNW raptors are generally less cycle-dependent than their boreal counterparts. However, Snowshoe Hare remains a significant prey item for Northern Goshawk in the montane zone, and goshawk territory occupancy in northeastern Washington and the north Cascades correlates with hare density indices [Module 1B, MAM-01].

### 6.2 Hawk-Songbird Dynamics

Accipiters (Cooper's Hawk, Sharp-shinned Hawk) are specialized bird-hunting hawks whose populations are linked to songbird abundance:

**Cooper's Hawk (*Accipiter cooperii*) — Suburban bird feeder dynamics:**

Cooper's Hawk has undergone one of the strongest population increases of any PNW raptor (BBS trend +2.1% per year), driven in part by the proliferation of bird feeders in suburban landscapes. Bird feeders concentrate songbird prey (robins, jays, starlings, doves, finches) in predictable locations, reducing hawk search time and increasing foraging efficiency. Cooper's Hawks have colonized urban parks, residential neighborhoods, and suburban backyards throughout the PNW, becoming the most common raptor in many urban areas.

**The feeder-predator paradox:** The same bird feeders that support songbird populations in winter also attract and support their predators. Feeder-associated Cooper's Hawk predation is a significant but usually sustainable mortality source for common feeder birds. However, for uncommon species that concentrate at feeders (Band-tailed Pigeon, which is declining for other reasons), feeder predation may represent additive mortality that compounds population stress [Module 1B, O1, O2].

**Sharp-shinned Hawk — Forest interior dynamics:**

Sharp-shinned Hawk, the smallest PNW accipiter, takes proportionally smaller prey (sparrows, warblers, kinglets, chickadees) than Cooper's Hawk and hunts more in forest interior. Sharp-shinned Hawk breeding territories in montane conifer forest are spaced at densities reflecting the local small-bird prey base. Migration concentrations at Cascade ridge hawk-watch sites (Chelan Ridge, Bonney Butte) provide annual indices of Sharp-shinned Hawk population trends [Module 1B, Module 3, O16].

### 6.3 Raptor Predation on Small Mammals (Avian-Mammalian Cross-Links)

Raptors are primary predators of small mammals documented in the MAM companion series:

| Raptor Species | Primary Mammalian Prey | Prey Module | Ecosystem Context |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Red-tailed Hawk | Voles (*Microtus*), ground squirrels, pocket gophers | MAM-02a | Agricultural/grassland pest regulation |

| Northern Harrier | Voles (*Microtus*) | MAM-02a | Grassland, marsh |

| Northern Spotted Owl | Northern Flying Squirrel, red tree vole | MAM-02a | Old-growth specialist |
| Barred Owl | Voles, mice, shrews, flying squirrels | MAM-02a, 02c | Generalist, expanding |

**Aggregate ecological significance:** Raptors collectively consume millions of small mammals annually across the PNW, providing biological pest regulation in agricultural and grassland ecosystems. Barn Owl nest-box programs are explicitly designed to harness this service — a single Barn Owl family consuming 3,000-4,000 rodents per year provides measurable pest suppression in orchards and crop fields. The economic value of raptor-mediated rodent control has been estimated at $100-200 per raptor per year in agricultural landscapes [Module 1B, MAM-02a, O1].

## VII. Scavenging Networks

### 7.1 Terrestrial Scavenging Guild

Scavenging is a critical ecological process that accelerates nutrient cycling and prevents disease transmission from carrion. PNW birds are the dominant vertebrate scavengers in most ecosystems.

**The scavenging hierarchy:**

| Tier | Species | Scavenging Role | Detection Method | Access Ability |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 (Primary) | Turkey Vulture | Obligate scavenger | Olfaction (only PNW bird with developed smell) | Tears soft tissue with hooked bill |
| 1 (Primary) | Common Raven | Facultative scavenger | Visual (soaring) + social information | Strong bill, can open hide at wounds |
| 2 (Secondary) | Bald Eagle | Facultative scavenger/predator | Visual (perch and soar) | Powerful bill and talons |
| 2 (Secondary) | Golden Eagle | Facultative scavenger (winter) | Visual | Powerful bill and talons |
| 3 (Tertiary) | American Crow | Opportunistic scavenger | Visual + social learning | Limited (cannot open hide easily) |
| 3 (Tertiary) | Black-billed Magpie | Opportunistic scavenger | Visual | Limited (uses openings) |
| 3 (Tertiary) | Steller's Jay | Incidental scavenger | Visual + acoustic (follows ravens) | Limited (small fragments) |
| 4 (Specialist) | Gray Jay / Canada Jay | Carcass cacher | Visual | Caches fragments using saliva |

**Detection and arrival sequence at a large carcass (e.g., winter-killed elk):**

```
Hours 0-2: Turkey Vulture detects carcass by olfaction (ethyl mercaptan)
  → Circles above carcass; descent signals location to other scavengers
Hours 1-4: Common Raven arrives (visual detection of vulture aggregation)
  → Ravens open carcass at natural openings (eyes, wounds, anus)
  → Raven vocalizations recruit additional ravens (information sharing)
Hours 2-8: Bald Eagle arrives at large carcasses near water
  → Dominates ravens and vultures at carcass through size/aggression
Hours 4-24: American Crow arrives (follows raven activity)
  → Feeds at margins, takes small fragments
Days 1-3: Black-billed Magpie (east-side), Steller's Jay (west-side)
  → Glean fragments from edges and bone surfaces
Days 1-7: Canada Jay (montane sites)
  → Caches fragments on tree branches using adhesive saliva
```

**Turkey Vulture as keystone scavenger:** Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*) is the only obligate avian scavenger in the PNW — it never kills live prey. Its olfactory capacity (proportionally the largest olfactory bulb of any bird) enables detection of ethyl mercaptan, a decomposition gas, at parts-per-trillion concentrations. This olfactory detection guides other scavengers that lack olfactory capability — ravens and eagles follow vulture aggregations to locate carcasses they cannot detect independently. Turkey Vulture is thus the "pathfinder" of the scavenging network. Its spring arrival in the PNW (March-April) coincides with the peak of winter-killed ungulate carcass availability [Module 1B, O1].

### 7.2 Wolf Kill Scavenging

Wolf kills provide carrion subsidies to a documented community of avian scavengers. In wolf recovery areas of the PNW (northeastern Washington, north Cascades), the establishment of wolf packs creates a network of predictable carcass sites:

**Avian scavengers at wolf kills:**

```
Wolf pack kills elk or deer
  → Wolf pack feeds for 2-5 days, consuming 50-80% of carcass
    → Remainder available to scavengers:
      → Common Raven (primary avian scavenger at wolf kills)
        → Ravens follow wolf packs and arrive at kills within hours
        → Raven-wolf relationship is ancient: ravens vocalize near
           potential prey, possibly alerting wolves; wolves create
           carcasses ravens cannot produce on their own
      → Bald Eagle (at kills near water or open areas)
      → Golden Eagle (at kills in open terrain, east-side steppe)
      → Black-billed Magpie (at kills in open country, east of Cascades)
      → American Crow (at kills near urban-wildland interface)
      → Turkey Vulture (spring-fall only; seasonal presence)
```

**Raven-wolf coevolution:** The Common Raven shows behavioral adaptations to wolf predation that suggest deep coevolutionary history. Ravens track wolf packs across the landscape, using their aerial vantage to monitor wolf movements and arrive at kills rapidly. Ethnographic accounts from multiple PNW nations describe raven-wolf association — in Kwakwaka'wakw and Tlingit traditions, raven and wolf are linked figures, with raven as the guide and beneficiary of wolf's hunting [Module 1C, MAM-01, C2, C10].

### 7.3 Marine Scavenging: Whale Falls and Beach-Cast Carcasses

**Whale carcass scavenging (beach-cast):**

When whale carcasses wash ashore on PNW beaches, they create massive, short-term food subsidies:

```
Beach-cast whale carcass
  → Bald Eagle aggregation (dozens may gather at large carcasses)
  → Common Raven (primary corvid scavenger on beaches)
  → Glaucous-winged Gull, Western Gull (large gulls tear tissue)
  → American Crow (follows gulls and ravens)
  → Turkey Vulture (coastal populations; olfactory detection)
```

**Beach-cast marine mammal scavenging** (seal, sea lion carcasses) follows the same hierarchy at smaller scale. Bald Eagles on the Olympic and Oregon coasts regularly scavenge dead seals, sea lion pups, and pinniped afterbirth at haul-out sites [MAM-03, Module 2C].

**Pelagic scavenging and feeding events:** Offshore, Northern Fulmar, Sooty Shearwater, and various albatross species scavenge whale carcasses floating at the surface. These pelagic scavenging events are rarely observed but may be ecologically significant for pelagic seabird populations during the non-breeding season [Module 2C].

## VIII. Climate Change Effects on Ecological Networks

### 8.1 Phenological Mismatches

Climate warming is advancing the timing of spring events (plant bloom, insect emergence, snowmelt) at different rates for different organisms, creating temporal mismatches between interacting species:

**Documented and predicted mismatches in PNW avian networks:**

| Network Link | Mismatch Type | Mechanism | Evidence |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|----------|
| Rufous Hummingbird ↔ Red-flowering currant | Temporal | Bloom advancing ~1 week/decade; migration timing slower to adjust | Rufous Hummingbird declining 60-65% rangewide since 1970 [O4] |
| White-tailed Ptarmigan ↔ Alpine insect emergence | Temporal | Snowmelt date advancing; chick hatch timing constrained by physiology | Predicted — alpine insectivore/chick survival models [G7] |
| Spotted Owl ↔ Northern Flying Squirrel | Spatial | Flying squirrel habitat shifting upslope with warming; owl territories fixed | Documented decline in flying squirrel capture rates in some long-term study areas [G6] |
| Marbled Murrelet ↔ Forage fish | Temporal/Spatial | Ocean warming shifting forage fish distribution; murrelet nest timing fixed | Declining murrelet productivity; forage fish timing disruption [G9] |
| Bark beetle outbreaks ↔ Woodpecker response | Intensity | Warmer winters → larger, more synchronized bark beetle outbreaks → exceeding woodpecker response capacity | Mountain pine beetle outbreak extent exceeding historical norms [G8] |
| Sage-Grouse ↔ Sagebrush | Spatial | Cheatgrass invasion (fire-facilitated by warming) converting sagebrush to annual grassland | Sagebrush habitat loss accelerating in eastern OR/WA [P14, G1] |

### 8.2 Range Shifts Disrupting Established Relationships

Climate-driven range shifts are rearranging the species composition of PNW communities, breaking established network connections and creating novel interactions:

**Upslope movement:**
- **Barred Owl:** Expanding into higher-elevation forests as conditions moderate, increasing encounter rates with Northern Spotted Owl and potentially displacing Western Screech-Owl at higher elevations [G6, Module 1B].
- **Common Raven:** Expanding into alpine zones formerly unoccupied, increasing predation on White-tailed Ptarmigan and alpine ground-nesting species [Module 1C, Module 3].
- **Anna's Hummingbird:** Northward range expansion into the PNW over 60 years, now resident year-round as far north as southern BC. This inserts a novel year-round hummingbird into a system that historically had only seasonal hummingbird presence [Module 1B].

**Poleward movement:**
- **Great Egret, Cattle Egret:** Expanding northward into the PNW from historical California range. Great Egret is now a regular breeder in the Willamette Valley. These species insert novel wading-bird predators into PNW wetland food webs [Module 1B].
- **Red-shouldered Hawk, White-tailed Kite:** Both expanding northward into southern Oregon from California. These species add novel raptor nodes to PNW food webs [Module 1B].

**Alpine contraction:**
- **White-tailed Ptarmigan:** The most climate-vulnerable bird in the PNW. Alpine habitat area is contracting upslope at 1-3 m elevation per decade. Climate models project 40-70% loss of suitable habitat in the Washington Cascades by 2080. For a species already at mountaintops, upslope retreat is impossible — this is the "escalator to extinction" [G9, G7, Module 1B, Module 3].
- **Clark's Nutcracker:** Whitebark pine decline (driven by climate-facilitated blister rust and mountain pine beetle) is causing nutcracker range contraction as birds abandon areas with >70% pine mortality. This breaks the nutcracker-pine mutualism, halting pine regeneration and accelerating ecosystem conversion [P10, G1, Module 1C].

### 8.3 Cascading Network Effects of Warming

The interconnected nature of PNW ecological networks means that climate effects cascade through multiple trophic levels:

**Cascade 1: Ocean warming → Forage fish → Seabirds → Predators**
```
Pacific Ocean warming (PDO warm phase, marine heat waves)
  → Forage fish (herring, sand lance, anchovy) shift distribution or decline
    → Seabird breeding failure (murrelets, auklets, puffins, terns)
      → Reduced prey for Peregrine Falcon (fewer seabirds at colonies)
      → Reduced prey for Bald Eagle at marine sites
      → Altered scavenging dynamics (fewer dead seabirds)
```

**Cascade 2: Snowpack decline → Stream temperature → Salmon → Eagles/Dippers**
```
Reduced winter snowpack (documented 20-30% decline in PNW since 1950s)
  → Earlier, shorter spring runoff → lower summer stream flows
    → Warmer stream temperatures → salmon habitat degradation
      → Reduced salmon runs → less carrion for eagles, ravens, gulls
      → Reduced salmon egg/alevin availability → American Dipper decline
      → Weaker salmon-forest nutrient feedback → slower forest growth
        → Long-term reduction in old-growth nesting habitat quality
```

**Cascade 3: Fire regime shift → Forest structure → Cavity community**
```
Climate-driven increase in wildfire frequency and severity
  → More frequent stand-replacing fire in montane zone
    → Short-term: Snag creation → Black-backed Woodpecker, Lewis's Woodpecker boom
    → Long-term: If fire return interval < 80 years, forest never reaches old-growth
      → Permanent loss of large-diameter snags → Pileated Woodpecker decline
        → Cavity-nesting community collapse (Section IV.1)
      → Loss of multi-layered canopy → Spotted Owl, Goshawk, flying squirrel decline
        → Mycorrhizal spore dispersal network degraded
```

## IX. Network Resilience and Vulnerability Assessment

### 9.1 Network Vulnerability Rankings

The following assessment ranks PNW avian ecological networks by vulnerability to disruption, based on: (1) number of single-point-of-failure nodes, (2) redundancy of functional roles, (3) speed of disruption relative to recovery capacity, and (4) documented decline trajectory.

| Network | Vulnerability | Key Vulnerability | Redundancy |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|------------|
| **Clark's Nutcracker ↔ Whitebark Pine** | CRITICAL | Obligate mutualism; no substitute disperser. Blister rust + beetle + climate = triple threat. Positive feedback collapse underway. | NONE — no other PNW bird disperses wingless pine seeds at landscape scale |
| **Northern Spotted Owl food web** | CRITICAL | Specialist predator losing to generalist invader (Barred Owl). Low reproductive rate prevents population recovery. Old-growth habitat continuing to decline on non-federal lands. | LOW — no functional substitute for old-growth specialist predator niche |
| **Rufous Hummingbird pollination network** | HIGH | 60-65% population decline. Phenological mismatch with nectar plants. Migration timing difficult to adjust. | MODERATE — Anna's Hummingbird fills some pollination function but not same species/timing/elevation |
| **Salmon-nutrient pathway (avian nodes)** | HIGH | Dependent on salmon run health (declining in many watersheds). Grizzly bear absent from most of pathway in WA/OR. Eagle populations stable but pathway efficiency reduced. | MODERATE — multiple avian scavengers, but bears are irreplaceable for large-volume transport |
| **Alpine food web** | HIGH | Climate-driven habitat compression. Alpine zone has no upslope refuge. Ptarmigan and rosy-finch at existential risk. | LOW — specialized alpine species have no lowland substitutes |
| **Sea otter ↔ Kelp forest ↔ Seabirds** | MODERATE-HIGH | Otter recovery slow (2,500 from 59 founders). Range limited to Olympic coast. Puget Sound, San Juans, Oregon coast remain in "urchin barren" state. | LOW — no substitute for sea otter urchin control |
| **Woodpecker cavity cascade** | MODERATE | Dependent on snag availability, which is reduced in managed forests. Pileated Woodpecker is the irreplaceable large-cavity creator. | MODERATE — multiple woodpecker species create cavities, but only Pileated creates large cavities |
| **Wolf → Elk → Riparian → Songbird cascade** | MODERATE | Wolf recovery underway but slow (42 packs in WA). Cascade effects require decades to manifest in vegetation and bird communities. | MODERATE — cougar provides partial predation function but different behavioral effects on elk |
| **Lowland/Agricultural food web** | LOW | High species diversity, generalist species, broad dietary flexibility. Red-tailed Hawk, Barn Owl, American Kestrel populations generally stable. | HIGH — multiple species fill each functional role |

### 9.2 Critical Monitoring Targets

Based on the vulnerability assessment, the following network monitoring targets would provide the most informative early-warning signals for PNW avian ecological network health:

1. **Clark's Nutcracker occupancy in whitebark pine stands** — annual cache-site density surveys along the Cascade crest would track mutualism health. Nutcracker abandonment of pine stands is a leading indicator of irreversible ecosystem conversion.

2. **Northern Spotted Owl territory occupancy** — existing USFS monitoring protocols (demography studies) provide direct measurement of competitive displacement by Barred Owl. Trends in territory occupancy are the most sensitive indicator of population trajectory [G6].

3. **Rufous Hummingbird phenology** — citizen science (eBird) first-arrival dates compared with red-flowering currant bloom dates would quantify phenological mismatch in real time [O2].

4. **Bald Eagle density at salmon-spawning streams** — annual winter eagle counts (e.g., Skagit River survey) track the salmon-nutrient pathway health as integrated by the apex avian consumer [G9].

5. **Woodpecker cavity density in managed forests** — snag surveys on national forest lands would assess the adequacy of retention guidelines for maintaining the cavity-nesting community [G6, G8].

6. **White-tailed Ptarmigan alpine occupancy** — point counts at established alpine sites would track the species most immediately threatened by climate-driven habitat loss in the PNW [G9].

## X. Network Synthesis: Birds as Connectors

### 10.1 The Unique Role of Birds in PNW Ecosystems

Birds are disproportionately important as network connectors relative to their biomass because of three traits no other vertebrate group shares:

1. **Powered flight:** Birds cross habitat boundaries that are barriers to terrestrial species. The Marbled Murrelet connects ocean to old-growth forest. Clark's Nutcracker connects alpine to montane. Bald Eagle connects marine, freshwater, and forest. Rufous Hummingbird connects California wintering grounds to PNW breeding grounds to subalpine meadows — spanning 6,000+ km of latitude and 3,000+ m of elevation annually.

2. **Migration:** The Pacific Flyway funnels 350+ migratory species through the PNW seasonally, creating temporary network connections between tropical, temperate, and arctic ecosystems. A Rufous Hummingbird pollinates flowers in Mexican cloud forest in February, PNW subalpine meadows in July, and Arizona montane forest in September — connecting ecosystems across 12 degrees of latitude within a single year.

3. **Metabolic rate:** Birds consume 2-10x more food per unit body mass than mammals, creating high-throughput energy and nutrient flows. Hummingbirds operate at the physical limits of vertebrate metabolism (1,200+ heartbeats per minute during hovering). This metabolic intensity means that even small-bodied birds exert ecological effects disproportionate to their mass.

### 10.3 Conservation Network Priorities

The ecological networks documented in this module suggest five conservation priorities that operate at the network level rather than the single-species level:

1. **Maintain the salmon-forest nutrient cycle.** Salmon recovery, riparian buffer protection, and eagle/corvid scavenger access to spawning streams are components of a single ecological system. Managing any one component in isolation misses the network architecture.

2. **Preserve the old-growth cavity cascade.** Snag retention in managed forests, Pileated Woodpecker population monitoring, and secondary cavity nester surveys should be managed as a linked system. The threshold question is not "how many snags per hectare?" but "does the cavity creation rate match the cavity demand of the dependent community?"

3. **Stabilize the nutcracker-whitebark pine mutualism.** Whitebark pine restoration (blister-rust-resistant planting, targeted fire management) and Clark's Nutcracker monitoring should be coupled. Without nutcrackers, planted whitebark pines will not regenerate naturally.

4. **Address the Barred Owl invasion as a network disruption.** The Barred Owl's impact extends beyond Northern Spotted Owl to the entire nocturnal predator guild — screech-owls, pygmy-owls, and the prey communities of old-growth forests. Management decisions about Barred Owl removal should be evaluated for network-level effects, not just Spotted Owl occupancy.

5. **Track phenological mismatch as a network-health metric.** Climate-driven mismatches between migrant arrival and food availability are not species-level problems — they are network-level problems that will ripple through pollination, predator-prey, and competition interactions simultaneously.
