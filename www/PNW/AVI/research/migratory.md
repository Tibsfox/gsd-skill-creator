> The Pacific Northwest sits at the intersection of the Pacific Flyway — North America's westernmost migration corridor — and the continental divide, creating one of the richest migratory avifaunas on the continent. Approximately 200 species use the PNW as breeding ground, stopover, or wintering habitat, with an additional ~100 vagrant species documented by state bird records committees. This document covers all migratory species in four parts: neotropical migrants, winter visitors and staging waterbirds, and vagrant species.

## Family: Tyrannidae (Tyrant Flycatchers)

The tyrant flycatchers are the New World's largest passerine family, with roughly 430 species from Patagonia to the boreal treeline. The PNW receives 12 regular neotropical migrant species, all arriving between late April and early June to exploit the explosive emergence of flying insects that accompanies Pacific Northwest spring. Their arrival timing is tightly coupled to insect phenology: the earliest species (Say's Phoebe) track low-elevation insect emergence in late March, while high-elevation specialists (Hammond's Flycatcher, Olive-sided Flycatcher) delay until late May when montane insect populations peak.

PNW flycatchers partition habitat along an elevation-vegetation gradient that reduces interspecific competition. The *Empidonax* complex — five visually near-identical species breeding in the region — provides a textbook case of ecological niche partitioning. Hammond's Flycatcher occupies closed-canopy coniferous forest at middle to high elevations, Dusky Flycatcher selects open montane woodland and shrubby edges, Pacific-slope Flycatcher inhabits moist lowland and mid-elevation forests (especially ravines), Willow Flycatcher breeds in willow thickets along riparian corridors, and Gray Flycatcher occupies dry east-side ponderosa pine and juniper woodland. In the field, voice is the only reliable identification tool for these species (source: O-07, O-08).

Conservation concerns center on Olive-sided Flycatcher, which has experienced a 79% range-wide population decline since 1970 according to BBS data (source: G-18), and Willow Flycatcher, whose southwestern subspecies (*E. t. extimus*) is federally Endangered though PNW subspecies are not listed. Aerial insectivores as a guild show steeper population declines than most other North American bird guilds, a trend linked to reduced insect biomass, pesticide exposure, and climate-driven phenological mismatch between insect emergence and bird arrival (source: O-04, G-18).

## Family: Hirundinidae (Swallows)

Swallows are among the most visible and ecologically important aerial insectivores in the Pacific Northwest. All seven PNW species are neotropical or medium-distance migrants that arrive between late February (Tree Swallow) and mid-May (Purple Martin), filling the skies over wetlands, rivers, agricultural fields, and coastal bluffs. Their arrival is one of the most anticipated signs of spring across the region, and their autumn staging aggregations — particularly Barn Swallows and Tree Swallows massing on lakeside wires — can involve tens of thousands of individuals.

Swallow nesting ecology in the PNW spans a wide range of strategies. Tree and Violet-green Swallows are secondary cavity nesters that depend on woodpecker holes and nest boxes; Cliff and Barn Swallows construct mud nests on vertical surfaces; Bank Swallows excavate colonial burrows in sand and clay banks; Northern Rough-winged Swallows use existing cavities in banks and structures; and Purple Martins nest in natural cavities or nest boxes (almost exclusively the latter in the PNW). This diversity of nesting substrates makes the family collectively sensitive to different habitat changes — cavity nesters respond to snag retention and nest box programs, while mud nesters track the availability of mud sources and structures.

Conservation of PNW swallows is a growing concern. Aerial insectivores as a group have declined more steeply than any other North American bird guild over the past 50 years, with causes that remain incompletely understood. BBS data show significant declines for Bank Swallow, Barn Swallow, and Purple Martin in parts of their range (source: G-18). Purple Martin is a Species of Concern in Washington and Oregon, with the western population almost entirely dependent on artificial nest structures managed by volunteer programs. Factors suspected in aerial insectivore declines include reduced insect biomass (linked to pesticides and land-use change), climate-driven phenological mismatch, loss of nesting substrates, and mercury bioaccumulation (source: O-04).

## Family: Parulidae (Wood-Warblers)

The wood-warblers are the jewels of the neotropical migrant community. Fourteen species breed regularly in the Pacific Northwest, and their arrival between late April and late May transforms the region's forests, riparian corridors, and shrublands with color and song. PNW warblers represent a cross-section of the family's ecological diversity: canopy gleaners (Townsend's, Hermit, Black-throated Gray), understory skulkers (MacGillivray's, Common Yellowthroat), riparian shrub specialists (Yellow Warbler, Wilson's Warbler, Yellow-breasted Chat), and generalists that span multiple strata (Orange-crowned Warbler, Nashville Warbler).

The Townsend's-Hermit Warbler complex is of particular biogeographic interest. These two closely related species meet in a well-studied hybrid zone along the Washington and Oregon Cascades, where Townsend's Warbler is competitively dominant and the hybrid zone is moving southward over time — a rare documented case of one species displacing another through behavioral dominance rather than habitat loss (source: P-09). The hybrid zone runs roughly from Snoqualmie Pass south through the central Oregon Cascades, with pure Hermit Warbler populations to the south and pure Townsend's populations to the north and at higher elevations. Hybrids show intermediate plumage and mixed song types.

Conservation status of PNW warblers is generally favorable, with most species showing stable or increasing population trends in BBS data (source: G-18). The notable exception is Yellow-breasted Chat, which is listed as Endangered in BC and a Species of Concern in Washington, having declined sharply with the loss of lowland riparian shrub habitat to agriculture and development. Common Yellowthroat has experienced localized declines tied to wetland loss. At the continental scale, Partners in Flight identifies long-distance neotropical migrant warblers as collectively vulnerable to tropical deforestation on wintering grounds, particularly in Central American shade-coffee landscapes where multiple PNW breeding species overwinter (source: O-04).

## Family: Vireonidae (Vireos)

The vireos are deliberate, methodical foliage gleaners that fill an ecological niche between the active, flitting warblers and the larger, more sedentary thrushes. Four species occur regularly in the PNW: three are unambiguous neotropical migrants (Warbling, Cassin's, and Red-eyed Vireo) and one (Hutton's Vireo) is a year-round resident included here for completeness and comparison. Vireos are distinguished from warblers by their heavier, slightly hooked bills (adapted for manipulating larger insect prey), more deliberate foraging movements, and persistent singing — males of most species sing throughout the day, even during the heat of midday when most other songbirds fall silent.

PNW vireos partition habitat along a moisture gradient. Cassin's Vireo occupies dry to mesic conifer and mixed forests across the Cascades and interior mountains, Warbling Vireo selects deciduous canopy along riparian corridors and in aspen groves, and Red-eyed Vireo breeds in tall deciduous forest in the northeastern corner of the region. Hutton's Vireo is a west-side resident of live oak and mixed conifer-hardwood forest. This partitioning minimizes interspecific competition, though Cassin's and Warbling Vireo occasionally share forest edges at middle elevations.

Conservation status for PNW vireos is generally stable. Warbling Vireo is one of the most common riparian songbirds in the region, and Cassin's Vireo populations have been stable to slightly increasing in BBS data (source: G-18). Red-eyed Vireo, at the western periphery of its range, has always been uncommon in the PNW but appears stable in its limited range. The former "Solitary Vireo" was split into three species in 1997 (Cassin's, Blue-headed, and Plumbeous); Cassin's Vireo is the PNW representative of this complex (source: O-03).

## Tanagers, Grosbeaks, Buntings, and Orioles

The Pacific Northwest receives a colorful assemblage of neotropical songbirds each spring — tanagers, grosbeaks, buntings, and orioles that winter from Mexico to Central and South America. These species occupy mid-canopy to canopy foraging strata in coniferous and mixed forests, riparian woodlands, and shrubby edges. Their arrival in May coincides with peak insect emergence, and most depart by September. The Western Tanager is perhaps the most visually striking summer bird of PNW montane forests, its flame-orange head visible against dark conifers. Black-headed Grosbeaks are among the few birds that regularly consume Monarch butterflies, tolerating the cardiac glycosides that deter other predators. Bullock's Orioles weave pendant nests in riparian cottonwoods, while Lazuli Buntings paint brushy hillsides with turquoise. The Bobolink, once more widespread, now clings to remnant grassland patches in eastern Oregon and Washington.

## Sparrows (Summer Breeders)

The PNW hosts a diverse summer assemblage of Emberizid sparrows, most of which breed in the drier east-side habitats — sagebrush steppe, grasslands, open pine woodlands, and shrubby montane meadows. These are ground-nesting or low-shrub nesting species, cryptically plumaged in browns and grays, identified primarily by song. Most winter in the southwestern United States and Mexico. The sagebrush-obligate species (Brewer's Sparrow, Sagebrush Sparrow, Sage Thrasher) are of particular conservation concern as the sagebrush ecosystem faces ongoing fragmentation from agriculture, energy development, and invasive annual grasses. Chipping Sparrows are the most widespread of the group, breeding in open conifer forests from sea level to the subalpine. Grasshopper Sparrows, with their insect-like buzzing song, represent the rarest PNW breeder in this suite.

## Thrushes

PNW thrushes include two neotropical migrants — Swainson's Thrush and Veery — whose ethereal flute-like songs define the soundscape of riparian forests and moist coniferous woodlands in summer. The two bluebird species are partial migrants; some populations are resident year-round in the PNW while others undertake short-distance movements to lower elevations or southward in winter. Swainson's Thrush is one of the most abundant landbirds in PNW wet forests and a significant nocturnal migrant, traveling to South America and back each year. The Veery, while widespread in eastern North America, reaches the western limit of its breeding range in the PNW's moist interior valleys, making it an uncommon but regular breeder in northeastern Oregon, northern Idaho, and eastern Washington.

### Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*)

## Hummingbirds

The PNW's hummingbird guild includes four species that illustrate strikingly different migratory strategies. The Rufous Hummingbird, fiery orange and fiercely territorial, makes one of the longest migrations relative to body size of any bird on Earth — from Mexico to southern Alaska and back, covering over 6,000 km each way. The Calliope Hummingbird, North America's smallest bird, breeds in montane meadows and migrates through the Rocky Mountain corridor. The Black-chinned Hummingbird occupies drier east-side habitats. Anna's Hummingbird, historically a California species, has expanded dramatically northward since the mid-20th century and now winters throughout western Washington and Oregon — a range expansion driven largely by the proliferation of winter-blooming ornamental plants and hummingbird feeders in suburban landscapes.

Hummingbirds are critical pollinators of tubular wildflowers in PNW montane meadows and riparian corridors. The Rufous Hummingbird earns a **salmon thread** flag: it is a primary pollinator of red columbine (*Aquilegia formosa*), Indian paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.), and other tubular flowers that stabilize riparian banks along salmon-bearing streams. The plant-pollinator-salmon nexus represents an indirect but ecologically meaningful connection.

---

### Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: NT (Near Threatened — significant population decline) | PIF: Yellow Watch List
**Size:** 7-9 cm length, 11 cm wingspan, 2.5-4.5 g mass

**Nesting:** Tiny cup of plant down and spider silk on drooping branch, clutch 2, incubation 15-17 days
**Migration:** Arrives Mar-Apr (males first) | Departs Jul-Sep (staggered: adult males, then females and juveniles) | Winters: Mexico (Pacific slope)
**Interaction:** The most aggressive hummingbird in North America — defends flower patches against all comers including species twice its size. Males migrate north along the Pacific coast in spring, then return south through the Rocky Mountain corridor in late summer, tracking sequential wildflower blooms at increasing elevation — a phenomenon called "altitude tracking." Population has declined an estimated 62% since 1970 (source: O-28, Partners in Flight). **Salmon thread species:** pollinates riparian plants that stabilize stream banks in salmon watersheds.

## Nightjars and Swifts

The nightjars and swifts represent two distinct orders — Caprimulgiformes and Apodiformes — united by their aerial lifestyles and crepuscular or nocturnal activity patterns. Common Nighthawk and Common Poorwill are nightjars: cryptically plumaged, ground-nesting insectivores with enormous gapes for scooping moths and beetles from the air. The Poorwill holds a unique distinction as the only bird known to hibernate (enter prolonged torpor lasting days to weeks). Vaux's Swift is a small, fast-flying insectivore famous for its communal roosting behavior — thousands of birds funneling into large hollow trees or chimneys at dusk during migration, creating one of the PNW's most spectacular avian phenomena.

---

### Common Nighthawk (*Chordeiles minor*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-COASTAL

**Migration:** Arrives late May-Jun | Departs Aug-Sep | Winters: South America (east of Andes)
**Interaction:** Despite "nighthawk" in the name, most active at dusk and dawn, coursing over open country with erratic, buoyant wingbeats. Male's courtship dive produces a distinctive "boom" — a mechanical sound created by air rushing through spread primaries at the bottom of a power dive. ## Migratory Raptors

Five raptor species occurring in the PNW are predominantly or entirely migratory, withdrawing from the region in winter. These range from the iconic Osprey — the world's most widespread raptor, intimately tied to the salmon cycle — to the rare Mississippi Kite at the extreme edge of its range. Swainson's Hawk is a champion long-distance migrant, traveling from the PNW sagebrush steppe to the Argentine pampas in flocks numbering thousands. The Turkey Vulture, while common enough to seem permanent, is actually a complete migrant in the PNW, departing entirely for southern latitudes by November. Broad-winged Hawk passes through the PNW in small numbers during migration but rarely breeds in the region. See also the companion `raptors.md` for full-format profiles of resident raptors, which includes expanded prey lists and MAM cross-references.

### Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-MONTANE
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC
**Size:** 50-66 cm length, 127-180 cm wingspan, 1,120-2,050 g mass (female larger)

### Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-COASTAL
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC

## Additional Migratory Species

This section profiles migratory species that fall outside the major guild groups above but are ecologically significant neotropical or medium-distance migrants in the PNW. The Black Swift is one of North America's most mysterious birds, nesting behind waterfalls and spending virtually its entire non-breeding life on the wing over South America. Lewis's Woodpecker, an atypical woodpecker that hawks insects on the wing, has migratory populations that withdraw from higher elevations and northern areas. The Cedar Waxwing is a nomadic frugivore whose movements track fruit availability rather than following a fixed migration schedule.

---

### Black Swift (*Cypseloides niger*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (rare/local) | **Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-ALPINE
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: ST-SC | OR: ST-SC | PIF: Yellow Watch List
**Size:** 15-18 cm length, 38-46 cm wingspan, 35-55 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Flying insects (captured at high altitude) | Aerial insectivore | Aerial
**Nesting:** Shallow cup of moss and mud on cliff ledge behind or beside waterfall, clutch 1, incubation 24-27 days
**Migration:** Arrives late May-Jun | Departs Sep-Oct | Winters: South America (Brazil — only recently confirmed via geolocators)
**Interaction:** One of the most enigmatic North American birds. Nests exclusively on wet cliff faces behind waterfalls or in sea caves, often at high elevation in the Cascades and Olympics. Feeds at great altitude (3,000+ m), often invisible from the ground. Single-egg clutch and long incubation period are unusual for a passeriform-sized bird. Geolocator studies published in 2012 finally confirmed the winter range in western Brazil, solving a longstanding ornithological mystery. Colonial nesting sites in the PNW are few and widely scattered — any known colony is conservation-significant.
**Salmon Thread:** No

### Loggerhead Shrike (*Lanius ludovicianus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (declining) | **Bands:** ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed (but *L. l. mearnsi* — San Clemente subspecies — is ESA-E) | IUCN: LC (but declining rangewide) | WA: ST-SC | OR: ST-SC
**Size:** 20-23 cm length, 28-32 cm wingspan, 35-50 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Large insects, small mammals, lizards, small birds | Ambush predator / Impaler | Ground to Understory
**Nesting:** Bulky cup in dense thorny shrub or tree, clutch 5-7, incubation 15-17 days
**Migration:** Partially migratory in PNW — some populations move south Sep-Oct, return Mar-Apr | Winters: Southern US to Mexico
**Interaction:** A passerine predator — the "butcher bird" — that impales prey on thorns, barbed wire, or sharp twigs to cache food and dismember items too large to eat at once. Hunts from exposed perches, swooping down on prey with a heavy undulating flight. In the PNW, breeds in sagebrush steppe and open shrublands east of the Cascades, where it has declined significantly due to habitat loss, pesticide exposure (reduced large-insect prey), and competition with the expanding Northern Shrike. Black mask, gray upperparts, and hooked bill give it a raptor-like appearance despite being a songbird (order Passeriformes).
**Salmon Thread:** No

### Horned Lark (*Eremophila alpestris*) — Migratory Populations
**Status:** Resident (some pops) / Partial Migrant | **Bands:** ELEV-ALPINE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-COASTAL, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-T (*E. a. strigata* — streaked horned lark, listed 2013) | IUCN: LC (species level)
**Size:** 16-20 cm length, 30-35 cm wingspan, 28-48 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Seeds, insects | Ground forager | Ground
**Nesting:** Ground nest in bare or sparse vegetation, clutch 2-5, incubation 10-12 days
**Migration:** Alpine populations descend to lowlands Oct-Mar; *strigata* populations partially migratory within the Willamette Valley and coastal WA | Winters: Lower elevations locally and south to California
**Interaction:** The only true lark (family Alaudidae) native to North America. ### Lewis's Woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*) — Migratory Populations
**Status:** Summer Breeder / Partial Migrant | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | WA: Not listed | OR: ST-SC | PIF: Yellow Watch List
**Size:** 26-28 cm length, 49-52 cm wingspan, 88-138 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Insects (aerial capture — flycatcher-style), acorns, fruit | Aerial sallier / Granivore | Mid-canopy to Aerial
**Nesting:** Secondary cavity nester (uses old woodpecker holes, natural cavities), clutch 5-9, incubation 13-14 days
**Migration:** Partially migratory — northern and high-elevation populations withdraw Oct-Nov, return Apr | Winters: Oak woodlands in southern OR, CA, or locally where acorn crops are adequate
**Interaction:** The most atypical woodpecker in North America — rarely excavates or drums, instead catching insects on the wing in swooping sallies like a large flycatcher. Dark greenish-black upperparts, gray collar, and pink belly create a unique appearance unlike any other woodpecker. Breeds in open ponderosa pine forests, burned areas with snag patches, and oak-pine woodlands. Winter distribution in the PNW is driven by oak mast crop availability; birds aggregate in productive oak groves and cache acorns in bark crevices like a nuthatch. Declining due to snag removal, fire suppression, and loss of open-canopy ponderosa pine habitat.
**Salmon Thread:** No

### Cedar Waxwing (*Bombycilla cedrorum*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder / Partial Migrant (year-round in some areas) | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC
**Size:** 14-17 cm length, 22-30 cm wingspan, 30-36 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Fruit (primary year-round), insects (breeding season supplement) | Frugivore | Mid-canopy to Canopy
**Nesting:** Open cup in deciduous or coniferous tree, clutch 3-6, incubation 11-13 days
**Migration:** Nomadic/irruptive rather than fixed-route migrant — movements track fruit availability | Some populations resident year-round in PNW; others move south Oct-Mar
**Interaction:** One of the most specialized frugivores in North America, with a digestive tract adapted for rapid fruit processing — berries pass through the gut in as little as 16 minutes, making waxwings highly effective seed dispersers. Flocks descend on fruiting trees — mountain ash (*Sorbus*), hawthorn (*Crataegus*), juniper, holly — and strip them bare. Occasionally become intoxicated on fermented fruit, leading to erratic flight. Late breeders (Jun-Aug) compared to most PNW passerines, timed to coincide with fruit ripening.
**Salmon Thread:** No

## Wrens and Additional Ground Nesters

The PNW hosts several migratory wren species and ground-nesting species that withdraw from higher elevations or northern portions of their range in winter. The House Wren's explosive bubbling song is one of the most familiar summer sounds in PNW gardens and woodland edges. Marsh Wrens in some populations are resident, while others migrate south from northern marshes. The Rock Wren occupies talus slopes and rocky outcrops east of the Cascades, its dry buzzing song echoing off canyon walls.

---

### House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC
**Size:** 11-13 cm length, 15-17 cm wingspan, 10-12 g mass
**Diet/Guild:** Insects, spiders | Cavity gleaner / Understory forager | Ground to Mid-canopy
**Nesting:** Secondary cavity nester (woodpecker holes, nest boxes, any enclosed space), clutch 5-8, incubation 12-15 days
**Migration:** Arrives late Apr-May | Departs Sep-Oct | Winters: Southern US to Mexico
**Interaction:** One of the most aggressive cavity competitors in the PNW despite its tiny size. Males build multiple "dummy nests" (stick-filled cavities) to attract females, and routinely destroy eggs and nestlings of other cavity-nesting species — including bluebirds, chickadees, and other wrens — by puncturing eggs with their bills. This aggression can significantly impact local populations of cavity nesters. Explosive, bubbling song is delivered with whole-body vibration, tail cocked vertically. Breeds in open woodlands, edges, gardens, and riparian corridors throughout the PNW up to ~2,000 m.
**Salmon Thread:** No

### Marsh Wren (*Cistothorus palustris*) — Migratory Populations
**Status:** Resident (some pops) / Partial Migrant | **Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-COASTAL

### Rock Wren (*Salpinctes obsoletus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder / Partial Migrant | **Bands:** ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-ALPINE

### Pacific Flyway Overview

The Pacific Flyway is the westernmost of four administrative flyways in North America, stretching from Arctic breeding grounds in Alaska and the Yukon through the Pacific coastal states to wintering areas in Mexico and Central America (source: G-02). The flyway encompasses approximately 1.6 million km² of wetland, coastal, and open-water habitats, managed cooperatively by the Pacific Flyway Council (source: G-11).

Within the PNW, the Pacific Flyway funnels through four critical landscape bottlenecks:

**Klamath Basin (OR/CA border).** The largest complex of freshwater marshes remaining on the Pacific coast. Lower Klamath, Tule Lake, Upper Klamath, and Clear Lake NWRs together support peak autumn concentrations exceeding one million waterbirds, including up to 80% of the Pacific Flyway's fall migrant waterfowl (source: G-19). Tundra Swans, Snow Geese, Northern Pintails, and Greater White-fronted Geese stage here in enormous numbers. The basin also supports the largest wintering concentration of Bald Eagles in the contiguous United States outside Alaska (source: G-14).

**Malheur National Wildlife Refuge (SE Oregon).** High-desert marshes at 1,250 m elevation in the Harney Basin. Malheur Lake, Mud Lake, and the Blitzen Valley provide breeding and staging habitat for Sandhill Cranes, American Avocets, Wilson's Phalaropes, Long-billed Curlews, and Cinnamon Teal. Spring staging of Greater Sandhill Cranes can exceed 3,000 individuals (source: G-19).

**Skagit and Samish Flats (NW Washington).** Estuarine agricultural lowlands at the mouths of the Skagit and Samish Rivers support the largest wintering concentrations of Snow Geese (Fir Island fields: 30,000-50,000 birds), Trumpeter and Tundra Swans, and Dunlin in Washington State (source: G-14, G-03). The Skagit River corridor also draws winter Bald Eagle concentrations to salmon spawning reaches.

**Grays Harbor (WA coast).** An internationally recognized WHSRN site, Grays Harbor Estuary supports spring peak shorebird concentrations of 500,000-1,000,000 Western Sandpipers and Dunlin during northbound migration (source: O-19). Bowerman Basin mudflats are the primary staging area. Willapa Bay and Padilla Bay provide complementary estuarine habitat along the outer coast and Salish Sea, respectively.

These four sites, along with the Salish Sea marine corridor, Columbia River estuary, and Willamette Valley wetlands, form the spine of the Pacific Flyway within PNW boundaries. Winter waterfowl surveys conducted annually by USFWS and state agencies document population trends that inform harvest regulations and habitat management (source: G-03, G-11).

## Winter Visitors — Waterfowl

Winter waterfowl are the most conspicuous avian feature of PNW lowlands from October through March. The region's mild maritime climate, extensive estuaries, and productive agricultural flats attract enormous numbers of Arctic and subarctic-breeding geese, swans, and dabbling ducks. East-side wetlands and the Klamath Basin add high-desert wintering habitat for diving ducks and geese that breed on interior tundra and boreal ponds.

## Winter Visitors — Passerines

Passerine winter visitors to the PNW arrive from boreal and Arctic breeding grounds to exploit lower-elevation food resources. Sparrows and finches dominate this guild, with several irruptive species appearing in large numbers only in years of northern seed crop failure.

### Northern Shrike (*Lanius borealis*)

## Winter Visitors — Irruptive Finches

Boreal finches and their allies exhibit a distinctive migration pattern driven not by photoperiod but by seed crop availability. In years when cone, catkin, and berry crops fail across the boreal zone, massive irruptions carry these species far south of their normal range. The PNW, with its extensive conifer forests, is a frequent destination for irruptive boreal finches.

Irruptive cycles are loosely periodic but unpredictable. Spruce-cone years, birch-catkin crops, and mountain-ash berry production fluctuate independently across vast boreal regions, creating complex multi-species irruption patterns. The Finch Research Network tracks these cycles through cone crop surveys and volunteer observations (source: O-02).

---

### Pine Grosbeak (*Pinicola enucleator*)
**Status:** Irruptive Winter Visitor (uncommon to rare)
**Bands:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND (invasion years)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 20-25 cm length, 33-35 cm wingspan, 45-65 g mass
**Plumage:** Adult male: rosy-red body with gray wings bearing two white wing bars, dark tail. Female and immature male: gray overall with olive-yellow to rusty head and rump, same wing pattern. Large, robust finch with stubby dark bill. Remarkably tame and approachable.
**Diet/Guild:** Seeds, buds, berries, insects | Granivore/frugivore | Canopy, Mid-canopy
**Migration:** Irruptive / Nomadic | Arrives Nov-Dec (invasion years) | Departs Mar-Apr | Breeds: boreal spruce-fir forests, subalpine zones
**Key PNW sites:** Subalpine parklands (Mt. Rainier, North Cascades, Hurricane Ridge); lowlands only in strong irruption years — urban areas with ornamental crabapple and mountain ash
**Interaction:** Sluggish, tame finch that allows close approach — likely due to limited experience with predators in remote boreal habitat. Feeds on mountain ash berries, crabapples, and conifer buds. During irruption years, small groups appear at lowland feeding stations and ornamental plantings. Less frequent in PNW irruptions than Common Redpoll or White-winged Crossbill.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** O-02, O-07, O-16

### Common Redpoll (*Acanthis flammea*)

**Interaction:** During irruption years, flocks of 50-200+ Common Redpolls descend on birch trees and nyjer (thistle) feeders. Can store seeds in a throat pouch (crop diverticulum) to feed later in sheltered locations — an adaptation to extreme cold. Metabolic rate requires consumption of approximately 40% of body mass daily in seeds during winter (source: O-07).

### White-winged Crossbill (*Loxia leucoptera*)
**Status:** Irruptive Winter Visitor
**Bands:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND (irruptions)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC

## Winter Visitors — Raptors

Three raptor species reach the PNW primarily as winter visitors from Arctic and subarctic breeding grounds. Two are regular; one is a celebrated rarity.

### Rough-legged Hawk (*Buteo lagopus*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE, ELEV-COASTAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 46-56 cm length, 120-153 cm wingspan, 715-1,400 g mass
**Plumage:** Highly variable. Light morph: white body with dark belly band, dark carpal patches on underwing, dark tail band. Dark morph: entirely dark brown with pale flight feathers. During the "superirruption" winter of 2011-2012 and the strong irruption of 2013-2014, Snowy Owls appeared across the PNW in unprecedented numbers. ## Altitudinal Migrants

Altitudinal migration — the seasonal downslope movement from high-elevation breeding habitat to lower-elevation wintering areas — is one of the PNW's most characteristic bird movement patterns. Unlike latitudinal migration across hundreds or thousands of kilometers, altitudinal migrants may travel only 5-30 km horizontally while descending 1,000-2,500 m in elevation. The result is that many species common in lowland PNW during winter are essentially absent from the same locations during summer.

This pattern is particularly well-expressed in the PNW because of the region's extreme topographic relief — from sea level to 4,392 m (Mt. Rainier) within 100 km. Climate change is altering altitudinal migration patterns, with some species spending increasingly mild winters at higher elevations than historically recorded, compressing the elevation range of seasonal movement (source: G-04).

---

### Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)
**Status:** Altitudinal Migrant (iconic PNW species)
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (breeding) → ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-COASTAL (winter)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 20-26 cm length, 34-40 cm wingspan, 65-100 g mass
**Plumage:** Male: deep blue-gray above, orange breast with bold black breast band, orange eyebrow, orange wing bars and flight feather edges. Female: similar pattern but olive-brown where male is gray, with less distinct breast band. One of the most beautifully marked passerines in North America.
**Diet/Guild:** Insects, earthworms, berries (especially *Arbutus*, *Vaccinium*) | Omnivore | Ground, low shrub
**Migration:** Altitudinal | Arrives in lowlands Oct-Nov | Returns upslope Apr-May | Breeds: dense montane and subalpine conifer forests (old-growth preferred)
**Key PNW sites:** Breeds throughout Cascades, Olympics, Coast Range in dense conifer forests. Winters in lowland parks, gardens, and second-growth throughout western WA/OR. Abundant winter visitor to Puget Sound and Willamette Valley yards.
**Interaction:** The Varied Thrush's single, ethereal, ringing whistle on a minor key is the signature sound of old-growth PNW forests. Breeds in dark understory of mature conifer forests at 600-1,800 m elevation, then descends to lowland gardens and parks for winter. Forages by flipping leaf litter like a robin. The dramatic seasonal shift from remote montane forest to suburban yard makes this one of the most visible examples of altitudinal migration in North America.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes
- Breeds in riparian old-growth forests along salmon-bearing mountain streams. Benefits from forest productivity maintained by marine-derived nutrients transported upstream by spawning salmon and distributed by Bald Eagles and mammalian scavengers (source: P-18).
**Sources:** G-14, P-18, O-02, O-07

### Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*)
**Status:** Altitudinal Migrant / Year-round Resident (complex)
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (breeding concentration) → ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-COASTAL (winter concentration)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 13-17 cm length, 18-25 cm wingspan, 15-30 g mass
**Plumage:** PNW subspecies: *oreganus* group — dark slate-gray to black hood, rusty-brown back, pinkish-brown flanks, white belly, white outer tail feathers (flash in flight). Female paler and browner than male. Other subspecies groups pass through or winter irregularly (*hyemalis* "Slate-colored," *caniceps* "Gray-headed"). Pink bill.
**Diet/Guild:** Seeds, small insects | Granivore/insectivore | Ground
**Migration:** Altitudinal + Short-distance latitudinal | Lowland winter numbers increase Oct | Decrease Apr | Breeds: conifer and mixed forests at all elevations (but highest density at montane-subalpine)
**Key PNW sites:** Breeds throughout forested mountains. Winters everywhere in lowlands — one of the most abundant feeder birds in PNW. Concentrated in urban and suburban areas, parks, hedgerows, brush piles.
**Interaction:** Often called the "snowbird" because its autumn arrival at lowland feeders coincides with the first mountain snowfalls. PNW populations exhibit a complex blend of altitudinal and latitudinal migration: mountain breeders descend to valleys while some northern populations migrate south into PNW lowlands for winter. Winter flocks of 10-30 birds forage on the ground beneath feeders, establishing dominance hierarchies (source: O-07). The most frequently reported bird on PNW Christmas Bird Counts (source: O-16).

### Townsend's Solitaire (*Myadestes townsendi*)
**Status:** Altitudinal Migrant
**Bands:** ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE (breeding) → ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE (winter)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC

**Plumage:** Uniform gray overall with bold white eye-ring, buffy wing patches (prominent in flight), white outer tail feathers. Slender, upright posture. Sexes similar. Subtle beauty — resembles a gray mockingbird in shape and behavior.

**Interaction:** One of the most strictly altitudinal migrants in the PNW. Shifts from insect-hawking on alpine slopes in summer to territorial defense of individual juniper trees in winter. Each wintering bird defends a specific juniper grove against conspecifics and other berry-eating birds (American Robins, waxwings), singing a remarkable warbling flight song from treetops. ### American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*)
**Status:** Year-round Resident with altitudinal shift
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (breeding) → ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-COASTAL (winter concentration)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC

**Interaction:** PNW American Robins illustrate the complexity of partial altitudinal migration: mountain breeders descend to lowlands, while lowland breeders may be essentially sedentary or make short movements. Winter behavior shifts dramatically from territorial earthworm-hunting to gregarious berry-eating in large nomadic flocks. Winter roosts can number in the thousands, occupying ivy-covered trees in urban areas. The species that best demonstrates how "resident" and "migratory" are not discrete categories.

### Mountain Chickadee (*Poecile gambeli*)
**Status:** Altitudinal Migrant / Year-round Resident
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (breeding) → ELEV-LOWLAND (winter, partial)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 12-14 cm length, 19-21 cm wingspan, 9-14 g mass

**Diet/Guild:** Insects, spiders (breeding); seeds, cached conifer seeds (winter) | Insectivore/granivore | Canopy, bark

### Pine Siskin (*Spinus pinus*)
**Status:** Nomadic / Irruptive / Altitudinal Migrant
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-LOWLAND (complex — present at any elevation in any season depending on cone crop)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 11-14 cm length, 18-22 cm wingspan, 10-18 g mass
**Plumage:** Brown-streaked overall with yellow patches in wings and base of tail (visible in flight and when perched with wings slightly spread). Sharply pointed bill for a finch. Sexes similar; male shows slightly more yellow. Often mistaken for a female House Finch or sparrow.

A bird banded in one state may appear hundreds of kilometers away within weeks. Winter flocks at feeders can number 50-200+ birds but may vanish overnight when a distant seed crop becomes available. Highly gregarious; mixed flocks with goldfinches and redpolls. Susceptible to *Salmonella* outbreaks at feeders (source: G-14).

### Red Crossbill (*Loxia curvirostra*)
**Status:** Nomadic / Irruptive (complex of call types)
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-LOWLAND (irruptions)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL (except Type 1 *Cassia Crossbill* — ESA-T; note: PNW types are not ESA-listed) | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 14-18 cm length, 25-29 cm wingspan, 25-40 g mass
**Plumage:** Male: brick-red to orange-red body, dark wings and tail. Female: olive-yellow to gray-green with streaked underparts. Both sexes show crossed mandibles. No wing bars (distinguishing from White-winged Crossbill). Juveniles streaked.
**Diet/Guild:** Conifer cone seeds (specialist) | Granivore (conifer seed specialist) | Canopy
**Migration:** Nomadic / Irruptive | No fixed schedule — follows cone crops | Breeds: any time of year when cone crop sufficient, including winter
**Key PNW sites:** All conifer forests from sea level to subalpine. Different call types associated with different conifers: Type 3 (western hemlock), Type 4 (Douglas-fir), Type 5 (lodgepole pine), Type 10 (Sitka spruce). Multiple types may co-occur.
**Interaction:** The PNW's Red Crossbill complex includes at least 5-6 call types, each adapted to different conifer species with varying bill depths and palate structures. These call types may represent incipient species — they assortatively mate by call type and show bill morphology matched to their preferred conifer (source: P-09 parallel, O-01). Type 3 specializes on western hemlock, Type 4 on Douglas-fir. The crossed mandible evolves convergently with cone-feeding — a textbook example of adaptive radiation in the PNW's diverse conifer forests. Can breed in any month when cone crops are adequate.

**Sources:** O-01, O-02, O-07, O-03

### Evening Grosbeak (*Coccothraustes vespertinus*)
**Status:** Nomadic / Irruptive / Altitudinal Migrant
**Bands:** ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SUBALPINE (breeding) → ELEV-LOWLAND (winter irruptions)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC (but significant long-term population decline documented; source: G-18)
**Size:** 16-22 cm length, 30-36 cm wingspan, 38-73 g mass
**Plumage:** Male: bold black, white, and bright yellow — dark head with yellow eyebrow stripe, bright yellow body, black wings with large white wing patches, massive pale conical bill. Female: gray-brown with yellowish nape, white-and-black wing pattern. Unmistakable in all plumages.
**Diet/Guild:** Seeds (conifer, maple, box elder), buds, insects (breeding), berries | Granivore | Canopy, Ground (feeders)
**Migration:** Nomadic / Irruptive | Arrives in lowlands Oct-Jan (variable) | Departs Mar-Apr | Breeds: montane mixed forests with spruce, fir, and deciduous trees
**Key PNW sites:** Breeds in Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains in mixed conifer-deciduous forest. Winter irruptions bring flocks to lowland feeders — sunflower seed is irresistible. Once one of the most abundant feeder birds during irruption winters; now dramatically reduced.
**Interaction:** Evening Grosbeak populations have declined by an estimated 92% since 1970 across North America (source: G-18), making this one of the steepest declines of any North American songbird. The cause is not fully understood but may involve spruce budworm cycle changes, reduced food availability, and window-collision mortality during winter irruptions. In the PNW, large flocks at sunflower feeders — once a regular winter spectacle — have become increasingly rare. This species illustrates how an irruptive nomad can decline catastrophically without the conspicuous range contraction that alerts conservation attention for other species.

**Sources:** G-18, O-02, O-04, O-07

## Pacific Flyway Waterbirds

This section covers waterbird species that use PNW wetlands, estuaries, and grasslands as breeding, wintering, or staging habitat within the Pacific Flyway system. These species span the spectrum from year-round residents with supplemental migratory populations to full long-distance migrants that merely stage through the PNW.

---

### Sandhill Crane (*Antigone canadensis*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant / Summer Breeder (Greater subspecies breeds in PNW; Lesser subspecies passes through)
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL (Lesser subspecies) / WA: ST-E for Greater subspecies breeding population (source: G-14) | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 100-120 cm length, 160-210 cm wingspan, 3,000-6,000 g mass (Greater subspecies larger)
**Plumage:** Gray overall with bare red forehead patch, pale cheek, long legs and neck. Adults often stain plumage rusty-brown by preening with iron-rich mud. Sexes similar; male slightly larger. Juveniles lack red forehead, show rusty-brown plumage.
**Diet/Guild:** Waste grain, tubers, insects, small vertebrates | Omnivore | Ground
**Migration:** Long-distance (Lesser) / Medium-distance (Greater) | Greater: arrives Mar, departs Oct | Lesser: passes through Mar-Apr and Sep-Oct | Breeds: Greater subspecies — PNW marshes; Lesser — Arctic tundra
**Key PNW sites:** Malheur NWR (major staging/breeding — 3,000+ cranes; source: G-19), Ridgefield NWR, Conboy Lake NWR, Sauvie Island, Klamath Basin, Columbia Basin wetlands
**Interaction:** The spectacle of Sandhill Crane staging at Malheur NWR is one of the premier wildlife events in the PNW. ## Winter Waterfowl Ecology: Regional Concentrations

### Skagit and Samish Flats

The Skagit-Samish Delta in northwestern Washington represents the single most important wintering waterfowl area in the northern Puget Sound region. The 14,000-hectare estuarine agricultural landscape supports:

- **Snow Geese:** 30,000-50,000 wintering birds of the Wrangel Island population, feeding on winter wheat and pasture grasses on Fir Island and surrounding farmland (source: G-14).
- **Trumpeter Swans:** 5,000-8,000 birds of the Pacific population, the largest Trumpeter Swan wintering concentration in the contiguous United States (source: G-14).
- **Tundra Swans:** 500-1,500 birds, often in mixed flocks with Trumpeters.
- **Dabbling ducks:** Northern Pintail, American Wigeon, Green-winged Teal, and Mallard in the tens of thousands across flooded fields and tidal sloughs.
- **Raptors:** Bald Eagles (150-300 wintering on salmon streams), Peregrine Falcon, Rough-legged Hawk, Northern Harrier, Short-eared Owl, and occasional Gyrfalcon and Snowy Owl.

The agricultural landscape is maintained through cooperative agreements between farmers and wildlife agencies. Winter flooding of harvested fields creates foraging habitat for waterfowl while providing ecosystem services (nutrient cycling, soil health) for the farming operation (source: G-14). The Skagit River's salmon runs (Chinook, Coho, Chum, Pink) draw the winter eagle concentrations that are inseparable from the waterfowl spectacle.

### Sauvie Island (OR)

The 10,400-hectare island at the Columbia-Willamette confluence supports wintering concentrations of 100,000+ waterfowl, including Snow Geese, Cackling Geese, Northern Pintails, and Tundra Swans. ODFW manages 5,300 hectares as Sauvie Island Wildlife Area with seasonal flooding for waterfowl (source: G-15).

### Klamath Basin (OR/CA)

The largest remaining freshwater marsh complex on the Pacific coast. Autumn peak concentrations exceed 1,000,000 waterbirds. Lower Klamath NWR (established 1908 — the nation's first waterfowl refuge) and Tule Lake NWR together support up to 80% of the Pacific Flyway's autumn waterfowl migration (source: G-19). The basin's importance extends to the largest wintering Bald Eagle concentration in the lower 48 states (500+ birds). Water allocation conflicts between agriculture and wildlife have resulted in dramatic habitat loss — Klamath Basin wetlands have been reduced to approximately 25% of historical extent (source: G-19).

## Irruptive Species: Seed Crop Cycles and Invasion Years

Boreal finch irruptions follow predictable ecological logic even though their timing is unpredictable. The cycle operates as follows:

**Step 1: Masting.** Northern conifer and deciduous trees produce bumper seed crops at irregular intervals (2-7 years depending on species). Spruce, birch, mountain ash, and larch each have independent masting cycles driven by growing-season weather (source: O-02).

**Step 2: Population boom.** High food availability allows boreal finches to produce larger clutches and higher fledging success. Crossbill and siskin populations build during mast years.

**Step 3: Crop failure.** The year after a mast, trees typically produce poor seed crops. With large populations and insufficient food, birds are forced to move south.

**Step 4: Irruption.** Waves of finches — crossbills, siskins, redpolls, grosbeaks, waxwings — appear in the PNW, often in numbers not seen for several years. The species composition of each irruption depends on which tree crops failed.

**Step 5: Return or settle.** Some irruptive birds return north. Others breed in the PNW if conditions are adequate — Red Crossbills and Pine Siskins regularly breed at lowland locations during irruption years, taking advantage of local cone crops.

Notable PNW irruption years in the 21st century include 2004-2005 (crossbills and siskins), 2012-2013 (Snowy Owls and redpolls), and 2020-2021 (Pine Siskins in historic numbers). The Evening Grosbeak, once the most dramatic irruptive species, has declined so severely that irruptions are now a shadow of historical events (source: G-18, O-02).

## Altitudinal Migration as Climate Adaptation

The PNW's steep topographic gradients create a unique natural laboratory for studying altitudinal migration. As climate warms, altitudinal migration patterns are shifting in three documented ways:

**1. Compressed elevation range.** Species like Varied Thrush and Dark-eyed Junco that historically descended to sea level for winter are increasingly remaining at mid-elevations (300-600 m) where milder winters now provide adequate food resources. The lowland winter range is contracting upslope (source: G-04).

**2. Upslope breeding shift.** Some altitudinal migrants are breeding at higher elevations than historical records indicate. Mountain Chickadee breeding records above 2,200 m have increased since 2000, suggesting upslope range expansion (source: O-02 — eBird trend data).

**3. Reduced migratory behavior.** Partial migration is becoming more common: a larger proportion of individuals in traditionally migratory populations are remaining at breeding elevation year-round, converting from obligate altitudinal migrants to facultative migrants or residents. This is documented in American Robin and Dark-eyed Junco populations in the Washington Cascades (source: G-04).

These shifts have consequences for lowland ecosystems. Reduced winter presence of altitudinal migrants in lowland forests and gardens alters seed dispersal, invertebrate predation, and nutrient cycling in these habitats. The cultural experience of "winter birds" — juncos arriving at feeders, Varied Thrush appearing in suburban yards — may diminish as these species find adequate resources at higher elevations.

## River's Witness: Salmon-Connected Winter Visitors

The salmon thread — the ecological connection between Pacific salmon spawning and terrestrial ecosystems — extends into winter bird communities through several pathways.

**Eagle congregations.** The most visible salmon-bird connection. Bald Eagles concentrate on spawning rivers from October through February, feeding on live spawning adults and post-spawn carcasses. Skagit River (WA): 300-600 eagles; Nooksack River (WA): 100-200 eagles; upper Columbia tributaries: hundreds of eagles. These congregations redistribute marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen-15, phosphorus) from river to riparian forest through carcass transport and fecal deposition (source: P-18).

**Merganser predation on salmon fry.** Common Mergansers and Red-breasted Mergansers consume juvenile salmon (fry and smolts) as a primary winter and spring food source. While this predation has sometimes been viewed as a management problem, healthy merganser populations indicate healthy salmon-bearing streams (source: G-20).

**Riparian forest productivity.** Winter visitors that depend on riparian old-growth forests (Varied Thrush, Hooded Merganser, Wood Duck) benefit indirectly from salmon-derived nutrients that enhance tree growth and forest productivity. The marine-derived nitrogen pathway — salmon to bear/eagle carcass transport to soil nitrogen to tree growth to cavity availability — connects anadromous fish to the cavity nester guild that includes Wood Duck and Hooded Merganser (source: P-18).

**Estuary food webs.** Pacific Flyway waterbirds staging in PNW estuaries (Grays Harbor, Columbia River, Skagit Delta) forage in ecosystems subsidized by salmon-derived nutrients. Juvenile salmon themselves are prey for mergansers, herons, gulls, and kingfishers, creating direct trophic links between the flyway and the salmon lifecycle (source: G-20).

## Part 3: Vagrant Species (CF-16)

> Vagrant species are those that occur irregularly in the Pacific Northwest, outside their normal range. The Washington Bird Records Committee (G-10) maintains a state checklist of 522 species as of November 2021; approximately 100 of these are vagrants with fewer than 20 documented records. Oregon and Idaho maintain similar records. This section provides reduced profiles for all regularly documented vagrant species, organized by origin.
>
> **Card format:** Reduced — common name, scientific name, normal range, vagrant pattern, last PNW record, IUCN status.

---

### Vagrant Classification Criteria

A species is classified as a vagrant for the PNW if it meets all of the following:

1. **Fewer than 20 accepted records** in Washington, Oregon, or Idaho combined (per WBRC, OBRC, or IBRC), OR documented annually but in numbers below 5 individuals per season.
2. **Normal breeding and wintering range** lies entirely outside the PNW region as defined by this mission (WA, OR, ID, southern BC).
3. **No established breeding population** in the PNW, even if occasional nesting has been documented.

Species transitioning from vagrant to regular status are noted in Section 6 (Range Expansion Species). Cross-references to Part 1 (passage migrants) or Part 2 (winter visitors, irruptive species) are provided where a species has both regular and vagrant populations.

**Sources:** Washington Bird Records Committee checklist (G-10), Oregon Bird Records Committee annual reports, Idaho Bird Records Committee, eBird occurrence data (O-11), *Birds of Washington* (O-06), *Birds of Oregon* (O-07).

## Section 1: Eastern North American Vagrants

Eastern vagrants are the largest category of PNW strays. The prevailing westerly winds of North America make eastward vagrancy far more common than westward vagrancy; eastern vagrants reaching the PNW typically involve either reverse migration (juveniles migrating in the wrong direction, usually in fall), overshooting during spring migration, or individuals caught up in atypical weather systems. The majority of records occur September through November, with a smaller spring peak in May-June.

---

### Warblers (Parulidae)

Eastern warblers constitute the single largest group of PNW vagrants. Their small size, nocturnal migration habits, and tendency for juveniles to exhibit reverse migration make them prone to long-distance vagrancy.

---

### Palm Warbler (*Setophaga palmarum*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds across boreal Canada from NWT to Newfoundland; winters southeastern US, Caribbean
**Vagrant Pattern:** Reverse migration (fall juveniles); occasional overshooting in spring
**PNW Records:** Multiple — among the most frequently recorded eastern warblers; annual in small numbers along the coast
**Last PNW Record:** Regular in small numbers, annually October-November; coastal WA and OR
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Blackpoll Warbler (*Setophaga striata*) — VAGRANT

**PNW Records:** Multiple — regular vagrant, 5-15 records most years
**Last PNW Record:** Regular in small numbers, primarily September-October
**IUCN:** LC

## Section 2: Asian and Siberian Vagrants

Asian vagrants reach the PNW primarily through two mechanisms: (1) overshooting during spring migration through western Alaska, and (2) displacement by strong weather systems during fall migration along the East Asian flyway. The Pacific Northwest's position on the eastern rim of the Pacific Basin makes it a regular recipient of Asian species, particularly in coastal areas. Records concentrate along the outer coast of Washington and Oregon and in western Alaska's nearshore islands.

---

### Brambling (*Fringilla montifringilla*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds across Palearctic from Scandinavia to Kamchatka; winters Europe, Middle East, East Asia
**Vagrant Pattern:** Overshooting; reverse migration from western Alaska staging areas — one of the most regular Asian vagrants
**PNW Records:** Multiple — near-annual, particularly at feeder stations in winter
**Last PNW Record:** Regular in small numbers, primarily November-March
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Eyebrowed Thrush (*Turdus obscurus*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds Siberian taiga from central Siberia to Kamchatka; winters Southeast Asia
**Vagrant Pattern:** Overshooting from spring migration through western Alaska
**PNW Records:** Fewer than 10 accepted records
**Last PNW Record:** 2016, coastal Washington
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Siberian Rubythroat (*Calliope calliope*) — VAGRANT

**Vagrant Pattern:** Overshooting; accidental — primarily an Aleutian and western Alaska vagrant; PNW records are exceptional
**PNW Records:** Fewer than 5 accepted records

**IUCN:** LC

## Section 3: Southern and Tropical Vagrants

Southern vagrants reach the PNW through northward overshooting during spring migration, post-breeding dispersal (particularly in hummingbirds and seabirds), or association with warm-water El Nino events that push tropical marine species northward. These records tend to peak in late summer through early fall when post-breeding dispersal is greatest.

---

### Magnificent Frigatebird (*Fregata magnificens*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Tropical and subtropical coasts of the Americas; breeds on offshore islands from Baja California to Ecuador (Pacific) and Caribbean to Brazil (Atlantic)
**Vagrant Pattern:** Storm-driven; post-breeding dispersal — most PNW records associated with tropical storm remnants or strong El Nino events
**PNW Records:** 10-15 accepted records
**Last PNW Record:** 2020, outer coast of Oregon
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Blue-footed Booby (*Sula nebouxii*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds Pacific coast from Gulf of California to Peru, including Galapagos Islands; generally non-migratory
**Vagrant Pattern:** Post-breeding dispersal; irruptive northward movements during warm-water events — 2013-2014 saw unprecedented numbers reaching southern California and beyond
**PNW Records:** Fewer than 10 accepted records; most during 2013-2014 irruption
**Last PNW Record:** 2014, southern Oregon coast
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Brown Booby (*Sula leucogaster*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Pan-tropical; breeds on oceanic islands worldwide; nearest breeding colonies in Gulf of California and offshore Mexico
**Vagrant Pattern:** Post-breeding dispersal; storm-driven — increasingly recorded along the Pacific coast in late summer and fall
**PNW Records:** Fewer than 10 accepted records
**Last PNW Record:** 2021, outer coast of Washington
**IUCN:** LC

---

### Elegant Tern (*Thalasseus elegans*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds primarily at Isla Rasa, Gulf of California, and a few southern California sites; disperses north along Pacific coast post-breeding
**Vagrant Pattern:** Post-breeding dispersal — northward post-breeding movement is regular to central California; PNW records represent the extreme of this dispersal
**PNW Records:** Multiple — near-annual in late summer, primarily Oregon coast
**Last PNW Record:** Regular in small numbers, July-September; increasing frequency
**IUCN:** NT

---

### Yellow-billed Cuckoo (*Coccyzus americanus*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds riparian woodlands across eastern and historically western North America; winters South America
**Vagrant Pattern:** Reverse migration; overshooting — western populations (DPS) are federally threatened; former breeder in PNW riparian cottonwood forests
**PNW Records:** Multiple — annual records, mostly eastern Oregon and southern Idaho; rare breeder candidate under recovery planning
**Last PNW Record:** Regular in small numbers, June-September; breeding investigations ongoing at Malheur NWR
**IUCN:** LC (species-level; western DPS federally Threatened)

---

### Groove-billed Ani (*Crotophaga sulcirostris*) — VAGRANT
**Normal Range:** Breeds from southern Texas through Central America to northern South America in open scrub and agricultural areas
**Vagrant Pattern:** Post-breeding dispersal; overshooting — highly gregarious, but PNW vagrants are always singles

**IUCN:** LC

Arctic vagrants reaching the PNW typically represent southward displacement during severe winter storms, post-breeding dispersal from high Arctic breeding grounds, or irruptive movements during food shortages. ## Section 5: Pelagic Vagrants

Pelagic vagrants are oceanic species whose normal ranges lie far offshore or in other ocean basins but which occasionally appear in PNW waters. Detection requires dedicated pelagic birding trips or strong onshore weather events that push birds close to land. The California Current system, running southward along the PNW coast, creates productive upwelling zones that attract foraging seabirds from across the Pacific Basin.

## Vagrant Ecology: Patterns and Drivers

The Pacific Northwest occupies a distinctive biogeographic position that exposes it to vagrant birds from virtually every compass direction. To the east, the continent's breadth and the orientation of the Rocky Mountains channel displaced eastern migrants westward. To the west, the Pacific Ocean connects the PNW to Asian breeding populations via the Aleutian Island stepping stones and trans-Pacific wind patterns. To the south, the California Current system draws tropical marine species northward, while El Nino events periodically inject warm-water species into normally cold PNW waters. To the north, the Arctic's proximity means that severe winter weather regularly displaces high-latitude species southward into the region. This four-directional exposure creates one of the richest vagrant assemblages in North America, with the Washington Bird Records Committee (G-10) documenting over 100 species outside normal range expectations on its state checklist.

Climate change is amplifying the frequency and diversity of PNW vagrancy in measurable ways. Range expansion species — Great-tailed Grackle, Eurasian Collared-Dove, White-winged Dove, Costa's Hummingbird — demonstrate that today's vagrant may be tomorrow's resident. Ornithologists have described vagrants as "advance scouts" of range shifts: species appearing irregularly at the periphery of their range are often the first signal of distributional changes that may take decades to formalize. The northward movement of southwestern species (Zone-tailed Hawk, Broad-tailed Hummingbird, Tropical Kingbird) and the increasing frequency of Asian species on the PNW coast suggest that the region's avifauna is in a period of active compositional change. The Breeding Bird Survey (G-18) and eBird citizen science data (O-11) provide critical monitoring infrastructure for detecting these shifts, but the inherent rarity and unpredictability of vagrant occurrence means that many arrivals go undetected.

The Washington Bird Records Committee, Oregon Bird Records Committee, and Idaho Bird Records Committee serve as the formal arbiters of vagrant documentation in the PNW. These volunteer committees evaluate submitted records against established identification criteria, requiring detailed written descriptions, photographs, audio recordings, or specimen evidence before accepting a record. Their cumulative archives represent decades of systematic documentation and form the foundation of our understanding of PNW vagrancy patterns. Notable irruption events in PNW history include the 2013-2014 Blue-footed Booby invasion associated with warm eastern Pacific waters, periodic Snowy Owl irruptions from the Arctic (most dramatically in winters 2011-2012 and 2017-2018), the ongoing northwestward expansion of Great-tailed Grackle through the interior Columbia Basin, and sporadic appearances of Asian species like Red-flanked Bluetail that may presage future colonization of western North America as boreal habitat zones shift northward under warming temperatures. Conservation implications are significant: vagrant monitoring provides an early warning system for range shifts, population changes, and ecosystem disruptions that may not be detectable through traditional breeding bird surveys alone. Species like Short-tailed Albatross, whose increasing PNW occurrence reflects genuine population recovery from near-extinction, demonstrate that vagrant trends can carry conservation good news as well.
