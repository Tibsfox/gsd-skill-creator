# PNW Avian Taxonomy -- Module 6: Cultural Ornithology

> **Module 6 deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document synthesizes the cultural relationships between Pacific Northwest Indigenous peoples and the region's avifauna. It draws exclusively on published, publicly available ethnographic and scientific sources -- university press books, Smithsonian publications, museum collections, and peer-reviewed research. Birds are embedded in every dimension of PNW Indigenous life: language, cosmology, ceremonial practice, seasonal calendars, material culture, ecological knowledge, and governance. This module documents that embeddedness while maintaining strict compliance with Indigenous data sovereignty frameworks.
>
> **Design principle:** Cultural ornithology is not an appendix to biological science. Indigenous peoples have observed, named, classified, and understood PNW birds for at least 10,000 years -- an unbroken tradition of ecological knowledge that dwarfs the 200-year history of Western ornithology in the region. This module treats Indigenous bird knowledge as a parallel knowledge system of immense depth and value, while respecting the boundaries that communities have placed around sacred and restricted knowledge.
>
> **Safety boundaries (ABSOLUTE):**
> - **OCAP compliance:** Ownership, Control, Access, Possession -- all data presented here is from published, community-approved sources. No unpublished or restricted knowledge.
> - **CARE principles:** Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics -- content serves educational purposes and respects community authority over cultural information.
> - **UNDRIP alignment:** United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples -- Indigenous peoples have the right to maintain, control, protect, and develop their cultural heritage and traditional knowledge.
> - No Level 2 (community-restricted) or Level 3 (sacred/ceremonial) knowledge.
> - Every reference names a specific nation. Never "Indigenous peoples" generically without attribution.
> - No GPS coordinates, specific ceremonial site locations, or harvest site details.
> - All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies.
> - AOS Check-list is taxonomic authority for species nomenclature.

# PART I: KNOWLEDGE SYSTEM FRAMEWORK

## 1.1 Two Knowledge Systems

Western scientific ornithology and Indigenous ornithological knowledge are distinct but complementary systems for understanding birds. They differ in their foundations, methods, and goals:

| Dimension | Western Ornithology | Indigenous Ornithological Knowledge |
|-----------|--------------------|------------------------------------|
| **Time depth** | ~200 years in PNW (Lewis & Clark 1805) | 10,000+ years continuous observation |
| **Classification basis** | Morphology, genetics, phylogeny | Behavior, ecology, cultural role, seasonal presence |
| **Knowledge transmission** | Written publication, peer review | Oral tradition, apprenticeship, ceremonial instruction |
| **Observation method** | Standardized surveys, banding, genetics | Multi-generational place-based observation |
| **Spatial scale** | Continental to global | Watershed to territory scale, with extraordinary local depth |
| **Temporal resolution** | Snapshot surveys (BBS, CBC) | Continuous multi-generational baseline |
| **Authority** | Scientific consensus | Elders, knowledge keepers, ceremonial leaders |
| **Access** | Public (publication) | Tiered (public, community, restricted, sacred) |

Neither system is superior. Western science excels at standardized, replicable measurement across large scales. Indigenous knowledge excels at deep temporal baselines, nuanced behavioral observation, and understanding of ecological relationships within specific landscapes. The most complete understanding of PNW avifauna draws on both [C9, C14].

## 1.2 Knowledge Tiers and This Document's Scope

Indigenous knowledge about birds operates at multiple levels of accessibility, which vary by nation and community. A generalized framework:

**Level 1 -- Public knowledge:** Information that communities have chosen to share through published books, museum exhibits, educational programs, and tribal cultural center materials. This is the ONLY level documented in this module. Examples: published bird names in Indigenous languages, general ecological observations in university press ethnographies, museum-displayed regalia descriptions.

**Level 2 -- Community knowledge:** Information shared within the community but not intended for external publication. NOT included here. Examples: specific harvesting protocols, family-held ecological knowledge, community resource management details.

**Level 3 -- Sacred/Ceremonial knowledge:** Information restricted to specific individuals, families, or ceremonial participants. NOT included here. NOT referenced. NOT speculated about. Examples: ceremonial uses of specific species, spiritual practices involving birds, sacred site information.

**This document's commitment:** Every piece of information presented here is traceable to a published, publicly available source -- a university press book, a Smithsonian publication, a tribal cultural center's public exhibits, or a peer-reviewed ethnographic study. Where published sources describe the existence of restricted knowledge without revealing its content, this document may note that deeper knowledge exists but does not attempt to characterize it [C1, C2, C7, C8].

## 1.3 Limitations and Gaps

This document is incomplete by design. The published ethnographic record:

- **Overrepresents coastal nations** (Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwakwaka'wakw, Tlingit) because early anthropologists concentrated on Northwest Coast peoples. Interior Plateau, Great Basin, and northern nations are less well documented.
- **Reflects historical bias:** Much of the ethnographic record was compiled by non-Indigenous researchers (Boas, Drucker, Suttles, Gunther) who filtered observations through Western frameworks. Bird names may have been recorded inaccurately or out of context.
- **Represents a snapshot:** Published sources capture knowledge as it existed when recorded (predominantly 1880-1970). Living cultural knowledge continues to evolve and may differ from published accounts.
- **Omits by necessity:** The most culturally significant knowledge -- ceremonial uses, spiritual relationships, restricted protocols -- is precisely what is not published and therefore not included here.

**What is present here is the tip of an iceberg.** The depth of Indigenous ornithological knowledge vastly exceeds what any published source can convey [C2, C9].

# PART II: NATIONS OF THE PNW STUDY AREA

The PNW study area (southern BC through WA, OR, and western ID) encompasses the traditional territories of dozens of distinct Indigenous nations, each with unique languages, cultural practices, and ecological knowledge traditions. The nations most frequently referenced in published ornithological ethnography include:

## 2.1 Northwest Coast Nations

### Coast Salish Peoples

The Coast Salish are a linguistically and culturally related group of nations occupying the Salish Sea region (Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, Strait of Juan de Fuca) and adjacent coastal areas. They include numerous distinct nations and communities:

- **Lushootseed-speaking peoples:** Duwamish, Muckleshoot, Puyallup, Tulalip, Snohomish, Snoqualmie, Stillaguamish, Suquamish, Swinomish, and others of the Puget Sound region
- **Twana** (Skokomish): Hood Canal and southern Puget Sound
- **Halkomelem-speaking peoples:** Stolo, Musqueam, Tsawwassen, Cowichan, Nanaimo, and others of the Fraser River and eastern Vancouver Island
- **Straits Salish:** Lummi, Samish, Klallam (Jamestown, Lower Elwha, Port Gamble) of the San Juan Islands and Strait of Juan de Fuca

Coast Salish peoples are marine-adapted cultures with deep knowledge of seabirds, waterfowl, raptors, and forest birds. Published ethnographic sources -- particularly Suttles (1987, 1990), Haeberlin and Gunther (1930), and Elmendorf (1993) -- document extensive bird vocabulary, seasonal calendars keyed to bird behavior, and material culture incorporating bird products [C1, C2, C16, C17].

### Nuu-chah-nulth (Nootka)

The Nuu-chah-nulth peoples occupy the west coast of Vancouver Island, one of the most ecologically productive marine environments in the PNW. Their maritime culture -- centered on whaling, sealing, and deep-sea fishing -- gives them unparalleled knowledge of pelagic and coastal bird species. Published sources include Drucker (1951) and Nuu-chah-nulth Tribal Council public materials [C7, C12].

### Kwakwaka'wakw (Kwakiutl)

The Kwakwaka'wakw peoples occupy the northern end of Vancouver Island and the adjacent mainland of BC. Their potlatch tradition and elaborate ceremonial art incorporate bird imagery extensively -- Thunderbird, Raven, Eagle, Kolus (a supernatural bird) are central to their visual and performative arts. Published sources include Boas (1966) and Kwakwaka'wakw cultural center exhibits [C8, C11].

### Makah

The Makah people occupy the tip of the Olympic Peninsula at Neah Bay, Washington. Their ocean-going whaling tradition placed them in direct contact with pelagic seabirds (albatrosses, shearwaters, storm-petrels) that most other PNW peoples encountered only incidentally. The Makah Cultural and Research Center provides public educational materials on their maritime ecological knowledge [C4].

### Tlingit

The Tlingit people occupy southeastern Alaska and the northernmost extent of the PNW study area. Their moiety system (Raven and Eagle/Wolf) embeds avian identity into the fundamental social structure. Thornton (2008) provides detailed documentation of Tlingit place-based ecological knowledge, including bird-habitat relationships [C10].

## 2.2 Interior Plateau Nations

### Sahaptin-Speaking Peoples

The Yakama, Warm Springs, Umatilla, Nez Perce (Nimiipuu), and related peoples of the Columbia Plateau have ecological knowledge centered on the interior steppe, river systems, and montane forests east of the Cascades. Hunn (1990) documents Sahaptin ecological vocabulary and seasonal round, including bird phenology keyed to the salmon cycle [C14].

### Klamath and Modoc

The Klamath and Modoc peoples of the Upper Klamath Basin in southern Oregon occupy one of the most important wetland complexes in the Pacific Flyway. Their ecological knowledge of waterfowl, marsh birds, and seasonal bird movements at Klamath Lake and its associated marshes represents a multi-millennial baseline for what is now one of the most contested water management regions in the West [C2].

## 2.3 Great Basin Nations

### Shoshone-Bannock and Northern Paiute

The Shoshone-Bannock and Northern Paiute peoples of the Snake River Plain and Great Basin occupy sagebrush steppe habitat with ecological knowledge of sage-grouse, raptors, and steppe-adapted species. Their traditional ecological knowledge of sage-grouse lek locations and seasonal movements predates Western scientific surveys (see Module 1b: Greater Sage-Grouse) [C2].

# PART III: BIRD NAMES IN INDIGENOUS LANGUAGES

## 3.1 Linguistic Classification as Ecological Knowledge

Bird names in Indigenous languages are not merely labels -- they encode ecological knowledge, behavioral observations, and cultural significance into the name itself. Many Indigenous bird names are onomatopoeic (imitating the bird's call), descriptive (describing appearance or behavior), or relational (describing the bird's role in the ecosystem or human community). Examining published bird vocabularies reveals classification systems that sometimes parallel and sometimes diverge from Western taxonomy [C1, C2, C14].

### Naming Strategies

Published sources document several naming strategies across PNW languages:

**Onomatopoeic names** -- The bird's call encoded in the name. This strategy is especially common for species with distinctive vocalizations:

| Species | Nation/Language | Published name | Basis | Source |
|---------|----------------|---------------|-------|--------|
| Great Horned Owl | Lushootseed (Coast Salish) | Approximated as echoing the bird's deep hooting | Vocal imitation | [C17] |
| Common Raven | Multiple NW Coast languages | Names often reflect the bird's varied, complex vocalizations | Vocal character | [C2] |
| Sandhill Crane | Sahaptin (Yakama, Warm Springs) | Names reflecting the crane's far-carrying bugling call | Vocal imitation | [C14] |

**Descriptive names** -- Appearance or behavior encoded:

| Species | Nation/Language | Description basis | Source |
|---------|----------------|------------------|--------|
| Bald Eagle | Multiple NW Coast | Names referencing the white head/tail of adults | [C1, C2] |
| Rufous Hummingbird | Coast Salish | Names reflecting the bird's small size, brilliance, or hovering behavior | [C13] |
| Pileated Woodpecker | Lushootseed (Coast Salish) | Names referencing the bird's hammering behavior or red crest | [C17] |
| Northern Flicker | Multiple Salish languages | Names often referencing the salmon-red underwing flash | [C1] |

**Relational/ecological names** -- The bird's role or ecological niche encoded:

Some published sources describe bird names that reference the species' relationship to other organisms, to seasonal events, or to human activities. For example, Hunn (1990) documents that Sahaptin bird names sometimes embed the bird's seasonal association -- birds named in relation to the time of year they appear, linking avian phenology to the broader seasonal round of resource harvesting [C14].

## 3.2 Classification Depth

Published ethnographic sources reveal that PNW Indigenous languages recognize fine-grained taxonomic distinctions that sometimes exceed those of early Western ornithology:

**Subspecies-level recognition:** Suttles (1987) documents that Coast Salish bird vocabulary distinguished between forms that Western science treated as a single species until recent decades. For example, published sources indicate that some Salish languages had distinct terms for what Western ornithology formerly lumped as "Blue Grouse" -- now split into Sooty Grouse and Dusky Grouse (AOS 2006). Indigenous observers recognized the ecological and morphological differences between west-side and east-side forms long before the formal taxonomic split [C1].

**Seasonal plumage recognition:** Published vocabularies indicate that some languages had distinct names for the same species in different seasonal plumages, reflecting close observation of molt cycles. This is particularly documented for ptarmigan (white winter vs. mottled summer plumage) and some waterfowl (eclipse vs. breeding plumage) [C2, C10].

**Age-class recognition:** Published Tlingit ecological vocabulary includes distinct terms for juvenile and adult plumages of Bald Eagle -- a species that takes five years to acquire adult plumage. The four-to-five distinct plumage stages were recognized and named, reflecting sustained, multi-generational observation of a long-lived species [C10].

**Behavioral classification:** Some documented Indigenous classification systems group birds by behavior rather than morphology -- "birds that dive," "birds that fly high," "birds that come with the salmon" -- creating functional ecological categories that cross Western taxonomic boundaries. Hunn (1990) documents this approach in Sahaptin bird classification [C14].

## 3.3 What Is Not Documented Here

Published bird vocabularies in PNW Indigenous languages represent only a fraction of the total linguistic knowledge about birds. Many languages were documented during periods of active cultural suppression (residential schools, language loss), and the published records often reflect the vocabulary of only a few speakers. Languages with ongoing revitalization efforts (Lushootseed, Halkomelem, Nuu-chah-nulth, Kwak'wala) continue to recover bird vocabulary that was not captured in historical ethnographies. This document does not attempt to reconstruct unpublished vocabulary or to present partial word lists that might misrepresent the depth and accuracy of Indigenous classification systems [C2, C7].

# PART IV: COSMOLOGICAL AND CEREMONIAL BIRDS

## 4.1 Raven (*Corvus corax*) -- The Transformer

The Common Raven holds a position in Northwest Coast cosmology that has no equivalent in Western ornithology. Published sources across multiple nations document Raven as a cosmological figure of first importance:

### Tlingit Raven

The Tlingit social system is organized into two moieties: Raven (Yeil) and Eagle/Wolf (Ch'aak'/Gooch). Every Tlingit person belongs to one moiety, determining marriage rules, ceremonial responsibilities, and social identity. Raven moiety is not "named after" the bird in a casual sense -- the relationship is foundational, encoding social structure into the natural world. Thornton (2008) describes how Tlingit place names throughout southeastern Alaska reference Raven's activities in the landscape, creating a geography of narrative that links ecological knowledge to cosmological understanding [C10].

Published Tlingit oral literature describes Raven as the being who released the sun, moon, and stars from a box where they were hidden -- bringing light to the world. Raven is simultaneously the creator and the trickster, a figure of immense power and unpredictable behavior. These are not "myths" in the Western sense of fictional stories but are understood within Tlingit tradition as accounts of actual events in a time when the world was being formed [C10].

### Kwakwaka'wakw Raven

In Kwakwaka'wakw ceremonial art, Raven appears on totem poles, house posts, masks, and regalia. Boas (1966) documented Kwakwaka'wakw narratives in which Raven functions as a culture hero and transformer. The Raven mask (*Gwaxw'lakw*) is among the most iconic objects in Northwest Coast art, featuring an articulated beak that opens to reveal a human face within -- representing Raven's power of transformation [C8, C11].

### Haida Raven

Raven is one of the two Haida moieties (Raven and Eagle). Published Haida oral literature, as documented by Swanton (1905) and subsequent researchers, positions Raven as the central figure in the creation of the Haida world. Raven's image dominates Haida art -- the most widely recognized Indigenous art tradition in the PNW. The Royal BC Museum and Burke Museum collections include numerous Raven representations [C5, C6].

### Ecological Observations Encoded in Raven Narratives

Published Raven narratives across PNW nations encode accurate ecological observations:

- Raven's theft of food from other animals reflects the species' kleptoparasitic behavior (stealing food from other birds and predators -- documented in Western ornithology as a common foraging strategy; see Module 1c)
- Raven's vocalizations are described with extraordinary precision in oral traditions -- published accounts distinguish between alarm calls, food-finding calls, play vocalizations, and pair-bonding sounds, paralleling Western descriptions of the species' vocal repertoire (>30 distinct call types documented by Western science)
- Raven's intelligence and problem-solving ability are central themes in published narratives -- consistent with modern cognitive science research documenting corvid tool use, planning, and social intelligence [C2, C10, C11, O1]

## 4.2 Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus* and *Aquila chrysaetos*) -- Sacred Authority

### Bald Eagle Across PNW Nations

Bald Eagle holds profound significance for virtually all PNW Indigenous nations. Published sources document eagle feather protocols, ceremonial uses (at Level 1 -- publicly described only), and the eagle's role as a symbol of authority and spiritual power. Key documented aspects:

**Eagle feathers and federal law.** The intersection of cultural sovereignty and federal wildlife law is made tangible in eagle feather policy. The Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (1940) makes it illegal to possess eagle feathers without authorization. The National Eagle Repository (Commerce City, Colorado) distributes naturally shed or salvaged eagle feathers to enrolled members of federally recognized tribes for ceremonial use. Demand far exceeds supply -- wait times can exceed two years. This regulatory framework is itself a product of the collision between Indigenous cultural rights and federal wildlife conservation, and its history illuminates the ongoing negotiation between these systems [G1, C2].

**Coast Salish eagle knowledge.** Suttles (1987) documents that Coast Salish peoples distinguished between immature and adult Bald Eagles in their ecological knowledge, recognizing the multi-year maturation process. Eagle sightings were integrated into seasonal ecological calendars -- eagles gathering at salmon streams signaled the salmon runs that structured the Coast Salish annual round [C1].

**Kwakwaka'wakw Thunderbird.** The Thunderbird (*Kwanusila*) of Kwakwaka'wakw tradition -- a being of immense power whose wingbeats cause thunder and whose eyes flash lightning -- is iconographically represented with features of both eagle and supernatural bird. Thunderbird appears on totem poles, house fronts, and ceremonial regalia throughout Kwakwaka'wakw territory. Published descriptions make clear that Thunderbird is not simply a "mythological eagle" but a distinct cosmological being, though its visual representation draws on eagle morphology. Kwakwaka'wakw cultural centers at Alert Bay and Campbell River display Thunderbird imagery in public exhibits [C8, C11].

**Nuu-chah-nulth eagle ceremonial context.** Drucker (1951) documents that among the Nuu-chah-nulth, eagle down was used in public welcome ceremonies -- scattered as a gesture of peace and goodwill. This use of eagle down is one of the most widely documented ceremonial practices across Northwest Coast nations and is regularly described in published ethnographic literature and public cultural events [C7, C12].

### Golden Eagle

Golden Eagle is less frequently documented in published Northwest Coast ethnography than Bald Eagle (consistent with the Golden Eagle's preference for interior, open habitats versus the Bald Eagle's coastal/riparian distribution), but is prominent in published accounts from interior Plateau and Great Basin nations. Shoshone-Bannock and Northern Paiute eagle knowledge is documented in published ethnographic sources, though much of the specific ceremonial context remains unpublished and restricted [C2].

## 4.3 Thunderbird -- Beyond Any Single Species

The Thunderbird concept appears in published accounts from nearly every PNW Indigenous nation. It is critical to understand that Thunderbird is not a "mythologized eagle" -- it is a distinct cosmological being that exists outside Western taxonomic classification. Published accounts describe Thunderbird as:

- Enormously larger than any living bird
- Capable of causing thunder with its wingbeats
- Capable of causing lightning from its eyes
- A being of the upper world/sky domain
- An adversary of the great serpent or whale (Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth traditions)

The Thunderbird-Whale conflict is one of the most widely documented motifs in Northwest Coast published oral literature. It appears in Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, Makah, and Coast Salish published traditions. The motif encodes an understanding of fundamental natural forces (sky vs. ocean, air vs. water) within a narrative framework [C2, C8, C11, C12].

This document records Thunderbird in this section because of its avian imagery, while acknowledging that reducing Thunderbird to a biological species would be a category error. Thunderbird is a cosmological entity, not a taxonomic one [C2].

## 4.4 Owl -- Complexity and Caution

Published ethnographic sources from multiple PNW nations indicate that owls carry complex and often ambivalent cultural associations. However, the specific nature of these associations varies by nation and in many cases involves restricted knowledge that is not appropriate for publication.

What published sources consistently document:

- **Vocal recognition:** PNW Indigenous peoples distinguished between owl species by call with great precision. Published Sahaptin vocabulary includes distinct terms for different owl species based on their vocalizations [C14].
- **Nocturnal significance:** Owls' nocturnal habits -- their presence in darkness, their ability to see what others cannot -- are consistently noted in published ethnographic sources as culturally significant, though the specific significance varies by nation and knowledge tier [C2].
- **Behavioral observation:** Published accounts document precise observations of owl hunting behavior, prey selection, and habitat preferences that parallel and sometimes exceed Western ornithological descriptions [C14].

**Note:** Published sources indicate that in some PNW traditions, specific owl species carry associations with death, the spirit world, or transformation. This document does not elaborate on these associations because: (a) the specific nature varies by nation and cannot be generalized, (b) elaboration may inadvertently cross into Level 2 or 3 knowledge, and (c) the associations are best understood within their specific cultural contexts, not extracted and presented in isolation [C2].

## 4.5 Hummingbird -- Speed, Beauty, and Resilience

The Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*) is the most ecologically prominent hummingbird in the PNW, and published ethnographic sources document its cultural significance across multiple nations:

**Coast Salish.** Published accounts describe the hummingbird as a being of remarkable beauty and determination. Its early spring arrival -- among the first Neotropical migrants to return to the PNW -- made it a seasonal marker. Gunther (1973) documents Coast Salish recognition of the hummingbird's role in pollination, linking the bird to the flowering of important plant species [C13, C17].

**Nuu-chah-nulth.** The hummingbird appears in published Nuu-chah-nulth art, typically as a small but powerful figure. Its ability to hover -- unique among PNW birds -- and its iridescent plumage made it an object of sustained aesthetic and ecological observation [C7].

**Contemporary relevance.** The Rufous Hummingbird's documented population decline (BBS: -2.0% per year since 1966; see Module 2b) has been noted by Indigenous communities whose seasonal observations provide a longer-term baseline for tracking the species' presence. Indigenous phenological observations of hummingbird arrival dates, while not systematically published, represent a potential multi-generational dataset for understanding climate-driven shifts in migration timing [O1, G4].

# PART V: TRADITIONAL ECOLOGICAL KNOWLEDGE OF BIRDS

## 5.1 Phenological Calendars -- Birds as Seasonal Markers

PNW Indigenous seasonal calendars, as documented in published sources, use bird behavior as key indicators of ecological transitions. These are not casual observations but structured knowledge systems in which specific bird events signal specific resource availability:

### Coast Salish Seasonal Round and Bird Phenology

Suttles (1987, 1990) and Haeberlin and Gunther (1930) document a Coast Salish seasonal round in which bird events serve as phenological markers:

| Season | Bird Event | Ecological Signal | Resource Implication |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Late winter | Eagles gather at river mouths | Early salmon runs approaching | Prepare fishing gear |
| Early spring | Rufous Hummingbird arrival | Salmonberry and red-flowering currant blooming | Root and shoot harvesting begins |
| Spring | Sandhill Crane return to marshes | Wetland warming, invertebrate emergence | Wetland plant harvesting |
| Spring | Shorebird staging at estuaries | Tide-flat productivity peak | Shellfish harvesting optimal |
| Late spring | Swallow arrival | Sustained warm weather, insect emergence | Major fishing preparations |
| Summer | Seabird colony activity peaks | Ocean productivity high | Offshore fishing and marine resource harvesting |
| Late summer | Shorebird southbound staging | Season turning, daylight shortening | Berry harvesting, fish preservation |
| Autumn | Waterfowl arrival from north | Winter approaching, streams full | Salmon spawning, winter food preparation |
| Winter | Owl activity increases (calling) | Shortest days, deep cold | Winter ceremonial season |

These phenological links are not coincidental -- they reflect millennia of observation that birds and human-relevant resources respond to the same environmental cues (temperature, day length, precipitation). The Coast Salish calendar is, in effect, an ecological forecasting system that uses avian behavior as leading indicators [C1, C2, C13, C17].

### Sahaptin Seasonal Round

Hunn (1990) documents the Sahaptin (Yakama, Warm Springs) seasonal round in the Columbia Plateau, where bird phenology is integrated with the salmon cycle, root harvest, and berry harvest:

- **The first meadowlark song** in late winter signals the approaching end of the cold season and the beginning of root-gathering preparations
- **Crane return** to Klamath and Columbia Basin wetlands signals spring warming
- **Nighthawk appearance** marks the onset of true summer heat in the interior steppe
- **Waterfowl southbound flights** over the Columbia Basin signal the approach of winter and the final preparations for cold-season subsistence [C14]

### Tlingit Seasonal Knowledge

Thornton (2008) documents Tlingit seasonal ecological knowledge in southeastern Alaska, where bird events are embedded in a landscape of named places. Specific locations are known as places where particular bird events occur at particular times -- herring spawn sites where seabird aggregations signal the herring run, river mouths where eagle gatherings mark salmon arrival. This spatial-temporal knowledge links birds, fish, and place into an integrated ecological map [C10].

## 5.2 Behavioral Observations

Published ethnographic sources document Indigenous behavioral observations of PNW birds that demonstrate sustained, precise ecological attention:

### Corvid Intelligence

Published accounts from multiple PNW nations describe corvid intelligence with a sophistication that parallels modern cognitive ethology:

- **Raven's food caching:** Multiple published sources describe Raven hiding food for later retrieval -- an observation consistent with Western documentation of corvid caching behavior and spatial memory. Published Tlingit accounts describe Raven's ability to remember cache locations, anticipate the behavior of cache-raiding competitors, and adjust caching strategy accordingly -- observations that align with experimental demonstrations of corvid future planning [C10, O1].
- **Crow social dynamics:** Coast Salish observations of crow social behavior -- published in Suttles (1987) and Haeberlin and Gunther (1930) -- describe communal roosting, sentinel behavior, and coordinated mobbing of predators. These descriptions match Western ornithological accounts of Northwestern Crow and American Crow social systems [C1, C17].
- **Steller's Jay alarm behavior:** Published accounts from Coast Salish and Kwakwaka'wakw peoples describe the Steller's Jay as a forest alarm system -- its calls alerting the forest community (including human hunters) to approaching disturbance. This observation is documented in Western ornithology as heterospecific alarm calling, where other species (including squirrels, deer) respond to jay alarm calls (see Module 1c) [C1, C8].

### Eagle Fishing Behavior

Published Coast Salish and Nuu-chah-nulth accounts describe Bald Eagle fishing behavior with observational detail:

- Eagles observed selecting specific fishing locations based on water clarity and fish visibility
- Seasonal shifts in eagle distribution tracking salmon run timing and location
- Age-related differences in fishing success (immature eagles less skilled than adults)
- Kleptoparasitism -- eagles stealing fish from Osprey, a behavior described in published Indigenous accounts before it was formally documented in Western ornithology

These observations, embedded in oral traditions transmitted across generations, represent a behavioral ecology dataset with a temporal depth that Western science cannot replicate [C1, C2, C3].

### Grouse Ecology

Published accounts from Sahaptin-speaking peoples describe Greater Sage-Grouse lek behavior with precision:

- Males gathering at traditional display grounds in early spring
- The distinctive popping/booming sounds of the male display
- Female observation and mate selection
- Lek-site fidelity across years

Hunn (1990) documents that Sahaptin ecological knowledge included the understanding that sage-grouse were "of the sagebrush" in a fundamental way -- their presence indicated healthy sagebrush habitat, and their absence indicated ecological degradation. This is functionally equivalent to the modern Western concept of an indicator species, derived independently through multi-generational observation [C14].

## 5.3 Habitat Knowledge

Published ethnographic sources document Indigenous understanding of bird-habitat relationships that in some cases exceeds the resolution of early Western ecological surveys:

### Old-Growth Forest Birds

Coast Salish and Kwakwaka'wakw peoples, as documented in published sources, recognized the association between old-growth forest structure and specific bird assemblages. Published accounts describe:

- Large woodpeckers (Pileated Woodpecker) requiring large-diameter snags -- an observation formalized in Western forest ecology only in the late 20th century
- Marbled Murrelet's peculiar habit of flying inland from the ocean to old-growth forest -- observed by coastal peoples long before Western ornithologists discovered the species' tree-nesting behavior in 1974
- The association between old-growth canopy complexity and high bird diversity -- described in published Coast Salish ecological knowledge as the understanding that "old forests are full of life" in ways that young forests are not [C1, C9, C15]

### Wetland Birds and Water Management

Published Klamath ecological knowledge documents an understanding of the relationship between water levels and bird communities in Klamath Basin wetlands:

- High water supporting diving ducks and grebes
- Low water exposing mudflats for shorebirds
- Marsh management (burning, water-level manipulation) to maintain diverse bird habitat
- The recognition that bird diversity was highest when the wetland supported a mosaic of depths and vegetation types

This understanding -- that habitat heterogeneity supports species diversity -- is a foundational principle of modern conservation biology, arrived at independently through millennia of place-based observation [C2, C14].

### Marine and Pelagic Knowledge

The Makah and Nuu-chah-nulth, as ocean-going peoples, possessed published-documented knowledge of pelagic bird ecology that most land-based cultures lacked:

- Seabird aggregations as indicators of fish schools and productive ocean zones
- Albatross occurrence as a signal of offshore convergence currents
- Storm-petrel behavior as a weather indicator
- Seasonal shifts in seabird distribution reflecting oceanographic changes

This maritime ornithological knowledge enabled navigational and resource-finding applications -- seabirds served as guides to productive fishing areas. The Makah Cultural and Research Center documents these relationships in public educational materials [C4, C12].

# PART VI: MATERIAL CULTURE AND BIRDS

## 6.1 Feather Use in Regalia and Material Culture

Published museum collections and ethnographic descriptions document extensive use of bird products in PNW Indigenous material culture. The following summary draws only on published, publicly exhibited, or peer-reviewed sources:

### Eagle Feathers

Eagle feathers are documented in published sources as the most culturally significant avian material across PNW nations. Their use is governed by protocols that vary by nation and that are only partially described in published literature:

- **Headdresses and ceremonial regalia:** Eagle feathers in headdresses and dance regalia are among the most visually prominent elements of PNW ceremonial life, as documented in museum collections (Burke Museum, Royal BC Museum, Makah Cultural and Research Center). The specific meaning and protocols attached to eagle feather regalia vary by nation and are largely unpublished [C4, C5, C6].
- **Eagle down:** Scattered as a welcoming gesture in public ceremonies -- one of the most widely documented and publicly performed uses of eagle material in the Northwest Coast [C7, C12].
- **Federal regulatory framework:** The Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act, the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, and the National Eagle Repository system reflect the ongoing negotiation between Indigenous cultural rights and federal wildlife law. Published legal and ethnographic literature documents this intersection [G1].

### Other Avian Materials in Published Ethnography

Published sources document the use of feathers and other materials from numerous non-eagle species:

| Species | Material | Published use | Source |
|---------|----------|--------------|--------|
| Common Raven | Feathers | Regalia, narrative art objects | [C8, C11] |
| Steller's Jay | Feathers | Decorative use in published museum collections | [C5, C6] |
| Great Blue Heron | Feathers | Documented in published descriptions of ceremonial headgear | [C2] |
| Trumpeter/Tundra Swan | Down, feathers | Documented as valued material in published accounts | [C2, C3] |
| Waterfowl (various) | Down, feathers, skin | Blankets, capes, and ceremonial items described in published ethnography | [C2, C12] |
| Woodpecker (various) | Scalps, feathers | Red woodpecker scalps documented in published descriptions of regalia (widespread in California and southern PNW) | [C2] |
| Cormorant | Feathers | Waterproof applications described in published sources | [C3] |
| Flicker | Feathers | The salmon-red feathers of the Red-shafted Flicker (Northern Flicker) documented in published descriptions of headbands, ear ornaments, and decorative applications | [C2, C5] |

### Bird Imagery in Art

Northwest Coast art is among the most widely recognized Indigenous art traditions in the world, and bird imagery is central to it. Published sources and public museum collections document:

**Formline design:** The distinctive two-dimensional art style of Northwest Coast peoples (Coast Salish, Haida, Tlingit, Kwakwaka'wakw, Nuu-chah-nulth, Tsimshian) frequently depicts birds -- Raven, Eagle, Thunderbird, Heron, Kingfisher, and others. Bird images appear on totem poles, house fronts, bentwood boxes, masks, prints, blankets, and contemporary art. The formline tradition represents birds with species-specific iconographic features (Raven's straight beak vs. Eagle's curved beak, Heron's long neck, Thunderbird's curled horns) that enable identification [C5, C6, C8].

**Totem poles:** Published descriptions of totem poles from multiple Northwest Coast nations document avian figures as among the most common crest elements. Eagle, Raven, Thunderbird, and Owl appear frequently. The position of a bird on the pole and its associated narrative are specific to the family or clan that commissioned the pole [C6, C8].

**Masks:** The Kwakwaka'wakw transformation mask -- a hinged mask that opens to reveal a different face within -- is one of the most celebrated art forms of the Northwest Coast. Published Raven transformation masks, which open from Raven's beak to reveal a human face, are displayed in museums worldwide and represent the cosmological theme of transformation between human and animal states [C8, C11].

## 6.2 Bird Hunting Technologies

Published ethnographic sources document specialized technologies for harvesting birds in the PNW:

### Waterfowl Hunting

Stewart (1977), Suttles (1987), and other published sources describe Coast Salish waterfowl hunting technologies:

- **Net drives:** Long nets strung across channels or tidal narrows to intercept flying ducks. Published accounts describe coordinated community drives in which canoes were used to flush waterfowl into nets -- requiring precise knowledge of duck flight patterns, wind direction, and tidal timing [C3, C17].
- **Decoys:** Published accounts and museum collections document carved wooden duck decoys used by Coast Salish and Plateau peoples. Some documented decoys were remarkably naturalistic, suggesting detailed observation of waterfowl posture and behavior [C5].
- **Bow and arrow / spear:** Published accounts describe the use of bird-specific arrows (blunt tips for stunning, multi-pronged tips for waterfowl) [C3].

### Seabird and Shorebird Harvesting

Published Makah and Nuu-chah-nulth sources document:

- Harvesting of seabirds and eggs from nesting colonies on offshore rocks and islands -- requiring navigational skill and ecological knowledge of colony locations and timing [C4, C12]
- Night harvesting of storm-petrels and auklets using fire attraction -- a technique documented in published ethnographic sources from multiple maritime PNW nations [C12]

### Grouse Hunting

Published Sahaptin and Plateau sources document:

- Snaring of grouse using horsehair nooses set along trails
- Communal drives of sage-grouse using knowledge of lek locations and flight patterns
- Detailed knowledge of grouse seasonal movements used to predict where birds would be concentrated [C14]

# PART VII: BIRDS IN ORAL LITERATURE

## 7.1 Published Narrative Traditions

The oral literatures of PNW nations are vast, complex, and only partially published. Published collections of narratives -- by Boas, Drucker, Swanton, Elmendorf, and others -- contain numerous stories featuring avian characters. These narratives are not "bird stories" in the Western children's literature sense but are sophisticated philosophical and ecological texts that encode knowledge about the natural world, social relations, and cosmological order.

### Raven Narratives

Published Raven narrative cycles exist for Tlingit (Thornton 2008, Swanton 1909), Haida (Swanton 1905), Kwakwaka'wakw (Boas 1966), and Tsimshian traditions. Common published themes include:

- **Raven as light-bringer:** Raven releases the sun, moon, and stars from a box, bringing light to the world. Published across multiple nations with local variations.
- **Raven as food-obtainer:** Raven uses cleverness and trickery to obtain salmon, eulachon, and other foods. These narratives encode ecological knowledge about food resources and the social norms governing their distribution.
- **Raven as transformer:** Raven changes the landscape, creates rivers, establishes the tides, and sets the natural order. These narratives provide etiological accounts of landscape features while encoding ecological relationships.
- **Raven and the other animals:** Narratives in which Raven interacts with Eagle, Crow, Bear, Salmon, and other beings encode inter-species relationships that parallel ecological network descriptions (see Module 5) [C10, C11].

### Eagle Narratives

Published Eagle narratives are less numerous than Raven cycles but appear across PNW nations:

- **Eagle as authority figure:** In published narratives from multiple nations, Eagle represents authority, high status, and clarity of vision. Eagle's position at the top of tall trees parallels its social position in narrative hierarchies.
- **Thunderbird-Whale narratives:** Published Kwakwaka'wakw and Nuu-chah-nulth narratives describe the cosmic battle between Thunderbird (drawing on Eagle imagery) and Whale, representing the conflict between sky/land and ocean powers [C8, C11, C12].

### Other Avian Characters in Published Literature

| Species | Narrative role (published sources) | Nations (published) |
|---------|-----------------------------------|---------------------|
| Blue Jay / Steller's Jay | Gossip, noisemaker, alarm-giver | Coast Salish, Kwakwaka'wakw [C1, C8] |
| Wren | Small but clever, brave despite tiny size | Coast Salish [C16] |
| Heron | Patient fisher, stillness as strategy | Coast Salish, Nuu-chah-nulth [C1, C7] |
| Kingfisher | Skilled diver, precision hunter | Coast Salish [C17] |
| Woodpecker | Hard worker, persistent | Coast Salish, Sahaptin [C14, C17] |
| Crane | Grace, seasonal fidelity, long life | Klamath, Sahaptin [C2, C14] |
| Grouse | Drumming as communication, forest presence | Sahaptin, Plateau peoples [C14] |
| Crow | Social intelligence, community, urban adaptability | Coast Salish [C1, C17] |

### Ecological Knowledge Encoded in Narratives

Published oral literature is not merely "about" birds -- it transmits ecological knowledge:

**Predator-prey relationships:** Published narratives frequently describe realistic predator-prey interactions (Eagle catches Salmon, Owl hunts Mouse, Hawk catches Grouse). These descriptions encode food-web knowledge into memorable narrative form, ensuring its transmission across generations [C10, C14].

**Habitat associations:** Published narratives place animals in their correct habitats -- Raven in the forest, Eagle at the river, Grouse in the thicket, Ptarmigan on the mountain. Listeners absorb habitat-species associations through narrative context rather than through explicit instruction [C14].

**Behavioral ecology:** Published narratives describe animal behaviors with precision -- Raven's caching, Eagle's patience, Heron's stillness, Woodpecker's persistence. These behavioral descriptions serve dual purposes: teaching ecological knowledge and modeling social virtues (patience, persistence, cleverness, cooperation) [C9, C10].

**Seasonal patterns:** Published narratives encode seasonal knowledge -- the arrival and departure of migratory species, the seasonal availability of resources, the ecological signals that mark seasonal transitions. Listeners absorb phenological knowledge through stories set at specific times of year [C14].

# PART VIII: CONTEMPORARY INDIGENOUS ORNITHOLOGY

## 8.1 Collaborative Research

Contemporary partnerships between Indigenous communities and Western scientists are producing integrated ecological knowledge that draws on both traditions:

### Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) in Conservation

Published examples of TEK integration in PNW avian conservation include:

**Sage-grouse management.** Shoshone-Bannock traditional ecological knowledge of sage-grouse lek locations and seasonal movements has been documented as exceeding the spatial and temporal resolution of Western survey data in some areas. Published reports describe collaborative efforts to incorporate TEK into sage-grouse conservation planning on tribal lands [C2, G1].

**Salmon-eagle relationships.** Coast Salish ecological knowledge of eagle-salmon relationships -- particularly the seasonal and spatial patterns of eagle aggregation at salmon streams -- provides a multi-generational baseline for understanding changes in both eagle and salmon populations. This knowledge informed the development of Bald Eagle management plans in the Puget Sound region [C1, G9].

**Klamath Basin water management.** Klamath tribal ecological knowledge of waterfowl, marsh birds, and fish populations in Upper Klamath Lake and its associated wetlands has been cited in published water management proceedings. The Klamath peoples' multi-millennial observation of bird-water relationships provides ecological context that Western monitoring data (post-1900) cannot replicate [C2, C14].

**Marine bird monitoring.** Makah and Nuu-chah-nulth maritime ecological knowledge informs seabird monitoring in the outer coast and offshore zones. Published accounts describe tribal observations of changes in seabird distribution and abundance that correlate with oceanographic shifts documented by NOAA and other agencies [C4, C7, G15].

## 8.2 Language Revitalization and Bird Knowledge

Several PNW Indigenous languages are undergoing active revitalization efforts that include recovery of ecological vocabulary:

- **Lushootseed:** The Puyallup Tribal Language Program and Tulalip Lushootseed Department are actively teaching Lushootseed, including ecological and natural history vocabulary. Bird names are part of this curriculum.
- **Halkomelem:** The Stolo Nation and other Halkomelem-speaking communities have published language learning materials that include bird vocabulary and ecological terms.
- **Nuu-chah-nulth:** The Nuu-chah-nulth Tribal Council supports language programs that maintain and transmit ecological knowledge vocabulary [C7].
- **Kwak'wala:** Kwakwaka'wakw language programs include ecological terminology as part of cultural education [C8].

Language revitalization is directly relevant to ornithology because bird names in Indigenous languages encode ecological knowledge -- classification systems, behavioral observations, habitat associations, and seasonal patterns -- that cannot be preserved in translation. The loss of a language means the loss of a classification system that may distinguish ecological categories invisible to English speakers [C9].

## 8.3 Indigenous Protected Areas and Avian Conservation

Several PNW tribal nations have established or co-manage protected areas where avian conservation is informed by both traditional ecological knowledge and Western science:

- **Makah Tribal lands:** Management of marine resources and seabird habitats on the Olympic Peninsula tip, informed by traditional maritime ecological knowledge [C4]
- **Klamath Basin:** Ongoing tribal involvement in wetland management decisions that affect waterfowl habitat on traditional Klamath lands [C2]
- **Haida Heritage Foundation:** Collaborative management of Haida Gwaii lands and waters, including seabird colony protection, drawing on Haida ecological knowledge and Western monitoring [C6]
- **Various Coast Salish co-management agreements:** Tribal involvement in Puget Sound and Salish Sea marine resource management, with implications for seabird, shorebird, and waterfowl conservation [C1, G9]

## 8.4 Citizen Science and Indigenous Participation

Published eBird data [O2] shows increasing participation from Indigenous communities in PNW citizen science programs. While eBird and similar platforms are Western-structured tools, they provide a mechanism for recording observations that complement -- but do not replace -- traditional ecological knowledge systems. Some tribal programs use eBird alongside traditional observation protocols, creating parallel records that can be compared across knowledge systems.

**Important distinction:** Citizen science participation by Indigenous communities does not constitute sharing of traditional ecological knowledge. eBird observations are individual data points; TEK is a system of integrated knowledge transmitted across generations. The two are complementary but not equivalent [O2, C9].

# SYNTHESIS: CULTURAL ORNITHOLOGY AND THE FUTURE

## Why Cultural Ornithology Matters for Conservation

The integration of Indigenous ornithological knowledge with Western science has practical conservation implications:

1. **Temporal depth.** Western ornithological monitoring in the PNW dates to approximately 1900 (with scattered earlier observations). Indigenous ecological knowledge provides baselines extending thousands of years -- enabling detection of changes invisible in the Western record. Published examples include long-term changes in waterfowl abundance at Klamath Basin, shifts in eagle distribution along the Salish Sea, and changes in grouse populations on the Columbia Plateau [C2, C14].

2. **Spatial resolution.** Indigenous territorial knowledge encompasses specific watersheds, estuaries, and landscapes at a resolution that broad-scale Western surveys (BBS routes, eBird hotspots) may miss. Published TEK data have identified ecological relationships and habitat requirements not captured in standardized survey protocols [C1, C10].

3. **Species-level observations.** Published Indigenous bird knowledge includes behavioral, ecological, and distributional observations that complement or exceed published Western data for some species. Documented examples include sage-grouse lek knowledge, eagle-salmon phenological relationships, and seabird-oceanographic correlations [C4, C14].

4. **Values and motivation.** Indigenous worldviews that treat birds as relatives, neighbors, and teachers -- rather than as resources or study subjects -- offer a motivational framework for conservation that complements the utilitarian and scientific frameworks of Western wildlife management [C9].

5. **Governance models.** Indigenous governance systems that managed bird populations for millennia -- through seasonal harvesting protocols, habitat management (burning, water-level control), and social norms governing take -- represent tested approaches to sustainable resource management [C14, C15].

## What This Document Cannot Do

This document presents the publicly available fraction of Indigenous ornithological knowledge. It cannot:

- Replace engagement with specific Indigenous communities
- Represent the full depth of any nation's bird knowledge
- Substitute for consultation with knowledge keepers
- Authorize use of any cultural content beyond educational reference
- Speak for any community's relationship to birds

**The definitive authorities on Indigenous ornithological knowledge are the Indigenous peoples themselves.** Published sources are guides to a vast landscape of knowledge whose full extent is known only to its keepers [C2, C9].
