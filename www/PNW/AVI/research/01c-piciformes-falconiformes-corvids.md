# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Module 1C: Piciformes, Falconiformes, and Passeriformes (Tyrannidae through Corvidae)

> **Module 1C deliverable for the PNW Avian Taxonomy mission ("Wings of the Pacific Northwest")**
>
> This document covers three taxonomic orders — Piciformes (woodpeckers), Falconiformes (falcons), and the first families of Passeriformes (tyrant flycatchers, shrikes, vireos, corvids) — following AOS taxonomic sequence. These groups include some of the PNW's most ecologically consequential species: cavity creators that structure entire forest communities, falcon predators that regulate prey populations, and corvids whose cognitive abilities and seed-caching behaviors shape forest regeneration across millennia.
>
> **Species count:** ~41 species (12 Picidae, 5 Falconidae, 11 Tyrannidae, 1 Laniidae, 4 Vireonidae, 10 Corvidae)
>
> **Design principle:** Woodpeckers are ecosystem engineers — their cavities become the housing stock for dozens of secondary cavity nesters. Falcons are apex aerial predators whose population trajectories mirror environmental contamination and recovery. Corvids are cognitive generalists whose tool use, caching, and social learning represent the upper bound of avian intelligence. These three groups anchor the structural, predatory, and cognitive axes of PNW bird communities.
>
> **Safety boundaries:** No GPS coordinates or specific nest sites for ESA-listed species (county/watershed level only). All population data attributed to specific agencies or peer-reviewed studies. No policy advocacy. Conservation threats presented with sourced data. Only Level 1 (publicly available) cultural content. All Indigenous knowledge references name specific nations.

## Order Piciformes — Woodpeckers

### Family Picidae — Woodpeckers and Sapsuckers

The Picidae are arguably the most ecologically consequential bird family in PNW forests. As primary cavity excavators, woodpeckers create the nesting and roosting sites used by dozens of secondary cavity nesters — small owls, chickadees, nuthatches, bluebirds, swallows, flying squirrels, and bats. A single Pileated Woodpecker cavity tree may be used by 5-10 species over its lifetime. The removal of snags and dead wood from managed forests directly reduces woodpecker populations and cascades through the entire cavity-nesting community [P18, G6].

PNW woodpeckers partition forest resources along multiple axes: tree species, tree condition (live vs. dead), bark thickness, trunk diameter, foraging height, and fire history. This niche partitioning allows 8-10 species to coexist in structurally complex old-growth stands while simplified second-growth supports only 3-4. East-side dry forests and post-fire landscapes support a distinct woodpecker guild (White-headed, Black-backed, Lewis's) absent from west-side forests [P9, O1].

Woodpecker drumming — rapid bill strikes against resonant wood — serves as territorial advertisement analogous to song in passerines. Each species drums at a characteristic tempo, amplitude, and duration, enabling species identification by sound alone [P9].

### Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Dryocopus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (common)
- State OR: Not listed (common)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 40-49 cm (15.8-19.3 in) — largest woodpecker in the PNW, largest in North America north of Mexico
- **Wingspan:** 66-75 cm (26-29.5 in)
- **Mass:** 250-400 g (8.8-14.1 oz); males average 10-15% heavier than females
- **Plumage:** Predominantly black with white neck stripes, white wing linings visible in flight, prominent red crest. Males have red moustachial stripe extending from bill base; females have black forehead with red crest beginning at crown. Juveniles duller with shorter crest.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males slightly larger, red moustachial stripe diagnostic. Bill length averages 5-7% longer in males.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Mature and old-growth conifer and mixed forest with large-diameter snags (>50 cm DBH) for cavity excavation. Requires extensive forest tracts — home range 400-800 hectares in PNW old-growth, larger in fragmented landscapes. Prefers stands with >15 snags/hectare of suitable excavation size [G6, P18].
- **Wintering:** Same as breeding; non-migratory. May range more widely in winter, visiting forest edges and occasionally suburban areas with large trees.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~6,000 ft, concentrated below 4,500 ft in mature forest.
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland), Zone 5 (Riparian — large cottonwood)
- **Microhabitat:** Selects trees with heartwood decay (often caused by fungal infection — *Fomitopsis* spp.) that softens the interior while bark remains hard. Foraging concentrated on large-diameter logs and snags harboring carpenter ant colonies (*Camponotus* spp.).

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Carpenter ants (*Camponotus* spp.) comprise 40-60% of diet year-round. Also wood-boring beetle larvae (Cerambycidae, Buprestidae), other ants, and supplementary fruits (wild grape, dogwood berries, elderberry) in fall-winter.
- **Foraging strategy:** Powerful excavation — strips large slabs of bark and chisels deep rectangular holes in decaying wood to access ant galleries. Foraging excavations are diagnostic: large, roughly rectangular holes 10-20 cm deep, often at the base of snags or logs. Tongue extends 7-8 cm beyond bill tip, coated with sticky saliva and backward-pointing barbs for extracting ants from galleries.
- **Seasonal variation:** Carpenter ants dominate diet spring through fall. Supplementary fruit consumption increases in autumn. Winter foraging shifts to wood-boring beetle larvae in snags when ant colonies are dormant.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Primary cavity excavator — excavates a new cavity each year, typically 3-8 meters above ground in a large-diameter snag (>38 cm DBH preferred). Cavity entrance roughly oval, 8-10 cm diameter. Cavity depth 25-60 cm. Excavation takes 3-6 weeks, with both sexes contributing.
- **Clutch size:** 3-5 eggs (typically 4)
- **Incubation:** 15-18 days; both sexes incubate, male incubating at night
- **Fledging:** 26-28 days; young remain with adults for 2-3 months post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-July in PNW (cavity excavation begins March)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to slightly increasing (BBS trend +0.5% per year in WA/OR, 2006-2019) [G4]
- **Estimate:** Approximately 2.6 million individuals continent-wide (PIF 2020). PNW populations considered healthy relative to historical baseline [O4].
- **Threats:** Loss of large-diameter snags in managed forests (primary threat), forest fragmentation reducing territory connectivity, competition with European Starling for cavities in suburban interface [G6].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], Partners in Flight [O4]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Common | Keystone cavity creator |
| Zone 3: Lowland Forest | Year-round | Fairly common | Cavity creator in urban-forest interface |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Uncommon | Cavity creator where large snags persist |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Uncommon | Uses large cottonwood snags in river corridors |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Loud, irregular series of *kuk-kuk-kuk-kuk-kuk* — resonant, far-carrying, often given in flight or while perched. Slower and more irregular than Northern Flicker's similar call.
- **Drumming:** Deep, powerful, resonant rolls lasting 1-3 seconds on large-diameter resonant snags. Drum tempo approximately 15 strikes/second — slower and more powerful than smaller woodpeckers. Drumming audible at 0.5-1 km in quiet forest conditions.
- **Contact call:** Softer *wuk-wuk-wuk* between pair members during foraging.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Dryocopus pileatus* is the sole surviving New World representative of the genus *Dryocopus*, which also includes the Black Woodpecker (*D. martius*) of Eurasia — the only other woodpecker of comparable size in the Northern Hemisphere. The genus dates to the late Miocene (~8-10 Ma). The Ivory-billed Woodpecker (*Campephilus principalis*), the only larger North American woodpecker, is almost certainly extinct (last confirmed sighting: Singer Tract, Louisiana, 1944; contested reports since). The Pileated Woodpecker now occupies the large-woodpecker niche alone in North America. Its persistence in the PNW is directly linked to the survival of old-growth forest — a habitat that has declined ~80% since European settlement but remains more extensive in the PNW than in any other region of the contiguous United States [O1, G6].

#### Ecological Keystone Role

The Pileated Woodpecker's cavity-creating activity structures entire forest vertebrate communities. Cavities excavated by Pileated Woodpeckers are used by at least 38 species of secondary cavity nesters and roosters in PNW forests, including: Northern Saw-whet Owl, Western Screech-Owl, Barrow's Goldeneye (near water), Bufflehead, Wood Duck, American Kestrel, Vaux's Swift, Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, Black-capped Chickadee, Chestnut-backed Chickadee, Red-breasted Nuthatch, White-breasted Nuthatch, Northern Flying Squirrel, Douglas Squirrel, Bushy-tailed Woodrat, and several bat species (*Myotis* spp.). The loss of Pileated Woodpeckers from a forest stand initiates a cascade of secondary cavity nester declines within 5-15 years as existing cavities degrade and no new ones are created [G6, P18].

### Lewis's Woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Melanerpes*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (partially resident in southern OR/eastern WA)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (Candidate species review completed, not warranted)
- IUCN: Least Concern (but declining rangewide)
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Critical
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (steep decline)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 26-28 cm (10.2-11.0 in)
- **Wingspan:** 49-52 cm (19.3-20.5 in)
- **Mass:** 88-138 g (3.1-4.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Distinctive and unlike any other PNW woodpecker. Dark iridescent greenish-black back, silver-gray collar and breast, pinkish-red belly and face. In flight, broad rounded wings and slow, crow-like wingbeats — does not undulate like other woodpeckers. Juvenile duller with brownish head and reduced pink.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — sexes similar in plumage. Males average slightly larger.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open woodlands with standing dead trees: burned ponderosa pine forest (primary), Oregon white oak woodland with snags, cottonwood riparian forest with open understory. Requires open aerial space for flycatching foraging style. Strongly associated with post-fire landscapes 2-8 years after burn [O5, G8].
- **Wintering:** Oak woodland with acorn crops (Willamette Valley margins, Rogue Valley, Klamath), occasionally cottonwood riparian. Partial migrant — some individuals winter near breeding areas if acorn/nut crops adequate.
- **Elevation range:** 300-5,500 ft, concentrated 1,000-4,000 ft on east side
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak Woodland), Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — post-fire ponderosa)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 5 (Riparian — cottonwood stands)
- **Microhabitat:** Requires open canopy (<40% closure) with snags for nesting and aerial foraging space. Avoids dense forest. The intersection of snag availability and open canopy is the critical microhabitat constraint — modern fire suppression eliminates both.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects caught on the wing (spring-summer), acorns and other mast (fall-winter). A unique foraging generalist among North American woodpeckers.
- **Foraging strategy:** Sallies from exposed perches to catch insects in flight — a flycatcher-like foraging mode unique among PNW woodpeckers. Does not excavate wood for prey. In fall, harvests and caches acorns in bark crevices, fence posts, and other storage sites, defending caches through winter. This dual foraging strategy — aerial insectivory plus mast caching — is found in no other PNW woodpecker.
- **Seasonal variation:** Diet shifts dramatically: 70-90% aerial insects May-August, 70-90% acorns/nuts September-February [O1].

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cavity nester — uses natural cavities or excavates in soft snags; also readily uses previously excavated cavities (including Pileated and Northern Flicker cavities). Nest height 2-15 m.
- **Clutch size:** 5-9 eggs (typically 6-7)
- **Incubation:** 13-14 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** 28-34 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -2.5% per year rangewide (1966-2019), steeper declines in PNW. Washington populations have declined an estimated 70-80% since the 1960s [G4, G9].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 130,000 individuals continent-wide (PIF 2020); PNW populations now fragmented and local [O4].
- **Threats:** Fire suppression (eliminates snag habitat and open woodland structure), loss of Oregon white oak woodland (>90% decline in Willamette Valley), conversion of open ponderosa pine to closed-canopy forest, competition with European Starling for cavities, loss of cottonwood riparian stands [G9, O5].
- **Source:** USGS BBS [G4], WDFW [G9], PIF [O4]

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Breeding + some winter | Uncommon, declining | Indicator of open oak/burn habitat |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Breeding | Uncommon, local | Post-fire ponderosa specialist |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Breeding + some winter | Rare | Cottonwood cavity nester |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Harsh, chattering *churr-churr-churr* — lower pitched and less sharp than other *Melanerpes* species.
- **Drumming:** Weak and infrequent — consistent with flycatching foraging mode. Drums soft, short rolls at ~10 strikes/second.

#### Evolutionary Note

Lewis's Woodpecker was described by Alexander Wilson in 1811 from specimens collected during the Lewis and Clark expedition (1804-1806), making it one of the earliest Pacific Northwest bird species formally described by Euro-American science. The species is the most behaviorally aberrant member of genus *Melanerpes* — its flycatching foraging, non-undulating flight, and reduced excavation behavior represent a dramatic ecological shift from the ancestral woodpecker bauplan. Molecular phylogenetics place it as sister to the Acorn Woodpecker (*M. formicivorus*), another mast-caching *Melanerpes* — suggesting that the transition to aerial insectivory evolved from an already generalized foraging ancestor [O1, P9].

#### Conservation Context

Lewis's Woodpecker is one of the most rapidly declining landbirds in the PNW. The species' dependence on post-fire ponderosa pine and open oak woodland places it in direct conflict with decades of fire suppression policy. Prescribed fire and managed wildfire programs on USFS lands in eastern Oregon and Washington have produced some of the most successful habitat restoration for this species. The Okanogan-Wenatchee National Forest post-fire salvage logging debate is emblematic: salvage logging removes the very snags that Lewis's Woodpeckers colonize within 1-3 years of fire. Studies on the 2012 Wenatchee Complex fire documented Lewis's Woodpecker colonization densities of 3-5 pairs/km2 in unsalvaged burn areas versus 0-0.5 pairs/km2 in salvaged areas [G8, G9].

### White-headed Woodpecker (*Dryobates albolarvatus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Dryobates*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Yellow Watch List

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 21-23 cm (8.3-9.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 40-42 cm (15.7-16.5 in)
- **Mass:** 55-65 g (1.9-2.3 oz)
- **Plumage:** Striking black-and-white pattern unique among North American woodpeckers. Entirely white head contrasting with solid black body; white wing patch visible in flight. Male has small red patch on nape, absent in female. No barring on back or wings — the simplest plumage pattern in the family.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Male has red nape patch; otherwise similar.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open, mature ponderosa pine (*Pinus ponderosa*) forest on the east slope of the Cascades and in the Blue Mountains. Requires large-diameter ponderosa (>50 cm DBH) with open canopy (30-50% closure) and abundant snags. Strongly associated with fire-maintained open pine stands — the "park-like" ponderosa forests described by early Euro-American explorers [G7, G8].
- **Wintering:** Same as breeding; non-migratory. May descend slightly in elevation during severe winters.
- **Elevation range:** 600-2,400 m (2,000-7,900 ft), concentrated 900-1,800 m (3,000-6,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — east-side ponderosa)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill — ponderosa/oak transition)
- **Microhabitat:** Obligate associate of large-diameter ponderosa pine. Bark-flaking foraging technique requires thick, furrowed ponderosa bark. Nest trees typically >50 cm DBH with advanced heartwood decay.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Pine seeds (ponderosa, sugar pine, Jeffrey pine) extracted from cones, plus bark-surface insects (beetles, spiders, ants).
- **Foraging strategy:** Flakes bark from ponderosa trunks and large branches, probing for insects in bark crevices — does not excavate deep into wood like Hairy or Pileated woodpeckers. Extracts pine seeds from cones by wedging cone in bark crevice and prying scales apart. This bark-flaking and seed-extraction foraging mode requires large-diameter ponderosa and is unavailable in young, thin-barked stands.
- **Seasonal variation:** Pine seeds dominate diet in fall-winter when cones mature. Insect prey increases in spring-summer.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cavity excavator in dead ponderosa snags or dead limbs of live trees. Nest height 2-7 m.
- **Clutch size:** 3-7 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 14 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** 26 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining in many areas due to habitat alteration. BBS data insufficient for robust trend (low detection probability, patchy distribution) but local studies document declines correlating with fire suppression [G4, G9].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 130,000 individuals continent-wide (PIF 2020); PNW populations concentrated in eastern OR and WA Cascades [O4].
- **Threats:** Fire suppression converting open ponderosa woodland to dense mixed-conifer (primary threat), logging of large-diameter ponderosa, loss of snag habitat, post-fire salvage logging removing nesting substrate [G8, G9].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane (east-side) | Year-round | Uncommon, local | Indicator of mature ponderosa pine |
| Zone 4: Foothill (ponderosa) | Year-round | Rare | Peripheral populations |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Sharp *peek-peek-peek* — similar to Hairy Woodpecker but slightly higher pitched and faster.
- **Drumming:** Moderate-tempo rolls, ~20 strikes/second, shorter than Hairy Woodpecker.

#### Evolutionary Note

The White-headed Woodpecker is nearly endemic to the interior Pacific Northwest and adjacent mountain ranges. Its range extends from southern British Columbia through eastern WA and OR to the southern Sierra Nevada, tracking the distribution of large-diameter ponderosa pine. No close ecological analogue exists in eastern North American forests. The species' bark-flaking foraging and pine seed extraction represent a specialized niche that became available only after the spread of large-cone pines during the Pliocene. Genetic studies indicate low genetic diversity across the range, consistent with historical population bottlenecks during glacial periods when ponderosa pine habitat contracted [O1].

### Black-backed Woodpecker (*Picoides arcticus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Picoides*
**Status:** Year-round resident (irruptive in response to fire)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (petitioned for listing; USFWS determined not warranted in 2020)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Yellow Watch List

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 23-25 cm (9.1-9.8 in)
- **Wingspan:** 39-42 cm (15.4-16.5 in)
- **Mass:** 61-88 g (2.2-3.1 oz)
- **Plumage:** Solid black back (no white barring — diagnostic), white breast with barred flanks, white moustachial stripe. Male has yellow crown patch, female all black head. Only three toes (most woodpeckers have four) — an adaptation for more powerful bark scaling strokes.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Male has bright yellow crown patch; female lacks crown coloration.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Recently burned conifer forest, 1-8 years post-fire. This is the quintessential post-fire specialist — population densities peak in burned stands harboring beetle irruptions (particularly wood-boring beetles, Cerambycidae and Buprestidae) that colonize fire-killed trees. Also occurs at lower density in unburned forest with bark beetle outbreaks and in mature/old-growth forest with high snag density [G8].
- **Wintering:** Same as breeding in most years; may move to adjacent unburned forest with bark beetle outbreaks during winters without recent fire.
- **Elevation range:** 600-2,700 m (2,000-8,900 ft); concentrated at mid-elevation montane (2,500-6,000 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — post-fire)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Subalpine — post-fire), Zone 4 (Foothill ponderosa — post-fire)
- **Microhabitat:** Recently killed conifer snags with tight bark harboring wood-boring beetle larvae. Optimal habitat is 2-5 years post-fire, when beetle larval density peaks. Snag density >100 snags/hectare of >23 cm DBH supports highest breeding densities.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Wood-boring beetle larvae (Cerambycidae — longhorn beetles, Buprestidae — jewel beetles) constitute 75-95% of diet. These larvae colonize fire-killed and bark beetle-killed trees, growing to 3-5 cm length within the wood.
- **Foraging strategy:** Systematic bark scaling — strips bark from fire-killed trees starting at the base and working upward, flaking off plates of bark to expose beetle galleries underneath. Three-toed foot provides superior leverage for these powerful bark-stripping strokes compared to four-toed woodpeckers. Does not deeply excavate into sound wood.
- **Seasonal variation:** Diet consistently dominated by beetle larvae year-round in post-fire habitat.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Cavity excavator in fire-killed snags, typically 2-5 m above ground. Prefers snags with partially decomposed wood for easier excavation.
- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs
- **Incubation:** 12-14 days; both sexes
- **Fledging:** 24-25 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** May-July in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Difficult to assess — populations are inherently nomadic, tracking fire across the landscape. Local populations boom 1-3 years after fire, then decline as beetle larvae are consumed and snags decay. Rangewide trend considered stable if adequate fire occurs, but fire suppression reduces available habitat [G4, G8].
- **Estimate:** 1.2 million continent-wide (PIF 2020); highly concentrated in recently burned areas [O4].
- **Threats:** Post-fire salvage logging (primary threat — removes the exact snag habitat the species requires), fire suppression reducing frequency and extent of fire, short rotation forestry producing trees too small for optimal beetle colonization [G8].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer (post-fire) | Year-round | Common locally after fire | Indicator of post-fire ecosystem health |
| Zone 1: Subalpine (post-fire) | Year-round | Uncommon | High-elevation post-fire specialist |
| Zone 2: Montane (unburned, beetle outbreak) | Year-round | Rare | Background population in beetle-kill |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Sharp, single *pik* — lower-pitched than Hairy Woodpecker.
- **Drumming:** Fast, accelerating rolls averaging 15-18 strikes/second, often on resonant fire-killed snags.

#### Evolutionary Note

The Black-backed Woodpecker is one of only two three-toed woodpeckers in North America (the other being the American Three-toed Woodpecker, *P. dorsalis*). The loss of the fourth toe is an independently evolved adaptation in both species (also occurring in the Eurasian Three-toed Woodpecker, *P. tridactylus*) that biomechanically enhances bark-scaling power by concentrating force through fewer but stronger digits. The species' dependence on post-fire habitat creates a "nomadic specialist" life history strategy that is unique among PNW woodpeckers — populations are transient at any given site but persistent at the landscape scale as long as fire continues to create habitat. This life history strategy is effectively impossible to conserve through site-based protection; it requires landscape-level fire management [P9, O1].

### Downy Woodpecker (*Dryobates pubescens*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Dryobates*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 14-18 cm (5.5-7.1 in) — smallest PNW woodpecker
- **Wingspan:** 25-31 cm (9.8-12.2 in)
- **Mass:** 21-28 g (0.7-1.0 oz)
- **Plumage:** Black-and-white striped head, white back stripe, black wings with white spotting, white underparts. Male has small red nape patch. Nearly identical to Hairy Woodpecker in pattern but ~1/3 the size, with a proportionally shorter, stubbier bill (bill length approximately equal to head depth — diagnostic vs. Hairy's longer bill).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Male has red nape patch; female nape is black.

**Habitat:** Deciduous and mixed forest, riparian woodland, suburban areas with mature trees, orchards. More tolerant of small trees and fragmented habitat than larger woodpeckers. Found across all forested ecoregions at lower elevations.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,500 m (8,200 ft), most common below 1,500 m
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane), Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban — common), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian — common)

**Diet and Foraging:** Small insects (beetles, ants, caterpillars) gleaned from bark surfaces and small branches. Forages on smaller-diameter branches and weed stems that larger woodpeckers cannot access — a classic niche partitioning example. Also eats seeds, berries, suet at feeders.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator in dead wood; clutch 3-8 (typically 4-5); incubation 12 days; fledging 18-21 days; 1 brood. Breeding May-July.

**Population:** Stable — BBS trend +0.2% per year in PNW (2006-2019) [G4]. Estimated 13 million continent-wide [O4]. Adapts well to suburban environments.

**Vocalizations:** High-pitched *pik* call; descending whinny *ki-ki-ki-ki-ki*; drumming fast and even, ~15 strikes/second, softer than Hairy.

**Evolutionary note:** The Downy Woodpecker is not closely related to the Hairy Woodpecker despite their near-identical plumage patterns. Molecular phylogenetics place them in separate clades within *Dryobates*, indicating convergent plumage evolution — a textbook case of interspecific social dominance mimicry (the smaller Downy may benefit from resembling the larger, dominant Hairy) [O1].

### Hairy Woodpecker (*Dryobates villosus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Dryobates*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 18-26 cm (7.1-10.2 in)
- **Wingspan:** 33-43 cm (13-17 in)
- **Mass:** 40-95 g (1.4-3.4 oz) — substantial size range across subspecies; PNW birds (*D. v. harrisi* on west side, *D. v. orius* on east side) are among the larger races
- **Plumage:** Identical pattern to Downy Woodpecker — black-and-white striped head, white back stripe, black wings with white spotting, white underparts. Male has red nape patch. Distinguished from Downy by larger size, proportionally longer bill (bill length noticeably exceeds head depth), clean white outer tail feathers (no black barring), and louder, sharper vocalizations.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Male has red nape patch.

**Habitat:** Mature conifer and mixed forest with moderate to high snag density. More interior-forest dependent than Downy Woodpecker. Found in old-growth, mature second-growth, and large riparian corridors. Avoids heavily fragmented habitat.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~3,000 m (9,800 ft) — broader elevational range than Downy
**Ecoregions:** Zone 1 (Subalpine — uncommon), Zone 2 (Montane — common), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)

**Diet and Foraging:** Wood-boring beetle larvae, bark beetles, ants excavated from live and dead trees. Forages on larger-diameter trunks and branches than Downy. More reliant on excavation (drilling into wood) than bark gleaning.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator in dead wood; clutch 3-6 (typically 4); incubation 11-12 days; fledging 28-30 days; 1 brood. Breeding April-July.

**Population:** Stable — BBS trend -0.2% per year in PNW (not significant) [G4]. Estimated 9 million continent-wide [O4]. Less common in suburban areas than Downy.

**Vocalizations:** Sharp, explosive *peek!* — louder and sharper than Downy's *pik*. Rattle call is steady-pitched (Downy's descends). Drumming faster and louder than Downy, ~25 strikes/second.

**Evolutionary note:** Hairy Woodpecker shows extraordinary geographic variation across North America — 12-17 recognized subspecies varying in size, plumage tone, and bill dimensions. PNW west-side birds (*D. v. harrisi*) are among the darkest and largest races, consistent with Gloger's Rule (darker pigmentation in humid environments). East-side PNW birds (*D. v. orius*) are paler and slightly smaller [O1, P16].

### Red-breasted Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus ruber*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Sphyrapicus*
**Status:** Year-round resident (some altitudinal migration)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 20-22 cm (7.9-8.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 37-41 cm (14.6-16.1 in)
- **Mass:** 49-63 g (1.7-2.2 oz)
- **Plumage:** Entirely red head and breast (both sexes) — the only PNW woodpecker with an all-red head. Black back with white rump patch and white wing bars. Two subspecies in PNW: northern *S. r. ruber* (brighter red, less black on breast, west-side WA/BC) and southern *S. r. daggetti* (duller red, more black mottling, OR/southern WA). Hybridizes with Red-naped Sapsucker along the east slope of the Cascades.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — both sexes red-headed. Males may average slightly brighter.

**Habitat:** Conifer and mixed forest, particularly with deciduous component (red alder, bigleaf maple, cottonwood) for sap drilling. Prefers forest edges, openings, and riparian corridors.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,000 m (6,500 ft); descends in winter
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — breeding), Zone 3 (Lowland — year-round), Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian — important)

**Diet and Foraging:** Drills orderly rows of small holes ("sap wells") in tree bark, returning to drink sap and eat insects attracted to sap flow. Preferred sap trees include red alder, birch, hemlock, and willow. Sap wells also attract hummingbirds, warblers, and other species — the sapsucker is a facilitative ecosystem engineer. Also eats insects, fruits, and cambium.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator in dead or decaying trees, often in deciduous snags; clutch 4-7; incubation 12-13 days; fledging 25-29 days; 1 brood. Breeding April-June.

**Population:** Stable — BBS trend +0.1% per year in PNW [G4]. Common throughout forested west-side lowlands and foothills.

**Vocalizations:** Mewing *wee-ah* call, cat-like. Irregular drumming with characteristic stutter pattern: rapid initial burst, then irregular slow taps — diagnostic for sapsuckers.

**Evolutionary note:** The four *Sphyrapicus* sapsuckers (Red-breasted, Red-naped, Yellow-bellied, Williamson's) form a North American radiation. Red-breasted and Red-naped were formerly considered conspecific. Their contact zone along the east Cascade slope produces extensive hybrids showing intermediate head patterns (red head with red-naped's black-bordered throat patch). This hybrid zone has been stable for at least 50 years, suggesting reproductive isolation is incomplete but maintained by habitat-mediated assortative mating [O1, P3].

### Red-naped Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus nuchalis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Sphyrapicus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates south in winter)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 19-21 cm (7.5-8.3 in)
- **Wingspan:** 37-40 cm (14.6-15.7 in)
- **Mass:** 46-60 g (1.6-2.1 oz)
- **Plumage:** Red crown, red nape patch (diagnostic), red throat bordered by black; black-and-white face pattern; barred black-and-white back. Female has white or partially white throat.

**Habitat:** Deciduous and mixed forest on east side of Cascades: aspen groves, cottonwood riparian, mixed conifer-aspen. Requires deciduous trees for sap wells and nesting. Replaces Red-breasted Sapsucker on the east side.
**Elevation range:** 600-2,500 m (2,000-8,200 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — east-side), Zone 5 (Riparian — east-side cottonwood/aspen), Zone 7 (Steppe riparian)

**Diet and Foraging:** Sap wells in aspen, birch, willow; insects; fruit. Same facilitative sap-well ecology as Red-breasted Sapsucker on the east side.

**Population:** Stable in PNW range [G4]. Common in east-side aspen and riparian habitats.

**Vocalizations:** Similar to Red-breasted — mewing *wee-ah* and stuttered drumming.

**Evolutionary note:** Split from Red-breasted Sapsucker in 1983 by AOS. The hybrid zone between these two species on the east Cascade slope is one of the best-studied woodpecker contact zones in North America [O1, O3].

### Williamson's Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus thyroideus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Sphyrapicus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates south in winter; rare winter resident in southern OR)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | State WA: Candidate species | PIF: Yellow Watch List

#### Profile

- **Length:** 21-25 cm (8.3-9.8 in)
- **Wingspan:** 40-44 cm (15.7-17.3 in)
- **Mass:** 44-55 g (1.6-1.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Extreme sexual dimorphism — the most dimorphic woodpecker in North America. Male: black head, white facial stripes, large white wing patch, red throat, yellow belly, black breast band. Female: brown barred overall with brown head and no red or black — so different from male that the two sexes were originally described as separate species (1852). Juveniles resemble adult females.

**Habitat:** Montane conifer forest on east side of Cascades: ponderosa pine, western larch, Douglas-fir. Prefers open to moderately dense stands at mid-elevation with adequate snag density. East-side specialist; rare on west side.
**Elevation range:** 900-2,700 m (3,000-8,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — east-side, primary), Zone 1 (Subalpine — upper breeding range)

**Diet and Foraging:** Drills sap wells in conifer bark (unlike other sapsuckers that prefer deciduous trees); also eats ants, beetles, cambium. Sap wells typically in Douglas-fir and larch.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator, prefers western larch snags; clutch 3-7; incubation 12-14 days; fledging 31-35 days; 1 brood. Breeding May-July.

**Population:** Declining in portions of range. Washington populations fragmented. BBS data suggest -1.8% per year rangewide (1966-2019) [G4]. Threatened by loss of large-diameter larch and ponderosa snags [G9].

**Vocalizations:** Nasal *chee-yer* call; stuttered sapsucker drumming pattern.

**Evolutionary note:** The extreme sexual dimorphism in Williamson's Sapsucker — male and female looking like entirely different species — delayed taxonomic recognition. John Cassin described the female as a separate species ("Thyroid Woodpecker") in 1852. The two "species" were not recognized as male and female of the same species until Henry Henshaw collected a breeding pair from the same nest in 1873. This remains one of the most dramatic examples of delayed species recognition due to sexual dimorphism in ornithological history [O1].

### Northern Flicker (*Colaptes auratus* — Red-shafted subspecies group)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Colaptes*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial altitudinal migrant)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern (but declining)

#### Profile

- **Length:** 28-36 cm (11-14 in)
- **Wingspan:** 42-54 cm (16.5-21.3 in)
- **Mass:** 86-167 g (3.0-5.9 oz) — second-largest PNW woodpecker after Pileated
- **Plumage (Red-shafted form):** Brown barred back, spotted underparts with black crescent breast band, salmon-red under wings and tail shafts (visible in flight — diagnostic). Gray face with brown crown, red moustachial stripe in male (absent in female). Rump white, conspicuous in undulating flight.

**Habitat:** Open woodlands, forest edges, suburban areas, parks, agricultural land with scattered trees. The most terrestrial PNW woodpecker — forages extensively on the ground. Extremely broad habitat tolerance.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~3,000 m (9,800 ft)
**Ecoregions:** All zones except Zone 6 (Marine) — the most broadly distributed PNW woodpecker

**Diet and Foraging:** Primarily ants and beetles taken from ground — hops on lawns and open ground, probing soil with its long, barbed tongue (extending 5+ cm beyond bill). The most terrestrial foraging mode of any PNW woodpecker. Also takes berries, seeds, and other insects.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator in dead trees, power poles, and buildings; also uses nest boxes; clutch 3-12 (typically 5-8); incubation 11-13 days; fledging 25-28 days; 1-2 broods. Breeding April-July. Among the most important cavity creators for secondary cavity nesters in open habitats.

**Population:** Declining — BBS trend -1.2% per year in PNW (1966-2019) [G4]. Still abundant (estimated 12 million continent-wide) but long-term decline concerning [O4]. Competition with European Starling for cavities is a documented threat.

**Vocalizations:** Loud *wick-wick-wick-wick* series (territorial); single loud *kyeer*; long, even-tempo drumming rolls on metal surfaces (utility poles, gutters) — characteristic suburban sound.

**Evolutionary note:** The Northern Flicker subspecies complex is one of the most studied avian hybrid zones in North America. Red-shafted (western) and Yellow-shafted (eastern) forms were formerly treated as separate species. They hybridize extensively across a broad zone along the Great Plains. In the PNW, pure Red-shafted birds predominate, but Yellow-shafted traits (yellow wing/tail shafts, red nape crescent, black moustachial stripe in male) appear at low frequency, especially in eastern WA/ID near the hybrid zone's western margin. Genomic studies (Aguillon et al. 2018) show remarkably low genetic divergence between the two forms despite dramatic plumage differences — indicating that plumage divergence is driven by strong selection on relatively few pigmentation genes [P3, O1].

### American Three-toed Woodpecker (*Picoides dorsalis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Picoides*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 21-23 cm (8.3-9.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 37-39 cm (14.6-15.4 in)
- **Mass:** 44-65 g (1.6-2.3 oz)
- **Plumage:** Black back with variable white barring (distinguishes from all-black-backed Black-backed Woodpecker), barred flanks, white underparts. Male has yellow crown patch. Three toes (like Black-backed Woodpecker).

**Habitat:** Boreal and subalpine conifer forest, particularly spruce-fir stands with bark beetle outbreaks. Less tied to post-fire habitat than Black-backed Woodpecker, though responds to fire. Prefers dense, mature subalpine forest with high snag density.
**Elevation range:** 900-2,700 m (3,000-8,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 1 (Subalpine — uncommon), Zone 2 (Montane — uncommon, higher elevations)

**Diet and Foraging:** Bark beetles (Scolytinae) and wood-boring beetles excavated from bark and outer wood of spruce, fir, and larch. Bark-flaking technique similar to Black-backed but on smaller-diameter trees and branches.

**Reproduction:** Cavity excavator in dead conifers; clutch 3-4; incubation 12-14 days; fledging 22-26 days; 1 brood. Breeding May-July.

**Population:** Stable but naturally uncommon. Low-density populations across boreal/subalpine range [G4, O4].

**Vocalizations:** Soft *pik* call; drumming similar to Black-backed but slightly higher pitched.

**Evolutionary note:** Split from the Eurasian Three-toed Woodpecker (*P. tridactylus*) by AOS in 2003 based on molecular and vocal differences. The three-toed condition evolved independently in the *Picoides* lineage and in unrelated three-toed woodpeckers (African genus *Sasia*), representing convergent biomechanical adaptation for bark scaling [O1, O3].

### Acorn Woodpecker (*Melanerpes formicivorus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Piciformes, Family Picidae, Genus *Melanerpes*
**Status:** Year-round resident (limited PNW range)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 21-23 cm (8.3-9.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 35-43 cm (13.8-16.9 in)
- **Mass:** 65-90 g (2.3-3.2 oz)
- **Plumage:** Striking clown-like face pattern: white forehead, red crown, black chin, white eyes (yellowish iris), black back and wings. White wing patches conspicuous in flight.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Male has red crown touching white forehead; female has black band separating red crown from white forehead.

**Habitat:** Oak woodland — nearly obligate associate of acorn-producing oaks. In PNW, restricted to Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) stands in southern Oregon (Rogue Valley, Umpqua Valley) and barely entering southwestern Washington. At northern range limit in PNW.
**Elevation range:** 200-1,500 m (650-4,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — primary, southern OR only)

**Diet and Foraging:** Acorns (primary — stored in granary trees), insects caught on the wing, sap, fruit. Maintains communal "granary trees" — single snags or large live trees drilled with thousands of individually sized holes, each holding one acorn. A single granary may contain 10,000-50,000 holes accumulated over decades. Colony members defend granary against jays and squirrels.

**Reproduction:** Cooperative breeder — groups of 2-15 birds (helpers at the nest) share a single breeding cavity. Complex social system with breeding coalitions (up to 3 males, 2 females per nest). Clutch 3-7; incubation 11-12 days; fledging 30-32 days.

**Population:** Stable in Oregon range. At northern range limit in PNW — restricted to oak habitats that are themselves declining [O4, G12].

**Vocalizations:** Raucous *waka-waka-waka* and *ja-cob, ja-cob* calls; colony members vocalize constantly. Among the most vocal woodpeckers.

**Evolutionary note:** The Acorn Woodpecker's cooperative breeding system — one of the most complex in birds — has been studied continuously at Hastings Reservation (California) since 1968. Cooperative breeding evolved in this lineage in association with the spatially and temporally variable acorn crop, where group defense of stored food provides a selective advantage. Sister species to Lewis's Woodpecker, suggesting ancestral *Melanerpes* were already pre-adapted for mast exploitation before the two species diverged into distinct foraging strategies [O1].

## Order Falconiformes — Falcons

### Family Falconidae — Falcons and Caracaras

Falcons are aerial predators distinguished from hawks and eagles (Accipitriformes) by their convergent morphology rather than common ancestry — molecular phylogenetics revealed in the early 2000s that falcons are more closely related to parrots and passerines than to hawks. This dramatic reclassification repositioned Falconiformes far from Accipitriformes in the AOS taxonomic sequence. Falcons kill prey with a tomial tooth (a notch on the upper bill) that severs the cervical vertebrae — a killing mechanism unique to the family and convergently evolved in shrikes (Laniidae) [O1, O3].

PNW falcons range from the cosmopolitan Peregrine Falcon — the fastest animal on Earth — to the declining American Kestrel, whose population collapse mirrors that of Lewis's Woodpecker in its association with open habitat loss. The family is well-represented across all PNW ecoregion zones, from coastal cliffs to alpine peaks to agricultural steppe.

### Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Falconiformes, Family Falconidae, Genus *Falco*
**Status:** Year-round resident (coastal) to partial migrant (inland)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Delisted (2009) — recovered. Formerly Endangered (listed 1970, delisted 1999 in US)
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Sensitive
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Low concern (recovered population)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 36-49 cm (14.2-19.3 in); females 15-20% larger than males
- **Wingspan:** 100-120 cm (39.4-47.2 in)
- **Mass:** Males 550-720 g (1.2-1.6 lb); Females 720-1,100 g (1.6-2.4 lb) — among the most size-dimorphic of North American raptors
- **Plumage:** Dark blue-gray upperparts, pale barred underparts, diagnostic dark "helmet" (malar stripe extending below eye, giving hooded appearance). PNW subspecies *F. p. pealei* (coastal, resident) is darker overall with heavier barring than interior *F. p. anatum*. Juvenile brown above with streaked (not barred) underparts.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Females substantially larger (reverse sexual size dimorphism, typical of raptors). Plumage similar between sexes.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Cliff ledges with open aerial foraging space — coastal sea cliffs, river gorge cliffs (Columbia River Gorge), building ledges (urban — Portland, Seattle, Boise), bridge structures. Pealei's subspecies breeds exclusively on coastal islands and sea stacks (San Juan Islands, outer coast rocks). Interior *anatum* subspecies uses inland cliff sites at variable elevation.
- **Wintering:** Coastal areas with dense shorebird and waterfowl concentrations (primary winter habitat); urban areas with pigeon concentrations; river corridors. Pealei's subspecies largely resident on coast year-round. Interior birds move to coast or lower elevations.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,500 m (8,200 ft) breeding; primarily below 500 m in winter
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 6 (Coastal/Marine — pealei), Zone 5 (Riparian — cliff-nesting anatum)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban — building-nesting pairs), Zone 7 (East-Side — cliff-nesting)
- **Microhabitat:** Requires vertical cliff or structure >10 m tall with ledge or scrape for nest, and open aerial hunting space within 5-10 km. Avoids forested landscapes without clearings.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Birds — almost exclusively avian prey, taken in aerial pursuit. Diet varies by population: coastal pealei takes alcids (Ancient Murrelet, Cassin's Auklet), storm-petrels, and shorebirds; interior anatum takes pigeons, doves, jays, shorebirds, waterfowl, and passerines.
- **Foraging strategy:** High-speed aerial pursuit and stoop. The stoop (power dive from height) is the iconic Peregrine hunting technique — the falcon gains altitude above prey, then folds its wings and dives at speeds documented up to 389 km/h (242 mph), striking prey with clenched talons. Kills dispatched with tomial tooth bite to cervical vertebrae. Also pursues prey in level flight and from concealed perch ambush at cliff edges.
- **Seasonal variation:** Coastal populations prey heavily on migrant shorebirds (spring and fall) and alcids (summer). Urban populations take Rock Pigeons year-round.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Scrape on cliff ledge — no nest construction. Also uses building ledges, bridge I-beams, and (historically) large tree hollows in old-growth (rare). Will use hack boxes placed for restoration.
- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs (typically 3)
- **Incubation:** 33-35 days; female primary incubator, male provides food
- **Fledging:** 35-42 days; juveniles dependent on adults for 4-6 weeks post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** March-June in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Recovered — one of the great conservation success stories. PNW populations crashed to near-zero by the 1960s due to DDT-induced eggshell thinning. Following DDT ban (1972), captive breeding, and hacking programs, populations recovered. Washington now supports ~70-80 known breeding territories; Oregon ~60-70; BC >100. Numbers still below historical levels but stable and increasing [G1, G9].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 140,000 individuals in North America (post-recovery). PNW populations among the healthiest, particularly coastal pealei [O4, G1].
- **Threats (current):** Human disturbance at nest sites (climbing, photography), continued low-level organochlorine contamination in marine food webs, collision with buildings and vehicles (urban populations), occasional persecution, climate-driven shifts in prey availability [G1, G9].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 6: Coastal/Marine | Year-round | Uncommon (pealei resident) | Apex aerial predator of seabird communities |
| Zone 5: Riparian (cliffs) | Breeding | Uncommon | Cliff-nesting predator along river gorges |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Rare | Building-nesting urban raptor |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Breeding | Rare | Cliff-nesting interior populations |

#### Vocalizations

- **Alarm call:** Rapid, harsh *kak-kak-kak-kak* — the "cacking" call given when intruders approach nest cliff. Audible at great distance.
- **Wail:** Drawn-out *wee-chew* or *ee-chip* — courtship and food-transfer call.
- **Juveniles:** Persistent begging calls during dependency period.

#### Evolutionary Note

The Peregrine Falcon has the most cosmopolitan distribution of any raptor — breeding on every continent except Antarctica. This global range, combined with 19 recognized subspecies, represents one of the most successful raptor radiations. PNW populations include two subspecies: the dark coastal *F. p. pealei* (one of the largest and darkest races globally, resident on outer coast islands) and the interior *F. p. anatum* (continental form, partially migratory). Pealei was less affected by DDT than anatum because its marine food web accumulated lower pesticide concentrations than the terrestrial food web. The species' phylogenetic placement near parrots and passerines (not hawks) was one of the most surprising results of avian molecular systematics — the falcon body plan, with its pointed wings, tomial tooth, and aerial hunting lifestyle, evolved convergently with hawks rather than from a shared raptor ancestor [O1, O3].

#### ESA Recovery Story

The Peregrine Falcon's near-extinction and subsequent recovery represents the definitive case study of pesticide-driven wildlife decline and regulatory recovery. DDT, used extensively after World War II, bioaccumulated through food chains. Peregrines, as apex predators, concentrated DDE (DDT metabolite) to levels that interfered with calcium deposition in eggshells. Shell thickness declined by 15-25%, causing eggs to break under incubating adults. By 1965, fewer than 40 breeding pairs remained in the contiguous United States; the Pacific Northwest had zero confirmed successful nests. Following the 1972 DDT ban, the Peregrine Fund's captive breeding program released >6,000 falcons nationwide between 1974 and 1999. In the PNW, hack sites on coastal cliffs and Columbia Gorge cliffs reestablished breeding populations by the 1980s. The species was delisted in 1999 (US) and 2009 (removed from post-delisting monitoring). PNW populations continue to increase, with new territory establishment in urban areas (Portland, Seattle, Tacoma, Spokane) representing a novel habitat expansion [G1, G9].

### American Kestrel (*Falco sparverius*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Falconiformes, Family Falconidae, Genus *Falco*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant — northern breeders move south)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (but declining)
- State OR: Not listed (but declining)
- PIF: Common Birds in Steep Decline

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 22-31 cm (8.7-12.2 in)
- **Wingspan:** 51-61 cm (20.1-24.0 in)
- **Mass:** Males 80-105 g (2.8-3.7 oz); Females 100-150 g (3.5-5.3 oz) — smallest North American falcon
- **Plumage:** Among the most colorful North American raptors. Male: blue-gray wings, rufous back with black barring, rufous tail with black subterminal band and white tip, pale underparts with variable spotting, bold black-and-white face pattern with two vertical dark stripes. Female: rufous wings, back, and tail with dark barring throughout; streaked underparts; same facial pattern as male but browner.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Pronounced — male has blue-gray wings; female is entirely rufous and barred. Males smaller than females.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open country with elevated perches and cavity nest sites: agricultural land, grassland, steppe, oak savanna, forest edges with open ground, suburban areas with nest boxes. Requires cavity for nesting (secondary cavity nester — uses woodpecker holes, natural cavities, nest boxes, building crevices).
- **Wintering:** Same habitat types; partial withdrawal from highest-elevation and northernmost breeding sites.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,500 m (8,200 ft)
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland — agricultural and suburban), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Microhabitat:** Requires elevated perch (fence post, wire, tree snag) with 360-degree view of open ground for hover-and-drop hunting. Avoids dense forest.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Insects (grasshoppers, crickets, beetles — 60-80% of diet by frequency in summer) and small mammals (voles, mice — dominant prey in winter). Also small birds, lizards, and occasional small snakes.
- **Foraging strategy:** Hunts from elevated perch (sit-and-wait), scanning open ground below and dropping on prey. Also hovers (kites) at 10-30 m height over open fields — one of the few North American raptors that regularly hovers (using wind or sustained wingbeats). Strikes prey on the ground with talons.
- **Seasonal variation:** Insect-dominated diet in summer shifts to small mammal-dominated diet in winter as insect availability declines.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Secondary cavity nester — does not excavate. Uses natural tree cavities, abandoned Northern Flicker or Pileated Woodpecker holes, nest boxes, cliff crevices, building cavities. Nest boxes have been critical for monitoring and conservation.
- **Clutch size:** 4-6 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 29-31 days; female primary incubator
- **Fledging:** 28-31 days
- **Broods per year:** 1 (rarely 2 in southern range)
- **Breeding season:** April-July in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -1.4% per year rangewide (1966-2019), with steeper declines in PNW (-2.1% per year). An estimated 50% decline since 1970 continent-wide. This decline is alarming for what was historically one of North America's most abundant raptors [G4, O4].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 9.2 million continent-wide (PIF 2020 — down from estimated 18 million in 1970) [O4].
- **Threats:** Loss of open habitat (grassland conversion, agricultural intensification), cavity loss (snag removal, competition with European Starling), second-generation anticoagulant rodenticides (consumed via poisoned rodent prey), pesticide reduction of insect prey base, climate-driven changes in vole populations [G4, O4].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Year-round | Common (declining) | Primary small raptor of open country |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common (declining) | Open woodland raptor |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Uncommon (declining) | Agricultural/suburban raptor |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Uncommon | Open riparian corridor predator |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Rapid, high-pitched *killy-killy-killy* — given in alarm and territorial display. Distinctive and far-carrying.
- **Courtship:** Slower, more deliberate *klee-klee-klee* during display flights.

#### Evolutionary Note

The American Kestrel is phylogenetically isolated within *Falco* — molecular data place it as an early-diverging lineage with no close living relatives. It is the only New World representative of the "kestrel" body plan (small, hovering falcon), which evolved convergently in the Old World Common Kestrel (*F. tinnunculus*) and its relatives. Despite the name and ecological similarity, American Kestrel is not closely related to Eurasian kestrels. The species shows one of the most striking examples of sexual dichromatism in raptors — the male's blue-gray wing and the female's rufous wing may have evolved through sexual selection, as females choose mates partly based on wing patch brightness [O1].

#### Decline Context

The American Kestrel decline is one of the most concerning raptor population trends in North America. Unlike most raptors, kestrels responded positively to agricultural expansion in the 19th and early 20th centuries (open habitat and prey-rich farmland). The current decline reverses that trend. Multiple hypotheses are under investigation: (1) second-generation anticoagulant rodenticides (brodifacoum, bromadiolone) accumulating in prey items at sublethal but reproductively impairing doses; (2) Cooper's Hawk population recovery (following DDT era) increasing predation on kestrels; (3) loss of nesting cavities as rural buildings are demolished and snags removed; (4) neonicotinoid insecticides reducing the insect prey base. Nest box programs across the PNW provide both nesting sites and monitoring data — the American Kestrel Partnership coordinates continent-wide nest box monitoring [O4, G4].

### Merlin (*Falco columbarius*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Falconiformes, Family Falconidae, Genus *Falco*
**Status:** Year-round resident (some populations) to winter visitor (interior breeders)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 24-33 cm (9.4-13.0 in)
- **Wingspan:** 53-68 cm (20.9-26.8 in)
- **Mass:** Males 125-187 g; Females 168-240 g
- **Plumage:** Small, compact falcon. Male blue-gray above (Pacific NW race *F. c. suckleyi* — very dark, nearly blackish above), streaked underparts, indistinct facial pattern (no bold malar stripe like Peregrine). Female and juvenile brown above with heavy streaking below. Suckleyi race is the darkest Merlin subspecies globally.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males smaller and bluer/darker above; females larger and browner.

**Habitat:** Forest edges, open woodland, coastal areas, urban parks. Breeds in conifer forest (often using old crow or hawk nests) near open areas for hunting. Winters in lowland areas with dense passerine populations. Increasingly urban in winter — follows flocks of House Sparrows, European Starlings, and waxwings into cities.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,800 m (5,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — breeding), Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban — wintering), Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 6 (Coastal — wintering)

**Diet and Foraging:** Primarily small birds caught in explosive low-altitude pursuit. Diet includes House Sparrows, waxwings, shorebirds, and songbirds. Also dragonflies (important prey in late summer). Hunting style is fast, low-level attack from concealment — rarely stoops from height like Peregrine.

**Reproduction:** Does not build nest — uses abandoned crow, magpie, or hawk nests in conifers; occasionally cavity. Clutch 4-6; incubation 28-32 days; fledging 25-35 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Increasing — BBS trend +2.1% per year rangewide (1966-2019) [G4]. Urban expansion and adaptation to suburban environments driving population growth. Once rare in PNW lowlands, now regular in winter in Seattle, Portland, and other cities.

**Vocalizations:** Rapid, shrill *ki-ki-ki-ki* — higher-pitched and more rapid than Peregrine or Kestrel.

**Evolutionary note:** Three Merlin subspecies occur in the PNW: *F. c. suckleyi* (dark Pacific coastal resident), *F. c. columbarius* (taiga race — winter visitor from boreal Canada), and *F. c. richardsonii* (prairie race — rare winter visitor from Great Plains). The suckleyi race, darker than any other Merlin population globally, conforms to Gloger's Rule and was formerly considered a separate species ("Black Pigeon Hawk"). AOS maintains all three as subspecies [O1, O3].

### Prairie Falcon (*Falco mexicanus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Falconiformes, Family Falconidae, Genus *Falco*
**Status:** Year-round resident (east side); rare winter visitor (west side)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 37-47 cm (14.6-18.5 in)
- **Wingspan:** 90-113 cm (35.4-44.5 in)
- **Mass:** Males 420-635 g; Females 720-975 g
- **Plumage:** Pale sandy-brown above, white below with fine spotting, thin dark moustachial stripe, dark axillaries (diagnostic — dark "wing pits" visible in flight, distinguishing from all other PNW falcons). Paler overall than Peregrine with narrower moustache.

**Habitat:** Arid open country with cliff nest sites — canyon walls, rimrock, coulees. The falcon of the Interior West. In PNW, restricted to east-side steppe and canyon lands: Snake River Canyon, Columbia Basin rimrock, Malheur Basin, John Day Fossil Beds area. Avoids forested landscapes.
**Elevation range:** 200-2,500 m (650-8,200 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — primary), Zone 1 (Alpine — cliff nesting at higher elevations)

**Diet and Foraging:** Primarily ground squirrels (Townsend's ground squirrel, Belding's ground squirrel) during breeding season; shifts to birds (Horned Lark, meadowlarks, starlings) when ground squirrels are dormant. Low-level pursuit hunter — less inclined to stoop than Peregrine, more likely to chase prey in level or ascending flight near the ground.

**Reproduction:** Scrape on cliff ledge or in pothole/cave on cliff face. Does not build nest. Clutch 3-6 (typically 4-5); incubation 29-33 days; fledging 35-42 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable in PNW range [G4]. Population tied to ground squirrel availability, which can fluctuate dramatically. Snake River Birds of Prey National Conservation Area (Idaho) supports one of the densest Prairie Falcon breeding populations in North America.

**Vocalizations:** Harsh *kik-kik-kik* — similar to Peregrine but thinner and higher-pitched. Less vocal overall than Peregrine.

**Evolutionary note:** Prairie Falcon is endemic to western North America — one of only two falcon species restricted entirely to the New World (the other being the South American Aplomado Falcon complex). Molecular studies confirm it as a relatively early divergence within *Falco*, not closely related to the morphologically similar Peregrine despite their co-occurrence. The species' ground squirrel specialization during breeding and bird-hunting in winter represents a dramatic seasonal dietary shift driven by prey availability [O1].

### Gyrfalcon (*Falco rusticolus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Falconiformes, Family Falconidae, Genus *Falco*
**Status:** Rare winter visitor
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Not assessed (non-breeding in PNW)

#### Profile

- **Length:** 48-65 cm (18.9-25.6 in) — largest falcon in the world
- **Wingspan:** 110-130 cm (43.3-51.2 in)
- **Mass:** Males 805-1,350 g; Females 1,000-2,100 g
- **Plumage:** Highly variable — three color morphs (white, gray, dark). Most PNW records involve gray morph birds. Larger and heavier than Peregrine with broader wings and longer tail, giving a more buteo-like flight profile. Faint moustachial stripe (less defined than Peregrine).

**Habitat:** Breeds in Arctic tundra (not in PNW). Winter records in PNW are typically open coastal areas with waterfowl concentrations, large estuaries, agricultural lowlands, and occasionally east-side steppe. Extremely rare — 1-5 records per winter in Washington, fewer in Oregon.
**Ecoregions:** Zone 6 (Coastal — rare winter), Zone 7 (East-Side — rare winter)

**Diet and Foraging:** In PNW winter range, takes waterfowl (ducks, geese), shorebirds, and ptarmigan. Powerful level-pursuit hunter — less reliant on stooping than Peregrine, more likely to chase prey in sustained level flight using raw power.

**Population:** Breeds in Arctic; not a PNW breeding species. Winter occurrences variable and unpredictable — possibly fewer than 10 individuals winter in the entire PNW in most years. Status stable rangewide [O1].

**Vocalizations:** Rarely vocal in PNW winter range. Breeding calls similar to Peregrine but deeper.

**Evolutionary note:** Largest living falcon. Arctic-breeding specialist whose occasional winter incursions south into the PNW represent the southern fringe of a predominantly High Arctic range. The Gyrfalcon is the sister species of the Saker Falcon (*F. cherrug*) of Eurasia, forming a lineage of large, powerful falcons adapted to open terrain and sustained-pursuit hunting. Color morph variation (white to dark) is controlled by relatively few melanocortin receptor genes and varies clinally with latitude — white morphs predominate at highest latitudes [O1].

## Order Passeriformes — Perching Birds (Tyrannidae through Corvidae)

Passeriformes is the largest avian order, containing over 60% of all bird species. This module covers four early-diverging Passeriformes families in the AOS sequence: Tyrannidae (tyrant flycatchers), Laniidae (shrikes), Vireonidae (vireos), and Corvidae (crows, jays, and allies). These families span the ecological gamut from aerial insectivores (flycatchers) to impaling predators (shrikes) to canopy gleaners (vireos) to cognitive generalists (corvids).

In the suboscine-oscine split that defines Passeriformes phylogeny, Tyrannidae is the only suboscine family well-represented in the PNW — their vocalizations are innate (not learned), distinguishing them from all oscine passerines whose songs are culturally transmitted. Corvidae represents the cognitive apex of the oscine radiation — tool manufacture, episodic-like memory, mirror self-recognition, and social deception are all documented in corvid species occurring in the PNW [O1].

---

### Family Tyrannidae — Tyrant Flycatchers

Tyrant flycatchers are the largest family of birds in the Western Hemisphere (~400 species). All PNW species are Neotropical-Nearctic migrants that breed in the region and winter from Mexico to South America. They share a fundamental foraging strategy — sallying from an exposed perch to catch insects in flight — but partition habitat by forest structure, elevation, and canopy position. Identification of *Empidonax* flycatchers (Willow, Hammond's, Pacific-slope, Dusky, Gray) is one of the most challenging problems in PNW field ornithology; vocal identification is usually required [O1, O7].

### Olive-sided Flycatcher (*Contopus cooperi*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Contopus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (Neotropical migrant; winters central Panama to Andes)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Near Threatened
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Sensitive — Vulnerable
- PIF: Common Birds in Steep Decline (Yellow Watch List)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 18-20 cm (7.1-7.9 in)
- **Wingspan:** 32-33 cm (12.6-13.0 in)
- **Mass:** 28-40 g (1.0-1.4 oz) — largest PNW *Contopus*
- **Plumage:** Dark olive-brown above and on sides, contrasting with white center of breast and belly — gives a "vested" appearance (dark jacket, white shirt). Large head, short tail, stout bill (wider than deep, typical of aerial insectivore). White tufts on lower back sometimes visible (often concealed by folded wings).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — sexes similar.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Open conifer forest edges, burns, bogs, meadow margins — anywhere with tall snag perches overlooking open airspace. Strongly associated with forest openings: post-fire landscapes, lakeshores, clearcut margins, bog edges, subalpine meadow margins. Requires exposed perch >10 m tall for sallying. Avoids dense closed-canopy forest [P18, G8].
- **Wintering:** Montane forest edges in Central and South America (Panama to Bolivia)
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,500 m (8,200 ft), concentrated 500-2,000 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — forest openings)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 1 (Subalpine — meadow edges), Zone 5 (Riparian — bog/lake margins)
- **Microhabitat:** The key habitat requirement is the juxtaposition of tall dead trees (perches) and open airspace (foraging volume). This combination occurs naturally at burn margins, bog edges, lake shores, and subalpine meadow edges — all habitats that are declining or changing character.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Flying insects — primarily Hymenoptera (bees, wasps) and winged ants, plus beetles, moths, and dragonflies. Specializes in large-bodied flying insects.
- **Foraging strategy:** Classic sallying flycatcher — perches motionless on exposed dead branch at or above canopy level, sallies out 10-50 m to intercept flying insects, returns to same or nearby perch. Sallies are among the longest of any PNW flycatcher, reflecting the species' preference for large, open airspace volumes. Will hawk bees near hive entrances and intercept ants during nuptial flights.
- **Seasonal variation:** Entirely insectivorous; no seasonal diet shift.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Open cup on horizontal conifer branch, typically well out from trunk, 3-20 m above ground. Nests often on dead or dying branches near forest edge.
- **Clutch size:** 3-4 eggs (typically 3)
- **Incubation:** 16-17 days; female only
- **Fledging:** 21-23 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** Late May-July in PNW (arrives late May, one of the later migrants)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — BBS trend -3.5% per year rangewide (1966-2019), representing approximately 78% cumulative population decline since 1970. One of the most rapidly declining landbirds in North America [G4, O4].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 6.4 million continent-wide (PIF 2020 — down from an estimated 29 million in 1970) [O4].
- **Threats:** Wintering ground deforestation in Andes and Central American highlands (primary suspected cause), fire suppression reducing forest openings on breeding grounds, decline in aerial insect populations (potential — under investigation), loss of snag perches in managed forests [G4, O4].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer (openings) | Breeding | Uncommon, declining | Indicator of open-canopy forest structure |
| Zone 1: Subalpine (meadow edge) | Breeding | Uncommon | High-elevation edge specialist |
| Zone 5: Riparian (bog/lake) | Breeding | Uncommon, local | Wetland-edge sentinel forager |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary song:** Unmistakable — loud, ringing *quick, THREE BEERS!* (ascending whistle). One of the most far-carrying and recognizable songs in PNW conifer forests. Delivered from the highest exposed perch available, often a dead tree top. Can be heard at 500+ m.
- **Call:** Sharp *pip-pip-pip* given in alarm.

#### Evolutionary Note

*Contopus cooperi* is the largest member of genus *Contopus* (pewees) in North America. The genus is restricted to the New World and represents one of the few suboscine flycatcher lineages that has radiated extensively into temperate North America. Suboscine vocalizations are innate (genetically encoded) rather than learned — the Olive-sided Flycatcher's "quick THREE BEERS" song is hardwired, not culturally transmitted. This makes suboscine species excellent models for studying the genetic basis of complex vocalizations, in contrast to the culturally transmitted songs of oscine passerines [O1].

#### Decline Context

The Olive-sided Flycatcher's decline is particularly concerning because the primary drivers appear to be on the wintering grounds and along the migratory route — areas outside the management authority of PNW land agencies. The species' dependence on montane forest edges in the northern Andes during winter makes it vulnerable to the ongoing conversion of Andean cloud forest to agriculture and pasture. On the breeding grounds, fire suppression has reduced the open-canopy habitat the species requires, but prescribed fire and managed wildfire programs could partially offset this loss. The species is a bellwether for the health of the full-annual-cycle conservation challenge: even perfect breeding habitat cannot sustain a population if wintering habitat is destroyed [O4, G4].

### Western Wood-Pewee (*Contopus sordidulus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Contopus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (Neotropical migrant; winters South America)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 14-17 cm (5.5-6.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 26-27 cm (10.2-10.6 in)
- **Mass:** 11-14 g (0.4-0.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Drab olive-gray above, pale below with dusky wash on breast and sides, two thin wing bars, no eye ring. Nearly identical to Eastern Wood-Pewee (range overlap minimal in PNW). Bill dark above, pale (orange-yellow) at base of lower mandible.

**Habitat:** Open and semi-open woodland — ponderosa pine, mixed conifer with openings, oak-conifer, riparian cottonwood and willow. Prefers drier, more open forest than *Empidonax* flycatchers. Common in east-side forests and foothill woodlands.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,700 m (8,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — open conifer), Zone 3 (Lowland — woodland edges), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian — cottonwood), Zone 7 (Steppe — riparian)

**Diet and Foraging:** Flying insects caught by sallying from exposed perch at mid-canopy level. Forages from lower perches than Olive-sided Flycatcher and makes shorter sallies, partitioning vertical airspace between the two *Contopus* species.

**Reproduction:** Open cup nest on horizontal branch, well out from trunk; clutch 2-4; incubation 12-13 days; fledging 14-18 days; 1 brood. Breeding June-July.

**Population:** Slight decline — BBS trend -1.0% per year in PNW (1966-2019) [G4]. Still common across its range.

**Vocalizations:** Burry, descending *pee-eer* — the classic sound of open PNW woodland. Also a thin *pwee* and a rapid three-note *pee-dee-eer* at dawn. Suboscine: song innate.

**Evolutionary note:** Western Wood-Pewee and Eastern Wood-Pewee (*C. virens*) are nearly identical in appearance but easily distinguished by voice — a pattern consistent with suboscine song being genetically encoded rather than learned. The two species are not sister taxa; *C. sordidulus* is more closely related to Tropical Pewee (*C. cinereus*), suggesting that the pewee radiation involved multiple independent colonizations of temperate North America from tropical ancestors [O1].

### Willow Flycatcher (*Empidonax traillii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Empidonax*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (Neotropical migrant)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Endangered (*E. t. extimus* — Southwestern subspecies; PNW subspecies *E. t. brewsteri* and *E. t. adastus* not listed) | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern (PNW populations)

#### Profile

- **Length:** 13-17 cm (5.1-6.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 19-24 cm (7.5-9.4 in)
- **Mass:** 11-16 g (0.4-0.6 oz)
- **Plumage:** Olive-brown above, whitish below with olive wash on breast, two whitish wing bars, minimal or absent eye ring (distinguishes from nearly identical Alder Flycatcher). PNW birds are subspecies *E. t. brewsteri* (west side) and *E. t. adastus* (east side).

**Habitat:** Dense riparian shrub and willow thickets, wet meadow edges, beaver pond margins. Strongly associated with water. Avoids dry upland habitat. One of the most riparian-obligate PNW passerines.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,000 m (6,500 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 5 (Riparian — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland — wetland edges), Zone 7 (Steppe — riparian corridors)

**Diet and Foraging:** Flying insects caught by sallying from low perch in dense shrub canopy. Shorter sallies than Wood-Pewee, within or just above the shrub canopy.

**Reproduction:** Open cup low in dense willow/shrub; clutch 3-4; incubation 12-15 days; fledging 12-15 days; 1 brood. Frequent Brown-headed Cowbird host — parasitism rates high in fragmented riparian.

**Population:** Variable — some PNW populations declining due to riparian habitat loss. BBS trend -0.6% per year in PNW (not significant) [G4].

**Vocalizations:** Sneezy *FITZ-bew* — diagnostic. The single most reliable identification character for this species within the *Empidonax* complex.

**Evolutionary note:** The Willow-Alder Flycatcher complex was split by AOS in 1973 — they are nearly identical in plumage but differ in song (Willow: *FITZ-bew*; Alder: *fee-BEE-o*), habitat (Willow: drier shrub; Alder: wetter alder/birch), and genetics. Their contact zone in the PNW is poorly mapped. The Southwestern Willow Flycatcher (*E. t. extimus*), ESA-listed as Endangered, does not occur in the PNW study area [O1, O3].

### Hammond's Flycatcher (*Empidonax hammondii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Empidonax*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (Neotropical migrant)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 12-14 cm (4.7-5.5 in)
- **Wingspan:** 22-23 cm (8.7-9.1 in)
- **Mass:** 8-12 g (0.3-0.4 oz) — one of smallest PNW flycatchers
- **Plumage:** Olive-gray above, grayish breast, yellowish belly, narrow eye ring, two wing bars, short narrow bill (shorter than Dusky Flycatcher — a key ID character). Primary projection extends well past tertials (long-winged).

**Habitat:** Dense, mature conifer forest — Douglas-fir, spruce-fir, mountain hemlock. The most interior-forest-dependent *Empidonax* in the PNW. Forages in mid- to upper canopy of tall conifers. Avoids openings (contrast with Dusky and Gray Flycatchers).
**Elevation range:** 600-2,500 m (2,000-8,200 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — primary), Zone 1 (Subalpine — upper breeding range)

**Diet and Foraging:** Small flying insects caught by sallying within conifer canopy. Sallies are short and within the canopy — more gleaning and hovering than open-air sallying.

**Reproduction:** Open cup on conifer branch against trunk, 4-18 m up; clutch 3-4; incubation 15-16 days; fledging 16-18 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable [G4]. Common in mature PNW conifer forests.

**Vocalizations:** Thin, burry *se-brt* or *pwee-brrt, pree, pweet* — a three- or four-element song. Voice is the primary field ID tool for separating from Dusky Flycatcher.

**Evolutionary note:** Hammond's Flycatcher is not closely related to the Dusky Flycatcher despite their similar appearance and adjacent ranges — molecular phylogenetics shows Hammond's is sister to the Dusky-capped Flycatcher (*Myiarchus tuberculifer*) of Mexico and Central America, a striking example of convergent morphology in unrelated *Empidonax* lineages [O1].

### Pacific-slope Flycatcher (*Empidonax difficilis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Empidonax*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (Neotropical migrant)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 14-17 cm (5.5-6.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 20-22 cm (7.9-8.7 in)
- **Mass:** 9-13 g (0.3-0.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Olive-green above, yellowish below with olive wash on breast, prominent teardrop-shaped eye ring, two wing bars. The most yellowish *Empidonax* in the PNW. Formerly lumped with Cordilleran Flycatcher as "Western Flycatcher."

**Habitat:** Shaded, moist conifer and mixed forest — understory and lower canopy. Associated with ravines, stream corridors, and shaded north-facing slopes in mature forest. The most shade-tolerant PNW flycatcher. Nests on structures (cliff ledges, bridges, building eaves) as well as tree cavities and root balls.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,800 m (5,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — moist forest), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — ravines, parks)

**Diet and Foraging:** Small flying insects caught by sallying from low to mid-canopy perch in shaded forest. Also hovers to glean insects from leaves (more than most *Empidonax*).

**Reproduction:** Nest on sheltered ledge, cavity, root mass, or building structure; clutch 3-5; incubation 14-15 days; fledging 14-18 days; 1-2 broods.

**Population:** Stable — common in moist PNW forests [G4].

**Vocalizations:** Upslurred *su-WEET* — a rising, thin whistle. Also a sharp *pseet* note. Male's position note given throughout the day from shaded perch.

**Evolutionary note:** Split from Cordilleran Flycatcher (*E. occidentalis*) in 1989 by AOS. The two species' ranges overlap narrowly in the PNW (Pacific-slope on west side, Cordilleran on east side of Cascades). Separation in the field is essentially impossible by plumage; voice and range are the primary tools. The two species are sister taxa with limited hybridization in the contact zone [O1, O3].

### Dusky Flycatcher (*Empidonax oberholseri*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Empidonax*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates to Mexico in winter)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 13-15 cm (5.1-5.9 in)
- **Wingspan:** 20-23 cm (7.9-9.1 in)
- **Mass:** 9-12 g (0.3-0.4 oz)
- **Plumage:** Grayish-olive above, pale below with faint olive wash on breast, moderate eye ring, two wing bars. Bill longer than Hammond's (lower mandible mostly pale — diagnostic). Primary projection shorter than Hammond's (shorter-winged).

**Habitat:** Open montane shrub and woodland — dry brushy slopes, mountain mahogany, sagebrush-aspen ecotone, open ponderosa pine understory, regenerating burns with shrub growth. Occupies drier, more open habitat than Hammond's or Pacific-slope.
**Elevation range:** 600-2,700 m (2,000-8,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 2 (Montane — open east-side), Zone 4 (Foothill — shrub), Zone 7 (Steppe — brushy margins)

**Diet and Foraging:** Flying insects caught by sallying from exposed shrub perch. Sallies tend to be more horizontal and into open airspace (contrast with Hammond's vertical canopy sallies).

**Reproduction:** Open cup in upright shrub fork, 1-3 m; clutch 3-5; incubation 12-16 days; fledging 15-20 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable [G4]. Common in east-side montane shrub habitats.

**Vocalizations:** Dry, burry *duh-hic, perrt, pseet* — a distinctive three-element song pattern. Lower-pitched and drier than Hammond's.

**Evolutionary note:** Dusky Flycatcher is sister to Gray Flycatcher, not to the more similar-looking Hammond's — a classic example of convergent morphology misleading pre-molecular taxonomy. The Dusky-Gray pair occupies open shrubby habitats (divergent from forest-interior Hammond's) and likely speciated in allopatric arid-land refugia during the Pleistocene [O1].

### Gray Flycatcher (*Empidonax wrightii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Empidonax*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates to Mexico in winter)

#### Profile

- **Length:** 14-16 cm (5.5-6.3 in)

**Habitat:** Open juniper-sagebrush woodland — the driest habitat occupied by any PNW *Empidonax*. Associated with western juniper (*Juniperus occidentalis*) stands and tall sagebrush in eastern Oregon and Washington.

**Population:** Increasing — BBS trend +1.5% per year (1966-2019), correlated with juniper woodland expansion into sagebrush [G4].

**Vocalizations:** Two-element *chi-bit* — simpler than Dusky or Hammond's.

**Evolutionary note:** The Gray Flycatcher's downward tail-dip is unique among *Empidonax* and is a reliable behavioral field mark. This behavior may function in prey detection (flushing ground insects) or as a visual signal. The species' range expansion tracking juniper encroachment into sagebrush is a real-time example of habitat modification driving distributional change [O1].

### Ash-throated Flycatcher (*Myiarchus cinerascens*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Myiarchus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates to Mexico in winter)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 19-22 cm (7.5-8.7 in)
- **Wingspan:** 29-32 cm (11.4-12.6 in)
- **Mass:** 21-30 g (0.7-1.1 oz)
- **Plumage:** Brown-gray above, pale gray throat and breast, pale yellow belly, two wing bars, rufous in tail and primaries (visible in flight). Bushy crested head.

**Habitat:** Dry open woodland — oak, juniper, ponderosa pine, riparian cottonwood. The only regularly occurring *Myiarchus* in the PNW. At northern range limit in southern Oregon and eastern Washington.
**Elevation range:** 200-1,800 m (650-5,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — primary), Zone 7 (East-Side — juniper/riparian)

**Evolutionary note:** *Myiarchus* is a large Neotropical genus (~20 species) with only two species reaching the PNW. ### Western Kingbird (*Tyrannus verticalis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Tyrannus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (migrates to Mexico/Central America in winter)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 20-24 cm (7.9-9.4 in)
- **Wingspan:** 38-41 cm (15.0-16.1 in)
- **Mass:** 33-45 g (1.2-1.6 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray head and breast, bright yellow belly, olive-gray back, dark wings, black tail with white outer edges (diagnostic in flight). Concealed orange-red crown patch (rarely visible).

**Habitat:** Open country with scattered trees, utility poles, fences — agricultural land, rangeland, suburban edges. Common breeding bird of east-side lowlands and valleys.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,800 m (5,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland — agricultural), Zone 5 (Riparian — open)

**Diet and Foraging:** Flying insects (bees, wasps, grasshoppers, beetles) caught by sallying from elevated perches (utility wires, fence posts, tree tops). Aggressive aerial predator — regularly attacks and drives away much larger birds (hawks, crows, ravens) that approach nest area.

**Evolutionary note:** *Tyrannus* kingbirds are the namesakes of the family Tyrannidae — "tyrant flycatchers" — named for their aggressive mobbing of much larger birds. The genus is pan-American with ~13 species. Western Kingbird has expanded its range northward and eastward over the past century, colonizing areas formerly occupied only by Eastern Kingbird — a range expansion facilitated by the planting of shade trees and utility poles in previously treeless grassland [O1].

### Eastern Kingbird (*Tyrannus tyrannus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Tyrannidae, Genus *Tyrannus*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (long-distance Neotropical migrant; winters Amazon basin)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern (slight decline)

#### Profile

- **Length:** 19-23 cm (7.5-9.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 33-38 cm (13.0-15.0 in)
- **Mass:** 33-55 g (1.2-1.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Blackish above, white below, with white terminal tail band (diagnostic). Concealed orange-red crown patch. No yellow on belly (separates from Western Kingbird).

**Habitat:** Open areas near water — riparian edges, lakeshores, marshlands, orchards, and open agricultural land with scattered trees. More associated with water than Western Kingbird in the PNW.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,500 m (4,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 5 (Riparian — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland — open), Zone 7 (Steppe — riparian)

**Diet and Foraging:** Flying insects in summer (sallying from exposed perch); shifts to fruit on wintering grounds in Amazonia — a dramatic seasonal dietary switch from animal to plant food, unusual among flycatchers.

**Reproduction:** Open cup on horizontal branch, often overhanging water; clutch 3-5; incubation 16-18 days; fledging 16-18 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Slight decline — BBS trend -1.3% per year rangewide (1966-2019) [G4]. Still common but trending downward.

**Vocalizations:** Stuttering, buzzy *kit-kit-kitteer-kitteer* — electric and emphatic. Highly vocal.

**Evolutionary note:** Despite its name, Eastern Kingbird breeds across most of North America including the PNW — the "Eastern" label reflects early Euro-American ornithological bias toward Atlantic coast populations. On its Amazonian wintering grounds, the species is entirely frugivorous and forms large communal flocks — a completely different ecological role from its solitary, insectivorous breeding season in North America. This dual ecological identity across hemispheres is among the most dramatic seasonal niche shifts in any passerine [O1].

### Family Laniidae — Shrikes

### Loggerhead Shrike (*Lanius ludovicianus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Laniidae, Genus *Lanius*
**Status:** Year-round resident (partial migrant — some winter withdrawal from coldest areas)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed (subspecies *L. l. mearnsi* on San Clemente Island listed Endangered — not in PNW)
- IUCN: Near Threatened (species-level assessment due to rangewide decline)
- State WA: Candidate species
- State OR: Sensitive — Critical
- PIF: Common Birds in Steep Decline

#### Profile

- **Length:** 20-23 cm (7.9-9.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 28-32 cm (11.0-12.6 in)
- **Mass:** 35-50 g (1.2-1.8 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray above, white below, bold black mask through eye (wider than Northern Shrike's mask), black wings with white wing patch visible in flight, black tail with white outer feathers. Stout hooked bill — the "butcher bird."
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — sexes similar, female slightly browner.

**Habitat:** Open country with scattered perches — sagebrush steppe with fence lines, dry grassland with shrubs, pasture with hedgerows. Requires open ground for hunting, elevated perches for prey detection, and thorny shrubs or barbed-wire fences for prey impaling. In PNW, restricted to east-side steppe and dry lowlands.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,500 m (4,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — primary), Zone 4 (Foothill — dry grassland)

**Diet and Foraging:** Large insects (grasshoppers, beetles, crickets), small mammals (voles, mice), small birds, lizards, and occasionally small snakes. The shrike is a passerine predator — it catches vertebrate prey by striking with hooked bill, then impales prey on thorns, barbed wire, or sharp twigs to anchor it for dismemberment. This impaling behavior functions for food storage (creating "larders" of cached prey items) and for males to display food stores to prospective mates. The tomial tooth on the bill (convergent with falcons) enables cervical dislocation of vertebrate prey.

**Reproduction:** Open cup in dense thorny shrub (often hawthorn or multiflora rose) or small tree; clutch 4-8; incubation 16 days; fledging 17-21 days; 1-2 broods.

**Population:** Declining — BBS trend -2.7% per year rangewide (1966-2019). Washington populations have declined ~85% since the 1960s. Now rare and local in eastern WA and OR [G4, G9].
**Estimate:** Approximately 5.3 million continent-wide (PIF 2020 — down from ~16 million in 1970) [O4].
**Threats:** Pesticide-driven reduction of large insect prey base (primary), conversion of grassland and sagebrush to agriculture, loss of fence-line and hedgerow perch sites through clean farming, highway mortality (low-flying hunting mode), competition with expanding Northern Shrike winter range [G9, O4].

**Vocalizations:** Harsh, varied song of buzzes, warbles, and mimicked phrases — complex but rarely heard. Call a harsh *shrack* or *zhee-zhee*.

**Evolutionary note:** Laniidae (shrikes) are an Old World family with only two species in North America — Loggerhead Shrike and Northern Shrike (*L. borealis*, winter visitor). The convergent evolution of the tomial tooth (notched bill for cervical dislocation) in both shrikes and falcons is a textbook example of functional convergence in unrelated predatory lineages. Despite superficial similarity, the two families are separated by ~90 million years of independent evolution. Loggerhead Shrike's decline across North America mirrors the Kestrel decline and likely shares some of the same drivers — pesticide-reduced prey base and loss of open habitat structure [O1].

### Family Vireonidae — Vireos

Vireos are small, deliberate canopy gleaners — they move slowly through foliage, searching leaf surfaces for insects with a methodical pace that contrasts with the quick, restless movements of warblers. The family is entirely New World, with about 52 species; only four regularly breed in the PNW. Vireos are characterized by sturdy, slightly hooked bills (broader and stronger than warbler bills), deliberate movements, and persistent, repetitive songs delivered throughout the day — including during the heat of midday when most songbirds are silent [O1].

### Cassin's Vireo (*Vireo cassinii*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Vireonidae, Genus *Vireo*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (short-distance migrant; winters Mexico)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 12-14 cm (4.7-5.5 in)
- **Wingspan:** 24-25 cm (9.4-9.8 in)
- **Mass:** 14-20 g (0.5-0.7 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray head with white spectacles (bold eye ring connected by white loral bridge — diagnostic for the "solitary vireo" complex), olive-green back, two white wing bars, white underparts with yellowish flanks.

**Habitat:** Mixed conifer-deciduous forest at mid-elevation — Douglas-fir with madrone, bigleaf maple, or tanoak. Also open conifer forest with shrubby understory. Prefers forest with moderate canopy closure and well-developed understory or edge.

The three resulting species — Cassin's Vireo (West), Blue-headed Vireo (East), and Plumbeous Vireo (Interior) — form a ring-like distribution around the western mountains, with narrow hybrid zones. Cassin's and Plumbeous overlap in eastern Oregon and the Blue Mountains [O1, O3].

### Hutton's Vireo (*Vireo huttoni*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Vireonidae, Genus *Vireo*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 11-13 cm (4.3-5.1 in)
- **Wingspan:** 20-21 cm (7.9-8.3 in)
- **Mass:** 10-14 g (0.4-0.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Olive-green overall, pale eye ring broken above eye (incomplete spectacles), two wing bars, pale lores, stout vireo bill. Easily confused with Ruby-crowned Kinglet — separated by larger size, heavier bill, lack of dark area below lower wing bar, and slower movements.

**Habitat:** Oak and mixed oak-conifer woodland — Oregon white oak, madrone-Douglas-fir, tanoak. Strongly associated with live oaks and the insects they harbor. Year-round resident that does not migrate, unlike other PNW vireos.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,200 m (3,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — oak groves)

**Diet and Foraging:** Insects gleaned slowly from oak foliage and twigs. Movement pattern distinctively slow and methodical — reportedly the slowest-moving PNW passerine.

**Reproduction:** Hanging cup in oak or conifer, 3-8 m; clutch 3-4; incubation 14-16 days; fledging 14 days; 1-2 broods.

**Population:** Stable but limited by oak habitat availability [G4]. Declining where oak woodland is converted to conifer.

**Vocalizations:** Monotonous, repeated *zu-wee, zu-wee, zu-wee* — a two-note phrase repeated endlessly. Also a scolding *cheh-cheh-cheh* when alarmed.

**Evolutionary note:** Hutton's Vireo is one of the few year-round resident vireos in North America (most vireos are Neotropical migrants). Its sedentary lifestyle is associated with the mild winters of the Pacific Coast and the year-round food availability in evergreen oak canopy. The species' remarkable convergence with Ruby-crowned Kinglet in size, plumage, and habitat is a classic teaching example in field ornithology — the two species are not related and occupy different foraging niches (vireo: slow gleaner; kinglet: rapid hover-gleaner) [O1].

### Red-eyed Vireo (*Vireo olivaceus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Vireonidae, Genus *Vireo*
**Status:** Breeding visitor (long-distance Neotropical migrant; winters Amazon basin)
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 13-14 cm (5.1-5.5 in)
- **Wingspan:** 23-26 cm (9.1-10.2 in)
- **Mass:** 12-26 g (0.4-0.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Olive-green above, white below, gray crown bordered by black lateral crown stripes, white supercilium bordered by dark eye line, red iris in adults (dark in juveniles), no wing bars. Stout vireo bill.

**Habitat:** Deciduous and mixed forest canopy — in the PNW, primarily in east-side riparian cottonwood and aspen groves, and rarely in west-side mixed deciduous forests. Less common in the PNW than in eastern North America, where it is the most abundant breeding bird in deciduous forest.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,500 m (4,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 5 (Riparian — east-side cottonwood/aspen, primary), Zone 3 (Lowland Forest — rare in west-side deciduous)

**Diet and Foraging:** Insects gleaned from deciduous canopy; supplements with small fruits in late summer. The most persistent singer of any North American bird — individual males may deliver 20,000-40,000 song phrases per day during breeding season.

**Reproduction:** Hanging cup in deciduous tree, 2-18 m; clutch 3-5; incubation 11-14 days; fledging 10-12 days; 1 brood. Very frequent Brown-headed Cowbird host.

**Population:** Stable to slightly declining in PNW (peripheral western range) [G4]. Abundant in eastern North America.

**Vocalizations:** Persistent, deliberate, robin-like phrases with pauses: *here-I-am...up-here...see-me?...here-I-am* — repeated continuously, all day, for hours. Holds the record for most songs delivered in a single day by any North American bird species (documented at 22,197 phrases in one day). Song is learned (oscine — unlike the innate songs of suboscine flycatchers in the same forest).

**Evolutionary note:** Red-eyed Vireo's massive continental population (~130 million individuals, the most abundant landbird in eastern North American forests) contrasts with its peripheral, uncommon status in the PNW — an illustration of how a species' ecological dominance can be sharply bounded by biogeographic limits. The species' red iris develops during the first year and may function in species recognition or mate quality signaling [O1].

### Family Corvidae — Crows, Jays, Magpies, and Nutcrackers

Corvidae represents the cognitive pinnacle of the avian class. Tool manufacture, episodic-like memory (remembering what was cached, where, and when), mirror self-recognition, social deception, analogical reasoning, and the ability to plan for future needs have all been documented in corvid species. PNW corvids include the iconic Steller's Jay (the region's most recognizable forest bird), Clark's Nutcracker (whose seed-caching mutualism with whitebark pine structures entire subalpine ecosystems), the American Crow (whose urban cognitive ecology is among the most studied in the world), and the Common Raven (whose behavioral flexibility spans alpine to marine to desert habitats).

Corvids are the largest passerines. All PNW species are year-round residents (non-migratory or with only short-distance seasonal movements). All cache food — this single behavioral trait underlies much of their ecological influence, from forest regeneration (nutcrackers planting trees) to community dynamics (jays dispersing acorns) to human-wildlife interaction (crows remembering individual human faces) [O1, P10, P19].

### Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Cyanocitta*
**Status:** Year-round resident (short-distance altitudinal movements)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (common)
- State OR: Not listed (common)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 30-34 cm (11.8-13.4 in)
- **Wingspan:** 43-45 cm (16.9-17.7 in)
- **Mass:** 100-140 g (3.5-4.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Dark charcoal-black head, crest, throat, and upper breast; deep blue wings, tail, and lower body. PNW birds are the darkest-headed populations in the species' range — coastal *C. s. stelleri* has a blacker head and darker blue body than interior subspecies. Prominent triangular crest (erectable) is the single most recognizable field mark. Blue structural coloration produced by light scattering in feather nanostructures (not pigment — a crushed Steller's Jay feather is brownish).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — sexes similar in plumage. Males average slightly larger.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Conifer and mixed conifer-deciduous forest, forest edges, suburban areas with mature conifers. Extremely adaptable — from deep old-growth to suburban backyards. Prefers areas with some canopy cover; avoids completely open habitat. The most common PNW corvid in forested environments.
- **Wintering:** Same as breeding with some altitudinal descent from highest-elevation breeding sites. Regular at bird feeders in winter.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,500 m (8,200 ft) — broad elevational range
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 2 (Montane Conifer Forest)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 3 (Lowland Forest/Urban), Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak), Zone 5 (Riparian)
- **Microhabitat:** Forages at all levels from ground to canopy. Nest placement in dense conifer near trunk suggests preference for concealment. Regularly visits human food sources (campgrounds, picnic areas, feeders).

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Omnivorous generalist — acorns and other mast (25-30% of annual diet), pine seeds, other seeds and nuts, insects (beetles, wasps, caterpillars), small vertebrates (nestling birds, lizards), eggs (significant nest predator), berries, human food refuse.
- **Foraging strategy:** Highly flexible — gleans insects from foliage, hammers open acorns and pine cones, raids other birds' nests, scavenges at campsites, visits feeders. Caches food (acorns, pine seeds, nuts) in bark crevices, soil, leaf litter, and crevices for later retrieval. Caching behavior contributes to oak and pine regeneration — unretrieved caches germinate.
- **Seasonal variation:** Acorn and seed caching peaks in fall. Insect consumption peaks in summer breeding season. Human food exploitation highest in summer (campground season) and winter (feeders).

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky stick cup lined with mud, rootlets, and pine needles. Placed in conifer near trunk, 3-20 m above ground. Both sexes build.
- **Clutch size:** 2-6 eggs (typically 3-5)
- **Incubation:** 16-18 days; female only (male feeds female on nest)
- **Fledging:** 16-21 days; young remain with parents for ~1 month post-fledging
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** April-June in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable — BBS trend +0.2% per year in PNW (2006-2019) [G4].
- **Estimate:** Approximately 12 million continent-wide [O4]. Abundant throughout PNW forests and suburbs.
- **Threats:** West Nile Virus (corvids are highly susceptible — WNV arrived in PNW ~2005-2007, causing detectable but not catastrophic mortality), window collisions in suburban habitat, occasional persecution as nest predator [G4].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Common | Seed disperser, nest predator |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common | Human-commensal generalist |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Fairly common | Acorn cacher and disperser |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Uncommon | Forest corridor user |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Harsh, descending *shaack-shaack-shaack* — the dominant voice of PNW conifer forest. Delivered with great volume and variation. Functions as alarm call (alerts entire forest community to predator presence) and territorial advertisement.
- **Mimicry:** Steller's Jays are accomplished mimics — accurately imitate Red-tailed Hawk screams (*keeee-aarr*), Northern Goshawk calls, and occasionally other corvid and raptor calls. Hawk mimicry may function to clear competitors from feeding areas.
- **Whisper song:** Quiet, complex subsong with musical notes, rattles, and mimicry — delivered at close range during courtship or intraspecific interactions. Rarely heard by casual observers.

#### Evolutionary Note

Steller's Jay is the western counterpart of the Blue Jay (*C. cristata*) — the two are sister species that hybridize in a narrow contact zone along the eastern front of the Rocky Mountains. In the PNW, Steller's Jay is the sole representative of the genus. The species was named by Georg Wilhelm Steller, the German naturalist on Vitus Bering's 1741 expedition to Alaska — Steller's Jay was the first North American bird species Steller encountered (in Kayak Island, Alaska), and his observation that it resembled the Blue Jay of Mark Catesby's illustrations helped confirm that the expedition had reached the American continent [O1].

#### Cultural Significance

Coast Salish and Kwakwaka'wakw peoples recognize the Steller's Jay as a noisy, bold forest presence — a bird whose alarm calls alert the forest to approaching disturbance. In published ethnographic materials from multiple Northwest Coast nations, jays are characterized by their intelligence, boldness, and vocality — traits immediately apparent to anyone who has spent time in PNW forests. The Steller's Jay is the provincial bird of British Columbia [C1, C2, C8].

### Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Nucifraga*
**Status:** Year-round resident (irruptive — descends to lowlands in poor cone crop years)
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Yellow Watch List (declining due to whitebark pine loss)

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 27-30 cm (10.6-11.8 in)
- **Wingspan:** 55-58 cm (21.7-22.8 in)
- **Mass:** 106-161 g (3.7-5.7 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray body with black wings and tail, white outer tail feathers (conspicuous in flight), white wing patches, black bill (long, pointed, and powerful — specialized for prying open pine cones). Black legs. No crest. Overall impression in flight is of a gray-and-white bird with flashing black-and-white wing and tail pattern.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — sexes similar.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Subalpine forest, particularly whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) and limber pine (*Pinus flexilis*) stands. Also uses subalpine fir, Engelmann spruce, and mountain hemlock forests adjacent to pine stands. Breeding tied to large-cone pine seed availability [P10].
- **Wintering:** Same habitat when cone crops adequate. During poor cone crop years (crop failure), nutcrackers irrupt downslope to montane and occasionally lowland elevations, appearing at feeders, campgrounds, and suburban areas where they are normally absent.
- **Elevation range:** 900-3,500 m (3,000-11,500 ft) — highest average elevation of any PNW corvid
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 1 (Alpine/Subalpine)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 2 (Montane — particularly east-side larch/pine forests)
- **Microhabitat:** Requires whitebark or limber pine cone crops for seed harvest. Caches seeds across wide elevational and habitat range — cache sites include exposed ridges, south-facing slopes, and wind-scoured areas where snow melts early (enabling winter cache retrieval).

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Pine seeds — whitebark pine seeds constitute the primary food source. Also takes ponderosa pine, Douglas-fir, and lodgepole pine seeds. Supplements with insects (beetles, grasshoppers), carrion, berries, and small vertebrates.
- **Foraging strategy:** Harvests pine seeds from cones by inserting long bill into cone scales and prying open to extract seeds. Can carry up to 150 seeds at a time in a sublingual pouch (expandable pouch beneath the tongue — unique to this genus). Caches seeds in clusters of 1-15 at depths of 2-3 cm across the landscape. A single nutcracker caches 30,000-100,000 seeds per year across thousands of cache sites spanning several km2 [P10].
- **Seasonal variation:** Cone harvest and caching peaks August-October. Cache retrieval continues through winter and spring. Insects and other foods supplemental during summer.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Bulky stick cup lined with bark strips, placed in conifer near trunk, 3-20 m. Often nests at high elevation in February-March while snow still deep — one of the earliest-nesting PNW birds (timed to have young ready to harvest the next year's pine cone crop).
- **Clutch size:** 2-6 eggs (typically 2-4)
- **Incubation:** 16-22 days; both sexes (the only PNW corvid where both sexes regularly incubate)
- **Fledging:** 18-28 days
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** February-May in PNW (remarkably early for high elevation)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Declining — tied directly to whitebark pine decline from white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*) and mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*). Nutcrackers abandon areas where whitebark pine mortality exceeds ~70%, creating a positive feedback loop: fewer nutcrackers → fewer cached seeds → reduced whitebark pine regeneration → further nutcracker decline [P10, G1].
- **Estimate:** 200,000-400,000 continent-wide (poorly known; remote habitat limits survey accuracy) [O4].
- **Threats:** Whitebark pine decline (primary — ESA-listed as Threatened in 2022), climate change driving treeline upslope and reducing subalpine habitat, fire exclusion allowing shade-tolerant conifers to replace open pine stands [G1, O4, P10].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Year-round | Uncommon to fairly common | Keystone seed disperser for whitebark pine |
| Zone 2: Montane (east-side) | Year-round | Uncommon | Pine seed harvester in ponderosa/larch |
| Zone 3: Lowland | Irruptive (poor cone years) | Rare, irregular | Vagrant during crop failure |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Harsh, grating *kraaah* — loud, nasal, far-carrying. Given in flight and from perch. One of the most characteristic sounds of the PNW subalpine zone.
- **Soft vocalizations:** Various clicks, rattles, and mewing calls in social interactions.

#### Evolutionary Note

Clark's Nutcracker was described by Alexander Wilson in 1811 from specimens collected by William Clark during the Lewis and Clark expedition (along the Salmon River, Idaho, August 1805). The genus *Nucifraga* (nutcrackers) contains only two species worldwide: Clark's Nutcracker in western North America and the Spotted Nutcracker (*N. caryocatactes*) in Eurasia. Both are obligate seed-caching mutualists with large-seeded pines — Clark's with whitebark/limber pine, Spotted with Swiss stone pine (*Pinus cembra*) and Korean pine (*P. koraiensis*). This intercontinental mutualism between *Nucifraga* corvids and five-needle pines (*Pinus* subgenus *Strobus*) has been maintained for at least 5-10 million years and represents one of the most tightly coevolved bird-plant interactions documented in temperate forests [P10, O1].

#### Mutualism with Whitebark Pine

The Clark's Nutcracker-whitebark pine mutualism is the most consequential bird-plant interaction in PNW subalpine ecosystems. Whitebark pine seeds are large (15 mm), wingless, and cannot be wind-dispersed — the tree is entirely dependent on nutcrackers for seed dispersal. Nutcrackers cache seeds 1-10 km from harvest sites, at depths enabling germination, in microsites (ridge crests, south-facing slopes, wind-swept areas) favorable for seedling establishment. Approximately 50-80% of cached seeds are retrieved; the remainder germinate, establishing the next generation of whitebark pine. The decline of whitebark pine from blister rust has reduced seed availability for nutcrackers, causing population declines and range contractions. In areas where whitebark pine has been eliminated, nutcrackers have disappeared entirely, halting natural regeneration and converting subalpine pine forest to spruce-fir or alpine meadow. This mutualism collapse is one of the clearest examples of coextinction risk in North American ecology [P10, G1].

### American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Corvus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed (abundant)
- State OR: Not listed (abundant)
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 40-53 cm (15.7-20.9 in)
- **Wingspan:** 85-100 cm (33.5-39.4 in)
- **Mass:** 316-620 g (11.1-21.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Entirely glossy black with slight purplish iridescence. Bill, legs, and feet black. Distinguished from Common Raven by smaller size (approximately half Raven's mass), shorter and straighter bill (Raven's bill is massive and curved), squared tail in flight (Raven's is wedge-shaped), and different flight profile (Crow: steady wingbeats, flap-flap-flap; Raven: soaring, gliding, acrobatic).
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Minimal — males average slightly larger. No plumage differences.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Extremely broad habitat tolerance — urban, suburban, agricultural, woodland edge, riparian, coastal. Avoids dense forest interior and extreme alpine. Has expanded dramatically into urban environments over the past century. The most urbanized corvid in the PNW.
- **Wintering:** Same habitat, with concentration into large communal roosts (hundreds to thousands of individuals) in urban areas during winter. Seattle, Portland, and other PNW cities host crow winter roosts of 5,000-50,000+ individuals.
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,000 m (6,500 ft), concentrated below 1,000 m
- **Ecoregion primary:** Zone 3 (Lowland/Urban)
- **Ecoregion secondary:** Zone 4 (Foothill), Zone 5 (Riparian), Zone 7 (East-Side — agricultural)
- **Microhabitat:** Highly flexible — forages in open ground, lawns, parking lots, agricultural fields, mudflats, roadsides, garbage areas. Nests in trees (both conifer and deciduous), 5-20 m above ground.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Omnivorous — agricultural grain, invertebrates (earthworms, insects, crustaceans), human food refuse, carrion, eggs and nestlings of other birds, small vertebrates, fruits, nuts. One of the broadest diets of any PNW bird.
- **Foraging strategy:** Highly flexible and intelligent foraging. Uses tools (sticks to extract food from crevices), drops hard-shelled prey (walnuts, shellfish) from height onto pavement to crack them, caches food, raids other birds' nests, scavenges roadkill, exploits garbage containers. Forages both individually and in cooperative groups.
- **Seasonal variation:** More reliant on invertebrates and nesting bird eggs in spring-summer. Grain, nuts, and human refuse more important in fall-winter.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Stick platform with cup lined with bark, grass, hair, and soft materials. Placed in tree fork, 5-20 m. Both sexes build, but female does most construction.
- **Clutch size:** 3-6 eggs (typically 4-5)
- **Incubation:** 16-18 days; female only (male feeds female)
- **Fledging:** 28-35 days; young remain with parents and may assist with subsequent nesting (cooperative breeding with helpers)
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** March-June in PNW

#### Population

- **Trend:** Stable to increasing in PNW — urban populations expanding. West Nile Virus caused significant mortality when it arrived in the PNW (~2005-2007), with local populations declining 30-45% in some areas. Populations have largely recovered [G4].
- **Estimate:** 27 million continent-wide [O4]. Abundant throughout PNW lowlands.
- **Threats:** West Nile Virus (corvids are among the most susceptible bird families — 100% mortality in experimental infection), illegal shooting, poisoning, vehicle collisions [G4].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Abundant | Human commensal, nest predator, scavenger |
| Zone 4: Foothill | Year-round | Common | Generalist |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Common | Riparian generalist |
| Zone 7: East-Side (agricultural) | Year-round | Common | Agricultural generalist |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** The familiar *caw-caw-caw* — the most recognized bird vocalization in North America. Highly variable — at least 20 distinct caw variants documented, encoding information about predator type, predator urgency, individual identity, and social context.
- **Scolding/mobbing:** Rapid, harsh cawing in dense sequences when mobbing raptors (especially owls and hawks). Recruits other crows from hundreds of meters.
- **Soft vocalizations:** Complex repertoire of rattles, clicks, coos, and structured sequences used in pair bonding and social interactions.

#### Evolutionary Note

American Crows are members of the cosmopolitan genus *Corvus* (~45 species worldwide), which includes ravens, crows, rooks, and jackdaws. The genus originated in Central Asia ~17 million years ago and colonized North America via Beringia. American Crow is sister to the Northwestern Crow (*C. caurinus*) — see Northwestern Crow entry for the ongoing taxonomic debate about their species-level distinction. The Corvus radiation represents one of the most successful avian diversifications: the genus occupies every continent except South America and Antarctica, and includes species ranging from 150 g (Little Crow) to 1,500 g (Common Raven) [O1].

#### Cognitive Ecology

The American Crow is among the most intensively studied bird species for cognitive abilities. Research at the University of Washington (Seattle) by John Marzluff and colleagues has documented:

- **Facial recognition:** Crows recognize individual human faces and retain recognition for at least 5 years. Crows that experienced capture by a researcher wearing a specific mask subsequently scolded and mobbed anyone wearing that mask, and transmitted this knowledge to crows that had never been captured — cultural transmission of threat information [P19].
- **Tool use:** PNW crows use sticks, leaves, and other objects to extract food. Tool use is flexible and context-dependent, not stereotyped.
- **Future planning:** Crows cache food in locations and quantities adjusted for anticipated future need — evidence of episodic-like future planning.
- **Social learning:** Juvenile crows learn foraging techniques, predator recognition, and territory boundaries from parents and family groups during a 1-2 year association period.
- **Communal roosting:** Winter roosts function as information centers — naive birds follow successful foragers from roost to food sources. Roost locations are culturally transmitted across generations.

These cognitive capabilities, combined with extreme dietary flexibility and social complexity, make American Crows a model organism for studying intelligence convergence between avian and mammalian brains. Crows and great apes represent independent evolutionary experiments in complex cognition, achieved through entirely different neural architectures (avian pallium vs. mammalian neocortex) [P19, O1].

### Common Raven (*Corvus corax*) — EXTENDED

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Corvus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:**
- Federal ESA: Not listed
- IUCN: Least Concern
- State WA: Not listed
- State OR: Not listed
- PIF: Low concern

#### Morphometrics

- **Length:** 54-67 cm (21.3-26.4 in) — largest passerine in the world
- **Wingspan:** 115-150 cm (45.3-59.1 in)
- **Mass:** 689-1,625 g (1.5-3.6 lb) — mass range overlaps with many raptors
- **Plumage:** Entirely glossy black with strong purplish-blue iridescence. Massive, heavy bill with curved culmen (upper ridge). Shaggy throat hackles (elongated throat feathers that can be erected in display). Wedge-shaped tail (diagnostic in flight vs. Crow's squared tail). Long, broad wings capable of sustained soaring and acrobatic flight.
- **Sexual dimorphism:** Males average larger (especially bill) but overlap extensive. No plumage differences.

#### Habitat

- **Breeding:** Extremely broad — cliff ledges, large trees (conifer and deciduous), communication towers, bridge structures, building ledges. Occupies virtually every habitat from alpine peaks to coastal sea stacks to desert canyons to urban cores. The most habitat-generalist large bird in the PNW.
- **Wintering:** Same — non-migratory with occasional short-distance movements. Concentrates near food sources (landfills, agricultural operations, road corridors with roadkill).
- **Elevation range:** Sea level to >4,300 m (14,000 ft) — the broadest elevational range of any PNW passerine
- **Ecoregion primary:** All zones — the only PNW bird species equally at home in all seven ecoregion zones
- **Microhabitat:** Cliff ledges and tall structures for nesting; open ground for foraging; updrafts and thermals for soaring. Ravens regularly soar on thermals for hours, covering vast distances.

#### Diet and Foraging

- **Primary diet:** Omnivorous and opportunistic to an extreme degree — carrion (the most important food source in many areas), rodents, insects, eggs and nestlings, grain, garbage, human food waste, marine invertebrates, fish, reptiles, amphibians, berries, and virtually any edible organic material.
- **Foraging strategy:** Highly intelligent and flexible. Kleptoparasitism (stealing food from other predators — documented stealing from eagles, hawks, wolves, and bears), cooperative hunting (pairs coordinate to raid nests — one distracts parent while the other takes eggs), tool use (using sticks and stones), food caching, dumpster raiding, roadkill scavenging. Ravens have been documented rolling up fishing lines to access bait.
- **Seasonal variation:** Carrion more important in winter; invertebrates and nesting bird prey more important in spring-summer. Alpine and steppe populations exploit winter bighorn sheep and deer carcasses.

#### Reproduction

- **Nest type:** Massive stick platform (up to 1.5 m across) lined with wool, fur, bark, and grass. Placed on cliff ledge (primary), tall conifer, communication tower, or bridge structure. 5-30 m above ground. Pairs may reuse and add to the same nest structure for years.
- **Clutch size:** 3-7 eggs (typically 4-6)
- **Incubation:** 20-25 days; female only (male feeds female on nest)
- **Fledging:** 38-44 days — among the longest fledging periods of any PNW passerine
- **Broods per year:** 1
- **Breeding season:** February-May in PNW (early nester — eggs often laid while snow still deep)

#### Population

- **Trend:** Increasing — BBS trend +2.5% per year in PNW (1966-2019). Ravens have expanded dramatically into urban and suburban areas over the past 40 years, following the expansion of food subsidies (landfills, agricultural waste, roadkill corridors). Historically restricted to wild and rural areas; now regular in city centers [G4].
- **Estimate:** 20 million continent-wide [O4]. Increasingly common throughout PNW.
- **Threats:** Ravens are currently so abundant that they are considered a conservation concern as predators — their population increase is a threat to ESA-listed species including Greater Sage-Grouse (nest predation), Marbled Murrelet (nest predation), and desert tortoises (predation on juveniles). Raven management (subsidized predator reduction) is an active and controversial conservation management tool [G1, G4].

#### Ecoregion Presence

| Zone | Season | Abundance | Role |
|------|--------|-----------|------|
| Zone 1: Alpine/Subalpine | Year-round | Uncommon | Scavenger, predator |
| Zone 2: Montane Conifer | Year-round | Fairly common | Nest predator, scavenger |
| Zone 3: Lowland/Urban | Year-round | Common, increasing | Human-commensal generalist |
| Zone 4: Foothill/Oak | Year-round | Common | Generalist |
| Zone 5: Riparian | Year-round | Common | Generalist, salmon carcass scavenger |
| Zone 6: Coastal/Marine | Year-round | Common | Intertidal forager, nest predator |
| Zone 7: East-Side Steppe | Year-round | Common | Steppe predator and scavenger |

#### Vocalizations

- **Primary call:** Deep, resonant *cronk* or *pruk* — lower-pitched and more guttural than any Crow vocalization. Highly variable — at least 30 distinct call types documented.
- **Aerial displays:** Ravens in courtship perform paired aerial acrobatics — barrel rolls, half-rolls, inverted flight, synchronized diving and climbing — accompanied by complex vocalizations including clicks, knocks, bell-like tones, and gurgling sequences.
- **"Talking" ability:** Ravens in captivity can learn to mimic human speech with remarkable accuracy. Wild ravens occasionally incorporate novel sounds (car alarms, other bird species, mechanical sounds) into their vocal repertoire.

#### Evolutionary Note

The Common Raven has the largest natural range of any passerine — Holarctic distribution spanning North America, Europe, Asia, and North Africa. PNW populations belong to the *C. c. sinuatus* subspecies (western North America). The species' success is attributed to extreme behavioral flexibility, problem-solving ability, and generalist diet — a cognitive generalist strategy that has allowed ravens to occupy every terrestrial habitat from Arctic tundra to Sonoran desert. Molecular studies suggest that North American and Eurasian raven populations diverged relatively recently (~2 million years ago) and may warrant subspecific reevaluation [O1].

#### Relationship with Crows

Ravens and American Crows overlap broadly in the PNW lowlands, creating a two-corvid system where the two species partition resources by size and habitat structure. Ravens dominate crows in direct encounters (body mass advantage), take larger food items (carrion, large invertebrates), and nest on cliffs and tall structures that crows cannot access. Crows are more numerous in dense urban areas, form larger social flocks, and are more effective at mobbing predators (numerical advantage). The recent expansion of ravens into urban areas has increased the spatial overlap between these two species, and competitive interactions are intensifying in suburban zones [O1, P19].

### Black-billed Magpie (*Pica hudsonia*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Pica*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 45-60 cm (17.7-23.6 in) — long tail accounts for >50% of total length
- **Wingspan:** 56-61 cm (22.0-24.0 in)
- **Mass:** 145-210 g (5.1-7.4 oz)
- **Plumage:** Boldly patterned — black head, breast, and back; white belly and scapulars; long iridescent blue-green tail (greenish in good light); iridescent blue-green wings with large white patches visible in flight.

**Habitat:** Open country with scattered trees and shrubs — ranch land, sagebrush with riparian thickets, agricultural land with hedgerows, suburban edges on east side. Avoids dense forest. Uncommon to absent on west side of Cascades.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~2,000 m (6,500 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — primary), Zone 5 (Riparian — east-side), Zone 4 (Foothill — east-side)

**Diet and Foraging:** Omnivorous — insects (grasshoppers major prey), carrion, grain, fruit, eggs, small vertebrates, garbage. Regularly rides on livestock to pick ticks and insects. Scavenges roadkill. Caches food in ground and vegetation.

**Reproduction:** Massive domed stick nest (up to 1 m tall) with side entrance, placed in dense shrub or tree, 1-10 m. One of the most elaborate nest structures of any North American bird. Clutch 6-9; incubation 16-21 days; fledging 24-30 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable in PNW range [G4]. Common in east-side open country.

**Vocalizations:** Rapidly repeated *mag-mag-mag* or *yak-yak-yak* — harsh, nasal, and persistent. Complex vocabulary of calls in social contexts.

**Evolutionary note:** Black-billed Magpie was split from Eurasian Magpie (*P. pica*) by AOS in 2000 based on molecular evidence showing the two are not sister species — the similar plumage pattern evolved convergently. The North American and Eurasian magpie lineages diverged ~4-5 million years ago. Black-billed Magpie is more closely related to Yellow-billed Magpie (*P. nuttalli* — California endemic) than to the Eurasian species [O1, O3].

### Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Perisoreus*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 25-33 cm (9.8-13.0 in)
- **Wingspan:** 45-46 cm (17.7-18.1 in)
- **Mass:** 62-82 g (2.2-2.9 oz)
- **Plumage:** Gray above, whitish below, with dark gray or blackish nape and partial cap contrasting with white forehead. Fluffy, loose feathering gives a round-headed, soft appearance. PNW birds (subspecies *P. c. obscurus* — Oregon and Washington Cascades) are darker overall with more extensive dark cap than boreal populations. No crest.

**Habitat:** Dense conifer forest at mid- to high elevation — subalpine fir, spruce, mountain hemlock, Pacific silver fir. Prefers cool, moist conifer forest. The quintessential bird of the deep PNW mountain forest. Formerly called "Gray Jay" — renamed Canada Jay by AOS in 2018 (restoring the original common name used from 1831 to 1957).
**Elevation range:** 600-2,700 m (2,000-8,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 1 (Subalpine — primary), Zone 2 (Montane Conifer — common)

**Diet and Foraging:** Omnivorous — insects, berries, fungi, carrion, nestling birds, cached food. Famous for caching food using sticky saliva to glue food items (berries, meat, insects) to conifer branches, bark crevices, and lichen clusters. Unlike most corvid cachers (which bury items in soil), Canada Jays use above-ground adhesive caching — an adaptation for snowy environments where ground caches would be inaccessible. A single jay may create thousands of caches in fall for winter retrieval.

**Reproduction:** Notably early nester — incubation often begins in late February or March while deep snow persists. Nest is a well-insulated cup of bark strips, lichens, and caterpillar silk in dense conifer near trunk. Clutch 2-5; incubation 18-19 days; fledging 22-24 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable but potential climate vulnerability — southern range margin populations may be declining as warming temperatures spoil cached food (bacterial and fungal degradation of cached food increases with temperature) [G4, O4].

**Vocalizations:** Quiet, varied — soft *whee-ah* whistles, clucks, and chatters. Much less raucous than Steller's Jay. Also mimics other species. Approaches humans boldly and silently.

**Evolutionary note:** Canada Jay's food-caching strategy — saliva-bonded above-ground caches in conifer bark — is unique among corvids and represents an adaptation to the deep-snow environments of boreal and subalpine forest. The species' remarkably early breeding (eggs in February-March) is timed to produce young that have the entire spring-summer to learn caching behavior and build food stores for their first winter. Climate warming threatens this species through a mechanism unique to scatter-hoarding corvids: warmer autumn temperatures cause cached food to rot before winter, reducing overwinter survival of dependent juveniles. This "refrigerator failure" hypothesis links climate change to Canada Jay population decline at the southern range margin [O1, O4].

### California Scrub-Jay (*Aphelocoma californica*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Aphelocoma*
**Status:** Year-round resident
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Low concern

#### Profile

- **Length:** 27-31 cm (10.6-12.2 in)
- **Wingspan:** 39-41 cm (15.4-16.1 in)
- **Mass:** 70-100 g (2.5-3.5 oz)
- **Plumage:** Blue head, wings, and tail; gray-brown back; white throat with blue necklace; pale gray underparts. No crest (distinguishes from Steller's Jay at a glance). Long tail. Formerly lumped with Woodhouse's Scrub-Jay as "Western Scrub-Jay."

**Habitat:** Oak woodland, scrubby chaparral, suburban gardens, parks. In PNW, restricted to Oregon (Rogue Valley, Umpqua, Willamette Valley margins) and barely into southwestern Washington. Expanding northward. More open-habitat and lower-elevation than Steller's Jay, with minimal range overlap.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~1,200 m (3,900 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 4 (Foothill/Oak — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland/Suburban — expanding)

**Diet and Foraging:** Acorns (primary — cached extensively), insects, berries, eggs, nestlings, small vertebrates, human-provided food. Caches thousands of acorns per year in soil, contributing to oak regeneration.

**Reproduction:** Open cup in dense shrub or tree, 1-10 m; clutch 3-6; incubation 15-17 days; fledging 18 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Stable to increasing in PNW — expanding northward, possibly facilitated by warming climate and suburban habitat expansion [G4].

**Vocalizations:** Harsh, rising *shreep-shreep-shreep* — less rhythmic than Steller's Jay. Also a rapid, scratchy rattle.

**Evolutionary note:** Split from Woodhouse's Scrub-Jay (*A. woodhouseii*) by AOS in 2016. California Scrub-Jay is the coastal/lowland species; Woodhouse's is the interior/arid species. In the PNW, only California Scrub-Jay occurs. The *Aphelocoma* genus demonstrates one of the best-documented cases of episodic-like memory in non-human animals — California Scrub-Jays remember what food they cached, where they cached it, and when they cached it, adjusting retrieval behavior based on the perishability of cached items (retrieving perishable items first) [O1, O3].

### Pinyon Jay (*Gymnorhinus cyanocephalus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Gymnorhinus*
**Status:** Irregular visitor / rare resident at eastern PNW margins
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: Vulnerable (rangewide decline) | PIF: Red Watch List

#### Profile

- **Length:** 25-28 cm (9.8-11.0 in)
- **Wingspan:** 43-46 cm (16.9-18.1 in)
- **Mass:** 90-120 g (3.2-4.2 oz)
- **Plumage:** Entirely dull blue (duller than other blue PNW corvids), slightly darker on head. Short tail, long pointed bill. No crest. Gregarious — always in flocks.

**Habitat:** Pinyon-juniper woodland — at the extreme eastern margin of PNW range in eastern Oregon (juniper steppe) and southern Idaho. A Great Basin species whose range barely enters the PNW study area. Obligate associate of pinyon pine (*Pinus monophylla*, *P. edulis*) where available; uses western juniper and ponderosa pine in PNW.
**Elevation range:** 1,000-2,500 m (3,300-8,200 ft)
**Ecoregions:** Zone 7 (East-Side Steppe — rare, peripheral)

**Diet and Foraging:** Pinyon pine seeds (obligate when available); juniper berries; insects. Highly social — forages, breeds, and roosts in large flocks of 50-500. Caches seeds communally.

**Reproduction:** Colonial nester — 10-150 nests per colony in juniper/pine. Clutch 3-6; incubation 16 days; fledging 21 days; 1 brood. Breeding timing varies dramatically by cone crop.

**Population:** Declining severely rangewide — BBS trend -3.5% per year (1966-2019). Listed as Vulnerable by IUCN in 2023 due to ~70% population decline. PNW populations marginal and sporadic [G4, O4].

**Vocalizations:** Mewing *kraw-kraw* and nasal laughing calls. Flock vocalizations constant and far-carrying.

**Evolutionary note:** Pinyon Jay is the only member of genus *Gymnorhinus* — a monotypic corvid genus endemic to western North America. The long, pointed bill (lacking the feathered nostrils found in other jays) is adapted for probing pine cones to extract seeds. Like Clark's Nutcracker, Pinyon Jay is an obligate mutualist with large-seeded pines and faces population decline as pinyon-juniper woodland is degraded. However, unlike Clark's Nutcracker, Pinyon Jay is a highly social species — flocks maintain complex social hierarchies, and colonial breeding is obligate. This sociality makes the species vulnerable to Allee effects (population declines below the threshold needed for effective flock function) [O1].

### Northwestern Crow (*Corvus caurinus*)

**Taxonomic position:** Order Passeriformes, Family Corvidae, Genus *Corvus*
**Status:** Year-round resident — **taxonomic status under active debate**
**Conservation status:** Federal ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC | PIF: Not assessed separately from American Crow

#### Profile

- **Length:** 40-45 cm (15.7-17.7 in) — averaging slightly smaller than American Crow
- **Wingspan:** 83-93 cm (32.7-36.6 in)
- **Mass:** 280-450 g (9.9-15.9 oz) — averaging slightly smaller than American Crow
- **Plumage:** Entirely glossy black — indistinguishable from American Crow by plumage. Averages slightly smaller with a slightly lower-pitched, hoarser voice, but overlap in all characters is extensive.

**Habitat:** Strictly coastal — tidal zones, beaches, rocky intertidal, coastal towns, fish processing areas, harbors. In the PNW, restricted to a narrow coastal strip from southern Alaska through BC to the Puget Sound region. Replaces American Crow in coastal habitat (American Crow dominates inland). The two species co-occur and apparently hybridize extensively in the Puget Sound lowlands.
**Elevation range:** Sea level to ~100 m — the most elevation-restricted PNW corvid
**Ecoregions:** Zone 6 (Coastal — primary), Zone 3 (Lowland — coastal strip)

**Diet and Foraging:** Marine invertebrates (crabs, mussels, clams, snails, sea urchins), fish, marine carrion, intertidal organisms. Drops shellfish from height onto rocks to crack them (same technique as American Crow but more frequently employed). Also takes garbage, human food, and small terrestrial prey.

**Reproduction:** Similar to American Crow — stick nest in tree, 5-15 m; clutch 4-5; incubation 17-18 days; fledging ~30 days; 1 brood.

**Population:** Uncertain due to taxonomic ambiguity. If treated as a species, population is confined to coastal Pacific Northwest and may be declining due to hybridization with expanding American Crow populations [O1].

**Vocalizations:** Slightly lower-pitched, hoarser *caw* than American Crow — but extensive overlap makes vocal separation unreliable except in direct comparison.

**Evolutionary note:** The Northwestern Crow represents one of the most contentious taxonomic questions in PNW ornithology. The AOS currently recognizes it as a full species based on the original 1858 description and ecological differences (coastal habitat, marine diet, slightly smaller size, lower-pitched voice). However, genomic studies have found no consistent genetic differentiation between Northwestern Crow and American Crow — mitochondrial and nuclear DNA markers show extensive gene flow across the contact zone. Many contemporary ornithologists (including the eBird/Clements taxonomy) have recommended lumping Northwestern Crow with American Crow, treating it as a coastal ecotype rather than a biological species. AOS has deferred formal action pending comprehensive genomic analysis across the entire range. The Northwestern Crow case illustrates the fundamental difficulty of defining species boundaries in recently diverged, geographically adjacent lineages [O1, O3, P20].
