## Introduction: Birds as Network Architects

An ecosystem is not a list of species. It is a web of relationships — and birds are among the most prolific architects of that web.

In the Pacific Northwest, birds function simultaneously as apex predators, mesopredators, seed dispersers, pollinators, nutrient vectors, cavity engineers, sentinel species, and information relays. A single Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*) excavating a nest cavity in a dying Douglas-fir initiates a cascade of ecological consequences that ripples through the forest for decades: the cavity houses owls, then ducks, then bats; the excavation accelerates wood decay; the decay releases nutrients into the soil; the weakened snag eventually falls and becomes nurse log habitat for the next generation of conifers. One bird, one action, a hundred downstream effects.

This document maps the network connections — the wires — that PNW birds create, maintain, and depend upon. Where the species profiles in Modules 1-4 describe individual nodes, this module describes the topology of the graph. The organizational framework follows seven major network types:

1. **Trophic Networks** — who eats whom, and what regulates what
2. **Seed Dispersal Networks** — who carries seeds, and where they end up
3. **Pollination Networks** — nectar feeders as pollen vectors
4. **Nutrient Cycling Networks** — the salmon thread and beyond
5. **Cavity Nester Guild Networks** — the woodpecker economy
6. **Mixed-Species Flock Networks** — information transfer through social structure
7. **Ecosystem Engineering** — birds as physical habitat modifiers

An eighth section addresses **Network Resilience** — what happens when nodes are removed, connections severed, or timing disrupted.

**Design principle:** Every network claim names specific species, cites specific sources, and connects to specific profiles in Modules 1-4 and to the ECO/MAM research series. No vague "birds are important" generalizations. The network is concrete or it is not documented.

## 1. Trophic Networks

Trophic networks describe the flow of energy through an ecosystem via predator-prey relationships. PNW avian trophic networks span at least five trophic levels — from primary producers (seeds, fruits, nectar consumed by granivores and nectarivores) through primary consumers (herbivorous and insectivorous birds), secondary consumers (raptors feeding on songbirds), tertiary consumers (Great Horned Owls feeding on other raptors), and the scavenger guild that processes energy from carcasses at every trophic level.

### 1.1 Raptor Food Webs

The PNW raptor community constitutes the most complex avian trophic network in the region, with 32 species profiled in `raptors.md` occupying positions from mesopredator to apex. The raptor food web is structured by three axes of niche partitioning: prey type, habitat, and temporal activity period (diurnal vs. nocturnal).

#### Apex Predators — Top of the Avian Trophic Pyramid

**Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*)** — The dominant apex avian predator in PNW riparian and coastal ecosystems. Prey base is extraordinarily broad: Pacific salmon (primary — see Section 4), waterfowl (American Coot, Mallard, American Wigeon, Canada Goose goslings), medium mammals (Snowshoe Hare, muskrat, cottontails), marine fish, and carrion. The Bald Eagle occupies a unique dual role as both active predator and obligate scavenger, switching between modes seasonally. Winter salmon carcass scavenging (see Section 4) makes the Bald Eagle a keystone nutrient vector. (See `raptors.md`: Bald Eagle; sources G-14, G-20, P-18.)

**Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)** — Apex predator of PNW open landscapes — alpine meadows, sagebrush steppe, Columbia Plateau grasslands. Primary prey: marmots (Yellow-bellied Marmot *Marmota flaviventris*, Hoary Marmot *Marmota caligata*), jackrabbits (Black-tailed Jackrabbit *Lepus californicus*, White-tailed Jackrabbit *Lepus townsendii*), and ground squirrels (Columbian Ground Squirrel *Urocitellus columbianus*). Exerts strong top-down pressure on colonial ground squirrel populations and marmot colonies, with documented local population regulation in alpine habitats. (See `raptors.md`: Golden Eagle; sources G-14, G-16, O-21.)

**Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*)** — The nocturnal apex predator, with the broadest prey base of any PNW raptor. Kills and consumes other raptors including Red-tailed Hawks, Barred Owls, and Long-eared Owls. Prey spans from insects to skunks (up to 1.8 kg). The Great Horned Owl is the primary nocturnal regulator of medium mammal populations and exerts intraguild predation pressure on every smaller owl species in the PNW. (See `raptors.md`: Great Horned Owl; sources G-14, O-07.)

#### Mesopredator Raptors — Mid-Trophic Specialists

The mesopredator raptor guild includes species that are predators of small vertebrates but also prey for apex raptors. This dual position creates the mesopredator release dynamic discussed in Section 8.

**Accipiter Guild — Forest Interior Specialists:**
- **Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*)** — Apex forest raptor; takes grouse, squirrels, rabbits, and other birds up to duck size. Old-growth specialist. (See `raptors.md`; source G-24.)
- **Cooper's Hawk (*Accipiter cooperii*)** — Medium songbird specialist (jays, robins, flickers); primary avian predator in suburban woodlands. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)
- **Sharp-shinned Hawk (*Accipiter striatus*)** — Small songbird specialist (sparrows, warblers, chickadees); the smallest accipiter, filling the niche below Cooper's Hawk. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)

The three accipiters demonstrate classic body-size niche partitioning: Sharp-shinned Hawk (87-218 g) takes prey up to ~60 g, Cooper's Hawk (220-680 g) takes prey up to ~350 g, and Northern Goshawk (630-1,364 g) takes prey up to ~1,500 g. This partitioning reduces interspecific competition while maintaining predation pressure across the full range of forest bird and mammal body sizes.

**Buteo Guild — Open Country Soarers:**
- **Red-tailed Hawk (*Buteo jamaicensis*)** — The most abundant large raptor in the PNW. Generalist predator of voles, gophers, ground squirrels, rabbits, and snakes. Population density: estimated 1 pair per 1-3 km^2 in suitable habitat. The Red-tailed Hawk defines the baseline predation rate on small mammal populations across PNW lowlands and open montane habitats. (See `raptors.md`; source G-14, O-07.)
- **Rough-legged Hawk (*Buteo lagopus*)** — Arctic breeder, PNW winter visitor. Specializes on voles and lemmings on the breeding grounds; in the PNW winters on open agricultural land and grassland, where it supplements Red-tailed Hawk predation on vole populations during winter months. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)
- **Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*)** — East-side steppe specialist. Primarily jackrabbits and ground squirrels in sagebrush and grassland. WA State Threatened. Population closely tracks jackrabbit abundance cycles. (See `raptors.md`; source G-14, G-16.)
- **Swainson's Hawk (*Buteo swainsoni*)** — Neotropical migrant; east-side PNW breeder. Unusual diet shift: primarily mammals (ground squirrels, voles) during breeding season but switches to insects (grasshoppers, dragonflies) during migration and on South American wintering grounds. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)

**Falcon Guild — Aerial Pursuit Specialists:**
- **Peregrine Falcon (*Falco peregrinus*)** — The fastest animal on Earth (documented stoops exceeding 240 mph). Prey: medium birds (pigeons, shorebirds, ducks) taken in aerial pursuit. PNW populations recovered dramatically after DDT ban. Cliff-nesting and increasingly urban bridge-nesting. (See `raptors.md`; source G-14, G-17.)
- **Merlin (*Falco columbarius*)** — Small bird specialist; takes sparrows, warblers, swallows in rapid tail-chase pursuit through open and edge habitats. Winter visitor and rare breeder in PNW. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)
- **American Kestrel (*Falco sparverius*)** — Smallest PNW falcon. Diet split: insects (grasshoppers, beetles) in summer, small mammals (voles, mice) in winter. Cavity nester (see Section 5). Declining across North America — down 50% since 1966 per BBS data (source: G-18). The kestrel decline is one of the most concerning raptor population trends in North America, with causes debated: habitat loss, West Nile Virus, Cooper's Hawk predation pressure, and pesticide exposure are all implicated.
- **Prairie Falcon (*Falco mexicanus*)** — East-side cliff nester; takes ground squirrels and small birds. Overlaps with Peregrine in cliff nesting habitat but partitions by prey: Prairie Falcon hunts terrestrially while Peregrine hunts aerially. (See `raptors.md`; source G-16.)

#### Nocturnal Raptor Web — Owl Guild Trophic Structure

The PNW owl community (14 species profiled in `raptors.md`) forms a parallel nocturnal trophic web that complements the diurnal raptor guild. Niche partitioning among owls follows body size, habitat, and prey type:

| Size Class | Species | Primary Prey | Habitat |
|-----------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Large (>800 g) | Great Horned Owl, Snowy Owl, Great Gray Owl | Rabbits, hares, voles, other raptors | Generalist / Open / Forest edge |
| Medium (300-800 g) | Barred Owl, Spotted Owl, Long-eared Owl, Short-eared Owl | Flying squirrels, woodrats, voles | Forest / Open grassland |
| Small (100-300 g) | Western Screech-Owl, Boreal Owl, Barn Owl | Mice, voles, insects | Forest edge / Agricultural |
| Very Small (<100 g) | Northern Saw-whet Owl, Northern Pygmy-Owl, Flammulated Owl | Mice, insects, small birds | Forest / Montane |

**Key intraguild predation links:**
- Great Horned Owl preys on Barred Owl, Long-eared Owl, and Western Screech-Owl (source: O-07)
- Barred Owl outcompetes Northern Spotted Owl through both interference competition and some hybridization (source: P-13, P-14) — see Section 8
- Northern Pygmy-Owl, despite weighing only 60-75 g, takes prey up to its own body mass including small songbirds, functioning as a diurnal-crepuscular "micro-raptor" that elicits mobbing responses from passerine flocks (source: O-07)

### 1.2 Insectivore Guilds and Pest Regulation

Insectivorous birds provide ecosystem services valued at billions of dollars annually through suppression of pest insect populations. In the PNW, insectivore guilds partition the insect resource along three axes: foraging stratum (ground, bark, foliage, aerial), prey type (caterpillars, beetles, flies, ants, spiders), and temporal activity (diurnal vs. crepuscular).

#### Aerial Insectivores — The Declining Guild

Aerial insectivores — birds that catch insects on the wing — have experienced steeper population declines than any other North American bird guild over the past 50 years (source: G-18, O-04). PNW species include:

- **Vaux's Swift (*Chaetura vauxi*)** — Forages high in the aerial column; nests in hollow old-growth snags and chimneys. Gregarious roosting in migration (thousands in single chimney). Declining with loss of old-growth snag habitat. (See `resident.md`; source G-14.)
- **Olive-sided Flycatcher (*Contopus cooperi*)** — 79% population decline since 1970. Forages from exposed treetop perches in montane burned and logged areas. WA State Species of Concern, IUCN Near Threatened, PIF Yellow Watch List. (See `migratory.md`; source G-18.)
- **Western Wood-Pewee (*Contopus sordidulus*)** — Mid-canopy sally-gleaner in open conifer forests. (See `migratory.md`; source G-18.)
- **Violet-green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*)**, **Tree Swallow (*Tachycineta bicolor*)**, **Barn Swallow (*Hirundo rustica*)**, **Cliff Swallow (*Petrochelidon pyrrhonota*)**, **Bank Swallow (*Riparia riparia*)**, **Northern Rough-winged Swallow (*Stelgidopteryx serripennis*)** — Six swallow species partition aerial insect habitat by flight altitude, habitat association, and nest type. All are declining (source: G-18).

The cause of aerial insectivore decline is debated and likely multi-factorial: reduced insect biomass (the "insect apocalypse" hypothesis), neonicotinoid pesticide exposure, climate-driven phenological mismatch between insect emergence and bird arrival/breeding, and habitat loss at both breeding and wintering sites (source: O-04, G-18).

**Pest regulation service:** A single Violet-green Swallow consumes an estimated 2,000-3,000 flying insects per day during the breeding season when feeding nestlings. Extrapolated across PNW breeding populations, swallows alone remove billions of mosquitoes, midges, and agricultural pest flies annually (source: O-07).

#### Bark Probers — The Woodpecker Pest Control Network

Woodpeckers (family Picidae — 11 PNW species) provide critical pest regulation in forests by consuming bark beetles (Dendroctonus, Ips, Scolytus), wood-boring beetle larvae (Cerambycidae, Buprestidae), and carpenter ants (Camponotus spp.). Woodpecker predation is one of the few natural checks on bark beetle outbreaks that kill millions of trees across PNW forests.

- **Black-backed Woodpecker (*Picoides arcticus*)** — Post-fire specialist. Consumes wood-boring beetle larvae (especially Cerambycidae) that colonize fire-killed trees. One of the few bird species that increases in abundance after wildfire. Exerts significant predation on beetle larvae in the 2-5 year post-fire window. (See `resident.md`; source G-22.)
- **Three-toed Woodpecker (*Picoides dorsalis*)** — Bark-flaking specialist in high-elevation spruce-fir forests. Primary predator of Engelmann spruce beetle (*Dendroctonus rufipennis*) larvae. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Hairy Woodpecker (*Leuconotopicus villosus*)** — Generalist bark prober; takes both bark beetles and wood-boring larvae across all forest types. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*)** — Carpenter ant specialist. Excavates deep rectangular holes in snags and logs to access Camponotus colonies. A single Pileated Woodpecker territory may contain 40-60 carpenter ant colonies; the bird's foraging activity controls ant populations and slows wood decomposition rates by reducing ant biomass. (See `resident.md`; source O-07, G-22.)
- **Downy Woodpecker (*Dryobates pubescens*)** — Smallest PNW woodpecker; forages on small branches and weed stems that larger woodpeckers cannot access. Takes small beetle larvae and caterpillars. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Forest health service:** In bark beetle outbreak zones, woodpecker predation can reduce beetle larvae survival by 40-99% in individual trees (source: G-22). Post-fire Black-backed Woodpecker populations track beetle larvae abundance, creating a natural negative feedback on beetle outbreak intensity. This ecological service has direct economic value: bark beetle outbreaks cost the U.S. timber industry billions of dollars annually, and woodpecker predation reduces the scale and duration of outbreaks.

#### Foliage Gleaners — The Caterpillar Patrol

Warblers (family Parulidae), vireos (family Vireonidae), kinglets (family Regulidae), and chickadees (family Paridae) form the foliage-gleaning insectivore guild — birds that pick caterpillars, aphids, and spiders from leaves and twigs.

- **Black-capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*)** and **Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*)** — Year-round residents that consume enormous quantities of lepidopteran larvae, aphids, and scale insects. Chickadees are estimated to consume 6,000-9,000 caterpillars to raise a single brood (source: O-07). As nuclear species in mixed flocks (see Section 6), chickadees organize the distribution of gleaning effort across the forest canopy.
- **Ruby-crowned Kinglet (*Corthylio calendula*)** and **Golden-crowned Kinglet (*Regulus satrapa*)** — Tiny (5-7 g) year-round gleaners specializing on insect eggs, larvae, and spiders gleaned from conifer needle clusters. Golden-crowned Kinglet is the smallest PNW passerine and must eat nearly continuously during winter daylight hours to survive. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Orange-crowned Warbler (*Leiothlypis celata*)**, **Yellow-rumped Warbler (*Setophaga coronata*)**, **Townsend's Warbler (*Setophaga townsendi*)**, **Wilson's Warbler (*Cardellina pusilla*)** — Neotropical migrants that partition the foliage-gleaning niche by canopy height: Townsend's in upper canopy, Orange-crowned in mid-canopy, Wilson's in understory shrubs. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07, G-18.)

**Pest regulation service:** Exclosure experiments (where birds are excluded from tree canopies using netting) consistently demonstrate that insectivorous birds reduce caterpillar abundance by 40-70% and measurably increase tree foliage retention and growth (source: O-04). In western Oregon Douglas-fir plantations, chickadee and warbler predation on western spruce budworm larvae (*Choristoneura occidentalis*) reduces defoliation severity during budworm outbreak years.

#### Ground Foragers — Soil Invertebrate Predators

- **American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*)** — The iconic earthworm and ground-invertebrate specialist. Also takes caterpillars, beetles, and berries. Year-round resident with some altitudinal migration. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)** — Old-growth forest floor specialist; takes earthworms, beetles, millipedes, and snails from leaf litter. A PNW endemic breeding species. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Spotted Towhee (*Pipilo maculatus*)** — Double-scratch foraging technique: jumps forward and rakes both feet backward simultaneously to expose insects in leaf litter. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

### 1.3 Piscivore Networks

Fish-eating birds in the PNW form a trophic network that connects marine, freshwater, and terrestrial ecosystems. PNW piscivores partition the fish resource by water body type, prey size, and foraging technique.

#### River and Stream Piscivores

**Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*)** — Obligate fish specialist with unique morphological adaptations: reversible outer toe, spiny foot pads, closable nostrils, dense oily plumage for water immersion. In the PNW, primary prey includes trout, suckers, and juvenile salmon (smolts). Nests near water on tall structures (dead trees, platforms, utility poles). Population recovery after DDT ban is a major PNW conservation success story. (See `raptors.md`: Osprey; source G-14, O-22.)

**Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*)** — Hovers and plunge-dives for fish up to 10 cm. Territorial on linear stretches of river and stream. Excavates nest burrow in exposed earth banks. Takes juvenile salmon (fry and parr) in salmon-bearing streams — a minor predator on salmon populations but an indicator of stream health. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)** — The only aquatic songbird in North America. Walks underwater on stream bottoms, foraging for aquatic invertebrates (caddisfly, stonefly, and mayfly larvae) and small fish including salmon fry. The dipper is a bioindicator of stream water quality — presence indicates clean, well-oxygenated, cold water. Dipper territory density correlates with salmon spawning activity because salmon carcasses boost aquatic invertebrate production. (See `resident.md`; source O-07, P-18.)

**Harlequin Duck (*Histrionicus histrionicus*)** — Breeds on fast-moving mountain streams; dives for aquatic invertebrates and small fish. Winters on rocky marine coastline. The annual migration from marine coast to mountain headwaters and back links two ecosystems through a single species. (See `resident.md`; source G-14.)

#### Mergansers — Diving Pursuit Predators

Three merganser species in the PNW partition the freshwater piscivore niche by water body type and prey size:

- **Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*)** — Largest; takes fish up to 30 cm. Breeds on rivers and lakes; forms large winter flocks. Significant predator of juvenile salmon (smolts) in rivers — a documented management concern on some intensively managed salmon streams. (See `resident.md`; source G-14, O-07.)
- **Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*)** — Smallest; takes small fish, crayfish, and aquatic insects in sheltered freshwater. Cavity nester (see Section 5). (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Red-breasted Merganser (*Mergus serrator*)** — Primarily marine and estuarine; takes small marine fish and smolts in nearshore waters. Winter and passage species in PNW. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)

#### Colonial Waterbird Piscivores

- **Double-crested Cormorant (*Nannopterum auritum*)** — Pursuit diver consuming fish up to 25 cm. Colonial nester on islands, bridges, and artificial structures. Population has increased dramatically; at the mouth of the Columbia River, cormorant predation on juvenile salmon has become a significant conservation management issue, with USACE implementing hazing and habitat modification on East Sand Island (source: G-20, O-07).
- **Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*)** — Stalking wader; takes fish, frogs, snakes, voles, and occasionally small birds. Colonial nester in "heronries" in large trees near water. The largest PNW heron, with a prey base spanning both aquatic and terrestrial food webs. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

### 1.4 Scavenger Networks

Scavenging — consuming carrion — is not a marginal ecological role. In the PNW, scavenger networks process enormous quantities of dead biomass, accelerating nutrient cycling and reducing disease transmission from decomposing carcasses. The scavenger guild includes obligate scavengers (species that depend primarily on carrion) and facultative scavengers (species that supplement their diet with carrion opportunistically).

**Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*)** — The only obligate avian scavenger breeding in the PNW. Uses olfaction (highly unusual among birds) to locate carrion beneath forest canopy. Summer breeder; arrives March-April, departs September-October. Soars on thermals, covering large areas at low energy cost. Processes road-killed deer, livestock carcasses, and marine mammal carcasses on beaches. Performs a critical sanitation function by consuming carcasses before they become disease reservoirs. (See `raptors.md`; source O-07.)

**Corvid Scavenger Guild:**
- **Common Raven (*Corvus corax*)** — The PNW's most versatile scavenger. Exploits road-killed ungulates, salmon carcasses (see Section 4), marine mammal carcasses on beaches, and anthropogenic food waste. Caches carcass fragments, distributing nutrients across the landscape. Ravens are also significant nest predators on other bird species, creating top-down trophic effects in avian communities. (See `resident.md`; source P-22, O-07.)
- **American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*)** — Urban and agricultural scavenger. Congregates at landfills, agricultural fields (crop waste), and salmon spawning streams. Winter roost aggregations of 5,000-50,000 crows in PNW urban areas (Bothell, WA; Portland, OR) represent massive concentrations of scavenging capacity. (See `resident.md`; source P-22.)
- **Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*)** — Forest corvid; scavenges at campsites, picnic areas, and salmon streams. Also a significant nest predator (eggs and nestlings of smaller birds). (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Eagle Scavenging:**
Bald Eagles are the dominant large-bird scavenger in PNW riparian zones. On salmon spawning streams, eagles transition from active predation (catching live fish) to scavenging as spawning progresses and carcass abundance increases. At major congregations (Skagit River, Nooksack, upper Columbia), eagles compete with ravens, crows, and gulls for carcass access, creating a hierarchical scavenger community with Bald Eagle as the dominant competitor (source: G-14, P-18).

**Gull Scavenging:**
- **Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*)** — Year-round coastal and lowland scavenger; exploits marine carcasses, salmon spawning streams, agricultural waste, and urban refuse. Breeds colonially on islands and rooftops. (See `shorebirds.md`; source O-07.)
- **Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus*)**, **California Gull (*Larus californicus*)**, **Ring-billed Gull (*Larus delawarensis*)** — Seasonal and resident gull species that supplement the scavenger guild, particularly at inland sites (Klamath Basin, Columbia Plateau lakes, agricultural areas). (See `migratory.md`, `shorebirds.md`; source O-07.)

**Scavenger Network Structure:**

The PNW scavenger network operates as a hierarchical access queue — larger, more dominant species feed first, smaller species scavenge remnants:

```
Carcass Appears
     |
     v
[Level 1] Turkey Vulture detects via olfaction → arrives first
     |
     v
[Level 2] Bald Eagle displaces vulture → feeds on choice portions
     |
     v
[Level 3] Common Raven, American Crow → scavenge fragments; cache pieces
     |
     v
[Level 4] Steller's Jay, Gray Jay → small fragments at periphery
     |
     v
[Level 5] Invertebrates (blowflies, beetles) → process remaining tissue
     |
     v
[Level 6] Songbirds consume blowfly larvae → nutrient entry to passerine food web
```

This hierarchical processing ensures rapid and thorough carcass utilization. In winter, when insect scavengers are inactive, vertebrate scavengers become even more critical for nutrient cycling.

## 2. Seed Dispersal Networks

Birds are among the most important seed dispersal vectors in PNW forests. Unlike wind dispersal (which drops seeds near the parent tree) or gravity dispersal (which limits seeds to downslope movement), bird dispersal can move seeds across barriers — ridgelines, valleys, clearcuts, rivers — that would otherwise be impassable. This makes avian seed dispersal critical for forest regeneration, species range shifts in response to climate change, and genetic connectivity between isolated plant populations.

### 2.1 Clark's Nutcracker and Whitebark Pine — The Keystone Mutualism

**Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*)** and whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) form the most consequential bird-plant mutualism in the PNW. This is not merely a feeding relationship — it is an obligate codependency upon which an entire subalpine ecosystem depends.

**The biology:** Whitebark pine produces large, wingless seeds that cannot disperse by wind. The tree is entirely dependent on Clark's Nutcracker for seed dispersal and regeneration. In late summer and fall, nutcrackers harvest whitebark pine seeds and cache them in the ground — each bird storing an estimated 30,000-100,000 seeds per season in thousands of individual caches scattered across the subalpine landscape (source: P-16). Caches are buried 1-3 cm deep in exposed, south-facing microsites at and above timberline — precisely the conditions that whitebark pine seedlings require for establishment. The nutcracker recovers approximately 70-90% of cached seeds during winter and spring, leaving 10-30% to germinate. These unretrieved caches are the sole regeneration pathway for whitebark pine (source: P-16).

**The mutualism economics:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Seeds cached per bird per season | 30,000-100,000 |
| Cache sites per bird per season | 2,500-10,000 |
| Seeds per cache | 3-15 (avg. 4-5) |
| Cache recovery rate | 70-90% |
| Seeds left to germinate | 10-30% of caches (~3,000-30,000 seeds/bird) |
| Dispersal distance | Up to 22 km from harvest tree |
| Cache depth | 1-3 cm |
| Cache microsite | South-facing, exposed, thin soil — optimal germination conditions |

**Why this matters for the PNW:** Whitebark pine is a keystone species of PNW subalpine ecosystems (ELEV-SUBALPINE, 5,500-7,500 ft). It provides food (seeds) for grizzly bears, red squirrels, and other Clark's Nutcrackers; it pioneers exposed sites at and above treeline; and it regulates snowmelt — whitebark pine "snow traps" on exposed ridges capture windblown snow, creating snowfields that persist later into summer and provide critical late-season water to downslope watersheds. Without Clark's Nutcracker, whitebark pine cannot regenerate. Without whitebark pine, the subalpine ecosystem loses its keystone tree.

**The crisis:** Whitebark pine is under severe threat from white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola* — an introduced fungal pathogen), mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae* — outbreaks exacerbated by climate warming), and wildfire. The USFWS listed whitebark pine as Threatened under the ESA in December 2022. As whitebark pine populations decline, Clark's Nutcracker populations may decline in response (less food), creating a positive feedback loop: fewer nutcrackers = less seed dispersal = less whitebark pine regeneration = fewer nutcrackers. This mutualism collapse scenario is one of the most concerning conservation dynamics in PNW high-elevation ecosystems (source: P-16, G-22).

**Cross-reference:** See ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 3 (Mycorrhizal Network) — whitebark pine also depends on ectomycorrhizal fungi for establishment; see ECO `flora-survey.md` for whitebark pine habitat characterization.

### 2.2 Corvid Scatter-Hoarders — Oak and Conifer Dispersal

Beyond Clark's Nutcracker, other corvids play significant roles in PNW seed dispersal through scatter-hoarding behavior — caching food items in scattered locations for later retrieval.

**Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*)** — Caches acorns (Oregon white oak *Quercus garryana*, California black oak *Q. kelloggii*), hazelnuts (*Corylus cornuta*), pine seeds, and Douglas-fir seeds. Steller's Jays transport acorns up to 4 km from the parent tree and bury them in soil, leaf litter, and bark crevices. Unretrieved caches germinate, making Steller's Jay a primary dispersal agent for Oregon white oak — a tree whose heavy acorns cannot disperse effectively by wind or gravity. Oregon white oak savanna and woodland is one of the most endangered habitats in the PNW (less than 5% of historical extent remains in the Willamette Valley), and Steller's Jay dispersal is critical for oak regeneration in restoration sites. (See `resident.md`; source O-07, P-22.)

**California Scrub-Jay (*Aphelocoma californica*)** — Lowland oak woodland specialist in southern Oregon and the Willamette Valley. Caches acorns extensively — estimated 3,000-5,000 acorns per bird per season. Like Steller's Jay, unretrieved caches drive oak regeneration. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Gray Jay (Canada Jay, *Perisoreus canadensis*)** — Caches food items (seeds, arthropods, berries, carrion fragments) in tree bark crevices and branch forks using sticky saliva. Unlike ground-caching corvids, Gray Jay caches are arboreal, reducing rodent pilferage. Caches serve as winter food reserves in boreal and subalpine forests where ground foraging is impossible. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

### 2.3 Band-tailed Pigeon — Long-Distance Seed Mover

**Band-tailed Pigeon (*Patagioenas fasciata*)** — The only native pigeon in PNW forests. A large (300-400 g) frugivore-granivore that forms flocks of 20-200+ birds and undertakes seasonal movements tracking fruit and seed crops. Primary food: acorns, Pacific madrone berries (*Arbutus menziesii*), elderberry (*Sambucus* spp.), cascara (*Frangula purshiana*), and mineral sources (mineral springs, exposed mineral soil). Band-tailed Pigeons swallow acorns whole and transport them in the crop for distances of 10-30+ km, depositing them in feces at roost sites and mineral springs far from the parent tree. This makes Band-tailed Pigeon one of the few PNW birds capable of long-distance oak and madrone dispersal. Population has declined significantly — down ~60% from historical levels — due to historical overhunting and habitat loss (source: G-14, G-18). (See `resident.md`.)

### 2.4 Berry Dispersal Networks — The Frugivore Guild

PNW forests and shrublands produce enormous berry crops that are critical food for dozens of bird species, particularly in late summer and fall. The berry-dispersal network links plants and birds in a mutualism: plants produce nutritious fruit to attract birds; birds consume fruit and deposit seeds in feces at locations away from the parent plant, reducing competition between parent and offspring and enabling colonization of new sites.

**Cedar Waxwing (*Bombycilla cedrorum*)** — The quintessential PNW frugivore. Specialized gut: short intestinal retention time (15-45 minutes) means seeds pass through intact and viable, deposited far from the parent plant. Flocks of 20-200 waxwings descend on berry-producing trees and shrubs, stripping crops efficiently. Primary PNW berry species consumed: red elderberry (*Sambucus racemosa*), black elderberry (*S. nigra* ssp. *cerulea*), mountain ash (*Sorbus* spp.), Pacific dogwood (*Cornus nuttallii*), Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*), salal (*Gaultheria shallon*), red huckleberry (*Vaccinium parvifolium*), serviceberry (*Amelanchier alnifolia*), and cascara (*Frangula purshiana*). (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Bohemian Waxwing (*Bombycilla garrulus*)** — Winter visitor to PNW; arrives in irruptive years when northern berry crops fail. Forms large flocks (sometimes thousands) that consume mountain ash, juniper berries (*Juniperus* spp.), and hawthorn (*Crataegus* spp.) fruits in urban and suburban areas. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)

**Thrush Frugivore Guild:**
- **American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*)** — Omnivore that switches to heavy frugivory in fall and winter. Major disperser of chokecherry (*Prunus virginiana*), hawthorn, and mountain ash seeds. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)** — Takes huckleberries, salal berries, and Pacific dogwood fruits in old-growth forest understory. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- **Hermit Thrush (*Catharus guttatus*)** — Takes understory berries including salal, red huckleberry, and Oregon grape. Breeds in montane forests; winters at lower elevations. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)
- **Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*)** — Neotropical migrant; consumes salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), red huckleberry, and elderberry heavily before fall migration for fuel loading. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)

**Berry Dispersal Network Map:**

| Plant Species | Primary Avian Dispersers | Seed Treatment | Dispersal Distance |
|--------------|------------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Red elderberry (*Sambucus racemosa*) | Cedar Waxwing, American Robin, Band-tailed Pigeon | Gut passage — viable | 100 m to 5+ km |
| Salal (*Gaultheria shallon*) | Varied Thrush, Hermit Thrush, American Robin | Gut passage — viable | 50 m to 2 km |
| Red huckleberry (*Vaccinium parvifolium*) | Varied Thrush, Swainson's Thrush, Cedar Waxwing | Gut passage — viable | 50 m to 2 km |
| Oregon grape (*Mahonia aquifolium*) | American Robin, Cedar Waxwing, Spotted Towhee | Gut passage — viable | 100 m to 3 km |
| Pacific dogwood (*Cornus nuttallii*) | Band-tailed Pigeon, Cedar Waxwing, Varied Thrush | Gut passage — viable | 100 m to 10 km |
| Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) | Steller's Jay, California Scrub-Jay | Scatter-hoarded — buried | 100 m to 4 km |
| Whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) | Clark's Nutcracker | Scatter-hoarded — buried | Up to 22 km |
| Cascara (*Frangula purshiana*) | Cedar Waxwing, Band-tailed Pigeon, American Robin | Gut passage — viable | 100 m to 10+ km |

**Network significance:** The berry dispersal network is critical for PNW forest regeneration after disturbance (wildfire, logging, windthrow). Pioneer shrubs like elderberry, salmonberry, and salal colonize disturbed sites rapidly when birds deposit seeds from adjacent intact forest. Without avian dispersal, revegetation of disturbed sites proceeds much more slowly and from a narrower range of seed sources.

## 3. Pollination Networks

Birds are secondary pollinators in the PNW compared to insects (bees, butterflies, flies) and wind. However, one bird species plays a disproportionate pollination role: the Rufous Hummingbird.

### 3.1 Rufous Hummingbird — The Mountain Pollinator

**Rufous Hummingbird (*Selasphorus rufus*)** — The most significant avian pollinator in the PNW and arguably the most important bird pollinator in temperate North America. This tiny bird (2.5-4.5 g) migrates from Mexico to breeding grounds as far north as southeast Alaska — the longest migration relative to body size of any bird in the world (source: P-17).

**Pollination mechanism:** Rufous Hummingbirds visit tubular flowers to extract nectar, and in doing so contact anthers and stigmas with their head and bill, transferring pollen between flowers. The bird's long, slender bill and extendable tongue are morphological adaptations for nectar extraction from tubular corollas. Unlike insects, hummingbirds visit flowers in a regular "trapline" pattern — visiting the same flowers in the same sequence daily — which promotes outcrossing (pollen transfer between genetically distinct plants) rather than self-pollination.

**PNW plant species pollinated by Rufous Hummingbird:**

| Plant | Family | Flower Color | Bloom Period | Habitat |
|-------|--------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Red columbine (*Aquilegia formosa*) | Ranunculaceae | Red-yellow | May-July | Moist forest edges, meadows |
| Indian paintbrush (*Castilleja* spp.) | Orobanchaceae | Red-orange | June-August | Meadows, open slopes |
| Salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*) | Rosaceae | Pink-magenta | March-June | Riparian, moist lowland forest |
| Red-flowering currant (*Ribes sanguineum*) | Grossulariaceae | Pink-red | March-May | Forest edges, second-growth |
| Penstemon (*Penstemon* spp.) | Plantaginaceae | Red, purple, blue | May-August | Rocky slopes, meadows |
| Scarlet gilia (*Ipomopsis aggregata*) | Polemoniaceae | Scarlet red | June-August | East-side dry meadows |
| Twinberry (*Lonicera involucrata*) | Caprifoliaceae | Yellow-orange | May-July | Riparian, wet meadows |
| Fireweed (*Chamerion angustifolium*) | Onagraceae | Pink-magenta | June-September | Disturbed sites, burns |
| Devil's club (*Oplopanax horridus*) | Araliaceae | Greenish-white | May-July | Moist old-growth understory |
| Tiger lily (*Lilium columbianum*) | Liliaceae | Orange-red | June-July | Open forest, meadows |

**The red flower syndrome:** Most hummingbird-pollinated PNW flowers are red, orange, or pink — colors that attract hummingbirds but are less visible to most insects. This color bias represents convergent evolution: plants that evolved red tubular flowers gained preferential access to hummingbird pollination, which is more reliable than insect pollination in cool, wet, or windy conditions when insects are inactive (source: P-17, O-07).

**Migration-pollination phenology:** The Rufous Hummingbird's northward migration in spring tracks the sequential blooming of nectar-producing flowers up the Pacific coast and inland mountains. Red-flowering currant blooms in March in lowland western Washington, overlapping with the earliest arriving Rufous Hummingbirds. As the season progresses, hummingbirds follow the bloom front upslope: salmonberry (April-May), columbine (May-June), paintbrush (June-July), and fireweed (July-August) — a "nectar corridor" from sea level to subalpine meadows. This phenological tracking means that climate-driven shifts in bloom timing directly affect hummingbird migration success — if flowers bloom before hummingbirds arrive, the birds find depleted nectar supplies and the plants miss their pollinator (source: P-17).

**Anna's Hummingbird (*Calypte anna*)** — A recent range expansion story. Historically a California species, Anna's Hummingbird has colonized western Washington and Oregon over the past 50 years, likely facilitated by urban hummingbird feeders and ornamental plantings that provide winter nectar in a region where no native winter-blooming hummingbird flowers exist. Anna's Hummingbird is now a year-round resident in lowland western PNW, potentially competing with Rufous Hummingbird for early-spring nectar sources. The ecological consequences of this range expansion are not yet fully understood. (See `resident.md`; source G-14, O-02.)

**Calliope Hummingbird (*Selasphorus calliope*)** — The smallest bird in North America (2.5-3.5 g). Breeds in PNW montane meadows and open forests; pollinates paintbrush, penstemon, and columbine at mid- to high elevations. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)

## 4. Nutrient Cycling — The River's Witness

This is the avian chapter of the PNW salmon nutrient story. It is the thread that connects ocean to forest through the bodies of birds.

The PNW salmon nutrient pathway — the movement of marine-derived nitrogen (MDN), phosphorus, and carbon from the Pacific Ocean into terrestrial ecosystems via spawning and dying salmon — is documented in full in ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1. This section focuses specifically on the role of birds as nutrient vectors, scavengers, and indicators within that pathway. The mammalian chapter is documented in MAM `carnivores.md` (bears as primary large-body vectors).

### 4.1 The Salmon Nutrient Pathway — Avian Participation

When Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.) return to their natal streams to spawn and die, they deliver a pulse of marine-derived nutrients measured in tonnes per watershed. The biological vectors that scatter salmon carcasses from stream into forest are the key agents of nutrient transfer — and birds are among the most important of these vectors.

**Scale of the resource:** A single Chinook salmon returning at 20-40 lbs carries approximately 130-260 g of marine-derived nitrogen and 20-30 g of phosphorus. In historical conditions (pre-dam, pre-commercial fishing), the Columbia River system alone received an estimated 10-16 million adult salmon annually, delivering thousands of tonnes of MDN to PNW watersheds (source: P-18; see ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1 quantitative dimensions).

**Avian participation occurs through four mechanisms:**

1. **Direct predation** — birds catching and consuming live spawning salmon or salmon carcasses
2. **Carcass scattering** — birds dragging or carrying carcass fragments away from the stream into the surrounding forest
3. **Guano deposition** — birds excreting salmon-derived nutrients at perch sites, roosts, and nest sites distant from the stream
4. **Invertebrate bridge** — birds consuming blowfly larvae and aquatic invertebrates that fed on salmon carcasses, transferring salmon-derived nutrients into the terrestrial insectivore food web

### 4.2 Bald Eagle Congregations at Spawning Streams

The Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) is the largest and most ecologically significant avian participant in the PNW salmon nutrient pathway. Winter eagle congregations on salmon spawning rivers represent one of the most spectacular wildlife phenomena in North America — and one of the most important nutrient vectoring events.

**Key PNW eagle-salmon congregations:**

| River System | State | Peak Eagle Count | Salmon Species | Timing | Source |
|-------------|-------|-----------------|---------------|--------|--------|
| Skagit River | WA | 300-700+ | Chum, Chinook | Nov-Feb | G-14, P-18 |
| Nooksack River | WA | 100-400 | Chum, Chinook | Nov-Jan | G-14 |
| Samish River | WA | 50-200 | Chum | Nov-Jan | G-14 |
| Upper Skagit (Ross/Diablo) | WA | 100-300 | Sockeye, Chum | Nov-Jan | G-25 |
| Hoh River | WA | 50-150 | Chinook, Chum, Coho | Oct-Jan | G-14 |
| Elwha River (post-dam removal) | WA | 50-200 (increasing) | Chinook, Coho, Steelhead | Nov-Feb | G-25 |
| Klamath River | OR | 100-300 | Chinook, Coho | Sep-Dec | G-15 |
| Upper Columbia / Wenatchee | WA | 50-200 | Chinook, Sockeye | Oct-Dec | G-14 |
| Chilkat River | AK (reference) | 3,000-4,000 | Chum | Oct-Feb | O-22 |

**The Skagit River system** is the most significant eagle-salmon congregation in Washington state. The Skagit River Bald Eagle Natural Area (established 1978) and adjacent lands along the upper Skagit support the largest winter eagle concentration in the lower 48 states outside Alaska. Eagles begin arriving in October as Chinook carcasses become available, with numbers peaking in December-January during the chum salmon spawning peak. During peak weeks, observers have counted 300-700+ Bald Eagles along a 10-mile stretch of the upper Skagit between Rockport and Marblemount (source: G-14).

**Nutrient vectoring mechanism:** Eagles feed on salmon carcasses at gravel bars and shallow riffles, then carry carcass fragments (typically the head and brain, which contain the highest nutrient concentrations) to perch trees 50-200 m from the stream. Fragment pieces drop below perch trees, and eagle guano — enriched in marine-derived nitrogen (N-15) — accumulates at the base of regularly used perch trees. Stable isotope analysis has demonstrated significantly elevated N-15 signatures in the foliage of eagle perch trees compared to non-perch trees at the same distance from stream, confirming that eagles are actively transporting salmon-derived nutrients into the riparian forest canopy (Reimchen et al. 2003; see ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1, Stage 3).

**Quantitative estimate:** A single Bald Eagle consuming 300-500 g of salmon per day over a 90-day winter season processes approximately 27-45 kg of salmon tissue. With 300-700 eagles on the Skagit system, the aggregate annual salmon consumption by eagles alone is on the order of 8,000-31,500 kg — 8-31 tonnes of salmon tissue converted to eagle biomass, guano, and scattered carcass fragments deposited in the riparian forest.

### 4.3 Dipper, Kingfisher, and Merganser — In-Stream Predators

While Bald Eagles work the carcass scavenging end of the salmon nutrient pathway, three bird groups interact with the living salmon population — particularly with juvenile salmon (fry, parr, smolt):

**American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)** — Feeds on aquatic invertebrates (caddisfly, stonefly, mayfly larvae) and takes salmon fry and eggs opportunistically. The dipper's role in the salmon nutrient pathway is indirect but important: salmon carcasses boost aquatic invertebrate production by 25-100% in spawning reaches (fertilization effect), and dipper territory density and reproductive success increase on streams with active salmon runs (source: P-18, O-07). Dippers also deposit guano on mid-stream rocks and banks, cycling a portion of salmon-derived nutrients back into the riparian zone. The dipper is thus both a beneficiary and a minor vector of the salmon nutrient pulse.

**Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*)** — Takes juvenile salmon (fry and parr up to ~8 cm) from shallow riffles and pool margins. Kingfisher predation on salmon is minor at the population level but locally significant in small headwater streams where juvenile salmon densities are low. Kingfisher presence indicates stream conditions suitable for juvenile salmon rearing: clear water, riffle-pool complexity, vegetated banks (source: O-07).

**Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*)** — The most significant avian predator of juvenile salmon in the PNW. Mergansers are pursuit divers that take smolts (juvenile salmon migrating downstream to the ocean) during the spring smolt migration period (March-June). On intensively managed salmon rivers (e.g., tributaries of the Columbia River), merganser predation on smolts has been estimated at 5-20% of the smolt population in some reaches — sufficient to generate management concern and periodic calls for merganser control (source: G-20). However, the ecological reality is more nuanced: mergansers selectively take weak, sick, and parasite-infested smolts (selective predation), potentially improving the average fitness of the surviving smolt cohort.

### 4.4 Corvid and Gull Scavenging at Salmon Runs

**Common Raven (*Corvus corax*)** and **American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*)** — Both species congregate at salmon spawning streams, particularly on the Skagit, Nooksack, Hoh, and Klamath river systems. Ravens are bold scavengers that access carcasses immediately; crows form flocks at gravel bar carcass accumulations. Corvids carry salmon fragments into adjacent forest for caching — ravens cache in tree bark crevices, under rocks, and in soil, distributing salmon-derived nutrients across a wider area than eagles, which concentrate nutrient deposition at perch trees. Corvid caching extends the temporal availability of salmon nutrients by storing fragments that decompose slowly through winter (source: P-22, P-18).

**Gull scavenging:** Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*), Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus*), and other gull species aggregate at salmon spawning streams in large numbers (hundreds to thousands), consuming carcass fragments and spawned-out roe. Gulls deposit salmon-derived guano at roost sites on gravel bars, nearby agricultural fields, and urban parking lots — extending the spatial footprint of salmon nutrient distribution well beyond the riparian corridor. In estuarine reaches, gulls consuming carcasses at river mouths deposit guano on nearby islands and headlands, creating nutrient-enriched patches that support lush vegetation growth (source: O-07, G-20).

### 4.5 Nutrient Deposition in Riparian Forests via Bird Guano

The cumulative effect of avian participation in the salmon nutrient pathway is measurable in riparian forest chemistry. Stable isotope analysis (N-15/N-14 ratio) in riparian vegetation, soil, and even arthropods within 100 m of salmon-bearing streams shows enriched N-15 signatures — the isotopic fingerprint of marine-derived nitrogen transported through the food web (source: P-18).

**Avian nutrient deposition pathways:**

| Vector | Deposition Mode | Distance from Stream | Temporal Pattern |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Bald Eagle | Carcass fragments at perch trees; guano | 50-200 m | Nov-Feb (peak) |
| Common Raven | Cached carcass fragments in forest | 100-500 m | Oct-Mar |
| American Crow | Guano at roost sites | 200 m to 10+ km | Oct-Mar |
| Glaucous-winged Gull | Guano at roost sites, islands | 500 m to 10+ km | Oct-Jan |
| Steller's Jay | Small cached fragments | 50-200 m | Oct-Jan |
| American Dipper | Guano on stream rocks and banks | 0-10 m (in-stream) | Year-round |
| Songbirds (via blowfly larvae) | Guano from insect consumption | 0-500 m | Oct-Mar |

**The blowfly bridge:** Blowflies (*Calliphoridae*) colonize salmon carcasses within hours of death, laying eggs that hatch into larvae feeding on carcass tissue. These larvae incorporate marine-derived nitrogen from salmon into their body tissue. When blowfly larvae pupate and emerge as adult flies, or when they are consumed by songbirds (Winter Wrens, chickadees, kinglets foraging on carcasses), the salmon-derived nitrogen enters the passerine food web — a pathway that moves marine nutrients into the bodies of birds that may never directly interact with a salmon. This "insect bridge" is documented in ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1, Stage 3 (source: P-18).

### 4.6 Colonial Seabird Guano — Marine-to-Terrestrial Nutrient Transfer

Beyond the salmon pathway, colonial seabirds create a parallel marine-to-terrestrial nutrient transfer through guano deposition on breeding islands and headlands.

**Key colonial seabird guano systems in the PNW:**

- **Oregon Islands NWR** — Over 1.2 million seabirds nest on 1,853 rocks, reefs, and islands along the Oregon coast, including Common Murre (*Uria aalge*), Brandt's Cormorant (*Urile penicillatus*), Tufted Puffin (*Fratercula cirrhata*), Rhinoceros Auklet (*Cerorhinca monocerata*), and Pigeon Guillemot (*Cepphus columba*). Colonial guano deposits fertilize island vegetation, creating lush plant growth on otherwise barren rock surfaces. The nutrient subsidy from seabird guano can increase island soil nitrogen concentrations by 5-10x compared to non-colony islands (source: G-01, O-07).
- **Protection Island NWR (WA)** — Hosts 72% of Washington's nesting seabirds, including 36,000+ pairs of Rhinoceros Auklets and significant Tufted Puffin and Glaucous-winged Gull colonies. Auklet guano and discarded fish enriches island soils (source: G-01).
- **Tatoosh Island (WA)** — Small rocky island at Cape Flattery; supports breeding Common Murre, Tufted Puffin, Rhinoceros Auklet, Pelagic Cormorant, and Glaucous-winged Gull colonies. One of the most studied seabird colony sites in the PNW (source: G-01, O-07).

**Nutrient flow:** Colonial seabirds forage at sea (consuming fish, krill, squid) and return to breeding colonies to nest, feed chicks, and roost. The guano deposited at colonies contains concentrated marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus. Over decades to centuries, guano accumulation transforms island soil chemistry, creating "ornithogenic soils" that support plant communities distinct from surrounding non-colony habitats. On PNW islands, ornithogenic soils support dense stands of salal, elderberry, and grasses that would not grow on the thin, nutrient-poor native soils of basalt sea stacks.

## 5. Cavity Nester Guild Networks

The cavity nester guild — birds that nest inside holes in trees — forms one of the most structured ecological networks in PNW forests. The network is organized around a fundamental resource: tree cavities. And the supply of that resource is controlled by a small number of primary excavators — woodpeckers — whose actions create housing for dozens of secondary cavity-using species.

### 5.1 Primary Excavators — The Woodpecker Guild

Woodpeckers (family Picidae) are the only PNW birds that regularly excavate nest cavities in living and dead wood. They are the keystone architects of the cavity nester network.

**PNW primary excavators ranked by cavity size:**

| Species | Cavity Diameter | Tree Preference | Forest Type | Ecological Significance |
|---------|----------------|----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Pileated Woodpecker** (*Dryocopus pileatus*) | 9-12 cm (3.5-5 in) | Large-diameter snags and live trees with heartrot; prefers Douglas-fir, western redcedar, grand fir | Old-growth, mature second-growth | Creates the largest cavities; used by Wood Duck, Barrow's Goldeneye, Vaux's Swift, Northern Saw-whet Owl, American Marten |
| **Northern Flicker** (*Colaptes auratus*) | 6-8 cm (2.5-3 in) | Soft-wood snags, dead deciduous trees (cottonwood, aspen, alder) | Riparian, edge, suburban | Most productive cavity producer in PNW; creates more cavities than any other woodpecker; widely used by secondary users |
| **Hairy Woodpecker** (*Leuconotopicus villosus*) | 5-6 cm (2-2.5 in) | Medium snags, both conifer and deciduous | All forest types | Medium-sized cavities; used by Tree Swallow, Violet-green Swallow, chickadees, nuthatches |
| **Red-breasted Sapsucker** (*Sphyrapicus ruber*) | 4-5 cm (1.5-2 in) | Living trees with soft wood; red alder, bigleaf maple | Lowland riparian, mixed forest | Creates sap wells in addition to nest cavities; sap wells are used by hummingbirds, warblers, and other species for food (see Section 7) |
| **Downy Woodpecker** (*Dryobates pubescens*) | 3-4 cm (1.2-1.5 in) | Small dead branches and small snags | All forest types, urban | Smallest cavities; used by chickadees, House Wren, Bewick's Wren |
| **White-headed Woodpecker** (*Dryobates albolarvatus*) | 5-6 cm (2-2.5 in) | Ponderosa pine snags | East-side dry conifer forest | Endemic to ponderosa pine ecosystems; creates cavities used by Western Bluebird, Pygmy Nuthatch |
| **Black-backed Woodpecker** (*Picoides arcticus*) | 5-6 cm (2-2.5 in) | Fire-killed conifer snags | Post-fire forest | Creates cavities in recently burned forests; used by American Three-toed Woodpecker, Mountain Bluebird |
| **Lewis's Woodpecker** (*Melanerpes lewis*) | 6-7 cm (2.5-2.8 in) | Large cottonwood and ponderosa pine snags | East-side riparian, oak-pine transition | Unusual among woodpeckers: an aerial flycatcher; declining species; cavities used by European Starling (competitor) |

### 5.2 Secondary Cavity Users — The Tenants

Secondary cavity users cannot excavate their own cavities and depend entirely on woodpecker-created holes (or natural decay cavities) for nesting. The diversity of secondary cavity users in PNW forests far exceeds the number of primary excavators — a structural dependency that makes woodpecker populations critical for overall avian diversity.

**PNW secondary cavity users (birds only — mammals such as flying squirrels and marten also use cavities):**

**Owls:**
- Northern Saw-whet Owl (*Aegolius acadicus*) — Uses Northern Flicker and Pileated Woodpecker cavities; the most common cavity-nesting owl in PNW forests. (See `raptors.md`.)
- Western Screech-Owl (*Megascops kennicottii*) — Uses Northern Flicker cavities in riparian and suburban habitats. (See `raptors.md`.)
- Boreal Owl (*Aegolius funereus*) — Uses Pileated Woodpecker cavities in subalpine spruce-fir forests. Rare and secretive in PNW. (See `raptors.md`.)
- Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) — Uses natural tree cavities and occasionally old Pileated Woodpecker cavities; also nests on platforms (hawk nests, squirrel nests). (See `raptors.md`.)

**Ducks:**
- Wood Duck (*Aix sponsa*) — Uses Pileated Woodpecker cavities near water; also uses nest boxes. One of the most successful nest box conservation programs in North American ornithology. (See `resident.md`; source G-14.)
- Barrow's Goldeneye (*Bucephala islandica*) — Uses Pileated Woodpecker cavities near mountain lakes and rivers. Interior PNW breeder. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- Common Goldeneye (*Bucephala clangula*) — Uses large cavities near water. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*) — Uses Pileated Woodpecker cavities near slow-moving water. (See Section 1.3.)
- Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*) — Uses Northern Flicker cavities near water; the smallest cavity-nesting duck in North America, able to use flicker-sized holes that exclude larger ducks. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Swallows:**
- Tree Swallow (*Tachycineta bicolor*) — Uses small to medium woodpecker cavities and nest boxes; one of the most common secondary cavity nesters. (See `migratory.md`; source G-18.)
- Violet-green Swallow (*Tachycineta thalassina*) — Uses small woodpecker cavities, cliff crevices, and building cavities. (See `migratory.md`; source G-18.)

**Chickadees and Nuthatches:**
- Black-capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*) — Uses Downy Woodpecker cavities; also excavates its own cavities in very soft wood (rotten birch, alder). (See `resident.md`.)
- Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*) — Uses small woodpecker cavities; the west-side chickadee. (See `resident.md`.)
- Mountain Chickadee (*Poecile gambeli*) — East-side counterpart; uses cavities in ponderosa pine and Douglas-fir. (See `resident.md`.)
- Red-breasted Nuthatch (*Sitta canadensis*) — Uses small woodpecker cavities and occasionally excavates its own. Smears pitch (conifer resin) around cavity entrance as an anti-predator defense — one of the few tool-use behaviors documented in PNW birds. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)
- White-breasted Nuthatch (*Sitta carolinensis*) — Uses medium woodpecker cavities in deciduous and mixed forests. (See `resident.md`.)
- Pygmy Nuthatch (*Sitta pygmaea*) — East-side ponderosa pine specialist; communal roosting (up to 100+ birds in a single cavity) for winter thermoregulation. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Wrens:**
- House Wren (*Troglodytes aedon*) — Uses any small cavity; aggressively fills unused cavities with sticks to prevent competitors from using them. This "plug-filling" behavior reduces cavity availability for other species. (See `migratory.md`; source O-07.)
- Bewick's Wren (*Thryomanes bewickii*) — Uses small woodpecker cavities and crevices. (See `resident.md`.)

**Bluebirds:**
- Western Bluebird (*Sialia mexicana*) — East-side open forest and savanna; uses old woodpecker cavities in ponderosa pine and oak snags. Nest box programs have significantly bolstered PNW populations. (See `resident.md`; source G-14.)
- Mountain Bluebird (*Sialia currucoides*) — High-elevation meadow edges and burns; uses cavities in subalpine snags. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

### 5.3 The Cavity Economy — Supply, Demand, and Competition

The cavity nester network operates as an economic system with supply (cavities created by woodpeckers and natural decay), demand (secondary cavity users needing nest sites), and competition (interspecific contests for limited cavities).

**Supply side:**
- Woodpeckers typically excavate a new cavity each year, abandoning previous cavities (though Pileated Woodpeckers may reuse cavities for multiple years)
- Natural decay cavities form in trees with heartrot fungi, particularly old-growth trees >200 years old
- Cavity supply is directly proportional to snag density — forests managed for timber with short rotation cycles (<80 years) have dramatically fewer snags and cavities than old-growth or mature forests

**Demand side:**
- In PNW old-growth forests, secondary cavity users outnumber primary excavators by approximately 3-5:1 (source: G-22)
- Competition for cavities is intense: European Starling (*Sturnus vulgaris*), an invasive species, aggressively displaces native cavity nesters from both woodpecker cavities and nest boxes. Starlings are implicated in population declines of Lewis's Woodpecker, Western Bluebird, and Purple Martin in the PNW (source: G-18)
- House Sparrow (*Passer domesticus*) — Another invasive cavity competitor; displaces native species from cavities in urban and suburban habitats

**Competition hierarchy (dominant to subordinate):**
1. European Starling (aggressive — evicts most native species)
2. Northern Flicker (largest native secondary user by body size; defends cavities aggressively)
3. Wood Duck / Barrow's Goldeneye (large body size; evict smaller species)
4. Owls (occupy cavities year-round; not easily displaced)
5. Swallows (fast entry; mob competitors)
6. Chickadees, nuthatches, wrens (small body size; subordinate in most interactions)

**Snag management implications:** Forest management practices that retain snags (standing dead trees) directly support the cavity nester network. PNW forest management guidelines (source: G-22, USFS NWFP) recommend retaining 4-6 snags per hectare in managed forests, with an emphasis on large-diameter snags (>50 cm DBH) suitable for Pileated Woodpecker cavities. The loss of old-growth snags through salvage logging after wildfire removes critical cavity habitat precisely when post-fire specialists (Black-backed Woodpecker, Lewis's Woodpecker) most need it. This creates a management tension: salvage logging recovers economic timber value but eliminates cavity resources for decades.

## 6. Mixed-Species Flock Networks

Mixed-species flocks — groups of birds composed of two or more species foraging together — are a defining feature of PNW winter forest bird communities. These flocks are not random aggregations. They are structured social networks with consistent species composition, predictable leadership, and measurable benefits for participants.

### 6.1 Nuclear Species — The Flock Organizers

Every mixed-species flock has one or two "nuclear" species — species whose presence and activity organize the flock. In PNW forests, chickadees (family Paridae) are the universal nuclear species.

**Black-capped Chickadee (*Poecile atricapillus*)** — Nuclear species in lowland and riparian mixed flocks west of the Cascades. Chickadees produce a continuous stream of contact calls ("chick-a-dee-dee-dee") that function as a social beacon, attracting satellite species. Critically, the chickadee alarm call system encodes threat-specific information: the number of "dee" notes appended to the "chick-a-dee" call correlates with predator size and threat level — more "dee" notes = smaller, more maneuverable predator = higher threat to small birds (Templeton et al. 2005). This information-rich alarm system provides satellite species with predator intelligence they would not have foraging alone. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Chestnut-backed Chickadee (*Poecile rufescens*)** — Nuclear species in wet west-side conifer forests. Functionally equivalent to Black-capped Chickadee as a flock organizer but in wetter, more heavily forested habitats. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

**Mountain Chickadee (*Poecile gambeli*)** — Nuclear species in east-side montane and ponderosa pine forests. Organizes mixed flocks at higher elevations and in drier east-side forests. (See `resident.md`; source O-07.)

### 6.2 Satellite Species — The Flock Followers

Satellite species join chickadee-organized flocks to gain anti-predator benefits (more eyes scanning for hawks and owls) and foraging efficiency (locating food patches discovered by other flock members).

**Typical PNW winter mixed-species flock composition:**

| Species | Flock Role | Foraging Niche | Anti-predator Benefit |
|---------|-----------|---------------|---------------------|
| Black-capped Chickadee | Nuclear | Bark, twigs, leaf surfaces | Alarm calls (information source) |
| Red-breasted Nuthatch | Regular satellite | Bark (downward-facing) | Vigilance sharing |
| Brown Creeper (*Certhia americana*) | Regular satellite | Bark (upward-spiraling) | Vigilance sharing |
| Golden-crowned Kinglet | Regular satellite | Conifer needle clusters | Vigilance sharing |
| Downy Woodpecker | Regular satellite | Small branches, bark | Sentinel position (higher in tree) |
| Hairy Woodpecker | Occasional satellite | Medium branches, trunk | Sentinel position |
| Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*) | Occasional satellite | Dense undergrowth, root masses | Proximity to alarm caller |
| Dark-eyed Junco (*Junco hyemalis*) | Peripheral satellite | Ground beneath flock | Flush response (alarm to ground feeders) |
| Ruby-crowned Kinglet | Occasional satellite | Outer foliage | Vigilance sharing |
| Varied Thrush | Peripheral | Ground, leaf litter | Flush response |

### 6.3 Information Transfer and Predator Detection

The primary adaptive function of mixed-species flocks is predator detection. In a flock, each individual can spend more time foraging and less time scanning for predators because other flock members are scanning simultaneously. The "many eyes" hypothesis predicts that per-capita vigilance decreases (and foraging time increases) as flock size increases — a prediction consistently supported by field studies.

**The chickadee alarm system as information network:**

The Black-capped Chickadee "chick-a-dee" alarm call is one of the most information-rich alarm signals in the animal kingdom. Field experiments demonstrate:

- **Threat specificity:** More "dee" syllables appended to calls made in response to small, agile raptors (Northern Pygmy-Owl, Sharp-shinned Hawk) compared to calls made in response to large, less maneuverable predators (Great Horned Owl, Red-tailed Hawk). A pygmy-owl perch may elicit "chick-a-dee-dee-dee-dee" (4 dee notes), while a Great Horned Owl perch elicits "chick-a-dee" (1 dee note). (Source: O-07; Templeton et al. 2005.)
- **Heterospecific response:** Red-breasted Nuthatches, Brown Creepers, and other satellite species respond appropriately to chickadee alarm calls — increasing vigilance or fleeing in response to high-threat calls, but showing less response to low-threat calls. This cross-species communication means that the chickadee is providing a "broadcast predator warning service" to the entire mixed-species flock.
- **Mobbing recruitment:** High-threat chickadee alarms recruit satellite species into mobbing aggregations — groups of small birds that harass a perched predator with alarm calls and close approaches until the predator leaves. Mobbing is a collective defense behavior that benefits all participating species.

**Network topology:** The mixed-species flock functions as a star network with the chickadee at the hub. Information (predator location, threat level, food patch discovery) radiates outward from the nuclear species to satellite species. The chickadee receives reciprocal benefit: satellite species that spot predators before the chickadee does will give their own alarm calls, adding detection capacity to the network.

### 6.4 Seasonal Dynamics

Mixed-species flocks in the PNW are primarily a winter phenomenon (October-March). During the breeding season (April-August), territorial behavior breaks up flocks as individual pairs defend exclusive territories. The transition from flock to territory and back follows a predictable annual cycle:

- **September:** Post-breeding dispersal; juveniles from the summer settle into winter ranges
- **October:** Flocks begin forming as territories dissolve; chickadees establish winter dominance hierarchies
- **November-February:** Stable flock membership; the same 10-20 individuals travel the same route through the same home range daily
- **March-April:** Flocks dissolve as breeding territories are established; pair bonds form
- **May-August:** Territorial breeding; no mixed-species flocking

## 7. Ecosystem Engineering

Ecosystem engineers are organisms that physically modify, maintain, or create habitat for other species. Several PNW bird species qualify as ecosystem engineers — their physical activities create habitat structures that persist long after the bird has moved on.

### 7.1 Woodpecker Excavation — Decay Acceleration and Structural Modification

Woodpecker foraging and nesting activity physically alters wood structure, accelerating decomposition and creating micro-habitats:

**Foraging excavations:** Pileated Woodpecker foraging creates deep rectangular holes (sometimes 15-30 cm deep) in snags and logs to access carpenter ant (*Camponotus* spp.) colonies. These excavations:
- Expose heartwood to moisture, fungi, and bacteria, accelerating wood decay by years to decades
- Create entry points for fungal colonization that would not otherwise reach interior heartwood
- Produce wood chips that accumulate at the base of the tree, adding to soil organic matter
- Create cavities that are subsequently used by invertebrates, amphibians (Pacific Giant Salamander, *Dicamptodon tenebrosus*), and small mammals as shelter

**Nesting excavations:** Every woodpecker nest cavity is a habitat investment that persists for decades. A cavity excavated in 2025 may house Northern Saw-whet Owls in 2026, Tree Swallows in 2027, flying squirrels in 2028-2035, and eventually contribute to the structural failure and fall of the snag, creating coarse woody debris on the forest floor. The temporal cascade from excavation to snag fall to nurse log may span 30-100+ years.

**Sapsucker sap wells:** Red-breasted Sapsucker (*Sphyrapicus ruber*) drills ordered rows of small holes in the bark of living trees (red alder, bigleaf maple, western hemlock), creating sap wells that exude tree sap. These sap wells are an engineered food source exploited by:
- Rufous Hummingbird (see Section 3) — drinks sap as an early-spring nectar supplement when flowers are scarce
- Orange-crowned Warbler — drinks sap; arrival timing in spring coincides with sapsucker sap well activity
- Red-breasted Nuthatch, Downy Woodpecker — lick sap from wells
- Ants, beetles, butterflies — feed on sap; become secondary prey for the sapsucker and other birds
- Douglas Squirrel (*Tamiasciurus douglasii*) — licks sap wells

The sapsucker thus engineers a "sap well ecosystem" — a localized food web centered on deliberately created wounds in tree bark. (Source: O-07.)

### 7.2 Colonial Seabird Guano — Island Fertilization

Colonial nesting seabirds transform island ecosystems through sustained guano deposition (see also Section 4.6):

**Physical modification:** Tufted Puffin (*Fratercula cirrhata*) and Rhinoceros Auklet (*Cerorhinca monocerata*) excavate nesting burrows in island soil, physically turning over soil and creating tunnel networks 1-3 m long. On Protection Island NWR (WA), puffin and auklet burrow density can reach 2-3 burrows per square meter, creating a honeycomb of tunnels that alters soil drainage, aeration, and structure. The burrowing activity combines with guano deposition to create ornithogenic soils with dramatically different chemical and physical properties from non-colony soils. (Source: G-01, O-07.)

**Vegetation transformation:** Guano enrichment on breeding islands drives succession toward nitrophilous (nitrogen-loving) plant communities. Islands with dense seabird colonies develop dense stands of cow parsnip (*Heracleum maximum*), stinging nettle (*Urtica dioica*), and grasses that would not grow on the thin native soils. This vegetation provides habitat for nesting songbirds (Song Sparrow, Fox Sparrow), invertebrates, and small mammals, creating a cascading habitat modification from seabird guano → soil enrichment → vegetation change → secondary species colonization.

### 7.3 Waterfowl Bioturbation in Wetlands

Waterfowl (ducks, geese, swans) physically modify wetland habitats through feeding activities:

**Bottom-feeding bioturbation:** Dabbling ducks (Mallard *Anas platyrhynchos*, American Wigeon *Mareca americana*, Northern Shoveler *Spatula clypeata*) and diving ducks (Canvasback *Aythya valisineria*, Lesser Scaup *Aythya affinis*) disturb wetland sediments while foraging for aquatic invertebrates, seeds, and tubers. This bioturbation:
- Releases nutrients from anoxic sediments into the water column, stimulating algal and phytoplankton growth
- Creates small depressions in mudflats that serve as micro-pools for invertebrate larvae
- Suppresses emergent vegetation in intensively grazed areas, maintaining open water that shorebirds and wading birds require

**Grazing pressure:** Canada Goose (*Branta canadensis*) and Snow Goose (*Anser caerulescens*) grazing on wetland and agricultural vegetation alters plant community composition. In the Fraser River delta and Skagit River estuary, Snow Goose grazing on saltmarsh vegetation maintains short-sward habitat conditions preferred by migrating shorebirds. Intensive goose grazing can also damage agricultural crops and degrade urban park vegetation — a management concern in the Puget Sound lowlands. (See `resident.md`; source G-14, O-07.)

**Nest construction:** Grebes (Western Grebe *Aechmophorus occidentalis*, Red-necked Grebe *Podiceps grisegena*) build floating nests of aquatic vegetation on lakes and marshes. Abandoned grebe nests become floating platforms used by other species — turtles bask on them, invertebrates colonize the decaying vegetation, and the decomposing nest material returns nutrients to the water column.

## 8. Network Resilience — When Connections Break

Ecological networks are not static. They are dynamic, adaptive, and — critically — vulnerable to disruption. When a keystone node is removed, connections are severed, or timing is altered, the network reorganizes in ways that may or may not maintain ecosystem function. This section examines three PNW case studies in network disruption.

### 8.1 Northern Spotted Owl Decline — Mesopredator Release?

The Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) is one of the most studied birds in North American conservation history. Listed as Threatened under the ESA in 1990, Spotted Owl populations have declined by an estimated 50-75% across their range over the past three decades, driven by two factors: old-growth habitat loss (logging) and competition from the range-expanding Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) (source: G-06, P-13, P-14).

**Network disruption scenario — mesopredator release:**

The Spotted Owl is a mesopredator in PNW old-growth forests, feeding primarily on Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*), Red Tree Vole (*Arborimus longicaudus*), and bushy-tailed woodrat (*Neotoma cinerea*). If Spotted Owl populations collapse, what happens to its prey populations and to the broader forest food web?

**Hypothesis 1: Simple prey release.** Without Spotted Owl predation, flying squirrel and woodrat populations increase. This could lead to increased consumption of truffles (flying squirrel diet) and tree seeds (woodrat diet), potentially altering fungal dispersal patterns (flying squirrels are the primary dispersers of ectomycorrhizal truffle spores in old-growth forests — see ECO `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 3) and tree regeneration dynamics.

**Hypothesis 2: Barred Owl replacement.** The Barred Owl, which is displacing the Spotted Owl, has a much broader diet — including crayfish, frogs, reptiles, insects, and small birds in addition to the mammals that Spotted Owls eat. Barred Owl predation may therefore exert novel trophic pressures on prey populations that the Spotted Owl did not affect. The ecological consequences of replacing a specialist predator (Spotted Owl) with a generalist predator (Barred Owl) in the same habitat are not yet fully understood (source: P-13, P-14).

**Hypothesis 3: Trophic cascade.** The Spotted Owl sits within a multi-level food web: Great Horned Owl → Spotted Owl → Flying Squirrel → Truffles → Mycorrhizal fungi → Tree roots. Disruption at the Spotted Owl node could cascade in either direction. If Great Horned Owl predation keeps Barred Owl populations in check, the system may find a new equilibrium. If not, the Barred Owl's generalist diet may restructure the entire forest food web.

**USFWS management response:** Since 2015, the USFWS has conducted experimental Barred Owl removal in select study areas to test whether Spotted Owl populations stabilize when the competitor is removed. Early results from Washington and Oregon suggest that Spotted Owl territory occupancy stabilizes or increases in removal areas (source: P-13) — evidence that the competition effect is reversible if managed.

### 8.2 Climate-Driven Network Disruption — Phenological Mismatch

Climate change is altering the timing of biological events — bloom dates, insect emergence, bird migration, salmon runs — in ways that can decouple ecological interactions that depend on temporal synchrony.

**Rufous Hummingbird phenological mismatch:** If red-flowering currant blooms earlier due to warmer spring temperatures but Rufous Hummingbird migration timing (cued by photoperiod, not temperature) does not advance proportionally, hummingbirds arrive at depleted nectar supplies. This mismatch reduces hummingbird fitness (less fuel for migration and breeding) and reduces pollination success for early-blooming plants (pollinator absence during peak bloom). Rufous Hummingbird populations have declined by an estimated 60% since 1966 (BBS data; source: G-18), with phenological mismatch proposed as one contributing factor.

**Aerial insectivore timing mismatch:** Olive-sided Flycatcher, Western Wood-Pewee, and other neotropical migrants time their arrival to coincide with peak insect emergence. If warming springs cause insects to emerge earlier while bird arrival remains tied to photoperiod-driven departure from tropical wintering grounds, the birds miss the insect peak. This is particularly concerning for species that feed nestlings on specific insect prey: if the caterpillar peak has passed before nestlings hatch, chick starvation increases and breeding success declines (source: O-04, G-18).

**Whitebark pine / Clark's Nutcracker disruption:** Climate warming enables mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*) to complete life cycles at elevations previously too cold for beetle reproduction. Beetle outbreaks in subalpine whitebark pine stands reduce cone production, which reduces food availability for Clark's Nutcracker, which reduces seed dispersal, which reduces whitebark pine regeneration — a positive feedback loop accelerated by climate change (source: P-16, G-22).

### 8.3 What Happens When Keystone Species Are Removed?

The concept of "keystone species" — a species whose ecological impact is disproportionately large relative to its abundance — applies to several PNW birds whose removal would restructure ecological networks:

**Keystone removal scenarios:**

| Keystone Species | Network Role | Removal Consequence |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Pileated Woodpecker | Primary excavator of large cavities | Loss of large-cavity nesting habitat for Wood Duck, Barrow's Goldeneye, Northern Saw-whet Owl, Vaux's Swift, American Marten. No other PNW bird creates cavities of equivalent size. |
| Clark's Nutcracker | Sole disperser of whitebark pine | Whitebark pine regeneration ceases; subalpine ecosystem keystone tree lost within 1-2 generations. Cascading effects on grizzly bear food supply, snowmelt regulation, and subalpine biodiversity. |
| Black-capped Chickadee | Nuclear species in mixed flocks | Mixed-species flocks dissolve; satellite species lose predator warning network; per-capita predation risk increases for nuthatches, creepers, kinglets, and other small forest birds. |
| Bald Eagle | Primary avian nutrient vector (salmon) | Salmon-derived nutrient transport to riparian forest perch trees ceases; riparian forest receives less marine-derived nitrogen; long-term reduction in riparian tree growth and stream shading. |
| Rufous Hummingbird | Primary avian pollinator | Pollination failure for red-tubular wildflowers (columbine, paintbrush, currant, penstemon). Seed set declines; long-term reduction in wildflower populations; cascading effects on insect pollinators that visit the same plant communities. |

**The paradox of redundancy:** Some ecological functions have built-in redundancy — multiple species performing similar roles — while others do not. Berry dispersal has high redundancy (Cedar Waxwing, thrushes, and robins all disperse elderberry seeds). Large-cavity excavation has low redundancy (only Pileated Woodpecker creates cavities >9 cm diameter in PNW forests). Whitebark pine dispersal has zero redundancy (Clark's Nutcracker is the sole effective disperser). The vulnerability of a network connection is inversely proportional to the number of species that can maintain it.
