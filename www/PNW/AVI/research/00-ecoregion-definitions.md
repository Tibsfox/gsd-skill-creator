# PNW Avian Taxonomy — Ecoregion Zone Definitions

**Version:** 1.0
**Purpose:** Define the 7 ecoregion zones used for bird community analysis across the PNW study region
**Sources:** USFS Ecoregion Province descriptions (M242, M261, 342), Ricketts et al. (1999), One Earth ecoregion profiles

---

## Study Region Boundaries

- **North:** Southern British Columbia (approximately 54°N latitude)
- **South:** Northern California border / Klamath Mountains (approximately 42°N latitude)
- **West:** Pacific Ocean outer continental shelf
- **East:** Western Idaho, eastern Oregon/Washington steppe margins (approximately 117°W longitude)
- **States/Provinces:** Washington, Oregon, Idaho (western), British Columbia (southern)

---

## Zone 1: Alpine and Subalpine

**Elevation:** >5,000 ft (variable by latitude; timberline 7,700–10,000 ft in OR, lower in WA)
**Mountain ranges:** Cascades, Olympics, Blue Mountains, Selkirks, North Cascades
**Vegetation:** Alpine meadows, subalpine fir/mountain hemlock parkland, krummholz, snowfield edges, whitebark pine stands
**Climate:** Short growing season, heavy snowpack, high UV exposure, extreme temperature variation

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) — only ptarmigan native to contiguous PNW Cascades
- **Keystone species:** Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — whitebark pine seed dispersal mutualist
- **Characteristic species:** Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*), American Pipit (*Anthus rubescens*), Horned Lark (*Eremophila alpestris*), White-crowned Sparrow (*Zonotrichia leucophrys* — elevational maximum), Mountain Chickadee, Red Crossbill
- **Seasonal visitors:** Raptors using ridge thermals (Golden Eagle, Red-tailed Hawk), Mountain Bluebird

### Avian Community Notes
- Extremely low species diversity but high specialization
- Ptarmigan and rosy-finches show extreme physiological cold adaptation
- Snowfield-edge foraging as a distinct microhabitat niche
- Climate-driven upslope range contraction documented for multiple species
- Source: USFS Province Description M242, Johnson & Dueser 2010

---

## Zone 2: Montane Conifer Forest

**Elevation:** 2,500–5,000 ft
**Extent:** Mid-elevation belts on both slopes of Cascades, Olympics (east side), Blue Mountains, Selkirks
**Vegetation:** Douglas-fir, Pacific silver fir, mountain hemlock, western larch (east side), grand fir, subalpine fir
**Climate:** Heavy winter snowpack, moderate summer temperatures, variable precipitation (wet west side, drier east side)

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — ESA-threatened, old-growth obligate
- **Keystone species:** Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*) — apex forest raptor
- **Characteristic species:** Townsend's Warbler, Winter Wren, Red-breasted Nuthatch, Gray Jay (Canada Jay), Steller's Jay, Varied Thrush, Blue Grouse (Dusky Grouse), Ruffed Grouse, Hairy Woodpecker, Brown Creeper, Golden-crowned Kinglet, Hermit Warbler
- **Old-growth specialists:** Northern Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet (inland nesting), Pileated Woodpecker, Vaux's Swift (old-growth snag nesters)

### Avian Community Notes
- Highest forest bird diversity in the PNW
- Old-growth vs. second-growth forest creates dramatically different bird communities
- Barred Owl invasion fundamentally restructuring owl community dynamics
- East-side montane forests support distinct species mix (White-headed Woodpecker, Williamson's Sapsucker)
- Source: USFS Cascade Mixed Forest Province Ecoregion M242, Hagar 2007

---

## Zone 3: Lowland Forest and Urban

**Elevation:** <500 ft (western lowlands)
**Extent:** Puget Trough, Willamette lowlands, coastal plains, urban areas
**Vegetation:** Remnant Douglas-fir/western red cedar forests, riparian corridors, urban parks and gardens, agricultural lands
**Climate:** Mild maritime, moderate year-round temperatures, wet winters, dry summers

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*) — common across forest-urban interface
- **Keystone species:** American Robin — dominant frugivore and seed disperser
- **Characteristic species:** Black-capped Chickadee, Bewick's Wren, Spotted Towhee, Dark-eyed Junco, Song Sparrow, Anna's Hummingbird (year-round since range expansion), European Starling (invasive), House Finch, House Sparrow (invasive)
- **Riparian specialists:** Willow Flycatcher, Yellow Warbler, Cedar Waxwing, Red-eyed Vireo (eastern WA riparian)

### Avian Community Notes
- Highest human-bird interaction zone — urban ecology increasingly studied
- Anna's Hummingbird range expansion northward (formerly southern CA/OR, now year-round to BC) is a dramatic recent range shift
- Invasive species (Starling, House Sparrow, Rock Pigeon) compete with native cavity nesters
- Agricultural lands provide winter foraging for waterfowl and raptors
- Source: One Earth Central Pacific Northwest Coastal Forests Ecoregion, eBird abundance data

## Zone 4: Foothill / Oak Woodland

**Elevation:** 500–2,500 ft
**Extent:** Willamette Valley margins, Klamath Mountains, East Cascades lower slopes, Rogue Valley
**Vegetation:** Oregon white oak (*Quercus garryana*) woodland, mixed oak-conifer, madrone, ponderosa pine (east side)
**Climate:** Warm dry summers, mild wet winters, fire-adapted ecosystems

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** Acorn Woodpecker (*Melanerpes formicivorus*) — oak obligate
- **Keystone species:** Lewis's Woodpecker (*Melanerpes lewis*) — oak/burn cavity nester, declining
- **Characteristic species:** Western Bluebird, White-breasted Nuthatch (oak form), Oak Titmouse (Klamath region), Western Scrub-Jay (California Scrub-Jay), Hutton's Vireo, Ash-throated Flycatcher
- **Species of concern:** Lewis's Woodpecker (declining — fire suppression reducing snag habitat), Streaked Horned Lark (ESA-threatened, prairie-oak habitat)

### Avian Community Notes
- Four of six PNW obligate cavity-nesting species are oak-associated
- Oak woodland has declined >90% in Willamette Valley since European settlement
- Twenty highly oak-associated species reach highest densities here
- Fire suppression converting open oak woodland to closed conifer — critical conservation concern
- Source: Pacific Birds Habitat Joint Venture, "Land Managers Guide to Bird Habitat and Populations in Oak Ecosystems of the Pacific Northwest"

---

## Zone 5: Riparian, Wetland, and Estuarine

**Elevation:** Variable (follows waterways from mountains to coast)
**Extent:** Columbia River system, Skagit Delta, Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Fraser River delta, Snake River, Deschutes River, John Day River, Klamath Basin
**Vegetation:** Riparian cottonwood/willow, marshes, tidal mudflats, estuary grass/sedge
**Climate:** Variable by location; moderated by water proximity

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*) — aquatic passerine, stream health indicator
- **Keystone species:** Belted Kingfisher — riparian predator across all elevations
- **Characteristic species:** Great Blue Heron, Green Heron, Osprey, Bald Eagle (salmon streams), Marsh Wren, Common Yellowthroat, Red-winged Blackbird, Tree Swallow, Bank Swallow, Yellow-headed Blackbird (eastern marshes)
- **Staging area specialists:** Western Sandpiper, Dunlin (Grays Harbor), Snow Goose (Skagit/Fraser deltas), Trumpeter Swan (Skagit Valley)

### Avian Community Notes
- Riparian corridors serve as migration highways through otherwise inhospitable terrain
- Salmon-bird nutrient cycling is a defining ecological interaction (Module 5 cross-link)
- Estuaries (Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Skagit Delta) are internationally significant shorebird staging areas
- Eastern Oregon riparian corridors (Snake, Deschutes, John Day) support species absent from surrounding steppe
- Klamath Basin historically hosts up to 80% of Pacific Flyway waterfowl in fall
- Source: USFWS Pacific Flyway, Pacific Birds Habitat Joint Venture, WHSRN

## Zone 6: Coastal / Marine / Pelagic

**Elevation:** Sea level to offshore waters
**Extent:** Outer coast (Cape Flattery to CA border), Strait of Juan de Fuca, Salish Sea (Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, SJdF), offshore to continental shelf edge
**Vegetation:** Coastal scrub, sea stacks, rocky intertidal, kelp forests, open ocean
**Climate:** Heavy maritime influence, persistent fog, moderate temperatures year-round

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*) — ESA-threatened, old-growth/marine dual habitat
- **Keystone species:** Common Murre — dominant offshore breeding seabird (700,000+ in OR)
- **Characteristic species:** Tufted Puffin, Rhinoceros Auklet, Cassin's Auklet, Pigeon Guillemot, Leach's Storm-Petrel, Black Oystercatcher, Western Gull, California Brown Pelican (post-breeding visitor), Sooty Shearwater (pelagic migrant), Brandt's Cormorant
- **Salish Sea wintering specialists:** Harlequin Duck, Barrow's Goldeneye, Surf Scoter, Western Grebe, Pacific Loon, Marbled Murrelet (winter)

### Avian Community Notes
- Oregon Islands NWR protects breeding colonies on offshore rocks (15 breeding sites)
- Salish Sea supports major populations of wintering sea ducks, alcids, loons, grebes
- Seabird colonies extremely sensitive to oil spills, marine heat waves, prey availability
- Marbled Murrelet's dual habitat requirement (old-growth forest + ocean) makes it uniquely vulnerable
- Pelagic zone species (shearwaters, albatrosses, storm-petrels) documented beyond shelf edge
- Source: USFWS Pacific Northwest Seabird Program, WDFW marine bird surveys

## Zone 7: East-Side Steppe and Shrubland

**Elevation:** 500–4,000 ft (east of Cascades)
**Extent:** Columbia Plateau, Malheur Basin, Great Basin fringe, sagebrush steppe, channeled scablands
**Vegetation:** Sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*), bunchgrass, ponderosa pine (higher elevations), juniper woodland, irrigated agriculture
**Climate:** Continental — hot dry summers, cold winters, low precipitation (<15 inches/year)

### Characteristic Avifauna
- **Indicator species:** Greater Sage-Grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*) — sagebrush obligate, declining
- **Keystone species:** Burrowing Owl (*Athene cunicularia*) — ground-nesting predator, population declining
- **Characteristic species:** Loggerhead Shrike, Sagebrush Sparrow (*Artemisiospiza nevadensis*), Brewer's Sparrow, Say's Phoebe, Long-billed Curlew, Ferruginous Hawk, Prairie Falcon, Grasshopper Sparrow, Western Meadowlark
- **Riparian oasis species:** Bullock's Oriole, Yellow-breasted Chat, Lewis's Woodpecker (riparian cottonwood)

### Avian Community Notes
- Completely distinct avifauna from west-side PNW — steppe species absent from forests
- Greater Sage-Grouse lek displays are iconic; populations driven major conservation policy
- Malheur NWR is critical staging area for migrants and breeding ground for numerous waterbirds
- Juniper encroachment into sagebrush is a growing threat to steppe-obligate species
- Riparian corridors (Snake, Deschutes, John Day) provide critical habitat for neotropical migrants
- Source: USFS, WDFW Priority Habitats and Species, Schroeder et al. 2004

## Cross-Zone Species

Many PNW bird species occupy multiple ecoregion zones. Key cross-zone patterns:

| Pattern | Example Species | Zones |
|---------|----------------|-------|
| Elevational migrant | Mountain Quail, Townsend's Solitaire | Zones 1–2 (summer) → Zone 3–4 (winter) |
| Forest generalist | Dark-eyed Junco, Red-tailed Hawk | Zones 2, 3, 4 |
| Riparian corridor | Song Sparrow, Yellow Warbler | Zone 5 crossing through all terrestrial zones |
| Marine-forest dual habitat | Marbled Murrelet, Bald Eagle | Zones 2, 6 |
| Steppe-riparian | Bullock's Oriole, Yellow-breasted Chat | Zones 5, 7 |
| Pan-zone raptor | Red-tailed Hawk, American Kestrel | All terrestrial zones |
| Flyway transient | Western Sandpiper, Snow Goose | Zones 5, 6 (staging) → all zones (flyover) |
