## Section 1: Shorebirds (Order Charadriiformes)

The Pacific Northwest coast hosts one of North America's most significant shorebird staging complexes. The estuaries and tidal flats of Washington's outer coast — Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, and the Bowerman Basin unit of the Grays Harbor National Wildlife Refuge — form the northern anchor of the Pacific Americas Flyway, funneling millions of shorebirds between Arctic breeding grounds and wintering areas from California to Patagonia.

### Grays Harbor: Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve

Grays Harbor, Washington, holds designation as a site of Hemispheric Importance within the Western Hemisphere Shorebird Reserve Network (WHSRN), one of only a handful of sites in the contiguous United States to achieve this highest classification (source: O-19). The designation reflects peak spring counts of 500,000 to 1,000,000 shorebirds — predominantly Western Sandpipers and Dunlin — that concentrate on the 3,400 hectares of intertidal mudflat between mid-April and early May (source: G-19, O-19).

The Bowerman Basin unit, a 1,500-acre tidal mudflat and salt marsh at the northeast corner of Grays Harbor, serves as the primary roosting and foraging area during spring migration. At peak passage, shorebird densities on exposed mudflats can exceed 10,000 birds per hectare, creating one of the most spectacular wildlife aggregations in western North America. The birds feed on biofilm (a thin layer of diatoms, bacteria, and organic matter coating mud particles) and on invertebrates including polychaete worms, amphipods, and bivalve spat (source: G-19).

The timing is precise and driven by prey phenology: the spring bloom of benthic diatoms on Grays Harbor mudflats peaks in late April, coinciding with the northward push of Western Sandpipers from California and Mexican wintering grounds. Birds arriving lean from flights of 2,000-4,000 km must refuel rapidly, gaining 1-2% of body mass per day to sustain the next leg to Alaska (source: G-19, O-19).

### Willapa Bay

Willapa Bay, immediately south of Grays Harbor along the Washington coast, holds WHSRN designation as a site of Regional Importance. While smaller in peak shorebird numbers than Grays Harbor (typical spring peak: 50,000-100,000), Willapa Bay provides critical habitat diversity that Grays Harbor lacks: extensive eelgrass beds (*Zostera marina* and *Z. japonica*), oyster beds, and protected tidal channels (source: G-19). Black-bellied Plovers, Dunlin, and dowitchers use Willapa Bay extensively during fall migration (August-October), when the estuary's more sheltered geography buffers against early Pacific storms. The bay also supports significant wintering shorebird populations, particularly Dunlin and Black-bellied Plovers that remain through December-February.

### Bandon Marsh and Oregon Estuaries

Along the Oregon coast, Bandon Marsh NWR, Siletz Bay, Tillamook Bay, and the Coquille River estuary provide additional shorebird staging habitat, though none approach the concentrations of Grays Harbor. Bandon Marsh is notable for its restored tidal wetlands and supports consistent populations of Whimbrel and Marbled Godwit during fall migration (source: G-15, G-19).

### Fraser River Delta (British Columbia)

Though outside the core PNW study area, the Fraser River delta (Boundary Bay, Roberts Bank, Sturgeon Bank) is ecologically continuous with the Pacific Flyway staging system. Boundary Bay holds WHSRN Hemispheric Importance designation and supports 100,000+ Western Sandpipers and Dunlin in spring, with significant numbers of Black-bellied Plovers and dowitchers (source: G-21, O-19). Many shorebirds passing through Grays Harbor continue north to the Fraser delta before the final push to Alaska.

### Western Sandpiper (*Calidris mauri*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (spring/fall); minor winter presence
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 14-17 cm length, 25-29 cm wingspan, 22-35 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding adults show rufous-chestnut scapulars and crown, white underparts with chevron-shaped streaking on flanks; non-breeding birds plain gray-brown above, white below. Bill slightly longer and more drooped at tip than Semipalmated Sandpiper — the key field mark.
**Diet/Foraging:** Biofilm (diatoms, bacteria), polychaetes, amphipods, bivalve spat | Tactile probing and surface pecking | Tidal mudflats, wet sand
**Nesting:** Scrape on ground in dwarf shrub tundra, clutch 4, breeds western Alaska only
**Migration:** Long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor (500K+ spring), Willapa Bay, Boundary Bay (BC), Bandon Marsh (OR)
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** The Western Sandpiper is the keystone shorebird of Pacific coast mudflats, its biofilm-feeding behavior bioturbating sediment surfaces and influencing nutrient cycling. At peak Grays Harbor concentrations, flock movements create predator-swamping effects that benefit co-occurring smaller shorebirds (source: G-19, O-19).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-19, O-02, G-03

### Dunlin (*Calidris alpina*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor and Passage Migrant; abundant October-May
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 16-22 cm length, 32-36 cm wingspan, 44-64 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage distinctive: rufous-brown back, black belly patch diagnostic among PNW sandpipers. Non-breeding birds uniform gray-brown above, white below with faint grayish breast band. Subspecies *C. a. pacifica* (breeds western Alaska) predominates in PNW; *C. a. hudsonia* rare.
**Diet/Foraging:** Polychaetes, amphipods, insects, snails, bivalves | Rapid probing in soft mud, "sewing machine" action | Tidal mudflats, flooded agricultural fields, wet pastures
**Nesting:** Scrape on tundra hummocks, clutch 4, breeds Arctic Alaska and western Canada
**Migration:** Medium-to-long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor (hundreds of thousands in spring), Willapa Bay (major wintering), Skagit River delta (WA), Bandon Marsh (OR), Sauvie Island (OR)
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Dunlin form the backbone of wintering shorebird flocks on PNW estuaries, with flocks of 5,000-20,000 performing coordinated aerial maneuvers (murmurations) when flushed by Peregrine Falcons or Merlins. These predator-prey dynamics structure the spatial use of mudflats across entire estuaries (source: G-19, G-14).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, G-14, O-02, O-19

### Semipalmated Plover (*Charadrius semipalmatus*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (spring and fall); uncommon
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 17-19 cm length, 35-41 cm wingspan, 28-47 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding birds show single black breast band, brown upperparts, orange-based bill with black tip, orange eye-ring and legs. Non-breeding plumage similar but breast band brown, bill all dark. Distinguished from Killdeer by single breast band and smaller size.
**Diet/Foraging:** Polychaetes, amphipods, insects, small crustaceans | Visual run-and-stop foraging on exposed flats | Tidal mudflats, sandy beaches, river gravel bars
**Nesting:** Scrape on gravel or sand, clutch 4, breeds across Arctic and subarctic Canada and Alaska
**Migration:** Long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, outer coast sandy beaches, Columbia River mouth
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Forages in mixed-species flocks with Western Sandpipers and Least Sandpipers on mudflats, but partitions habitat by preferring firmer, sandier substrates near the tide line rather than deep mud.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, O-07

### Killdeer (*Charadrius vociferus*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round); augmented by migrants in winter
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 23-27 cm length, 46-48 cm wingspan, 72-121 g mass
**Plumage:** Two black breast bands diagnostic (all ages), brown upperparts, rufous-orange rump and tail conspicuous in flight. Bright red eye-ring. Sexes similar. Downy chicks precocial with single breast band.
**Diet/Foraging:** Insects (beetles, grasshoppers, fly larvae), earthworms, snails, seeds | Visual run-and-stop on open ground | Agricultural fields, gravel lots, road shoulders, lawns, river gravel bars
**Nesting:** Scrape on bare ground (gravel, rooftops, parking lots, river bars), clutch 3-5, famous broken-wing distraction display
**Migration:** Short-distance to sedentary | PNW residents year-round; northern breeders augment winter populations
**Marine Zone:** Supratidal (occasionally intertidal)
**Interaction:** The most terrestrial and human-adapted shorebird in the PNW, Killdeer exploit agricultural landscapes and urbanized areas inaccessible to other shorebirds. Their broken-wing distraction display is among the most elaborate anti-predator behaviors documented in North American birds.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-14, O-02, O-07

### Black-bellied Plover (*Pluvialis squatarola*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor and Passage Migrant; present September-May
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 27-30 cm length, 56-63 cm wingspan, 160-277 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage striking: jet-black face, breast, and belly contrasting with white-speckled upperparts and white undertail. Non-breeding birds plain gray above, whitish below with faint breast mottling. Black axillaries ("armpits") visible in flight in all plumages — diagnostic field mark separating from American and Pacific Golden-Plovers.
**Diet/Foraging:** Polychaetes, bivalves, crustaceans, insects | Visual forager, run-and-stop technique | Tidal mudflats, sandy beaches, short-grass fields
**Nesting:** Scrape on dry tundra, clutch 4, breeds high Arctic
**Migration:** Long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Boundary Bay (BC), Tillamook Bay (OR), Skagit delta
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Black-bellied Plovers are sentinel species on tidal flats, often the first to detect approaching raptors and give alarm calls that trigger flock movements in other shorebird species. Their large size and upright posture give them a detection advantage over smaller probing species.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, G-14, O-02, O-19

### Black Oystercatcher (*Haematopus bachmani*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round along rocky coastline)
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC | PIF: Continental Watch List (declining populations)
**Size:** 42-47 cm length, 78-82 cm wingspan, 500-700 g mass
**Plumage:** Entirely black plumage, bright red-orange bill (laterally compressed, chisel-shaped), yellow iris, pink legs. Sexes similar; juveniles show duller brownish bill. No seasonal plumage change.
**Diet/Foraging:** Mussels (*Mytilus*), limpets, chitons, barnacles, sea urchins | Pries open or hammers bivalves with specialized laterally compressed bill | Rocky intertidal zone exclusively
**Nesting:** Scrape on rocky shoreline or offshore rocks above high-tide line, clutch 2-3, strongly site-faithful
**Migration:** Sedentary | Post-breeding dispersal to nearby rocky headlands; some short-distance movement to winter roost sites
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal (rocky substrate obligate)
**Interaction:** Black Oystercatchers are indicator species for rocky intertidal ecosystem health. Their dependence on mussel beds creates a direct link to sea star wasting disease — the collapse of *Pisaster ochraceus* (ochre sea star) populations has led to mussel bed expansion in some areas, potentially benefiting oystercatchers, though the broader trophic cascade effects remain under study (source: G-14, G-01).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-01, G-14, O-23, O-02

### Western Snowy Plover (*Charadrius nivosus nivosus*)
**Status:** Resident and partial migrant (breeds along coast March-September; some winter locally)
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-T (Threatened, Pacific coast DPS listed 1993) | IUCN: IUCN-NT (Near Threatened) | WA: ST-E | OR: ST-T
**Size:** 15-17 cm length, 34-37 cm wingspan, 34-58 g mass
**Plumage:** Pale sandy-brown upperparts, white underparts, incomplete breast band (dark lateral patches, not meeting in center — diagnostic vs. Semipalmated Plover). Thin dark bill, dark legs. Breeding males show darker head markings. Extremely cryptic on dry sand — plumage is near-perfect sand camouflage.
**Diet/Foraging:** Beach-dwelling invertebrates: kelp flies, sand hoppers (amphipods), beetles, polychaetes | Visual run-and-stop on dry sand and wrack line | Dry sandy beaches, dune edges, salt flats, dredge spoil islands
**Nesting:** Scrape on open sand above high-tide line, often near driftwood or sparse vegetation; clutch 3; highly vulnerable to human disturbance, predation (corvids, foxes, gulls), and habitat loss. Nest exclosures used at managed sites (source: G-17, G-15).
**Migration:** Partial migrant | Breeding populations on OR coast (Coos Bay, Bandon, Siltcoos) and WA coast (Leadbetter Point, Midway Beach); some winter movement to California. Most WA/OR breeders are sedentary or short-distance migrants.
**Marine Zone:** Supratidal (dry sand above tide line)
**Interaction:** The Western Snowy Plover is the most critically habitat-limited shorebird on the PNW coast. Beach management for plover nesting — including seasonal closures, predator management, and habitat restoration — directly conflicts with recreational use and has been the subject of sustained conservation litigation. Recovery efforts at Leadbetter Point (WA) and several Oregon coast sites have stabilized local populations but the species remains vulnerable to habitat squeeze between rising sea levels and coastal development (source: G-17, G-15).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-17, G-15, G-14, O-02

### Whimbrel (*Numenius phaeopus*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (spring and fall); rare winter records
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 40-46 cm length, 76-89 cm wingspan, 270-493 g mass
**Plumage:** Large, brown-streaked shorebird with distinctive long decurved bill and bold head pattern: dark crown stripes bracketing pale median crown stripe. Brown-streaked above and below. Superficially similar to Long-billed Curlew but smaller with shorter bill and prominent crown stripes.
**Diet/Foraging:** Crabs (especially *Pachygrapsus* and *Hemigrapsus*), polychaetes, bivalves, berries (on tundra) | Probes mud and picks from rock surfaces | Tidal mudflats, rocky intertidal, salt marshes, upper beaches
**Nesting:** Scrape on subarctic tundra, clutch 4, breeds western and northern Alaska
**Migration:** Long-distance (some individuals travel 12,000+ km one-way to southern South America) | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Bandon Marsh (OR), Coos Bay (OR)
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Whimbrels are crab specialists on PNW mudflats, their long decurved bill extracting burrowing crabs from soft sediment. They often forage singly or in small groups, spaced along channels and creek banks within estuaries, showing strong site fidelity to specific foraging territories during multi-week staging stopovers.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, O-19, G-15

### Long-billed Curlew (*Numenius americanus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (interior grasslands); Passage Migrant and winter visitor (coast)
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, AVI-HAB-GRASSLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC | PIF: Continental Watch List
**Size:** 50-65 cm length, 62-68 cm wingspan (body); bill 11-22 cm; 490-950 g mass
**Plumage:** Warm buffy-cinnamon overall, heavily streaked above, with exceptionally long decurved bill — the longest bill of any North American shorebird. Cinnamon underwing linings visible in flight. Lacks the bold head stripes of Whimbrel.
**Diet/Foraging:** Grasshoppers, beetles, caterpillars (on breeding grounds); crabs, shrimp, marine worms (on coast) | Deep probing and surface picking | Native grassland (breeding), tidal mudflats and estuaries (non-breeding)
**Nesting:** Scrape in native bunchgrass prairie, clutch 4, requires large unbroken grassland tracts; breeds in interior PNW (Columbia Basin, eastern OR/WA, ID)
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Breeds interior PNW April-July, migrates to coast and California for winter. Key PNW coastal sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Bandon Marsh
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal (non-breeding season)
**Interaction:** The Long-billed Curlew bridges two threatened PNW habitats: native grassland (breeding) and coastal estuary (wintering). Loss of native bunchgrass prairie to agriculture in the Columbia Basin directly limits breeding populations, while coastal habitat degradation affects wintering carrying capacity. The species thus serves as an indicator for both ecosystems (source: G-14, G-15).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-14, G-15, G-19, O-02

### Marbled Godwit (*Limosa fedoa*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant and Winter Visitor (coast); uncommon
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 40-50 cm length, 70-80 cm wingspan, 285-454 g mass
**Plumage:** Large, warm buffy-cinnamon shorebird with long, slightly upturned bicolored bill (pink base, dark tip). Rich cinnamon-barred plumage overall. Cinnamon underwing linings conspicuous in flight. Non-breeding plumage slightly paler but pattern similar.
**Diet/Foraging:** Polychaetes, bivalves, crustaceans, aquatic insects | Deep probing in soft mud and shallow water | Tidal mudflats, shallow estuarine waters, flooded agricultural fields
**Nesting:** Scrape on northern Great Plains grassland, clutch 4; does not breed in PNW
**Migration:** Medium-to-long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Bandon Marsh (OR), Coos Bay (OR); primarily fall migrant (July-October), smaller numbers winter
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Marbled Godwits forage alongside Whimbrels and dowitchers on PNW mudflats but partition prey by probing depth — the godwit's longer bill reaches deeper-burrowing invertebrates. Flocks are typically small (5-30) compared to the massive peep aggregations.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, O-19

### Short-billed Dowitcher (*Limnodromus griseus*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (primarily fall); uncommon winter visitor
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 25-29 cm length, 46-52 cm wingspan, 73-155 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage richly spotted and barred rufous-orange on breast and flanks (subspecies *L. g. caurinus* — Pacific coast form); non-breeding plain gray above, white below. Distinguished from Long-billed Dowitcher by call (rapid *tu-tu-tu* vs. single sharp *keek*), subtly shorter bill, and habitat preference for saltwater flats.
**Diet/Foraging:** Polychaetes, amphipods, bivalves, snails, insect larvae | Rapid vertical "sewing-machine" probing in shallow water | Tidal mudflats, salt marshes, shallow estuarine pools
**Nesting:** Scrape on muskeg bogs in subarctic Alaska and Canada, clutch 4
**Migration:** Long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Tillamook Bay (OR), Boundary Bay (BC); strong fall passage July-September
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal
**Interaction:** Short-billed and Long-billed Dowitchers present one of the classic identification challenges for PNW birders. The two species partition habitat: Short-billed prefers coastal saltwater mudflats during migration, while Long-billed favors freshwater marshes and flooded fields. However, both species occur together at transitional sites (source: O-07, O-08).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, O-07, O-08

### Long-billed Dowitcher (*Limnodromus scolopaceus*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant and Winter Visitor; common in freshwater habitats
**Bands:** ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 27-30 cm length, 46-52 cm wingspan, 90-160 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage dark rufous-chestnut below with barring on flanks (vs. spotting in Short-billed); non-breeding plain gray. Bill averages slightly longer than Short-billed but overlap is extensive. Best separated by call: sharp single *keek* in flight.
**Diet/Foraging:** Aquatic insect larvae (chironomids), snails, seeds, polychaetes | Rapid vertical probing in shallow water and flooded fields | Freshwater marshes, flooded agricultural fields, sewage ponds, also tidal mudflats
**Nesting:** Scrape on wet sedge tundra, clutch 4, breeds Arctic Alaska and Siberia
**Migration:** Long-distance | Key PNW sites: Malheur NWR (OR), Klamath Basin (OR/CA), Skagit delta (WA), Sauvie Island (OR), Ridgefield NWR (WA); winters more commonly inland than Short-billed
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to freshwater
**Interaction:** Long-billed Dowitchers are abundant users of managed wetlands in the PNW interior, particularly Malheur NWR and the Klamath Basin, where water management for waterfowl also benefits shorebird staging. Their preference for freshwater complements the Short-billed Dowitcher's coastal focus.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, G-15, O-07

### Least Sandpiper (*Calidris minutilla*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant and Winter Visitor; common
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 13-15 cm length, 27-28 cm wingspan, 19-30 g mass
**Plumage:** The smallest shorebird in the world. Brown-streaked upperparts, brown breast band, white belly. Yellow-green legs diagnostic among PNW peeps (Western and Semipalmated Sandpipers have dark legs). Bill fine and slightly drooped. Non-breeding plumage grayer above but leg color persists.
**Diet/Foraging:** Amphipods, insect larvae, small snails, seeds | Visual picking and shallow probing at mud edges | Upper mudflat margins, salt marsh edges, freshwater pond margins, wet meadows
**Nesting:** Scrape in subarctic muskeg and bog, clutch 4
**Migration:** Medium-to-long-distance | Key PNW sites: Grays Harbor, Willapa Bay, Malheur NWR, Klamath Basin, sewage treatment ponds throughout interior valleys
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to freshwater (upper margin specialist)
**Interaction:** Least Sandpipers partition habitat from Western Sandpipers by foraging at higher tidal elevations — the upper edges of mudflats, salt marsh borders, and vegetated pool margins — rather than the open mud favored by Western and Semipalmated Sandpipers. This vertical zonation reduces direct competition in mixed-species flocks.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-19, O-02, O-07

### Sanderling (*Calidris alba*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor and Passage Migrant; common on sandy beaches
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 18-21 cm length, 35-39 cm wingspan, 40-100 g mass
**Plumage:** Non-breeding plumage pale — palest of all small sandpipers: clean white below, pale gray above, bold white wing stripe in flight. Black bill, black legs. Breeding plumage (rarely seen in PNW) rufous-brown on head and breast. Lacks hind toe (unique among PNW Calidris).
**Diet/Foraging:** Sand crabs (mole crabs, *Emerita*), amphipods, isopods, polychaetes | Characteristic chase-the-waves foraging: runs with retreating waves, probes wet sand, retreats with incoming surf | Sandy ocean beaches exclusively in non-breeding season
**Nesting:** Scrape on high Arctic stony tundra, clutch 4; one of the most northerly-breeding shorebirds
**Migration:** Long-distance (some individuals winter as far south as southern Chile/Argentina) | Key PNW sites: Long Beach Peninsula (WA), Cannon Beach (OR), all exposed sandy ocean beaches
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal (sandy beach obligate in non-breeding season)
**Interaction:** The Sanderling's wave-chasing behavior makes it the most recognizable shorebird to the general public on PNW ocean beaches. It is a sandy beach specialist, rarely seen on mudflats, partitioning habitat from the mudflat-specialist Western Sandpiper and Dunlin almost completely.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** O-02, O-07, G-19

### Red-necked Phalarope (*Phalaropus lobatus*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (pelagic and coastal); abundant offshore spring and fall
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 18-19 cm length, 31-34 cm wingspan, 25-50 g mass
**Plumage:** Reversed sexual dimorphism — breeding females more brightly colored than males. Breeding female: dark gray head, bright rufous neck stripe, white throat, dark back with buffy stripes. Breeding male: duller version. Non-breeding: gray above, white below, dark eye patch — "phalarope mask." Females brighter; males provide all parental care.
**Diet/Foraging:** Zooplankton (copepods), insect larvae, small crustaceans | Spinning in tight circles on water surface to create upwelling vortex that brings prey within reach — unique foraging behavior among shorebirds | Pelagic ocean surface, coastal waters, freshwater lakes during migration
**Nesting:** Scrape near freshwater ponds on Arctic tundra, clutch 4, polyandrous — female may lay for multiple males
**Migration:** Long-distance pelagic | Migrates offshore in large numbers; concentrations at oceanic upwelling zones off WA/OR coast (source: G-01, O-23)
**Marine Zone:** Pelagic to nearshore
**Interaction:** Red-necked Phalaropes are the most pelagic of PNW shorebirds, spending most of their non-breeding life at sea. Their spinning foraging behavior exploits the same zooplankton concentrations used by baleen whales and small pelagic fish, making phalarope aggregations an indicator of productive upwelling zones off the PNW coast.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-01, O-23, O-02, G-03

### Red Phalarope (*Phalaropus fulicarius*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant (pelagic); uncommon to locally common offshore
**Bands:** ELEV-DEEP-MARINE, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 20-22 cm length, 37-40 cm wingspan, 37-77 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding female spectacular: dark rufous-red underparts, white face patch, black crown, yellow-based bill. Males duller. Non-breeding birds very similar to Red-necked Phalarope but slightly larger, with thicker bill, plainer gray back, and broader dark eye patch. Most PNW records are non-breeding birds at sea.
**Diet/Foraging:** Zooplankton, small jellyfish, fish eggs, crustaceans | Spinning and surface-picking on open ocean | Pelagic waters, occasionally driven onshore by storms
**Nesting:** Scrape on Arctic tundra near ponds, clutch 4, polyandrous
**Migration:** Long-distance pelagic | Winters at sea in tropical eastern Pacific; passes through PNW offshore waters mainly September-November. Storm-wreck events occasionally push birds to inland lakes and rivers.
**Marine Zone:** Pelagic
**Interaction:** More truly pelagic than Red-necked Phalarope, Red Phalaropes associate with floating Sargassum, jellyfish aggregations, and oceanic fronts. Storm-driven onshore movements (known as "wrecks") occasionally deposit hundreds of birds on interior PNW lakes, where they appear incongruously on freshwater — a phenomenon well documented by PNW birders (source: O-02, O-20).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-01, O-23, O-02

### Wilson's Phalarope (*Phalaropus tricolor*)

### Rocky Intertidal Shorebird Guild: A Note on Community Ecology

The PNW rocky coastline supports a distinctive shorebird guild — Black Turnstone, Surfbird, Rock Sandpiper, Wandering Tattler, and Black Oystercatcher — that is ecologically distinct from the mudflat-associated shorebird community at Grays Harbor and Willapa Bay. These five species share rocky intertidal habitat but partition it by foraging technique (flipping, prying, probing, hammering), position in the tidal zone (high, mid, low intertidal), prey size (barnacles vs. mussels vs. limpets vs. small crabs), and activity timing (some species forage through high tide on emergent rocks while others wait for exposure).

This guild has no equivalent on the East Coast of North America, where rocky intertidal shorebird communities are depauperate by comparison. The PNW's extensive rocky coastline — from the Olympic Peninsula's sea stacks to Oregon's basalt headlands — provides the physical template for this community, while the productive California Current upwelling system fuels the invertebrate prey base (source: G-01, G-14).

## Section 2: Alcids (Family Alcidae)

The alcids — murres, murrelets, auklets, and puffins — are the ecological equivalents of penguins in the Northern Hemisphere: wing-propelled pursuit divers that spend most of their lives at sea and come ashore only to breed. The Pacific Northwest coastline supports 8 regularly occurring alcid species, ranging from the abundant Common Murre to the endangered Marbled Murrelet and the precipitously declining Tufted Puffin.

### The Old-Growth-Ocean Connection: Marbled Murrelet Ecology

No species better illustrates the ecological linkage between old-growth forest and ocean in the PNW than the Marbled Murrelet. This small alcid is unique among seabirds worldwide in nesting on moss-covered branches of old-growth conifers, sometimes 50+ km inland and 60+ m above the ground. The nesting requirement — large, moss-laden horizontal branches found only in trees 200+ years old — ties the species' reproductive success directly to the fate of coastal old-growth forests, making the Marbled Murrelet a flagship species for old-growth conservation (source: G-09, G-13, P-04, P-05).

The inland flight path of murrelets commuting between ocean foraging grounds and old-growth nests has been documented through radar studies and radio-telemetry. Adults make high-speed (80-100 km/h) flights at dawn and dusk, following river valleys and ridgelines. The loss of old-growth forest to logging has fragmented this commuting landscape, increasing flight distances and exposing nesting adults to higher predation risk from corvids at forest edges (source: P-04, P-05, G-24).

### Colony Declines and Conservation

PNW alcid breeding colonies have experienced significant declines over the past century. The Common Murre colony at Three Arch Rocks (OR) once numbered over 250,000 birds but has fluctuated dramatically due to disturbance, oil spills, and El Nino-driven prey failures. Tufted Puffin colonies throughout Washington have declined by an estimated 80-90% since the 1970s, leading to the species' listing as State Endangered in Washington (source: G-01, G-14, O-23).

Colony declines have multiple interacting drivers: warming ocean temperatures reducing forage fish (sand lance, smelt, herring) availability; increasing Bald Eagle disturbance at colony sites (a paradoxical consequence of eagle recovery); marine pollution; and gillnet bycatch. The combination of low reproductive rates (most alcids produce a single egg per year) and long generation times means that population recovery is inherently slow even when threats are reduced (source: G-01, O-23).

### Pursuit Diving Ecology

Alcids are the only PNW seabird family that uses wing-propelled underwater pursuit diving as a primary foraging strategy. Common Murres regularly dive to 60-100 m (deepest recorded: ~180 m), while the smaller Cassin's Auklet and Rhinoceros Auklet typically forage in the upper 30-40 m. This diving capability allows alcids to exploit forage fish schools and zooplankton layers that are inaccessible to surface-feeding seabirds like gulls and terns (source: G-01, O-23).

The wing morphology of alcids represents an evolutionary compromise: wings short enough to serve as efficient underwater flippers but long enough to sustain flight — barely. The result is rapid, buzzy flight with very high wingbeat frequency, and alcids are among the most energetically expensive fliers of all birds. The largest alcids (murres) approach the upper size limit for wing-propelled flight and diving simultaneously; the extinct Great Auk (*Pinguinus impennis*) crossed that threshold and became flightless.

### Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round in nearshore waters; nests in old-growth forest)
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-LOWLAND (old-growth nesting)
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-T (Threatened, listed 1992) | IUCN: IUCN-EN (Endangered) | WA: ST-T | OR: ST-T
**Size:** 23-25 cm length, 40-43 cm wingspan, 188-268 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage: cryptic dark brown mottled and barred overall — camouflage for moss-branch nesting. **Diet/Foraging:** Sand lance (*Ammodytes*), Pacific herring, smelt, euphausiids (krill), amphipods | Wing-propelled pursuit diving in nearshore waters, typically within 2 km of shore and in waters <30 m deep | Nearshore marine waters, bays, fjords
**Nesting:** Moss platform on large horizontal branch of old-growth conifer (Douglas-fir, Sitka spruce, western hemlock, western red cedar), 30-60+ m above ground, typically in trees >200 years old; single egg; no nest construction — egg laid directly on moss pad. Corvid predation (Steller's Jay, Common Raven) at nest sites increases dramatically at forest edges created by clearcuts, creating an indirect effect of fragmentation beyond simple habitat loss (source: P-04, P-05, G-24). The species' at-sea distribution is a sensitive indicator of nearshore marine productivity, particularly sand lance and herring availability.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Marbled Murrelets forage in nearshore waters that overlap with juvenile salmon rearing habitat, and both species depend on the same forage fish base (herring, sand lance). The connection is indirect but ecologically significant: healthy nearshore ecosystems support both murrelet foraging and salmon survival.

### Common Murre (*Uria aalge*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round in offshore waters; breeds colonially on sea cliffs)

**Plumage:** Breeding: entirely sooty black with large white wing patches (upper and underwing), bright red-orange legs and feet, red mouth lining. Non-breeding: white below, mottled gray-brown and white above, retaining white wing patches. Red feet visible at nesting sites. Smaller and more slender-billed than murres.
**Diet/Foraging:** Benthic fish (sculpins, blennies, gunnels), shrimp, polychaetes, crabs | Pursuit diving in shallow nearshore waters (<30 m depth), primarily near-bottom foraging | Sheltered nearshore waters, bays, harbors, protected coastline
**Nesting:** Cavity nester — uses rock crevices, boulder piles, under driftwood, abandoned burrows, and human structures (wharves, riprap, jetties); clutch 1-2; semicolonial; visible carrying fish to nest cavities (fish carried crosswise in bill)
**Migration:** Sedentary | Year-round resident along PNW coast; slight dispersal from nesting sites in winter
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore (bottom-foraging specialist)
**Interaction:** Pigeon Guillemots are the most visible nearshore alcid in the PNW, commonly seen from docks, jetties, and ferry terminals throughout Puget Sound and the outer coast. Their use of human structures for nesting (wharf pilings, riprap) makes them unusually tolerant of coastal development compared to other alcids. Fish-carrying flights to nest sites provide a conspicuous indicator of nearshore prey availability.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-01, G-14, O-02, O-23

### Rhinoceros Auklet (*Cerorhinca monocerata*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round; colonial breeder on offshore islands)
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 35-38 cm length, 56-58 cm wingspan, 480-620 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding: dark gray-brown overall with two prominent white facial plumes (above and below eye), distinctive pale horn ("rhinoceros") at base of orange bill. Non-breeding: duller, no horn or plumes, bill less colorful. Thick-set, chunky body with relatively large head. Flight heavy and direct with rapid wingbeats.
**Diet/Foraging:** Pacific sand lance, Pacific herring, smelt, anchovy, squid, euphausiids | Wing-propelled pursuit diving, typically 20-40 m depth | Offshore and nearshore waters; often feeds at dusk and dawn
**Nesting:** Burrow nester — excavates 1-5 m burrow in soil on offshore islands; single egg; chick fed in burrow for ~50 days. Major PNW colonies: Protection Island (WA) — 36,000+ pairs, one of the largest colonies in the world; Destruction Island (WA); Smith Island (WA); Triangle Island (BC) (source: G-01, G-14, O-23).
**Migration:** Sedentary to short-distance dispersal | Remains in PNW waters year-round; post-breeding dispersal offshore
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore to pelagic
**Interaction:** Protection Island NWR in the Strait of Juan de Fuca hosts approximately 70% of Washington's breeding Rhinoceros Auklets and is one of the largest colonies in the world. Adults return to burrows at dusk carrying multiple fish crosswise in their bills — a behavior shared with Atlantic Puffins and functionally convergent. The colony's productivity is a sensitive index of local forage fish availability, particularly sand lance abundance (source: G-01, G-14).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Rhinoceros Auklets and juvenile Pacific salmon share forage fish prey (sand lance, herring). Auklet diet studies at Protection Island provide an indirect index of forage fish availability in salmon rearing habitat.
**Sources:** G-01, G-14, O-23, G-20

### Cassin's Auklet (*Ptychoramphus aleuticus*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round; breeds on offshore islands)
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-NT (Near Threatened)
**Size:** 20-23 cm length, 37-40 cm wingspan, 150-200 g mass
**Plumage:** Uniformly dark gray overall with paler gray belly; small white crescents above and below eye. Pale base to lower mandible. No seasonal plumage change. Small, compact, nondescript — the least visually distinctive alcid. Nocturnal at colonies.
**Diet/Foraging:** Euphausiids (krill), copepods, amphipods, larval fish | Wing-propelled pursuit diving in upper water column, typically <30 m | Offshore waters over continental shelf, particularly at upwelling zones
**Nesting:** Burrow nester on offshore islands; single egg; strictly nocturnal at colony (to avoid gull predation). PNW colonies small and scattered; largest colonies in California (Farallon Islands) and British Columbia (Triangle Island).
**Migration:** Sedentary to nomadic | Moves with prey concentrations; large-scale die-offs documented during warm-water events (El Nino, marine heatwaves — the 2014-2016 "blob" caused massive Cassin's Auklet mortality on PNW beaches) (source: O-23, G-01).
**Marine Zone:** Pelagic to nearshore
**Interaction:** Cassin's Auklets are zooplankton specialists, making them one of the most climate-sensitive seabirds in the PNW. The 2014-2016 northeast Pacific marine heatwave ("the blob") caused unprecedented Cassin's Auklet die-offs, with thousands of emaciated birds washing up on Oregon and Washington beaches. Their mortality serves as an early warning indicator of marine ecosystem disruption driven by ocean warming (source: O-23, G-01).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Cassin's Auklets feed on the same zooplankton (euphausiids, copepods) that sustains juvenile salmon in offshore waters. Mass auklet mortality during warm-water events correlates with reduced salmon survival, linking both species to the same oceanographic conditions.
**Sources:** G-01, O-23, G-14

### Tufted Puffin (*Fratercula cirrhata*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (colonial, offshore islands); rare in winter
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL (petitioned for listing) | IUCN: IUCN-LC | WA: ST-E (State Endangered)
**Size:** 36-41 cm length, 63-66 cm wingspan, 660-840 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding plumage unmistakable: jet-black body, white face, long golden head plumes swept back from behind eye, massive bright orange-red compressed bill. Non-breeding: face dusky, plumes absent, bill smaller and duller. Juveniles dark overall with small dark bill. One of the most charismatic seabirds in the PNW.
**Diet/Foraging:** Pacific sand lance, smelt, herring, squid, polychaetes, euphausiids | Wing-propelled pursuit diving, typically 20-60 m | Offshore waters, primarily over continental shelf
**Nesting:** Burrow nester on grassy slopes of offshore islands; single egg; chick fed in burrow for ~45 days. PNW colonies have declined catastrophically: Haystack Rock (Cannon Beach, OR), Destruction Island (WA), Alexander Island (WA), and scattered small colonies. Washington populations declined ~80-90% from historical levels (source: G-01, G-14, O-23).
**Migration:** Partial migrant | After breeding, disperses offshore into North Pacific; winter distribution poorly known. Some remain in PNW waters, others move into open ocean.
**Marine Zone:** Pelagic to nearshore (breeding season nearshore)
**Interaction:** The Tufted Puffin's dramatic decline in Washington — from an estimated 25,000+ breeding birds in the 1970s to fewer than 3,000 today — represents one of the most severe seabird population crashes in the PNW. Causes include reduced forage fish availability linked to warming ocean temperatures, increased Bald Eagle disturbance at colony sites, and possibly shifting prey distributions. The Haystack Rock colony at Cannon Beach (OR) is one of the most accessible puffin viewing sites in the lower 48 states and an important ecotourism asset (source: G-01, G-14, O-23).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Tufted Puffins depend on the same forage fish prey base (sand lance, herring, smelt) as Pacific salmon. Puffin colony productivity is a direct indicator of forage fish availability in salmon habitat, and both taxa face similar threats from ocean warming and prey shifts.
**Sources:** G-01, G-14, O-23, G-17

### Ancient Murrelet (*Synthliboramphus antiquus*)
**Status:** Rare winter visitor and passage migrant; breeds in BC
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-DEEP-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 24-27 cm length, 43-45 cm wingspan, 195-260 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding: black head and throat with white streaks on sides of crown (the "ancient" or aged appearance), blue-gray back, white underparts, short pale bill. Non-breeding: similar but reduced head streaking. Small, compact alcid with distinctive pale bill.
**Diet/Foraging:** Euphausiids, copepods, small fish (sand lance, herring larvae) | Wing-propelled pursuit diving | Offshore and nearshore waters
**Nesting:** Burrow nester on forested offshore islands; single egg (sometimes 2); chick is superprecocial — departs nest at 2 days old (before it can fly), parents call from ocean at night, chick scrambles down to water and joins parents at sea. **Migration:** Partial migrant | Post-breeding dispersal brings birds into WA and OR waters October-March; occasionally wrecked onshore during storms
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore to pelagic
**Interaction:** The Ancient Murrelet's superprecocial chick departure strategy — leaving the nest burrow at 2 days old, unable to fly, scrambling to the sea at night guided by parental calls — is one of the most extraordinary life-history strategies among North American birds. This strategy evolved to reduce nest predation by introduced rats and native predators on breeding islands. Rat eradication programs on BC islands have benefited murrelet colonies (source: G-21, O-23).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-21, O-23, O-02

## Section 3: Gulls and Terns (Family Laridae)

Gulls and terns are the most visible and familiar seabirds of the PNW coast, yet the group harbors complexities that belie their commonness: extensive hybridization between gull species, dramatic colony management controversies involving terns and salmon, and wide-ranging ecological roles from coastal scavenger to transoceanic migrant.

### Gull Hybridization in the PNW

The PNW is a zone of extensive hybridization between Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*) and Western Gull (*Larus occidentalis*), creating a hybrid swarm along the Washington and Oregon coast that has challenged taxonomists and field birders for decades. In the Puget Sound region and along the outer Washington coast, "pure" Glaucous-winged and Western Gulls interbreed freely, producing a continuum of plumages with intermediate wingtip patterns (ranging from the pale gray tips of pure Glaucous-winged to the jet-black tips of pure Western). Many PNW gull researchers refer to these hybrids as "Olympic Gulls" informally, though this has no formal taxonomic status (source: O-03, O-02, G-14).

The hybrid zone extends from approximately the southern Puget Sound south through the Oregon coast, with "pure" Glaucous-winged Gulls predominating north of the San Juan Islands and "pure" Western Gulls south of Coos Bay. Between these endpoints, the majority of large white-headed gulls are hybrids of varying parentage. This situation complicates field identification, population monitoring, and conservation assessment — a Glaucous-winged Gull count for Washington inevitably includes a substantial proportion of hybrid birds (source: O-02, G-14).

Herring Gulls (*Larus argentatus*) also hybridize with Glaucous-winged Gulls where their ranges overlap in southern Alaska, and some of these hybrid individuals winter in the PNW, adding further complexity to the identification challenge.

### Caspian Tern Colony Management: East Sand Island

The management of the Caspian Tern colony on East Sand Island at the mouth of the Columbia River represents one of the most contentious wildlife management issues in the PNW, pitting seabird conservation against salmonid recovery. The East Sand Island colony grew to become the largest Caspian Tern breeding colony in North America by the early 2000s, with 8,000-10,000 nesting pairs. Diet studies documented that Caspian Terns from this colony consumed significant numbers of juvenile salmonids (smolts) migrating downriver through the Columbia estuary, including ESA-listed steelhead and Chinook salmon (source: G-20, G-17).

In response, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers and USFWS implemented a management plan to reduce the East Sand Island colony by approximately two-thirds through habitat modification (reducing available nesting area) and creation of alternative colony sites in San Francisco Bay, interior Oregon, and other Pacific coast locations. The goal was to redistribute terns across multiple smaller colonies rather than maintain a single mega-colony at the Columbia mouth. The plan was controversial, drawing opposition from seabird conservation organizations who argued that tern predation was a minor factor in salmon decline compared to dams, habitat loss, and ocean conditions (source: G-20, G-17, O-23).

The management actions reduced the East Sand Island colony to approximately 3,500-4,000 pairs by the 2020s, with new colonies established at alternative sites. Whether the redistribution has measurably improved salmonid survival remains debated, though the reduced tern numbers at the Columbia mouth have lowered smolt predation rates at that specific chokepoint (source: G-20).

### Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round); the dominant large gull of the PNW
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 61-68 cm length, 132-142 cm wingspan, 730-1,350 g mass
**Plumage:** Adults: pale gray mantle with gray wingtips (same shade as mantle — unique among large PNW gulls), white head and underparts, pink legs, yellow bill with red spot. Immatures take 4 years to reach adult plumage, progressing through mottled brown stages. Hybridizes extensively with Western Gull (see narrative above), producing birds with intermediate darker wingtips.
**Diet/Foraging:** Extreme generalist: fish, invertebrates (mussels, sea urchins, crabs), garbage, carrion, eggs, chicks of other seabirds, berries | Scavenging, kleptoparasitism, predation, surface dipping, shallow diving | All coastal and urban habitats from parking lots to pelagic waters
**Nesting:** Platform nest of vegetation on ground, flat rooftops, jetties, offshore rocks; clutch 2-3; colonial. Major colonies throughout San Juan Islands, Protection Island, Puget Sound islands, outer coast rocks.
**Migration:** Sedentary to short-distance | Year-round resident; some dispersal to harbors and urban areas in winter
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to nearshore
**Interaction:** The Glaucous-winged Gull is the ecological generalist par excellence of the PNW coast, exploiting natural food sources from rocky intertidal to pelagic surface, plus human-associated food (landfills, fishing boats, fast-food parking lots). Its population has benefited enormously from human food subsidies, and its abundance makes it the primary egg and chick predator at other seabird colonies, including murre and puffin sites, creating a management tension between gull abundance and colonial seabird conservation (source: G-01, G-14, O-23).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Glaucous-winged Gulls scavenge salmon carcasses on spawning rivers and feed on salmon eggs during spawning runs. They also consume juvenile salmonid smolts at river mouths and hatchery release sites, though at lower rates than Caspian Terns.
**Sources:** G-01, G-14, O-02, O-23

### Western Gull (*Larus occidentalis*)
**Status:** Resident (year-round); common on outer coast, less common inland
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 60-66 cm length, 130-140 cm wingspan, 800-1,250 g mass
**Plumage:** Adults: dark slate-gray mantle (noticeably darker than Glaucous-winged Gull), jet-black wingtips with white mirrors, white head and underparts, pink legs, yellow bill with red and black spot at gonys. 4-year maturation. "Pure" Western Gulls in PNW mainly south of Coos Bay (OR); northward, intergrades with Glaucous-winged Gull predominate.
**Diet/Foraging:** Fish, marine invertebrates (sea urchins, mussels, crabs), carrion, garbage, seabird eggs and chicks | Scavenging, predation, surface-dipping, dropping hard-shelled prey from height | Rocky intertidal, harbors, offshore waters
**Nesting:** Ground nest on offshore rocks and islands; clutch 2-3; colonial. Breeds primarily on Oregon and California offshore rocks.
**Migration:** Sedentary | Year-round along coast; post-breeding dispersal may bring some birds northward into Washington
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to nearshore
**Interaction:** Western Gulls are significant predators at colonial seabird breeding sites, consuming eggs and chicks of murres, cormorants, and auklets on offshore rocks. In Oregon, the Western Gull × Glaucous-winged Gull hybrid zone creates a gradient in appearance that tracks latitude — southern Oregon birds resemble pure Western Gulls, while northern Oregon/Washington birds increasingly show Glaucous-winged influence (source: G-01, O-02).
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Similar to Glaucous-winged Gull, Western Gulls scavenge salmon carcasses and prey on smolts at river mouths, contributing to the gull guild's role in the salmon food web.
**Sources:** G-01, O-02, O-03, G-14

### Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor; uncommon but regular
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 56-66 cm length, 120-150 cm wingspan, 800-1,250 g mass
**Plumage:** Adults: medium-gray mantle (intermediate between dark Western and pale Glaucous-winged), black wingtips with white mirrors, pink legs, yellow bill with red gonys spot. Paler-eyed than Western Gull. 4-year maturation. PNW birds are predominantly *L. a. smithsonianus* (American Herring Gull). Complicated identification in context of Glaucous-winged × Western hybrid swarm.
**Diet/Foraging:** Fish, invertebrates, carrion, garbage | Generalist scavenger and predator | Harbors, landfills, coastline, inland lakes and rivers
**Nesting:** Does NOT breed in PNW (breeds northern North America, Great Lakes region)
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Arrives PNW October-November, departs March-April. Most frequent in Puget Sound, lower Columbia River, and Willamette Valley.
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to freshwater
**Interaction:** Herring Gulls add to the complexity of PNW large gull identification, particularly because they hybridize with Glaucous-winged Gulls in Alaska, and some hybrid individuals winter in the PNW. Distinguishing Herring Gulls from Glaucous-winged × Western Gull hybrids requires careful assessment of mantle shade, wingtip pattern, head shape, and eye color — a skill challenge that has made PNW "gull watching" a specialized birding discipline (source: O-02, O-07, O-03).
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** O-02, O-07, O-03

### California Gull (*Larus californicus*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (interior lakes) and Winter Visitor (coast); common
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 47-54 cm length, 122-137 cm wingspan, 430-1,045 g mass
**Plumage:** Adults: medium-gray mantle, black wingtips with white mirrors, yellow-green legs (not pink — key field mark vs. Herring and Western Gulls), yellow bill with red and black spots at gonys. Dark eye (darker than Herring Gull). 3-4 year maturation. Slimmer and more elegant than Herring or Western Gulls.
**Diet/Foraging:** Insects (major crop pest consumer during breeding season), fish, brine shrimp, garbage, carrion | Aerial hawking for insects, surface-dipping, scavenging | Inland lakes and agricultural fields (breeding), coast and urban areas (non-breeding)
**Nesting:** Ground nest on islands in interior alkaline lakes; clutch 2-3; colonial. Major PNW colonies at Malheur NWR, Summer Lake (OR), Great Basin lakes.
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Breeds at interior lakes April-August, moves to coast and valleys September-March. Abundant in urban areas of Portland, Seattle, and Willamette Valley during winter.
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to freshwater (seasonal shift)
**Interaction:** California Gulls undergo one of the most dramatic habitat shifts of any PNW bird: breeding on alkaline desert lakes in summer, then wintering in coastal cities and estuaries. At inland breeding colonies, they are significant consumers of agricultural pest insects (grasshoppers, Mormon crickets), providing an ecosystem service to ranchers and farmers in the Great Basin — the origin of the California Gull's honored status as Utah's state bird.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-15, O-02, G-19

### Mew Gull (*Larus canus*)
**Status:** Winter Visitor; common in western PNW lowlands
**Bands:** ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 40-46 cm length, 100-115 cm wingspan, 315-550 g mass
**Plumage:** Adults: medium-gray mantle, black wingtips with large white mirrors, unmarked yellow bill (no red or black spots — key field mark), yellow-green legs, dark eye, rounded head shape. Small for a white-headed gull. 3-year maturation (faster than larger gulls). "Cute" proportions — large eye, rounded head — distinctive among PNW gulls.
**Diet/Foraging:** Earthworms, insects, small fish, berries, garbage | Forages on lawns, agricultural fields, parking lots, mudflats, river bars | Urban lawns, playing fields, estuaries, rivers, farmland
**Nesting:** Does NOT breed in PNW (breeds Alaska and northern BC); platform nest on ground, tree, or stump near water
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Arrives PNW September-October, departs April-May. Abundant on urban lawns and playing fields in Portland, Seattle, and Willamette Valley during winter. Often the most numerous gull on interior urban lawns.
**Marine Zone:** Intertidal to freshwater
**Interaction:** Mew Gulls are the characteristic "lawn gull" of PNW cities in winter, foraging for earthworms on playing fields, parks, and school grounds in flocks of dozens to hundreds. Their reliance on urban green spaces makes them the most visible and approachable gull species for PNW urban residents, forming many people's primary experience with gull ecology.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Mew Gulls forage along salmon spawning rivers and consume salmon eggs and carcass fragments, particularly on the Skagit, Nooksack, and other lowland salmon rivers during winter spawning runs.

### Bonaparte's Gull (*Chroicocephalus philadelphia*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant and Winter Visitor; common
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 28-38 cm length, 76-84 cm wingspan, 150-230 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding: complete black hood, white eye crescents, thin black bill, red-orange legs, pale gray mantle, prominent white leading edge to outer wing (flash pattern). Non-breeding: white head with dark ear spot, black bill, pink legs. Small, elegant, tern-like flight. 2-year maturation.
**Diet/Foraging:** Small fish, euphausiids, insects (especially emergent aquatic insects), amphipods | Plunge-dipping, surface-dipping, aerial hawking for insects | Nearshore marine waters, river mouths, lake surfaces
**Nesting:** Does NOT breed in PNW (breeds in boreal forest of interior Alaska and Canada — uniquely among gulls, nests in coniferous trees)
**Migration:** Medium-to-long-distance | Abundant in PNW during fall (September-November) and spring (March-May) migration; moderate winter numbers. Concentrations at Columbia River mouth, Puget Sound, and along outer coast during herring spawning events.
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore to intertidal
**Interaction:** Bonaparte's Gulls congregate in large feeding flocks at herring spawning events in Puget Sound and the San Juan Islands, where their plunge-diving behavior contrasts with the surface-scavenging of larger gulls. They are the most tern-like PNW gull in flight and foraging style, and their boreal-forest tree-nesting habit is unique among the world's gulls.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Bonaparte's Gulls feed at herring spawning aggregations that are also critical prey for adult and juvenile salmon. Their presence in Puget Sound during herring runs connects them to the forage fish web underlying salmon ecology.
**Sources:** O-02, G-14, G-01

### Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*)
**Status:** Summer Breeder (colonial); present April-September
**Bands:** ELEV-INTERTIDAL, ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-RIPARIAN
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 47-54 cm length, 127-140 cm wingspan, 530-782 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding: black cap, massive coral-red bill (the most prominent field mark — largest bill of any tern), pale gray mantle, white underparts, shallow tail fork, black underwing primaries. Non-breeding: streaked crown, duller bill. The largest tern in the world. Heavy, direct flight resembling a gull more than the buoyant flight of smaller terns.
**Diet/Foraging:** Fish exclusively — juvenile salmonids (smolts), perch, shad, herring, sculpin | Plunge-diving from height (10-15 m), also kleptoparasitizes other terns and gulls | Estuaries, rivers, large lakes, nearshore marine
**Nesting:** Scrape on bare sand or gravel on islands; clutch 1-3; colonial. East Sand Island (Columbia River mouth) — major colony, historically 8,000-10,000 pairs, reduced to ~3,500-4,000 through management (see narrative above). Other PNW colonies at Crescent Island (Columbia River), various managed sites (source: G-20, G-17).
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Arrives PNW April, departs September-October; winters along California and Mexican coasts
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore to freshwater
**Interaction:** The Caspian Tern's consumption of ESA-listed juvenile salmonids at the Columbia River mouth has made it the center of the most significant seabird-fisheries management conflict in the PNW. Individual terns from the East Sand Island colony consumed estimated thousands of smolts per breeding season, with steelhead and upper Columbia spring Chinook particularly affected at this migration chokepoint. Colony reduction through habitat management has been implemented but remains controversial (source: G-20, G-17, O-23). See Section 3 narrative on East Sand Island colony management.
**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Caspian Terns are the most significant avian predator of juvenile salmonids in the Columbia River basin. Diet studies documented that the East Sand Island colony consumed millions of smolts annually, representing a measurable source of mortality for ESA-listed salmon and steelhead populations. This direct predation pressure drove the colony management actions described above.
**Sources:** G-20, G-17, O-23, G-01

### Common Tern (*Sterna hirundo*)
**Status:** Passage Migrant; uncommon
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-INTERTIDAL
**Conservation:** ESA: ESA-NL | IUCN: IUCN-LC
**Size:** 31-35 cm length, 77-98 cm wingspan, 97-146 g mass
**Plumage:** Breeding: black cap, red bill with black tip, pale gray mantle, white underparts with gray wash, deeply forked tail, translucent primaries visible from below in flight. **Migration:** Long-distance | Passes through PNW in small numbers during fall (August-October) migration; rare in spring. Primarily coastal during passage.
**Marine Zone:** Nearshore
**Interaction:** Common Terns are uncommon in the PNW compared to the abundant Caspian Tern, and most records are of migrating birds along the coast or at large inland water bodies. ## Section 4: Loons and Grebes

The diving birds of orders Gaviiformes and Podicipediformes occupy a distinct ecological niche in the PNW's marine and freshwater systems. Loons are primarily winter visitors to the Salish Sea and outer coast, arriving from boreal breeding grounds in late autumn and departing by late spring. Their presence in Puget Sound, the Strait of Juan de Fuca, and along the Oregon coast represents one of the region's most reliable winter bird spectacles. Grebes span the full freshwater-to-marine gradient, with some species breeding on inland marshes and lakes while others form massive wintering rafts on coastal bays.

The ecological significance of this group lies in their position as mid-trophic piscivores. Loons and grebes consume vast quantities of small fish, invertebrates, and crustaceans, translating primary productivity in nearshore waters into biomass accessible to higher predators. Their sensitivity to water quality, prey availability, and disturbance makes them effective bioindicators for the health of PNW aquatic systems. The Western Grebe and Clark's Grebe sibling species complex is one of the most studied cases of sympatric speciation in North American ornithology, with both species co-occurring on PNW breeding lakes and wintering waters.

### Loon Winter Ecology in Puget Sound

The Salish Sea is the most important wintering area for loons in the Pacific Northwest. Common Loons, Pacific Loons, and Red-throated Loons all converge on these protected inland waters from October through April, drawn by abundant forage fish populations including Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*), Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), and surf smelt (*Hypomesus pretiosus*). The three species partition the marine habitat vertically and spatially: Common Loons favor deeper channels and open water where they pursue larger fish at depths of 10-60 m; Pacific Loons concentrate in mid-depth nearshore zones, often forming loose flocks of dozens to hundreds; Red-throated Loons prefer shallower inshore waters and are the most likely loon found in estuaries and river mouths (source: O-14, O-20).

Winter loon densities in Puget Sound have been monitored by WDFW through aerial and boat-based surveys since the 1990s. These surveys document significant variation in loon abundance correlated with forage fish availability, water temperature, and disturbance levels from vessel traffic (source: G-14). Marine heat wave events in 2014-2016 ("The Blob") and subsequent warm-water anomalies caused measurable declines in forage fish stocks, with cascading effects on loon winter condition and survival. Loons found dead or emaciated during these events showed depleted fat reserves consistent with prey scarcity rather than disease (source: G-04).

The transition from winter plumage to breeding plumage occurs in late winter through early spring while loons are still on PNW waters, making March and April excellent months for observing the dramatic plumage transformation, particularly in Common and Pacific Loons. By mid-May, most loons have departed for boreal breeding grounds in Alaska, British Columbia, and the Canadian interior.

### Grebe Courtship, Nesting, and the Western/Clark's Complex

Grebes are the consummate freshwater divers of the PNW, though several species transition to marine habitats outside the breeding season. The family Podicipedidae is represented by five regularly occurring species in the region, with the Western Grebe and Clark's Grebe forming one of ornithology's most compelling sibling species pairs.

Western and Clark's Grebes were considered a single species until 1985, when behavioral, vocal, and plumage differences led the AOS to split them (source: O-03). The two species breed sympatrically on large interior lakes from the Columbia Basin to the Great Basin fringe, sometimes in mixed colonies. Their courtship display, the "rushing ceremony," is among the most spectacular in North American birds: paired birds rise from the water and sprint across the surface side by side, bodies nearly vertical, feet pattering in synchronized rhythm. The display has been extensively documented at Upper Klamath Lake (OR), Banks Lake (WA), and Lake Chelan (WA).

Despite their visual similarity, the two species maintain reproductive isolation through vocal differences (the Clark's single-noted *cree* versus the Western's two-noted *cree-creet*), facial pattern (Clark's has orange-yellow bill with white extending above the eye; Western has greenish-yellow bill with black extending below the eye), and assortative mating during the rushing ceremony. Hybridization occurs but at low rates (~1-2% of pairs in mixed colonies), confirming effective reproductive isolation (source: P-06, O-01).

Grebe nesting is distinctive: all PNW species build floating platforms of aquatic vegetation anchored to emergent plants. The nests rise and fall with water levels, a critical adaptation in the region's seasonally fluctuating lakes and marshes. However, this adaptation has limits — rapid water level changes from irrigation drawdowns or dam operations can strand or flood nests, a significant conservation concern at managed water bodies like Malheur NWR and Klamath Basin refuges (source: G-19).

The Pied-billed Grebe occupies a different ecological niche from the larger grebes, preferring small ponds and marshes with dense emergent vegetation. It is the only grebe likely to be found on urban ponds and suburban wetlands in the PNW, and its characteristic *cow-cow-cow-cow-cow-cowp* call is a familiar sound in lowland wetlands from the Willamette Valley to the Puget Sound lowlands.

## Section 5: Pelicans and Cormorants

The Pelecaniformes and Suliformes of the Pacific Northwest include some of the region's most visible and ecologically significant large waterbirds. Brown Pelicans cruising the surf line are iconic along the Oregon and Washington coasts, their story one of conservation's greatest triumphs. American White Pelicans, far less expected in the maritime PNW, maintain important inland breeding colonies on the region's alkaline lakes. The three cormorant species — Double-crested, Pelagic, and Brandt's — occupy distinct but overlapping niches along the coast and in the Salish Sea, and their relationship with commercial and sport fisheries has generated persistent controversy.

### Brown Pelican Recovery: An ESA Success Story

The Brown Pelican's arc from near-extinction to abundant coastal resident is one of the defining conservation narratives of the 20th century. By the late 1960s and early 1970s, DDT and related organochlorine pesticides had caused catastrophic reproductive failure across the species' range. Eggshell thinning — the result of DDE (a DDT metabolite) interfering with calcium deposition — reduced hatching success to near zero in many California and Gulf Coast colonies. The California Brown Pelican (*P. o. californicus*) was listed as Endangered under the ESA in 1970, the year the law was enacted (source: G-17).

The ban on DDT in 1972, combined with aggressive colony protection and monitoring, allowed a slow recovery through the 1980s and 1990s. By the 2000s, nesting success at Channel Islands colonies had returned to pre-DDT levels, and post-breeding dispersal northward into PNW waters resumed its historical pattern. The U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service delisted the Brown Pelican in 2009, declaring recovery complete — one of only a handful of ESA delisting success stories driven by actual population recovery rather than taxonomic revision (source: G-17, G-12).

Today, Brown Pelicans are abundant along the Oregon and Washington coasts from June through November, with peak numbers in August-September when post-breeding birds from California and Mexico colonies disperse northward. They do not currently breed in the PNW, though historical nesting records exist from the Columbia River estuary, and northward range expansion with climate warming may eventually re-establish breeding populations in southern Oregon (source: O-23).

The pelican recovery story carries lessons for current conservation efforts: it demonstrates that removing a primary threat (DDT) while protecting critical habitat (nesting islands) can produce population recovery within decades, even for a long-lived, slow-reproducing species. It also highlights the importance of the ESA framework itself — without listing, the legal protections enabling recovery would not have existed.

### Cormorant-Fisheries Conflict

The Double-crested Cormorant has been at the center of contentious resource conflicts in the PNW, particularly at East Sand Island in the Columbia River estuary. The island hosts one of the world's largest Double-crested Cormorant colonies, which at its peak in the early 2010s exceeded 15,000 nesting pairs. Research by USGS and NOAA demonstrated that the colony consumed millions of juvenile salmonids annually, including ESA-listed steelhead and Chinook salmon smolts transiting the estuary (source: G-20, P-18).

The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers implemented a management plan to reduce the colony by more than half through egg-oiling and habitat modification, aiming to reduce predation on ESA-listed salmonids. The program achieved its reduction targets but drew sharp criticism from bird conservation organizations who argued that cormorant predation was a minor mortality factor compared to dam passage, habitat loss, and ocean conditions. The controversy illuminates a genuine conservation dilemma: managing one native species to benefit another when both are affected by the same anthropogenic system (hydroelectric dams creating unnatural fish concentration points that attract predators).

Pelagic and Brandt's Cormorants largely avoid this conflict because their diet is overwhelmingly marine fish rather than anadromous salmonids. Both species are cliff-nesters with very different colony ecology from the island-nesting Double-crested, and their populations face different threats — primarily marine pollution, disturbance at nesting colonies, and prey depletion from marine heat waves.

## Section 6: Pelagic Seabirds

The offshore waters of the Pacific Northwest support one of the most productive pelagic seabird assemblages in the temperate Pacific. Seasonal upwelling driven by the California Current System brings nutrient-rich deep water to the surface along the continental shelf break, fueling massive phytoplankton blooms that cascade through zooplankton, forage fish, and squid to sustain millions of seabirds. The PNW sits at a biogeographic crossroads where cold-water subarctic species overlap with warm-water California Current species, creating exceptional diversity within 100 km of the coast.

### Pelagic Ecology and Upwelling

The continental shelf off Oregon and Washington extends 20-70 km offshore before dropping steeply at the shelf break into deep ocean. Wind-driven coastal upwelling, strongest from April through September, creates a persistent band of cold, nutrient-rich water along the shelf edge that supports the region's marine food web. In productive years, this upwelling fuels dense concentrations of euphausiid shrimp (krill), particularly *Euphausia pacifica* and *Thysanoessa spinifera*, which in turn attract massive aggregations of seabirds, marine mammals, and commercially important fish.

The timing and intensity of upwelling varies dramatically between years, with consequences that ripple through the entire seabird community. During warm-water anomalies — such as the 2014-2016 marine heat wave ("The Blob") and subsequent El Nino events — upwelling weakens, krill populations crash, and seabirds face prey scarcity. Mass mortality events of Cassin's Auklets, Common Murres, and other alcids have been documented during these warm-water periods, with thousands of emaciated birds washing ashore on PNW beaches (source: G-04, O-23).

Pelagic seabirds partition the offshore environment by both horizontal distance from shore and vertical depth in the water column. Storm-petrels and shearwaters forage at or near the surface, taking small prey items within the top meter. Albatrosses range the widest, covering thousands of square kilometers in foraging trips that exploit wind energy through dynamic soaring. Fulmars occupy an intermediate niche, scavenging at the surface and making shallow dives for fish and squid.

### Trans-Hemispheric Migration: The Sooty Shearwater

The Sooty Shearwater's annual migration is one of the most extraordinary journeys in the animal kingdom. Breeding on islands around New Zealand, southeastern Australia, and southern South America during the austral summer (November-March), Sooty Shearwaters undertake a figure-eight migration through the Pacific Ocean that covers an estimated 64,000 km — the longest electronically tracked migration of any animal (source: O-23). They arrive in PNW waters in late April and reach peak abundance from June through September, when millions transit the continental shelf waters of Oregon and Washington.

During peak migration, Sooty Shearwaters form some of the largest single-species aggregations of any bird in the world. Flocks of 100,000 or more have been documented from pelagic surveys off the Oregon coast, streaming in long ribbons over the water surface as they move between foraging areas. Their sheer biomass makes them one of the most numerically important vertebrate consumers in the California Current ecosystem during summer months.

Despite their enormous global population (estimated at 20+ million breeding pairs), Sooty Shearwaters have shown significant declines in PNW waters over the past several decades. Analysis of California Cooperative Oceanic Fisheries Investigations (CalCOFI) data and at-sea surveys suggests a 90% decline in California Current shearwater abundance since the 1980s, likely linked to warming ocean temperatures, shifts in prey distribution, and reduced breeding success at southern hemisphere colonies (source: O-23, G-04). This decline represents a major ecosystem-level signal that the California Current marine environment is changing fundamentally.

## Section 7: Sea Ducks and Diving Ducks

The Mergini and allied tribes of the family Anatidae contribute some of the most ecologically distinctive waterfowl in the PNW. Sea ducks are characterized by their marine-adapted physiology — salt glands for excreting excess sodium, dense waterproof plumage, and powerful legs set far back on the body for efficient underwater propulsion. Several species undergo dramatic seasonal habitat shifts, breeding on mountain lakes or fast-flowing rivers and wintering on the open coast, creating a river-to-sea lifecycle that parallels the salmon's ocean-to-river journey.

### Harlequin Duck: River-to-Sea Lifecycle and the Salmon Thread

The Harlequin Duck (*Histrionicus histrionicus*) is the species in this group with the most compelling salmon thread connection, and its lifecycle illustrates the deep integration between PNW river and marine ecosystems. Harlequin Ducks breed on fast-flowing mountain rivers and streams from April through August, nesting in concealed sites among boulders, root tangles, and overhanging vegetation along the same torrential headwater streams used by spawning salmon. After nesting, adults and juveniles migrate to the coast, where they spend the winter on rocky intertidal shorelines, foraging on mussels, snails, chitons, and marine invertebrates (source: G-14, O-20).

The salmon thread operates through habitat rather than diet: Harlequin Ducks breed on rivers where salmon-derived nutrients (marine-derived nitrogen and phosphorus from spawning carcasses) enrich the riparian invertebrate community that duck broods depend upon. Studies of salmon nutrient subsidies in PNW streams demonstrate that aquatic insect biomass is significantly higher in stream reaches receiving salmon carcasses, meaning that Harlequin Duck brood-rearing habitat quality is indirectly linked to salmon run abundance (source: P-18). This creates a feedback loop where salmon declines could affect duck productivity through reduced invertebrate prey, even though the ducks do not consume salmon directly.

### Scoter Decline and Conservation Concerns

The three scoter species (Surf, White-winged, and Black) have shown significant and largely unexplained population declines across their North American range. Surf Scoters, the most abundant scoter in PNW waters, have declined by an estimated 50-70% since the 1970s based on wintering survey data and breeding ground surveys (source: G-18, O-16). White-winged Scoter declines have been even steeper, with some estimates suggesting 75%+ declines since mid-century.

The causes remain poorly understood, which makes the decline particularly concerning. Potential factors include degradation of boreal breeding habitat, increased nest predation, changes in marine food web productivity, oil pollution on wintering grounds, and cumulative effects of environmental contaminants. Scoters are long-lived, late-maturing ducks with low annual reproductive output, making them vulnerable to even small increases in adult mortality or decreases in nesting success. Their population dynamics more closely resemble those of albatrosses than of most waterfowl (source: G-18).

In the PNW, scoters winter in large flocks on sheltered bays and along the outer coast, where they dive for bivalve mollusks (mussels, clams), crustaceans, and other benthic invertebrates. Important scoter wintering sites include Padilla Bay, Boundary Bay, Sequim Bay, and the southern Oregon coast estuaries. These sites deserve conservation attention as critical non-breeding habitat for declining populations.

### Barrow's Goldeneye: Mountain Lake Breeder

Barrow's Goldeneye represents an unusual biogeographic pattern among PNW ducks. This species breeds primarily on mountain lakes in the Cascade Range and northern Rockies, nesting in tree cavities near alpine and subalpine water bodies. The PNW population is the core of the species' Pacific range, with breeding birds scattered across remote mountain lakes from southern Oregon to northern British Columbia. After breeding, most birds descend to lowland rivers and coastal waters for winter, with important concentrations on the Columbia River, Puget Sound, and the Strait of Georgia (source: G-14, O-02).

The cavity-nesting habit connects Barrow's Goldeneye to the broader cavity nester guild of old-growth forests. Females prefer natural cavities in large-diameter snags near water, competing with Common Goldeneyes, Buffleheads, and Hooded Mergansers for suitable nest sites. In areas where old-growth snags have been removed by logging, nest box programs have been used to supplement cavity availability — with variable success, as Barrow's Goldeneye is more selective about cavity dimensions than many other cavity-nesting ducks (source: G-14, G-22).

**Migration:** Medium-distance | Arrives Oct-Nov, departs Apr | Key PNW sites: Columbia River, Puget Sound, Klamath Basin, interior rivers and lakes
**Marine Zone:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (winter), freshwater (breeding)
**Interaction:** Sibling species with Barrow's Goldeneye; the two species coexist on PNW wintering waters but Common Goldeneye is more associated with lowland rivers and sheltered bays while Barrow's prefers faster-flowing mountain rivers and more exposed coastal sites.
**Salmon Thread:** No — limited direct connection, though uses rivers and lakes within salmon watersheds.
**Sources:** G-14, O-02, O-20

### Bufflehead (*Bucephala albeola*)
**Status:** Winter visitor, rare breeder
**Bands:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, ELEV-RIPARIAN, ELEV-LOWLAND
**Conservation:** ESA: Not listed | IUCN: LC
**Size:** 32-40 cm length, 50-60 cm wingspan, 270-550 g mass
**Plumage:** Smallest diving duck in North America. Male striking: black back, white flanks, large white patch wrapping from behind eye over crown on iridescent purple-green head. Female dark gray-brown with small white cheek patch.
**Diet/Foraging:** Aquatic insects (breeding), small crustaceans, mollusks, fish eggs (winter) | Shallow diving to 3-5 m | Sheltered bays, estuaries, ponds, small rivers
**Nesting:** Cavity nester in woodpecker holes (especially Northern Flicker), clutch 6-11; breeds on interior lakes (rare in PNW — most breed in BC/interior)
**Migration:** Medium-distance | Arrives Oct, departs Apr | Key PNW sites: Puget Sound, Grays Harbor, Padilla Bay, Malheur NWR, interior rivers
**Marine Zone:** ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (winter), freshwater (breeding)
**Interaction:** The smallest cavity-nesting duck; almost entirely dependent on Northern Flicker (*Colaptes auratus*) cavities for nesting, creating an obligate mutualism with the woodpecker's excavation activity. Tolerates proximity to human activity, commonly found on urban ponds and marina waters in winter.
**Salmon Thread:** No
**Sources:** G-14, O-02, O-20

## Section 8: Synthesis — Marine Avian Ecology of the PNW

The species profiled in Sections 4-7 collectively represent the marine avian community of the Pacific Northwest — approximately 26 species spanning six taxonomic orders and occupying every marine habitat from the highest-energy rocky intertidal shoreline to the open ocean hundreds of kilometers from land. This synthesis draws together the ecological patterns, conservation priorities, and salmon thread connections that emerge from considering these species as an integrated community rather than as isolated profiles.

### 8.1 Species Richness by Marine Zone

The PNW's marine avian community partitions into three broad zones, each with distinctive species assemblages, energy regimes, and conservation challenges.

**Intertidal Zone (ELEV-INTERTIDAL)**

The intertidal zone — the dynamic interface between land and sea — supports 15+ of the species in this document, making it the most species-rich marine zone for waterbirds. Harlequin Ducks, scoters, and goldeneyes forage on rocky intertidal invertebrates; Red-throated Loons and Pied-billed Grebes exploit estuarine fish communities; cormorants roost on intertidal rocks between foraging bouts; and pelicans, gulls, and shorebirds concentrate at river mouths where freshwater meets salt. The intertidal zone's high bird diversity reflects its extraordinary productivity — more biomass per unit area than any other marine zone — and its accessibility from both land and sea.

Key intertidal sites: Grays Harbor (WA), Willapa Bay (WA), Padilla Bay (WA), Tillamook Bay (OR), Yaquina Bay (OR), Boundary Bay (BC/WA).

**Nearshore Zone (ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE, 0-200 m depth)**

The nearshore zone extending from the low-tide line to the continental shelf edge is the primary foraging habitat for the majority of marine bird species in this document. Common Loons, Pacific Loons, Western Grebes, all three cormorant species, Brown Pelicans, scoters, and goldeneyes forage primarily in this zone. Species richness peaks during winter (October-April) when boreal breeding species augment the resident community, and again during post-breeding dispersal (July-September) when southern hemisphere migrants arrive.

Key nearshore sites: Puget Sound (WA), Strait of Juan de Fuca (WA/BC), San Juan Islands (WA), Strait of Georgia (BC), outer coast within 30 km of shore (OR/WA).

**Pelagic Zone (ELEV-DEEP-MARINE, 200 m+ depth)**

The deep pelagic zone beyond the continental shelf supports a specialized community of tube-nosed seabirds (order Procellariiformes) and the deepest-diving ducks. Sooty Shearwaters, Black-footed Albatrosses, Northern Fulmars, and storm-petrels dominate this zone, their populations driven by upwelling-mediated productivity along the shelf break. The pelagic zone has the lowest species count but the highest individual abundance during summer months, when millions of Sooty Shearwaters transit PNW waters. Long-tailed Duck is the only anatid regularly exploiting this zone's deep-water prey.

Key pelagic sites: Astoria Canyon, Heceta Bank, Juan de Fuca Canyon, continental shelf break 30-80 km offshore.

| Marine Zone | Species Count (this document) | Peak Season | Dominant Orders | Primary Productivity Driver |
|-------------|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Intertidal | 15+ | Year-round (peak winter) | Anseriformes, Gaviiformes, Suliformes | Tidal exchange, river input |
| Nearshore (0-200 m) | 20+ | Winter (Oct-Apr) | Gaviiformes, Podicipediformes, Suliformes, Anseriformes | Coastal upwelling, river plumes |

### 8.3 Conservation Priority Species

Among the ~26 species profiled, seven warrant elevated conservation concern based on population trends, IUCN status, or regulatory significance.

None of these species are currently ESA-listed, which represents a significant gap between federal regulatory protection and actual conservation need. Several are candidates for future listing if declines continue, and all would benefit from enhanced monitoring and habitat protection at key PNW sites.

The Brown Pelican (ESA-DL, 2009) represents the sole ESA success story in this group — demonstrating that threat removal (DDT ban) plus habitat protection can reverse catastrophic declines even in long-lived, slow-reproducing seabirds.

### 8.4 Salmon Thread Connections in the Marine Environment

Six of the ~26 species profiled in this document have documented connections to Pacific salmon ecology, operating through four distinct pathways:

**Pathway 1: Direct predation on juvenile salmonids**
- Double-crested Cormorant — the most significant avian predator of ESA-listed smolts in the Columbia River estuary (source: G-20)
- Brown Pelican — consumes juvenile salmonids in nearshore waters during post-breeding dispersal
- Common Loon — documented predation on salmon smolts in nearshore rearing habitat

**Pathway 2: Nutrient subsidy from salmon carcasses**
- Harlequin Duck — breeds on salmon streams where marine-derived nutrients enrich the aquatic invertebrate community that duck broods depend upon (source: P-18)

**Pathway 3: Shared marine forage base**
- Sooty Shearwater — dietary overlap with juvenile salmon prey (krill, small fish) creates indirect competition for forage resources in the California Current

**Pathway 4: Habitat overlap at salmon life-stage transition points**
- Red-throated Loon — forages in estuarine zones critical for salmon smolt outmigration
- Barrow's Goldeneye — winter concentrations on the Columbia River overlap with salmon spawning areas; forages on salmon eggs

The salmon thread in the marine environment operates differently from the riparian salmon thread documented in the raptors module (see AVI: raptors — Bald Eagle, Osprey). Where riparian birds benefit from salmon through nutrient subsidy and direct consumption of carcasses, marine and coastal birds interact with salmon primarily as predators of juvenile life stages or as competitors for shared prey. The Double-crested Cormorant situation at East Sand Island exemplifies the management complexity this creates: reducing cormorant predation on ESA-listed smolts requires intervening against one native species to benefit another, a decision that inevitably weighs ecological values against each other.

### 8.5 Climate Change Impacts: Marine Heat Waves, Prey Shifts, and Colony Failures

The PNW marine avian community faces escalating threats from climate-driven changes in ocean conditions. Three interconnected impact pathways have been documented:

**Marine Heat Waves**

The 2014-2016 northeastern Pacific marine heat wave ("The Blob") was the most severe warming event in the instrumental record for PNW waters, with sea surface temperatures 2-4 degrees C above the long-term mean persisting for over two years. The ecological consequences for seabirds were severe and well-documented: mass mortality events of Common Murres (estimated 500,000-1,000,000 deaths), significant die-offs of Cassin's Auklets, and elevated winter mortality in loons, grebes, and scoters (source: G-04). The proximate cause was prey collapse — warm water disrupted upwelling, krill populations crashed, forage fish redistributed or declined, and seabirds starved.

Subsequent warm-water events in 2019 and 2023-2024 produced similar but less severe impacts, suggesting that marine heat waves are becoming a recurring rather than exceptional feature of PNW ocean conditions. For long-lived seabirds with low annual reproductive output, chronic rather than acute prey scarcity may be more damaging to populations than single catastrophic events (source: G-04, O-23).

**Prey Distribution Shifts**

Warming ocean temperatures are driving northward range shifts in key prey species. Pacific sardine (*Sardinops sagax*), historically the primary food of Brown Pelicans and Brandt's Cormorants in the California Current, has undergone boom-bust cycles correlated with Pacific Decadal Oscillation phases. In warm phases, sardine abundance collapses in the southern California Current (the historical core), potentially benefiting PNW waters as populations shift north — but this northward shift is unpredictable and creates boom-bust dynamics in PNW seabird food webs. Conversely, cold-water prey species like krill and sand lance may decline in PNW waters as thermal regimes warm, disadvantaging species dependent on these prey (source: G-04).

**Colony Failures and Reproductive Impacts**

Seabird colony productivity in the PNW has become increasingly variable, with complete colony failures occurring more frequently at monitored sites. Fork-tailed Storm-Petrel and Leach's Storm-Petrel colonies on Oregon offshore islands have shown reduced reproductive success during warm-water years, likely due to reduced prey delivery rates to chicks (source: G-01, O-23). Cormorant colonies have experienced nest abandonment events linked to prey scarcity and increased Bald Eagle disturbance (eagle populations have recovered dramatically post-DDT-ban, creating novel predation pressure on seabird colonies that had not experienced significant eagle predation for decades).

The combination of warming ocean conditions, shifting prey distributions, and increased disturbance at nesting colonies creates a compounding threat landscape for PNW marine birds. Species with restricted breeding ranges (storm-petrels confined to a few predator-free islands), specialized diets (scoters dependent on bivalve beds), or long-distance migrations (Sooty Shearwater dependent on both hemisphere conditions) are most vulnerable.
