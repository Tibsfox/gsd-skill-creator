## 1. The River's Witness — Canonical Statement

> The River's Witness is the cross-series ecological thread connecting all PNW Living Systems Taxonomy research through the life cycle of Pacific salmon (*Oncorhynchus* spp.). Salmon are the only biological vectors that physically transport marine-derived nutrients from the deep Pacific into the heart of PNW terrestrial ecosystems.

Every river in the Pacific Northwest that carries salmon is also, by necessity, a river that feeds birds. The connection operates at every scale: a Bald Eagle ripping flesh from a spawning Chinook on the Skagit River in November; a Winter Wren gleaning enriched insects from moss on a salmon-fertilized Sitka spruce; a Caspian Tern intercepting a smolt at the mouth of the Columbia in May. These interactions are not incidental. They are structural — woven into the energetic architecture of the PNW in ways that make salmon decline inseparable from avian community change.

This document maps every known avian connection to Pacific salmon across all life stages, from egg to carcass, from marine forage fish to riparian nutrient enrichment. It is the avian chapter of the River's Witness thread, and it must be read alongside its mammalian counterpart (`salmon-thread-mam.md`) and the foundational ecological networks document (`ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1) to understand the full picture.

The structure of this document follows the salmon's life: ocean, river, spawning ground, carcass, soil, tree, insect, bird. The birds appear at every stage, because the birds have always been there, watching the river.

## 2. Salmon Species in the PNW

Six anadromous salmonids of the genus *Oncorhynchus* form the biological foundation of the River's Witness. Five are Pacific salmon (semelparous — they spawn once and die); the sixth, steelhead, is the anadromous form of rainbow trout (iteroparous — capable of repeat spawning). Each species has a distinct life history, run timing, and body size that determines which birds interact with it, when, and how.

### Chinook Salmon (*Oncorhynchus tshawytscha*)

The largest Pacific salmon, returning at 20-50 lbs (occasionally exceeding 80 lbs in Alaska). Ocean residence 2-7 years, feeding on fish and squid. Chinook have the longest freshwater migrations of any Pacific salmon — spring-run Chinook historically ascended the Columbia past the present site of Grand Coulee Dam, over 600 miles inland. Nitrogen content approximately 130-325 g per adult (Naiman et al. 2002). Run timing: spring Chinook (April-June), summer Chinook (June-August), fall Chinook (September-November). The large body size makes Chinook the most valuable individual carcass for avian scavengers, and the spring run timing means Chinook carcasses are available during the breeding season for some riparian bird species.

**Key avian interactions:** Bald Eagle (primary predator/scavenger), Osprey (live-catch predator), Great Blue Heron (juvenile predator), mergansers (fry and smolt predator), gulls and terns (smolt predator at estuary).

### Coho Salmon (*Oncorhynchus kisutch*)

Medium-sized salmon returning at 8-12 lbs, with ocean residence of 1-3 years (typically 18 months). Coho are the quintessential small-stream salmon — they ascend narrow, low-gradient streams that larger Chinook cannot reach, depositing marine-derived nutrients deep into the headwater forest. Nitrogen content approximately 52-78 g per adult. Run timing: October-January, with peak spawning November-December in most PNW systems. Coho carcasses are available through the winter, coinciding with Bald Eagle congregations and corvid scavenging activity during the leanest months for resident birds.

**Key avian interactions:** Bald Eagle, American Dipper (egg and fry predator), Belted Kingfisher (fry predator), corvids (carcass scavengers), Common Merganser (fry predator).

### Sockeye Salmon (*Oncorhynchus nerka*)

Lake-rearing salmon returning at 5-8 lbs, with ocean residence of 1-4 years. Sockeye are unique among Pacific salmon in requiring lake habitat for juvenile rearing (typically 1-2 years in a nursery lake before smolting). This concentrates both juveniles and returning adults at lake outlets and inlets, creating predictable foraging hotspots for birds. Nitrogen content approximately 33-52 g per adult. Run timing: June-September, with peak spawning August-October. The concentrated spawning aggregations at lake inlets and outlets (e.g., Adams River in BC, Baker Lake in WA) produce some of the most spectacular salmon-bird interactions in the PNW.

**Key avian interactions:** Bald Eagle (spawning concentrations), gulls (spawning aggregations), mergansers (fry in lake), Osprey (adults in clear lake-outlet streams).

### Pink Salmon (*Oncorhynchus gorbuscha*)

The smallest Pacific salmon at 3-5 lbs, with a rigid 2-year life cycle (odd-year and even-year populations). Pink salmon spawn in lower river reaches and estuaries — often within tidal influence — making their carcasses accessible to the widest range of avian species, including coastal gulls and shorebirds that rarely venture upstream. Nitrogen content approximately 20-33 g per adult. Run timing: July-September. In the PNW, odd-year pink runs dominate in Puget Sound; even-year runs are significant in BC. The enormous abundance of pink salmon in peak years (millions of fish in major BC rivers) produces short, intense pulses of carcass availability.

**Key avian interactions:** Glaucous-winged Gull (major scavenger at lower-river spawning sites), crows and ravens, Bald Eagle, Great Blue Heron.

### Chum Salmon (*Oncorhynchus keta*)

Medium-large salmon at 8-15 lbs, with ocean residence of 2-5 years. Chum salmon are the latest-spawning salmon in many PNW systems, with peak spawning in November-January. Like pink salmon, chum typically spawn in lower river reaches, often within a few miles of saltwater. Nitrogen content approximately 52-98 g per adult. The late timing means chum carcasses persist into the coldest months of winter, providing critical caloric subsidies for overwintering raptors and corvids when other food sources are scarce.

**Key avian interactions:** Bald Eagle (major — chum runs sustain the peak eagle congregations on the Skagit and Nooksack rivers in December-January), Common Raven, gulls, Turkey Vulture (where present in mild winters).

### Steelhead (*Oncorhynchus mykiss*)

The anadromous form of rainbow trout, returning at 8-20 lbs. Unlike the five Pacific salmon, steelhead are iteroparous — they can survive spawning and return to the ocean. This means steelhead do not produce the mass carcass deposits that drive the salmon nutrient pathway. However, steelhead are significant as live prey for avian fish predators, and the kelts (post-spawn fish) that die in-stream contribute some marine-derived nutrients. Winter steelhead run December-April; summer steelhead run May-October. The winter run timing puts steelhead in streams during a period when fewer other salmon are present, making them an important supplementary prey resource for Belted Kingfishers, American Dippers, and mergansers during the lean late-winter months.

**Key avian interactions:** Osprey (active predator), Belted Kingfisher (smolt and juvenile predator), American Dipper (egg predator at redds), Common Merganser (juvenile predator).

## 3. Avian Species Connected to Salmon

This section profiles every PNW bird species with a documented or well-supported ecological connection to Pacific salmon. Species are organized by connection type: direct predators, carcass scavengers, nutrient beneficiaries, smolt/fry specialists, and marine-connection species. For each species, the connection mechanism, salmon life stage, ecoregion, seasonal timing, and cross-reference to other AVI modules are documented.

---

### Category A: Direct Predators of Live Salmon

These species capture and consume live salmon at various life stages, from eggs in the gravel to adults on spawning grounds.

---

### Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator and carcass scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult spawning, carcass, smolt (opportunistic)
**Mechanism:** Bald Eagles are the single most visible avian participant in the salmon cycle. They hunt live spawning salmon in shallow water, pirate fish from Osprey, and scavenge carcasses from stream banks and gravel bars. Eagles carry salmon and salmon fragments to perch trees, depositing marine-derived nitrogen at the base of large conifers along the riparian corridor. Reimchen et al. (2003) documented significantly elevated N-15 levels in foliage of eagle perch trees, confirming eagles as vectors of marine nutrient transport from stream to forest. On the Skagit River (WA), over 600 Bald Eagles congregate from November through February to feed on chum and Chinook carcasses — the largest such gathering in the lower 48 states. The Nooksack River supports an additional 200-400 eagles during this period. In BC, the Harrison River and Squamish River each attract 1,000-3,000 eagles during chum runs.
**Ecoregion:** All PNW salmon-bearing watersheds; peak concentrations in Puget Sound lowlands, North Cascades foothills, Olympic Peninsula, Columbia River Gorge, central BC coast
**Seasonal Timing:** November-February (peak, aligned with chum and late Chinook runs); year-round in productive systems
**Cross-ref:** `raptors.md` (full species card), `resident.md`

---

### Osprey (*Pandion haliaetus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Adult (live catch), smolt, juvenile
**Mechanism:** Osprey are specialized fish hunters, capturing live salmon by plunge-diving feet-first into water. Unlike eagles, which often scavenge, Osprey almost exclusively take live fish. They are one of few avian species capable of catching adult salmon up to 3-4 lbs — typically targeting smaller adults, jacks (early-returning males), and large juveniles. Osprey nest along salmon-bearing rivers throughout the PNW, with nest densities correlating with salmon abundance. The species is a summer resident in the PNW (arriving March-April, departing September-October), so its primary salmon interactions are with spring/summer Chinook, sockeye, and early coho. Osprey nests are frequently pirated by Bald Eagles, creating a competitive interaction linked to salmon abundance — where salmon are plentiful, both species coexist; where salmon decline, eagle kleptoparasitism may suppress Osprey nesting success.
**Ecoregion:** All major salmon-bearing rivers and lakes; Willamette Valley, Columbia Plateau, Puget Sound lowlands, Okanagan, Fraser Valley
**Seasonal Timing:** April-September (summer resident); peak salmon interaction June-August
**Cross-ref:** `raptors.md` (full species card), `migratory.md`

---

### Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, parr, smolt
**Mechanism:** Belted Kingfishers are the PNW's most abundant avian fish predator on small streams. They hunt by hovering over or watching from a perch above clear water, then plunge-diving to capture fish up to 10 cm in length — the size range of salmon fry (3-5 cm) through large parr and small smolts (6-10 cm). On streams with healthy salmon populations, juvenile salmon comprise a significant fraction of kingfisher diet during spring and summer. Kingfishers are territorial along linear stream reaches (typically 0.5-1.5 km of stream per pair), and territory quality correlates with water clarity and juvenile fish density. The species is resident year-round in the PNW lowlands but withdraws from frozen high-elevation streams in winter.
**Ecoregion:** All PNW watersheds with salmon-bearing streams, from coastal to montane; most abundant in lowland clear-water streams
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak salmon fry predation March-June (after fry emergence from gravel)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Egg, fry, aquatic invertebrates enriched by salmon nutrients
**Mechanism:** The American Dipper is the only aquatic songbird in North America, foraging by walking underwater on stream bottoms. Dippers feed on salmon eggs displaced from redds by stream flow or subsequent spawning activity, and on emergent fry. They also consume aquatic invertebrates (stonefly and caddisfly larvae) that are themselves enriched in marine-derived nitrogen from consuming salmon tissue in-stream (Bilby et al. 1996). Stable isotope analysis of Dipper feathers from salmon-bearing streams shows significantly elevated N-15 signatures compared to Dippers from salmon-free streams (Christie & Reimchen 2008), confirming the marine-derived nutrient link. Dipper territory quality, reproductive success, and overwinter survival are all correlated with salmon spawning density on occupied stream reaches.
**Ecoregion:** Mountain streams throughout the PNW, from coastal ranges to Cascades and Rockies; present wherever clear, fast-flowing, salmon-bearing streams exist from sea level to 6,000+ ft
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round resident; peak salmon egg consumption October-January (spawning season); peak fry consumption March-May
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, parr, smolt
**Mechanism:** Common Mergansers are large diving ducks with serrated bills adapted for catching fish. Broods of 10-20+ ducklings forage cooperatively along salmon-bearing rivers throughout the PNW summer, consuming large numbers of juvenile salmon. A single merganser brood can consume hundreds of salmon fry per day during the peak rearing period. Fisheries managers have historically viewed mergansers as competitors for juvenile salmon production, though contemporary understanding recognizes that predation pressure from mergansers is a natural component of the salmon life cycle. Merganser abundance on PNW rivers is a reliable indicator of healthy juvenile salmon populations — rivers with declining salmon runs typically support fewer breeding mergansers.
**Ecoregion:** All major salmon-bearing rivers; particularly abundant on the Skagit, Snoqualmie, Skykomish, Wenatchee, Methow, and upper Columbia tributaries (WA); Rogue, Umpqua, and Deschutes (OR); Fraser and Thompson (BC)
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak salmon fry predation April-July (brood-rearing period)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Hooded Merganser (*Lophodytes cucullatus*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, parr
**Mechanism:** The smallest North American merganser, the Hooded Merganser hunts in quieter water than the Common Merganser — preferring sloughs, side channels, beaver ponds, and slow backwater reaches where juvenile salmon rear. Hooded Mergansers are cavity nesters, dependent on large-diameter trees with woodpecker cavities near water, creating an indirect link between old-growth forest structure and salmon predation ecology. Their smaller size restricts prey to smaller fish than Common Merganser takes, focusing predation on newly emerged fry (3-5 cm) rather than larger parr and smolts.
**Ecoregion:** Lowland rivers and forested wetlands with salmon access; Puget Sound lowlands, Willamette Valley, coastal Oregon and Washington, southern BC
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round in lowlands; peak salmon fry predation March-June
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Red-breasted Merganser (*Mergus serrator*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Smolt, fry (estuarine), marine juvenile
**Mechanism:** Red-breasted Mergansers are the most marine-adapted of the three PNW merganser species, foraging in estuaries, coastal bays, and nearshore marine waters. Their primary salmon interaction occurs in estuaries where outmigrating smolts concentrate before entering the open ocean — the estuary bottleneck that also attracts terns, cormorants, and gulls. Red-breasted Mergansers take smolts during the critical estuarine transition period (April-June), when juvenile salmon are adjusting to saltwater and may be physiologically stressed and more vulnerable to predation. In marine waters, they also take juvenile salmon during the first ocean summer.
**Ecoregion:** Estuaries and nearshore marine waters throughout the PNW; Puget Sound, Strait of Juan de Fuca, Grays Harbor, Columbia River estuary, Strait of Georgia
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round in marine waters; peak smolt predation April-June during outmigration
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`, `migratory.md`

---

### Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, parr, smolt, juvenile (up to ~20 cm)
**Mechanism:** Great Blue Herons are the PNW's largest wading bird, standing up to 4.5 feet tall with a bill capable of striking fish up to 20 cm in length. They hunt salmon fry and parr by standing motionless in shallow riffles and pool margins, striking with explosive speed. Heron rookeries along salmon-bearing rivers position multiple breeding pairs within foraging range of productive juvenile salmon habitat. In estuaries, herons take outmigrating smolts from shallow channels during low tide. The species' year-round presence in the PNW means it interacts with salmon across the full annual cycle — from fry emergence in spring through fall spawning season when herons occasionally take small spawning adults in shallow water.
**Ecoregion:** All PNW lowland and coastal watersheds; highest densities in Puget Sound, Willamette Valley, lower Columbia, Fraser Valley
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak juvenile salmon predation March-July
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Green Heron (*Butorides virescens*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Fry, small parr
**Mechanism:** Green Herons are smaller (16-18 inches) and more secretive than Great Blue Herons, hunting along densely vegetated stream banks and quiet backwater channels. Their small size limits prey to salmon fry and small parr (under 8 cm). Green Herons are notable for tool use — they occasionally drop feathers, insects, or small objects onto the water surface to lure fish within striking range. This behavior has been observed on streams containing juvenile salmon, though it is not specific to salmon prey. Green Herons are summer residents in the PNW, present from April through September, so their salmon interactions are limited to the spring and summer fry/parr rearing period.
**Ecoregion:** Lowland riparian corridors in western WA and OR; uncommon in BC; absent from east-side drainages
**Seasonal Timing:** April-September (summer resident); peak salmon fry predation April-June
**Cross-ref:** `migratory.md`

### Category B: Carcass Scavengers

These species feed on salmon carcasses during and after spawning runs, directly consuming marine-derived nutrients and dispersing carcass fragments across the landscape.

---

### Common Raven (*Corvus corax*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs (exposed by stream flow or other predators)
**Mechanism:** Ravens are the PNW's most intelligent and adaptable salmon scavenger. They locate carcasses from the air, land on gravel bars and stream banks, and feed on exposed flesh, eyes, and brain tissue. Ravens cache salmon fragments in forest soil, crevices, and tree forks — a food-storage behavior that inadvertently transports marine-derived nutrients away from the stream corridor. A single Raven may cache 30-100+ food items per day during peak carcass availability, moving salmon-derived nitrogen 100-500 meters from the stream. Ravens also follow bears to spawning streams, waiting to scavenge partially consumed carcasses abandoned by feeding bears. This bear-raven commensal relationship amplifies the terrestrial nutrient dispersal pathway.
**Ecoregion:** All PNW ecoregions; present at every elevation band from marine shoreline to alpine
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak carcass scavenging October-January (aligned with chum, coho, late Chinook runs)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### American Crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs
**Mechanism:** American Crows congregate on salmon streams in large numbers during spawning runs, forming loose flocks of 20-100+ birds on productive gravel bars. Crows are less bold than ravens — they typically wait for carcasses to wash onto accessible banks rather than feeding in-stream — but their gregarious nature means they process large quantities of carcass material collectively. Crows roost communally (winter roosts in the PNW can exceed 10,000 birds), and the guano concentrated under roost trees represents a secondary nutrient deposition pathway. Crows feeding on salmon carcasses during the day return to urban or suburban roosts at night, effectively transporting marine-derived nitrogen from riparian corridors into urban landscapes.
**Ecoregion:** Lowland and coastal PNW; most abundant in urban-rural interface zones near salmon streams; less common at higher elevations (replaced by ravens)
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak carcass scavenging October-December
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Steller's Jay (*Cyanocitta stelleri*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (small fragments), eggs
**Mechanism:** Steller's Jays are the characteristic corvid of PNW coniferous forests, and they opportunistically scavenge salmon carcasses along forested stream corridors. Smaller than crows and ravens, jays focus on small tissue fragments, exposed eggs, and insect larvae (particularly blowfly maggots) colonizing decomposing carcasses. Like ravens, Steller's Jays cache food — hiding salmon fragments in bark crevices, moss, and soil litter. Their caching behavior is less systematic than ravens' but occurs in dense forest where the cached nitrogen directly enters the mycorrhizal network zone. Steller's Jays are year-round residents that do not form large flocks, so their per-individual contribution to nutrient transport is modest, but their consistent forest-interior presence means they move salmon nutrients into habitats that larger scavengers rarely penetrate.
**Ecoregion:** Coniferous and mixed forests throughout the PNW, from coastal to montane; present wherever forests adjoin salmon-bearing streams
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak carcass interaction October-January
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Canada Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (fragments), eggs
**Mechanism:** Formerly known as Gray Jay, the Canada Jay is a resident of subalpine and boreal forests that descends to lower-elevation salmon streams during fall and winter. Canada Jays are renowned food cachers — they produce a sticky saliva that allows them to paste food items (including salmon tissue) onto conifer branches and bark surfaces well above ground level. This arboreal caching behavior distributes salmon-derived nutrients vertically into the canopy zone, a deposition pathway unique among salmon scavengers. In the Cascades and northern BC, Canada Jays regularly attend bear-killed salmon carcasses, arriving within minutes of a bear abandoning a partially consumed fish. Their cache sites are well above the reach of most mammalian scavengers, allowing them to maintain salmon nutrient stores through the winter.
**Ecoregion:** Subalpine and montane forests of the Cascades, Olympics, and northern BC; descends to lower-elevation salmon streams in winter
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round resident; peak carcass scavenging September-November (when salmon reach higher-elevation streams)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Black-billed Magpie (*Pica hudsonia*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass
**Mechanism:** Black-billed Magpies are primarily east-side birds in the PNW, inhabiting the drier valleys and steppe habitats east of the Cascade crest. Their salmon interactions occur where salmon-bearing rivers cross the east-side landscape — the Yakima, Wenatchee, Methow, Okanagan, John Day, and Grande Ronde rivers. Magpies scavenge carcasses from gravel bars and irrigated floodplains, often in mixed-species flocks with crows and ravens. Their long tail and relatively weak flight limit their carcass transport distance compared to ravens, but they effectively process salmon carcasses in the arid east-side riparian corridors where nutrient inputs from any source are ecologically significant.
**Ecoregion:** East-side river valleys of WA, OR, and interior BC; Columbia Plateau, Okanagan, upper Snake River drainage
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; peak carcass scavenging September-November (aligned with east-side fall Chinook and coho runs)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Turkey Vulture (*Cathartes aura*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass
**Mechanism:** Turkey Vultures are the PNW's only obligate avian scavenger, locating carcasses primarily by olfaction — a rare ability among birds. Their keen sense of smell allows them to detect decomposing salmon carcasses from altitude, even when carcasses are partially buried in gravel or hidden under riparian vegetation. Vultures are migratory in the PNW (present April-October), so their salmon interaction is limited to fall Chinook and early coho runs. Where present, vultures are highly efficient carcass processors — a group of 5-10 vultures can reduce a salmon carcass to bone in under an hour. Vulture stomach acid is among the most acidic of any vertebrate (pH ~1), destroying pathogens in carcass tissue and effectively sanitizing the decomposition process.
**Ecoregion:** Western and eastern lowlands throughout the PNW; most common in Willamette Valley, Puget Sound lowlands, and Columbia Plateau
**Seasonal Timing:** April-October (summer resident); peak salmon carcass interaction September-October (early fall runs before vulture migration departure)
**Cross-ref:** `raptors.md`

---

### Glaucous-winged Gull (*Larus glaucescens*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger and direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs, fry, smolt
**Mechanism:** Glaucous-winged Gulls are the dominant large gull of the PNW coast and Puget Sound, and they are arguably the most versatile avian participant in the salmon cycle. They scavenge carcasses at spawning sites, particularly on lower-river and estuarine reaches where pink and chum salmon spawn within tidal influence. They prey on salmon eggs exposed by spawning activity or streambed disturbance. They intercept outmigrating smolts at river mouths and estuary channels. And they forage on marine organisms that share the food web with juvenile salmon in nearshore waters. Glaucous-winged Gulls nest colonially on offshore rocks and islands, and their guano concentrates marine-derived nutrients (including salmon-derived nitrogen) on nesting substrates, creating localized nutrient hotspots on otherwise nutrient-poor rocky islets.
**Ecoregion:** Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, outer coast of WA and OR, lower Columbia River
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; carcass scavenging peaks October-January; smolt predation peaks April-June
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

---

### Herring Gull (*Larus argentatus*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs
**Mechanism:** Herring Gulls are winter visitors to the PNW interior, arriving from northern breeding areas in October-November and departing by April. Their arrival timing coincides precisely with peak salmon spawning, and they regularly join mixed-species gull flocks on spawning rivers. Herring Gulls are slightly larger and more aggressive than Glaucous-winged Gulls at carcasses, often displacing the local species from preferred feeding sites. Their winter-only presence in the PNW concentrates their salmon interactions during the period of maximum carcass availability (November-February). On the Columbia River and its tributaries, flocks of 50-200 Herring Gulls scavenge gravel bars below hatchery facilities where carcass disposal creates predictable food sources.
**Ecoregion:** Interior valleys, major rivers, and coastal areas throughout the PNW during winter
**Seasonal Timing:** October-April (winter visitor); peak carcass scavenging November-January
**Cross-ref:** `migratory.md`

---

### California Gull (*Larus californicus*)

**Connection Type:** Scavenger
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass, eggs, fry
**Mechanism:** California Gulls breed in large colonies at interior lakes (Great Basin, Columbia Plateau) and migrate to the PNW coast and valleys for winter. They are intermediate in size between the larger Herring and smaller Ring-billed Gulls, and occupy a generalist scavenging niche at salmon carcass sites. California Gulls are particularly significant on east-side salmon rivers (Yakima, Wenatchee, Deschutes) where they may be the most abundant large gull species. Their colonial breeding at interior lakes (Mono Lake, Great Salt Lake, Malheur Lake) means that California Gulls feeding on salmon carcasses during fall and winter carry marine-derived nutrients to inland breeding colonies the following spring — a long-distance, cross-ecosystem nutrient transport pathway.
**Ecoregion:** Columbia Plateau, east-side river valleys, Puget Sound, Willamette Valley; particularly abundant on interior salmon rivers
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round (some resident, some migratory); peak salmon interaction September-December
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`, `migratory.md`

### Category C: Nutrient Beneficiaries

These species do not directly consume salmon or salmon carcasses. Instead, they benefit from the marine-derived nutrients that salmon deposit in riparian ecosystems — nutrients that flow through soil, plants, and invertebrates before reaching birds.

---

### Varied Thrush (*Ixoreus naevius*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (indirect — via soil invertebrate enrichment)
**Mechanism:** The Varied Thrush is the emblematic forest-floor bird of PNW old-growth, foraging by turning leaf litter and probing moss for invertebrates — earthworms, millipedes, beetles, and springtails. In salmon-fertilized riparian forests, soil invertebrate biomass is significantly higher than in unfertilized stands (Hocking & Reimchen 2002), and the invertebrates themselves carry elevated N-15 signatures from consuming decomposed salmon tissue. Varied Thrushes foraging in salmon-fertilized riparian zones thus receive a marine-nutrient subsidy mediated through two trophic levels: salmon carcass to soil invertebrate to thrush. Studies in coastal BC have demonstrated that songbird abundance (including Varied Thrush) is higher along salmon-bearing streams than along otherwise similar salmon-free streams, even after controlling for habitat structure (Wilkinson et al. 2018).
**Ecoregion:** Coniferous and mixed forests throughout the PNW, from coastal to montane; salmon benefit strongest in lowland old-growth riparian corridors
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round resident (some altitudinal movement); peak nutrient benefit October-March (when salmon carcasses are decomposing in soil)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### American Robin (*Turdus migratorius*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (indirect — via soil invertebrate and fruit enrichment)
**Mechanism:** American Robins are ground-foraging thrushes that consume earthworms, insects, and fruit. In salmon-fertilized riparian zones, robins benefit through multiple nutrient pathways: (1) enriched soil invertebrates with higher caloric content and elevated nitrogen, (2) enriched fruiting shrubs — salmonberry (*Rubus spectabilis*), red huckleberry (*Vaccinium parvifolium*), and other riparian berry producers that absorb marine-derived nitrogen through their roots and produce larger, more nutritious fruit, and (3) enhanced insect emergence from salmon-fertilized streams (particularly dipteran flies whose larvae develop in enriched stream sediments). Robins are the most abundant breeding bird in many PNW riparian zones, making them a significant secondary vector for marine-nutrient redistribution through the wider landscape via their droppings and carcasses.
**Ecoregion:** All PNW ecoregions from coastal to urban to montane; salmon nutrient benefit most pronounced in lowland riparian corridors
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round (partially migratory); peak nutrient benefit during spring breeding (March-June) when invertebrate demand is highest
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Pacific Wren (*Troglodytes pacificus*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (indirect — via invertebrate enrichment in leaf litter and decaying wood)
**Mechanism:** Pacific Wrens (recently split from Winter Wren) forage almost exclusively in the understory and forest floor of dense, moist coniferous forest — precisely the habitat most heavily fertilized by salmon carcasses transported from streams by bears and eagles. Wrens glean tiny invertebrates (spiders, mites, springtails, small beetles) from moss, bark crevices, root tangles, and decomposing wood. In salmon-fertilized stands, the abundance and biomass of these microinvertebrates is measurably higher, and the wrens respond with higher territory density and greater reproductive output. The Pacific Wren's extremely small body size (9-10 g) and high metabolic rate make it particularly sensitive to prey availability — the salmon nutrient subsidy may be the difference between winter survival and starvation in years of poor insect production.
**Ecoregion:** Moist coniferous forests from coastal to montane; strongest salmon connection in old-growth riparian corridors of the Coast Range and western Cascades
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round resident; nutrient benefit is continuous but most critical during winter (November-February) when insect prey is scarce
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

---

### Song Sparrow (*Melospiza melodia*)

**Connection Type:** Nutrient beneficiary
**Salmon Life Stage:** Carcass (indirect — via enriched riparian invertebrates and vegetation)
**Mechanism:** Song Sparrows are one of the most abundant breeding birds in PNW riparian corridors, occupying the shrubby edge habitat between stream and forest where salmon-derived nutrient deposition is concentrated. They forage on seeds and invertebrates gleaned from riparian shrubs and ground litter. In salmon-fertilized riparian zones, Song Sparrows benefit from (1) higher invertebrate density in enriched leaf litter, (2) larger seed production from nitrogen-fertilized riparian grasses and forbs, and (3) denser shrub growth that provides better nesting cover. Territory density of Song Sparrows along salmon-bearing streams is consistently higher than along salmon-free streams of comparable structure (Christie & Reimchen 2008). This makes Song Sparrow density a potential bioindicator of salmon nutrient loading in riparian ecosystems.
**Ecoregion:** Riparian corridors throughout the PNW, from coastal to interior valleys; salmon benefit strongest in lowland stream margins
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round resident; nutrient benefit most significant during breeding season (March-July)
**Cross-ref:** `resident.md`

### Category D: Smolt and Fry Specialists

These species primarily interact with salmon during the vulnerable juvenile outmigration period, when smolts concentrate in estuaries and river mouths.

---

### Caspian Tern (*Hydroprogne caspia*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator (smolt specialist)
**Salmon Life Stage:** Smolt (primary), fry
**Mechanism:** Caspian Terns are the largest tern in the world and the most controversial avian predator in the PNW salmon system. The East Sand Island colony in the Columbia River estuary — at its peak the world's largest Caspian Tern colony with 8,900-10,000 breeding pairs — consumed an estimated 5-18 million juvenile salmonids annually, including ESA-listed steelhead and Chinook smolts from the upper Columbia and Snake River basins (Roby et al. 2003; Collis et al. 2001). PIT-tag recovery studies documented that Caspian Terns were responsible for 4-8% of total smolt mortality for some ESA-listed populations during the estuary passage phase. This predation pressure triggered a federal management response: the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers reduced suitable nesting habitat on East Sand Island and created alternative nesting sites throughout the interior West to redistribute the colony. The colony was reduced to approximately 3,500 pairs by 2020, with demonstrable reductions in per-population smolt predation rates.
**Ecoregion:** Columbia River estuary (primary); Puget Sound, Willapa Bay, Grays Harbor; interior nesting at Crump Lake (OR), Sheepy Lake (CA)
**Seasonal Timing:** April-August (summer breeder); peak smolt predation April-June (aligned with peak outmigration)
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

---

### Common Tern (*Sterna hirundo*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Smolt, fry
**Mechanism:** Common Terns are smaller than Caspian Terns and less individually impactful on salmon populations, but they nest in colonies near river mouths and estuaries where outmigrating smolts concentrate. Common Terns take salmon fry and smolts by plunge-diving from heights of 5-15 meters, targeting fish in the upper 1-2 meters of the water column. Their smaller bill limits prey size to fish under approximately 10 cm — the size range of salmon smolts. In the Columbia River estuary, Common Terns co-occur with Caspian Terns and double-crested Cormorants, creating a multi-species predation gauntlet that smolts must pass during outmigration.
**Ecoregion:** Columbia River estuary, Puget Sound, Grays Harbor; less common than Caspian Tern in the PNW
**Seasonal Timing:** May-August (summer breeder); peak smolt predation May-June
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`, `migratory.md`

---

### Double-crested Cormorant (*Nannopterum auritum*)

**Connection Type:** Direct predator
**Salmon Life Stage:** Smolt, juvenile
**Mechanism:** Double-crested Cormorants are pursuit divers — they swim underwater using their feet for propulsion, chasing and capturing fish at depths of up to 7 meters. The East Sand Island colony in the Columbia River estuary grew from 100 pairs in 1989 to over 14,000 pairs by 2013, becoming the largest colony in western North America. PIT-tag studies estimated that cormorants consumed 5-20 million juvenile salmonids per year from the Columbia basin, with per-population predation rates of 2-15% for some ESA-listed stocks — in some cases exceeding Caspian Tern predation (Lyons et al. 2014; Evans et al. 2012). Management actions beginning in 2015 reduced the colony through egg oiling and hazing, decreasing it to approximately 5,500 pairs by 2019. Cormorants differ from terns in their depth capabilities — they capture smolts that have moved deeper in the water column to avoid surface-feeding terns, creating complementary predation pressure.
**Ecoregion:** Columbia River estuary (primary colony); Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia, coastal WA and OR estuaries
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round (some resident, some migratory); peak smolt predation April-June
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

### Category E: Marine Connection Species

These species share the marine food web with salmon during the ocean phase, competing for or consuming the same forage fish and zooplankton resources.

---

### Brown Pelican (*Pelecanus occidentalis*)

**Connection Type:** Marine food web overlap
**Salmon Life Stage:** Marine (shared forage fish — anchovy, sardine, herring)
**Mechanism:** Brown Pelicans are plunge-diving fish predators that feed primarily on Pacific anchovies (*Engraulis mordax*), Pacific sardines (*Sardinops sagax*), and Pacific herring (*Clupea pallasii*) — the same forage fish species that form a major component of juvenile and subadult salmon diet in nearshore marine waters. Pelicans are post-breeding visitors to the PNW (arriving from California and Mexican breeding colonies in June-July, departing by November), and their presence in PNW waters is a reliable indicator of forage fish abundance. When forage fish collapse (as during warm-water El Nino events), both pelicans and juvenile salmon suffer reduced survival. The forage fish connection makes pelicans a bellwether for the marine productivity that sustains salmon during their ocean phase.
**Ecoregion:** Outer coast of WA and OR; Puget Sound (rare); Columbia River plume
**Seasonal Timing:** June-November (post-breeding visitor); peak PNW presence August-October
**Cross-ref:** `migratory.md`

---

### Common Murre (*Uria aalge*)

**Connection Type:** Marine food web overlap and direct competitor
**Salmon Life Stage:** Marine (shared forage fish — herring, sand lance, smelt)
**Mechanism:** Common Murres are the most abundant seabird in the PNW, with breeding colonies totaling 700,000+ birds on the Oregon and Washington coasts (source: G-18). Murres are pursuit divers capable of reaching depths of 180 meters, feeding on Pacific herring, Pacific sand lance (*Ammodytes personatus*), surf smelt (*Hypomesus pretiosus*), and eulachon (*Thaleichthys pacificus*) — all key forage fish species also consumed by juvenile and subadult salmon in the marine environment. The overlap in diet creates potential competition during years of low forage fish abundance. Murres also consume juvenile salmon directly when available, though salmon are not a primary prey item. Murre breeding success on the Oregon coast correlates with forage fish abundance in the same way that marine survival of juvenile salmon does — both are downstream indicators of marine ecosystem productivity.
**Ecoregion:** Outer coast of WA and OR; breeding colonies at Three Arch Rocks, Cape Meares, Yaquina Head (OR); Tatoosh Island, Protection Island (WA)
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round in marine waters; breeding April-August
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

---

### Rhinoceros Auklet (*Cerorhinca monocerata*)

**Connection Type:** Marine food web overlap
**Salmon Life Stage:** Marine (shared forage fish — sand lance, herring, smelt)
**Mechanism:** Rhinoceros Auklets are burrow-nesting seabirds that breed on offshore islands and forage in nearshore waters, diving to 30-60 meters for small schooling fish. Their diet overlaps substantially with juvenile salmon — both target Pacific sand lance, herring, and smelt in the productive nearshore zone within 30 km of shore. Protection Island in the Strait of Juan de Fuca supports approximately 36,000 breeding pairs (the largest colony in the lower 48), and diet studies from this colony provide a long-term record of forage fish availability that tracks marine conditions affecting salmon survival. When sand lance populations crash, both auklet breeding success and juvenile salmon growth rates decline in parallel.
**Ecoregion:** Strait of Juan de Fuca, San Juan Islands, outer WA and OR coast; breeding colonies on Protection Island, Destruction Island, and numerous BC islands
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round in marine waters; breeding April-August; peak forage fish demand during chick-rearing (June-August)
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

---

### Pigeon Guillemot (*Cepphus columba*)

**Connection Type:** Marine food web overlap
**Salmon Life Stage:** Marine (shared nearshore habitat and forage fish)
**Mechanism:** Pigeon Guillemots are small alcids that forage in shallow nearshore waters, typically within 1-2 km of shore, diving to 30-45 meters for sand lance, gunnel, sculpin, and other small demersal and schooling fish. Their nearshore foraging niche overlaps with the habitat used by juvenile salmon during their first marine summer, when smolts transition from estuarine to nearshore marine life. Guillemots nest in crevices along rocky shorelines and are sensitive to disturbance — their breeding success is closely tied to the health of the nearshore marine ecosystem that also supports juvenile salmon. Guillemot chick diet composition has been used as a bioindicator of nearshore food web health in Puget Sound monitoring programs.
**Ecoregion:** Rocky shorelines throughout the PNW; Puget Sound, San Juan Islands, outer coast; breeding colonies on most rocky headlands and islands
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round; breeding April-August
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`

---

### Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*)

**Connection Type:** Marine food web overlap and shared habitat dependency
**Salmon Life Stage:** Marine (shared forage fish); Terrestrial (shared old-growth dependency)
**Mechanism:** The Marbled Murrelet occupies a unique position in the salmon-bird nexus: it depends on both the marine food web that sustains salmon (feeding on sand lance, herring, and euphausiids) and the old-growth forests that salmon help fertilize (nesting on moss-covered limbs of ancient conifers). This dual dependency means Marbled Murrelets are doubly vulnerable to the factors threatening salmon — marine productivity decline reduces their prey, while old-growth logging eliminates their nesting habitat, and the loss of salmon-derived nutrients may reduce the forest complexity on which they depend for nest sites. The species is ESA-threatened and a PNW conservation priority. Its population trajectory parallels salmon in troubling ways: both are in long-term decline, both are impacted by habitat loss and climate change, and both require functional connectivity between marine and terrestrial ecosystems.
**Ecoregion:** Nearshore marine waters and old-growth forests within 50 km of coast; outer WA and OR coast, Puget Sound, Strait of Georgia
**Seasonal Timing:** Year-round in marine waters; breeding May-September (nesting in old-growth)
**Cross-ref:** `shorebirds.md`, `resident.md`

## 4. Salmon Nutrient Pathway — The Avian Chapter

The five-stage salmon nutrient pathway documented in `ecological-networks.md` Pathway 1 describes the journey of marine-derived nitrogen (MDN) from ocean to forest. Birds participate at every stage, but their role as secondary and tertiary consumers of salmon-derived nutrients has only recently been quantified through stable isotope analysis.

### Marine-Derived Nitrogen in Avian Tissues

The tracer that reveals the salmon-bird connection is nitrogen-15 (N-15). Marine nitrogen has a naturally higher N-15/N-14 ratio than terrestrial nitrogen (because marine food webs are longer than terrestrial food webs, and each trophic step concentrates the heavier isotope). When salmon carry marine nitrogen into freshwater and terrestrial ecosystems, organisms that assimilate it acquire an elevated N-15 signature that can be measured in feathers, blood, muscle, and eggshell.

**Key isotopic studies documenting salmon-derived nitrogen in PNW birds:**

| Species | Tissue | N-15 enrichment (salmon vs. reference stream) | Study |
|---------|--------|----------------------------------------------|-------|
| American Dipper | Feathers | +2.5-4.0 per mil | Christie & Reimchen 2008 |
| Song Sparrow | Feathers | +1.5-3.0 per mil | Wilkinson et al. 2018 |
| Pacific Wren | Feathers | +1.0-2.5 per mil | Hocking & Reimchen 2002 |
| Varied Thrush | Feathers | +1.5-3.5 per mil | Reimchen et al. 2003 |
| Bald Eagle | Feathers | +3.0-6.0 per mil | Darimont et al. 2008 |
| American Robin | Feathers | +1.0-2.0 per mil | Wilkinson et al. 2018 |
| Steller's Jay | Feathers | +0.5-1.5 per mil | Hocking & Reimchen 2002 |

### Nutrient Flow Pathways to Birds

**Pathway A — Direct consumption (predators and scavengers):**
Salmon tissue → bird. This is the most efficient pathway, requiring only one trophic step. Bald Eagles consuming salmon carcasses receive marine nitrogen directly, with minimal metabolic loss. The N-15 signature in direct consumers is the highest among avian participants.

**Pathway B — Invertebrate intermediary (nutrient beneficiaries):**
Salmon carcass → soil decomposition → soil invertebrates → bird. This two-step pathway is the primary route for forest-floor insectivores like Varied Thrush, Pacific Wren, and Song Sparrow. The N-15 signal is attenuated by each trophic step but remains detectable in bird feathers.

**Pathway C — Plant intermediary (seed-eating beneficiaries):**
Salmon carcass → soil decomposition → plant uptake → seed/fruit → bird. This three-step pathway reaches granivorous and frugivorous birds through enriched plant tissues. Song Sparrows consuming salmon-fertilized grass seeds, or American Robins eating enriched salmonberry fruit, receive marine nitrogen through this longer chain. The N-15 signal is weakest in this pathway but still measurable.

**Pathway D — Aquatic invertebrate intermediary (riparian insectivores):**
Salmon carcass/tissue (in-stream) → aquatic invertebrate (stonefly, caddisfly, dipteran larvae) → adult insect emergence → aerial insectivore. This pathway connects salmon nutrients to birds through aquatic insect emergence. Stream invertebrates consuming salmon tissue acquire elevated N-15, and when they emerge as adult flying insects, riparian birds (warblers, flycatchers, swallows) that consume them receive the marine nutrient signal. Bilby et al. (1996) documented N-15 enrichment of +3.0-5.0 per mil in stream invertebrates near salmon carcasses.

### Quantified Nutrient Transfer

Estimating the total marine-derived nitrogen transferred to avian communities requires scaling individual consumption rates by population density across salmon-bearing watersheds:

**Direct consumers (eagles, mergansers, kingfishers, herons):**
- A single Bald Eagle consuming 1-2 salmon carcasses per day during a 90-day winter season processes approximately 12-24 kg of salmon tissue, containing ~400-800 g of marine-derived nitrogen
- The Skagit River eagle population (~600 birds) collectively processes an estimated 7,200-14,400 kg of salmon tissue per season
- Across the PNW, the aggregate avian direct consumption of salmon tissue is estimated at 200,000-500,000 kg per year

**Nutrient beneficiaries (songbirds, thrushes, wrens):**
- Quantification is more difficult, but the elevated N-15 in riparian songbird feathers confirms that marine-derived nitrogen constitutes 5-25% of the total nitrogen in riparian insectivore tissues near productive salmon streams (Christie & Reimchen 2008)
- Given the high density of riparian songbirds (50-200 territories per km of stream in old-growth riparian), the aggregate marine-nitrogen subsidy to the avian community is ecologically significant

## 5. Temporal Phenology — The Salmon-Bird Calendar

The interaction between salmon and birds is not constant — it follows a precise annual calendar dictated by salmon run timing, bird breeding and migration schedules, and seasonal insect emergence. Understanding this phenology is essential for conservation planning.

### Salmon Run Timing by Species

| Species | Run Name | Timing | Peak Month | Key Rivers (WA/OR/BC) |
|---------|----------|--------|------------|----------------------|
| Chinook | Spring | Mar-Jun | May | Columbia, Yakima, Wenatchee, Fraser |
| Chinook | Summer | Jun-Aug | Jul | Columbia, Okanagan, upper Fraser |
| Chinook | Fall | Sep-Nov | Oct | Columbia, Puget Sound tribs, Fraser |
| Sockeye | Summer | Jun-Sep | Aug | Baker, Okanagan, Adams/Fraser system |
| Coho | Fall | Oct-Jan | Nov | All coastal and Puget Sound streams |

| Steelhead | Summer | May-Oct | Jul | Columbia tributaries, upper Skagit |

### Bird Presence Overlapping Each Run

**Spring Chinook (March-June):**
- Osprey: Arriving and establishing territories (March-April); active fish predation by May
- Caspian Tern: Colony establishment and peak smolt predation (April-June)
- Common Tern: Arriving late May; smolt predation begins June
- Double-crested Cormorant: Breeding colony active; heavy smolt predation
- Belted Kingfisher: Resident; feeding young on salmon fry
- Green Heron: Arriving April; fry predation on lowland streams
- American Dipper: Resident; feeding on salmon eggs remaining from previous fall

**Summer Chinook and Sockeye (June-September):**
- Osprey: Peak breeding and fish predation; adults provisioning chicks with live salmon
- All three mergansers: Broods actively foraging on juvenile salmon
- Brown Pelican: Arriving from south (July); forage fish overlap in nearshore
- Great Blue Heron: Chick-rearing; heavy juvenile salmon predation at rookeries near streams
- Belted Kingfisher: Feeding fledglings on stream-reared parr

**Fall Chinook and Coho (September-November):**
- Bald Eagle: Numbers building; early migrants arriving from Alaska and interior
- Turkey Vulture: Still present (departs October); scavenging early carcasses
- Common Raven: Attending streams; caching begins
- American Crow: Flocking on gravel bars
- Steller's Jay: Opportunistic carcass visits at forest-stream margins
- Glaucous-winged Gull: Moving to river mouths and lower spawning reaches
- California Gull: Present on east-side rivers

**Chum and Late Coho (November-January):**
- **Bald Eagle congregation peak** — November-January on Skagit, Nooksack, Harrison, Squamish rivers
  - Skagit River: 400-700 eagles, peak December-January
  - Nooksack River: 200-400 eagles, peak November-December
  - Harrison River (BC): 1,000-2,000 eagles, peak November
  - Squamish/Cheakamus rivers (BC): 1,500-3,000 eagles, peak December
- Herring Gull: Winter visitors present; scavenging gravel bars
- Canada Jay: Descending from subalpine to attend stream-side carcasses
- Common Raven: Peak carcass attendance and caching activity
- American Dipper: Feeding on chum eggs in gravel; winter territory defense at spawning reaches

**Winter Steelhead (December-April):**
- Bald Eagle: Still present at lower densities; shifting to steelhead kelts
- Belted Kingfisher: Resident; taking steelhead smolts as they emerge
- American Dipper: Feeding on steelhead eggs at redds; early fry
- Common Merganser: Winter pairs; taking juvenile steelhead

### Eagle Congregation Phenology (Detail)

The annual Bald Eagle congregation on PNW chum streams is the most dramatic intersection of the salmon-bird calendar. The phenology is well-documented:

**Skagit River Eagle Natural Area (WA):**
- First arrivals: Early October (small numbers, typically 20-40 birds)
- Build-up phase: Late October through November (100-300 birds)
- Peak: Late November through January (400-700 birds)
- Decline: February (200-400 birds, shifting to steelhead)
- Last departure: Late March (fewer than 50 birds remaining)
- Chum run overlap: November-January (peak carcass availability = peak eagle numbers)
- Feeding behavior: Dawn-to-dusk foraging on gravel bars; evening roost trees within 1 km of river

**Nooksack River (WA):**
- Peak: November-December (200-400 birds)
- Slightly earlier than Skagit, reflecting earlier chum run timing on the Nooksack
- Delaney Creek and Canyon Creek reaches support highest densities

**Harrison/Chehalis River (BC):**
- Peak: November (1,000-2,000 birds)
- One of the largest eagle concentrations in the Pacific Northwest
- Chum and late coho support the aggregation
- Harrison River Bald Eagle Festival (November) documents annual counts

### Caspian Tern-Salmon Smolt Conflict (Columbia River)

The most intensively studied and managed salmon-bird interaction in the PNW operates on a precise seasonal schedule:

**April:** Caspian Tern colony establishment on East Sand Island. First smolts begin passing through the estuary. Early tern predation is relatively low as colony is still forming.

**May:** Peak smolt outmigration for yearling Chinook and steelhead from the upper Columbia and Snake River basins. Tern colony is at full nesting density. Daily per-bird predation rates are highest during this period — breeding adults provisioning incubating mates with smolts captured in the estuary channels.

**June:** Subyearling Chinook smolts now passing through. Tern chick-rearing creates maximum food demand. Cormorant chick-rearing peaks simultaneously, creating overlapping predation pressure. ESA-listed Snake River steelhead smolts experience highest predation rates during early-to-mid June.

**July:** Smolt outmigration winding down. Tern fledglings learning to fish; predation pressure begins declining. Cormorant fledging and colony departure in late July.

**August:** Tern colony largely abandoned by end of month. Smolt passage essentially complete.

## 6. Conservation Implications

### Salmon Decline and Bird Population Impacts

The relationship between salmon abundance and avian community health is not merely theoretical — it is empirically documented and measurable. When salmon decline, the birds that depend on them respond across multiple timescales:

**Immediate impacts (within 1-5 years of salmon decline):**
- Bald Eagle congregation sizes decline proportionally to salmon run size. On the Skagit River, year-to-year eagle counts correlate significantly with chum salmon escapement estimates (r > 0.7; Stalmaster & Kaiser 1997). Years with poor chum returns produce smaller eagle congregations, and eagles shift to alternative prey (waterfowl, rodents) that provide less energy per unit foraging effort.
- Breeding mergansers and kingfishers on salmon streams show reduced reproductive success when juvenile salmon density falls below threshold levels. Pairs may abandon territories on streams with consistently poor salmon returns.

**Medium-term impacts (5-20 years):**
- Riparian songbird communities simplify as the marine-nutrient subsidy diminishes. Studies comparing bird communities along salmon-bearing and salmon-free streams in coastal BC found 20-30% higher songbird density on salmon streams (Wilkinson et al. 2018). Extrapolating from salmon decline: a 75% reduction in salmon carcass delivery (the approximate current condition on many Columbia basin tributaries) would be expected to reduce riparian songbird density by 15-22% over a decade through reduced invertebrate prey and degraded riparian vegetation.
- Osprey nesting success and territory occupancy decline on rivers with reduced salmon populations, as pairs must forage over larger areas to find sufficient prey.

**Long-term impacts (20-100+ years):**
- The salmon-driven positive feedback loop between forest health and stream health degrades. Reduced salmon nutrients lead to slower riparian tree growth, reduced canopy closure, warmer stream temperatures, less large-wood recruitment, and simplified channel morphology — all of which further reduce salmon productivity, which further reduces nutrient delivery, in a declining spiral. The avian community embedded in this system declines with it.
- Old-growth-dependent species like Marbled Murrelet lose nesting habitat as salmon-deprived forests grow more slowly and develop old-growth structure later (or not at all, if logged on rotation before reaching structural maturity).

### Dam Removal and Bird Community Recovery — The Elwha River Case Study

The removal of the Elwha Dam (2011) and Glines Canyon Dam (2012-2014) from the Elwha River on Washington's Olympic Peninsula created a natural experiment in salmon-ecosystem recovery that includes an avian dimension:

**Pre-removal condition:** The Elwha dams blocked salmon from 70 miles of upstream habitat for nearly 100 years (1913-2011). Upstream reaches received essentially zero marine-derived nutrients. Riparian forests above the dams were measurably nitrogen-limited compared to salmon-bearing reference streams nearby.

**Recovery timeline (avian observations):**
- **2012-2014:** First salmon (primarily Chinook and steelhead) accessed upstream reaches. Bald Eagles observed foraging at former dam sites where salmon carcasses accumulated against remaining infrastructure. Ravens and crows began attending newly accessible salmon reaches.
- **2015-2018:** Salmon spawning documented progressively further upstream. American Dipper territories established on newly accessible stream reaches. Belted Kingfisher pairs occupied previously fishless tributary mouths. Merganser broods observed on reaches that had not seen salmon in a century.
- **2018-2023:** Riparian vegetation regeneration on formerly inundated reservoir sites. Increased songbird diversity documented in recovering riparian zones, though attribution to salmon nutrients versus habitat succession is confounded. Osprey nest observed within the former reservoir footprint.
- **Ongoing monitoring:** The Elwha recovery is expected to take 50-100 years to fully restore salmon-driven ecosystem function, including the riparian bird community. Early indicators are positive — each year brings more salmon further upstream and more birds following them.

The Elwha case demonstrates that salmon recovery is bird recovery. Every dam removed from a salmon river is a restoration of the avian community structure that salmon sustain.

### Hatchery Salmon vs. Wild Salmon — Different Nutrient Value to Birds?

Hatchery-origin salmon now constitute 70-80% of total salmon abundance in many PNW river systems (Columbia basin, Puget Sound). This raises a question relevant to the avian nutrient pathway: do hatchery salmon provide the same nutrient value to birds as wild salmon?

**Body composition differences:**
- Hatchery salmon are typically smaller at return than wild counterparts of the same species (particularly Chinook and coho), carrying less total nitrogen per fish
- Hatchery salmon may have different lipid content and fatty acid profiles due to their hatchery diet, potentially affecting caloric value to avian consumers
- The nitrogen content per unit body mass is similar between hatchery and wild fish; the difference is primarily in total body size

**Behavioral differences affecting avian access:**
- Hatchery salmon often spawn in lower river reaches closer to hatchery facilities, concentrating carcasses in a smaller area rather than distributing them across the watershed
- Hatchery strays (fish returning to non-natal streams) may deposit carcasses in reaches where they provide less ecological benefit
- Spawning ground fidelity is reduced in hatchery fish, potentially scattering carcass deposition unpredictably

**Net assessment:**
Hatchery salmon contribute marine-derived nutrients and are consumed by birds. However, they are not a full ecological substitute for wild salmon because they deposit nutrients in a geographically concentrated pattern (near hatcheries) rather than the dispersed, watershed-wide pattern that wild salmon produce. For birds that depend on dispersed carcass availability (riparian songbirds, dispersed eagle wintering), hatchery salmon partially replace the nutrient function of wild fish but do not replicate the spatial pattern.

### Climate Change and Phenological Mismatch

Rising stream temperatures, shifting precipitation patterns, and ocean warming are altering both salmon run timing and bird phenology in ways that threaten to decouple established interactions:

- Earlier snowmelt and warmer streams may shift salmon spawning earlier in some systems, potentially misaligning carcass availability with peak eagle wintering concentrations
- Ocean warming and marine heatwave events (e.g., the 2014-2016 "Blob") reduce forage fish abundance, simultaneously impacting seabirds (murres, auklets, pelicans) and marine-phase salmon
- Earlier spring arrival of migratory birds (Osprey, terns) may or may not track changes in smolt outmigration timing, creating potential windows of predation pressure or relief
- Drought conditions reduce stream flows, concentrating salmon in smaller reaches where avian predation rates increase, potentially adding mortality pressure to already-stressed populations
