## 1. ELEV-ALPINE: Alpine Avian Communities (6,500+ ft)

### 1.1 Zone Characterization

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Elevation range** | 6,500-14,410 ft (1,981-4,392 m) |
| **Minecraft Y range** | y=121 to y=319 |
| **Precipitation** | 80-150 in/yr; predominantly snow; 200+ in snowpack on volcanic peaks |
| **ECO Band ID** | ELEV-ALPINE |
| **AVI Zone ID** | AVI-ZONE-1 (shared with ELEV-SUBALPINE) |
| **Habitat IDs** | HAB-ALPINE-MEADOW, AVI-HAB-CLIFF |
| **Breeding season** | Late June through early August (~6-8 weeks) |
| **Mean temperature (July)** | 38-50°F (3-10°C) |
| **Growing season** | 45-90 days |
| **Dominant substrate** | Fellfield, krummholz, talus, permanent snowfield, glacial ice |

The alpine zone of the Pacific Northwest is an archipelago of sky islands — isolated mountain summits and ridgelines separated by forested valleys. This insular geography profoundly shapes avian community structure. Each major volcanic peak (Mt. Rainier, Mt. Adams, Mt. Hood, Mt. Baker, Glacier Peak, the Three Sisters) and extended alpine ridge system (North Cascades, Olympic Mountains) supports a semi-isolated avian community connected to neighboring populations only through occasional dispersal across subalpine forest gaps (source: G-07, G-25).

The breeding season is compressed to approximately six to eight weeks, with snow-free conditions typically lasting from late June to mid-September. This compression forces synchronous breeding, compressed molt cycles, and rapid juvenile development. Species that breed here are extreme specialists — generalists cannot compete under these constraints (source: G-07).

Alpine avian species richness is the lowest of any PNW ecoregion band. The combination of extreme weather, compressed breeding season, limited food resources, and small habitat area limits community membership to fewer than 20 breeding species. Year-round residency is restricted to species with specific physiological or behavioral adaptations to survive winter conditions above treeline: seasonal plumage change (White-tailed Ptarmigan), roost-site hypothermia tolerance (Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch), and subnivean foraging (ptarmigan digging through snow for frozen plant material).

### 1.4 Guild Structure

The alpine avian community is organized into four primary functional guilds, reflecting the extreme simplification of food web structure at high elevation.

**Guild 1: Ground Foraging Insectivore-Granivores (70% of breeding community)**

This guild dominates the alpine community numerically. Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, American Pipit, Horned Lark, and White-crowned Sparrow all forage on bare ground, fellfield, and snowfield margins for arthropods and seeds. Their coexistence is mediated by fine-scale microhabitat partitioning:

- **Rosy-Finch:** Specializes on snowfield margins where wind-blown insects (aeolian deposits) accumulate on snow surfaces. Forages on and adjacent to permanent snowfields and glaciers. Nests in rock crevices on cliffs above 8,000 ft. The only PNW songbird that routinely forages on snow and ice (source: G-25, O-07).
- **American Pipit:** Forages on bare ground in alpine meadows and fellfield, avoiding snow surfaces. Takes invertebrates from soil surface and low vegetation. Nests on the ground in tundra-like fellfield, often under a protective rock overhang (source: G-18, O-07).
- **Horned Lark:** Uses the most barren microsites — loose rock, gravel patches, and heavily wind-scoured ridgetops. Avoids dense vegetation. Ground nester with a scrape nest often placed next to a rock or soil clump for wind protection (source: G-18).
- **White-crowned Sparrow:** Restricted to the lower alpine margin where krummholz provides song perches and dense shrub cover for nesting. Does not range onto open fellfield or snowfields. The *oriantha* subspecies breeds at high elevation in the PNW (source: O-03, O-07).

This microhabitat partitioning allows four ground-foraging species to coexist in a zone that appears, from a distance, to be homogeneous bare ground. The key resource axis is substrate type (snow vs. bare ground vs. gravel vs. krummholz edge), not food type — all four take similar invertebrate and seed prey.

**Guild 2: Herbivorous Ground Bird (White-tailed Ptarmigan)**

White-tailed Ptarmigan occupies a guild of one in the PNW alpine. No other bird species fills the large-bodied herbivore niche above treeline. Ptarmigan feed on willow buds (*Salix* spp.), alpine forbs, and sedge seeds during the breeding season, switching to frozen willow buds and conifer needles in winter. Their complete seasonal plumage change — cryptic brown-and-gray in summer, pure white in winter — is unique among PNW birds. Ptarmigan are the only bird species that remains in the alpine zone through the PNW winter, surviving temperatures below -20°F by roosting in snow burrows (subnivean cavities) that insulate them from wind and extreme cold (source: G-25, O-07).

The ptarmigan's ecological role extends beyond herbivory. As a year-round resident, it provides prey for Golden Eagle, Northern Goshawk (occasional visitor from subalpine), and mammalian predators including wolverine, marten, and coyote. Ptarmigan nesting success is an indicator of alpine ecosystem health and climate stability (source: G-25).

**See CAS: fauna (Zone 3 — White-tailed Ptarmigan as alpine specialist)**
**See MAM (pending): Mountain Goat (*Oreamnos americanus*) (alpine habitat associate)**

**Guild 3: Aerial Insectivores (transient)**

Vaux's Swift and Violet-green Swallow forage over the alpine zone during summer months but do not breed there. They exploit insect emergence events — warm updrafts carry arthropods from lower elevations onto alpine snowfields and meadows, creating ephemeral concentrations of aerial prey. These species are not community members in the strict sense (no breeding territory) but contribute to insect population regulation and serve as prey for falcons transiting the alpine (source: O-02).

**Guild 4: Raptors (apex predators)**

Golden Eagle and Prairie Falcon are the apex avian predators of the alpine zone, though both nest on cliffs at or slightly below the alpine boundary. Golden Eagle takes ptarmigan, marmots, pikas, ground squirrels, and hares across a territory that typically spans the alpine and subalpine zones. Prairie Falcon hunts from cliff-launch perches, taking ground squirrels and small birds in open terrain. Red-tailed Hawk is a summer transient that soars over alpine thermals but does not nest above treeline (source: G-25, O-22).

**See MAM (pending): Hoary Marmot (*Marmota caligata*) (Golden Eagle primary prey)**

### 1.5 Indicator Species

#### White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*) — Primary Alpine Indicator

**Ecological justification:** The ptarmigan is the only bird species that occupies the PNW alpine zone year-round. Its population status integrates multiple environmental signals: winter snowpack depth (determines subnivean roosting quality), summer growing season length (determines food availability and chick survival), treeline position (determines habitat extent), and predator pressure (wolverine, eagle, marten). Because it cannot migrate, disperse to new habitat, or tolerate forest conditions, ptarmigan population trends directly reflect alpine ecosystem integrity (source: G-25).

**Indicator signals:**
- Population decline indicates alpine habitat compression (treeline advancing upslope)
- Low chick survival indicates shortened growing season or insect phenology mismatch
- Reduced winter survival indicates inadequate snowpack for subnivean roosting
- Range contraction indicates warming beyond thermal tolerance at lower alpine margin

**Conservation status:** Not federally listed (ESA-NL), but designated as a Species of Concern in Washington State (ST-SC). The Washington Cascades populations represent the southern range limit of the species in the Pacific Northwest. Populations at range margins are the most sensitive to climate change (source: G-14, G-25).

#### Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch (*Leucosticte tephrocotis*) — Snowfield Community Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Rosy-Finch population density tracks snowfield and glacier extent directly. As permanent snow and ice recede, the aeolian insect fallout zone that constitutes the rosy-finch's primary foraging habitat shrinks. Declining rosy-finch populations in a given alpine area are an early warning of glacial recession and permanent snowfield loss (source: G-25, O-02).

### 1.6 Keystone Interactions

#### Interaction 1: Clark's Nutcracker — Whitebark Pine Mutualism (Alpine-Subalpine Bridge)

**Relationship ID:** REL-MUT-CLNU-WBPI
**Type:** Mutualism (Seed Dispersal)
**Species A:** Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*)
**Species B:** Whitebark Pine (*Pinus albicaulis*)
**Mutualism Type:** Scatter-hoarding seed dispersal
**Reciprocal Benefit:** Nutcracker gets high-calorie seeds / Pine gets dispersal to new germination sites above current treeline

Clark's Nutcracker is the primary seed dispersal agent for whitebark pine, the dominant krummholz conifer at the alpine-subalpine boundary. A single nutcracker caches 20,000-30,000 seeds per year (some estimates up to 100,000) in thousands of individual cache sites across the subalpine and alpine landscape (source: P-16). Unretrieved caches germinate, making the nutcracker the primary vector for whitebark pine colonization of new sites, including upslope expansion into the alpine zone as climate warms.

This mutualism is the single most important ecological interaction bridging the alpine and subalpine zones. Whitebark pine seeds are the primary high-calorie food source for nutcrackers during fall caching and winter retrieval. The pine's large, wingless seeds cannot disperse by wind — they are entirely dependent on nutcracker caching for germination beyond the parent tree's canopy shadow (source: P-16).

**Threat:** Whitebark pine is declining across the PNW due to white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*), mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*), and altered fire regimes. As whitebark pine dies, nutcracker populations decline, reducing seed dispersal, which accelerates whitebark pine decline — a positive feedback loop. Whitebark pine was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 2022 (source: G-17).

**See ECO: flora-survey (whitebark pine status and blister rust)**
**See CAS: fauna (Clark's Nutcracker as keystone seed disperser)**

#### Interaction 2: Golden Eagle — Marmot Predation (Top-Down Regulation)

**Relationship ID:** REL-PRED-GOEA-HOMA
**Type:** Predator-Prey
**Predator:** Golden Eagle (*Aquila chrysaetos*)
**Prey:** Hoary Marmot (*Marmota caligata*), American Pika (*Ochotona princeps*), White-tailed Ptarmigan (*Lagopus leucura*)
**Interaction Strength:** Primary (marmots constitute 20-40% of Golden Eagle diet in alpine PNW)

Golden Eagle is the apex avian predator of the alpine zone, exerting top-down regulation on marmot, pika, ground squirrel, and ptarmigan populations. Eagle predation pressure shapes the behavioral ecology of the entire alpine vertebrate community: marmot alarm-whistling systems, pika sentinel behavior, and ptarmigan cryptic coloration are all anti-predator adaptations driven by eagle (and other raptor) hunting (source: G-25, O-22).

**See MAM (pending): Hoary Marmot (*Marmota caligata*) (prey species profile)**

#### Interaction 3: Aeolian Insect Fallout — Snowfield Foraging Community

**Type:** Commensal resource provisioning
**Mechanism:** Wind-transported arthropods deposited on snowfields and glaciers

This is not a species-to-species interaction but a community-structuring process. Updraft winds carry insects from lower-elevation forests and meadows onto alpine snowfields, where they become chilled and immobilized on the snow surface. This "aeolian fallout" is the primary food source for Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch and a supplemental food source for American Pipit and Horned Lark during the breeding season. The interaction is entirely wind-mediated — alpine birds depend on insect production in lower zones delivered by physical processes (source: G-25, P-08).

This interaction links the alpine food web to productivity in the montane and subalpine zones. Poor insect years at lower elevations reduce alpine food availability, depressing rosy-finch breeding success. The interaction also makes alpine bird communities sensitive to pesticide use in lower-elevation forests and agricultural lands — contaminated insects transported to snowfields could expose alpine foragers to toxins originating far from the alpine zone.

### 1.7 Threats

#### 1.7.1 Alpine Compression

The most severe and irreversible threat to PNW alpine avian communities is the upslope advance of treeline driven by warming temperatures. As treeline moves upward, the area of alpine habitat shrinks. On conical volcanic peaks, each increment of upslope treeline advance eliminates a geometrically increasing proportion of remaining alpine habitat (the cone narrows toward the summit). On Mt. Rainier, modeling suggests a 2°C mean annual temperature increase could shift treeline from approximately 6,500 ft to 7,500 ft, reducing available alpine habitat by approximately 30-50% (source: G-25, G-07).

Species that cannot retreat further upslope (because they are already at or near the summit) face local extirpation. White-tailed Ptarmigan, obligate alpine residents, have no upslope refugia on most Cascade volcanic peaks. The North Cascades, with more extensive high-elevation ridgeline habitat, provide better long-term refugia than isolated volcanic peaks.

#### 1.7.2 Snowpack Loss

Declining winter snowpack directly threatens ptarmigan (subnivean roosting), rosy-finch (snowfield foraging habitat), and the alpine-subalpine boundary ecosystem. The Washington Cascades snowpack declined approximately 25% between 1950 and 2020, with losses concentrated at lower elevations but increasingly affecting the alpine zone (source: G-07).

Reduced snowpack also shifts the timing of snowmelt, potentially creating phenological mismatches between insect emergence and bird breeding schedules. Alpine birds time breeding to coincide with peak insect availability in the narrow post-snowmelt window. If snowmelt occurs earlier but photoperiod-triggered breeding cues do not shift, chick-rearing may occur before or after the insect peak.

#### 1.7.3 Recreational Disturbance

Alpine areas accessible by trail receive concentrated recreational use during the short summer season. Trail-adjacent ptarmigan and pipit nesting sites are vulnerable to disturbance from hikers, off-trail travel, and unleashed dogs. Mount Rainier National Park, North Cascades National Park, and the Alpine Lakes Wilderness Area all document visitor impacts on alpine bird habitat (source: G-25).

#### 1.7.4 Climate-Driven Range Shifts in Predators

Warming temperatures allow forest-dwelling predators (marten, coyote, red fox) to access alpine habitats earlier and more frequently, increasing predation pressure on ground-nesting birds. The Cascade red fox (*Vulpes vulpes cascadensis*), historically a subalpine specialist, may expand upslope as snow conditions change (source: G-25).

**See CAS: threats (climate compression threats to alpine species)**

### 1.8 Salmon Thread

**Salmon Thread:** Indirect

Alpine zones are headwater source areas for glacial-fed rivers that support anadromous salmon populations at lower elevations. Alpine avian communities do not directly interact with salmon, but glacial meltwater quantity and timing affect downstream salmon habitat quality. Alpine snowpack decline reduces late-summer stream flow, increasing water temperatures in salmon-bearing montane and lowland reaches. The alpine community is therefore indirectly connected to the salmon thread through hydrology.

The subalpine zone is a mosaic landscape — tree clumps alternating with meadow openings, created by snow deposition patterns, wind exposure, soil depth, and cold-air drainage. This parkland structure is the defining physical characteristic of the zone and the primary driver of avian community diversity. The mosaic provides simultaneous access to forest cover (thermal refuge, nesting substrate, predator avoidance) and open meadow (foraging, insect prey, berry crops) within a single bird territory (source: G-07, G-25).

Deep winter snowpack (10-20 ft) buries most of the landscape for 6-8 months, creating a fundamentally different winter environment from the alpine zone above (which is wind-scoured and exposed) or the montane zone below (where snow is shallower and intermittent). Species that overwinter in the subalpine must tolerate deep snow: grouse eat conifer needles, nutcrackers retrieve cached seeds, and chickadees use hypothermic roosting in tree cavities (source: G-07).

### 2.4 Guild Structure

The subalpine avian community is organized into six functional guilds, reflecting the more complex habitat structure compared to the alpine zone.

**Guild 1: Conifer Seed Specialists (Clark's Nutcracker, crossbills, Pine Grosbeak, Cassin's Finch)**

This guild is the ecological signature of the subalpine zone. Species in this guild have evolved specialized bill morphology and behavior to exploit conifer seeds — the most abundant and energy-rich food source in the subalpine. Clark's Nutcracker, the guild keystone, has a specialized sublingual pouch that can carry up to 150 whitebark pine seeds for transport to cache sites up to 22 km from the source tree (source: P-16).

Guild membership is structured by cone specialization:
- **Clark's Nutcracker:** Whitebark pine cones (large seeds, indehiscent cones requiring active extraction)
- **White-winged Crossbill:** Subalpine fir and mountain hemlock cones (smaller seeds, mandible crossover for prying apart cone scales)
- **Pine Grosbeak:** Buds, seeds, and fruits broadly; subalpine fir and mountain ash berries
- **Cassin's Finch:** Smaller conifer seeds and forbs; feeds at lower strata than crossbills

The irruptive dynamics of crossbills and Pine Grosbeak add temporal complexity to guild structure. In years with poor cone crops, these species may be absent from the subalpine entirely, emigrating to lower elevations or distant mountain ranges. This boom-bust cycle is a fundamental rhythm of the subalpine bird community (source: O-02, O-16).

**Guild 2: Bark Probers and Trunk Foragers (Mountain Chickadee, Red-breasted Nuthatch, Brown Creeper, Hairy Woodpecker)**

The bark prober guild exploits arthropods and their larvae concealed in bark crevices, under bark scales, and in dead wood. Guild members partition the trunk-and-branch foraging space vertically and behaviorally:

- **Mountain Chickadee:** Small branches and twig tips; also gleans from foliage; caches food items in bark crevices (important winter food supply)
- **Red-breasted Nuthatch:** Descends trunk headfirst (unique foraging direction allows access to prey items hidden from upward-climbing species); mid-trunk to large branches
- **Brown Creeper:** Ascends trunk from base in spiral pattern; probes deep bark crevices with curved bill; small prey items
- **Hairy Woodpecker:** Excavates bark and dead wood; accesses deeper-buried larvae; creates feeding holes used by other species

This guild is the primary consumer of bark beetle larvae and overwintering arthropod eggs, providing measurable pest control services in subalpine forests (source: G-07, O-20).

**Guild 3: Canopy Gleaners (kinglets, warblers)**

Golden-crowned Kinglet, Ruby-crowned Kinglet, Townsend's Warbler, and Yellow-rumped Warbler forage by gleaning arthropods from conifer foliage in the canopy and subcanopy. The two kinglet species are among the smallest North American songbirds (5-6 g) and can access the outermost twig tips where larger birds cannot forage. Their high surface-area-to-volume ratio makes them vulnerable to cold — Golden-crowned Kinglets survive winter nights in the subalpine by roosting in dense conifer clumps and entering controlled hypothermia, reducing body temperature by several degrees to conserve energy (source: O-07).

**Guild 4: Ground Foraging Insectivores (Hermit Thrush, Varied Thrush, Dark-eyed Junco)**

The thrush guild forages in the leaf litter and moss layer of the subalpine forest floor, consuming invertebrates, soft-bodied larvae, and seasonal berries. Hermit Thrush is the most abundant ground-foraging insectivore of the subalpine zone and among the last migratory songbirds to depart in fall. Its song — a series of ethereal, spiraling phrases — is the characteristic sound of PNW subalpine forests at dusk (source: O-07, O-26).

**Guild 5: Aerial Insectivores (Olive-sided Flycatcher, Hammond's Flycatcher, Mountain Bluebird)**

These species capture flying insects from exposed perches. Olive-sided Flycatcher uses the tallest snags and treetops as sally perches, hawking large flying insects over the canopy. Its distinctive "Quick, three beers!" call is diagnostic of subalpine parkland in the breeding season. Olive-sided Flycatcher is classified as Near Threatened (IUCN-NT) due to population declines across its range, driven by wintering habitat loss in Central and South America (source: O-04, G-18).

**Guild 6: Grouse (Herbivorous Residents)**

Dusky Grouse and Spruce Grouse are the large-bodied herbivorous residents of the subalpine, analogous to ptarmigan in the alpine zone. Their winter ecology is remarkable — both species undergo a reversed altitudinal migration: they move upslope in fall from lower-elevation breeding territories to dense subalpine conifer stands, where they spend winter feeding almost exclusively on conifer needles (subalpine fir and Douglas-fir). This upslope winter movement is counterintuitive but adaptive — dense subalpine forest provides thermal cover and unlimited conifer-needle food, while lower-elevation deciduous forests lose both cover and food in winter (source: G-25, O-07).

- **Dusky Grouse** (formerly Blue Grouse): More common; uses subalpine fir and Douglas-fir stands. Males perform courtship displays on open ridges in spring, producing deep hooting calls amplified by inflated cervical air sacs.
- **Spruce Grouse:** More boreal in affinity; associated with spruce and subalpine fir in the North Cascades and northern mountains. Designated as a Species of Concern in Washington (ST-SC) due to limited range and habitat specificity.

### 2.5 Indicator Species

#### Clark's Nutcracker (*Nucifraga columbiana*) — Primary Subalpine Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Clark's Nutcracker is the keystone mutualist of the subalpine zone. Its population density is a direct proxy for whitebark pine seed crop size, which in turn reflects whitebark pine health (blister rust infection, beetle kill, fire history). Nutcracker populations that decline or abandon a subalpine area signal whitebark pine failure. Because whitebark pine is a foundation species of the upper subalpine, nutcracker absence indicates ecosystem-level degradation. Furthermore, nutcracker caching behavior is the primary regeneration mechanism for whitebark pine — if nutcrackers leave, whitebark pine recruitment stops (source: P-16, G-17).

**Indicator signals:**
- Nutcracker absence from historically occupied subalpine areas = whitebark pine failure
- Reduced caching activity = poor cone crop (rust, beetle, drought)
- Range contraction downslope = subalpine ecosystem degradation
- Irruptive invasion into lowland areas = subalpine cone crop failure (emergency emigration)

#### Northern Goshawk (*Accipiter gentilis*) — Apex Predator Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Northern Goshawk requires large, contiguous tracts of mature and old-growth forest with closed canopy and open understory for its hunting flights. Goshawk territory occupancy is one of the best available indicators of forest structural integrity at the landscape scale. A territory that supports a breeding pair of goshawks reliably contains mature forest structure, adequate prey density (grouse, squirrels, hares, small birds), and forest connectivity to adjacent territories. Goshawk is designated a Species of Concern in both Washington and Oregon and is a Management Indicator Species on multiple National Forests in the PNW (source: G-24, G-22, O-22).

**Indicator signals:**
- Territory abandonment = forest fragmentation or structural degradation
- Low nest success = prey base decline (often grouse or squirrel)
- Increased territory size = reduced habitat quality (birds must range farther for food)

**See CAS: fauna (Zone 2 — Northern Goshawk as forest interior raptor)**

### 2.6 Keystone Interactions

#### Interaction 1: Clark's Nutcracker — Whitebark Pine Obligate Mutualism

**Relationship ID:** REL-MUT-CLNU-WBPI (same as alpine section — this interaction bridges both zones)

This mutualism is fully described in Section 1.6 above. In the subalpine zone, it operates at higher intensity because the zone contains the densest whitebark pine stands and the majority of nutcracker nesting habitat. Nutcrackers typically nest in subalpine conifers at 5,000-6,500 ft while caching seeds across both the subalpine and alpine zones, making them the primary biological link between the two zones (source: P-16).

#### Interaction 2: Dusky Grouse — Conifer Needle Herbivory (Winter Trophic Link)

**Relationship ID:** REL-PRED-DUGR-ABLA
**Type:** Herbivore-Plant
**Herbivore:** Dusky Grouse (*Dendragapus obscurus*)
**Plant:** Subalpine fir (*Abies lasiocarpa*), Douglas-fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*)
**Interaction Strength:** Primary (95%+ of winter diet is conifer needles)

Dusky Grouse winter herbivory on conifer needles represents a significant trophic pathway from primary production to vertebrate biomass in the subalpine zone. Individual grouse consume approximately 40-50 g of conifer needles per day throughout the 5-6 month winter, sustained by specialized gut microbiota that ferment cellulose. The grouse's cecal fermentation chamber, proportionally among the largest of any bird, allows extraction of nutrients from the low-quality needle diet. This adaptation enables year-round residency without food caching, migration, or hibernation (source: G-25, O-07).

Grouse in turn provide prey for Northern Goshawk, Great Horned Owl, and mammalian predators (marten, coyote, bobcat), transmitting primary production energy from conifers to apex predators through a single-link chain.

#### Interaction 3: Gray Jay (Canada Jay) — Food Caching and Nest Timing

**Relationship ID:** REL-CACHE-CAJA-SUBALPINE
**Type:** Behavioral ecology — food caching as community process

Gray Jay (*Perisoreus canadensis*) is the most prolific avian food cacher in the subalpine zone after Clark's Nutcracker. Gray Jays cache thousands of food items per season — insects, berries, fungi, carrion fragments, and seeds — in bark crevices, under lichen, and in conifer needle clusters throughout their territory. They use saliva to adhere cached items to branches, creating a dispersed "pantry" that funds their extraordinary nesting phenology: Gray Jays begin nesting in February-March, when the subalpine is still buried under deep snow, relying entirely on cached food to fuel incubation and early chick-rearing (source: O-07, G-25).

This early nesting phenology means Gray Jay nest success depends on fall food caching success, which depends on summer and fall food abundance, which depends on the previous growing season's productivity. The jay's breeding success therefore integrates ecological conditions across a full annual cycle, making it a sensitive indicator of subalpine productivity trends (source: G-25).

Gray Jay cached food items that are not retrieved may germinate (seeds) or inoculate trees with fungi, providing secondary dispersal services that parallel nutcracker seed caching at smaller scale.

### 2.7 Threats

#### 2.7.1 Whitebark Pine Decline

Whitebark pine (*Pinus albicaulis*) mortality from white pine blister rust (*Cronartium ribicola*), mountain pine beetle (*Dendroctonus ponderosae*), and altered fire regimes represents the single largest threat to subalpine avian community structure. Whitebark pine was listed as Threatened under the ESA in 2022 (source: G-17). In some areas of the PNW Cascades, whitebark pine mortality exceeds 50% of standing trees. The loss of whitebark pine eliminates the primary food source for Clark's Nutcracker, reduces mast crops that support bears, squirrels, and other seed consumers, and removes the krummholz matrix that shelters alpine-subalpine transition bird communities (source: P-16, G-17).

#### 2.7.2 Fire Regime Change

Fire suppression over the past century has altered subalpine forest structure. Historically, subalpine forests experienced infrequent but high-severity fires at 100-300 year intervals that created mosaic age-class patterns supporting diverse bird communities. Contemporary fire suppression has allowed fuel accumulation, increasing the risk of unnaturally large, homogeneous stand-replacing fires that eliminate the mosaic structure. Conversely, climate-driven fire intensification is producing mega-fires that exceed historical severity, burning into subalpine and alpine zones that historically were too moist and cold to carry fire (source: G-07, G-08).

Post-fire subalpine bird communities undergo a predictable succession: immediate post-fire habitat supports woodpeckers (especially Black-backed Woodpecker), flycatchers, and bluebirds that exploit dead-tree resources and open-ground foraging; within 10-20 years, dense shrub and young conifer regeneration supports warblers, sparrows, and thrushes; full recovery to pre-fire community composition requires 100-300 years (source: G-22).

#### 2.7.3 Snowpack Decline and Seasonality Shifts

Subalpine snowpack is declining and melting earlier. Earlier snowmelt advances the timing of insect emergence and plant phenology, potentially creating mismatches with bird breeding phenology. Species like Olive-sided Flycatcher that are cued by photoperiod rather than snow conditions may arrive to find peak food availability already past. Reduced snowpack also degrades winter habitat for grouse (shallower subnivean space) and may alter the reversed altitudinal migration pattern of Dusky Grouse if winter conditions in the subalpine become more variable (source: G-07, G-25).

#### 2.7.4 Barred Owl Expansion

Barred Owl (*Strix varia*) is expanding into the upper montane and lower subalpine zones of the PNW, driven by westward range expansion across the northern boreal forest. Barred Owls are larger, more aggressive, and more generalized than most native subalpine owl species. Their presence in the subalpine creates competitive pressure on Northern Pygmy-Owl, Northern Saw-whet Owl, and potentially Great Gray Owl. The most severe competition is with Northern Spotted Owl in the montane zone below, but subalpine community effects are beginning to be documented (source: P-13, P-14, G-06).

**See CAS: threats (fire regime change in subalpine zone)**

### 2.8 Salmon Thread

**Salmon Thread:** Indirect (riparian corridor connection)

Subalpine streams are spawning habitat for some high-elevation salmon populations (bull trout, cutthroat trout, and in some watersheds, steelhead). Where anadromous fish reach subalpine elevations, salmon carcasses provide marine-derived nutrients to riparian vegetation and the terrestrial food web. Gray Jay and Common Raven scavenge salmon carcasses at subalpine stream sites. However, most subalpine streams in the PNW are above anadromous barriers, limiting direct salmon thread connections. The subalpine zone's primary salmon thread relevance is as a source of clean, cold water for downstream salmon habitat.

The montane zone is the ecological heart of the PNW — the zone of maximum avian diversity, maximum forest biomass, and maximum structural complexity. Old-growth stands in this zone contain trees exceeding 1,000 years of age with trunk diameters of 10+ feet, living biomass of 500-1,000 tonnes per hectare, and canopy heights of 180-300 feet (source: P-07, G-07). The montane zone is also the most historically impacted by industrial timber harvest. More than 90% of old-growth forest below 4,500 ft on the west slope of the Cascades has been logged since 1850 (source: G-22). The avian communities of the montane zone are therefore defined as much by what has been lost as by what remains. Old-growth obligate species — Northern Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Vaux's Swift — persist in fragmented remnants of a once-continuous forest, and their population trajectories reflect the cumulative impact of habitat loss, continued logging on private lands, and the emerging threat of Barred Owl competition (source: G-06, P-13, P-14).

**Nest web relationships:** The montane cavity nester guild forms a hierarchical "nest web" in which primary excavators (woodpeckers) create cavities that are subsequently occupied by a diversity of secondary users. Pileated Woodpecker is the most important excavator because it creates the largest cavities — large enough for owls, ducks, and swifts. A single Pileated Woodpecker territory may contain 15-20 excavated cavities, of which only one is used for breeding in a given year; the remainder provide nesting and roosting sites for 8-12 secondary-user species (source: G-24, G-22).

The nest web has a critical vulnerability: the loss of large-diameter snags (through logging, salvage harvest, or firewood cutting) eliminates the substrate for cavity excavation. Because Pileated Woodpecker requires snags >20 inches DBH and old-growth-associated snag creation rates are low (individual trees must reach 200-400 years of age and then die), snag supply is the limiting resource for the entire cavity nester guild. Managed forests that remove snags for safety or salvage value eliminate the foundation of the nest web (source: G-22, G-24).

#### Black-backed Woodpecker (*Picoides arcticus*) — Fire Specialist

Black-backed Woodpecker is the signature species of post-fire montane forest. It is the only PNW woodpecker that preferentially selects recently burned forest over unburned forest. Its ecology is tightly linked to wood-boring beetle larvae (primarily *Monochamus* and *Melanophila* species) that colonize fire-killed trees within the first 1-3 years after fire. A single pair of Black-backed Woodpeckers consumes an estimated 13,500 beetle larvae per year from fire-killed trees (source: G-22, G-08).

Black-backed Woodpecker's presence in post-fire habitat typically peaks at 3-5 years post-fire and declines as beetle larval populations decrease and bark begins to slough from dead trees. This narrow window of peak abundance makes the species vulnerable to post-fire salvage logging, which removes the snag-rich habitat that supports both the woodpecker and its beetle prey. The retention of unlogged burned areas ("snag patches" or "biological legacies") is a central management controversy in post-fire forest policy (source: G-22).

### 3.4 Guild Structure

The montane avian community is the most guild-rich of any PNW ecoregion band, with eight major functional guilds reflecting the multi-layered forest architecture.

**Guild 1: Old-Growth Obligates**

Northern Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, and Vaux's Swift define this guild. All three require forest structural features that develop only in trees exceeding 200 years of age: multi-layered canopy (Spotted Owl), wide moss-covered limbs (Murrelet), and large hollow trunks (Swift). The guild's dependence on old-growth structure makes it the most conservation-sensitive avian guild in the PNW and the ecological justification for old-growth preservation (source: G-06, G-09, P-04, G-22).

Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*): Primary nesting zone in Pacific silver fir belt (2,500-4,500 ft). Home ranges of 2,000-14,000 acres in Washington Cascades (source: G-06). Population declining approximately 3.8% per year in Washington due to old-growth loss and Barred Owl competition (source: P-14). Feeds primarily on Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) — a mycophagous old-growth obligate that disperses ectomycorrhizal fungi spores. The trophic chain — old-growth tree > truffle > flying squirrel > Spotted Owl — embodies the guild's dependence on forest structural complexity (source: G-06).

Marbled Murrelet (*Brachyramphus marmoratus*): A seabird that nests in old-growth forests — one of the most extraordinary ecological anomalies in North American ornithology. Murrelets fly from marine foraging areas (ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE) to inland nesting sites (ELEV-MONTANE), commuting up to 80 km each way. They nest on wide, moss-covered limbs of old-growth conifers, typically Douglas-fir, western hemlock, or Sitka spruce trees exceeding 200 years of age. The species is the primary biological bridge between ELEV-MONTANE (nesting) and ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE (foraging), making it the most important cross-band indicator in the PNW AVI system (source: G-09, P-04, P-05, G-13).

**See ECO: shared-attributes (Marbled Murrelet cross-band bridge species)**

**Guild 2: Canopy Gleaners (warblers, kinglets)**

Hermit Warbler, Townsend's Warbler, Yellow-rumped Warbler, and Golden-crowned Kinglet forage in the upper canopy by gleaning arthropods from conifer foliage. Hermit Warbler and Townsend's Warbler are particularly notable because they form a narrow hybrid zone in the Washington Cascades where their ranges meet — one of the best-studied avian hybrid zones in North America (source: P-09). The hybrid zone is moving southward over time, with Townsend's Warbler displacing Hermit Warbler, apparently through competitive interference during territorial establishment (source: P-09).

**Guild 3: Bark Prober/Excavator Complex (woodpeckers, creepers, nuthatches)**

Described in detail in the cavity nester guild section above. This guild is the most species-rich in the montane zone, with 6-8 woodpecker species plus Brown Creeper, nuthatches, and chickadees forming a vertically stratified foraging community on the trunk-and-branch substrate of old-growth trees (source: G-24).

**Guild 4: Understory and Ground Foragers**

Varied Thrush, Swainson's Thrush (*Catharus ustulatus*), Pacific Wren, and Dark-eyed Junco forage in the lower strata of the montane forest. Pacific Wren (formerly lumped with Winter Wren until 2010 taxonomic split; source: O-03) is the characteristic understory bird of PNW old-growth, producing a complex, rapid song that resonates in the cathedral-like spaces between massive tree trunks. Varied Thrush song — a single, ethereal, sustained tone — is perhaps the most iconic sound of the PNW montane forest (source: O-07, O-26).

**Guild 5: Aerial Insectivores**

Olive-sided Flycatcher, Western Wood-Pewee (*Contopus sordidulus*), Hammond's Flycatcher, and Pacific-slope Flycatcher (*Empidonax difficilis*) partition the aerial insectivore niche by foraging height and sally distance. Olive-sided Flycatcher uses the tallest snags and treetops; Pacific-slope Flycatcher operates in the understory and along stream corridors (source: G-18, O-07).

**Guild 6: Forest Raptors**

Northern Goshawk (apex, forest interior), Cooper's Hawk (forest edge and interior), Sharp-shinned Hawk (forest edge, specializes on small birds), Great Horned Owl (generalist apex predator), and Northern Spotted Owl (old-growth specialist) form a predator guild structured by body size, hunting strategy, and habitat selectivity. Goshawk and Spotted Owl are the most habitat-selective, both requiring large, contiguous forest tracts. Cooper's Hawk and Sharp-shinned Hawk are more tolerant of fragmented forest and forest edges (source: O-22, G-06).

**Guild 7: Corvid Generalists**

Steller's Jay, Gray Jay, and Common Raven are the montane corvids — intelligent, behaviorally flexible generalists that exploit a wide range of food sources and serve as nest predators (consuming eggs and nestlings of smaller species), scavengers, and seed dispersers. Steller's Jay is the most abundant corvid in the montane zone and the primary nest predator of many small passerines. Jay nest predation rates increase near forest edges and campgrounds, where jay densities are elevated by human food subsidies (source: P-22, G-22).

**Guild 8: Riparian Specialists (montane streams)**

American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*), Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*), and Harlequin Duck (*Histrionicus histrionicus*) form a small but ecologically important guild associated with montane streams. American Dipper — the only North American passerine that walks underwater — is an indicator of stream invertebrate density and water quality. Dipper presence on a montane stream reliably indicates clean, cold, oxygen-rich water with abundant aquatic insect larvae (mayflies, stoneflies, caddisflies). Harlequin Duck breeds on fast-flowing montane streams and winters in the marine intertidal zone (ELEV-INTERTIDAL), making it a cross-band indicator species (source: G-25, O-07).

### 3.5 Indicator Species

#### Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) — Primary Montane Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Northern Spotted Owl is the quintessential old-growth indicator species and the basis of the Northwest Forest Plan (1994) — the largest landscape-scale conservation program in United States history. Spotted Owl territory occupancy integrates old-growth forest extent, structural complexity, prey abundance (flying squirrels and woodrats), and predator pressure (Barred Owl). A landscape that supports a stable Spotted Owl population is, by definition, maintaining old-growth forest ecosystem function across a large area (source: G-06, G-12, G-22).

**Indicator signals:**
- Territory abandonment = old-growth loss or Barred Owl displacement
- Declining productivity (fewer fledglings) = prey base decline or nest disturbance
- Population trend reversal following Barred Owl removal = competitive release demonstrating Barred Owl as primary threat (source: P-13)
- Range contraction to higher elevations = Barred Owl competitive exclusion at lower elevations

**Conservation status:** ESA-T (Threatened, listed 1990); WA ST-E (State Endangered); OR ST-T (State Threatened); IUCN-NT (Near Threatened) (source: G-17, G-12).

#### Pileated Woodpecker (*Dryocopus pileatus*) — Cavity Web Keystone

**Ecological justification:** Pileated Woodpecker is the keystone of the montane cavity nester guild. Its large excavated cavities support 10+ secondary-user species. Pileated Woodpecker territory occupancy indicates the presence of large-diameter snags (>20 in DBH), mature forest structure, and adequate carpenter ant populations (primary food source). Loss of Pileated Woodpecker from a forest stand cascades through the cavity nester web, reducing nesting opportunities for owls, swifts, ducks, and small passerines (source: G-24, G-22).

### 3.6 Keystone Interactions

#### Interaction 1: Northern Spotted Owl — Northern Flying Squirrel — Truffle — Old-Growth Tree

**Relationship ID:** REL-PRED-NSOW-NOFL
**Type:** Trophic chain (4-level)
**Top predator:** Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*)
**Primary prey:** Northern Flying Squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*)
**Squirrel food:** Hypogeous ectomycorrhizal fungi (truffles)
**Truffle host:** Old-growth Douglas-fir, western hemlock root systems

This four-level trophic chain is the defining ecological interaction of the PNW montane zone. Northern Spotted Owl preys primarily on Northern Flying Squirrel, which feeds primarily on truffles (hypogeous fungi), which form mycorrhizal associations with old-growth conifer root systems. The flying squirrel disperses truffle spores through fecal pellets, inoculating new root systems and maintaining the mycorrhizal network that supports forest health. Each link depends on old-growth forest structure: the owl needs complex canopy for nesting and hunting, the squirrel needs large hollow trees for denning and continuous canopy for aerial travel, the truffles need undisturbed old-growth root systems, and the trees need mycorrhizal fungi for nutrient uptake (source: G-06, G-22).

Removing old-growth structure at any point breaks the chain. This interaction is the strongest empirical argument for old-growth preservation in the PNW and was central to the biological justification for the Northwest Forest Plan (source: G-22).

**See CAS: fauna (Zone 2 — Northern Flying Squirrel as keystone mycophage)**
**See ECO: fungi-microbiome-survey (ectomycorrhizal networks in old-growth forest)**

#### Interaction 2: Barred Owl — Northern Spotted Owl Competition

**Relationship ID:** REL-COMP-BAOW-NSOW
**Type:** Competition
**Species A:** Barred Owl (*Strix varia*)
**Species B:** Northern Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*)
**Competition Type:** Interference (territorial aggression) and exploitative (shared prey base)
**Resource Contested:** Territory, nest sites, Northern Flying Squirrel prey
**Outcome:** Ongoing displacement — Barred Owl replacing Spotted Owl across most of range

Barred Owl is the most significant single threat to Northern Spotted Owl persistence. Barred Owls expanded their range westward across the northern boreal forest during the 20th century, reaching the PNW in the 1960s-1970s, and are now established throughout the Spotted Owl's range. Barred Owls are larger, more aggressive, more generalized in diet, and produce more offspring than Spotted Owls. Where Barred Owls establish territories, Spotted Owls are displaced through direct aggression, competition for prey, and hybridization (source: P-11, P-13, P-14).

Experimental Barred Owl removal in study areas has demonstrated that Spotted Owl survival and territory occupancy increase when Barred Owls are removed, confirming the competitive mechanism (source: P-13). The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service has authorized Barred Owl removal as a management tool in critical Spotted Owl habitat — a controversial decision that pits the conservation of one native species against the management of another (source: G-12, P-14).

#### Interaction 3: Pileated Woodpecker — Cavity Web Cascade

Described in Section 3.3 (Cavity Nester Guild). The Pileated Woodpecker's excavation activity creates the physical infrastructure (large cavities in snags) that supports a community of 10+ species, making it the primary ecosystem engineer of the montane bird community. Loss of Pileated Woodpecker cascades through the entire cavity nester web (source: G-24).

#### Interaction 4: Marbled Murrelet — Marine-Terrestrial Bridge

**Relationship ID:** REL-MUT-MAMU-OLDFR
**Type:** Cross-band ecological bridge
**Marine component:** Foraging on fish and zooplankton in ELEV-SHALLOW-MARINE
**Terrestrial component:** Nesting on old-growth limbs in ELEV-MONTANE

The Marbled Murrelet physically connects the montane forest and the shallow marine ecosystem through daily commuting flights of up to 80 km each way during the breeding season. Murrelet fecal deposition at nest sites transports marine-derived nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, trace minerals) into old-growth forest canopy, contributing to nutrient cycling at a small but measurable scale. More importantly, the murrelet's dual-habitat dependency means its population status integrates threats from both ecosystems: old-growth logging reduces nesting habitat, while marine pollution, oil spills, gillnet bycatch, and forage fish decline reduce foraging success. A declining murrelet population may signal degradation in either or both ecosystems (source: P-04, P-05, G-09, G-13).

### 3.7 Threats

#### 3.7.1 Old-Growth Habitat Loss

The overriding threat to montane avian communities is the continued loss and fragmentation of old-growth forest. Although federal timber harvest on National Forest lands was dramatically reduced by the Northwest Forest Plan (1994), logging continues on state and private lands, and the cumulative legacy of past harvest means that less than 10% of original old-growth forest remains in the montane zone of western Oregon and Washington (source: G-22). Old-growth obligate species (Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Vaux's Swift) cannot persist in young or managed forest. Recovery of old-growth structure in logged areas requires 200+ years — far exceeding any management planning horizon (source: G-22, G-24).

#### 3.7.2 Barred Owl Invasion

Described in detail in Interaction 2 above. Barred Owl expansion is the most acute ongoing threat to Northern Spotted Owl, and by extension to the conservation policy framework (ESA listing, Northwest Forest Plan, critical habitat designations) that protects montane old-growth communities. If Spotted Owls cannot be stabilized even with old-growth protection, the political will to maintain those protections may erode (source: P-14).

#### 3.7.3 Climate-Altered Fire Regimes

Climate change is increasing fire frequency and severity in the montane zone, particularly on the east side of the Cascades. The 2020 fire season in Oregon burned over 1 million acres, including extensive montane forest. While fire is a natural disturbance agent and creates habitat for fire-specialist species (Black-backed Woodpecker, Mountain Bluebird), the scale and severity of contemporary mega-fires exceeds historical patterns, converting large areas of old-growth from closed-canopy forest to early-successional shrubland in a single event. Old-growth structure that required 200-500 years to develop is destroyed in hours. Post-fire salvage logging compounds the impact by removing snag habitat needed by fire-specialist birds (source: G-22, G-08).

#### 3.7.4 Fragmentation and Edge Effects

Forest fragmentation increases edge-to-interior ratios, elevating nest predation rates (by jays, crows, and squirrels) and brood parasitism rates (by Brown-headed Cowbird) for forest interior species. Fragmentation also increases wind exposure and microclimate extremes in forest remnants, reducing habitat quality for moisture-dependent species like Pacific Wren and Varied Thrush. The Northwest Forest Plan's Late-Successional Reserve network was designed to address fragmentation, but connectivity between reserves remains incomplete, particularly on private lands (source: G-22, P-20).

### 3.8 Salmon Thread

**Salmon Thread:** Yes — Direct

The montane zone contains extensive anadromous fish habitat. Chinook salmon, coho salmon, and steelhead spawn in montane streams throughout the Cascades, Olympics, and Coast Range. Avian species directly connected to the salmon nutrient pathway include:

- **Bald Eagle (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*):** Concentrates on salmon-bearing montane streams during spawning runs (October-February). Skagit River winter eagle concentrations exceed 500 individuals in peak years (source: G-18, G-25). Eagles scavenge spent salmon carcasses, distributing marine-derived nutrients away from stream corridors.

- **American Dipper (*Cinclus mexicanus*):** Feeds on aquatic invertebrates whose populations are subsidized by marine-derived nutrients from decomposing salmon carcasses. Dipper density is positively correlated with salmon escapement on montane streams (source: G-25).

- **Common Merganser (*Mergus merganser*):** Feeds on juvenile salmon in montane streams. Predation on salmon smolts is a documented interaction, though its population-level impact on salmon is debated.

- **Great Blue Heron (*Ardea herodias*):** Forages on salmon fry and other fish in montane riparian zones. Colonial nesting in large riparian trees.

- **Belted Kingfisher (*Megaceryle alcyon*):** Feeds on juvenile salmonids in clear montane streams. Presence indicates adequate fish density and water clarity.

The salmon-forest nutrient cycle (marine-derived nitrogen transported by salmon, distributed by bears and eagles, incorporated into riparian vegetation) is the primary mechanism by which the montane avian food web is subsidized by marine productivity. Isotope studies demonstrate that 20-40% of nitrogen in riparian vegetation along salmon-bearing montane streams is marine in origin (source: P-18, G-20).

The shrub-steppe is ecologically and physiographically distinct from all west-side PNW ecoregion bands. It exists in the rain shadow of the Cascade Range, where the orographic barrier strips moisture from Pacific air masses and creates semi-arid conditions that more closely resemble the intermountain West than the mesic PNW. The dominant plant community — big sagebrush with native bunchgrass understory — once covered over 100 million acres across the Columbia Plateau and Great Basin portions of Washington, Oregon, and Idaho. Today, more than half of this habitat has been lost to agriculture, urban development, invasive species (especially cheatgrass), and altered fire regimes (source: P-15, G-15, G-16).

The shrub-steppe avian community is fundamentally different from west-side forest communities. Instead of vertical stratification in tall forest, species partition a low-stature, horizontally structured landscape. Sagebrush canopy height rarely exceeds 4 feet, and most bird species forage and nest within 3 feet of the ground. Community structure is driven by sagebrush density and height, grass cover, bare ground proportion, and the presence of juniper woodland at the upper elevation margin (source: G-07, G-15).

### 4.4 Guild Structure

The shrub-steppe avian community is organized into five functional guilds, reflecting the horizontally structured, low-stature habitat.

**Guild 1: Sagebrush Obligates (Sage-Grouse, Sage Thrasher, Sagebrush Sparrow, Brewer's Sparrow)**

This guild is entirely dependent on sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*) for breeding, foraging, and/or cover. Guild members cannot persist in habitat that has lost its sagebrush canopy. Greater Sage-Grouse is the guild flagship — a large, charismatic grouse whose elaborate lek displays (males inflate cervical air sacs, fan tail feathers, and produce deep bubbling sounds) occur on traditional display grounds (leks) that may be used continuously for decades (source: P-15, G-15).

Sage-Grouse is the ultimate sagebrush obligate. Adults eat sagebrush leaves as their primary winter food (they are among the few vertebrates that can digest sagebrush terpenes). Nesting hens require sagebrush cover >15 inches tall for nest concealment. Brood-rearing hens require intershrub forb and grass cover for insect-rich chick foraging habitat. The species' multi-habitat-stage requirements mean that intact sagebrush landscapes must provide mature sagebrush for cover, intershrub herbaceous understory for chick-rearing, and undisturbed leks for breeding — all within a few miles of each other (source: P-15).

Greater Sage-Grouse was considered for federal ESA listing in 2010 and 2015 but was deemed "not warranted" both times, partly due to ongoing conservation efforts across western states. The species remains an ESA candidate and state-listed in Washington (ST-T). Range-wide population decline is estimated at >50% since 1965 (source: G-17, G-14, P-15).

**Guild 2: Open Ground Foragers (Horned Lark, Long-billed Curlew, Western Meadowlark)**

This guild uses open ground and native bunchgrass for nesting and foraging. All species are ground nesters, making them vulnerable to predation by ground-based predators (coyote, badger, raven, magpie) and to nest destruction by livestock trampling. Long-billed Curlew — North America's largest shorebird by body size — nests in short-grass areas of the shrub-steppe despite being classified as a shorebird. It uses its extremely long, curved bill to probe for invertebrates in grassland soil, an adaptation that links it ecologically to grassland rather than shoreline habitats (source: G-18, O-02).

**Guild 3: Shrub-Steppe Raptors**

The raptor community is described in Section 4.3 above. Key guild characteristics include:

- **Ferruginous Hawk:** The largest North American buteo. Nests on elevated platforms — rimrock cliffs, isolated trees, utility poles, and haystacks — overlooking open steppe. Feeds primarily on ground squirrels, jackrabbits, and pocket gophers. Population declining due to habitat conversion and prey base reduction. Listed as Threatened in Washington State (source: G-14, O-22).

- **Burrowing Owl:** Uses abandoned ground squirrel and badger burrows for nesting — the only North American owl that nests underground. Population declining across the PNW due to loss of burrow sites (ground squirrel control programs eliminate both squirrels and their burrows) and conversion of grassland to agriculture. Designated as a Species of Concern in Washington and Oregon (source: G-14, G-15, O-22).

- **Short-eared Owl:** Ground-nesting, crepuscular raptor of open grassland and marsh. Hunts by low quartering flight over grass. Population fluctuates with vole abundance cycles. Declining across range due to grassland loss (source: G-14).

**Guild 4: Ground Squirrel-Dependent Community (commensal relationships)**

Columbian ground squirrel (*Urocitellus columbianus*) and related burrowing mammals are ecosystem engineers of the shrub-steppe, creating burrow networks that are used by Burrowing Owl, rattlesnakes, and numerous invertebrate species. The ground squirrel population also provides the primary prey base for Ferruginous Hawk, Prairie Falcon, Golden Eagle, and Badger. The ground squirrel-raptor-Burrowing Owl community constitutes an integrated ecological unit — management actions that reduce ground squirrel populations (poisoning, shooting) cascade through the raptor and Burrowing Owl communities (source: G-16, O-22).

**See MAM (pending): American Badger (*Taxidea taxus*) (fossorial predator)**

**Guild 5: Juniper Woodland Transition (Pinyon Jay, Ash-throated Flycatcher)**

Described in Section 4.3 above. This guild occupies the upper elevation ecotone where sagebrush transitions to juniper woodland. Guild members have Great Basin biogeographic affinities and represent the western edge of an intermountain community that extends east to the Rocky Mountains and south to the Southwest.

### 4.5 Indicator Species

#### Greater Sage-Grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*) — Primary Shrub-Steppe Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Sage-Grouse is a landscape-scale indicator because its habitat requirements integrate multiple elements of sagebrush ecosystem integrity: mature sagebrush canopy (winter food and nesting cover), herbaceous understory (chick foraging habitat), undisturbed lek sites (breeding), and landscape connectivity (seasonal movements between winter, breeding, and brood-rearing habitats). A landscape that supports viable Sage-Grouse populations necessarily maintains these multiple habitat components at sufficient scale and connectivity. Population trend data from lek counts provide standardized, long-term monitoring of sagebrush ecosystem health across the PNW east side (source: P-15, G-15, G-16).

**Indicator signals:**
- Lek count decline = sagebrush habitat loss, fragmentation, or degradation
- Lek abandonment = site-level disturbance (energy development, roads, fire)
- Reduced hen-to-lek ratio = poor breeding success (nest predation, weather)
- Range contraction = landscape-scale habitat loss (cheatgrass conversion, fire)

**Conservation status:** ESA-C (candidate); WA ST-T; OR ST-SC; IUCN-NT (source: G-17, G-14, G-15).

#### Brewer's Sparrow (*Spizella breweri*) — Sagebrush Passerine Indicator

**Ecological justification:** Brewer's Sparrow is the most abundant breeding songbird in intact sagebrush steppe and one of the most sensitive to sagebrush degradation. Its population density responds directly to sagebrush canopy cover, height, and patch size. BBS data show that Brewer's Sparrow population trends closely track sagebrush habitat extent — areas where sagebrush has been lost to cheatgrass or juniper encroachment show corresponding Brewer's Sparrow population declines. Because it is small, abundant, and easily detected by song, Brewer's Sparrow is the most practical and cost-effective songbird indicator for sagebrush ecosystem monitoring (source: G-18, O-04).

### 4.6 Keystone Interactions

#### Interaction 1: Greater Sage-Grouse — Sagebrush Obligate Mutualism

**Relationship ID:** REL-MUT-GRSG-ARTR
**Type:** Herbivore-Plant (trophic dependency, not pollination mutualism)
**Species A:** Greater Sage-Grouse (*Centrocercus urophasianus*)
**Species B:** Big Sagebrush (*Artemisia tridentata*)

Sage-Grouse and sagebrush are tightly linked but their relationship is asymmetric: the grouse is entirely dependent on sagebrush, while sagebrush persists readily without grouse. The grouse's dependence is dietary (sagebrush leaves constitute >99% of winter diet), structural (sagebrush canopy provides year-round concealment), and behavioral (leks are traditionally sited in sagebrush openings). This dependency means Sage-Grouse serves as an umbrella species for the entire sagebrush ecosystem — management that protects enough sagebrush to support Sage-Grouse also protects habitat for the other 350+ species associated with the sagebrush biome (source: P-15, G-15).

#### Interaction 2: Burrowing Owl — Ground Squirrel Commensalism

**Relationship ID:** REL-COMM-BUOW-URCOL
**Type:** Commensalism (Burrowing Owl uses ground squirrel burrows)
**Species A:** Burrowing Owl (*Athene cunicularia*)
**Species B:** Columbian Ground Squirrel (*Urocitellus columbianus*), American Badger (*Taxidea taxus*)
**Commensalism Type:** Nest site provision

Burrowing Owl cannot excavate its own burrows — it depends entirely on the burrows of ground squirrels and badgers for nesting and roosting sites. This commensalism makes Burrowing Owl population trends a proxy for burrowing mammal community health. Where ground squirrel control programs (poisoning, shooting) have reduced ground squirrel populations, Burrowing Owl populations have declined in parallel. The relationship also extends to badger — badger burrows, excavated during predation on ground squirrels, provide the largest burrows preferred by nesting owls (source: G-14, G-15, O-22).

#### Interaction 3: Ferruginous Hawk — Ground Squirrel Predation (Top-Down Regulation)

**Relationship ID:** REL-PRED-FEHA-URCOL
**Type:** Predator-Prey
**Predator:** Ferruginous Hawk (*Buteo regalis*)
**Prey:** Columbian Ground Squirrel (*Urocitellus columbianus*), Black-tailed Jackrabbit (*Lepus californicus*)
**Interaction Strength:** Primary (ground squirrels constitute 50-80% of breeding-season diet)

Ferruginous Hawk is the apex avian predator of the shrub-steppe, filling the ecological role that Golden Eagle fills in the alpine zone. Its population dynamics are tightly coupled to ground squirrel abundance — in years with high ground squirrel populations, Ferruginous Hawks produce larger clutches and fledge more young; in low ground squirrel years, many pairs do not breed (source: O-22, G-16).

### 4.7 Threats

#### 4.7.1 Cheatgrass Invasion and Altered Fire Regime

Cheatgrass (*Bromus tectorum*) is the most destructive invasive plant in the PNW shrub-steppe. This Eurasian annual grass has invaded millions of acres of sagebrush habitat, fundamentally altering the fire regime. Native sagebrush steppe burned at 50-100 year intervals; cheatgrass-invaded areas burn at 3-10 year intervals because cheatgrass produces continuous fine fuel that carries fire readily. Repeated short-interval fire eliminates sagebrush (which cannot resprout after fire and takes 15-25 years to re-establish from seed) and converts sagebrush steppe to cheatgrass monoculture — a permanent type conversion that eliminates habitat for all sagebrush obligate bird species (source: G-15, G-16).

The cheatgrass-fire cycle is the single greatest threat to the sagebrush ecosystem and the species that depend on it. Once sagebrush is replaced by cheatgrass, recovery to native vegetation is extremely difficult and expensive. The scale of cheatgrass invasion across the Columbia Plateau and Great Basin (hundreds of millions of acres) means that sagebrush habitat loss is proceeding faster than any restoration effort can reverse (source: G-15).

#### 4.7.2 Agricultural Conversion

The Columbia Basin Irrigation Project and related irrigation infrastructure converted millions of acres of native shrub-steppe to irrigated agriculture (wheat, potatoes, orchard crops, vineyards) during the 20th century. This conversion permanently eliminates sagebrush habitat and the avian communities it supports. Remaining sagebrush fragments are often too small and isolated to support viable populations of area-sensitive species like Sage-Grouse. Agricultural runoff from irrigated fields carries pesticides and fertilizers into adjacent sagebrush habitat and downstream waterways (source: G-15, G-16).

#### 4.7.3 Energy Development and Infrastructure

Wind energy facilities, transmission lines, roads, and associated infrastructure are increasingly sited in shrub-steppe areas due to reliable wind resources and perceived low ecological value. These installations fragment sagebrush habitat, create movement barriers for ground-dwelling species, and cause direct mortality to raptors and other birds through collision with turbine blades and power lines. Greater Sage-Grouse avoids infrastructure — leks within 3 miles of wind turbines or transmission lines show reduced attendance and eventual abandonment (source: P-15, G-15).

#### 4.7.4 Ground Squirrel Control Programs

Intentional reduction of ground squirrel populations through poisoning and shooting programs (to protect crops and reduce rangeland damage) cascades through the raptor and Burrowing Owl communities that depend on ground squirrels for food and/or burrows. Rodenticide use also poses secondary poisoning risks to raptors that consume poisoned ground squirrels (source: G-16, O-22).

### 4.8 Salmon Thread

**Salmon Thread:** Indirect (east-side riparian corridors)

The shrub-steppe zone contains important anadromous fish habitat along the major east-side river systems — the Yakima, Wenatchee, Methow, Okanogan, John Day, and Deschutes rivers. These rivers support Chinook salmon, steelhead, and (historically) sockeye salmon runs that pass through shrub-steppe landscapes. Riparian corridors along these rivers are ribbons of cottonwood and willow forest embedded in the surrounding steppe, supporting avian communities more similar to west-side riparian habitats than to adjacent sagebrush. Bald Eagle, Osprey, and Great Blue Heron concentrate along east-side salmon-bearing rivers during spawning season (source: G-18, G-20).

The salmon thread in the shrub-steppe is geographically linear (confined to river corridors) rather than spatially extensive, but the ecological importance is high — east-side salmon runs are among the most endangered in the PNW, and their loss would remove the marine nutrient subsidy from riparian forests that support locally elevated bird diversity within the steppe landscape.

## 5. Cross-Zone Community Dynamics

### 5.1 Altitudinal Migration

Several PNW bird species undergo altitudinal migration — seasonal vertical movements between elevation zones rather than latitudinal migration between PNW and distant wintering grounds. These species are the biological links between the community zones described above.

| Species | Scientific Name | Breeding Zone | Wintering Zone | Migration Mechanism |
|---------|----------------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Dusky Grouse | *Dendragapus obscurus* | ELEV-MONTANE (meadows, open forest) | ELEV-SUBALPINE (dense conifer) | Reversed altitudinal: moves upslope in fall |
| Mountain Quail | *Oreortyx pictus* | ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE (upper) | ELEV-LOWLAND, lower ELEV-MONTANE | Walks downhill in coveys (one of the few birds that migrates on foot) |
| Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch | *Leucosticte tephrocotis* | ELEV-ALPINE | ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE | Descends to lower elevations in flocks during severe winter |
| Mountain Bluebird | *Sialia currucoides* | ELEV-SUBALPINE, upper ELEV-MONTANE | ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE | Moves to open lowland habitats in winter |
| Townsend's Solitaire | *Myadestes townsendi* | ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE | ELEV-LOWLAND (juniper woodland) | Defends individual winter territories around juniper berry crops |
| American Dipper | *Cinclus mexicanus* | ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-MONTANE (streams) | ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-RIPARIAN (lower) | Moves downslope when high-elevation streams freeze |
| Clark's Nutcracker | *Nucifraga columbiana* | ELEV-SUBALPINE, ELEV-ALPINE | ELEV-MONTANE, ELEV-LOWLAND (irruptive) | Irruptive downslope movement in cone crop failure years |
| Cassin's Finch | *Haemorhous cassinii* | ELEV-SUBALPINE, upper ELEV-MONTANE | ELEV-LOWLAND, ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE | Irregular altitudinal migrant |

Altitudinal migration means that a single species may be a member of different community zones at different times of year. Conservation of altitudinal migrants requires protection of habitat across the entire elevation gradient they use — a species may breed in a protected alpine wilderness but winter in unprotected lowland habitat (source: G-25, O-02).

### 5.3 Climate-Driven Community Reorganization

Climate change is predicted to shift avian community zones upslope, but the shift will not be a simple translation of existing communities to higher elevations. Instead, community reorganization will occur as individual species respond to climate at different rates, creating novel assemblages with no historical analog. Key predictions for PNW high-elevation and east-side avian communities include (source: G-07, G-25):

1. **Alpine community contraction:** White-tailed Ptarmigan, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch, and American Pipit breeding habitat will shrink as treeline advances. On isolated volcanic peaks with limited summit area, alpine communities may be entirely eliminated.

2. **Subalpine community transition:** Montane species (Varied Thrush, Townsend's Warbler) will colonize subalpine elevations, potentially displacing subalpine specialists (Mountain Chickadee at lower margin, Clark's Nutcracker if whitebark pine fails).

3. **Montane community resilience:** The montane zone is large enough and structurally diverse enough to buffer significant climate change. However, increased fire frequency and severity may convert closed-canopy forest to early-successional habitat faster than old-growth structure can re-develop, shifting the community toward fire-associated species.

4. **Shrub-steppe community collapse (worst case):** If cheatgrass-fire cycles continue to eliminate sagebrush, the sagebrush obligate guild could lose most of its PNW habitat within 50-100 years. Sage-Grouse, Sage Thrasher, Sagebrush Sparrow, and Brewer's Sparrow would decline proportionally.

## 6. Synthesis: Elevation as Community Architect

### 6.1 Diversity Gradient

Avian species richness across the four zones covered in this document follows a clear elevational pattern:

| Zone | Breeding Species | Year-Round Residents | Primary Limiting Factor | Conservation Priority Species |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| ELEV-ALPINE | 15-20 | 3-5 | Habitat area, breeding season length | White-tailed Ptarmigan, Gray-crowned Rosy-Finch |
| ELEV-SUBALPINE | 40-55 | 15-20 | Snowpack, cone crop availability | Clark's Nutcracker, Spruce Grouse, Northern Goshawk |
| ELEV-MONTANE | 80-120 | 35-50 | Old-growth forest extent, Barred Owl | Northern Spotted Owl, Marbled Murrelet, Black-backed Woodpecker |
| ELEV-SHRUB-STEPPE | 50-70 | 15-25 | Sagebrush extent, cheatgrass invasion | Greater Sage-Grouse, Ferruginous Hawk, Burrowing Owl |

The montane zone's peak diversity reflects its combination of maximum structural complexity (vertical stratification in old-growth forest), moderate climate (longer breeding season than alpine/subalpine), and largest geographic extent. The shrub-steppe's moderate diversity despite low structural complexity reflects the horizontal habitat heterogeneity (sagebrush, grassland, juniper, riparian, cliff, and bare ground) packed into the east-side landscape.
