# Fungi Survey: Mycological Biodiversity of PNW Temperate Rainforests

> **PNW Rainforest Biodiversity Research (v1.49.22) -- Phase 605**
>
> Survey module 3 of 4. Uses shared schemas from `00-shared-schemas.md` for species entries and relationship definitions.

Beneath the visible forest -- the towering Douglas firs, the carpeted fern understory, the salmon-threaded rivers -- lies a second forest. This one is made of thread. Fungal hyphae, each thinner than a human hair, weave through every cubic centimeter of forest soil, wrapping tree roots, penetrating decaying logs, and connecting organisms that appear, above ground, to be solitary. In PNW temperate rainforests, fungi are not peripheral organisms catalogued after the trees and animals. They are the connective infrastructure -- the communication network, the nutrient highway, the memory of the forest encoded in mycelium. The phrase "wood wide web," coined to describe mycorrhizal networks, is not metaphor. It is architecture.

This document maps that architecture. It describes how ectomycorrhizal and arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi form the two major association types linking fungi to plant roots; how Common Mycorrhizal Networks enable trees to share carbon, water, and chemical defense signals across a hub-node topology centered on ancient mother trees; why old-growth forests harbor fungal diversity that young plantations cannot replicate for centuries; and which species carry ecological, cultural, or conservation significance in the region. Throughout, the emphasis is on relationships -- fungi as the tissue connecting the ecosystem's parts into a functioning whole, not a species list with descriptions appended.

## 1. Mycorrhizal Network Architecture

Mycorrhizal associations -- mutualistic relationships between fungi and plant roots -- are the dominant ecological interaction in PNW forest soils. Over 90% of terrestrial plants form mycorrhizal partnerships, but in the conifer-dominated rainforests of the Pacific Northwest, two association types structure the below-ground ecosystem in fundamentally different ways.

### 1.1 Ectomycorrhizal Fungi (EMF)

Ectomycorrhizal fungi form the dominant below-ground association in PNW coniferous forests. In an EMF partnership, fungal hyphae grow around the exterior of tree root tips, forming a dense sheath called the mantle. From the mantle, hyphae penetrate inward between the cortical cells of the root, creating an intercellular interface known as the Hartig net. Critically, EMF hyphae do not penetrate the cell walls themselves -- the exchange of nutrients occurs across intact cell membranes at the Hartig net interface, a structural distinction that separates EMF from arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi.

The ecological dominance of EMF in PNW forests is a function of the tree species that define these ecosystems. Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*), and western red cedar (*Thuja plicata*) are all EMF-dependent. Without their fungal partners, these trees cannot access soil phosphorus and nitrogen efficiently enough to sustain the biomass densities that characterize old-growth stands. In return, EMF fungi receive photosynthetic carbon from their host trees -- up to 20% of a tree's total fixed carbon may be allocated to its mycorrhizal partners (GOV-05). This is not a minor metabolic footnote. It is a fundamental reallocation of energy that shapes forest carbon budgets.

The taxonomic diversity of EMF in PNW forests is remarkable. Old-growth stands support over 200 EMF species per stand (GOV-06), drawn from genera including *Rhizopogon* (hypogeous truffles), *Russula*, *Cortinarius*, *Amanita*, *Suillus*, and *Tricholoma* (which includes the commercially significant matsutake). Each genus occupies a distinct ecological niche: *Rhizopogon* species form extensive mycelial mats that are critical food sources for small mammals; *Cortinarius* species are among the most species-rich EMF genera globally but are poorly studied taxonomically; *Amanita* species include both beneficial mycorrhizal partners and some of the most toxic mushrooms known. This diversity is not ornamental -- it represents functional redundancy and specialization that allows EMF communities to sustain forest nutrient cycling under varying conditions of drought, disturbance, and successional change.

### 1.2 Arbuscular Mycorrhizal Fungi (AMF)

Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi represent an older evolutionary lineage, with fossil evidence dating AMF associations to over 400 million years ago -- predating the evolution of roots themselves. Unlike EMF, AMF hyphae penetrate the cell walls of root cortical cells, forming highly branched intracellular structures called arbuscules. These arbuscules are the primary sites of nutrient exchange: the fungus delivers phosphorus and other soil minerals directly into the plant cell's cytoplasm, receiving carbon compounds in return. The arbuscule's branched architecture maximizes surface area for this exchange within the confined space of a single plant cell.

In PNW conifer forests, AMF are less dominant than EMF. The major canopy conifers are EMF-associated, and AMF diversity in temperate coniferous forests is lower than in tropical or grassland systems. However, AMF are critical in the understory. Herbaceous plants, ferns, and deciduous species in the forest understory rely on AMF partnerships. Red alder (*Alnus rubra*), the most common deciduous hardwood in PNW riparian zones, forms a particularly notable three-way symbiosis: AMF fungi colonize alder roots, providing phosphorus access, while nitrogen-fixing *Frankia* bacteria in root nodules convert atmospheric nitrogen to biologically available forms. This alder-AMF-Frankia triad makes red alder a key nitrogen source in young forests and disturbed riparian areas, connecting the AMF system to the broader nutrient cycling of the forest.

Despite their lower species diversity compared to EMF in temperate forests, AMF should not be treated as ecologically marginal. They are the mycorrhizal type for the majority of Earth's plant species, and in PNW understory communities, they mediate nutrient access for the herbaceous layer that provides ground cover, erosion control, and habitat structure for invertebrates and amphibians.

### 1.3 Common Mycorrhizal Networks -- The Mother Tree Model

The most consequential discovery in mycorrhizal ecology in the past three decades is that individual mycorrhizal fungi do not simply connect one tree to one fungal partner. They connect trees to each other. A single fungal mycelium can colonize the roots of multiple trees simultaneously, creating a Common Mycorrhizal Network (CMN) -- a below-ground web through which carbon, water, nitrogen, phosphorus, and chemical defense signals flow between connected trees.

The foundational evidence for CMN function came from Simard et al. (1997), who conducted field experiments demonstrating bidirectional carbon transfer between paper birch (*Betula papyrifera*) and Douglas fir via shared ectomycorrhizal networks (PR-03). Using carbon isotope labeling (C-13 and C-14), Simard showed that carbon fixed by one tree species moved through fungal hyphae and appeared in the tissues of the other species. The transfer was not random leakage -- it was directional, with net carbon flowing from the species in sun (birch, in summer) to the species in shade (fir, under birch canopy). This was the first field evidence that trees could share resources through a fungal intermediary, overturning the assumption that below-ground competition was the dominant root-level interaction.

Simard's subsequent decades of research, synthesized in *Finding the Mother Tree* (PR-05), revealed that CMNs are not egalitarian webs. They have topology. Large, old trees -- mother trees -- function as hub nodes in the network. A single large Douglas fir can be connected to 47 other trees via shared mycorrhizal fungi (PR-05). These hub trees preferentially allocate resources to their own kin: when Simard tested carbon transfer from mother trees to seedlings, related seedlings (same mother tree genotype) received more carbon than unrelated seedlings. The mother tree recognizes her offspring through chemical signaling at the root-fungal interface and biases resource flow accordingly.

The hub-node architecture of CMNs has four documented functions:

1. **Carbon redistribution:** Shaded seedlings on the forest floor, unable to photosynthesize enough carbon to sustain growth, receive carbon subsidies from sunlit adults via the network. This is not charity -- it is a kin-selected investment that increases the mother tree's inclusive fitness by improving offspring survival.

2. **Water sharing during drought:** During water stress, trees with access to deep water sources can redistribute water through the network to neighbors with shallower root systems. The fungal hyphae act as conduits, extending the effective rooting depth of connected trees.

3. **Chemical defense signaling:** When a tree is attacked by defoliating insects, it produces defense compounds (terpenes, phenolics) and also transmits chemical signals through the CMN that trigger defensive enzyme production in connected neighbors -- trees that have not yet been attacked. The network functions as an early warning system.

4. **Nitrogen and phosphorus transfer:** Nutrients move through the network from areas of high concentration to areas of low concentration, mediated by fungal hyphae that can access soil microsites inaccessible to tree roots alone.

The ecological implication is profound and direct: removing mother trees destroys the hub nodes of the network. Clearcutting does not merely remove trees. It fragments the CMN into isolated patches, eliminating carbon subsidies to seedlings, severing defense signaling pathways, and collapsing the nutrient redistribution system that sustains forest regeneration. Seedling survival rates decline measurably in clearcuts compared to partial harvests that retain mother trees (PR-05). The network is the organism. The individual tree is a node.

## 2. Fungal Diversity and Forest Age

The relationship between forest age and fungal community composition is not linear and not subtle. It is a successional trajectory that takes centuries to complete, and the endpoint -- old-growth EMF community diversity -- is effectively irreplaceable on human timescales.

### 2.1 Successional Stages and EMF Community Composition

EMF community structure changes dramatically across forest age classes. The progression is driven by changes in soil chemistry, organic layer depth, canopy structure, and the accumulating complexity of root networks.

**Young plantations (0-30 years)** are dominated by early-successional EMF species -- generalists with broad host ranges and small mycelial extents. *Thelephora terrestris* and *Cenococcum geophilum* are typical colonizers of young plantation seedling roots. These species tolerate the exposed, mineral-rich, organic-poor soils of recent clearcuts. They provide basic mycorrhizal function (phosphorus access, some drought resistance) but do not form the extensive networks characteristic of mature forests. EMF species richness in young plantations is low, typically 20-40 species per stand (GOV-06).

**Mid-rotation forests (30-80 years)** show increasing EMF diversity as the canopy closes and litter accumulation builds a deeper organic soil layer. *Rhizopogon* and *Russula* species begin to appear, indicating that soil conditions have shifted enough to support fungi with larger mycelial ranges and greater substrate specificity. However, the EMF community at this stage remains structurally simplified compared to old-growth -- the hub-node CMN architecture has not yet developed because no trees have reached the size and root extent needed to function as network hubs. Mid-rotation forests have the trees but not yet the relationships.

**Old-growth forests (200+ years)** support the full EMF community. Species richness exceeds 200 EMF species per stand (GOV-06). The critical metric is truffle diversity: hypogeous (below-ground fruiting) EMF species -- the *Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, and *Hysterangium* genera that produce truffles -- are 4 times more diverse in old-growth stands than in young plantations (GOV-06). This 4x truffle diversity ratio is the clearest quantitative indicator of old-growth fungal community integrity, because truffle-producing EMF species are the most habitat-specific and the slowest to recolonize after disturbance.

### 2.2 Why Old-Growth Matters for Fungi

The 4x truffle diversity differential is not accidental. It reflects specific structural features of old-growth forests that cannot be replicated in younger stands:

**Large-diameter nurse logs** are irreplaceable substrates. When an old-growth tree falls, its trunk -- often over a meter in diameter -- provides a decomposition substrate that sustains saprotrophic fungi for decades. The nurse log creates a moisture-retaining, nutrient-rich microhabitat where seedlings establish their first mycorrhizal partnerships. The size of the log matters: large logs maintain interior moisture through summer drought when small logs desiccate completely. Only old-growth forests produce large-diameter nurse logs at ecologically significant densities.

**Deep organic soil layers** accumulated over centuries of litter deposition provide the substrate complexity that supports diverse EMF communities. Hyphal networks in old-growth soils can extend for kilometers, connecting trees across topographic gradients. These networks are not merely long -- they are structurally complex, with multiple fungal species occupying different soil horizons and accessing different mineral sources. Centuries of uninterrupted fungal growth build this complexity; a clearcut resets it to zero.

**Structural complexity** of the old-growth canopy -- gaps created by fallen trees, standing snags, multi-layered understory -- generates microhabitat diversity for fungal fruiting bodies. Epigeous (above-ground) mushrooms require specific light, humidity, and substrate conditions to fruit. The mosaic of light gaps, shade patches, and moisture gradients in old-growth provides these conditions across a wider range of species than the uniform canopy of a managed plantation.

**Old-growth trees are the hub nodes.** The largest, oldest trees have the most extensive root systems and therefore the most extensive mycorrhizal networks. They are the hubs of the CMN. Their root networks may span a hectare or more, connecting to dozens of neighboring trees and hundreds of fungal species. No young tree can replicate this network architecture -- it takes centuries of root growth and fungal colonization to build.

### 2.3 What Is Lost in Young Forests

The consequences of replacing old-growth with young plantations extend beyond reduced species counts:

**Late-successional EMF specialists disappear.** Species in the genera *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*, and certain *Rhizopogon* species (notably *R. vinicolor*) are absent or extremely rare in plantation forests. These are not generalists that could survive in simplified habitats if given time -- they require the specific soil chemistry, moisture regimes, and root network complexity of old-growth (GOV-06). Their absence from plantations is structural, not merely successional.

**Truffle-dependent mammals lose food sources.** The northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) and California red-backed vole (*Myodes californicus*) depend on truffles as primary food sources. When truffle diversity collapses in young forests, these mammals decline -- which in turn removes primary prey species for the northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*). This is the truffle-mammal-raptor cascade (documented fully in Section 5 as CASCADE-02), a cross-module chain linking fungal diversity to raptor conservation through two intermediate trophic levels.

**Network fragmentation collapses the CMN.** Without mother trees, the hub-node architecture of the CMN collapses to isolated patches. Individual seedlings in clearcuts may form simple EMF partnerships, but they lack the network connections that provide carbon subsidies, water sharing, and defense signaling. Seedling mortality is higher, growth is slower, and the regenerating forest is functionally impoverished compared to a forest regenerating under retained mother trees (PR-05).

**Recovery timelines are measured in centuries.** EMF community composition may take 150-200 years or more to fully recover after clearcutting (GOV-06). This is not a silvicultural inconvenience -- it means that a single rotation of industrial forestry (typically 40-60 years) harvests a forest long before its fungal community has recovered from the previous harvest. Each successive rotation starts with a more impoverished EMF community. The cumulative effect is a progressive loss of below-ground biodiversity that is invisible from above ground, where the plantation appears to be a functional forest.

## 3. Ecologically Significant Species Profiles

The following profiles document fungi of particular ecological, cultural, or conservation significance in PNW temperate rainforests. Each profile incorporates the shared schema fields from `00-shared-schemas.md` -- identity fields, distribution fields, conservation fields, and fungi extension fields -- woven into narrative prose rather than presented as raw data tables. The intent is to convey each species' ecological role and relationships, not merely its taxonomic coordinates.

### 3.1 Pacific Golden Chanterelle (*Cantharellus formosus*)

The Pacific golden chanterelle is the most commercially harvested wild mushroom in the Pacific Northwest and Oregon's official state mushroom. It is an ectomycorrhizal fungus (taxonomic_group: `ectomycorrhizal_fungus`; mycorrhizal_type: `EMF`) that forms obligate partnerships with coniferous host trees, primarily Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and Sitka spruce (*Picea sitchensis*). The chanterelle's golden, funnel-shaped fruiting body emerges from forest duff in autumn, and its distribution spans all four study zones (zones_present: `olympic_peninsula`, `cascade_western_slopes`, `columbia_river_gorge`, `oregon_coast_range`).

Chanterelles are not old-growth dependent (old_growth_dependent: `false`) -- they fruit in second-growth forests as well as old-growth stands. However, fruiting body size and abundance are significantly greater in mature and old-growth forests, where deeper organic soil layers and more extensive mycorrhizal networks support larger mycelial colonies. The species is commercially harvested (commercially_harvested: `true`), supporting a multi-million dollar annual harvest industry in Oregon and Washington. Commercial picking regulations vary by land management unit: some National Forest districts require permits, others prohibit commercial harvest entirely, and enforcement is inconsistent.

The chanterelle's ecological role extends beyond its mycorrhizal partnership. Its fruiting bodies are a food source for California red-backed voles, banana slugs (*Ariolimax columbianus*), and black-tailed deer (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*), connecting the chanterelle to the fauna survey module through trophic relationships. As an EMF partner, it provides its host conifers with enhanced phosphorus and water uptake in exchange for photosynthetic carbon (GOV-05).

Conservation status: federal_status: `none`; state_status_wa: `none`; state_status_or: `none`; trend: `stable` but subject to localized overharvesting pressure. The primary conservation concern is not species survival but sustainable harvest management -- ensuring that commercial picking does not degrade EMF network function by removing fruiting bodies before spore dispersal. Primary source: GOV-05; data_quality: `verified_agency`.

### 3.2 Agarikon (*Laricifomes officinalis*)

Agarikon is the most critically imperiled macrofungus in PNW temperate rainforests and one of the clearest biological indicators of old-growth forest conditions. It is a wood-decay fungus with dual classification: primarily saprotrophic, decomposing the heartwood of standing old-growth conifers, but also exhibiting parasitic behavior on living trees (taxonomic_group: `saprotrophic_fungus`; mycorrhizal_type: `saprotrophic`/`parasitic` -- dual classification acknowledged). Its perennial fruiting body -- a large, elongated, chalky-white conk that can grow for decades -- hangs from the trunks of old-growth Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western larch (*Larix occidentalis*), and noble fir (*Abies procera*).

Agarikon is exclusively found on old-growth conifers, typically trees 200 years old or older (old_growth_dependent: `true`). This is the key conservation claim for this species: it cannot persist in managed forests. Its fruiting body requires the thick, deeply furrowed bark and large trunk diameter of old-growth trees for attachment and moisture regulation. Young plantation trees lack both the bark structure and the heartwood decay conditions that agarikon requires.

The species' geographic range has contracted severely. Agarikon was once widespread across northern hemisphere temperate forests -- from Scandinavia through Siberia to the Pacific coast of North America. It has been extirpated from most of Europe due to old-growth logging and is now effectively restricted to remnant old-growth stands in the PNW (zones_present: `olympic_peninsula`, `cascade_western_slopes`, `oregon_coast_range` -- increasingly rare in all three). It is too rare to be commercially harvested (commercially_harvested: `false`).

Conservation status: federal_status: `species_of_concern` (not formally listed under the Endangered Species Act but recognized as imperiled by multiple agencies); state_status_wa: `candidate`; state_status_or: `sensitive`; trend: `declining`. The primary threat is old-growth habitat loss from timber harvest. No captive propagation or reintroduction program exists. Where agarikon survives, old-growth forest conditions persist -- making it an indicator species whose presence confirms habitat integrity and whose absence signals irreversible structural simplification.

Historical and cultural context: Indigenous peoples of the PNW used agarikon medicinally for centuries, as documented in ethnobotanical records. More recently, mycologist Paul Stamets has identified antiviral compounds in agarikon extracts with potential pharmaceutical applications. This medicinal research is noted as expert knowledge and ongoing investigation, not peer-reviewed ecological data. Primary source: GOV-06; data_quality: `verified_agency`.

### 3.3 American Matsutake (*Tricholoma murrillianum*)

The American matsutake is an ectomycorrhizal fungus (taxonomic_group: `ectomycorrhizal_fungus`; mycorrhizal_type: `EMF`) that forms partnerships with Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*), western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*), and lodgepole pine (*Pinus contorta*). Its distinctive spicy-aromatic scent and dense, firm flesh make it one of the most culturally valued wild mushrooms in the world -- in Japan, the closely related *Tricholoma matsutake* is a prized culinary and ceremonial ingredient, and American matsutake is commercially harvested primarily for export to Japanese markets (commercially_harvested: `true`).

Matsutake fruit in well-drained, mineral soils under conifer canopy, often on slopes with sparse understory. They are present in the Cascade western slopes and Oregon Coast Range (zones_present: `cascade_western_slopes`, `oregon_coast_range`). Unlike agarikon, matsutake is not old-growth dependent (old_growth_dependent: `false`) -- it fruits in forests of various ages, provided soil drainage and mineral composition are suitable. However, matsutake colonies form persistent mycelial mats in the soil called "shiros" (Japanese for "white") that can persist for decades, slowly expanding outward at a rate of a few centimeters per year. A productive shiro represents decades of fungal growth and is destroyed by soil disturbance from road building or site preparation for replanting.

Conservation status: federal_status: `none`; state_status_wa: `none`; state_status_or: `none`; trend: `stable`. The primary management concern is sustainable harvest: commercial pickers who dig up immature buttons (before the cap opens and spores disperse) reduce reproductive output. Some National Forest districts have implemented minimum size regulations. Primary source: GOV-05; data_quality: `verified_agency`.

The ecological role of matsutake is straightforward EMF mutualism: nutrient exchange with host trees via the Hartig net, with the added structural contribution of shiro mats that stabilize soil and create localized zones of enhanced nutrient cycling.

### 3.4 PNW Truffle Complex (*Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium* spp.)

The PNW truffle complex is profiled as a functional group rather than individual species, consistent with this document's emphasis on ecological relationships over taxonomic enumeration. Truffles -- hypogeous (below-ground fruiting) ectomycorrhizal fungi -- are among the most ecologically consequential organisms in PNW forests, and their significance lies not in any single species but in the collective role they play as nutrient brokers and food web keystones.

All PNW truffles profiled here are ectomycorrhizal (taxonomic_group: `ectomycorrhizal_fungus`; mycorrhizal_type: `EMF`), forming partnerships primarily with Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) and western hemlock (*Tsuga heterophylla*). Old-growth dependency varies by genus and species: *Rhizopogon vinicolor* is strongly old-growth associated, its mycelial mats most abundant and extensive in forests exceeding 200 years (GOV-06); generalist *Rhizopogon* species fruit in younger forests as well. *Gautieria* and *Hysterangium* species are predominantly late-successional specialists, absent or extremely rare in plantation forests (old_growth_dependent: varies by species, with the complex trending old-growth associated as a functional group).

The Oregon truffle industry is growing (commercially_harvested: `true`), with commercial harvest of the Oregon black truffle (*Leucangium carthusianum*), Oregon white truffle (*Tuber oregonense*), and Oregon winter white truffle (*Tuber gibbosum*). These culinary species overlap partially with the ecological truffle complex but are not the same set -- the ecologically critical truffles (*Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*) are not culinary targets but are the primary food source for mycophagous mammals.

The truffle complex's ecological significance centers on a single trophic cascade: truffles are the primary food source for the northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) and the California red-backed vole (*Myodes californicus*). These small mammals locate truffles by scent, excavate them, and in the process disperse truffle spores through their fecal pellets -- a mutualistic dispersal mechanism analogous to fruit-eating birds dispersing plant seeds. Flying squirrels and red-backed voles are, in turn, the primary prey of the northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*). This truffle-mammal-raptor cascade (CASCADE-02, Section 5) is why old-growth logging threatens spotted owl populations through an indirect pathway: reduced truffle diversity leads to reduced mammal prey density, which reduces spotted owl reproductive success.

The 4x truffle diversity metric documented in Section 2 applies here: truffle species richness is the clearest quantitative indicator of old-growth fungal community integrity (GOV-06). When truffle diversity is high, the mammal prey base is robust, and the raptor population can sustain itself. When truffle diversity collapses -- as it does in young plantations -- the entire cascade degrades.

Conservation status: federal_status: `none` (as a complex; individual species are not listed); trend: `declining` in managed forest landscapes, `stable` in protected old-growth. Primary source: GOV-06; data_quality: `primary_data`.

### 3.5 Lung Lichen (*Lobaria pulmonaria*)

Lung lichen is a lichenized fungus -- a composite organism in which a fungal partner (the mycobiont) forms the structural body of the lichen and houses photosynthetic partners within its tissues (taxonomic_group: `lichenized_fungus`; mycorrhizal_type: `lichenized`). *Lobaria pulmonaria* is a large, foliose (leaf-like) lichen that grows on the bark and branches of old-growth trees, particularly bigleaf maple (*Acer macrophyllum*) and old conifers, in moist PNW forests. Its distinctive lung-like, ridged surface gives it both its common name and its historical use in European folk medicine (doctrine of signatures).

What makes *Lobaria* ecologically significant is its nitrogen-fixing capacity. Unlike most lichens, which contain only a green algal photobiont, *Lobaria pulmonaria* also harbors cyanobacterial photobionts (internal cephalodia containing *Nostoc* cells) that fix atmospheric nitrogen (N2) into biologically available ammonia. In old-growth canopy ecosystems where mineral nitrogen deposition is low, *Lobaria* and related cyanolichens can be the dominant source of new nitrogen input to the forest canopy. This nitrogen enters the ecosystem when lichen fragments fall to the forest floor or are washed off branches by rain, enriching the soil beneath old trees.

*Lobaria* is old-growth dependent (old_growth_dependent: `true`). It requires the stable bark substrate of large, old trees -- the deeply furrowed bark of old-growth conifers and the rough bark of mature bigleaf maples provide the attachment surface and moisture-retaining microtopography that *Lobaria* needs. Young plantation trees with smooth bark cannot support *Lobaria* colonies. The species is also sensitive to air pollution, making it an indicator of both old-growth forest structure and air quality.

Distribution spans three of the four study zones (zones_present: `olympic_peninsula`, `cascade_western_slopes`, `oregon_coast_range`), with greatest abundance in the wettest old-growth stands where canopy humidity is consistently high. It is not commercially harvested (commercially_harvested: `false`).

Conservation status: federal_status: `none`; state_status_wa: `sensitive`; state_status_or: `sensitive`; trend: `declining` due to old-growth habitat loss and air quality degradation. As an indicator species, *Lobaria*'s presence confirms that old-growth canopy conditions -- large trees, stable bark, high humidity, clean air -- persist. Its absence from a forest stand suggests structural simplification even when the canopy appears superficially intact. Primary source: GOV-05; data_quality: `verified_agency`.

## 5. Cascade Contributions

Cascade relationships trace multi-step ecological chains across survey module boundaries. They are the structural evidence for the "network of relationships" model -- each step in a cascade is documented individually, but the cascade's ecological significance lies in the chain as a whole. Disrupting any single step degrades the entire pathway.

### 5.1 CASCADE-01 Extension: Fungi in the Salmon-Forest Nutrient Cascade

CASCADE-01 (Salmon-Forest Nutrient Cascade), defined in `00-shared-schemas.md`, traces marine-derived nitrogen from ocean through salmon, bear predation, soil decomposition, and into the forest via mycorrhizal networks. Steps 4 and 5 of that cascade are fungi-mediated, and this section expands on the fungal mechanism.

**Step 4 -- Tree roots access marine-derived nutrients via EMF:**
When salmon carcass nitrogen (isotopically distinguishable as marine-origin N-15) enters riparian soil through decomposition or bear scat, it becomes available to EMF hyphae. Ectomycorrhizal fungi -- particularly *Rhizopogon* species with extensive mycelial mats -- absorb dissolved nitrogen from soil microsites that tree roots cannot access directly. The nitrogen moves from soil to fungal hyphae to the Hartig net interface, where it is transferred to the host tree's root cortical cells. Studies tracing N-15 isotopes have detected marine-derived nitrogen in tree tissues kilometers from salmon streams (PR-02), a distance that only makes sense if the nitrogen was redistributed through hyphal networks extending far beyond the riparian zone.

**Step 5 -- CMN redistributes nutrients across the forest:**
Once marine-derived nitrogen enters a mother tree's tissues via EMF, the Common Mycorrhizal Network can redistribute it. A mother tree connected to 47 other trees via shared fungal networks (PR-05) can transfer nitrogen to kin seedlings and neighbors that have no direct access to riparian soil. This is how salmon-derived nutrients reach trees on ridgelines and upper slopes -- not through groundwater transport, but through the fungal network. The cascade thus extends from ocean to stream to bear to soil to fungus to tree to network to distant seedling. Each step is documented; together, they demonstrate that salmon productivity and inland forest growth are coupled through fungal infrastructure (PR-02, PR-03).

### 5.2 CASCADE-02: Truffle-Mammal-Raptor Cascade

This cascade is defined here as a new multi-step chain demonstrating why old-growth logging threatens the northern spotted owl through an indirect pathway mediated by fungal diversity.

| cascade_id | CASCADE-02 |
|---|---|
| cascade_name | Truffle-Mammal-Raptor Cascade |
| modules_spanned | fungi, fauna |
| source | GOV-06 |

| Step | Species A | Relationship Type | Species B | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | Old-growth EMF community (*Rhizopogon*, *Gautieria*, *Hysterangium*) | symbiotic (mycorrhizal_network) | Old-growth conifers (Douglas fir, western hemlock) | EMF fungi require old-growth root networks and deep organic soils to maintain full species diversity. Truffle diversity is 4x higher in old-growth than young plantations (GOV-06). |
| 2 | Truffle fruiting bodies | predator_prey (mycophagy) | Northern flying squirrel (*Glaucomys sabrinus*) | Flying squirrels depend on truffles for 80-90% of diet. They locate truffles by scent, excavate, and consume. Spore dispersal via fecal pellets completes mutualistic loop. |
| 3 | Truffle fruiting bodies | predator_prey (mycophagy) | California red-backed vole (*Myodes californicus*) | Red-backed voles are the second major mycophagous mammal in PNW forests. Truffle dependency is obligate in old-growth habitats. |
| 4 | Northern flying squirrel + red-backed vole | predator_prey (predation) | Northern spotted owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) | Flying squirrels and red-backed voles are primary prey species for spotted owls in PNW old-growth. Owl reproductive success correlates with small mammal prey density. |

**Cascade logic:** When old-growth is clearcut, Step 1 breaks -- EMF truffle diversity collapses (4x reduction; GOV-06). With fewer truffle species fruiting in lower abundance, Step 2 and Step 3 degrade -- flying squirrel and vole populations decline due to food scarcity. With reduced prey density, Step 4 degrades -- spotted owl pairs cannot provision chicks adequately, and reproductive success declines. The spotted owl's endangered status is conventionally attributed to habitat loss (nesting trees), but CASCADE-02 demonstrates that the threat pathway runs equally through below-ground fungal diversity. Protecting spotted owl habitat means protecting truffle habitat, which means protecting old-growth fungal communities. The owl crisis is, in part, a fungi crisis.

### 5.3 Cross-Module Connections for Phase 607

This document establishes three primary cross-module pathways that the Phase 607 network synthesis will integrate:

**Fungi to Flora (01-flora.md):**
- Host tree dependencies: all five profiled species/groups associate with Douglas fir, western hemlock, or other major PNW conifers documented in the flora survey
- Canopy nitrogen: *Lobaria pulmonaria* nitrogen fixation is a fungi-mediated nutrient input to the forest canopy ecosystem
- Nurse log substrates: saprotrophic fungi decompose fallen old-growth trees into the nurse logs that serve as seedling establishment sites -- a fungal service that regenerates the flora

**Fungi to Fauna (02-fauna.md):**
- Truffle-mammal-raptor cascade (CASCADE-02): truffle diversity drives flying squirrel and vole populations, which drive spotted owl reproductive success
- Chanterelle as food source: fruiting bodies consumed by red-backed voles, banana slugs, and deer
- Truffle spore dispersal: mycophagous mammals are the primary dispersal agents for hypogeous EMF species, creating a mutualistic dependency loop

**Fungi to Aquatic (04-aquatic.md):**
- Salmon-forest nutrient cascade (CASCADE-01, Steps 4-5): marine-derived nitrogen from salmon enters the forest nutrient system via EMF fungi and is redistributed through CMNs
- The connection is bidirectional in ecological terms: salmon productivity feeds forest fungi, and healthy mycorrhizal forests produce the shade, large woody debris, and stable streambanks that salmon need for spawning habitat
