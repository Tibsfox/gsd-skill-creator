# The Scientific Method

*From Bacon's idols to Bayesian design -- four centuries of learning how to learn*

---

## 1. Francis Bacon's Novum Organum

Before Francis Bacon, natural philosophy was a spectator sport. The educated gentleman observed nature, consulted Aristotle, and reasoned his way to truth from first principles. The syllogism was king: all heavy objects fall; this stone is heavy; therefore this stone falls. The logic was impeccable. The knowledge it produced was stagnant. For two thousand years, Western philosophy operated on the assumption that truth could be derived from categories and axioms already known, and that the purpose of inquiry was to arrange existing knowledge more elegantly -- not to discover anything genuinely new.

Bacon saw this for what it was: a machine for confirming what you already believed.

His **Novum Organum** ("New Instrument"), published in 1620, was a direct challenge to Aristotle's **Organon** -- the original logical toolkit. Where Aristotle's deduction moved from general principles to specific conclusions, Bacon proposed **induction**: moving from specific observations to general principles. Do not begin with a theory. Begin with observations. Gather many of them. Tabulate them. Look for patterns. Construct cautious generalizations. Test those generalizations against further observations. Build knowledge from the ground up, not the top down.

But Bacon understood that the human mind is not a blank slate waiting to receive data. It is an active, distorting instrument, and before you can use it for reliable inquiry you must understand its systematic biases. He catalogued these as the **four idols** -- persistent illusions that corrupt reasoning:

**The Idols of the Tribe** (*Idola Tribus*) are the biases inherent in human nature itself. We see patterns where none exist, give disproportionate weight to confirming evidence, and overgeneralize from limited experience. These are not personal failings; they are features of the hardware. Four centuries later, cognitive science would rediscover each under names like **confirmation bias** and **base rate neglect** -- but Bacon got there first.

**The Idols of the Cave** (*Idola Specus*) are the biases peculiar to the individual. Your education, your temperament, your experiences create a private cave of assumptions. A physician sees everything through the lens of disease; a mathematician through the lens of quantity. A person trained in one tradition will unconsciously reject evidence that challenges it -- not from dishonesty but from genuine inability to perceive it. Bacon anticipates Kuhn's "paradigm blindness" by 340 years.

**The Idols of the Marketplace** (*Idola Fori*) are the distortions created by language. We use terms like "cause" and "force" as if they had fixed meanings, but they smuggle in assumptions that shape conclusions before reasoning begins. In modern terms, Bacon identified the problem of **operationalization**: the gap between what a word means in common usage and what it means as a measurable quantity.

**The Idols of the Theater** (*Idola Theatri*) are the dogmas of received philosophical systems -- Aristotelianism, Scholasticism, various schools of natural philosophy. Elaborate intellectual performances, internally consistent and aesthetically satisfying, but staged rather than discovered. The danger is not that they are entirely wrong but that they present themselves as complete, discouraging further investigation. Why look through a telescope when Aristotle has already told you what you will see?

Bacon's contribution was not a specific technique but a **stance**: suspicion of the unaided intellect, insistence on systematic observation, and the conviction that knowledge must be built rather than inherited. He did not do much experimental science himself -- he was a lawyer and statesman. But he articulated, for the first time, the idea that the pursuit of knowledge requires a *method* with safeguards against the knower's own limitations. Every subsequent development in scientific methodology is a refinement of this Baconian program: a better technique for protecting inquiry from the idols.

## 2. Galileo and the Birth of Experiment

If Bacon supplied the philosophy, Galileo Galilei supplied the practice.

Galileo's achievement was not merely that he observed nature -- people had been observing nature for millennia. It was that he **measured** it, and that he designed situations specifically to make measurement informative. This is the invention of the experiment in the modern sense: a deliberate intervention in nature, structured to answer a specific question, with quantitative results that can be compared against a prediction.

The **inclined plane** experiments are the canonical example. Galileo wanted to understand free fall, but objects in free fall move too quickly to time accurately with early-1600s instruments. So he slowed the process down. He rolled polished bronze balls down an inclined wooden channel, smoothed with parchment, and timed their descent using a water clock -- collecting water during each run and weighing it to measure elapsed time. The key move was not the observation but the **design**. He constructed a controlled situation that isolated the variable he cared about (the relationship between distance and time-squared) from confounding variables (air resistance, terminal velocity, the difficulty of timing short intervals). He made the unmeasurable measurable -- not by building better clocks but by redesigning the question.

The **telescope** was Galileo's other great instrument of empirical disruption. He did not invent it -- that credit belongs to Hans Lippershey and others in the Netherlands -- but he was the first to point it at the sky with scientific intent. The moons of Jupiter, the phases of Venus, the mountains of the Moon, the stars of the Milky Way. The phases of Venus were particularly devastating to the Ptolemaic system: they were consistent with a heliocentric model and flatly impossible under Ptolemy. The data did not require interpretation or argument. It required only looking.

This is the enduring lesson: **make measurable what is not measurable**. The question "how fast does an object fall?" becomes "how far does a ball roll in one second on a 10-degree incline?" The question "does the Earth orbit the Sun?" becomes "what shape are the phases of Venus?" Science advances not only by collecting new data but by finding new ways to translate the intractable into the tractable.

## 3. The Hypothetico-Deductive Method

The standard textbook model of scientific method crystallized over the 19th and 20th centuries into the **hypothetico-deductive** (H-D) framework. It is taught in every introductory science course and looks roughly like this:

1. **Observe** a phenomenon.
2. **Formulate a hypothesis** to explain it.
3. **Derive a testable prediction** from the hypothesis.
4. **Design and conduct an experiment** to test the prediction.
5. **Analyze the results.**
6. **Accept, reject, or revise** the hypothesis.
7. **Repeat.**

This framework captures something real: the interplay between creative conjecture and disciplined testing. Karl Popper formalized the logic in the 1930s with his criterion of **falsifiability**: a statement is scientific if and only if it is possible to specify an observation that would prove it wrong. "All swans are white" is scientific because one black swan refutes it. The asymmetry is the heart of Popperian epistemology: you can never prove a universal law true, but you can prove one false.

But the textbook version is an idealization. Real science rarely proceeds in tidy steps. The "reject" step almost never happens cleanly: when a prediction fails, scientists suspect the experimental setup, the auxiliary assumptions, or the instrumentation before abandoning the core hypothesis. Pierre Duhem pointed out as early as 1906 that any test of a hypothesis simultaneously tests every background assumption that generated the prediction. When the prediction fails, logic alone cannot tell you which assumption is at fault.

Imre Lakatos refined Popper into the concept of **research programmes** -- a "hard core" of central commitments surrounded by a "protective belt" of auxiliary hypotheses. A programme degenerates when belt adjustments become increasingly *ad hoc*; it progresses when adjustments lead to novel, confirmed predictions.

The H-D method remains the backbone of scientific reasoning. Its power lies in its clarity as prescription: state your hypothesis clearly enough that it could be wrong, then try to make it wrong.

## 4. Experimental Design

If the hypothetico-deductive method is the logic of science, experimental design is its engineering. The question shifts from "what should I test?" to "how do I test it in a way that actually answers the question?"

**Controls** are the foundation. An experiment without a control is an anecdote. The **negative control** receives no treatment and establishes the baseline: how does the system behave when you do nothing? The **positive control** receives a treatment known to work and verifies that the experimental system is capable of detecting an effect. If your positive control fails, your experiment is broken. The **vehicle control** (or placebo) receives the delivery mechanism without the active ingredient -- the saline injection, the sugar pill, the empty vector -- and distinguishes the effect of the treatment from the effect of being treated.

**Randomization** was elevated to a formal principle by **Ronald Fisher** at the Rothamsted Experimental Station in the 1920s. Studying which fertilizer produces the best wheat yield, Fisher confronted the problem that soil is not uniform -- plots near drainage are wetter, hillside plots get more sun. If you systematically assign treatments to locations, you cannot distinguish fertilizer effects from terrain effects.

Fisher's solution: **assign treatments at random**. Let the law of large numbers ensure that confounding factors distribute roughly equally across groups. He proved mathematically that randomization was the *only* reliable way to attribute observed differences to the treatment. He developed **analysis of variance** (ANOVA) to quantify the result, and his 1935 *The Design of Experiments* became the bible of experimental methodology.

**Blinding** addresses a different class of confound: the influence of expectation on measurement. In a **single-blind** experiment, the subjects do not know which treatment they are receiving, eliminating the placebo effect. In a **double-blind** experiment, neither the subjects nor the experimenters measuring outcomes know the assignment, eliminating experimenter bias in assessment. **Triple-blind** designs extend the concealment to the statisticians analyzing the data. Each layer of blinding removes a pathway through which human expectation can contaminate the result.

**Sample size and power analysis** determine whether the experiment can detect the effect you're looking for. Statistical **power** -- the probability of detecting a real effect of a given size -- depends on the sample size, the effect size, and the significance threshold. Calculating required sample size before the experiment begins is one of the most important and most frequently neglected steps in design. An underpowered study is not merely inconclusive; it is wasteful, consuming resources without the ability to answer its own question.

## 5. Reproducibility and Replication

Science claims to produce knowledge that is independent of the knower. If only one person can obtain a result, under circumstances that cannot be specified precisely enough for anyone else to recreate, the result is an anecdote, not a finding. **Reproducibility** and **replicability** are the mechanisms that enforce this independence -- but the two terms mean different things, and the distinction matters.

**Reproducibility** means that given the same data and the same analysis procedures, a different analyst obtains the same results. This is the minimum bar -- verifying that results are not artifacts of software bugs or undocumented analysis steps. It sounds straightforward (share the data, share the code), but in practice datasets are proprietary, code depends on extinct library versions, and pipelines involve manual steps nobody documented.

**Replicability** is the harder test: given new data collected by different researchers using the same method, do you get the same result? A replicable finding is not an artifact of one dataset or one laboratory. It is a feature of the world.

The **replication crisis** revealed that much published research fails this test. John Ioannidis's 2005 paper **"Why Most Published Research Findings Are False"** constructed a probabilistic model showing that under plausible assumptions about base rates, statistical power, and researcher bias, the majority of published significant findings are false positives. The argument was mathematical, but it predicted exactly what subsequent replication efforts would find.

The **Open Science Collaboration** provided empirical confirmation in 2015: 270 researchers attempted to replicate 100 psychology studies from three top journals. Only **39 of 100** replications produced significant effects in the same direction. Even successful replications showed effect sizes typically half the original magnitude. This was not a finding about psychology specifically; it was a finding about publishing incentives. Journals prefer novel, significant results. Null results go unpublished. The entire system selects for publishable results regardless of whether they are true.

The crisis has since been documented across fields: cancer biology, economics (Camerer et al. 2016 replicated only 11 of 18 studies), and pre-clinical medicine (Begley and Ellis 2012 found that Amgen could reproduce only 6 of 53 "landmark" cancer studies). The problem is not that scientists are dishonest. The problem is that the incentive structure rewards a very specific kind of error.

## 6. Pre-registration

If the replication crisis is the diagnosis, **pre-registration** is the strongest available treatment.

The core problem is **researcher degrees of freedom** -- the many small, undocumented choices made during data analysis that can turn a null result into a significant one. Which outliers to exclude. Which covariates to control for. Which subgroups to analyze. Whether to use a one-tailed or two-tailed test. Whether to log-transform the dependent variable. Whether to stop collecting data now (when p = 0.04) or continue (when p might drift above 0.05). None of these choices is inherently wrong. But when they are made *after* seeing the data, they become opportunities for **p-hacking** -- consciously or unconsciously adjusting the analysis until something reaches the p < 0.05 threshold.

A related problem is **HARKing**: Hypothesizing After the Results are Known. A researcher tests twenty hypotheses, finds one significant result, and writes the paper as if that one hypothesis was the only one they ever intended to test. The result looks like a clean confirmation of a specific prediction. In reality, it is an exploratory finding dressed up in confirmatory clothing. With twenty tests at alpha = 0.05, you expect one false positive even when there is no real effect.

Pre-registration attacks both problems by requiring researchers to **commit to their hypotheses, methods, and analysis plans before collecting data**, registered in a public, timestamped repository (the Open Science Framework, ClinicalTrials.gov, AsPredicted.org). Deviations are permitted but must be reported and justified as exploratory, not confirmatory.

The **Registered Reports** format goes further: the journal reviews and accepts the study *before data collection*. If the plan is sound, the journal commits to publishing regardless of whether results are positive, negative, or null. This eliminates publication bias at the editorial level.

The evidence suggests it works. A 2019 analysis found that Registered Reports had a positive-result rate of 44% compared to 96% for traditional papers in the same journals. The traditional pipeline massively inflates apparent positive findings. The 96% figure is the anomaly, not the 44%.

## 7. Observational vs. Experimental

Not all science can experiment. You cannot randomly assign people to smoke for thirty years to study lung cancer. You cannot rerun the Big Bang with different initial conditions. You cannot randomly assign countries to different economic systems. Entire fields -- astronomy, epidemiology, paleontology, much of ecology, most of history -- must reason about causation from observation alone. This is harder than experimenting, but it is not impossible. It simply requires different tools and greater caution.

**Natural experiments** occur when nature or circumstance produces something approximating random assignment. John Snow's investigation of the 1854 Broad Street cholera outbreak is the founding example of modern epidemiology. Two water companies served overlapping neighborhoods in London, drawing from different points on the Thames -- one upstream of the city's sewage outflows, one downstream. Residents did not choose their water company; the assignment was essentially random with respect to individual health. Snow compared cholera rates between the two customer bases and demonstrated that contaminated water, not "miasma," was the transmission vector. The logic was the logic of a randomized controlled trial, but the randomization was supplied by history rather than by the investigator.

**Quasi-experimental designs** formalize this intuition. **Regression discontinuity** exploits arbitrary cutoffs (students scoring 70 get a scholarship; those at 69 do not -- if outcomes diverge at the cutoff, the effect is plausibly causal). **Difference-in-differences** compares changes in outcomes between treated and control groups before and after intervention. **Instrumental variables** exploit exogenous variation to isolate causal effects from confounded data.

**Sir Austin Bradford Hill** codified the logic of observational causal inference in 1965 with his nine criteria: strength of association, consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence, experimental evidence, and analogy. These are not rules -- Hill himself insisted they were "viewpoints" -- but they provide a structured framework for evaluating whether an observed association is likely causal. They were developed in the smoking-lung cancer debate, where randomized experiments were impossible but accumulated observational evidence was overwhelming.

The critical discipline is **not claiming more than the design supports**. An observational study can establish association and temporal sequence. It cannot, by itself, establish causation with the certainty of a well-designed experiment. This is not a weakness; it is an honest accounting of limits.

## 8. The Role of Theory

The naive empiricist model -- observe, then theorize -- fails on inspection. There is no such thing as a theory-free observation.

**Norwood Russell Hanson** articulated this most clearly in *Patterns of Discovery* (1958) with the thesis that **"all observation is theory-laden."** When Tycho Brahe and Johannes Kepler watched the sunrise, they saw the same photons but different events. Tycho saw the Sun rising above a stationary Earth. Kepler saw the Earth's horizon dropping below a stationary Sun. The sensory input was identical. The observation -- the meaningful, reportable content of what was seen -- was determined by the theoretical framework each man brought to the experience.

This is not a claim that reality is subjective. It is a claim about the relationship between perception and conceptual structure. You cannot observe without categorizing, and your categories are supplied by your training, your language, and your theoretical commitments. A pathologist and a first-year medical student looking at the same tissue slide do not "see" the same thing. The pathologist sees a ductal carcinoma in situ. The student sees pink and purple shapes. The difference is not in their eyes; it is in their minds.

The **underdetermination of theory by evidence** is the logical complement. For any finite body of evidence, there are infinitely many theories consistent with it. The theory is chosen on grounds that include simplicity, fertility (the ability to generate new predictions), and coherence with other well-established theories. These criteria are not arbitrary -- rooted in the mathematics of model selection and overfitting -- but they are not derivable from the data alone. This is why **data alone is never enough**. A dataset without a theoretical framework is a collection of numbers. Theory guides observation, and observation constrains theory, in a cycle that has no fixed starting point. Otto Neurath's metaphor: we are like sailors who must rebuild their ship on the open sea, never able to start from scratch in dry dock.

This does not lead to relativism. Some theories predict better than others. Some have survived more and harsher tests. General relativity may not be the final word on gravity, but GPS satellites that depend on its predictions deliver positioning accurate to meters -- the theory is tested, at scale, billions of times per day.

## 9. Modern Synthesis

The 21st century has seen a transformation in how experiments are designed, analyzed, and interpreted, driven by three converging developments: the **Bayesian turn** in statistics, the rise of **adaptive experimental designs**, and the integration of **machine learning** into the scientific process.

**Bayesian statistics** represents a fundamentally different philosophy of inference from the frequentist framework that dominated the 20th century. Where frequentist inference asks "how often would I get this result if the null hypothesis were true?" (the p-value), Bayesian inference asks "given the data I observed, what is the probability that each hypothesis is true?" Bayes' theorem provides the machinery: the posterior probability of a hypothesis is proportional to the prior probability (what you believed before the experiment) times the likelihood (how well the hypothesis predicts the observed data). The prior is often criticized as "subjective," but in practice it allows the formal incorporation of existing knowledge and domain expertise -- information that frequentist methods must officially ignore but that every scientist informally considers.

**Sequential analysis** makes data collection itself adaptive. You monitor data as it accumulates and can stop the experiment early if the evidence crosses a predetermined threshold. This is not the same as p-hacking through "optional stopping," because sequential methods adjust evidence thresholds to account for multiple looks. The **Bayes factor** -- the ratio of data likelihoods under two competing hypotheses -- provides a natural stopping criterion: when the evidence is sufficiently overwhelming, collecting more data is wasteful.

**Adaptive trial designs** extend this principle to experimental structure. Interim analyses can trigger pre-specified modifications: dropping ineffective treatment arms, adjusting dosages, re-allocating patients to the most promising treatments. The COVID-19 RECOVERY trial demonstrated the power of this approach: its adaptive platform design identified dexamethasone as the first treatment to reduce COVID mortality in just three months -- a timeline impossible under traditional fixed-design methodology.

**Machine learning** introduces a different kind of tool. Where traditional methods test *hypotheses*, machine learning detects *patterns* -- often patterns too complex for human researchers to specify in advance. But it also introduces the **interpretability problem**: a neural network that predicts drug toxicity with 95% accuracy may be entirely unable to explain *why* a compound is toxic. The most promising frontier is integration: using machine learning to identify patterns and generate hypotheses, then using traditional experimental methods to test them and build mechanistic understanding.

The modern synthesis is not a replacement for classical methods but an extension. Fisher's randomization still matters. Pre-registration still matters. The Bayesian framework and adaptive designs provide more flexible tools for the same fundamental task: structuring inquiry to protect against the idols that Bacon identified four centuries ago.

## 10. Our Connection

GSD's phase-based workflow is, at its core, a scientific method applied to software construction. The parallel is not metaphorical. It is structural.

**PLAN.md is the hypothesis** -- a falsifiable claim that implementing these changes in this order will produce a system that behaves in a specified way. **execute-phase is the experiment** -- controlled manipulation of the codebase according to the plan, Galileo's inclined plane applied to software. **verify-work is the observation** -- tests run, assertions pass or fail, the system behaves as predicted or it does not. The verify step does not ask "do you feel good about the code?" It asks "does the code do what the plan said it would do?" The answer is empirical. **The next phase is the revision** -- when verification reveals discrepancies, the plan is adjusted, the cycle repeats. This is Lakatos's research programme in miniature: adjust the protective belt (implementation details) while preserving the hard core (architectural commitments).

**Nyquist verification is sampling theory applied to software correctness.** The Nyquist-Shannon theorem states that to reconstruct a signal, you must sample at twice the highest frequency component. In GSD's framework, your tests must sample the behavior space at sufficient density to reconstruct actual system behavior with confidence. Under-sampling (too few tests, testing only the happy path) produces aliasing -- an illusion of correctness that misrepresents the true signal.

Software engineering has its own idols. The idol of the demo (it works on my machine, therefore it works). The idol of coverage (the tests pass, therefore the code is correct). The idol of velocity (we shipped quickly, therefore we shipped well). GSD's methodology applies the same centuries-old Baconian insight to a new domain: reliable knowledge about whether your software works requires a method that is systematically skeptical of the developer's confidence, demands empirical evidence at every phase boundary, and treats failed verification not as an embarrassment but as the most informative possible outcome.

Bacon would recognize the pattern. Galileo would approve of the measurements. Fisher would check the sample sizes. And the work continues.
