# Cameras in Space — A Deep History of Space Photography

**Foundation text:** GSD Research Mission Package, 31 pages (PDF/LaTeX/HTML). Full pipeline: Vision → Research → Mission.
**Project code:** CIS
**Mission start:** 2026-04-12, Session 021.
**Research pattern:** Six enrichment modules spanning 1961-2026, each covering one facet of the camera-crew-curriculum triangle that defines space photography.

## The foundation

Every photograph from space is a twice-impossible act. First, it required humanity to leave its cradle. Second, it required someone on that vehicle to understand light, optics, and composition well enough to capture what they were seeing in a way that another human, standing on Earth, could feel. Those two impossibilities colliding is the story of cameras in space.

The history begins in 1962 with Wally Schirra walking into a Houston camera shop and refusing to accept a bad camera. It ends — for now — on April 6, 2026, with an Earthset photograph taken through the window of the Orion spacecraft by an astronaut trained by a flight operations imagery instructor from Rochester, New York, using a Nikon D5. Between those two moments: twelve Hasselblad bodies left on the Moon, a sixty-year institutional partnership, a digital transition that changed everything, and a curriculum pipeline from RIT to NASA that has trained over 150 astronauts in photography.

**Core concept:** Every photograph from space is a triangle — camera, crew, and curriculum — and the history of space photography is the history of how those three vertices were aligned across six decades.

## Through-line

Wally Schirra walking into a Houston camera shop in 1962. A Hasselblad 500C stripped down to its essential light-gathering soul. A camera on the Moon. Twelve cameras still on the Moon. A curriculum built in Rochester. Four astronauts with a Nikon and a window. An Earthset photograph. The chain is unbroken.
