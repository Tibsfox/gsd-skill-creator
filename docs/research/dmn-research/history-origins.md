# Data Mining: History and Origins

*From Fisher's field trials to Fayyad's pipeline --- the long quarrel between statistics, databases, and discovery*

---

## 1. Prehistory: Statistics Before There Were Databases

Data mining did not begin as data mining. For most of its prehistory it was simply called statistics, and the people who practiced it spent more time worrying about how to collect a few hundred observations than about how to sift through a few hundred million. The discipline that would eventually become data mining grew out of a nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century effort to put empirical reasoning on a mathematical footing, and its earliest ancestors are the same figures who built modern statistics.

The lineage runs through **Thomas Bayes**, whose posthumous 1763 *Essay Towards Solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances*, communicated to the Royal Society by Richard Price, introduced what we now call Bayes' theorem. Bayes proposed a way to reason backward from observed data to the probability of a hypothesis --- a direction of inference that would, two and a half centuries later, underwrite entire classes of data mining algorithms, from naive Bayes classifiers to Bayesian belief networks. **Pierre-Simon Laplace** formalized and extended inverse probability in his 1812 *Theorie analytique des probabilites*, applying it to problems as varied as the masses of planets and the ratio of male to female births in Paris. Laplace's insistence that probability was "nothing but common sense reduced to calculation" captures the spirit that would later animate exploratory data analysis: let the numbers speak, but give them a principled grammar.

The nineteenth century added the tools of regression and correlation. **Francis Galton**, studying the heights of parents and their adult children in the 1880s, noticed what he called "regression toward mediocrity" and in 1888 introduced the correlation coefficient. His protege **Karl Pearson** placed these ideas on a formal footing, founding the journal *Biometrika* in 1901 and defining the product-moment correlation, the chi-squared test, and much of the vocabulary of descriptive statistics. Pearson's *The Grammar of Science* (1892) argued that science was the classification and analysis of facts --- an almost programmatic statement of what data mining would later claim as its charter.

The single most important figure for what came next, however, was **Sir Ronald A. Fisher**. Beginning in 1919 at the Rothamsted Experimental Station in Hertfordshire, Fisher developed the modern theory of experimental design, including randomization, replication, and blocking. His 1925 *Statistical Methods for Research Workers* and his 1935 *The Design of Experiments* introduced analysis of variance (ANOVA), maximum likelihood estimation, and the formal machinery of hypothesis testing that would dominate twentieth-century empirical science. Fisher's famous "lady tasting tea" thought experiment, from the opening chapter of *The Design of Experiments*, gave the world its first carefully argued example of a hypothesis test as a pedagogical object. Fisher also introduced the term "linear discriminant" in a 1936 paper on Iris classification --- the same Iris dataset that would, sixty years later, be the "hello world" example in every data mining textbook. The continuity is striking: the Iris measurements that Edgar Anderson collected on the Gaspe Peninsula in 1935 and that Fisher analyzed in *The Annals of Eugenics* are still being classified by students learning decision trees and k-nearest-neighbors in the 2020s.

Alongside Fisher, **Jerzy Neyman** and **Egon Pearson** (Karl's son) formulated in a series of papers from 1928 to 1933 the Neyman-Pearson lemma and the now-standard framework of null and alternative hypotheses, Type I and Type II errors, and the power of a test. The Fisher-Neyman-Pearson synthesis --- never a comfortable one; the three quarreled bitterly for decades --- defined the shape of statistical inference for the rest of the twentieth century and provided the intellectual backbone against which data mining would later have to justify itself.

## 2. The 1960s and 1970s: Databases, Pattern Recognition, and Exploratory Analysis

Data mining could not exist until there was data to mine, and data in the modern computational sense is a product of the 1960s. **Charles Bachman**'s Integrated Data Store (IDS), developed at General Electric between 1961 and 1964, was arguably the first general-purpose database management system; it earned Bachman the 1973 Turing Award. **Edgar F. Codd** published "A Relational Model of Data for Large Shared Data Banks" in the *Communications of the ACM* in June 1970, proposing the relational model that would come to dominate commercial data management and win him his own Turing Award in 1981. IBM's System R project, beginning in 1974, produced the first working relational database prototype and gave the world SQL. By the end of the 1970s, organizations routinely held millions of records in queryable form, and the question of what to do with all that stored data --- beyond the transactional purposes for which it had been collected --- was becoming visible.

The decade also saw the birth of **pattern recognition** as an engineering discipline. The 1957 perceptron of **Frank Rosenblatt**, based on earlier McCulloch-Pitts neurons from 1943 and on Hebb's 1949 learning rule, demonstrated that a machine could learn a linear classifier from examples. **Richard Duda** and **Peter Hart**'s 1973 textbook *Pattern Classification and Scene Analysis* codified the field, introducing a generation of engineers to Bayes classifiers, nearest-neighbor methods, clustering, and feature selection. The same year, **Vladimir Vapnik** and Alexey Chervonenkis were publishing, in Russian, the foundations of statistical learning theory and the VC dimension --- work that would not become widely known in the West until the 1990s but that laid the mathematical groundwork for support vector machines and much of modern generalization theory.

The decade's most culturally important contribution to what would become data mining came from a different direction entirely. **John W. Tukey**, a Princeton statistician already famous for coining the words "bit" and "software," published *Exploratory Data Analysis* in 1977. The book was a manifesto. Tukey argued that the dominant confirmatory tradition --- formulate a hypothesis, design an experiment, compute a p-value --- left out the most creative and important part of statistics: looking at the data. He introduced the box plot, the stem-and-leaf display, and a suite of resistant summary statistics, and he insisted that "the greatest value of a picture is when it forces us to notice what we never expected to see." Tukey's distinction between *exploratory* and *confirmatory* analysis was essentially the distinction that data mining would later make between discovery and validation, and his legitimization of open-ended, hypothesis-generating investigation created the cultural space in which data mining could grow. His 1977 book is cited in Fayyad, Piatetsky-Shapiro, and Smyth's canonical 1996 definition of the KDD process as one of the intellectual ancestors of the field.

## 3. The 1980s: Machine Learning Rises, Decision Trees, Neural Networks Return

If the 1970s gave data mining its databases and its licence to explore, the 1980s gave it its algorithms. Machine learning, which had languished after Minsky and Papert's 1969 *Perceptrons* allegedly killed neural network research, reemerged in multiple forms at once.

**J. Ross Quinlan**, an Australian computer scientist, published "Induction of Decision Trees" in the first issue of the journal *Machine Learning* in 1986. The paper introduced **ID3**, an algorithm that built classification trees top-down by choosing, at each node, the attribute with the highest information gain --- a measure derived from Shannon's 1948 entropy. ID3 and its successors, **C4.5** (1993) and **C5.0**, became the workhorses of practical classification for two decades. Quinlan's 1993 book *C4.5: Programs for Machine Learning* is still one of the most cited texts in the field. Decision trees had appealing properties: they were fast to train, they produced human-readable rules, and they handled both categorical and numerical attributes without fuss. When data mining as a commercial discipline arrived in the 1990s, decision trees were its native dialect.

Parallel to Quinlan's work, **Leo Breiman**, **Jerome Friedman**, **Richard Olshen**, and **Charles Stone** published *Classification and Regression Trees* (CART) in 1984. CART used the Gini index rather than information gain, supported regression as well as classification, and introduced cost-complexity pruning. Breiman, a Berkeley statistician who had spent years in industry before returning to academia, would go on to champion an explicitly empirical, algorithm-first view of data analysis that he contrasted with what he called "the data modeling culture" in his influential 2001 paper "Statistical Modeling: The Two Cultures" in *Statistical Science*. Breiman's two-cultures argument --- that statisticians should pay more attention to predictive accuracy and less to distributional assumptions --- is essentially a philosophical defense of data mining against classical statistics, and it remains the most cited articulation of the tension between the two traditions.

Neural networks returned in the mid-1980s through the independent rediscovery of **backpropagation**. **David Rumelhart**, **Geoffrey Hinton**, and **Ronald Williams** published "Learning Representations by Back-Propagating Errors" in *Nature* in October 1986, demonstrating that multi-layer perceptrons could learn internal representations through gradient descent on a differentiable error function. (The underlying mathematics had been worked out earlier by Paul Werbos in his 1974 Harvard PhD thesis and, in different form, by Seppo Linnainmaa in 1970, but the 1986 paper made the technique widely known.) The *Parallel Distributed Processing* volumes edited by Rumelhart and James McClelland in 1986 became a generational event in cognitive science and machine learning, and they set the stage for the deep learning revolution twenty-five years later. At the time, however, neural networks were only one of several machine learning paradigms competing for attention, alongside decision trees, nearest neighbors, rule induction systems like **CN2** (Clark and Niblett, 1989), and the symbolic learning systems descended from Michalski's AQ family.

## 4. 1989: KDD Is Named at IJCAI in Detroit

The term under which data mining was first academically respectable is not "data mining" but **Knowledge Discovery in Databases**, abbreviated KDD. The name was coined by **Gregory Piatetsky-Shapiro** at a workshop he organized at the **Eleventh International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence (IJCAI-89)** held in Detroit in August 1989. Piatetsky-Shapiro, then a researcher at GTE Laboratories in Waltham, Massachusetts, had noticed that the intersection of database research, machine learning, and statistics was attracting a growing number of papers but lacked a unifying name or venue. The 1989 workshop was the first dedicated gathering of what would become the KDD community. It was followed by further KDD workshops at AAAI (1991, 1993, 1994), and by 1995 the series had grown into the annual **ACM SIGKDD International Conference on Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining**, which remains the field's premier venue.

The term "data mining" itself was older and had a more troubled origin. Statisticians had been using it since at least the 1960s as a pejorative, roughly equivalent to "data dredging" --- the disreputable practice of running so many tests against a dataset that something was bound to come out statistically significant by chance. **Ronald Coase**'s quip --- "If you torture the data long enough, it will confess" --- captured the suspicion, and the statistical literature through the 1970s is full of warnings against fishing expeditions. When the database and AI communities adopted "data mining" in the late 1980s and early 1990s as a positive label for their activity, classical statisticians objected. The word "knowledge discovery in databases," Piatetsky-Shapiro has said in interviews, was chosen partly to avoid the stigma. The two terms --- KDD and data mining --- coexisted uneasily for years. In the 1996 consensus definition offered by Fayyad, Piatetsky-Shapiro, and Smyth, data mining was defined as one *step* within the larger KDD process, specifically the step that applies algorithms to produce patterns. In common usage, and especially in commercial marketing, the distinction collapsed, and "data mining" won.

## 5. The 1990s: Data Warehousing, Association Rules, and the Fayyad-Piatetsky-Shapiro-Smyth Paper

The 1990s were the decade in which data mining became an industry. Two developments made it possible. The first was **data warehousing**: the architectural idea, championed by **Bill Inmon** in his 1992 *Building the Data Warehouse* and by **Ralph Kimball** in his 1996 *The Data Warehouse Toolkit*, that organizations should maintain a consolidated, read-optimized historical record of their transactions separately from the operational databases that ran the business. A data warehouse, once built, was a ready-made subject for mining. The second was the explosive growth of the retail sector's appetite for customer analytics, driven in part by the introduction of checkout scanners, loyalty cards, and point-of-sale systems that generated transactional data on a scale unimaginable a decade earlier.

The algorithmic landmark of the decade was the **Apriori algorithm** for mining association rules, published by **Rakesh Agrawal** and **Ramakrishnan Srikant** of IBM Almaden Research Center in "Fast Algorithms for Mining Association Rules" at VLDB 1994. Apriori exploited a simple monotonicity property --- if an itemset is frequent, all its subsets must be frequent --- to prune the search space of candidate rules, making it feasible to find frequent patterns in transaction databases with millions of records. The canonical example, apocryphal in its details but real in its influence, was the "beer and diapers" story: the claim that analysis of supermarket transactions had revealed that fathers sent out for diapers on weekends also bought beer. Whether or not the story is literally true --- its origins trace to a 1992 Osco Drug analysis conducted by Thomas Blischok of Teradata, and the specific "beer and diapers" framing appears to have been added in retelling --- it became the parable by which a generation of managers understood what data mining could do.

The decade's canonical synthesis came in 1996, when **Usama Fayyad**, Gregory Piatetsky-Shapiro, and **Padhraic Smyth** published "From Data Mining to Knowledge Discovery in Databases" in *AI Magazine* (Fall 1996, vol. 17, no. 3). The paper offered a definition of KDD --- "the nontrivial process of identifying valid, novel, potentially useful, and ultimately understandable patterns in data" --- that is still quoted in almost every data mining textbook. It described the process as a sequence of stages: selection, preprocessing, transformation, data mining (in the narrow sense of pattern-producing algorithms), and interpretation/evaluation. It argued explicitly that data mining was not an alternative to statistics but an interdisciplinary synthesis drawing on statistics, machine learning, pattern recognition, databases, and visualization. And it acknowledged the terminological dispute directly, treating "data mining" and "knowledge discovery in databases" as overlapping but distinct, with data mining being the algorithmic core of the broader KDD process. The same year, Fayyad, Piatetsky-Shapiro, Smyth, and Ramasamy Uthurusamy edited *Advances in Knowledge Discovery and Data Mining*, an AAAI Press volume that gathered the state of the art and served as the field's first authoritative reference.

## 6. CRISP-DM, Open Source, and the Netflix Prize

By the late 1990s, enough organizations were attempting data mining projects --- and enough were failing at them --- that practitioners began to ask for a process standard. The result was **CRISP-DM**, the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining, developed between 1996 and 1999 by a consortium that included **DaimlerChrysler**, **SPSS** (later acquired by IBM), **NCR**, and **OHRA** (a Dutch insurer), with partial funding from the European Commission's ESPRIT program. CRISP-DM version 1.0 was published in 1999 and publicly released in 2000. It defined six iterative phases: business understanding, data understanding, data preparation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment. CRISP-DM was never a formal standard in the ISO sense, but its diagrams and terminology became the lingua franca of commercial data mining practice. Polls of KDnuggets readers throughout the 2000s and 2010s consistently showed that a majority of practitioners either used CRISP-DM or used a process they recognized as derived from it. Twenty-five years after its publication, CRISP-DM is still the most widely taught data science process model in industry.

The same years saw data mining escape from expensive commercial tool suites into the hands of anyone with a computer. **Weka** (Waikato Environment for Knowledge Analysis), developed by **Ian Witten**'s group at the University of Waikato in New Zealand beginning in 1993 and rewritten in Java between 1997 and 1999, provided a free, graphical workbench that bundled decision trees, clustering algorithms, association rule miners, and classifier evaluation tools in a single interface. Witten and **Eibe Frank**'s textbook *Data Mining: Practical Machine Learning Tools and Techniques*, first published by Morgan Kaufmann in 1999, became one of the standard teaching texts for the field. The **R** language, created by Ross Ihaka and Robert Gentleman at the University of Auckland in 1993 and released under the GPL in 1995, grew throughout the 2000s into a rich ecosystem of statistical and data mining packages, with Friedrich Leisch's CRAN (Comprehensive R Archive Network) hosting thousands of contributed libraries. Python's scientific stack --- **NumPy** (2006, descended from Numeric/Numarray), **scikit-learn** (first public release 2010, from David Cournapeau's 2007 Google Summer of Code project at INRIA), and **pandas** (Wes McKinney, 2008, at AQR Capital Management) --- would eventually overtake R as the dominant platform for working data scientists, though that transition belongs to the 2010s.

The cultural turning point of the 2000s was the **Netflix Prize**, announced on October 2, 2006, offering one million US dollars to the first team whose algorithm could beat Netflix's existing Cinematch collaborative-filtering system by 10 percent in root mean squared error on the task of predicting movie ratings. The contest ran for almost three years, attracted more than 40,000 teams from 186 countries, and was won on July 26, 2009, by a team called "BellKor's Pragmatic Chaos" --- itself a merger of three earlier teams --- using an ensemble of matrix factorization, neighborhood methods, and restricted Boltzmann machines. The prize did more than any academic paper to convince corporate executives that data mining could produce real money, and it seeded a generation of competitive data science communities, most visibly **Kaggle**, founded in 2010 by Anthony Goldbloom and acquired by Google in 2017.

## 7. The 2010s: Big Data, Spark, and Convergence with Deep Learning

The 2010s are the decade in which "data mining" as a distinct label began to fade into the broader categories of "data science," "machine learning," and "analytics." Three forces drove the absorption. The first was the big-data wave. **Hadoop**, the open-source implementation of Google's MapReduce and Google File System papers (Dean and Ghemawat, OSDI 2004; Ghemawat, Gobioff, and Leung, SOSP 2003) initiated by Doug Cutting and Mike Cafarella at Yahoo in 2005--2006, made it practically possible to store and process petabytes of data on commodity hardware. **Apache Spark**, developed by Matei Zaharia at UC Berkeley's AMPLab beginning in 2009 and released as open source in 2010, offered a faster in-memory alternative and included **MLlib**, a distributed library of data mining algorithms. The data mining problems that had required expensive proprietary hardware in the 1990s could now be solved on rented cloud clusters by graduate students.

The second force was the deep learning revolution. Geoffrey Hinton's group at Toronto, with **Alex Krizhevsky** and **Ilya Sutskever**, entered a convolutional neural network called **AlexNet** in the 2012 ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge and won by a margin that astonished the computer vision community, cutting the top-five error rate from roughly 26 percent to 15.3 percent. The Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton paper "ImageNet Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks," published at NeurIPS 2012, is conventionally cited as the moment the deep learning era began. Within five years, neural networks had displaced most of the classical data mining toolkit --- decision trees, SVMs, naive Bayes --- for problems in vision, speech, and natural language, and many data mining departments quietly relabeled themselves machine learning groups.

The third force was the cultural rise of **data science** as a job title. DJ Patil and Jeff Hammerbacher are generally credited with popularizing the term in the late 2000s, and Thomas Davenport and Patil's October 2012 *Harvard Business Review* article "Data Scientist: The Sexiest Job of the 21st Century" made it official. Data mining, once the name of the whole field, was absorbed into data science as one of its constituent activities, alongside data engineering, statistical modeling, and machine learning. The SIGKDD conference continued, now drawing research on deep learning, graph neural networks, fairness, and causal inference, but the term "data mining" in everyday usage increasingly referred to the older, pre-deep-learning toolkit: association rules, decision trees, clustering, and classical classification methods applied to structured tabular data.

## 8. The 2020s: Data-Centric AI and the Training-Data Era

In the current decade, data mining has returned to prominence under new names and for a new purpose. The large language models that define the frontier of AI in the mid-2020s are not, at first glance, data mining systems; they are deep neural networks trained with gradient descent on enormous text corpora. But the activities that make them possible --- assembling, cleaning, deduplicating, classifying, filtering, and re-weighting web-scale datasets --- are data mining in all but name. The **Common Crawl** archive, begun by Gil Elbaz in 2007, provides petabytes of raw web data that every major language model training pipeline processes and selects from. The filtering and classification steps that turn Common Crawl into a useful training set --- language identification, quality classification, toxicity filtering, deduplication with MinHash or SimHash, and subject-matter balancing --- are data mining pipelines built at a scale that would have been inconceivable in 1996.

**Andrew Ng**'s call in 2021 for a shift from "model-centric" to "data-centric AI" --- made most visibly in a widely circulated talk and in the associated NeurIPS 2021 Data-Centric AI Workshop --- crystallized a growing recognition that the marginal returns to model architecture were diminishing while the marginal returns to careful data curation were rising. Benchmark leaderboards began to include data sheets and provenance documents. Research attention returned to questions --- selection bias, label noise, measurement error, representativeness --- that would have been familiar to Fisher at Rothamsted in 1925. The tools and the scale are new; the problems are not.

Synthetic data, generated either by statistical simulation or by letting one generative model produce training examples for another, has emerged as both a solution and a new source of problems. The rise of retrieval-augmented generation, in which a language model's outputs are conditioned on a dynamically mined collection of documents, fuses classical information retrieval and data mining with modern generative AI. The 2020s, in short, are not a departure from the data mining tradition but a continuation of it under generative auspices. The central questions --- what patterns are real, which are artifacts, what counts as knowledge, and how do we justify a claim derived from data --- remain the ones Fisher asked at Rothamsted, Tukey asked at Princeton, and Piatetsky-Shapiro asked at IJCAI in Detroit.

## 9. Terminological Disputes and Contested Origins

No history of data mining is complete without an acknowledgment that the field has always had an uneasy relationship with its own name. The statisticians who coined "data mining" as a term of abuse in the 1960s never entirely made peace with its later reclamation. David A. Freedman of Berkeley, writing in the 1980s and 1990s, warned repeatedly against the multiple-testing hazards that he saw as inherent in automated pattern search. The statistical literature on the "garden of forking paths" (Gelman and Loken, 2013), on pre-registration, and on the replication crisis in the social and biomedical sciences is in part a delayed response to the data-mining mindset taken to its logical extreme. The fact that data mining's most famous pedagogical example, the Iris dataset, was collected by a eugenicist (Fisher) and published in *The Annals of Eugenics* is itself a reminder that the field's statistical inheritance comes with political and ethical freight that practitioners have not always confronted.

The dispute between KDD and data mining as competing names was largely settled by commercial usage in favor of data mining, though SIGKDD's conference retains the longer phrase in its official name. The later dispute between data mining and machine learning was settled by machine learning; and the still later dispute between machine learning and data science was settled, for now, by data science. Each successor term has absorbed its predecessor rather than replaced it, and the result is that the intellectual lineage traced in this document --- Bayes to Fisher to Tukey to Quinlan to Piatetsky-Shapiro to Fayyad to Ng --- is a single continuous tradition appearing under a series of names. The practitioners change jobs and change titles, the algorithms are reinvented and renamed, but the underlying project is the one Laplace articulated in 1814: common sense, reduced to calculation, applied to whatever data we happen to have.
