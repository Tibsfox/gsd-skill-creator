## 1. Entity Formation

The choice of business entity is the foundational legal decision. Every subsequent obligation -- tax treatment, liability exposure, governance structure, ability to raise capital, and regulatory requirements -- flows from this first choice. The five primary entity types in the United States each carry a distinct combination of protections and constraints.

### Sole Proprietorship

The simplest structure. No formation filing is required -- a sole proprietorship comes into existence the moment a person begins conducting business. The owner reports all business income on Schedule C of their personal 1040. There is no liability protection: the owner's personal assets are exposed to all business debts and legal claims. The simplicity is the advantage and the risk [1][2].

### Partnership (General and Limited)

A general partnership forms when two or more persons associate to carry on a business for profit. Like sole proprietorships, general partnerships offer no liability protection for general partners. Limited partnerships (LPs) add a class of limited partners who contribute capital but do not participate in management; their liability is capped at their investment. Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs) extend liability protection to all partners and are commonly used by professional firms -- law, accounting, architecture [1][3].

### Limited Liability Company (LLC)

The LLC is the most popular entity for small businesses in the United States. Created by filing articles of organization with the state, an LLC provides limited liability (members' personal assets are protected from business debts) with flexible taxation. By default, a single-member LLC is taxed as a disregarded entity (Schedule C) and a multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership (Form 1065). An LLC may elect to be taxed as an S-Corp or C-Corp by filing IRS Form 8832 or Form 2553 [1][4].

**Washington State LLC Formation:** Filing fee is $200 with the Secretary of State. Annual report fee is $60. The LLC must designate a registered agent in Washington and comply with the Uniform Business Organizations Code (RCW 23.95) for naming requirements. Washington has no state income tax -- the Business & Occupation (B&O) tax is levied on gross receipts instead [5][6].

**Oregon LLC Formation:** Filing fee is $100 with the Oregon Secretary of State. Annual report fee is $100. Oregon levies a corporate income tax (6.6% on first $1M, 7.6% above) and a minimum corporate excise tax. The Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) of 0.57% applies to Oregon-sourced commercial activity exceeding $1M [7].

### S-Corporation

An S-Corp is not a distinct entity type but a tax election available to qualifying corporations (and LLCs that elect corporate taxation). The S-Corp election under Subchapter S of the Internal Revenue Code allows corporate income to pass through to shareholders' personal returns, avoiding double taxation. Requirements: no more than 100 shareholders, all shareholders must be U.S. citizens or residents, only one class of stock. The S-Corp must pay shareholders who are also employees a "reasonable salary" subject to payroll taxes; distributions above reasonable compensation are not subject to self-employment tax [1][8].

### C-Corporation

The C-Corp is the standard corporate entity under Subchapter C of the IRC. It is a separate taxpaying entity -- the corporation pays federal income tax at 21% (post-TCJA, flat rate), and shareholders pay tax again on dividends (double taxation). The C-Corp's advantages are structural: unlimited shareholders, multiple classes of stock (common, preferred, with different voting and economic rights), and the ability to retain earnings at corporate rates. C-Corps are the required structure for venture capital investment and public markets [1][9].

### The Choice Matrix

| Factor | Sole Prop | Partnership | LLC | S-Corp | C-Corp |
|--------|-----------|-------------|-----|--------|--------|
| Liability protection | None | None (GP) | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Formation complexity | None | Low | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through | Pass-through | Flexible | Pass-through | Double taxation |
| Self-employment tax | Full | Full | Full (default) | Reduced | N/A |
| Fundraising capability | Very limited | Limited | Limited | Limited | Unlimited |
| Number of owners | 1 | 2+ | 1+ | 1-100 | Unlimited |
| Stock classes | N/A | N/A | N/A | 1 | Unlimited |

**WA/OR Focus:** Both Washington and Oregon are business-friendly for LLC formation. Washington's absence of a state income tax (replaced by B&O tax on gross receipts) makes it particularly attractive for service businesses. Oregon's lower filing fees but presence of corporate income tax and the Commercial Activity Tax create a different calculus. Many Pacific Northwest businesses form in Washington for tax reasons while operating across both states [5][6][7].

## 2. Cooperative Structures

A cooperative is a business owned and democratically controlled by the people who use its services or by the people who work there. The cooperative difference from standard corporations is fundamental: cooperatives exist to serve their members, not to maximize returns for external investors. The governance principle is one-member-one-vote regardless of capital contribution [10][11].

### Types of Cooperatives

**Worker Cooperatives.** Owned and governed by the workers. Each worker-member has one vote. Surplus is distributed as patronage dividends based on hours worked or compensation earned, not capital invested. Examples: Mondragon Corporation (Spain, 80,000+ worker-owners across 96 cooperatives), Cooperative Home Care Associates (New York, 2,000+ home care aides), Equal Exchange (fair trade coffee) [10][12].

**Consumer Cooperatives.** Owned by the customers who purchase goods or services. REI (Recreational Equipment Inc.) is the largest consumer cooperative in the United States -- 23+ million members, $3.8B+ in annual revenue. Members receive an annual dividend based on purchases. Credit unions are consumer cooperatives in the financial sector: member-owned, not-for-profit, serving 130+ million Americans through 4,700+ institutions [10][13][14].

**Producer Cooperatives.** Owned by the producers who supply raw materials or products. Agricultural cooperatives are the classic example: Land O'Lakes, Ocean Spray, Sunkist, Blue Diamond. Producers pool resources for processing, marketing, and distribution, gaining negotiating power they would lack individually. Producer cooperatives account for a significant share of U.S. agricultural output [10][15].

### Cooperative Accounting

Cooperative accounting differs from standard corporate accounting in several key areas:

**Patronage Dividends.** The cooperative's net income (after operating expenses and reserves) is allocated to members based on their patronage -- the volume or value of business each member conducted with the cooperative. In a worker cooperative, patronage is typically measured by hours worked. In a consumer cooperative, by purchases. In a producer cooperative, by products delivered. The patronage dividend is the cooperative's mechanism for returning surplus to its members rather than retaining it as corporate profit [16][17].

**Member Equity and Internal Capital Accounts.** Members build equity through their patronage allocations. Internal capital accounts track each member's accumulated equity stake. When a member departs, the cooperative redeems their capital account -- typically through a scheduled payout over several years to preserve cooperative liquidity. This is fundamentally different from selling shares on a market [16][18].

**Retained Earnings and Collective Reserves.** Cooperatives maintain collective reserve accounts (indivisible reserves in some traditions) that belong to the cooperative as a whole rather than to individual members. These reserves provide working capital, fund expansion, and ensure long-term stability. The balance between member allocations and collective reserves is a core governance decision [16].

### Subchapter T Taxation

Cooperative taxation under Subchapter T of the Internal Revenue Code (IRC Sections 1381-1388) provides the mechanism by which cooperatives avoid double taxation. The cooperative deducts patronage dividends from its taxable income. Members include the dividends in their personal taxable income. The effect is single taxation at the member level [17][19].

**Qualified vs. Nonqualified Written Notices of Allocation.** Qualified notices are taxed to the member in the year allocated (even if retained in the capital account); the cooperative deducts them immediately. Nonqualified notices are not deductible by the cooperative when allocated but are deductible when later redeemed in cash; the member is taxed at redemption. Federal law requires a minimum of 20% of qualified patronage dividends be paid in cash [17][19].

### The Cooperative Difference

The Rochdale Principles, established in 1844 by the Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers in England, remain the foundation of cooperative identity worldwide: voluntary and open membership, democratic member control, member economic participation, autonomy and independence, education and training, cooperation among cooperatives, and concern for community. The International Co-operative Alliance (ICA) maintains these principles as the global standard [10][20].

**Mondragon.** The Mondragon Corporation in the Basque Country of Spain is the world's largest federation of worker cooperatives. Founded in 1956 by a priest, Jose Maria Arizmendiarrieta, Mondragon comprises 96 cooperatives employing 80,000+ people across manufacturing, retail, finance, and education. The pay ratio between the highest and lowest paid worker is capped (originally 3:1, now approximately 6:1 in most cooperatives). Mondragon's Caja Laboral (labor bank) provides cooperative-specific financial services. The federation demonstrated that worker ownership can operate at industrial scale [12][21].

## 3. Tribal Sovereignty & Business

Tribal sovereignty is not a grant from the federal government -- it is inherent. Federally recognized tribes are sovereign nations that predate the United States Constitution. This sovereignty has profound implications for commercial activity: tribal enterprises operate under their own law, not state law, and are subject to federal law only to the extent that Congress has explicitly made it applicable. The legal framework for tribal business is fundamentally different from state-chartered business, and that difference must be understood on its own terms [22][23].

**Disclaimer:** This section describes general legal frameworks. Tribal governance is nation-specific. Each tribal nation has its own constitution, laws, and governance structure. Nothing in this section constitutes legal advice or describes any specific nation's internal governance.

### Tribal Sovereign Immunity

Tribal sovereign immunity means that tribal entities generally cannot be sued without the tribe's consent. This immunity extends to tribally owned enterprises that function as arms of the tribe. The doctrine has been consistently upheld by the U.S. Supreme Court, most significantly in *Kiowa Tribe of Oklahoma v. Manufacturing Technologies* (1998), which held that tribal immunity applies to both governmental and commercial activities, on and off reservation [22][24].

For joint ventures between tribal and non-tribal entities, limited waivers of sovereign immunity are negotiated on a transaction-by-transaction basis. Waivers can be limited by scope (specific assets only), type of relief (damages but not injunctive relief), monetary cap, and forum (arbitration rather than state courts) [22][24].

### Section 17 Federal Corporations

Section 17 of the Indian Reorganization Act of 1934 (IRA) authorizes tribes to obtain federal corporate charters from the Secretary of the Interior. A Section 17 corporation is wholly owned by the tribe but legally separate from the tribal government. Key characteristics: the IRS has ruled these corporations exempt from federal income tax; the charter provides a veil of limited liability protecting tribal government assets; the corporation may issue tax-exempt bonds for essential governmental services; it may enter 25-year land leases without Secretary approval [22][25].

### Tribal LLCs and Tribally Chartered Entities

Many tribes form business entities under their own tribal law -- tribally chartered corporations or tribal LLCs. These entities enjoy sovereign immunity and avoidance of state taxation when doing business on Indian land. The advantage is complete tribal control without federal approval processes. The tradeoff is reduced familiarity for lenders and partners accustomed to state-chartered entities [22][25].

State-chartered tribal entities (formed under state LLC or corporation statutes) provide transparency and familiarity for external partners but sacrifice the presumption of sovereign immunity and are subject to state taxation and regulation [25].

### Joint Ventures

Joint ventures between tribal nations and non-tribal entities are a growing model for economic development on tribal lands. Structuring considerations include: majority tribal equity and board control (preserving sovereign governance), limited waivers of immunity for specific joint venture assets, allocation of income between on-reservation and off-reservation activities for tax purposes, and compliance with tribal employment preferences and procurement requirements [22][23][25].

### Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (IGRA)

The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act of 1988 established the framework for tribal gaming operations. IGRA divides gaming into three classes: Class I (traditional/ceremonial, regulated exclusively by tribes), Class II (bingo and similar, regulated by tribes with NIGC oversight), and Class III (casino-style gaming, requiring a tribal-state compact). Tribal gaming generates $40B+ annually and funds tribal government services, infrastructure, and per-capita distributions in many nations. Gaming revenue has been the primary funding source for tribal economic diversification into non-gaming enterprises [26][27].

### Economic Development

The Harvard Project on American Indian Economic Development (Harvard Kennedy School) has documented key factors in successful tribal economic development: sovereignty (nations that exercise genuine self-governance outperform those managed by federal agencies), separation of politics from business (insulating enterprise management from political cycles), cultural match (governance structures that align with the nation's cultural values), and strategic orientation (long-term planning rather than program-by-program responses) [23][28].

## 4. Federal Regulatory Compliance

Federal regulatory compliance is not optional and not decorative. It is the infrastructure that makes capital markets possible -- the rules that let strangers trust each other with money. The compliance burden scales with company size, capital raised, and regulatory exposure.

### SEC Registration and Exemptions

The Securities Act of 1933 requires that any offer or sale of securities be registered with the SEC unless an exemption applies. Registration is expensive ($100K+ in legal and accounting fees) and time-consuming (3-6+ months). Most private companies raise capital under exemptions [29][30].

**Regulation D.** The most commonly used private placement exemption. Rule 506(b) permits unlimited capital from accredited investors plus up to 35 non-accredited sophisticated investors; no general solicitation allowed. Rule 506(c) permits general solicitation (advertising) but only accredited investors may purchase; the issuer must take reasonable steps to verify accredited status [29][30].

**Accredited Investor Definition (post-2020).** Individual with income exceeding $200K ($300K joint) in each of the last two years with expectation of the same, OR net worth exceeding $1M excluding primary residence. The 2020 SEC amendments added holders of Series 7, 65, and 82 licenses and "knowledgeable employees" of private funds regardless of wealth [29][31].

### Sarbanes-Oxley (SOX)

The Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, enacted after the Enron and WorldCom accounting scandals, imposed internal control and audit requirements on public companies. Key provisions: CEO and CFO must personally certify financial statements (Section 302), companies must maintain and assess internal controls over financial reporting (Section 404), the external auditor must attest to the effectiveness of internal controls (Section 404(b), applies to large accelerated filers), and criminal penalties for destroying, altering, or concealing documents to obstruct investigations (Section 802) [32][33].

SOX applies fully to public companies. Private companies are not directly subject to SOX but are influenced by its standards -- investors, lenders, and auditors increasingly expect SOX-level controls from large private companies, especially those preparing for IPO [32].

### AML/KYC Requirements

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) requirements apply to financial institutions, money services businesses, and increasingly to any company handling significant financial transactions. The Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) requires filing Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs) for cash transactions over $10,000 and Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) for transactions that appear to involve funds from illegal activity. The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), effective 2024, requires most companies to report beneficial ownership information to FinCEN [34][35].

### The Compliance Stack

For a growing company, the compliance stack builds in layers:

| Stage | Requirements |
|-------|-------------|
| Formation | State filing, registered agent, EIN, business licenses |
| First revenue | Tax registration (federal, state, local), payroll tax, workers' comp |
| First employees | Employment law compliance (FLSA, FMLA, ADA, Title VII), I-9 verification |
| Raising capital | Securities exemption compliance (Reg D filing, investor verification) |
| Revenue > $1M | More complex tax obligations, potential B&O surtax (WA), audit preparation |
| Going public | Full SEC registration, SOX compliance, quarterly/annual reporting |

## 5. Equity & Securities

### Equity Compensation

Equity compensation aligns employee incentives with company success. The primary instruments:

**Stock Options.** The right to purchase stock at a fixed price (the "exercise" or "strike" price) in the future. Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) receive favorable tax treatment (no tax at exercise if held 1+ year after exercise and 2+ years after grant; gains taxed as capital gains). Non-Qualified Stock Options (NQSOs) are taxed as ordinary income at exercise on the spread between strike price and fair market value [36][37].

**Restricted Stock Units (RSUs).** A promise to deliver shares upon vesting. No purchase price. Taxed as ordinary income at vesting (fair market value at delivery). RSUs have become the dominant equity compensation vehicle at large public companies because they have value even if the stock price falls (unlike options, which can become worthless "underwater") [36][37].

**Phantom Equity.** Cash-based rights that track the value of actual equity without issuing real shares. Used by LLCs, partnerships, and companies that don't want to add shareholders. Stock Appreciation Rights (SARs) pay the increase in value; phantom stock pays the full value. Taxed as ordinary income when paid [36].

### Cap Table Management

The capitalization table (cap table) tracks every equity stake in the company -- founders, investors, employees, advisors. It is the constitutional document of ownership. A clean cap table shows exactly who owns what percentage, at what price, under what terms, and what happens under various scenarios (new funding rounds, exits, dilution) [38].

**Dilution.** When new shares are issued (to investors, employees, or through conversion of convertible notes), existing shareholders' percentage ownership decreases. Understanding dilution is essential for founders: a Series A round that sells 20% of the company to investors reduces each founder's percentage by 20% of their holding. Option pools (typically 10-20% of pre-money shares) are created for employee equity and further dilute existing holders [38][39].

### 409A Valuations

Section 409A of the Internal Revenue Code requires that stock options be granted at no less than fair market value (FMV). Private companies must obtain an independent valuation (a "409A valuation") to establish FMV. Failure to grant options at FMV results in severe tax penalties for the recipient: immediate taxation on vesting, a 20% penalty tax, and interest. 409A valuations are typically performed by independent valuation firms every 12 months or after any material event (funding round, significant revenue change, acquisition offer) [36][40].

### Vesting Schedules

The standard vesting schedule is 4 years with a 1-year cliff: no equity vests during the first year; at the 12-month mark, 25% vests at once (the "cliff"); the remaining 75% vests monthly over the following 36 months. The cliff protects the company from giving equity to employees who leave quickly. Some companies use 3-year vesting; others use performance-based vesting tied to milestones [36][37].

### Common vs. Preferred Stock

**Common stock** represents basic ownership with voting rights and residual claim on assets. Founders and employees typically hold common stock.

**Preferred stock** is issued to investors and carries additional rights: liquidation preference (investors get their money back before common holders receive anything), anti-dilution protection (price adjustment if the company raises future rounds at a lower valuation), board seats, pro-rata participation rights (right to invest in future rounds), information rights, and protective provisions (veto power over major decisions) [38][39].

### Convertible Notes and SAFEs

**Convertible Notes.** Short-term debt that converts into equity at the next priced round. The note accrues interest and typically includes a valuation cap (maximum valuation at which the note converts) and a discount (typically 15-25% discount to the next round's price). If no priced round occurs, the note is repayable as debt at maturity [38][39].

**SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity).** Created by Y Combinator as a simpler alternative to convertible notes. A SAFE is not debt -- it has no maturity date, no interest rate, and no repayment obligation. It converts to equity at the next priced round at either a discount or a valuation cap (whichever gives the investor more shares). Post-money SAFEs (the current standard) make dilution more predictable [38][41].

## 6. Double-Entry Bookkeeping

### Pacioli's Gift

In 1494, Luca Pacioli, a Franciscan friar and mathematician, published *Summa de Arithmetica, Geometria, Proportioni et Proportionalita* in Venice. Chapter 36, *De Computis et Scripturis* (Of Reckonings and Writings), contained the first published description of the double-entry bookkeeping system that Venetian merchants had been using for at least a century. Pacioli did not invent double-entry -- he documented it, systematized it, and made it accessible. Five hundred and thirty years later, his system remains the foundation of all financial accounting worldwide [42][43].

### The Accounting Equation

The fundamental equation of double-entry bookkeeping:

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

Every transaction affects at least two accounts and preserves this equation. If a company borrows $10,000 from a bank, assets increase (cash +$10,000) and liabilities increase (loan payable +$10,000). The equation balances. If the company earns $5,000 in revenue, assets increase (cash or accounts receivable +$5,000) and equity increases (retained earnings +$5,000). The equation balances. If it ever does not balance, there is an error [42][43][44].

### Debits and Credits

Every account has a debit side (left) and a credit side (right). The rules:

| Account Type | Increases With | Decreases With |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Assets | Debit | Credit |
| Liabilities | Credit | Debit |
| Equity | Credit | Debit |
| Revenue | Credit | Debit |
| Expenses | Debit | Credit |

Every transaction requires at least one debit and at least one credit, and the total debits must equal the total credits. This is not a convention -- it is the mathematical consequence of the accounting equation. The system is self-checking: if debits do not equal credits, the books are wrong [42][43].

### Chart of Accounts

The chart of accounts is the taxonomy of a company's financial life. Every transaction is classified into one of five categories (assets, liabilities, equity, revenue, expenses) and further subdivided into specific accounts. A typical small business might have 30-50 accounts; a large corporation might have thousands. The chart of accounts is numbered for organizational consistency (assets 1000-1999, liabilities 2000-2999, equity 3000-3999, revenue 4000-4999, expenses 5000-5999) [44][45].

### Journal Entries

A journal entry records a transaction in the general journal. Each entry includes the date, the accounts affected, the debit and credit amounts, and a description. Example:

| Date | Account | Debit | Credit |
|------|---------|-------|--------|
| Mar 25 | Cash | $5,000 | |
| Mar 25 | Service Revenue | | $5,000 |
| | *Received payment for consulting services* | | |

The journal entry flows to the general ledger (each account's running balance), which feeds the trial balance (a list of all account balances to verify debits equal credits), which feeds the financial statements [42][44].

### Financial Statements

**Balance Sheet (Statement of Financial Position).** A snapshot of the accounting equation at a point in time. Assets on one side, liabilities and equity on the other. They must balance [44][45].

**Income Statement (Profit and Loss Statement).** Revenue minus expenses over a period. The bottom line (net income or net loss) flows to retained earnings on the balance sheet [44][45].

**Statement of Cash Flows.** Cash inflows and outflows categorized as operating activities, investing activities, and financing activities. The income statement uses accrual accounting (revenue recognized when earned, expenses when incurred); the cash flow statement shows actual cash movement [44][45].

### GAAP vs. IFRS

**GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles).** The U.S. standard, maintained by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB). Rules-based: specific guidance for specific situations. Required for U.S. public companies and any company reporting to the SEC [44][46].

**IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards).** The global standard, maintained by the International Accounting Standards Board (IASB). Used by 140+ countries. Principles-based: broader principles with more judgment required. Key differences from GAAP include LIFO inventory prohibition, revaluation of assets permitted, and different lease classification [44][46].

### Accrual vs. Cash Basis

**Accrual basis:** Revenue is recognized when earned (regardless of cash receipt); expenses are recognized when incurred (regardless of payment). Required by GAAP for public companies. Provides a more accurate picture of financial performance but is more complex [44].

**Cash basis:** Revenue is recognized when cash is received; expenses when cash is paid. Simpler, used by many small businesses. The IRS permits cash basis for businesses with average annual gross receipts of $29M or less (indexed for inflation) [44].
