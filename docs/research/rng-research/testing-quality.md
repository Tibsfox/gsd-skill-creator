# Testing Random Number Generators

*The art of proving something is random enough -- but never random*

---

## 1. The Fundamental Question: Can You Prove Randomness?

No.

This is worth stating plainly, because the entire field of statistical testing rests on an asymmetry that is easy to gloss over and hard to internalize. You cannot prove that a sequence of numbers is random. You can only fail to prove that it is not random. Every statistical test for randomness is a **falsification test** -- it looks for a specific kind of pattern, and if it finds one, it declares the generator suspect. If it does not find a pattern, the generator passes *that particular test*. But passing does not mean the generator is good. It means the generator is not bad in the specific way the test was looking for.

This is not a philosophical quibble. It has concrete consequences.

Consider a generator that produces the sequence 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, ... repeating forever. A frequency test that counts how often each digit 0-7 appears will find a perfectly uniform distribution. The generator passes. A serial test that looks at pairs of consecutive values will find that (0,1), (1,2), (2,3), ... each appear with equal frequency -- but (0,3) or (5,2) never appear. The serial test catches what the frequency test missed. A runs-up-and-down test will notice that the sequence always ascends 0 through 7 and then drops -- a glaring pattern. Each successive test probes a different dimension of the generator's output, and failure can hide in any dimension you neglect to check.

This is why the field has moved from individual tests to **batteries** -- coordinated suites of tests that probe many different statistical properties at once. The history of those batteries is a history of generators that passed the previous generation of tests and then were discovered, years later, to be catastrophically flawed by a new test that looked in a direction nobody had thought to look before.

The mathematical framework is **hypothesis testing**. Each test defines a null hypothesis H0: "this sequence was generated by a true random process." The test computes a statistic -- some measurable property of the sequence -- and compares it to the theoretical distribution that statistic would have under H0. If the observed statistic falls in the extreme tails (typically beyond a p-value threshold of 0.01 or 0.001), the test rejects H0. The generator "fails."

The subtlety: a true random sequence will occasionally produce extreme statistics. By definition, a p-value of 0.01 means that 1% of truly random sequences will fail the test. This is why test batteries run many tests and look for *systematic* failure patterns, not isolated p-value excursions. A single failure at p = 0.005 is not alarming. Ten failures out of fifteen tests, each at p < 0.01, is damning.

## 2. Knuth's Classical Tests

Donald Knuth's *The Art of Computer Programming*, Volume 2, Chapter 3.3, published in 1969 and revised through the third edition in 1997, codified the first systematic approach to testing pseudorandom number generators. These tests are the foundation -- not because they are sufficient (they are not, and Knuth knew it), but because they establish the vocabulary and intuition for everything that followed.

### The Frequency Test (Equidistribution)

The simplest question: does each possible value appear roughly equally often? Divide the output range into *d* equal bins and count the number of values falling into each bin over *n* observations. Under true randomness, each bin should contain approximately n/d values. Apply a chi-squared test to the bin counts. If any bin is dramatically over- or under-represented, the generator is biased.

The intuition: a loaded die will eventually reveal itself if you roll it enough times and count the faces. This test catches gross biases but misses everything else. A generator that outputs 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 1, ... passes the frequency test perfectly (for two bins) while being maximally non-random.

### The Serial Test (Pairs)

Extend the frequency test to pairs of consecutive values. If the generator produces values in {0, 1, ..., d-1}, then there are d^2 possible pairs. Count how often each pair appears. Under true randomness, all pairs should be equally likely. This catches sequential dependencies that the frequency test misses -- the 0, 1, 0, 1, ... generator would fail immediately, because only two of the four possible pairs ever appear.

The serial test generalizes to triples, quadruples, and higher-order tuples, though the number of bins grows exponentially and the required sample size grows with it. In practice, pairs and triples are the most commonly used.

### The Gap Test

Pick a value *a* (or range of values). Scan through the output sequence and record the lengths of "gaps" -- the number of values between successive occurrences of *a*. Under true randomness, gap lengths follow a geometric distribution with parameter p = probability of generating *a*. A chi-squared test compares the observed gap-length distribution to the geometric distribution.

The intuition: if a die avoids showing 6 for long stretches and then clusters sixes together, something is wrong. The gap test detects this kind of "clumping" or "avoidance" behavior.

### The Poker Test

Take groups of five consecutive values and classify each group by its pattern: all different (a "bust" in poker terms), one pair, two pairs, three of a kind, full house, four of a kind, five of a kind. Count how often each pattern appears and compare to the theoretical probabilities.

The name is whimsical -- Knuth liked the analogy -- but the test is probing the joint distribution of five consecutive outputs. It catches correlations that pair-wise tests miss.

### The Coupon Collector's Test

How long does it take to see every value at least once? If the generator produces values in {0, 1, ..., d-1}, start scanning and count how many values you must observe before all *d* distinct values have appeared. This count follows a known distribution (the coupon collector distribution). Repeat many times and compare the observed distribution to the theoretical one.

The intuition: if you are collecting baseball cards from random packs, theory predicts how many packs you need to complete the set. If a generator completes the set too quickly (values are too spread out) or too slowly (values cluster), something is wrong.

### The Permutation Test

Take blocks of *t* consecutive values and determine their relative ordering (i.e., which is smallest, which is second smallest, etc.). There are t! possible orderings; under true randomness, each should appear with probability 1/t!. Count and chi-squared test.

This is a powerful test because it is entirely about ordering, not magnitude. Even a generator that perfectly matches the frequency and serial tests can produce orderings that are non-uniform -- for instance, if values tend to increase locally (a common artifact of linear congruential generators with certain parameters).

### The Runs Test

A "run" is a maximal ascending or descending subsequence. Count the lengths of runs up (ascending sequences) and runs down (descending sequences). Under true randomness, the distribution of run lengths follows a specific probability law. A generator that produces locally-sorted output will have runs that are too long; one that oscillates will have runs that are too short.

Knuth also described the **runs above/below the mean**: count how many consecutive values fall above or below the mean of the sequence. Under true randomness, these runs have a predictable distribution. A generator that drifts -- spending long periods above the mean and then long periods below it -- will produce runs that are too long.

## 3. The Diehard Battery

In 1995, **George Marsaglia** at Florida State University released the **Diehard** battery of statistical tests. The motivation was frustration -- not academic frustration, but the genuine exasperation of a man who had spent decades watching generators fail and yet be adopted by major software libraries.

Marsaglia had discovered in 1968 that the widely-used RANDU generator (an LCG with multiplier 65539, used by IBM and shipped with their systems) produced triples that fell on just 15 parallel planes in three-dimensional space. This meant that any Monte Carlo simulation using three random numbers at a time was sampling from a 15-element set, not a continuum. The discovery should have killed RANDU immediately. It did not. RANDU continued to ship with IBM systems for years, and papers based on RANDU results continued to be published. Marsaglia learned the hard way that demonstrating a flaw is not enough -- you need a test battery that makes flaws undeniable.

Diehard contains 15 tests, each probing a different structural property:

1. **Birthday Spacings.** Choose *m* random "birthdays" from a "year" of *n* days. Count the number of duplicate spacings. Under true randomness, this count follows a Poisson distribution. This test is extraordinarily sensitive to lattice structure in the output.

2. **Overlapping 5-Permutations.** Take overlapping sequences of 5 consecutive 32-bit integers and record their relative ordering (one of 120 permutations). Count occurrences of each permutation.

3. **Binary Rank (31x31 and 32x32).** Form a matrix of random bits and compute its rank over GF(2). The rank distribution is known for truly random matrices. This catches linear dependencies in the bit structure of the output.

4. **Bitstream.** Treat the output as a stream of bits. Take overlapping 20-bit words and count how many of the 2^20 possible words are missing. Under true randomness, the expected number of missing words follows a normal distribution with known mean and variance.

5. **OPSO (Overlapping-Pairs-Sparse-Occupancy).** Similar to the bitstream test but using overlapping pairs of 10-bit values drawn from consecutive 32-bit words.

6. **OQSO (Overlapping-Quadruples-Sparse-Occupancy).** Like OPSO but with overlapping quadruples of 5-bit values.

7. **DNA.** Treat the output as a sequence of 2-bit "letters" (like DNA bases: A, C, G, T). Take overlapping 10-letter "words" and count missing words. The name was a joke, but the test is serious -- it probes the fine bit-level structure that generators with linear feedback often corrupt.

8. **Count the 1's (stream and specific bytes).** Count the number of 1-bits in each byte. The distribution should follow a binomial. This catches bit-level biases that are invisible at the integer level.

9. **Parking Lot.** Randomly "park" 12,000 unit-radius circles in a 100x100 square. Count how many can be placed without overlapping. Under true randomness, the number successfully parked follows a normal distribution with known mean (~3,523) and standard deviation (~21.9).

10. **Minimum Distance.** Place 8,000 points in a 10,000x10,000 square. Find the minimum distance between any two points. Under true randomness, the square of the minimum distance follows an exponential distribution.

11. **3D Spheres.** Place 4,000 points in a cube of side 1,000. For each point, find the distance to the nearest other point and compute the volume of the sphere with that radius. Under true randomness, the minimum such volume follows an exponential distribution.

12. **Squeeze.** Start with k = 2^31 and repeatedly multiply by a random float in (0,1) until k reaches 1. Count the number of iterations required. Repeat 100,000 times and compare the iteration-count distribution to the theoretical one.

13. **Overlapping Sums.** Take overlapping sequences of 100 consecutive uniform floats, sum them, and test whether the sums follow the expected distribution.

14. **Runs.** Count runs up and runs down in the sequence (similar to Knuth's test but with Diehard's own implementation and calibration).

15. **Craps.** Simulate 200,000 games of craps. Count wins and losses, and record the distribution of game lengths. Compare to theoretical probabilities. This is simultaneously a test and a commentary on the practical stakes of bad randomness -- if you are running a casino, you need to know your generator is fair.

Marsaglia distributed Diehard as a free C program with a fixed binary input format. You generated a file of random bytes, fed it to Diehard, and got a report. The battery was influential not because it was perfect -- it was not, and it could not be extended -- but because it set the expectation that serious generator proposals would come with test results. Before Diehard, claiming a generator was "good" was an assertion. After Diehard, it was an empirical question.

## 4. TestU01: The Gold Standard

In 2007, **Pierre L'Ecuyer** and **Richard Simard** at the Universite de Montreal published **TestU01**, a comprehensive C library for statistical testing of random number generators. Where Diehard was a fixed battery, TestU01 was a framework -- users could compose their own test suites, add new tests, and test generators written in any language via a simple interface.

TestU01 ships with three predefined batteries of increasing severity:

**SmallCrush** -- 10 tests. A quick screening that takes seconds. Useful for catching grossly flawed generators. Essentially a sanity check.

**Crush** -- 96 tests. A thorough battery that takes minutes to hours. Most well-designed generators pass Crush. Passing Crush is necessary but not sufficient for publication-quality work.

**BigCrush** -- 106 tests. The most comprehensive battery publicly available (at the time of publication and still as of 2025). BigCrush runs 106 statistical tests, each calibrated to detect subtle deviations from randomness. A full BigCrush run processes approximately 2^38 (~274 billion) random numbers and takes several hours on modern hardware.

The power of TestU01 is best understood through its results table, which has become a reference point for the entire PRNG community:

| Generator | SmallCrush | Crush | BigCrush |
|-----------|-----------|-------|----------|
| java.util.Random (LCG, 48-bit) | FAIL | FAIL | FAIL |
| MINSTD (LCG, Park-Miller) | FAIL | FAIL | FAIL |
| RANDU (LCG, IBM) | FAIL | FAIL | FAIL |
| Mersenne Twister (MT19937) | Pass | Pass | FAIL (2 tests) |
| xorshift128 (Marsaglia 2003) | FAIL | FAIL | FAIL |
| xorshift128+ | Pass | Pass | FAIL (1 test) |
| xoshiro256++ (Blackman-Vigna) | Pass | Pass | Pass |
| xoshiro256** (Blackman-Vigna) | Pass | Pass | Pass |
| PCG64 (O'Neill 2014) | Pass | Pass | Pass |
| SplittableRandom (Steele et al.) | Pass | Pass | Pass |
| ChaCha20 (Bernstein) | Pass | Pass | Pass |
| AES-CTR | Pass | Pass | Pass |

Several results here deserve commentary:

**Mersenne Twister fails BigCrush.** This is perhaps the most consequential result in the table. MT19937 has been the default PRNG in Python (`random` module), R, MATLAB, Ruby, PHP, and dozens of other languages and libraries since its publication in 1998. It was explicitly designed to pass Diehard, and it does. But BigCrush probes dimensions that Diehard does not -- specifically, the linear complexity test and the matrix rank test expose MT19937's underlying GF(2)-linear structure. The output, viewed as a binary sequence, has lower linear complexity than a truly random sequence would. For most applications this does not matter. For any application that depends on bit-level independence (cryptography, certain financial simulations, advanced Monte Carlo methods), it is a real flaw.

**LCGs fail everything.** This is not surprising, but it is worth stating: the generators that shipped as system defaults in Unix, Windows, and Java for decades cannot pass even SmallCrush. The entire scientific literature of the 1970s through 1990s that relied on `rand()` or `drand48()` is built on a foundation of generators that are, by modern standards, broken.

**PCG and xoshiro pass everything.** These modern generators, published in 2014 and 2018 respectively, were designed with TestU01 results as a primary quality metric. They represent the current state of the art for non-cryptographic PRNGs.

L'Ecuyer's contribution goes beyond the software. His 2007 paper provides a taxonomy of test types, a theory of test power (how many samples a test needs to detect a given deviation), and a framework for thinking about what "passing" means. He emphasizes that passing BigCrush is necessary for a generator to be taken seriously, but it is not a proof of quality -- it is merely the absence of detected failure, with the best detection tools currently available.

## 5. PractRand: The Endurance Test

**PractRand** (Practically Random), written by **Chris Doty-Humphrey** and released in 2010 with ongoing updates through the 2020s, takes a fundamentally different approach from TestU01. Where TestU01 runs a fixed number of tests on a fixed amount of data, PractRand is a **streaming** test system that consumes data continuously and reports failures as they emerge.

The design is simple: pipe random bytes into PractRand's standard input. PractRand runs a battery of tests at exponentially increasing data thresholds -- 2^10 bytes, 2^11 bytes, ..., 2^40 bytes and beyond. At each threshold, it reports whether any test has failed. The output looks like:

```
RNG_test using PractRand version 0.95
RNG = RNG_stdin, seed = unknown
test set = core, folding = standard

rng=RNG_stdin, seed=unknown
length= 256 megabytes (2^28 bytes), time= 3.2 seconds
  no anomalies in 171 test results

rng=RNG_stdin, seed=unknown
length= 512 megabytes (2^29 bytes), time= 6.1 seconds
  no anomalies in 182 test results

rng=RNG_stdin, seed=unknown
length= 1 gigabyte (2^30 bytes), time= 12.4 seconds
  no anomalies in 196 test results
```

For a failing generator, the output instead shows:

```
length= 64 megabytes (2^26 bytes), time= 1.4 seconds
  Test Name                         Raw       Processed     Evaluation
  [Low1/8]FPF-14+6/16:all          R= +57.9  p~= 3.2e-52    FAIL !!!!
  ...and 1 test result(s) without anomalies
```

The "FAIL at 2^N bytes" format has become a standard way to characterize generator quality in the PRNG community. A generator that fails at 2^26 bytes is usable for small tasks but broken for anything substantial. A generator that survives to 2^45 bytes (32 terabytes) without failure is very strong.

PractRand's streaming design gives it two advantages over TestU01:

First, **early failure detection.** A badly flawed generator will fail at 2^16 or 2^20 bytes -- within seconds. You do not need to wait for a multi-hour BigCrush run to learn that your generator is broken. This makes PractRand ideal for rapid screening during development.

Second, **scalability.** TestU01's BigCrush tests a fixed amount of data. PractRand can test petabytes. Some generator flaws only become statistically detectable at very large sample sizes -- the deviation from randomness is real but small, and you need enormous amounts of data to distinguish it from sampling noise. PractRand has caught flaws in generators that pass BigCrush cleanly, simply by testing more data than BigCrush ever examines.

The most notable example is **xorshift128+**, which passes TestU01's BigCrush but fails PractRand at around 2^36 bytes (64 gigabytes). The failure is in the low-order bits, which exhibit linear structure that BigCrush's tests are not sensitive to at the sample sizes BigCrush uses, but which PractRand's longer run exposes. This discovery led directly to the development of **xoshiro256++**, which adds a scrambling step to eliminate the low-bit weakness.

PractRand also introduced the concept of **folding** -- testing the output after applying various bit transformations (reversing bits, taking the low half, XORing with shifted copies). Generators that pass "straight" testing sometimes fail when their output is folded, revealing hidden structure in specific bit positions. This technique has since been adopted by other testing frameworks.

## 6. NIST SP 800-22: Regulatory Testing

The **National Institute of Standards and Technology** published **Special Publication 800-22** ("A Statistical Test Suite for Random and Pseudorandom Number Generators for Cryptographic Applications") in 2001, with revisions in 2010. Unlike the previous test suites, NIST SP 800-22 exists in a regulatory context: generators used in cryptographic applications certified under FIPS 140-2 (and its successor FIPS 140-3) must pass these tests.

The suite contains 15 statistical tests:

1. **Frequency (monobit)** -- proportion of 0s and 1s in the entire sequence
2. **Block frequency** -- proportion of 1s within M-bit blocks
3. **Runs** -- total number of uninterrupted runs of identical bits
4. **Longest run of ones** -- longest run of 1s within M-bit blocks
5. **Binary matrix rank** -- rank of disjoint sub-matrices of the sequence
6. **Discrete Fourier transform (spectral)** -- peak heights in the DFT of the sequence
7. **Non-overlapping template matching** -- occurrences of pre-specified target strings
8. **Overlapping template matching** -- number of occurrences of m-bit runs of ones
9. **Maurer's universal statistical test** -- compressibility of the sequence
10. **Linear complexity** -- length of the shortest LFSR that generates the sequence
11. **Serial** -- frequency of all possible 2^m m-bit patterns
12. **Approximate entropy** -- frequency of all possible overlapping m-bit patterns compared to (m+1)-bit patterns
13. **Cumulative sums** -- maximal excursion of the random walk defined by the sequence
14. **Random excursions** -- number of cycles in a random walk having exactly K visits to a particular state
15. **Random excursions variant** -- total number of times a particular state occurs in a random walk

The NIST tests are notable for their **conservatism**. They are designed to certify that a generator is not obviously broken, not to push the boundaries of what can be detected. TestU01's BigCrush is substantially more powerful -- generators can pass NIST SP 800-22 while failing BigCrush. This is by design: the NIST suite is a minimum bar for regulatory compliance, not a research tool.

The regulatory context matters. Hardware random number generators in security modules, smart cards, and cryptographic accelerators must pass NIST SP 800-22 as part of their FIPS certification. The tests are run on startup (power-on self-tests) and periodically during operation (continuous health tests). A failure triggers an alarm condition that disables the generator. This is one of the few contexts where statistical test results have direct operational consequences -- a failed test does not just mean "publish a different paper," it means "this hardware cannot be shipped."

The cryptographic community generally regards NIST SP 800-22 as necessary but not sufficient. A generator intended for cryptographic use should also pass TestU01's BigCrush and PractRand at high data volumes, and -- more importantly -- should have a theoretical security proof or at least a reduction to a well-studied hard problem. Statistical testing can detect *accidental* patterns but cannot detect *deliberately hidden* patterns (backdoors), which is why cryptographic RNG evaluation combines statistical testing with mathematical analysis and sometimes formal verification.

The progression from left to right tells the story of the field. Knuth's tests were the state of the art in 1969; they pass generators that Diehard (1995) exposes as flawed. Diehard passes generators that BigCrush (2007) catches. BigCrush passes generators that PractRand's extended runs occasionally catch. Each new battery was created because someone found a generator that passed all existing tests but was demonstrably flawed.

## 8. How Much Testing Is Enough?

This is the question that has no clean answer, and the honest practitioner must sit with the discomfort.

The fundamental tension is between **practical quality** and **theoretical guarantees**. Statistical testing provides practical quality -- a generator that passes BigCrush and PractRand to 2^45 bytes is, empirically, indistinguishable from true randomness by any known test. But this is not a guarantee. There is no theorem that says "passing these tests implies the generator is good for all applications." There is only the empirical observation that no known application has been damaged by a generator that passes all modern tests.

For most applications, the practical answer is straightforward:

**If your application is not cryptographic and does not involve scientific simulation at extreme scale**, almost any generator above the LCG tier is fine. The difference between MT19937 and PCG64 is real but irrelevant if you are shuffling a playlist, generating test data, or running a game. Use whatever your language provides as its default -- Python's `random` module (MT19937), Rust's `rand` crate (ChaCha12 by default), Go's `math/rand/v2` (PCG or ChaCha8) -- and do not worry about it.

**If your application is scientific simulation (Monte Carlo, stochastic modeling)**, use a generator that passes BigCrush and PractRand. PCG64, xoshiro256++, and SFC64 are all excellent choices. Avoid MT19937 not because it will definitely cause problems, but because better options exist at the same computational cost, and there is no reason to accept known (even minor) statistical flaws when the alternative is free.

**If your application is cryptographic**, statistical testing is necessary but not sufficient. Use a CSPRNG (ChaCha20, AES-CTR, or your OS's entropy source via `/dev/urandom` or `getrandom()`). The security of a CSPRNG rests on computational hardness assumptions, not on statistical test results. A CSPRNG that passes all statistical tests but has a mathematical weakness (say, a short cycle for certain keys) is still broken for cryptographic purposes.

**If your application is gambling or lottery**, regulatory bodies typically require both NIST SP 800-22 certification and independent third-party testing, often using Diehard or TestU01 in addition. The standards are conservative because the stakes are high and the adversaries are motivated.

The deeper question -- when does PRNG quality actually affect simulation results? -- has been studied empirically by Ferrenberg, Landau, and Wong (1992), who demonstrated that the Wolff algorithm for the 2D Ising model produces systematically wrong results when driven by certain linear congruential generators, even though those generators pass all classical statistical tests. The errors are not random noise; they are systematic biases that shift the estimated critical temperature. This result is sobering: it means that there exist real-world computations where generator quality matters in ways that no generic statistical test can predict. The only defense is to use the best generators available, re-run critical simulations with a different generator family as a cross-check, and remain humble about what "passing all tests" actually means.

Knuth, characteristically, had the last word. In the concluding remarks of Chapter 3 of *The Art of Computer Programming*, he wrote:

> *"Random numbers should not be generated with a method chosen at random. Some theory should be used."*

The theory has gotten better since 1969. The tests have gotten better. The generators have gotten better. But the fundamental epistemological problem remains: randomness is a property we can test for but never confirm. Every statistical test is a flashlight illuminating one corner of a vast dark room. The room may be empty, or something may be hiding in a corner we have not yet thought to illuminate. The best we can do -- the best anyone can do -- is to use more flashlights, point them in more directions, and accept that certainty is not available.
