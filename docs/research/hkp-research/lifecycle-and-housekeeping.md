# Lifecycle and Housekeeping: The Care and Feeding of Data

*— on aging, decay, deletion, and the surprising difficulty of forgetting —*

## 1. A Model for the Life of a Datum

The Data Management Association's *DAMA-DMBOK* (first edition 2009, second edition 2017) popularized a staged lifecycle that most enterprise architects still draw on a whiteboard when asked to justify a budget: **creation (or acquisition) → storage → usage → maintenance → archival → disposition**. Each stage has its own cost model, its own failure modes, and its own regulatory exposure. The model is a simplification — real data loops back (an archived record is restored; a tombstoned row is revived by a legal hold) — but it is the common vocabulary that makes retention conversations possible between lawyers, DBAs, and auditors.

Within this frame, practitioners distinguish **active** data (touched in the current transactional window), **dormant** data (rarely read but legally or operationally required to be online), and **archived** data (offline, cold, and slow by design). The cost of each tier, measured in dollars-per-gigabyte-per-month, varies by roughly three orders of magnitude between a hot NVMe pool and a tape library on the Amazon S3 Glacier Deep Archive tier (which Amazon priced at $0.00099/GB/month when it launched in March 2019). That three-decade price gap is why lifecycle management — the craft of moving data between tiers at the right moment — is a discipline at all.

## 2. Retention Is Policy, Not Plumbing

Retention rules are the one corner of data management where the legal department dictates engineering. The **Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002**, enacted after the Enron and WorldCom collapses, imposed a seven-year retention rule on audit workpapers under Section 802 and created criminal liability for their destruction; SOX sits alongside the **Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA)**, whose implementing regulations require covered entities to retain documentation of privacy and security policies for six years. The **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)**, in force since 25 May 2018, inverts the posture: Article 5(1)(e) — the *storage limitation* principle — requires that personal data be kept "no longer than is necessary" for the purposes for which it was collected. The net effect is that a modern enterprise must simultaneously keep some classes of data for a fixed minimum and delete other classes at a fixed maximum, and the two policies cannot share a schema.

The "keep everything forever" temptation — which fell out of the Hadoop-era conviction that storage was free and schema could be deferred — turns out to be expensive along axes that do not appear on the invoice. Every byte retained is a byte that must be backed up, replicated, indexed, discovered in litigation, scanned for PII, and re-encrypted when keys rotate. It is also a byte of **breach blast radius**: the 2017 Equifax disclosure put 147 million records into the public record in part because historical data had been consolidated into one queryable surface. The dark total cost of retention is the sum of the storage bill, the compliance tooling, and the probability-weighted cost of a breach conditional on the data existing at all.

## 3. MVCC, Bloat, and the Uber Debate

A relational database under **multi-version concurrency control (MVCC)** never quite overwrites a row. PostgreSQL, whose MVCC implementation dates to the Berkeley POSTGRES project of the late 1980s (Stonebraker and Rowe, 1986), turns every `UPDATE` into an `INSERT` of a new tuple version plus an invalidation of the old one. Readers at older snapshots can still see the dead version; the dead versions accumulate as **bloat**. PostgreSQL's `VACUUM` process reclaims that space. `VACUUM` (introduced early and generalized into the **autovacuum** daemon in PostgreSQL 8.1, November 2005) marks dead tuples as free for reuse; `VACUUM FULL` rewrites the table to compact it, at the cost of an `ACCESS EXCLUSIVE` lock; `VACUUM ANALYZE` additionally refreshes the planner's statistics.

The pathology is well-documented. A long-running transaction holds the xmin horizon back, autovacuum cannot clean newer dead tuples, and the table silently grows. In a widely read 2016 blog post, Uber's engineering team explained their migration from PostgreSQL to MySQL by naming write amplification from index updates on every tuple version, replication fragility under logical streaming, and the operational burden of vacuum tuning as primary drivers. The post provoked a substantial rebuttal from the PostgreSQL community — Robert Haas, Christophe Pettus, and Markus Winand each published responses in late 2016 — and ultimately motivated the **heap-only tuple (HOT) updates**, **zheap** experiments, and **freeze map** improvements that landed through the PostgreSQL 9.6–14 cycle. The episode remains the canonical example of the principle that in an MVCC store, deletion is never free, and *not deleting on time* is the most expensive form of deferred work.

## 4. Compaction: The LSM Bargain

Log-structured merge (LSM) trees — formalized by O'Neil, Cheng, Gawlick, and O'Neil in 1996 — trade read and space amplification for sequential, write-friendly I/O. A write lands in a memtable and is flushed to an immutable sorted file; over time, background **compaction** merges files to maintain ordering invariants. **RocksDB**, Facebook's 2012 fork of Google's LevelDB, offers **leveled compaction** (narrow levels, low space amplification, high write amplification), **tiered / universal compaction** (fewer merges, higher space amplification), and an **FIFO** style for time-series workloads. The engineer who tunes a RocksDB instance is really choosing a point on the three-way trade between read amplification, write amplification, and space amplification — the "RAF triangle" that Mark Callaghan repeatedly diagrammed on the MySQL@Facebook blog between 2013 and 2018.

Compaction is how LSM stores pay the housekeeping tax that relational MVCC stores pay through vacuum: in both cases, writes create dead bytes, and a background process must reclaim them before they smother steady-state performance. Compaction's cruelty is that it competes for the same disk bandwidth as foreground writes, and tuning it badly produces a feedback loop in which falling behind on merges inflates read amplification, which inflates latency, which induces more queueing, which starves compaction further.

## 5. Tombstones, Soft Deletes, and Zombie Data

**Apache Cassandra** (2008, open-sourced by Facebook via the original Dynamo-inspired design) does not delete rows; it writes **tombstones**. A tombstone is a marker that a cell or row has been deleted, propagated through the normal write path, and respected by read queries via a merge on the read side. The tombstone lives until `gc_grace_seconds` (default 864,000 — ten days) has elapsed on every replica, at which point compaction may drop it. The design gracefully handles delete-versus-write conflicts in an eventually consistent ring. It punishes workloads that delete heavily: partitions full of tombstones slow reads, because every query must scan and discard them, and a delete-then-re-insert pattern can produce **zombie data** — deleted rows reappearing when a replica that missed the delete comes back online after the grace period.

**Kafka log compaction** — introduced around Kafka 0.8.1 (March 2014) — applies a similar idea to a commit log. A *tombstone* is a record with a null value for a given key; after compaction, only the latest value per key survives, so a tombstone effectively deletes the key. Log compaction is the substrate of many CDC and event-sourced pipelines precisely because it lets a Kafka topic act like a compactable materialized view.

**Soft deletes** in an application schema — a `deleted_at` column, a `status = 'archived'` flag — share the same pathology. The row is out of sight but on disk; every index still carries it; every query must filter it; and over months the "deleted" percentage of a table can exceed the "live" percentage. Soft delete is a useful pattern, but it is a *deferral*, not a substitute, for real housekeeping.

## 6. Backup, Restore, and the Disaster Recovery Gap

The **3-2-1 rule**, attributed to photographer Peter Krogh in his 2005 *The DAM Book: Digital Asset Management for Photographers* and widely propagated by the US-CERT advisory channel afterwards, prescribes three copies of the data, on two different media, with one copy off-site. It survived into the cloud era because the constraint it encodes — *independent failure domains* — is architectural, not media-specific. The discipline maps onto full / incremental / differential schedules, onto **recovery point objective (RPO)** and **recovery time objective (RTO)** SLAs, and onto **point-in-time recovery (PITR)** capabilities that write-ahead-log-based databases have offered since the 1990s.

An untested backup is the canonical example of a silent failure. The aphorism — sometimes called **Schrödinger's backup** — that *the condition of any backup is unknown until a restore is attempted* has become industry shorthand for the discipline of running regular restore drills. The **2017 GitLab.com incident** of 31 January, in which a tired operator ran `rm -rf` on the wrong replica and every one of five backup mechanisms turned out to be broken or empty, produced a blameless public postmortem that is still cited in SRE curricula as the empirical argument for periodic restore testing.

Disaster recovery is a superset of backup. DR tiers run from **pilot light** (minimal infrastructure kept warm in a second region, scaled up during a disaster), through **warm standby** (always-on but under-provisioned), to **multi-site active-active** (full capacity in every region, ready to absorb load immediately). The **2017 AWS S3 us-east-1 outage** on 28 February — in which a debugging command removed a larger-than-intended set of index subsystem servers — took down a significant slice of the public internet and taught the industry two lessons simultaneously: regional single-points-of-failure exist even in "the cloud," and blast radius is a design variable, not an emergent property.

## 7. Immutability, Versioning, and the Right to be Forgotten

A parallel tradition holds that the safest data is data you never change. **Git**, released by Linus Torvalds in April 2005, is the canonical modern example: every object is content-addressed, history is append-only, and "delete" is really a pointer move that leaves the old commits reachable via the reflog. **Nathan Marz's Lambda Architecture**, introduced in a 2011 blog post and expanded in the 2015 Manning book *Big Data* (Marz and Warren), built an entire processing model on an immutable master dataset plus derived views. **Event sourcing**, popularized by Greg Young from 2006 onward, stores each state change as an immutable event and derives current state by folding over the log. **Write-Once-Read-Many (WORM)** compliance storage — from IBM's 3995 optical jukeboxes in the 1990s through modern S3 Object Lock (launched November 2018) — enforces immutability in hardware or policy for regulated workloads.

Immutable architectures collide with **GDPR Article 17**, the right to erasure ("the right to be forgotten"). If the master dataset is append-only and the derived views are regenerated from it, how does one delete a natural person's records on request? Three main workarounds have emerged. **Crypto-shredding** — encrypting each subject's data under a subject-specific key and deleting the key to render the ciphertext unreadable — makes deletion a key-management operation, which is orders of magnitude cheaper than rewriting storage. **Compaction with redaction** rewrites an immutable log to remove a subject's events, at the cost of breaking hash chains. **Machine unlearning** is an active research area (Bourtoule et al., *SISA Training: Machine Unlearning*, IEEE S&P 2021) that aims to remove a training example's influence from a trained model without retraining from scratch — still mostly academic, but tracked carefully by anyone running large-scale inference over personal data.

## 8. Deduplication and Compression

Not all lifecycle reduction comes from deletion. **Deduplication** removes redundancy between files or blocks without the application knowing. **Content-defined chunking** — built on Michael Rabin's 1981 paper *Fingerprinting by Random Polynomials* — lets a backup system split files at content-sensitive boundaries so that insertions near the beginning of a file do not shift every chunk. The commercial high-water mark was **Data Domain's** inline deduplicating appliances (founded 2001, acquired by EMC in 2009 for $2.4 billion). In open source, **ZFS** (Sun, 2005) offered block-level dedup early, at the cost of a memory-hungry dedup table; **borg backup** (2015, a fork of Attic) and **restic** (Alexander Neumann, 2015) made content-defined chunking with encryption the default for personal backups. The perennial trade-off is dedup ratio against CPU and memory cost: ZFS dedup's reputation for RAM hunger cooled its adoption for a decade until persistent memory and better hashing revived it.

**Compression** stacks on top. Lempel–Ziv derivatives — `gzip` (Gailly & Adler, 1992), `lz4` (Yann Collet, 2011), and especially `zstd` (Collet again, released at Facebook in 2015 and standardized as RFC 8478 in October 2018) — have reshaped the space-vs-CPU frontier. Google's **Brotli** (2015) optimized for static web content; columnar formats like **Apache Parquet** (2013) and **Apache ORC** (2013) pair dictionary encoding, run-length encoding, and page-level compression to deliver 5–20× shrinkage on analytic workloads. Zstandard's decade of dominance — since roughly 2015 — stems from its decoupling of compression ratio from decompression speed, which broke the old assumption that better ratios always meant slower reads.

## 9. Archival and the Long Now

Archival is a different problem from backup. Backup asks, *can I restore yesterday?* Archival asks, *can I read this in thirty years?* **Bit rot** — the slow accumulation of silent flips in storage media — is real: a 2007 CERN study famously measured 22 corrupt files in 8.7 TB after three weeks of writes. The response is **checksum-based integrity** (ZFS `scrub`, Btrfs data checksums, S3 object integrity, par2 parity files) plus **periodic migration** onto new media before old media become unreadable. The **LOCKSS** project at Stanford (*Lots of Copies Keep Stuff Safe*, launched 1999 by Vicky Reich and David Rosenthal) built an entire preservation model around peer-to-peer replication and continuous integrity voting among libraries. Stewart Brand's *The Clock of the Long Now* (1999) and the associated Long Now Foundation reframe archival as a ten-thousand-year design problem: every storage format is a bet on future decoders, and the only hedge is migration.

## 10. The Cost of Actually Deleting

It is a cliché among database engineers that **delete is the most expensive operation**. A single `DELETE` in a relational database may cascade through foreign keys, fire triggers, update every secondary index, write WAL records, fragment B-tree pages, escalate locks, and produce MVCC dead tuples that VACUUM must later clean. This is why `TRUNCATE TABLE` is so much faster — it skips the row-by-row journey in favor of a metadata swap — and why "archive table" patterns (move rows to a sibling table, then drop it) often outperform straight deletes at scale. On distributed stores the cost multiplies: Cassandra's tombstones, DynamoDB's partition-level sweeps, BigQuery's time-travel retention (seven days by default), and Iceberg's snapshot expiration all turn delete into a *scheduled background cost* rather than an instantaneous event.

The moral is that a lifecycle policy is only honest if it accounts for the *work* of transitioning between stages, not only the resting cost at each stage. Creation is cheap; storage is cheap; usage is cheap; *transition* — compaction, vacuum, promotion, archival migration, deletion, crypto-shred, backup verification — is where the budget goes, and it is precisely this transition work that most teams underfund until something breaks. The care and feeding of data, in the end, is the work of paying those taxes on time.
