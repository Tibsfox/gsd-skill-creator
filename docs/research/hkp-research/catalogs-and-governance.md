# Catalogs, Lineage, and Governance: Making Data Accountable
*—from "I know we have this somewhere" to contracts, provenance, and the slow birth of data as a product—*

## 1. The Discoverability Problem

Every organization that has ever crossed the threshold from one database to several has discovered the same embarrassing truth: the hardest question in data is not *how do we query it?* but *where is it?* The marketing analyst asks for last quarter's regional breakdown. Someone built that report. Someone exported it. Someone emailed a CSV to a director, who pasted it into a slide deck. Three months later nobody can reproduce the number. The Census of Marine Life, a ten-year international effort that catalogued roughly 250,000 marine species between 2000 and 2010, spent much of its final years grappling with this very problem at planetary scale — making sure datasets assembled by hundreds of institutions could be found, cited, and reused after the program ended, rather than evaporating into retired filesystems and orphaned spreadsheets. Studies of research data loss have repeatedly found that datasets become effectively unrecoverable within a handful of years of creation: storage media fail, file formats age out, authors change affiliations, and the person who knew what `column_17` meant retires. Timothy Vines' 2014 *Current Biology* study of 516 ecology papers found that the odds of datasets being retrievable dropped by roughly 17% per year after publication. The scientist who cannot find her own data after five years is not a cartoon; she is the modal case.

Inside enterprises the same story plays out with different nouns. Gartner's perennial surveys on analytics productivity estimate that data workers spend something like 30-40% of their time simply locating and validating data before any analysis can begin. That is a staggering tax on velocity, and it falls most heavily on the knowledge workers organizations are paying the most to think. The governance layer — the catalog, the lineage graph, the quality test, the contract, the provenance record — exists to collapse that tax. It answers three simple questions: *where is the data, where did it come from, and can we trust it?*

## 2. A Short History of Data Catalogs

The first data catalogs were not called data catalogs. They were "data dictionaries," and they grew out of the operational need to keep hundreds of COBOL copybooks and CICS transactions from drifting apart. IBM's DB/DC Data Dictionary, released in the mid-1970s, and MSP's DATAMANAGER from around the same period were early entrants. Through the 1980s and 1990s the category broadened into metadata "repositories" — Platinum Technology's Repository (acquired by Computer Associates in 1999 and rebranded CA Repository), Rochade (from R&O, later acquired by ASG and then Rocket Software), and CA's Datacom-based dictionary products. These systems stored column definitions, code-set values, owner contacts, and cross-references, and they were invariably a chore to maintain. Human curators typed. Humans lied. The repository drifted out of sync with the databases it was supposed to describe within weeks.

The open-source renaissance in the 2010s changed the economics. At LinkedIn, the WhereHows project (open-sourced in 2016) became the seed of DataHub, released in 2019 under the Apache License and still actively developed today. At Lyft, engineers built Amundsen and open-sourced it in October 2019; its name is a nod to Roald Amundsen, the Norwegian explorer, and its promise was Google-for-your-warehouse. Apache Atlas, incubated within the Hadoop ecosystem and graduated to a top-level Apache project in 2017, offered a metadata and governance framework with native Hive and Kafka integration. Netflix released Metacat. Uber released Databook internally. Marquez came out of WeWork's data platform team in 2018, focused specifically on lineage. The commercial wave arrived in parallel: Collibra (founded 2008 in Brussels), Alation (founded 2012 in Redwood City), Informatica Enterprise Data Catalog, Atlan (founded 2019), Secoda, Castor, Select Star. Where the 1990s data dictionaries had been stodgy reference stores curated by hand, the new catalogs pulled metadata automatically from source systems, parsed query logs to infer usage, scraped BI tools for popularity signals, and presented the result as a searchable web app that looked more like GitHub than like a mainframe utility.

## 3. FAIR and the Scientific Data Community

The clearest normative articulation of what a catalog is *for* came not from industry but from the open-science community. In March 2016, Mark D. Wilkinson and forty-nine co-authors published "The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management and stewardship" in *Nature Scientific Data*. The paper defined four properties every scientific dataset ought to have: Findable (with a globally unique, persistent identifier and rich machine-readable metadata), Accessible (retrievable through a standard, open protocol, with clear authentication and authorization rules), Interoperable (encoded in formal, shared, broadly applicable languages and vocabularies), and Reusable (richly described with a clear usage license and detailed provenance). FAIR was not a technology; it was a contract between dataset producers and the future. The European Open Science Cloud and the GO FAIR initiative pushed the principles across disciplines, and within a few years FAIR vocabulary had bled into enterprise vendor marketing — sometimes honestly, sometimes not.

## 4. Data Lineage: Where Did This Column Come From?

Lineage is the causal graph of data. At the coarsest granularity it answers "which tables are upstream of this dashboard?"; at the finest, "which character in which source column contributed to this cell?" Historically lineage was documented in Visio diagrams that were wrong before the ink dried. Automated lineage extraction — parsing SQL, reading ETL job definitions, instrumenting runtime engines — became the only viable approach once warehouses grew past a few hundred tables. The OpenLineage specification, launched in 2021 under the stewardship of the LF AI & Data Foundation, standardized the wire format for lineage events: each job run emits a structured record describing inputs, outputs, and the code that transformed one into the other. Marquez became the reference implementation, and integrations landed for Airflow, dbt, Spark, Flink, and Great Expectations.

The dbt revolution is worth a paragraph of its own. When Fishtown Analytics (now dbt Labs) released dbt in 2016, it treated SQL transformations as version-controlled code with explicit `ref()` and `source()` macros. Because dbt knows every `ref()`, it can compute the full project DAG for free, rendering it as an interactive graph. Lineage became a side effect of writing transformations correctly, rather than a separate documentation effort. Analytics engineers across the industry discovered that they wanted lineage so badly they were willing to rewrite their entire transformation layer to get it. DataHub and Atlan import dbt manifests directly; lineage is transitive across warehouse, transformation, and BI tool.

## 5. Provenance and W3C PROV

Where lineage is usually a pragmatic engineering artifact, provenance is its philosophically rigorous sibling. The W3C Provenance Working Group spent 2011 and 2012 producing a family of recommendations published in April 2013: PROV-DM (the data model), PROV-O (the OWL ontology), PROV-N (a human-readable notation), and companion documents. The model is built on three core classes — *Entity*, *Activity*, and *Agent* — linked by relations like `wasGeneratedBy`, `used`, `wasAttributedTo`, `wasDerivedFrom`, and `wasAssociatedWith`. Entities are the things whose history we care about (a dataset, a report, a decision). Activities are processes acting on entities. Agents are the parties responsible for activities. The schema is simple enough to fit on an index card and expressive enough to capture research pipelines, legal chains of custody, or the internals of a data warehouse. PROV-O has become the lingua franca of reproducible-research infrastructure, feeding systems like CWLProv, ProvStore, and the provenance tracks of Jupyter and Snakemake.

## 6. Data Quality: The Six Dimensions

Most practitioners converge on six dimensions of data quality: *accuracy* (does the value match reality?), *completeness* (are required attributes present?), *consistency* (do values agree across systems?), *timeliness* (is the data fresh enough for its use?), *validity* (does it conform to defined rules?), and *uniqueness* (are entities represented exactly once?). DAMA International's DMBOK and the DAMA UK working paper on data quality both enumerate these, and they have been rediscovered under slightly different names by every practitioner who ever wrote a retrospective. The truism "data quality is a journey, not a destination" means something specific: quality is not a state you achieve and bank; it is a property of an ongoing negotiation between producers, consumers, and reality. Software makes this negotiation testable.

Great Expectations, released as open source by Superconductive in 2018, reframed quality as *expectation suites* — declarative assertions attached to columns ("this column must be non-null", "the 99th percentile must be under 5000", "values must match this regex"). Failed expectations produce a failing run and a "data doc" humans can read. dbt added its own `tests:` blocks around the same time (unique, not_null, accepted_values, relationships), initially crude but now supplemented by the dbt-expectations and dbt-utils packages. Soda Core arrived with SodaCL, a YAML-based quality DSL. Commercial observability vendors — Monte Carlo (founded 2019), Acceldata, Databand (acquired by IBM in 2022), Bigeye, Anomalo — layered ML-driven anomaly detection on top, watching freshness, volume, schema, distribution, and lineage for surprises the engineers had not thought to test for. "Data observability" became the umbrella term, consciously echoing the SRE world's observability pillars.

## 7. Schemas, Registries, and Breaking Changes

Every time a producer changes a schema, consumers break. The confluence of Kafka, Avro, and microservices made this problem operationally urgent, and Confluent's Schema Registry (first released in 2015) became the canonical solution in that ecosystem. The registry stores schemas by subject, assigns versions, and enforces a configurable *compatibility mode*: BACKWARD (new schema can read old data), FORWARD (old schema can read new data), FULL (both), or NONE. Producers negotiate with the registry before publishing; incompatible changes fail loudly at development time rather than silently at 3 a.m. Red Hat's Apicurio Registry offers a similar feature set for Avro, Protobuf, JSON Schema, OpenAPI, and AsyncAPI. Buf Schema Registry serves Protobuf with Git-native semantics. The common thread is that schema evolution is a conversation with defined rules, not an unannounced change shoved into production.

## 8. Data Contracts

In 2022 the term *data contract* went from obscure jargon to industry conference headline. Chad Sanderson — then at Convoy, later founder of Gable — wrote a series of widely-circulated essays arguing that the implicit handshake between data producers (backend engineers) and data consumers (analysts, ML engineers) had failed at scale, and that the fix was to make the handshake explicit. A data contract is a versioned, machine-readable document declaring schema, semantics, freshness SLAs, ownership, and change-management rules, enforced in CI so that breaking changes fail pull requests instead of dashboards. PayPal open-sourced its Data Contract Template. Bitol, a Linux Foundation sandbox project, published an Open Data Contract Standard (ODCS). Snowflake and Databricks both shipped first-party contract features in 2023 and 2024. The movement is young, but its philosophical claim is simple and hard to argue with: if we expect APIs between services to be contracts, we should expect data between teams to be contracts too.

## 9. Data Mesh and Federated Governance

In May 2019, Zhamak Dehghani published "How to Move Beyond a Monolithic Data Lake to a Distributed Data Mesh" on Martin Fowler's website. The post reframed the data warehouse debate through the lens of domain-driven design, arguing for four principles: domain-oriented decentralized data ownership, data-as-a-product, self-serve data infrastructure as a platform, and federated computational governance. Her 2022 O'Reilly book *Data Mesh: Delivering Data-Driven Value at Scale* expanded the argument. The reception split the industry. Enthusiasts saw a long-overdue organizational pattern; skeptics pointed out that most companies did not have enough platform maturity to hand every domain team the burden of shipping production data products. The truth, as usual, was a messy middle: the language of *data as a product* stuck, the language of *federated governance* became the honest alternative to the central data team bottleneck, and the actual adoption looked less like a revolution and more like a gradual re-labeling of decentralization efforts already in progress.

## 10. Master Data Management and the Canonical Record

Meanwhile, a quieter decades-long struggle continued. Master Data Management (MDM) is the discipline of maintaining a single, canonical version of the entities that appear everywhere in an enterprise: customers, products, suppliers, locations, employees. The match-and-merge problem — deciding that *Robert Smith, 123 Main St* and *Bob Smith, 123 Main Street Apt. 2* are the same person — has occupied rule engines, probabilistic matchers, and (lately) machine-learning models for thirty years. Informatica MDM (descended from Siperian, acquired 2010) and IBM InfoSphere MDM are the established enterprise players; Reltio launched a cloud-native alternative in 2011; Tamr, co-founded in 2013 by Michael Stonebraker, took a machine-learning-first approach to entity resolution at scale. MDM is not glamorous, but without it a company cannot answer "how many customers do we have?" with a straight face.

## 11. Governance Frameworks and the Chief Data Officer

DAMA International's *Data Management Body of Knowledge* (DMBOK), first published in 2009 and revised as DMBOK2 in 2017, is the closest thing the field has to a shared textbook. It organizes the practice into eleven knowledge areas, with data governance sitting as the hub of the wheel. The Data Governance Institute, founded in 2003 by Gwen Thomas, published a complementary framework focused on roles, responsibilities, and decision rights. Both predate GDPR, but the May 2018 enforcement of GDPR, followed by CCPA in 2020, turned governance from a best practice into a legal requirement with nine-figure fines attached. The Chief Data Officer role — essentially unknown before Capital One appointed the first one in 2002 — spread to roughly two-thirds of Fortune 1000 companies by the early 2020s. CDOs are the organizational admission that someone needs to own the catalog, the contracts, the policies, and the conversations those artifacts enable.

## 12. Discovery in the Age of Embeddings

By 2023 a new discovery pattern had emerged: embedding table descriptions, column comments, sample values, and query history into a vector space and retrieving them with semantic search. "Find the table about customer churn in the European region" stopped requiring an exact keyword match. DataHub, Atlan, Select Star, and Secoda all shipped LLM-powered assistants that combined natural-language retrieval with schema-aware SQL generation. The limitation was the quality of the underlying metadata — an embedding cannot rescue a table whose columns are named `col1` through `col37` — which quietly made the catalog market the most direct beneficiary of the LLM wave: suddenly everyone cared about documentation quality because documentation fed a chatbot that executives demoed.

## 13. The Governance-versus-Velocity Tension

Rigid governance kills experimentation: if every new dataset needs six weeks of stewardship review before an analyst can look at it, the analyst will simply export to Excel and nobody will ever see the result again. No governance at all creates compliance liabilities and nobody can find anything. The resolution — still being worked out — is the DataOps and Data Mesh response: treat governance as code, put contracts in Git, let federated domain teams own their data products, and invest in a central platform that makes *doing it right* the path of least resistance. This is the same bargain SRE made with reliability (error budgets rather than gates) and that security made with DevSecOps (shift-left rather than phase-gate). The catalog, the lineage graph, the quality test, the contract, and the provenance record are not obstacles to velocity; they are the scaffolding that lets velocity exist without the organization forgetting what it knows.

## 14. The Accountable Layer

The common thread across every tool in this document is accountability. A data catalog makes the existence of a dataset accountable — there is an entry, with an owner, a description, and a steward. A lineage graph makes its derivation accountable — there is a chain of transformations from source to consumer, inspectable and re-executable. A provenance record makes its history accountable — there is a PROV-style story of who did what, when, and with which tools. A quality test makes its correctness accountable — there is a passing or failing assertion, logged on every run. A contract makes its interface accountable — there is a versioned agreement between producer and consumer, enforced in CI. A governance framework makes the whole system accountable to law, policy, and reason. None of these pieces is new. The data dictionaries of the 1970s attempted the same job with punched-card tools and human discipline, and failed at the human-discipline step. What changed is that the metadata is now extracted automatically, the tests run automatically, the lineage updates automatically, and the people involved get to focus on the interesting parts of the negotiation. The bits still need to be stored somewhere, still need to be retrieved quickly, still need to be compressed and backed up and encrypted. But above all the bits sits this thin, stubborn, absolutely load-bearing layer whose only job is to ensure that when someone asks *where is the data, where did it come from, and can we trust it?* — the answer exists.
