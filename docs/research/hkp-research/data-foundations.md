# Data Foundations: What We Are Managing

*From Shannon's bits to schema.org --- the quiet question of what the noun "data" actually refers to when we say we are managing it*

---

## 1. The Slippery Noun

Before anyone can speak sensibly about storing, indexing, retrieving, or governing data, they have to answer a question that the working vocabulary of the field tends to skip: what, exactly, *is* data? The word is so embedded in the infrastructure of contemporary life --- data center, data lake, data scientist, data protection --- that it has come to feel like a natural kind, as unproblematic as "water" or "stone." But the moment one looks closely, the noun splinters. Sometimes it means the raw bytes on a disk. Sometimes it means the rows in a table. Sometimes it means the numbers a sensor emits before they are cleaned. Sometimes it means the cleaned, modeled, annotated product that ends up in a report. Sometimes, colloquially, it means facts, evidence, even knowledge itself. A housekeeping discipline that treats all of these as the same thing will fail, quietly and expensively, because it will apply file-system tools to knowledge problems and knowledge tools to file-system problems. The starting point of any honest account of data management is therefore a deliberate disambiguation.

The most widely cited attempt at that disambiguation in the information-science literature is the **DIKW pyramid** --- the model that arranges Data, Information, Knowledge, and Wisdom in rising layers, with each level supposedly derived from the one below. The hierarchy is older than it looks. Its modern articulation in management theory is usually traced to **Milan Zeleny**'s 1987 paper *Management Support Systems: Towards Integrated Knowledge Management*, published in *Human Systems Management* volume 7, which proposed a progression in which knowledge was not, in Zeleny's memorable phrase, "the processing of information" but "a coordination of action." Russell Ackoff gave the pyramid its popular form in his 1989 presidential address to the International Society for General Systems Research, "From Data to Wisdom," published in the *Journal of Applied Systems Analysis*. Ackoff added a fifth layer, understanding, between knowledge and wisdom, though later retellings usually drop it. The most rigorous examination of the pyramid --- and the reason any serious discussion of it still begins there --- is **Jennifer Rowley**'s 2007 review article "The Wisdom Hierarchy: Representations of the DIKW Hierarchy," published in the *Journal of Information Science* volume 33, issue 2, pages 163 to 180. Rowley surveyed the treatment of the hierarchy across dozens of widely used information-management textbooks and concluded that while there was broad consensus that each level was somehow "built on" the one below, there was almost no consensus about what the transformation between levels actually consisted of. Data became information, everyone agreed, through the addition of context; information became knowledge through the addition of meaning or experience; knowledge became wisdom through the addition of judgment. But the verbs --- "context," "meaning," "experience," "judgment" --- were themselves undefined, and the pyramid turned out to be less a theory than a mnemonic.

Rowley's critique matters for the practical work of data management because it exposes why naive definitions of data as "raw facts" are so leaky. The "raw" in that phrase is doing almost all of the work, and it never survives contact with reality. Every measurement is already theory-laden --- a thermometer reading presumes a unit, a scale, an ambient pressure, a calibration procedure, an instrument that was designed to measure temperature and not something else. What looks like "just data" at one layer of a system is always structured information at the layer below. The DIKW pyramid is a useful intuition pump so long as one treats its levels as *roles* that bytes play in different contexts, not as intrinsic properties of the bytes themselves.

## 2. Shannon and the Formal Sense of "Information"

If there is one moment when the word "information" acquired a formal, mathematical meaning, it is the publication of **Claude E. Shannon**'s "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" in the *Bell System Technical Journal* in July and October 1948. Shannon was not trying to build a philosophy of knowledge. He was trying to solve an engineering problem: how much signal could be pushed through a noisy channel before errors swamped the message? His answer --- a quantity he called *entropy*, measured in a unit he called the *bit* (a coinage John Tukey had suggested to him), defined as the negative logarithm of probability summed over the symbols of a source --- turned out to be one of the most consequential definitions of the twentieth century. For Shannon, information was the resolution of uncertainty. A message carried more information the more it surprised you. This was utterly divorced from meaning, and Shannon said so explicitly: "semantic aspects of communication are irrelevant to the engineering problem." The quantity he defined measured only the statistical structure of the symbols, not what those symbols were *about*.

This is an indispensable distinction for data management. Shannon gave the field a precise, measurable notion of information in the signal-theoretic sense, and that notion underwrites everything from compression algorithms to database page layouts to the channel budgets of the deep-space network carrying Artemis II telemetry back from the Moon. But Shannon's information is not the same thing as the *semantic* information carried in a patient record, a bank transaction, or a scientific measurement. Semantic content lives at a different level, and the confusion of the two --- treating Shannon-information as if it settled questions about meaning --- has caused endless trouble in popular writing about data. When this document, or any of the eight that follow it in the housekeeping series, talks about "information," it will try to say which sense is meant: the Shannon sense of measurable surprise, or the semantic sense of propositional content about the world.

## 3. Structured, Semi-Structured, Unstructured

The most common practical taxonomy of data divides it into structured, semi-structured, and unstructured, and every term in that division deserves scrutiny. **Structured data** is the oldest and most tractable category. Its canonical form is the relation: a fixed-schema table of tuples, each tuple a row, each column a strongly typed attribute. The relational model was defined by **Edgar F. Codd** in his 1970 *Communications of the ACM* paper "A Relational Model of Data for Large Shared Data Banks," a paper that would win him the Turing Award in 1981 and anchor the entire commercial database industry from System R in 1974 through Oracle, DB2, PostgreSQL, and beyond. Structured data has the property that its shape is known in advance; the schema is a contract the data must satisfy before it is allowed into the store. This is called **schema-on-write**, and it is the source of both the relational model's reliability and its rigidity.

**Unstructured data** is a more recent coinage, and it is an actively misleading one. The term became widespread in the 2000s as enterprises confronted the fact that most of the bytes they were accumulating --- email, memos, contracts, PDFs, images, audio, video --- did not fit neatly into rows and columns. The industry analyst **Seth Grimes** crystallized the critique in his 2008 Breakthrough Analysis column "Unstructured Data and the 80 Percent Rule," which traced the ubiquitous claim that "80 percent of enterprise data is unstructured" back to a Merrill Lynch report that itself had no primary citation, and argued that the figure was folk wisdom rather than research. Grimes's deeper point was that the label "unstructured" was a category mistake. A Word document has structure --- paragraphs, sentences, headings, font runs, revision history. A JPEG has structure --- markers, quantization tables, Huffman trees, pixel blocks. A spoken sentence has prosodic, phonemic, lexical, and syntactic structure all the way down. What these artifacts lack is *relational* structure, meaning structure that a SQL query planner can exploit. Calling them unstructured is an admission of the tools' limits, not a property of the data. The more accurate framing, which has gradually replaced the old trichotomy in careful writing, is that data exists along a spectrum of *schema rigidity*, from fixed-schema relations at one end through self-describing records like JSON and XML in the middle to media and free text at the other end, where structure is present but must be recovered from the content rather than assumed from a declared schema.

**Semi-structured data** sits in the middle. It carries its own schema inline --- JSON objects are trees of named fields, XML elements are tagged, Protocol Buffers ship with a compiled descriptor, Apache Avro writes its schema into every file header, Apache Parquet records a schema per column group. This family of formats traces a lineage from SGML in the 1980s through XML (W3C Recommendation in February 1998) to JSON (formalized by Douglas Crockford around 2001 and ratified as ECMA-404 in 2013) and then into the binary serialization formats that now dominate data engineering: Google's Protocol Buffers (open-sourced in 2008), Apache Avro (born from the Hadoop project around 2009), and Parquet (a joint Twitter--Cloudera project released in 2013). Each of these formats picks a slightly different place on the spectrum between schema-on-write discipline and schema-on-read flexibility. Parquet and Avro effectively bring relational-style schemas back to the world of distributed files; JSON gives up schema discipline almost entirely in exchange for effortless interoperability.

The **schema-on-read** label, popularized in the data-lake era around 2010 by vendors like Cloudera and MapR, describes the alternative philosophy: store the data as it arrives, impose a schema only at query time when a specific consumer needs one. Pandas DataFrames in Python work this way in practice, as do most analytical notebooks --- the columns acquire their types when someone reads them, not when they are written. Schema-on-read is flexible and forgiving; it is also a reliable source of the silent data-quality failures that haunt analytics pipelines, because mistakes in the data are not caught until long after the data was produced.

## 4. Records, Tuples, Objects, Documents: What a "Thing" Is

Different data models disagree about what the smallest addressable "thing" is. In the relational model it is the **tuple**, a row in a table, uniquely identified by a primary key. In the object-oriented tradition that rose in the 1980s with Smalltalk and C++ and reached the database world in the 1990s through systems like ObjectStore and GemStone, the addressable thing is the **object**, an encapsulated bundle of state and behavior referred to by an object identifier. In the document model that came in with CouchDB (2005) and MongoDB (2009), the thing is a **document**, a self-describing tree of nested fields usually stored as BSON or JSON. In a key-value store it is a value bound to a key. In a graph database like Neo4j (first released 2007) it is a node or an edge.

These models disagree about identity. Codd's relational model privileged the **natural key** --- some combination of the real-world attributes of the thing, such as a social security number or an ISBN, that uniquely identified it. Practical relational design quickly discovered that natural keys were fragile: they changed, they had duplicates, they were sometimes absent, and they leaked business information into places it did not belong. Surrogate keys --- synthetic integers or UUIDs with no meaning outside the database --- became the normal practice for anything larger than a toy schema. The tradeoff is well known: natural keys are meaningful but unstable; surrogate keys are stable but meaningless, and they push the problem of cross-system identity out into the application layer, where it reappears as the problem of **record linkage**.

## 5. Naming Is Load-Bearing

The best-known joke in computer science is usually attributed to **Phil Karlton**, who worked on Netscape Navigator in the 1990s, and it runs: "There are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things." Martin Fowler, who popularized the quote on his *bliki*, admits he could never find an authoritative first citation, and the earliest dated sightings on the open web are in Tim Bray's blog around 1996 or 1997; Karlton's own family, in a 2017 post on karlton.org, recall him saying it earlier than that. The exact provenance is less important than why the joke has stuck. Naming is load-bearing in a way that is hard to see until it fails. A name is the handle by which every other piece of machinery in a system touches a thing. If the name is wrong --- ambiguous, unstable, collision-prone, locale-specific, unintelligible to a future reader --- every cache, index, reference, log entry, and backup that mentions it inherits that wrongness. Naming is where epistemology meets engineering, because a name is an implicit claim that two mentions of the same string refer to the same thing, and that claim is the foundation on which distributed systems, databases, and even ordinary file systems rest.

The web's confrontation with naming produced the **URI / URL / URN** distinction codified in Tim Berners-Lee, Roy Fielding, and Larry Masinter's RFC 3986 of January 2005 (which replaced the earlier RFC 2396 of 1998 and, before that, RFC 1738 of 1994). A URI is a Uniform Resource *Identifier*; a URL is a URI that also tells you how to *locate* the thing named; a URN is a URI that names a thing without committing to any particular location. The distinction matters because a great deal of data management pain comes from conflating identification with location. When files move, URLs break, but URNs --- and their modern descendants, content-addressed identifiers --- do not. **Git** (released by Linus Torvalds in April 2005) and **IPFS** (Juan Benet's 2014 whitepaper, first implementation in 2015) both took the more radical step of naming objects by the cryptographic hash of their content. Content addressing collapses identification and integrity: if two bytes have the same hash they are the same bytes, and if they have different hashes they are different. The price is that any change to the content changes the name, which means content-addressed systems need a separate naming layer --- refs, tags, DNSLink --- to track moving targets.

## 6. Metadata Moves From Afterthought to First Class

For most of the history of computing, metadata was the thing you wrote on the sleeve of the tape before you put it in the cabinet. It was second-class, generated by hand, frequently missing, and treated as a labor tax on the real work of producing data. This changed slowly and then suddenly. The inflection point in the scholarly-communication world was the **OCLC / NCSA Metadata Workshop** held at OCLC headquarters in Dublin, Ohio, March 1 to 3, 1995. Fifty-two librarians, archivists, humanities scholars, and internet standards people met to define a minimal common vocabulary for describing networked resources. The result, named after the town, was the **Dublin Core Metadata Element Set** --- fifteen elements (title, creator, subject, description, publisher, contributor, date, type, format, identifier, source, language, relation, coverage, rights) that any resource could carry. Dublin Core became an ISO standard (ISO 15836) in 2003 and remains the minimum viable metadata vocabulary for library and archival systems. Stuart Weibel's July 1995 *D-Lib Magazine* article "Metadata: The Foundations of Resource Description" was the workshop's public announcement.

The semantic web built on this foundation. RDF became a W3C recommendation in 1999 (revised in 2004), and by the mid-2010s **schema.org**, launched in June 2011 by Google, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Yandex, had become the de facto vocabulary for embedding machine-readable metadata in HTML. At the same time, the scientific data community pushed in a complementary direction. The **FAIR principles** --- Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable --- were formulated by a 2014 Lorentz Center workshop and published by Mark Wilkinson and forty-eight co-authors as "The FAIR Guiding Principles for Scientific Data Management and Stewardship" in *Scientific Data* volume 3 in March 2016. FAIR did not specify technology; it specified obligations, and in doing so it made metadata a precondition of doing science at scale, not an afterthought.

The single most thorough standardization of metadata about *how data was produced* is the **W3C PROV family**, released as thirteen coordinated documents on April 30, 2013. At its heart is **PROV-DM**, the PROV Data Model, which treats provenance as a graph of *entities*, *activities*, and *agents*, linked by relations such as *wasGeneratedBy*, *used*, *wasDerivedFrom*, *wasAttributedTo*, and *wasAssociatedWith*. PROV deliberately refused to pick a domain, because provenance is a cross-cutting concern that astronomical catalogs, clinical trial registries, and government open-data portals all share. PROV's importance for housekeeping is that it moved provenance from a nice-to-have audit field into a first-class, queryable structure. Once provenance is a graph, it can be indexed, it can be verified, and it can be held against the data the way a receipt is held against a purchase.

## 7. Identity, Equality, and the Problem of Being the Same Thing

If naming is the first hard problem, deciding when two records refer to the same real-world entity is the second. **Record linkage** is older than computers --- **Halbert Dunn**'s 1946 *American Journal of Public Health* paper "Record Linkage" gave the field its name in the context of public-health statistics --- but its mathematical form was fixed by **Ivan Fellegi** and **Alan Sunter** in their 1969 *Journal of the American Statistical Association* paper "A Theory for Record Linkage," which introduced the probabilistic comparison of field-level agreement patterns. Entity resolution, deduplication, and fuzzy matching are all the children of Fellegi-Sunter. They matter because the world produces the same person's name with four different spellings, the same company's address with three different zip-code formats, and the same scientific instrument's identifier in two different local conventions, and a data-management system that cannot decide whether two such records are the same thing will either double-count its entities or quietly merge distinct ones. Which error is worse depends on the application, and either error is fatal if it is silent.

## 8. Data as Material: Gravity, Mass, and Logical-Versus-Physical

The long-running split between the **logical** view of data (what it *means*) and the **physical** view (what it *weighs* on a disk) is as old as the relational model. Codd's 1970 paper is in part a manifesto for logical independence: users of a database should not have to know or care how the bytes are laid out on storage, and the storage layer should be free to reorganize itself without breaking applications. That independence has held up remarkably well, but it is not total. The twenty-first-century cloud discovered that moving large datasets between regions, providers, and services was slow and expensive enough that architectures began to bend around where the data physically was. The term **data gravity** --- the observation that applications, services, and other datasets are pulled toward large existing data stores the way small masses are pulled toward large ones --- was coined by Dave McCrory in a 2010 blog post and formalized by him in 2012, and it is rightly credited to McCrory, not to Jim Gray. But the underlying insight that data is expensive to move was a persistent theme of Gray's later work. Gray, a Turing laureate for his foundational contributions to transaction processing, spent the last decade of his career arguing that the cost of moving bytes dwarfed almost every other cost in scientific computing, and that system design should therefore push computation to the data rather than data to the computation. His 2003 paper with Alex Szalay on the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, and his contributions to the posthumous 2009 Microsoft Research volume *The Fourth Paradigm*, articulated the view that at petabyte scale the physical-versus-logical split could no longer be treated as a free abstraction. Data had mass. Planning had to account for it.

## 9. The Ontological Quarrel: Discovered or Constructed?

Underneath all of this sits an older, unresolved argument about whether data is *found* or *made*. The positivist tradition, running from Comte through the logical positivists of the 1920s and into twentieth-century empiricism, treats data as what you get when you strip away interpretation: raw, objective measurements of a world that exists independently of the observer. The constructivist tradition --- Thomas Kuhn, Bruno Latour, feminist philosophers of science such as Sandra Harding and Donna Haraway --- replies that there is no such stripping; every measurement encodes choices about what is worth measuring, in what units, against what baseline, for what purpose, and those choices come from human institutions, not from the world. The most thorough contemporary statement of this quarrel as it applies to digital data is **Rob Kitchin**'s 2014 *The Data Revolution: Big Data, Open Data, Data Infrastructures and Their Consequences*, published by Sage. Kitchin argues that our *ontologies* of data --- our implicit answers to the question of what data are --- are inseparable from our *epistemologies* of data, our assumptions about how data yield knowledge. He uses the term *data assemblage* to insist that any given dataset is the product of a sociotechnical apparatus of instruments, protocols, institutions, funding bodies, publication norms, and disciplinary conventions, and that the apparatus is always at least as consequential for what the data means as the world the data purports to be about. Kitchin is not anti-empirical. He is arguing that the positivist self-image of data --- raw, unmediated, purely factual --- is itself a construction, and a dangerous one, because it encourages users of data to forget the assumptions baked into the instruments.

This quarrel is not idle for a housekeeping discipline. A data-management system that assumes its inputs are positivist "raw facts" will under-invest in provenance, in schema versioning, in calibration metadata, in the audit trail of the instruments that produced the data. A data-management system that takes Kitchin seriously will build provenance in from the start, will treat schema and context as load-bearing, and will be much harder to fool into repeating the mistakes of the apparatus that produced its inputs. The eight documents that follow this one --- on storage tiers, indexing, retrieval, compression, replication, governance, archival, and the long-term economics of bytes --- all rest on decisions made here, in the vocabulary. Getting the noun right, before the verbs arrive, is the first act of housekeeping.
