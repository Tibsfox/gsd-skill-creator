## 1. Video 1 — Introduction to Supply Chain Management (112 min)

**Presenter:** Eric Kimberling, CEO, Third Stage Consulting
**Source:** `yt-supply-chain-full-sANREcJ8Mhs.en.vtt`

### Key Claims

1. Supply chain management is fundamentally cross-functional — it spans procurement, production, warehousing, distribution, cash collection, and demand forecasting in a single end-to-end cycle from customer order to fulfillment.
2. COVID-19 exposed single-vendor dependency as the dominant structural failure in modern supply chains; domino-effect cascades from one broken node disrupted entire global pipelines.
3. Truck driver shortages can halt fully stocked supply chains — finished goods sitting in warehouses go nowhere without the final transport link, illustrating that labor is a supply chain input, not just overhead.
4. Supplier diversification — maintaining multiple vendors for any critical raw material — is now a first-order strategic priority, not an optional hedge.
5. Technology (ERP, WMS, AI, predictive analytics) is the primary lever to mitigate the four root causes of supply chain failure: labor shortages, vendor concentration, demand variability, and visibility gaps.
6. The best supply chains in the world (Schneider Electric, Intel, Amazon, Target) share three traits: real-time visibility, continuous process improvement, and deliberate sustainability programs extending to suppliers.
7. Point-of-sale data feeds directly into inventory reorder systems — every purchase event triggers automated replenishment decisions up the chain (demonstrated via Target store walkthrough).
8. Intel's 2012 response to chip shortage — building two domestic manufacturing plants — proves that supply chain investment is a direct competitive and throughput lever, not just cost management.

### Technical Details

- **Supply chain components:** Procurement (direct + indirect), inventory management, production planning, warehousing, transportation/logistics, demand forecasting, financial settlement (order-to-cash cycle).
- **Direct procurement:** Raw materials required to build the product being sold.
- **Indirect procurement:** Supporting materials (laptops, office supplies) not embedded in the product itself.
- **Inventory management tools:** Physical inventories (full count), cycle counts (high-value/high-volume subsets), WMS (Warehouse Management Systems) for location and level tracking.
- **Digital supply chain characteristics:** Improved business processes defined before digitization, real-time data capture, AI/ML for demand sensing, automation at pick/pack/ship.
- **Supply chain risks taxonomy:** Labor shortages, vendor concentration, technology gaps, geopolitical disruption, demand volatility.
- **ERP integration:** CRM feeds sales data → supply chain planning → procurement triggers.

### Numbers

- Schneider Electric operates in **100+ countries** with a sustainability-linked supply chain program.
- Intel built **2 manufacturing plants** domestically to respond to the 2012 chip shortage.
- Supply chain disruptions in the 2020s described as affecting **hundreds of thousands of touch points** within a single chain.
- COVID-19 identified as a turning point — supply chain failures occurred **not because of total system collapse** but because small node failures cascaded.

### Key Quotes

> "The customer order goes through procurement of materials, production of materials, the storage of materials in a warehouse, the shipment of materials to a customer or to a third party distributor, and ultimately the collection of cash — and ultimately the forecasting of what might happen in the future as it relates to that entire cycle."

> "Little pieces within the supply chain broke down that caused a domino effect throughout the entire supply chain."

### Study Guide Topics

1. The seven major components of an end-to-end supply chain and how they interconnect.
2. Direct vs. indirect procurement — definitions, examples, and how procurement KPIs differ between them.
3. Why single-vendor dependency is catastrophically fragile and how to design vendor diversification policies.
4. The role of ERP systems in bridging sales data with supply chain planning.
5. How point-of-sale triggers propagate as reorder signals up the supply chain (Target walkthrough model).
6. The five root-cause categories of supply chain failure and which technology addresses each.
7. Why sustainability requirements are now extended to suppliers, not just internal operations.
8. Case study comparison: Intel (throughput investment) vs. companies that failed during COVID (single-vendor dependency).

### DIY Try Sessions

**Session A — Map a Personal Supply Chain**
Pick something you buy regularly (coffee beans, computing hardware). Map the full supply chain from raw material origin to your hands: identify at least 5 nodes, who owns each node, what could break each node, and what the backup is if it fails. Write a one-paragraph risk assessment.

**Session B — Simulate a Vendor Disruption**
Choose any 3-ingredient recipe or simple hardware build. Assume your primary supplier of ingredient/component 1 goes offline for 4 weeks. Document: What is the next available source? What is the lead time delta? What is the cost delta? How many days of buffer stock do you need to bridge the gap without missing delivery? Calculate the buffer inventory required.

## 3. Video 3 — Warehousing: 10 Principles of Design and Operations (22 min)

**Presenter:** Warehouse specialist, Logistics Bureau (filmed at seminar)
**Source:** `yt-warehouse-principles-inNAvP5kwB0.en.vtt`

### Key Claims

1. There is a real science behind distribution center design and operation — flow management, product placement, and layout geometry all have measurable, quantifiable impacts on operating cost and labor efficiency.
2. Minimizing product touches (handling events) is the primary lean principle applied to warehousing — every unnecessary pick-up/set-down adds cost without adding value.
3. One-way flow through the warehouse is the second foundational design principle — it enables predictable picking paths, eliminates counter-flow collision risks, and simplifies replenishment routing.
4. Product slotting (placing products in the right zone based on velocity) can achieve 20–30% savings in operating labor — the most impactful single operational improvement available.
5. The "triadic warehouse" model (zones: fast-moving, medium-moving, slow-moving product) is the standard framework for velocity-based slotting; non-triadic warehouses leave major efficiency gains uncaptured.
6. Warehouse management systems (WMS) are required once operational complexity grows — they enable radio-frequency picking, voice picking, and automated replenishment decisions.
7. Consulting widely (drivers, dock workers, managing directors, suppliers, architects) during warehouse design is essential — the people closest to the physical work carry operational knowledge that design teams miss.

### Technical Details

- **10 principles covered:** (1) Minimal product touches, (2) one-way flow, (3) correct product slotting, (4) right inventory levels, (5) WMS deployment, (6) wide stakeholder consultation, (7) appropriate layout design, (8) KPI measurement, (9) automation integration, (10) safety and ergonomics.
- **Touch count benchmarks:** Automated warehouses target zero touches. Manual non-automated warehouses average 7–8 touches. Three to four touches in a non-automated warehouse is considered good performance.
- **Warehouse layout types:**
  - **U-shaped:** Receiving and dispatch on same end. Most land-efficient. Standard for moderate volume.
  - **I-shaped (flow-through):** Receiving on one end, dispatch on the other. Best for high-volume flow. Requires more land (hardstand at both ends).
  - **L-shaped:** Designed for cross-docking — goods in one side, out the other, often without racking.
- **Triadic warehouse zones:** Fast-moving product closest to dispatch (shortest pick travel), medium-moving in middle zone, slow-moving furthest from dispatch.
- **Inventory measurement error:** Clients often measure inventory by sales value — specialists measure by demand velocity (units per day, not revenue per unit).
- **WMS capabilities:** Radio-frequency picking, voice-directed picking (hands-free headset), location tracking, automated replenishment signals.
- **Automated vs. manual:** Automated Storage and Retrieval Systems (AS/RS) do not require triadic slotting — the system finds stock regardless of location. Manual operations require triadic layout to minimize worker travel distance.

### Numbers

- **20–30% labor cost savings** achievable through correct product slotting alone.
- Automated warehouses achieve **zero touches** per product in transit.
- Non-automated warehouses average **7–8 touches** per product.
- **3–4 touches** is the benchmark for a well-run non-automated warehouse.
- Vehicle circulation direction: **clockwise** in Australia (driver reverses on right side), **counterclockwise** in the USA.

### Key Quotes

> "If you correctly slot product into those zones and you have a triadic warehouse, you can potentially achieve twenty to thirty percent saving in operating labor."

> "Long gone are the days of just two or three people sitting in a room — I'll go and talk to the fork truck driver, the receptionist, the managing director, the suppliers — just to find out what's going on and what are the requirements for that particular warehouse."

### Study Guide Topics

1. The 10 principles of warehouse design and operations — rationale for each.
2. How to calculate and reduce product touch count — what counts as a touch, and what the cost of each touch is.
3. Product slotting methodology — how to classify products by velocity and assign zone placement.
4. The three warehouse layout types (U, I, L) — when to use each, trade-offs in land use vs. throughput.
5. Triadic vs. non-triadic warehouse models — when automation makes triadic unnecessary.
6. WMS selection and capability — when a WMS becomes necessary vs. when spreadsheets suffice.
7. Voice-directed picking and RF picking — how they work and where they apply.
8. Cross-docking: what it is, when it applies, and which layout supports it best.

### DIY Try Sessions

**Session A — Slot a Miniature Warehouse**
Take a list of 20 items in any storage space you manage (pantry, tool shed, parts bin). Classify each item as fast-moving (used weekly), medium-moving (used monthly), or slow-moving (used rarely). Reorganize the physical space so fast items are closest to the access point. Time how long it takes to retrieve 5 items before and after slotting. Calculate your personal labor efficiency gain.

**Session B — Design a Flow Path**
Sketch a simple warehouse floor plan (20 aisles, U-shaped). Draw the current picking path for a 10-item order assuming random slotting. Then redraw the same 10-item pick using triadic slotting. Measure total distance traveled in each scenario. Extrapolate: if pickers complete 50 picks per day, what is the weekly travel distance difference?

## 4. Video 4 — Lean Demystified: Operations Science Explains and Expands Lean (31 min)

**Presenter:** Dr. Mark Spearman, co-author of *Factory Physics*, Technical Director at Strategic Project Solutions
**Source:** `yt-lean-operations-k6Vc0uS8Zf4.en.vtt`

### Key Claims

1. Lean without operations science is dangerous — blindly copying another company's lean implementation (as Boeing did with Toyota's moving assembly line) can cause massive losses without producing the intended improvement.
2. The three Japanese pillars of lean — muda (waste), mura (unevenness/variability), and muri (overburdening) — are real but incomplete without quantitative models to measure them and optimize trade-offs.
3. Waiting and inventory are not two independent forms of waste — they are two sides of the same coin. You cannot reduce both simultaneously without reducing variability. Treating them as separate targets is conceptually wrong.
4. Toyota's production system operates at approximately 70% capacity utilization deliberately — this is not inefficiency, it is a designed capacity buffer that absorbs variability and prevents catastrophic throughput collapse.
5. Pushing utilization from 83% to 95% (what finance departments typically demand) more than doubles total operating cost when inventory carrying cost increases are included — high utilization is often economically irrational.
6. Operations Science (OS) lean adds quantitative optimization to lean's qualitative guidelines — you redesign the system, then re-optimize after every change, cycling until minimum total buffering cost is achieved.
7. The correct implementation sequence for OS lean: (1) find and eliminate wasted capacity, (2) re-optimize the production system, (3) reduce WIP to expose problems, (4) fix problems to reduce variability, (5) repeat.

### Technical Details

- **Seven mudas (wastes):** Transport (excess product movement), inventory (excess stock), motion (excess machine/worker movement), waiting (people or equipment idle), overproduction (making before needed), over-processing (doing more than the customer requires), defects.
- **Mura:** Unevenness or non-uniformity in operations — essentially variability. Lean describes it qualitatively; operations science provides the mathematical relationship between variability, inventory, and throughput.
- **Muri:** Overburdening — operating above 100% capacity. Operations science shows the system needs to run significantly below 100% to absorb variability; Toyota targets ~70%.
- **Factory Physics laws (key):** Variability is variability regardless of its source; extra capacity mitigates the time buffer; inventory and backorders are mirror trade-offs controlled by variability reduction.
- **WIP (Work In Process):** Reducing WIP is the lean mechanism for exposing hidden problems — when the buffer disappears, problems become visible and must be fixed. This is intentional.
- **Total cost optimization:** Total cost = inventory carrying cost + backorder cost + extra capacity cost. OS lean minimizes this sum, not any single component.
- **Boeing 777 case:** Spent $250M on moving assembly line (2006–2007), lost revenue from plant shutdowns, reduced throughput by enforcing slowest-station takt time. The actual throughput improvement came from pre-wiring harnesses and new fuselage drilling technology — unrelated to the moving line.
- **Takt time:** The rate at which products must be completed to meet customer demand. Too slow = backlog builds. Too fast = overproduction.

### Numbers

- Boeing spent **$250 million** implementing the 777 moving assembly line (2006–2007).
- Boeing's 777 takt time: **one plane every 3 days**.
- Toyota classic TPS: **two shifts, 10 hours each**, assembly line faster than slowest station takt time.
- Toyota system capacity utilization: **approximately 70%** (deliberately below 100%).
- Increasing utilization from 83% to 95%: increases total cost by **113%** over optimal when inventory carrying costs are included.
- At 83% utilization: inventory carrying cost $1.5M/yr, backorder cost $0.5M/yr, extra capacity $2M/yr → total ~$4M.
- At 95% utilization: capacity cost drops from $2M to $0.5M but inventory carrying cost spikes → total cost more than doubles.

### Key Quotes

> "Lean methods without operation science can be disastrous — usually things get worse before they get better, so when you start reducing the WIP inventory buffer, a lot of times you reduce the throughput."

> "The only way to reduce both waiting and inventory simultaneously is to reduce variability."

### Study Guide Topics

1. The three pillars of lean (muda, mura, muri) — definitions, examples, and quantitative limitations of each.
2. The seven forms of muda and how to identify each in a real production environment.
3. Why Toyota deliberately targets ~70% utilization and the mathematical argument for capacity buffers.
4. The WIP reduction strategy — why reducing buffer inventory intentionally exposes problems, and why that is the mechanism, not a side effect.
5. The total cost optimization model — how inventory carrying cost, backorder cost, and extra capacity cost trade off, and where the true minimum lies.
6. The Boeing 777 case study — what went wrong, why, and what the real source of improvement was.
7. The OS lean implementation sequence — how to cycle through redesign and re-optimization until minimum buffering cost is achieved.
8. Takt time — definition, calculation, and its relationship to throughput and backlog.

### DIY Try Sessions

**Session A — Waste Hunt**
Pick any recurring workflow you do (writing a research document, processing email, deploying code). Map each step. Label each step as: value-adding (customer would pay for this), necessary non-value-adding (required overhead), or pure waste (one of the seven mudas). Calculate what percentage of your total time is pure waste. Propose one elimination for the largest waste category.

**Session B — Utilization vs. Cost Model**
Build a simple spreadsheet: columns are utilization % (50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, 90%, 95%), rows are (a) extra capacity cost, (b) inventory carrying cost (rises sharply as utilization rises), (c) backorder cost, (d) total. Use illustrative numbers (you can use Dr. Spearman's $1.5M/$0.5M/$2M example as a seed). Graph the U-shaped total cost curve. Find the minimum. Now explain to yourself why a finance team demanding 95% utilization is actually the most expensive choice.

## 5. Video 5 — How Toyota Changed The Way We Make Things (5 min)

**Presenter:** Documentary narration (BBC/business journalism style)
**Source:** `yt-toyota-lean-F5vtCRFRAK0.en.vtt`

### Key Claims

1. Toyota became the first company to produce more than 10 million vehicles per year — a milestone rooted in production system efficiency, not just market share.
2. The just-in-time system was inspired not by another manufacturer but by the Piggly Wiggly supermarket — Taiichi Ohno was struck by the self-service model where only what was consumed was restocked.
3. Post-war material scarcity was the forcing function for lean: Toyota could not afford months of stock sitting in warehouses tying up capital it didn't have.
4. Stopping the entire assembly line when a defect is spotted — Ohno's andon cord concept — was counterintuitive to Western manufacturers but proved to be the mechanism that drove error rates to dramatically lower levels.
5. Kaizen (continuous incremental improvement) combined with just-in-time created a self-reinforcing system: fewer defects → more reliable processes → less buffer needed → more defects exposed → fewer defects.
6. Toyota's efficiency advantage was so great that by the early 1970s it could produce a car in **1.6 man-hours** — far below U.S., Swedish, and European competitors.

### Technical Details

- **Toyota founding:** Sakichi Toyoda founded the loom business; son Kiichiro Toyoda founded the motor company in **1937**.
- **WWII constraints:** Model K truck reduced to one headlamp and brakes on only one axle due to material shortages — extreme constraint-driven engineering as cultural origin of lean.
- **1950s US visit:** Kiichiro's cousin Eiji Toyoda and Taiichi Ohno visited Ford's River Rouge plant in Michigan — impressed by scale but concluded Japan couldn't replicate it.
- **Piggly Wiggly inspiration:** Self-service supermarket revealed the pull model — only restock what was consumed. Ohno applied this directly: only produce in phase N what was used in phase N+1.
- **Kanban:** Sign-based scheduling system developed by Ohno — tracks goods in, goods in production, goods out. Considered a precursor to barcodes.
- **Andon cord:** A physical cord above every station that any worker could pull to stop the entire assembly line. The whole team then worked on the problem to prevent recurrence.
- **American alternative:** Ford-style operations ran at full tilt to maximize economies of scale, then repaired defects post-production — treating defect repair as a normal end-of-line process.
- **Kaizen:** Continuous incremental improvement culture — small, constant improvements by all workers at all levels, not periodic large-batch upgrades.

### Numbers

- Toyota motor company founded: **1937**.
- **1962:** Toyota produced its 1 millionth vehicle.
- **1972:** Toyota produced its 10 millionth vehicle — 10x growth in 10 years.
- **1.6 man-hours** per car produced in the early 1970s — vs. significantly higher labor content at U.S., Swedish, and European plants.
- First company to produce **10 million vehicles per year**.

### Key Quotes

> "Only enough parts were produced in the first phase to replace what was used in the second, and so on — this is where the just-in-time system really took shape."

> "Ohno believed this caused more problems and didn't encourage workers or machines to stop making the mistake — so he placed a cord above every station which any worker could pull to stop the entire assembly."

### Study Guide Topics

1. The historical and material-scarcity context that forced Toyota to invent lean — why constraint was the origin of the system.
2. The Piggly Wiggly insight — how a supermarket pull model translated directly into just-in-time production sequencing.
3. Kanban: what it is, what problem it solved, and why it is considered a precursor to modern barcode/RFID systems.
4. The andon cord — what it does, why it was counterintuitive to Western manufacturers, and how it mechanically reduced defect rates.
5. Kaizen — the philosophy of continuous incremental improvement vs. periodic large-batch redesign.
6. The 1.6 man-hours per car achievement — what it means in competitive context and what made it possible.
7. Why Ford-style maximum-utilization / post-production-repair was inferior to Toyota's stop-and-fix approach.
8. How Toyota's success validated and spread lean manufacturing globally.

### DIY Try Sessions

**Session A — Personal Kanban Implementation**
Implement a physical kanban board (even 3 sticky notes on a wall: To Do / In Progress / Done) for one week of personal work. At the end of the week: Was WIP ever more than 2 items? Where did blockers appear? What would your andon cord equivalent be (the moment you pull it and stop all other work to fix a problem)? Write a brief retrospective.

What was the hardest part of the mental switch from push to pull?

## 6. Video 6 — Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) Explained (31 min)

**Presenter:** Supply chain / operations management lecturer (university course format)
**Source:** `yt-eoq-inventory-KiCFfD5zBWQ.en.vtt`

### Key Claims

1. EOQ is a classic model designed to minimize total annual inventory cost — it finds the mathematically optimal order size that balances the trade-off between ordering costs (high when ordering frequently) and holding costs (high when ordering in large batches).
2. The total cost curve is U-shaped — holding costs rise linearly with order quantity, ordering costs fall non-linearly as order quantity rises. EOQ sits at the bottom of the U where holding cost = ordering cost.
3. EOQ is calculated per SKU (stock-keeping unit) — every product has its own independent demand, its own holding cost, and therefore its own optimal order quantity.
4. The model assumes constant demand, constant lead time, single delivery per order, and no quantity discounts — these assumptions are the model's limitations and must be managed in real implementation.
5. For high-value/high-holding-cost products, it is often economically better to order more frequently in smaller batches — reducing average inventory carried despite higher ordering transaction costs.
6. The EOQ formula is the square root of (2 × Annual Demand × Setup Cost) / Holding Cost — a formula that makes the total cost minimum mathematically tractable.
7. Ordering cost includes the full labor cost of the ordering process: buyer time + stock room clerk time + inventory clerk time to receive, process, and put away.

### Technical Details

- **EOQ formula:** `EOQ = √(2DS/H)` where D = annual demand, S = setup/ordering cost per order, H = holding cost per unit per year.
- **Total cost formula:** `TC = Annual Setup Cost + Annual Holding Cost = (D/Q)×S + (Q/2)×H`
- **Orders per year (N):** `N = D / EOQ`
- **Time between orders (T):** `T = Working Days / N`
- **EOQ equilibrium condition:** At the optimal order quantity, annual holding cost = annual ordering cost.
- **Holding cost components:** Capital tied up in inventory, storage space cost, insurance, obsolescence/spoilage risk, physical handling.
- **Ordering cost components:** Buyer labor (hours per order × wage), stock room receiving labor, inventory clerk entry labor, any purchasing system transaction costs.
- **EOQ assumptions:** (1) Single product per calculation, (2) constant known demand, (3) constant known lead time, (4) entire order received in one delivery, (5) no quantity discounts available.
- **Practical adjustment:** High-value products → favor small frequent orders (holding cost dominates). Low-value/low-holding-cost products → favor large infrequent orders (ordering cost dominates).
- **Raw materials → WIP → finished goods:** Inventory management applies at every stage of the value transformation chain, not just at the finished goods stage.

### Numbers

**Worked example (tire distributor):**
- Annual demand (D): **9,600 tires/year**
- Holding cost (H): **$16/tire/year**
- Ordering/setup cost (S): **$75/order**
- Operating days: **288/year**
- EOQ = √(2 × 9,600 × 75 / 16) = **300 tires per order**
- Orders per year (N) = 9,600 / 300 = **32 orders/year**
- Time between orders (T) = 288 / 32 = **9 working days**
- Total cost at EOQ: holding cost = (300/2) × $16 = **$2,400/yr**, ordering cost = 32 × $75 = **$2,400/yr** → **$4,800/yr total** (holding = ordering at optimum, confirming EOQ is correct).

### Key Quotes

> "Your holding costs and your ordering costs will be the same — and that's how you know you've got your economic order quantity calculation correct."

> "Finding that balance of having the right inventory on hand to lower our costs and minimize costs while also meeting our customer demands is the fundamental principle of inventory management."

### Study Guide Topics

1. The EOQ formula — derivation intuition (why the square root appears) and what each variable represents.
2. The U-shaped total cost curve — why holding costs are linear and ordering costs are non-linear, and where their intersection is the optimum.
3. The five EOQ model assumptions — what each means and what real-world conditions violate each assumption.
4. How to calculate ordering cost from first principles (labor time × wage × staff involved).
5. How to calculate holding cost from first principles (capital cost + storage + insurance + obsolescence).
6. The high-value product preference for small frequent orders — walk through a worked example vs. a low-value commodity.
7. EOQ in context: where does it fit in the broader inventory management toolbox (alongside safety stock, reorder points, MRP)?
8. Limitations of EOQ: what happens when demand is not constant, when lead time varies, or when quantity discounts are available?

### DIY Try Sessions

**Session A — Calculate Your Own EOQ**
Pick any consumable you purchase regularly (coffee, printer paper, a common component). Estimate: annual usage quantity (D), what it costs you per order in time and effort (S), and what it costs you per unit per year to hold it (H, including money tied up). Apply the EOQ formula. Are you currently ordering more or less than EOQ? What is the cost difference?

**Session B — Sensitivity Analysis**
Using the tire example (D=9,600, S=$75, H=$16, EOQ=300): Recompute total cost when ordering 150 units/order and when ordering 600 units/order. Graph holding cost, ordering cost, and total cost across these three order quantities. Verify that EOQ produces the lowest total cost. Then ask: what if holding cost doubled to $32/unit? What is the new EOQ? What does this tell you about how rising capital costs (interest rates, storage) should change your inventory strategy?

## 9. Fox Companies Alignment

### FoxFiber (Hardware Manufacturing)

Logistics and supply chain operations are non-negotiable for any hardware company. Key principles from this research that apply directly:

- **Vendor diversification is mandatory from day one.** FoxFiber cannot afford single-source dependency on any critical component (transceivers, fiber connectors, PCBs, enclosures). Design the sourcing strategy with at least two qualified suppliers per critical SKU before first production run.
- **EOQ applies immediately.** When prototyping transitions to production, calculate EOQ for every high-cost component. The formula prevents both understock (production stops) and overstock (capital tied up in components that may iterate).
- **Warehouse design matters at small scale.** Even a 1,000 sq ft fulfillment operation benefits from triadic slotting. Fast-moving consumables (cables, SFPs) near dispatch. Slow-moving spare assemblies in back.
- **Lean applies to manufacturing workflow.** The andon cord principle (stop and fix defects, never propagate bad units) is critical for hardware quality. A defective unit shipped to a customer is orders of magnitude more expensive than stopping the line.

### FoxCompute (Compute Infrastructure)

- **Fleet management maps directly to server/rack lifecycle management.** Managing a fleet of compute nodes (provisioning, maintenance, decommission) follows the same logistics principles as fleet management.
- **Inventory management of compute resources.** How many spare drives, NICs, and PSUs do you keep on-hand? EOQ applies to hardware spares management in a data center context.
- **Supply chain resilience for compute components.** GPU shortages (RTX 4060 Ti class and above) mirror the chip shortage dynamics discussed — diversified sourcing and strategic buffer stock are direct mitigations.

### Cross-Company Principles

- **Supply chain strategy has a 10–20 year horizon** (Video 2). Fox Companies infrastructure decisions made now will define operational costs and capability for a decade. Design with resilience, not just efficiency.
- **The customer drives the supply chain.** For Fox Companies, the customer is researchers, educators, and cooperative network participants. Their demand patterns should drive inventory, fulfillment, and support logistics decisions.
- **Kaizen as corporate culture.** The continuous-improvement discipline that made Toyota dominant is directly applicable to Fox Companies software release cadence (sweep commits, hourly releases) and hardware iteration.

This addendum was added in April 2026 as part of a catalog-wide

### The 2025–2026 supply-chain environment

The body's framing that supply chain strategy has a 10–20 year
horizon has been stress-tested through the 2020–2024 pandemic-plus-
tariff-plus-chip-shortage cycle. Practitioner writing through 2025
has converged on three durable takeaways that are consistent with
the body's analysis:

- **Diversified sourcing wins.** Every 2024–2025 retrospective on
  the 2020–2023 crisis identified concentration risk as the main
  preventable failure mode. Firms that had second and third
  suppliers across geographies recovered faster than firms that
  had optimized for single-supplier cost.
- **Inventory is no longer a dirty word.** JIT absolutism gave way
  through 2022–2024 to a more nuanced position in which strategic
  buffer stock for critical components is treated as insurance
  rather than waste. This matches the body's Fox Companies
  "design with resilience, not just efficiency" recommendation.
- **Reshoring is partial, not wholesale.** The 2020–2024
  geopolitical shocks produced selective reshoring of strategic
  components (semiconductors via CHIPS Act, medical supplies,
  some pharmaceuticals) but did not produce a wholesale
  repatriation of manufacturing. The 10–20 year horizon the
  body references is playing out as continued globalization with
  hedges, not as reversal.
