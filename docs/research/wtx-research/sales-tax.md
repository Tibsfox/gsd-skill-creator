# Washington State Retail Sales Tax, Use Tax, and the 2025 SB 5814 Service Expansion

*A Practitioner's Guide to the Largest Structural Change in Washington's Sales Tax Base Since 1935*

---

## Document Abstract

This document is a comprehensive educational reference on Washington State's retail sales tax and use tax system, with particular focus on the landmark structural reform enacted by Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5814 (ESSB 5814), signed by Governor Bob Ferguson on May 20, 2025 and effective October 1, 2025 for most provisions.

Washington's sales tax is one of the oldest continuous state sales taxes in the United States, tracing its origin to the Revenue Act of 1935. For nine decades, it has been a tax primarily on tangible personal property, with a relatively narrow set of enumerated services pulled into the base by specific legislation. The 2025 reform represents the largest single expansion of that base in the tax's history, adding information technology services, custom website development, advertising services, live presentations, temporary staffing, investigation and security services, custom software, and customization of prewritten software to the definition of "retail sale."

This document treats the Washington sales tax as an object of study. It is written for practitioners, students of state and local taxation, policy analysts, and anyone who needs a rigorous, source-grounded reference. It is not legal advice. Readers with specific compliance questions should consult the Washington Department of Revenue or a qualified state tax practitioner.

# Part 1: Foundations of the Washington Retail Sales Tax

## 1.1 Origins: The Revenue Act of 1935

The Washington retail sales tax is almost exactly as old as the modern state sales tax itself. When Governor Clarence D. Martin signed the Revenue Act of 1935 on March 25, 1935, Washington joined a wave of Depression-era states experimenting with consumption taxes as a replacement for property-tax revenue that had collapsed under mass defaults and foreclosures. Mississippi had adopted the first modern state retail sales tax in 1930; by 1935 more than half the states had followed. Washington's version was particularly consequential because it came packaged with the business and occupation (B&O) tax, creating a hybrid "excise tax" structure that still defines the state's fiscal personality nine decades later.

The Revenue Act of 1935 established a retail sales tax at **2 percent** on the selling price of tangible personal property sold at retail. It was controversial from the start. Critics argued it was regressive, falling harder on lower-income households than on the wealthy. Proponents argued that Washington had no practical alternative: the state supreme court had struck down a graduated personal income tax as unconstitutional in *Culliton v. Chase* (1933), the federal government had not yet created the programs that would later define modern social policy, and property taxes had simply run out of rope. A 2 percent sales tax, the 1935 legislature concluded, was the only feasible path to solvency.

The 1935 statute also included several features that are recognizably "modern" in design:

1. **A complementary use tax** on goods purchased out-of-state and used in Washington, enacted simultaneously so that the sales tax would not create an arbitrage opportunity for border shoppers.
2. **A reseller exemption** allowing wholesalers and retailers to buy inventory for resale without paying tax on the intermediate transaction.
3. **A narrow set of enumerated services**—primarily those integral to the retail sale of goods, such as installation and repair—pulled into the base.
4. **Delegation to the Tax Commission** (the predecessor to the modern Department of Revenue) for administration, audit, and rulemaking.

What the 1935 statute did *not* include is almost as important. It did not tax professional services (legal, accounting, medical), it did not tax most personal services (barbering, laundering), and it did not tax what would later be called "information services" or "intellectual services." Those exclusions defined the tax base for nine decades, and the 2025 reform can only be understood as the belated cracking of that 1935 consensus.

## 1.2 The 6.5% State Base Rate and Its Long Stability

Since 1983, Washington's state retail sales tax rate has been **6.5 percent**, applied to the selling price of retail sales delivered to a Washington location. This rate has been remarkably stable—stable enough that it is now one of the longest continuous state sales tax rates in the country. Forty-two years without a state rate increase is an unusual record in a fiscal environment where most states have raised rates multiple times since the early 1980s.

The stability is not an accident. Several structural factors reinforce it:

- **A very broad local layer.** Because Washington permits counties, cities, transit districts, and special districts to layer their own sales taxes on top of the state rate, the state has been able to raise effective rates in specific jurisdictions without touching the statewide number. The total combined rate in Seattle is now above 10 percent even though the state component has not moved since 1983.
- **The B&O tax as a revenue alternative.** When the state needs more revenue, it has historically turned to B&O rate increases or new B&O surcharges (such as the advanced computing surcharge) rather than touching the sales tax rate.
- **Political signaling.** A state sales tax increase is politically visible in a way that local rate increases and B&O adjustments are not. Keeping the headline number at 6.5 percent has been a deliberate choice by multiple legislatures across both parties.
- **SSUTA constraints.** Since Washington joined the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement in 2008, certain structural changes to the sales tax have had to conform to SSUTA requirements, creating a procedural friction against rate manipulation.

The 6.5 percent figure is referenced throughout this document as "the state rate." Every combined rate in Washington starts from this base and adds local components.

## 1.3 Rate History: Eight Increases in Ninety Years

Since the original 2 percent rate was enacted in 1935, the Washington state retail sales tax rate has been increased a total of eight times, arriving at the present 6.5 percent in 1983. The following table consolidates the rate progression. It is drawn from the Department of Revenue's historical tax publications, which remain the authoritative source for this history.

| Year | Event | State Rate |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 1935 | Revenue Act of 1935 enacted | 2.0% |
| 1941 | First wartime increase | 3.0% |
| 1951 | Post-war rate increase | 3.33% |
| 1955 | Rate adjustment | 3.5% |
| 1959 | Rate increase | 4.0% |
| 1965 | Rate increase | 4.2% |
| 1967 | Rate increase | 4.5% |
| 1976 | Rate reaches 4.6% | 4.6% |
| 1982 | Fiscal crisis increase | 5.5% |
| 1983 | Final increase to modern rate | **6.5%** |

The precise year-to-year detail between 1935 and 1983 is available in the DOR historical data tables. For the purposes of this document, what matters is the overall shape: the rate moved roughly in line with the state's fiscal needs for half a century and then froze in 1983 just as Washington's economy was beginning its long transition from a resource and manufacturing economy to a technology and services economy.

That freeze is structurally significant. As the Washington economy shifted toward services over the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s, the state sales tax base—tied to tangible goods—shrank as a share of economic activity even as the nominal rate stayed constant. This is the backdrop against which SB 5814 must be read: a state whose sales tax had been designed in 1935 for a goods-dominated economy, operating unchanged at its headline rate since 1983, increasingly out of alignment with the actual composition of state GDP.

## 1.4 Local Sales Taxes: A Layered System

Washington permits local governments to layer their own sales taxes on top of the 6.5 percent state rate. The local layer is not uniform; it varies by address and by the specific set of taxing districts that cover that address. The principal local components include:

1. **County basic sales tax**: Up to 0.5 percent, authorized for general county government.
2. **County optional sales tax**: An additional 0.5 percent, authorized in 1982 during the fiscal crisis.
3. **City basic sales tax**: Up to 0.5 percent, authorized for general municipal government.
4. **City optional sales tax**: An additional 0.5 percent.
5. **Transit district taxes**: Including Sound Transit (covering parts of King, Pierce, and Snohomish counties), Community Transit, Pierce Transit, King County Metro, Spokane Transit, and similar bodies. These rates vary but are among the largest local components.
6. **Public facilities district taxes**: For stadiums, convention centers, and similar facilities.
7. **Transportation Benefit District (TBD) taxes**: Locally voted, typically 0.1 to 0.2 percent.
8. **Criminal justice sales taxes**: Authorized for jail construction and public safety.
9. **Public safety sales taxes**: For city and county emergency services.
10. **Mental health and chemical dependency taxes**: Authorized in many counties.
11. **Cultural access tax**: For arts and cultural programs in certain cities.
12. **Housing and related services tax**: For affordable housing.
13. **Hospital district sales taxes**: In some counties.
14. **Regional Transportation Investment District (RTID) taxes**: Where authorized.
15. **Zoo and aquarium taxes**: In certain counties.

The sum of these components, added to the 6.5 percent state rate, gives the "combined rate" for an address. The Department of Revenue publishes a quarterly Local Sales and Use Tax Rate Table listing every jurisdiction in the state.

## 1.6 The Historical Tax Base: What Was Taxed, What Was Not

Before the 2025 reform, the Washington retail sales tax applied to the following broad categories:

**Always taxable (historically):**
- Tangible personal property sold at retail
- Canned (prewritten) software, whether on physical media or downloaded
- Digital products (music, books, movies, games) after 2009
- Remote access software / Software as a Service after 2009
- Construction labor on real property
- Installation labor tied to tangible goods
- Repair labor on tangible personal property
- Landscaping services
- Extended warranties and service contracts
- Lodging (hotel, motel, short-term rental)
- Amusement and recreation services (tickets, green fees, gym memberships)
- Telecommunications services
- Prepared food sold at restaurants
- Certain tattoo and tanning services

**Generally not taxable (historically):**
- Professional services: legal, accounting, medical, architectural, engineering, consulting
- Most personal services: barbering, hairdressing, nail care, dry cleaning, shoe repair
- Most financial services: banking, insurance, investment
- Advertising services
- Information technology services
- Custom software development
- Data processing
- Temporary staffing
- Training, workshops, and live presentations
- Investigation and security services
- Real estate services (brokerage, property management)
- Transportation services (except certain local passenger transport)
- Education and tuition
- Healthcare services (with narrow exceptions)
- Social assistance services
- Religious services
- Funeral services (with exceptions for goods sold)
- Babysitting, housekeeping, pet care

Of the categories in the second list, the 2025 reform specifically targets advertising, IT services, custom software, custom website development, live presentations (workshops, webinars, courses), temporary staffing, investigation, and security. The others remain outside the base.

This is worth stating clearly: even after SB 5814, Washington's sales tax is *not* a general tax on services. Legal, accounting, medical, architectural, engineering, most personal services, and most financial services remain outside the base. The reform is a significant but narrow expansion.

# Part 2: The Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement

## 2.1 Why SSUTA Exists

The Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement (SSUTA) is a cooperative agreement among states intended to simplify and harmonize state sales tax administration. It grew out of a coalition of states and businesses in the late 1990s responding to two pressures:

1. **The burden of compliance for multistate sellers.** Before SSUTA, every state's definitions, exemptions, sourcing rules, filing schedules, and audit procedures were different. A single retailer selling to customers in forty states effectively had to run forty parallel compliance systems. This was widely regarded as a barrier to interstate commerce.

2. **The *Quill* problem.** In *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota* (1992), the U.S. Supreme Court had reaffirmed that states could not require out-of-state sellers without physical presence to collect sales tax, partly on the grounds that compliance complexity made such collection unduly burdensome on interstate commerce. States wanted to overturn *Quill*, but they first needed to show that compliance could be simplified enough to remove that constitutional objection.

The SSUTA was formally adopted on November 12, 2002 and entered into effect on October 1, 2005 after a minimum number of states had enacted conforming legislation. Among its key features are:

- Uniform definitions of taxable items and services
- Destination-based sourcing
- State-level administration of local taxes
- Simplified rate structures within each state
- Uniform exemption administration
- Free software (Certified Service Providers) for multistate compliance
- Amnesty for prior uncollected tax in exchange for voluntary registration

## 2.2 Washington's Road to Full Membership

Washington participated in the drafting of SSUTA and adopted conforming legislation in 2007 through Substitute Senate Bill 5089. The conforming legislation took effect on July 1, 2008, at which point Washington became a **full member** of the Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement. As a full member, Washington has a seat on the SSUTA Governing Board and participates in the votes on amendments and interpretations.

Washington's transition to SSUTA was not without controversy. The most visible change was the shift from origin-based to destination-based sourcing for intrastate sales, which moved significant local tax revenue from the locations where businesses were headquartered to the locations where customers received delivery. This was particularly sensitive for municipalities in eastern Washington whose local economies were anchored by a few large distribution centers: those cities lost revenue; the destination cities gained it. The 2008 conforming legislation included a several-year mitigation fund to compensate origin cities that suffered significant revenue losses.

## 2.3 Destination Sourcing: The Big Switch of 2008

Destination sourcing is the principle that a retail sale is sourced to the location where the buyer receives the product (or, for services, where the service is delivered or used). Before July 1, 2008, Washington sourced most intrastate sales to the origin—typically the location of the seller's place of business. After July 1, 2008, it sources them to the destination.

For a simple physical goods transaction, destination sourcing means the local rate applied is the rate at the delivery address. A Seattle-based e-commerce retailer shipping a product to a Mukilteo customer charges the Mukilteo combined rate (10.60 percent), not the Seattle rate (10.35 percent). For services, sourcing depends on category: some services are sourced where performed, others where the benefit is received, others where the customer is located.

Destination sourcing has become particularly important in the SB 5814 context because services delivered electronically—IT support, training, digital advertising—have no obvious physical "place of performance." The DOR's interim guidance has had to specify sourcing rules for each newly taxable service category, which has been a source of considerable practitioner commentary and some confusion.

# Part 3: The 2025 SB 5814 Service Expansion

## 3.1 Legislative Context and Signing

Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5814 was the largest single component of a broader 2025 tax package, estimated at roughly $9.4 billion in new revenue over the biennium, that Washington's Democratic-controlled legislature enacted in response to a projected operating budget shortfall. The package included a capital gains tax expansion, several B&O surcharges, and other measures. SB 5814 itself was projected to generate approximately $2.9 billion over the next budget and $4.7 billion over four years, making it the largest single revenue item in the package.

The bill was introduced in the 2025 regular session. It moved through committees in April 2025, passed the Senate 27-22 along largely party lines, moved through the House with amendments, and returned to the Senate for final concurrence. Governor Bob Ferguson signed it on **May 20, 2025**. Most provisions took effect on **October 1, 2025**, giving businesses approximately four months and ten days from signing to implementation.

The political context is important. Governor Ferguson, a Democrat who had taken office in January 2025 after a career as Attorney General, had campaigned on fiscal responsibility and had initially expressed skepticism about some elements of the tax package. He signed SB 5814 with a public statement acknowledging the structural problems it created for small businesses but arguing that the state's fiscal situation left no workable alternative. The state research council and several practitioner organizations publicly criticized the compressed implementation timeline.

## 3.2 The Structure of the Reform

SB 5814 does not restructure the sales tax. It does something more surgical: it amends the statutory definition of "retail sale" in RCW 82.04.050 to include an enumerated list of new service categories, and it repeals several exclusions that had previously sheltered digital services from the tax.

The mechanism is important because it tells us what the reform is and is not:

- **It is not a gross receipts tax.** Washington's B&O tax already functions as a gross receipts tax, but SB 5814 works within the retail sales tax architecture, not outside it.
- **It is not a general services tax.** Only enumerated service categories become taxable. Anything not enumerated remains outside the base.
- **It operates through the retail sale definition.** When a service is pulled into "retail sale," three consequences follow automatically: (a) the seller must collect sales tax from the buyer, (b) the seller's gross receipts from that service are classified under the Retailing B&O classification (currently 0.471 percent) instead of whatever classification they were under previously (often Service and Other Activities at 1.5 to 1.75 percent), and (c) the buyer may owe use tax if the seller does not collect.
- **It changes the B&O classification for affected services.** This is a feature some practitioners overlook: for many categories, the B&O effect of moving from Service and Other Activities to Retailing is actually a net B&O *reduction* for the seller, partly offset by the new sales tax collection obligation. The bill's architecture pushes the incidence of the new tax onto buyers while marginally lowering the B&O burden on sellers.

## 3.4 Advertising Services

Advertising services are the category most likely to attract the attention of tech companies, marketing agencies, and media buyers. Under the new definition, "advertising services" refers broadly to all digital and non-digital services related to the creation, preparation, production, or dissemination of advertisements. The statutory enumeration includes:

- Acquisition of advertising space
- Art direction and graphic design
- Layout and mechanical preparation
- Placement and production supervision
- Referrals (including online referrals)
- Rendering of advice on advertising campaigns
- Acquisition of advertising space on the internet
- Online referrals, including cost-per-click and cost-per-action arrangements
- Search engine marketing
- Lead generation optimization
- Web campaign planning
- Monitoring and evaluation of website traffic for purposes of determining the effectiveness of an advertising campaign

**Critical exclusions.** The bill excludes several traditional advertising channels from the new taxable category, which has become a flashpoint for legal challenges:

- Newspapers (print)
- Printing and publishing
- Radio advertising
- Television advertising (including cable and streaming under certain interpretations)
- Out-of-home advertising: billboards, transit displays, fixed signage
- Web hosting (excluded from IT services as well)
- Domain name registration

The asymmetry between taxable digital advertising and exempt traditional advertising is the explicit target of a September 2025 lawsuit filed by Comcast alleging that the selective taxation violates the federal Internet Tax Freedom Act (ITFA), which prohibits states from imposing "discriminatory" taxes on electronic commerce. Comcast's argument is that streaming video advertising is taxed while functionally equivalent cable or broadcast advertising is not. The lawsuit was pending as of late 2025; a similar challenge to Maryland's 2021 digital advertising tax resulted in a trial-court ruling striking down the tax on ITFA and First Amendment grounds before being reversed on jurisdictional grounds. Washington's narrower statutory approach may or may not survive ITFA scrutiny—that question will likely take years to resolve.

**B2B versus B2C.** SB 5814's advertising provision applies to both business-to-business and business-to-consumer advertising. There is no small-seller exemption within the statute. A local marketing agency selling campaign management services to a small Seattle bakery is, as of October 1, 2025, required to collect Washington sales tax on those services at the bakery's address rate.

**Sourcing.** Advertising services are sourced to the buyer's location. For a multistate advertiser, the portion of an advertising service allocable to Washington is taxable at Washington rates. The DOR interim guidance for advertising services, issued in late September 2025, specifies that practitioners should use reasonable allocation methods where exact allocation is not feasible.

## 3.5 Information Technology Services

The IT services category is perhaps the most sweeping in scope. The statutory language, as interpreted by the DOR in its initial guidance, includes:

- Help desk support (Level 1, Level 2, Level 3)
- Network assistance and administration
- IT consulting of all kinds
- System administration and managed services
- Data processing
- Data entry
- IT training, whether in-person or remote, whether synchronous or asynchronous
- Virtual IT support (remote troubleshooting, remote patching)
- Information technology contract work

**Exclusions.** The statute excludes from IT services:

- Web hosting
- Domain name registration
- Payment processing services
- Telehealth and telemedicine services (newly carved out of DAS to avoid pulling medical IT into the base)

The web hosting and domain registration carveouts were added during legislative negotiation after small ISPs and hosting companies testified that taxing these services would drive customers to out-of-state providers. The carveouts are narrow: they protect hosting and registration as such, but related services (managed hosting with support, custom website development, IT consulting layered on top of hosting) remain taxable.

**The "managed services" interpretation problem.** One of the most contested questions in the early implementation period has been where managed IT services sit relative to the carveouts. A typical managed services provider (MSP) contract bundles hosting, monitoring, patching, help desk, and occasional custom development into a single monthly fee. The DOR interim guidance indicates that the bundled service is generally taxable unless the hosting and registration components are separately stated on the invoice and clearly allocable. This has driven a wave of contract restructuring and invoice redesign across the MSP industry.

**Sourcing.** IT services are sourced to the customer's location. A Seattle MSP serving a customer in Spokane charges the Spokane rate on the portion of services delivered to that customer.

**Impact on Washington-based tech sector.** This is the provision most directly painful to Washington's tech economy. The state is home to Microsoft, Amazon, and hundreds of smaller software and services companies. For B2B sellers the pain is partly theoretical—their customers pay the tax—but for the sellers themselves, the compliance cost is substantial: invoice redesign, billing system updates, exemption certificate collection, nexus analysis for out-of-state customers, and ongoing audit exposure. For B2C sellers (a smaller share of the Washington tech base) the tax is a direct price increase on Washington consumers.

## 3.6 Custom Website Development

Custom website development is pulled in as a separate enumerated category even though it overlaps substantially with both IT services and custom software. The statutory language encompasses:

- Website design
- Website development and coding
- Website support services
- Ongoing maintenance of custom websites

**Exclusions:**
- Web hosting (as noted above)
- Domain registration (as noted above)

The rationale for listing custom website development separately from IT services is essentially defensive: the drafters wanted to ensure that no argument could be made that website development fell into some gap between the software provisions and the general IT services provision. By enumerating it separately, they closed that potential loophole.

## 3.7 Live Presentations

The "live presentations" category is conceptually the most novel and arguably the most culturally significant of the SB 5814 additions. It pulls into the sales tax base a category of commerce that has never before been taxed in Washington: live instructional and presentational events. The statutory language covers:

- In-person workshops, seminars, and courses
- Online workshops and webinars with real-time interaction
- Professional development courses
- Live presentations of all kinds where there is "real-time interaction" between presenter and attendees

**Exclusions:**
- Preschool through higher education classes (meaning formal tuition at accredited institutions)
- Performances (concerts, theater, stand-up comedy—these were already taxable under amusement/recreation)
- Sports events (already taxable under amusement/recreation)
- Tutoring services (one-on-one tutoring, distinct from group instruction)

**The real-time interaction test.** The key interpretive question is what counts as "real-time interaction." The DOR guidance indicates that a live webinar with chat Q&A is taxable; a pre-recorded video course without live interaction is taxable as a digital product (as it has been since 2009) but not as a live presentation; a live streaming performance is typically handled as an amusement charge. The line between live-interactive and pre-recorded has been the subject of considerable practitioner commentary, particularly because many modern training products blur the distinction (live kickoff session, asynchronous modules, live Q&A wrap-up—is that one live presentation, multiple transactions, or a bundled service?).

**Impact on educational and professional development providers.** Independent consultants, continuing education providers, professional associations, skill-training companies, and online course platforms with Washington customers have had to evaluate whether their offerings fall under this provision. Many organizations that had previously not collected Washington sales tax at all—because they believed themselves to be in the education business and therefore exempt—discovered during September 2025 that they had a compliance obligation starting October 1.

**Sourcing.** Live presentations are sourced to where the attendee participates. For an in-person event, that is the event location. For an online webinar, it is the attendee's location. Multi-attendee webinars must therefore be allocated across attendee locations for tax purposes. This is administratively complex and has driven many smaller providers to consolidate on platforms that handle the allocation automatically.

## 3.8 Investigation, Security, and Armored Car Services

The investigation and security category has a simpler structure than the IT and advertising categories because the underlying services are more clearly defined. It includes:

- Private investigation services
- Security guard services
- Background check services (pre-employment, tenant, criminal)
- Armored car transport
- Alarm monitoring and other security systems services
- Executive protection

**Exclusions:**
- Locksmith services (a noteworthy carveout)

The locksmith carveout reflects lobbying by the locksmith trade association, which argued that locksmithing was already substantially burdened by regulation and that most locksmith work was closer to installation labor on tangible goods (which is handled differently under the retail sales tax) than to security services.

**Sourcing.** Security services are typically sourced to where the service is performed.

## 3.9 Temporary Staffing Services

Temporary staffing services—the supply of contract workers for short-term assignments—became taxable as of October 1, 2025. The definition covers:

- Temp-to-perm arrangements (the temp portion)
- Short-term contract labor placement
- Day labor services
- Long-term contract placement where the staffing agency remains the employer of record

**Exclusions:**
- Hospital staffing (a significant carveout, reflecting healthcare's general shelter from the reform)
- Permanent hires / direct placement (where the placement firm acts as a recruiter and the worker becomes an employee of the client)
- Independent contractor arrangements (where the worker is self-employed and not supplied through a staffing firm)
- Paymaster-only services (where the firm processes payroll but does not supply workers)

**The employer-of-record question.** The taxability distinction often turns on who is the legal employer. If the staffing agency is the employer of record—withholding taxes, providing benefits, carrying workers' comp—the arrangement is taxable. If the worker is placed and then becomes an employee of the client, the placement fee is typically exempt as a recruitment service. The distinction is important because many firms use hybrid arrangements that may fall on either side of the line.

**Sourcing.** Temporary staffing services are sourced to where the worker performs the work, which is typically the client's location.

## 3.10 Custom Software and Customization of Prewritten Software

The custom software provision overturns one of the most long-standing lines in Washington sales tax law. Under prior law:

- **Canned software** (prewritten, off-the-shelf) was taxable, whether delivered on tangible media, downloaded, or accessed remotely. This has been the rule since at least 2009, and arguably longer.
- **Custom software** (software written specifically for a single customer) was exempt. It was treated as a professional service, akin to consulting, and taxed under B&O but not sales tax.
- **Customization of prewritten software** occupied an ambiguous middle ground. Modifications to off-the-shelf software could be exempt if they were substantial and separately stated on the invoice, but the taxability depended on the specific facts.

Under SB 5814, effective October 1, 2025:

- **Canned / prewritten software**: Taxable (no change)
- **Custom software**: **Now taxable**
- **Customization of prewritten software**: **Now taxable**
- **Unmodified off-the-shelf software**: Still taxable (no change)

The policy consequence is significant. Every category of software is now in the sales tax base. The old "if it's custom, it's a service" distinction, which had driven thirty years of tax planning by software companies, is gone. A Seattle software development shop billing a Washington enterprise customer for a custom application now collects sales tax on the entire invoice, where previously the invoice would have been sales-tax-free.

**Sourcing.** Custom software is sourced to where the customer uses it. For a single-site deployment, this is straightforward. For enterprise customers with multi-state deployments, allocation is required, which practitioners have found to be one of the most difficult aspects of the reform.

## 3.11 Digital Automated Services: Removing the Human-Effort Exclusion

Washington's 2009 digital products legislation created a category called "digital automated services" (DAS), defined as services transferred electronically that use some combination of hardware and software to automate a process. DAS was made taxable in 2009 along with other digital products, but the legislation included several exclusions, the most important of which was the "primarily human effort" exclusion: services that relied primarily on human effort, even when delivered electronically, were excluded from DAS and therefore remained exempt.

This exclusion was the doctrinal home of most services that "happened to be delivered online." A consulting engagement delivered by video conference, an online training course taught by a live instructor, a research report compiled by human analysts and delivered as a PDF—all of these were sheltered by the human-effort exclusion.

SB 5814 repeals several DAS exclusions:

- The "primarily human effort" exclusion is repealed
- The advertising exclusion is repealed
- The live presentations exclusion is repealed
- The data processing exclusion is repealed

And it adds one new exclusion:

- Telehealth and telemedicine services are newly excluded from DAS

The repeal of the human-effort exclusion is the DAS-side companion to the enumerated-service additions. It ensures that services which might otherwise fall between the enumerated categories are still captured. The telehealth carveout reflects a policy decision to shelter medical services from the reform.

## 3.12 Existing Contracts and Transition Relief

Recognizing that the compressed implementation schedule (signing on May 20, effective October 1) would create significant disruption for long-term contracts, the legislature included a transition relief provision for contracts existing before October 1, 2025.

The DOR issued an interim guidance statement specifically addressing pre-October 1 contracts. The key rules are:

1. **Qualifying contracts** are contracts executed before October 1, 2025 that obligate the provider to deliver services that would be newly taxable under SB 5814.
2. **Unaltered qualifying contracts** are exempt from the new sales tax and the corresponding Retailing B&O classification through **March 31, 2026**.
3. **Starting April 1, 2026**, even unaltered qualifying contracts become subject to the sales tax and Retailing B&O on the services delivered on and after that date.
4. **Altered contracts** lose their qualifying status upon alteration. An alteration includes any amendment, extension, renewal, change order, scope change, or similar modification.
5. **Auto-renewing contracts** typically qualify through the first renewal but may lose qualifying status depending on how the renewal is characterized.

The March 31, 2026 cutoff effectively gives businesses six months from the October 1 effective date to continue invoicing legacy contracts without collecting tax, but no more. The interim guidance cautions that the six-month window should not be used for business-as-usual: contracts should be renegotiated, priced for the new tax, and tax collection systems should be in place well before the April 1 deadline.

## 3.13 The Prepayment Obligation

One of the more novel features of SB 5814 is a prepayment requirement for larger sellers. Businesses that generate $3 million or more in taxable sales during 2026 must prepay 80 percent of their collected sales taxes by June 25, 2027, with a 10 percent penalty for non-compliance. This represents an accelerated cash-flow obligation—essentially, the state collects nine months of tax in six months for large sellers during the first post-reform year.

Practitioners have noted that this provision is almost certain to be challenged by affected businesses. The legal theory for a challenge would likely be that the prepayment obligation, by requiring tax collection before the underlying transactions actually occur, creates a due-process problem. As of the writing of this document, no such challenge had yet been filed, but the issue is one to watch.

## 3.14 Implementation Challenges

The four-month implementation window between signing (May 20, 2025) and effective date (October 1, 2025) was, by widespread practitioner consensus, inadequate for a reform of this scope. The specific challenges have included:

**Invoice system updates.** Billing systems had to be reconfigured to apply sales tax to previously exempt service categories. For large software companies with complex billing architectures, this was a multi-month engineering project compressed into a few weeks.

**Contract language.** Long-term contracts with fixed pricing had to be amended or priced to account for the new tax. Where contracts prohibited price increases, vendors had to absorb the tax (reducing margin) or negotiate amendments (requiring customer cooperation).

**Nexus analysis for out-of-state customers.** A Washington-based service provider selling to out-of-state customers has to determine whether the customer is in a state where the service is sourced to the customer's location and whether the customer has a Washington nexus exposure. This cascades into multistate compliance analysis that some smaller providers had never had to perform.

**Exemption certificate collection.** Resale exemptions apply to services just as they apply to goods. A Washington IT services firm selling help desk services to a reseller (for example, a managed services aggregator) can accept the sale as a resale transaction and not collect tax. But the firm must obtain and retain a valid reseller permit from the buyer. Collecting permits from hundreds or thousands of existing customers on short notice has been a significant operational burden.

**Customer communication.** Many customers have never paid sales tax on the services they purchase from their vendors and reacted strongly to the first invoices that included the tax. Customer support and billing teams have absorbed a wave of questions, objections, and disputes in the early months of the reform.

**DOR guidance lag.** The DOR has published interim guidance statements for most of the newly taxable categories, but the guidance has rolled out over several months, sometimes after the effective date, and has included clarifications and revisions. Practitioners have had to make interim judgments under uncertainty and in some cases correct their positions later.

**Sourcing complexity.** As noted throughout this section, the sourcing rules for each newly taxable service category are often ambiguous. The DOR has taken the position that reasonable allocation methods should be used where exact allocation is not feasible, but "reasonable" has been subject to interpretation.

**Audit exposure.** Because the reform is so new, practitioners have uniformly counseled clients to document their sourcing decisions, their exempt-sale decisions, and their contract categorization decisions carefully. The first wave of DOR audits on SB 5814 compliance is expected to begin in 2027 or 2028.

# Part 4: Nexus in the Post-Wayfair Framework

## 4.1 Wayfair and the End of Physical Presence

*South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.*, 585 U.S. ___ (2018), was decided on June 21, 2018. In a 5-4 majority opinion written by Justice Anthony Kennedy, the U.S. Supreme Court overturned the physical presence requirement that had governed state sales tax collection authority since *National Bellas Hess v. Illinois* (1967) and *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota* (1992). The Court held that states may require out-of-state sellers to collect sales tax when the seller has "substantial nexus" with the state, and that substantial nexus can be established by economic activity alone—without any physical presence.

The *Wayfair* decision specifically upheld South Dakota's economic nexus statute, which required out-of-state sellers with (a) more than $100,000 in annual sales to South Dakota or (b) more than 200 annual transactions to South Dakota to register, collect, and remit South Dakota sales tax. The $100,000 / 200-transaction threshold became a de facto template that dozens of states adopted in the months following the decision.

The decision's reasoning rested heavily on three facts about the modern commercial environment that the Court found had changed since *Quill*:

1. The volume and ubiquity of internet commerce had made the "substantial nexus" analysis untenable as a physical-presence test.
2. Compliance software had dramatically reduced the practical burden of collecting tax in multiple jurisdictions (the very problem SSUTA had been designed to address).
3. The *Quill* rule had distorted competition in favor of out-of-state sellers and starved states of revenue that the current constitutional framework no longer justified withholding.

## 4.2 Washington's Economic Nexus Rule

Washington implemented economic nexus on **October 1, 2018**, shortly after the *Wayfair* decision was handed down. The Washington rule originally mirrored South Dakota's:

- $100,000 in annual gross receipts from Washington sales, **or**
- 200 annual transactions

The 200-transaction threshold was eliminated on **March 14, 2019**, leaving the current single-threshold rule: any out-of-state seller with $100,000 or more in cumulative gross receipts from Washington sales in the current or prior calendar year must register with the Washington Department of Revenue and begin collecting Washington sales tax on all taxable Washington sales.

**Counting rules.** Marketplace sales are included in the $100,000 threshold calculation, even though the marketplace itself is responsible for collecting the tax on those sales. This means a seller who sells $80,000 through Amazon and $25,000 through its own website would cross the threshold and must register to collect on its direct sales.

**Registration once, collection forever.** Once a seller crosses the threshold and registers, the registration obligation continues even if the seller later drops below the threshold. Sellers can close their Washington registration only through a formal voluntary disclosure or account closure process.

**B&O implications.** Crossing the sales tax nexus threshold usually also creates B&O nexus, which is a separate tax. B&O nexus has historically been an even lower threshold in Washington, and with the 2018 post-Wayfair environment it is effectively collapsed into the sales tax threshold for most practical purposes.

## 4.3 Marketplace Facilitator Rules

Washington's marketplace facilitator statute was one of the earliest in the nation. Enacted in 2018 and refined several times since, it requires "marketplace facilitators"—platforms that facilitate sales between third-party sellers and buyers—to collect and remit Washington sales tax on sales made through the platform on behalf of their third-party sellers.

The statute captures the major e-commerce platforms:

- Amazon (as both a direct retailer and a marketplace)
- eBay
- Etsy
- Walmart Marketplace
- Target Plus
- Shopify (for certain facilitated transactions)
- Airbnb and VRBO (for lodging)
- DoorDash, Uber Eats, Grubhub (for restaurant delivery)
- StubHub (for event tickets)

When a marketplace facilitator collects tax on a third-party sale, the third-party seller does not need to collect the same tax again. The sale is counted once, toward the marketplace's remittance. The third-party seller is still required to report the sale on its own return but can claim a deduction for sales where the marketplace collected.

The marketplace facilitator framework has significantly simplified compliance for small third-party sellers, most of whom would otherwise face substantial registration and filing burdens across many states. It has also produced a modest but real increase in overall state sales tax collections by capturing transactions that individual small sellers were unlikely to report accurately.

## 4.4 Physical Nexus: Still a Thing

Physical presence nexus did not disappear after *Wayfair*. It was supplemented, not replaced. A business with physical presence in Washington still has nexus regardless of sales volume. Physical presence includes:

- Employees in the state (including remote workers who live in Washington)
- Offices, warehouses, stores, or other fixed locations
- Inventory stored in the state (including inventory held by Amazon FBA in Washington fulfillment centers)
- Owned or leased real property
- Owned tangible personal property used in business
- Regular solicitation through traveling salespeople
- Trade show attendance exceeding de minimis thresholds
- Ownership of an affiliate that creates nexus through related-party transactions

The physical presence rules retain practical importance because they create nexus at the very first transaction, not only after $100,000 in sales. A small out-of-state retailer selling just a few thousand dollars to Washington customers can still have nexus if it keeps inventory at an Amazon Washington warehouse.

# Part 5: Use Tax

## 5.1 The Use Tax as Sales Tax Complement

The Washington use tax is the structural complement to the retail sales tax. It applies the same rate (state 6.5 percent plus applicable local rates) to the same transactions, but it is imposed on the *buyer* rather than the *seller*, and it applies when a Washington sales tax was not collected on a purchase that should have been subject to one.

The use tax is paid on:

- Purchases from out-of-state sellers who do not collect Washington sales tax
- Purchases from out-of-state sellers below the $100,000 economic nexus threshold
- Purchases of goods brought into Washington after being purchased in another state
- Goods withdrawn from inventory for a taxable use (e.g., a retailer uses inventory as office supplies)
- Goods converted from an exempt use to a taxable use

The use tax rate is always equal to the sales tax rate that would have applied had the sale been sourced to the Washington location of use. In practice this means the buyer's address in Washington determines the rate. A Mukilteo resident paying use tax on an out-of-state purchase owes 10.60 percent; a Spokane resident owes 8.90 percent.

## 5.2 Consumer Use Tax and Business Use Tax

Washington distinguishes between consumer use tax and business use tax for administrative purposes, though the legal obligation is the same.

**Consumer use tax** is owed by individual Washington residents on purchases they make from non-collecting out-of-state sellers. It is reported on the individual's Washington consumer use tax return (a standalone form) or, in rare cases, on a line of the individual's state tax filing. In practice, consumer use tax has historically been massively underreported: academic studies have estimated consumer use tax compliance at single-digit percentages, with the vast majority of individual Washington residents paying nothing even when they owe significant amounts.

The rise of e-commerce from the mid-1990s through the mid-2010s created a large and growing consumer use tax enforcement gap. The 2018 *Wayfair* decision and Washington's economic nexus and marketplace facilitator rules have substantially closed this gap for most common e-commerce transactions, because the burden has shifted from the individual consumer to the marketplace or remote seller. Consumers still owe use tax on purchases from small, non-collecting out-of-state sellers, but the total volume of such transactions has declined dramatically.

**Business use tax** is owed by Washington businesses on their taxable purchases from non-collecting sellers. It is reported on the Combined Excise Tax Return on dedicated lines for both state and local use tax. Unlike consumer use tax, business use tax compliance is much stronger, partly because businesses are routinely audited and partly because use tax is built into modern accounting and purchasing systems.

## 5.3 Deferred Sales Tax

A specific quirk of Washington's system is the "deferred sales tax." This is sales tax that *should* have been collected by the seller at the point of sale but was not, and that is now owed by the buyer. The mechanism is:

1. A Washington-registered seller fails to collect sales tax on a taxable sale (for example, misclassifies the transaction as exempt).
2. The buyer receives the goods or services without paying tax.
3. On audit, the DOR determines that tax should have been collected.
4. The DOR may pursue the seller (for the uncollected tax plus penalties) or the buyer (through a deferred sales tax assessment).

Deferred sales tax is reported on the Combined Excise Tax Return on a dedicated line, distinct from use tax. Businesses that discover they were under-charged by a seller can self-assess the deferred sales tax rather than going back to the seller to correct.

The distinction between deferred sales tax and use tax is technical but matters for audit and interest calculation purposes. Use tax is a buyer-level obligation that exists independently of seller behavior. Deferred sales tax is a seller-failure obligation that shifts to the buyer because the seller should have collected.

## 5.4 The Use Tax Enforcement Gap

The consumer use tax compliance problem is not unique to Washington. Every state that imposes a sales tax faces the same issue, and the academic literature on consumer use tax compliance is remarkably consistent across jurisdictions: individual consumers do not pay use tax unless the tax is collected at the point of sale or there is an automated reminder mechanism.

Washington has tried several strategies to improve consumer use tax compliance:

1. **Voluntary disclosure through tax filings.** The state provides a consumer use tax return form and publicizes it. Uptake is minimal.
2. **Notice-and-report requirements.** The state requires non-collecting remote sellers above certain thresholds to notify Washington customers of their use tax obligation and to report purchase data to DOR. The usefulness of this has declined with *Wayfair* because most affected sellers now simply collect the tax directly.
3. **Education and outreach.** The DOR periodically publishes guidance and reminders, particularly around holiday shopping seasons.
4. **Enforcement against high-value items.** Boats, aircraft, and vehicles purchased out-of-state are typically caught at registration because the state will not title or register them without proof of sales or use tax payment.

The 2018 post-Wayfair framework substantially closed the gap for most common consumer transactions. Marketplace facilitators now collect on the largest share of e-commerce volume. Out-of-state sellers above the $100,000 threshold now collect directly. The residual gap is limited to purchases from small out-of-state sellers and from foreign sellers, and those transactions represent a relatively modest share of total e-commerce.

# Part 6: Exemptions and the Reseller Permit

## 6.1 The Major Exemptions

Washington's sales tax includes a long list of statutory exemptions. The major ones fall into several categories.

**Resale exemption.** Sales of property for resale in the regular course of business are exempt. The buyer presents a reseller permit to the seller at the time of purchase. This is the single most important exemption in practical terms and is discussed in detail below.

**Manufacturing input exemption.** Sales of ingredients or components that become part of a manufactured product are exempt. Sales of certain machinery and equipment used directly in manufacturing are also exempt under the M&E exemption.

**Food for home consumption.** Unprepared grocery foods are exempt. This exemption has a long and complex history; it was briefly repealed during the 1982 fiscal crisis and restored by citizen initiative. The precise definition of "food for home consumption" versus "prepared food" involves rules about heating, serving, and preparation that generate significant audit disputes.

**Prescription drugs and certain medical devices.** Prescription drugs are exempt. Certain medical equipment and devices are exempt. Over-the-counter drugs are generally taxable.

**Sales to the federal government.** Sales directly to federal agencies are exempt under the constitutional doctrine that prevents state taxation of federal instrumentalities. Sales to federal employees on federal business are not exempt unless paid with a federal account.

**Sales to qualifying nonprofits.** Certain nonprofit organizations qualify for sales tax exemptions on specific transaction types. The exemption is narrower than many practitioners assume—Washington does not have a general nonprofit exemption the way many states do.

**Interstate commerce exemptions.** Sales where the buyer takes delivery outside Washington are sourced outside the state and therefore not subject to Washington tax. Sales that would be subject to Washington tax but for a specific constitutional protection (such as sales in foreign commerce) are exempt.

**Trade-in allowances.** The value of a traded-in item is excluded from the taxable selling price of the new item (for example, trading in a used car when buying a new one).

**Newspapers.** Newspapers are exempt from sales tax. This exemption has historical roots in First Amendment considerations and continues.

**Motor vehicle fuel.** Motor vehicle fuel is taxed under a separate motor fuel tax, not the general sales tax. Diesel, gasoline, and biofuels are all treated under the separate motor fuel framework.

**Certain services specific to professional activities.** Examples include legal services (not in the base), medical services (not in the base), and certain other professional services.

**Aircraft and watercraft used in interstate commerce.** Commercial aircraft and large commercial vessels engaged in interstate or international commerce are exempt.

**Farm machinery and products.** Certain farm inputs and machinery are exempt.

**Sales to Indian tribal members on reservation.** Sales to enrolled members of federally recognized tribes delivered on the tribe's reservation are exempt under federal law and Washington's implementing statute.

## 6.2 The Reseller Permit System

The reseller permit is Washington's mechanism for documenting that a sale qualifies for the resale exemption. It replaces the older "resale certificate" approach that was used before 2009.

**Who can obtain a reseller permit.** Retailers, wholesalers, and qualified manufacturers that are registered with the Washington DOR and have an active Unified Business Identifier (UBI) can apply for a reseller permit through My DOR. Permits are free. Eligibility depends on the business type and the nature of the business's activity; not every registered business is automatically eligible, and some new businesses must wait before qualifying.

**Permit duration.** Reseller permits are generally valid for four years. New businesses and construction contractors may initially receive permits valid for two years. The permit number encodes the expiration year in its last two digits, so a quick visual check tells the holder when renewal is required.

**Using a permit.** A buyer wishing to claim the resale exemption presents the permit (or provides the permit number) to the seller at the time of sale. The seller must record the exemption and retain documentation. The permit itself serves as the exemption certificate.

**Verifying permits.** Sellers are required to verify that a presented permit is valid. The DOR provides two verification tools:

1. **Individual business lookup** on the DOR website, which returns the validity status of a single permit.
2. **Bulk reseller permit verification service**, which allows sellers to upload a file of permit numbers and receive a validity report. This is essential for large wholesalers with thousands of reseller customers.

Verification must be performed at least once per calendar year for each customer who presents a permit. A seller that fails to verify and accepts an invalid permit becomes liable for the uncollected tax.

**Misuse and enforcement.** Using a reseller permit for a purchase that is not genuinely for resale is a serious matter. The buyer becomes liable for the tax plus a 50 percent penalty, and repeat misuse can lead to permit revocation. The DOR aggressively audits reseller permit usage in industries where misuse is historically common (construction, wholesale distribution, certain retail).

## 6.3 Exemption Certificates

Beyond the reseller permit, Washington uses a family of exemption certificates for specific exempt transaction types. The most common are:

- **Buyer's Retail Sales Tax Exemption Certificate** (form ST-101 or equivalent) for general exempt purchases
- **Manufacturer's Sales and Use Tax Exemption Certificate** for M&E and manufacturing input exemptions
- **Farm Machinery Exemption Certificate**
- **Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement Certificate of Exemption** for transactions with out-of-state sellers subject to SSUTA rules
- **Digital Products and Remote Access Software Exemption Certificate** for specific digital product purchases
- **Nonresident Exemption Certificate** (historical; largely phased out)

Exemption certificates must be properly completed at the time of sale and retained by the seller for audit purposes. An incomplete or missing certificate typically results in the seller being held liable for the uncollected tax on audit.

# Part 7: Digital Products, Software, SaaS, and Streaming

## 7.1 The 2009 Digital Products Tax

Engrossed Substitute House Bill 2075, enacted in 2009 and effective July 26, 2009, brought digital products into the Washington retail sales tax base. Prior to 2009, Washington had taxed tangible personal property (including canned software on CDs and DVDs) but had struggled to tax downloads and streaming because of definitional gaps. The 2009 legislation rewrote the tax base to cover:

- Digital goods (music, videos, books, games, and similar products transferred electronically)
- Digital audiovisual works
- Digital books
- Digital automated services (DAS)
- Remote access software

The 2009 legislation was framed as a "modernization" of the tax to match consumer behavior, which was rapidly migrating from physical media to downloads and streaming. It was one of the earliest comprehensive state digital products taxes in the country and served as a model for similar legislation in other states.

The 2009 bill also addressed the "prewritten software" question directly. Prewritten software accessed remotely (Remote Access Software, or RAS) was classified as taxable under the retail sales tax, eliminating an ambiguity that had existed under prior law about whether cloud-delivered software was a taxable product or a nontaxable service.

## 7.2 Canned vs. Custom Software: The Historical Line

For decades, Washington maintained a distinction between **canned software** (prewritten, off-the-shelf, designed for general use) and **custom software** (written specifically for a single customer). The distinction had significant tax consequences:

- **Canned software** was classified as tangible personal property (or, after 2009, as a digital good) and was taxable.
- **Custom software** was classified as a service (specifically, a programming service) and was not taxable.

The distinction drove considerable tax planning. Software companies structured engagements to emphasize the custom nature of the work when they wanted to preserve the service classification. Invoices distinguished between "license fees" (taxable canned software) and "programming services" (non-taxable custom software). Customers sometimes rejected bundled offerings in favor of separately-stated fees to maintain the exemption.

The line was always somewhat artificial. Much modern software development is neither purely canned nor purely custom: it involves customer-specific configurations of general-purpose platforms, integrations between off-the-shelf systems, modifications of prewritten code, and combinations of licensed components with custom work. The DOR had developed a body of rulings to navigate these cases, but the distinction was always fragile.

SB 5814 eliminates the distinction by making both custom software and customization of prewritten software taxable. As of October 1, 2025, every category of software sold in Washington is in the sales tax base. Tax planning based on the canned/custom distinction is now historical.

## 7.3 SaaS and Remote Access Software

Software as a Service—the delivery of software over the internet on a subscription basis, without the customer installing or owning a copy—has been taxable in Washington since 2009. The statutory vehicle is the "Remote Access Software" (RAS) definition, which covers software that resides on the seller's (or a third party's) server and that the customer pays to access and use remotely.

RAS is treated as prewritten software for sales tax purposes. Sales of RAS are subject to:

- Retail sales tax at the combined rate for the customer's location
- Retailing B&O tax at 0.471 percent

There are a few specific features of the RAS framework worth noting:

**Sourcing.** RAS sales are sourced to the primary place of use by the customer. For a multi-location customer, this requires allocation. The DOR has published guidance on reasonable allocation methods, including per-user counts, per-location headcount, and revenue-based allocation.

**Exemption for certain uses.** Washington offers a narrow exemption for RAS purchased by a business that will resell access to its own customers (a reseller-type exemption specific to the digital products category).

**Interaction with SB 5814.** RAS was already taxable before SB 5814, so the 2025 reform does not change its basic treatment. But SB 5814 captures adjacent services—customization of prewritten software, IT services, training—that are often sold alongside RAS. The result is that RAS subscriptions with any bundled service component now typically include multiple taxable line items where previously some of those line items might have been exempt.

## 7.4 Streaming and Subscription Services

Subscription-based entertainment services have been taxable in Washington since the 2009 digital products legislation. The statutory classification depends on the specific nature of the service:

- **Netflix, Disney+, Hulu, Max, and similar subscription video-on-demand services** are taxable as digital audiovisual works.
- **Spotify, Apple Music, and similar music streaming services** are taxable as digital audio works.
- **Audible and similar audiobook subscriptions** are taxable as digital audio works.
- **Kindle Unlimited and similar e-book subscriptions** are taxable as digital books.
- **Gaming services (Xbox Game Pass, PlayStation Plus)** are taxable as digital products.
- **Live streaming of sports or events** may be taxable as an amusement charge or as a digital product, depending on specific facts.

The treatment of subscription services has not changed under SB 5814. What has changed is the adjacent category of training, tutorials, and live-interactive educational content on streaming platforms. A pre-recorded cooking video on a streaming service remains taxable as a digital product, as it was before. But a live-interactive cooking class on the same platform is now a taxable "live presentation" in addition to whatever digital product treatment applies to any recorded component.

# Part 8: Administration and Compliance

## 8.1 Destination-Based Sourcing in Practice

Washington's destination sourcing rules require sellers to determine the correct local combined rate based on where the buyer receives the product or service. For most physical goods transactions, this is the delivery address. The rules get more complex for:

**Services.** Most services are sourced to the location where the customer receives the benefit. For on-site services (landscaping, construction, repair performed at the customer's location), this is the service location. For remote services (IT support, consulting, training delivered by video conference), this is typically the customer's billing address or, where available, the customer's primary place of use.

**Digital products.** Digital products are sourced to the customer's primary address, typically the billing address or the address of the device used to access the product.

**Subscription services.** Subscription services are sourced to the customer's billing address unless the customer has provided a different service address.

**Multi-location customers.** When a business customer has operations in multiple locations, services and digital products delivered for multi-location use must be allocated. The DOR guidance permits several reasonable allocation methods:

- Per-user or per-seat allocation
- Headcount allocation
- Revenue-based allocation
- Equal allocation across locations

The seller must be able to document its allocation methodology on audit. Changes in methodology mid-year or retroactive restatements can create audit exposure.

## 8.2 The DOR Rate Lookup Tool

The Washington Department of Revenue publishes a rate lookup tool on its website that provides the correct combined rate for any Washington address. The tool:

- Accepts addresses in several formats
- Returns the combined rate, including the state, county, city, and special district components
- Provides a printable certificate that can be used for audit documentation
- Is updated quarterly when new local rates take effect

The rate lookup tool is essential for any Washington business that sells across multiple localities. Using the tool to determine the correct rate provides a "safe harbor" for the seller: if the DOR's own tool returns a specific rate for an address, the seller can rely on that rate and is not liable for an incorrect calculation that results from an error in the tool itself.

For high-volume sellers, the rate lookup is typically integrated into the billing system through an API or by importing the DOR's quarterly rate file. Certified Service Providers under the SSUTA framework offer Washington rate calculation as part of their multistate compliance services.

## 8.3 The Combined Excise Tax Return

Washington uses a single consolidated return—the Combined Excise Tax Return (CETR)—for most state and local excise taxes. The CETR combines reporting for:

- Retail sales tax (state and local components)
- Use tax and deferred sales tax
- Business and occupation (B&O) tax (all classifications)
- Public utility tax (for certain businesses)
- Specialty taxes (litter, tobacco, etc.)

The consolidation is a feature, not a coincidence. It reflects Washington's decision to keep its excise tax administration in a single system, which reduces duplication and allows cross-checking between different tax types. A business that reports $1 million in taxable sales on the sales tax line of the CETR but only $500,000 under the Retailing B&O classification will trigger a DOR inquiry because the numbers should agree.

The CETR is structured in several sections:

1. **Gross amount** for each tax classification
2. **Deductions** claimed (with a detail schedule)
3. **Taxable amount** after deductions
4. **Tax rate** (either fixed or looked up by classification)
5. **Tax due** for each line
6. **Local tax summary** rolling up all local components

Filers must file electronically through My DOR. Paper filings are generally not accepted. Filing frequency depends on the business's annual tax liability: monthly, quarterly, or annually.

## 8.4 Registration and My DOR

Businesses doing business in Washington register through the My DOR online portal. The registration process:

1. Establishes a Business License Service (BLS) application, which covers state registration, city registration (for participating cities), and various regulatory endorsements.
2. Assigns a Unified Business Identifier (UBI), which is the state's universal business identifier.
3. Creates a tax account with DOR for excise tax filing.
4. Determines the business's filing frequency based on expected tax liability.
5. Opens access to the reseller permit application process (if eligible).

Registration is required:

- Before making any taxable sales in Washington (for businesses with physical presence)
- Within a short window after crossing the $100,000 economic nexus threshold (for remote sellers)
- Before the first reseller purchase (for businesses purchasing for resale)

Failure to register when required results in back tax liability, penalties, and potential personal liability for company officers.

# Part 9: Comparative Analysis

## 9.1 The Four Broad-Base States: Hawaii, New Mexico, South Dakota, West Virginia

Four U.S. states tax services by default under their general consumption tax, with exceptions only for services specifically exempted by statute. This is the opposite of the majority pattern, in which services are exempt by default and enumerated services are pulled into the base by specific legislation.

**Hawaii General Excise Tax (GET).** Hawaii's GET, at a general rate of 4.0 percent (plus county surcharges bringing it to 4.5 percent in some counties), is technically a tax on the privilege of doing business rather than a sales tax, but in practice it operates as a broad consumption tax that applies to almost all economic activity. The GET includes:

- Retail sales of goods
- Wholesale sales (at a reduced rate)
- Services of all kinds (legal, medical, accounting, personal, professional)
- Rent, including commercial and residential
- Insurance commissions
- Contracting

Hawaii's tax base is often cited as including 108 percent of the state's GDP, because the GET cascades through multiple transaction stages. A product may be taxed at the wholesale level and then taxed again at the retail level, producing a total burden greater than the nominal rate would suggest.

**New Mexico Gross Receipts Tax (GRT).** New Mexico's GRT, at state rates of about 4.875 percent plus local components bringing the combined rate close to 8 percent in many municipalities, has historically been even broader than Hawaii's GET. It applies to:

- All retail sales of goods
- Services, including professional services
- Research and development services
- Leasing and licensing
- Interest income (for certain financial services)

New Mexico recently restructured its GRT to be more sales-tax-like, but the underlying broad base remains. New Mexico, alone among states, is frequently said to have a tax base at or exceeding 60 percent of the state economy.

**South Dakota sales tax.** South Dakota has a 4 percent state sales tax (plus local components) and explicitly taxes services as the default rule. Its service tax base was expanded in 2016 and again in 2018 as the state navigated the legal environment around *Wayfair* (which South Dakota itself argued and won). The South Dakota tax base is estimated to include 67 percent of the state economy.

**West Virginia consumer sales and service tax.** West Virginia's combined sales and service tax at 6 percent state rate is sometimes overlooked in comparisons because its overall tax environment is modest. It nonetheless operates on the "services taxable by default" principle.

Washington is **not** one of these four states. Washington's base, even after SB 5814, is narrower than any of the four broad-base states. Many service categories remain outside the Washington base.

## 9.2 Narrow-Base Expansions: New Jersey, Connecticut, Iowa

A number of narrower-base states have expanded their sales tax bases into services over the last twenty years without going as far as the four broad-base states. These expansions provide a useful comparison for evaluating Washington's 2025 reform.

**New Jersey (2006).** New Jersey's 2006 sales tax reform added several service categories including information services, storage, investigation and security services, and certain other commercial services. The reform was controversial at the time but has proved durable; the expanded base has remained substantially in place since 2006 and is now treated as part of the state's normal fiscal structure.

**Connecticut (various years).** Connecticut has expanded its sales tax base into services in several waves, notably the 1989 expansion that added dozens of service categories. Subsequent expansions in 2011 and 2019 added digital goods, car washes, dry cleaning, and various commercial services.

**Iowa (2019).** Iowa's 2019 tax reform added digital products, ride-sharing, and several service categories to the sales tax base. It also tightened the treatment of software and cloud services.

**Kentucky (2018, 2022).** Kentucky expanded its sales tax base into services in 2018 (adding several personal and commercial services) and again in 2022 (adding more services).

**Maryland (2021).** Maryland enacted the nation's first digital advertising tax in 2021. The tax was structured differently from Washington's approach—it was an excise tax on gross receipts from digital advertising rather than an inclusion of digital advertising in the existing sales tax—and it has faced extensive legal challenges. A trial court struck down the Maryland tax on ITFA and First Amendment grounds in 2022, although that decision was reversed on jurisdictional grounds by the state's highest court. The Maryland experience has been a cautionary reference point for Washington practitioners evaluating the SB 5814 advertising provisions.

## 9.3 The "Tax Services, Not Goods" Policy Debate

Academic and policy commentary on state sales taxes has long argued that tax bases designed around tangible goods are increasingly mismatched with modern service-dominated economies. The argument has several strands:

**Equity.** A tax base that covers goods but not services effectively taxes lower-income households (who spend proportionally more on goods) more heavily than higher-income households (who spend proportionally more on services like legal advice, investment management, and elite health care).

**Neutrality.** A narrow tax base creates distortions by taxing some forms of consumption and not others. A household can effectively avoid sales tax by substituting services for goods—eating at home with prepared food delivery (some portion taxable) rather than buying groceries (exempt) and cooking them.

**Revenue stability.** As goods decline as a share of household spending and services grow, a goods-only tax base shrinks as a share of GDP, requiring either higher rates on the shrinking base or revenue from other sources.

**Economic competition.** A narrow tax base with high rates creates distortions in cross-border shopping and in business location decisions. A broad tax base with low rates is closer to economic neutrality.

Against these arguments, critics of service expansion have raised several concerns:

**Administrative complexity.** Services are inherently harder to source and to tax than goods. A sweater is delivered to an address; an IT consulting engagement may involve a team in multiple locations serving a customer in multiple locations and crossing state lines in the process.

**Compliance burden on small businesses.** Many service providers are small businesses that have never had to deal with sales tax compliance. Adding them to the base creates a significant operational burden.

**Cascading.** When business-to-business services are taxed, the tax can cascade through supply chains, with the final burden exceeding the nominal rate.

**Political acceptability.** Service taxation is politically difficult. Service providers are often vocal and organized constituencies; service consumers are often spread out and unorganized.

Washington's 2025 reform represents a relatively narrow and targeted expansion that tries to capture the highest-value service categories (tech, advertising, staffing) while leaving the most politically sensitive categories (legal, medical, accounting) alone. Whether this is the right balance is a matter of ongoing debate.

## 9.4 Washington's 2025 Reform in Historical Perspective

In the context of both Washington's own history and the broader state-tax landscape, SB 5814 is significant but not radical. It is significant because it is the largest single expansion of the Washington sales tax base in the tax's 90-year history. It is not radical because:

- It operates within the existing retail sales tax architecture rather than creating a new tax.
- It targets enumerated categories rather than shifting to a services-taxable-by-default model.
- It preserves the exemptions for professional services, healthcare, and most personal services.
- It does not change the 6.5 percent state rate.
- It conforms with SSUTA requirements.

Compared to Hawaii, New Mexico, or South Dakota, Washington's reform is modest. Compared to New Jersey in 2006 or Kentucky in 2018, Washington's reform is comparable in scope but with a notably greater emphasis on technology services—which makes sense given Washington's position as one of the most tech-intensive state economies in the country.

The political economy of the reform is also notable. SB 5814 passed a Democratic-controlled legislature with unified Democratic support and was signed by a Democratic governor. The business community, particularly the tech sector, opposed the reform but did not succeed in stopping it. The reform was framed politically as a response to a specific budget shortfall rather than as a long-term restructuring, which may or may not prove to be an accurate characterization over time. A tax base expansion of this magnitude tends to become part of the permanent fiscal structure even when it is enacted as a temporary measure.

# Part 10: Practical Commentary

Several observations for practitioners and students of state and local taxation in light of SB 5814 and its implementation experience:

**1. The era of "software is a service so it's exempt" is over.** The canned-versus-custom distinction that drove software industry tax planning for decades no longer applies in Washington. All software is in the base. Practitioners advising software companies with Washington customers should reset their mental models accordingly.

**2. B2B services are not protected from the reform.** Some practitioners in the early weeks of the reform assumed that business-to-business transactions would be less aggressively pursued than business-to-consumer transactions. The DOR's guidance has made clear that there is no B2B exemption. Resale exemptions apply in the normal course (where a service is genuinely purchased for resale), but no general B2B carveout exists.

**3. The "reasonable sourcing" standard is an audit risk.** Where sourcing is ambiguous—multi-location customers, remote services, allocation across states—practitioners must document their methodology. The DOR has stated that reasonable methods will be accepted, but "reasonable" is a term that is defined in hindsight during audit. Practitioners should document contemporaneously.

**4. Exemption certificate collection is operationally difficult for service sellers.** Goods sellers have long maintained systems for collecting and verifying reseller permits. Services sellers, particularly those who have historically had no sales tax obligation, are starting from scratch. Small service providers in particular are at significant audit risk if they have not implemented a disciplined permit collection process.

**5. Contract reviews are essential for pre-October 2025 contracts.** The March 31, 2026 transition relief window is narrow. Contracts that extend beyond that date will need amendments, and contracts that auto-renew or that are amended in any way lose their protected status. Contract managers should be running a review of all pre-existing service contracts as a matter of priority.

**6. Multistate sellers face cascading complexity.** A Washington-based IT services firm selling to customers in other states now has to determine, for each customer, (a) whether Washington sources the transaction to Washington or to the customer's state, (b) whether the customer's state taxes the service, and (c) whether the Washington-based firm has nexus in the customer's state. This is the multistate compliance cascade that the SSUTA framework was intended to simplify but that remains complex in practice.

**7. The advertising services provision is vulnerable to legal challenge.** Practitioners should monitor the Comcast litigation and any similar cases. If the advertising provision is struck down on ITFA grounds, refund claims may become available for tax paid during the intervening period. Sellers of advertising services should maintain detailed records to support potential refund claims.

**8. The prepayment obligation is an operational burden that has not yet been fully tested.** The $3 million threshold is low enough to capture mid-size sellers, and the 80 percent prepayment plus 10 percent penalty structure creates significant cash-flow exposure for affected businesses. Tax and finance departments should model the 2027 prepayment cash flow impact well in advance.

**9. Rate lookup discipline is essential.** With combined rates ranging from 7.0 percent to 10.6 percent across Washington jurisdictions, an error in rate calculation can produce significant over- or under-collection. The DOR rate lookup tool should be used for every transaction unless the seller has integrated an equivalent automated rate engine.

**10. The reform will probably be permanent.** Even though SB 5814 was framed politically as a response to a specific budget shortfall, tax base expansions of this scale historically become permanent features of the fiscal landscape. Practitioners should plan for this as a long-term structural change, not a temporary measure.

# Appendix A: The Business and Occupation Tax Interaction

No discussion of Washington's retail sales tax is complete without understanding its interaction with the business and occupation (B&O) tax. The B&O tax is Washington's unique gross receipts tax, imposed on the privilege of doing business in the state, measured by the gross income of the business, with no deduction for cost of goods sold, labor, or overhead. It is one of only a handful of state-level gross receipts taxes in the country, and its existence shapes virtually every aspect of how sales tax operates in Washington.

## A.1 The Retailing Classification

When a business makes a sale that is subject to the retail sales tax, that same gross receipt is simultaneously subject to the B&O tax under the "Retailing" classification. The Retailing B&O rate is currently 0.471 percent. So a $1,000 retail sale in a 10.60 percent combined rate jurisdiction produces:

- $106.00 in retail sales tax collected from the buyer (remitted to the state and local jurisdictions)
- $4.71 in Retailing B&O tax owed by the seller (out of the seller's own receipts)

The Retailing B&O is a cost to the seller. It is not collected from the buyer as a separate line item (though it is implicitly embedded in prices). The practical effect is that every retail sale carries both a visible consumer tax (sales tax) and an invisible seller-side tax (Retailing B&O).

## A.2 Other B&O Classifications Relevant to the Reform

The B&O tax has several classifications with different rates, and the classification depends on the type of activity:

- **Retailing**: 0.471 percent (for retail sales)
- **Wholesaling**: 0.484 percent (for sales to resellers)
- **Manufacturing**: 0.484 percent (for manufacturing activity)
- **Service and Other Activities**: 1.5 percent or 1.75 percent depending on business size (for services not otherwise classified)
- **Royalties**: 0.484 percent
- **Real Estate**: 1.5 percent
- **Printing and Publishing**: 0.484 percent
- **Insurance Producers**: 1.5 percent
- **Travel Agents**: 0.275 percent
- **Radio and Television Broadcasting**: 0.484 percent

The Service and Other Activities classification is the catch-all for services not specifically enumerated in another category. Its 1.5 to 1.75 percent rate is significantly higher than the Retailing rate of 0.471 percent. This creates an important but often overlooked consequence of SB 5814:

**For services that are newly taxable under SB 5814, the seller's B&O classification shifts from Service and Other Activities (1.5 to 1.75 percent) to Retailing (0.471 percent).** This is a net reduction in B&O tax of roughly 1.0 to 1.3 percentage points on the same gross receipts.

Consider a Washington IT consulting firm selling $1,000,000 in annual taxable services:

**Before SB 5814:**
- Sales tax collected from customers: $0
- Service and Other Activities B&O tax owed by firm: $15,000 to $17,500

**After SB 5814:**
- Sales tax collected from customers: Approximately $85,000 to $106,000 depending on customer location mix
- Retailing B&O tax owed by firm: $4,710
- **Net B&O tax reduction for the firm: approximately $10,000 to $13,000**

From a pure B&O perspective, the firm is better off. Its tax cost has gone down. The pain is borne by customers, who now pay roughly 8.5 to 10.6 percent more for the same services. The firm's role is administrative: it collects the new tax and remits it, but it does not ultimately pay it.

This structural point is often missed in public commentary about SB 5814. The bill is not a tax increase on service providers as such; it is a tax increase on service consumers, administered through service providers, with a modest B&O offset for the providers themselves.

## A.3 The Credit for Sales Tax Paid on Purchases

Washington does not have a general credit mechanism allowing businesses to deduct sales tax paid on their own purchases. This is different from a value-added tax, where input tax is creditable against output tax. In Washington, sales tax paid on business inputs is a cost, full stop.

The major exceptions are:

- **Reseller purchases**, which are exempt at the point of purchase rather than credited later
- **Manufacturing inputs that become part of a manufactured product**, which are exempt under the manufacturing input exemption
- **M&E exemption for manufacturing machinery and equipment**, which is a point-of-sale exemption for qualifying capital purchases

For services purchased by businesses under SB 5814, the general rule applies: no credit. A Washington law firm paying sales tax on its IT services contract cannot credit that tax against anything. It is a cost of doing business. The law firm may pass the cost through in its fees (though law firm fees themselves are not subject to sales tax, so the pass-through is implicit rather than explicit).

This creates a cascading effect. A service that is taxed at one level of the supply chain and then consumed at another level produces double taxation on the final consumer. In theory, the B&O tax already produces some cascading (it is imposed on each gross receipt without netting), so the additional cascading from SB 5814 is incremental rather than a new structural problem. In practice, practitioners have raised cascading concerns in several specific industries, particularly those with complex multi-tier service arrangements.

## A.4 The Small Business B&O Credit

Washington provides a small business credit against the B&O tax for businesses with modest tax liabilities. The credit phases out as liability grows and effectively exempts the smallest businesses from B&O tax entirely. The credit does not apply to sales tax, so a small business that is newly required to collect sales tax under SB 5814 has a new compliance obligation even if it pays no B&O tax.

## A.5 Advanced Computing Surcharge

An important B&O surcharge is worth mentioning in this context: the Advanced Computing Surcharge, enacted in 2019 and revised several times since. This surcharge applies at an additional B&O rate of 1.22 percent (on top of the regular Service and Other Activities rate) for the largest technology companies (those with worldwide gross income exceeding $25 billion). The surcharge effectively captures a significant share of Microsoft's and Amazon's Washington-attributable service revenue.

The Advanced Computing Surcharge is separate from SB 5814 but creates context for how the tech sector is taxed in Washington. A large Washington tech company is now subject to:

1. The Advanced Computing Surcharge on service revenue at approximately 2.7 to 2.97 percent
2. The new sales tax collection obligation on IT and custom software services
3. The regular Retailing B&O on those same services
4. Plus federal, employment, and other taxes

The cumulative effect is that Washington is now, by at least some measures, one of the most heavily taxed states for technology services, even though it has no state income tax.

# Appendix C: Service Categorization Worked Examples

This appendix works through specific business scenarios under the SB 5814 framework to illustrate how the new rules apply in practice. These examples are for educational illustration only.

## C.1 Scenario: Washington-Based IT Consulting Firm

**Facts.** ABC Consulting is a Seattle-based firm with twenty employees providing IT consulting, network management, and help desk services to enterprise customers. Before October 1, 2025, none of its service revenue was subject to retail sales tax. It collected no sales tax and reported all service revenue under the Service and Other Activities B&O classification.

**Customer base.** Sixty percent of its revenue comes from Washington-based customers. Twenty percent comes from customers headquartered in Washington but with multi-state operations. Twenty percent comes from out-of-state customers.

**October 1, 2025 Impact:**

1. **Washington customers:** All services to Washington customers are now taxable. ABC must collect sales tax at the customer's location rate. For a Mukilteo customer, that is 10.60 percent. For a Vancouver customer, that is 8.70 percent. ABC's billing system must be able to look up the correct rate for each customer address and apply it.

2. **Multi-state customers headquartered in Washington:** ABC must allocate the services. A reasonable methodology would be to allocate based on headcount at each customer location or based on documented usage. The portion allocable to Washington locations is taxable; the portion allocable to out-of-state locations is sourced outside Washington and may be taxable in the customer's state depending on that state's rules.

3. **Out-of-state customers:** Services sourced outside Washington are not subject to Washington sales tax. ABC may, however, have nexus exposure in the customer's state and should determine whether it must register there.

**Existing contract treatment.** ABC has a three-year master services agreement with a major Washington customer that was signed in March 2025. Under the DOR transition guidance, this qualifies as a pre-existing contract and is protected from the new sales tax through March 31, 2026, as long as it is not altered. On April 1, 2026, the contract becomes subject to the new tax regardless of whether it has been formally amended.

**B&O impact.** ABC's service revenue previously classified as Service and Other Activities (1.5 to 1.75 percent) now shifts to Retailing (0.471 percent). Its B&O tax cost decreases.

**Customer relations.** ABC's sales team has to communicate the new tax to customers, many of whom have never paid sales tax on these services and react strongly. Some customers demand explanations, some dispute the charge, and some attempt to renegotiate the underlying contract to spread the burden.

## C.2 Scenario: Independent Consultant Providing Live Webinars

**Facts.** Jane Doe is a self-employed consultant in Tacoma who provides live interactive webinars on technical topics to professional audiences. Her typical webinar has 30 to 50 attendees spread across multiple states. She charges $500 per attendee and runs 20 webinars per year, grossing approximately $400,000 annually. Before October 1, 2025, none of her revenue was subject to retail sales tax.

**October 1, 2025 Impact:**

1. **Live presentation classification:** Her webinars fall squarely within the new "live presentation" category. They are taxable.

2. **Sourcing:** Each attendee's portion of the fee is sourced to the attendee's location. For attendees in Washington, Jane must collect Washington sales tax at the attendee's address rate. For attendees in other states, she must determine whether those states tax her service and whether she has nexus.

3. **Compliance challenge:** Jane has been operating as a sole proprietor with a simple invoicing setup. She now needs to implement address collection (she has not historically captured attendee addresses beyond email), rate lookup, sales tax collection, and monthly or quarterly filing. This is a significant new compliance burden for a one-person business.

4. **Nexus in other states:** Jane has no out-of-state nexus beyond the sales themselves. In states with an economic nexus threshold similar to Washington's ($100,000), she may or may not cross the threshold. Careful tracking is required.

5. **Pricing response:** Jane has several options. She can absorb the tax (reducing her effective net revenue), pass it through as a separate line item (the traditional approach), or raise her base price to cover the tax (which may affect demand).

**Registration decision:** Jane crosses the $100,000 Washington gross receipts threshold for her own state obligation well before October 1, 2025 because she is a Washington resident. She must register with Washington DOR and begin filing a Combined Excise Tax Return.

## C.3 Scenario: Regional Managed Services Provider

**Facts.** Northwest MSP is a managed services provider with offices in Seattle and Portland, providing bundled IT services to small and mid-sized businesses across Washington and Oregon. Its typical engagement is a monthly retainer that includes help desk, network monitoring, backup management, security monitoring, and a quarterly strategic review.

**October 1, 2025 Impact:**

1. **Service mix.** The monthly retainer covers several categories that are now taxable (help desk, network monitoring, security monitoring, strategic review) and some that may or may not be (the backup management portion, which may be characterized as storage or as a service, depending on specifics).

2. **Bundled pricing.** Northwest MSP currently bills a single flat monthly fee. Under DOR guidance, a bundled fee that includes both taxable and non-taxable services is generally fully taxable unless the components are separately stated on the invoice and properly allocated.

3. **Invoice restructuring.** Northwest MSP faces a choice: (a) restructure its invoices to separately state each component and attempt to treat backup management as exempt, or (b) accept that the entire monthly fee is taxable. Most MSPs in practice have chosen option (b) because the compliance and audit risk of allocation is too high.

4. **Oregon customers.** Services to Oregon customers are sourced to Oregon and not subject to Washington sales tax. Oregon, which has no state sales tax, does not impose an equivalent tax. For the Oregon portion of the business, nothing changes.

5. **Mixed Oregon/Washington customers.** Customers with operations on both sides of the Columbia River require allocation. Northwest MSP must document its methodology.

6. **Contract exposure.** Northwest MSP has long-term managed services contracts with automatic annual renewal. Under DOR guidance, the automatic renewal itself may trigger loss of pre-existing-contract protection, depending on the specific language. Contract review is essential.

## C.4 Scenario: Enterprise Software Customization

**Facts.** A Washington-based enterprise customer contracts with a consulting firm to customize a prewritten enterprise resource planning (ERP) system. The customization includes configuration, custom reporting modules, integration with other systems, and user training. The total engagement is $2 million.

**Pre-October 2025 treatment:**

- Configuration: Exempt (professional services)
- Custom reporting modules: Potentially exempt if classified as custom software
- Integration: Exempt (professional services)
- User training: Exempt
- Underlying ERP software license: Taxable (prewritten software)

**Post-October 1, 2025 treatment:**

- Configuration: **Taxable** (customization of prewritten software or IT services)
- Custom reporting modules: **Taxable** (custom software)
- Integration: **Taxable** (IT services)
- User training: **Taxable** (IT services, potentially also live presentation)
- Underlying ERP software license: Taxable (no change)

**Result:** The entire $2 million engagement is now subject to Washington sales tax. At the customer's combined rate (assume 10.25 percent for a Seattle headquarters), the tax is approximately $205,000. This is a significant new line item that did not exist before October 1, 2025.

**Contract implications:** The original contract may have been signed before October 1, 2025. If so, it qualifies for the March 31, 2026 transition relief. But any amendment or change order after October 1 could strip that protection, so the parties must manage scope changes carefully.

## C.5 Scenario: Digital Advertising Agency

**Facts.** A Seattle-based digital advertising agency provides search engine marketing, social media campaign management, and display ad placement services to retail clients. Annual revenue is $10 million, with 70 percent from Washington clients.

**October 1, 2025 Impact:**

1. **All core services taxable.** Search engine marketing, campaign management, ad placement, and campaign analytics are all within the "advertising services" definition under SB 5814.

2. **Ad spend pass-through.** A significant portion of the agency's invoices represents pass-through ad spend (money the agency pays to Google, Meta, or other platforms on behalf of clients). The DOR guidance addresses this by allowing agencies to exclude pass-through ad spend from the taxable base if it is properly documented and separately stated on the invoice. Agency service fees are taxable; platform media costs are not.

3. **Platform fees and commissions.** Commissions paid to the agency by ad platforms (if any) may be taxable as advertising services or may be treated separately, depending on the specific arrangement.

4. **Legal uncertainty.** The agency's services are directly in the path of the Comcast ITFA challenge. If the advertising provision is struck down, the agency may be entitled to refunds of tax collected during the interim. The agency should maintain detailed records of tax collected by customer and period.

5. **Invoice redesign.** The agency needs to restructure its invoicing to clearly separate agency fees (taxable) from pass-through media costs (not taxable). This is a significant billing system project.

# Appendix E: Frequently Asked Questions

## E.1 General

**Q: Does SB 5814 apply retroactively?**

A: No. The reform applies to sales on and after October 1, 2025. Sales before that date are governed by prior law.

**Q: Does SB 5814 change the state sales tax rate?**

A: No. The state rate remains 6.5 percent. The reform changes what is subject to the tax, not the rate at which it is imposed.

**Q: Does SB 5814 apply to all services?**

A: No. It applies to enumerated service categories: advertising, IT services, custom website development, live presentations, investigation and security services, temporary staffing, custom software and customization of prewritten software, and certain digital automated services where exclusions were repealed. Services not in these categories remain outside the sales tax base.

**Q: Are legal services taxable now?**

A: No. Legal services are not in the enumerated list and remain exempt.

**Q: Are accounting services taxable now?**

A: No. Accounting services are not in the enumerated list and remain exempt.

**Q: Are medical services taxable now?**

A: No. Medical services, including the newly carved-out telehealth services, remain exempt.

**Q: Are architectural and engineering services taxable now?**

A: No. Architectural and engineering services remain exempt.

**Q: Are management consulting services taxable now?**

A: Generally no, but with an important caveat. Pure management consulting is not enumerated. However, if a management consulting engagement includes IT components (technical advice, system analysis, process automation using technology), those components may be taxable as IT services. The line is not always clear, and the DOR has not issued definitive guidance on the boundary.

## E.2 Custom Software

**Q: I wrote a custom application for a single customer under a "programming services" contract. Is it taxable?**

A: Yes, after October 1, 2025. Custom software is now taxable.

**Q: I modified an off-the-shelf CRM system for a customer. Is the modification work taxable?**

A: Yes. Customization of prewritten software is now taxable.

**Q: The modification work was separately stated from the underlying license on my invoice. Does that preserve an exemption?**

A: No. The separately-stated approach that worked before October 1, 2025 no longer produces an exempt result, because customization is now taxable whether stated separately or not.

## E.3 IT Services

**Q: I provide web hosting services. Do I need to collect sales tax?**

A: No. Web hosting is specifically excluded from the IT services definition and from the custom website development definition.

**Q: I provide managed IT services that include hosting, monitoring, help desk, and patching in a single bundled fee. How do I handle this?**

A: Under DOR guidance, a bundled fee that includes both exempt components (hosting) and taxable components (monitoring, help desk, patching) is generally taxable in full unless the components are separately stated and properly allocated. Most MSPs have concluded that attempting to allocate is more trouble and audit risk than it is worth, and have simply treated the entire bundled fee as taxable.

**Q: I do IT training for my clients, both in-person and online. Is training taxable?**

A: Yes. IT training is specifically enumerated in the IT services definition. In-person and online training are both taxable. Note that online training may also qualify as a "live presentation" if it includes real-time interaction.

## E.4 Advertising

**Q: I run a small marketing agency. Do I have to collect sales tax on my services?**

A: Yes, if your services fall within the "advertising services" definition. This includes campaign management, ad placement, search engine marketing, lead generation, and most digital advertising work.

**Q: What if I pass through ad spend to clients—for example, I bill them for Google Ads costs that I paid on their behalf?**

A: Pass-through ad spend is generally not subject to sales tax if it is separately stated on the invoice and documented as a pass-through cost. Your agency service fee is taxable; the media spend itself is not.

**Q: Is print advertising taxable?**

A: No. Print advertising in newspapers and other publications is specifically excluded from the advertising services definition.

**Q: Is television advertising taxable?**

A: No, at least as the statute is currently interpreted. Traditional television advertising is excluded. Streaming video advertising is in a legal gray area and is the subject of the Comcast ITFA litigation.

## E.5 Live Presentations

**Q: I teach in-person workshops. Are they taxable?**

A: Yes, if they involve real-time interaction with attendees, which in-person workshops typically do.

**Q: I teach a recorded online course. Is it taxable?**

A: Your recorded online course has been taxable since the 2009 digital products legislation, as a digital product. SB 5814 does not change its treatment.

**Q: I teach a live webinar with Q&A. Is it taxable?**

A: Yes. Live webinars with real-time interaction are within the "live presentations" definition.

**Q: I teach at a university. Is tuition taxable?**

A: No. Preschool through higher education tuition at accredited institutions is specifically excluded.

**Q: I provide one-on-one tutoring. Is it taxable?**

A: No. Tutoring services are specifically excluded.

## E.6 Temporary Staffing

**Q: I run a staffing agency. Do I have to collect sales tax?**

A: Yes, on temporary staffing services where your agency remains the employer of record.

**Q: What about permanent placement fees, where the worker becomes an employee of my client?**

A: Permanent placement fees (direct hire recruiting) are excluded and remain exempt.

**Q: What about hospital staffing?**

A: Hospital staffing is specifically excluded and remains exempt.

## E.7 Compliance

**Q: My customer claims they are a reseller and shouldn't pay sales tax. What do I do?**

A: Obtain a valid Washington reseller permit from the customer. Verify the permit using the DOR's lookup tool. Retain documentation. If the customer has a valid permit and the sale is genuinely for resale (for example, an IT services firm providing help desk services that will be resold to an end customer), you can accept the sale as a resale transaction and not collect tax.

**Q: I'm an out-of-state service provider with Washington customers. Do I need to register?**

A: Possibly. You need to register if you have $100,000 or more in gross Washington receipts in the current or prior calendar year. Before SB 5814, many out-of-state service providers had no Washington collection obligation because their services were not taxable. After October 1, 2025, the calculus changes: if your services are now in the newly taxable categories and your Washington sales exceed the threshold, you must register and collect.

**Q: I'm a small service provider and this is overwhelming. What should I do?**

A: Consult a qualified tax practitioner or contact the Washington DOR taxpayer assistance line. The DOR has publicly stated that businesses making good-faith efforts to comply will be treated more leniently than those who ignore the reform. Registering, filing accurately to the best of your ability, and documenting your decisions are all protective steps.

# Appendix F: Timeline of Key Events

A chronological summary of the major events in Washington's sales tax history and the development of SB 5814:

- **March 25, 1935**: Governor Clarence D. Martin signs the Revenue Act of 1935, establishing Washington's retail sales tax at 2.0 percent.
- **1941**: First wartime rate increase to 3.0 percent.
- **1983**: Rate reaches 6.5 percent, the current rate.
- **November 12, 2002**: Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement adopted nationally.
- **October 1, 2005**: SSUTA enters into effect.
- **2007**: Washington legislature enacts SSB 5089, bringing state law into conformity with SSUTA.
- **July 1, 2008**: Washington becomes a full member of SSUTA; destination sourcing becomes effective.
- **July 26, 2009**: Digital products legislation (ESHB 2075) becomes effective, bringing digital goods, digital audiovisual works, digital books, digital automated services, and remote access software into the sales tax base.
- **June 21, 2018**: U.S. Supreme Court decides *South Dakota v. Wayfair*, overturning *Quill*.
- **October 1, 2018**: Washington economic nexus and marketplace facilitator rules take effect. Threshold is $100,000 gross receipts or 200 transactions.
- **March 14, 2019**: Washington eliminates the 200-transaction threshold, leaving only the $100,000 threshold.
- **2019**: Advanced Computing Surcharge enacted.
- **2025 Legislative Session**: SB 5814 introduced and debated as part of a broader $9.4 billion tax package.
- **April 19, 2025**: Senate Bill Report on SB 5814 published.
- **Late April 2025**: Senate passes SB 5814, 27-22.
- **May 20, 2025**: Governor Bob Ferguson signs ESSB 5814 into law.
- **June-August 2025**: DOR develops and begins publishing interim guidance.
- **September 2025**: Final DOR interim guidance statements published for major categories.
- **September 9, 2025**: Comcast files lawsuit alleging that the advertising services provision violates the Internet Tax Freedom Act.
- **October 1, 2025**: SB 5814 most provisions take effect. Services become taxable.
- **March 31, 2026**: End of transition relief window for pre-existing contracts.
- **April 1, 2026**: Pre-existing contracts become subject to the new sales tax and Retailing B&O regardless of alteration status.
- **June 25, 2027**: First prepayment deadline for large sellers under the SB 5814 prepayment obligation (80 percent of 2026 sales tax, 10 percent penalty for non-compliance).

# Appendix G: Economic and Fiscal Context

## G.1 The 2025 Tax Package in Aggregate

SB 5814 was the largest single component of a broader 2025 tax package enacted by the Washington legislature. Other major components included:

1. **Capital gains tax expansion.** Washington's narrow capital gains tax (enacted in 2021, upheld by the state supreme court in 2023) was expanded in scope and rate.
2. **B&O surcharge extensions and increases.** Several B&O surcharges on large businesses were extended or increased.
3. **Estate tax adjustments.** Washington's estate tax was modified.
4. **Various smaller revenue measures.**

The total revenue estimate for the package, over the 2025-27 biennium, was approximately $9.4 billion. SB 5814 accounted for approximately $2.9 billion of that total over the biennium, or roughly 31 percent of the total new revenue. Over four years, SB 5814 is projected to generate approximately $4.7 billion, making it the single largest revenue item in the package.

The context for the package was a projected operating budget shortfall of approximately $16 billion over four years, driven by slower-than-expected revenue growth, Medicaid cost increases, K-12 education obligations under the state supreme court's *McCleary* decision, and expanded social services commitments.

## G.2 Distribution of the New Tax Burden

Who actually pays the SB 5814 tax is a more subtle question than it might seem. Several points:

1. **Direct incidence.** The tax is collected from customers of the newly taxable services. For a Washington business buying IT services from a Washington provider, the business pays the tax (as a buyer).

2. **B2B cascade.** When a business pays sales tax on its own inputs, the cost may be passed through to the business's own customers in the form of higher prices. A law firm buying IT services and paying tax on them may raise its fees to cover the cost (though the law firm's services are not subject to sales tax, so the pass-through is implicit). Whether and how much of the tax is passed through depends on competitive conditions in the buyer's market.

3. **Geographic distribution.** Because sourcing rules are destination-based, the tax is collected where the customer is located, not where the service provider is located. Washington-based service providers selling to out-of-state customers do not create Washington tax revenue on those sales. Out-of-state service providers selling to Washington customers do create Washington tax revenue on those sales.

4. **Sector distribution.** Technology-intensive sectors are disproportionately affected, because they are heavier consumers of IT services, custom software, and digital advertising. Traditional service sectors (legal, medical, accounting) are less affected because their own services are not taxable and their input purchases in the newly taxable categories are smaller.

## G.3 Comparison to 1982-83 Fiscal Crisis

The 1982-83 fiscal crisis is the most relevant historical precedent for the 2025 reform. During that crisis, Washington responded to a sharp recession-driven revenue decline by temporarily restoring sales tax on groceries, increasing the state sales tax rate, and enacting several other emergency revenue measures. The grocery tax was subsequently repealed by citizen initiative, but the rate increase to 6.5 percent was permanent and has remained in place for forty-two years.

The 2025 reform differs from 1982-83 in several ways:

- **Mechanism.** 1982-83 used rate increases; 2025 uses base expansion.
- **Framing.** 1982-83 was explicitly framed as temporary crisis response; 2025 is framed as a combination of crisis response and long-overdue modernization.
- **Sectoral focus.** 1982-83 affected all consumers equally; 2025 disproportionately affects business consumers and the tech sector specifically.
- **Speed.** 1982-83 was enacted and implemented over roughly a year; 2025 was enacted in May and implemented in October, a much faster timeline.

The political durability of the 2025 reform is an open question. Base expansions historically tend to be durable because repealing them is politically difficult (it would create a new revenue hole) and because the affected constituencies are specific sectors rather than broad consumer populations. Barring a successful legal challenge to key provisions (the Comcast ITFA case being the most immediate risk), SB 5814 is likely to become a permanent feature of Washington's fiscal structure.

## G.4 Interaction with Federal Deductibility Limits

One federal tax consideration worth noting: state and local taxes paid by individuals are limited in federal deductibility under the 2017 federal tax reform, which capped the SALT deduction at $10,000 per return. For business taxpayers, however, state sales tax paid on business inputs is generally deductible as a business expense without limitation. This means the federal tax consequence of SB 5814 is different for consumers (who may not be able to deduct the additional tax) and for businesses (who generally can). For a business paying sales tax on, say, $1 million of newly taxable IT services at a 10 percent rate, the $100,000 in sales tax is a deductible business expense, meaning the after-federal-tax cost is $79,000 at a 21 percent federal corporate rate.

# Appendix I: Additional Practitioner Notes on Specific Provisions

## I.1 Sourcing for Digital Automated Services

The DAS category has its own sourcing rules that differ slightly from the general service sourcing rules. Under RCW 82.32.730 and related guidance, DAS is sourced to the customer's primary use location, with specific fallback rules if that location cannot be determined:

1. Primary use location (if known)
2. Billing address (if primary use unknown)
3. Address of the device used to access the service (if billing address is a generic corporate address)
4. Address of the credit card used for payment (as a last resort)

These fallback rules matter because many DAS products are sold through automated e-commerce channels where the seller may not have detailed customer information beyond billing and payment details. The DOR has indicated that reasonable reliance on billing address information is generally acceptable.

## I.2 The "Substantial Change" Test for Pre-Existing Contracts

The DOR interim guidance on pre-existing contracts uses a "substantial change" test to determine whether a contract has been "altered" and therefore lost its transition relief protection. The test is fact-specific, but the guidance identifies several categories of changes that would qualify as substantial:

- Price changes (including scheduled escalators that require affirmative action)
- Scope changes adding new services
- Scope changes removing services
- Term extensions
- Renewals (even automatic renewals, in some interpretations)
- Assignment to a different party
- Merger or acquisition affecting either party
- Material amendments to payment terms, performance obligations, or service levels

Changes that would generally not be substantial include:

- Routine administrative updates (contact information, billing addresses)
- Minor corrections to typographical errors
- Automatic CPI adjustments that do not require affirmative action

Practitioners should document every contract amendment carefully during the transition period and should err on the side of assuming that any amendment strips the protection.

## I.3 Bundled Transactions Under SB 5814

Washington's treatment of bundled transactions (sales that combine taxable and nontaxable components) is generally unfavorable to sellers. Under RCW 82.08.190 and related rules, a bundled transaction is generally fully taxable if it contains any taxable component, unless:

1. The components are separately stated on the invoice
2. The non-taxable components are genuinely severable (the customer could buy them separately)
3. The pricing allocation is reasonable

SB 5814 has increased the volume of bundled transaction issues because many service providers previously sold bundled offerings that were entirely non-taxable and that now contain newly taxable components. Practitioners have reported that DOR auditors are scrutinizing bundled transaction allocation closely, and that providers who do not have clear invoice separation typically end up taxed on the entire bundle.

## I.4 Software Customization Boundary Cases

Several boundary cases in the software customization area deserve specific attention:

**Configuration vs. customization.** Pure configuration of a prewritten software product (changing settings, populating fields, uploading logos) is arguably not "customization" in the sense the statute uses. The DOR guidance has not drawn a bright line, but practitioners have generally taken the position that configuration is a subset of customization and therefore taxable under SB 5814.

**Consulting vs. customization.** A consulting engagement that includes advice about how to implement a prewritten software package, without actually performing the implementation, may not be "customization" but is likely "IT services," which is also taxable. Either way, the revenue is in the sales tax base.

**Professional services tied to a software purchase.** A customer buying a prewritten software license plus implementation services bundled together has an obvious bundled transaction issue. Both components are taxable under SB 5814, so the bundled treatment does not create a new tax problem, but the specific B&O classification may differ depending on whether the implementation is characterized as software customization, IT services, or something else.

**Testing, QA, and support.** Ongoing support services for custom software (bug fixes, enhancements, maintenance) are generally taxable as IT services or as customization work.

## I.5 Multi-Jurisdictional Sourcing for IT Services

Many Washington IT service providers have customers with operations in multiple states or multiple countries. Sourcing the service is a two-step process:

1. **Determine whether the service is Washington-source at all.** Services delivered to a customer's Washington location (or to employees working at Washington locations) are Washington-source. Services delivered to customers' out-of-state locations are not.

2. **Determine the Washington local rate.** For the portion sourced to Washington, the local rate is determined by the specific Washington location of the customer, using the DOR rate lookup.

For a multi-state customer, the allocation between Washington and non-Washington is often the most contentious sourcing question. The DOR guidance permits reasonable allocation methods, with several options:

- Headcount allocation (percentage of the customer's Washington employees)
- Office allocation (percentage of the customer's Washington offices)
- Revenue allocation (percentage of the customer's Washington revenue)
- User allocation (for services with identifiable per-user consumption)
- Time allocation (for services where consultant time can be tracked by location)

The chosen methodology must be reasonable, consistently applied, and documented. Changing methodology mid-year or between contracts without documented reasons creates audit exposure.

## I.6 Impact on Nonprofit Organizations

Washington's nonprofit sales tax exemptions are narrower than many practitioners expect. Nonprofit organizations generally pay sales tax on their purchases, subject to specific statutory exemptions for certain categories (for example, sales to qualifying nonprofits of certain goods and services, sales by nonprofits in certain fundraising contexts).

SB 5814 creates a new burden for nonprofits that purchase newly taxable services. A Washington nonprofit that hires an IT consulting firm now pays sales tax on the engagement, reducing the amount of its budget available for mission work. Since nonprofits typically cannot pass the cost through to anyone, the tax is a direct drain on the organization's resources.

The 2025 legislative debate considered but did not ultimately include a broad nonprofit carveout from SB 5814. Specific nonprofit categories (for example, charitable fundraising services) retain their pre-existing exemptions, but the general nonprofit-as-buyer situation is no more favorable than any other buyer.

## I.7 Impact on Government Buyers

Sales to the federal government are constitutionally exempt. Sales to the State of Washington and to its political subdivisions are subject to specific statutory rules, generally treated as taxable with certain exceptions for specific categories.

A tech company selling services to a Washington state agency or a city government is subject to SB 5814 in the same way as a sale to any other buyer, with the result that state and local government buyers must now pay the tax on these services. This creates a minor accounting loop (state tax flowing from state agency budgets back to state general fund) but the practical effect is real: government agencies now have to budget for the sales tax component of their service contracts.

# Appendix J: Washington Sales Tax Glossary

**B&O tax (Business and Occupation tax).** Washington's gross receipts tax imposed on the privilege of doing business. Imposed in addition to the retail sales tax on taxable sales.

**Bundled transaction.** A sale that combines both taxable and non-taxable components. Generally fully taxable unless components are separately stated, severable, and reasonably allocated.

**Canned software.** Prewritten software, commercially available off-the-shelf. Taxable in Washington since long before the 2009 digital products legislation.

**Combined Excise Tax Return (CETR).** The consolidated tax return used for filing Washington sales tax, use tax, B&O tax, and related excise taxes.

**Combined rate.** The total sales tax rate applied to a specific Washington address, combining the 6.5 percent state rate with applicable local rates.

**Consumer use tax.** Use tax owed by an individual Washington resident on purchases from non-collecting sellers. Historically widely underreported.

**Custom software.** Software written specifically for a single customer. Exempt from Washington retail sales tax before October 1, 2025; taxable after.

**Customization of prewritten software.** Modification of off-the-shelf software to meet a customer's specific needs. Exempt before October 1, 2025 (if separately stated); taxable after.

**Deferred sales tax.** Sales tax that should have been collected by the seller but was not, owed by the buyer. Reported on the CETR.

**Destination sourcing.** The principle that a sale is sourced to the location where the buyer receives the product or service. Adopted by Washington in 2008 as part of SSUTA conformity.

**Digital automated service (DAS).** Services transferred electronically using a combination of hardware and software to automate a process. Taxable in Washington since 2009.

**Digital products.** Electronically delivered goods including digital audio, digital video, digital books, digital games, and similar products. Taxable since 2009.

**DOR.** Washington Department of Revenue, the state agency administering the sales tax, B&O tax, and other excise taxes.

**Economic nexus.** The principle that a state can require a seller to collect sales tax based on the seller's economic activity in the state, even without physical presence. Established by *Wayfair* (2018).

**ESSB 5814.** Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5814, signed May 20, 2025, the legislation at the center of this document.

**Exemption certificate.** A document provided by a buyer to a seller claiming an exemption from sales tax on a specific transaction.

**Fiscal note.** An official estimate of the revenue impact of proposed legislation.

**Gross receipts tax.** A tax imposed on total business receipts without deduction for costs. Washington's B&O tax is a gross receipts tax.

**ITFA.** Internet Tax Freedom Act, federal law prohibiting states from imposing discriminatory taxes on electronic commerce. Subject of the Comcast challenge to SB 5814's advertising provision.

**Live presentation.** Under SB 5814, in-person or online workshops, webinars, and courses with real-time interaction. Taxable after October 1, 2025.

**Local sales tax.** Sales taxes imposed by counties, cities, transit districts, and special districts on top of the state rate.

**Marketplace facilitator.** A platform that facilitates sales between third-party sellers and buyers. Required to collect and remit Washington sales tax on facilitated transactions.

**M&E exemption.** Machinery and equipment exemption for qualifying manufacturing capital purchases.

**My DOR.** The Washington DOR's online portal for tax registration, filing, and account management.

**Nexus.** The connection between a seller and a state that permits the state to impose collection obligations on the seller.

**Prewritten software.** See canned software.

**Quill.** *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota* (1992), the U.S. Supreme Court decision establishing physical presence as the nexus standard for state sales tax. Overturned by *Wayfair* in 2018.

**Remote Access Software (RAS).** Software residing on a seller's server that a customer accesses remotely. The primary statutory vehicle for taxing SaaS in Washington.

**Reseller permit.** Washington's mechanism for documenting that a sale qualifies for the resale exemption. Obtained through My DOR.

**Retailing classification.** The B&O tax classification for businesses making retail sales. Currently 0.471 percent.

**Sales tax.** The tax on retail sales of tangible personal property and enumerated services. State rate is 6.5 percent; local rates add 1.0 to 4.1 percent.

**SaaS.** Software as a Service, taxed in Washington as Remote Access Software.

**Service and Other Activities classification.** The B&O tax classification for services not otherwise classified. Rate is 1.5 percent or 1.75 percent depending on business size.

**Sourcing.** The rules determining which jurisdiction's sales tax applies to a sale.

**SSUTA.** Streamlined Sales and Use Tax Agreement, the multistate agreement harmonizing state sales tax administration. Washington is a full member since 2008.

**Tangible personal property.** Physical goods that can be seen, weighed, measured, or touched. The original subject of the Washington retail sales tax.

**Temporary staffing services.** Under SB 5814, the supply of contract workers for short-term assignments. Taxable after October 1, 2025.

**UBI.** Unified Business Identifier, Washington's universal business identification number.

**Use tax.** The complementary tax on goods and services purchased without paying Washington sales tax. Same rate as sales tax.

**Wayfair.** *South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.* (2018), the U.S. Supreme Court decision establishing economic nexus as a permissible basis for state sales tax collection obligations.

# Appendix K: A Longer Look at the Policy Debate

## K.1 Arguments Supporting the Reform

Supporters of SB 5814 have offered several arguments in favor of the service base expansion:

**Modernization.** The core argument is that Washington's sales tax base, designed in 1935 for a tangible-goods economy, has become increasingly mismatched with a service-dominated economy. As goods have declined as a share of household and business spending, a goods-only sales tax base has shrunk as a share of GDP. Expanding into services is a long-overdue modernization.

**Horizontal equity.** A sales tax that covers goods but not services treats two economically similar transactions differently. A consumer buying a physical self-help book pays sales tax; a consumer paying for a self-help coaching session does not. A business buying computer hardware pays sales tax; a business paying for computer services does not. Expanding into services reduces these inconsistencies.

**Vertical equity.** Because higher-income households tend to spend a larger share of their budgets on services (legal advice, investment management, concierge health care, elite education), taxing services can make the sales tax more progressive—or, at minimum, less regressive.

**Revenue stability.** A broader base produces more stable revenue over the economic cycle. Services tend to be less volatile than durable goods purchases, which collapse in recessions. Expanding into services smooths revenue.

**Neutrality.** Taxing only part of consumption creates distortions. Households and businesses can substitute services for goods to reduce their tax burden. A broader base reduces substitution distortions.

**Fiscal necessity.** Washington faced a projected $16 billion operating budget shortfall over four years. Without new revenue, program cuts would have been required in K-12 education, Medicaid, and other critical areas. SB 5814 provided approximately $4.7 billion of the needed revenue with a single measure.

## K.2 Arguments Against the Reform

Critics have raised several arguments:

**Administrative complexity.** Services are harder to source, harder to define, and harder to audit than goods. The compliance burden on service providers, particularly small providers, is significant. The compressed implementation timeline compounded the difficulty.

**Compliance cost to small businesses.** Many small service providers have never had a sales tax obligation. The cost of setting up billing systems, collecting exemption certificates, registering with DOR, and filing returns is a real burden relative to the size of the businesses affected.

**Cascading.** When business-to-business services are taxed, the tax can cascade through supply chains. An IT consulting firm that buys managed services, which itself buys software subscriptions, which itself uses custom development—each layer potentially generates tax that is embedded in the next layer's costs.

**Competitive disadvantage.** Washington-based service providers now have a price disadvantage relative to out-of-state competitors who may not have Washington collection obligations. While economic nexus rules mitigate this somewhat, the mitigation is imperfect, particularly for smaller out-of-state providers below the $100,000 threshold.

**Tech sector impact.** The reform disproportionately affects the Washington tech sector, which is one of the state's largest employers and taxpayers. Imposing new compliance and cost burdens on the tech sector risks long-term negative effects on investment and employment decisions.

**Compressed timeline.** The four-month window between signing and implementation was widely criticized as inadequate. Several practitioner organizations and business groups formally requested a longer implementation period and were denied.

**Legal uncertainty.** The advertising services provision, in particular, faces a credible legal challenge under the federal Internet Tax Freedom Act. If the provision is struck down, the state will face refund claims and the revenue estimate will be overstated.

**Regressivity of B2B cascade.** Although higher-income households consume more high-value services directly, the B2B cascade may shift a portion of the tax burden back onto lower-income consumers through higher prices for everyday goods whose producers buy taxable services as inputs.

## K.3 The Long-Term Outlook

Predicting the long-term outlook for SB 5814 requires separating several distinct questions:

**Will it be repealed?** Unlikely, unless a successful legal challenge forces repeal of specific provisions. Base expansions tend to be durable because repealing them creates a revenue hole that legislatures must fill another way.

**Will it be expanded?** Possible over time. If the 2025 reform proves to be administrable and politically tolerable, future legislatures may consider expanding into additional service categories. Legal, medical, accounting, and financial services remain off-limits for now, but that consensus could shift over a decade or two.

**Will it be refined?** Almost certainly. The DOR's interim guidance will evolve into more detailed administrative rules. Specific provisions may be amended in clean-up legislation to address implementation problems. Some carveouts may be added; others may be removed.

**Will it survive legal challenge?** Uncertain, particularly for the advertising services provision. Maryland's digital advertising tax has faced extensive litigation and the legal landscape remains unsettled. Washington's approach is different in structure (it uses the existing sales tax rather than a standalone excise), which may or may not prove defensible under ITFA.

**Will other states follow?** Washington's reform, if it proves durable, may provide a template for other states looking to expand their own sales tax bases into services. Several states have been watching Washington's experience closely, and a successful Washington implementation could accelerate similar reforms elsewhere.

## K.4 What the Reform Does Not Address

It is worth noting what SB 5814 does not do, because the omissions are as politically significant as the inclusions:

- **It does not impose a state income tax.** Washington remains one of a small number of states without an income tax. The 1933 state supreme court decision in *Culliton v. Chase* continues to bar a graduated income tax absent a constitutional amendment.
- **It does not tax legal services.** The legal profession is politically powerful in Washington and has successfully resisted sales tax inclusion in previous reform efforts.
- **It does not tax healthcare services.** Healthcare is politically untouchable for structural reasons: taxing medical services would be widely perceived as taxing illness.
- **It does not tax financial services.** Banking, insurance, and investment services remain outside the base.
- **It does not broadly tax real estate services.** Brokerage and property management services remain exempt.
- **It does not tax tuition at accredited schools.** Higher education and K-12 tuition remain exempt.

These omissions reflect a political calculation: SB 5814 targets the service categories where opposition was weakest and revenue potential was highest, while avoiding the sectors where opposition would have been strongest. This is how tax reform usually works in practice.

## K.5 Broader Policy Context

SB 5814 fits into a broader national conversation about state and local tax reform in response to the digital economy. The conversation has several strands:

1. **The base erosion problem.** As economic activity shifts to services and digital products, traditional sales tax bases have eroded. States have responded with various approaches: base expansion (like Washington's 2025 reform), rate increases, targeted excise taxes (like Maryland's digital ad tax), and new forms of taxation (like the California franchise tax's digital advertising component).

2. **The interstate commerce problem.** The digital economy makes geographic sourcing difficult. Services delivered electronically from a server farm in one state to a user in another state have no obvious "place of transaction." SSUTA has simplified this for goods but services remain complicated.

3. **The international dimension.** Major digital services providers (Google, Meta, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon) are global in scope. State tax policy interacts with international tax policy in ways that are sometimes contradictory. OECD Pillar One and Pillar Two rules, once implemented, may reshape how states can tax multinational digital businesses.

4. **The federalism dimension.** The tension between state sovereignty over tax policy and federal preemption under ITFA is ongoing. The Comcast challenge to Washington's advertising provision is just one instance of a broader pattern.

Washington's 2025 reform is a specific, enumerated response to these broader issues. It is not a comprehensive solution, and it will not be the last word on state sales tax modernization.
