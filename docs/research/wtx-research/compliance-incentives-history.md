# Part 1 — History of the Washington Tax System

## 1. Pre-statehood tax policy and the territorial era (1853-1889)

Washington Territory was carved out of Oregon Territory in 1853 and remained a territory until statehood on November 11, 1889. The territorial government inherited the basic Anglo-American fiscal architecture that the early American republic had borrowed from colonial Massachusetts and from the late English county system: a property tax as the dominant, almost sole, revenue source for both the territorial government and the counties that composed it.

Throughout the territorial period, property taxation performed three structural functions. First, it funded the limited general operations of the territorial government — a legislature that met biennially, a governor appointed by the President of the United States, a small territorial bureaucracy, and the courts. Second, it funded county government, which at that time bore the operational weight of civil administration: roads, bridges, sheriffs' offices, jails, and the recording of property transactions. Third, it funded the common schools, which from the earliest territorial school laws were understood as a county (and, as population grew, a district) responsibility.

The property tax in the territorial period was a *general* property tax: it applied, in principle, to all real and personal property within the jurisdiction, assessed at a uniform rate. This approach had deep roots in the nineteenth-century American political imagination. The general property tax was perceived as fundamentally fair because wealth was widely assumed to consist principally of visible, tangible, and relatively easy-to-assess assets: land, buildings, livestock, farm implements, wagons, and the tools of trade. A tax on all such property was thought to capture most of what mattered economically, and to do so with a rough proportionality to each taxpayer's ability to pay.

The weakness of this approach — that intangible wealth such as stocks, bonds, mortgages, and bank accounts could escape assessment unless the taxpayer voluntarily disclosed it — did not become fully apparent until the closing decades of the nineteenth century, when the industrial economy and the maturation of capital markets made intangible wealth a larger share of the total. This problem, which would eventually push many states to seek non-property revenue alternatives, was already visible in Washington Territory but did not yet dominate the discussion.

The territorial legislature had comparatively little latitude to deviate from this framework. Territorial charters, the Organic Act, and the prevailing orthodoxy of the time all pushed in the same direction. A handful of minor license taxes — saloon licenses, peddler licenses, ferry licenses — supplemented property taxation but did not change the basic character of the system.

As the territorial period drew to a close and statehood approached, the population of Washington Territory had grown from roughly 4,000 in 1853 to approximately 357,000 in 1889. The economic base had diversified from fur trading, timber, and subsistence agriculture into a more industrial and commercial economy built around timber, fishing, shipping, coal, and early manufacturing. The tax system, however, had barely changed. It was still a general property tax, administered by counties, for the benefit of counties and the territorial (soon state) government and common schools.

## 2. Early statehood and the property tax regime (1889-1929)

The Washington State Constitution of 1889 enshrined a strict uniformity clause for property taxation. Article VII, Section 1, as originally adopted, declared that "all property in the state . . . shall be taxed in proportion to its value" and that taxes "shall be uniform upon the same class of property within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax." This constitutional language — seemingly technical, nearly invisible to ordinary voters in 1889 — would later become the single most consequential clause in the Washington fiscal constitution, because it formed the doctrinal basis for the Washington Supreme Court's 1933 decision striking down the graduated income tax.

From statehood through the First World War, the Washington property tax continued to do most of the fiscal work for the state government, local governments, and schools. Several incremental changes occurred. The legislature created a State Board of Equalization to harmonize county assessments. Public utility property (especially railroad property) was assessed at the state level to avoid the severe under-assessment it tended to receive at the county level. A limited set of excise taxes — on inheritance, on certain licensed businesses, on insurance premiums — was layered on top of the property tax without displacing it.

By the 1920s, however, three forces were eroding the political viability of the property tax monopoly.

First, the tax was deeply unpopular with farmers, whose visible, assessable real property made them easy targets even as their cash incomes fell during the agricultural depression that began in the early 1920s. Second, the rise of the automobile and the demand for paved roads required new revenue sources that were more directly connected to road use; this led to the motor vehicle excise tax and the motor fuel tax, both early examples of Washington's willingness to experiment with dedicated excise taxes. Third, the growing share of wealth held in intangible assets — corporate stock, bonds, savings deposits — made the general property tax look increasingly incomplete as a wealth tax, because so much actual wealth was escaping it.

Reform pressure built throughout the 1920s but was not yet acute enough to force structural change. Washington remained, on the eve of the Great Depression, a property-tax state. Then the Depression hit, and the fiscal crisis forced a reckoning that would reshape the system permanently.

## 3. The Great Depression, the fiscal crisis, and the 1932 property tax limitation

The Great Depression arrived in the Pacific Northwest with particular force. Timber prices collapsed. Shipping dried up. Farm prices, already depressed throughout the 1920s, fell further. Unemployment in Seattle and Tacoma climbed into double digits and stayed there. Property owners — especially farmers, but also many residential homeowners — began to default on their property taxes in numbers that had been literally unimaginable during prior economic downturns. County treasurers across the state saw delinquency rates climb from the historical norm of a few percent to twenty, thirty, or in some rural counties even fifty percent of assessed property taxes. The general property tax, the foundational pillar of the Washington fiscal system, was breaking under the weight of the economic emergency.

In this environment, the Washington Grange and other agricultural interests sponsored a ballot initiative to limit property taxes. Initiative 64, adopted by voters in November 1932, capped the total regular property tax levy at 40 mills — that is, 4 percent of true and fair value — for all regular taxing jurisdictions combined. This was a drastic reduction from pre-existing levy practice and meant that state and local governments would have to find other revenue, quickly, or cut services to the bone.

Initiative 64 did not mention income taxes, sales taxes, or any particular replacement revenue source. It simply capped the property tax. The political theory behind it was straightforward: voters were furious at rising property tax bills during a period of falling income, and they wanted the bills cut. What should replace the lost revenue was left for the legislature to figure out.

The 1933 legislative session convened against this backdrop. Governor Clarence D. Martin, a Democrat elected in the Roosevelt landslide of 1932, inherited a fiscal mess: the state and its counties had lost a significant share of their property tax base through delinquency, the voters had capped what remained, and the Depression continued to deepen. Some new revenue source was needed. Two main candidates emerged: a state income tax, proposed by Grange and labor interests as a fair, progressive alternative to the regressive property tax; and a retail sales tax, proposed by business interests as a broad, low-rate alternative that would be administratively feasible.

The 1933 legislature enacted a graduated personal and corporate income tax. What happened next is the single most important event in the history of Washington taxation.

## 4. The 1933 graduated income tax and *Culliton v. Chase*

The 1933 income tax was challenged almost immediately. The plaintiff, Culliton, argued that the graduated income tax violated the uniformity clause of Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution, because the graduation meant that income of the same "class" was being taxed at different rates based on the amount.

In September 1933, the Washington Supreme Court, in *Culliton v. Chase*, 174 Wash. 363, 25 P.2d 81 (1933), struck down the income tax by a 5-4 vote. The majority reasoned — in a doctrinal move that has been debated by Washington tax lawyers for more than ninety years — that income constitutes *property*, that the uniformity clause therefore applied to it, and that a graduated rate structure was by definition not uniform. A flat-rate income tax might have survived under this reasoning, but a graduated one could not.

The dissenting justices argued that income is not property in the constitutional sense, and that an income tax should be analyzed as a privilege or excise tax rather than as a property tax. This minority view has never carried the day in Washington, despite repeated attempts over the decades to persuade the court to reconsider. The *Culliton* doctrine is the reason Washington has no state personal income tax. It is the doctrinal ghost that hangs over every discussion of Washington fiscal reform.

*Culliton* was decided 5-4. It has never been overruled. Subsequent Washington Supreme Court decisions — most recently the 2023 decision in *Quinn v. State* (discussed below) — have danced around it, distinguishing it rather than confronting it. But it remains the operative constitutional rule as of 2026.

Two technical points are worth stressing. First, the *Culliton* court did *not* strike down a B&O tax or a retail sales tax. These excise-style taxes were understood to be measured by transactions rather than by property, and therefore fell outside the uniformity clause. When the legislature returned in 1935 to enact replacement revenue, it was already doctrinally clear that the excise path was open even though the income path was closed. Second, the *Culliton* decision relied on federal precedents (notably *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.* (1895)) that had been effectively superseded at the federal level by the 16th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. The 16th Amendment did not, however, amend the Washington State Constitution, and so *Culliton's* reasoning, however anachronistic in federal terms, remains binding on Washington.

## 5. The Revenue Act of 1935

The 1935 legislative session produced the single most important piece of tax legislation in Washington's history: the Revenue Act of 1935. Signed by Governor Clarence D. Martin on March 25, 1935, the act contained 17 titles and approximately 200 sections. It was a comprehensive rewrite of the state's revenue structure. Nearly a century later, the core architecture of the Revenue Act of 1935 is still recognizable in the Washington tax system.

### What the Revenue Act of 1935 created

**The Business and Occupation (B&O) tax.** A gross receipts tax on the privilege of doing business in Washington. The tax is measured by gross income, with rates varying by business activity (manufacturing, wholesaling, retailing, services, and others). It is levied on the seller, not the buyer, and is not recoverable from the customer as a separate line item (though its cost is of course passed through to customers in the form of prices). The B&O tax has no deductions for cost of goods sold, labor, rent, or other ordinary business expenses. It is literally a tax on gross receipts.

**The retail sales tax.** A transaction tax on retail sales of tangible personal property (and, by various later amendments, certain services). Initially set at 2 percent, the sales tax was understood as a broad, low-rate consumption tax. It is levied on the buyer but collected and remitted by the seller as an agent for the state. Unlike the B&O tax, the sales tax is separately stated on receipts and is explicitly passed through.

**The use tax.** A complementary tax on the use of tangible personal property in Washington when sales tax was not collected at the point of sale. The use tax is conceptually important because it closes the interstate-purchase loophole: a Washington resident or business that buys goods from an out-of-state seller and brings them into Washington owes use tax at the same rate as the retail sales tax would have been. Without the use tax, the sales tax would create a massive incentive to purchase from out-of-state suppliers.

**The public utility tax.** A tax on gross receipts of specific utility activities: electric, gas, water, sewer, telephone, railroad, and certain other utilities. This tax is separate from the B&O tax and is imposed at different (generally higher) rates, reflecting the historical view that utilities enjoy monopoly rents that could support a higher tax burden.

Several smaller taxes rounded out the package, including taxes on cigarettes, alcohol, and certain other items. Some of the taxes originally in the Revenue Act — fuel oil, radios, stock transfers, toiletries, and medicines — were line-vetoed by Governor Martin before signing. The vetoed items were politically controversial and Martin judged them more likely to generate backlash than revenue.

### Immediate judicial challenges

Lawsuits were filed immediately challenging the B&O tax and the retail sales tax. Thurston County Superior Court Judge D. F. Wright, who had struck down the 1933 income tax earlier, once again struck down an income tax component of the 1935 act but *upheld* the retail sales tax and B&O tax. The sales and B&O components survived constitutional review because they were, in the court's view, excise taxes rather than property taxes, and therefore outside the uniformity clause.

With that judicial imprimatur, the B&O tax and the retail sales tax became the two pillars of Washington state taxation — a role they continue to play nine decades later. By fiscal year 2003, taxes authorized in the Revenue Act of 1935 generated approximately three-fourths of all state general fund revenue, and the proportion has remained in that range ever since.

### The policy debate that the 1935 act resolved

It is worth dwelling on the policy logic of the Revenue Act of 1935, because the choices made in 1935 still shape every conversation about Washington taxation in the 2020s.

The advocates of the income tax — labor, Grange, and progressive intellectuals — argued that an income tax was the only fair way to distribute the tax burden, because it tracks ability to pay. They lost in 1933 not because their argument was rejected politically, but because the Supreme Court declared their chosen tool unconstitutional. The 1935 legislature could not enact what the court had just struck down.

The advocates of excise taxes — principally retail merchants, manufacturers, and the business community — argued that a broad sales tax and a gross receipts business tax were administratively simple, broadly spread, and avoided the complexity of income measurement. They won in 1935 partly by default (the income tax path was closed) and partly on the merits of administrative simplicity.

What neither side fully anticipated in 1935 was that gross receipts taxation has dramatic incentive and distributional effects of its own. The B&O tax rewards high-margin businesses (because the tax is a smaller fraction of net income when margins are high) and punishes low-margin, high-volume businesses (where the B&O tax can exceed the net profit margin entirely). The retail sales tax is deeply regressive, because low-income households spend a much larger share of their income on sales-taxable goods than high-income households do. These features were visible to contemporary economists even in 1935, but they were not the dominant considerations in the immediate fiscal crisis.

## 6. Post-war expansion and the rise of classification complexity (1945-1980)

After World War II, Washington's population boomed and its economy diversified. Boeing's rise to dominance in commercial aviation turned the Puget Sound region into an aerospace cluster. Hanford's plutonium operations made the Tri-Cities a federal science center. Hydroelectric power development turned eastern Washington into an aluminum smelting hub. Forest products, agriculture, and fishing remained major sectors, and Seattle's port became one of the largest on the West Coast.

Against this backdrop of economic growth, the Washington tax system expanded through two mechanisms: rate increases and base broadening. The retail sales tax rate was gradually raised from the original 2 percent to 3 percent, then 3.3 percent, then 4.5 percent, then 5 percent, with further increases in subsequent decades. Local governments were eventually authorized to add their own sales tax on top of the state rate, producing the layered state-and-local sales tax rates that are familiar today. B&O tax rates were adjusted multiple times. The list of B&O tax classifications grew, as the legislature sought to recognize that different kinds of business activity have different economic characteristics and therefore might warrant different rates.

By 1980, the Washington B&O tax had approximately a dozen main classifications — retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, service and other business activities, printing and publishing, extracting, public road construction, and several others — each with its own rate and its own body of interpretive rulings and Washington Administrative Code provisions. The retail sales tax had been extended to cover certain services (notably construction labor, some personal services, and selected repair services), but the basic rule was still that the sales tax applied to tangible personal property sales, not to services, and not to business-to-business intermediate goods (which were instead subject to wholesaling B&O).

The complexity of the system grew gradually but steadily. Every legislative session produced new classifications, new exemptions, new deductions, and new credits, each advocated by a particular industry or constituency. The system became progressively harder to explain to newcomers, but the underlying architecture — B&O tax plus sales tax, with a smaller set of specialized excise taxes layered on — remained intact.

## 7. The initiative era and the anti-income-tax consensus (1980s-2000s)

From approximately 1980 to 2010, Washington politics was dominated by a powerful anti-tax movement, expressed principally through the initiative process. Tim Eyman, a businessman from Mukilteo, became the most visible figure in this movement, filing dozens of initiatives over more than two decades to limit taxes of various kinds. Not all of Eyman's initiatives passed, and several of those that did were later struck down by the Washington Supreme Court on single-subject or constitutional grounds, but the cumulative political effect was significant: the state legislature knew that any tax increase would face a possible initiative-driven rollback, and this knowledge shaped legislative behavior for a generation.

Multiple attempts to introduce a state income tax — either by constitutional amendment (which would require a two-thirds supermajority in the legislature and voter ratification) or by statute (which would face *Culliton*) — failed during this period. The most notable was Initiative 1098 in 2010, which would have imposed an income tax on high earners. It lost by a wide margin, 64 percent to 36 percent, despite being sponsored by Bill Gates Sr. and other prominent Washington progressives. The defeat of I-1098 cemented the political conventional wisdom: Washington voters will not approve an income tax, even on the very wealthy, even during a period of progressive political mobilization.

During the same period, successive initiatives tightened property tax limits. Referendum 47, approved in November 1997, limited the growth rate of state and local regular property tax levies to the lesser of 6 percent or 100 percent plus inflation (for jurisdictions with populations over 10,000). This was itself a tightening of earlier, more generous growth allowances. Then in 2001 came the most significant property tax limitation of all.

## 8. Initiative 747 and the 1% property tax levy growth cap (2001)

Initiative 747, sponsored by Tim Eyman, limited regular property tax levy growth in all taxing jurisdictions in Washington to 101 percent of the previous year's levy (plus new construction). In other words, the total dollar amount collected by a jurisdiction in regular property taxes could grow at most 1 percent per year, without voter approval for a larger increase. Voters could still approve levy lid lifts by simple majority in specific elections, but the automatic year-over-year growth was capped at 1 percent.

I-747 passed in the November 6, 2001 general election by 58 percent to 42 percent. The political message was clear: Washington voters were tired of automatic property tax growth tied to inflation or assessed value increases and wanted a hard cap that could only be exceeded by affirmative voter consent.

The initiative was immediately challenged in court. In 2007, the Washington Supreme Court ruled that I-747 was unconstitutional on single-subject and ballot title grounds — specifically because it modified statutes that had themselves been superseded before the initiative was voted on, making the initiative's effect different from what voters had been told. This is a technical holding, not a ruling on the substantive merits of a 1 percent cap. Within days of the court's ruling, Governor Christine Gregoire called the legislature into a one-day special session, and the legislature re-enacted the substance of I-747 statutorily in November 2007. The 1 percent cap has remained in place as a statutory matter ever since.

The long-term effects of I-747 on Washington fiscal policy have been significant. Because most taxing jurisdictions' costs — wages, health care, capital projects — grow faster than 1 percent per year, the 1 percent cap creates a structural squeeze on local government revenues relative to local government costs. Over time, jurisdictions have had to choose between cutting services, raising voter-approved excess levies (especially for schools), and deferring maintenance. The McCleary school funding litigation, which dominated Washington fiscal policy from 2012 to 2018, can be understood in part as a consequence of the I-747 squeeze on local school district revenues, which the state was ultimately ordered to offset through state-level funding increases.

I-747 is the single most visible policy outcome of the initiative era. But the era also produced a broader political consensus that has proven extraordinarily durable: no state income tax, a capped property tax, and the continuation of the 1935 Revenue Act architecture as the default tax structure. This consensus began to crack only in the late 2010s.

## 9. The "Broadening the Tax Base" (BTB) reforms of 2010

The Great Recession of 2008-2010 produced Washington's worst fiscal crisis since the Great Depression. State revenues fell sharply as consumption and business activity contracted. The state faced multi-billion dollar budget shortfalls across multiple biennia. In response, the 2010 legislature enacted a set of base-broadening measures intended to bring in additional revenue without raising rates on most taxpayers.

The headline change was a temporary increase in the B&O tax rate for service and other business activities from 1.5 percent to 1.8 percent. This 20 percent increase in the service B&O rate was sold as a temporary Depression-era expedient and was scheduled to expire. It did in fact expire on schedule, and the service B&O rate returned to 1.5 percent. The 2010 rate increase is remembered as a successful example of a temporary revenue measure actually sunsetting as promised.

The 2010 session also included a number of smaller base-broadening measures: narrowing of certain deductions, closing of several preferences that had been used by narrow classes of taxpayers, and clarification of nexus rules. Collectively these measures raised perhaps a few hundred million dollars of additional revenue per biennium — useful but not transformative.

The deeper significance of the 2010 session was that it signaled a willingness on the part of the legislature to actually increase taxes in response to a fiscal emergency, something that had been politically difficult throughout the preceding initiative era. In that sense, 2010 was a bridge between the initiative-era anti-tax consensus and the more assertive progressive taxation that would emerge at the end of the decade.

## 10. The 2017-2022 progressive tax wave

Beginning around 2017, the political center of gravity in Olympia shifted. Democratic majorities in both chambers, a Democratic governor (Jay Inslee), and a generation of progressive activists inside and outside state government began pushing a progressive tax agenda with more seriousness than at any time since the 1970s. Three developments mark this period.

**First**, the 2018 legislature enacted the Working Families Tax Credit (WFTC), a state-level earned income tax credit analog that provides cash refunds to low-income working families. Although structured as a credit against the sales tax, the WFTC was the first significant piece of progressive distributional tax policy in Washington in decades. It did not begin paying out until 2023 after an extended implementation period.

**Second**, the 2020 legislature authorized (and Seattle enacted) a set of progressive revenue measures at the city level, including Seattle's JumpStart payroll tax (discussed below). Although these were local, they signaled that large Washington cities were prepared to move forward with progressive taxation even if the state was still constrained by *Culliton*.

**Third**, in 2021 the state legislature enacted a capital gains excise tax (discussed in more detail below), directly challenging the *Culliton* doctrine by characterizing the new levy as an excise tax rather than an income tax. The 2022-2023 litigation in *Quinn v. State* became the most consequential tax case before the Washington Supreme Court in decades.

By the end of this period, the anti-income-tax consensus had not collapsed, but it had been significantly eroded. Progressive tax advocates had successfully passed a capital gains tax, upheld it in the state Supreme Court, seen the U.S. Supreme Court decline to review, and built the political coalition that would drive the 2025 reforms.

## 11. Seattle's attempted income tax

In 2017, the Seattle City Council enacted an ordinance imposing a 2.25 percent tax on high-income households — a city-level income tax in the city where it was most politically popular to levy one. The ordinance was immediately challenged. The superior court struck it down on multiple grounds, including a state statutory prohibition on city income taxes (RCW 36.65.030) and the *Culliton* constitutional doctrine.

The case worked its way up to the Washington Court of Appeals, which in 2019 held that the statutory prohibition was unconstitutional as applied (because it violated the state constitution's prohibition on special legislation) but that the Seattle ordinance was nevertheless invalid because it was a non-uniform income tax that ran afoul of *Culliton*. The Washington Supreme Court declined to review the appellate decision. The practical effect was that Seattle could not enact a progressive income tax, but cities in Washington did become free, under the appellate court's reasoning, to enact flat-rate income taxes — a hollow victory because no Washington city actually wanted to enact a flat income tax and the political appetite was entirely for progressive rates.

The Seattle income tax saga is a footnote in the broader story, but it is a footnote that matters because it demonstrated two things simultaneously: that progressive political energy was growing, and that *Culliton* remained the binding constraint.

## 12. The Seattle JumpStart payroll tax (2020)

On July 6, 2020, the Seattle City Council enacted a new payroll expense tax, branded as "JumpStart Seattle." The tax applies to businesses operating in Seattle with at least $7 million in annual Seattle payroll, and it is imposed on compensation paid to Seattle employees earning at least $150,000 per year. The rate structure is tiered by both business size and employee compensation, ranging from 0.7 percent to 2.4 percent, with the highest rate applied to salaries of at least $400,000 at companies with at least $1 billion in annual payroll. The tax took effect on January 1, 2021, and is scheduled to continue through December 31, 2040.

The JumpStart tax raised, by some estimates, at least $231 million in its first year of collection. It has been a significant new revenue source for Seattle, funding housing, small business support, and other city programs.

Legally, JumpStart was challenged by the Seattle Chamber of Commerce, which argued among other things that it was in substance an income tax. The Washington courts upheld JumpStart as a payroll expense tax imposed on the employer, distinct from an income tax imposed on the employee. The distinction is technically coherent (the tax is calculated from payroll paid and is the employer's liability), but critics noted that the economic incidence of a payroll tax inevitably falls on employees to some extent, and the distinction between a payroll expense tax and an income tax becomes blurry at the edges. Nevertheless, the courts sustained the tax, and it remains in place.

JumpStart matters for the broader history because it demonstrated that a progressive tax on high-income earners could be designed, enacted, and judicially sustained in Washington by characterizing it as an employer-level excise tax. This structural lesson — that the formal structure of the tax matters enormously under Washington constitutional doctrine — would be applied again, at the state level, in the 2021 capital gains tax.

## 13. The state capital gains tax (2021) and *Quinn v. State* (2022-2023)

In 2021, the Washington legislature enacted ESSB 5096, imposing a 7 percent excise tax on any individual's long-term capital gains in excess of $250,000 per calendar year (indexed for inflation) from the sale or exchange of certain long-term capital assets — principally stocks, bonds, business interests, and similar investments. Important exclusions include gains from the sale of real estate, gains from retirement accounts, gains from livestock held for specified purposes, and gains from certain small business sales. The tax became effective January 1, 2022, with the first returns due April 2023.

The capital gains tax was immediately challenged on the ground that it was in substance an income tax, and therefore a graduated property tax under *Culliton*. In March 2022, Douglas County Superior Court Judge Brian Huber agreed with the challengers and ruled the tax unconstitutional. The state appealed directly to the Washington Supreme Court, and in November 2022 the Supreme Court allowed the state to begin collecting the tax while the appeal was pending.

In March 2023, the Washington Supreme Court reversed the trial court in *Quinn v. State*, 196 Wn.2d 693 (2023). The Court held 7-2 that the capital gains tax is an excise tax, not an income tax, because it is imposed on the privilege of selling or exchanging capital assets rather than on income as such. The Court relied on the distinction between excise taxes (which measure a taxable event or privilege) and property taxes (which measure ownership or possession of property). Because the capital gains tax is triggered by a transaction — a sale or exchange — and is measured by the gain on that transaction, the Court reasoned that it is structurally an excise tax, similar in principle to the real estate excise tax that Washington has imposed for decades.

The dissent argued that this reasoning strains the ordinary meaning of "excise tax" beyond recognition. Under the majority's logic, the dissenters argued, almost any tax could be recharacterized as an excise tax by identifying some "taxable event" to which it is nominally attached. The dissent would have held that the tax is functionally an income tax on realized capital gains and therefore unconstitutional under *Culliton*.

The challengers sought U.S. Supreme Court review. On January 16, 2024, the U.S. Supreme Court denied certiorari in *Quinn v. Washington*, without explanation. The effect was to leave the Washington Supreme Court's decision in place and to establish the capital gains tax as a permanent feature of the Washington tax system.

The capital gains tax raised approximately $900 million in its first year of collection — significantly more than the state had projected. The top 0.2 percent of Washington households now pay this tax, making it by far the most progressive element of the state tax system. Because the tax is concentrated on a very small number of high-wealth taxpayers, it is volatile from year to year (dependent on stock market performance and on the timing of large asset sales), but over multi-year averages it produces substantial revenue.

The doctrinal significance of *Quinn* is enormous. By distinguishing *Culliton* rather than overruling it, the Washington Supreme Court preserved the formal rule that graduated income taxes are unconstitutional while simultaneously opening a path for progressive taxation via the excise-tax label. In the 2025 reforms, this path would be traveled further.

## 14. The 2025 tectonic shift — HB 2081, SB 5813, and SB 5814

On May 20, 2025, Washington Governor Bob Ferguson signed into law three bills that together constitute the largest set of tax changes in Washington State history: HB 2081, SB 5813, and SB 5814. These bills emerged from the 2025 regular session as part of the biennial budget process, driven by a projected revenue shortfall for the 2025-2027 biennium and by progressive political pressure to make the tax system less regressive.

### HB 2081 — B&O tax changes

HB 2081 made three principal changes to the B&O tax.

**First**, it raised the B&O rate on service and other business activities for high-revenue taxpayers. Service providers with more than $5 million in annual gross receipts saw their rate increased from 1.75 percent to 2.1 percent. This is a 20 percent relative rate increase at the top end, and it applies to the largest services firms (law firms, consulting firms, accounting firms, large technology service providers, and many others).

**Second**, it added a new B&O surcharge of 0.5 percent on Washington-apportioned gross income in excess of $250 million per year. The surcharge takes effect on January 1, 2026, and sunsets at the end of 2030. This surcharge was explicitly targeted at very large businesses — companies with more than a quarter of a billion dollars in annual Washington receipts — and was sold politically as a "big business" tax.

**Third**, HB 2081 codified a legislative response to the Washington Supreme Court's October 2024 decision in *Antio, LLC v. Washington Department of Revenue*. The *Antio* decision had sharply narrowed the investment income deduction under RCW 82.04.4281, holding that the deduction applies only to investment income that is "incidental" to the taxpayer's main business activity. HB 2081 provides a quantitative definition of "incidental": investment income is incidental if it represents less than 5 percent of the taxpayer's total annual worldwide gross income. It also defines what types of entities may take the deduction and the specific categories of income that qualify. The statutory clarification reduced, but did not eliminate, the sharp narrowing effect of *Antio*. Separately, the Department of Revenue created a voluntary disclosure program for taxpayers who had relied on the pre-*Antio* understanding of the deduction and needed to come into compliance with the narrower rule.

### SB 5813 — Capital gains and estate tax increases

SB 5813 increased the capital gains excise tax rate on gains in excess of $1 million per year. For gains above $1 million, the rate increased from 7 percent to 9.9 percent (a 2.9 percentage point increase). Gains between $250,000 (the exclusion amount) and $1 million continue to be taxed at 7 percent. The net effect is to make the capital gains tax more steeply progressive at the high end, concentrating additional burden on very large gains.

SB 5813 also made major changes to the state estate tax, taking effect July 1, 2025:

- The estate tax exemption increased from $2.193 million per decedent to $3 million per decedent, and is indexed for inflation going forward.
- The top marginal rate on large estates increased, with a new top rate of 35 percent applying to the portion of an estate above $9 million. The prior rate structure topped out at 20 percent.
- Intermediate brackets were restructured, with the effect that estates between approximately $3 million and $9 million faced rates that increased more gradually than under prior law, while the very largest estates faced a significant rate increase.

The net effect of the estate tax changes is to exempt a larger share of middle-class estates (families with $2 million to $3 million of net worth are now below the threshold) while increasing the burden on very large estates.

### SB 5814 — Retail sales tax base expansion

SB 5814 expanded the retail sales tax base to include a wide range of services that had previously been outside the sales tax base. Effective July 1, 2025, the sales tax applies to services including (but not limited to) advertising services, certain IT services, live presentations, certain custom software services, temporary staffing services, and various other professional and business services.

The base expansion is significant both for its revenue impact and for its doctrinal significance. Washington's retail sales tax has historically been a tax on tangible personal property with a limited (but growing) list of taxable services. The 2025 expansion represents the largest one-time broadening of the taxable services base in the history of the tax. For many service providers, the change required a fundamental rethinking of invoicing, pricing, and sales tax collection infrastructure, all on a compressed implementation timeline between the May 20, 2025 signing date and the July 1, 2025 effective date. The Department of Revenue issued a series of special notices and FAQ documents during the intervening six weeks to explain the new rules, and the compliance community (CPAs, tax attorneys, industry associations) produced a stream of guidance and alerts.

### The political coalition that enabled the 2025 reforms

Several factors converged to make the 2025 reforms possible:

- A continuing projected revenue shortfall for the 2025-2027 biennium, driven by a combination of lower-than-expected sales tax collections, rising human services and education costs, and previously authorized spending commitments.
- Strong Democratic majorities in both chambers of the Washington legislature, producing the votes needed to pass significant tax increases.
- A newly elected progressive governor (Bob Ferguson, who succeeded Jay Inslee in January 2025) committed to progressive tax reform and willing to sign large tax bills.
- Continued progressive political mobilization around the argument that Washington's tax system is the second-most regressive in the nation (according to ITEP's "Who Pays?" report).
- The doctrinal opening created by *Quinn v. State*, which had demonstrated that the Washington Supreme Court would uphold creatively labeled excise taxes even when they functioned like income taxes.

### Business community response

The business community response to the 2025 reforms was sharply negative, particularly among services firms and large employers. Industry groups argued that the rate increases and base expansion would make Washington less competitive for professional services and would drive some employers to move operations to lower-tax states. Several high-profile Washington-based companies made public statements about the business climate, though (as of late 2025) few had actually relocated operations as a direct result of the changes.

The more common response was operational: compliance teams scrambled to implement the new rules, tax advisors rewrote internal guidance, invoicing systems were reconfigured, and customers were notified of new sales tax line items on invoices. The compliance cost of the 2025 changes — particularly the SB 5814 services expansion — was significant and ongoing.

The longer-term economic impact of the 2025 reforms remains to be seen. Washington Policy Center and other center-right think tanks have predicted significant negative effects. Progressive organizations such as ITEP and the Economic Opportunity Institute have predicted only modest negative effects, offset by the value of the services the new revenue funds. These predictions will take years to resolve empirically, and the 0.5 percent surcharge's 2030 sunset will provide a natural experiment in how large surcharges affect business location decisions.

# Part 2 — The Department of Revenue

## 15. DOR overview, history, and structure

The Washington Department of Revenue (DOR) is the primary state agency responsible for administering Washington's tax laws. It was established in its modern form in 1967, consolidating functions that had previously been scattered across the Tax Commission, the Department of General Administration, and other agencies. DOR is headed by a director appointed by the Governor and confirmed by the state Senate. The director is a cabinet-level official.

DOR's principal responsibilities include:

- Administering the state retail sales tax, use tax, business and occupation tax, public utility tax, and other major excise taxes.
- Administering the state real estate excise tax (in cooperation with county treasurers, who handle the front-line collection).
- Administering the state capital gains excise tax (since 2022).
- Administering the state estate tax.
- Issuing business licenses through the Business Licensing Service (BLS), which is a division of DOR.
- Publishing interpretive guidance (rules, determinations, special notices, and industry guides).
- Conducting taxpayer education and outreach.
- Conducting audits and enforcement.
- Administering unclaimed property.

DOR is organized into several divisions, including the Taxpayer Services Division (which handles registration, returns processing, and general taxpayer interaction), the Audit Division (which conducts field and desk audits), the Compliance Division (which handles delinquent collections), the Administrative Review and Hearings Division (which hears administrative appeals), the Property Tax Division (which oversees county-level assessment practices), the Business Licensing Service, and the Research and Fiscal Analysis Division.

As of 2025, DOR has approximately 1,500 full-time employees and operates from a headquarters in Tumwater (near Olympia) with regional offices in Seattle, Bellingham, Richland, Spokane, Tacoma, Vancouver, and Wenatchee.

DOR's culture has, over the decades, emphasized the idea that Washington's tax system is complex enough that ordinary taxpayers need proactive guidance to comply. This has produced a substantial body of interpretive material — rules, industry guides, special notices, and determinations — that is generally regarded as well-organized and accessible by the standards of state tax administration. The annual Washington State Tax Reference Manual, the Washington Administrative Code (WAC) Chapter 458 (on taxation), and DOR's public website are the principal repositories of this material.

## 16. The My DOR portal

The My DOR portal is Washington's electronic tax administration platform. It serves as the primary interface for taxpayer registration, return filing, payment, account management, and correspondence with DOR. Over the past decade, DOR has progressively migrated functions from paper and legacy systems into My DOR, with the result that the portal is now the default channel for almost all taxpayer interactions.

Functions available through My DOR include:

- **Registration**: A new business can register for a Business License, obtain a UBI number, and create a DOR account all through the unified My DOR workflow.
- **Excise tax return filing**: The Combined Excise Tax Return (CETR) can be filed electronically. Most businesses are required to file electronically.
- **Payment**: Payments can be made by ACH debit, ACH credit (EFT), credit card (with a processing fee), or check (with limited exceptions for electronic filing mandates).
- **Account management**: Taxpayers can view their filing history, current account balances, and DOR correspondence.
- **Correspondence**: DOR's letters, notices, and audit correspondence are increasingly delivered through My DOR's secure messaging rather than by paper mail.
- **Business license renewal**: Annual business license renewals are processed through My DOR.

Compared to the tax portals of other states, My DOR is generally well-regarded. It is more capable than the portals of many small and medium states, and roughly comparable to the portals of large states such as California's MyFTB or New York's Online Services. It is not quite as polished as some state-of-the-art commercial tax software, but it is sufficient for the vast majority of compliance tasks and is continually improved with new features.

One notable characteristic of My DOR is the relatively unified workflow for business licensing and excise tax. In many states, the business licensing function and the tax administration function are handled by separate agencies with separate systems; a new business must interact with multiple portals to complete registration. In Washington, the Business Licensing Service is part of DOR, and My DOR allows a new business to complete registration for the license and for tax purposes in a single session. This is a meaningful administrative convenience and reflects a longstanding design choice at DOR to treat business licensing and tax registration as fundamentally related functions.

## 17. Industry guides as operational doctrine

DOR publishes a substantial library of industry guides — detailed interpretive documents that explain how the various Washington taxes apply to specific industries. These guides are not formally binding rules, but they represent DOR's considered position on how the law applies to the industry in question, and they are treated as authoritative by practitioners, by DOR auditors, and (in most cases) by the courts.

Industry guides currently exist for, among other sectors:

- Construction and contracting
- Manufacturing
- Retail
- Restaurants and food service
- Professional services (including legal, accounting, consulting)
- Information technology and software
- Healthcare
- Real estate
- Financial services
- Agriculture and farming
- Transportation and logistics

Each industry guide walks through the major tax issues an industry faces — which B&O classification applies, what sales tax issues arise, what exemptions and deductions are available, what record-keeping is required, and common audit issues. For a new business entering a particular industry, the relevant industry guide is usually the best starting point for understanding its tax obligations. For an experienced practitioner, the industry guide is the document most frequently cited in letters to clients and in responses to auditor inquiries.

The industry guides are updated periodically, though the update cadence varies across guides. Major legislative changes (such as the 2025 reforms) typically trigger updates to affected guides within six months to a year. Minor changes may take longer to be reflected.

## 18. Special notices as interim authority

When the legislature enacts a new tax law or amends an existing one, there is typically a gap between the effective date of the change and the formal publication of updated rules (Washington Administrative Code provisions) or updated industry guides. DOR fills this gap with "special notices" — interim guidance documents that explain how DOR intends to administer the new law.

Special notices are not formal rules. They have not gone through the formal rulemaking process of the Administrative Procedure Act. But they represent DOR's official position, and taxpayers who rely in good faith on a special notice generally receive some measure of protection from retroactive penalty assessment if DOR's position later changes (under doctrines of detrimental reliance and administrative estoppel).

Special notices are particularly important in the immediate aftermath of major legislative changes. After the 2025 reforms, for example, DOR issued a series of special notices on:

- The expanded services sales tax base under SB 5814, including guidance on what constitutes a "taxable service" in the various new categories.
- The B&O rate increase for high-revenue services firms under HB 2081, including guidance on how the $5 million threshold is calculated and how it interacts with the existing rate structure.
- The Washington-apportioned gross income surcharge, including guidance on the computation of Washington-apportioned receipts for purposes of the surcharge.
- The new investment income deduction rules under the *Antio*-response provisions, including the definition of "incidental" investment income and the related voluntary disclosure program.
- The changes to the capital gains tax rate and the estate tax exemption.

Practitioners track special notices closely through DOR's website, which maintains a running list of recent notices. A well-organized tax department in a Washington business will typically monitor the DOR special notices page on a weekly or monthly basis and flag notices relevant to the business for internal review.

## 19. Rulings, determinations, and the Administrative Review and Hearings Division

For specific fact patterns, DOR issues two categories of ruling.

**Letter rulings** (also called "private letter rulings") are responses to taxpayer-initiated requests for guidance on specific factual situations. A taxpayer submits a detailed description of a transaction or business operation and asks DOR to confirm how the various Washington taxes will apply. DOR's response is binding on DOR with respect to the specific taxpayer and the specific facts presented, provided those facts are accurate and complete. Letter rulings are a useful tool for taxpayers who are considering a significant transaction or business reorganization and want certainty before acting.

**Determinations** are the written decisions issued by the Administrative Review and Hearings Division (ARHD) in the context of taxpayer appeals of audit assessments. When a taxpayer disagrees with an audit assessment, the first level of appeal is to ARHD. ARHD reviews the audit, considers the taxpayer's arguments and any additional evidence, and issues a written determination that either affirms, modifies, or reverses the assessment. Determinations are issued in a published, anonymized format ("Det. No. XX-XXX, XX WTD XXX") and form a body of administrative precedent that is influential (though not binding) in subsequent disputes.

The *Washington Tax Decisions* series (WTD) is the official publication of these determinations. It is published periodically and is searchable through DOR's website. Practitioners treat the WTD as an important interpretive source, second only to the WAC and the statutes themselves.

ARHD is not a court. It is an internal DOR body, albeit one that operates with some functional independence from the Audit Division whose work it reviews. A taxpayer who receives an adverse ARHD determination has two main options: pay the tax and sue for a refund in Thurston County Superior Court, or appeal the determination to the Washington Board of Tax Appeals.

## 20. The Washington Board of Tax Appeals

The Washington State Board of Tax Appeals (BTA) was created by the legislature in 1967 as an independent state agency. It is not part of DOR and is not part of any other state agency. The BTA hears appeals from DOR determinations and from county boards of equalization on property tax valuations. BTA decisions are written by a small number of board members (typically three) and are published.

Appeals from DOR determinations must be filed with the BTA within 30 days of the determination date. This is a strict deadline; DOR does not have authority to extend it. Missing the 30-day window forecloses the BTA path and typically leaves only the pay-and-sue-for-refund option in Superior Court.

The BTA offers two procedural tracks:

- **Informal hearings**: The default option for most disputes. Informal hearings are less procedurally rigorous, generally faster, and less expensive. A decision issued in an informal hearing, however, cannot be appealed to court. It is the end of the line for that dispute.
- **Formal hearings**: Conducted in accordance with the Administrative Procedure Act, Chapter 34.05 RCW. Formal hearings produce a complete record (including testimony, exhibits, and findings of fact) that can be used on appeal to Superior Court if either party is dissatisfied with the BTA's decision. Formal hearings are more procedurally intensive and are typically chosen by larger or more complex disputes.

A taxpayer who is dissatisfied with a BTA formal decision can appeal to Thurston County Superior Court, then to the Washington Court of Appeals, and ultimately to the Washington Supreme Court. These judicial appeal paths are rarely used — the vast majority of tax disputes are resolved at the DOR determination stage or at the BTA — but they are available for cases raising significant legal issues.

The BTA's decisions are published and searchable. Together with DOR's administrative determinations, they constitute the body of precedent practitioners consult when analyzing novel tax issues.

# Part 3 — Tax Compliance Framework

## 21. Registration and the UBI number

Every business operating in Washington needs to register with the state. Registration is accomplished through the Business Licensing Service (BLS), which is part of DOR, via the Business License Application. The application can be filed electronically through the My DOR portal or (less commonly) on paper.

Upon successful registration, DOR issues:

- A **Business License** (formerly called the Master Business License), which is the basic legal authorization to do business in Washington.
- A **Unified Business Identifier (UBI) number**, which is a nine-digit number that serves as the business's unique identifier for Washington state agency purposes. The same UBI number is used for DOR tax accounts, Department of Labor and Industries accounts, Employment Security Department accounts, and Secretary of State entity records.

The UBI number is not a tax ID in the federal sense (the federal Employer Identification Number, EIN, is a separate number issued by the IRS). But within Washington, the UBI is the master identifier that ties together all of a business's state registrations.

### When a Business License is required

A Business License is generally required if any of the following apply:

- The business requires city or state endorsements (most kinds of business do).
- The business is using a name other than the owner's full legal name (a "trade name" or "doing business as" name).
- The business plans to hire employees within the next 90 days.
- The business sells taxable goods or services and is required to collect sales tax.
- The business has gross income of $12,000 per year or more.

In practice, almost every business that operates in Washington on a sustained basis will meet one or more of these criteria and will need a Business License.

### Processing time and cost

The Business License Application typically takes approximately 10 business days to process after submission. There is a base application fee (historically around $90, subject to change) plus optional endorsement fees for specific city or state endorsements. An expedited processing option is available for an additional fee.

### The unified registration workflow

One of the strengths of Washington's business registration system is that the Business License Application collects, in a single form, all the information needed to register a business for Washington DOR tax accounts, the Department of Labor and Industries (workers' compensation), and the Employment Security Department (unemployment insurance). A business that hires employees will need all three registrations, and the unified workflow saves significant time compared to systems (in some other states) where each agency has a separate registration process.

After initial registration, a business receives a welcome letter from DOR indicating:

- Its assigned UBI number.
- Its Washington DOR tax account number.
- Its assigned excise tax return filing frequency (monthly, quarterly, or annual).
- Information on how to log into My DOR and begin filing returns.

## 22. The Combined Excise Tax Return

The Combined Excise Tax Return (CETR) is the primary return used by most Washington businesses to report and pay the state excise taxes administered by DOR. As its name suggests, the CETR combines multiple taxes onto a single return form:

- **Business and occupation (B&O) tax** — reported by classification (retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, services, etc.), with separate lines for each classification that applies to the business.
- **Retail sales tax** — reported on gross taxable sales, with separate line items for the various local sales tax jurisdictions in which the business has a taxable presence.
- **Use tax** — reported on use-taxable purchases where sales tax was not collected at purchase.
- **Public utility tax** — reported by utility classification where applicable.
- **Other excise taxes** — reported as applicable (litter tax, tire tax, and a number of narrow industry-specific taxes).

The CETR is filed electronically through My DOR by most taxpayers. Electronic filing is mandatory for businesses above certain thresholds, and essentially all active businesses file electronically in practice.

The CETR's line-item structure is detailed and can be confusing to new taxpayers. A typical return for a services business might have, under the B&O section, entries in the "Service and other business activities" classification line. A retail business might have entries under "Retailing" B&O and under the sales tax section, with breakdowns by local tax jurisdiction. A manufacturer might have entries under "Manufacturing" B&O, "Wholesaling" B&O (for sales to distributors), and sometimes "Retailing" B&O (for direct-to-consumer sales), plus use tax on certain purchases.

The return also includes deductions, credits, and exemptions reported separately. A business that qualifies for the Small Business B&O Credit, for example, reports it on a specific line; a business claiming the High Technology B&O Credit reports it on another line.

## 23. Filing frequencies and how they are assigned

DOR assigns each business a filing frequency based on its tax liability. The frequencies are:

- **Monthly**: Required for businesses whose estimated or actual tax liability exceeds approximately $4,800 per year ($400 per month). Monthly returns are due on the 25th of the month following the reporting period.
- **Quarterly**: For businesses with liability between approximately $1,050 per year and $4,800 per year. Quarterly returns are due at the end of the month following the end of each calendar quarter (April 30, July 31, October 31, and January 31).
- **Annual**: For small businesses with liability below approximately $1,050 per year. Annual returns are due by April 15 of the following year (historically) or by the date specified in the assignment letter.

Note: the dollar thresholds for frequency assignment are updated periodically by DOR and should be verified against the current year's guidance. The rough tiers stay roughly stable but the exact cutoff amounts can shift.

A new business is assigned an initial frequency based on DOR's estimate of its expected activity, which in turn is based on the information provided in the Business License Application. After the business has a year of actual filing history, DOR will reassess the frequency and may move the business to a different tier.

A taxpayer who believes they are on the wrong frequency (for example, because their business has shrunk and they would prefer quarterly filing over monthly) can request a frequency change from DOR. The request is generally granted if the actual tax liability supports the requested frequency.

Filing frequency matters for several reasons beyond the obvious one of how often the taxpayer must file returns. The penalty structure (discussed below) is keyed to due dates, and the due dates depend on the filing frequency. The cash flow implications of monthly vs. quarterly filing can be significant for small businesses. And the audit risk profile differs somewhat across frequencies, with monthly filers facing slightly higher scrutiny because they are typically larger and have more complex operations.

## 24. Payment mechanics and EFT

Washington excise tax payments can be made by several methods:

- **ACH debit (EFT)**: The taxpayer authorizes DOR to pull funds from a specified bank account on a specified date. This is the default payment method for most businesses using My DOR. It is free (no processing fees) and reliable.
- **ACH credit**: The taxpayer initiates an EFT transfer from their bank to DOR's designated account. This method is used mainly by very large taxpayers whose treasury operations prefer to control the timing of fund movements. It requires coordination with the taxpayer's bank and is more complex than ACH debit.
- **Credit card**: Accepted, but with a processing fee (historically around 2.3 percent) that is paid by the taxpayer. This is generally not cost-effective for large payments.
- **Check**: Still permitted for certain categories of taxpayers and certain tax types, but increasingly discouraged.

Large taxpayers (those whose payments exceed certain thresholds) are required to use EFT and cannot pay by check. The threshold for mandatory EFT is relatively low by 2020s standards — on the order of a few thousand dollars per payment — so in practice almost all taxpayers with meaningful tax obligations are using EFT.

Same-day payment requirements apply to very large taxpayers in certain circumstances. A taxpayer whose prior-year tax liability exceeded a specified threshold may be required to make payments by the same day the return is due (rather than initiating an ACH debit that settles the following day). These same-day requirements are intended to ensure that the state treasury has immediate access to the largest payments and to prevent float gaming by the largest taxpayers.

## 25. The penalty schedule in detail

Washington imposes a structured penalty schedule for late payment and late filing of excise tax returns. The penalties are prescribed by statute and administered by DOR with limited discretion to waive them. The key penalties are:

### Late payment penalty

**9% late payment penalty** — Applied when the tax due on a filed return is not paid by the original due date. The 9 percent penalty accrues on any unpaid tax from the due date through the end of the month following the due date.

**19% late payment penalty** — Applied if the tax remains unpaid past the end of the month following the due date. This is not an additional 10 percent on top of the 9 percent; it is a step-up in the cumulative penalty rate. The tax is now subject to a total 19 percent penalty.

**29% late payment penalty** — Applied if the tax remains unpaid past the end of the second month following the due date. Again, this is a step-up, not an additional layer. The cumulative penalty is 29 percent of the tax due.

The structure is best understood through a concrete example. Suppose a monthly filer's January return is due on February 25. If the return is filed on time but the tax is not paid:

- On February 26, a 9 percent late payment penalty attaches to the unpaid tax.
- If the tax is still unpaid on April 1 (one day after the end of March, which is the month following the February 25 due date), the penalty rises to 19 percent.
- If the tax is still unpaid on May 1 (one day after the end of April, which is the second month following the due date), the penalty rises to 29 percent.

The 29 percent late payment penalty is the maximum that applies purely on the basis of late payment. Additional penalties can apply for late filing or for more serious offenses.

### Late filing penalty

**5% late filing penalty** — Applied when a return is not filed by the due date, even if no tax is due. The minimum late filing penalty is $5.

The late filing penalty is separate from the late payment penalty. A taxpayer who files on time but pays late incurs the late payment penalty alone. A taxpayer who both files late and pays late incurs both penalties. A taxpayer who files late but has zero tax due still incurs the minimum $5 late filing penalty.

### Willful non-payment and fraud penalty

**39% maximum penalty** — Applied in cases of willful non-payment, willful evasion, or fraud. This is a stacked penalty: the 29 percent late payment penalty plus an additional 10 percent for willfulness. It is reserved for cases where DOR determines that the taxpayer intentionally evaded or willfully refused to pay. The standard is high, and the 39 percent penalty is relatively rare in practice.

### Interest

In addition to penalties, interest accrues on underpayments from the due date until the tax is paid. The interest rate is set periodically by DOR based on the prior-year average federal short-term rate plus a statutory margin. Interest is compounded and accrues monthly.

### Minimum late payment penalty

The minimum late payment penalty is $5 even for very small underpayments. This minimum matters principally for annual filers whose tax liability is low.

### Penalty waiver

DOR has limited authority to waive late payment and late filing penalties. Waiver is available in narrowly defined circumstances, including:

- **Circumstances beyond the taxpayer's control**: Examples include natural disasters, serious illness, death of an owner or key employee, and postal service failures.
- **DOR error**: If DOR's own action or inaction caused the late filing or payment (for example, a problem with the My DOR portal on the due date).
- **Timely filing with good-faith error**: A one-time "free pass" is available for taxpayers who have a clean filing history and make a late payment for the first time in 24 months, provided certain conditions are met.

Routine penalty waiver requests are handled by DOR through a written request process. The taxpayer explains the circumstances, provides supporting documentation, and DOR responds in writing. Success rates vary: circumstances that clearly meet the statutory criteria (a hospitalized owner during the filing period, for example) are routinely waived; circumstances that are more ambiguous (general business difficulties, cash flow problems) are routinely denied.

## 26. Audit procedures and the statute of limitations

DOR conducts audits through its Audit Division. The audit process varies depending on the size and complexity of the taxpayer, but the basic structure is consistent.

### Types of audits

- **Field audits**: Conducted at the taxpayer's place of business. The auditor (or audit team) spends days, weeks, or in large cases months on-site reviewing the taxpayer's books and records, interviewing personnel, and examining specific transactions. Field audits are the standard approach for large taxpayers and for complex issues.
- **Desk audits**: Conducted at a DOR office. The taxpayer is asked to submit specified records and documents to DOR, and the auditor reviews them remotely. Desk audits are more common for smaller taxpayers and for narrowly scoped issues.
- **Limited-scope audits**: Focused on a specific issue or category of transaction (for example, use tax on fixed-asset purchases, or nexus for out-of-state sellers). These can be conducted as desk audits or field audits depending on complexity.

### Statute of limitations

The general statute of limitations for Washington excise tax assessment is **four years** from the due date of the return (or the filing date, if later). After four years, DOR generally cannot assess additional tax for a period, even if the taxpayer underreported.

Several exceptions extend or eliminate the four-year limit:

- **Unfiled returns**: If a taxpayer never filed a return for a period, there is no statute of limitations for that period. DOR can assess tax for unfiled periods indefinitely.
- **Fraud**: If DOR can demonstrate fraud (a high standard, requiring clear and convincing evidence of intentional evasion), the statute of limitations is tolled — that is, DOR can assess for periods older than four years.
- **Consent to extend**: In the context of an audit, taxpayers and DOR sometimes agree to extend the statute of limitations by signed waiver, to allow additional time for the audit without forcing DOR to issue a protective assessment. These waivers are common in large, complex audits.

### Audit selection

DOR selects audits through a combination of methods:

- **Risk-based selection**: DOR uses various indicators (filing patterns, industry norms, deductions claimed, ratio analysis, tips from other taxpayers or employees) to identify taxpayers who are more likely to have underreported.
- **Random selection**: A small percentage of audits are selected randomly, to ensure that no industry or size category is entirely exempt from audit attention.
- **Industry sweeps**: Periodically, DOR conducts a sweep of taxpayers in a particular industry, using audit findings from one taxpayer to identify common issues across the industry.
- **Referrals**: Audits can be triggered by referrals from other state agencies, from federal agencies (particularly the IRS), or from whistleblowers.

### Audit process

A typical field audit proceeds as follows:

1. **Notification**: The taxpayer receives a letter from DOR indicating the audit, the audit period, and the auditor assigned.
2. **Opening conference**: The auditor and the taxpayer (or the taxpayer's representative, typically a CPA or tax attorney) meet to discuss the audit scope, the taxpayer's business, and the record-production schedule.
3. **Record review**: The auditor reviews the taxpayer's books, returns, supporting documents, and other records. This phase can last from days to months depending on size and complexity.
4. **Issue identification**: The auditor identifies potential issues — transactions, deductions, or classifications that may have been reported incorrectly.
5. **Taxpayer response**: The taxpayer has the opportunity to respond to identified issues, provide additional documentation, or present legal arguments.
6. **Preliminary assessment**: The auditor issues a preliminary assessment, which the taxpayer can contest.
7. **Final assessment**: After any further exchange, the auditor issues a final audit assessment with formal findings and calculations.
8. **Appeal rights**: The taxpayer has 30 days to request an administrative review by the DOR Administrative Review and Hearings Division, or to pay and then sue for refund.

### Audit advocacy

Taxpayers under audit are generally well-advised to engage professional representation — a CPA or tax attorney with Washington excise tax experience. The issues can be technical, the dollar amounts can be significant, and the difference between a well-defended audit and a poorly defended one can be very large. Many CPAs and law firms in Washington have dedicated state and local tax (SALT) practices focused on Washington audits.

## 27. Appeals and dispute resolution

As discussed above, the formal appeal path for a Washington excise tax dispute is:

1. **Audit assessment** issued by the DOR Audit Division.
2. **Petition for review** to the DOR Administrative Review and Hearings Division (ARHD), filed within the required window (typically 30 days, though the exact window depends on the type of assessment).
3. **Administrative determination** issued by ARHD. This is the first formal adjudication, and it produces a written determination explaining DOR's position.
4. **Appeal to the Board of Tax Appeals (BTA)** within 30 days of the ARHD determination, or alternatively payment of the disputed tax and a refund action in Thurston County Superior Court.
5. **BTA decision**: issued after an informal or formal hearing.
6. **Judicial review** (only from formal BTA decisions): Thurston County Superior Court, then Washington Court of Appeals, then Washington Supreme Court on discretionary review.

At each stage, the taxpayer's legal and factual position must be preserved. Failure to raise an issue at the ARHD stage may foreclose raising it at the BTA stage; failure to raise it at the BTA stage may foreclose judicial review. Effective dispute advocacy requires careful thought about which issues to raise, which to concede, and how to frame the factual record at each level.

In most disputes, resolution occurs at the ARHD stage or earlier. Only a small fraction of audit assessments are appealed to the BTA, and only a tiny fraction are pursued to judicial review.

## 28. Voluntary disclosure

Washington operates a Voluntary Disclosure Program (VDP) for businesses that have unfiled returns or under-reported tax and want to come into compliance proactively. The VDP is administered by DOR and offers substantial benefits to qualifying taxpayers.

### Benefits of voluntary disclosure

- **Penalty waiver**: The 5 percent late filing penalty, the 5 percent unregistered business penalty, and the late payment penalties are generally waived for periods covered by the VDP agreement.
- **Limited lookback**: The lookback period is limited to the four prior years plus the current year. This means that the taxpayer does not have to file returns for periods earlier than four years before the current year, even if the business has been operating longer. Absent voluntary disclosure, DOR could potentially assess for any unfiled periods (no statute of limitations) and could impose penalties for each.
- **Confidentiality during negotiation**: The taxpayer can enter the VDP anonymously (through a representative) while negotiating the terms of the agreement, revealing its identity only when the agreement is finalized.

### Eligibility

To qualify for the VDP, the taxpayer must:

- Not currently be registered with DOR, or have a specific factual pattern (such as the post-*Antio* investment income disclosure program) that qualifies it for an otherwise unavailable program.
- Not have been contacted by DOR enforcement about the issue. Contact by DOR enforcement (as distinguished from routine taxpayer services contact) typically disqualifies the taxpayer from the VDP.
- Not have engaged in fraud or intentional evasion. The VDP is for good-faith non-compliance, not for deliberate evasion.

### Unlimited lookback for collected-but-not-remitted sales tax

One important exception to the limited lookback: if the taxpayer collected retail sales tax from customers but failed to remit it to the state, the VDP does not provide a limited lookback for that collected-but-unremitted tax. DOR takes the position that collected-but-unremitted sales tax is essentially held in trust for the state, and the taxpayer has no good-faith claim to relief for failing to remit it. The lookback is unlimited for that portion, even within the VDP framework.

### Failure to complete the VDP

If a taxpayer initiates the VDP process but fails to complete it (for example, fails to sign the Voluntary Disclosure Agreement within the required timeframe), the taxpayer loses the VDP benefits. The lookback can then be extended up to seven years plus the current year, and the late payment penalty schedule (up to 29 percent for late payment, 39 percent for willful non-payment) becomes fully applicable. The consequences of starting and then abandoning the VDP are significantly worse than either completing it or never starting it, so taxpayers should commit to the process before initiating.

### Post-Antio voluntary disclosure

In 2025, following the Washington Supreme Court's *Antio, LLC* decision narrowing the investment income deduction, DOR created a special Voluntary Disclosure Program beginning July 1, 2025. The special program was designed for taxpayers who had relied on the pre-*Antio* understanding of the investment income deduction and now faced potential liability under the narrower post-*Antio* rule. The special program extended standard VDP benefits (limited lookback, penalty waiver) to taxpayers in this specific factual situation, even if they had previously been registered with DOR and had filed returns claiming the pre-*Antio* deduction. This kind of targeted VDP, responding to a specific case development, illustrates DOR's willingness to use voluntary disclosure as a policy tool to manage large-scale compliance transitions.

## 29. The managed audit program

For larger taxpayers, DOR offers a Managed Audit Program that allows the taxpayer to conduct an internal audit with DOR oversight, rather than having DOR auditors perform the full audit directly. The managed audit program is designed to accommodate very large taxpayers whose operations are too complex for a conventional DOR audit within reasonable time and cost constraints.

Under the managed audit program:

- The taxpayer (or its professional representatives) conduct the audit work, using methodology agreed upon with DOR.
- DOR provides oversight, reviews the methodology and sample selection, and audits the audit — that is, verifies that the taxpayer's work is being conducted competently and in accordance with the agreed protocol.
- The taxpayer produces an audit report with findings, which DOR reviews and either accepts, modifies, or rejects.
- Penalties on any underpayment identified through the managed audit are typically reduced or waived, as an incentive for participation.

The managed audit program is not available to every taxpayer. It is generally reserved for large, sophisticated taxpayers with robust internal control systems, mature tax compliance operations, and the capacity to conduct a rigorous internal audit. For most businesses, a conventional DOR audit is the norm and the managed audit program is not a realistic option.

The benefits to the taxpayer of participating in the managed audit program are: control over timing and pace of the audit work, reduced disruption to ongoing operations, and penalty relief. The benefits to DOR are: leverage of limited audit resources by delegating audit labor to the taxpayer, and more thorough audits of very large taxpayers whose complexity exceeds DOR's internal audit capacity.

# Part 4 — A Year-One Compliance Calendar (Generic)

This part presents a generic year-one compliance calendar for a business formed in Washington. It is illustrative and should not be taken as specific legal or tax advice. Actual deadlines vary based on the specific facts of the business, the DOR-assigned filing frequency, and applicable extensions.

## 30. Business formation checklist

The initial registration and setup tasks typically include:

| Task | Agency | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Form the legal entity (LLC, corporation, etc.) | Washington Secretary of State | Day 1 |
| Obtain federal EIN | IRS | Day 1-2 |
| File Business License Application | DOR (Business Licensing Service) | Day 1-7 |
| Receive UBI number and Business License | DOR | Approximately day 10-14 |
| Register DOR tax account (automatic with Business License) | DOR | Part of BLA |
| Register Labor and Industries account (if hiring) | L&I | Part of BLA |
| Register Employment Security Department account (if hiring) | ESD | Part of BLA |
| Open business bank account | Bank of choice | Days 10-14 |
| Register with city for city B&O tax (if applicable) | City (Seattle, Tacoma, Bellevue, etc.) | Within the city's required window |
| Set up My DOR portal access | DOR | Days 10-14 |
| Confirm DOR-assigned filing frequency | DOR | Day 10-14 (in welcome letter) |
| Set up accounting system to track B&O and sales tax | Internal | Weeks 1-4 |

The sequence above assumes a straightforward business with no specialized licensing requirements. Businesses in regulated industries (food service, alcohol, professional services requiring state licensure, health care, transportation, cannabis, etc.) will have additional registration steps with the relevant regulatory agencies.

## 31. First excise tax return

The first Combined Excise Tax Return is due based on the DOR-assigned filing frequency:

- **Monthly filers**: The first return is due by the 25th of the month following the first full reporting month. For example, a business that begins operations on March 1 and is assigned monthly filing will file its first return (covering March activity) by April 25.
- **Quarterly filers**: The first return is due at the end of the month following the end of the first calendar quarter in which the business had activity. A business beginning operations on March 1 and assigned quarterly filing would file its first return (covering the January-March quarter) by April 30.
- **Annual filers**: The first return is due by April 15 of the year following the reporting year (historically; exact dates should be confirmed with DOR). A business beginning operations in March 2026 and assigned annual filing would file its first return (covering calendar year 2026 activity) by April 15, 2027, or by the specific date assigned by DOR.

The first excise tax return is operationally important because it establishes the business's filing pattern and its first interaction with DOR beyond the registration process. Common mistakes on first returns include:

- Reporting activity under the wrong B&O classification (choosing "Services" when "Retailing" would be correct, or vice versa).
- Failing to report sales tax collected on local tax jurisdictions where the business has nexus.
- Misclassifying exempt vs. taxable sales.
- Missing deductions that the business is entitled to claim.

A tax professional's review of the first several returns is highly advisable. Errors that are caught in year one are much easier to correct than errors that continue for years and are eventually caught in an audit.

## 32. Annual business license renewal

The Washington Business License must be renewed annually. The renewal is processed through My DOR and includes:

- Verification of business information (address, owners, officers).
- Payment of the renewal fee (historically around $71, subject to change).
- Renewal of any city and state endorsements.
- Confirmation of the business's continuing operation in Washington.

The renewal is typically due on the last day of the month in which the original license was issued. For example, a business that obtained its Business License in March must renew by the end of March each subsequent year. DOR sends renewal reminders through My DOR and (for taxpayers who have opted in) by email.

Failure to renew the Business License on time can result in:

- A late renewal penalty.
- Loss of good standing with DOR.
- Inability to legally conduct business in Washington until the renewal is completed.
- Potential secondary effects on banking relationships, contracts, and licensing.

The renewal is a routine process for most businesses and takes only a few minutes through My DOR, provided the taxpayer's information is current.

## 33. Federal coordination (scope note)

Federal tax obligations are outside the scope of this Washington-focused reference, but for completeness, a new Washington business should coordinate its state registration with its federal tax obligations:

- **Federal EIN**: Obtained from the IRS, typically through the online application. Required for most businesses and all employers.
- **Federal income tax filings**: Depend on entity type. A corporation files Form 1120; an S corporation files Form 1120-S; a partnership files Form 1065; a single-member LLC defaults to sole proprietor or corporate treatment based on elections.
- **Federal quarterly estimated tax payments**: Corporations making Form 1120 payments, and certain owners of pass-through entities, make quarterly estimated tax payments to the IRS on specified dates (typically April, June, September, and January of the following year).
- **Federal employment taxes**: Employers are responsible for withholding federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare taxes from employee wages, and for the employer share of Social Security and Medicare. Federal unemployment tax (FUTA) is also owed.

Washington's no-income-tax status means that there is no state income tax component that mirrors the federal income tax, and no need to coordinate federal and state income tax returns in the way required in states with income taxes. This simplifies some aspects of year-end compliance for Washington businesses (especially for owners of pass-through entities), but it does not eliminate the need for careful federal compliance.

## 34. Local tax obligations

A number of Washington cities impose their own local B&O taxes, in addition to the state B&O tax. A business operating in these cities must register and file separately at the city level.

### Seattle

Seattle imposes a city B&O tax on businesses with Seattle nexus. The rates vary by classification (retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, services, and others), with the service rate historically around 0.427 percent and the retailing rate around 0.219 percent. For 2025, the Seattle B&O threshold is $100,000 of Seattle gross receipts. (As a result of Proposition 2, Seattle's B&O tax threshold is scheduled to increase to $2 million effective January 1, 2026, significantly reducing the compliance burden on small businesses while concentrating the tax on larger entities.)

Seattle also imposes the JumpStart payroll expense tax, as described earlier, on businesses with at least $7 million in annual Seattle payroll.

### Tacoma

Tacoma imposes a city B&O tax with rates varying by classification. The threshold and rates are set by Tacoma city ordinance and are subject to periodic adjustment. Taxpayers operating in Tacoma should consult the Tacoma Finance Department for current rates and filing procedures.

### Bellevue

Bellevue imposes a city B&O tax with a base rate of 0.1496 percent of taxable gross receipts. Bellevue's threshold (historically around $195,000 of annual Bellevue gross receipts) is set by ordinance and is subject to periodic adjustment.

### Other cities

Approximately 40 Washington cities impose local B&O taxes. The rates, thresholds, and procedures vary widely. Cities that impose B&O tax include Everett, Bellingham, Olympia, Bremerton, Lake Forest Park, Kirkland, and many others. A multi-city business may face a significant compliance burden consisting of state B&O, state sales tax, multiple city B&O taxes, and (in Seattle) the JumpStart payroll tax.

### Interaction between state and local B&O

One important simplification: city B&O taxes are paid *in addition to* state B&O tax. There is no credit or offset between them. A dollar of revenue can be subject to both state B&O tax (at the relevant state classification rate) and city B&O tax (at the relevant city classification rate, for each city with nexus). The combined burden on a services business operating in Seattle, for example, can easily exceed 2 percent of gross receipts when state service rate, Seattle B&O rate, and the high-revenue HB 2081 rate are aggregated.

Another important point: most cities' B&O tax administration is separate from state DOR. Cities have their own filing portals, their own audit teams, and their own interpretive rules. The state cannot waive city B&O taxes, and city tax authorities do not typically coordinate audits with DOR. A taxpayer facing a city B&O audit must engage with the city directly.

# Part 5 — Tax Incentives and Credits

Washington's tax system includes a substantial number of credits, exemptions, and deferrals designed to support particular industries, activities, or policy objectives. This part provides a practitioner-oriented tour of the principal incentives.

## 35. The High Technology B&O Tax Credit

The High Technology B&O Tax Credit was historically one of the most significant tax incentives for Washington's technology sector. It provided a credit against the state B&O tax for qualifying research and development expenditures in specified "high technology" categories.

### Eligible technology categories

The credit applied to R&D in five specified categories:

1. **Advanced computing** — hardware, software, algorithms, and related technologies.
2. **Advanced materials** — novel materials and their applications.
3. **Biotechnology** — including genetic engineering, pharmaceutical research, and related life sciences.
4. **Electronic device technology** — semiconductor, communications, and related hardware.
5. **Environmental technology** — including pollution control, renewable energy, and related.

### Credit amount and structure

The credit was equal to 1.5 percent of qualifying R&D expenditures, capped at $2 million per year per taxpayer. Nonprofit research organizations received a somewhat lower credit rate (approximately 0.484 percent).

To qualify, a firm had to spend at least 0.92 percent of its taxable income on R&D activities in the qualifying categories. This minimum-expenditure threshold was designed to ensure that the credit rewarded businesses with a meaningful research commitment, not incidental R&D.

### Documentation requirements

Taxpayers claiming the credit were required to document:

- The qualifying R&D activities in detail (scope, methodology, personnel, expenditures).
- The relationship between the expenditures and the specified technology categories.
- The calculation of the credit.
- Maintain records adequate for DOR audit.

### Program history and impact

Over the program's life, approximately 1,311 firms utilized the B&O tax credit, generating cumulative tax savings of approximately $204 million. The credit was an important component of Washington's technology-sector support, particularly for mid-size firms that could not access the aerospace-specific or semiconductor-specific incentives discussed below.

### Expiration

The High Technology B&O Tax Credit expired in 2015 and was not immediately renewed. The legislature has considered renewing or replacing the credit several times since then, but as of 2026 no direct replacement has been enacted. Firms that historically relied on the credit have had to adjust their tax planning; some have pursued the federal R&D credit (a separate incentive administered by the IRS) or the sales and use tax exemption for R&D machinery and equipment (which remains available for certain investments). The expiration of the High Technology B&O Credit is often cited by Washington technology advocates as a significant gap in the state's industry support toolkit.

## 36. The rural county B&O credit

The rural county B&O credit provides a credit to manufacturers, research and development facilities, and commercial testing facilities that create new jobs in qualifying rural counties. The purpose is to encourage economic development in counties that have historically lagged the Puget Sound metropolitan areas in employment and investment.

### Qualifying counties

The list of qualifying rural counties is updated annually by DOR. Generally, a county qualifies if it has a population density below a specified threshold or meets other rural-area criteria. The list excludes King, Pierce, Snohomish, Spokane, and Thurston counties (the major metropolitan areas) and typically includes most of eastern Washington and the Olympic Peninsula counties. Community Empowerment Zones (CEZs) within otherwise non-qualifying counties may also be eligible.

### Credit amount

- **$2,000 per qualified position** with annual wages/benefits of $40,000 or less.
- **$4,000 per qualified position** with annual wages/benefits of more than $40,000.

### Employment increase requirement

To qualify, the business must create new, permanent, full-time positions that increase total employment by at least 15 percent over the following four calendar quarters, measured from the period in which the employee was hired. This requirement is designed to reward genuine expansion, not incidental hiring that replaces departing employees.

### Application procedure

Businesses must submit the Rural Area Application for New Employee B&O Tax Credit within 90 days of filling the first qualified position. Late applications are typically denied, so businesses must track their hiring carefully to ensure timely application. The credit is claimed on the business's regular Combined Excise Tax Return after the application is approved.

### Holding and clawback

Generally, the qualifying positions must be maintained for a period after the credit is claimed. If the business fails to maintain the positions (for example, by laying off the workers shortly after claiming the credit), DOR can clawback the credit. The specific holding period and clawback rules are set out in DOR's rural area guidance and should be confirmed before application.

## 37. The investment income deduction and the *Antio* clarification

RCW 82.04.4281 historically provided a deduction from the B&O tax for "amounts derived from investments." For decades, this deduction was interpreted relatively broadly, allowing many businesses to exclude investment income (interest, dividends, capital gains on securities) from their B&O tax base.

### The *Antio, LLC* decision (October 24, 2024)

In *Antio, LLC v. Washington Department of Revenue*, the Washington Supreme Court sharply narrowed the investment income deduction. The case involved 16 related limited liability companies that generated all of their income from investments and attempted to deduct 100 percent of their investment income under RCW 82.04.4281, resulting in zero B&O tax liability. The state had challenged this treatment, arguing that the deduction was never intended to apply to entities whose primary business is investment.

The Washington Supreme Court agreed with the state. The court held that the investment income deduction applies only to investment income that is *incidental* to the taxpayer's main business activity. For entities whose main business is investment, the deduction is unavailable.

The decision had significant retroactive implications. Many taxpayers — particularly investment partnerships, holding companies, and various pass-through entities with substantial investment income — had relied on the broader pre-*Antio* interpretation and now faced potential B&O tax liability for prior years.

### The 2025 legislative clarification (HB 2081)

HB 2081, signed May 20, 2025, codified a quantitative definition of "incidental." Under the amended RCW 82.04.4281, investment income is incidental to the main purpose of the taxpayer's business if the total worldwide income derived from investments is less than 5 percent of the taxpayer's total annual worldwide gross income. Investment income in excess of this 5 percent threshold does not qualify for the deduction.

HB 2081 also clarified:

- Which categories of income count as "investment income" for purposes of the deduction.
- Which types of entities may take advantage of the deduction.
- The treatment of collective investment vehicles (mutual funds, exchange-traded funds, and similar structures).

### Voluntary disclosure program

Beginning July 1, 2025, DOR created a special Voluntary Disclosure Program specifically for taxpayers who had improperly deducted investment income under the pre-*Antio* interpretation. The program:

- Limits the lookback to four years plus the current year.
- Waives penalties for underpayment of taxes, failure to register, and late return payments.
- Waives interest on the late-paid taxes.
- Is open only to taxpayers who have not already been contacted by DOR about the issue.
- Includes the taxpayer's affiliates in its scope.

The *Antio* voluntary disclosure program is a good example of targeted voluntary disclosure used as a policy tool: DOR and the legislature needed a way to transition taxpayers from the pre-*Antio* interpretation to the post-*Antio* rule without generating a wave of enforcement action and litigation, and the VDP provides that transition mechanism.

## 38. Aerospace industry credits

Washington's aerospace tax incentives are historically the largest and most significant industry-specific tax preferences in the state. Their existence reflects Boeing's economic importance to Washington and the state's sustained effort to retain and grow its aerospace cluster.

### The B&O rate preference

Aerospace manufacturers receive a preferential B&O rate (substantially below the standard manufacturing rate) on income from the manufacture of commercial airplanes and related components. The preferential rate has varied over time as the legislature has adjusted the program. At various points, the rate has been set at 0.2904 percent, 0.4235 percent, and other figures, depending on the specific activity and the legislative vintage.

### The B&O credit for aerospace M&E and computer equipment

Aerospace businesses receive a B&O tax credit for manufacturing machinery and equipment, computer hardware, computer peripherals, and software if these items are exempt from sales and use taxes. The credit is calculated based on the firm's aerospace product income as a percentage of its total manufactured goods income. In other words, a firm that does primarily aerospace work receives a larger credit; a firm that does aerospace work as part of a broader manufacturing mix receives a proportional credit.

Key features of the credit:

- Cannot be claimed until the real and personal property taxes have been paid (a cash-flow mechanism to ensure that state and local governments are paid before the credit reduces state tax).
- Credits in excess of B&O tax owed can be carried forward one year.
- Unused credits are not refundable.

### Machinery and equipment sales and use tax exemption

Machinery and equipment designed and used primarily for maintenance, repair, overhaul, or refurbishing of commercial airplanes by FAR Part 145 certified repair stations are eligible for sales and use tax exemption. This exemption has been particularly valuable for the MRO (maintenance, repair, and overhaul) sector of the Washington aerospace cluster.

### Workforce training credits

The aerospace package historically included credits tied to workforce training expenditures, recognizing that the aerospace sector depends on a steady supply of skilled technicians and engineers. These training credits have been adjusted multiple times and their current form should be verified against current DOR guidance.

### Economic impact of the aerospace cluster

Boeing is Washington's largest private employer and the anchor of the state's aerospace cluster. In 2022, Washington's aerospace and other transportation equipment manufacturing industries contributed $27.4 billion to the national GDP — more than any other state in this industry category. Washington's 234 aerospace establishments employed 73,852 workers in 2022. The aerospace cluster is concentrated in King, Snohomish, and Pierce counties but also includes significant presence in Spokane and the Tri-Cities.

### Controversy over the aerospace package

The aerospace incentive package has been controversial throughout its life. Critics argue that the incentives amount to a significant giveaway to Boeing (and to a lesser extent its suppliers) without producing proportional benefits to the state. A 2014 expansion of the aerospace incentives, enacted to retain Boeing 777X production, was particularly controversial. The state's Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee (JLARC) has conducted multiple reviews of the aerospace preferences and has generally concluded that the direct economic benefits are substantial but that the indirect costs (in terms of foregone state revenue and the political precedent of targeted industry incentives) are real.

The WTO (World Trade Organization) has ruled multiple times that certain elements of the Washington aerospace package constitute prohibited subsidies under international trade law, in the long-running Boeing-Airbus dispute. Washington has adjusted the program in response to these rulings, but the core preferences remain in place.

## 39. Semiconductor industry credits

In 2003, the Washington legislature enacted a six-preference package targeting the semiconductor industry, with the goal of retaining existing semiconductor firms in Washington and attracting new ones. The package was modeled in part on the aerospace incentive experience and included:

### B&O rate reduction

Effective December 1, 2006, the B&O rate for persons engaged in manufacturing or processing for hire semiconductor materials was reduced to 0.275 percent. This is a substantially lower rate than the standard manufacturing B&O rate.

### Sales and use tax exemption for gases and chemicals

Sales of gases and chemicals used by a manufacturer or processor for hire in the production of semiconductor materials were exempted from retail sales tax and use tax. The exemption recognizes that semiconductor fabrication uses very large volumes of high-cost specialty chemicals, and a sales tax on these inputs would represent a significant cost of doing semiconductor business in Washington.

### Investment threshold

The preferences only become effective if a firm makes an investment of at least $1 billion in new buildings, machinery, and equipment to site and operate a semiconductor microchip fabrication facility. This large-investment threshold was included to ensure that the preferences would benefit only serious semiconductor operations, not small or incidental semiconductor-related businesses.

### Impact

The semiconductor preferences have had limited uptake because the $1 billion investment threshold is very high. A handful of semiconductor operations in Washington have qualified, but the state has not become a major semiconductor hub on the scale of Arizona, Texas, or Oregon. The 2003 package is nevertheless significant as an example of targeted industry incentives structured around a minimum-investment threshold.

## 40. Biotechnology and medical device credits

Washington's biotechnology and medical device sector benefits from several overlapping incentives:

- **High Technology B&O Credit** (historical; expired 2015) — Biotechnology was one of the five qualifying categories, and many biotech firms utilized the credit.
- **Sales and use tax exemption for R&D machinery and equipment** — Under Chapter 82.63 RCW, qualifying R&D investment in machinery and equipment is exempt from sales and use tax. This exemption has been particularly valuable for biotech firms with significant laboratory investment.
- **Federal R&D credit coordination** — Although separate from state incentives, the federal R&D credit is a major factor in biotech tax planning, and Washington biotech firms typically access both federal and state incentives.

Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center, the Institute for Systems Biology, the Allen Institute, and the cluster of commercial biotech firms around South Lake Union in Seattle constitute one of the country's most important life sciences concentrations. Maintaining a supportive tax environment for this sector has been a consistent policy priority across multiple administrations.

## 41. Film and video production credits

The Motion Picture Competitiveness Program (MPCP) was first established in 2006 and provides a rebate-style incentive for qualifying film and video production in Washington. Unlike a tax credit that reduces tax liability, the MPCP provides direct cash payments to qualifying productions from a pool of funds contributed by Washington businesses that receive a B&O tax credit in exchange for their contributions. The program is administered by Washington Filmworks, a nonprofit organization with a nine-member board appointed by the Governor.

### Rebate structure

- **Feature films and episodic series**: 30 to 45 percent rebate on qualifying in-state expenditures.
- **Commercials**: 30 percent rebate on qualifying in-state expenditures.
- **Minimum in-state spending thresholds**:
  - Feature films: $500,000
  - Episodic series: $300,000 per episode
  - Commercials: $150,000

### Program funding

The MPCP is backed by approximately $15 million in annual funding, which renews each January. There are no per-project caps, so a single large production can potentially receive a substantial rebate (subject to the availability of pool funds and the program's prioritization rules).

### Unique requirement

Washington's MPCP is the only state film incentive program in the country that requires employers to provide health and retirement benefits for workers. This reflects a broader Washington policy emphasis on labor standards and was included to ensure that the film incentive does not subsidize precarious employment.

### Uses and limits

Washington has been moderately successful in attracting film and television production with the MPCP, though it has been outcompeted by larger-incentive states like Georgia, New Mexico, and Louisiana for the biggest productions. The MPCP has been particularly effective at attracting mid-size productions, episodic series, and commercials that benefit from Washington's distinctive locations (Seattle urban settings, Olympic Peninsula forests, eastern Washington scrublands, San Juan Islands coastal scenes) without requiring the massive incentives offered by the most aggressive competitor states.

## 42. Commercial airplane manufacturing preferences

The commercial airplane manufacturing preferences are a component of the broader aerospace incentive package (section 38) and deserve separate mention because of their size and controversy.

The preferences include:

- A preferential B&O rate for commercial airplane manufacturing.
- A credit for preproduction development expenditures.
- A credit for aerospace product development expenditures.
- A sales and use tax exemption for the construction of facilities used in aerospace product development.

The preferences were expanded significantly in 2003 (in connection with the launch of the 787 Dreamliner program) and again in 2013 (in connection with the 777X program). The 2013 expansion was particularly controversial because it was enacted in a special legislative session during a compressed timeframe, under explicit pressure from Boeing to commit to 777X assembly in Everett. The expanded preferences extend through 2040 under current law, making them among the longest-running industry-specific preferences in Washington tax history.

## 43. Renewable energy incentives

Washington supports renewable energy through a combination of state and federal incentives.

### State sales and use tax exemption

Certain renewable energy equipment — solar panels, wind turbines, and related equipment — has historically been eligible for sales and use tax exemption. The exemption has been adjusted multiple times to reflect changing technology and policy priorities.

### Public utility tax credits for renewable generation

Renewable electricity generators (solar, wind, biomass, small hydro) have been eligible for credits against the state public utility tax for generation that exceeds specified thresholds. The credit structure has evolved significantly with each legislative revision.

### Washington State renewable energy production incentive

A production incentive for renewable electricity has been provided through a direct payment program administered by DOR. Eligible participants include residential solar installations, commercial solar, wind, anaerobic digestion, and related systems. The program has been popular and has contributed to Washington's growth in residential solar.

### Federal interaction post-IRA

The federal Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 dramatically expanded federal tax credits for renewable energy, including the investment tax credit (ITC), the production tax credit (PTC), and credits for residential energy efficiency and electric vehicles. These federal credits are substantially larger than the state-level incentives in most cases. Washington-specific incentives remain relevant primarily for the sales and use tax exemptions and for specific projects that benefit from state-level production incentives, but the federal IRA credits have become the dominant factor in most renewable energy project finance.

## 44. Opportunity Zone interactions

The federal Opportunity Zone program, created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, provides federal capital gains tax deferral and exclusion for investments in designated low-income census tracts. Washington has approximately 139 designated Opportunity Zones, including zones in Seattle, Tacoma, Spokane, Yakima, and various rural areas.

Because Washington does not tax income (and until 2022 did not tax capital gains at all), the federal Opportunity Zone benefits do not layer with a parallel state benefit in the way they do in income-tax states. However:

- Washington's capital gains excise tax (7 percent / 9.9 percent for very large gains) applies to long-term capital gains above the exclusion threshold. Opportunity Zone investments that produce realized gains for Washington residents can be subject to the state capital gains tax. The interaction between federal Opportunity Zone deferral and the state capital gains tax is technically complex and depends on the timing and character of specific transactions.
- Opportunity Zone investments do not receive Washington B&O tax relief, sales tax relief, or property tax relief as a consequence of being in an Opportunity Zone. The Opportunity Zone benefit is purely federal.

Washington-specific investments in Opportunity Zones — particularly real estate developments — have been modestly popular, but the absence of a state-level layered benefit has limited Washington's competitive position relative to income-tax states where Opportunity Zones interact with state income tax deferral.

# Part 6 — Multi-State Issues and Economic Nexus

## 45. Economic nexus thresholds after *Wayfair*

The U.S. Supreme Court's 2018 decision in *South Dakota v. Wayfair* eliminated the physical-presence requirement for state tax nexus that had applied since 1992's *Quill v. North Dakota*. After *Wayfair*, states are free to impose sales tax collection obligations on remote sellers based on economic nexus — that is, on having enough economic activity in the state, without physical presence.

Washington moved quickly to adopt an economic nexus standard. Effective January 1, 2020, Washington's economic nexus threshold was set at:

- **$100,000 in cumulative gross receipts** from Washington sources in the current or prior calendar year, OR
- **200 separate transactions** with Washington customers in the current or prior calendar year.

This unified threshold applies to both the B&O tax and the retail sales tax. Any business that exceeds either prong must register with Washington DOR and begin filing Combined Excise Tax Returns. The $100,000 threshold aligns with the threshold discussed favorably in the *Wayfair* decision itself, providing legal cover against challenges that the threshold is burdensome on out-of-state commerce.

### The 200-transaction prong

The 200-transaction prong is less commonly discussed than the $100,000 revenue prong but can be important for certain businesses. A seller with modest dollar volume but many small transactions (for example, a subscription business, an e-commerce business selling many low-price items, a mobile app with many microtransactions) can exceed the 200-transaction threshold without exceeding $100,000 in revenue, and is still required to register.

The 200-transaction prong has been criticized as arbitrary and disproportionately burdensome for small sellers. Some states have eliminated their transaction-count thresholds in recent years in response to these criticisms. Washington, as of 2026, retains the 200-transaction prong.

### What happens when an out-of-state seller exceeds the threshold

An out-of-state seller that exceeds the economic nexus threshold must:

1. Register with Washington DOR via the Business License Application.
2. Begin collecting Washington retail sales tax on taxable sales to Washington customers.
3. Begin reporting and paying Washington B&O tax on Washington sales.
4. File Combined Excise Tax Returns on the assigned frequency.
5. Comply with Washington recordkeeping and audit requirements.

The obligation is prospective — it begins once the threshold is exceeded. However, if a seller exceeded the threshold without registering, DOR can assess back taxes, penalties, and interest for the period after the threshold was exceeded. The Voluntary Disclosure Program (section 28) is often the appropriate response for out-of-state sellers who discover belatedly that they had nexus.

## 46. Apportionment for multi-state businesses

For multi-state businesses, Washington uses a single-factor receipts apportionment formula. Unlike the three-factor formula (property, payroll, and sales) that was historically common in income-tax states, and unlike the equally-weighted or double-weighted sales formulas used in some other states, Washington uses *only* the sales factor. This means that a business's Washington B&O tax base is determined by the fraction of its total gross receipts that are sourced to Washington.

### Market-based sourcing for services (post-2010)

Prior to 2010, Washington sourced services to the state where the service-provider's costs of performance were incurred. This approach is sometimes called "cost-of-performance" sourcing and is still used in some states. Under cost-of-performance sourcing, a services business with most of its personnel in Washington would source most of its service receipts to Washington regardless of where its customers were located.

In 2010, Washington shifted to **market-based sourcing** for services. Under market-based sourcing, service receipts are sourced to the state where the customer receives the benefit of the service — typically the customer's location. This means that a Washington-based services firm serving out-of-state customers sources those receipts to the customers' states, not to Washington, reducing the Washington B&O base.

Conversely, an out-of-state services firm serving Washington customers sources those receipts to Washington. This is the reason why the post-*Wayfair* economic nexus regime has been particularly impactful for out-of-state services firms: they now source their Washington-customer receipts to Washington (under market-based sourcing) AND must register under the post-*Wayfair* nexus rules (based on exceeding the $100,000 threshold).

The 2024 update to WAC 458-20-19402 simplified several aspects of the apportionment rules, reducing ambiguity in how certain types of service receipts are sourced.

### Differences from income-tax states

Many income-tax states use a three-factor apportionment formula (property, payroll, sales) or a heavily sales-weighted variant. The three-factor formula is designed to apportion income based on where a business has its inputs and its customers, giving roughly equal weight to each.

Washington's single-factor receipts formula is simpler but has different incentive effects. Because only sales matter for apportionment, a business has no incentive (from a B&O tax perspective) to locate its employees or its physical property in Washington — only its sales to Washington customers matter. This is one reason why Washington has been able to maintain a significant high-revenue services sector without the property- and payroll-based incentives that would apply in three-factor states.

### B&O and federal income tax are not coordinated

Because the B&O tax is not an income tax, it does not integrate with the federal income tax in the way that state corporate income taxes do. There is no "separate corporate tax return" that connects to the federal Form 1120; the B&O tax is reported on the Combined Excise Tax Return, which is its own standalone return. This independence is a defining feature of Washington tax compliance and is one of the reasons that state and local tax (SALT) practitioners in Washington develop significant expertise distinct from federal tax practitioners.

## 47. Throwout and throwback

In apportionment formulas, "throwout" and "throwback" are rules that address sales to states where the seller has no nexus and therefore cannot be taxed by the destination state.

**Throwback**: Under a throwback rule, sales to destinations where the seller has no nexus are "thrown back" to the origin state, increasing the origin state's apportionment fraction. This ensures that the income from those sales is taxed *somewhere*.

**Throwout**: Under a throwout rule, sales to destinations where the seller has no nexus are simply removed ("thrown out") from the denominator of the apportionment fraction. This also increases the origin state's apportionment fraction (by reducing the denominator) but in a different mathematical way than throwback.

Washington generally does not apply throwback or throwout rules to its B&O tax. Because Washington's tax is a gross receipts tax on the privilege of doing business in Washington, the state's general position is that only receipts with a Washington nexus are taxable, and receipts attributable to states where the seller has no nexus are simply outside the Washington base. This is simpler than throwback/throwout rules but means that a business operating in multiple states with limited nexus may have some receipts that are not taxed in any state.

## 48. Credit for taxes paid to other states

Washington generally does not provide credits for business taxes paid in other states. The rationale is straightforward: Washington does not tax income, so there is no structural basis for a credit that would offset taxes paid on the same income to another jurisdiction. The B&O tax is conceptually a tax on the privilege of doing business in Washington, not on income, and therefore does not overlap with out-of-state income taxes in the same way that two income taxes would overlap.

There are limited exceptions. For the capital gains excise tax (2022+), credits may be available for capital gains taxes paid to other states on the same transaction, to prevent double taxation of the same gain. The mechanics of these credits are addressed in the capital gains tax statute and in DOR guidance.

For most business taxpayers, however, the absence of cross-state tax credits is a planning consideration: a multi-state business may face both a Washington B&O tax liability and an income tax liability in other states on what is economically the same income stream, without the ability to offset one against the other. Effective multi-state tax planning for Washington-based businesses therefore focuses on sourcing and apportionment choices rather than on credits.

## 49. Multi-state voluntary disclosure

The Multistate Tax Commission (MTC) operates a national voluntary disclosure program that allows out-of-state sellers with potential nexus liability in multiple states to come into compliance through a single coordinated process. Washington participates in the MTC voluntary disclosure program, which is particularly useful for remote sellers who discover post-*Wayfair* that they have nexus in multiple states.

The MTC voluntary disclosure program provides:

- **Coordinated filing**: The taxpayer submits a single anonymous application to the MTC, which then coordinates with participating states.
- **Limited lookback**: Most participating states, including Washington, limit the lookback to a specified number of years (typically three or four).
- **Penalty relief**: Participating states typically waive late-filing and unregistered-business penalties.
- **Confidentiality**: The taxpayer's identity is not revealed to participating states until the agreements are finalized.

For out-of-state sellers with Washington nexus exposure and similar exposure in other states, the MTC program is often more efficient than filing separate voluntary disclosure requests with each state.

# Part 7 — Special Topics

## 50. Nonprofit taxation

Washington does not exempt 501(c)(3) nonprofits from B&O tax. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood features of Washington tax law. Nonprofits that are exempt from federal income tax under Section 501(c)(3) are not automatically exempt from Washington B&O tax.

### What nonprofits do pay

Most nonprofits operating in Washington pay B&O tax on:

- **Unrelated business income** — Income from activities unrelated to the nonprofit's exempt purpose is subject to B&O tax in most cases.
- **Program service revenue** — Income from activities that *are* related to the exempt purpose is also subject to B&O tax in most cases, unless a specific statutory exemption applies. This is a major departure from federal tax treatment, where program service revenue is generally not subject to federal income tax.
- **Retail sales** — If a nonprofit sells taxable goods (for example, through a gift shop or a fundraising sale), it must collect and remit sales tax.
- **Use tax** — Nonprofits must pay use tax on purchases where sales tax was not collected.

### Specific nonprofit exemptions

A limited set of specific statutory exemptions covers particular categories of nonprofit activity:

- Certain fundraising activities (by volunteer, with specific limits).
- Certain charity auctions.
- Certain church activities.
- Certain health-care-related activities by nonprofit hospitals.
- Certain educational activities by accredited educational institutions.

Each exemption is narrow and specific. Nonprofits should not assume they are exempt from B&O tax unless they can identify a specific statutory provision that applies.

### Nonprofit hospitals

Nonprofit hospitals in Washington face a complex and controversial tax treatment. They are generally subject to B&O tax on most patient care revenue, with limited exemptions for certain charity care and for government-funded care. The interaction between nonprofit status, B&O tax, and the Washington Health Access Program (and its predecessors) has been the subject of extensive litigation and legislative revision.

### Practical takeaway

A nonprofit entering Washington should assume it will owe B&O tax on most of its Washington-source revenue and should plan accordingly. The nonprofit should still register with DOR, file Combined Excise Tax Returns, and pay the applicable taxes. A careful analysis by Washington tax counsel may identify specific exemptions that apply, but the default position is that B&O tax applies.

## 51. Tribal tax issues

Washington's 29 federally recognized tribes exercise significant sovereignty over taxation within their reservations. The intersection of state tax law and tribal sovereignty is complex and is governed by a mixture of federal Indian law, state-tribal compacts, and specific statutory provisions.

### General principles

- **Tribal members on reservation**: Washington generally cannot impose state taxes (including sales tax) on transactions between tribes or tribal members conducted on tribal land. The U.S. Supreme Court has consistently held that states have very limited authority to tax tribal activities on tribal land.
- **Non-tribal members on reservation**: Washington's authority to tax non-tribal individuals or businesses conducting activities on tribal land depends on several factors, including whether the tribe has an agreement with the state, whether the state tax would interfere with tribal self-government, and whether specific federal preemption applies.
- **Off-reservation**: Washington tax applies normally to off-reservation transactions, regardless of tribal status of the parties.

### State-tribal tax agreements

Washington has entered into tax agreements with many (though not all) tribes. These agreements address:

- Retail sales tax on reservation sales, particularly at tribal gas stations and smoke shops.
- Cigarette tax collection.
- Motor vehicle fuel tax.
- Other specific tax categories.

The agreements typically provide for some form of tax collection and revenue sharing between the tribe and the state, structured in a way that respects tribal sovereignty while preventing significant tax avoidance through reservation-based transactions.

### Cannabis taxation on tribal lands

The intersection of state cannabis taxation (a significant revenue source) and tribal sovereignty over cannabis is an active area of legal and regulatory development. Some tribes have chosen to participate in the state cannabis regulatory and tax system; others have asserted independent cannabis operations not subject to state tax.

### Practical takeaway

Tribal tax issues are specialized and should be handled by counsel with specific experience. A business doing work with or on tribal lands needs to understand the applicable state-tribal compact (if any) and the specific federal law that applies. General Washington tax rules cannot safely be applied without adjustment to tribal contexts.

## 52. The Real Estate Excise Tax (REET)

The Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) is Washington's transfer tax on real property sales. It is imposed on the seller at the time of a real estate transaction and is paid to the county treasurer (for both state and local components), who then remits the state portion to DOR.

### State REET rate structure (graduated, 2020+)

Prior to 2020, Washington imposed a flat state REET rate of 1.28 percent. Effective January 1, 2020, Washington adopted a graduated state REET rate structure for most real estate transactions:

| Sale Price Bracket | State REET Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 - $525,000 | 1.10% |
| $525,001 - $1,525,000 | 1.28% |
| $1,525,001 - $3,025,000 | 2.75% |
| Above $3,025,000 | 3.00% |

The brackets are marginal, meaning that each portion of the sale price is taxed at the applicable bracket rate. For example, a $1,000,000 sale is taxed at 1.10 percent on the first $525,000 and 1.28 percent on the remaining $475,000.

### Exclusions from the graduated rate

Certain categories of property remain at a flat 1.28 percent state REET rate regardless of sale price:

- Agricultural land.
- Timberland.
- Certain timber interests.

These exclusions were included in the graduated-rate legislation to protect agricultural and forestry property from the higher rates that would apply under the graduated structure.

### Local REET

In addition to the state REET, local jurisdictions (primarily cities and counties) can impose local REET of up to 0.50 percent (in some cases 0.25 percent + 0.25 percent, depending on the jurisdiction and authorization). The combined state and local REET rate for most urban Washington sales therefore ranges from approximately 1.60 percent (for small transactions in jurisdictions with minimum local REET) to 3.50 percent (for large transactions in jurisdictions with maximum local REET and at the top bracket of the state rate).

### Administration

REET is paid by the seller at the time of closing. The county treasurer collects REET as part of the recording process for the deed: the deed cannot be recorded until REET is paid or a valid exemption is claimed. This front-end collection structure makes REET one of the most reliably collected state taxes — it is essentially impossible to sell Washington real property without paying REET or claiming an exemption.

### Exemptions

REET includes a long list of exemptions for specific transaction types: gifts, inheritances, foreclosures, tax deed sales, certain transfers to government, and others. Each exemption has specific documentation requirements, and claiming an exemption without proper documentation can result in the seller being assessed REET plus penalties later.

### 2025 REET reform

The 2025 legislative session produced some REET-related reforms, particularly around agricultural land and certain local REET provisions. Practitioners involved in real estate transactions should consult the 2025 updates to DOR's REET guidance.

## 53. Property tax administration

Washington's property tax is administered at the county level. Each of Washington's 39 counties has an assessor's office responsible for valuing property and a treasurer's office responsible for collecting the tax. The state provides coordination and oversight through DOR's Property Tax Division.

### Key features of Washington property tax

- **Annual assessment**: Most property is assessed annually, with assessed values updated each year to reflect current market conditions.
- **Assessed value**: Generally, Washington assessed values should approximate 100 percent of market value (true and fair value). This is the constitutional standard under Article VII.
- **Levy rate structure**: The total property tax bill is composed of multiple levies — state school levy, county general, city general, school district, fire district, port district, library district, and others depending on location. Each levy is computed as a rate (dollars per $1,000 of assessed value) and applied to the assessed value.
- **Exemptions**: Various exemptions apply, including the senior citizen and disabled person property tax exemption (RCW 84.36.381 et seq.), which can significantly reduce property tax for qualifying low-income seniors and disabled individuals.

### Initiative 747 and the 1% cap

As discussed in Part 1, Initiative 747 (2001) and its 2007 statutory re-enactment capped the year-over-year growth of regular property tax levies at 1 percent (plus new construction). This cap applies at the level of total dollars collected by a jurisdiction, not at the level of individual property tax bills. An individual property's tax bill can grow by more than 1 percent if its assessed value has increased faster than the average for the jurisdiction, but the total dollars collected by the jurisdiction cannot grow by more than 1 percent without voter-approved excess levy authority.

### School funding and the McCleary decision

The interaction between local property tax caps and school funding has been a central issue in Washington fiscal policy throughout the 2010s. In *McCleary v. State*, the Washington Supreme Court held that the state had failed to adequately fund basic education under Article IX of the state constitution, in part because over-reliance on local property tax levies had created unequal funding across school districts. The response, worked out over several legislative sessions, involved increasing the state property tax rate (the state school levy) while capping local school district levies. The net effect was to shift a larger share of school funding to the state level, where it could be equalized across districts, while preserving the overall property tax structure.

### Appeals

Property tax assessments can be appealed to the county board of equalization, and thence to the Washington Board of Tax Appeals. Property tax appeals are a significant volume of the BTA's workload (alongside DOR determination appeals).

## 54. The Washington estate tax

Washington is one of a small number of states with a state estate tax. The estate tax has been the subject of significant legislative change in 2025.

### Pre-July 1, 2025 rules

Under the rules in effect through June 30, 2025:

- **Exemption**: $2.193 million per decedent.
- **Rate structure**: Graduated from 10 percent to 20 percent, depending on the size of the taxable estate above the exemption.
- **Tax base**: Washington-situs property of Washington residents, plus Washington real property of non-residents.

### Post-July 1, 2025 rules (SB 5813)

Effective July 1, 2025:

- **Exemption**: Increased to $3 million per decedent, and indexed for inflation going forward. This increase significantly reduces the number of Washington estates subject to the tax.
- **Rate structure**: Steeper graduation, with a new top rate of 35 percent on the portion of the estate above $9 million. The intermediate rates are higher than the pre-2025 structure.
- **Net effect**: Smaller estates (those between the old $2.193 million and the new $3 million threshold) are now exempt. Mid-size estates (between $3 million and approximately $9 million) may pay somewhat more or less than before, depending on the specific estate size. Large estates (above $9 million) pay substantially more.

### Relevance for founders and investors

The Washington estate tax is a significant planning consideration for Washington residents with substantial net worth. Because the tax applies to worldwide assets of Washington residents (with some credits for property taxed by other jurisdictions), a Washington resident with a diversified investment portfolio, real estate holdings, and business interests can face a significant Washington estate tax even if most of the wealth is held outside Washington.

Common planning techniques include:

- Use of the gift tax exemption during life to reduce the taxable estate.
- Use of various trust structures (QTIP trusts, grantor trusts, dynasty trusts) to manage the transfer of wealth.
- Consideration of domicile changes for very high-net-worth individuals who are willing to relocate to no-estate-tax states.

The domicile planning consideration has become more pointed after the 2025 rate increases. A resident whose estate is likely to exceed $9 million may find significant savings from relocating to Florida, Texas, or another no-estate-tax state. The practical feasibility of domicile change depends on many factors beyond taxes, but the 2025 changes have made the analysis more important.

### Interaction with the federal estate tax

The federal estate tax exemption is significantly higher than the Washington exemption (approximately $13.6 million per decedent for 2024, scheduled to revert to approximately $6.8 million in 2026 unless Congress acts). For many Washington estates, the Washington estate tax applies even though no federal estate tax is owed, because the Washington exemption is much lower. This gap between state and federal exemptions is the source of most Washington estate tax liability.

# Part 8 — Comparative and Strategic Context

## 55. Why gross receipts taxation produces unique incentives

Washington's B&O tax — a gross receipts tax — has distributional and incentive effects that are different from those of net income taxes and value-added taxes. Understanding these effects is essential to understanding both the history of Washington tax policy and the strategic considerations that shape business location decisions in the state.

### High-margin businesses vs. low-margin businesses

A gross receipts tax at a fixed rate (say, 1.5 percent of revenue) takes a *fixed percentage of revenue* regardless of the business's profit margin. Consider two businesses:

- **Business A** has $10 million in revenue and a 20 percent net margin ($2 million in profit). Its B&O tax is $150,000 (1.5 percent of $10 million), which is 7.5 percent of its profit.
- **Business B** has $10 million in revenue and a 2 percent net margin ($200,000 in profit). Its B&O tax is also $150,000, which is *75 percent* of its profit.

The B&O tax takes a much larger share of profit from the low-margin business. In extreme cases, the B&O tax can exceed the business's net profit entirely, meaning the business is effectively taxed into a loss.

### Implications for industry mix

This feature of gross receipts taxation has predictable effects on Washington's industry mix over time:

- **High-margin businesses thrive**: Technology, software, intellectual property, professional services with premium pricing, and other high-margin businesses are well-suited to Washington's tax environment. A software firm with 40 percent net margins can easily absorb a 1.5 percent (now 2.1 percent for large services firms) B&O tax.
- **Low-margin businesses struggle**: Distribution, logistics, grocery, food service, and other thin-margin businesses face a difficult tax environment. Grocery stores in particular are often cited as structurally disadvantaged by the Washington tax system, operating on 1-2 percent net margins while facing a 0.471 percent retailing B&O rate that can represent 25-50 percent of their profit.

Over decades, this incentive structure has contributed to Washington's development as a high-margin, high-value-added economy concentrated in software, aerospace, life sciences, and other knowledge-intensive sectors. Lower-margin activities, while not absent, tend to locate elsewhere or operate with tight geographic concentration.

### The pyramiding problem

A second distinctive feature of gross receipts taxation is "pyramiding": because the B&O tax applies at each stage of production and distribution without any offset for tax paid at previous stages, a product that passes through multiple Washington businesses before reaching the final consumer can effectively be taxed multiple times. A Washington manufacturer pays manufacturing B&O on sales to a Washington wholesaler, who pays wholesaling B&O on sales to a Washington retailer, who pays retailing B&O on sales to the final consumer. Each stage pays B&O on the *full* value at that stage, not just on the value added.

Pyramiding is a well-known problem with gross receipts taxes. Value-added taxes (used in most developed economies outside the U.S.) avoid pyramiding by providing a credit for input tax at each stage. Washington's B&O tax, by contrast, does not provide input tax credits, so pyramiding is structural.

In practice, pyramiding is partially mitigated by:

- **Lower rates for intermediate transactions**: The wholesaling B&O rate (0.484 percent) is lower than the retailing rate (0.471 percent), which is lower than most service rates. This reduces but does not eliminate pyramiding.
- **Single-stage structures**: Many businesses are vertically integrated or source inputs from out-of-state suppliers, reducing the number of Washington taxable stages.
- **Exemptions**: Some intermediate transactions are specifically exempt to prevent pyramiding in particular industries.

Despite these mitigations, pyramiding remains a significant feature of the Washington tax system and is one of the reasons Washington B&O tax rates appear low in international comparison even as the effective burden on Washington businesses can be substantial.

## 56. The regressivity debate

The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) publishes a "Who Pays?" report that ranks state and local tax systems by their distributional effects. The most recent editions of Who Pays? have consistently ranked Washington as one of the most regressive state and local tax systems in the country. In the 6th edition (2018), Washington was ranked as the single most regressive state. In the 7th edition (2024), Washington moved to the 2nd most regressive state, behind only Florida, following the implementation of the Working Families Tax Credit and the capital gains excise tax.

### ITEP's findings for Washington

According to ITEP's analysis:

- The poorest 20 percent of Washington families pay approximately 13.8 percent of their income in state and local taxes.
- The middle 20 percent pay approximately 10.9 percent.
- The wealthiest 1 percent pay approximately 4.1 percent.

The ratio between the bottom-quintile effective tax rate (13.8 percent) and the top-1-percent rate (4.1 percent) is approximately 3.4:1, meaning low-income Washingtonians pay more than three times as much of their income in state and local taxes as the wealthiest Washingtonians. This is the definition of a steeply regressive tax system.

### Why Washington is regressive

The regressivity of Washington's tax system is structural and has three main components:

1. **Heavy reliance on sales tax**: The retail sales tax is a regressive tax because low-income households spend a much higher share of their income on taxable consumption than high-income households do. Washington's state and local sales tax rates are among the highest in the nation (typically 9-10 percent in urban areas), and sales tax is a dominant revenue source.

2. **No income tax**: Income taxes are typically the most progressive element of state tax systems. Because Washington has no personal income tax, it lacks the progressive element that offsets regressive sales and property taxes in most other states.

3. **B&O tax pass-through**: To the extent the B&O tax is passed through to consumers as higher prices, it functions as an additional layer of consumption taxation, adding to the regressivity of the system.

### Counterarguments and limitations

Defenders of the Washington system make several counterarguments:

- **No income tax advantage**: The absence of a state income tax benefits all Washingtonians, including lower-income ones who would otherwise pay a lower-but-positive state income tax. The direct comparison with income-tax states is therefore not straightforward.
- **Tax incidence is uncertain**: The true distributional incidence of the B&O tax depends on how much is passed through to consumers, how much is absorbed by owners, and how much is shifted to workers. These incidence assumptions vary across studies.
- **Transfer programs offset regressivity**: Washington has relatively generous transfer programs for low-income residents (basic health, food assistance, housing assistance), which offset some of the tax system's regressivity in terms of net fiscal incidence.
- **The Working Families Tax Credit**: Beginning 2023, the Working Families Tax Credit provides cash refunds to low-income households that partially offset sales tax burden. Post-WFTC, Washington's tax system is somewhat less regressive than before, though still highly regressive.

### The regressivity argument as political fuel

The ITEP rankings and the broader regressivity argument have been politically important in Washington. They provided the moral framing for the capital gains excise tax (2021), the Working Families Tax Credit, the Seattle JumpStart payroll tax, and ultimately the 2025 reform package. Progressive advocates consistently cite the ITEP rankings to argue that the current system is unfair and that progressive reform is necessary. Business and tax-limit advocates generally contest the ITEP methodology or argue that the benefits of no income tax outweigh the regressivity costs.

This debate is not going to be settled on purely empirical grounds. It involves fundamental disagreements about:

- The appropriate measure of "fairness" in taxation (ability-to-pay vs. benefit principle vs. flat proportional taxation).
- The correct treatment of tax incidence and pass-through effects.
- The proper comparison between states with and without income taxes.
- The weight given to transfer programs versus tax structure in assessing net fiscal fairness.

What is clear is that the regressivity argument has become a significant political force in Washington and has driven much of the recent progressive tax reform.

## 57. The 2025 reforms as a progressive response

The 2025 tax reforms (HB 2081, SB 5813, SB 5814) collectively represent the most significant progressive tax shift in Washington history. Their net effect is to move the tax burden — at the margin — from lower- and middle-income households toward large businesses and very wealthy individuals.

### HB 2081: Targeting high-revenue services firms and very large businesses

The service B&O rate increase from 1.75 percent to 2.1 percent applies only to services firms with more than $5 million in annual gross receipts. This is a fairly high threshold that excludes small and medium services firms. The 0.5 percent surcharge on Washington-apportioned gross income above $250 million per year applies only to the very largest businesses in Washington — perhaps a few dozen firms. Both changes concentrate the additional B&O burden on large and very large taxpayers.

The investment income deduction clarification (the *Antio* response) is less obviously progressive, but its distributional effect is progressive in practice because investment income is concentrated in high-wealth households and entities. The narrowing of the deduction effectively increases the tax burden on investment-income-heavy entities, which are disproportionately owned by wealthy individuals.

### SB 5813: Targeting very large capital gains and very large estates

The increase in the capital gains excise tax rate from 7 percent to 9.9 percent applies only to gains above $1 million per year. Gains between the $250,000 exclusion and $1 million continue to be taxed at 7 percent. The higher rate therefore falls only on very large capital gains transactions, which are concentrated among a small number of very wealthy taxpayers.

The estate tax changes — exemption increased to $3 million, top rate raised to 35 percent on estates above $9 million — have the same pattern: smaller estates are protected or benefit, larger estates face a higher burden.

### SB 5814: Broadening the sales tax base to services

The sales tax base expansion is not straightforwardly progressive. Sales tax is generally regressive, because lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on taxable consumption. Extending sales tax to certain services might therefore seem to add to regressivity.

However, the specific services covered by SB 5814 — advertising services, certain IT services, temporary staffing, certain custom software, live presentations, and others — are services consumed predominantly by businesses and by higher-income households. The incidence of sales tax on these services is therefore less regressive than sales tax on, say, groceries or clothing. The extent to which the services expansion is regressive depends on the specific pattern of service consumption, and reasonable economists disagree about the net effect.

### Political positioning

Progressive advocates presented the 2025 reforms as a long-overdue rebalancing of Washington's tax burden, moving away from the regressive structure that had persisted since 1935. Business advocates presented the reforms as a significant increase in the cost of doing business in Washington and predicted negative effects on competitiveness. The truth — insofar as it can be assessed empirically — is somewhere in between.

What is clear is that the 2025 reforms significantly shift the center of gravity of Washington's tax system. They do not abolish the Revenue Act of 1935 architecture (B&O tax, sales tax, and related excises), but they graft onto that architecture a set of progressive features (capital gains tax, estate tax at higher rates, surcharge on very large businesses) that make the system less regressive than it was before.

### The long-term trajectory

Looking forward, the 2025 reforms suggest a trajectory in which Washington's tax system will continue to evolve incrementally toward greater progressivity without breaking the *Culliton* doctrine. Further reforms may include:

- Additional excise-style taxes on wealth or high incomes, structured carefully to avoid *Culliton* challenges.
- Further expansion of the sales tax base to services, on the theory that the services-heavy modern economy should bear a proportional share of the sales tax burden.
- Continued tightening of the investment income deduction and other longstanding exclusions that historically benefited wealth-holders.
- Possible reforms to the B&O tax structure to reduce pyramiding and address the high-margin-vs-low-margin distortion.

Whether these changes come as a single comprehensive reform or as a series of incremental adjustments remains to be seen. The 2025 package demonstrates that the political coalition for progressive tax reform in Washington is real and capable of enacting significant legislation. Whether it can sustain a second major reform in the next few years — particularly if economic conditions change — is an open question.

## 58. Concluding observations

Washington's tax system is the product of nine decades of political, legal, and economic evolution from the Revenue Act of 1935. The structural features of the system — B&O tax, sales tax, no income tax, complex classification system, layered state-local structure — were established in 1935 in response to a fiscal emergency during the Great Depression, under the constraint imposed by *Culliton v. Chase*.

Over the decades, the system has proven both durable and adaptable. It is durable in that the fundamental architecture has never been replaced: every Washington taxpayer in 2026 files essentially the same kinds of returns (adapted forms of the same 1935 excise taxes) that taxpayers filed in 1936. It is adaptable in that the legislature has repeatedly modified the system to address specific needs — adding classifications for new industries, creating incentives to attract particular investments, tightening or loosening particular exemptions, and most recently (in 2025) adding progressive features through careful legal engineering around the *Culliton* constraint.

For the practitioner, the Washington tax system requires a distinctive combination of skills: familiarity with gross receipts taxation (unusual in the U.S. context), deep knowledge of the classification system and industry guides, understanding of the post-*Wayfair* nexus rules and market-based sourcing, and comfort with the administrative structure (DOR, ARHD, BTA, and the interaction with city tax authorities). The compliance burden is real and the penalty structure is unforgiving, particularly the graduated 9/19/29 percent late payment penalty schedule that quickly compounds on unresolved liabilities.

For the historian and policy analyst, the Washington tax system illustrates how contingent and path-dependent state tax policy can be. The 5-4 *Culliton* decision in 1933 — a close call that could easily have gone the other way — fundamentally shaped nine decades of Washington fiscal history. The Revenue Act of 1935 was a crisis response that became permanent. The anti-income-tax consensus of the initiative era was sustained by political conditions that only began to change in the late 2010s. The 2025 reforms would have been unthinkable in 2015 and may themselves look like a transitional moment in retrospect.

For the business community, the 2025 reforms represent a significant change in the cost of doing business in Washington. Large services firms, businesses with more than $250 million in Washington receipts, and businesses whose owners face substantial capital gains and estate tax exposure all need to reassess their Washington tax planning in light of the new rules. At the same time, the fundamentals of the Washington business environment — the absence of an income tax, the generally supportive attitude toward technology and innovation, the strong base of aerospace, software, and life sciences clusters — remain in place. Whether the 2025 changes will materially alter business location decisions or whether they will be absorbed as one more adjustment to a complex but familiar system remains to be seen.

What is certain is that Washington's tax system will continue to evolve. The Revenue Act of 1935 has lasted longer than most of its drafters could have imagined. Whether it will last another nine decades — or whether the pressures of economic change, political realignment, and legal innovation will eventually break its basic architecture — is a question that will be answered by the next several decades of Washington political and economic history.
