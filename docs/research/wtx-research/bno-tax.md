# Part 1: The Gross Receipts Tax Model — Historical and Comparative Context

## 1.1 Washington's Tax Architecture

### 1.1.1 The No-Income-Tax States

Washington State occupies a distinctive position in the American fiscal landscape. It is one of only nine states that impose no broad-based personal income tax on their residents. This fact, more than almost any other feature of the state's governance, shapes the entire structure of Washington's public finance — from how its schools are funded to how its highways are maintained to how its social safety net is constructed.

The nine states that forgo a broad-based personal income tax are:

| State | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **Alaska** | No state income tax; no state sales tax. Relies on oil revenue (Permanent Fund). |
| **Florida** | No state income tax. Heavy reliance on sales tax (6%) and tourism revenue. |
| **Nevada** | No state income tax. Gaming taxes, Commerce Tax (gross receipts, 2015). |
| **New Hampshire** | No tax on earned wages. Historically taxed interest and dividends (repealed 2025). |
| **South Dakota** | No state income tax. Sales tax (4.5%) and property taxes. |
| **Tennessee** | No tax on earned wages. Hall Income Tax on investment income repealed 2021. |
| **Texas** | No state income tax. Franchise Tax (modified gross receipts), sales tax (6.25%). |
| **Washington** | No state income tax. B&O tax (gross receipts), sales tax (6.5% state). |
| **Wyoming** | No state income tax. Mineral severance taxes, property taxes. |

This list deserves careful parsing because the phrase "no income tax" is deceptively simple. What it means in practice varies significantly across these nine states. Alaska has neither an income tax nor a general sales tax — it funds state government primarily through oil extraction revenues deposited in the Alaska Permanent Fund, and it actually pays residents an annual dividend from that fund's investment earnings. Florida and Texas rely heavily on sales taxes. Nevada leans on gaming revenue and, since 2015, a Commerce Tax that functions as a gross receipts levy. New Hampshire and Tennessee historically occupied a middle ground — they did not tax earned wages but did tax certain investment income, though both have now fully repealed those investment income taxes (New Hampshire's interest and dividends tax was phased out entirely as of January 1, 2025; Tennessee's Hall Income Tax was repealed effective January 1, 2021).

Washington's approach is arguably the most complex and, in the eyes of many tax scholars, the most economically significant of the group. Washington does not merely forgo an income tax and replace it with a higher sales tax. Instead, Washington has constructed an entire parallel revenue architecture built around an unusually comprehensive gross receipts tax — the Business and Occupation (B&O) tax — that applies to virtually every form of commercial activity conducted within or directed at the state.

### 1.1.2 The Three Pillars of Washington Revenue

Washington's state revenue system rests on three principal pillars:

1. **The Retail Sales and Use Tax** — A 6.5% state-level tax on retail sales of tangible personal property and certain services, supplemented by local option taxes that can bring the combined rate to over 10% in some jurisdictions (the highest combined rate in King County, encompassing Seattle, reached 10.25% as of 2025). The sales tax is Washington's single largest revenue source, generating approximately $14 billion annually for the state general fund.

2. **The Business and Occupation (B&O) Tax** — A gross receipts excise tax imposed on the privilege of engaging in business activities within Washington State. The B&O tax is the subject of this study. It generates approximately $5–6 billion annually for the state general fund (a figure that will increase substantially under HB 2081).

3. **Property Taxes** — Administered primarily at the county level, property taxes fund local government services, school districts, and special purpose districts. The state also levies a state property tax for public schools.

Together, these three pillars — sales tax, B&O tax, and property tax — constitute the vast majority of Washington's state and local tax revenue. What is conspicuously absent is any levy measured by net income. There is no corporate income tax. There is no personal income tax. There is no franchise tax measured by net worth (though Washington did enact a capital gains excise tax in 2021, upheld by the Washington Supreme Court in *Quinn v. State* in 2023 as an excise tax on the privilege of selling capital assets, not a tax on income or property).

### 1.1.3 The Consequences of the No-Income-Tax Architecture

The absence of an income tax has profound consequences for Washington's fiscal system, its economy, and its politics:

**Revenue composition.** Without income tax revenue — which typically constitutes 30–50% of state general fund revenue in income-tax states — Washington must generate equivalent revenue through other channels. This places enormous pressure on the sales tax and the B&O tax to fund the full scope of state government operations. The result is that Washington's combined state and local sales tax rates rank among the highest in the nation, and the B&O tax applies at rates and across a breadth of activities that have no close parallel in most other states.

**Economic migration effects.** Washington's no-income-tax status has been a significant factor in attracting both high-income individuals and large corporations to the state. The Puget Sound region's emergence as a global technology hub was accelerated by the absence of a state income tax, which made the area financially attractive to highly compensated technology workers who would face significant state income tax liabilities in California (top marginal rate of 13.3%), New York (top marginal rate of 10.9%), or Oregon (top marginal rate of 9.9%). The presence of major employers including technology, aerospace, and retail companies in the Puget Sound corridor is not solely attributable to the tax structure — the University of Washington's research output, the region's quality of life, and the historical presence of the aerospace industry all played roles — but the absence of an income tax was and remains a material factor in location decisions.

**Distributional effects.** Tax systems that rely on sales taxes and gross receipts taxes rather than income taxes tend to be regressive — that is, they impose a proportionally higher burden on lower-income households than on higher-income households. This is because lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on taxable consumption. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) has consistently ranked Washington's tax system among the most regressive in the nation. In ITEP's 2024 "Who Pays?" report, Washington ranked as the state with the most regressive tax system in the country, with the lowest-income quintile paying an effective state and local tax rate of approximately 17.8% of family income, while the top 1% of earners paid approximately 4.2%. This distributional reality has fueled decades of political debate over whether Washington should adopt an income tax.

**Political durability.** Despite these distributional concerns, the no-income-tax structure has proven remarkably durable politically. Multiple ballot initiatives proposing a state income tax have failed, most recently Initiative 1098 in 2010 (which would have imposed an income tax on high earners) and Initiative 1929 in 2024. The reasons for this political durability are complex — they include distrust of government spending, fear that an income tax initially limited to high earners would eventually be broadened, the political influence of businesses and high-income residents who benefit from the current system, and a cultural identity around Washington being a "no income tax" state that has become self-reinforcing.

### 1.1.4 The Capital Gains Excise Tax: A Potential Crack in the Foundation

In 2021, the Washington Legislature enacted a 7% excise tax on the sale or exchange of long-term capital assets (stocks, bonds, and other investments, but not real estate) by Washington residents realizing gains exceeding $250,000. This levy was immediately challenged as an unconstitutional income tax.

In *Quinn v. State of Washington* (2023), the Washington Supreme Court upheld the tax in a 7-2 decision, characterizing it as an excise tax on the "privilege" of selling capital assets rather than a tax on income. The majority opinion relied heavily on the legal fiction that the tax was imposed on the transaction (the sale) rather than on the gain (the income), thereby placing it in the same constitutional category as the B&O tax and the sales tax — excise taxes on activities — rather than in the prohibited category of taxes on property (which, under *Culliton v. Chase*, includes income).

The U.S. Supreme Court declined to review the case, denying certiorari in January 2024. The capital gains excise tax therefore stands.

In 2025, as part of the HB 2081 / SB 5813 legislative package, the capital gains excise tax was restructured into a progressive rate:

| Capital Gains Amount | Rate |
|---------------------|------|
| Up to $1,000,000 | 7.0% |
| Exceeding $1,000,000 | 9.9% |

This progressive structure — a higher rate on higher gains — represents a significant step toward graduated taxation in Washington, even though it is officially characterized as an excise tax. Many observers view the *Quinn* decision as having cracked the constitutional barrier that *Culliton v. Chase* erected in 1933. If an excise tax can be graduated based on the amount of gain, the logical distance between that and a graduated income tax (characterized as an excise tax on the privilege of earning income) may not be as vast as the 90-year-old constitutional prohibition once seemed.

## 1.2 The Gross Receipts Tax Family

### 1.2.1 What Is a Gross Receipts Tax?

A gross receipts tax is a levy imposed on the total revenue (gross receipts) of a business, without any deduction for costs of goods sold, labor costs, rent, interest, or other business expenses. The tax base is the entire top line of a company's income statement — every dollar that comes in the door — rather than the bottom line (net income after expenses).

This is the defining characteristic that distinguishes gross receipts taxes from income taxes and makes them simultaneously simpler to administer and more controversial among economists. An income tax taxes profit — what is left after the costs of doing business are subtracted. A gross receipts tax taxes revenue — the total volume of economic activity, regardless of whether that activity is profitable.

To illustrate the practical significance of this distinction, consider two hypothetical service businesses, each generating $2,000,000 in annual revenue:

| Metric | Business A (High Margin) | Business B (Low Margin) |
|--------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Annual Revenue | $2,000,000 | $2,000,000 |
| Cost of Goods Sold | $200,000 | $1,400,000 |
| Operating Expenses | $600,000 | $400,000 |
| Net Income (Profit) | $1,200,000 | $200,000 |
| Profit Margin | 60% | 10% |
| B&O Tax at 1.75% (Tier 2) | $35,000 | $35,000 |
| B&O as % of Revenue | 1.75% | 1.75% |
| **B&O as % of Net Income** | **2.9%** | **17.5%** |

Both businesses pay exactly the same B&O tax ($35,000), because they have the same gross receipts. But the low-margin business (Business B) is paying an effective rate of 17.5% of its net income, while the high-margin business (Business A) is paying an effective rate of only 2.9% of its net income. This disparity is the fundamental structural critique of gross receipts taxation and the root of the "pyramiding" problem discussed in Part 8.

### 1.2.2 The Gross Receipts Tax Family: A Survey

Washington's B&O tax is not unique — it belongs to a family of state-level gross receipts taxes that have existed in various forms throughout American history. The following table surveys the current landscape:

| State | Tax Name | Enacted | Current Rate(s) | Threshold | Notes |
|-------|----------|---------|-----------------|-----------|-------|
| **Washington** | Business & Occupation Tax | 1933 | 0.471%–2.1% (varies by classification) | ~$28,000/year (small business credit) | Oldest continuously operating GRT. No deductions. |
| **Ohio** | Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) | 2005 | 0.26% | $6,000,000 (2025) | Replaced tangible personal property tax. Phasing out for small businesses. |
| **Oregon** | Corporate Activity Tax (CAT) | 2019 | 0.57% + $250 flat | $1,000,000 | Allows 35% subtraction for cost of goods sold or labor. |
| **Delaware** | Gross Receipts Tax | 1917 | 0.0945%–1.9914% (varies by industry) | $100,000/month exclusion (varies) | Among the oldest. No general sales tax in Delaware. |
| **Nevada** | Commerce Tax | 2015 | 0.051%–0.331% (26 industry categories) | $4,000,000 | Industry classification based on NAICS codes. |
| **Texas** | Franchise Tax (Margin Tax) | 2006 (restructured) | 0.375%–0.75% | $2,470,000 (2024) | Modified GRT — allows some cost deductions. Not a pure gross receipts tax. |
| **Hawaii** | General Excise Tax (GET) | 1935 | 0.5%–4.0% (varies by activity) | None (all businesses) | Functions as both a GRT and a quasi-sales tax. Visibly passed through to consumers. |

Several historical gross receipts taxes deserve mention as well, because their enactment and subsequent repeal illuminate the economic and political dynamics of this tax form:

**Michigan Single Business Tax (SBT), 1975–2007.** Michigan enacted the SBT in 1975 as a value-added tax (technically a subtraction-method VAT rather than a pure gross receipts tax, though it shared characteristics). The SBT was replaced in 2007 by the Michigan Business Tax (MBT), which imposed both a 4.95% business income tax and a 0.8% modified gross receipts tax. The MBT was itself replaced in 2012 by a 6% corporate income tax. Michigan's journey — from a modified gross receipts tax to a hybrid, and finally back to a conventional corporate income tax — illustrates the political difficulty of sustaining gross receipts-style taxation in the face of sustained business opposition.

**Kentucky Gross Receipts Tax, 2005–2006.** Kentucky briefly enacted a gross receipts tax as part of a comprehensive tax reform package in 2005, but repealed it barely a year later due to intense opposition from the business community. The tax lasted a single fiscal year.

**New Jersey Business Tax, 2002–2006.** The "Alternative Minimum Assessment" imposed by New Jersey functioned as a gross receipts floor under the corporate income tax for businesses with receipts above $2 million. It was repealed effective for tax years beginning after 2006.

### 1.2.3 Hawaii's General Excise Tax: The Closest Structural Analog

Hawaii's General Excise Tax (GET) deserves extended discussion because it is structurally the closest analog to Washington's B&O tax among currently operating state taxes. Both were enacted during the Great Depression (Hawaii's GET in 1935, the same year as Washington's Revenue Act), both are comprehensive gross receipts taxes with no deduction for business expenses, and both play outsized roles in their states' revenue systems.

**Rate structure.** Hawaii's GET applies at four primary rates:

| Activity | GET Rate |
|----------|----------|
| Wholesaling | 0.5% |
| Manufacturing | 0.5% |
| Insurance commissions | 0.15% |
| All other activities (including retail and services) | 4.0% |

Additionally, Honolulu County imposes a 0.5% surcharge on all GET-taxable transactions, bringing the effective retail rate in Honolulu to 4.5%.

**Visibility to consumers.** A critical difference between Hawaii's GET and Washington's B&O tax is consumer visibility. Hawaii businesses are permitted — and virtually all of them do — to visibly pass the GET through to consumers as a separate line item on receipts and invoices. Consumers see "GET 4.0%" or "GET 4.5%" on their receipts in exactly the same way they would see a sales tax in other states. In practice, Hawaii's GET functions as both a gross receipts tax on businesses and a de facto sales tax on consumers, even though it is technically imposed on the business rather than the consumer.

Washington's B&O tax, by contrast, is generally not visible to consumers. It is embedded in the price of goods and services — businesses absorb the B&O tax as a cost of doing business and pass it through to consumers implicitly via higher prices, but there is no separate line item on a receipt. Washington consumers see a "Sales Tax" line but no "B&O Tax" line. This distinction affects public perception: Hawaii's GET is perceived by consumers as a sales tax (and generates the same political dynamics as a sales tax), while Washington's B&O tax is perceived primarily as a business tax, even though its economic incidence ultimately falls on consumers through higher prices.

**Revenue significance.** Hawaii's GET generates approximately $3.5 billion annually, making it the state's single largest revenue source — accounting for roughly 50% of the state general fund. This is comparable to the role of the B&O tax in Washington, though the B&O tax accounts for a somewhat smaller share of Washington's total revenue (approximately 25–30%) because Washington also relies heavily on its retail sales tax.

**Pyramiding in Hawaii.** Because Hawaii's GET rate (4.0% for retail and services) is much higher than Washington's goods-producing rates (0.471%–0.5%), pyramiding is a far more severe problem in Hawaii than in Washington for the manufacturing and distribution sectors. However, Hawaii mitigates this somewhat through the lower 0.5% wholesale and manufacturing rates, which reduce the per-stage tax burden earlier in the supply chain.

**No threshold.** Unlike every other gross receipts tax surveyed here, Hawaii's GET has no minimum threshold. Every business with any amount of GET-taxable activity must register and pay the tax from the first dollar. There is no small business credit or de minimis exception. This makes Hawaii's GET the most comprehensive and inescapable gross receipts tax in the United States — and it is one reason Hawaii consistently ranks near the bottom of state business tax climate indices published by the Tax Foundation and similar organizations.

The comparison between Hawaii's GET and Washington's B&O tax illuminates a broader point: gross receipts taxes exist on a spectrum. At one end, low-rate, high-threshold taxes like Ohio's CAT (0.26%, $6 million threshold) function as modest surcharges on larger businesses. At the other end, high-rate, no-threshold taxes like Hawaii's GET (4.0%, no threshold) function as comprehensive revenue systems that are the backbone of state finance. Washington's B&O tax sits in the upper-middle portion of this spectrum — higher rates and lower thresholds than most, but not as extreme as Hawaii's GET.

### 1.2.4 Washington's Place in the Family

Among the currently operating gross receipts taxes, Washington's B&O tax is distinguished by several features:

**Longevity.** The B&O tax has been in continuous operation since 1933, making it one of the oldest gross receipts taxes still in force anywhere in the United States. Only Delaware's gross receipts tax (1917) has a longer pedigree among currently operating levies.

**Breadth.** The B&O tax applies to virtually every form of business activity — retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, extracting (natural resources), services, financial activities, real estate, construction, government contracting, and more. There are very few categorical exemptions. If an entity is conducting business in Washington, it almost certainly owes B&O tax.

**Rate structure.** Washington's B&O rates, particularly for the "Service and Other Activities" classification, are significantly higher than those of other gross receipts taxes. At 1.5% (Tier 1) to 2.1% (Tier 3, post-HB 2081), Washington's service rate is roughly 4–8 times the rate of Ohio's CAT (0.26%) and Nevada's Commerce Tax (0.051%–0.331%). This is partly because Washington relies on the B&O tax to generate revenue that income-tax states would raise through their income tax.

**No deductions.** Unlike Oregon's CAT (which allows a 35% subtraction for cost inputs) or Texas's Franchise Tax (which permits deduction of cost of goods sold or compensation), Washington's B&O tax permits no deduction for business expenses of any kind. The tax base is truly gross receipts — the complete top line — with only a limited number of statutory deductions for specific types of income (such as the investment income deduction and interstate commerce deductions).

**Classification system.** Washington assigns businesses to tax classifications based on the nature of their activities, with different rates for different classifications. This is conceptually similar to Nevada's 26-industry-category approach but more granular and with wider rate differentials. A manufacturer pays 0.484% (0.5% from 2027); a service provider can pay up to 2.1%. This 4:1 rate differential between manufacturing and services reflects a deliberate policy choice to tax services more heavily than goods production — a choice rooted in Washington's historical identity as a manufacturing and resource-extraction economy.

## 1.3 Historical Origins of the B&O Tax

### 1.3.1 The Great Depression and Washington's Fiscal Crisis

The story of the B&O tax begins, as do so many stories of American state taxation, during the Great Depression. To understand why Washington chose a gross receipts tax — and why that choice has persisted for over ninety years — requires understanding the specific fiscal and constitutional crisis that engulfed the state in the early 1930s.

Washington entered the Depression with a state revenue system built almost entirely on property taxes. The state levied a general property tax, and local governments (counties, cities, school districts) levied their own property taxes. There was no general sales tax, no income tax, and no broad-based business tax. The state's economy was dominated by natural resource extraction (timber, fishing, mining), agriculture, and the emerging aerospace industry (Boeing had been incorporated in 1916 and was growing rapidly, though still a modest operation by later standards).

When the Depression struck, property values collapsed. Tax delinquency rates soared — farmers, homeowners, and businesses simply could not pay their property tax assessments on properties that had lost 30–50% of their value. The state's revenue base cratered. At the same time, demand for public services surged: unemployment relief, infrastructure maintenance, public health needs, and educational funding all required increased spending at exactly the moment when revenue was falling.

The Washington Legislature recognized that the state needed new, non-property-tax revenue sources. Two principal options were debated: an income tax and a gross receipts tax on business activities.

### 1.3.2 The Income Tax Attempt and *Culliton v. Chase* (1933)

In 1932, the Washington Legislature enacted a graduated personal income tax, hoping to diversify the state's revenue base away from its dependence on property taxes. The income tax was designed along conventional lines — a graduated rate structure where higher incomes faced higher marginal rates.

The tax was immediately challenged in court. In *Culliton v. Chase*, 174 Wash. 363, 25 P.2d 81 (1933), the Washington Supreme Court struck down the graduated income tax in a 5-4 decision that would reverberate through nearly a century of Washington fiscal policy.

The facts of the case are themselves revealing. The lead plaintiff, William Culliton, was a small Seattle insurance agent. The defendant, Samuel Chase, was the chairman of the Washington State Tax Commission, charged with implementing the new income tax. The case presented a single dispositive question: under the Washington State Constitution, is income "property"?

Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington State Constitution provides that "[a]ll taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax." This uniformity clause requires that property taxes be applied at the same rate to all property of the same class — they cannot be graduated (i.e., higher rates on more valuable property). If income is "property," then a graduated income tax would violate the uniformity clause because it imposes different rates on different amounts of income.

The majority opinion in *Culliton* held that income is indeed a form of property. The reasoning traced back to the broad common-law concept of property as anything of economic value that a person owns or has a right to. Income, being a thing of value received by a person, is therefore property. A graduated income tax — which imposes a 2% rate on the first $10,000 of income but a 6% rate on income above $50,000, for example — taxes different quantities of the same class of property (income) at different rates. This violates the uniformity clause.

The decision was dramatic. The court was initially expected to be deadlocked 4-4, with one justice too ill to participate. That justice stepped down, and Governor Clarence Martin appointed a replacement who was expected to support the income tax. But when the decision was handed down in September 1933, one of the justices who had been expected to uphold the tax changed his position, producing the 5-4 result that struck it down.

The four dissenters argued vigorously that income is not property in the constitutional sense — that it is, rather, a flow of value, a gain, an accession to wealth, and that the Legislature has the power to classify and tax it differently from real and personal property. This dissenting view has been endorsed by many tax scholars over the decades, and it reflects the majority position in federal constitutional law (the Sixteenth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, ratified in 1913, explicitly authorizes Congress to tax incomes "from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States"). But in Washington, it was the majority opinion in *Culliton* that became the law.

### 1.3.3 The Aftermath: Why Washington Got the B&O Tax

The *Culliton* decision did not merely strike down one specific income tax statute. It established a constitutional principle — income is property, and therefore any tax measured by income must comply with the uniformity clause, meaning it must be imposed at a uniform rate — that made it effectively impossible for the Legislature to enact a progressive (graduated) income tax without first amending the state constitution. A flat-rate income tax would technically be permissible under *Culliton* (since a single rate applied uniformly to all income would satisfy the uniformity clause), but the political appetite for a flat income tax — which would necessarily be regressive compared to a graduated structure — has never materialized.

With the income tax option effectively foreclosed by the court, the Legislature turned to the alternative: a broad-based tax on business activity. The key insight was that such a tax could be structured as an "excise tax" — a tax on the privilege of engaging in a particular activity — rather than as a property tax or income tax. Excise taxes are not subject to the uniformity clause in the same way that property taxes are. The Legislature could impose an excise tax at different rates on different activities without violating the constitutional requirement of uniformity, because different activities constitute different "classes" subject to their own uniform treatment.

### 1.3.4 The Revenue Act of 1935

The comprehensive restructuring came in 1935 with the Revenue Act of 1935 (Chapter 180, Laws of 1935), which established the basic framework of Washington's tax system that persists to this day. The Revenue Act created three new major taxes:

1. **The Retail Sales Tax** — A 2% tax on retail sales of tangible personal property (later increased multiple times to the current 6.5% state rate).

2. **The Business and Occupation Tax** — A gross receipts excise tax on the privilege of engaging in business activities in Washington, with different rates for different classifications of business activity.

3. **The Use Tax** — A complementary tax to the sales tax, imposed on the use of tangible personal property purchased from out of state on which Washington sales tax was not paid, to prevent erosion of the sales tax base through out-of-state purchases.

The B&O tax as enacted in 1935 was explicitly structured as an excise tax — not a tax on property (including income) but a tax on the "privilege" of doing business. This legal characterization was essential to its constitutional survival. The Washington courts upheld the B&O tax on exactly this basis: it is not a tax on the income a business earns (which would be a property tax subject to the uniformity clause), but a tax on the activity of doing business, merely measured by gross receipts as a convenient proxy for the volume of that activity.

This legal fiction — that a tax measured by a business's revenue is not a tax on that revenue but rather a tax on the privilege of earning that revenue — has been criticized by scholars but has proven constitutionally durable. It is the same legal fiction that the Washington Supreme Court employed 88 years later in *Quinn v. State* (2023) to uphold the capital gains excise tax: the tax is not on the capital gain (income/property) but on the privilege of selling the asset (an activity).

### 1.3.5 Early Structure and Evolution

The original 1935 B&O tax rate structure was simpler than today's:

| Classification | Original Rate (1935) |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Retailing | 0.25% |
| Wholesaling | 0.25% |
| Manufacturing | 0.25% |
| Services | 0.50% |

The rates were low by modern standards, reflecting the modest revenue needs of Depression-era state government and the political necessity of keeping rates manageable to avoid the political fate of the income tax. Over the following decades, the rates were increased incrementally:

- **1950s–1960s:** Rates gradually increased as state government expanded its scope and services. The service rate began its long climb upward relative to the goods-producing rates.

- **1970s–1980s:** The differential between service rates and manufacturing/retailing rates widened. The Legislature increasingly used the B&O tax as a policy tool, creating new classifications and rates for specific industries (public utilities, financial institutions, insurance companies).

- **1993:** The service rate reached 1.5%, where it would remain for over 30 years (with a brief increase to 1.8% from 2010 to 2013).

- **2010 (E2SSB 6143):** Temporary increase of the service rate from 1.5% to 1.8%, coupled with a fundamental restructuring of the apportionment rules (shifting from cost-of-performance to single-factor receipts / market-based sourcing for apportionable activities). The temporary rate increase expired, but the apportionment changes were permanent.

- **2019 (E2SHB 2158 et al.):** Creation of the Workforce Education Investment surcharge, including the "advanced computing" surcharge targeting large technology companies.

- **2025 (ESSB 2081):** The HB 2081 reform — the subject of Part 3 of this study — which introduced the three-tier service rate structure, the $250 million surcharge, and numerous other changes.

## 1.4 The Constitutional Income Tax Question

### 1.4.1 The Persistence of *Culliton*

The 1933 *Culliton v. Chase* decision has proven to be one of the most consequential and durable judicial decisions in Washington State history. Despite nearly a century of criticism from tax scholars, progressive advocates, and fiscal policy experts, the core holding — that income is property under the Washington Constitution and therefore subject to the uniformity clause — has never been overruled.

This does not mean it has not been challenged. Multiple attempts have been made to either overrule *Culliton* through a constitutional amendment or to distinguish it through creative legislative drafting:

**Constitutional Amendment Attempts:**

| Year | Initiative/Amendment | Result |
|------|---------------------|--------|
| 1934 | Initiative 69 | Failed at ballot |
| 1936 | Initiative 114 | Failed at ballot |
| 1938 | SJR 7 | Failed at ballot |
| 1942 | SJR 5 | Failed at ballot |
| 1970 | HJR 42 | Failed at ballot |
| 1973 | SJR 104 | Failed in Legislature |
| 2010 | Initiative 1098 | Failed at ballot (64%–36%) |

Initiative 1098 in 2010 was the most recent serious attempt. Sponsored by Bill Gates Sr. (the father of the Microsoft co-founder), the initiative would have imposed a 5% income tax on individuals earning over $200,000 and a 9% rate on incomes above $500,000, with the revenue dedicated to education and health services. Despite initial polling showing support, the initiative was defeated 64%–36% after an extensive campaign funded in part by opponents arguing that the tax, once enacted, would inevitably be expanded to lower-income taxpayers.

### 1.4.2 The *Quinn* End Run and Its Implications

The 2023 *Quinn v. State* decision did not overrule *Culliton*, but it executed a flanking maneuver around it. The capital gains excise tax is functionally identical to an income tax on a specific type of income (long-term capital gains), but the court upheld it as an excise tax on the transaction (the sale of the asset) rather than a tax on the income (the gain from the sale).

This distinction is, to many observers, sophistic. The tax is measured by the gain — if you sell an asset at a loss, no tax is owed. The tax amount increases as the gain increases. It walks like an income tax, it quacks like an income tax, and yet the court said it is an excise tax. But the 7-2 majority opinion in *Quinn* made a policy choice to preserve *Culliton* as nominal precedent while permitting the Legislature to achieve functionally similar results through creative statutory drafting.

The implications for the B&O tax and for Washington's broader fiscal architecture are significant. If the Legislature can impose a graduated excise tax measured by the amount of a capital gain, can it impose a graduated excise tax measured by the amount of a business's net income? What about a graduated excise tax measured by the amount of an individual's total income? The *Quinn* majority disclaimed any intent to permit such extensions, but the logical pathway is now open, and future legislatures and courts will decide how far down that path to travel.

### 1.4.3 The Income Tax Debate Today

As of 2025–2026, the Washington income tax debate continues to evolve. The political dynamics have shifted in important ways:

**Progressive advocacy.** The 2025 legislative session — the same session that produced HB 2081 — saw significant progressive energy directed at expanding Washington's tax base. Governor Bob Ferguson, who took office in January 2025 succeeding Jay Inslee, supported the HB 2081 / SB 5813 / SB 5814 tax package as a necessary response to projected budget shortfalls. Progressive legislators and advocacy organizations (including the Washington Budget & Policy Center, the Economic Opportunity Institute, and various labor unions) have framed the 2025 tax changes as a step toward a more equitable tax system but continue to view a true income tax as the long-term goal.

**Constitutional concern.** The primary obstacle remains the Washington Constitution as interpreted by *Culliton*. Amending the constitution requires a two-thirds vote of both legislative chambers to place an amendment on the ballot, followed by majority approval by voters. The two-thirds legislative threshold is a significant hurdle, particularly because rural and suburban legislative districts — where opposition to an income tax tends to be strongest — are overrepresented relative to their population in the state senate due to district geography.

**The *Quinn* pathway.** Some legal scholars have suggested that the *Quinn* decision opens a pathway to impose what is functionally an income tax without calling it one — an "excise tax on the privilege of earning income," structured and graduated like an income tax but legally characterized as an excise. Whether the courts would uphold such a statute is uncertain, but the *Quinn* precedent makes the argument at least plausible.

# Part 2: B&O Tax Mechanics

## 2.1 The Gross Receipts Concept

### 2.1.1 What Counts as Gross Receipts?

Under RCW 82.04.080, the B&O tax is imposed on:

> "...the value of products, gross proceeds of sale, or gross income of the business, as the case may be."

These three terms correspond to different types of business activity:

- **"Value of products"** is used for the Manufacturing and Extracting classifications. It is generally determined by the selling price of the manufactured or extracted products.

- **"Gross proceeds of sale"** is used for the Retailing and Wholesaling classifications. It means "the value proceeding or accruing from the sale of tangible personal property... without any deduction on account of the cost of property sold, the cost of materials used, labor costs, interest, discount paid, delivery costs, taxes, or any other expense whatsoever" (RCW 82.04.070).

- **"Gross income of the business"** is the catchall used for the Service and Other Activities classification and most other classifications. It means "the value proceeding or accruing by reason of the transaction of the business engaged in and includes gross proceeds of sales, compensation for the rendition of services, gains realized from trading in stocks, bonds, or other evidences of indebtedness, interest, discount, rents, royalties, fees, commissions, dividends, and other emoluments however designated" (RCW 82.04.080).

The critical feature of all three definitions is the phrase "without any deduction." The B&O tax base is the gross amount — the total revenue line — before any deduction for costs, expenses, or losses. This is what makes the B&O tax a gross receipts tax rather than an income tax.

### 2.1.2 What Is Excluded?

While the B&O tax base is broad, certain categories of receipts are excluded or exempted by specific statutory provisions:

**Statutory Deductions (selected examples):**

| Deduction | Statute | Description |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Interstate commerce | RCW 82.04.4266 | Receipts from sales delivered out of state where the seller is not taxable in the destination state |
| Government contracting (federal) | RCW 82.04.4289 | Amounts received from the U.S. government under certain contracts |
| Agricultural products | RCW 82.04.330 | Various exemptions for farmers and agricultural cooperatives |
| Investment income | RCW 82.04.4281 | Income from investments that are "incidental" to the business (subject to the *Antio* 5% safe harbor, as codified by HB 2081) |
| Bad debts | RCW 82.04.4284 | Amounts that become uncollectible (written off as bad debts) |
| Cash discounts | RCW 82.04.4283 | Cash discounts allowed and taken by customers |
| Returns and allowances | RCW 82.04.4285 | Amounts refunded to customers for returned goods |
| Amounts derived from the sale of real estate | RCW 82.04.390 | Exemption for casual or isolated sales of real property |

These deductions are narrow and specifically enumerated. There is no general deduction for cost of goods sold, no deduction for wages paid, no deduction for rent, and no deduction for most ordinary business expenses. The list of deductions has grown over the decades through targeted legislation, but the fundamental principle remains: the B&O tax base is gross receipts, and deductions are exceptions that must be specifically authorized by statute.

### 2.1.3 Cash vs. Accrual Basis

Washington's B&O tax allows businesses to report on either the cash basis or the accrual basis, but once a method is chosen, it must be used consistently. Most businesses report on the accrual basis (recognizing revenue when earned, regardless of when payment is received), which aligns with generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP). However, smaller businesses often use the cash basis (recognizing revenue when payment is received), which can be simpler to administer.

The choice of reporting method matters primarily for the timing of tax liability — when revenue is recognized and reported. It does not affect the total amount of tax owed over time, assuming all invoiced amounts are eventually collected.

### 2.1.4 The Multiple Activities Tax Credit (MATC)

Because the B&O tax is structured around classifications — and because a single business can engage in activities falling under multiple classifications — there is a risk of double taxation. For example, a company that manufactures goods in Washington and then sells them at retail within Washington would potentially owe B&O tax on the same economic value twice: once under the Manufacturing classification (taxed on the value of the manufactured products) and again under the Retailing classification (taxed on the gross proceeds of the retail sale).

The Multiple Activities Tax Credit (MATC), codified at RCW 82.04.440, addresses this problem. The MATC allows a credit against the tax due under one classification for the tax paid under another classification on the same products. In the manufacturer-retailer example, the company would report under both classifications but would receive a credit against the retailing tax for the amount of manufacturing tax already paid on those specific products.

The MATC is an essential structural feature of the B&O tax that prevents the tax from being even more pyramidal than it already is, at least within a single vertically integrated business.

## 2.2 B&O Classifications

### 2.2.1 The Classification System

The B&O tax organizes all business activities into "classifications," each with its own tax rate. A business does not choose its classification — it is determined by the nature of the activity being conducted. If a business engages in multiple types of activities (which is common), it must separately classify and report the income from each type of activity under the appropriate classification and rate.

This classification system is the heart of the B&O tax. It determines not only the rate of tax but also the reporting requirements, the applicable deductions, and (for multi-state businesses) the apportionment methodology.

### 2.2.3 Understanding the Service and Other Activities Classification

The "Service and Other Activities" classification (RCW 82.04.290) is the most important and most broadly applicable B&O classification. It serves as the residual or catchall classification: any business activity that does not fit into another specific classification defaults to "Service and Other Activities."

This classification encompasses an extraordinarily broad range of economic activity:

- **Professional services:** Law firms, accounting firms, consulting firms, architecture and engineering firms, medical and dental practices, veterinary practices
- **Technology services:** Software development, IT consulting, cloud computing, SaaS, data processing, website development, managed services
- **Financial services:** Banks, credit unions, mortgage companies, investment advisors, insurance companies (where not classified under a more specific rate)
- **Real estate services:** Property management, real estate development, appraisal services
- **Construction services:** General contractors, specialty contractors, engineering firms (note: construction activities may also be classified as retailing depending on the nature of the contract)
- **Personal services:** Hair salons, fitness centers, cleaning services, pet grooming, tutoring
- **Transportation services:** Trucking (not classified as public utility), logistics, delivery services
- **Hospitality services:** Hotels, event planning, catering (food sales may be separately classified as retailing)
- **Media and communications:** Advertising agencies, public relations firms, graphic design, photography, video production
- **Education:** Private schools, tutoring services, training companies, test preparation
- **Healthcare:** Hospitals (note: special rate applies post-HB 2081), clinics, home health, therapy, laboratory services

The breadth of this classification means that the Service and Other Activities rate is the single most important rate in the entire B&O tax system, both in terms of the number of businesses affected and the total revenue generated. It is also the rate that was most significantly restructured by HB 2081, with the introduction of the three-tier structure.

### 2.2.4 Multiple Classifications and Split Reporting

A single business may engage in activities falling under multiple classifications and must report each type of income separately. Consider a hypothetical construction company:

- **Retailing:** Income from retail construction contracts (lump-sum contracts where the contractor provides both labor and materials) is reported under the Retailing classification at 0.471% (0.5% from 2027).
- **Service and Other Activities:** Income from service contracts (time-and-materials contracts where the customer provides materials and the contractor provides only labor) is reported under the Service classification at 1.5%–2.1%.
- **Wholesaling:** If the company also sells building materials to other contractors, that income is reported under Wholesaling at 0.484% (0.5% from 2027).

The company must maintain records sufficient to allocate its income among the applicable classifications. This allocation must be supportable in the event of a Department of Revenue audit.

### 2.2.5 The Manufacturing Classification: Special Rules

The Manufacturing classification deserves special attention because it includes unique rules about valuation and reporting that differ from other classifications.

Under RCW 82.04.240, the Manufacturing B&O tax is imposed on "the value of the products, including by-products, manufactured." This creates a potential measurement problem: if a manufacturer sells its products (triggering the manufacturing tax) and also sells them at retail or wholesale in Washington (triggering the retailing or wholesaling tax), the same economic value could be taxed twice.

The resolution is twofold:

1. **Value = selling price.** For products that are sold, the "value of products" is generally determined by the selling price. This aligns the manufacturing tax base with the selling tax base.

2. **MATC.** The Multiple Activities Tax Credit prevents double taxation by providing a credit for manufacturing tax paid on products that are also taxed under retailing or wholesaling when sold.

For products that are not sold but are consumed by the manufacturer (used internally in further manufacturing or in providing services), a separate valuation methodology applies, generally based on the "comparable selling price" of similar products.

## 2.3 Rate Structure (Pre-HB 2081)

### 2.3.1 The Historic Rate Landscape

For many years prior to the 2025 HB 2081 reform, the B&O tax rate structure had been relatively stable. The rates that were in effect as of January 1, 2025 (before HB 2081 changes began taking effect) can be grouped into three broad tiers:

**Tier 1 — Goods-Producing Activities (approximately 0.47%–0.48%):**
- Retailing: 0.471%
- Wholesaling: 0.484%
- Manufacturing: 0.484%
- Extracting: 0.484%
- Printing and Publishing: 0.484%

**Tier 2 — Service Activities (1.5%):**
- Service and Other Activities: 1.5%
- Real Estate Commissions: 1.5%
- Gambling Contests of Chance: 1.5%

**Tier 3 — Regulated Industries (1.0%–3.9%):**
- Insurance Agents: 1.0%
- Public Utilities: 3.0%–3.873% (varies by utility type)

This three-tier structure reflected a conscious policy choice: goods-producing activities (manufacturing, retailing, wholesaling) were taxed at rates roughly one-third of the rate applied to service activities. The policy rationale had both historical and economic dimensions:

**Historical rationale:** When the B&O tax was first enacted, Washington's economy was dominated by goods production — timber, agriculture, fishing, manufacturing (primarily Boeing and associated aerospace suppliers). Taxing these dominant industries at lower rates was both a political necessity (they wielded significant legislative influence) and an economic judgment (manufacturing involves long supply chains with multiple B&O-taxable transactions, so a lower rate partially offsets the pyramiding effect).

**Economic rationale:** Manufacturing and retailing involve the purchase of tangible inputs (raw materials, inventory, supplies) whose costs typically represent a large fraction of gross receipts. A manufacturer with $10 million in gross receipts might have $7 million in materials costs, leaving only $3 million in gross margin. A service business with $10 million in gross receipts might have $1 million in non-labor costs, with the remaining $9 million being gross margin (mostly salaries and profit). The lower manufacturing rate partially compensates for the fact that a larger share of manufacturing revenue represents pass-through costs rather than value added by the manufacturer.

### 2.3.2 The 2010 Temporary Rate Increase

In 2010, facing a severe budget shortfall in the aftermath of the 2008–2009 financial crisis, the Legislature enacted E2SSB 6143, which temporarily increased the Service and Other Activities rate from 1.5% to 1.8%. This 20% rate increase was intended to be temporary, and it expired on schedule in 2013, reverting the rate to 1.5%.

The 2010 legislation was significant for two additional reasons:

1. **Apportionment reform.** The same bill fundamentally restructured the apportionment rules for multi-state service businesses, shifting from a cost-of-performance methodology to single-factor receipts apportionment with market-based sourcing. This change — discussed in detail in Part 4 — was permanent and represented the most significant structural reform of the B&O tax since 1935.

2. **Rate ratchet precedent.** The temporary increase established a precedent that the Service and Other Activities rate could be adjusted upward relatively easily by the Legislature when budget needs arose. The 2025 HB 2081 reform drew on this precedent, but went much further — making the increases permanent and introducing the unprecedented three-tier structure.

### 2.3.3 The 2019 Workforce Education Investment Act

In 2019, the Legislature enacted the Workforce Education Investment Act (E2SHB 2158), which imposed a tiered "workforce education investment surcharge" on certain service businesses:

| Prior Year Gross Income | Surcharge Rate |
|------------------------|---------------|
| Less than $1 million | 0% (no surcharge) |
| $1 million to $5 million | 0% (no surcharge) |
| $5 million to $25 billion | 0.2% |
| Over $25 billion | 0.4% |

Additionally, "select advanced computing businesses" (defined as businesses with more than $25 billion in worldwide affiliated group revenue that are primarily engaged in cloud computing, online services, or similar technology activities) were subject to a separate 1.2% surcharge on their service income, capped at $9 million annually.

"Specified financial institutions" (financial institutions with more than $1 billion in annual net income) were subject to a separate 1.2% additional tax on their service income.

The 2019 surcharges were, in retrospect, a dress rehearsal for the far more sweeping changes enacted in HB 2081 six years later. They established the structural concept of tiering B&O rates based on the size of the taxpayer — a departure from the traditional B&O structure where all businesses in the same classification paid the same rate regardless of size.

## 2.4 The Small Business B&O Credit

### 2.4.1 Purpose and Structure

The Small Business B&O Tax Credit is Washington's principal mechanism for relieving the B&O tax burden on the smallest businesses. It operates as a graduated credit that effectively eliminates B&O tax liability for very small businesses and phases out as the business grows.

The credit is codified at RCW 82.04.4451 and administered through WAC 458-20-104.

The credit works as follows:

1. A business calculates its B&O tax liability for the reporting period (monthly, quarterly, or annual) in the normal manner.
2. If the calculated B&O tax liability falls below a specified threshold, the business claims a credit equal to the full amount of the tax due, reducing the liability to zero.
3. As the calculated tax liability increases above the threshold, the credit begins to phase out — the credit amount decreases proportionally, so the business pays an increasing share of the full tax amount.
4. Above a higher threshold, the credit is fully phased out and the business pays the full tax amount.

### 2.4.2 Credit Tables

The Department of Revenue publishes Small Business Tax Credit Tables that provide the exact credit amounts based on the business's tax liability and the proportion of income reported under different classification rates. The tables are structured as follows:

**Table 1 — For businesses where 50% or more of taxable income is reported under classifications with rates at or above 1.5% (primarily Service and Other Activities):**

| Annual B&O Tax Due | Credit Amount |
|-------------------|---------------|
| $0 – $1,920 | Full credit (tax = $0) |
| $1,920 – $3,840 | Graduated phase-out |
| Over $3,840 | No credit |

Monthly equivalent: full credit if tax due is under $160/month, phase-out between $160 and $320/month.
Quarterly equivalent: full credit if tax due is under $480/quarter, phase-out between $480 and $960/quarter.

**Table 2 — For businesses where less than 50% of taxable income is reported under higher-rate classifications (primarily retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing):**

| Annual B&O Tax Due | Credit Amount |
|-------------------|---------------|
| $0 – $660 | Full credit (tax = $0) |
| $660 – $1,320 | Graduated phase-out |
| Over $1,320 | No credit |

Monthly equivalent: full credit if tax due is under $55/month, phase-out between $55 and $110/month.
Quarterly equivalent: full credit if tax due is under $165/quarter, phase-out between $165 and $330/quarter.

### 2.4.3 Practical Effect

The small business credit effectively creates a de facto exemption for the smallest businesses. To understand what these credit thresholds mean in terms of gross receipts:

**For a pure service business (1.5% rate, Table 1):**
- Full credit (zero B&O tax): annual gross receipts up to approximately $128,000
- Partial credit (reduced B&O tax): annual gross receipts $128,000–$256,000
- No credit (full B&O tax): annual gross receipts over $256,000

**For a pure retailing business (0.471% rate, Table 2):**
- Full credit (zero B&O tax): annual gross receipts up to approximately $140,000
- Partial credit (reduced B&O tax): annual gross receipts $140,000–$280,000
- No credit (full B&O tax): annual gross receipts over $280,000

These thresholds mean that many solo practitioners, part-time businesses, and very small operations pay no B&O tax at all. The credit is a blunt but effective instrument for keeping the smallest businesses out of the B&O tax system, at a modest cost to the state's revenue.

### 2.4.4 Filing Still Required

Importantly, the small business credit reduces the tax amount to zero but does not eliminate the filing obligation. A business that qualifies for the full small business credit must still register with the Department of Revenue, file excise tax returns on the appropriate schedule, and report its gross receipts. The credit is then applied on the return to zero out the tax due.

However, businesses with very low gross receipts may be assigned an annual filing frequency, which reduces the administrative burden to a single return per year. Additionally, businesses that are inactive or have ceased operations may request to have their account placed in an active-non-reporting status.

### 2.4.5 Interaction with HB 2081 Tiers

The small business credit continues to operate post-HB 2081. For Tier 1 service businesses (those with prior-year gross income under $1 million), the 1.5% rate is unchanged, and the small business credit continues to apply as before, effectively exempting the smallest service businesses from B&O tax.

For Tier 2 and Tier 3 businesses, the small business credit is generally irrelevant — businesses with $1 million or more in prior-year gross income will have annual B&O tax liabilities far exceeding the credit thresholds.

# Part 3: The 2025 HB 2081 Reform — The Largest Washington Tax Change in History

## 3.1 Context and Legislative History

### 3.1.1 The Budget Shortfall

The 2025 legislative session convened in January 2025 against a backdrop of significant fiscal pressure. Washington State faced a projected budget shortfall for the 2025–2027 biennium that, depending on the estimate and the assumptions used, ranged from $10 billion to $16 billion. This shortfall was driven by several converging factors:

**Revenue slowdown.** After a period of robust revenue growth during 2021–2023 (fueled by post-pandemic economic recovery, strong technology sector earnings, and a surge in retail sales tax collections from the e-commerce boom), Washington's revenue growth decelerated in 2024. Technology sector layoffs — which had begun in late 2022 and continued through 2024 — reduced both payroll-driven B&O tax revenue and consumer spending by displaced technology workers. Real estate market cooling reduced Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) collections. Sales tax growth, while positive, fell below the trajectory assumed in the previous biennium's budget.

**Spending commitments.** The Legislature had made significant new spending commitments during the 2023 session, particularly in education funding (responding to ongoing McCleary-related mandates and new early childhood education programs), behavioral health services, housing and homelessness programs, and infrastructure. These commitments created a structural increase in baseline spending that was difficult to reduce without politically costly service cuts.

**The McCleary legacy.** Washington's ongoing school funding obligations under the Washington Supreme Court's 2012 *McCleary v. State* decision continued to consume a growing share of the state budget. While the Legislature had technically satisfied the court's mandate in 2018, the underlying dynamic — a constitutionally required "paramount duty" to amply fund basic education — created a floor under education spending that limited fiscal flexibility.

**Federal funding cliff.** The expiration of pandemic-era federal aid — particularly the enhanced Medicaid match rates, the Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief (ESSER) funds, and various American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) allocations — left gaps in state program budgets that had been temporarily backfilled by federal money.

### 3.1.2 The Legislative Process

The 2025 tax reform legislation moved through the Washington Legislature in the form of three interconnected bills:

1. **HB 2081** — The B&O tax reform bill, which restructured Service and Other Activities rates into three tiers, increased manufacturing/wholesaling/retailing rates, created the $250 million surcharge, increased the advanced computing surcharge, increased the financial institutions surcharge, and codified the *Antio* investment income deduction standard.

2. **SB 5813** — The capital gains excise tax reform bill, which restructured the 7% flat capital gains rate into a progressive rate (7% up to $1 million, 9.9% above $1 million) and modified the estate tax rates.

3. **SB 5814** — The sales tax expansion bill, which extended the retail sales tax to several categories of services that had previously been exempt, including temporary staffing services, IT services, custom software development, investigation and security services, and advertising services.

Together, these three bills constituted what analysts from the Association of Washington Business, the Tax Foundation, and various professional accounting firms characterized as **the single largest tax increase package in Washington State history** — both in terms of the breadth of changes and the estimated revenue impact.

Governor Bob Ferguson signed all three bills on **May 20, 2025**.

### 3.1.3 Revenue Estimates

The combined revenue impact of the three-bill package was estimated as follows:

| Bill | Estimated Biennial Revenue (2025–2027) | Estimated Annual Revenue (at full implementation) |
|------|---------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| HB 2081 (B&O changes) | $4.5–5.5 billion | $3.0–3.5 billion |
| SB 5813 (Capital gains / estate) | $1.5–2.0 billion | $1.0–1.2 billion |
| SB 5814 (Sales tax expansion) | $2.0–2.5 billion | $1.5–1.8 billion |
| **Total** | **$8.0–10.0 billion** | **$5.5–6.5 billion** |

These estimates were subject to significant uncertainty, particularly for the B&O tax tiers (because the interaction between the tier structure and business behavior was difficult to model) and for the sales tax expansion (because the applicability of sales tax to services was novel in Washington and involved complex definitional questions about which services were covered).

### 3.1.4 Political Dynamics

The 2025 tax package reflected a significant shift in Washington's political dynamics. Several factors combined to make major tax increases politically viable in 2025 in a way they had not been in previous sessions:

**Unified progressive control.** Democrats held solid majorities in both the state House and Senate, and newly elected Governor Ferguson was supportive of progressive fiscal policy. This gave the majority party sufficient votes to pass tax increases without bipartisan support.

**Post-*Quinn* confidence.** The Washington Supreme Court's 2023 decision upholding the capital gains excise tax gave the Legislature confidence that creative tax structures — including graduated rates based on income levels — could survive constitutional challenge. The *Quinn* decision was widely interpreted as signaling that the court was more receptive to progressive taxation than its predecessors had been.

**Business community division.** The business community was not monolithic in its opposition. Many small businesses were insulated from the worst effects of HB 2081 by the Tier 1 rate (unchanged at 1.5% for businesses with under $1 million in prior-year gross income). Large technology companies, while opposed to the increased advanced computing surcharge, had a complex relationship with Washington's tax structure (they benefited enormously from the absence of a corporate income tax and were reluctant to advocate too aggressively for the status quo, lest the Legislature consider even more dramatic structural changes). The Association of Washington Business coordinated opposition but faced challenges in building a unified business coalition against a package that was carefully structured to concentrate costs on larger businesses.

**Progressive advocacy infrastructure.** Organizations including the Washington Budget & Policy Center, the Economic Opportunity Institute, SEIU, and various community-based organizations had spent years building the case for progressive tax reform, publishing research on Washington's regressive tax structure (drawing heavily on the ITEP "Who Pays?" data), and organizing grassroots support for tax changes that would shift the burden toward higher-income individuals and larger businesses.

## 3.2 HB 2081 — The B&O Changes in Detail

### 3.2.1 The Three-Tier Service Rate Structure

The centerpiece of HB 2081 is the restructuring of the Service and Other Activities B&O tax rate from a flat 1.5% to a three-tier structure based on the taxpayer's prior calendar year gross income:

| Tier | Prior Year Gross Income | Rate | Effective Date | Percentage Increase from Previous 1.5% |
|------|------------------------|------|----------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Tier 1** | Less than $1,000,000 | 1.50% | October 1, 2025 | 0% (unchanged) |
| **Tier 2** | $1,000,000 to $4,999,999.99 | 1.75% | October 1, 2025 | +16.7% |
| **Tier 3** | $5,000,000 or more | 2.10% | October 1, 2025 | +40.0% |

**Statutory authority:** ESSB 2081, Chapter 240, Laws of 2025, Section 109, amending RCW 82.04.290.

Several technical details of the tier structure are critically important:

**Prior-year determination.** The tier is determined by the taxpayer's total gross income from all Washington activities during the immediately preceding calendar year. A business's tier for 2026 is determined by its 2025 gross income. This creates a one-year lag — a business that experiences rapid growth in the current year pays the rate corresponding to its smaller prior-year size, while a business that experiences a revenue decline pays the rate corresponding to its larger prior-year size.

**Affiliated group aggregation.** HB 2081 requires that gross income be measured at the "affiliated group" level, not at the individual entity level. An "affiliated group" is defined as a group of entities sharing common control, where control means "direct or indirect possession of more than 80 percent of the power to direct or cause the direction of the management and policies of an entity." This means that a large corporation cannot avoid Tier 3 rates by splitting its Washington operations among multiple subsidiaries each reporting less than $5 million in gross income. The affiliated group's total Washington gross income determines the tier for all entities within the group.

**Entire year at a single tier.** Once a tier is determined based on prior-year income, the business pays the corresponding rate for the entire subsequent calendar year. The tiers are not marginal rates — a business in Tier 3 pays 2.1% on all of its service income, not 1.5% on the first $1 million, 1.75% on $1M–$5M, and 2.1% on the excess. This is a significant structural choice that creates "cliff effects" at the tier boundaries.

### 3.2.2 The Cliff Effect

The non-marginal tier structure creates sharp discontinuities at the $1 million and $5 million thresholds. Consider a service business with $999,999 in prior-year gross income (Tier 1) compared to a business with $1,000,001 (Tier 2):

| Metric | Business at $999,999 | Business at $1,000,001 |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Prior-year income | $999,999 | $1,000,001 |
| B&O rate (next year) | 1.50% | 1.75% |
| Assuming same revenue next year: | | |
| B&O tax at prior year's revenue | $15,000 | $17,500 |
| **Additional B&O from crossing $1M** | — | **$2,500** |
| Additional revenue earned | $2 | $2 |

The business that earned $2 more in the prior year will pay approximately $2,500 more in B&O tax the following year. This cliff effect is most pronounced at the $5 million threshold:

| Metric | Business at $4,999,999 | Business at $5,000,001 |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Prior-year income | $4,999,999 | $5,000,001 |
| B&O rate (next year) | 1.75% | 2.10% |
| Assuming $5M revenue next year: | | |
| B&O tax | $87,500 | $105,000 |
| **Additional B&O from crossing $5M** | — | **$17,500** |

These cliff effects create incentives for businesses near the thresholds to manage their revenue — deferring billing, accelerating expenses, or restructuring operations — to stay below the threshold. The Department of Revenue will need to develop anti-avoidance rules to address these incentive effects.

### 3.2.3 Exceptions to the Tiered Structure

Two categories of businesses are excepted from the tiered rate structure and continue to pay 1.5% regardless of their gross income:

1. **Hospitals.** Hospitals, as defined by the relevant healthcare licensing statutes, pay the 1.5% rate regardless of size. This exception reflects the political reality that increasing taxes on hospitals — many of which are nonprofit community institutions — would have generated significant opposition and potentially affected healthcare costs and access.

2. **Select Advanced Computing Businesses.** Businesses that qualify as "select advanced computing businesses" under the workforce education investment surcharge provisions continue to pay the 1.5% base rate under the Service and Other Activities classification. This does not mean they escape additional taxation — the advanced computing surcharge (see Section 3.2.5) applies on top of the 1.5% base rate. The exception from the tiered structure simply prevents these businesses from being subject to both the tiered rate increase and the separate advanced computing surcharge, which would represent double layering of targeted increases.

### 3.2.4 Manufacturing, Wholesaling, Retailing, and Extracting Rate Increases

Effective January 1, 2027, HB 2081 increases the B&O tax rates for goods-producing and goods-distributing classifications:

| Classification | Pre-HB 2081 Rate | Post-HB 2081 Rate | Increase |
|---------------|-------------------|--------------------|----|
| Retailing | 0.471% | 0.500% | +6.2% |
| Wholesaling | 0.484% | 0.500% | +3.3% |
| Manufacturing | 0.484% | 0.500% | +3.3% |
| Extracting | 0.484% | 0.500% | +3.3% |
| Printing and Publishing | 0.484% | 0.500% | +3.3% |
| Radio and Television Broadcasting | 0.484% | 0.500% | +3.3% |

The alignment of these rates to a uniform 0.5% represents both a simplification (eliminating the odd 0.471% retailing rate and the 0.484% rate used across multiple classifications) and a modest revenue increase. The increases are small in percentage terms (3.3%–6.2% rate increases), but they apply to large revenue bases and will generate meaningful revenue. The delayed effective date (January 1, 2027, versus October 1, 2025 for the service tier changes) gives goods-producing businesses additional time to adjust.

### 3.2.5 The $250 Million Surcharge

HB 2081 creates a new surcharge applicable to very large businesses:

**Structure:** A 0.5% surcharge on the taxable B&O income of any business (measured at the affiliated group level) with total annual Washington gross receipts exceeding $250 million. The surcharge applies only to gross receipts in excess of $250 million — the first $250 million is exempt from the surcharge.

**Effective date:** January 1, 2026.

**Sunset:** December 31, 2029 (the surcharge is temporary).

**Exclusions:** The surcharge does not apply to:
- Select advanced computing businesses (subject to their own surcharge)
- Specified financial institutions (subject to their own surcharge)
- Manufacturers (whose goods-producing income is already taxed at the lower manufacturing rate)
- Farmers
- Fuel sellers (subject to separate fuel tax regimes)

**Practical effect:** A business with $300 million in Washington gross receipts would pay a surcharge of 0.5% on $50 million (the amount exceeding $250 million), or $250,000, in addition to its regular B&O tax. A business with $1 billion in qualifying Washington gross receipts would pay a surcharge of 0.5% on $750 million, or $3.75 million.

The number of businesses affected by the $250 million surcharge is small in absolute terms — likely fewer than 100 Washington-based or Washington-nexus businesses have gross receipts exceeding $250 million — but the revenue impact is significant because these businesses represent a large fraction of total Washington economic activity.

### 3.2.6 The Advanced Computing Surcharge Increase

HB 2081 dramatically increases the surcharge applicable to "select advanced computing businesses":

| Parameter | Pre-HB 2081 | Post-HB 2081 |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| Surcharge rate | 1.2% | 7.5% |
| Annual cap | $9 million | $75 million |
| Qualifying threshold | $25B+ worldwide affiliated revenue | $25B+ worldwide affiliated revenue |
| Effective date | — | January 1, 2026 |

This is, proportionally, the most dramatic rate increase in the entire HB 2081 package — a more than sixfold increase in the surcharge rate and an eightfold increase in the annual cap. The practical effect is that the largest technology companies operating in Washington (those with over $25 billion in worldwide revenue from advanced computing activities) will face a combined B&O rate on their Service and Other Activities income of:

- 1.5% (base rate, exempt from tiered structure) + 7.5% (surcharge) = **9.0%**

This combined 9.0% rate is, effectively, a targeted tax on the largest cloud computing and artificial intelligence platform companies. While the statute uses neutral language ("select advanced computing businesses"), the practical reality is that the provision is primarily directed at a small number of very large technology companies headquartered in or with major operations in the Puget Sound region.

The revenue estimate for the increased advanced computing surcharge is significant — potentially $1.5–2.0 billion per biennium — because even with the $75 million annual cap per taxpayer, the total tax collected from the small group of qualifying companies is substantial.

### 3.2.7 The Financial Institutions Surcharge Increase

HB 2081 also increases the surcharge on "specified financial institutions":

| Parameter | Pre-HB 2081 | Post-HB 2081 |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| Surcharge rate | 1.2% | 1.5% |
| Qualifying threshold | $1B+ in annual net income | $1B+ in annual net income |
| Effective date | — | October 1, 2025 |

This is a more modest increase than the advanced computing surcharge change, but it still represents a 25% increase in the surcharge rate. The combined rate for qualifying financial institutions is:

- 1.5% (base rate) + 1.5% (surcharge) = **3.0%**

This combined 3.0% rate makes Washington's B&O tax on large financial institutions competitive (in terms of effective burden) with some states' corporate income taxes, even though it is measured by gross receipts rather than net income.

### 3.2.8 The Investment Income Deduction Codification

HB 2081 codifies the *Antio LLC v. Washington Department of Revenue* standard for the investment income deduction. Prior to HB 2081, the investment income deduction (RCW 82.04.4281) allowed businesses to deduct "amounts derived from investments" from their B&O tax base. The scope of this deduction — and particularly the meaning of "investments" — had been the subject of extensive litigation culminating in the Washington Supreme Court's October 2024 decision in *Antio*.

HB 2081 codifies the following standard:

**The 5% safe harbor:** Investment income qualifies for the deduction only when the taxpayer's total worldwide gross income from investments constitutes less than 5% of the taxpayer's total worldwide gross income. If investment income is 5% or more of worldwide gross income, the deduction is unavailable for any investment income.

**Exemptions from the 5% threshold:** The following entities are exempt from the 5% threshold and may claim the investment income deduction regardless of the proportion of their income derived from investments:
- Nonprofit organizations
- Collective investment vehicles (mutual funds, REITs, etc.)
- Retirement accounts and pension funds
- Family investment entities meeting certain criteria

This codification is discussed in greater detail in Part 6 (Section 6.1).

## 3.3 Why the Tiered Structure?

### 3.3.1 Policy Rationale

The three-tier Service and Other Activities rate structure represents a fundamental philosophical departure for the B&O tax. For its entire prior history, the B&O tax had been structured on the principle that all businesses in the same classification pay the same rate, regardless of size. The 2019 Workforce Education Investment surcharge introduced size-based differentiation through a surcharge layered on top of the base rate, but HB 2081 went further by making size-based rate differentiation the core structure of the classification itself.

The Legislature's stated policy rationales for the tiered structure included:

**Ability to pay.** Larger businesses, as measured by gross receipts, have greater ability to absorb a higher tax rate. This is a standard progressive-taxation argument, adapted to the gross receipts context. While it is true that gross receipts are an imperfect proxy for ability to pay (a business with $10 million in gross receipts and a 2% profit margin is in a very different financial position than a business with $10 million in gross receipts and a 40% profit margin), the Legislature judged that, on average, higher gross receipts correlate with greater economic capacity.

**Small business protection.** By holding the Tier 1 rate unchanged at 1.5%, the Legislature could increase rates on larger businesses while protecting the smallest service businesses from any rate increase. This was both good policy (small businesses are generally more vulnerable to tax increases) and good politics (small business owners are a sympathetic constituency, and the Legislature could characterize the reform as affecting "only" businesses with over $1 million in revenue).

**Revenue generation with political viability.** A flat increase in the Service rate from 1.5% to 2.1% — applied to all service businesses regardless of size — would have generated even more revenue but would have been politically far more difficult, because it would have directly affected hundreds of thousands of small businesses. The tiered structure allowed the Legislature to generate significant revenue while limiting the direct impact to a smaller number of larger businesses.

### 3.3.2 The Progressive Structure Debate

The adoption of a tiered rate structure in the B&O tax mirrors the broader progressive-taxation debate that has characterized Washington politics for decades. Proponents argue:

- Washington's overall tax system is the most regressive in the nation (per ITEP data), and any mechanism that shifts more of the tax burden to larger, higher-revenue businesses helps address this regressivity.
- The B&O tax has historically been criticized as unfair to small businesses because it applies the same rate regardless of a business's ability to pay. The tiered structure partially addresses this critique by ensuring that the smallest businesses face the lowest rate.
- The tiered structure brings the B&O tax more in line with the progressive structures used in other states' business taxes (graduated corporate income tax rates, for example, are common in states that levy corporate income taxes).

Opponents argue:

- The B&O tax is already a deeply flawed tax from an economic efficiency standpoint (due to pyramiding and the taxation of gross receipts rather than net income), and adding a progressive rate structure compounds these flaws by introducing additional distortions.
- The affiliated-group aggregation rule means that franchise operations, multi-entity real estate holdings, and other business structures common in the service sector will be pulled into higher tiers based on the total activity of the group, even if individual entities are relatively small.
- The cliff effects at the $1 million and $5 million thresholds create perverse incentives for businesses to manage their revenue downward or restructure their operations to avoid crossing thresholds, reducing economic efficiency and creating administrative complexity.
- The prior-year look-back creates mismatches: a business that had a strong year followed by a weak year will pay a higher rate during the weak year based on the strong year's revenue, adding a procyclical element to the tax at exactly the wrong moment.

### 3.3.3 Revenue Estimates for the Tiered Structure

The Department of Revenue and the Office of Financial Management estimated that the three-tier Service and Other Activities structure would generate approximately $1.5–2.0 billion in additional revenue per biennium compared to the flat 1.5% rate. The distribution of this revenue across the three tiers illustrates the concentration of economic activity:

- **Tier 1 (under $1M):** Accounts for the vast majority of businesses by count (estimated 85–90% of all businesses reporting under Service and Other Activities) but a relatively small share of total gross receipts (estimated 15–20%). No additional revenue generated (rate unchanged).

- **Tier 2 ($1M–$5M):** Accounts for an estimated 8–12% of businesses by count and 15–20% of total gross receipts. Generates moderate additional revenue from the 0.25 percentage point rate increase.

- **Tier 3 ($5M+):** Accounts for an estimated 2–5% of businesses by count but 60–70% of total gross receipts. Generates the lion's share of additional revenue from the 0.60 percentage point rate increase.

This distribution — where a small number of large businesses generate most of the taxable activity — is a common feature of business tax bases and was a key factor in the Legislature's decision to adopt the tiered structure rather than a flat rate increase.

## 3.4 Winners and Losers

### 3.4.1 Who Is Unchanged?

**Small service businesses (under $1M in prior-year gross income).** These businesses — which constitute the vast majority of Washington service businesses by count — experience no change to their B&O rate under HB 2081. They continue to pay 1.5%. For the smallest of these businesses, the small business credit continues to apply, reducing or eliminating their B&O liability entirely. From a pure B&O tax perspective, HB 2081 is a non-event for these businesses.

However, many small service businesses may be affected by SB 5814 (the sales tax expansion to services), which imposes retail sales tax on certain service categories (IT services, temporary staffing, security services, advertising) for the first time. The interaction of unchanged B&O rates with new sales tax obligations creates a mixed picture for some small service businesses.

### 3.4.2 Who Pays More?

**Mid-market service businesses ($1M–$5M in prior-year gross income).** These businesses face a 16.7% rate increase, from 1.5% to 1.75%. For a business with $3 million in service revenue, the annual B&O tax increase is approximately $7,500 (from $45,000 to $52,500). This is a meaningful but generally manageable increase for a business of this size.

**Large service businesses ($5M+ in prior-year gross income).** These businesses face a 40% rate increase, from 1.5% to 2.1%. For a business with $20 million in service revenue, the annual B&O tax increase is approximately $120,000 (from $300,000 to $420,000). For a business with $100 million in service revenue, the increase is approximately $600,000. These are substantial increases that will materially affect the profitability of large service businesses operating in Washington.

**Very large businesses ($250M+ in gross receipts).** These businesses face the 0.5% surcharge on receipts exceeding $250 million, in addition to the tier-based rate increases. The combined effect can be significant: a service business with $500 million in Washington gross receipts would face a B&O tax of approximately $10.5 million under the 2.1% Tier 3 rate, plus a surcharge of $1.25 million on the $250 million exceeding the threshold, for a total of approximately $11.75 million — compared to approximately $7.5 million under the old 1.5% flat rate. This represents a 56.7% increase in total B&O liability.

**Advanced computing companies.** With the surcharge increasing from 1.2% to 7.5% and the annual cap rising from $9 million to $75 million, the largest technology companies face dramatic increases. A qualifying company that previously paid $9 million annually in advanced computing surcharges could pay up to $75 million.

**Financial institutions ($1B+ in net income).** The financial institutions surcharge increase from 1.2% to 1.5% represents a 25% increase in the surcharge component. Combined with the base rate, these institutions pay 3.0% on their service income.

**Manufacturers, wholesalers, retailers.** These businesses face modest increases (3.3%–6.2%) effective January 1, 2027. The impact is small in rate terms but applies broadly, affecting virtually every goods-producing and goods-distributing business in the state.

### 3.4.3 Revenue Distribution Analysis

The following table estimates the approximate distribution of HB 2081's B&O-related revenue impact across different categories of businesses:

| Category | Estimated Share of Additional B&O Revenue |
|----------|------------------------------------------|
| Tier 3 service businesses ($5M+) | 35–40% |
| Advanced computing surcharge increase | 25–30% |
| $250M surcharge | 10–15% |
| Financial institutions surcharge increase | 5–8% |
| Tier 2 service businesses ($1M–$5M) | 5–8% |
| Manufacturing/wholesaling/retailing rate increase | 8–12% |
| Gambling rate increase | <1% |

This distribution confirms that the HB 2081 revenue increase is heavily concentrated on large businesses, with the advanced computing surcharge and the Tier 3 rate increase together accounting for roughly 60–70% of the additional revenue. The political design is evident: protect small businesses (Tier 1 unchanged), impose moderate increases on mid-market businesses (Tier 2), and concentrate the largest increases on the biggest businesses (Tier 3, advanced computing, financial institutions, $250M surcharge).

# Part 4: Nexus, Apportionment, and Multi-State Operations

## 4.1 Physical Nexus

### 4.1.1 Traditional Physical Presence Standard

Historically, a business was subject to Washington's B&O tax only if it had "physical nexus" — a physical connection to the state sufficient to establish the state's jurisdiction to tax. The physical nexus standard, derived from the U.S. Supreme Court's 1967 decision in *National Bellas Hess v. Illinois* and its 1992 successor *Quill Corp. v. North Dakota*, required that a business have some tangible presence in Washington before the state could impose tax obligations.

Physical nexus is established through any of the following:

- **Employees or agents.** Having one or more employees, sales representatives, independent contractors, or other agents who perform services in Washington on behalf of the business.
- **Property.** Owning or leasing real property (offices, warehouses, retail locations) or tangible personal property (equipment, inventory, servers) located in Washington.
- **Regular physical presence.** Having employees or agents who regularly (not merely occasionally) enter Washington to perform business activities, even if the business does not maintain a permanent office or facility in the state.

The physical nexus standard created a bright line: businesses with no physical presence in Washington could sell goods and services to Washington customers without owing B&O tax (or sales tax collection obligations). This created competitive advantages for out-of-state sellers and, as the economy became increasingly digital, became a significant source of revenue leakage for the state.

### 4.1.2 The Evolving Standard: Click-Through Nexus and Agency Nexus

In the years leading up to the *Wayfair* decision, many states — including Washington — adopted expanded nexus theories that extended the reach of their business taxes beyond traditional physical presence:

**Click-through nexus.** If an out-of-state business had arrangements with Washington-based affiliates who referred customers through internet links (affiliate marketing), the out-of-state business could be deemed to have nexus in Washington through the click-through relationship.

**Agency nexus.** If an out-of-state business used Washington-based independent contractors who performed functions beyond mere solicitation (such as servicing accounts, installing products, or providing technical support), those activities could create nexus for the out-of-state business.

**Attributional nexus.** If an out-of-state business had a related entity (parent, subsidiary, affiliate) that maintained a physical presence in Washington, that presence could be attributed to the out-of-state entity.

These expanded nexus theories filled some of the gaps in the traditional physical presence standard but were subject to constitutional challenge under the Commerce Clause and created complex factual questions about the nature and extent of in-state activities.

## 4.2 Economic Nexus (Post-*Wayfair*)

### 4.2.1 The *South Dakota v. Wayfair* Revolution

On June 21, 2018, the U.S. Supreme Court decided *South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc.*, 585 U.S. 162 (2018), overruling the physical presence requirement of *Quill* and holding that states may tax businesses based on their "economic presence" in the state — that is, based on the volume of their sales to customers in the state, even if the business has no physical presence there.

The *Wayfair* decision was a watershed moment for state taxation. It removed the constitutional barrier that had prevented states from taxing remote sellers and opened the door for states to adopt "economic nexus" thresholds — minimum levels of sales activity that, once exceeded, create a tax obligation for the out-of-state business.

### 4.2.2 Washington's Economic Nexus Rules

Washington moved quickly to implement economic nexus in the wake of *Wayfair*. The state's current economic nexus rules, effective January 1, 2020 (after an initial implementation in 2018 with different thresholds), establish a single, unified threshold:

**$100,000 in cumulative gross receipts from Washington sales in the current or immediately preceding calendar year.**

This threshold applies to both B&O tax obligations and retail sales tax collection obligations. Key features of Washington's economic nexus rules:

**Single threshold.** Washington uses only a dollar-volume threshold ($100,000). Unlike some states that also have a transaction-count threshold (e.g., 200 separate transactions), Washington eliminated its transaction threshold effective March 15, 2019.

**Cumulative measurement.** The $100,000 threshold is measured on a cumulative basis during the calendar year. Once the threshold is crossed, the business has nexus for the remainder of that year and the entirety of the following year.

**All sales counted.** When determining whether the $100,000 threshold has been met, all sales to Washington customers are counted — including sales made through marketplace facilitators (such as Amazon, Etsy, or other platforms) where the marketplace facilitator collects and remits the sales tax. This means a business's total Washington sales can cross the $100,000 threshold even if the business does not directly collect any sales tax on marketplace-facilitated sales.

**B&O and sales tax nexus unified.** The $100,000 threshold creates nexus for both B&O tax and retail sales tax simultaneously. Prior to 2020, the B&O tax economic nexus threshold for service and wholesale activities had been $285,000 — higher than the sales tax threshold — but this was reduced to $100,000 effective January 1, 2020, unifying the two thresholds.

### 4.2.3 Practical Implications

The economic nexus rules have significantly expanded the number of businesses subject to Washington's B&O tax. Any out-of-state service provider, software company, consulting firm, or other business that generates $100,000 or more in annual revenue from Washington customers must now register for a Washington business license, file excise tax returns, and pay B&O tax on its Washington-sourced income.

For a pure service business subject to the 1.5% Tier 1 rate, $100,000 in Washington revenue would generate a B&O tax liability of $1,500 (before any small business credit). This modest tax amount — combined with the compliance costs of registering in Washington, filing returns, and maintaining records — has been a point of criticism. Some tax practitioners have argued that the economic nexus threshold should be higher for B&O tax purposes, or that there should be a de minimis exception that exempts businesses with modest Washington sales from B&O tax obligations even if they exceed the $100,000 threshold.

## 4.3 Apportionment for Multi-State Businesses

### 4.3.1 The Problem of Multi-State Activity

When a business operates in multiple states and generates income from activities that are not confined to a single state, a fundamental allocation question arises: how much of the business's income should be subject to tax in each state?

This question is particularly acute for service businesses, whose activities are often performed partly in one state and partly in another. A consulting firm based in Oregon might have employees who work from home in Washington, travel to client sites in Washington, and provide services over video conference to Washington clients from Oregon — all generating income that is potentially taxable in both states.

Apportionment is the mechanism by which a multi-state business allocates its income among the states in which it operates for purposes of state taxation. The goal of apportionment is to ensure that a business pays tax on a reasonable approximation of the income attributable to its activities in each state — neither too much (which would amount to extraterritorial taxation) nor too little (which would allow income to escape taxation by falling between two states' tax systems).

### 4.3.2 Washington's Single-Factor Receipts Apportionment

Since June 1, 2010, Washington has used **single-factor receipts apportionment** for all "apportionable activities" — primarily service income and income from the sale of intangible assets. This methodology, codified at RCW 82.04.462 and WAC 458-20-19402, works as follows:

**The formula:**

```
Washington B&O Tax Base = Total Gross Income × (Washington Receipts / Worldwide Receipts*)
```

*Worldwide Receipts is adjusted by excluding receipts attributed to states where the business is not taxable, under certain conditions (see Section 4.4 on throwout rules).

**Single factor.** Unlike the traditional three-factor formula used in many states for corporate income tax apportionment — which considers the business's property, payroll, and sales in the state — Washington uses only one factor: receipts (sales). This means that a business with 100% of its property and 100% of its employees in Washington but only 10% of its sales to Washington customers would apportion only 10% of its income to Washington.

Single-factor receipts apportionment is increasingly common among states, because it rewards businesses for locating jobs and facilities within the state (those factors are not in the formula, so adding more employees in Washington does not increase the tax base) while ensuring that businesses selling to Washington customers pay tax proportional to their Washington sales. From a policy perspective, single-factor receipts apportionment is an incentive for businesses to locate operations (employees, offices, factories) in Washington — they pay tax based on where they sell, not where they produce.

### 4.3.3 Market-Based Sourcing

The complementary question to apportionment methodology is "sourcing" — how to determine which receipts are "Washington receipts" for purposes of the apportionment formula. Washington uses **market-based sourcing**, meaning that receipts are attributed to the state where the customer receives the benefit of the service.

The specific sourcing rules for different types of services are detailed in WAC 458-20-19402 and include:

- **Services with respect to real property:** Sourced to the state where the real property is located.
- **Services with respect to tangible personal property:** Sourced to the state where the tangible property is located when the service is performed.
- **Professional services for a specific customer:** Sourced to the state where the customer receives the benefit of the service. If the customer is a business, this is generally the customer's location. If the customer is an individual, this is the individual's residence.
- **Services delivered electronically:** Sourced to the state where the customer's address is located (billing address or, for digital services, the address of the device accessing the service).
- **Receipts from intangible property (royalties, licensing):** Sourced to the state where the intangible is used.

Market-based sourcing replaced the prior "cost-of-performance" methodology in 2010. Under the old cost-of-performance method, service income was attributed to the state where the majority of the income-producing activity was performed — which typically meant the state where the service provider's employees were located. Market-based sourcing shifts the attribution to the customer's location, which significantly changes the tax landscape for businesses that have employees in one state but customers in many states.

### 4.3.4 Activities Subject to Apportionment

Not all B&O classifications are subject to apportionment. The following are "apportionable" activities:

- Service and Other Activities (the primary apportionable classification)
- Royalties (RCW 82.04.2907)
- Travel agents and tour operators
- International investment management services
- Certain financial activities

The following classifications are **not** apportioned and are instead taxed based on where the activity occurs or where the sale is completed:

- Retailing (taxed where the sale occurs, or where goods are delivered to the buyer)
- Wholesaling (taxed where the sale occurs)
- Manufacturing (taxed in the state where manufacturing occurs)
- Extracting (taxed in the state where extraction occurs)

## 4.4 Throwout and Throwback Rules

### 4.4.1 The Problem of Nowhere Income

Apportionment creates the possibility of "nowhere income" — income that is not taxed by any state because it falls through the gaps between states' tax systems. This can happen when a Washington-based service provider sells services to customers in states where the provider has no nexus and therefore no tax obligation. Under a pure market-based sourcing rule, that income would be attributed to the customer's state — but if the provider has no nexus in that state, the state cannot tax it.

### 4.4.2 Washington's Throwout Rule

Washington addresses the "nowhere income" problem through a **throwout rule** rather than a throwback rule. The distinction is important:

- **Throwback rule (used by some states for income tax):** If income would be attributed to a state where the taxpayer is not taxable, that income is "thrown back" to the state where the taxpayer is located, and taxed there. This ensures that all income is taxed somewhere but increases the tax burden in the taxpayer's home state.

- **Throwout rule (Washington's approach):** If income would be attributed to a state where the taxpayer is not taxable, that income is excluded from both the numerator and the denominator of the apportionment formula. The effect is to increase the apportionment percentage for the states where the taxpayer is taxable, spreading the tax proportionally among those states rather than concentrating it in the home state.

The throwout rule is codified in the apportionment formula itself: the denominator is "the gross income of the business from engaging in an apportionable activity everywhere in the world" minus "amounts that would be attributed under the receipts factor to a state from which the taxpayer is not taxable."

### 4.4.3 Practical Example

Consider a hypothetical consulting firm with the following revenue profile:

| Market | Revenue | Nexus? |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Washington | $3,000,000 | Yes (headquartered here) |
| Oregon | $2,000,000 | Yes (employees and office) |
| California | $4,000,000 | Yes (employees and office) |
| Texas | $1,000,000 | No (no physical or economic nexus) |
| **Total** | **$10,000,000** | |

**Without throwout:** Washington receipts factor = $3M / $10M = 30%. Washington B&O base = $10M × 30% = $3M.

**With throwout:** Texas income ($1M) is excluded from both numerator and denominator because the firm is not taxable in Texas. Washington receipts factor = $3M / ($10M − $1M) = $3M / $9M = 33.3%. Washington B&O base = $10M × 33.3% = $3.33M.

The throwout rule increases the firm's Washington B&O base by $330,000 and the resulting B&O tax by $4,950–$6,993 (depending on the applicable tier rate).

# Part 5: Filing, Administration, and Penalties

## 5.1 The My DOR Portal

### 5.1.1 Overview

The Washington Department of Revenue (DOR) administers the B&O tax through its online platform, **My DOR** (my.dor.wa.gov). My DOR is the centralized portal through which businesses:

- Register for a Washington business license (Unified Business Identifier, or UBI)
- File Combined Excise Tax Returns (which include B&O tax, retail sales tax, use tax, and other excise taxes)
- Make tax payments
- Manage their account (update business information, change filing frequency, close accounts)
- Receive official correspondence, notices, and assessments
- Request penalty waivers and respond to audits
- Access their filing history and payment records

My DOR replaced the earlier paper-based filing system and has been iteratively improved over the years. As of 2025, the vast majority of Washington businesses file their excise tax returns electronically through My DOR or through approved electronic filing services.

### 5.1.2 Registration

Any person (individual, partnership, corporation, LLC, or other legal entity) engaging in business activities in Washington must register with the Department of Revenue and obtain a UBI number before beginning operations. Registration is accomplished through the Washington State Business Licensing Service (BLS), which is coordinated through the Department of Revenue but also handles city business licenses and other regulatory registrations.

Registration triggers several obligations:

1. The DOR assigns an initial filing frequency (monthly, quarterly, or annual) based on the anticipated volume of business activity.
2. The business receives its UBI number and tax reporting account.
3. If the business will make retail sales, the DOR issues a reseller permit authorizing the business to purchase goods for resale without paying sales tax (the sales tax is instead collected from the end consumer).

Out-of-state businesses that establish nexus in Washington (either physical nexus or economic nexus) must also register, even if they have no physical location in the state. The registration process for out-of-state businesses is available through My DOR and requires designation of a registered agent in Washington for service of process.

### 5.1.3 Electronic Filing

Washington strongly encourages (and for most businesses effectively requires) electronic filing. The My DOR system allows businesses to:

- Complete and submit their Combined Excise Tax Return online
- Calculate tax liability with built-in calculations (including automatic application of the small business credit for eligible businesses)
- Make payments via Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT), credit card, or ACH debit
- Set up automatic payment arrangements for ongoing obligations
- File amended returns if corrections are needed

For larger businesses or businesses that use accounting software, the DOR supports electronic data interchange (EDI) filing, allowing returns to be transmitted directly from accounting systems without manual entry.

## 5.2 Filing Frequencies

### 5.2.1 Frequency Assignment

The Department of Revenue assigns each business a filing frequency based on the anticipated (for new businesses) or actual (for existing businesses) volume of excise tax liability:

| Filing Frequency | Typical Threshold | Due Date |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Monthly** | Estimated tax liability exceeds $4,800/month (approximately $576,000/year in gross receipts at the service rate, or approximately $1.02M/year at the retailing rate) | 25th of the following month |
| **Quarterly** | Estimated tax liability between $1,050 and $4,800/month | Last day of the month following the quarter-end (April 30, July 31, October 31, January 31) |
| **Annual** | Estimated tax liability below $1,050/month (approximately $840,000/year in gross receipts at the service rate, or approximately $2.67M/year at the retailing rate) | April 15 of the following year |

These thresholds are approximate and are adjusted periodically by the DOR. The DOR reviews business accounts periodically and may change the assigned frequency based on actual filing history. A business whose revenue grows significantly may be moved from annual to quarterly or quarterly to monthly filing.

### 5.2.2 Due Dates and Extensions

**Monthly returns** are due on the 25th of the month following the reporting period. For example, January's B&O tax return is due February 25.

**Quarterly returns** are due on the last day of the month following the quarter:
- Q1 (January–March): Due April 30
- Q2 (April–June): Due July 31
- Q3 (July–September): Due October 31
- Q4 (October–December): Due January 31

**Annual returns** are due April 15 of the following year. This due date was changed from the previous January 31 due date to align with other tax deadlines and provide businesses additional time to compile annual data.

Extensions may be available in limited circumstances but are not routine. The DOR generally expects timely filing and payment, and penalties accrue automatically for late filing or late payment (see Section 5.4).

### 5.2.3 Seasonal Businesses

Businesses with seasonal operations (e.g., agricultural businesses, tourism-related businesses, construction companies in areas with seasonal weather constraints) may be assigned filing frequencies that reflect their seasonal patterns. A seasonal business that operates only during summer months, for example, might file quarterly returns for the active quarters and report zero activity for the inactive quarters.

### 5.2.4 Active Non-Reporting Status

Businesses that temporarily cease operations or have no taxable activity for an extended period may request to be placed in "active non-reporting" status. This status suspends the filing obligation while keeping the business registration active. The business must resume filing when it recommences taxable activities. Active non-reporting status is not available for businesses that continue to have any taxable activity, even at minimal levels.

## 5.3 The Combined Excise Tax Return

### 5.3.1 Structure

Washington's approach to business tax reporting is distinctive: instead of separate returns for different taxes, most state excise taxes are reported on a single **Combined Excise Tax Return**. This return includes:

1. **Business and Occupation Tax** — Reported by classification, with separate lines for each B&O classification under which the business has taxable income.

2. **Retail Sales Tax** — Reported by location (since local sales tax rates vary by jurisdiction), with the state portion (6.5%) and applicable local portions calculated separately.

3. **Use Tax** — Tax on the use of goods purchased from out of state on which no Washington sales tax was paid.

4. **Other Excise Taxes** — Including but not limited to:
   - Public utility tax
   - Tobacco products tax
   - Spirits liter tax
   - Enhanced food fish tax
   - Various specialty taxes

### 5.3.2 B&O Tax Lines on the Return

The B&O tax section of the Combined Excise Tax Return requires the business to allocate its gross receipts among the applicable classifications and calculate the tax for each classification separately. A typical multi-classification return might include lines such as:

| Line | Classification | Gross Income | Tax Rate | Tax Due |
|------|---------------|-------------|----------|---------|
| 1 | Service and Other Activities (Tier 1) | $450,000 | 1.50% | $6,750 |
| 2 | Retailing | $200,000 | 0.471% | $942 |
| 3 | Wholesaling | $75,000 | 0.484% | $363 |
| | **Total B&O Tax** | | | **$8,055** |
| | Small Business Credit | | | ($0) |
| | MATC Credit | | | ($363) |
| | **Net B&O Tax Due** | | | **$7,692** |

In this example, the business engages in three types of activities and reports each on a separate line. The MATC credit eliminates the double taxation that would occur from both manufacturing/wholesaling and retailing the same products.

### 5.3.3 Post-HB 2081 Reporting Changes

The introduction of the three-tier Service and Other Activities rate structure under HB 2081 required changes to the Combined Excise Tax Return. Beginning with the October 2025 reporting period, businesses reporting under Service and Other Activities must select the appropriate tier based on their prior-year gross income. The return form was updated to include three separate Service and Other Activities lines:

- Service and Other Activities — Tier 1 (Less than $1,000,000 prior year) at 1.50%
- Service and Other Activities — Tier 2 ($1,000,000 to $4,999,999.99 prior year) at 1.75%
- Service and Other Activities — Tier 3 ($5,000,000 or more prior year) at 2.10%

A business selects a single tier based on its prior-year gross income (aggregated at the affiliated group level) and reports all of its Service and Other Activities income on that tier's line. The tier determination applies to the entire calendar year — a business does not split its service income among multiple tiers.

## 5.4 Penalty Schedule

### 5.4.1 Late Payment Penalties

Washington's late payment penalty structure is aggressive and escalating:

| Timing | Penalty Rate | Description |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| 1–30 days past due | 9% | Assessed on the unpaid tax balance |
| 31–60 days past due | 19% | Replaces (not added to) the 9% penalty |
| 61+ days past due | 29% | Replaces (not added to) the 19% penalty |

These penalties are not cumulative — the percentage increases as the delinquency lengthens, but only the highest applicable penalty is assessed. A tax payment that is 45 days late is subject to a 19% penalty (not 9% + 19%).

### 5.4.2 Late Filing Penalty

A separate **5% penalty** is assessed for failure to file the return by the due date, even if the tax is paid on time. The late filing penalty is in addition to (not in lieu of) the late payment penalty. A business that files its return 45 days late and pays the tax 45 days late would owe a 5% late filing penalty plus a 19% late payment penalty, for a combined penalty of 24%.

### 5.4.3 Unregistered Business Penalty

Businesses that fail to register with the Department of Revenue and are discovered through audit or other enforcement mechanisms face the most severe penalty: **39%** of the assessed tax liability, plus interest. The DOR may assess up to **seven years** of back taxes (plus the current year) on unregistered businesses, compared to the standard four-year statute of limitations for registered businesses.

This 39% penalty for unregistered businesses is a significant enforcement tool. It creates a strong incentive for businesses — including out-of-state businesses that may be unaware of their Washington nexus — to proactively register and file. The practical effect of the unregistered business penalty is illustrated by this example:

Consider an out-of-state service business that has been selling $500,000 annually to Washington customers for eight years without registering. At the Tier 1 rate of 1.5%, the annual B&O tax would be $7,500, and the seven-year assessment would be $52,500. The 39% penalty on $52,500 is $20,475, and interest at the statutory rate (approximately 5–9% depending on the period) could add another $10,000–$20,000. The total assessment could reach $80,000–$93,000 — a significant liability for a business that may have been genuinely unaware of its Washington tax obligations.

### 5.4.4 Interest

Interest accrues on unpaid tax from the original due date until the date of payment. The interest rate is set by the DOR and is adjusted periodically based on federal rates. The interest rate for 2025 is set by reference to the IRS underpayment rate, and historically has ranged from approximately 3% to 9% per annum.

Interest is not a penalty and cannot be waived. Even if the DOR grants a penalty waiver (which it may do for first-time delinquencies or for circumstances beyond the taxpayer's control), interest continues to accrue.

### 5.4.5 Penalty Waivers

The DOR has authority to waive penalties (but not interest) in certain circumstances:

- **First-time waiver.** A business with a clean compliance history may be eligible for a one-time waiver of late payment or late filing penalties. This is not automatic — the business must request the waiver and the DOR exercises discretion.
- **Reasonable cause.** Penalties may be waived if the business can demonstrate that the failure to file or pay was due to circumstances beyond its control (natural disaster, serious illness, destruction of business records, etc.).
- **Written advice.** If a business relied in good faith on written advice from the DOR that later proved to be incorrect, penalties (and in some cases, the underlying tax) may be waived.

Penalty waivers are requested through My DOR or by written correspondence to the DOR. The DOR's decision on a waiver request is itself subject to administrative review and appeal.

## 5.5 Audit Procedures and Appeals

### 5.5.1 The Audit Process

The Department of Revenue conducts audits of business taxpayers on a regular basis. Audits may be triggered by:

- **Random selection.** The DOR conducts routine audits of randomly selected businesses as part of its compliance program.
- **Risk-based selection.** The DOR uses data analytics to identify businesses whose filing patterns suggest potential underreporting, misclassification, or non-compliance. Factors that may trigger a risk-based audit include: inconsistencies between reported B&O income and reported sales tax collections; significant year-over-year changes in reported income; industry-specific risk factors; or tips and referrals.
- **Nexus discovery.** The DOR actively identifies out-of-state businesses with potential Washington nexus and may initiate contact to determine whether the business should be registered and filing.
- **Referrals.** Information from federal tax agencies (IRS), other state agencies, or third parties may trigger an audit.

A typical DOR audit follows this sequence:

1. **Notification.** The DOR sends a formal audit notification letter, identifying the tax periods to be audited and requesting specific records.
2. **Document request.** The auditor requests financial records, including general ledgers, income statements, bank statements, sales records, purchase records, contracts, and other documents relevant to the tax classifications and amounts reported.
3. **Fieldwork.** The auditor reviews the records, may conduct interviews with business personnel, and may visit the business premises (for in-state businesses).
4. **Preliminary findings.** The auditor prepares preliminary findings and shares them with the business for review and response.
5. **Assessment or no-change letter.** Based on the audit findings and the business's response, the DOR issues either an assessment (if additional tax is owed) or a no-change letter (if the audit found no deficiencies).

### 5.5.2 Statute of Limitations

The standard statute of limitations for DOR assessments is **four years** from the end of the calendar year in which the tax was incurred. For example, B&O tax for the calendar year 2025 must generally be assessed by December 31, 2029.

Exceptions to the four-year statute:

| Circumstance | Extended Period |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Unregistered business** | Seven years plus current year (potentially eight years total) |
| **Fraud or misrepresentation** | No statute of limitations (unlimited look-back) |
| **Failure to file a return** | No statute of limitations for the specific unfiled period |
| **Voluntary disclosure** | Varies; the DOR may agree to a limited look-back as part of a voluntary disclosure agreement |
| **Federal audit adjustment** | Two years from the date the federal adjustment becomes final |

### 5.5.3 Administrative Review and Appeals

A business that disagrees with a DOR assessment has several levels of administrative and judicial review:

**Step 1: Informal conference with the auditor's supervisor.** Before filing a formal appeal, the business may request an informal conference to discuss the audit findings and attempt to resolve disagreements.

**Step 2: Administrative review petition.** The business may file a petition for administrative review with the DOR's Administrative Review and Hearings Division (ARHD). The ARHD reviews the audit findings and the business's arguments and issues a formal determination. The ARHD is part of the DOR but is functionally independent from the audit division.

**Step 3: Board of Tax Appeals (BTA).** If the business disagrees with the ARHD determination, it may appeal to the Washington Board of Tax Appeals, an independent quasi-judicial agency separate from the DOR. The BTA conducts hearings (either formal or informal, depending on the amount at issue and the complexity of the case) and issues binding decisions.

**Step 4: Superior Court.** BTA decisions may be appealed to Thurston County Superior Court (or, in some cases, to the superior court in the county where the business is located).

**Step 5: Court of Appeals / Supreme Court.** Superior Court decisions may be appealed through the normal appellate channels — the Washington Court of Appeals and, on discretionary review, the Washington Supreme Court.

The appellate process can be lengthy — a contested B&O tax assessment may take 2–5 years to resolve through the full administrative and judicial review process. During this time, the assessed tax (plus interest) continues to accrue, though the business may apply for a "stay of collection" to prevent the DOR from collecting the disputed amount while the appeal is pending.

# Part 6: Deductions, Exemptions, and Credits

## 6.1 The Investment Income Deduction

### 6.1.1 The Deduction Before *Antio*

The investment income deduction, codified at RCW 82.04.4281, has historically been one of the most significant deductions available under the B&O tax. The statute allows businesses to deduct "amounts derived from investments" from their gross income for B&O tax purposes.

The policy rationale for the deduction is straightforward: a business that generates income from investments (interest on bank deposits, dividends on stock holdings, capital gains on securities) is not engaging in a "business activity" with respect to those investments in the same way it is when it sells goods or provides services. The investment income is passive — it arises from the ownership of financial assets rather than from the active conduct of business. Taxing passive investment income under the B&O tax would arguably extend the tax beyond its intended scope (taxing the "privilege of doing business") and into the realm of a pure wealth or income tax.

However, the deduction created a significant administrative and legal challenge: defining the boundary between "investment income" (deductible) and "business income" (taxable). For a bank, is interest income from loans "investment income" or "business income"? For a real estate holding company, are rental income and capital gains from property sales "investment income" or "business income"? For a private equity fund, are returns from portfolio investments "investment income" or the core business activity of the fund?

### 6.1.2 The *Antio* Decision

The Washington Supreme Court addressed this boundary question definitively in *Antio, LLC v. Washington State Department of Revenue*, decided October 24, 2024.

**Facts:** Antio LLC comprised 16 related limited liability companies whose sole purpose was to hold and manage investments. All of their income came from investments — interest, dividends, and capital gains. The LLCs claimed the full investment income deduction on all of their income, resulting in zero B&O tax liability.

**The DOR's position:** The Department of Revenue denied the deduction, arguing that for these entities, investment activity was not "incidental" to their business but was their entire business. The deduction was intended for businesses whose primary activity is something other than investing (a manufacturer that earns interest on its cash reserves, for example), not for entities whose sole purpose is investment.

**The Court's holding:** The Washington Supreme Court, in a closely watched decision, upheld the DOR's position. The court held that the investment income deduction is available only for investments that are "incidental to the main purpose of the taxpayer's business." This standard, drawn from the court's earlier decision in *O'Leary v. Department of Revenue*, limits the deduction to businesses for which investing is a secondary, ancillary, or incidental activity — not the primary activity.

**The 5% safe harbor:** Following the *Antio* decision, the DOR adopted an administrative position that investment income constituting less than 5% of a taxpayer's total worldwide gross income would be presumptively "incidental" and eligible for the deduction. Investment income at or above 5% would be presumptively non-incidental and would require the taxpayer to demonstrate that the investments were truly incidental to the business's main purpose.

### 6.1.3 HB 2081's Codification

HB 2081 codified the *Antio* standard and the 5% safe harbor into statute:

**The statutory rule:** A business may deduct investment income from its B&O tax base only if the business's total worldwide gross income from investments constitutes less than 5% of the business's total worldwide gross income.

**Above 5%:** If investment income equals or exceeds 5% of worldwide gross income, the deduction is unavailable for any investment income — not just the amount exceeding the 5% threshold. This is a cliff, not a gradual phase-out: at 4.99%, the full deduction applies; at 5.01%, no deduction applies.

**Exempted entities:** Certain types of entities are exempt from the 5% threshold and may claim the investment income deduction regardless of the proportion of their income from investments:

| Entity Type | Rationale |
|-------------|-----------|
| Nonprofit organizations | Investment income of nonprofits is typically endowment-related and does not represent commercial activity |
| Collective investment vehicles (mutual funds, REITs, etc.) | These entities exist specifically to pool investments; applying the 5% rule would be anomalous |
| Retirement accounts and pension funds | Retirement savings are public-policy-favored; taxing their investment returns would undermine retirement security |
| Family investment entities | Narrowly defined; entities meeting specific criteria for family wealth management |

### 6.1.4 Practical Impact

The codification of the *Antio* standard has significant practical implications:

**Holding companies and investment vehicles.** Entities whose primary or sole activity is holding and managing investments — holding companies, family offices, investment partnerships — can no longer claim the investment income deduction. Their investment income is fully subject to B&O tax at the applicable rate (typically Service and Other Activities, at 1.5%–2.1% depending on the tier).

**Operating companies with investment portfolios.** For operating companies that also hold investment assets, the 5% safe harbor provides clarity: if investment income stays below 5% of total worldwide gross income, the deduction is available. Most operating companies with modest investment portfolios (cash reserves earning interest, retirement plan investments, small stock portfolios) will easily remain below the 5% threshold.

**Transition-year challenges.** Companies that have historically claimed the investment income deduction on amounts exceeding the 5% threshold face a transition to full taxation of that income. The change is effective for the tax year beginning after HB 2081's enactment, requiring affected businesses to adjust their tax planning and financial projections.

## 6.2 The High Technology B&O Tax Credit

### 6.2.1 History and Expiration

Washington's High Technology B&O Tax Credit, originally enacted in 1994 (Chapter 24, Laws of 1994), provided a credit against B&O tax for qualifying research and development (R&D) expenditures in specified high-technology fields. The credit was designed to incentivize technology companies to conduct R&D activities in Washington, complementing the state's no-income-tax advantage with a direct tax reduction for innovation-oriented spending.

The qualifying fields included:

- Advanced computing
- Advanced materials
- Biotechnology
- Electronic device technology
- Environmental technology
- Pilot-scale manufacturing (for testing and refining new manufacturing processes)

The credit was calculated as a percentage of qualified R&D spending and could be applied against the taxpayer's B&O tax liability, subject to certain limitations and documentation requirements.

**The credit expired on January 1, 2015.** The Legislature did not renew it, and as of 2025, Washington does not offer a general R&D tax credit for B&O tax purposes. This is a notable gap in Washington's tax incentive landscape, particularly given that many states (including neighboring Oregon) offer R&D tax credits to encourage technology investment.

### 6.2.2 Current State: No General R&D Credit

The expiration of the High Technology B&O Tax Credit means that Washington-based technology companies conducting R&D do not receive any state-level tax credit for those activities. They may still claim the federal R&D tax credit under Internal Revenue Code Section 41, but there is no Washington counterpart.

The absence of a state R&D credit is sometimes cited as a competitive disadvantage for Washington relative to states that offer such credits (California, Massachusetts, Texas, Oregon, and many others). However, the overall tax burden comparison is complex — Washington's lack of a corporate income tax means that the starting tax burden on technology companies is lower than in most income-tax states, even without an R&D credit.

### 6.2.3 Periodic Proposals to Reinstate

The Washington Legislature has periodically considered proposals to reinstate some form of R&D tax credit, but none have been enacted since the 2015 expiration. The primary obstacle is cost: any meaningful R&D credit would reduce B&O tax revenue at a time when the Legislature is seeking additional revenue, not revenue reductions.

## 6.3 Rural County B&O Credit

### 6.3.1 Purpose

The Rural County B&O Tax Credit (RCW 82.62) is designed to incentivize job creation in Washington's rural counties — counties with lower population density and fewer economic opportunities compared to the urban Puget Sound corridor. The credit provides a direct reduction in B&O tax liability for businesses that create new, qualifying jobs in designated rural counties.

### 6.3.2 Qualifying Counties

A county qualifies as a "rural county" for purposes of this credit if it meets specific population density thresholds. As of 2025, approximately 30 of Washington's 39 counties qualify as rural counties. The qualifying counties are predominantly located in eastern Washington, the Olympic Peninsula, and other areas outside the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area.

### 6.3.3 Credit Amount

The credit is calculated on a per-employee basis for each new qualifying position created:

| Job Location | Credit per Employee per Year |
|-------------|------|
| Rural county (standard) | $2,000 |
| Rural county (community empowerment zone) | $4,000 |

To qualify, the new position must:
- Pay at least $40,000 annually (or the county average wage, whichever is lower)
- Be a full-time position (at least 35 hours per week)
- Be maintained for at least 12 months
- Represent a net new position (not a transfer or replacement)

### 6.3.4 Limitations

The credit may not exceed the taxpayer's B&O tax liability for the reporting period — it is non-refundable. Unused credits may be carried forward for one year.

The practical impact of the rural county credit is modest for most businesses, given the relatively small per-employee amounts and the geographic restrictions. However, for businesses that are expanding into rural areas — particularly manufacturing, agricultural processing, and technology companies establishing remote or satellite operations — the credit can provide meaningful tax relief during the expansion period.

## 6.4 Other Specialty Credits

### 6.4.1 Aerospace Industry Incentives

Washington maintains an extensive package of tax incentives for the aerospace industry, reflecting the state's historical and ongoing role as a major center for commercial airplane manufacturing. The aerospace incentive package includes:

**Preferential B&O tax rate.** Commercial airplane manufacturing and the manufacture of component parts for commercial airplanes are subject to a reduced B&O tax rate of 0.2904% — significantly below the standard manufacturing rate of 0.484% (0.5% from 2027). This preferential rate was extended through July 1, 2040, contingent on a significant commercial airplane manufacturing program being sited in Washington, a contingency that was met when major aircraft assembly operations continued at Washington facilities.

**Sales and use tax exemptions.** Various exemptions from retail sales tax and use tax for purchases of equipment, materials, and services used directly in the manufacture of commercial airplanes.

**Property tax exemptions.** Exemptions from property tax for certain manufacturing facilities and equipment used in commercial airplane production.

The aerospace incentives are among the most economically significant tax preferences in Washington's system, representing hundreds of millions of dollars in annual tax expenditures. They have been the subject of international trade disputes (the World Trade Organization found certain Washington aerospace tax breaks to be prohibited subsidies in the long-running Boeing-Airbus dispute) and domestic political controversy (critics argue that the incentives primarily benefit a single large corporation at the expense of the general tax base).

### 6.4.2 Semiconductor Manufacturing

Washington offers a preferential B&O tax rate of 0.275% for the manufacturing of semiconductor materials — well below the standard manufacturing rate of 0.484% (0.5% from 2027). This incentive was extended and enhanced by HB 2482 in the 2023–2024 session, conditional on a significant capital investment of $500 million or more in semiconductor manufacturing facilities in Washington.

The semiconductor incentive reflects the global competition among states (and nations) to attract semiconductor fabrication facilities, particularly in the wake of the federal CHIPS and Science Act (2022), which provides substantial federal incentives for domestic semiconductor manufacturing.

### 6.4.3 Clean Energy and Environmental Credits

Washington offers various B&O tax credits and incentives related to clean energy and environmental protection, including:

- Credits for manufacturing of solar energy systems and their components
- Credits for qualifying activities in the development and manufacturing of clean energy technologies
- Incentives for the production of biofuels and alternative fuels

These credits are periodically enacted, modified, and allowed to expire as the Legislature's policy priorities evolve.

### 6.4.4 International Services

A B&O tax exemption (or reduced rate) is available for certain international services — services performed in Washington for the benefit of customers located outside the United States. The rationale is to encourage the development of internationally competitive service industries in Washington by reducing the B&O tax burden on export-oriented service activities.

### 6.4.5 Survey of All Credits

The Department of Revenue maintains a comprehensive catalog of all currently available B&O tax credits, exemptions, deductions, and preferential rates. As of 2025, this catalog includes over 700 separate tax preferences (across all state taxes, not just B&O). The Joint Legislative Audit and Review Committee (JLARC) is responsible for reviewing these preferences periodically and reporting to the Legislature on their effectiveness and cost.

The proliferation of specialty credits and exemptions has been a recurring point of concern for fiscal policy analysts. Each credit represents a deliberate policy choice to reduce the tax burden on a specific industry, activity, or type of business — but collectively, these credits narrow the B&O tax base and shift the burden to businesses and activities that do not qualify for any special treatment. The tension between targeted incentives (which can attract or retain specific industries) and a broad, low-rate tax base (which minimizes economic distortions) is a perennial theme in Washington tax policy.

# Part 7: Comparative Analysis

## 7.1 Ohio Commercial Activity Tax (CAT)

### 7.1.1 Origins and Structure

Ohio's Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) was enacted in 2005 as part of a comprehensive restructuring of Ohio's business tax system. The CAT replaced the state's franchise tax and tangible personal property tax, both of which had been criticized as outdated and economically inefficient. The adoption of a gross receipts tax in place of an income-based business tax was a deliberate policy choice: Ohio's then-Governor Bob Taft and legislative leadership sought a tax that was simpler to administer, harder to avoid through corporate structuring, and broader in its base than the existing franchise tax.

**Rate:** The CAT rate is 0.26% on taxable gross receipts above the exclusion amount. This is a flat rate — unlike Washington's tiered structure, all businesses subject to the CAT pay the same rate regardless of their size.

**Threshold:** The exclusion amount has been increasing as part of a legislatively mandated phase-out for smaller businesses. In 2024, businesses with taxable gross receipts below $3 million were exempt. In 2025, this exclusion increased to **$6 million**. This rising threshold is gradually removing smaller businesses from the CAT altogether, effectively converting it from a broad-based gross receipts tax into a tax on larger businesses.

**Filing:** The CAT is filed quarterly (not monthly) on a calendar-year basis. Annual minimum tax payments apply for businesses in certain gross receipts brackets.

### 7.1.2 Comparison with Washington's B&O Tax

| Feature | Washington B&O | Ohio CAT |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Rate range | 0.471%–2.1% (varies by classification) | 0.26% (flat) |
| Tax base | Gross receipts, no deductions | Gross receipts, limited exclusions |
| Threshold | ~$128K (via small business credit) | $6M (2025, rising) |
| Classification system | Yes (10+ classifications, different rates) | No (single rate for all) |
| Tiered rates by size | Yes (post-HB 2081 for services) | No |
| Apportionment | Single-factor receipts (services) | Situsing based on delivery |
| Number of affected businesses | ~600,000+ | ~120,000 (declining) |

The most striking difference is the rate: Washington's service rate (1.5%–2.1%) is **6 to 8 times** higher than Ohio's CAT rate (0.26%). This differential reflects the fundamentally different roles these taxes play in their respective states' revenue systems. In Ohio, the CAT is a supplemental revenue source layered on top of a state income tax system (Ohio levies both personal income tax and a commercial activity tax but repealed its corporate franchise tax). In Washington, the B&O tax must generate revenue that income-tax states raise through their income taxes, requiring a much higher rate.

The threshold difference is also dramatic. Ohio's $6 million exclusion means that most Ohio businesses — including many substantial mid-market companies — pay no CAT at all. Washington's B&O tax, with its de facto threshold of approximately $128,000–$280,000 (via the small business credit), reaches far deeper into the small business sector.

### 7.1.3 Ohio's CAT Phase-Out Trajectory

The Ohio Legislature has been incrementally raising the CAT exclusion threshold with the apparent intention of eventually limiting the tax to only the largest businesses or phasing it out entirely. The trajectory of threshold increases suggests that within a few years, the CAT may apply only to businesses with $10 million or more in gross receipts — transforming it from a broad-based tax into a large-business surcharge.

This trajectory stands in stark contrast to Washington's 2025 reforms, which moved in the opposite direction — not only maintaining the broad base of the B&O tax but adding a graduated rate structure that increases the burden on larger businesses. The two states are pursuing fundamentally different visions of gross receipts taxation: Ohio is narrowing its gross receipts tax, while Washington is deepening and progressivizing its own.

## 7.2 Oregon Corporate Activity Tax (CAT)

### 7.2.1 Origins and Structure

Oregon enacted its Corporate Activity Tax (CAT) in 2019 (effective January 1, 2020), becoming one of the most recent states to adopt a gross receipts-style tax. The Oregon CAT was enacted to fund K-12 education, following a contentious political debate that included a failed ballot measure (Measure 97 in 2016) that would have imposed a 2.5% gross receipts tax on businesses with over $25 million in Oregon sales.

**Rate:** The Oregon CAT rate is 0.57% on taxable commercial activity above $1 million, plus a flat $250 filing fee.

**Key distinction — the 35% subtraction:** Unlike Washington's B&O tax, which allows no deduction for business expenses, the Oregon CAT allows businesses to subtract **35% of either cost of goods sold (COGS) or labor costs** (whichever is greater, but not both) from their gross receipts before applying the tax rate. This subtraction partially addresses the pyramiding problem by removing a portion of input costs from the tax base.

**Threshold:** Businesses with $750,000 or more in Oregon commercial activity must register for the CAT. Businesses with $1 million or more must file and pay.

### 7.2.2 The 35% Subtraction: A Middle Ground

Oregon's 35% subtraction for COGS or labor represents a design compromise between a pure gross receipts tax (no deductions) and a net income tax (full deduction for all expenses). By allowing a partial deduction for the largest single cost category (either materials/inventory or wages), the CAT reduces — but does not eliminate — the pyramiding effect and the disproportionate impact on low-margin businesses.

To illustrate the practical significance, consider two hypothetical Oregon businesses:

| Metric | Manufacturer | Software Company |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Oregon commercial activity | $5,000,000 | $5,000,000 |
| COGS | $3,500,000 | $200,000 |
| Labor costs | $500,000 | $3,000,000 |
| Greater of COGS or labor | $3,500,000 | $3,000,000 |
| 35% subtraction | $1,225,000 | $1,050,000 |
| Taxable amount ($5M - $1M threshold - subtraction) | $2,775,000 | $2,950,000 |
| CAT at 0.57% + $250 | $16,068 | $17,067 |

The 35% subtraction substantially narrows the gap between the effective tax rates of high-COGS businesses (manufacturers) and high-labor businesses (service companies), though it does not fully equalize them.

### 7.2.3 Comparison with Washington

| Feature | Washington B&O | Oregon CAT |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Rate | 0.471%–2.1% | 0.57% (flat) |
| COGS/labor deduction | None | 35% of greater of COGS or labor |
| Threshold | ~$128K | $1,000,000 |
| Classification system | Yes (multiple rates) | No (single rate) |
| Revenue purpose | General fund | K-12 education (dedicated) |
| Year enacted | 1933 | 2019 |

Oregon's CAT is structurally more modern than Washington's B&O tax, having been designed with the benefit of decades of economic analysis of gross receipts taxes. The 35% subtraction and the $1 million threshold address two of the most common criticisms of gross receipts taxes (pyramiding and small business burden) that Washington's B&O tax does not address. However, Oregon also levies a state corporate income tax (6.6%–7.6%), meaning the CAT is an additional layer of taxation on top of an income tax — while Washington's B&O tax is the only broad-based business tax.

## 7.3 Delaware Gross Receipts Tax

### 7.3.1 Overview

Delaware's gross receipts tax is, along with Washington's B&O tax, one of the oldest continuously operating gross receipts taxes in the United States, having been in effect since 1917. Delaware, like Washington, does not levy a state-level general sales tax — making it one of five states (along with Alaska, Montana, New Hampshire, and Oregon) without a broad-based sales tax. The gross receipts tax partially fills the role that a sales tax plays in other states.

### 7.3.2 Rate Structure

Delaware's gross receipts tax rates vary by industry, ranging from a low of approximately 0.0945% to a high of approximately 1.9914%. The rates are grouped by business activity:

| Industry Category | Approximate Rate |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| General retail (most goods) | 0.7468% |
| Restaurants | 0.6472% |

### 7.3.3 Monthly Exclusions

Delaware provides **monthly exclusions** — effectively, a standard deduction — that exempts a specified amount of gross receipts from tax each month. The exclusion amount varies by business type:

- Most retailers: $100,000/month
- Most service providers: $100,000/month
- Manufacturers and wholesalers: Higher exclusions in some cases

The monthly exclusion means that a retailer with $100,000 or less in monthly gross receipts (approximately $1.2 million annually) pays no gross receipts tax. This is more generous than Washington's small business credit, which effectively zeros out the tax at much lower income levels.

### 7.3.4 Comparison with Washington

Delaware's gross receipts tax is significantly smaller in scope and revenue impact than Washington's B&O tax. Delaware's rates are generally lower (particularly for manufacturing and wholesaling), and the monthly exclusions exempt a larger share of small business activity. However, Delaware is a much smaller state with a different economic profile (financial services, corporate domicile, chemical manufacturing) and a much smaller revenue need.

The most notable comparison point is that Delaware, like Washington, uses a gross receipts tax as a primary business tax rather than a corporate income tax. Delaware does levy a corporate income tax (8.7% flat rate), but the gross receipts tax provides a floor of taxation that applies even to unprofitable businesses — a feature shared with Washington's B&O tax.

## 7.4 Nevada Commerce Tax

### 7.4.1 Overview

Nevada enacted its Commerce Tax in 2015 (effective July 1, 2015), becoming the most recent state to adopt a gross receipts tax of any significant scope. The Commerce Tax was enacted as part of a broader education funding package championed by then-Governor Brian Sandoval, a Republican, in an unusual bipartisan effort to address Nevada's chronically underfunded K-12 education system.

### 7.4.2 Rate Structure

The Commerce Tax uses an industry-based rate structure with **26 categories** based on the North American Industry Classification System (NAICS). Rates range from 0.051% to 0.331%, with a default rate of 0.128% for businesses that do not fit into an assigned category.

### 7.4.4 Comparison with Washington

| Feature | Washington B&O | Nevada Commerce Tax |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Rate range | 0.471%–2.1% | 0.051%–0.331% |

This reflects the different revenue role of the Commerce Tax in Nevada's fiscal system: Nevada also levies a 6.85% state sales tax, gaming taxes, and a Modified Business Tax (a payroll tax), so the Commerce Tax is a supplemental revenue source rather than a primary one.

The $4 million threshold means that the Commerce Tax is, in practice, a tax on large businesses only. Small and mid-market businesses in Nevada are entirely exempt.

One interesting structural difference is Nevada's "dominant category" approach versus Washington's "split reporting" approach. In Nevada, a business that operates in multiple industry categories pays the rate corresponding to the category from which it derives the majority of its revenue. Nevada's approach is simpler to administer but less precise; Washington's approach is more precise but more complex.

## 7.5 The Federal Income Tax Baseline

### 7.5.1 The Missing Context

Any discussion of state business tax burdens is incomplete without acknowledging the federal income tax layer. All of the gross receipts taxes discussed in this study — Washington's B&O, Ohio's CAT, Oregon's CAT, Delaware's gross receipts tax, and Nevada's Commerce Tax — are imposed on top of the federal corporate income tax for businesses organized as C corporations.

**Federal corporate income tax rate:** 21% (flat rate, effective January 1, 2018, under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017).

**Pass-through entities:** For businesses organized as S corporations, partnerships, or LLCs taxed as partnerships, the business income flows through to the individual owners and is taxed at the individual federal income tax rates (10%–37%, graduated).

### 7.5.2 The "No Income Tax" Framing

When Washington is described as a "no income tax" state, this refers exclusively to the state level. All Washington businesses and residents remain fully subject to federal income taxes. A Washington C corporation with $1 million in net income pays $210,000 in federal income tax (21%) but no state income tax. A California C corporation with $1 million in net income pays $210,000 in federal income tax plus approximately $88,000 in California state income tax (8.84%), for a total of $298,000.

However, the Washington business also pays B&O tax on its gross receipts. If that business has $5 million in gross receipts, the B&O tax (at the Tier 2 rate of 1.75%) would be $87,500. The total state + federal tax burden is then $210,000 + $87,500 = $297,500 — remarkably close to the California business's total of $298,000 in this particular example.

This comparison illustrates an important but often overlooked point: **Washington's B&O tax, at its current rates, can produce a total tax burden on profitable businesses that is comparable to income-tax states' combined federal + state burden.** The difference is in how the burden is distributed — the B&O tax falls equally on profitable and unprofitable businesses (because it is measured by gross receipts), while income taxes fall only on profitable businesses (because they are measured by net income).

### 7.5.3 The Deductibility Question

State and local taxes (including the B&O tax) are deductible as ordinary business expenses for federal income tax purposes. This means that a portion of the B&O tax burden is effectively offset by a reduction in federal income tax liability. The federal tax benefit of the B&O deduction depends on the taxpayer's marginal federal tax rate:

| Entity Type | Marginal Federal Rate | Federal Savings per $1,000 of B&O Tax |
|-------------|----------------------|---------------------------------------|
| C corporation | 21% | $210 |
| Individual (37% bracket) | 37% | $370 |
| Individual (24% bracket) | 24% | $240 |
| Individual (12% bracket) | 12% | $120 |

For a C corporation, the effective after-federal-tax cost of $1,000 in B&O tax is $790 ($1,000 minus the $210 federal tax savings). This deductibility partially mitigates the burden of the B&O tax but does not eliminate it.

# Part 8: Economic and Policy Analysis

## 8.1 Pyramiding

### 8.1.1 The Concept

Tax pyramiding is the fundamental economic criticism of gross receipts taxation. Pyramiding occurs when the same economic value is taxed multiple times as it moves through the production and distribution chain. Unlike a well-designed retail sales tax — which is imposed only once, on the final sale to the end consumer — a gross receipts tax is imposed at every stage of the production chain: on the extraction of raw materials, on the manufacture of intermediate goods, on the manufacture of finished goods, on the wholesale distribution of those goods, and on their retail sale to consumers.

At each stage, the gross receipts tax is embedded in the price of the goods, which becomes part of the taxable gross receipts of the next stage. The effect is that the tax cascades — or "pyramids" — through the supply chain, with the effective tax rate on the final consumer price significantly exceeding the nominal statutory rate.

### 8.1.2 A Worked Example

Consider a simplified supply chain for a manufactured consumer product sold at retail in Washington, using the post-HB 2081 rates (effective January 1, 2027):

**Stage 1: Timber Extraction**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Revenue from selling raw timber | $100,000 |
| B&O tax (Extracting, 0.5%) | $500 |
| Price to manufacturer | $100,000 |

**Stage 2: Lumber Manufacturing**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Revenue from selling manufactured lumber | $180,000 |
| Cost of raw timber (includes $500 embedded B&O) | $100,000 |
| Value added by manufacturer | $80,000 |
| B&O tax (Manufacturing, 0.5%) | $900 |
| Cumulative B&O paid to this point | $1,400 |

**Stage 3: Furniture Manufacturing**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Revenue from selling finished furniture | $350,000 |
| Cost of lumber (includes $1,400 cumulative embedded B&O) | $180,000 |
| Value added by furniture maker | $170,000 |
| B&O tax (Manufacturing, 0.5%) | $1,750 |
| Cumulative B&O paid to this point | $3,150 |

**Stage 4: Wholesale Distribution**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Revenue from selling to retailer | $450,000 |
| Cost of furniture (includes $3,150 cumulative embedded B&O) | $350,000 |
| Value added by wholesaler | $100,000 |
| B&O tax (Wholesaling, 0.5%) | $2,250 |
| Cumulative B&O paid to this point | $5,400 |

**Stage 5: Retail Sale**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Revenue from selling to consumer | $700,000 |
| Cost of goods (includes $5,400 cumulative embedded B&O) | $450,000 |
| Value added by retailer | $250,000 |
| B&O tax (Retailing, 0.5%) | $3,500 |
| Cumulative B&O paid to this point | $8,900 |
| Retail sales tax (6.5% + local) | $66,500+ |

**Summary:**

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Final consumer price | $700,000 |
| Total value added across all stages | $700,000 |
| Total B&O tax paid across all stages | $8,900 |
| Effective B&O tax rate (cumulative B&O / final price) | **1.27%** |
| Nominal B&O tax rate (each stage) | 0.5% |
| **Pyramiding factor** | **2.54x** |

The effective B&O rate (1.27%) is 2.54 times the nominal rate (0.5%). This means the consumer is bearing a B&O tax burden that is more than double what a single-stage tax at 0.5% would produce. The pyramiding factor increases with the length of the supply chain — a product that passes through 10 stages of production and distribution would have an even higher pyramiding factor.

### 8.1.3 The Service Sector and Pyramiding

Pyramiding is typically less severe in the service sector than in goods production, because service supply chains tend to be shorter. A consulting firm providing advice directly to an end client involves only one stage of B&O taxation. However, subcontracting is common in service industries — a general contractor may subcontract to specialty firms, a technology company may use staffing agencies, a law firm may hire local counsel — and each subcontracting relationship creates an additional stage of B&O taxation.

With HB 2081's higher service rates (up to 2.1%), pyramiding in service supply chains becomes more economically significant:

| Scenario | Stages | Cumulative B&O (at 2.1%) | Effective Rate |
|----------|--------|-------------------------|----------------|
| Direct service (1 stage) | 1 | 2.1% | 2.1% |
| Subcontracted once (2 stages) | 2 | ~4.0% | ~4.0% |
| Double-subcontracted (3 stages) | 3 | ~5.8% | ~5.8% |

A service engagement that is subcontracted twice results in an effective B&O rate of nearly 6% — three times the nominal rate.

### 8.1.4 Economic Consequences of Pyramiding

The Tax Foundation and other economic research organizations have identified several systematic economic consequences of pyramiding:

**Vertical integration incentive.** Because each inter-firm transaction creates a new taxable event, businesses have a tax incentive to bring activities in-house (vertical integration) rather than purchasing them from external suppliers. A manufacturer that owns its own trucking fleet avoids the wholesaler's B&O tax that would be imposed if it used a third-party distributor. This tax-driven vertical integration can reduce economic efficiency if businesses integrate activities that would be more efficiently performed by specialized external providers.

**Supply chain shortening.** Relatedly, businesses have an incentive to shorten their supply chains — to use fewer intermediaries and fewer stages of production — to reduce the number of taxable transactions. This can distort the natural economic organization of production.

**Price distortion.** Because the embedded B&O tax varies by the length and structure of the supply chain, final consumer prices include variable and non-transparent amounts of embedded B&O tax. Two identical products can have different amounts of embedded tax depending on how they were produced and distributed. This makes it difficult for consumers to compare prices on a pre-tax basis and creates arbitrary competitive advantages for businesses with shorter supply chains.

**Anti-specialization.** Pyramiding creates a tax penalty for specialization and the division of labor. An economy with many specialized firms trading with each other (the textbook model of efficient market organization) generates more taxable transactions — and therefore more B&O tax — than an economy with fewer, larger, vertically integrated firms doing everything in-house. To the extent that the B&O tax discourages specialization, it reduces the economic gains from trade and the division of labor that are at the heart of modern economic organization.

### 8.1.5 Mitigating Factors

Several features of Washington's B&O tax partially mitigate the pyramiding problem:

**The MATC.** The Multiple Activities Tax Credit (Section 2.1.4) prevents pyramiding within a single vertically integrated business by providing a credit for manufacturing tax paid on products that are also taxed when sold at retail or wholesale.

**Low goods-producing rates.** The 0.471%–0.5% rates on manufacturing, wholesaling, and retailing are low enough that the pyramided effective rate — even through a multi-stage supply chain — remains relatively modest in absolute terms (as illustrated in the worked example above, where the effective rate was 1.27% through five stages).

**Service chains are shorter.** Service businesses, which face the highest nominal rates (1.5%–2.1%), tend to have shorter supply chains than goods producers, so the pyramiding factor is typically lower.

**The B&O tax is a deductible business expense.** For federal income tax purposes, the B&O tax is deductible, which reduces the after-tax cost of the pyramided tax.

However, these mitigating factors do not eliminate the pyramiding problem — they merely reduce its severity. Tax economists continue to identify pyramiding as the most fundamental structural flaw of the B&O tax and of gross receipts taxes generally.

## 8.2 Regressivity and Small Business Impact

### 8.2.1 The Margin Problem

The most immediate practical consequence of taxing gross receipts rather than net income is the margin problem: the B&O tax imposes a dramatically higher effective income tax rate on low-margin businesses than on high-margin businesses. This is not a side effect of the tax — it is an inherent structural feature.

Consider three hypothetical service businesses, all with $5 million in gross receipts (Tier 3, 2.1% rate post-HB 2081):

| Metric | Low Margin (5%) | Medium Margin (20%) | High Margin (50%) |
|--------|-----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Gross receipts | $5,000,000 | $5,000,000 | $5,000,000 |
| Net income | $250,000 | $1,000,000 | $2,500,000 |
| B&O tax at 2.1% | $105,000 | $105,000 | $105,000 |
| **B&O as % of net income** | **42.0%** | **10.5%** | **4.2%** |
| After-B&O net income | $145,000 | $895,000 | $2,395,000 |
| B&O tax federal deduction (21% C-corp) | $22,050 | $22,050 | $22,050 |
| **After-tax effective B&O rate** | **33.2%** | **8.3%** | **3.3%** |

The low-margin business is paying an effective income tax rate of 42% (33.2% after the federal deduction) on its net income — solely from the B&O tax, before any other taxes are considered. The high-margin business pays an effective rate of 4.2% (3.3% after deduction). This 10:1 disparity in effective rates is directly and exclusively a function of the gross receipts tax structure.

This disparity has concrete economic consequences. Industries with typically thin margins — grocery retailing (1–3% margins), construction (3–5%), staffing services (2–5%), commodity manufacturing (3–8%) — face much higher effective tax rates than industries with typically high margins — software (25–40%), professional services (15–30%), financial services (20–40%). The B&O tax is, in effect, a regressive tax across industries — it hits hardest the industries least able to absorb it.

### 8.2.2 The Small Business Dimension

While the small business credit effectively exempts the smallest businesses from B&O tax (those with under approximately $128,000–$280,000 in gross receipts), businesses just above the credit threshold face a disproportionate impact. A young, growing business that has crossed the credit threshold but is operating at thin margins (as many growing businesses do) faces a B&O tax obligation that can consume a significant share of its modest profits.

The HB 2081 tier structure partially addresses this concern by holding the Tier 1 rate unchanged at 1.5% for businesses with under $1 million in prior-year gross income. But the margin problem persists at every tier — a Tier 1 business with $500,000 in gross receipts and a 5% margin pays B&O tax equal to 30% of its net income, while a Tier 1 business with $500,000 in gross receipts and a 40% margin pays B&O tax equal to 3.75% of its net income.

### 8.2.3 The System-Level Regressivity Question

At the state tax system level (not just the B&O tax), Washington's overall tax structure is among the most regressive in the nation. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) has consistently ranked Washington's tax system as the most or second-most regressive state tax system in the United States, measured by the ratio of effective tax rates paid by the lowest-income quintile to the highest-income quintile.

The B&O tax contributes to this system-level regressivity in two ways:

1. **Direct regressivity.** The B&O tax is passed through to consumers in the form of higher prices for goods and services. Because lower-income households spend a larger share of their income on consumption (and save a smaller share), the consumption-price effect of the B&O tax is regressive across households.

2. **Revenue replacement regressivity.** The B&O tax generates revenue that, in income-tax states, would be raised through a progressive income tax (where higher-income individuals pay a higher percentage of their income). By using a gross receipts tax instead of an income tax, Washington foregoes the progressive distributional effects of income taxation and replaces them with the regressive distributional effects of consumption-embedded taxation.

The 2025 HB 2081 reforms partially address this concern through the tiered rate structure (which imposes higher rates on larger businesses, whose owners and shareholders tend to be higher-income individuals) and through the enhanced capital gains excise tax (SB 5813, which directly targets high-income investment income). But these measures are incremental adjustments to a fundamentally regressive system, not a transformation of that system.

## 8.3 Stability vs. Volatility

### 8.3.1 The Revenue Stability Argument

One of the most frequently cited advantages of gross receipts taxes — and one of the historical reasons Washington adopted the B&O tax — is revenue stability. Because gross receipts taxes are levied on total revenue rather than net income, they are less sensitive to economic downturns than income taxes.

During a recession:

- **Net income** can drop dramatically or turn negative, because fixed costs (rent, debt service, salaries under contract) continue while revenue falls. Income tax revenue therefore falls sharply — often by 20–40% or more — during recessions.
- **Gross receipts** decline during recessions, but rarely as sharply as net income. A business that loses money still has gross receipts (just not enough to cover its costs). A 10% decline in economic activity might produce a 10% decline in gross receipts but a 50% decline in net income.

This difference means that the B&O tax provides more stable revenue during economic downturns than an income tax would. Washington experienced this stability advantage during the 2008–2009 financial crisis: while income-tax states saw corporate income tax collections drop by 20–30%, Washington's B&O tax collections declined by approximately 8–12% — still a significant decline, but far less severe.

### 8.3.2 The Counterarguments

Tax scholars have raised several counterarguments to the stability advantage:

**Stability is not free.** The reason the B&O tax is more stable is that it taxes businesses even when they are unprofitable. From the state's perspective, this is a feature — reliable revenue. From the business's perspective, it is a significant burden — paying tax during the worst economic times, when cash flow is tightest and the business can least afford it. The stability of the B&O tax comes at the cost of pro-cyclical pressure on businesses: the tax is most burdensome (relative to ability to pay) during economic downturns, when businesses are struggling.

**The Tax Foundation's revenue volatility research.** The Tax Foundation has argued that gross receipts taxes are not as stable as proponents claim, because transaction volumes do decline during recessions (fewer sales, fewer contracts, fewer business-to-business transactions). While the decline is typically less severe than the decline in net income, it is not negligible — and the stability advantage must be weighed against the economic distortions (pyramiding, margin effects, vertical integration incentives) that gross receipts taxes create during both good times and bad.

**The 2020 pandemic anomaly.** During the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, Washington's B&O tax revenue initially declined sharply as broad sectors of the economy were shut down or severely restricted. However, revenue recovered more quickly than anticipated, driven in part by the surge in e-commerce and technology sector activity. The pandemic experience provided mixed evidence on the stability argument: the initial decline was sharp (contradicting the stability thesis), but the recovery was rapid (supporting the thesis that gross receipts taxes recover faster than income taxes because they capture the volume of economic activity regardless of profitability).

### 8.3.3 Long-Term Revenue Growth

Over the long term, gross receipts tax revenue tends to grow roughly in proportion to nominal economic growth (GDP growth plus inflation). This is because gross receipts — the total volume of economic transactions — is closely correlated with nominal GDP. Income tax revenue, by contrast, can grow faster than GDP during economic expansions (because profit margins expand) but slower during contractions (because margins compress).

For Washington, this long-term growth characteristic has been a double-edged sword. During periods of strong economic growth (the technology boom of the 2010s and early 2020s), B&O tax revenue grew robustly, providing the state with ample resources. But B&O tax revenue does not capture the "super-growth" of high-margin industries the way an income tax does — a technology company that doubles its revenue but triples its profits generates only double the B&O tax, not triple. This is one reason why Washington's revenue growth, while steady, has not kept pace with the explosive growth of the state's technology sector — and one reason the Legislature turned to surcharges on advanced computing companies to capture more of that growth.

## 8.4 Progressive Tax Advocacy

### 8.4.1 The Long Arc

The debate over whether Washington should adopt a state income tax is the single most persistent policy question in Washington State politics. It has been debated continuously since the 1930s — longer than virtually any other ongoing policy debate in any state. The question has been presented to voters through ballot initiatives and constitutional amendment proposals at least seven times, and has been defeated every time.

Yet the advocacy continues, driven by a fundamental critique: Washington's no-income-tax system is regressive, it produces volatile and inadequate revenue, and it places disproportionate burdens on small businesses and low-income households. The progressive case for an income tax rests on several arguments:

**Equity.** An income tax, particularly a graduated one, distributes the tax burden according to ability to pay. Washington's current system — sales taxes, property taxes, and the gross-receipts-based B&O tax — distributes the burden according to consumption and economic activity, which is inherently regressive.

**Adequacy.** Washington's revenue system has struggled to keep pace with the growing cost of state government services, particularly education, healthcare, and infrastructure. An income tax would provide access to a growing and elastic revenue base that expands naturally with economic growth and would be less susceptible to the structural limitations of the current system.

**Simplicity for businesses.** While this may seem counterintuitive, progressive advocates argue that a corporate income tax — which taxes only net income — would be simpler and less burdensome for businesses than the B&O tax, which requires businesses to pay tax regardless of profitability and creates the pyramiding and margin problems discussed in Sections 8.1 and 8.2.

### 8.4.2 The Opposition

Opposition to a state income tax in Washington is broad, deep, and well-funded. The principal arguments against:

**The ratchet effect.** Opponents argue that an income tax, once enacted, would never remain limited to high earners (as proposals like Initiative 1098 promise). The Legislature would inevitably broaden and increase the tax over time, eventually imposing an income tax on all earners — while the B&O tax, sales tax, and property tax would remain in place, not be reduced. The result would be a higher total tax burden, not a redistribution of the existing burden.

**Economic competitiveness.** Washington's no-income-tax status is a competitive advantage in attracting businesses and high-income workers. Enacting an income tax would erode this advantage and could drive business relocation to other no-income-tax states (Texas, Florida, Nevada, Tennessee).

**Constitutional concerns.** Under *Culliton v. Chase*, a graduated income tax requires a constitutional amendment (two-thirds legislative vote to place on the ballot, plus majority voter approval). This is a high procedural bar that income tax advocates have never been able to clear.

**Cultural identity.** For many Washingtonians, the absence of an income tax is a point of state identity and pride. Proposals to enact one are perceived not just as tax policy changes but as attacks on a fundamental feature of Washington's character.

### 8.4.3 The HB 2081 Compromise

The 2025 HB 2081 reform can be understood as a political compromise between these positions. Progressive legislators who favored an income tax accepted instead a restructured B&O tax with progressive features (tiered rates, size-based surcharges) that achieves some of the distributional effects of an income tax without requiring a constitutional amendment.

Business interests that opposed an income tax accepted higher B&O rates and new surcharges as the price of maintaining the fundamental no-income-tax architecture that remains Washington's principal competitive advantage.

This compromise — progressivizing the B&O tax rather than replacing it with an income tax — may represent the path of least resistance for Washington tax policy for the foreseeable future. The *Quinn* decision suggests that the courts will be receptive to creative taxation that achieves progressive distributional effects while remaining formally characterized as an excise tax. The HB 2081 tiered structure is a step in this direction, and future legislatures may push further — adding more tiers, steeper rates, additional surcharges, or even extending the tiered concept to other B&O classifications.

## 8.5 The Antio Decision and Legislative Response

### 8.5.1 The Legal Significance

The *Antio LLC v. Washington Department of Revenue* decision (October 24, 2024) is significant not only for its immediate impact on the investment income deduction (discussed in Section 6.1) but for its broader implications for the scope and purpose of the B&O tax.

At its core, the *Antio* decision addresses a fundamental question: what does it mean to be "doing business" in Washington? The B&O tax is an excise tax on the "privilege of doing business." If a company's sole activity is holding investments and collecting passive income, is it "doing business" in the relevant sense? The Washington Supreme Court's answer — yes, it is doing business, and its investment income is fully taxable because it is not "incidental" to some other primary business — effectively expands the B&O tax base to include passive investment activities that some taxpayers had previously treated as outside the tax's reach.

### 8.5.2 The Legislative Codification

HB 2081's codification of the *Antio* standard (the 5% safe harbor) represents a legislative endorsement of the court's reasoning, with the added precision of a specific numerical threshold. The codification serves several purposes:

**Legal certainty.** The 5% threshold provides clear guidance to taxpayers, reducing the fact-intensive, case-by-case analysis that would otherwise be required under the court-developed "incidental" standard.

**Revenue protection.** By codifying the narrow interpretation of the investment income deduction, the Legislature ensures that the deduction cannot be re-expanded by a future court decision or DOR administrative action without new legislation.

**Policy alignment.** The codification aligns with the overall thrust of HB 2081, which is to broaden the B&O tax base and increase rates on larger taxpayers. Limiting the investment income deduction is consistent with the Legislature's goal of ensuring that large, sophisticated businesses cannot reduce their B&O liability through aggressive use of deductions.

### 8.5.3 Ongoing Implications

The *Antio* codification will continue to have ripple effects across Washington's business landscape:

**Private equity and venture capital.** Funds domiciled in or with management operations in Washington that generate investment returns may face B&O tax on those returns, depending on the structure of the fund and the proportion of investment income relative to management fee income.

**Real estate holding companies.** Companies that hold Washington real estate primarily for investment purposes (rental income, capital appreciation) may lose access to the investment income deduction if investment income exceeds 5% of their total income — which, for a pure holding company, it almost certainly will.

**Insurance companies.** Insurance companies earn substantial investment income from their investment portfolios (premiums received are invested pending payment of claims). The interaction between the *Antio* standard and the insurance industry's investment activities is an area of ongoing analysis and potential future litigation.

**Wealth management.** Individual residents of Washington who manage their personal investments through LLCs or other entities may find those entities subject to B&O tax on their investment returns, if the entities' investment income exceeds the 5% threshold (which, for a dedicated investment entity, it almost always will).

## Appendix C: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Affiliated group** | Entities sharing common control (>80% direct or indirect power to direct management and policies). Used for tier determination under HB 2081. |
| **Apportionable activity** | A business activity whose income is subject to apportionment among states using the receipts factor formula. Primarily service activities and royalties. |
| **B&O tax** | Business and Occupation tax — Washington's gross receipts excise tax on the privilege of engaging in business. |
| **Combined Excise Tax Return** | The single return form used to report B&O tax, retail sales tax, use tax, and other state excise taxes. |
| **Economic nexus** | Tax jurisdiction based on the volume of sales into a state ($100,000 threshold in Washington), regardless of physical presence. |
| **Excise tax** | A tax on the privilege of engaging in an activity (as distinguished from a tax on property or income). The B&O tax is classified as an excise tax. |
| **Gross income of the business** | The measure of the B&O tax base for the Service and Other Activities classification — all value accruing from business transactions, without deduction. |
| **Gross proceeds of sale** | The measure of the B&O tax base for the Retailing and Wholesaling classifications — the total amount received from sales, without deduction. |
| **Gross receipts tax** | A tax imposed on total business revenue before any deduction for expenses. The B&O tax is a gross receipts tax. |
| **Market-based sourcing** | A methodology that attributes service receipts to the state where the customer receives the benefit of the service (as opposed to where the service is performed). |
| **MATC** | Multiple Activities Tax Credit — a credit that prevents double B&O taxation when a business both manufactures and sells products. |
| **My DOR** | The Department of Revenue's online portal for business tax registration, filing, and account management. |
| **Physical nexus** | Tax jurisdiction based on physical presence in a state (employees, offices, property). |
| **Prior-year gross income** | The total gross income from the immediately preceding calendar year, used to determine the applicable tier under the post-HB 2081 tiered Service rate structure. |
| **Pyramiding** | The cascading effect of a gross receipts tax through multiple stages of production, resulting in the same economic value being taxed multiple times. |
| **Single-factor receipts apportionment** | An apportionment formula that uses only the receipts (sales) factor to allocate income among states. Washington adopted this for service activities in 2010. |
| **Small business credit** | A graduated B&O tax credit that reduces or eliminates B&O liability for the smallest businesses (those with annual tax liability below specified thresholds). |
| **Throwout rule** | Washington's approach to "nowhere income" — receipts attributed to states where the taxpayer has no nexus are excluded from both the numerator and denominator of the apportionment formula. |
| **Tier** | One of three gross-income-based rate categories for the Service and Other Activities classification under HB 2081 (Tier 1: <$1M; Tier 2: $1M–$5M; Tier 3: $5M+). |
| **UBI** | Unified Business Identifier — Washington's business registration number, used for tax reporting and business licensing. |
| **Uniformity clause** | Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution, requiring that property taxes be applied at uniform rates within the same class of property. The basis for *Culliton v. Chase*. |
| **Value of products** | The measure of the B&O tax base for the Manufacturing and Extracting classifications — generally the selling price of manufactured or extracted products. |

This addendum was added in April 2026 as part of a catalog-wide enrichment

### Signing and effective dates

Governor Bob Ferguson signed both bills on **May 20, 2025**. The
Washington state 2025 legislative session produced the most substantial
tax package the state has passed in roughly a decade, and the two bills
that carry most of the revenue impact are:

- **HB 2081** — B&O tax restructuring and rate increases
- **SB 5814** — Retail sales tax expansion to services

The 2025-2027 biennium budget depends on the revenue from both bills.

**Source:** [The 2025 Washington Legislative Session: Small Business, Taxation, and Business Regulation — Washington Policy Center](https://www.washingtonpolicy.org/publications/detail/the-2025-washington-legislative-session-small-business-taxation-and-business-regulation)

### HB 2081 — B&O specifics

The concrete changes that the main body's tier framework describes at
a conceptual level:

- **Large-taxpayer surcharge (effective January 1, 2026).** A
  **0.5% surcharge** applies to all B&O tax classifications for
  businesses exceeding **$250 million of Washington-taxable revenue**.
  This is structurally similar to the advanced-computing surcharge
  but applies to a much broader taxpayer population.
- **Service and Other Activities rate increase.** Service providers
  with more than **$5 million in annual gross receipts** move from
  the old 1.75% Service & Other Activities rate to a new **2.1%**
  rate. The main body's Tier 3 definition in the glossary describes
  this threshold.
- **Advanced computing surcharge increase (effective January 1,
  2026).** The advanced computing surcharge rate rises from
  **1.22% to 7.5%**, and the surcharge cap rises from **$9 million
  to $75 million annually**. This is one of the largest single
  rate increases in Washington's B&O history and is directly
  targeted at the state's largest tech-sector taxpayers (principally
  Microsoft, Amazon, and a handful of other hyperscalers).
- **Antio codification.** HB 2081 addresses the Washington Supreme
  Court's 2024 decision in **Antio LLC v. Department of Revenue** by
  reinforcing that the B&O deduction for certain investment income
  is only applicable to investment income that is **incidental to
  the trade or business of the taxpayer**. HB 2081 defines
  "incidental" as **comprising less than 5 percent of the total
  income of the taxpayer**. The body's treatment of the investment
  income deduction should be read against this 5% threshold as the
  controlling interpretation going forward.

**Sources:** [Washington State: Significant Sales Tax and B&O Tax Bills — KPMG SALT alert, May 28, 2025](https://kpmg.com/kpmg-us/content/dam/kpmg/taxnewsflash/pdf/2025/05/washington-salt-alert-may28-2025.pdf) · [What you need to know about HB 2081 — a major B&O tax overhaul — Association of Washington Business](https://www.awb.org/what-you-need-to-know-about-hb-2081-a-major-bo-tax-overhaul/) · [How 2025 Washington state tax reform impacts businesses and taxpayers — Wipfli](https://www.wipfli.com/insights/articles/tax-how-2025-washington-state-tax-reform-impacts-businesses-and-taxpayers) · [Washington Enacts Significant B&O and Sales Tax Changes — Forvis Mazars US](https://www.forvismazars.us/forsights/2025/09/washington-enacts-significant-b-o-and-sales-tax-changes) · [Legal Update: Washington Enacts New Tax Laws — Equinox Law Group](https://equinoxbusinesslaw.com/blog/legal-update-washington-enacts-new-tax-laws-affecting-capital-gains-sales-and-bo-rates/)

### SB 5814 — the services sales tax expansion

The companion sales tax bill (covered in more detail in
`sales-tax.md`) is the revenue-heaviest piece of the 2025 package.
Starting **October 2025**, Washington's retail sales tax applies to
a substantially expanded set of digital and professional services
that had previously been exempt. The services newly subject to
sales tax include:

- **Digital and nondigital advertising services** — including
  online referrals, search engine marketing, and lead generation
  optimization. The statutory definition is broad and includes
  all services related to creating, preparing, producing, or
  disseminating advertisements.
- **IT training and support services.**
- **Custom website and custom software development services.**
- **Temporary staffing services.**
- **Live presentation services, investigation, security,
  security monitoring, and armored car services.**

SB 5814 **eliminates the "human effort" exclusion** that had
previously exempted certain digital services involving manual
input or oversight. This is a structural change that significantly
narrows the boundary between exempt and taxable services.

The projected revenue impact is **$1.1 billion over the 2025–2027
biennium**, which makes SB 5814 one of the largest revenue-raising
measures of the session. The practitioner concern — echoed in
multiple 2025 tax advisories — is that Washington-based service
providers are now subject to a tax that their out-of-state
competitors are not, creating a structural disadvantage for
Washington service businesses.

### What this means for the paper

The body of this document was written at a point where HB 2081 was
the current reference and the tier structure could be described
definitionally. With the May 20, 2025 signing and the January 1,
2026 effective date for the surcharge, the paper's B&O treatment
is now describing **operative law** rather than pending legislation.
The numbers above (the 0.5% surcharge, the $250M threshold, the
2.1% Service & Other rate for the largest tier, the 7.5% advanced
computing surcharge, the 5% Antio-codification threshold) are the
specific operating values that any practitioner using this paper
as a starting point will need to look up, and this addendum puts
them all in one place.

Similarly, the `sales-tax.md` companion file's treatment of SB
5814's service expansion should be read alongside the Moss Adams
October 2025 interim guidance, which is the single most useful
practical reference for practitioners trying to determine whether
a specific service falls under the new sales tax.
