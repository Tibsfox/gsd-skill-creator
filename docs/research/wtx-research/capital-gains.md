# Washington State Capital Gains Excise Tax

## A Doctrinal, Constitutional, and Comparative Analysis of RCW Chapter 82.87, the Quinn Litigation, the 2025 SB 5813 Surcharge, and the Interaction with Federal Section 1202 (QSBS)

*Prepared for the PNW Research Series — Taxation and Constitutional Law Cluster*

---

## Abstract

Washington is a notoriously "no income tax" state. Yet in 2021, the Legislature enacted, and in 2023 the Washington Supreme Court upheld, a tax that, in most other jurisdictions, would be recognized as nothing more than a sliver of the personal income tax base: a tax on the long-term capital gains of individual residents. The Legislature labeled it an "excise tax on the sale or exchange of capital assets," codified it in Title 82 (Excise Taxes) rather than in any hypothetical income-tax chapter, and structured its incidence around the realization event rather than around the accumulation of income. The Washington Supreme Court, in *Quinn v. State*, accepted that label. The tax survived the uniformity clause of Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution, the dormant Commerce Clause of the federal Constitution, and a cluster of ancillary challenges. The United States Supreme Court denied certiorari in January 2024.

Four years after enactment, in 2025, Governor Ferguson signed Senate Bill 5813, layering a new 2.9 percent surcharge onto long-term gains exceeding one million dollars, effective retroactively to January 1, 2025. The top marginal rate became 9.9 percent. The standard deduction, adjusted annually for inflation, rose to $278,000 for tax year 2025.

The doctrinal edifice is both novel and precarious. It rests on a linguistic distinction (excise versus property) that the 1933 *Culliton* court could not have foreseen being drawn this way. It remains in quiet conversation with federal doctrine, especially Internal Revenue Code Section 1202, which excludes qualifying gain on Qualified Small Business Stock from federal gross income altogether — and thereby, through the mechanics of the Washington base definition, from Washington's excise tax as well.

This document is an extended doctrinal analysis of the tax. It treats Washington's capital gains excise tax as an object of study: its constitutional genesis, its legislative history, its doctrinal defense in the Quinn litigation, its 2025 amendments, its interaction with the federal Internal Revenue Code, and its ongoing academic and policy controversies.

# Part 1. The Constitutional Context — Culliton and the Long No-Income-Tax Consensus

## 1.1 Why Washington Was Supposed to Be Different

For nearly a century, Washington has occupied an unusual position in American fiscal federalism: it is a densely populated, economically powerful, coastally situated state with no general personal income tax. That distinction is not a matter of legislative preference alone — it is, by the dominant reading of the Washington Constitution since 1933, a constitutional prohibition. Every attempt to enact a graduated personal income tax in Washington has run into a 1933 decision of the Washington Supreme Court, *Culliton v. Chase*, and every attempt to amend the state constitution to overturn that decision — of which there have been many — has failed at the ballot box or in the legislature.

The story of Washington's capital gains excise tax cannot be told without first telling the story of *Culliton*. The 2023 decision in *Quinn v. State* is not only a ruling about capital gains. It is, in substance, a ruling about whether *Culliton* can be distinguished — whether there is conceptual space, within a framework that forbids taxing income as "property," for a tax that is measured by capital gains but imposed on the transactional act of selling or exchanging a capital asset.

That conceptual space is the excise-tax space. Whether that space is real or illusory is the analytical question at the heart of the Washington capital gains tax.

## 1.2 The Uniformity Clause — Article VII, Section 1

The relevant constitutional text is Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution, which provides, in relevant part:

> "All taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax and shall be levied and collected for public purposes only."

On its face, this is a classical uniformity clause. It does not, on its face, prohibit income taxes. It does not, on its face, prohibit graduated taxation of anything other than property. What it says is that within any given class of property, the tax must be uniform — the same rate applied to the same class. An ad valorem property tax applied at 1 percent to one parcel and 2 percent to a neighboring identical parcel would be unconstitutional. That much is unremarkable.

The question that turned this clause into a prohibition on graduated income taxes is a narrow but consequential definitional question: Is "income" a form of "property" within the meaning of Article VII, Section 1? If income is property, then taxing it at graduated rates means taxing the same class of property non-uniformly — and the uniformity clause prohibits that. If income is *not* property, then the uniformity clause has nothing to say about income taxation, and the Legislature is free to impose graduated rates.

In 1933, in the middle of the Great Depression, amid a statewide fiscal crisis and an insurgent rural populist movement led by the Washington State Grange, the Washington Supreme Court held that income is property.

## 1.3 Initiative 69 and the Economic Context of 1932

The Great Depression devastated Washington's tax base. Washington in the early twentieth century raised most of its state and local revenue from ad valorem property taxes. That system was, by design and in practice, regressive. It placed heavy burdens on farmers, who held land as their principal asset but whose cash income had collapsed. It was also procyclical: property tax delinquencies mushroomed as property values and cash incomes fell. Washington's effective property tax collection rate, measured by the ratio of taxes actually collected to taxes assessed, fell from roughly 90 percent at the end of the 1920s to about 70 percent by 1932. Foreclosures mounted. Farmers faced loss of their land for unpaid tax bills.

Against this background, the Washington State Grange, a durable rural-populist organization with deep ties to the state's agricultural counties, organized a ballot initiative — Initiative 69 — to enact a graduated net income tax. The stated rationale was twofold: first, that the income tax would shift fiscal burden from land-poor, cash-poor farmers to professionals, corporations, and urban wage-earners who had nominal incomes even in depressed conditions; and second, that it would raise enough new revenue to stabilize county and municipal finances, which were crumbling.

Initiative 69 went to the voters in November 1932. It passed with approximately 70 percent of the vote — an overwhelming mandate by any measure. Few ballot initiatives in Washington history have approached that margin. It was not close. It was not contested. The voters of Washington, in the middle of the Depression, had voted in a landslide to enact a graduated income tax.

The Washington Supreme Court struck it down.

## 1.4 The *Culliton* Decision

*Culliton v. Chase* was decided on September 8, 1933. The case is reported at 174 Wash. 363, 25 P.2d 81 (1933). (Some secondary sources cite it at 185 Wash. 476, 53 P.2d 614, which is a different but related citation in the subsequent case history; the foundational decision is the 174 Wash. 363 report.) The vote was 5-4. The case name comes from the plaintiff, H. R. Culliton, who was a taxpayer challenging the application of the tax; the defendant, Chase, was the state official charged with administering it.

The majority opinion held that the graduated net income tax enacted by Initiative 69 was unconstitutional. The reasoning proceeded in two steps. First, the court held that "income" is "property" within the meaning of Article VII, Section 1 — a proposition that it defended by appeal to common-law definitions of property, by analogy to federal constitutional reasoning of the era (notably the United States Supreme Court's decisions in *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust*, 157 U.S. 429 (1895), which had held that a federal income tax on income from property was a direct tax and thus unconstitutional absent apportionment, and which was overturned only by the Sixteenth Amendment), and by reference to earlier Washington cases that had used the word "property" expansively.

Second, the court held that because income is property, it must be taxed uniformly — meaning that a flat tax on all income would be permissible, but a graduated tax was not, because the graduated structure by definition failed to tax the same class of property uniformly.

The critical passage, endlessly quoted in the nine decades of subsequent commentary, is this:

> "One who pays a tax on a $2,000 taxable income pays a tax on precisely the same class of property as one who pays a tax on a $1,000 taxable income, and to tax the one at a progressively higher rate than the other positively violates the other clause of the amendment, that all taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property."

The logical structure is austere. If A's income is the same *class* of property as B's income, then any rate differential is non-uniform. If the rate must be uniform, the tax must be flat. A flat income tax would satisfy the clause. A graduated income tax cannot.

The dissent — Justice Beals — disagreed principally with the first step, arguing that "income" was not "property" in the constitutional sense but rather a measure of economic activity or yield, categorically distinct from the *res* of ownership. The dissent lost, and lost 5-4.

## 1.5 The Nine-Decade Aftermath of *Culliton*

*Culliton* was not the last word on the subject. It was, however, the durable word. Every subsequent attempt to establish a progressive income tax in Washington has run into it. And every attempt to reverse it — either by constitutional amendment or by persuading the Washington Supreme Court to overrule itself — has failed.

The pattern of failed constitutional amendments is long and telling. The Washington State Grange and its allies, undeterred by the 1933 decision, put forward constitutional amendments in 1934, 1936, and 1938. All three were defeated at the polls. The Legislature put forward another amendment in 1941, which lost by approximately a 2-to-1 margin. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, during a period of renewed public interest in tax reform, additional proposals were floated; none gained traction. A 1973 Tax Reform Commission report recommended an income tax; no amendment followed. In 2010, at the tail end of the Great Recession, Initiative 1098 proposed a high-earner income tax combined with a reduction in property and business and occupation taxes. It failed decisively, with roughly 64 percent of voters rejecting it.

By one common tally, there have been between six and ten distinct constitutional amendment attempts to authorize a graduated income tax in Washington since 1933, depending on how one counts legislative referrals, voter initiatives, and other vehicles. None has succeeded. The political durability of the no-income-tax consensus in Washington is a remarkable fact of American state fiscal history — more durable than such consensuses typically are, and more durable than many commentators in the middle of the twentieth century expected.

That durability has two sources. The first is the electorate's persistent skepticism of introducing a new tax vehicle, particularly one that would be visible, individually assessed, and apparently open-ended. The second is the court's refusal to reconsider *Culliton* through the ordinary judicial mechanism of overruling. Over the decades, advocates have argued that *Culliton* was wrongly decided — that the equation of "income" with "property" was an analytical mistake of the era, that the federal constitutional reasoning it relied upon (*Pollock*) had itself been abandoned, and that the Washington Supreme Court should take a fresh look. The court has declined. The justices have, in various cases and various ways, left *Culliton* formally untouched.

## 1.6 The Excise-Tax Workaround Question

Against this background, the critical analytical question of the 2010s and early 2020s was: Could a tax on capital gains be structured not as an "income tax" — which would run into *Culliton* — but as an "excise tax" — which would not? The answer depends on whether a court would accept that the distinction between "excise" and "income" is substantive rather than merely formal. And that, in turn, depends on the traditional common-law definition of an excise tax.

Traditionally, an excise tax is a tax on a transaction, an event, an activity, or the exercise of a right. The classic examples are taxes on the sale of specific goods (tobacco, liquor, gasoline), taxes on the performance of a particular economic act (business-and-occupation taxes on gross receipts), and taxes on the exercise of particular rights or privileges (license fees). An excise tax is contrasted with a property tax, which falls on the ownership or possession of an asset, and with an income tax, which falls on the accretion of wealth regardless of whether it is realized in a particular transaction.

The conceptual question for a capital gains tax is this: Is a tax imposed on the gain realized in a sale or exchange of a capital asset properly characterized as a tax on the transaction (the sale or exchange), or is it properly characterized as a tax on the underlying income (the gain)? If it is a tax on the transaction, it is an excise tax — because it is imposed on an event, not on a state of affairs. If it is a tax on the income, it is an income tax — because it is imposed on the accretion of wealth.

The analytical insight that animated the 2021 legislation was that this is, in principle, a real distinction. The tax could be drafted and structured so as to emphasize its transactional character: so that it was imposed only when a realization event occurred, so that it was not collected on unrealized appreciation, so that it was tied to the act of sale or exchange rather than to the taxpayer's overall income position. Drafters believed — and the Legislature ultimately agreed — that a tax so structured could be characterized as an excise tax and thus could escape *Culliton's* reach.

Whether that belief was analytically sound, or whether the Washington Supreme Court would accept it, was the question left for the Quinn litigation.

# Part 2. The 2021 Enactment — Senate Bill 5096 and the Structure of RCW 82.87

## 2.1 Legislative History of SB 5096

Senate Bill 5096 was introduced in the Washington Legislature early in the 2021 session. It had been a long-discussed proposal: versions of a Washington capital gains tax had been floated for several legislative sessions before finally achieving the votes necessary for passage. The 2021 bill was sponsored in the Senate primarily by Senator June Robinson (D-Everett), with multiple co-sponsors from the Democratic majority.

The bill moved through the Legislature on narrow margins. It passed the Senate on a 25-24 vote — the slimmest possible majority in a 49-seat chamber — and passed the House by a wider but still contested margin. Governor Jay Inslee signed it into law on May 4, 2021. The act's effective date for imposition of the tax was January 1, 2022; the first tax year was 2022; the first returns were due in April 2023.

The bill was subjected to an unsuccessful attempt at referendum. Opponents sought to refer the bill to the voters under Washington's referendum mechanism, but because the bill contained an appropriation (the revenue was directed to an education legacy trust account and to school construction), it was arguably protected from referendum under the Washington Constitution's provision exempting "appropriations for the support of the state government and its existing public institutions" from the referendum power. The Washington Supreme Court ultimately addressed this procedural question as well in the Quinn litigation.

## 2.2 The Tax as Enacted

As enacted, the capital gains excise tax had these core features:

- A 7 percent excise tax on long-term capital gains.
- Applied to individuals only — not to C corporations directly, not to partnerships, not to LLCs or S corporations as entities (though pass-through gains flow to individual owners).
- Imposed on the sale or exchange of long-term capital assets.
- Computed on "Washington capital gains," defined in reference to the taxpayer's federal net long-term capital gain for the taxable year, subject to Washington-specific adjustments.
- A standard deduction of $250,000 per year, indexed for inflation annually beginning in 2023.
- Major categorical exclusions: real estate, retirement accounts, livestock used in farming, agricultural land, timber and timberlands, commercial fishing privileges, and others.
- A qualifying family-owned small business deduction for certain closely held companies.
- A charitable deduction of up to $100,000 per year (indexed).
- Revenue directed to the Education Legacy Trust Account and the Common School Construction Account.

The tax was codified as a new chapter in Title 82 of the Revised Code of Washington — Title 82 being the title governing excise taxes. This codification decision was itself an act of legal characterization: by placing the tax in Title 82 rather than in any freestanding chapter, the Legislature was signaling, in the structure of the statutory code, that the tax was to be understood as an excise tax.

Section 040 is the imposition section. Section 050 lists the categorical exemptions (items that never come into the tax base). Sections 070 and 080 handle the two principal quantitative deductions (family business and charitable). Section 150 provides the annual inflation adjustment mechanism.

## 2.4 RCW 82.87.020 — Key Definitions

The central definitional provisions deserve close reading, because much of the analytical substance of the tax — and much of the legal defensibility of its "excise" characterization — depends on them.

**"Long-term capital asset"** is defined by reference to federal law: a capital asset held for more than one year, as that concept is understood under the Internal Revenue Code. This ties the state tax base to the federal holding-period framework, avoiding duplicative Washington-only holding-period rules.

**"Washington capital gains"** is the key operative concept. It is defined as the individual's "adjusted capital gain," as modified under RCW 82.87.060, for the taxable year. The statute specifies that Washington capital gains are computed on the basis of the taxpayer's federal tax return for the year.

**"Adjusted capital gain"** is defined as federal net long-term capital gain, subject to specified Washington adjustments. Those adjustments include:

- Exempt losses (losses on excluded categories such as real estate).
- Non-Washington gains and losses (gains and losses allocated to other states under RCW 82.87.100).
- Loss carryforwards.

The critical analytical point is this: because "Washington capital gains" is computed by starting from "federal net long-term capital gain," anything that is *not in* federal net long-term capital gain to begin with is automatically not in the Washington base. This becomes the mechanical hinge on which the Section 1202 QSBS question turns — a point we return to in Part 5.

**"Individual"** is defined narrowly. Corporations, partnerships, trusts (other than grantor trusts), and other entities are generally not subject to the tax as taxpayers — only natural persons are. Pass-through entity gains are attributed to the individual owners in the year realized.

**"Sale or exchange"** is defined to include the transactions that are treated as sales or exchanges under federal tax law, incorporating federal doctrine on what constitutes a realization event. This alignment with federal realization doctrine is important for both administrative simplicity and for the tax's conceptual self-presentation as a transactional excise.

## 2.5 RCW 82.87.040 — The Imposition Provision

RCW 82.87.040 imposes the tax. As enacted in 2021 (and prior to the 2025 amendments), it provided for a single 7 percent rate on Washington capital gains. The text of the provision structures the imposition in transactional terms: the tax is imposed on the "sale or exchange" of long-term capital assets, with the tax measured by the gain. The transactional framing is deliberate. The Legislature did not write "there is hereby imposed a tax on the income from capital gains"; it wrote, in effect, "there is hereby imposed a tax on the sale or exchange of a long-term capital asset, measured by the gain realized."

This drafting choice is not mere stylistics. It is the textual vehicle for the entire excise-tax theory. If the tax falls on the act of sale or exchange, then (the theory runs) it is a tax on a transaction and hence an excise. If the tax fell, textually, on the gain itself, then it would be harder to distinguish from an income tax.

## 2.6 RCW 82.87.050 — Exemptions

The exemption provisions of the capital gains excise tax are among its most consequential features. They are categorical: certain classes of transactions and assets are entirely outside the tax base, regardless of amount. They include:

- **Real estate.** All real estate is exempt — single-family homes, multi-family residential, commercial, industrial, land, and interests in entities whose value is primarily real estate. This is by far the largest categorical exemption, and it removes from the Washington tax base the largest single source of capital gains realized by Washington residents. It is also a political decision of first importance: the exemption of real estate was, in part, a response to the political concern that the tax not reach home sales or primary residences.

- **Retirement accounts.** Transactions within qualified retirement accounts — 401(k)s, 403(b)s, IRAs, Roths, and other qualified plans — are exempt. Gains realized within these accounts do not trigger the tax, whether the account is tax-deferred or tax-free under federal law.

- **Certain livestock used in farming or ranching.** The statute exempts livestock used for breeding, dairy, draft, or similar productive purposes, in line with the agricultural exemption tradition.

- **Timber and timberlands.** This exemption recognizes Washington's timber industry and its traditional separate treatment under the state forest excise tax regime.

- **Commercial fishing privileges.** Limited-entry permits and similar commercial fishing rights are exempt, recognizing the state's fisheries industry.

- **Depreciable business assets.** Assets subject to depreciation under IRC Section 167 or expensing under Section 179 are excluded. This exempts the gains on the sale of business equipment, machinery, and similar property — that is, Section 1231 assets to a significant extent.

- **Goodwill from the sale of a franchised auto dealership.** A narrow and specific exemption, added during legislative negotiations to secure a particular vote or to accommodate a particular constituency.

- **Condemnation proceeds.** Gains realized in connection with the exercise of eminent domain or condemnation are exempt.

These exemptions are not merely accommodations; they define the scope of what the tax actually reaches. In practice, the Washington capital gains excise tax reaches primarily publicly traded securities, privately held business stock in corporations, private investment funds (to the extent they generate realization events to individual investors), and a narrow set of other long-term capital assets held by high-wealth individuals. It does not reach home sales, business equipment, farm assets, retirement accounts, timber, or fishing rights.

## 2.7 RCW 82.87.060 — Standard Deduction and Other Deductions

RCW 82.87.060 establishes the standard deduction. As originally enacted, the standard deduction was $250,000 per taxpayer per year, regardless of filing status. The standard deduction was set to be indexed for inflation beginning in 2023, under a formula specified in RCW 82.87.150.

The standard deduction is structured as a deduction, not as an exemption amount: a taxpayer whose Washington capital gains are below the standard deduction threshold owes no tax. A taxpayer with Washington capital gains above the standard deduction owes tax only on the excess. Thus the tax is, in mechanical terms, a tax on the amount by which an individual's Washington capital gains exceed the standard deduction.

The $250,000 standard deduction was a structural choice of enormous consequence. It meant that the tax would reach only a very small number of taxpayers — in practice, taxpayers in roughly the top half of one percent of the state's income distribution, and in many years a much smaller group. It also meant that the revenue would be drawn almost entirely from a narrow slice of the population with highly concentrated wealth and episodic realization events.

The standard deduction is **not** a filing-status-differentiated amount. A single filer, a married couple filing jointly, and registered domestic partners all use the same $250,000 (2022 dollars) standard deduction. There is no "married filing jointly" deduction of $500,000, for example. This choice is unusual compared to federal income-tax practice and has drawn critical comment: joint filers are effectively taxed as if they had half the deduction of otherwise-similar single filers. The Legislature's rationale was that because the tax is an excise on transactions, the taxpayer is the individual who engages in the transaction — not the marital unit — and thus the deduction properly runs per individual rather than per filing unit. Critics argue this is a rationalization of a decision made primarily for revenue reasons.

## 2.8 RCW 82.87.070 — Qualified Family-Owned Small Business Deduction

The family-business deduction is a complete deduction (not merely partial) for gains from the sale of a qualifying family-owned business. To qualify, the business must satisfy three cumulative conditions:

1. The taxpayer (or immediate family) must have held a qualifying interest in the business for at least five years immediately preceding the sale.
2. The taxpayer or family members must have "materially participated" in operating the business for at least five of the ten years immediately preceding the sale.
3. The business's worldwide gross revenue must not have exceeded a specified threshold in the trailing 12-month period. As originally enacted, that threshold was $10,000,000. It is indexed for inflation: for 2024, the inflation-adjusted threshold was approximately $10,790,000.

The material participation concept is borrowed from federal law, specifically from the Section 469 passive activity rules. An individual "materially participates" in a business if the individual is involved in the operations of the business on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis.

This deduction is **structurally distinct** from the federal QSBS regime. It is not a percentage exclusion; it is a full deduction of qualifying gains. It does not require that the business be a C corporation; an S corporation, LLC, or partnership can qualify. It does not impose the federal $50 million gross asset test; it instead uses a revenue test of approximately $10.79 million (2024) on gross revenue. It does not impose the federal active-business list of excluded industries; it relies on the material participation test and the revenue ceiling.

In practice, this deduction is narrower than federal QSBS in many dimensions (the revenue test is far more restrictive than the $50 million gross asset test) but wider in others (no C-corporation requirement, no excluded-industry list). For founders of genuinely small family-owned businesses that remain small up to the point of sale, it is often the more accessible exclusion. For founders of venture-scale companies that cross the $10.79 million revenue threshold early, it is useless — and the federal QSBS exclusion (if available) becomes the operative exclusion both federally and, via the base-definition mechanism, for Washington purposes.

## 2.9 RCW 82.87.080 — Charitable Donation Deduction

The charitable deduction provides that a taxpayer may deduct charitable contributions made to Washington-based charitable organizations during the taxable year, up to an annual cap. As originally enacted, the cap was $100,000 per year, and the deduction was available only for donations exceeding a specified floor tied to the standard deduction.

The charitable deduction is also indexed. By 2025, the floor and cap had been adjusted for inflation. For 2025, the charitable-donation mechanism operated as follows (subject to subsequent DOR guidance):

- A taxpayer may deduct charitable donations to Washington charities, but the donations must exceed $278,000 in aggregate during the tax year (the 2025 standard deduction amount).
- The total charitable deduction cannot exceed $111,000 (the 2025 inflation-adjusted cap).

The exact interaction between the charitable-deduction floor and the standard deduction is a subject of ongoing DOR interpretation. The essential policy choice was to allow charitable giving to offset the tax for high-gain taxpayers, but only for taxpayers making very large charitable commitments, and only up to a capped amount.

## 2.10 RCW 82.87.100 — Allocation and Credit for Non-Washington Gains

One of the technically most complex provisions is the allocation rule. RCW 82.87.100 allocates gains between Washington and other states for multi-state taxpayers, and provides a credit mechanism for gains that are subject to an income tax in another state.

The basic rule is that Washington-sourced long-term capital gains are those where:

- For tangible personal property, the asset was located in Washington at the time of the sale or exchange.
- For intangible property (which includes most stock), the sale or exchange is allocated to Washington if the taxpayer was domiciled in Washington at the time.

A taxpayer who realizes a capital gain on intangibles while domiciled in Washington will have that gain allocated to Washington even if the underlying entity is located elsewhere. This is consistent with the traditional "mobilia sequuntur personam" principle for intangibles.

The credit provision in RCW 82.87.100 provides a credit against Washington's capital gains excise tax for income taxes paid to another state on the same gain. This prevents the most obvious double-taxation scenarios — for example, a taxpayer who moves between Washington and another income-tax state during the tax year, or who has a gain that might be allocated to both states under conflicting rules.

# Part 3. Quinn v. State — The 2023 Decision and Its Doctrinal Architecture

## 3.1 Procedural History

The constitutional challenge to the capital gains excise tax began almost immediately after enactment. Plaintiffs — individuals and interest groups — filed suit in Douglas County Superior Court, which was chosen both for procedural reasons and because the county was seen as a friendly forum for a challenge to a tax enacted by the urban Democratic majority in Olympia. The case was captioned *Quinn v. State*, after the lead plaintiff Chris Quinn.

In the Douglas County Superior Court, the challenge was successful. In a decision issued in 2022, the trial court held that the capital gains tax was an unconstitutional income tax under Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution. The trial court reasoned that despite the Legislature's "excise tax" labeling, the tax was in economic substance a tax on income. It was measured by the gain, rather than by the value of the transaction or by the ad valorem value of the underlying property. It was owed by the individual rather than by the transaction participant as such. It was annual, rather than imposed on each transaction separately. For these reasons, the trial court concluded, it was indistinguishable from an income tax and violated *Culliton*.

The state appealed directly to the Washington Supreme Court, which took the case on direct review — bypassing the intermediate Court of Appeals. The Washington Supreme Court heard oral argument in 2022 and issued its decision on March 24, 2023.

## 3.2 The 7-2 Decision and Its Structure

The Washington Supreme Court reversed. The vote was 7-2. The majority opinion was authored by Justice Debra Stephens. The dissent was authored by Justice Sheryl Gordon McCloud, joined by Chief Justice Steven González (in the relevant section). (Justice González concurred in part and dissented in part; the precise vote breakdowns on individual issues varied somewhat across the opinions.)

The majority held that the capital gains excise tax is an excise tax, not a property tax, and therefore is not subject to the uniformity requirements of Article VII, Section 1. The majority's reasoning proceeded through several distinct analytical moves.

### 3.2.1 The Excise-versus-Property Distinction

The core holding was that the tax is "appropriately characterized as an excise because it is levied on the sale or exchange of capital assets, not on capital assets or gains themselves." The majority elaborated this in terms of a distinction between two conceptual categories of taxes:

- A **property tax** is a tax on the mere ownership or possession of an asset — a tax that is owed simply because the taxpayer holds property, regardless of whether any transaction has taken place. Ad valorem property taxes on real estate are the paradigmatic example.

- An **excise tax** is a tax on the "exercise of rights associated with property ownership" — in particular, the power to sell, exchange, transfer, use, or otherwise dispose of property. Taxes on the sale of goods, on the transfer of real property, on the exercise of a license, and on the performance of a business activity are the paradigmatic examples.

The majority held that the capital gains excise tax falls into the second category. It is not imposed on the ownership of capital assets — a taxpayer who holds appreciated stock forever, without ever selling, owes no capital gains excise tax. It is imposed only when the taxpayer exercises the right to sell or exchange the asset. That exercise — the sale or exchange — is a taxable event. The tax is, in the majority's framing, a tax on the event, measured by the gain.

The majority supported this characterization by reference to "a long line of precedent recognizing excise taxes as those levied on the exercise of rights associated with property ownership, such as the power to sell or exchange property, in contrast to property taxes levied on property itself." The precedent cited included Washington cases characterizing the real estate excise tax, the business and occupation tax, and various transactional taxes as excises.

### 3.2.2 The Distinction from *Culliton*

Because the tax was characterized as an excise rather than as a property tax, it was not subject to the uniformity clause of Article VII, Section 1. And because it was not subject to the uniformity clause, *Culliton* did not apply. The majority was careful to say — repeatedly — that it was not overruling *Culliton*. It was distinguishing it.

This is an important doctrinal move. Had the majority said "we overrule *Culliton*," it would have unleashed a wave of legislative proposals for general income taxes in Washington, and the political consequences would have been enormous. By instead distinguishing *Culliton* — holding that *Culliton* governs income taxes understood as taxes on the accretion of income as a species of property, while the capital gains excise tax is a different kind of tax altogether — the majority preserved *Culliton's* nominal force as a precedent while carving out a space in which the Legislature could impose a tax that in economic substance reaches a portion of the income-taxation territory.

The critical conceptual move is that *Culliton* addresses the *characterization* of a tax. *Culliton* held that an income tax (understood as a tax imposed on income as property) violates the uniformity clause. *Culliton* does not, on the majority's reading, hold that a transactional excise tax measured by gain runs afoul of the uniformity clause. The two are different kinds of animal. The fact that both bring revenue to the state and both are measured by reference to gains does not make them the same kind of tax for constitutional purposes.

### 3.2.3 The Dissent's Critique

The dissent pushed back on exactly this move. Justice Gordon McCloud's dissent argued, in substance, that the majority's distinction is formalistic rather than substantive. If you examine the economic incidence of the capital gains excise tax, the dissent argued, you find:

- The tax is owed by the individual taxpayer, not by the transaction participant as an entity.
- The tax is measured by the individual's aggregate gains over the year, not by each transaction separately.
- The tax is reported on an annual return that looks substantially like an income-tax return.
- The tax is progressive by virtue of the standard deduction, which effectively creates a zero bracket.
- The tax coordinates with federal income-tax reporting, drawing its base from the federal Schedule D.

On these facts, the dissent argued, the tax is in economic substance an income tax. Calling it an excise tax is a labeling exercise, not a substantive distinction. Under the longstanding principle of substance over form in tax law, the court should look past the label and recognize the tax for what it is — and then, recognizing it, strike it down under *Culliton*.

The dissent's concerns about formalism are well-founded as a matter of pure doctrine. The majority responded by noting that the substance-over-form principle cuts in both directions — a tax that looks transactional, that is triggered by a transaction, that is owed only when a transaction occurs, and that would not exist absent a transaction is, in substance, a transactional excise. The "substance" of the capital gains excise tax, on the majority's view, is that it is a tax on transactions.

Which side is correct in the formalism-versus-substance debate is the central unsettled question in Washington tax law after *Quinn*. Academic commentary has continued to divide sharply on it.

## 3.3 The Subsidiary Holdings

*Quinn* addressed a number of ancillary challenges in addition to the central uniformity-clause question. The majority rejected all of them.

### 3.3.1 The Privileges and Immunities Clause

The Washington Constitution contains a privileges and immunities clause (Article I, Section 12), which the Washington Supreme Court has interpreted to have independent force above and beyond the federal Fourteenth Amendment. The plaintiffs argued that the capital gains excise tax discriminated in violation of this clause — principally because the tax applies only to individuals and not to corporations, and because certain exclusions (real estate, retirement accounts, etc.) favor particular categories of capital over others.

The majority rejected this challenge, holding that the tax's classifications were rationally related to legitimate state interests. The individual-only application was justified by the state's existing alternative taxation of corporations (through the business and occupation tax). The categorical exclusions were justified by a range of policy rationales, including the coordination with existing real estate excise taxes and the federal tax treatment of retirement accounts.

### 3.3.2 The Federal Dormant Commerce Clause

The plaintiffs also argued that the tax violated the federal dormant Commerce Clause because of its application to gains on intangibles (such as stock) realized by Washington residents, even when the underlying entity or transaction had no connection to Washington. The argument was essentially that Washington was reaching out-of-state economic activity by taxing the realization of gains on assets with no Washington situs.

The majority rejected this. The tax is imposed on the taxpayer by virtue of Washington domicile — a traditional nexus for state taxation under the dormant Commerce Clause. The allocation rules in RCW 82.87.100 ensure that the tax does not reach gains properly allocated to other states. The credit provision addresses the residual double-taxation risk. On these grounds, the tax does not discriminate against interstate commerce and does not place a disproportionate burden on out-of-state commerce.

### 3.3.3 Referendum Exemption

The plaintiffs raised a procedural challenge based on the Washington Constitution's restriction on initiatives and referenda. They argued that SB 5096 was subject to referendum and that the Legislature had improperly shielded it from referendum by designating it as an appropriation. The majority rejected this as well, holding that the bill's revenue-allocation provisions (directing funds to education) brought it within the constitutional exemption from referendum for appropriations.

## 3.4 The United States Supreme Court Denial of Certiorari

The plaintiffs petitioned the United States Supreme Court for a writ of certiorari, seeking review principally of the federal dormant Commerce Clause question. The petition was filed in August 2023 and docketed as No. 23-171. Amicus briefs were filed on both sides. On January 16, 2024, the United States Supreme Court denied certiorari without comment, leaving the Washington Supreme Court's decision intact.

The denial of certiorari is not a decision on the merits. It does not affirm the reasoning of the Washington Supreme Court. It simply declines to hear the case. But as a practical matter, it is the end of the federal judicial road for this challenge: the constitutional status of the Washington capital gains excise tax is now settled at the state-court level, and the United States Supreme Court has declined the opportunity to second-guess that settlement.

## 3.5 Ongoing Academic and Practitioner Debate

Academic and practitioner commentary on *Quinn* has continued in the two years since the decision. The major fault lines are roughly these:

- **Defenders** of the decision argue that the excise-versus-property-tax distinction, although formalistic in appearance, reflects a real and longstanding categorical distinction in tax law. They note that many taxes — the real estate excise tax, the business and occupation tax, the federal estate tax — are understood as transactional taxes despite being measured by reference to the value of property. They argue that the Washington Supreme Court's approach in *Quinn* is consistent with how excise taxes have been understood for well over a century, and that the alternative (treating every transactional tax as an income or property tax depending on what it is measured by) would collapse the longstanding category structure of tax law.

- **Critics** argue that the *Quinn* majority effectively overruled *Culliton* without saying so, and that the decision is an exercise in judicial labeling rather than substantive constitutional interpretation. They point to the economic reality that the tax functions as a high-end income tax targeted at the investment income of the wealthy. They argue that if the Legislature can re-label an income tax as an "excise tax on the sale or exchange of capital assets" and thereby escape the uniformity clause, it could equally re-label a payroll tax as an "excise tax on the performance of labor services" — and at that point, *Culliton* would be a dead letter.

- **A middle position** holds that *Quinn* is doctrinally defensible on its own terms but that the decision has placed enormous analytical pressure on *Culliton*, such that subsequent litigation — particularly over any future income tax — will likely force the Washington Supreme Court to either formally overrule *Culliton* or to clarify the scope of the distinction drawn in *Quinn*.

Commentary from Bloomberg Tax, Tax Notes, the Tax Foundation, and various academic law reviews has explored each of these positions at length. There is no consensus. The analytical question remains live.

# Part 4. The 2025 SB 5813 Surcharge and the Retroactivity Question

## 4.1 The 2025 Legislative Context

By the 2025 legislative session, Washington faced a significant projected budget deficit — estimates in the range of $12 billion to $16 billion over the 2025-2027 biennium. Governor Bob Ferguson, newly inaugurated after succeeding Jay Inslee, and the Democratic majority in the Legislature, moved to close the deficit with a combination of spending restraint and tax increases. The tax-increase package was one of the largest in Washington state history.

Among the tax-increase measures was Senate Bill 5813 — which focused specifically on the capital gains excise tax and the state estate tax. Governor Ferguson signed the bill into law on May 20, 2025. Its provisions took effect at different dates: the capital gains provisions were retroactive to January 1, 2025 (the start of the tax year in which the bill was enacted), while the estate tax provisions took effect for deaths occurring on or after July 1, 2025.

## 4.2 The 2.9 Percent Surcharge

The central capital-gains change in SB 5813 was the addition of a new 2.9 percent surcharge on long-term capital gains in excess of $1 million per year. Combined with the existing 7 percent base rate, the surcharge produced a top marginal rate of 9.9 percent on gains above the $1 million tier.

The rate structure as of 2025 is:

| Tier | Amount | Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 0 | $0 to $278,000 (standard deduction) | 0% (exempt) |
| Tier 1 | $278,000 to $1,278,000 | 7% |
| Tier 2 | Above $1,278,000 | 9.9% |

The threshold structure is important to understand correctly. The $1 million threshold for the surcharge is measured *after* the standard deduction is applied. So the 9.9 percent rate applies only to Washington capital gains (after the standard deduction) that exceed $1 million — which means, as a dollar amount of gross gain, that the top tier begins at approximately $1,278,000 of gross Washington capital gains for tax year 2025 ($278,000 standard deduction plus $1,000,000 first-tier amount).

## 4.3 The 2025 Standard Deduction and Inflation Adjustment

The standard deduction for 2025 was set at $278,000. This is the result of the annual inflation adjustment under RCW 82.87.150, applied cumulatively from the original $250,000 figure in 2022. The 2022 standard deduction of $250,000 was adjusted to approximately $262,000 in 2023, approximately $270,000 in 2024, and $278,000 in 2025, reflecting the inflation measures applied in each intervening year.

The inflation adjustment mechanism is specified in statute and is based on Washington-specific cost indices rather than on the federal inflation factors used in the Internal Revenue Code. This means that the Washington standard deduction may drift over time relative to the federal deduction frameworks (though not directly comparable, since federal capital gains are not subject to a deduction of this type).

The annual inflation adjustment is published by the Department of Revenue each year. For 2024, the standard deduction was $270,000; for 2025, it is $278,000; the 2026 figure will be published in late 2025 or early 2026. Taxpayers should confirm the current year's figure from DOR's published guidance, not from statutory estimates.

## 4.4 The Retroactivity Provision and Its Constitutional Dimension

A distinctive and controversial feature of SB 5813 is its retroactive effective date. The bill was signed into law on May 20, 2025. Its capital gains provisions are retroactive to January 1, 2025 — which means they apply to gains realized throughout 2025, including gains realized in the five months before the bill became law. A taxpayer who closed a sale in January 2025 faced, at the time of the transaction, a top rate of 7 percent. By the time of the April 2026 filing, that taxpayer faced a top rate of 9.9 percent on the portion of the gain above $1 million. The retroactive reach meant that a taxpayer who had planned their transaction based on the legal rate in effect at the time could not avoid the subsequent increase by any ex ante planning.

Retroactive tax legislation is generally permissible under federal and state constitutional law, subject to due-process limits. The United States Supreme Court's decisions in *United States v. Carlton*, 512 U.S. 26 (1994), and earlier cases have upheld retroactive tax changes, even significant ones, against due process challenges, provided that the retroactive period is not excessively long and that the legislative purpose is rational. A retroactive effective date to the beginning of the current tax year is within the zone that federal courts have uniformly upheld.

Under Washington constitutional law, the same principle applies. The Washington Constitution does not include an explicit prohibition on retroactive legislation (beyond the general federal due process protections). The courts have upheld retroactive tax increases where the retroactive reach is to the beginning of the current tax year and the legislative purpose is rational.

Nevertheless, the retroactive element of SB 5813 has drawn significant criticism from taxpayers and practitioners. The critique is twofold: (1) the retroactive change defeats legitimate reliance interests of taxpayers who executed transactions in early 2025 based on the 7 percent rate, and (2) it creates incentives for future taxpayers to accelerate realizations to avoid speculative future rate increases, which is economically distorting. Whether these critiques would translate into a successful constitutional challenge remains uncertain; no such challenge has reached the Washington Supreme Court as of this writing.

## 4.5 The Estate Tax Changes in SB 5813

Although not directly the subject of this analysis, the estate tax changes in SB 5813 are worth noting for context. The estate tax exemption was increased from the prior $2.193 million to $3 million, with annual inflation adjustments resuming in 2026. At the same time, the top estate tax rate on estates exceeding $9 million was increased from 20 percent to 35 percent. The estate tax changes took effect for estates of decedents dying on or after July 1, 2025.

The combined effect of the capital gains surcharge and the increased estate tax rates is a substantial increase in the tax burden on high-wealth Washington residents. For an estate that would be subject to both federal estate tax and the new Washington estate tax rates, the combined state-federal marginal rate can approach 61 percent (versus approximately 52 percent under prior law, according to practitioner estimates).

# Part 5. Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202) and the Washington Base

## 5.1 The Federal QSBS Regime — Overview

Internal Revenue Code Section 1202 was added to the Code in 1993 as a targeted incentive for investment in qualified small business stock — stock of domestic C corporations that meet specified size and active-business requirements. At the time of its enactment, Section 1202 provided a 50 percent exclusion for gain on QSBS held for at least five years, with an Alternative Minimum Tax preference that partially offset the benefit. The exclusion was significantly expanded in 2010 and 2015 — first to 75 percent, then to 100 percent — and the AMT preference was removed for qualifying post-2010 stock.

As a result of these expansions, QSBS acquired between September 28, 2010, and July 4, 2025, can qualify for a **100 percent federal exclusion** on gain, subject to a cap of the greater of $10 million per issuer or 10 times the taxpayer's basis in the stock. For QSBS held at least five years and satisfying the other requirements, a founder or early investor can exclude up to $10 million of gain on each qualifying issuer entirely from federal taxation.

For QSBS issued after July 4, 2025, under the 2025 One Big Beautiful Bill Act, the cap is raised to $15 million per issuer (the 10x basis alternative remains available), the gross assets test is raised to $75 million, and a new tiered holding period structure applies: 50 percent exclusion for 3-year holding, 75 percent for 4-year holding, and 100 percent for 5-year holding. But for QSBS issued under the pre-OBBBA regime (through July 4, 2025), the 100 percent exclusion at the 5-year mark continues to apply, and pre-OBBBA stock retains its pre-OBBBA cap structure of $10 million / 10x basis.

## 5.2 QSBS Eligibility Requirements

To qualify as QSBS, stock must satisfy each of the following requirements:

### 5.2.1 Entity Type: Domestic C Corporation Only

The issuer must be a domestic C corporation. Stock issued by an S corporation, LLC (taxed as partnership or disregarded entity), partnership, or foreign corporation does not qualify. The issuer must remain a C corporation during substantially all of the shareholder's holding period and must be a C corporation at the time the stock is sold. An LLC that elects to be taxed as a C corporation qualifies if the election is effective at the time of stock issuance.

This C corporation requirement is the single most important structural requirement. It means that founders and investors who form their businesses as LLCs or S corporations — the default entity choice for many small businesses for non-tax reasons — do not have QSBS-eligible stock. To obtain QSBS benefits, the business must be formed (or converted to) a C corporation before the stock is issued.

### 5.2.2 Aggregate Gross Assets Test: $50 Million

At the time of stock issuance (and immediately after), the issuer's aggregate gross assets must not exceed $50 million (for QSBS issued before July 5, 2025; $75 million for QSBS issued after July 4, 2025). This is a one-time test, measured at the time of stock issuance. Once the corporation exceeds the threshold, any subsequently issued stock cannot qualify as QSBS — but stock issued before the threshold was exceeded retains its QSBS status. Thus a company that issues QSBS at founding, scales to billions of dollars in assets, and is sold years later can still have its founders and early investors benefit from the exclusion, as long as the original issuance satisfied the test.

Aggregate gross assets is measured by the corporation's total assets (at adjusted basis or fair market value, whichever is higher, with special rules for contributed property). It is a gross measure, not a net-of-liabilities measure.

### 5.2.3 Active Business Requirement

The issuer must be an "active business." During substantially all of the taxpayer's holding period, at least 80 percent (by value) of the issuer's assets must be used in the active conduct of a "qualified trade or business." A qualified trade or business specifically *excludes* businesses in the following categories:

- Health, law, engineering, architecture, accounting, actuarial science, performing arts, consulting, athletics, financial services, brokerage services, or any business whose principal asset is the reputation or skill of one or more of its employees;
- Banking, insurance, financing, leasing, investing, or similar businesses;
- Farming (including the business of raising or harvesting trees);
- Production or extraction of natural resources eligible for percentage depletion under IRC Sections 613 or 613A;
- Hotels, motels, restaurants, or similar hospitality businesses.

This list is designed to target the incentive at operating businesses (manufacturing, technology, services beyond professional services, consumer products) rather than at professional service firms, financial intermediaries, or capital-intensive industries with other tax preferences. The list is also expansive enough to disqualify many service businesses, which is a point of significant criticism from practitioners who argue that the professional-services exclusion unduly limits the Section 1202 incentive.

### 5.2.4 Original Issuance and Holding Period

The taxpayer must have acquired the stock at original issuance from the issuer, in exchange for money, other property (other than stock), or services. Stock acquired on a secondary market does not qualify. Stock acquired through stock-for-stock exchanges does not qualify as original issuance for the transferee (though there are special rules for Section 351 and 368 reorganizations that can preserve QSBS status).

The holding period is five years. For pre-OBBBA stock, there is no partial exclusion for shorter holding periods — it is a cliff: no exclusion before five years, 100 percent exclusion after five years. For post-OBBBA stock, the new tiered structure applies.

Certain transfers do not break the holding period and preserve QSBS status for the recipient: gifts, transfers at death, and certain transfers to partnerships (with limitations). These transfer rules are the basis for "stacking" strategies discussed below.

### 5.2.5 Eligible Holders

Only individuals and trusts (including non-grantor trusts) can directly claim the Section 1202 exclusion. A C corporation that holds QSBS cannot claim the exclusion on its own sale of the QSBS. A pass-through entity (a partnership or S corporation) can hold QSBS, and the exclusion flows through to the individual or trust owners of the pass-through, subject to additional rules.

## 5.3 QSBS Exclusion Amounts and the 10x Basis Alternative

The exclusion is capped at the *greater* of:

- $10 million of gain per taxpayer per issuer (or $15 million under OBBBA for post-2025 stock), or
- 10 times the taxpayer's aggregate adjusted basis in the QSBS sold during the tax year.

The 10x basis alternative is significant for founders who paid meaningful amounts for their stock (through capital contribution, compensation recognition, or otherwise). A founder with a $5 million basis in QSBS can exclude up to $50 million of gain on the sale, rather than being limited to $10 million. A founder with a $1 million basis can exclude up to $10 million (from the standard cap) — the basis alternative is not larger in this case.

The cap is *per taxpayer per issuer per year.* This is the linguistic formulation that enables "stacking" strategies.

## 5.4 Stacking Strategies

Because the exclusion cap runs per taxpayer per issuer, a founder who wants to exclude more than $10 million of gain on a single issuer can do so by distributing QSBS to multiple taxpayers before the sale. The most common mechanisms are:

### 5.4.1 Gifting to Family Members

A founder can gift QSBS to a spouse, children, or other family members. Each donee receives their own $10 million (or $15 million under OBBBA) cap. A founder with $30 million of expected gain on a single QSBS issuer can, by gifting portions of the stock to three family members (retaining some for themselves), enable each of the four taxpayers to claim an exclusion of up to $10 million, for a total of $40 million. The five-year holding period, however, runs from original issuance — not from the gift — for the recipient, so gifts must be made sufficiently in advance of the sale.

Gift tax implications must be considered. A transfer of significant QSBS to family members will likely exceed the annual gift tax exclusion and will require the use of lifetime gift tax exemption. For many founders, the lifetime exemption ($13.99 million per individual in 2025) is sufficient for significant QSBS gifts, but planning must be coordinated with estate planning.

### 5.4.2 Non-Grantor Trust Stacking

More sophisticated stacking uses non-grantor trusts. A founder can transfer QSBS to a non-grantor trust (a trust that is not treated as the founder's own asset for income tax purposes), and the trust receives its own $10 million exclusion cap. By creating multiple non-grantor trusts — each for the benefit of different family members — a founder can multiply the exclusion multiple times over.

The trust-stacking strategy raises complex questions about trust administration, grantor-trust rules, state trust income tax, and the "DING trust" and "NING trust" structures (Delaware or Nevada Incomplete Non-Grantor trusts) that are frequently used for high-wealth planning. It also raises questions about the federal step-transaction doctrine: if the only purpose of the trust is to multiply the exclusion, the IRS may challenge the arrangement. However, courts have generally been tolerant of QSBS stacking when the trusts have legitimate non-tax purposes (estate planning, asset protection, etc.).

### 5.4.3 Multiple Issuers

The $10 million cap runs per issuer. A founder or investor with QSBS in multiple qualifying corporations can claim a separate $10 million exclusion for each. This is an "organic" form of stacking that does not require any transfer or trust structuring — it arises naturally from having diversified QSBS holdings.

## 5.5 Interaction with the Washington Capital Gains Excise Tax

The critical question for Washington residents holding QSBS is: Does the federal exclusion under Section 1202 also exclude the gain from Washington's capital gains excise tax? The answer appears to be yes, based on the mechanical structure of the Washington tax base — though the answer comes not from any explicit Washington exclusion for QSBS, but from the definition of the Washington tax base itself.

### 5.5.1 The Mechanical Structure of the Washington Base

RCW 82.87.020 defines "Washington capital gains" by reference to "adjusted capital gain," which is in turn defined by reference to federal net long-term capital gain, subject to certain Washington-specific adjustments. The Washington adjustments do not include any add-back for gains excluded under Section 1202.

Section 1202 operates by excluding qualifying gain from **federal gross income**. The exclusion is upstream of everything: upstream of adjusted gross income, upstream of taxable income, and — critically for present purposes — upstream of "federal net long-term capital gain." Gain that is excluded under Section 1202 is not in the taxpayer's federal net long-term capital gain for the year. It never enters the federal tax base.

And because the Washington "adjusted capital gain" definition begins with federal net long-term capital gain, gain excluded under Section 1202 is not in the Washington base either. There is no Washington add-back provision. The gain simply does not appear in the Washington computation.

The result is that **QSBS gain that qualifies for the federal Section 1202 exclusion is also excluded from the Washington capital gains excise tax** — not because Washington independently excludes it, but because the gain never makes it into the Washington base in the first place.

### 5.5.2 The Absence of DOR Guidance

As of early 2026, the Washington Department of Revenue has not issued published guidance specifically addressing the interaction between Section 1202 and the capital gains excise tax. Practitioners have relied on the mechanical base definition and on informal DOR communications. No audit has publicly attacked the mechanical position, and no DOR interpretation has suggested that Washington would add back excluded QSBS gain.

The absence of DOR guidance creates some practitioner uncertainty. A cautious taxpayer would include, in their Washington capital gains return, a disclosure statement explaining that the return does not report QSBS gain because such gain is not in the federal base. A more aggressive taxpayer might simply file the return without disclosure and rely on the obvious mechanical point. Most practitioners recommend the disclosure-statement approach, at least until DOR issues formal guidance.

### 5.5.3 Pending Legislation — SB 6229 and the QSBS Add-Back Question

In the 2026 Washington legislative session, Senate Bill 6229 and its companion House Bill 2292 were introduced to require an add-back of federally excluded QSBS gain for Washington capital gains excise tax purposes. The legislation would have decoupled Washington's tax base from the federal Section 1202 exclusion, effectively making the Section 1202 exclusion a federal-only benefit. The bills did not pass in the 2026 session — they died without advancing — but their introduction signals legislative awareness of the issue and the possibility of future action.

Oregon's 2026 Senate Bill 1507, enacted in that state, took the add-back approach. Oregon's statute explicitly decouples the Oregon tax base from the Section 1202 exclusion, so that QSBS gain excluded federally must be added back for Oregon purposes. This creates a contrast with Washington's current conformity: founders and investors in Oregon pay Oregon tax on gain that would be federally excluded, while in Washington they do not. The divergence may prompt Washington to reconsider the question in future legislative sessions.

### 5.5.4 Section 1045 Rollovers

Section 1045 of the Internal Revenue Code permits a tax-free rollover of QSBS sale proceeds into replacement QSBS within 60 days of the original sale. The replacement QSBS "tacks" the holding period of the original QSBS for Section 1202 purposes, allowing the holding period to continue unbroken. Section 1045 rollovers are frequently used by early-stage founders and investors who want to exit one investment but reinvest quickly.

For Washington capital gains excise tax purposes, the treatment of Section 1045 rollovers depends on how the gain on the original QSBS sale is characterized for federal purposes. Under Section 1045, the rollover gain is not recognized federally to the extent it is reinvested in replacement QSBS within the 60-day window. Because the gain is not recognized federally, it is not in the federal net long-term capital gain base, and therefore is not in the Washington base either. The rollover thus provides the same deferral benefit for Washington purposes that it provides federally.

## 5.6 The Post-OBBBA Landscape

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, enacted by Congress in 2025 and signed into law on July 4, 2025, made several important changes to Section 1202 that apply to QSBS issued after July 4, 2025:

1. **Increased cap.** The per-issuer exclusion cap is raised from $10 million to $15 million. (The 10x basis alternative remains available.)

2. **Expanded gross assets test.** The issuer's aggregate gross assets test at the time of issuance is raised from $50 million to $75 million.

3. **Tiered holding period.** The prior all-or-nothing 5-year rule is replaced with a tiered structure: 50 percent exclusion for 3-year holding, 75 percent for 4-year holding, and 100 percent for 5-year holding.

4. **Anti-conversion rule.** Pre-OBBBA stock cannot be converted to post-OBBBA stock through stock exchanges or Section 1045 rollovers to capture the post-OBBBA enhancements. Each issuance is locked to its own regime.

5. **AMT treatment.** For the new 50 percent and 75 percent exclusion tiers, the excluded gain is not treated as a preference item for AMT purposes (unlike for pre-1993 QSBS, where the 50 percent exclusion carried an AMT preference).

The interaction of these OBBBA changes with Washington's capital gains excise tax is the same as the interaction of pre-OBBBA Section 1202: because the exclusion operates at the federal gross income level, and because Washington starts its base from federal net long-term capital gain, the excluded portion of the gain does not appear in the Washington base.

# Part 6. Washington's Own Small Business Carveout (RCW 82.87.070)

## 6.1 Comparing Washington's Family Business Deduction to Federal QSBS

Washington's own family-owned small business deduction under RCW 82.87.070 is conceptually distinct from the federal Section 1202 exclusion. The two regimes differ in several key dimensions:

| Dimension | Federal Section 1202 (pre-OBBBA) | Washington Family Business (RCW 82.87.070) |
|---|---|---|
| Entity type | Domestic C corporation only | Any entity type (C corp, S corp, LLC, partnership, sole proprietorship) |
| Size test | $50M aggregate gross assets at issuance | ~$10.79M worldwide gross revenue (2024 figure, indexed) |
| Active business list | Long list of excluded industries | No industry exclusion (subject to material participation) |
| Original issuance | Must be original issuance | Not required |
| Holding period | 5 years for original QSBS | 5 years of qualifying interest |
| Participation requirement | None | Material participation 5 of 10 years |
| Exclusion amount | Up to $10M / 10x basis | Full deduction of gain |
| Character of benefit | Exclusion from federal gross income | Deduction from Washington adjusted capital gain |

The two regimes are complementary rather than overlapping. Section 1202 is designed as a federal incentive for investment in small C corporations, with a strong preference for scalable tech and manufacturing businesses that can grow large. Washington's family business deduction is designed as a state incentive for small family-run operations that remain small.

A business could, in principle, qualify for both regimes simultaneously: a C corporation with gross assets under $50 million, gross revenue under $10.79 million, and a family that has materially participated for the required period could qualify for both the federal QSBS exclusion (on the federal return) and the Washington family business deduction (on the state return). In practice, however, the two regimes tend to apply to different sets of businesses. Venture-scale companies that qualify for QSBS are rarely small enough to satisfy Washington's revenue test and rarely have the material participation pattern that Washington requires. Genuinely small family-owned businesses that satisfy Washington's requirements are rarely structured as C corporations or rarely raise capital in a way that would provide QSBS benefits.

## 6.2 The Material Participation Test

The material participation requirement under RCW 82.87.070 is based on federal Section 469 (the passive activity rules). An individual materially participates in a business if:

- The individual participates in the business for more than 500 hours during the year; or
- The individual's participation constitutes substantially all of the participation in the business for the year; or
- The individual participates for more than 100 hours and no other individual participates more; or
- Various other specific tests set out in the Section 469 regulations.

For Washington purposes, the question is whether the taxpayer (or immediate family members) materially participated for at least 5 of the 10 years immediately preceding the sale. This is a significant commitment: it requires a demonstrated operational involvement in the business over an extended period, not merely an investment interest.

## 6.3 The Revenue Threshold and Its Annual Adjustment

The revenue threshold is set at $10 million in the original statute, with annual inflation adjustments. For 2024, the adjusted threshold was approximately $10,790,000. For 2025, the threshold is approximately $11 million (subject to DOR confirmation). The threshold is applied to worldwide gross revenue in the 12-month period preceding the sale — gross revenue, not net income, not revenue in Washington only.

For a family business that has grown close to the threshold, the timing of a sale becomes strategically important. A sale in a year when revenue is below the threshold qualifies for the deduction; a sale in a year when revenue is above the threshold does not. Taxpayers facing this situation have an incentive to accelerate or defer the sale based on the revenue trajectory.

## 6.4 The "Qualifying Interest" Requirement

To claim the deduction, the taxpayer must have held a "qualifying interest" in the business for at least five years. A qualifying interest is defined, in substance, as an ownership interest representing a meaningful stake in the business — not a trivial percentage and not a nominal interest held for tax-benefit purposes.

The holding period is measured in years, with the requirement that the interest be held for "at least five years." The holding period rules are more flexible than the federal Section 1202 original-issuance rule: a taxpayer who inherits a qualifying interest, or who acquires it through gift from a family member, can tack the prior holder's holding period. This tacking rule is important for multi-generational family businesses where the current owner may not have held their interest for five years as an individual but the family has held the business for much longer.

# Part 7. Compliance, Filing, and Record-Keeping

## 7.1 Who Must File

The Washington capital gains excise tax is a filing-required tax: taxpayers who owe any tax under the regime must file a return, and only those taxpayers are required to file. An individual with Washington capital gains below the standard deduction for the year has no tax liability and no filing obligation.

A return must be filed by any individual who has Washington capital gains (after all applicable deductions other than the standard deduction) in excess of the standard deduction for the year. For 2025, that means individuals with Washington capital gains in excess of $278,000.

Filing status is individual. The Washington capital gains tax is **not** jointly filed. Unlike the federal income tax (where married couples can elect to file jointly, with the standard deduction, rate brackets, and credits scaled accordingly), the Washington tax treats each individual as a separate taxpayer. This has important implications for married couples:

- A married couple both with significant capital gains files two separate Washington returns.
- Each spouse receives their own $278,000 standard deduction.
- A couple with $400,000 of gain realized equally by each spouse owes no Washington tax (because each spouse has $200,000 of Washington capital gains, below the $278,000 threshold).
- A couple with $400,000 of gain realized entirely by one spouse owes Washington tax on $122,000 ($400,000 minus $278,000) at 7 percent.

The allocation of gain between spouses is governed by Washington community property principles for married residents. Washington is a community property state, so gain on community property is generally allocated 50/50 between spouses, regardless of whose name is on the title. This default allocation is taxpayer-friendly in many situations (because it effectively doubles the standard deduction for married couples) but requires careful attention to separate property versus community property distinctions.

## 7.2 Filing Mechanics and Due Dates

Returns must be filed electronically through the Washington Department of Revenue's online filing system. Paper filing is not permitted under current DOR practice.

The due date for the Washington capital gains excise tax return is the same as the federal income tax return due date — generally April 15 of the year following the tax year. For taxpayers who receive a federal extension, Washington grants a corresponding extension. Extensions are limited to six months (to October 15).

The filer must attach (or at least reference) their federal Schedule D for the year. Washington's system accepts the federal Schedule D data as the starting point for the Washington computation, and then applies the Washington-specific adjustments (exempt losses, non-Washington allocation, carryforwards, deductions).

Payment of tax is due on the original due date (April 15) regardless of any extension. An extension of time to file does not extend the time to pay, and interest and penalties accrue on unpaid tax from the April 15 due date.

## 7.3 Record-Keeping Requirements

The record-keeping requirements for the Washington capital gains excise tax track federal income tax record-keeping requirements for capital gains:

- Documentation of basis (cost, including acquisition costs, improvements, and other additions to basis).
- Documentation of holding period (acquisition date and sale date).
- Documentation of exclusion eligibility (for gains that are claimed to be excluded, such as QSBS gains or family business gains).
- Documentation of Washington allocation (for gains that are allocated to other states under RCW 82.87.100).
- Documentation of credits claimed (for income taxes paid to other states on the same gain).

DOR has the authority to audit returns, and taxpayers must be able to produce supporting documentation on request. The statute of limitations for DOR audits is generally four years from the filing of the return, with extensions available for certain circumstances.

## 7.4 The Coordination with Federal Schedule D

The practical reality of filing the Washington capital gains excise tax is that it is a derivative computation from the federal return. The taxpayer first computes their federal capital gains on Schedule D, then takes the federal net long-term capital gain figure and runs it through the Washington adjustments and deductions to arrive at the Washington tax. The entire workflow is designed to minimize independent computation — the federal return is the primary document, and the Washington return is a follow-on.

This has two important practical implications:

1. **Consistency.** A taxpayer's Washington return is only as reliable as their federal return. Errors in the federal Schedule D translate directly into Washington errors.

2. **Timing.** Because the Washington return depends on the federal return, taxpayers typically complete the federal return first. This works well for most taxpayers but creates compression during the January-April tax preparation period for taxpayers with complex capital gains situations.

## 7.5 Brokers and Form 1099-B Reporting

RCW 82.87.170 requires brokers and barter exchanges that issue Form 1099-B to Washington residents to also submit the forms to the Washington Department of Revenue. This is a third-party reporting regime that parallels federal Form 1099-B reporting to the IRS. It provides DOR with independent information about capital gains transactions realized by Washington residents, enabling cross-verification against filed returns.

The broker reporting requirement went into effect with the capital gains excise tax. It applies to any broker that issues a Form 1099-B to a Washington resident for a transaction occurring during the tax year. DOR maintains data on the matching of reported broker transactions to filed returns, and discrepancies can trigger audit activity.

## 7.6 Penalties and Enforcement

The statutory penalty regime for the Washington capital gains excise tax includes:

- **Late filing penalty.** A percentage of the tax owed for failure to file on time.
- **Late payment penalty.** A percentage of the unpaid tax for failure to pay on time.
- **Accuracy-related penalties.** Penalties for substantial understatements of tax due to negligence or disregard of regulations.
- **Fraud penalties.** More severe penalties for fraudulent returns or willful evasion.
- **Criminal penalties.** RCW 82.87.140 provides for criminal penalties for willful violations.

Interest on unpaid tax accrues from the original due date at the statutory rate (which varies). Interest and penalties are separate charges that compound the cost of non-compliance.

DOR's enforcement approach has been measured in the early years of the tax. DOR has focused on large filers with clear discrepancies, using 1099-B matching and other third-party data to identify non-filers and underreporters. Criminal prosecutions have been rare to date, consistent with DOR's general approach of using audit and civil penalties as the primary enforcement tools.

# Part 8. Distributional and Policy Analysis

## 8.1 Who Pays — The Distributional Picture

Washington's capital gains excise tax is among the most progressively distributed taxes in the American state tax system. The concentration of the tax burden reflects the concentration of capital gains realizations in the underlying economy: in the United States generally, and in Washington specifically, realized capital gains are heavily concentrated among the top one percent of the income distribution. By some estimates, the top 1 percent of earners account for 60 to 75 percent of all realized long-term capital gains in a typical year.

For Washington specifically, the $278,000 standard deduction and the narrow tax base (excluding real estate and retirement accounts) mean that only a small number of taxpayers owe the tax in any given year. Estimates from the Department of Revenue and independent analysts suggest that the number of taxpayers affected in each year is on the order of several thousand — perhaps 3,000 to 5,000 taxpayers in a typical year, depending on market conditions. Fewer than 8,200 households are estimated to be potentially affected by the tax in any given year; the number actually owing tax is smaller because many of those households have sub-threshold gains.

Within that affected population, the tax burden is further concentrated. Department of Revenue data on tax year 2022 filings showed that:

- Approximately 4,461 returns were filed.
- The top end of the distribution — fewer than 400 returns — accounted for the vast majority of revenue.
- Among those top returns, 333 payments were $240,000 or more — representing approximately $778 million in revenue from this small group alone.

This degree of concentration is striking. A tax that collects most of its revenue from a few hundred taxpayers is a fundamentally different instrument from a broad-based income tax that collects modest amounts from millions of taxpayers. It has different revenue dynamics, different political economy, and different distributional characteristics.

## 8.2 Revenue Dynamics — Collections by Year

The first four years of the tax provide initial data on its revenue performance:

| Tax Year | Collections | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 2022 | ~$840.3 million | First year of imposition; collected in 2023 |
| 2023 | ~$418.6 million | Significant drop from first year |
| 2024 | ~$560.6 million | Partial recovery |
| 2025 | (TBD) | First year with 9.9% top rate |

The 2022 collections of approximately $840 million significantly exceeded pre-enactment forecasts. This was partly attributable to the strong equity market performance of 2021 and early 2022, which generated a large number of realization events. It was also attributable to a certain amount of front-loading: taxpayers who anticipated higher future rates or constitutional uncertainty may have accelerated realizations into 2022, compressing what would otherwise have been multi-year realizations into the first year.

The 2023 collections of approximately $418 million were a sharp drop from 2022. This drop was attributable to the significant market correction in late 2022 and 2023, which reduced the number of realization events and also reduced the magnitude of gains. The Department of Revenue analysis at the time emphasized that the drop was market-driven rather than driven by any mass taxpayer migration or avoidance.

The 2024 collections of approximately $560 million represented a partial recovery, reflecting the strong market performance of 2023 and the first half of 2024. The figure is still substantially below the 2022 peak, consistent with the general pattern of capital gains tax revenue being highly market-dependent.

The 2025 collections (the first year under the 9.9 percent top rate) are not yet known as of early 2026. They will depend on the combination of market performance during 2025 and the behavioral response to the surcharge.

## 8.3 Revenue Volatility

The revenue volatility of the capital gains excise tax has been, from its enactment, one of its most salient criticisms. Tax Foundation, Washington Research Council, and other analysts warned before enactment that capital gains taxes are among the most volatile state revenue sources, subject to wild swings from one year to the next based on market conditions, investor behavior, and realization-timing decisions.

The early data from Washington confirms this concern. A tax that collects $840 million in year one and $418 million in year two has a year-over-year volatility of roughly 50 percent. Few other state revenue sources exhibit such volatility. Sales taxes vary with consumer spending but generally within a narrower band. Property taxes are remarkably stable. Business and occupation taxes have some cyclical sensitivity but nothing like capital gains.

The volatility problem is compounded by the fact that revenue has been directly pledged to education — to the Education Legacy Trust Account and the Common School Construction Account. Education funding commitments are long-term and steady; capital gains revenue is neither. Reliance on capital gains to fund education creates an acute budgeting challenge: if market conditions deteriorate, education funding is at immediate risk, or the state must make up the shortfall from other sources. Washington's experience in 2023, when the collection drop of $420 million required budget adjustments, is a preview of the tensions that will recur.

Some analysts have proposed smoothing mechanisms — a multi-year moving average of collections, with a reserve fund to buffer year-to-year variation — but no such mechanism is currently in place. The revenue flows as it is collected, year by year.

## 8.4 The Tax Flight Question

A persistent debate about the Washington capital gains excise tax is whether it drives wealthy residents to relocate to no-income-tax and no-capital-gains-tax states. Nevada, Texas, Florida, and Wyoming are the commonly cited alternatives. The argument, in its simplest form, is that a taxpayer facing a 9.9 percent Washington state tax on a large capital gain has a strong incentive to establish domicile elsewhere before the realization event. For a $100 million gain, the Washington tax at the 9.9 percent top rate is approximately $9.9 million. For taxpayers who can credibly relocate, that is a meaningful sum.

The counter-argument is that domicile changes for tax purposes are costly and difficult. Washington has a high quality of life for high-wealth residents — particularly in the Puget Sound region. Many wealthy Washington residents have strong personal, family, professional, and investment ties to Washington that cannot easily be replicated elsewhere. A domicile change is not merely a paperwork matter: it requires actual residence elsewhere, severance of Washington ties, and careful attention to state tax rules on audit. The transaction costs are high, and they are front-loaded: a taxpayer must endure them before realizing the expected benefit.

The empirical evidence, such as it is, is thin and contested. The DOR reported in 2024 that the revenue drop from 2022 to 2023 was attributable to market conditions rather than to migration. Critics argued that even if gross migration effects are modest, there is a selection effect on who migrates: those who do leave are those with the largest expected gains, and their departure has an outsized revenue impact. Supporters noted that similar tax-flight concerns have been raised about income taxes in California and New York for decades, and that while there is some real tax-motivated migration, the magnitude is generally smaller than critics predict.

A 2024 Washington State Standard analysis of the revenue data concluded that the 2023 drop was attributable to market volatility rather than to migration, but acknowledged that the multi-year pattern would need to be observed before any firm conclusion could be drawn. The 2025 data (now available) will provide a further data point.

More recent academic work on state tax migration (for example, work using tax return address changes as a proxy for domicile migration) has generally found that high-earner migration is more responsive to state taxation than low-earner migration, but that the overall migration elasticity is modest — perhaps on the order of 1 to 2 percentage points per percentage point of tax rate differential. Applied to Washington, this would suggest that the capital gains excise tax might drive a measurable but modest amount of out-migration, not a flood.

## 8.5 The Education Funding Commitment

The revenue from the capital gains excise tax is earmarked, by statute, for education. Specifically, under RCW 82.87.030:

- The first $500 million (adjusted for inflation each year — approximately $524 million in recent years) is deposited into the Education Legacy Trust Account, which supports various education programs.
- Any remaining revenue is deposited into the Common School Construction Account, which supports construction and modernization of public schools.

This earmarking was, in part, a political design decision: it made the tax politically more palatable by framing the revenue as dedicated to a popular purpose. It was also, in part, a constitutional design decision: under the Washington Constitution's provisions on appropriations and referenda, a tax whose revenue is directed to the support of state institutions (including public schools) may be exempt from referendum challenge. The education earmark was thus both a political and a legal hedge against repeal and reversal.

The constitutional hedge was tested and validated in *Quinn*, where the Washington Supreme Court upheld the Legislature's designation of SB 5096 as not subject to referendum because of its appropriation character.

## 8.6 Comparative Context — Other States

Washington is unusual among U.S. states in having a stand-alone capital gains tax without a general personal income tax. Most states with income taxes include capital gains within the income tax base (often taxing them at the same rate as ordinary income; a few, like Montana, tax them at a slightly lower rate). A few states — New Hampshire, until it phased out its interest-and-dividends tax; Alaska; and a handful of others — have no state income tax and no separate tax on capital gains at all.

Washington occupies a unique position: no general income tax (prohibited by *Culliton*) but a tax on capital gains (an "excise tax" under *Quinn*). This structure is structurally unprecedented. The Tax Foundation's analysis at the time of enactment noted that Washington would become "the only state to tax capital gains but not impose a general income tax" — a characterization that remains accurate.

The comparative oddity is not merely an administrative quirk. It has substantive implications:

- **Base coordination.** In other states, capital gains are part of the income tax base, and the income tax machinery (withholding, estimated payments, integration with the federal return) handles them. In Washington, the capital gains tax is a stand-alone regime with its own filing requirements, deadlines, and base rules.

- **Rate comparison.** Washington's top rate of 9.9 percent is at the high end of U.S. state capital gains tax rates. California, the highest-tax state, imposes a top combined rate on capital gains of approximately 13.3 percent. New York State imposes approximately 10.9 percent at the top. So Washington's 9.9 percent is within the range of the highest-tax states, even though Washington has no other state income tax. For a wealthy taxpayer considering relocation, Washington is no longer a "low-tax" state for capital gains; it is in the same rate tier as New York.

- **Federal coordination.** Because the Washington tax starts from federal net long-term capital gain, federal changes propagate automatically into the Washington base. This is both a benefit (simplicity) and a vulnerability (Washington is hostage to federal drafting decisions, including the Section 1202 exclusion).

## 8.7 Competitiveness and the Founder Question

Among the most intensely debated effects of the Washington capital gains excise tax is its impact on the state's startup and technology ecosystem. The Puget Sound region is home to Amazon, Microsoft, and a deep bench of venture-backed technology companies. Founders, early employees, and investors in these companies typically receive equity compensation and realize their economic return through a capital gains event — an initial public offering, an acquisition, or a secondary sale.

For founders of C corporations, the interaction with Section 1202 is critical. A founder of a Washington C corporation that qualifies for QSBS and that is sold after 5 years can exclude up to $10 million of gain federally — and, under the current interpretation of the Washington base, can exclude the same $10 million from the Washington capital gains excise tax as well. This is a significant tax benefit that mitigates the impact of the Washington tax for QSBS-eligible founders.

For founders of non-QSBS businesses — S corporations, LLCs taxed as partnerships, and similar pass-through entities — the Washington tax applies to the full gain above the standard deduction, with no QSBS-type exclusion. These founders face a 9.9 percent top rate on gains above $1 million, in addition to federal capital gains taxes. The total federal-plus-state tax burden on a large exit can approach 35 percent.

The policy debate about whether the capital gains excise tax harms Washington's technology ecosystem has two sides:

- **The concern side** argues that the tax, particularly the 9.9 percent top rate, creates a significant incentive for founders and investors to structure their exits around Washington — by establishing domicile elsewhere before the exit, by structuring the sale to minimize Washington-sourced gain, or by avoiding C-corporation status to qualify for the family business deduction. To the extent these behaviors materialize, they represent a loss of Washington economic activity.

- **The mitigation side** argues that QSBS eligibility for C corporation founders, the family business deduction for non-C corporation businesses, and the strong non-tax advantages of the Seattle technology ecosystem (talent, capital, network effects) outweigh the tax incentive to leave. The actual economic impact on the ecosystem, on this view, is modest.

The empirical evidence is, again, limited. The continued strong performance of the Puget Sound technology ecosystem through the first four years of the tax is consistent with the mitigation view. But the counterfactual is hard to measure: we cannot know how much stronger the ecosystem would have been absent the tax.

# Part 9. Comparative State Treatments of Capital Gains

## 9.1 The Taxonomy of State Treatment

State treatment of capital gains can be classified into several categories:

**Category A: No state income tax, no capital gains tax.** These are the "true" low-tax states: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming. (New Hampshire recently joined this category with the phase-out of its interest-and-dividends tax.) In these states, residents pay no state tax on capital gains.

**Category B: State income tax including capital gains at ordinary rates.** This is the most common approach. States including California, New York, Oregon, Minnesota, New Jersey, and most others tax capital gains as part of the personal income tax base, at the same rates as ordinary income. California's top combined rate on capital gains is approximately 13.3 percent (the state's top income tax rate plus the mental health services tax). New York's is approximately 10.9 percent (the state rate). Oregon's is approximately 9.9 percent.

**Category C: State income tax with preferential capital gains treatment.** A few states tax capital gains at lower rates than ordinary income. Montana, for example, provides a partial credit or exclusion for capital gains, resulting in effective rates below the ordinary income rate. Wisconsin provides a 30 percent exclusion on long-term gains. New Mexico has a capital gains deduction that effectively reduces the tax. Hawaii had historically provided a preferential rate. These are exceptions rather than the rule.

**Category D: No state income tax but capital gains tax (the Washington model).** This is a category of one: only Washington currently occupies it. Washington has no general income tax but has a stand-alone excise tax on certain long-term capital gains of individuals. The structural oddity of this position is the reason *Quinn* was so closely watched by state tax practitioners nationwide.

## 9.2 The Washington Model as a Potential Template

One of the open questions raised by *Quinn* is whether the Washington model — a stand-alone capital gains tax characterized as an excise tax — could be adopted by other states facing similar constitutional constraints on graduated income taxes. In the United States, there are very few states in this position. Most state constitutions either explicitly permit a graduated income tax (either in the original text or through subsequent amendment) or have been interpreted by state courts to permit it. Washington's situation — where the uniformity clause was interpreted in 1933 to forbid graduated income taxes and that interpretation has survived despite multiple attempts at reversal — is unusual.

A few states have somewhat analogous constitutional restrictions, though none is identical:

- **Massachusetts.** Massachusetts had a constitutional restriction similar to Washington's, requiring income to be taxed at a uniform rate. In 2022, Massachusetts voters amended their constitution to permit a graduated income tax (the "Fair Share Amendment"), adding a 4 percent surtax on income above $1 million. The amendment was passed after previous attempts had failed.

- **Illinois.** Illinois's constitution requires a flat (non-graduated) income tax. A constitutional amendment to permit graduation was rejected by voters in 2020. Illinois thus remains constitutionally restricted to flat-rate income taxation but has no capital gains tax of the Washington type.

- **Colorado.** Colorado has a flat-rate income tax by constitutional provision (TABOR). Like Illinois, it has no stand-alone capital gains tax.

None of these states has moved to adopt a Washington-style capital gains excise tax as a workaround to their respective constitutional restrictions. The political durability of the constitutional restrictions, combined with the doctrinal complexity of the "excise tax" workaround, has kept the Washington model unique.

## 9.3 The Washington Model and Federal Preemption Concerns

Another open question is whether the federal government could preempt state capital gains taxation in some fashion. Congress has broad power under the Commerce Clause and the Sixteenth Amendment to regulate federal income taxation and to restrict state taxation of items within the federal tax base. However, there is no current federal statute that preempts state capital gains taxation. States are generally free to impose their own taxes on items that are also within the federal income tax base (subject to the dormant Commerce Clause and due process limits).

The most plausible preemption scenario is not general preemption but specific federal intervention in response to perceived state overreach — for example, if Washington attempted to tax gains that had no connection to Washington at all, or if multiple states attempted to tax the same gain. These situations are addressed by the dormant Commerce Clause and by due process principles, which require a nexus between the taxpayer and the taxing state. The Washington domicile rule in RCW 82.87.100 is consistent with these constitutional limits.

# Part 10. Ongoing Academic Debate and Prospective Challenges

## 10.1 The Formalism-Substance Debate

The most fundamental ongoing debate about the Washington capital gains excise tax is the formalism-substance debate raised in the *Quinn* majority and dissent. The Washington Supreme Court held that the label "excise tax" controls, based on the transactional character of the imposition. Critics argue that this is formalism, and that the substantive reality of the tax is that it functions as an income tax on realized capital gains of wealthy Washington residents.

The debate has practical implications beyond academic interest. If the formalism-substance distinction can be drawn in this way, then the Legislature has a broader set of options for future taxation. It could, in principle:

- Enact a payroll tax characterized as an "excise tax on the performance of labor services," thereby taxing wages without running into *Culliton's* uniformity restriction.
- Enact a tax on dividends characterized as an "excise tax on the receipt of corporate distributions."
- Enact a tax on interest income characterized as an "excise tax on the provision of credit."

Each of these would raise the same formalism-substance tension as the capital gains excise tax. Each would be measured by an income concept but labeled as a transactional excise. Whether the Washington Supreme Court would uphold them would depend on how the court extends or limits the reasoning of *Quinn*.

Defenders of *Quinn* argue that the court's reasoning is grounded in a principled distinction — the distinction between taxes on events and taxes on states of affairs — and that the reasoning would not support an unbounded expansion. The critical features of the capital gains excise tax, on this view, are (1) the tax is triggered only by a specific realization event, (2) no tax is owed absent such an event, and (3) the event is a discrete transaction involving a change of ownership or legal status. A payroll tax on wages does not involve a change of ownership of a capital asset; a dividend tax does not involve a transaction in which the taxpayer transfers anything. On this view, the excise-tax space is real but narrower than critics fear.

Critics respond that the distinction between events and states of affairs is elusive and manipulable. Every tax can be characterized as imposed on an event (the event of having income, the event of owning property). The question is what the court considers to be a sufficient "event" for excise-tax characterization. *Quinn* provides an answer for capital gains transactions but does not clearly define the boundaries of the category.

## 10.2 Prospective Challenges to the Tax

Despite the *Quinn* decision and the denial of certiorari, there remain several avenues for future challenges to the Washington capital gains excise tax:

### 10.2.1 As-Applied Challenges

*Quinn* was a facial challenge to the statute. It held that the statute is not facially unconstitutional. It did not address as-applied challenges — challenges based on the particular facts of a specific taxpayer's situation. A taxpayer who could show that the tax, as applied to them, violated constitutional principles could, in principle, bring an as-applied challenge. For example:

- A taxpayer who realized a gain on a transaction that had no meaningful nexus to Washington (perhaps a non-resident who briefly happened to be in Washington when the sale closed) could argue that the tax lacks the constitutional nexus required by the dormant Commerce Clause.
- A taxpayer facing double taxation from Washington and another state on the same gain could argue that the credit under RCW 82.87.100 is inadequate, creating an impermissible burden on interstate commerce.

No significant as-applied challenges have reached the Washington Supreme Court as of early 2026, but the category of potential challenges remains open.

### 10.2.2 Challenges to the 2025 Amendments

The 2025 SB 5813 amendments added new substantive provisions — particularly the 2.9 percent surcharge and the retroactivity provision. These amendments have not been tested in court. A taxpayer could challenge:

- The retroactivity of SB 5813 on due process grounds, arguing that the retroactive imposition of the surcharge impairs vested rights or defeats legitimate reliance interests. Based on federal precedent (*Carlton*), such a challenge would likely fail for a retroactivity limited to the current tax year, but the Washington Supreme Court might approach the question with fresh attention.
- The tiered rate structure on uniformity grounds, arguing that the 7 percent and 9.9 percent tiers within the same "class of property" violate the uniformity clause even if the original 7 percent flat rate did not. This challenge is intellectually similar to the original *Quinn* challenge but has an additional analytical wrinkle: the rate differential is now internal to the tax, not merely between the capital gains excise tax and other taxes.

Neither challenge has yet been filed publicly. But both are within the plausible range of future litigation.

### 10.2.3 Broader Doctrinal Reconsideration

The most far-reaching future challenge would be a motion for the Washington Supreme Court to reconsider the *Quinn* decision itself. Under Washington Supreme Court practice, the court can reconsider prior decisions when circumstances warrant — typically, when a new case presents facts or arguments not considered in the prior decision, or when a change in the court's composition provides an opportunity to revisit a contested holding. The *Quinn* decision was a close call in some analytical respects (especially the formalism-substance debate), and a future court could, in principle, reach a different conclusion.

The political dynamics make such reconsideration unlikely in the near term. The tax has been enacted and is generating substantial revenue; reversing *Quinn* would blow a multi-hundred-million-dollar hole in the state budget. Courts are generally reluctant to take such actions absent strong new legal arguments. But the formalism-substance debate is sufficiently live that a future court, facing a new case with somewhat different facts, might choose to narrow *Quinn* or to add clarifying principles.

## 10.3 The Interaction with a Potential General Income Tax

A final ongoing question is how the Washington capital gains excise tax would interact with a potential future general income tax. In 2026, Washington enacted (by ESSB 6346) a new 9.9 percent tax on Washington taxable income above $1 million per household, effective January 1, 2028. This income tax, if it survives constitutional challenge, would overlap with the capital gains excise tax for high-earning taxpayers.

The coordination between the two taxes is not yet fully specified. The 2026 legislation includes provisions to avoid double taxation: gains that are already subject to the capital gains excise tax would be credited against the income tax, or excluded from the income tax base, so that the same dollar of gain would not be taxed twice. The exact mechanism is a matter of implementing regulations and DOR guidance.

The more fundamental question is whether the 2026 general income tax can itself survive *Culliton*. The 2026 income tax is clearly an income tax — the Legislature did not even attempt to label it as an excise. Its survival depends on whether the Washington Supreme Court distinguishes *Culliton* again, or overrules *Culliton* explicitly, or accepts some other theory that permits the tax. As of early 2026, the tax has been enacted but not yet tested in litigation; the legal challenge is expected in the near future, and the outcome is uncertain.

- **2026 (SB 6229)** — QSBS add-back proposal; did not pass.

## 11.3 Key Cases

- **Culliton v. Chase**, 174 Wash. 363, 25 P.2d 81 (1933). The foundational uniformity-clause case holding that a graduated net income tax is unconstitutional under Article VII, Section 1 of the Washington Constitution.
- **Quinn v. State of Washington**, 198 Wn.2d 554, 497 P.3d 899 (Wash. 2021) [trial court / intermediate history]; 526 P.3d 1 (Wash. 2023) [Supreme Court decision]. Decided March 24, 2023, 7-2 majority by Justice Stephens, dissent by Justice Gordon McCloud. Held that the capital gains excise tax is an excise tax and is not subject to the uniformity clause.
- **Quinn v. Washington**, cert. denied, 144 S. Ct. 552 (2024). The U.S. Supreme Court denied certiorari on January 16, 2024.
- **United States v. Carlton**, 512 U.S. 26 (1994). Federal precedent upholding retroactive tax legislation under due process analysis; relevant to the retroactivity analysis of SB 5813.
- **Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.**, 157 U.S. 429 (1895); 158 U.S. 601 (1895). Federal precedent holding that a federal tax on income from property was a direct tax requiring apportionment; the conceptual ancestor of the *Culliton* reasoning, though overturned federally by the Sixteenth Amendment in 1913.

## 11.4 Key Washington Constitutional Provisions

- **Article VII, Section 1** (Washington Constitution): Uniformity clause. "All taxes shall be uniform upon the same class of property within the territorial limits of the authority levying the tax and shall be levied and collected for public purposes only."
- **Article I, Section 12** (Washington Constitution): Privileges and immunities clause, independently enforced by Washington courts and raised in *Quinn*.
- **Article II, Section 1(b)** (Washington Constitution): Exemption from referendum for appropriations for support of the state government and existing public institutions; invoked to shield SB 5096 from referendum challenge.

## 11.5 Key Federal Statutory Provisions

- **IRC Section 1202** — Partial or full exclusion for gain from qualified small business stock (C corporations only; $10 million / 10x basis cap for pre-OBBBA stock; $15 million for post-OBBBA stock).
- **IRC Section 1045** — Tax-free rollover of QSBS sale proceeds into replacement QSBS within 60 days.
- **IRC Section 469** — Passive activity rules; incorporated by reference for Washington's material participation test under RCW 82.87.070.
- **IRC Section 167** — Depreciation rules; assets subject to Section 167 depreciation are excluded from the Washington capital gains tax base.
- **IRC Section 179** — Expensing rules for business property.
- **IRC Section 351, 368** — Reorganization and contribution rules; relevant to QSBS holding period and original issuance analysis.
- **IRC Section 613, 613A** — Percentage depletion; relevant to QSBS active-business exclusions.

# Closing Note

The Washington capital gains excise tax is a live and evolving area of state tax law. The 2023 *Quinn* decision settled the foundational constitutional question but left open a range of secondary and interpretive questions. The 2025 SB 5813 amendments added new complications — the surcharge, the retroactivity, the interaction with the increased estate tax. The 2026 legislative session opened additional questions about QSBS add-back, which did not pass but may return. The 2028 general income tax (enacted in 2026) will, if it survives constitutional challenge, overlap with the capital gains excise tax and require further coordination rules.

Researchers and practitioners working in this area should monitor:

- New DOR rule-making on the capital gains excise tax (particularly on QSBS interaction, which remains unaddressed in published guidance as of early 2026).
- Subsequent Washington Supreme Court decisions on related tax questions, which may refine or limit *Quinn*.
- Federal legislative action on Section 1202 (where OBBBA in 2025 already introduced significant changes, and further changes are periodically proposed).
- State legislative action in other states that may adopt the Washington "excise tax" model or that may decouple from Section 1202 (as Oregon did in 2026 with SB 1507).

The tax is now an established feature of the Washington fiscal landscape. It is controversial and contested, but it is also collecting hundreds of millions of dollars per year and funding significant portions of the state's education budget. Its doctrinal foundations rest on a fragile-seeming distinction — between excise taxes and property taxes, between transactions and states of affairs — that the Washington Supreme Court has nonetheless validated in a 7-2 decision. Whether that validation will survive the coming years of additional litigation and legislative amendment remains to be seen.
