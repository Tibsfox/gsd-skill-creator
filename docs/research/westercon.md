# Westercon -- The West Coast Science Fantasy Conference

> A deep research document covering the history, governance, cultural impact, and proposed retirement of one of the oldest science fiction conventions in the world.

---

## 1. History

In September 1948, seventy-seven science fiction fans gathered in Los Angeles for a one-day event that would become one of the longest-running traditions in speculative fiction fandom. Walter J. "Doc" Daugherty of the Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society (LASFS) proposed the idea: a regional convention for West Coast fans who could not afford the cross-country journey to Worldcon, the World Science Fiction Convention that had been running since 1939. E. Everett Evans introduced the formal motion to create the event and chaired the first gathering, with Daugherty chairing the second. The original name was the "West Coast Scienti-Fantasy Conference" -- a name that captures both the era's terminology and the breadth of the enterprise [1][2][5].

What began as an act of accessibility -- bringing fandom to the fans who couldn't travel east -- became an institution. By the early 1950s, Westercon had settled into a format that would endure for decades: a weekend-long convention held over the American Independence Day holiday, rotating among cities in the western United States and Canada. The July 4th weekend timing was pragmatic -- it guaranteed a long weekend for working fans -- and symbolic: an independence celebration for the independent-minded [1][5].

Several early Westercons were held jointly with Worldcon. The 1954 event featured John W. Campbell as the Worldcon Guest of Honor and Jack Williamson as Westercon's. These combined events in 1954, 1958, and beyond reflected the intertwined nature of the two institutions -- Westercon as the regional tributary feeding the global river of organized fandom [1][5][6].

Through the growth decades of the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s, Westercon expanded alongside the broader explosion of science fiction fandom. The rotating-host model meant that different cities -- Los Angeles, San Francisco, Portland, Seattle, San Diego, Phoenix, Salt Lake City, Boise, Honolulu -- took turns building organizational capacity. Each host committee brought its local flavor. A Westercon in Portland felt different from one in San Diego, which felt different from one in Honolulu. The convention was a traveling exhibition of West Coast fandom, and each stop added a new layer to the community's collective experience [1][5].

Peak attendance varied significantly by location and whether the event was co-hosted with other conventions. Westercon 65 in Seattle (2012) drew approximately 920 attending members. Westercon 72 "Spikecon" in Layton, Utah (2019), combined with NASFiC, attracted around 808 attending members. The convention was never enormous in the way that modern media conventions are -- San Diego Comic-Con draws 130,000 -- but Westercon was never trying to be enormous. It was trying to sustain a community, and for nearly eight decades, it did [1][5].

The 21st century brought challenges. The convention culture that had sustained Westercon -- volunteer-run, travel-dependent, programming-focused -- faced competition from the internet (which provided year-round fan community without requiring travel), from massive media conventions (which offered celebrity access and spectacle), and from the simple economics of convention attendance (hotel rooms, travel costs, registration fees). Westercon was built for an era when seeing your fan friends required physically gathering in the same place. That era was ending [1][9].

## 2. The Traveling Convention Model

Westercon's defining structural feature is its traveling nature. Unlike Norwescon (which has been at the DoubleTree by Hilton Seattle Airport since 1994) or Dragon Con (which has been in Atlanta since 1987), Westercon moves to a different city every year. The city is selected by vote of the convention's membership, two years in advance, through a formal site selection process modeled on the World Science Fiction Convention's own bidding system [1][3][5].

The site selection process works like this: groups of fans who want to host Westercon form a bid committee. They must have at minimum a Chair and Treasurer, an organizing instrument (typically incorporation documents), a hotel letter of intent, and evidence of non-profit status. The site must be on the North American continent west of the 104th meridian west, or in the state of Hawaii. A whimsical 1998 amendment -- proposed by Down Under Fan Fund delegate Terry Frost -- also permits Australian sites, but only if either Australia or the United States annexes the other. An attempt to repeal this provision at the 2003 Business Meeting failed, because fandom takes its jokes seriously [1][3].

Bids are presented to the attending membership of the current Westercon, and members vote to select the site for the convention two years hence. This means that at Westercon 77 in 2025, members would vote on where Westercon 79 should be held. The system creates a rolling two-year pipeline of organizational preparation [1][3].

The advantages of the traveling model are real. Each year, a different city's fan community gets the experience of organizing a major convention, building skills and infrastructure that benefit the broader community. Fans in Portland get to attend without flying to Los Angeles. Fans in Phoenix get to host without permanently committing to an annual event. The convention serves as a circuit rider, carrying fandom culture from city to city and pollinating each local community in turn [1].

The disadvantages are equally real. Every year, the institutional memory resets. The team that ran last year's Westercon in San Jose has no organizational relationship to the team running next year's in Portland. The hotel relationship starts from scratch. The volunteer pool starts from scratch. The programming infrastructure starts from scratch. The only continuity is the bylaws, the service mark (held by LASFS), and whatever informal mentoring happens between outgoing and incoming committees [1][3][9].

Compare this to Norwescon's fixed-venue model. Norwescon has been at the same hotel for over 30 years. The hotel management knows the convention's needs. The volunteer corps has institutional memory spanning decades. The programming team refines its approach year over year. The trade-off is geographic limitation -- Norwescon serves the Pacific Northwest but cannot serve Phoenix or Honolulu -- but the organizational stability is enormous [10].

The traveling model is, in systems architecture terms, a distributed system with no persistent state. Each node (annual convention) must bootstrap from the shared protocol (bylaws) and whatever cached state the organizers carry in their heads. It is resilient in the sense that no single point of failure can kill the system -- if one city's committee collapses, the failsafe provisions route the convention elsewhere. But it is fragile in the sense that it depends on a continuous supply of volunteers willing to bootstrap a major event from near-zero every year [1][3].

## 3. Governance and Organization

Westercon's governance is a miniature exercise in democratic self-rule, codified in bylaws that have been amended at annual business meetings for decades. The system is fascinating both as a functional governance structure and as an artifact of fan culture's love of parliamentary procedure [1][3].

The name "Westercon" is a registered service mark of the Los Angeles Science Fantasy Society, Inc. (LASFS). LASFS, founded October 27, 1934, is the oldest continuously operating science fiction club in the world. Its motto is "De Profundis Ad Astra" (From the Depths to the Stars), and its unofficial Rule 0 is "Death will not release you." LASFS retains specific responsibilities for Westercon: archiving bylaws and business meeting minutes, providing a prototype site-selection ballot to each administering Westercon, and acting as the ultimate failsafe if a convention committee declares itself unable to fulfill its duties [1][2][3].

The bylaws system governs everything from site selection to badge font size. Key provisions include:

- **Geographic eligibility:** Sites must be west of the 104th meridian or in Hawaii (with the Australian annexation joke clause).
- **Site selection timing:** Two years in advance, by member vote.
- **Bid requirements:** At least two people (Chair and Treasurer), an organizing instrument, a hotel letter of intent, and non-profit status evidence.
- **Membership classes:** Supporting and attending memberships must be offered.
- **Name badges:** Pre-registered member names must be in no less than 24-point bold type. This is in the bylaws. Fandom takes typography seriously.
- **Failsafe provision (Section 3.16):** If no bid wins site selection, the Business Meeting may select a site by three-fourths vote. If the Meeting cannot decide, LASFS selects.
- **Amendment process:** Changes require first passage at one Business Meeting and ratification at the next year's Meeting. This two-year amendment cycle provides stability but makes the system slow to adapt [1][3].

Kevin Standlee led the committee that redrafted the Westercon Bylaws in the 1990s and is effectively the primary author of the current version. He chaired Westercon 74 in Tonopah, Nevada (2022) and has been the most consistent voice in documenting and maintaining the convention's institutional structure. His work on westercon.org provides the most authoritative public record of governance history [1][3].

The relationship to Worldcon (the World Science Fiction Convention, governed by the World Science Fiction Society, WSFS) is one of parallel evolution. Both use a rotating-host, bid-and-vote model. Both have business meetings open to all members. Both use Robert's Rules. But Worldcon is the global event with thousands of attendees, while Westercon is the regional event with hundreds. Westercon's governance DNA influenced -- and was influenced by -- WSFS, creating a shared culture of democratic fan governance that extends across the convention ecosystem [1][5][6].

## 4. Cultural Impact and Legacy

Westercon's most significant cultural legacy is one that its 1948 founders could never have predicted: it was the birthplace of organized furry fandom.

The timeline begins at Westercon 38 in Sacramento, July 1985. Mark Merlino (known in fandom as Sy Sable) and Rod O'Riley (Vinson Mink), both co-founders of the Cartoon/Fantasy Organization (C/FO), hosted a room party in Sheldon Linker and Toni Poper's room at the Red Lion Inn. The party was called "The Prancing Skiltaire" after Merlino's house, itself named for a mink-like alien species he had created, referencing the Prancing Pony Inn from *The Lord of the Rings*. The party screened a fan-compiled version of *Animalympics*. This was the first openly publicized funny-animal fan party at a science fiction convention [7][8][11].

The following year, at Westercon 39 in San Diego (1986), Merlino and O'Riley held the first party explicitly called a "furry party." In 1987, at the Oakland Westercon, the annual party had grown large enough to earn the nickname "Das FurryParty." The 1987 BayCon Furry Party in San Jose attracted attendees who proposed starting furry fanzines and establishing a dedicated convention [7][8][11].

In 1989, the momentum culminated in ConFurence Zero -- the world's first dedicated furry convention, held in Costa Mesa, California, with 65 attendees. The direct line from a Westercon room party in 1985 to a standalone convention in 1989 is one of the most documented examples of subcultural genesis in fandom history [7][8][11].

By 2024, the global furry fandom supported over 200 conventions worldwide, with the largest (Midwest FurFest) drawing over 14,000 attendees. Mark Merlino passed away on February 20, 2024, after battling cancer. The community he helped birth in a Sacramento hotel room had grown beyond anything a 77-person regional SF convention could have imagined [7][11].

Beyond furry fandom, Westercon's cultural contributions include:

- **The Other Change of Hobbit (1977):** Attendees who ran a portable bookstore at Westercon went on to found this beloved Berkeley science fiction bookstore, which operated until 2016 [1][5].
- **Convention governance template:** Westercon's rotating-host, bid-and-vote, business-meeting model influenced the governance of numerous regional conventions across the western United States [1][3].
- **Regency Dance tradition:** Popularized at Westercon, adopted by many fan conventions as a programming staple [1].
- **C/FO and anime fandom:** The Cartoon/Fantasy Organization, whose members were central to early furry parties at Westercon, held its first meeting at LASFS in 1977 and contributed to the founding of North American anime fandom [7][8].
- **Guest of Honor tradition:** Westercon's GoH selections across seven decades -- emphasizing authors, artists, and scientists from the western region -- helped define the literary focus that distinguishes fan-run conventions from commercial media events [1][5].

The convention that was built to bring Worldcon's spirit to the West Coast ended up incubating entirely new forms of creative community. Westercon was the seed crystal. The cultures that grew from it were the crystal lattice.

## 5. Convention Culture

Westercon's convention culture is a catalog of traditions that have been adopted, adapted, and propagated across the fan convention ecosystem. The traveling nature of the convention means these traditions travel too -- each host city inherits the template and adds its own variations.

**The July 4th Weekend Format:** Established in the 1950s, the four-day Independence Day weekend format became Westercon's signature temporal identity. The specific days vary year to year depending on how July 4th falls, but the association between Westercon and Independence Day is deeply rooted in the convention's culture [1][5].

**The Masquerade:** A costume contest typically held on the second or Saturday night, the masquerade is one of the convention's most enduring traditions. Entrants present costumes -- ranging from faithful recreations of book cover art to original designs -- before judges and an audience. The masquerade tradition at Westercon predates and influenced similar events at other conventions [1].

**The Art Show and Dealers Room:** The art show provides a gallery space for science fiction and fantasy artists, with works available for purchase through a silent auction or "quick sale" system. The dealers room (historically called the "huckster room") is where bookstores, publishers, and merchandise vendors set up shop. Together, they form the commercial and artistic marketplace of the convention [1].

**Room Party Culture:** Evenings at Westercon are defined by room parties -- gatherings hosted in hotel rooms by groups promoting other conventions, fan clubs, or just socializing. Room parties are where the formal hierarchy of the convention dissolves. A first-time attendee can end up in a conversation with a Hugo Award-winning author over a plate of cheese and crackers. It was room party culture that directly incubated the furry party tradition [1][7].

**The Business Meeting:** Open to all members, usually held on the second day, following Robert's Rules of Order. The Business Meeting is where bylaws are amended, site selection results are announced, and the democratic governance of the convention is exercised. For fans who love parliamentary procedure -- and science fiction fans are disproportionately represented in this category -- the Business Meeting is not a necessary evil but a highlight [1][3].

**First Night Icebreaker / "Meet the Guests":** Held on the first evening, an informal setting for members to meet the professional attendees (Guests of Honor, special guests, spotlight publishers). The icebreaker sets the tone: this is a community event, not a celebrity appearance [1].

**Ice Cream Social:** Introduced by Mike Glyer in 1978, borrowed from Loscon, the ice cream social is not held at every convention but is a recognized tradition -- a sweet, deliberately low-key gathering that embodies the convention's social mission [1][9].

**Regency Dance:** A staple for many years, the regency dance offered a structured social dance experience alongside the more informal rock and roll dances. The regency tradition reflected fandom's interest in historical recreation and its willingness to engage with the past as a creative act [1].

## 6. Decline and Proposed Retirement

The story of Westercon's decline is not a single catastrophic event but a gradual erosion that was dramatically accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic.

**COVID-19 Impact:** Westercon 73, originally scheduled for Seattle in July 2020, was postponed to 2021, then went virtual, then the committee disbanded entirely in April 2021. LASFS assumed official Westercon functions and combined them with Loscon 47 in November 2021. Westercon 74 was similarly postponed. The disruption broke the convention's continuity and exposed the fragility of its all-volunteer organizing model. In a fixed-venue convention like Norwescon, the organizational infrastructure survived COVID because the institution was rooted in place. In a traveling convention, the disruption severed the connection between successive organizing committees [1][9].

**Repeated Bid Failures:** The post-COVID years revealed that the pipeline of willing host committees had dried up:

- Westercon 75 (2023): Convention chair cancelled due to "a cascade of circumstances"; Loscon assumed duties.
- Westercon 76 (2024): No eligible bids filed by the deadline. A Utah bid received 55 of 58 preference votes but was ineligible due to missing non-profit documentation. The Business Meeting selected them anyway under the Section 3.16 failsafe.
- Westercon 77 (2025): No bids filed. The Westercon 75 Business Meeting awarded it to Kevin Standlee and Lisa Hayes as placeholders; eventually transferred to BayCon 2025.
- Westercons 78 and 79: Both awarded to BayCon in Santa Clara, effectively becoming a co-hosted event rather than an independent convention [1][3][9].

**The Bylaws Repeal Motion:** Kayla Allen and Linda Deneroff, longtime Westercon administrators, assessed the situation directly: "Westercon simply doesn't have enough interest anymore, and rather than just have it fizzle out completely, we should try to organize an orderly shutdown by repealing the Bylaws and handing the convention's charter back to LASFS" [9].

The motion was submitted to the Westercon 77 Business Meeting at BayCon 2025 in Santa Clara and received first passage on July 6, 2025. Under Westercon's two-year amendment process, it must be ratified at Westercon 78 (BayCon 2026, July 3-6, 2026) to take effect. If ratified, Westercons 79 and 80 would still be held (already selected), but those final Westercons would not conduct site selection or hold a business meeting. Westercon 80 (2028) would be the final convention under the current bylaws. The disposition of the "Westercon" name would revert entirely to LASFS [1][3][9].

The factors driving the decline are interconnected:

1. **Volunteer exhaustion:** The traveling model requires a new volunteer corps every year. As the pool of experienced convention organizers aged and was not replenished at the same rate, the labor supply contracted.
2. **Competition from fixed-venue conventions:** Regional conventions like Norwescon, Baycon, and Loscon offered the same community experience without the organizational overhead of annual bootstrapping.
3. **Internet-mediated community:** The social function that conventions once uniquely served -- connecting geographically dispersed fans -- was increasingly fulfilled by online communities, social media, and virtual events.
4. **Economic pressures:** Rising hotel costs, travel expenses, and the general economics of convention attendance priced out younger fans who might have replenished the volunteer pipeline.
5. **Pandemic acceleration:** COVID-19 did not cause Westercon's decline but accelerated it by breaking the continuity chain between successive organizing committees [1][9].

The proposed retirement is not a failure. It is the deliberate choice of a community that recognizes the difference between an institution that has fulfilled its purpose and one that has been abandoned. Westercon taught the West Coast how to organize conventions. It incubated furry fandom. It sustained a democratic governance tradition for nearly eight decades. The question it now faces is whether ending well is as honorable as beginning well. The answer, from the people proposing the retirement, is yes [9].

## 7. Connection to NWC (Norwescon)

Westercon and Norwescon are parent and child. Norwescon was founded in 1978 by Pacific Northwest fans who wanted to build the organizational experience needed to bid for the 1981 Worldcon. But the model they were drawing from -- the regional convention as training ground, community builder, and cultural incubator -- was the model Westercon had established thirty years earlier [1][10].

The key structural difference between them is the difference between a traveling convention and a fixed-venue convention. Westercon moves; Norwescon stays. This difference explains much of their divergent trajectories. Norwescon's 30-year relationship with the DoubleTree gives it institutional memory, volunteer continuity, and venue optimization that Westercon's rotating model cannot match. But Westercon's traveling model gave it geographic reach and cultural pollination capacity that Norwescon, anchored in SeaTac, does not have [1][10].

Together, WCN and NWC tell the complete story of West Coast SF convention culture. Westercon is the original -- the seed convention that demonstrated how to organize, govern, and sustain a fan community. Norwescon is the descendant -- the convention that took the lessons of the traveling model and proved that putting down roots could create something more durable. The parent taught the child, and the child may outlast the parent.

## 8. Connection to This Project

Westercon is a case study in the lifecycle of cultural institutions: founding, growth, maturity, decline, and the deliberate choice of an orderly sunset. This lifecycle parallels the technology lifecycle that runs through the Research series -- systems are born, they serve their purpose, they are succeeded by systems better adapted to current conditions, and the measure of their success is not whether they persist forever but whether they left the ecosystem better than they found it.

The parallel to Burning Man's traveling regional model is structural: both Westercon and the regional burn network operate as distributed systems where each node bootstraps from shared principles. The parallel to mesh networking is architectural: Westercon is a packet-switched convention, routing fandom culture from city to city using a shared protocol (the bylaws). The parallel to this project's trust system is philosophical: Westercon's web of trust -- who do you know, who vouches for the bid committee, who will run the room parties -- is a social graph sustained by face-to-face interaction, not credentials.

The Rosetta Stone connection is explicit. Westercon translates between city-specific fandoms. A fan from Portland and a fan from Phoenix share the Westercon experience. The convention is a translation layer between local cultures, preserving the core protocol (democratic governance, volunteer labor, literary focus) while adapting to the local dialect (this city's hotels, this city's fan community, this city's July weather).

WCN and NWC together -- the traveling convention and the fixed-venue convention -- present a complete architecture for community infrastructure: the distributed model for reach and cultural exchange, the fixed model for stability and institutional depth. The research question is not which is better but what each reveals about the conditions under which communities thrive.
