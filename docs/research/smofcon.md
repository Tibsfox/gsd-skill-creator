## 1. What SMOF Means

The acronym "SMOF" stands for "Secret Masters of Fandom" -- a tongue-in-cheek title for the people who actually run science fiction conventions. The term did not originate with the convention that bears its name; it predates SMOFcon by roughly two decades and carries a history as layered as the community it describes.

### Origin and Etymology

The phrase "Secret Masters of Fandom" derives from a humorous conspiracy theory that circulated in science fiction fan circles during the early 1960s. The concept -- that a shadowy cabal of powerful fans secretly controlled fandom from behind the scenes -- drew inspiration from Gerald Kersh's 1953 novel *The Secret Masters*, which Bob Tucker referenced in a 1950s fan article. The initialism "SMOF" was likely coined by Jack L. Chalker around 1962-1963, emerging from correspondence with Ted Johnstone (Dave McDaniel) in late 1962 and spreading through fan circles during 1963 [1][2][3].

The earliest documented print appearance came in the New York Times on September 6, 1971, in an article covering the World Science Fiction Convention in Boston. The term subsequently appeared in the 1973 third edition of Wilson Tucker's *Neo-Fan's Guide to Science Fiction Fandom*, though it was absent from the original 1955 edition. A "SMOF Award" was presented to Theodore Sturgeon at the 1963 Worldcon, with Forrest J Ackerman accepting on Sturgeon's behalf [1][2].

### Semantic Evolution

The term evolved through three distinct phases. In the 1960s, it functioned purely as conspiracy humor -- jokes about powerful fans who supposedly controlled fandom from smoke-filled rooms. By the 1970s, as the number of fan conventions proliferated and the work of organizing them became more visible, the term attached itself to the practical community of experienced convention problem-solvers: former Worldcon chairs, hotel negotiators, programming directors, and the volunteers who kept the whole apparatus running.

The third phase began in 1984, when the community formalized itself. The creation of the Smofs mailing list and the founding of SMOFcon as an annual gathering transformed "smof" from insider slang into a self-applied professional identity. Today, "smof" (lowercase) is used as both noun and verb: "smoffing" means discussing convention operations, and the collective community of convention runners is sometimes called "smofdom" [1][2][4].

### The Self-Deprecating Humor

What makes the name work is the irony. There is nothing secret about what SMOFs do. Convention-running is unglamorous, thankless work performed by volunteers who receive no compensation beyond the satisfaction of having made something happen. The "secret" is that there is no secret -- just hundreds of hours of hotel negotiations, programming spreadsheets, volunteer coordination, and crisis management performed by people who care enough to show up.

Jodie Offutt offered a characteristically fannish definition at Discon 2: the real Secret Masters of Fandom were the people who kept convention hotel bathtubs filled with ice and beer. Bruce Pelz once declared himself "Smof #2 -- leaving it to others to fight out who is #1." The humor is not about power. It is about the absurdity of calling yourself a "master" when your primary job is making sure the hotel function rooms have enough chairs [1].

The culture of SMOFs forms a distinct community within fandom. These are not the authors, artists, or media celebrities who attract attention at conventions. They are the people who built the stage, negotiated the room block, wrote the programming schedule, trained the volunteers, and will stay until 2 AM tearing down the art show. They are, in the truest sense, the infrastructure. And like all infrastructure, their work is most visible when it fails.

## 2. SMOFcon History

### Founding

SMOFcon was founded in 1984 as an annual convention specifically for convention organizers. The founding committee originally attempted to name it "ConCon," but the fan community insisted on "Smofcon" -- the community's own name for itself [1][2]. A precursor convention focused on convention-running had been held in New York City in the early 1970s, later retroactively designated "Smofcon 0" [1].

The concept was straightforward: create a dedicated space where the people who run science fiction conventions could meet, share knowledge, discuss problems, and learn from each other -- without the distraction of the conventions they were organizing. No general programming. No dealers' room. No masquerade. Just the people who make conventions happen, talking about how to make conventions happen.

### Scale and Character

SMOFcon is deliberately small. Typical attendance ranges from 100 to 150 people -- a single-track convention that fits in one conference room and a hospitality suite. This scale is not a failure of growth; it is a design choice. The value of SMOFcon lies in direct conversation between experienced convention organizers. A gathering of 150 people all engaged in the same specialized work creates a density of relevant expertise that no larger convention can match [1][5].

The convention has traditionally been held on the first weekend of December in the United States, though recent years have seen significant geographic expansion.

The geographic pattern tells a story. For the first 27 years (1984-2010), every SMOFcon was held in the United States. Then Amsterdam in 2011 broke the pattern. Lisbon in 2021, Stockholm in 2025, Lisbon again in 2026 -- three European SMOFcons in six years, representing a dramatic acceleration of internationalization after nearly three decades of US-only gatherings [5][6][7].

### Organizational Model

Each SMOFcon is organized by an independent committee, typically operating under the umbrella of an existing fan organization. MCFI (Massachusetts Convention Fandom, Inc.) has been the most prolific host, organizing SMOFcons 3, 15, 25, and 40. SFSFC (San Francisco Science Fiction Conventions, Inc.) has sponsored events and scholarship programs. SWOC hosted Smofcon 41 in Seattle. The hosting rotates through competitive bidding, mirroring the Worldcon model at a smaller scale [1][5].

## 3. What Happens at SMOFcon

### Programming

SMOFcon programming is entirely focused on the craft of convention-running. There are no author readings, no film screenings, no gaming rooms. Every panel, workshop, and presentation addresses an operational challenge that convention organizers face. Typical programming includes [1][5][8]:

- **Hotel contract negotiation** -- the art and science of negotiating room blocks, function space, food and beverage minimums, and attrition clauses with hotel sales teams
- **Convention programming** -- scheduling panels, managing guest lists, diversifying content without tokenizing participants, balancing literary and media programming
- **Registration systems** -- membership management, badge design, at-door vs. advance registration, electronic payment processing
- **Volunteer management** -- recruiting, training, scheduling, and recognizing the dozens to hundreds of volunteers who staff a convention
- **Budgeting and finance** -- revenue projections, expense management, the economics of break-even vs. surplus conventions, handling losses
- **Safety and code of conduct** -- developing policies, training response teams, handling incidents, balancing inclusion with enforcement
- **Accessibility** -- physical accessibility, sensory accommodations, financial accessibility, neurodiversity considerations
- **Marketing and public relations** -- reaching new audiences, social media strategy, press relations, community outreach
- **Crisis management** -- what to do when things go wrong: medical emergencies, disruptive attendees, facility failures, weather events

Smofcon 25 (Boston, 2007) exemplified the thematic approach, emphasizing marketing and publicity with a keynote by Dr. Gloria Boone on convention branding. Smofcon 35 (Boston, 2017) featured a two-hour panel on "Diversifying Programme" that focused on avoiding tokenization while expanding participant diversity [5][8].

### The Worldcon Q&A

One of SMOFcon's most significant recurring program elements is the Worldcon bid Q&A session. Seated Worldcons (those already selected) and active bids present updates and answer questions from an audience of experienced convention runners. This functions as an informal accountability mechanism -- bid committees must demonstrate organizational readiness, financial viability, and facility adequacy to the people who know exactly what questions to ask [5][6].

At SMOFcon 40 (Providence, 2023), these presentations were recorded and uploaded to YouTube by host MCFI. At SMOFcon 42 (Stockholm, 2025), the Q&A featured presentations from LAcon V (2026 Worldcon), Montreal 2027, and three active 2028 bids: Brisbane, Nuremberg, and ConKigali/Rwanda. Expressions of interest extended as far as 2035, with the host noting the relevance of the approaching Worldcon centennial in 2039 [6][7].

### The Consuite

A distinctive feature of SMOFcon is the consuite -- a hospitality area providing continuous food and drinks, funded by the convention and often supplemented by sponsoring conventions and organizations. At Smofcon 35, Dublin 2019 and Arisia sponsored portions of the food. The consuite is where the informal work of SMOFcon happens: the hallway conversations, the impromptu mentoring, the relationship-building that makes the formal programming effective [8].

### The Zoo Con

Some SMOFcons feature a "zoo con" -- a role-playing simulation game where participants take on convention committee roles and navigate scenarios requiring real-time decision-making. The game teaches convention management through experience, compressing months of committee work into hours of gameplay. It is one of the few formal mechanisms for training new convention organizers outside of actual convention work [8].

## 4. The Knowledge Transfer Problem

### Why SMOFcon Exists

SMOFcon exists because convention-running knowledge is fragile. The skills required to organize a multi-day event for hundreds or thousands of people -- hotel negotiation, programming scheduling, volunteer management, crisis response, financial planning -- are mostly learned through apprenticeship. An experienced convention chair carries decades of institutional knowledge: which hotels to avoid, which contract clauses are negotiable, how to handle a medical emergency in the dealers' room, what happens when the keynote speaker cancels 48 hours before opening ceremonies.

This knowledge lives primarily in people's heads. When experienced convention runners retire, burn out, or die, their knowledge goes with them. The Worldcon Runners' Guide exists but is perpetually under revision -- the WSFS website notes that the guide "is in the process of being updated" and provides older PDF versions as interim resources. The ConRunner wiki captures some operational knowledge but cannot replicate the judgment calls and vendor relationships that come from years of experience [1][4][5].

### The Oral Tradition

Convention knowledge transmission operates on an oral tradition model. The primary mechanism is face-to-face conversation: in panel rooms, in consuites, in hotel lobbies at midnight. A new convention chair learns not from a manual but from sitting down with someone who has done it before and asking questions. SMOFcon is the formalized version of this process -- an annual gathering where the oral tradition can happen at scale [1][5].

The oral tradition has strengths. It transmits tacit knowledge -- the kind that resists documentation. How to read a hotel sales manager. When to push back on a contract clause and when to accept it. The body language that signals a volunteer is about to quit. These are skills that develop through experience and mentoring, not through reading.

But the oral tradition has a critical weakness: it requires continuity of participants. If the experienced people stop showing up, the knowledge chain breaks. And convention-running is an activity with high burnout rates. Organizing a multi-day event for thousands of people, on a volunteer basis, while managing a day job and a personal life, is unsustainable for most people. The typical convention organizer has a finite number of conventions in them before exhaustion, frustration, or life changes pull them away [1][5].

### The Documentation Challenge

The knowledge artifacts that exist in the SMOFcon ecosystem include [4][5][9]:

- **The Worldcon Runners' Guide (WCRG)** -- maintained by a standing committee of the WSFS Business Meeting, structured as a wiki at wcrg.conrunner.net with parallel PDF sections on wsfs.org. It includes a mechanism for handling disagreement: recommended approaches are stated, "dissenting views" are noted with named partisans, and historically disastrous options are explicitly flagged.
- **The ConRunner Wiki** (conrunner.net) -- broader than Worldcon-specific, covering convention operations generally. The "Smof" article alone has been accessed over 16,000 times.
- **Fancyclopedia 3** (fancyclopedia.org) -- a comprehensive fan-historical encyclopedia with over 71,000 pages as of 2025, documenting conventions, organizations, terminology, people, and publications.
- **The Smofs Mailing List** -- a conrunners email list specifically for Worldcon management discussion, predating modern social media and facilitating coordination that was previously possible only face-to-face.
- **File 770** (file770.com) -- Mike Glyer's Hugo Award-winning fan news journal, functioning as the journal of record for SMOFcon proceedings, Worldcon bids, WSFS governance, and community developments.

Each of these artifacts captures a different slice of the knowledge. None captures all of it. And the most valuable knowledge -- the judgment, the relationships, the lessons learned from disasters nobody wrote down -- resists capture in any format. This is the fundamental tension that SMOFcon addresses: how to transfer knowledge that cannot be written down, in a community where the knowledge-holders are volunteers with finite endurance.

### Parallels to Other Knowledge Transfer Challenges

The SMOFcon knowledge problem is not unique to fandom. It mirrors challenges in:

- **Indigenous knowledge systems** -- where cultural knowledge transmitted through oral tradition is vulnerable to generational disruption, and codification risks losing the context that makes it meaningful
- **Trade skills and craftsmanship** -- where apprenticeship-based learning creates expertise that formal education cannot replicate
- **Institutional memory in organizations** -- where departure of key personnel creates knowledge gaps that documentation cannot fully prevent
- **Open source software communities** -- where the bus factor (how many key contributors can be lost before a project stalls) determines project sustainability

In each case, the challenge is the same: valuable knowledge that lives in people rather than documents, transmitted through relationships rather than reading, and vulnerable to the departure of key individuals. SMOFcon is one community's answer to this challenge -- an annual gathering designed to make the oral tradition more resilient by making it more frequent and more inclusive.

## 5. Relationship to Worldcon, Westercon, and Regional Cons

### The Worldcon Connection

The World Science Fiction Convention (Worldcon) is an 85-year institution that has been running since 1939. It presents the Hugo Awards, moves to a different city each year (and increasingly, a different country), and requires years of advance planning by hundreds of volunteers. The World Science Fiction Society (WSFS) is the unincorporated literary society that governs Worldcon -- an organization with no permanent officers, no board of directors, and authority distributed across annually rotating committees [4][9].

SMOFcon is where the people who run Worldcon learn the trade, recruit help, present their bids, and pass institutional memory from one generation to the next. The Worldcon bid Q&A session is one of the convention's centerpiece events -- a formal accountability mechanism where bid committees must demonstrate competence to an audience of people who know what competence looks like [5][6].

### WSFS Governance

Understanding SMOFcon requires understanding the governance system it supports. WSFS operates through an annual Business Meeting held at each Worldcon -- typically three morning sessions on successive days. Constitutional amendments require a two-year ratification cycle: proposed at one year's Business Meeting, ratified or rejected at the next. The only permanent standing committee is the Mark Protection Committee (MPC), responsible for maintaining trademarks including "Worldcon," "WSFS," "Hugo Award," and "NASFiC" [4][9].

Worldcon site selection is performed two years in advance by secret ballot. Voters must hold a membership in the current Worldcon and purchase an Advance WSFS Membership in the convention being voted on. "None of the Above" appears on every ballot -- if it wins, the Business Meeting selects the site [4][9].

SMOFcon serves as the informal pre-selection venue where these bids are first discussed, refined, and stress-tested before the formal vote at Worldcon. It is, in effect, the governance layer's back-channel -- the place where the democratic process is informed by the expertise of the people who have done this before.

### Westercon and Regional Conventions

Westercon, the West Coast Science Fantasy Conference, has been running since 1948 -- a traveling convention that rotates among western US cities. It is the convention that taught an entire region how to organize fan gatherings. Regional conventions like Norwescon (since 1978) grew from the organizational capacity that Westercon built. SMOFcon is where the people who run these conventions learn from each other [10].

The relationship is directional but reciprocal. SMOFcon draws its attendees from the community of people who run Worldcon, Westercon, and regional conventions. In turn, those conventions benefit from the knowledge exchanged at SMOFcon. A lesson learned at a Norwescon committee meeting might be shared at SMOFcon with someone organizing a convention in Melbourne. A hotel negotiation technique developed for a Worldcon in Glasgow might be passed to a regional convention committee in Portland.

### Accessibility and Internationalization

SMOFcon's geographic expansion reflects the internationalization of the convention community it serves. But expansion brings challenges. At SMOFcon 42 (Stockholm, 2025), the ConKigali bid team -- proposing a Worldcon in Rwanda -- was denied Swedish visas, with bid chair Micheal Kabunga describing "amorphous reasons" typical of visa denials from Western countries. This incident highlighted structural barriers facing convention organizers from the Global South [6][7].

Multiple organizations have developed scholarship and bursary programs to address financial accessibility:

| Program | Amount | Details |
|---------|--------|---------|
| SFSFC Scholarships | $500 each | Two per year since at least 2004; targets promising convention runners |
| CanSMOF Scholarships | Varies | Canadian organization funding international attendees |
| Glasgow 2024 Bursaries | Travel + hotel + membership | Up to 18 bursaries for SMOFcon 42 |

SMOFcon 43 (Lisbon, 2026) has announced plans to maximize virtual accessibility: a Discord server for both in-person and remote participants, live-streaming of recorded sessions, and provisions for online presentations by teams unable to travel [7].

## 6. Connection to NWC and WCN

### The Conventions Stack

Within this research series, three projects form a complete conventions sub-cluster:

- **NWC (Norwescon)** -- the convention itself. A fixed-venue, annual literary SF convention in SeaTac, Washington, running since 1978. The thing that gets built. 48 years of books, science, art, and the people who imagine what comes next.
- **WCN (Westercon)** -- the traveling convention. A rotating-host institution that built West Coast fandom's organizational capacity since 1948. The network that connects conventions. 77 years of democratic fan governance, now facing the question of whether to continue.
- **SMF (SMOFcon)** -- the convention about conventions. The meta-layer. Where the people who run NWC and organize WCN learn the craft, share the knowledge, and make the bids. The training and governance infrastructure that keeps the whole system running.

This is a three-layer stack:

| Layer | Project | Function | Analogy |
|-------|---------|----------|---------|
| Application | NWC | The convention fans attend | The user-facing service |
| Network | WCN | The traveling convention connecting regions | The routing layer |
| Infrastructure | SMF | The convention for convention runners | The control plane |

NWC is the convention you go to. WCN is the system that taught regions how to build conventions like NWC. SMOFcon is where the people who run both learn to do it better.

### The Meta-Pattern

The recursive quality is inherent. A convention for people who run conventions is inherently meta. But the meta-ness is not abstract -- it is operational. SMOFcon does not theorize about convention-running. It addresses specific, practical problems: how to negotiate a better hotel rate, how to handle a code-of-conduct violation, how to recruit volunteers when your committee is aging out. The abstraction layer serves the concrete layer.

This is the pattern that connects the conventions cluster to the broader research series. Every system benefits from a meta-layer that examines how the system itself works. NWC benefits from SMOFcon the way any application benefits from observability tools. WCN benefits from SMOFcon the way any network benefits from a control plane. The convention about conventions is the monitoring system for the convention ecosystem.

## 7. Connection to This Project

### Convention-Running as Systems Engineering

Running a convention is systems engineering performed by volunteers on a deadline with no budget. The skill set maps precisely: requirements gathering (what do attendees want?), resource allocation (how many function rooms, how many volunteer shifts?), risk management (what if the keynote cancels?), stakeholder communication (hotel, vendors, guests, attendees, volunteers), and post-mortem analysis (what went wrong, what went right, what changes for next year).

SMOFcon is the continuing education program for this volunteer systems engineering workforce. It is the training layer -- the place where the systems engineers meet to share techniques, troubleshoot failures, and mentor the next generation.

### The Knowledge Transfer Parallel

The knowledge transfer problem that defines SMOFcon is the same problem this project addresses. Convention-running knowledge lives in people's heads, transmitted through oral tradition, vulnerable to the departure of key individuals. This project's core activity -- documentation -- is the systematic attempt to capture and preserve knowledge that would otherwise exist only in the minds of its practitioners.

SMOFcon is what happens when a community recognizes the problem and creates an institution to address it. This project is the same impulse expressed through a different medium: instead of an annual gathering, a persistent documentation system. Instead of oral tradition, structured research. But the underlying motivation is identical: knowledge matters, knowledge is fragile, and preserving it requires deliberate, sustained effort.

### The Recursive Pattern

The deepest connection is structural. SMOFcon is a convention for people who run conventions -- a meta-convention. This project is a skill-creator that creates skills for creating skills -- a meta-tool. The recursive pattern is the same: a system that examines and improves its own kind.

The Amiga Principle applies: architectural leverage. A gathering of 150 people at SMOFcon structures the knowledge that enables conventions serving thousands. A skill-creator that creates skills for creating skills structures the capability that enables everything built on top of it. Small inputs, amplified through the right architecture, produce disproportionate outputs. The leverage comes from operating at the meta-level -- not doing the work directly, but improving the capacity of everyone who does the work.

The convention about conventions. The skill-creator for skill-creators. The documentation project about documentation. Each is the training layer for its own domain, and each demonstrates the same principle: the most impactful work is often not the work itself, but the work of making the work better.
