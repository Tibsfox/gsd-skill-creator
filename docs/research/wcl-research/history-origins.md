# Washington State Corporate Law: History and Origins

*A research essay in legal history, prepared for the Pacific Northwest Research Series.*

---

## Abstract

This paper traces the statutory, intellectual, and institutional history of Washington State's corporate law, from the Territorial incorporation statutes of the nineteenth century through the 1933 Washington Business Corporation Act, the 1965 mid-century modernization as Title 23A RCW, the 1989 enactment of the Washington Business Corporation Act (WBCA) as Title 23B RCW, the 2012 introduction of the Social Purpose Corporation in chapter 23B.25 RCW, the long period of incremental refinement between 2014 and 2023, and the 2024 amendments altering the default shareholder vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation. It situates Washington's statute within the larger intellectual lineage of the Model Business Corporation Act (MBCA) drafted under the auspices of the American Bar Association's Committee on Corporate Laws, compares the Washington approach to the Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL) model, and offers a comparative treatment of the "benefit corporation" movement in Maryland, Vermont, Delaware, and California. The essay closes with a philosophical reflection on the architectural choices embedded in Washington's statute: a preference for a small, well-designed, composable substrate over heavy prescription, a choice which places Washington in a particular and interesting position on the spectrum of American corporate law regimes.

The central thesis is that Washington's corporate law, like many MBCA-family statutes, is best understood not as a detailed prescription of corporate behavior but as a *leveraged substrate* — a compact layer of enabling rules, mandatory defaults, and opt-out provisions that trust the parties (drafters, boards, shareholders, and courts) to compose the rest. This architectural preference has shaped every major inflection point in the statute's history, from the 1965 adoption of the ABA model, through the 1989 recodification, to the 2012 Social Purpose Corporation experiment, to the 2024 vote threshold recalibration. Washington's statute rewards careful drafting. It does not reward passive reliance on defaults. Understanding that philosophical orientation is, in the author's view, the key to understanding the Act as a whole.

## 1. Introduction: Washington as an MBCA-Family Jurisdiction

To begin, a simple taxonomy. American corporate law has, since roughly the middle of the twentieth century, bifurcated into two principal traditions. The first is the Delaware tradition, anchored in the Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL), refined through case law by the Delaware Court of Chancery and Delaware Supreme Court, and exported through the mechanism of charter competition — the phenomenon by which corporations, particularly publicly held corporations, choose to incorporate in Delaware regardless of where their principal operations are located. The second is the Model Business Corporation Act tradition, a body of statutory text promulgated and periodically revised by the Corporate Laws Committee (formerly the Committee on Corporate Laws) of the Business Law Section of the American Bar Association, and adopted, in whole or in substantial part, by approximately thirty-six states.

Washington State sits firmly within the MBCA tradition. It has been there since 1965, when the Legislature enacted Title 23A RCW, which was "substantially patterned after the model business corporation act, which was prepared as a research project of the American Bar Foundation." It reaffirmed that tradition in 1989, when the Legislature replaced Title 23A with Title 23B — the Washington Business Corporation Act ("WBCA") — based on the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act ("RMBCA"). It has continued in that tradition through every subsequent amendment cycle, including the 2024 amendments. Washington is, in short, an MBCA jurisdiction, and to understand its corporate law one must first understand the model whose DNA pervades it.

But "MBCA jurisdiction" is a generic label, not a complete description. Each adopting state makes choices — which chapters to include, which sections to modify, which defaults to shift, which innovations to decline. Washington has made its share of these choices, and they are not trivial. The Washington statute diverges from the RMBCA in several meaningful respects, and in some areas — the Social Purpose Corporation, the treatment of forum selection provisions, the structure of indemnification — Washington has been a net contributor of ideas back to the model, or has innovated on its own. The task of this paper is to trace those choices in historical order, to identify the persons and institutions that made them, and to assess their consequences.

A note on sources and scope. This essay relies on primary source material where available: the Revised Code of Washington, the Session Laws of Washington, the published opinions of Washington appellate courts, official commentary from the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section, and the text of the MBCA and RMBCA. Where secondary sources are used, they are scholarly treatments published in law reviews, treatises, or ABA publications. The essay is educational rather than advisory; nothing in it constitutes legal advice or should be treated as a substitute for a practitioner's analysis of a specific question. The goal is to explain the statute as a historical artifact and as a piece of legal engineering — to show what it is, how it came to be, and why it has the shape that it has.

## 2. Pre-1989 Washington Corporate Law: The Long Arc from Territory to Title 23A

### 2.1 Territorial and Early Statehood Incorporation

Washington's experience with corporate law begins before statehood. The Washington Territory, organized by Act of Congress in 1853, adopted early general incorporation statutes modeled on those of the mid-nineteenth-century midwestern and eastern states. These early statutes were brief, formal, and largely concerned with the mechanics of chartering: the number of incorporators required, the contents of articles of association, the publication requirements, and the formal prerequisites for the issuance of stock. They reflected the dominant American approach to corporate chartering that had emerged after the Jacksonian-era revolt against special legislative charters: general incorporation acts under which any qualified group of citizens could, by filing specified documents and paying a fee, bring a corporation into existence without individualized legislative action.

The shift from special chartering to general incorporation is one of the great structural changes in nineteenth-century American law. In the early republic, corporations were regarded as creatures of the sovereign, brought into existence by individual acts of the legislature. Each charter was a special statute; the legislature negotiated with the incorporators about purposes, capital, duration, and powers; and the resulting corporate entity was understood as a kind of public-private partnership, with specific privileges and specific responsibilities. Banks, canals, turnpikes, bridges, and railroads were typically chartered in this way. The early special charters often included monopoly provisions, tax exemptions, and other privileges that we would today find startling, and they were accompanied by public duties — the obligation to serve all comers, to maintain facilities, to report to the legislature — that reflected the entity's quasi-public character.

The Jacksonian revolt against special chartering was driven by the perception that the special charter system was corrupt and anti-democratic. Charters were obtained through lobbying and political favor; the benefits flowed disproportionately to the well-connected; and the resulting corporations were regarded by many as engines of privilege rather than as vehicles for the orderly conduct of business. The response, which took hold gradually across the American states between the 1830s and the 1870s, was to replace special chartering with general incorporation: the enactment of statutes that set forth the qualifications and procedures for forming a corporation, and that permitted any qualified group of citizens to form a corporation by complying with the procedures, without any individualized legislative action. The New York Act of 1811 is often cited as the first general incorporation statute of broad scope in the United States, and by the late nineteenth century every state had adopted some form of general incorporation law.

Washington Territory followed this pattern. The territorial legislature adopted general incorporation statutes for various purposes — business corporations, mining corporations, railroad corporations, and eleemosynary corporations — and permitted the formation of corporations within the territory on terms that were broadly similar to those of other western territories of the same period. The territorial statutes were compiled and recompiled several times during the territorial period, and they were carried forward, in essential respects, into the early statutes of the new state.

Statehood came in 1889. The new state constitution included provisions — many of them typical of the constitutions of other states admitted in the late nineteenth century — addressing corporate matters. The Washington Constitution required that corporations be formed under general laws (Art. XII), prohibited special charters for private corporations, required the maintenance of a principal place of business or registered agent within the state, and imposed certain substantive limitations on corporate power. Professor Leslie J. Ayer of the University of Washington School of Law, writing in the Washington Law Review in 1933, observed that these constitutional provisions conferred on the Legislature a reserved power to alter or amend corporate charters, a reserved power that would become a recurring theme in Washington's corporate law jurisprudence. Ayer's article, "The New Washington Business Corporation Act — Reserved Power of Legislature," is one of the earliest scholarly treatments of the subject and remains cited in discussions of legislative authority over the internal affairs of Washington corporations.

The reserved power doctrine deserves more than a passing mention, because it has shaped Washington corporate law in subtle but important ways. The doctrine holds that when a state confers a corporate charter — whether through a special act in the pre-Jacksonian era, or through compliance with a general incorporation statute in the modern era — the state retains the power to alter or amend the charter, unilaterally, subject to certain constitutional limits. The doctrine was famously articulated in the United States Supreme Court's decision in *Trustees of Dartmouth College v. Woodward*, 17 U.S. (4 Wheat.) 518 (1819), which held that a corporate charter is a contract between the state and the corporation that is protected by the federal Contract Clause. Justice Story's concurrence in *Dartmouth College* suggested, however, that states could preserve their power to alter charters by including a reservation clause in their general incorporation statutes or constitutions. Most states, including Washington, took up this suggestion and included reserved-power provisions in their constitutions and statutes.

The practical effect of the reserved power doctrine in Washington has been to make Title 23B (and its predecessors) robustly amendable. When the Legislature amends the corporate statute, the amendments apply to existing corporations as well as to newly formed corporations, unless the amendment provides otherwise. Existing corporations cannot claim that the prior version of the statute is part of a vested contract that cannot be altered without compensation. This is an important architectural feature of Washington corporate law: it means that the statute is a living instrument that can be updated as circumstances change, without requiring the statute to "freeze" with respect to pre-existing corporations. The 2024 amendment to RCW 23B.10.030, discussed in section 8 below, is an interesting example: it reduces the default vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation, and it does so by a date-of-formation cutoff rather than a uniform change, reflecting both the reserved power (which would permit a uniform change) and a legislative judgment that existing corporations have settled expectations that should be preserved. Both the reserved power and the legislative judgment are real; the 2024 bifurcated approach honors both.

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries were a period of considerable growth in the population of Washington corporations. The state's economy in this period was dominated by resource extraction (timber, mining, fishing), transportation (railroads, shipping), and early manufacturing. Many of these industries were capital-intensive and required the corporate form to aggregate the necessary capital. The Washington State Archives contain records of thousands of corporations formed during this period, including timber companies in the Olympic Peninsula and Cascade foothills, mining companies in the Kootenai and Okanogan regions, shipping companies operating on Puget Sound and the lower Columbia River, and manufacturing firms in Seattle, Tacoma, Spokane, Everett, and other urban centers. The Corporations Division of the Secretary of State's office — then a much smaller operation than it is today — was the administrative face of corporate law for this growing population.

Among the noteworthy corporate forms that emerged in Washington during this period were specialized statutes for particular kinds of enterprises: mutual savings banks, mutual insurance companies, cooperatives, and various nonprofit organizations. These specialized statutes existed alongside the general business corporation statute and each had its own set of rules, filings, and administrative procedures. The modern Title 23B is confined to business corporations; nonprofit corporations are governed by Title 24 RCW, banks by Title 30A RCW, insurance companies by Title 48 RCW, and so on. This division of labor among titles reflects the specialization that has characterized American state corporate law since the late nineteenth century, and it means that a full account of "Washington corporate law" would have to address a number of adjacent titles in addition to Title 23B. This paper focuses on Title 23B because it is the principal business corporation statute and because it is the subject on which the user's research inquiry is centered, but the reader should be aware that the broader field is larger.

Between statehood and the 1930s, Washington corporate law existed as a patchwork of statutes addressing particular subjects — the formation of business corporations, foreign corporations qualifying to do business in the state, the annual report and license fee regime, the powers of directors, the rights of shareholders, and the dissolution of corporations. The result was workable but not elegant. It reflected the general condition of American corporate law in the first third of the twentieth century: a body of law that had grown by accretion, that had not been comprehensively rationalized, and that differed materially from state to state in ways that created friction for interstate commerce.

### 2.2 The 1933 Washington Business Corporation Act

#### 2.2.1 The Uniform Business Corporation Act of 1928 as Model

Before turning to the 1933 Washington Act itself, it is worth saying something about the Uniform Business Corporation Act of 1928, which served as its model. The Uniform Act was the product of the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws (NCCUSL), an organization founded in 1892 to draft uniform state laws and thereby reduce the legal fragmentation that had arisen from the federal system. NCCUSL's early successes included the Uniform Negotiable Instruments Law (1896), the Uniform Sales Act (1906), the Uniform Partnership Act (1914), and the Uniform Limited Partnership Act (1916). By the 1920s, NCCUSL had turned its attention to business corporations, which were then governed by an increasingly disparate set of state statutes.

The drafting of the Uniform Business Corporation Act took several years and was completed in 1928. The reporter for the project was a distinguished academic lawyer whose work had considerable influence on the final product. The Act was promulgated by NCCUSL in 1928 with the recommendation that the states adopt it. In practice, only a handful of states adopted the Uniform Act in substantial form — Washington among them — and NCCUSL's leadership eventually concluded that uniformity in corporate law was not an achievable goal. As noted above, in 1943 NCCUSL withdrew the Uniform Business Corporation Act and rechristened it as a "Model or State Business Corporation Act," acknowledging that states would want to depart from the uniform text in various ways.

The 1928 Uniform Act itself was, by the standards of its time, a modern and well-drafted statute. It included a coherent treatment of incorporation, capital structure, governance, amendments, mergers, dissenting shareholders, dissolution, and foreign corporations. It used par value stock as its basic capital unit and included the stated capital / surplus accounting concept that would later fall out of favor. Its treatment of director duties was relatively brief, leaving much to judicial interpretation. Its merger provisions were adequate but clumsy by later standards.

The 1933 Washington Act followed the 1928 Uniform Act closely in most respects. A careful comparison of the two statutes — not undertaken in this paper, but available to interested readers through the legislative archives and the Revised Code of Washington as it existed in the 1930s — would reveal that Washington adopted most of the Uniform Act's structural choices while making a handful of Washington-specific modifications to reflect local practice and local policy preferences. This is the same pattern that Washington would follow in 1965 with the first MBCA adoption and in 1989 with the WBCA: take a national model, adopt most of it, modify the parts that Washington practitioners wanted to modify, and codify the result.

#### 2.2.2 Enactment and Effective Date

In the 1933 legislative session, which convened on January 9, 1933, and adjourned on March 9, 1933, the Washington Legislature enacted a comprehensive new business corporation act. The bill was approved by the Governor on March 21, 1933, and became effective on January 1, 1934, codified as chapter 23.01 RCW and related chapters. The 1933 Act was patterned on the Uniform Business Corporation Act, a model statute drafted by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws (now the Uniform Law Commission) and promulgated in 1928. Washington was among the states that took up the Uniform Act relatively early. The 1933 Act replaced the earlier patchwork and established, for the first time in Washington, a single coherent statutory framework for the formation, governance, and dissolution of business corporations.

The historical context of the 1933 Act is worth pausing on. The United States was in the depths of the Great Depression. Banking reform, securities regulation (the federal Securities Act of 1933 was enacted the same year), and the New Deal more generally were reshaping the legal environment in which corporations operated. At the same time, public hostility to corporations, which had been intense in the Populist and Progressive eras, had begun to cool. As one historian of American corporate law has observed, "in the late 1920s, several states began modernizing their statutes, partly due to a lessening of public hostility against corporations." The 1928 Uniform Business Corporation Act was one product of that softening. The 1933 Washington Act was Washington's adoption of that moment's best thinking about how to write a corporate statute.

The 1933 Act had the virtues and the vices of its model. On the positive side, it imposed a coherent vocabulary — articles of incorporation, board of directors, registered agent, stated capital, par value, dissolution — on a body of law that had previously been fragmentary. It established mandatory minimum procedures for incorporation, for the election of directors, for the holding of shareholder meetings, for the amendment of articles, and for dissolution. It gave corporations a statutory list of powers and imposed certain mandatory limitations. On the negative side, it was a creature of its time: it retained the concept of par value stock (with attendant complications about stated capital and surplus), it was structured around fairly formal notions of corporate existence (with the de facto corporation doctrine and the corporation-by-estoppel doctrine surviving as judicial gap-fillers), and it reflected a relatively prescriptive regulatory impulse. The statutes of this era tended to tell corporations what they could and could not do, rather than setting default rules that could be varied by careful drafting.

Over the next three decades, the 1933 Act was amended repeatedly, in ways that addressed particular problems as they arose but did not revisit the overall architecture. By the early 1960s, there was a growing sense — among Washington practitioners, among the Washington State Bar Association's Corporations Committee, and among scholars at the University of Washington School of Law — that the Act had become dated. The par value concept, which still dominated the Act's treatment of stock, was increasingly regarded as a vestigial formality, creating accounting complications without serving any meaningful investor-protection function. The rules on mergers were clumsy. The treatment of close corporations — small, closely held entities with a handful of shareholders, which by the mid-twentieth century represented the overwhelming majority of Washington corporations by number — was awkward. And the national conversation about corporate law had moved on. The American Bar Association had published the first edition of the Model Business Corporation Act in 1950, and had been revising it steadily; many states had begun adopting it in whole or in part.

### 2.3 The 1965 Act: Title 23A RCW and the First MBCA Adoption

In the 1965 regular session, the Washington Legislature enacted chapter 53, Laws of 1965, a comprehensive new business corporation act codified as Title 23A RCW and effective July 1, 1967. Title 23A was Washington's first adoption of the Model Business Corporation Act. The official disposition notes for the statute state plainly that "the 1965 c 53 codified as Title 23A RCW is based upon the Model Business Corporation Act prepared by the Committee on Corporate Laws (Section of Corporation, Banking, and Business Law) of the American Bar Association." The 1965 Act adopted most of the ABA Model Act, with Washington-specific modifications reflecting local practice, local judicial decisions, and policy judgments made by the Washington State Bar Association's Corporations Committee.

Title 23A represented a substantial modernization. Among its key features:

- **Shareholder consent in lieu of meeting.** The 1965 Act permitted shareholder action by unanimous written consent, a feature particularly valuable for closely held corporations.
- **Statutory merger procedures.** The Act provided cleaner merger and consolidation procedures, with explicit appraisal rights for dissenting shareholders.
- **Director liability.** The Act codified standards of conduct for directors in terms that tracked the MBCA's fiduciary duty framework.
- **Indemnification.** The Act included a reasonably modern indemnification regime, modeled on the MBCA, under which corporations could indemnify directors and officers for liabilities and expenses incurred by reason of their service.
- **Dissolution and winding up.** The Act provided clearer procedures for voluntary and involuntary dissolution and for the administrative dissolution of delinquent corporations.

But Title 23A was not a perfect instrument. It retained the par value concept, which the MBCA itself would abandon in the 1979 revisions that moved the model toward a stated capital / board-authorized consideration model. It did not reflect the refinements in director duty of care and business judgment rule analysis that would mature over the 1970s and early 1980s, both in Delaware case law and in MBCA commentary. Its treatment of the close corporation was basic. And like all MBCA-family statutes of its generation, it was in some respects overdue for a recodification by the time the Revised Model Business Corporation Act was published in 1984.

### 2.4 Building Pressure for a Second Modernization

Between 1965 and the mid-1980s, three independent developments combined to put pressure on Washington to undertake a second corporate law recodification.

First, the RMBCA itself. The ABA's Committee on Corporate Laws, under successive chairmanships, completed a major revision of the MBCA in 1984, publishing the Revised Model Business Corporation Act. The 1984 revision was more than a tidying-up. It adopted a new numbering scheme, reorganized the chapters in a more logical flow, abandoned the par value concept in favor of a simpler authorized-consideration model, refined the statutory statement of director duties, modernized the indemnification provisions, and introduced a cleaner framework for mergers, dissenters' rights, and dissolution. The 1984 revision was widely acclaimed. States that had previously adopted the pre-1984 MBCA now had a natural opportunity to update their statutes.

Second, Delaware case law. During the 1980s, the Delaware Supreme Court and Court of Chancery handed down a series of decisions — *Smith v. Van Gorkom* (1985), *Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum* (1985), *Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings* (1986), and a number of others — that reshaped the understanding of director duties, particularly in the context of change-of-control transactions. These decisions drew attention to the role of the business judgment rule, the standards governing defensive measures in hostile takeovers, and the procedural protections required of boards considering significant transactions. MBCA jurisdictions found themselves asking whether their own statutes adequately addressed these issues, and whether they should codify protections against liability of the kind that Delaware had begun to allow in section 102(b)(7) of the DGCL (adopted by Delaware in 1986 in response to *Smith v. Van Gorkom*).

Third, local demand. Washington's bar, and particularly the Business Law Section of the WSBA, included a growing number of practitioners who were regularly advising Washington corporations on complex transactional and governance matters. These practitioners wanted a statute that was easier to work with, that provided clearer answers to common questions, that reduced the need to cross-reference outdated provisions of the 1965 Act, and that positioned Washington corporate law as a modern and credible regime. Washington was not in direct charter competition with Delaware for the charters of large public corporations — most large public corporations would continue to choose Delaware regardless — but it was in competition with neighboring states (California, Oregon, Idaho) and with the general Delaware default. A modern, well-drafted Washington statute would make Washington a more attractive home for the middle tier of closely held and mid-sized corporations, particularly those whose principal operations were in Washington and for whom out-of-state incorporation carried no meaningful advantage.

These three pressures converged in the second half of the 1980s, and by 1987 the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section had assembled a drafting group to prepare a comprehensive recodification.

## 3. The Model Business Corporation Act: Intellectual Lineage

Before turning to the 1989 Washington enactment, it is necessary to say more about the Model Business Corporation Act itself. Washington's statute is, to a large extent, a Washington-localized version of the RMBCA. Understanding the model is essential to understanding the statute.

### 3.1 Origins: The ABA Committee and the 1940s

The story of the MBCA begins in 1940. In that year, the American Bar Association's Section of Corporation, Banking and Business Law established a Committee on Corporate Laws — later renamed the Committee on Corporate Laws of the ABA Section of Business Law, and later still the Corporate Laws Committee of the ABA Business Law Section. The Committee's charge was to develop a model statute that would address the inconsistencies among state corporate laws and provide a coherent starting point for states that wanted to modernize.

The Committee's first major product appeared in 1946, under the title *Model for State Business Corporation Acts*. The 1946 draft drew heavily from the Illinois Business Corporation Act, a fact that has been attributed to the disproportionate Illinois representation on the drafting committee. Several of the key drafters were members of the Illinois bar, and they brought the Illinois statute's structure, vocabulary, and substantive choices to the ABA drafting table. As has been noted in scholarly accounts, "the Committee's work drew heavily from the Illinois Business Corporation Act, which is not surprising because several of the drafters were members of the Illinois bar."

The 1946 draft was an interim product. The Committee continued its work through the late 1940s and, in 1950, published what it regarded as the first full version of the Model Business Corporation Act. That 1950 version is the conventional starting point for the modern MBCA tradition. It is sometimes referred to in the literature as the "Model Business Corporation Act (Revised)," the "Revised" qualifier serving to distinguish it from the 1946 draft rather than from any later revision.

### 3.2 The Uniform Act Problem and the Choice of "Model"

One of the more interesting historical features of the MBCA is that it is a "model" act, not a "uniform" act. The distinction matters.

Until 1943, there had been a Uniform Business Corporation Act, drafted by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws and promulgated in its original form in 1928 (this was the Act on which Washington's 1933 statute was patterned). Uniform acts are designed to produce identical text across adopting states, in the service of interstate consistency. But the Uniform Business Corporation Act ran into resistance. State bar associations and legislatures were reluctant to surrender the ability to tailor corporate law to local circumstances. In 1943, the Uniform Business Corporation Act was formally withdrawn as a "uniform" act and renamed "A Model or State Business Corporation Act," reflecting a deliberate shift in philosophy. The revised posture was that states should have the opportunity to depart from the model in ways that would recognize special local considerations and would allow experimentation with different approaches to the issues. The ABA's subsequent work took up that model-act philosophy: publish a well-drafted template, invite adoption, and accept — indeed, anticipate — that each adopting state will modify the template in ways that reflect its own legal culture and policy judgments.

That philosophy has been one of the most important features of the MBCA tradition. Delaware, famously, has never adopted the MBCA. Many other states have adopted it only in part, or have adopted it and then drifted away from it through subsequent amendments that tracked Delaware innovations rather than MBCA updates. The result is a family of statutes that share a common DNA but exhibit real genetic variation. Washington, as we shall see, is a fairly faithful MBCA adopter but has its own set of Washington-specific mutations.

### 3.3 Key Drafters: Ray Garrett, Elliott Goldstein, and Robert Hamilton

A full prosopography of the MBCA's drafters would fill a volume of its own, but three names deserve mention in any history of the statute.

**Ray Garrett Jr.** (1920–1980) was a Chicago lawyer who served on the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws during the formative period of the MBCA. His article "History, Purpose and Summary of the Model Business Corporation Act," published in the journal *Business Lawyer* in 1950, is the standard early account of the Act. Garrett later served as chairman of the editorial committee for the Model Business Corporation Act Annotated, a research project of the American Bar Foundation. He was also Chairman of the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission from 1973 to 1975, a position that gave him a national platform from which to advocate for coherent and investor-protective corporate governance. Garrett represents the MBCA's early Chicago orientation and its close ties to federal securities regulation.

**Elliott Goldstein** was a principal drafter of the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act. His article "Revision of the Model Business Corporation Act," published in the *Texas Law Review* in 1985, is the leading contemporaneous account of the 1984 revision's goals, methods, and substantive choices. With Robert W. Hamilton of the University of Texas School of Law, Goldstein also published "The Revised Model Business Corporation Act" in *Business Lawyer* in 1983, a preview article that described the drafting process and signaled the direction of the revision to the bar.

**Robert W. Hamilton** (1931–2015), Minerva House Drysdale Regents Chair in Law Emeritus at the University of Texas School of Law, was arguably the single most influential academic in the MBCA tradition during the period from the late 1970s through the 2000s. He served as the reporter for the 1984 RMBCA revision, meaning that he was primarily responsible for drafting the text and the official commentary. Hamilton's treatises on corporate law, his casebooks, and his scholarly articles shaped the thinking of a generation of lawyers and judges in MBCA jurisdictions. He was not a Washington lawyer, but his influence on the 1984 RMBCA, and through it on Title 23B, is unmistakable.

Beyond these three, a list of the Committee on Corporate Laws chairs and members during the 1970s and 1980s would include dozens of distinguished practitioners and academics. The Committee operates on a rolling-membership basis, with new members added and old members rotating off on a regular cycle, so that the institutional memory is continuous but the composition evolves. One of the striking features of the MBCA project, viewed from a distance, is how much of the drafting work has been done by practicing lawyers on a volunteer basis — Committee members are not compensated for their drafting work, and the time commitment is substantial. The result is a statute that bears the fingerprints of an enormous number of people, no single one of whom can be credited as its author.

### 3.4 From MBCA (1950) to RMBCA (1984)

Between 1950 and 1984, the MBCA underwent many revisions, some substantial and some cosmetic. The most important milestones are worth noting briefly:

- **1950.** First full publication of the MBCA. The text is recognizably an ancestor of the modern statute but retains many features — par value, stated capital, formal corporate existence doctrines — that later revisions would abandon.
- **1960s.** Periodic amendments tracked state-level developments and clarified ambiguous provisions. Washington's 1965 adoption as Title 23A RCW was based on the MBCA as of that date.
- **1969.** A significant revision introduced a more modern treatment of dissenting shareholders' appraisal rights.
- **1979.** A major revision abandoned the par value concept and introduced a simpler treatment of authorized consideration for shares, one of the most consequential substantive changes in the Act's history.
- **1984.** The Revised Model Business Corporation Act. This was a comprehensive recodification rather than an incremental amendment. The 1984 revision reorganized the chapters, renumbered the sections (introducing the familiar "chapter.section" format in which, for example, section 8.30 refers to chapter 8 (directors and officers), section 30 (standards of conduct)), refined the fiduciary duty provisions, modernized indemnification, and generally brought the Act up to date with the state of the art in corporate law drafting.

The 1984 RMBCA is the statute on which Washington's 1989 Title 23B RCW is based. The 1984 RMBCA chapter numbering and section structure are largely preserved in Title 23B; readers who are familiar with the RMBCA will find their way through Title 23B without difficulty, and vice versa.

After 1984, the RMBCA continued to evolve. The Corporate Laws Committee publishes amendments periodically, typically after extended deliberation, exposure drafts, and public comment. Notable post-1984 amendments include the 1988 amendments (clarifying director liability standards), the 1998 amendments (streamlining merger and share exchange provisions), the 2016 revisions (which in some jurisdictions are referred to as the "MBCA (4th Edition)"), and most recently the 2024 comprehensive revision (the 75th anniversary edition). Washington has tracked some but not all of these amendments, in ways that will be described in the section on amendments below.

## 4. The 1989 Enactment of Title 23B RCW

### 4.1 The Drafting Process

The 1989 Washington Business Corporation Act was, as corporate recodifications in American states go, a relatively efficient and well-managed project. The principal drafting venue was the Washington State Bar Association, and specifically the Business Law Section of the WSBA. Within the Business Law Section, a Corporate Act Revision Committee was constituted to do the detailed drafting work. The Committee was composed of Washington practitioners with significant corporate law experience — large-firm transactional lawyers, small-firm general counsel, and academic advisors — together with liaison representatives from the Secretary of State's Corporations Division and the Legislature.

The Committee's mandate was to prepare a replacement for Title 23A RCW that would:

1. Track the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act in general structure and content;
2. Preserve those features of Title 23A that had worked well and that Washington practitioners were comfortable with;
3. Make Washington-specific modifications where Washington law, Washington practice, or Washington policy differed from the national default;
4. Fix drafting problems and clarify ambiguities that had accumulated in Title 23A over the preceding two decades;
5. Modernize the Act's treatment of areas (par value stock, merger procedures, director duties, indemnification) where the RMBCA represented a clear improvement; and
6. Produce a text that would be enacted by the Legislature with minimal amendment and that would be easy for Washington courts to apply.

The Committee worked through the late 1980s, producing draft after draft, each accompanied by commentary explaining the reasoning behind each section and identifying points of divergence from the RMBCA. When the drafts were substantially complete, the Committee presented them to the WSBA Board of Governors and, with the Board's endorsement, to the Legislature. The Committee's drafting commentary would later be preserved in the WBCA Sourcebook, a practitioner reference work published and periodically updated by the WSBA Business Law Section, which remains the leading single source of information about the Act's legislative history and intent.

### 4.2 The Legislative Enactment

The 1989 WBCA was introduced in the 51st Washington Legislature, which convened in January 1989. The bill was enacted as chapter 165 of the Laws of 1989 — the customary citation is "Laws of 1989, ch. 165" or "1989 c 165" — and became effective on July 1, 1990, giving existing corporations and their counsel a full year to prepare for the new regime. The Senate and House Journals of the 51st Legislature contain extensive documentation of the legislative process, including floor debate, committee reports, and amendments offered during passage. The Senate Journal in particular contains the official legislative history of many provisions; the Sourcebook's section-by-section commentary regularly cites specific pages of the 1989 Senate Journal (e.g., pages 2985-86, 2990, 2991-92, 3000, 3003-04, 3034-35, 3105-06) as authority for the understanding of particular provisions.

An important feature of the legislative record is that the Committee's official commentary — prepared by the WSBA Business Law Section and explaining the proposed amendments to the Board of Governors and to the Legislature — was adopted as part of the legislative history of the Act. This gives the Sourcebook's commentary a particular authoritative status: it is not merely a practitioner's treatise but a quasi-official document that Washington courts have treated as persuasive authority when interpreting ambiguous provisions of Title 23B.

### 4.3 Architecture: The Nineteen Chapters

Title 23B RCW, as enacted in 1989, is organized into nineteen chapters that closely track the 1984 RMBCA's organization. The chapters are, in order:

| Chapter | Short title | Subject matter |

| 23B.01 | General Provisions | Title, purpose, definitions, applicability, fees, service of process |
| 23B.02 | Incorporation | Incorporators, articles of incorporation, filing, organization meeting, bylaws |
| 23B.03 | Purposes and Powers | Corporate purpose, statutory powers, ultra vires |
| 23B.04 | Name | Corporate name requirements, reserved names, registered names, fictitious names |
| 23B.05 | Office and Agent | Registered office and registered agent requirements |
| 23B.06 | Shares and Distributions | Authorized and issued shares, classes and series, subscriptions, stock certificates, dividends and distributions |
| 23B.07 | Shareholders | Annual and special meetings, notice, quorum, voting, proxies, record dates, shareholder agreements |

| 23B.10 | Amendment of Articles and Bylaws | Procedures for amending articles of incorporation and bylaws |
| 23B.11 | Merger and Share Exchange | Original merger and share exchange provisions (now partially superseded by 23B.11A) |
| 23B.11A | Mergers and Share Exchanges | Modernized merger and share exchange provisions |

(The precise chapter count and any additions over time — 23B.11A, 23B.25, intervening reserved chapters — reflect the Act's growth over the three decades since 1989. The nineteen-chapter figure refers to the enacted 1989 architecture; later additions have brought the count to its current state, which practitioners sometimes refer to as "twenty-plus chapters" depending on what is counted.)

The architecture is deliberately flat. Each chapter is a self-contained treatment of a subject area. Within chapters, sections are numbered sequentially with the familiar chapter-section format: RCW 23B.08.300, for example, refers to chapter 8 (directors and officers), section 300 (general standards for directors), while RCW 23B.10.030 refers to chapter 10 (amendments), section 030 (amendment by board of directors and shareholders). This format is inherited from the RMBCA and is one of the MBCA tradition's most useful contributions to the clarity of American corporate statutes.

The sections themselves follow a drafting convention that is characteristic of the RMBCA: begin with the operative rule, follow with exceptions, follow with procedural requirements, follow with the consequences of non-compliance. Many sections include "default" rules that apply unless the articles of incorporation provide otherwise, and "mandatory" rules that cannot be varied. The Act is explicit about which rules are default and which are mandatory, a feature that makes it much easier to draft articles of incorporation and bylaws with confidence about what can and cannot be customized.

### 4.4 Washington-Specific Divergences from the 1984 RMBCA

Title 23B is not a slavish copy of the 1984 RMBCA. In a number of areas, the Washington Act diverged from the model in ways that reflected Washington practice, Washington case law, or Washington policy judgment. A partial list:

- **De facto corporation and corporation by estoppel.** Sections 23B.02.030 and 23B.02.040 (the Washington analogues of RMBCA sections 2.03 and 2.04) together abolished the de facto corporation doctrine and the corporation-by-estoppel doctrine in Washington. The Sourcebook commentary notes that the ABA Committee Comment from the RMBCA (Official Text at 44-46 (1984)) had concluded from a review of recent cases that the principal equitable consideration in applications of de facto and estoppel doctrines related to protecting persons who acted with the good faith belief that a corporation existed, and that this protection could be provided more cleanly through the filing and effective-date mechanisms of the Act itself. Washington accepted that reasoning and codified the abolition.
- **Director liability limitations.** Washington adopted a version of the RMBCA's director exculpation provisions (analogous to DGCL section 102(b)(7)) in RCW 23B.08.320, permitting corporations to limit or eliminate director personal liability for certain breaches of the duty of care. The scope of the Washington provision is somewhat narrower than Delaware's but broader than the default RMBCA provision, and it reflects specific Washington bar judgments about where exculpation is and is not appropriate.
- **Indemnification.** Chapter 23B.08 contains a detailed indemnification regime that draws on the RMBCA but includes Washington-specific provisions addressing, among other things, mandatory indemnification for successful defense, advancement of expenses, and the relationship between statutory indemnification and contractual indemnification.
- **Shareholder agreements.** RCW 23B.07.320 (the analogue of RMBCA section 7.32) authorizes shareholder agreements that depart from some of the Act's default governance rules. Washington's treatment of shareholder agreements is broadly in line with the RMBCA but includes some Washington-specific refinements, particularly with respect to the treatment of shareholder agreements in close corporations.
- **Dissenters' rights.** Chapter 23B.13 provides appraisal rights to dissenting shareholders in specified transactions. The Washington version tracks the RMBCA but has been amended over time to reflect Washington-specific judgments about the scope of appraisal.
- **Dissolution.** Chapter 23B.14 provides for voluntary and involuntary dissolution, with a Washington-specific procedure for administrative dissolution by the Secretary of State for failure to file annual reports or pay fees.

The divergences, taken individually, are incremental. Taken together, they give Title 23B a distinctly Washington character. A practitioner who has worked with MBCA-family statutes in other states — say, Oregon's Business Corporation Act (ORS chapter 60) or Idaho's Business Corporation Act — will find Title 23B immediately familiar, but will also notice the Washington-specific rules and will need to attend to them.

### 4.5 The "Minimal Leveraged Substrate" Approach

One of the defining features of the 1989 WBCA, and a theme to which this paper will return, is its preference for a "minimal leveraged substrate" approach to corporate law. By this I mean that the Act provides a small set of mandatory rules, a larger set of default rules that can be varied by the articles of incorporation or bylaws, and an explicit invitation for parties to customize the governance of their corporations through careful drafting.

Some examples. The Act does not prescribe a minimum or maximum number of directors; it provides a default of one or more and invites the articles or bylaws to specify a number. It does not prescribe how directors are elected; it provides a default of plurality voting and permits cumulative voting if the articles so provide. It does not prescribe the terms of officers; it provides a default and permits the bylaws to vary. It does not prescribe quorum or voting thresholds for shareholder action on most matters; it provides defaults and permits the articles or bylaws to vary (within limits). It does not prescribe the allocation of authority between the board and the shareholders; it provides defaults and permits shareholder agreements under RCW 23B.07.320 to reallocate that authority within broad limits.

The consequence is that a Washington corporation can have a governance structure that looks very similar to the Act's default structure, or it can have a governance structure that has been heavily customized. Both are permitted, and the Act supports both. The Act does not force a particular governance pattern on all corporations; it provides the building blocks and lets the parties assemble them.

This is the "leveraged substrate" idea. The substrate is small — the Act's mandatory rules, once identified, are relatively few — but the leverage is high, because a small number of well-chosen mandatory rules can produce a legal environment in which a wide range of corporate forms can flourish. The analogy to operating system design, or to the design of a programming language with a small core and a rich standard library, is not incidental. It reflects a particular attitude toward law-drafting: an attitude that trusts the parties to know what they need, that is skeptical of heavy prescription, and that prioritizes clarity and composability over comprehensive regulation. I return to this theme in the closing section of this paper.

## 5. Key Amendments and Judicial Interpretation, 1990–2011

Between the effective date of Title 23B on July 1, 1990, and the 2012 enactment of the Social Purpose Corporation chapter, the Washington Legislature amended Title 23B many times. Most of the amendments were incremental: clarifying ambiguous language, conforming Washington to subsequent RMBCA amendments, fixing drafting errors, and updating procedural rules to reflect changes in the Secretary of State's filing systems. A comprehensive enumeration of these amendments would be tedious and is outside the scope of this paper. I focus instead on a handful of substantively significant changes and on the judicial decisions that have shaped the understanding of the Act.

### 5.1 Legislative Amendments

**Director liability and exculpation.** Early in the statute's life, there were refinements to RCW 23B.08.320 (the director exculpation provision) to clarify the scope of permitted exculpation and to align the provision more closely with the evolving national consensus. Washington's exculpation provision permits articles of incorporation to eliminate or limit the personal liability of directors for certain breaches of the duty of care, but preserves liability for breaches of the duty of loyalty, for acts or omissions not in good faith, for intentional misconduct, and for knowing violations of law. This list of carve-outs is substantively similar to DGCL section 102(b)(7) but uses different wording and, in places, a somewhat broader scope of permissible exculpation.

**Indemnification.** Chapter 23B.08 has been amended several times to refine the indemnification regime. Key features that emerged from these amendments include the mandatory indemnification for directors and officers who successfully defend against claims (RCW 23B.08.520), the detailed advancement-of-expenses procedure (RCW 23B.08.530), and the explicit statement that statutory indemnification is non-exclusive — corporations may provide broader indemnification by contract, subject to public policy limits.

**Merger and share exchange.** Chapter 23B.11, the original merger and share exchange chapter, was significantly augmented by the addition of chapter 23B.11A, a modernized merger regime that permits more flexible transaction structures including domestications and conversions. The precise dating of this amendment and the details of its drafting are best understood by consulting the current WBCA Sourcebook and the relevant session laws; the point for present purposes is that Washington has kept its merger statutes reasonably current with national practice and has not let them drift into obsolescence.

**Annual reports and fees.** The procedural chapters governing annual reports, fees, and administrative dissolution have been amended repeatedly, in ways that have tracked the Secretary of State's operational needs and the transition from paper to digital filings. These amendments are administratively significant but do not change the substance of the corporate law regime.

**Conforming amendments.** A steady stream of conforming amendments has kept Title 23B aligned with other provisions of Washington law (for example, amendments to the Uniform Commercial Code, the Securities Act of Washington, the Limited Liability Company Act, and various tax statutes). These amendments are invisible to casual readers but are essential to the Act's functioning as part of a coherent commercial law environment.

### 5.2 Judicial Decisions

Washington appellate decisions interpreting Title 23B are not as numerous as Delaware decisions interpreting the DGCL — a fact that reflects Washington's smaller corporate docket rather than any deficiency in the statute — but they include several cases that are important for understanding how the Act has been applied in practice.

**Para-Medical Leasing, Inc. v. Hangen, 48 Wn. App. 389, 739 P.2d 717 (1987).** Although decided under the 1965 Title 23A rather than the 1989 Title 23B, this Court of Appeals decision has continued to be cited in post-1989 cases for its treatment of the business judgment rule. The facts are compact: in the summer of 1981, Dr. Gene Mason, the sole shareholder and director of Para-Medical (a small vehicle and equipment leasing company), hired Todd Hangen, a certified public accountant who had been Para-Medical's accountant for several years, to act as interim manager while Dr. Mason took a ten-month leave of absence to teach in Africa. Upon his return, Dr. Mason found that Para-Medical had been poorly managed, and Para-Medical sued Hangen for negligent mismanagement. The Court of Appeals applied the business judgment rule to Hangen notwithstanding his status as a temporary officer rather than a director, holding that the rule "shields the corporate officer from liability so long as he acts in good faith without a corrupt motive." The court rejected Para-Medical's argument that the business judgment rule applied only to directors, and affirmed the trial court's dismissal in favor of Hangen. *Para-Medical Leasing* is significant because it applied the business judgment rule broadly — not just to boards of directors but to corporate officers acting in a management capacity — and it did so in the context of a small, closely held Washington corporation, the kind of entity for which Washington's corporate law is most relevant.

**Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. v. Bucholz, 45 Wn. App. 502, 728 P.2d 597 (1986).** Another pre-1989 Court of Appeals decision that has continued to influence post-1989 jurisprudence. The case involved Edward M. Blackburn, Jr., a minority shareholder of Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. (IPA), a closely held Washington corporation, who brought suit against IPA and Terry M. Bucholz, IPA's majority shareholder, for various acts of self-dealing. The court's analysis included a clear statement of the fiduciary duty framework applicable to directors and officers of Washington corporations: "directors and officers stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation they serve and are not permitted to retain any personal profit or advantage gleaned 'on the side.'" In *Interlake Porsche*, Bucholz had, for the period 1980 through September 1984, expended a total of $534,797 in corporate funds for expenses that included "customer relations, demo expense, sales promotion, company vehicle expense, travel and entertainment, membership and dues, and owner's life insurance," with substantial amounts being for Bucholz's personal benefit. The court treated these expenditures as presumptively a breach of the duty of loyalty, required Bucholz to justify them, and — finding the justification inadequate — required disgorgement. *Interlake Porsche* has been cited in subsequent cases for the proposition that the duty of loyalty in Washington is robust and that corporate funds must not be used for personal benefit without board authorization.

**In re Spokane Concrete Products, Inc., 126 Wn.2d 269, 892 P.2d 98 (1995).** A Washington Supreme Court decision that has become a standard reference for the scope of the business judgment rule in Washington. The court held that the presumption provided by the business judgment rule — that directors, in making a business decision, acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation — "is not limitless." To overcome the presumption, the plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that the directors defrauded the corporation or were dishonest or incompetent — in other words, that they were disloyal or acted in bad faith. *Spokane Concrete Products* is one of the rare Washington Supreme Court decisions that squarely addresses the business judgment rule in the context of the 1989 WBCA, and it is an authority that practitioners rely on heavily when advising boards about the standards applicable to their deliberations.

**Grassmueck v. Barnett, 281 F. Supp. 2d 1227 (W.D. Wash. 2003).** A federal district court decision applying Washington corporate law. The court held that, under Washington law, plaintiffs may bring a general claim of breach of fiduciary duty against directors as long as they show that the directors' acts or omissions involved (1) intentional misconduct, (2) a knowing violation of law, (3) conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310 (the provision governing directors who discharge their duties in good faith under RCW 23B.08.300), or (4) any transaction from which the director will personally receive a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director is not legally entitled. *Grassmueck* is particularly useful as a practitioner's guide because it identifies, in a single passage, the four principal categories of conduct that pierce Washington's director exculpation provisions.

**Rodriguez v. Loudeye Corp., 144 Wn. App. 709, 189 P.3d 168 (2008).** A Court of Appeals decision that has become a standard reference for the elements of director fiduciary duties in Washington. The court articulated the three primary fiduciary duties of directors — care, loyalty, and good faith — in terms that track the RMBCA's duty framework and the Delaware case law as then stood. *Rodriguez* is frequently cited by Washington practitioners for its clean articulation of the duty framework and for its treatment of the interaction between director duties and shareholder remedies.

**Riss v. Angel, 131 Wn.2d 612, 934 P.2d 669 (1997).** Although primarily a condominium case, *Riss v. Angel* contains a widely cited statement of the general fiduciary duty standard applicable to directors of Washington nonprofit entities (and, by extension, of Washington business corporations): directors must "exercise ordinary care in performing their duties and to act reasonably and in good faith." The "ordinary care" formulation is characteristic of MBCA jurisdictions, in contrast to the Delaware "gross negligence" standard for duty of care claims, and *Riss* has been one of the authorities that has anchored Washington's ordinary-care formulation in the case law.

These cases, taken together, give a reasonable picture of Washington's judicial gloss on the 1989 WBCA as of the early 2010s. The business judgment rule operates as a rebuttable presumption; directors are protected when they act in good faith, on an informed basis, and without self-dealing; the duty of loyalty is robust and is enforced through disgorgement; exculpation provisions are effective within their stated scope but do not reach disloyalty, intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, or transactions from which directors derive improper personal benefit. Washington courts have applied these principles pragmatically, with attention to the specific facts of each case, and they have generally resisted the temptation to import Delaware case law wholesale. Washington is an MBCA jurisdiction, and its courts apply an MBCA-style duty framework, not a DGCL-style duty framework. The differences are real if sometimes subtle.

### 5.3 The Evolution of Indemnification

One area that deserves particular attention in this period is the evolution of Washington's indemnification regime. Indemnification — the corporation's commitment to reimburse directors and officers for liabilities and expenses incurred in their capacities as directors and officers — has always been an important feature of American corporate law, and it became more important in the 1980s and 1990s as the liability environment for directors became more complex.

Washington's 1989 WBCA, as enacted, included a detailed indemnification regime in RCW 23B.08.500 and following. The principal features were:

- **Mandatory indemnification** for directors who are wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise, in the defense of a proceeding (RCW 23B.08.520).
- **Permissive indemnification** for directors who meet the statutory standard of conduct (good faith and reasonable belief that conduct was in the corporation's best interests) (RCW 23B.08.510).
- **Advancement of expenses** for directors during the pendency of a proceeding, subject to certain undertakings (RCW 23B.08.530).
- **Court-ordered indemnification** where a court determines that it is fair and reasonable (RCW 23B.08.540).
- **Non-exclusivity.** The statutory indemnification regime is not exclusive; corporations may provide broader indemnification by bylaws, agreement, or other means, subject to certain limitations (RCW 23B.08.560).
- **Insurance.** Corporations are authorized to purchase and maintain directors' and officers' (D&O) liability insurance for directors, officers, employees, and agents (RCW 23B.08.560).

Subsequent amendments refined the mechanics. The concept of "advancement of expenses" evolved in the 1990s and 2000s as Delaware case law and academic commentary clarified what a meaningful advancement right looked like. Washington's provisions were amended to align with the best practices that emerged from that period. RCW 23B.08.603, for example, addresses the situation where an indemnification provision is amended or repealed after the conduct in question has occurred — a situation in which the indemnified person has a reasonable expectation that the prior provision will continue to apply. Washington's answer, like Delaware's, is that later amendments cannot retroactively reduce indemnification rights absent the indemnified person's consent.

The net effect of these refinements has been to make Washington's indemnification regime quite robust. Directors and officers of Washington corporations have strong statutory and contractual protection against personal liability for conduct within the scope of their duties, and the scope of those protections is clearer today than it was in 1989. That clarity is a function of careful drafting, careful amendment, and careful application by Washington courts.

## 6. The 2012 Social Purpose Corporation: Chapter 23B.25

### 6.1 Background: The Benefit Corporation Movement

The story of the Washington Social Purpose Corporation ("SPC") cannot be told without first describing the larger movement of which it is a part. In the late 2000s and early 2010s, a number of American states began experimenting with new corporate forms that would allow for-profit corporations to formally incorporate social and environmental goals into their mission statements and their directors' fiduciary duties. The motivation was twofold: first, to address a perceived gap in traditional corporate law, under which directors of for-profit corporations were thought to be obligated (or at least permitted) to prioritize shareholder financial returns above all other considerations; and second, to provide a visible signaling device by which entrepreneurs and investors could identify companies that were committed to a "double bottom line" or "triple bottom line" approach to business.

The movement had two principal strands. The first strand, associated with the nonprofit B Lab (founded in 2006), promoted a specific model statute — the Model Benefit Corporation Legislation — that provided for a tightly structured corporate form with mandatory directorial consideration of specified stakeholder interests, mandatory annual benefit reports, and third-party certification. The B Lab model was designed to produce strong accountability, strong public commitment, and strong brand differentiation. Maryland was the first state to adopt the B Lab model, in April 2010, and Vermont followed shortly thereafter (with legislation signed into law on May 19, 2010, and effective July 1, 2011). By the early 2010s, a growing list of states had followed — New Jersey, Virginia, Hawaii, California, and others.

The second strand was less prescriptive. California, in S.B. 201 (signed October 9, 2011, effective January 1, 2012), created a new corporate form called the "Flexible Purpose Corporation," which permitted but did not require directors to consider specified non-shareholder interests and which imposed lighter reporting obligations than the B Lab model. California's 2011 statute was, in effect, a compromise between the B Lab model and the traditional for-profit corporation, giving entrepreneurs a middle path between full benefit corporation status and ordinary for-profit status. In 2014, California renamed the Flexible Purpose Corporation as the "Social Purpose Corporation" by S.B. 1301 (signed by Governor Jerry Brown, effective January 1, 2015), and amended the statute in various ways to refine its operation. Importantly, California did not abandon the form; it renamed and enhanced it. California today has both Benefit Corporations (under the B Lab model) and Social Purpose Corporations (under the former Flexible Purpose Corporation regime), offering entrepreneurs a choice between the two.

Washington's 2012 enactment fits into this second strand. Washington chose to create a Social Purpose Corporation rather than a Benefit Corporation, and it did so in a way that paralleled California's then-recent Flexible Purpose Corporation experiment more closely than it paralleled Maryland's B Lab model.

### 6.2 The Washington Enactment: HB 2239 and the 2012 Legislative Process

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation was introduced in the 2012 regular session as House Bill 2239. The bill was the product of roughly two years of drafting work by the Corporate Act Revision Committee of the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section — a 13-member committee that had been working on the proposal since the early part of 2010. The Committee's chair, during the drafting, was an experienced Washington corporate lawyer, and the membership included practitioners from several major Washington firms, in-house counsel from Washington companies, and academic advisors.

The Committee's charge was to examine the benefit corporation movement and to recommend a Washington response. The Committee considered, and ultimately rejected, the direct adoption of the B Lab Model Benefit Corporation Legislation. The principal reasons for that rejection, as reflected in the Committee's working papers and in subsequent scholarship, were:

1. **Rigidity.** The B Lab model required directors to consider a specified list of stakeholder interests (employees, suppliers, customers, the community, the environment), and imposed a "general public benefit" obligation that the Committee regarded as potentially difficult to apply in practice and potentially ripe for litigation.
2. **Third-party certification.** The B Lab model contemplated, though did not strictly require, the use of third-party standards (such as B Lab's own certification regime) for measuring a corporation's benefit performance. The Committee was concerned that embedding third-party certification in state law would give a private organization (B Lab) a quasi-regulatory role over Washington corporations, a role that the Committee did not believe was appropriate.
3. **Reporting burden.** The B Lab model required annual "benefit reports" that disclosed the corporation's benefit performance against a specified standard. The Committee was concerned that the reporting burden might deter Washington entrepreneurs from adopting the form.
4. **Enforcement concerns.** The B Lab model contemplated enforcement of the benefit obligation through "benefit enforcement proceedings" brought by specified parties. The Committee was concerned that these proceedings could create a novel category of litigation risk that Washington corporate law had not previously addressed.

After considering these concerns, the Committee proposed, and the Legislature adopted, a different approach: the Social Purpose Corporation. The key features of the Washington SPC, as enacted in chapter 23B.25 RCW, are:

- **Statement of social purpose.** An SPC must state its social purpose in its articles of incorporation. The statement of purpose may be broad or narrow, general or specific; Washington law does not prescribe a particular form.
- **Director consideration.** Directors of an SPC *may* (but are not required to) consider the corporation's social purpose and the interests of non-shareholder constituencies when making business decisions. This is a permissive rather than mandatory framework, in contrast to the B Lab model.
- **Annual report.** An SPC must publish an annual report describing how it has advanced its stated social purpose. The report need not use any particular format and need not be audited by a third party.
- **No third-party certification.** Washington law does not require or endorse any particular third-party certification regime. An SPC is free to seek certification from B Lab or any other organization, but is not required to do so.
- **Shareholder remedies.** Shareholders of an SPC retain their ordinary corporate law remedies (derivative actions, direct actions, appraisal rights in specified transactions). The Act does not create a novel "benefit enforcement" proceeding.
- **Conversion.** Existing Washington corporations may convert to SPC status by amending their articles of incorporation, subject to the procedural requirements of chapter 23B.10.
- **Exit.** An SPC may cease to be an SPC by amending its articles of incorporation to remove the social purpose statement, again subject to procedural requirements.

This framework was the product of deliberate choice by the Committee and the Legislature. It is a light-touch framework, trusting Washington entrepreneurs to use the form responsibly and trusting ordinary corporate law remedies to police bad behavior. It is also, it should be said, a framework that has been criticized — most notably in scholarship published in the *Seattle Journal of Environmental Law* — as being insufficiently demanding, as creating a "halo" for corporations that adopt the form without imposing meaningful accountability. This critique is a matter of genuine disagreement among thoughtful people, and the present paper does not purport to resolve it. What can be said with confidence is that Washington's SPC represents a distinct approach to the benefit corporation problem, one that is meaningfully different from the B Lab model and from the Delaware PBC approach, and one that deserves to be understood on its own terms.

HB 2239 was signed by Governor Christine Gregoire on March 30, 2012, and became effective on June 7, 2012. The statute took effect as chapter 23B.25 RCW, a new chapter slotted into Title 23B between the existing chapters and preserving the Title 23B architecture. The Social Purpose Corporation was now part of Washington's corporate law toolkit.

### 6.3 The Nomenclature Choice: "Social Purpose" vs "Benefit"

A small but meaningful point deserves explicit attention: the choice of nomenclature. Washington did not call its new corporate form a "benefit corporation." It called it a "social purpose corporation." The difference in name is not accidental; it reflects the Committee's conscious decision to distinguish the Washington form from the B Lab model.

"Benefit corporation," in common usage, carries the specific connotation of the B Lab model — the tightly structured form with mandatory stakeholder consideration, mandatory benefit reports, and implicit or explicit third-party certification. Calling the Washington form a "benefit corporation" would have invited confusion, both with the B Lab certification regime (which is a private certification offered by B Lab to any company that meets its standards, whether or not the company is incorporated as a legal benefit corporation) and with the Maryland-style statutory benefit corporation regime.

"Social purpose corporation," by contrast, is a term that is associated with a lighter-touch, more flexible approach. California was using (or would shortly begin using) the same term, and the term captures the key idea without implying the specific B Lab structure. The nomenclature choice thus communicates a substantive message: this is not a B Lab benefit corporation; it is something different.

A Washington SPC can still apply for B Lab certification if it wishes. Certification and statutory form are independent. B Lab's own FAQs make clear that a company can be B Lab-certified regardless of its legal form, and many Washington companies that are not SPCs are B Lab-certified while many Washington SPCs are not. But the statutory form and the certification regime are conceptually separate, and the Washington nomenclature reinforces that separation.

### 6.4 The 2014 Corrective Amendments

As often happens with new legislation, experience with the 2012 SPC statute revealed a number of drafting issues — some technical, some substantive — that called for corrective amendments. In 2014, the Legislature adopted a set of amendments to chapter 23B.25 that addressed these issues. The amendments included:

- **Clarification of formation requirements.** Ambiguities in the original statute about what must be included in the articles of incorporation of an SPC were resolved, with clearer language about the required statement of purpose and the permissible scope of that statement.
- **Clarification of director duties.** The statute's treatment of director duties was refined to make clear that directors of SPCs are governed by the same fundamental fiduciary duty framework as directors of ordinary Washington corporations, with the addition of the permissive authority (not requirement) to consider the corporation's social purpose.
- **Clarification of conversion procedures.** The procedures for an existing corporation to convert to SPC status, and for an SPC to cease to be an SPC, were clarified.
- **Annual report format.** Requirements for the annual report were refined to ensure that the report would actually convey useful information about the corporation's social purpose activities, while preserving the flexibility to avoid imposing a one-size-fits-all template.

These amendments were not controversial. They represented the kind of iterative refinement that new corporate law regimes typically require during their first years of operation. The 2014 amendments left the fundamental structure of the SPC intact: a permissive, light-touch framework for for-profit corporations that wanted to signal and pursue social or environmental objectives alongside financial returns.

### 6.5 Washington's SPC in Operation

In the years since 2012, a modest but real population of Washington SPCs has formed. They include consumer brands, technology startups, professional services firms, and various mission-driven businesses. The Washington Secretary of State's Corporations and Charities Filing System provides the SPC form as an option on its online incorporation workflow, and the form is listed in the Secretary of State's forms library. Academic commentary on the Washington SPC has been mixed, as noted above; proponents have praised the form's flexibility and low barrier to entry, while critics have argued that the absence of mandatory stakeholder consideration, third-party standards, and enforceable benefit obligations makes the form less meaningful as an accountability mechanism.

Both sides of that debate have a point. The Washington SPC is a less demanding form than the B Lab benefit corporation, and that is by design. Whether the lesser demand produces meaningful results depends on the intentions and practices of the particular corporations that adopt the form. In a jurisdiction that trusts the parties and provides a small leveraged substrate, the outcomes are driven by the users of the substrate, not by the substrate itself.

## 7. The 2014–2023 Refinement Period

Between the 2012 SPC enactment and the 2024 amendments, Washington corporate law entered a period of incremental refinement. The principal amendments during this period tracked the evolution of the RMBCA, responded to particular concerns raised by Washington practitioners, and updated procedural rules to reflect changes in how business is done. I highlight a handful of the most notable changes.

### 7.1 Forum Selection Provisions: RCW 23B.02.080

RCW 23B.02.080, the forum selection provision of Washington's corporate law, authorizes articles of incorporation or bylaws to require that specified categories of "internal corporate proceedings" be commenced and maintained exclusively in a specified court or courts. The provision was added to Title 23B during this period (2014–2023), following parallel developments in Delaware (where the issue had been litigated and ultimately resolved through the 2015 amendments to the DGCL) and in the RMBCA.

The provision's scope is broad but bounded. An "internal corporate proceeding" is defined as any proceeding asserting a claim based on a violation of a duty under Washington law by a current or former director, officer, or shareholder in such capacity; any proceeding commenced or maintained in the right of the corporation (i.e., a derivative action); any proceeding asserting a claim arising pursuant to any provision of the Act or the corporation's articles of incorporation or bylaws; and any proceeding asserting a claim concerning the internal affairs of the corporation. The provision thus covers the core categories of intra-corporate litigation — derivative actions, breach of fiduciary duty claims, claims under the corporate statute, and internal affairs claims — while leaving ordinary commercial litigation (contract claims, tort claims against third parties, etc.) unaffected.

The provision is limited by several important safeguards. A forum selection provision may not confer jurisdiction on any court, over any person, or of any proceeding; it may not prohibit commencing or maintaining an internal corporate proceeding in the courts of Washington; and it may not require claims asserted in an internal corporate proceeding to be determined by arbitration. In other words, a Washington corporation may specify Washington courts (or specified Washington courts) as the exclusive venue, but it cannot push litigation outside Washington (absent a reasonable relationship with another jurisdiction), cannot eliminate Washington courts from the list of permitted venues, and cannot substitute arbitration for litigation. These limits reflect Washington's judgment that the corporate law relationship is a creature of Washington law and that Washington courts should always be available to adjudicate disputes about it.

The forum selection provision has been useful to Washington corporations that want to avoid the risk of multi-jurisdictional shareholder litigation. It provides a degree of certainty about where internal disputes will be resolved, which in turn makes the corporate law environment more predictable. It is one of the clearest examples of Washington's willingness to update Title 23B in response to evolving national practice, and it illustrates the statute's architectural preference for providing opt-in customization rather than imposing mandatory rules.

### 7.2 Emergency Bylaws and Emergency Powers

Another set of amendments during this period addressed emergency situations — the possibility that a corporation might need to operate under unusual circumstances (natural disaster, terrorist attack, pandemic, loss of key personnel) in which normal governance procedures were impractical. The amendments added provisions permitting the adoption of emergency bylaws that would take effect under specified emergency conditions and that would authorize the corporation to take specified actions (hold meetings remotely, relocate, designate alternate directors and officers, etc.) during the emergency.

These amendments were, in retrospect, prescient. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020–2022 created exactly the kind of situation that the emergency bylaws provisions were designed to address: widespread inability to hold in-person meetings, need for remote deliberation, occasional difficulty in maintaining quorum with directors unable to travel or become ill. Corporations with well-drafted emergency bylaws were better positioned to navigate the pandemic than those relying on default provisions, and the Washington emergency bylaws framework proved useful in practice.

### 7.3 The 2020 Business Opportunity Disclaimer Amendments

In 2020, the Washington Legislature enacted amendments to RCW 23B.02.020 and related provisions to permit articles of incorporation to include a "business opportunity disclaimer." The amendments, adopted as 2020 c 194 § 3 (building on earlier 2019 c 141 § 1), address a specific problem that had become increasingly significant in venture capital and private equity contexts: the problem of directors and affiliates who sit on multiple boards and who, in the ordinary course, encounter business opportunities that might be of interest to more than one of their portfolio companies.

Under traditional corporate law, the "corporate opportunity" doctrine provides that a director or officer may not personally take advantage of a business opportunity that properly belongs to the corporation. The rule is a specific application of the duty of loyalty. But the rule creates practical difficulties for venture capital firms and private equity funds, which routinely place their partners on the boards of portfolio companies and which may encounter opportunities that are of interest to multiple portfolio companies (or to the fund itself). Without a clear rule, such firms face a risk of litigation every time they allocate opportunities among competing claimants, and that litigation risk can chill legitimate investment activity.

The 2020 amendments address this problem by permitting corporations to include in their articles of incorporation a provision that limits or eliminates the duty of directors or other specified persons to offer the corporation the right to have or participate in business opportunities prior to the director's or other person's pursuit or taking of the opportunity. The provision must be adopted in the manner specified by the statute (typically at formation or by shareholder-approved amendment), and it operates within the framework of RCW 23B.08.735, the underlying "business opportunities" provision of the directors chapter.

The effect of the 2020 amendments is to give Washington corporations a flexible tool for opting out of the default corporate opportunity doctrine, while preserving the doctrine as the default. A corporation that does not include a business opportunity disclaimer in its articles is governed by the default rule; a corporation that includes a disclaimer has opted into a different framework. This is another example of the Washington statute's architectural preference for defaults that can be varied by careful drafting, rather than mandatory rules that apply to all corporations regardless of circumstance.

### 7.4 Other Refinements

A number of other amendments during this period deserve brief mention:

- **Electronic filings and digital signatures.** Amendments to the procedural chapters permitted and, in some cases, required electronic filings and digital signatures. These amendments tracked the evolution of the Secretary of State's Corporations and Charities Filing System from a paper-based to a digital operation.
- **Registered agent requirements.** Amendments clarified the requirements for registered agents, including the permissibility of commercial registered agent services and the procedures for changing registered agents.
- **Record retention.** Amendments updated the record retention requirements to reflect modern record-keeping practices, including the use of electronic records.
- **Annual report content.** Amendments refined the content of annual reports to ensure that the Secretary of State received the information needed to maintain an accurate record of Washington corporations.
- **Conforming amendments.** A steady stream of conforming amendments kept Title 23B aligned with other provisions of Washington law and with evolving national practice.

The refinement period also included ongoing work by the WSBA Business Law Section, which convened periodically to review the statute, identify issues, and propose amendments. The Section's work is reflected in the current edition of the WBCA Sourcebook and in the drafts of legislative proposals that the Section has submitted to the Legislature over the years.

## 8. The 2024 Amendments: Recalibrating the Default Vote

The most recent significant amendment to Title 23B is the 2024 amendment to RCW 23B.10.030, which changed the default shareholder vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation. The amendment was enacted as section 20 of chapter 22 of the Laws of 2024 — the customary citation is "Laws of 2024, ch. 22" or "2024 c 22" — and it took effect on August 1, 2024.

### 8.1 The Before and After

Before the 2024 amendment, RCW 23B.10.030 provided the following default voting rule for amendments to the articles of incorporation: to approve an amendment, the corporation's shareholders had to approve the amendment by the affirmative vote of two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment, and two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment by each other voting group entitled to vote separately on the amendment. The only exception was for public companies, for which the default was a simple majority rather than two-thirds.

After the 2024 amendment, the default for corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, is a simple majority of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment, together with a simple majority of the votes entitled to be cast by each other voting group entitled to vote separately on the amendment. For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the prior two-thirds default continues to apply (again, with the public-company exception for a simple majority).

The 2024 amendment also preserved the ability of articles of incorporation to specify a different vote threshold than the default, provided that the required vote is not less than a majority of all votes entitled to be cast on the amendment and of each other voting group entitled to vote separately on the amendment. In other words, articles may increase the threshold (to two-thirds, three-fourths, unanimity, etc.) but may not decrease it below a majority.

The net effect is a shift in Washington's default voting rule for amendments: from two-thirds (pre-August 1, 2024, non-public companies) to a simple majority (post-August 1, 2024, all companies, subject to articles specifying otherwise). Corporations formed before the effective date continue to operate under the prior default unless and until they amend their articles of incorporation to adopt the new default.

### 8.2 Why the Change?

The change is not a radical departure from national practice. Most MBCA-family states have moved, or are moving, toward a simple majority default for amendments to the articles of incorporation. Delaware's DGCL (section 242(b)) has long provided a simple majority default. The RMBCA has been revised in a similar direction. Washington's pre-2024 two-thirds default was, by the late 2010s, an outlier among American corporate statutes, and the change brought Washington into line with the national consensus.

The policy rationale for the change is grounded in a particular view of the relationship between shareholders and the corporation. Under the traditional view, amendments to the articles of incorporation were regarded as fundamental changes to the corporate contract, and fundamental changes deserved a supermajority threshold to protect minority shareholders. Under the modern view, articles of incorporation are regarded as subject to ordinary majority governance, with specific protective provisions (class voting, appraisal rights, dissenters' rights) available to protect minorities against particular kinds of changes. The modern view allows the corporation to adapt to changing circumstances without requiring a supermajority, while still providing meaningful protections for shareholders who are adversely affected by specific amendments.

The practical effect of the 2024 amendment for newly formed Washington corporations is to reduce the friction of article amendments. A corporation that needs to adjust its capitalization, change its name, add or remove provisions about director duties, add a forum selection clause, convert to SPC status, or make any of the many other changes that amendments to articles typically accomplish, can do so with a simple majority of its shareholders rather than a two-thirds supermajority. This is a meaningful operational improvement, particularly for venture-backed startups that may need to amend their articles frequently during early-stage financing rounds.

For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the change has less immediate effect. Those corporations continue to operate under the two-thirds default unless and until they amend their articles. Many will amend their articles at the next convenient opportunity (e.g., at the next financing round, or when another amendment is needed for other reasons) to adopt the new default; others will retain the two-thirds default, either because they prefer it or because the cost of amendment is not worth the benefit.

The bifurcation of the default by date of formation is an interesting drafting choice. It preserves the settled expectations of existing corporations — which were formed on the understanding that a two-thirds vote would be required for amendments, and whose shareholders may have made investment decisions in reliance on that expectation — while shifting the default for new corporations to align with national practice. The Washington drafters could have applied the new default to all corporations regardless of formation date, but that would have been a retroactive change that altered the contractual expectations of existing shareholders. They could have applied the old default to all corporations, but that would have preserved Washington's outlier status. The bifurcated approach gives new corporations the benefit of the modern default while leaving old corporations with the familiar rule, and it allows old corporations to opt into the new rule at their own pace.

### 8.3 The Broader 2024 Package

Chapter 22 of the Laws of 2024 is a larger package than the single amendment to RCW 23B.10.030. It includes a range of amendments to Title 23B that address various procedural and substantive issues, most of them incremental refinements rather than fundamental changes. Section 20 of the chapter is the provision that amends RCW 23B.10.030; other sections amend other provisions of Title 23B. The package was the product of continuing work by the WSBA Business Law Section and reflects the Section's ongoing stewardship of the Washington corporate statute.

The 2024 amendments are, in a sense, a good marker of the current state of Washington corporate law. The statute is well-maintained. The Bar Association section that oversees it is active and engaged. The Legislature is willing to enact refinements when they are proposed. The courts are applying the statute in ways that are generally consistent with the drafters' intent. And the statute continues to evolve in response to changing commercial realities and evolving national practice. That is, in the large, what a well-functioning state corporate law regime looks like.

## 9. People and Institutions: The Secretariat of Incorporation

A corporate law statute is not self-executing. It operates through institutions — the Secretary of State's Corporations Division, the Washington State Bar Association, the Washington Supreme Court and Court of Appeals, the Washington Legislature, and the broader community of Washington corporate law practitioners. This section says a little about each of these institutions and about some of the people who have shaped them.

### 9.1 The Washington Secretary of State's Corporations Division

The Washington Secretary of State is a constitutional officer, elected statewide, responsible for a wide range of functions including elections administration, corporations and charities filings, archives, and library services. Within the Secretary of State's office, the Corporations and Charities Division (sometimes called simply the Corporations Division) is responsible for the administration of the corporate law regime: receiving and processing articles of incorporation, annual reports, and other filings; maintaining the corporate registry; issuing certificates of good standing; administering the dissolution of delinquent corporations; and serving as the registered office for service of process on corporations without a current registered agent in the state.

The Corporations Division's history is a history of gradual modernization. Early in the twentieth century, the Division operated on a paper-and-ledger basis: articles of incorporation were filed in person or by mail, entries were made in bound registers, and searches of the corporate registry required physical inspection of the records. This was the standard mode of operation for state corporations offices throughout the country in the early twentieth century.

**The UBI system and the 1977 modernization.** In 1977, Washington undertook a significant modernization of its corporate and tax administration with the introduction of the Unified Business Identifier (UBI) system. The UBI is a nine-digit number assigned to each business entity registered with the state, and it serves as a universal identifier across multiple state agencies — not just the Secretary of State's Corporations Division, but also the Department of Revenue, the Department of Labor and Industries, the Employment Security Department, and others. Before the UBI system, each state agency maintained its own identification numbering scheme, and cross-agency matching of business records was cumbersome. After the UBI system, all agencies shared a common identifier, which dramatically improved the coherence of the state's regulatory relationship with businesses.

The UBI system has been expanded over time to include additional fields, to interface with additional agencies, and to support online filings. Today, the Washington Business License application (administered jointly by the Department of Revenue and the Secretary of State) assigns a UBI to any new business entity, and the UBI follows the entity throughout its lifetime. When a corporation is formed under Title 23B, it receives a UBI at the time of its initial filing, and that UBI is the principal identifier by which the state tracks it thereafter.

**The transition from paper to digital.** During the 1990s and 2000s, the Corporations Division transitioned from paper filings to digital filings. The transition was gradual, with paper filings remaining available alongside digital options for many years. Today, the Division operates the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS), an online platform that supports electronic incorporation, electronic amendments, electronic annual reports, and electronic searches of the corporate registry. The CCFS represents a significant improvement in convenience and efficiency for Washington corporate filers, and it has reduced the Division's administrative burden. It has also produced a publicly searchable database of Washington corporations that makes Washington corporate information much more accessible than it was in the paper era.

**Current leadership.** The current Secretary of State of Washington is Steve Hobbs, who assumed office in 2021 following his appointment by Governor Jay Inslee after the resignation of the previous Secretary of State, and who was subsequently elected in his own right. Secretary Hobbs has continued the Division's ongoing modernization work and has overseen the CCFS during its current period of operation. Previous Secretaries of State — including Kim Wyman, Sam Reed, and Ralph Munro — each contributed to the modernization of the Corporations Division over their respective tenures.

The Corporations Division is the public face of Washington corporate law for most Washington business owners. Most Washington entrepreneurs' interaction with Title 23B consists of filing articles of incorporation with the Division, filing annual reports with the Division, and occasionally filing amendments with the Division. The Division's efficiency and responsiveness matter enormously for the practical experience of Washington corporate law, and the Division has generally performed well in this respect. The current CCFS system, while not without its occasional glitches, is among the more capable online corporations filing systems operated by American state governments.

### 9.2 The Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section

The Washington State Bar Association (WSBA) is the statewide bar association for Washington lawyers, with mandatory membership and regulatory authority delegated by the Washington Supreme Court. Within the WSBA, the Business Law Section is a voluntary membership section for Washington lawyers whose practice includes business law, including corporate law, commercial transactions, mergers and acquisitions, securities, and related fields.

The Business Law Section has been, for most of the history of modern Washington corporate law, the principal venue for drafting and reviewing corporate law legislation. The Section convenes committees — including, most notably, the Corporate Act Revision Committee — to propose, draft, and review amendments to Title 23B. The Section's work product is submitted to the WSBA Board of Governors for approval, and then to the Legislature for consideration. The Section also maintains the WBCA Sourcebook, a practitioner reference that collects the Act, the legislative history, the Committee's commentary, and selected judicial case notes. The Sourcebook is revised periodically, with the 5th edition having been published in 2016.

The Section is a voluntary organization, and its members serve on committees without compensation. This is in keeping with the broader American tradition of bar-led law reform, in which practicing lawyers contribute their time to the improvement of the legal system. The quality of Washington's corporate law is, in significant measure, a reflection of the quality of the Business Law Section's work over the decades since the 1989 WBCA was enacted. The Section has been well-served by a succession of dedicated chairs and committee members, many of whom have devoted substantial portions of their careers to the stewardship of the statute.

### 9.3 Washington Courts

Washington's state courts — principally the Washington Supreme Court, the three divisions of the Washington Court of Appeals, and the superior courts of the thirty-nine counties — are the forum in which Washington corporate law is applied to specific disputes. The federal district courts in Washington (the Western District and the Eastern District) also hear corporate law cases, typically in the context of diversity jurisdiction or federal question jurisdiction over related federal claims.

Washington's corporate law docket is not as large as Delaware's — Washington does not have a specialized court of chancery, and Washington corporate law cases are mixed in with all other civil cases on the dockets of generalist judges — but it is large enough to have produced a respectable body of case law. The cases discussed in section 5 above (*Para-Medical Leasing*, *Interlake Porsche*, *Spokane Concrete Products*, *Grassmueck*, *Rodriguez*, *Riss v. Angel*) represent some of the most important decisions, but they are not the only ones. A comprehensive survey of Washington corporate law case law would fill a treatise.

Washington courts have generally been thoughtful in their application of Title 23B. They have been willing to look to Delaware case law for guidance on issues that arise infrequently in Washington, while maintaining appropriate independence and recognizing that Washington is an MBCA jurisdiction with its own statutory framework. They have been attentive to the WSBA Business Law Section's commentary when interpreting ambiguous provisions. And they have generally applied the business judgment rule, the fiduciary duty framework, and the indemnification provisions in ways that are faithful to the drafters' intent.

### 9.4 Washington Practitioners and the Practicing Bar

Finally, the people. Washington corporate law is applied in practice by a community of practicing lawyers — corporate lawyers at major Washington firms, small-firm general counsel, in-house counsel at Washington companies, and solo practitioners advising closely held businesses and startups. This community is the day-to-day user of Title 23B, and its collective experience, captured and transmitted through bar publications, continuing legal education programs, and the WBCA Sourcebook, is the principal means by which the statute is interpreted and applied in practice.

A full prosopography of Washington corporate law practitioners is beyond the scope of this paper, and would in any event risk being either incomplete or invidious. It is worth noting, though, that the Washington corporate bar has been historically productive of national leaders: Washington practitioners have served on the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws, have contributed to the RMBCA drafting process, and have published scholarship in national journals. The relationship between the Washington corporate bar and the national MBCA community has been close and productive, and Washington's corporate statute is a product of that relationship.

## 10. Comparative Benefit Corporation History

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation, as discussed in section 6, is part of a larger American experiment in creating new corporate forms that accommodate social and environmental objectives alongside shareholder financial returns. This section situates the Washington approach in the broader comparative context.

### 10.1 Maryland: The First Mover (April 2010)

Maryland was the first American state to enact benefit corporation legislation. The Maryland Benefit Corporation Act was signed into law in April 2010 and took effect on October 1, 2010. The statute was modeled directly on B Lab's Model Benefit Corporation Legislation and represents the paradigmatic B Lab approach. Its principal features are:

- **Mandatory purpose.** A benefit corporation must have a purpose of creating "general public benefit," defined as "a material, positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole, assessed against a third-party standard."
- **Specific benefits.** A benefit corporation may also identify one or more "specific public benefits" in its articles.
- **Director duties.** Directors must consider, in addition to shareholder interests, the interests of employees, suppliers, customers, the community and local, regional, national and global environment, the short-term and long-term interests of the corporation, and the ability of the corporation to accomplish its general and specific public benefit purposes.
- **Annual benefit report.** A benefit corporation must prepare and publish an annual benefit report that assesses its overall social and environmental performance against a third-party standard, and must deliver the report to shareholders and post it on the corporation's website.
- **Benefit enforcement proceedings.** Specified parties — the corporation itself, shareholders meeting a threshold ownership test, directors, and specified other parties — may bring a "benefit enforcement proceeding" to enforce the corporation's benefit obligations.
- **Third-party standards.** The statute does not require certification by a particular organization, but it requires the use of a "third-party standard" for assessing benefit performance, and B Lab's own standards are the most commonly used.

The Maryland statute is a fairly prescriptive framework. It imposes specific director duties, specific reporting requirements, and specific enforcement mechanisms. It is not the only way to structure a benefit corporation, but it is the approach that B Lab has championed and that has been adopted (with variations) by most of the states that have enacted benefit corporation statutes. As of the mid-2020s, more than thirty states have adopted statutes in the Maryland/B Lab tradition.

### 10.2 Vermont: The Close Second (May 2010, effective July 2011)

Vermont followed Maryland closely. The Vermont Benefit Corporations Act (added by Acts 2009, No. 113 (Adjourned Session), § 1) was signed into law on May 19, 2010, and took effect on July 1, 2011. The Vermont statute is codified at 11A V.S.A. chapter 21. Like the Maryland statute, it is based on the B Lab Model Benefit Corporation Legislation and follows the B Lab template closely: mandatory general public benefit purpose, specified stakeholder consideration by directors, annual benefit reports assessed against third-party standards, and benefit enforcement proceedings.

Vermont's adoption is historically significant as the second state to enact a benefit corporation statute, and it has been widely cited as evidence that the B Lab model could gain traction beyond its original home in Maryland. Vermont has a long tradition of innovative policy-making in areas of environmental and social concern, and the adoption of benefit corporation legislation was consistent with that tradition.

### 10.3 California: Two Forms in Parallel (2012 and onward)

California took a different approach. In 2011, Governor Jerry Brown signed two separate bills into law: one creating a California "Benefit Corporation" based on the B Lab model, and the other creating a California "Flexible Purpose Corporation" based on a lighter-touch model. Both statutes took effect on January 1, 2012. California thus became the first state to offer two parallel benefit-style corporate forms.

The California Benefit Corporation tracks the B Lab model closely and is similar in structure to the Maryland and Vermont statutes. The California Flexible Purpose Corporation was a different animal: a for-profit corporation that could identify one or more specific social or environmental purposes in its articles, that permitted (but did not require) directors to consider those purposes alongside shareholder interests, that required an annual special purpose report but without the B Lab model's third-party standard requirement, and that did not include the benefit enforcement proceeding mechanism.

In 2014, the California Legislature adopted S.B. 1301, which renamed the Flexible Purpose Corporation as the "Social Purpose Corporation" (S.B. 1301 was signed by Governor Brown and took effect on January 1, 2015). The renaming was accompanied by a number of clarifying amendments but did not alter the fundamental character of the form. California thus today offers both Benefit Corporations (in the B Lab tradition) and Social Purpose Corporations (in the Flexible Purpose Corporation tradition), giving entrepreneurs a choice between the two.

It is worth noting explicitly — because there is some confusion about this in the literature — that California did not phase out the Social Purpose Corporation. The form remains available, and California entrepreneurs can still elect to form one. What changed in 2014 was the name, not the availability.

Washington's 2012 SPC, enacted at almost the same time as California's Flexible Purpose Corporation, is conceptually aligned with the Flexible Purpose / California Social Purpose Corporation tradition rather than the B Lab Benefit Corporation tradition. The two Washington/California lighter-touch approaches are cousins; they share the basic design philosophy of permissive rather than mandatory stakeholder consideration, the avoidance of third-party certification requirements, and the preservation of ordinary corporate law remedies rather than novel enforcement mechanisms.

### 10.4 Delaware: The Public Benefit Corporation (2013, amended 2015 and 2020)

Delaware, as usual, waited and watched before adopting. On August 1, 2013, Delaware amended its General Corporation Law to authorize a new form of corporation, the "Public Benefit Corporation" (PBC), under subchapter XV of the DGCL (sections 361 through 368). The Delaware PBC statute is an intermediate design, neither as prescriptive as the B Lab model nor as permissive as the California Flexible Purpose Corporation / Washington SPC model.

Key features of the original 2013 Delaware PBC statute:

- **Balancing requirement.** A PBC is required to be managed in a manner that balances (i) the pecuniary interests of the stockholders, (ii) the best interests of those materially affected by the corporation's conduct, and (iii) the specific public benefit or public benefits identified in the corporation's certificate of incorporation.
- **Specific purpose.** A PBC must identify in its certificate of incorporation the specific public benefit(s) it will pursue.
- **Director duties.** Directors must balance the three interests listed above, and they are not liable for the actions or inactions taken in pursuit of the balance so long as they are informed, disinterested, and act with the care expected of directors of a Delaware corporation.
- **Periodic reports.** A PBC must provide biennial (not annual) reports to its stockholders describing its pursuit of the identified public benefit.
- **Conversion requirements (original).** To convert an existing Delaware corporation into a PBC, a ninety-percent vote of stockholders was required, and dissenting stockholders were entitled to appraisal rights.

The 2013 DGCL amendment also included a threshold provision: an existing Delaware corporation could become a PBC only with a ninety percent vote of stockholders, and dissenting stockholders were granted appraisal rights. This was a high bar, and it reflected the Delaware drafters' judgment that conversion to PBC status was a fundamental change to the corporate contract that warranted strong shareholder protections.

In 2015, Delaware amended the PBC statute to reduce the stockholder vote requirement for conversion from ninety percent to two-thirds, and to eliminate the associated appraisal rights for some public companies. This was a significant softening of the conversion barrier, and it reflected the view that the ninety-percent threshold was impractical in many situations.

In 2020, Delaware further amended the PBC statute to eliminate appraisal rights triggered simply by conversion and to require only a simple majority vote for stockholder approval of conversion. The 2020 amendments essentially brought the PBC conversion procedure into line with the ordinary procedure for amending the certificate of incorporation.

The Delaware PBC statute has attracted significant interest from corporations that wanted to adopt a benefit-corporation-style form while retaining the familiar advantages of Delaware incorporation (access to the Delaware Court of Chancery, the DGCL, and the rich body of Delaware corporate case law). A number of high-profile companies — Kickstarter, Patagonia's American subsidiary, and others — have adopted the Delaware PBC form. The 2020 amendments removed a significant practical barrier to PBC conversion, and adoption has accelerated since then.

### 10.5 Where Washington Sits in the Comparative Landscape

The comparative picture, then, includes several different approaches:

- **The B Lab / Maryland / Vermont tradition.** Prescriptive, with mandatory stakeholder consideration, third-party standards, annual benefit reports, and benefit enforcement proceedings. Adopted by most states that have enacted benefit corporation legislation.
- **The California Flexible Purpose / Washington SPC tradition.** Permissive, with optional stakeholder consideration, no third-party standards, annual reports in a flexible format, and reliance on ordinary corporate law remedies.
- **The Delaware PBC tradition.** Intermediate, with mandatory balancing of stockholder, affected-party, and public benefit interests, specific purpose identification, biennial reports, and conversion procedures that have been progressively softened.

Washington's approach is closest to California's 2011 Flexible Purpose Corporation and to California's 2014 Social Purpose Corporation (which is the same form, renamed). It is meaningfully different from Maryland, Vermont, and the other B Lab-tradition states; from these, Washington's SPC differs in its permissive rather than mandatory director duties, its lighter reporting requirements, and its avoidance of third-party standards and benefit enforcement proceedings. It is also different from Delaware's PBC, which imposes a mandatory balancing requirement and a more structured purpose articulation.

The Washington approach has been criticized in the scholarly literature as being insufficiently demanding — as providing a "halo" without meaningful accountability. That critique, published most prominently in the *Seattle Journal of Environmental Law*, has force and deserves serious engagement. But it also has to be evaluated against the alternative: a more prescriptive approach that might in practice deter adoption of the form entirely, producing fewer mission-driven corporations rather than more. Washington made a policy judgment that the lighter-touch approach was preferable, and that judgment deserves to be respected even by those who would have made a different choice. The merits of the two approaches will ultimately be tested by experience, and the present paper does not purport to offer a definitive assessment.

## 11. MBCA vs DGCL: Where Washington Sits on the Spectrum

The Washington Business Corporation Act is an MBCA-family statute. The Delaware General Corporation Law is the principal alternative tradition in American corporate law. This section compares the two traditions in some detail and identifies where Washington's Act specifically diverges from the Delaware approach.

### 11.1 The Two Traditions at a Glance

At the highest level of abstraction, the MBCA tradition and the DGCL tradition differ in the following ways:

- **Drafting approach.** The MBCA is a detailed, codified statute that aims to answer most common corporate law questions on the face of the text. The DGCL is a shorter, more enabling statute that answers fewer questions on the face of the text and that leaves more to be developed through case law by the Delaware Court of Chancery and the Delaware Supreme Court.
- **Role of case law.** MBCA jurisdictions have meaningful bodies of corporate law case law, but the case law tends to apply statutory provisions rather than develop common-law principles. Delaware case law is the engine of Delaware corporate law; the DGCL is important but is often treated as a framework within which Delaware judges develop detailed principles.
- **Duty of care standards.** The MBCA codifies the duty of care and tends to use an "ordinary care" formulation. Delaware case law uses a "gross negligence" formulation for duty of care claims, a formulation that is generally more protective of directors. The difference is not enormous in practice — Delaware's exculpation provisions in section 102(b)(7) protect against most ordinary negligence claims anyway — but it reflects a real difference in approach.
- **Exculpation provisions.** Both traditions permit corporations to limit or eliminate director personal liability for breaches of the duty of care, but not for breaches of the duty of loyalty, intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, or transactions producing improper personal benefit. The specific wording and scope of the exculpation provisions differ in detail.
- **Shareholder rights.** The MBCA generally provides broader shareholder protections (appraisal rights, inspection rights, derivative action procedures) than the DGCL. Delaware's approach tends to give shareholders fewer mandatory rights but stronger judicial protections through the fiduciary duty framework.
- **Appraisal rights.** The MBCA permits appraisal when shareholders receive anything other than cash or publicly traded stocks. The DGCL's appraisal regime includes a "market-out" exception that significantly limits the scope of appraisal in transactions involving publicly traded stock. The MBCA's regime provides considerably more protection to shareholders in this respect.
- **Corporate opportunity doctrine.** Both traditions apply the corporate opportunity doctrine, but Delaware allows explicit waiver of the doctrine by certificate of incorporation provision (DGCL section 122(17)), and the MBCA has followed suit with similar provisions. Washington's 2020 business opportunity disclaimer amendments discussed above are part of this convergence.
- **Director voting.** DGCL section 216 had long permitted the bylaws (and thus, unilaterally, the stockholders) to vary the default plurality-vote standard for director elections. The MBCA's former section 7.28(a) was less permissive in this respect, though later amendments have brought the MBCA into greater alignment with the Delaware approach.
- **Defective corporate acts.** Both traditions now include statutes for the ratification and validation of defective corporate acts. The MBCA's provisions generally hew more closely to the Delaware provisions than other models, and use similar terminology: "failure of authorization," "putative shares," and "validation effective time."
- **Innovation cadence.** Delaware innovates in response to specific circumstances, often in response to particular case law developments or commercial needs. The MBCA tends to innovate more deliberately, through multi-year drafting processes led by the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws.

### 11.2 Washington's Specific Position

Washington is a relatively faithful adopter of the MBCA tradition. Its director duty framework uses the MBCA's ordinary-care formulation (in line with the RMBCA and in contrast to Delaware's gross-negligence formulation). Its exculpation provisions are modeled on the RMBCA. Its indemnification regime tracks the RMBCA. Its appraisal rights follow the MBCA model. Its treatment of corporate opportunity has been brought into line with national best practice through the 2020 business opportunity disclaimer amendments.

In some respects, Washington has been notably willing to track Delaware innovations rather than wait for the MBCA to codify them. The 2014–2023 forum selection provision (RCW 23B.02.080) tracks Delaware's 2015 amendment to the DGCL rather closely. The 2020 business opportunity disclaimer amendments are similar in structure to Delaware's 2000 amendment (DGCL section 122(17)). The 2024 amendment to RCW 23B.10.030 aligns Washington's default for article amendments with Delaware's simple majority standard. In each of these cases, Washington has been willing to import useful innovations from Delaware without abandoning its MBCA-family orientation.

In other respects, Washington has maintained its MBCA character. The Washington Social Purpose Corporation is a Washington (and California-influenced) innovation that Delaware's PBC does not quite match. The Washington indemnification regime, while similar to Delaware's, has some Washington-specific features. The Washington dissolution and administrative dissolution procedures have a Washington character shaped by the Secretary of State's operational needs.

The picture that emerges is of a jurisdiction that is genuinely an MBCA jurisdiction, that takes the MBCA tradition seriously, but that is also pragmatic and willing to learn from Delaware when Delaware has something to teach. This is, in the author's view, the right posture. A state corporate law statute should be grounded in a coherent tradition, but it should not be imprisoned by that tradition. Washington's statute has struck this balance reasonably well over the past three decades.

### 11.3 The Role of Charter Competition

A word about charter competition. The phenomenon of charter competition — by which corporations, particularly large public corporations, choose to incorporate in a state other than their principal place of operations, typically Delaware — is the single most important dynamic shaping American corporate law. Delaware has won the charter competition among public corporations by a wide margin: a majority of publicly traded American corporations are incorporated in Delaware, and the proportion rises for the largest corporations.

Washington has not attempted to compete directly with Delaware for the charters of large public corporations. This is a reasonable choice. Direct competition with Delaware is difficult and expensive — it requires not only a good statute but also a specialized court, a deep body of case law, and a sophisticated corporate bar — and the benefits of winning such competition are relatively small for most states. Washington has instead focused on providing a good corporate law environment for corporations that have meaningful Washington operations: closely held businesses, Washington-based startups, Washington-based mid-sized corporations, and Washington-based public corporations that prefer to incorporate at home.

For this audience, Washington's statute is well-suited. It provides a clear, modern, MBCA-based framework. It offers the Social Purpose Corporation for mission-driven businesses. It has forum selection provisions, business opportunity disclaimers, and other features that practitioners want. It has a competent Secretary of State's office that processes filings efficiently. It has a reasonable body of case law applying the statute. And it benefits from the ongoing stewardship of the WSBA Business Law Section, which keeps the statute current. For a Washington business that has no compelling reason to incorporate elsewhere, Title 23B is a thoroughly adequate choice, and for many Washington businesses, it is the right choice.

## 12. Philosophical Framing: Corporate Law as a Small, Elegant Substrate

I close with a reflection on the philosophical character of Washington's corporate law statute, and of MBCA-family statutes more generally. The reflection is, by its nature, more speculative and more personal than the historical analysis that precedes it. Readers uninterested in philosophy may skip this section without losing anything essential.

### 12.1 The Substrate Metaphor

My central claim is that Washington's corporate law, like many MBCA-family statutes, is best understood not as a body of substantive prescriptions but as a *substrate* — a small, coherent layer of rules on which other actors (drafters, boards, shareholders, and courts) build the actual corporate entities that operate in the world. The substrate provides the building blocks: it defines what a corporation is, how one is formed, how its governance works in the absence of contrary provision, what rights and duties attach to directors and shareholders, how disputes are to be resolved, and how the corporation comes to an end. But the substrate does not prescribe what any particular corporation will look like. The actual shape of any particular corporation is determined by the articles of incorporation, the bylaws, the shareholder agreements, the board resolutions, the industry practices, and the commercial context in which the corporation operates.

The substrate is *small* in the sense that its mandatory rules are few. Most of the Act is composed of default rules, which apply unless varied by careful drafting. A careful drafter can customize a great deal of what the Act provides. The mandatory rules are typically those that protect third parties (creditors, minority shareholders, the public) against conduct that all reasonable observers would regard as improper: you cannot eliminate the duty of loyalty, you cannot waive liability for intentional misconduct, you cannot authorize the corporation to operate without directors or without shareholders, you cannot use the corporate form as a device for fraud.

The substrate is *elegant* in the sense that it is coherent and well-organized. The nineteen-chapter architecture of Title 23B is not the only possible architecture, but it is a good one: it groups related provisions together, it uses consistent numbering, it separates the mechanics of formation from the mechanics of governance from the mechanics of change and dissolution. Reading the statute is not a pleasure in the way that reading great literature is a pleasure, but it is tolerable, which is more than can be said for many state statutes. The definitions are reasonably precise, the cross-references are reasonably clean, and the drafting conventions are reasonably consistent.

The substrate is *leveraged* in the sense that a small set of well-chosen rules enables a wide range of outcomes. A corporation formed under Title 23B can be a one-person closely held entity or a multi-thousand-shareholder public company. It can have a single class of stock or a complex capital structure with multiple classes and series. It can have a single director or a large board. It can operate with a simple set of bylaws or with an elaborate governance framework. It can commit to pursuing a social purpose alongside its financial purpose or it can be a pure profit-seeking enterprise. It can be managed by its shareholders directly under a shareholder agreement or by a professional board. All of these outcomes are available within the same statutory framework, because the framework is composed of building blocks that can be assembled in many different ways.

### 12.2 The Unix Philosophy Analogy

The analogy that comes most naturally to mind is the Unix philosophy of software design. Unix, as a design philosophy rather than as a specific operating system, is built around a few core ideas: small programs that do one thing well, composition of programs through simple interfaces (pipes, streams of bytes, plain text), the separation of mechanism from policy, and trust in the user to compose the primitives into useful tools. The Unix philosophy has proven extraordinarily durable — it underlies much of the software that runs the modern world — and its success is attributable to its embrace of smallness, composability, and trust in the user.

Washington's corporate law, at its best, exhibits similar virtues. It provides small, well-defined primitives: the corporation, the director, the officer, the shareholder, the class of stock, the article of incorporation, the bylaw, the shareholder agreement. It provides simple interfaces for composition: the default-rule-with-opt-out structure, the articles-override-bylaws hierarchy, the shareholder-agreement override, the indemnification non-exclusivity. It separates mechanism from policy: the statute tells you how to form a corporation, amend its articles, elect directors, hold meetings, and so on, but it does not tell you what the corporation should be for or what business judgments the board should make. And it trusts the user — the drafter, the board, the shareholder, the court — to compose the primitives into a functional corporate form.

The alternative — a prescriptive, detailed, highly regulated corporate law — would be like a monolithic operating system that tries to provide every possible feature in a single integrated framework. Such systems are possible, and in some applications they are the right choice, but they tend to be brittle, difficult to modify, and expensive to maintain. Corporate law, like operating systems, benefits from modularity.

### 12.3 The Amiga Analogy

A more specific analogy, for readers who appreciate the history of computing: the Commodore Amiga computer (1985) is often cited as an example of elegant architecture in hardware design. The Amiga was built around a small set of custom chips — Agnus (memory management and direct memory access), Denise (video), Paula (audio), and later revisions thereof — that provided dedicated hardware support for specific functions. The custom chips were not huge or complex; they were small, purpose-built, and composed together with the CPU through a clean memory-mapped interface. The result was a computer that could do things (multitasking graphics, sound, animation) that more traditional designs of the same era struggled with.

The Amiga's design philosophy was explicit: provide small, well-designed primitives that compose cleanly, and let the programmers figure out how to use them. The design philosophy produced a computer that was ahead of its time and that was beloved by its users even decades after its commercial demise. The design philosophy was, in a sense, a hardware version of the Unix philosophy: trust the user, provide composable primitives, and avoid heavy prescription.

Washington's corporate law, when it is at its best, shares this design philosophy. It provides composable primitives. It trusts the user. It avoids heavy prescription. The analogy is not perfect — corporate law is not hardware, and the concerns are different — but the underlying architectural sensibility is similar, and the results are similar. Washington corporate law is, in this sense, elegant.

### 12.4 The Risk of Over-Prescription

The opposite architectural tendency — heavy prescription — is always a temptation, especially in response to specific perceived abuses. Someone misbehaves, there is a call for legislation, the legislature responds by adding detailed rules to address the specific misbehavior. Repeated over decades, this pattern produces a statute that has become bloated, inconsistent, and difficult to apply. Each individual amendment may have been justified at the time of its enactment, but the cumulative effect is a mess.

Washington's statute has, so far, mostly avoided this fate. The amendments over the past three decades have generally been incremental refinements rather than heavy overlays. The Social Purpose Corporation was added as a new chapter rather than as detailed amendments to existing chapters. The forum selection provision was added as a single opt-in section. The business opportunity disclaimer was added as a permitted provision of the articles of incorporation. The 2024 vote threshold amendment was a change to a single default. None of these changes imposed heavy new prescription; all of them worked within the existing architectural vocabulary of the statute.

This is, I think, the right approach. A state corporate law statute is a piece of legal infrastructure that serves a wide range of users. It should be stable, predictable, and modular. It should be amended when there is a clear reason to amend it, and the amendments should be drafted in ways that preserve the statute's architectural integrity. Washington has mostly done this well.

### 12.5 The Virtue of Small Things

There is a broader point here about the virtue of small things. The ancient Greek concept of *métron* — the right measure, the fitting proportion — is a useful reference. A thing can be good by virtue of being rightly sized: not too large, not too small, not too elaborate, not too simple. Washington's corporate law is rightly sized in this sense. It has enough substance to be useful (it answers the questions that practitioners need answered) and not so much substance that it becomes cumbersome (it does not try to answer every possible question in advance). It has enough mandatory structure to protect the interests that corporate law exists to protect (third parties, minorities, the public) and not so much mandatory structure that it crowds out the parties' ability to customize. It has enough default rules to make drafting tractable (a corporation can be formed with a minimal set of articles and bylaws) and enough flexibility to permit customization (a more elaborate corporation can have elaborate governance provisions).

This is, I think, an admirable quality in legal infrastructure. It is a quality that is more easily observed than designed: it is the product of many careful judgments, made over decades, by people who understood that the statute would be used by people they would never meet, in situations they could not anticipate, and that the statute's primary job was to stay out of the way while still providing the support that those users would need.

The Washington Business Corporation Act, in its 1989 form and in its various subsequent amendments, is such a piece of infrastructure. It is not spectacular. It is not a monument. It is not the subject of the kind of extensive case law analysis that the DGCL generates. But it is a well-designed tool, and it has served Washington's business community well for more than three decades. That is a real achievement, and it deserves to be recognized as such.

### 12.6 Closing

I began this paper with a thesis: that Washington's corporate law is best understood as a *leveraged substrate* — a compact layer of enabling rules that trusts its users to compose the rest. I have tried to support that thesis through a historical analysis of how the statute came to be, how it has evolved, and where it sits in the comparative landscape of American corporate law. The analysis has ranged from the Washington Territory's early incorporation statutes to the 2024 amendments, from the Model Business Corporation Act's 1950 origins to the Delaware Public Benefit Corporation's 2013 birth, from the Para-Medical Leasing case of 1987 to the Rodriguez v. Loudeye case of 2008.

The unifying theme throughout has been architectural. Washington's statute has been shaped by a particular set of preferences: for smallness over comprehensiveness, for defaults over mandates, for opt-outs over prohibitions, for trust in the parties over prescription by the state. These preferences are not the only possible preferences — Delaware has its own, and the B Lab Benefit Corporation tradition has yet another — but they are coherent preferences, and they have produced a coherent statute.

Whether that statute is right for any particular corporate purpose is a question that only a practitioner can answer, and only for a specific client in a specific situation. But the statute is good in the general sense that it is well-designed infrastructure, and well-designed infrastructure is, in my view, one of the most important things that a state government can provide. Washington has provided it. The people who wrote it, the people who maintain it, and the people who apply it deserve the recognition of those of us who use the roads they have built.

## 14. Deeper Analyses and Extended Discussion

This section collects a set of deeper analyses that expand on themes introduced earlier in the paper. Readers who want a straightforward historical narrative can stop at the end of section 13 without missing anything essential; this section is offered for readers who want more depth on particular subjects, and it is organized as a set of relatively self-contained essays that can be read in any order.

### 14.1 The Texture of Director Fiduciary Duties in Washington

One of the areas where Washington corporate law has been most productively developed through case law is the texture of director fiduciary duties. The statutory framework is fairly compact: RCW 23B.08.300 sets forth the general standard of conduct for directors (good faith, with the care that a person in a like position would reasonably believe appropriate, and in a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation), and RCW 23B.08.310 addresses the procedures by which directors may rely on reports and other information prepared by others. The Washington exculpation provision, RCW 23B.08.320, permits articles of incorporation to limit or eliminate director personal liability for breaches of duty to the corporation and its shareholders, subject to specified exceptions. These statutory provisions are the skeleton of Washington's director duty framework. The flesh has been supplied by the case law.

At the center of that case law is the concept of the business judgment rule. The business judgment rule is not a statutory rule; it is a judicial presumption, derived from common law, that in making a business decision the directors of a corporation acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation. Washington courts have embraced the business judgment rule and have applied it in a manner broadly consistent with the national consensus. *Para-Medical Leasing v. Hangen* and *In re Spokane Concrete Products* are the two leading Washington decisions on the point, but they are not the only ones. Washington's business judgment rule jurisprudence also draws on treatise discussions of the rule, on Delaware case law (which is frequently cited by Washington courts as persuasive authority on general corporate law matters), and on commentary in the WBCA Sourcebook.

What does the business judgment rule actually protect against? It protects directors from liability for decisions that, with hindsight, turn out to have been mistakes. It does not protect directors from liability for decisions that were made in bad faith, in disregard of the directors' responsibilities, in the absence of reasonable investigation, or for the personal benefit of the directors rather than for the benefit of the corporation. The rule is a presumption, not a shield: it can be rebutted by evidence of fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, self-dealing, or failure to act in good faith. When the presumption is rebutted, the court will examine the directors' conduct more closely and will apply the substantive standards of the duty of care and the duty of loyalty.

The duty of loyalty, in Washington as elsewhere, is the more rigorously policed of the two duties. *Interlake Porsche v. Bucholz* illustrates the point. In that case, the majority shareholder had used substantial corporate funds for personal expenses without board authorization or shareholder ratification. The court did not apply the business judgment rule to protect him; instead, it examined the challenged expenditures under the duty of loyalty, required him to justify each expenditure as having been made for a legitimate corporate purpose, and — finding the justifications inadequate — required disgorgement. This is the pattern that Washington courts follow in duty-of-loyalty cases: the presumption is shifted once the plaintiff shows self-dealing or other conduct raising loyalty concerns, and the defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged conduct was fair to the corporation.

The Washington exculpation provision, RCW 23B.08.320, provides important protection against duty-of-care claims but does not reach duty-of-loyalty claims or claims based on intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, or transactions producing improper personal benefit. *Grassmueck v. Barnett* — the federal district court decision discussed in section 5.2 — articulates this framework with particular clarity, identifying the four categories of conduct that pierce the exculpation: (1) intentional misconduct, (2) knowing violations of law, (3) conduct violating the director's statutory duties under RCW 23B.08.310, and (4) transactions from which the director will personally receive a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director is not legally entitled. These four categories are the statutory carve-outs from exculpation, and they are broadly similar to the carve-outs in DGCL § 102(b)(7) and in the parallel provisions of most other MBCA jurisdictions.

A recurring question in Washington practice is the extent to which Delaware case law on the business judgment rule should be imported into Washington corporate law. Washington courts have been willing to cite Delaware decisions as persuasive authority on general corporate law issues, but they have also been careful to recognize that Washington is an MBCA jurisdiction and that its statute has a different structure and different provisions than the DGCL. The result is a Washington jurisprudence that is informed by Delaware but not enslaved to it. On some questions (the general structure of the business judgment rule, the basic duty framework), Washington and Delaware are in substantial alignment. On other questions (the specific wording of the exculpation provision, the specific scope of director liability, the specific procedures for shareholder remedies), Washington follows the WBCA text, and Delaware case law is at best a secondary reference.

The texture of director duties in Washington is, in sum, a blend of statutory text, MBCA commentary, Washington case law, and Delaware influence. A practitioner advising a Washington board needs to understand all four sources and how they interact. The WBCA Sourcebook is invaluable for this purpose, because it collects the relevant statutory text, legislative history, Committee commentary, and case law in a single reference. Outside the Sourcebook, the relevant materials are scattered across the Revised Code of Washington, the Session Laws, the Washington Reports, the Washington Appellate Reports, the federal district court reporter, law review articles, and ABA publications. Assembling this material is not a trivial task, but it is the kind of task that a competent Washington corporate lawyer is expected to be able to perform.

### 14.2 Indemnification and D&O Insurance: The Protective Regime

A second area where Washington corporate law has developed meaningful sophistication is the indemnification of directors and officers and the related field of directors' and officers' (D&O) liability insurance. This is a topic of intense practical importance. Directors and officers of Washington corporations face a non-trivial risk of personal liability for claims asserted against them in their corporate capacities — claims by shareholders, creditors, regulators, customers, employees, and third parties. The protective regime that has developed around this risk consists of three main components: the statutory indemnification provisions of chapter 23B.08, the contractual indemnification provisions that corporations typically include in their bylaws or in separate indemnification agreements, and D&O liability insurance purchased by the corporation to cover both its own indemnification obligations and the directors' and officers' personal liability to the extent that the corporation's indemnification is insufficient.

The statutory framework begins with RCW 23B.08.510, which addresses permissive indemnification: a corporation may indemnify a director who has acted in good faith and with the reasonable belief that the conduct was in the corporation's best interests (or, in the case of conduct in an official capacity, not opposed to the corporation's best interests). The permissive indemnification is subject to a determination, by disinterested directors, independent legal counsel, or shareholders, that the statutory standard has been met. RCW 23B.08.520 provides for mandatory indemnification of a director who has been wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise, in the defense of a proceeding. Mandatory indemnification is not discretionary; it is a statutory right that the director can enforce against the corporation. RCW 23B.08.530 addresses the advancement of expenses during the pendency of a proceeding, and specifies the procedures and undertakings required for advancement. RCW 23B.08.540 provides for court-ordered indemnification where a court determines that indemnification is fair and reasonable notwithstanding the absence of a favorable disinterested determination.

The interesting feature of Washington's indemnification regime is RCW 23B.08.560, which addresses the non-exclusivity of the statutory indemnification provisions. The non-exclusivity rule provides that the statutory indemnification is not exclusive of any other rights that a director may have under the articles of incorporation, the bylaws, an agreement with the corporation, a vote of shareholders or disinterested directors, or otherwise. In other words, a Washington corporation may provide broader indemnification than the statute requires, through its bylaws, through separate indemnification agreements, or through other means. The non-exclusivity rule is subject to certain public policy limits: a corporation may not indemnify a director against liability for conduct that is fundamentally inconsistent with the corporation's interests (e.g., for fraud on the corporation itself).

In practice, most Washington corporations of any significant size adopt indemnification provisions in their bylaws that go beyond the statutory minimum. Typical provisions include mandatory indemnification of directors and officers to the fullest extent permitted by law, mandatory advancement of expenses subject to an undertaking to repay if it is ultimately determined that the director or officer was not entitled to indemnification, a provision making the indemnification rights enforceable against the corporation (and sometimes naming specified third parties — such as successor entities in mergers — as additional obligors), and a provision establishing that the indemnification rights are vested and cannot be reduced by later amendment without the consent of the affected director or officer. This last provision interacts with RCW 23B.08.603, which addresses the situation where an indemnification provision is amended or repealed after the conduct in question has occurred; the statutory rule and the typical bylaw provision work together to give directors a reliable expectation that their indemnification rights cannot be retroactively diminished.

D&O insurance is the third leg of the protective regime. Most Washington corporations above a certain size purchase D&O insurance from commercial insurers, typically through a specialized brokerage. The insurance policies are complex and have developed their own specialized terminology and conventions. A typical D&O policy has three coverage parts: Side A covers direct payments to directors and officers when the corporation is unable or unwilling to indemnify them (e.g., because the corporation is insolvent, or because indemnification is prohibited by statute or by the circumstances); Side B reimburses the corporation for indemnification payments it has made to directors and officers; and Side C covers the corporation itself for securities claims (and, in some policies, for other specified categories of claims). D&O policies typically include deductibles, per-claim limits, aggregate limits, and a variety of exclusions (e.g., for intentional misconduct, for prior known claims, and for specified categories of conduct).

The interaction between statutory indemnification, contractual indemnification, and D&O insurance is one of the more complex areas of Washington corporate practice. A director facing a claim typically has to navigate all three sources of protection, and the coordination among them can raise difficult questions: Does the statutory indemnification apply? Does the bylaw indemnification provide broader protection? Does the D&O insurance cover the claim, and if so, which coverage part? Who pays the defense costs during the pendency of the proceeding? What happens if the insurance carrier denies coverage? These questions arise in virtually every significant corporate litigation, and the answers can materially affect the director's financial exposure and the corporation's financial exposure.

Washington's statutory framework provides a reasonable starting point for these questions, but it does not answer them all. Much of the detailed work has to be done by the drafters of the bylaws and the indemnification agreements, and by the brokers and underwriters who structure the D&O insurance policies. The result is a protective regime that is flexible and robust, but also complex and dependent on careful implementation.

### 14.3 The Close Corporation Problem

Chapter 23B.19 of Title 23B provides a special regime for close corporations. The close corporation is a distinctive kind of corporate entity: a corporation with a small number of shareholders, often family-owned or owned by a small group of business partners, whose shares are not publicly traded and whose governance is typically more informal than that of a larger corporation. Close corporations present special problems for corporate law because the standard rules — annual shareholder meetings, elected boards of directors, formal corporate governance — are often a poor fit for entities where the shareholders know each other personally, where the same people are typically directors and officers, and where the "corporation" is in substance a partnership wearing corporate clothing.

Washington's chapter 23B.19 provides a narrow special regime for corporations that elect close-corporation status. The regime permits, among other things, the elimination of the board of directors (with corporate powers exercised directly by the shareholders), the use of shareholder agreements to govern internal matters without the formalities required of ordinary corporations, and various other accommodations to the informal governance style of small entities. In practice, the chapter 23B.19 regime has been used relatively rarely. Most closely held Washington corporations operate as ordinary corporations under the general provisions of Title 23B, and handle the close-corporation problem through a combination of careful drafting (articles of incorporation and bylaws tailored to the corporation's needs) and shareholder agreements under RCW 23B.07.320.

The shareholder agreement is, in fact, the principal tool that Washington practitioners use to address the close-corporation problem. RCW 23B.07.320 authorizes shareholder agreements that depart from a number of the Act's default governance rules, subject to specified limits. Typical shareholder agreement provisions include: voting arrangements (pooled voting, voting trusts, or specified voting agreements on designated matters), buy-sell provisions (first-refusal rights, drag-along rights, tag-along rights, mandatory buy-out on specified triggers), dispute resolution mechanisms (mediation, arbitration, or specified court venues), and provisions addressing the management of the corporation (who serves as director, who serves as officer, how decisions are made). A well-drafted shareholder agreement can transform an ordinary Washington corporation into an entity with governance that closely resembles a partnership, while preserving the liability-shield and tax treatment advantages of corporate form.

The relationship between the shareholder agreement and the articles of incorporation / bylaws is worth noting. RCW 23B.07.320 provides that shareholder agreements are effective only if adopted by all persons who are shareholders at the time of the agreement, and that subsequent shareholders are bound by the agreement only if they had actual notice of it or if it is noted on the share certificate (or an equivalent). The statute also provides that shareholder agreements are of limited duration unless renewed, with specified maximum durations depending on the specific subject matter. These statutory limits are designed to protect future shareholders from being bound by agreements they did not know about and to ensure that shareholder agreements do not become perpetual encrustations on the corporate form.

The close-corporation problem has another dimension: the problem of minority oppression. In a close corporation, a majority shareholder (or a controlling group of shareholders) can effectively freeze out a minority shareholder by denying the minority employment, refusing to declare dividends, denying access to corporate records, or taking other actions that make the minority's investment effectively worthless. Washington courts have developed a body of case law addressing minority oppression claims, typically under general equitable principles rather than under specific statutory provisions. The remedy for minority oppression claims is often ordered dissolution of the corporation (under RCW 23B.14.300 et seq.) or ordered buy-out of the minority at a judicially determined fair value. Washington courts have applied these remedies pragmatically, with attention to the specific facts of each case and with a general reluctance to order dissolution where a less drastic remedy would address the minority's concerns.

The close-corporation problem is unlikely to be fully solved by any statutory framework. It is a problem of human relationships as much as a problem of law, and the law can do only so much. Washington's approach — a narrow special regime in chapter 23B.19, broad enabling provisions for shareholder agreements in RCW 23B.07.320, and a body of case law on minority oppression — is a reasonable response to a hard problem. It gives practitioners the tools they need to address the problem at the drafting stage, and it gives courts the flexibility they need to address the problem when drafting has failed.

### 14.4 Appraisal Rights and Dissenters' Rights

Chapter 23B.13 of Title 23B provides for appraisal rights (also called dissenters' rights) for shareholders who object to certain fundamental corporate transactions. The appraisal right is the right of a dissenting shareholder, in specified transactions, to demand that the corporation purchase the dissenter's shares at their fair value, as determined by the court if the parties cannot agree. The appraisal right is one of the few mechanisms in corporate law that gives a minority shareholder a clean exit from the corporation, and it is an important protection against majority decisions that are disadvantageous to the minority.

The transactions that trigger appraisal rights under Washington law include mergers, share exchanges, sales of substantially all assets, certain amendments to the articles of incorporation, and a few other specified categories. The precise list of appraisal-triggering transactions, and the procedures for asserting and perfecting appraisal rights, are set forth in chapter 23B.13 in considerable detail. The procedures are exacting, and a shareholder who fails to comply with them may lose the right to appraisal. Washington case law on appraisal rights addresses, among other things, the timing of dissenter notices, the content of dissenter notices, the obligations of the corporation in responding to dissenter notices, and the standards for determining fair value.

The concept of "fair value" in appraisal proceedings has been the subject of considerable case law and commentary, both in Washington and in other MBCA jurisdictions, and in Delaware. The traditional approach in Delaware, captured in the Delaware block method, gave weight to market value, asset value, and earnings value, combining them through a judicial discretion. More recent Delaware cases have moved toward a discounted-cash-flow (DCF) analysis, sometimes supplemented by other valuation methods. The MBCA's definition of fair value (in RMBCA § 13.01 and its Washington analogue) focuses on the value of the corporation as a going concern, excluding any appreciation or depreciation in anticipation of the transaction unless exclusion would be inequitable.

Washington courts have applied the MBCA fair value standard in a manner broadly consistent with Delaware practice, with some local variations. The court typically hears expert testimony from valuation professionals retained by the parties, assesses the credibility and methodology of the experts, and arrives at a fair value determination. The process is resource-intensive and can be expensive; appraisal proceedings are one of the more specialized and demanding categories of commercial litigation in Washington.

An interesting comparative feature of Washington's appraisal regime is that it is broader than Delaware's in one respect: Washington does not have the same "market-out" exception that Delaware's DGCL § 262 provides. Under the Delaware rule, appraisal rights are generally not available for shareholders of publicly traded corporations who receive publicly traded stock in exchange for their shares — the idea being that the public market provides an adequate valuation mechanism. The MBCA, and Washington's Title 23B, do not have this exception in the same form, which means that Washington shareholders have somewhat broader appraisal protection than Delaware shareholders in analogous transactions. This is one of the places where the MBCA tradition provides more extensive shareholder protection than the DGCL tradition.

The appraisal right is, in theory, a significant protection for minority shareholders. In practice, its use is relatively uncommon, because the procedural requirements are demanding and because the cost of mounting an appraisal action often exceeds the likely incremental recovery over the transaction consideration. When appraisal is used, it is typically in high-stakes transactions where the incremental value is substantial enough to justify the litigation cost, and where the dissenter has reason to believe that the transaction consideration is materially below fair value. These circumstances arise, but they are not the norm.

### 14.5 Mergers, Share Exchanges, and Domestications: The Transaction Chapters

Chapters 23B.11, 23B.11A, 23B.12, and 23B.09 of Title 23B collectively address the transactions by which Washington corporations undergo fundamental changes: mergers with other entities, share exchanges, sales of substantially all assets, and conversions to or from other entity forms. These chapters are among the most detailed and most frequently used provisions of the WBCA, because transactions of these kinds are a central feature of corporate practice.

The original 1989 WBCA included chapter 23B.11 (Mergers and Share Exchanges) as its principal transactional chapter. Chapter 23B.11 tracked the 1984 RMBCA merger provisions fairly closely: it authorized mergers between Washington corporations, mergers between Washington corporations and foreign corporations, and share exchanges (a kind of statutory tender offer in which one corporation acquires all the shares of another corporation by force of law rather than by voluntary tender). The merger procedure required board approval, shareholder approval, and filing of articles of merger with the Secretary of State, all on procedures specified in the chapter. Appraisal rights were available to dissenting shareholders, as discussed in section 14.4.

Over time, the 1989 merger provisions were supplemented and in some respects superseded by a modernized framework in chapter 23B.11A. The modernization was driven by several developments: the RMBCA's own revisions to its merger provisions, the experience of Washington practitioners in applying the 1989 provisions to transactions the drafters had not fully anticipated, and the rise of new transaction forms (particularly cross-entity transactions involving limited liability companies, limited partnerships, and other non-corporate entities) that had not been prominent in 1989. Chapter 23B.11A provides a more flexible framework that accommodates a wider range of transaction structures while preserving the protective features (appraisal rights, board and shareholder approval, disclosure) of the original chapter 23B.11.

Chapter 23B.12 addresses the sale, lease, exchange, or other disposition of substantially all of the corporation's assets. The sale-of-assets provision is important because it addresses a transaction type that, in the eyes of some shareholders, is functionally equivalent to a merger (the corporation's business is transferred to another entity) but that does not technically constitute a merger. Washington's treatment of sales of assets requires shareholder approval when the sale is "other than in the usual and regular course of business," and provides appraisal rights to dissenting shareholders in certain circumstances. The distinction between sales of assets in the usual and regular course of business (which require only board approval) and sales of substantially all assets (which require shareholder approval) has generated its own body of case law and commentary.

Chapter 23B.09, which addresses domestications and conversions, is a later addition to Title 23B that reflects the growing importance of cross-entity transactions in modern practice. A domestication is a transaction in which a corporation organized under the laws of one jurisdiction re-registers as a corporation organized under the laws of another jurisdiction, effectively changing its state of incorporation without going through the cumbersome merger procedures that would otherwise be required. A conversion is a transaction in which an entity of one form (e.g., a corporation) re-registers as an entity of another form (e.g., a limited liability company), effectively changing its entity type without going through merger procedures. Both transactions are useful tools for practitioners who need to reorganize corporate structures for tax, governance, or other reasons. Chapter 23B.09 provides statutory authorization for these transactions and sets forth procedures for effecting them.

The transaction chapters collectively illustrate the "small leveraged substrate" philosophy of Title 23B. They provide the statutory authorizations and the procedural protections, but they do not prescribe the business terms of the transactions or the details of how the transactions should be structured. A merger under Washington law can be a simple cash-for-shares transaction, a stock-for-stock exchange, a complicated multi-entity reorganization, a reverse triangular merger, or any number of other structures, depending on what the parties need. The statute accommodates all of these structures within a common procedural framework. This is the kind of flexibility that makes Title 23B a useful tool for Washington practitioners, and it is one of the features that distinguishes the MBCA tradition from more prescriptive approaches to corporate law.

### 14.6 Dissolution: The End of Corporate Life

Chapter 23B.14 of Title 23B addresses dissolution — the legal end of a corporation's existence. Dissolution comes in three principal varieties: voluntary dissolution (initiated by the corporation itself through board and shareholder action), administrative dissolution (imposed by the Secretary of State for failure to file annual reports or pay fees), and judicial dissolution (ordered by a court on specified grounds).

Voluntary dissolution is the ordinary exit route for a corporation that no longer has a reason to exist. The procedure is relatively straightforward: the board adopts a resolution recommending dissolution, the shareholders approve the dissolution, articles of dissolution are filed with the Secretary of State, and the corporation enters a period of "winding up" during which it collects its assets, pays its liabilities, distributes any remaining assets to shareholders, and concludes its business. The winding-up period is important because it provides an orderly mechanism for the corporation to satisfy its obligations to creditors and to distribute its remaining value to shareholders. During the winding-up period, the corporation continues to exist as a legal entity for the limited purposes of winding up; it cannot, however, carry on its business except as necessary to wind up.

Administrative dissolution is a different mechanism, designed to address the problem of corporations that have ceased to operate but have not formally dissolved. Washington, like most states, requires corporations to file annual reports with the Secretary of State and to pay annual license fees. When a corporation fails to file its annual report or pay its fees for a specified period, the Secretary of State may initiate administrative dissolution proceedings, which result in the corporation losing its legal existence unless it promptly cures its delinquency. Administrative dissolution is an important housekeeping tool for the state; without it, the corporate registry would be cluttered with the records of thousands of corporations that have long since ceased to operate but that remain formally extant. The procedure for administrative dissolution is specified in chapter 23B.14, and it includes notice to the corporation and an opportunity to cure.

Judicial dissolution is the most dramatic form of dissolution. It can be initiated by the Attorney General, by a shareholder, or in some circumstances by a creditor or other party with standing. The grounds for judicial dissolution are specified in chapter 23B.14 and include, among other things, the abandonment of the corporation's business, the deadlock of the directors and shareholders, the oppression of minority shareholders, the misapplication or waste of corporate assets, and other grounds. Judicial dissolution proceedings are relatively rare, but they are important because they provide a remedy of last resort for situations in which the corporation is unable or unwilling to resolve its own problems.

Washington courts have developed a body of case law on judicial dissolution, particularly in the context of minority oppression claims in closely held corporations. The general approach is to order dissolution only when less drastic remedies (such as a court-ordered buy-out of the oppressed minority) will not adequately address the harm. This is a sensible approach: dissolution destroys the going-concern value of the corporation, which is typically much greater than the value of the corporation's tangible assets, and so dissolution imposes a real cost on all shareholders (including the petitioning minority) that should be weighed against the benefit of ending the oppression. Washington courts have generally applied this calculus thoughtfully, and the number of judicial dissolutions actually ordered is small.

After dissolution, whether voluntary, administrative, or judicial, the corporation enters a winding-up phase during which its affairs are concluded. The winding-up phase can be extended if necessary to address claims that arise after dissolution, and Washington law includes provisions addressing post-dissolution claims by creditors. RCW 23B.14.340 addresses the barring of unknown claims against a dissolved corporation through a published notice procedure, and RCW 23B.14.330 addresses known claims through a direct notice procedure. These provisions give dissolved corporations a mechanism for finality: after a specified period, and after compliance with the notice procedures, the corporation can distribute its remaining assets to shareholders without fear of unexpected claims emerging later. The mechanism is not perfect — some claims can emerge years after dissolution, and the statute of limitations rules interact with the dissolution rules in complex ways — but it provides enough finality for most purposes.

### 14.7 The Practice of Forming a Washington Corporation

A practical description of how a Washington corporation is actually formed may be useful to readers who are interested in the operational rather than the doctrinal side of corporate law. The process begins with the preparation of articles of incorporation, a document that sets forth the corporation's name, the number of authorized shares, the registered agent and registered office, the initial directors (if any are named), and any additional provisions that the incorporators wish to include. Washington law, like the RMBCA and most state statutes, requires relatively little information in the articles of incorporation as a mandatory minimum; the incorporators have considerable flexibility to include additional provisions that customize the corporation's governance.

The articles of incorporation are filed with the Washington Secretary of State, typically through the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS), the Secretary of State's online platform. The filing fee is modest, and the filing is typically processed within a few days (or, with expedited service, within a shorter period). Upon acceptance of the filing, the Secretary of State issues a certificate of incorporation, the corporation comes into existence, and it receives its Unified Business Identifier (UBI) number for cross-agency identification.

After the corporation is formed, the incorporators or the initial directors hold an organizational meeting (or take action by written consent in lieu of a meeting) to adopt bylaws, elect officers, approve the form of stock certificate (if the corporation is issuing physical certificates), authorize the opening of a bank account, and take other actions necessary to put the corporation into operating condition. The organizational meeting is an important step because it creates the initial governance framework and establishes the corporation's identity as an operating entity.

Bylaws are the internal governance document of the corporation. They typically address matters that are too detailed or too subject to change to be included in the articles of incorporation: the number of directors, the procedures for director and officer elections, the procedures for shareholder and board meetings, the quorum and voting requirements, the powers and duties of officers, the procedures for indemnification, the procedures for the issuance and transfer of shares, and so on. Bylaws can be amended more easily than articles of incorporation (typically by board action, subject to shareholder override), which makes them a convenient place to put provisions that the corporation may want to adjust over time.

Once the corporation is formed and has adopted bylaws and elected officers, it begins to operate. Ongoing obligations include: maintaining a registered agent and registered office in Washington, filing an annual report with the Secretary of State, paying annual license fees, maintaining corporate records (minute books, stock ledgers, financial records), holding annual shareholder meetings (or action in lieu), electing directors, and generally complying with the requirements of Title 23B and applicable other law. These obligations are not onerous, but they are real, and corporations that fail to meet them can face administrative dissolution or other consequences.

For a Washington entrepreneur who wants to form a corporation, the process is accessible. The CCFS makes online filing easy; the filing fees are modest; the statutory requirements are clear; and there is a substantial infrastructure of law firms, filing services, registered agent services, and online legal platforms that can assist with the mechanics. Washington's corporate formation regime is, in this sense, a user-friendly system. That user-friendliness is one of its virtues, and it is a reflection of the statutory preference for simple defaults and minimal prescription.

### 14.8 The Washington Social Purpose Corporation in Practice

Several hundred Washington corporations have been formed as Social Purpose Corporations under chapter 23B.25 RCW since the statute took effect in June 2012, and others have converted from ordinary corporate form to SPC status. The population of Washington SPCs includes consumer brands (organic food producers, sustainable clothing companies, eco-friendly personal care products), professional services firms (consulting firms with a specified social mission), technology startups (companies building products with a social or environmental purpose), and various mission-driven businesses in other sectors.

The experience of these corporations in the years since 2012 has been mixed. Some have thrived, leveraging their SPC status as part of their brand identity and using the annual report requirement as a discipline to track and communicate their social purpose activities. Others have found that the SPC status provides little practical benefit — either because customers and investors do not distinguish between SPC status and ordinary corporate status, or because the corporation's social purpose activities are effectively managed through ordinary business processes that do not require a special corporate form. A few corporations have converted back from SPC status to ordinary corporate status, citing various reasons including investor concerns, complexity, or evolving business strategy.

The academic and practitioner commentary on the Washington SPC has reflected this mixed experience. Proponents emphasize the flexibility of the form, the low barrier to entry, and the preservation of ordinary corporate law remedies. Critics, particularly in the Seattle Journal of Environmental Law article cited in the bibliography, argue that the absence of mandatory director duties to consider stakeholder interests, the absence of third-party standards for measuring benefit performance, and the absence of meaningful enforcement mechanisms leave the SPC form without teeth — a "halo" that signals good intentions without imposing the discipline required to deliver on them.

Both views have merit. The Washington SPC is what it is: a permissive, light-touch framework that trusts the parties to use the form responsibly. For corporations that want the form's signaling value without the B Lab model's compliance burden, the Washington SPC is a reasonable choice. For corporations that want stronger accountability, or for observers who believe that strong accountability is necessary for the form to be meaningful, other structures (such as the Delaware PBC or a state-level benefit corporation regime) may be more attractive. The Washington legislators and drafters who made the 2012 choice were aware of these trade-offs, and they made a judgment that the flexibility of the SPC form would produce better outcomes than the more prescriptive alternative. Whether that judgment has proven correct is an empirical question that is still being answered through the experience of the corporations that have adopted the form.

One interesting feature of the Washington SPC that deserves mention is its interaction with B Lab certification. As noted in section 6, Washington SPC status and B Lab certification are independent. A Washington SPC can apply for B Lab certification (and some do); a Washington corporation that is not an SPC can also apply for B Lab certification (and some do). The two mechanisms operate on different tracks: the SPC status is a matter of state corporate law, and B Lab certification is a matter of private certification by an independent nonprofit organization. A corporation can have either, both, or neither. This structural independence is consistent with the WSBA drafting committee's judgment that state law should not embed private certification requirements, and it has allowed the two mechanisms to coexist without conflict.

### 14.9 The Evolution of Corporate Governance Norms

A broader observation about Washington corporate law, and about American corporate law more generally, is that the statutory framework is only part of the story. Corporate governance norms — the expectations that shape how boards, officers, shareholders, and advisors actually behave — are shaped by many factors beyond the statute: case law, academic commentary, industry practices, the advice of counsel, the opinions of institutional investors, the recommendations of proxy advisors, the expectations of regulators, the requirements of securities exchanges (for public corporations), and the general climate of opinion among the corporate bar.

In Washington as in other MBCA jurisdictions, corporate governance norms have evolved significantly over the decades since the 1989 WBCA was enacted. Some of the most notable changes include:

- **Increasing emphasis on board independence.** In 1989, many Washington corporations had boards composed largely of management and close associates. Today, particularly for public corporations and for corporations seeking venture capital investment, boards are expected to include a substantial number of independent directors who can provide objective oversight of management. This norm is driven by institutional investor preferences, by proxy advisor recommendations, by the listing standards of the major securities exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ), and by Delaware case law that has interpreted the duty of loyalty as requiring independent director involvement in certain decisions. Washington courts have not codified the board independence norm in any formal sense, but Washington corporations whose transactions are reviewed by courts (particularly in the context of change-of-control transactions or conflict-of-interest situations) are expected to have at least some independent director involvement.
- **Increasing emphasis on audit committees.** Federal law (the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002) requires public corporations to have independent audit committees composed of directors who meet specified independence requirements. While SOX is a federal statute, it has influenced norms in non-public corporations as well, and many closely held Washington corporations now have audit committees or audit functions that did not exist in 1989. The audit committee is an important governance mechanism because it provides independent oversight of the corporation's financial reporting, its internal controls, and its relationship with external auditors.
- **Increasing emphasis on compensation committees.** Similar to audit committees, compensation committees composed of independent directors are now the norm for public corporations and many closely held corporations. The compensation committee is responsible for setting the compensation of senior executives and for ensuring that the compensation is aligned with the corporation's long-term interests. This is an important governance mechanism because executive compensation is one of the most contentious and most litigated areas of corporate governance.
- **Increasing emphasis on risk management.** Boards are expected to take an active role in overseeing the corporation's risk management function, including financial risks, operational risks, legal and regulatory risks, cybersecurity risks, and reputational risks. This expectation has grown particularly since the financial crisis of 2008 and the various cybersecurity incidents that have followed. The board's role is not to manage risk itself — that is the job of management — but to oversee the management of risk and to ensure that the corporation has appropriate risk management procedures in place.
- **Increasing emphasis on environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors.** Many institutional investors now consider ESG factors when evaluating investment opportunities, and corporations are expected to provide disclosure about their ESG practices. The ESG norm is controversial in some quarters — there are ongoing debates about whether ESG considerations are consistent with the directors' duty to act in the corporation's best interests — but it is a reality of modern corporate practice, particularly for public corporations and for corporations seeking institutional investment.

These governance norms are not mandatory rules imposed by Washington corporate law. They are expectations that have evolved through the interaction of the statute, the case law, the institutional investor community, the proxy advisors, the securities exchanges, and the broader corporate bar. Washington corporations are not required to follow them, but corporations that depart from them in significant ways risk adverse consequences: institutional investors may decline to invest, proxy advisors may recommend against management proposals, the corporation may face reputational damage, and in some cases the corporation may face litigation. The norms are, in a real sense, a second layer of corporate law — a soft-law layer that sits on top of the statutory framework and that is at least as important to the day-to-day operation of corporations as the statutes themselves.

### 14.10 The Relationship Between State Corporate Law and Federal Securities Law

A full understanding of Washington corporate law requires some attention to the relationship between state corporate law and federal securities law. The federal securities laws — principally the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, along with various other statutes and the regulations promulgated by the Securities and Exchange Commission — impose a layer of regulation on top of state corporate law that is of critical importance for public corporations and for corporations engaged in securities offerings.

The federal securities laws address, among other things: the registration and disclosure of securities offerings (Securities Act of 1933), the ongoing reporting obligations of public corporations (Exchange Act of 1934), the proxy solicitation rules (Exchange Act and SEC regulations), the tender offer rules (Exchange Act and the Williams Act), the rules governing insider trading (Exchange Act § 10(b) and Rule 10b-5), the rules governing short-swing profits by insiders (Exchange Act § 16), and the rules governing corporate governance of public corporations (Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 and Dodd-Frank Act of 2010). These federal provisions are not technically part of "corporate law" in the strict sense — they are securities regulation — but they overlap with corporate law in important ways and have shaped the practice of corporate law for decades.

For Washington public corporations, the interaction between state corporate law and federal securities law is a constant practical concern. Washington's Title 23B provides the framework for the corporation's governance, its transactions, and its relationship with its shareholders. The federal securities laws provide the framework for the corporation's disclosure obligations, its public communications, its insider trading restrictions, and certain aspects of its governance. The two frameworks operate in parallel, and a transaction that is permitted by one may nonetheless be constrained or prohibited by the other.

One particularly important area of overlap is the disclosure obligations that apply to public corporations in connection with transactions. When a Washington public corporation engages in a merger, an acquisition, a significant asset sale, or another major transaction, it is typically required to disclose the transaction to its shareholders and to the public through SEC filings (Form 8-K, proxy statements, registration statements, and others). The disclosure obligations are extensive, and they are enforced by the SEC and by private plaintiffs through Rule 10b-5 litigation. State corporate law (including Washington's) has its own disclosure requirements, typically less extensive than the federal requirements but covering some of the same ground.

Another area of overlap is the regulation of proxy solicitation. Under the Exchange Act and SEC regulations, a public corporation that solicits proxies for a shareholder meeting must file a proxy statement with the SEC that contains specified disclosures about the matters to be voted on, the identity of the solicitor, and related subjects. The proxy statement is typically a lengthy document, often dozens or hundreds of pages, and it is the principal mechanism by which public corporations communicate with their shareholders about matters to be voted on at annual meetings. Washington's Title 23B provides the state-law framework for shareholder meetings (notice, quorum, voting, and related matters), and the federal rules provide the disclosure framework. The two operate together.

A third area is the regulation of tender offers. Under the Williams Act (adopted in 1968 and incorporated into the Exchange Act), a person who makes a tender offer for the securities of a public corporation is subject to extensive disclosure obligations, timing requirements, and substantive restrictions. Washington's Title 23B does not address tender offers in the same detail as the federal law, but it includes provisions (in the merger and share exchange chapters) that interact with the federal rules in important ways. A practitioner advising on a tender offer for a Washington public corporation needs to navigate both the federal and the state-law frameworks.

A fourth area is Sarbanes-Oxley and Dodd-Frank. These two federal statutes, enacted in 2002 and 2010 respectively, imposed significant new governance requirements on public corporations, including requirements relating to audit committees, CEO/CFO certifications of financial statements, internal controls, whistleblower protections, compensation committees, clawback policies, and various other matters. These federal requirements apply to Washington public corporations as much as to public corporations incorporated elsewhere, and they operate as a layer of federal corporate governance regulation on top of the state corporate law framework.

The interaction between state corporate law and federal securities law is one of the most complex areas of modern corporate practice. A full treatment would fill a separate volume. For present purposes, the point is that Washington's Title 23B does not operate in isolation: it operates within a federal regulatory framework that provides additional rules, additional enforcement mechanisms, and additional constraints on the behavior of Washington corporations. Understanding Washington corporate law requires understanding this federal overlay, at least in broad terms.

### 14.11 Closely Held Corporations and the Venture Capital Context

A significant portion of the active corporate law work done in Washington relates to closely held corporations, particularly early-stage startups and venture-backed companies. Washington, and particularly the Seattle metropolitan area, has been a major center of technology entrepreneurship since the 1980s, and the associated corporate practice has shaped the way that Washington corporate law is actually used. This section describes some of the features of closely held and venture-backed corporate practice in Washington.

At the earliest stage, a startup is typically organized as a Washington corporation with a simple capital structure (common stock, a small number of founders as shareholders, a minimal board of directors composed of the founders) and minimal customization. The initial articles of incorporation are typically short: name, authorized shares, registered agent, incorporator. The initial bylaws are typically drawn from a standard template, customized in minor ways. The initial shareholders (the founders) often enter into a founders' agreement that addresses matters such as vesting of founders' stock, restrictions on transfer, and repurchase rights on departure.

As the startup grows and raises capital from outside investors, the corporate structure becomes more elaborate. The first major step is typically the issuance of convertible preferred stock to investors in a seed or Series A financing. The preferred stock has rights that the common stock does not: a liquidation preference (the right to receive the preferred purchase price back before the common stock receives anything in a liquidation), dividends (typically non-cumulative and conditional), voting rights (sometimes enhanced, sometimes restricted), protective provisions (the right to approve certain actions), and various other rights. The issuance of preferred stock typically requires amendments to the articles of incorporation to create the preferred class, and these amendments are one of the principal ways in which venture-backed Washington corporations use the provisions of chapter 23B.10.

The preferred stock financing also typically involves the execution of several ancillary agreements: a stock purchase agreement (the contract under which the investors purchase the preferred stock), an investors' rights agreement (which typically addresses information rights, registration rights, and rights of first offer on future issuances), a voting agreement (which typically addresses the composition of the board of directors and the voting of shares on certain matters), and a right of first refusal and co-sale agreement (which typically restricts transfers of common stock by the founders). These ancillary agreements, together with the articles of incorporation and the bylaws, form the corporate governance framework of the venture-backed corporation.

The 2024 amendment to RCW 23B.10.030 is particularly relevant to venture-backed corporations, because these corporations often need to amend their articles of incorporation during successive financing rounds. Each new round of financing typically involves the creation of a new series of preferred stock with rights that the prior series did not have, and creating a new series requires an amendment to the articles. Under the pre-2024 default, each such amendment required a two-thirds shareholder vote, which could be a significant coordination problem for corporations with a large and diverse shareholder base. Under the post-2024 default (for corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024), only a simple majority is required. This change reduces the transaction cost of amendments and makes the multi-round financing process more straightforward.

Washington's venture capital ecosystem has benefited from the availability of the Washington corporate form, but it has also been shaped by the fact that many venture investors prefer Delaware incorporation. A significant portion of Washington-based startups — particularly those that aim for venture capital financing from out-of-state investors — are organized as Delaware corporations rather than Washington corporations. The Delaware preference is driven by investor familiarity with Delaware law, the availability of the Delaware Court of Chancery for disputes, and the well-developed body of Delaware case law on venture-backed transactions. For these corporations, Washington's Title 23B is not the governing law, and the corporations operate under the DGCL for governance purposes while maintaining their operational headquarters and employees in Washington.

This pattern — Delaware corporate law for governance, Washington law for everything else — is common in American venture capital practice. It is sometimes criticized as depriving Washington of the tax revenue and legal business that would otherwise flow to Washington-incorporated entities, but it is a rational response to the realities of the venture capital market. Washington's Title 23B is a good corporate statute, but it is not Delaware, and for corporations that are aiming for outside venture investment, Delaware's advantages often outweigh Washington's. The pattern also means that the population of Washington-incorporated closely held corporations skews toward corporations that are not primarily venture-backed: family businesses, professional services firms, small and mid-sized operating companies, and other non-venture-driven enterprises. For these corporations, Title 23B is well-suited to their needs.

### 14.12 The Role of the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section

The Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section has played a distinctive role in the history of Washington corporate law. It is not merely a social or educational organization; it is, in a practical sense, the principal drafter of Washington corporate law, in partnership with the Washington Legislature. This section describes the Section's role in more detail than the main text allows.

The Business Law Section is a voluntary-membership section of the Washington State Bar Association, open to all WSBA members whose practice includes business law. The Section's membership includes corporate lawyers at large Washington firms, in-house counsel at Washington companies, solo and small-firm practitioners who advise closely held businesses, academic lawyers from Washington law schools, and retired practitioners who remain engaged with corporate law issues. The Section's membership fluctuates but typically includes several hundred to a few thousand members at any given time.

The Section operates through a governance structure that includes an elected Executive Committee (responsible for the Section's overall direction), standing committees (including the Corporate Act Revision Committee and other subject-matter committees), and ad hoc committees established for particular projects. The Section holds regular meetings, typically several times per year, and publishes a newsletter that addresses developments in Washington business law.

The Corporate Act Revision Committee (CARC) is the Section's principal working committee on corporate law. The CARC's membership includes practitioners with significant corporate law experience, selected for their knowledge of the field and their willingness to commit time to the Committee's work. The CARC's mandate is to monitor developments in corporate law (both in Washington and nationally), to identify issues with Title 23B that warrant legislative attention, and to draft proposed amendments for submission to the WSBA Board of Governors and, ultimately, to the Washington Legislature.

The CARC's work is substantial. Monitoring developments means reading national publications (the Business Lawyer, Business Law Today, the various ABA Committee on Corporate Laws publications, the Harvard Forum on Corporate Governance, law review articles, court decisions from Washington and other jurisdictions), attending conferences, and maintaining informal relationships with corporate lawyers in other states. Identifying issues means exercising judgment about which developments are significant enough to warrant Washington attention, which are better left alone, and which need further study. Drafting proposed amendments means the detailed work of reducing policy judgments to statutory text, with all the attention to cross-references, drafting conventions, and consistency that good statutory drafting requires.

The CARC does not draft in isolation. Its drafts are circulated within the Business Law Section for comment, and substantial drafts are typically the subject of multiple rounds of discussion before they are finalized. The Section's Executive Committee reviews the CARC's work and, for significant amendments, recommends the proposals to the WSBA Board of Governors. The Board of Governors, in turn, decides whether to endorse the proposals for submission to the Legislature. Endorsement is not automatic, but in practice most CARC-drafted proposals that have been through the Section's internal review process are endorsed by the Board.

Once endorsed, the proposed amendments are submitted to the Legislature, typically through a sponsoring legislator who introduces the bill. The bill then goes through the normal legislative process: committee hearings, amendments, floor debate, and passage (or, in some cases, death in committee). The CARC typically works with the sponsoring legislator and with legislative staff to ensure that the bill is understood and that any technical issues are addressed during the legislative process. The Section's members may provide testimony at committee hearings, and the Section may publish written commentary to assist legislators in understanding the proposed amendments.

This process is, in substance, a form of collaborative legislation in which the practicing bar drafts the statute and the Legislature enacts it. The arrangement is not unique to Washington — many states have similar arrangements — but it is one of the features that makes Washington corporate law a product of bar leadership rather than legislative initiative. The quality of Washington's corporate statute is, in significant measure, a reflection of the quality of the CARC's work over the decades. And the Section's continuing work — the ongoing monitoring, the ongoing drafting, the ongoing engagement with the Legislature — is the mechanism by which Title 23B stays current.

A practical consequence of this arrangement is that most significant proposed amendments to Title 23B are, by the time they reach the Legislature, relatively well-drafted and relatively uncontroversial. They have been through the CARC's drafting process, the Section's review, and the Board of Governors' endorsement. They typically enjoy bipartisan support in the Legislature (corporate law amendments do not usually divide along partisan lines). The result is that amendments to Title 23B tend to pass with relatively little controversy and relatively few substantive changes during the legislative process. The Legislature's role, in practice, is to review the proposals for public policy concerns, to add any amendments that reflect legislative judgment, and to enact the result. The heavy lifting of drafting is done by the Section.

### 14.13 Comparative Notes: Washington and Its Neighbors

A comparative perspective on Washington corporate law, placing it in the context of the corporate laws of neighboring states and of other MBCA jurisdictions, may be useful.

**Oregon.** Oregon's Business Corporation Act is codified at ORS chapter 60. Like Washington's Title 23B, Oregon's statute is based on the MBCA, and the two statutes are broadly similar. Oregon was a somewhat earlier adopter of certain provisions (Oregon's current Business Corporation Act dates from 1987, two years before Washington's 1989 WBCA), but the differences between the two are primarily matters of drafting detail and local customization rather than fundamental structural differences. Oregon and Washington corporate lawyers routinely cross-reference each other's statutes when confronting novel issues, and the jurisprudence of each state is frequently cited in the other.

**Idaho.** Idaho's Business Corporation Act is codified at Idaho Code Title 30. Like Oregon's and Washington's, Idaho's statute is MBCA-based, though with some Idaho-specific provisions. Idaho's corporate docket is smaller than Washington's or Oregon's, and its case law is correspondingly less developed, but the basic framework is familiar.

**California.** California's corporate law is in a somewhat different tradition. The California General Corporation Law is codified at California Corporations Code sections 100 et seq., and it is not based on the MBCA in the same way that Washington's and Oregon's statutes are. California's statute has its own distinctive features, including unusual provisions on director liability, unusual provisions on shareholder inspection rights, and unusual provisions on mergers and acquisitions. California is also notable for its cumulative voting requirement (under specified circumstances) and for its treatment of what it calls "quasi-foreign" corporations (corporations that have the majority of their business and shareholders in California but are incorporated elsewhere) under California Corporations Code section 2115, a provision that attempts to apply California corporate law to certain out-of-state corporations with significant California connections. California's distinctive approach has been controversial and has generated its own body of case law.

**Alaska.** Alaska's Business Corporation Act is codified at Alaska Statutes Title 10, chapter 06. Alaska's statute is MBCA-based, though with some Alaska-specific provisions reflecting the state's distinctive economic and demographic characteristics. Alaska's corporate docket is smaller than those of the Pacific coast states, and its jurisprudence is less developed, but the basic framework is familiar.

**Hawaii.** Hawaii's Business Corporation Act is codified at Hawaii Revised Statutes chapter 414. Like the Pacific Northwest states' statutes, it is MBCA-based, with some Hawaii-specific customizations. Hawaii adopted benefit corporation legislation relatively early, in 2011, following the Maryland/Vermont model rather than the California/Washington model.

**Other MBCA jurisdictions.** As noted earlier, approximately thirty-six states have adopted the MBCA in substantial form. These include states in the South (Georgia, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina, Tennessee, Virginia, West Virginia), the Midwest (Iowa, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, North Dakota, South Dakota, Wisconsin), the Southwest (Arizona, New Mexico), the Mountain West (Colorado, Idaho, Montana, Nevada, Utah, Wyoming), New England (Connecticut, Maine, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, Vermont), and the Mid-Atlantic (Maryland). The statutes of these states are broadly similar to Washington's, with variations reflecting local drafting judgments and policy preferences. A practitioner who is familiar with one MBCA statute can typically make sense of another MBCA statute without too much difficulty, though the details do matter and cross-state practice requires attention to those details.

**Non-MBCA jurisdictions.** The principal non-MBCA jurisdictions are Delaware, New York, Texas, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois, and a handful of others. Each has its own distinctive corporate law tradition. Delaware is the most important of these because of its dominance in the charter market for large public corporations. New York's Business Corporation Law has its own distinctive features and is the governing law for a substantial number of New York-based corporations (though many large New York-based public corporations are nonetheless incorporated in Delaware). Texas's Business Organizations Code is a hybrid of features from various sources and has its own distinctive character. Each of these jurisdictions offers a different flavor of corporate law, and the choice of incorporation jurisdiction is a significant decision for corporations whose operations are not tied to a particular state.

Washington, in this comparative picture, occupies a middle position. It is a faithful adopter of the MBCA, it is reasonably well-maintained through the WSBA Business Law Section's ongoing work, it has a moderately developed body of case law, it has a competent and modernized Secretary of State's office, and it is a reasonable choice of incorporation for corporations with significant Washington connections that do not need the specific features of Delaware law. It is not a jurisdiction that aggressively competes for out-of-state charters, and it is not a jurisdiction that has pursued distinctive innovations in the way that California has. It is, one might say, a competent MBCA jurisdiction with some distinctive local features (the SPC being the most notable), and that is a reasonable description of what it is aiming to be.

### 14.14 Loose Ends: Some Topics Not Covered in Detail

A complete treatment of Washington corporate law would cover many topics that this paper has not addressed in detail. Some of the most notable omissions:

- **Nonprofit corporations.** Washington nonprofit corporations are governed by Title 24 RCW, a separate title that has its own history and its own body of case law. The 2021 amendments to Title 24 (the new Washington Nonprofit Corporation Act) brought significant modernization. This paper has not addressed Title 24.
- **Professional corporations.** Washington permits professional corporations under chapter 18.100 RCW and related provisions. Professional corporations are subject to special rules reflecting the regulated nature of the professions they serve (medicine, law, accounting, architecture, and others). This paper has not addressed professional corporations.
- **Limited liability companies.** Washington limited liability companies are governed by Title 25 RCW (the Washington Limited Liability Company Act) and the subsequent Washington Uniform Limited Liability Company Act. LLCs are an increasingly important alternative to corporations for closely held business entities, and the relationship between LLC law and corporate law is one of the more interesting areas of modern business organization law. This paper has not addressed LLCs.
- **Partnerships and limited partnerships.** Washington general and limited partnerships are governed by Title 25 RCW. Partnership law is the historical foundation on which modern LLC law was built, and partnership concepts continue to influence the interpretation of LLC statutes. This paper has not addressed partnerships.
- **Securities regulation.** Washington securities law is governed by the Securities Act of Washington (chapter 21.20 RCW) and is administered by the Washington Department of Financial Institutions. Washington securities law interacts with federal securities law in complex ways. This paper has touched on the federal side of securities regulation but has not addressed the Washington side in any detail.
- **Tax issues.** Washington imposes a Business and Occupation (B&O) tax on gross receipts of businesses operating in Washington, and various other taxes. Washington is one of the few states that does not impose a corporate income tax. The tax treatment of Washington corporations is an important practical matter, but it is outside the scope of this paper.
- **Employment law.** Washington corporations are subject to Washington employment law, which has its own body of statutes, regulations, and case law. Employment law is an important practical matter for Washington corporations but is not addressed in this paper.
- **Intellectual property.** Washington corporations often hold significant intellectual property (patents, copyrights, trademarks, trade secrets), and the law governing intellectual property is an important practical matter. Most intellectual property law is federal rather than state, but some aspects (trade secrets, state trademark registrations) are state law. This paper has not addressed intellectual property.
- **Dispute resolution.** Washington corporations frequently use alternative dispute resolution (mediation, arbitration) to resolve commercial and corporate disputes. Washington has its own rules on ADR, and federal law (the Federal Arbitration Act) provides an overlay. This paper has not addressed ADR.
- **Bankruptcy and reorganization.** Washington corporations that become insolvent are typically subject to federal bankruptcy law (Title 11 of the U.S. Code). State corporate law interacts with federal bankruptcy law in complex ways, particularly in the areas of director duties to creditors, the treatment of corporate assets in bankruptcy, and the rights of shareholders in reorganization proceedings. This paper has not addressed bankruptcy.

These omissions are deliberate: the paper is focused on the history and structure of Title 23B RCW and its antecedents, and it does not attempt to cover all of Washington business law. A comprehensive treatment of Washington business law would fill several volumes, and even then it would not be complete. The interested reader is referred to the treatise literature and to the various specialized practitioner references for detailed treatments of these and other topics.

### 14.15 Lessons from the Washington Experience

What general lessons can be drawn from the history of Washington corporate law? The question is inherently speculative, and different readers will draw different lessons. But a few observations seem worth making.

First, the value of institutional continuity. Washington's corporate law has been well-served by the continuing engagement of the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section, and by the willingness of generations of practitioners to contribute their time and expertise to the maintenance of the statute. This kind of institutional continuity is not automatic; it requires deliberate cultivation, and it can easily be lost if the bar organization loses interest or if the practitioner community fragments. Washington has been fortunate in this respect, and the Section's ongoing work is one of the reasons that Washington corporate law is in as good a shape as it is.

Second, the value of architectural consistency. Washington has adhered to the MBCA tradition since 1965, through two major recodifications (1965 Title 23A and 1989 Title 23B) and numerous amendments. This architectural consistency has made Washington's statute easier to work with, easier to understand, and easier to amend in coherent ways. States that have drifted between corporate law traditions, or that have adopted ad hoc amendments without a unifying framework, have had a more difficult time maintaining coherent statutes. Washington's choice to stay within the MBCA tradition, while customizing where appropriate, has served it well.

Third, the value of pragmatic borrowing. Washington has been willing to borrow useful innovations from other jurisdictions (the forum selection provision, the business opportunity disclaimer, the simple-majority amendment vote threshold) without abandoning its own tradition. This pragmatic borrowing has kept Washington's statute current with national practice while preserving its architectural integrity. The lesson is that a state corporate statute does not have to be either a faithful adopter of a national model or a distinctive outlier; it can be both a faithful adopter and a selective borrower, depending on what its circumstances call for.

Fourth, the value of small and elegant. Washington's statute is not spectacular; it is not a monument to legislative creativity; it is not the subject of extensive scholarly attention. But it is small and elegant, in the sense described in section 12: a compact leveraged substrate that trusts its users to compose the details. This is, in the author's view, a genuine virtue. Legal infrastructure does not need to be spectacular to be good; it needs to be reliable, clear, composable, and well-maintained. Washington's Title 23B meets these criteria, and that is enough.

Fifth, the limits of the statute. Corporate law, however well-drafted, cannot do everything. It cannot prevent bad actors from mismanaging corporations; it cannot ensure that directors will act with integrity and good judgment; it cannot guarantee that corporations will serve their intended purposes; it cannot solve every problem that arises in the life of a corporation. What it can do is provide a framework within which good actors can operate effectively, and a set of remedies for the consequences when bad actors misbehave. The work of making corporations actually function well is done by the directors, officers, shareholders, and advisors who operate under the statute, not by the statute itself. Washington's statute is well-designed to support this work, but it is not a substitute for the work.

Sixth, the ongoing nature of the project. Corporate law is never finished. The economy changes, business practices evolve, new transaction types emerge, old problems find new forms, and the statute has to keep up. The Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section's continuing work on Title 23B is a reminder that the project is ongoing. The 2024 amendments are not the end of the story. There will be more amendments, more refinements, more responses to emerging issues. The best we can hope for is that the work continues to be done well and that the statute continues to serve its purposes.

These are the lessons I would draw from the history of Washington corporate law. Other readers will draw different lessons, and the lessons will change over time as Washington's experience with the statute continues to develop. But the basic story — a well-maintained MBCA-family statute, anchored in the work of a competent bar organization, periodically refined through collaboration between the bar and the Legislature — is one that is worth attention, both for its intrinsic interest and for what it can teach us about the design and maintenance of legal infrastructure more generally.

## Appendix C: Glossary of Key Terms

**Articles of incorporation.** The fundamental charter document of a corporation, filed with the Secretary of State, which establishes the corporation's existence and sets forth its basic characteristics (name, purpose, authorized capital, registered agent, initial directors).

**Bylaws.** A secondary governance document, adopted by the incorporators or the board of directors, which establishes the detailed rules for the corporation's internal governance.

**Business judgment rule.** A common-law and statutory presumption that directors, in making business decisions, acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation. In Washington, the rule is rebuttable by a showing of fraud, dishonesty, or incompetence.

**Corporate opportunity doctrine.** A specific application of the duty of loyalty holding that directors and officers may not personally take advantage of business opportunities that properly belong to the corporation. Washington permits waiver of the doctrine by provision in the articles of incorporation (business opportunity disclaimer).

**Default rule.** A rule provided by the statute that applies unless the articles of incorporation or bylaws provide otherwise.

**Duty of care.** The fiduciary duty of directors and officers to exercise reasonable care in the performance of their corporate responsibilities. In Washington, the standard is "ordinary care" rather than the Delaware "gross negligence" standard.

**Duty of loyalty.** The fiduciary duty of directors and officers to act in the best interests of the corporation and not for their own personal benefit. Breaches include self-dealing, taking corporate opportunities, and usurping corporate assets.

**Exculpation provision.** A provision in the articles of incorporation that limits or eliminates director personal liability for certain breaches of the duty of care, within statutory limits.

**Forum selection provision.** A provision in the articles of incorporation or bylaws that requires certain categories of internal corporate disputes to be resolved in a specified court.

**Indemnification.** The corporation's commitment to reimburse directors and officers for liabilities and expenses incurred in their corporate capacities, subject to statutory limits.

**MBCA.** The Model Business Corporation Act, promulgated by the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws and periodically revised.

**RMBCA.** The Revised Model Business Corporation Act, the 1984 comprehensive revision of the MBCA.

**SPC.** Social Purpose Corporation; in Washington, a for-profit corporation formed under chapter 23B.25 RCW that has adopted a social purpose in addition to ordinary profit motives.

**Shareholder agreement.** An agreement among shareholders that may, under RCW 23B.07.320, depart from certain default governance rules of the Act.

**UBI.** Unified Business Identifier; a nine-digit number assigned to Washington business entities for cross-agency identification, introduced in 1977.

**WBCA.** Washington Business Corporation Act; the 1989 statute codified as Title 23B RCW.

**Wong (not used in this paper; placeholder for glossary alphabetization).**

### E.1 Extended Note on *Para-Medical Leasing v. Hangen*

The *Para-Medical Leasing v. Hangen* decision, 48 Wn. App. 389, 739 P.2d 717 (1987), deserves more detailed treatment than the main text provides, because it is a fairly clean illustration of how Washington courts have historically approached the business judgment rule in the context of a closely held corporation.

The facts: Dr. Gene Mason was the sole shareholder and sole director of Para-Medical Leasing, Inc., a small Washington corporation engaged in the leasing of vehicles and equipment (principally to medical professionals, as the name suggests). In 1981, Dr. Mason decided to take a ten-month leave of absence to teach in Africa, and he needed someone to manage Para-Medical during his absence. He turned to Todd Hangen, a certified public accountant who had served as Para-Medical's outside accountant for several years and who was therefore familiar with the business. Hangen agreed to serve as interim manager, and the two entered into an arrangement under which Hangen would run the company during Dr. Mason's absence.

When Dr. Mason returned from Africa, he found that Para-Medical's business had deteriorated. Some customers had been lost; some leases had gone into default; the company's financial position had weakened. Dr. Mason blamed Hangen for the deterioration and, acting on behalf of Para-Medical, sued Hangen for negligent mismanagement. The case was tried to the bench (no jury), and after the presentation of Para-Medical's evidence, Hangen moved for dismissal. The trial court granted the motion and dismissed the case. Para-Medical appealed.

The Court of Appeals, Division I, affirmed. The court's analysis proceeded in two steps. First, the court considered whether the business judgment rule applied to Hangen at all. Para-Medical argued that the rule applied only to directors of corporations, and that Hangen — as a temporary officer rather than a director — was not entitled to its protection. The court rejected this argument, holding that the business judgment rule applies to corporate officers as well as to directors. The court reasoned that the policy rationale for the rule — to encourage vigorous corporate management by protecting officers from liability for good-faith business decisions that, with hindsight, turn out to have been mistakes — applies equally to officers and to directors. The court cited general corporate law treatises and MBCA commentary in support of this conclusion.

Second, the court considered whether Hangen had in fact acted in good faith and without corrupt motive. The court found that the trial record supported the trial court's conclusion that Hangen had done so. Hangen had made business decisions during his tenure, some of which had turned out to be wrong, but the decisions had been made with the information that was available to him at the time, in the honest belief that they were in the corporation's best interests, and without any self-dealing or corrupt motive. Under these circumstances, the business judgment rule shielded Hangen from liability, and the trial court's dismissal was affirmed.

*Para-Medical Leasing* is important for several reasons. First, it extends the business judgment rule's protection to corporate officers, not just directors. This is a potentially significant extension, particularly in closely held corporations where the same people often serve as both officers and directors, and where the line between "officer" conduct and "director" conduct can be blurry. Second, it emphasizes the "good faith" and "corrupt motive" elements of the rule. The rule does not protect officers who act in bad faith or who have corrupt motives; it protects officers who act honestly and without self-dealing. Third, it illustrates the rule's application in a fact pattern that is typical of closely held corporations: a business deterioration during a period of management transition, followed by litigation brought by the returning principal against the interim manager.

The case also raises some questions that the court did not fully address. One question is the standard of care that Hangen was required to meet. The court's analysis focused on the "good faith" element of the business judgment rule, but the rule also has a "reasonable investigation" or "informed decision" element that was not extensively discussed. If Hangen had made decisions without any investigation, would the rule still have applied? The court's opinion does not squarely address this question, though the overall reasoning suggests that some degree of investigation would be required.

Another question is the relationship between the business judgment rule and the standard of care specified by the corporate law statute then in effect (Title 23A RCW). Title 23A included a statutory standard of care for directors, tracking the MBCA formulation, but the court's opinion did not closely analyze the interaction between the statutory standard and the common-law business judgment rule. The question of how these two interact — whether the statutory standard supersedes the common-law rule, whether the two operate in parallel, or whether the common-law rule provides a gloss on the statutory standard — has been the subject of scholarly debate and some judicial consideration, but it was not squarely addressed in *Para-Medical*.

Despite these unanswered questions, *Para-Medical Leasing* remains a useful Washington precedent on the scope of the business judgment rule. It has been cited in subsequent cases and in treatises, and it stands for the general proposition that Washington applies the rule in a manner broadly consistent with the national consensus.

### E.2 Extended Note on *Interlake Porsche v. Bucholz*

*Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. v. Bucholz*, 45 Wn. App. 502, 728 P.2d 597 (1986), is the companion Washington precedent to *Para-Medical Leasing* on the duty of loyalty. Where *Para-Medical* addressed the business judgment rule's protection of good-faith officer conduct, *Interlake Porsche* addressed the rule's inapplicability to self-dealing and the remedies available for duty-of-loyalty breaches.

The facts: Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. (IPA) was a closely held Washington corporation engaged in the automobile dealership business. Terry M. Bucholz was the majority shareholder and effective manager of IPA. Edward M. Blackburn, Jr., was a minority shareholder. Over a period of years (1980 through September 1984), Bucholz used substantial corporate funds for expenses that were, in substantial part, for his personal benefit rather than for the corporation's benefit. The expenses were categorized as "customer relations," "demo expense," "sales promotion," "company vehicle expense," "travel and entertainment," "membership and dues," and "owner's life insurance," and the total amount exceeded $534,000. Blackburn, as the minority shareholder, brought suit against Bucholz and the corporation, alleging that Bucholz's expenditures constituted a breach of his fiduciary duties to the corporation and to the minority shareholder.

The Court of Appeals, Division I, ruled in favor of Blackburn. The court's analysis began with the general proposition that "directors and officers stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation they serve and are not permitted to retain any personal profit or advantage gleaned 'on the side.'" This proposition, taken from general corporate law principles, establishes the duty of loyalty as a fundamental obligation of directors and officers. The court then applied the proposition to Bucholz's conduct. Because Bucholz had used corporate funds for personal expenses without board authorization or shareholder ratification, the court held that the burden shifted to Bucholz to justify the expenditures as having been made for legitimate corporate purposes. Bucholz's justifications were, in the court's view, inadequate: many of the expenses were for items that had little or no relationship to IPA's business, and the record did not support Bucholz's claims that the expenditures were legitimate corporate expenses.

The remedy was disgorgement. Bucholz was required to repay to IPA the amount that he had improperly expended. This is the classic remedy for duty-of-loyalty breaches: the disloyal fiduciary is required to return to the corporation the value that he has wrongfully taken, so that the corporation is made whole and the fiduciary does not profit from his disloyalty. Washington courts have applied this remedy consistently in duty-of-loyalty cases, and *Interlake Porsche* is one of the cleaner precedents for it.

The case is notable for its clear statement of the fiduciary duty framework and its application of the burden-shifting rule in duty-of-loyalty cases. It is also notable for the scope of the conduct involved: Bucholz's expenditures were not a single transaction or a small amount of money; they were a pattern of expenditures over a period of years, totaling more than half a million dollars. This kind of sustained self-dealing is, unfortunately, not uncommon in closely held corporations where the majority shareholder has effective control of the corporation's finances, and cases like *Interlake Porsche* are the principal judicial response to it.

The case also illustrates the limitations of the business judgment rule. The rule protects good-faith business decisions, but it does not protect decisions that are tainted by self-dealing. When a director or officer has a personal interest in a transaction (as Bucholz did in his various personal expenditures), the rule does not apply, and the court will scrutinize the transaction under the more demanding standards of the duty of loyalty. This scrutiny typically involves burden-shifting (the defendant must justify the transaction) and may result in disgorgement, injunctive relief, or other remedies. *Interlake Porsche* is a straightforward application of this principle.

One question that *Interlake Porsche* raises is the availability of indemnification for a director who has been found liable for a duty-of-loyalty breach. Washington law, like the MBCA and most other state corporate statutes, prohibits indemnification of a director who has been adjudicated liable for conduct that is not in good faith or that is contrary to the corporation's best interests. A director in Bucholz's position would not be entitled to indemnification from the corporation for the amounts he was required to disgorge, because his conduct was fundamentally inconsistent with the corporation's interests. This is an important limitation on the scope of indemnification, and it is one of the reasons that indemnification rights are not a complete shield against personal liability for corporate fiduciaries.

### E.3 Extended Note on the 1933 Uniform Business Corporation Act

The 1928 Uniform Business Corporation Act (UBCA), on which Washington's 1933 statute was patterned, deserves more extended treatment because it is the foundation on which modern Washington corporate law was built and because its influence can still be traced in the current statute.

The UBCA was drafted by the National Conference of Commissioners on Uniform State Laws (NCCUSL), an organization that had been founded in 1892 to promote uniformity in state law. NCCUSL's early work focused on areas where uniformity was clearly beneficial: commercial paper, sales of goods, partnership, and similar fields where interstate commerce was hindered by the variation in state laws. By the mid-1920s, NCCUSL had identified corporate law as a candidate for uniformity, and it commissioned a drafting project to produce a uniform corporate statute.

The drafting project was substantial. The reporter was an experienced corporate lawyer, and the drafting committee included representatives from various state bar associations and from academia. The committee considered the existing state corporate statutes, reviewed the academic literature on corporate law, and drafted text through multiple rounds of revision. The resulting UBCA was published in 1928 as a model statute recommended for adoption by the states.

The UBCA addressed all the principal topics of a corporate statute: formation, capital structure, governance, amendments, mergers, dissolution, and foreign corporations. Its treatment of these topics was, by the standards of its time, relatively modern. The Act included provisions on shareholder voting rights, director duties, and dissenters' rights that were more developed than those of many existing state statutes. It used par value stock as its basic capital unit (a common feature of the era), and it included the stated capital / surplus accounting concept.

But the UBCA faced resistance. State bar associations were reluctant to give up the power to customize corporate law to local circumstances. State legislatures were reluctant to adopt a uniform model without local modification. And the commercial bar in Delaware and New York, the two most important corporate law jurisdictions of the era, had little interest in moving to a uniform model when they were already reasonably satisfied with their own statutes. By the early 1940s, only a handful of states had adopted the UBCA in substantial form, and NCCUSL concluded that uniformity in corporate law was not achievable.

In 1943, NCCUSL formally withdrew the UBCA as a "uniform" act and rechristened it as a "Model or State Business Corporation Act." The rechristening was not merely cosmetic; it reflected a substantive shift in philosophy. The new label acknowledged that states would want to depart from the model in various ways, and it invited them to do so. The result was that the model became a starting point for state drafting rather than a destination, and state statutes diverged over time as each state made its own choices.

Washington's 1933 adoption of the UBCA (before the 1943 rechristening) placed Washington among the early adopters of the uniform model. The 1933 Washington statute departed from the UBCA in various ways to reflect Washington practice and policy, but the basic structure was taken from the uniform model. When the 1933 Washington statute was later replaced by Title 23A (1965) and then by Title 23B (1989), those later statutes were based on different national models (the 1950 and 1984 versions of the MBCA, respectively), but they retained some features that had been inherited from the 1933 Washington statute and ultimately from the 1928 UBCA.

This is an interesting example of path dependence in legal drafting. Decisions made in the drafting of the 1928 UBCA — decisions about vocabulary, structure, and substantive rules — continue to influence Washington corporate law nearly a century later, even though the specific statute that brought those decisions into Washington law has long since been replaced. This is a general feature of legal drafting: early choices tend to persist because subsequent drafters work within the framework established by their predecessors, and wholesale departures from that framework are relatively rare.

### E.4 Extended Note on the 1984 RMBCA Revisions

The 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act (RMBCA) is the principal source for Washington's 1989 Title 23B, and it deserves more extended treatment. The 1984 revision was a major undertaking, spanning several years of drafting work by the ABA Committee on Corporate Laws under the reporting of Robert W. Hamilton.

The principal goals of the 1984 revision were:

1. **Reorganization and renumbering.** The 1950 MBCA, as amended through the 1970s, had accumulated organizational inconsistencies and numbering anomalies. The 1984 revision reorganized the chapters and sections in a more logical flow and introduced a new numbering scheme that was easier to work with. The chapter-and-section format (e.g., § 8.30 for "chapter 8, section 30") became the standard, and it has been followed by most subsequent MBCA-family statutes, including Washington's Title 23B.
2. **Modernization of language.** The 1950 MBCA used some drafting conventions and terminology that had become dated by the 1980s. The 1984 revision updated the language to be clearer and more consistent, and it adopted modern drafting conventions that improved the readability of the text.
3. **Abandonment of par value.** The 1950 MBCA retained the par value concept, which required corporations to track stated capital and surplus as separate accounting categories and imposed various restrictions on distributions from stated capital. The 1984 revision abandoned par value in favor of a simpler authorized-consideration model, under which corporations simply had to issue shares for consideration that the board determined to be adequate. This was a substantial simplification and one of the most consequential changes in the 1984 revision.
4. **Refinement of director duties.** The 1984 revision refined the statutory statement of director duties, incorporating lessons from two decades of case law and academic commentary. The revised provisions (in what became chapter 8 of the RMBCA) included clearer statements of the duty of care, the duty of loyalty, and the conditions under which directors could rely on reports and information from others.
5. **Modernization of indemnification.** The 1984 revision modernized the indemnification provisions to reflect the experience of MBCA jurisdictions over the preceding decades. The revised provisions included clearer rules on mandatory indemnification for successful defense, advancement of expenses, non-exclusivity, and the scope of permissible indemnification.
6. **Modernization of merger and share exchange.** The 1984 revision introduced cleaner merger procedures and added the "share exchange" as a new statutory transaction form. The share exchange is a useful tool for certain kinds of transactions, particularly those where the acquirer wants to use corporate mechanics (rather than a voluntary tender offer) to acquire all of the outstanding shares of a target corporation.
7. **Clearer dissenters' rights.** The 1984 revision clarified the procedures for dissenters' rights, making them more administratively workable.
8. **Improved treatment of dissolution.** The 1984 revision included an improved treatment of voluntary dissolution, administrative dissolution, and judicial dissolution, with clearer procedures and clearer grounds for each.

These changes, taken together, produced a statute that was significantly more modern than the 1950 MBCA. The 1984 RMBCA was not a radical departure from the 1950 Act — the basic structure and many of the specific rules were retained — but it was a comprehensive refinement that addressed the accumulated issues of three decades of practice.

When Washington adopted the 1984 RMBCA as the basis for its 1989 Title 23B, it took advantage of all these improvements. The Washington statute abandoned the par value concept (which Washington had retained through the 1965 Title 23A), adopted the improved director duty provisions, modernized its indemnification rules, and so on. The 1989 Washington statute was thus a significant step forward from the 1965 statute, and many of the improvements can be traced directly to the 1984 RMBCA revision.

### E.5 Extended Note on the 2012 SPC Enactment

The 2012 enactment of chapter 23B.25 RCW (the Social Purpose Corporation chapter) was the product of a drafting process that deserves more extended treatment than the main text provides.

The drafting was led by the Corporate Act Revision Committee (CARC) of the WSBA Business Law Section, a thirteen-member committee of Washington corporate lawyers. The Committee began its work in 2010, in response to the growing interest in benefit corporation legislation that had emerged after Maryland's adoption of the first such statute in April 2010. The Committee's charge was to examine the benefit corporation movement, assess its relevance to Washington, and recommend a Washington response.

The Committee's deliberations took roughly two years. During that period, the Committee examined the B Lab Model Benefit Corporation Legislation and the various state adoptions that had followed; examined the California Flexible Purpose Corporation statute, which had been enacted in 2011 and which took a different approach; consulted with practitioners, academics, and representatives of the mission-driven business community; and drafted multiple versions of potential legislation.

The Committee's internal debates were substantial. Some members favored direct adoption of the B Lab model, on the grounds that uniformity with other states would make Washington's new corporate form more readily understood by investors, customers, and the public. Other members favored the California Flexible Purpose Corporation approach, which was less prescriptive and more consistent with Washington's general preference for light-touch regulatory frameworks. Still other members favored a completely novel Washington approach that would not track either the B Lab model or the California model.

After extended discussion, the Committee arrived at what became the Washington SPC: a framework that was closer to the California Flexible Purpose Corporation than to the B Lab model, but that included Washington-specific features. The key choices were:

1. **Permissive rather than mandatory director consideration.** Directors of an SPC would be permitted to consider the corporation's social purpose and the interests of non-shareholder constituencies, but would not be required to do so. This was a significant departure from the B Lab model, which required mandatory consideration of specified stakeholder interests.
2. **No third-party standards.** The Washington SPC would not require the use of any third-party standard for assessing benefit performance. This was another departure from the B Lab model, which contemplated (though did not strictly require) the use of third-party standards.
3. **Flexible annual reporting.** The Washington SPC would require an annual report describing how the corporation had advanced its social purpose, but the report would not be required to use any particular format or to be audited by a third party. This was a significant simplification compared to the B Lab model's more elaborate benefit report requirements.
4. **No benefit enforcement proceeding.** The Washington SPC would not include a novel "benefit enforcement proceeding." Shareholders who believed that the corporation was not adequately pursuing its social purpose would have their ordinary corporate law remedies (derivative actions, direct actions), but no new statutory cause of action would be created.
5. **Easy conversion in and out.** The Washington SPC would be easy to enter (by filing articles of incorporation as an SPC or by amending existing articles to adopt SPC status) and easy to exit (by amending articles to remove the SPC designation). This was designed to give corporations the flexibility to experiment with SPC status without committing to it irrevocably.

These choices were not made easily. The Committee engaged in extended debate about each of them, and there was genuine disagreement among Committee members about the right balance. The final choices reflected the Committee's collective judgment that a light-touch framework would produce better outcomes in Washington than a more prescriptive framework, but the judgment was not unanimous.

Once the Committee had arrived at its final proposal, the proposal was circulated within the WSBA Business Law Section for comment, and the Section's Executive Committee reviewed and endorsed it. The proposal was then presented to the WSBA Board of Governors, which also endorsed it. The proposal was introduced in the 2012 regular session of the Washington Legislature as House Bill 2239.

The legislative process in the 2012 session was relatively brief. The bill received favorable reports from the relevant committees, passed both chambers with bipartisan support, and was signed by Governor Christine Gregoire on March 30, 2012. The bill took effect on June 7, 2012, the normal effective date for bills signed in late March (ninety days after the end of the regular session).

The legislative history of HB 2239 reflects the light-touch nature of the drafting process. Committee hearings on the bill were not particularly contentious; there was some testimony from proponents (representatives of the mission-driven business community and the WSBA Business Law Section) and some questioning from legislators, but there was no significant opposition. The bill was amended in minor ways during the legislative process but its basic structure was preserved.

After enactment, the Washington SPC became one of the first social-purpose corporate forms available in any state and one of the only forms that followed the California-style light-touch approach (the California Flexible Purpose Corporation having been enacted a few months earlier). In the years since, the Washington SPC has been used by a modest but growing number of corporations, as described in section 14.8. The 2014 amendments, discussed in the main text, corrected some drafting issues that had been identified after the statute's first year in operation, but did not change the basic structure of the form.

The Washington SPC is, in sum, a product of the WSBA Business Law Section's work, and it reflects the Section's general preferences for light-touch regulation, flexibility, and reliance on ordinary corporate law remedies. Whether these preferences have produced the best possible corporate form is a question that reasonable people can disagree about, and the academic critique of the Washington SPC (cited in the bibliography) offers one view. But the preferences themselves are coherent, they are grounded in a considered view of how corporate law should operate, and they have produced a statute that is consistent with the broader architecture of Washington corporate law.

### E.6 Extended Note on the 2024 Amendments

The 2024 amendments to Title 23B, codified as chapter 22 of the Laws of 2024, deserve more extended treatment because they represent the most recent significant recalibration of Washington corporate law and because they illustrate the ongoing process of statutory maintenance that characterizes the relationship between the WSBA Business Law Section and the Washington Legislature.

The principal provision of the 2024 amendments, as discussed in section 8 of the main text, is section 20, which amends RCW 23B.10.030 to change the default shareholder vote threshold for amendments to the articles of incorporation. For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, the default is a simple majority (of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment and of the votes entitled to be cast by each other voting group entitled to vote separately on the amendment). For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the prior default of two-thirds (with the exception of public companies, for which the default was already a simple majority) continues to apply unless the corporation amends its articles.

The policy rationale for this change is grounded in several observations:

1. **National practice.** Most MBCA-family states and Delaware had long used a simple majority default for amendments to the articles of incorporation. Washington's two-thirds default was, by 2024, an outlier among American corporate statutes.
2. **Transaction cost.** The two-thirds default imposed a significant transaction cost on corporations that needed to amend their articles, particularly venture-backed corporations that typically need to amend their articles at each new financing round.
3. **Minority protection alternatives.** Protection of minority shareholders against disadvantageous amendments was available through mechanisms other than the supermajority vote requirement, including class voting (which provides separate approval rights to affected classes), appraisal rights (which provide a cash-out option for dissenters), and the general fiduciary duty framework (which provides a remedy against self-dealing).
4. **Settled expectations.** Corporations formed before August 1, 2024, had been formed with the expectation of a two-thirds supermajority default, and their shareholders may have made investment decisions in reliance on that expectation. Changing the default retroactively would have altered these settled expectations in ways that might be unfair.

The bifurcated approach — new default for new corporations, old default for existing corporations — reflects a balance among these considerations. New corporations can benefit from the modern default without the need for special drafting. Existing corporations retain their settled expectations. And existing corporations that wish to adopt the new default can do so by amending their articles, an amendment that would itself require a two-thirds vote under the old default.

The 2024 amendments include other provisions beyond section 20. The broader package addresses various procedural and substantive issues in Title 23B, including refinements to the merger and share exchange provisions (chapter 23B.11A), refinements to the dissolution provisions (chapter 23B.14), and technical corrections to various other sections. These additional provisions are mostly incremental refinements rather than fundamental changes, and they reflect the CARC's ongoing monitoring of issues that arise in practice.

The 2024 amendments, taken as a whole, are a good illustration of how Washington corporate law evolves. The changes are modest in scope, focused on identified issues, grounded in considered policy rationales, and drafted in ways that preserve the statute's architectural integrity. They do not represent a fundamental rethinking of Washington corporate law; they represent the kind of periodic maintenance that any well-functioning statute requires. And they are the product of continuing collaboration between the WSBA Business Law Section (which does most of the drafting work) and the Washington Legislature (which enacts the result).

Looking ahead, one can expect further amendments to Title 23B in the coming years. The CARC will continue its monitoring work, identifying issues and drafting responses. The WSBA Business Law Section will continue to review and endorse the CARC's proposals. The Legislature will continue to enact them, more or less as drafted. And Title 23B will continue to evolve as Washington's corporate law needs evolve. This is the ongoing project of statutory maintenance that characterizes a well-maintained corporate law regime, and Washington has been fortunate in having the institutional infrastructure to support it.

### E.7 A Note on Primary and Secondary Source Use

A final note on methodology. This paper has relied on a mix of primary and secondary sources. Primary sources — the Revised Code of Washington, the Session Laws, the published opinions of Washington courts, the Washington State Archives, and similar materials — provide the authoritative text of the law. Secondary sources — law review articles, treatises, practitioner publications, and scholarly commentary — provide interpretive and analytical guidance.

The relationship between primary and secondary sources in legal research is a recurring methodological question. Primary sources are authoritative but often unclear: statutory text can be ambiguous, case law can be fragmentary, and legislative history can be sparse. Secondary sources are not authoritative but are often clearer: treatises explain; articles analyze; practitioner publications synthesize. A responsible legal researcher needs both: primary sources for authority, secondary sources for understanding.

This paper has tried to use both, with appropriate attention to the distinction. Where the paper states a proposition of law, it has tried to cite primary authority (a statute, a case, or a session law). Where the paper offers an analytical or historical observation, it has often cited secondary sources (law review articles, treatises, practitioner materials). The references and bibliography section lists both primary and secondary sources, clearly labeled, so that readers who want to dig deeper can follow the citations back to the originals.

A particular word is warranted about the WBCA Sourcebook, which has been cited repeatedly in this paper. The Sourcebook is, strictly speaking, a secondary source: it is a practitioner reference compiled by the WSBA Business Law Section. But it has a somewhat elevated status because the Committee commentary it contains was adopted as part of the legislative history of the 1989 WBCA and subsequent amendments. Washington courts have treated the Sourcebook's Committee commentary as persuasive authority when interpreting ambiguous provisions of Title 23B, and practitioners routinely cite it in briefs and opinions. The Sourcebook thus occupies a middle ground between primary and secondary authority: not quite primary, but more authoritative than an ordinary secondary source.

For readers who want to do their own research on Washington corporate law, the Sourcebook is probably the most valuable single reference. It collects the statutory text, the legislative history, the Committee commentary, and selected case notes in a single volume, and it is periodically updated to reflect amendments. The most recent edition is the 5th edition (2016), which will eventually be superseded by a new edition reflecting post-2016 amendments. Between editions, practitioners typically supplement the Sourcebook with current session laws and current case law. The WSBA Business Law Section's website provides access to some of the Sourcebook materials.

### F.1 RCW 23B.01.400 — Definitions

Chapter 23B.01 includes a definitions section (numbered as RCW 23B.01.400 in the current statutory text, though the precise section number has varied over time) that defines key terms used throughout Title 23B. The defined terms include:

- **"Articles of incorporation."** The original and all amended articles of incorporation.
- **"Authorized shares."** The shares of all classes that a corporation is authorized to issue.
- **"Board of directors."** The body authorized to manage the business and affairs of the corporation.
- **"Corporation" or "domestic corporation."** A corporation for profit subject to the provisions of Title 23B.
- **"Deliver" or "delivery."** Any method of delivery used in conventional commercial practice, including delivery by mail, facsimile, or electronic transmission.
- **"Director."** A member of the board of directors.
- **"Distribution."** A direct or indirect transfer of money or other property to or for the benefit of shareholders.
- **"Effective date of notice."** The date on which notice becomes effective under RCW 23B.01.410.
- **"Employee."** An officer, nonofficer employee, or independent contractor who is not an independent contractor solely by virtue of being a director.
- **"Entity."** A corporation, foreign corporation, nonprofit corporation, foreign nonprofit corporation, limited liability company, and similar entities.
- **"Foreign corporation."** A corporation for profit incorporated under a law other than the law of this state.
- **"Governmental subdivision."** Includes authority, county, district, and municipality.
- **"Includes" and "including."** Denotes a partial definition.
- **"Individual."** A natural person, or the personal representative of a deceased individual.
- **"Notice."** A writing or other form of communication that meets the requirements of RCW 23B.01.410.
- **"Person."** Includes an individual and an entity.
- **"Principal office."** The office (in or out of this state) where the principal executive offices of a corporation are located.
- **"Proceeding."** A civil, criminal, administrative, or investigatory action.
- **"Record date."** The date established for determining the shareholders entitled to notice of and to vote at a shareholder meeting, to receive a distribution, or for any other purpose.
- **"Shareholder."** The person in whose name shares are registered in the records of a corporation.
- **"Shares."** The units into which the proprietary interests in a corporation are divided.
- **"Subscriber."** A person who subscribes for shares in a corporation, whether before or after incorporation.
- **"United States."** Includes a district, authority, bureau, commission, department, and any other agency of the United States.
- **"Voting group."** All shares of one or more classes or series that under the articles of incorporation or Title 23B are entitled to vote and be counted together collectively on a matter at a meeting of shareholders.

These definitions are important because they establish the meaning of terms that are used throughout the statute. A practitioner working with Title 23B needs to know what the defined terms mean, because the meanings may not be intuitive or may differ from ordinary usage.

### F.2 RCW 23B.02.020 — Articles of Incorporation

RCW 23B.02.020 specifies the required and permitted contents of the articles of incorporation. The required contents include:

1. A corporate name that satisfies the requirements of chapter 23B.04.
2. The number of shares the corporation is authorized to issue.
3. The street address of the corporation's initial registered office and the name of its initial registered agent at that office.
4. The name and address of each incorporator.

The articles of incorporation may also contain:

1. The names and addresses of the individuals who are to serve as the initial directors.
2. Provisions not inconsistent with law regarding the purpose or purposes for which the corporation is organized, the management of the business and the regulation of the affairs of the corporation, the definition, limitation, and regulation of the powers of the corporation, its board of directors, and shareholders, a par value for authorized shares or classes of shares, and the imposition of personal liability on shareholders for the debts of the corporation.
3. Any provision that under Title 23B is required or permitted to be set forth in the bylaws.
4. A provision eliminating or limiting the personal liability of a director to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director, as permitted by RCW 23B.08.320.
5. A provision limiting or eliminating any duty of a director or any other person to offer the corporation the right to have or participate in specified business opportunities (the "business opportunity disclaimer" added by the 2020 amendments).
6. Other provisions permitted by specific sections of Title 23B.

The structure of RCW 23B.02.020 is characteristic of the WBCA's drafting approach: a short list of mandatory requirements, a longer list of permitted (but optional) provisions, and a general authorization to include other provisions consistent with law. This structure gives incorporators considerable flexibility to customize the corporation's articles while ensuring that the minimum necessary information is always included.

### F.3 RCW 23B.02.080 — Forum Selection

RCW 23B.02.080, added during the 2014-2023 refinement period, authorizes forum selection provisions in articles of incorporation or bylaws. The key features of the provision are:

1. A forum selection provision may require internal corporate proceedings to be commenced and maintained exclusively in specified courts.
2. The specified courts must be courts of this state, or, if the corporation has a reasonable relationship with another jurisdiction, courts in that jurisdiction.
3. An "internal corporate proceeding" is defined as a proceeding asserting a claim based on a violation of a duty under this state's laws by a current or former director, officer, or shareholder in such capacity; a proceeding commenced or maintained in the right of the corporation; a proceeding asserting a claim arising pursuant to any provision of the Act or the corporation's articles of incorporation or bylaws; or a proceeding asserting a claim concerning the internal affairs of the corporation.
4. A forum selection provision may not confer jurisdiction on any court, over any person, or of any proceeding.
5. A forum selection provision may not prohibit commencing or maintaining an internal corporate proceeding in the courts of this state.
6. A forum selection provision may not require claims asserted in an internal corporate proceeding to be determined by arbitration.

The limits on forum selection provisions are important. They preserve the availability of Washington courts for internal corporate disputes, they prevent the provision from being used as an end-run around the personal jurisdiction rules, and they prevent the provision from being used to substitute arbitration for litigation (which would raise concerns about class action waivers and about the scope of judicial review).

### F.4 RCW 23B.07.320 — Shareholder Agreements

RCW 23B.07.320 authorizes shareholder agreements that depart from the default governance rules of the Act. The key features of the provision are:

1. A shareholder agreement that complies with the section is effective to govern the exercise of corporate powers, management of the business and affairs of the corporation, or the relationship among the shareholders, the directors, and the corporation, notwithstanding any other provision of the Act that would require different conduct.
2. A shareholder agreement may: eliminate the board of directors or restrict its discretion or powers; govern the authorization or making of distributions whether or not in proportion to ownership of shares, subject to the limitations of RCW 23B.06.400; establish who shall be directors or officers of the corporation, or their terms of office, or manner of selection or removal; govern the exercise or division of voting power by or between the shareholders and directors; establish the terms and conditions of any agreement for the transfer or use of property or the provision of services between the corporation and any shareholder, director, officer, or employee; transfer to one or more shareholders or other persons all or part of the authority to exercise the corporate powers or to manage the business and affairs of the corporation; require dissolution of the corporation at the request of one or more of the shareholders or upon the occurrence of a specified event or contingency; and otherwise govern the exercise of corporate powers or the management of the business and affairs of the corporation.
3. A shareholder agreement is effective only if: set forth in the articles of incorporation or bylaws and approved by all persons who are shareholders at the time of the agreement, or set forth in a written agreement that is signed by all persons who are shareholders at the time of the agreement and is made known to the corporation; subject to amendment only by all persons who are shareholders at the time of the amendment; and valid for ten years unless the agreement provides otherwise.
4. The existence of a shareholder agreement must be noted conspicuously on the front or back of each share certificate or on the information statement for uncertificated shares.
5. A shareholder agreement does not cease to be effective because the corporation thereafter has shares that are listed on a national securities exchange or regularly traded in a market maintained by broker-dealers.

RCW 23B.07.320 is one of the most important provisions of the WBCA for closely held corporations, because it permits substantial deviation from the default governance rules. A well-drafted shareholder agreement can transform an ordinary Washington corporation into an entity with governance closely resembling that of a partnership, while retaining the liability-shield and other advantages of corporate form.

### F.5 RCW 23B.08.300 — General Standards for Directors

RCW 23B.08.300 sets forth the general standards of conduct for directors. The key features of the provision are:

1. A director shall discharge the duties of a director, including duties as a member of a committee:
   - (a) In good faith;
   - (b) With the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances; and
   - (c) In a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation.
2. In discharging the duties of a director, a director is entitled to rely on information, opinions, reports, or statements, including financial statements and other financial data, if prepared or presented by specified categories of persons (officers and employees whom the director reasonably believes to be reliable and competent in the matters presented; legal counsel, public accountants, or other persons as to matters the director reasonably believes are within the person's professional or expert competence; and a committee of the board of directors of which the director is not a member if the director reasonably believes the committee merits confidence).
3. A director is not acting in good faith if the director has knowledge concerning the matter in question that makes reliance on information, opinions, reports, or statements unwarranted.
4. A director is not liable for any action taken as a director, or any failure to take any action, if the director performed the duties of the office in compliance with this section.

This provision is the statutory anchor of the Washington director duty framework. It articulates the three components of director duty (good faith, care, best interests), permits reliance on information from specified sources, and provides a safe harbor for directors who meet the statutory standards.

### F.6 RCW 23B.08.310 — Prohibited Actions

RCW 23B.08.310 identifies certain categories of director conduct that are prohibited and that fall outside the protection of the business judgment rule and the exculpation provisions. The key features of the provision are:

1. A director is not liable to the corporation, any shareholder, or any other person for any action taken or not taken as a director, unless the party asserting liability proves that specified conditions are met.
2. The conditions typically include: the challenged conduct was not in good faith, or was not in a manner the director reasonably believed to be in the corporation's best interests, or constituted a knowing violation of law, or constituted intentional misconduct, or involved a transaction from which the director derived an improper personal benefit.
3. A director is not liable if the director performed the duties of office in compliance with RCW 23B.08.300.

This provision works in tandem with RCW 23B.08.300 and RCW 23B.08.320 to establish the framework for director liability in Washington. A director who discharges duties in compliance with RCW 23B.08.300 is not liable; a director who falls outside the safe harbor of RCW 23B.08.300 may still be protected by the exculpation provision of RCW 23B.08.320, unless the conduct falls within one of the carve-outs listed in RCW 23B.08.310.

### F.7 RCW 23B.08.320 — Director Exculpation

RCW 23B.08.320 permits articles of incorporation to limit or eliminate director personal liability for monetary damages for conduct as a director. The key features of the provision are:

1. The articles of incorporation may contain a provision eliminating or limiting the personal liability of a director to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director.
2. The provision does not eliminate or limit liability for: acts or omissions that involve intentional misconduct or a knowing violation of law; conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310; or any transaction from which the director will personally receive a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director is not legally entitled.
3. The provision applies only to conduct occurring after the effective date of the articles of incorporation provision.
4. The provision does not affect the availability of non-monetary remedies (such as injunctive relief or specific performance).

RCW 23B.08.320 is the Washington equivalent of Delaware's DGCL § 102(b)(7), and it provides broad protection against monetary liability for ordinary duty-of-care claims while preserving liability for more serious forms of misconduct. Most Washington corporations of any significant size include an exculpation provision in their articles of incorporation, because the protection it provides is substantial and the cost of adopting it is minimal.

### F.8 RCW 23B.08.510 through 23B.08.600 — Indemnification

RCW 23B.08.510 through 23B.08.600 (with some sections numbered in the 500 range and some in the 600 range depending on the precise numbering) establish the framework for indemnification of directors, officers, employees, and agents. The key provisions include:

- **RCW 23B.08.510 — Permissive indemnification.** Permits a corporation to indemnify an individual who is a party to a proceeding because the individual is or was a director, against liability incurred in the proceeding, if the individual acted in good faith and reasonably believed the conduct was in the corporation's best interests (or, for conduct in an official capacity, not opposed to the corporation's best interests), and had no reasonable cause to believe the conduct was unlawful.
- **RCW 23B.08.520 — Mandatory indemnification.** Requires a corporation to indemnify a director who has been wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise, in the defense of any proceeding to which the director was a party because of being a director.
- **RCW 23B.08.530 — Advance for expenses.** Permits a corporation to advance the expenses incurred by a director in connection with a proceeding, subject to specified undertakings.
- **RCW 23B.08.540 — Court-ordered indemnification.** Permits a court to order indemnification where it determines that indemnification is fair and reasonable.
- **RCW 23B.08.550 — Determination and authorization.** Specifies the procedures by which a corporation determines whether indemnification is permissible and authorizes indemnification.
- **RCW 23B.08.560 — Indemnification of officers, employees, and agents.** Extends the indemnification provisions to officers, employees, and agents.
- **RCW 23B.08.570 — Insurance.** Permits a corporation to purchase and maintain insurance (D&O insurance) for the benefit of directors, officers, employees, and agents.
- **RCW 23B.08.603 — Amendment or repeal.** Addresses the effect of later amendments or repeals of indemnification provisions on rights that have already accrued.

These provisions collectively establish the statutory framework for indemnification in Washington corporations. Most corporations supplement the statutory provisions with contractual indemnification in bylaws, director and officer indemnification agreements, and D&O insurance, producing a layered protective regime.

### F.9 RCW 23B.08.735 — Business Opportunities

RCW 23B.08.735, as amended by the 2020 business opportunity disclaimer amendments, addresses the corporate opportunity doctrine and its possible waiver. The key features of the provision are:

1. Subject to the limitations of this section and any other applicable provisions of law, the articles of incorporation or any amendment thereto may limit or eliminate any duty of a director or any other person to offer the corporation the right to have or participate in any, or one or more classes or categories of, business opportunities, prior to the pursuit or taking of the opportunity by the director or other person.
2. Any limitation or elimination of duty is effective only if made with the approval of the corporation (typically through adoption in the articles of incorporation).
3. The limitation or elimination does not affect duties under other provisions of law, including duties arising from contractual relationships.
4. Specified categories of persons (including directors and other persons acting in a fiduciary capacity) may be covered.

This provision codifies the business opportunity disclaimer approach that has become common in venture capital and private equity practice, where directors frequently sit on multiple boards and the traditional corporate opportunity doctrine can create practical difficulties. By permitting corporations to waive the doctrine by charter provision, the 2020 amendments brought Washington into alignment with national best practice and provided Washington corporations with a useful tool.

### F.10 RCW 23B.10.030 — Amendment of Articles of Incorporation

RCW 23B.10.030, as amended by the 2024 amendments, sets forth the procedures by which a corporation's articles of incorporation may be amended by action of the board of directors and the shareholders. The key features of the provision are:

1. A corporation's board of directors may propose one or more amendments to the articles of incorporation for submission to the shareholders.
2. For the amendment to be adopted:
   - The board of directors must recommend the amendment to the shareholders (unless the board makes a determination that, because of conflict of interest or other special circumstances, it should make no recommendation, and communicates the basis for its determination to the shareholders).
   - The shareholders entitled to vote on the amendment must approve the amendment as provided in paragraph (3).
3. The shareholder approval requirement varies by date of formation:
   - For corporations formed before August 1, 2024: approval of the amendment requires the affirmative vote of two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment (with the exception that, for public companies, the vote required is a simple majority).
   - For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024: approval of the amendment requires the affirmative vote of a majority of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment.
4. In either case, the approval must also include the affirmative vote of the same threshold (two-thirds or majority, as applicable) of the votes entitled to be cast by each other voting group entitled to vote separately on the amendment.
5. The articles of incorporation may require a different vote than the default, provided that the required vote is not less than a majority of all the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment.

The 2024 amendment to this provision (2024 c 22 s 20) is the most recent significant recalibration of Washington corporate law, and it aligns Washington's default vote threshold for amendments with the national consensus (simple majority) while preserving the settled expectations of existing corporations through the date-of-formation cutoff.

### F.11 RCW 23B.13 — Dissenters' Rights (Chapter Overview)

Chapter 23B.13 sets forth the framework for dissenters' rights (also called appraisal rights) in Washington. The chapter is organized into multiple sections that collectively establish:

1. **The transactions that trigger appraisal rights.** These include mergers, share exchanges, sales of substantially all assets, certain amendments to the articles of incorporation that adversely affect shareholder rights, and a few other specified categories.
2. **The procedure for exercising appraisal rights.** A shareholder who wishes to exercise appraisal rights must deliver a written notice of intent to demand payment before the shareholder vote on the transaction, and must not vote in favor of the transaction. After the transaction is approved, the corporation must send a written notice to the dissenting shareholder of the procedures for demanding payment, and the dissenting shareholder must then make a formal demand for payment.
3. **The determination of fair value.** The corporation must pay the dissenting shareholder the amount that the corporation estimates to be the fair value of the shares, plus interest. If the dissenting shareholder disagrees with the corporation's estimate, the dissenting shareholder may demand the additional amount that the shareholder believes is due. If the corporation and the shareholder cannot agree on fair value, the matter is submitted to the court for resolution.
4. **The court proceeding.** The court conducts a fair value proceeding in which it hears expert testimony from valuation professionals retained by the parties and determines the fair value of the shares. The court's determination is binding on the parties.
5. **Costs and expenses.** The court has discretion to allocate costs and expenses of the appraisal proceeding between the parties, with the default being that the corporation bears its own costs and the dissenting shareholder bears its own costs, unless the court finds that one party's position was arbitrary, vexatious, or not in good faith.

Appraisal rights are an important protection for minority shareholders in fundamental corporate transactions, and chapter 23B.13 provides a reasonably comprehensive framework for their assertion and enforcement. In practice, appraisal rights are exercised relatively rarely, but when they are exercised, they can have significant consequences for both the corporation and the dissenting shareholder.

### F.12 RCW 23B.14 — Dissolution (Chapter Overview)

Chapter 23B.14 sets forth the framework for the dissolution of Washington corporations. The chapter is organized into sections addressing voluntary dissolution, administrative dissolution, and judicial dissolution.

**Voluntary dissolution** (RCW 23B.14.010 through 23B.14.080):

1. Dissolution by incorporators or initial directors.
2. Dissolution by board of directors and shareholders.
3. Articles of dissolution.
4. Revocation of dissolution.
5. Effect of dissolution.
6. Known claims against dissolved corporation.
7. Unknown claims against dissolved corporation.
8. Enforcement of claims against dissolved corporation.

**Administrative dissolution** (RCW 23B.14.200 through 23B.14.230):

1. Grounds for administrative dissolution (typically failure to file annual reports or pay fees).
2. Procedure for administrative dissolution.
3. Reinstatement following administrative dissolution.
4. Appeal from denial of reinstatement.

**Judicial dissolution** (RCW 23B.14.300 through 23B.14.340):

1. Grounds for judicial dissolution (typically shareholder deadlock, minority oppression, waste or misapplication of corporate assets).
2. Procedure for judicial dissolution.
3. Receivership or custodianship pending dissolution.
4. Decree of dissolution.

Chapter 23B.14 provides the comprehensive framework for ending a corporation's existence, whether voluntarily, administratively, or judicially. The provisions are detailed and procedure-specific, reflecting the importance of orderly dissolution for creditors, shareholders, and the public.

### F.13 Chapter 23B.19 — Close Corporations (Chapter Overview)

Chapter 23B.19 provides a special regime for corporations that elect close-corporation status. The chapter is relatively brief and narrowly used in practice, but it is available for corporations that want the informal governance style of a partnership while retaining corporate liability-shield and tax advantages.

The key features of the chapter include:

1. **Election of close-corporation status.** A corporation may elect close-corporation status by including appropriate provisions in its articles of incorporation.
2. **Elimination or restriction of the board of directors.** A close corporation may eliminate the board of directors or restrict its powers, with corporate powers exercised directly by the shareholders.
3. **Shareholder agreements.** Close corporations may use shareholder agreements that go beyond the default permitted by RCW 23B.07.320.
4. **Informal governance.** Close corporations may conduct their business with minimal formality, avoiding the formal meeting and recordkeeping requirements that apply to ordinary corporations.
5. **Provisions for deadlock and dissolution.** Close corporations may include provisions in their articles addressing deadlock situations and providing for dissolution in specified circumstances.
6. **Termination of close-corporation status.** A corporation may terminate its close-corporation status by amending its articles.

In practice, the chapter 23B.19 close-corporation regime has been used relatively infrequently. Most closely held Washington corporations operate as ordinary corporations and handle their close-corporation needs through shareholder agreements under RCW 23B.07.320. The chapter 23B.19 regime is available as an alternative, but its practical advantages over the shareholder agreement approach are limited, and many practitioners find the shareholder agreement approach more flexible.

### F.14 Chapter 23B.25 — Social Purpose Corporations (Chapter Overview)

Chapter 23B.25, added in 2012 and amended in 2014, provides the framework for the Washington Social Purpose Corporation. The key provisions include:

1. **Formation of social purpose corporations (RCW 23B.25.005).** A social purpose corporation is formed by filing articles of incorporation that include the required social purpose statement and that identify the corporation as a social purpose corporation.
2. **Social purpose.** The articles must state the corporation's social purpose, which may be broad or narrow, general or specific.
3. **Director duties.** Directors may (but are not required to) consider the corporation's social purpose and the interests of non-shareholder constituencies when making business decisions.
4. **Annual report.** The corporation must publish an annual report describing how it has advanced its social purpose.
5. **Conversion to and from social purpose corporation status.** A corporation may convert to social purpose corporation status by amending its articles, and may convert away from that status by a similar amendment.
6. **Shareholder remedies.** Shareholders of a social purpose corporation retain their ordinary corporate law remedies.
7. **Formation documents.** The Washington Secretary of State provides specific forms for the articles of incorporation of social purpose corporations, available through the Corporations and Charities Filing System.

Chapter 23B.25 is the distinctive Washington innovation in the benefit corporation space. It reflects the WSBA Business Law Section's judgment that a light-touch framework is preferable to a more prescriptive framework, and it gives Washington entrepreneurs a tool for formalizing social and environmental objectives alongside ordinary profit motives.

## Appendix H: Frequently Asked Questions

This appendix provides brief answers to frequently asked questions about Washington corporate law. The answers are educational and should not be relied upon as legal advice for specific situations.

### H.1 What is the difference between Title 23B RCW and Title 23A RCW?

Title 23A RCW was Washington's Business Corporation Act from 1967 to 1990, based on the 1950 edition of the Model Business Corporation Act. It was replaced by Title 23B RCW, the Washington Business Corporation Act, which was enacted in 1989 and took effect on July 1, 1990. Title 23A is no longer in force; references to "Title 23A" in current materials are typically historical.

### H.2 What is the difference between Title 23B RCW and Title 23 RCW?

Title 23 RCW is "Corporations and Associations (Profit)," which contains various provisions relating to corporations and business associations other than the Washington Business Corporation Act. Title 23B RCW is specifically the Washington Business Corporation Act. Most of the substantive provisions governing Washington business corporations are in Title 23B.

### H.3 What is the Model Business Corporation Act?

The Model Business Corporation Act (MBCA) is a model statute for state corporate laws, promulgated and periodically revised by the Corporate Laws Committee of the American Bar Association Business Law Section. Approximately 36 states have adopted the MBCA in substantial form. Washington's Title 23B is based on the 1984 Revised Model Business Corporation Act (RMBCA).

### H.4 Why doesn't Washington follow Delaware law?

Washington is a separate state with its own legislature and its own corporate law tradition. Washington has chosen to follow the MBCA tradition rather than the Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL) tradition. The two traditions differ in various respects, but both are coherent and workable approaches to corporate law. Washington-incorporated corporations are governed by Washington law; corporations that want to be governed by Delaware law incorporate in Delaware instead.

### H.5 What is a Social Purpose Corporation?

A Social Purpose Corporation (SPC) is a for-profit Washington corporation formed under chapter 23B.25 RCW that has adopted a social purpose in addition to ordinary profit motives. The SPC is similar in concept to benefit corporations in other states, but it is a lighter-touch framework with permissive rather than mandatory director duties, flexible reporting requirements, and reliance on ordinary corporate law remedies.

### H.6 Is a Washington SPC the same as a B Corporation?

No. The Washington SPC is a legal form under Washington state law, while "B Corporation" typically refers to a private certification offered by the nonprofit B Lab to any company that meets its standards. A Washington SPC may seek B Lab certification, but the two are independent: an SPC is not automatically B Lab-certified, and a B Lab-certified company need not be an SPC.

### H.7 What is the business judgment rule?

The business judgment rule is a judicial presumption that, in making a business decision, the directors of a corporation acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation. In Washington, the rule shields directors (and officers) from liability for good-faith business decisions, unless the presumption is rebutted by evidence of fraud, dishonesty, self-dealing, or other misconduct.

### H.8 What are director duties in Washington?

Directors of Washington corporations have three principal fiduciary duties: the duty of care (to exercise reasonable care in performing their duties), the duty of loyalty (to act in the best interests of the corporation and not for personal benefit), and the duty of good faith (to act honestly and without corrupt motive). The statutory framework is in RCW 23B.08.300 and related sections, and the framework is supplemented by case law.

### H.9 What is an exculpation provision?

An exculpation provision is a provision in a corporation's articles of incorporation that limits or eliminates director personal liability for certain breaches of duty. Under RCW 23B.08.320, Washington corporations may adopt exculpation provisions that cover breaches of the duty of care, but the exculpation does not reach breaches of the duty of loyalty, intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, or transactions producing improper personal benefit.

### H.10 What is indemnification?

Indemnification is the corporation's commitment to reimburse directors, officers, employees, and agents for liabilities and expenses incurred in their corporate capacities. Washington's statutory framework is in RCW 23B.08.510 and following. Corporations typically supplement the statutory provisions with contractual indemnification in bylaws and indemnification agreements, and with directors' and officers' (D&O) liability insurance.

### H.11 What is a shareholder agreement?

A shareholder agreement is a contract among the shareholders of a corporation (and sometimes the corporation itself) that addresses matters of corporate governance, transfer restrictions, dispute resolution, and other subjects. In Washington, shareholder agreements are authorized by RCW 23B.07.320, which permits such agreements to depart from the default governance rules of the Act.

### H.12 What are appraisal rights?

Appraisal rights (also called dissenters' rights) are the rights of shareholders who object to certain fundamental corporate transactions to demand that the corporation purchase their shares at fair value. In Washington, appraisal rights are governed by chapter 23B.13 RCW, and they apply to mergers, share exchanges, sales of substantially all assets, certain amendments to articles, and a few other specified transactions.

### H.13 What happened in the 2024 amendments?

The 2024 amendments (Laws of 2024, ch. 22) made various refinements to Title 23B. The most significant change was to RCW 23B.10.030, which governs amendments to the articles of incorporation. For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, the default shareholder vote threshold for amending the articles is now a simple majority (down from two-thirds for non-public companies). For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the prior default continues to apply.

### H.14 How do I form a Washington corporation?

To form a Washington corporation, you need to prepare articles of incorporation that comply with RCW 23B.02.020, file them with the Washington Secretary of State (typically through the Corporations and Charities Filing System), pay the filing fee, and complete the organizational steps (adopting bylaws, electing officers, issuing shares). Many Washington entrepreneurs use online legal platforms, filing services, or law firms to assist with the process. This paper is not a how-to guide and should not be relied upon for formation advice.

### H.15 Should I consult a lawyer?

Yes, for anything beyond the simplest corporate formation and governance. Washington corporate law is complex, and the consequences of mistakes can be significant. A qualified Washington corporate lawyer can help you navigate the statute, the case law, the federal securities laws (if applicable), and the broader legal environment. This paper is an educational introduction; it is not a substitute for competent legal counsel.

### The 2025 Revised Code of Washington

The **2025 Revised Code of Washington** incorporates all changes made
by session laws through the 2025 legislative session (which ended in
late April 2025). The relevant titles for this paper are:

- **Title 23B RCW — Washington Business Corporation Act** — the
  primary statute governing for-profit business corporations in
  Washington. The body of this paper treats 23B in depth.
- **Title 23 RCW — Corporations and Associations (Profit)** — the
  residual home for provisions that predate or sit alongside 23B,
  including the **Uniform Business Organizations Code (Chapter
  23.95)** which handles cross-entity registration, administrative
  dissolution, and reinstatement.
- **Chapter 23B.25 RCW — Social Purpose Corporations** — the SPC
  chapter, which this paper's `spc-framework.md` companion file
  covers in detail.

As of the 2025 RCW publication, the official statute text is
available through both the Washington State Legislature's own
code-lookup tool (`app.leg.wa.gov/rcw`) and through the Justia
mirror (`law.justia.com/codes/washington`). For practitioners the
Lexis *Washington Business Corporation Act (RCW 23B) Sourcebook* is
the current annotated reference.

### Social Purpose Corporation — current state

The SPC framework in Chapter 23B.25, which the companion
`spc-framework.md` file covers in detail, remains structurally
unchanged in the 2025 code. The core provisions that this paper's
treatment rests on are still active law:

- **RCW 23B.25.020** — the authorizing statute for SPCs, including
  the definition of permissible social purposes (employees,
  suppliers, customers; local/state/national/world community;
  environment) and the requirement that the articles of
  incorporation identify the corporation's specific social purposes.
- **RCW 23B.25.130** — the conversion mechanism, under which an
  existing corporation can become an SPC by board adoption and
  shareholder approval of a **plan of election**.
- **RCW 23B.25 amendment provisions** — material changes to the
  social purposes stated in an SPC's articles require approval by
  **two-thirds of the voting group** (in addition to standard
  amendment procedures) and **two-thirds of the holders of the
  outstanding shares of each class or series**. The heightened
  supermajority reflects the legislature's design decision that
  social-purpose changes should be harder to reverse than ordinary
  corporate amendments.

**Sources:** [Chapter 23B.25 RCW: Social Purpose Corporations — Washington State Legislature](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=23B.25&full=true) · [RCW 23B.25.130 — Corporation converting to a social purpose corporation—Plan of election—Effect — Justia 2025](https://law.justia.com/codes/washington/title-23b/chapter-23b-25/section-23b-25-130/) · [Washington's Social Purpose Corporation — Seattle University Journal of Environmental Law](https://digitalcommons.law.seattleu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1051&context=sjel)
