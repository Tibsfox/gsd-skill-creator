## Introduction and Scope

The Washington Business Corporation Act ("WBCA"), codified at Title 23B of the Revised Code of Washington, governs the formation, internal affairs, operation, and dissolution of for-profit corporations chartered under Washington law. Enacted originally by Laws of 1989, chapter 165, the WBCA closely tracks the Revised Model Business Corporation Act ("RMBCA" or "MBCA") promulgated by the American Bar Association's Committee on Corporate Laws, though Washington has added, subtracted, and tuned numerous provisions to reflect local practice, statutory history, and legislative choices about the balance between managerial authority, shareholder democracy, and judicial oversight.

This chapter covers three focused slices of the WBCA that are critical to anyone who wishes to understand how a Washington corporation is brought into existence and how its governance operates at the highest level:

1. **Formation and incorporation** under RCW 23B.02, which establishes who may form a corporation, what must appear in the articles of incorporation, what may permissibly be added, when corporate existence begins, how liability is allocated for persons who act before incorporation, and how the first meetings and bylaws are organized. This part also covers emergency bylaws and forum-selection provisions, two features that have been added or refined in successive legislative revisions.
2. **Shareholders** under RCW 23B.07, which addresses meetings (annual, special, and court-ordered), the mechanics of notice and record date, proxies, action by written consent, the voting machinery (quorum, voting groups, and thresholds), and the Washington shareholder agreement statute — the latter being one of the most powerful planning tools available for closely held Washington corporations.
3. **Directors and officers** under RCW 23B.08, which establishes the board as the organ through which corporate powers are exercised, sets the terms, elections, vacancies, and removal procedures for directors, specifies how boards meet and act (including committees, consent actions, and telephonic or video participation), and — perhaps most importantly — codifies the standards of conduct for directors, the related business judgment rule as developed by Washington case law, the conflict-of-interest safe harbors (including the 2020 business-opportunities provision), the officer framework, indemnification, and director resignation.

The chapter quotes or closely paraphrases the operative statutory language wherever precision matters, supplies practical commentary on how each provision operates in practice, and compares Washington to Delaware and the MBCA where the contrast is illuminating. It cites specific Washington cases that bear on the business judgment rule, and it flags the numerous cross-references that tie Title 23B to the Uniform Business Organizations Code at RCW 23.95.

A preliminary terminological note: throughout this chapter, "the Act" and "Title 23B" refer to the Washington Business Corporation Act. "RCW" refers to the Revised Code of Washington, the codified statutes of Washington State. The WBCA uses "corporation" to mean a for-profit business corporation incorporated under Title 23B. Nonprofits are governed by a separate Washington statute, the Washington Nonprofit Corporation Act, and are not covered here. Limited liability companies are governed by Title 25, and are likewise out of scope.

## Part 1: Formation and Incorporation (RCW 23B.02)

### 1. Who May Incorporate — RCW 23B.02.010

RCW 23B.02.010 is the gateway provision of Washington corporate formation. Its text is deceptively terse:

> "One or more persons may act as the incorporator or incorporators of a corporation by delivering articles of incorporation to the secretary of state for filing."

Although this sentence occupies a single line of statute, it encodes several important policy choices.

**"One or more persons."** At common law and under many older state statutes, incorporation required a minimum of three incorporators — a holdover from the nineteenth-century practice of treating corporations as quasi-public chartered entities that demanded a minimum quantum of civic participation. Washington's Business Corporation Act, following the RMBCA, jettisoned that requirement. Today a single natural person may incorporate a Washington corporation, and nothing in RCW 23B.02.010 prohibits that single incorporator from also being the corporation's sole initial director, sole officer, and sole shareholder. The consequence is that the WBCA fully accommodates the modern reality of the closely held corporation and the single-person professional corporation. Historical contrast: in the United States generally, the shift from "three or more" to "one or more" traces to the RMBCA's 1984 revision, adopted in Washington when the WBCA was enacted in 1989. A 2020 amendment preserved this formulation.

**"Persons."** Washington corporate law, like the MBCA, treats "person" broadly. RCW 23B.01.400 (definitions) defines "person" to include both natural individuals and entities (corporations, limited liability companies, partnerships, trusts, estates, and other legal persons). Consequently an incorporator need not be a living human — a holding company may incorporate a subsidiary, a trust may incorporate on behalf of its beneficiaries, and a limited liability company may cause the formation of a wholly-owned corporate subsidiary. This is the statutory foundation of corporate-group structures.

**No residency or citizenship requirement.** RCW 23B.02.010 imposes no requirement that the incorporator reside in Washington, be a United States citizen, or have any physical nexus with the state whatsoever. The only geographic hooks in the formation process are (a) the requirement of a Washington registered agent with a physical Washington street address, and (b) delivery of the articles to the Washington Secretary of State. A California resident, a Tokyo-based holding company, or an Estonian e-resident may be the sole incorporator of a Washington corporation. This matches the pattern in most American jurisdictions and reflects a long-standing policy in favor of mobility of capital and ease of formation.

**"By delivering articles of incorporation to the secretary of state for filing."** The act of incorporation is, as a legal matter, the act of delivery. The incorporator does not become an incorporator by intention, by contract, or by planning; the incorporator becomes an incorporator at the moment the articles are lodged with the Office of the Secretary of State. Delivery may be effected in paper form or, more commonly today, through the Secretary of State's Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS), which accepts online filings.

**Role of the incorporator.** The incorporator's role is narrow but not trivial. The incorporator signs and delivers the articles, and — if the articles do not name initial directors — the incorporator holds the organizational meeting at which initial directors are elected and the bylaws adopted. See RCW 23B.02.050, discussed below. Once the corporation is organized and directors are in place, the incorporator's role is exhausted; thereafter, authority resides with the board.

**Practical implication.** An incorporator should be chosen with two considerations in mind. First, the name and address of the incorporator appear in the articles and become public record; this is not the place for a person who values privacy. Second, the incorporator is legally on the hook for the accuracy of the statements in the articles and for the organizational meeting that follows. In closely held corporations the incorporator is almost always the founder or founders. In more complex formations (venture-capital-backed startups, holding-company spinoffs, professional-services corporations formed with the assistance of outside counsel), the incorporator is often the filing attorney or paralegal; this is legally permissible and procedurally convenient, but the incorporator remains liable under RCW 23B.02.040 if he or she acts purporting to represent a corporation that does not in fact exist.

**Historical note.** The original 1889 Washington territorial corporation statute required a minimum of three incorporators. The 1965 Business Corporation Act retained that requirement. Only with the 1989 Business Corporation Act — the WBCA as enacted by Laws of 1989, chapter 165, section 26 — did the "one or more" formulation enter Washington law.

**The incorporator as agent, not principal.** A helpful conceptual framing: the incorporator is best understood as a procedural agent, not a substantive principal. The incorporator's role is to deliver the articles and, if necessary, call the organizational meeting. Once the corporation is organized, the incorporator has no continuing rights or obligations by virtue of incorporator status. An incorporator who is also a shareholder or director continues in those capacities, but the incorporator qua incorporator has discharged the role upon organization. This distinguishes Washington from some older common-law systems in which incorporators had continuing fiduciary-like obligations as "promoters" — a category that Washington recognizes only in the narrow preincorporation-liability context of RCW 23B.02.040.

**A note on "attorney as incorporator."** A common pattern is for outside counsel to serve as incorporator while preparing formation documents for a client. This is permissible under RCW 23B.02.010, though it raises two practical considerations. First, the attorney-incorporator's name and address become public record and are discoverable through a simple Secretary of State search; this can associate the attorney's firm with the new entity in ways that may or may not be desired. Second, an attorney-incorporator who signs articles describing a corporation that the attorney has reason to believe will not actually be formed — e.g., because the client has not completed its share subscription or has not provided funding — could in principle face preincorporation-liability exposure under RCW 23B.02.040. In practice, attorneys manage this risk by ensuring that organizational steps are promptly completed and by maintaining engagement letters that clearly allocate responsibility for post-incorporation steps.

**Comparative snapshot — California, Delaware, Texas.** California Corporations Code § 200(a) likewise permits "one or more natural persons, partnerships, associations, or corporations, domestic or foreign" to form a corporation. Delaware GCL § 101(a) uses the phrasing "any person, partnership, association or corporation, singly or jointly with others, and without regard to his or their residence, domicile or state of incorporation, may incorporate or organize a corporation." Texas Business Organizations Code § 3.004 requires "one or more organizers" and uses similar broad language. Washington's "one or more persons" formulation is thus mainstream; the "one or more" minimum is the universal modern rule in U.S. corporate practice.

### 2. Articles of Incorporation — Mandatory Content — RCW 23B.02.020(1)

RCW 23B.02.020(1) establishes the minimum informational content that must appear in articles of incorporation filed with the Washington Secretary of State. The statute requires four items:

> "(1) The articles of incorporation must set forth:
>   (a) A corporate name for the corporation that satisfies the requirements of Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW;
>   (b) The number of shares the corporation is authorized to issue in accordance with RCW 23B.06.010 and 23B.06.020;
>   (c) The name and address of the corporation's initial registered agent designated in accordance with Article 4 of chapter 23.95 RCW; and
>   (d) The name and address of each incorporator in accordance with RCW 23B.02.010."

Each element has substantive content that rewards close examination.

#### 2(a). Corporate Name — Cross-Reference to RCW 23.95 Article 3

The corporate name must satisfy Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW — the Washington Uniform Business Organizations Code (UBOC), which supplies a common name/registered agent regime for all Washington business entities (corporations, LLCs, limited partnerships, limited liability partnerships, and others). The relevant provisions appear at RCW 23.95.300 through 23.95.345.

**The distinguishability standard.** A Washington corporate name must be "distinguishable on the records of the secretary of state" from the names of all other business entities and reserved names. RCW 23.95.305. "Distinguishable on the records" is a term of art: minor variations in spelling, punctuation, or corporate suffix (Inc., Corp., Ltd.) may or may not render two names distinguishable. The Secretary of State publishes guidelines. In general, a corporate name is distinguishable if an ordinary person examining the records would not confuse it with any existing registered name; merely swapping "Inc." for "Corp." or inserting an initial article ("The") is not sufficient.

**Required corporate identifier.** RCW 23.95.305(1)(a) requires a Washington corporation's name to contain one of the following words or abbreviations: "corporation," "incorporated," "company," "limited," or the abbreviations "Corp.," "Inc.," "Co.," or "Ltd." The purpose is to give notice to third parties that they are dealing with a limited-liability entity rather than a natural person or general partnership. The "Co." or "Company" formulations have historically been acceptable in Washington, though some practitioners avoid them because they do not unambiguously signal limited liability to unsophisticated counterparties.

**Prohibited and restricted words.** RCW 23.95.310 and related provisions prohibit corporate names that imply a purpose for which the corporation is not authorized (e.g., using "Bank," "Trust," or "Insurance" in a name without the corresponding regulatory authority), or that suggest a connection with a governmental agency. Professional designations (such as "Engineer," "Architect," "Physician") may trigger restrictions under the Washington Professional Service Corporation Act, RCW 18.100.

**Name reservation and registration.** RCW 23.95.320 permits reservation of a corporate name for a renewable 180-day period. This is useful when the articles are not quite ready but the founders want to lock in the chosen name.

**Practical implication.** The name selection process is effectively a two-step inquiry: first, run a search against the Secretary of State's online database; second, run a broader commercial-availability search (trademark, domain name, trade name) to ensure the name is not just legally available under Washington corporate law but commercially usable. The Secretary of State's distinguishability standard is narrow and should never be mistaken for trademark clearance.

#### 2(b). Authorized Shares — Cross-Reference to RCW 23B.06.010 and RCW 23B.06.020

The articles must specify "the number of shares the corporation is authorized to issue." RCW 23B.06.010 provides that the articles must "authorize (a) one or more classes or series of shares that together have unlimited voting rights, and (b) one or more classes or series of shares (which may be the same class or series as those with voting rights) that together are entitled to receive the net assets of the corporation upon dissolution." The effect is that every Washington corporation must have at least one class of voting common stock and at least one class of residual-claimant common stock — though these can, and in simple closely held corporations always do, collapse into a single class of common stock bearing both votes and residual rights.

RCW 23B.06.020 governs the creation of classes and series, the power to fix preferences and rights by board resolution (a "blank check" preferred stock authorization), and the minimum informational content required when multiple classes or series are created. The articles must set forth for each class or series: the designation, the number of shares authorized, and the rights, preferences, and limitations — or, if authority to fix preferences and rights is delegated to the board, a statement of that delegation and the limits (if any) imposed on the board's discretion.

**Practical implication.** A minimal single-class share structure reads "The corporation is authorized to issue [number] shares of common stock." For venture-capital-ready startups, a typical structure reads "The corporation is authorized to issue [X] shares of common stock and [Y] shares of preferred stock, par value $0.0001 per share. The board of directors is authorized, subject to any limitations prescribed by law, to provide for the issuance of the preferred stock in one or more series, and to fix the number of shares, designations, powers, preferences, and relative, participating, optional, or other special rights, and the qualifications, limitations, or restrictions thereof, of each such series."

#### 2(c). Registered Agent — Cross-Reference to RCW 23.95 Article 4

The articles must name "the corporation's initial registered agent designated in accordance with Article 4 of chapter 23.95 RCW." Article 4 of UBOC appears at RCW 23.95.410 through 23.95.470.

**Who may serve.** Under RCW 23.95.415, a registered agent may be (a) a natural person who is a resident of Washington, or (b) a domestic or foreign entity that is authorized to do business in Washington and that has a physical street address (not a P.O. box) in Washington. Washington recognizes "commercial registered agent" filings — a commercial registered agent is an entity that has filed a listing with the Secretary of State identifying itself as professionally in the business of serving as registered agent for multiple principals. See RCW 23.95.425.

**Physical Washington address requirement.** The registered agent's listed address must be a physical Washington street address. A post office box is not sufficient. The rationale is that process must be capable of being personally served on the registered agent during normal business hours; a P.O. box affords no such opportunity.

**Consent.** Under RCW 23.95.420, a person or entity cannot be named as a registered agent without having consented to the designation. In practice, the commercial registered agent services provide a consent form as part of their onboarding process; for individual registered agents, consent is typically documented by the agent signing the initial report or a dedicated consent form.

**Practical implication.** Corporations that intend to operate from multiple states, or whose founders reside outside Washington, almost universally engage a commercial registered agent (CT Corporation, Cogency Global, Northwest Registered Agent, Washington Registered Agent LLC, and similar providers) rather than designate a natural person. The annual fee is modest (typically $75 to $200) and the operational benefit is substantial: service of process, official state notices, and annual report reminders are all routed through a professional intermediary, and the registered agent's address, rather than the founder's home address, appears in the public record.

#### 2(d). Incorporator Name and Address

Finally, the articles must set forth "the name and address of each incorporator." The WBCA does not require the incorporator to sign the articles in any particular form (though the Secretary of State's filing system prompts for a signature or electronic attestation). The incorporator's address must be a real address; a P.O. box is generally acceptable for the incorporator (unlike for the registered agent), though this varies in practice.

**Comparison to Delaware.** Delaware General Corporation Law § 102(a) requires the certificate of incorporation to state the name of the corporation, the address of its registered office in Delaware, the nature of the business or purposes, the total number of authorized shares, and the name and mailing address of each incorporator. The items overlap significantly with Washington's RCW 23B.02.020(1), but Delaware also requires a statement of "the nature of the business or purposes to be conducted or promoted" — though the statute explicitly permits the ubiquitous formulation "to engage in any lawful act or activity." Washington does not require a purpose statement at all in the articles; the purpose is implicit (and any-and-all lawful activity is assumed unless the articles opt into a narrower scope).

**Comparison to the MBCA.** Washington's mandatory content list tracks the RMBCA § 2.02(a) exactly, with the UBOC-specific cross-references for name and registered agent. This alignment is deliberate: the drafters of the 1989 WBCA were self-consciously adopting the MBCA template, and successive amendments have largely preserved that alignment. Practitioners familiar with MBCA jurisdictions (most U.S. states other than Delaware, California, New York, and a handful of others) will find Washington's formation requirements comfortably familiar.

**The "minimum viable articles."** In its minimum viable form, a set of Washington articles of incorporation can fit on a single page. A worked example — suitable for a solo-founder closely held corporation and stripped of all optional content — might read:

> **Articles of Incorporation of [NAME] Inc.**
>
> Pursuant to the provisions of Chapter 23B.02 of the Revised Code of Washington, the undersigned hereby forms a corporation under the following articles:
>
> **Article I — Name.** The name of the corporation is [NAME] Inc.
>
> **Article II — Authorized Shares.** The corporation is authorized to issue 10,000,000 shares of common stock.
>
> **Article III — Registered Agent.** The name of the corporation's initial registered agent is [Agent Name], and the address of the corporation's initial registered office is [physical Washington street address].
>
> **Article IV — Incorporator.** The name and address of the incorporator is [Incorporator Name], [Incorporator Address].
>
> Executed this [day] of [month], [year].

This bare template satisfies every mandatory element of RCW 23B.02.020(1) and would be accepted for filing by the Secretary of State. In practice, sophisticated filings add optional content (exculpation, indemnification, forum selection, corporate-opportunity waiver) and are typically five to fifteen pages in length, but the statutory minimum is strikingly minimal.

**Name reservation in practice.** The Washington Secretary of State charges a small fee for name reservation (currently $30 for a mail filing or $50 for expedited service) and reserves the name for 180 days. The reservation is non-renewable, though a new reservation can be filed if the original lapses. In competitive naming environments — where two founders may be racing to form entities with similar names — the reservation mechanism provides an important advance-lockout function. In ordinary practice, however, the availability of same-day online filing through CCFS has made name reservation less important: a founder who has a name cleared at 9:00 AM can file articles by 10:00 AM and secure the name by the time of filing acceptance.

**The "distinguishability" standard in the Washington case law.** Very little Washington case law applies RCW 23.95.305's distinguishability standard directly. The Secretary of State's internal practice is more influential than judicial precedent: the agency publishes administrative guidance on what constitutes distinguishability, and applicants whose proposed names are rejected by the agency generally revise rather than litigate. Where litigation does arise, it typically takes the form of a trade-mark infringement or unfair-competition action by the holder of a similar mark, not a direct challenge to the agency's distinguishability determination. The practical message is that a founder who clears the Secretary of State's distinguishability threshold should still conduct a separate trademark availability analysis if the name has branding value.

### 3. Articles of Incorporation — Optional Content — RCW 23B.02.020(2)

RCW 23B.02.020(2) enumerates what may (but need not) be included in the articles:

> "(2) The articles of incorporation may set forth:
>   (a) The names and addresses of the individuals who are to serve as initial directors;
>   (b) The par value of authorized shares or classes of shares;
>   (c) Provisions not inconsistent with law regarding:
>     (i) The purpose or purposes for which the corporation is organized;
>     (ii) Managing the business and regulating the affairs of the corporation;
>     (iii) Defining, limiting, and regulating the powers of the corporation, its board of directors, and shareholders;
>   (d) Any provision that under this title is required or permitted to be set forth in the bylaws;
>   (e) A provision limiting or eliminating the personal liability of a director to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director (see RCW 23B.08.320);
>   (f) A provision permitted by RCW 23B.08.560 obligating the corporation to indemnify a director or advance expenses;
>   (g) A provision permitted by RCW 23B.08.735(1)(b) limiting or eliminating the duty of directors or officers to offer business opportunities to the corporation."

(The statute also provides that RCW 23B.02.080 forum-selection provisions may be included.)

Each optional item deserves commentary.

#### 3(a). Initial Directors

Naming initial directors in the articles eliminates the need for the incorporator to hold an organizational meeting: the initial directors can proceed directly to their own organizational meeting (RCW 23B.02.050(1)(a)). In simple closely held corporations this is a common optimization. In complex or attorney-assisted filings where the identity of directors has not yet been finalized, the articles often omit initial directors, leaving that step to the incorporator's organizational meeting.

#### 3(b). Par Value

Par value is a vestigial concept from nineteenth-century corporate finance. Originally, par value represented the minimum price at which shares could be issued and established a floor for creditor protection (shares issued at less than par were said to be "watered stock," and holders could be pursued for the difference). Modern corporate finance has almost entirely moved away from meaningful par value. Most Washington corporations either designate a nominal par value ($0.001 or $0.0001 per share) or declare "no par value." Par value still matters in three contexts:

1. **Franchise tax computations** in certain jurisdictions (notably Delaware, though not Washington — Washington uses a license fee and initial filing fee tied to filings, not to par value).
2. **Stated capital and capital surplus** under RCW 23B.06, which are accounting distinctions that matter for distribution calculations.
3. **Foreign qualification fees** in certain jurisdictions that impose fees by reference to par value.

Washington practitioners typically declare a nominal par value for venture-ready corporations (to preserve maximum flexibility for future foreign qualification) and no par value for purely in-state closely held corporations.

#### 3(c). Purpose and Powers Limitations

The articles may state a purpose or scope narrower than "any lawful business or activity." This is uncommon. Narrowing the purpose can expose the corporation to ultra vires challenges if it engages in activities outside its stated purpose, and it serves no apparent benefit unless the narrower purpose is contractually mandated (e.g., a joint venture agreement requiring a special-purpose entity) or regulatorily required (e.g., professional corporations limited to the practice of a single profession).

#### 3(d). Bylaw-Equivalent Provisions

The articles may "contain any provision that under this title is required or permitted to be set forth in the bylaws." This is the hook by which "supermajority" provisions, staggered boards, director qualification requirements, voting trust restrictions, and similar governance features can be entrenched. The reason to put such provisions in the articles rather than in the bylaws is that the articles can be amended only with shareholder approval (RCW 23B.10), whereas the bylaws can ordinarily be amended by the board alone (RCW 23B.10.200). A provision entrenched in the articles therefore requires a higher hurdle to change.

#### 3(e). Director Liability Limitation — Cross-Reference to RCW 23B.08.320

RCW 23B.08.320 authorizes the articles to "eliminate or limit the personal liability of a director to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director." This is the Washington analogue to Delaware's celebrated § 102(b)(7) exculpation provision, adopted in the wake of *Smith v. Van Gorkom* (Del. 1985). The exculpation is powerful but carries four statutory exceptions, discussed in detail at Section 28 below.

#### 3(f). Indemnification Provisions — Cross-Reference to RCW 23B.08.560

RCW 23B.08.560 authorizes articles or bylaws to obligate the corporation to indemnify directors and advance their expenses, subject to certain exceptions. The default statutory indemnification scheme (RCW 23B.08.510 through .550) is largely permissive; most Washington corporations opt into mandatory indemnification and expense advancement through their articles or bylaws. See Section 31.

#### 3(g). Business Opportunity Disclaimer — 2020 Addition — RCW 23B.08.735(1)(b)

In 2020, the Washington Legislature adopted Chapter 194 of the Laws of 2020, amending RCW 23B.08.735 to permit the articles of incorporation to limit or eliminate the duty of directors and officers to offer business opportunities to the corporation. This addition brought Washington into alignment with Delaware GCL § 122(17), which has long permitted the Delaware analogue. The provision is particularly important for corporations with directors or officers who have portfolio relationships (venture capitalists sitting on multiple boards, private equity professionals with overlapping investments, family-office executives with parallel business interests) because without an express disclaimer such individuals can be constructively required to offer every plausibly-within-scope opportunity to the corporation first, on pain of corporate-opportunity doctrine liability.

**Practical implication.** A corporate-opportunity disclaimer is a standard provision in the articles of VC-backed Washington corporations formed after 2020. A typical formulation reads: "To the fullest extent permitted by RCW 23B.08.735(1)(b), the corporation hereby renounces any interest or expectancy of the corporation in, or in being offered an opportunity to participate in, any business opportunities that are presented to the corporation or any of its directors, officers, or stockholders (other than officers solely in their capacity as such)."

#### 3(h). Forum Selection — 2017 Addition — RCW 23B.02.080

Added by Laws of 2017 chapter 28, section 9, RCW 23B.02.080 permits forum-selection provisions in the articles or bylaws. See Section 9 below for detailed treatment.

### 4. Effect of Filing — RCW 23B.02.030

RCW 23B.02.030 establishes when corporate existence begins and the evidentiary weight of the Secretary of State's filing:

> "(1) Unless a delayed effective date is specified, the corporate existence begins when the articles of incorporation are filed.
> (2) The secretary of state's filing of the articles of incorporation is conclusive proof that the incorporators satisfied all conditions precedent to the incorporation except in a proceeding by the state to cancel or revoke the incorporation or involuntarily to dissolve the corporation."

**The bright line of corporate existence.** Section (1) adopts a bright-line rule: corporate existence begins at the moment the articles are filed. This is, in an important sense, a legal fiction — the corporation has no assets, no bylaws, no board, and no bank account at the moment of filing — but it matters enormously because it establishes the earliest point at which the corporation can (a) enter contracts, (b) be sued, (c) hold property, and (d) afford limited liability to its promoters. Before the filing moment, none of these legal consequences attaches, and the preincorporation liability regime of RCW 23B.02.040 governs.

**Delayed effective date.** The statute permits the articles to specify a delayed effective date — a mechanism borrowed from the MBCA that allows founders to file articles in advance but delay their effect until a date certain. RCW 23B.01.230 (the general filing-effective-date provision) limits the delayed effective date to not more than 90 days after the filing date. This is useful in end-of-year transactions (e.g., filing in late December but effective January 1 to simplify tax accounting), in transaction closings (e.g., filing a newly-formed merger subsidiary in advance but effective on the closing date), and in scheduled reorganizations.

**Conclusive proof.** Section (2) establishes that the Secretary of State's filing is "conclusive proof" that all conditions precedent to incorporation were satisfied. This is a profoundly important evidentiary provision. It means that once the articles are on file, no private litigant can challenge the validity of the incorporation on the grounds that, for example, the incorporator failed to sign properly, or the articles omitted a required item, or the fees were underpaid. Only the State itself — through a proceeding by the attorney general to cancel, revoke, or involuntarily dissolve — can challenge the corporate existence. This "conclusive presumption" serves two goals: it protects good-faith third parties who rely on the existence of the corporation, and it eliminates the technical defenses that would otherwise clutter corporate litigation.

**Comparison to the common-law "de facto corporation" doctrine.** Before modern corporation statutes, the common law recognized two doctrines to protect persons who had attempted in good faith to incorporate but had fallen short of some formal requirement: the "de facto corporation" doctrine (protecting the corporation against direct challenge by private parties if there was a valid statute, a good faith attempt to comply, and actual exercise of corporate powers) and the "corporation by estoppel" doctrine (protecting third parties from denying corporate existence when they had dealt with the entity as a corporation). Under modern statutes, the first of these doctrines is largely displaced by the "conclusive proof" rule of RCW 23B.02.030(2), which achieves the same protective effect through a statutory rather than common-law mechanism. Washington courts continue to apply the corporation-by-estoppel doctrine in some circumstances, but the need for it has largely evaporated now that incorporation is cheap, fast, and documentarily minimal.

**Practical implication.** The filing moment is the moment of birth. Founders should not sign contracts "on behalf of Newco, Inc." until the filing is complete and the effective date has arrived; any earlier activity falls under RCW 23B.02.040. In practice, sophisticated founders instruct counsel to confirm the filing before any preincorporation commitments are documented as entity commitments.

### 5. Preincorporation Liability — RCW 23B.02.040

RCW 23B.02.040 is the statutory complement to RCW 23B.02.030. It governs the liability of persons who act as if they were representing a corporation that does not yet exist:

> "All persons purporting to act as or on behalf of a corporation, knowing there was no incorporation under this title, are jointly and severally liable for liabilities created while so acting except for any liability to any person who also knew that there was no incorporation."

Three features of the provision repay close reading.

**The scienter requirement — "knowing there was no incorporation."** Liability attaches only to persons who act with knowledge that no incorporation has occurred. A promoter who genuinely (even if unreasonably) believed that incorporation had been completed is not liable under the statute. This is a significant departure from the older common-law rule, which in some formulations imposed strict liability on promoters who acted for a nonexistent corporation. The MBCA and Washington have moved toward a knowledge standard, which accords better with modern intuitions about fault and fairness.

**Joint and several liability.** All persons who act on behalf of a nonexistent corporation are jointly and severally liable. This means that a creditor can proceed against any one of them for the full amount of the obligation, leaving that person to seek contribution from the others. The joint-and-several structure is a powerful incentive to ensure that incorporation is completed before any business activity begins.

**Exclusion for insiders who knew.** The statute excludes from recovery "any person who also knew that there was no incorporation." This is the "Frick rule" (from *Robertson v. Levy*, 197 A.2d 443 (D.C. 1964), and similar authorities): a counterparty who knew that the corporation did not exist has no equitable claim to recover from the acting promoter, because that counterparty took the risk with open eyes. The exclusion applies only to the person who shared the knowledge; other counterparties who were ignorant of the nonexistence can still recover.

**Status of the de facto corporation doctrine in Washington.** Washington courts have not formally abolished the de facto corporation doctrine, but its practical relevance is near zero given (a) the ease of modern incorporation, (b) the conclusive-proof rule of RCW 23B.02.030(2), and (c) the scienter-limited scope of RCW 23B.02.040. The de facto doctrine arose in an era when incorporation could fail for any of dozens of technical reasons; today, if you have filed articles and received acceptance, you are incorporated, and the statute is the exclusive source of promoter liability.

**Practical implication.** A founder who signs a lease, an employment agreement, or a purchase order "on behalf of Newco, Inc." before the articles have been accepted for filing is personally liable on that contract unless (a) the counterparty knew the corporation did not exist, or (b) the contract is later ratified by the corporation after its formation and the counterparty agrees to a novation. Ratification alone does not release the promoter from liability — novation (a three-party agreement in which the counterparty consents to substitute the corporation for the promoter as the contracting party) is required.

### 6. Organizational Meeting — RCW 23B.02.050

RCW 23B.02.050 supplies the post-filing organizational mechanics:

> "(1)(a) If initial directors are named in the articles of incorporation, the initial directors shall hold an organizational meeting, at the call of a majority of the directors, to complete the organization of the corporation by appointing officers, adopting bylaws, and carrying on any other business brought before the meeting;
> (1)(b) If initial directors are not named in the articles, the incorporator or incorporators shall hold an organizational meeting at the call of a majority of the incorporators to elect directors and complete the organization of the corporation, or to elect a board of directors who shall complete the organization of the corporation.
> (2) Corporate action required or permitted by this title to be taken by incorporators at an organizational meeting may be taken without a meeting if the corporate action taken is evidenced by one or more written consents describing the corporate action taken and executed by each incorporator.
> (3) An organizational meeting may be held in or out of this state.
> (4) A corporation must file an initial report as provided in RCW 23.95.255."

**Two paths.** The organizational step depends on whether initial directors were named in the articles. If they were (the "directors-in-articles" path), the initial directors go directly to their organizational meeting. If not (the "incorporator meeting" path), the incorporator holds the meeting to elect directors and either complete the organization there or defer it to a subsequent board meeting.

**Written consent alternative.** Section (2) — mirrored for director actions in RCW 23B.08.210 — permits the organizational action to be taken by unanimous written consent of the incorporators in lieu of an actual meeting. In practice, modern Washington formations almost always proceed by written consent: the founders execute a single "Action by Written Consent of Incorporator" (or "Action by Written Consent of Initial Directors") that adopts the bylaws, elects the officers, authorizes the issuance of founder shares, ratifies any preincorporation contracts, and designates an accounting firm. This approach is efficient, documentarily clean, and — perhaps most importantly — can be executed entirely remotely.

**In or out of state.** The meeting may be held anywhere. This is purely a permissive provision with no substantive content; it is included to forestall any argument that organizational meetings must take place within the state of incorporation.

**Initial report filing.** Section (4) imposes an independent duty — the filing of the "initial report" under RCW 23.95.255 (the UBOC initial-report provision). The initial report is a separate filing from the articles and is due within 120 days of incorporation. It requires the corporation to identify its officers and directors, its principal office, and (if different) its email address. The initial report is filed online through the Secretary of State's CCFS system. Failure to file triggers administrative dissolution after further delay.

**Practical implication.** The organizational step is not optional, but it is rarely onerous. The standard "incorporator written consent" runs three to eight pages and addresses: adoption of bylaws, election of directors, designation of officers, authorization of share issuances, authorization of bank account opening, ratification of preincorporation activities, adoption of stock certificate form, fixing of fiscal year, adoption of indemnification agreements (if any), and adoption of a § 83(b) election reminder for founders receiving restricted stock.

### 7. Bylaws — RCW 23B.02.060

RCW 23B.02.060 governs the initial adoption and permissible scope of corporate bylaws:

> "(1) The incorporators or board of directors of a corporation must adopt initial bylaws for the corporation.
> (2) The bylaws of a corporation may contain any provision for managing the business and regulating the affairs of the corporation to the extent the provision does not infringe upon or limit the exclusive authority of the board of directors under RCW 23B.08.010(2)(b) or otherwise conflict with this title or any other law, the articles of incorporation, or a shareholders' agreement authorized by RCW 23B.07.320."

Three points bear emphasis.

**Mandatory adoption.** Section (1) requires the corporation to have initial bylaws — adopted either by the incorporators (as part of the organizational meeting or written consent) or by the board of directors. A Washington corporation cannot lawfully operate without bylaws. The statute does not dictate any particular content; the content is entirely at the discretion of the adopting body, subject only to the limitations of section (2).

**The permissible scope of bylaws.** Section (2) is an important provision for governance design. It establishes four constraints on what can appear in bylaws:

1. Bylaws cannot "infringe upon or limit the exclusive authority of the board of directors under RCW 23B.08.010(2)(b)." This is a reference to the board's reserved power over "substantive decisions concerning management of the corporation's business." Bylaws cannot, for example, transfer fundamental management decisions from the board to the shareholders (other than through the RCW 23B.07.320 shareholder-agreement mechanism).
2. Bylaws cannot conflict with "this title" (i.e., Title 23B RCW). For example, bylaws cannot reduce the director standard of care below that established by RCW 23B.08.300.
3. Bylaws cannot conflict with "any other law" — securities laws, tax laws, employment laws, professional licensing laws.
4. Bylaws cannot conflict with the articles of incorporation or with a shareholder agreement. This establishes the conflict hierarchy: articles > bylaws > board resolutions.

**The conflict hierarchy.** The WBCA implies (and Washington case law confirms) a strict conflict hierarchy among the three tiers of corporate governance documents:

- **Articles of incorporation** are the highest and most durable tier. They can be amended only with shareholder approval (RCW 23B.10.030).
- **Bylaws** are the middle tier. They can generally be amended by the board (unless the articles reserve that power to the shareholders) (RCW 23B.10.200).
- **Board resolutions** are the bottom tier. They implement the articles and bylaws but cannot override them.

If a bylaw and the articles conflict, the articles govern. If a board resolution and a bylaw conflict, the bylaw governs. If a shareholder agreement (under RCW 23B.07.320) and the articles conflict, the shareholder agreement governs only to the extent authorized by RCW 23B.07.320 — which is a broad but not unlimited authorization.

**Practical implication.** For closely held corporations with founding shareholders who want to freeze governance in place, the key question is: where should the governance rule live? Rules placed in the articles are maximally durable but maximally inflexible. Rules placed in the bylaws can be changed by the board, which may be either a feature or a bug depending on whether the founders trust the board. Rules placed in a shareholder agreement under RCW 23B.07.320 are maximally flexible and can entrench decisions that neither the articles nor the bylaws could entrench. A sophisticated founding structure typically uses all three documents in concert.

### 8. Emergency Bylaws — RCW 23B.02.070

RCW 23B.02.070 permits corporations to adopt bylaws that take effect only in an emergency:

> "(1) Unless the articles of incorporation provide otherwise, the board of directors of a corporation may adopt bylaws to be effective only in an emergency [as defined in subsection (4)].
> (2) All provisions of the regular bylaws consistent with the emergency bylaws remain effective during the emergency. The emergency bylaws are not effective after the emergency ends.
> (3) Corporate action taken in good faith in accordance with the emergency bylaws: (a) binds the corporation; and (b) may not be used to impose liability on a corporate director, officer, employee, or agent.
> (4) An emergency exists for purposes of this section if a quorum of the corporation's directors cannot readily be assembled because of some catastrophic event."

**Activation trigger.** Emergency bylaws take effect only when "a quorum of the corporation's directors cannot readily be assembled because of some catastrophic event." The provision was drafted in the Cold War era to address scenarios in which a nuclear strike, natural disaster, or comparable calamity might physically eliminate or incapacitate a majority of the board. Its modern relevance expanded substantially during the COVID-19 pandemic, when many corporate boards discovered that "catastrophic event" encompasses public-health emergencies that render physical assembly impossible and, in some cases, render even electronic assembly unreliable.

**Scope of emergency bylaws.** The statute does not enumerate the permissible content of emergency bylaws, but the common elements include:

- **Reduced quorum requirements** — e.g., two directors rather than the usual majority.
- **Substitute director provisions** — permitting designated officers, senior employees, or (in some formulations) former directors to serve as substitute directors until the emergency ends.
- **Modified notice requirements** — permitting meetings to be called on short notice through any reasonable means.
- **Reduced committee requirements** — permitting any available director to exercise committee powers.
- **Succession of officers** — designating automatic successors to the CEO, CFO, and other key officers.

**Good-faith shield.** Section (3)(b) provides that corporate action taken in good faith pursuant to emergency bylaws "may not be used to impose liability on a corporate director, officer, employee, or agent." This is a powerful insulation provision — it protects good-faith actors from hindsight second-guessing when the emergency has passed and the conventional governance machinery resumes.

**Interaction with director succession.** Emergency bylaws typically provide that when the regular director slots cannot be filled because of the emergency, a specified roster of substitute directors fills in. These substitutes are not directors in the RCW 23B.08 sense during normal operations, but during the emergency they exercise full board authority. When the emergency ends, the substitutes' tenure ends, and the ordinary director-election machinery resumes (though the board may need to fill any vacancies under RCW 23B.08.100).

**Practical implication.** Emergency bylaws are underused. Many Washington corporations do not have them, and in ordinary times their absence has no consequence. But the 2020 pandemic demonstrated that even sophisticated corporate governance machinery can be strained by unforeseen emergencies, and the marginal cost of drafting emergency bylaws is low. A typical emergency-bylaw package occupies two or three pages and sits in a file until needed.

### 9. Forum Selection — RCW 23B.02.080

RCW 23B.02.080 was added to Title 23B by Laws of 2017, chapter 28, section 9. It authorizes the corporation to specify a forum for internal corporate disputes:

> "(1) The articles of incorporation or bylaws may require, consistent with applicable jurisdictional requirements, that any or all internal corporate proceedings shall be commenced and maintained exclusively in any specified court or courts of this state or, if so specified, any additional courts in this state or in any other jurisdictions with which the corporation has a reasonable relationship.
> (2) A provision permitted under subsection (1) of this section: (a) May not confer jurisdiction on any court, over any person, or of any proceeding; and (b) May not (i) prohibit commencing or maintaining an internal corporate proceeding in the courts of this state or (ii) require claims asserted in an internal corporate proceeding to be determined by arbitration."

**Permissible forum selections.** The statute authorizes the articles or bylaws to specify (a) a particular Washington court or set of Washington courts, (b) additional Washington courts, or (c) courts in other jurisdictions "with which the corporation has a reasonable relationship." A corporation cannot specify an arbitrary foreign forum (e.g., a corporation with no Texas contacts cannot specify Texas state court as the exclusive forum), but it can specify Delaware Chancery Court if the corporation has substantive Delaware contacts (e.g., a Delaware subsidiary) or can specify federal courts sitting in Washington for federal securities claims arising from Washington operations.

**Three structural prohibitions.** Section (2) imposes three absolute limits:

1. **Cannot confer jurisdiction.** A forum-selection provision is a contractual and procedural device, not a jurisdictional-grant mechanism. If the specified court would not otherwise have jurisdiction over the person or the proceeding, the forum-selection provision cannot create that jurisdiction. This avoids conflicts with constitutional and statutory jurisdictional rules.
2. **Cannot prohibit Washington proceedings.** This is a significant limitation. A Washington corporation cannot, through a forum-selection provision, oust Washington courts of jurisdiction over internal corporate disputes involving a Washington-incorporated entity. By contrast, Delaware permits forum-selection provisions that exclusively channel internal disputes to Delaware Chancery (see *Boilermakers Local 154 Ret. Fund v. Chevron Corp.*, 73 A.3d 934 (Del. Ch. 2013), and the subsequent Delaware GCL § 115). Washington's rule is more plaintiff-friendly: a Washington shareholder can always bring internal claims in Washington courts, even if the articles or bylaws specify another forum as "exclusive."
3. **Cannot mandate arbitration of internal disputes.** This is the most significant limitation. Washington — unlike a number of jurisdictions that have allowed mandatory arbitration of intracorporate disputes — prohibits a Washington corporation from using its articles or bylaws to force internal corporate proceedings into arbitration. The rationale is that internal corporate disputes often implicate the public interest (minority-shareholder protection, fiduciary-duty enforcement, derivative actions for corporate wrongs) and should not be diverted to private adjudication.

**"Internal corporate proceedings."** The statute defines this term by reference to claims based on violations of duty by directors, officers, or shareholders; derivative actions; claims under the WBCA or the corporation's governing documents; and other claims concerning internal corporate affairs. External claims — contract disputes with third parties, employment claims by non-insiders, tort claims by third parties — are not governed by this provision and are subject to the ordinary jurisdictional and venue rules.

**Comparison to Delaware.** Delaware GCL § 115 authorizes forum-selection provisions and explicitly permits Delaware-incorporated corporations to designate Delaware Chancery Court as the exclusive forum for internal corporate disputes. Delaware's rule is broader than Washington's in two respects: (a) Delaware permits the bylaws to channel ALL internal disputes to Chancery Court, excluding even Delaware Superior Court, and (b) Delaware allows federal-court forum-selection provisions for federal securities claims (*Salzberg v. Sciabacucchi*, 227 A.3d 102 (Del. 2020)). Washington permits Washington-court channeling but never permits ouster of Washington courts' jurisdiction over internal disputes.

**Practical implication.** A Washington corporation with aspirations to venture-capital financing and an eventual IPO often grapples with whether to reincorporate in Delaware. One of the considerations is forum selection: Delaware offers the specialized expertise of the Chancery Court and the ability to channel all internal disputes to a single forum with extensive corporate-law jurisprudence. Washington offers the right to litigate in Washington state courts but does not offer the Chancery-Court specialization. For most closely held Washington corporations the difference is academic; for venture-backed corporations at an inflection point, it is material.

## Part 2: Shareholders (RCW 23B.07)

### 10. Annual Meeting — RCW 23B.07.010

RCW 23B.07.010 establishes the annual shareholder meeting requirement:

> "(1) A corporation shall hold a meeting of shareholders annually for the election of directors at a time stated in or fixed in accordance with the bylaws. [Exception for investment companies.] Shareholders may elect directors by written consent as permitted by RCW 23B.07.040 in lieu of holding an annual meeting.
> (2) Annual shareholder meetings may be held in or out of this state at the place stated in or fixed in accordance with the bylaws. If no place is stated in or fixed in accordance with the bylaws, annual meetings shall be held at the corporation's principal office. [Board may determine that meetings be held solely by means of remote communication.]
> (3) The failure to hold an annual meeting at the time stated in or fixed in accordance with a corporation's bylaws does not affect the validity of any corporate action."

**Timing.** The bylaws set the annual meeting date (or the procedure for fixing it). Most Washington corporations adopt a formulation like "the annual meeting shall be held in [month] on a date to be determined by the board." This gives the board flexibility to set the meeting date based on the timing of the audit, the fiscal year end, and the availability of key personnel.

**Who calls the meeting.** The statute does not specify who calls the annual meeting, but by default the board (typically through the secretary, on board direction) issues the notice and calls the meeting.

**Location flexibility.** Section (2) permits the meeting to be held in or out of Washington. Section (2) also authorizes "meetings held solely by means of remote communication" — the virtual-meeting authorization that has become standard practice for public companies and many closely held corporations since 2020.

**Written consent in lieu of meeting.** Section (1) explicitly permits shareholders to elect directors by written consent under RCW 23B.07.040, rather than by holding an annual meeting. For closely held corporations with unanimous shareholder cooperation, the annual written consent is a convenient alternative to the ceremony of an annual meeting.

**Validity of corporate action despite failure to hold annual meeting.** Section (3) is an important curative provision: failure to hold the annual meeting does not invalidate any corporate action. This protects closely held corporations that go through periods of neglect, and it eliminates a category of technical defenses that might otherwise cloud corporate title. (A shareholder can, however, bring a court-ordered meeting under RCW 23B.07.030 if the annual meeting is chronically skipped.)

### 11. Special Meetings — RCW 23B.07.020

RCW 23B.07.020 governs special (non-annual) shareholder meetings:

> "(1) A corporation shall hold a special meeting of shareholders: (a) On call of its board of directors or the person or persons authorized to do so by the articles of incorporation or bylaws; or (b) [if not a public company] if the holders of at least ten percent of all the votes entitled to be cast on any issue proposed to be considered at the proposed special meeting sign, date, and deliver to the corporation's secretary one or more written demands for the meeting describing the purpose or purposes for which it is to be held."

(Additional provisions address public companies, the possibility of raising the threshold up to 25 percent by article or bylaw provision, and the 60-day lookback for aggregating demands.)

**Who can call.** Special meetings can be called by the board, by the person(s) authorized in the articles or bylaws (typically the president or a specified officer), or by shareholders holding at least 10 percent of the votes entitled to be cast.

**The 10 percent threshold.** Shareholders holding 10 percent can demand a special meeting. This is a meaningful minority-shareholder right: it allows a dissatisfied but sub-majority bloc to force the corporation to convene the shareholders and consider a proposal. The articles can raise the threshold up to 25 percent (for private corporations) or eliminate it entirely (for public companies). The articles cannot lower the threshold below 10 percent for private corporations.

**The 60-day rule.** Written demands are aggregated for a rolling 60-day period. If multiple shareholders deliver demands over a span of weeks, they are combined for the threshold calculation as long as all demands fall within 60 days of the earliest demand.

**Public-company modification.** For public companies, the shareholder-demand right can be "limited or denied to the extent provided in the articles of incorporation." This reflects the reality that in public-company contexts the shareholder-meeting machinery is expensive and can be weaponized by activist shareholders; public-company articles commonly eliminate the shareholder special-meeting right entirely.

**Practical implication.** In closely held corporations the 10 percent threshold rarely becomes an issue because the founding shareholders typically cooperate with board-driven meeting procedures. In venture-backed corporations, the preferred-stock holders sometimes have contractual rights to call special meetings through shareholder-agreement provisions that layer on top of the statutory default.

### 12. Court-Ordered Meetings — RCW 23B.07.030

When a corporation fails to hold an annual meeting or fails to respond to a valid shareholder demand for a special meeting, RCW 23B.07.030 provides a judicial remedy:

> "(1) The superior court of the county in which the corporation's registered office is located may, after notice to the corporation, summarily order a meeting to be held: (a) On application of any shareholder of the corporation entitled to participate in an annual meeting if an annual meeting was not held [within the required timeframes]; or (b) On application of a shareholder who signed a valid demand for a special meeting, if notice of the special meeting was not given [or the meeting was not held]."

The court's authority is broad. Upon an application by a qualifying shareholder, the superior court may "fix the time and place of the meeting, determine the shares and shareholders entitled to participate in the meeting, specify a record date for determining shareholders entitled to notice of and to vote at the meeting," and take such other orders as are necessary to effect the purposes of the statute.

**Venue.** The application is filed in the superior court of the county where the corporation's registered office is located. A shareholder cannot forum-shop.

**Summary nature of the proceeding.** The statute describes the remedy as "summary," meaning it is expected to be resolved quickly and without the ordinary discovery and trial machinery. The court's role is procedural — it orders the meeting, sets the logistics, and lets the shareholders take it from there. The court does not decide the substantive corporate question being put to the shareholders.

**Practical implication.** Court-ordered meetings are relatively rare in Washington. They arise most often in closely held corporations where one faction has seized control of the board and refuses to convene the shareholders (often to stave off a director-removal vote). The shareholder petition under RCW 23B.07.030 is the primary mechanism for breaking this kind of deadlock.

### 13. Notice — RCW 23B.07.050

RCW 23B.07.050 establishes the notice requirements for shareholder meetings:

> "(1) A corporation shall give notice consistent with its bylaws of meetings of shareholders in a fair and reasonable manner. Any notice which conforms to the requirements of subsection (2) of this section is fair and reasonable, but other means of giving notice may also be fair and reasonable when all the circumstances are considered.
> (2) Notice is fair and reasonable if: (a) The corporation notifies its shareholders of the place, date, and time of each annual and special shareholders' meeting no fewer than ten nor more than sixty days before the meeting date; (b) notice of an annual meeting includes a description of any matter or matters which must be approved by the shareholders under RCW 23B.10.030 through 23B.10.040, RCW 23B.11.030, RCW 23B.12.020, or RCW 23B.14.020; and (c) notice of a special meeting includes a description of the purpose or purposes for which the meeting is called."

**Timing — ten to sixty days.** The notice period is 10 to 60 days before the meeting date. Notice given fewer than 10 days in advance is untimely; notice given more than 60 days in advance is stale. For meetings at which fundamental actions are to be considered (amendments to articles, mergers, sale of substantially all assets, dissolution), the timing is 20 to 60 days.

**Content.** The notice must state the place, date, and time of the meeting. For an annual meeting, the notice need not describe the purpose unless the articles or bylaws require it, or unless a fundamental action will be voted on. For a special meeting, the notice must describe the purpose of the meeting — and only matters within the described purpose can be voted on.

**Waiver.** Under RCW 23B.07.060, a shareholder may waive notice in writing or by attending the meeting without objecting to lack of notice. Waiver is common in closely held corporations where all shareholders are informal participants in governance.

### 14. Record Date — RCW 23B.07.070

RCW 23B.07.070 governs the record date — the cutoff date that determines which shareholders are entitled to notice of and to vote at a meeting:

> "(1) The bylaws may fix or provide the manner of fixing the record date for one or more voting groups. If the bylaws do not fix or provide for fixing a record date, the board of directors may fix a record date.
> (2) If a record date is not otherwise fixed, the record date is the day before the first notice is delivered to shareholders.
> (3) A record date fixed under this section may not be more than seventy days before the meeting or action requiring a determination of shareholders.
> (4) [Adjournment within 120 days uses the original record date; adjournment beyond 120 days requires a new record date.]
> (5) [Court-ordered adjournment exceeding 120 days: court may maintain or set new record date.]"

**Who sets the record date.** The bylaws may fix the record date, or the board may fix it. In the absence of either, the default is the day before the first notice is delivered.

**The 70-day cap.** A fixed record date cannot be more than 70 days before the meeting date. This prevents the corporation from using a stale shareholder list to exclude recent purchasers.

**Adjournment rules.** If a meeting is adjourned for fewer than 120 days, the original record date carries over to the reconvened meeting. If adjourned for more than 120 days, a new record date must be set and new notice must be given.

**Practical implication.** Setting the record date is a routine board action for public companies but rare for closely held corporations (where the shareholders are known and do not change between meetings). The record-date machinery exists to handle the mechanics of public-company proxy voting.

### 15. Proxies — RCW 23B.07.220

RCW 23B.07.220 governs shareholder proxies:

> "(1) A shareholder may vote the shareholder's shares in person or by proxy.
> (2) A shareholder or the shareholder's agent or attorney-in-fact may appoint a proxy by executing a writing or by an electronic transmission [with identifying information].
> (3) An appointment of a proxy is effective when a signed appointment form or an electronic transmission of the appointment is received by the inspector of election or the officer or agent of the corporation authorized to tabulate votes. An appointment is valid for eleven months unless a longer period is expressly provided in the appointment form.
> (4) An appointment of a proxy is revocable by the shareholder unless the appointment form or electronic transmission states that it is irrevocable and the appointment is coupled with an interest [specified categories]."

**Written or electronic.** Proxies can be paper or electronic. The electronic form must include identifying information sufficient to establish that the shareholder (or authorized agent) made the appointment. Modern corporations use electronic proxy platforms (Broadridge, ComputerShare) for public-company elections.

**Eleven-month default.** A proxy is valid for 11 months unless the appointment form specifies a longer period. The 11-month default is designed to ensure that proxies do not become indefinitely binding; shareholders are expected to reaffirm their proxies each year.

**Irrevocability coupled with an interest.** A proxy is revocable by default, but the appointment form may state that it is irrevocable if it is coupled with an interest — for example, a proxy given to a pledgee of the shares, to a share purchaser, to a corporate creditor, to an employee under a voting agreement, or to a party to a voting agreement under RCW 23B.07.310.

**Death or incapacity.** Section (5) provides that the death or incapacity of the shareholder does not affect the validity of the proxy unless the tabulating officer receives notice of the death or incapacity before the proxy is exercised.

**Practical implication.** In closely held corporations, proxies are rare; shareholders tend to appear in person or execute written consents. In venture-backed corporations with many preferred holders, proxies are common, and in public companies the proxy machinery drives the entire shareholder-meeting process.

### 16. Action Without a Meeting — RCW 23B.07.040

RCW 23B.07.040 permits shareholders to act by written consent in lieu of a meeting. This is one of the most important provisions for closely held corporations.

> "(1) Corporate action required or permitted by this title to be approved by a shareholder vote at a meeting may be approved without a meeting or a vote if either: (i) The corporate action is approved by all shareholders entitled to vote on the corporate action; or (ii) [with advance notice, approved by the minimum number of votes required].
> (2) [Written consent requirements.]
> (3) Shareholder approval obtained under this section is effective when: (a) executed shareholder consents sufficient to approve the proposed corporate action have been delivered to the corporation, and (b) any period of advance notice required by the articles has been satisfied.
> (4) No written consent is effective to approve a proposed corporate action unless, within sixty days after the earliest date on which a consent delivered to the corporation was executed, written consents executed by a sufficient number of shareholders to approve the corporate action are delivered to the corporation."

**Two approval pathways.**

1. **Unanimous written consent.** If all shareholders entitled to vote sign the consent, the action is approved without further procedural requirements. This is the most common approach in closely held corporations.
2. **Majority written consent with advance notice.** If less than all shareholders are needed (i.e., the action can be approved by a majority or other specified vote), the statute permits non-unanimous written consent but requires advance notice to non-consenting shareholders. This pathway is more complex and is primarily used in venture-backed corporations where the preferred holders want to act by consent without convening a meeting but cannot always obtain unanimity.

**The 60-day rolling window.** Consents delivered to the corporation must, within 60 days of the earliest signed consent, add up to the required approval threshold. If the 60-day window expires without reaching the threshold, the consent process fails and must be restarted.

**Effectiveness.** The consents become effective upon delivery to the corporation (not upon signature), assuming all procedural conditions are satisfied.

**Practical implication.** Written consent is the workhorse of closely held corporate governance. A typical closely held Washington corporation conducts its entire year of shareholder business through a single "Annual Action by Written Consent" that elects directors, ratifies board actions, and approves any fundamental transactions. Venture-backed corporations typically use a mix of consents for routine items and meetings for major financing events (where the formality of a meeting provides additional evidentiary weight).

### 17. Quorum and Voting Thresholds — RCW 23B.07.250

RCW 23B.07.250 establishes the default quorum and voting rules:

> "(1) Unless this title or the articles of incorporation provide otherwise, a majority of the votes entitled to be cast on the corporate action by the voting group constitutes a quorum of that voting group for approval of that corporate action.
> (2) Once a share is represented for any purpose at a meeting, other than solely to object to holding the meeting or transacting business at the meeting, it is deemed present for quorum purposes for the remainder of the meeting and for any adjournment of that meeting unless a new record date is or must be set for that adjourned meeting.
> (3) If a quorum exists, action on a matter, other than the election of directors, by a voting group is approved if the votes cast within the voting group favoring the action exceed the votes cast within the voting group opposing the action, unless this title or the articles of incorporation require a greater number of affirmative votes.
> (4) [Director elections governed by RCW 23B.07.280.]"

**Default quorum: majority.** The default quorum is a majority of the voting power entitled to vote. The articles of incorporation can lower this (but not below one-third per section (5) of related sections) or raise it (e.g., to two-thirds or supermajority levels).

**Default approval: more votes in favor than against.** The default approval standard is a simple majority of votes cast at the meeting — not a majority of shares entitled to vote. This is an important distinction: abstentions and broker non-votes do not count as "against" votes; they simply reduce the denominator. The articles can raise this (e.g., to a supermajority of shares entitled to vote, which would make abstentions effectively "against" votes).

**Sticky quorum.** Once a share is represented for any purpose at the meeting, it is deemed present for quorum purposes for the remainder of the meeting. A shareholder cannot attend, witness the meeting reach quorum, and then walk out to defeat quorum.

**Director elections.** Director elections are governed separately by RCW 23B.07.280, which permits either plurality voting (the default) or majority voting (by opt-in).

### 18. Voting Groups — RCW 23B.07.250

A "voting group" is a class or series of shares entitled to vote separately on a matter. RCW 23B.07.250 applies separately to each voting group: each voting group has its own quorum and its own approval threshold.

**When do voting groups arise?** Voting groups arise when the articles create multiple classes of stock and specify that certain matters require separate approval by a particular class. Common examples:

- **Protected class voting on charter amendments.** The articles may provide that amendments affecting a particular class require approval of that class voting as a separate voting group.
- **Mergers and acquisitions.** RCW 23B.11.030 requires separate class voting on mergers in certain circumstances.
- **Issuances of senior securities.** Venture-capital preferred stock typically carries contractual rights to vote as a separate class on the issuance of additional preferred stock with superior rights.

**Practical implication.** Voting-group analysis is critical in venture-backed corporations. A typical preferred-stock financing document specifies (a) which matters require board approval, (b) which matters require common-stock approval, (c) which matters require preferred-stock approval voting as a single class, and (d) which matters require separate series voting (e.g., Series A voting separately from Series B). Getting this structure right is the domain of experienced venture-capital counsel.

### 19. Shareholder Agreements — RCW 23B.07.320

RCW 23B.07.320 is one of the most powerful planning tools in the Washington corporate statute. It permits shareholders to enter into an agreement that deviates, in specified ways, from the default corporate governance rules — even rules that would otherwise be mandatory.

> "(1) An agreement among the shareholders of a corporation that is not contrary to public policy and that complies with this section is effective among the shareholders and the corporation even though it is inconsistent with one or more other provisions of this title in that it: (a) Eliminates the board of directors or restricts the discretion or powers of the board; (b) Governs the authorization or making of distributions whether or not in proportion to ownership of shares; (c) Establishes who shall be directors or officers of the corporation, or their terms of office or manner of selection or removal; (d) Governs the exercise or division of voting power by or between the shareholders and directors; (e) Establishes the terms and conditions of any agreement for the transfer or use of property or the provision of services between the corporation and any shareholder, director, officer, or employee; (f) Transfers to one or more shareholders or other persons all or part of the authority to exercise the corporate powers or to manage the business and affairs of the corporation; (g) Requires dissolution of the corporation at the request of one or more of the shareholders or upon the occurrence of a specified event or contingency; or (h) Otherwise governs the exercise of the corporate powers or the management of the business and affairs of the corporation or the relationship among the shareholders, the directors, and the corporation."

**The breadth of permissible agreements.** The enumerated list of permissible subjects is extraordinarily broad. A shareholder agreement may:

1. **Eliminate the board of directors.** This is the closest thing Washington has to a "statutory close corporation" regime. Washington does not, unlike some states, have a separate close-corporation statute; instead, the shareholder agreement mechanism of 23B.07.320 serves the same function by allowing shareholders to dispense with the board and run the corporation directly.
2. **Govern distributions.** Including non-pro-rata distributions — an arrangement impossible under default corporate law, which requires distributions to be pro rata within each class.
3. **Determine directors and officers.** Including the identity, terms, and removal procedures for directors and officers. The shareholder agreement can establish an iron-clad board composition that cannot be altered by any later shareholder or board action.
4. **Allocate voting power.** Including allocations that would otherwise violate the one-share-one-vote default.
5. **Govern side contracts.** The shareholder agreement can pre-authorize and establish the terms of contracts between the corporation and its shareholders (such as employment agreements, consulting arrangements, equipment leases).
6. **Transfer corporate powers.** Including transfers to shareholders or to outside persons. This is the partner-like arrangement by which closely held corporations can simulate a partnership while retaining limited liability.
7. **Require dissolution on specified events.** Including deadlock, the death or departure of a key shareholder, or the occurrence of any other specified contingency.
8. **Govern anything else.** The catchall at (h) is textually broad enough to encompass any arrangement touching corporate governance, the relationship among shareholders, or the operation of the business.

**Formal requirements.** To be effective, the shareholder agreement must:

1. **Be in writing and executed by all shareholders at the time of the agreement.** Unanimity at the moment of execution is required. If even one shareholder refuses to sign, the agreement fails.
2. **Be made known to the corporation.** The agreement must be delivered to the corporation so that it has notice.
3. **Be noted conspicuously on the face (or on a written statement referenced on the face) of each share certificate.** Or, if the corporation is uncertificated, noted in the information statement sent to shareholders. This ensures that future acquirers of shares are on notice of the agreement's existence and terms.
4. **Be amended only by unanimous shareholder consent** (unless the agreement itself specifies a lower threshold).

**Automatic binding of transferees.** Section (5) provides that "a person acquiring shares of the corporation after the execution of an agreement under this section is deemed to have assented to the agreement and to be a party to the agreement for all purposes, unless the agreement provides otherwise or the person was without knowledge of the existence of the agreement at the time of acquiring the shares and no notice of the agreement was noted conspicuously on the face or back of the certificate for the shares."

**Termination.** The agreement terminates when the corporation becomes a public company (unless the agreement provides otherwise), because the shareholder-agreement mechanism is incompatible with the open-market trading environment of a public company.

**The "close corporation substitute."** Many states (Delaware, California, Texas, others) have separate close-corporation statutes that create a distinct corporate subform with modified governance rules. Washington does not. Instead, Washington relies on RCW 23B.07.320 to achieve the same effect: any Washington corporation can, by unanimous shareholder agreement, simulate a close corporation's flexibility. The advantage of this approach is simplicity — a single corporate form serves both open and closed businesses. The disadvantage is that the shareholder agreement must be drafted with care and must be updated whenever shareholders change.

**Interaction with the articles and bylaws.** A shareholder agreement trumps inconsistent provisions of the articles and bylaws (because the statute explicitly authorizes it to be "inconsistent with one or more other provisions of this title"). This makes the shareholder agreement the highest tier of the corporate governance stack for closely held corporations. The conflict hierarchy becomes:

1. Shareholder agreement (RCW 23B.07.320) — within its authorized scope
2. Articles of incorporation
3. Bylaws
4. Board resolutions

**Practical implication.** Every closely held Washington corporation with more than one shareholder should consider a 23B.07.320 shareholder agreement. The agreement is the vehicle through which the founders can commit to governance norms that would otherwise be unenforceable — unanimous director-election voting, pro-rata capital-call obligations, buy-sell provisions on death or departure, drag-along and tag-along rights, non-compete obligations, and many other arrangements that cannot be captured by articles and bylaws alone.

## Part 3: Directors and Officers (RCW 23B.08)

### 20. Board Authority — RCW 23B.08.010

RCW 23B.08.010 is the foundational provision of corporate governance: it establishes the board of directors as the organ through which corporate powers are exercised.

> "(1) Each corporation must have a board of directors, except that a corporation may dispense with or limit the authority of its board of directors by including provisions in its articles of incorporation or shareholders' agreement authorized by RCW 23B.07.320.
> (2) All corporate powers shall be exercised by or under the authority of the corporation's board of directors, and the business and affairs of the corporation shall be managed under the direction of its board of directors, which shall have exclusive authority as to substantive decisions concerning management of the corporation's business, subject to any limitation set forth in this title, the articles of incorporation, or a shareholders' agreement authorized by RCW 23B.07.320."

**The board requirement and its exceptions.** Section (1) requires every corporation to have a board — but permits the articles or a shareholder agreement to dispense with or limit the board's authority. This is the link to RCW 23B.07.320: a shareholder agreement can transfer management authority from the board to the shareholders (or to a subset of shareholders) directly.

**"Exclusive authority as to substantive decisions."** Section (2) is a crucial provision for understanding the balance between board and shareholder authority. By default, the board has "exclusive authority as to substantive decisions concerning management of the corporation's business." Shareholders do not manage the corporation; they elect directors, vote on fundamental transactions, and otherwise leave management to the board. This is the representative-democracy structure of corporate governance: shareholders select representatives (directors) who then act as fiduciaries on the shareholders' behalf.

**Limitations on board authority.** The board's authority is subject to three types of limitations:

1. **Statutory limits.** The statute reserves certain decisions for shareholders — amendments to articles (23B.10), mergers (23B.11), dispositions of substantially all assets (23B.12), dissolution (23B.14).
2. **Articles of incorporation.** The articles can reserve additional matters to the shareholders.
3. **Shareholder agreements.** A 23B.07.320 agreement can reallocate management authority.

**What cannot be pushed to shareholders.** In a normal governance structure (no shareholder agreement), the board's exclusive-authority zone includes day-to-day management decisions, strategic planning, officer appointment and compensation, capital allocation, and contract approval. These cannot be transferred to shareholders without running afoul of the exclusive-authority rule — unless the articles or a shareholder agreement expressly transfer them.

**Practical implication.** In a closely held corporation where the shareholders and directors are the same people, the allocation of authority is largely academic. In a venture-backed corporation where the shareholders include outside investors who may not sit on the board, the allocation becomes critically important. Venture-capital preferred-stock financings typically include "protective provisions" — contractual or charter-based veto rights over specified corporate actions — that carve specific matters out of the board's exclusive authority and reserve them to preferred-stock approval.

### 21. Board Composition — RCW 23B.08.030

RCW 23B.08.030 governs the number of directors and the method of specifying that number:

> "(1) A board of directors must consist of one or more individuals, with the number specified in or fixed in accordance with the articles of incorporation or bylaws.
> (2) The number of directors may be increased or decreased from time to time by amendment to, or in the manner provided in, the articles of incorporation or the bylaws. [Other provisions address the manner of specifying the number.]"

**Minimum of one.** Washington requires at least one director. A single-director board is lawful — and for solo-founder corporations, typical. Some states (historically California for larger corporations) imposed higher minimums, but Washington follows the MBCA's one-director rule.

**Variable range.** The articles or bylaws may specify either a fixed number or a range (e.g., "not less than three nor more than nine directors"). A variable range gives the board flexibility to expand or contract without a formal amendment. The actual number within the range is then fixed by board resolution (or by the shareholders if the articles so specify).

**Director qualifications.** Washington does not impose any default qualifications for directors. A director need not be a shareholder, need not be a Washington resident, need not have any particular expertise, and need not be a natural person of any particular age (though the director must be an "individual," which by implication excludes entities). The articles or bylaws may impose qualification requirements — age minimums, residency requirements, share-ownership requirements, independence requirements, and so on.

**Amendment of the number.** The number of directors can be changed by amending the articles or bylaws, or by any other mechanism specified in those documents. The amendment process is constrained by the ordinary amendment rules (RCW 23B.10).

**Practical implication.** The choice of board size is an important governance decision. A one-director board offers maximum speed and minimum ceremony but no collegial check. A three-director board is the smallest size that offers the benefit of board deliberation and is by far the most common size for small corporations. Five- and seven-director boards are common for more mature companies. Venture-backed corporations often have boards in the range of five to seven members, with specified seats for common-stock representatives, preferred-stock representatives, and independent directors.

### 22. Election and Terms — RCW 23B.08.050, .060, .070

Director terms are governed by RCW 23B.08.050 (general rule), with modifications permitted by RCW 23B.08.060 (staggered terms) and RCW 23B.08.070 (resignation).

#### 22.1 — RCW 23B.08.050 (General Term Rule)

> "(1) The terms of the initial directors of a corporation expire at the first shareholders' meeting at which directors are elected.
> (2) The terms of all other directors expire at the next annual shareholders' meeting following their election unless their terms are staggered under RCW 23B.08.060 or RCW 23B.05.050 is applicable.
> (3) A decrease in the number of directors does not shorten an incumbent director's term.
> (4) The term of a director elected to fill a vacancy expires at the next shareholders' meeting at which directors are elected.
> (5) Despite the expiration of a director's term, the director continues to serve until the director's successor is elected and qualifies or until there is a decrease in the number of directors, as provided in bylaws adopted pursuant to RCW 23B.10.205."

The default term is one year — from the annual meeting at which the director is elected until the next annual meeting. This is the "annual election" regime, in which all directors stand for reelection every year.

**The holdover rule.** Section (5) is important: even after a director's term expires, the director continues to serve until a successor is elected and qualifies. This prevents the corporation from having an empty board merely because an annual meeting was delayed. A director in holdover status has full authority to act — the director is not a "lame duck" in any legal sense.

**Term and position are distinct.** The expiration of a term does not automatically vacate the director position; it simply triggers the obligation to elect a successor. Until the successor is elected, the incumbent continues. This is a subtle but important distinction.

#### 22.2 — RCW 23B.08.060 (Staggered Terms)

> "(1) The articles of incorporation may provide for staggering the terms of directors by dividing the total number of directors into two or three groups, with each group containing one-half or one-third of the total, as near as may be."

A "classified" or "staggered" board has directors elected for multi-year terms with only a subset standing for reelection each year. Washington permits two-class staggering (each class serves two years) or three-class staggering (each class serves three years). Staggering cannot go beyond three classes.

**Anti-takeover use.** Classified boards are an anti-takeover device: even if an acquirer gains majority shareholder support, it cannot replace the board in a single election cycle. Instead, it takes at least two election cycles to replace a majority of a three-year classified board (with the first cycle replacing one-third, leaving the acquirer with only one-third of the seats). The acquirer must wait another year to obtain the second third, at which point it finally has a majority. This delay can give the incumbent board time to negotiate with the acquirer or implement defensive measures.

**Declassification trend.** Over the past two decades, institutional shareholders in public-company contexts have pushed for declassification of boards on the theory that annual accountability is more protective of shareholder interests. Most large public companies have declassified under shareholder pressure. Closely held and venture-backed companies, which are less subject to activist pressure, often retain classification to stabilize governance.

**Cumulative voting and staggering.** When cumulative voting is authorized (see RCW 23B.07.260), a classified board must elect at least three directors at each annual meeting — otherwise the minority-protection purpose of cumulative voting would be defeated by electing directors one at a time.

#### 22.3 — RCW 23B.08.070 (Resignation)

> "(1) A director may resign at any time by delivering a written notice of resignation to the board of directors, its chairperson, the president, or the secretary of the corporation.
> (2) A resignation is effective as provided in RCW 23B.01.410(9) unless the notice provides for a delayed effectiveness, including effectiveness determined upon a future event or events. A resignation that is conditioned upon failing to receive a specified vote for election as a director may provide that it is irrevocable."

**Effectiveness.** A resignation is effective upon delivery (per the general effectiveness rule of 23B.01.410(9)) unless the notice specifies a delayed date. A director cannot retroactively undo a resignation — once the notice is delivered, it is effective according to its terms.

**Conditional resignations.** Section (2) explicitly authorizes "conditional resignations" — resignations that take effect only upon the occurrence of a specified future event. The most important application is the "majority voting policy" in public-company governance: directors may be required, as a condition of nomination, to submit an irrevocable conditional resignation that takes effect if they fail to receive a majority of votes in an uncontested election. This transforms plurality voting into de facto majority voting without amending the articles.

**Practical implication.** A director who wishes to resign should do so in writing and should specify the effective date clearly. Oral resignations are not clearly effective under the statute, and ambiguity in the effective date can create governance headaches. Conditional resignations are increasingly common in public-company contexts and occasionally in larger closely held corporations with formal governance policies.

### 23. Vacancies and Removal — RCW 23B.08.080, .100

Two provisions handle the death, resignation, and removal of directors.

#### 23.1 — RCW 23B.08.100 (Filling Vacancies)

> "(1) Unless the articles of incorporation provide otherwise, if a vacancy occurs on a board of directors, including a vacancy resulting from an increase in the number of directors: (a) The shareholders may fill the vacancy; (b) the board of directors may fill the vacancy; or (c) if the directors remaining in office constitute fewer than a quorum of the board, they may fill the vacancy by the affirmative vote of a majority of all the directors remaining in office.
> (2) If the vacant office was held by a director elected by a voting group of shareholders, only the holders of shares of that voting group are entitled to vote to fill the vacancy if it is filled by the shareholders.
> (3) A vacancy that will occur at a specific later date (by reason of a resignation effective at a later date, or otherwise) may be filled before the vacancy occurs but the new director may not take office until the vacancy occurs."

**Three pathways.** A vacancy can be filled by (a) the shareholders, (b) the board, or (c) the remaining directors if they do not constitute a quorum. The third pathway is crucial: without it, a series of resignations that dropped the board below quorum could paralyze governance by eliminating the ability to fill the vacancies. The statute authorizes the remaining directors (even sub-quorum) to appoint replacements.

**Voting-group restriction.** If a vacancy is for a seat elected by a particular voting group (e.g., a "common stock director" seat in a classified-stock structure), only the holders of that voting group can fill the vacancy by shareholder vote.

**Advance filling.** A vacancy that will occur on a known future date may be filled in advance, with the new director taking office when the vacancy actually occurs. This is useful for orderly transitions.

#### 23.2 — RCW 23B.08.080 (Removal)

> "(1) The shareholders may remove one or more directors with or without cause unless the articles of incorporation provide that directors may be removed only for cause.
> (2) If a director is elected by a voting group of shareholders, only the shareholders of that voting group may participate in the vote to remove the director.
> (3) A director may be removed only at a meeting called for the purpose of removing the director and the meeting notice must state that the purpose, or one of the purposes, of the meeting is removal of the director.
> (4) In computing whether a director is removed under this section, a director is removed if the number of votes cast to remove the director exceeds the number of votes cast not to remove the director, except to the extent that the articles of incorporation or bylaws require a greater number or a different proportion of votes."

**Default: removable without cause.** The Washington default is that directors are removable by shareholder vote with or without cause. The articles may restrict removal to "for cause" only.

**Special meeting requirement.** Removal requires a meeting specifically called for the purpose of removing the director, and the meeting notice must identify removal as a purpose. Removal cannot be sprung on unsuspecting shareholders without notice.

**Majority-of-votes-cast standard.** A director is removed if the votes to remove exceed the votes not to remove. Abstentions do not count against removal.

**Cumulative voting exception.** When cumulative voting is authorized, a director cannot be removed if the number of votes that would have been sufficient to elect that director (under cumulative voting) is cast against removal. This protects minority-elected directors from being removed by the majority.

### 24. Meetings and Action — RCW 23B.08.200, .210, .240

Directors act either through meetings or through unanimous written consent.

#### 24.1 — RCW 23B.08.200 (Regular and Special Meetings)

> "(1) The board of directors may hold regular or special meetings in or out of this state.
> (2) Unless the articles of incorporation or bylaws provide otherwise, the board of directors may permit any or all directors to participate in a regular or special meeting by, or conduct the meeting through the use of, any means of communication by which all directors participating can hear each other during the meeting. A director participating in a meeting by this means is deemed to be present in person at the meeting."

**Location and mode.** Meetings can be held anywhere (in Washington or not) and through any communication means that allow all directors to hear each other simultaneously. The latter provision is the statutory basis for telephone, video, and other electronic meetings.

**Requirement of simultaneity.** The "hear each other" requirement rules out asynchronous communication. Email chains and discussion forums are not meetings — they may constitute informal communication, but a meeting requires real-time participation. A hybrid meeting (some directors in person, some by videoconference) is lawful as long as all can hear each other.

#### 24.2 — RCW 23B.08.210 (Action Without a Meeting)

> "(1) Unless the articles of incorporation or bylaws provide otherwise, corporate action required or permitted by this title to be taken at a board of directors' meeting may be taken without a meeting if the corporate action is approved by all members of the board. The corporate action must be evidenced by one or more written consents describing the corporate action taken, executed by each director.
> (2) Corporate action taken under this section is effective when the last director executes the consent, unless the consent specifies a later effective date.
> (3) A consent under this section has the effect of a meeting vote and may be described as such in any document."

**Unanimity required.** Unlike shareholder consent (which in some circumstances can be less than unanimous), director consent must be unanimous. If even one director refuses or fails to sign, the consent fails and a meeting must be held.

**Written or electronic form.** The consent can be in writing or by electronic transmission (subject to the authentication requirements of 23B.01.410). A typical director consent is a PDF signed electronically by all directors via DocuSign or similar.

**Effectiveness.** The consent is effective when the last director signs (unless a later effective date is specified). Until the last signature, the action is not approved.

**Practical implication.** Director consents are the workhorse of efficient board governance. Most closely held corporations take routine actions (approving contracts, ratifying officer actions, authorizing bank signatories) by consent rather than by meeting. Meetings are reserved for major decisions requiring deliberation and discussion.

#### 24.3 — RCW 23B.08.240 (Quorum and Voting at Board Meetings)

> "(1) Unless the articles of incorporation or bylaws require a greater number, a quorum of a board of directors consists of a majority of the number of directors specified in or fixed in accordance with the articles of incorporation or bylaws.
> (2) The articles of incorporation or bylaws may authorize a quorum of a board of directors to consist of no fewer than one-third of the number of directors specified in or fixed in accordance with the articles of incorporation or bylaws.
> (3) If a quorum is present when a vote is taken, the affirmative vote of a majority of directors present is the act of the board of directors unless the articles of incorporation or bylaws require the vote of a greater number of directors.
> (4) A director who is present at a meeting of the board of directors or a committee of the board of directors when corporate action is taken is deemed to have assented to the corporate action unless: (a) The director objects at the beginning of the meeting, or promptly upon the director's arrival, to holding it or transacting business at the meeting; (b) the director's dissent or abstention from the corporate action is entered in the minutes of the meeting; or (c) the director delivers written notice of the director's dissent or abstention to the presiding officer of the meeting before its adjournment or to the corporation within a reasonable time after adjournment of the meeting."

**Default quorum: majority.** A majority of the directors constitutes a quorum for the board. This is the standard rule.

**Minimum floor: one-third.** The articles or bylaws can lower the quorum requirement but not below one-third. This prevents an absurd quorum of one or two directors on a large board.

**Default approval: majority of those present.** An action is approved if a majority of directors present (not a majority of all directors) vote in favor. If a quorum is five directors and only four actually vote (one abstains), three affirmative votes are sufficient.

**Deemed assent rule.** Section (4) is an important protection against after-the-fact dissent. A director who is present at a meeting is deemed to have assented to every action taken unless the director objects at the start of the meeting, has the dissent noted in the minutes, or delivers a written dissent before or promptly after adjournment. A director cannot quietly sit through a vote and later claim to have disagreed.

**Practical implication.** The deemed-assent rule is a trap for inattentive directors. A director who wants to disagree must speak up and ensure the dissent is recorded. Silence is acquiescence.

### 25. Committees — RCW 23B.08.250

RCW 23B.08.250 authorizes the formation of board committees and regulates the scope of authority that can be delegated to them.

> "(1) Unless this title, the articles of incorporation, or the bylaws provide otherwise, a board of directors may establish one or more board committees composed exclusively of one or more directors to perform functions authorized by the board.
> (2) The establishment of a committee and appointment of members to it must be approved by the greater of (a) a majority of all the directors in office when the corporate action is taken or (b) the number of directors required by the articles of incorporation or bylaws to take action.
> (3) [Meeting provisions of 23B.08.200 through .240 apply to committees.]
> (4) To the extent specified by the board of directors, or in the articles of incorporation or bylaws, each committee may exercise the powers of the board of directors under RCW 23B.08.010, except that a committee may not: (a) Authorize or approve distributions, except according to a formula or method prescribed by the board of directors; (b) approve or propose to shareholders corporate action that this title requires be approved by shareholders; (c) fill vacancies on the board of directors or on any of its committees; or (d) adopt, amend, or repeal bylaws.
> (5) The board of directors may appoint one or more directors as alternate members of any committee to replace any absent or disqualified member during the member's absence or disqualification. [Present members may unanimously appoint replacements if bylaws permit.]"

**Formation and authority.** The board can create committees of one or more directors and delegate any of its powers to them, subject to four non-delegable functions.

**Non-delegable functions.** Committees cannot:

1. **Authorize or approve distributions** (except on a formula prescribed by the board). Distributions are a fundamental capital-allocation decision reserved to the full board.
2. **Approve or propose fundamental corporate actions requiring shareholder approval.** This includes amendments to articles, mergers, sales of substantially all assets, and dissolution. The full board must vote on these matters.
3. **Fill board or committee vacancies.** Vacancy-filling is reserved to the full board.
4. **Adopt, amend, or repeal bylaws.** Bylaw amendments are reserved to the full board (or to the shareholders, as the articles provide).

**Common committee structures.** Public companies typically have three standing committees: Audit, Compensation, and Nominating/Governance. Each has specialized functions:

- **Audit Committee.** Oversees financial reporting, external auditors, and internal controls. Required for SEC-registered companies under Sarbanes-Oxley.
- **Compensation Committee.** Reviews and approves executive compensation. Required for listed companies under stock-exchange rules.
- **Nominating/Governance Committee.** Reviews director candidates and governance policies.

Closely held corporations generally do not have standing committees; the full board acts on all matters.

**Alternate members.** Section (5) permits the board to appoint alternate members who step in during the absence or disqualification of a regular member. This preserves committee quorum even when a member is unavailable.

### 26. Standards of Conduct for Directors — RCW 23B.08.300

RCW 23B.08.300 codifies the director's duties of care and loyalty and the standard of good faith:

> "(1) A director shall discharge the duties of a director, including the director's duties as a member of a committee: (a) In good faith; (b) with the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances; and (c) in a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation.
> (2) In discharging the duties of a director, a director is entitled to rely on information, opinions, reports, or statements, including financial statements and other financial data, if prepared or presented by: (a) One or more officers or employees of the corporation whom the director reasonably believes to be reliable and competent in the matters presented; (b) legal counsel, public accountants, or other persons as to matters the director reasonably believes are within the person's professional or expert competence; or (c) a committee of the board of directors of which the director is not a member, as to matters within its designated authority, if the director reasonably believes the committee merits confidence.
> (3) A director is not acting in good faith if the director has knowledge concerning the matter in question that makes reliance otherwise permitted by subsection (2) of this section unwarranted.
> (4) A director is not liable for any action taken as a director, or any failure to take any action, if the director performed the duties of the director's office in compliance with this section."

**The three-part standard: good faith, care, and best interests.**

1. **Good faith.** The director must act honestly, without improper motive, and with fidelity to the corporation's interests. "Good faith" is a subjective standard: it asks what the director actually believed and intended, not what a reasonable director would have believed.

2. **Care.** The director must exercise "the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances." This is the duty of care, and it is objective: it asks what a reasonable director would have done, not what this director actually did. The "like position" and "similar circumstances" modifiers are important: a director is held to the standard of a reasonably prudent director, not a reasonably prudent member of the general public.

3. **Best interests.** The director must act "in a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation." This blends subjective belief ("the director reasonably believes") with an objective reasonableness check ("reasonably"). A director who honestly but unreasonably believes an action is in the corporation's best interests fails this prong.

**The reliance safe harbor.** Section (2) establishes that directors can rely on information prepared by officers, employees, lawyers, accountants, and board committees. The reliance must be "reasonable" — the director must have a reasonable belief that the source is reliable and competent. This is a crucial protection for directors, who by the nature of their role cannot personally verify every fact on which their decisions rest.

**Reliance-vitiating knowledge.** Section (3) negates the reliance safe harbor when the director has "knowledge concerning the matter in question that makes reliance otherwise permitted by subsection (2) of this section unwarranted." A director who knows that the CFO is cooking the books cannot rely on the CFO's financial reports. A director who knows that outside counsel has a conflict of interest cannot rely on counsel's opinion.

**Liability shield.** Section (4) is the ultimate statement of the director's immunity: a director who complies with the section is not liable for any action or failure to act. This is the statutory foundation of the Washington business judgment rule as applied by Washington courts.

**Practical implication.** The director standard of conduct is the most consequential provision of the WBCA for directors facing personal-liability exposure. A director who can establish compliance with RCW 23B.08.300 is immune from monetary damages for the decision in question. Compliance is established through meeting minutes reflecting deliberation, reliance on expert advice, careful inquiry into material facts, and (critically) good-faith reasoning. Directors who rubber-stamp management recommendations without inquiry risk failing the care prong.

### 27. Business Judgment Rule in Washington Case Law

The Washington business judgment rule ("BJR") is the judicial doctrine through which courts defer to the substantive business decisions of directors who have complied with their duty of care. The BJR is not codified as such in the WBCA; it exists in Washington case law, applied by the courts against the statutory backdrop of RCW 23B.08.300.

The leading Washington cases are:

**1. *Nursing Home Building Corp. v. DeHart*, 13 Wn. App. 489, 535 P.2d 137 (1975).** The Washington Court of Appeals applied what it called "the business judgment rule," stating that courts "generally will not substitute their judgment for that of officers or directors of a corporation and, absent bad faith or fraud, will not impose liability on them for mistakes in the course of corporate transactions involving business judgments." The case involved allegations of self-dealing and fraudulent misappropriation of corporate funds; the court affirmed judgment for the plaintiff corporation against the DeHarts, finding that the BJR did not shield fiduciaries who acted in bad faith or with conflicts of interest. *DeHart* is often cited for the basic formulation of the rule and for the proposition that the BJR does not protect self-dealing transactions.

**2. *Interlake Porsche + Audi, Inc. v. Bucholz*, 45 Wn. App. 502, 728 P.2d 597 (1986).** This is arguably Washington's most cited BJR case. The Court of Appeals stated: "The 'business judgment' rule immunizes management from liability in a corporate transaction undertaken within the corporation's power and management's authority where a reasonable basis exists to indicate that the transaction was made in good faith. Such immunity from liability is absent where a corporate director or officer is shown to have acted in bad faith and with a corrupt motive." The court also emphasized that directors and officers "stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation they serve and are not permitted to retain any personal profit or advantage gleaned 'on the side.'" And on the burden of proof: "In a shareholder derivative action the plaintiff bears the burden of proving (1) a breach of fiduciary duty to the corporation and (2) the breach was the proximate cause of the losses sustained." *Interlake Porsche* is the go-to Washington authority for the classic BJR formulation.

**3. *Para-Medical Leasing, Inc. v. Hangen*, 48 Wn. App. 389, 739 P.2d 717 (1987).** Another Washington Court of Appeals decision applying the BJR in a closely held corporation context. The court reaffirmed that directors are not liable for business decisions made in good faith and with a reasonable basis, even if the decisions turn out poorly in hindsight. *Para-Medical* is cited for the proposition that ordinary business losses — even large ones — do not establish breach of duty absent evidence of bad faith, gross negligence, or self-dealing.

**4. *Schwarzmann v. Association of Apartment Owners*, 33 Wn. App. 397, 655 P.2d 1177 (1982).** Applied the BJR in the context of a condominium association board. While not a business corporation case strictly, Washington courts have occasionally drawn from it for the proposition that deferential review applies across various association-type boards.

**5. *Scott v. Trans-System, Inc.*, 148 Wn.2d 701, 64 P.3d 1 (2003).** A Washington Supreme Court decision in the closely held corporation context. The court addressed director duties in a situation involving a buy-out dispute and, in dicta, discussed the interplay between the statutory standard of conduct in RCW 23B.08.300 and the judicially developed BJR. *Scott* is important because it is the Washington Supreme Court's most recent substantive discussion of director duty and is sometimes cited for the proposition that the duty of care in the statute is incorporated into the BJR standard.

**6. *Riss v. Angel*, 131 Wn.2d 612, 934 P.2d 669 (1997).** Another Washington Supreme Court case discussing director fiduciary duties. *Riss* reaffirmed the basic principle that corporate directors owe duties of care and loyalty to the corporation and its shareholders, and that courts will defer to good-faith business judgments.

**The "Richins critique."** In his Washington Law Review article *Risky Business: Directors Making Business Judgments in Washington State*, 80 Wash. L. Rev. 977 (2005), Adam J. Richins argues that the Washington Supreme Court has, in dicta, suggested that the ordinary-prudent-person standard of RCW 23B.08.300 is itself an element of the BJR's liability standard — a position Richins critiques on the grounds that it blurs the distinction between the standard of conduct (what directors should do) and the standard of liability (when courts will hold them responsible). Richins argues for keeping the two separate: the statutory standard of conduct is what directors should aspire to, while the BJR is the judicial deference rule that protects directors from monetary liability even when they fall short of the statutory standard, provided they have not engaged in bad faith, self-dealing, or gross negligence.

**The Washington BJR in practice.** Synthesizing the cases and commentary, the Washington BJR can be stated as follows:

> Courts will not second-guess the substantive merits of a director's business decision, and will not impose liability on the director for the outcome of the decision, if:
>
> 1. The director acted in good faith (subjective honest intent);
> 2. The director acted on an informed basis (having made such inquiry as the circumstances reasonably required);
> 3. The director had a reasonable basis to believe the decision was in the corporation's best interests; and
> 4. The director did not engage in self-dealing, fraud, or illegality.
>
> When these elements are established, the courts will defer to the director's judgment even if the decision turns out poorly or even if the court itself would have decided differently.

**Contrast with Delaware.** Delaware's BJR (articulated in *Aronson v. Lewis*, 473 A.2d 805 (Del. 1984), and refined in numerous subsequent decisions) applies a presumption: directors are presumed to have acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action was in the best interests of the corporation. The plaintiff bears the burden of rebutting the presumption by pleading specific facts showing breach of duty. If the plaintiff succeeds in rebutting the presumption, the burden shifts to the directors to prove the "entire fairness" of the transaction. Washington's case law does not articulate the rule in exactly these presumption-burden-shifting terms, but the practical effect is similar: directors who act in good faith and on an informed basis are entitled to deference, and plaintiffs face a high burden in challenging substantive business decisions.

**Duty of loyalty vs. duty of care.** The BJR protects directors against duty-of-care claims (i.e., claims that the director's decision was negligently made). The BJR does NOT protect against duty-of-loyalty claims (i.e., claims that the director had a conflict of interest, engaged in self-dealing, acted with a corrupt motive, or took a corporate opportunity). Loyalty breaches are subject to a fairness review, not deferential BJR review. See Sections 28 and 29 for the treatment of loyalty issues under Washington law.

### 28. Director Liability Limitation — RCW 23B.08.320

RCW 23B.08.320 permits the articles of incorporation to exculpate directors from personal liability for monetary damages in certain circumstances:

> "The articles of incorporation may contain provisions not inconsistent with law that: (1) Eliminate or limit the personal liability of a director to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director, provided that such provisions shall not eliminate or limit the liability of a director for acts or omissions that involve intentional misconduct by a director or a knowing violation of law by a director, for conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310, or for any transaction from which the director will personally receive a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director is not legally entitled. No such provision shall eliminate or limit the liability of a director for any act or omission occurring prior to the date when the provision becomes effective."

**What can be limited.** The articles can eliminate or limit personal liability for monetary damages. This is an exculpation provision, directly analogous to Delaware GCL § 102(b)(7). It is not a prohibition on suit — directors can still be sued for equitable relief, and they can still be the subject of derivative claims — but if successful, the plaintiff cannot recover monetary damages against the director.

**Four exceptions.** The exculpation cannot cover:

1. **Intentional misconduct.** Deliberate bad acts are never exculpated.
2. **Knowing violations of law.** Directors who knowingly cause the corporation to violate the law cannot be exculpated for the resulting liability.
3. **Conduct violating RCW 23B.08.310.** RCW 23B.08.310 addresses liability for unlawful distributions — directors who vote for or assent to distributions that violate the statutory distribution rules are personally liable to the corporation for the excess amount. This liability cannot be exculpated.
4. **Transactions from which the director personally benefits.** Self-dealing transactions are never exculpated. The director retains personal liability for any improper benefit received.

**No retroactive effect.** The provision cannot exculpate conduct that occurred before the provision became effective. A corporation cannot, after the fact, amend its articles to exculpate a director for a past bad act.

**Practical implication.** The exculpation provision is standard in the articles of Washington corporations formed with any degree of sophistication. A typical formulation reads: "To the fullest extent permitted by RCW 23B.08.320 as it now exists or may hereafter be amended, no director of the corporation shall be liable to the corporation or its shareholders for monetary damages for conduct as a director, except for liability that by law cannot be eliminated." Together with robust indemnification and D&O insurance, exculpation substantially de-risks director service.

**Connection to the BJR.** Exculpation and the BJR serve overlapping but distinct functions. The BJR protects directors from substantive challenges to their business judgment even absent an exculpation clause — it is a judicial doctrine that applies to all Washington corporations. Exculpation goes further: it protects directors from monetary liability even for negligent decisions that might not enjoy BJR protection (for example, a decision made on an inadequately informed basis). Exculpation and the BJR together provide layered protection against personal liability.

### 29. Conflicts of Interest — RCW 23B.08.730–.735

Washington's conflict-of-interest regime establishes safe harbors for transactions between the corporation and its directors (or affiliated persons). The relevant sections are RCW 23B.08.700 through .740.

#### 29.1 — The Safe Harbor Structure

Under the safe-harbor framework, a transaction between the corporation and an interested director is not voidable, and does not give rise to director liability, if any of three conditions is satisfied:

1. **Disinterested director approval** (RCW 23B.08.720). The transaction is approved by a majority of the disinterested directors after full disclosure of the director's interest and the material facts of the transaction.
2. **Shareholder approval** (RCW 23B.08.730). The transaction is approved by a majority of the "qualified shares" (excluding shares held by the conflicted director or related persons) after disclosure.
3. **Fairness** (RCW 23B.08.710(2)(c)). The transaction is fair to the corporation at the time it is entered into, regardless of approval.

Any of these three is sufficient. The "disjunctive" structure means that a transaction can be defended on fairness grounds even without disinterested approval — but the burden of proving fairness rests on the director.

#### 29.2 — Shareholder Action (RCW 23B.08.730)

> "Shareholders' action respecting a transaction is effective for purposes of RCW 23B.08.710(2)(b) if a majority of the votes entitled to be cast by the holders of all qualified shares were cast in favor of the transaction after (a) notice to shareholders describing the director's conflicting interest transaction, (b) provision of the information referred to in subsection (3) of this section, and (c) required disclosure to the shareholders who voted on the transaction to the extent the information was not known by them."

**"Qualified shares."** Qualified shares are those entitled to vote on the transaction but excluding shares owned or controlled by the conflicted director or related persons. This is the "MOM" (Majority-of-Minority) concept: only disinterested shareholders count in the approval vote.

**Disclosure obligations.** A director with a conflict must inform the corporation's secretary of all shares they or related parties beneficially own before the vote. Failure to disclose can vitiate the safe harbor — unless the director proves the failure did not determine the vote outcome.

**Curative provision.** The statute includes a judicial remedy: if a vote fails to comply with the disclosure procedures, but the director can prove the noncompliance did not determine the vote outcome, the court may take appropriate action considering all the circumstances.

#### 29.3 — Business Opportunities (RCW 23B.08.735)

The corporate opportunity doctrine is a subset of the duty of loyalty: directors and officers must offer business opportunities that belong to the corporation to the corporation before taking them personally. RCW 23B.08.735 codifies and modernizes this doctrine.

> "(1) A director's or officer's taking advantage, directly or indirectly, of a business opportunity may not be the basis for an equitable, legal, or other action by or in the right of the corporation against the director or officer or related person of the director or officer, or for another sanction, on the ground that such opportunity should have first been offered to the corporation, if either: (a) Before the director, officer, or related person becomes legally obligated respecting the opportunity, the director or officer brings it to the attention of the corporation, and [action is taken by qualified directors or shareholders to disclaim interest after full disclosure]; or (b) The duty to offer the corporation the particular business opportunity or business opportunities of a specified class or category has been limited or eliminated pursuant to a provision of the articles of incorporation adopted, and where applicable, made effective by action of qualified directors, in accordance with this section."

**Two safe harbors.**

1. **Transaction-by-transaction disclosure and approval** (subsection 1(a)). Before the director takes the opportunity, the director discloses it to the corporation, and the corporation (acting through qualified directors or shareholders) disclaims the opportunity. This preserves flexibility for one-off situations.

2. **Advance categorical waiver** (subsection 1(b)). The articles of incorporation can prospectively waive the duty to offer opportunities — either generally or within specified categories. For officers (as distinct from directors), the waiver must be adopted (and made effective against the particular officer) by action of qualified directors. This was the 2020 addition (Laws of 2020, chapter 194), which brought Washington into alignment with Delaware GCL § 122(17).

**Burden of proof.** Subsection (2) provides that failure to follow the disclosure procedures "does not create an inference that the opportunity should have first been presented to the corporation or alter the burden of proof otherwise applicable." This is a plaintiff-friendly default: the corporation still bears the burden of establishing that the opportunity was, in fact, a corporate opportunity — it cannot rely on the director's procedural failure alone.

**Practical implication.** The advance-waiver provision is important for venture-capital-backed corporations, private-equity portfolio companies, and any corporation whose directors or officers have legitimate overlapping business interests. Without a charter waiver, every such director or officer faces the risk of a corporate-opportunity claim whenever they pursue an opportunity in an adjacent line of business. With a charter waiver, the risk is substantially reduced — though not eliminated, because the waiver can be limited to specified categories and cannot authorize outright breaches of the duty of loyalty.

### 30. Officers — RCW 23B.08.400

RCW 23B.08.400 establishes the officer framework:

> "(1) A corporation has the officers described in its bylaws or appointed by the board of directors in accordance with the bylaws.
> (2) The bylaws or the board of directors shall delegate to one of the officers responsibility for preparing minutes of the directors' and shareholders' meetings and for authenticating records of the corporation.
> (3) The same individual may simultaneously hold more than one office in a corporation."

**Flexibility.** The WBCA does not require any particular officer titles or structure. The bylaws (or the board in accordance with the bylaws) define the officer positions. A corporation may have a traditional "president/secretary/treasurer" structure, a modern "CEO/CFO/Secretary" structure, or something entirely bespoke.

**Minutes and records.** Section (2) requires the bylaws or board to designate an officer responsible for preparing minutes and authenticating records. Traditionally this is the "secretary," though the title is not prescribed.

**One person, multiple offices.** Section (3) expressly permits one person to hold multiple offices simultaneously. This accommodates single-founder corporations where the same person is CEO, president, secretary, and treasurer.

**Historical traditional trio.** Older corporate statutes often required a "president, secretary, and treasurer" at a minimum. Washington's modern approach dispenses with any required trio; the only substantive officer requirement is the minutes/records function. This modernization reflects the reality that officer titles matter less than functional responsibility.

**Additional officer provisions.** Related sections (RCW 23B.08.410 through .440) cover officer duties, standards of conduct (mirroring the director standards), appointment, resignation, and removal. Key points:

- **Standards of conduct.** RCW 23B.08.420 imposes on officers the same good-faith, care, and best-interests duties as directors.
- **Appointment.** Officers are appointed by the board (or pursuant to the bylaws). Officer appointment is a matter for the board, not the shareholders, in the default allocation.
- **Removal.** RCW 23B.08.440 authorizes the board to remove any officer with or without cause, and authorizes an officer to resign at any time. Removal is without prejudice to the contractual rights of the officer (so removing a CEO from the office does not extinguish the CEO's rights under an employment contract — only the corporate office is terminated).

**Practical implication.** Officer structure is highly flexible, and most Washington corporations adopt bylaws that enumerate a set of named officer positions (president, CEO, secretary, treasurer) and authorize the board to create additional positions (COO, CFO, general counsel, various vice presidents) by resolution. The board can also designate "assistant" officers (assistant secretary, assistant treasurer) to cover for the primary officers.

### 31. Indemnification Framework — RCW 23B.08.500–.600

Washington's indemnification framework is one of the more intricate portions of the WBCA. It addresses three categories of indemnification — permissive, mandatory, and shareholder-authorized — plus advancement of expenses, court-ordered indemnification, and D&O insurance.

#### 31.1 — Definitions (RCW 23B.08.500)

"Director" includes anyone serving or having served as a director, and extends to estates and personal representatives. "Expenses" includes counsel fees and related costs. "Liability" includes judgments, settlements, penalties, fines, and reasonable expenses. "Official capacity" denotes the director's office in the corporation. "Party" includes anyone sued or threatened with suit. "Proceeding" encompasses any threatened, pending, or completed action — civil, criminal, administrative, or investigative.

#### 31.2 — Permissive Indemnification (RCW 23B.08.510)

> "(1) Except as provided in subsection (4) of this section, a corporation may indemnify an individual made a party to a proceeding because the individual is or was a director against liability incurred in the proceeding if: (a) The individual acted in good faith; and (b) the individual reasonably believed: (i) In the case of conduct in the individual's official capacity with the corporation, that the individual's conduct was in its best interests; and (ii) in all other cases, that the individual's conduct was at least not opposed to its best interests; and (c) in the case of any criminal proceeding, the individual had no reasonable cause to believe the individual's conduct was unlawful."

**The three-part test.** To be indemnified permissively, the director must have (a) acted in good faith, (b) reasonably believed the conduct was in the corporation's best interests (or at least not opposed to them, for non-official-capacity actions), and (c) had no reasonable belief in criminal unlawfulness (for criminal proceedings).

**Exclusions.** Section (4) excludes indemnification (a) where the director was adjudged liable to the corporation, or (b) where the director was adjudged to have received an improper personal benefit — except that reasonable defense expenses can still be indemnified in derivative suits.

#### 31.3 — Mandatory Indemnification (RCW 23B.08.520)

> "Unless limited by its articles of incorporation, a corporation shall indemnify a director who was wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise, in the defense of any proceeding to which the director was a party because of being a director of the corporation against reasonable expenses incurred by the director in connection with the proceeding."

**"Wholly successful."** If the director prevails completely — by judgment, settlement, dismissal, or any other resolution that does not result in liability — the corporation MUST indemnify. The articles can opt out, but they rarely do. Partial success (e.g., prevailing on some claims but losing on others) does not trigger mandatory indemnification, though permissive indemnification remains available.

**Expenses only.** Mandatory indemnification covers "reasonable expenses" — not judgments or settlements. The rationale is that a director who successfully defends a claim should not be personally burdened with the costs of defense; but if there is no judgment or settlement against the director, there are no costs to indemnify beyond expenses.

#### 31.4 — Advancement of Expenses (RCW 23B.08.530)

Advance payment of expenses is authorized subject to certain conditions, most importantly the director's undertaking to repay if it is ultimately determined that the director was not entitled to indemnification. Advancement is critical in practice because the defense of a significant lawsuit can easily cost hundreds of thousands of dollars before any determination is made on the merits, and a director who must front these costs personally may be effectively denied the ability to mount a defense.

#### 31.5 — Court-Ordered Indemnification (RCW 23B.08.540)

If the corporation refuses to indemnify a director (or refuses to advance expenses), the director may petition a court for an order of indemnification. The court may order indemnification if it finds that the director is entitled under the statute and that denial would be unfair.

#### 31.6 — Shareholder-Approved Indemnification (RCW 23B.08.560)

> "If authorized by the articles of incorporation, a bylaw adopted or ratified by shareholders, or a resolution adopted or ratified, before or after the event, by the shareholders, a corporation shall have the power to indemnify a director made a party to a proceeding, or obligate itself to advance or reimburse expenses incurred in a proceeding, without regard to the limitations in RCW 23B.08.510 through 23B.08.550, except that no such indemnity shall indemnify any director from or on account of: (a) Acts or omissions finally adjudged to be intentional misconduct or a knowing violation of law; (b) conduct finally adjudged to be in violation of RCW 23B.08.310; or (c) any transaction with respect to which it was finally adjudged that such director personally received a benefit in money, property, or services to which the director was not legally entitled."

**Broader scope.** Shareholder-approved indemnification reaches beyond the "good faith" and "reasonable belief" limits of permissive indemnification. A corporation whose articles, bylaws, or shareholder resolutions authorize broader indemnification may indemnify without regard to the subjective good-faith inquiry.

**Three exceptions.** The broad indemnification cannot cover (a) intentional misconduct or knowing violations of law, (b) unlawful-distribution liability, or (c) transactions from which the director received improper personal benefits. These are the same exceptions that apply to director exculpation under RCW 23B.08.320 — reflecting a consistent Washington policy that certain categories of wrongdoing cannot be indemnified or exculpated.

#### 31.7 — D&O Insurance (RCW 23B.08.570)

A Washington corporation is expressly authorized to purchase directors' and officers' liability insurance. D&O insurance is effectively universal in modern corporate practice, because the limits of statutory indemnification (plus the creditworthiness of the corporation itself, which may be questionable in the situations where indemnification is most needed) make corporate-funded defense and judgment payment uncertain. D&O insurance pays regardless of the corporation's solvency and covers claims that the corporation cannot indemnify (such as derivative-suit settlements paid to the corporation itself).

**Practical implication.** Every Washington corporation that takes director service seriously should adopt mandatory indemnification in its articles or bylaws, authorize advancement of expenses, and purchase D&O insurance with adequate limits. The three layers — statutory indemnification, contractual (article/bylaw) indemnification, and D&O insurance — work in concert to protect directors from personal exposure.

### 32. Resignation — RCW 23B.08.070

Director resignation is governed by RCW 23B.08.070, already discussed at Section 22.3 above. To recap:

- A director may resign at any time by delivering written notice to the board, its chairperson, the president, or the secretary.
- The resignation is effective as provided in the general effectiveness rule (RCW 23B.01.410(9)) unless the notice specifies a delayed date.
- Conditional resignations — including "irrevocable" resignations triggered by failing to receive a specified vote — are expressly authorized.

The statute does not require the resigning director to state a reason. Nor does it impose any procedural steps on the corporation — the resignation is effective upon delivery, without need for acceptance by the board.

**Board composition after resignation.** A resignation may cause a board vacancy (which is then filled under RCW 23B.08.100) or may reduce the board to a number below the stated minimum (in which case the remaining directors must promptly fill the vacancies). A resignation may also trigger the emergency bylaws (RCW 23B.02.070) if it leaves the board unable to assemble a quorum — though in ordinary circumstances, directors time their resignations to avoid this result.

**Officer resignation.** Officer resignation is governed by RCW 23B.08.440, which similarly permits an officer to resign at any time upon notice. As with directors, the resignation is effective upon delivery (or at a specified later date).

## Appendix A: Integrated Commentary on the Incorporation Process

The individual sections above treat each RCW provision as a discrete unit, but in practice the formation of a Washington corporation is a single integrated workflow in which the statutory provisions interlock. This appendix walks through the workflow end to end, identifying the decision points, the document hierarchy, and the statutory hooks at each step.

### A.1 — Pre-Formation Planning

Before any document is filed, the founders must resolve a series of planning questions that will shape the formation documents. These questions include:

**1. Entity form.** Is a corporation the correct choice, or is a limited liability company (LLC) under Chapter 25.15 RCW more appropriate? Corporations offer mature governance machinery, a well-understood share structure, and an established path to institutional financing; LLCs offer pass-through tax treatment by default, greater flexibility in governance, and fewer formalities. For businesses targeting venture-capital financing, a C-corporation is typically required because VC funds generally cannot accept flow-through entities as portfolio investments. For operating businesses not seeking outside capital, an LLC is often preferable.

**2. State of incorporation.** Should the corporation be formed in Washington or in Delaware? For a Washington business with all operations in Washington and no near-term plans for public-market access, Washington incorporation is usually the better choice: it avoids the complexity of foreign qualification, the administrative overhead of maintaining a Delaware registered agent, and the Delaware franchise tax. For a corporation that anticipates raising institutional capital or going public, Delaware is often preferred because of the depth of Delaware case law, the specialization of the Chancery Court, and the familiarity of venture and public-market counsel with Delaware procedures.

**3. Share structure.** Will the corporation have a single class of common stock, or multiple classes with differential voting and economic rights? For a simple closely held corporation, a single class of common stock is almost always correct. For venture-capital-ready corporations, a structure with authorized common stock and authorized "blank check" preferred stock (enabling future financing rounds without further charter amendments) is standard.

**4. Authorized share count.** How many shares should the corporation be authorized to issue? Too few creates friction for future issuances; too many can (in some state tax regimes) generate franchise-tax exposure, though this is not a concern in Washington. A typical starting point is 10 million authorized shares of common stock, with some portion (say, 8 million) actually issued to founders and the balance reserved for future equity incentive plans and financing.

**5. Initial capitalization.** How will the founders acquire their shares — by contributing cash, services, intellectual property, or a combination? Each form of contribution has tax implications (particularly the risk of phantom taxable income under § 83 of the Internal Revenue Code for services-based contributions) that should be analyzed with tax counsel before issuance.

**6. Governance structure.** How many directors? One or multiple officers? Will there be a shareholder agreement, and if so, what will it cover? Will the articles include exculpation and mandatory indemnification? Will the bylaws address emergency governance and forum selection?

**7. Jurisdictional and regulatory overlays.** Is the business in a regulated industry (banking, insurance, telecommunications, healthcare, professional services) that imposes additional formation requirements? Does the corporation need to obtain licenses, register with professional boards, or comply with specialized statutes in addition to the WBCA?

These planning questions should be resolved before any formation documents are drafted. A corporation formed without thinking through these issues can later be restructured, but restructuring is almost always more expensive and more complicated than getting it right the first time.

### A.2 — The Document Cascade

A typical Washington corporate formation produces a cascade of documents, each dependent on the ones above it:

**1. Articles of incorporation.** The foundational document. Filed with the Secretary of State. Contains the mandatory content of RCW 23B.02.020(1) and any selected optional content from 23B.02.020(2).

**2. Statement of consent of registered agent.** Required to establish that the named registered agent has consented to the appointment. Typically a one-page form signed by the agent or, for commercial registered agents, pre-filed with the Secretary of State.

**3. Action by incorporator.** A written consent (or meeting minutes) by the incorporator adopting the bylaws, electing initial directors, and ratifying the formation. If initial directors were named in the articles, this step may be shortened or skipped.

**4. Bylaws.** Adopted by the incorporator or by the initial board. Governs the internal procedures of the corporation.

**5. Action by initial board.** The first board meeting (or written consent) that elects officers, authorizes the issuance of founder shares, opens a bank account, ratifies pre-incorporation activities, adopts a fiscal year, authorizes the § 83(b) election reminder, adopts indemnification agreements, and takes other organizational actions.

**6. Stock certificates and stock ledger.** If the corporation issues certificated shares, certificates are prepared and delivered to shareholders. A stock ledger (either paper or electronic) tracks all share issuances and transfers.

**7. Founder stock purchase agreements.** Each founder executes a stock purchase agreement reflecting the shares issued, the purchase price (which may be par value, nominal, or the fair value of the consideration), and — critically — any vesting provisions subjecting the founder's shares to a repurchase right tied to continued service. Founder vesting is standard in venture-backable corporations and is an important tool for managing the risk of early-founder departures.

**8. Section 83(b) elections.** Founders who receive restricted stock (i.e., shares subject to vesting) should file a § 83(b) election with the IRS within 30 days of issuance. This election makes the shares immediately taxable at their current (low) value, avoiding the risk of phantom taxable income as the shares vest later at higher values.

**9. Initial report.** Filed with the Secretary of State within 120 days of incorporation, per RCW 23.95.255.

**10. Shareholder agreement (if applicable).** A separate written agreement among the shareholders and the corporation under RCW 23B.07.320, addressing governance, transfer restrictions, buy-sell provisions, drag-along rights, and other matters.

**11. Indemnification agreements.** Separate contractual agreements between the corporation and each director and officer, providing contractual indemnification rights that supplement the statutory framework.

**12. Bank account resolutions.** Formal corporate resolutions authorizing the opening of bank accounts and designating authorized signatories.

**13. Federal EIN application.** Form SS-4 filed with the IRS to obtain an employer identification number. This is not strictly part of Washington corporate law but is a universal step in corporate formation.

**14. State tax registration.** Registration with the Washington Department of Revenue for business and occupation (B&O) tax, sales tax (if applicable), and other state tax obligations.

**15. Local licenses.** City and county business licenses, plus any industry-specific local permits.

A sophisticated formation is thus substantially more than just "filing the articles." The articles are the foundational legal document, but the full document cascade is what actually operationalizes the corporation.

### A.3 — Timing Considerations

Corporate formation can be completed in as little as one day (using expedited filing) or stretched over weeks depending on the complexity of the entity, the availability of signatories, and the speed of the Secretary of State's processing queue.

**Same-day filing.** Washington offers same-day expedited filing for an additional fee. Articles filed before the Secretary of State's internal cutoff are accepted and returned that day.

**Standard filing.** Non-expedited filing typically clears in two to five business days.

**Delayed effective date.** As noted in Section 4, the articles may specify a delayed effective date of up to 90 days in the future. This is useful for year-end formations where the founders want documents in the system before year-end but effect on January 1.

**Back-timing.** Washington does not permit back-dating of corporate existence. A corporation comes into existence on the effective date of the articles — never earlier. Any "back-dated" documents (contracts, employment agreements, equity issuances) before the effective date are subject to the preincorporation-liability regime of RCW 23B.02.040.

### A.4 — Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Based on patterns observed in Washington corporate practice, the most common formation mistakes include:

**1. Filing with the wrong corporate name.** Founders sometimes file articles with a name that is technically available under the Secretary of State's distinguishability standard but conflicts with a registered trademark or an existing domain name. This creates an expensive renaming problem later. *Cure:* conduct trademark and domain searches before filing.

**2. Naming the registered agent incorrectly.** Founders sometimes name themselves as registered agent without realizing that this puts their home address on the public record. *Cure:* use a commercial registered agent for privacy.

**3. Omitting founder vesting.** Founders sometimes issue themselves fully vested stock at formation, without any repurchase right on departure. This creates problems later when one founder leaves and retains a large equity stake that no longer reflects ongoing contribution. *Cure:* implement standard four-year vesting with one-year cliffs from day one.

**4. Missing the § 83(b) election deadline.** Founders who receive restricted stock but fail to file § 83(b) elections within 30 days of issuance face potentially enormous tax consequences if the stock appreciates significantly before vesting. *Cure:* make § 83(b) filing part of the formation checklist, with calendar reminders.

**5. Failing to issue founder shares.** Surprisingly common: founders complete all the formation steps except the actual issuance of shares to themselves. This leaves the corporation technically without shareholders, which creates major complications if the corporation later needs to hold a shareholder vote. *Cure:* include share issuance in the initial board action and document it explicitly.

**6. Ignoring the initial report requirement.** The initial report under RCW 23.95.255 is easy to forget because it comes due months after the articles are filed. Missing the initial report can result in administrative dissolution. *Cure:* calendar the deadline at the time of formation and add it to the corporation's compliance calendar.

**7. Failing to adopt bylaws.** The organizational written consent sometimes omits bylaw adoption, leaving the corporation without operational procedures. *Cure:* adopt bylaws simultaneously with the organizational action.

**8. Mismatched share structure.** Founders sometimes authorize too few shares (making future stock issuances impossible without a charter amendment) or fail to authorize preferred stock (requiring a charter amendment at the first venture round, with associated legal fees). *Cure:* consult with experienced corporate counsel before deciding the initial share structure.

**9. Overlooking the shareholder agreement.** Multi-founder corporations sometimes omit a shareholder agreement entirely, relying on default corporate law. Default corporate law does not address founder buyouts, right-of-first-refusal on transfers, drag-along rights, or many other matters that sophisticated founders want to address. *Cure:* execute a shareholder agreement under RCW 23B.07.320 at the time of formation.

**10. Forgetting the registered agent consent.** The Secretary of State requires a consent from the registered agent. Without it, the articles may be rejected. *Cure:* obtain the consent in writing before filing.

## Appendix B: Deep Dive on the Business Judgment Rule

### B.1 — The Function of the Business Judgment Rule

The business judgment rule ("BJR") is a judicial doctrine of deference. Its function is to prevent courts from second-guessing the substantive merits of corporate business decisions. Without the BJR, every losing business decision by a corporate board could become the subject of a shareholder-plaintiff lawsuit alleging negligence, with courts called upon to assess whether the board's judgment was "correct" in hindsight. The BJR takes this judicial supervisory role off the table: if the board acted in good faith, on an informed basis, and in the honest belief that its decision was in the corporation's best interests, courts will not substitute their judgment for the board's — even if the decision turned out badly.

This deference is critical to the functioning of the corporate form. Directors are, after all, ordinary human beings (with rare exceptions) who have agreed to serve on boards for modest compensation. If every business decision they made could become the subject of personal liability under a hindsight-driven negligence standard, no competent person would serve on a board. The BJR preserves the willingness of qualified individuals to accept board service.

### B.2 — The Four Elements

Washington case law, together with the general American corporate law tradition, identifies four elements of BJR protection:

**1. A business decision.** The BJR protects decisions, not inaction. A board that simply fails to act is not protected by the BJR; rather, it may be protected by the duty of care's "reasonable inquiry" standard or by exculpation under RCW 23B.08.320. But the classic BJR is a doctrine about affirmative decisions: mergers, acquisitions, contract approvals, capital allocations, and similar active choices.

**2. Good faith.** The director must have acted with honest intent, without fraudulent or corrupt motive. A director who approves a transaction while harboring a secret plan to benefit personally at the corporation's expense is not acting in good faith and does not enjoy BJR protection.

**3. An informed basis.** The director must have made reasonable inquiry into the material facts before deciding. The quintessential BJR failure case is *Smith v. Van Gorkom*, the Delaware decision that led to the adoption of § 102(b)(7) exculpation statutes across the country. In *Van Gorkom*, the board approved a merger at a $55-per-share price after a two-hour meeting at which the CEO presented the deal without documentation and the board did not inquire into how the $55 price had been derived. The Delaware Supreme Court held that the board failed the "informed basis" prong and was not protected by the BJR, even though the deal turned out to be financially acceptable. The lesson: a board must do enough homework to establish that its decision was reasoned, even if the decision is ultimately correct.

**4. An honest belief that the decision was in the best interests of the corporation.** The director must actually have believed that the decision served the corporation's interests. A director who disagrees with a decision but goes along for political reasons may not enjoy BJR protection if the decision is later challenged.

### B.3 — What the BJR Does Not Protect

The BJR is a robust doctrine of deference, but it has limits:

**1. Loyalty breaches.** The BJR does not protect against duty-of-loyalty claims — claims that the director had a conflict of interest, engaged in self-dealing, or took a corporate opportunity. These claims are reviewed under the "entire fairness" standard, not the BJR's deferential standard.

**2. Bad faith.** The BJR does not protect directors who acted with a corrupt motive, or who acted without actually caring about the corporation's interests.

**3. Illegality.** The BJR does not protect directors who knowingly cause the corporation to violate the law. Even a well-informed, good-faith decision cannot be protected if the underlying conduct is illegal.

**4. Gross negligence.** Washington case law, like Delaware case law, recognizes that "gross negligence" may breach the duty of care in a way that defeats BJR protection. The line between ordinary negligence (protected) and gross negligence (not protected) is not always clearly drawn, but the paradigmatic gross-negligence case is one in which the board failed to inform itself at all about a material decision.

**5. Waste of corporate assets.** The BJR does not protect decisions that constitute "waste" — transactions so one-sided that no reasonable person could believe they were in the corporation's interest. The waste doctrine is rarely successful because its threshold is extraordinarily high, but it remains theoretically available.

### B.4 — The BJR as a Presumption

In Delaware and in many Washington opinions, the BJR is framed as a presumption: courts presume that directors acted in good faith, on an informed basis, and in the honest belief that their decision was in the best interests of the corporation. The plaintiff bears the burden of rebutting the presumption. If the plaintiff cannot plead specific facts showing a failure of one of the BJR elements, the court dismisses the complaint without reaching the substantive merits of the board's decision.

This presumption-and-burden-shifting structure is crucial to the BJR's protective function. Without it, a plaintiff could force a full trial merely by alleging that the decision was bad — and the cost and distraction of trial might effectively deter directors from making any risky decisions, no matter how necessary those decisions were to the business.

Washington case law is not as explicit as Delaware's about the presumption framing, but the practical effect is similar. A Washington plaintiff who cannot plead specific facts showing a BJR failure faces a dismissal motion and an uphill battle.

### B.5 — Interaction with Exculpation

RCW 23B.08.320 exculpation adds another layer of protection. Even if the BJR presumption is rebutted — for example, because the plaintiff successfully pleads that the board was grossly negligent in failing to inform itself — the exculpation provision in the articles may still prevent monetary recovery. Exculpation does not prevent the case from going forward (the plaintiff may still seek equitable relief such as rescission of a transaction), but it blocks the personal monetary liability that is the primary risk for directors.

### B.6 — Interaction with Indemnification and D&O Insurance

Even when neither the BJR nor exculpation saves the director, indemnification and D&O insurance may cover the resulting liability. Indemnification (Chapter 23B.08, Sections 500-600) is a corporate obligation to pay the director's defense costs and — in some circumstances — the director's judgments and settlements. D&O insurance is a third-party payment source that applies even when the corporation cannot pay indemnification (e.g., because the corporation is insolvent).

The stacked protections — BJR, exculpation, indemnification, insurance — are sometimes collectively called the "director's shield." Together they de-risk director service to the point where the primary remaining risks are reputational rather than financial.

### B.7 — The BJR in Closely Held Corporations

Washington's BJR cases overwhelmingly arise in the closely held corporation context (for example, *Nursing Home Building Corp. v. DeHart*, *Interlake Porsche + Audi v. Bucholz*, *Para-Medical Leasing v. Hangen*). This is partly because closely held corporations are the dominant form of Washington business corporation, and partly because closely held corporations are more prone to the factual patterns (self-dealing, disputes among founder-shareholders, breakups of family businesses) that give rise to director-liability litigation.

In the closely held context, the BJR operates somewhat differently than in the public-company context. Because closely held corporations often have concentrated ownership, overlapping officer/director/shareholder roles, and informal governance procedures, disputes often involve allegations of self-dealing and conflict of interest rather than pure business-judgment failures. Washington courts tend to focus on the loyalty dimension of director conduct in closely held cases — *did the director take an improper personal benefit?* — more than on the care dimension. The BJR remains important but is often a secondary consideration.

### B.8 — The Richins Critique Revisited

Adam Richins' 2005 Washington Law Review article *Risky Business* argues that the Washington Supreme Court has, in dicta, suggested that the ordinary-prudent-person standard of RCW 23B.08.300(1)(b) is an element of the BJR's liability standard. Richins critiques this formulation on the ground that it blurs the distinction between the "standard of conduct" (what directors should do) and the "standard of liability" (when directors can be held responsible).

The Richins distinction is worth elaborating. Under the MBCA's intended structure:

- **Standard of conduct (RCW 23B.08.300).** Directors should act in good faith, with the care of an ordinarily prudent person, and in the reasonable belief that their conduct is in the corporation's best interests. This is the aspirational standard — the target directors should aim for.

- **Standard of liability (the BJR).** Directors are liable to the corporation or its shareholders only if they (a) acted in bad faith, (b) engaged in self-dealing, (c) engaged in gross negligence (failure to inform themselves at all), or (d) acted with actual knowledge of illegality. Ordinary negligence — failure to meet the standard of conduct — is not sufficient to establish liability.

Under this two-tier structure, a director can fail the standard of conduct (by acting negligently) without incurring liability (because the BJR's higher standard of liability is not met). This is a deliberate policy choice: the standard of conduct encourages good decision-making, while the higher standard of liability ensures that directors are not deterred from taking reasonable business risks by the threat of negligence suits.

Richins argues that the Washington Supreme Court's dicta conflates these two standards, and that Washington courts should be more explicit about distinguishing them. Whether or not one agrees with Richins' normative argument, the distinction is analytically useful for practitioners trying to understand the scope of director-liability exposure in Washington.

## Appendix C: The Washington Shareholder Agreement as a Planning Tool

### C.1 — Why the Shareholder Agreement Matters

RCW 23B.07.320 is arguably the most powerful planning provision in the Washington Business Corporation Act. It allows shareholders to contract around default corporate law — even mandatory default rules — in ways that no other provision permits. A properly drafted shareholder agreement under 23B.07.320 can:

- Eliminate the board of directors entirely.
- Specify who shall be directors, with multi-year guaranteed terms.
- Establish non-pro-rata distributions.
- Freeze officer positions and compensation.
- Pre-authorize related-party transactions.
- Create mandatory dissolution triggers.
- Transfer management authority from directors to specified shareholders.

No other provision of corporate law offers this degree of flexibility. The shareholder agreement thus deserves careful consideration in the formation of any closely held Washington corporation with more than one shareholder.

### C.2 — Formal Requirements Revisited

As noted in Section 19, the formal requirements for a valid 23B.07.320 agreement are:

1. Written agreement
2. Executed by all shareholders at the time of execution
3. Made known to the corporation
4. Noted conspicuously on share certificates or information statements
5. Amendable only by unanimous shareholder consent (unless otherwise specified)

These requirements are strict, and failure to comply can render the agreement unenforceable under the statute (though it may remain enforceable as an ordinary contract among the parties). The "conspicuous notation" requirement is particularly important — a future purchaser of shares cannot be bound unless they had notice (constructive or actual) of the agreement.

### C.3 — Typical Provisions

A sophisticated Washington shareholder agreement might include:

**Management provisions.**
- Board composition (e.g., three directors, with one appointed by each of three founders).
- Director election by class voting, guaranteeing each founder a seat.
- Supermajority approval required for certain fundamental actions.
- Veto rights for certain shareholders on certain actions.

**Transfer restrictions.**
- Right of first refusal (ROFR): if a shareholder wants to sell to a third party, the other shareholders (or the corporation) have the right to purchase at the same price.
- Right of first offer (ROFO): a selling shareholder must first offer to sell to the other shareholders before approaching third parties.
- Drag-along rights: if a majority of shareholders want to sell the corporation to a buyer, they can force the minority to join.
- Tag-along rights: if a majority shareholder wants to sell, minority shareholders can require the buyer to purchase their shares on the same terms.
- Outright prohibition on transfers except to permitted transferees (spouses, trusts for family members, etc.).

**Buy-sell provisions.**
- Mandatory buyback on death, disability, or termination of employment.
- Formula-based valuation (book value, EBITDA multiple, third-party appraisal).
- Payment terms (lump sum vs. installment).
- Insurance funding for mandatory buybacks.

**Capital provisions.**
- Capital call rights: mandatory follow-on investment by existing shareholders.
- Anti-dilution protections.
- Pre-emptive rights: existing shareholders have the right to maintain their percentage ownership in future issuances.

**Exit provisions.**
- Initial public offering (IPO) triggers that terminate the agreement.
- Mandatory dissolution on specified events.
- Deadlock resolution procedures.

**Dispute resolution.**
- Mediation clauses.
- Governing law (typically Washington).
- Forum selection (subject to the limits of RCW 23B.02.080).

### C.4 — The Shareholder Agreement vs. the Bylaws

A common question in formation: should a particular rule go in the bylaws or in the shareholder agreement? The answer depends on how entrenched the rule should be and who can change it.

**Bylaws** can be amended by the board alone (unless the articles reserve amendment to the shareholders). This makes bylaws flexible but vulnerable to board-majority changes.

**Shareholder agreements** can be amended only by unanimous shareholder consent (unless the agreement itself specifies a lower threshold). This makes them rigid but impenetrable to board manipulation.

As a general rule:

- **Operational procedures** (meeting notice requirements, officer titles, record-keeping rules) belong in the bylaws, where they can be updated efficiently as the corporation evolves.
- **Governance bargains among founders** (who will be directors, how shares transfer, when the corporation dissolves) belong in the shareholder agreement, where they are protected from majoritarian revision.

### C.5 — Termination and Sunset

Shareholder agreements under 23B.07.320 typically contain termination provisions that specify the end of the agreement. Common termination events include:

- **IPO.** The agreement terminates when the corporation becomes a public company. This is actually mandated by the statute in most interpretations: the 23B.07.320 regime is incompatible with the open-market trading of public shares.
- **Acquisition.** The agreement terminates when the corporation is acquired by a third party.
- **Dissolution.** The agreement terminates when the corporation is dissolved.
- **Unanimous agreement to terminate.** The shareholders can always agree to end the agreement.
- **Expiration.** The agreement may include a specified end date or a rolling renewal provision.

Well-drafted agreements specify what happens to outstanding obligations (e.g., unpaid buyback obligations) after termination. Poorly drafted agreements can leave residual obligations in limbo.

### C.6 — The Case for Every Closely Held Corporation

Every closely held Washington corporation with more than one shareholder should at least consider a 23B.07.320 agreement. The marginal cost of drafting one at the time of formation is modest (a few hours of attorney time for a straightforward agreement), and the benefit is substantial: founder-shareholders can entrench the governance bargain they actually agreed upon, rather than relying on default corporate law that may diverge from their expectations.

Corporations that start without a shareholder agreement often find, years later, that they need one — typically when a founder wants to leave, when a dispute arises, or when a new investor demands one. At that point, negotiating the terms is far more difficult because the parties' interests have diverged. The lesson: get it done at the start, when everyone still agrees and the stakes feel low.

## Appendix D: Comparative Notes on Washington vs. Delaware for Closely Held Corporations

### D.1 — Why Founders Choose Delaware

Delaware is the dominant state of incorporation for publicly held and venture-backed companies. The reasons are well understood:

**1. Depth of case law.** Delaware has by far the most developed body of corporate case law in the United States. Almost every conceivable corporate-governance question has been addressed in Delaware precedent, giving attorneys and directors clearer guidance on risk.

**2. The Court of Chancery.** Delaware's specialized corporate court handles matters faster and with greater expertise than general-jurisdiction courts in other states. Decisions are typically rendered by judges with deep corporate-law experience.

**3. Predictability.** Investors and their counsel know Delaware law. A Delaware corporation can be understood quickly by a New York, California, or Texas investor without additional research into local quirks.

**4. Flexibility.** The Delaware General Corporation Law permits a wide range of charter customizations, including forum-selection clauses that channel all internal disputes to Delaware Chancery, broad exculpation, and categorical corporate-opportunity waivers.

**5. Institutional signaling.** Venture capitalists and institutional investors expect Delaware incorporation. A Washington corporation seeking VC funding will often be asked to reincorporate in Delaware as a condition of investment.

### D.2 — Why Founders Might Stay in Washington

Despite Delaware's dominance, Washington incorporation has advantages for certain founders:

**1. Cost.** Washington avoids the Delaware franchise tax (which can be substantial for corporations with large numbers of authorized shares) and the Delaware registered agent fee. For a small closely held corporation, annual Delaware compliance costs can run $300-$800; Washington compliance is comparable but without the franchise-tax variability.

**2. Local jurisdiction.** A Washington corporation is subject to Washington courts for internal disputes. This is an advantage for founders who have confidence in Washington state courts and who do not want to litigate corporate disputes in Delaware.

**3. Single-state simplicity.** A Washington business that operates only in Washington and incorporates in Delaware must foreign-qualify in Washington, creating two sets of annual reports, two registered agents, and two regulatory touch-points. Washington incorporation for a Washington-only business eliminates this duplication.

**4. No intention to IPO.** Most closely held businesses will never become public companies, never raise institutional capital, and never need the depth of Delaware corporate law. For such businesses, the marginal benefit of Delaware incorporation is negligible.

**5. Charter flexibility via 23B.07.320.** The Washington shareholder agreement mechanism offers a degree of governance flexibility that some Delaware corporations can achieve only through the separate "close corporation" regime under DGCL §§ 341-356 (which is rarely used in modern Delaware practice).

### D.3 — The Reincorporation Question

Many founders grapple with the question: "Should I form in Washington now and reincorporate in Delaware later, or form in Delaware from the start?"

**Form now in Delaware if:**
- Institutional financing is imminent or likely within 12 months.
- The founders already have experience with Delaware corporate practice.
- The corporation's business model inherently targets national or international markets where Delaware's familiarity is important.

**Form now in Washington if:**
- The corporation will be primarily a Washington operating business.
- Institutional financing is speculative or distant.
- The founders want to minimize formation complexity and cost.
- The corporation expects to remain closely held for the foreseeable future.

**The reincorporation mechanics.** A Washington-to-Delaware reincorporation is typically accomplished by merging the Washington corporation into a newly formed Delaware corporation. The merger is a taxable event at the corporate level (though generally not at the shareholder level if properly structured as a Type F reorganization) and requires shareholder approval. The cost of reincorporation — legal fees, filing fees, tax analysis — can run $10,000 to $50,000 depending on complexity. If reincorporation is likely within the first year or two of operation, the cost and disruption of reincorporation may exceed the savings from initially forming in Washington.

### D.4 — Reverse Reincorporation: Delaware to Washington

Rare but not unheard of: some corporations reincorporate from Delaware back to Washington after a change in business circumstances (e.g., abandoning public-market ambitions, selling the business, or simplifying the governance structure after a founder buyout). The mechanics are analogous to Washington-to-Delaware reincorporation — a merger of the Delaware corporation into a newly formed Washington corporation. The tax and cost considerations are similar.

## Appendix F: Glossary of Key Terms

**Articles of Incorporation.** The foundational charter document of a Washington corporation, filed with the Secretary of State under RCW 23B.02.020. Also called the "charter" or "certificate of incorporation" in other jurisdictions.

**Authorized Shares.** The maximum number of shares a corporation is permitted to issue. Set in the articles of incorporation and changed only by charter amendment.

**Bylaws.** The internal operating rules of a corporation, adopted by the incorporator or the initial board under RCW 23B.02.060. Bylaws govern procedural matters such as meeting procedures, officer duties, and committee structures.

**Board of Directors.** The body charged with managing the corporation under RCW 23B.08.010.

**Business Judgment Rule.** A judicial doctrine of deference to the substantive business decisions of corporate directors who have acted in good faith, on an informed basis, and in the honest belief that their decision was in the corporation's best interests.

**CCFS.** The Washington Secretary of State's Corporations and Charities Filing System — the online portal for corporate filings.

**Conflict of Interest.** A situation in which a director's personal interest (or the interest of a related person) diverges from the interests of the corporation. Addressed under RCW 23B.08.700–.740.

**Corporate Opportunity.** A business opportunity that, under fiduciary principles, a director or officer is obligated to offer to the corporation before taking personally. Governed by RCW 23B.08.735.

**Cumulative Voting.** A voting system in which shareholders can allocate their total votes among candidates for director as they choose (including concentrating all votes on a single candidate). Designed to protect minority shareholders by allowing them to elect at least some directors. Governed by RCW 23B.07.260.

**Derivative Action.** A lawsuit brought by a shareholder on behalf of the corporation, asserting claims that belong to the corporation but that the corporation has failed to pursue. The classic vehicle for challenging director misconduct.

**D&O Insurance.** Directors and officers liability insurance. A third-party insurance policy that covers personal liability of directors and officers for claims arising from their corporate service.

**Duty of Care.** The obligation of a director to act with the care of an ordinarily prudent person in a like position. Codified at RCW 23B.08.300(1)(b).

**Duty of Loyalty.** The obligation of a director to act in the best interests of the corporation, not in the director's own interest. Not explicitly codified but inherent in the "best interests" prong of RCW 23B.08.300(1)(c) and reinforced by the conflict-of-interest provisions.

**Exculpation.** A charter provision limiting the monetary liability of directors for breach of the duty of care. Authorized by RCW 23B.08.320.

**Fiduciary Duty.** The general obligation of loyalty and care that directors and officers owe to the corporation and its shareholders.

**Forum Selection.** A charter or bylaw provision specifying the court(s) in which internal corporate disputes must be litigated. Authorized by RCW 23B.02.080.

**Incorporator.** The person who signs and delivers the articles of incorporation. Governed by RCW 23B.02.010.

**Indemnification.** Corporate payment of a director's or officer's expenses, judgments, and settlements arising from legal proceedings. Governed by RCW 23B.08.500–.600.

**Par Value.** A nominal minimum price at which shares may be issued. Largely vestigial in modern corporate practice.

**Proxy.** An authorization to vote shares on behalf of a shareholder. Governed by RCW 23B.07.220.

**Quorum.** The minimum number of voting power that must be represented at a meeting for valid action. For shareholders, a majority by default (RCW 23B.07.250); for directors, a majority by default (RCW 23B.08.240).

**Record Date.** The cutoff date for determining which shareholders are entitled to notice of and to vote at a meeting. Governed by RCW 23B.07.070.

**Registered Agent.** A person or entity designated to receive service of process and official notices on behalf of the corporation. Governed by RCW 23.95 Article 4.

**Registered Office.** The Washington street address of the registered agent.

**Shareholder Agreement.** A contract among the shareholders (and the corporation) governing corporate affairs. A 23B.07.320 agreement has special statutory effect that overrides inconsistent provisions of the articles, bylaws, or Title 23B itself.

**Special Meeting.** A shareholder meeting called for a specific purpose other than the annual election of directors. Governed by RCW 23B.07.020.

**Staggered Board.** A board of directors divided into classes with overlapping multi-year terms, so that only a subset of directors stands for reelection at each annual meeting. Authorized by RCW 23B.08.060.

**Ultra Vires.** Beyond the powers of the corporation. Largely obsolete as a doctrine in modern corporate law but still relevant when a corporation's articles narrow its purpose.

**UBOC.** The Washington Uniform Business Organizations Code, codified at Chapter 23.95 RCW. Supplies the common name and registered agent framework for all Washington business entities.

**Voting Group.** A class or series of shares entitled to vote separately on a matter. Each voting group has its own quorum and its own approval threshold.

**Wholly Successful.** A term of art in the indemnification context (RCW 23B.08.520). A director is "wholly successful" in defending a proceeding if the director prevails completely — by judgment, settlement, dismissal, or any other resolution that does not result in personal liability.

**WBCA.** The Washington Business Corporation Act, codified at Title 23B of the Revised Code of Washington.

**Written Consent.** An action taken by signed writings rather than at a meeting. Permitted for shareholders (23B.07.040) and directors (23B.08.210), though with different unanimity requirements.

## Appendix G: Statutory Amendment History — Selected Highlights

The Washington Business Corporation Act has been amended regularly since its 1989 enactment. This appendix highlights a few of the most significant amendments affecting formation and governance.

### 1989 — Original Enactment (Chapter 165, Laws of 1989)

The original WBCA was a wholesale replacement of the 1965 Business Corporation Act. The drafters worked from the RMBCA template and substantially modernized Washington corporate law. Key changes from the 1965 Act included:

- Reducing the incorporator requirement from three to one.
- Adopting the "one or more persons" formulation.
- Eliminating the distinction between common-law and statutory corporations.
- Modernizing the director standard of conduct to track the RMBCA's good-faith/care/best-interests framework.
- Introducing the shareholder agreement mechanism of RCW 23B.07.320.
- Updating the indemnification framework to provide clearer mandatory and permissive categories.
- Introducing the director exculpation provision of RCW 23B.08.320.

The 1989 Act was a major overhaul and is the foundation on which all subsequent amendments build.

### 2002 — Amendments to Shareholder Meeting Provisions

Laws of 2002 chapter 297 updated various shareholder meeting provisions to modernize notice, voting, and consent procedures. Among the changes was the introduction of more explicit provisions for electronic notices and proxies.

### 2009 — Technology Amendments

Laws of 2009 chapter 189 further modernized the provisions governing electronic communication, including clarification that board and shareholder meetings may be held through electronic means.

### 2015 — UBOC Integration

Laws of 2015 chapter 176 integrated Title 23B with the new Uniform Business Organizations Code (Chapter 23.95 RCW). This was a significant structural change: the name, registered agent, and filing provisions that had previously lived inside Title 23B were moved to the UBOC, and Title 23B was revised to cross-reference the UBOC provisions. The substantive rules did not change dramatically, but the location of the authoritative text moved.

### 2017 — Forum Selection (Chapter 28)

Laws of 2017 chapter 28 section 9 added RCW 23B.02.080, authorizing forum-selection provisions in articles and bylaws. This aligned Washington with Delaware's authorization of forum-selection bylaws (which had been validated in the *Boilermakers Local 154* decision in 2013) but with more restrictive limits: Washington's statute prohibits exclusion of Washington court jurisdiction and prohibits mandatory arbitration of internal disputes.

### 2020 — Comprehensive Modernization (Chapter 194)

Laws of 2020 chapter 194 was the most significant WBCA amendment package in many years. Key changes included:

- **Business opportunity waivers (RCW 23B.08.735).** The charter can now waive the duty to offer business opportunities to the corporation, either generally or within specified categories. This brought Washington into alignment with Delaware GCL § 122(17).
- **Updates to the conflict-of-interest provisions** to harmonize with MBCA updates and clarify the safe-harbor procedures.
- **Modernization of director and officer provisions** including resignation mechanics and various technical cleanups.
- **Virtual meeting provisions** clarifying that shareholder and board meetings may be held entirely by electronic means, subject to the "hear each other" requirement.
- **Updates to the indemnification framework** including clarification of the advancement-of-expenses procedures.

The 2020 amendments reflected a broad consultation among Washington corporate counsel through the WSBA Business Law Section and brought Washington substantially into alignment with MBCA updates that had accumulated over the preceding decade.

### 2020 — Technical Amendments (Chapter 57)

Laws of 2020 chapter 57 included various technical amendments, including updates to the director resignation provision at RCW 23B.08.070. These amendments were primarily housekeeping rather than substantive.

### Ongoing Legislative Activity

The WBCA continues to evolve. Practitioners should consult the current RCW for the most recent text and the Washington State Legislature's session laws for pending or recently enacted amendments.

## Appendix H: Practical Guidance for Directors Newly Appointed to a Washington Board

A person newly appointed to the board of a Washington corporation — whether a closely held family business, a venture-backed startup, or a mature operating company — faces a learning curve that often combines corporate-law education with industry-specific education. This appendix offers practical guidance for that new director.

### H.1 — Read the Governing Documents

Before attending the first board meeting, the new director should obtain and read:

1. **The articles of incorporation.** Including all amendments. The articles define the basic framework of the corporation — authorized shares, any exculpation or indemnification provisions, any special governance rules.

2. **The bylaws.** Including all amendments. The bylaws define the procedural rules — meeting notice, quorum, committee structures.

3. **Any shareholder agreement.** If the corporation has a 23B.07.320 agreement, the new director should read it carefully. The shareholder agreement may contain governance rules that override the bylaws and articles.

4. **The minutes of recent board meetings.** Typically the most recent six to twelve months of minutes, to understand what the board has been working on.

5. **Recent financial statements.** Audited financial statements (if prepared) plus the most recent quarterly or monthly financials.

6. **The D&O insurance policy.** Including the declarations page, the terms and conditions, and any endorsements. The director should know what is covered and what is not.

7. **The indemnification agreement (if any).** Directors should have a contractual indemnification agreement in addition to the statutory and bylaw rights. This agreement should be reviewed carefully.

### H.2 — Understand the Business

In addition to the governing documents, the new director should develop an understanding of the business itself:

- What products or services does the corporation provide?
- Who are the customers and what does the sales cycle look like?
- Who are the competitors?
- What is the capital structure (debt, equity, preferred stock, warrants, options)?
- What are the key employee relationships and the management team?
- What are the principal contracts and commitments?
- What regulatory or legal matters are pending or anticipated?

A director cannot exercise the duty of care without understanding the business. "The care of an ordinarily prudent person in a like position under similar circumstances" is impossible to meet if the director is ignorant of the circumstances.

### H.3 — Engage in Deliberation

Directors must deliberate, not rubber-stamp. This means:

- **Ask questions.** If a board paper presents a proposed transaction, ask about the alternatives, the risks, the financial projections, and the reasoning behind the recommendation.
- **Challenge assumptions.** Management's projections are often optimistic. A good director probes the assumptions and stress-tests the downside.
- **Seek expert advice.** If a matter requires expertise the director does not have, insist on expert advice from qualified professionals (accountants, lawyers, industry consultants).
- **Record dissent.** If the director disagrees with a proposed action, say so at the meeting and insist that the dissent be recorded in the minutes. Silence is acquiescence under RCW 23B.08.240(4).

### H.4 — Watch for Conflicts

The director should be alert to conflicts of interest in matters that come before the board. Conflicts arise in many forms:

- **Self-dealing.** A transaction between the corporation and the director (or a related party).
- **Competing opportunities.** A business opportunity that falls within the corporation's scope and the director's personal interests.
- **Outside commitments.** Service on other boards or consulting relationships with competitors or customers.
- **Financial relationships.** Loans, investments, or other financial ties that could bias the director's judgment.

When a conflict arises, the director should disclose it to the full board and abstain from voting on the conflicted matter. The conflict-of-interest provisions of RCW 23B.08.700-.740 provide safe harbors but only if followed correctly.

### H.5 — Respect Confidentiality

Directors receive confidential information about the corporation — strategy, financial performance, personnel matters, pending transactions. This information must be kept confidential. Directors must not share corporate confidential information with family members, friends, or business associates outside the corporation, except as authorized by the corporation.

### H.6 — Maintain Independence

Even if the director was recruited to represent a particular constituency (a shareholder group, an investor, a founder), the director's legal duties run to the corporation and all its shareholders — not to the nominating constituency. A director who represents only a narrow interest group and ignores the broader corporate interest is in breach of duty.

### H.7 — Know When to Resign

A director who can no longer serve effectively — whether because of a schedule conflict, a personal crisis, a newly arising conflict of interest, or a fundamental disagreement with the corporation's direction — should consider resignation. Resignation is not a failure; it is a responsible discharge of the duty to ensure that the board is effective. Under RCW 23B.08.070, resignation is simple: written notice to the corporation.

## Appendix I: The Interface with Other Chapters of Title 23B

The formation and governance provisions of Chapters 23B.02, 23B.07, and 23B.08 do not operate in isolation. They interface with other chapters of Title 23B that govern specific aspects of corporate operations. This appendix identifies the key interfaces.

### I.1 — Chapter 23B.06 (Shares and Distributions)

Chapter 23B.06 governs the authorization, issuance, and repurchase of corporate shares. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.06.010** (authorized shares). Every corporation must authorize at least one class of voting common stock and at least one class of residual-claimant common stock (which can be the same class).
- **RCW 23B.06.020** (classes and series). Provides the framework for creating multiple classes and series, including "blank check" preferred stock that can be further subdivided by board resolution.
- **RCW 23B.06.200 through .270** (issuance of shares). Governs consideration for shares, form of certificates, and the stock ledger.
- **RCW 23B.06.400** (distributions). Governs dividends and other distributions, including the statutory solvency tests that must be satisfied before a distribution is made.

The formation provisions of Chapter 23B.02 cross-reference the share provisions of Chapter 23B.06, particularly in the mandatory content of articles under RCW 23B.02.020(1)(b).

### I.2 — Chapter 23B.10 (Amendment of Articles and Bylaws)

Chapter 23B.10 governs amendments to the articles and bylaws. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.10.020 through .060** (article amendments). Amendments to the articles generally require board approval plus shareholder approval.
- **RCW 23B.10.200 through .207** (bylaw amendments). Bylaws can generally be amended by the board, unless the articles reserve the amendment power to the shareholders.
- **RCW 23B.10.205** (holdover directors). Authorizes bylaws to address the holdover-director situation covered in RCW 23B.08.050(5).

### I.3 — Chapter 23B.11 (Mergers and Share Exchanges)

Chapter 23B.11 governs mergers, share exchanges, and related fundamental transactions. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.11.030** (shareholder approval of mergers). Requires separate class voting in certain circumstances.
- **RCW 23B.11.040** (effect of merger). Establishes the automatic transfer of assets, liabilities, and contract rights upon merger.

The fundamental-transaction provisions of Chapter 23B.11 interface with the shareholder meeting and voting provisions of Chapter 23B.07.

### I.4 — Chapter 23B.12 (Disposition of Assets)

Chapter 23B.12 governs the disposition of substantially all corporate assets outside the ordinary course of business. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.12.020** (shareholder approval of asset dispositions). Requires shareholder approval when a corporation sells or otherwise disposes of "all, or substantially all, of its property" other than in the ordinary course of business.

### I.5 — Chapter 23B.13 (Dissenters' Rights)

Chapter 23B.13 establishes the dissenters' rights (also called "appraisal rights") of shareholders who disagree with certain fundamental transactions. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.13.020** (right to dissent). Shareholders may dissent from mergers, share exchanges, dispositions of substantially all assets, and certain other fundamental transactions.
- **RCW 23B.13.210 through .310** (procedure for dissent). Establishes the notice, demand, and appraisal procedures.

Dissenters' rights are an important protection for minority shareholders against fundamental transactions they do not support. A shareholder who properly dissents is entitled to receive "fair value" for their shares, as determined by the corporation or, if disputed, by the superior court.

### I.6 — Chapter 23B.14 (Dissolution)

Chapter 23B.14 governs voluntary and involuntary dissolution. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.14.020** (shareholder approval of dissolution). Requires shareholder approval for voluntary dissolution.
- **RCW 23B.14.300** (judicial dissolution). Authorizes superior courts to dissolve a corporation on petition of a shareholder in certain circumstances (deadlock, oppression, waste).

The judicial dissolution remedy of RCW 23B.14.300 is particularly important in closely held corporations, where it provides a "last resort" mechanism for resolving otherwise intractable disputes among shareholder-directors.

### I.7 — Chapter 23B.15 (Foreign Corporations)

Chapter 23B.15 governs the registration and operation of foreign (non-Washington) corporations doing business in Washington. This is not directly relevant to the formation of Washington corporations but is relevant to Washington corporations that have decided to reincorporate in another state while retaining Washington operations.

### I.8 — Chapter 23B.16 (Records and Reports)

Chapter 23B.16 governs the corporation's record-keeping obligations and shareholder inspection rights. Key provisions:

- **RCW 23B.16.010 through .030** (corporate records). Requires the corporation to maintain records including minute books, stock ledger, financial statements, and governance documents.
- **RCW 23B.16.020** (inspection rights). Shareholders have the right to inspect corporate records, subject to certain procedural requirements and scope limits.

## Appendix J: Selected Practice Pointers

This final appendix collects several practice pointers that experienced Washington corporate counsel find useful.

**1. Use a commercial registered agent.** Unless there is a specific reason to designate a natural person (e.g., the corporation is extremely small and cost-sensitive), a commercial registered agent offers privacy, reliability, and scalability.

**2. Include mandatory indemnification and exculpation in the articles.** Directors of Washington corporations should insist on mandatory indemnification (rather than permissive) and on the full scope of exculpation permitted by RCW 23B.08.320. These protections substantially reduce personal-liability exposure.

**3. Draft a 23B.07.320 shareholder agreement for any multi-founder corporation.** The marginal cost of drafting the agreement at formation is low; the cost of negotiating it after a dispute arises is very high.

**4. Use founder vesting from day one.** Founder shares should be subject to four-year vesting with a one-year cliff. This protects the corporation from the risk that a founder leaves with a large unearned equity stake.

**5. File § 83(b) elections promptly.** The 30-day deadline is strict. Missing it can result in significant tax consequences if the stock appreciates.

**6. Calendar compliance deadlines.** The initial report deadline (120 days post-formation) and the annual report deadline are easy to forget. Calendar them at formation and set reminders.

**7. Document board deliberations carefully.** Board minutes should reflect that the directors considered the matter, reviewed relevant information, and deliberated. Bare-bones "approved" minutes are a trap under the BJR, which requires evidence of an informed decision-making process.

**8. Obtain D&O insurance before the first outside investor joins the board.** Independent directors generally will not accept appointments without adequate D&O coverage.

**9. Update the bylaws and articles when the corporation's circumstances change.** Stale governance documents can create unexpected problems when a transaction is under time pressure. Regular review is a modest investment that prevents larger future problems.

**10. Remember that formation is just the start.** The real work of corporate governance begins after formation, with the ongoing obligations of meeting compliance, record-keeping, deliberation, and fiduciary duty. The formation documents create the framework; the ongoing governance activity fills it in.

## Appendix K: Detailed Analysis of the Washington Director's Duty of Care

The duty of care under RCW 23B.08.300(1)(b) is the single most analyzed director duty in Washington corporate law. This appendix offers an extended treatment of the duty's meaning, scope, and application.

### K.1 — The Ordinary Prudent Person Standard

The statutory formulation — "the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances" — is drawn directly from the RMBCA and has parallels in virtually every American corporate statute. The formulation has three components that repay separate analysis.

**"Ordinarily prudent person."** This is the classic tort-law standard of care, transplanted into corporate law. The "ordinarily prudent person" is a legal fiction — not the most cautious person imaginable, nor the most aggressive, but a reasonable average. The standard is objective: it asks what such a person would do, not what the particular director actually did. A director whose actual judgment fell below the ordinary prudent person standard has failed the duty of care, regardless of the director's subjective good faith or honest best efforts.

**"In a like position."** This modifier narrows the comparison. The standard is not "what would an ordinarily prudent person do in general?" but "what would an ordinarily prudent person in a like position to this director do?" A director of a bank is compared to other bank directors; a director of a technology startup to other startup directors. The modifier acknowledges that different corporate contexts call for different levels of expertise and attention.

**"Under similar circumstances."** This further modifier acknowledges that circumstances matter. A director facing an urgent decision with limited time and incomplete information is judged by a different standard than a director with weeks to deliberate and full access to expert advice. The "similar circumstances" language permits the court to take account of real-world constraints.

### K.2 — What the Duty of Care Requires

Synthesizing the statute and the case law, the duty of care requires the director to:

1. **Attend meetings.** A director who chronically skips meetings cannot reasonably be said to have exercised care. Regular attendance is a foundational obligation.

2. **Prepare for meetings.** The director should review board materials in advance and come prepared to discuss them.

3. **Ask questions.** The director should probe the material and identify potential issues. Silence at meetings is not necessarily assent (see RCW 23B.08.240(4)), but it is a red flag for the careful observer.

4. **Consider alternatives.** A director should consider whether alternatives to the proposed action exist and whether they might be preferable.

5. **Rely reasonably on experts.** The director may rely on officer reports, legal counsel, accountants, and expert consultants — but only to the extent such reliance is reasonable under RCW 23B.08.300(2).

6. **Monitor the corporation's operations.** Directors have an ongoing obligation to monitor the corporation's activities. They cannot simply "show up" for scheduled meetings and ignore the corporation between meetings.

7. **Ensure adequate information systems.** Directors should ensure that management has in place adequate information systems to detect and report on risks. The Delaware *Caremark* doctrine (derived from *In re Caremark International Inc. Derivative Litigation*, 698 A.2d 959 (Del. Ch. 1996)) imposes a duty to establish monitoring systems. Washington case law has not explicitly adopted *Caremark* but the duty of care arguably encompasses a similar obligation.

8. **Respond to red flags.** When information comes to the director's attention that suggests something is wrong, the director must follow up. Ignoring red flags is a classic duty-of-care failure.

### K.3 — What the Duty of Care Does Not Require

Equally important is what the duty of care does not require:

1. **Perfection.** Directors are not required to make perfect decisions. They are required to make reasonable decisions.

2. **Omniscience.** Directors are not required to know everything. They may rely on experts.

3. **Independence from management influence.** Directors are permitted to work with management and to give weight to management recommendations. The duty of care does not require adversarial skepticism of every management proposal.

4. **Hindsight correctness.** A decision that turns out badly does not, by that fact alone, breach the duty of care. The duty is evaluated at the time of the decision, on the information then available.

5. **Participation in every detail.** Directors supervise; they do not manage day to day. The duty of care does not require the director to second-guess every operational decision of management.

### K.4 — Gross Negligence vs. Ordinary Negligence

Washington case law, borrowing from Delaware, distinguishes between ordinary negligence (which may breach the statutory standard of conduct but does not defeat the business judgment rule) and gross negligence (which can defeat the BJR). The line between the two is not precise, but the Delaware cases offer guidance:

- **Ordinary negligence.** A decision made with imperfect preparation, reasonable (but not exhaustive) inquiry, and honest (but imperfect) reasoning. Protected by the BJR.
- **Gross negligence.** A decision made without any meaningful preparation, without any inquiry into material facts, or with reckless disregard for the interests of the corporation. Not protected by the BJR.

The paradigm case of gross negligence is *Smith v. Van Gorkom*, in which the Delaware Supreme Court held that a board that approved a merger at a $55 price after a two-hour meeting without documentation and without inquiring into the derivation of the price was grossly negligent. The decision was well-intentioned and the price was (arguably) reasonable, but the process was so inadequate that the board had no basis for its judgment. That is the hallmark of gross negligence: not bad faith, not self-dealing, but a complete absence of deliberation.

### K.5 — The Reliance Safe Harbor

RCW 23B.08.300(2) provides directors with a "reliance" safe harbor: directors may rely on information, opinions, reports, and statements prepared by officers, employees, legal counsel, accountants, and board committees. This reliance is valid if:

1. **The director reasonably believes the source is reliable and competent.** This is a reasonableness test, not a good-faith test. A director who reasonably believes the CFO is competent may rely on the CFO's financial reports; a director who knows the CFO is incompetent or dishonest cannot.

2. **The director does not have knowledge that would make reliance unwarranted.** Section (3) carves out reliance when the director actually knows facts that would make reliance unreasonable.

The reliance safe harbor is one of the most important protections for directors in practice. Directors cannot personally verify every fact underlying a corporate decision; the corporation's information systems and expert advisors are the directors' eyes and ears. The safe harbor gives legal recognition to this reality.

### K.6 — Application in Specific Contexts

**Mergers and acquisitions.** In M&A transactions, the duty of care requires the board to (a) understand the strategic rationale for the transaction, (b) evaluate the financial terms (often with the help of a fairness opinion from an investment banker), (c) consider alternatives (including the "do nothing" alternative), and (d) negotiate for the benefit of shareholders. The board should deliberate over multiple meetings, with adequate time to absorb complex information.

**Capital allocation.** In major capital allocation decisions (large investments, debt issuances, dividend policies), the duty of care requires the board to consider the corporation's financial position, the risks of the proposed action, and the alternatives. Financial projections should be examined with skepticism, not rubber-stamped.

**Executive compensation.** In executive compensation decisions, the duty of care requires the board (or compensation committee) to consider market benchmarks, performance history, retention risks, and alignment with shareholder interests. Excessive compensation can breach the duty of care if the process was inadequate, even if no director benefited personally.

**Risk oversight.** In ongoing risk oversight, the duty of care requires the board to ensure that management has in place adequate systems for identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks. The board is not expected to manage risk directly, but it is expected to ensure that risk is being managed.

**Compliance.** The board must ensure that the corporation is operating in compliance with applicable laws. This does not mean the board is personally responsible for every compliance decision, but it does mean the board must oversee the compliance function and respond to compliance failures.

### K.7 — Process vs. Substance

A recurring theme in duty-of-care analysis is the distinction between process and substance. The business judgment rule (and, by extension, the duty of care as a liability standard) focuses primarily on process: did the directors go through an adequate deliberative process? Courts are deferential to the substance of the decision — the actual choice made — as long as the process was adequate.

This process-focused approach has several implications:

1. **Directors can make "bad" decisions without liability,** as long as the process leading to the decision was adequate.
2. **Directors can be liable for "good" decisions,** if the process was inadequate (as in *Van Gorkom*, where the Delaware Supreme Court held that an acceptable price was reached through an unacceptable process).
3. **Defense counsel focuses on process.** When a duty-of-care case is litigated, the defense typically focuses on documenting the thoroughness of the board's deliberative process, not on justifying the substantive correctness of the decision.
4. **Minutes matter.** Because litigation focuses on process, corporate minutes become critically important. Minutes that document the directors' deliberations, their review of materials, their consideration of alternatives, and their reliance on expert advice are a powerful defense.

## Appendix L: The Washington Director's Duty of Loyalty

The duty of loyalty is the second great pillar of director fiduciary duty, alongside the duty of care. Unlike the duty of care, which is codified in RCW 23B.08.300(1)(b), the duty of loyalty is not directly codified — it is inherent in the "best interests" prong of RCW 23B.08.300(1)(c) and is reinforced by the conflict-of-interest provisions of RCW 23B.08.700-.740 and the corporate-opportunity provision of RCW 23B.08.735.

### L.1 — The Core Principle

The duty of loyalty requires a director to act in the best interests of the corporation and not in the director's own interest. When the director's interest diverges from the corporation's interest, the corporation's interest must prevail.

This principle has ancient roots. The duty of loyalty derives from the law of trusts — a trustee cannot profit from the trust at the expense of the beneficiaries. Corporate directors are analogous to trustees: they hold positions of trust and are obligated to put the beneficiaries (shareholders and the corporation) first.

### L.2 — Categories of Loyalty Breaches

Loyalty breaches come in several categories:

**1. Self-dealing transactions.** A transaction between the corporation and the director (or a person related to the director). Self-dealing is suspect because the director is on both sides of the table and cannot be presumed to have represented the corporation's interests adequately.

**2. Corporate opportunity takings.** A director (or officer) takes for themselves a business opportunity that belongs to the corporation. Governed by RCW 23B.08.735.

**3. Usurpation of corporate property.** A director uses corporate assets, confidential information, or business relationships for personal benefit.

**4. Competing with the corporation.** A director runs or invests in a business that competes with the corporation.

**5. Entrenchment.** A director takes actions to preserve their own position (or the board's position) at the expense of shareholder interests. For example, using corporate assets to defeat a takeover bid that would benefit shareholders.

**6. Domination of a subsidiary.** A parent corporation's directors use their control of a subsidiary to benefit the parent at the expense of minority shareholders in the subsidiary.

### L.3 — The Safe Harbors

Washington law provides safe harbors for certain loyalty-implicating transactions. The conflict-of-interest safe harbors (RCW 23B.08.720 and .730) permit self-dealing transactions if approved by disinterested directors or disinterested shareholders after full disclosure, or if the transaction is fair. The corporate-opportunity safe harbors (RCW 23B.08.735) permit a director to take an opportunity if the corporation has disclaimed interest after disclosure, or if the duty has been categorically waived in the articles.

These safe harbors reflect a policy judgment: self-dealing transactions are not automatically prohibited, because they may be in the corporation's interest (the director may be uniquely positioned to provide a service or product the corporation needs), but they must be subject to procedural protections to ensure that the corporation's interests are actually served.

### L.4 — The Fairness Standard

When a loyalty-implicating transaction is not covered by a safe harbor, the transaction is reviewed under the "fairness" standard. In Delaware, this is called the "entire fairness" standard: the transaction must be fair in both its process ("fair dealing") and its terms ("fair price"). Washington case law has not always explicitly adopted the Delaware "entire fairness" terminology, but the substantive inquiry is similar.

**Fair dealing.** The process by which the transaction was negotiated and approved should have protected the corporation's interests. Factors include: Was the conflicted director excluded from the negotiation? Was an independent advisor retained? Was the decision-making process rigorous?

**Fair price.** The terms of the transaction should be commercially reasonable. Factors include: How does the price compare to market benchmarks? How does the price compare to the corporation's internal valuation? Did the corporation obtain a third-party fairness opinion?

Under the fairness standard, the burden of proof is typically on the director to establish that the transaction was fair. This reverses the usual burden in litigation (where the plaintiff must prove the claim) and reflects the special suspicion that attaches to loyalty-implicating transactions.

### L.5 — Loyalty and the BJR

The business judgment rule protects against duty-of-care claims but not against duty-of-loyalty claims. This is one of the most important structural features of the BJR: courts defer to directors' business judgment when the directors are acting in good faith on the corporation's behalf, but courts do not defer when the directors are acting in their own interest.

A shareholder alleging a loyalty breach does not need to overcome the BJR presumption in the same way as a shareholder alleging a care breach. The shareholder need only establish facts showing that the director had a conflict or took a personal benefit; at that point, the burden shifts to the director to establish fairness or safe harbor protection.

### L.6 — The Growing Sophistication of Loyalty Doctrine

Washington loyalty doctrine is less developed than Delaware's, but it has been growing in sophistication over the past several decades. Key developments:

- **Integration with the statutory safe harbors.** The 2002 and 2020 amendments updated RCW 23B.08.700-.740 to provide clearer safe-harbor procedures, reducing the area in which fairness review was required.
- **The business opportunity waiver.** The 2020 addition of RCW 23B.08.735(1)(b) introduced categorical waiver of the corporate-opportunity duty, a mechanism not previously available in Washington.
- **Harmonization with the MBCA.** Successive amendments have brought Washington loyalty doctrine closer to the MBCA template, making Washington law more predictable for practitioners accustomed to MBCA jurisdictions.

### L.7 — Practical Implications

For directors navigating loyalty issues, the practical guidance is clear:

1. **Disclose.** At the first sign of a potential conflict, disclose it to the full board. Nondisclosure compounds liability.
2. **Recuse.** Abstain from board discussion and voting on the conflicted matter.
3. **Use a safe harbor.** Ensure that the transaction is approved by disinterested directors (under 23B.08.720) or disinterested shareholders (under 23B.08.730), or document that it is fair.
4. **Document.** Memorialize the disclosure, the recusal, and the approval process in the minutes.
5. **Consider a categorical waiver.** For directors with regular conflict exposure (e.g., VC directors sitting on multiple boards), a 23B.08.735(1)(b) corporate-opportunity waiver in the articles can reduce ongoing friction.

## Appendix M: Shareholder Voting Mechanics — A Deeper Look

Shareholder voting is the mechanism by which the shareholders exercise residual authority over the corporation. It is used to elect directors, approve fundamental transactions, amend the articles, and (in some corporations) approve certain operational matters. This appendix examines the mechanics of shareholder voting in greater depth.

### M.1 — The Voting Universe

Not every share is entitled to vote on every matter. The "voting universe" for a particular matter is defined by the articles, the bylaws, and any shareholder agreements. Key principles:

1. **Common stock typically votes.** Common stock usually has one vote per share on all matters, unless the articles provide otherwise.
2. **Preferred stock often has limited voting rights.** Traditional preferred stock votes only on matters that directly affect its preferences (liquidation preferences, redemption rights, conversion rights). Venture-capital preferred often has additional voting rights specified in the articles.
3. **Non-voting common stock.** Some corporations create a class of non-voting common stock, typically for founder control purposes. Non-voting common does not vote on any matter except (typically) fundamental transactions where class voting is required by statute.
4. **Class and series voting.** When the articles provide for class or series voting, the votes of each class are tallied separately. A matter may require approval by (a) all voting shares as a single group, (b) all voting shares plus a separate vote of one class, or (c) both all voting shares and separate votes of multiple classes.

### M.2 — Cumulative Voting

Cumulative voting is an alternative director-election method authorized by RCW 23B.07.260. Under cumulative voting, each shareholder has votes equal to the number of shares times the number of directors being elected, and can allocate these votes among candidates as the shareholder chooses (including concentrating all votes on a single candidate).

The purpose of cumulative voting is to protect minority shareholders by allowing them to elect at least one director if they have a sufficient minority stake. The minimum stake needed to elect one director under cumulative voting is:

> (Shares outstanding / (Directors being elected + 1)) + 1 share

For example, if 1,000 shares are outstanding and 5 directors are being elected, a minority shareholder holding (1000 / 6) + 1 = 167 + 1 = 168 shares can elect one director.

Cumulative voting was once widely used — it was even mandatory in some states — but it has become less common in modern corporate practice. Most modern corporations use plurality voting or majority voting for director elections, without cumulative voting.

**Washington's approach.** RCW 23B.07.260 permits cumulative voting only if the articles specifically authorize it. The default is no cumulative voting. This "opt-in" approach reflects the modern trend.

### M.3 — Plurality vs. Majority Voting

For director elections, Washington corporations can use either plurality voting (the default under RCW 23B.07.280) or majority voting (by opt-in):

**Plurality voting.** The candidates with the most votes win, even if they receive fewer "yes" votes than "no" votes. In a contested election with more candidates than seats, plurality voting produces a decisive winner. In an uncontested election with the same number of candidates as seats, plurality voting means that every candidate wins as long as each receives at least one vote.

**Majority voting.** A candidate must receive more "yes" votes than "no" votes (or, in some formulations, more "yes" votes than abstentions and "no" votes combined). If a candidate does not receive majority support, the candidate does not win, and the seat may remain vacant or be filled by a subsequent nomination.

Majority voting has become popular in public-company governance as a way of giving shareholders meaningful opposition power in uncontested elections. It is typically paired with a conditional resignation policy: directors submit an irrevocable conditional resignation that takes effect if they fail to receive majority support.

In closely held corporations, majority voting is less important because directors are almost always elected by consensus or by contractual agreement among the shareholders.

### M.4 — Supermajority Provisions

The articles can require supermajority approval for certain actions — typically amendments to the articles, mergers, sales of substantially all assets, or dissolution. Common supermajority thresholds are two-thirds, 75%, or 80% of the voting shares.

Supermajority provisions serve two functions:

1. **Minority protection.** A supermajority requirement makes it harder for a bare majority to push through decisions that harm minority interests.
2. **Anti-takeover protection.** Supermajority requirements can delay or defeat hostile takeover bids, giving the target board time to negotiate or implement defensive measures.

Washington permits supermajority provisions in the articles. The statute does not impose a ceiling on how high the threshold can be set — in principle, an article provision could require unanimous approval, though this is unusual outside of closely held contexts.

### M.5 — Voting Trusts and Voting Agreements

RCW 23B.07.300 and .310 authorize voting trusts and voting agreements, respectively.

**Voting trust.** A voting trust is an arrangement in which shareholders transfer legal title to their shares to a trustee, who votes the shares according to the terms of the trust agreement. Voting trusts are useful for consolidating voting control — for example, founders can create a voting trust that concentrates voting power in a single trustee, ensuring unified decision-making even if the founders hold shares individually. Washington voting trusts must be in writing and must have a specified term.

**Voting agreement.** A voting agreement is a contract among shareholders in which they agree to vote their shares in a specified way. Voting agreements are simpler than voting trusts (no trustee, no transfer of legal title) but they can be harder to enforce against a shareholder who breaches.

Both mechanisms are overlapping with the shareholder agreement mechanism of RCW 23B.07.320. In practice, most modern closely held corporations use a 23B.07.320 shareholder agreement to consolidate voting arrangements, rather than a separate voting trust or voting agreement.

### M.6 — The Shareholder Consent Mechanism in Detail

RCW 23B.07.040 permits shareholders to act by written consent. The mechanics are important:

**Unanimous consent.** If every shareholder entitled to vote signs the consent, the action is approved upon delivery of the final signature to the corporation. Notice to non-consenting shareholders is not required because there are no non-consenting shareholders.

**Majority consent.** If less than all shareholders sign, the consent is still effective, but only if (a) the articles permit majority consent, and (b) advance notice is given to non-consenting shareholders. The majority-consent procedure is rarely used because of the complexity of the advance-notice requirement; most closely held corporations rely on unanimous consent.

**The 60-day window.** Consents delivered to the corporation must aggregate to the required approval threshold within 60 days of the earliest-signed consent. If the window expires without reaching the threshold, the process must be restarted.

**Form of consent.** Written consents can be paper or electronic. Electronic consents must meet the authentication requirements of RCW 23B.01.410 and similar provisions. DocuSign, Adobe Sign, and similar tools are commonly used for electronic consents.

**Effectiveness.** The consent is effective when the last required signature is delivered to the corporation, subject to any specified later date.

### M.7 — Remote and Virtual Meetings

RCW 23B.07.010(2) authorizes shareholder meetings to be held "solely by means of remote communication" — i.e., fully virtual meetings with no physical gathering. This provision became critically important during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, when physical meetings were impossible, and has remained important for public companies and closely held corporations alike.

**Technical requirements.** A virtual meeting must comply with the general meeting requirements (notice, quorum, voting) but the "place" requirement is satisfied by the electronic platform. Shareholders must be able to participate in real time, ask questions (at least in writing), and vote.

**Practical considerations.** Virtual meetings are efficient but present challenges: authenticating attendees, managing question submission, tallying votes, and documenting the meeting. Public companies typically use specialized virtual meeting platforms (Broadridge, ComputerShare, Mediasite) that handle these functions. Closely held corporations can use general-purpose video conferencing tools (Zoom, Teams) with simpler protocols.

### M.8 — Inspectors of Election

For corporations with large shareholder bases, "inspectors of election" are often appointed to verify voting results. The inspectors are typically employees of the corporation's transfer agent or proxy services firm. They receive proxies, tally votes, resolve disputes about ballot validity, and certify the final results. Washington does not mandate the use of inspectors for private corporations, but public companies often use them as a matter of best practice and to comply with stock exchange rules.

### M.9 — Proxy Contests

In a proxy contest, two (or more) factions solicit proxies from shareholders in support of competing slates of directors or competing positions on a proposed action. Proxy contests are rare in closely held corporations but are a significant feature of public-company governance.

The mechanics of a proxy contest involve federal securities law (the proxy rules under Section 14(a) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934) in addition to state corporate law. The state-law dimensions are governed by the proxy provisions of RCW 23B.07.220 and related sections, but the federal regulatory overlay is typically dominant.

## Appendix N: Special Considerations for Venture-Backed Washington Corporations

A substantial subset of Washington corporations seek venture-capital financing. Venture-backed corporations face specific issues that deserve dedicated treatment.

### N.1 — The Typical VC Structure

A venture-backed corporation typically has:

- **Common stock** held by founders, employees, and early investors.
- **Preferred stock** issued in one or more financing rounds (Series A, Series B, etc.), with each series having specific preferences.
- **Stock options and equity incentives** held by employees under an equity incentive plan.
- **Convertible notes or SAFEs** from early-stage financing, convertible into preferred stock upon the next financing round.

The capital structure is documented in the articles of incorporation (which authorize and describe each class of stock), a stock option plan document, a certificate of designation for each preferred series (if using blank-check preferred), and various stockholder agreements (investors' rights agreement, right of first refusal agreement, voting agreement, co-sale agreement).

### N.2 — Protective Provisions

A central feature of venture-backed governance is the "protective provisions" — contractual or charter-based veto rights held by the preferred stock over specified corporate actions. Common protective provisions include:

- **Amendments to the articles or bylaws** that adversely affect the preferred stock.
- **Authorization of additional preferred stock** with rights senior to or pari passu with existing preferred.
- **Payment of dividends** other than as provided in the articles.
- **Mergers, consolidations, or sales of substantially all assets.**
- **Dissolution or voluntary bankruptcy.**
- **Increases in the size of the board.**
- **Large capital expenditures or incurrence of indebtedness** above specified thresholds.
- **Material changes in the business.**
- **Issuance of equity or convertible securities** other than under the approved equity incentive plan.

Protective provisions are typically set forth in the articles of incorporation (for charter-level protections) and in the investors' rights agreement (for contractual protections). Under RCW 23B.07.250 voting-group rules, preferred-stock class voting on charter amendments creates a natural mechanism for protective provisions.

### N.3 — Board Composition

Venture-backed corporations typically have board structures that reflect the investor composition. A common structure for a post-Series A company might be:

- **Two common directors.** Typically the two founder-CEOs.
- **Two preferred directors.** Typically designated by the lead Series A investor.
- **One independent director.** Jointly selected by the common and preferred directors.

This 2-2-1 structure is designed to balance founder control and investor oversight, with the independent director serving as a tiebreaker on contentious issues.

Board composition rules are typically entrenched in a voting agreement (signed by all shareholders) rather than in the articles or bylaws. This allows the composition to be changed more easily as the corporation raises additional rounds and adds additional preferred series. The voting agreement typically provides that each party agrees to vote their shares in favor of the specified board slate.

### N.4 — Anti-Dilution Provisions

Venture preferred stock typically includes "anti-dilution" provisions that adjust the conversion ratio (the number of common shares into which each preferred share converts) to protect against dilutive issuances of common stock at lower prices. Two common formulations:

- **Weighted average anti-dilution.** The conversion ratio adjusts based on a weighted-average formula that takes into account both the price of the new issuance and the size of the new issuance relative to the total outstanding.
- **Full ratchet anti-dilution.** The conversion ratio adjusts to the lowest price at which any subsequent issuance occurs, regardless of the size. Full ratchet is more protective of the preferred holder but is less common because of its punitive effect on common shareholders.

Anti-dilution provisions are set forth in the articles of incorporation (as part of the certificate of designation for the preferred series).

### N.5 — Liquidation Preferences

Venture preferred stock typically has a "liquidation preference" that gives the preferred holders priority in any liquidation, merger, or sale of the corporation. The basic liquidation preference provides that the preferred holders receive a specified amount (typically 1x to 3x their original investment) before the common holders receive anything. In some structures, the preferred also "participates" alongside the common in any remaining proceeds, creating a "participating preferred" that provides even greater protection.

Liquidation preferences are fundamental to the venture-capital bargain and are set forth in the articles of incorporation. They interact with shareholder approval rules (mergers typically require shareholder approval under RCW 23B.11.030) and with dissenters' rights (shareholders may dissent from mergers under RCW 23B.13.020).

### N.6 — Drag-Along Rights

A drag-along right permits a specified majority of shareholders to force the remaining shareholders to sell their shares in a sale of the corporation. Drag-along rights are important for enabling acquirers to obtain 100% ownership without holdout minorities. Typical drag-along formulations require:

- Approval by the board.
- Approval by the preferred holders (or specified majority thereof).
- Approval by a majority of the common.
- Compliance with any procedural requirements (notice, appraisal, etc.).

Drag-along rights are typically documented in the voting agreement (or in a separate stockholders' agreement) rather than in the articles or bylaws. They are enforced as contractual obligations.

### N.7 — The Transition to Public Company

If a venture-backed corporation eventually goes public, the venture structure must be dismantled:

- **Preferred stock converts to common stock** at or before the IPO.
- **The 23B.07.320 shareholder agreement terminates.**
- **Protective provisions and voting agreements fall away.**
- **The corporation becomes subject to SEC reporting, stock exchange rules, and public-company governance standards** (Sarbanes-Oxley independence, audit committee requirements, compensation committee requirements, etc.).

This transition is complex and typically takes 12-18 months of preparation. It is governed by a combination of state corporate law, federal securities law, and stock exchange rules.

## Appendix O: Looking Forward — Emerging Issues in Washington Corporate Law

The Washington Business Corporation Act, like all corporate statutes, evolves over time in response to changing business practices, court decisions, and legislative priorities. This appendix identifies several emerging issues that may shape Washington corporate law over the next several years.

### O.1 — Benefit Corporations and Social Purpose Corporations

Washington was an early adopter of the "social purpose corporation" form (Chapter 23B.25 RCW), enacted in 2012. Social purpose corporations are for-profit corporations that are authorized to consider social and environmental purposes alongside financial returns. This form is distinct from the "benefit corporation" form that has been adopted in many other states, but the policy rationale is similar: to create a corporate form that is explicitly pro-social.

As ESG (environmental, social, and governance) investing grows in importance, the role of social purpose corporations and similar pro-social corporate forms is likely to evolve. Future amendments to the WBCA may further refine these provisions.

### O.2 — Virtual Meetings and Electronic Governance

The 2020 pandemic accelerated the adoption of virtual meeting technologies. Washington's statutes already permit virtual meetings for shareholders and directors, but questions remain about best practices, technical requirements, and the legal treatment of particular virtual meeting features (breakout rooms, chat functions, automatic recording, etc.).

Future amendments may clarify these issues. In the meantime, practitioners should document virtual meeting procedures carefully to ensure compliance with the existing statutory framework.

### O.3 — Artificial Intelligence and Board Decision-Making

As AI tools become more capable, some corporate boards have begun using AI for decision-support functions — analyzing board materials, identifying risks, summarizing transactions, and even recommending specific actions. The use of AI in board decision-making raises questions about the duty of care: can a director rely on AI-generated analysis under the RCW 23B.08.300(2) reliance safe harbor?

The current answer is uncertain. The reliance safe harbor permits reliance on "persons" as to matters within their expertise, but AI tools are not "persons" in any conventional sense. A careful director using AI tools should probably treat the AI output as an input to the director's own judgment, rather than as a substitute for the director's judgment — much like a director might treat a market research report or an industry analyst's note.

### O.4 — Cybersecurity and Oversight Duties

Cybersecurity failures have become a major source of corporate liability, and shareholder plaintiffs have begun bringing derivative actions against directors alleging failure to oversee cybersecurity. Delaware courts have recently held (in cases like *Marchand v. Barnhill* and *In re McDonald's Corporation Stockholder Derivative Litigation*) that directors have an affirmative duty to oversee mission-critical risks, and cybersecurity is increasingly considered mission-critical.

Washington has not yet produced its own major cybersecurity oversight case, but the principles from Delaware are likely to influence Washington courts. Directors of Washington corporations should take cybersecurity oversight seriously, including:

- Regular board briefings on cybersecurity risks and incidents.
- Establishment of a cybersecurity committee (or assignment of cybersecurity to the audit committee).
- Engagement with external cybersecurity advisors.
- Review of cybersecurity insurance coverage.
- Oversight of incident response procedures.

### O.5 — Environmental and Climate Disclosures

Federal SEC regulations increasingly require climate-related disclosures from public companies. These federal rules interact with state corporate law duties: directors of Washington corporations (even closely held ones) increasingly must consider climate-related risks and opportunities as part of their duty of care.

Future Washington legislative amendments may address corporate climate disclosures more directly. In the meantime, directors should ensure that the corporation has adequate systems for identifying and managing climate-related risks.

### O.6 — Stakeholder Governance

The traditional view of corporate law is that directors owe their duties exclusively to the corporation and its shareholders. A growing "stakeholder governance" movement argues that directors should also consider the interests of employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and the environment. Washington's social purpose corporation statute permits (but does not require) this broader consideration; future amendments may extend it to ordinary corporations.

### O.7 — Alternative Financing Mechanisms

New financing mechanisms — crypto tokens, tokenized equity, blockchain-based cap tables — raise questions about how traditional corporate law concepts apply. Is a token an equity security? Does tokenized equity count as "shares" under RCW 23B.06? How do transfer restrictions apply to tokens? These questions are not yet fully resolved.

Washington has been a relatively crypto-friendly jurisdiction, but the full integration of blockchain-based financing with traditional corporate law will require legislative, regulatory, and judicial development.

### O.8 — Remote Work and Corporate Residence

The rise of remote work has blurred the concept of corporate "residence." A Washington corporation whose CEO works from Idaho, whose CFO works from Arizona, and whose directors are scattered across multiple states is still legally a Washington corporation for incorporation purposes — but its tax status, its employment-law exposure, and its regulatory obligations may be affected by the geographic distribution of its key personnel.

Future corporate-law amendments may need to address how the traditional concept of "registered office" and "principal office" applies in a fully remote world.

## Appendix P: The Washington Corporate Records Regime

RCW 23B.16 establishes the record-keeping requirements for Washington corporations. This appendix examines the record-keeping obligations in greater depth.

### P.1 — Required Records

RCW 23B.16.010 requires a Washington corporation to keep as permanent records the following:

1. **Minutes of all meetings** of shareholders and board of directors.
2. **Records of all actions taken by shareholders or directors without a meeting.**
3. **Records of all actions taken by committees** of the board of directors exercising the authority of the board on behalf of the corporation.
4. **Appropriate accounting records.**
5. **A record of shareholders** in a form that permits preparation of a list of the names and addresses of all shareholders, in alphabetical order by class of shares, showing the number of shares held by each.

These records must be maintained on an ongoing basis. The corporation cannot "catch up" on record-keeping after the fact with retroactive minutes or reconstructed ledgers; the records must reflect the actual sequence of corporate events.

### P.2 — Where Records Must Be Kept

RCW 23B.16.010(4) requires the corporation to keep at its principal office a copy of the following records:

1. Its articles of incorporation (or restated articles).
2. Its bylaws (or restated bylaws).
3. Resolutions adopted by the board of directors creating classes or series of shares and fixing their rights and preferences.
4. Minutes of all shareholder meetings and records of all actions taken by shareholders without a meeting, for the past three years.
5. Written communications to shareholders generally within the past three years, including financial statements.
6. A list of the names and business addresses of the current directors and officers.
7. The most recent annual report delivered to the Secretary of State.

These records must be available for inspection by shareholders under the procedures set forth in RCW 23B.16.020.

### P.3 — Shareholder Inspection Rights

RCW 23B.16.020 grants shareholders the right to inspect corporate records. The inspection right is not absolute; it is subject to specific conditions:

**Unqualified inspection rights.** A shareholder may inspect the basic governance documents (articles, bylaws, director/officer list, annual reports, and similar documents) upon five business days' notice, without demonstrating any particular purpose.

**Qualified inspection rights.** For more sensitive records (accounting records, shareholder lists, board meeting minutes), a shareholder must (a) give at least five business days' notice, (b) demand the inspection in good faith and for a proper purpose, (c) describe with reasonable particularity the shareholder's purpose and the records desired, and (d) demonstrate that the records are directly connected with the purpose.

**"Proper purpose."** A proper purpose is one "reasonably related to the shareholder's interest as a shareholder." Classic proper purposes include investigating potential mismanagement, valuing shares for a potential sale, and communicating with other shareholders about corporate matters. A purpose that is hostile to the corporation (e.g., gathering information for a competing business) is not a proper purpose.

### P.4 — Corporate Minutes as Legal Evidence

Corporate minutes are admissible in court as evidence of the actions taken at the meetings they describe. This gives them special importance: a board decision recorded in properly prepared minutes is much harder to challenge than an oral or informal decision.

**What should be in the minutes.** Effective corporate minutes should include:

- The date, time, and location of the meeting (physical or virtual).
- The names of the directors or shareholders present, absent, or participating remotely.
- A statement that notice was given or waived.
- A statement that a quorum was present.
- A description of each matter considered, including a summary of the discussion.
- The motions made and the votes taken.
- The actions approved.
- The time of adjournment.
- The signature of the officer preparing the minutes.

**What should not be in the minutes.** Minutes should avoid verbatim transcripts (which can create unnecessary evidentiary risks), personal opinions or editorial comments, or confidential information that the corporation would not want to disclose in litigation. Minutes should be a factual record of what occurred, not a narrative or a justification.

**Approval of minutes.** Minutes are typically approved at the next board meeting. Once approved, they are the authoritative record of the prior meeting. Corrections to minutes are rare and should be documented as amendments rather than silent edits.

### P.5 — The Stock Ledger

The stock ledger is the official record of share ownership. It shows:

- The name of each shareholder.
- The address of each shareholder.
- The class and series of shares held.
- The number of shares held.
- The date of issuance or transfer.
- The certificate number (for certificated shares) or the book-entry reference (for uncertificated shares).

The stock ledger must be kept current as shares are issued, transferred, and repurchased. Inaccuracies in the stock ledger can create serious problems — for example, a mismatch between the stock ledger and the shareholders attending a meeting can vitiate the validity of corporate actions taken at the meeting.

**Electronic stock ledgers.** Modern corporations typically maintain electronic stock ledgers using specialized software (Carta, Pulley, Shareworks). These platforms handle share issuances, option grants, vesting schedules, and transfer restrictions automatically.

### P.6 — Retention Periods

The WBCA does not specify precise retention periods for all corporate records, but best practices include:

- **Articles of incorporation and bylaws:** Permanent.
- **Minutes of shareholder and board meetings:** Permanent.
- **Stock ledger:** Permanent.
- **Financial statements:** At least 7 years for tax purposes; longer for litigation purposes.
- **Contracts and commitments:** At least the contract term plus any applicable statute of limitations.
- **Employment records:** Varies by type (federal and state employment laws impose specific retention periods).
- **Tax records:** At least 7 years federal; longer for certain items.

Electronic records should be backed up regularly and stored in a way that permits retrieval over the long term. Formats should be chosen with an eye toward long-term compatibility (e.g., PDF/A rather than proprietary word-processor formats).

## Appendix Q: Closely Held Corporation Disputes and Remedies

Closely held corporations are particularly prone to intractable disputes among their shareholder-directors. This appendix surveys the principal remedies available under Washington law.

### Q.1 — The Deadlock Problem

A "deadlock" occurs when the board or the shareholders cannot reach a decision on an important matter because the relevant voting group is evenly split. Deadlocks are especially common in two-shareholder corporations where each shareholder holds 50% of the voting power and serves as one of two directors.

Deadlock is not intrinsically a corporate-law problem — the corporation can continue operating in a deadlock, with neither party able to change the status quo. But deadlock becomes a problem when the status quo is unsustainable: when the corporation needs to make a decision to survive, when one shareholder's conduct is harming the corporation, or when the personal relationship between shareholders has broken down beyond repair.

### Q.2 — Judicial Dissolution for Deadlock

RCW 23B.14.300 authorizes judicial dissolution of a corporation by superior court. Grounds include:

- **Director deadlock** that cannot be broken and is causing irreparable injury to the corporation or to the shareholders.
- **Shareholder deadlock** that has prevented the election of directors for at least two consecutive annual meetings.
- **Oppression** — directors or those in control acting in a manner that is illegal, fraudulent, or oppressive.
- **Waste** — corporate assets being misapplied or wasted.

**Remedies short of dissolution.** The court has discretion to order remedies short of outright dissolution, including:

- Ordering a buyout of one shareholder's shares by the other.
- Appointing a provisional director to break the deadlock.
- Appointing a custodian to manage the corporation.
- Ordering a sale of the corporation to a third party.

**The oppression doctrine in Washington.** Washington courts have applied the oppression doctrine in several cases, recognizing that minority shareholders in closely held corporations can be vulnerable to abuse by controlling shareholders. The classic oppression patterns include freezing out a minority shareholder from employment, terminating distributions while the majority continues to receive salary, and denying access to corporate records.

### Q.3 — Derivative Actions

A derivative action is a lawsuit brought by a shareholder on behalf of the corporation, asserting claims that belong to the corporation but that the corporation has failed to pursue. Derivative actions are the principal vehicle for challenging director misconduct (such as self-dealing or gross mismanagement) in closely held corporations.

**Procedural requirements.** Derivative actions are subject to specific procedural requirements:

1. **Contemporaneous ownership.** The plaintiff must have been a shareholder at the time of the challenged transaction (and throughout the litigation).
2. **Demand or futility.** The plaintiff must either have made a demand on the board to pursue the claim (and been refused) or must plead with particularity why demand would be futile.
3. **Fair and adequate representation.** The plaintiff must be able to fairly and adequately represent the interests of the other shareholders.

These requirements are designed to prevent nuisance suits and to ensure that the corporation has a genuine opportunity to evaluate the claim before being forced into litigation.

**Washington derivative action procedure.** Washington follows the MBCA framework for derivative actions, which includes a demand requirement, a procedure for board or committee review of the demand, and the possibility of dismissal upon the recommendation of an independent committee. The specific procedures are set forth in Chapter 23B.07 and related rules of civil procedure.

### Q.4 — Direct Actions

In addition to derivative actions, a shareholder may bring a direct action for injuries suffered personally (rather than through the corporation). Examples of direct claims include:

- **Breach of the shareholder agreement.** If a shareholder agreement under RCW 23B.07.320 is breached, the aggrieved shareholder can sue directly as a party to the agreement.
- **Breach of preemptive rights.** A shareholder with preemptive rights who is denied those rights can sue directly.
- **Fraud in the inducement.** A shareholder who was defrauded into purchasing shares can sue directly for rescission or damages.
- **Oppression.** Some oppression claims can be brought directly, though many are also framed as derivative claims.

### Q.5 — Buyout Remedies

In many closely held corporation disputes, the practical remedy is a buyout: one shareholder buys out the other, ending the dispute and allowing the business to continue under unified ownership. Buyouts can be achieved through:

- **Negotiated agreements.** The parties negotiate a buyout price and terms, sometimes with the assistance of a mediator.
- **Court-ordered buyouts.** Under RCW 23B.14.300, the court can order a buyout as an alternative to dissolution.
- **Contractual buyouts.** Many shareholder agreements include buyout triggers (death, disability, termination of employment, deadlock) and pre-negotiated valuation methodologies.

Valuation in buyout disputes is often contested. The principal valuation methodologies include:

- **Book value.** The accounting value of the corporation's equity. Simple but often unrepresentative of fair value.
- **Discounted cash flow (DCF).** Projecting future cash flows and discounting to present value. More sophisticated but sensitive to assumptions.
- **Market multiples.** Comparing the corporation to publicly traded peers or recent transactions in the same industry. Useful when comparable data is available.
- **Asset-based.** Valuing the corporation's net assets, particularly for asset-heavy businesses.
- **Third-party appraisal.** Engaging an independent valuation expert to determine fair value.

Under RCW 23B.14.300, courts typically use "fair value" as the valuation standard — a standard that is closer to market value than to book value, and that may or may not include discounts for lack of control or lack of marketability (depending on the circumstances).

### Q.6 — Alternative Dispute Resolution

Many closely held corporation disputes can be resolved through alternative dispute resolution (ADR) rather than litigation. Common ADR mechanisms:

- **Mediation.** A neutral mediator facilitates negotiation between the parties. Non-binding unless the parties reach a settlement.
- **Arbitration.** A neutral arbitrator decides the dispute after a hearing. Binding if the parties have agreed to arbitration. Note: under RCW 23B.02.080, mandatory arbitration of internal corporate disputes cannot be imposed through the articles or bylaws, but shareholders can agree to arbitration contractually (e.g., in a shareholder agreement).
- **Expert determination.** A neutral expert (often an accountant or appraiser) resolves valuation disputes.

ADR is often faster, cheaper, and less public than litigation. It is particularly well-suited to closely held corporation disputes, where the parties often have ongoing business or personal relationships that litigation would permanently damage.

### Q.7 — Practical Guidance for Minimizing Disputes

The best way to handle closely held corporation disputes is to prevent them. Practical steps:

1. **Document the founding bargain.** A written shareholder agreement under RCW 23B.07.320 that captures the founders' understanding of governance, equity, and exit rights.
2. **Build in escape valves.** Buyout triggers, put/call options, and right-of-first-refusal provisions give shareholders a way out when relationships deteriorate.
3. **Specify valuation methods in advance.** Pre-negotiated valuation methodologies reduce the scope of later disputes.
4. **Communicate regularly.** Many disputes grow from misunderstandings. Regular board meetings, financial reporting, and open communication prevent disputes from escalating.
5. **Address compensation carefully.** Founders often receive both salary (for services) and distributions (as owners). Clear policies on both reduce the risk of conflict.
6. **Use employment agreements.** A formal employment agreement with each founder clarifies the employment terms separately from the ownership terms.
7. **Consider life events.** Death, disability, divorce, and retirement can destabilize closely held corporations. Plan for these events before they occur.

## Appendix R: Contemporary Practice — A Snapshot

This appendix offers a brief snapshot of contemporary Washington corporate practice, with particular attention to patterns that have emerged over the past decade.

### R.1 — Formation Volume and Trends

Washington has experienced significant growth in corporate formations over the past decade, driven by:

- The expansion of the technology sector centered in Seattle and the Eastside.
- The growth of biotechnology and life sciences in the Puget Sound region.
- The diversification of the broader Pacific Northwest economy.
- The increasing use of Washington as a base for remote-first businesses.

Annual corporate formations in Washington now run into the tens of thousands. The Secretary of State's online filing system has made formation fast and inexpensive, accommodating this growth.

### R.2 — Common Formation Patterns

Contemporary Washington corporate formations typically fall into several patterns:

**1. Solo-founder operating business.** A single founder forms a corporation to operate a business (consulting, professional services, small operating company). Usually a single class of common stock, a simple bylaws document, and no shareholder agreement.

**2. Co-founder startup.** Two or more founders form a corporation to develop a product or service with growth potential. Usually a single class of common stock initially, with authorized (but unissued) preferred stock for future financing rounds. Includes founder vesting, a shareholder agreement, and exculpation/indemnification provisions in the articles.

**3. Venture-backed startup.** A corporation that has raised or plans to raise institutional capital from venture funds. Complex capital structure with multiple classes of stock, protective provisions, drag-along rights, and other investor protections. Often Delaware-incorporated, but a non-trivial number remain in Washington.

**4. Holding company.** A corporation formed to hold ownership interests in other entities (operating subsidiaries, real estate, investments). Usually simple governance structure with the ultimate owners serving as directors.

**5. Professional services corporation.** A corporation formed for the provision of professional services (medicine, law, accounting, engineering, architecture). Subject to additional requirements under Chapter 18.100 RCW and professional licensing statutes.

**6. Real estate holding corporation.** Less common today (most real estate is held in LLCs), but still occasionally formed for specific tax or liability purposes.

### R.3 — Common Post-Formation Patterns

After formation, the principal activities of Washington corporations in their first few years include:

- **Hiring employees and issuing equity.** Founder vesting, employee option grants, stock purchase plans.
- **Raising capital.** Friends-and-family rounds, angel investments, convertible notes, SAFEs, venture financing.
- **Entering contracts.** Customer contracts, vendor contracts, employment contracts, real estate leases.
- **Regulatory compliance.** Tax filings, employment compliance, industry-specific compliance.
- **Governance maintenance.** Annual meetings, annual reports, board meetings, written consents.

These activities occupy the bulk of corporate counsel's time and represent the practical application of the corporate-law principles discussed in this chapter.

### R.4 — The Role of Technology

Technology has transformed corporate practice over the past decade. Key developments:

- **Online filing.** The Secretary of State's CCFS system has replaced paper filings for most purposes.
- **Electronic signatures.** DocuSign, Adobe Sign, and similar tools have replaced physical signatures for most corporate documents.
- **Cap table management software.** Carta, Pulley, Shareworks, and similar platforms have replaced paper stock ledgers.
- **Virtual meetings.** Zoom, Teams, and Google Meet have replaced in-person meetings for most routine board and shareholder activities.
- **AI-assisted drafting.** Legal AI tools are increasingly used for drafting routine corporate documents, though with careful human review.

These technological changes have not altered the substantive law but have dramatically reduced the friction and cost of corporate administration.

### R.5 — The Future

Washington corporate practice will continue to evolve in response to:

- Ongoing amendments to the WBCA by the Washington Legislature.
- Developments in Delaware corporate law (which strongly influence Washington).
- Federal securities law changes affecting public and near-public companies.
- Changes in business practices (remote work, distributed governance, new financing mechanisms).
- Judicial decisions interpreting existing statutes.

Practitioners should stay current with all of these dimensions. The Washington State Bar Association's Business Law Section publishes regular updates, the Legislature posts bill-tracking information on its website, and specialized publications (Washington Legal Times, WSBA Bar News, Law360) cover recent developments.

## Appendix T: A Concluding Observation on Corporate Law as a System

A final reflection may be useful. The Washington Business Corporation Act is often treated by non-lawyers — and even by practitioners focused narrowly on particular transactions — as a collection of discrete rules governing discrete situations. How many directors? What goes in the articles? How is a meeting called? These are the questions that prompt readers to consult the statute, and each question has a definite answer.

But the WBCA is more than a collection of rules. It is a system, with a particular internal logic and a particular allocation of authority, responsibility, and protection among its participants. Understanding the system as a whole is essential for understanding any of its parts.

### The Allocation of Authority

The central allocation of authority in the WBCA runs from shareholders to directors to officers to employees. Shareholders own; directors govern; officers manage; employees execute. Each tier has authority appropriate to its role, and each tier is accountable to the tier above.

Shareholders, as owners, have the ultimate authority — but it is exercised only in specific ways at specific moments. Shareholders elect directors, approve fundamental transactions, and amend the articles. They do not manage the business day to day.

Directors, as governors, have the operational authority. They establish strategy, appoint officers, approve contracts and transactions, monitor performance, and exercise oversight. They are accountable to shareholders through annual elections and to the courts through fiduciary duty doctrine.

Officers, as managers, execute the directors' decisions and run the business day to day. They are accountable to the directors who appointed them.

Employees, as executors, perform the tasks assigned to them by the officers. They are accountable to the officers who supervise them.

This hierarchical structure is not arbitrary. It reflects a judgment about how corporate decision-making should be organized to balance efficiency (concentration of authority in a small group of decision-makers) with accountability (the ability of higher tiers to check lower tiers).

### The Allocation of Responsibility

Responsibility tracks authority. Those who have authority also have responsibility for exercising it correctly. Directors have fiduciary duties because they have governance authority. Officers have similar duties because they have managerial authority. Shareholders have fewer duties because they have fewer active management responsibilities — but they are not wholly without duties, particularly in closely held contexts where controlling shareholders can face liability for oppression of minorities.

The statutory standard of conduct (RCW 23B.08.300) is the clearest articulation of this responsibility: directors must act in good faith, with care, and in the best interests of the corporation. These are not optional aspirations; they are legal duties.

### The Allocation of Protection

Authority and responsibility are counterbalanced by protection. Directors who might otherwise refuse to serve are protected by the business judgment rule, exculpation, indemnification, and insurance. Officers are similarly protected. Shareholders have limited liability, which protects their personal assets from corporate debts.

The layering of protections is deliberate. It reflects a judgment that corporate service — particularly director service — is valuable and should be encouraged by reducing personal-liability risk. Without these protections, no rational person would accept the responsibilities that corporate law imposes.

### The System in Balance

When the system is in balance, all three elements — authority, responsibility, protection — work together. Directors exercise authority carefully, knowing that they are protected if they act in good faith but exposed if they act badly. Officers manage prudently, knowing that their decisions will be reviewed by the board. Shareholders retain ultimate authority without micromanaging. The corporation pursues its purposes, and the various constituencies are served in rough proportion to the capital and effort they have committed.

When the system is out of balance — when directors have too much protection and too little responsibility, or when shareholders have too much authority and too little information, or when officers are insufficiently accountable to anyone — the system produces bad outcomes: corporate fraud, waste of capital, abuse of minority shareholders, destruction of businesses.

The job of corporate law is to maintain the balance. The Washington Business Corporation Act, through its many provisions and cross-references, is the instrument by which Washington has chosen to do so. It is imperfect, as all such instruments are, but it is the product of more than a century of accumulated experience with the challenges of corporate governance, and it represents a workable synthesis of the competing interests at stake.

Practitioners who understand the WBCA as a system — and not merely as a collection of rules — are better positioned to advise their clients effectively, to draft documents that reflect the underlying balance, and to anticipate the issues that may arise as corporations grow, evolve, and eventually transition or wind down.

This chapter has attempted, in considerable detail, to make that systemic understanding accessible to the careful reader. The material is technical and the volume of statutory text is substantial, but the underlying principles are neither obscure nor alien. A corporation is a creature of positive law — it exists because the legislature has created a framework in which it can exist — and the framework is open to inspection by any citizen who cares to look.
