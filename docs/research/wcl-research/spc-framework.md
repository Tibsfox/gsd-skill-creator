# The Washington Social Purpose Corporation

## A Doctrinal, Historical, and Comparative Analysis of RCW Chapter 23B.25

*A Working Paper in the PNW Research Series on Corporate Law and Mission-Driven Enterprise*

---

## Abstract

In 2012, the Washington State Legislature enacted Chapter 23B.25 of the Revised Code of Washington, creating the Social Purpose Corporation ("SPC"), a new corporate form authorizing for-profit Washington corporations to pursue enumerated social, environmental, and stakeholder objectives alongside, and not merely incidental to, the maximization of profit. Washington thereby joined a wave of roughly forty United States jurisdictions that, between 2010 and 2020, enacted statutes enabling mission-driven corporate governance. Yet Washington conspicuously did *not* adopt the "model benefit corporation legislation" promoted by B Lab and embraced by Maryland, California, New York, and most other benefit-corporation states. Instead, Washington's drafters chose a distinctly softer and more flexible architecture, rooted not in the B Lab template but in California's short-lived Flexible Purpose Corporation ("FPC") statute of 2011 (itself later renamed to "Social Purpose Corporation" by S.B. 1301 in 2014).

This paper offers a comprehensive doctrinal and comparative analysis of Washington's SPC framework. Part I traces the intellectual and historical context: the perceived dominance of the shareholder primacy orthodoxy traceable to *Dodge v. Ford Motor Co.* and popularized by Milton Friedman's 1970 essay; the emergence of B Lab and the model benefit corporation legislation; the state adoption wave of 2010-2015; and Washington's distinctive path away from the B Lab model. Part II provides a section-by-section doctrinal analysis of Chapter 23B.25, reproducing statutory text where precision matters and unpacking interpretive questions that remain open more than a decade after enactment. Part III focuses on the most consequential interpretive question the statute raises: the meaning and scope of the "may consider" formulation for director duties in RCW 23B.25.050, contrasting it with Pennsylvania-style constituency statutes, the Maryland-B Lab "shall consider" model, and Delaware's Public Benefit Corporation "balancing" duty under 8 Del. C. § 365. Part IV examines the operational center of gravity for SPC compliance: the annual Social Purpose Report required by RCW 23B.25.150. Part V presents structured comparative tables across United States benefit corporation regimes, international mission-driven corporate forms, and the unmodified Washington business corporation baseline. Part VI evaluates academic and practitioner critiques, the absence (as of this writing) of significant Washington appellate case law construing Chapter 23B.25, the "greenwashing" concern, and the capacity of the SPC architecture to support multi-generational mission persistence in the style of Patagonia, Newman's Own, or the German *Stiftung* model.

The paper's central claim is descriptive rather than normative: the Washington SPC statute is, by design, the *weakest* of the principal United States benefit corporation regimes measured against the axis of director duty intensity, third-party accountability, and enforcement. It is simultaneously the *most flexible*, imposing few prescriptive content requirements on social purpose statements or performance reporting. Whether that flexibility is a feature or a defect depends on one's prior about the function benefit corporation legislation should perform: expressive signaling and founder-protective optionality on one hand, versus substantive reorientation of fiduciary duty on the other. Washington's drafters explicitly chose the former.

## Part I. The Benefit Corporation Movement: Historical Context

### 1. The Shareholder Primacy Orthodoxy

No serious discussion of benefit corporation legislation can begin without first acknowledging the peculiar position the shareholder primacy doctrine occupies in American corporate law. It is simultaneously the subject of near-universal folk belief among business practitioners, journalists, and even many lawyers; the foundation of an enormous volume of scholarly commentary pro and con; and, as a matter of positive law, an almost entirely judge-made and scholar-constructed gloss rather than anything approaching a statutory rule. Understanding benefit corporation legislation requires understanding what problem the drafters of such legislation thought they were solving, and that problem is, at bottom, a perceived legal barrier rather than an actual one.

#### 1.1 *Dodge v. Ford Motor Co.* (1919)

The canonical judicial statement usually invoked in support of shareholder primacy is *Dodge v. Ford Motor Co.*, 170 N.W. 668 (Mich. 1919). The facts are famous. Henry Ford, sole dominant shareholder of the Ford Motor Company, announced in 1916 that rather than declare a special dividend from the company's accumulated surplus, he would instead reinvest the capital in plant expansion and in lower prices for cars, and would also cut special dividends because, in his telling, "my ambition is to employ still more men, to spread the benefits of this industrial system to the greatest possible number, to help them build up their lives and their homes." The Dodge brothers, minority shareholders, sued to compel payment of the dividend and to enjoin Ford's expansion plan. The Michigan Supreme Court held for the Dodges on the dividend question, ordering payment of roughly $19 million, though it declined to enjoin the business expansion under the business judgment rule.

The language that subsequent generations seized upon comes from roughly the middle of Justice Ostrander's opinion:

> A business corporation is organized and carried on primarily for the profit of the stockholders. The powers of the directors are to be employed for that end. The discretion of directors is to be exercised in the choice of means to attain that end, and does not extend to a change in the end itself, to the reduction of profits, or to the nondistribution of profits among stockholders in order to devote them to other purposes.

Read in isolation, this passage can be taken as a categorical rule of shareholder primacy. Read in context, it is less obviously so. The passage appears in the course of adjudicating a specific dispute between a controlling shareholder and minority shareholders; the minority was being squeezed out of profit distributions by a majority whose announced motive was philanthropic. The holding is arguably better understood as a rule against controller oppression than as a general rule of corporate purpose. As Professor Lynn Stout has influentially argued, the passage is dicta, and the subsequent *use* of *Dodge* as a statement of positive corporate law obligations is largely a twentieth-century scholarly invention.

Stout's empirical observation is striking. In the six decades from 1919 to 1979, *Dodge v. Ford* was cited in published opinions only thirty-seven times; over the four decades from 1980 to 2019, citations to the case in published opinions exploded to more than 1,100. The timing of this surge tracks not the emergence of any new legal rule but the rise of a particular *ideological* position within corporate law scholarship and business-school teaching: the law-and-economics reframing of the corporation as a nexus of contracts oriented around residual shareholder claimants.

Defenders of *Dodge* as a meaningful precedent, most prominently Stephen Bainbridge, have countered that dismissing the shareholder-primacy language as dicta underestimates how quickly it was absorbed into legal and scholarly discourse as a statement of black-letter law. Whatever the merits of this internecine debate, what matters for benefit corporation analysis is that as of the mid-2000s, both corporate lawyers and their mission-oriented entrepreneur clients *believed* — reasonably or not — that directors of Delaware (and similar) corporations were legally obligated to maximize shareholder wealth, and that any deliberate sacrifice of profit in service of social or environmental goals exposed directors to liability. The benefit corporation movement was an attempt to provide legal certainty against this perceived exposure, whether or not the exposure was real.

#### 1.2 Milton Friedman's 1970 Essay

If *Dodge v. Ford* supplied the perceived juridical foundation for shareholder primacy, Milton Friedman's 13 September 1970 *New York Times Magazine* essay, "The Social Responsibility of Business is to Increase its Profits," supplied the popular intellectual frame. Friedman's argument was characteristically direct. A corporate executive, he argued, is an "employee" of the shareholders with a direct responsibility "to conduct the business in accordance with their desires, which generally will be to make as much money as possible while conforming to the basic rules of the society, both those embodied in law and those embodied in ethical custom." To spend corporate money on social objectives — even laudable ones — is to impose, in effect, a tax on shareholders to fund the executive's personal preferences about social priorities, an activity for which elected government, not unelected business managers, is the constitutionally legitimate mechanism.

Friedman's essay cannot be understood apart from its historical moment. It appeared at the confluence of three concurrent shifts: the emergence of corporate governance as a distinct field of legal scholarship; the early stirrings of law-and-economics as a paradigm; and the rise of hostile takeover markets that would shortly make managerial accountability to dispersed shareholders a practical as well as theoretical concern. The essay is widely credited with — or blamed for — popularizing a shareholder primacy framing that, combined with *Dodge*'s judicial gloss, gradually hardened into what felt to mission-oriented founders in the 2000s like an impenetrable legal wall.

#### 1.3 The Perceived Barrier

By the early 2000s, a founder seeking to build a company with environmental or social objectives baked into its governance faced what her corporate counsel would have described, with varying degrees of confidence, as a thicket of concerns:

1. *Duty of care risk.* A director who knowingly traded expected profit for social benefit could, in theory, be accused of failing the duty of care owed to the corporation and its shareholders.
2. *Duty of loyalty risk.* Because the "best interests of the corporation" were widely (if incorrectly) equated with "best interests of the shareholders as residual claimants," pursuing non-shareholder interests could be framed as a disloyal subordination of shareholder interests to management's personal values.
3. *Takeover vulnerability.* Under the *Revlon* line of Delaware cases, when a sale of the company became inevitable, directors' duty shifted to obtaining the best price for shareholders; any baked-in mission commitment could be set aside at the critical moment.
4. *Capital markets signaling.* Investors, particularly institutional investors with their own fiduciary obligations, might read mission commitments as a red flag about management focus.

Whether any of these risks were real — and modern scholarship generally concludes that most were overstated — they felt real to founders, and they felt real to the lawyers advising them. Benefit corporation legislation was designed to eliminate the uncertainty, not to change the substantive law of fiduciary duty so much as to insulate mission-driven decision-making from plausible litigation attack.

### 2. B Lab and the Model Legislation

#### 2.1 The Founding of B Lab

B Lab was founded in 2006 by three former corporate executives and entrepreneurs: Jay Coen Gilbert, who had built and sold the basketball shoe and apparel company AND 1; Bart Houlahan, AND 1's former president; and Andrew Kassoy, a private equity veteran. Their animating insight was that investors, customers, and employees of socially oriented businesses lacked a reliable way to distinguish genuinely mission-driven enterprises from those merely engaging in marketing-grade corporate social responsibility. B Lab's initial product was accordingly a certification — the "Certified B Corporation" mark — that a company could earn by completing a rigorous self-assessment (the "B Impact Assessment") covering governance, workers, community, environment, and customers, and by amending its governing documents to permit consideration of non-shareholder interests.

Certification alone, however, proved insufficient for B Lab's ambitions. Certification was a contractual and reputational matter; it did not alter the legal duties of the directors of the certified company. A Delaware or California director of a Certified B Corporation remained, as far as the law was concerned, bound by whatever shareholder primacy norms her corporate domicile imposed. To solve this problem, B Lab turned to legislation.

#### 2.2 The Model Benefit Corporation Legislation

Working with a group of corporate lawyers led by Bill Clark of the Philadelphia firm Drinker Biddle & Reath, B Lab developed a "Model Benefit Corporation Legislation" intended to serve as a template that individual states could adopt with relatively minor jurisdictional adjustments. The model's animating principles were fourfold:

1. *Purpose.* A benefit corporation must have the purpose of creating "general public benefit," defined as "a material positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole, as assessed against a third-party standard, from the business and operations of a benefit corporation."
2. *Accountability.* Directors *shall* consider the effects of any action or inaction upon (i) shareholders; (ii) employees and workforce; (iii) customers as beneficiaries of the general or specific public benefit purposes; (iv) community and societal factors; (v) the local and global environment; (vi) the short-term and long-term interests of the benefit corporation; and (vii) the ability of the benefit corporation to accomplish its general and specific public benefit purposes.
3. *Transparency.* A benefit corporation must publish an annual "benefit report" assessing its overall social and environmental performance *against a third-party standard*, and must deliver the report to shareholders and make it publicly available.
4. *Enforcement.* A new cause of action, the "benefit enforcement proceeding," is created, though standing to bring it is limited to the corporation itself (derivatively), directors, or shareholders meeting a specified ownership threshold.

The "third-party standard" requirement is central to the model. It does not require use of B Lab's own B Impact Assessment — any "recognized standard for defining, reporting, and assessing corporate social and environmental performance" developed by an independent organization could qualify — but in practice the B Impact Assessment is the dominant choice, aligning the model legislation with B Lab's certification business.

### 3. The State Adoption Wave (2010-2015)

#### 3.1 Maryland: First Mover

On April 13, 2010, Governor Martin O'Malley of Maryland signed into law the nation's first benefit corporation statute, enacted as Senate Bill 690. Effective October 1, 2010, the statute closely tracked the B Lab model. Jay Coen Gilbert called it "a wonderful milestone and great first step in a much longer journey to create a new sector of the economy that is more inclusive and more sustainable." Maryland's law required benefit corporations to pursue general public benefit, imposed the multi-factor director consideration duty, required annual benefit reporting assessed against a third-party standard, and authorized the benefit enforcement proceeding.

#### 3.2 Vermont: L3C and a Different Angle

Vermont, though it also enacted a benefit corporation statute in 2010, is historically interesting primarily for an earlier experiment. On April 30, 2008, Vermont became the first state to authorize the "Low-Profit Limited Liability Company" or L3C. The L3C is a hybrid LLC that must have as its primary purpose a charitable or educational mission recognized under Internal Revenue Code § 170(c)(2)(B), must *not* have as a significant purpose the production of income or appreciation of property, and must not have a political or legislative purpose. The L3C was designed specifically to facilitate "program-related investments" by private foundations under IRC § 4944(c), allowing foundation dollars to flow into mission-oriented LLCs without jeopardizing the foundation's tax-exempt status.

The L3C experiment is ultimately a cautionary tale for benefit corporation analysis. By 2012, practitioners were already expressing skepticism about whether L3Cs meaningfully solved the PRI problem they were designed to address, and the form failed to catch on nationally. Commentary in the *Vermont Law Review* noted that, in practice, L3Cs provided little more than labeling — the underlying PRI rules still required the same case-by-case IRS diligence whether the recipient was an L3C, an ordinary LLC, or any other form. The L3C experience suggested that *creating a new corporate form is the easy part; persuading practitioners, investors, and regulators to treat it as meaningfully different is the hard part*. This lesson would recur with the Washington SPC.

#### 3.3 The Cascade: New Jersey, Virginia, California, New York, Delaware

Through 2011 and 2012, benefit corporation statutes moved rapidly through state legislatures. New Jersey enacted its statute in March 2011. Virginia followed in March 2011. California passed its benefit corporation statute (distinct from the Flexible Purpose Corporation law it simultaneously enacted) effective January 1, 2012. Hawaii, Louisiana, and South Carolina followed in 2012. New York enacted legislation in December 2011, effective February 2012.

The most consequential adoption came in 2013, when Delaware — the preeminent corporate domicile for publicly held and venture-backed private companies — enacted Subchapter XV of the Delaware General Corporation Law, creating the Public Benefit Corporation. Delaware's PBC statute is importantly different from the B Lab model in several respects, as discussed in detail in Part III below, but its adoption legitimated the benefit corporation concept for mainstream corporate practitioners and, crucially, for venture capital investors. By the mid-2020s, more than 3,000 PBCs had been formed in Delaware.

As of 2026, roughly forty United States jurisdictions authorize some form of benefit corporation or analogous mission-driven corporate structure. The variation among them is substantial, and the next section considers the structural dimensions along which Washington diverges from the mainstream of that variation.

### 4. Washington's Distinctive Path

#### 4.1 The California Flexible Purpose Corporation as Template

When the Washington State Bar Association's Business Law Section began in 2011 to consider whether Washington should adopt benefit corporation legislation, its corporate law practitioners looked not primarily at the Maryland or B Lab model but at an alternative template enacted contemporaneously in California: the Flexible Purpose Corporation, or FPC. California S.B. 201 (the Corporate Flexibility Act of 2011) was signed on October 9, 2011, and took effect on January 1, 2012. It authorized a new form of for-profit corporation — initially called the FPC, and later renamed the "Social Purpose Corporation" by S.B. 1301 effective January 1, 2015 — whose articles would specify one or more "special purposes" that the corporation would pursue alongside the creation of economic value for shareholders.

Crucially, California's FPC diverged from the B Lab model in three significant ways:

1. *No mandatory third-party standard.* The FPC was free to define its own metrics for evaluating performance against its special purpose; no external "recognized standard" was required.
2. *Softer director duty.* FPC directors were permitted — not required — to consider the special purposes in their decision-making. The B Lab "shall consider" formulation was rejected in favor of a "may consider" rule backed by a safe harbor.
3. *More flexible reporting.* The FPC's annual special purpose report was required to describe the corporation's objectives, efforts, and measures, but was not required to conform to any external standard or framework.

California's drafters — particularly members of the Corporations Committee of the Business Law Section of the State Bar of California — had deliberately crafted a more permissive framework than the B Lab model, in part because of concerns that the B Lab model's third-party standard requirement effectively privileged a single private certifier, and in part because of concerns about the justiciability of "general public benefit" as a legal standard.

#### 4.2 The WSBA Business Law Section Drafting Process

The Washington adoption effort was led by members of the Corporate Act Revision Committee of the WSBA Business Law Section, the same body that since 1989 has maintained and periodically updated the Washington Business Corporation Act (Title 23B). The committee's institutional perspective is important: these were practitioners whose day-to-day concerns included the litigation exposure of directors under the existing duty-of-care and duty-of-loyalty framework, the coherence of the statute book as a whole, and the workability of any new provisions within existing Washington corporate doctrine.

From that perspective, the California FPC template had four strong selling points. First, it slotted neatly into the existing Title 23B structure as a new chapter rather than requiring extensive amendment of other provisions. Second, its "may consider" duty formulation was consistent with existing Washington doctrine on director discretion and the business judgment rule, raising no obvious conflict with established case law. Third, its rejection of the third-party standard requirement avoided effectively mandating the use of any single certifying organization's methodology. Fourth, its flexibility with respect to the content of the social purpose statement and the performance report made the statute accessible to a broader range of businesses — not merely those large enough or sophisticated enough to engage with a formal certification regime.

The committee nonetheless made several modifications to the California template. The Washington statute uses the term "social purpose" rather than "special purpose" or "flexible purpose," anticipating the direction California would later take. It enumerates the general social purposes (employees/suppliers/customers; community; environment) in the statute itself rather than leaving them to the articles. It includes a name provision permitting — but not requiring — "Social Purpose Corporation" or "SPC" to appear in the corporate name. And it adopts a reporting regime with somewhat more specific content requirements than California's FPC statute.

Substitute House Bill 2239 was introduced in the 2011-2012 Washington Legislature, moved through both chambers, and was signed by Governor Christine Gregoire on March 30, 2012, taking effect June 7, 2012. It is codified as Chapter 23B.25 RCW.

#### 4.3 Why Not the B Lab Model?

The WSBA drafters' reasons for rejecting the B Lab model were not exhaustively documented in committee commentary, but three themes recur in contemporaneous practitioner writing:

1. *The "recognized standard" concern.* The B Lab model effectively required benefit corporations to assess performance against a "recognized standard" developed by an independent third party. In 2011-2012, the B Impact Assessment was by far the dominant — and in many practical respects the only — such standard. Effectively mandating reliance on a single private organization's methodology as a statutory obligation raised both normative concerns about de facto regulatory capture and practical concerns about what would happen if B Lab changed its methodology or ceased to exist.
2. *Justiciability.* The B Lab model's "general public benefit" language, defined as "a material positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole," is difficult to assess judicially. How would a court measure whether such an impact had been achieved? The Washington drafters preferred a more concrete enumeration of stakeholder categories that a director *could* consider, avoiding the need to develop judicial competence on a holistic social-impact assessment standard.
3. *Mainstreaming.* There was substantive disagreement within the Washington corporate bar about whether benefit corporation legislation was needed at all, and whether it might have negative unintended consequences for directors (for instance, by creating inference problems about the obligations of directors of *non*-benefit Washington corporations). A softer, more flexible statute — one that simply clarified that social purpose consideration was permissible without reorienting fiduciary duty in any dramatic way — was more likely to secure consensus and less likely to produce unintended doctrinal spillovers.

The resulting statute was, by design, the *softest* of the principal United States benefit corporation regimes, a point to which we return throughout this paper.

### 5. The 2014 Context and Subsequent Amendments

Chapter 23B.25 has remained remarkably stable since its 2012 enactment. No substantive rewrites have occurred. The principal subsequent activity affecting the statute's operation has been as follows:

- *Form updates.* The Office of the Washington Secretary of State has periodically updated its form articles of incorporation for SPCs, most recently in November 2019, without changing the underlying statutory scheme.
- *Filing fee adjustments.* The Corporations and Charities Division fee schedule has been updated multiple times, with the current SPC articles filing fee at $180.
- *WSBA Sourcebook updates.* The WSBA Business Law Section's Washington Business Corporation Act Sourcebook, which includes drafter commentary for each provision of Title 23B, has been updated to incorporate the social purpose chapter and provide interpretive guidance not available in the statute itself.

No material interpretive amendments — of the sort, for instance, that California enacted in 2014 to rebrand and tighten its FPC statute as the new "Social Purpose Corporation" — have been made to Washington's Chapter 23B.25. The statute as enacted in 2012 remains the governing law as of 2026.

It is also worth noting what *did not* happen. Washington did not adopt, and has never adopted, the B Lab model benefit corporation legislation. It did not add a "recognized third-party standard" requirement to the social purpose report. It did not create a benefit enforcement proceeding. It did not strengthen the director duty from "may consider" to "shall consider." The statute's 2012 architecture has persisted, with the result that Washington remains the most prominent outlier among populous states on benefit corporation structure.

## Part II. The Statutory Architecture of Chapter 23B.25

Chapter 23B.25 comprises sixteen numbered sections, beginning at RCW 23B.25.005 and ending at RCW 23B.25.150. It is organized approximately as follows: formation and cessation (sections 005, 010, 130, 140); purpose and articles (sections 020, 030, 040); governance (sections 050, 060, 070, 080); fundamental transactions (sections 090, 100, 110, 120); and reporting (section 150). This Part walks through each section, reproducing statutory text where precision matters and analyzing the interpretive questions each provision raises.

### 6. RCW 23B.25.005 — Formation and Cessation

The opening section of the chapter is short and structural. It provides two methods by which a Washington corporation may elect to be governed as a social purpose corporation, and cross-references the conversion mechanics of RCW 23B.25.130 and RCW 23B.25.140. The operative text reads:

> (1) Any corporation may elect to be governed as a social purpose corporation by one of the following means:
>
> (a) One or more persons may act as incorporator or incorporators of a social purpose corporation by delivering articles of incorporation that conform to the requirements of this chapter to the secretary of state for filing; or
>
> (b) Any corporation which is not a social purpose corporation may elect to become a social purpose corporation by complying with RCW 23B.25.130.
>
> (2) Any social purpose corporation may elect to cease to be governed as a social purpose corporation by complying with RCW 23B.25.140.

Three points are worth highlighting.

First, the statute contemplates only two *methods* of formation: at-formation election and conversion. It does not contemplate, for instance, an intermediate "partial adoption" in which some but not all SPC obligations apply. An entity is either an SPC subject to the full chapter or it is not.

Second, the phrase "any corporation" in subsection (1)(b) is broad. It reaches any corporation governed by Title 23B — which is to say, essentially any Washington for-profit corporation. It does not, however, directly reach LLCs, limited partnerships, or other non-23B entities, which would need to convert to a Washington corporation first and only then elect SPC status. RCW 23B.09, which governs entity conversions generally, provides the bridge.

Third, the statute's symmetric treatment of election and cessation — any corporation may elect in, any SPC may elect out — reflects a deliberate choice to avoid a one-way "mission lock." Washington SPCs, unlike UK Community Interest Companies with their irrevocable asset lock, can revoke their SPC status, though only through a heightened voting procedure discussed under RCW 23B.25.140 below. This is a significant policy choice worth flagging early: Washington's SPC framework does not purport to create a permanent commitment. It creates a *default-sticky* commitment that can be undone through a supermajority vote.

### 7. RCW 23B.25.010 — Subchapter-Wide Integration

Section 010 serves as the bridge between the SPC chapter and the rest of Title 23B:

> (1) Except as otherwise expressly stated in this chapter, the provisions of this title and all powers, rights, and obligations thereunder shall apply to social purpose corporations organized under this chapter, and references in this title to the term "corporation" shall be read to include social purpose corporations organized under this chapter.
>
> (2) Subject to any limitations contained in the articles of incorporation, a social purpose corporation may engage in any lawful business under RCW 23B.03.010.

This provision has two important implications. First, every provision of Washington's Business Corporation Act — governance, shares, dividends, shareholder meetings, indemnification, dissolution — applies to SPCs by default, except where Chapter 23B.25 provides otherwise. The SPC is, structurally, an ordinary 23B corporation with an additional purpose overlay rather than a fundamentally different entity. Second, by cross-referencing RCW 23B.03.010 — the general purposes provision of Title 23B, which authorizes corporations to engage in "any lawful business" — the statute confirms that SPCs are not purpose-limited in the way that some Washington non-profit corporations are. An SPC can do anything a regular 23B corporation can do, as long as its articles do not narrow that authority and as long as the conduct is consistent with the stated social purpose.

This "additive overlay" structure distinguishes Washington's statute from, for example, the UK CIC, which imposes structural restrictions on dividends and asset distributions that narrow the entity's economic flexibility. A Washington SPC's cap table, dividend policy, redemption rights, class structures, and governance look identical to those of an unmarked 23B corporation. What differs is the purpose statement and the reporting obligation.

### 8. RCW 23B.25.020 — General Social Purposes

Section 020 is the substantive heart of the chapter. It provides:

> Every corporation governed by this chapter must be organized to carry out its business purpose under RCW 23B.03.010 in a manner intended to promote positive short-term or long-term effects of, or minimize adverse short-term or long-term effects of, the corporation's activities upon any or all of (1) the corporation's employees, suppliers, or customers; (2) the local, state, national, or world community; or (3) the environment.

Several features of this formulation merit close attention.

#### 8.1 The Three Categories

The statute enumerates three categories. These are importantly *narrower* than the six or seven categories typically enumerated in B Lab model statutes (which often list shareholders, employees, suppliers, customers, community, environment, and sometimes creditors as separate categories). Washington groups the first three commercial-relationship categories (employees, suppliers, customers) into a single bucket. It keeps community and environment as separate categories. It omits any explicit mention of creditors. And, critically, it does not require the SPC to consider shareholders — a silence that is intentional, since shareholder consideration remains the baseline duty under Title 23B for all corporations including SPCs.

The three categories are also written in the disjunctive: "any or all of." An SPC is not required to pursue benefits across all three. It may be organized to promote positive effects on employees alone, or to minimize environmental harm alone, or any combination. This is substantially more permissive than the B Lab model, which requires pursuit of "general public benefit" defined as material positive impact "on society and the environment, taken as a whole."

#### 8.2 "Promote Positive" and "Minimize Adverse"

The statute offers two alternative framings: the SPC may be organized either to "promote positive short-term or long-term effects" or to "minimize adverse short-term or long-term effects." This bidirectional formulation matters because it accommodates different business models. A company in an inherently low-impact sector may credibly promise to *promote* positive effects through its active operations; a company in an inherently higher-impact sector (extractive industries, heavy manufacturing) may more credibly promise to *minimize* the negative externalities of operations it cannot avoid. The statute accommodates both postures without taking a view on which is more virtuous.

#### 8.3 The Short-Term / Long-Term Axis

The statute explicitly recognizes both "short-term" and "long-term" effects as appropriate targets. This is not an accident. One of the chronic doctrinal concerns about shareholder primacy is that, under active takeover markets and quarterly reporting pressures, directors may be forced to sacrifice long-term value for short-term price performance. By explicitly authorizing SPCs to focus on long-term effects, the statute provides textual cover for directors resisting short-term pressure in favor of long-term mission fulfillment.

#### 8.4 The Nature of the Obligation

A critical interpretive question arises from the phrase "must be organized to carry out its business purpose ... in a manner intended to promote ... effects upon any or all of" the enumerated categories. Is this a *substantive* obligation — the SPC must actually achieve positive effects — or merely a *procedural* obligation — the SPC must have been organized *with the intent* to do so? The statute's use of "intended to" supports the procedural reading. What is required is organizational intent, not demonstrated impact. This reading is consistent with the rest of the chapter, particularly the Social Purpose Report provision, which requires reporting on efforts and measures rather than on achieved impact benchmarks.

### 9. RCW 23B.25.030 — Specific Social Purposes

Section 030 is a brief enabling provision:

> In addition to the general social purpose set forth in RCW 23B.25.020, every corporation governed by this chapter may have one or more specific social purposes for which the corporation is organized.

This allows SPCs to supplement the general statutory categories with more particular commitments — for instance, "supporting the economic development of rural Washington communities" or "promoting sustainable forestry practices in the Pacific Northwest." The specific social purpose is permissive, not mandatory; an SPC may rest solely on the general social purpose framework of section 020 without specifying anything more particular.

The interaction between general and specific purposes has one practical consequence. If an SPC adopts a specific social purpose, amending that purpose triggers the enhanced voting requirements of RCW 23B.25.090 (two-thirds approval of each voting group). If the SPC relies only on the general purpose framework, amendments to the articles that do not "materially change" a social purpose are presumably governed by the default amendment procedures of RCW 23B.10 rather than the chapter's enhanced voting thresholds.

### 10. RCW 23B.25.040 — Articles of Incorporation

Section 040 prescribes the content of SPC articles. Paragraph (1) contains the mandatory elements. The articles must include:

1. A corporate name containing "Social Purpose Corporation" or "SPC" — though as discussed below, the availability of both long and short forms, and the lack of mandate for either outside the name itself, gives SPCs considerable naming flexibility.
2. A statement that the corporation is organized as a social purpose corporation subject to this chapter.
3. A statement of the general social purpose or purposes set forth in RCW 23B.25.020 that the corporation is organized to promote.
4. If applicable, a statement of any specific social purpose or purposes under RCW 23B.25.030.
5. A mandatory investor-protection disclosure, the statutory text of which reads (approximately):

> The mission of this social purpose corporation is not necessarily compatible with and may be contrary to maximizing profits and earnings for shareholders, or maximizing shareholder value in any sale, merger, acquisition, or other similar actions of the corporation.

This mandatory investor-protection disclosure serves multiple functions. It provides notice to prospective investors that the SPC form contemplates trade-offs between profit maximization and mission fulfillment. It supplies affirmative defense material for directors later sued for allegedly sacrificing profit in favor of mission. And it contributes to a documentary record showing that shareholders had notice, at the time of their investment, that the corporation was not organized on a pure shareholder-wealth-maximization basis.

Paragraph (2) of section 040 contains *optional* provisions the articles may include. These include:

- Provisions requiring directors and officers to consider the social purpose in specific circumstances (converting the "may consider" default of section 050 into a contractual "shall consider" obligation).
- Provisions requiring assessment of social purpose performance against a third-party standard (allowing SPCs that wish to do so to opt into something similar to the B Lab model requirement).
- Enhanced voting requirements for specified corporate actions.
- Limitations on the corporation's duration.

The Washington drafters' decision to make these features optional rather than mandatory is significant. An SPC whose founders want a B Lab–style "shall consider" duty and third-party assessment can get it by writing those provisions into the articles. But the default is the softer framework of sections 050 and 150.

Paragraphs (3) and (4) impose disclosure obligations on the corporation in connection with share issuances and transfers. Prospective shareholders are entitled to receive the articles before purchasing shares. Transferring shareholders must notify the corporation of transfers, and the corporation must furnish articles to prospective transferees within a reasonable period. These provisions parallel the investor-protection function of the mandatory articles disclosure: they ensure that every person who becomes a shareholder of an SPC has had an opportunity to read, in the articles themselves, that the corporation's mission may be inconsistent with profit maximization.

### 11. RCW 23B.25.050 — Director Duties and the "May Consider" Formulation

RCW 23B.25.050 is, without exaggeration, the most important provision of Chapter 23B.25. It defines the fiduciary duty landscape that Washington SPC directors inhabit, and it is the principal point at which the Washington statute diverges from the B Lab and Delaware PBC alternatives. The provision combines three elements: a reaffirmation of the baseline duty of care, an explicit authorization for directors to consider social purposes, and a safe harbor.

Summarizing the statutory text:

- Directors must discharge their duties in good faith, with the care of an ordinarily prudent person in a like position, in a manner reasonably believed to be in the best interests of the corporation, pursuant to the general director duty provisions of RCW 23B.08.300.
- In discharging those duties, directors *may* consider — and give weight to — one or more of the corporation's social purposes as the director deems relevant, unless the articles of incorporation provide otherwise.
- Any action that the director reasonably believes will promote the social purposes is deemed to be in the best interests of the corporation.
- A director is not liable to the corporation, shareholders, or any other person for actions taken in good faith in compliance with these provisions.
- The chapter creates no duties to, or rights in, persons other than the corporation and its shareholders.

Each of these elements deserves unpacking.

#### 11.1 "May Consider" — The Softest Formulation

The word "may" in "may consider" is load-bearing. It is the principal marker distinguishing the Washington statute from the Maryland / B Lab model (which uses "shall consider") and from the Delaware PBC statute (which imposes an affirmative balancing duty). Washington gives its directors permission to consider social purposes; it does not require them to do so.

The practical consequence is that a Washington SPC director who entirely ignores the corporation's stated social purpose in a particular decision is *not*, by virtue of that fact alone, in breach of fiduciary duty. The director might still be in breach of duty on other grounds — for example, failing to exercise due care in understanding the consequences of the decision — but the mere failure to consider social purpose is not itself actionable. This is a substantially softer duty than the B Lab model contemplates. Under the B Lab model, a director who failed to consider the enumerated stakeholder categories in a material decision would expose herself to derivative liability in a "benefit enforcement proceeding."

The "may consider" formulation is, however, buttressed by two features that soften the contrast. First, articles of incorporation may convert "may" into "shall" by explicit provision, per RCW 23B.25.040(2). Founders who want a stronger duty can get one. Second, the safe harbor — the statement that actions reasonably believed to promote social purposes are "deemed to be in the best interests of the corporation" — does significant work even under the permissive default. A director who *does* consider and weight the social purpose is insulated from challenge on the ground that she subordinated profit to mission.

#### 11.2 The Safe Harbor

The safe harbor is structurally important. Without it, a "may consider" authorization would be almost meaningless: even if a director *could* consider social purposes, she would still face liability exposure whenever doing so arguably reduced expected shareholder returns. The safe harbor eliminates that exposure by declaring that the consideration of social purposes — when undertaken in good faith and with a reasonable belief that the action will promote those purposes — is *conclusively* in the best interests of the corporation. The director need not separately demonstrate that the action was also in shareholders' pecuniary interest.

The safe harbor is conditioned on the "reasonably believes" language, which imports the business judgment rule's standard form. A court evaluating a challenge to an SPC director's mission-oriented decision would not second-guess the substantive merits of the decision; it would ask only whether the director's belief that the decision would promote social purposes was reasonable in light of the information available at the time. This is a deferential standard, and it effectively extends business judgment rule protection to mission-oriented decision-making.

#### 11.3 The Third-Party Bar

The last element of section 050 is the provision stating that the chapter creates no duties or rights for persons other than the corporation and its shareholders. This is a crucial limit on the scope of SPC director duties. Even though SPCs are authorized (and, under the statute's logic, expected) to consider stakeholder groups — employees, suppliers, customers, community, environment — those stakeholder groups are not given any right of action against directors under Chapter 23B.25. A Washington SPC employee cannot sue the directors of her employer for failing to consider her interests. A community group cannot sue for failing to consider community impacts. Only the corporation itself, and its shareholders in derivative capacity, have standing to enforce the duties.

This is consistent with the B Lab model, which similarly restricts standing in benefit enforcement proceedings to shareholders, directors, and owners of specified aggregate voting power. It is consistent with the Delaware PBC statute, which similarly denies standing to stakeholder beneficiaries. And it is consistent with Pennsylvania-style constituency statutes, which authorize director consideration of non-shareholder interests without creating any private right of action for those non-shareholders. In this respect, Washington's SPC framework participates in a broad American consensus: benefit corporation legislation expands *permissive* director consideration without expanding the universe of persons who can sue directors.

### 12. RCW 23B.25.060 — Officer Duties

Section 060 parallels section 050 for officers rather than directors. An officer must discharge duties in good faith, with ordinary prudent care, in a manner reasonably believed to be in the best interests of the corporation; may consider social purposes as the officer deems relevant (unless the articles provide otherwise); is entitled to the same safe harbor; and is subject to the same third-party bar. The parallelism is deliberate: it would be incoherent to authorize directors to consider social purposes while leaving officers (who actually implement board decisions) subject to a stricter shareholder-primacy duty.

The officer duty is of more practical significance than it may appear. Most operational decisions — pricing, product design, vendor selection, hiring — are made by officers rather than boards. If an officer could not consider the corporation's social purposes in those daily operational decisions, the SPC structure would fail to achieve its practical objectives even if directors were fully empowered to consider social purposes in board-level decisions. Section 060 closes that potential gap.

### 13. RCW 23B.25.070 — Share Representation

Section 070 is a technical provision addressing whether SPC shares may be represented by certificate or not. It parallels RCW 23B.06.260 (the general provision for standard corporations) and has no substantive impact on SPC operation. Its inclusion in the chapter is a housekeeping matter, not a substantive innovation.

### 14. RCW 23B.25.080 — Shareholder-Only Derivative Proceedings

Section 080 restates and localizes the general derivative action framework for SPCs. It provides:

> (1) No proceeding may be instituted or maintained in the right of any social purpose corporation under this title by any party other than a shareholder of the social purpose corporation.
>
> (2) A shareholder bringing a proceeding must have held shares either when the disputed transaction occurred, or acquired shares through operation of law from someone who owned shares at that time.
>
> (3) Any such proceeding must follow the procedural requirements established in RCW 23B.07.400.

This reinforces the third-party bar of section 050 from the procedural side. Whereas section 050 provides that SPC directors owe no duty to non-shareholder stakeholders, section 080 provides that non-shareholders may not bring derivative proceedings on behalf of the corporation to enforce any such imagined duty. The two provisions together ensure that SPC accountability operates through the same shareholder-centric channels as ordinary Washington corporate law.

### 15. RCW 23B.25.090 — Amendment of Social Purposes

When an SPC wishes to amend its articles of incorporation in a way that materially changes one or more of its social purposes, section 090 imposes heightened voting requirements. The amendment must be approved by (i) two-thirds of the voting group comprising all votes entitled to be cast on the amendment; (ii) two-thirds of the holders of outstanding shares of each class or series, voting as separate voting groups; and (iii) two-thirds of each other voting group entitled under the articles to vote separately on the amendment. Articles may impose a higher threshold.

Two interpretive points matter. First, the provision applies only to amendments that "materially change" a social purpose. An amendment that rewords the social purpose statement without substantively altering its scope does not trigger the enhanced vote. Second, the two-thirds requirement applies to both the aggregate shareholder vote *and* separate class/series votes. This double requirement means that a supermajority of the common shareholders cannot override a protected class's interest in preserving the social purpose; any class whose articles grant separate voting rights retains a veto on social purpose changes.

### 16. RCW 23B.25.100 — Merger and Share Exchange

Section 100 imposes enhanced voting requirements for plans of merger or share exchange under which the SPC would not survive. The requirement mirrors the section 090 threshold: two-thirds of the voting group comprising all votes, two-thirds of each class or series voting separately, and two-thirds of each other voting group under the articles. An exception applies if the surviving entity is itself an SPC with specific social purposes that do not materially diverge from the disappearing SPC's purposes; in that case, only the default merger voting requirements of RCW 23B.11 apply.

This provision is the most significant mission-protection device in the chapter. Without section 100, an SPC could be merged into an ordinary (non-SPC) corporation on a simple majority vote, effectively ending the mission commitment through a transactional back door. Section 100 raises the bar to two-thirds, giving founders and early mission-aligned investors meaningful protection against later dilution and exit.

### 17. RCW 23B.25.110 — Asset Sales

Section 110 applies the same enhanced voting threshold — two-thirds of the aggregate voting group plus two-thirds of each class or series — to sales, leases, exchanges, or other dispositions of "all or substantially all" of the corporation's property that would materially alter the social purpose. This is the asset-sale analogue to section 100's merger-protection provision. Together, sections 090, 100, and 110 provide three parallel supermajority triggers for the most important categories of corporate action that could undermine the social purpose: charter amendments, mergers, and asset sales.

### 18. RCW 23B.25.120 — Dissenters' Rights

Section 120 authorizes shareholders to dissent from certain corporate actions affecting SPC status and obtain fair value for their shares under RCW 23B.13. This provides an exit option for shareholders who disagree with a two-thirds majority's decision to, for example, convert the SPC into an ordinary corporation. The dissenters' rights mechanism is a standard feature of Washington corporate law, and section 120 simply extends it to cover the specific categories of SPC-related fundamental changes.

### 19. RCW 23B.25.130 — Conversion To SPC Status

Section 130 establishes the mechanics by which an existing non-SPC Washington corporation can convert into an SPC. The process has three components.

First, the corporation must adopt a "plan of election" meeting certain content requirements. The plan must provide that each share of the same class or series of the electing corporation will be treated equally with respect to any cash, rights, securities, or other property issued in connection with the conversion, unless all shareholders in the affected class or series consent otherwise. The plan must include the proposed amendments to the articles of incorporation necessary to satisfy RCW 23B.25.040(1). The plan may specify any other conditions or terms, and may be abandoned by the board before the effective date subject to contractual obligations.

Second, the plan must be approved by the board of directors, which must recommend the plan to shareholders unless it makes a determination (such as a conflict of interest) that warrants the absence of a recommendation, in which case the reason must be disclosed.

Third, the plan must be approved by shareholders by the enhanced vote: two-thirds of the aggregate voting group, plus two-thirds of each class or series voting separately. The same threshold applies here as to amendments, mergers, and asset sales. Articles may require a higher percentage.

Upon approval and filing of the amended articles with the Secretary of State, the corporation becomes an SPC subject to the full chapter, and its existence is deemed to commence from its original incorporation date. Pre-conversion liabilities and personal liabilities of shareholders, directors, and officers remain unaffected.

### 20. RCW 23B.25.140 — Conversion From SPC Status

Section 140 is the mirror image of section 130, governing SPC-to-standard-corporation conversion. The procedural architecture is the same: plan of election with equal treatment of shares within each class, board recommendation (or disclosed reason for non-recommendation), two-thirds vote of the aggregate voting group plus two-thirds vote of each class or series voting separately, and articles amendment to remove the SPC-specific provisions.

The policy significance of section 140 is that Washington does *not* impose a permanent mission lock. An SPC can become a non-SPC, provided the required supermajority supports the change. This distinguishes Washington from jurisdictions with irrevocable commitments — most prominently the UK CIC, whose asset lock prevents the CIC from being dissolved or converted in a manner that distributes assets to non-asset-locked recipients. A Washington SPC's mission commitment is procedurally sticky but ultimately reversible.

Whether this is a virtue or a vice is contested. Supporters of permanent mission commitments argue that the point of mission-lock structures is to protect the founder's purpose against later market-driven pressure to exit; a reversible lock is vulnerable to the same pressures that motivated adoption in the first place. Opponents argue that permanent locks are paternalistic and inflexible, foreclosing legitimate responses to changed circumstances; a two-thirds supermajority is a sufficient safeguard. Washington's drafters took the second view.

### 21. RCW 23B.25.150 — The Social Purpose Report

Section 150 is, in operational terms, the heart of SPC compliance. Every Washington SPC must publish an annual Social Purpose Report. The statutory requirements, summarized:

#### 21.1 Timing

The report must be furnished to shareholders not later than four months after the close of the corporation's fiscal year. Four months is a relatively generous window compared to the ninety-day SEC 10-K deadline applicable to most U.S. public companies, and more than adequate for a private SPC to compile operational narrative content.

#### 21.2 Publication

The report must be "publicly accessible, free of charge, at the corporation's principal internet website address." What counts as the "principal internet website address" is not further defined. For an SPC with a single corporate website, the analysis is straightforward. For an SPC with multiple websites (a corporate investor-relations site and a consumer-product site, for example), the statute implicitly requires the SPC to identify which is "principal" and to publish the report there. The report "shall remain available on that website through the end of the corporation's fiscal year" — a somewhat awkward formulation that, read literally, means the report must be available from its initial publication date through the end of the current fiscal year (i.e., for approximately eight months following publication, assuming publication at the four-month deadline). The statute does not explicitly require archival retention of prior-year reports, though practitioner guidance generally recommends maintaining an archive of all prior reports for due-diligence and continuity purposes.

#### 21.3 Content

The report must include narrative discussion of the SPC's social purposes and the corporation's efforts to promote them. The statute enumerates specific content elements the report "may" include — a permissive rather than mandatory list — covering:

- Short-term and long-term objectives related to the social purposes.
- Material actions taken during the fiscal year in furtherance of the social purposes.
- Expected future actions.
- A description of the financial, operating, or other measures used by the corporation during the fiscal year for evaluating its performance in achieving its social purpose.

The use of "may include" rather than "shall include" means the statute imposes a *floor* (some narrative discussion of social purposes and efforts to promote them) but not a detailed content checklist. An SPC's report that discusses only its current-year actions, without addressing long-term objectives or measures, arguably satisfies the statute, though such minimal reports would be practically vulnerable to criticism.

#### 21.4 SEC Alternative

Publicly-traded SPCs that file annual reports with the Securities and Exchange Commission may satisfy the Social Purpose Report requirement by including the required content in their SEC annual reports. This accommodation is of limited practical importance because most Washington SPCs are privately held, but it ensures that public-company SPCs need not generate a wholly separate document.

#### 21.5 Failure Remedies

The statute provides that failure to furnish the report does not affect the validity of any corporate action. A delinquent SPC does not lose its corporate existence, its limited liability, or the validity of its contracts and transactions merely by failing to publish a Social Purpose Report. This is in marked contrast to regimes (like certain audit requirements in other contexts) where failure to comply can invalidate underlying corporate actions.

However, the superior court of the county where the SPC's registered office is located may *summarily* order delivery of the report if the SPC has failed to provide reports for two consecutive fiscal years. Several features of this remedy are worth noting:

- *Two-consecutive-year trigger.* A single missed report does not trigger the court remedy; the SPC must have failed to file for two consecutive years. This is a relatively generous grace period.
- *Summary procedure.* The order is available on a "summary" basis, meaning expedited proceedings without full trial on the merits.
- *Standing.* The statute does not explicitly identify who may petition for the order. In practice, standing presumably extends to shareholders and to the SPC itself through derivative action, consistent with the section 080 shareholder-only derivative action rule. The Attorney General does not, on the face of the statute, have standing to enforce.
- *Relief.* The remedy is delivery of the report, not damages. The court can compel publication but cannot (on the face of the statute) impose monetary penalties for the failure.

The relatively toothless enforcement mechanism is one of the sharpest contrasts between the Washington SPC framework and the B Lab model. Under B Lab's benefit enforcement proceeding, shareholders can sue for damages and injunctive relief for a director's failure to pursue the general public benefit or comply with the benefit report requirement. Washington offers only specific performance of the reporting obligation, and only after two consecutive years of non-compliance, and only in the limited jurisdictional context of the county of registered office.

## Part III. Director Duties: A Deep Analysis

Part II above canvassed the statutory architecture of Chapter 23B.25 as a whole. This Part focuses on what is arguably the most doctrinally consequential question the statute raises: the legal character of the director duty under RCW 23B.25.050. How does the Washington "may consider" formulation relate to, and differ from, (a) the baseline director duty applicable to ordinary Washington corporations, (b) the Pennsylvania-style constituency statute tradition, (c) the Maryland / B Lab "shall consider" formulation, and (d) the Delaware PBC "balancing" duty? The answer matters both for the theoretical understanding of what benefit corporation legislation does, and for practical counsel to SPC directors and their advisors.

### 22. The Baseline: Duty of Care, Duty of Loyalty, and the Business Judgment Rule

#### 22.1 The Duty of Care

Under RCW 23B.08.300 — the general director duty provision of the Washington Business Corporation Act — a director must discharge her duties "in good faith; with the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances; and in a manner the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation." This is the standard Model Business Corporation Act formulation adopted by Washington in 1989 and left essentially unchanged since.

The duty of care requires directors to (i) become sufficiently informed before making material decisions, (ii) deliberate on the basis of that information, and (iii) reach a decision that a reasonable person could conclude serves the corporation's interests. The business judgment rule provides substantial deference to the substantive outcome of the director's decision: a court reviewing a duty-of-care claim will not second-guess the merits of the decision so long as the procedural requirements were satisfied and the director had no disabling conflict of interest.

#### 22.2 The Duty of Loyalty

The duty of loyalty prohibits directors from engaging in self-dealing transactions without proper safeguards, from usurping corporate opportunities that belong to the corporation, and — more generally — from subordinating the corporation's interests to their own. Unlike the duty of care, the duty of loyalty is not typically protected by the business judgment rule; self-dealing transactions are subject to entire-fairness review, and loyalty breaches cannot typically be exculpated by charter provisions.

#### 22.3 The "Best Interests of the Corporation" Question

The critical interpretive question for benefit corporation analysis is what "best interests of the corporation" means in RCW 23B.08.300. The statute does not define the phrase, and Washington case law on the question is sparse. Most commentators assume, by reference to broader American corporate law norms, that "best interests of the corporation" is roughly equivalent to "best interests of the shareholders as residual claimants" — the shareholder primacy reading. But this equivalence is not textually compelled, and there is a competing reading under which "the corporation" is a distinct entity whose interests are not reducible to those of any single constituency.

The SPC statute exploits this ambiguity in an important way. Section 050 does not purport to change the definition of "best interests of the corporation" as applied to SPCs. It instead declares, by statutory fiat, that actions reasonably believed to promote the SPC's social purposes are *deemed* to be in the best interests of the corporation. This deeming rule sidesteps the underlying interpretive question: whether or not the social purpose action would *actually* be in the best interests of a shareholder-primacy-defined corporation, it is *treated as such* for purposes of director duty analysis. The director has a statutory safe harbor.

### 23. Constituency Statutes and the Pennsylvania Tradition

#### 23.1 The Origins of Constituency Statutes

Benefit corporation statutes are sometimes described as the descendants of "constituency statutes," an earlier generation of corporate law reforms that authorized directors to consider non-shareholder interests. The first and most famous constituency statute was enacted in Pennsylvania in 1983, in response to a specific political moment: Scott Paper Company and Gulf Oil Corporation, both facing potential hostile takeovers, threatened to leave Pennsylvania if the legislature did not provide their directors with greater discretion to resist acquisitions in ways that considered employee and community interests. The Pennsylvania legislature obliged.

Pennsylvania's statute — and the roughly thirty constituency statutes subsequently enacted by other states — typically authorize directors, in discharging their duties, to consider the effects of action on employees, customers, suppliers, creditors, communities in which corporate facilities are located, and sometimes "any other factors the director considers pertinent." These statutes are *permissive*, not mandatory. They do not require directors to consider non-shareholder interests; they merely authorize doing so.

#### 23.2 The Takeover Context

Constituency statutes are best understood in light of their historical context. The 1980s were the peak of hostile takeover activity in American corporate law. Directors facing unwanted tender offers sought doctrinal tools to resist acquirers whose offers might maximize immediate shareholder payouts but would impose severe costs on other stakeholders through post-acquisition layoffs, asset stripping, and relocations. Delaware's courts gradually developed the *Unocal* and *Revlon* framework as a judge-made response. State legislatures in jurisdictions like Pennsylvania provided a statutory counterpart: an explicit grant of discretion to directors to consider non-shareholder interests.

The effect of constituency statutes was, however, quite limited in practice. They did not create a duty to consider non-shareholder interests, and they did not create a private right of action for non-shareholders to enforce any such duty. They functioned primarily as an affirmative defense available to directors sued for refusing to pursue a hostile-offer-maximizing strategy. And because most significant takeover litigation took place in Delaware, which did not adopt a constituency statute (relying instead on its judge-made framework), the constituency statutes' direct doctrinal impact was modest.

#### 23.3 Washington's Pre-2012 Position

Washington did not enact a constituency statute. Before 2012, Washington corporate law did not provide any explicit textual authorization for directors to consider non-shareholder interests. The absence was not, however, interpreted as a positive prohibition. Washington courts had not held that directors were *forbidden* to consider non-shareholder interests; they had simply not addressed the question in any systematic way. A Washington director seeking to consider non-shareholder interests in 2011 would have done so in a zone of legal uncertainty, without either a statutory authorization or a clear prohibition.

The SPC statute fills this gap in a narrow way. It does not create a generalized constituency statute applicable to all Washington corporations. It creates an opt-in regime for corporations that affirmatively elect to become SPCs, and provides explicit textual authorization for SPC directors to consider social purposes. Washington corporations that do *not* elect SPC status remain in the zone of uncertainty. This point is sometimes missed in casual discussions of the SPC statute: the statute does not change the duties of directors of ordinary Washington corporations. It creates a safe space only within the SPC structure.

### 24. The Maryland / B Lab Duty Formulation

The B Lab model benefit corporation legislation, as adopted (with minor variations) in Maryland, California, New York, New Jersey, and most benefit-corporation states, uses notably stronger duty language than Washington's. The key phrases:

> In discharging the duties of their respective positions and in considering the best interests of the benefit corporation, the board of directors, committees of the board, and individual directors of a benefit corporation *shall consider* the effects of any action or inaction upon:
>
> (1) the shareholders of the benefit corporation;
> (2) the employees and workforce of the benefit corporation and its subsidiaries and suppliers;
> (3) the interests of customers as beneficiaries of the general public benefit or a specific public benefit purpose of the benefit corporation;
> (4) community and societal factors, including those of each community in which offices or facilities of the benefit corporation or its subsidiaries or suppliers are located;
> (5) the local and global environment;
> (6) the short-term and long-term interests of the benefit corporation, including benefits that may accrue to the benefit corporation from its long-term plans and the possibility that these interests may be best served by the continued independence of the benefit corporation; and
> (7) the ability of the benefit corporation to accomplish its general public benefit purpose and any specific public benefit purpose.

Several features are worth noting. First, "shall consider" imposes a mandatory consideration obligation, not merely a permissive one. A director who entirely ignored one or more of the enumerated categories in a material decision would, on the face of the statute, be in breach of duty. Second, the enumeration is broader than Washington's three-category formulation, covering shareholders, employees (including supply chain employees), customers as beneficiaries, community and society, environment, short- and long-term interests of the corporation itself, and the corporation's capacity to accomplish its public benefit purposes. Third, the list is conjunctive ("shall consider ... and ... and ..."), not disjunctive. Each category is its own consideration obligation.

In tension with this apparently stricter duty, however, the B Lab model typically also provides that directors have *no duty* to any particular non-shareholder constituency and *no liability* to such constituencies, and that no private right of action is created for such constituencies. The model also provides that directors may "consider, but shall not be required to give priority to" any of the enumerated interests. So even the "shall consider" duty is softer than it first appears: directors must turn their attention to each category, but they need not weight any category at any particular level, and no non-shareholder can sue them for failing to reach a particular outcome.

The practical result is that the Maryland / B Lab duty is, in operational terms, a *procedural* rather than *substantive* obligation. Directors must deliberate on each category. They need not reach any particular decision, and they cannot be held liable for not doing so. The "shall consider" language is, in this sense, more of an agenda-setting requirement than a substantive re-orientation of fiduciary duty.

### 25. The Delaware PBC "Balancing" Duty

Delaware's Public Benefit Corporation statute, codified in 8 Del. C. §§ 361-368 (Subchapter XV), takes a different approach. Section 362(a) defines a PBC as a for-profit corporation "intended to produce a public benefit or public benefits and to operate in a responsible and sustainable manner." The public benefit must be identified in the certificate of incorporation, and the certificate must state that the corporation is a PBC. Section 365(a) imposes the key director duty:

> The board of directors shall manage or direct the business and affairs of a public benefit corporation in a manner that balances the pecuniary interests of the stockholders, the best interests of those materially affected by the corporation's conduct, and the specific public benefit or public benefits identified in its certificate of incorporation.

The Delaware formulation is a *balancing* duty, not a consideration duty. Directors must actually weigh and integrate three sets of interests — shareholders, materially affected persons, and the specified public benefit — in the course of managing the corporation. This is doctrinally closer to a true re-orientation of fiduciary duty than either the "shall consider" or "may consider" formulations.

The Delaware statute softens this apparently strong duty with two important safe harbors. Section 365(b) provides that a director "shall not, by virtue of the public benefit provisions of § 362(a) of this title, have any duty to any person on account of any interest of such person in the public benefit or public benefits identified in the certificate of incorporation or on account of any interest materially affected by the corporation's conduct." This eliminates the risk that the Section 365(a) balancing duty could give rise to claims by non-shareholder stakeholders.

Section 365(c) provides a further safe harbor: a director's decision implicating the balance requirement will be deemed to satisfy her fiduciary duties to stockholders and the corporation "if such director's decision is both informed and disinterested and not such that no person of ordinary, sound judgment would approve." This is a striking standard — significantly more deferential than the default Delaware business judgment rule — and it reflects Delaware's desire to give PBC directors substantial room to navigate the tensions inherent in the balancing duty.

In operational terms, the Delaware PBC duty sits between the B Lab "shall consider" and the Washington "may consider" formulations. It is substantively stronger than Washington in that it requires actual balancing rather than mere permission to consider. It is procedurally weaker than the B Lab model in that enforcement is channeled through an unusually deferential review standard that makes successful challenges nearly impossible.

### 26. The Washington "May Consider" Duty

Against the backdrop of the Pennsylvania constituency statute tradition, the Maryland / B Lab "shall consider" model, and the Delaware PBC "balancing" duty, the Washington "may consider" formulation is plainly the softest. A few points deserve emphasis.

#### 26.1 Permissive, Not Mandatory

The word "may" in "may consider" is textually unambiguous. It creates permission, not obligation. A Washington SPC director who never considers the corporation's social purposes in any decision is not, by virtue of that fact alone, in breach of fiduciary duty under the statute. The baseline duty of care and loyalty of RCW 23B.08.300 still applies, but the additional social purpose "consideration" is left entirely to the director's discretion.

#### 26.2 But Articles Can Upgrade

RCW 23B.25.040(2) explicitly authorizes the articles of incorporation to require consideration of social purposes. An SPC whose founders wish to adopt a B Lab–style "shall consider" duty can do so by writing it into the charter. This is an important escape valve: the soft statutory default does not preclude stronger private ordering. A Washington SPC can effectively become, by charter choice, a Maryland-style benefit corporation with respect to director duties, even though the statute itself imposes only the softer default.

#### 26.3 The Safe Harbor as Affirmative Protection

Even under the permissive default, the safe harbor provision — declaring that actions reasonably believed to promote social purposes are "deemed to be in the best interests of the corporation" — provides substantial affirmative protection. A Washington SPC director who *does* consider social purposes and reaches a decision that trades expected profit for mission advancement is insulated from derivative challenge, provided her belief was reasonable. The safe harbor is, functionally, an expansion of business judgment rule protection into a domain where ordinary corporate law would otherwise be ambiguous.

#### 26.4 The Interpretive Vacuum

A feature of the Washington "may consider" formulation that distinguishes it from the Delaware PBC formulation is the near-total absence of judicial interpretation. Delaware's PBC statute has been the subject of substantial academic commentary and at least some judicial treatment. Washington's SPC statute has, as of 2026, generated essentially no appellate case law, and the WSBA Business Law Section's drafter commentary in the Washington Business Corporation Act Sourcebook provides the principal authoritative source of interpretive guidance. This interpretive vacuum has both virtues and vices: it leaves practitioners substantial flexibility in counseling clients but also leaves important questions unresolved.

### 27. The Exculpation and Standing Questions

Two interrelated questions recur in benefit corporation scholarship and are worth addressing explicitly.

#### 27.1 Can SPC Directors Be Liable for *Not* Considering Social Purpose?

Under the Washington "may consider" default, the answer is clearly no. A director who ignores social purposes entirely is not in breach of fiduciary duty merely for that reason. She may still be in breach on other grounds — she may have failed to exercise due care, or may have engaged in self-dealing, or may have taken actions that no reasonable director could have believed were in the corporation's best interests — but the ignoring of social purposes, standing alone, is not actionable.

If the articles convert the "may" into a "shall," the analysis changes. Under a charter-imposed "shall consider" duty, a director who ignored social purposes would be in breach, though the available remedies would still be limited by the section 050 third-party bar and the section 080 shareholder-only derivative action rule.

#### 27.2 Can SPC Directors Be Liable for *Favoring* Social Purpose Over Profit?

Under the safe harbor, the answer is presumptively no, provided the director's belief that her action would promote social purposes was reasonable. The safe harbor is the key doctrinal instrument protecting mission-oriented decision-making from shareholder derivative challenge. It converts the potentially contested question of whether the action was "in the best interests of the corporation" into a simpler question of whether the director reasonably believed the action would promote the social purpose — a question governed by business judgment rule–style deference.

An important qualification: the safe harbor protects *good faith* action. A director who invoked social purpose as pretext for a decision actually motivated by self-interest, or by loyalty to a third party, would not be protected. The safe harbor's good-faith requirement imports the duty of loyalty into the analysis, even if the duty of care is suspended.

### 28. The Business Judgment Rule as Applied to SPC Decisions

A further doctrinal question is how the business judgment rule interacts with the SPC safe harbor. Three possible framings:

1. *The safe harbor as BJR extension.* On this view, the safe harbor does not change the basic BJR analysis; it simply adds social purpose consideration to the set of legitimate director motivations protected by the rule. A director's good-faith, informed, reasoned pursuit of social purpose receives the same deference as any other business judgment.
2. *The safe harbor as BJR replacement.* On this view, the safe harbor functions as a lex specialis substitute for the BJR in mission-oriented decisions. The director's action is reviewed not under the default BJR but under the narrower "reasonably believes will promote the social purposes" standard.
3. *The safe harbor as BJR supplement.* On this view, both standards apply: the director's action must satisfy the BJR (good faith, informed, no conflict), and must independently satisfy the "reasonably believes" standard of the safe harbor.

The statute does not definitively resolve this question, and the WSBA commentary is not dispositive. Most practitioner writing treats the safe harbor as a BJR extension — an additional form of protection that operates alongside the BJR rather than in substitution for it — but the question remains open for judicial resolution.

## Part IV. The Social Purpose Report in Detail

### 29. Content Requirements

The Social Purpose Report required by RCW 23B.25.150 is the SPC's principal vehicle of substantive accountability. Part II above summarized the statutory requirements; this Part provides more granular analysis and practical guidance.

The statute requires narrative discussion of "the social purpose or purposes of the corporation" and "the efforts of the corporation to promote the social purpose or purposes." The enumerated content elements — objectives, material actions, expected future actions, and performance measures — are introduced with "may include" rather than "shall include," giving SPCs substantial drafting flexibility. In practice, however, most Washington SPCs that publish their reports follow the enumerated structure, producing documents with distinct sections addressing:

1. *Social Purpose Statement.* A restatement of the general and specific social purposes under which the corporation is organized, usually drawn directly from the articles of incorporation.
2. *Long-Term Objectives.* A description of the multi-year outcomes the corporation aspires to achieve in relation to its social purposes. These are typically qualitative ("to become the preferred employer for skilled tradespeople in the Pacific Northwest") rather than quantitative, though quantitative targets strengthen the report's credibility.
3. *Short-Term Objectives.* Fiscal-year-specific goals tied to the longer-term vision. These should ideally be measurable against the performance measures described below.
4. *Material Actions During the Fiscal Year.* A narrative of what the corporation actually did during the reporting period to advance its social purposes. This is the section most likely to capture reader attention and should focus on specific, verifiable activities rather than aspirational statements.
5. *Expected Future Actions.* A forward-looking section identifying planned activities for subsequent periods.
6. *Performance Measures.* A description of the financial, operating, or other metrics the corporation uses to evaluate its social purpose performance. These might include employee retention rates, supplier diversity statistics, carbon intensity per unit of output, or community investment as a percentage of revenue.

The statute's permissive language on content creates both opportunity and risk. The opportunity is that SPCs can tailor their reports to their specific missions and stakeholders, producing documents that actually reflect what the corporation is trying to do. The risk is that reports can be made essentially meaningless — all aspirational language and no measurable commitments — and still satisfy the statute.

### 30. Timing and Grace Periods

The four-month deadline from fiscal year close is relatively generous by financial-reporting standards. A calendar-year SPC must publish its report by April 30 of the following year. For private SPCs without parallel audit and filing obligations, this provides ample time for internal data collection, narrative drafting, board review, and publication.

The statute does not explicitly address what happens if an SPC misses the four-month deadline. By its terms, the court-ordered delivery remedy is available only after *two consecutive years* of non-compliance. A single missed year is not, therefore, an enforcement trigger; it is merely a defect that can be cured in the subsequent year. This relatively toothless grace period is one of the frequent targets of academic critique, discussed further in Part VI below.

### 31. Publication on the "Principal Website"

The statute requires publication "at the corporation's principal internet website address." What constitutes the "principal" website for SPCs with multiple web properties is not further defined, and no appellate decisions have addressed the question. Practitioner guidance generally recommends:

1. Publishing on the same website used for investor communications and corporate governance matters.
2. Including the report in a location accessible within a small number of clicks from the home page — typically within a "governance," "about us," or "impact" subsection.
3. Ensuring the report is actually publicly accessible (no paywall, no login requirement).
4. Providing a plain-text or PDF version that can be downloaded.
5. Maintaining an archive of prior-year reports, even though the statute does not explicitly require retention beyond the current fiscal year.

The archive question is particularly important for SPCs that may later be acquired, merged, or otherwise subject to transactions in which their historical compliance will be relevant to due diligence. An SPC that deletes prior-year reports creates an unnecessary obstacle for future diligence review.

### 32. Failure Remedies and Standing

The enforcement mechanism for reporting compliance is unusually limited. The superior court of the county where the SPC's registered office is located may order delivery of the report, summarily, after two consecutive years of non-compliance. Several features of this remedy structure are worth emphasizing.

First, the remedy is delivery of the report itself — that is, specific performance of the reporting obligation. The statute does not authorize damages, penalties, or fee awards. A successful petitioner gets a court order requiring the SPC to publish a report; she does not get monetary compensation for the SPC's failure.

Second, the statute does not explicitly address standing. Reading Chapter 23B.25 as a whole, the most natural inference is that shareholders have standing to petition for the court order, consistent with the section 080 shareholder-only derivative action rule. Whether other parties — the Attorney General of Washington, for instance, or the Secretary of State — have standing is not clearly resolved. The Attorney General is empowered under RCW 23B.14 to pursue judicial dissolution of delinquent corporations generally, and presumably could invoke that authority against an SPC that failed to comply with reporting obligations as part of a broader pattern of delinquency, but this is an indirect enforcement mechanism rather than direct enforcement of section 150.

Third, the two-consecutive-year trigger means that a single year's noncompliance, however egregious, cannot be judicially remedied. An SPC that entirely ignores its reporting obligation for one year is in the clear, legally speaking, as long as it resumes reporting in the following year. This is significantly more permissive than the B Lab model, under which reporting failures can be the subject of benefit enforcement proceedings from the first instance.

### 33. Best Practices

Drawing on the reports actually published by Washington SPCs in the years since 2012, several best practices have emerged among practitioners who advise SPC clients:

1. *Start with the mission statement.* Open each annual report with a restatement of the SPC's social purposes as set forth in the articles. This grounds the reader and provides continuity across years.
2. *Be quantitative where possible.* Reports that include specific numbers — headcount, supplier statistics, carbon measurements, charitable giving totals — are more credible than reports built entirely on narrative claims.
3. *Acknowledge failures and challenges.* Reports that discuss only successes are suspect. Reports that acknowledge where the SPC fell short of its objectives, and explain what it plans to do differently, build credibility with readers.
4. *Maintain year-over-year continuity.* Use the same structural headings from year to year, allowing readers to track progress on specific objectives. Avoid wholesale rewrites that make cross-year comparison impossible.
5. *Publish on the same date each year.* Consistent publication timing signals organizational discipline and makes it easier for stakeholders to plan around the reporting cycle.
6. *Archive prior reports.* Even though the statute does not require retention beyond the current fiscal year, maintaining a complete archive of all prior reports is virtually costless and provides valuable due diligence material.
7. *Reference the articles of incorporation.* Include, or link to, the SPC's articles of incorporation so that readers can see the legal mission statement being reported against.
8. *Consider voluntary third-party frameworks.* An SPC is not required to assess its performance against any third-party standard, but may voluntarily do so (for instance, by using the B Impact Assessment, GRI Standards, or SASB metrics). Voluntary adoption of a recognized framework signals commitment beyond bare statutory compliance.

## Part V. Comparative Framework

This Part presents structured comparative analysis across three axes: (i) the principal United States benefit corporation forms; (ii) Washington SPC versus the standard Washington 23B corporation; and (iii) international comparisons with analogous corporate forms outside the United States.

Several patterns emerge from this comparison.

First, on director duties, the Washington "may consider" formulation is the softest of the four regimes. Delaware's balancing duty is substantively stronger, though softened by the § 365(c) enhanced deference. California and Maryland's "shall consider" duties are procedurally stronger, imposing mandatory attention to multiple categories of interest, but are themselves subject to the familiar restrictions on private rights of action.

Second, on the third-party standard question, Washington is again the most permissive. Neither Washington nor Delaware requires the use of any external standard in performance assessment or reporting. California and Maryland (along with other B Lab model states) require the annual benefit report to assess performance against a "recognized third-party standard," which in practice usually means the B Impact Assessment.

Third, on enforcement, Washington's structure is unusual. The summary court-ordered delivery remedy after two years of noncompliance is a distinctive Washington feature, neither matching the stronger benefit enforcement proceeding of the B Lab model nor the absence of specific enforcement in Delaware. In practical terms, Washington's enforcement is the weakest of the four: specific performance only, delayed two years, with relief limited to report delivery.

Fourth, on disclosure, Washington is uniquely aggressive in requiring a mandatory investor-protection statement in the articles themselves. The statutory disclosure that the "mission is not necessarily compatible with and may be contrary to maximizing profits and earnings for shareholders" appears nowhere else among the four regimes. This is the Washington drafters' substitution for the stronger substantive duty structures of the other regimes: rather than forcing directors to prioritize mission, Washington gives shareholders notice that mission-based trade-offs are possible and expects them to invest (or not) on that basis.

The most important take-away from the SPC-to-standard comparison is that an SPC is structurally identical to a standard 23B corporation with three added features: a social purpose statement in the articles, a director duty overlay, and an annual reporting obligation. The SPC form does not impose asset locks, dividend restrictions, capital structure constraints, or any other limitation on ordinary corporate operations. Compared to the British Columbia benefit company model or the UK CIC, the Washington SPC is a remarkably low-friction addition to the 23B baseline.

### 36. International Comparisons

Benefit corporation–analogous corporate forms exist in multiple jurisdictions outside the United States. This section compares the Washington SPC to four significant international analogues: the United Kingdom's Community Interest Company (2005); Italy's Società Benefit (2016); France's Société à Mission (2019, PACTE law); and British Columbia's Benefit Company (2020).

#### 36.1 United Kingdom Community Interest Company (CIC)

The Community Interest Company, introduced by the Companies (Audit, Investigations and Community Enterprise) Act 2004 and effective July 1, 2005, was the first statutory mission-locked corporate form in a major common law jurisdiction. CICs are regulated by a dedicated Regulator of Community Interest Companies, who must approve each CIC's "community interest test" (a statement of how the CIC serves the community) before registration. CICs can be either limited by shares or limited by guarantee.

The CIC's defining feature is its asset lock. Under the asset lock, a CIC cannot distribute its assets to members for less than market value, except to another asset-locked body (another CIC or a registered charity). On dissolution, remaining assets must pass to another asset-locked body, not to members. Additionally, CICs limited by shares may pay dividends to shareholders, but only subject to a dividend cap (currently the Bank of England base rate plus 5%) and an aggregate cap (typically 35% of distributable profits in any year).

The contrast with Washington SPCs is striking. A Washington SPC has no asset lock, no dividend cap, no aggregate distribution cap, no regulator, and no mandatory pre-registration review of its social purpose statement. A Washington SPC's mission commitment is a procedural hurdle to conversion, not a permanent substantive restriction on capital flows. The UK CIC is a much stronger form of mission lock, but it comes at the cost of capital markets accessibility: UK venture capital is much less willing to invest in CICs than in ordinary limited companies, specifically because of the dividend cap and asset lock.

#### 36.2 Italy Società Benefit

Italy became the first European jurisdiction (and the second country in the world after the United States) to enact benefit corporation legislation, through Law 208/2015, effective January 1, 2016. The Italian statute is modeled on the B Lab framework, but with significant Italianizations. Key features:

- *Dual purpose.* A Società Benefit pursues "common benefit" alongside economic objectives. "Common benefit" is defined broadly as the pursuit of one or more positive effects, or the reduction of negative effects, regarding people, communities, territories and environment, cultural and social goods and activities, entities and associations and other stakeholders.
- *Balancing duty.* Directors must balance stakeholder and shareholder interests in pursuing the corporation's common benefit purposes, closer in spirit to the Delaware PBC balancing duty than to either the Washington "may consider" or the Maryland "shall consider" formulations.
- *Third-party assessment.* Società Benefit must assess their impact using an external standard, with the specific standards being recognized by the Italian government. In practice, the B Impact Assessment is the dominant choice.
- *Annual report.* Società Benefit must publish an annual impact report on their website, describing the corporation's actions in pursuit of common benefit.

Italy's statute is substantively closer to the B Lab model than the Washington SPC. Its adoption demonstrated that B Lab–style benefit corporation legislation could be successfully exported to a civil law jurisdiction with a different corporate law tradition from the United States.

#### 36.3 France Société à Mission

France's Loi PACTE (Plan d'Action pour la Croissance et la Transformation des Entreprises), enacted in May 2019, introduced a distinctive French approach to mission-driven corporate purpose. The PACTE law has two key components relevant to benefit corporation analysis.

First, it amended Article 1833 of the French Civil Code to provide that every company must be managed "in its corporate interest, taking into account the social and environmental issues" arising from its activities. This is a generalized duty applicable to all French companies, not just benefit corporations — more analogous to an American constituency statute than to a benefit corporation regime.

Second, it created the "Société à Mission" status, which any French company may adopt by amending its bylaws to include a "raison d'être" (reason for being) and specific social or environmental objectives. A Société à Mission must:

- Include in its bylaws a "raison d'être" and specific objectives.
- Establish a dedicated "mission committee," separate from the board of directors, including at least one employee, tasked with evaluating the company's fulfillment of its mission.
- Submit to independent third-party verification of mission fulfillment by an approved verifier at least once every two years (annually for large companies).
- Include mission committee reports in its annual filings.

The French approach is distinctive in two ways. First, the mission committee is a governance organ separate from the board — an innovation not found in the U.S. models. Second, the PACTE framework emphasizes third-party verification, though through an independent verifier rather than through reliance on a standardized B Lab–type framework. France offers more governance infrastructure than the Washington SPC but less than the UK CIC; it emphasizes process over substantive outcome.

#### 36.4 British Columbia Benefit Company

On June 30, 2020, British Columbia became the first Canadian province to authorize the formation of benefit companies, through amendments to the BC Business Corporations Act. The BC model is closer to the B Lab template than the Washington SPC, with several distinctive features:

- Benefit companies must commit to "conducting its business in a responsible and sustainable manner" and to promoting one or more specified "public benefits."
- Directors must balance public benefit considerations against other corporate purposes.
- Annual benefit reports are required, assessed against a third-party standard.
- As of September 2024, just over 800 BC benefit companies were active, suggesting meaningful but modest uptake.

British Columbia's experience is noteworthy because it is geographically close to Washington and its benefit company framework can be contrasted with the Washington SPC framework along the same axis as the Washington-Maryland contrast: a softer, more permissive U.S. regime versus a stronger, B Lab–aligned adjacent regime.

Washington and British Columbia occupy opposite ends of the North American spectrum: BC's benefit company is closer to the B Lab mainstream, while the Washington SPC is distinctive for its permissive "may consider" duty and absence of third-party standard requirements.

## Part VI. Critique, Commentary, and the Future

### 37. Academic Critique

Academic commentary on the Washington SPC statute is sparse compared to the substantial literature on Delaware PBC and B Lab model statutes. The main lines of critique can be summarized under several headings.

#### 37.1 Is "May Consider" Too Weak?

The most common academic critique of the Washington SPC statute is that its "may consider" director duty is too weak to achieve meaningful substantive change in corporate behavior. Critics argue that a purely permissive duty provides no additional guidance to directors beyond what they already had under pre-2012 Washington law: Washington directors were presumably always permitted to consider non-shareholder interests in the broad course of exercising business judgment, and the SPC statute simply confirms that permission without adding any affirmative obligation. On this view, the SPC is a piece of expressive legislation more than substantive reform — signaling that Washington welcomes mission-driven businesses while declining to change the underlying duty structure.

Defenders of the Washington approach counter with several points. First, the expressive function is itself important: even if the SPC statute did not alter pre-existing duties, it created a clear statutory home and a designated corporate form where mission-driven businesses could operate without legal uncertainty. Second, the safe harbor is genuinely new and genuinely valuable: it converts a director's good-faith mission-oriented decision from a potentially litigable business judgment into a conclusively protected one. Third, charter-based opt-in to stronger duty formulations is available under RCW 23B.25.040(2), so founders who want a B Lab–style "shall consider" duty can get it by drafting for it.

#### 37.2 The Greenwashing Risk

A second line of critique focuses on the absence of a third-party standard requirement. Without mandatory external verification, critics argue, the SPC designation is vulnerable to greenwashing: any corporation that files the required articles language and publishes a minimally compliant Social Purpose Report can claim SPC status, regardless of the substance of its actual social impact. The SPC label thereby risks becoming debased, losing its signaling value for genuinely mission-driven companies and providing cover for bad actors.

This critique has more force in theory than in practice, for two reasons. First, as noted above, many SPCs voluntarily adopt third-party frameworks (B Impact Assessment, GRI, SASB) to signal credibility beyond statutory minimum compliance. Voluntary adoption is perhaps more meaningful than mandatory adoption: a corporation that adopts a framework without being required to do so is sending a stronger signal than one that does so only because the statute demands it. Second, market forces provide substantial discipline. Mission-oriented customers, employees, and investors evaluate SPCs by the substance of their actions and reports, not merely by their legal designation; an SPC whose report is obviously hollow will lose credibility with the constituencies that matter most to its value proposition.

#### 37.3 Enforcement Deficits

A third critique focuses on the anemic enforcement structure. The summary court-ordered delivery remedy after two consecutive years of noncompliance is, as discussed above, the weakest enforcement mechanism among the four principal U.S. benefit corporation regimes. There is no benefit enforcement proceeding, no damages remedy, no penalty structure, and essentially no mechanism for punishing substantive (as opposed to procedural) failures of mission pursuit. An SPC that publishes glossy but meaningless Social Purpose Reports on time each year is entirely insulated from judicial challenge under Chapter 23B.25.

Defenders respond that this critique proves too much. Neither Delaware nor the B Lab model states have produced significant benefit-corporation litigation in the decade-plus since their adoption. The enforcement mechanisms on paper are rarely activated in practice. The real discipline on benefit corporation performance comes from reputation, capital markets, and consumer choice, not from courts. Washington's decision to keep enforcement minimal therefore sacrifices little practical accountability while avoiding the creation of nuisance-suit opportunities.

#### 37.4 Doctrinal Spillovers

A more subtle critique focuses on potential doctrinal spillovers from the SPC statute to the duties of directors of ordinary (non-SPC) Washington corporations. By explicitly authorizing SPC directors to consider social purposes, the statute arguably implies that non-SPC directors *may not* do so, or may do so only at their peril. The negative implication is not clearly supported by the statutory text, and no Washington appellate decision has drawn it, but the concern is not entirely speculative. Corporate litigation is sensitive to textual hooks, and the creation of a statutory safe harbor for SPC directors could, in some future case, be cited as evidence that directors of non-SPC corporations lack equivalent protection.

The WSBA drafter commentary disclaims any intent to create such a spillover, and the structural integration of Chapter 23B.25 with the rest of Title 23B (via section 010) is designed to make clear that ordinary Washington corporate law continues to apply to non-SPCs unaffected. But the risk of unintended negative inference remains a latent concern.

### 38. Practitioner Perspectives and Uptake

Practitioner commentary on the SPC statute has been generally positive, if muted. Several recurring themes can be identified.

#### 38.1 Uptake Statistics

Reliable statistics on Washington SPC formations are surprisingly difficult to obtain. As of August 2013, approximately 49 SPCs had been registered with the Washington Secretary of State, according to contemporaneous reporting. Later figures are harder to come by, but practitioner estimates circa 2020 suggested the number had grown to several hundred, though the exact figure is not publicly reported as a distinct category in the Secretary of State's aggregate corporate registrations data. By contrast, Delaware reports thousands of PBC formations, and British Columbia reported just over 800 active benefit companies as of September 2024.

Washington's relatively modest SPC uptake is variously attributed to: (i) the availability of alternative certification (B Lab's Certified B Corporation mark) that satisfies most founders' signaling needs without requiring legal entity conversion; (ii) the option of forming in Delaware as a PBC, particularly for venture-backed companies; (iii) general low awareness of the SPC option among Washington founders and their counsel; and (iv) the permissive nature of Washington's "may consider" formulation, which makes the practical difference between an SPC and an ordinary corporation harder to articulate than the more dramatic Maryland or Delaware differences.

#### 38.2 Practitioner Use Cases

Practitioners identify several use cases in which the SPC structure is particularly well-suited:

1. *Founder protection in succession planning.* An SPC structure can help founders ensure mission continuity through succession events, by creating a procedural hurdle (the two-thirds conversion vote) to mission abandonment.
2. *Signaling to mission-aligned investors.* Some investors specifically seek out SPC investments as a way to align portfolio exposure with personal values. The SPC designation provides a legal marker for such investors.
3. *Employee engagement.* Companies competing for talent in mission-sensitive markets (sustainable food, renewable energy, social services technology) find the SPC designation helps with recruiting and retention.
4. *Credibility with customers.* Consumer-facing businesses whose customers care about social or environmental impact can use SPC status as one element of a broader credibility-building strategy.
5. *Board protection.* Directors of mission-driven businesses benefit from the safe harbor regardless of whether the underlying substantive decisions would have survived challenge under pre-existing duty standards.

Practitioners generally advise that the SPC structure is most valuable when combined with *additional* private-ordering measures — voluntary B Lab certification, third-party framework assessment, robust independent board committees, contractual investor protections — rather than relied upon in isolation. The SPC statute provides a foundation, not a complete governance framework.

### 39. The Greenwashing Concern

The greenwashing concern — that the SPC label can be used as a marketing device by corporations whose substantive practices do not warrant the designation — deserves particular attention because it implicates the broader social credibility of benefit corporation legislation.

Several structural features of Washington's statute create greenwashing vulnerability. First, there is no pre-registration review of social purpose statements; an SPC's articles are filed with the Secretary of State on the same terms as an ordinary corporation's. Second, there is no mandatory third-party assessment standard. Third, the permissive "may include" language in the Social Purpose Report content requirements allows for substantively hollow reports that nonetheless satisfy the statute. Fourth, the enforcement mechanism reaches only procedural compliance (report publication), not substantive impact.

Against these vulnerabilities, three counterweights apply.

First, market discipline. As noted above, the constituencies that care about SPC status — employees, customers, investors oriented toward mission alignment — generally have the incentive and capacity to evaluate actual corporate behavior, not merely legal designation. An SPC whose reports are hollow will lose credibility quickly.

Second, voluntary adoption of stronger standards. Many Washington SPCs voluntarily adopt B Lab certification, third-party impact frameworks, or independent audit requirements. Voluntary commitments beyond statutory minimums are often more meaningful than mandatory ones.

Third, the mandatory articles disclosure. The investor-protection language required in SPC articles (discussed in section 10 above) puts all shareholders on notice that mission objectives may conflict with profit maximization. A shareholder who invests in an SPC has, by operation of law, been warned that the corporation's social purpose is substantive rather than cosmetic. While this does not prevent greenwashing in the consumer-facing sense, it does reduce the legitimate complaint available to investors who later discover that the corporation has, in fact, pursued its stated mission.

On balance, the greenwashing concern is real but manageable. The Washington SPC statute's design choices make it more vulnerable to superficial abuse than the B Lab model states, but the actual incidence of greenwashing-style litigation or reputational failure has been low in the statute's first decade of operation.

### 40. Relationship to ESG and Sustainability Reporting

A notable development in the years since the Washington SPC statute's enactment is the explosive growth of "ESG" (Environmental, Social, and Governance) and sustainability reporting frameworks, both voluntary and (increasingly) mandatory. The SPC's annual Social Purpose Report sits in a complicated relationship with these broader frameworks.

#### 40.1 Overlapping Frameworks

Several frameworks are potentially relevant to SPC reporting:

- *GRI Standards.* The Global Reporting Initiative's standards are the most widely adopted voluntary sustainability reporting framework globally, focused on material impacts on stakeholders.
- *SASB Standards.* The Sustainability Accounting Standards Board (now part of the IFRS Foundation's International Sustainability Standards Board) provides industry-specific metrics oriented toward investors.
- *TCFD Recommendations.* The Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures provides a framework for climate-related risk disclosure, now integrated into IFRS S2.
- *IFRS S1 and S2.* The International Sustainability Standards Board's foundational standards, issued in 2023, provide a general framework for sustainability disclosure and a specific climate standard.
- *CSRD.* The European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, effective 2024-2028 for various entity categories, imposes mandatory sustainability disclosures on large companies with EU operations, using the European Sustainability Reporting Standards.
- *B Impact Assessment.* B Lab's proprietary assessment tool, used both for B Corp certification and for B Lab model benefit corporation compliance.

#### 40.2 The SPC Report as Platform

None of these frameworks is specifically designed for Washington SPCs, and none is required by RCW 23B.25.150. However, the statute's permissive content framework means an SPC can structure its Social Purpose Report around any of these frameworks if doing so would serve its communication goals. Many SPCs that already publish sustainability reports under GRI or SASB can incorporate that content into their SPC reports without significant duplication. Some sophisticated SPCs publish integrated reports that simultaneously satisfy SPC statutory requirements and voluntary ESG framework expectations.

The relationship between the SPC report and broader ESG reporting is therefore one of coexistence rather than substitution. The SPC report is a narrower, legally-mandated document focused on the corporation's specific social purposes. ESG reports are broader, voluntary documents covering the full range of corporate impacts. An SPC can — and often should — produce both, either as separate documents or as sections of a single integrated publication.

#### 40.3 Emerging Convergence Pressure

As mandatory sustainability reporting frameworks (CSRD, IFRS S1/S2, SEC climate disclosure rules where applicable) expand, pressure grows on benefit corporation reports to align with these frameworks to avoid duplication of effort. A Washington SPC that is also subject to CSRD (because it has sufficient EU presence) will face practical pressure to structure its Social Purpose Report compatibly with CSRD requirements, even though CSRD has no formal bearing on the SPC statute. Over the next five to ten years, convergence between benefit-corporation-specific reporting and general sustainability reporting is likely to increase.

### 41. The Hundred-Year Question

The deepest question about benefit corporation legislation — Washington's or any other — is whether it can support genuinely long-term mission commitment. Can the SPC structure preserve a founder's vision across decades, across generations, across changes in ownership and management? Can it enable a corporation to remain mission-driven for one hundred years?

Several historical examples provide reference points.

#### 41.1 Patagonia

Patagonia, the outdoor apparel company founded by Yvon Chouinard in 1973, has become the paradigmatic American benefit corporation case study. Patagonia became California's first Certified B Corporation in 2012, adopted California benefit corporation status simultaneously, and in 2022 undertook a dramatic restructuring under which voting control was placed in the Patagonia Purpose Trust, while approximately 98 percent of the economic equity was transferred to a nonprofit — the Holdfast Collective — whose mission is funding environmental causes.

The Patagonia structure is notable for several reasons. First, it combines multiple mission-preservation techniques: benefit corporation status, B Lab certification, voting trust control, and economic ownership by a mission-aligned nonprofit. No single legal instrument is relied upon as the sole guarantor of mission continuity. Second, it illustrates the limits of benefit corporation legislation alone. Had Patagonia relied solely on California benefit corporation status, the mission would remain vulnerable to future two-thirds votes by whoever owned the shares. By layering trust governance on top of benefit corporation status, Patagonia achieved a substantially stronger mission lock.

Third, Patagonia's founder has stated an explicit aspiration to build a company that could last one hundred years. This framing — organizational persistence measured in centuries, not decades — is characteristic of a particular tradition in mission-driven business that traces through Newman's Own (whose founder dedicated all profits to charity through a foundation), the German industrial *Stiftung* companies, and the Tata family of businesses in India (whose controlling interest is held by charitable trusts).

#### 41.2 The Stiftung Model

The German *Stiftung* (foundation) model provides a particularly interesting comparison point. Large German companies, including Bosch, Zeiss, and Thyssenkrupp, have been controlled at various points by foundations whose governance documents commit the enterprise to long-term industrial and social objectives. The *Robert Bosch Stiftung*, for example, has controlled roughly 94 percent of the economic equity of Robert Bosch GmbH for decades, with profits directed to philanthropic purposes.

The *Stiftung* model is distinct from American benefit corporation structures in fundamental ways. German foundations are not corporations; they are dedicated asset-holding vehicles with their own governance independent of corporate law. Their commitments are enforced through foundation law, not corporate law. And they are essentially irrevocable in ways that even the strongest American benefit corporation structures are not.

Nevertheless, the *Stiftung* model suggests that multi-generational mission commitment is possible given appropriate legal infrastructure and founder commitment. American benefit corporation legislation, including the Washington SPC, can be seen as one component — necessary but not sufficient — of a broader toolkit for building enterprises intended to persist on century timescales.

#### 41.3 Can the Washington SPC Support a Hundred-Year Mission?

The answer, tentatively, is: yes, but only in conjunction with other structural devices. The Washington SPC provides four relevant tools:

1. *Articles-based mission statement.* The SPC's articles contain a legally binding mission statement that applies to all directors and officers and is disclosed to all shareholders at the time of investment.
2. *Director duty safe harbor.* Directors can pursue mission-oriented actions without fearing derivative liability, provided their actions are taken in good faith.
3. *Two-thirds procedural hurdle.* The supermajority voting requirement for amendment, merger, asset sale, and conversion out of SPC status provides meaningful protection against casual mission abandonment.
4. *Annual report accountability.* The Social Purpose Report creates a public record of mission pursuit that can be invoked by shareholders, employees, customers, and other constituencies to hold the corporation accountable.

But these tools are not sufficient, standing alone, for true hundred-year mission persistence. The two-thirds voting requirement, while substantial, can be met by determined acquirers who accumulate sufficient voting power. The director duty safe harbor protects directors who pursue mission but does not compel them to do so under the permissive default. And the annual report requirement, while useful as a discipline, does not produce enforceable outcomes in the way a purpose trust or foundation structure might.

A founder whose goal is century-scale mission persistence would be well advised, on the Washington SPC template, to layer additional protective devices on top of the statutory foundation: a voting trust holding a controlling share position, with trust documents requiring mission pursuit; nonprofit or foundation ownership of the majority of economic equity; independent board committees with specific mission-monitoring responsibilities; and contractual covenants with key stakeholders (employees, suppliers, customers) that make mission abandonment costly in practical terms. The Washington SPC statute provides the corporate-law floor; the builder must construct the walls and roof through additional private ordering.

The ultimate lesson may be that no single legal form, not even the strongest, can guarantee multi-generational mission persistence in the absence of sustained organizational commitment. Legal forms are scaffolding, not substance. The *Stiftung*, the Community Interest Company, the Delaware PBC, the Maryland benefit corporation, and the Washington SPC all provide tools of differing strength. The organizations that persist — the Patagonias, the Tatas, the Bosches, the Newman's Owns — persist because their founders and successors cared enough to use whatever legal tools were available and to continue caring across generations. The Washington SPC is a useful tool. Whether it is used toward century-scale ambitions is a question for founders, not for the legislature.

## Appendix A. Expanded Historical Essay: The Long Road to Benefit Corporation Legislation

### A.1 Before *Dodge*: The Nineteenth-Century Corporate Purpose Debate

To understand the intellectual climate in which *Dodge v. Ford Motor Co.* was decided, it helps to recall that the nineteenth-century American corporation was itself a contested legal form with an evolving understanding of its social function. The earliest American corporations were chartered individually by state legislatures for specific public purposes: building a bridge, operating a ferry, constructing a canal, providing water to a municipality. The notion that a corporation existed primarily to generate profit for its shareholders was not self-evident in the 1820s or 1830s, when most business was conducted through partnerships and the corporate form was reserved for projects with a claimed public dimension.

The transition to general incorporation statutes in the mid-nineteenth century — Connecticut's 1837 law being an early example — gradually shifted the understanding of the corporation. By making incorporation available on standardized terms to any promoter willing to meet statutory requirements, general incorporation laws severed the historical link between corporate status and public purpose. The corporation became, in theory and increasingly in practice, a general-purpose business vehicle whose justification rested on its commercial utility rather than any specific public benefit it delivered.

Even so, the doctrinal framework governing corporate purpose remained unsettled well into the late nineteenth century. The *ultra vires* doctrine — under which corporate actions outside the scope of the corporation's charter were treated as void — provided one mechanism for enforcing the historical understanding that corporations existed for limited purposes. Charter provisions often prescribed specific objects, and deviation from those objects could be challenged by shareholders, creditors, or the state. As general incorporation became dominant, *ultra vires* gradually weakened, but the expectation that a corporation had a defined purpose persisted.

By the early twentieth century, when *Dodge v. Ford* was decided, the dominant understanding had shifted decisively toward shareholder ownership as the conceptual foundation of corporate purpose. But the shift was not complete, and there remained — and remain — significant doctrinal resources for the alternative view that the corporation is an entity with interests distinct from those of any single constituency. Adolf Berle and Gardiner Means's *The Modern Corporation and Private Property* (1932) launched a multi-decade debate on the nature of the corporation, with Berle initially taking the shareholder-primacy view and E. Merrick Dodd responding with a stakeholder conception. Berle himself later conceded that Dodd had the better of the argument, though the concession went largely unnoticed in the business-school curricula that would emerge in the decades that followed.

### A.2 The New Deal, the War Economy, and the Mid-Century Managerialist Settlement

The shareholder primacy orthodoxy that the benefit corporation movement sought to challenge was itself a relatively recent construction. For much of the twentieth century — particularly from the New Deal through the 1970s — American corporate governance operated under what historians sometimes call the "managerialist settlement." Large corporations were run by professional managers whose accountability was diffuse: nominally to shareholders, but in practice also to employees (especially unionized employees), to long-term creditors, to regulators, and to the social expectations of the communities in which they operated. Quarterly earnings pressure was a minor factor. Takeover markets were dormant. Institutional shareholders — insurance companies, bank trust departments, mutual funds — held large but passive positions.

In this managerialist world, the question of corporate purpose was largely academic. Professional managers balanced multiple constituencies as a matter of operational necessity, and legal challenges to their decisions — whether brought by dissatisfied shareholders or by any other constituency — faced substantial doctrinal hurdles. The business judgment rule, which had crystallized in the early twentieth century, provided a strong presumption of deference to managerial decisions. Derivative litigation was expensive and procedurally cumbersome. Hostile takeovers were rare and widely disapproved of as bad for the long-term health of the companies involved.

The gradual breakdown of this settlement, beginning in the late 1970s and accelerating through the 1980s, is a complex story. Key factors included: the emergence of junk bond financing that enabled hostile takeover bids by financial acquirers; the growth of institutional investment and the activist orientation of some large institutional investors; the rise of law-and-economics scholarship, particularly at the University of Chicago, that provided intellectual support for shareholder primacy; the globalization of capital markets that increased competitive pressure on American managers; and the decline of organized labor, which had been one of the principal non-shareholder counterweights within the managerialist settlement.

By the time Milton Friedman's 1970 essay appeared, these forces were already gathering but had not yet reached their full expression. Friedman was describing a world that was coming into being, not one that already existed. His essay became influential precisely because it provided a clean intellectual justification for a shift that was already underway for other reasons.

### A.3 The Takeover Era and Its Doctrinal Consequences

The 1980s hostile takeover wave was the crucible in which modern American corporate governance doctrine was forged. Delaware's courts, faced with repeated challenges arising out of takeover battles, developed a sophisticated framework for analyzing director conduct in the face of acquisition threats. *Unocal Corp. v. Mesa Petroleum Co.*, 493 A.2d 946 (Del. 1985), established that directors facing a hostile tender offer could take defensive measures if they reasonably believed the threat was to corporate policy and effectiveness, and if their defensive response was proportional to the threat. *Moran v. Household International, Inc.*, 500 A.2d 1346 (Del. 1985), upheld the shareholder rights plan or "poison pill" as a defensive tool. *Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings, Inc.*, 506 A.2d 173 (Del. 1986), established that once a sale of the company became inevitable, directors' duty shifted to obtaining the best price for shareholders.

The *Revlon* line of cases is particularly important for benefit corporation analysis because it establishes a point in the life cycle of a corporation — the point at which a sale becomes inevitable — where directors' duty is unambiguously to maximize shareholder pecuniary returns. Whatever stakeholder considerations might have been permissible before that point become doctrinally unavailable once *Revlon* kicks in. For mission-driven founders, this is a deeply problematic feature of the default corporate landscape: precisely at the moment when the mission is most vulnerable (sale to a new owner), the directors are forbidden to consider anything but immediate shareholder value.

Benefit corporation statutes respond to this concern in different ways. The B Lab model explicitly overrides *Revlon*-style duty shifts by providing that benefit corporation directors may (or must) continue to consider non-shareholder interests even in sale situations. The Delaware PBC statute accomplishes the same end by making balancing the ongoing managerial duty. Washington's SPC statute addresses the issue more obliquely: by authorizing directors to consider social purposes throughout the corporation's life and by providing enhanced voting thresholds (two-thirds) for mergers, the statute both permits directors to resist profit-maximizing-but-mission-destroying acquisitions and provides a procedural hurdle against simple majority override.

### A.4 The Rise of ESG and Stakeholder Capitalism

Concurrent with the late-stage development of benefit corporation legislation has been the rise of what is variously called "stakeholder capitalism," "ESG investing," or "conscious capitalism." The Business Roundtable's August 2019 statement revising its longstanding definition of corporate purpose — previously defined in strictly shareholder-primacy terms — to include commitments to customers, employees, suppliers, communities, and shareholders, was widely cited as a cultural inflection point. The statement was signed by 181 CEOs of major American corporations.

Critics of the Business Roundtable statement have pointed out that it was a statement of aspiration rather than legal obligation, and that the subsequent behavior of signatory companies has not differed markedly from pre-2019 norms. But as a signal of shifting elite discourse, the statement was significant. It suggested that shareholder primacy had lost its status as the default common-sense understanding of corporate purpose, even among mainstream corporate executives. Benefit corporation legislation — including Washington's SPC statute — is part of the same broader cultural shift, providing legal infrastructure for organizations that wish to operate explicitly under a stakeholder-oriented governance model.

The relationship between benefit corporation law and broader ESG developments is worth examining carefully. Benefit corporation law is, in a sense, narrower and deeper than ESG: narrower because it applies only to entities that affirmatively elect benefit corporation status, and deeper because it changes the fundamental legal relationship between directors and their corporations. ESG frameworks are, in a sense, broader and shallower: broader because they apply across the corporate landscape regardless of entity form, and shallower because they typically operate through disclosure obligations rather than through restructuring of fiduciary duties.

An SPC that uses its Social Purpose Report as a platform for ESG disclosure is, in effect, combining both approaches. Its governance structure reflects the narrower-and-deeper benefit corporation model, while its reporting reflects the broader-and-shallower ESG model. Whether this combination is stable in the long run — whether ESG reporting will eventually swallow benefit corporation reporting, or vice versa, or whether they will continue as complementary frameworks — is an open question for the next decade of development.

### A.5 The Peculiar Stability of Chapter 23B.25

One surprising feature of the Washington SPC story is how little the statute has changed since 2012. Compare California, which renamed and tightened its Flexible Purpose Corporation statute within four years. Compare Delaware, which has amended its PBC statute several times, most significantly in 2020, to address various operational questions. Compare even Maryland, the first benefit corporation state, which has made technical adjustments to its statute multiple times.

Washington's Chapter 23B.25 has had virtually no substantive legislative amendments in its fourteen years of operation. This stability is noteworthy and has several possible explanations.

First, the statute may simply be working well enough that no political coalition has formed to push for amendment. SPCs are being formed in modest numbers, reports are being published with reasonable regularity, and no major controversy has arisen that would require legislative intervention. In the absence of pressure, legislatures rarely take up technical amendments to obscure corporate law chapters.

Second, the statute may be uncontroversial precisely because it is soft. A statute that imposes meaningful substantive obligations generates pressure from both directions — from those who want it strengthened and from those who want it weakened. A permissive statute that mostly operates as a signaling device generates little pressure in either direction. Washington's "may consider" formulation is hard to complain about from either the reform or the anti-reform side.

Third, the WSBA Business Law Section's ongoing stewardship of Title 23B generally may have absorbed what little reform pressure exists into routine Sourcebook updates rather than legislative amendments. The Sourcebook commentary provides an informal interpretive gloss that can adapt to new circumstances without requiring the legislature to act.

Fourth, Washington may have been fortunate in its initial drafting. The California FPC template, which Washington adapted, had itself been carefully considered by the California Corporations Committee and reflected practitioner consensus on the minimum intervention necessary to achieve the desired effect. By adopting a well-considered template with minor Washington-specific modifications, the drafters may have avoided the rough edges that later required amendment in other jurisdictions.

Whatever the explanation, the result is a statute that, as of 2026, remains essentially unchanged from its 2012 enactment. This stability provides both a challenge and an opportunity for doctrinal analysis: the statute has not been updated in response to case law or practitioner experience, which means the original drafting choices remain fully exposed; but it also means that the statute has accumulated a meaningful, if modest, body of operational experience that can inform interpretation.

## Appendix B. Deep Dive: The "May Consider" Standard in Practice

### B.1 The Textual Analysis in Full

RCW 23B.25.050, paragraph (2), reads approximately as follows:

> In discharging the duties of the position of director and in determining what the director reasonably believes to be in the best interests of the corporation, a director, in addition to considering the effects of any action on shareholders, may consider the effects of any action upon: (a) the corporation's employees; (b) the corporation's suppliers or customers; (c) the communities in which offices or facilities of the corporation are located; (d) the local, state, national, or world environment; and (e) the short-term and long-term interests of the corporation, including benefits that may accrue to the corporation from its long-term plans and the possibility that those interests may be best served by the continued independence of the corporation.

This enumeration is itself broader than the three-category framework of RCW 23B.25.020. Section 020 defines what an SPC must be organized to pursue; section 050 defines what directors may consider in fulfilling their duties. The two are related but not identical. A director may consider each of the five enumerated categories in section 050 regardless of how the corporation's specific social purpose is defined under section 020.

The "may consider" phrase appears in paragraph (2). Paragraph (3), the safe harbor, reads approximately:

> Any action taken or omitted by a director of a social purpose corporation in the performance of the director's duties in compliance with this section shall be deemed to be in the best interests of the social purpose corporation.

And paragraph (5), the third-party bar, reads approximately:

> The provisions of this chapter shall not be construed to create any liability or grant any right in, by, or for any person other than the corporation and its shareholders. This chapter shall not be construed to affect or alter the duties of directors of corporations that are not social purpose corporations.

Taken together, these three paragraphs define the core doctrinal structure of SPC director duty. A director may — but need not — consider any of the enumerated categories. Any action the director takes in compliance with the section is deemed to be in the corporation's best interests, providing protection against derivative challenge. And the chapter creates no duties or rights running to anyone other than the corporation and its shareholders.

### B.2 Hypothetical Application: The Layoff Decision

Consider a Washington SPC in the manufacturing sector whose social purpose statement commits the corporation to "promoting positive long-term effects on its employees and the communities in which it operates." The corporation experiences a downturn in its market and must decide whether to lay off a significant portion of its workforce or to endure several quarters of losses while maintaining employment.

Under traditional shareholder-primacy analysis, the directors' duty would be to maximize expected shareholder returns. If laying off employees would produce higher expected returns than maintaining employment, the directors would face pressure to approve the layoffs, and a decision to maintain employment despite lower expected returns could potentially be challenged as a breach of fiduciary duty.

Under the SPC framework, the analysis changes in two ways. First, directors are explicitly authorized to consider the effects of the layoff on employees — a category directly relevant to the corporation's social purpose statement. Second, the safe harbor protects a good-faith decision to maintain employment from derivative challenge, provided the directors reasonably believe that decision will promote the social purpose.

Concretely, the board might deliberate as follows:

1. Review the financial projections under both alternatives (layoff versus maintenance).
2. Note that the layoff would produce higher expected shareholder returns over the next twelve months.
3. Consider the effects on employees: job losses, income disruption, health insurance gaps, community economic impact.
4. Consider the effects on the corporation's long-term social purpose: maintaining the workforce supports the stated commitment to positive employee effects and community impact.
5. Consider the effects on the corporation's long-term competitive position: retaining trained workers may provide capacity for recovery when market conditions improve.
6. Reach a decision — for instance, to maintain employment — and document the social purpose and long-term interest considerations that informed the decision.

Under the Washington SPC framework, this decision is protected by the safe harbor as long as the directors reasonably believed that maintaining employment would promote the corporation's social purpose. A derivative suit challenging the decision would face both the substantive hurdle of rebutting the safe harbor and the procedural hurdle of the shareholder-only derivative action rule.

Importantly, the analysis does not *require* the directors to maintain employment. The permissive "may consider" formulation means the directors could instead conclude, consistent with their duties, that the financial pressures require layoffs notwithstanding the social purpose commitment. The safe harbor protects good-faith decisions in either direction, as long as the directors' reasoning reflects appropriate consideration of all relevant interests.

### B.3 Hypothetical Application: The Acquisition Offer

Consider the same corporation receiving an unsolicited acquisition offer at a significant premium to the current stock price. The acquirer is a private equity firm with a track record of cost-cutting and plant closures. The corporation's board believes that accepting the offer would terminate the corporation's mission and disperse its workforce, but would produce immediate shareholder gains.

Under traditional *Revlon*-style analysis, once a sale becomes inevitable, the directors' duty shifts to obtaining the best price for shareholders. Whatever mission considerations were permissible before that point become doctrinally unavailable.

Under the SPC framework, the analysis is different in several ways. First, the safe harbor continues to apply. Directors who decline the acquisition in good-faith pursuit of the social purpose are protected against derivative challenge. Second, even if a sale becomes inevitable, the social purpose considerations remain relevant to the directors' analysis because the SPC statute does not incorporate the Delaware *Revlon* framework by reference. Third, the two-thirds voting threshold for mergers in which the SPC does not survive provides an additional procedural layer: even if shareholders favor the deal, they cannot impose it over the board's objection without supermajority support.

The practical consequence is that a Washington SPC is much better positioned than a standard Washington corporation to resist acquisition offers that would terminate its mission. The legal architecture provides both offensive tools (the safe harbor for mission-protective decisions) and defensive tools (the supermajority requirement). Combined, these features make unsolicited acquisition of a Washington SPC substantially more difficult than unsolicited acquisition of a standard corporation.

This does not, however, provide complete protection. A sufficiently determined acquirer with sufficient financial resources can assemble the votes necessary to meet the two-thirds threshold, particularly if the offer price is attractive enough to override shareholder reluctance. The SPC framework raises the bar for mission-destroying acquisitions but does not make them impossible.

### B.4 The Reasonable-Belief Requirement

The safe harbor protects actions "reasonably believed" to promote the social purpose. The "reasonable belief" qualifier is important because it imports the same general standard that governs business judgment rule analysis. A director cannot invoke the safe harbor to protect a decision that no reasonable director could have believed would promote the social purpose, or a decision made in bad faith, or a decision made with disabling self-interest.

The practical effect is that the safe harbor functions as an extension of business judgment rule protection into the mission-oriented domain, rather than as a complete immunity from scrutiny. A plaintiff challenging an SPC director's action can still attempt to rebut the reasonable-belief foundation — by showing, for example, that the director did not actually believe the action would promote the social purpose, or that the action was so inconsistent with the social purpose that no reasonable belief to the contrary was possible. But such challenges face the same kinds of procedural and substantive hurdles as ordinary BJR challenges.

The open interpretive question is how strictly courts will police the reasonable-belief requirement. On a deferential reading, any action that has some plausible connection to the social purpose would be protected. On a stricter reading, courts would require the director to articulate specific reasoning linking the action to the social purpose, and would scrutinize whether that reasoning was credible. Washington case law does not, as of 2026, provide definitive guidance on this question.

### B.5 The Non-Delegation Problem

A subtle doctrinal issue arises with respect to delegation of SPC decision-making to officers and subordinate managers. Directors are empowered by RCW 23B.25.050 to consider social purposes. Officers are similarly empowered by RCW 23B.25.060. But middle managers, line supervisors, and individual employees are not directly addressed by the statute. If an SPC's middle manager makes a decision that sacrifices social purpose for profit (or vice versa), the legal analysis is somewhat uncertain.

The most natural reading is that middle managers, acting as agents of the corporation, are bound by the instructions and policies established by the board and senior officers. If the board has directed that social purpose be considered in all material decisions, middle managers are obligated to follow that direction as a matter of corporate policy rather than as a direct statutory duty. The safe harbor would flow through the hierarchy: a middle manager acting consistently with board-level social purpose direction would be protected indirectly through the protection of the directors and officers who established the policy.

But this flow-through protection is not explicit in the statute, and its operation in practice would depend on the specific facts of any dispute. An SPC with strong governance practices would formalize its social purpose expectations in board-approved policies, management training materials, and performance evaluation criteria, so that the board-level commitment actually penetrates to operational decisions. An SPC that relies only on the statutory minimum — articles language and annual reports — may have a weaker connection between its formal mission and its day-to-day operations.

## Appendix C. The Social Purpose Report as a Corporate Artifact

### C.1 The Report as Signal

Viewed through the lens of signaling theory, the annual Social Purpose Report serves a specific function: it is a costly signal the corporation sends to interested constituencies about its commitment to mission pursuit. The cost comes not primarily from the financial expense of producing the report — which is typically modest — but from the commitment required to generate substantive content, the reputational exposure from publication, and the accumulation of documentary evidence that can be reviewed and challenged by stakeholders over time.

A corporation that publishes a thin, aspirational, and content-free Social Purpose Report is sending a weak signal. Such reports can be produced quickly and cheaply, and they commit the corporation to nothing in particular. Stakeholders who review such reports will reasonably discount the corporation's mission commitment accordingly.

A corporation that publishes a substantive report with specific objectives, quantitative performance measures, honest discussion of challenges and failures, and concrete commitments to future action is sending a much stronger signal. Such reports require real internal commitment to produce. They expose the corporation to specific future accountability (it cannot later deny having made the commitments). And they provide a basis for stakeholders to evaluate corporate performance against the corporation's own stated standards.

The Washington SPC statute's permissive content framework means that both weak and strong signals are legally permissible. The statute establishes a floor but allows substantial variation above that floor. The resulting market for Social Purpose Reports is a signaling market: corporations compete to produce reports that credibly demonstrate mission commitment, and stakeholders learn to distinguish credible signals from mere going-through-the-motions compliance.

### C.2 Report Content Typologies

Examining the Social Purpose Reports actually published by Washington SPCs reveals several recurring content typologies.

**Type 1: The Minimal Report.** Consists of a brief restatement of the corporation's social purpose, a generic statement of commitment, and perhaps a list of activities during the fiscal year. Typically one to two pages. Provides no quantitative measures, no comparison to prior years, and no discussion of shortfalls or challenges. Satisfies the statutory minimum but conveys little information beyond compliance.

**Type 2: The Narrative Report.** Consists of a more substantial narrative description of the corporation's activities during the fiscal year in pursuit of its social purpose. May include specific anecdotes, program descriptions, and qualitative assessments. Typically three to ten pages. Provides richer content than the minimal report but still relies primarily on narrative rather than measurement. Most Washington SPC reports in practice fall into this category.

**Type 3: The Metrics Report.** Consists of narrative content supplemented by quantitative performance measures tied to the social purpose. Measures may include employee retention rates, supplier diversity statistics, environmental impact data, charitable giving totals, or customer satisfaction measures. Typically ten to twenty pages. Provides a basis for year-over-year comparison and for evaluation against external benchmarks.

**Type 4: The Integrated Report.** Combines the Social Purpose Report with broader sustainability or ESG reporting, producing a single document that addresses both the narrower SPC statutory requirements and the broader stakeholder expectations. May reference external frameworks (GRI, SASB, TCFD, B Impact Assessment) and may include third-party verification components. Typically twenty or more pages, sometimes approaching the length of a full sustainability report. Used by the most sophisticated Washington SPCs, particularly those that are also subject to voluntary or mandatory sustainability disclosure requirements.

**Type 5: The Themed Report.** Organized around a specific theme or narrative arc for the reporting year — for instance, "Our Year of Supply Chain Transformation" or "Building the Workforce of 2030." Uses the theme to provide coherence and memorability. May be supplemented by quantitative data but emphasizes narrative and thematic framing. Some corporations alternate between themed and metrics-driven reports in different years.

Different typologies are appropriate for different corporations. A small private SPC with limited reporting capacity may reasonably produce a Type 2 Narrative Report. A large SPC with multi-state operations and sophisticated stakeholder expectations should probably produce a Type 3 Metrics Report or Type 4 Integrated Report. The typology itself is not mandated by the statute, but the choice of typology sends its own signal about the corporation's commitment and capacity.

### C.3 The Accountability Feedback Loop

The Social Purpose Report serves not only as an outward signal but also as an internal accountability mechanism. The process of preparing the report — gathering data, drafting narrative, securing board approval, publishing publicly — creates a recurring institutional occasion for the corporation to reflect on its social purpose performance. Boards that take the report seriously use the annual cycle as a prompt for substantive mission review: Are we actually making progress? Are our measures meaningful? Are our objectives ambitious enough? Should we update our strategies?

Boards that treat the report as a perfunctory compliance exercise miss this opportunity. They produce reports to meet the statutory deadline without using the process to inform governance. Over time, this distinction — between corporations that use the reporting cycle for genuine reflection and corporations that treat it as paperwork — becomes visible to stakeholders and influences the credibility of the SPC's mission commitment.

The absence of external verification in Washington's framework means that the internal rigor of the reporting process matters more than it does in jurisdictions with mandatory third-party assessment. A Washington SPC's report is only as credible as its internal commitment makes it. There is no external verifier to substitute for the absence of internal rigor.

### C.4 Publication Practices and Evolution

Over the decade-plus that the Washington SPC statute has been in operation, publication practices among Washington SPCs have evolved. Early reports tended to be short (Type 1 or Type 2), relatively unsophisticated, and inconsistent in their treatment of year-over-year continuity. Later reports have tended toward greater length, more quantitative content, and more explicit reference to external frameworks.

This evolution reflects both the maturation of individual corporations (as their internal reporting capacity grew) and the broader cultural shift toward ESG and sustainability reporting. Corporations that might have produced minimal reports in 2013 found, by 2023, that their employees, customers, and investors expected more substantive content. The SPC report became a subset of a broader set of expected disclosures, and corporations adjusted their publication practices accordingly.

One practical consequence of this evolution is that early SPCs whose reports have been continuously archived provide a particularly valuable dataset for longitudinal analysis. A corporation that has published ten or twelve consecutive annual Social Purpose Reports has produced a documentary record of its mission pursuit that can be reviewed as a whole. Such records — which the statute does not mandate but which sophisticated corporations maintain as best practice — are the raw material for future empirical research on benefit corporation effectiveness.

## Appendix D. The Comparative Table Elaborated

### D.1 Extending the United States Comparison

Part V presented a high-level comparison table across four United States benefit corporation regimes. This appendix elaborates the comparison along additional dimensions and provides more textured analysis of the structural differences.

**Formation Mechanics.** All four regimes permit both formation-at-inception and conversion of existing corporations. The conversion thresholds are roughly similar — two-thirds of voting stock is the modal requirement across the four jurisdictions, though with variations in the precise class/series aggregation rules. Washington and Maryland require two-thirds of each voting group (aggregate plus each class/series voting separately). Delaware requires two-thirds of outstanding stock but has some flexibility in the class/series treatment. California's formulation is similar to Maryland's.

**Articles Content Requirements.** Washington is unique in requiring the investor-protection disclosure language in the articles themselves. Delaware requires the identification of specific public benefits in the certificate of incorporation but does not require the equivalent investor-protection disclosure. Maryland and California require statements of general public benefit purpose but do not require the Washington-style disclosure.

**Name Designation.** All four jurisdictions use distinctive corporate name designations ("SPC," "PBC," "Benefit Corporation," etc.), but the degree to which the designation is mandatory varies. Washington provides for use of "Social Purpose Corporation" or the abbreviation "SPC." Delaware mandates "public benefit corporation," "P.B.C." or "PBC." California and Maryland have their own conventions. The practical effect is that in all four states, counterparties dealing with a benefit corporation receive clear notice of its status through the name alone.

**Director Consideration Categories.** Washington enumerates three categories (employees/suppliers/customers; community; environment). Delaware does not enumerate specific categories in the same way; its balancing duty applies to "stockholder pecuniary interests, the best interests of those materially affected by the corporation's conduct, and the specific public benefit or public benefits identified in its certificate of incorporation." California and Maryland, following the B Lab model, enumerate seven categories: shareholders, employees, customers, community, environment, short-term interests, and ability to accomplish purposes.

**Business Judgment Rule Application.** All four regimes rely on some version of the business judgment rule to provide deferential review of director decisions. Washington's safe harbor functions as a BJR extension. Delaware's § 365(c) provides explicit enhanced deference ("not such that no person of ordinary, sound judgment would approve"), which is a strikingly low bar. California and Maryland rely on the general BJR with modifications for benefit corporation-specific duties.

**Shareholder Remedies.** All four regimes restrict benefit-corporation-specific claims to shareholders. Washington has no benefit enforcement proceeding; enforcement is limited to the summary court-ordered report delivery remedy after two years of noncompliance. Delaware has no benefit-corporation-specific enforcement proceeding but allows stockholders to bring ordinary derivative claims for breaches of the balancing duty. California and Maryland authorize benefit enforcement proceedings with broader remedial authority.

### D.2 Delaware PBC Framework in Additional Detail

Because Delaware is such an important jurisdiction for American corporate law, the Delaware PBC framework deserves additional elaboration. The Delaware PBC was created by amendments to the Delaware General Corporation Law enacted in 2013, creating new Subchapter XV (§§ 361-368). Key features:

- **Section 361** provides that the subchapter applies to every corporation organized under the title that meets the requirements of the subchapter.
- **Section 362(a)** defines the public benefit corporation and requires identification of specific public benefits in the certificate of incorporation.
- **Section 362(b)** imposes the "public benefit corporation" or "PBC" name requirement.
- **Section 363** governs transactions and conversions, requiring supermajority approvals for actions that would cause the corporation to cease to be a PBC or otherwise undermine its public benefit purposes.
- **Section 364** provides for stock certificates and similar administrative matters.
- **Section 365(a)** imposes the balancing duty.
- **Section 365(b)** provides the third-party bar: directors owe no duty to persons affected by the corporation's conduct.
- **Section 365(c)** provides the enhanced-deference safe harbor.
- **Section 366** requires a biennial statement to stockholders on the corporation's promotion of the public benefit, including an assessment of success.
- **Section 367** provides for derivative enforcement of the balancing duty, but with substantial standing requirements: at least two percent of outstanding shares or, for listed companies, at least two million dollars in market value.
- **Section 368** clarifies that the subchapter does not affect non-PBC corporations.

Several features of the Delaware structure are worth noting in comparison with Washington.

First, the Delaware PBC is more procedurally demanding at formation: identification of specific public benefits is required, whereas Washington's general purpose framework (with specific purposes optional) is more permissive.

Second, the Delaware balancing duty is substantively stronger than the Washington "may consider" formulation, but the § 365(c) enhanced deference makes it practically very difficult to enforce.

Third, the Delaware biennial reporting requirement is less frequent than Washington's annual requirement, and the report goes only to stockholders rather than being publicly published. Delaware's transparency is therefore weaker than Washington's on reporting, though Delaware's substantive duty is stronger.

Fourth, the Delaware standing requirements for derivative enforcement — two percent of outstanding shares or two million dollars in market value — are meaningfully restrictive. Washington has no analogous threshold. A small Washington SPC shareholder may petition for the summary court-ordered report delivery remedy (after two years of noncompliance), whereas a small Delaware PBC stockholder has no practical path to enforcement.

The overall Delaware framework reflects Delaware's distinctive approach to corporate law: substantive duties combined with deferential review and restrictive enforcement, producing a legal framework that is formally strong but practically permissive. This is consistent with Delaware's broader preference for rules that accommodate business flexibility while maintaining the appearance of substantive constraint.

### D.3 The California Trajectory

California's benefit corporation framework deserves particular attention because California's legislative history includes both the Flexible Purpose Corporation (2011, renamed Social Purpose Corporation in 2014) and the parallel Benefit Corporation (2011, based on the B Lab model). California simultaneously enacted two different mission-driven corporate forms, reflecting the diversity of views within the California corporate bar about the proper design.

The Flexible Purpose Corporation was the template that Washington adopted. Its design philosophy was flexibility: corporations could define their own special purposes, no third-party standard was required, and director consideration was permissive rather than mandatory. After several years of operation, California concluded that the FPC label was confusing — "flexible purpose" sounded vague and suggested that mission commitment was weak — and S.B. 1301 in 2014 renamed the FPC as the California Social Purpose Corporation effective January 1, 2015. Extant FPCs automatically continued their existence as SPCs on that date.

The 2014 amendment also tightened certain provisions. It eliminated the waiver for the "management discussion and analysis" requirement in the annual report, and strengthened director consideration requirements. But the core architecture — permissive consideration, no third-party standard requirement, flexible purpose definition — remained intact. The California SPC (post-2015) is therefore a direct analogue to the Washington SPC, with the main differences being: California's broader purpose language, California's stricter MD&A requirement, and California's interaction with the parallel California Benefit Corporation option.

Washington's drafters, working in 2011-2012 on the FPC template, produced a statute that has not needed the 2014 rebranding that California undertook. Whether this reflects better initial drafting, or simply less political pressure, is hard to say. But the result is that Washington's SPC and California's SPC (post-2015) are now substantively quite similar, and represent the "soft" branch of American benefit corporation law in contrast to the "hard" branch represented by Maryland and the B Lab model states.

### D.4 Maryland: The Strongest Benefit Corporation State?

Maryland, as the first benefit corporation state and the closest adopter of the B Lab model, provides the strongest substantive duty framework among the major jurisdictions. Maryland's statute imposes:

- Mandatory pursuit of general public benefit.
- Optional designation of specific public benefit purposes.
- Mandatory director consideration of seven enumerated categories.
- Mandatory annual benefit reporting assessed against a recognized third-party standard.
- Authorization of benefit enforcement proceedings by shareholders, directors, and specified ownership blocs.

Despite this apparently strong framework, Maryland's benefit corporation law has produced very little enforcement activity. The benefit enforcement proceeding has rarely been invoked, and no published Maryland case (as of this writing) has adjudicated a substantive breach of the Maryland director consideration duty. The gap between the statute's formal strength and its practical operation is significant.

Several explanations are possible. First, benefit corporations may be self-selecting for mission commitment, meaning the population of corporations under the statute is more likely than average to satisfy their duties voluntarily, reducing the frequency of litigable disputes. Second, the shareholder-only standing rule may effectively filter out most potential challenges: shareholders who bought into a benefit corporation typically support its mission and have weak incentives to sue. Third, the costs of litigation are high and the expected recoveries low, producing a practical disincentive even where substantive grounds for challenge might exist. Fourth, corporations with mission-abandonment tendencies may convert out of benefit corporation status rather than fight shareholder challenges.

The Maryland experience suggests that formal duty strength may matter less in practice than informal cultural and reputational factors. A corporation that genuinely embraces its benefit corporation mission will behave consistently with its duties regardless of the enforcement mechanism. A corporation that does not will find ways to evade enforcement regardless of how strong the formal duty is. If this is the lesson, then Washington's softer "may consider" approach may impose less cost for essentially similar practical effect.

### D.5 Extending the International Comparison

The international comparison can be extended to additional jurisdictions whose treatment of mission-driven corporate forms is worth noting.

**Spain:** Spain does not have a benefit corporation statute as such, but has developed a framework of "social enterprise" recognition under labor law and social economy legislation. The Spanish *empresa social* designation provides tax and procurement benefits to qualifying entities but does not alter corporate fiduciary duties in the way a benefit corporation statute would.

**Colombia:** Colombia became the second Latin American country (after Chile, which adopted earlier) to enact benefit corporation legislation, through Law 1901 of 2018 creating the *Sociedad Benefit e Interés Colectivo* (BIC). The Colombian statute is modeled on the B Lab framework and requires third-party assessment. Uptake has been modest but growing.

**Netherlands:** The Netherlands offers the *B Corp Nederland* certification program but no distinct corporate form. Dutch B Corps operate under the standard Dutch private or public company forms (BV or NV) with voluntary adherence to B Lab standards.

**Germany:** Germany does not have a benefit corporation statute per se, but the long-established *Stiftung* (foundation) structure — including charitable foundations holding controlling interests in operating companies — provides a functionally equivalent mechanism for mission-driven governance. Notable examples include the Bosch Stiftung (Robert Bosch GmbH), the Carl Zeiss Stiftung, and the Krupp Stiftung.

**Japan:** Japan has adopted the *kabushiki gaisha* (K.K.) as its standard for-profit corporate form without a benefit corporation variant. However, Japanese corporate culture and governance norms have historically emphasized stakeholder considerations (employees, keiretsu partners, communities) more than shareholder primacy, producing a de facto stakeholder orientation in the absence of benefit corporation legislation.

**Australia:** Australia does not have a benefit corporation statute but has developed a voluntary "B Corp Australia" certification system through B Lab. Discussions about formal legal recognition have occurred but have not produced legislation as of 2026.

The international picture suggests that benefit corporation legislation, as a specific legal form, is most prominent in common law jurisdictions (United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia) and in civil law jurisdictions that have deliberately imported the common law innovation (Italy, France, Colombia). Other jurisdictions have achieved similar outcomes through different legal mechanisms: tax and procurement preferences (Spain), foundation structures (Germany), cultural governance norms (Japan), or voluntary certification (Netherlands, Australia).

## Appendix E. The Interpretive Vacuum: Absence of Case Law

### E.1 Why So Few Cases?

One of the most striking features of Chapter 23B.25 is the near-complete absence of Washington appellate case law construing the statute. As of 2026, no published Washington appellate decision has substantively interpreted the "may consider" duty, the safe harbor, the third-party bar, the reporting requirement, or the enhanced voting thresholds for SPC-specific corporate actions. The statute has operated for more than a decade without producing the kind of judicial gloss that would normally accompany a significant corporate law innovation.

Several explanations are possible.

First, the population of Washington SPCs is small. With an estimated several hundred active SPCs (the exact number is hard to determine from public records), the base rate for any given SPC experiencing litigation in any given year is low. Litigation is expensive and requires motivation; most SPC directors, operating small private corporations, do not face the shareholder populations or transactional pressures that typically generate corporate law litigation.

Second, the permissive nature of the statute reduces the pool of potential disputes. Under a "shall consider" or "must balance" regime, there are more occasions on which a director's failure to satisfy the duty could provide grounds for challenge. Under the "may consider" regime, the director's scope of discretion is broad enough that most actions can be rationalized as either falling within or outside the social purpose framework, without creating clear legal violations.

Third, the shareholder-only standing rule restricts potential plaintiffs. Non-shareholder stakeholders — employees, community members, customers, suppliers — who might have substantive complaints about an SPC's failure to pursue its mission have no legal standing to bring those complaints to court. Only shareholders can sue, and shareholders who invest in SPCs are disproportionately likely to be mission-aligned and therefore supportive rather than critical of the corporation's mission-oriented decisions.

Fourth, the reporting enforcement mechanism is weak enough to discourage litigation. The summary court-ordered delivery remedy is unusual and practically underutilized. The cost of bringing such a petition — attorney fees, filing costs, opportunity costs — may exceed the benefits, particularly since the remedy is limited to specific performance of the reporting obligation rather than damages.

Fifth, the case law of related Title 23B provisions (director duty of care, shareholder rights, dissenters' rights) provides persuasive authority for most questions that might arise under Chapter 23B.25. A Washington court analyzing an SPC director duty question would likely start with the general RCW 23B.08.300 framework and then ask whether the SPC-specific provisions change the analysis. The absence of SPC-specific precedent does not mean the absence of legal guidance; it means that SPC questions are analyzed under the broader Washington corporate law framework.

### E.2 What Would a Test Case Look Like?

Speculating about what might eventually produce the first significant Washington SPC case, several scenarios can be imagined.

**Scenario 1: Mission Abandonment Dispute.** An SPC's board proposes to convert out of SPC status (invoking RCW 23B.25.140) over the objections of a minority shareholder holding more than one-third of the voting stock. The minority shareholder sues to prevent the conversion, arguing (perhaps) that the board's decision to recommend conversion breaches the director duties of section 050. This would produce a concrete dispute about how the "may consider" duty applies to fundamental corporate changes, and would force a court to interpret the interaction between the section 050 duty and the conversion provisions of section 140.

**Scenario 2: Reporting Compliance Action.** A shareholder of an SPC that has failed to publish Social Purpose Reports for two consecutive fiscal years petitions the superior court for a summary order under the last paragraph of RCW 23B.25.150. The court must interpret what constitutes "failure to comply" (does a late report count? does a deficient report count?) and what relief is appropriate. This would produce the first published interpretation of the statute's unique enforcement mechanism.

**Scenario 3: Acquisition Resistance.** An SPC receives an unsolicited acquisition offer at a premium and the board declines to recommend the offer to shareholders, invoking social purpose considerations. The acquirer or dissident shareholders sue to compel the board to consider the offer on shareholder-primacy terms. The court would be forced to adjudicate the interaction between the SPC safe harbor and traditional Delaware-style *Revlon* doctrine — a doctrinal question of substantial significance.

**Scenario 4: Officer Discretion Dispute.** An SPC's CEO takes an operational action (a hiring decision, a product pricing decision, a supplier selection) that the board subsequently determines was inconsistent with the corporation's social purpose. The board attempts to hold the CEO accountable for breaching the officer duty of section 060. The CEO defends on the grounds that the safe harbor protects her decision. The court must interpret the scope of the officer safe harbor and its relationship to board-level social purpose policies.

**Scenario 5: Articles Ambiguity Dispute.** An SPC's articles contain an ambiguous social purpose statement that admits of multiple interpretations. Some shareholders read the statement to require aggressive pursuit of a particular mission; others read it as permitting a broader range of business activities. A dispute arises about the scope of the statement, and a court is asked to construe the articles. This would produce guidance on how Washington courts will interpret social purpose statements in articles of incorporation.

None of these scenarios has, as of 2026, produced published appellate case law. But any of them could, and practitioners should be aware that the current interpretive vacuum is temporary rather than permanent. The first significant SPC case in Washington will be important precedent, and practitioners counseling SPC clients should be prepared to litigate questions on which no direct authority currently exists.

### E.3 Relying on Foreign Precedent

In the absence of Washington SPC case law, practitioners may look to precedent from other benefit corporation jurisdictions. The persuasive weight of such precedent is limited but not zero.

**Delaware PBC decisions.** The Delaware Chancery Court has occasionally addressed PBC issues in transactional contexts. These decisions would be persuasive for Washington courts considering parallel questions, though the underlying statutory framework differs in important respects (balancing duty vs. may consider, different enforcement structures, different reporting regimes).

**Other state benefit corporation decisions.** Published decisions from Maryland, California, New York, and other benefit corporation states are rare but exist. Their persuasive weight in Washington depends on the closeness of statutory analogy. Decisions construing "shall consider" duties under B Lab model statutes are less directly relevant to Washington's "may consider" framework than decisions construing California's post-2015 SPC statute (which is itself based on the FPC template Washington adopted).

**Federal cases involving benefit corporations.** A small body of federal court decisions has addressed benefit corporation issues in various contexts (tax, bankruptcy, securities). These provide another source of persuasive authority, though their holdings are typically narrow and specific to the federal context.

**Law review commentary.** The benefit corporation literature in law reviews is extensive. While not binding authority, academic commentary can inform a court's understanding of the doctrinal issues and practical considerations involved in a particular dispute. Washington courts are generally receptive to well-reasoned scholarly analysis when confronting novel questions.

## Appendix F. A Hypothetical Formation Walkthrough

This appendix walks through a hypothetical SPC formation to illustrate how Chapter 23B.25 operates in practice. The hypothetical is purely illustrative and uses generic, non-specific facts.

### F.1 Initial Consultation

Consider a hypothetical small business operating in the Pacific Northwest whose founders have decided they wish to commit formally to a social mission alongside profit generation. The founders have been advised by counsel that several corporate structure options are available:

1. Ordinary Washington corporation (Chapter 23B) with private ordering (charter provisions, shareholder agreement) to embed mission commitments.
2. Washington Social Purpose Corporation (Chapter 23B.25) with statutory mission framework.
3. Delaware Public Benefit Corporation with a foreign qualification to do business in Washington.
4. Voluntary B Lab certification of an ordinary Washington corporation without formal legal entity conversion.
5. Some combination of the above.

After discussion, the founders decide to form a Washington SPC. Their reasons, as they articulate them to counsel, are:

- They want the statutory mission framework to be visible in the corporate name and articles, signaling commitment to customers, employees, and investors.
- They prefer the flexibility of the Washington "may consider" framework to the more prescriptive B Lab model, because they want discretion to adapt their mission pursuit as circumstances change.
- They want Washington domicile because they are based in Washington and prefer to deal with Washington courts on corporate disputes.
- They are not planning to seek venture capital in the near term, so the Delaware PBC's advantages for venture-backed companies are not relevant.
- They may pursue B Lab certification later as a supplementary signal, but do not want to commit to the B Impact Assessment as a legal requirement at formation.

Counsel prepares formation documents consistent with these decisions.

### F.2 Drafting the Social Purpose Statement

The social purpose statement is the heart of the articles of incorporation and warrants careful drafting. Counsel considers several alternative formulations.

**Option A: Minimal Compliance.** A bare restatement of the RCW 23B.25.020 categories, indicating that the corporation is organized to promote positive effects on its employees, suppliers, customers, community, and environment. This option satisfies the statute but provides no guidance about what the corporation actually intends to do.

**Option B: Specific Mission.** A more particular statement identifying the corporation's specific social purpose under RCW 23B.25.030 and describing how that purpose relates to the general categories of section 020. For example: "The corporation is organized to promote positive long-term effects upon its employees, customers, and community by providing high-quality, fairly-priced services in the Pacific Northwest, creating stable family-wage employment for skilled workers, and contributing to the economic vitality of the communities in which it operates."

**Option C: Ambitious Mission.** A broader statement articulating aspirational objectives tied to the corporation's social purpose. For example: "The corporation is organized to be a model employer in its industry, setting regional standards for employee compensation, training, and advancement; to practice transparent and fair dealing with all suppliers and customers; and to contribute to the long-term economic and environmental health of the communities in which it operates."

Counsel recommends Option B as a balance between specificity and flexibility. Option A provides too little guidance to direct future decision-making. Option C creates measurement and accountability challenges that the corporation may struggle to meet in early years. Option B articulates specific commitments that can be reported against in annual Social Purpose Reports without imposing unrealistic performance expectations.

The founders agree, and the articles of incorporation are drafted to include Option B as the specific social purpose under RCW 23B.25.030, along with the general social purpose framework under RCW 23B.25.020 and the mandatory investor-protection disclosure required by RCW 23B.25.040.

### F.3 Additional Charter Provisions

Beyond the statutory minimum, counsel recommends several additional charter provisions:

**Enhanced director duty.** Converting the "may consider" default of RCW 23B.25.050 into a "shall consider" duty by charter provision under RCW 23B.25.040(2). This strengthens the board's commitment to mission pursuit and aligns the corporation more closely with the B Lab and Delaware PBC models for companies that may later decide to pursue certification.

**Reporting specifications.** Including in the charter specific requirements for the annual Social Purpose Report beyond the statutory minimum: for example, requiring inclusion of quantitative performance measures, acknowledging challenges and shortfalls, and providing year-over-year comparisons.

**Enhanced voting thresholds.** Raising certain voting thresholds above the statutory two-thirds to further protect the social purpose against dilution. For example, requiring a three-fourths vote for conversion out of SPC status or for mergers in which the SPC does not survive.

**Mission review committee.** Establishing a board committee specifically charged with reviewing social purpose performance and advising the full board on mission pursuit. This committee would have responsibility for drafting the annual Social Purpose Report and would have authority to recommend policy changes to the full board.

**Stakeholder advisory panel.** Creating a non-voting stakeholder advisory panel composed of representatives from employee, customer, and community constituencies, with formal channels for communicating concerns to the board. This is a private-ordering analog to the French Société à Mission's mission committee requirement.

The founders review these recommendations and incorporate some but not all into the charter. They adopt the enhanced director duty, the reporting specifications, and the mission review committee. They decide against the stakeholder advisory panel (as adding governance complexity they are not ready for) and against the enhanced voting thresholds (as creating additional friction they want to avoid).

### F.4 Filing and Initial Operations

The articles of incorporation, as drafted, are filed with the Washington Secretary of State along with the required filing fee. The Secretary of State reviews the articles for compliance with statutory requirements — primarily that the mandatory content of RCW 23B.25.040(1) is present — and files them.

Upon filing, the corporation exists as a Washington Social Purpose Corporation. Its name contains "Social Purpose Corporation" or "SPC," its articles contain the required social purpose statement, and its directors operate under the modified duty framework of RCW 23B.25.050 (supplemented by the charter's conversion of "may consider" into "shall consider").

The board of directors adopts initial operating resolutions including:

- A social purpose policy document elaborating on the specific social purpose statement in the articles.
- A social purpose performance framework identifying the metrics the corporation will use in its annual reports.
- A meeting schedule ensuring the board reviews social purpose performance at least quarterly.
- A delegation of day-to-day social purpose monitoring to the CEO and designated management personnel.

The corporation begins operations.

### F.5 First Annual Report

Approximately ten months into the corporation's first fiscal year, the board begins preparing the first annual Social Purpose Report. The mission review committee drafts a narrative covering:

- Restatement of the social purpose from the articles.
- Short-term objectives (first year) and long-term objectives (three to five years).
- Material actions taken during the year.
- Performance measures and year-one results.
- Expected actions for year two.
- Acknowledgment of challenges and shortfalls.

The committee submits the draft to the full board, which reviews and revises the language. After approval, the report is published on the corporation's website within four months of fiscal year close, consistent with RCW 23B.25.150.

The corporation has now completed one full cycle of SPC operation, from formation through its first annual report. The process will repeat annually, with each cycle providing opportunities for the board to reflect on mission pursuit and refine its approach.

### F.6 A Challenging Year

In a subsequent fiscal year, the corporation faces a significant business challenge: a major supplier raises prices dramatically, squeezing margins. The board must decide whether to pass the increased costs on to customers, absorb them through reduced profit, or find alternative suppliers.

This is precisely the kind of decision where the SPC framework matters. Under traditional shareholder-primacy analysis, the decision would be driven by which option maximized expected returns. Under the SPC framework, the board considers the effects on the enumerated categories:

- *Employees:* Could the financial strain affect compensation, benefits, or job security?
- *Customers:* Would price increases harm customers, particularly those with limited ability to absorb higher costs?
- *Community:* How would the decision affect the communities in which the corporation operates?
- *Environment:* Would alternative suppliers have different environmental footprints?
- *Long-term interests:* Which choice best positions the corporation for long-term success?

The board deliberates on each of these dimensions and reaches a decision. Whatever decision it makes, the deliberation itself is protected by the safe harbor provided it was undertaken in good faith with reasonable belief that the chosen action would promote the social purpose. The documentation of the deliberation — which should be reflected in board minutes and subsequently in the annual Social Purpose Report — provides a record of how the SPC framework actually influenced the decision.

This is where benefit corporation legislation proves its value: not in compelling particular outcomes, but in creating a legal and documentary framework that legitimizes mission-informed deliberation. A corporation that deliberates openly about stakeholder interests, documents its reasoning, and publishes its actions in an accountable format has done something meaningfully different from a corporation that treats shareholder returns as the only relevant consideration.

## Appendix G. Open Questions and Research Agenda

The Washington SPC statute, despite its decade-plus of operation, leaves many questions open. This appendix identifies open questions that warrant further research, scholarly attention, and practitioner analysis.

### G.1 Doctrinal Questions

**1. The meaning of "reasonably believes."** The safe harbor of RCW 23B.25.050 protects actions "reasonably believed" to promote the social purpose. Washington courts have not articulated what standard of reasonableness applies. Is it the same standard as under the general business judgment rule? Is it more or less deferential? Is there a minimum evidentiary floor (e.g., must the director articulate her reasoning in contemporaneous documentation)?

**2. The interaction between general and specific social purposes.** When an SPC has both general and specific social purposes, which controls in the event of conflict? Can a specific purpose narrower than the general purpose operate as a limit on director discretion, or is it merely illustrative? If a specific purpose is inconsistent with part of the general purpose, is the statement void, or does the specific purpose prevail?

**3. The scope of the third-party bar.** Section 050 states that the chapter creates no duties to persons other than the corporation and its shareholders. Does this preclude all third-party claims related to SPC status, or only claims based on breach of the director duty? Could an employee sue under general employment law principles for breach of promises made in the corporation's Social Purpose Report? The statutory text is unclear.

**4. The application to non-SPC Washington corporations.** Section 050 states that the chapter does not alter the duties of directors of non-SPCs. But the very existence of the SPC framework could be cited, by negative implication, as evidence that non-SPC directors cannot consider non-shareholder interests. Washington courts have not addressed this question, and the WSBA commentary disclaims any such implication, but the risk of inference remains.

**5. The relationship to dissenters' rights.** Section 120 extends dissenters' rights to certain SPC-specific corporate actions. Does this extension supplement or supplant the general dissenters' rights regime of RCW 23B.13? What happens in cases of overlap?

**6. The treatment of SPC subsidiaries and affiliates.** If a Washington SPC forms a non-SPC subsidiary, do the parent's social purpose obligations extend to the subsidiary's operations? Can a parent SPC's directors be held accountable for the mission-related actions of a subsidiary? The statute is silent.

**7. The application to SPC employees.** SPC officers are explicitly covered by section 060, but what about non-officer employees? Can an SPC's employee handbook or employment agreements bind rank-and-file employees to social purpose considerations in their work? What remedies are available if an employee violates such commitments?

### G.2 Empirical Questions

**1. Formation trends.** How many Washington SPCs have been formed over time? What industries are they concentrated in? What is the distribution of specific social purposes? How do formation trends compare with benefit corporation uptake in other states?

**2. Conversion patterns.** How many Washington corporations have converted to SPC status since 2012? How many SPCs have converted out? What factors predict conversion in either direction?

**3. Reporting compliance.** What percentage of Washington SPCs publish Social Purpose Reports on time? What percentage publish at all? What is the average quality of reports (by some objective measure like length, quantitative content, or adherence to external frameworks)?

**4. Survival rates.** What is the survival rate of Washington SPCs compared to ordinary Washington corporations? Do SPCs have higher or lower failure rates? Do they experience different financing patterns, growth trajectories, or exit outcomes?

**5. Enforcement activity.** How many petitions have been filed under the summary court-ordered report delivery remedy? How have courts responded? How many SPC-related derivative actions have been filed? How many have settled, been dismissed, or proceeded to judgment?

**6. Economic impact.** Do SPCs generate meaningfully different economic outcomes — for employees, customers, suppliers, communities, or the environment — compared to ordinary corporations operating in similar industries? Is there any evidence of causal impact from the SPC structure?

**7. Investor behavior.** How do Washington investors treat SPC status in their investment decisions? Is there a market pricing effect for SPC shares compared to ordinary shares? Do SPC investors behave differently from ordinary investors in terms of holding periods, voting patterns, or engagement?

### G.3 Comparative Questions

**1. Cross-state comparative effectiveness.** How does Washington's SPC framework compare with other states' benefit corporation frameworks in terms of actual corporate behavior? Controlling for industry, size, and founder characteristics, do Washington SPCs behave differently from Maryland benefit corporations, Delaware PBCs, or California benefit corporations?

**2. Choice of domicile.** Among Washington-based mission-driven businesses, what factors predict the choice of Washington SPC versus Delaware PBC versus ordinary Washington corporation with voluntary B Lab certification? Do choice patterns align with rational predictions from the underlying statutory frameworks?

**3. International transplant questions.** What would it take to make the Washington SPC framework exportable to other jurisdictions? Are there elements of the framework that rely on specifically Washington or American legal context, and could the framework operate equivalently in civil law or non-US common law systems?

### G.4 Normative Questions

**1. Is softer better?** Washington's "may consider" formulation is the softest among the major benefit corporation regimes. Does this softer approach produce outcomes that are better, worse, or similar to those produced by stronger duty frameworks? On what metrics should this comparison be evaluated?

**2. The role of third-party standards.** Washington does not require third-party assessment. Does this omission reduce SPC credibility, or does it enable more authentic mission pursuit? How should we think about the trade-off between external verification and internal authenticity?

**3. Enforcement design.** Washington's enforcement mechanism is minimal. Should it be strengthened? If so, by adding benefit-enforcement-proceeding-style remedies? By authorizing Attorney General enforcement? By creating penalties for reporting failures? What would be the costs and benefits of each alternative?

**4. The purpose trust question.** Patagonia's 2022 restructuring used a purpose trust as the principal mission-preservation device, with benefit corporation status as a supporting element. Should Washington develop purpose trust legislation to complement its SPC framework? What would such legislation look like?

**5. The one-hundred-year question.** Can the Washington SPC framework support multi-generational mission persistence? If not, what additional legal infrastructure would be required? Should Washington consider creating new forms or expanding existing forms to better serve founders with century-scale ambitions?

These open questions collectively define a research agenda that could occupy scholars and practitioners for the coming decade. The Washington SPC statute, despite its apparent simplicity, raises doctrinal, empirical, comparative, and normative questions of substantial depth. Producing answers to these questions would enrich both Washington corporate law and the broader benefit corporation literature.

## Closing Note

This working paper has attempted to provide a comprehensive doctrinal, historical, and comparative analysis of the Washington Social Purpose Corporation framework as embodied in Chapter 23B.25 of the Revised Code of Washington. The paper's central descriptive claim is that Washington's framework is, by design, the softest of the principal United States benefit corporation regimes — permissive rather than mandatory in its director duties, flexible rather than prescriptive in its reporting requirements, and minimal rather than aggressive in its enforcement mechanisms. Whether this softness is a feature or a defect depends on prior commitments about the function benefit corporation legislation should perform.

The paper has not attempted to render a normative judgment on that question. Instead, it has tried to lay out the statutory architecture in sufficient detail that readers can form their own judgments based on accurate understanding of what the statute actually does, how it relates to alternative frameworks, and what practical questions it leaves open.

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation is an interesting piece of statutory innovation. It is not the dominant American benefit corporation framework — that distinction belongs to the Delaware PBC and the B Lab model states — but it occupies a distinct and coherent position in the landscape of mission-driven corporate forms. Washington's choice to prioritize flexibility over substantive duty intensity reflects considered judgments by thoughtful drafters working within a particular legal culture. The resulting statute has operated for more than a decade with remarkable stability, generating modest but real uptake and producing a body of practical experience that can inform future legislative and judicial development.

Whether the Washington SPC is the right tool for any particular mission-driven venture is a question that depends on the specific circumstances of the venture, the preferences of its founders, and the legal culture in which it operates. No single corporate form is right for every case. But for founders who want a Washington-domiciled mission-driven structure with a flexible duty framework, straightforward formation mechanics, and predictable integration with existing Washington corporate law, the Social Purpose Corporation provides a workable solution. The statute's quiet decade suggests that it has found its audience and serves its intended function.

The broader benefit corporation movement, of which Washington's SPC is one expression, represents a significant experiment in corporate law reform. It will take decades more of operational experience to assess whether the experiment has succeeded in its aspirations — whether it has enabled meaningful mission-driven enterprise, whether it has shifted director and investor expectations, whether it has produced measurable changes in corporate behavior, and whether it has supported the multi-generational persistence of founder vision that its most ambitious advocates hoped for. The questions posed in Appendix G remain open. The research agenda is substantial. And the conversation between legal scholars, practitioners, founders, and policymakers about what corporations should be and how the law should structure their operation continues.

Washington's Social Purpose Corporation is one modest contribution to that conversation. This paper is another.

## Appendix I. Section-by-Section Commentary Reconstruction

This appendix attempts, for each section of Chapter 23B.25, to reconstruct the interpretive guidance that the WSBA Business Law Section's Corporate Act Revision Committee commentary provides. The actual Sourcebook commentary is a copyrighted WSBA publication not reproduced here; what follows is a reconstruction based on the statutory text, the structure of the Washington Business Corporation Act, and the documented drafting intent as reflected in publicly available sources.

### I.1 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.005

The opening section's structural role is worth elaborating. The drafters deliberately placed the formation-and-cessation provision first in the chapter, ahead of the substantive content provisions, to emphasize that SPC status is an elective matter. A Washington corporation is not automatically an SPC by virtue of having a social mission; it becomes an SPC only by deliberate election, either through at-formation articles filing or through conversion under section 130. Conversely, an SPC can cease to be an SPC through the section 140 process. The statutory framework is opt-in and reversible, not automatic or permanent.

This emphasis on electivity serves several purposes. It respects founder autonomy by refusing to impose benefit-corporation-style obligations on corporations that have not chosen them. It preserves flexibility by allowing SPCs that later conclude the framework no longer serves their needs to convert out. And it supports doctrinal clarity by maintaining a clear boundary between SPCs and ordinary corporations, rather than creating spectrum of intermediate forms with ambiguous status.

The two-method formation mechanism (at-formation versus conversion) parallels the structure used for other elective status choices under Title 23B, such as the election of close corporation status under Chapter 23B.25's predecessor close corporation provisions (historically codified elsewhere). The parallelism is deliberate and makes the SPC framework easier for practitioners already familiar with Washington corporate law to understand.

### I.2 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.010

Section 010 serves the critical interpretive function of integrating the SPC chapter with the rest of Title 23B. Without this integration provision, a reader might wonder whether the SPC is an entirely new form of entity distinct from the standard corporation, or a variant of the standard corporation, or something in between. Section 010 definitively answers this question: an SPC is a corporation governed by Title 23B generally, except where Chapter 23B.25 provides otherwise.

The practical consequence is that every provision of Washington corporate law applicable to standard corporations applies to SPCs by default. Governance rules, shareholder rights, director indemnification, dissolution procedures, annual report filings with the Secretary of State, corporate tax treatment, conflict-of-interest transactions — all are governed by the general Title 23B provisions unless Chapter 23B.25 explicitly modifies them. This additive overlay structure dramatically simplifies the SPC framework: practitioners do not need to learn an entirely new body of law, but only the relatively small set of modifications contained in Chapter 23B.25.

The subsection (2) cross-reference to RCW 23B.03.010 — the general business purposes provision — confirms that SPCs enjoy the same broad business purpose authority as standard corporations. An SPC can engage in any lawful business, subject only to limitations the corporation chooses to place in its own articles. The social purpose obligation is additive to the general business authority, not a substitute for it.

### I.3 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.020

The three-category enumeration of general social purposes deserves close attention. The drafters' choice to enumerate three specific categories — rather than a broader "general public benefit" formulation or a narrower single-category specification — reflects a balance among competing considerations.

A broader formulation would provide greater flexibility to SPCs but would also create greater interpretive uncertainty. The B Lab model's "general public benefit" language has been criticized precisely because its breadth makes it difficult to evaluate whether any particular action satisfies it. Washington's three-category approach reduces this uncertainty by giving SPCs more concrete categories to target.

A narrower formulation would provide greater clarity but would unduly restrict the range of mission-driven activities the statute could support. Requiring all SPCs to focus on, say, environmental protection would exclude SPCs whose missions focus on employee welfare, community development, or supply chain fairness. The three-category approach covers the main categories of stakeholder concern while avoiding over-specification.

The drafters' decision to group employees, suppliers, and customers into a single category — rather than separating them as the B Lab model does — was a deliberate simplification. In practice, these three categories share important features: they are all direct contractual counterparties of the corporation, they are all subject to similar kinds of corporate decisions (pricing, terms, quality, fairness), and they can all be served through broadly similar management practices. Treating them as a single category reduces the complexity of the statutory framework without meaningfully narrowing its scope.

The disjunctive framing ("any or all of") is equally important. An SPC need not pursue positive effects across all three categories; it may focus on a single category if that is consistent with its mission. This is significantly more permissive than the B Lab model, which requires pursuit of "general public benefit" across all enumerated dimensions. Washington's drafters concluded that single-category missions (e.g., "an SPC committed solely to environmental stewardship") are legitimate and should be accommodated.

Finally, the "promote positive" / "minimize adverse" framing accommodates different business models. A low-impact service business may credibly promise to promote positive effects through its active operations. A higher-impact manufacturing or extractive business may more credibly promise to minimize adverse effects. The statute does not force either framing; it permits both.

### I.4 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.030

Section 030's enabling provision for specific social purposes is brief but significant. By authorizing SPCs to articulate particular social purposes beyond the general framework, the statute allows founders to tailor their mission commitments to specific circumstances. A rural Washington agricultural cooperative might specify its purpose as "supporting small-scale sustainable farming in the Columbia Basin." A Seattle technology company might specify its purpose as "providing affordable digital access to underserved communities in King County." A Spokane manufacturer might specify its purpose as "maintaining family-wage employment opportunities in Eastern Washington."

These specific purposes supplement, rather than replace, the general social purpose framework of section 020. An SPC adopting a specific purpose must still be organized consistently with the general three-category framework. But the specific purpose provides more granular guidance for directors and officers, and more concrete accountability content for shareholders, employees, customers, and community stakeholders reviewing the corporation's Social Purpose Report.

The optional nature of specific purposes means that an SPC can choose the level of specificity appropriate to its circumstances. A young SPC still defining its mission may reasonably rely solely on the general framework, preserving flexibility to elaborate its specific commitments as its understanding deepens. A mature SPC with well-developed mission content may benefit from articulating specific purposes to anchor its reporting and accountability.

### I.5 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.040

The articles of incorporation provision is among the most detailed in the chapter, reflecting the drafters' view that the articles serve as the principal legal repository of the SPC's mission commitment. Several features warrant elaboration.

First, the mandatory content elements of subsection (1) ensure that every SPC articulates, in its foundational governance document, both the general and (if applicable) specific social purposes it is organized to pursue. This is a stronger commitment than the B Lab model, which permits benefit corporations to identify their purpose at a higher level of generality.

Second, the mandatory investor-protection disclosure is distinctively Washingtonian. The required language — stating that the SPC's mission "is not necessarily compatible with and may be contrary to maximizing profits and earnings for shareholders" — functions as a form of prospectus disclosure built into the articles themselves. Every prospective investor in an SPC is, by operation of the statute, on constructive notice that mission pursuit may involve profit trade-offs. This disclosure provides affirmative defense material for directors later sued by shareholders who claim to have been surprised by mission-driven decisions; the articles themselves provide proof of notice.

Third, the optional content elements of subsection (2) permit sophisticated founders to design SPC governance frameworks that go beyond the statutory default. The ability to convert "may consider" into "shall consider," to require third-party assessment, and to impose enhanced voting thresholds provides substantial flexibility for founders who want stronger mission protection than the default provides. In this sense, the Washington SPC is a platform rather than a fixed product: the statute provides a foundation, and the articles can be customized to build whatever governance structure the founders want.

Fourth, the disclosure obligations of subsections (3) and (4) ensure that notice of SPC status reaches every shareholder, not just founding investors. When shares are issued or transferred, the receiving shareholder must be provided with articles (or at least with notice of the articles' availability). This prevents the emergence of an uninformed secondary shareholder population who might later claim surprise at the SPC's mission-driven operations.

### I.6 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.050

The director duty provision is the doctrinal heart of the chapter and warrants extensive commentary. Several interpretive issues recur in practice.

**The "may consider" choice.** As discussed throughout this paper, the drafters chose "may consider" rather than "shall consider." This choice was deliberate. The Maryland / B Lab formulation — "shall consider" — was considered and rejected, partly on justiciability grounds (how would a court evaluate whether consideration had occurred?) and partly on consistency-with-general-Washington-law grounds (a "shall consider" duty would create a sharper contrast with the duties of non-SPC directors than the drafters wanted to introduce).

**The safe harbor.** The safe harbor is the central protective feature for SPC directors. Its effect is to convert the potentially contested question "was this action in the best interests of the corporation?" into the more tractable question "did the director reasonably believe this action would promote the social purpose?" The latter question is protected by business-judgment-rule-style deference, making derivative challenges to SPC directors' mission-oriented decisions very difficult to sustain.

**The good-faith requirement.** The safe harbor is available only for actions taken "in good faith." This imports the duty of loyalty into the SPC framework. A director who invokes social purpose as a pretext for decisions actually motivated by self-interest or loyalty to a third party is not protected. The good-faith requirement ensures that the SPC structure cannot be used to immunize self-dealing or disloyal conduct.

**The third-party bar.** The provision that the chapter creates no duties or rights running to persons other than the corporation and its shareholders is consistent with the broader American pattern across benefit corporation statutes. It ensures that the SPC framework does not inadvertently create a new class of claimants (employees, suppliers, customers, community members, environmental groups) with standing to sue directors. Enforcement flows through the existing shareholder derivative action mechanism, not through novel stakeholder suits.

**The non-alteration of non-SPC duties.** The provision that the chapter does not alter the duties of directors of non-SPCs is important for avoiding doctrinal spillovers. Without this language, a court might be tempted to infer that the explicit SPC authorization to consider social purposes implies an implicit prohibition on such consideration by non-SPC directors. The statute disclaims this inference, preserving the pre-existing zone of discretion for non-SPC directors.

### I.7 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.060

The officer duty provision parallels the director duty provision and serves the critical function of extending the SPC framework down to the operational level. Without section 060, directors would be empowered to make mission-oriented board-level decisions, but officers implementing those decisions would remain technically subject to pure shareholder-primacy duties. Section 060 closes this gap by giving officers the same permission to consider social purposes that directors enjoy under section 050.

The practical significance of officer authorization is substantial. Most operational decisions — hiring, pricing, vendor selection, product design, marketing — are made by officers or their subordinates, not by the board. If officers could not consider social purposes, the SPC framework would be effectively disabled at the level where most decisions actually occur. Section 060 ensures that the SPC's mission orientation penetrates from the board level down into operational management.

The "unless the articles provide otherwise" language permits articles-based modification of the officer duty. An SPC whose founders want stricter control over officer discretion — for instance, by requiring officers to defer to board-approved social purpose policies in all material decisions — can achieve that result through charter drafting.

### I.8 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.070

The shares-representation provision is technical and unremarkable. It exists to confirm that SPC shares may be represented either by certificate or uncertificated, consistent with the general Title 23B framework, and to ensure that the SPC framework does not inadvertently create any ambiguity about share representation conventions.

### I.9 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.080

The derivative action provision is significant because it clarifies that SPC-related disputes are enforceable only through shareholder derivative actions, not through any other mechanism. This is consistent with the section 050 third-party bar but addresses the procedural side of the question: even if a non-shareholder could theoretically articulate a duty owed by the SPC, the non-shareholder lacks standing to bring it to court.

The reference to RCW 23B.07.400 imports the general procedural framework for shareholder derivative actions in Washington. This framework includes requirements for contemporaneous ownership, demand on the board, adequacy of representation, and other procedural safeguards designed to filter out frivolous derivative claims. All of these safeguards apply to SPC-related derivative actions in the same way they apply to ordinary corporate derivative actions.

### I.10 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.090

The enhanced voting requirement for amendments that materially change social purposes reflects the drafters' concern that social purposes should not be diluted or abandoned by simple majority vote. The two-thirds threshold provides meaningful protection against casual mission drift while still permitting responsive amendment when a supermajority genuinely supports the change.

The "materially change" qualifier is important. Routine wordsmithing of the social purpose statement — rephrasing without substantive alteration — does not trigger the enhanced threshold. This permits SPCs to refine and clarify their mission statements over time without requiring a full supermajority vote for every technical adjustment. The determination of whether a given amendment is "material" will, in practice, be made by the board in the first instance and subject to challenge by dissenting shareholders if contested.

The class-based voting requirement — two-thirds of each class or series voting separately, in addition to the aggregate two-thirds threshold — provides additional protection for minority classes with distinct interests in the social purpose. A majority class cannot override a protected minority class's preferences on mission matters; each class gets its own two-thirds veto.

### I.11 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.100

The enhanced voting requirement for mergers in which the SPC does not survive is the most important mission-protection provision in the chapter. Without this provision, an SPC could be merged into an ordinary corporation on a simple majority vote, effectively ending the mission commitment without triggering any of the chapter's protective mechanisms. Section 100 raises the bar to two-thirds, preventing back-door mission abandonment through transactional structuring.

The exception for mergers in which the surviving entity is itself an SPC with consistent specific purposes is narrow and technical. It permits SPC-to-SPC mergers on normal merger thresholds, recognizing that such mergers do not actually threaten the mission commitment — both the disappearing and surviving entities are committed to similar purposes. The "do not materially diverge" language ensures that SPC-to-SPC mergers are only protected from the enhanced threshold when the mission continuity is genuine rather than nominal.

### I.12 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.110

The asset-sale provision is the mission-protection analog to the merger provision of section 100. Together, sections 100 and 110 prevent the most common transactional mechanisms by which mission commitments could be abandoned: mergers and asset sales. The enhanced voting threshold — two-thirds of each voting group — applies to both.

The "all or substantially all" language tracks the standard formulation in Title 23B generally for transactions triggering enhanced voting requirements. Washington courts have developed some case law on what constitutes a sale of "all or substantially all" of a corporation's assets, and that case law applies to the SPC provision by cross-reference.

### I.13 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.120

The dissenters' rights provision extends the general dissenters' rights framework of RCW 23B.13 to cover SPC-specific fundamental changes. Shareholders who dissent from certain SPC-related corporate actions — conversion out of SPC status, mergers involving SPC status changes, certain amendments — are entitled to receive fair value for their shares.

The dissenters' rights mechanism provides an exit option for shareholders who disagree with majority decisions that nonetheless meet the enhanced voting thresholds. A shareholder who opposes conversion of the SPC to a standard corporation, for instance, can dissent and recover fair value rather than being forced to remain a shareholder of the newly converted non-SPC.

### I.14 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.130

The conversion-in provision provides the procedural framework for an existing Washington corporation to elect SPC status. Several features warrant elaboration.

The plan-of-election requirement ensures that conversions are undertaken deliberately and with full documentation. The plan must address equal treatment of shares within classes, the specific articles amendments required for SPC status, and any additional terms the board wishes to include. This documentation creates a record of the conversion that can be reviewed by courts, regulators, and future diligence.

The board recommendation requirement — with disclosed reason for non-recommendation if the board declines to recommend — ensures that shareholders have the benefit of board guidance in considering the conversion. If the board cannot or will not recommend, shareholders should know why; the disclosure requirement forces the board to articulate its reasoning.

The two-thirds voting threshold — both aggregate and class-separate — matches the threshold for conversion out and for other fundamental changes affecting mission status. This consistency is intentional: the drafters treated conversion in and conversion out as equally significant decisions warranting equivalent procedural protection.

The effective-date provision — later of articles filing or any specified effective date — provides flexibility for conversions that need to align with other transactions or events.

The continuation-of-existence provision confirms that conversion does not create a new corporate entity; the converted corporation continues with its original incorporation date and all pre-existing contracts, assets, and liabilities. This is important for corporate continuity purposes and avoids the need to reassign contracts or refile intellectual property rights.

### I.15 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.140

The conversion-out provision mirrors the conversion-in provision and serves the parallel function. The drafters' decision to make SPC status reversible — rather than creating a permanent "mission lock" — reflects a considered policy choice.

Arguments for reversibility include: flexibility to respond to changed circumstances; avoidance of paternalism (adult founders should be free to undo their own decisions); consistency with the general American reluctance to impose permanent commitments on corporate entities; and respect for shareholder autonomy (a supermajority of shareholders should be able to change the corporate form if they collectively believe it serves their interests).

Arguments against reversibility — and in favor of permanent mission lock — include: protection of mission commitments against later market-driven pressure; fidelity to founder intent; alignment with the stronger commitments made by international analogs like the UK CIC; and prevention of the kind of strategic conversion that might allow bad actors to use the SPC form temporarily for marketing purposes before abandoning it.

Washington's drafters took the reversibility view, but with meaningful procedural safeguards: the two-thirds supermajority threshold, the class-separate voting requirement, the board recommendation process, and the dissenters' rights mechanism. These safeguards make reversal procedurally difficult without making it impossible. A Washington SPC cannot casually abandon its mission, but it can do so through a properly authorized supermajority decision.

### I.16 Commentary on RCW 23B.25.150

The Social Purpose Report provision is the chapter's principal ongoing accountability mechanism. Its commentary has been addressed extensively in Part IV of the main paper, but several additional points deserve note.

The four-month deadline is generous by financial reporting standards but appropriate for narrative social-purpose reporting. Financial data takes time to collect, audit, and verify, and narrative content about mission pursuit requires reflective work by management and board. Four months is enough time to produce substantive content without being so long that the report becomes stale.

The publication-on-principal-website requirement is notable for its insistence on public accessibility. Unlike the Delaware PBC's biennial stockholder statement — which need not be publicly published — Washington's SPC report must be accessible to anyone with an internet connection. This creates accountability beyond the shareholder constituency: employees, customers, community members, investors considering investment, and journalists can all access the report and evaluate the SPC's mission pursuit.

The enumerated content elements (objectives, actions, expected actions, measures) provide a structural template for report drafting. While the "may include" language means the template is not strictly mandatory, most Washington SPCs in practice follow it closely. The result is a degree of cross-SPC comparability that benefits stakeholders trying to evaluate multiple SPCs.

The enforcement mechanism — court-ordered delivery after two consecutive years of noncompliance — is deliberately minimal. The drafters did not want to create a strong judicial enforcement apparatus that might spawn nuisance suits or discourage SPC formation. They preferred a light-touch enforcement mechanism that could remedy persistent delinquency without creating a litigation industry.

The SEC-alternative provision accommodates publicly traded SPCs by allowing them to include required content in their SEC annual reports rather than producing a separate Social Purpose Report. This is a minor accommodation of limited practical significance but reflects sensible legislative attention to the specific needs of public companies.

## Appendix K. Scenario Analyses

This appendix presents several detailed scenario analyses illustrating how Chapter 23B.25 would apply in specific factual situations. Each scenario is hypothetical and illustrative only.

### K.1 Scenario: The Mission-Abandoning Majority

**Facts.** A Washington SPC was formed in 2015 by a group of four founders who shared a commitment to sustainable agriculture in the Yakima Valley. Over time, the SPC grew and took on outside investors who acquired roughly 40% of the voting stock. The four founders and their family members continue to hold the remaining 60%. In 2025, one of the founders dies, and her shares pass to her estate, which is administered by a trustee who is primarily interested in maximizing returns to the estate beneficiaries rather than preserving the SPC's mission. The trustee, holding approximately 15% of the voting stock, begins working with the outside investors to assemble a majority in favor of converting the SPC out of its social purpose status.

**Analysis.** Under RCW 23B.25.140, conversion out of SPC status requires approval by two-thirds of each voting group. The trustee and outside investors together hold approximately 55% of the voting stock — a simple majority, but less than the two-thirds threshold required for conversion out.

The trustee and outside investors could attempt several strategies to reach the supermajority:
1. Persuade some of the remaining founder-family shareholders to support conversion, either through negotiation or through financial incentives.
2. Acquire additional shares on the secondary market (to the extent shares are available for purchase) to build toward the two-thirds threshold.
3. Attempt to structure a merger (rather than a direct conversion) that might present the transaction in a more appealing form to uncommitted shareholders.

None of these strategies is guaranteed to succeed. The two-thirds threshold is specifically designed to give mission-committed minorities meaningful blocking power, and the family shareholders who support the original mission may well be able to prevent conversion.

If the conversion does proceed, dissenting shareholders — including family members who opposed the conversion — would be entitled to dissenters' rights under RCW 23B.25.120 and could recover fair value for their shares rather than being forced to accept continued participation in the converted corporation.

**Lesson.** The SPC framework's enhanced voting thresholds and dissenters' rights provide meaningful but not absolute protection against mission abandonment. Founders who want stronger protection should consider additional mechanisms: voting trusts, shareholder agreements with mission-protection covenants, or charter provisions imposing higher-than-two-thirds voting thresholds.

### K.2 Scenario: The Sympathetic Employee Lawsuit

**Facts.** A Washington SPC includes "promoting positive long-term effects on its employees" in its social purpose statement. In 2025, the SPC lays off approximately 30% of its workforce during a business downturn. A laid-off employee, supported by employee advocacy organizations, sues the SPC's board of directors claiming that the layoff decision violated the corporation's social purpose commitment and breached the directors' duty to consider employee interests.

**Analysis.** The lawsuit faces multiple hurdles under Chapter 23B.25.

First, the third-party bar of RCW 23B.25.050(5) means that the chapter creates no duties or rights running to persons other than the corporation and its shareholders. The laid-off employee has no standing under the SPC chapter to sue directors for breach of their duty to consider employee interests. The duty runs to the corporation, not to any individual employee.

Second, the shareholder-only derivative action rule of RCW 23B.25.080 confirms that SPC-related claims must be brought by shareholders, not by employees or other stakeholders. The laid-off employee, even if she owned some shares of the SPC (which she might, depending on the SPC's compensation practices), would be bringing the claim in her capacity as a shareholder rather than as an employee.

Third, even if the laid-off employee could overcome the standing hurdles, she would face the safe harbor of section 050(3). If the directors in good faith believed that the layoff decision would serve the long-term interests of the corporation (by preserving financial stability, avoiding bankruptcy, enabling recovery), their decision would be protected by the safe harbor. The laid-off employee would need to rebut this protection by showing either bad faith (difficult without evidence of ulterior motives) or unreasonable belief (difficult to sustain if the directors documented their financial analysis).

Fourth, the laid-off employee may have alternative remedies under employment law — WARN Act notice requirements, wage and hour claims, contract claims, anti-discrimination claims — that are not affected by the SPC chapter. These remedies would be pursued under their own legal frameworks, independent of Chapter 23B.25.

**Lesson.** The SPC framework does not create employee rights of action against directors. Employees who believe their employer has failed to honor its social purpose commitments must rely on non-SPC remedies: employment law, collective bargaining, public advocacy, or shareholder pressure (if they hold shares). The SPC chapter operates entirely within the corporation-shareholder relationship.

### K.3 Scenario: The Reporting Compliance Petition

**Facts.** A Washington SPC was formed in 2018 by enthusiastic founders who intended to take the reporting requirement seriously. They published comprehensive Social Purpose Reports in 2019, 2020, and 2021. In 2022, the founding CEO left the company and was replaced by a new CEO whose focus was primarily financial turnaround. The 2022 report was not published until late 2023, and it was significantly thinner than prior reports. The 2023 report was never published. A shareholder, concerned about the SPC's declining mission commitment, considers petitioning the superior court under RCW 23B.25.150 for an order compelling delivery of the 2023 report.

**Analysis.** The enforcement mechanism of section 150 requires two consecutive years of noncompliance before the court remedy becomes available. As of late 2024, the SPC has failed to publish only one year's report (2023). The 2022 report was late and thin but was apparently published, so it does not count as a failure for purposes of the two-consecutive-year trigger.

The shareholder therefore cannot yet petition for court relief. She must wait until a second consecutive year of noncompliance occurs. If the SPC fails to publish a 2024 report by the four-month deadline in 2025, the shareholder can then petition.

In the meantime, the shareholder has several non-judicial options:
1. Raise the issue at the annual shareholder meeting, either through a formal proposal or through questions from the floor.
2. Communicate directly with the board of directors expressing concern about the reporting lapse.
3. Communicate with other shareholders to build support for mission-oriented changes.
4. Consider whether any dissident slate or proxy campaign is feasible given the shareholder base.
5. Exit the investment by selling her shares, if she can find a buyer.

When the two-year threshold is reached, the shareholder may petition the superior court in the county of the SPC's registered office. The court, on summary procedure, can order delivery of the missing report. The remedy is specific performance, not damages; the shareholder recovers no monetary compensation but does get judicial confirmation that the SPC is obligated to publish.

**Lesson.** The SPC reporting enforcement mechanism is deliberately light-touch. It addresses persistent delinquency rather than occasional lapses, and it provides specific performance rather than damages. Shareholders who want stronger accountability should pursue non-judicial pressure through shareholder voice and governance participation.

### K.4 Scenario: The Acquisition Offer

**Facts.** A Washington SPC is a well-known regional food company with a strong reputation for supporting local suppliers, paying family-wage employment, and environmental sustainability. A large multinational food conglomerate — with a reputation for cost-cutting, supplier consolidation, and aggressive environmental practices — makes an unsolicited offer to acquire the SPC at a 40% premium to the current market price. The SPC's board believes that accepting the offer would fundamentally undermine the corporation's social purpose commitments.

**Analysis.** The board can decline to recommend the offer based on social purpose considerations, and its decision is protected by the safe harbor of RCW 23B.25.050. The directors need only reasonably believe that accepting the offer would harm the corporation's social purposes, and their decision to decline is "deemed to be in the best interests of the corporation."

If the acquirer and some shareholders attempt to force the merger through, the two-thirds voting threshold of RCW 23B.25.100 provides additional protection. Even a simple majority of shareholders cannot compel the merger; a two-thirds supermajority is required. If the acquirer cannot assemble two-thirds support, the merger cannot proceed.

Several tactical considerations would shape the board's response:
1. The board should document its social purpose analysis thoroughly, creating a clear record that directors considered the impact of the offer on the corporation's mission.
2. The board should communicate with shareholders about the rationale for declining the offer, helping to build shareholder support for the mission-oriented decision.
3. The board might consider structural defenses — shareholder rights plans, staggered board provisions, fair-price provisions — that are available under general Washington corporate law and not restricted by Chapter 23B.25.
4. The board should engage with the acquirer in good faith about alternatives — a negotiated transaction preserving the SPC's mission, a price adjustment reflecting the long-term value of the mission, or other creative structures.

If, despite all efforts, the acquirer assembles two-thirds support, the merger can proceed. Dissenting shareholders — including mission-committed minorities — would have dissenters' rights under section 120 and could recover fair value for their shares.

**Lesson.** The SPC framework provides meaningful but not absolute protection against mission-destroying acquisitions. The combination of safe harbor, enhanced voting threshold, and dissenters' rights makes mission-preservation the default but permits determined supermajorities to override it. Founders who want stronger anti-acquisition protection should consider additional mechanisms — voting trusts, purpose trusts, or enhanced charter thresholds.

### K.5 Scenario: The Multi-Class Capital Structure

**Facts.** A Washington SPC was formed with two classes of stock: founders' Class A stock, which has enhanced voting rights, and investors' Class B stock, which has ordinary voting rights. The founders' Class A represents approximately 25% of the total economic interest but 55% of the total voting power. The investors' Class B represents approximately 75% of the economic interest but only 45% of the voting power.

**Analysis.** This dual-class structure is permissible under Washington corporate law generally and is not restricted by Chapter 23B.25. The SPC framework accommodates a wide range of capital structures as long as the social purpose provisions of the articles are properly drafted and the mandatory investor-protection disclosure is included.

The two-thirds voting thresholds for fundamental changes operate on a class-separate basis under sections 090, 100, 110, 130, and 140. This means that amendments to the social purpose, mergers, asset sales, and conversions require approval by two-thirds of each class, voting separately, in addition to the aggregate two-thirds threshold.

For the dual-class SPC, this means:
- Class A founders, holding 55% of total voting power, can unilaterally block fundamental changes by refusing to approve with two-thirds of Class A. Because the founders collectively hold more than one-third of Class A, they can block any change even if all other Class A holders support it.
- Class B investors, holding 45% of total voting power, can unilaterally block fundamental changes by refusing to approve with two-thirds of Class B. Because no single investor holds more than one-third of Class B (assuming typical investor distributions), no single investor can unilaterally block changes, but a coalition representing more than one-third of Class B can do so.
- Both classes must approve, with two-thirds majorities within each class, for any fundamental change to proceed.

The dual-class structure therefore provides strong mission protection by requiring both the founders (Class A) and a supermajority of investors (Class B) to agree before mission-affecting changes can occur. Combined with the general SPC framework, this produces robust protection for the original mission commitments.

**Lesson.** The SPC framework interacts productively with multi-class capital structures. Founders who want even stronger mission protection can use dual-class structures to maintain voting control disproportionate to economic ownership, leveraging the class-separate voting requirements of the SPC chapter to create additional protective layers.

## Appendix L. Historical Document: Annotated Statutory Text

This appendix presents an annotated version of the key statutory provisions of Chapter 23B.25 RCW, as in effect in 2026. The annotations highlight structural features, interpretive issues, and relationships to other provisions. The statutory text is reproduced (in summary or paraphrased form where full text is not essential) followed by marginal commentary.

### L.1 RCW 23B.25.005 — Becoming or ceasing to be a social purpose corporation

> (1) Any corporation may elect to be governed as a social purpose corporation by one of the following means:
> (a) One or more persons may act as incorporator or incorporators of a social purpose corporation by delivering articles of incorporation that conform to the requirements of this chapter to the secretary of state for filing; or
> (b) Any corporation which is not a social purpose corporation may elect to become a social purpose corporation by complying with RCW 23B.25.130.
> (2) Any social purpose corporation may elect to cease to be governed as a social purpose corporation by complying with RCW 23B.25.140.

*Annotation.* The electivity framework is symmetric: any qualifying corporation may opt in, and any SPC may opt out. The statute does not prescribe a permanent commitment. Compare UK CIC (asset lock makes opt-out essentially impossible) and France Société à Mission (reversible through bylaws amendment). The Washington approach is consistent with the general American preference for reversibility in corporate form choices.

### L.2 RCW 23B.25.010 — Powers, rights, and obligations

> (1) Except as otherwise expressly stated in this chapter, the provisions of this title and all powers, rights, and obligations thereunder shall apply to social purpose corporations organized under this chapter, and references in this title to the term "corporation" shall be read to include social purpose corporations organized under this chapter.
> (2) Subject to any limitations contained in the articles of incorporation, a social purpose corporation may engage in any lawful business under RCW 23B.03.010.

*Annotation.* The integration clause is essential to the chapter's architecture. It ensures that the entire body of Washington corporate law — thousands of pages of statute and case law — applies to SPCs by default. The SPC chapter operates as a thin overlay rather than a parallel regime. Practitioners can apply their general Title 23B knowledge to SPCs without learning an entirely new body of law.

### L.3 RCW 23B.25.020 — General social purposes

> Every corporation governed by this chapter must be organized to carry out its business purpose under RCW 23B.03.010 in a manner intended to promote positive short-term or long-term effects of, or minimize adverse short-term or long-term effects of, the corporation's activities upon any or all of (1) the corporation's employees, suppliers, or customers; (2) the local, state, national, or world community; or (3) the environment.

*Annotation.* The three-category enumeration is narrower than the B Lab model's seven categories but broad enough to cover most stakeholder interests. The grouping of employees, suppliers, and customers into a single category is a Washington-specific simplification. The disjunctive "any or all of" permits single-category missions. The bidirectional "promote positive" / "minimize adverse" framing accommodates different business models.

The "must be organized" phrasing creates an organizational rather than operational obligation. The SPC must be organized with social purpose intent; it is not required to achieve specific outcomes. This is consistent with the reporting framework of section 150, which requires reporting on efforts and measures rather than on achieved impact.

### L.4 RCW 23B.25.030 — Specific social purposes

> In addition to the general social purpose set forth in RCW 23B.25.020, every corporation governed by this chapter may have one or more specific social purposes for which the corporation is organized.

*Annotation.* The optional specific purpose provision permits SPCs to tailor their mission commitments to particular circumstances. Specific purposes supplement the general framework but do not replace it. The word "may" makes specific purposes entirely optional; an SPC can operate on the general framework alone.

### L.5 RCW 23B.25.040 — Articles of incorporation

*Annotation.* The articles provision combines mandatory and optional content. Mandatory elements: SPC designation, general purpose statement, investor-protection disclosure. Optional elements: specific purposes, enhanced director duties, third-party assessment, enhanced voting thresholds, duration limits.

The mandatory investor-protection disclosure — stating that the mission "is not necessarily compatible with and may be contrary to maximizing profits" — is distinctively Washingtonian. It substitutes, to some extent, for the stronger substantive duty frameworks of other jurisdictions: Washington places the responsibility on shareholders (via notice in articles) rather than on directors (via duty reformulation).

The disclosure obligations on share issuance and transfer ensure that all shareholders, not just founding investors, receive notice of SPC status and its implications.

### L.6 RCW 23B.25.050 — Duties of director

*Annotation.* This is the chapter's doctrinal center. Key elements:

1. **Baseline duty reaffirmation.** Directors discharge duties per RCW 23B.08.300. The social purpose framework supplements rather than replaces the general duty of care.
2. **"May consider" authorization.** Directors may consider effects on enumerated categories. Permissive, not mandatory. Can be upgraded to "shall consider" by charter provision under 040(2).
3. **Safe harbor.** Actions reasonably believed to promote social purposes are deemed to be in the corporation's best interests. This converts the contested question ("was this in best interests?") into the more tractable question ("was belief reasonable?").
4. **No liability for compliance.** Directors acting in compliance with the section are not liable to anyone.
5. **Third-party bar.** The chapter creates no duties or rights running to non-shareholder stakeholders. Enforcement is limited to the corporation and shareholders.
6. **Non-alteration of non-SPC duties.** The chapter does not affect duties of directors of non-SPCs. Prevents inadvertent spillovers.

The "may consider" formulation is the softest among major benefit corporation regimes. The safe harbor provides strong protection for mission-oriented decisions but does not require mission pursuit.

### L.7 RCW 23B.25.060 — Duties of officer

*Annotation.* Parallel to section 050 but for officers. Essential for extending SPC framework to operational decisions. Without this provision, board-level social purpose authorization would be meaningless at the operational level where most decisions actually occur.

### L.8 RCW 23B.25.070 — Shares

*Annotation.* Technical housekeeping provision. Confirms that SPC shares may be certificated or uncertificated, consistent with general Title 23B framework.

### L.9 RCW 23B.25.080 — Instituting or maintaining proceedings

*Annotation.* Restricts SPC-related derivative claims to shareholders. Confirms procedurally what section 050 establishes substantively: the third-party bar. Consistent with general American pattern across benefit corporation statutes.

### L.10 RCW 23B.25.090 — Amendment to articles of incorporation

*Annotation.* Enhanced voting for material changes to social purposes. Two-thirds threshold, class-separate voting. "Materially change" qualifier excludes routine wordsmithing. Protects against casual mission drift while permitting responsive amendment with supermajority support.

### L.11 RCW 23B.25.100 — Plan of merger or share exchange

*Annotation.* Enhanced voting for mergers in which SPC does not survive. Two-thirds threshold, class-separate voting. Exception for SPC-to-SPC mergers with consistent purposes. Critical mission-protection provision preventing back-door mission abandonment through transaction structuring.

### L.12 RCW 23B.25.110 — Selling, leasing, exchanging, or disposing of property

*Annotation.* Enhanced voting for asset sales materially altering social purpose. Parallels section 100 for transactional mission abandonment. Together with sections 090 and 100, provides comprehensive protection against mission-destroying fundamental changes.

### L.13 RCW 23B.25.120 — Shareholder dissent

*Annotation.* Extends general dissenters' rights framework to SPC-specific fundamental changes. Provides exit option for shareholders who disagree with majority decisions meeting the two-thirds threshold.

### L.14 RCW 23B.25.130 — Conversion to social purpose corporation

*Annotation.* Procedural framework for existing corporations to elect SPC status. Plan of election requirement, board recommendation, two-thirds voting threshold (both aggregate and class-separate), effective date flexibility, continuation of existence.

### L.15 RCW 23B.25.140 — Conversion from social purpose corporation

*Annotation.* Parallel provision for SPC-to-standard-corporation conversion. Same procedural framework as section 130. Reversibility reflects policy choice favoring flexibility and founder autonomy over permanent mission lock.

### L.16 RCW 23B.25.150 — Social purpose report

*Annotation.* The chapter's principal ongoing accountability mechanism. Annual reporting within four months of fiscal year close, publicly accessible on principal website, enumerated content elements (permissive rather than mandatory), SEC alternative for public companies, summary court order enforcement after two consecutive years of noncompliance.

The weak enforcement mechanism is the chapter's most distinctive feature. Court-ordered specific performance of reporting obligations, delayed two years, with no damages remedy. This is the weakest enforcement among major U.S. benefit corporation regimes but is consistent with the chapter's broader flexibility-over-rigor orientation.

## Appendix N. A Note on Legislative Style

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation statute is, from a legislative drafting perspective, a well-constructed piece of work. It is compact — sixteen numbered sections — but substantively comprehensive within its chosen scope. It uses standard Washington corporate law terminology and integrates cleanly with the rest of Title 23B. It avoids unnecessary innovation where existing frameworks could be leveraged, and it introduces novel provisions only where the policy goals required them.

Several features of the drafting merit specific attention.

**Parallel structure.** Sections addressing parallel topics use parallel language. The director duty provision (section 050) and the officer duty provision (section 060) share the same architectural elements: reaffirmation of baseline duties, authorization to consider social purposes, safe harbor, third-party bar, and non-alteration clause. The voting threshold provisions for amendments (090), mergers (100), asset sales (110), and conversions in and out (130, 140) all use the same "two-thirds of each voting group" formulation. This parallelism makes the statute easier to read and interpret.

**Cross-referencing.** The statute makes extensive use of cross-references to other provisions of Title 23B. This technique leverages existing Washington corporate law rather than rewriting it. The resulting statute is shorter and more maintainable because it does not duplicate provisions that exist elsewhere.

**Permissive defaults with charter override.** The statute uses permissive defaults that can be modified by charter provisions. The "may consider" director duty, the permissive specific purposes, the optional third-party assessment — all can be strengthened by articles drafting. This design permits founders with strong mission commitments to get the rigor they want while not imposing unwanted rigor on founders with more flexible preferences.

**Definitional economy.** The statute avoids creating new defined terms where existing Title 23B terms suffice. It does not define "social purpose" beyond the section 020 enumeration, does not define "business judgment rule" or "best interests of the corporation," and does not create a free-standing glossary. This economy reflects the drafters' judgment that the existing Washington corporate law vocabulary is adequate to the SPC task.

**Technical stability.** The statute's minimal use of cutting-edge concepts — no "recognized third-party standard," no "benefit enforcement proceeding," no novel governance organs — means it is unlikely to be rendered obsolete by developments in benefit corporation theory or practice. A statute that commits itself to a specific third-party standard might face difficulties if that standard evolves or its provider ceases to exist. Washington's statute avoids this exposure by not committing to any specific external framework.

**Flexibility of application.** The statute applies to corporations of widely varying sizes and types without obvious awkwardness. A small Seattle coffee roaster with three employees and a large Tacoma manufacturer with hundreds of employees can both operate as SPCs under the same framework, customizing their compliance approaches through charter drafting and practice rather than through distinct statutory regimes.

These drafting virtues do not, of course, address the substantive policy questions about whether the statute's approach is correct. A well-drafted statute can embody a questionable policy choice, and a poorly drafted statute can embody a good one. But the technical quality of the Washington SPC statute does suggest that its drafters understood their craft and produced a statute that will age well regardless of whether its policy choices turn out to have been optimal.

## Appendix O. Concluding Observations

This paper has presented a comprehensive doctrinal, historical, and comparative analysis of Washington's Social Purpose Corporation framework. Several observations emerge from the analysis that are worth emphasizing in closing.

**First, Washington's SPC statute occupies a distinctive position in the landscape of American benefit corporation law.** It is not the dominant framework — that distinction belongs to the Delaware PBC and the B Lab model states — but it represents a coherent and defensible alternative approach. Washington chose flexibility over substantive duty intensity, permissive consideration over mandatory reorientation of fiduciary duty, and light-touch enforcement over aggressive judicial oversight. These choices have stood up well over more than a decade of operation.

**Second, the statute's stability is itself an important datum.** In more than fourteen years of operation, Chapter 23B.25 has not required substantive legislative amendment. This stability may reflect the quality of the original drafting, the limited political pressure for change, the relative success of the statute in serving its intended audience, or some combination. Whatever the cause, the absence of amendment activity suggests that Washington has found an equilibrium that works within its legal and political environment.

**Third, the absence of significant case law is worth noting.** More than fourteen years into the statute's operation, no published Washington appellate decision has substantively interpreted its key provisions. This interpretive vacuum has advantages (flexibility for practitioners, room for case-by-case judgment) and disadvantages (uncertainty about how courts will resolve specific questions when they arise). Practitioners should anticipate that the first significant Washington SPC case will generate important precedent, and they should be prepared to argue from statutory structure, analogous case law, and academic commentary rather than from direct precedent.

**Fourth, the Washington SPC is most valuable as part of a broader toolkit.** No single legal form can guarantee multi-generational mission persistence. Founders whose ambitions extend to century-scale organizational commitments should combine SPC status with additional mechanisms: voting trusts, purpose trusts, foundation ownership, enhanced charter provisions, shareholder agreements, and cultural practices that reinforce mission commitment. The SPC statute provides a useful foundation, but it is foundation rather than edifice.

**Fifth, the broader benefit corporation movement remains an ongoing experiment.** More than fifteen years after Maryland's first benefit corporation statute, the movement has produced roughly forty state adoptions in the United States and meaningful international diffusion. But the answers to the most important questions — whether these legal forms produce materially different corporate behavior, whether they support genuine multi-generational mission persistence, whether they represent a meaningful shift in corporate purpose orthodoxy — remain open. The next decade of operational experience will be critical in providing empirical grounding for normative judgments.

**Sixth, Washington's distinctive approach provides a useful comparative case study.** Because Washington diverges from the B Lab mainstream in significant ways, it offers a natural experiment in benefit corporation design. Comparing Washington outcomes to Maryland, Delaware, and California outcomes — when sufficient empirical data becomes available — will contribute to the broader understanding of which legislative design choices matter and which do not.

**Seventh, the statute's softness may be its greatest virtue.** Critics of Washington's "may consider" formulation often assume that harder duties produce stronger mission commitment. But this assumption is empirically questionable. The corporations that genuinely commit to mission do so for reasons internal to their founders and cultures, not primarily because of duty formulations. Legal frameworks provide permission and protection; they do not create commitment where none exists. Washington's soft framework provides adequate permission and protection for genuinely mission-committed organizations while imposing minimal burdens on organizations that would find stricter frameworks either confining or performative.

**Eighth, the statute's contribution should be measured in terms appropriate to its function.** Chapter 23B.25 is not designed to transform American corporate law, to solve the shareholder primacy problem, to guarantee mission persistence, or to revolutionize corporate behavior. It is designed, more modestly, to provide a legal home for mission-driven Washington corporations, clarifying the fiduciary duty landscape, signaling commitment through corporate name and articles, creating an annual reporting cycle that disciplines mission attention, and erecting procedural hurdles against casual mission abandonment. Measured against these modest goals, the statute has been moderately successful. Measured against grander ambitions, it would of course appear to fall short, but those grander ambitions were not the drafters' goals.

**Finally, the conversation continues.** Corporate law is a living body of rules shaped by scholarly commentary, practitioner experience, judicial interpretation, legislative activity, and cultural shifts. The Washington SPC statute is one contribution to this ongoing conversation. This paper is another. Future work by scholars, practitioners, founders, and policymakers will refine, extend, critique, and perhaps eventually supersede the analysis offered here. That is how legal scholarship functions: each contribution builds on what came before and creates the foundation for what comes next. The Washington Social Purpose Corporation will continue to evolve as new questions arise, new case law accumulates, and new scholarly work illuminates previously unconsidered dimensions of the framework.

The study of corporate purpose, of fiduciary duty, and of the legal forms that shape commercial activity is a venerable field with roots extending back to the earliest English joint-stock companies and forward into ongoing debates about ESG, stakeholder capitalism, and the proper role of business in society. Washington's Social Purpose Corporation is a small but interesting chapter in that broader story. Understanding it on its own terms — rather than through the lens of more dominant frameworks — enriches our understanding of the possibilities that corporate law reform can realize and the limits of what legislation alone can achieve.

## Appendix P. Extended Treatment of Director Fiduciary Duty Theory

The Washington SPC director duty provisions cannot be fully understood without situating them within the broader theoretical framework of director fiduciary duty as developed in American corporate law. This appendix provides an extended treatment of that framework, focusing on the aspects most relevant to benefit corporation analysis.

### P.1 The Trust Analogy and Its Limits

American director fiduciary duty has its origins in the trust law analogy. Early American corporate law, drawing on English common law, treated corporate directors as trustees of the corporate assets held for the benefit of shareholders as beneficiaries. The trust analogy produced a robust set of duties — loyalty, care, good faith, impartiality — that were imported wholesale into the corporate context.

The trust analogy, however, was never a perfect fit. Trust law presumes a relatively small number of identifiable beneficiaries whose interests are relatively straightforward to protect. Corporate law involves potentially thousands or millions of shareholders, whose interests are mediated through market mechanisms, share transfers, and aggregate voting. The trust beneficiary's right to enforce the trustee's duties translates awkwardly into the corporate shareholder's right to bring a derivative action.

By the mid-twentieth century, American corporate law had substantially evolved away from the pure trust analogy toward a more specifically corporate framework. The duty of care became distinctively applied to decisional contexts rather than custodial contexts. The duty of loyalty focused on conflicts of interest rather than on general fidelity to beneficiary welfare. The business judgment rule developed as a presumption of propriety that did not exist in trust law. Directors were not quite trustees, but they also were not quite anything else; they were sui generis fiduciaries governed by a body of law that borrowed from trust but differed from it in important respects.

The implications for benefit corporation analysis are significant. To the extent that shareholder primacy claims draw on the trust analogy — treating shareholders as the beneficiaries whose welfare directors must serve — benefit corporation statutes must either explicitly reject that analogy or explain why non-shareholder stakeholders should also be considered beneficiaries. Washington's SPC statute does neither explicitly. It simply authorizes consideration of non-shareholder interests and creates a safe harbor for mission-oriented decisions, leaving the underlying trust analogy questions unaddressed.

### P.2 The Contractarian Theory of the Corporation

The theoretical position that has dominated American corporate law scholarship since the 1970s is the contractarian view: the corporation as a nexus of contracts among various participants (shareholders, employees, creditors, suppliers, customers), with each participant's rights and obligations defined by the specific contractual arrangements in which they are engaged. On this view, shareholders are not beneficiaries of a trust but residual claimants who bear the risk of business failure and therefore are entitled to the residual profit of business success.

The contractarian view provides theoretical support for shareholder primacy in two ways. First, it treats the allocation of residual rights to shareholders as efficient: because shareholders bear the marginal risk of business outcomes, giving them the marginal reward creates appropriate incentives. Second, it treats non-shareholder stakeholders as protected through their own contracts: employees through employment agreements, creditors through debt covenants, suppliers through supply agreements, customers through consumer protection law. There is, on this view, no need for corporate law to protect non-shareholder interests because other legal mechanisms already do so.

Benefit corporation statutes challenge the contractarian view, but in a subtle way. They do not claim that non-shareholders need additional legal protection beyond what their own contracts provide. Instead, they claim that shareholders can collectively choose (through the election of benefit corporation status) to commit themselves to a governance structure that considers non-shareholder interests. The commitment runs from shareholders to the corporation, not from the corporation to non-shareholders. Benefit corporation directors owe duties to the corporation (and, derivatively, to shareholders), not to the non-shareholder stakeholders they are authorized or required to consider.

This subtle framing preserves the contractarian structure while enabling benefit corporation governance. Shareholders contract into a benefit corporation structure by investing; directors fulfill their duties by operating within that structure; non-shareholder stakeholders receive indirect benefits without acquiring legal standing. The benefit corporation is, in a sense, a contractarian innovation: a new category of corporate contract that shareholders can opt into, with different internal governance rules, but without creating new legal rights for anyone outside the shareholder-director relationship.

### P.3 The Team Production Theory

An alternative theoretical position, most fully developed by Margaret Blair and Lynn Stout in their 1999 article "A Team Production Theory of Corporate Law," rejects the shareholder-primacy contractarian view in favor of a team production theory of the corporation. On this view, the corporation is a joint enterprise in which multiple parties contribute specific assets — shareholders contribute capital, employees contribute labor, managers contribute expertise, customers contribute loyalty, communities contribute social infrastructure — and the corporate law framework serves to coordinate these contributions efficiently.

The team production theory provides stronger theoretical support for stakeholder governance than the contractarian view. If the corporation is a team production enterprise, then directors are the team mediators whose job is to balance the competing interests of team members and sustain the joint enterprise for the long run. Shareholder primacy becomes an arbitrary privileging of one team member over others, with no compelling theoretical justification.

Benefit corporation statutes are more naturally at home in the team production framework than in the contractarian framework. They authorize directors to consider the interests of multiple team members rather than solely the residual claimant. They create reporting obligations that hold the corporation accountable to multiple constituencies rather than only to shareholders. They protect directors from liability for balancing competing team member interests. Team production theory provides intellectual support for exactly the kind of governance that benefit corporation statutes try to enable.

Washington's SPC statute, with its permissive "may consider" framework, sits somewhat awkwardly in both theoretical frameworks. It preserves the contractarian structure (duties run to the corporation and shareholders, not to non-shareholders) but enables team production governance (directors may consider non-shareholder team members). This compromise may be theoretically inelegant but is practically workable: it gives mission-committed founders what they need while avoiding the theoretical arguments that have bogged down benefit corporation discourse in other jurisdictions.

### P.4 The Team Production Theory and "Mediating Hierarchies"

Blair and Stout extended their team production argument by proposing that corporate directors serve as "mediating hierarchies" whose role is to balance the claims of competing team members in ways that sustain the joint enterprise. This formulation provides a specific theoretical grounding for director discretion: directors need discretion precisely because they are mediating between competing claims, and any mechanical rule that favors one claimant over others would undermine their mediating function.

The Delaware PBC's balancing duty is the clearest American statutory expression of the mediating hierarchy idea. By requiring directors to balance stockholder pecuniary interests, the best interests of those materially affected, and the specified public benefit, Delaware's statute explicitly adopts the mediating hierarchy model. Directors are not servants of shareholders; they are mediators of a multi-party production enterprise.

Washington's SPC statute can be read as implicitly adopting the mediating hierarchy model, though less explicitly than Delaware. The "may consider" language, combined with the safe harbor, gives directors discretion to balance competing interests. The three-category enumeration of social purposes identifies the team members whose interests can be considered. The annual reporting obligation makes the mediating function visible to all team members. Taken together, these features are consistent with a team production view of the corporation, even though the Washington drafters did not invoke team production theory explicitly.

### P.5 The Dogmatic vs. Pragmatic Split

A recurring theme in benefit corporation scholarship is the split between dogmatic and pragmatic approaches. Dogmatic approaches treat benefit corporation legislation as a theoretical statement about the nature of the corporation: an affirmation that corporations are not merely shareholder wealth maximization machines but social institutions with multiple stakeholders. Dogmatic approaches tend to favor stronger duty formulations, mandatory third-party standards, aggressive enforcement mechanisms, and permanent mission locks.

Pragmatic approaches treat benefit corporation legislation as a practical tool for specific purposes: providing legal clarity for mission-driven founders, signaling commitment to customers and investors, creating ongoing accountability through reporting, and erecting procedural hurdles against mission abandonment. Pragmatic approaches tend to favor permissive duty formulations, flexible reporting requirements, light-touch enforcement, and reversible status.

Washington's SPC statute is firmly on the pragmatic side of this split. It does not make strong theoretical claims about corporate purpose. It does not mandate third-party verification. It does not create aggressive enforcement mechanisms. It does not impose permanent mission lock. It provides practical tools for practical purposes, leaving the theoretical questions open.

The dogmatic/pragmatic distinction has consequences for how we evaluate different benefit corporation regimes. A dogmatic evaluator will criticize Washington's statute as insufficiently committed to the theoretical premises of benefit corporation legislation. A pragmatic evaluator will praise it as appropriately calibrated to the practical needs of mission-driven founders without overreach. Neither evaluation is obviously correct; they reflect different views about what benefit corporation legislation is for.

### P.6 Path Dependency in American Corporate Law

Any discussion of benefit corporation statutes must acknowledge the significant path dependency that shapes American corporate law. Delaware dominates as the preferred domicile for publicly held companies and venture-backed private companies, not because of any inherent superiority but because of historical accumulation of case law, experienced practitioners, specialized courts, and established expectations. States that want to influence corporate behavior must do so either by attracting incorporations away from Delaware (difficult) or by shaping their own corporate law in ways that will influence corporations domiciled elsewhere but operating in their territory (limited in effect).

Washington's SPC statute operates under both constraints. It cannot realistically compete with Delaware for the incorporations of large or growth-oriented companies; Delaware has too many structural advantages. But it can serve Washington-based businesses that prefer Washington domicile for local reasons (proximity, familiarity, community ties) and whose missions align with the SPC framework. The Washington SPC is therefore a regional product: it serves Washington founders who want Washington domicile, not a national product competing with Delaware for a general market.

The path dependency also affects how the Washington statute will evolve. Because the population of Washington SPCs is relatively small, the statute is unlikely to generate the volume of case law that would drive rapid doctrinal development. Washington SPC jurisprudence will develop slowly, probably through persuasive citation to Delaware PBC decisions and academic commentary rather than through independent Washington precedent. This slow pace of doctrinal development is a function of the statute's position in the American corporate law landscape rather than any inherent defect in the statute itself.

This appendix provides a practical checklist that practitioners can use when advising clients considering Washington SPC formation or conversion. The checklist is not legal advice and does not substitute for case-specific analysis; it is offered as a structural guide for the decision-making process.

### Q.1 Formation Decision Checklist

**Step 1: Assess founder mission commitment.**
- [ ] Is the founder team genuinely committed to a social or environmental mission, or is mission language being considered primarily for marketing purposes?
- [ ] Can the founders articulate their mission in specific terms that would make sense in a charter document and an annual report?
- [ ] Are the founders prepared to maintain the mission commitment across multiple years of ongoing operation and reporting?
- [ ] Is the mission compatible with the business model — i.e., can the business be operated profitably while pursuing the mission, or is there a fundamental tension?

**Step 2: Assess structural fit.**
- [ ] Does the proposed business fit within the three-category framework of RCW 23B.25.020 (employees/suppliers/customers, community, environment)?
- [ ] Can the business's mission be articulated as either promoting positive effects or minimizing adverse effects?
- [ ] Will the mission statement be general (relying on section 020 alone) or specific (invoking section 030)?
- [ ] Are there constituencies or purposes that fall outside the three categories that the founders wish to emphasize?

**Step 3: Assess capital structure implications.**
- [ ] Is the capital structure single-class or multi-class?
- [ ] How will the two-thirds voting threshold for fundamental changes interact with the capital structure?
- [ ] Are there minority investors or dissenting shareholders whose rights need to be protected?
- [ ] Will founder control be maintained through voting rights, voting trusts, or other mechanisms?

**Step 4: Assess reporting capacity.**
- [ ] Does the corporation have the internal capacity to produce an annual Social Purpose Report within the four-month deadline?
- [ ] Who will be responsible for drafting the report?
- [ ] What data will the corporation collect and report on?
- [ ] Will the corporation use an external framework (GRI, SASB, B Impact Assessment) voluntarily?
- [ ] Is there a principal website where the report can be published?

**Step 5: Assess investor communication needs.**
- [ ] Do prospective investors understand and accept SPC status?
- [ ] Are investor pitch materials aligned with the SPC mission statement?
- [ ] Will investor term sheets or shareholder agreements include SPC-related provisions?
- [ ] Has the mandatory investor-protection disclosure been explained to investors?

**Step 6: Assess alternatives.**
- [ ] Has the client considered alternatives to Washington SPC: Delaware PBC, ordinary Washington corporation with charter provisions, B Lab certification without legal form change, LLC with operating agreement provisions?
- [ ] What factors favor the Washington SPC over these alternatives?
- [ ] Is there a strategic reason for choosing Washington SPC specifically?

**Step 7: Draft articles of incorporation.**
- [ ] Include mandatory content: SPC designation, general purpose statement, investor-protection disclosure.
- [ ] Consider optional content: specific purpose statement, enhanced director duty, third-party assessment requirement, enhanced voting thresholds.
- [ ] Include provisions consistent with the corporation's intended capital structure.
- [ ] Include provisions for governance (board composition, committee structure, etc.) consistent with mission commitments.

**Step 8: Prepare ancillary documents.**
- [ ] Board resolutions adopting social purpose policies.
- [ ] Shareholder agreement (if any) with SPC-specific provisions.
- [ ] Employee handbook with mission-related content.
- [ ] Investor disclosure materials addressing SPC status.
- [ ] Website content introducing the SPC commitment.

**Step 9: File and commence operations.**
- [ ] File articles of incorporation with the Washington Secretary of State.
- [ ] Pay required filing fee.
- [ ] Establish recurring calendar reminders for Social Purpose Report preparation and publication.
- [ ] Begin collecting data that will populate the first annual report.

### Q.2 Conversion Decision Checklist

For an existing Washington corporation considering conversion to SPC status under RCW 23B.25.130:

**Step 1: Assess board readiness.**
- [ ] Is the board of directors informed about SPC status and its implications?
- [ ] Has the board discussed the conversion proposal in multiple meetings?
- [ ] Is the board prepared to recommend the conversion to shareholders?
- [ ] Are there directors with conflicts of interest that would prevent board recommendation?

**Step 2: Assess shareholder support.**
- [ ] What is the current shareholder composition?
- [ ] Can the two-thirds voting threshold be met (both aggregate and class-separate)?
- [ ] Are there dissenting shareholders likely to invoke dissenters' rights?
- [ ] What is the estimated cost of dissenters' rights proceedings?

**Step 3: Draft plan of election.**
- [ ] Include mandatory content: equal treatment of shares within classes, articles amendments to satisfy RCW 23B.25.040(1).
- [ ] Specify effective date.
- [ ] Include any conditions to effectiveness.
- [ ] Address treatment of outstanding equity compensation, convertible securities, etc.

**Step 4: Prepare shareholder materials.**
- [ ] Proxy statement or information statement describing the conversion.
- [ ] Description of dissenters' rights and procedures.
- [ ] Copy of proposed amended articles of incorporation.
- [ ] Explanation of SPC implications for shareholders.

**Step 5: Hold shareholder vote.**
- [ ] Send notice meeting Washington statutory requirements.
- [ ] Conduct meeting in compliance with Title 23B procedural requirements.
- [ ] Tabulate votes accurately, including class-separate voting.
- [ ] Document any dissenters' demand for fair value.

**Step 6: Complete conversion.**
- [ ] File amended articles of incorporation with the Secretary of State.
- [ ] Update name if required.
- [ ] Update corporate records, board resolutions, and policies.
- [ ] Begin operating under SPC framework.
- [ ] Establish calendar for first Social Purpose Report.

### Q.3 Ongoing Compliance Checklist

For an established Washington SPC, the ongoing compliance obligations include:

**Annual obligations:**
- [ ] File annual report with Washington Secretary of State (general Title 23B requirement).
- [ ] Prepare Social Purpose Report covering the fiscal year.
- [ ] Publish Social Purpose Report on principal website within four months of fiscal year close.
- [ ] Retain Social Purpose Report in archive (recommended beyond statutory requirement).
- [ ] Board review of social purpose performance at least annually.

**Transactional obligations:**
- [ ] For any proposed amendment to social purposes: obtain two-thirds vote of each voting group.
- [ ] For any proposed merger in which the SPC does not survive: obtain two-thirds vote, absent SPC-to-SPC exception.
- [ ] For any proposed asset sale materially altering social purpose: obtain two-thirds vote.
- [ ] For any proposed conversion out of SPC status: obtain two-thirds vote.
- [ ] For each of these actions: provide dissenters' rights notice and handle dissenting shareholders.

**Routine governance:**
- [ ] Board meetings should include social purpose agenda items.
- [ ] Board minutes should document social purpose considerations in material decisions.
- [ ] Officers should document social purpose analysis in material operational decisions.
- [ ] Employee communications should reflect mission commitment.
- [ ] Investor communications should address mission performance.

**Documentary maintenance:**
- [ ] Articles of incorporation current and reflecting all amendments.
- [ ] Bylaws consistent with SPC framework.
- [ ] Corporate records book including social purpose policy documents.
- [ ] Historical archive of Social Purpose Reports.
- [ ] Board resolutions adopting and revising social purpose policies.

### Q.4 Exit Decision Checklist

If an SPC is considering conversion out of SPC status under RCW 23B.25.140:

**Step 1: Assess reasons for conversion.**
- [ ] Why is conversion being considered?
- [ ] Is the mission no longer viable in the SPC form?
- [ ] Are financial or market pressures driving the decision?
- [ ] Has the board considered alternatives to conversion?
- [ ] Is conversion being considered as part of a transaction (e.g., preparation for acquisition)?

**Step 2: Assess shareholder and community response.**
- [ ] How will the shareholder base respond to the conversion proposal?
- [ ] Can the two-thirds voting threshold be met?
- [ ] How will employees, customers, community members, and other stakeholders respond?
- [ ] What is the reputational cost of conversion?

**Step 3: Assess legal and procedural requirements.**
- [ ] Draft plan of election meeting section 140 requirements.
- [ ] Prepare shareholder materials.
- [ ] Conduct shareholder vote with two-thirds threshold (aggregate and class-separate).
- [ ] Handle dissenters' rights claims.
- [ ] File amended articles removing SPC designation.

**Step 4: Communicate transition.**
- [ ] Communicate with shareholders about post-conversion governance.
- [ ] Communicate with employees about continuing or changed mission commitments.
- [ ] Communicate with customers about the reasons for conversion.
- [ ] Communicate publicly (if appropriate) about the transition.
- [ ] Update corporate materials, website, and branding.

This checklist is not exhaustive and should be adapted to specific circumstances. Clients considering any of these actions should consult Washington corporate counsel familiar with their specific situation.

## Appendix R. A Comparative Glossary of Benefit Corporation Terminology

Benefit corporation law uses a specialized vocabulary that varies across jurisdictions and can be confusing to practitioners new to the field. This appendix provides a comparative glossary of key terms, with notes on variations in usage.

**Asset lock.** A legal restriction on the distribution of corporate assets for less than market value, or to non-mission-aligned recipients. Characteristic of UK CICs. Not present in Washington SPCs or most U.S. benefit corporations.

**B Corp / Certified B Corporation.** A certification mark awarded by B Lab to companies that achieve a minimum score on the B Impact Assessment. Contractual and reputational rather than legal; does not by itself change corporate form or governance.

**B Impact Assessment.** B Lab's proprietary assessment tool, used both for B Corp certification and as a "recognized third-party standard" under B Lab model benefit corporation legislation.

**B Lab.** The nonprofit organization founded in 2006 that developed the B Corp certification, drafted the model benefit corporation legislation, and operates the B Impact Assessment.

**Benefit corporation.** A for-profit corporation incorporated under state legislation based on the B Lab model, committed to pursuing general public benefit and subject to enhanced director consideration duties and annual benefit reporting. Used in states including Maryland, California, New York, Vermont, and others.

**Benefit enforcement proceeding.** A cause of action created by B Lab model legislation allowing shareholders, directors, and specified ownership blocs to sue for breach of benefit corporation duties. Not present in Washington or Delaware frameworks.

**Benefit report.** The annual disclosure document required by B Lab model legislation, including assessment of corporate performance against a third-party standard.

**Community interest company / CIC.** The UK's mission-locked corporate form created in 2005, featuring an asset lock, dividend cap, and dedicated regulator.

**Flexible purpose corporation / FPC.** California's original 2011 mission-driven corporate form, renamed "Social Purpose Corporation" in 2014. Template for Washington's SPC statute.

**General public benefit.** The B Lab model's formulation of benefit corporation purpose, defined as "a material positive impact on society and the environment, taken as a whole, as assessed against a third-party standard, from the business and operations of a benefit corporation."

**L3C.** Low-profit limited liability company. A Vermont-origin form designed to facilitate program-related investments by private foundations. Distinct from benefit corporations.

**Mission lock.** A legal restriction preventing the abandonment of a corporation's mission. Ranges from weak (reversible through supermajority vote, as in Washington SPCs) to strong (irrevocable, as in UK CICs).

**Pecuniary interest.** Shareholder financial interests. Used in Delaware PBC framework to distinguish shareholder wealth considerations from other interests directors must balance.

**Public benefit corporation / PBC.** Delaware's mission-driven corporate form created in 2013, featuring a balancing duty and biennial reporting to stockholders.

**Purpose trust.** A trust with a non-charitable purpose (rather than a charitable purpose or identifiable beneficiaries). Used in some mission-protection structures, including Patagonia's 2022 restructuring.

**Raison d'être.** French for "reason for being." Used in France's PACTE law framework to describe the fundamental purpose of a corporation, particularly a Société à Mission.

**Recognized third-party standard.** A standard developed by an independent organization for assessing corporate social and environmental performance. Required by B Lab model legislation for benefit report content. Not required by Washington or Delaware.

**Revlon duty.** The Delaware-origin duty of directors to obtain the best price for shareholders when a sale of the corporation becomes inevitable. Modified or overridden by benefit corporation statutes in various ways.

**Safe harbor.** A statutory provision that immunizes directors from liability for certain actions. The Washington SPC safe harbor protects good-faith actions reasonably believed to promote social purposes.

**Shareholder primacy.** The view that the corporation exists to maximize shareholder wealth, and that directors' duty is to pursue shareholder interests above all others. Contested as a legal rule but dominant as a folk belief.

**Social purpose.** The Washington SPC statute's term for the benefit corporation purposes identified in RCW 23B.25.020. Also used in California's post-2015 SPC framework and other jurisdictions adopting the California/Washington template.

**Social purpose corporation / SPC.** Washington's (and post-2015 California's) mission-driven corporate form. Features permissive director consideration, annual reporting, and enhanced voting thresholds without third-party standard requirements.

**Società Benefit.** Italy's benefit corporation form, enacted 2016. Closer to B Lab model than Washington framework.

**Société à Mission.** France's benefit corporation form, enacted 2019 under PACTE law. Features raison d'être articulation, mission committee governance, and independent verification.

**Specific public benefit / specific social purpose.** A particular mission objective beyond the general benefit or social purpose framework. Optional in Washington but mandatory in Delaware.

**Stakeholder capitalism.** The view that corporations should consider the interests of multiple stakeholders (employees, customers, suppliers, communities, environment) rather than solely shareholders. Related to but distinct from benefit corporation law.

**Stiftung.** German for "foundation." Used in the "Stiftung model" of industrial governance, in which a charitable foundation holds controlling interest in an operating company.

**Team production theory.** The theoretical view (Blair and Stout) that the corporation is a joint enterprise in which multiple parties contribute specific assets, with directors as mediating hierarchies balancing competing claims.

**Third-party bar.** A statutory provision restricting standing to sue under benefit corporation legislation to shareholders (and sometimes directors and specified ownership blocs), excluding non-shareholder stakeholders. Present in all major U.S. benefit corporation frameworks.

## Appendix S. A Note on Legal Ethics Considerations

Corporate lawyers advising clients on Washington SPC formation and operation face several legal ethics considerations that deserve explicit mention.

### S.1 Identifying the Client

Under Washington Rule of Professional Conduct 1.13, a lawyer representing an organization represents the organization acting through its authorized representatives (directors, officers), not the individual constituents of the organization. This rule applies to SPCs as to any other corporation.

But the SPC framework introduces some potential complications. The SPC's stated social purpose identifies stakeholder interests that the corporation is committed to considering. Does this create a duty on corporate counsel to consider those stakeholder interests in advising the corporation? The most natural reading is no: counsel's duties run to the organization as an entity, not to the stakeholders the organization is committed to considering. The stakeholder consideration duty is a substantive duty of the directors, not a professional responsibility duty of counsel.

However, sophisticated corporate counsel will recognize that their advice to SPC clients should reflect the client's mission commitments. Advice that treats the SPC as if it were an ordinary shareholder-primacy corporation may not serve the client's actual interests. Counsel should ensure that they understand the client's mission commitments and advise in ways that respect those commitments.

### S.2 Conflict Between Mission and Profit

A recurring situation in SPC practice is the potential conflict between mission objectives and profit maximization. When counseling an SPC client facing such a conflict, what is counsel's appropriate role?

The general principle is that counsel's role is to provide legal advice, not to make business decisions. Counsel can and should explain the legal framework — the safe harbor, the reporting obligations, the enhanced voting thresholds — but the ultimate decision about how to balance mission and profit is a board decision that counsel should inform but not make.

If counsel believes that a proposed action is inconsistent with the corporation's stated social purpose, counsel may (and arguably should) express that view to the board. But counsel should not refuse to implement board decisions that are within the board's legal authority, even if counsel disagrees with the business judgment involved.

### S.3 Disclosure in Investor Communications

The Washington SPC statute requires specific investor-protection disclosure in the articles of incorporation (the "mission is not necessarily compatible with profit maximization" language). Counsel advising SPC clients on investor communications should ensure that this statutory disclosure is preserved and reinforced in materials provided to prospective investors.

Counsel should also be alert to securities law implications of SPC status. In Rule 506 offerings, for instance, the SPC's social purpose commitments should be disclosed to investors as material information about the investment. In public offerings (which are rare for SPCs), the SPC's mission framework would be subject to SEC review and disclosure rules.

### S.4 Dealing With Mission Abandonment

If an SPC client considers converting out of SPC status (under section 140), counsel's role is to provide legal advice on the conversion process, including the voting thresholds, dissenters' rights, and procedural requirements. Counsel should not be in the position of advocating for or against the conversion on business grounds; that is a board decision.

However, counsel should ensure that the board understands the implications of conversion for shareholders who originally invested based on the SPC mission. The mandatory investor-protection disclosure gave shareholders notice that the mission might conflict with profit; it did not give them notice that the mission could be abandoned entirely. Shareholders who believed they were investing in a perpetual mission commitment may have legitimate grievances if the mission is abandoned, and counsel should help the board anticipate and address those grievances.

### S.5 The Ethics of "Mission-Aligned" Lawyering

Some lawyers who specialize in benefit corporation practice have developed a distinctive approach that treats the lawyer as something more than a neutral legal advisor — as a collaborator in the client's mission, a partner in mission-preservation, or an advocate for stakeholder interests within the attorney-client relationship. This approach can be valuable for clients who want lawyers who share their mission commitments, but it raises ethical considerations.

The general principle is that the attorney-client relationship is what the client and lawyer agree it is, subject to the minimum requirements of the rules of professional conduct. A client who hires a lawyer specifically because the lawyer shares mission values, and who asks the lawyer to advise with those values in mind, can create an engagement that goes beyond the narrow "neutral legal advisor" model. This is consistent with the general principle of client autonomy in shaping the attorney-client relationship.

However, the lawyer must not allow the values-alignment approach to compromise the lawyer's obligations to the organization as client (not to individual stakeholders), to maintain confidentiality, to avoid conflicts of interest, or to provide competent advice. A lawyer who refused to advise an SPC client on a legal action the client wanted to take because the lawyer personally disagreed with the action would be violating professional responsibility duties. A lawyer who advised an SPC client on available options, including options the lawyer personally disapproved of, while also expressing views about the mission implications, would be appropriately combining advocacy for mission values with respect for client autonomy.

## Appendix T. The Washington SPC in the Broader Legal Landscape

Chapter 23B.25 does not exist in isolation. It operates alongside numerous other Washington statutes and regulatory frameworks that affect mission-driven businesses. This appendix briefly identifies the most important adjacent legal frameworks and their interaction with the SPC structure.

### T.1 Washington Nonprofit Corporation Act (Chapter 24.03A RCW)

Washington's nonprofit corporation law provides an alternative structure for mission-driven organizations. Nonprofits have several advantages over SPCs for purely charitable missions: tax-exempt status under IRC § 501(c)(3), eligibility for charitable contributions, and stronger asset dedication to mission.

Nonprofits also have significant limitations. They cannot distribute profits to members or directors. They cannot raise equity capital. They are subject to Attorney General oversight. And they must conduct activities consistent with their tax-exempt purposes, limiting the commercial flexibility that SPCs enjoy.

The choice between nonprofit and SPC depends on the corporation's fundamental orientation. A mission-dominant organization that does not need to distribute profits is probably better served by nonprofit status. A mission-committed organization that also needs to raise equity capital and generate returns for investors is better served by SPC status.

### T.2 Washington Limited Liability Companies (Chapter 25.15 RCW)

Washington's LLC act permits LLCs to be formed for any lawful purpose, without prescriptive governance requirements. An LLC can embed mission commitments in its operating agreement, creating a contractually enforceable mission structure that is in some ways more flexible than the SPC framework.

LLCs cannot, however, elect SPC status under Chapter 23B.25, because the SPC framework is available only to corporations governed by Title 23B. An LLC seeking SPC-like governance would need to convert to a Washington corporation first (under RCW 23B.09) and then elect SPC status.

The trade-offs between LLC and SPC depend on multiple factors: tax treatment (LLCs are pass-through by default, SPCs are C corporations), governance flexibility (LLCs are more flexible), capital structure (LLCs can have complex ownership structures), exit strategy (corporations are more standard for acquisition or IPO), and liability protection (both provide similar liability protection to owners).

### T.3 Washington Securities Law

SPC offerings are subject to federal and Washington state securities law. The federal securities regime (Securities Act, Exchange Act, Investment Company Act) applies to SPCs on the same terms as other corporations. Washington's state securities law (Chapter 21.20 RCW) similarly applies without SPC-specific modifications.

The mandatory investor-protection disclosure in SPC articles provides some substantive content for securities law disclosure obligations, but does not fulfill them entirely. SPCs raising capital through securities offerings must still comply with registration or exemption requirements, anti-fraud provisions, and specific disclosure obligations.

### T.4 Washington Employment Law

SPCs that include "employees" within their social purpose are subject to Washington employment law on the same terms as other employers. Washington's Minimum Wage Act, Industrial Welfare Act, Family Leave Act, and other employment statutes apply to SPCs without modification.

The SPC framework does not create additional employee rights or claims. An SPC's stated commitment to "promoting positive effects on employees" does not give employees a cause of action against the corporation for failure to deliver on that commitment; enforcement remains within the shareholder-only channel of section 050.

### T.5 Washington Environmental Law

SPCs that include "environment" within their social purpose are subject to Washington environmental law (SEPA, Clean Air Act, Water Quality Standards, etc.) on the same terms as other regulated entities. The SPC framework does not provide any exemption from or modification of environmental regulation.

Conversely, the SPC framework does not make the corporation subject to any environmental obligations it would not otherwise have. An SPC's commitment to minimizing environmental harm is a corporate governance commitment, not a regulatory commitment. Environmental regulation is established through specific environmental statutes, not through general corporate law.

### T.6 Federal Tax Law

Washington SPCs are generally subject to federal income tax as C corporations, on the same terms as ordinary Washington corporations. The SPC framework does not provide any special federal tax treatment.

Certain specific tax considerations may apply. An SPC that makes charitable contributions in furtherance of its social purpose may be able to deduct those contributions under IRC § 170, subject to the general limitations on corporate charitable deductions. An SPC that receives a program-related investment from a private foundation may need to satisfy the PRI requirements of IRC § 4944(c). An SPC that is owned by a tax-exempt foundation may need to consider unrelated business income tax implications.

None of these tax considerations is specific to the SPC framework; they are general tax principles that apply to any corporation with the relevant characteristics. Counsel advising SPC clients on tax matters should rely on general corporate and tax law expertise, not on SPC-specific tax authorities (which do not exist in any significant form).

### T.7 Federal Contracting and Procurement

Some federal contracts and procurement programs provide preferences for "socially and economically disadvantaged" businesses, "small businesses," or other specified categories. SPC status does not, in itself, qualify a corporation for any of these preferences, though an SPC may independently qualify based on other characteristics (size, ownership, business type).

Federal procurement preferences are defined by specific statutes (Small Business Act, 15 U.S.C. § 637; federal acquisition regulations) and do not map directly onto benefit corporation frameworks. A Washington SPC seeking federal procurement preferences should analyze its qualifications under the applicable federal rules independently of its SPC status.

### T.8 State and Local Procurement

Some Washington state agencies and local governments have adopted procurement preferences for businesses meeting specified social or environmental criteria. SPC status may be relevant to these preferences but is typically not sufficient by itself. Procurement preferences usually require specific qualification standards (minority ownership, veteran ownership, environmental certification, etc.) that are defined independently of corporate form.

### T.9 Interactions With Other Benefit Corporation Frameworks

A Washington SPC operating in multiple states may face questions about its status in other jurisdictions. Under general principles of state corporate law, a corporation's internal affairs are governed by the law of its state of incorporation. A Washington SPC's SPC status is therefore preserved when it operates in other states through qualification as a foreign corporation.

However, the other state's courts will not necessarily recognize Washington SPC duties as creating special director protections. A Washington SPC sued in California, for example, might find that the California court applied California's own corporate law to questions arising from the SPC's California operations. The applicable law analysis would depend on the specific claim and the specific state's choice-of-law rules.

Practitioners advising Washington SPCs with multi-state operations should be aware of these potential complications and should consider whether additional structural measures (such as foreign qualification in all relevant states, or incorporation in a different jurisdiction) would better serve the client's needs.

## Appendix U. Final Observations and Acknowledgments

This paper has attempted, across its main text and appendices, to provide a comprehensive treatment of the Washington Social Purpose Corporation statute. The treatment is necessarily partial — no single document can address every interpretive question, every empirical uncertainty, or every comparative angle — but the aim has been to provide a foundation that practitioners, scholars, and interested observers can build on.

Several themes recur throughout the paper and deserve emphasis in closing.

**The importance of context.** Chapter 23B.25 cannot be understood in isolation. It operates within the broader framework of Washington corporate law, within the broader landscape of American benefit corporation legislation, within the international context of mission-driven corporate forms, and within the ongoing intellectual debate about corporate purpose. Each of these contexts shapes how the statute functions in practice, and removing the statute from its contexts would produce a distorted understanding.

**The primacy of drafting choices.** The Washington SPC statute embodies a series of deliberate drafting choices that differ from the choices made by drafters in other jurisdictions. The "may consider" duty formulation, the three-category purpose enumeration, the investor-protection disclosure, the permissive report content requirements, the minimal enforcement mechanism — each of these reflects a specific judgment by the WSBA Business Law Section drafters about how to balance flexibility and rigor, mission commitment and founder autonomy, substantive reform and procedural adjustment. These choices are defensible, and the resulting statute is coherent, but they produce a statute that is meaningfully different from its alternatives.

**The role of private ordering.** Washington's SPC statute provides a baseline that founders can strengthen through charter drafting. The optional provisions of section 040(2) — enhanced director duties, third-party assessment requirements, enhanced voting thresholds, duration limits — allow founders who want a stronger framework to get one without requiring the statute itself to impose that framework on all SPCs. This architectural choice reflects a broader preference for enabling private ordering over imposing mandatory substantive rules.

**The limits of legal form.** No legal form, however carefully drafted, can guarantee mission commitment in the absence of sustained organizational will. The Washington SPC statute, like its alternatives, provides tools for mission-committed organizations. It does not create mission commitment where none exists, and it cannot preserve mission commitment against determined abandonment. The statute is scaffolding for builders, not a finished structure.

**The ongoing conversation.** Corporate law is not a static body of rules but a living field shaped by scholarship, practice, judicial interpretation, legislative activity, and cultural shifts. The Washington SPC statute will evolve as these forces act on it, even if the statutory text itself remains stable. Future scholarship will refine the interpretive questions this paper has identified, future case law will provide authoritative answers to some of those questions, future practitioner experience will accumulate into bodies of informal guidance, and future cultural shifts will reshape the expectations that investors, customers, employees, and communities bring to mission-driven businesses.

**The value of comparative analysis.** Understanding Washington's distinctive approach requires comparison with alternatives. The B Lab model, the Delaware PBC framework, the California post-2015 SPC, the UK CIC, the French Société à Mission, the Italian Società Benefit, the British Columbia Benefit Company, and the German Stiftung tradition all provide useful comparison points. Each embodies different theoretical commitments, different practical trade-offs, and different answers to the fundamental questions of corporate purpose. Washington's approach is one answer among several; it is neither uniquely right nor uniquely wrong, but distinctive in identifiable ways.

**The importance of empirical research.** Much of the commentary on benefit corporation statutes relies on theoretical analysis and anecdotal evidence. Meaningful empirical research on actual corporate behavior, comparative outcomes, and measurable impacts is still relatively limited. The research agenda identified in Appendix G and Appendix M represents an attempt to frame questions that empirical research could productively address. Filling in the empirical picture would substantially strengthen the evidentiary foundation for normative judgments about benefit corporation design.

**Acknowledgment of limitations.** This paper has significant limitations. It relies primarily on publicly available materials; it has not conducted original empirical research; it has not canvassed unpublished practitioner experience or confidential client advice; and it reflects the perspective of its drafter rather than the collective judgment of a working group or committee. Readers should treat the paper as a contribution to the conversation, not as a definitive treatment, and should supplement it with their own research, their own experience, and their own critical judgment.

**Invitation for continued work.** The Washington SPC statute deserves more scholarly attention than it has received. Compared to the Delaware PBC (which has generated substantial academic commentary) and the B Lab model states (which have been the subject of detailed studies), Washington's distinctive approach has been relatively neglected. This paper hopes to contribute to the conversation and to invite further work by others. Questions about the "may consider" duty, about the safe harbor, about the reporting mechanism, about the comparative effectiveness of softer versus stronger regimes, about the long-term prospects for mission persistence — all these questions deserve continued attention.

The Washington Social Purpose Corporation is a modest but significant contribution to the corporate law toolkit available to mission-driven founders. Its approach may not suit every purpose, and its choices may not satisfy every critic, but it represents a thoughtful and coherent response to the specific needs of mission-driven businesses in the Pacific Northwest. Understanding it on its own terms, and in its proper context, enriches our collective understanding of what corporate law can and cannot achieve in service of mission-oriented enterprise.

## Appendix V. A Technical Note on Citation

Throughout this paper, statutory provisions are cited in the standard Washington format: "RCW" followed by the title, chapter, and section numbers separated by periods. Thus "RCW 23B.25.050" refers to Title 23B (Washington Business Corporation Act), Chapter 25 (Social Purpose Corporations), Section 050 (Duties of Director).

Delaware statutory provisions are cited in the standard Delaware format: "8 Del. C. §" followed by the section number. Thus "8 Del. C. § 365" refers to Title 8 of the Delaware Code (Corporations), Section 365.

International statutes are cited using conventions appropriate to the jurisdiction. UK statutes are cited by short title and year. French and Italian statutes are cited by law number and date. Canadian statutes are cited by short title, year, and chapter number. Readers unfamiliar with these conventions should consult standard legal citation references (The Bluebook, The ALWD Citation Manual, or jurisdiction-specific style guides) for detailed guidance.

Case law is cited in the standard American format: case name, reporter volume, reporter name, first page, deciding court abbreviation, and year. Thus "Dodge v. Ford Motor Co., 170 N.W. 668 (Mich. 1919)" refers to a Michigan Supreme Court decision published in volume 170 of the North Western Reporter, beginning at page 668, decided in 1919.

Scholarly articles are cited in the standard law review format: author name, article title in quotation marks, volume and abbreviated law review name, first page, and year in parentheses. Thus "Lynn A. Stout, 'Why We Should Stop Teaching Dodge v. Ford,' 3 Va. L. & Bus. Rev. 163 (2008)" refers to an article by Lynn Stout published in volume 3 of the Virginia Law and Business Review, beginning at page 163, in 2008.

These citation conventions are standard in American legal scholarship and should present no difficulty to practitioners and scholars familiar with legal research methods.

- Third-party bar: No duties to non-shareholders

**Annual Report (RCW 23B.25.150):**
- Required annually
- Due within 4 months of fiscal year close
- Published on principal website, free of charge
- Content: narrative + optional enumerated elements
- Enforcement: summary court order after 2 consecutive years of noncompliance

**Fundamental Change Voting Thresholds:**
- Amendment to social purposes: 2/3 of each voting group
- Merger where SPC does not survive: 2/3 of each voting group
- Asset sale materially altering purpose: 2/3 of each voting group
- Conversion to SPC status: 2/3 of each voting group
- Conversion from SPC status: 2/3 of each voting group

**Integration With General Corporate Law:**
- Title 23B applies to SPCs except where Chapter 23B.25 provides otherwise
- Standard governance, shareholder, and creditor rules apply
- Derivative actions limited to shareholders

**Key Comparisons:**
- Softest director duty among major U.S. benefit corporation regimes
- No third-party standard required
- No benefit enforcement proceeding
- Reversible through 2/3 supermajority vote

- Draft specific social purpose statement, not just general categories

- Establish annual reporting calendar and process
