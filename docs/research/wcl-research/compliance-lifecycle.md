**A Practitioner's Reference to the Life Cycle of a Washington Business Corporation**

*Part of the PNW Research Series — Washington Corporate Law (WCL) Module*

## Preface

This chapter treats Washington State business corporation law as an object of study. It is written as a practitioner treatise: citation-heavy, structurally deep, and oriented toward the lawyer or compliance professional who must advise a Washington corporation across its entire operational life — from the moment articles are filed through amendments, interim compliance, extraordinary transactions (mergers, asset sales, conversions), and eventual dissolution.

The primary source law is the Washington Business Corporation Act (WBCA), codified at Title 23B RCW, and the Washington Uniform Business Organizations Code (WUBOC), codified at Chapter 23.95 RCW. The two chapters are interlocking. Title 23B contains substantive corporate governance rules specific to business corporations. Chapter 23.95 contains the "hub" rules that apply across all entity types: naming, registered agents, annual reports, filing mechanics, administrative dissolution, and reinstatement. A reader who attempts to work only from Title 23B will miss half of the applicable law — the WUBOC hub is load-bearing for every corporation.

Two events dominate the legal landscape as of this writing. The first is the 2024 amendment cycle (2024 c 22, Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5335), which fundamentally restructured the shareholder voting thresholds for amendments, mergers, asset sales, and dissolution for corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024. This change created a formation-date-dependent dual regime that practitioners must carry in working memory for the next several decades. The second is the ongoing effect of the 2015 WUBOC recodification (2015 c 176), which moved naming and registered agent rules out of Title 23B and into Chapter 23.95. Older treatises and form books that still cite the pre-2016 sections (e.g., the old RCW 23B.04.010 text and the old RCW 23B.05.010 address rules) are actively misleading.

This chapter proceeds in twelve parts, tracking the life cycle of a Washington corporation from naming through dissolution, with a concluding synthesis section providing a month-by-month compliance calendar. Each part opens with the statutory framework, quotes operative language where precision matters, offers practitioner commentary, and — where helpful — provides comparative notes to Delaware or the Model Business Corporation Act (MBCA) from which Title 23B descends.

## Part 1: Corporate Name

Washington's corporate naming regime is a layered structure. The substantive rules live in Chapter 23.95 RCW (the WUBOC), which governs all entity types. Title 23B RCW (the WBCA) incorporates those rules by reference. The Department of Revenue administers a separate trade-name registration regime for assumed names (DBAs), which operates independently of Secretary of State corporate naming.

A practitioner advising on corporate naming must therefore consult three bodies of law simultaneously: (1) RCW 23.95 Article 3 for the distinguishability and content rules, (2) RCW 23B.04 for the WBCA cross-references, and (3) RCW 19.80 and DOR administrative practice for trade names. Failure to clear all three creates avoidable rework at the Secretary of State rejection stage or, worse, a post-formation naming dispute.

### 1.1 Statutory Architecture

**RCW 23B.04.010** provides, in full: *"A corporate name must comply with the requirements of Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW."* That single sentence is the gateway. Everything substantive about naming a Washington business corporation lives in RCW 23.95.300 through 23.95.325 (Article 3 of the WUBOC).

**RCW 23B.04.020** likewise provides: *"A person may reserve the exclusive use of a corporate name in accordance with RCW 23.95.310."* Reservation, too, is a WUBOC-hub operation.

**RCW 23B.04.030** provides: *"A foreign corporation may register its corporate name in accordance with RCW 23.95.315."* Registration of a foreign corporate name — distinct from reservation and from obtaining a certificate of authority to transact business — again flows through the WUBOC.

The 2015 WUBOC recodification (2015 c 176, effective January 1, 2016) performed this consolidation. Before that date, Title 23B carried its own naming rules, and practitioners developed habits and form files referencing old section numbers. Those habits persist in some older templates and must be updated.

### 1.2 The Distinguishability Standard

The core naming test under Washington law is whether a proposed name is **"distinguishable on the records of the secretary of state"** from names already on file. This language, drawn from RCW 23.95.300, replaces the older "deceptively similar" standard that had been common in U.S. corporate statutes for much of the twentieth century. The shift matters: "distinguishable on the records" is a more mechanical, records-based test than "deceptively similar," which invited a consumer-confusion inquiry closer to trademark law.

**What the name must be distinguishable from.** RCW 23.95.300 requires distinguishability from:

1. The names of all domestic entities on the Secretary of State's records.
2. The names of all registered foreign entities authorized to transact business in Washington.
3. All names currently reserved under RCW 23.95.310.
4. All names currently registered under RCW 23.95.315.
5. Any name the use of which is restricted under other law.

**What does NOT create distinguishability.** The statute is explicit that superficial variations do not make a name distinguishable. The following differences are statutorily insufficient:

| Insufficient Difference | Example |
|---|---|
| Entity-type suffix variation | "Acme Corp." vs. "Acme Inc." vs. "Acme LLC" |
| Articles and conjunctions | "The Acme Company" vs. "Acme and Company" |
| Punctuation and capitalization | "ACME, Inc." vs. "Acme Inc" |
| Special characters | "Acme+" vs. "Acme" |
| Singular versus plural | "Acme Holding" vs. "Acme Holdings" |

This list is non-exhaustive; the operating principle is that the names must differ in their substantive lexical content, not merely in formal decoration. Practitioners should assume that any two names a reasonable reader would pronounce identically will be treated as indistinguishable.

**Consent exception.** An entity may use a name that would otherwise be indistinguishable if it obtains written consent from the holder of the conflicting name and files an undertaking to change its own name if required. This exception is used most often in affiliated-entity structures (parent, subsidiary, and holding-company chains that share a brand). It is less often useful in arm's-length disputes, since the holder of the first name rarely consents without consideration.

**Practitioner commentary.** In practice, the Secretary of State's examiner reviews name availability at the point of filing. Pre-filing name searches through the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS) are the rule, not the exception. However, a CCFS search is not a guarantee: examiner discretion at the filing window occasionally reaches conclusions that a search interface does not foreshadow. The defensive practice is (1) a CCFS search, (2) a reservation under RCW 23.95.310 if the name is strategically important, and (3) a telephone or email pre-clearance with the Corporations Division for borderline cases.

### 1.3 Required and Prohibited Words

**Required corporate designators (RCW 23.95.305).** The name of a Washington business corporation must contain one of the following words or abbreviations:

- "Corporation"
- "Incorporated"
- "Company"
- "Limited"
- "Corp."
- "Inc."
- "Co."
- "Ltd."

A Social Purpose Corporation (SPC) formed under Chapter 23B.25 RCW must additionally contain "Social Purpose Corporation," "SPC," or "S.P.C." (see Section 1.7 below).

The purpose of the designator requirement is creditor notice: any party dealing with the entity can tell from its name alone that they are contracting with a limited-liability entity and that the personal assets of the owners are generally shielded. Washington does not permit a corporation to exist without a designator.

**Prohibited words.** RCW 23.95.305 restricts the use of certain industry-regulated terms. A corporation may not use the following words in its name unless it satisfies additional regulatory preconditions (typically a license or certificate of authority from the relevant state regulator):

| Prohibited Word | Regulator / Precondition |
|---|---|
| "Bank," "banking" | Washington Department of Financial Institutions; federal banking agencies |
| "Trust" | DFI trust company charter |
| "Cooperative" | Must be formed under cooperative statutes |
| "Industrial and Loan" | Former industrial loan company regime |
| Combinations of "building," "savings," "loan," "home," "association," "society" | Historical anti-deception rules for thrift-type institutions |
| "Insurance" | Insurance Commissioner certificate of authority |

Prohibited-word screens are enforced at filing. A name containing "Bank" will not clear the examiner without documentation of DFI approval.

**Purpose limitation.** RCW 23.95.300 also provides that an entity name may not imply that the entity is organized for a purpose other than the purposes permitted by its governing documents. A corporation whose articles state only general business purposes cannot name itself "Acme Insurance Corporation" even if it does not intend to sell insurance — the implication is enough to be denied.

### 1.4 Name Reservation

**RCW 23.95.310** governs name reservation. Key terms:

- **Duration.** 180 days of exclusive use.
- **Transfer.** The reservation holder may transfer the reservation to a non-individual entity (i.e., to a corporation, LLC, partnership, etc., but not to another individual) by delivering an executed transfer notice to the Secretary of State identifying the transferee's name and address.
- **Renewal.** The statute does not provide for indefinite renewal. Practitioners who need longer than 180 days either form the entity sooner or allow the reservation to lapse and re-reserve, subject to the risk that an intervening third party may have claimed the name.
- **Fee.** The Washington Secretary of State's published fee schedule sets a fee for paper and online reservation filings, which is updated periodically; see the Corporations Division's current fee schedule.

The reservation mechanism is most useful in two practitioner contexts: (1) locking in a name during pre-formation due diligence and fundraising, when the founders are not yet ready to file articles but want to prevent a competing filer from taking the name; and (2) during a pending name-change amendment, when the corporation wants to confirm the new name is available before the board adopts the amendment.

### 1.5 Name Registration for Foreign Corporations

**RCW 23.95.315** provides a separate mechanism for foreign corporations that are not yet qualified to transact business in Washington but want to protect their name against use by a Washington domestic entity. The foreign entity files an application containing its name, jurisdiction, formation date, and a certificate of existence from its home jurisdiction. Registration runs through the end of the calendar year in which filed and may be renewed for successive one-year periods by application filed at least three months before expiration.

This is a protective mechanism, not a qualification to do business. A foreign corporation that actually transacts business in Washington must separately obtain a certificate of authority under RCW 23.95.505. Registration under 23.95.315 is used mainly by national brands that want to preserve namespace in Washington while they evaluate whether to qualify.

### 1.6 Name Changes: The Amendment Pathway

A Washington corporation that wishes to change its name amends its articles of incorporation. The change of corporate name is one of the few amendments that the board may adopt without shareholder approval under RCW 23B.10.020(5): *"to change the corporate name."* This board-only authority is standard across MBCA jurisdictions and reflects the limited substantive impact of a pure name change on shareholder rights.

The mechanical steps:

1. Board resolution adopting articles of amendment that change the corporate name.
2. Confirmation of name availability (CCFS search, ideally a reservation).
3. Filing of articles of amendment with the Secretary of State under RCW 23B.10.060.
4. Internal updates: bank accounts, licenses, contracts, letterhead, website, DBA registrations, seals, tax accounts with the Department of Revenue, employment tax accounts with ESD and L&I, federal tax accounts with IRS (Form 8822-B for responsible-party changes, though the EIN does not change for a pure name change).
5. Notice to material counterparties under any contractual notice clauses.

A name change that is part of a larger restructuring — for example, adopted as part of a restated articles filing under RCW 23B.10.070 — is handled through the restatement mechanism rather than a standalone articles of amendment.

### 1.7 Social Purpose Corporation Name Option

Washington authorized the Social Purpose Corporation (SPC) form in 2012 through the enactment of Chapter 23B.25 RCW. An SPC is a for-profit corporation whose articles expressly identify one or more social purposes that the board may consider (along with shareholder interests) in decision-making. It is Washington's analog to Delaware's Public Benefit Corporation, though with somewhat looser reporting requirements.

An SPC's name must include "Social Purpose Corporation," "SPC," or "S.P.C." as a designator. This is a substantive rule, not a formal one: the designator is required because it puts counterparties on notice that the corporation's fiduciary duties run not solely to shareholders but also to the stated social purpose.

An SPC is not the same as a Washington nonprofit corporation (Chapter 24.03A RCW) nor the same as a Certified B Corporation (which is a private certification, not a legal entity form). Practitioners should distinguish the three carefully when advising founders.

### 1.8 Assumed Names (DBAs) and Trade Name Registration

A Washington corporation's legal name is the name on its articles. Its **trade name** (also called an assumed name or DBA — "doing business as") is a separate concept, registered through the Department of Revenue rather than the Secretary of State. The legal basis is the Business Licensing Service (BLS) administered by DOR under Chapter 19.80 RCW.

**Mechanics.** A corporation registers a trade name as part of its Business License Application (BLA) filed with DOR. The fee is modest (a few dollars per trade name in addition to the BLA processing fee). Trade names do not expire and do not require renewal. A single corporation may register multiple trade names.

**Distinguishability.** Trade names in Washington are NOT subject to the same distinguishability test as corporate names. Multiple unrelated entities can register the same trade name with DOR; DOR does not enforce uniqueness. This is a notable trap for founders who assume that "registering the trade name" secures brand rights. It does not. Trade name registration is primarily a consumer-protection disclosure: it allows consumers and creditors to identify the real legal entity operating under a trade name. Actual brand protection comes from state or federal trademark registration (Chapter 19.77 RCW for state trademarks; the Lanham Act for federal).

**Practitioner commentary.** When advising a Washington corporation on naming, distinguish three layers: (1) the legal corporate name (Secretary of State, distinguishability test, creditor-notice designator required); (2) the trade name or DBA (DOR, disclosure only, no uniqueness); and (3) the trademark (state or federal, consumer-confusion test, exclusive use in commerce). All three can and often should be pursued in parallel.

## Part 2: Registered Agent

Every Washington corporation must continuously maintain a registered agent and a registered office in Washington. The registered agent is the corporation's legally designated recipient for service of process and official notices from the state. Failure to maintain a registered agent is a ground for administrative dissolution. The rules are in RCW 23B.05 (which, after 2015, contains mostly cross-references) and RCW 23.95 Article 4 (the substantive WUBOC rules).

### 2.1 The Continuous-Maintenance Obligation

**RCW 23B.05.010** provides: *"Each corporation must continuously maintain in this state a registered agent in accordance with Article 4 of chapter 23.95 RCW."*

Three words carry weight in that sentence: "continuously," "in this state," and "registered agent." Continuously means without a gap — even a brief lapse can trigger administrative dissolution proceedings. "In this state" means the agent's address must be a Washington street address. "Registered agent" is a term of art defined in RCW 23.95.400 and governed by the appointment, change, and resignation rules in RCW 23.95.415, .425, .430, and .435.

### 2.2 Who May Serve as Registered Agent

**RCW 23.95.415** establishes the appointment rules. A registered agent must be one of the following:

1. **An individual** who resides in Washington and has a business office (street address) in Washington at which service of process can be made during normal business hours.
2. **A domestic entity** (Washington corporation, LLC, etc.) with a Washington business office.
3. **A foreign entity** authorized to transact business in Washington with a Washington business office.
4. **A commercial registered agent** — a person or entity that has filed a listing statement with the Secretary of State under RCW 23.95.420 agreeing to serve as registered agent for multiple represented entities.

**Consent requirement.** Critically, RCW 23.95.415 provides: *"A registered agent shall not be appointed without having given prior consent in a record to the appointment."* Consent must be delivered to the Secretary of State in the prescribed form and filed at the time of the initial appointment. If a person is listed as a registered agent without consent, that person may deliver a notarized statement to the Secretary of State attesting to the fact, and the name will be removed from the records. This is a meaningful protection against fraud and against the accidental designation of unwilling third parties.

### 2.3 The Street Address Requirement — No P.O. Boxes

The registered office must be a **street address** in Washington where the agent can be served during normal business hours. P.O. boxes are expressly prohibited. This rule is absolute and has no exceptions. A P.O. box is insufficient because service of process under Washington's civil procedure rules contemplates physical delivery to a person (or, where authorized, by mail to a physical address after posting).

**Practical implications.** For small corporations without a physical Washington presence, this requirement often drives the decision to hire a commercial registered agent. A founder who lives outside Washington but wishes to incorporate in Washington cannot simply list their out-of-state home address. They need either (a) a Washington resident willing to serve, (b) a Washington office they lease, or (c) a commercial registered agent service.

### 2.4 Commercial Registered Agent Services

Washington maintains a public listing of commercial registered agents under RCW 23.95.420. Once a person is listed as a commercial registered agent, they may be appointed as registered agent for any number of represented entities simply by identification — the new entity's filing does not need to attach a separate consent record because the commercial listing serves as standing consent.

**Pricing norms.** Commercial registered agent services in Washington are a competitive market. As of the current period, typical pricing runs from roughly $100 to $300 per year for standard services, with some national providers at the lower end and boutique local firms at the higher end. Higher-tier services include mail scanning, document forwarding, compliance monitoring, annual report reminders, and sometimes year-round legal-mail triage. National providers include Corporation Service Company (CSC), CT Corporation, Registered Agents Inc., Northwest Registered Agent (headquartered in Spokane), and many others.

**Advantages of commercial registered agent services vs. self-serving.**

| Factor | Self-Serve (Founder) | Commercial Service |
|---|---|---|
| Cost | $0 | $100–$300/year |
| Privacy | Founder's address is public | Provider's address is public |
| Reliability during travel | Founder must be present during business hours | Provider handles continuously |
| Compliance reminders | Founder must track | Provider typically sends reminders |
| Embarrassment risk | Process server may arrive at home office | Process served at provider's commercial office |
| Change management | Founder must file statement of change when they move | Provider's address is stable |
| Scaling to multiple entities | Each entity needs its own agent | Single provider can serve many entities |

For most closely held corporations, the commercial registered agent service is worth the cost for privacy and reliability alone. Founders who use their home address as the registered office often come to regret it the first time a process server arrives during a family dinner.

### 2.5 Change of Registered Agent

**RCW 23.95.430** governs the change mechanism. An entity changes its registered agent by filing a **statement of change** with the Secretary of State. The statement must contain the entity's name and the new registered agent information required under RCW 23.95.415(1). Critically:

> *"The interest holders or governors of a domestic entity need not approve the filing of [a statement of change of registered agent]."*

In other words, the board's designated officer (typically the secretary) may file a change of registered agent without a formal board resolution. This is a housekeeping filing, not a substantive corporate act. The new registered agent's consent must accompany the statement.

**RCW 23.95.435** handles the related case of a noncommercial registered agent whose name or address changes (without the entity choosing to replace the agent). The agent is obligated to deliver a statement of change and to notify the represented entity.

The filing fee for a statement of change is set by the Secretary of State's fee schedule.

**Collateral updates.** When an agent changes, the corporation should also update: annual-report records (though the annual report filing itself will pick up the change); any other state's foreign-qualification records if the entity is qualified in multiple states; and internal records including minute book and operational address directories.

### 2.6 Resignation of Registered Agent

A registered agent may resign. The mechanism is governed by RCW 23.95.425 (for commercial agents terminating a listing) and by related provisions for noncommercial agents. The structural rule to remember is the **delayed-effectiveness period**: under RCW 23.95.425, *"A commercial-registered-agent termination statement takes effect at 12:01 a.m. on the 31st day after the day on which it is delivered to the secretary of state for filing."*

This 31-day window exists to give the represented entities time to appoint a replacement agent. During the window, the resigning agent must promptly notify each represented entity in writing. After the effective date, the agent no longer represents the entity. If no replacement has been appointed, service of process may be made on the entity under the fallback procedure of RCW 23.95.450, which typically means service on the Secretary of State itself, with forwarding to the entity's last known address.

**Practitioner commentary.** A registered agent resignation is a compliance alarm. If the resignation is coming from the agent (not initiated by the corporation), it often signals that the agent has not been paid, cannot reach the entity, or has decided the entity is not in good standing. A resigning agent typically sends notice to the entity's last known address, which may no longer be current. The result: the corporation may not learn that its agent has resigned until the 31-day window has run and the entity has entered an agent-less state, triggering the administrative-dissolution chain. The defensive practice is to pay registered agent fees promptly and to keep a second point of contact (a personal email of the founder, not just a generic company address) in the agent's records.

### 2.7 Consequences of Having No Registered Agent

Failure to maintain a registered agent is a statutory ground for administrative dissolution under RCW 23.95.605 (the WUBOC grounds-for-dissolution section, which replaced the former RCW 23B.14.210, repealed by 2015 c 176 § 2149 effective January 1, 2016). The Secretary of State identifies the deficiency, typically through a failed annual-report cycle or a returned mailing, and initiates the procedure in RCW 23.95.610:

1. Service of written notice on the entity identifying the grounds.
2. A 60-day cure period during which the entity may remedy the deficiency (by appointing a new agent and filing a statement of change) or demonstrate to the Secretary of State that grounds do not exist.
3. If cure fails, the Secretary of State executes and files a statement of administrative dissolution.
4. The entity's legal existence is restricted to winding up and seeking reinstatement.

Reinstatement is available under RCW 23.95.615 within a five-year window; see Part 10 below for full treatment.

### 2.8 Service of Process on a Registered Agent

Service of process on a Washington corporation is effected by personal delivery to the registered agent at the registered office during normal business hours. See CR 4(d)(4) and the WUBOC provisions incorporating service of process concepts. The service-of-process dimension of the registered agent role is why the street-address rule exists: the drafters wanted to ensure that there is always a physical location in Washington where a process server can physically hand a summons to a person, rather than relying on mail or publication.

## Part 3: Amendments to Articles

The articles of incorporation are the corporation's constitutional document. They contain the elements required by RCW 23B.02.020 — name, authorized shares, registered agent and office, incorporator — and may contain a broad range of optional provisions governing corporate powers, indemnification, shareholder rights, and internal governance.

Because articles are constitutional, changes require formality. Chapter 23B.10 RCW distinguishes between (a) amendments the board may adopt alone without shareholder approval and (b) amendments that require both board adoption and shareholder approval. The latter category is governed by the voting thresholds that were restructured by 2024 c 22 — one of the most significant changes to Washington corporate law in the past decade.

### 3.1 Board-Only Amendments: RCW 23B.10.020

**RCW 23B.10.020** lists the categories of amendments that the board of directors may adopt unilaterally, without shareholder action, unless the articles expressly reserve such amendments to the shareholders. The list is exhaustive (not illustrative), and it has been modestly expanded over the years. As of the current version:

1. **Par value adjustments** — to provide, change, or eliminate any provision with respect to the par value of any class of shares, when the corporation has only one class of shares outstanding.
2. **Removal of historical incorporator/director information** — to delete the names and addresses of the initial directors from the articles.
3. **Removal of registered agent/office information** — to delete the registered agent or registered office designation from the articles, provided that a statement of change has been filed.
4. **Stock splits** — to effect a forward or reverse stock split, with proportional adjustments to authorized shares, when the corporation has only one class of shares outstanding.
5. **Corporate name change** — to change the corporate name.
6. **Other expressly permitted changes** — any change that is expressly permitted elsewhere in Title 23B to be made by the board alone.

**The single-class limitation.** Several of these board-only authorities — par value, stock splits — require that the corporation have only one class of shares outstanding. This limitation exists because changes to par value and share quantity in a multi-class capital structure have distributional consequences among classes that implicate fiduciary duty and fairness concerns. A corporation with preferred shares outstanding cannot use RCW 23B.10.020 to do a forward split of its common; it needs a shareholder vote with separate voting groups under RCW 23B.10.030 and .040.

**Opting out.** The articles may provide that some or all of these board-only authorities are reserved to the shareholders. Form articles for venture-backed corporations often reserve the name-change and stock-split powers to the shareholders (or, more commonly, subject them to a specific series approval requirement), to give investors a veto over cosmetic changes that could nevertheless affect brand identity or dilution calculations.

### 3.2 Shareholder-Approved Amendments: RCW 23B.10.030 and the 2024 Change

**RCW 23B.10.030** is the gateway for substantive amendments. It sets a two-step process: board adoption followed by shareholder approval. The two steps cannot be compressed — the board must first formally adopt the amendment and recommend it to the shareholders before the shareholder vote is taken.

**The pre-August 2024 threshold.** For corporations formed before August 1, 2024, the vote required to approve an amendment under RCW 23B.10.030 is:

> Two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment by each voting group, except that for public companies the threshold is a majority of the votes entitled to be cast. The articles may require a higher threshold (but not lower).

This two-thirds threshold is the historical Washington default, inherited from the original 1989 enactment of the Washington Business Corporation Act. It is significantly higher than the Model Business Corporation Act default (which is a majority of votes cast at a meeting at which a quorum is present) and higher than the Delaware default (which is a majority of outstanding shares).

**The post-August 2024 threshold.** For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, the 2024 amendment (2024 c 22 § 20, Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5335) changed the default to:

> A majority of the votes entitled to be cast on the amendment by each voting group, unless the articles require a greater vote.

**Why the change.** The two-thirds default had long been an outlier. Washington was one of a small number of states that retained a supermajority default for ordinary amendments to articles. Venture investors and corporate counsel had, for years, been drafting around the default by including articles provisions that specified a majority vote — but the drafting-around solution was fragile because it had to be remembered, and mistakes (missing or ambiguous language in form documents) occasionally left corporations stuck at the supermajority default.

The legislature's stated rationale in 2024 was alignment with the MBCA mainstream, ease of capital formation, and removal of a surprise supermajority that occasionally defeated reasonable amendments. The legislature made a considered choice, however, to grandfather existing corporations under the two-thirds rule: retroactive application of a threshold change would have altered the governance expectations of shareholders in long-standing corporations. The compromise was a formation-date dichotomy.

**Dual regime in effect.** The result is that practitioners must now track a formation-date-dependent threshold for every Washington corporation they advise. A corporation formed July 31, 2024 operates under the two-thirds default; a corporation formed August 1, 2024 operates under the majority default. This dichotomy will persist until the legislature either harmonizes the rule or the two-thirds-default population shrinks to insignificance through dissolutions, conversions, and acquisitions.

**Practical effect on M&A and control transactions.** The threshold change meaningfully affects the mechanics of contested control transactions. Under the two-thirds rule, a blocking minority of 33.4% could defeat most amendments; under the majority rule, the blocking threshold rises to 50.1%. For sponsor-backed and family-held corporations, the change reduces the leverage of minority holders to extract concessions in amendment-driven restructurings. Practitioners should counsel clients considering formation under the new regime that minority protection is no longer the statutory default — if desired, it must be affirmatively specified in the articles.

**Articles may require more.** Both the pre-2024 and post-2024 rules permit the articles to specify a higher threshold. Neither permits a lower threshold: the articles cannot, for example, require only 40% approval. The floor is one vote above the statutory default, and the ceiling is unanimity (or any higher number the drafters choose). Practitioners drafting articles for closely held corporations under the new majority rule should consider whether to restore a supermajority requirement contractually for certain categories of amendments that directly affect ownership rights.

**Board may impose conditions.** RCW 23B.10.030 also permits the board to condition its submission of an amendment on any basis. A common example: the board may submit an amendment to the shareholders subject to the condition that the amendment becomes effective only if a related financing transaction closes, or only if a specified percentage of a particular class approves. Conditional submission is a useful technique for packaging amendment with a larger transaction.

**Notice requirements.** If shareholder approval is sought at a meeting, all shareholders entitled to vote on the amendment must receive notice of the meeting and a copy (or summary) of the proposed amendment. The notice requirements in RCW 23B.07.050 apply to the meeting itself; the substantive notice-of-amendment requirement is in RCW 23B.10.030. Failure of notice invalidates the vote.

### 3.3 Voting Groups: RCW 23B.10.040

**RCW 23B.10.040** is the Washington analog to MBCA § 10.04 and identifies the circumstances in which holders of a particular class or series of shares are entitled to vote as a separate voting group on a proposed amendment. Separate voting group treatment means that the amendment must satisfy the threshold (two-thirds or majority, depending on formation date) **within each voting group**, not just across the aggregate of all voting shares.

The categories of amendments that trigger separate-voting-group rights are:

1. **Increase the number of authorized shares of the class.**
2. **Effect an exchange or reclassification** of all or part of the shares of the class into shares of another class.
3. **Effect an exchange or reclassification** of shares of another class into shares of the class.
4. **Change the rights, preferences, or limitations** of the shares of the class.
5. **Change the shares of the class** into a different number of shares of the same class.
6. **Create a new class of shares** having rights or preferences with respect to distributions or dissolution that are prior, superior, or substantially equal to the shares of the class.
7. **Increase the rights, preferences, or number of authorized shares** of any class that, after giving effect to the amendment, has rights or preferences prior, superior, or substantially equal to the shares of the class.
8. **Limit or deny an existing preemptive right** of the shares of the class.
9. **Cancel or otherwise affect rights to distributions** or dividends that have accumulated but not yet been declared on the shares of the class.

**Multiple affected classes.** When an amendment affects multiple classes in substantially the same way, the statute permits the affected classes to vote together as a single voting group rather than separately, unless the articles provide otherwise or the board conditions submission on separate voting.

**Opt-out.** The articles may, with specific language, limit or eliminate voting-group rights for certain amendments (increases in authorized shares, creation of senior classes, increases in senior class rights). The opt-out must reference the specific affected provisions; generic opt-out language is insufficient.

**Why this section matters.** Voting groups are the principal mechanism by which Washington corporate law protects preferred shareholders and other special classes against amendment-driven dilution. Without the separate-voting-group protection, a sufficiently determined common-share majority could amend the articles to strip rights from preferred shares. The voting-group mechanism ensures that each affected class has its own up-or-down vote.

### 3.4 Amendment Before Share Issuance: RCW 23B.10.010, .050

Before a corporation has issued any shares, its articles may be amended by either the incorporator (if no directors have been named or elected) or the board of directors, without shareholder action. This pre-issuance amendment authority is governed by RCW 23B.10.010 and is frequently used during the window between filing initial articles and closing a first-round financing, when last-minute changes are needed to accommodate investor terms.

### 3.5 Amendment by Court Order: RCW 23B.10.050

**RCW 23B.10.050** provides a mechanism for amendment by court order in the context of reorganization proceedings (typically bankruptcy). A court order entered under a federal reorganization statute may effect amendments to the articles without the normal board and shareholder approvals. The corporation delivers articles of amendment to the Secretary of State identifying the court order. This is a narrow provision used mainly in Chapter 11 cases where the plan of reorganization alters the corporate structure.

### 3.6 Articles of Amendment: RCW 23B.10.060

**RCW 23B.10.060** sets the content requirements for the articles of amendment to be filed with the Secretary of State. The filing must contain:

1. The name of the corporation.
2. The text of each amendment adopted.
3. If the amendment provides for an exchange, reclassification, or cancellation of issued shares, provisions for implementing the amendment if not contained in the amendment itself.
4. The date of each amendment's adoption.
5. If the amendment was adopted by the board without shareholder action, a statement that shareholder action was not required and that the amendment was duly approved by the board or incorporators.
6. If the amendment required shareholder action, a statement that the amendment was duly approved by the shareholders in accordance with RCW 23B.10.030 and RCW 23B.10.040.
7. If applicable under RCW 23B.01.200(3)(e), a statement of the effective date.

**Effective date.** Articles of amendment become effective on filing unless a later effective date (not more than 90 days after filing) is specified. Delayed effectiveness is useful for coordinating amendments with closings of other transactions (a name change timed to the closing of a rebranding effort, for example).

**Filing fee.** The filing fee for articles of amendment is set by the Secretary of State's current fee schedule and is distinct from the annual report fee.

### 3.7 Restated Articles: RCW 23B.10.070

**RCW 23B.10.070** permits a corporation to restate its articles of incorporation at any time. A restatement is a consolidation of the original articles and all subsequent amendments into a single document that supersedes all prior filings. Restatement is useful after several rounds of amendments have left the articles in a confusing patchwork state.

**Three variants of restatement:**

1. **Pure restatement (no amendments).** Any corporate officer may restate the articles at any time without board or shareholder action, provided the restatement does not make any substantive changes. The restatement simply consolidates existing language.
2. **Restatement with board-only amendments.** If the restatement includes amendments that the board may adopt alone under RCW 23B.10.020, the board adopts the restatement. Shareholder action is not required.
3. **Restatement with shareholder-approved amendments.** If the restatement includes amendments that require shareholder approval, the board first adopts the restatement and then submits it to the shareholders under RCW 23B.10.030.

**Required certificate.** The articles of restatement filed with the Secretary of State must include a certificate stating the restatement's name, the restated text, whether amendments are included, and — if amendments requiring shareholder action are included — the required compliance statements.

**Practitioner commentary.** Restatement is often overlooked as a tool. In a corporation that has been through multiple rounds of financing, each adding a new class or series with its own preferences, the articles can become nearly unreadable. A clean restatement at each major inflection point (typically at a Series B or Series C financing, or before a pre-IPO audit) improves counsel's ability to advise, investors' ability to diligence, and management's ability to understand its own capital structure. The drafting cost is usually modest, and the downstream benefits are substantial.

## Part 4: Bylaws Amendments

Bylaws are the corporation's internal operating document. They are not filed with the Secretary of State and are not part of the public record. They govern matters such as officer titles and duties, meeting procedures, quorum and voting rules at the board and shareholder level, committee structures, and indemnification procedures. The amendment rules for bylaws are in RCW 23B.10.200 through .220.

### 4.1 Default Rule: Concurrent Board and Shareholder Authority

**RCW 23B.10.200** establishes that both the board and the shareholders may amend, repeal, or adopt bylaws. The board's authority to amend bylaws is the default rule. Shareholders retain concurrent authority and may amend bylaws even when the board also possesses such authority.

> *The board of directors may amend or repeal the corporation's bylaws, or adopt new bylaws, unless: (a) the articles of incorporation or this title reserve this power exclusively to the shareholders in whole or in part; or (b) the shareholders in amending, repealing, or adopting a particular bylaw expressly provide that the board of directors may not amend, repeal, or reinstate that bylaw.*

The practical effect is a two-tier structure: the board handles most bylaw changes (procedural housekeeping, officer title updates, committee charter changes) without shareholder involvement, but the shareholders retain a reserved power to lock in a particular bylaw against board-level change.

### 4.2 Shareholder-Adopted Bylaws the Board Cannot Amend

The statute creates a carve-out: if the shareholders adopt a particular bylaw and expressly provide that the board may not amend, repeal, or reinstate it, the board's concurrent authority is extinguished as to that bylaw. This is the **"shareholder-adopted protection"** mechanism.

**Use case.** The shareholder-adopted protection is used most often in closely held corporations where the minority shareholders want to guarantee that a governance rule (for example, a requirement that certain decisions require unanimous board consent, or a supermajority requirement for certain officer elections) cannot be rolled back by a board majority friendly to the majority shareholder. It is also occasionally used in public companies to entrench a takeover defense — though in the public company context, such measures often draw activist shareholder criticism.

**Interplay with articles-level protection.** A governance rule that needs strong protection is typically placed in the articles rather than the bylaws, because articles require both board and shareholder approval to amend and can require supermajority thresholds. Shareholder-adopted bylaws are a lighter-weight alternative when articles-level protection is not warranted.

### 4.3 Bylaws Increasing Quorum or Voting Requirements

**RCW 23B.10.210** addresses the special case of bylaws that establish heightened quorum or voting requirements for the board of directors. Amendments to such bylaws are subject to additional constraints:

- If the shareholders originally adopted the heightened requirement, the shareholders may amend or repeal it.
- If the board originally adopted the heightened requirement, the board may amend or repeal it — but the amendment must satisfy the existing heightened requirement (or the proposed new requirement, whichever is greater).

The purpose of this cascade rule is to prevent a board majority from using a simple-majority vote to amend away a supermajority requirement, which would defeat the purpose of the supermajority rule in the first place.

### 4.4 Bylaws Increasing Quorum or Voting for Shareholders

**RCW 23B.10.220** is the shareholder-meeting analog of RCW 23B.10.210. Amendments to bylaws that establish heightened quorum or voting requirements for shareholder meetings are subject to similar cascade rules: the amendment must satisfy the existing requirement (or the proposed new one, whichever is greater), whether the amendment is by shareholder action alone or by the board.

## Part 5: Annual Reports and Good Standing

The annual report is the centerpiece of ongoing Washington corporate compliance. It is a short, mostly informational filing, but the consequences of failure are severe: administrative dissolution, loss of good standing, and eventual termination of legal existence if not cured within the five-year reinstatement window.

### 5.1 The Initial Report

**RCW 23.95.255(1)** requires every newly formed domestic entity (except limited liability partnerships and nonprofit corporations, which have separate regimes) to file an **initial report** within 120 days after the entity's public organic record (the articles of incorporation, in the case of a business corporation) becomes effective. The initial report contains the same information as the annual report. Its purpose is to capture post-formation updates — for example, if the registered agent listed in the initial articles has been replaced, or if the initial directors have appointed officers and the officer information needs to be captured.

The 120-day window is counted from the effective date of the articles, not from the date of filing. This distinction matters when articles are filed with a delayed effective date: the initial report deadline runs from the effective date.

**Consequences of failing to file an initial report.** Failure to file the initial report within 120 days is a statutory ground for administrative dissolution under RCW 23.95.605. The Secretary of State does not typically move to dissolve immediately on day 121; there is an internal grace and notice sequence. But the deficiency is a black mark that must be cured before the next annual cycle.

### 5.2 The Annual Report

Subsequent reports are filed annually, on a schedule determined by the Secretary of State — typically tied to the anniversary month of the entity's formation. The Secretary of State sends a reminder notice 30 to 90 days before the deadline, but the statutory obligation to file runs regardless of whether the reminder is received.

**RCW 23.95.255** lists the content of the annual report:

1. The entity name and jurisdiction of formation.
2. The registered agent's name and street and mailing addresses in Washington.
3. The principal office's street and mailing addresses.
4. For foreign entities, the principal office location in the entity's home jurisdiction.
5. The names of the entity's governors (i.e., directors, for a business corporation).
6. A brief description of the nature of the entity's business.
7. The Unified Business Identifier (UBI) number.
8. For nonprofit corporations, the federal Employer Identification Number (EIN) and any additional information required by the nonprofit statute.

The annual report does not require financial information, tax information, or details about shareholders. It is an identity and contact-information filing, not a transparency or governance filing.

**Current-as-of date.** The information in the report must be current as of the date the report is executed. This matters: if a director has resigned two months before the annual report is filed, the report must reflect the post-resignation director set, not the stale set from the prior annual report.

### 5.3 The UBI Number and Cross-Agency Coordination

The Unified Business Identifier (UBI) is Washington's unified tax and regulatory identification number, administered through the Business Licensing Service (BLS) run by the Department of Revenue. Every Washington business registers with BLS and receives a UBI. The UBI is the key by which DOR, ESD (Employment Security Department), L&I (Labor and Industries), and other state agencies cross-reference the same entity.

The UBI appears on the annual report as a way of tying the Secretary of State filing to the broader BLS record. A mismatch between the SOS record and the DOR record — for example, different addresses or different entity names — is a common source of confusion and occasional enforcement issues.

### 5.4 Fee Structure

The Washington Secretary of State's current fee schedule imposes an annual report fee for business corporations. As of the most recent increase, the annual report fee for a for-profit business corporation is $70; the fee for a nonprofit or mutual corporation is $10. Fees are periodically adjusted, and practitioners should verify the current amount at the time of filing.

**Expedited service.** Expedited processing is available for an additional $100 per entity (three-working-day target) or same-day processing for $150. Expedited service is rarely needed for routine annual reports but is sometimes used when the corporation is racing to restore good standing for a closing.

**Page surcharge.** Filings exceeding 100 pages carry an additional $13 charge per 50-page increment. This is relevant mostly to restated articles or complex merger filings, not to annual reports (which are short).

### 5.5 Good Standing Certificate

A **certificate of existence** (also called a certificate of good standing) is a document issued by the Secretary of State certifying that the entity is on record with the state and in compliance with the annual-report and registered-agent requirements. Certificates are commonly required in connection with:

- Bank financing (lenders want to know the borrower exists and is in good standing).
- M&A transactions (due diligence confirmation).
- Contract closings with large counterparties.
- Foreign qualification in other states (the target state's SOS wants a certificate from the home state).
- Litigation (proof of capacity to sue or be sued).

**What the certificate covers.** The certificate is a narrow document. It certifies that, as of the date of issuance: the entity is a validly existing domestic or registered foreign entity, the Secretary of State has not filed a statement of dissolution or administrative dissolution, and the entity's annual reports and fees are current. The certificate does NOT certify that the entity is compliant with other state laws (tax, employment, regulatory), does NOT certify that no litigation is pending, and does NOT certify accuracy of the information in the corporate filings. It is a narrow existence and compliance certificate, nothing more.

**How to obtain.** Certificates are available through the Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS) online portal, typically within minutes for a standard fee (verify current fee), with expedited delivery available. The certificate is a PDF with a state seal.

### 5.6 Delinquency Consequences and the Path to Administrative Dissolution

If the annual report is not filed by its deadline, the entity enters a delinquency period. The Secretary of State sends a delinquency notice. If the report is still not filed after the notice period, the Secretary of State initiates administrative dissolution under RCW 23.95.610.

**Sequence:**

1. Annual report due date passes without filing.
2. Delinquency notice sent to the registered agent / last known address.
3. Grace period (varies, typically 60 days after due date, confirm current practice).
4. If still not filed, Secretary of State files a statement of administrative dissolution under RCW 23.95.610. This requires a written notice with the specific grounds and a 60-day cure period under the WUBOC procedure.
5. After the 60-day cure period, administrative dissolution takes effect.
6. Reinstatement is available under RCW 23.95.615 for up to five years.

**Reinstatement mechanics.** To reinstate, the entity files a reinstatement application with the Secretary of State containing: proof that the name is still available (or a new name, if the old one has been taken), current principal office and registered agent information, the effective date of the administrative dissolution, and documentation that the grounds for dissolution have been cured. Payment is required for (a) all back annual report fees for the years missed, (b) a penalty fee set by Secretary of State rule, and (c) the current year's annual report fee. Upon approval, the reinstatement **relates back** to the effective date of dissolution — meaning that, for most purposes, the entity is treated as having been continuously in existence. This retroactive effect is critical: contracts signed during the dissolution period are not automatically void, and lawsuits filed by the dissolved entity are preserved.

**Caveat on retroactivity.** The retroactive effect does not impair third-party rights arising from reliance on the dissolved status before the party learned of the reinstatement. A creditor who, in good-faith reliance on the dissolved status, took action against individual directors or shareholders, is not stripped of those rights by the retroactive reinstatement.

## Part 6: Records and Reports

Chapter 23B.16 RCW imposes record-keeping obligations on every Washington corporation and grants shareholders the right to inspect many categories of records. This is the internal-transparency regime.

### 6.1 Required Records: RCW 23B.16.010

**RCW 23B.16.010** requires a corporation to maintain:

1. **Minutes** of all meetings of shareholders and the board of directors, and a record of actions taken without a meeting (written consents).
2. **Minutes** of all meetings of committees of the board and a record of committee actions taken without a meeting.
3. **Appropriate accounting records.**
4. **A record of shareholders** — current, in a form permitting preparation of an alphabetical list showing each shareholder's name and address, the number and class (or series) of shares held, and the date each share certificate was issued.
5. **Records at the principal office:**
   - Current articles of incorporation and all amendments.
   - Current bylaws and all amendments.
   - Minutes of shareholder meetings and records of shareholder action without meeting for the past three years.
   - Financial statements for the past three years.
   - All written communications to shareholders generally within the past three years (including the financial statements).
   - A list of the names and business addresses of current directors and officers.
   - The most recent annual report filed with the Secretary of State.

**Format.** Records may be kept in any form capable of conversion to paper form within a reasonable time.

### 6.2 Retention Periods

Washington law does not specify a universal retention period for most corporate records. The three-year windows in RCW 23B.16.010 apply only to the principal-office subset of records. For longer-term retention, practitioners typically counsel clients to retain:

| Record Type | Suggested Retention |
|---|---|
| Articles, bylaws, and amendments | Permanently |
| Minute book (shareholder and board meetings) | Permanently |
| Stock ledger and transfer records | Permanently |
| Audited financial statements | 7–10 years |
| Tax returns and supporting documents | 7 years (federal) / follow WA DOR guidance |
| Significant contracts | Term + applicable SOL |
| Employment records | Per WA L&I and federal requirements |

These are recommendations, not statutory minimums. The permanent-retention items should be preserved in a secure form — ideally both a physical minute book and digital backups.

### 6.3 Shareholder Inspection Rights: RCW 23B.16.020

**RCW 23B.16.020** grants shareholders the right to inspect corporate records. The statute distinguishes two tiers of inspection:

**Tier 1 — basic inspection (no special showing required).** A shareholder, on at least five business days' written notice, may inspect and copy the "principal office records" listed in RCW 23B.16.010(5) — the articles, bylaws, recent minutes, recent financial statements, recent shareholder communications, directors/officers list, and most recent annual report. Tier 1 inspection is essentially unconditional: any shareholder of record may demand it without explaining their purpose.

**Tier 2 — extended inspection (proper purpose required).** On at least five business days' written notice, a shareholder may inspect and copy:

- Excerpts from any minutes of board meetings, records of board action without meeting, minutes of shareholder meetings, and records of shareholder action without meeting.
- Accounting records of the corporation.
- The record of shareholders (for purposes other than a meeting, which is covered separately).

Tier 2 inspection requires the shareholder to satisfy three conditions:

1. The shareholder's demand is made **in good faith and for a proper purpose.**
2. The shareholder **describes with reasonable particularity** the purpose and the records the shareholder wishes to inspect.
3. The records are **directly connected with the stated purpose.**

**The "proper purpose" test.** "Proper purpose" is a well-developed concept in Washington and in corporate law generally. It means a purpose reasonably related to the person's interest as a shareholder. Examples of proper purposes:

- Investigating possible mismanagement or corporate waste.
- Valuing shares for a contemplated sale or for estate planning.
- Communicating with other shareholders about a proxy contest.
- Investigating potential derivative claims.

Examples of improper purposes:

- Harassment.
- Curiosity without a rational connection to shareholder interest.
- Obtaining a shareholder list to solicit business unrelated to the corporation.
- Gathering competitive intelligence for a competing business.

The good-faith requirement and the proper-purpose requirement work together: a purpose stated in bad faith fails both prongs.

### 6.4 Scope of Inspection, Copying, and Representation

Shareholders exercising inspection rights may:

- **Inspect** records in person at the principal office during regular business hours.
- **Copy** records (typically at the shareholder's expense for reasonable reproduction costs).
- **Be accompanied** by an attorney, accountant, or other agent during the inspection. The right to representation is important: shareholders often lack the expertise to evaluate accounting records without professional assistance.

The corporation may impose reasonable procedural requirements (scheduling the inspection, providing a conference room, requiring the shareholder to sign a confidentiality agreement for sensitive records). It may not impose requirements so onerous as to defeat the right of inspection.

### 6.5 Denial of Inspection: RCW 23B.16.040

If a corporation denies a properly demanded inspection, the shareholder may petition the superior court to order the inspection. RCW 23B.16.040 provides that the court may summarily order inspection on the petition of a shareholder who satisfies the statutory requirements. The corporation bears the burden of proving that the denial was justified.

If the court orders inspection, it may also order the corporation to pay the shareholder's costs and attorneys' fees incurred in seeking the order. This fee-shifting provision is significant: it ensures that shareholders with legitimate inspection demands are not deterred by the cost of enforcement.

### 6.6 Financial Statements: RCW 23B.16.200

**RCW 23B.16.200** imposes an affirmative obligation on the corporation to prepare and furnish annual financial statements:

- **Preparation.** Within four months after the close of each fiscal year, the corporation must prepare (at a minimum) a balance sheet as of the end of the fiscal year and an income statement for the fiscal year.
- **Distribution on request.** On written request, any shareholder is entitled to receive a copy of the most recent balance sheet and income statement.
- **Consolidated statements.** Statements may be on a consolidated basis if the corporation has subsidiaries.
- **Accounting basis.** If the statements are prepared using GAAP, they must so state. If they are prepared on another basis, they must state that basis and disclose any material departures from GAAP.
- **Audit status.** If the statements are audited, the auditor's report must accompany them. If unaudited, a certification by the president or principal accounting officer must accompany them, confirming the preparation basis.

**Practical effect.** Closely held corporations that do not otherwise prepare financial statements (very small family corporations, for example) are nevertheless required to produce an annual balance sheet and income statement. Compliance is not universal; the statute is under-enforced. But a shareholder demanding statements under RCW 23B.16.200 has a clear entitlement, and a corporation unable to produce them exposes itself to inspection litigation under RCW 23B.16.020 and potential derivative claims for mismanagement.

### 6.7 Shareholder Lists for Meetings: RCW 23B.07.200

Separate from the general inspection rights of RCW 23B.16.020, RCW 23B.07.200 imposes a specific obligation to prepare a shareholder list before each shareholder meeting. The list must be available for inspection by any shareholder beginning two business days after notice of the meeting is given, for the period of the meeting, and at the meeting itself.

The shareholder list is used to determine who is entitled to vote at the meeting, to verify credentials, and to facilitate communication among shareholders who may wish to solicit proxies or coordinate voting. The meeting-list right is unconditional — no proper-purpose showing is required.

## Part 7: Mergers, Share Exchanges, Conversions

Chapter 23B.11A RCW governs mergers and share exchanges under the Washington Business Corporation Act. (The older Chapter 23B.11 was restructured into Chapter 23B.11A as part of ongoing WBCA modernization, with the new chapter reflecting the current Model Business Corporation Act framework.) Conversions — changes of entity type — are handled separately under Chapter 23B.09 (or the WUBOC conversion provisions in Chapter 23.95 Article 9).

### 7.1 Merger Definitions: RCW 23B.11A.010

**RCW 23B.11A.010** defines the key terms:

- **Party to a merger.** Any domestic corporation or other entity that will merge under a plan of merger.
- **Surviving entity.** The corporation or other entity into which one or more other parties to the merger are merged. The surviving entity may be an existing party or may be created by the merger itself.
- **Acquired entity** (in a share exchange). The domestic corporation that will have all of one or more classes or series of its shares acquired in the exchange.
- **Acquiring entity** (in a share exchange). The domestic corporation that will acquire the shares.
- **New owner liability.** Owner liability that applies to a different entity than where shares were held before the transaction, or involves changed liability terms at the same entity.

**Structural taxonomy.** The statute contemplates several transaction structures:

| Structure | Description |
|---|---|
| **Statutory merger** | Two or more entities combine; one survives, others disappear. |
| **Consolidation** | Two or more entities combine into a new entity; all predecessors disappear. (Treated as a form of merger under current Washington law.) |
| **Share exchange** | One entity acquires all of one or more classes of shares of another entity in exchange for consideration; both entities continue to exist. |
| **Triangular merger (forward)** | Target merges into a subsidiary of acquirer; subsidiary survives. |
| **Triangular merger (reverse)** | Subsidiary of acquirer merges into target; target survives as subsidiary of acquirer. |
| **Short-form merger** | Parent owning 90% of subsidiary merges with subsidiary without subsidiary shareholder vote. |

Each structure has different tax, liability, and consent implications. The choice of structure is a core M&A planning decision driven by tax treatment (qualification for tax-free reorganization under IRC § 368), assumption of contracts (change-of-control consent triggers), and preservation of specific liabilities or assets (e.g., real estate transfer taxes in some jurisdictions).

### 7.2 Plan of Merger Requirements

The plan of merger is the contractual instrument that defines the transaction. While RCW 23B.11A.010 is primarily definitional, the plan content is driven by the substantive approval provisions in RCW 23B.11A.020 (board approval) and .030 / .040 (shareholder approval). The plan must contain:

1. The names of the parties to the merger and the name of the surviving entity.
2. The terms and conditions of the merger.
3. The manner and basis of converting the shares of each party into shares, other securities, cash, other property, or any combination, of the surviving entity (the "merger consideration").
4. If the surviving entity is to amend its articles as part of the merger, the amendments.
5. Any other provisions required by the parties.

Plans of merger may include termination conditions, closing conditions, representations and warranties, indemnification provisions, and dispute resolution clauses — all the standard M&A contract elements. But for the statutory filing purpose, the plan must cover at minimum the above.

### 7.3 Board Approval: RCW 23B.11A.020

The board of directors of each party to a merger must approve the plan of merger and recommend it to the shareholders (except in the narrow circumstances where shareholder approval is not required — short-form merger and certain survivor-corporation mergers). The board's recommendation must be affirmative unless the board determines that, because of conflicts of interest or other special circumstances, it should not make such a recommendation. In that case, the board must communicate the basis for its determination to the shareholders.

Board adoption requires a majority vote at a meeting at which a quorum is present, unless the articles or bylaws require a higher vote. The board's duty of care applies: the approval must be an informed decision, typically supported by financial advisor opinions, legal counsel advice, and due diligence materials. In transactions where a controlling shareholder is involved, the board should consider establishing a special committee of disinterested directors to negotiate and approve the transaction, to obtain the protections of the "entire fairness / business judgment" framework developed in Delaware and adopted in substance by Washington courts.

### 7.4 Shareholder Approval: RCW 23B.11A.040 — The 2024 Change Applies

**RCW 23B.11A.040** sets the shareholder-approval threshold for mergers and share exchanges. The 2024 c 22 amendment restructured this section in parallel with RCW 23B.10.030:

**For corporations formed before August 1, 2024:** Two-thirds of the votes entitled to be cast by each voting group, unless the articles require a different vote (no lower than a majority).

**For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024:** Majority of the votes entitled to be cast by each voting group, unless the articles require a greater vote.

**Voting groups in mergers.** Just as with amendments, a merger may require separate voting group approval when the merger would affect the rights, preferences, or limitations of a particular class. RCW 23B.11A.041 addresses separate voting groups for mergers.

**Short-form merger exception.** Shareholder approval is not required when all of the following are true:

- The corporation survives the merger.
- The articles are not amended, except as permitted by RCW 23B.10.020 (i.e., board-only amendments).
- Each shareholder of the surviving corporation holds, after the merger, the same number of shares with identical rights and preferences as before.

This exception, common to MBCA jurisdictions, recognizes that a merger which does not change the survivor's capital structure or the rights of the survivor's shareholders is equivalent to an ordinary asset-level transaction from the survivor's perspective and does not warrant a shareholder vote.

**New owner liability consent.** If the merger would result in a shareholder becoming subject to new owner liability (for example, if the surviving entity is a general partnership with unlimited partner liability), each affected shareholder must provide express written consent. Silence is insufficient; affirmative consent is required.

### 7.5 Short-Form Parent-Subsidiary Merger: RCW 23B.11A.045

**RCW 23B.11A.045** (formerly .040 under the prior numbering) provides a streamlined procedure for the merger of a parent corporation with a subsidiary of which it owns at least 90% of the outstanding shares of each class. The short-form merger:

- **Requires only the parent's board approval** — no shareholder vote of either the parent or the subsidiary is required.
- **Does not require subsidiary board approval or execution of the merger articles** by the subsidiary.
- **Requires notice to minority shareholders of the subsidiary** within 10 days after the merger becomes effective. The notice must include a copy of the plan of merger (or a summary of material terms) and the consideration to be received.
- **Preserves dissenters' rights** for minority shareholders of the subsidiary under RCW 23B.13.020.

**Use case.** The short-form merger is the standard mechanism for "squeezing out" minority shareholders after a parent has acquired a majority-but-not-all interest. It is also used in corporate housekeeping to eliminate wholly or nearly-wholly owned subsidiaries that are no longer useful as separate entities.

**Fiduciary duty caveat.** While the short-form merger requires no subsidiary shareholder vote, the parent's board (and directors who are fiduciaries of the subsidiary as well) remain subject to fiduciary duties. A short-form merger at an unfair price can give rise to appraisal and breach-of-duty claims. The fair-price determination in the context of a short-form merger is the subject of extensive Delaware jurisprudence (Weinberger, Glassman v. Unocal, etc.), which Washington courts generally follow.

### 7.6 Triangular Mergers

Triangular mergers are not separately statute-authorized as distinct transaction types; they are statutory mergers between a target and a wholly owned subsidiary of an acquirer, with two orientations:

**Forward triangular merger.** The target merges into a subsidiary of the acquirer. The subsidiary is the surviving entity. The target disappears. The acquirer holds the surviving subsidiary.

- **Advantage:** Target's contracts do not automatically transfer (since the target disappears); this can trigger anti-assignment clauses that the acquirer wishes to trigger (e.g., to exit unfavorable contracts). Conversely, it forces renegotiation of key contracts that the acquirer wants to keep, which can be a disadvantage.
- **Tax treatment:** Can qualify as tax-free reorganization under IRC § 368(a)(2)(D) if the acquirer's stock is used as consideration and "substantially all" of the target's assets end up in the subsidiary.

**Reverse triangular merger.** The subsidiary merges into the target. The target is the surviving entity. The subsidiary disappears. The acquirer holds the surviving target as a subsidiary.

- **Advantage:** Target's contracts generally remain in place without triggering assignment clauses, because the target continues to exist. This is the structure of choice for acquisitions where contract continuity is critical (government contracts, technology licenses, long-term customer contracts).
- **Tax treatment:** Can qualify as tax-free reorganization under IRC § 368(a)(2)(E) if the acquirer's stock is used for at least 80% of the consideration and the target holds "substantially all" of its assets and "substantially all" of the subsidiary's assets after the merger.

The choice between forward and reverse triangular mergers is driven primarily by contract portability and secondary tax considerations.

### 7.7 Articles of Merger: RCW 23B.11A.050 and .060

After the plan of merger has been approved, the surviving entity files **articles of merger** with the Secretary of State. RCW 23B.11A.060 sets the content requirements:

1. The name, jurisdiction of organization, and type of entity of each party to the merger.
2. The name, jurisdiction of organization, and type of entity of the surviving entity.
3. If the articles of the surviving entity are amended as part of the merger, the amendments (or a restated articles of incorporation as amended).
4. A statement that the plan of merger was duly approved by each domestic corporation party to the merger in accordance with this chapter, or a statement that approval was not required.
5. A statement that the plan was approved by each non-corporate domestic party in accordance with its organic law.
6. A statement that each foreign entity party has complied with its home-jurisdiction organic law.

**Effective date.** The merger becomes effective when the articles of merger are filed with the Secretary of State, unless a later effective date (not more than 90 days after filing) is specified. Where non-domestic parties are involved, effectiveness may be delayed until all required foreign filings become effective.

**Combined filings.** The merger filing may be combined with other filings (e.g., the surviving entity's restated articles, or a statement of change of registered agent) if both sets of content requirements are satisfied.

**Filing fee.** The Secretary of State's current fee schedule specifies the articles-of-merger fee, which is typically higher than a simple amendment fee because of the complexity of the filing.

### 7.8 Effect of Merger: RCW 23B.11A.060 (and successor provisions)

The effect of the merger, once effective, is comprehensive:

1. **Each party other than the surviving entity ceases to exist.** The disappearing entities are terminated as legal persons.
2. **Title to all property** owned by each party vests in the surviving entity by operation of law without further act. No separate deeds, assignments, or bills of sale are required.
3. **All obligations and liabilities** of each party become obligations and liabilities of the surviving entity. This is the doctrine of successor liability in the merger context — the surviving entity inherits everything, known and unknown.
4. **All pending legal proceedings** may be continued as if the merger had not occurred, or the surviving entity may be substituted as the party in interest.
5. **The articles of incorporation** (or equivalent organic documents) of the surviving entity are amended as set forth in the plan of merger.
6. **The shares** of each party are converted as set forth in the plan — into shares of the surviving entity, into other securities, into cash, into property, or any combination.
7. **Shareholders with perfected dissenters' rights** under Chapter 23B.13 are entitled to the statutory remedy.

The automatic transfer of assets and liabilities is the defining feature of a statutory merger and the principal reason parties choose this structure over alternatives. Compared to an asset sale, the merger eliminates the need for bills of sale, assignment consents, and individual conveyancing for each asset.

### 7.9 Share Exchanges: RCW 23B.11A.030

A share exchange is a conceptual cousin to a merger. In a share exchange, the acquiring entity acquires all of the shares of one or more classes of the acquired entity in exchange for consideration. Both entities continue to exist after the exchange: the acquired entity becomes a subsidiary of the acquiring entity, but it is not terminated.

**Plan of share exchange content.** The plan must identify the acquiring and acquired entities, specify the classes or series of shares to be acquired, and set the manner and basis of the exchange (the consideration). It may also contain amendments to the acquired entity's articles and any other provisions the parties wish to include.

**Approval.** The plan must be approved by the board of the acquired entity and by its shareholders under the RCW 23B.11A.040 thresholds (which are subject to the 2024 formation-date dichotomy). The acquiring entity's board must also approve the plan, but acquiring-entity shareholder approval is generally not required because the acquiring entity does not experience a structural change — it is merely acquiring new shares.

**When used.** Share exchanges are used most often when the parties want the acquired entity to continue to exist as a legal person (to preserve contracts, licenses, regulatory authorizations) without a full triangular merger structure. They are also the mechanism of choice for certain tax structures where a direct share-for-share exchange qualifies as a tax-free "B reorganization" under IRC § 368(a)(1)(B).

### 7.10 Corporate Conversions: Chapter 23B.09 and WUBOC Article 9

**Conversion** is the change of an entity from one type to another — for example, a corporation converting to a limited liability company, or an LLC converting to a corporation, or a corporation converting to a social purpose corporation, or a business corporation converting to a benefit corporation (if and when Washington adopts a benefit corporation statute distinct from the SPC regime).

Conversions are governed by Chapter 23B.09 RCW (for WBCA-specific conversions) and by Article 9 of Chapter 23.95 RCW (for the WUBOC cross-entity conversion framework). The basic mechanics mirror mergers:

1. **Plan of conversion** — a document specifying the new entity type, the new organic documents, and the treatment of ownership interests.
2. **Board approval.**
3. **Shareholder/member approval** under the applicable threshold (for corporations, the 2024 formation-date dichotomy applies).
4. **Articles of conversion** — filed with the Secretary of State, typically accompanied by the new organic document for the post-conversion entity type.
5. **Effective date** — the conversion is effective on filing, unless a later date is specified.

**Effect of conversion.** The converting entity continues to exist as the same legal person, merely in a new form. Assets, liabilities, and legal proceedings continue without interruption. The conversion is therefore a cleaner mechanism than a merger into a newly formed entity of the desired type, because it avoids the conceptual termination of the old entity and the rebirth of a new one.

**Dissenters' rights in conversions.** Under RCW 23B.13.020, certain conversions trigger dissenters' rights — in particular, conversions to a foreign corporation where shareholders do not receive shares with equivalent terms, or conversions to a non-corporate entity. A corporation-to-LLC conversion will typically trigger dissenters' rights for shareholders who do not wish to become LLC members.

**Tax considerations.** Conversions can have significant tax consequences that depend on the entity types involved. Corporation-to-LLC conversions generally trigger liquidation treatment at the corporate level (deemed asset distribution followed by contribution to the LLC), which can be costly. LLC-to-corporation conversions typically qualify as tax-free formations under IRC § 351. Practitioners should always coordinate conversion planning with tax counsel.

## Part 8: Asset Sales

Asset sales are the alternative to mergers for acquiring a business. Instead of combining entities, an asset sale transfers specified assets (and, usually, specified liabilities) from a seller to a buyer, leaving the seller as an empty shell (or with retained assets and liabilities). The WBCA addresses asset sales in Chapter 23B.12 RCW.

### 8.1 Ordinary Course vs. Substantial Asset Sales

**RCW 23B.12.010** establishes that a corporation may sell, lease, exchange, or otherwise dispose of its property in the **usual and regular course of its business** without shareholder approval. A retailer sells inventory every day without a shareholder vote; a real estate company buys and sells buildings in the ordinary course.

**RCW 23B.12.020** addresses the non-ordinary-course case: a sale, lease, exchange, or other disposition of property **other than in the usual and regular course** that would leave the corporation without a "significant continuing business activity." This is the statutory test for "all or substantially all" — the Washington formulation of the classic doctrine.

### 8.2 The "Significant Continuing Business" Threshold

The statute defines "significant continuing business activity" as a continuing business that represents at least:

- **25% of total assets** at the end of the most recently completed fiscal year; AND
- **Either 25% of income** from continuing operations, or 25% of revenues from continuing operations, for the most recently completed fiscal year.

This two-part, numeric test is a notable feature of Washington law. It replaces the purely qualitative "all or substantially all" test that Delaware and many other MBCA jurisdictions continue to use. The Washington test is more predictable: a corporation can, in principle, run the numbers and determine with confidence whether shareholder approval is required.

**Practitioner commentary.** The numeric test is most useful when the numbers are clearly above or below the thresholds. It is harder to apply when the numbers are close to the thresholds, when the corporation's fiscal-year-end financials are not yet finalized, or when the business is changing rapidly. In close cases, counsel usually recommends obtaining shareholder approval as a protective measure even if the strict numeric test is not clearly triggered.

### 8.3 Shareholder Approval Requirement: RCW 23B.12.020

If the disposition would leave the corporation without a significant continuing business activity (as defined above), shareholder approval is required. The procedure:

1. **Board adoption and recommendation.** The board adopts a resolution authorizing the disposition and recommends shareholder approval (subject to the same conflict-of-interest caveat as in amendments and mergers).
2. **Notice.** Shareholders are notified and provided a copy or summary of the transaction terms.
3. **Shareholder vote.** The vote threshold applies:
   - **Pre-August 1, 2024 corporations:** two-thirds of votes entitled to be cast.
   - **Post-August 1, 2024 corporations:** majority of votes entitled to be cast.

The 2024 dichotomy applies to asset sales just as it does to amendments and mergers.

**Exception — wholly-owned subsidiary transfers.** Parent corporations do not need shareholder approval to transfer assets to wholly-owned subsidiaries. This reflects the reality that such transfers do not change the parent's ultimate economic position; they are internal reorganizations, not transactions with third parties.

### 8.4 "All or Substantially All" Interpretation — Washington and Comparative Law

Washington's numeric test does not entirely eliminate interpretive questions. For example: what if the corporation has multiple lines of business and the sale disposes of one line entirely but leaves the others intact? What if the "continuing business" is a small but high-margin unit that, though numerically above 25% of income, is not the business that shareholders understood themselves to be invested in?

Washington courts have relatively few published decisions interpreting RCW 23B.12.020, so practitioners often look to Delaware case law for guidance on the qualitative dimension of the test:

- **Katz v. Bregman (Del. Ch. 1981).** A sale of the company's principal operating assets (a pipe business) triggered shareholder approval under Delaware's "all or substantially all" test, even though the company retained other assets, because the sold assets represented the core of the business as shareholders understood it. Katz is the canonical statement of the qualitative dimension: the test asks not only how much is being sold, but whether what is being sold was "quantitatively vital and qualitatively the core" of the enterprise.
- **Hollinger Inc. v. Hollinger International, Inc. (Del. Ch. 2004).** The Court of Chancery declined to find that a sale of major newspaper properties triggered shareholder approval, emphasizing that the company retained substantial other operating assets and that the "substantially all" test should be applied conservatively to avoid second-guessing ordinary business decisions. Hollinger is often cited for the proposition that "substantially all" means substantially all, not merely "a lot."

Washington's numeric test shields most cases from the need to apply the qualitative analysis. But in close cases — particularly multi-line-of-business corporations where the 25% thresholds are met by a combination of retained businesses that do not match the historical identity of the corporation — practitioners should be alert to the qualitative dimension and, when in doubt, seek shareholder approval.

### 8.5 Successor Liability in Asset Sales

A key question in any asset sale is whether the buyer inherits the seller's liabilities. The general common-law rule is that an asset purchaser does **not** inherit the seller's liabilities — in contrast to a statutory merger, where the surviving entity inherits everything by operation of law. This is one of the principal reasons buyers often prefer asset sales to mergers: they can cherry-pick which liabilities to assume.

**Exceptions to the general rule.** Courts have developed four exceptions under which an asset purchaser may be held liable for seller obligations:

1. **Express or implied assumption.** The purchase agreement expressly assumes the obligations, or the purchaser's conduct implies assumption.
2. **De facto merger.** The transaction is structured as an asset sale but functions as a merger — the buyer takes all or substantially all of the seller's assets, the seller immediately dissolves, there is continuity of ownership (the seller's shareholders become shareholders of the buyer), and continuity of management and operations. A de facto merger is treated as a statutory merger for liability purposes.
3. **Mere continuation.** The buyer is essentially the same entity as the seller, with a new name. Common factors: shared ownership, same directors and officers, same business location, same customers, same employees. The mere-continuation doctrine overlaps with de facto merger but focuses on identity rather than the merger-like nature of the transaction.
4. **Fraudulent transfer.** The transaction was intended to defraud creditors of the seller. This exception is statutorily reinforced by the Washington Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (RCW 19.40).

**Product liability successor exception.** A fifth exception has been adopted in some jurisdictions (notably California) for product liability claims, under which the buyer of a product line inherits liability for products manufactured by the seller. Washington has not definitively adopted the product-line exception; its treatment remains uncertain.

**Environmental liability.** Under CERCLA and Washington's Model Toxics Control Act (MTCA, Chapter 70A.305 RCW), environmental liability often follows real property regardless of the contractual structure of the transfer. Asset purchasers of contaminated property may inherit cleanup obligations even when the purchase agreement expressly disclaims assumption of environmental liabilities. Environmental diligence is therefore critical in any asset sale involving real property.

**Tax liability.** Certain tax liabilities, particularly state withholding taxes and state sales/use taxes, may follow the assets under successor-liability statutes even in a pure asset sale. Washington's Department of Revenue has a successor liability statute at RCW 82.32.140 that imposes on asset purchasers the unpaid tax obligations of the seller, subject to a tax clearance procedure.

**Employment liability.** Under the federal WARN Act and various state and federal discrimination statutes, successor liability can attach when the buyer is deemed a "successor employer" — typically where there is substantial continuity of operations and workforce.

**Drafting implications.** A well-drafted asset purchase agreement addresses successor liability through (a) a precise definition of "Assumed Liabilities" and "Excluded Liabilities," (b) representations and warranties by the seller about undisclosed liabilities, (c) indemnification provisions backed by escrows or insurance, and (d) a bulk sales analysis where applicable (Washington repealed its bulk sales law, so bulk sales compliance is no longer a concern for Washington transactions, but transactions touching other states may still need to address bulk sales in those states).

## Part 9: Dissenters' Rights

Chapter 23B.13 RCW creates the Washington version of the statutory appraisal remedy, known in this state as **dissenters' rights**. The remedy allows a shareholder who dissents from certain specified corporate actions to receive fair value for their shares in cash, in lieu of accepting the consideration offered by the transaction. The remedy is the shareholder's principal protection against being dragged into an unfair transaction by a majority vote.

### 9.1 Triggering Events: RCW 23B.13.020

**RCW 23B.13.020** identifies the triggering events that give rise to dissenters' rights. The list is exhaustive; dissenters' rights exist only as to these events:

1. **Mergers.** Consummation of a merger to which the corporation is a party, if shareholder approval is required under RCW 23B.11A.040 and the shareholder was entitled to vote on the merger. Shares of the surviving corporation that remain outstanding after the merger are excluded (since their holders retain their ownership interest).
2. **Share exchanges.** Consummation of a share exchange in which the corporation is the acquired entity, if the shareholder was entitled to vote.
3. **Asset dispositions.** A sale or other disposition of all or substantially all of the corporation's property other than in the usual and regular course of business, if the shareholder was entitled to vote under RCW 23B.12.020.
4. **Certain amendments.** An amendment to the articles that effects a redemption or cancellation of all of the shareholder's shares in exchange for cash or other consideration.
5. **Entity conversions (to foreign corporation).** A conversion of the corporation to a foreign corporation in which the shareholder does not receive shares with terms and voting percentages substantially equivalent to those of the pre-conversion shares.
6. **Corporate conversions (to non-corporate entity).** A conversion of the corporation to another entity type that is not a foreign corporation (e.g., corporation-to-LLC).
7. **Other events as specified in the articles.** The articles may expand the list by designating additional events that trigger dissenters' rights.

### 9.2 The Market-Out Exception

**RCW 23B.13.020(4)** contains an important exception. Dissenters' rights do **not** apply to a disposition for cash pursuant to a plan by which all or substantially all of the net proceeds of the disposition will be distributed to the shareholders within one year after the date of the disposition.

The rationale: if the corporation is selling substantially all its assets for cash and distributing the cash to shareholders within a year, the shareholders are being paid out anyway. The dissenters' rights machinery is unnecessary because the shareholders are not being forced to accept illiquid or complex consideration in exchange for their ownership — they are simply receiving cash as part of an orderly liquidation.

More broadly, many jurisdictions (including Delaware and most public-company practice) recognize a **"market out" exception** that withdraws dissenters' rights for shareholders of publicly traded corporations, on the theory that a liquid public market provides an adequate alternative to the appraisal remedy — the dissenting shareholder can simply sell their shares into the market. Washington's market-out exception is narrower than Delaware's and does not blanket-exclude all public-company shareholders from dissenters' rights. Practitioners representing Washington public companies should confirm the applicability of dissenters' rights on a transaction-specific basis.

### 9.3 Perfection Procedure

Dissenters' rights are perfected through a multi-step procedural sequence. Each step has strict timing requirements, and a failure at any step forfeits the right. The sequence is governed by RCW 23B.13.200 through .280:

**Step 1: Notice of dissenters' rights (corporation to shareholders).** When proposed corporate action creating dissenters' rights is submitted for shareholder approval, the meeting notice must state that shareholders are or may be entitled to assert dissenters' rights and must be accompanied by a copy of Chapter 23B.13 (RCW 23B.13.200). Failure of this notice gives shareholders longer windows to exercise rights.

**Step 2: Notice of intent to dissent (shareholder to corporation, before vote).** A shareholder who wishes to dissent must, before the vote is taken, deliver to the corporation a written notice of intent to demand payment if the action is effectuated. The shareholder must also not vote in favor of the action.

**Step 3: Consummation of the action.** If the action is approved and consummated, the corporation delivers a **dissenters' notice** to all shareholders who timely gave notice of intent under Step 2. The dissenters' notice must:

- State where the payment demand must be sent and where share certificates (if any) must be deposited.
- Supply a form for demanding payment.
- Set a date by which the corporation must receive the payment demand (not fewer than 30 nor more than 60 days after the date the dissenters' notice is delivered).
- Inform shareholders of the right to estimate fair value.
- Be accompanied by a copy of Chapter 23B.13.

**Step 4: Payment demand and deposit (shareholder to corporation).** The dissenting shareholder sends the payment demand and deposits the share certificates (for certificated shares). Failure to make a timely demand or deposit the shares extinguishes the dissenter's rights.

**Step 5: Payment by corporation.** The corporation must pay the dissenter its estimate of fair value plus accrued interest, promptly after the later of (a) the effective date of the action or (b) receipt of the payment demand.

**Step 6: Dissenter's objection (if dissenter disputes the corporation's payment).** If the dissenter believes the payment is inadequate, the dissenter has 30 days from the date of payment to notify the corporation of the dissenter's own estimate of fair value and to demand payment of the difference.

**Step 7: Court proceeding.** If the corporation and dissenter cannot agree on fair value within 60 days after the dissenter's objection under Step 6, the corporation must commence a court proceeding to determine fair value. If the corporation fails to commence a proceeding within that 60-day window, the corporation must pay the dissenter the amount demanded.

**Strict compliance.** Washington courts have generally required strict compliance with the perfection steps. A dissenter who misses a deadline or fails to follow the prescribed form loses the right. This is unforgiving. Counsel advising dissenting shareholders should diagram the timeline carefully and err on the side of early filings.

### 9.4 Fair Value Determination: RCW 23B.13.300

**Fair value** is the heart of the dissenters' rights remedy. RCW 23B.13 defines fair value as the value of the shares **immediately before the effectuation of the corporate action**, **excluding any appreciation or depreciation in anticipation of the action**, unless exclusion would be inequitable.

This definition has important implications:

- **Appraisal date.** The valuation date is the moment before the action, not the date of the payment demand or the date of trial.
- **No merger premium.** The "appreciation in anticipation of the action" exclusion means that the merger premium paid by the acquirer does not automatically become the floor for fair value. If the target's stand-alone value was $40 per share and the merger consideration is $55 per share (reflecting a $15 premium), the fair value for dissenters may be $40, not $55.
- **No minority discount (majority view).** Most jurisdictions, including Washington, reject the application of a minority discount in the dissenters' rights context. The shareholder is being involuntarily cashed out, and it would be unfair to penalize the shareholder for their minority position.
- **No marketability discount (contested).** Whether a marketability discount may be applied is contested across jurisdictions. Washington courts have not definitively resolved the question, but the trend in appraisal jurisprudence nationally is to disallow marketability discounts in the statutory appraisal context.

**Valuation methodology.** Courts typically consider several valuation methodologies and blend them: (a) the discounted cash flow (DCF) method, which projects future cash flows and discounts to present value; (b) the market multiple method, which applies trading multiples from comparable public companies; (c) the precedent transaction method, which applies multiples from comparable M&A transactions; and (d) occasionally the net asset value method, for asset-heavy businesses. Delaware's appraisal jurisprudence has extensively developed these methodologies; Washington courts generally follow Delaware's lead.

**Deal price as evidence of fair value.** Recent Delaware case law (Dell, DFC Global, Aruba Networks) has held that in certain circumstances, the merger deal price is strong (or even dispositive) evidence of fair value — particularly when the deal process was robust, arm's-length, and subject to a market check. This line of cases has shifted the landscape of appraisal litigation significantly over the past decade. Washington courts have not yet fully engaged with this question in published opinions, so the practitioner should treat Delaware's deal-price-as-evidence jurisprudence as persuasive but not binding.

### 9.5 Court Proceeding: RCW 23B.13.300

If the corporation and dissenter cannot agree, the corporation commences a court proceeding to determine fair value. The proceeding has several distinctive features:

- **Venue.** Superior court where the corporation's principal office (or registered office, if no principal office is in Washington) is located. For foreign corporations, venue is where the acquired domestic corporation's registered office was located.
- **All dissenters joined.** All dissenters with unsettled demands become parties to the action. The court typically consolidates individual dissenter claims into a single proceeding for efficiency.
- **Nonresident service.** Nonresident dissenters may be served by registered or certified mail or by publication, as provided by the civil rules.
- **Appraisers.** The court may appoint appraisers to receive evidence and recommend a decision on fair value. Discovery proceeds under the ordinary civil rules.
- **Judgment.** The court enters judgment for each dissenter in the amount by which fair value plus accrued interest exceeds what the corporation paid, or — if the corporation did not make any payment — for the full fair value plus interest.
- **Costs and fees.** The court has discretion to assess the costs of the proceeding against the corporation or the dissenters. If the court finds that the corporation did not substantially comply with the procedural requirements, or that the dissenters acted arbitrarily or vexatiously, the court may shift attorneys' fees accordingly.

**Compliance challenges.** The corporation may challenge whether a particular shareholder complied with the dissenter requirements (timely notice of intent, timely payment demand, timely share deposit). Non-complying shareholders are dismissed from the proceeding.

### 9.6 Practitioner Commentary on Dissenters' Rights Strategy

For the corporation undertaking a transaction that will trigger dissenters' rights:

- **Budget for dissent.** Set aside cash for expected dissenters before the transaction closes. Dissenters' rights are most often exercised by institutional investors or activist shareholders, and a large dissenter pool can consume meaningful transaction consideration.
- **Procedural compliance.** Deliver proper notices on time. The strict-compliance doctrine cuts both ways: if the corporation fails to follow the statutory procedure, the corporation may be compelled to pay larger amounts or face fee-shifting.
- **Fair-value analysis pre-closing.** Engage a valuation firm to prepare a defensible fair-value analysis before closing. This supports the corporation's initial payment estimate and positions it favorably in any subsequent litigation.
- **Consider deal structure.** Certain deal structures (such as the market-out cash liquidation exception) avoid dissenters' rights entirely. Structure the deal to minimize exposure if possible.

For the dissenting shareholder:

- **Diagram the timeline.** Know every deadline. Missing one forfeits everything.
- **Deposit shares and comply technically.** Dissenters' rights have been forfeited for reasons as minor as depositing physical share certificates at the wrong address.
- **Preserve discovery.** Start gathering valuation evidence early. Appraisal is expert-driven, and the quality of your expert's work is often determinative.
- **Evaluate economic rationality.** Appraisal litigation is expensive. Litigation costs can easily exceed $100,000 for a moderately complex case. The dissenting shareholder should confirm that the likely recovery materially exceeds the deal price plus litigation costs before proceeding.

## Part 10: Dissolution

Chapter 23B.14 RCW governs dissolution of Washington business corporations. Dissolution is the legal ending of the corporation's existence. Washington law recognizes three pathways: voluntary dissolution (by the corporation itself), administrative dissolution (by the Secretary of State for procedural failures), and judicial dissolution (by court order).

### 10.1 Voluntary Dissolution — Pre-Activity: RCW 23B.14.010

**RCW 23B.14.010** provides a streamlined dissolution path for corporations that have not yet begun business and have not issued shares. A majority of the incorporators or the initial directors may deliver articles of dissolution to the Secretary of State. The articles must state the corporate name, the date of incorporation, that no shares have been issued and no business has been transacted, that all debts of the corporation (if any) have been paid, and that a majority of the incorporators or initial directors authorized the dissolution.

The pre-activity dissolution path is used when a corporation was formed in anticipation of a transaction that did not proceed — for example, a founder formed a Washington corporation in preparation for a venture financing that ultimately fell through. Rather than carrying the corporation indefinitely, the founders dissolve it before it acquires any obligations or complications.

### 10.2 Voluntary Dissolution — Standard Process: RCW 23B.14.020

**RCW 23B.14.020** governs the standard voluntary dissolution after the corporation has begun business or issued shares. The procedure is:

1. **Board proposal.** The board of directors proposes dissolution for submission to the shareholders. The board must recommend dissolution unless conflicts of interest or special circumstances apply (in which case the basis for the lack of recommendation must be disclosed).
2. **Board may impose conditions.** The board may condition its submission on any basis, including the affirmative vote of a specified percentage of shares.
3. **Shareholder notification.** Shareholders are notified of the proposed dissolution through either a shareholders' meeting notice or a written consent process.
4. **Shareholder vote.** The 2024 formation-date dichotomy applies:
   - **Pre-August 1, 2024 corporations:** two-thirds of votes entitled to be cast, plus two-thirds of each voting group entitled to vote separately.
   - **Post-August 1, 2024 corporations:** majority of votes entitled to be cast, plus majority of each voting group.
5. **Articles of dissolution.** Upon approval, the corporation files articles of dissolution with the Secretary of State under RCW 23B.14.030.

The board is the initiating party for voluntary dissolution under standard practice. Shareholders do not have independent authority to initiate voluntary dissolution; they can only vote on a proposal that the board has submitted. Shareholders who want to force dissolution over board opposition must pursue judicial dissolution under RCW 23B.14.300 (see Section 10.11 below).

### 10.3 Articles of Dissolution: RCW 23B.14.030

**RCW 23B.14.030** specifies the content of the articles of dissolution. Two key documents must be delivered to the Secretary of State:

1. **A revenue clearance certificate** issued pursuant to RCW 82.32.260. This is a certificate from the Washington Department of Revenue confirming that the corporation has filed all required tax returns and paid all taxes due. Obtaining the revenue clearance certificate requires an application to DOR and can take weeks or longer if there are outstanding tax issues. Practitioners should initiate the DOR clearance process at the outset of dissolution planning, not at the end.

2. **Articles of dissolution** containing:
   - The name of the corporation.
   - The date dissolution was authorized.
   - A statement that dissolution was duly authorized under either RCW 23B.14.010 (pre-activity) or RCW 23B.14.020 (standard).

**Effective date.** Dissolution is effective on the effective date of the articles of dissolution (which, like all WBCA filings, may be on filing or up to 90 days later).

### 10.4 Publication of Notice

Within **30 days** after dissolution becomes effective, the dissolved corporation must publish a notice requesting creditors to present claims. The notice must:

- Be published once a week for three consecutive weeks in a newspaper of general circulation in the county where the principal office (or, if none, the registered office) is located.
- Describe the information required for claim submission.
- Provide a mailing address for claims.
- State that claims against the dissolved corporation may be barred in accordance with the provisions of Chapter 23B.14 if not timely asserted.

Failure to publish does not affect the validity of the dissolution itself, but it does forfeit the benefit of the three-year bar date for unknown claims (see Section 10.7 below).

### 10.5 Revocation of Dissolution: RCW 23B.14.040

**RCW 23B.14.040** allows a corporation to revoke its dissolution within **120 days** after the effective date. Revocation requires the same authorization as the original dissolution: board action and, if the original dissolution required shareholder approval, shareholder approval (under the applicable 2024-dichotomy threshold).

On revocation, the corporation files articles of revocation with the Secretary of State. The revocation relates back to the effective date of the dissolution, meaning that the corporation is treated as having been continuously in existence during the 120-day window. Third-party rights arising from good-faith reliance on the dissolved status are preserved, paralleling the reinstatement-from-administrative-dissolution rule.

**Use case.** Revocation is used when dissolution was initiated based on a plan that has since changed — for example, a corporation dissolved in anticipation of a sale that fell through, or a corporation dissolved in anticipation of merger into an affiliate that has been called off.

### 10.6 Effect of Dissolution: RCW 23B.14.050

**RCW 23B.14.050** establishes the critical principle that dissolution does not terminate the corporation's legal existence immediately. Instead, the corporation continues to exist for the limited purpose of **winding up** its affairs:

- Collecting its assets.
- Disposing of properties that will not be distributed in kind.
- Discharging or making provision for discharging its liabilities.
- Distributing remaining property to shareholders according to their interests.
- Doing every other act necessary to wind up and liquidate its business and affairs.

During the winding-up period, the corporation may sue and be sued in its corporate name, may continue to transact business necessary to winding up, and retains its directors and officers in their capacities. The corporation may NOT carry on business except as appropriate to winding up.

**Continuation of registered agent.** Administrative dissolution expressly preserves the authority of the registered agent (RCW 23.95.610). The same principle applies to voluntary dissolution: the corporation needs a continuing recipient for service of process during the winding-up period, which can extend for years.

### 10.7 Known Claims Procedure: RCW 23B.14.060

**RCW 23B.14.060** allows the dissolved corporation to cut off **known claims** through a written notice procedure. A "known claim" is a claim against the corporation that is either:

- Legally assertable before or after dissolution (matured or contingent), or
- Unmatured but potentially arising under existing executory contracts (excluding product warranties).

The procedure:

1. **Written notice** to known claimants. The notice must describe the claim, provide a mailing address for submission, state a deadline (not fewer than 120 days from the effective date of the notice), and state that the claim will be barred if not timely delivered.
2. **Claimant response.** The claimant must deliver its claim within the 120-day period. Failure to deliver bars the claim.
3. **Rejection.** If the corporation rejects the claim, the claimant has **90 days** from the rejection to commence legal proceedings. Failure to file within 90 days also bars the claim.
4. **Acceptance.** If the corporation accepts the claim, it pays or provides for payment from corporate assets.

The known-claims procedure provides predictable resolution of identified liabilities and a mechanism for the corporation to calculate its net-of-liabilities distributable estate. It is the primary tool used by orderly winding-up to cap the corporation's downside.

### 10.8 Unknown Claims Procedure: RCW 23B.14.070

**RCW 23B.14.070** addresses the more difficult problem of **unknown claims** — claims the corporation has no way to notify individually because it does not know the identity of the claimant. The newspaper publication under RCW 23B.14.030 is the mechanism.

**Effect of publication.** If the corporation publishes as required, claims by unknown claimants are barred unless the claimant commences a proceeding to enforce the claim within **three years** after the publication date. This is the "three-year publication bar" — a statutory sunset for the corporation's exposure to latent claims.

**Shareholder clawback.** Even after the three-year bar, if corporate assets are insufficient to pay a late-filed claim that is not otherwise barred, the claimant may seek to recover from shareholders who received distributions in the dissolution, but only up to the amount of the distributions received. This is the "shareholder clawback" rule, which preserves some creditor remedies while capping shareholder exposure at the amount distributed.

**Director liability for distributions: RCW 23B.14.080.** Directors who authorize improper distributions during dissolution (distributions in violation of the corporation's obligations to known or unknown claimants) can be held personally liable. The standard of care is the ordinary director duty of care; a director who makes a reasonable inquiry and acts in good faith is protected.

### 10.9 Administrative Dissolution: Chapter 23.95 Article 6

**Administrative dissolution** is dissolution by the Secretary of State for procedural failures. The grounds were originally in RCW 23B.14.210 through .230, but these sections were **repealed by 2015 c 176 § 2149, effective January 1, 2016**. The substantive grounds were moved to **RCW 23.95.605** (as part of the WUBOC consolidation), and the procedure is in **RCW 23.95.610**.

**Grounds for administrative dissolution (RCW 23.95.605).** The Secretary of State may administratively dissolve a domestic entity for:

1. **Failure to pay** any fee, tax, or penalty due to the Secretary of State.
2. **Failure to file** an annual report when due.
3. **Failure to maintain** a registered agent in the state.
4. **Failure to file** a statement of change of registered agent within a required period after the agent has resigned or become unavailable.
5. **Expiration** of the period of duration (if the articles specify a fixed duration).
6. Other statutory grounds as specified in the chapter.

**Procedure (RCW 23.95.610).**

1. The Secretary of State serves written notice on the entity identifying the grounds and stating that dissolution will occur unless the entity cures within 60 days.
2. The entity has 60 days to cure or demonstrate that grounds do not exist.
3. If the entity fails to cure, the Secretary of State executes a statement of administrative dissolution identifying the grounds and the effective date, and files the statement.
4. The entity's legal existence is restricted to winding up and seeking reinstatement. The registered agent's authority is preserved.

**Reinstatement after administrative dissolution: RCW 23.95.615.** The administratively dissolved entity may apply for reinstatement within **five years** of the effective date of administrative dissolution. The application must include:

- A statement that the entity's name is still available (or a new name if not).
- Current principal office and registered agent information.
- The effective date of the administrative dissolution.
- Documentation that the grounds for dissolution have been cured.

Payment accompanies the application: all back annual report fees for missed years, a reinstatement penalty set by SOS rule, and the current year's annual report fee.

**Retroactive effect.** Upon reinstatement, the reinstatement **relates back** to the effective date of administrative dissolution. The entity is treated as having been in continuous existence. Third-party rights that arose from good-faith reliance on the dissolved status during the dissolution period are preserved.

**Beyond five years.** If the five-year window expires without reinstatement, the entity is permanently dissolved and must start over with a new formation if it wants to resume operations. Contracts signed in the name of the dissolved entity during the post-window period are legally suspect. This is a hard cutoff.

### 10.10 Judicial Dissolution: RCW 23B.14.300

**RCW 23B.14.300** authorizes the superior court to dissolve a corporation in three categories of proceedings:

**Attorney General proceedings.** The Washington Attorney General may petition for dissolution if:

- The corporation obtained its articles of incorporation through fraud.
- The corporation has continued to exceed or abuse the authority conferred upon it by law.

These proceedings are rare. They are used primarily against corporations engaged in systematic fraud or in serious and continuing violations of public welfare statutes.

**Shareholder proceedings.** Shareholders may petition for dissolution on any of five grounds:

1. **Director deadlock.** The directors are deadlocked in the management of the corporation's affairs, the shareholders are unable to break the deadlock, and irreparable injury to the corporation is threatened or being suffered (or the corporation's business cannot be conducted to the advantage of the shareholders generally).
2. **Oppression, illegality, or fraud.** Those in control of the corporation have acted, are acting, or will act in a manner that is illegal, oppressive, or fraudulent.
3. **Shareholder deadlock.** The shareholders are deadlocked in voting power and have failed, for a period that includes at least two consecutive annual meeting dates, to elect successors to directors whose terms have expired.
4. **Misapplication or waste.** The corporate assets are being misapplied or wasted.
5. **Failure to dissolve.** The corporation has ceased operations and has failed to dissolve, liquidate its assets, or distribute remaining funds within a reasonable period.

**The oppression ground.** The oppression ground is the most frequently litigated. "Oppression" has been interpreted in Washington and other MBCA jurisdictions as conduct that substantially defeats the reasonable expectations of minority shareholders on which they relied when investing in the corporation. It is most commonly invoked in closely held corporations where the majority has frozen out the minority — removing the minority from employment, stopping dividends, refusing to redeem shares at fair value, or otherwise treating the minority as if they had no ownership interest.

**The deadlock grounds.** Director deadlock and shareholder deadlock are both recognized in Washington. Director deadlock requires that the deadlock threaten or cause irreparable injury — mere disagreement is not enough. Shareholder deadlock requires that the shareholders fail to elect directors for two consecutive annual meetings; this is a more mechanical test but is rarely triggered because most corporations manage to fill at least the minimum director slots through compromise.

**Alternative remedies.** Courts have broad remedial discretion in shareholder-petition cases. Rather than dissolving the corporation, the court may order alternatives: (a) a buy-out of the petitioning shareholder's shares at fair value, (b) appointment of a provisional director to break a deadlock, (c) appointment of a custodian to oversee corporate affairs temporarily, (d) injunctions against oppressive conduct. Dissolution is often considered the remedy of last resort, used when alternative remedies cannot cure the underlying problem.

**Creditor proceedings.** A creditor may petition for dissolution if:

- The creditor has a judgment against the corporation that has not been satisfied and the corporation is insolvent, or
- The corporation has admitted in writing that the creditor's claim is due and owing and the corporation is insolvent.

Creditor-initiated judicial dissolution is rare in practice because creditors typically prefer bankruptcy (which has a better-developed procedural framework) or individual collection actions (which are faster and cheaper).

### 10.11 Court Supervision of Dissolution

The court may also assume supervision of a voluntarily initiated dissolution if the corporation or any of its shareholders or creditors petitions for court supervision. Court supervision provides a neutral forum for resolving disputes during winding up and can be particularly useful in contested dissolutions where the shareholders cannot agree on the distribution of assets.

## Part 11: Significant Business Transactions

Chapter 23B.19 RCW is Washington's anti-takeover statute. Enacted in 1987, the chapter imposes a **five-year moratorium** on certain transactions between a "target corporation" and an "acquiring person" who has obtained 10% or more of the target's voting power. The statute is Washington's most visible protection against hostile takeovers and is one of the most restrictive such statutes in the country.

### 11.1 Purpose and History

Washington enacted Chapter 23B.19 in 1987 as part of the broad wave of state anti-takeover statutes adopted in response to the hostile-takeover boom of the 1980s. The policy rationale articulated by the legislature was to protect Washington corporations and their stakeholders (employees, communities, suppliers) from coercive takeover tactics — particularly the two-tier front-end-loaded tender offer, in which a bidder offered a premium for enough shares to gain control, with the understanding that any remaining shareholders would be squeezed out at a lower price in a subsequent freeze-out merger.

The two-tier offer created a prisoner's-dilemma dynamic: shareholders had to tender their shares at the front-end price (even if they preferred the target to remain independent) because the alternative — being cashed out at the back-end price — was worse. Anti-takeover statutes like Chapter 23B.19 were designed to disrupt this dynamic by imposing a moratorium on the back-end merger, thereby giving target boards leverage to negotiate with bidders and giving shareholders a real choice about whether to tender.

Washington's statute is part of the same family as Delaware Section 203 (which was enacted in 1988), Pennsylvania's control-transaction statute, Ohio's control-share statute, and many others. But Washington's is notably stricter than Delaware's on several dimensions: the moratorium is five years (Delaware's is three), the exceptions are narrower, and the permitted transactions within the moratorium are more limited.

### 11.2 Key Definitions: RCW 23B.19.020

**RCW 23B.19.020** provides the key definitions:

**"Target corporation."** A domestic corporation that has (a) voting shares registered under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, or (b) amended its articles of incorporation to elect coverage under Chapter 23B.19. Foreign corporations may also qualify as target corporations if they meet specified criteria regarding shareholder residency, employee presence, and asset location in Washington. In practice, the statute's primary application is to publicly traded Washington-chartered corporations.

**Opting in.** Private Washington corporations are not automatically covered by Chapter 23B.19. They become covered only by amending their articles to opt in. This is a significant distinction from Delaware Section 203, which automatically applies to Delaware public corporations (unless they opt out). In Washington, private corporations that want the moratorium protection must affirmatively elect it.

**"Acquiring person."** Any individual or entity (other than the target or its subsidiary) that beneficially owns voting shares representing **10% or more** of the target's voting power. Certain persons are excluded: those who owned shares before March 23, 1988 (the effective date of the statute); those who received shares through gift or inheritance; those who held shares before the target's SEC registration.

**"Significant business transaction."** A broad category that includes:

- A merger, share exchange, or consolidation of the target or any subsidiary with the acquiring person.
- A sale, lease, exchange, or other disposition of the target's assets to the acquiring person with a market value equal to 5% or more of the target's total assets or voting shares.
- The termination of 5% or more of the target's employees as a result of the transaction.
- An issuance of voting shares to the acquiring person with a market value equal to 5% or more of the target's voting shares.
- The liquidation of the target under a plan proposed or agreed to by the acquiring person.
- A reclassification, recapitalization, or merger that increases the acquiring person's proportionate ownership.

**"Beneficial owner."** A person who has the power to vote shares, to direct their disposition, or to acquire them through agreements, warrants, or options (whether exercisable immediately or later). This definition captures various forms of economic ownership that might not be reflected in the formal shareholder registry.

### 11.3 The Five-Year Moratorium: RCW 23B.19.040

**RCW 23B.19.040** establishes the core prohibition:

> *A target corporation shall not, for a period of five years following the acquiring person's share acquisition time, engage in a significant business transaction with such acquiring person.*

Five years. This is the central fact of Washington's anti-takeover regime, and it is a longer moratorium than any peer jurisdiction. Delaware imposes a three-year moratorium under Section 203; most other MBCA states that have adopted similar statutes impose three-year moratoriums as well. Washington's five-year period is an outlier that materially affects M&A planning.

### 11.4 Exceptions to the Moratorium

The statute provides three categories of exceptions:

1. **RCW 23B.19.030 exemptions.** Certain transactions are categorically excluded from the statute — for example, transactions involving the acquisition of shares from the target itself, transactions that have been approved in advance under specified procedures, and certain good-faith transactions that do not implicate the statute's anti-takeover purpose.

2. **Pre-acquisition board approval.** If the target's board of directors approved either the transaction or the acquisition of shares by the acquiring person **before** the acquiring person crossed the 10% threshold, the moratorium does not apply. This exception is critical in friendly transactions: if the acquiring person and the target negotiate a friendly deal, the target board can approve the acquisition in advance, thereby qualifying the acquiring person for the exception and avoiding the moratorium.

3. **Post-acquisition approval by board and supermajority shareholders.** If, after the acquisition, the target's board approves the transaction AND a **two-thirds** vote of the target's voting shares (excluding shares held by the acquiring person) approves the transaction, the moratorium does not apply. This is the "cleansing vote" mechanism: the majority of independent shareholders can authorize a transaction that would otherwise be blocked.

### 11.5 Fair-Price Alternative

Even after the five-year moratorium expires, Washington imposes an additional **fair-price requirement** for certain transactions. Under RCW 23B.19.040, a significant business transaction is permitted only if it meets one of the following:

- The transaction occurs more than five years after the acquiring person's share acquisition time **and** the transaction satisfies specified fair-price conditions, including (a) a minimum cash consideration equal to the highest per-share price previously paid by the acquiring person (adjusted for interest), (b) the same form of consideration as previously paid, and (c) shareholder approval at least five years after the acquisition.

The fair-price requirement is intended to prevent the acquiring person from using the five-year wait to reduce the target's stock price (through hoarding cash, skipping dividends, or other tactics) and then squeezing out the minority at a reduced price after the moratorium expires.

### 11.6 Board Response Requirement

When an acquiring person submits a written good-faith proposal for a significant business transaction, the target's board must respond in writing within 30 days (or such shorter period as the federal securities laws may require). This is a procedural requirement designed to prevent the target from simply ignoring the bidder.

### 11.7 Consequence of Violation

A significant business transaction that violates the moratorium is **void**. Not voidable — void. The transaction has no legal effect, and the parties are returned to the status quo ante. This is a severe consequence that effectively freezes hostile transactions in place during the five-year window.

### 11.8 Comparative: Delaware Section 203 and Other Peer Statutes

| Feature | Washington 23B.19 | Delaware § 203 |
|---|---|---|
| Moratorium length | 5 years | 3 years |
| Threshold | 10% | 15% |
| Coverage | Public corps + opted-in private | Public corps (unless opted out) |
| Exception: board pre-approval | Yes | Yes |
| Exception: cleansing vote | 2/3 of non-acquirer shares | 2/3 of non-acquirer shares |
| Exception: 85% acquisition in tender | No | Yes (85% of non-excluded shares) |
| Fair-price requirement after expiration | Yes | No |
| Violation effect | Void | Voidable (may be ratified) |

Washington is tougher on hostile acquirers along multiple dimensions. The longer moratorium, lower threshold, narrower exceptions, additional fair-price requirement, and void-not-voidable consequence combine to make Washington one of the least friendly jurisdictions in the United States for unfriendly takeover attempts. This was a deliberate legislative choice in 1987 and has been consistently reaffirmed by the legislature in the intervening decades.

### 11.9 Practical Implications for M&A Structuring

The five-year moratorium has several important consequences for M&A practitioners advising transactions involving Washington public corporations:

- **Friendly deals are strongly preferred.** Since hostile deals face a five-year moratorium, the economic rationality of hostile bids for Washington targets is sharply reduced. Most bidders approach Washington targets through negotiated transactions.
- **Friendly deals should secure pre-acquisition board approval.** The target board's approval of the acquisition itself (not just the subsequent merger) is the cleanest way to avoid the moratorium. This is typically documented in the deal documents with an express representation that the board has approved the acquisition under RCW 23B.19.
- **Opt-in provisions in private corporations are unusual.** Because private corporations are not automatically covered, Washington private corporations rarely opt in. Doing so would signal that the corporation expects to go public or otherwise attract potential hostile acquirers — a signal most private corporations do not want to send.
- **Proxy contests are governed separately.** The significant-business-transaction rules do not directly regulate proxy contests or the election of directors; those are governed by federal securities law and state corporate law generally. However, a successful proxy contest that installs new directors is often a prelude to a significant business transaction, and Chapter 23B.19 can complicate the endgame even when the proxy contest itself is legal.
- **Change-of-control provisions in contracts and equity awards.** Washington's long moratorium means that corporate contracts and equity awards should be drafted carefully to avoid unintended change-of-control triggers. A provision that accelerates vesting on a "change in control" can be triggered by a 10% acquisition, which might then interact with the Chapter 23B.19 moratorium in unexpected ways.

## Part 12: Compliance Calendar Synthesis

The preceding parts covered the substantive corporate law in topical groupings. This part synthesizes those rules into a practical compliance calendar — a generic 12-month cycle showing the recurring obligations of a Washington business corporation. The calendar is illustrative, not exhaustive, and must be adapted to each corporation's specific facts (fiscal year, SPC status, industry regulation, etc.).

### 12.1 Initial Compliance: Post-Formation (First 120 Days)

The corporation's first four months are dominated by onboarding compliance tasks:

| Day | Task | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Day 0 | Articles of incorporation effective | RCW 23B.02.030 |
| Day 1-30 | Organizational meeting: elect directors, adopt bylaws, appoint officers, authorize bank accounts, issue initial shares, adopt share certificate form | RCW 23B.02.050 |
| Day 1-30 | Obtain federal EIN from IRS | IRC |
| Day 1-30 | Apply for Washington Business License (UBI) with DOR; register trade names | RCW 19.02 |
| Day 1-60 | Open corporate bank account | Bank KYC policies |
| Day 1-60 | Appoint commercial registered agent (if not done in articles) | RCW 23.95.415 |
| Day 1-60 | Adopt corporate policies: indemnification, conflict of interest, code of conduct | RCW 23B.08.560 |
| Day 1-90 | Register for state employment taxes (ESD) if hiring employees | RCW 50.12 |
| Day 1-90 | Register for state workers' compensation (L&I) if hiring employees | RCW 51 |
| Day 120 | **DEADLINE: Initial report due with Secretary of State** | RCW 23.95.255(1) |

### 12.2 Annual Compliance Calendar (Generic 12-Month Cycle)

Once the corporation is established, its compliance rhythm becomes annual. The following calendar assumes a calendar-year fiscal year and a January formation date. Adjust the month references for other fiscal years.

**January.**
- Year-end close procedures, annual financial statement preparation (due by end of April under RCW 23B.16.200).
- Federal W-2s and 1099s to employees/contractors by January 31.
- Federal quarterly employment tax return (Form 941) for Q4 due by January 31.
- Washington ESD quarterly reports for Q4 due.

**February.**
- Continue annual financial statement preparation.
- Federal W-3 transmittal and 1096 transmittal due by February 28 (paper) or March 31 (electronic).
- Washington B&O tax filings (monthly or quarterly, depending on revenue threshold).

**March.**
- Federal corporate income tax returns (Form 1120 or 1120-S) due by March 15 (calendar-year S-corps) or April 15 (C-corps). Extension available.
- Begin preparing annual shareholder meeting materials (notice, proxy statement, financial statements).

**April.**
- **Annual financial statements must be prepared by end of April** (four months after fiscal year end) under RCW 23B.16.200.
- Federal estimated tax payment Q1 due.
- Washington B&O quarterly filing due.
- Federal quarterly employment tax (Form 941) for Q1 due.
- **For Social Purpose Corporations: Annual social purpose report due four months after fiscal year end** (RCW 23B.25.150). SPCs with calendar year ends must post the social purpose report on the corporation's website by April 30.

**May.**
- Annual shareholder meeting (if bylaws set meeting date in May — many corporations schedule for spring after financial statements are prepared).
- Annual meeting preparation tasks: notice of meeting (typically 10-60 days before under RCW 23B.07.050), proxy solicitation (if public company), preparation of shareholder list (RCW 23B.07.200).

**June.**
- Federal estimated tax payment Q2 due (June 15).
- Washington B&O filing as required.
- Mid-year corporate review: strategic planning, director evaluation, officer performance review.

**July.**
- Federal quarterly employment tax (Form 941) for Q2 due.
- Washington B&O quarterly filing due.
- **Annual report due to Secretary of State** (if formation anniversary is in July — the annual report is due on the anniversary month of formation).

**August.**
- Review registered agent arrangement; ensure commercial registered agent's fees are current (most commercial agents bill annually; prudent to stage fee payments at consistent points in the year).
- Review corporate records for inspection-readiness: ensure articles, bylaws, recent minutes, recent financial statements, and officer/director list are all current and accessible at the principal office.

**September.**
- Federal estimated tax payment Q3 due (September 15).
- Washington B&O filing.
- Begin preparation of year-end planning documents.

**October.**
- Federal corporate return extended deadline (October 15).
- Federal quarterly employment tax (Form 941) for Q3 due.
- Washington B&O quarterly filing due.

**November.**
- Washington B&O filing.
- Q4 and year-end planning meetings.
- Begin board meeting schedule for Q4 actions.

**December.**
- Year-end actions: declare year-end dividends (if applicable); complete year-end bonus and compensation actions; last-minute tax planning.
- Final board meeting of the year: review annual performance, approve budgets, ratify officer actions.
- Final minute book update for the year.

### 12.3 Ongoing Compliance Obligations Throughout the Year

In addition to the dated calendar, several obligations run continuously:

**Minute book maintenance.** Every board meeting, committee meeting, and shareholder meeting must be documented in minutes promptly after the meeting. Delayed minutes create risk: if years pass without minutes being prepared, reconstruction is difficult and the corporate record is impaired.

**Written consents.** When the board or shareholders act without a meeting through written consents (RCW 23B.07.040 for shareholders, RCW 23B.08.210 for directors), the consents must be executed and filed in the minute book.

**Officer and director changes.** When officers or directors change during the year, the corporate record should be updated in the minute book and, if the changes affect information in the annual report, reflected in the next annual report.

**Share transfers.** Share transfers must be recorded in the share ledger (or equivalent book-entry system) in accordance with RCW 23B.06.250.

**Registered agent communications.** Any official communications received by the registered agent must be promptly forwarded to the corporation's primary legal contact. Commercial registered agents typically provide this service as part of their annual fee.

**Responding to inspection demands.** Any shareholder inspection demand under RCW 23B.16.020 must be responded to within the statutory timeframe (five business days' notice before inspection, per RCW 23B.16.020(1)).

**Financial statement preparation.** Annual balance sheet and income statement must be prepared within four months of fiscal year end under RCW 23B.16.200, regardless of whether any shareholder has requested them.

**Changes triggering amendment or statement of change.** Changes to registered agent, principal office, directors, or officers during the year should be evaluated against the statement-of-change filing requirements; some changes require immediate filings, others are captured in the next annual report.

### 12.4 Event-Triggered Obligations

Beyond the calendar, certain events trigger immediate obligations:

| Event | Obligation | Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Registered agent resigns | Appoint replacement; file statement of change within period to avoid lapse | RCW 23.95.430, .435 |
| Registered agent changes address | Registered agent files statement of change | RCW 23.95.435 |
| Principal office changes | Reflect in next annual report, or file statement of change if material | RCW 23.95.255 |
| Corporate name changes | File articles of amendment | RCW 23B.10.020(5) |
| Board authorizes amendment to articles | File articles of amendment | RCW 23B.10.060 |
| Board authorizes merger | File articles of merger | RCW 23B.11A.050 |
| Shareholders approve dissolution | File articles of dissolution; obtain revenue clearance; publish notice | RCW 23B.14.030, .060-.070 |
| Corporation becomes administratively dissolved | Assess reinstatement; 60-day cure period | RCW 23.95.610 |
| Corporation receives shareholder inspection demand | Prepare records; respond within 5 business days | RCW 23B.16.020 |
| Corporation receives dissenter's notice of intent | Prepare dissenters' notice per RCW 23B.13.200 | Chapter 23B.13 |

### 12.5 Compliance Failures and Remediation Paths

**Failure to file annual report.**
- *Remedy:* File the delinquent annual report and pay current fees. If 60-day cure notice has not yet been issued, late filing cures the deficiency without further action.
- *Escalation:* If the Secretary of State has issued a notice of grounds for administrative dissolution, the 60-day cure period applies. Cure requires filing the report and any additional documentation needed.
- *Terminal stage:* If administrative dissolution has occurred, reinstatement is available for five years under RCW 23.95.615.

**Failure to maintain registered agent.**
- *Remedy:* Appoint a new registered agent and file a statement of change.
- *Escalation:* Same 60-day cure and reinstatement path as annual report failure.

**Failure to prepare financial statements.**
- *Remedy:* Prepare the statements retroactively. No statutory filing requirement exists, but shareholders may demand them.
- *Escalation:* Shareholder inspection litigation under RCW 23B.16.020 / .040.

**Failure to hold annual shareholder meeting.**
- *Remedy:* Hold the meeting as soon as practicable. Washington law does not impose a direct penalty for failure to hold an annual meeting, but failure for two consecutive years can trigger judicial dissolution on shareholder deadlock grounds (RCW 23B.14.300).
- *Escalation:* Any shareholder may petition the superior court for an order compelling a meeting (RCW 23B.07.030).

**Failure to maintain minute book.**
- *Remedy:* Reconstruct the minutes from available records (board resolutions, notice of meetings, financial records). Engage counsel to document the reconstruction.
- *Escalation:* Impaired minute book is evidence of corporate disregard, potentially supporting piercing the corporate veil in litigation. Directors and officers may face personal liability if the corporate formalities are found to have been ignored.

**Failure to file tax returns (DOR, ESD, L&I, IRS).**
- *Remedy:* File delinquent returns with applicable penalties and interest. Engage a tax professional to negotiate penalty abatement where possible.
- *Escalation:* Liens, levies, and enforcement actions by the revenue agencies. For sufficient delinquencies, administrative dissolution may follow.

**Failure to obtain revenue clearance before dissolution.**
- *Remedy:* Cannot file articles of dissolution without the clearance certificate. Must resolve underlying tax issues with DOR before dissolution can proceed.
- *Escalation:* Dissolution is blocked until DOR issues clearance. Long-running tax disputes can keep a corporation in indefinite limbo.

### 12.6 Recommendations for Compliance Infrastructure

Practitioners advising Washington corporations on compliance should consider establishing the following infrastructure:

1. **Compliance calendar.** A living calendar tracking all statutory deadlines, tax deadlines, and internal deadlines. Ideally integrated with the corporation's general calendar system and with automated reminders well in advance of each deadline.

2. **Corporate records platform.** A centralized repository for the corporate minute book, stock ledger, and key documents (articles, bylaws, material contracts). Cloud-based corporate records platforms have become the norm for sophisticated corporations; paper minute books should be the exception.

3. **Registered agent relationship.** A commercial registered agent (or a carefully selected individual agent) with reliable communication and clear escalation procedures. Avoid using a founder's home address as the registered office.

4. **Relationship with Secretary of State's CCFS.** Establish online access to the Corporations and Charities Filing System for the person responsible for corporate filings. This enables prompt filings, searches, and certificate requests without paper.

5. **Annual governance calendar.** A schedule of annual board and shareholder meetings, committee meetings, officer review, and strategic planning sessions, synchronized with the compliance calendar.

6. **Board package template.** Standardized board meeting packages that include agenda, financial statements, management updates, and resolutions. A consistent package format improves minute quality and supports the director's duty-of-care defense.

7. **Counsel relationship.** A designated outside counsel (or in-house counsel for larger corporations) responsible for annual compliance review, amendment drafting, and extraordinary-transaction planning. Many compliance failures result from the absence of regular counsel touchpoints rather than from willful neglect.

8. **Document retention policy.** A written retention policy that specifies retention periods for each document category, identifies custodians, and establishes destruction procedures. The permanent-retention categories (articles, bylaws, minute book, stock ledger) should be explicitly protected from destruction.

9. **Litigation hold procedures.** Procedures to suspend the retention policy when litigation is reasonably anticipated, to prevent spoliation claims. This is a discipline unrelated to corporate law proper but essential for defensive posture.

10. **Change management procedures.** Procedures for managing changes to registered agent, officers, directors, principal office, and other routine changes, ensuring that each change triggers the appropriate filing and recording tasks.

**Practitioner commentary on fees.** The Washington fee structure is middle-of-the-pack among U.S. states: not as low as states that deliberately court out-of-state formations (Wyoming, Nevada, New Mexico), but substantially lower than Delaware's combination of initial filing fees and annual franchise tax. The annual report fee of $70 for business corporations is flat and does not scale with authorized shares or net worth — a notable simplification compared with Delaware's franchise tax, which can run into six figures annually for large corporations.

**Revenue clearance certificate fee.** Obtaining the revenue clearance certificate required for dissolution under RCW 23B.14.030 carries no direct fee from DOR, but the corporation must be current on all its tax returns and payments to obtain the certificate. Corporations with complex tax histories should budget time and professional fees for tax cleanup before seeking dissolution.

**Department of Revenue fees.** Washington's Business Licensing Service charges a non-refundable Business License Application processing fee (approximately $19 as of the most recent schedule) plus a fee for each trade name (approximately $5 per name). These fees are separate from the Secretary of State's fees and are paid to DOR.

**County and city fees.** Many Washington cities require their own business licenses in addition to the state license. Seattle, for example, requires a City of Seattle Business License Tax Certificate separate from the state BLS. These city-level obligations are not within the scope of this chapter but are important components of a complete compliance checklist.

### 13.2 The Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS)

The Secretary of State's online portal for corporate filings is the **Corporations and Charities Filing System (CCFS)**. As of the most recent platform generation, CCFS supports:

- **Entity search.** Free public search of corporate records by entity name, UBI number, or filing number.
- **Online filings.** Direct submission of articles of incorporation, amendments, annual reports, mergers, dissolutions, and other standard filings.
- **Account-based access.** Authorized users can link entities to their accounts for easier repeat filings.
- **Document download.** Historical filings for most entities are available as PDFs, typically back through the late 1990s.
- **Status checks.** Real-time entity status (active, delinquent, administratively dissolved).
- **Certificate requests.** Certificate of existence, certificate of formation, and other official documents can be requested and delivered electronically.

**Access strategy for counsel.** Counsel representing multiple Washington corporations should establish a CCFS account linked to all client entities. This allows prompt filings, search across the client portfolio, and quick retrieval of historical documents. Some large law firms maintain dedicated paralegals with CCFS access and expertise.

**Paper filings remain available.** Despite the electronic system, paper filings are still accepted for most transactions. Paper filings are slower and carry the same fees; there is no advantage to paper except in specialized cases (unusual filings, accommodations for parties without internet access).

### 13.3 Deeper Treatment of Indemnification and D&O Insurance

While indemnification is not directly within the scope of this compliance-and-lifecycle chapter, it arises so frequently in connection with the amendment and merger topics that a brief treatment is warranted.

**Statutory framework.** RCW 23B.08.510 through .600 set the Washington indemnification framework. The statute distinguishes:

- **Mandatory indemnification** (RCW 23B.08.520) — the corporation must indemnify a director or officer who is "wholly successful, on the merits or otherwise," in the defense of a proceeding.
- **Permissive indemnification** (RCW 23B.08.510) — the corporation may indemnify in other cases if the person acted in good faith and reasonably believed the conduct was in (or not opposed to) the corporation's best interests.
- **Indemnification for expenses** (RCW 23B.08.520(2)) — limited circumstances where expenses are indemnifiable even if substantive liability is not.
- **Court-ordered indemnification** (RCW 23B.08.540) — a court may order indemnification in cases where the corporation has declined, if the court finds that indemnification is fair and reasonable.

**Advancement of expenses** (RCW 23B.08.530) — the corporation may advance expenses in advance of final disposition, subject to an undertaking to repay if the person is ultimately found not to have met the applicable standard of conduct.

**Charter and bylaw expansion.** RCW 23B.08.560 permits the articles or bylaws to expand indemnification beyond the statutory default, subject to limitations (no indemnification for intentional misconduct, knowing violations of law, improper personal benefit, or unlawful distributions under RCW 23B.08.310).

**D&O insurance.** Most corporations supplement statutory indemnification with Directors and Officers (D&O) liability insurance. D&O policies typically cover indemnification obligations (Side A), direct claims against directors and officers (Side B), and sometimes entity claims (Side C). Public companies uniformly carry D&O; private companies increasingly do as well, especially after a first round of venture financing.

**Why this matters for lifecycle events.** Mergers, amendments, and dissolutions frequently generate claims against directors — breach of fiduciary duty claims in mergers, challenges to amendments, claims of improper distributions in dissolution. A corporation without adequate indemnification provisions and D&O insurance enters these transactions with elevated risk; counsel should confirm the indemnification and insurance posture before closing any significant transaction.

### 13.4 The Washington Equivalent of Delaware's "Revlon Duties"

Washington has not separately codified the Delaware Revlon doctrine (Revlon, Inc. v. MacAndrews & Forbes Holdings, 506 A.2d 173 (Del. 1986)), under which a target board's duties shift from preserving the corporation's long-term value to maximizing shareholder value when a sale becomes inevitable. But the doctrine is understood by Washington practitioners to inform the fiduciary-duty analysis in Washington target-board decisions.

**Washington's business judgment rule.** RCW 23B.08.300 establishes the director duty of care: to act in good faith, with the care an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances, and in a manner the director reasonably believes is in the best interests of the corporation. This is a standard business-judgment-rule formulation, derived from the MBCA.

**Intermediate scrutiny in control transactions.** Washington courts have not developed a separate "intermediate scrutiny" framework for target-board decisions in control transactions comparable to Delaware's Unocal/Revlon line. But in practice, Washington courts reviewing target-board decisions in M&A cases look to Delaware jurisprudence for guidance, and the Unocal/Revlon framework is treated as persuasive authority.

**Practical implications.** Target boards in Washington mergers should conduct themselves as if Unocal and Revlon applied: engage financial advisors, consider alternatives, document the decision-making process, take measures to obtain the best available price once a sale becomes inevitable, and avoid defensive measures that cannot be justified on proper corporate purpose grounds.

### 13.5 The Role of the Chief Executive Officer in Filings

An underappreciated point: most Washington corporate filings are signed by an officer — typically the president or secretary — not by the directors directly. The signature on an articles of amendment, articles of merger, or articles of dissolution is typically that of an officer certifying the action taken by the board and (where required) the shareholders.

**Who may sign.** The WBCA does not specify which officer must sign each type of filing. Bylaws typically designate the president and secretary as the authorized signatories. A corporation with unusual officer structures should include an authorized-signatory provision in its bylaws or board resolutions to prevent filing delays.

**Officer authority and the corporation's obligations.** An officer who signs a filing without actual authority creates risk. The corporation may be bound to the filing as against third parties, but the officer may face internal liability for exceeding authority. Corporations should adopt clear policies and documentation procedures for officer signatures on filings.

**The secretary's role.** The corporate secretary traditionally serves as the custodian of the corporate records and as the primary signatory on filings that certify corporate action. This role is distinct from the administrative-assistant role sometimes associated with the "secretary" title in corporate hierarchies; the corporate secretary is a fiduciary officer with meaningful legal responsibilities.

### 13.6 Beneficial Ownership Reporting (Corporate Transparency Act)

The federal Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), enacted in 2021 and taking effect in 2024, imposes beneficial ownership reporting obligations on most U.S. corporations and LLCs. While the CTA is federal law (administered by the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network, FinCEN) and not directly within the scope of Washington state corporate law, every Washington corporation must consider its CTA obligations.

**What the CTA requires.** Covered entities must file a Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) report with FinCEN identifying each "beneficial owner" — generally, any individual who directly or indirectly owns 25% or more of the entity or exercises substantial control over it. Reports must be filed within 30 days of formation (for new entities) or on a periodic basis (for existing entities, initial report due in 2024 or 2025 depending on formation date).

**Exemptions.** The CTA exempts publicly traded companies, certain regulated entities (banks, broker-dealers, insurance companies), large operating companies (over 20 employees, over $5 million in gross receipts, and physical presence in the U.S.), and certain inactive entities. Most small and mid-sized Washington corporations are NOT exempt and must file.

**Washington's response.** Washington state has not enacted a separate beneficial ownership reporting regime at the state level. The CTA is the sole beneficial ownership reporting obligation for most Washington corporations. Counsel should advise clients of the CTA obligations as part of the standard formation and annual compliance checklist.

**Status as of this writing.** CTA implementation has been contested in federal court, with several injunctions, stays, and reinstatements occurring since the effective date. Practitioners should verify the current status and the applicability of the CTA to each client at the time of each new formation or annual review.

### 13.7 The Law Governing Internal Affairs — Choice of Law

A recurring question in multi-state corporate transactions is which state's law governs the internal affairs of a Washington corporation. The traditional rule — the "internal affairs doctrine" — provides that the law of the state of incorporation governs the corporation's internal affairs, regardless of where the corporation does business or where its directors, officers, and shareholders reside. For a Washington corporation, this means that Washington law applies to:

- The rights and obligations of shareholders.
- The duties of directors and officers.
- The validity of corporate actions (amendments, mergers, dissolutions).
- The appraisal remedy (dissenters' rights).
- The standards for judicial dissolution.

**Limits of the doctrine.** The internal affairs doctrine does not preclude the application of another state's law to matters that are not purely "internal" — for example, tort claims by third parties, contract disputes, employment matters, tax liability, and regulatory compliance. A Washington corporation operating in California is subject to California employment law, California tax law, and California tort law, even though its internal governance is governed by Washington law.

**California's challenge to the internal affairs doctrine.** California has enacted statutes that purport to apply certain California corporate law provisions to "pseudo-foreign corporations" — out-of-state corporations with a majority of shareholders and operations in California. These statutes (California Corporations Code § 2115) have been challenged on federal constitutional grounds and their application is contested. A Washington corporation with a substantial California presence should evaluate whether California's pseudo-foreign statute applies and whether compliance with both regimes is feasible.

**Planning implications.** The choice to incorporate in Washington (versus Delaware, Nevada, or another state) commits the corporation to Washington's internal affairs rules for the life of the corporation (or until a conversion or re-domestication). Founders should consider the choice carefully at formation, and counsel should revisit the choice in connection with significant corporate events (financing rounds, pre-IPO planning, acquisition preparation). Re-domestication from Washington to Delaware (or vice versa) is typically accomplished by a cross-border conversion or a merger with a newly formed shell in the target state.

### 13.8 Pre-IPO Governance Changes

Washington corporations preparing for an IPO typically undertake a series of pre-IPO governance changes. While an IPO itself is a securities law transaction governed primarily by federal law, the accompanying corporate changes implicate state corporate law in important ways.

**Typical pre-IPO amendments:**

- **Reclassification of capital structure.** Conversion of preferred stock into common stock (or into new classes of common); creation of a single or dual-class common stock structure; elimination of anti-dilution adjustments that are inconsistent with public company status.
- **Charter opt-ins and opt-outs.** Adoption (or opt-out) of specific governance provisions such as staggered boards, anti-takeover provisions, forum selection clauses, exclusive forum provisions for intra-corporate disputes, and limitations on shareholder action by written consent.
- **Chapter 23B.19 opt-in or opt-out.** A pre-IPO Washington corporation that has not been covered by Chapter 23B.19 (because it is not publicly traded and has not opted in) will automatically become covered upon registering under the Securities Exchange Act. Boards should evaluate whether to opt out of Chapter 23B.19 before the IPO; however, opting out is controversial and often resisted by underwriters and institutional investors.
- **Indemnification and D&O provisions.** Expansion of indemnification provisions to the maximum extent permitted by law; adoption of separate indemnification agreements with directors and officers; purchase of public-company D&O insurance with appropriate Side A coverage.
- **Committee structures.** Formation of audit, compensation, and nominating/corporate governance committees meeting the listing standards of the target exchange.
- **Officer and director recruitment.** Addition of independent directors sufficient to meet listing standards and to populate the required committees.
- **Code of business conduct.** Adoption of a code of business conduct and ethics; implementation of whistleblower policies.

**Timing.** Pre-IPO governance changes should be adopted well in advance of the IPO filing — typically six to twelve months before the initial S-1 filing — to allow time for underwriter review, SEC comment, and any necessary additional changes. Last-minute governance changes during the IPO process create risk and can delay the transaction.

**Dissenters' rights trap.** A pre-IPO restructuring that involves an amendment triggering dissenters' rights under RCW 23B.13.020 can create appraisal risk. For example, an amendment that effects a redemption or cancellation of a class of shares (triggering dissenters' rights under RCW 23B.13.020(4)) can generate dissent demands from pre-IPO shareholders who believe they are being undervalued. Counsel should structure pre-IPO restructurings to minimize dissenters' rights triggers and should be prepared to respond promptly to any dissenter demands that arise.

### 13.9 Close Corporation Agreements Under RCW 23B.07.320

**RCW 23B.07.320** authorizes shareholders of a Washington corporation to enter into a **shareholders' agreement** that deviates from the default corporate governance rules in a wide variety of ways. Such agreements are particularly important in closely held corporations where the founders want to establish governance arrangements tailored to their specific needs.

**What a shareholders' agreement can do.** A shareholders' agreement under RCW 23B.07.320 may:

1. **Eliminate the board** or restrict the discretion or powers of the board.
2. **Govern the authorization or making of distributions.**
3. **Establish who will be directors or officers** or their terms of office or manner of selection or removal.
4. **Govern the exercise or division of voting power.**
5. **Establish the terms of any contract** between the corporation and a shareholder, director, officer, or employee.
6. **Transfer to one or more shareholders** or other persons all or part of the authority to exercise corporate powers, manage the business, or govern the authorization or making of distributions.
7. **Require dissolution** of the corporation at the request of one or more of the shareholders or upon the occurrence of a specified event or contingency.
8. **Any other matter** related to the exercise of corporate powers, the management of the business, or the relationship among shareholders, directors, or the corporation, that is not expressly prohibited by law.

**Limitations.** Section 23B.07.320 provides that a shareholders' agreement is not effective if it is contrary to any required provisions of the articles of incorporation, fails to meet formal requirements (must be set forth in the articles, in the bylaws, or in a written agreement signed by all shareholders and made known to the corporation), or would impose on non-party shareholders or third parties an obligation they have not agreed to.

**Effect on fiduciary duty.** The statute preserves fiduciary duties of directors and officers but permits the allocation of governance responsibility among shareholders to displace some of the director-level duties. When shareholders themselves exercise management authority, they may assume associated duties to the corporation and to other shareholders.

**Termination.** A shareholders' agreement typically terminates automatically when the corporation becomes a public company (shares listed on a national securities exchange or regularly traded in a market) or at the specified term. Termination rules should be specified in the agreement itself.

**Practitioner commentary.** The shareholders' agreement is one of the most flexible planning tools in the Washington corporate toolkit. It is the primary mechanism by which closely held corporations accommodate the specific needs of their founders — deadlock resolution, buy-sell arrangements, special voting on specified matters, restrictions on share transfers, employment and compensation commitments, successor planning. Counsel should consider whether a shareholders' agreement is appropriate at formation and at each subsequent round of corporate events.

### 13.10 Restrictions on Transfer of Shares

Closely held corporations frequently impose restrictions on the transfer of shares to preserve the closely held character of the corporation, control who becomes a shareholder, provide liquidity to departing shareholders, and implement buy-sell arrangements. These restrictions are governed by RCW 23B.06.270.

**Permissible restrictions.** RCW 23B.06.270 permits restrictions on share transfers that are:

1. **Reasonable** in scope and duration.
2. **Noted conspicuously** on the share certificate (or, for uncertificated shares, in the initial transaction statement delivered to the shareholder).

**Common restriction types:**

- **Right of first refusal** — the corporation or other shareholders have the option to purchase shares offered for sale before the selling shareholder may sell to a third party.
- **Right of first offer** — the selling shareholder must first offer shares to the corporation or other shareholders; if they decline, the shareholder may sell to a third party on terms no less favorable than those offered.
- **Mandatory buy-sell on specified events** — the shareholder must sell (and the corporation or other shareholders must buy) upon the occurrence of specified events such as death, disability, divorce, termination of employment, or insolvency.
- **Prohibition on certain transferees** — shareholders may not transfer shares to competitors, to persons with conflicting interests, or to certain classes of individuals or entities.
- **Consent requirements** — transfers require the consent of the board or of a specified percentage of shareholders.

**Enforceability.** Restrictions must be reasonable under the circumstances. A restriction that is unduly harsh — for example, one that entirely prohibits transfers without providing any exit mechanism — may be unenforceable as an unreasonable restraint on alienation. The reasonableness analysis typically considers the purpose of the restriction, the scope of the restriction, the valuation mechanism (if the restriction includes a forced sale), and the presence of alternative exits.

**Buy-sell valuation.** When a restriction includes a mandatory buy-sell provision, the valuation mechanism is critical. Common approaches:

- **Fixed formula** (book value, multiples of earnings, specified dollar amount updated periodically).
- **Appraisal** (independent appraisal on triggering event, often with alternative procedures if the parties dispute the appraisal).
- **Agreed price** (parties meet annually to agree on a price; if they fail to agree, an alternative mechanism applies).

The choice of valuation mechanism has significant implications for tax (estate tax valuation, income tax treatment of the buyer and seller) and for dispute resolution.

**Notice on certificates.** Restrictions must be noted on the share certificate to be enforceable against transferees. The notation should be clear, legible, and specific enough to put transferees on notice of the nature and source of the restrictions. A common formulation: "The transfer of the shares represented by this certificate is restricted by the provisions of a shareholders' agreement dated [date], a copy of which is on file at the corporation's principal office."

### 13.11 Shareholder Meetings — Formalities and Pitfalls

Annual and special shareholder meetings are governed by Chapter 23B.07 RCW. The key provisions:

**Annual meeting (RCW 23B.07.010).** The corporation must hold an annual meeting of shareholders at a time stated in or fixed in accordance with the bylaws. The annual meeting is traditionally the forum for electing directors and conducting other ordinary shareholder business.

**Failure to hold annual meeting.** Failure to hold an annual meeting does not invalidate corporate acts or work a forfeiture of corporate existence. However, it may give rise to judicial intervention: any shareholder entitled to vote may petition the superior court for an order compelling a meeting (RCW 23B.07.030). Failure for two consecutive years is also a statutory ground for judicial dissolution on shareholder-deadlock grounds (RCW 23B.14.300).

**Special meetings (RCW 23B.07.020).** Special meetings may be called by the board, by persons authorized by the articles or bylaws to call such meetings, or (in public companies, subject to specified thresholds) by shareholders holding a specified percentage of voting power. Bylaws typically specify the procedures for calling special meetings.

**Notice (RCW 23B.07.050).** Notice of each meeting must be given to each shareholder entitled to vote, no fewer than 10 nor more than 60 days before the meeting date. The notice must state the date, time, and place of the meeting. For special meetings, the notice must also state the purpose or purposes for which the meeting is called. For meetings at which any of certain fundamental transactions will be voted on — amendments, mergers, share exchanges, asset sales, or dissolution — the notice must additionally identify the transaction and include or be accompanied by the relevant documents.

**Record date (RCW 23B.07.070).** The record date for determining shareholders entitled to notice and to vote is fixed by the board. If the board does not fix a record date, the record date is the day before the notice is delivered. The record date may not be more than 70 days before the meeting date.

**Quorum (RCW 23B.07.250).** The default quorum for a shareholder meeting is a majority of the votes entitled to be cast on the matter by the voting group. The articles or bylaws may require a greater quorum (up to unanimity) but may not permit a lower quorum (articles may, in limited circumstances, specify less than a majority; see RCW 23B.07.270).

**Voting (RCW 23B.07.250).** For most matters, the default voting threshold at a meeting is a majority of votes cast at the meeting (assuming a quorum). The 2024 c 22 majority-vote amendment affects the default for certain fundamental transactions — amendments, mergers, asset sales, and dissolution — which require approval by a majority (for post-August 2024 corporations) or two-thirds (for pre-August 2024 corporations) of the votes entitled to be cast, not merely the votes cast. This distinction between "votes entitled to be cast" and "votes cast" is important: the former includes abstentions and non-votes, while the latter does not.

**Action by written consent (RCW 23B.07.040).** Shareholders may take action without a meeting if all shareholders entitled to vote consent in writing. (For private corporations, the default is unanimous consent; the articles may permit action by consent of the minimum number of votes that would be required to approve the action at a meeting.) The written consent must be maintained in the corporate records.

**Electronic meetings.** Washington law permits shareholder meetings to be held by electronic means if authorized by the bylaws or by the board. During the COVID-19 pandemic, electronic-only meetings became common; the practice has continued in many corporations. Electronic meetings must provide shareholders with a reasonable opportunity to participate, including the ability to hear the proceedings, to ask questions, and to vote.

### 13.12 Board Meetings — Formalities and Pitfalls

Board meetings are governed by Chapter 23B.08 RCW. The core provisions parallel the shareholder meeting rules but with somewhat different defaults.

**Regular meetings (RCW 23B.08.200).** Regular board meetings may be held without notice of the date, time, place, or purpose if the bylaws so provide. In practice, boards typically adopt a schedule of regular meetings at the beginning of each year.

**Special meetings.** Special board meetings require notice of the date, time, and place at least two days in advance (unless the articles or bylaws specify otherwise). The notice need not specify the purpose of a special meeting unless required by the articles or bylaws.

**Waiver of notice (RCW 23B.08.220).** A director may waive notice of a meeting, either in writing or by attending the meeting without objecting to lack of notice.

**Quorum (RCW 23B.08.240).** The default quorum for a board meeting is a majority of the fixed number of directors (or, if no fixed number, a majority of the number of directors in office). The articles or bylaws may require a greater quorum (up to unanimity) but may not require fewer than one-third of the directors.

**Voting (RCW 23B.08.240).** The default voting threshold for board action is a majority of directors present at a meeting at which a quorum is present. The articles or bylaws may require a greater vote.

**Action by written consent (RCW 23B.08.210).** The board may take action without a meeting if all directors consent in writing. Unlike the shareholder consent rule, there is no provision for less-than-unanimous written consent at the board level — all directors must consent.

**Telephonic and electronic meetings (RCW 23B.08.200).** Directors may participate in a board meeting by means of a conference telephone or similar communications equipment that allows all persons participating in the meeting to hear each other at the same time. Participation by such means constitutes presence in person at the meeting.

**Committees (RCW 23B.08.250).** The board may create committees and delegate certain authorities to them. Certain fundamental actions — approving amendments, mergers, dissolution, distributions, issuance of shares — cannot be delegated to a committee. This limitation preserves the full board's authority over the most important corporate decisions.

**Documentation and minutes.** Board actions should be documented in minutes approved at a subsequent meeting (or in the written consent, for actions without a meeting). Minutes should identify the directors present, the matters considered, the resolutions adopted, and the vote on each resolution. Well-drafted minutes support the business judgment rule by demonstrating that the directors were informed, engaged, and acted in good faith.

### 13.13 The Stock Ledger and Share Issuance

The **stock ledger** (also called the share transfer ledger or stock record book) is the official record of share ownership. It identifies each shareholder, the number and class of shares held, the date of acquisition, the certificate number (for certificated shares), and the consideration paid. The stock ledger is required under RCW 23B.16.010(4) and is the definitive source for determining share ownership for voting, dividend, and other purposes.

**Certificated vs. uncertificated shares.** Washington corporations may issue certificated shares (paper certificates) or uncertificated shares (book-entry only). RCW 23B.06.260 requires that uncertificated shareholders receive a written statement containing information equivalent to that required on a share certificate.

**Issuance of shares (RCW 23B.06.210).** Shares are issued when the corporation receives consideration for the shares as approved by the board. Consideration may be cash, property, or past services (with some limitations on future services). The board's determination of the adequacy of consideration is binding if made in good faith.

**Par value and stated capital.** Washington permits par value shares but does not require them. RCW 23B.06.210 eliminates most of the traditional par-value restrictions; boards need not concern themselves with the old rules about issuing shares below par. However, par-value provisions may still appear in legacy corporations' articles and must be respected until amended.

**Stock splits and reverse splits.** Under RCW 23B.10.020(4), the board may (in a single-class corporation) effect a forward or reverse stock split without shareholder approval. The split is accomplished by a board resolution and an articles amendment that adjusts the number of authorized and outstanding shares proportionally.

**Treasury shares.** Washington does not recognize "treasury shares" as a separate category. Shares reacquired by the corporation are considered authorized but unissued shares and may be re-issued without additional authorization. This is a simplification from the older treasury-share framework.

### 13.14 The Interaction Between Title 23B and Chapter 23.95

A recurring challenge for practitioners new to Washington corporate law is understanding how Title 23B (the WBCA) interacts with Chapter 23.95 (the WUBOC). The following summary may be useful:

**Title 23B (WBCA) contains:**

- Substantive corporate governance rules specific to business corporations.
- Director duties and liabilities.
- Shareholder voting rules and voting thresholds (the 2024 dichotomy).
- Amendment procedures.
- Merger and share exchange procedures.
- Asset sale procedures.
- Dissenters' rights.
- Voluntary and judicial dissolution.
- Indemnification.
- Significant business transactions (anti-takeover).
- Social purpose corporation rules (Chapter 23B.25).

**Chapter 23.95 (WUBOC) contains:**

- Rules applicable to all entity types (corporations, LLCs, partnerships, limited partnerships, etc.).
- Naming rules (Article 3).
- Registered agent rules (Article 4).
- Filing mechanics (Article 2).
- Annual report obligations.
- Administrative dissolution procedures and grounds.
- Reinstatement procedures.
- Foreign entity qualification and withdrawal.
- Cross-entity conversion rules (Article 9).

**Cross-reference pattern.** Title 23B often cross-references Chapter 23.95 for topics that the WUBOC handles uniformly. For example, RCW 23B.04.010 says only that "A corporate name must comply with the requirements of Article 3 of chapter 23.95 RCW." The substantive rules are in 23.95, and Title 23B acts as a gateway.

**Rule of thumb.** When researching a Washington corporate law question, check both chapters. If the topic is about how the corporation is governed internally (who votes, what the vote must be, what approvals are required), the answer is likely in Title 23B. If the topic is about how the corporation interacts with the Secretary of State (naming, registered agent, annual report, administrative dissolution, reinstatement), the answer is likely in Chapter 23.95.

### 13.15 Washington Corporate Law in the National Context

Washington is neither a "hub" state for out-of-state incorporations (like Delaware) nor an "outlier" state that has adopted an idiosyncratic framework (like Nevada or Wyoming). It is a Model Business Corporation Act jurisdiction with several distinctive features:

**MBCA origin.** Title 23B RCW was adopted in 1989, based largely on the 1984 revision of the Model Business Corporation Act. Subsequent amendments have tracked MBCA updates, with lag and with Washington-specific variations. Washington is therefore "mainstream" in the sense that its framework will be familiar to lawyers trained in MBCA jurisdictions.

**Delaware as persuasive authority.** Washington courts routinely cite Delaware case law on corporate governance issues, particularly in areas where Washington authority is sparse (fiduciary duty, entire fairness, Unocal/Revlon, entire fairness in controlling shareholder transactions, fair value in appraisal). This is not because Washington is formally "following" Delaware but because Delaware case law is the most developed corporate jurisprudence in the United States and provides useful analytical frameworks.

**Distinctive Washington features.**

1. **The 2024 formation-date dichotomy** — unique to Washington among MBCA jurisdictions.
2. **The numeric "significant continuing business" test** in RCW 23B.12.020 — a Washington innovation that most other states have not adopted.
3. **The five-year moratorium in Chapter 23B.19** — longer than any peer jurisdiction's anti-takeover statute.
4. **The social purpose corporation regime in Chapter 23B.25** — earlier and looser than Delaware's public benefit corporation.
5. **The WUBOC (Chapter 23.95) consolidation** — most states have not consolidated their naming, registered agent, and administrative dissolution rules across entity types.

**Implications for planning.** Washington is a reasonable choice for the incorporation of Washington-based businesses, particularly closely held and family-owned corporations that value local counsel familiarity and the reasonable fee structure. It is less commonly chosen by venture-backed companies planning a Delaware re-domestication before IPO, primarily because of the distinctive features (longer moratorium, formation-date dichotomy) that create complexity relative to Delaware's more familiar framework. Counsel should discuss the Washington-vs.-Delaware choice with founders at formation; the right answer depends on business specifics, not on a general preference.

### 13.16 Foreign Qualification in Washington

Out-of-state corporations that transact business in Washington must obtain a **certificate of authority** from the Washington Secretary of State under RCW 23.95.505 and following sections. The qualification requirement exists to ensure that out-of-state entities doing business in Washington are subject to the same regulatory and service-of-process requirements as domestic entities.

**What counts as "transacting business."** Washington's definition of "transacting business" (which triggers the qualification requirement) is similar to the MBCA formulation. RCW 23.95.520 lists activities that do NOT constitute transacting business, including:

- Maintaining or defending legal proceedings.
- Holding meetings of directors, shareholders, or members.
- Maintaining bank accounts.
- Selling through independent contractors.
- Soliciting or procuring orders (if those orders require acceptance outside Washington).
- Creating or acquiring indebtedness, mortgages, and security interests in real or personal property.
- Securing or collecting debts or enforcing mortgages and security interests in property securing such debts.
- Owning, without more, real or personal property.
- Conducting an isolated transaction completed within 30 days and not in the course of a series of similar transactions.

Activities not on this list — such as having employees in Washington, maintaining an office, advertising locally, or engaging in recurring in-state transactions — generally do constitute transacting business.

**Consequences of unauthorized transaction of business.** A foreign corporation that transacts business in Washington without a certificate of authority faces several consequences:

1. **Cannot maintain suit.** The foreign corporation cannot maintain a proceeding in any Washington court until it obtains a certificate of authority.
2. **Statutory penalty.** The corporation may be subject to a statutory penalty for the period of unauthorized activity.
3. **Individual liability.** In certain cases, officers or agents who transact business in the name of the unauthorized corporation may be personally liable for obligations incurred.

**Note:** Unauthorized transaction of business does NOT invalidate the underlying contracts; the corporation's substantive rights are preserved, only the procedural ability to sue is suspended. The corporation can cure by obtaining a certificate of authority and filing all required back annual reports.

**Withdrawal.** A foreign corporation that ceases to transact business in Washington may withdraw by filing a certificate of withdrawal under RCW 23.95.550. Withdrawal ends the qualification and terminates the registered agent obligation. Failure to withdraw leaves the corporation exposed to ongoing annual report obligations and potential administrative revocation of the certificate of authority.

### 13.17 The Social Purpose Corporation — A Closer Look

Washington's Social Purpose Corporation (SPC) regime, codified at Chapter 23B.25 RCW, deserves closer treatment because it is often misunderstood and because it interacts with the general WBCA rules in specific ways.

**Formation.** An SPC is formed either by filing articles that designate the entity as a social purpose corporation at the outset, or by amending an existing business corporation's articles to elect SPC status under RCW 23B.25.005. The articles must identify the corporation's social purpose or purposes, which must be lawful and articulated with reasonable specificity.

**Permitted social purposes.** The statute is permissive about what qualifies as a social purpose. Recognized categories include (but are not limited to):

- Promotion of positive short- or long-term effects on the corporation's employees, suppliers, customers, or creditors.
- Promotion of positive effects on the community, society, or environment.
- Promotion of the arts, sciences, or advancement of knowledge.
- Promotion of any other specific public or charitable purpose.

The articles may state one or more social purposes, and the specificity of the statement is left to the incorporators.

**Director duties.** An SPC director's duty of care is modified by the social purpose: the director "may" (not "must") consider the social purpose in addition to the interests of shareholders. This is a permissive expansion of fiduciary duty, not a mandatory reallocation. The director is not required to prioritize the social purpose over shareholder interests; the director is permitted to do so if the director believes it advances the long-term interests of the corporation.

**Annual social purpose report.** RCW 23B.25.150 requires the SPC's board to cause an annual social purpose report to be furnished to shareholders by posting it on the corporation's website (free of charge) within **four months after the close of the corporation's fiscal year**. The report must include a narrative discussion of the corporation's social purpose efforts during the fiscal year, short-term and long-term objectives, material actions taken, material actions expected, and the measures used to evaluate performance.

**Failure to file.** The statute provides that failure to furnish a social purpose report does not affect the validity of any corporate action. This is notable: unlike the annual report to the Secretary of State (which can lead to administrative dissolution), the social purpose report is a reporting obligation with limited enforcement consequences. Nevertheless, failure to report undermines the SPC's credibility with stakeholders and may give rise to shareholder derivative claims for breach of statutory duty.

**Ceasing to be an SPC.** An SPC may cease to be a social purpose corporation by amending its articles to remove the SPC designation. The amendment requires the ordinary amendment vote thresholds (two-thirds for pre-August 2024 corporations, majority for post-August 2024 corporations) with additional voting group rights for certain classes of shareholders. Converting back to an ordinary business corporation is treated as a major change and may trigger dissenters' rights for opposing shareholders.

**Comparison to Delaware Public Benefit Corporation.** Delaware's PBC regime (enacted in 2013, codified at 8 Del. C. § 361 et seq.) has several differences from Washington's SPC regime:

| Feature | Washington SPC | Delaware PBC |
|---|---|---|
| Director duty | Permissive consideration of social purpose | Mandatory balancing of shareholder interests, social purpose, and stakeholder interests |
| Reporting | Annual social purpose report; posted on website | Biennial statement to stockholders; optional third-party standard |
| Failure to report | No effect on corporate actions | Similar permissive regime |
| Third-party certification | Not required | Not required (but common to use B Lab certification) |
| Transition from ordinary corp | Amendment requires supermajority (or majority) | 2/3 approval required; subject to appraisal rights |

Washington's SPC is generally regarded as a looser, more flexible regime than Delaware's PBC. It imposes fewer constraints on director behavior and fewer reporting requirements. For founders whose primary motivation is brand or marketing — showing customers and employees that the corporation "cares" — the Washington SPC is often sufficient. For founders whose primary motivation is commitment to a stakeholder-balancing duty, Delaware's PBC or a nonprofit structure may be more appropriate.

### 13.18 Washington B&O Tax and Its Interaction with Corporate Law

Although tax law is generally outside the scope of this corporate law chapter, one aspect of Washington tax law interacts so directly with corporate lifecycle events that it deserves brief mention: the Washington Business and Occupation (B&O) tax.

**Overview.** Washington's B&O tax is a gross receipts tax levied on the "privilege of engaging in business" in Washington. It is administered by the Department of Revenue under RCW 82.04. Unlike an income tax, the B&O is assessed on revenue, not profit. Rates vary by business classification (retailing, wholesaling, manufacturing, service and other activities, etc.).

**Interaction with dissolution.** As noted in Part 10, dissolution requires a revenue clearance certificate from DOR under RCW 82.32.260. The revenue clearance certificate will not issue unless the corporation is current on all B&O returns and has paid all outstanding B&O tax. Corporations with B&O delinquencies cannot dissolve until the delinquencies are cured.

**Interaction with mergers and asset sales.** Successor liability for B&O tax applies under RCW 82.32.140. A purchaser of business assets in a non-ordinary-course transaction may inherit unpaid B&O tax obligations of the seller. Asset purchase agreements should address this successor liability through representations, warranties, indemnification, and a tax clearance procedure.

**Interaction with reinstatement.** A corporation seeking reinstatement after administrative dissolution must bring its B&O returns current in addition to filing back annual reports with the Secretary of State. Reinstatement and tax cleanup are often parallel processes that must be coordinated.

### 13.19 Recordkeeping Best Practices

Beyond the minimum statutory requirements under RCW 23B.16.010, the following recordkeeping best practices will serve most Washington corporations well:

**Permanent records (never destroy).**

- Articles of incorporation and all amendments (filed versions with Secretary of State stamps).
- Bylaws and all amendments.
- Minute book: minutes of all shareholder, board, and committee meetings, and written consents.
- Stock ledger (share register).
- Corporate seal (if any).
- Federal EIN assignment letter.
- Initial Washington Business License and all renewals.
- Registered agent appointment records.
- Material contracts of lasting significance (real estate, major customer and supplier agreements, employment agreements with key personnel, indemnification agreements).

**Long-term records (retain 7–10 years or longer).**

- Annual financial statements and tax returns.
- Audit reports and management letters.
- Material vendor and customer contracts (for the life of the contract plus statute of limitations).
- Employment records (per WA L&I and federal requirements).

**Medium-term records (retain 3–7 years).**

- Routine correspondence.
- Bank statements and canceled checks.
- Non-material contracts.

**Short-term records (retain 1–3 years).**

- Draft documents.
- Working papers.
- Email (unless subject to litigation hold).

**Litigation holds.** When litigation is reasonably anticipated, all retention policies are suspended as to relevant records, and a litigation hold notice should be distributed to all custodians. Failure to preserve records under a litigation hold can result in spoliation sanctions.

**Digital vs. paper.** Modern corporations maintain records primarily in digital form, with cloud-based storage. The digital format should be durable (not dependent on proprietary software that may become obsolete), searchable, and backed up. For the most critical records — articles, minute book, stock ledger — a parallel physical or printed copy is still recommended as a hedge against digital failure.

### 13.20 Common Drafting Traps

The following are drafting issues that recur in Washington corporate practice and that cause preventable problems:

**1. Forgetting to state "voting power" precisely.** Provisions referring to "a majority of shareholders" are ambiguous: does this mean a majority of shares or a majority of shareholders by number? Always specify "a majority of the outstanding shares entitled to vote" or "a majority of the votes entitled to be cast" to eliminate ambiguity.

**2. Voting thresholds below the statutory floor.** Articles and bylaws cannot specify a voting threshold below the applicable statutory minimum. For post-August 2024 corporations, the floor for amendments is a majority; an articles provision requiring only "a plurality" is void and reverts to the statutory default.

**3. Missing separate voting group language.** Amendments that affect multiple classes of shares must consider separate voting group requirements. Drafters who specify "a majority of the outstanding voting shares" without also addressing the separate voting groups can inadvertently violate RCW 23B.10.040.

**4. Stale director information in articles.** Original articles typically list the initial directors and the incorporator. Failure to remove this information in subsequent amendments creates a stale public record. RCW 23B.10.020(2) and (3) permit the board to remove this information without shareholder action; most corporations do so as part of the first substantive amendment.

**5. Unenforceable transfer restrictions.** Share transfer restrictions must be noted conspicuously on the certificate and must be reasonable. Restrictions that are overly broad (prohibiting any transfer without consent, with no alternative exit mechanism) are likely unenforceable.

**6. Inadequate buy-sell valuation mechanisms.** Buy-sell provisions that rely on a formula or a fixed price often become unfair over time as the corporation's value changes. Mechanisms should include a periodic adjustment (annual price update, appraisal process).

**7. Failure to address the 2024 threshold dichotomy.** Form articles copied from pre-2024 templates may still specify two-thirds voting thresholds that are now the default for pre-August 2024 corporations but not for post-August 2024 corporations. Drafters of articles for post-August 2024 corporations should explicitly specify the desired threshold rather than relying on the default.

**8. Missing statutory references in amendments.** Articles of amendment should reference the specific statutory provision under which they are filed (typically RCW 23B.10.060). Missing references can cause Secretary of State rejection.

**9. Improper execution.** Articles of amendment must be signed by an authorized officer. Signatures by persons without documented authority can cause filing rejection or create later authority challenges.

**10. Effective date confusion.** Amendments may specify a later effective date (up to 90 days after filing) but the default is on filing. Drafters who want a later effective date must specify it explicitly; silence defaults to immediate effectiveness.

## Part 14: Worked Examples

This part presents worked examples showing how the rules in this chapter apply in representative fact patterns. The examples are stylized and are intended to illustrate analytical method rather than to provide definitive answers for any real transaction.

### Example 14.1: Name Change for a Growing Corporation

**Facts.** Acme Widgets Corporation, a Washington business corporation formed in 2018, has grown into a diversified products company. Its board wants to change the name to "Acme Products Corporation."

**Analysis.**

1. **Board authority.** RCW 23B.10.020(5) permits the board to adopt this amendment without shareholder approval. This is a board-only amendment.
2. **Name availability.** The new name must be distinguishable on the records of the Secretary of State under RCW 23.95.300. A CCFS search should be conducted to confirm that "Acme Products Corporation" is available.
3. **Name reservation (optional).** If the board wants to lock in the name before adopting the amendment, it can reserve the name for 180 days under RCW 23.95.310.
4. **Articles of amendment.** Draft articles of amendment stating: the corporation's current name; the text of the amendment ("The name of the corporation is hereby changed from 'Acme Widgets Corporation' to 'Acme Products Corporation.'"); the date of board adoption; and a statement that shareholder approval was not required under RCW 23B.10.020.
5. **Filing.** File the articles of amendment with the Secretary of State with the applicable fee.
6. **Collateral updates.** Update bank accounts, licenses, contracts, letterhead, website, trade name registrations (if the old name was registered as a trade name, cancel the registration; if the new name is to be used as a trade name, register it), IRS (file Form 8822-B if the responsible party information needs updating; the EIN does not change), ESD, L&I, and other state and local registrations.
7. **Notice to counterparties.** Review material contracts for notice requirements on name change; notify counterparties as required.

**Timing.** This amendment can be completed in as little as a few days if the name is clearly available and the documents are prepared promptly. Expedited filing is available if needed.

### Example 14.2: Authorized Share Increase Requiring Shareholder Approval

**Facts.** Acme Products Corporation (from Example 14.1) wants to increase its authorized common stock from 10 million shares to 50 million shares to support an anticipated Series B financing. Acme was formed in 2018 (pre-August 2024).

**Analysis.**

1. **Board authority.** An increase in authorized shares is not a board-only amendment under RCW 23B.10.020 (unless the corporation has only one class of shares AND the statute's single-class conditions are met). Acme likely has multiple classes due to its prior Series A financing, so board authority alone is insufficient.
2. **Shareholder approval required.** Under RCW 23B.10.030, the amendment must be approved by a two-thirds vote (because Acme is a pre-August 2024 corporation) of the votes entitled to be cast.
3. **Separate voting group.** Under RCW 23B.10.040(1), an increase in authorized shares of a class entitles the class to vote as a separate voting group. The common stock must approve the increase in common stock as a separate voting group by two-thirds. The preferred stock may also have separate voting group rights under the articles or the Certificate of Designation.
4. **Board adoption and recommendation.** The board adopts the proposed amendment and recommends its approval to the shareholders.
5. **Shareholder meeting notice.** Notice is sent to all shareholders entitled to vote, 10 to 60 days before the meeting, identifying the amendment and stating that dissenters' rights may apply (though in this case dissenters' rights do not apply to a simple authorized share increase — only to certain specific amendments under RCW 23B.13.020).
6. **Shareholder vote.** The meeting is held. Assuming quorum, the amendment is approved if it receives two-thirds approval from the common stock voting group (and any separate preferred voting groups entitled to vote).
7. **Articles of amendment.** File articles of amendment with the Secretary of State containing the amendment text, date of adoption, and confirmation of shareholder approval under RCW 23B.10.030 and .040.
8. **Stock ledger update.** Update the stock ledger to reflect the new authorized share count. Existing shares are not affected; only the authorized count changes.

**Timing.** This amendment typically takes 30–60 days from initial planning to effective filing, depending on the notice period and the need to schedule a shareholder meeting (or obtain written consents).

### Example 14.3: Short-Form Parent-Subsidiary Merger

**Facts.** Acme Products Corporation owns 95% of the common stock of Beta Subsidiary Inc., a Washington business corporation. The remaining 5% is held by a former founder who has been out of contact for several years. Acme wants to eliminate Beta and integrate its operations.

**Analysis.**

1. **Short-form merger eligibility.** Under RCW 23B.11A.045, Acme's 95% ownership (well above the 90% threshold) qualifies for short-form merger treatment.
2. **Board approval.** Acme's board adopts a plan of merger providing that Beta merges into Acme, with Acme surviving, and that the 5% minority stake in Beta is converted into the right to receive a specified cash amount per share.
3. **No subsidiary approval needed.** Under the short-form merger rules, Beta's board and Beta's shareholders do not need to approve the merger. The articles of merger do not need to be executed by Beta.
4. **Articles of merger.** Acme files articles of merger with the Secretary of State identifying both parties, the surviving entity, and the consideration for the minority Beta shareholders.
5. **Notice to minority shareholders.** Within 10 days after the merger becomes effective, Acme sends notice to the former founder (at his last known address) describing the merger, the consideration to be received, and the dissenters' rights.
6. **Dissenters' rights.** The former founder has dissenters' rights under RCW 23B.13.020. If he fails to respond within the statutory windows, his rights are forfeited and he is entitled only to the cash consideration specified in the plan.
7. **Fairness analysis.** Even though the short-form merger avoids shareholder voting, Acme's board must ensure that the cash consideration is fair. If the consideration is below fair value, the former founder (if he perfects dissenters' rights) could obtain court-determined fair value. Acme's board should obtain a fairness opinion or valuation supporting the chosen consideration.

**Timing.** This transaction can be completed in 30–60 days, including the mandatory notice periods for dissenters' rights perfection.

### Example 14.4: Voluntary Dissolution of an Inactive Corporation

**Facts.** Gamma Corporation, a Washington business corporation formed in 2010, ceased operations in 2023. The sole shareholder and founder wants to wind up the corporation and dissolve it.

**Analysis.**

1. **Board and shareholder approval.** Under RCW 23B.14.020, dissolution requires board proposal and shareholder approval. For a sole-shareholder corporation, this is straightforward: the board (likely consisting of the sole shareholder as sole director) adopts a resolution proposing dissolution, and the sole shareholder approves by written consent.
2. **Tax cleanup.** Before filing articles of dissolution, Gamma must bring its B&O returns current and obtain a revenue clearance certificate from DOR under RCW 82.32.260. This may take several weeks if there are unpaid returns.
3. **Articles of dissolution.** Once the revenue clearance certificate is obtained, Gamma files articles of dissolution with the Secretary of State, stating the corporation's name, the date of dissolution authorization, and a statement of compliance with RCW 23B.14.020.
4. **Publication of notice.** Within 30 days after dissolution becomes effective, Gamma publishes a notice to creditors in a newspaper of general circulation in the county of the principal office, once a week for three consecutive weeks.
5. **Known claims.** Gamma sends written notices to any known creditors (vendors, landlords, former employees) under RCW 23B.14.060, giving them 120 days to submit claims.
6. **Winding up.** Gamma collects receivables, sells remaining assets, pays debts as claims come in, and distributes remaining assets to the sole shareholder.
7. **Unknown claims.** Claims not known at the time of publication are barred under RCW 23B.14.070 unless the claimant files suit within three years after publication.
8. **Federal tax.** Gamma files a final corporate tax return (Form 1120 marked "Final Return") with the IRS. The EIN is not formally canceled, but the corporation is removed from active status.
9. **State tax.** Gamma closes its BLS account with DOR.
10. **Record retention.** Gamma's sole shareholder retains the corporate records (articles, bylaws, minute book, stock ledger) for at least seven years after dissolution in case of IRS audit or late-filed claims.

**Timing.** From initial planning to final dissolution, this process typically takes 4–6 months, driven primarily by the DOR revenue clearance cleanup and the newspaper publication period.

### Example 14.5: Administrative Dissolution and Reinstatement

**Facts.** Delta Corporation, a Washington business corporation formed in 2020, missed its 2024 annual report due to the sole officer's personal health issues. In 2025, Delta received a notice from the Secretary of State. Delta failed to respond and was administratively dissolved. In 2026, Delta's officer wants to restore the corporation.

**Analysis.**

1. **Eligibility for reinstatement.** Delta was administratively dissolved in 2025. Under RCW 23.95.615, Delta has five years from the effective date of administrative dissolution to apply for reinstatement. As of 2026, Delta is well within the window.
2. **Application content.** Delta's reinstatement application must include:
   - A statement that the entity name is still available or a new name (if the old name has been taken).
   - Current principal office and registered agent information.
   - The effective date of the administrative dissolution.
   - Documentation that the grounds for dissolution have been cured (i.e., all back annual reports must be filed).
3. **Name availability.** Delta should conduct a CCFS search to confirm that "Delta Corporation" is still available. If not, Delta must select a new name.
4. **Back reports and fees.** Delta must pay:
   - All annual report fees for the missed years (2024, 2025, 2026 annual reports, at approximately $70 each, plus any adjustments).
   - A reinstatement penalty set by Secretary of State rule.
   - The current year's annual report fee.
5. **Registered agent.** Delta must have a current registered agent. If the registered agent from before dissolution is no longer serving, Delta must appoint a new agent.
6. **Filing.** Delta submits the reinstatement application through CCFS or in paper form.
7. **Retroactive effect.** Upon approval, reinstatement relates back to the effective date of administrative dissolution. For most purposes, Delta is treated as having been in continuous existence. Contracts signed by Delta during the dissolution period (including any signed with third parties who did not know of the dissolution) are preserved.
8. **Caveat: third-party reliance.** If any third party relied in good faith on Delta's dissolved status during the dissolution period (for example, a creditor who brought action against Delta's officer personally), that reliance is preserved by the statutory exception to retroactive effect.

**Timing.** Reinstatement typically takes a few days to a few weeks, depending on the complexity of the cure (back reports, tax issues, name availability) and the processing time at the Secretary of State.

## Part 15: Glossary of Terms

**Acquired entity.** In a share exchange, the entity whose shares are being acquired. See RCW 23B.11A.010.

**Acquiring entity.** In a share exchange, the entity acquiring the shares. See RCW 23B.11A.010.

**Acquiring person.** For purposes of Chapter 23B.19 (significant business transactions), a person who beneficially owns 10% or more of a target corporation's voting power. See RCW 23B.19.020.

**Administrative dissolution.** Dissolution of a corporation by the Secretary of State for procedural failures (failure to file annual report, failure to maintain registered agent, etc.). See RCW 23.95.605, .610. Subject to reinstatement for five years.

**All or substantially all.** The threshold at which a sale of corporate property triggers shareholder approval. In Washington, defined numerically: the corporation must retain at least 25% of total assets and either 25% of income or 25% of revenues to avoid the shareholder approval requirement. See RCW 23B.12.020.

**Annual report.** The yearly filing required by every Washington corporation under RCW 23.95.255, containing the entity's registered agent, principal office, officers, directors, and other identifying information.

**Articles of amendment.** The document filed with the Secretary of State to amend the articles of incorporation. See RCW 23B.10.060.

**Articles of dissolution.** The document filed with the Secretary of State to dissolve a corporation. See RCW 23B.14.030.

**Articles of incorporation.** The foundational document that creates a corporation, containing its name, authorized shares, registered agent, and other required information. See RCW 23B.02.020.

**Articles of merger.** The document filed with the Secretary of State to effectuate a merger. See RCW 23B.11A.050, .060.

**Bylaws.** The internal operating document of a corporation, not filed with the Secretary of State, governing meeting procedures, officer duties, committee structures, and internal governance matters. See RCW 23B.02.060.

**Certificate of authority.** The document a foreign corporation obtains to transact business in Washington. See RCW 23.95.505.

**Certificate of existence.** The Washington equivalent of a "good standing certificate." Issued by the Secretary of State to certify that the corporation is currently in compliance.

**Close corporation.** A corporation with a small number of shareholders, no public market for its shares, and substantial shareholder participation in management. Washington does not have a separate close corporation statute, but Chapter 23B.07.320 (shareholders' agreements) permits close-corporation-style governance.

**Conversion.** A change of entity type (e.g., corporation to LLC). See Chapter 23B.09 and Chapter 23.95 Article 9.

**Dissenters' rights.** The statutory right of a shareholder to receive fair value for their shares in cash, in lieu of the consideration offered by certain specified corporate transactions. See Chapter 23B.13 RCW.

**Distinguishable on the records.** The Washington naming test. A proposed name must be distinguishable from existing names on the Secretary of State's records, not merely different in trivial ways such as punctuation, capitalization, or plurality. See RCW 23.95.300.

**DBA / Doing business as / Trade name.** A name under which a business operates that is different from its legal name. In Washington, trade names are registered with the Department of Revenue's Business Licensing Service, not with the Secretary of State. See RCW 19.80.

**Dissolution.** The legal ending of a corporation's existence. Washington recognizes voluntary dissolution (by the corporation itself), administrative dissolution (by the Secretary of State), and judicial dissolution (by court order).

**Distribution.** A transfer of money or other property by a corporation to its shareholders. Subject to restrictions in RCW 23B.06.400 (must not render the corporation insolvent).

**Domestic entity.** An entity formed under Washington law (as opposed to a foreign entity formed under another state's law).

**Fair value.** The value of shares for purposes of dissenters' rights, determined as of the moment before the triggering corporate action, excluding any appreciation or depreciation in anticipation of the action. See RCW 23B.13.

**Foreign entity.** An entity formed under the law of another state or country. Foreign entities may qualify to transact business in Washington by obtaining a certificate of authority.

**Good faith.** A term of art in corporate law, generally meaning honesty in fact and observance of reasonable commercial standards of fair dealing. See, e.g., RCW 23B.08.300 (director duty of care).

**Good standing.** Informally, the status of a corporation that is current on all its compliance obligations. Washington uses the term "certificate of existence" for the official confirmation.

**Governor.** A generic term used in Chapter 23.95 to refer to persons who manage an entity. For a business corporation, governors are the directors.

**Indemnification.** The corporation's obligation (or permission) to reimburse directors, officers, and others for expenses and losses arising from their corporate service. See Chapter 23B.08.

**Initial report.** The first filing required of a newly formed corporation, due within 120 days after formation. See RCW 23.95.255(1).

**Internal affairs doctrine.** The choice-of-law rule that the law of the state of incorporation governs the internal affairs of a corporation.

**Known claim.** For purposes of dissolution, a claim against the corporation of which the corporation has actual knowledge (claimant identity, contact information, and facts supporting the claim). Subject to the 120-day bar date procedure of RCW 23B.14.060.

**Majority vote.** The voting threshold for amendments, mergers, and certain other actions for corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, under the 2024 c 22 amendment. For corporations formed before that date, the default is two-thirds.

**Market-out exception.** In dissenters' rights law, an exception that withdraws dissenters' rights for certain publicly traded shares or cash-for-all-assets transactions. Washington's market-out exception is narrower than Delaware's. See RCW 23B.13.020.

**Merger.** A transaction in which one or more entities combine, with one surviving and others disappearing. Governed by Chapter 23B.11A.

**Minute book.** The collection of minutes and written consents documenting corporate actions. Required under RCW 23B.16.010.

**Model Business Corporation Act (MBCA).** A model statute developed and revised by the American Bar Association Business Law Section. Washington's Title 23B is based on the 1984 MBCA revision, with subsequent updates.

**Name reservation.** The process of reserving a corporate name for 180 days before formation. See RCW 23.95.310.

**Organic record.** Under Chapter 23.95, the public-record document that creates an entity — for a corporation, the articles of incorporation.

**Par value.** The nominal value assigned to shares in the articles. Washington has largely eliminated the legal significance of par value but permits corporations to continue using par value if desired.

**Plan of merger.** The contractual instrument defining a merger transaction.

**Principal office.** The corporation's main office, which need not be in Washington. (Compare to the registered office, which must be in Washington.) See RCW 23.95.

**Proper purpose.** A term in shareholder inspection law. A shareholder seeking tier-2 inspection (minutes, accounting records, shareholder register) must state a purpose reasonably related to their interest as a shareholder. See RCW 23B.16.020.

**Record date.** The date used to determine which shareholders are entitled to notice of, and to vote at, a shareholder meeting. See RCW 23B.07.070.

**Registered agent.** The person or entity designated to receive service of process on behalf of a corporation. Must be continuously maintained. See RCW 23B.05 and RCW 23.95 Article 4.

**Registered office.** The Washington address of the registered agent, where service of process can be made during business hours. Must be a street address, not a P.O. box.

**Reinstatement.** The restoration of an administratively dissolved corporation, available for five years after dissolution. See RCW 23.95.615.

**Restated articles.** Articles of incorporation consolidated to include all prior amendments in a single document. See RCW 23B.10.070.

**Revenue clearance certificate.** A certificate from the Department of Revenue confirming that all Washington tax obligations are current. Required for voluntary dissolution. See RCW 82.32.260.

**Revocation of dissolution.** The undoing of a voluntary dissolution within 120 days after its effective date. See RCW 23B.14.040.

**Separate voting group.** In amendment and merger law, a class or series of shares that must vote separately from other classes on the proposed action. See RCW 23B.10.040.

**Share exchange.** A transaction in which one entity acquires all of one or more classes of shares of another entity, with both entities continuing to exist. See RCW 23B.11A.030.

**Shareholders' agreement.** An agreement among shareholders governing the exercise of their rights and the management of the corporation. Can displace many default governance rules under RCW 23B.07.320.

**Short-form merger.** A streamlined merger in which a parent corporation owning at least 90% of a subsidiary merges with the subsidiary without subsidiary shareholder approval. See RCW 23B.11A.045.

**Significant business transaction.** A defined category of transactions between a target corporation and an acquiring person under Chapter 23B.19. Subject to a five-year moratorium after the acquiring person crosses the 10% ownership threshold.

**Social purpose corporation (SPC).** A Washington business corporation that has designated one or more social purposes in its articles and is subject to the SPC regime of Chapter 23B.25.

**Statement of change.** A filing with the Secretary of State to change the registered agent or registered office. See RCW 23.95.430.

**Stock ledger.** The record of share ownership, required under RCW 23B.16.010. Identifies each shareholder, the number and class of shares, and relevant transaction details.

**Subsidiary.** A corporation controlled (typically by majority share ownership) by another corporation (the parent).

**Successor liability.** The legal doctrine under which a purchaser of business assets or the surviving entity in a merger inherits liabilities of the seller or disappearing entity.

**Target corporation.** Under Chapter 23B.19, a corporation subject to the anti-takeover moratorium. Generally, a publicly traded Washington corporation (or a private corporation that has opted in by articles amendment).

**Trade name.** See DBA / Doing business as.

**Two-thirds vote.** The default voting threshold for amendments, mergers, and certain other actions for corporations formed before August 1, 2024.

**Unknown claim.** For purposes of dissolution, a claim against the corporation not known to the corporation at the time of dissolution. Subject to the three-year bar date of RCW 23B.14.070 if the publication notice procedure is followed.

**Voluntary dissolution.** Dissolution initiated by the corporation itself (through board proposal and shareholder approval), as distinguished from administrative or judicial dissolution.

**Voting group.** A group of shareholders entitled to vote together on a matter. For amendments affecting multiple classes, each affected class typically forms its own voting group.

**WBCA.** Washington Business Corporation Act, Title 23B RCW.

**WUBOC.** Washington Uniform Business Organizations Code, Chapter 23.95 RCW. The hub chapter for entity-type-neutral rules.

**Winding up.** The process of collecting assets, paying debts, and distributing remaining property after dissolution. See RCW 23B.14.050.

## Conclusion

Washington corporate law is a comprehensive and (in many respects) distinctive body of statutory and case law. Its distinctive features include: the interlocking Title 23B (WBCA) and Chapter 23.95 (WUBOC) structure; the formation-date dichotomy introduced by 2024 c 22 in shareholder voting thresholds; the numeric "significant continuing business" test in RCW 23B.12.020; the five-year significant-business-transaction moratorium in Chapter 23B.19, one of the strictest in the country; and the layered naming and registered-agent rules that must be traced across two chapters.

For the practitioner, the enduring lessons are: first, never work only from Title 23B — always check the parallel WUBOC provisions. Second, track the formation-date dichotomy in every matter involving shareholder voting. Third, treat the annual report, registered agent, and initial report as the core ongoing compliance obligations, because their failure sets the irreversible cascade toward administrative dissolution. Fourth, understand that the Washington legislature has consistently chosen strong protections for the corporate form itself and for the stability of Washington-chartered corporations against hostile change — the five-year moratorium is the clearest expression of this policy choice. Fifth, remember that dissolution is a process, not an event, and that the winding-up procedures in RCW 23B.14.050 through .080 provide meaningful tools for orderly closure but require disciplined execution.

Washington's corporate law is also a living body. The 2024 amendment cycle reshaped the voting-threshold landscape; future cycles will likely continue to modernize the WBCA in alignment with the Model Business Corporation Act. Practitioners should maintain an active reading of Washington session laws each biennium and of Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section publications, which are the principal source of practitioner guidance on current developments.

## Part 16: Extended Analysis and Additional Practitioner Materials

### 16.1 A Deeper Look at the 2024 Threshold Change

The 2024 amendment introduced by Engrossed Substitute Senate Bill 5335 (2024 c 22) is the most significant change to Washington corporate voting thresholds in a generation. It deserves extended treatment because it reshapes practitioner practice in several distinct areas simultaneously.

**The legislative history.** SB 5335 originated in 2023 and moved through the Senate Business, Financial Services, Gaming and Trade Committee before being engrossed and substituted. The bill's principal sponsor emphasized the alignment goal: bringing Washington in line with the Model Business Corporation Act and with the modal practice in MBCA states. Washington's two-thirds default had long been identified by the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section as an outlier that imposed unnecessary friction on capital formation and routine corporate housekeeping.

**What the bill did NOT change.** The 2024 amendment is precise in its scope. It changed the default voting thresholds in certain enumerated sections — principally RCW 23B.10.030 (amendments), 23B.11A.040 (mergers and share exchanges), 23B.12.020 (asset sales), and 23B.14.020 (voluntary dissolution). It did NOT change:

- The voting thresholds in RCW 23B.19 (significant business transactions), which continue to require a two-thirds cleansing vote regardless of formation date.
- The voting thresholds for election of directors (which are governed by separate rules).
- The quorum requirements for shareholder meetings.
- The rules for separate voting groups (RCW 23B.10.040), which continue to apply alongside the threshold change.
- The right of the articles to specify higher thresholds.
- The operation of any existing articles provision that specified a threshold (either higher or lower than the pre-2024 default).

**Grandfathering rule.** The statute provides that a provision in the articles of incorporation of a corporation formed before August 1, 2024 that limited or eliminated separate voting rights continues to have the same effect under the new law. This means that existing corporations' governance arrangements are not disturbed by the change — the two-thirds default continues to apply, and any existing articles customizations continue to operate.

**Practical consequences for law firms.** Law firms advising Washington corporations must now maintain two distinct form sets: one for pre-August 2024 corporations (with the two-thirds default assumed) and one for post-August 2024 corporations (with the majority default assumed). Amendment resolutions, merger agreements, dissolution resolutions, and shareholder consent forms must all be tailored to the formation date.

**Due diligence implications.** In M&A due diligence, counsel should verify the formation date of any Washington target corporation to determine which threshold applies. A target formed July 30, 2024 has the two-thirds default; a target formed August 2, 2024 has the majority default. The two-day difference has material implications for merger approval mechanics.

**Re-incorporation considerations.** A pre-August 2024 Washington corporation that wants to avail itself of the majority default can achieve this by re-incorporating (dissolving and re-forming, or merging into a newly formed entity). Whether this is worth doing depends on the specific circumstances; for most corporations, the existing two-thirds default is not so burdensome as to justify re-incorporation, but for corporations facing frequent amendments or recurring acquisitions, the calculus may differ.

**Articles drafting for new corporations.** For corporations formed on or after August 1, 2024, drafters have several choices:

1. **Accept the majority default.** The simplest approach. The corporation's voting thresholds will be the new statutory majority.
2. **Specify a higher threshold.** The drafters may specify two-thirds, three-fourths, or any other higher threshold for some or all of the covered actions. This is appropriate when minority protection is desired.
3. **Specify a per-action threshold.** Different thresholds for different types of actions (e.g., two-thirds for mergers but majority for amendments). This requires more careful drafting but can be tailored to the specific concerns of the founders.
4. **Specify a higher threshold for specific voting groups.** The articles can specify separate voting group thresholds that differ from the general threshold.

**Articles drafting for legacy corporations.** For pre-August 2024 corporations that want to opt into the new majority default, counsel should draft an articles amendment specifying the majority threshold, submit it to the shareholders under the existing (two-thirds) threshold, and thereafter operate under the lowered threshold. This is a one-time amendment that can be done as part of any broader restatement or as a stand-alone amendment.

### 16.2 The Practical Mechanics of Filing with the Secretary of State

This section walks through the procedural mechanics of filing with the Washington Secretary of State at a level of detail useful to counsel who are new to Washington practice.

**Step 1: Choose the filing method.** Filings may be submitted:

- **Online through CCFS** — the fastest and most common method. Requires a CCFS account. Fees are paid by credit card or ACH.
- **By mail** — paper filings mailed to the Corporations Division in Olympia. Slower; fees typically paid by check.
- **In person** — filings delivered to the Corporations Division counter in Olympia. Same-day processing available for an additional fee.

**Step 2: Prepare the filing document.** Standard forms are available on the Secretary of State's website for most common filings. Use of standard forms is not required; custom-drafted filings are accepted if they contain the required content. The advantage of standard forms is that they are pre-approved and will not be rejected for formatting reasons.

**Step 3: Verify signatures.** Most filings require an authorized signature. The authorized signer varies by filing type:

- **Articles of incorporation** — incorporator.
- **Articles of amendment** — officer.
- **Articles of merger** — officer of the surviving entity.
- **Articles of dissolution** — officer.
- **Annual report** — officer.
- **Statement of change of registered agent** — officer, plus consent from the new agent.

**Step 4: Pay the fee.** Fees are paid at the time of filing. Expedited fees are added if expedited processing is requested. Payment may be by credit card, ACH, or (for paper filings) check.

**Step 5: Receive confirmation.** Online filings typically generate confirmation within minutes; expedited filings are processed within the stated time frame; standard filings may take several days to a few weeks depending on volume. The Secretary of State issues a stamped copy of the filed document and, for filings that create or modify records (articles of incorporation, amendments, mergers, dissolutions), a confirmation letter.

**Step 6: Update internal records.** Upon receipt of the stamped filing, update the corporation's minute book to reflect the filed document. Send copies to relevant parties (e.g., new registered agent, shareholders whose rights are affected by the amendment).

**Common rejection reasons.** Filings may be rejected for a variety of reasons. The most common:

- **Name not distinguishable.** The new name conflicts with an existing name or reserved name.
- **Missing required signature.** The filing is not signed by an authorized person.
- **Missing required content.** The filing does not contain all statutorily required content.
- **Fee discrepancy.** The wrong fee was paid or the payment method was declined.
- **Prohibited words.** The name contains a regulated word without the required regulator approval.
- **Wrong form.** An outdated or incorrect form was used.

Rejected filings are returned with an explanation. The corporation has the opportunity to correct and resubmit, but the resubmission is typically treated as a new filing for fee and date purposes.

### 16.3 Additional Notes on Washington's Treatment of Closely Held Corporations

Washington does not have a separate statutory framework for closely held corporations (analogous to the Delaware close corporation statute or the California close corporation statute). Instead, closely held corporations are governed by the general WBCA, supplemented by Chapter 23B.07.320 (shareholders' agreements) and by general close-corporation case law principles.

**Consequences of this structure.** A Washington closely held corporation operates under the same default rules as a public corporation, with the following practical implications:

1. **Mandatory annual meetings.** The default rules require annual shareholder meetings and annual director elections, even for single-shareholder corporations. Compliance can be handled through written consents in lieu of meetings.
2. **Director duties to corporation.** Directors owe fiduciary duties to the corporation, not directly to shareholders. In a closely held corporation with shareholder deadlock or oppression, shareholders' remedies lie in derivative actions (for corporate harm) or in judicial dissolution proceedings (for oppression).
3. **No close corporation shield against piercing.** Washington courts have not developed a special doctrine that strengthens or weakens veil piercing for closely held corporations. The same factors apply: undercapitalization, commingling of assets, failure to observe corporate formalities, use of the corporation as an instrumentality or alter ego of the shareholders.
4. **Flexibility through shareholders' agreement.** A closely held corporation can achieve close-corporation-style flexibility through a shareholders' agreement under RCW 23B.07.320. This is the standard planning tool and should be considered in every closely held corporation of any complexity.

**Planning recommendations for closely held corporations.**

1. **Adopt a shareholders' agreement at formation.** Do not wait. The agreement should address deadlock resolution, buy-sell provisions, voting arrangements, and succession planning.
2. **Implement restrictive legends on share certificates.** Noting the shareholders' agreement and any transfer restrictions is essential to binding future transferees.
3. **Hold regular (even if pro forma) board and shareholder meetings.** Written consents are acceptable but should be prepared contemporaneously with the actions, not reconstructed after the fact.
4. **Maintain the minute book.** Even for a single-shareholder corporation, the minute book is a critical record. It demonstrates that corporate formalities are being observed and supports the limited liability shield.
5. **Keep corporate and personal finances separate.** Separate bank accounts, separate credit cards, separate bookkeeping. Commingling is a classic veil-piercing risk factor.
6. **Carry adequate insurance.** Closely held corporations are often undercapitalized; insurance compensates by providing a deep pocket for third-party claims.

### 16.4 Washington's Relationship to Other MBCA States and Non-MBCA States

Understanding Washington's position in the national landscape of corporate law helps practitioners evaluate whether the Washington choice is optimal for a given corporation.

**MBCA states.** Washington is one of approximately 30 states that have adopted the Model Business Corporation Act (or the earlier Model Act) as the basis for their business corporation statutes. Other MBCA states include Oregon, Idaho, Montana, Utah, Colorado, Wyoming, Texas (partially), Virginia, Tennessee, Florida, Massachusetts, and many others. MBCA states share a common statutory framework, making it relatively easy for practitioners trained in one MBCA state to navigate the others.

**Non-MBCA states.** The most important non-MBCA states are Delaware, California, and New York. Each has its own distinctive statutory framework:

- **Delaware.** The dominant jurisdiction for large-corporation and venture-backed incorporations. Delaware's general corporation law has evolved through sophisticated case law over more than a century, and the Delaware Court of Chancery is widely regarded as the most knowledgeable corporate law court in the country. Delaware is favored for its predictability, its well-developed fiduciary duty doctrine, its sophisticated appraisal jurisprudence, and its franchise tax revenue model (which keeps filing costs low for incorporators in return for annual franchise tax).
- **California.** The most populous state, with a distinctive corporate governance framework that includes special rules for close corporations, protections for minority shareholders, and the controversial pseudo-foreign corporation statute (§ 2115) that purports to apply California rules to out-of-state corporations with substantial California connections.
- **New York.** The second-largest commercial jurisdiction, with a Business Corporation Law that differs in important ways from both the MBCA and Delaware. New York has strong minority shareholder protections and a well-developed fiduciary duty doctrine of its own.

**When is Washington the right choice?** Washington is a reasonable choice for:

- Corporations with their headquarters, operations, and most of their shareholders in Washington.
- Closely held family corporations that value local counsel familiarity.
- Corporations that do not anticipate a Delaware re-domestication before IPO.
- Corporations that value the reasonable fee structure and the absence of annual franchise tax.

Washington is less commonly chosen for:

- Venture-backed corporations planning a Delaware re-domestication before IPO (they typically start in Delaware, or re-domesticate to Delaware before a substantial venture round).
- Corporations whose primary investor base is outside the Pacific Northwest (investors often prefer Delaware for familiarity).
- Corporations engaged in hostile M&A activity (Chapter 23B.19's five-year moratorium complicates hostile transactions).
- Multinational corporations that benefit from Delaware's international reputation.

**The re-domestication option.** Any Washington corporation can re-domesticate to Delaware (or another state) through a merger with a newly formed shell corporation in the target state. This is a standard transaction with well-developed forms and procedures. The Washington corporation dissolves (or, in a reverse merger structure, the Delaware shell disappears) and the resulting entity is domiciled in the target state. Tax considerations, dissenters' rights, and transaction costs all bear on the decision whether to re-domesticate.

### 16.5 Cautions and Traps for the Unwary

The following are recurring issues that have caused problems for Washington practitioners and their clients. Treat this as a checklist of "things to double-check" before filing or advising.

**Trap 1: Confusing Title 23B with the WUBOC.** New practitioners often look only in Title 23B for naming rules or registered agent rules and are confused when they find only cross-references. The substantive rules are in Chapter 23.95.

**Trap 2: Relying on outdated treatises.** Many Washington corporate law treatises on the market predate either the 2015 WUBOC recodification or the 2024 threshold amendment, or both. Always verify citations against the current statutes.

**Trap 3: Copying forms from other states.** Forms from Delaware, California, or other states may use voting threshold language that conflicts with Washington defaults. Always localize forms for Washington use.

**Trap 4: Missing separate voting group analysis.** Amendments affecting preferred stock often require separate voting group approval in addition to the general shareholder vote. Missing this analysis can lead to invalid amendments.

**Trap 5: Failing to file the initial report.** The 120-day initial report deadline is easy to miss because it does not correspond to the anniversary of formation. A newly formed corporation should put the initial report deadline on its compliance calendar immediately upon formation.

**Trap 6: Using a P.O. box for the registered office.** The street-address requirement is absolute. P.O. boxes are rejected.

**Trap 7: Failing to maintain the registered agent relationship.** Registered agents resign when they are not paid or when they lose contact with the represented entity. Prompt payment and clear communication prevent unexpected resignations.

**Trap 8: Assuming reinstatement is automatic.** Reinstatement after administrative dissolution requires affirmative application and payment. Waiting beyond the five-year window forfeits the option permanently.

**Trap 9: Confusing voluntary dissolution with administrative dissolution.** Voluntary dissolution requires board and shareholder approval (and a revenue clearance certificate). Administrative dissolution is imposed by the Secretary of State for compliance failures. These are distinct processes with distinct procedures.

**Trap 10: Overlooking the newspaper publication requirement in dissolution.** The publication requirement in RCW 23B.14.030 is often forgotten or deprioritized. Failure to publish forfeits the benefit of the three-year unknown-claims bar, potentially leaving the corporation and its former shareholders exposed to late claims.

**Trap 11: Missing the 10-day post-merger notice in short-form mergers.** Short-form mergers require notice to minority shareholders within 10 days. Failure to give timely notice can expose the surviving entity to extended dissenters' rights windows.

**Trap 12: Confusing dissenters' rights deadlines.** The dissenters' rights procedure has multiple deadlines (notice of intent, payment demand, objection, court proceeding). Each deadline has strict compliance requirements. Miss one and the dissenter loses rights; miss one on the corporation side and the corporation pays more.

**Trap 13: Ignoring Chapter 23B.19 in public-company transactions.** When a Washington publicly traded corporation is involved in a control transaction, Chapter 23B.19 must be analyzed. The five-year moratorium is too consequential to leave to post-closing analysis.

**Trap 14: Forgetting the CTA.** The federal Corporate Transparency Act imposes separate beneficial ownership reporting obligations. Washington compliance does not satisfy CTA compliance.

**Trap 15: Assuming LLC rules apply to corporations.** Washington LLCs are governed by Chapter 25.15 RCW, not Title 23B. The rules are similar in many respects but not identical. Advisors familiar with LLC practice should not assume that LLC habits transfer to corporations.

### 16.7 Looking Forward: Anticipated Developments

While this chapter describes the law as it currently stands, practitioners should be aware that Washington corporate law continues to evolve. Several areas of anticipated development:

**Continued MBCA alignment.** The 2024 amendment brought Washington's voting thresholds into alignment with the MBCA mainstream. Future amendments may continue this alignment in other areas, such as director duties, indemnification, and conflict-of-interest transactions.

**Benefit corporation statute.** Washington has the SPC regime but has not adopted a formal "benefit corporation" statute along the lines of Delaware's public benefit corporation. Proposals to do so have been discussed but have not advanced. Counsel should monitor for potential future adoption.

**Electronic-only meetings.** The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of electronic-only shareholder meetings. Washington law currently permits hybrid and electronic meetings but may be further modified to clarify procedures and expand options.

**Beneficial ownership transparency.** The federal Corporate Transparency Act has reshaped the beneficial ownership reporting landscape. States may adopt their own complementary or coordinated regimes. Washington has not yet done so but may in the future.

**Climate-related disclosure.** The SEC has proposed climate-related disclosure rules for public companies. State-level disclosure requirements for private companies (particularly ESG-focused corporations and SPCs) may follow.

**AI and corporate governance.** The use of artificial intelligence in board decision-making, director duty analysis, and compliance monitoring is a developing area. Washington has not adopted specific rules, but practitioners should anticipate future legal development.

### 16.8 Final Practitioner Checklist

As a capstone to this chapter, the following checklist summarizes the core compliance obligations for every Washington business corporation. Counsel advising any Washington corporation should be able to answer "yes" to each item:

1. Is the corporation's name on file with the Secretary of State, distinguishable from other names, and containing a required designator?
2. Is there a current registered agent with a Washington street address?
3. Has the initial report been filed (if the corporation is newly formed)?
4. Has the most recent annual report been filed, and is the entity in good standing?
5. Are the articles of incorporation current, and do they reflect all amendments?
6. Are the bylaws current, and are they consistent with the articles?
7. Is the minute book current, with minutes or written consents for all board and shareholder actions?
8. Is the stock ledger current, reflecting all share issuances and transfers?
9. Are the financial statements prepared and available for shareholders on request?
10. Are the records being maintained in accordance with RCW 23B.16.010?
11. Is the corporation current on its Washington B&O tax and other state tax obligations?
12. Does the corporation have adequate D&O insurance (for corporations with independent directors or meaningful liability exposure)?
13. Has the corporation complied with the federal Corporate Transparency Act beneficial ownership reporting requirements?
14. Are any change-of-control or anti-takeover provisions in the articles current and consistent with the business's strategic direction?
15. Is there a plan for addressing any pending or anticipated corporate lifecycle events (amendments, mergers, dissolutions)?
16. If the corporation is closely held, is there a current shareholders' agreement addressing deadlock, buy-sell, and succession?
17. If the corporation is a Social Purpose Corporation, has the annual social purpose report been posted?
18. Are share transfer restrictions properly documented in the corporate records and noted on share certificates?
19. Are all board and shareholder meetings being held (or written consents being obtained) in compliance with the statutory and bylaws requirements?
20. Is the corporation aware of the formation-date-dependent voting thresholds under the 2024 amendment and operating under the correct threshold?

## Conclusion to the Extended Analysis

Washington corporate law rewards the diligent practitioner with a coherent, well-structured framework that balances shareholder protection, flexibility for closely held corporations, and predictability for larger commercial entities. It also rewards the lazy practitioner with traps: outdated treatises, formation-date dichotomies, cross-referenced statutes that require checking two chapters, and compliance obligations with cascading consequences for failure.

The best advice for any lawyer entering Washington corporate practice is to read the current statute text directly rather than relying on summaries, to develop a relationship with the Corporations Division at the Secretary of State (whose staff are generally helpful and responsive), to participate actively in the Washington State Bar Association Business Law Section (for both the substantive content and the practitioner network), and to treat compliance calendars and minute books as load-bearing rather than housekeeping. The life cycle of a Washington corporation is a long sequence of small decisions, and the quality of counsel over that life cycle is the sum of those decisions.

This chapter has attempted to trace that life cycle from naming through dissolution, touching every major statutory regime and highlighting the most important practitioner concerns along the way. It is intended as a starting point and a reference, not as a substitute for primary statutory research or professional judgment. Washington corporate law is a living body of law, and this chapter is a snapshot of its current state. Future research updates will track ongoing developments as they occur.

## Appendix D: Frequently Asked Practitioner Questions

**Q: Can a Washington corporation have a single shareholder who is also the sole director and sole officer?**

A: Yes. Washington permits a single-shareholder, single-director, single-officer corporation. The corporation must still observe corporate formalities (minute book, annual meetings in the form of written consents, separate bank accounts, etc.) to preserve the limited liability shield.

**Q: What happens if a corporation misses its annual report by one day?**

A: Nothing catastrophic. The entity becomes delinquent and will receive a delinquency notice. Late filing before the administrative dissolution process begins cures the deficiency. However, if the delinquency persists and the Secretary of State initiates administrative dissolution under RCW 23.95.610, the 60-day cure period applies, and if cure fails, the entity is administratively dissolved.

**Q: Can articles of amendment be filed at the same time as articles of merger?**

A: Yes. The Secretary of State permits combined filings when both sets of requirements are met. This is common when a merger involves changes to the surviving entity's articles.

**Q: What is the difference between a merger and a conversion?**

A: A merger combines two or more entities into one, with one surviving. A conversion changes a single entity from one type (e.g., corporation) to another (e.g., LLC) without combining with another entity. Both are governed by statute and both typically require board and shareholder approval.

**Q: Can a Washington corporation have a non-Washington principal office?**

A: Yes. The principal office need not be in Washington. Only the registered office must be in Washington. Many Washington corporations with operations headquartered out of state maintain only a commercial registered agent's address in Washington.

**Q: Can a Washington corporation convert to a Delaware corporation?**

A: Yes, through a cross-border conversion or through a merger with a Delaware shell corporation. The transaction typically triggers dissenters' rights and requires shareholder approval. Tax considerations should be evaluated carefully.

**Q: What happens to a Washington corporation that fails to reinstate within five years of administrative dissolution?**

A: The corporation is permanently dissolved. It cannot be reinstated. A new corporation can be formed, but it has no legal continuity with the dissolved corporation. Contracts, assets, and liabilities do not automatically transfer to the new entity.

**Q: Is the minute book really necessary for a single-shareholder corporation?**

A: Yes. The minute book is essential for maintaining the corporate form. Courts consider the adequacy of corporate records in piercing-the-corporate-veil analysis. A corporation without a minute book is vulnerable to the claim that it is merely the alter ego of its shareholder.

**Q: Can the board amend the articles without shareholder approval to increase authorized shares?**

A: Only in limited circumstances. RCW 23B.10.020(4) permits the board to effect a forward or reverse stock split (with proportional adjustment to authorized shares) only if the corporation has a single class of shares outstanding. Any other increase in authorized shares requires shareholder approval.

**Q: What is the most common mistake made by non-Washington counsel handling a Washington filing?**

A: Failing to check Chapter 23.95 RCW in addition to Title 23B. The WUBOC contains substantive rules that Title 23B merely cross-references. Non-Washington counsel who look only at Title 23B often miss the substantive rules and file defective documents.

**Q: Does Washington require a corporate seal?**

A: No. Washington eliminated the corporate seal requirement years ago. A corporation may use a seal if it wishes, but it is not required.

**Q: Can a Washington corporation have classes of stock with different voting rights?**

A: Yes. The articles may specify different classes of stock with different voting rights, distribution rights, and other preferences. Multi-class structures are common in venture-backed corporations and family-owned corporations.

**Q: Are Washington corporations subject to Delaware's "entire fairness" standard in controlling shareholder transactions?**

A: Not formally, but Washington courts look to Delaware jurisprudence for guidance on fiduciary duty matters. The entire fairness standard (requiring fair dealing and fair price in controlling shareholder transactions) is treated as persuasive authority in Washington courts. Practitioners advising on controlling shareholder transactions in Washington should apply the Delaware framework as a planning baseline.

**Q: What is the consequence of a Washington corporation failing to hold an annual shareholder meeting?**

A: The failure does not automatically invalidate corporate actions or forfeit corporate existence. However, it may give rise to judicial intervention (a shareholder can petition for an order compelling a meeting) and, if failure persists for two or more years, may be grounds for judicial dissolution on deadlock grounds.

**Q: Can a corporation pay dividends during a winding-up period after dissolution?**

A: Distributions during winding up are regulated. The corporation may distribute assets to shareholders only after paying or making provision for known creditors. Director liability under RCW 23B.14.080 attaches to improper distributions.

**Q: Is there any way to avoid the revenue clearance certificate requirement for dissolution?**

A: No. The revenue clearance certificate requirement in RCW 23B.14.030 is mandatory. A corporation cannot file articles of dissolution without it. Corporations with outstanding B&O or other Washington tax obligations must resolve those obligations before dissolving.

**Q: How long does a Washington corporation remain liable for pre-dissolution obligations after dissolution?**

A: Indefinitely in theory, limited by the known-claims and unknown-claims bar procedures. If the corporation follows the statutory notice procedures, known claims are barred 120 days after notice (plus any rejection/litigation period) and unknown claims are barred three years after publication. After these bar periods, the corporation's officers, directors, and shareholders have significant protection against late-arising claims.
