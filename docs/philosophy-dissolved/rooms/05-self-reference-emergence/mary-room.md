## The Paradox

In 1982, the philosopher Frank Jackson proposed a thought experiment that became one of the most debated arguments in the philosophy of mind. Mary is a brilliant neuroscientist who has spent her entire life inside a black-and-white room. She has never seen color. But she has studied color exhaustively — she knows the exact wavelengths of every hue, the precise response curves of every cone cell in the retina, the complete neural pathways from optic nerve to visual cortex, the computational transformations the brain applies to spectral data. She knows the physics, the biology, the neuroscience, and the psychophysics of color vision with perfect completeness. There is no physical fact about color that Mary does not know.

One day, the door opens, and Mary steps outside. She sees a red rose. Does she learn something new?

If she does, then her complete physical knowledge was not actually complete — there was something about the experience of red that all the physics, neuroscience, and equations could not capture. This is devastating for physicalism, the view that everything is ultimately physical. If a complete physical description of color leaves something out, then the physical description of the world is incomplete. There are facts — facts about what it is *like* to experience something — that escape the net of physics. Jackson called these facts "qualia," and he argued that they constitute a genuine addition to Mary's knowledge when she finally sees red.

If she does not learn something new, then she already knew what it was like to see red, sitting in her black-and-white room, reading equations. This strikes nearly everyone as absurd. Knowing that red light has a wavelength of 620-750 nanometers and that it activates L-cones more strongly than M-cones does not seem to tell you what red *looks like*. There is an explanatory gap between the objective description and the subjective experience, and it is difficult to see how any amount of additional objective description could bridge it. The thought experiment has a peculiar staying power because it forces a confrontation with something we all recognize but struggle to articulate: the difference between knowing about a thing and knowing the thing itself. Between the map and the territory. Between reading about the ocean and standing in the waves.

## The Foundation

**Instrument: Information Theory (F7) — Channel Capacity and Encoding**

Claude Shannon's mathematical theory of communication (1948) provides the resolving framework. Shannon's central insight is that information is not a vague concept meaning "knowledge" or "data" — it is a precise, measurable quantity defined by the reduction of uncertainty. But the theory does not stop at measuring quantity. It also formalizes the concept of a *channel*: a medium through which information is transmitted, with a specific encoding scheme, a specific bandwidth, and a specific capacity.

Shannon's channel capacity theorem states that the maximum rate at which information can be reliably transmitted over a channel depends on the channel's properties — its bandwidth, its signal-to-noise ratio, its encoding scheme. Different channels have different capacities and, crucially, carry information in different encodings. A radio channel and a fiber optic cable can both transmit the same song, but they encode it differently, carry it at different bandwidths, and have different fidelity characteristics. The information-theoretic question is not just "how much?" but "through what channel, in what encoding?"

This is the right instrument for Mary's Room because the entire debate has been conducted as if knowledge were a single undifferentiated substance — as if "knowing everything about color" meant the same thing regardless of how the knowledge arrived. Information theory provides the vocabulary to say what philosophy could not: knowledge arrives through channels, and channels are not interchangeable.

**The framework:** Information is channel-dependent. Different channels carry different encodings. Complete knowledge through one channel does not constitute complete knowledge through all channels. Channel capacity is a theorem, not a metaphor.

## The Resolution

### Two Channels, Two Encodings

Mary had access to one channel: *propositional knowledge*. This channel transmits information through language, equations, diagrams, and formal descriptions. Its encoding scheme is symbolic — discrete tokens arranged according to syntactic rules. The information it carries about color includes wavelength ranges, cone response curves, neural firing rates, opponent-process models, and everything else that can be written down or spoken aloud.

When Mary sees red, she gains access to a second channel: *experiential knowledge*. This channel transmits information through direct sensory perception. Its encoding scheme is fundamentally different — continuous, high-dimensional, integrated with spatial context, temporal dynamics, emotional valence, and embodied response. The visual channel does not deliver a number ($\lambda = 650\text{ nm}$). It delivers a quale — a unified perceptual state that integrates spectral information with memory, affect, and attentional context into something that propositional language can describe but cannot reproduce.

### The Channel Capacity Argument

Shannon's noisy channel coding theorem establishes that a channel with capacity $C$ can transmit information reliably at any rate $R < C$, and cannot transmit reliably at any rate $R > C$. The capacity depends on the channel's physical properties:

$$C = B \log_2\left(1 + \frac{S}{N}\right)$$

where $B$ is bandwidth, $S$ is signal power, and $N$ is noise power. Different channels have different values of $B$, $S$, and $N$, and therefore different capacities. More importantly for our purposes, different channels have different *encoding schemes* — the way information is mapped onto the channel's physical substrate.

The propositional channel encodes color as a symbolic specification: "the wavelength of red light is approximately $620{-}750\text{ nm}$." This is a compact encoding — perhaps 10-20 bits specifying a range within the electromagnetic spectrum. It is precise, transmissible, and lossless within its own encoding scheme.

The experiential channel encodes color as a perceptual state: a high-dimensional vector integrating spectral response, spatial context, luminance contrast, chromatic adaptation, emotional association, and attentional salience. The encoding is continuous, embodied, and context-dependent. Its dimensionality is not fully characterized, but it is vastly richer than a wavelength specification. A person who has seen thousands of reds has built an internal encoding that discriminates between scarlet and crimson, between the red of a sunset and the red of a warning light, between a red that feels warm and a red that feels aggressive. None of these discriminations are captured by "$620{-}750\text{ nm}$."

### The Codec Analogy

The clearest way to see the resolution is through an analogy that information theory makes precise. Consider an audio codec — say, MP3. The codec's specification is a document: a complete description of the encoding algorithm, the psychoacoustic model, the Huffman tables, the modified discrete cosine transform, every parameter and every bit allocation strategy. A person who has read and understood the complete MP3 specification knows everything there is to know about how the codec works. They can predict, for any input waveform, exactly what bits the encoder will produce and exactly what waveform the decoder will reconstruct.

But reading the specification is not the same as hearing music.

The specification is propositional knowledge transmitted through the textual channel. The music is experiential knowledge transmitted through the auditory channel. Both are physical processes. Both carry information. Both are fully describable by physics. There is nothing supernatural about the difference. It is simply that the textual channel and the auditory channel have different encoding schemes, and the information they carry — while related — is not interchangeable. You cannot hear a symphony by reading its score, no matter how thoroughly you understand musical notation. The score and the performance are two encodings of related but non-identical information, transmitted through channels with fundamentally different properties.

### What Mary Learns

Mary *does* learn something new when she sees red. She receives information through a channel she had never used before. The experiential channel delivers an encoding of color that the propositional channel cannot carry — not because the propositional channel is deficient, but because the two channels have different encoding schemes and different capacities. The information is different in kind, not just in quantity.

This is not a refutation of physicalism. Both channels are physical. The propositional channel operates through photons striking a page, neural processing of symbolic representations, and storage in declarative memory. The experiential channel operates through photons striking the retina, neural processing of spectral data, and storage in perceptual memory. Both are fully physical, fully causal, fully amenable to scientific description. The difference is in the encoding, not in the metaphysics.

### The Explanatory Gap as Channel Gap

The "explanatory gap" — the seemingly unbridgeable divide between objective description and subjective experience — is a channel gap. "Complete physical knowledge" as delivered through the propositional channel is complete *within that channel*. It is all the information that symbolic encoding can carry about color. It is not all the information that *all channels* can carry about color. The gap between knowing the wavelength of red and knowing what red looks like is the gap between two encoding schemes, not the gap between the physical and the non-physical.

Jackson himself came to a similar conclusion, recanting his original argument in 2003. He concluded that Mary does gain a new *ability* — the ability to recognize and imagine red — but not new *factual* knowledge about the world. Information theory provides a sharper formulation: Mary gains access to a new channel, and that channel carries information in an encoding that her previous channel could not reproduce. The world has not changed. The physics has not changed. What has changed is the set of channels through which Mary receives information about the world.

### The Key Insight

The knowledge argument does not demonstrate that physical knowledge is incomplete. It demonstrates that knowledge is channel-dependent. Different channels carry different encodings of reality, and complete reception through one channel does not constitute complete reception through all channels. The explanatory gap between objective description and subjective experience is the information-theoretic gap between two encoding schemes — real, measurable, and entirely physical.

## The Architecture

### Experiential Learning vs Documentation

The GSD skill-creator makes an architectural distinction that Mary's Room proves is epistemologically necessary: the distinction between *documentation* and *observation*.

A skill's documentation is propositional knowledge. It describes what the skill does, what inputs it expects, what outputs it produces, what constraints it respects. A developer who reads the complete documentation for a skill knows everything that the propositional channel can carry about that skill's behavior. This is Mary in the black-and-white room, reading the complete physics of color.

The observation engine provides the experiential channel. It watches skills execute on real data, records their actual behavior, measures their performance characteristics, and captures patterns that emerge only in operation. A skill that is documented as "validates commit messages" might, in practice, exhibit latency spikes on messages containing Unicode, or silently pass malformed conventional-commit prefixes that technically satisfy the regex but violate the intent. These behaviors are information carried by the experiential channel — the channel of execution observation — that the propositional channel of documentation cannot reproduce.

The skill-creator maintains both channels because Mary's Room tells us it must. Complete documentation, no matter how thorough, does not carry the information that execution observation carries. The observation engine exists for the same reason Mary learns something new when she sees red: different channels, different encodings, different information.

### Educational Pack Design

The same principle shapes how the educational packs are structured. A pack that provides only reading material — markdown files, equations, historical context — transmits through the propositional channel alone. This is Mary's black-and-white room, furnished with excellent textbooks.

The wasteland newcomer pack includes both RESOURCES (reading material, propositional channel) and PHASES with checkpoints (guided practice, experiential channel). A newcomer who reads about the GSD workflow has propositional knowledge. A newcomer who executes a milestone — who watches a plan go through RED-GREEN cycles, who sees a commit blocked by a hook and understands *why* through the experience of it — has experiential knowledge. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.

Mary's Room is not a puzzle to be solved. It is an architectural requirement. Any system that claims to transmit complete knowledge through a single channel is making a claim that information theory proves false. The skill-creator's dual-channel architecture — documentation plus observation, reading plus doing, propositional plus experiential — is the engineering response to a philosophical insight that took forty years to resolve and that Shannon's theorem could have settled in 1948.

**Component:** GSD Observation Engine + Educational Pack Architecture
**Correspondence:** Mary's Room proves that propositional and experiential channels carry non-interchangeable information. The observation engine (experiential channel) exists alongside documentation (propositional channel) because neither alone constitutes complete knowledge. Educational packs include both reading and guided practice for the same information-theoretic reason: the codec spec is not the music.
