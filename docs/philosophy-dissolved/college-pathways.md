# College Pathways — Philosophy Dissolved

**Purpose:** Maps each room and paradox to its home in the college department structure, creating bidirectional navigation between the pack and the curriculum.

---

## Department Anchors

The pack is cross-listed across four departments:

| Department | Wing(s) | Role |
|-----------|---------|------|
| **Philosophy** | Epistemology (primary), Wonder & Questioning, Logic & Reasoning | Home department. Epistemology wing owns the core question: "how do we know?" |
| **Logic** | Applied Logic, Formal Logic, Arguments & Reasoning | The formal systems that resolve the paradoxes live here |
| **Critical Thinking** | Claims & Evidence, Applied Thinking, Metacognition | The practical application: evaluating evidence, recognizing reasoning failures |
| **Mathematics** | (cross-department) | Every resolution depends on a mathematical foundation (F3-F8) |

## Room-to-Wing Mapping

### Room 1: Evidence & Confirmation

```
Primary:    Philosophy → Epistemology
            "How do we know what counts as evidence?"

Cross-listed:
  Critical Thinking → Claims & Evidence
    - Hempel teaches: evidence has measurable weight, not just presence/absence
    - Goodman teaches: predicate choice determines what evidence means
    - Duhem-Quine teaches: no hypothesis is tested in isolation

  Mathematics
    - Information Theory: mutual information, Bayes factors, MDL
    - Category Theory: commutative diagrams for holistic testing
```

**Concepts introduced:** Bayesian confirmation, mutual information I(X;Y), Kolmogorov complexity, Minimum Description Length, commutative diagrams, diagnostic weight

**Prerequisite knowledge:** Basic probability (what is P(A|B)?), basic set theory (what is a set?)

---

### Room 2: Identity & Persistence

```
Primary:    Philosophy → Epistemology
            "What makes a thing itself across time and change?"

Cross-listed:
  Critical Thinking → Metacognition
    - Ship of Theseus teaches: identity is pattern, not substrate
    - Sorites teaches: demanding binary answers to continuous questions creates paradoxes
    - Teletransportation teaches: identity can be measured as information fidelity

  Mathematics
    - Set Theory: fuzzy membership functions, equivalence classes
    - Information Theory: standing waves, channel capacity, no-cloning theorem
```

**Concepts introduced:** Fuzzy set membership mu(x) in [0,1], eigenvalue identity, information-theoretic identity, channel fidelity, quantum no-cloning

**Prerequisite knowledge:** Room 1 concepts (information theory basics), basic calculus (what is a function?)

---

### Room 3: Infinity & Motion

```
Primary:    Mathematics
            "How can infinite processes have finite results?"

Cross-listed:
  Logic → Formal Logic
    - Zeno teaches: intuitions about infinity can be wrong and provably so
    - Thomson teaches: not all infinite processes converge

  Philosophy → Wonder & Questioning
    - These are the oldest philosophical paradoxes (5th century BCE)
    - They motivated the invention of calculus 2000 years later
```

**Concepts introduced:** Convergent series, geometric series formula, limits, trajectory equations, simultaneous solution, supertasks, discrete vs continuous topology

**Prerequisite knowledge:** Arithmetic series, basic algebra

---

### Room 4: Decision & Prediction

```
Primary:    Philosophy → Epistemology
            "How should we act under uncertainty?"

Cross-listed:
  Logic → Arguments & Reasoning
    - Newcomb teaches: causal reasoning needs information-theoretic extension
    - Surprise Exam teaches: backward induction fails when beliefs are self-referential

  Critical Thinking → Applied Thinking
    - Sleeping Beauty teaches: ambiguous questions have multiple correct answers
    - These paradoxes have direct application in AI decision-making
```

**Concepts introduced:** Evidential vs causal decision theory, mutual information between algorithms, fixed-point problems, self-defeating prophecy, self-locating belief, sample space sensitivity

**Prerequisite knowledge:** Room 1 concepts (Bayesian updating), basic probability

---

### Room 5: Self-Reference & Emergence

```
Primary:    Logic → Applied Logic
            "What are the limits of systems that describe themselves?"

Cross-listed:
  Philosophy → Epistemology
    - Mary's Room asks: can complete propositional knowledge replace experience?
    - Chinese Room asks: can symbol manipulation constitute understanding?

  Philosophy → Logic & Reasoning
    - Liar's Paradox is the foundation of incompleteness theorems

  Critical Thinking → Metacognition
    - These paradoxes are about thinking about thinking
    - They reveal structural limits that metacognition must respect
```

**Concepts introduced:** L-system production rules, Godel's incompleteness, Tarski's undefinability, functors as structure-preserving maps, natural transformations, emergence, channel capacity and encoding schemes

**Prerequisite knowledge:** Room 3 concepts (understanding infinite processes), basic category theory intuition (what is a mapping?)

## Learning Sequences

### The Full Path (Rooms 1-5 in order)

```
Evidence → Identity → Infinity → Decision → Self-Reference
   F7        F5+F7      F3+F4       F7          F6+F8
```

This is the recommended sequence. Each room builds on concepts from the previous:
- Room 1 introduces Bayesian reasoning (used in Rooms 2, 4)
- Room 2 introduces fuzzy sets and continuous identity (used in Room 3)
- Room 3 introduces convergent processes (used in Room 4, 5)
- Room 4 introduces self-referential belief (used in Room 5)
- Room 5 synthesizes all foundations into the limits of formal systems

### The Flagship Tour (3 documents)

For readers who want the core insights without the full curriculum:

1. **Hempel's Raven** (Room 1) — evidence has measurable weight
2. **Ship of Theseus** (Room 2) — identity is pattern, not substance
3. **Chinese Room** (Room 5) — understanding is emergent structure

### The Mathematics Track

For readers entering from the math department:

1. **Zeno's Dichotomy** (Room 3) — convergent series kill Zeno
2. **Sorites** (Room 2) — fuzzy sets dissolve vague predicates
3. **Liar's Paradox** (Room 5) — L-systems formalize self-reference
4. **Hempel's Raven** (Room 1) — information theory measures evidence

### The Logic Track

For readers entering from the logic department:

1. **Liar's Paradox** (Room 5) — the limits of formal systems
2. **Duhem-Quine** (Room 1) — testing as commutative diagrams
3. **Surprise Examination** (Room 4) — backward induction failures
4. **Chinese Room** (Room 5) — understanding as functor

## Bidirectional Navigation

**From college to pack:** A student in Philosophy → Epistemology encounters "the problem of induction." The department can point them to Room 1 (Hempel, Goodman) where induction problems are resolved with mathematical precision.

**From pack to college:** A reader finishing Room 5 (Chinese Room) wants to understand functors more deeply. The pathway points them to Mathematics and Logic → Formal Logic for category theory foundations.

**The circle:** College identifies questions → Pack provides resolutions → Resolutions point to deeper mathematics → Mathematics generates new questions → College receives them. The pack sits at the intersection, translating between philosophical inquiry and mathematical rigor.
