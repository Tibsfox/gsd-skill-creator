## Summary

**The Kingsmen** arrive at E=7 -- the Portland band that recorded **"Louie Louie"** in 1963 in a single take for $50, producing one of the most famous recordings in American rock history and triggering an **FBI investigation** that lasted over two years. The FBI investigation (case number 62-111027) was initiated after complaints that the lyrics, which were barely intelligible due to the recording conditions and the microphone placement, were obscene. The Bureau employed professional analysts who reviewed the recording at multiple speeds, including half-speed and double-speed, and ultimately concluded in 1965 that the lyrics were "unintelligible at any speed" and that no obscene content could be established. The investigation is rock's most absurd bureaucratic moment, and it belongs to a Portland band. The Kingsmen are the Portland entry in the PNW garage rock triangle -- Paul Revere and the Raiders (Portland, degree 240) opened it, and The Kingsmen close it. The Garage Rock Era arc that began at the two-thirds mark at degree 240 closes here at degree 244 with E=7, a strong close that does not drop from the arc's characteristic energy range.

"Louie Louie" was written by Richard Berry in 1955 and recorded by Berry in 1957. The Kingsmen's version was not the first cover -- Paul Revere and the Raiders recorded it before The Kingsmen -- but it is the version that became the cultural monument, the song that is on every list of essential American rock recordings, the song that every garage band has played. The Kingsmen's recording is the document of what a band sounds like when it is playing at full capacity in a room with bad acoustics and a microphone in the wrong position: fully committed, slightly distorted, and completely indifferent to production values. That is the garage rock sound. The Kingsmen captured it accidentally and perfectly.

On the species side, **White Sturgeon** enters at E=4 -- **leap splash**, the first non-E=2/E=1 entry since the Steelhead at degree 239. *Acipenser transmontanus* is the **FIRST ACIPENSERIDAE entry in the engine's history** -- the first sturgeon, a family that has been on Earth for approximately **200 million years**. White Sturgeon are the largest freshwater fish in North America, reaching lengths over 20 feet and weights over 1,500 pounds in exceptional specimens; the largest confirmed White Sturgeon was over 100 years old. They are living fossils in the accurate sense: the Acipenseridae body plan has changed so little over 200 million years that Jurassic and Cretaceous fossil sturgeon are recognizable as sturgeon to any observer. The White Sturgeon's leap splash at E=4 is a significant acoustic event -- a fish the size of a large piece of furniture clearing the water and returning -- and it reflects the sturgeon's known leaping behavior, which scientists do not fully understand but which is consistent and well-documented.

White Sturgeon are also deeply connected to Celilo Falls. The sturgeon fishery at Celilo was as important as the salmon fishery to Plateau tribes, and the sturgeon that held in the deep pools below the falls were ancient fish -- individual animals that may have been alive for 80 or 100 years before the dam was built. The Dalles Dam destroyed that fishery. The degree 244 entry of the engine documents White Sturgeon and closes the Celilo Falls documentation thread that has run through degrees 236, 237, 238, 239, 243, and 244 -- salmon, lamprey, and sturgeon, all connected to the same place, all changed by the same dam.
