## What Could Be Better

- **The shader renders procedural geometry rather than archived mission imagery.** The MRO shader uses analytic curves and procedural noise rather than loading actual mission data or HiRISE imagery. A future revision could render a real HiRISE scene — a dune field, a fresh ice-exposing crater, or a documented recurring-slope-lineae site (publicly available from the Planetary Data System) — for a higher-fidelity view.
- **The MRO diorama is a forthcoming artifact.** The 3D-printable diorama STL files are referenced as forthcoming rather than provided; a future ship could include the actual STL geometry of the orbiter with its large high-gain antenna, its solar arrays, and the HiRISE / CTX / CRISM instrument deck.

## Surprises

- **A camera resolved the surface of Mars in the finest detail ever seen from orbit.** MRO's HiRISE, a 0.5 m telescope reaching about 30 cm per pixel, returned the highest-resolution images of Mars ever taken from orbit — resolving dunes and their migration, gullies, layered rock, fresh ice-exposing craters, recurring slope lineae, and even the rovers and landers on the surface, including Phoenix descending under its parachute.
- **An orbiter discovered a new class of present-day surface activity.** MRO and HiRISE discovered and identified recurring slope lineae (RSL) — narrow, seasonal dark flows that appear and lengthen on warm Martian slopes and fade in cooler seasons (McEwen et al. 2011), with later spectral evidence of hydrated salts (Ojha et al. 2015) — a newly recognized seasonal surface phenomenon, presented without overclaiming confirmed flowing liquid water.
- **A single orbiter returned more data than all previous Mars missions combined.** MRO has returned more data than any other Mars mission, enabled by its large high-gain antenna and high-rate downlink, and it serves as a primary telecommunications relay for the surface missions via Electra — the data-volume superlative, distinct from Odyssey's longest-operating record.
- **An orbiter has kept watch over Mars for about twenty years and is still working.** MRO launched in 2005 and is still operating as of 2026, with HiRISE having passed 100,000 images — a long, productive, still-active reconnaissance mission, while its CRISM instrument was powered off in 2023 after about seventeen years.
- **A jumping spider has the sharpest eye for its size.** The Johnson's Jumping Spider (*Phidippus johnsoni*), a common, boldly colored red-and-black jumping spider of the Pacific Northwest and the western US, has — like all jumping spiders (Salticidae) — the highest spatial visual acuity of any animal its size, using its large forward principal eyes to study and judge its target in fine detail before a precise leap (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of MRO's HiRISE resolving Mars in the finest detail.
- **A forest herb hides intricate detail in finely fringed petals.** Fringecup (*Tellima grandiflora*), a perennial of moist Pacific Northwest forests, streambanks, and shaded slopes (Saxifragaceae), carries small greenish-to-pink flowers whose petals are deeply and finely fringed into delicate thread-like lobes — intricate fine detail visible only on a close look (ecological description only) — a mirror of the fine detail HiRISE resolves.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain keeps the predecessor's axis vocabulary and replaces only the predecessor-specific content.** MRO shares the Mars-exploration vocabulary with the Phoenix Mars Lander predecessor (Mars, the red planet, the surface, the subsurface, water ice, dust, in situ, planetary exploration, the poles, the atmosphere, minerals) — that vocabulary is shared and correct. The rewrite removed only the Phoenix-specific content (the Phoenix name, the Mars Scout Program, PI Peter H. Smith, the Robotic Arm / TEGA / MECA / SSI / MARDI / MET instruments, the arctic landing / Vastitas Borealis / Green Valley, the perchlorate / touch-water-ice / snow-from-clouds science, the Mars Surveyor 2001 Lander / Mars Polar Lander heritage, the 2007/2008 dates, and the American Ermine + Mountain Avens pairing) and replaced it with MRO content.
2. **A sustain has no axis rotation to frame — and 1.248 was itself a sustain, so there is no rotation framing to remove.** The Mars axis opened at 1.238 (Viking, substrate-axis rotation #36); 1.239 through 1.249 are all SUSTAINS, so the clone source carries no rotation narrative, and at most a single distant note records that the Mars axis itself opened at 1.238 by rotating from the X-ray-astronomy axis (rotation #35, held complete at obs#15).
3. **The imaging first is the highest-RESOLUTION imaging from orbit — not "first images" or "first map."** HiRISE (~30 cm/pixel) returned the highest-resolution images of Mars from orbit; the catalog scopes this as the highest-RESOLUTION spatial imaging from orbit, notes that Mariner 9 (obs#2) made the first global IMAGE map and MGS (obs#9) the first global TOPOGRAPHIC map and thermal-infrared mineral survey, and never re-claims those firsts.
4. **The RSL first is the first detection of recurring slope lineae — not confirmed liquid water.** MRO's HiRISE discovered and identified recurring slope lineae, seasonal dark flows on warm slopes; the catalog scopes this as the first detection/identification of RSL and presents them as a newly identified seasonal surface phenomenon possibly involving briny or hydrated salts, without overclaiming confirmed flowing liquid water.
5. **CRISM and SHARAD are described but NOT anchored.** ESA's Mars Express OMEGA detected clays and phyllosilicates from orbit first (2004–06), so CRISM is described in the past tense (powered off 3 April 2023) and is not anchored; Mars Express's MARSIS was the first subsurface sounding radar at Mars (2005), so SHARAD is described only and is not anchored. ESA's Mars Express appears as the correct holder of those firsts — a scoping foil, not a leak.
6. **MRO is an ORBITER — never a lander or rover.** MRO works from orbit; it does not land or rove. The catalog never describes MRO landing or roving and never re-claims any lander or rover first.
7. **The mission is framed positively and in the present tense — MRO is still operating as of 2026.** MRO has worked at Mars for about twenty years and is still operating, with HiRISE having passed 100,000 images; the catalog frames the mission positively in the present tense. The one past-tense exception is CRISM, powered off on 3 April 2023, while MRO itself remains active.
8. **A sustain references its predecessor exactly once, deliberately.** The only correct predecessor reference is a single nav-prev lineage note to v1.248 Phoenix Mars Lander (Mars obs#11 — the arctic lander HiRISE imaged descending under its parachute), plus lineage references to Odyssey (1.247, obs#10), MGS (1.246, obs#9), MAVEN (1.245, obs#8), InSight (1.244, obs#7), Perseverance (1.243, obs#6), Curiosity (1.242, obs#5), the Mars Exploration Rovers (1.241, obs#4), Mars Pathfinder (1.240, obs#3), Mariner 9 (1.239, obs#2), Viking (1.238, obs#1), and Mariner 4 (1.36, the first Mars flyby).
