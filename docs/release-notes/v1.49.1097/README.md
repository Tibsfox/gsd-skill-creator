## Mission Overview

LRO is the obs#5 sustainer of the ROBOTIC-LUNAR-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA Goddard's comprehensive modern lunar mapping orbiter that circles the Moon in a low polar orbit and maps essentially the whole surface in fine detail, ranging the terrain with a laser altimeter, imaging the regolith at meter scale, and reading the surface temperatures into the coldest dark of the permanently-shadowed polar craters. ★ It was originally built and flown under NASA's Exploration Systems Mission Directorate (ESMD) as a lunar-exploration precursor — scouting future landing sites and characterizing the lunar environment for later exploration — and then transferred (about September 2010, after roughly a one-year exploration phase) to the Science Mission Directorate (SMD) for an extended science mission; it remains built and managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center. The orbiter launched on 18 June 2009 on an Atlas V from Cape Canaveral, co-manifested with the LCROSS impactor mission, and settled into a low polar mapping orbit: a dedicated comprehensive lunar mapper. The story is the launch from Cape Canaveral, the cruise into lunar orbit, the pole-to-pole mapping orbit sweeping the whole surface pass after pass, LOLA ranging the terrain to the highest resolution, LROC imaging the surface at meter scale (including the Apollo landing sites), Diviner reading the surface temperatures, and the long ongoing watch that continues year after year. The science is central: LOLA produced the highest-resolution, most precise global topographic map of the Moon — the modern lunar geodetic reference (about 6.5 billion range measurements, ~10 cm vertical precision, ~1 m accuracy, 3–5× better vertical accuracy than its MOLA predecessor; the imaging-derived component, the GLD100, comes from LROC WAC stereo); LROC imaged essentially the whole Moon at high resolution (the Narrow Angle Cameras at ~0.5 m/pixel), returning the first orbital images detailed enough to show the Apollo landing hardware (descent stages, experiment packages) and the astronauts' surface tracks — the first detailed orbital imaging of the Apollo landing sites; and Diviner mapped surface temperatures globally and measured the floors of the permanently-shadowed polar craters at a few tens of kelvin (~25–30 K) — among the coldest temperatures measured anywhere in the solar system. ★ The polar-ice result is load-bearing: LRO's instruments (LEND, Mini-RF, LAMP, Diviner) mapped and characterized the polar hydrogen / water ice and the cold traps in the permanently-shadowed regions, strengthening the picture of lunar polar volatiles — but LRO MAPS and CHARACTERIZES the ice; it is NOT the first strong evidence (Lunar Prospector, 1998) and NOT the confirmation (LCROSS, 2009). The lineage is: Clementine HINTED (1994) → Lunar Prospector gave the first STRONG evidence (1998) → LCROSS confirmed (2009) → LRO maps and characterizes the cold traps and the polar volatiles in comprehensive detail. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: the HIGHEST-RESOLUTION topography (LOLA-led, the modern reference) — distinct from Clementine's FIRST (coarse) laser-altimetry topography and from GRAIL's GRAVITY field (interior mass); the FIRST detailed orbital imaging of the Apollo landing sites (LROC resolving the hardware and tracks, where earlier orbiters imaged the regions but not at hardware-resolving scale); the COLDEST known temperatures in the solar system (Diviner, the permanently-shadowed polar crater floors); and the polar-ice MAPPING/CHARACTERIZATION (not first evidence or confirmation). ★★ The mission is STILL OPERATING (since 2009), so the catalog frames its completed firsts and the 2009 launch in the PAST (LRO launched, settled into orbit, produced the highest-resolution topographic map, returned the first detailed Apollo-site images, measured the coldest crater floors) while its ongoing role is framed in the PRESENT (LRO orbits the Moon, continues to map the surface, has returned an enormous dataset, and remains operational — the longest-lived lunar orbiter, funded through extended missions with fuel to roughly 2027). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 18 June 2009; the ESMD-to-SMD transfer about September 2010; the orbit moved to its elliptical configuration on 4 May 2015. ## Surprises

- **LOLA collected about 6.5 billion range measurements to build the highest-resolution global topographic map of the Moon.** The Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter ranged the surface pulse by pulse to about 6.5 billion measurements at ~10 cm vertical precision and ~1 m accuracy — 3–5× better vertical accuracy than its MOLA predecessor — producing the highest-resolution, most precise global topographic map of the Moon, the modern lunar geodetic reference, with the imaging-derived GLD100 component contributed by LROC WAC stereo.
- **LROC's cameras resolved the Apollo landing hardware and the astronauts' tracks from orbit.** LROC's Narrow Angle Cameras imaged essentially the whole Moon at ~0.5 m/pixel and returned the first orbital images detailed enough to show the Apollo descent stages, experiment packages, and the astronauts' surface tracks — the first detailed orbital imaging of the Apollo landing sites, where earlier orbiters had imaged the regions but not at hardware-resolving scale.

- **A long-lived freshwater turtle that surveys the same waters for decades.** The Western Pond Turtle (*Actinemys marmorata*), a long-lived freshwater turtle of Pacific Northwest ponds, slow streams, and wetlands, can live for decades, basking watchfully on logs and patiently surveying the same quiet waters for the whole of a long life, enduring far beyond the span of most of its neighbours: a mirror of LRO keeping its comprehensive watch over the whole Moon far longer than its planned mission (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A succulent that holds on year after year on bare, harsh rock.** The Broadleaf Stonecrop (*Sedum spathulifolium*), a Pacific Northwest succulent of bare rock outcrops, cliffs, and thin-soil ledges, forms rosettes of thick water-storing leaves that persist through drought and exposure on hard, sun-baked ground where little else can hold on, enduring year after year on the bare rock (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of the long endurance, keeping the watch and holding on far beyond the ordinary span.

## Dedication

v1.280 LRO is dedicated to the people across NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and its partners who built and still fly the orbiter that maps the whole Moon in fine detail. The teams who lifted it from Cape Canaveral on an Atlas V alongside LCROSS and guided it into a low polar orbit around the Moon. The engineers behind the laser altimeter, who range the terrain pulse by pulse to draw the highest-resolution topographic map yet made, and behind the cameras, who image the surface at meter scale — sharp enough to show, from orbit, the descent stages and the tracks left at the Apollo landing sites. The team behind the thermal radiometer, who read the floors of the permanently-shadowed polar craters and found among the coldest temperatures measured anywhere, and the teams behind the neutron, ultraviolet, and radar instruments who map and characterize the ice held in that cold dark. They keep a long watch over the whole Moon, year after year, far beyond the mission first planned — the longest-lived orbiter at the Moon, still circling and still mapping. They map a world in fine detail, by light and by laser, and the watch they keep goes on.
