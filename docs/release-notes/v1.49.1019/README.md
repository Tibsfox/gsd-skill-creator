## Summary

v1.49.1019 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.209 to 1.210, adding Terra — the flagship of NASA's Earth Observing System, a single spacecraft carrying five instruments to measure the land, the oceans, and the atmosphere together — as the obs#2 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis (axis-rotation #30 sustained at obs#2). The earth-observation axis opened at v1018 Landsat with the systematic multispectral imaging of Earth's land surface and the longest continuous land record, the land-surface form; at v1019 Terra extends the axis with a substrate-form-distinct observation: the flagship multi-instrument observatory that takes the whole Earth system as its subject. Where Landsat carried a single high-resolution land imager on a 16-day repeat, Terra carries five instruments on one platform and images the whole planet every day or two at moderate resolution.

Terra is managed by NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center and is an international partnership, with the spacecraft and three instruments from the United States, ASTER contributed by Japan, and MOPITT by Canada. The spacecraft reached its Sun-synchronous near-polar orbit on 18 December 1999, launched as EOS AM-1 on an Atlas IIAS rocket from Vandenberg Air Force Base, California, crossing the equator in the morning at about 705 kilometers. Designed for about a six-year life, the flagship has operated far beyond it, continuing today to measure the whole Earth system.

The signature of the flagship is the multi-instrument Earth-system observatory. Terra carries five instruments on one spacecraft — MODIS, ASTER, CERES, MISR, and MOPITT — to measure the land, the oceans, and the atmosphere together, extending Earth observation from the land surface to the whole Earth system.

The flagship of the Earth Observing System opens the era of the great observatories. Terra was the first of NASA's great EOS observatories, the flagship of a program built to study the whole planet as a connected system over the long term, preparing the way for the companion observatories that followed.

The whole-Earth-system synergy is the measurement the flagship made possible. Because Terra's five instruments view the same scene at the same time, the land, the clouds, the aerosols, the energy budget, and the air can be measured together and related to one another, so the parts of the Earth system are studied as one connected whole.

The near-daily global moderate-resolution coverage carries the survey at a daily cadence. Terra's MODIS images the whole planet every day or two at moderate resolution, a near-daily global view of the land, the oceans, and the atmosphere, where Landsat's high-resolution land record repeats every couple of weeks.

The energy budget and the atmosphere are read alongside the surface. CERES measures the planet's incoming sunlight against its outgoing reflected light and emitted heat to give the radiation balance, MISR views each scene from nine angles to read aerosols and clouds, and MOPITT measures the gases of the lower atmosphere.

The axis-relevant substrate of Terra at this degree is its CONTINUATION of the EARTH-OBSERVATION substrate-axis at obs#2, the flagship multi-instrument observation of the whole Earth system. Terra sustains the EARTH-OBSERVATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#2 NEW LOCKED and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#82 cumulative (axis-rotation #30 sustained at obs#2). The forward continuation may favor a later Earth-observing mission that sustains the axis at obs#3 — the afternoon companion observatory of the water cycle, an ice-and-elevation altimetry mission, or a gravity / soil-moisture mission — or a substrate-axis rotation #31 to an under-served axis.

Terra extends the earth-observation way of seeing on the catalog, and the mission records the Terra team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the MODIS, CERES, and MISR instrument teams, the Japanese ASTER team, and the Canadian MOPITT team. The engine state otherwise advances: the NASA degree advances 1.209 to 1.210, the counter-cadence count is unchanged at 5, and MUS, ELC, SPS, and TRS all remain SCAFFOLD-PENDING at obs#93 cumulative.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis grows by extending its subject, not only by opening new axes.** By extending the systematic Earth observation Landsat established for the land surface to the whole Earth system, Terra sustained the earth-observation axis at obs#2 with a substrate-form-distinct observation, showing that an axis can continue at a new substrate-form rather than always rotating to a new axis.
2. **Many instruments on one platform measure the connections between the parts.** By carrying five instruments that view the same scene at the same time, Terra let the land, the clouds, the aerosols, the energy budget, and the air be measured together and related to one another, showing that simultaneous, co-located measurement reads the connections that separate instruments cannot.
3. **A flagship opens an era and prepares the way.** By being the first of the great EOS observatories, Terra opened the era of multi-instrument whole-Earth-system observatories and prepared the way for the companions that followed, showing that a flagship sets the form the program builds on.
4. **A wide swath buys a near-daily cadence at moderate resolution.** By imaging a wide swath from a morning orbit, MODIS tiled the whole planet in a day or two, showing that breadth of coverage and frequency of revisit trade against fine resolution — a different point on the same design space from a high-resolution land imager.
5. **The energy budget is read by measuring both what comes in and what goes out.** By measuring the incoming sunlight against the outgoing reflected light and emitted heat, CERES read the radiation balance directly, showing that the budget that drives the climate system is a measurement of both halves at once.
6. **An international partnership joins many contributions into one observatory.** By carrying instruments from the United States, Japan, and Canada on one platform with open data, Terra joined many contributions into one flagship, showing that a shared platform and open data turn separate instruments into one observatory of the whole planet.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across an intra-axis continuation.** Because Terra reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.209 Landsat template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the flagship while keeping the warm palette, making the intra-axis continuation a clean build.

A future revision could load encoded Terra imagery for a higher-fidelity rendering keyed to real land cover, ocean color, and clouds.

## Dedication

v1.210 Terra is dedicated to the people who built and have kept the flagship of NASA's Earth Observing System, the observatory that measures the whole planet as one connected system: the Terra team at NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, who lead the flagship and keep it measuring the land, the oceans, and the atmosphere together; the MODIS team, whose instrument images the whole Earth every day or two; the CERES team at NASA's Langley Research Center, who measure the planet's incoming and outgoing energy; the MISR team at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, who view each scene from nine angles at once; the Japanese ASTER team, whose high-resolution imager is Japan's contribution to the flagship; and the Canadian MOPITT team, whose instrument reads the gases of the lower air, Canada's contribution. Terra views the same scene with five instruments together, so the parts of the Earth system can be measured as one connected whole, and the flagship continues today, far beyond its planned life.
