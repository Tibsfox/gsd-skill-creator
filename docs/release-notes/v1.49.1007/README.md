## Summary

v1.49.1007 advances the NASA degree catalog from 1.197 to 1.198, adding RHESSI — the Reuven Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager — as the obs#3 INTRA-AXIS continuation that sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#3). The axis opened at v1005 OSO with the continuous solar-cycle monitoring program and sustained at v1006 TRACE with the high-resolution extreme-ultraviolet imaging of the corona. RHESSI extends that solar watch into the high-energy regime: where TRACE imaged the corona and its fine loops in the EUV, RHESSI imaged the flare itself in hard X-rays and gamma rays, resolving where on the Sun the flare releases its energy and where it accelerates electrons and ions. The axis now holds three observations on the Sun, the continuous-monitoring program at OSO, the high-resolution EUV imaging at TRACE, and the high-energy flare imaging spectroscopy at RHESSI, sustaining at obs#3 cumulative.

RHESSI was a NASA Small Explorer (SMEX) led by the University of California, Berkeley Space Sciences Laboratory, managed with NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, with Robert Lin as principal investigator, and named in honor of the solar gamma-ray physicist Reuven Ramaty. The mission launched on 5 February 2002 aboard a Pegasus-XL into a low-inclination Earth orbit and operated through 2018. A spin-stabilized spacecraft carried nine bi-grid rotating modulation collimators in front of nine cooled germanium detectors, imaging and measuring the spectra of solar flares in hard X-rays and gamma rays from a few kiloelectronvolts to about seventeen megaelectronvolts.

The signature of the mission is the first imaging spectroscopy of solar flares in hard X-rays and gamma rays. Because light at these energies cannot be focused by ordinary mirrors, RHESSI imaged indirectly: the spin of the spacecraft carried the fine grids of its collimators across the incoming flux, and the image of the flare was reconstructed from the time-varying signal by a Fourier-style method, while the germanium detectors measured the spectrum at every imaged location.

The mission recorded a landmark in the study of the flare energy release. By imaging the hard-X-ray footpoints and coronal sources, RHESSI mapped where on the Sun flares accelerate electrons; by imaging the gamma-ray lines emitted when accelerated ions strike the lower atmosphere, it mapped where flares accelerate ions, the first images of the locations of ion acceleration in a flare. Comparing the two showed that accelerated ions and electrons can reach distinct parts of a flare. RHESSI also measured the solar shape precisely and detected terrestrial gamma-ray flashes as a bonus.

The spin-stabilized platform gave the imaging its foundation. By carrying the collimator grids across the incoming high-energy flux, the steady spin modulated the signal of a source so its position could be recovered, the rotation itself central to the indirect imaging the wide-band detectors recorded.

The axis-relevant substrate of RHESSI at this degree is its continuation of the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY substrate-axis at obs#3 on the Sun, extending the EUV imaging TRACE established into the high-energy regime. RHESSI sustains the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION thread at obs#3 NEW LOCKED, extends the SOLAR-OBSERVATORY-IMAGING-LINEAGE thread at obs#2 cumulative, and sustains the SUBSTRATE-AXIS-ROTATION-DISCIPLINE thread at obs#70 cumulative (axis-rotation #26 sustained at obs#3). The forward continuation favors later solar observatories — dedicated coronagraphs, full-disk high-cadence dynamics observatories, interface-region spectrographs, and heliospheric and in-situ solar-wind missions.

RHESSI opens the high-energy end of the solar-imaging lineage on the solar axis, and the mission records the RHESSI team at the University of California, Berkeley Space Sciences Laboratory, Robert Lin and the mission team, NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, and the namesake Reuven Ramaty. ## Lessons Learned

1. **Imaging where energy is released reveals what a flux measurement averages over.** Rather than measuring the total flux of a flare, RHESSI imaged where on the Sun the high-energy emission came from, showing that resolving a source into its sites reveals the footpoints and coronal sources a single total only sums.
2. **An indirect method can image where focusing optics do not work.** By using rotating modulation collimators and the spin to modulate the flux, RHESSI reconstructed images at energies that cannot be focused by mirrors, showing that an indirect Fourier method opens imaging where direct optics fail.
3. **Imaging and spectroscopy together tie energy to location.** By measuring the spectrum at every imaged location, RHESSI read both where the flare released its energy and what kind of emission it was, showing that imaging spectroscopy carries more than either imaging or spectroscopy alone.
4. **Imaging the gamma-ray lines separates ion from electron acceleration.** By imaging the gamma-ray lines of ion acceleration and the hard-X-ray footpoints of electron acceleration, RHESSI showed that ions and electrons can reach distinct parts of a flare, separating two acceleration sites for the first time.
5. **A continuation can deepen an axis across the spectrum.** By extending the EUV imaging of the corona into the high-energy imaging of the flare, RHESSI sustained the solar-observatory axis at obs#3 on the same target, showing that an axis can deepen across the spectrum without rotating away.
6. **The spin can be the imaging instrument, not just the stabilizer.** By making the spin carry the collimator grids across the incoming flux, RHESSI turned the rotation that stabilized the spacecraft into the mechanism that modulated the signal, showing that a design choice can serve two ends at once.
7. **A reference template carries forward cleanly across a distinct-palette mission.** Because RHESSI reuses the canonical card structure of the v1.197 template, the build preserved the structural template exactly and swapped the content to the high-energy flare imaging with a distinct flare-amber / footpoint-gold / gamma-bright palette, making the distinct-palette mission a clean build.

## Surprises

- **A spinning instrument with grids imaged the Sun where mirrors could not.** Without any focusing optics, RHESSI reconstructed images of flares in hard X-rays and gamma rays from the modulated signal of its collimators, so the catalog's first high-energy solar imaging came from a spin and a set of grids rather than a telescope.
- **A pinpointing woodpecker mirrors the flare-site imager.** The Pileated Woodpecker, which locates the hidden galleries beneath the bark and strikes the exact spot, enters as the pinpointing mirror to the instrument that resolved where on the Sun a flare releases its energy, so the catalog pairs a precision-striking woodpecker with a flare-site imaging mission.
- **A self-heating plant mirrors the energetic flare core.** The Western Skunk Cabbage warms its own spadix above the cold wetland, so the catalog's high-energy flare imaging is paired with a plant that generates its own energetic core, the warmth against the cold mirroring the flare against the quiet Sun.

## Dedication

v1.198 RHESSI is dedicated to the team that conceived and flew the Reuven Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager: the RHESSI team at the University of California, Berkeley Space Sciences Laboratory who led the mission and built the rotating modulation collimators and the cooled germanium detectors; Robert Lin and the mission team who guided the high-energy imaging spectroscopy of solar flares; NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, which managed the mission within the Small Explorer program; and the operations and science teams who held the spin steady and reconstructed the flare images. It is dedicated as well to the namesake, the solar gamma-ray physicist Reuven Ramaty, whose work on flare-accelerated particles the mission carried forward, and to the wider community of solar physicists whose picture of the flare energy release these high-energy images grounded. RHESSI imaged solar flares in hard X-rays and gamma rays, resolved where on the Sun a flare releases its energy, and produced the first images of where flares accelerate electrons and ions.
