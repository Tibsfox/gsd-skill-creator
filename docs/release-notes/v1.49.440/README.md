## Part B: Great Horned Owl Territorial War — Multi-Night Hooting Battle at E=6, *Bubo virginianus*, Strigidae, Deep Resonant hoo-hoo-hoo at 200-500 Hz male and 300-600 Hz female, 1-3 km Carrying Distance, November-December Territorial Establishment Before the January-February Nesting Calendar, RETURN TO STRIGIDAE after the Northern Spotted Owl at degree 4

- **Strigidae Return and the Generalist-Specialist Contrast Completion.** The **Great Horned Owl Territorial War** at degree 299 is the first **Strigidae** entry since the **Northern Spotted Owl** at degree 4 of the engine's SPS track. The Spotted Owl (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) is the family's specialist pole: silent hunter, dark-eyed (not yellow), old-growth-dependent, culturally contentious as the species whose habitat requirements drove the Pacific Northwest old-growth timber controversy. The Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*) is the family's generalist pole: loud and assertive, yellow-eyed, present in virtually every forested or partially forested habitat type in the region, ecologically dominant as the apex avian predator in most Pacific Northwest habitats. The two engine entries together constitute a complete portrait of the family's internal ecological diversity, and the contrast is structurally load-bearing: the Great Horned Owl is the primary predator of the **Barred Owl** (*Strix varia*) and one of the few predators of the adult Spotted Owl, and the species' predatory dominance shapes the nocturnal raptor community in any area it occupies. The Spotted Owl is specifically displaced from areas with high Great Horned Owl densities, and the competitive-and-predatory relationship is one of the drivers of Spotted Owl range contraction. The engine's documentation preserves this intra-family ecological contrast at first-class resolution rather than treating Strigidae as a generic category.
- ***Bubo virginianus* Morphology: Ear Tufts, Facial Disc, Yellow Eyes, Wingspan 36-60 Inches.** The Great Horned Owl (*Bubo virginianus*, described by **Gmelin** in 1788 from the Virginia type locality) is a large, heavy-bodied owl. The wingspan is 36-60 inches (91-152 cm). The prominent ear tufts ("horns") are feather tufts that function as communication structures — not ears — and the species' actual ear openings are located asymmetrically on the skull to enable precise localization of sound. The facial disc channels sound to the ear openings. The eyes are yellow, in contrast to the dark eyes of the Spotted Owl. The plumage is rich brown with barred underparts and a white throat patch. The scientific name *Bubo* comes from classical Latin (the bubo or great horned owl), itself from the Greek *boubos* (the Eurasian Eagle-Owl); *virginianus* means "of Virginia" from the type locality. The species is one of the most widespread owls in North America, occurring from the Arctic tree line to Tierra del Fuego, and the Pacific Northwest populations are continuous with the broader continental distribution.
- **The Territorial Hooting War: Multi-Night Sustained Acoustic Interaction.** The Great Horned Owl's territorial "war" is a sustained multi-night acoustic event. The primary territorial vocalization is the deep, resonant **hoo-hoo-hoo** sequence: typically 3-8 notes delivered in a low-pitched, rhythmic pattern. The fundamental frequency is approximately 200-500 Hz for the male (lower-pitched) and 300-600 Hz for the female (higher-pitched) — one of the lowest-pitched bird vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest, and the depth and resonance of the call allows it to propagate across large distances through forest and open terrain. A territorial war involves two or more owls exchanging calls across a territorial boundary for extended periods: the interaction begins with one owl calling and a neighboring owl responding from the adjacent territory; the exchange can continue for hours, each owl calling in the gaps of the other's calls (counter-calling) or calling simultaneously (overlapping calls, which signal heightened territorial competition); the interaction may repeat over multiple consecutive nights. Mated pairs of Great Horned Owls often call in coordinated **duets** during territorial and pair-bonding interactions, with the male's deeper hoo and the female's higher-pitched hoo produced in alternation creating a two-part vocal texture that is distinctively more complex than a single owl calling.
- **Carrying Distance and the 1-3 km Acoustic Range in Pacific Northwest Winter.** The Great Horned Owl's hoo sequence carries 1-3 kilometers in ideal conditions — calm night, clear temperature gradient, minimal atmospheric turbulence. In the Pacific Northwest winter, where December-January nights are often calm and cold, the carrying distance is maximized. An owl calling from a **Douglas-fir** grove can be heard across a valley. The spatial scale of a territorial war is correspondingly large, and the negotiation of territorial boundaries is conducted across the same scale — a pair of adjacent owls may be 1-2 kilometers apart, with the territorial exchange reaching them both across a terrain that includes mixed forest, open woodland, and riparian corridors. The carrying distance is structurally important for the engine's SPS documentation because it establishes the spatial register at which the territorial war operates, and the register is distinct from the smaller-scale territorial registers of smaller owls or of songbirds.
- **The Winter Acoustic Vacuum and the Counter-Seasonal Loudness Argument.** The Great Horned Owl's territorial hooting war takes place in a specific acoustic environment that the engine documents at first-class resolution. December-January in the Pacific Northwest is acoustically sparse: most songbirds are silent or have departed, frogs are dormant, insects are absent, and the forest is structurally simplified by the loss of deciduous canopy. Into this acoustic vacuum the Great Horned Owl's deep hoo carries unimpeded through the cold calm air, reaching human ears at distances where other bird sounds have become inaudible. E=6 in December is equivalent to a higher energy level in May, because the acoustic context of the winter night is so sparse that the owl's call dominates the soundscape at a level that would require much higher absolute amplitude to achieve in the acoustic richness of spring. The counter-seasonal loudness argument is structurally important because it registers the ecological context in which the owl's territorial behavior becomes maximally audible, and the context cannot be reduced to absolute sound pressure measurement. The Great Horned Owl is one of the loudest sounds in the winter Pacific Northwest night precisely because it is one of the only sounds.
- **Early-Nesting Phenology and the November-December Territorial Establishment Window.** The Great Horned Owl is the earliest-nesting owl in North America, often earlier than most birds in the Pacific Northwest. Eggs are typically laid January-February, with incubation lasting approximately 28-35 days; chicks hatch in February-March when Pacific Northwest temperatures are still cold and prey is limited. The early-nesting strategy allows the Great Horned Owl to take advantage of high early-spring prey abundance as chicks are growing (March-April, when small mammals and waterfowl are highly active), so that by the time food demand is maximum the spring prey abundance is at its peak. To be ready to nest in January, Great Horned Owls must establish and defend territories in November-December. The hooting wars of December and January are the direct consequence of this early calendar: owls are competing for the territories they need to hold through the breeding season that begins in a matter of weeks. The territorial establishment precedes the nesting, and the territorial hooting is the acoustic infrastructure of the early-calendar nesting strategy.
- **Apex Predator Ecology: Northern Flying Squirrel, Snowshoe Hare, American Coot, Raptor-on-Raptor Predation.** The Great Horned Owl is a powerful generalist predator. Prey in the Pacific Northwest includes **Northern Flying Squirrel**, **Douglas squirrel**, **snowshoe hare**, **cottontail rabbit**, **American coot**, ducks, **American crow**, and other raptors including smaller owls and occasionally **Osprey** or **Red-tailed Hawk**. The Great Horned Owl is the apex avian predator in most Pacific Northwest habitats, and the species will prey on other raptors when the opportunity presents. The predatory dominance is ecologically load-bearing: it shapes the nocturnal raptor community in any area the Great Horned Owl occupies, with the **Barred Owl** (*Strix varia*) specifically vulnerable to Great Horned Owl predation and the **Northern Spotted Owl** (*Strix occidentalis caurina*) at risk from Great Horned Owl predation where the species' ranges overlap. The predatory dominance is one of the factors contributing to Spotted Owl range contraction, and the engine's documentation preserves the relationship at first-class resolution.
- **Broad Habitat Distribution: Old-Growth, Second-Growth, Forest-Edge, Riparian, Suburban Parks, Farmland Woodlots.** The Great Horned Owl is a habitat generalist of unusual breadth. It occurs in virtually every forested and partially forested habitat type in the Pacific Northwest: old-growth forest, second-growth forest, mixed forest-grassland edge, riparian corridors, suburban parks, farmland with woodlots, open woodland with large trees. The species can live in almost any environment that provides some tree cover and prey. This breadth is ecologically distinct from the Northern Spotted Owl's narrow old-growth requirement, and the contrast is structurally load-bearing for the engine's Strigidae documentation. The broad habitat distribution means that the Great Horned Owl's territorial war is audible across a wide range of Pacific Northwest ecological contexts — not only in remote old-growth forest but in suburban parks, in farmland with woodlots, in the riparian corridors that thread through the urbanized Puget Sound lowlands. The species' winter prominence in human soundscape perception is a direct function of this distribution: the Great Horned Owl's hoo is one of the most commonly reported wildlife sounds by winter hikers, campers, and anyone in rural or semi-rural Pacific Northwest environments in December-January, because the owl occupies habitats that are accessible to human observation and because the acoustic vacuum of the winter night allows the call to carry at audible distances from human settlements.
