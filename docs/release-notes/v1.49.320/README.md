**Pacific Harbor Seal Subspecies, Population, and Salish Sea Distribution.** The Pacific Harbor Seal subspecies *Phoca vitulina richardii* is the **most abundant pinniped** in the Salish Sea, with a current Washington inland-waters population estimated at **~15,000-17,000 animals** based on aerial haul-out surveys coordinated by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) Marine Mammal Program** and the **National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) National Marine Fisheries Service**. The subspecies is distinct from the Atlantic harbor seal (*P. v. vitulina* European and *P. v. concolor* western North Atlantic), the freshwater Seal Lake population (*P. v. mellonae* in northern Quebec), and the western Pacific subspecies (*P. v. stejnegeri* in Japan and the Sea of Okhotsk). The Pacific Harbor Seal is a **year-round resident** of the Salish Sea (non-migratory), hauling out on rocks, log booms, gravel spits, sand bars, and floating docks throughout Puget Sound, Hood Canal, Admiralty Inlet, Padilla Bay, Skagit Bay, and the northern Strait of Juan de Fuca. Principal haul-out sites include **Protection Island** (which also hosts the Rhinoceros Auklet colony documented at degree 175), **San Juan Islands** (with **Friday Harbor** as the nearest human-settlement reference), **Bainbridge Island**, **Blake Island**, **Vashon Island**, **Fort Flagler**, **Dungeness Spit**, and numerous working log booms along commercial-navigation channels. Pupping season runs May-September with peak births in June-July; pups are born **precocial** (eyes open, fur developed, capable of swimming within hours of birth) and nurse for approximately **4-6 weeks** on milk that is approximately **50% fat** — an extreme high-fat nursing regime that allows the pup to gain approximately **1 kilogram per day** during the nursing period and to reach weaning weight of approximately **24 kilograms**. After weaning, pups forage independently and survive at approximately 50% first-year survival rate depending on local conditions.

**The Low Breathy Hwooo and the Mammalian Acoustic Register.** The Harbor Seal's defining acoustic signature for the engine's purposes is the **low breathy hwooo** — a relaxed exhalation vocalization produced at haul-outs during daylight rest periods, typically between individuals as part of social communication and spacing behavior. The engine's acoustic vocabulary at degree 180 now includes the mammalian laryngeal-exhalation register as a first-class acoustic category distinct from the syringeal vocal production of Aves, and the E=4 calibration reflects the call's clarity and carry distance at the individual haul-out scale while recognizing that larger haul-outs (tens to hundreds of individuals) produce a collective soundscape at a higher aggregate intensity. ## Part B: Harbor Seal — The First Marine Mammal and the Crossing of the Taxonomic Boundary Into Mammalia

- **First Mammal of Any Kind in the Engine's First 180 Degrees.** The Harbor Seal (*Phoca vitulina richardii*) enters the engine at degree 180 as the **FIRST MAMMAL** — not merely the first marine mammal, but the first mammal of any class — after one hundred and seventy-nine consecutive degrees of **class Aves**, a taxonomic boundary-crossing event whose structural magnitude is proportional to the geometric halfway mark of the full 360-degree rotation and opens simultaneously the class Mammalia, the order Carnivora, the family Phocidae, the subfamily Phocinae, and the genus Phoca as a taxonomic multi-opening event unprecedented in the engine.
- **Class Mammalia, Order Carnivora, Family Phocidae Simultaneous Opening.** The Harbor Seal's entry opens **class Mammalia** (vertebrate class distinct from Aves), **order Carnivora** (mammalian order that includes cats, dogs, bears, weasels, and pinnipeds among others), **family Phocidae** (true or earless seals, distinguishing from family Otariidae eared-seals-and-sea-lions), **subfamily Phocinae**, and **genus Phoca** (the type genus of Phocidae), all simultaneously at degree 180; future Mammalia entries can reference the Harbor Seal as the established baseline taxonomic opening.
- **Pacific Harbor Seal Subspecies *richardii*.** The Pacific Harbor Seal subspecies *Phoca vitulina richardii* is the Pacific Northwest genetic population, distinct from the Atlantic harbor seal (*P. v. vitulina* European and *P. v. concolor* western North Atlantic), the Seal Lake freshwater subspecies (*P. v. mellonae* in northern Quebec), and the western Pacific subspecies (*P. v. stejnegeri* in Japan and the Sea of Okhotsk); subspecies identification anchors the engine's subspecific taxonomy vocabulary as well.
- **Low Breathy Hwooo and the Mammalian Acoustic Register.** The defining acoustic signature is a **low breathy hwooo** exhalation vocalization produced at haul-outs during daylight rest periods — non-syringeal (using the larynx rather than the avian syrinx), exhalation-based, low fundamental frequency (typically 100-300 Hz), and breathy unstructured timbre — establishing the mammalian laryngeal-exhalation acoustic register at E=4 as distinct from all 179 prior Aves syringeal vocalizations in the engine.
- **Haul-Out Behavior as First Non-Nesting Terrestrial Register.** Harbor Seals emerge from water onto rocks, log booms, gravel spits, sand bars, and floating docks for rest, thermoregulation, molting, and pupping during daylight hours; the haul-out behavior enters the engine's behavioral-ecology vocabulary as the first non-nesting terrestrial register, introducing concepts including thermoregulation-by-emergence, tidal-cycle synchronization, social spacing, and site-fidelity-without-nest-construction as first-class behavioral-ecology vocabulary.
- **Salish Sea Distribution and Haul-Out Site Geography.** The Pacific Harbor Seal is the **most abundant pinniped** in the Salish Sea at an estimated **~15,000-17,000 animals** across Washington inland waters, with principal haul-out sites including **Hood Canal**, **Admiralty Inlet**, **Padilla Bay**, **Skagit Bay**, **Protection Island** (which also hosts the Rhinoceros Auklet colony from degree 175), the **San Juan Islands**, **Bainbridge Island**, **Blake Island**, **Vashon Island**, **Fort Flagler**, **Dungeness Spit**, and numerous log booms along commercial-navigation channels; the Salish Sea enters the engine's primary marine-geography vocabulary at degree 180.
- **Precocial Pupping and High-Fat Nursing Regime.** Pupping season runs May-September with peak births in June-July; Harbor Seal pups are born **precocial** with eyes open, fur developed, and the ability to swim within hours of birth, and nurse for approximately **4-6 weeks** on milk that is approximately **50% fat** — an extreme high-fat nursing regime that allows the pup to gain approximately **1 kilogram per day** and reach weaning weight of approximately **24 kilograms** before independent foraging; the precocial-pupping register enters the engine's life-history vocabulary.
- **Principal Prey and Salish Sea Food Web Role.** Primary prey species include **Pacific herring**, **Pacific hake**, **salmon smolts**, **flatfish** (flounder, sole, sand dabs), **rockfish**, and **octopus**, with diet composition varying by location and season; the Harbor Seal occupies a mid-level predatory role in the Salish Sea food web and interacts with the broader marine-predator community including Orcas (transient killer whales, which prey on Harbor Seals), Steller Sea Lions, California Sea Lions, and the seabird community that shares haul-out sites and prey species.
- **Marine Mammal Protection Act Context and Recovery Trajectory.** Harbor Seals have been protected under the **Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) of 1972** since its passage, administered by **NOAA National Marine Fisheries Service**, and the species has recovered from mid-twentieth-century low populations (due to commercial bounty hunting for fish-conflict reasons through 1960 in Washington state) to current abundance; the MMPA framework enters the engine's conservation vocabulary as distinct from the **Endangered Species Act (ESA)** and enables all marine mammal documentation across the remainder of the engine.
- **NOAA Fisheries and WDFW Stock Assessment and Monitoring.** Population monitoring is coordinated by **NOAA Fisheries West Coast Region** through periodic stock assessment reports and by the **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) Marine Mammal Program** through aerial haul-out surveys during pupping and molting seasons; additional monitoring and stranding response occurs through **The Whale Museum** (**Friday Harbor**) **Soundwatch Boater Education Program** and the **San Juan County Marine Mammal Stranding Network**.
