## Mission Overview

ECOSTRESS is the obs#27 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA/JPL's ISS-hosted thermal-infrared radiometer, the ECOsystem Spaceborne Thermal Radiometer Experiment on Space Station that measures the land surface temperature of Earth's living land from the International Space Station and reads from that temperature the evapotranspiration, the water use, and the water stress of the world's plants. ★ ECOSTRESS is a NASA Earth Venture Instrument (EVI-2) mission built and led by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), hosted on the ISS and mounted on the Japanese Experiment Module – Exposed Facility (JEM-EF / Kibō Exposed Facility). ★★ Its instrument is the PHyTIR (Prototype HyspIRI Thermal Infrared Radiometer), a cross-track push-whiskbroom multiband thermal-infrared radiometer with five bands in the ~8–12.5 µm thermal window (plus one shortwave band for cloud and geolocation), at roughly ~38 m in-track × ~69 m cross-track (~70 m; science requirement 100 m) over a ~400 km swath. ★ It maps the land surface temperature and emissivity (the L2_LSTE product) and from it the evapotranspiration (the L3_ET product, by the PT-JPL algorithm, partitioned into canopy transpiration, soil evaporation, and interception), the Water Use Efficiency (L4_WUE), and the Evaporative Stress Index (L4_ESI = actual ET / potential ET = plant water stress), all at ~70 m. ECOSTRESS images the heat the land radiates globally across the ~52°N–52°S band the ISS orbit reaches, reading where plants are thirsty and how much water the land breathes back to the sky — the heat and the thirst of the living land. The mission launches into the story on 29 June 2018 (09:42 UTC) on a SpaceX Falcon 9 on the CRS-15 Dragon cargo flight from Cape Canaveral (SLC-40); the Dragon berths 2 July 2018, and ECOSTRESS is robotically installed on the ISS JEM-EF. The story is the CRS-15 launch from Cape Canaveral, the robotic installation on the JEM-EF, the PHyTIR thermal radiometer scanning five bands in the ~8–12.5 µm window across the ~400 km swath at ~70 m, the land surface temperature inverted to the evapotranspiration and the Evaporative Stress Index, the hotter canopy of the thirsty water-stressed plant set against the cooler canopy of the transpiring one, and the precessing orbit sampling the same land at different times of day — the heat of the living land read from above, scene after scene. The science is central: from the ISS orbit ECOSTRESS reads the thermal radiance the land emits and turns it, by the radiometric calibration and the LST/emissivity and PT-JPL retrievals, into the evapotranspiration and the water stress of the land's plants, repeated across the diurnal cycle. ★★★ ECOSTRESS is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (ECOSTRESS measures the land surface temperature, maps the evapotranspiration, images the thermal field, senses the plant water stress, and reads the heat of the living land); it launched 29 June 2018, was installed in 2018, and operates today — the launch and the installation being the only past events. ECOSTRESS's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first spaceborne mission to measure plant water stress and evapotranspiration at field scale (~70 m) across the diurnal cycle, and it brings a thermal-infrared land-surface-temperature radiometer to the ISS at ~70 m. ★ The scope is load-bearing: thermal-infrared sensors flew in space before ECOSTRESS — AVHRR, MODIS, ASTER, and Landsat TIRS preceded it — so ECOSTRESS is NOT "the first thermal infrared from space," and coarser, fixed-overpass-time evapotranspiration and LST products existed, so ECOSTRESS is NOT "the first evapotranspiration from space"; its distinction is the first field-scale, diurnal mapping of plant water stress and ET, and the first thermal-infrared radiometer hosted on the ISS. ## Surprises

- **The heat the land radiates turned into the thirst of its plants.** ECOSTRESS does not measure water directly — it reads heat. The PHyTIR radiometer measures the thermal radiance the land emits across five bands in the ~8–12.5 µm window, and from that temperature the retrieval reads the evapotranspiration: a transpiring, well-watered canopy runs cooler because evaporation carries heat away, while a thirsty, water-stressed canopy runs hotter. The heat returning from the living land, read from the Station, becomes a map of how much water its plants are using and where they are under stress.
- **An instrument that catches the daily rhythm of plant water use.** Rather than a single fixed-time snapshot, ECOSTRESS rides the precessing ISS orbit and so sees the same land at different times of day across roughly a ~90-day cycle, tracing the daily rise and fall of surface temperature and plant water use — the first spaceborne mapping of plant water stress and evapotranspiration at field scale across the diurnal cycle, the daily rhythm that a fixed sun-synchronous overpass cannot reach.
- **A thermal mapper at the field scale.** Where the earlier thermal sensors read the surface temperature at coarse resolution from a fixed overpass time, ECOSTRESS maps the land surface temperature at ~70 m — fine enough to resolve individual fields and stands — bringing field-scale, diurnal thermal sensing to the spaceborne record and serving agricultural water use, drought, urban heat islands, and wildfire.
- **A thermal radiometer riding the Station, given a global vantage.** ECOSTRESS carries the PHyTIR — built from the prototype HyspIRI thermal-infrared radiometer heritage — to the ISS, where the ~51.6° / ~400 ± 25 km precessing orbit happens to sweep the ~52°N–52°S band again and again at varying local times — a thermal-IR radiometer aboard the Station, given a global, diurnal vantage over the heat of the living land.
- **A semi-aquatic vole of the watered bank.** The American Water Vole (*Microtus richardsoni*), the largest North American vole and a semi-aquatic vole of cold, clear, high-country streams, seeps, and wet meadows of the Pacific Northwest mountains, lives along the watered bank and the runways at the water's edge — a creature of the watered land ECOSTRESS reads from above (behavioral / ecological description only).

## Dedication

v1.307 ECOSTRESS is dedicated to the people at NASA and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and across the thermal-radiometry and evapotranspiration-science teams, who built and operate the radiometer that reads the heat of the living land from the Space Station and turns the temperature the Earth radiates into maps of the water its plants use and the stress they are under. To the engineers who shaped the PHyTIR thermal optics and the cooled detector from the prototype heritage, and tuned the five bands of the thermal window to read the surface temperature true. To the crews who carried it aloft on a Falcon 9 in June 2018 and the robotic arm that set it on the Station's Japanese exposed facility. To the scientists who learned to read the cooler transpiring canopy from the hotter thirsty one, and to follow the daily rhythm of plant water use across the times of day. The heat and the thirst of the living land, and the water it breathes back to the sky, are theirs to read.
