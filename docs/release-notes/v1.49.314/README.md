**The Pigeon Guillemot and the First Alcidae.** The **Pigeon Guillemot** (*Cepphus columba*) enters the engine at degree 174 as the **FIRST ALCIDAE** — the first member of the **auk family** to appear in the SPS axis across the engine's first 174 degrees of rotation. The **Alcidae** family contains approximately 25 species worldwide, distributed across the Northern Hemisphere's cold-to-temperate oceans, and occupies the ecological niche filled in the Southern Hemisphere by penguins: **wing-propelled underwater pursuit** of small fish and invertebrates, **colonial breeding** on rocky coasts and islands, **pelagic non-breeding ranges** in nearshore or offshore marine waters. Auks evolved their wing-propelled underwater flight **independently** of penguins — the two lineages are unrelated, but **convergent evolution** toward the same ecological strategy produced the same mechanical adaptation in both hemispheres. The **Pigeon Guillemot** is one of the most visible Alcidae from Seattle-area waterfront viewpoints: its **year-round Salish Sea** residence, its **nearshore** habitat preferences (in contrast to the pelagic habits of puffins and murres), and its tendency to nest in **pier pilings**, **bluff crevices**, and **natural shoreline cracks** make it reliably observable from ferry decks, piers, and kayak passages throughout Puget Sound. **The Genus Cepphus and the Nearshore Auk Niche.** **Cepphus** is the genus of medium-sized auks distinguished by their **nearshore / rocky-coast** habitat preferences, their **year-round residential status** along temperate coasts (as opposed to the pelagic migratory habits of most other Alcidae genera), and their **breeding-plumage red soft-tissue features** (red feet, red inside the mouth). The genus contains three species globally: the **Pigeon Guillemot** (*C. columba*, North Pacific), the **Black Guillemot** (*C. grylle*, North Atlantic), and the **Spectacled Guillemot** (*C. carbo*, East Asia). The engine's entry at degree 174 opens the genus with the Pigeon Guillemot, and the nearshore-niche framing distinguishes Cepphus from the pelagic-niche framing that will accompany future auk entries (Rhinoceros Auklet at 175, Common Murre, Marbled Murrelet, puffins). The **nearshore Cepphus niche** reads as a first-class ecological category in the engine's vocabulary: species that occupy the zone between true pelagic seabirds and shorebirds, diving beneath the surface in near-shore waters while nesting on the adjacent rocky coast. The **year-round residential status** further distinguishes Cepphus from the migratory Alcidae: while the Rhinoceros Auklet breeds on specific island colonies and disperses to sea in non-breeding months, the Pigeon Guillemot remains in Puget Sound and the Salish Sea throughout the year, making it one of the most reliably observable Alcidae from Seattle waterfronts.

**The Wing-Propelled Underwater Flight.** The **Alcidae** mechanical-ecological adaptation that enters the engine's behavior vocabulary at degree 174 is **wing-propelled underwater flight** — the use of the wings rather than the feet to propel the bird beneath the water's surface in pursuit of prey. The adaptation **evolved independently** from the Southern Hemisphere's penguin wing-propulsion but produces convergent ecology: both lineages transformed their wings from primary aerial-flight organs into subsurface-propulsion organs, and both produced the characteristic stiff, short, paddle-like wing shape optimized for underwater stroke mechanics. Auks retain the ability to fly in the air — penguins lost it entirely — and the compromise between aerial and subsurface flight efficiency defines the shape of the auk wing: shorter and more rigid than typical seabird wings, producing the rapid wingbeat and low-altitude flight profile that distinguishes Alcidae in flight from every other Pacific Northwest seabird. The Pigeon Guillemot, at degree 174, opens the wing-propelled underwater flight vocabulary for the engine, and the adaptation will recur in every subsequent Alcidae entry — a first-class behavioral register that the engine's documentation preserves as the defining auk-family signature.

## Part B: Pigeon Guillemot — The First Alcidae and the Nearshore Auk Debut

- **First Alcidae in the Engine.** The auk family enters the engine at degree 174 with the Pigeon Guillemot (*Cepphus columba*), introducing the Northern Hemisphere's diving-seabird lineage as the ecological parallel to Southern Hemisphere penguins; approximately 25 Alcidae species worldwide occupy the Northern cold-to-temperate ocean niche, and Cepphus opens the engine's auk-family documentation with a nearshore / rocky-coast representative.

- **Genus Cepphus Opens.** The guillemot lineage enters the engine at degree 174 — **Cepphus** contains three species globally (**Pigeon Guillemot** *C. columba* North Pacific, **Black Guillemot** *C. grylle* North Atlantic, **Spectacled Guillemot** *C. carbo* East Asia), and the Pigeon Guillemot anchors the nearshore / rocky-coast / year-round-resident niche distinct from pelagic / migratory Alcidae like puffins, murres, and the Rhinoceros Auklet that will follow at degree 175.
- **Wing-Propelled Underwater Flight.** The Alcidae mechanical-ecological adaptation — the use of wings rather than feet for subsurface propulsion in pursuit of small fish and invertebrates — enters the engine's behavior vocabulary at degree 174 as a first-class register; the adaptation evolved convergently with Southern Hemisphere penguin wing-propulsion, producing the characteristic short-stiff-paddle wing shape and the rapid-wingbeat low-altitude aerial flight profile that distinguishes auks from all other Pacific Northwest seabirds.
- **Thin High-Pitched *seee* Whistle (E=3).** The characteristic vocalization is a single sustained narrow-frequency-band whistled note, often delivered near the water surface as a contact call between nearby birds; E=3 reflects the call's narrowness and near-water delivery — audible and distinctive but not resonant at the gull-cry or crane-bugle scale; the call enters the Alcidae acoustic register as the family's first engine representation.
- **Rocky-Crevice Colonial Nesting.** The Pigeon Guillemot breeds in colonies placed in **natural cliff cracks**, **bluff crevices**, and **human-built pier pilings** along the shoreline — a nesting adaptation that distinguishes Cepphus from pelagic-island-colony Alcidae like puffins and murres; the behavior enters the engine's nesting-habitat vocabulary at degree 174 as a first-class shoreline-adjacent colonial register.
- **Year-Round Salish Sea Resident.** The species is **non-migratory** along the Salish Sea — breeding birds remain in the same shoreline habitats throughout the year, making the Pigeon Guillemot one of the most reliably observable Alcidae from Seattle-area ferries, piers, and waterfront viewpoints across all seasons; the residency status enters the engine's vocabulary as a first-class non-migratory register, distinct from the trans-hemispheric migration opened at degree 170 with the Whimbrel.
- **Black Plumage, Red Feet, Red Mouth.** The breeding-plumage *Cepphus columba* carries **solid black body plumage with bold white wing patches**, **bright red feet**, and **red inside the mouth** (visible when the mouth opens during territorial display or calling); the morphological signature enters the engine's breeding-plumage-signature vocabulary at degree 174 and distinguishes the Pigeon Guillemot from every previous Puget Sound seabird in the engine.
