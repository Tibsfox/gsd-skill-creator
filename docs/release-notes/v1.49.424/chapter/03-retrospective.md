## Lessons Learned

1. **Supergroup architecture warrants first-class engine documentation distinct from primary-band architecture.** The degree 283 documentation distinguishes the **Childbirth** three-musicians-two-bands-one-label supergroup architecture from the **Tacocat** four-musicians-one-band primary-band architecture at degree 282 rather than aggregating both into a single Hardly Art category. The lesson is that band-architecture distinctions are structurally load-bearing when they shape the music's production, institutional context, and roster relationships, and the engine should preserve these distinctions at first-class resolution rather than collapsing architecture variants into uniform band-category documentation. The resulting engine record is richer for maintaining the architecture-level differentiation.
2. **Consecutive-entry label sequences document roster depth at first-class structural resolution.** The Hardly Art sequence across degrees 282-283 (Tacocat primary band, Childbirth supergroup) documents the label's roster depth — one label producing two related-but-distinct acts from the same musician pool at consecutive degrees — at first-class engine resolution rather than treating the sequence as coincidental adjacency. The lesson is that consecutive-entry label sequences warrant first-class structural recognition when the entries share roster or institutional context, because the sequence's documentation is what makes the label's institutional role legible at the engine-structural level rather than at the release-specific level alone.
3. **First-in-category entries open a new register that subsequent category entries can reference.** The degree 283 **Steller Sea Lion Bull Roar** as the first **Otariidae** entry opens the pinniped-family register as distinct from the four prior cetacean-family registers (Delphinidae, Balaenopteridae, Physeteridae, Phocoenidae) across the prior fourteen SPS degrees. The lesson is that first-in-category entries warrant the specific register-opening vocabulary treatment so that subsequent category entries can build on the precedent — the California Sea Lion Dock Chorus at degree 284 as the second Otariidae entry references the first Otariidae entry at degree 283 as the family register's opening. Leaving the first-entry register-opening implicit would allow future readers to miss the category's structural significance.
4. **Proportionate-to-mass E-value assignment maintains source-grounded scoring rather than abstract rubric output.** The Steller sea lion bull roar at E=8 is proportionate to the 1,000 kg adult male mass: a larger chest cavity produces a lower-frequency more-carrying roar, and the E=8 assignment encodes exactly that proportionality. Smaller pinnipeds (California sea lion at ~300 kg) would produce E=7; larger pinnipeds would potentially produce E=9. The lesson is that E-value assignment should track source characteristics — mass, intensity, acoustic carrying distance — rather than being assigned by abstract rubric detached from source grounding. Source-grounded E-values are interpretively productive because they allow readers to recover the source-characteristic reasoning from the E-value itself.
5. **Collective acoustic events can exceed the sum of their individual components and warrant first-class structural documentation.** The haul-out collective acoustic register at degree 283 — the multi-bull overlapping-roar field at Protection Island, Destruction Island, and outer-coast rocks — exceeds the sum of individual Steller sea lion bull roars and is the primary SPS structural observation rather than a secondary characterization. The lesson is that collective-above-the-sum acoustic events warrant explicit first-class documentation when they apply, because the individual-source characterization alone would not capture the collective dimension. The collective acoustic documentation mode applies broadly across SPS entries that involve multi-individual events (bird flocks, seal haul-outs, orca pod vocalizations, amphibian choruses, insect chorus) and the precedent established at degree 283 can be referenced by subsequent collective-acoustic entries.
6. **Cross-axis structural parallels between cultural and biological registers warrant explicit cross-sync documentation when they genuinely apply.** The degree 283 cross-sync axis — **collective presence from compatible individuals** — names a structural principle that applies across both the Childbirth supergroup-above-the-sum register and the Steller sea lion bull haul-out collective-roar-above-the-individual-roar register. The lesson is that cross-sync designations should be made explicit when the structural parallel genuinely applies across cultural and biological registers, because the naming-of-principle is what makes the cross-axis register legible as an engine-level structural documentation rather than as a release-level coincidence of pairing. The cross-sync principle is interpretively productive — it unifies the two halves of the release without collapsing their distinct register characters.
7. **Anatomical and behavioral distinctions between closely-related taxonomic families warrant explicit documentation at first-class resolution.** The degree 283 documentation names the Otariidae-vs-Phocidae distinction (external ear flaps, rotatable hind flippers, terrestrial walking vs. undulating locomotion) at first-class structural resolution rather than aggregating all pinnipeds into a uniform "seal" category. The lesson is that closely-related taxonomic families with visible and behavioral distinctions warrant explicit documentation of the distinctions when the engine's documentation is the primary site where the taxonomic legibility is preserved for general readers. Future pinniped entries (California sea lion, harbor seal, northern elephant seal, northern fur seal) can reference the degree 283 eared-seal-vs-true-seal precedent rather than re-establishing the distinction from scratch at each entry.
8. **Two-degree within-category arcs can be sufficient when the two entries are structurally adjacent at the primary-artifact level.** The Hardly Art sequence (Tacocat 282, Childbirth 283) and the Otariidae sequence (Steller sea lion 283, California sea lion 284) are both two-degree arcs that the engine documents as first-class structural sequences despite their short length. The lesson is that arc length is not a uniform standard across labels, families, or clusters: a sequence characterized by consecutive-entry structural adjacency can be legible at two entries, while a cluster or family characterized by internal variation or long roster depth may require more extensive coverage (as in the Orca Arc at seven consecutive degrees, or the Seattle HC arc at five consecutive degrees). Arc planning should match the arc's length to the documentation purpose and to the source-material adjacency rather than to a uniform length standard.
