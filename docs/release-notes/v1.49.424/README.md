## Part B: Steller Sea Lion Bull Roar — First Otariidae Entry, Largest Pinniped in Pacific Northwest, Haul-Out Collective Acoustic Register, and the Eared-Seal-vs-True-Seal Anatomical Distinction

- **First Otariidae Entry in the Engine:** The **Steller Sea Lion Bull Roar** at degree 283 is the engine's first **Otariidae** (eared seal) entry — the first case where a non-cetacean marine mammal family enters the SPS record. - **Eumetopias jubatus and Georg Wilhelm Steller's 1741 Description:** The Steller sea lion is *Eumetopias jubatus* — the sole species in the genus Eumetopias — and is named after **Georg Wilhelm Steller**, the German naturalist whose 1741 observations during the Great Northern Expedition led to the species' scientific description. Steller's observations of the species during Vitus Bering's second Kamchatka expedition provide the primary historical anchor for the species' scientific name, and the engine preserves the Steller-expedition-1741 context as a first-class element of the degree 283 documentation. The species is distributed across the North Pacific from the Kuril Islands and Sea of Okhotsk through the Aleutians to southeast Alaska, British Columbia, Washington, Oregon, and northern California.
- **1,000 kg Adult Male Mass and 3-Meter Length:** Adult male Steller sea lions reach 1,000 kg in mass and 3 meters in length. This makes the Steller sea lion the largest member of Family Otariidae in the North Pacific and the largest pinniped in the Pacific Northwest — ten times heavier than a large harbor seal (*Phoca vitulina*, ~100 kg), several times heavier than the California sea lion (*Zalophus californianus*, adult male ~300 kg), and five times heavier than the Dall's porpoise (*Phocoenoides dalli*, ~200 kg). In the wider pinniped record, only the northern elephant seal (*Mirounga angustirostris*, a Phocid rather than an Otariid, adult male ~2,500 kg) and the southern elephant seal (*Mirounga leonina*, adult male up to 4,000 kg) exceed the Steller sea lion in adult male mass.
- **Guttural Low-Frequency Territorial Roar from Massive Chest Cavity:** The territorial roar the Steller sea lion bull produces is proportionate to its mass. A massive 1,000 kg chest cavity produces a guttural low-frequency roar that carries over considerable distance — the larger the chest cavity, the lower and more carrying the vocalization. At active haul-out sites the Steller roar is heard from kilometers away. Individual roars are the loudest pinniped vocalizations in the Pacific Northwest nearshore marine record. The roar functions as both a territorial-defense vocalization (adult males defending haul-out positions and breeding territories against rival males) and a social communication signal within the haul-out community.
- **Haul-Out Sites — Protection Island, Destruction Island, Outer Coast:** Active Steller sea lion haul-out sites in the Pacific Northwest include **Protection Island** (the protected wildlife refuge in the Strait of Juan de Fuca whose sheltered waters and offshore rocks provide one of the most significant pinniped haul-out sites in the inner Salish Sea), **Destruction Island** (the offshore Washington coast island whose rocky shore provides an active haul-out on the outer Pacific coast), and the scattered rocky marine headlands and offshore rocks of the outer coast along the Washington and Oregon coastline. These sites are protected by the **Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA)** of 1972 and are monitored by **NOAA Fisheries** through the agency's pinniped research and population programs. The **Olympic Coast National Marine Sanctuary** encompasses much of the outer Washington coast including Destruction Island.

- **Eared-Seal-vs-True-Seal Anatomical Distinction and the Terrestrial Walking Locomotion:** The Otariidae (eared seals, including sea lions and fur seals) are distinguished from the Phocidae (the true seals, including harbor seals, elephant seals, grey seals, and ringed seals) by two architectural features: external ear flaps (visible from the outside; Phocidae have only ear openings without external flaps) and the ability to rotate the hind flippers forward for terrestrial locomotion (sea lions and fur seals walk on their flippers with the body elevated above the substrate; harbor seals and elephant seals undulate their bodies like caterpillars on land with the body flat against the substrate). This distinction is behavioral as much as anatomical. A sea lion on a rock is a different kind of marine mammal presence than a harbor seal on a rock: the sea lion walks upright or on all four flippers with its body elevated above the rock surface, while the harbor seal lies flat and moves by undulating its body. The Steller sea lion's terrestrial mobility is part of what makes it a different kind of marine mammal presence on the rocks and beaches of the Pacific Northwest than the harbor seal. The engine's degree 283 documentation names this distinction explicitly as the **eared-seal-vs-true-seal anatomical register** — the first engine entry where the Otariidae-vs-Phocidae distinction is first-class structural context.
