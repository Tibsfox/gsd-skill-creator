## DJ Riz -- The Central District

DJ Riz is the figure who moves the engine out of Capitol Hill and into the **Central District** -- Seattle's historic Black neighborhood and the geographic heart of the city's hip-hop culture. After ten consecutive radio figures based at Capitol Hill or in Capitol Hill-adjacent contexts (126-135), DJ Riz shifts the map.

**206 Zulu** -- DJ Riz founded the Pacific Northwest chapter of the **Universal Zulu Nation**, the hip-hop cultural organization that traces its lineage to Afrika Bambaataa and the Bronx. 206 Zulu (named for Seattle's area code) was infrastructure, not entertainment. It connected Seattle's hip-hop community to the national movement, provided a framework for local artists, and anchored hip-hop culture in the Central District. DJ Riz didn't just play hip-hop; he *organized* it.

E=5. Same energy as B-Mello (134) and Indica Jones (135). Three consecutive E=5 DJs closing the radio era. The energy escalation is deliberate: the radio sequence began with E=3-4 hosts and executives, then surged to E=5 DJs. The arc climbs.

## Great Horned Owl Pair Duet -- The Conversation

The Great Horned Owl appears for the second consecutive degree -- making it the engine's **third species double-entry** (after previous species that appeared in back-to-back degrees). Degree 135 introduced the solo call. Degree 136 introduces the **pair duet** -- male and female calling together.

The duet is one of the most remarkable sounds in the Pacific Northwest forest. The male calls his deep *hoo-h'HOO-hoo-hoo*. The female answers with a higher-pitched version -- same rhythm, same pattern, shifted up. The two calls interlock, creating a pitch-shifted conversation that carries through the forest canopy. The duet functions as territory declaration and pair bonding: *this is our territory, we are here, together*.

E=5. Higher energy than the solo call (E=4). The duet is louder, more complex, and more acoustically dominant than a single bird calling. Two Great Horned Owls duetting at midnight in old-growth forest is one of the most powerful natural sounds in the Pacific Northwest.

**Size-pitch paradox.** The female Great Horned Owl is **10-25% larger** than the male -- a pattern called reverse sexual dimorphism common in raptors. But the female calls at a *higher* pitch. Larger body, higher voice. In most animals, larger bodies produce lower-pitched sounds (the Trumpeter Swan's deep bugle, the Canada Goose's baritone honk). The Great Horned Owl reverses this. The paradox is consistent across the species but not fully explained. The syrinx (voice organ) structure may differ between sexes independently of body size.

**Earliest nester in North America.** Great Horned Owls begin nesting in **January and February** -- deep winter. They incubate eggs while snow covers the ground, while temperatures drop below freezing, while most birds are still weeks or months from breeding season. By the time spring arrives and other raptors begin courtship, Great Horned Owl chicks are already hatching. The early nesting strategy gives the young the longest possible development period before their first winter -- but it demands that the adults endure the harshest conditions while sitting on eggs.

The species uses **old nests** built by other large birds -- Red-tailed Hawks, Bald Eagles, crows -- because it nests so early that there's no time to build. The Great Horned Owl doesn't construct; it appropriates. Another expression of its apex-predator status: even the nest belongs to someone else.
