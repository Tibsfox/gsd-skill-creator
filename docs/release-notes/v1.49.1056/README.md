## Mission Overview

Mariner 9 is the obs#2 sustain of the MARS-EXPLORATION axis — the surviving spacecraft of NASA's Mariner Mars '71 program, whose defining achievement is becoming the first spacecraft to orbit another planet and, from that sustained orbital station, producing the first global map of Mars. It was a wholly NASA / United States mission, managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory; its identical twin, Mariner 8, was lost in an Atlas-Centaur launch failure on 8 May 1971 (local) when the Centaur upper stage malfunctioned and the payload fell into the Atlantic, so Mariner 9 carried out the program alone. It launched on an Atlas SLV-3C/Centaur from Cape Canaveral Launch Complex 36B on 30 May 1971 (22:23 UTC; NSSDCA/COSPAR 1971-051A), with a dry mass of 558.8 kg (1,232 lb) and a launch mass of 997.9 kg (2,200 lb). After a five-and-a-half-month cruise, it entered Mars orbit on 14 November 1971 (UTC) — the first artificial satellite of another world — settling into a mapping orbit ~1,650 × 16,860 km with a period of ~12 hours (11.99 h), an inclination of 64.4°, and an eccentricity of ~0.60. The qualifier is load-bearing: the Soviet Mars 2 reached Mars orbit on 27 November 1971 (~13 days later) and Mars 3 on ~2 December 1971 (~18 days later) — both AFTER Mariner 9, despite Mars 2 having launched earlier — so Mariner 9 was first to arrive and orbit. The craft carried a wide-angle and a narrow-angle TV camera (the imaging experiment), an ultraviolet spectrometer (UVS), an infrared interferometer spectrometer (IRIS), and an infrared radiometer. It arrived into a planet-encircling dust storm that hid the surface; only the four dark Tharsis spots and the south polar cap showed above the dust — the first clue to the giant volcanoes — and the spacecraft held station and waited about two months for the dust to settle. It then systematically mapped the surface over hundreds of orbits, returning 7,329 images (~54 billion bits) covering about 85% of Mars (1–2 km/pixel, a small fraction at ~100 m, exceeding the ~70% objective) — the basis for the first global map (and the first globe) of another planet. Mariner 9 resolved the classical albedo feature Nix Olympica as a giant shield volcano (renamed Olympus Mons, the largest known volcano in the solar system) and imaged the Tharsis shield volcanoes; it imaged the vast canyon system named Valles Marineris ("Valleys of the Mariner") in its honor; it revealed polar layered deposits, dune fields, and sinuous channels and valley networks implying past liquid water; and it returned the first detailed close-up images of Phobos and Deimos (December 1971 – February 1972). The mission ended on 27 October 1972, after ~349 days in orbit, when the attitude-control nitrogen gas was depleted and the transmitter was commanded off — a long, hugely successful mission far beyond its 90-day primary objective. Mariner 9 SUSTAINS the MARS-EXPLORATION substrate-axis at obs#2 (substrate-axis rotation #36 sustains, 1st consecutive).

## Surprises

- **A 1971 spacecraft became the first artificial satellite of another planet — and beat the Soviet orbiters that had launched earlier.** Mariner 9 entered Mars orbit on 14 November 1971, ~13 days before the Soviet Mars 2 and ~18 days before Mars 3, despite Mars 2 having launched earlier on 19 May 1971 — first to arrive and orbit.
- **Mariner 9 arrived to find the entire surface hidden by a planet-encircling dust storm — and the wait became part of the achievement.** Only the four dark Tharsis spots and the south polar cap showed above the dust; the spacecraft held station and waited about two months for the dust to settle, then systematically mapped the surface. The patience of the orbital platform is why the first global map was possible.
- **The first global map of Mars came from a single surviving spacecraft flying alone.** With its identical twin Mariner 8 lost at launch, Mariner 9 carried out the whole Mariner Mars '71 program alone, returning 7,329 images that mapped about 85% of Mars — the basis for the first global map (and the first globe) of another planet.
- **Mariner 9 revealed Mars as a geologically dynamic world, not the dead, cratered planet the flybys had suggested.** It resolved the classical albedo feature Nix Olympica as a giant shield volcano (Olympus Mons, the largest known volcano in the solar system), imaged the Tharsis shield volcanoes and the vast canyon named Valles Marineris in its honor, and revealed polar layered deposits and channels implying past liquid water — a transformation from the Mariner 4/6/7 flyby view.
- **A Pacific Northwest raptor holds a sustained overhead survey before it acts.** The White-tailed Kite (*Elanus leucurus*), resident in western Oregon and expanding into southwest Washington, is famous for "kiting" — hovering in sustained, near-stationary flight over open grassland and marsh, methodically scanning the ground below before dropping onto prey — a behavioral analogue of Mariner 9 holding orbital station and methodically surveying the terrain below (the kite is never said to "see" or "map" Mars).
- **A low cushion plant comprehensively tiles bare rocky ground into a single continuous carpet.** Spreading Phlox (*Phlox diffusa*), a low cushion- or mat-forming Pacific Northwest perennial of open rocky subalpine and alpine ground, tiles and comprehensively covers bare stone with a dense carpet of foliage studded with flowers — a single continuous cover built to span the whole, mirroring Mariner 9's first complete imaging coverage / first global map of the Martian surface.

## Dedication

v1.239 Mariner 9 is dedicated to the people who built and flew NASA's Mariner Mars '71 program — the surviving spacecraft that became the first to orbit another planet and mapped the whole of Mars from above. William H. Pickering and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory who managed and operated it; the navigation and engineering teams who carried it through orbit insertion on 14 November 1971 and held its station through a planet-encircling dust storm; Harold Masursky and the television experiment team who built the first global map of Mars from 7,329 images; the imaging scientists who resolved Nix Olympica as Olympus Mons and traced the vast canyon later named Valles Marineris in the spacecraft's honor; Rudolf Hanel and the infrared interferometer spectrometer team, and the ultraviolet-spectrometer and infrared-radiometer investigators who read the Martian atmosphere; and the teams who returned the first detailed pictures of Phobos and Deimos. They are remembered alongside those who flew the twin Mariner 8, lost at launch, so that Mariner 9 could carry the program forward alone. For about a year a single craft held its orbit and surveyed the surface below, and the red planet first appeared to us as a living, geologically dynamic world.
