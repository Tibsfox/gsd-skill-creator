- **Loud Scolding Chatter Rattle (81st Species-Side Acoustic Element).** Aggressive sustained machine-gun rattle at 3,000-10,000 Hz. 2-10+ seconds sustained. E=5. Audible 200+ meters through dense conifer forest. The most vocal mammal in PNW forests. The LONGEST sustained vocalization in the Sciuridae run. Distinct from the sharp chip (79, single percussive note), chirping trill (78, rapid short bursts), multi-note whistle (77, musical melodic), and penetrating whistle (76, single tonal).
- **John Muir's Favorite Animal.** **John Muir** wrote extensively about the Douglas Squirrel in *The Mountains of California* (1894), devoting an entire chapter (**Chapter XIV**) -- the only mammal to receive a full chapter. Muir called it **"by far the most influential of all the Sierra animals."** Extraordinary literary-historical anchoring; uniquely powerful in the engine's species library.
- **Named for David Douglas.** **David Douglas** (1799-1834) -- Scottish botanist who explored the Pacific Northwest in the 1820s-1830s. The squirrel that lives in the tree named for the same person. **Douglas Squirrel in the Douglas-fir.** Cross-naming elegance: a single 19th-century botanist names both the predominant PNW conifer and its predominant arboreal mammal.
- **Cross-Sextet Predation: Mustelidae Hunts Sciuridae.** **American Marten** (Mustelidae sextet, degree 65) is the Douglas Squirrel's PRIMARY PREDATOR. Both arboreal. Both work the conifer canopy. The canopy arms race. A Mustelidae sextet member hunting a Sciuridae sextet member -- the engine's two longest family runs locked in direct predator-prey relationship.
- **Twentieth Consecutive Original CSV Species (61-80).** TWO DECADES of original CSV without substitution. CSV reliability proven at scale. The engine's species library has now run twenty consecutive degrees on the original mapping.
- **Cone-Scale Middens and Truffle Dispersal.** Strips conifer cone scales at favored feeding perches; the discarded scales accumulate in massive piles ("middens") meters wide and decades old. Caches green cones in cool, moist locations. Truffle disperser -- key mycorrhizal fungi inoculator. Does NOT hibernate (the only sextet member without true hibernation); active year-round in the canopy.
- **The Chickaree.** Common name in Sierra Nevada and parts of PNW. Small (150-300 g, 5-10 oz). Dark olive-brown above, orange-russet belly, prominent white eye ring. Aggressively territorial. The scolding chatter IS the territory boundary.
