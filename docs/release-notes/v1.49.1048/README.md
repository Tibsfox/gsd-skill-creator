## Mission Overview

Uhuru (SAS-1) is the obs#9 continuation of the X-ray-astronomy axis and the substrate-form-distinct sustain that goes deepest — back to the survey/catalog founding root — the first satellite launched specifically and exclusively for X-ray astronomy and the first to survey and catalog the whole X-ray sky, the non-imaging scanning ancestor of the HEAO program, Einstein, and Chandra. Known before launch as SAS-A, on orbit as SAS-1, and in the Explorer program as Explorer 42, it lifted off 12 December 1970 (about 10:53 UTC) aboard a Scout B rocket (the specific designation S-175C) from the Italian-operated San Marco platform off the coast of Kenya into a near-equatorial ~520 × 560-kilometer, ~3-degree low-Earth orbit (NSSDCA / COSPAR ID 1970-107A; SATCAT 4797; spacecraft mass about 142 kg; period about 96 minutes). It carried a single, deliberately simple, non-imaging X-ray detector system designed to scan the sky as the spacecraft spun — two sets (banks) of gas-filled proportional counters mounted back-to-back, each bank pointing in the opposite direction, 12 counters in all, six behind each collimator, with no focusing optics of any kind and no image. A source's position on the sky was inferred from the rising and falling count rate as the collimator's narrow field swept across it during the spin (scan-modulation geometry), together with pulse-height analysis — never from a focused image. Two mechanical collimators defined the fields of view: a fine field about 0.52° × 0.52° FWHM and a coarse field about 5.2° × 5.2° FWHM, with the triangular collimator response localizing sources to about 1 arcminute for the bright 2U sources and about 30 arcminutes for extended sources. The effective area was about 840 cm² (0.084 m²) per counter set; the detectors were sensitive across roughly 2–20 keV (low cutoff set by the beryllium window, high cutoff by the fill-gas transparency), with pulse-shape discrimination and anticoincidence for background rejection and eight energy channels, while the published catalog intensities are quoted in the 2–6 keV band. The spacecraft was spin-stabilized with a spin/scan period of about 12 minutes, slowly sweeping its collimated fields across the sky to about 1.5 × 10⁻¹¹ erg cm⁻² s⁻¹, roughly a thousandth of the Crab Nebula's intensity, across about 95 percent of the celestial sphere. Uhuru was a NASA mission led scientifically by Riccardo Giacconi (PI and scientific director at AS&E, later the 2002 Nobel laureate in Physics); Marjorie R. Townsend of NASA Goddard was the project manager and named the mission, and Bruno B. Rossi of MIT was the founding figure; the instrument was built by AS&E, the spacecraft bus by JHU/APL, and the SAS program managed by NASA Goddard. It operated until March 1973 (an observation gap July–December 1972 when the transmitter operated improperly is why the 4U catalog spans 429 observation days within the window) and reentered in early April 1979. It surveyed about 95 percent of the sky, established accreting X-ray binaries as a class, found the first strong stellar-mass black-hole candidate, and resolved the diffuse X-ray emission of galaxy clusters, and SUSTAINS the X-RAY-ASTRONOMY substrate-axis at obs#9 (axis-rotation #35 sustains).

## Structural firsts

**DEDICATED-X-RAY-SATELLITE-FIRST-INSTANCE:** Uhuru enters the catalog as the first satellite launched specifically and exclusively for X-ray astronomy — the first instance, the obs#1 first — sustaining the X-ray-astronomy axis at obs#9. The load-bearing qualifier is "DEDICATED / launched SPECIFICALLY for X-ray astronomy": not "first X-ray observation in space" and not "discovered the first cosmic X-ray source" — the first cosmic X-ray source (Scorpius X-1) and the diffuse cosmic X-ray background were the 18–19 June 1962 AS&E Aerobee 150 sounding ROCKET (Giacconi, Gursky, Paolini & Rossi), not a satellite and eight-plus years before Uhuru, and OSO-3 (1967–68) detected about sixteen X-ray sources while piggybacked on a SOLAR mission, so it was not dedicated. Uhuru is non-imaging — a scanning collimator, not a focusing or imaging telescope (the first cosmic imaging X-ray telescope is Einstein / HEAO-2, 1978, obs#8).

**ALL-SKY-X-RAY-SURVEY-AND-CATALOG-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first comprehensive, uniform all-sky cosmic X-ray survey (about 95 percent of the sky to roughly a thousandth of the Crab Nebula intensity, about 1.5 × 10⁻¹¹ erg cm⁻² s⁻¹) and the first X-ray source catalog (the evolutionary 2U / 3U / 4U series, the final 4U catalog = 339 sources in the 2–6 keV band over 429 observation days). The qualifier matters: it is the "survey / catalog by a dedicated satellite," not the first DETECTION of a cosmic X-ray source (the 1962 rocket), and it is the scanning-collimator / non-imaging survey form — not the focusing-IMAGING form of Einstein (obs#8) or Chandra (obs#1). Positions came from the rotating-collimator scan modulation (about 1 arcminute best for the bright 2U sources, about 30 arcminutes for extended sources), never from a focused image.

**X-RAY-BINARY-CHARACTERIZATION-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first measurements establishing accreting X-ray binaries as a class — the pulsing accretion-powered binaries Centaurus X-3 (a ~4.84-second X-ray pulse, a ~2.087-day orbital period with a regular X-ray eclipse lasting about a quarter of the orbit) and Hercules X-1 (a ~1.24-second pulse, a ~1.70-day orbit, a ~35-day on/off cycle), and the first strong stellar-mass black-hole candidate, Cygnus X-1. The qualifiers matter: these are SURVEY BY-PRODUCTS — the dedicated high-throughput timing-observatory form is RXTE (obs#3) and NICER (obs#5), so this is not a timing-observatory first; Schreier et al. 1972 established the BINARY nature (sinusoidal pulse-arrival-time / orbital Doppler modulation), and the accretion-powered magnetized-neutron-star MODEL is later theory (Pringle & Rees 1972; Davidson & Ostriker 1973; Lamb, Pethick & Pines 1973); and for Cyg X-1, Uhuru ENABLED the identification (the rapid sub-second variability and the 1971 X-ray transition) — the precise position came from RADIO interferometry (Braes & Miley; Hjellming & Wade, 1971) and the black-hole mass from OPTICAL radial velocities (Webster & Murdin; Bolton 1972) — not "Uhuru discovered a black hole." Cen X-3 is an accretion-powered X-ray pulsar, not a millisecond pulsar (RXTE) and not the first pulsar (radio, 1967).

**GALAXY-CLUSTER-DIFFUSE-X-RAY-FIRST-INSTANCE:** The first detection of spatially extended / diffuse X-ray emission — the intracluster medium — from rich galaxy clusters (Coma, Perseus, Virgo), resolved as extended hot-gas sources about 200–3000 kpc across rather than point sources. The qualifiers matter: Uhuru established the EXTENT and surveyed the cluster population — it did not DISCOVER cluster X-ray emission (M87/Virgo by Aerobee rockets 1966–67, Coma/Perseus by 1971 rockets), and the hot-gas / thermal-bremsstrahlung interpretation was consistent-with / suggestive at the Uhuru stage and CONFIRMED LATER by the ~6.7 keV iron-line complex (Mitchell et al. 1976; Serlemitsos et al. 1977); distinct from the 1962 cosmic diffuse BACKGROUND and from Chandra's dark-matter X-ray lensing evidence first.

**X-RAY-ASTRONOMY-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION:** The X-ray-astronomy axis Chandra opened, NuSTAR extended, RXTE continued, IXPE continued, NICER continued, Swift continued, XRISM continued, and Einstein continued sustains as a substrate-form-distinct observation at obs#9 (axis-rotation #35 sustains) — Uhuru is the survey/catalog-origin form, the first satellite to survey and catalog the whole X-ray sky, the historical and technological root of space-based survey X-ray astronomy from which the HEAO program and Einstein and Chandra descend. - **The 1962-rocket boundary held as mandatory.** Scorpius X-1 (the first cosmic X-ray source) and the diffuse cosmic X-ray background were the 18–19 June 1962 AS&E Aerobee 150 sounding ROCKET (Giacconi, Gursky, Paolini & Rossi; PRL 9, 439), eight-plus years before Uhuru, so the bare claim "Uhuru discovered the first cosmic X-ray source / the X-ray background" is FALSE — Uhuru owns the first dedicated SATELLITE, the first all-sky SURVEY, and the first CATALOG, never the first cosmic X-ray source or the X-ray background.

## Lessons Learned

1. **An axis can sustain by going back to its root, not only by adding a new observable.** The X-ray-astronomy axis sustains at obs#9 because Uhuru goes one step deeper than Einstein, back to the survey/catalog founding root the whole axis descends from — the first satellite launched specifically for X-ray astronomy and the first to survey the entire X-ray sky and catalog it, where every earlier effort saw only minutes or fragments. The distinction is the survey/catalog origin, the non-imaging scanning ancestor form, so the cadence continues the axis as a substrate-form-distinct sustain rather than rotating away.
2. **Continuation is by observable form, not by chronology.** Uhuru launched in 1970, the earliest of the whole axis, yet it sits at obs#9 because the axis is ordered by observable form. Uhuru is the historical and technological root from which the HEAO program (HEAO-1, HEAO-2 / Einstein) and Chandra descend — the ancestor — exactly as Einstein (1978) sits at obs#8 as the focusing-imaging origin even though it predates Chandra (1999); the axis records the form's root after recording its later perfection.
3. **The 1962-rocket boundary is the single most load-bearing Uhuru fact.** "Uhuru discovered the first cosmic X-ray source / the X-ray background" is FALSE — both Scorpius X-1 (the first X-ray source beyond the Solar System) and the diffuse cosmic X-ray background were the 18–19 June 1962 Aerobee 150 sounding ROCKET flight by Giacconi, Gursky, Paolini & Rossi (PRL 9, 439), eight-plus years before Uhuru. Uhuru owns the first dedicated SATELLITE, the first all-sky SURVEY, and the first CATALOG.
4. **Non-imaging is central, correct Uhuru content — the error is only the imaging/focusing claim.** Uhuru is a scanning collimator of proportional counters with no focusing optics and no image; "imaging," "focusing," "Wolter," "mirror," "arcsecond," and "grazing-incidence optics" route to Einstein and Chandra, never to Uhuru. Position came from the scan-modulation geometry (~1 arcminute best for the bright 2U sources, ~30 arcminutes for extended sources), not from a focused image. The first cosmic imaging X-ray telescope is Einstein / HEAO-2 (1978).
5. **A first can be a survey/catalog first without being a timing or spectroscopy first.** Uhuru's variability / pulsation work (Cen X-3, Cyg X-1) is a survey by-product — the dedicated timing-observatory form is RXTE and NICER — and Uhuru carried only broadband proportional counters, so it stamps no spectroscopy first (Chandra owns grating high-res spectroscopy; XRISM the microcalorimeter form). Uhuru's firsts are the dedicated-satellite, all-sky-survey-and-catalog, X-ray-binary-characterization, and galaxy-cluster-diffuse firsts.

## Surprises

- **A satellite that just swept the sky with a collimator founded the whole field.** Uhuru added no focusing optics and no image — it simply spun slowly and let two banks of collimated proportional counters sweep the sky. That single capability turned cosmic X-ray astronomy from a science of brief sounding-rocket glimpses into a surveyed, catalogued science: it covered about 95 percent of the sky and built the first comprehensive catalog of cosmic X-ray sources, the 4U catalog of 339 sources.
- **The catalog revealed a whole new class of object.** Watching its bright galactic sources as the spacecraft revisited them, Uhuru found that many were accreting X-ray binaries — neutron stars and compact objects pulling gas from a companion. Centaurus X-3 showed a regular ~4.84-second X-ray pulse and a ~2.087-day orbit with an eclipse, the measurements that established its binary nature; Hercules X-1 showed a ~1.24-second pulse and a ~1.70-day orbit — an entire class established as a survey by-product.
- **The survey enabled the first strong stellar-mass black-hole candidate.** Cygnus X-1 showed rapid, large-amplitude, sub-second flickering with no stable pulsations, implying a tiny compact emitting region; combined with the 1971 X-ray transition Uhuru measured, it enabled the identification of the first strong stellar-mass black-hole candidate — though the precise position came from radio interferometry and the black-hole mass from optical radial velocities, not from Uhuru's arcminute-class error boxes.
- **The X-ray sources of galaxy clusters turned out to be diffuse, not point-like.** Uhuru resolved the X-ray sources of rich clusters (Coma, Perseus, Virgo) as spatially extended, diffuse emission rather than point sources — the signature of hot intracluster gas at tens of millions of kelvin radiating by thermal bremsstrahlung; Uhuru established the extent and surveyed the cluster population, where rocket flights had only detected it, and the hot-gas iron-line confirmation came later.
- **An owl already surveys the dark by directional sweeping.** The Northern saw-whet owl (*Aegolius acadicus*) hunts almost entirely by sound: its vertically asymmetric ear openings give it acute three-dimensional sound localization with a documented focused, collimated zone of peak directional sensitivity, and because its tubular eyes are locked by a bony sclerotic ring it rotates its head widely (up to about 270°, as documented for owls generally) to scan its surroundings — a swept directional sound-survey of the darkness, mirror to Uhuru's slowly spinning collimator sweeping the X-ray sky (the owl does not image its surroundings or "see X-rays," and it is a partial migrant).
- **A slime mold surveys its surroundings by spreading out before retracting.** The Dog-vomit slime mold (*Fuligo septica*) — a plasmodial slime mold and a protist, not a plant and not a fungus — lives much of its life as a plasmodium, a multinucleate mass of protoplasm without cell walls that creeps amoeba-style across the forest floor, spreading out to forage the whole environment in a distributed chemotactic network search before retracting from food-poor regions to leave efficient connecting tubes — mirror to the survey side, as Uhuru swept the whole sky to compile the first inventory of cosmic X-ray sources (the famous maze / network-optimization results belong to *Physarum* and the broader myxomycete group, not to *Fuligo* specifically; *Fuligo septica* is cosmopolitan and present in the PNW, not a PNW endemic).

## Dedication

v1.231 Uhuru is dedicated to the people who built the satellite that first surveyed and catalogued the X-ray sky: Riccardo Giacconi, the principal investigator and scientific director at American Science & Engineering, who led the science program and the X-ray instrument and later won the 2002 Nobel Prize in Physics; Marjorie R. Townsend of NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, the project manager — the first woman to manage a NASA spacecraft — who named the mission "Uhuru," Swahili for freedom; Bruno Rossi of MIT, who founded X-ray astronomy with Giacconi and the 1962 rocket flight that discovered Scorpius X-1 and first urged the field into being; Herbert Gursky, Edwin Kellogg, Harvey Tananbaum, Ethan Schreier, and Stephen Murray of the AS&E team, who reduced the scans into the catalog and the discoveries; the engineers at the Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory who built the spacecraft bus; and the staff of the San Marco platform off Kenya who launched it. From a low, near-equatorial orbit a slowly spinning collimator swept the X-ray sky — and where the field had seen only brief glimpses, the first map of the X-ray universe appeared.
