**The Chitlin' Circuit as Engine Subject in Its Own Right.** The **Chitlin' Circuit** is the name given (not always approvingly) to the network of venues that catered to Black audiences across the American South, border states, and Eastern cities during the era of legal segregation and into the post-Civil-Rights period. **The Little Richard Apprenticeship — Firings and Rehirings as Pedagogy.** The 1964 residency behind **Little Richard Penniman** was the most formative apprenticeship of Hendrix's early career. Little Richard (born 1932 in **Macon, Georgia**) was one of the architects of rock and roll — the screaming, flamboyant, gospel-derived performer who shaped **Elvis Presley**, **The Beatles**, **James Brown**, and virtually everyone who came after. His live show commanded complete attention and demanded complete surrender from his audience. The sidemen's role was to be invisible — to provide the musical foundation while Richard delivered the spectacle. Hendrix could not maintain this invisibility. He would improvise, add flourishes, play too loud, attract audience attention to himself. Richard reportedly fired and rehired Hendrix multiple times over the residency. What Hendrix learned from Little Richard was the physical performance — the showmanship, the audience command, the use of the body as theatrical instrument. The specific tricks Hendrix would later use at **Monterey Pop** in 1967 (burning the guitar) and at subsequent performances (playing guitar with the teeth, behind the head, between the legs) were not invented by Hendrix — they were learned from the Black rhythm-and-blues tradition on the circuit and then pushed further than anyone had imagined. **In parallel**, the **Snoqualmie Tribe**'s relationship to **Snoqualmie Falls** demonstrates a different form of learning: the oral tradition of the **Sahalie Tyee** creation narrative, passed down through generations as the central organizing story of who the Snoqualmie are, preserves cultural knowledge through repetition, ritual practice at the falls, and continuous transmission from elder to younger generations across ten thousand years. **The same** transmission-through-repetition-and-correction structural principle organizes both halves: Hendrix learning performance through the repeated firing-and-rehiring pedagogy of Little Richard's demanding circuit, and the Snoqualmie preserving Sahalie Tyee through the disciplined repetition of oral tradition across millennia. **Each side** demonstrates that sustained knowledge requires sustained contact with a demanding tradition, and the **paired coordinate** at degree 246 converts the individual transmission observations into a multiplicative statement about how cultural knowledge is held and passed across both cross-cultural-apprenticeship and intra-cultural-lineage axes.

**Snoqualmie Falls — 268 Feet Over the Columbia River Basalt Group.** **Snoqualmie Falls** is 268 feet (82 meters) tall — 100 feet taller than **Niagara Falls** at its Horseshoe Falls. ## Part A: Jimi Hendrix (Early Career) — The Formation Years from Seattle Central District Through Fort Campbell Through the Chitlin' Circuit to the Cafe Wha? Discovery

- **Seattle Central District Origin and the Childhood Guitar:** **James Marshall Hendrix** was born on **November 27, 1942** at **King County Hospital** in Seattle. He grew up in the **Central District**, the historically Black neighborhood that would also produce **Quincy Jones** and **Bruce Lee** across overlapping decades. Around 1958, roughly when his mother **Lucille Jeter Hendrix** died, Hendrix acquired first a ukulele and then a one-string acoustic guitar — accounts vary on the exact instruments, but the important fact is the self-directed learning: no lessons, no formal instruction, no teacher. Learning by ear, by watching other guitarists, by imitation of records. The acoustic district of the Central Area — jukebox bars, record stores, and the radio — provided the cultural substrate through which the young Hendrix absorbed the current of popular music: **Little Richard**'s screaming gospel-rock, **Chuck Berry**'s two-handed guitar lines, **Muddy Waters**' electric blues, **B.B. King**'s control of the single note. He was a student of everything on the radio, and the absence of a teacher meant he had to invent his own way of reproducing what he heard. This self-directed learning period is the foundation of every technical choice Hendrix would subsequently make.
- **The Left-Hand Inversion — Rebuilding Chord Vocabulary From First Principles:** **Jimi Hendrix** was left-handed. The instruments available to him in 1958-1960 were right-handed guitars. Rather than seek out left-handed instruments (rare and expensive) or learn to play right-handed (the conventional solution for left-handed guitarists), Hendrix restrung right-handed guitars upside-down and played them with his left hand. This choice forced every chord shape to be rethought from first principles: the standard first-position chord shapes that right-handed guitarists learn first were not available to him, because the string order (low E to high E) was inverted relative to their fingering diagrams. He built his chord vocabulary from scratch. The consequence — subtle but decisive — is that Hendrix's harmonic intuition developed through a reconfigured instrument rather than through the standard chord-diagram pedagogy, and the unconventional fingerings produced unconventional voicings that became characteristic of his mature playing. The left-hand inversion technique is not a trivia item; it is the specific physical configuration that shaped Hendrix's entire subsequent musical vocabulary.
- **The Velvetones and the Rocking Kings — Teenage Covers Bands:** **The Velvetones** (1958-1959) and the **Rocking Kings** were the first performing bands Hendrix played in during his late-teens Central District years. These were R&B covers bands playing at dances and small venues, and they are not significant in themselves — they were the practice sessions, the repetitions that built physical facility. A working teenage covers band in Seattle in 1958-1960 played the standard repertoire of contemporary R&B, rock and roll, and blues; Hendrix played that repertoire night after night, learning song structure, stage behavior, and the basic economics of working as a musician. There are accounts of **Jimi** auditioning for a local television program and not succeeding; the auditions and rejections of the early years are part of the formation, learning what does not work and developing resilience. By 1961 he was ready to leave Seattle.
- **The US Army 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell — Meeting Billy Cox:** In **1961**, facing minor legal trouble in Seattle (two joyriding arrests), **Jimi Hendrix** enlisted in the **US Army**. He was assigned to the **101st Airborne Division** at **Fort Campbell, Kentucky** — one of the most demanding postings in the Army, known for its airborne and special forces units. He completed jump school and made at least one parachute jump. At Fort Campbell, Hendrix met **Billy Cox**, a bass player from **Pittsburgh**. The two bonded over music and began playing together at the base. Cox would become Hendrix's most enduring musical partner, the bassist for the **Band of Gypsys** in 1969-1970 and for Hendrix's final 1970 touring period including the **Isle of Wight Festival** in August 1970. Hendrix received a general discharge in 1962 — accounts suggest a letter from a fellow soldier about inappropriate behavior (some accounts suggest the letter described Hendrix falling asleep on duty). The discharge was not honorable but not dishonorable; it was the Army's way of releasing a soldier who clearly had priorities elsewhere. Hendrix and Cox played briefly in **Nashville** post-Army — Nashville in 1962-1963 was a music city dominated by country but with an active R&B scene, and Hendrix's time there represented his first post-Army professional musical activity.
- **The Chitlin' Circuit 1963-1966 — Education Through Black-Audience Venue Infrastructure:** From approximately 1963 through 1966, **Jimi Hendrix** worked the **Chitlin' Circuit** — the network of venues catering to Black audiences across the American South, border states, and Eastern cities during the era of segregation and into the post-Civil-Rights period. The circuit's infrastructure included the **Green Book**-listed hotels, restaurants, and services serving Black travelers, the Black-owned venues that hosted touring Black acts, the promoters and managers who organized the economic relationships, and the transportation routes between cities that could be safely navigated by Black touring musicians. The circuit was not a metaphor — it was literal infrastructure, sustained across decades by the economic and social necessity created by legal segregation and continuing for years after the formal end of segregation because the patterns of cultural and commercial separation persisted. Hendrix navigated this circuit as a working sideman, playing behind the major touring Black acts of the 1964-1966 period.
- **The Little Richard 1964 Apprenticeship — Firings and Rehirings as Pedagogy:** The most significant apprenticeship of Hendrix's early career was the **1964** residency behind **Little Richard Penniman**. Little Richard (born 1932 in **Macon, Georgia**) was one of the architects of rock and roll — the screaming, flamboyant, gospel-derived performer who shaped **Elvis Presley**, **The Beatles**, **James Brown**, and virtually everyone who came after. His live show commanded complete attention and demanded complete surrender from his audience. The sideman's role was to be invisible, to provide the musical foundation while Richard delivered the spectacle. Hendrix could not maintain this invisibility: he would improvise, add flourishes, play too loud, attract audience attention to himself. Richard reportedly fired and rehired him multiple times. What Hendrix learned from Little Richard was the physical performance — the showmanship, the audience command, the use of the body as theatrical instrument. Hendrix watched Richard obsessively and absorbed every technique of stagecraft. The specific tricks Hendrix would later use at **Monterey Pop** in 1967 (burning the guitar) and at subsequent performances (playing guitar behind the head, between the legs, with the teeth) were not invented by Hendrix; they were learned from the Black rhythm-and-blues tradition and then pushed further than anyone had imagined.
- **The Isley Brothers and Curtis Knight — Subsequent Circuit Residencies:** After Little Richard, Hendrix played with **The Isley Brothers** (**Ernie**, **Rudolph**, **Ronald Isley**) whose catalog included "Shout" (1959) and "Twist and Shout" (1962). Playing with the Isley Brothers exposed Hendrix to a professional major-label touring operation — a step up in organizational sophistication from the local circuit bands. The Isley Brothers' guitar-forward sound also gave Hendrix more space than Little Richard's piano-centered arrangements. With **Billy Cox** on bass, Hendrix briefly played Nashville in the **King Casuals**. In 1965-1966, Hendrix played with **Curtis Knight and the Squires** in New York. Curtis Knight was a singer and bandleader whose personnel varied in the mid-1960s; Hendrix's time with the Squires produced the recordings that were made under the 1965 **Ed Chalpin** / **PPX Enterprises** contract — the key documentary recordings of pre-Experience Hendrix, and the recordings that would generate the subsequent Reprise-settled litigation after the Experience became famous.
- **The PPX Contract and the Exploitation Pattern:** In **October 1965**, Hendrix signed a contract with **Ed Chalpin**'s **PPX Enterprises** agreeing to make recordings for a minimal payment ($1 and a royalty). Chalpin would subsequently release and repackage these recordings in various forms after Hendrix became famous with the Experience, generating legal action that resulted in the settlement under which the **Band of Gypsys** album was originally released to **Capitol Records** (Chalpin's US distributor) as a resolution of the PPX claim against **Reprise Records**. The contract is a reminder that the music industry's exploitation of young Black musicians was systematic rather than exceptional, and the engine records this without editorializing as part of the history rather than as a moral lesson. The PPX contract and its subsequent litigation are documented in extensive post-Hendrix published sources; the terms ($1 payment, minimal royalty) are widely attested in biographies and in the litigation record.
- **The Cafe Wha? and the Chas Chandler Discovery:** By mid-**1966**, Hendrix was playing his own set as **Jimmy James and the Blue Flames** at the **Cafe Wha?** on **MacDougal Street** in **Greenwich Village**. The Cafe Wha? was one of the central venues of the New York folk and rock scene — **Bob Dylan** had played there, **The Mamas & the Papas** had played there, and in 1966 Hendrix was playing there with a backing band, playing his own music for the first time as a headliner rather than a sideman. **Chas Chandler**, bassist for **The Animals**, saw Hendrix at the Cafe Wha? in late June or early July 1966. Chandler was himself a professional musician, not an unsophisticated observer. His recognition of Hendrix was an expert witness's verdict. Chandler immediately began planning to bring Hendrix to London. The June-July 1966 Cafe Wha? residency was the threshold — the last formation-era venue before the London transition. Everything that followed — the Experience, **Noel Redding**, **Mitch Mitchell**, "Hey Joe," "Purple Haze," Monterey, Woodstock, "The Star-Spangled Banner," Electric Ladyland — was built on the foundation of those 1958-1966 Seattle-through-Cafe-Wha? years.

## Part B: Waterfall Snoqualmie — The 268-Foot Sacred Basalt-Cliff Drop, the Snoqualmie Tribe's Sahalie Tyee Creation Site, the 1899 Underground Powerhouse, and the *Twin Peaks* Opening Establishing Shot

- **Physical Baseline — 268 Feet Over the Columbia River Basalt Group:** **Snoqualmie Falls** is 268 feet (82 meters) tall — 100 feet taller than **Niagara Falls** at its Horseshoe Falls. The cliff face is **Columbia River Basalt Group** — a massive volcanic formation laid down between 17 and 6 million years ago by successive lava flows from fissure eruptions in what is now eastern Washington and Oregon. The basalt flowed westward from the source region, filling river valleys and eventually reaching the Pacific. The flat, columnar structure of basalt combined with the differential hardness of basalt versus the softer soils above creates the vertical cliff face that makes the falls possible. The **Snoqualmie River** simply runs off the edge of the hard basalt into the softer valley below. The geology is not incidental — it is causal. Without the Columbia River Basalt Group, there would be no cliff; without the cliff, no falls.
- **Flow Volume and Acoustic Character:** At peak flow (spring snowmelt), **Snoqualmie Falls** carries approximately 25,000 cubic feet per second. At minimum flow (late summer), the falls may carry less than 1,000 cubic feet per second. The acoustic character changes dramatically between these extremes: at peak flow, the falls is an overwhelming, physically felt roar that obscures all other sounds within hundreds of meters; at low flow, the falls is still powerful but the individual water streams are discernible. Below the falls, the **Snoqualmie River** enters a steep gorge before widening into the broader **Snoqualmie Valley**. The gorge walls amplify the falls' sound, creating a resonance that increases apparent volume and adds reverberation. A visitor standing at the base observation platform is immersed in sound that seems to come from every direction. The viewing platforms are an upper observation deck (looking down at the falls) and a lower trail leading to a base area. The base area provides the closest access and the most overwhelming acoustic experience — approximately 85-100 decibels at typical viewing distances, in the range where conversation is difficult and sustained exposure becomes physiologically stressful.
- **Roaring White-Noise Wall at E=7 — The Acoustic Concept:** The sound of **Snoqualmie Falls** approaches white noise. White noise is sound that contains equal energy at all frequencies within a defined bandwidth — the audio equivalent of white light. A large waterfall generates sound at every audible frequency simultaneously through the turbulent, chaotic collision of enormous volumes of water with a deep pool. There is no dominant pitch, only an omnipresent broadband roar. The E=7 rating reflects this physical presence: the falls is loud in the way that a very large amount of water falling a very long way is loud — not the crack of thunder or the impact of a body-slap but the sustained roar of a continuous event that does not stop. The E=7 compared to E=5 for **River Current Over Boulders** at degree 245 represents the step change when a river collects its distributed energy into a single vertical drop: the distributed energy of a river in motion becomes the concentrated energy of free fall.
- **The Snoqualmie Tribe and the Sahalie Tyee Creation Narrative:** The **Snoqualmie Tribe** (also spelled Snoqualmoo) are a **Coast Salish** people who have lived in the **Snoqualmie Valley** and the surrounding Cascade foothills since time immemorial. Their territory extended from the Cascade Mountain passes to the shores of Puget Sound, with the **Snoqualmie River** as the central geographical and cultural axis. In Snoqualmie oral tradition, the falls is **Sahalie Tyee** — the Transformer. This is the location where the ancestral figure Transformer created the first human beings, shaping them from the land itself. The falls is the origin point — the place where the present world came into being. It is not a metaphor; it is the literal site of creation in Snoqualmie cosmology. The falls has been a site of spiritual practice, purification, and prayer for the Snoqualmie Tribe for thousands of years. Tribal members still practice these traditions at the site. The water of the falls, falling from the heights, carries spiritual significance: it comes from the mountains (associated with spiritual power) and falls to earth (the transition between worlds). The mist of the falls is itself considered to have purifying properties.
- **The 1855 Treaty of Point Elliott and the Retained Sacred-Site Rights:** The Snoqualmie ceded most of their territory in the **1855 Treaty of Point Elliott** but retained rights to the falls site as a sacred place. The treaty was signed at **Mukilteo** by multiple Puget Sound tribes and guaranteed fishing rights "in common with all citizens" while reserving certain lands. The Snoqualmie signed this treaty. The subsequent history of land management around the falls involved multiple non-tribal entities taking control of the surrounding property. The tribe's relationship to the falls has required ongoing negotiation and advocacy across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The **Snoqualmie Indian Tribe** was federally re-recognized in 1999 after decades of administrative termination — a restoration of legal standing that affirmed the tribe's continuing relationship to the Snoqualmie Valley and the falls at its center.
- **The 1899 Snoqualmie Falls Power Company and the Underground Powerhouse:** The first hydroelectric plant at **Snoqualmie Falls** was built in 1899 by the **Snoqualmie Falls Power Company**. It was one of the first underground powerhouses in the world — the generators are located inside the cliff, carved into the rock below the falls. Water is diverted from above the falls, channeled through penstocks within the cliff, and released through turbines to generate power before re-entering the river below. The current underground powerhouse is operated by **Puget Sound Energy** and has a generating capacity of approximately 56 megawatts. The plant continues to supply power to the regional grid. The hydroelectric diversion reduces the volume of water going over the falls. At low-flow periods, the visible curtain of water is smaller than the river's natural flow would produce, and the acoustic character of the falls during these periods is also reduced. The diversion is a trade-off between power generation and the aesthetic/ecological experience of the falls at full flow — and a trade-off layered over the pre-existing sacred-site relationship that the Snoqualmie Tribe maintains to the full-flow falls. The underground powerhouse is a feat of 1890s engineering; the tradeoff it imposes is a feature of a different order.
- ***Twin Peaks* Opening Establishing Shot — Mass-Culture Encoding:** **David Lynch** and **Mark Frost**'s *Twin Peaks* (1990-1991 television series, 1992 *Fire Walk With Me* theatrical film, 2017 *The Return* limited series) opens every episode with a sequence of establishing shots: a bird on a wire, a sawmill, and — most memorably — **Snoqualmie Falls**. The falling water, accompanied by **Angelo Badalamenti**'s iconic theme music, is the visual statement that grounds the show in the Pacific Northwest before any narrative element appears. Lynch and Frost chose the falls for its combination of visual grandeur and the sense of hidden depth — the falls plunges into a gorge that cannot be seen into from above. This visual quality, something powerful and large happening beyond line of sight in a place that cannot be reached, is central to the thematic content of the show. *Twin Peaks* is about what lies beneath the beautiful surface of an American small town; the falls, which hides its base from above, is the show's central metaphor made landscape. The fictional **Great Northern Hotel** is the **Salish Lodge** on the clifftop directly above the falls. The diner, the roadhouse, the high school, and the other series locations were filmed in actual places in **King County**, Washington — **Snoqualmie** and **North Bend** especially. The show pioneered the serialized narrative, the unreliable narrator, and the blending of soap opera, horror, mystery, and surrealism in a format that would not be matched until the prestige-TV era of the 2000s-2010s. For an entire generation of television viewers, Snoqualmie Falls became the most recognizable image of the Pacific Northwest in American mass culture.
- **The Sacred-and-Spectacular Overlay — Multiple Meanings of a Single Place:** **Snoqualmie Falls** is simultaneously a major natural soundscape, a site of living indigenous spiritual practice, a nineteenth-century industrial-heritage landmark, a twenty-first-century utility operation, a tourist destination, and a film-location landmark — a layering of uses and meanings that is unusual even by Pacific Northwest standards. The engine documents all of these simultaneously rather than privileging one over the others: the sacred-geography dimension through the Snoqualmie Tribe, the industrial-heritage dimension through the 1899 underground powerhouse, the utility dimension through Puget Sound Energy's current operation, the tourism dimension through the observation decks and the **Salish Lodge**, and the mass-culture dimension through *Twin Peaks*. The falls does not reduce to any one of these. The engine records them all and leaves the reader to hold the complexity rather than smoothing it into a single interpretive frame.
- **The Hydrological Arc Position — Second of Four:** **Waterfall Snoqualmie** at degree 246 is the second entry in the four-degree hydrological arc spanning 245-248: **River Current Over Boulders** (degree 245) opens the arc as the first abiotic SPS entry at E=5 polyrhythmic; **Waterfall Snoqualmie** (degree 246) continues at E=7 roaring white-noise wall; **Sol Duc Falls** (degree 247, projected) will continue the waterfall register at a different scale; **rain on maple** (degree 248, projected) will close the arc at a distributed-precipitation register. The arc moves from river to waterfall to waterfall to rain, documenting the water cycle as a continuous acoustic system. The engine has committed four consecutive degrees to the hydrological cycle — an unusual commitment for non-biological subjects — because the water cycle is the acoustic substrate beneath every living organism the engine has documented, and its explicit documentation as an arc organizes the engine's shift from biological-species SPS to landscape-level SPS across the second half of Quadrant III.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Exceptional commitments require exceptional justification and should not be repeated.** The engine's two-degree Hendrix commitment across degrees 245-246 violates the one-degree-per-subject rule that organized the first 244 degrees. The justification is specific: the **Chas Chandler** discovery at the **Cafe Wha?** in 1966 produced a discontinuity sharp enough to organize Hendrix's work into two distinct artistic registers. The engine has committed not to repeat this exception. Future engine releases should identify rule-exceptions when they occur, articulate the specific justification, and commit to non-repetition where the justification is subject-specific rather than category-general.
2. **Reverse chronological ordering can privilege analytic order over biographical order.** The engine documented the Experience era first (degree 245 at E=8) and the early career second (degree 246 at E=7) because the analytic order privileges the peak creative period over the formation backstory. Future engine releases should consider non-chronological ordering when analytic order carries more interpretive weight than biographical order, because the decision to order by analytic importance rather than by timeline is a first-class engine-design choice rather than a default.
3. **Background infrastructure deserves documentation as a first-class subject rather than as framing for more visible figures.** The **Chitlin' Circuit** is not backstory for Hendrix's fame; it is the infrastructure of Black American popular music under legal segregation and a subject in its own right. The **Snoqualmie Tribe**'s relationship to **Sahalie Tyee** is not caption for *Twin Peaks*' location shooting; it is an indigenous cosmological claim spanning ten thousand years. Future engine releases should identify subjects whose structural importance exists beneath the visible mass-culture surface and should document them as first-class entries rather than as ornamental framing.
4. **Transmission through repetition and correction is a recurring pedagogical pattern across cultural domains.** **Hendrix**'s apprenticeship under **Little Richard** proceeded through the repeated firing-and-rehiring pedagogy under which Hendrix learned the sideman role through violation and correction rather than explicit instruction. The **Snoqualmie Tribe**'s preservation of **Sahalie Tyee** proceeded through disciplined repetition of oral tradition with generational correction toward canonical form. Future engine releases should identify transmission-through-repetition-and-correction when it organizes significant cultural preservation or learning, because the register documents a specific pedagogical pattern distinct from explicit-instruction pedagogies.
5. **Extractive infrastructure can be layered over pre-existing cultural or individual claims.** The **Ed Chalpin** / **PPX Enterprises** 1965 contract extracted value from Hendrix's pre-fame recordings against royalty terms that did not anticipate subsequent commercial success. The 1899 **Snoqualmie Falls Power Company** (now **Puget Sound Energy**) underground powerhouse extracts hydroelectric value from a sacred site whose tribal claim predates the utility by an order of magnitude in time. Future engine releases should identify extractive-infrastructure-over-pre-existing-claim when it occurs, because the pattern documents a recurring layered-extraction dynamic across both individual-scale and infrastructure-scale contexts.
6. **Enduring partnerships can operate across vastly different temporal scales without losing structural character.** **Billy Cox** and **Jimi Hendrix** partnered from the 1961 **Fort Campbell** meeting through the 1970 **Isle of Wight Festival** — sixty-year-equivalent human scale. The **Snoqualmie River** and **Snoqualmie Falls** partnered across the 17-6 million-year lifetime of the **Columbia River Basalt Group** cliff face — geological scale. Future engine releases should identify enduring partnerships across both human-biographical and geological-deep-time durations, because the partnership-vocabulary holds its structural character across wildly different temporal scales.
7. **Expert-witness recognition events define the threshold between pre-mass-culture obscurity and mass-culture visibility.** **Chas Chandler**'s June-July 1966 recognition of Hendrix at the **Cafe Wha?** was an expert witness's verdict, not an uninformed audience's cheer. **David Lynch** and **Mark Frost**'s choice of **Snoqualmie Falls** as the *Twin Peaks* opening establishing shot was similarly an expert witness's recognition of a visual signature. Future engine releases should identify threshold-recognition-events when they define the pipeline from expert recognition to subsequent mass-culture encoding, because the pattern documents a specific mechanism through which subjects become mass-culture visible.
8. **Physical substrate determines acoustic character more than acoustic intentions alone.** The **Columbia River Basalt Group** cliff and the **Snoqualmie River** flow volume determine the falls' acoustic output; the venues and available amplification on the **Chitlin' Circuit** determined the formative Hendrix acoustic output. Future engine releases should identify physical-substrate-determines-acoustic-character when it applies, because the pattern documents that acoustic outcomes emerge from physical constraints rather than from acoustic intentions alone, and the principle extends across both geological-hydrological and musical-performance subjects.
9. **Multiple simultaneous meanings can coexist at a single place without reducing to a single interpretive frame.** **Snoqualmie Falls** is simultaneously a natural soundscape, an indigenous sacred site, a nineteenth-century industrial-heritage landmark, a twenty-first-century utility operation, a tourist destination, and a film-location landmark. Future engine releases should record multiple simultaneous meanings at a single place or subject rather than privileging one interpretive frame, because the engine's documentation vocabulary can hold complexity rather than smoothing it into a single register.
10. **Arc ordering can privilege acoustic progression over geological or biographical sequence.** The hydrological arc (degrees 245-248) orders entries by acoustic progression (river → waterfall → waterfall → rain) rather than by geological-history or water-cycle-chronology sequence. Future engine releases should consider acoustic-progression ordering when the acoustic sequence carries more interpretive weight than the alternative ordering, because ordering is a first-class design choice rather than a default.
