## Summary

**Jimi Hendrix (Early)** arrives at E=7 -- the pre-fame years, the chitlin circuit sideman, the guitarist who had not yet become Jimi Hendrix. The engine devotes two consecutive degrees to Hendrix because the two phases of his career are distinct enough to require separate documentation. The early Hendrix -- the man playing behind Little Richard in 1964, behind the Isley Brothers in 1964 and 1965, behind King Curtis, Joey Dee, and Ike and Tina Turner -- is not the Hendrix of "Purple Haze." He is a guitarist in subordinate roles, learning the touring economy, developing technique under conditions that required adaptation rather than expression. He was regularly reprimanded by Little Richard for drawing too much attention from the audience with his playing. He was fired more than once. He was broke. The E=7 assigned to this phase is not a downgrade -- it reflects a different kind of energy, the energy of a fully formed musician working within constraints, playing blues and soul rather than psychedelic rock, playing for dancers rather than listeners.

By 1965 Hendrix had moved to New York and was leading his own group, Jimmy James and the Blue Flames, when Chas Chandler found him. Everything that followed -- the Experience, London, "Hey Joe," Monterey, Woodstock -- was built on the foundation of those years as a sideman. The blues vocabulary that Hendrix carried was learned on the road, in clubs, behind other people's names. Degree 246 documents that foundation.

On the soundscape side, **Waterfall Snoqualmie** enters the engine -- **Snoqualmie Falls**, 268 feet, one of the most visited natural sites in Washington State and one of the most photographically documented waterfalls in the Pacific Northwest. *Snoqualmie Falls* drops in a single continuous fall into a pool that generates a permanent mist visible from the observation decks. The **roaring wall** sound -- the sustained acoustic presence of a large waterfall heard from proximity, a broadband noise that fills the entire frequency range and physically vibrates the body -- registers at E=7. This is the loudest SPS entry since the White Sturgeon's splash at E=4, and the highest abiotic SPS energy reading so far.

Snoqualmie Falls is sacred to the Snoqualmie people, who have lived in the Snoqualmie Valley for thousands of years. The falls is *Snoq* in the Snoqualmie language -- the name of the people and the name of the falls are the same word. The Snoqualmie Tribe's creation story places the origin of the people at the falls. Puget Sound Energy operates hydroelectric generators at the base of the falls, built in 1898, the first underground powerhouse in the world. The turbines are still running. The falls continues to fall. The Snoqualmie people are still there.

The cultural resonance extends into American film and television: *Twin Peaks*, David Lynch's 1990-1991 television series and 1992 film, opens with a sustained sequence shot at Snoqualmie Falls. The opening credits of *Twin Peaks* use the falls as the establishing image of the Pacific Northwest -- the logging, the water, the mist, the mountains. The falls at degree 246, paired with the early Hendrix, carries both the geological and the cultural weight of one of the region's defining visual landmarks.
