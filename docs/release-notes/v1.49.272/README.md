## Part B: Greater White-fronted Goose — The Musical Laughing Call and the First *Anser* Genus

- **Fifteenth consecutive Anatidae extends the all-time family-streak record into unprecedented territory.** The **Greater White-fronted Goose** (*Anser albifrons*) extends the **Anatidae** streak to **fifteen consecutive waterfowl** at degrees 118-132. The streak spans Harlequin Duck (118, E=5), Common Merganser (119, E=4), Hooded Merganser (120, E=4), Wood Duck (121, E=3), Bufflehead (122, E=3), Barrow's Goldeneye (123, E=3), Common Goldeneye (124, E=4), Greater Scaup (125, E=3), Canvasback (126, E=4), Tundra Swan (127, E=5), Trumpeter Swan (128, E=6), Canada Goose (129, E=5), Cackling Goose (130, E=4), Brant (131, E=4), Greater White-fronted Goose (132, E=4). Fifteen species. Seven genera. Every major Anatidae subfamily present in the Pacific Northwest: sea ducks, diving ducks, dabbling ducks, mergansers, swans, and both major goose genera. The streak is now **nearly double the Picidae nonet** (107-115, 9 consecutive) that previously held the all-time-prior family-streak record before this Anatidae run surpassed it. Unprecedented territory.
- **First *Anser* genus entry introduces the gray geese after three consecutive *Branta* black-goose entries.** Until degree 132, the engine's goose coverage had stayed entirely within ***Branta*** — the black geese with black heads, black bills, black legs, and white cheek patches (Canada, Cackling) or white necklace (Brant). Degree 132 shifts the genus to ***Anser*** — the gray geese with gray-brown heads, pink-orange bills, orange legs, and diagnostic white foreheads. The visual difference is dramatic. The acoustic difference is dramatic. The two genera represent distinct evolutionary lineages within the Anatidae family, and the Pacific Northwest hosts both during winter. The genus shift from *Branta* to *Anser* at degree 132 parallels the earlier shift from *Cygnus* (swans) at 127-128 to *Branta* (geese) at 129-131 — both transitions mark engine progression through the family's generic diversity while preserving family-level continuity.
- **The musical laughing call as the most complex goose vocalization in the Pacific Northwest.** The Greater White-fronted Goose's call is a **high-pitched, laughing, yodeling "kla-ha" or "kow-yow"** at 500-3,000 Hz, with a liquid, musical quality and a distinctive laughing cadence. Two to three syllables, with pitch rising on the second syllable. The "laughing" description is apt — the sound genuinely resembles human laughter, especially when multiple birds call simultaneously. The frequency range is higher than all other PNW geese: Canada Goose (200-2,500 Hz energy centered low), Brant (200-1,200 Hz rolling murmur), Cackling Goose (400-2,000 Hz energy centered at 600-1,200 Hz). The syrinx structure produces the distinctive pitch modulation — the rising and falling that creates the laughing quality — and the call is more complex in structure than the simpler honks and cackles of the *Branta* geese. The laughing call is the 132nd species-side acoustic element in the engine's accumulating catalog.
- **The goose quartet complete across four species, two genera, and four distinct acoustic strategies.** The four-part goose quartet (129-132) now documents every major goose vocalization strategy present in the Pacific Northwest: deep honk (Canada Goose, E=5, brassy commanding rolling honk wall), higher cackle (Cackling Goose, E=4, sharp rapid chattering), low rolling murmur (Brant, E=4, continuous gabble with oceanic quality), musical laugh (Greater White-fronted Goose, E=4, yodeling liquid flock-chorus). Four species, four sounds, two genera. The quartet completes the engine's goose acoustic survey at degree 132 and demonstrates that Anatidae family cohesion contains substantial species-level acoustic differentiation — same family, different genera, different calls, different ecologies, unified by the family's shared migratory flocking social structure.
- **Specklebelly as diagnostic field mark and widely-used common name.** The Greater White-fronted Goose's appearance is distinctly different from the *Branta* geese. **Gray-brown overall**. No black head or neck (unlike all three *Branta* geese). The head and neck are gray-brown, the same shade as the body. The white "front" (white band at the base of the pink-orange bill) is the diagnostic field mark that gives the species its official name. Dark bars and blotches on the belly create a "speckled" appearance — the source of the nickname **"specklebelly"**, widely used by hunters and birders in the field. The belly markings are variable across individuals and can range from sparse bars to heavy blotching. The pink-orange bill (*Branta* species have black bills) and orange legs (*Branta* species have black legs) are further distinctive features that separate *Anser albifrons* from the *Branta* goose-era entries at degrees 129-131.
- **Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta breeding grounds and circumpolar distribution.** The Greater White-fronted Goose is primarily a **winter visitor** to the Pacific Northwest. Its breeding grounds are the **Arctic tundra** of Alaska and northern Canada. The Pacific population breeds on the **Yukon-Kuskokwim Delta** and North Slope of Alaska, and migrates purely with no PNW resident populations. Globally, *Anser albifrons* has a **circumpolar distribution** — breeding across the Arctic from Greenland through Siberia to Alaska, which makes the Greater White-fronted Goose one of the most widely distributed waterfowl in the world. The North American populations are a fraction of the global population (concentrated in Eurasia), but the Pacific population provides the PNW winter presence that degree 132 documents. Every Greater White-fronted Goose in the Pacific Northwest in winter bred in the Arctic that summer and will return to the Arctic in spring.
- **Skagit Valley, Willamette Valley, and Ridgefield NWR as primary PNW wintering grounds.** The species winters in Pacific Northwest agricultural lowlands from October through March. The **Skagit Valley** in Washington hosts Greater White-fronted Geese in agricultural fields alongside Canada Geese, Snow Geese, and Trumpeter/Tundra Swans. The **Willamette Valley** in Oregon hosts wintering flocks mixed with Cackling Geese and Canada Geese in waste-grain and winter-wheat fields. **Ridgefield NWR** along the Columbia River hosts wintering Greater White-fronted Geese in agricultural and wetland habitats. The species is uncommon to fairly common in appropriate habitat but never numerically dominant (Canada Goose dominates PNW goose counts). In mixed flocks, the Greater White-fronted Goose is often **first detected by voice** — the laughing call stands out among the deeper honks and sharper cackles of the more numerous *Branta* geese and separates immediately as "the one that sounds different." Voice is the fastest detector.

This field-specialist strategy parallels the Canada Goose and Cackling Goose strategies but diverges from Brant, and the agricultural adaptation provides reliable food resources that make the species less ecologically constrained than the marine-specialist Brant. The global population is large and stable because the agricultural adaptation has scaled with agricultural intensification across North America and Eurasia. Not a conservation concern.
