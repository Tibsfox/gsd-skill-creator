**Buck Grunt: 50-200 Hz Territorial Vocalization at E=4.** The **buck grunt** is the vocal component of Black-tailed Deer territorial behavior during the fall rut. The vocalization is a low, guttural "uurrh" pitched at **50-200 Hz**, produced through chest-cavity resonance and lasting 0.3-1.0 seconds per grunt with intervals of 5-30 seconds during active territorial display. The low frequency is functionally significant: low-frequency sound propagates farther through forest vegetation than high-frequency sound, enabling the grunt to carry 100-300 meters through the dense western-Washington second-growth forest, second-growth mixed conifer-deciduous forest, and forest-edge habitats that Black-tailed Deer typically occupy. The grunt serves dual communicative functions: **mate attraction** (signaling the buck's presence to reproductively-available does within his territory) and **territorial claim** (warning rival bucks that the territory is occupied and challenging them to leave or contest). Rival-buck encounters often escalate from mutual grunting through **sparring** (locking antlers and pushing), though these contests are usually brief and rarely lethal. The grunt's E=4 rating reflects the vocalization's forcefulness balanced against its moderate absolute amplitude — a buck grunt is audible and carrying, but not loud by mammalian standards (a sea lion roar registers at E=5-6, a whale breach at E=7), and the E=4 rating positions the vocalization as the energy of controlled assertion rather than explosive amplitude.

**Antler Cycle and the Coupling of Acoustic Behavior to Biology.** The buck grunt is coupled to the **antler cycle**, one of the fastest tissue-growth processes in the animal kingdom. Antlers grow from pedicles on the buck's skull beginning in **April-May** at a rate of **1-2 cm per day**, covered in blood-rich **velvet** tissue that supplies oxygen and nutrients to the growing bone. By **late August through September**, the antlers have reached their full size and the velvet dries and is rubbed off against trees and brush, producing the **buck rubs** that are part of the territorial marking system. From **October through November** the antlers are fully hardened and polished, ready for use as weapons and visual displays during the peak of the rut. After the rut, antlers are **shed** between **December and March**, the pedicles heal, and the cycle restarts in spring. The buck grunt only occurs during the hardened-antler phase of the cycle (November-December rut); buck vocalizations during the remainder of the year are limited to snorts and alarm calls. ## Part B: Black-tailed Deer Buck Grunt — The Fawn-Buck Pair, the Rut, and the Antler Cycle

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** The **Black-tailed Deer** (*Odocoileus hemionus columbianus*) is a member of **Order Artiodactyla / Family Cervidae / Subfamily Capreolinae / Genus Odocoileus / species hemionus / subspecies columbianus**. The species is the Pacific Northwest variant of the broader Mule Deer (*O. hemionus*), distinguished from other Mule Deer subspecies by coat coloration, antler configuration, and geographic range — Columbian Black-tailed Deer occupy western Washington, western Oregon, and northern California west of the Cascade crest. The subspecies is the most commonly encountered large mammal in western Washington with populations of 300,000-500,000 individuals statewide across forest, forest-edge, suburban, and island habitats from the Olympic Peninsula through the Puget Sound lowlands through the Cascade foothills.
- **Second Black-tailed Deer Entry and the Fawn-Buck Comparison Pair:** The degree 196 buck entry is the **second Black-tailed Deer entry** after the first at degree 195 (v1.49.336, fawn maa bleat at E=3). The two consecutive-degree entries form the engine's **first fawn-buck comparison pair**, establishing a new documentation technique for capturing **ontogenetic variation** — how an animal's sound changes across its life cycle. The fawn and buck have opposite vocal frequencies (500-1,500 Hz fawn bleat vs. 50-200 Hz buck grunt, more than an order of magnitude range), opposite functions (contact call "where are you?" vs. territorial claim "I am here"), opposite amplitudes (thin vulnerability vs. forceful dominance), and a +1 energy step (E=3 to E=4) mapping the biological transition from dependent infant to mature territorial adult. The comparison pair preserves the full vocal range of a single Pacific Northwest species across two consecutive engine degrees.
- **The 50-200 Hz Territorial Grunt:** The **buck grunt** is the vocal component of Black-tailed Deer territorial behavior during the fall rut. The vocalization is a low, guttural "uurrh" pitched at **50-200 Hz**, produced through chest-cavity resonance and lasting 0.3-1.0 seconds per grunt with intervals of 5-30 seconds during active territorial display. The low frequency is functionally significant: low-frequency sound propagates farther through forest vegetation than high-frequency sound, enabling the grunt to carry 100-300 meters through the dense western-Washington second-growth mixed conifer-deciduous forest that Black-tailed Deer typically occupy. The grunt's carrying distance is longer than the fawn bleat's (which is designed to reach only the mother at short range), reflecting the buck's territorial claim to a larger area than the fawn's contact call.
- **Rut Timing and Territorial Behavior:** The **fall rut** occurs in **November-December** in western Washington. During the rut, bucks become increasingly active, territorial, and vocal as the rut approaches its peak. Each buck establishes and patrols a territory, marking boundaries through a coordinated multi-channel signaling system that includes the vocal grunt, visual **buck rubs** (antlers rubbed against trees to remove velvet and mark territory), physical **scrapes** (ground scraped with hooves), and chemical scent deposits from glands on the forehead and legs. The coordinated marking system communicates to does (mate attraction) and to rival bucks (territorial claim) simultaneously, and the different channels (vocal, visual, chemical) function over different distances and time-scales, with the grunt operating as the longest-range immediate-signal channel.
- **Sparring and Rival-Buck Encounters:** Rival-buck encounters during the rut can escalate from mutual grunting through **sparring**, in which two bucks lock antlers and push against each other. Sparring contests are usually brief and rarely lethal, serving as ritualized dominance displays rather than fights to the death. The grunt often precedes sparring as an escalation signal, warning the rival that further approach will be met with direct physical contest. The sparring-contest-outcome determines territorial dominance within the immediate area, with the dominant buck retaining mate access and the subordinate buck retreating to a different territory.
- **Antler Cycle as One of the Fastest Tissue Growth Processes in the Animal Kingdom:** The buck's **antler cycle** is one of the fastest tissue-growth processes in the animal kingdom. Antlers grow from pedicles on the skull beginning in **April-May** at a rate of **1-2 cm per day**, covered in blood-rich **velvet** tissue that supplies oxygen and nutrients to the growing bone. By **late August through September**, the antlers have reached their full size and the velvet dries and is rubbed off against trees and brush. From **October through November** the antlers are fully hardened and polished, ready for use as weapons and visual displays during the peak of the rut. After the rut, antlers are **shed** between **December and March**, the pedicles heal, and the cycle restarts in spring. The cycle proceeds regardless of the buck's preceding success or failure in the previous rut — pedicle healing is constitutive, not reward-based — and antler size correlates with age and nutrition rather than with prior-season reproductive outcomes.
- **Antler Size, Age, and Nutrition:** Antler size correlates with the buck's **age** and **nutrition**. A healthy mature buck (4-6 years) typically grows 3-4 points per side (six-point or eight-point total). Younger bucks have smaller antlers — spike antlers in their first year, forked antlers in the second year, and the full mature rack configuration emerging around year four. Older bucks past their prime (8-10+ years) often have declining antler size as overall body condition declines. Regional nutritional variation (Olympic Peninsula versus Cascade foothills versus Puget Sound lowlands) produces observable antler-size variation across Washington state's Black-tailed Deer population.
- **Acoustic-Biological Coupling and the Engine's First-Class Register:** The buck grunt's coupling to the antler cycle — the grunt only occurs during the November-December hardened-antler phase of the annual cycle — is a first-class example of **acoustic-biological coupling** in which vocal behavior is coupled to specific biological states rather than operating as a free-running communication channel. The coupling enters the engine's SPS vocabulary at degree 196 as a mammal-biology register distinct from continuous-vocalization species (songbirds, whales, many marine mammals) whose vocal behavior operates independently of specific biological states. Future cervid entries (Elk, Moose) will likely reference the same acoustic-biological coupling via their respective bugle-and-roar vocalizations coupled to their respective antler cycles, establishing the coupling as a Cervidae-family register.
- **Snort as Complementary Alarm Call:** Alongside the territorial grunt, Black-tailed Deer of both sexes and all ages produce a **snort** alarm call — a sharp, explosive exhalation through the nose pitched at 200-800 Hz (higher than the grunt but lower than the fawn bleat) that signals predator detection. The snort is year-round rather than rut-specific and serves a different communicative function (danger alert rather than territorial claim), and its acoustic characteristics (explosive onset, nasal resonance, higher frequency) distinguish it from the grunt's low-frequency chest resonance. The engine's documentation at degree 196 notes the snort as the secondary vocalization alongside the primary rut grunt.
- **Forest Sound Propagation and Habitat Acoustics:** The buck grunt's carrying distance of 100-300 meters reflects the acoustic properties of the western-Washington second-growth mixed conifer-deciduous forest habitat that Black-tailed Deer typically occupy. Low-frequency sound (50-200 Hz) propagates through forest vegetation via long-wavelength transmission that is less affected by foliage scattering than high-frequency sound. The habitat acoustics shape the vocalization characteristics: a buck grunt that worked well for territorial claim in dense forest vegetation differs from the bugle of an Elk bull in more open grassland-forest-edge habitat, and the engine's documentation captures the habitat-vocalization coupling as part of the species documentation.

The technique is reusable for other species with distinct life-stage vocalizations, and future engine iterations should identify candidates where consecutive-degree life-stage pairs would add structural depth to species documentation.

2. **Second entries at identical E ratings across large degree separations signal sonic-identity stability.** 764-HERO's 84-degree separation between degree 112 and degree 196 at identical E=4 ratings converts a coincidence (same rating, different contexts) into a structural observation (the band's sonic identity was fixed from the start). The observation is diagnostic: a band whose two entries land at different ratings would signal identity drift, whereas identical ratings signal stability. Future two-context artist documentation should explicitly check the E-rating-stability pattern as part of the documentation.

3. **Three-label triads structure indie-scene documentation better than single-label entries.** The Pacific Northwest indie triad (Sub Pop, K Records, Up Records) organizes three distinct aesthetic strands (defiance-through-distortion, smallness-as-resistance, restraint-as-method) into a three-label framework that any future Pacific Northwest indie artist can be situated within. The triad converts three separate label entries into a structural framework, and the framework is more productive than flat label listings. Future regional-indie documentation should look for similar triad structures in other regions (Chapel Hill, Athens GA, Montreal, Glasgow) where three or more distinct indie traditions can be identified.

4. **Acoustic-biological coupling is a first-class species-documentation register distinct from continuous vocalization.** The buck grunt's coupling to the antler cycle — grunts only occur during the hardened-antler phase, which is tied to the full annual cycle of growth-velvet-shedding-hardening-rut-shedding — distinguishes coupled-vocalization species from continuous-vocalization species (songbirds, whales, many marine mammals). Future SPS documentation should explicitly identify when a species' vocal behavior is coupled to biological states (breeding seasons, antler cycles, molt cycles, hibernation cycles) and treat the coupling as part of the acoustic documentation.

5. **Controlled expression outperforms undisciplined output across trained and instinctive sources.** John Atkins' indie-rock restraint (trained aesthetic discipline) and the buck's rut grunt (instinctive territorial signaling) both land at E=4, both achieve effective communication through minimum-necessary output, and both demonstrate that controlled expression is more effective than undisciplined output. The parallel across trained and instinctive sources is structurally productive because it shows the controlled-expression principle is not confined to trained artistic practice but also operates in biological signaling systems that evolved under selection for communicative effectiveness.

6. **The two-context artist register needs explicit documentation to prevent conflation with career-phase splits.** 764-HERO's documentation at degrees 112 (Indie/Electronic) and 196 (Indie Rock) is a two-context artist entry (simultaneous dual identity) rather than a career-phase split (like DoNormaal's early/late entries at degrees 67/188 documenting different career eras). The distinction matters because the two structures have different implications: two-context signals sustained duality, career-phase split signals evolution over time. Future multi-entry artist documentation should explicitly identify which structure applies and avoid conflating them.

7. **Up Records and Kill Rock Stars form the Seattle-Olympia intersection for indie rock.** The Up Records / Kill Rock Stars dual-label presence across 764-HERO and other Pacific Northwest indie artists (Sleater-Kinney on Kill Rock Stars, The Decemberists on Kill Rock Stars later) documents an intersection point between Seattle-based Up Records and Olympia-based Kill Rock Stars that is structurally distinct from the K Records Olympia twee-pop tradition. Future engine documentation should maintain the distinction between the two Olympia traditions (K Records twee, Kill Rock Stars indie-rock-and-riot-grrl) rather than collapsing them into a single Olympia-indie register.

8. **The Pacific Northwest base dataset is reinforced by every pair where both halves are PNW-specific.** Degree 196 is a reinforcement event because both halves (764-HERO and Black-tailed Deer) are PNW-specific subjects that could not exist in a non-PNW engine. The reinforcement pattern should be tracked across the engine's documentation to surface how often pair releases feature both-halves-PNW versus one-half-PNW versus neither-half-PNW, and the pattern can inform future scope decisions about whether the engine should stay strictly PNW-grounded or expand to broader regional coverage.

9. **Capitol Hill as recurring geographic node deserves cross-release documentation.** Capitol Hill has appeared at multiple degrees across multiple genres (188 DoNormaal hip-hop, 191 The Posies power pop, 194 Tullycraft twee pop, 196 764-HERO indie rock) but no consolidated Capitol Hill cross-release artifact exists. The cumulative pattern — a single neighborhood hosting the full genre spectrum across a two-decade-plus era — is structurally significant and deserves a dedicated cross-release documentation artifact rather than being scattered across individual release retrospectives.

10. **Multi-entry family arcs benefit from two-entry starts that establish arc intent.** The Cervidae family arc begins at degree 195 (fawn) and extends at degree 196 (buck), and the two-entry start establishes from the beginning that Cervidae documentation will extend across multiple degrees rather than being collapsed into a single representative entry. The same two-entry family-arc-start technique is applicable to other large mammal families (Canidae via Coyote and Gray Wolf, Felidae via Bobcat and Cougar, Ursidae via Black Bear and Grizzly Bear) where multiple PNW-relevant species warrant documentation. Future engine iterations should consider two-entry starts as the default for family arcs rather than one-entry introductions that require later retcon to establish arc intent.
