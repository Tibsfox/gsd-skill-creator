## Surprises

- **The largest space telescope ever flown launched folded inside a rocket.** JWST's 6.5 m segmented primary mirror (18 gold-coated hexagons) and its five-layer, tennis-court-sized sunshield were far too large for any rocket fairing, so they launched folded and unfolded in space over several weeks during the month-long cruise to L2 — a slow, intricate origami of the largest optics ever flown to a space telescope, with hundreds of single-point failure steps that all had to work.
- **The deepest view of the early universe must be infrared.** JWST's First Deep Field (SMACS 0723, July 2022) reaches the earliest, most distant galaxies — light from a few hundred million years after the Big Bang — and that light is necessarily infrared, stretched out of the optical/ultraviolet by the expansion of the universe, which is exactly why the deepest view of the earliest galaxies is the infrared one (further back than Hubble's optical deep fields could see) and why Spitzer's infrared successor was built so large.
- **JWST flies far beyond any servicing reach, at the Sun-Earth L2 point.** Where Hubble orbits low enough for astronaut servicing, JWST flies in a halo orbit around the Sun-Earth L2 Lagrange point, ~1.5 million km from Earth on the night side, so the Sun, the Earth, and the Moon all stay behind the sunshield — a cold, stable, sunshielded platform that can never be serviced but keeps the infrared detectors near ~40 K.
- **JWST reads the air of distant worlds, including the carbon dioxide in one.** JWST (with NIRSpec) made the first clear, definitive detection of carbon dioxide (CO2) in an exoplanet atmosphere — the hot Jupiter WASP-39b (2022) — by watching the molecular absorption feature appear in the starlight filtered through the planet's atmosphere as it transited, characterizing the kind of worlds that surveys like TESS and Kepler found, a distinct first from Hubble's first exoplanet atmosphere (sodium) and Spitzer's first exoplanet light.
- **A living relic of an ancient rodent lineage burrows in the cool dark of the deep old forest.** The Mountain Beaver (*Aplodontia rufa*), the most primitive / ancient living rodent — the sole survivor of an ancient family (Aplodontiidae), a "living fossil" of deep evolutionary time — lives in cool, dark, deep burrow systems in the moist Pacific Northwest old-growth understory (documented natural history only; NOT a true beaver, a distinct ancient lineage, distinguished from the American Beaver *Castor canadensis*) — a relic of deep time in the deep, dim forest, a mirror of the deep time JWST gazes into.
- **An ancient plant lineage unfurls its delicate triangular fronds in the deep forest shade.** The Oak Fern (*Gymnocarpium dryopteris*), a small, delicate, triangular-fronded fern of cool, shaded, moist Pacific Northwest old-growth and streamside forest, belongs (like all ferns) to an ancient plant lineage far older than the flowering plants, and its young fronds unfurl from coiled fiddleheads in the deep forest shade (ecological description only, distinguished from other ferns such as the Spreading Wood Fern *Dryopteris expansa*) — an ancient one of the deep, shaded forest, bound with JWST's deep-time gaze.

## Dedication

v1.264 JWST is dedicated to the people across NASA, ESA, the Canadian Space Agency, the Goddard Space Flight Center, the Space Telescope Science Institute, Northrop Grumman, and the mission's science teams who built and fly the largest space telescope ever sent aloft. They look into the deep and the old, and the first light of the early universe comes home.
