## Surprises

- **The reflected sunlight turned into the minerals of the dry land.** EMIT does not sample the soil directly — it reads sunlight. The Dyson imaging spectrometer disperses the reflected sunlight across ~285 contiguous bands from the visible to the shortwave infrared, and the diagnostic absorption features in those bands — the deep iron-oxide bands of hematite and goethite, the clay and carbonate and sulfate features — are fit to the surface mineralogy. The light returning from the dry land, read from the Station, becomes a map of the minerals it is made of.
- **An instrument that tells warming dust from cooling dust.** Rather than treating wind-blown dust as a single grey haze, EMIT maps the actual minerals in the dust-source soil, and the ratio of the dark, iron-rich minerals to the lighter clays and carbonates sets whether the airborne dust warms or cools the climate — the first global spaceborne surface-mineral dataset for dust radiative forcing, the input the climate models lacked.
- **A mineral mapper that also finds the plumes.** A surface-mineral instrument turned out to read the air too: the same high-fidelity imaging spectroscopy that fits the mineral absorptions also recovers the methane and carbon-dioxide absorption of super-emitter point-source plumes, mapping individual leaks and stacks — a bonus of the dust-mineral mission, distinct from a dedicated atmospheric-CO₂-column observatory.
- **A long instrument heritage, given the Station's vantage.** EMIT's Dyson imaging spectrometer carries the long airborne heritage of AVIRIS and AVIRIS-NG into orbit on the ISS, where the ~51.6° / ~407 km orbit happens to cross the arid dust belt again and again — the heritage of decades of airborne imaging spectroscopy, given a global vantage over the dry mineral land.
- **A small digger of the dry mineral soil.** The Pygmy Rabbit (*Brachylagus idahoensis*), the smallest North American rabbit and a sagebrush-obligate of the Columbia Basin and Great Basin shrub-steppe, digs its own burrows in the deep, friable, dry mineral soil beneath big sagebrush — the small digger of the dry mineral land EMIT reads from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A shrub-steppe buckwheat rooted in the bare mineral ground.** The Snow Buckwheat (*Eriogonum niveum*), a low woody buckwheat of the dry, sandy and rocky mineral soils of the Columbia Basin shrub-steppe, with woolly grey leaves and creamy-white late-summer flowers, roots in the bare dry mineral ground — a plant of the dry mineral land, bound with the throughline of the dry mineral land.
