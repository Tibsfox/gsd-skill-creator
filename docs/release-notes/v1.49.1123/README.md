## Mission Overview

EMIT is the obs#26 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA/JPL's ISS-hosted visible-to-shortwave-infrared imaging spectrometer, the Earth Surface Mineral Dust Source Investigation that maps the surface mineral composition of Earth's arid dust-source regions from the International Space Station and turns the reflected sunlight it reads in hundreds of contiguous spectral bands into maps of the mineral colour of the dry land. ★ EMIT is a NASA Earth Venture Instrument (EVI-4) mission built and led by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), hosted on the ISS and mounted on the ExPRESS Logistics Carrier 1 (ELC-1, on the Flight Releasable Attachment Mechanism FRAM-8). ★★ Its instrument is a Dyson-form imaging spectrometer (AVIRIS / AVIRIS-NG heritage; F/1.8, a HgCdTe 1280 × 480 detector) that reads the reflected sunlight across the VSWIR (~380–2500 nm) in ~285 contiguous product bands at ~7.4 nm spectral sampling, at roughly ~60 m spatial resolution over a ~75 km swath. ★ It maps the surface mineral composition of the arid dust sources — the ratio of dark, warming iron-oxide minerals (hematite, goethite) to lighter clays, carbonates, and sulfates (the ten prime minerals) — to constrain the radiative forcing of wind-blown mineral dust; as a bonus, the same high-fidelity spectroscopy maps methane and carbon-dioxide point-source plumes. EMIT images the land surface globally across the arid dust belt, identifying the minerals that determine whether wind-blown dust warms or cools the climate — the mineral colour of the dry land and the dust it sends to the sky. The mission launches into the story on 14 July 2022 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 on the CRS-25 Dragon cargo flight from NASA's Kennedy Space Center (LC-39A); the Dragon berths at Harmony on 16 July 2022, and EMIT is robotically installed externally on the ISS ELC-1. The story is the CRS-25 launch from Kennedy, the robotic installation on the ELC-1, the Dyson imaging spectrometer dispersing the reflected sunlight across ~285 contiguous VSWIR bands onto the focal plane, the absorption features identifying the iron oxides and the clays and the carbonates and the sulfates of the dust-source land, the ten prime minerals mapped to constrain the radiative forcing of the wind-blown dust, and the methane and carbon-dioxide point-source plumes the same instrument also reveals — the mineral colour of the dry land read from above, scene after scene. The science is central: from the ISS orbit EMIT reads the reflected sunlight in hundreds of contiguous bands and turns it, by the imaging spectroscopy and the spectral-fitting retrievals, into the surface mineral composition of the arid dust sources and the maps of the point-source plumes, repeated across the dust belt. ★★★ EMIT is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (EMIT maps the surface minerals, images the dust-source land, identifies the iron oxides, scans the arid belt, and maps the methane and carbon-dioxide plumes); it launched 14 July 2022, was installed in 2022, and operates today — the launch and the installation being the only past events. EMIT's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first imaging-spectrometer mission dedicated to mapping the mineralogy of Earth's arid dust-source regions; it provides the first global spaceborne surface-mineral dataset for dust radiative-forcing models; and it demonstrated high-fidelity imaging-spectrometer mapping of methane and CO₂ point-source emissions. ★ The scope is load-bearing: imaging spectrometers flew in space before EMIT — Hyperion (EO-1), PRISMA, and DESIS preceded it — so EMIT is NOT "the first imaging spectrometer in space," and methane was mapped from space before EMIT — SCIAMACHY, TROPOMI, and GHGSat preceded it — so EMIT is NOT "the first methane from space"; its distinction is the first imaging spectrometer dedicated to arid dust-source mineralogy, the first surface-mineral dataset for dust radiative forcing, and the demonstrated imaging-spectrometer greenhouse-gas point-source mapping. ## Dedication

v1.306 EMIT is dedicated to the people at NASA and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and across the imaging-spectroscopy and dust-science teams, who built and operate the spectrometer that reads the mineral colour of the dry land from the Space Station and turns the reflected sunlight the Earth returns in hundreds of contiguous bands into maps of the surface minerals of the arid dust-source regions. To the engineers who shaped the Dyson optics and the cooled detector from the long airborne heritage, and tuned them to tell the iron oxides, the clays, the carbonates, and the sulfates apart. To the crews who carried it aloft on a Falcon 9 in July 2022 and the robotic arm that set it on the Station's exposed logistics carrier. To the scientists who learned to read the dark, warming dust from the lighter, cooling dust, and to map the methane and carbon-dioxide plumes the same instrument reveals. The mineral colour of the dry living land, and the dust it sends to the sky, are theirs to read.
