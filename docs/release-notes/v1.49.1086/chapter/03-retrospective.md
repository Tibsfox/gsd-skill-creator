## Surprises

- **A mission whose whole purpose was to bring the Sun home.** Genesis flew to the Sun-Earth L1 point, opened ultra-pure wafers of silicon, sapphire, gold, and diamond-like carbon, and patiently let the solar wind embed itself in them for about 2.3 years — then closed the wafers and carried the Sun's own scattered material back to Earth to be read in the laboratory, the first mission dedicated to capturing and returning solar-wind samples.
- **The Sun sorted into fast, slow, and stormy.** Genesis carried separate collector arrays for the fast (coronal-hole), slow (interstream), and coronal-mass-ejection regimes of the solar wind, with onboard ion and electron monitors sensing which kind of solar wind was passing and switching in the matching array — so the returned samples preserved the composition of each regime separately.
- **The first sample return from beyond the Moon.** Genesis returned material gathered far out at L1 in September 2004 — NASA's first sample return since Apollo (1972) and the first from beyond the Moon, ahead of Stardust's comet-dust return (2006) and Hayabusa's asteroid return (2010).
- **The Sun's oxygen and nitrogen, written in the wafers.** The returned samples revealed that the Sun is enriched in oxygen-16 relative to Earth and the inner planets (McKeegan et al. 2011) and is markedly poorer in nitrogen-15 (Marty et al. 2011) — a measurement of the composition of the solar nebula from which the planets formed, read from the captured solar wind.
- **A native packrat whose gathered midden is a deep-time archive.** The Bushy-tailed Woodrat (*Neotoma cinerea*), a native Pacific Northwest rodent ("packrat") of rocky cliffs, talus, and montane / dry-interior canyons, gathers plant fragments, seeds, and objects into middens; cemented by crystallized urine ("amberat") and preserved in dry shelters, those middens persist for tens of thousands of years and are read by scientists as archives of past vegetation and climate (documented natural history only) — a mirror of the mission that gathered the Sun's material and stored it to be read long after.
- **A long-lived dry-slope shrub that records long spans of time.** Curl-leaf Mountain-mahogany (*Cercocarpus ledifolius*), a long-lived, slow-growing evergreen shrub or small tree of dry rocky montane slopes in the interior PNW (rose family), with small leathery curl-edged leaves and feathery-plumed seeds, can live many centuries — itself a long record of dry-slope time, and among the plant material woodrats gather into their middens (ecological description only, distinguished from the true mahoganies and from mountain-ash) — bound with Genesis's gathered solar archive by the gathered archive.
