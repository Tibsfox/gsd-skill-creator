## Mission Overview

Genesis is the obs#4 sustainer of the HELIOPHYSICS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA Discovery-class mission whose defining records are the first capture-and-return of solar-wind samples, the first sample return from beyond the Moon, and the laboratory reading of the Sun's oxygen and nitrogen isotopic composition. The spacecraft was built by Lockheed Martin Astronautics (Denver) and the mission was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), with principal investigator Donald Burnett of Caltech — NOT JHU APL / STScI / ESA, and the firsts concern solar-wind sample return, not the builder. The fifth flight of NASA's Discovery program, Genesis launched 8 August 2001 on a Delta II 7326 from Cape Canaveral (SLC-17A) and cruised to the Sun-Earth L1 point, where it entered a halo orbit and opened its collector arrays. The collector materials were ultra-pure wafers — silicon, sapphire (corundum), gold, diamond-like carbon, and others (about nine materials across five arrays, roughly 300 collectors) — exposed to embed solar-wind ions, with separate arrays for the fast (coronal-hole), slow (interstream), and coronal-mass-ejection regimes, the arrays selected by onboard ion and electron monitors that sensed which kind of solar wind was passing. Genesis collected for about 2.3 years — roughly 850 days, from 3 December 2001 to 1 April 2004 — then closed and stowed the arrays and flew home. The story is the Delta II launch, the cruise to L1, the years of patient collection as the Sun's scattered material accumulated in the wafers, and the return of those wafers to Earth to be read in the laboratory. The science is central: Genesis measured the solar wind's elemental and isotopic composition, standing in for the composition of the Sun and the solar nebula from which the planets formed; the marquee results were the Sun's oxygen isotopic composition (the Sun is enriched in oxygen-16 relative to Earth and the inner planets; McKeegan et al. 2011) and the Sun's nitrogen isotopic composition (markedly poorer in nitrogen-15 than Earth; Marty et al. 2011), both a major constraint on solar-system formation. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Genesis is a SOLAR / heliophysics sample-return mission, NOT a space telescope, and re-stamps NO space-telescope-axis anchor; it is a NASA Discovery mission built by Lockheed Martin and managed by JPL, NOT a JHU APL / STScI / ESA mission; its firsts are the capture-and-return of solar-wind material and the first sample return from beyond the Moon (distinct from STEREO, which IMAGED the Sun in 3-D; from Ulysses, which flew OVER the poles; and from Parker, which dove THROUGH the corona). The first-to-capture-and-return claim is scoped carefully — Apollo flew brief solar-wind collection foils on the lunar surface (the Solar Wind Composition experiment, exposed for hours), so Genesis is framed as the first mission DEDICATED to solar-wind sample return and the first since Apollo to return such material from deep space, beyond the Moon. ★ The page is framed in PAST tense: the sample-return capsule reentered over the Utah Test and Training Range on 8 September 2004 and its parachute did not deploy, so it landed hard in the desert; the recovery team recovered the collectors, and the science team cleaned and analyzed fragments — the flight mission ended with the 2004 recovery, while sample analysis continued for many years. ## Surprises

- **A mission whose whole purpose was to bring the Sun home.** Genesis flew to the Sun-Earth L1 point, opened ultra-pure wafers of silicon, sapphire, gold, and diamond-like carbon, and patiently let the solar wind embed itself in them for about 2.3 years — then closed the wafers and carried the Sun's own scattered material back to Earth to be read in the laboratory, the first mission dedicated to capturing and returning solar-wind samples.
- **The Sun sorted into fast, slow, and stormy.** Genesis carried separate collector arrays for the fast (coronal-hole), slow (interstream), and coronal-mass-ejection regimes of the solar wind, with onboard ion and electron monitors sensing which kind of solar wind was passing and switching in the matching array — so the returned samples preserved the composition of each regime separately.
- **The first sample return from beyond the Moon.** Genesis returned material gathered far out at L1 in September 2004 — NASA's first sample return since Apollo (1972) and the first from beyond the Moon, ahead of Stardust's comet-dust return (2006) and Hayabusa's asteroid return (2010).

## Dedication

v1.269 Genesis is dedicated to the people across NASA, the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, Lockheed Martin, and Caltech who built and flew the mission that gathered the Sun's own material and carried it home. The engineers who made wafers of silicon, sapphire, gold, and diamond-like carbon pure enough to hold the few atoms of the solar wind, and the navigators who set the spacecraft on its long loop about the Sun-Earth L1 point. The instrument teams whose monitors sensed the fast, slow, and stormy moods of the wind and opened the matching collectors; the recovery team in the Utah desert who, when the parachute did not open and the capsule reached the ground hard, gathered up the collectors and brought them in; and the scientists led by Donald Burnett who cleaned the fragments and read from them the Sun's oxygen and nitrogen — the Sun richer in oxygen-16 than the planets, poorer in nitrogen-15 than Earth — a measurement of the cloud from which the solar system formed. They sent out a collector, waited while it filled, and brought back a record of the Sun written atom by atom. The first material returned from beyond the Moon, read long after it was gathered.
