## Mission Overview

OCO-3 is the obs#25 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA/JPL's ISS-hosted atmospheric-carbon-dioxide spectrometer, the Orbiting Carbon Observatory 3 that measures the column carbon dioxide of the atmosphere and the solar-induced fluorescence of photosynthesis from the International Space Station and turns the reflected sunlight it reads in three near-infrared bands into maps of the carbon in the air. ★ OCO-3 is a NASA mission built and managed by the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), assembled largely from the OCO-2 flight-spare instrument and ground-test hardware, and hosted on the ISS, mounted on the Japanese Experiment Module – Exposed Facility (JEM-EF). ★★ Its instrument is three co-bore-sighted imaging grating spectrometers measuring reflected sunlight in three near-infrared bands — the O₂ A-band (~0.765 µm), the weak CO₂ band (~1.61 µm), and the strong CO₂ band (~2.06 µm) — retrieving XCO₂ (the column-averaged dry-air mole fraction of CO₂; better than ~0.25% / ~1 ppm precision); it also retrieves the solar-induced fluorescence (SIF), a proxy for photosynthesis. ★ It adds a Pointing Mirror Assembly (PMA) that gives agile pointing OCO-2 lacks, enabling four modes — nadir, glint, target, and the Snapshot Area Map (SAM, a dense ~80 × 80 km map acquired in ~2 minutes). OCO-3 measures atmospheric carbon dioxide globally, with the PMA enabling dense city-/region-scale Snapshot Area Maps, plus SIF as a photosynthesis proxy — the carbon sources and sinks of the Earth system. The mission launches into the story on 4 May 2019 (06:48 UTC) on a SpaceX Falcon 9 on the CRS-17 Dragon cargo flight from Cape Canaveral, and is robotically installed on the ISS JEM-EF on 10 May 2019. The story is the CRS-17 launch from Cape Canaveral, the robotic installation on the JEM-EF, the three co-bore-sighted spectrometers reading the reflected sunlight in the O₂ A-band, the weak CO₂ band, and the strong CO₂ band, the retrieval of the column XCO₂ better than ~0.25% / ~1 ppm, the Pointing Mirror Assembly sweeping a dense ~80 × 80 km Snapshot Area Map over a city in ~2 minutes, and the precessing orbit carrying the spectrometer across the full range of daylight local times — the carbon in the air and the fluorescence of photosynthesis read together, day after day. The science is central: from the ISS precessing orbit OCO-3 reads the reflected sunlight in three near-infrared bands and turns it, by the grating spectroscopy and the retrieval algorithms, into the column carbon dioxide of the atmosphere and the solar-induced fluorescence of photosynthesis, global and repeated across the diurnal cycle. ★★★ OCO-3 is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (OCO-3 measures the column carbon dioxide, maps the Snapshot Area, scans the city scene, samples the diurnal carbon, and retrieves the fluorescence of photosynthesis); it launched 4 May 2019, was installed 10 May 2019, and operates today — the launch, the installation, and the November 2023 storage → July 2024 reinstall being the only past events. OCO-3's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first carbon-dioxide spectrometer hosted on the ISS; the first to make Snapshot Area Maps of CO₂ from space (the PMA agile-pointing dense regional maps); and the first dedicated CO₂ observatory to sample the full daylight range of local times (the ISS precessing orbit). ★ The scope is load-bearing: CO₂ was measured from space before OCO-3 — SCIAMACHY (Envisat), GOSAT/IBUKI, and OCO-2 preceded it — so OCO-3 is NOT "the first CO₂ from space" and NOT "the first dedicated CO₂ mission" (that is OCO-2); its distinction is the first CO₂ spectrometer on the ISS, the first Snapshot Area Mapping of CO₂, and the first CO₂ observatory to sample the full daylight range of local times. ## Surprises

- **The reflected sunlight turned into the carbon within the air.** OCO-3 does not sample the air directly — it reads sunlight. The three grating spectrometers measure the reflected sunlight in three near-infrared bands, and the depth of the carbon-dioxide absorption lines in the weak CO₂ band and the strong CO₂ band, anchored by the O₂ A-band's read of the surface pressure and the light path, is inverted to the column-averaged dry-air mole fraction of CO₂. The light returning from the surface, read from the Station, becomes a map of the carbon in the air.
- **A spectrometer that can paint a map of a city.** Rather than reading only the thin track beneath it, OCO-3 adds a Pointing Mirror Assembly that steers the boresight, and in about two minutes it sweeps a dense ~80 × 80 km Snapshot Area Map of XCO₂ over a city or a region — the first Snapshot Area Mapping of CO₂ from space, the agile pointing the OCO-2 design it was built from could not provide.
- **A carbon observatory that reads the hour of the day.** Because the ISS orbit precesses rather than holding one fixed local time, OCO-3's equator crossing drifts about twenty minutes earlier each day, and over a ~63-day illumination cycle it samples the carbon — and the fluorescence of photosynthesis — across the full range of daylight local times. It is the first dedicated CO₂ observatory to see the diurnal cycle of carbon, which sun-synchronous missions fixed at one local time cannot.
- **An instrument built from a spare, given a new sky.** OCO-3's three-band spectrometer is the flight spare of OCO-2, yet on the Station it became a new mission: where OCO-2 flies free in a sun-synchronous orbit, OCO-3 rides the ISS, adds the agile Snapshot Area Map, and samples the diurnal carbon — the same spectrometer design, a different vantage and a new capability.
- **A montane thrush of the high open air above the canopy.** Townsend's Solitaire (*Myadestes townsendi*), a slim montane thrush of the open conifer forests and rocky slopes of the interior Pacific Northwest, perches high on conifer spires in the open air, sallies for insects in summer, and winters largely on juniper berries — a bird of the high open air above the canopy where OCO-3 reads the carbon (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A long-lived conifer that draws the carbon into its wood.** The Incense Cedar (*Calocedrus decurrens*), a tall, long-lived, aromatic conifer of the montane mixed-conifer forests of the interior Pacific Northwest, the Cascades, and the Sierra Nevada, draws carbon from the air into long-lived wood — a carbon-holding tree of the living forest, bound with the throughline of the carbon and the canopy air.

## Dedication

v1.305 OCO-3 is dedicated to the people at NASA and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and across the carbon-observing science teams, who built and operate the spectrometer that reads the carbon in the air from the Space Station and turns the reflected sunlight the Earth returns into a map of the column carbon dioxide of the atmosphere. To the engineers who assembled the instrument from the flight spare of OCO-2 and gave it a Pointing Mirror Assembly, the agile mirror that paints a dense Snapshot Area Map of a city in about two minutes. To the crews who carried it aloft on a Falcon 9 in May 2019 and the robotic arm that set it on the Japanese Experiment Module's exposed deck, and to the operators who returned it to that deck after its season in storage. To the teams who calibrate the three near-infrared bands, retrieve the column carbon dioxide and the fluorescence of photosynthesis, and share the record with the world. The scientists who learned to read the carbon across the full range of daylight hours from the Station's drifting orbit. The carbon in the air and the breath of the living land are theirs to read.
