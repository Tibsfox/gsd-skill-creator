### PHYSICAL DESCRIPTION

3-5 cm (1-2 inches). 2-5 g. One of the smallest vertebrates in the engine. Roughly 1/200th the mass of the Muskrat at degree 85. Expanded adhesive toe pads for climbing. Males have a large, dark vocal sac that inflates during calling, amplifying the call to approximately 80 dB at 1 meter. Highly variable coloration -- green, brown, tan, reddish, or gray, can change color over hours to days. **Dark eye stripe** from nostril through eye to shoulder -- the key field mark, present in all color morphs. Cream to white belly. Smooth, moist skin.

### THE SOUND: AMPLIFIED KREK-KREK WALL OF SOUND -- 87th Species-Side Acoustic Element

**Individual call:** A two-note "krek-ek, krek-ek" -- loud, high-pitched, rapidly repeated. Approximately 1,000-4,000 Hz with peak energy at 2,000-2,500 Hz. Each male calls approximately 2-3 times per second at ~80 dB at 1 meter.

**The chorus:** When hundreds or thousands call simultaneously, the individual calls blend into a **wall of sound** -- continuous, pulsing, amplified. No gaps. Asynchronous calling creates dense, layered texture. Audible from **hundreds of meters**. Dominates the marsh on warm, humid spring nights.

**The "Hollywood frog":** The Pacific Treefrog's call is the sound used in films, television, and media worldwide to signify "nighttime outdoors" -- even in scenes set far from the Pacific Northwest. Arguably the most widely heard frog call on Earth through sound design.

**Why E=4:** Individual calls already loud for body size. Chorus amplified by numbers -- hundreds calling simultaneously. Audible from hundreds of meters. Dominates the marsh soundscape. E=4 reflects overwhelming collective volume. The loudest SPS entry by collective amplification.

Contrasts:

### ECOLOGY: THE SPRING CHORUS

**Breeding:** Late winter through spring. Peaks on warm, humid nights above ~8C / 46F. Males call from marsh edges, often partially submerged. Chorus builds through the evening, peaks mid-night, subsides toward dawn. Females deposit eggs in small clusters attached to submerged vegetation.

**Habitat:** Freshwater marshes, ponds, wetlands, wet meadows. Remarkably adaptable -- also gardens, forests, grasslands. Consecutive marsh with Muskrat (85). Beaver ponds (61) become treefrog breeding habitat.

**Diet:** Insectivorous -- spiders, beetles, flies, ants. **Predators:** Garter snakes, herons, raccoons, bullfrogs (invasive competitor).
