- ***Haliaeetus leucocephalus* — The White-Headed Sea Eagle of North America.** *Haliaeetus leucocephalus* (Linnaeus, 1766) is the only eagle endemic to North America. The genus name *Haliaeetus* combines Greek *halos* ("sea") with *aetos* ("eagle") — "sea eagle" — capturing the species' preferential affinity for water-adjacent habitats. The species name *leucocephalus* combines *leukos* ("white") with *kephale* ("head") — "white head" — identifying the species' diagnostic adult field mark. Adults display white head and tail contrasting with dark brown body plumage, a massive yellow bill, and a wingspan of 6-7.5 feet (180-230 cm), making the Bald Eagle one of the largest raptors in North America. Immature birds are mottled brown-white through approximately four years before reaching adult plumage. Body weight ranges 4-14 pounds, typically 5-9 pounds for adults, and the mass-weight of two eagles in locked-talon freefall is the physical basis of the combat display's cartwheeling dynamics.
- **The Locked-Talon Aerial Cartwheeling Display: Physics and Biology.** The locked-talon combat display is one of the most extraordinary behaviors in North American raptor biology and the SPS track's most physically-committed individual-scale behavior. Two eagles in aerial conflict approach each other at speed; at the moment of conflict, both birds deliberately extend their talons and interlock — each grasping the other's feet with their powerful talons — and the combined weight of two 4-14-pound birds plus the aerodynamic forces acting on two locked birds in freefall produces a tumbling, cartwheeling motion. The birds spin around their combined center of mass, sometimes completing multiple full rotations before separating. Eagles have been documented tumbling in locked-talon freefall for 100 feet or more; the descent is rapid; and the commitment required — to lock talons with an adversary at altitude and accept the ensuing freefall — is physiologically and behaviorally extreme. The display demonstrates willingness to accept serious risk in defense of territory, and an intruder that witnesses or experiences the locked-talon response learns that the territory holder will escalate to maximum commitment.
- **Separation Before Impact Requires Judgment and Physical Capability.** Before ground or water impact, the birds generally separate — releasing their grip and spreading their wings to arrest the descent. The timing of separation requires judgment and physical capability. Occasionally eagles fail to separate in time and hit the ground or water together; this can result in injury or drowning. The risk is real and demonstrably fatal in documented cases. The locked-talon display is therefore not display-for-its-own-sake but a commitment under real physical risk, and the willingness to accept that risk is precisely the communicative content: the territory holder is demonstrating that they will engage with the challenger at a level the challenger may not be willing to match. The display often ends the territorial dispute without further escalation, because once the kinesthetic reality of the freefall is experienced, few challengers are willing to return for a second round.
- **The Acoustic Component: Kee-Kee-Kee Pushed to Maximum Urgency.** Throughout the locked-talon tumble, both eagles vocalize continuously: high-pitched screaming, the same kee-kee-kee call that was documented at degree 221 at E=5 now pushed to maximum volume and urgency. The combat sound is not just visual spectacle — it is an acoustic event visible and audible across the territory. Witnesses and recordists have documented hearing the combined screaming from hundreds of meters away, and the continuous screaming throughout the descent is the primary acoustic content that the degree 297 SPS entry documents at first-class resolution. The acoustic signature at E=7 is the same species' vocalization from the degree 221 contact-call entry at E=5, transformed by context and intensity. The graduated progression E=5 (contact) → E=6 (territorial) → E=7 (combat) demonstrates that the SPS track can document the same species three times with three distinct amplitude registers by selecting three distinct behavioral contexts that make each register structurally appropriate.
- **Pacific Northwest Range Core: Skagit River, San Juan Islands, Puget Sound, Major Coastal Rivers.** The Bald Eagle's Pacific Northwest distribution is characteristic of the species' range nationally, with Washington State holding one of the highest Bald Eagle populations in the contiguous United States. The highest regional concentrations occur along the **Skagit River** (for smelt and salmon runs, particularly the annual November-January chum salmon run that can congregate hundreds of eagles), the **San Juan Islands** (breeding territories), **Puget Sound** shorelines (coastal nesting), and major coastal rivers including the Nooksack, the Stillaguamish, the Skykomish, and the Olympic Peninsula rivers. The **Skagit River Bald Eagle Interpretive Center** documents the congregation during the chum salmon run as the densest Bald Eagle concentration in the contiguous United States during peak years. The salmon dependency connects Bald Eagle ecology directly to the salmon ecosystem — one of the central threads in the SPS engine's ecological documentation — and the riparian-corridor territorial behavior makes the species inseparable from the Pacific Northwest's river-and-coast geography.
- **Territorial Behavior and Nesting Ecology: 10-Foot Aeries Used for Decades.** Bald Eagle territorial behavior is inseparable from nesting ecology. Eagles maintain nesting territories centered on the nest site (the aerie), and territory size varies with habitat quality and prey availability: in areas with abundant salmon (Skagit River, coastal rivers), territories may be smaller because prey is concentrated; in areas with dispersed prey, territories may extend over several miles of waterfront. The Bald Eagle nest is one of the largest bird nests in North America. Eagles use the same nest for many years, adding material each season. The largest recorded nests are 10 feet across, 20 feet deep, and weigh over 2,000 pounds — the accumulated material of decades of annual returns. The nest is itself a territorial marker: visible from long distances, a permanent structure that signals occupancy. Year-round territory defense is most intense during breeding season (nest building November-January, incubation February-March, chick-rearing April-July), and the aerial combat display is most commonly observed during late winter and early spring when egg-laying is imminent and the investment in the territory is at its annual maximum.
- **Population Recovery from DDT: One of the Most Successful Conservation Stories in North American Wildlife Management.** The Bald Eagle was listed under the federal Endangered Species Act in 1978 after DDT-related decline nearly eliminated the species from much of its range. The 1972 DDT ban, coupled with habitat protection measures under the ESA and the **Migratory Bird Treaty Act of 1918**, allowed Bald Eagle populations to recover. The species was removed from the federal endangered species list in 2007 after a remarkable multi-decade recovery, and the Pacific Northwest population has been growing steadily since the 1970s. The recovery is one of the most successful conservation stories in North American wildlife management, and the fact that the locked-talon aerial combat display is now observable at high frequency along Washington rivers and shorelines is itself a conservation success story: the behavior requires high-density territorial engagement that depends on a recovered population, and three decades ago the combat display was rare in many parts of the range because there simply weren't enough eagles to produce the territorial conflicts that the display is performed to resolve.
