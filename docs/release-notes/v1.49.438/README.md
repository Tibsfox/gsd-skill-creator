**Skin Yard (return)** arrive at E=7 -- the second appearance of the Seattle proto-metal band, returning six degrees after their entry at degree 264 (which documented Jack Endino's role as their guitarist and the $606 *Bleach* production story). The return entry at degree 297 shifts the frame: degree 264 was the Endino-as-producer document; degree 297 is Skin Yard as a band in their own right, with the emphasis on **Jack Endino as guitarist** in the context of a proto-grunge sequence. The distinction matters. Endino's work as a producer is how most of the world knows him, but he was a guitarist first -- Skin Yard's guitarist, playing dense, slow, heavy proto-metal riffs in a band he co-founded with Ben McMillan, Daniel House, and Matt Cameron. The return entry foregrounds the musician rather than the production credit.

The proto-grunge context of the current sequence (degrees 293-300) is where Skin Yard's second appearance makes structural sense. Skin Yard were part of that environment from 1985 -- before Sub Pop was Sub Pop, before grunge was named -- and their return at degree 297 acknowledges that their contribution to the proto-grunge record extends beyond Endino's role as the scene's defining producer. The band itself mattered. The band was playing heavy rock in Seattle in 1985, at E=7, in a time when that was a genuinely unusual and generationally productive thing to do.

E=7 for the return is consistent with the first entry at degree 264, reflecting the same catalog assessed from a different angle. The proto-metal density that placed Skin Yard at E=7 the first time is the same density that places them there on return -- the second entry simply emphasizes the musician over the producer.

**Bald Eagle Combat** arrives at E=7 on the SPS track -- the acoustic event produced by two Bald Eagles (*Haliaeetus leucocephalus*) engaged in aerial talon-locking combat: the dramatic territorial and mating behavior in which eagles lock talons in mid-air and spiral in tumbling descent, releasing only when close to the ground, producing a combination of loud shrieking calls and the physical sounds of two large birds in aerial contact. The shrieking tumble is one of the most acoustically and visually dramatic raptor behaviors observable in the Pacific Northwest. Bald Eagles engage in talon combat during territory disputes and, more spectacularly, during courtship -- the cartwheel display in which paired eagles lock talons and roll together toward the earth, a bonding display that looks like an attack. The acoustic component -- the shrieking calls produced during the tumble -- is the SPS entry.

The raptor behaviors arc continues: Peregrine stoop (296, E=8) → Bald Eagle combat (297, E=7) → Goshawk alarm (298, E=7). Three consecutive degrees documenting raptor physical behaviors at high energy. The Bald Eagle's earlier SPS appearances were at degree 221 (thin kee-kee-kee, E=5) and degree 222 (territorial calls, E=6). The combat entry at degree 297 brings the Eagle's SPS maximum to E=7, documenting the highest-energy acoustic event in the species' behavioral register. The three Bald Eagle SPS entries together (221, 222, 297) span five degrees of acoustic documentation, from the softest call to the most physically intense behavior.
