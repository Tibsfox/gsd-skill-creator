## What Could Be Better

- **The Temple of the Dog entry's doubled-posthumous weight is asymmetric across subject and primary-author documentation.** Two of the record's key figures are posthumous — **Andrew Wood** (died 1990, the tribute's subject) and **Chris Cornell** (died 2017, the tribute's primary author) — but the engine's documentation of the tribute register emphasizes Wood's presence as subject while treating Cornell's posthumous status as subsidiary temporal-context information. Future Temple of the Dog documentation (if the record or its members appear in subsequent engine contexts) would benefit from more symmetric documentation of Cornell's posthumous status, particularly the way his 2017 death adds a second layer of posthumous weight to the tribute-record register that the 1991 first-hearing could not carry. The doubled-posthumous-weight register is distinct enough from single-posthumous-weight registers that the engine should document the distinction explicitly rather than treating all posthumous status as equivalent.
- **The forest-interior-apex-raptor register requires care to avoid over-structuring future forest-habitat raptor entries.** The Northern Goshawk's status as the first accipiter and the first forest-interior raptor in the engine could encourage future SPS entries to over-emphasize forest-interior habitat as automatic apex-raptor positioning when the species-specific ecological specializations are the more interpretively valuable aspects. Future forest-raptor documentation (e.g., Cooper's Hawk at 226, Sharp-shinned Hawk at 227) should foreground the specific habitat-preference refinements (Cooper's Hawk's preference for woodland edges and suburban-interface forest, Sharp-shinned Hawk's preference for coniferous thickets and regenerating second-growth) rather than relying on the forest-interior-apex framing as a default categorization, because the apex framing applies specifically to the largest accipiter and does not transfer to the smaller accipiters whose ecological niches are fundamentally different.
- **The Hunger Strike serendipitous-arrangement documentation relies on retrospective music-journalism narrative rather than primary-source recording documentation.** The current documentation summarizes the Cornell-Vedder "Hunger Strike" arrangement based on retrospective interviews (Cornell, Vedder, and Gossard have all described the serendipitous arrangement in subsequent decades) rather than on primary-source studio documentation (A&M session logs, engineer's notes, multitrack recordings) that would confirm the specific sequence of events. Future Temple of the Dog documentation would benefit from primary-source studio documentation where available, because the retrospective-narrative register is subject to the kinds of memory-distortion and mythologization that make precise reconstruction difficult — particularly given Cornell's 2017 death, which forecloses further interview clarification from the song's primary author.

1. **Posthumous-tribute formation contexts are a distinct formation-context register from addiction-treatment-center origins.** Temple of the Dog's formation as compositional tribute to Andrew Wood is constitutive of the band's existence in the same way that Mad Season's Hazelden addiction-treatment-center origin is constitutive of Mad Season — but the two formation-context registers are structurally distinct rather than variations of a single supergroup-origin template. The engine's documentation of two distinct formation-context registers within a single three-degree cluster (224-226) demonstrates that the GRUNGE SUPERGROUP CLUSTER includes register-diversity rather than register-uniformity, and future supergroup documentation should explicitly distinguish formation-context registers rather than treating all supergroups as equivalent.
2. **Consecutive-degree supergroup entries can establish back-to-back constituent-member-overlap as a distinct documentation pattern.** Mike McCready's participation in both Mad Season (224) and Temple of the Dog (225) establishes McCready as a cross-supergroup constituent member whose back-to-back appearance across consecutive degrees produces a specific grunge-supergroup-cluster register. The constituent-member-overlap pattern is interpretively valuable because it documents the density of collaborative networks within the Seattle scene — the way specific musicians participated in multiple supergroup configurations across short time windows — and future engine documentation should attend to constituent-member-overlap patterns explicitly when they occur.
3. **Serendipitous collaborative arrangements carry distinct structural weight from planned collaborative arrangements.** The Cornell-Vedder "Hunger Strike" two-voice arrangement emerged serendipitously from Vedder's presence in adjacent A&M studios, and the serendipitous character of the arrangement is constitutive of the song's subsequent reputation as a moment of fortuitous collaboration rather than as a planned collaborative moment. The serendipitous-two-voice-collaboration register adds to the engine's artist-side vocabulary as a distinct register from planned-collaboration registers, and future artist documentation should record serendipitous formation moments explicitly because the serendipity shapes the eventual reception of the collaborative output.
