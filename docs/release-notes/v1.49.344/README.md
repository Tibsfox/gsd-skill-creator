**Mountain Goat and the First Bovidae in the Engine.** The **Mountain Goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) enters the Sound of Puget Sound engine at degree 203 as the **FIRST BOVIDAE** — the cattle/goat/sheep family's debut in the engine's species documentation. Bovidae is the largest family of large ungulates globally, encompassing cattle, bison, goats, sheep, antelopes, gazelles, and the specialized cliff-dwelling goat-antelope lineage that includes Mountain Goats and chamois. That the first Bovidae entry in the engine is the Mountain Goat rather than a more familiar domestic-or-pastoral bovid reflects the engine's Pacific Northwest focus: the Mountain Goat is the region's only wild Bovidae species, and its alpine cliff specialization makes it both the most geographically specialized large mammal documented in the engine and the most elevation-specialized native mammal across the North Cascades, Olympic Mountains (where it is introduced rather than native), Mount Rainier National Park, and Glacier Peak Wilderness. The degree 203 documentation covers specifically the **adult contact bleat** — a nasal goat-like "baaah" vocalization at 200-600 Hz with 0.5-1.5 second duration, used between herd members on cliff faces where visual contact breaks behind ridges, talus slopes, and snow features. The bleat is functionally analogous to the Roosevelt Elk cow call (documented at degree 198, E=5) in serving the intra-group contact-maintenance role, but the acoustic environment and the taxonomic register are entirely different: cervid forest contact calls and bovid alpine-cliff contact calls operate in different physical contexts and at different sound-physics parameters.

**Not Actually a Goat: The Naming Misidentification.** The Mountain Goat's taxonomic identity is one of the most consequential naming misidentifications in North American vertebrate classification. The common name "Mountain Goat" reflects early-European-naturalist classification based on behavioral and habitat similarity to *Capra* goats — the animal looks like a goat, lives on mountains like a goat, behaves like a goat in many respects, and the European naturalists who encountered it in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries had no phylogenetic analysis available to them to determine that the similarity was convergent evolution rather than shared ancestry. In actual phylogenetic relationship, *Oreamnos americanus* is not in the genus *Capra* (true goats such as the domestic goat, the ibex, the wild bezoar, and related species) at all. It is in the subfamily Caprinae (which includes true goats, true sheep, and goat-antelopes) but in the separate **goat-antelope** group, and its nearest extant relative is the European **chamois** (*Rupicapra rupicapra*), the Alps and Pyrenees cliff-dwelling specialist whose parallel ecology emerged through independent evolution on a different continent. The Mountain Goat is the only living species in the genus *Oreamnos*. The common name is, strictly speaking, a lie that happens to describe the behavior accurately: the animal is not a goat, but it behaves sufficiently like one that early observers' taxonomic error produced a name that captures the functional register even as it misrepresents the phylogenetic register.

**Convergent Evolution and the Cliff-Dwelling Niche.** The relationship between North American Mountain Goats and European chamois is one of the clearest convergent-evolution case studies in mammalian biogeography. Both lineages occupy alpine cliff-dwelling niches on mountain systems separated by approximately 8,000 kilometers and approximately 20 million years of independent evolutionary history from their most recent common ancestor. Both developed split-hoof structures with soft rubbery inner pads that conform to rock surface topography and provide climbing-shoe-level grip on near-vertical terrain. Both developed dense double-layered winter coats suitable for -40 to -50°C alpine conditions (Mountain Goat wool is documented as the warmest coat of any North American mammal). Both developed permanent dagger-like horns present in both sexes (unlike the seasonally-shed antlers of documented Cervidae at degrees 197-199). Both adapted social structures for small-herd cliff-face traversal where herd cohesion requires acoustic contact vocalizations (the Mountain Goat bleat documented at degree 203, and the chamois whistles and bleats documented in European cervid-and-bovid literature). The convergence is sufficiently precise that early European naturalists encountering Mountain Goats reached for goat-or-antelope classification rather than recognizing the independent lineage, and that contemporary taxonomic literature on both species treats them as a comparative pair for exploring how ecological niche pressures drive parallel morphological and behavioral adaptation.

**The Cliff-Fortress Ecological Strategy.** The Mountain Goat's defining behavioral-ecological strategy is the **cliff fortress** — occupying terrain so vertical and unstable that predators physiologically cannot navigate it. The Pacific Northwest's primary Mountain Goat predators (**Mountain Lions**, **gray wolves**, **black bears**, and in some habitats **golden eagles** targeting kids) cannot follow Mountain Goats onto the near-vertical rock faces and narrow cliff ledges where the species conducts daily foraging, bedding, and travel. The Mountain Goat is not a fast runner on flat ground; its body mass (100-180 kg), short-legged structure, and cliff-optimized musculature make it distinctly slower on open terrain than cervid or ovid prey species. The strategy inverts the typical prey-species approach: rather than outrunning predators on terrain where both species can operate, the Mountain Goat retreats to terrain where predators structurally cannot operate. The cost is that Mountain Goats themselves suffer falls from cliffs as a leading cause of mortality — even for a species optimized for vertical terrain, the habitat is lethal — but the benefit is near-complete predator exclusion from the cliff zone. The strategy is a geological-terrain-based predator-avoidance mechanism distinct from the speed-based (cervid flight), camouflage-based (cryptic coloration), and herd-based (collective vigilance) strategies documented elsewhere in the SPS engine.

**The Olympics Introduction and Helicopter Removal.** The Mountain Goat's Pacific Northwest distribution has been substantially modified by twentieth-century human intervention. In the 1920s, approximately 11-12 Mountain Goats were released in the **Olympic Mountains** by hunters who brought breeding stock from Alaska and British Columbia, establishing an introduced population where no native Mountain Goat population had existed. The population grew over subsequent decades to over 1,000 individuals by the 2000s, and the non-native goats caused substantial damage to Olympics-endemic alpine plant communities (species including **Olympic larkspur**, **Piper's bellflower**, and **Flett's violet** evolved without goat-grazing pressure and have no evolutionary defenses against it). Between 2018 and 2020, the **National Park Service** executed a removal program that airlifted approximately 325 Mountain Goats from the Olympics to the North Cascades, where Mountain Goats are native and where the population had been declining. The removal program involved helicopter operations, sedation of individual goats, transport in slings, and release at North Cascades sites. The program is the first documented large-scale twenty-first-century helicopter-assisted removal of an introduced ungulate population in the continental United States, and it is both an ecological-restoration intervention (removing non-natives from the Olympics) and a conservation augmentation (strengthening the declining Cascades native population).

**The Adult Bleat in the Alpine Acoustic Environment.** The **adult bleat** documented at degree 203 is the Mountain Goat's primary contact-maintenance vocalization, used between nannies (adult females) and kids and between herd members traversing cliff terrain where visual contact regularly breaks behind ridges, outcroppings, talus slopes, and snow features. The bleat is a nasal "baaah" pitched at approximately 200-600 Hz with a duration of 0.5-1.5 seconds and a characteristic wavering timbre. The hard rock surfaces that dominate the alpine environment reflect and amplify the vocalization substantially, so that the E=4 amplitude carries effectively across rocky basins at 200-500 meter distances that would require E=5 or higher amplitude in dense forest to achieve the same functional range. ## Part B: Mountain Goat Bleat — The First Bovidae and the Not-Actually-A-Goat

- **Species Baseline and Taxonomic Identity:** **Mountain Goat** (*Oreamnos americanus*) is a member of **Order Artiodactyla / Family Bovidae / Subfamily Caprinae / Genus Oreamnos / species americanus**, the only living species in its genus and the first Bovidae documented in the engine. Despite the common name, *Oreamnos americanus* is not a true goat (genus *Capra*); it is a **goat-antelope**, and its nearest extant relative is the European **chamois** (*Rupicapra rupicapra*) with which it shares the cliff-dwelling niche through convergent evolution. Adult body mass ranges 100-180 kg for large bulls with exceptional individuals exceeding 200 kg; both sexes carry permanent dagger-like horns (unlike the seasonally-shed antlers of Cervidae documented at degrees 197-199); the species exhibits substantial sexual monomorphism relative to many bovids, with females (nannies) and males (billies) similar in size and horn morphology though distinguishable by close observation of horn curvature and size.
- **Pacific Northwest Distribution and Habitat:** Mountain Goat populations in the Pacific Northwest concentrate across the **North Cascades** (the species' Washington native range and the densest extant Pacific Northwest population), **Mount Rainier National Park**, **Glacier Peak Wilderness**, **Alpine Lakes Wilderness**, **Mount Baker**, and the **Olympic Mountains** (where the species is non-native, introduced in the 1920s from Alaska and British Columbia stock). The species occupies alpine and subalpine zones typically 1,500-3,000 meters elevation, above treeline for much of the year, with seasonal altitudinal migration into higher-elevation summer range and lower-elevation winter range where snow depth permits. The distribution corresponds to exposed-cliff-face habitat rather than forested or prairie habitat, making Mountain Goats one of the most elevation-specialized and habitat-specialized large mammals in the engine.
- **Split-Hoof Cliff-Climbing Mechanics:** The Mountain Goat's cliff-dwelling strategy depends on specialized hoof morphology evolved for vertical-terrain traversal. The split-hoof structure includes a hard outer edge that cuts into rock irregularities and a soft rubbery inner pad that conforms to rock surface topography and provides climbing-shoe-level grip on near-vertical surfaces. The dewclaws provide additional traction on steep descents. The hoof morphology is supplemented by powerful shoulder and foreleg musculature for pulling-up climbing and by a low center of gravity that stabilizes the animal on narrow ledges. The combined morphology enables Mountain Goats to traverse rock faces, talus slopes, and cliff ledges that are functionally impassable to predators (**Mountain Lions**, **gray wolves**, **black bears**) and to most other large Pacific Northwest mammals including cervids.
- **Dense Double-Layered Wool and Cold Tolerance:** The Mountain Goat's winter coat is among the most thermally-protective of any North American mammal, documented as the warmest coat of any native species. The coat is double-layered: a soft dense undercoat provides primary insulation, and longer guard hairs provide weather resistance against wind, snow, and rain. The combined coat enables the species to withstand ambient temperatures down to -40 to -50°C with wind speeds exceeding 150 km/h, conditions that would be lethal to most large mammals. The coat sheds extensively in spring, producing the characteristic visible shedding pattern where Mountain Goats appear partially denuded as they transition to summer coat. The shed wool is sometimes collected from cliff faces and shrubs by researchers and craftspeople for fiber-arts purposes; Qiviut-style Mountain Goat wool fiber has documented commercial and craft value.
- **Dagger-Like Horns and Bilateral Sex Horning:** Both sexes carry permanent dagger-like horns that are not shed (unlike the seasonally-replaced antlers of Cervidae documented at degrees 197-199). The horns are made of keratin over a bony core and grow continuously across the animal's life, though growth slows with age and horns exhibit growth rings that correspond to annual cycles. Male (billy) horns are typically thicker at the base and more strongly curved than female (nanny) horns, though field distinction between the sexes from horn morphology alone requires close observation. Both sexes use the horns defensively against predators and in intraspecific combat, though serious injury and mortality from intraspecific combat is less frequent than in antlered cervids because the permanent-horn structure lacks the breakage-vulnerable tine structure of antlers.
- **The Cliff-Fortress Ecological Strategy:** Mountain Goats' primary predator-avoidance strategy is the **cliff fortress** — occupying near-vertical terrain and unstable talus slopes where predators physiologically cannot operate. The strategy inverts the typical prey-species speed-based approach: Mountain Goats are not fast on flat ground, and they cannot outrun **Mountain Lions**, **gray wolves**, or **black bears** in open terrain. Instead, they retreat to cliff habitat where the pursuers cannot follow safely. The strategy carries the cost that falls from cliffs are a leading cause of Mountain Goat mortality — even for a cliff-adapted species, the terrain is lethal — but the benefit is near-complete predator exclusion from the cliff zone. The strategy is a geological-terrain-based predator-avoidance mechanism distinct from the speed-based (cervid flight), camouflage-based (cryptic coloration), and herd-based (collective vigilance) strategies documented elsewhere in the SPS engine, and it establishes the terrain-based-defense register as a first-class category in the engine's predator-avoidance vocabulary.
- **The Adult Bleat: Contact Vocalization in Alpine-Cliff Acoustic Environment:** The **adult bleat** documented at degree 203 is the Mountain Goat's primary contact-maintenance vocalization, used between nannies and kids and between herd members on cliff terrain. The bleat is a nasal "baaah" pitched at approximately 200-600 Hz with 0.5-1.5 second duration and characteristic wavering timbre. Its nasal quality serves a specific acoustic function: alpine cliff environments are dominated by constant wind (sustained at 30-50 km/h with gusts to 150 km/h in winter-storm conditions) that introduces broadband noise across the vocalization frequency range, and the nasal-register emphasis provides the bleat with formant structure that other goats can discriminate from wind noise even at low signal-to-noise ratios. Hard rock surfaces reflect and amplify the vocalization, enabling the E=4 amplitude to carry effectively across rocky basins at 200-500 meter distances. The bleat is distinct from the kid **higher-pitched thinner "meeeh"** (400-1,000 Hz, documented separately in the degree 204 kid entry) and from the billy **rut grunt** (100-300 Hz, documented in the November-December rut-season vocalization register).
- **Naming Misidentification and Convergent Evolution:** The Mountain Goat's common name reflects **early-European-naturalist misidentification**: the species was named "goat" based on behavioral and habitat similarity to *Capra* goats, but phylogenetic analysis establishes that it is not a goat in genus *Capra*. It is a goat-antelope in genus *Oreamnos*, the only species in its genus, and its nearest extant relative is the European **chamois** (*Rupicapra rupicapra*). The North American Mountain Goat and European chamois diverged from their most recent common ancestor approximately 20 million years ago and evolved independently on separate continents, developing similar cliff-dwelling morphology and behavior through convergent evolution rather than shared-ancestry inheritance. The common-name preservation despite phylogenetic correction reflects the tension between taxonomic precision and linguistic conservatism: "Mountain Goat" captures the functional register (animal resembles a goat in behavior) even as it misrepresents the phylogenetic register (animal is not a goat in phylogeny). The naming situation is the first documented case of common-name misidentification in the engine's species vocabulary and inaugurates the **naming-misidentification register**.
- **Olympics Non-Native Population and the 2018-2020 Helicopter Removal:** Mountain Goat distribution in the Pacific Northwest has been substantially modified by twentieth-century human introduction and twenty-first-century removal. In the 1920s, 11-12 Mountain Goats were released in the **Olympic Mountains** by hunters who brought breeding stock from Alaska and British Columbia, establishing an introduced population where no native Mountain Goat population had existed. The population grew to over 1,000 individuals by the 2000s, damaging Olympics-endemic alpine plant communities (**Olympic larkspur**, **Piper's bellflower**, **Flett's violet** — species that evolved without goat-grazing pressure and lack evolutionary defenses against it). Between 2018 and 2020, the **National Park Service**, in partnership with **Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife** and affiliated organizations, executed a removal program that airlifted approximately 325 Mountain Goats from the Olympics to the North Cascades via helicopter operations, sedation protocols, sling-transport systems, and coordinated release at Cascade sites. The program simultaneously accomplished ecological restoration (removing non-natives from the Olympics) and conservation augmentation (strengthening the declining Cascades native population), and it is documented as the first large-scale twenty-first-century helicopter-assisted removal of an introduced ungulate population in the continental United States.

## Lessons Learned

1. **Cross-axes emerge from genuine independent research rather than from forced construction.** The misidentification-and-defiance cross-axis at degree 203 emerged from researching Tacocat's genre-defiance and the Mountain Goat's taxonomic-misidentification independently and noticing the structural overlap, not from deciding in advance to construct a cross-axis linking the two halves. Future degree documentation should prioritize independent research of each half and let cross-axes emerge through comparison, because organically-emerged cross-axes are structurally more robust than forced-constructed cross-axes and produce more interpretive depth per unit of documentation effort.

2. **Multi-degree label or family blocks deserve treatment as first-class interpretive units.** The Hardly Art four-degree streak (201-204) is more interpretive than any single Hardly Art entry, and the Bovidae family inauguration at 203 (with anticipated future Bovidae entries) is more interpretive than any single Bovidae entry. Future engine documentation should explicitly track multi-degree label, family, and cluster blocks as first-class units and provide cross-references, summary artifacts, and energy-or-feature arc documentation that captures the block-level structure rather than requiring individual-degree documentation to surface it piecemeal.

3. **Common-name misidentification is a productive taxonomy-vocabulary register.** The Mountain Goat's not-actually-a-goat status opens the naming-misidentification register as a first-class taxonomy-vocabulary category, and future species documentation should explicitly note when common names reflect early-European-naturalist misclassification, convergent-evolution false-cognate naming, or post-taxonomic-revision linguistic conservatism. The register is substantive because it surfaces the tension between functional-description names (which survive because they capture behavior-and-habitat) and phylogenetic-description names (which require taxonomic revision and public-communication uptake that common names often lack).

4. **Terrain-based predator-avoidance strategy is qualitatively distinct from speed, herd, or camouflage strategies.** The Mountain Goat cliff-fortress case establishes that predator-avoidance via occupation of terrain predators cannot enter is a first-class strategy category, not merely an extreme form of habitat selection. Future SPS documentation of habitat-specialist species should consider whether the habitat specialization functions as predator-avoidance-by-terrain-exclusion and document the strategy category explicitly when it applies, rather than treating habitat specialization as a category orthogonal to predator-avoidance.

5. **Convergent evolution provides a first-class biogeography register for Pacific Northwest species with trans-continental parallels.** The Mountain Goat / European chamois comparison establishes convergent evolution as a structural principle worth explicitly documenting for species whose ecological niche has been independently developed across separated lineages. Future species entries where convergent parallels exist (marine mammals with trans-oceanic parallels, forest specialists with trans-continental parallels) should develop the convergent-evolution register explicitly rather than treating such parallels as incidental observations.

6. **Humor as political vehicle is distinct from irony and distinct from confrontation.** The Tacocat case establishes humor-as-political-vehicle as a first-class delivery register in the engine's aesthetic-vocabulary. Prior feminist-punk documentation has covered the confrontational register (Riot Grrrl tradition) and the ironic register (Chastity Belt at degree 201); the humor register is structurally distinct, operating through open inclusion rather than performed detachment or adversarial directness. Future music documentation should distinguish these three registers rather than collapsing them into a single "comedic" or "ironic" category, because the delivery mechanisms differ even when the political substrate overlaps.

7. **Conservation-management interventions deserve first-class documentation as structural events.** The 2018-2020 Olympics helicopter-removal program is a substantive conservation-management intervention that modified Pacific Northwest Mountain Goat distribution at landscape scale, and its documentation within the degree 203 entry establishes that conservation-history events are first-class interpretive material for the species-documentation vocabulary. Future species entries where recent or ongoing conservation interventions have shaped the species' presence or distribution should treat those interventions as documented structural events rather than as background context.

8. **Palindrome-and-wordplay identity choices can be load-bearing aesthetic theses rather than surface decoration.** Tacocat's palindrome-name-as-thesis case establishes that identity choices like palindromes, wordplay, absurdist names, and similar aesthetic-structural devices can be load-bearing for an artistic proposition rather than ornamental. Future artist-documentation should take seriously aesthetic-structural identity choices when they function as theses rather than decorations, distinguishing band-names-as-branding from band-names-as-aesthetic-thesis when the distinction applies.

9. **Capitol Hill personnel-overlap networks are structurally richer than venue-adjacency observations.** The Tacocat-Childbirth shared-member connection via Bree McKenna, and the broader Tacocat-Childbirth-Chastity Belt-La Luz personnel-and-venue network, are documented in the degree 203 entry and extend through degree 204, establishing that scene-level documentation benefits from tracking personnel-overlap explicitly rather than treating venue-adjacency or label-adjacency as the primary network-structural marker. Future scene documentation should map personnel overlap explicitly where the data supports it.

10. **Alpine-acoustic environments require distinct sound-physics vocabulary from forest and marine contexts.** The Mountain Goat bleat's operation in alpine cliff terrain, with rock-surface reflection, constant-wind interference, and nasal-vocalization adaptation, establishes that the engine's sound-physics vocabulary requires alpine-specific register documentation distinct from forest (Cervidae entries at 197-199) and marine (cetacean entries elsewhere) acoustic contexts. Future species entries in habitat-distinct acoustic environments should develop habitat-specific sound-physics register documentation rather than applying a single generic acoustic-analysis framework across all contexts.
