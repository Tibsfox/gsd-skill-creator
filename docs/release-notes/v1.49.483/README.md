**Gruntruck** arrive at E=8 -- the Seattle metal/grunge band formed in 1990 by Ben McMillan (vocals), Tom Niemeyer (guitar), Tim Paul (bass), and Scott Ledger (drums), releasing *Inside Yours* (1992) on East West Records, and occupying a specific and emotionally weighted position in the Pacific Northwest rock genealogy: McMillan had been the vocalist in Skin Yard (degree 338), the proto-metal band that preceded the Metal Summit's central cluster, and Gruntruck was his next project after Skin Yard's dissolution -- a band that moved from Skin Yard's proto-metal into a harder, more direct metal/grunge register that deployed McMillan's powerful vocal instrument in a heavier context. Gruntruck's sound is heavier than Skin Yard's, more aggressive in its approach to guitar density and tempo, carrying the metal influence that Skin Yard anticipated into a fully formed execution. McMillan's vocal instrument -- a baritone with unusual natural power and a range that extended comfortably into the mid-tenor register for melodic passages -- is the defining acoustic element of Gruntruck's best recordings, deployed over guitar arrangements that the band's metal orientation produced with more consistency and less experimentation than Skin Yard's more diverse approach.

Ben McMillan died on January 20, 2008, at the age of 40, from complications of Type 1 diabetes. Sea lion colonies on the Pacific Northwest coast -- at haul-out sites from the Oregon-Washington border north through the Olympic Peninsula and into the Salish Sea -- produce the colony's aggregate vocalization: hundreds to thousands of individuals producing the bark, growl, and roar of intraspecific communication simultaneously, the aggregate sound carrying across miles of open water and clearly audible above the storm noise at distances of hundreds of meters from the colony edge. The Steller sea lion -- the world's largest sea lion, with bulls reaching 2,500 pounds -- produces individual vocalizations of substantial acoustic power; California sea lions, smaller and more abundant on Pacific Northwest haul-outs, produce higher-frequency barks in dense, overlapping layers. The storm layers over the colony: sustained wind, breaking waves at the haul-out rocks, rain on the water surface, the storm's pressure variation audible as the system's infrasound beneath the colony's vocalization and the storm's wind over both.
