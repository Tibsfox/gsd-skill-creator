## Mission Overview

SMOS is the obs#24 sustainer of the EARTH-SYSTEM-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — ESA's pioneering dual-variable water-cycle mission, the Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity mission that measures both the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea from a single L-band sensor and turns the brightness temperature the Earth radiates at 1.413 GHz into maps of the soil moisture of the land and the sea-surface salinity of the ocean. ★ SMOS is ESA's SECOND Earth Explorer Opportunity mission within the ESA Living Planet Programme, led by ESA with CNES (France, ground segment at Toulouse) and CDTI (Spain); the single MIRAS instrument was built by EADS CASA Espacio (now Airbus Defence and Space). ★★ It carries one payload, MIRAS (Microwave Imaging Radiometer using Aperture Synthesis) — an L-band (1.413 GHz, ~21 cm wavelength) 2-D INTERFEROMETRIC / aperture-synthesis passive radiometer, a Y-shaped array on three deployable arms carrying 69 LICEF receivers that synthesize a two-dimensional aperture across the L-band scene, fully polarimetric, measuring brightness temperature across a range of incidence angles, with NO large mechanical antenna (a synthetic aperture instead). SMOS retrieves soil moisture over land (the top ~5 cm; ~35–50 km resolution; a target of ~4% volumetric accuracy; a ~2.5–3 day revisit) and sea-surface salinity over ocean (from the L-band brightness temperature, the goal of ~0.1 psu reached over a 10–30 day average at 200×200 km), the two water variables of the global water cycle mapped together from one sensor. The mission launches into the story on 2 November 2009 (02:50 CET) on a Rockot (Eurockot, Breeze-KM upper stage) from the Plesetsk Cosmodrome, Russia, into a sun-synchronous, quasi-circular DAWN-DUSK orbit (~6am/6pm; 6:00 ascending node), mean altitude ~758 km, inclination 98.44°; commissioning follows. The story is the 2 November 2009 Rockot launch from Plesetsk, the deployment of MIRAS's three arms into the Y-shaped array, the 69 LICEF receivers synthesizing a two-dimensional aperture across the L-band scene, the radiometer measuring the brightness temperature the Earth radiates at 1.413 GHz across a range of incidence angles, the L-band brightness temperature over land inverted to the soil moisture in the top ~5 cm, and the L-band brightness temperature over ocean inverted to the sea-surface salinity — the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea mapped together, day after day, for more than fifteen years and counting. The science is central: from the sun-synchronous dawn-dusk orbit SMOS measures the brightness temperature the Earth radiates at L-band — the natural microwave emission of the land and the sea — and turns it, by the MIRAS aperture-synthesis interferometry and the retrieval algorithms, into the soil moisture of the land and the sea-surface salinity of the ocean, global and repeated. ★★★ SMOS is OPERATING, so the catalog frames the whole MISSION in the PRESENT (SMOS orbits the Earth, measures the brightness temperature, reads the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea, and maps the soil moisture and the sea-surface salinity); it launched 2 November 2009 and still operates 15+ years on, having far exceeded its nominal ~3-year design lifetime — the launch and the commissioning being the only past events. SMOS's firsts are scoped carefully: it is the first satellite mission to measure BOTH soil moisture and sea-surface salinity from one L-band sensor; the first spaceborne 2-D aperture-synthesis (interferometric) L-band radiometer (MIRAS); and the first of the trio of dedicated L-band water missions (SMOS 2009 → Aquarius 2011 → SMAP 2015). ★ The scope is load-bearing: soil moisture was estimated from space before SMOS — earlier C-band / scatterometer-based products (ERS/ASCAT, AMSR-E) preceded it — so SMOS is NOT "the first soil moisture from space"; its distinction is being the first DEDICATED L-band soil-moisture + salinity mission and the first 2-D interferometric L-band radiometer in orbit. ## Surprises

- **The brightness of the Earth at L-band turned into the water within it.** SMOS does not measure the water of the Earth directly — it measures faint microwave light. The MIRAS radiometer reads the brightness temperature the land and the sea radiate at 1.413 GHz, the natural microwave emission of the surface, and from that brightness, inverted through the retrieval algorithms, it recovers the soil moisture of the land and the sea-surface salinity of the ocean. The microwave glow of the surface, read from above, becomes a map of the water cycle's two key variables.
- **A radiometer with no antenna to point.** Rather than sweeping a single large dish across the scene, MIRAS synthesizes its aperture. Sixty-nine LICEF receivers spread along the three arms of a Y-shaped array correlate in pairs, and from those correlations a two-dimensional brightness-temperature image is reconstructed — the first 2-D aperture-synthesis L-band radiometer in space, an interferometer borrowed from radio astronomy and pointed back at the Earth, with no large mechanical antenna to rotate.
- **One sensor, two faces of the water cycle.** The same L-band brightness temperature that, over land, carries the moisture of the soil also, over ocean, carries the salt of the sea. SMOS reads both from one instrument: over the land the emission inverts to the soil moisture in the top few centimetres, and over the ocean the small salinity sensitivity inverts, with patient multi-pass averaging, to the sea-surface salinity — the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea measured together, the first mission to do so.
- **A three-year mission still working after fifteen.** SMOS launched in November 2009 for a nominal mission of only a few years, yet it continues to operate 15+ years on, its data products still updated and its record still extended — a pioneering mission that has far exceeded its design lifetime and goes on mapping the water cycle's two key variables.
- **A PNW shrew that hunts in the moist living soil.** The Pacific Water Shrew (*Sorex bendirii*), a semi-aquatic shrew of PNW riparian wet soils, seeps, streambanks, and wetlands, hunts in saturated soil and along water margins — a small insectivore of the moist living land, its world the soil moisture SMOS measures from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A creeping rosaceous plant of the brackish tidal meadow.** The Pacific Silverweed (*Argentina egedii*), a low creeping rosaceous perennial of PNW coastal salt marshes, brackish tidal meadows, and estuary edges, roots in the moist saline soil where land water meets sea salt, with silvery pinnate leaves and yellow flowers — a coastal halophyte bound with the throughline of the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea.
