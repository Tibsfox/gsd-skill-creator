## Surprises

- **The brightness of the Earth at L-band turned into the water within it.** SMOS does not measure the water of the Earth directly — it measures faint microwave light. The MIRAS radiometer reads the brightness temperature the land and the sea radiate at 1.413 GHz, the natural microwave emission of the surface, and from that brightness, inverted through the retrieval algorithms, it recovers the soil moisture of the land and the sea-surface salinity of the ocean. The microwave glow of the surface, read from above, becomes a map of the water cycle's two key variables.
- **A radiometer with no antenna to point.** Rather than sweeping a single large dish across the scene, MIRAS synthesizes its aperture. Sixty-nine LICEF receivers spread along the three arms of a Y-shaped array correlate in pairs, and from those correlations a two-dimensional brightness-temperature image is reconstructed — the first 2-D aperture-synthesis L-band radiometer in space, an interferometer borrowed from radio astronomy and pointed back at the Earth, with no large mechanical antenna to rotate.
- **One sensor, two faces of the water cycle.** The same L-band brightness temperature that, over land, carries the moisture of the soil also, over ocean, carries the salt of the sea. SMOS reads both from one instrument: over the land the emission inverts to the soil moisture in the top few centimetres, and over the ocean the small salinity sensitivity inverts, with patient multi-pass averaging, to the sea-surface salinity — the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea measured together, the first mission to do so.
- **A three-year mission still working after fifteen.** SMOS launched in November 2009 for a nominal mission of only a few years, yet it continues to operate 15+ years on, its data products still updated and its record still extended — a pioneering mission that has far exceeded its design lifetime and goes on mapping the water cycle's two key variables.
- **A PNW shrew that hunts in the moist living soil.** The Pacific Water Shrew (*Sorex bendirii*), a semi-aquatic shrew of PNW riparian wet soils, seeps, streambanks, and wetlands, hunts in saturated soil and along water margins — a small insectivore of the moist living land, its world the soil moisture SMOS measures from above (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A creeping rosaceous plant of the brackish tidal meadow.** The Pacific Silverweed (*Argentina egedii*), a low creeping rosaceous perennial of PNW coastal salt marshes, brackish tidal meadows, and estuary edges, roots in the moist saline soil where land water meets sea salt, with silvery pinnate leaves and yellow flowers — a coastal halophyte bound with the throughline of the moisture of the land and the salt of the sea.
