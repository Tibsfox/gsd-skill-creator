## Mission Overview

Galileo is the obs#5 sustain of the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis — a NASA deep-space mission to Jupiter whose defining achievement is being the first spacecraft to orbit a giant planet and the first to send an entry probe down into one. It is managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) — like the Voyagers, distinct from the Ames-managed Pioneer siblings. An orbiter paired with an atmospheric entry probe — not a flyby probe like the Pioneers and Voyagers — it launched aboard the Space Shuttle Atlantis on mission STS-34 on 18 October 1989 and was boosted out of low Earth orbit by a two-stage Inertial Upper Stage, NOT on an expendable rocket. Because a direct Jupiter trajectory was beyond the IUS, the mission flew a VEEGA (Venus-Earth-Earth Gravity Assist) path — a flyby of Venus on 10 February 1990 and two flybys of Earth, on 8 December 1990 and 8 December 1992 — using the inner planets' gravity to build up the energy to reach Jupiter over about six years. The orbiter carried the solid-state imaging camera (SSI), the near-infrared mapping spectrometer (NIMS), the ultraviolet spectrometer (UVS), the photopolarimeter-radiometer (PPR), and a fields-and-particles suite (the magnetometer, the energetic-particles detector, the plasma and plasma-wave instruments, and the dust detector); the descent probe carried an atmospheric structure instrument, a neutral mass spectrometer, a helium-abundance detector, a nephelometer, a net-flux radiometer, and a lightning-and-energetic-particles instrument. The story is the long road to Jupiter and the work in the Jovian system: across the inner solar system on the VEEGA loop, where en route through the asteroid belt Galileo made the first close flyby of an asteroid, 951 Gaspra, on 29 October 1991 (closest approach about 1,600 km), and at 243 Ida discovered Dactyl, the first known moon of an asteroid. On 7 December 1995 Galileo became the first spacecraft to enter orbit around Jupiter, and on the same day the Galileo Probe parachuted into the giant planet's atmosphere, the first probe to directly sample a gas giant, returning about 58 minutes of in-situ measurements before the rising pressure ended its transmission. The scoping is load-bearing: the orbit is the first ORBIT of Jupiter (the Pioneers and Voyagers only flew PAST, and Cassini orbited Saturn later, 2004) — NOT the first Jupiter flyby (Pioneer 10, 1973); the probe is the first atmospheric entry probe at a GIANT planet (Venus and Mars probes came earlier); the asteroid flyby is the first close encounter with a named, targeted asteroid (Pioneer 10 traversed the belt but did not fly by a specific body); and Galileo STUDIED Io's volcanism from orbit but did NOT discover it (that is Voyager 1's Io). Over nearly eight years and 35 moon encounters the orbiter studied Jupiter and the Galilean moons, finding strong evidence for a subsurface salty ocean on Europa (a science result, not a first), and on 21 September 2003 made a planned final descent into Jupiter, a final state aligned with planetary-protection guidelines for Europa.
