## Surprises

- **One spacecraft both circled a giant planet for years and sent a probe down into it on the same day.** On 7 December 1995 Galileo entered orbit around Jupiter — the first craft ever to orbit a giant planet — while its descent probe parachuted into the atmosphere, the first probe to directly sample a gas giant, returning about 58 minutes of in-situ data before the rising pressure ended its transmission.
- **A probe descended into a giant planet's clouds and read the weather from inside.** The Galileo Probe measured temperature, pressure, winds, and composition as it parachuted through Jupiter's atmosphere down to about 23 atmospheres, the first time any craft sampled the inside of a gas giant.
- **The first close look at an asteroid came on the way to somewhere else.** En route to Jupiter, Galileo made the first close flyby of an asteroid (951 Gaspra, 29 October 1991) and discovered the first known moon of an asteroid (Dactyl, at 243 Ida, 1993) — firsts gathered while crossing the belt toward the giant planet.
- **A mission flew on its backup antenna and still returned the science.** Galileo's high-gain antenna never fully deployed, yet the orbiter completed nearly eight years and 35 moon encounters over its low-gain antenna with onboard data compression — an engineering recovery that returned the Jovian-system science, including the strong evidence for a subsurface ocean at Europa.
- **A diving beetle carries its own air down into the deep water.** The Predaceous Diving Beetle (*Dytiscus marginicollis*), a large predaceous diving beetle of Pacific Northwest ponds and lakes, dives down into the deep water to hunt carrying a renewable bubble of air trapped beneath its wing-covers, returning to the surface to refresh it (behavioral / ecological natural history only) — a mirror of Galileo circling and descending into the giant.
- **An aquatic plant roots in the deep bottom and floats its leaves circling on the surface above.** Watershield (*Brasenia schreberi*), a floating-leaved aquatic plant of cold, deep Pacific Northwest ponds and lakes, roots in the deep bottom mud and floats small oval leaves on the surface, its young stems and leaf undersides coated in a clear gelatinous slime (ecological description only) — a mirror of the long circling above and the deep reach below.

## Lessons Learned

1. **A sustain KEEPS the predecessor's shared domain vocabulary — it does not remove it.** Unlike a rotation, this is a SUSTAIN (mode=continuation): Galileo and the Voyager / Pioneer siblings share almost all of their domain vocabulary, so the rewrite KEPT the shared outer-planets content (the outer solar system, deep space, the giant planets, Jupiter, the Galilean moons, RTG power, the flyby, the gravity assist, the orbit insertion, the atmospheric probe, the heliosphere) and replaced only the predecessor-specific facts — Voyager 2 as the subject, the 20 August 1977 Titan IIIE-Centaur launch, the first and only flybys of Uranus (1986) and Neptune (1989), the Grand Tour of all four giant planets, the 2018 heliopause crossing, Triton's geysers, and the Northern Elephant Seal + Mountain Bog Gentian pairing — with Galileo's: the JPL management (like the Voyagers), the 18 October 1989 Space Shuttle Atlantis launch (STS-34) with the Inertial Upper Stage, the VEEGA trajectory, the first asteroid flyby (Gaspra, 1991) and first asteroid moon (Dactyl, 1993), the Jupiter orbit insertion (7 December 1995) as the first Jupiter orbiter, the atmospheric entry probe's deep descent (the first at a giant planet), the long orbital tour and the Europa subsurface-ocean evidence, the planned final descent (21 September 2003), and the Predaceous Diving Beetle + Watershield pairing.
2. **A sustain SUSTAINS the axis — the obs counter advances and there are prior siblings on the same axis.** Galileo sustains the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION axis at obs#5 (the fourth consecutive sustain on the axis opened by Pioneer 10, sustained by Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, and Voyager 2), so only the OUTER-PLANETS-EXPLORATION-AXIS-INTRA-CONTINUATION counter advances (obs#4 → obs#5) and the substrate-axis-rotation discipline holds UNCHANGED at obs#101 (no NEW rotation observed). The single deliberate backward reference is the nav-prev to the 1.253 Voyager 2 sibling, with a lineage thread to Voyager 1 and the Pioneers.
3. **The management is JPL, like the Voyagers, NOT Ames — a correct intra-axis distinction.** Galileo was managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, like the Voyagers, whereas the Pioneer siblings (obs#1/#2) were managed by Ames; this is a correct distinction within the shared axis, not a contradiction, and the catalog states JPL precisely throughout.
4. **Galileo launched on the Space Shuttle (STS-34 Atlantis) + IUS, NOT an expendable rocket; it flew a VEEGA trajectory.** Galileo launched 18 October 1989 aboard the Space Shuttle Atlantis and was boosted out of Earth orbit by a two-stage Inertial Upper Stage, then flew a VEEGA gravity-assist path (Venus 1990, Earth 1990 and 1992) over about six years; the catalog states this precisely and never describes an expendable Titan/Atlas launch.
5. **The Jupiter achievement is the first ORBIT, not the first flyby.** On 7 December 1995 Galileo became the first spacecraft to orbit Jupiter (and the first orbiter of an outer planet); the catalog scopes this strictly to ORBIT / gravitational capture, ceding the first Jupiter FLYBY to Pioneer 10 (1973) — the Pioneers and Voyagers only flew PAST, and Cassini orbited Saturn later (2004).
6. **The probe is the first atmospheric entry probe at a GIANT planet — the qualifier is load-bearing.** On 7 December 1995 the Galileo Probe descended into Jupiter's atmosphere, the first to directly sample a gas giant; the catalog keeps the GIANT / gas-giant qualifier essential, since atmospheric entry probes at the terrestrial planets Venus and Mars were earlier.
7. **The asteroid encounter is the first asteroid FLYBY — a named, targeted asteroid — plus the first asteroid moon.** On 29 October 1991 Galileo made the first close flyby of 951 Gaspra, and at 243 Ida (1993) discovered Dactyl, the first asteroid moon; the catalog scopes this as the first close encounter with a named, targeted asteroid, distinct from Pioneer 10's first asteroid-belt traverse (1972, which did not fly by a specific body).
8. **0 re-stamps — Galileo shares the predecessors' journey but not their firsts.** Galileo made the first Jupiter orbit (NOT the first flyby — Pioneer 10) and studied Io's volcanism (NOT discovered it — Voyager 1); it does not re-stamp Pioneer 10's outer-solar-system / belt-traverse / Jupiter-flyby / escape-trajectory / plaque firsts, Pioneer 11's Saturn-flyby / F-ring / magnetic-field firsts, Voyager 1's Io-volcanism / most-distant / first-heliopause-crossing firsts, or Voyager 2's Uranus / Neptune / Grand-Tour firsts. Galileo's firsts are all at the first Jupiter orbit, the first giant-planet atmospheric entry probe, and the first asteroid flyby, yielding 0 predecessor re-stamps.
9. **The Europa subsurface-ocean evidence is a science result, not an anchor.** Over its orbital tour Galileo found strong evidence (chiefly from its magnetometer) for a subsurface salty ocean on Europa; the catalog frames this as a major finding, NOT a first, and notes that Galileo studied Io's volcanism from orbit rather than discovering it.
10. **The stuck high-gain antenna is an engineering recovery — positive framing.** Galileo's high-gain antenna never fully deployed; the catalog frames the success over the low-gain antenna with onboard data compression positively, as the engineering recovery that returned the orbital science.
11. **The mission is framed positively — the 21 September 2003 end of mission is a planned final state.** On 21 September 2003 Galileo made a planned final descent into Jupiter; the catalog frames this strictly and positively as a planned final state aligned with planetary-protection guidelines, a deliberate choice to keep the spacecraft's path clear of the ocean moon Europa.
