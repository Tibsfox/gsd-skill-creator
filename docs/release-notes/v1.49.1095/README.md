## Mission Overview

LADEE is the obs#3 sustainer of the ROBOTIC-LUNAR-SCIENCE-EXPLORATION axis — NASA's lunar exosphere-and-dust orbiter, built and managed by NASA's Ames Research Center, that circled the Moon in a low retrograde near-equatorial orbit and sampled the tenuous gas and the faint suspended dust just above the surface while demonstrating high-rate laser communications from lunar distance. The project scientist was Rick Elphic and the project manager was Butler Hine; the mission was part of NASA's Lunar Quest Program. LADEE was the first spacecraft to fly the Modular Common Spacecraft Bus — a low-cost modular architecture developed at Ames — and the first spacecraft fully built and tested at Ames. ★ LADEE was NOT a Discovery mission (Lunar Prospector holds the first-Discovery-to-the-Moon anchor; LADEE was a Lunar Quest Program mission). The spacecraft launched on the evening of 6 September 2013 (local EDT) / 7 September 2013 (about 03:27 UTC) on an Orbital Sciences Minotaur V from Pad 0B at the Mid-Atlantic Regional Spaceport, NASA's Wallops Flight Facility, Virginia — the first flight of the Minotaur V and the first lunar launch from Wallops — cruised a series of phasing loops to the Moon, reached lunar-orbit insertion about 6 October 2013, and lowered into a low retrograde near-equatorial science orbit (about 50 km, varying roughly 12 to 60 km, reached about 20 November 2013) to skim tens of kilometres above the surface: a dedicated exosphere-and-dust mapper. The story is the launch from Wallops, the looping phasing cruise to the Moon, the low orbit skimming just above the surface one pass after another, the instruments reading the almost-nothing envelope of individual atoms and the faint suspended dust, the laser pulse crossing the dark between the Moon and Earth, and the planned final descent onto the far side. The science is central: the Neutral Mass Spectrometer characterized the composition and variability of the Moon's surface-boundary exosphere — a near-vacuum envelope of individual atoms — finding helium, neon, and argon most abundant (neon the novel discovery, NASA press-released); the Ultraviolet and Visible Light Spectrometer measured sodium and potassium, about a twofold increase during the Geminids meteor shower; and the Lunar Dust Experiment found a permanent, asymmetric dust cloud (dawn-enhanced) produced by meteoroid-impact ejecta from high-speed cometary meteoroids (Horanyi et al. 2015; Benna et al. 2015; Colaprete et al. 2016). ★ Investigating the decades-old "lunar horizon glow" reported by Surveyor and Apollo, LADEE found no evidence of the expected high-altitude electrostatically lofted dust population — it set stringent upper limits (from a few kilometres to about 250 km above the terminator) that argue against the lofted-dust explanation, so the catalog frames LADEE as having INVESTIGATED the horizon-glow question rather than confirmed lofted dust. The Lunar Laser Communication Demonstration achieved the first two-way (high-rate optical) laser communication from lunar orbit, with a record downlink (about 622 Mbps) and a 20 Mbps error-free uplink (Boroson et al. 2014). The scoping is load-bearing throughout: the EXOSPHERE and near-surface DUST environment — distinct from GRAIL's gravity and INTERIOR structure and from Lunar Prospector's surface GEOCHEMISTRY and water ice; the LLCD first scoped to "from lunar orbit / lunar distance" (earlier near-Earth optical links existed); and the first flight of the Modular Common Spacecraft Bus, an engineering and architecture first about the BUS. ★★ The mission ended in April 2014 (launched 6/7 September 2013, entered lunar orbit about 6 October 2013, sampled the exosphere and dust and demonstrated the laser link for about six months, ended 18 April 2014 with a planned final descent onto the far side), so the catalog frames the story in the PAST tense throughout (LADEE launched, orbited, sampled, measured, demonstrated, ended). ★ The dates are kept distinct: launch 6/7 September 2013, lunar-orbit insertion about 6 October 2013, end of mission 18 April 2014. ## Surprises

- **LADEE read the Moon's almost-nothing exosphere atom by atom.** The Moon's surface-boundary exosphere is a near-vacuum envelope of individual atoms rather than a true atmosphere; LADEE's Neutral Mass Spectrometer measured its composition and variability, finding helium, neon, and argon most abundant — and neon, long predicted, became the novel discovery that NASA press-released.
- **The dust around the Moon is a permanent, asymmetric cloud of meteoroid-impact ejecta.** The Lunar Dust Experiment found a permanent, dawn-enhanced, asymmetric cloud of fine dust around the Moon, produced not by electrostatic lofting but by ballistic ejecta thrown up by high-speed cometary meteoroid impacts — and it set stringent upper limits that argue against the lofted-dust explanation for the decades-old lunar horizon glow.
- **LADEE pulsed laser light across lunar distance and closed a two-way link at a record rate.** The Lunar Laser Communication Demonstration sent laser pulses across the dark between the Moon and Earth and achieved the first two-way laser communication from lunar orbit, with a record downlink of about 622 Mbps and a 20 Mbps error-free uplink — a first at lunar distance.
- **A night-flying moth that finds its mate by a few molecules on the thin air.** The Ceanothus Silkmoth (*Hyalophora euryalus*), a large saturniid of Pacific Northwest woodlands whose non-feeding adult lives only a week or two, finds a female across great distance in the dark when the male detects a few molecules of her pheromone carried on the thin night air with large feathered antennae, navigating to the faintest trace — a mirror of LADEE sensing the faintest traces in the thinnest medium (behavioral / ecological description only).
- **A lichen that draws its whole life from the thin air and the passing fog.** The Horsehair Lichen (*Bryoria fremontii*), a dark-brown filamentous lichen that hangs in fine threads from PNW conifer branches, is a fungus-and-alga symbiont with no roots that draws all its moisture and nourishment directly from the thin air and the fog, living on the bare surface of the branch (ecological description only) — bound with the throughline of drawing the faintest trace from the thinnest air.

## Dedication

v1.278 LADEE is dedicated to the people across NASA's Ames Research Center, Goddard Space Flight Center, Marshall Space Flight Center, and MIT Lincoln Laboratory who built and flew the orbiter that read the Moon's thinnest envelope. The project scientist, Rick Elphic, who carried a delicate exosphere-and-dust mission to flight, and the project manager, Butler Hine, who led it. The engineers at Ames who built and tested the first Modular Common Spacecraft Bus and the flight team who lifted it from Wallops on the first Minotaur V, held the looping cruise, and settled the orbiter into a low retrograde orbit just above the surface. The teams behind the Neutral Mass Spectrometer, the Ultraviolet and Visible Light Spectrometer, and the Lunar Dust Experiment, who measured helium, neon, and argon atom by atom and counted the faint dust grain by grain, and the teams at Goddard and Lincoln Laboratory behind the Lunar Laser Communication Demonstration, who pulsed light across the dark and closed the first two-way link from lunar orbit. They read a world from what was barely there — the faintest trace in the thinnest air — and guided the orbiter at last to a quiet rest on the far side.
