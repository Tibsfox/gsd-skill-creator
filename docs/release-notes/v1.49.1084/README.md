## Mission Overview

Ulysses is the obs#2 sustainer of the HELIOPHYSICS-EXPLORATION axis — a joint ESA/NASA mission whose defining records are the first spacecraft to fly over the Sun's poles, the first in-situ survey of the heliosphere at all solar latitudes, and the ESA/NASA Jupiter-gravity-assist method that flung it out of the ecliptic. ESA designed and built the spacecraft (prime contractor Dornier Systems, Germany); NASA provided the launch, the radioisotope thermoelectric generator (RTG) power, Deep Space Network tracking, and about half of the roughly 12 science instruments — NOT JHU APL, NOT NASA's Living With a Star program. A polar-orbit solar mission, it launched 6 October 1990 on Space Shuttle Discovery (mission STS-41), boosted by an IUS + PAM-S upper-stage stack onto a fast trajectory to Jupiter; an 8 February 1992 Jupiter gravity assist bent its path steeply out of the ecliptic plane into a high-inclination (~80° to the ecliptic) heliocentric polar orbit of about a six-year period. Because the route reached Jupiter's distance, where sunlight is too weak for solar arrays, the spacecraft drew its power from a GPHS-RTG. Its instrument set was SWOOPS (solar-wind plasma), SWICS (solar-wind ion composition), dual magnetometers (VHM/FGM), URAP (unified radio and plasma waves), COSPIN + HI-SCALE (cosmic rays and energetic particles), GRB (gamma-ray bursts), DUST (interstellar and interplanetary dust), and GAS (interstellar neutral helium). The story is the Discovery launch, the cruise to Jupiter, the slingshot up and out of the flat ecliptic plane, and the high-inclination loops carrying Ulysses over the Sun's south and north poles across three epochs (1994-95, 2000-01, 2007-08). The science is central: Ulysses sampled the solar wind, ions, fields, waves, and energetic particles from pole to pole, mapping the 3-D structure of the solar wind — fast (~750 km/s) uniform wind streaming from the large polar coronal holes versus slow (~350 km/s), variable wind near the equator — together with the heliospheric magnetic field over the poles, cosmic-ray modulation with latitude, and the first in-situ detection of interstellar dust and interstellar neutral helium streaming into the heliosphere. The scoping is load-bearing throughout: Ulysses is a SOLAR / heliophysics mission, NOT a space telescope, and re-stamps NO space-telescope-axis anchor; it is a joint ESA/NASA mission (ESA spacecraft from Dornier; NASA launch + RTG + DSN + about half the instruments), NOT JHU APL, NOT Living With a Star; it was the first spacecraft to fly OVER the Sun's poles (distinct from Parker, which flew THROUGH the corona near the equator); and it made the first 3-D survey of the heliosphere. ★ Ulysses' operations were terminated 30 June 2009 (after ~18.6 years, far beyond the planned ~5), so the catalog frames the mission in the PAST tense throughout (Ulysses flew, surveyed, discovered; the mission ended), in contrast with the present-tense Parker page. ## Surprises

- **A spacecraft that left the flat plane every planet shares to fly over the Sun's poles.** Ulysses was the first (and for decades the only) spacecraft to fly over the Sun's poles — it rose out of the ecliptic plane into a ~80° polar solar orbit and looped over the south and north solar poles across three epochs (1994-95, 2000-01, 2007-08), reaching heliographic latitudes no spacecraft had visited.
- **A flyby of Jupiter to reach the Sun's poles.** Ulysses could not have reached a polar solar orbit by rocket alone, so it flew first to Jupiter and used an 8 February 1992 gravity assist (closest approach ~6.3 Jupiter radii) to crank its inclination up to ~80° and fling itself out of the ecliptic — a slingshot at Jupiter's distance that bent the path back toward the Sun's poles.
- **An RTG to reach the Sun.** Because the route to a polar solar orbit ran out to Jupiter's distance, where sunlight is too weak for solar arrays, Ulysses drew its power from a radioisotope thermoelectric generator — a mission to study the Sun that could not run on sunlight.
- **The 3-D shape of the solar wind, mapped pole to pole.** Ulysses made the first in-situ survey of the heliosphere at all solar latitudes, finding a fast (~750 km/s) uniform wind streaming from the large polar coronal holes and a slow (~350 km/s), variable wind near the equator — the first measured 3-D structure of the solar wind, along with the heliospheric magnetic field over the poles and the first in-situ detection of interstellar dust and neutral helium.
- **A slender alpine songbird that breeds above treeline in the highest mountains.** The American Pipit (*Anthus rubescens*), a ground-walking songbird that breeds on the high alpine tundra above treeline in the Pacific Northwest's highest Cascades and Olympics, the exposed high realm above the forested zone, performs a parachuting flight-song display over the alpine and winters down in open lowlands (documented natural history only; distinguished from the Horned Lark *Eremophila alpestris*) — living in the high country above the plane, a mirror of the mission that rose above the ecliptic to fly over the Sun's poles.
- **A low evergreen mat that endures the exposed alpine heights.** White Mountain-heather (*Cassiope mertensiana*), a low, mat-forming evergreen alpine shrublet of the highest PNW meadows and rocky ridges (at and above treeline, among the highest-growing flowering plants), with tiny appressed scale-like leaves and nodding white bell flowers, endures the extreme exposed high-alpine environment (ecological description only, distinguished from Pink Mountain-heather *Phyllodoce empetriformis*) — enduring the high country above the plane, bound with Ulysses' out-of-ecliptic flight by the high country above the plane.

## Dedication

v1.267 Ulysses is dedicated to the people across ESA and NASA who built and flew the first spacecraft to fly over the Sun's poles. The engineers at Dornier who built a small spinning craft to ride a radioisotope generator out to Jupiter's distance and back toward the Sun, where no sunlight could power it; the navigators who could not reach a polar solar orbit by rocket and so flew first to Jupiter, trading a flyby for the steep climb up out of the flat plane every planet shares; the teams at the Deep Space Network who tracked the craft over the south and north poles of the Sun across three passes and almost two decades; and the scientists who read the solar wind, the fields, and the particles from pole to pole and found the wind's true three-dimensional shape — fast from the open poles, slow and restless at the equator. It is named for Ulysses of the old poem, who sailed past the known edge of the world. They sent a small craft over the top of the Sun, into the high country above the plane, and it surveyed the shape of the wind before the mission ended.
